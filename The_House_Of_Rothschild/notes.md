## Introduction: Reality and Myth

> "Banking consists essentially of facilitating the movement
> of money from Point A, where it is, to Point B, where it
> is needed." — **3rd Lord Rothschild**

- Rothschild name (German "Redshield") generated mythology
  rivaling the firm — 153 insect species, 58 birds, 18
  mammals, 14 plants named after them; dedications by
  **Chopin** and **Rossini**; portraits by **Balzac**/**Heine**
- Focus: how they accumulated and maintained wealth
- **N. M. Rothschild & Sons** not a bank per **Bagehot** in
  *Lombard Street* (1873) — dealt in large sums long-term,
  not small deposits. They were **foreign bill-brokers**
- At zenith (1820s–1860s), five houses as one **multinational**:
  London (Nathan), Frankfurt (Amschel), Paris (James),
  Naples (Carl), Vienna (Salomon)
- Cross-ownership into the 20th century; male partners only
  until 1960; London: only uninterrupted house to present
- 1815–1914: **easily the biggest bank in the world** —
  no modern equivalent; Nathan and James owned as large a
  share of world wealth as anyone in history

### The Bond Market

- All states ran deficits; war was primary driver; options:
  sell assets, debase currency, raise taxes, borrow
- Ferguson's thesis: **Rothschilds created the international
  bond market in its modern form** — enabling investors to
  buy tradeable, fixed-interest bonds of foreign states,
  liquid 5.5 days/week on major bourses
- Bond prices = **daily opinion poll** on government
  credibility; two threats: **war** and **internal instability**
- Three advantages: (1) **proximity** to political power,
  (2) **speed** of news, (3) ability to **manipulate**
  transmission — used **carrier pigeons**, private couriers,
  **salaried agents**

### Beyond Bonds

- Bullion broking/refining, commercial bills, FX arbitrage,
  commodities, insurance, "personal banking" for royalty
- Financed railways across France/Austria/Germany from 1830s;
  James built **pan-European railway network**
- Mining: **Almadén** mercury (1830s); 1880s–90s: gold,
  copper, diamonds, oil — South Africa to Burma

### Family and Political Dimensions

- Unity enforced through **partnership agreements** and
  **nuptial agreements** (Rothschild married Rothschild)
- Husbands of Rothschild women barred from business
- Social ascent: from ghetto to great palaces; **English
  peerage** 1885; NOT merely "feudalized" bourgeoisie —
  brought **commercial logic** to aristocratic milieu
- Resembled **royalty**: extreme endogamy, self-described
  as "our royal family"
- Remained **committed to Judaism**; used financial leverage
  for **Jewish emancipation** across Europe
- Political network: Nathan/Herries/Wellington/Liverpool;
  James/Villèle/Louis Philippe; Salomon/**Metternich**
  (central 1818–48); Bismarck harder to cultivate

### The Rothschild Myths

- **Sympathetic myth**: 1813 **Cohen** memoir; 1827 **Gentz**
  *Brockhaus* entry (paid by Rothschilds); **Elector's

  - Queen **Victoria** refused Lionel a peerage
  - **Byron**, *Don Juan* (1823): "Jew Rothschild, and his

## Chapter 1: "Our Blessed Father" — Origins

> "Yes, my dear fellow, it all amounts to this: in order
> to do something you must be something." — **Goethe**, 1828

### The Judengasse: Frankfurt's Jewish Ghetto

- A traveller crossing the **Sachsenhäuser Bridge** to the
  **Fahrtor Gate** encountered the *Judensau* — an
  officially sanctioned obscene graffito; alongside it, a
  reference to the 1475 "ritual murder" of **Simon of Trent**
- Frankfurt Jews date to the 12th century; three-quarters
  massacred in 1241 **"Battle of the Jews"**; second pogrom
  1349 blamed Jews for attracting the plague
- 1458: **Emperor Frederick III** confined Jews to the
  **Judengasse** — quarter-mile long, **no more than twelve
  feet wide** (in places less than ten)
- By 1711: **3,024 people** crammed into the lane; houses
  just **8 feet wide**, up to 4 storeys
- A four-room ghetto house cost as much as Goethe's
  father's **twenty-room mansion**
- Average mortality 58% **higher** among Jews than Gentiles
  in the 1780s; traveller (1795): "most of the people look
  like the walking dead"

> "The lack of space, the dirt, the throng of people...
> made the most unpleasant impression." — **Goethe**

- **Ludwig Börne** (born **Juda Löw Baruch**) described it
  as "a long dark prison, into which the highly celebrated
  light of the eighteenth century has not yet been able
  to penetrate"

### The Stättigkeit

- Statute governed Jewish life: population capped at **500
  families**; weddings rationed to **12/year**; barred from
  farming, weapons, spices, wine, grain; confined to ghetto
  nights/Sundays/festivals; **two concentric yellow rings**
  (until 1726); **double tolls**; special oath invoking
  curses; if a boy cried *"Jud, mach mores!"* the Jew must
  doff hat and step aside
- **Fettmilch riots**: looting of Judengasse; Jews expelled
  temporarily; imperial troops hanged leaders, marched Jews
  back
- Frankfurt refused **Joseph II's Edict of Toleration**
  (1782); confiscated **Lessing's** *Nathan the Wise*
- Real driver: **Gentile business feared competition**

### Frankfurt as Financial Center

- Junction of major trade routes; two annual fairs; enormous
  variety of coinage made **money-changing** and
  **bill-broking** indispensable
- **Bethmann brothers** dominated pre-1800 by importing
  **"sub-bonds"** (*Partialobligationen*) from Amsterdam:
  large loans subdivided — between 1754–1778 floated nearly
  **2 million gulden**; in the next five years, **30 million**
- Jews dominated money-changing because most fields closed
  to them; by mid-18th century acting as court agents for
  the Palatinate, Mainz, Hesse-Darmstadt, Prussia, Vienna,
  Hesse-Kassel, and Saxe-Weimar
- **Joseph Süss-Oppenheimer** (*Jud Süss*): most notorious
  court Jew — executed 1737 for "wielding excessive
  political power"; became centerpiece of anti-Semitic legend

### Mayer Amschel Rothschild: Origins

- Born 1743/1744 in the Judengasse; name from ancestor
  **Isak's** house "zum roten Schild" (1560s); descendants
  used both "Rothschild" and "Bauer" for generations
- Father **Amschel Moses**: studious Torah observer; family
  living in modest Hinterpfann house (ground-floor office,
  first-floor kitchen, cramped bedrooms)
- Parents died of epidemic **1755–1756** — Mayer just
  **twelve years old**
- Sent to **Hanover** to learn business at **Wolf Jakob
  Oppenheim's** firm — first contact with **court agents**
  (Oppenheims had deep court connections)
- Became dealer in **rare coins and medals**; mastered
  **Samuel Maddai's** numismatic classification
- First transaction with **William, Hereditary Prince of
  Hesse-Kassel**: June 1765, just **38 gulden and 30
  kreuzers**; title of **court agent** granted September
  **1769**
- August **1770**: married **Gutle Schnapper** (dowry:
  **2,400 gulden**) — first in a succession of calculated
  Rothschild marriages

### The Coin Business and Transition to Banking

- Partnership with brother **Kalman** (until death 1782);
  became Frankfurt's leading dealer in coins, medals,
  antiques; meticulous catalogues circulated to widening
  aristocratic clientele
- 1783: bought "**zum grünen Schild**" for **11,000 gulden**
  — only 14 feet wide; sons later recalled sleeping "all
  in one little attic room"
- Income from *Maaserbuch* (tithe book): ~**2,400 gulden
  annually** — comparable to the Goethe family income
- By mid-1780s: estimated wealth **150,000 gulden**
- **Gutle** gave birth nearly every year 1771–1792; of ~19
  children, **ten survived**: Schönche (1771), **Amschel**
  (1773), **Salomon** (1774), **Nathan** (1777), Isabella,
  Babette, **Carl** (1788), Julie, Henrietta, **James** (1792)
- Banking emerged naturally from antique-dealing; the 1790s
  were a **real break** — rapid wealth growth:
  - 1795: taxable wealth doubled
  - 1796: moved into top tax bracket; listed as **tenth
    richest** in the Judengasse (**60,000+ gulden**)
  - By 1800: among **eleven richest families**
- Hired multilingual accountant **Seligmann Geisenheimer**
- Eldest son Amschel married **Eva Hanau** (1796) with
  **30,000 gulden** business share

### First Balance Sheet (1797)

- Discovered in the Moscow "trophy" archive (captured
  WWII documents)
- **Total assets**: 843,485 gulden; **liabilities**: 734,981;
  **net capital**: 108,504 gulden (~£10,000) — *"the balance
  of my capital, praise God"*
- Geographic range already international: Kassel, Hamburg,
  Amsterdam, Paris, London
- Creditors included **Bethmanns, de Neufvilles, Brentanos**;
  art collector **Städel** had deposits of 17,600 gulden
- Hesse-Kassel government owed 24,093 gulden; officials
  **Harnier** and **Buderus** appear as creditors
- **Liebmann embezzlement** (1797): junior employee stole
  **~30,000 gulden** — exposed three systemic failures:
  (1) employees traded on own account, (2) **no safe**, (3)
  **woefully primitive bookkeeping**
  - Discovered when broker appeared trying to buy an
    Austrian bill Liebmann was selling
  - Liebmann had sent money to his notoriously poor parents
    who suddenly could afford a 500 gulden dowry

### The Dual Revolution

- French bombardment (1796) destroyed nearly half the
  Judengasse — paradoxically forced Frankfurt to relax
  residence restrictions; Mayer rented warehouse on
  **Schnurgasse**
- War created profitable supply contracts: Mayer, **Schott**,
  and **Rindskopf** supplied Austrian army with grain
- **British Industrial Revolution** equally transformative:
  German demand for cheap British cloth grew rapidly; ~15
  Jewish firms importing English textiles by 1800; eight
  German merchants settled in Manchester as permanent agents

### Nathan's Arrival in England

- Arrived in Manchester ~**1799** (not 1798 or 1800)

> "There was not room enough for all of us in that city
> ... On the Thursday I started." — Nathan to MP
> **Thomas Fowell Buxton**, 1834

- Archives clear: **Nathan initially operated as Mayer
  Amschel's agent** — letters signed "pp. Meyer Amschel
  Rothschild"; cloth chests bore insignia "MAR"
- Key strategy: pay suppliers in cash, buying cloth
  **15–20% cheaper** than credit-paying rivals; charged
  only **5% on cost price** vs. industry norm of ~20%

> "I soon found that there were three profits — the raw
> material, the dyeing, and the manufacturing. I said to
> the manufacturer, 'I will supply you with material and
> dye, and you will supply me with manufactured goods.' So
> I got three profits instead of one." — Nathan

- By 1808: "large and commodious" warehouse + town house
  at **25 Mosley Street**, "the most elegant street in
  Manchester"
- Gross sales ~£800,000 (1800–1811); profit ~£40,000
- Nathan chronically disorganized — Mayer Amschel: "all
  our correspondents complain about you... if you are so
  disorganised you will be swindled"
- Nathan combative: threatened Scottish manufacturers,
  accused French buyer of "chicanery"; Hamburg banker
  **Behrens** called him "a great rascal"

### The Napoleonic Blockade and Smuggling

- **Berlin Decree** (November 1806): Nathan became **a
  smuggler** — fake Dutch documents, American-registered
  ships, contraband via Heligoland and Baltic ports
- September 1809: large Riga shipment seized, released
  only via bribery ("a heavy tax indeed")
- **Edict of Fontainebleau** (October 14, 1810): two
  infantry regiments occupied Frankfurt; 234 firms raided;
  **Mayer Amschel** caught with 60,000 gulden contraband;
  fined ~20,000 francs; seized goods (~100,000 gulden)
  publicly burned
- **Watershed**: commodity trade would play declining role
- October 1806: Nathan married **Hannah**, daughter of
  **Levi Barent Cohen** (dowry: £3,248) — foothold in
  London's Jewish financial community; Cohen encouraged
  the pivot to banking
- By 1808 acquired London address (12 Great St Helens);
  banking operations by 1810
- **July 1811**: formally opened counting-house at
  **No. 2 New Court, St Swithin's Lane, London**

## Chapter 2: The Elector's Treasure

> "The Old Man... made our fortune." — **Carl Rothschild**

### William IX and the Mercenary Business

- **William of Hesse-Kassel**: nearly Mayer Amschel's
  contemporary; notorious for avarice and prolific
  illegitimacy (12+ children by three mistresses, including
  7 by **Rosalie Dorothea Ritter**)
- Hesse-Kassel unusually rich: **30–40 million gulden** at
  accession — wealth from **selling troops** (usually to
  Britain)
- American War of Independence: sold ~2,000 men at **76
  gulden/man**, plus 25/wounded, 76/killed — paid in non-
  interest-bearing bills of exchange
- Total assets in 1806: **46 million gulden** (>£4 million):
  28.8 million in loans to German princes, 4.6 million in
  English annuities; net income ~**900,000 gulden/year**
- State finances resembled a bank: **Kammerkasse** (regular
  revenues), **Kriegskasse** (funded by mercenary hiring
  and investment interest)

### Mayer Amschel's Slow Climb

- Still a "nobody" at Kassel in 1785; by 1789 entered
  English bills market only by offering to **pay more**
  than established firms
- Initially granted only £800 credit; rival **Feidel David**
  had £25,000
- **Karl Friedrich Buderus** — the inside man: began as
  tutor to William's illegitimate children; moved into
  financial administration; classic "instrumental friendship"
- **1796 breakthrough**: Buderus tipped off Mayer Amschel
  to offer Frankfurt bonds at **97.5%** vs. rivals' 98% —
  loss-leader securing crucial foothold
- By 1798 most English bills passed through Mayer Amschel
- 1801–1806: involved in **eleven major loans** for William
  (Denmark, Hesse-Darmstadt, Baden, Order of St John)
- Established monopoly of Danish business — total Danish
  bonds: at least **4.5 million gulden**; Hesse-Darmstadt:
  **1.3 million**; Baden: **1.4 million**
- Rüppell & Harnier (1806) complained that "the name
  Rothschild" carried more credit than the Danish government
- At this stage, William was the real banker; Mayer Amschel

- By 1803: collected court agent titles from Order of St
  John, Thurn und Taxis, Hesse-Darmstadt, Isenburg, and
  most prestigiously the **Austrian Emperor** (1800)

### The Myth of the Buried Treasure

- Napoleon's invasion (autumn 1806): William too committed
  to England to join the **Confederation of the Rhine**
- William fled to Gottorp (Danish territory); General
  **Lagrange** occupied Kassel, confiscated all assets
- Napoleon: "My object is to remove the House of
  Hesse-Kassel from rulership and to strike it out of
  the list of powers"
- **The famous story is fiction** — only a few unimportant
  items came into Mayer Amschel's possession
- Most stored in caches: **Wilhelmshöhe** (24 chests hidden
  under stairs), **Löwenburg cellar** (24 chests),
  **Sababurg** (47 chests of silverware)
- Lagrange bribed with **260,000 francs** to allow 42
  chests spirited away; French "official" inventory: 19.8
  million gulden (actual ~27 million kept off books)
- The **four chests given to Mayer Amschel** contained only
  routine War Chest and Privy Purse papers

### The Real Story: Nathan and the English Consols

- William already had massive English portfolio: **£635,400

  **£100,150** in English subsidies
- From 1809 (at Buderus's prompting): purchased **3%
  consols** — first at face value £150,000 at 73.5;
  **nine purchases** totaling **£664,850**
- Profit mechanism: William didn't put up all cash at once;
  Rothschilds bought with largely borrowed money; profited
  from price/exchange rate differences (~2% on early ones)
- As late as March 1813, consols of face value £121,000
  nominally registered in Nathan's name
- Carl (1814): "if Nathan had not had the Elector's
  £300,000 in hand he would have got nowhere" — gave
  Nathan a "security," impression of resources far
  exceeding actual capital
- Amschel (1818): "Up to then Nathan did not even know
  what stocks looked like"

- **Dalberg** — playing both sides: Mayer facilitated

## Chapter 3: "The Commanding General" (1813–1815)

> "My brother in London is the commanding general, I am
> his field marshal." — **Salomon**

### War Finance and Wellington's Cash Crisis

- **Nathan** became principal conduit for British war
  finance to continental battlefields
- Wellington's military chest: ~**£100,000/month**; March
  1811 threatened to halt campaign for lack of cash;
  officers paid in depreciated paper, lower ranks not
  paid at all
- UK expenditure: **£18m/year → £100m/year** (1793–1815);
  total war cost: **~£830 million** of which £59 million
  in ally subsidies
- National debt: **£240m → £900m** (~200% of national
  income); 1797: Bank of England **suspended gold
  convertibility**; prices roughly doubled before 1815
- By 1812 the Iberian bill market was **saturated**

### How Nathan Got the Commission

1. **Absence of competition**: 1810 crisis destroyed
   rivals — **Abraham Goldsmid** committed suicide; Barings
   lost ~£43,000; **Hope & Co.** a "mere empty shell"
2. **John Charles Herries** as Commissary-in-Chief (October
   1811) — Nathan's "Buderus"; a Germanophile who had
   translated **Gentz's** anti-French tract; personal
   connection via Leipzig and the **Limburger** illegitimate-
   child affair (brothers eventually paid £15,000 blackmail)
3. **The bullion route**: before March 1811, Rothschilds
   already smuggling gold England→France — **tolerated by
   Napoleon** whose finance minister **Mollien** believed
   bullion outflows signaled British weakness (a fatal
   miscalculation)

> "I bought it all... The Government sent for me... I sent
> it to France; and that was the best business I ever did."
> — Nathan

- **James** operated from Gravelines/Dunkirk; typical April
  1812: **£27,300 in guineas** sent → returned bills worth
  **£65,798**
- Code system: Nathan = **"Langbein"**; London =
  **"Jerusalem"**; bullion = **"Rabbi Moses"**; shipments =
  **"beer," "fish," "children"**
- Secrecy so ingrained that Salomon's son **Anselm** at
  age eleven refused to let his teacher read a letter to
  his father: "How can I divulge the secrets which I share
  with my father?"

### The Official Commission (January 1814)

- Nathan charged with collecting up to **£600,000** in
  French/German gold and silver coins within two months;
  commission: **2%**; strict secrecy
- Agent **Meyer Davidson** sent to Amsterdam found French
  occupation had depleted coins; new **napoléons d'or**
  had to be struck
- By end of February: only **£150,000** — "like a drop of
  water in the ocean"
- Allied subsidy business: Britain paid ~**£42 million to
  allies 1811–1815**
- Lord Liverpool: **"I do not know what we should have done
  without him last year"**
- By mid-May Nathan owed **£1,167,000 by the government**

### The Russian Deal — *Meistergeschäft*

- Britain promised **£1,333,333 to Russia** and **£666,666
  to Prussia**; paid in 15 monthly instalments
- Russian diplomat **Gervais** approached Hope & Co.; Hope
  **hesitated**; Rothschilds snapped up the business —
  offered to convert 4 million thaler into gold coins AND
  deliver to Hamburg, Dresden, Warsaw
- James: Gervais **"needed cash, and soon"** and no other
  firm would risk delivering to remote Warsaw
- Bribery standard: **"our friend baksheesh"** — Gervais
  received 1% commission into his pocket; Salomon thought
  James gave "too much"; James: "The money given to Gervais
  makes all the difference"
- Commission structure: ~**8% gross profit** per tranche
- Also furnished: **Louis XVIII** with bills on Paris;
  **Hesse-Kassel** took a **250,000 gulden** loan when
  Russian troops requisitioned grain

### Exchange Rate Arbitrage

- Two or three profit streams per transfer: **commissions**,
  **exchange rate arbitrage**, **forward speculation**
- Key advantage: Rothschilds *themselves* caused exchange
  volatility — large subsidy transfers weakened sterling
- James by early 1815: "It depends solely on me whether
  the pound rises or falls in Paris"
- Carl: **"When we buy, everybody buys"** — markets followed
  the Rothschild lead
- Communications: **color-coded envelopes** (blue = sterling
  rising, red = falling)
- **July 1814 sterling crisis**: Nathan remitted £100,000+
  to Frankfurt "like a madman," causing pound to drop;
  Salomon warned: "If you are not careful you will not
  remain the master of the Stock Exchange"

### Nathan's Waterloo — The Myth Demolished

- **"Reality is diametrically opposite to myth"** — Ferguson
- Nathan miscalculated twice: expected lengthy war and
  minimal competition; in fact three months from return
  to Waterloo; continental rivals competed
- By May 5 told to **"desist in my operations"** —
  Wellington had more bullion than needed
- Root cause: **Heckscher** undercut them by pointing out

- Nathan charged **half a per cent less** than competitors, profiting on exchange rate differentials rather than commission
- Nathan's acceptances £300,000 vs. Barings £520,000 (1825); gap widened — Barings dominated transatlantic trade

**Bullion and Specie**:
- Substantial business with Bank of England and Banque de France; silver-ingot vans blocking New Court access

**Commodity Trade**:
- Strategy: **dominant position in select markets** — cotton, tobacco, sugar, copper, **mercury** (Spain — most important)

**Insurance**:
- Nathan co-founded **Alliance Assurance Company** (1824) — capital £5 million
- Motivations: discrimination by existing insurers; breaking the Lloyd's/London Assurance/Royal Exchange cartel on marine insurance
- Lloyd's injunction required creation of separate **Alliance Marine** (also £5m capital)
- Alliance proved long-lived and prosperous; further ventures: **Colonia** fire insurance (1839), **Cologne Reinsurance Society** (1852)

> "He never hesitated for a moment in fixing the rate... his memory was so retentive, that, notwithstanding the immense transactions... he never took a note of them, he could dictate the whole on his return home with perfect exactness."
> — *The Times* obituary on Nathan

### Expanding the Partnership — Next Generation

- **Anselm** (Salomon's son): partner 1826; within a year criticizing uncles' investment strategy — James: "he really has character"
- **Lionel** (Nathan's eldest): formally initiated 1828; stepped into father's shoes 1836
- **Anthony**: hated Frankfurt ("that stinking place"); "hard working" but failed to bring lasting deals; never lost unreliable reputation
- **Nat**: resented Naples posting; eventually became James's favourite nephew; spent career in James's shadow, "a disgruntled English gentleman in exile"

### Why No Sixth House?

- Brothers discussed Madrid, St Petersburg, US house — remained a pipe-dream
- Nathan's widow **Hannah** refused to allow younger sons posted overseas — a decisive obstacle

### Salaried Clerks and Agents

- Frankfurt office 1818: at least **9 clerks** with specialized roles; costs **150,000 gulden/year (~£14,000)**
- Paris: James spent **34,000 francs/year** on 8 clerks plus staff
- Key clerk dynasty: **Goldschmidt family** in Vienna — Moritz was Salomon's chief clerk
- By 1830s New Court: **30-40 people**, earning £50-£500/year
- When Nathan lay dying: first time clerks given **powers of attorney** — unclear who was senior

### The Agent Problem

- Remote agents acquired local status and made independent decisions; Rothschilds constantly suspected them of **trading on their own account**
- **Gasser** (St Petersburg): "That stiff dog, who is only too glad to hold on to your money at no cost to himself"
- **Lazare Richtenberger** (Brussels, 1832): first fully-fledged agent
- **Daniel Weisweiller** (Madrid, 1834): most important; "triple dose of vanity" but indispensable — Anthony: "an uncommonly clever agent... I have seldom or never seen a more disagreeable cold hearted calculating agent"
- **Hanau** (New York): criticized for "over-hasty dealings"

### Associated Banks and Rivals

- Associates in: Amsterdam, Baltimore, Berlin, Cologne, Constantinople, Florence, Hamburg, Milan, Odessa, Rome, Trieste
- **Warburg** (Hamburg): regular dealings not until 1830s
- **Samuel Bleichröder** (Berlin): not until 1860s that son **Gerson** gained special status via proximity to **Bismarck**

> "Bleichröder? What is Bleichröder? Bleichröder is the one per cent I let him have." — James to Herbert Bismarck

- By 1830s-40s, willing to rescue rivals — assisted **Laffitte** in 1831 and 1838; debated bailing out **Geymüller** in 1841
- **Quasi-central-bank mentality**: Lionel (1839) on joint-stock regulation: "how to keep these gentlemen from getting too fat and getting themselves and the country into scrapes"
- Symbiotic with actual central banks: Bank of England, Banque de France, Austrian Nationalbank

### Bourse Tactics and Press Relations

- Favourite brokers: **Menet & Cazenove** sold £2m of foreign stocks in 1834 alone
- **Alfred Wagg** recalled Lionel writing an **arbitrary fee of £500 or £1,000** "varying in amount according to the humour he was in"
- Nathan's counter-tactics: if bullish, he would commission brokers **initially to sell** half a million; one set selling and another buying simultaneously
- Salomon in Vienna: **jobber on 12,000 gulden fixed salary** with runners to both the official Bourse and the "Panduren-Lager" (Grünangergasse café)
- Press: Salomon through **Gentz**, **Heine** as Paris correspondent, James expanding influence over *Moniteur Universel* and *Journal des Débats*
- James (1839): **"If one can't make oneself loved then one has to make oneself feared"** (quoting Mayer Amschel)
- Nathan built relationship with **The Times** — pigeon-post service; friend **Thomas Massa Alsager** (City correspondent)

### Legal Disputes

- **Brookman case (1829)**: Nathan advised selling French rentes and buying Prussian bonds; kept the rentes for himself; charged fees for transactions that never occurred; Vice-Chancellor found Nathan guilty of "fallacious statements"
- More often **victims** of fraud: clerk stole ~1.5m francs in specially designed belt (1825); Madrid office robbed of ~£40,000 (1845)

## Chapter 12: Love and Debt — Rothschild Culture

### Art Collections

- **Heine** (1843): James had "the capacity of finding (if not always of judging) the leading practitioners in every other sphere" — compared to **Louis XIV**
- **Disraeli** on James: "a happy mixture of the French Dandy & the orange boy"
- First notable purchase: **Greuze**'s *The Milkmaid* (James, 1818)
- **Lionel** discovered **Murillo** in Madrid (1834); by end of 1840s James, Lionel, and **Hannah** all owned Murillos
- Dominant taste: **17th-century Dutch masters** — James owned **Rembrandt**'s *The Standard Bearer* from George IV's collection; also Hals, van Dyck, Rubens, Ruisdael, Wouwermans
- Lionel had distinct enthusiasm for **18th-century English portraiture** — **Reynolds**, **Gainsborough** (*Portrait of the Hon. Frances Duncombe* for £1,500 in 1871)
- **Mayer Carl** (Frankfurt): more interested in gold/silver ornaments — amassed a "**Goldschatz**" of 5,000 items including **Wentzel Jamnitzer**'s masterpiece; an ivory horn turned out to be a **forgery by Reinhold Vasters**
- **Art as investment**: James's collection insured for **10 million francs (£400,000)** — a quarter of his share in the five houses' capital. Mayer's philosophy: "one ought always to buy the very best of everything and not to mind the price"

### Musical Patronage

- **Nathan** (1828): post-prandial performance by **Ignaz Moscheles**
- **James**: **Rossini**, **Chopin** (Paris career reportedly launched at rue Laffitte in 1832)
- Other performers: **Mendelssohn**, **Liszt**, **Charles Hallé**, **Joseph Joachim**
- Anthony on Liszt's 1842 recital: "as curious to look at as to hear, with his long hair at times streaming over his face"
- Piano was "the nearest thing the 19th century had to the television" — female Rothschilds expected to excel
- **Charlotte**'s *livre d'or*: lessons from Moscheles, Mendelssohn, Bellini, Spohr, Rossini, Meyerbeer, Cherubini, Chopin
- **Chopin** dedicated **Waltz Op. 64 No. 2** and **Ballade Op. 52** to the family
- **Hannah Mathilde** composed piano pieces, an orchestral waltz, and songs; her "Si vous n'avez rien à me dire" performed at the **Paris Opera** by **Adelina Patti**
- Even **Richard Wagner** — who demonised "Jewry in Music" — effectively banked with the Rothschilds (wife **Cosima** held a Paris account)

### Heine and Balzac

**Heinrich Heine**:
- Nephew of Hamburg banker **Salomon Heine**; had early negative encounter with Nathan (reportedly told him he "had no talent for business"); by 1834 cultivated close relationship with French Rothschilds
- Grillparzer observed: "It was apparent that his hosts were afraid of Heine, and he exploited their fear by slyly poking fun at them"
- Famous jests at James's expense (James as slow-witted straight man):
  - On wine called "Lachrymae Christi": "Christ weeps when rich Jews can afford such good wines while so many poor folk go hungry"
  - James: "Comment trouvez-vous mon chenil?" — Heine: "Don't you know that *chenil* means dog-kennel?"
  - On the Seine's polluted state: James notes the source is clear — Heine: "Yes, M. le baron; and I hear that your late father was a most honest man"
- Reality behind the jests: correspondence shows Heine increasingly a **humble supplicant**
- In *Ludwig Börne*: called James's financial capital as socially revolutionary as Richelieu and Robespierre — far from insulting
- In *Augsburger Zeitung* articles (early 1840s): repeatedly praised James, compared him favourably to banker **Benoit Fould**
- June 1843 "Lutetia" piece: likened James to the sun — everyone pays homage hoping to be warmed by golden rays

> "I counsel every man who is in dire need of money to go to M. de Rothschild; not in order to borrow from him (I doubt whether he would have much success!) but to comfort himself with the sight of the misery money can cause." — Heine, "Lutetia"

- Financial dependence deepened after uncle **Salomon Heine**'s death (December 1844): allowance halved by cousin Carl; bitter family wrangle until 1847
- James involved Heine in railway share speculation earning 20,000 francs (1846); offered "most preferential treatment" in French loan (1847)
- By 1852 a **Schnorrer** (beggar), writing abject letters linking requests to James's financial successes; received Creditanstalt shares from Anselm; dead within two months of last request
- Key insight from Heine's "Retrospective Explanation": friendship endured only because he **never asked for money** — "Had I done so, our friendship would soon have been at an end"
- Role of **Betty de Rothschild**: Heine conducted "elegant flirtation" with her; signed himself "your faithful protege"; called her "earliest patroness in Paris"; friendship faded in 1840s as money corrupted the relationship

**Honoré de Balzac**:
- Mirror image of Heine: caricatured James with only perfunctory disclaimers; where Heine decorously flirted with Betty, Balzac tried to palm off an old mistress on James; where Heine agonised over share options, Balzac happily borrowed and avoided repaying
- Famous aside (1837): encountering Heine and James in the street — "*C'est-a-dire tout l'esprit et tout l'argent des Juifs*" — it was the money that interested him more; characteristically misspelt "Rostchild"
- First met at **Aix** (summer 1832); James offered letters of introduction and courier use
- 1844: dedicated *Un Homme d'affaires* to James; James reciprocated with **150 shares in the Northern railway** (1846)
- Balzac borrowed 17,000 francs against those shares; also ~50,000 francs mortgaging fiancée's Polish estate to buy house in **rue Fortunee**
- After 1848 relations strained: James refused a 2,500 franc draft; Balzac attempted crude fraud (payment rerouted under mother's maiden name)
- Balzac acknowledged Rothschilds' post-1848 position: "The House of Rothschild, like beavers after a thunderstorm, has to occupy itself with repairing the disasters which 1848 has wrought"
- James walked behind Balzac's coffin (August 1850) alongside Victor Hugo and Dumas; 32 years later Rothschilds bought the rue Fortunee house from his widow for **10x** the original price

**Baron Nucingen** (Balzac's fictional banker):
- Balzac told his future wife (1844): James was "Nucingen to the last detail, and worse"
- Introduced in *Le Père Goriot* (1834-5): German accent, lives ostentatiously
- Key to Nucingen's success: a succession of **bogus suspensions of payment** — manufactured bankruptcies
- Underlying moral of *La Maison Nucingen*: "the debtor is stronger than the creditor"
- Ferguson: "Nucingen *is* James, to the extent that Balzac could never have created the former had he never known the latter"

## Chapter 13: Quicksilver and Hickory (1834-1839)

<!-- pp. 501-540 -->

### Iberian Dilemmas

- Iberian politics: dynastic civil wars, not ideological revolutions
- Rothschilds' core fear: other powers becoming embroiled, triggering a general European war
- **Spanish and Portuguese bonds** played same role in 1830s as Latin American bonds had in 1820s
- Root cause: both countries lost transatlantic empire revenues → chronic insolvency

**Portugal — The Less Complex, Less Lucrative Story**:
- Nathan had lent to both sides in the coming civil war: **Dom Miguel** (whose 1828 coup he backed) and **Dom Pedro** (Emperor of Brazil, father of **Queen Maria II**)
- April 1831: Pedro forced to abdicate in Brazil, set off to restore his daughter; by May 1834 Miguel surrendered, aided by English sea captain **Charles Napier**
- Pedro died four months after restoring Maria — lived just long enough
- Political instability continued: Maria's supporters split into **"Chartists"** (1826 constitution) and **"Septembrists"** (1822 constitution); coups in 1837 and 1842
- By 1832 **James** began participating in operations of Spanish financier **Mendizabal**; pessimistic from start — called it "Portuguese rubbish"
- 1835: Rothschilds issued **£4 million loan** — knowingly dealing in **junk bonds**; bonds issued at 67.5, fell to 55 within a year, by 1839 stood at just 25
- James: "We have a great many asses who have been buying this shit"
- James's rationale for staying: these bonds were "the only thing on which one can gamble and speculate... one can never hold on to them" — pure speculation, not investment

> "Your Portuguese are giving me a fever. Never before in my life has anything upset me so much. These people are nothing more than the scum of the earth." — James, December 1836

- Trap: if they always covered interest, bondholders would expect it indefinitely — "you will be unable to retreat"
- Led to "a protracted and highly embarrassing legal wrangle" with Portuguese government

**Spain — More Complex, More Promising**:
- Dynastic question: did **Salic law** apply? If so, Ferdinand VII's brother **Don Carlos** was heir; if not, his daughter **Isabella** (born 1830) would reign
- Isabella's legal case was stronger — Salic law had been secretly repealed by **Carlos IV** in the **Pragmatic Sanction of 1789**
- Ferdinand died September 1833; Carlos returned ten months later — civil war inevitable
- Ideological overlay: **Carlism** strongest in rural Navarre and Basque provinces; Isabella's cause appealed to Madrid bureaucrats and commercial interests
- Four reasons to back Isabella: (1) short-run bond profits from high-yield paper (2) **Quadruple Alliance of 1834** (Britain, Portugal, Spain, France) (3) **Almadén mercury mines** — one of only two major world mercury sources, essential for refining silver/gold in Latin America (the Fuggers had held them in the 16th century) (4) Cuba tobacco trade
- Three reasons for caution: (1) protracted civil war — Carlists not defeated until 1839; repeated government changes between **Moderados** and **Progresistas** (2) "Cortes bonds" — issued under liberal regime of 1820s, repudiated by Ferdinand; English holders blocked new Spanish paper on London exchange (3) Metternich's pressure through Salomon
- **James** committed the Rothschilds to share all Spanish business with a Paris consortium led by **Aguado** — provided camouflage but awkward restrictions
- Spain placed greater strain on family harmony than any other pre-1848 issue — threatened to break up the partnership

**Nathan's Unilateral Plunge (April 1834)**:
- Nathan ignored warnings from Metternich, Austrian ambassador Apponyi, Russian ambassador Pozzo, and three French ministers
- April 18: unilaterally proposed advancing Spanish government **15 million francs** to pay June interest — no firm guarantee on Cortes bonds, no security
- Wellington sardonically noted they were now "in the boat"; it "began to sink almost at once"
- Public reaction: two cartoons portrayed Nathan as a "**Jew-dish-us cakeman**" selling a "rice cake" marked "Loan" — "Who's for a slice? All Hot! All Hot!"
- Nathan's probable motive: pre-empt rivals (**Thomas Wilson** or Aguado) and establish himself as court banker ahead of a major new loan
- French consortium bankers promptly sued James; had to offer Aguado a share of all future loans
- New Finance Minister **Toreno** reneged — claimed Rothschilds promised twice the amount; planned 75% debt reduction — Rothschilds called it "declaration of bankruptcy"
- Bond prices plummeted, causing suicides and murder threats on the Paris bourse
- James's fundamental weakness: "My dear Nathan, we don't have any troops to force the Government to do that which it does not want to do"
- **Lionel** sent to Madrid (accompanied by lawyer **Adolphe Cremieux**) to negotiate
- Salomon estimated losses on the whole contract at **1.6 million francs**
- By 1837: Rothschilds had more or less completely withdrawn from Spanish bond market; Nathan's last instructions to his sons were to liquidate all Spanish holdings
- Spanish bonds trading as low as 19 — James called them "muck" or "shit"
- As late as 1840 — after Don Carlos was defeated — James still refused to lend under the Rothschild name without a guarantee from England and France

> "You know my dear Papa how he [James] is — one minute he is for and one minute he is against the Business." — Lionel

**Metternich's Leverage**:
- Metternich used intelligence from Austrian charge **Hummelauer** to stay informed of Nathan's actions
- Nathan — responding to Salomon's pressure — leaked an Anglo-French guaranteed loan plan to Vienna, effectively killing it
- Nathan told Palmerston he had "no confidence in the solvency of Mendizabal's government"
- When Palmerston noted planned crown land sales would raise money, Nathan replied: "Yes, in time, but not in time for the May dividend. It is like telling me at seven o'clock when I want my dinner [that] there is a calf feeding in a field a mile off"

**The Almadén Prize**:
- Lionel outbid four rivals — essentially by **bribing Toreno and the Queen** and offering 5% more than highest rival bid
- Mines producing 16,000-18,000 hundredweight of mercury/year in 1835
- Paid 54.5 pesetas (£2.18)/hundredweight; resold at 76-80 in London, up to 150 in Mexico
- Minimum profit: **£13,000/year**; when production stepped up in 1838, annual income rose to **£32,000** — over 13% of London house total net revenue, 38% of profits (half shared with Paris)
- By 1840s, **20% return** was James's target from Almadén
- Strategic pivot: instead of bonds for Spain, Rothschilds financed government through short-run advances on mercury royalties; later extended to copper and Cuba tobacco
- **Commodities proved the best security for loans to unstable states**

**Mercury Contract Renewal Crisis (1847)**:
- Rising purchase price (54.5 to 70 pesetas) + falling world prices after **Lionel Davidson** confirmed Mexican deposits (1845) = deadlock
- Spanish government demanded £600,000 advance; Rothschilds refused without better terms
- James: "If one could earn 20 per cent then I would be all in favour, but as matters stand we stand to make only a trivial sum"

**Salomon's Letter to Metternich** (intended for Metternich's eyes):
> "My brother Nathan Mayer is one of the ablest men as far as the Exchequer and price movements are concerned but has no special aptitude in other matters... he is a child in politics... apart from [the Bourse], between ourselves, he can hardly spell his own name."

### "That Blasted Country": America

- Ferguson calls the failure to establish a US base the **single greatest strategic error**
- **Real obstacles**: (1) minimal federal borrowing needs (2) anti-bank populism (3) no reliable central bank partner
- **Andrew Jackson** killed the Second BUS (1832): "The Bank is trying to kill me, but I will kill it"
- Anti-Semitic hostility: **Governor McNutt of Mississippi** denounced "Baron Rothschild" with "blood of Judas and Shylock"

**August Belmont — The Accidental Agent**:
- Born **Schönberg**; joined Frankfurt house as apprentice at 15, risen rapidly; sent to America in 1837 ostensibly to assess the crisis and then proceed to Havana
- James's real priority was Cuba: "Spain receives all her income from that land and it is one of the most profitable business ventures" — US commitments written off as "a lost cause"
- Belmont defied orders: instead of going to Havana, set up at **78 Wall Street**, announcing **August Belmont & Co.** as Rothschild agent

> "He is a stupid young man... and we are not so desperate for new business... Instead of going to Philadelphia to collect the 300,000 francs from Cohen he says, 'I shall remain in New York.' Such an ass needs to be kept on a short leash." — James

- James spent months trying to persuade nephews **Anthony** or **Anselm** to go; no one wanted to; fundamental problem: **London house** (not Paris) conducted most US business because Britain was bigger buyer of American cotton/tobacco
- Belmont leveraged his position, threatened to switch to rival houses if denied; raised salary to £500, got £10,000 credit line
- Rothschilds "never quite forgave" him: fought a duel (1841), converted to Christianity, built political career leading the **Democrat Party**
- **Lionel Davidson** sent to New York (1839) to control Belmont; eventually redirected to Mexico; **Hanau** sent to New Orleans (1843)

**BUS Disaster**:
- After Barings dropped BUS as European agent (1836-7), Rothschilds eagerly stepped in; James called BUS "the wealthiest people in America"
- Initial flow of American state bonds — New York, Indiana, Alabama, Missouri, Michigan — plus bank and canal shares
- By September 1839 BUS clearly over-extended: cotton crop failure, capital tied up in long-term ventures
- BUS agent **Samuel Jaudon** warned he couldn't meet acceptances; Rothschild advances reached £300,000; reluctantly took over BUS drafts on Hottinguer worth 5.5m francs (£220,000)
- **Lionel** argued caveat emptor; **Nat** blamed James for the whole mess — Paris house had accepted Jaudon's bills over London's objections
- October 1839: BUS suspended payments; 1841: collapsed, triggering state defaults on bonds Jaudon had used as security

> "You may tell your government that you have seen the man who is at the head of the finances of Europe, and that he has told you that you cannot borrow a dollar, not a dollar." — James to US Treasury

- **Anthony (1842)**: "let us get rid of that blasted country... it is the most blasted & the most stinking country in the world"

### The 1836-9 Crisis and Bank of England Feud

- American crisis strained Rothschild-Bank of England relations to breaking point
- London house owed BoE at least £300,000 (borrowed December 1835) + £120,000 short-term advance (June 1836)
- James committed to supplying Banque de France with silver while sending gold to London only belatedly
- James alarmed by rumour France considering switch to **silver standard**; when BoE began buying silver (summer 1837), James threatened to stop sending gold
- James's advice — "under no circumstances should he allow any American House to collapse" — was ignored
- Former BoE Governor **Timothy Curtis** wrote conciliatory letter; by March James convinced "your English Bank is out to destroy our business"
- 1839: BoE reserves reduced to £3.7m; turned to Banque de France for help
- James initially wholly opposed — argued Banque lending to BoE in peacetime was dangerous; refused to participate
- **Anselm's** warning invoked Nathan's legacy: "do not trust the Bank without any guarantees... you are not your good father"
- **Hannah** similarly cautious: "I would not advance any thing without having Exchequer Bills or other Government security"
- James's boycott failed: deal between Curtis and **d'Argout** proceeded without Rothschilds; Barings and Paris consortium got the business
- James forced into humiliating volte-face: "they are stronger than us" — could not start a "war with the Bank"
- James fantasized about revenge against Curtis (canceling a private £5,000 loan)
- **1843**: Lionel closed the Rothschild account at the Bank of England
- Net result of James's American enthusiasm: large bad debts from defunct BUS and lasting bad feeling with the BoE

## Chapter 14: Between Retrenchment and Rearmament (1840)

<!-- pp. 541-580 -->

### The Paradox of Peace

- Governments that heeded Rothschild advice and avoided war also stopped needing loans
- After 1833 major powers "effectively ceased to be Rothschild clients"
- **Prussia**: debt service fell from 22% of expenditure (1821) to 11% (1850)
- **Britain**: 1835 slave-owner compensation was the last major loan before Crimean War

### Peel's Fiscal Revolution

- Four elements: (1) debt conversion (3.5% → 3.25%) (2) income tax reintroduced at 7d/£ (3) Bank Charter reform (4) trade liberalisation → Corn Law repeal
- Sustained fiscal surpluses → Rothschilds marginalised from British sovereign finance
- **Peel** conceived income tax partly to "reach such men as Baring, his father, Rothschild"
- **Nat** foresaw difficulty: "How will tax collectors ascertain the real incomes of merchants & bankers?"
- Biggest concern: if **foreign investment income taxed**, bondholders switch to domestic — catastrophic for capital exporters
- **James** feared income tax spreading to France — ending the golden age when governments *borrowed* from capitalists rather than *taxed* them

### Rothschild Reputation by the Early 1840s

- **Alexandre Weill** (1844): while "Rothschild" had *needed* the states to become "Rothschild," he now no longer needed them
- **Jules Michelet** (1842): James knew "Europe prince by prince, and the bourse courtier by courtier"
- States borrowed: conservative states to avoid parliamentary oversight; progressive states (Belgium, German states) to fund railways

### Lean Years and Russian Loan Failure

- No new Austrian loans; France's debt actually *reduced* by 169m francs
- Russia: **Kankrin's currency reforms** (1839, 1843) created demand for a stabilisation loan
- James eagerly proposed floating it in London and Paris: investing in Russia would make wealthy people "suffer a financial loss if ever such an unfortunate idea as to march into Russia" — a peace insurance mechanism
- Repeated failure: Rothschilds offered terms too low; refused firm underwriting
- "Once it had seemed that the Rothschilds no longer needed the great powers; now it began to seem that the powers no longer needed the Rothschilds"

### Spreading the Net: German States

- Response to diminished great-power business: James and Salomon moved into **industrial finance** (railways); Frankfurt house targeted **smaller German states**
- Frankfurt house transactions: Saxe-Coburg-Gotha (3.5m gulden, 1837-42), Nassau (9.9m), Hesse-Kassel (6.7m **lottery loan**, 1845), Baden (14m gulden, 1845), Bavaria (1835 — Carl and Amschel appointed "court bankers"), Württemberg, Denmark (£3m, 1835)
- **Prussia** was the exception: debt-to-revenue ratio *fell* 1825-1850 due to 1819 State Debt Decree restricting borrowing; all other German states saw ratios rise (railway construction debt)
- Minor transactions demanded as much effort as great-power loans, due to pressure from representative bodies
- Dominance so complete that locals talked of "**Rothschild shipments**" and "**Rothschild money**"
- Liberals saw Rothschilds as propping up reactionary regimes; **Johann Stüve** (Hanoverian liberal, 1848) sought to avoid "dirty transactions with Rothschild"
- Politically indifferent: lent to constitutional Baden and reactionary Hanover (after King Ernst August abrogated its constitution in 1837) alike — both got loans
- Also lent to aristocratic clients: Hungarian aristocrats including **Prince Paul Esterhazy**, Count Moric Sandor — 12.3m gulden (1843-45) from Salomon's Vienna associates
- These loans analogous to Coutts lending to English aristocracy — land-rich, cash-poor grandees
- Political embarrassment: Hungary would soon plunge into secessionist war with Austria — Rothschilds had financial ties to both sides

### Italy and Belgium

### Italy: Naples, the Papacy, Piedmont

- Continued dominance in **Two Sicilies** (Bourbon regime); James on Neapolitan government: "Your Finance Minister is not a man you can reliably count on. He is a real blackguard"
- **Papal finances**: Salomon used leverage over Rome's debt to protest ill-treatment of the **Jewish community of Rome** through Metternich — feeding the perception (in **Alfred de Vigny's** words) that "A Jew now reigns over the Pope and Christianity"
- Ferguson: primary concern was profit, not reform of the Papal regime

**Piedmont-Sardinia Negotiation (1834)**:
- James sent **Lionel** to Turin; correspondence reveals Rothschild negotiating doctrine:
- James's instructions: "our competitors *come to you (for in no case must you go to them yourself)*... the business must be done by you in *our sole name*"
- On losing the deal: "*If the business be in the least feasible, do it, even if it should give no profit whatever, even if it were necessary to lose 2 or 3 hundred thousand francs to prove that we are not afraid of a sacrifice when we want to baffle them*"
- Lionel ultimately outmaneuvered by the "**Bande Noire**" of French bankers led by **Hagermann**
- **Belgium**: Best case study of pre-1848 Rothschild strategy
- After Belgian secession from Dutch rule (1830), James moved swiftly — lifeline of credit in stormy first three years
- Key instrument: state loans to finance **King Leopold I's** railway network — Belgium was breaking British precedent by having government finance railways (strategic: needed to avoid dependence on Dutch-controlled canals)
- Rothschild preference: state bonds always less risky than private railway shares
- **Belgian Crisis of 1838-39**: the 1832 articles required Belgium to evacuate **Luxembourg** and **Limburg** and split pre-1830 Dutch debt; Belgium resisted
- James eager for new 36m-franc loan — first major bond issue since Nathan's death — to assert dominance
- Belgian coal bubble burst in 1838: nearly broke the **Banque de Belgique** and pressured **Societe Generale** — James stepped in, bailed both out
- James had war-escape clause inserted in the loan agreement; Belgium could not get "a farthing" until it accepted the settlement
- After settlement, James aimed at total monopoly: "the arrangement of the Belgian question is going to be followed by a need for money and this will be a moment we ought to exploit in order to make ourselves the **absolute masters of the finances of this country**"
- By 1840 James in Brussels personally: "I lectured them about how to put themselves on a better footing... They are content enough to let us guide them"
- Belgium raised **five major loans 1830-1844**, combined ~300m francs — almost all Rothschild-underwritten
- When James fell out with a Belgian minister in 1842, he instructed Lionel to visit **King Leopold** at Windsor and subtly indicate "there will be no market anywhere for Belgian bonds" if that minister remained
- Classic tactic: when Belgium tried direct public subscription, Nat and James quietly sold Belgian bonds to make markets "flat" — government returned cap in hand
- French ambassador confirmed: without Rothschild, Belgium "realised that it would be impossible for it to find a penny on any bourse, domestic or foreign"
- **Dutch operations**: Anselm visited the Hague (1840); acted on behalf of both Belgium and Holland in same international transfer — characteristic arbitrage role; Dutch Finance Minister told Anselm "I wish most sincerely to do alone with you, if only I could save my reputation of an honest man" — forced to resign under opposition pressure

### The Eastern Question

- The **"Eastern Question"**: could the sprawling **Ottoman Empire** be preserved intact? Economically backward, religiously divided, administratively ramshackle
- Rothschilds vital because money was always needed — fundamental problem was the **chronic narrowness of the tax base**
- **Greek independence**: bonds guaranteed by Britain, France, Russia — 60m francs; 11m to pay Turkey as indemnity (through Rothschilds)
- November 1831: **Mehemet Ali** (Pasha of Egypt) revolted against Sultan **Mahmud II** — son **Ibrahim** invaded Syria, seizing Gaza, Jerusalem, Damascus
- Sultan turned to Russia: **Treaty of Unkiar Skelessi** (1833) — secret article binding Sultan to close Black Sea Straits to warships
- 1836-7: France resumes colonisation of Algeria; **Palmerston** steers Britain toward pro-Turkish policy
- When war re-erupts (April 1839), France backs Mehemet Ali; Britain and Russia cut a deal to force Ali out of Syria

### The Damascus Blood Libel (February 1840)

- **February 5, 1840**: Sardinian Capuchin friar **Father Tommaso** and servant vanished in Damascus; last seen in Jewish quarter
- French consul **Ratti-Menton** fanned ritual murder allegations; pressured Egyptian governor to arrest Jews and torture confessions
- Pattern: "As with early modern witch-hunts, the more bizarre the story grew, the greater the number implicated"
- ~70 arrested; children taken hostage; one Jew tortured to death; barber lashed 500 times
- Arrest of **Isaac de Picciotto** (Austrian subject) transformed it into an international incident
- Austrian consul-general **Anton Laurin** sent reports to James (who was Austrian consul-general in Paris)
- By March, Constantinople and Dutch Jewish communities writing to the Rothschilds appealing to "the tie which so strongly binds together the whole Jewish community"

> "the only means we have left is the all-powerful method here of calling in the newspapers to our assistance" — James, April 7, 1840

- James planted detailed account in *Journal des Debats* and *Allgemeine Zeitung*; enlisted **Adolphe Cremieux**
- **Nat** proposed funding Cremieux's Middle East trip; **Lionel** attended Board of Deputies meeting
- Rothschilds contributed **£2,500** toward Cremieux-Montefiore expedition to Alexandria
- **Salomon** persuaded Metternich to press the Vatican; **Carl** loaded Montefiore's ship
- **Thiers** (just become premier) doubled down: told James "the case is based on truth... the Jews in the East still maintain such superstitions"
- Montefiore mission succeeded: Mehemet Ali issued *firman* (August 28) denying ritual murder; prisoners freed
- Ferguson: Rothschilds *were* sincerely outraged, but **James and Salomon attached more importance to the diplomatic ramifications** — the affair was an ideal tool to undermine Thiers
- Wild speculation (from 1830) that Rothschilds aimed to "reclaim the Holy Land"; **Fourier**: Rothschilds should "lead the Hebrews back to Jerusalem"
- Ferguson: **no evidence** the Rothschilds harboured Zionist intentions; "a much later development"
- **Lionel** privately uneasy about the "rumpus" made by Cremieux; Montefiore sent partly "to moderate his zeal"

### Toppling Thiers: The Financial Mechanism

- 3% rentes fell from **87 (July)** to **73.5 (October)** as British naval expedition closed on Ibrahim Pasha
- Rothschilds did **not** cause the fall — but they had already cleared out of French bonds entirely
- Financial logic: Thiers needed to borrow for military preparations, but with rentes collapsing he could not
- James's reply to *Constitutionnel* attack: "If I desire peace, I desire it honourably... *Financiers have the opportunity of rendering services to the country under any circumstances*" — the menace was unmistakable
- **October 20, 1840**: Thiers resigns; new government formed by **Soult and Guizot**

### Rearmament Loans 1841-1847

- Eastern Crisis demonstrated: tension could be *profitable* — provided it didn't escalate to war
- Thiers' legacy (Paris fortifications) obliged Soult government to issue a major new loan
- Finance Minister **Humann** (James privately: "a rogue and a blackguard"); James drove a hard bargain — repeatedly threatened to walk away
- **150 million franc** French loan (October 1841); further loans in 1842 and 1844 (200m each); competitors Hottinguer, Baring, Laffitte couldn't dislodge dominance
- **Austrian rearmament**: **Anselm**: "As long as France continues arming, Germany must follow" — 38.5m gulden loan (1841, shared with Sina and Arnstein & Eskeles); further 40m gulden two years later
- "Once the Rothschilds had regarded peace as the *sine qua non*; but an armed peace was more profitable"
- James's power: **Countess Nesselrode** thought him "viceroy and even King" — he told her Louis Philippe "has complete confidence in me, listens and takes account of everything I say"
- Rothschild financial support for Guizot **conditional on avoiding outright conflict with Britain** and devoting loan proceeds to railways rather than fortifications

### The Spanish Marriages Crisis (1846-7)

- **Louis-Philippe** planned to marry his son (Duc de Montpensier) to the Spanish queen's sister — Palmerston treated it as potential *casus belli*
- James shuttled back and forth; Guizot told James "very angrily" to keep his opinions to himself
- James: "Never before have I seen the Government so strong and stubborn. I think that even if this were to lead to an outbreak of war, God forbid, they would still not change their stand"
- Crisis resolved peacefully by February 1847

### Limits of Financial Power

- Belgium and Syria crises seemed to prove that "war could be averted by discreet manipulation of purse-strings"
- But Ferguson warns: **financial power was not absolute** — depended on *internal stability* of states
- "It was not a war which brought the defensive walls of the July Monarchy tumbling down but a revolution; and against that threat the fortifications round Paris offered no protection"

## Chapter 15: "Satan Harnessed": Playing at Railways (1830-1846)

<!-- pp. 581-620 -->

### Railways as Creative Destruction

- **Friedrich List**: railway is "a tonic for the national spirit" and "a tight belt around the loins of the German nation"
- **Metternich** regarded railways with deep unease: threatened the Central European status quo

### Nathan's Absence from British Railways

- Nathan took virtually no part in industrial finance once established as a London banker
- Exception: abortive North Wales mining venture in 1825 (lost money)
- Nathan took no part in the **Liverpool-Manchester Railway** (opened 1830)
- Nathan's principle (told to **Thomas Fowell Buxton's** son Edward): "If I were to listen to all the projects proposed to me, I should ruin myself very soon. Stick to one business, young man. Stick to your brewery, and you may be the great brewer of London. Be a brewer, and a banker, and a merchant, and a manufacturer, and you will soon be in the Gazette."
- **Hannah** was the sole railway enthusiast among English Rothschilds — evangelised travel's advantages; **Nat** declined to meet Lionel at Calais because "railway travelling makes my head ache so terribly"
- James visiting northern England (1843): "What is happening here with the railways is quite amazing and had I come here earlier we would most certainly have built this railway. It must yield enormous sums. Never have I seen so many people as [are travelling] between Liverpool and Manchester"
- Rothschilds viewed **railway shares as surrogate state bonds** at a time when governments issued fewer bonds
- But fundamentally different: bonds = predictable interest; railway shares = unspecified future dividends based on traffic guesswork

### Salomon's Nordbahn (Austria)

- First Rothschild railway conceived in **Habsburg territory** — paradoxical given Salomon's conservatism
- Salomon personally disliked railway travel (still refused Belgian rail link as late as 1846)
- Idea from **Franz Xavier Riepel** (Vienna Polytechnic) — proposed linking salt/coal mines of Galicia/Moravia to Vienna, over 200 miles, plus extension south to Trieste
- Initial petition shelved; Crown Prince quipped: "Even the coach to Kagran isn't always full"
- Salomon took over existing horse-drawn railway from insolvent French engineer **Zola** (the novelist's father)
- Named line **"Kaiser-Ferdinands-Nordbahn"** to neutralise opposition; enlisted **Metternich**, **Kolowrat**, **Count Mittrowsky** as board members (nobles lending respectability for financial perks)
- Projected cost 12m gulden; actual ~27,750 gulden/mile vs. projected 16,600 — massive overrun
- Share issue spectacular: 12,000 shares (1,000 gulden each) attracted **27,490 applications**
- Competing banker **Sina** immediately sought Vienna-Trieste concession; **Pereira** (Arnstein & Eskeles) launched financial critique
- Boardroom battle October 1836: Salomon won 76 of 83 votes, forced Sina and Eskeles to resign
- Early years: financial drain — 14,000 workers for zero return; Rothschild advance of **8m gulden**; shares below par
- Turning point from 1841: unexpectedly high passenger volumes (Sunday day-trippers) — up to **10,000 people per day** on Vienna-Neustadt stretch
- Shares: par → 103 (1843) → 129 (1844) → **228 (1845)** — enormous capital gain

### Vertical Integration

- Salomon envisaged **integrated Austrian transport system**: Galicia/Moravia → Vienna → Trieste → Hungary
- His 1844 trip to Pressburg treated as near-royal visit; Danube steamer made unscheduled stop; local Jewish community gathered on riverbank
- **Witkowitz Ironworks**: first bid blocked because Jews prohibited from owning land; leased indirectly via Archbishop of Olmütz; **first Habsburg works to use puddling process** for rail production; remained core Rothschild asset for nearly a century
- Co-founded **Austrian Steamship Company (Austrian Lloyd)** in 1835; bailed it out with 500,000 gulden; profits grew from ~82,000 to ~370,000 gulden (1841-1847)

### Frankfurt: The Taunusbahn

- Frankfurt house played **modest role** — southern Germany's fragmented states meant even short railway lines crossed multiple jurisdictions
- The Frankfurt-Mainz line (under 20 miles) spanned three separate states; took **17 months** just to secure land purchase legislation in Hesse-Kassel
- Bavaria, Baden, Württemberg, and Hanover built railways on the Belgian model (state-funded) — Rothschilds could only underwrite bonds, not lead projects
- **Anselm** (1838): "here in Germany, railways get off the ground only with a great deal of effort"
- **Oppenheims** and **Bethmanns** led private railway building in southwest Germany; Rothschilds participated as sleeping partners only
- Oppenheim led Rhineland consortium at ~25% of 3m thaler capital; Rothschilds (Paris + Frankfurt combined) held only a fortieth
- James politely rebuffed a Florence-Leghorn proposal: "We have enough railways of our own [in France] without embarking on those of Italy as well"

### Paris: James and the Pereires

- **Emile** and **Isaac Pereire**: Sephardic Jews from Bordeaux, disciples of **Saint-Simon**
- Their pilot project (Paris to Saint-Germain) cost over 100x what brothers could muster (they had 30,000 francs)
- Political backing from **Emile Legrand** (Director-General of Ponts et Chaussees) and the skeptical **Thiers** (who dismissed railways as a "toy")
- Initial capital consortium: **d'Eichthal**, **Thurneyssen**, **Davillier**, James — d'Eichthal and James each held 23.5% of 5m franc capital
- Saint-Germain line: cost 11m francs vs. projected 3.9m; traffic fluctuated wildly (surge on summer Sundays, collapse on winter weekdays); nonetheless completed **ahead of schedule** August 1837
- Pereires reduced running costs from 52% to 44% of gross receipts in three years

> "I am absolutely convinced that people in France simply have no idea how easy it is to travel by such means... I am certain that in twenty years' time there won't be a single Postmaster left in the world, and people will only travel by train... I am in love with the railway." — James to Anthony, June 1836

- **Rive Droite** (Paris-Versailles via right bank): Rothschild largest shareholder at ~1/3 of 11m franc capital
- **Fould brothers** secured rival left-bank concession — Heine mocked the rivalry between "the Chief Rabbi of the Rive Droite" and "Chief Rabbi of the Rive Gauche"

### The Rive Droite and Rival Competition

- Pereires' ambition: concentrate all Paris rail traffic through Saint-Germain and **Gare Saint-Lazare**
- **Fould brothers** secured rival concession for parallel line to Versailles along the *left* bank
- Heine mocked the rivalry between "the Chief Rabbi of the Rive Droite, Baron Rothschild" and the "Chief Rabbi of the Rive Gauche":

> "For the French Jews, as for the French as a whole, gold is the god of our times and industry the prevailing religion... it is possible to divide the Jews of this country into two sects." — Heine

- Two parallel lines economically irrational — insufficient traffic for both; returns meagre; 1838 slump forced merger; Fould company absorbed

### The Nord Concession

- James used near-monopoly on rentes issues as explicit leverage: "If we get the loan then we are masters over the railways. The Finance Minister said to me today, 'I won't do anything without you'"
- James threatened French government openly: if Rive Droite not supported, "I will see to it that it is attacked in all the newspapers"; threatened to publicly resign: "If you can't gain someone's friendship then you have to make them fear you"
- **Legrand's Law of 1842**: state purchases rights of way and constructs buildings, leasing to companies who provide rolling stock — the framework James had long sought
- Nord concession finally awarded **September 1845**: Paris and London houses held **25.7%** of 200m franc capital; Hottinguer 20.15%, Laffitte-Blount 19.5%
- Pereires' preferred terminus (Gare Saint-Lazare) jettisoned; rivals given substantial stakes but all as junior partners
- **Laffitte** had hoped to lead a single grand loan for entire Legrand network; instead became sleeping partner — "effectively admitting defeat yet again"
- James saw various lines as interconnected: "the various railways have to be viewed as cousins of each other and when one rises the others will then follow"
- By November 1845 railway **merger mania** in full swing; bankers proposing to collectively acquire "the whole line of railroads from Calais to Avignon... to avoid competition"
- **Rothschild dominance**: their name in only 4 of 32 concessions, but of 225m francs contributed by French banks, Rothschilds accounted for **84.6 million — 38% of total**
- Key advantage: **direct familial link to London market** — roughly half of all French railway capital by 1847 was British

### The London Nephews' Ambivalence

- London brothers never lost deep suspicion of industrial finance
- **Anthony** (1838): "There is nothing new here — but Railroad Companies... there are so many that one gets quite sick of them"
- **Nat** openly hostile to Nord project: "The stinking railroads engross all our attention & for my part I wish his Majesty had them all"
- Nat's core objection: **operating** railways locked up capital in illiquid, dangerous ways
- **James** by contrast could not resist "le grand role" — driven by competitive fear of losing business
- Nat's portrait of James's psychology: "in a sort of fever about it, he thinks it a good business, & is afraid of it, he fancies the rival company will get it & then regrets it doubly"
- James recognized Nord depended on **London capital** — French market alone couldn't fund it
- By 1843 Nat's opposition softened: believed shares would "go like hot rolls" on London market
- **Vertical integration**: James invested **3m francs** in Belgian coal mines (with **Talabot brothers**), reasoning coal demand would grow with every new railway

### Strategic Divergence: Paris vs. London

- London house chose **not** to build a long-term industrial portfolio — safer short-term
- But **in the long run**, Paris house alone captured immense capital gains from industry
- This was a **profoundly important** strategic fork

### Macro Statistics

- 1828-1848: ~1,250 miles of track built in France
- Of 225m francs contributed by French banks to railway capital, Rothschilds accounted for **84.6 million — 38% of total**, nearly a tenth of all capital subscribed
- Key advantage: **direct familial link to London market** — roughly half of all French railway capital by 1847 was British; only a quarter of French locomotives were domestically produced

### Cultural Responses

- Poetaster **Hugelmann** composed "To Rothschild" for Nord inauguration — James as genius taming a dragon (the locomotive)
- Heine: "The House of Rothschild now places itself at the head of great national undertakings"
- But Heine warned railways concentrating power in a **"ruling aristocracy of money"**
- Shares issued above par meant every allocation was effectively a **"present of money"** from Rothschild to the recipient
- **Prosper Merimee** joke: rabbi asks boy the difference between good and bad *action* (share) — "A good action is an action of the Nord, a bad action is an action of the Rive Gauche"
- Betty's four-year-old **Edmond** prayed "for Papa and for the *chemin de fer du Nord*"

### Anti-Semitic Backlash

**Toussenel's *The Jews, Kings of the Epoch***:
- State pays ~100m francs for land; company advances ~60m for rails; receives ~14m annual income; state borrowing directly would have cost only 2.4m/year
- Socialist critique: profits came from "labour of the French worker and artisan"
- But economic argument **inextricably linked to visceral anti-Semitism**: "Jew" = **"exploitative capitalist"**

**Dairnvaell (*writing as "Satan"*)**:
- *The Edifying and Curious History of Rothschild I, King of the Jews*
- Alleged Rothschilds distributed **15,000 shares to deputies** for the Nord contract
- Charges unleashed **seven separate publications** — pro and anti-Rothschild pamphlet war
- Most dangerous: anonymous work equating Judaism with capitalism outright
- **This fusion of anti-Semitism and socialism** would prove the most dangerous long-term threat
- Recycled through 1880s-1890s (Chirac, Drumont, Scherb) and even after 1945

### Fampoux Disaster (July 8, 1846)

- June 14: James invited **1,700 guests** to celebrate Nord inauguration — party lunched at Lille, dined at Brussels, returned to Paris. Cantata by **Berlioz** and **Jules Janin**; invitations to **Victor Hugo, Alexandre Dumas, Merimee, Gautier**
- Dairnvaell: the day "when the royalty of Rothschild I was officially recognised"
- **July 8** (3 p.m.): Train derailed at **Fampoux** when a rail gave way on embankment beside marshy lake. Second locomotive halted suddenly, snapping chains; 13 carriages derailed, one crushed, three sank. **14-39 people died**
- Anthony's first bulletin: "Poor people... a thousand pities — it has certainly put me out a great deal"
- True concern in same letter: revenues were 27,000 francs/day; shares fell to 712; primary preoccupation was **limiting share-price damage**
- Salomon: "a stroke of luck no person of high rank was grieved by the disaster"
- Anthony: "The shares would certainly have been 800 fr[ancs]... the receipts will very soon get up again"
- With shares falling toward 650 and receipts down 40%, Anthony floated theory the accident was "done intentionally" — tampering with rail clamps — to allow short sellers to profit
- Recovery: traffic rebuilt from third-class passengers upward; gross profits estimated at **3.2m francs**
- **Mayer** with "heroic insouciance": "on the Austrian line a dreadful accident occurred the very first day and now the shares are at 100 per cent premium"
- Ferguson's verdict: the Rothschilds' "stark economic rationality" — prioritising share price over victims — made the 1848 revolution a kind of **"terrible nemesis"**
- Railways gave Rothschilds unprecedented public prominence and made them targets for social revolution
- Contemporaries saw Vienna and Paris railway ventures in **Faustian terms**: they had "harnessed Satan" — and now Satan (Dairnvaell) had turned on Faust

## Chapter 16: "The Worst Revolution That Ever Happened" — 1848

<!-- pp. 621-680 -->

### 1830 vs. 1848: The Rothschilds as Targets

- In 1830 James had been distanced enough from Charles X to switch easily; by 1848 deeply identified with established regimes across all Europe
- **Kretschmer**'s 1848 cartoon *Apotheosis and Adoration of the Idol of our Time*: "Rothschild" on a throne of money surrounded by kneeling potentates
- Poet **Karl Beck** (1846): lamented Rothschild's refusal to use financial power on behalf of the "peoples"
- Revolution was also *social* — **socialist** arguments against economic liberalism alongside older political liberalism
- Nord railway accident crystallized this: "Rothschild I" callously counted subsidized profits while third-class passengers died
- **Alexander Herzen** (1847): defined the bourgeoisie as "a solid estate, the limits of which are the electoral property qualification below and Baron Rothschild above"
- **Karl Marx**, "On the Jewish Question" (1844): "the practical Jewish spirit has become the practical spirit of the Christian nations"
- Marx quoted **Bruno Bauer**: "The Jew, who in Vienna, for example, is only tolerated, determines the fate of the whole Empire by his financial power"
- Ferguson notes socialist argument could stand without racial prejudice; it was **Richard Wagner** and others who developed the explicitly racial version
- **Wilhelm Marr** (1843): "The time is ripe to share Rothschild's property among 3,333,333.3 poor weavers" — roots of his later **Anti-Semitic League**
- **Heine's** satirical counter: "As soon as a man runs out of money, he becomes Rothschild's enemy"
- Heine's joke adapted as James's imagined reply to communism: "The communist wants Rothschild to share his 300m francs. Rothschild sends his share: exactly 9 sous. 'Now leave me alone!'"

### Charitable Counter-Measures

- **Salomon** (Vienna): 25,000 gulden for Danube aqueduct (1835); 40,000 gulden for scientific institute in Brünn; major donations after Hamburg fire (1842) and Pesth/Ofen floods
- Strategy shift: pre-1830s charity confined to Jewish communities; now targeted causes valued by **Habsburg elite**
- Salomon portrayed in 1850s novella as a Viennese Santa Claus, but **Hermann Goldschmidt** remembered him as "a brutal egoist" who ate to excess, was habitually rude, had a lecherous passion for "very young girls," and in 1847 spent immense sums building a new Renngasse residence
- **Amschel** (Frankfurt, May 1847): distributed bread ration cards during acute food shortages; nephew **Anselm** warned against corn-dealing visibility: "there were a great many riots all & everywhere against corn dealers"

### The Irish Famine and Relief

- ~775,000 deaths, 2 million emigrated — worst calamity of the 1840s
- **Lionel** established **British Association for Relief of Extreme Distress** at New Court — raised **£470,000**
- Rothschilds contributed £1,000 (second largest after Queen's £2,000, on par with Duke of Devonshire)
- Lionel personally involved in "regulat[ing] the purchase and shipment of provisions to Ireland"
- **W. E. Forster** impressed: "Rothschild, Kinnaird, and some dozen other millionaire city princes meeting every day, and working hard. A far greater sacrifice than mere gifts of money"
- Lionel was a **Free Trader** but recognized free trade alone wouldn't solve the famine given general European cereal deficit

### Pre-Revolutionary Warning Signs

- **Salomon's disastrous Austrian loan** (February 1847): 80m gulden (with Sina and ailing Eskeles), at 2.5% and 5%; ostensibly for railways but actually for arming against Lombardy/Venetia — Salomon knew from Metternich
- Added further 3.7m gulden at 4% bonds pledged *not to sell* — keeping them "in his own safe" for 4.6% while London short rates stood at 5.85%
- **Kubeck** warned Metternich: intervention in Italy would cause "the complete breakdown of our finances"; Austria "on the verge of an abyss"
- Total Vienna commitments by February 1848: **4.35m gulden** — more than double the house's 1844 capital
- **James** secured 250m francs of 350m-franc French rentes issue; bid apparently rigged (Finance Minister **Dumon** leaked the "secret minimum"); held 170m back expecting prices to rise — another miscalculation
- **Irish Famine Loan** (March 1847): £8m co-underwritten with Barings; issue price 89.5, quickly fell to 85
- French food crisis: 1846 wheat harvest 15% below average; James purchased grain; offered to buy 5m francs of corn/flour "at our risks & peril"; backfired — widely perceived as grain speculation; rumour spread **Rothschild bread** was laced with arsenic
- In Paris "nobody can imagine anything done disinterestedly" — widely perceived James had acted as a grain speculator
- Violence in faubourg **Saint-Antoine** (May 1847) partly directed against grain merchants including James

### Italian Loans and Pre-Revolutionary Warning Signs

- **Carl** (Naples): keen to lend to Bourbon regime but saved by its "chronic indecisiveness"
- Rome: Papal deficit doubled in 1847; Roman 5% rentes below par for first time since 1834
- James intrigued by election of **Pius IX** (1846) — "supposedly a liberal" — halted Roman bond sales expecting "positive changes" (partly re: Roman Jewish community)
- Italian agent **Hecht** warned revolution was "at the eve to break out"; **Adolph's** January 1848 visit to Rome confirmed alarm
- London house's "amazingly ill-timed" suggestion of a Piedmont loan (January 1848!) rejected by **Alphonse**: "a country which can be considered as already in full revolution"
- **Belgium** — loan request also turned down, yet it proved one of the *least* affected countries

### Harbingers

- Austrian annexation of **Cracow** read as just another Polish partition — Nat: "I suppose lots of them will be shot"
- True alarm: **artisans' revolt in Sicily** (January 1848) and **Ferdinand II's** granting of a liberal constitution — Nat: "stinking news"
- Carl still cool-headed enough to discuss a Bourbon loan as late as February 19, 1848
- **Anselm** on liberal attacks against Ludwig I in Munich: "in the highest politics just as in the most lowly social relations, the people imposes its will and dictates the law to the higher power"

### The Banking Crisis of 1846-47

- Agrarian crisis → monetary crisis: grain-importing countries drained gold/silver, hitting monetary systems across Europe
- Britain: corn imports surged from 251,000 tonnes (1843) to 1,749,000 (1847); gold reserves fell from £15.8m to £9.8m
- Bank of England rates from 2.5% (March 1845) to 10% (October 1847); forced suspension of **Bank Charter Act of 1844**
- **Lionel** and **Barings' Joshua Bates** consulted by PM **Lord John Russell** — but Chancellor **Wood** was "utterly confounded at the ignorance they displayed"
- **Peel** told Wood that Lionel was not among "those who really understood the question of currency" — a telling sign of the Rothschilds' *lost influence over monetary policy* since Nathan's death
- **Banque de France** crisis from October 1846: James excluded from 25m-franc silver transaction (done by **Hottinguer**); his 5m-franc offer rejected by Governor **d'Argout** — bad blood since Nathan's death
- **Benjamin Davidson** sent to St. Petersburg with gold-laden carriages to broker France/Russia deal — failed; "effectively unable to do business as a foreign Jew"
- **Beyfus collapse**: house of **Haber** collapsed, threatening Beyfus brothers' bank (two of Mayer Amschel's daughters married Beyfuses); bail-out 1.5m gulden done "with extremely bad grace"
- **Arnstein & Eskeles** also threatened (domino from Haber); Metternich called Salomon urgently; by September 1847 Salomon held 1,520,000 gulden in Eskeles drafts
- Salomon told Anselm: "With God's help we shall remain the Rothschilds" — brothers suspected a grave mistake; they were right

### French Rentes Loan and the Eve

- French budget by 1847: 55% higher than twelve years earlier (railway subsidies)
- **James** secured 250m francs of 350m-franc nominal rentes issue, priced at 75.25 — two points below market; bid apparently rigged: Finance Minister **Dumon** leaked the "secret minimum"
- James held 170m francs back (selling only a third at once), expecting 3% rentes to rise above 77 — another miscalculation

### Eve of Revolution: Nat's Reports

- The Rothschilds had never fully trusted the July Monarchy; death of Louis Philippe's eldest son (1842) deepened pessimism — the King confided that after his death "the Revolution of 1830 would begin again"
- James received a personal death threat in 1846: "The man who wants to shoot at me could just as well shoot at the King, or vice versa"
- **Nat**, February 20, 1848 (two days before the fateful Reform banquet):

> "I think a change of ministry wd. remedy the evil but in the mean time it is impossible to say what will occur — no one can tell how a french mob will behave... I really recommend you most strongly to sell stocks & public securities of all sorts and descriptions."

- The very next day Nat was more optimistic, comparing to 1830 — underestimating how fundamentally different 1848 would prove

### The Revolution

- **Nat** picked the worst possible moment to turn optimist — Feb 23, with National Guard mutinying, still hoped a ministry change would suffice; called the emeute "not of a very serious nature"
- **Rue des Capucins massacre**: 50 demonstrators shot dead by soldiers guarding the Foreign Ministry → triggered Louis Philippe's abdication
- **Louis Philippe** fled to England; provisional government formed: **Ledru-Rollin**, **Lamartine**, socialist **Louis Blanc**, and a token worker named Albert
- Nat: "We are in the midst of the worst revolution that ever happened" — immediately sent wives and children to Le Havre

**Pan-European Spread**:
- 1848 shaped by memory of past revolutions: those recalling 1830's failures wanted a more authentic republic; those fearing the 1790s feared neo-Jacobins
- Germany: began in Baden → Hesse-Kassel, Hesse-Darmstadt, Württemberg; Bavaria's **Ludwig** forced to abdicate (reputation damaged by **Lola Montez**)
- **Frankfurt** itself threatened — 1848 posed danger to ancient republics with narrow citizenship
- Austria: **Metternich** received news via Rothschild courier; reportedly said "Eh bien, mon cher, tout est fini" — fled March 14 in disguise with barely enough money, financed by credit-note from **Salomon**
- Emperor Ferdinand replaced Metternich with arch-rival **Kolowrat**; radicals (May 15) forced Emperor to flee to Innsbruck
- Prussia: Frederick William IV capitulated March 17 after street fighting in Berlin
- Italy: Ferdinand II granted Sicily a parliament; Charles Albert and Pius IX issued constitutions; Venice and Milan revolted against Austrian rule
- Structural pattern everywhere: two overlapping revolutions — one constitutional (educated classes), one economic (artisans, workers)

### Divergent Reactions

- **Not a uniform ideological response** — attitudes ranged widely within the family

**Salomon** (Vienna):
- Comprehended the calamity only in religious terms; blamed Louis Philippe's incompetence, Princess Metternich's vanity, Palmerston's irresponsibility
- Compared 1848 to 1789, the Peasants' Wars, the Crusades, and a biblical plague of locusts

**Nat** (Paris):
- Most traumatized; suffered something like nervous collapse
- "A worse 'political cholera' never yet infected the world"
- Every letter concluded with warnings to sell all stocks and shares

**Amschel and Carl**:
- Treated revolution like a natural disaster — inexplicable but survivable
- Carl dismissed Italian nationalism as "the stupid projects of a few deranged minds"
- Amschel baffled by Frankfurt parliament's inconclusive debates — contemporary cartoon shows him asking new "Reich Trade Minister" Duckwitz: "Nothing to trade yet, Mister Minister?"

**James** (Paris):
- Most pragmatic: "all regimes were at once unreliable and financially biddable"
- Refused to let Alphonse serve in National Guard
- Shed no tears for Louis Philippe

**Next generation** (Anselm, Lionel, Mayer Carl, Alphonse):
- Occasionally expressed sympathy with liberal reforms, but distinguished sharply from socialism
- Anselm attended Frankfurt pre-parliament debates with detached interest; warmly welcomed Austrian constitution of March 1849
- **Anthony**: regarded German princes as "a set of donkeys"; Frankfurt parliament's united Germany "*Right* and reasonable"
- **Gustave** (19): rushed back to Paris, disappointed by "tristesse" and poor calibre of republican politicians

**The Women — Starkest Contrasts**:
- **Betty** (James's wife): vehemently hostile; applauded her four-year-old grandson who declared: "If I had money, I would buy a gun to shoot the republic and republicans"
- Called National Assembly deputies "the wild beasts of our great Parisian menagerie"; delighted when **Robert Blum** was shot in Vienna
- Bizarrely (given parents born in Frankfurt ghetto) expressed nostalgia for the *ancien regime*
- **Charlotte** (Lionel's wife): derived moralistic satisfaction and "historical acceleration" from the crisis; thrilled by German unification; events in "six weeks have been almost as eventful as the six years which saw Louis XVI's death, the great Terror, the Convention and Napoleon"
- Could find positive things even about French republicans: **Ledru-Rollin** "has honest intentions"
- **Louisa**: saw positive aspects to "this wonderful Revolution" — if the house could weather the storm, losses were bearable; claimed "simple indifference, or, rather, dislike to grandeur and display"

### The Threat to Property

- Male Rothschilds were "singularly cool as the bullets began to fly": James on Feb 24 walked toward the ransacked Tuileries even as gunshots continued
- James told a young National Guardsman: "My young friend, I tank you for your adfice. But tell me, vy are you here? To do your duty, isn't it? Vell then, I too, Paron de Rothschild, haf come for the same ting... mine is to go to the Minister off Finance"
- Salomon stayed in Vienna until June; Anselm until Oct 6-7 — when armed revolutionaries occupied the Rothschild office roof after lynching of Count Latour; **Moritz Goldschmidt** disguised himself as a milkman to rescue bank's papers
- Amschel never left Frankfurt; slept through a crowd gathering; hung nationalist flags; office hit by four bullets
- Salomon's villa at Suresnes ransacked; arson on Nord railway stations/bridges
- Gustave: Paris walls showing "lists where to plunder and we are mentioned as having 600,000,000 francs"
- **Railway nationalization**: most dangerous formal threat; Nord owed government 72-87m francs; Nat actually wouldn't have minded; James resisted surrendering his industrial empire

### The Financial Catastrophe

| Security | Peak | Trough | Decline |
|----------|------|--------|---------|
| Austrian 5% metallique | 112.25 (Dec 1845) | 58.00 (Mar 1848) | **-48%** |
| French 3% rente | 84.25 (Feb 1846) | 32.00 (Apr 1848) | **-62%** |
| French 5% rente | 121.00 (Jul 1846) | 50.75 (Apr 1848) | **-58%** |
| British 3% consol | — | — | **-22%** |

- James held ~170m francs of new 3% rentes; by April worth less than half — still obligated to pay French Treasury ~10m francs/month over two years
- Paris house owed ~10m francs to railway companies (Nord, Strasbourg, Grand-Combe)
- Cash reserve reduced to **little more than 1m francs** — when a clerical error made it seem even less, he joked of "giving up business, going to the country and living off potatoes"
- Rumours circulated he was smuggling gold in manure carts; police placed him under surveillance
- Vienna house: **27% of assets** in illiquid industrial shares; pending obligations ~8m gulden; owed Frankfurt ~£1.7m
- Frankfurt pressed: owed money to Württemberg, Hesse-Kassel; "fortress money" deposited by German Confederation might be withdrawn
- Each house thought the other owed it money; none could pay; Salomon pledged houses and estates (5m gulden) as security
- **Three of five houses on verge of insolvency**

> "The Rothschilds, whose wealth only two months ago exceeded the reserve of the Bank of England, have lost the greatest part of that wealth." — Charlotte

### Metternich in Exile

- Metternich wrote Salomon philosophical apologia from exile
- Lionel gave Metternich 323,000 gulden advance against depreciated railway shares; debts still outstanding in the 1870s
- Anselm: Metternich's theories "can't be of great help to the world now"

### Survival

**Why revolutions failed** (and Rothschilds survived):
1. **Limited rural support**: republicanism shallow outside cities
2. **No great-power war**: Lamartine's France declined to export revolution
3. **Nationalist contradictions**: German, Magyar, Czech, Italian movements worked at cross-purposes
4. **Habsburg military recovery**: Windischgrätz, Jelacic, Radetzky crushed revolutionaries

**The sine qua non — family concordia**:
- London and Naples bailed out stricken Paris and Vienna — Mayer Amschel's injunction to maintain unity never mattered more
- **Naples house** relatively unaffected: profits slumped to 2,709 ducats (first half 1848) but recovered to 58,229 (second half); Carl sent money to Frankfurt in April
- **England** untouched: Chartist demonstration on Kennington Common proved "a damp squib"
- London house had lost £660,702 (1847 = 30% of capital) but rebuilt: profits £132,058 (1848), £334,524 (1849)
- Charlotte admitted losses to Count Dietrichstein, who delivered the pointed compliment that she "no longer enjoyed such a high position in the world"

### James's Survival in Paris

- Feb 24: visited Ministry of Finance; Feb 25: donated 50,000 francs to wounded fighters
- Feb 26: summoned by **Caussidiere** on suspicion of smuggling money; James steered between admitting bankruptcy and revealing true wealth:

> "People think I am made of money, but I only have paper. My fortune and my cash are converted into securities, which at the moment have no value... I would regard flight as cowardice."

- James responded with threats: failure of Rothschilds would "kill the goose that lays the golden egg" — government would lose both public and private services; Ferrieres could not be auctioned; employees' jobs lost; **Lamartine, Caussidiere, and Cremieux** had already been granted favours
- Clerks at de Rothschild Freres protested salary cuts: "You are richer than anyone and we won't [accept a pay cut]"
- James bought time; by third week of May, government commission decided to repurchase railway concessions rather than nationalise
- **June Days** (22-28 June 1848): working-class revolt crushed by General **Cavaignac** — Marx argued bourgeoisie allied with militarism against proletariat
- Rothschilds did little to promote restoration of "order"; James sent Alphonse to Frankfurt to keep him *out* of the fighting
- New Finance Minister **Goudchaux** converted 3% bonds into 5% — turning a 25m-franc loss into an 11m-franc profit
- Ferguson: James adopted the stance **Balzac** anticipated — "the indispensable debtor who owes his creditors so much they dare not let him fail"
- As **Merimee** noted: government's financial position was "diabolical"; collapse of de Rothschild Frères would have made matters catastrophically worse
- By July James was once again closeted with the Finance Minister, "now a great favourite and as there is no other Banker or person with money or disposition to come forward, he is naturally very much looked up to"
- Radical *Tocsin des Travailleurs* (August 1848): "Wealth fades away, glory is humbled, and dominion is broken, but the Jew, the monarch of our time, has held his throne"

### Diminished Political Influence

- Key difference from 1830: in 1848 Rothschilds too financially preoccupied to play diplomatic role
- London partners used new dominance to **veto** loan proposals to: Austria, Hungary, Rome, Lombardy, Prussia, Baden — all refused
- Paradox: **Salomon** appears to have suggested lending money to Hungarians to buy English guns even while lamenting Habsburg collapse

### The American Lifeline

- London's ability to assist depended on **Belmont** remitting funds from America
- Belmont had financed the **US-Mexico War**; advanced sums against **$15m indemnity** under **Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo**
- When Alphonse was sent to "bring the errant agent to heel," Belmont dispatched large silver consignments to London — the most important stabilising influence on European finance in 1848

### Central Bank Intervention

- **Bank of England** had suspended gold reserve rule October 1847, setting precedent
- **Banque de France** suspended convertibility; government set up *comptoirs d'escompte* for emergency liquidity; revolution's enduring effect was eliminating provincial banks of issue and centralising at Banque de France
- Vienna's **National Bank** banned gold/silver exports, suspended convertibility
- Rothschild access to American and English silver proved crucial: Banque de France placed large silver orders through New Court as early as April 1848
- Anselm leveraged silver supply as bargaining chip with Vienna's National Bank — bald threat: "Either the prolongation of the bills or the fall of the houses of Eskeles and Wertheimer, which would also seriously compromise the portfolio of the National Bank itself"

### Third-Generation Takeover

- **Lionel** refused easy bail-outs; made uncles "sweat and pay"; first reaction to Salomon's pleas was outright refusal
- When he sent silver to Frankfurt, London house turned a profit on the shipments
- **Anselm** arrived in Vienna April 10 to clean up Salomon's "Augean stables" with "a marked lack of filial compassion"; forbade further financial sacrifice for Arnstein & Eskeles
- Anselm to Salomon: situation was "fatal" and Salomon in "such a state of moral collapse" that staying longer would only harm his health
- 1848 crisis marked "the end of the dominance of the second generation"
- **Alphonse's** candid statement of the London partners' "true goal": to free London from "a solidarity incompatible with the political activities and the ardent business spirit of the first generation"
- London partners began distinguishing between letters uncles could see and those kept private — "a revolutionary suggestion" since private correspondence had been the lifeblood of the partnership

### Mayer Carl and Political Caution

- Austrian government asked Salomon to mediate "Italian difficulties"; younger Rothschilds refused
- **Mayer Carl**: "we should not mix in politics, because, however things turn out, it is harlequin who gets the kicks and harlequin, that is us"
- Summer 1848 "congress" at Frankfurt decided to preserve Vienna house by **writing off most of £1.7m** owed to Frankfurt
- Rothschilds had poor relations with **Palmerston** throughout 1848; Charlotte: his policy was "laughable"
- Betty: Palmerston was "the bad genie, breathing fire everywhere"

### James and Louis Napoleon

- **Louis Napoleon Bonaparte** was the figure Rothschilds viewed with the most suspicion in the entire 19th century
- Anthony: Louis Napoleon "gets drunk every night & God knows what else" and wanted only "plenty [of money] so that he can roger comfortably & get drunk when he likes"
- James called him "a stupid ass" — but pragmatic: dined with him just 18 days after election (Jan 16, 1849): "I could not refuse his invitation"
- James had lent Napoleon 20,000 francs before election; once he had government funds: "I promised him 20,000 francs before his budget was passed but now he is getting money from the government, so I don't want to throw away our money and so I won't give him a penny more"
- Betty loathed Napoleon more viscerally; told **Macaulay** France was "floundering between a nobody and a head garotted by a subversive, useless minority"
- Core fear: Napoleon would replicate uncle's expansionist foreign policy
- By early 1849, James assumed Napoleon would "not rest till he has made himself Emperor" — regarded this as "a great mistake"
- Relief when Napoleon intervened in Italy on the side of the Pope rather than backing the Roman republic

### Return to Business

- Only after military defeat of revolution did lending resume; Anselm dismissed Austrian 60m gulden loan as "a great nonsense"; James refused Paris city loan three times
- **Piedmont loan**: James negotiated skilfully — sounded out Turin in advance, hinted at rival deal with Italian bankers; secured control of more than half the **76m franc Piedmontese loan**
- Told **Cavour** he regarded Piedmont as "established on a much sounder basis than Austria"
- Cavour was shocked James had "bamboozled" Finance Minister Nigra; called him "this cunning old rascal Rothschild"
- James declared the loan could lead to a financial "marriage" with Italy as a whole

### Return of Confidence

- Paris 5% rentes rose from 74 to 93 in the year after December 1848
- **Nat** on Rome expedition: "In general when troops begin to move bondholders are frightened; in this case as it is for re-establishment of order, perhaps it will produce a good effect"
- Railway revival: **Pereires** launched Paris-Lyon-Avignon scheme (forerunner of Paris-Lyon-Marseille), with state investing 147m francs and guaranteeing 5% return — but approached **Barings** to raise capital: "a first hint of the breach which lay ahead"
- James fired **Isaac Pereire** off the Nord board (May 1849): "You have no idea what scoundrels these little fellows are... when these people don't need you, they give you a kick in the arse"
- James appeared alongside Napoleon at Nord line opening in July — symbolic reassertion
- James's restored confidence: "Above all, it is good that people realise that nothing gets off the ground without us and when we demand something, then it is a case of giving Rothschild all he wants"

### The Herzen Friendship

- James formed unlikely friendship in 1849 with **Alexander Herzen**, founding father of Russian socialism, who coined "Land and Freedom"
- Herzen had aligned with **Michael Bakunin** and **Proudhon** (*la propriete, c'est la vol*); personally financed Proudhon's journal *Voix du Peuple* 24,000 francs while Proudhon was in prison
- Herzen's fortune was aristocratic (illegitimate son); Rothschilds helped him bank ~10,000 roubles
- When Russian government blocked Herzen's inheritance, James confronted Russia personally: "No, I shan't allow myself to be trifled with; I shall bring an action against the bank!" — threatened legal action and press exposure
- Despite warning from **Count Kiselev** (Russian ambassador) about Herzen's politics, James took his side
- Six weeks later, the money was paid; Herzen: "When half an hour later I was mounting the staircase of the Winter Palace of Finance in the Rue Laffitte, the rival of Nicholas was coming down it"
- After Herzen's expulsion from Paris, James continued managing his investments; secured him travel permits; handed account to London house during English exile
- The Herzen episode substantiates **Heine's** claim that James was "at heart more a revolutionary than a reactionary"

### James and Political Neutrality

- Marx's accusation that Rothschilds were "financiers of reaction" was misleading; James's concern was resuming normal business — called himself "a friend of business"
- James willing to do business with the Roman Republic before the Pope was restored; accepted a deposit saying "as I am [now] a Republican"
- Any loan to the Pope conditional on granting civil rights to Roman Jews; Anselm on Pius IX: "intolerant in the highest degree, I am tempted even to say inhuman"
- Lionel from Wildbad (August): "The Liberal party in Germany is very different from the liberals in England. All persons of property or who are in business are for the old state of things" — 1848 had made European liberals more conservative

### Herzen's Arc — Ferguson's Conclusion

- Herzen's transformation from Rothschild critic to client **emblematic of a Europe-wide shift** post-1848
- James knew Herzen's funds were financing the radical press; it did not trouble him
- Ferguson's closing: rapid, sustained economic growth after 1849 made another 1848 much less likely — future lay in **conflicts within capitalism rather than against it**, and between states rather than between classes

## Appendix 1: Prices and Purchasing Power

### The GDP Method

- Conventional inflation multiplier: Nathan's £3.5m estate (1836) → **£124.25m** in 1995 terms
- Better: express as **proportion of GDP**
  - UK GDP in 1836: £562m; Nathan's £3.5m = **0.62% of UK GDP**
  - 0.62% of 1995 UK GDP = **£3,752 million** — close to being a **double billionaire**
- Per capita: Nathan = **160,000x** per capita national income
- Extreme 19th-century inequality: as late as 1911-13, **87% of adults** had total wealth under £100; only 0.2% had over £25,000

### Inequality Factor

- All GDP-based measures miss the **extreme inequality** of the 19th century — no progressive taxes, no welfare state
- As late as 1911-13, **87% of adults** in England & Wales had total wealth under £100; only 0.2% (32,000 people) had over £25,000
- Nathan's grandsons **Natty, Leo, and Alfred** died 1915-17 and left £6,494,000 combined — **0.1% of all capital** owned by adults in England & Wales

### Were the Rothschilds the Richest Family?

- Nathan left more than Crawshay, Peel, Arkwright, Duke of Queensberry, Duke of Sutherland, Duke of Cleveland
- Nearest rival at death: banker **William J. Denison** at £2.3m; not surpassed until 1857 by **James Morrison** (£4-6m)
- Period 1860-99: only 23 individuals left estates over £1.8m; **four were Rothschilds** (Lionel, Anthony, Nat, Mayer)
- Brothers collectively left **£8.4 million** — if Nathan had left it to a single heir, Lionel would have been unambiguously the richest man in Britain
- **James** probably richest man in the world: reported ~1,100m francs (£44m) at death in 1868; more realistic estimate ~193m francs (£7.7m)
- From 1900 onward, English Rothschilds ceased to be exceptional; French and Austrian houses remained dramatically richer
- 1905: **Edouard, Gustave, Edmond** each had personal shares worth £5.8m; **Albert** (Vienna) had £5.9m
- Combined capital at peak (December 1899): **£41.4 million** among 10 partners — excluding art and real estate
- **Bill Gates** (c. 1995): $36.4bn = **0.49% of US GDP** — slightly less than Nathan's 0.62%
- Per capita comparison favors Gates (1.3m x American per capita GDP vs. Nathan's 160,000x British) — but this mainly reflects **population growth**
- Ferguson: **"It is almost inconceivable that any other family could match this."**
