# The House of Rothschild: Money's Prophets (1798-1848)

**Author:** Niall Ferguson
**Published:** 1998
**Pages:** 765

## Summary

Niall Ferguson's first volume on the Rothschild banking dynasty traces the family from Mayer Amschel's cramped origins in Frankfurt's Judengasse to the European revolutions of 1848. Drawing on previously inaccessible archives -- including private letters written in Judendeutsch (German in Hebrew characters) for security -- Ferguson demolishes longstanding myths and reveals a family that essentially created the international bond market in its modern form.

The narrative follows five brothers dispatched to five European capitals (London, Paris, Frankfurt, Vienna, Naples), operating as a single multinational enterprise at a time when no comparable institution existed. At their zenith in the 1820s-1860s, the Rothschilds were easily the biggest bank in the world, with combined capital exceeding that of the Banque de France and dwarfing rivals like Baring Brothers by a factor of ten.

Ferguson's central argument is that the Rothschilds' power rested on three pillars: proximity to political power, speed of information (carrier pigeons, private couriers, color-coded envelopes), and the ability to manipulate financial markets through sheer scale. Their peace advocacy was not idealism but self-interest -- war crashed bond prices across all five houses simultaneously.

The book tracks their evolution from Nathan's wartime bullion smuggling and subsidy transfers for Wellington, through the post-Napoleonic bond boom, into the railway age where James built a pan-European network. It chronicles their persistent struggle for Jewish emancipation, their extraordinary endogamous marriage strategy (15 of 21 marriages between direct descendants), and the mounting anti-Semitic backlash that fused with socialist critiques to create a dangerous new mythology.

The climax is 1848, when revolution swept every European capital where the Rothschilds operated. Three of five houses teetered on insolvency. Yet the family survived -- through internal solidarity, London and Naples bailing out stricken Paris and Vienna, and James's pragmatic willingness to work with whatever regime emerged. Ferguson closes with the irony of James befriending Alexander Herzen, the father of Russian socialism, as emblematic of a post-revolutionary world where conflicts would play out within capitalism rather than against it.

## Key Themes

- The creation of the international bond market as a mechanism of political influence
- Information asymmetry as the foundation of financial power
- The tension between Jewish identity, emancipation activism, and social assimilation
- Family unity as competitive advantage -- Mayer Amschel's deathbed command
- The myth-reality gap: Waterloo legend demolished, "buried treasure" debunked
- Railways as creative destruction -- transforming merchant bankers into industrial capitalists
- The limits of financial power: money could underwrite peace but not prevent revolution
- Anti-Rothschild mythology as the template for modern anti-Semitic conspiracy theories
