# Economics 101

**Author:** Alfred Mill
**Published:** Adams Media (2016)
**Pages:** ~288

## One-Line Summary
A crash course covering the entire discipline of economics — from scarcity and supply/demand through GDP, inflation, unemployment, monetary and fiscal policy, the Federal Reserve, competing schools of thought, economic growth, and the 2008 financial crisis — structured as ~55 self-contained topic chapters designed to give a complete non-specialist education in how economies work.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Economics is the study of scarcity and choice:** Every decision involves trade-offs. Opportunity cost — the value of the next best alternative foregone — is the most fundamental concept in economics, driving everything from personal decisions to international trade.

2. **Markets are powerful but imperfect:** Supply and demand, left alone, tend to allocate resources efficiently (allocative efficiency = marginal benefit equals marginal cost). But market failures exist — externalities, public goods, information asymmetries, and monopoly power — and these justify some government intervention.

3. **Comparative advantage, not absolute advantage, drives trade:** Even if one country is better at producing everything, both nations benefit from specializing in their lowest-opportunity-cost goods and trading. This is the universal foundation of trade theory, accepted across the political spectrum.

4. **GDP measures output, not well-being:** GDP captures the market value of all final goods and services produced, but misses unpaid labor, environmental degradation, income distribution, and "everything that makes life worthwhile" (RFK). Alternative measures (Green GDP, Human Development Index, Genuine Progress Indicator) attempt to fill these gaps.

5. **Unemployment has multiple types requiring different solutions:** Frictional (between jobs — healthy), structural (skills mismatch — requires retraining), and cyclical (business cycle — requires policy intervention). Okun's Law: every 1% above the natural rate costs 2% of potential GDP.

6. **Inflation is a tax on cash holders and fixed-income earners:** Borrowers benefit from unexpected inflation; savers and lenders lose. Cost-push and demand-pull inflation require completely different policy responses — treating cost-push like demand-pull (as the 1970s Fed did) is like pouring water on a grease fire.

7. **The Federal Reserve's power is asymmetric:** The Fed can definitively stop inflation (raise rates with no upper limit) but can only encourage growth (lower rates, but zero is the floor). This asymmetry — able to slam the brakes but only suggest hitting the gas — defines the limits of monetary policy.

8. **Classical and Keynesian economics are not opposites but complements:** Classical economics trusts markets to self-correct (Say's Law, flexible wages, laissez-faire). Keynesian economics intervenes when markets fail (sticky wages, savings gluts, multiplier effect). The modern policy toolkit uses both: demand-side fiscal/monetary policy AND supply-side incentives.

9. **The Phillips Curve works in the short run but not the long run:** There is a temporary trade-off between inflation and unemployment when inflation expectations are stable. Once expectations adjust, the trade-off disappears — which is why Friedman and Phelps introduced the natural rate hypothesis.

10. **Money creation through fractional reserve banking is the foundation of modern economies:** Banks don't just store money — they create it through lending. A $100 deposit can generate $1,000 in total money supply (with a 10% reserve requirement). The money multiplier amplifies every Fed action.

11. **The 2008 crisis was a cascade of principal-agent problems:** Banks had no incentive to vet borrowers (they sold loans). Rating agencies had incentive to inflate ratings (they were paid by issuers). Institutional investors had incentive to buy highly rated assets (it was someone else's money). CDS sellers had incentive to sell insurance they couldn't fund (profits were immediate, losses theoretical). Every link in the chain had misaligned incentives.

12. **Deflation is more dangerous than inflation:** Falling prices sound good but create perverse incentives — consumers delay purchases, firms delay investment, manufacturing stalls, unemployment rises, reinforcing further deflation. And unlike inflation, the central bank has a hard floor at 0% interest rates (the liquidity trap).

13. **Economic growth requires institutions, not just resources:** Human capital, physical capital, R&D, rule of law, property rights, and stable monetary/fiscal policy are all necessary conditions. Natural resources alone are insufficient — Japan (few resources, high growth) vs. Russia (vast resources, lower growth).

14. **Supply-side economics' lasting contribution is that incentives matter:** Although the specific Reagan-era policies produced large deficits rather than increased revenue, the insight that government policies shape behavior and create unintended consequences remains valuable. Policymakers must consider both the demand AND supply effects of their actions.

15. **Income inequality threatens growth in a democracy:** Capitalism rewards the most productive, but universal suffrage means those on the "short end of capitalism's stick" can vote to redistribute. Proponents of growth must be prepared to share gains, or democratic forces will change the equation.

## Thematic Breakdowns

### Microeconomics Foundation (Chapters 1-20)
The book builds from first principles: scarcity → trade-offs → opportunity cost → marginal analysis → supply and demand → market equilibrium → elasticity → consumer/producer surplus → market structures (perfect competition → monopolistic competition → oligopoly → monopoly) → market failures (externalities, public goods, asymmetric information) → government intervention (price controls, taxes, subsidies). Each concept builds on the previous one, creating a complete micro toolkit.

### Money and Banking (Chapters ~20-25)
How money evolved from barter to commodity money to fiat currency. The fractional reserve banking system and money creation through the money multiplier. The loanable funds market, bond markets, stock markets. Foreign exchange and exchange rates. The relationship between interest rates, bond prices, and the real economy.

### Macroeconomic Measurement (Chapters ~25-35)
GDP and its three measurement approaches (expenditure, income, production). Nominal vs. real GDP and the GDP deflator. The business cycle and its four stages. What GDP doesn't measure. Unemployment definitions, classifications (U1-U6), types (frictional/structural/cyclical), and demographic disparities. Inflation measurement (CPI, PPI, PCE), types (demand-pull, cost-push, asset), and historical episodes.

### Schools of Economic Thought (Chapters ~35-45)
The classical view (Say's Law, laissez-faire, self-correcting markets). Keynes's revolution (sticky wages, unstable AD, multiplier effect, government intervention). The Phillips Curve and its breakdown in the 1970s. Monetarism (Friedman's natural rate hypothesis). Supply-side economics (Reagan, deregulation, tax cuts, "voodoo economics"). The book presents these as a toolkit — different tools for different problems.

### The Federal Reserve and Monetary Policy (Chapters ~45-50)
History from Hamilton through the Bank Panic of 1907 to the 1913 Federal Reserve Act. Structure (Board of Governors, 12 district banks, FOMC). Three tools: reserve requirements, discount rate, open market operations. How monetary policy transmits through the economy. Fiscal-monetary policy interaction. Short-run vs. long-run effects.

### The Great Recession (Chapters ~50-55)
Links to the Great Depression. The run-up: deregulation, Glass-Steagall repeal, shadow banking, 9/11, low interest rates, housing bubble. Securitization and CDOs. Principal-agent problems throughout the chain. Rating agency failure. Credit default swaps. Bear Stearns, Lehman Brothers, AIG. The Fed's unconventional response (QE, TAF). Government response (TARP, stimulus). Jobless recovery and the inflation tightrope.

## Author's Perspective
Mill writes as a textbook author presenting mainstream economics in an accessible format. He avoids strong ideological positions, presenting classical, Keynesian, and supply-side economics as legitimate schools with different strengths. His treatment is balanced: he notes that supply-side tax cuts produced deficits rather than revenue, but also acknowledges that incentives matter. He is clearly Keynesian-leaning in his treatment of the 2008 crisis (endorsing government intervention as having "averted a second Great Depression") but presents the classical self-correction argument fairly. The book's accessibility comes at the cost of nuance — complex debates are simplified to their essential outlines.

## Relevance to Trading/Investing
- **Interest rate transmission mechanism**: Understanding how Fed policy flows through reserves → money supply → interest rates → investment → GDP → employment is essential for anticipating market reactions to FOMC decisions
- **Inflation expectations drive markets**: The distinction between actual inflation and expected inflation is critical — markets price in expectations, and surprises move prices. The Fed manages expectations as much as actual policy
- **Business cycle positioning**: Recognizing the four stages (expansion, peak, contraction, trough) and their policy implications helps time sector rotations and risk management
- **Principal-agent awareness**: The 2008 crisis showed that misaligned incentives in the financial system can create systemic risk invisible to individual actors — understanding where incentives misalign is crucial for risk assessment
- **Phillips Curve for policy prediction**: Short-run trade-offs between inflation and unemployment help predict policy pivots — if unemployment is low and inflation rising, expect tightening; if both are rising (stagflation), expect policy confusion
- **Fiscal/monetary interaction**: Markets react to the combination of fiscal and monetary policy, not either alone — understanding when they reinforce vs. conflict helps predict outcomes
