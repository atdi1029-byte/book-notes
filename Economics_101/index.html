Key Takeaways 🔖

  1. Economics is the study of scarcity and choice. Every decision involves trade-offs. Opportunity cost—the value of the next best alternative foregone—is the single most fundamental concept, driving everything from a teenager’s career choice to international trade agreements.
  2. Markets are powerful but imperfect. Supply and demand, left alone, tend toward allocative efficiency (marginal benefit = marginal cost). But externalities, public goods, information asymmetries, and monopoly power create market failures that justify careful government intervention.
  3. Comparative advantage, not absolute advantage, drives trade. Even if one country is better at producing everything, both nations benefit from specializing in their lowest-opportunity-cost goods. This is the universal foundation of trade theory.
  4. GDP measures output, not well-being. It counts “air pollution and cigarette advertising” but not “the beauty of our poetry or the strength of our marriages.”
  5. The Federal Reserve’s power is asymmetric. It can definitively stop inflation (raise rates without limit) but can only encourage growth (lower rates, but zero is the floor). It can slam the brakes but only suggest hitting the gas.
  6. Cost-push and demand-pull inflation require opposite treatments. Treating cost-push inflation like demand-pull (as the 1970s Fed did) is “like pouring water on a grease fire.”
  7. The 2008 crisis was a cascade of principal-agent problems. Banks had no incentive to vet borrowers (they sold loans). Rating agencies had incentive to inflate ratings (paid by issuers). CDS sellers sold insurance they couldn’t fund. Every link in the chain had misaligned incentives.
  8. Classical and Keynesian economics are complements, not opposites. Different tools for different problems—self-correcting markets work much of the time; government intervention is necessary when they don’t.
  9. Deflation is more dangerous than moderate inflation. Falling prices create a deflationary spiral: consumers delay spending → firms cut production → unemployment rises → further deflation. And unlike inflation, the central bank hits a hard floor at 0% interest (the liquidity trap).
  10. Economic growth requires institutions, not just resources. Japan (few resources, high growth) vs. Russia (vast resources, lower growth) proves that human capital, rule of law, and property rights matter more than natural endowments.

Contrarian Ideas 🔖

Mill presents several ideas that challenge common intuitions about how economies work. These are not his personal contrarian positions but rather mainstream economic insights that surprise non-economists:

  • Wealth is not money. Mercantilists believed national wealth was measured in stockpiles of gold and silver. Adam Smith’s revolutionary insight was that wealth is the productive capacity of a nation’s people. A country buried in gold with no skilled workers is poor; a country with no gold but brilliant engineers is rich.
  • Trade benefits even the “worse” country. Even when one nation is better at producing absolutely everything, both countries benefit from trading based on comparative advantage. This is the most counterintuitive and universally accepted proposition in economics.
  • Minimum wage laws can increase unemployment. While intended to help low-wage workers, price floors above equilibrium create surpluses—in the labor market, that surplus is unemployment. Some workers earn more, but others lose their jobs entirely. Politicians know this and strategically set increases just below the equilibrium wage—claiming credit while minimizing economic disruption.
  • Monopolies aren’t always bad; competition isn’t always good. Natural monopolies (utilities, water systems) are more efficient as single providers. Patent-granted monopolies are necessary to encourage innovation. And perfect competition eliminates economic profit, which eliminates the incentive for R&D.
  • Banks create money out of thin air. Through fractional reserve lending, a $100 deposit can generate $1,000 in total money supply. The money multiplier means most “money” in the economy was never printed—it was created by lending.
  • Say’s Law is both obviously true and obviously false. “Supply creates its own demand” works most of the time (producing goods creates the income to buy goods), but the Great Depression proved that savings gluts and liquidity traps can break the link for extended periods.
  • The Phillips Curve trade-off is an illusion. For decades, policymakers believed they could “buy” lower unemployment with higher inflation. The 1970s proved this only works temporarily—once expectations adjust, you get both higher inflation and higher unemployment.
  • Deflation is worse than inflation. Falling prices sound like a consumer paradise, but they create a vicious cycle where everyone waits to buy, production collapses, and unemployment spirals. At least inflation can be fought by raising rates; deflation leaves policymakers at the zero bound with no ammunition.
  • A stronger economy can mean a weaker currency. As an economy strengthens and incomes rise, citizens import more, supplying more domestic currency to foreign exchange markets, driving the currency’s value down. “Strong” and “weak” don’t mean “good” and “bad.”

Notable Quotes & Analogies 🔖

“GDP counts air pollution and cigarette advertising, and ambulances to clear our highways of carnage… counts special locks for our doors and the jails for the people who break them… the destruction of the redwoods… napalm and nuclear warheads… Yet it does not allow for the health of our children, the quality of their education, or the joy of their play. It does not include the beauty of our poetry or the strength of our marriages… it measures everything, in short, except that which makes life worthwhile.” —Robert F. Kennedy, 1968
“I guess I should warn you, if I turn out to be particularly clear, you’ve probably misunderstood what I’ve said.” —Alan Greenspan, on the art of “Fed speak”
“It is not from the benevolence of the butcher, the brewer, or the baker that we expect our dinner, but from their regard to their own self-interest.” —Adam Smith
“The ideas of economists and political philosophers, both when they are right and when they are wrong, are more powerful than is commonly understood. Indeed, the world is ruled by little else. Practical men, who believe themselves to be quite exempt from any intellectual influences, are usually the slaves of some defunct economist.” —John Maynard Keynes
“Much of the economic backwardness in the world can be explained by lack of mutual confidence.” —Kenneth Arrow

The Highway Analogy (Business Cycles): Driving on a two-lane highway represents normal economic growth. Passing slower traffic represents expansion (spending exceeds productive capacity). Swerving back to avoid an oncoming truck represents contraction. Doing all of this blindfolded with a Swahili-speaking navigator represents how economists actually try to predict the future.

The Grease Fire Analogy (Inflation Policy): Demand-pull inflation is like a forest fire—pour water (monetary expansion) on it and it goes out. Cost-push inflation is like a grease fire—pour water on it and it explodes. The 1970s Fed poured monetary “water” on cost-push inflation, making it catastrophically worse.

The Parent-Teenager Analogy (Fed Credibility): A parent who threatens “if you don’t stop, I’ll take your phone” but never follows through loses all credibility. Likewise, a central bank that talks tough on inflation but never raises rates loses the power to manage expectations. Volcker restored Fed credibility by doing what he said he would do.

The Gas Pedal / Brake Pedal (Investment): Expected rate of return is the gas pedal—profit opportunities accelerate investment. The interest rate is the brake pedal—higher rates slow investment. If rates are low and expected profits are high, business grows. If the interest rate exceeds the expected return, firms are stationary.

The Bathtub Analogy (GDP vs. Wealth): Water flowing from the faucet is GDP (new production). Water accumulated in the tub is national wealth. Water draining out is depreciation. As long as GDP exceeds outflows, the nation’s wealth grows. GDP measures what is new, not what has accumulated.

The Briefcase Indicator: Investors during Greenspan’s tenure studied the size of his briefcase heading into FOMC meetings. Big and heavy meant he was carrying documentation to support a rate change. Light meant rates would likely stay unchanged.

The Laminated Menu Indicator: Restaurants that laminate their menus are testifying to price stability. When inflation is high, restaurants use chalkboards instead—prices change too fast to commit to lamination. Seafood prices are always on chalkboards because they’re inherently volatile.

Allegories & Cultural References 🔖

  • Adam Smith’s Invisible Hand: The most famous metaphor in economics—individuals pursuing their own self-interest are “led by an invisible hand” to promote the good of society. Mill references this throughout as the philosophical foundation of free-market economics.
  • Keynes’s “Animal Spirits”: Keynes described business investment as driven by emotion rather than pure calculation—businesses are “essentially bipolar,” swinging between manic investing and depressive withdrawal. This challenges the classical assumption of rational actors.
  • Weimar Germany’s Hyperinflation: The archetypal cautionary tale about money printing. People needed wheelbarrows of cash for bread. The destruction of the middle class’s savings created the social instability that enabled Hitler’s rise.
  • Hungary 1945–46: The worst hyperinflation in recorded history—prices reached octillions (1027), with prices doubling every 15 hours at the peak.
  • Jared Diamond’s “Guns, Germs, and Steel”: Referenced in the economic growth chapter. Diamond’s friend Yali’s question—“Why do European descendants have so much while the people of New Guinea have so little?”—frames the discussion of what conditions enable growth.
  • The Bank Panic of 1907: A Montana investor’s failed attempt to corner the copper market triggered cascading bank runs across New York. J.P. Morgan personally injected cash to stop the panic—proving America needed a permanent lender of last resort, leading to the Federal Reserve’s creation.
  • Andrew Jackson vs. the Second Bank: Jackson’s destruction of the Second Bank of the United States by withdrawing government deposits demonstrates the deeply American suspicion of central banking that persists to this day.
  • “Voodoo Economics”: George H.W. Bush’s label for Reagan’s supply-side economics during the 1980 Republican primary—ironic since Bush later became Reagan’s vice president and inherited the policy.
  • “Helicopter Ben”: Ben Bernanke earned this nickname after a 2002 speech referencing Milton Friedman’s suggestion that deflation could be cured by dropping cash from helicopters. When the 2008 crisis came, this is essentially what he did through quantitative easing.
  • The Chicago Boys in Chile: Friedman-trained economists who brought market reforms under Pinochet—halting inflation and creating growth, but forever associating free-market economics with dictatorship in Latin America.
  • De Beers and the Diamond Illusion: A classic monopoly example. De Beers controlled the diamond resource, coerced wholesalers and cutters, and created an illusion of scarcity—earning monopoly profits for over a century. “You bought the diamond because the benefit outweighed the cost. The problem is you never realized how much of the cost was De Beers’ profit.”
  • Smoot-Hawley Tariff Act (1930): Raised tariffs on 20,000+ imported goods to record levels. Other countries retaliated. World trade collapsed 65%. Widely blamed for deepening the Great Depression—the cautionary tale against protectionism.
  • Yap Island Money: Giant stone discs weighing hundreds of pounds used as currency on the Pacific island of Yap—decidedly not portable, illustrating how money’s characteristics evolved.

Author’s Key Proposals 🔖

As a textbook author, Mill doesn’t advocate specific policies so much as argue that economics literacy matters for everyone. His implicit proposals include:

  1. Use the complete toolbox. The book’s arc builds toward the conclusion that no single school of thought has all the answers. Classical self-correction, Keynesian demand management, monetarist money supply discipline, and supply-side incentive awareness are all necessary tools. Ignoring any one leads to policy failure.
  2. Understand incentives before creating policy. The supply-side chapter’s lasting lesson: every regulation, tax, and mandate changes behavior. Policymakers must anticipate unintended consequences. People are not going to stop acting like people.
  3. Maintain central bank independence. The Fed’s insulation from political pressure is essential for credible inflation-fighting. When governments can print money to fund spending, hyperinflation invariably follows.
  4. Distribute growth’s gains broadly. In a democracy, inequality that grows too large will be corrected at the ballot box. Proponents of growth should share gains willingly rather than having redistribution forced through political upheaval.
  5. Protect property rights and rule of law. These institutions are preconditions for capital accumulation and economic growth. Corruption raises the cost of capital and deters investment.
  6. Invest in human capital above all. The most important factor in economic growth is education, skills, and abilities. Natural resources without human capital development lead to the “resource paradox” (Russia vs. Japan).

Part I: Microeconomic Foundations 🔖

Scarcity, Choice & Opportunity Cost

The Study of Scarcity

Economics begins with a universal condition: scarcity. There is never enough time, money, or stuff to satisfy everyone’s wants. This is not a temporary problem to be solved but a permanent feature of the human condition—even the wealthiest societies face scarcity of time, attention, and clean air. Adam Smith, the father of modern economics, was actually a moral philosopher—economists develop theories to explain behavior and test them against data, though sometimes theories are put into practice without ever being tested.

The factors of production are land (all natural resources—renewable like pine trees or nonrenewable like oil; payment = rent), labor (human skills across three tiers—unskilled workers paid wages for repetitive tasks, skilled workers paid for knowledge, professionals paid for expertise), capital (tools, factories, and equipment—capital is NOT money; capital is the product of investment, always purchased with borrowed money and thus incurring interest), and entrepreneurship. Allocative efficiency means getting the right resources to the right people—achieved when marginal benefit equals marginal cost.

Opportunity Cost and Marginal Analysis

Every use of a factor incurs an opportunity cost—the next best alternative foregone. Costs are both explicit (labor, materials, overhead) and implicit (what you could have done instead). A teacher earning $5,000 a month who quits to sell snow cones might show an accounting profit of $4,000—but has an economic loss of $1,000 because the opportunity cost of teaching exceeds the snow cone revenue. The economic loss is a signal: your resources are better used elsewhere. But for a 12-year-old whose alternative is mowing lawns, the opportunity cost is near zero—snow cones are fine.

Economists assume people make decisions at the margin: do it if the marginal benefit exceeds the marginal cost, don’t if it doesn’t. Marginal benefit is measured in dollars or “utils”—units of happiness. If swimming gives 20 utils ($20 worth) and costs $5, you swim. At $20.01, you don’t.

Three Key Assumptions

Three assumptions underlie all economic analysis: ceteris paribus (all else equal—every cause-and-effect statement assumes nothing else changes), rationality (decisions use all available information, weighing costs and benefits), and self-interest (people are motivated by personal benefit—even altruistic acts can be framed as maximizing personal utility). The self-interest assumption is subject to extensive debate—most economists failed to predict the 2008 financial crisis, suggesting rational self-interest may not capture the full picture.

Free Trade & Comparative Advantage

The Fall of Mercantilism

Mercantilism dominated European economic thinking from the 16th to 18th century—the belief that national wealth equals stockpiles of gold and silver. Mercantilist countries exported as much as possible, imported as little as possible, hoarding precious metals. This led directly to colonialism: colonies existed to provide raw materials and buy finished goods from the mother country.

Adam Smith demolished this framework in 1776 with The Wealth of Nations, arguing that wealth is not gold but the productive capacity of a nation’s people. A country buried in gold with no skilled workers is poor; a country with no gold but brilliant engineers is rich. Nations should specialize in what they produce most efficiently and trade freely.

Absolute vs. Comparative Advantage

Smith’s absolute advantage was intuitive: if Art can perform 3 brain surgeries per day and Paul can write 3 hit songs, each should specialize. David Ricardo’s theory of comparative advantage (1817) went further: even if one country produces everything more efficiently, both countries benefit from specializing in their lowest-opportunity-cost goods. The Bangladesh T-shirt example illustrates this vividly—American workers are more productive at everything, but the opportunity cost of making T-shirts in the U.S. (giving up high-tech production) is enormous, while Bangladesh’s opportunity cost is low. This insight is the single most universally accepted proposition in economics, accepted across the political spectrum.

Trade Barriers and Their Consequences

In practice, free trade creates losers (domestic workers displaced by imports) even as it benefits society overall. Trade barriers take several forms: tariffs (taxes on imports—revenue tariffs raise money, protective tariffs shield domestic industry), quotas (hard ceilings on import quantities—U.S. sugar quotas keep domestic prices far above world prices), embargoes (total bans—the U.S. embargo on Cuba has lasted 60+ years without achieving its goal), and voluntary export restraints (Japan limited auto exports to the U.S. in the 1980s; Japanese manufacturers responded by building factories in the U.S.).

Post-WWII institutions designed to promote trade—Bretton Woods (1944), GATT (1947, became WTO in 1995), the IMF, and the World Bank—have progressively reduced tariffs from ~40% in the 1940s to ~5% by the 2000s. NAFTA (1994) boosted trade enormously but critics blame it for U.S. manufacturing job losses—illustrating the tension between aggregate gains and concentrated losses.

The Smoot-Hawley Tariff of 1930 remains the great cautionary tale against protectionism: Congress raised tariffs on 20,000+ imported goods to record levels. Other countries retaliated with their own tariffs. World trade collapsed by 65%. What was intended to protect American jobs instead deepened the Depression by destroying export markets. As Thomas Sowell noted, politicians consistently ignore that “there is no such thing as a free lunch”—protecting one industry always comes at the expense of others.

Economic Systems

Three Questions Every Economy Must Answer

Every economy must answer three fundamental questions: What to produce? (guns or butter? cars or computers?) How to produce it? (labor-intensive or capital-intensive? which technology?) For whom to produce? (distribute equally? by merit? by wealth?) Every economic system in history—from ancient Egypt to modern America—is defined by how it answers these three questions.

Traditional and Command Economies

Traditional economies (Kalahari Bushmen) do what their ancestors did—stable, predictable, tight-knit community, but no innovation, no growth, and vulnerable to external shocks. Virtually extinct in pure form.

Command economies have a central authority directing production. Ancient Egypt’s pharaohs directed labor to build pyramids; the Soviet Union’s five-year plans (starting 1928) set production quotas for the entire economy—rapid industrialization but terrible allocation: the USSR produced the world’s most tanks and steel but citizens waited in line for bread. The WWII United States temporarily became a command economy (War Production Board told factories what to make, rationed consumer goods, set prices). Command works for a single purpose in the short term but chronically misallocates because bureaucrats cannot know what millions of people want. No price signals means no feedback mechanism.

Market Economies and the Mix

Market economies answer the three questions through voluntary exchange. The price mechanism is the invisible hand—high prices signal producers to make more and consumers to buy less; low prices do the opposite. No central planner is needed; order emerges from millions of individual decisions. No pure market economy exists—all have some government intervention (police, courts, defense, regulation).

Every real economy is a mix—the U.S. has Social Security, Medicare, and the EPA; China has a massive private sector since Deng Xiaoping’s 1978 reforms. The spectrum runs from Hong Kong and Singapore (mostly free) to North Korea and Cuba (mostly controlled).

Capitalism vs. Socialism

Capitalism—private ownership, profit motive, limited government—is not synonymous with democracy. Socialism—collective ownership, central planning for key sectors, redistribution—is not synonymous with dictatorship. The conflation of economic and political systems is one of the most persistent errors in public discourse.

China has capitalist economics with authoritarian politics; Sweden has socialist-leaning economics with robust democracy. Sweden is often cited as a socialist success, but actually has very free markets with high taxes funding a generous welfare state—not government ownership of production, but government redistribution of income. The distinction matters: Sweden’s firms compete as vigorously as American ones; the government simply takes a larger share of the proceeds and redistributes them more broadly.

France mandates a 35-hour work week with generous vacation and strong unions—workers are well-protected but unemployment is chronically higher. Germany’s “social market economy” features co-determination (workers sit on corporate boards) and a 47.5% tax rate—lower inequality but lower growth. Britain under Thatcher reversed decades of creeping socialism by privatizing nationalized industries (British Telecom, British Gas, British Airways) and breaking union power; GDP surged but inequality widened.

Marx predicted the state would “wither away” after workers seized production; in practice, socialist governments became entrenched bureaucracies. As Keynes noted: “Practical men, who believe themselves to be quite exempt from any intellectual influences, are usually the slaves of some defunct economist.”

Supply, Demand & Equilibrium

The Laws of Supply and Demand

Demand is the willingness and ability to buy at various prices. The law of demand says quantity demanded falls as price rises, for three distinct reasons: diminishing marginal utility (each additional unit satisfies less, so you’ll only buy more if the price drops), the income effect (higher prices reduce your purchasing power—the same budget buys less), and the substitution effect (when one good’s price rises, consumers switch to cheaper alternatives—beef on sale means less chicken).

Supply is the willingness and ability to sell. The law of supply says quantity supplied rises as price rises, because rising marginal costs require higher prices to justify additional production. Rational, self-interested suppliers only produce if they can cover their costs.

Finding Equilibrium

Where the curves cross is equilibrium—the price where quantity demanded equals quantity supplied, and the market clears with neither surplus nor shortage.

Prices above equilibrium create surpluses—goods pile up on clearance racks. The clearance rack exists to lower the price enough to induce “some hapless, utility-maximizing individual” to purchase. Prices below equilibrium create shortages—buyers compete and bid prices up. “The consumer who wants the good the most gets it. How do you know he wanted it most? He offered the most money.”

Equilibrium maximizes the total gains from trade. Consumer surplus is what buyers would have been willing to pay minus what they actually paid. Producer surplus is what sellers received minus the minimum they would have accepted. Together, they represent the total benefit that trade creates.

Prices serve three functions simultaneously: they are fair (favor neither buyer nor seller), efficient (a child with $5 in a candy shop can instantly figure out what she can afford), and effective (ration goods to those who value them most, as revealed by willingness to pay).

Supply and Demand Shifters

A critical distinction that trips up even TV commentators: price affects quantity supplied (movement along the curve), but does NOT affect supply itself (the whole curve shifting). “Higher gas prices should lower demand” sounds smart but is technically imprecise—higher prices change quantity demanded (a slide along the curve), not demand itself.

Supply shifts when underlying conditions change: nature (weather, disease), input prices (land, seed, labor, equipment), competition (more producers entering the market), expected future prices, related goods’ profitability (if cocoa is more profitable, land shifts from coffee to cocoa), government policy (taxes, subsidies, regulation), or technology.

Demand shifts when consumer tastes change (an E. coli recall drops spinach demand overnight), when prices of complements (movie tickets and Junior Mints) or substitutes (airline and bus tickets) change, when income changes (normal goods rise with income; inferior goods like powdered milk fall), when the number of buyers changes (“consider before investing in the Russian housing market—the population there is shrinking”), or when expectations about future prices change (expect stock prices to rise → buy now → self-fulfilling prophecy).

Markets talk to each other: a rise in gas prices can ultimately cause starvation in Southeast Asia through a chain of ethanol demand, corn subsidies, wheat displacement, rice substitution, and unaffordable food prices.

Price Controls and Their Consequences

Government price ceilings (maximum prices, like rent control or Nixon’s 1970s food price controls) create shortages by holding price below equilibrium. The meat ceiling example: equilibrium at $5/pound, ceiling set at $3. At $3, consumers want to buy more meat than ever, but producers supply less—it’s not worth the cost to produce at that price. Result: shortage. “Consumers got their price ceiling, but many consumers got no meat at all.”

Price floors (minimum prices, like minimum wage) create surpluses by holding price above equilibrium. A city raising minimum wage from $8 to $10 in an area where equilibrium is $8 creates unemployment—more workers willing to supply labor at $10 (higher wages attract more applicants), but fewer employers willing to hire at that cost. The surplus of labor is unemployment by another name.

Politicians know this: they strategically pass minimum wage increases that keep the floor below the average equilibrium wage, claiming credit for helping workers while minimizing actual economic disruption. The increases look generous on paper but are carefully calibrated to avoid the worst consequences.

Both ceilings and floors create deadweight loss—transactions that would have benefited both parties but now don’t occur. Price controls also increase the need for government monitoring and enforcement—more bureaucracy, more taxes. As Mill notes, people are not going to stop acting like people—governments must consider whether their actions create perverse incentives.

Elasticity & Consumer Behavior

Utility and Diminishing Returns

Economists assume consumers try to make themselves as happy as possible—utility maximization—subject to their budget constraint. Diminishing marginal utility—each successive piece of candy gives fewer “happy points”—is the most fundamental law of consumer behavior.

It explains countless everyday behaviors: why not eat 50 peppermints? Why not exercise 24 hours? Why not wear a whole bottle of perfume? Because the marginal benefit of each additional unit decreases until it falls below the marginal cost. You consume until marginal benefit equals marginal cost. This is why “buy two, get the third for half-price” works: the reduced price makes the marginal benefit exceed marginal cost for the third doughnut, even though you wouldn’t have paid full price for it.

Price Elasticity of Demand

Elasticity measures how responsive quantity is to price changes. Elastic demand means consumers are very sensitive to price—three conditions make demand elastic: the purchase can be delayed, many substitutes exist, or the purchase takes a large share of income. Facelifts are elastic on all three counts: they can be delayed, Botox is an alternative, and they’re expensive.

Inelastic demand means consumers buy regardless of price changes. The purchase is urgent, no close substitutes exist, or it takes a small share of income. An emergency appendectomy is inelastic on all three counts: it can’t be delayed, there’s no substitute, and price is irrelevant when your life is at stake. The appendectomy vs. facelift comparison is the perfect illustration of the elasticity spectrum.

Supply Elasticity and Tax Incidence

Elasticity of supply depends primarily on time required to produce. Corn tortillas (readily available materials, quick production) have elastic supply—producers can respond quickly to price changes. Pinot Noir wine (vines take years to develop, grapes must ripen, wine needs aging) has inelastic supply—producers need considerable time to respond.

Elasticity determines who bears the burden of taxes—the more inelastic side absorbs more of the tax. This is why excise taxes on gasoline and cigarettes are largely paid by consumers rather than producers: demand for these products is relatively inelastic (consumers need gas to drive, smokers are addicted), so producers can pass most of the tax on in higher prices.

Cross-price elasticity measures how the price of one good affects demand for another: negative values indicate complements (movie tickets and Junior Mints—price of tickets rises, demand for both falls), positive values indicate substitutes (airline and bus tickets—price of airlines rises, demand for buses rises).

Production, Costs & Profit

The Production Function

The production function describes how output changes as a firm varies inputs. Businesses must make production decisions in response to market conditions—and the key question is always whether a change in demand is temporary or permanent. In the short run, firms can vary only one input (usually labor)—a restaurant can hire or fire workers but can’t expand the kitchen. In the long run, firms can vary all inputs (labor, capital, land)—the restaurant can expand the kitchen, add equipment, or relocate.

The production function passes through three stages. Increasing returns: each additional worker contributes more than the previous one through division of labor and specialization. A restaurant at 6 AM benefits enormously from adding workers 2 and 3—one cooks while the other serves, and both marginal product and total output rise.

Diminishing returns: output still grows, but each additional worker contributes less than the previous one. Marginal product is decreasing but positive. By employee #100, that worker produces less than #50—but still adds to total output.

Negative returns: adding more workers actually decreases total output. Too many people, too little space—workers literally get in each other’s way. Marginal product turns negative. The kitchen is “packed like sardines, workers immobilized, customers outraged, output drops to zero.” No rational firm operates in this stage, but understanding it completes the theoretical picture.

Costs and the Profit-Maximizing Rule

In competitive markets, firms are price-takers—if Target sells toilet paper for $1, Walmart must match or lose customers. Since they can’t control the price (and therefore can’t control revenue), profit maximization requires cost minimization. This is why so much of business strategy focuses on efficiency rather than pricing.

Firms face two fundamental types of costs:

Fixed costs (also called overhead): rent, property tax, management salaries, depreciation. These don’t change regardless of production level—the factory running at full capacity or sitting idle pays the same overhead. Depreciation is a fixed cost that deserves special mention: as capital ages, its value declines and eventually needs replacement.

Variable costs: hourly wages, utilities, per-unit taxes. These change with output level—more production means higher variable costs. Total cost = fixed costs + variable costs. In the long run, all costs are variable—firms can renegotiate rent, alter salaries, add or subtract capital. The fixed vs. variable distinction disappears over time.

Firms maximize profit where marginal cost equals marginal revenue (MC = MR). Each additional unit incurs more variable cost, so marginal cost increases as output grows. If producing one more hamburger costs 99¢ and it sells for 99¢, that’s the optimal output. If the cost is $1.29 but the price is 99¢, you’re producing at a loss per unit and have “some explaining to do to stockholders.”

Economic vs. Accounting Profit

The distinction between accounting profit and economic profit matters enormously. Accounting profit counts only explicit costs (total revenue minus labor, materials, overhead). Economic profit also subtracts opportunity costs—what your resources could have earned in their next best use.

Zero economic profit isn’t failure—it’s the market signal that resources are being used as efficiently as anywhere else. No firm has reason to enter or leave. Economic profits attract firms from other industries, increasing competition until profits disappear. Economic losses signal firms to leave, reducing competition until remaining firms can survive. In the long run, competition drives economic profit to zero—the most efficient allocation possible.

Market Structures

The Spectrum from Competition to Monopoly

Markets exist on a spectrum of four structures, ranging from most to least competitive. Like the Bohr model of the atom (simplified but useful for understanding), these models are idealized extremes that help explain real-world behavior:

Perfect competition: many sellers, identical products, no market power, perfect information, free entry and exit. A hypothetical “fairy-tale realm alongside unicorns and sea dragons”—no real market meets all conditions—but indispensable as a benchmark. Perfect competition is both allocatively efficient (consumers get maximum benefit at lowest price) and productively efficient (firms produce at lowest total cost per unit in the long run).

Monopolistic competition: many sellers, but products are differentiated. Think fast food: Taco Bell, McDonald’s, and Subway all compete in the same market but differentiate through menus and branding. Apple varieties evolved from just “red or green” to Honeycrisp, Pink Lady, and Fuji—product differentiation in action.

Oligopoly: few sellers, considerable barriers to entry, interdependent decisions. Auto manufacturers, airlines, telecoms. Oligopoly and monopoly are the most common market structures in the U.S.

Monopoly: one seller, no close substitutes, maximum market power. A local water utility, for example.

Perfect and Monopolistic Competition

In perfect competition, firms are price-takers—the industry sets the price, not individual businesses. The long run inevitably produces zero economic profit—profits attract new firms until competition eliminates them; losses drive firms out until the market stabilizes. Zero economic profit isn’t failure—it’s the market signal that resources are being used as efficiently as anywhere else. This is both the beauty and the terror of competition: beauty because it maximizes efficiency; terror because no firm earns more than its opportunity cost.

Monopolistic competitors differentiate endlessly through advertising. When producers differentiate successfully, they can charge higher prices than competitors (unlike price-takers in perfect competition). Apple varieties evolved from just “red or green” to Honeycrisp, Pink Lady, and Fuji—product differentiation in action. But competition still erodes profits eventually—the advertising trap means resources spent on differentiation are unavailable for production. The industry produces less than it would under perfect competition. But consumers have a strong preference for variety, so the benefits may equal the costs.

Oligopoly, Game Theory & Cartels

Oligopolists face the prisoner’s dilemma—a game theory scenario where individual rationality leads to collectively suboptimal outcomes. Two gas stations in an isolated town, out of sight of each other, can change prices once daily. The payoff matrix: both charging high = $1,000 each. One high, one low = $300 vs. $1,200. Both low = $750 each.

The dominant strategy is to set low prices—guaranteed $750 versus the $300 risk of being undercut. This is just like the prisoner’s dilemma: individually rational behavior produces a collectively worse outcome ($750 each instead of $1,000).

But there’s a key difference from the textbook prisoner’s dilemma: firms play this game every day, not just once. Day 1: both charge high ($1,000 each). Day 2: one cheats ($1,200 vs. $300). Day 3: the other retaliates—both go low ($750 each). Eventually both learn through tit-for-tat retaliation that cheating today means lower profits tomorrow. They settle into tacit collusion at high prices—without ever explicitly agreeing.

Adam Smith warned: “nothing beneficial comes from the heads of business getting together.” In the U.S., firms are forbidden from cooperating to set prices or production under the Sherman Antitrust Act and Clayton Antitrust Act. Cartels function as monopolies, but their Achilles’ heel is each member’s incentive to cheat. OPEC sets production quotas, but when prices rise, every member wants to exceed its quota. Saudi Arabia disciplines cheaters by flooding the market—as the lowest-cost producer, it can undercut everyone and remain profitable. Drug cartels use more direct methods of enforcement.

The Herfindahl-Hirschman Index (HHI) measures market concentration; the FTC uses it to decide whether to approve mergers. Price leadership (dominant firm sets price, rest follow) is a common form of tacit collusion. Price wars break out periodically—good for consumers but eventually firms return to price leadership. Product differentiation provides another competitive avenue: instead of competing on price, oligopolists compete on differences and uniqueness, spending heavily on advertising.

Monopoly: The Good, The Bad, and The Ugly

Natural monopolies (utilities, water systems) are the good: economies of scale make a single provider most efficient. One large power plant produces electricity at far lower cost per kilowatt than thousands of small generators. Government regulates utility prices to prevent monopoly pricing while allowing reasonable profit.

Technological monopolies (20-year patents) are also good: they encourage innovation by letting inventors recoup R&D costs. Without patent protection, firms would underinvest in research since competitors could immediately copy their discoveries.

Government monopolies (the U.S. Postal Service is the only entity legally allowed to deliver first-class mail) spark philosophical debates about efficiency versus public service.

Pure unregulated monopolies are the ugly: Standard Oil under Rockefeller controlled virtually all U.S. oil production, earning enormous monopoly profits and creating massive barriers to entry until the Sherman Antitrust Act broke it up. De Beers controlled the diamond resource, coerced wholesalers and cutters, and created an illusion of scarcity—“you bought the diamond because the benefit outweighed the cost. The problem is you never realized how much of the cost was De Beers’ profit.”

Market Failures & Government Intervention

When Markets Fail

Markets fail in several identifiable ways. Negative externalities occur when production creates costs for third parties—a shoe factory produces shoes AND air pollution, with neighbors bearing costs not reflected in shoe prices. There is too much of the good being produced because the full cost isn’t captured in the price. Government can tax the producer to reduce output and pollution toward the socially optimal level.

Positive externalities occur when production creates benefits for non-paying third parties—flu vaccines benefit non-vaccinated free-riders; a neighbor’s landscaping increases your home value. When production creates a positive externality, there is never enough of it, so government may subsidize its production. Public schools are the classic example—government subsidizes education because an educated workforce creates a positive externality that businesses don’t have to pay to train.

Public goods are non-excludable (the producer can’t withhold from those unwilling to pay) and non-rivalrous (one person’s consumption doesn’t diminish another’s). National defense and public highways are examples—private firms won’t provide them because they can’t charge free-riders. A movie theater is non-rival but excludable; a candy bar is rival and excludable. Government must provide truly non-excludable, non-rival goods directly.

Information asymmetry undermines market efficiency when one party knows more than the other. Used car sellers know more than buyers—the “lemons problem.” Licensing requirements are barriers to entry that reduce competition but may ensure quality. Concentrated market power (monopoly, oligopoly) allows firms to charge above-competitive prices.

Government can address failures through taxes (on negative externalities), subsidies (on positive externalities), public provision (of public goods), regulation (mandatory disclosure, licensing), and antitrust enforcement. But the response must be carefully calibrated—over-intervention creates its own problems.

Government Failures and Black Markets

Government intervention has its own failures. Pork-barrel politics directs spending to legislators’ districts (“all politics is local”). Regulatory capture lets industries control their regulators. And black markets inevitably develop whenever government attempts to control prices. Markets develop when there is supply and demand for a product—it doesn’t matter what the product is. If government intervention creates surpluses or shortages, someone will step in to circumvent controls and clear the market.

NYC rent control—dating back to WWII—creates a classic black market. Desirable apartments are available at extremely low prices, but almost none are actually available. Rent-controlled tenants sublet to someone willing to pay much more (still below market rate). Both parties benefit, the landlord is no worse off—yet the city actively pursues illegal sublets, spending scarce resources to enforce controls that prevent mutually beneficial transactions.

Ticket scalping exemplifies the same principle: who wants to see the event more—someone willing to pay $50 or $1,000? When ticket prices are limited, those willing to pay more must compete with those who would receive less utility from attending. Workers willing to work for less than minimum wage resort to contract labor or illegal cash payments. The important distinction is between exploitation (one party is coerced) and voluntary transactions where both sides benefit.

Taxes and Subsidies as Market-Shaping Tools

The government has a power businesses envy: the power to coerce payment through taxation. Government uses taxes and subsidies not only to raise revenue or redistribute income, but also to shape incentives and change the marketplace. If government wants to reduce production of a good, tax the producer—raising costs reduces supply.

Sin taxes on alcohol and cigarettes serve a dual purpose: generating revenue while reducing consumption of undesirable products. High sin taxes can, however, create black markets. Luxury taxes have little market impact—if you’re buying a $500,000 automobile, $501,000 doesn’t change your decision. The Civil War currency example shows taxes as market eliminators: prior to the Civil War, thousands of different currencies circulated with no national currency. Congress taxed all non-federal currencies out of existence—would you issue a dollar if it cost you $1.50?

Farm subsidies ensure adequate food supply and give American exports a price advantage, but sometimes pay farmers not to produce, keeping prices artificially high. Critics argue this impedes competition for poor nations dependent on agricultural exports—industrialized nations want poor countries to open up to free trade but are reluctant to end their own farm subsidies, a major sticking point in WTO negotiations.

College loan subsidies are intended to increase access to education, but some economists argue that subsidized loans and grants are part of the problem, not the cure—the financial aid system increases demand for college, which leads to higher tuition. Even food stamps generate black market activity: some recipients trade $2 in stamps for $1 cash, preferring the freedom to buy what they want.

The argument for cash transfers over in-kind benefits is compelling: the taxpayer pays less (same value at a fraction of administrative cost), and the recipient gets equal or greater utility (buys groceries without stigma, can purchase other valued items). The counter-argument—that some might spend cash on cigarettes or alcohol—actually undermines itself: the food stamp recipient who sells $100 in stamps for $50 to buy cigarettes ends up with $50 in cigarettes and no food. The cash recipient who spends $50 on cigarettes still has $50 left for food. The cash system removes the black market and is cheaper to administer.

Part II: Money, Banking & Financial Markets 🔖

The Evolution of Money

From Barter to Fiat

Money is one of humankind’s most widespread and useful inventions. Its evolution mirrors the evolution of trust itself.

Barter came first, but was limited by the “double coincidence of wants”—if the baker doesn’t need eggs, the farmer is stuck. Multi-step barter chains are absurdly complex. Commodity money (gold, silver, salt—Roman soldiers were paid in salt, origin of “salary”) solved this by providing a universal medium. Many things have served: tobacco, shells, stones, leather, cigarettes in prison economies.

Representative money (paper receipts redeemable for gold) was born when English goldsmiths realized people preferred trading lightweight receipts to lugging heavy metal. Finally, fiat money (paper valuable only because the government says so AND people are willing to accept it) emerged as the modern standard. FDR’s 1933 executive order completed the transformation, making the dollar inconvertible to gold for private citizens.

Functions and Properties

Money serves three functions: medium of exchange, unit of account (standard of value), and store of value. Five ideal characteristics: portability, durability, divisibility, stability, and acceptability. Yap island’s giant stone discs weighing hundreds of pounds were decidedly not portable.

Money as Imagination

Fiat money addresses the gold standard’s key weakness: gold constrains the money supply, limiting economic growth. If the economy can produce more goods and services but the money supply can’t grow to match, prices must fall—creating deflationary pressure. Adam Smith argued wealth is the sum of all goods and services an economy produces, so money supply should reflect productive capacity. Fiat money can grow as the economy grows—but the central bank must carefully control the supply. Too plentiful means inflation; too scarce means deflation. The art of central banking is finding the balance.

Today’s money is largely imaginary—“your bank account is not stacks of bills in a vault; it’s information stored on computers. You can go days without touching, seeing, or smelling money. Money is imaginary.” This is not hyperbole: the vast majority of the money supply exists as digital entries, not physical currency.

Because fiat money is backed by nothing but faith, anything that erodes faith destroys money. The Weimar Republic’s hyperinflation proved what happens when the checks and balances of the gold standard are absent and governments overprint: the currency becomes worthless, savings are destroyed, and social order breaks down. The discipline that gold once imposed must now be imposed by institutional design—independent central banks with a mandate for price stability.

The money supply includes M1 (cash + checking deposits + traveler’s checks—used as a medium of exchange) and M2 (M1 + savings + CDs + money market accounts + U.S. dollars in foreign banks—used as a store of value). Currency inside a bank vault is not counted in M1—“it’s not money until you walk out of the bank” (technically inaccurate for a bank robber to demand “money” while inside a bank). If M1 grows faster than labor force + productivity growth, expect inflation. If M2 grows at M1’s expense (people saving instead of spending), that’s a recession signal.

Banks & Money Creation

A Brief History of Banking

Banking traces back to ancient Egyptian granaries with records of deposits and withdrawals, through ancient moneychangers serving merchants and pilgrims, to Italian Renaissance financiers who circumvented the Church’s usury prohibition by lending in one currency and demanding repayment in another—profiting from exchange rate differences while technically not charging “interest.”

English goldsmiths made the crucial discovery: since depositors rarely all demanded their gold simultaneously, goldsmiths could issue more receipts than gold on deposit—lending out gold they didn’t technically own. This was the birth of fractional reserve banking, and with it the creation of money out of thin air. The Knights Templar developed an early form of the check, using signed documents to transfer wealth among their network of houses across Europe—a medieval wire transfer system.

Banks serve three essential functions. First, safe storage—Locke argued secure property is essential to freedom; Jefferson paraphrased him with “pursuit of happiness” instead of “property.” Second, facilitating trade—checks, debit cards, credit cards enable consumers to make purchases more often and in more places, which means businesses employ more resources and create fuller employment. Third, and most importantly, intermediation—connecting savers and borrowers, profiting on the spread between what they pay depositors and charge borrowers.

Savers benefit from safe, liquid returns without having to evaluate individual borrowers. Borrowers get access to a large pool of funds they could never assemble on their own. The economy benefits as borrowers invest in capital, housing, and durable goods. Financial intermediaries are “the grease that makes the wheels turn smoothly”—involved in virtually every transaction, from payroll deposits to mortgage lending, motivated by profit, not goodwill.

The Money Multiplier

Money creation through fractional reserve lending is the foundation of modern economies. Banks maintain capital requirements—actual cash proportional to the riskiness of their loans, ensuring they can pay depositors if borrowers default. With a 10% reserve requirement, a $100,000 deposit becomes $10,000 in required reserves + $90,000 in excess reserves available for lending. The $90,000 is lent, re-deposited, and generates $81,000 in new loans, and so on—ultimately creating up to $1,000,000 in total money supply.

The money multiplier (1 / reserve requirement) determines the maximum amplification. At 10% reserves, the multiplier is 10; at 25%, it’s 4. But the actual multiplier is always smaller than the theoretical maximum because some people hold cash rather than deposit it, and banks don’t always lend all excess reserves.

Money can be destroyed just as easily as it is created: when customers withdraw balances and pay off loans, the process reverses. Maria writes a $10,000 check to pay off her car loan—checkable deposits shrink, money supply shrinks. The multiplier works in both directions. “What’s good for you personally may not be so good for the economy.” This is why mass deleveraging during a financial crisis is so dangerous—as millions of borrowers pay down debt simultaneously, money is destroyed throughout the banking system, amplifying the contraction.

The Banking System and Bank Runs

Banks work as a system, lending and borrowing from each other in the fed funds market. Bank East has excess reserves but no local borrowers; Bank West has borrowers but no excess reserves—Bank West borrows at the fed funds rate. Both profit. But bank runs occur when fear becomes self-fulfilling—rumor triggers withdrawals, lines form, others see the lines and panic. Because banks hold far less than 100% reserves, if enough customers demand deposits simultaneously, the bank cannot pay. Banks can borrow from other banks to cover, but if panic is pervasive, banks refuse to lend to each other—systemic crisis.

The Bank Panic of 1907 led to the Federal Reserve Act of 1913; the Great Depression (when the Fed failed by restricting credit rather than providing liquidity) led to Glass-Steagall and the FDIC. The 1980s S&L crisis (aggressive lending + lax underwriting) cost taxpayers billions. Late 20th century deregulation allowed banks to expand services and operate nationwide but also blurred the line between commercial and investment banking—the key consequence being that “regulations separating traditional banks from speculative investment were repealed, and the seeds of another financial crisis were sowed.”

Loanable Funds & Interest Rates

The Time Value of Money

A dollar today is worth more than a dollar tomorrow—this fundamental principle drives the entire structure of interest rates. Three reasons explain why lending money requires compensation:

First, opportunity cost: you can’t use the money now for your own purposes. Second, inflation: when the loan is eventually repaid, inflation has eroded purchasing power—the chair that cost $50 when you lent the money now costs $55. Third, risk: your friend might move to Costa Rica and “forget” to repay.

Interest is the additional payment to compensate the lender for all three factors. Without interest, people would simply stuff money under their mattresses rather than make it available to borrowers. The entire financial system depends on this compensation mechanism.

The Building Blocks of Interest Rates

The interest rate is built from stacking blocks: the basic real rate (compensation for giving up immediate use), the expected inflation premium (the 1970s-80s saw 20% mortgage rates largely due to high inflation expectations), the default risk premium (why credit scores matter—they quantify default risk), the liquidity premium (how easily the loan can be resold—a 10-year car loan backed by a depreciating asset is harder to resell than a Treasury bond), and the maturity risk premium (risk that rates will change during the loan’s life—longer maturity means higher premium). A real rate of 2% + 3% inflation + 4% default risk + 2% liquidity + 1% maturity = 12% total rate. The Fisher equation distills this: nominal rate = real rate + expected inflation.

Two Competing Theories

The loanable funds market (classical theory) is a simple model for understanding how the real interest rate is determined. It brings together savers (the supply of loanable funds) and borrowers (the demand). The real interest rate is the price; equilibrium occurs where the amount saved equals the amount borrowed.

An important distinction: in economics, “investment” means borrowing to purchase physical capital (tools, factories, equipment). In finance, “investment” means buying securities (stocks, bonds). Savers engage in financial investment, which provides the funds for borrowers to engage in capital investment. The expanded view includes households, businesses, governments, and the foreign sector as both savers and borrowers. Foreign savers deciding to save more in the U.S. increase the supply of loanable funds, lowering the real interest rate. Government deficit spending increases the demand for loanable funds, raising it.

Keynes’s competing liquidity preference theory explains short-term nominal rates through consumers’ inclination for holding cash rather than interest-bearing assets. Central banks act as regulated monopolies, issuing money independent of the interest rate. At high nominal rates, people prefer bonds (sacrificing liquidity for interest income). At low rates, they prefer the safety and flexibility of cash (the opportunity cost of holding cash is low). The Fed’s tool is direct: to lower nominal rates, increase the money supply; to raise them, decrease it. Loanable funds theory is associated with classical economics and explains long-term rates; liquidity preference is associated with Keynesian economics and explains short-term rates.

Bonds, Stocks & Foreign Exchange

The Money Market

The money market handles short-term financing—securities with maturities of less than one year. This is where institutions manage their day-to-day cash needs.

Commercial paper consists of short-term unsecured IOUs issued by businesses with excellent credit—cheaper than longer-term debt, providing lenders a liquid way to earn interest on excess cash. Typically sold in large chunks (~$100,000 per issue), with demand much greater for higher-rated paper. Treasury bills are the government’s short-term borrowing instrument, auctioned for cash needs and sold at a discount from face value—the difference between purchase price and face value equals the interest payment.

Banks lend and borrow federal funds (reserves) from each other to satisfy legal reserve requirements. The fed funds rate—the interest rate banks charge each other for overnight use of these reserves—is the rate the Federal Reserve targets through its open market operations. Most reserves are kept on deposit with the Fed, so the exchange occurs almost immediately as banks swap reserve balances. The commercial paper market’s freeze during 2008 was one of the clearest signals that the financial crisis had reached Main Street.

The Bond Market

Bonds are debt instruments—IOUs that promise periodic interest and return of principal. For long-term financing, governments and firms borrow in the bond market. For issuers, bonds are an efficient means of borrowing large sums. For buyers, they provide relatively secure financial investment with interest income.

Coupon bonds are sold at or near face value and provide guaranteed interest payments. Zero coupon bonds are sold at a discount and pay face value at maturity—for example, buying a $1,000 T-bill for $950 and receiving $50 interest at maturity, a 5.26% return. Bond prices and interest rates move in opposite directions: when rates rise, existing bonds with lower coupons fall in value; when rates fall, existing bonds become more valuable.

The 10-year Treasury note is the crucial benchmark—corporate bonds and mortgages fluctuate with it. Treasury notes (2-10 year maturities) and Treasury bonds (30-year) are backed by the full faith and credit of the U.S. government, which has never defaulted in its 220+ year history. The secondary market for Treasuries is deep and liquid, making them the proxy for the risk-free rate of return.

Agency securities (Fannie Mae, Freddie Mac, Sallie Mae) lack the full guarantee but are seen as virtually guaranteed, offering slightly higher yields. Municipal bonds offer tax-exempt interest, allowing state and local governments to borrow more cheaply—attractive to investors because the tax advantage makes the effective yield competitive.

Corporate bonds offer higher rates to compensate for default risk but allow firms financial leverage: a company with $1,000 earning 10% makes $100, but if it borrows $1,000,000 at a lower rate and invests at 10%, it earns $100,000 without risking its own money. Rating agencies (Moody’s, S&P, Fitch) grade bonds from “prime” to “junk”—but as 2008 showed, many “prime” bonds turned out to be junk within months.

Bond risks include: default risk (governments or firms may fail to repay), inflation risk (rising inflation erodes the real return), interest rate risk (if rates rise, the bond’s value decreases until its effective yield equals the new higher rate), and early call risk (issuers retire bonds when rates drop, eliminating your high-interest income and forcing reinvestment at lower rates).

The Stock Market

Of all financial markets, the stock market receives the most media coverage. Unlike bonds (which are loans), stocks represent partial ownership in firms. Privately held companies keep shares among a small group (founders); publicly traded companies issue stock on exchanges.

Common stock carries voting rights for firm leadership. Preferred stock gets dividends first but has no vote—a trade-off for issuers between maintaining control (common stock risks founders being displaced) and guaranteed dividend obligations (preferred stock may hinder reinvestment). Investors expect either dividends (share of profits distributed to owners) or capital gains (selling at a higher price than purchase). Stock options—derivatives allowing purchase at a predetermined price—are issued to key employees as performance incentives; recipients can sell the option contract or wait and exercise when the stock price rises.

Most stock purchases occur in the secondary market—when you buy Coca-Cola stock, you’re buying from another individual, not from Coca-Cola. The firm only receives money from an IPO (initial public offering) or selling repurchased stock. IPOs are how firms raise money for capital investment by issuing stock for the first time.

Finding the right combination of stock and bond financing is the firm’s capital structure challenge. The inverse relationship between interest rates and investment is critical—the gas pedal/brake pedal analogy captures it: expected rate of return is the gas pedal (profit opportunities accelerate investment); interest rate is the brake pedal (higher rates slow investment). If rates are low and expected profits are high, business grows. When the interest rate exceeds the expected return, firms are stationary.

Foreign Exchange

Foreign exchange markets determine currency values—the world’s largest market, with annual trade volume approaching $1 quadrillion, transactions 24 hours a day, dominated by British, American, and Japanese banks.

Exchange rates are affected by several forces: taste and preference (American popularity of Korean electronics creates demand for the won), interest rate differentials (savers flow toward higher rates until interest rate parity equalizes returns—currency appreciation offsets the rate advantage), inflation (Zimbabwe’s inflation reduced its currency to less than the value of the paper it was printed on), and relative income.

Counterintuitively, as an economy strengthens and incomes rise, its currency can weaken—higher income means higher propensity to import, supplying more domestic currency to foreign exchange markets. “Strong” and “weak” don’t necessarily mean “good” and “bad”—a weak dollar is actually the remedy for a trade deficit.

Speculators bear exchange rate risk so risk-averse companies can use futures contracts to lock in rates and avoid surprises. Purchasing power parity says equivalent goods should cost the same across currencies after adjustment—if snow skis are $500 in the U.S. and SFr 600 in Switzerland when parity says SFr 550, consumers will import from America until the exchange rate adjusts.

Exports & the Balance of Trade

The Balance of Payments

The balance of payments records all currency inflows and outflows between a country and the rest of the world. It has two main accounts: the current account (net exports + net foreign factor income + net transfers) and the financial account (net foreign investment + official reserves).

A critical link: the balance on the current account is completely offset by the balance on the financial account. For every dollar spent on Chinese imports, there is a return flow as Chinese purchase American exports and financial assets. The U.S. trade deficit is the mirror image of foreign capital flowing into America—foreigners accumulate dollars from selling goods to Americans, then use those dollars to buy U.S. Treasury bonds, corporate stocks, and real estate.

The U.S. has a persistent trade deficit in goods (Americans prefer foreign consumer goods) but a surplus in services (transportation, engineering, legal, technical services). In 2008, the trade deficit was roughly $696 billion ($840 billion goods deficit + $144 billion services surplus). Despite engaging in the most international trade by volume, the U.S. remains one of the least trade-dependent nations because net exports are a small fraction of GDP. Half of U.S. trade occurs with just six countries: Canada, Mexico, China, Japan, Germany, and the United Kingdom.

Foreign Investment and Capital Flows

Remittances (unearned transfer payments) create significant outflows—India and Mexico each received roughly $25 billion annually from the U.S. as of 2006. Portfolio investment (foreigners purchasing financial assets like T-bills, stocks, and bonds) creates recurring income. During the 2008 crisis, the dollar actually appreciated as foreign investors sought U.S. Treasury safety—a testament to America’s reserve currency status.

The downside: savings can flow out as easily as they flow in. Argentina’s 2001 default on foreign debt triggered capital flight—savings flowed out, the currency collapsed, and financial and political turmoil followed. Capital controls can prevent outflows but discourage inflows—“the mere mention of capital controls can trigger outflows.” India fared well during the late-1990s Asian financial crisis precisely because capital controls limited outflows—but the same controls discouraged foreign investors from entering.

Exchange Rate Regimes

Exchange rate regimes vary significantly. The U.S. and U.K. let currencies float freely—policymakers can set interest rate policies for domestic growth without worrying about maintaining a fixed rate. China and Japan peg to the dollar by actively trading foreign reserves, keeping their exports relatively cheap and maintaining competitive advantage in the American market. China and Japan together held over $5 trillion in reserves as of 2015—a source of concern, since dumping those holdings could collapse the dollar (though doing so would be equally harmful to the dumpers).

Europe created the euro—the world’s second-largest currency—eliminating exchange rate risk within the eurozone but stripping individual countries of independent monetary policy. A Greek company trading with a German company no longer faced currency risk—a genuine benefit. But Greece’s 2010 sovereign debt crisis exposed the fatal drawback: unable to devalue its currency or set its own interest rates, Greece was forced into brutal austerity or risk default. A country with its own currency can always inflate away some of its debt; a eurozone member cannot.

The original Bretton Woods system (post-WWII fixed rates pegged to the dollar, which was pegged to gold at $35/ounce) forced countries to practice sound economic policy but completely collapsed by 1971 when the growing U.S. trade deficit made the gold standard unsustainable. Nixon “closed the gold window,” ending dollar-to-gold convertibility and ushering in the modern era of floating exchange rates.

Part III: Macroeconomics 🔖

The Circular Flow

The Private Sector

The circular flow model is the most fundamental diagram in macroeconomics, showing how the economy’s three sectors interact through markets. The private sector consists of households and businesses, connected through two markets.

In the product market, households buy goods and services from firms. In the factor market, households sell labor and entrepreneurship in exchange for wages and profits, and sell natural resources in exchange for rents. If you work for someone, you sell labor in the factor market to get money for goods in the product market. As a business owner, you sell entrepreneurial ability in exchange for profits. The key insight: money flows in a continuous loop—firms pay households for factors of production, and households pay firms for goods and services.

Two flows circulate between households and firms: goods, services, and factors of production flow in one direction; money (consumption spending and factor payments—rent, wages, interest, profits) flows the opposite direction.

The Public and Foreign Sectors

The public sector (all levels of government) interacts with both households and firms. Government purchases factors of production (land, labor) in exchange for factor payments, buys goods and services in the product market, combines them to provide public goods (national defense, police, fire protection, schools, libraries, roads), collects taxes from both product and factor markets (personal income tax, corporate profits tax, sales tax), and makes transfer payments (Social Security, welfare) and subsidies.

The foreign sector adds exports, imports, foreign factor payments, remittances (India and Mexico each received ~$25 billion annually from the U.S. as of 2006), and foreign investment. The U.S. enjoys cheap Chinese imports because of a counterbalancing flow of Chinese savings into U.S. financial assets. The balance on the current account is completely offset by the balance on the financial account—for every dollar spent on Chinese imports, there is a return flow as Chinese purchase American exports and financial assets.

Financial Intermediaries and the Savings Dimension

Financial intermediaries (banks, credit unions, insurance companies, stock exchanges) are “the grease that makes the wheels turn smoothly”—involved in virtually every transaction from payroll deposits to mortgage lending. Most product market transactions involve checks, debit cards, credit cards, ATM cash, or foreign exchange—all provided by financial intermediaries motivated by profit, not goodwill.

The savings dimension adds a critical third layer to the circular flow. Up to this point, the model has been dollar-in, dollar-out. But private, public, and foreign sectors also save—and these savings flow to financial intermediaries, which channel them (as loans) back to all sectors. Households save by buying stocks, bonds, and CDs. Governments finance spending beyond tax revenue by issuing Treasury securities. Capital itself is a factor of production, and the financial sector is where people find capital—generating significant revenue from interest on mortgages and loans.

GDP is represented three equivalent ways in the circular flow: (1) the value of all domestic production, (2) total spending by all sectors, and (3) all rent, wages, interest, and profits earned in the factor market—the nation’s income. These three measures should, in theory, yield the same number—every dollar spent is a dollar earned, and every dollar earned was spent on something produced. The circular flow model reveals this fundamental identity.

GDP & National Income

What GDP Measures

Gross Domestic Product is the market value of all final goods and services produced within a country in a given year. It was developed during WWII so the government could understand the economy’s capacity to produce war materials—a practical origin that explains why GDP measures output, not happiness. In 2015, U.S. GDP was $18.12 trillion, making it the world’s largest economy.

GDP vs. GNP: GDP measures production done in a country (geographic basis); GNP measures production done by a country (nationality basis). Toyotas manufactured in Texas count as U.S. GDP but not U.S. GNP—they are Japanese GNP. An American working in London contributes to U.K. GDP but U.S. GNP.

GDP is a flow (water from the faucet), not a stock (water accumulated in the tub). It measures new production per year, not accumulated wealth. Depreciation is the drain—capital wearing out, goods consumed. As long as GDP (inflow) exceeds depreciation (outflow), the nation’s wealth grows. GDP measures what is new, not what has accumulated.

Three Measurement Approaches

GDP can be measured three ways, all theoretically yielding the same answer:

The expenditure approach (C + I + G + NX) adds up all spending on final goods and services—the U.S. primarily uses this method. The income approach adds up all rent, wages, interest, and profits earned producing domestic output—theoretically identical to expenditure (every dollar spent is a dollar earned), but harder to calculate in practice because of income underreporting. The production approach calculates value added at each stage of production—not used by U.S. agencies because of the difficulty distinguishing intermediate goods from final goods (is a pound of sugar intermediate or final? It depends on whether it goes to a bakery or a consumer).

Components of GDP

Consumer spending accounts for roughly 70% of U.S. GDP—it is the economy’s engine, constrained chiefly by disposable income (income after taxes). This gives government considerable leverage over households’ capacity to consume. Disposable income is either spent in the product market or saved in financial markets—increased consumption means less saving, and vice versa.

Three factors influence consumption beyond income: wealth effects (increases in housing or stock prices encourage spending; decreases discourage it—the housing price implosion led to less consumption and more saving to rebuild net worth), interest rates (durable goods like cars and appliances are often purchased with borrowed money, so high rates discourage purchases and low rates encourage them), and expectations (when people fear the future, they save and avoid major purchases; when upbeat, they spend more). Autonomous consumption—necessities like food, rent, and healthcare—continues regardless of income. Post-9/11, autonomous consumption of necessities continued but durable goods and discretionary spending halted (except guns and ammo).

Private investment (~15-18%) is the most volatile component of GDP, including business capital purchases, inventory, and new home construction. Investment is heavily influenced by expectations of future business conditions—positive expectations fuel investment, negative expectations freeze it.

The distinction between planned and unplanned investment is crucial. Planned investment reflects deliberate decisions based on expected conditions. Unplanned investment—inventory piling up on shelves because consumers aren’t buying—is an ominous signal. Auto dealers stop ordering from factories, factories stop production, and if the pattern is pervasive, mass unemployment and recession follow. Watching inventory levels is one of the earliest warning signs of a turning business cycle.

Government spending (~20% of GDP) ranges from ~16% (Bangladesh) to ~65% (Cuba) globally, and trends upward regardless of which political party holds power. Representatives and senators direct federal spending to their districts for re-election—“all politics is local,” and failure to “bring home the pork” usually leads to replacement at the next election.

Net exports are typically negative for the U.S.—Americans import more than they export. Net exports are influenced by exchange rates and interest rates: dollar appreciation makes American goods more expensive abroad, reducing exports while making imports cheaper; dollar depreciation does the opposite.

What GDP Doesn’t Count

GDP excludes several categories of economic activity, revealing its limitations as a comprehensive measure:

Purely financial transactions: when you buy stock, only the broker’s commission counts—no good or service is produced in the exchange itself. Transfer payments: Social Security checks are not made in return for current production. Unpaid labor: a stay-at-home parent’s work is excluded entirely, but paying someone to do the exact same tasks would be counted—an obvious inconsistency that undervalues domestic labor. Resales: an existing home was counted in the year it was built; reselling it adds nothing new. Intermediate production: flour, butter, and sugar sold to a bakery are not counted separately—only the final bread is counted to avoid double-counting the same value at each stage.

What GDP does count includes imputed rent for homeowners (the BEA treats everyone as a renter, estimating what homeowners would pay) and new home construction (the only time a home purchase is counted in GDP).

Nominal vs. Real GDP

Nominal GDP uses current prices; real GDP holds prices constant to allow valid year-to-year comparisons. Without adjusting for inflation, the economy may appear to be growing when really only prices are rising. This distinction is critical for honest economic analysis.

The GDP deflator (nominal ÷ real) measures the difference between the two. Consider a beach ball economy: in year one, 100 balls are produced at $1 each = $100 nominal GDP. In year two, 100 balls at $1.25 each = $125 nominal GDP. Nominal GDP appears to grow 25%—but real GDP (using year-one prices) is unchanged at $100. Only inflation rose. The GDP deflator of 1.25 reveals the 25% price increase.

Beyond GDP

RFK’s famous 1968 critique noted that GDP counts napalm and nuclear warheads but not “the joy of play.” GDP per capita gives no indication of income distribution. The U.S. and Norway both have high GDP per capita, but U.S. incomes are much more unequally distributed.

Alternative measures attempt to fill these gaps: the Index of Sustainable Economic Welfare (includes income distribution and pollution), the Genuine Progress Indicator (includes unpaid household labor), the Fordham Index of Social Health (accounts for drug use and homicide), the UN Human Development Index (life expectancy and education), and the Happy Planet Index (personal well-being). Some countries are exploring “Green GDP” that accounts for the environmental cost of growth. Defenders counter that it is because of high GDP that people are wealthy enough to care about the environment in the first place—the highest-GDP countries often do the most to address environmental issues.

The Business Cycle

The Four Stages

Over the last 50+ years, America has alternated between periods of recession and expansion, against a background of sustained long-run growth. Since 1960, U.S. real GDP has increased by more than $10 trillion. The economy’s capacity to produce grows steadily as the workforce expands and productivity improves—this explains the long-run upward trend.

But around this upward trend, the economy cycles through four stages in a pattern that has repeated throughout recorded economic history:

Expansion: GDP grows month-over-month, unemployment declines, business confidence rises. Peak: real GDP spending at its highest—just before unemployment rises and indicators fall. Contraction: GDP growth slows or declines. A recession is formally defined as two consecutive quarters of declining real GDP. Trough: the lowest point before recovery begins, when GDP starts to rebound.

A key limitation: economists can only determine what stage the economy is in after the fact. The highway analogy captures this perfectly: driving on a two-lane highway represents normal economic growth. Passing slower traffic represents expansion (spending exceeds productive capacity). Swerving back to avoid an oncoming truck represents contraction. Doing all of this blindfolded with a Swahili-speaking navigator represents how economists actually try to predict the future.

Theories of Business Cycles

Three competing theories explain why economies cycle.

Milton Friedman’s monetarists blame poor management of the money supply—overexpansion means too much money chasing too few goods (inflation), while contraction means too little money for the economy’s needs (recession). Get the money supply right, Friedman argued, and the business cycle largely disappears.

Keynes’s “animal spirits” theory argues that businesses are essentially bipolar—manic investing leads to depressive withdrawal, driven by emotion that clouds rational decision-making. Investment decisions are not purely mathematical but are colored by waves of optimism and pessimism that feed on themselves in self-reinforcing cycles.

Real Business Cycle Theory argues that changes in productivity (technology shocks, resource availability) are the ultimate cause of economic fluctuations—not spending changes or monetary policy mistakes. In this view, recessions are the economy’s rational adjustment to real changes in productive capacity.

Unemployment

Defining and Measuring Unemployment

The unemployment rate measures the percentage of the labor force (not the total population) that is actively searching but not currently employed. Of America’s ~300 million people, only ~145 million are employed—but the remaining 155 million are not all unemployed. The labor force = employed + unemployed. Those meeting neither criterion—full-time students, retirees, stay-at-home parents, disabled persons, the institutionalized, and active military—are not in the labor force.

An important subtlety: the unemployment rate can rise even while total employment increases. When demographic shifts bring more people into the labor force (women entering the workforce during WWII, veterans returning to civilian life, baby boomers reaching working age), the ratio of unemployed to labor force can increase even as total jobs grow. The labor force is in constant flux—people constantly entering, exiting, re-entering; getting hired, fired, furloughed, quitting, retiring.

The BLS publishes six measures (U1-U6), from narrowest to broadest: U1 (unemployed 15+ weeks, most restrictive), U2 (job losers and temporary workers), U3 (the official rate—total unemployed as a percentage of the labor force), U4 (adds discouraged workers who’ve given up searching because they believe no jobs exist for them), U5 (adds all marginally attached workers—those who searched in the past 12 months but not the last 4 weeks), and U6 (adds involuntary part-timers who want full-time work but can’t find it—the most inclusive measure).

U6 is typically about double the official U3 rate and is widely considered a better gauge of true labor market weakness. Both the labor force participation rate (~65%) and the employment-to-population ratio have been declining—as young people delay entry into the labor force (college, military service)—suggesting the labor market may be considerably weaker than the headline rate implies.

Three Types of Unemployment

Frictional unemployment is voluntary and temporary—workers between jobs, seeking better opportunities, relocating, or entering the workforce for the first time. It is actually a positive sign of a dynamic economy where workers have the freedom and confidence to leave one job in search of something better. Better information (job boards, networking, employment agencies) reduces frictional unemployment by helping workers and employers find each other faster.

Structural unemployment is a mismatch between workers’ skills or location and available jobs. It can be caused by technological change, globalization, shifts in consumer demand, or geographic immobility. Schumpeter’s creative destruction captures the dynamic: typewriter manufacturers were destroyed by the personal computer, but new jobs emerged in software and hardware. The typewriter workers, however, couldn’t simply become programmers overnight—structural unemployment can persist for years as retraining takes time.

Efficiency wages—paying above-market rates to attract better workers, reduce turnover, and boost productivity—can themselves create structural unemployment. By holding wages sticky above equilibrium, they create a permanent surplus of workers who want those well-paying jobs but can’t get them. The firm benefits from higher quality workers, but the labor market as a whole has more unemployment than it otherwise would.

Cyclical unemployment is caused by business cycle contractions—the dangerous kind that creates feedback loops: one group becomes unemployed → they spend less → businesses lose revenue → more layoffs → more reduced spending → cascading unemployment. During the Great Depression, unemployment reached 25%. The 2007-2009 recession saw unemployment rise from about 5% to 10%. This is the type that concerns policymakers most.

Full Employment and Okun’s Law

Full employment does not mean zero unemployment—it means zero cyclical unemployment. The natural rate (roughly 4-6% in the U.S.) is the unemployment rate consistent with full employment, accounting for the frictional and structural unemployment that always exists in a dynamic economy. Developed by Friedman and Phelps, the natural rate is not fixed—it can change over time as technology, demographics, and labor market institutions evolve. Market-oriented economies tend to have lower natural rates; heavily regulated economies (like France’s 35-hour work week) tend to have higher ones.

Okun’s Law: for every 1% that unemployment exceeds the natural rate, actual GDP falls approximately 2% below potential. The 2:1 ratio captures the cascading effects of idle workers. In 2009, with ~10% unemployment and ~5% natural rate, the GDP gap was roughly $1-2 trillion per year—equivalent to France’s entire economy being wasted.

Beyond the economic cost, unemployment carries devastating human consequences: savings depleted, health deteriorates, families fracture, crime increases, and long-term “scarring” means workers who experience extended unemployment earn less for the rest of their careers. The unemployed do not simply pause and resume—their skills atrophy, their networks decay, and their confidence erodes.

Structural Shifts and Demographics

The U.S. economy has shifted from agrarian to manufacturing to service sector over two centuries, and each transition displaced workers who had to adapt or be left behind. Globalization shifted many low-skill manufacturing jobs overseas. Demand for labor is increasingly driven by worker productivity and skills rather than raw supply.

Education is consistently the strongest predictor of employment. May 2015 data reveals a stark gradient: less than HS diploma = 8.6% unemployment; high school graduate = 5.8%; some college = 4.4%; bachelor’s degree or higher = 2.7%. Each additional level of education roughly halves the unemployment rate.

Demographic disparities are equally striking: in 2008, married white females aged 25+ had 3.3% unemployment versus single black males under 25 at 17.5%—a five-fold gap. Race, age, gender, marital status, and education all intersect to create vastly different labor market experiences within the same economy.

Inflation, Disinflation & Deflation

What Inflation Is and How It’s Measured

Inflation is a general, sustained increase in prices—equivalently, a decrease in purchasing power. Moderate inflation (~2% annually) is considered healthy; prices double roughly every 36 years at this rate.

Historical extremes provide stark warnings. Weimar Germany (1921-23) saw such extreme hyperinflation that citizens needed wheelbarrows of cash to buy bread. The destruction of the middle class’s savings created the social instability that enabled Hitler’s rise—a reminder that monetary catastrophe has political consequences. Hungary 1945-46 holds the record for worst hyperinflation in history—prices reached octillions (1027), doubling every 15 hours at the peak. Every case of hyperinflation in recorded history involved governments monetizing their debt—printing money to finance spending. This is why central bank independence from political pressure is not a luxury but a necessity.

Three measures track inflation from different angles. The CPI (Consumer Price Index) tracks a fixed basket of goods monthly—most widely reported but doesn’t capture substitution behavior (when steak gets expensive, people buy chicken instead, but the CPI basket still prices steak). The PPI (Producer Price Index) measures wholesale/producer prices and serves as a leading indicator—when producer costs rise, those costs are eventually passed to consumers.

The PCE deflator (Personal Consumption Expenditure) is the Fed’s preferred measure because it adjusts for consumer substitution behavior, providing a more accurate picture of actual price pressures. Headline inflation includes everything; core inflation excludes volatile food and energy to reveal underlying trends. The Fed pays closest attention to core PCE when making policy decisions.

Types of Inflation

Demand-pull inflation (“too much money chasing too few goods”) is fueled by rising incomes, easy credit, or government spending. It can spread across borders as domestic consumers demand more imports.

Cost-push inflation occurs when input costs rise (like OPEC’s 1970s oil price shocks), creating the toxic combination of higher prices AND reduced output AND higher unemployment—stagflation. Cost-push is self-limiting: reduced GDP eventually brings prices back down. The critical policy insight: the correct response for each type is completely different. Treating cost-push like demand-pull—as the 1970s Fed did—is “like pouring water on a grease fire.”

Asset inflation (concentrated in specific sectors, like the mid-2000s housing bubble) can spill into broader demand-pull through the wealth effect. When housing prices rise, homeowners feel wealthier and spend more freely—the bubble inflates the broader economy until it pops.

Winners, Losers, and Hidden Costs

When inflation is expected and stable, it’s benign—people plan for it. When unexpected, it creates winners and losers. Winners: borrowers (debt repaid in cheaper dollars—if banks lend at 5% expecting 2% inflation but actual inflation is 4%, borrowers’ real rate drops to 1%), producers (prices rise before sticky wages catch up), and indebted governments (developing countries have historically tried to erase foreign debts by overprinting).

Losers: savers and lenders (a $1,000 CD paying 4% becomes a real loss when inflation rises to 5%—the lender is repaid in dollars worth less than those originally lent), low-income households (more wealth held in cash, which is directly eroded by rising prices), and fixed-income earners (COLA adjustments “are too little and come too late”). Inflation also distorts price signals, making it harder to distinguish between relative price changes (one good becoming more expensive than another) and general price level changes (all prices rising together). This confusion leads to misallocation of resources—producers may expand capacity for a product whose price rise reflects only inflation, not increased demand.

Inflation also creates shoe-leather costs (rushing to spend depreciating money, literally wearing out shoes) and menu costs (constantly repricing goods—reprinting menus, updating price tags, reprogramming registers). The laminated menu indicator testifies: restaurants that laminate their menus testify to price stability; chalkboard prices signal volatility.

Disinflation and the Power of Expectations

Paul Volcker ended the Great Inflation in the early 1980s by raising the fed funds rate above 20%, deliberately inducing the worst recession in decades. The short-term pain was severe: unemployment soared, businesses failed, farmers protested outside the Fed. But the long-term benefits were transformative: the Great Moderation followed—decades of lower and more stable interest rates, less pressure to increase wages, and capital investment that became less costly and easier to plan.

Volcker’s triumph was fundamentally about credibility. He said he would crush inflation, and he did—regardless of the political cost. The parent who follows through on their threat restores authority. The Fed that raises rates when it says it will manages expectations more effectively than any press release.

The fear of inflation can itself create inflation—self-fulfilling prophecies where consumers spend faster (demand-pull) and workers demand higher wages (cost-push), reinforcing each other in a vicious cycle. If policymakers fail to manage expectations, a higher expected inflation rate embeds itself into the economic consciousness. Managing expectations requires credible action, not just talk—Volcker “said what he meant and meant what he said.”

The Deflation Danger

Deflation—sustained falling prices—sounds like a consumer paradise but is extremely dangerous. The logic of the deflationary spiral is insidious: why buy a car today if it will be cheaper next month? Why invest in a factory if the goods it produces will sell for less by the time they’re finished? Consumers delay purchases → manufacturing grinds to a halt → widespread layoffs → reduced income → even less spending → further deflation. Each step reinforces the next.

The central bank hits the zero lower bound with no conventional ammunition left. Interest rates cannot go below zero (or close to it)—you cannot pay people to borrow. If consumers and investors won’t borrow even at 0% interest, conventional monetary policy is exhausted. This is Keynes’s liquidity trap—the economy sits in a pool of unused savings with no mechanism to convert them into spending.

Friedman argued that in an economy with fiat money, deflation should never be permanent—the central bank can always print more. “Like dropping cash from helicopters.” Bernanke reiterated this in a 2002 speech on deflation prevention, earning the nickname “Helicopter Ben.” When the 2008 crisis came, this is essentially what he did through quantitative easing—creating trillions of dollars to buy assets no one else wanted.

Aggregate Demand & Aggregate Supply

Aggregate Demand

Aggregate demand (AD) is the total demand for all final domestic production in a country—relating the overall price level to the amount of real GDP demanded. Unlike microeconomic demand for a single good, AD represents the entire economy’s spending at each price level. AD comes from all four sectors: households (consumption), businesses (investment), government (spending), and the foreign sector (net exports).

The AD curve slopes downward for three reasons: the wealth effect (higher prices reduce the purchasing power of money and financial assets), the interest rate effect (higher prices increase demand for money, pushing up interest rates and discouraging consumption and investment), and the net export effect (higher domestic prices make domestic goods more expensive for foreigners, reducing exports).

Shifts in AD occur when components change independent of the price level. Consumer and business confidence improves → more consumption and investment → AD increases. Government spending increases or taxes decrease → AD increases. Net exports increase → AD increases. Opposite changes decrease AD. These shifts—not movements along the curve—are what matter for policy analysis.

Short-Run Aggregate Supply

Short-run aggregate supply (SRAS) slopes upward because wages are sticky—they don’t adjust immediately to price changes. When prices rise but wages stay fixed, firms earn higher profits per unit and expand output. When prices fall with wages fixed, firms face losses and reduce output. This is the critical Keynesian insight: in the short run, price changes affect real output because input prices lag behind.

SRAS shifts when per-unit production costs change: productivity increases reduce costs, shifting SRAS right. Regulation increases raise compliance costs, shifting SRAS left. Taxes on producers reduce capacity (SRAS left); subsidies increase it (SRAS right). Rising inflationary expectations cause workers to demand higher wages, lenders to demand higher interest, and commodity speculation to increase—all shifting SRAS left.

Long-Run Aggregate Supply

Long-run aggregate supply (LRAS) is vertical at potential (full-employment) GDP. In the long run, input prices fully adjust to match output prices, so firms have no incentive to change output regardless of the price level. The distinction is temporal: the short run is when wages are sticky; the long run is when they’ve fully adjusted.

LRAS changes only when the factors of production themselves change—not prices, not policy, but the actual productive capacity of the economy. More factors mean economic growth—LRAS shifts right. Fewer factors mean long-term decline—LRAS shifts left.

Historical examples illustrate both directions: the Black Plague reduced Europe’s labor force by a third, shifting LRAS dramatically left. The steam engine increased productive capacity beyond anything previously imaginable, shifting LRAS right. Modern examples: immigration expands the labor force; technological innovation expands productivity; capital investment deepens the stock of tools and equipment available to each worker.

The Key Asymmetry

Macroeconomic equilibrium occurs where AD intersects AS. The key insight is an asymmetry that has enormous policy implications:

AD changes move GDP and prices in the same direction: AD increases → both real GDP and the price level rise; AD decreases → both fall. This makes demand-side problems relatively straightforward to diagnose—recession means AD has fallen, inflation means AD has risen.

SRAS changes move GDP and prices in opposite directions: SRAS decreases → less output at higher prices (stagflation); SRAS increases → more output at lower prices (the best of all worlds). This asymmetry explains why the 1970s confounded policymakers so completely: the problem was a supply shock (OPEC oil prices pushed SRAS left), but they kept treating it as a demand problem and applying demand-side tools. Stimulating demand against a supply shock is like pressing the gas pedal when the engine is broken.

Part IV: Economic Policy & Schools of Thought 🔖

The Classical View

The Self-Correcting Market

Classical economics, dominant before the Great Depression, espouses laissez-faire—the belief that markets are inherently stable and self-correcting. The school encompasses any market-based approach that emphasizes the inherent stability of aggregate demand and supply. Key assumptions: efficient markets quickly reach equilibrium, extended unemployment is impossible, and flexible wages and prices allow rapid adjustment to any shock.

The key mechanism is elegant: when consumers stop spending, they’re saving instead → more loanable funds → lower interest rates → more investment → consumption decreases are offset by investment increases → AD is stable. If shocks occur, workers accept lower wages in deflation and demand higher wages in inflation, and the economy self-corrects to full employment without intervention.

Say’s Law

Jean-Baptiste Say formulated the cornerstone: “supply creates its own demand.” The act of producing goods creates the income necessary to buy those goods. Surpluses and gluts cannot persist in a market economy. This is both obviously true (producing $1 of goods creates $1 of income somewhere) and, as Keynes would later show, obviously false (savings gluts and liquidity traps can break the link for extended periods).

The Classical Prescription

The classical response to recession: do nothing. Lower GDP creates unemployment → downward pressure on wages → workers accept less → firms hire more (lower costs) → SRAS increases → economy returns to full employment. No government intervention needed.

The classical response to inflation: also do nothing. Higher GDP → unemployment below the natural rate → competition for scarce workers drives wages up → production costs rise → firms reduce output → economy returns to full employment at a higher but stable price level.

The elegance of this framework made it orthodox for over a century. Markets had always recovered from downturns before—panics came and went, and the economy always bounced back. Then came the Great Depression, and the economy refused to self-correct. Unemployment stuck at 25% for years. Wages refused to fall enough to clear the labor market. Interest rates dropped to near zero, yet investment refused to materialize—businesses saw no customers, so no return on investment regardless of how cheap borrowing became. The classical model had no answer for a world where Say’s Law had broken down.

Keynes & Fiscal Policy

The Keynesian Revolution

John Maynard Keynes was a classically trained British economist who concluded during the Great Depression that classical assumptions did not describe reality. He wrote The General Theory of Employment, Interest, and Money (1936), challenging prevailing orthodoxy and concluding that government intervention was warranted. Crucially, Keynes was not a socialist—he was an advocate of capitalism who argued for a hybrid form that uses government intervention when markets fail.

His two devastating observations against classical economics:

(1) Saving does NOT automatically translate into investment—savings gluts can and did occur, meaning aggregate demand is inherently unstable. Decreases in consumption were NOT offset by increases in investment as classical theory predicted. People were saving more during the Depression, but banks had no one creditworthy to lend to—savings piled up without generating investment.

(2) Wages are NOT downwardly flexible—workers refuse pay cuts and employers don’t offer them. Sticky wages meant unemployment could persist indefinitely because market forces failed to bring the economy back to full employment. The classical prescription of “wait for wages to fall” was irrelevant when wages refused to budge.

The Multiplier Effect

The Keynesian prescription for recession: increase government spending and reduce taxes to spur aggregate demand, even if it means running deficits. This works because of the multiplier effect.

Keynes observed that individuals have a marginal propensity to consume (MPC)—if you give people a dollar, they spend some and save some. With an MPC of 80%, $50 billion in infrastructure spending cascades through the economy: contractors and employees receive $50 billion and spend $40 billion → those recipients spend $32 billion → the next round spends $25.6 billion → and on through successive rounds → total spending eventually reaches $250 billion. A single dollar of government spending generates five dollars of economic activity.

The multiplier formula is 1 / (1 - MPC). If MPC = 0.8, the multiplier is 5. If MPC = 0.9, the multiplier is 10—a more consumption-oriented society amplifies government spending more powerfully. Government spending yields a larger multiplier than equal-sized tax cuts because people save a portion of tax cuts rather than spending 100% of them. A tax cut of $50 billion with an MPC of 0.8 only injects $40 billion in the first round (the rest is saved), producing a smaller cascade.

Keynesian Policy in Practice

The Employment Act of 1946, signed by President Truman, made Keynesian policy the law of the land—requiring the federal government to pursue maximum employment, production, and purchasing power, and creating the Council of Economic Advisors to provide the President with economic analysis and recommendations. This was a watershed moment in American economic history: the federal government was now legally committed to managing the business cycle, not simply letting markets self-correct. The classical era of laissez-faire was over in practice, even if the debate continued in theory.

The classical counterargument persists: government spending crowds out more efficient private spending. Every dollar the government spends is a dollar taken from the private sector through taxes or borrowing. When government borrows to fund stimulus, it competes with private borrowers for loanable funds, driving up interest rates and discouraging the very investment it seeks to encourage.

Keynesians respond that during recessions, this argument breaks down—private spending has already collapsed, and the savings that would normally fund private investment are sitting idle. The government is spending money that would otherwise do nothing. The debate between “let you buy your television in peace” and “the multiplier will create more wealth than your television purchase” continues to this day, with the answer depending heavily on whether the economy is at full employment or in recession.

The Phillips Curve

Discovery and the “Menu of Choices”

A.W. Phillips, a New Zealand economist, discovered an inverse relationship between unemployment and wage inflation using nearly 100 years of British data. American economists Paul Samuelson and Robert Solow adapted it for the U.S., comparing general price inflation (not just wage inflation) to unemployment. Policymakers embraced the Phillips Curve as a “menu of choices”—they could reduce unemployment from 7% to 5% at the cost of inflation rising from 1% to 2%. For the first time, economic management seemed like a dial that could be turned to the desired setting.

The 1970s Demolition

The 1970s stagflation destroyed this comfortable framework. Both inflation AND unemployment rose simultaneously—an outcome the Phillips Curve said was impossible. OPEC’s oil embargo created a supply shock that pushed costs up (inflation) while reducing output (unemployment). The Misery Index (unemployment + inflation, normally around 7) reached an all-time high of 20.76 under Carter. The menu of choices had proven illusory—policymakers couldn’t simply dial between inflation and unemployment after all.

Milton Friedman and Edmund Phelps argued this was proof that the Phillips Curve was a short-run phenomenon only—and more importantly, proof that Keynesian economics was fundamentally flawed. They introduced the natural rate hypothesis: unemployment is independent of inflation in the long run. Government efforts to reduce unemployment by creating temporary inflation would be ineffective—workers would simply demand higher nominal wages to keep real wages constant.

Short-Run vs. Long-Run

The modern consensus recognizes two Phillips Curves. The short-run Phillips Curve exists during periods of stable inflation expectations—it shows a genuine trade-off between inflation and unemployment. The long-run Phillips Curve is vertical at the natural rate—no trade-off exists once expectations adjust.

Phillips’s original data came from a period of stable inflation expectations (the British gold standard era). The 1971 collapse of Bretton Woods introduced inflation uncertainty, breaking the short-run relationship. Money illusion—the Keynesian assumption that people prefer earning $100/hour paying $10/gallon for gas over earning $10/hour paying $1/gallon, even though real purchasing power is identical—allows the short-run trade-off to exist temporarily.

Implications for Policy

Greenspan credited productivity gains from globalization for allowing both low inflation and low unemployment simultaneously during his tenure—the short-run curve had shifted favorably. The key limitation remains: Keynesian AD-stimulating policies only work as long as inflation expectations remain stable. If those policies create higher expectations, attempts to reduce unemployment will only create more inflation. The 1970s proved this conclusively.

The Federal Reserve System

America’s Love-Hate Relationship with Central Banking

After the Revolution, Alexander Hamilton proposed the First Bank of the United States (1791-1811), modeled on the Bank of England and headquartered in Philadelphia. It was chartered to handle war debt and create a unified currency, but its charter was allowed to expire. The Second Bank of the United States (1816-1841) was created to help recover from the War of 1812 and establish uniform currency, but was believed corrupt. President Andrew Jackson killed it in 1833 by withdrawing government deposits to state-chartered banks—bankrupt by 1841.

During and after the Civil War, a decentralized system of national banks was created, reintroducing uniform currency and allowing government to borrow by issuing bonds. But this system didn’t account for contagion—a small bank run could cascade into systemic crisis.

The Bank Panic of 1907 and the Fed’s Creation

The Bank Panic of 1907 proved America needed a permanent lender of last resort. A Montana investor’s failed attempt to corner the copper market triggered a contagion of bank runs across New York. J.P. Morgan and Theodore Roosevelt’s Treasury secretary personally injected cash to stop the panic. The lesson was clear: a nation this large could not rely on one private banker’s personal wealth to prevent systemic collapse.

The Federal Reserve Act of 1913, signed by Woodrow Wilson, drew from the strengths of its predecessors while avoiding their weaknesses. The First and Second Banks had been too centralized and too politically vulnerable. The new system would be different: a decentralized, public-private banking system—neither purely governmental nor purely private—with 12 separate district banks across the country. This hybrid design was a deliberate compromise between those who feared government control and those who feared private banking power.

Structure

The Board of Governors (the government arm) is headquartered in Washington, appointed by the President, and confirmed by the Senate for staggered 14-year terms. The chairman and vice chairman serve unlimited 4-year terms. The Fed chairman is considered second in power only to the President when it comes to economic influence.

12 Federal Reserve District Banks act as bankers’ banks—accepting deposits from member banks, enforcing regulations, processing checks and electronic payments, and issuing currency obtained from the Treasury. Each district bank has its own president and board of directors. The Treasury keeps its accounts with the Fed; all taxes collected and bond proceeds flow into Treasury’s account, and government checks (refunds, Social Security) are drawn from them.

The FOMC

The Federal Open Market Committee is the chief architect of monetary policy—12 voting members including the Fed chairman, Board of Governors members, the New York Fed president (permanent), and four rotating district presidents. All district presidents attend meetings even when not voting. The FOMC meets 8 times per year (~every 6 weeks) to review economic conditions and set the fed funds rate target.

FOMC press releases are closely monitored by media and financial markets for clues about future policy direction—hence “Fed speak” and the famous briefcase indicator. Greenspan was notoriously cryptic: “I guess I should warn you, if I turn out to be particularly clear, you’ve probably misunderstood what I’ve said.”

Monetary Policy

Goals and Transmission

The Fed pursues three goals: price stability, full employment, and economic growth. The transmission mechanism traces how policy flows through the economy: Fed action → excess reserves in the banking system → money supply → interest rates → consumption, investment, net exports → aggregate demand → GDP, inflation, unemployment. Each link in this chain introduces a lag, which is why monetary policy operates with a delay—actions taken today may not fully affect the economy for 6-18 months.

The Fed’s power is fundamentally asymmetric: it can definitely stop inflation (raise rates without limit) but can only encourage growth (lower rates, but zero is the floor). It can slam the brakes but only suggest hitting the gas. This asymmetry defines the limits of monetary policy and explains why deflation is considered more dangerous than inflation—the Fed has unlimited ammunition against rising prices but runs out of conventional tools against falling ones.

Three Tools

Reserve requirements: The percentage of checking account balances banks must hold in reserve and cannot lend out. Raising it reduces lending and contracts the money supply; lowering it increases available reserves and expands lending. This is the most powerful tool in theory but rarely used in practice. The asymmetry is telling: it’s easy to lower (banks can lend more immediately), but nearly impossible to raise during inflation because banks likely have no excess reserves to comply with the increase, which would precipitate a liquidity crisis as banks scramble to call in existing loans.

The discount rate: The interest rate banks pay to borrow from the Fed overnight, usually when in financial distress. Raising it discourages borrowing and contracts the money supply. Useful mainly as a signal of future rate policy direction because banks are reluctant to use it—cheaper options exist in the interbank market, and going to the discount window is a public admission that something is wrong, scaring investors and shareholders.

Open market operations (OMO): The primary tool—the NY Fed’s open market desk buys or sells Treasury securities through 18 primary security dealers. When the Fed buys: cash flows to dealers, excess reserves increase, money supply grows, rates fall, spending rises. When the Fed sells: cash flows from dealers, excess reserves decrease, money supply shrinks, rates rise, spending falls.

Fiscal-Monetary Coordination

Monetary and fiscal policy don’t exist in isolation—they interact constantly, and their coordination (or lack thereof) determines the overall policy stance facing the economy.

During recessions: expansionary fiscal policy (more government spending, lower taxes) is the Keynesian prescription, but it puts upward pressure on interest rates as the government borrows more from the pool of loanable funds. Expansionary monetary policy must offset this upward rate pressure to keep borrowing costs low and prevent fiscal stimulus from being crowded out by higher rates.

During inflation: contractionary fiscal policy (less spending, more taxes) is politically unpopular—elected officials don’t win votes by cutting spending or raising taxes. But contractionary monetary policy is effective and politically feasible. The Fed’s insulation from political pressure makes it perfectly suited for inflation-fighting. Volcker was credited with whipping inflation when government could not—precisely because he didn’t have to face voters.

Two perpetual problems haunt this coordination. The ratchet problem: if government always uses expansionary fiscal policy during recessions and relies on the Fed to fight inflation, interest rates ratchet up over time and long-term growth is stymied. Government must eventually rein in spending and/or raise taxes. The timing problem: if the Fed introduces stimulus, it must also plan to remove it. Remove too early and the economy falls back into deeper recession. Wait too long and inflation takes hold, requiring even more painful tightening later. This is the Fed’s perpetual tightrope.

Supply-Side Economics

The Rise of “Voodoo Economics”

The 1970s stagflation was a supply problem—Keynesian demand-side solutions couldn’t work because spending to reduce unemployment would worsen inflation, while tightening to reduce inflation would worsen unemployment. The Misery Index (unemployment + inflation, normally ~7) reached an all-time high of 20.76 under Carter. George H.W. Bush called Reagan’s supply-side plan “voodoo economics” during the 1980 Republican primary—ironic since he later became Reagan’s vice president and inherited the policy.

The Supply-Side Prescription

Classical laissez-faire economics enjoyed a renaissance under Reagan: flexible, efficient markets maintain full employment, and government had “gummed up the works” from the 1930s through the 1970s. Three key policies defined the approach:

Deregulation (begun under Carter, accelerated under Reagan): airlines were freed to compete—more flights, cheaper prices, but inefficient airlines were driven out. Other industries were similarly deregulated, removing government controls that supply-siders argued “gummed up the works.”

Breaking union power: Reagan fired over 11,000 striking air traffic controllers in 1981 for violating federal law. The signal was clear: the government would no longer reflexively support organized labor. Unions’ political power declined sharply in the following decades.

Tax cuts on high earners: the theory was that high earners would spend and save more, leading to more consumption AND more capital investment, expanding productive capacity, increasing employment, boosting productivity, and ultimately lowering prices. The appeal crossed ideological lines—demand-siders saw a consumption boost, supply-siders saw an investment boost.

The supply-side spin on tax cuts was novel: cuts don’t just stimulate demand (Keynesians knew that)—they also stimulate supply by incentivizing investment in productive capacity. A tax cut has both demand AND supply effects, appealing to different constituencies.

Reality vs. Theory

Reality intervened: Reagan got his tax cuts AND his defense spending increases, resulting in massive budget deficits—exactly as critics predicted. Revenue fell rather than rising. The increased tax revenue that supply-siders promised failed to materialize.

A persistent criticism dogs supply-side economics: since the mantra is “cut taxes on income and capital gains,” the immediate and most visible beneficiary is those with significant income and capital gains to tax. The distributional effects are inherently unequal, even if the theory promises that benefits will eventually flow to all.

Though supply-side economics is rarely discussed as a formal academic school anymore, its ideas persist powerfully in Republican and Libertarian platforms—cutting taxes, creating savings and investment incentives, and maintaining deep skepticism of government involvement in the economy.

The Lasting Contribution

Supply-side economics’ enduring legacy is reminding everyone that incentives matter. Policies shape behavior, and unintended consequences must be anticipated. People are not going to stop acting like people—governments must consider whether their actions create perverse incentives.

Before supply-side thinking, policymakers had a narrow toolbox: increase or decrease aggregate demand. Recognizing that policies also affect aggregate supply doubles the options and the analytical framework. Every tax, regulation, and mandate changes behavior on both sides of the market. Government mandates that regulate behavior must account for how they alter incentives for consumers AND producers—or risk making problems worse while trying to solve them.

Part V: Economic Growth 🔖

What Growth Means

Economic growth—a sustained increase in real GDP per person—has transformed human life beyond recognition over the past century. The numbers are staggering: less than 2% of Americans are now farmers (versus the vast majority a century ago). Life expectancy rose from 48 to 78 years. The quality and quantity of material goods has increased enormously. The average workweek has decreased and vacation time has increased.

U.S. real GDP grows roughly 2% annually on average, meaning the economy doubles every ~36 years. Population grows ~1%, so real GDP per capita is roughly 3x what it was in 1960. These seemingly modest annual percentages compound into revolutionary transformation over decades.

GDP per capita correlates with happiness up to about $10,000 per person—after that, the relationship breaks down. “The first $10,000 does buy happiness. After that, who knows?” Growth frees people from subsistence agriculture to pursue interests and passions—this creative explosion over 150 years has advanced all fields of human endeavor. “An extra iPod or McMansion might be a small price to pay” for the substantive benefits.

Conditions for Growth

Human capital is the most important factor in economic growth—education, skills, and abilities. The average American child has over $250,000 invested in human capital by adulthood. Countries that invest heavily in their people (U.S., Japan, Western Europe) consistently grow faster than similarly endowed countries that don’t.

Individual freedom and property rights are essential preconditions—people invest in their own skills only when they can capture the returns. Population growth also matters: larger populations produce more output AND more innovation—more minds generating ideas, more workers producing goods. Aid organizations have found that delivering food rations to daughters who stay in school increases the daughter’s economic value to the family, reducing childhood pregnancy and prostitution—a powerful example of how targeting human capital investment can break cycles of poverty.

Physical capital (tools, factories, equipment) enables capital deepening—increasing the amount of capital available per worker. The average American worker is backed by roughly $130,000 worth of physical capital, which explains much of American productivity.

Capital can replace labor (robots welding car frames around the clock without breaks) or enhance it (a worker with a pneumatic jackhammer versus one with a sledgehammer). But capital needs infrastructure to realize its potential—roads, ports, power grids, telecommunications. The Soviet Union built massive factories but lacked the infrastructure to supply them with inputs or distribute their outputs effectively.

Research and development requires sacrificing current profits for future gains—patent protection is essential for firms to take this risk. The 20-year patent monopoly creates a deliberate trade-off: short-term allocative inefficiency (monopoly pricing) in exchange for long-term dynamic efficiency (innovation). China’s impressive growth has mainly come from producing goods developed elsewhere; weak intellectual property laws discourage domestic innovation. “Very little incentive exists for Chinese manufacturers to spend money on R&D if the factory next door can just copy them.”

Rule of law means government officials obey and apply laws uniformly. Corruption raises the cost of capital—Venezuela’s 2007 seizure of foreign oil fields deters future investment. As Kenneth Arrow observed, “Much of the economic backwardness in the world can be explained by lack of mutual confidence.”

Former British colonies (U.S., Canada, Australia, Hong Kong) inherited English common law with its emphasis on private property, making them safe for foreign investors and attracting capital accumulation. The institutional framework matters more than geography or natural resources—West Germans were far more productive than East Germans; South Koreans far more productive than North Koreans. Same people, same geography, radically different institutions, radically different outcomes.

Policy Effects on Growth

Government policy affects growth through three main channels.

Interest rate policy: the Fed promotes growth by maintaining predictable, stable interest rates. Although monetary policy primarily affects short-term rates, it’s the effect on long-term rates that influences growth—firms won’t make 20-year capital investments if uncertain about future rates or inflation over that horizon. The Fed must maintain a lid on both actual AND expected inflation. Short-term rate increases to dampen inflation may displease Wall Street but help keep long-term rates low and stable, encouraging the capital investment that drives sustained growth.

Fiscal policy: budget deficits (absent offsetting capital inflows or increased domestic saving) raise long-term interest rates, crowding out private investment and slowing growth. Even if interest rate effects are offset, large deficits create uncertainty that discourages investment. Budget surpluses reduce long-term rates and encourage capital investment.

Tax policy: higher taxes on businesses reduce their ability and incentive to invest in capital. Higher capital gains taxes reduce the flow of savings into physical capital investment. Capital is mobile—“the productive worker will either reduce productivity or move to where productivity is not taxed as highly.” Europe has suffered a brain drain as its best and brightest move to lower-tax countries; the OECD considers this serious enough that European countries are creating programs to lure expatriates back.

The natural resource paradox demonstrates that resource wealth can actually hinder growth: Russia has vastly more natural resources than Japan yet lower economic growth, because resource-rich governments often neglect human capital development—they rely on resource extraction rather than investing in their people. The paradox extends worldwide—many of the poorest nations sit atop the richest deposits of oil, minerals, and precious metals.

The Chicago Boys in Chile brought Friedman-trained market reforms under Pinochet that halted inflation and made Chile one of South America’s fastest-growing economies—though the association with dictatorship forever linked free-market economics with authoritarianism in Latin American politics. The lesson: economic policies do not exist in a political vacuum, and the context of their implementation matters as much as their content.

The Democratic Tension

Income inequality threatens growth in a democracy. From 1980-2008, the bottom quintile saw little change in income while the top saw steady increases. Causes include loss of union power, reduction in marginal tax rates, foreign competition, meritocratic assortative mating (the best marry the best and reproduce more of the best), and globalization shifting unskilled manufacturing overseas.

Capitalism rewards the most productive, but universal suffrage means those on the “short end of capitalism’s stick” will exercise their vote to redistribute. “Income redistribution is a fact of life in a society with universal suffrage. Proponents of economic growth must be prepared to share their earned gains with those who may not have earned it.” This is not a moral argument but a practical one: democratic societies that allow inequality to grow unchecked will eventually correct it through the ballot box, potentially in ways that are less efficient than voluntary sharing of growth’s gains.

Part VI: The Great Recession 🔖

Links to the Great Depression

The story begins with the Great Depression. Black Tuesday (October 29, 1929) triggered a decade of plunging income, stalled investment, 25% unemployment, and collapsing trade. The Fed failed its first major test—instead of flooding the system with inexpensive credit to restore confidence and liquidity, it restricted credit, choking off any hope of recovery. Critics from Friedman to Bernanke place much of the blame for the Depression’s severity squarely on the Fed’s misguided response.

The Glass-Steagall Act of 1933 was the legislative response: it created the FDIC to insure deposits (ending the threat of bank runs), prohibited commercial banks from engaging in speculative investment activities, and the Bank Holding Company Act prohibited banks from underwriting insurance. These firewalls restored confidence in the banking system for roughly 75 years. Their gradual erosion and eventual repeal set the stage for what followed.

The Run-Up to the Meltdown

Glass-Steagall’s repeal created a shadow banking industry outside traditional regulation—financial institutions that performed bank-like functions (lending, leveraging, creating complex securities) without being subject to bank-like oversight. Hedge funds, investment banks, and special purpose vehicles operated in a regulatory twilight zone.

The Fed and regulators increasingly relied on businesses to self-regulate, believing market forces would enforce sound practices. This was the laissez-faire assumption applied to banking—and it would prove catastrophically wrong. The fundamental error was assuming that rational self-interest would prevent firms from taking risks that could destroy them. In reality, the incentive structures rewarded short-term gains while the risks were diffused across the system.

After 9/11, the 2001 terrorist attacks created fear in an already-retreating stock market, triggering a shallow recession. The Fed immediately lowered the fed funds rate and injected money into the banking system. Bush tax cuts, two wars, a deregulated financial sector, and persistently low interest rates created a massive pool of money flowing into real estate.

From 2002 to 2005, home-equity borrowing at historically low rates fueled a spending binge—banks lent to “pretty much anybody who showed up for a loan.” Interest rates that should have risen were kept low by Chinese, Japanese, and oil-producing state savings pouring into U.S. assets. Faith in the Fed’s inflation-fighting capability kept inflation expectations subdued. Easy credit flowed toward housing, creating an asset price bubble—and the debate continues over whether central banks should raise rates to pop such bubbles or let them run their course.

In 2006, the housing market slowed. In 2007, it went into free-fall while oil prices shot up simultaneously. Consumers saw their wealth decreasing (falling home values) while food and energy costs rose. Homeowners began walking away from underwater properties—owing more on their mortgages than their homes were worth. The stage was set for collapse.

Securitization and the Principal-Agent Chain

Traditionally, banks made loans, kept them on their balance sheets, earned profit from interest, and had strong incentive to assess default risk. Financial innovation transformed this model entirely: banks became loan originators who charged fees and immediately sold loans to investment banks. Investment banks bundled thousands of these loans into CDOs (collateralized debt obligations)—complex securities that sliced mortgage pools into tranches of varying risk and return.

The fundamental principal-agent problem: because banks no longer held the loans, they had no incentive to carefully underwrite them. The loan officer’s job became volume, not quality. Investors relied on banks to vet borrowers, but banks didn’t care if loans went bad—the risk had been passed along.

CDO popularity drove more lending, which drove lower standards, which drove higher house prices—a reinforcing vicious cycle. CDOs were sold to institutional investors, insurance companies, other banks, and hedge funds around the world—all believing mortgages were inherently safe investments. The chain of misaligned incentives extended from the mortgage broker on Main Street to the pension fund manager in Oslo.

Rating Agency Failure and Credit Default Swaps

Institutional investors relied on rating agencies (Moody’s, S&P) to assess CDO safety. Many could only invest in AAA/AA+ rated bonds by law or charter. Agencies rated the top tranche of CDOs as investment-grade; lower tranches held riskier loans but paid higher interest, making the blended yield attractive.

Another principal-agent problem: rating agencies earned fees from those marketing CDOs—the very institutions whose products they were supposed to independently evaluate. The incentive to award high ratings was obvious. Meanwhile, institutional investors were investing other people’s money—as long as they bought highly rated assets, they had little incentive to look deeper. Nobody in the chain was spending their own money or bearing the ultimate risk.

Investment banks sold Credit Default Swaps—essentially insurance policies against CDO default. For investors, this made CDOs seem perfectly safe: even if the underlying loans went bad, the CDS would pay out. Banks made money selling CDOs AND charging premiums for CDS insurance. Profits soared.

The one small problem: CDOs were much less secure than believed, and CDSs were not adequately funded. The sellers of CDS insurance had collected premiums but set aside almost nothing to pay claims. If CDOs defaulted en masse, banks selling CDSs would be liable for hundreds of billions in payouts they couldn’t make. That is exactly what happened.

The Collapse

When housing prices collapsed in 2007, the entire edifice crumbled in a cascade of failures that vindicated every warning about interconnected risk.

Bear Stearns fell first—heavy CDO exposure created a toxic balance sheet that made other banks refuse to lend to it, fearing they wouldn’t be repaid. NY Fed president Tim Geithner orchestrated a bailout by lending to JPMorgan Chase to buy Bear Stearns at a deep discount, hoping to prevent widespread panic. Instead, the panic spread—if Bear Stearns could fail, anyone could.

Lehman Brothers followed—this time, no one came to the rescue. The decision not to bail out Lehman remains one of the most debated in financial history.

AIG, which had marketed the unfunded credit default swaps, faced hundreds of billions in losses as investors exercised their insurance contracts. AIG was deemed “too big to fail”—if it collapsed, the entire financial system could go down, because AIG had counterparty relationships with virtually every major bank. The U.S. government and Fed bailed out AIG.

Moral hazard—the expectation of bailout encourages risk-taking—was the elephant in the room. Economists and policymakers recognized that bailing out irresponsible firms rewarded bad behavior and would encourage more risk-taking in the future. But the alternative—letting the financial system collapse—was worse. “Ignoring moral hazard is a moral hazard.”

CDO values collapsed across the board. Banks stopped lending to each other, fearful of each other’s balance sheets—no one knew who was holding toxic assets. Banks stopped lending to businesses. The commercial paper market froze. Business ground to a halt. The Great Recession had begun.

The Fed’s Unprecedented Response

The Keynesian prescription called for expansionary monetary and fiscal policy. Problem: the tools that are normally effective were severely hampered. Traditional Fed tools (buy T-bills, let money creation work through the multiplier) didn’t work because banks were unwilling to lend—instead of creating money through the multiplier, the financial system was destroying money. CDO values collapsed, banks stopped lending to each other (fearful of each other’s balance sheets), the commercial paper market froze, and business ground to a halt.

Many failures occurred in the shadow banking system outside the Fed’s regulatory scope. To get things working, shadow banks had to be converted to traditional regulated banks—Goldman Sachs and Morgan Stanley changed their status to come under Fed regulation.

The Fed invented quantitative easing. First came the Term Auction Facility (TAF)—banks could bid on Fed loans without the stigma of the discount window (going to the discount window is a public admission that something is wrong, scaring investors and shareholders). Then the Fed began buying mortgage-backed securities and agency debt—creating markets for assets no one else wanted in exchange for cash. The FOMC reduced the fed funds rate to 0%—the maximum expansionary stance possible with conventional tools.

The Government Response

Treasury under Henry Paulson, Congress, and Presidents Bush and Obama planned massive fiscal responses. TARP (Troubled Asset Relief Program, 2008): gave Treasury the ability to recapitalize banks hit by CDO collapse with $700 billion in funding. The government purchased control of General Motors for $50 billion. Cash for Clunkers (CARS): rebates for trading in old vehicles for fuel-efficient ones, with 680,000 cars sold under the program. $8,000 tax credits for homebuyers. Extended unemployment benefits.

The Recovery Act of 2009: nearly $790 billion in stimulus spending. Combined with tax cuts, two wars, and 0% interest rates, “the stimulus is quite aggressive by historical standards.” Most economists believe the combined response averted a second Great Depression. Most emergency facilities expired in February 2010.

Jobless Recovery and the Fed’s Tightrope

The recovery was jobless—a pattern that has become increasingly common in modern recessions, and one of the most politically painful features of the modern economy.

The mechanism is straightforward: recession causes firms to cut workers fast. During recovery, the remaining workforce turns out to be more productive than expected—technology substitutes for labor, surviving employees work harder and longer, and firms discover they can increase output without hiring. They increase hours for existing workers before bringing on new ones. Only after accounting for all productivity gains—and only when demand clearly exceeds current capacity—do they finally hire new workers.

The result: GDP recovers while unemployment lingers. “GDP may increase, but unemployment can still remain high for a while. The unemployed vote and do not really care for the neat explanation just given.” This disconnect between Wall Street recovery and Main Street pain became a defining feature of the post-2008 political landscape.

The Fed walked a tightrope throughout the recovery. It had injected hundreds of billions to unfreeze credit markets, and the question became: when and how to withdraw that extraordinary support?

Two dangers loomed in opposite directions. First, if the Fed contracted the money supply too soon, the economy could fall back into a deeper recession—removing stimulus prematurely is like taking antibiotics for only half the course, allowing the infection to return stronger. Second, if the Fed waited too long, inflation could take hold as all that newly created money chased a recovering economy’s goods and services, requiring even more painful tightening later.

The story of expected inflation creating actual inflation is powerful and self-fulfilling. If consumers believe prices will rise, they spend faster—demand-pull pressure. If workers believe prices will rise, they demand higher wages—cost-push pressure. These reinforce each other in a vicious cycle that feeds on itself. If policymakers don’t address rising expectations credibly and decisively, the story becomes reality.

The entire arc of the crisis—from deregulation to securitization to bailout to recovery—illustrates Mill’s central theme: economics is not abstract theory but the study of real human behavior, with real consequences for real lives. Incentives drive behavior, behavior shapes markets, and markets determine the prosperity or poverty of nations. Understanding these mechanisms is not optional—it is the price of informed citizenship.

Glossary 🔖

Foundational Concepts

  • Scarcity: The universal condition that there are not enough resources to satisfy all wants. The fundamental problem economics exists to address.
  • Opportunity cost: The value of the next best alternative foregone. The true cost of any decision. Includes both explicit costs (labor, materials) and implicit costs (what you could have done instead).
  • Marginal analysis: Decision-making based on incremental costs and benefits. Do it if marginal benefit ≥ marginal cost.
  • Ceteris paribus: “All other things constant.” Every cause-and-effect statement in economics assumes nothing else changes.
  • Comparative advantage: The ability to produce a good at a lower opportunity cost than another producer. The basis for all mutually beneficial trade.
  • Allocative efficiency: When marginal benefit equals marginal cost—resources are directed to their highest-value use.
  • Factors of production: The four inputs to all economic activity: land (natural resources), labor (human skills), capital (tools and equipment—NOT money), and entrepreneurship.
  • Elasticity: Responsiveness of quantity to price changes. Elastic = sensitive (facelifts); inelastic = insensitive (appendectomies). Determines tax burden distribution.
  • Diminishing marginal utility: Each additional unit of consumption provides less satisfaction than the previous one. The most fundamental law of consumer behavior.
  • Economic profit: Revenue minus total cost including opportunity cost. Zero economic profit means resources are being used as efficiently as anywhere else.
  • Production function: How output changes as inputs are varied. Passes through three stages: increasing returns, diminishing returns, and negative returns.

Market & Industry

  • Equilibrium: The price/quantity combination where supply equals demand and the market clears. Above equilibrium creates surpluses; below creates shortages.
  • Consumer/producer surplus: The gains from trade—what buyers save vs. willingness to pay, and what sellers earn vs. minimum acceptable price.
  • Deadweight loss: Transactions that would have benefited both parties but do not occur, typically due to price controls, taxes, or monopoly power.
  • Price ceiling: Maximum legal price (e.g., rent control). Creates shortages when set below equilibrium.
  • Price floor: Minimum legal price (e.g., minimum wage). Creates surpluses when set above equilibrium.
  • Externality: Cost or benefit imposed on third parties not involved in the transaction. Pollution is negative; flu vaccination is positive.
  • Public goods: Goods that are non-rivalrous (one person’s use doesn’t diminish another’s) and non-excludable (can’t withhold from non-payers). National defense, highways.
  • Moral hazard: When insurance or expectation of bailout encourages risk-taking. “Ignoring moral hazard is a moral hazard.”
  • Principal-agent problem: When the incentives of a representative conflict with the party they represent. The defining structural flaw of the 2008 crisis.
  • Information asymmetry: When one party in a transaction has more information than the other. Used car sellers know more than buyers—the “lemons problem.”
  • Creative destruction: Schumpeter’s concept that innovation inevitably destroys old industries while creating new ones. Typewriters to PCs.
  • Prisoner’s dilemma: Game theory scenario where individual rationality leads to collectively suboptimal outcomes. Explains oligopolist pricing behavior and cartel instability.
  • Herfindahl-Hirschman Index (HHI): Measures market concentration. Used by the FTC to evaluate proposed mergers.
  • Price discrimination: Charging different customers different prices for the same good. Airlines charge business travelers more than vacationers for the same seat.

Money & Banking

  • Fiat money: Currency with no intrinsic value, declared legal tender by government decree. Backed by nothing but faith—“money is imaginary.”
  • Commodity money: Money with intrinsic value (gold, silver, salt). Roman soldiers were paid in salt—origin of “salary.”
  • M1 and M2: M1 = cash + checking deposits (medium of exchange). M2 = M1 + savings + CDs + money market accounts (store of value). M1 growing faster than productivity signals inflation.
  • Fractional reserve banking: Banks keep only a fraction of deposits in reserve, lending the rest. Creates money through the money multiplier.
  • Money multiplier: 1 / reserve requirement. Determines how much money the banking system creates from each dollar of reserves. At 10%, multiplier = 10.
  • Fed funds rate: The interest rate banks charge each other for overnight loans of reserves. The primary rate the Fed targets through open market operations.
  • Fisher equation: Nominal interest rate = real interest rate + expected inflation.
  • Liquidity preference: Keynes’s theory that short-term interest rates are determined by consumers’ desire to hold cash vs. interest-bearing assets.
  • Financial leverage: Using borrowed money to amplify returns. A company earning 10% on $1M borrowed at 5% keeps the spread without risking its own capital.
  • Securitization: Packaging loans into tradeable securities (CDOs). Separated loan origination from risk-bearing, creating moral hazard.
  • Credit Default Swap (CDS): Insurance against bond/CDO default. The unfunded CDS market was a central cause of the 2008 crisis.
  • Commercial paper: Short-term unsecured IOUs issued by businesses with excellent credit. A key part of the money market that froze during the 2008 crisis.

Macroeconomic Measurement

  • GDP: Market value of all final goods and services produced within a country in a year. Measured by expenditure (C+I+G+NX), income, or production approach.
  • GDP vs. GNP: GDP measures production done in a country; GNP measures production done by a country. Toyotas made in Texas = U.S. GDP but not U.S. GNP.
  • Real vs. nominal GDP: Real adjusts for inflation using constant prices; nominal uses current prices. The GDP deflator (nominal ÷ real) measures the difference.
  • CPI: Consumer Price Index—fixed basket of goods tracked monthly by the BLS. Most widely reported inflation measure. Doesn’t capture substitution behavior.
  • PPI: Producer Price Index—wholesale prices. A leading indicator: when producer costs rise, consumer prices follow.
  • PCE deflator: Personal Consumption Expenditure—broader than CPI, adjusts for substitution behavior. The Fed’s preferred inflation measure.
  • Headline vs. core inflation: Headline includes everything; core excludes volatile food and energy prices to reveal underlying trends.
  • Business cycle: Recurring pattern of expansion → peak → contraction → trough. A recession = two consecutive quarters of declining real GDP.
  • Okun’s Law: For every 1% unemployment exceeds the natural rate, GDP falls ~2% below potential.
  • Misery Index: Unemployment rate + inflation rate. Normally ~7; reached 20.76 under Carter.
  • Labor force participation rate: Percentage of working-age population (16+) in the labor force. Has been declining, suggesting weaker labor markets than headline unemployment implies.
  • U3 vs. U6: U3 is the official unemployment rate. U6 adds discouraged workers, marginally attached workers, and involuntary part-timers. U6 is typically double U3.

Policy & Theory

  • Aggregate demand (AD): Total demand for all final domestic production at each price level. Slopes downward due to wealth effect, interest rate effect, and net export effect.
  • SRAS/LRAS: Short-run aggregate supply slopes upward (sticky wages); long-run aggregate supply is vertical at potential GDP (wages fully adjust).
  • Sticky wages: The observation that wages don’t adjust downward quickly even when prices fall—workers refuse pay cuts and employers don’t offer them. The key Keynesian insight.
  • Say’s Law: “Supply creates its own demand.” Foundation of classical economics; challenged by Keynes.
  • Multiplier effect: Government spending generates multiple rounds of spending. Multiplier = 1 / (1 - MPC). Government spending has a larger multiplier than tax cuts.
  • Marginal propensity to consume (MPC): The fraction of each additional dollar that is spent rather than saved. If MPC = 0.8, 80 cents of every new dollar is spent.
  • Phillips Curve: Inverse relationship between inflation and unemployment. Exists in the short run (stable expectations); vertical in the long run (expectations adjust).
  • Natural rate of unemployment: The rate consistent with full employment (zero cyclical unemployment). Approximately 4-6% in the U.S. Developed by Friedman and Phelps.
  • Money illusion: Preference for higher nominal values even when real purchasing power is unchanged. Allows short-run Phillips Curve trade-off.
  • Stagflation: Simultaneous high inflation and high unemployment, caused by supply shocks. Broke the Phillips Curve and challenged Keynesian economics in the 1970s.
  • Quantitative easing: Central bank buys assets (government bonds, mortgage-backed securities) to inject money when conventional tools are exhausted. Invented by the Fed during the 2008 crisis.
  • Liquidity trap: When interest rates are at zero and consumers/investors still won’t borrow. Conventional monetary policy becomes impotent.
  • Crowding out: Government borrowing raises interest rates, displacing private investment. Classical argument against fiscal stimulus.
  • Ratchet problem: If government always uses expansionary fiscal policy for recessions and relies on the Fed for inflation, interest rates ratchet up over time, stymying long-term growth.
  • Purchasing power parity: After exchange rate adjustment, equivalent goods should cost the same across countries. Arbitrage forces convergence.
  • Interest rate parity: Savers flow toward higher interest rates until currency appreciation offsets the rate advantage, equalizing real returns across countries.
  • Reserve currency: A currency widely held by central banks as foreign reserves. The U.S. dollar is the dominant reserve currency, giving America unique advantages in global finance.
