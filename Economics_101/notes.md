## What Is Economics?

### The Child of Philosophy
- Economics studied scarcity and choice long before the field was named
- Adam Smith, the father of modern economics, was considered a **moral philosopher**, not an economist
- Economists develop theories to explain behavior, test against real-world data — though sometimes theories are put into practice without ever being tested

### Studying Scarcity
- **Economics** = the study of how individuals, institutions, and society choose to deal with **scarcity**
- Scarcity is universal — not enough time, money, or stuff to satisfy everyone's needs/wants
- Resources = **factors of production**: land, labor, capital, entrepreneurship

### Microeconomics
- Decision-making of **individuals and businesses**
- Primarily concerned with markets for goods, services, and resources
- Key questions: Are markets efficient? Do participants have adequate information? How do decisions in one market affect related markets?

### Macroeconomics
- How **entire nations** deal with scarcity
- Key questions: How to measure the economy? Why unemployment? How does money supply affect the economy? Government spending/tax impact? How to grow?

### Scarcity and Factors of Production
- **Land**: All natural resources (trees, minerals, fish, water, land itself). Renewable (pine trees, chickens) vs. nonrenewable (oil, Atlantic cod). Payment = **rent**
- **Labor**: People's skills and abilities. Three tiers:
  - **Unskilled**: paid wages for repetitive tasks (hamburgers, assembly line)
  - **Skilled**: paid wages for knowledge/ability (welders, electricians, plumbers)
  - **Professional**: paid for expertise (doctors, lawyers, engineers, teachers)
- **Capital**: Physical stuff used to make other stuff (tools, factories, equipment). Capital ≠ money. Capital = product of **investment** (money spent buying capital). Always purchased with borrowed money → incurs interest payment
- **Allocation**: Getting the right resources to the right people. **Allocative efficiency** = when marginal benefit equals marginal cost → greatest benefit to society

## Trade-Offs and Opportunity Cost

### Making an Assumption Out of You and Me
- Every use of a factor of production incurs a cost — because factors are **limited, not limitless**
- Using a resource for one purpose = losing the ability to use it for another
- **Trade-offs** = the choices you face
- **Opportunity cost** = the next best alternative use of a resource

### Implicit and Explicit Costs
- **Explicit costs**: labor, raw materials, overhead — easily calculated
- **Implicit costs** (= opportunity cost): harder to assess, but real
- Example: trip to Cozumel costs not just plane/hotel, but also the money you could have made working

### Marginal Analysis
- Economists think of people as computers counting benefit vs. cost for every decision
- **Marginal benefit**: benefit of one more unit (measured in dollars or **utils** — units of happiness)
- **Marginal cost**: cost of producing or consuming one more unit
- Decision rule: **Do it if MB ≥ MC; don't if MB < MC; indifferent if MB = MC**
- Swimming pool example: if swimming gives 20 utils ($20 worth) and costs $5, you swim. At $20.01 cost, you don't.

### Three Key Assumptions in Economics
1. **Ceteris paribus** ("all other things constant"): Every cause-and-effect statement assumes nothing else changes. "If income taxes fall, consumption increases" really means "if income taxes fall *and nothing else changes*"
2. **Rationality**: People make choices using all available information, weighing costs and benefits. Subject to debate, but useful for most economic decisions
3. **Self-interest**: People are selfish — motivated by self-interest first. Even altruistic acts (soldier jumping on grenade) can be framed as maximizing personal utility. Criticized as too abstract/theoretical — most economists failed to predict the 2008 financial crisis

## The Emergence of Free Trade and the Theory of Comparative Advantage

### The Rise and Fall of Mercantilism
- **Mercantilism** dominated 16th-18th century Europe — the idea that national wealth = stockpile of gold and silver
- Mercantilist countries exported as much as possible, imported as little as possible, hoarding precious metals
- Led to colonialism: colonies existed to provide raw materials and buy finished goods from the mother country
- **Adam Smith** (1776, *The Wealth of Nations*) argued mercantilism was wrong — nations should specialize in what they produce most efficiently and trade freely
- Smith's key insight: **wealth is not gold**, it's the productive capacity of a nation's people

### Absolute Advantage
- **Absolute advantage** = ability to produce more of a good with the same resources, OR produce the same amount with fewer resources
- Example: Art can perform 3 brain surgeries/day OR write 1 hit song/day; Paul can perform 0 brain surgeries/day OR write 3 hit songs/day
- Art has absolute advantage in brain surgery, Paul in songwriting
- Both benefit if each specializes in their strength and trades

### Comparative Advantage
- **David Ricardo** (1817, *Principles of Political Economy and Taxation*) showed that trade benefits nations even when one has absolute advantage in EVERYTHING
- **Comparative advantage** = ability to produce a good at a lower **opportunity cost** than another producer
- Even if Art is better at both surgery AND songwriting, if his opportunity cost of writing songs is higher (3 surgeries per song vs. Paul's 0), Paul should write songs and Art should do surgery
- This is the foundation of ALL modern trade theory — universally accepted by economists across the political spectrum
- Bangladesh T-shirt example: US workers are more productive than Bangladeshi workers at EVERYTHING, but the opportunity cost of making T-shirts in the US (giving up high-tech production) is enormous; Bangladesh's opportunity cost is low → Bangladesh should make T-shirts, US should make high-tech goods

### Real-World Complications
- Comparative advantage theory assumes: no transportation costs, no trade barriers, perfect mobility of resources, full employment — none perfectly true
- Despite complications, the core insight holds: **voluntary specialization and trade make both parties better off**

## International Trade and Trade Barriers

### The Case for Free Trade
- **Voluntary trade creates wealth** — otherwise people wouldn't do it
- International trade is just domestic trade extended across borders
- Benefits: greater variety of goods, lower prices through competition, economies of scale, technology transfer
- Post-WWII institutions designed to promote trade: **Bretton Woods** (1944), **GATT** (1947, became **WTO** in 1995), **IMF**, **World Bank**

### The Case Against Free Trade
- **Infant industry argument**: new domestic industries need temporary protection to grow before facing foreign competition
- **National security**: can't depend on foreign countries for defense-critical goods (steel, oil, technology)
- **Dumping**: foreign producers sell below cost to destroy domestic competition, then raise prices once competitors are gone — predatory pricing on an international scale
- **Job protection**: cheap foreign labor can displace domestic workers (the most politically potent argument, though economists note displaced workers eventually find new employment)
- **Environmental/labor standards**: countries with lax regulations have artificially low costs — "race to the bottom"

### Trade Barriers
- **Tariffs**: taxes on imports. Revenue tariffs = raise money for government; protective tariffs = shield domestic industry. Effect: raise prices for consumers, reduce quantity imported
- **Quotas**: legal limits on quantity of imports. More restrictive than tariffs because they set a hard ceiling. Example: US sugar quotas keep domestic sugar prices far above world prices
- **Embargoes**: total ban on trade with a country. Political weapon, not economic tool. Example: US embargo on Cuba (1960-present), intended to destabilize Castro regime — hasn't worked after 60+ years
- **Voluntary export restraints (VERs)**: exporting country "voluntarily" limits exports (usually under threat of worse restrictions). Japan limited auto exports to US in 1980s — Japanese manufacturers responded by building factories IN the US

### Historical Examples
- **Smoot-Hawley Tariff Act** (1930): raised tariffs on 20,000+ imported goods to record levels. Other countries retaliated. World trade collapsed 65%. Widely blamed for deepening the Great Depression
- **GATT/WTO**: 8 rounds of negotiations since 1947, progressively reducing tariffs. Average US tariff fell from ~40% (1940s) to ~5% (2000s)
- **NAFTA** (1994): free trade agreement between US, Canada, Mexico. Controversial — boosted trade enormously but critics blame it for US manufacturing job losses

## Traditional, Command, and Market Economies

### Three Questions Every Economy Must Answer
1. **What** to produce? (guns or butter? cars or computers?)
2. **How** to produce it? (labor-intensive or capital-intensive? which technology?)
3. **For whom** to produce? (how to distribute output? equally? by merit? by wealth?)

### Traditional Economies
- Oldest type — people do what their ancestors did
- **Kalahari Bushmen**: hunt and gather as they have for thousands of years. No specialization beyond gender roles. No surplus, no trade, no growth
- Advantages: stable, predictable, tight-knit community
- Disadvantages: no innovation, no growth, vulnerable to external shocks (drought, disease, conquest)
- Virtually extinct in pure form — almost all traditional economies have been absorbed by market or command systems

### Command Economies
- Central authority (government, king, pharaoh) answers all three questions
- **Ancient Egypt**: pharaohs directed labor to build pyramids, temples, irrigation — massive public works but no individual economic freedom
- **Soviet Union**: Stalin's **five-year plans** (starting 1928) set production quotas for entire economy. Rapid industrialization but terrible allocation: USSR produced world's most tanks and steel but citizens waited in line for bread
- **WWII United States**: temporarily became a command economy — War Production Board told factories what to make, rationed consumer goods, set prices, allocated raw materials. Worked in short term for single purpose (winning war)
- Advantages: can mobilize rapidly for a single goal (war, industrialization), can distribute evenly (in theory)
- Disadvantages: **no price signals** → chronic misallocation. Bureaucrats can't know what millions of people want. Innovation dies without profit motive. Political control → corruption, repression

### Market Economies
- Individuals and businesses answer the three questions through **voluntary exchange**
- **Price mechanism** = the invisible hand. Prices signal scarcity: high price → produce more, consume less; low price → produce less, consume more
- No central planner — order emerges from millions of individual decisions
- **Adam Smith**: "It is not from the benevolence of the butcher, the brewer, or the baker that we expect our dinner, but from their regard to their own self-interest"
- Examples of relatively free markets: **Hong Kong** (often ranked freest economy), **United States**, **Australia**
- **No pure market economy exists** — all have some government intervention (police, courts, national defense, regulations)
- Advantages: efficient allocation through prices, innovation through competition, consumer sovereignty, freedom of choice
- Disadvantages: income inequality, public goods underprovided, externalities (pollution), boom-bust cycles, monopoly power

### Mixed Economies
- Every real economy is a **mix** of market, command, and sometimes traditional elements
- US is predominantly market but has Social Security, Medicare, FDA, EPA, minimum wage, etc.
- China is nominally communist but has massive private sector since Deng Xiaoping's reforms (1978)
- The spectrum runs from "mostly free" (Hong Kong, Singapore) to "mostly controlled" (North Korea, Cuba)

## Capitalism and Socialism

### The Economic Spectrum
- No country is purely capitalist or purely socialist — all fall on a continuum
- **Capitalism** ≠ democracy. **Socialism** ≠ dictatorship. These are economic systems, not political ones (though they often correlate)
- China: capitalist economics + authoritarian politics. Sweden: socialist-leaning economics + robust democracy

### What Is Capitalism?
- **Private ownership** of the means of production (factories, farms, businesses)
- **Profit motive** drives economic decisions
- **Limited government** role — mainly enforcer of contracts and property rights
- **Competition** → efficiency → lower prices → better products
- Government's role: protect property rights, enforce contracts, provide national defense, maintain rule of law, regulate only when markets fail
- **Adam Smith** (1776): the "invisible hand" of self-interest guides resources to their most productive use without central planning

### What Is Socialism?
- **Government/collective ownership** of the means of production (or at least major industries)
- **Central planning** replaces market allocation for key sectors
- Goal: more **equal distribution** of wealth and opportunity
- Government's role: own/control major industries, provide universal services (healthcare, education, housing), redistribute income through taxation, regulate working conditions
- **Karl Marx** (1867, *Das Kapital*): capitalism inevitably creates exploitation of workers by owners; workers should seize the means of production; the state would eventually "wither away" leaving a classless society
- In practice: the state never withered away — socialist governments became entrenched bureaucracies

### Real-World Examples
- **France**: 35-hour work week (legally mandated), generous vacation, strong unions, extensive social safety net. Workers well-protected but unemployment chronically higher than US/UK
- **Germany**: "social market economy" — strong private sector but extensive regulation and 47.5% tax rate. Co-determination: workers sit on corporate boards. Result: lower inequality than US but lower growth
- **Britain under Thatcher** (1979-1990): reversed decades of creeping socialism. Privatized nationalized industries (British Telecom, British Gas, British Airways), broke union power, cut taxes. GDP growth surged but inequality widened
- **Sweden**: often cited as socialist success — but actually has very free markets with high taxes funding generous welfare state. Not government ownership of production, but government redistribution of income

### The Debate
- Capitalists argue: markets are more efficient, innovation requires profit motive, government ownership leads to stagnation and corruption
- Socialists argue: unregulated markets create inequality, exploit workers, ignore public goods, and are prone to crises
- **Keynes** (1936): "The ideas of economists and political philosophers, both when they are right and when they are wrong, are more powerful than is commonly understood. Indeed, the world is ruled by little else. Practical men, who believe themselves to be quite exempt from any intellectual influences, are usually the slaves of some defunct economist"

## Barter and the Development of Money

### Have You Got Change for a Cow?
- Money is one of humankind's most widespread and useful inventions — from barter to shells to coin to paper to digital
- **Barter** = exchanging one good/service directly for another (farmer trades eggs for bread)
- Problem: **double coincidence of wants** — both parties must want what the other has. If the baker doesn't need eggs, the farmer is stuck
- Multi-step barter chains are absurdly complex: farmer → eggs for sweater → sweater for trivets → trivets for bread. Inefficient.

### Functions and Characteristics of Money
- Money is anything that functions as a **medium of exchange**, **store of value**, or **standard of value**
  - **Medium of exchange**: used to buy and sell goods/services
  - **Store of value**: retains purchasing power over time
  - **Standard of value**: common unit to measure what things are worth
- Five ideal characteristics: **portability** (easy to carry), **durability** (survives laundry), **divisibility** (can be broken into smaller units), **stability** (value doesn't fluctuate wildly), **acceptability** (everyone agrees to take it)
- Fun fact: **Yap island** used giant stone discs weighing hundreds of pounds as money — decidedly *not* portable

### The Evolution of Money
- Many things have served as money: salt, tobacco, shells, stones, metals, leather, cigarettes
- Three stages of evolution:

#### Commodity Money
- Scarce minerals, metals, or agricultural products used directly as exchange medium
- Gold and silver coins = classic examples
- Advantage: commodity has intrinsic value (can be used for other purposes — gold jewelry, tobacco smoking)
- Disadvantage: if the commodity is also a resource, money may be "too precious to spend" — Roman soldiers were paid in salt (origin of "salary")
- 1980s fashion: women wearing Chinese Panda or Canadian Maple Leaf gold coins as jewelry

#### Representative Money
- Goldsmiths issued **receipts for gold on deposit** → people traded the receipts instead of heavy gold → paper money born
- Over time, people became comfortable trading paper representations of gold rather than gold itself
- Key insight: the receipt (paper) is worth nothing intrinsically — its value comes from the promise of gold behind it

#### Inconvertible Fiat Money
- **FDR, 1933**: executive order transformed the dollar from representative money (backed by gold) to **inconvertible fiat**
- **Fiat money** = paper/virtual money that is intrinsically worthless, not redeemable for any commodity. It is money because the government says so *and* people are willing to accept it
- US dollar, euro, pound, yen, and most world currencies = inconvertible fiat
- **The Gold Standard** (sidebar): money backed by fixed amount of gold. Prevents overprinting and devaluation. But limits economic growth — money supply can't grow faster than gold supply

## Inconvertible Fiat Explored

### Welcome to the Matrix
- Fiat addresses gold standard's weakness: gold constrains money supply, limiting growth
- Adam Smith: wealth ≠ gold stockpile, wealth = sum of all goods and services an economy produces
- Money supply should reflect productive capacity. As businesses expand, they need more money for tools, factories, equipment
- Fiat money can grow as the economy grows — not constrained by physical commodity
- Central bank must carefully control money supply — not too plentiful (inflation), not too scarce (deflation)

### Money: Everyone's Imaginary Friend
- "Money is debt" — the US dollar is a promise to pay from the Federal Reserve. "A promise to pay what?" Another dollar.
- Fiat money is backed by **nothing but faith** — faith that the government won't collapse and the money won't become worthless
- Your bank account is not stacks of bills in a vault — it's information stored on computers. You can go days without touching, seeing, or smelling money. **Money is imaginary.**

### Losing Faith and Collective Despair
- Because money is backed by faith, **anything that erodes faith destroys money**
- **Inflation** = too much money in circulation → prices rise → purchasing power falls
- Under a gold standard, inflation expectations are held in check (can't print more than you have gold for)
- Fiat has no such automatic check → governments can overprint
- **Weimar Republic** (interwar Germany): overprinted currency → hyperinflation → financial ruin. The checks and balances of the gold standard did not exist
- Overprinting and counterfeiting both threaten money supply → governments constantly develop anti-counterfeiting measures

### Money Supply Measures: M1 and M2
- **M1** = checking account balances + cash + coins + traveler's checks circulating in the economy. Used as a **medium of exchange**
- **M2** = M1 + savings accounts + CDs + money market accounts + US dollars in foreign banks. Used as a **store of value**. Larger and less liquid than M1
- Currency/coin *inside* a bank vault is NOT counted in M1 — "it's not money until you walk out of the bank" (technically inaccurate for a bank robber to demand "money" while in a bank)
- **M1/M2 ratio as indicator**: if M1 grows faster than labor force + productivity growth → inflation. If M2 grows at M1's expense (people saving, not spending) → recession signal

## The Time Value of Money and Interest Rates

### Carpe Diem!
- **A dollar today is worth more than a dollar tomorrow** — due to opportunity cost and inflation
- Lending $50 to a friend means: (1) you can't use the money now (opportunity cost), (2) when repaid, inflation has eroded purchasing power (the chair that cost $50 now costs $55), (3) risk friend moves to Costa Rica and "forgets" to repay
- **Interest** = the additional payment to compensate the lender for opportunity cost, inflation, and risk
- Without interest, people would just stuff money under their mattress

### Principles of Interest — The Building Blocks
- Interest rate = **price of using money**. Think of it as stacked blocks:

1. **Basic interest rate (real interest rate)**: compensation for opportunity cost alone — giving up immediate use of money. In a world with no inflation or risk, the rate that equates saving and borrowing. Varies by place and time — if people prefer spending now, rate must rise to entice saving
2. **Expected inflation rate**: added to the real rate to compensate for purchasing power loss. Real rate 2% + expected inflation 3% = **nominal interest rate 5%**. The 1970s-80s saw 20% mortgage rates largely due to high inflation expectations
3. **Default risk premium**: compensation for chance borrower won't repay. Better credit history → lower premium. This is why credit scores matter — they quantify default risk
4. **Liquidity premium**: compensation for difficulty of selling/transferring the loan. A 10-year car loan backed by a depreciating asset is harder to resell than a Treasury bond → demands higher rate
5. **Maturity risk premium**: compensation for the risk that interest rates will rise during the loan period, making your fixed-rate investment less attractive. Longer maturity → higher premium

- **Example calculation**: Real rate 2% + inflation 3% + default risk 4% + liquidity 2% + maturity risk 1% = **total interest rate 12%**

## The Origin of Banking

### Est. 2000 B.C.
- Banks are integral to capitalism — without them, capitalism would not function
- Banking's ubiquity reveals its importance: "In the time it takes to walk from one Starbucks to another, you'll pass at least a few banks"

### Early Banking
- **Ancient Egypt/Middle East**: agricultural commodities stored in government-operated granaries with records of deposits and withdrawals — the prototype of modern banking
- **Ancient moneychangers**: exchanged currency from different countries for merchants, travelers, pilgrims paying taxes or making religious offerings
- **Renaissance Italy**: Italian city-states housed the first true banks, financing trade, the state, and the Catholic Church
- To avoid the Church's prohibition on **usury** (charging interest): Italian bankers lent in one currency but demanded repayment in another, profiting from exchange rate differences
- **English goldsmiths**: stored gold, issued receipts → discovered they could issue **more receipts than gold on deposit** → birth of **fractional reserve banking**
- **American Revolution to Civil War**: relatively unregulated banking expansion financed the young republic
- **National Bank Act of 1863**: established modern US banking, gave government a means to finance the Civil War

### The Function of Banks
1. **Safe storage**: John Locke's *Second Treatise of Civil Government* — secure property is essential to freedom. Jefferson paraphrased Locke: "life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness" (Locke said "property"). Banks serve this security function
2. **Facilitate trade**: checks, debit cards, credit cards, cashier's checks → consumers can make purchases more often and in more places → businesses employ more land, labor, capital → fuller employment
3. **Intermediation** (most important): banks bring together **savers and borrowers**. Pay interest to savers, charge higher interest to borrowers, profit on the **spread**. Saver benefits (safe, liquid return without evaluating borrowers). Borrower benefits (access to large pool of funds). Economy benefits (borrowers buy durable goods, invest in capital/housing → job creation → economic growth)
- **Knights Templar and Hospitallers** (sidebar): historians trace the development of the check to these orders, who used signed documents to transfer wealth among their houses — precursor to the debit card

## How Banks Create Money

### Beyond "Abracadabra!"
- Contrary to popular belief, most money is NOT created by government printing presses
- Money is created when banks **accept deposits and make loans**

### Balance Sheets
- **Assets = Liabilities + Stockholder's Equity**
- **Assets**: buildings, equipment, loans to customers, Treasury securities, vault cash, reserves — everything the bank owns
- **Liabilities**: customers' deposits, loans from other banks or the Fed — everything the bank owes
- **Stockholder's equity**: ownership interest represented by shares of stock (financial capital)
- Changes in liabilities create equal changes in assets: $100,000 deposited → reserves increase $100,000. $25,000 withdrawn → reserves decrease $25,000

### Capital Requirements
- Banks must maintain **capital requirements** — actual cash (capital) proportional to the riskiness of their loans
- Riskier loans → greater capital requirements
- Purpose: ensure bank can pay depositors if borrowers default, and give banks a vested interest in making sound loans

### Bank Reserves
- **Reserves** = funds either available for lending or held against checkable deposits
- **Excess reserves**: available for lending
- **Required reserves**: held against checkable (but not savings) deposits, kept as vault cash or deposited in the bank's reserve account at the Fed

## How Banks Create Money (continued)

### The Required Reserve Ratio
- In the US, the **required reserve ratio** is set by the Fed — the percentage of checkable deposits a bank cannot lend
- For large banks: **10%** required reserve ratio
- Example: $100,000 deposited → $10,000 required reserves + $90,000 excess reserves (available for lending)
- When economy is healthy, banks lend out ALL excess reserves — they profit by charging interest, so they maximize lending

### The Abracadabra Part — Money Creation
- $100,000 deposit → bank lends $90,000 (excess reserves) → borrower buys recreational vehicle → seller deposits $90,000 → checkable deposits now $190,000. **Money was created.**
- Process doesn't stop: bank holds 10% of new $90,000 deposit ($9,000), lends $81,000 → re-deposited → $81,000 new money → and so on until all excess reserves are loaned out
- Each round creates less new money, but the cumulative effect is enormous

### The Money Multiplier
- **Money multiplier** = 1 / required reserve ratio × excess reserves
- At 10% reserve ratio → multiplier = **10**. At 25% reserve ratio → multiplier = 4
- Example: $30,000 deposit with 10% reserve → money supply can grow by max $270,000 ([$30,000 − $3,000] × 10)
- **Limitations**: assumes (1) banks lend ALL excess reserves, and (2) all loans are re-deposited. If either fails, actual multiplier is smaller. Many Americans hold cash → reduces the multiplier effect

### The Money Divider
- Money can be **destroyed** just as easily as created
- Money is created when deposits are made and loans are issued; money is **destroyed** when customers withdraw balances and pay off loans
- Example: Maria writes $10,000 check to pay off car loan → checkable deposits reduced by $10,000 → money supply shrinks
- "What's good for you personally may not be so good for the economy"

## Banks as a System: Regulation and Deregulation

### You Scratch My Back and I'll Scratch Yours
- Banks work as a **system**, lending and borrowing from each other
- **Fed funds market**: Bank East has excess reserves but no local borrowers; Bank West has borrowers but no excess reserves → Bank West borrows from Bank East at the **fed funds rate**. Both profit.

### Helping a Poor Banker Out
- If a bank is low on reserves at end of day → borrows overnight from other banks in the fed funds market
- Example: Acme Bank loses reserves when customer withdraws life savings → Roadrunner Bank lends its excess reserves at the fed funds rate (better than earning minimal interest at the Fed)

### Bank Runs
- **Bank run/panic**: because banks operate with far less than 100% reserves, if enough customers demand deposits simultaneously, the bank **cannot pay** → insolvency
- Caused by **fear** — rumor or speculation about a bank's health. If enough people believe it, they withdraw → line forms → others see the line and panic → self-fulfilling prophecy
- Banks can avert runs by borrowing from other banks, BUT if panic is pervasive, **banks refuse to lend to each other** → systemic run on entire financial system

### Bank Regulation History
- Pre-Civil War: banks chartered by states, issued their own currency
- **National Bank Act of 1863**: created federally chartered banks, national currency, government bonds
- **Bond** (definition): a security that is a promise from a borrower to pay a lender on a specified date with interest
- Larger banks accepted deposits from smaller banks as liquidity insurance. Problem: system didn't account for **contagion** — small bank run could cascade into systemic crisis

### Bank Regulations Since the 1900s
- **Bank Panic of 1907** → Congress passed **Federal Reserve Act of 1913** → created the Federal Reserve System (America's central bank)
- Fed = nation's chief bank regulator. Board of Governors regulates member banks; district banks supervise and enforce
- **Great Depression**: Fed failed — instead of providing liquidity, it **dried up credit**, arguably prolonging the Depression
- **1980s Savings & Loan Crisis**: aggressive lending + lax underwriting → wave of S&L failures. **FSLIC** (Federal Savings and Loan Insurance Corporation) paid depositors. American taxpayers ultimately paid billions to clean up the mess

### Bank Deregulation
- Throughout 20th century: American economy outgrew its fragmented, regional banking system
- National firms needed national banks → banking sector lobbied for deregulation
- Late 20th century deregulation: allowed banks to operate nationwide + expand services
- **Key consequence**: regulations separating traditional banks from speculative investment were repealed → banks entered speculative investment → **seeds of another financial crisis sowed**
- Today: push for new regulation to prevent future crises. Challenge: line between banks and other financial institutions has blurred → need regulatory framework encompassing ALL bank-like activities

## Supply and Demand: Markets

### Horse-Trading 101
- "Higher gas prices should lower demand" — sounds smart on TV, but there's a lot of bad economics going around
- Supply and demand concepts are central to even the most complex economic arguments

### What's a Market?
- **Market** = any place (physical or virtual) where buyers and sellers interact
- Markets exist wherever buyer and seller meet: physical location, mail, Internet
- Conditions for an **efficient market**:
  1. **Large number of buyers and sellers** acting independently in self-interest
  2. **Perfect information** — both sides know costs, quality, product details; no arbitrage opportunities
  3. **Freedom of entry and exit** — anyone can participate
- **Market efficiency debate** (sidebar): some economists argue prices capture all available information; others argue **information asymmetry** undermines efficiency
- **Monopsony** = one buyer (Walmart has monopsony power over many producers → dictates prices it pays). **Monopoly** = one seller
- Car buying example: seller has vastly more information than buyer → information asymmetry → market is less efficient
- **Licensing requirements** = barrier to entry → limits sellers → reduces competition → higher prices

### Competitive Markets
- Changes in one market ripple through related markets via price signals
- **SUV → starvation in Southeast Asia** example: gas prices rise → demand for ethanol rises → corn growers lobby for subsidies → more land planted with corn instead of wheat → wheat prices rise → rice (substitute) prices rise → people in South/Southeast Asia can't afford rice → starvation. "Markets talk to each other"
- Thomas Sowell (Hoover Institution): politicians ignore "there is no such thing as a free lunch"

### Unrealistic Models Explained
- Like the Bohr model of the atom (simplified but useful for understanding), **perfect competition** is a simplified model that helps understand real-world markets
- Conditions: large number of independent buyers/sellers, perfect information, free entry/exit, **identical products** (wheat from one farmer = wheat from another), and firms are **price-takers** (can't influence price)
- **Advantages of competitive markets** (sidebar): **allocatively efficient** (consumers get most benefit at lowest price without loss to producers) + **productively efficient** (in long run, firms produce at lowest total cost per unit)

## Supply and Demand: Consumer Behavior

### In Which Economists Define Happiness
- Economists assume people try to make themselves as happy as possible = **utility maximization**
- Consumers maximize utility **subject to their budget constraint** (limited income)

### Diminishing Marginal Utility
- Each piece of candy gives you "happy points" (utility), but each successive piece gives **fewer happy points** than the last
- This is **diminishing marginal utility** — the most fundamental law of consumer behavior
- Explains many behaviors: why not eat 50 peppermints? Why not exercise 24 hours? Why not wear a whole bottle of perfume? → diminishing marginal utility
- You consume until **marginal benefit (utility) = marginal cost (price)**. "Buy two, get the third for half-price" works because the reduced price makes MB ≥ MC for the third doughnut

### Demand
- **Demand** = willingness and ability to buy at various prices
- **Law of demand**: more people buy at lower prices than at higher prices (downward-sloping demand curve)
- Three reasons for the law of demand:

1. **Diminishing marginal utility**: each additional unit provides less satisfaction → you'll only buy more if price drops
2. **Income effect**: as price drops, your purchasing power increases (same budget buys more) → you buy more. Explains why sales/discounts work
3. **Substitution effect**: when one good's price drops relative to substitutes, consumers switch to the cheaper good. Beef on sale → buy more beef, less chicken

### Elasticity of Demand
- **Elasticity** = sensitivity of demand to price changes
- **Elastic demand** (price-sensitive): purchase can be delayed, many substitutes exist, or purchase takes large share of income. Example: facelifts — can be delayed, substitutes exist (Botox), expensive
- **Inelastic demand** (price-insensitive): purchase is urgent, no close substitutes, or small share of income. Example: emergency appendectomy — can't delay, no substitute, price is irrelevant
- Appendectomy vs. facelift = perfect illustration of elastic vs. inelastic demand

## Supply and Demand: A Price Is Born

### Finding Common Ground for Producers and Consumers
- **Law of supply**: producers are willing to sell more as the price increases (upward-sloping supply curve)
- Reason: as production increases, marginal costs rise → producers need higher prices to cover higher costs
- Rational, self-interested suppliers only produce if they can cover their costs

### Elasticity of Supply
- **Elasticity of supply** = how responsive producers are to price changes
- Key factor: **time to produce**
- **Elastic supply**: producers can respond quickly to price changes (corn tortillas — readily available materials, quick production)
- **Inelastic supply**: producers need considerable time to respond (Pinot Noir wine — vines take years to develop, grapes take time to ripen, wine needs aging)

### A Price Is Born — Equilibrium
- When supply meets demand → **equilibrium price** (market-clearing price)
- Prices are determined by impersonal forces of supply and demand, not central planners or oligarchs
- Consumers maximize utility vs. producers maximize profits → their tension determines price AND quantity
- Prices are **fair** (neutral — favor neither buyer nor seller), **efficient** (understood by all participants — a child with $5 in a candy shop can figure out what she can afford), and **effective** (ration goods to those who value them most)

### Finding Equilibrium
- **Equilibrium**: prevailing price where there is neither surplus nor shortage
- **Surplus** (price too high): quantity supplied > quantity demanded → goods pile up → clearance racks. The clearance rack exists to lower price enough to induce "some hapless, utility-maximizing individual" to purchase
- **Shortage** (price too low): quantity demanded > quantity supplied → buyers compete → bid price up → auctions exploit this. "The consumer who wants the good the most gets it. How do you know he wanted it the most? He offered the most money"

## Changes in Supply and Demand

### What Goes Up Must Come Down
- Change in demand or supply → changes in BOTH price and quantity
- **Critical distinction**: Price affects **quantity supplied** (movement along the curve), but price does NOT affect **supply** itself (the whole curve)
- Supply = producers' willingness to produce various amounts at various prices, NOT a fixed quantity
- Coffee example: supply of coffee is influenced by weather, land prices, other producers, futures, cocoa profits, subsidies — but NOT the current price of coffee

### What Causes Supply to Change (Supply Shifters)
1. **Nature**: weather, temperature, disease affect harvests. Good weather → more supply; bad weather → less
2. **Input prices**: land, seed, fertilizer, pesticide, labor, equipment, storage. Costs rise → supply decreases at every price; costs fall → supply increases
3. **Competition**: more producers entering market → more supply. Coffee's rising popularity → more growers → more competition → greater quantity supplied at each price
4. **Expected future prices**: if producers expect prices to rise → withhold production now (decrease current supply) to sell later at higher prices. If prices expected to fall → sell more now
5. **Related goods' profitability**: coffee-growing land is also good for cocoa. If cocoa is more profitable → land shifts from coffee to cocoa → coffee supply decreases
6. **Government**: taxes, subsidies, regulations all affect supply. Brazil taxing coffee production → supply decreases

## Changes in Supply and Demand (continued)

### More Supply Shifters
7. **Technology and capital**: technological innovation increases quantity firms can produce at every price. **Capital deepening** (more physical capital relative to labor) also increases supply
- Reminder: **capital** in economics = tools, factories, equipment used in production. Capital ≠ money. Capital is the product of investment.

### What Causes Changes in Demand (Demand Shifters)
1. **Consumer tastes**: subject to change due to health scares (*E. coli* spinach recall → spinach demand drops), advertising (ShamWow TV ads → demand surge not from price change but taste change), trends, fashion
2. **Complements and substitutes**:
   - **Complements** = goods used together (movie tickets + Junior Mints). Price of one rises → demand for both falls. Price of tickets rises → fewer people go → less Junior Mints demand
   - **Substitutes** = goods used in lieu of each other (airline tickets vs. bus tickets). Price of one rises → demand for the other rises. Airline tickets more expensive → more bus riders
   - **Cross-price elasticity** (sidebar): % change in quantity demanded of good A / % change in price of good B. Negative = complements, positive = substitutes
3. **Normal and inferior goods**:
   - **Normal goods**: income rises → demand rises (organic milk). Income and demand move in SAME direction
   - **Inferior goods**: income rises → demand falls (powdered milk). Income and demand move in OPPOSITE direction
   - Example: income increases → more organic milk demand, less powdered milk demand
4. **Number of buyers**: more buyers → more demand. "Consider before investing in the Russian housing market — the population there is shrinking"
5. **Expected future prices**: expect price to rise → demand increases now (buy before it goes up). Expect price to fall → demand decreases now (wait for the sale). Stock market: expect stock to rise → demand increases → price rises (self-fulfilling)

## Accounting Versus Economics

### You Say Potato, I Say Rutabaga
- Joke: "An accountant asked an economist why she chose economics. 'I'm good with numbers, but I don't have enough personality to be an accountant.'"
- **Accountant's view**: Total cost = explicit fixed costs + explicit variable costs. Profit = total revenue − total cost. Straightforward, measurable
- **Economist's view**: Total cost = explicit costs + **opportunity cost** (implicit cost). Economic profit = total revenue − total cost including opportunity cost
- Opportunity cost is harder to define and quantify, but gives a clearer picture of whether the business is **the best use of resources**

### A Case in Point — Snow Cone Example
- Teacher earning $5,000/month quits to sell snow cones. Explicit costs = $2,000. Revenue = $6,000
- **Accounting profit**: $6,000 − $2,000 = **$4,000 profit**
- **Economic profit**: $6,000 − ($2,000 + $5,000 opportunity cost) = **−$1,000 economic loss**
- The $1,000 economic loss signals: your resources are better used elsewhere (teaching)
- But for a 12-year-old whose alternative is mowing lawns → opportunity cost ≈ $0 → snow cones are fine

### Revenue and Profit (sidebar)
- **Revenue** = quantity sold × price (100 apples × $1 = $100 revenue)
- **Profit** = revenue − cost. Many people confuse the two

### Why Economic Profit Matters
- Economic profits attract firms from other industries → resources flow to their most efficient use
- In the long run, competition eliminates economic profits → **zero economic profit** = most efficient allocation
- At zero economic profit: no incentive for firms to enter or leave the market
- The $1,000 economic loss is a signal to leave the snow-cone industry; for the kid, making snow cones is as efficient as any alternative

## The Production Function

### The Only Constant Is Change
- Businesses must make production decisions in response to market conditions
- Key question: is a change in demand temporary or permanent?
- **Short run** vs. **long run** — NOT about fixed time periods, but about **flexibility of inputs**

### The Long and Short of It
- **Short run**: firms can vary only ONE input (usually labor). Restaurant example: short run = hire/fire workers, but can't expand the kitchen
- **Long run**: firms can vary ALL inputs (labor, capital, land). Restaurant example: long run = expand the kitchen, add new equipment, relocate

### The Stages of Production Function
- **Production function** = how output changes as a firm changes a single input (labor)
- Three stages:

1. **Increasing returns**: each additional worker contributes MORE to output than the previous one. **Marginal product** is increasing. Cheerful Candle Company: 1 worker = 10 candles, add worker 2 → 15 candles (through division of labor and specialization). Both output and marginal product are increasing
2. **Diminishing returns**: output still increases, but each additional worker contributes LESS than the previous one. Marginal product is decreasing but positive. By employee #100, that employee produces less than #50 — but still adds to total output
3. **Negative returns**: adding more workers actually DECREASES total output. Both output and marginal product fall. Too many people, too little space → workers get in each other's way

### Production Function Illustrated — Restaurant Example
- 6 AM: 1 employee does everything (cook, serve, cashier) — increasing returns as you add workers 2-3 (division of labor)
- Employees 4-7: more customers served, but productivity gains diminishing
- Employees 8-256: kitchen packed like sardines, workers immobilized, customers outraged, output drops to zero → **negative returns**

## Controlling Costs

### Because of Profit Motive
- "Law of Gordon 'Greed Is Good' Gekko" — firms strive for maximum profit
- In competitive markets, firms can't do much about revenue (they're price-takers — if Target sells toilet paper for $1, Walmart must match)
- What firms CAN control: **costs**. To maximize profits, produce at lowest cost possible
- **Depreciation** (sidebar): as capital ages, its value declines → eventually needs replacement

### Types of Costs
1. **Fixed costs (overhead)**: don't change regardless of production level. Rent, property tax, management salaries, depreciation. Factory running at full capacity or idle → overhead is the same
2. **Variable costs**: change with output level. Utilities, hourly wages, per-unit taxes. More production → higher variable costs
3. **Total cost** = fixed costs + variable costs
- **In the long run, ALL costs are variable** (sidebar) — firms can add/subtract capital, renegotiate rent, alter salaries. Fixed vs. variable distinction disappears over time
- For cost analysis: using accountant's view (Revenue − Costs = Profit), not economist's (which adds opportunity cost)

### Marginal Cost of Production
- **Marginal cost** = change in total cost for each additional unit produced
- Each additional unit incurs more variable cost → marginal cost increases
- Each Big Mac costs more to produce than the previous one (more ingredients + more worker time for each additional unit)

### Marginal Revenue
- Firms maximize profits where **marginal cost = marginal revenue** (MC = MR)
- If MC of "one more hamburger" = 99¢ and it sells for 99¢ → MC = MR → optimal production level
- If MC = $1.29 but price = 99¢ → producing at a loss per unit → "some explaining to do to stockholders"

## Perfect Competition in the Short Run

### Let's Pretend!
- Perfect competition = hypothetical "fairy-tale realm" alongside unicorns and sea dragons
- Useful model for understanding real-world market behavior, even though no real market meets all conditions

### Perfect Competition in Four Easy Pieces
1. **Perfect knowledge**: consumers and producers know everything about the product and market → rational decisions to maximize utility/profit
2. **Free entry and exit**: no barriers — no venture capitalists, no going-out-of-business sales needed
3. **Identical products**: all outputs are the same (Keds = Dolce & Gabbana in this world), all inputs are the same
4. **Many competitors**: no firm has pricing power — the **industry** sets the price, not individual businesses. Demand is **perfectly elastic** (many acceptable substitutes)
- Result: firms are **price-takers** — they sell at the market price, period

### Competition and the Short Run
- Short run = firms can't enter or exit (can only vary labor, not fixed capital)
- In the short run, firms in perfect competition can earn **economic profits** or operate at a **loss**
- **Glazed doughnut example**:
  - New Zealand scientists discover doughnuts + coffee = extremely healthy → demand surges → price rises → firms earn economic profits (revenue > total economic cost)
  - Six months later, California scientists debunk the study → demand crashes → price falls → firms produce at a loss (revenue < total cost)
  - In the short run, firms are stuck — can't quickly reduce fixed costs or quickly expand

---

## Perfect Competition in the Long Run

### The Race to Zero
- In the long run, firms CAN enter and exit the market
- **Economic profits attract new firms** → more competition → supply increases → price falls → profits disappear
- **Economic losses cause firms to exit** → less competition → supply decreases → price rises → losses disappear
- Doughnut example continued:
  - New Zealand discovery → economic profits → new firms enter → supply increases → price drops → profits eliminated
  - California debunking → economic losses → firms reach shutdown point → exit → supply decreases → price rises → losses eliminated
- **Long-run equilibrium**: zero economic profit. No incentive to enter or leave

### From Competition to Imperfect Competition: A Continuum
- Perfect competition doesn't really exist — but provides a benchmark for comparing real market structures
- **The Evolution of Markets** (sidebar): firms may start competitive and become monopolistic over time. Late 19th/early 20th century trusts rose from once-competitive industries. Some called for ending "ruinous competition"
- Reality: most firms face barriers to entry (cost, government requirements), products are differentiated (not identical), firms are large and NOT independent, information is NOT equally shared
- What you get: **imperfect competition**
- Market structure spectrum: perfectly competitive → monopolistic competition → oligopoly → monopoly

## Monopolistic Competition (from Perfect Competition in the Long Run chapter)

- Very similar to perfect competition: many buyers/sellers, low barriers to entry, information readily available
- KEY difference: firms do NOT offer identical products — they **differentiate** their products
- **Product differentiation** = convincing consumers your product is different from competitors'
- Fast food = textbook monopolistic competition: Taco Bell, Chick-fil-A, McDonald's, Subway all compete in the same market but each offers a different menu
- Apple varieties: once just "red or green," now Red Delicious, Pink Lady, Granny Smith, Gala, Fuji, Honeycrisp — product differentiation in action
- When producers differentiate successfully → they can charge **higher prices** than competitors (unlike price-takers in perfect competition)
- **Monopolies and Profit** (sidebar): Can monopolistically competitive firms maintain economic profits in the long run? **No.** Competition eventually erodes profits → industry settles at excess capacity, high cost, zero economic profits
- **The advertising trap**: differentiation is a never-ending process → firms spend huge sums on advertising → resources used in advertising are no longer available for production → industry produces less than it would under perfect competition
- BUT consumers have a strong preference for variety → benefits of monopolistic competition may equal the costs

## Oligopolies and Imperfectly Competitive Markets

### World Domination or Die!
- Major automobile manufacturers, airlines, telecoms, food producers, discount retailers = **oligopolies** (markets with few large producers)
- Many local utilities = monopolies
- Oligopoly and monopoly are the most common market structures in the US

### Oligopoly
- A few large producers dominate; more pricing power than competitive/monopolistically competitive firms
- **Considerable barriers to entry** due to sheer scale of existing firms
- Often the result of market maturation: monopolistically competitive firms grow, merge → fewer firms remain
- Oligopolies concern government regulators trying to preserve competition

### Loss of Competition
- As competition decreases: prices rise, productive and allocative efficiency are lost
- "Has anyone ever actually loved an airline the way they love a chocolate-chip cookie? An interesting coincidence"
- **Herfindahl-Hirschman Index (HHI)**: measures market concentration. Low HHI = competitive; high HHI = oligopoly
- FTC and Justice Department use HHI to decide whether to approve mergers — if merger would significantly increase HHI, it's likely blocked
- **Concentration ratios**: four-firm concentration ratio = % of market held by top 4 firms. Vacuum cleaner industry: 78% four-firm, 96.1% eight-firm → clearly oligopoly. Perfect competition ≈ 0%. Monopoly = 100%
- **What Defines an Industry?** (sidebar): definition is blurring. Newspapers were once an industry; now they're part of "media." Local paper may have 100% of newspaper market but small share of overall media

### Oligopolist Interdependence
- Unlike competitive firms that act independently, oligopolists have an **interdependent relationship**
- Each firm's decisions significantly impact market prices → they must consider competitors' likely responses before acting

## Collusion and Cartels

### World Domination or Die! Revisited
- Competition = blessing for consumers (lower prices), curse for producers (lower profits)
- Given a choice between competition and cooperation, profit-maximizing firms would prefer **cooperation**
- Adam Smith warned: "nothing beneficial comes from the heads of business getting together" — history has proven him right
- **Cartel** (definition): group of producers that agree to cooperate instead of compete, seeking higher profits by collectively reducing production to increase prices

### Collusion
- In the US, firms are **forbidden** from cooperating to set prices or production — **Sherman Antitrust Act** + **Clayton Antitrust Act**
- Late 19th/early 20th century trust abuses → Theodore Roosevelt's "trust-busting"
- **Collusion** = illegal conspiracy to cheat or defraud consumers by artificially reducing competition
- Despite illegality, firms still collude from time to time. Business meetings almost always have attorneys present to avoid collusion accusations

### Forming Cartels
- A cartel functions as a single producer/monopoly, charging whatever price the market will bear
- **OPEC** (Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries): 13 oil-exporting countries, not subject to US antitrust law. Each member agrees to a production quota to reduce output and increase prices
- **The Achilles' heel of cartels**: members have incentive to **cheat**. When cartel succeeds and prices rise, each member wants to slightly exceed their quota to sell more at the higher price. Since ALL members have this incentive → collective cheating → prices eventually fall
- Drug cartels discourage cheating with assassination and kidnapping; OPEC uses Saudi Arabia — as largest producer with lowest costs, Saudi Arabia can flood the market, undercutting cheaters and remaining profitable. After months of losses, cheaters cooperate again

## Game Theory

### I'd Like to Buy a Vowel, Please
- **Game theory** = study of interdependent decision-making — outcomes depend on the choices of others
- Useful for understanding oligopolist behavior

### The Prisoner's Dilemma
- Adam and Karl arrested for burglary. Separated, interrogated individually
- **Payoff matrix**: If one confesses and other is silent → confessor gets 2 years, silent partner gets 10. Both confess → 3 years each. Both silent → 1 year each (trespassing only)
- Best joint outcome: both stay silent (1 year each). But each individually reasons: "If I confess, I get 2 or 3 years. If I'm silent, I get 1 or 10 years." Risk of 10 years → **both confess** → 3 years each
- This is the **dominant strategy** — confessing is best regardless of what the other does

### Game Theory in Business
- Two gas stations in an isolated town, out of sight of each other, can change price once daily
- Both high price → $1,000 each. One high, one low → $300 vs. $1,200. Both low → $750 each
- With collusion: both set high price → $1,000 each
- Without collusion: dominant strategy = set low price (guaranteed $750 minimum vs. $300 risk) — just like prisoner's dilemma
- **Key difference from prisoner's dilemma**: firms play the "game" **every day**, not just once
- **Tacit collusion** through **tit for tat**: Day 1 both charge high ($1,000). Day 2 one cheats ($1,200). Day 3 other retaliates → both low ($750). Eventually both learn: cheating today means lower profits tomorrow → settle on both charging high = **tacit collusion**

## Pricing Behaviors

### Money, Money, Money
- Businesses engage in price and non-price competition to maximize profits
- Strategies depend on market structure (oligopoly vs. monopoly)

### Pricing and Oligopolies
- **Price leadership**: dominant firm sets the price, rest follow. Decisions made public well in advance → form of tacit collusion. Consumers fare better than under cartel, worse than under high competition
- **Price wars**: firms break out of price leadership, undercut each other. Good for consumers (competitive prices). Some firms finance price wars by raising prices in other market segments. Wars continue until firms reach tacit collusion again → return to price leadership
- **Product differentiation**: instead of competing on price, firms compete on differences and uniqueness. Oligopolists spend heavily on advertising. Example: major American beer brands compete on advertising, not price

### Pricing and Monopolies
- Monopoly = single seller. Generally not allowed in the US — FTC prevents their creation
- Monopolies create problems for consumers and competitors
- Reasons monopolies exist: **economies of scale**, **geography**, **government protection**, **government mandate**

#### Price Increases
- Monopoly achievable through: controlling key resources, mergers, political influence
- **Standard Oil Trust** (John D. Rockefeller): controlled virtually all US oil production. Result: high prices, inefficient production, significant barriers to entry. World's wealthiest man. Eventually broken up via **Sherman Antitrust Act**

#### Price Discrimination
- **Price discrimination** = charging different customers different prices for the same good/service
- Railroad monopolists charged different rates for same freight to different customers
- **Clayton Antitrust Act** made most price discrimination illegal
- Acceptable forms: senior/student/military discounts at theaters; airline pricing (business travelers pay more than vacationers for same flight — business demand is inelastic, vacation demand is elastic)
- Airlines can justify this: different elasticities of demand → better rationing of tickets between those who *need* vs. those who *want*

## Monopoly: The Good, The Bad, and The Ugly

### Not Just a Board Game
- Monopoly isn't always bad — sometimes allowed for good reasons. But even "good" monopolies can have negative effects

### The Good — Natural Monopoly
- **Natural monopoly**: economies of scale make a single producer most efficient
- Example: electrical utilities. One large power plant produces electricity at much lower cost per kilowatt than thousands of small generators
- Government regulates utility prices to prevent monopoly pricing while allowing reasonable profit

### The Good — Technological Monopoly
- **Patent protection**: 20 years of exclusive rights to produce a new invention
- Encourages innovation, R&D investment (without patents, firms wouldn't invest billions knowing competitors could copy immediately)
- American and European pharmaceutical companies develop life-saving medications *because* of patent protection → monopoly profits for limited time

### The Bad? — Government Monopoly
- Government sometimes enters the marketplace directly — e.g., **U.S. Postal Service** (only entity legally allowed to deliver letters in paper envelopes — UPS/FedEx are package delivery)
- Executive branch departments and agencies = government monopolies
- Debate is philosophical: conservatives/libertarians say government is wasteful and inefficient; populists say government is less wasteful and more efficient than private sector. "You decide."

### The Ugly — Pure Unregulated Monopoly
- A sole provider that prevents all competition → can charge whatever price the consumer will pay
- Most harmful to society: one good at a price that in no way reflects the true cost of production
- "To the victor go the spoils," but once-competitive firms that become monopolists must be checked by regulation or broken up
- **De Beers**: classic example of monopoly gone bad. Controlled the resource, coerced wholesalers and cutters, created an **illusion of scarcity** in the diamond market → earned economic profits for 100+ years. "You were not coerced to buy the diamond. You bought it because the benefit outweighed the cost. The problem is you never realized how much of the cost was De Beers' profit."

## Government in the Marketplace: Price Ceilings and Price Floors

### But They Meant Well
- People petition government to correct perceived wrongs in the market, but without considering how people respond to incentives, well-intentioned policies go astray
- People are not going to stop acting like people — governments must consider whether their actions create **perverse incentives**
- Two main tools: **price ceilings** and **price floors**

### Price Ceilings
- **Definition**: a legal maximum price for a good, service, or resource
- **Nixon's 1970s price controls**: in response to rising food prices, the Nixon administration enacted price ceilings on food — retailers could not charge above the government-mandated ceiling
- The theory: impose a ceiling and prices stop rising, everyone gets food at the price they want. But this assumes people don't behave like people
- Prices are the equilibrium of supply and demand, and both forces are shaped by human nature
- **Meat example**: meat selling at $5/pound equilibrium. Consumers petition for $3 ceiling. Politicians enact it to get re-elected. The $3 price signals consumers to purchase MORE but signals producers to produce LESS → result is a **shortage** of meat at $3. Consumers got their price ceiling, but many consumers got no meat at all
- **Why price controls are inefficient**: they increase the need for monitoring and enforcement → more government bureaucracy → more taxes or borrowing to pay for it
- America eventually abandoned price controls, but it took a decade to get the underlying inflation under control
- As late as 2007, people were asking for price limits on gasoline — people still want low prices and still don't understand that controlling the market produces unintended consequences

### Price Floors
- **Definition**: a legal minimum price for a good, service, or resource
- Best-known example: **minimum wage**
- In the labor market, households supply labor and businesses demand it
- Politicians representing areas with large unskilled labor populations are pressured to increase minimum wage
- **City council example**: federal minimum raised to $10/hour city minimum. Inner city equilibrium wage = $8, suburbs = $11
  - Inner city: more workers willing to supply labor at $10, but fewer employers willing to hire at $10 → **surplus of labor = unemployment**
  - Suburbs: no effect, since equilibrium ($11) already exceeds the floor ($10)
  - Result: the policy meant to help the poor helped those who kept their jobs, but caused unemployment for those laid off and those entering the job market at $10/hour
- **The political trick**: those most in favor of raising minimum wage are often those most harmed by the increase. Politicians know this — they strategically pass increases that keep minimum wage below the average equilibrium wage for unskilled labor. Example: if market equilibrium is $8, politicians gladly raise minimum from $6 to $7.50, knowing little economic effect, but claiming credit for "raising" the minimum wage

## Government in the Marketplace: Taxes and Subsidies

### The Only Certainties in Life
- Government has a power businesses envy: the power to coerce payment (taxation)
- A **subsidy** is the opposite of a tax: money given by government to support an industry or business
- Government uses both not only to raise revenue or redistribute income, but also to **shape incentives** and change the marketplace

### How Taxation Shapes Markets
- Taxes as microeconomic policy: if government wants to reduce production of a good, tax the producer → raises costs → reduces supply
- **Civil War currency example**: prior to the Civil War, thousands of different currencies circulated with no national currency. The war created impetus for a national currency (greenback). Congress taxed all other forms of currency, removing them from the market. Would you issue a dollar if it cost you $1.50?
- **Luxury taxes**: taxes on expensive goods and services raise revenue without necessarily affecting the market much — if you're buying a $500,000 automobile, $501,000 doesn't change your decision
- **Sin taxes**: taxes on nonessential but popular products like alcohol and cigarettes. Intended to both generate revenue and reduce consumption of undesirable products. High sin taxes can lead to black markets

### How Subsidies Shape Markets
- Subsidies encourage rather than discourage production — they increase supply and reduce price
- **Farm subsidies**: ensure adequate food supply + give American farm exports a price advantage. Sometimes subsidies are given to farmers NOT to produce, keeping production artificially low and prices artificially high (good for farmers, not consumers). Critics argue farm subsidies create inefficiency and misallocate scarce resources
- **Subsidies impede competition for poor nations**: many poor countries depend on agriculture as their chief export. Industrialized nations want poor nations to open up to free trade but are reluctant to end their own farm subsidies — a major sticking point in WTO negotiations
- **Other types of subsidies**: export subsidies (help domestic markets compete internationally), redevelopment subsidies (encourage producers to locate in certain areas), indirect subsidies (tax breaks, low-interest loan guarantees — one reason the income tax code is so complicated)
- **College loan subsidies**: some economists argue subsidized loans and grants are part of the problem, not the cure. The financial aid system, while well-intentioned, increases demand for college → increased demand leads to higher prices
- **Consumer subsidies**: directed at consumers rather than producers (e.g., rent subsidy for low-income earner). Meant to protect the disadvantaged, but they do affect the market

### Consumer Subsidies and the Black Market
- Even food subsidies for needy families are subject to black market activity
- Some receiving food assistance trade $2 in food stamps for $1 cash — they benefit from freedom to purchase what they want; the buyer benefits from half-price groceries
- The system is inefficient and creates disutility for recipients
- **Cash vs. in-kind transfer argument**: Under a cash system, the taxpayer wins (gives same value of service at fraction of cost), and the recipient wins (buys groceries without stigma, can buy other valued items)
- **Counter-argument**: some might use cash for alcohol, cigarettes, or drugs. But the food stamp recipient who sells $100 in stamps for $50 to buy cigarettes ends up with $50 in cigarettes and no food. The cash recipient who spends $50 on cigarettes still has $50 left for food — cash system removes the black market and is cheaper to administer

## Market Failures

### Come on and Take a Free Ride
- Sometimes the market fails to provide a necessary good or service, or fails to properly assign costs — economists call this a **market failure**
- Government has capacity to step in and deal with market failures

### Public Goods
- If a good is **non-rival** and **non-excludable**, the free market will probably not provide it
  - **Non-rival**: one person's consumption doesn't diminish another's (e.g., movie theater — another person's presence doesn't stop your viewing; contrast with a candy bar — rival good)
  - **Non-excludable**: the producer can't withhold it from those unwilling to pay (e.g., public highways)
- A movie theater is non-rival but excludable. Public highways are non-rival and non-excludable
- Private firms have little incentive to produce public highways at their own cost → government must provide these types of goods

### Positive Externality
- Sometimes production creates a spillover or unintended benefit for someone who is neither the producer nor the consumer — a **positive externality**
- **Examples**: neighbor's landscaping increases your home value; flu vaccines reduce others' chance of contracting disease (free-riders benefit without paying)
- When production creates a positive externality, there is never enough of it → government may subsidize its production
- **Public schools**: government subsidizes education because an educated population creates a positive externality — businesses don't have to teach workers to read or compute math

### Negative Externality
- Occur when production or consumption creates spillover costs to society
- **Pollution example**: shoe manufacturer produces shoes AND air pollution. The cost of pollution is not factored into the price of shoes. People near the factory bear costs but receive no payment. There is too much of the good being produced → government can tax the producer to reduce output and pollution

### Black Markets
- Whenever government attempts to control prices or meddle with supply and demand, a black market (illegal trade) will develop
- Markets develop when there is supply and demand for a product — doesn't matter what it is. If government intervention creates surpluses or shortages, someone will step in to circumvent government and clear the market

### Thwarting Price Ceilings
- **NYC rent control** (dating back to WWII): desirable apartments at extremely low prices, but almost none available. Black market subletting emerges — rent-controlled tenant sublets to someone willing to pay much more (still below market rate). Both benefit, landlord is no worse off. Yet the city actively pursues illegal sublets, spending scarce resources to enforce the controls. Solution: remove rent controls and let the market set rents
- **Ticket scalping**: who wants to see an event more — someone willing to pay $50 or $1,000? When ticket prices are limited, those willing to pay more compete with those who would receive less utility from seeing the event

### Thwarting Price Floors
- Workers willing to work for less than minimum wage resort to contract labor or illegal cash payments
- **Important distinction**: exploitation occurs when one party is unable to freely choose whether to engage in a transaction (forced low wages, threat of deportation). Assuming the transaction is voluntary, both business and workers benefit. If it were not mutually beneficial, it would not occur

## Financial Markets and Loanable Funds Theory

### What's Behind the Curtain?
- Financial markets serve a basic purpose: to connect people who have money with people who want money
- Even though they appear complicated, the lingo ("Dow Jones closed up 30 points," "ten-year treasury ended lower") describes simple matching of savers and borrowers

### Loanable Funds Theory
- Economists' simple model for understanding financial markets and how the **real interest rate** is determined
- Real interest rate = nominal interest rate minus inflation
- The hypothetical **loanable funds market** brings together "savers" (supply) and "borrowers" (demand)
- The real interest rate is the price; equilibrium occurs where amount saved equals amount borrowed
- **Savers as producers**: respond to price (real interest rate) by offering more funds as rate increases, less as it decreases
- **Borrowers as consumers**: at high interest rates, less willing to borrow; at low rates, more willing
- **"Investment" in economics vs. finance**: in economics, investment means borrowing to purchase physical capital. In finance, investment means saving/buying securities. Savers engage in financial investment, which provides funds for borrowers to engage in capital investment
- **Expanded view**: savers and borrowers include households, businesses, governments, and the foreign sector. Changes in saving/borrowing behavior of these sectors change the real interest rate and quantity of loanable funds exchanged
  - Foreign savers deciding to save more in the U.S. → lower real interest rate + more funds exchanged
  - U.S. government engaging in deficit spending → increases demand for loanable funds → higher real interest rate
- Loanable funds theory is useful for understanding changes in **long-term** interest rates

### Liquidity Preference (Keynes)
- **John Maynard Keynes's** theory explains short-term nominal interest rates
- Instead of saving/borrowing behavior, Keynes taught that short-term interest rates are a function of consumers' **liquidity preference** — their inclination for holding cash
- The **money market** (not loanable funds market) is central: the central bank supplies money, and households/businesses/government demand money at various nominal interest rates
- Central banks like the Federal Reserve act as **regulated monopolies** and issue money independent of the interest rate
- **Liquidity preference = demand for money**: at high nominal rates, people prefer interest-bearing assets like bonds over cash; as rates fall, people hold more cash because they're not sacrificing much interest
- **Competing theories**: loanable funds theory is associated with classical economics; money market theory is associated with Keynesian economics
- **Fed's tool**: to lower nominal rates → increase money supply; to raise rates → decrease money supply
- Changes in nominal GDP also cause money demand to increase or decrease
- If the Fed holds money supply constant: increased money demand → higher nominal rate; decreased money demand → lower nominal rate

## The Money Market

### I'll Have a T-Bill with a Side of Fries
- The money market is where the central bank supplies money, and firms, banks, and governments obtain short-term financing
- Securities with **maturities of less than one year** are included in this market

### Commercial Paper
- Short-term unsecured IOUs issued by businesses with excellent credit
- **Example**: Behemoth Corporation needs to pay employees before customers pay at month-end → issues commercial paper for cash → repays when customers pay
- Cheaper for companies than longer-term debt; provides lenders a liquid, relatively safe way to earn interest on extra cash
- **Unsecured debt**: no collateral, no ownership interest — you're repaid by cash flow. If the firm defaults, you're stuck
- Companies with less-than-perfect credit must offer higher interest rates to attract takers (more risk for lender)
- Demand is much greater for higher-rated commercial paper
- Typically sold in large chunks (~$100,000 per issue), interest is taxable, SEC doesn't regulate their sale
- This is why **money market funds** are popular with private investors — allow diversification across many commercial paper investments

### Treasury Bills
- U.S. government's short-term borrowing instrument — auctioned for cash needs, maturities less than one year
- Investors like T-bills: risk-free interest while maintaining liquidity
- Government likes T-bills: easy access to cash for spending
- **Sold at a discount from face value**: T-bills have a face value of $1,000, but buyers pay less. The difference = interest payment. Example: Rich Personne buys a 52-week $1,000 T-bill for $950 → receives $50 interest at maturity = 5.26% return on a $950 investment

### Federal Funds
- Banks sometimes need more cash than they have in the vault — they lend and borrow **federal funds** (reserves) from each other
- Banks borrow in the fed funds market to satisfy legal reserve requirements or meet contractual clearing balances
- Critical for maintaining bank liquidity and encouraging efficiency — banks put all available excess reserves to work earning a return
- Most reserves are kept on deposit with the Fed, so the exchange of federal funds occurs almost immediately as banks exchange reserve balances
- The **Federal Reserve influences** this market by targeting the **fed funds rate** — the interest rate banks charge each other for overnight use of federal funds

## The Bond Market

### Call Me Bond, Coupon Bond
- For long-term financing, governments and firms borrow in the bond market
- Investors buying bonds are lending money to sellers, expecting repayment of principal plus interest
- For issuers: efficient means of borrowing large sums. For buyers: relatively secure financial investment providing interest income

### Federal Government Bonds
- Two main types:
  - **Coupon bonds**: sold at or near face value, provide guaranteed interest payments
  - **Zero coupon bonds**: sold at a discount from face value, pay face value at maturity
- Either type provides interest income while preserving principal; secondary market trading makes them relatively liquid
- U.S. government issues several types with maturities > 1 year:
  - **Treasury notes**: medium-term, maturities 2-10 years
  - **Treasury bonds**: long-term, mature after 30 years
- The **10-year Treasury note** interest rate is crucial — it serves as a **benchmark** for both corporate bonds and mortgages. As the 10-year rate fluctuates, corporate rates and mortgage rates fluctuate too
- **Agency securities**: independent agencies of the U.S. government (Fannie Mae, Freddie Mac, Sallie Mae) issue bonds to finance operations. They lack the full repayment guarantee of Treasury securities but are backed by the government and seen as virtually guaranteed. Offer higher interest rates than Treasuries — attractive for investors wanting near-government security with better yield

### State and Local Government Bonds
- **Municipal bonds**: finance schools, roads, public projects
- Key advantage: interest is **exempt from federal income taxes** → attractive to investors
- Because interest is tax-exempt, munis don't need to offer as high a rate → state/local governments borrow more cheaply than the private sector

### Corporate Bonds
- Firms borrow by issuing corporate bonds — get money for capital investment without bank financing or diluting ownership
- Chief advantage: **financial leverage**. Example: company with $1,000 earning 10% = $100 return. But if the firm borrows $1,000,000 and invests at 10%, it earns $100,000 without risking its own money
- Investors require higher interest rates on corporate bonds than on Treasuries or munis because firms lack the ability to tax (and therefore occasionally default)

### Bond Risks
- **Investment risk** (default): governments or firms may fail to repay
- **Inflation risk**: rising inflation erodes the real return
- **Interest rate risk**: if rates rise, the bond's value decreases until its effective yield equals the new higher rate — bondholders may lose principal if selling before maturity
- **Early call risk**: if rates decline, the issuer may retire/call the bond and refinance at lower rates — bondholder loses out on higher interest payments

### Bond Ratings
- **Rating agencies** (Moody's, Standard & Poor's, Fitch) rate bonds from "prime" to "investment grade" to "junk" to "in default"
- As a bond's rating falls, the issuer must offer higher interest rates to compensate for additional risk
- **2008 crisis warning**: many bonds were so quickly downgraded that investors holding "prime" bonds discovered they had junk bonds within a short period — don't rely solely on ratings

### Interest Rates Revisited
- Interest rates are composed of: **real rate + expected inflation premium + default risk premium + liquidity premium + maturity risk**
- Real rate + expected inflation = **risk-free rate of return** (benchmark for all other interest rates)
- U.S. Treasury securities are the proxies for the risk-free rate — the U.S. has never defaulted in its 220+ year history, and the secondary market is "deep" (backed by full faith and credit)
- The only appreciable risk for Treasury holders is **maturity risk**: the longer the term, the greater the chance interest rates will change. Rising rates → bond value decreases → new bonds with higher rates make previously issued bonds less attractive and less valuable

## The Stock Market

### Taking Stock of Companies
- Of all financial markets, the stock market receives the most media coverage
- Unlike bonds (loans), stocks represent **partial ownership** in firms

### Stocks, Simplified
- Privately held companies keep shares among a small group (founders); publicly traded companies issue stock on exchanges
- **IPO** (Initial Public Offering): how firms raise money for capital investment by issuing stock for the first time
- Investors expect either **dividends** (share of profits distributed to owners) or **capital gains** (selling at a higher price than purchase)
  - Dividend example: 100 shares outstanding, firm earns $1M, distributes half → each share earns $5,000 dividend
  - Capital gain example: buy GM at $35.75, sell at $50.25 → $14.50 capital gain
- **Stock options**: a derivative allowing purchase of shares at a predetermined price. Companies issue them to key employees as performance rewards. Recipients can sell the option contract or wait and exercise when the stock price increases
- **Secondary market**: the majority of stock purchases occur here. When you buy Coca-Cola stock, you're buying from another individual, not from Coca-Cola. The firm only receives money from an IPO or selling repurchased stock

### Types of Stocks
- **Common stock**: partial ownership + voting rights for firm's leadership
- **Preferred stock**: ownership claim but no voting privileges; preferred stockholders get paid dividends before common stockholders
- Trade-offs for issuers: common stock → risk of founders being displaced if new stockholders gain majority; preferred stock → guaranteed dividends may hinder reinvesting profits for growth

### So What's the Point?
- All these markets (money market, bond market, stock market) serve to connect savers with borrowers
- Firms seeking capital investment look to bonds and stocks as sources of funds
- Finding the right combination of stock and bond financing = **capital structure** — important to both the business and the economy

### Interest Rate and Investment
- **Inverse relationship** between interest rate and investment: lower rates → lower cost of capital → more investment; higher rates → less investment
- But low interest rates alone aren't enough — firms also consider **expected rates of return**
- **Gas pedal / brake pedal analogy**: expected rate of return = gas pedal (profit opportunities accelerate investment); interest rate = brake pedal (higher rates slow investment). If interest rates are low and expected profits are high → business grows. If interest rate exceeds expected return → firms are stationary
- Increases in expected return accelerate investment; increases in interest rate slow or stop it

## Foreign Exchange and Exchange Rates

### C'est La Vie, No Es?
- Foreign exchange affects everyday life — products you buy, vacations you take
- **Balance of payments**: records all inflows and outflows of currency from a country
  - **Current account** = net exports + net foreign factor income + net transfers
  - **Financial account** = net foreign investment + official reserves

### Exchange Rates
- The exchange rate is simply the current price of one currency in terms of another (e.g., $1 = €0.73 = £0.64 = ¥89.41)
- Determined in the **foreign exchange market** — the world's largest market, annual trade volume approaching $1 quadrillion, transactions 24 hours a day
- Dominated by British, Americans, and Japanese; vast majority of trades in U.S. dollars. Euro, pound sterling, yen, and Swiss franc are other hard currencies most often traded
- **Participants**: large banks (main players, interconnected brokers serving corporate and individual customers), central banks (manipulate exchange rates or correct imbalances), speculators (profit from arbitrage)
- **Appreciation** = exchange rate increases; **depreciation** = exchange rate falls
- Changes in exchange rates can wreak havoc on businesses and entire economies

### What Affects Exchange Rates?

#### Taste and Preference
- As consumers' tastes for imported goods change, exchange rates between countries change
- Example: American popularity of South Korean cars/electronics creates demand for the won (₩) → Americans supply dollars and demand won → dollar depreciates, won appreciates
- Over time, appreciation of a currency may reduce product popularity as goods become relatively more expensive
- Foreign manufacturers may price products directly in dollars to insulate from exchange rate changes (e.g., German automakers pricing in dollars to offset euro/dollar fluctuations)

#### Interest Rates and Inflation
- Changes in real interest rates cause sudden fluctuations in exchange rates (unlike trade flows which are more stable)
- Savers are attracted to high interest rates → when one country's rate rises relative to another, savings flow toward the higher rate
- Example: U.S. rates rise relative to Japanese rates → increased supply of yen + increased demand for dollars → dollar appreciates, yen depreciates
- Eventually the dollar's appreciation offsets interest rate gains → **interest rate parity**
- **Cost of foreign exchange**: you face different rates when buying vs. selling currencies — the spread eats into interest rate advantages
- **Inflation destroys currency value**: Zimbabwe's inflation reduced its currency value until a sheet of paper was worth more than a unit of currency. Rational Zimbabweans eagerly supply their currency for any available stable currency (e.g., South Africa's rand). Inflation reduces a currency's value both domestically and in foreign exchange

#### Relative Income and Speculation
- **Counterintuitive outcome**: as an economy strengthens (income rises), its currency weakens. Why? Higher income → higher propensity to import → more domestic currency supplied in foreign exchange
- Example: Canada's income rises relative to U.S. → Canadian dollar weakens vs. U.S. dollar (Canadians shop more, supply more CAD in forex)
- **Strong vs. weak dollar**: a weak dollar is actually the remedy for a trade deficit — depreciated dollars encourage exports and discourage imports. "Strong" and "weak" don't necessarily mean "good" and "bad"
- **Speculation**: banks, companies, individuals profit from predicting currency movements. For most people, forex speculation is about as profitable as craps in Las Vegas
- But speculators serve an important economic function: they bear exchange rate risk so risk-averse companies can use **futures contracts** to lock in exchange rates and avoid surprises

### The Law of One Price and Purchasing Power Parity
- According to the economic law of one price / **purchasing power parity (PPP)**: after accounting for the exchange rate, prices of similar goods should be the same regardless of where they are purchased
- Example: if $1 = SFr 1.10, snow skis selling for $500 in the U.S. should sell for SFr 550 in Switzerland. If Swiss price is SFr 600, consumers benefit from importing from America → supply Swiss francs, demand dollars → exchange rate adjusts until PPP is reflected

## Exports and the Balance of Trade

### Nothing but Net
- Most U.S. producers focus on domestic market, but some export and others import goods the U.S. doesn't produce
- Half of U.S. trade occurs with Canada, Mexico, China, Japan, Germany, and the United Kingdom

### The Balance of Trade
- **Net exports** (balance of trade) = value of all exports minus value of all imports
- U.S. has a **trade deficit** (imports > exports), but is still the world's largest exporter (12% of global share vs. China's 6.4%)
- Balance of trade in **goods**: contributes to the trade deficit (Americans prefer foreign consumer goods and resources)
- Balance of trade in **services**: U.S. is a net exporter (transportation, logistics, engineering, legal, technical services)
- **BEA (Bureau of Economic Analysis)**: government agency measuring balance of trade. In 2008, the trade deficit was ~$696 billion ($840B goods deficit + $144B services surplus)

### Net Foreign Factor Income
- When factors of production (land, labor, capital, entrepreneurship) are employed abroad or their owners reside abroad, factor payments (rent, wages, interest, profits) require foreign exchange
- Whether inflow or outflow, foreign exchange is needed

### Net Transfers
- **Remittances** (unearned transfer payments) and **foreign aid** create outflows of currency from rich to poor countries
- U.S. aid payments to Pakistan, Israel, Egypt are examples

### Net Foreign Investment
- Unlike one-time exchanges (trade, factor income, transfers), net foreign investment creates recurring payments and income
- When citizens purchase real or financial assets of another country = net foreign investment
- **Portfolio investment**: foreigners purchasing financial assets (T-bills, stocks, bonds) of another country. During the 2008 crisis, the dollar appreciated as foreign investors sought U.S. Treasury safety
- Developing countries attract investment due to higher growth rates and interest rates
- **Critical link**: the balance on the current account is completely offset by the balance on the financial account
- **Downside**: savings can flow out as easily as they flow in. A sudden outflow may precipitate a currency crisis, inflation, and high interest rates
  - **Argentina (2001)**: defaulted on foreign debt → savings flowed out → currency crisis + financial and political turmoil
- **Capital controls**: countries fearing outflows limit the ability of savings to leave. India fared well during the late-1990s Asian financial crisis because capital controls limited outflows. But the downside: capital controls discourage foreign investors from entering — the mere mention of capital controls can trigger outflows

## Official Reserves and Exchange Rate Policy

### Or Why the Euro Exists
- The financial account of the balance of payments includes **official reserves** — foreign currency held by central banks
- Purpose: stabilize the foreign exchange market. Balance of payments deficit → Fed reduces foreign reserves to zero out the balance; surplus → Fed acquires additional reserves

### The U.S. Dollar as Foreign Reserve
- U.S. is in a unique position: it issues the **reserve currency** for most countries (most foreign reserve holdings are in dollars)
- China and Japan together held over **$5 trillion** in reserves (as of 2015) — a source of concern
- Fear: if China or Japan suddenly dumped dollar holdings, the surge in dollar supply could cause a collapse
- Counter-argument: dumping dollars would be equally harmful to the Chinese and Japanese, so they have little incentive to do so

### To Fix or Float? That Is the Question
- **Bretton Woods (post-WWII)**: countries met in Bretton Woods, NH and established a fixed exchange rate system pegged to the dollar. Benefits: businesses could trade without fear of fluctuating exchange rates; pegging to the dollar forced countries to practice sound economic policy. Initially successful, but completely collapsed by 1971
- Today, multiple approaches coexist:
  - **Floating**: U.S. and U.K. let currencies float freely — no official reserves needed to maintain rates. Benefit: policymakers can set interest rate policies for domestic growth/inflation without worrying about exchange rates
  - **Pegged**: China, Japan, Hong Kong peg to the U.S. dollar by actively trading foreign reserves. This keeps their exports relatively cheap and maintains competitive advantage in the American market. Downside for China: resources spent stabilizing the rate could be used more profitably; acquiring more dollar-denominated assets makes China vulnerable to American economic troubles
  - **Unified currency**: Europe created the **euro** — the world's second largest currency. France and Germany can trade freely without exchange rate concerns. Major drawback: individual countries can't manage their own monetary policy
- **Monetary policy** definition: efforts by the central bank to stabilize prices, promote full employment, and encourage long-run growth through controlling the money supply and interest rates
- **Greece (2010)**: suffered a severe blow when sovereign debt was downgraded. Unable to implement independent monetary policy under the euro, Greece was forced to cut spending and raise taxes or risk default. The EU's response will either weaken or bolster the euro's chances of becoming the dominant world currency

## The Private Sector and the Public Sector

### Just Goin' with the Flow
- In an economic system, people's self-interest manifests in markets and through the political process — both are intertwined
- The **circular flow model** shows the relationship between households, firms, governments, and the foreign sector as they interact in product markets (goods), factor markets (labor), and financial markets (stocks, bonds)

### The Private Sector
- The private sector = households + businesses
- Households buy goods and services from firms in the **product market**

#### The Factor Market
- Households sell labor and entrepreneurship to firms in exchange for wages and profits
- Households sell natural resources to firms in exchange for rents
- If you work for someone: you sell labor in the factor market to get money for goods in the product market
- As a business owner: you sell entrepreneurial ability in exchange for profits

#### Factor Flow and Market Flow
- Two flows between households and firms:
  1. Goods/services (product market) + land/labor/capital/entrepreneurship (factor market) flow one direction
  2. Consumption spending + factor payments (rent, wages, interest, profits) flow the opposite direction

### The Public Sector
- The public sector = all levels of government (local to federal)
- Government interacts with households by purchasing factors of production (land, labor) in exchange for factor payments (wages, rent, interest)
- Government interacts with firms by buying goods and services in the product market
- Government combines factors of production with purchased goods/services to provide **public goods** (national defense, police, fire protection, schools, libraries, roads)
- **Taxes**: government income comes from taxes in both the factor market (personal income tax, corporate profits tax) and the product market (sales tax). Government also subsidizes firms and provides transfer payments (Social Security, welfare) to households

## The Foreign Sector

### It's a Small, Small World
- The domestic economy doesn't exist in isolation — the U.S. is part of a much larger world economy

### You Say Potato, I Say Pomme de Terre
- The U.S. economy interacts with the rest of the world in product and factor markets
- The circular flow theory accounts for international market interactions

#### Import and Export
- Not all domestically produced goods go to domestic consumers — some are exported
- Not all goods purchased by Americans are domestically produced — Americans import from the rest of the world

#### Foreign Factor Payments and Income
- Americans also trade factors of production with the rest of the world
- Factors flow from the rest of the world in exchange for **foreign factor payments**; factors flow to the rest of the world in exchange for **foreign factor income**
- U.S. citizen earning income abroad = foreign factor income; payments to foreigners for use of factors of production = foreign factor payment
- Interest payments on foreign capital and dividends earned by Americans holding foreign stocks = foreign factor income

#### Foreign Transfers
- **Remittances**: money transferred to other countries to support family members — not a direct market exchange but affects the economy
- India and Mexico heavily dependent on remittances (as of 2006, each received ~$25 billion annually, much from the U.S.)

#### Foreign Investment
- Goal: earn foreign factor income in the form of interest and profits
- Foreign investment can also finance a trade deficit — the U.S. enjoys cheap Chinese imports because of a counterbalancing flow of Chinese savings into U.S. financial assets (for every dollar spent on Chinese imports, there is a return flow as Chinese purchase American exports and financial assets)

## The Financial Sector and Financial Markets

### A Finger in Every Pie
- Financial intermediaries (banks, credit unions, insurance companies, stock exchanges) have been left out of the circular flow discussion but are essential
- They facilitate virtually all transactions — "the grease that makes the wheels turn smoothly"

### Financial Intermediaries
- Most product market transactions involve checks, debit cards, credit cards, ATM cash, or foreign exchange — all provided by financial intermediaries (motivated by profit, not goodwill)

#### Financial Intermediaries and the Product Market
- The financial sector makes it easy to spend money (mobile payments, credit cards, etc.)
- Involved in: stock trading, currency exchange, startup funding, auto-pay bills

#### Financial Intermediaries and the Factor Market
- Banks are equally involved in the factor market — most Americans receive paychecks (not cash), deposited in banks/credit unions
- Buying/selling land almost always involves financial intermediaries
- Capital itself is a factor of production — and where do people find capital? The financial sector
- The financial sector generates significant revenue from interest on mortgages and loans

### The Financial Markets
- Up to now, the circular flow model has been dollar in = dollar out. But private, public, and foreign sectors also **save**
- This savings dimension = the **financial markets** (third dimension of the circular flow)
- Savings flow to financial intermediaries and from there (as loans) to all sectors:
  - Households save by buying stocks, bonds, CDs
  - Government finances spending beyond taxes by issuing Treasury securities

### A Look Back at Circular Flow
- Economy has three primary sectors: private, public, foreign (private further divided into households and businesses)
- Each sector contributes to GDP
- GDP is represented three ways in the circular flow: (1) value of all domestic production, (2) total spending, (3) rent/wages/interest/profits earned in the factor market = nation's income

## The Gross Domestic Product

### Keeping Score
- GDP was developed during WWII — the government wanted to understand the economy's capacity to generate war materials
- GDP is important today as an overall indicator of economic performance

### Wealth and Income
- **GDP** = total value of all final production within a country during the course of a year
- Also a measure of annual spending on new domestic production and a measure of income earned from domestic production
- Simple example: Frank pays Louise $1 to knit a blanket → GDP = $1 (spending = income = output). In 2015, U.S. GDP was $18.12 trillion
- **GDP vs. GNP**: GDP measures production done *in* a country; GNP measures production done *by* a country. Toyotas made in Texas = U.S. GDP but not U.S. GNP
- **GDP is a flow, not a stock**: bathtub analogy — water flowing from faucet = GDP, water accumulated in tub = national wealth, water exiting drain = depreciation. As long as GDP exceeds outflows, the nation's wealth grows. GDP measures what is *new*, not accumulated wealth

### Counted or Not?
- **Not counted in GDP**:
  - Purely financial transactions (stock purchases — only the broker's commission counts, because no good or service is produced)
  - Transfer payments (Social Security — not made in return for production)
  - Production with no financial transaction (stay-at-home parent's work, DIY home projects, mowing your own lawn — valuable but no monetary payment = excluded). Interestingly, paying someone to do these same tasks *would* be counted
  - Resale of existing homes (not new production; was counted in a previous year's GDP)
  - Intermediate production (flour/butter/sugar sold to a bakery — only the final bread is counted to avoid double-counting)
- **Counted in GDP**:
  - Apartment/house rent (included in consumption). Homeowners don't pay rent, so the BEA **imputes** a rental payment — when it comes to GDP, everyone is treated as a renter
  - New home construction (the only time a home purchase is counted)

## GDP: Private Spending and Investment

### Because You're Worth It
- GDP = sum of all expenditures on new domestic output
- Four spending categories: households, businesses, governments, foreign nations — each represented by a different variable in the GDP equation

### Personal Consumption Expenditure
- **Consumption** = the act of purchasing goods and services by households
- More than **two-thirds** of all U.S. GDP expenditures fall under consumption (~$9+ trillion per year)
- Consumption is constrained chiefly by **disposable income** (income after taxes) — this gives government considerable leverage over households' capacity to consume
- Disposable income is either spent (product market) or saved (financial markets) — increased consumption = less saving, and vice versa

#### Disposable Income Variables
- **Wealth**: increases in wealth encourage consumption; decreases discourage it. Housing price implosion → less consumption, more saving to rebuild net worth. Stock market changes (DJIA, S&P 500) also indicate household wealth direction and thus consumption
- **Interest rates**: durable goods (cars, appliances, furniture) are often purchased with borrowed money → high interest rates discourage durables consumption; low rates encourage it (0% financing, no payments for a year)
- **Expectations**: when people fear the future, they save and avoid major purchases; when upbeat, they spend more and save less

#### Autonomous Consumption
- Some consumption is independent of disposable income = **autonomous consumption**
- During recessions, durable goods consumption is curtailed but essentials (food, rent, clothing, healthcare) remain relatively unaffected
- Post-9/11 example: autonomous consumption of necessities and nondurables continued, but durable goods and discretionary spending came to a halt (except guns and ammo)

### Gross Private Investment
- **Gross private investment** = business purchases of capital + inventory + household purchases of new homes
  - **Net investment**: purchase of new capital that expands productive capacity
  - **Depreciation**: investment spending to replace worn capital
- Important: for GDP, "investment" is NOT stocks and bonds (that's saving/financial investment). GDP investment = **real investment** (physical capital, inventories, new homes). But real investment is *financed by* financial investment in financial markets

#### Future Expectation
- Investment is influenced by expectations of future business conditions and interest rates
- Positive expectations → more investment; negative expectations → less investment

## GDP: Private Spending and Investment (continued)

### Planned and Unplanned Investment
- Investment is divided into **planned** and **unplanned** investment
- **Planned investment**: during normal times, businesses invest in capital and inventory expecting to sell at a profit — assumes business conditions will continue per expectations
- **Unplanned investment**: when inventory begins to accumulate (consumers aren't buying), it signals bad things to come. Auto dealers order from factories to sell to consumers — if inventory piles up, dealers stop ordering → factory stops production → if pervasive, mass unemployment and recession

## GDP: Government Spending and Exports

### Spend It If You Got It
- GDP isn't just private spending and investment — also affected by government spending and international trade

### Government Spending
- Includes federal, state, and local expenditures on capital, infrastructure, and employee compensation (military, roads, teacher salaries)
- Financed by **taxation and borrowing**
- **Opportunity cost**: government spending = forgone consumption and gross private investment (the $500 you could have spent on a TV goes to Uncle Sam instead)
- Transfer payments (Social Security, business subsidies) are **NOT** included in government spending for GDP purposes
- Government spending accounts for about **40%** of U.S. GDP. Globally, ranges from ~16% (Bangladesh, Central African Republic) to ~65% (Cuba, Lesotho); most developed countries: 35-50%

#### Limits on Government Spending
- Limited by tax revenue and ability to borrow — for the U.S. this is not much of a limiting factor (taxes are mandatory, U.S. debt is a popular investment)
- Regardless of political party, government spending trends upward

#### Pork-Barrel Politics
- "All politics is local" — representatives and senators direct federal spending to their districts/states for re-election. Failure to "bring home the pork" usually leads to replacement

#### Stimulus Debate
- Government spending is often used to stimulate consumption during recessions (government has the ability and will to spend when others don't)
- **Keynesian view**: government spending has a **multiplier effect** — a dollar spent by government increases GDP by more than a dollar
- **Classical/libertarian view**: government spending crowds out more efficient private spending ("let you buy your television in peace instead of taking your money through taxes")

### Net Exports
- **Net exports** (exports minus imports) = the last spending variable in GDP
- Export of new domestic production adds to GDP; imports subtract from GDP
- U.S. typically runs a trade deficit, so net exports are usually deducted from GDP
- Despite engaging in the most international trade by volume, the U.S. remains one of the **least trade-dependent** nations in the world (net exports are a small percentage of GDP)
- For developing countries, net exports are the road to growth (e.g., China — dependent on net exports, but as its economy matures, may orient toward domestic production)
- Net exports are influenced by **exchange rates** and **interest rates**: dollar appreciation → American goods more expensive → exports decline, imports rise; dollar depreciation → opposite. High U.S. interest rates → dollar appreciation → reduced net exports; low rates → depreciation → more net exports

## Approaches to GDP

### Lies, Damned Lies, and Statistics
- GDP can be measured three ways, all should give roughly the same answer:
  1. **Expenditure approach** (add together consumer spending, private investment, government spending, and net exports)
  2. **Income approach** (add together all income earned producing domestic output)
  3. **Production/output approach** (add together value added at each stage of production)

### Income Approach to GDP
- Income = all rent, wages, interest, and profits earned by selling factors of production in the factor market
- Theoretically, income approach = expenditure approach (income equals expenditures, since saving is a form of expenditure)
- Measuring income is more complex than measuring spending: profits flow to corporations, shareholders, and proprietors; taxes and subsidies distort the difference between market price and producer income
- **Income underreporting**: producers and households have an incentive to underreport income to reduce tax liability

### Production or Output Approach
- Calculate the value added at each stage of production
- Must value both **market production** (goods for sale) and **nonmarket production** (government services, nonprofits — valued at cost of production)
- Drawback: determining what's intermediate vs. final (is a pound of sugar intermediate or final? Depends on who buys it and why)
- U.S. government agencies do not use this approach

### Nominal versus Real GDP
- **Nominal GDP**: reported using current prices
- **Real GDP**: holds prices constant to allow valid year-to-year comparisons
- Without adjusting for inflation, the economy may appear to be growing when really only prices are rising
- **Beach ball example**: 2014: 100 balls × $1 = $100 nominal GDP. 2015: 100 balls × $1.25 = $125 nominal GDP. Looks like 25% growth, but output was identical — only prices rose 25%. Using 2014 prices, real GDP for both years = $100
- **GDP Deflator**: nominal GDP ÷ real GDP = rate of inflation. If nominal = $125 and real = $100, inflation was 25%

## Real GDP Changes and the Business Cycle

### Buckle Your Seat Belts
- Over the last 50+ years, America has alternated between periods of recession and expansion, against a background of long-run growth
- Since 1960, U.S. real GDP has increased by more than $10 trillion

### Expansion and Contraction
- Economists call this series of expansions and contractions the **business cycle** (some prefer "fluctuation" since "cycle" implies a predictable pattern that doesn't exist)
- Economy's capacity to produce grows as workforce and productivity grow — this explains long-run growth
- Expansions: spending increases to the point where the economy exceeds long-run production capacity
- Contractions: total spending decreases and excess productive capacity remains
- **Business Cycle Measurements**: measured by looking at GDP and unemployment — expansion = GDP grows + unemployment declines; recession = opposite

### The Four Stages of the Business Cycle
1. **Expansion**: GDP grows month-over-month, unemployment declines
2. **Peak**: real GDP spending at its highest — just before unemployment rises and indicators fall
3. **Contraction**: GDP growth slows or declines. A **recession** = two consecutive quarters of declining real GDP
4. **Trough**: period between contraction and expansion as GDP begins to recover

- Key limitation: economists can only determine what stage the economy is in **after** it has happened

### Highway Analogy
- Driving on a two-lane highway = normal economic growth. Passing slower traffic = expansion (spending exceeds productive capacity). Swerving back to avoid an oncoming truck = contraction. Blindfolded with a Swahili-speaking navigator = how economists actually try to predict the future

### Theories of Business Cycles
- **Milton Friedman's monetarists**: business cycles caused by poor management of money supply — overexpansion = too much money; contraction = too little
- **Keynes's "animal spirits"**: businesses are essentially bipolar — manic investing leads to depression and unwillingness to invest. Animal spirits = emotion that clouds rational decision-making
- **Real Business Cycle Theory**: changes in productivity (not spending changes) are the ultimate cause of cycles

## What GDP Doesn't Tell Us

### Money Isn't Everything
- **Robert F. Kennedy (1968 speech)**: GDP "counts air pollution and cigarette advertising, and ambulances to clear our highways of carnage... counts special locks for our doors and the jails for the people who break them... the destruction of the redwoods... napalm and nuclear warheads... Yet it does not allow for the health of our children, the quality of their education, or the joy of their play. It does not include the beauty of our poetry or the strength of our marriages... it measures everything, in short, except that which makes life worthwhile."

### Real GDP and Social Good
- GDP excludes many important things (unpaid labor, caring for children/elderly)
- But as real GDP has increased, the burdens of scarcity and absolute poverty have been lifted for millions. Yesterday's relative wealth is today's relative poverty
- Healthcare, education, nutrition, sanitation, housing have all improved with rising real GDP → increased longevity
- Average workweek has declined, vacation days have increased — more leisure time
- **GDP per capita**: GDP ÷ population. Subject to a major flaw: gives no indication of income distribution. The U.S. and Norway both have high GDP per capita, but U.S. incomes are much more unequally distributed

### GDP and the Environment
- Critics: GDP doesn't account for environmental degradation. Its focus on spending/output creates incentives to pursue production at the cost of the environment (deforestation, climate change, pollution)
- Defenders: it is *because* of high real GDP that people are wealthy enough to care about the environment — the highest-GDP countries often do the most to address environmental issues
- **"Green GDP"**: some countries exploring measures that account for the environmental cost of growth

### Other Measures of Economies
- **Index of Sustainable Economic Welfare**: includes income distribution and pollution
- **Genuine Progress Indicator**: includes unpaid labor (household work, childcare, volunteer work)
- **Fordham Index of Social Health**: accounts for drug use, homicide, income inequality
- **United Nations Human Development Index**: includes life expectancy and access to education
- **Happy Planet Index**: measures personal happiness or well-being instead of economic health
- **Income inequality**: the uneven distribution of income among households in a country

## Unemployment Defined

### Where Did My Job Go?
- Unemployment makes economics suddenly very relevant to your life
- Economists define, measure, classify, evaluate, and seek to understand unemployment

### What Unemployment Is and Is Not
- 2008 U.S. population: ~300 million, of which 145 million employed. The remaining 155 million are NOT all unemployed (toddlers, retirees, etc.)
- **Unemployed**: persons 16+ who have actively searched for work in the last 4 weeks but are not currently employed
- **Employed**: those who have worked at least 1 hour in the previous 2 weeks
- **Labor force** = employed + unemployed (those meeting neither criterion are not in the labor force)
- **Unemployment rate** = percentage of the labor force (not the population) that is not currently employed
- **Unemployment rate can rise even while employment increases**: demographic shifts (women entering workforce during WWII, military returning to civilian life) can increase the ratio of unemployed to labor force even as total employment grows
- **Not in labor force**: full-time students, retirees, stay-at-home parents, the disabled, institutionalized, active military
- The labor force is in constant flux — people constantly entering, exiting, re-entering; getting hired, fired, furloughed, quitting, retiring

### Measuring Unemployment
- **Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS)** monitors unemployment; Census Bureau conducts the monthly **Current Population Survey** (~60,000 sample households)
- In addition to the survey, economists look at: payroll employment records, new claims for unemployment insurance, weekly hours worked in manufacturing (declining hours = factories idling, may lay off; increasing hours = firms may hire)

## Unemployment Classified

### Labor Force Participation
- **Labor force participation rate**: percentage of working-age population (16+) in the labor force — roughly 65% in the U.S.
- **Employment-to-population ratio**: percentage of working-age population that is employed — has been declining as young people delay entry into the labor force (college, military service)
- Both measures are declining, suggesting the labor market may be weaker than the headline unemployment rate implies

### Hidden Unemployment
- Many people who seem unemployed are not captured in official statistics:
  - **Marginally attached workers**: people who searched for work in the past 12 months but not in the last 4 weeks — they want work but have stopped actively searching
  - **Discouraged workers**: a subset of marginally attached who have given up searching entirely because they believe no jobs are available for them
  - **Involuntary part-time workers**: people working part-time who want full-time work but cannot find it — technically "employed" but underemployed

### BLS Unemployment Measures (U1-U6)
- The BLS publishes six measures of unemployment, from narrowest to broadest:
  1. **U1**: persons unemployed 15 weeks or longer (most restrictive)
  2. **U2**: job losers and persons who completed temporary jobs
  3. **U3**: total unemployed as % of labor force — the **official unemployment rate**
  4. **U4**: U3 + discouraged workers
  5. **U5**: U4 + all marginally attached workers
  6. **U6**: U5 + involuntary part-time workers — the **most inclusive measure**
- U6 is typically about double U3 and is considered a better gauge of true labor market slack

## Types of Unemployment

### Frictional Unemployment
- **Frictional unemployment**: voluntary unemployment that occurs when workers are between jobs — searching for better opportunities, relocating, or entering the workforce for the first time
- Actually a **positive sign** in a dynamic economy: workers are seeking better matches between their skills and available jobs
- Includes recent graduates, people who quit to find better work, and workers moving between industries
- Information reduces frictional unemployment (job boards, networking, employment agencies)

### Structural Unemployment
- **Structural unemployment**: mismatch between workers' skills/location and available jobs — the economy has changed but workers haven't adapted
- Caused by technological change, globalization, shifts in consumer demand, or geographic immobility
- **Schumpeter's creative destruction**: new industries destroy old ones — typewriter manufacturers were destroyed by the personal computer, but new jobs were created in software/hardware
- Can persist for long periods as retraining takes time and workers may be geographically stuck
- **Efficiency wages**: some employers pay above-market wages to attract better workers, reduce turnover, and boost productivity — but this creates structural unemployment because wages are "sticky" above the equilibrium level, creating a permanent surplus of workers who want those jobs but can't get them

### Cyclical Unemployment
- **Cyclical unemployment**: unemployment caused by contractions in the business cycle — when the economy shrinks, firms lay off workers
- Creates dangerous feedback loops: one group becomes unemployed → they spend less → businesses lose revenue → more layoffs → more reduced spending → cascading unemployment
- During the Great Depression, unemployment reached **25%**
- The 2007-2009 recession saw unemployment rise from about 5% to 10%
- Cyclical unemployment is the type that concerns policymakers most because it represents a failure of aggregate demand

## Full Employment and the Natural Rate

### Full Employment
- **Full employment**: the economy is at full employment when there is **zero cyclical unemployment** — frictional and structural unemployment still exist
- Full employment does NOT mean 0% unemployment rate — it means only voluntary (frictional) and mismatch (structural) unemployment remain
- **Natural rate of unemployment**: the unemployment rate consistent with full employment — historically estimated at roughly 4-6% for the U.S.

### Natural Rate Hypothesis
- Developed by **Milton Friedman** and **Edmund Phelps**: the economy tends toward a "natural" level of unemployment that is independent of inflation in the long run
- The natural rate can change over time if frictional or structural unemployment change (e.g., better job-matching technology reduces frictional unemployment, declining manufacturing increases structural unemployment)
- **Market-oriented economies** tend to have lower natural rates because labor markets are more flexible (easier to hire/fire, wages adjust more freely)
- **Socialist/heavily regulated economies** tend to have higher natural rates because labor market rigidities prevent efficient matching

## Why Unemployment Is Bad

### Okun's Law
- **Okun's Law**: for every 1 percentage point that unemployment exceeds the natural rate, actual GDP falls approximately **2% below potential GDP**
- This "GDP gap" represents goods and services that could have been produced but weren't
- **2009 example**: U.S. real GDP ~$14 trillion, unemployment ~10%, natural rate ~5% → GDP gap of roughly $1-2 trillion per year — equivalent to the entire annual output of France being wasted
- The economic cost of unemployment is not just lost wages for the unemployed — it's lost production for the entire economy

### Personal and Social Costs
- Savings depleted, debt accumulated, homes foreclosed
- Health deterioration: physical illness, depression, anxiety, substance abuse
- Family disruption: increased domestic violence, divorce, child neglect
- Social breakdown: crime increases, civil unrest, loss of community cohesion
- Long-term "scarring" effects: workers who experience extended unemployment earn less for the rest of their careers, even after being reemployed

## Trends in Unemployment

### Structural Shifts in Employment
- The U.S. economy has shifted from **agrarian → manufacturing → service sector** over two centuries
- **Globalization** shifted many low-skill manufacturing jobs overseas — American workers must now compete in a global labor market
- Demand for labor is increasingly driven by **worker productivity and skills** rather than raw labor supply
- Workers who cannot adapt to technological change face persistent structural unemployment
- The trend toward a knowledge-based economy means education and retraining are increasingly critical

### Demographic Disparities in Unemployment
- Unemployment is not evenly distributed across demographic groups:
  - **Gender**: men generally have slightly higher unemployment than women
  - **Race**: whites < Hispanics < blacks (persistent gap across all economic conditions)
  - **Age**: younger workers have significantly higher unemployment than older workers
  - **Education**: the single most important determinant of employment
- **2008 extremes**: married white females 25+ had 3.3% unemployment vs. single black males under 25 at 17.5% — a 5x gap
- **May 2015 by education**: less than high school diploma = 8.6%, high school graduate = 5.8%, some college = 4.4%, bachelor's degree or higher = 2.7%
- Education is consistently the strongest predictor of employment outcomes across all demographic categories

## Inflation Defined

### What Inflation Is
- **Inflation**: a general, sustained increase in the overall price level — equivalently, a decrease in the purchasing power of money
- Created by excessive demand relative to supply or by increased production costs, and sustained by too much money in circulation
- Moderate inflation (around 2% annually) is considered healthy and normal — prices double roughly every 36 years at this rate
- **Deflation** (falling prices) is generally considered worse than moderate inflation because it discourages spending and investment

### Historical Hyperinflation Episodes
- **Weimar Republic (Germany, 1920s)**: hyperinflation destroyed the middle class's savings and contributed to the social instability that led to Hitler's rise to power — people needed wheelbarrows of cash to buy bread
- **Hungary (1945-1946)**: the worst hyperinflation in history — prices reached octillions (10^27), with prices doubling every 15 hours at the peak
- **The Great Inflation (1970s U.S.)**: caused by a combination of factors — Vietnam War spending, OPEC oil embargoes, the collapse of the Bretton Woods international monetary system, and poor Federal Reserve monetary policy under Arthur Burns

---

## Measuring Inflation

### Consumer Price Index (CPI)
- **CPI**: measures changes in the price of a fixed "market basket" of goods and services purchased by typical urban consumers
- Compiled monthly by the **Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS)**
- The most widely used and reported measure of inflation
- Limitations: the fixed basket doesn't account for consumers substituting cheaper goods when prices rise (substitution bias)

### Producer Price Index (PPI)
- **PPI**: measures changes in prices received by domestic producers — essentially wholesale prices
- A **leading indicator** of CPI: when producer costs rise, those costs are eventually passed on to consumers
- Includes intermediate goods (components, raw materials) in addition to finished goods
- Useful for understanding inflation pressures before they reach consumers

### Personal Consumption Expenditure Deflator (PCE)
- **PCE deflator**: compiled by the **Bureau of Economic Analysis (BEA)**, broader and more comprehensive than CPI
- Unlike CPI, the PCE adjusts its basket to reflect actual consumer **substitution behavior** — if beef gets expensive and people buy chicken instead, PCE captures that shift
- The **Federal Reserve's preferred measure** of inflation for policy decisions

### Headline vs. Core Inflation
- **Headline inflation**: includes all goods and services, including food and energy
- **Core inflation**: excludes volatile food and energy prices to reveal underlying inflation trends
- The Fed pays closest attention to **core PCE** because food and energy prices fluctuate wildly due to weather, geopolitics, and speculation — core provides a cleaner signal of persistent inflation

---

## Types of Inflation

### Demand-Pull Inflation
- **Demand-pull inflation**: occurs when aggregate demand exceeds aggregate supply — "too much money chasing too few goods"
- Fueled by rising incomes, easy credit, government spending, or tax cuts that put more money in consumers' hands
- Persists if government or the central bank reinforces it by continuing expansionary policy
- Can spread across borders: as domestic consumers demand more, they import more, transmitting inflation to trading partners
- **Aggregate demand**: the total demand for goods and services in an economy at a given price level
- **Aggregate supply**: the total supply of goods and services produced in an economy at a given price level

### Asset Inflation
- **Asset inflation**: price increases concentrated in specific sectors rather than the general economy — e.g., the housing bubble of the mid-2000s
- Asset bubbles can create a "wealth effect" that spills into broader demand-pull inflation
- Dangerous because asset prices can collapse suddenly, creating deflationary shocks

### Monetizing the Debt
- **Monetizing the debt**: when a central bank purchases government debt, effectively printing money to finance government spending
- Extremely inflationary — every case of hyperinflation in history has involved the government monetizing its debt
- The central bank creates money out of thin air to buy government bonds, flooding the economy with new currency
- This is why central bank independence from political pressure is considered essential

### Cost-Push Inflation
- **Cost-push inflation**: occurs when the cost of production inputs rises, forcing producers to raise prices — simultaneously causes higher inflation AND reduced output AND higher unemployment (**stagflation**)
- **1970s OPEC example**: oil price shocks raised production costs across the entire economy, causing both inflation and recession simultaneously
- Cost-push inflation is **self-limiting**: reduced GDP eventually brings producer prices back down as demand falls
- **Critical policy insight**: the correct policy response for cost-push inflation is DIFFERENT from demand-pull inflation
  - Demand-pull: tighten monetary policy (raise rates, reduce money supply)
  - Cost-push: you cannot simply stimulate demand — that will worsen inflation without solving the supply problem
  - **Grease fire vs. forest fire analogy**: pouring water (money) on a grease fire (cost-push) makes it worse; the 1970s Fed made exactly this mistake by treating cost-push inflation with expansionary policy

### Good Inflation
- A moderate inflation rate of about **2% annually** is considered healthy — it encourages spending (mild incentive not to hoard cash), provides a buffer against deflation, and allows for real wage adjustments
- At 2% inflation, prices double approximately every 36 years (rule of 72)

## Inflation: Winners and Losers

### Who Benefits from Inflation
- **Key distinction**: when inflation is **expected and stable**, it is benign — people can plan for it. When inflation is **unexpected**, it creates winners and losers
- **Borrowers benefit**: when individuals, businesses, and governments borrow at a fixed nominal interest rate that has some expected inflation built in, and actual inflation exceeds expectations, the real value of their debt decreases
  - Example: banks lend at 5% nominal, expecting 2% inflation (real rate = 3%). If inflation unexpectedly rises to 4%, borrowers' real interest rate drops to 1% — the money lent was worth more than the money repaid
- **Producers benefit in the short run**: when unexpected inflation occurs, consumer prices rise while wages remain relatively stable (wages are "sticky") — this allows producers to enjoy temporarily higher profit margins until wages catch up
- **Governments with debt benefit**: developing countries have historically tried to erase foreign debts by overprinting currency and devaluing. Independent central banks exist partly as a check on this incentive — the Federal Reserve is somewhat insulated from political pressure

### Who Loses from Inflation
- **Lenders and savers lose**: both earn interest rates that assume some rate of inflation. When actual inflation exceeds expectations, their real returns become negative
  - Example: $1,000 CD paying 4% nominal interest. If inflation rises unexpectedly from 2% to 5%, the real interest rate becomes approximately -1% — you have more dollars but less purchasing power
- **Low-income households are hit hardest**: people with lower incomes hold more of their wealth in cash, which is directly eroded by inflation. Higher-income earners can offset inflation by holding assets that appreciate (real estate, stocks, commodities)
- **Fixed-income earners lose**: retirees on fixed pensions, professionals on fixed salaries — their real incomes decline when inflation exceeds the rate of pay increase
  - **Cost-of-living adjustments (COLA)**: employers and the government (Social Security) adjust pay for inflation, but COLA always lags — adjustments are "too little and come too late"

### Practical Costs of Inflation
- **Shoe-leather costs**: when money is quickly losing value, consumers rush to spend, increasing the frequency and cost of transactions — literally wearing out shoes running to spend before prices rise further
- **Menu costs**: businesses must constantly re-price goods during high inflation — this requires labor resources that could otherwise be productive (reprinting menus, updating price tags, reprogramming registers)
  - **Restaurant menu indicator**: laminated menus signal price stability; chalkboard prices (common for seafood) signal volatility. When restaurants stop laminating, inflation is a concern
- **Distorted price signals**: inflation makes it harder for consumers and producers to distinguish between relative price changes (one good becoming more valuable) and general price level changes (all prices rising) — this undermines the efficiency of the price system

## Disinflation and Deflation

### Disinflation
- **Paul Volcker's triumph (early 1980s)**: the Fed chairman raised interest rates dramatically, bringing inflation down from double digits to about 4% — ending the Great Inflation. This caused the worst recession in decades, but most economists agree it was worth the cost
- This ushered in the **Great Moderation** — a period from the 1980s onward of relatively low and stable inflation
- **Benefits of disinflation**: reduces pressure to increase wages, results in lower and more stable interest rates, makes capital investment less costly and easier to plan, and most importantly — lowers inflationary expectations, creating a more stable economic environment

### The Role of Expectations
- **Self-fulfilling prophecies**: the fear of inflation can itself create inflation. Consumers fearful of rising prices spend more and save less → demand-pull inflation. Workers demand higher wages → cost-push inflation. These reinforce each other in a vicious cycle
- If policymakers fail to manage expectations, a higher expected inflation rate embeds itself into the economic consciousness — the economy produces less output and faces higher prices than it otherwise should
- **"Fed speak"**: Alan Greenspan was famously cryptic — "I guess I should warn you, if I turn out to be particularly clear, you've probably misunderstood what I've said." Managing expectations requires careful communication
- The Fed must not just talk — it must **act credibly**. Like a parent who threatens consequences but never follows through, a central bank that doesn't act on inflation fears loses credibility. Volcker's chairmanship reinforced Fed credibility because he "said what he meant and meant what he said"

### Deflation
- **Deflation**: a sustained decrease in the average price level — money's purchasing power increases
- Sounds good but is actually **extremely dangerous**: creates perverse incentives where consumers and businesses delay purchases/investments because prices keep falling → manufacturing grinds to a halt → widespread unemployment → further deflation (deflationary spiral)
- **Policy dilemma**: central banks fight inflation by raising interest rates (no upper limit), but fight deflation by lowering rates — and there IS a lower bound: **zero**. Once rates hit zero, conventional monetary policy is exhausted
- **Liquidity trap** (Keynes): if consumers and investors won't borrow even at 0% interest, the central bank is out of options with conventional tools
- **Milton Friedman's deflation solution**: in an economy with fiat money, deflation should never be a permanent problem — just print money / monetize debt. "Like dropping cash from helicopters" — this was reiterated by Ben Bernanke in 2002, earning him the nickname **"Helicopter Ben"**

## Aggregate Demand and Aggregate Supply

### Aggregate Demand (AD)
- **Aggregate demand**: the total demand for all final domestic production in a country — relates the **price level** to the amount of **real GDP** demanded
- AD comes from all sectors: households, businesses, government, foreign sector
- The relationship between price level and real GDP demanded is **inverse** (downward-sloping AD curve):
  - Higher price level → money and financial assets lose purchasing power → higher interest rates → less investment and consumption → fewer exports → less real GDP demanded
  - Lower price level → opposite effects → more real GDP demanded
- **Shifts in AD** occur when components change independent of price level:
  - Consumer/business confidence improves → more consumption and investment → AD increases
  - Government spending increases or taxes decrease → AD increases
  - Net exports increase → AD increases
  - Opposite changes decrease AD

### Aggregate Supply

#### Short-Run Aggregate Supply (SRAS)
- In the **short run**, the amount of real GDP supplied is directly related to the price level (upward-sloping SRAS curve)
- **Why?** In the short run, input prices (primarily wages) don't adjust to price level changes. When prices rise but wages stay fixed, firms earn higher profits → they increase output. When prices fall with wages fixed, firms face losses → they reduce output
- **SRAS shifts** are affected by per-unit production costs:
  - **Productivity** increases → per-unit costs fall → SRAS increases
  - **Regulation** increases → compliance costs rise → SRAS decreases
  - **Taxes** on producers → reduce capacity → SRAS decreases; **subsidies** → increase capacity → SRAS increases
  - **Inflationary expectations** increase → workers demand higher wages, lenders demand higher interest, commodity speculation increases → SRAS decreases

#### Long-Run Aggregate Supply (LRAS)
- In the **long run**, the price level is irrelevant to real GDP — input prices fully adjust to match output prices, so firms have no incentive to change output
- LRAS is **vertical** at the economy's potential/full-employment GDP
- **Changes in LRAS** depend on the factors of production: land, labor, capital, entrepreneurship
  - More factors → LRAS increases → **economic growth**
  - Fewer factors → LRAS decreases → **long-term economic decline**
  - Examples: Black Plague reduced LRAS; steam engine increased LRAS

---

## Macroeconomic Equilibrium

### Short-Run and Long-Run Equilibrium
- **Macroeconomic equilibrium**: occurs when real GDP demanded equals real GDP supplied
- **Short-run equilibrium**: AD = SRAS
- **Long-run equilibrium**: AD = LRAS
- Changes in AD, SRAS, or LRAS shift equilibrium

### Effects of AD Changes
- **AD increases relative to SRAS**: both price level AND real GDP increase in the short run (but only price level increases in the long run, as wages catch up)
- **AD decreases relative to SRAS**: both price level and real GDP fall in the short run (in the long run, wages adjust down and economy returns to potential GDP at lower price level)
- **Unemployment and AD**: AD increases → real GDP increases → more demand for labor → unemployment falls (but at the cost of higher prices). AD decreases → opposite

### Effects of SRAS Changes
- Unlike AD changes (where GDP and prices move in the **same direction**), SRAS changes cause GDP and prices to move in **opposite directions**:
  - SRAS increases → higher real GDP at lower prices (productivity improvements, falling input costs)
  - SRAS decreases → lower real GDP at higher prices = **stagflation** (rising production costs, supply shocks)

## The Classical View

### Laissez-Faire Philosophy
- **Classical economics**: pre-Depression orthodox view, now encompasses any market-based approach that emphasizes the inherent stability of aggregate demand and supply
- **Key assumptions**: efficient markets quickly reach equilibrium, extended unemployment is impossible, flexible wages and prices allow rapid adjustment
- When consumers stop spending, they are saving → this increases the supply of loanable funds → reduces real interest rates → spurs investment → any decrease in consumption is offset by increased investment → AD is inherently stable
- If shocks occur, flexible wages/prices allow quick adjustment — workers accept lower wages in deflation, demand higher wages in inflation → economy self-corrects to full employment
- **Say's Law** (Jean-Baptiste Say): "supply creates its own demand" — surpluses and gluts cannot be sustained in a market economy. The act of producing creates the income to buy the production

### Classical Response to Recession and Inflation
- **Recession**: do nothing. Lower GDP → high unemployment → downward wage pressure → workers accept less → firms hire more (lower costs) → SRAS increases → economy returns to full employment. No government intervention needed
- **Inflation**: do nothing. Higher GDP → unemployment below natural rate → upward wage pressure → firms compete for workers with higher wages → production costs rise → firms reduce production → economy returns to full employment at higher but stable price level

## The Keynesian View and Fiscal Policy

### Keynes's Challenge to Classical Economics
- **John Maynard Keynes**: classically trained British economist who concluded during the Great Depression that classical assumptions did not describe reality
- Wrote *The General Theory of Employment, Interest, and Money* — challenged prevailing assumptions and concluded government intervention was warranted
- **Key observations**:
  1. Saving did NOT instantly translate into investment — savings gluts could and did occur, meaning AD was inherently **unstable** (decreases in consumption were NOT offset by increases in investment)
  2. Wages and input prices were NOT downwardly flexible — workers did not readily accept pay cuts, nor did employers offer them. This meant unemployment could **persist** because market forces failed to bring the economy back to full employment
- **Keynes was NOT a socialist** — he was an advocate of capitalism. His approach is better characterized as a hybrid form of capitalism that uses government intervention when markets fail

### Keynes and Recession (The Multiplier Effect)
- **Keynesian prescription for recession**: increase government spending and reduce income taxes to spur aggregate demand and return the economy to full employment
- Government must be willing to **run deficits** to carry out this policy
- **The multiplier effect**: Keynes observed that individuals have a **marginal propensity to consume (MPC)** — if you give people a dollar, they spend some and save some
  - Example: MPC = 80% (Americans spend 80¢ of each additional dollar). Government spends $50 billion on infrastructure → contractors/employees spend $40B → recipients spend $32B → and so on → total spending eventually = **$400 billion** (an 8x multiplier)
  - **Multiplier formula**: 1 / (1 - MPC). If MPC = 0.8, multiplier = 1/0.2 = 5. But the $50B example gives 8x because of compounding rounds
  - Government spending yields a **larger multiplier** than equal-sized tax cuts because people save a portion of tax cuts rather than spending 100%
- **Employment Act of 1946**: signed by President Truman, made Keynesian policy the law — requires government to pursue maximum employment, production, and purchasing power. Created the **Council of Economic Advisors**

### The Phillips Curve and the Inflation-Unemployment Trade-Off
- **A.W. Phillips** (New Zealand economist): discovered an inverse relationship between unemployment and wage inflation using nearly 100 years of British data
- The **Phillips Curve** gave policymakers a "menu" of choices — they could choose their preferred combination of inflation and unemployment through fiscal/monetary policy

## The Phillips Curve (continued)

### The Phillips Curve in Practice
- American economists **Paul Samuelson and Robert Solow** adapted Phillips's work to the U.S., comparing general price inflation (not just wage inflation) to unemployment
- Policymakers embraced it as a "menu of choices" — reduce unemployment from 7% to 5% at the cost of inflation rising from 1% to 2%

### The 1970s Disproof
- The 1970s **stagflation** destroyed the Phillips Curve's credibility — both inflation AND unemployment rose simultaneously, which the curve said was impossible
- **Milton Friedman and Edmund Phelps** argued this was proof that the Phillips Curve was a short-run phenomenon only, and more importantly, proof that Keynesian economics was flawed
- They introduced the **natural rate hypothesis**: unemployment is independent of inflation in the long run. Government efforts to reduce unemployment by creating temporary inflation would be ineffective — workers would simply demand higher nominal wages to keep real wages constant

### Short-Run vs. Long-Run Phillips Curves
- **Modern consensus**: there are TWO Phillips Curves:
  1. **Short-run Phillips Curve**: exists during periods of **stable inflation expectations** — shows a genuine trade-off between inflation and unemployment
  2. **Long-run Phillips Curve**: vertical at the natural rate of unemployment — no trade-off exists when expectations adjust
- Phillips's original data came from a period of stable inflation expectations (British gold standard era). The 1971 collapse of Bretton Woods introduced inflation uncertainty, breaking the short-run relationship
- **Money illusion** (Keynesian assumption): people prefer earning $100/hour paying $10/gallon of gas over earning $10/hour paying $1/gallon — even though real purchasing power is identical. This preference for higher nominal wages allows the short-run trade-off to exist temporarily
- **Greenspan's explanation**: productivity gains from globalization subdued inflation expectations during his tenure, allowing both low inflation and low unemployment simultaneously
- **Key limitation**: Keynesian AD-stimulating policies only work as long as inflation expectations remain stable. If those policies CREATE higher expectations, attempts to reduce unemployment will only create more inflation

## The Federal Reserve System

### History of American Banking
- After the Revolution, Alexander Hamilton proposed the **First Bank of the United States** (1791-1811), modeled on the Bank of England, headquartered in Philadelphia — chartered to handle war debt and create a unified currency. Charter allowed to expire
- **Second Bank of the United States** (1816-1841): created to help recover from War of 1812. Established uniform currency and served as Treasury depository. Believed to be corrupt. **President Andrew Jackson** killed it in 1833 by withdrawing government deposits to state-chartered banks. Bankrupt by 1841
- During/after the Civil War: decentralized national banks created, reintroduced uniform currency, allowed government to borrow by issuing bonds
- **Bank Panic of 1907**: a Montana investor's failed attempt to corner the copper market triggered a contagion of bank runs across New York. **J.P. Morgan** and Theodore Roosevelt's Treasury secretary injected cash to end the panic. Proved America needed a permanent lender of last resort

### Creation of the Federal Reserve (1913)
- **Federal Reserve Act of 1913**, signed by Woodrow Wilson — drew from strengths of predecessors
- Established a **decentralized, public-private** banking system — not a single central bank, but separate locations across the U.S.
- Neither purely governmental nor purely private — has features of both

### Structure of the Fed
- **The Fed as government's bank**: Treasury keeps accounts with the Fed. All taxes collected and bond proceeds go into Treasury's account. Government checks (refunds, Social Security) are drawn from these accounts
- **Board of Governors** (government arm): headquartered in Washington DC, appointed by the President, confirmed by the Senate for staggered 14-year terms. Chairman and vice chairman serve unlimited 4-year terms
- The Fed chairman is considered **second in power only to the President** when it comes to economic influence
- **12 Federal Reserve District Banks**: act as bankers' banks — accept deposits from member banks, enforce regulations, process checks/electronic payments, issue currency obtained from the Treasury
- Each district bank has its own president and board of directors

### The FOMC (Federal Open Market Committee)
- The **chief architect of monetary policy** — 12 voting members: Fed chairman, Board of Governors, NY Fed president (permanent), plus 4 other district presidents on rotation
- All district presidents attend meetings even if not voting
- Meets **8 times per year** (~every 6 weeks) to review economic conditions and set the **fed funds rate target**
- FOMC press releases are closely monitored by media and financial markets for clues about future policy direction
- **Briefcase Speculation**: investors during Greenspan's tenure would study the size of his briefcase heading into FOMC meetings — big/heavy = rate change documentation, light = no change likely

## Monetary Policy

### Goals and Transmission Mechanism
- **Goals**: price stability, full employment, and economic growth
- **Transmission mechanism**: Fed policy → excess reserves in banking system → money supply → interest rates → consumption, investment, net exports → aggregate demand → GDP, inflation, unemployment
- **Asymmetric power**: the Fed is capable of **stopping inflation** but only capable of **encouraging** full employment — it can slam the brakes but can only suggest hitting the gas

### Three Tools of Monetary Policy

#### 1. Reserve Requirement
- Percentage of checking account balances banks cannot lend out
- **Raising it**: fewer excess reserves → less lending → reduced money supply → higher interest rates → less consumption/investment → lower AD → lower GDP, higher unemployment, lower inflation
- **Lowering it**: opposite effect
- **Drawback**: easy to lower (banks can lend more), but nearly impossible to raise during inflation — banks likely have no excess reserves to comply with the increase, which would precipitate a liquidity crisis as banks call in loans

#### 2. Discount Rate
- Interest rate member banks pay to borrow from the Fed overnight, usually when in financial distress
- **Raising it**: discourages borrowing → excess reserves don't grow → money supply doesn't expand → interest rates rise → consumption/investment/net exports fall → AD decreases → GDP falls, unemployment rises, inflation falls
- **Lowering it**: opposite effect
- **Drawback**: banks are reluctant to borrow from the Fed because cheaper options exist in the interbank market — going to the Fed's discount window is a **public admission** that something is wrong. Scares investors and shareholders
- **Signaling function**: useful as a signal of future interest rate policy direction. Markets don't like surprises

#### 3. Open Market Operations (OMO)
- The **primary tool** of monetary policy — buying and selling U.S. Treasury securities between the NY Fed's open market desk and 18 **primary security dealers** (major banks and broker-dealers)
- **Fed buys Treasuries**: cash flows to dealers → excess reserves in banking system increase → money supply grows → interest rates fall → more lending/spending → AD increases
- **Fed sells Treasuries**: cash flows from dealers → excess reserves decrease → money supply shrinks → interest rates rise → less lending/spending → AD decreases

### Fiscal/Monetary Policy Interaction
- Monetary and fiscal policy don't exist in isolation — they must be coordinated
- **Recession**: expansionary fiscal policy (more government spending, less taxes) + expansionary monetary policy (lower rates). Monetary policy offsets upward interest rate pressure that fiscal spending creates
- **Inflation**: contractionary fiscal policy (less spending, more taxes) is politically unpopular, but **contractionary monetary policy** is effective. The Fed's insulation from political pressure makes it perfectly suited for inflation-fighting. Paul Volcker was credited with whipping inflation when government could not
- **Timing problem**: if the Fed introduces stimulus, it must also plan to remove it. Remove too early = prolonged recession. Remove too late = inflation takes hold. This is the Fed's perpetual tightrope
- **Ratchet problem**: if government always uses expansionary fiscal policy for recessions and relies on the Fed for inflation, interest rates will ratchet up over time and long-term growth will be stymied. Government must eventually rein in spending and/or raise taxes

## Supply-Side Economics

### The Rise of "Voodoo Economics"
- The 1970s stagflation was a **supply problem** — Keynesian demand-side solutions couldn't work because spending to reduce unemployment would worsen inflation, while tightening to reduce inflation would worsen unemployment
- **The Misery Index** (Arthur Okun): sum of unemployment rate + inflation rate. Normally around 7. During the Carter administration, it reached an all-time high of **20.76**
- **George H.W. Bush** called Reagan's supply-side plan "voodoo economics" during the 1980 Republican primary (ironic since he later became Reagan's VP)

### Supply-Side Theory
- Classical/laissez-faire economics enjoyed a renaissance under Reagan: flexible, efficient markets maintain full employment; government had "gummed up the works" from the 1930s-1970s
- **Key supply-side policies**:
  1. **Deregulation**: begun under Carter, accelerated under Reagan. Airlines were freed to compete → more flights, cheaper prices, but inefficient airlines driven out. Other industries similarly deregulated
  2. **Breaking union power**: Reagan fired over 11,000 striking air traffic controllers in 1981 for violating federal law. Signal was clear — labor unions' political power declined
  3. **Tax cuts on high earners**: theory was that high earners would spend and save more → more consumption AND more capital investment → businesses expand → higher employment → greater productivity → lower prices
- The **supply-side spin**: tax cuts don't just stimulate demand (Keynesians knew that) — they also stimulate **supply** by incentivizing investment in productive capacity

### Challenges to Supply-Side Economics
- **Reality check**: Reagan got his tax cuts AND his defense spending increases → massive budget deficits, exactly as critics predicted. Increased tax revenue failed to materialize. Revenue fell and deficits grew steadily
- **Redistribution criticism**: "tax cuts on income and capital gains" primarily benefits those with significant income — the immediate beneficiary is the wealthy
- **Legacy**: although supply-side economics is no longer discussed as a formal school, its ideas persist in Republican and Libertarian platforms — cutting taxes, creating savings/investment incentives, distrusting government involvement

### A Complete Toolbox
- Supply-side economics' lasting contribution: reminding policymakers that **incentives matter** — policies shape behavior, and unintended consequences must be anticipated
- Ignoring supply leads to a narrow toolbox (increase or decrease AD). Recognizing supply gives policymakers more options — a tax cut has both demand AND supply effects, appealing to different constituencies
- Government mandates that regulate behavior must account for how they alter incentives for consumers and producers

- U.S. real GDP grows ~2% annually on average → economy doubles every ~36 years. Population grows ~1% → real GDP per capita is roughly 3x what it was in 1960
- Growth is not guaranteed — recessions bring years of zero or negative growth

## Economic Growth (continued)

### Why Grow?
- Growth has produced enormous benefits: life span increased from 48 to 78 years, less than 2% of Americans are now farmers (vs. most a century ago), quality and quantity of material goods has increased, average workweek has decreased
- **Money and happiness**: GDP per capita correlates with happiness up to about $10,000 per person — after that, the relationship breaks down. "The first $10,000 does buy happiness. After that, who knows?"
- Growth frees people from subsistence agriculture to pursue interests and passions — this creative explosion over 150 years has advanced all fields of human endeavor
- **Material abundance** is a natural outflow of freedom from subsistence — "an extra iPod or McMansion might be a small price to pay" for the substantive benefits of growth
- Average American child has over **$250,000** invested in human capital
- Aid organizations have found that delivering food rations to daughters in school increases the daughter's value to the family, reducing childhood pregnancy and prostitution

### Conditions for Economic Growth

#### Human Capital
- **The most important element** in economic growth — education, skills, and abilities of individuals
- Countries that invest heavily in human capital (U.S., Japan, Western Europe) grow faster than similarly endowed countries that don't
- Individual freedom and private property rights are essential — West Germans were far more productive than East Germans; South Koreans far more productive than North Koreans
- **Population growth matters**: larger populations produce more output AND more innovation (more potential entrepreneurs). Population must grow alongside productivity
- **Natural resource paradox**: governments rich in natural resources often neglect human capital development. Russia has vastly more natural resources than Japan, yet Japan has enjoyed far greater economic growth because it invested in its people

#### Physical Capital
- Tools, factories, equipment used in production — as the stock increases, the nation experiences **capital deepening** (more capital per worker)
- Average American worker is backed by **$130,000** worth of physical capital — a key reason for America's productivity edge
- Capital can replace labor (robots welding car frames) or enhance it (pneumatic jackhammer vs. sledgehammers) — either way, it increases productivity
- **Interest rates** are critical: low, stable rates encourage investment. High or unstable rates discourage investment and reduce capital formation
- Capital needs **infrastructure** to realize its potential — roads, waterways, rail, utilities. Soviet Union built massive factories but lacked infrastructure to use them effectively. Developing world lacks the infrastructure necessary for efficient factory utilization

#### Research and Development
- Western and Japanese firms spend far more on R&D than developing-world firms
- R&D requires sacrificing current profits for future gains — **patent protection** is essential for firms to take this risk
- China's impressive growth has mainly come from producing goods developed elsewhere — weak intellectual property laws discourage domestic innovation. "Very little incentive exists for Chinese manufacturers to spend money on R&D if the factory next door can just copy them"

#### The Rule of Law
- Government officials must obey and apply laws uniformly and fairly
- **Corruption** and cronyism raise the cost of capital by introducing risk of seizure — Venezuela's 2007 seizure of foreign oil fields deters future investment
- Former British colonies (U.S., Canada, Australia, New Zealand, Hong Kong) inherited English common law with its emphasis on private property — this makes them safe for foreign investors, attracting more capital accumulation

### How Economic Policy Affects Growth

#### Interest Rate Policy
- The Fed promotes growth by maintaining **predictable, stable interest rates**
- Although monetary policy primarily affects short-term rates, it's the effect on **long-term rates** that influences growth — firms won't make long-term capital investments if uncertain about future rates/inflation
- The Fed must maintain a lid on both actual AND expected inflation — keeping rates too low too long creates inflation expectations that raise long-term rates and discourage capital investment
- Short-term rate increases to dampen inflation may displease Wall Street but help keep long-term rates low and stable → encouraging investment
- **Market Reform in Chile**: the "Chicago Boys" (Chilean economists trained under Milton Friedman at U. of Chicago) brought market reforms under Pinochet that halted inflation and made Chile one of South America's fastest-growing economies — though the association with dictatorship gave market economics a bad name on the continent

#### Fiscal Policy and Budget Deficits
- Budget deficits (absent capital inflows or increased domestic saving) → higher long-term interest rates → less capital investment → slower growth
- Even if interest rate effects are offset, large deficits create **uncertainty** that discourages investment
- Budget **surpluses** reduce long-term rates and encourage capital investment

#### Tax Policy
- Higher taxes on businesses reduce their ability and incentive to invest in capital
- Higher capital gains taxes reduce the flow of savings into physical capital investment
- Capital is **mobile** — businesses facing too-high tax burdens will move elsewhere ("capital is free to flow")
- Progressive taxation (higher rates on higher incomes) can discourage productivity — "the productive worker will either reduce productivity or move to where productivity is not taxed as highly"
- Europe has suffered a **brain drain** as its best and brightest move to lower-tax countries — according to the OECD, this is serious enough that European countries are creating programs to lure expatriates back
- **Flat tax**: taxes all households at the same rate regardless of income. Simple but would increase the tax burden for many households whose effective rates are much lower than their marginal brackets due to deductions and exemptions

### Downsides of Economic Growth
- **Income inequality**: over the last 50 years, U.S. income inequality has increased significantly. Census Bureau data (1980-2008): bottom quintile saw little or no change in average household income; top saw steady increases
- **Causes of inequality**: loss of union power, reduction in marginal tax rates, foreign competition, meritocracy (best marry best, reproduce more of the best)
- **Globalization's role**: unskilled manufacturing moved overseas, leaving unskilled Americans in lower-paying service-sector jobs
- **The democratic tension**: capitalism rewards the most productive, but everyone gets a vote. Those on the short end of capitalism's stick will exercise their vote to redistribute income. "Income redistribution is a fact of life in a society with universal suffrage. Proponents of economic growth must be prepared to share their earned gains with those who may not have earned it"

## The Great Depression Meets the Great Recession

### Links to the Great Depression
- **Black Tuesday** (October 29, 1929): stock market crashed. Over the next decade, personal income plunged, investment stalled, unemployment reached 25%, international trade plummeted
- The Fed **failed its first major test** — it restricted the flow of credit when it should have flooded the system with inexpensive credit. Critics place much of the blame for the Depression's severity on the Fed
- **Glass-Steagall Act of 1933**: created the FDIC to insure bank deposits, prohibited commercial banks from engaging in investment activities. The Bank Holding Company Act prohibited banks from underwriting insurance. Restored confidence for about 75 years

### The Run-Up to the Meltdown
- Glass-Steagall was **repealed** → a new shadow banking industry was created outside traditional regulation
- The Fed and regulators increasingly relied on businesses to **self-regulate**, believing market forces would enforce sound practices
- **9/11 and its aftermath**: the 2001 terrorist attacks created fear in an already-retreating stock market → shallow recession → Fed immediately lowered fed funds rate and injected money into the banking system
- Bush tax cuts + two wars + deregulated financial sector + low interest rates → massive pool of money flowing into real estate
- 2002-2005: home-equity borrowing at historically low rates fueled a spending binge. Banks lent to "pretty much anybody who showed up for a loan"
- Interest rates that should have risen were kept low by China, Japan, and oil-producing states saving large sums in the U.S. Faith in the Fed's inflation-fighting capability kept inflation expectations subdued
- **Asset price bubbles**: easy credit flowing toward housing. Debate continues over whether central banks should raise rates to pop asset bubbles or let them run their course
- 2006: housing market slowed. 2007: housing market went into **free-fall** while oil prices shot up. Consumers saw wealth decreasing while food/energy costs rose. Homeowners began walking away from underwater properties

### Securitization
- Traditionally: banks made loans → kept them on their balance sheets → earned profit from interest and fees → strong incentive to assess default risk
- **Financial innovation**: banks became loan **originators** — charged fees to make loans, then sold them to investment banks. Investment banks bundled loans into **CDOs** (collateralized debt obligations)
- **Principal-agent problem**: because banks no longer held the loans, they had no incentive to carefully underwrite them. Investors relied on banks to vet borrowers, but banks didn't care if loans went bad
- CDOs were sold to institutional investors, insurance companies, other banks, hedge funds — all believed mortgages were safe investments
- CDO popularity → banks could lend more → competition → even lower rates + even looser underwriting → more loans → higher house prices → reinforcing vicious cycle

### The Collapse of Investment Banking
- Institutional investors relied on **rating agencies** (Moody's, S&P) to assess CDO safety. Many could only invest in AAA/AA+ rated bonds. Many CDOs carried these high ratings
- **Rating agency failure**: agencies only rated the **top tranche** of CDOs (the secure, high-quality loans). Lower tranches held riskier loans but paid higher interest, making the overall CDO yield attractive
- **Another principal-agent problem**: rating agencies earned fees from those marketing CDOs → incentive to award high ratings. Institutional investors were investing other people's money → as long as they bought highly rated assets, little incentive to look deeper
- **Credit Default Swaps (CDS)**: investment banks sold insurance against CDO defaults. For investors, this made CDOs seem perfectly safe. Banks made money selling CDOs AND charging for CDS insurance. Profits soared
- **The one small problem**: CDOs were much less secure than believed, and CDSs were not adequately funded. If CDOs defaulted en masse, banks selling CDSs would be liable for hundreds of billions of dollars. That is exactly what happened

### The Crisis Hits Investment Banking
- **Bear Stearns**: first Wall Street victim. Heavy CDO exposure → toxic balance sheet → other banks refused to lend → faced insolvency. NY Fed president Tim Geithner orchestrated a bailout by lending to JPMorgan Chase to buy Bear Stearns at a deep discount. Hoped to prevent widespread panic — instead, it spread
- **Lehman Brothers**: on the ropes, but this time no one came to rescue. Panic had spread
- **Moral hazard**: insurance or expectation of government bailout encourages risk-taking. Economists and policymakers must address moral hazard; ignoring it encourages more risk-taking. "Ignoring moral hazard is a moral hazard"
- **AIG**: as CDOs defaulted, investors exercised their CDSs, which insurance giant AIG had marketed. AIG faced hundreds of billions in losses. Deemed **"too big to fail"** — if AIG failed, the entire financial system could collapse. U.S. government and Fed bailed out AIG
- CDO values collapsed → financial institutions stopped lending to each other (fearful of each other's balance sheets) → banks stopped lending to businesses → commercial paper market froze → **the Great Recession had begun**

## Fiscal Policy Under Fire

### The Fed's Response
- Keynesian prescription: expansionary monetary and fiscal policy. Problem: the tools that are normally effective were severely hampered
- Traditional Fed prescription (buy T-bills, let money creation work) didn't work because **banks were unwilling to lend** — instead of creating money, the financial system was **destroying money**
- Many failures occurred in the **shadow banking system** outside the Fed's scope — to get things working, shadow banks had to be converted to traditional regulated banks. Goldman Sachs and Morgan Stanley changed their status to come under Fed regulation
- **Quantitative Easing (QE)**: the Fed invented new tools. First was the **Term Auction Facility (TAF)** — banks could bid on Fed loans without the stigma of the discount window. Then the Fed began buying **mortgage-backed securities and agency debt** — creating markets for assets in exchange for cash
- FOMC reduced fed funds rate to **0%** — the maximum expansionary stance possible with open market operations
- Most emergency facilities expired in February 2010

### Government Gets Inventive
- Treasury under Henry Paulson + Congress + Presidents Bush and Obama planned massive responses
- **TARP** (Troubled Asset Relief Program, 2008): gave Treasury the ability to recapitalize banks hit by CDO collapse
- Government purchased control of **General Motors** for $50 billion
- **Cash for Clunkers** (CARS): rebates for trading in old vehicles for fuel-efficient ones. 680,000 cars sold under the program
- $8,000 tax credit for homebuyers, extended unemployment benefits
- **Recovery Act of 2009**: nearly $790 billion in stimulus spending
- Combined with tax cuts, two wars, and 0% interest rates — "the stimulus is quite aggressive by historical standards." Most economists believe the response averted a second Great Depression

### Theory vs. Practice and Jobless Recovery
- **Inflation expectations and self-fulfilling prophecies**: the story of expected inflation creating actual inflation is powerful — if policymakers don't address it, the story becomes reality
- **Jobless recovery**: recession → firms cut workers fast. Recovery → firms discover remaining workforce is more productive → increase output without hiring → increase hours before hiring → only after accounting for productivity gains do firms finally hire. "GDP may increase, but unemployment can still remain high for a while. The unemployed vote and do not really care for the neat explanation just given"
- **The Fed's tightrope**: it has injected hundreds of billions to unfreeze credit. Two dangers: (1) if the Fed contracts the money supply too soon, the economy falls back into deeper recession; (2) if the Fed waits too long, inflation may take hold, requiring even more painful tightening later
