## Chapter 10: The Great Crashes (continued)

### October 1929: The 1929 Crash

- **Winston Churchill witnessed** the October 24 crash from the exchange gallery
- Described calm, orderly scene despite panic
- Brokers offering "enormous blocks of securities at a third of their old prices and half their present value"

> "I expected to see pandemonium; but the spectacle that met my eyes was one of surprising calm and orderliness. [The brokers] are precluded by the strongest rules from running or raising their voices unduly." — Winston Churchill

### Richard Whitney's Rescue Attempt

- **Wall Street powers** (Charles Mitchell, Albert Wiggin, Thomas Lamont) deputized **Richard Whitney**, de facto head of stock exchange
- Whitney bid 205 for 10,000 shares of U.S. Steel—well above market price
- Made similar offers for dozens of other stocks
- **Market recovered briefly**, closing down only 2%

### Black Monday and Tuesday

- **October 28**: market fell 13%
- **October 29**: fell another 12%
- Consortium abandoned rescue efforts
- Market ended nearly 40% down from September high
- October 1929 was just the opening act—worse was yet to come

### The Three-Year Decline

- Market rebounded by early 1930, trading higher than beginning of 1929
- Over next two years, Dow fell much further
- **July 8, 1932**: Dow bottomed at 41, fully 89% off 1929 high
- **Roth's diary** records despair in Youngstown, Ohio
- Dentist asked patients to send family in for care even if they couldn't pay
- Toothache patients could only afford extraction, not treatment

### The Saloon-Keeper's Sentiment

- Roth's bankrupt saloon-keeper clients asked what to do with future money
- One replied tearfully he would "gratefully accept a 4%–5% return on high-quality bonds"
- This sentiment would persist for generations of American savers

### Volatility Works Both Ways

- During severe bear markets, abnormally large **price increases occur nearly as often as large falls**
- October 30 (day after Black Thursday): market rose 12.3%—almost as much as previous day's fall
- October 2008: market rose more than 10% twice; worst down day saw fall of less than 8%
- Volatility is volatility, whether upside or downside

### Benjamin Graham Goes Out on a Limb

- 1920s aftermath left **Benjamin Graham** deeply perplexed
- Result: manuscript with **David Dodd**, *Security Analysis*, published during Depression depths
- Graham identified what went wrong and how reasonable investors should approach stocks and bonds
- Still considered a classic, many editions since

### Post-Crash Public Sentiment

- By time Graham wrote, investing public had almost completely abandoned stocks
- **Lawrence Chamberlain** flatly stated in *Investment and Speculation* that only bonds suitable for investment
- **1940 Federal Reserve survey**: 90% of public opposed purchase of common stocks
- Attitude would remain unchanged for a generation

### Graham's Investment Philosophy

- Investment must promise **safety of principal and adequate return** upon thorough analysis
- Investments not meeting these requirements are speculative
- Introduced **amoral relativism** to investing: no intrinsically "good" or "bad" stocks
- At high enough price, even best companies are speculative
- At low enough price, even worst companies could be sound investment

### Graham's Portfolio Recommendations

- Most conservative investors: hold at least 25% in common stocks
- Most aggressive investors: hold no more than 75%
- Implication: average investor should hold **50/50 split between stocks and bonds**
- Tame by today's standards, but startling in depths of Depression

### 1932: The Bottom

- Prices so low aggregate stock market yielded **dividends of nearly 10%**, remaining above 6% for more than a decade
- Almost all stocks sold for less than book value
- Fully one-third sold for less than one-tenth of book value
- A few sold for less than their cash on hand
- Stocks could scarcely be given away at these prices
- Anyone paying good money for them considered certifiable

## FIND THE PIÑATA: 1930s EDITION

### Charlie Mitchell and National City Bank

- Country's "burnt customers" targeted **Charlie Mitchell**, president of National City Bank
- Bank's brokers plied trusting customers with worthless bonds and speculative stocks
- Mitchell described sales tactic: take neophyte to top floor of Bankers' Club
- "Look down there. There are six million people with incomes that aggregate in the thousands of millions of dollars. They are just waiting for someone to come and tell them what to do with their savings."

### The National City Sales Machine

- Brokers sold astonishing **$1.5 billion** in stocks and bonds from bank's investment arm
- Operation so successful it often ran out of newly issued securities

### Ferdinand Pecora vs. Charlie Mitchell

- **Ferdinand Pecora**, chief counsel of Senate Committee on Banking and Currency
- Humble Italian-American origin, connoisseur of criminal behavior
- Recognized Mitchell "saw nothing legally or ethically amiss with National City's high-pressure tactics"
- Over eight days of testimony, Pecora methodically dismantled Mitchell

### Mitchell's Compensation and Tactics

- Salesmen paid about $25,000 per year—30 times typical American salary
- Mitchell paid over one million dollars per year, unheard-of even for highest-paid executives
- Bank employees bought stock at inflated prices from paychecks, then fired after paying off shares
- Mitchell not actually prosecuted (hadn't violated securities laws of the period)
- But public demanded legal retribution for fraud; Mitchell acquitted on all counts

### Four Major Pieces of Federal Legislation

- **Securities Act of 1933**: made stock/bond issuance more open and fair
- **Securities Act of 1934**: regulated trading, established Securities Exchange Commission
- **Investment Company Act of 1940**: allowed development of modern mutual fund industry
- These three made US securities markets **most tightly regulated in the world**

### Glass-Steagall Act

- Fourth piece: 1933 Glass-Steagall Act separated commercial and investment banking
- Eroded during post-1980s free market fundamentalism
- Finally repealed 1999
- Global financial crisis saw no reprise of Pecora hearings or prosecution of fraudulent lending

### The Cycle Continues

- Financial history hasn't ended
- Likely that future will see return of Ferdinand Pecora to wreak justice on con men, rogues, and financial manias

## 1966–1982: The Long Bear Market

### Period Overview

- Longest stretch of negative real returns in US stock market history
- Aggravated by high inflation from oil crises
- Federal Reserve unwilling to tackle inflation with interest rate hikes
- 1979: appointment of chairman **Paul Volcker** spectacularly remedied this

### The Nifty Fifty Bubble

- Not all crashes follow well-established bubbles
- 1960s: modest enthusiasm for **"Nifty Fifty"**
- Glamorous companies with disruptive products seen as can't-miss crown jewels
- McDonald's, Disney, Polaroid, Hewlett-Packard, Xerox
- Typically sold at **P/E ratios approaching triple digits**

### The Decline

- During early bear market years, these companies held up
- Eventually fell victim as well
- Disney P/E fell from 76 to 11
- Polaroid: 90 to 16
- Hewlett-Packard: 65 to 18
- McDonald's: P/E of 9 as late as 1980
- By end of 1974, average stock sold at **seven times earnings**
- Fully one-third of companies could be bought at cheaper than five times earnings

## "The Death of Equities"

### BusinessWeek Article (August 1979)

- Cover story: "The Death of Equities"
- Dow traded at 875, six and a half years after topping 1,000 in January 1973
- Inflation running at almost 9%
- Dollar invested in 1973 purchased just **71 cents of consumer goods** (with reinvested dividends)

> "The masses long ago switched from stocks to investments having higher yields and more protection from inflation. Now the pension funds—the market's last hope—have won permission to quit stocks and bonds for real estate, futures, gold, and even diamonds. The death of equities looks like an almost permanent condition—reversible someday, but not soon."

### The Shift Away from Stocks

- Gold rose from $35/ounce (1968) to more than $500 (1979)
- Would peak at more than $800 following year (roughly $3,000 in today's dollars)
- Real estate boom: those who bought houses in 1950s/1960s sitting on real capital wealth beyond wildest dreams by 1980
- Stocks and bonds dismissed as "paper assets"

### Institutional Flight from Equities

> "At least 7 million shareholders have defected from the stock market since 1970, leaving equities more than ever the province of giant institutional investors. And now the institutions have been given the go-ahead to shift more of their money from stocks—and bonds—into other investments... Says Robert S. Salomon Jr., a general partner in Salomon Brothers: 'We are running the risk of immobilizing a substantial portion of the world's wealth in someone's stamp collection.'"

### Household Stock Ownership Decline

- Late 1960s: more than 30% of households owned stock
- 1970s and early 1980s: only 15%

### The Article's Attack on Stock Ownership

> "Further, this 'death of equity' can no longer be seen as something a stock market rally—however strong—will check. It has persisted for more than 10 years through market rallies, business cycles, recession, recoveries, and booms. The problem is not merely that there are 7 million fewer shareholders than there were in 1970. Younger investors, in particular, are avoiding stocks. Between 1970 and 1975, the number of investors declined in every age group but one: individuals 65 and older."

### The Sublime Irony

- Older people stuck with stocks in 1979: were they out of step, inattentive, or senile?
- Hardly—they were the only ones who remembered **how to value stocks by traditional criteria**
- Told them stocks were cheap, cheap, cheap
- Only investors with experience to know severe bear markets usually followed by powerful bull markets
- A few remembered depths of 1932, when capitalist system seemed threatened and some stocks yielded steady 10% dividends

### Adding Insult to Injury

> "Today, the old attitude of buying solid stocks as a cornerstone for one's life savings and retirement has simply disappeared. Says a young US executive: 'Have you been to an American stockholders' meeting lately? They're all old fogies. The stock market is just not where the action's at.'"

## Lessons from the BusinessWeek Article

### Markets Go to Extremes

- Article shows how markets can go to extremes—valuable lesson in itself
- Demonstrates several more salient points

### Human Nature and Recency Bias

- Human nature to be unduly influenced by **last 10 or even 20 years' returns**
- Just as hard to imagine US stocks good investment in 1979 as to imagine they might not be as good now

### Doubly True for Bonds

- Before 2022 bond market carnage, investors accustomed to nearly relentless four-decades-long fall in rates
- Thought of bond prices as one-way bet
- Forgot four decades between 1941 and 1980 saw "the euthanasia of the *rentier*" (*Rentier* = archaic term for bondholder)

### Look at Longest Data Series

- When recent returns very high or very low, **put faith in longest data series you can find**—not just most recent data
- If *BusinessWeek* article had explored historical record, would have found between 1900 and 1979, stocks returned inflation-adjusted 6%

### Estimate Expected Future Returns

- Make habit of estimating expected future returns
- At time article written: stocks yielding more than 5%, earnings continuing to grow at real rate of 2% per year
- Anyone able to add could have calculated **7% expected real return** from these two numbers
- Between article's publication and end of 2022, S&P 500 actually returned 8% after inflation
- Additional 1% from increase of valuations typical of recoveries from bear markets

## Three Formidable Roadblocks to Buying at the Bottom

### 1. Human Empathy

- Financially expensive emotion
- Channeling fear and greed of others often comes dear
- Corollary: evolutionarily derived tendency to imitate those around us
- Particularly if they seem to be getting rich with tech stocks and cryptocurrency

### Empathetic Investors Are Worst Investors

- Author's unscientific sampling: most empathetic tend to be worst investors
- Empathy extraordinarily difficult quality to self-assess
- Might be worthwhile to ask most intimate and trusted family and friends where you fit on scale
- Yiddish word: the more of a *mensch* you are, more likely to lose critical faculties during bubble and discipline during bear market

### 2. The "Rat Hole Problem"

- Impossible to know where "bottom" really is until far in rearview mirror
- Example bear market strategy: purchase more equities every time S&P 500 falls by 20%
- S&P closed at 1,565 on October 9, 2007
- Would have purchased at 1,253, again at 1,002, third time at 802
- Would you have had moxie to make that last purchase after severe losses on first two?
- S&P fell even further, finally bottoming March 9, 2009, at 677

### Historical Example: 1929–1932

- Same exercise with Dow from market peak September 1929 at 378 yielded **no fewer than nine successive purchases**
- Just barely missing tenth one on July 9, 1932, low of 42

### Benjamin Roth and Benjamin Graham

> "The difficulty is that no one has the cash to buy." — Benjamin Roth

> "Those with the enterprise haven't the money, and those with the money haven't the enterprise, to buy stocks when they are cheap." — Benjamin Graham

### 3. Alarming Narratives

- Stocks don't get cheap in a vacuum
- Alarming narratives always accompany dramatic price declines
- Both 1930s and 2008–2009: entire economy seized up, appeared to be plunging off cliff
- Recall Senator Burr instructing wife to withdraw as much cash as possible from bank

### The 1970s Narrative

- Americans saw savings corroded on almost daily basis by inflation
- 1979 *BusinessWeek* article: seemingly only addled old folks owned stocks
- Bonds widely deemed "certificates of confiscation"
- March 2020: easy to imagine world economy devastated by lethal pandemic for which vaccine seemed somewhere between distant and impossible

## The Courage Required

- Do not underestimate courage it takes to hold stocks during worst of times
- Let alone to purchase more
- Holding and buying assets everyone else is running from takes more fortitude than many investors can manage

### Humans: The Apes That Tell Stories

- Not only are humans the apes that imitate
- Also the apes that tell stories
- Humans understand world, first and foremost, through **narratives, not data and facts**
- Narratives often evolve into societal contagion that can prove expensive to unwary investor

### The Reward for the Dispassionate, Analytical, and Long-Lived

- As *BusinessWeek* article and subsequent history demonstrated, **cheap stocks excite only the dispassionate, the analytical, and the long-lived**
- If you can manage two of those three, you should be well rewarded

## CHAPTER 10 SUMMARY

- Modern capitalist system, relying on elastic credit from both private and government banks, is **fundamentally unstable**
- Because of this, typical investor will live through at least a few bubbles and a few panics
- Markets don't become too expensive or too cheap without good reason
- Just as market manias based on euphoric narratives, **pessimistic stories suffuse market bottoms**
- Ignore both kinds of narratives
- Adhere to math of investing, manage emotions with goodly pile of safe assets
- To make it through market bottoms—the main hazards on highway of riches—you'll need **patience, cash, and courage**, in that order
- Safe assets can be thought of as concentrate of courage
- Despite low yield, in long run the fortitude they supply make them arguably **highest-returning assets in your portfolio**
