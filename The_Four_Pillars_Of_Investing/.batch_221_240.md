### Emotional Damage and Risk Tolerance (continued from Chapter 11)

- Emotionally damaged subjects in behavioral study were MORE risk tolerant than controls, losses didn't impact investment approach
- **System 1** generates irrational fear from normal market randomness — shouldn't care about bad day/year if long-term returns are good
- System 1 fixates on short-term losses, ignores bigger risk: insufficient assets for long-term needs

### Loss Aversion: The 2:1 Emotional Ratio

- **Behavioral economics tenet**: losses impact us emotionally ~2x as much as gains
- $1 loss requires ~$2 gain to offset psychologically
- **Dow Jones 1896-2018**: up 51.9% of days, down 46.6% of days, unchanged 1.5%
- If one losing day = two gaining days emotionally, investors will feel bad about stocks on average
- Watching CNBC minute-by-minute makes things worse

### Time Horizon and Emotional Break-Even

- Lengthening observation to one month: 58.2% winning vs 41.8% losing — still doesn't hit 2:1 ratio
- At yearly interval: 81 winning years, 42 losing years (1896-2018) — still falls short of 2:1 emotional break-even
- **Only checking portfolios every 2+ years** clears the 2:1 psychological hurdle
- **"Risk aversion myopia"**: investors behave as if time horizon is ~1 year (where 2:1 hedonic hurdle lives)

## We're Too Fond of Analogies

- **Representativeness heuristic**: stereotypical thinking over base rates — costly in finance
- **Good company/good stock fallacy** (Chapter 2): glamorous growth companies don't generate higher returns long-term; value companies do better because they must offer higher returns to attract investors

### Representativeness and National Markets

- Intuitive to favor shares in countries with rapid economic growth — **opposite is true**
- **China 1993-2022**: economic growth often exceeding 10% annually, but MSCI China Index returned only 1.1%/year in USD (less than US CPI's 2.4%)
- **Asian tigers** (Korea, Singapore, Malaysia, Indonesia, Taiwan, Thailand): positive real returns since 1988, but still lower than low-growth United States

### The UK Example

- **United Kingdom**: no nation fell farther in relative economic/geopolitical position during 20th century
- 1900: Britannia ruled waves and world financial markets
- 2000: open-air theme park with nuclear deterrent
- **Result**: British equity returns were above developed-nation average during past 122 years
- 2007 **BRIC nations** (Brazil, Russia, India, China) with red-hot economies were investor rage — performed poorly since
- **Systemic pattern**: good economies → bad stock markets, vice versa

### Why Hot Economies Have Poor Stock Returns

1. **Share dilution**: Companies die/new ones replace them; new shares constantly sold in foreign countries (especially Asia) at much higher rate than US — reduces per-share earnings/dividends, erodes returns
2. **Weak shareholder protections**: Developed nations protect shareholders from rapacious management better; developing nations allow management/controlling shareholders to loot companies easily (particularly serious problem in Chinese stocks)

## Beware of Eloquence

- **Hypothesis**: inverse (or zero) correlation between eloquence and forecasting ability
- Prominent finance academics tend to be below-average public speakers
- Market-timing shooting stars (Chapter 3) deployed sparkling rhetorical skill on road to flameout

### Two Mechanisms Linking Eloquence to Poor Forecasting

1. **Eloquence obscures heuristic errors**: intimidates those with opposing views
2. **Media exposure**: best answer to CNBC host asking where market's headed is "How the heck should I know?" — but that person won't get invited back
- **Who gets invited back**: eloquent "boomsters and doomsters" making extreme predictions that appeal to audience and juice advertising revenue

### The Forecasting Death Spiral

- Media mechanisms create forecasting death spiral
- **Tetlock finding**: boomsters/doomsters are below-average forecasters
- Excessive media exposure → overconfidence → more extreme predictions
- More predictions → bigger audiences, more broadcasts
- **"Symbiotic triangle"** (Tetlock): authoritative-sounding experts + ratings-conscious media + attentive public locked together

> "The three principals—authoritative-sounding experts, the ratings-conscious media, and the attentive public—may thus be locked in a symbiotic triangle."

- **Takeaway**: when swayed by silver tongue, poor forecast likely follows

## WHACKED BY WYSIATI (WHAT YOU SEE IS ALL THERE IS): WHY THE RECENT PAST IS YOUR ENEMY

- Book written mid-2020 during one of worst bond market routs in financial history
- Hard to ignore error made by fixed-income investors in recent years

### The 2020 Yield Curve Crisis

- **June 30, 2020 yield curve**: 3-month T-bill yielded 0.16%, 5-year note 0.29%
- Extending maturity from 3 months to 5 years gained just 13 basis points (0.13% extra yield)
- **5-year note historical volatility (SD)**: 4.3% per year
- Mid-2020: investor got 0.13% more yield by taking 4.3% of risk = **0.03% return per 1% of risk**

### Comparison to Equity Risk/Reward

- **Equity risk premium**: ~4%, stocks have annualized SD of 16%
- Equities offer roughly **0.25% return per 1% of risk**
- 0.25%/1% return/risk ratio (stocks) vs 0.03%/1% (extending bond maturity) = stocks offer ~8x better risk/reward
- Provides handy rule of thumb for deciding bond maturity extension risk

### Bond Duration and Risk (Math Box)

- **Duration**: effective maturity calculated from present value of coupon + principal payments
- Always shorter than actual maturity because of coupons received before maturity
- Higher yield → larger gap between duration and maturity
- **Market definition**: ratio between changes in bond price and changes in yield
- 5-year note's duration in mid-2020 ≈ 5 years (minimal yield)
- **First 4 months of 2022**: 5-year Treasury yield rose 1.66%, price fell ~8%
- 1.66% rate rise over 4 months is rare event
- Even if rates had stayed same or fallen, decision to buy Treasuries of more than few months' duration in mid-2020 made little sense from risk/reward standpoint (especially given near-nonexistent coupon cushion)

### March 2023 Update: Inverted Yield Curve

- As of March 2023: yield curve "inverted" — peak return ~5% at 6 months
- Does this mean unwise to extend maturities beyond 6 months (like mid-2020)?
- **Not necessarily**: 2023's much higher yield offers generous cushion against loss (nonexistent in mid-2020 when rates at lowest point in 5 millennia)

### Raymond DeVoe Jr.'s Famous Maxim

> "More money has been lost reaching for yield than at the point of a gun."

### Recency Bias and 2020 Bond Yields

- 2020 bond yields had fallen continuously for 4 decades
- Many investors couldn't envision dramatic rate rise, let alone repeat of Keynes' "euthanasia of the rentier"
- **1999-2008**: worst 10-year stock return in US history
- Dramatic recovery (7x price increase off 2009 low by 2021) erased memory of painful equity losses and 17-year history of negative inflation-adjusted returns nearly two generations ago
- Just in time for 2022's carnage in both stocks and bonds

## More WYSIATI: The Stationarity Illusion

- **Robert Shiller's CAPE** (cyclically adjusted P/E): most popular valuation metric
- Current S&P 500 price ÷ 10-year average inflation-adjusted earnings
- Year-end 2021: CAPE = 38.3 (not far from 1999 peak of 44.2 at dot-com bubble peak)

### The CAPE Strategy Intuition

- What could be simpler? Buy when CAPE low, sell when high
- Historical low/high levels might be 10 and 35 respectively
- Figure 11.3: forward 10-year returns vs CAPE shows intuitive confirmation

### The Stationarity Fallacy

- **"Stationarity fallacy"**: subtle form of WYSIATI/recency
- Assumption: not only will future CAPE values look like prior ones, but relationship between current/future CAPE returns will remain same
- Imagine bottle containing all stock market's past returns and CAPE values
- If stock market is stationary system, all future values fit inside bottle
- **Reality**: they leak out onto the table
- Airfoils/electrical circuits behave same way under same conditions
- **Stocks and bonds do not** — problem often catches physics/engineering types attracted to finance
- If peak of skill set = phase diagrams and differential equations, it's tempting to focus on math of investing, not Shakespeare
- **Long-Term Capital Management**: call it "the engineer's mistake"

### Historical CAPE Trading Strategy Failures

- **Pre-1955**: every time dividend yield dipped below 4%, market suffered serious decline
- Sustained secular rise in stock prices drove yield below 4% in 1955
- **"Sell at 4%" rule**: kept investors out 90%+ of time between 1955-1985, 100% after 1985
- March 2009 market bottom: S&P 500 still yielded only 3.6%

### The Eugene Fama Example (Chapter 3 callback)

- Young **Eugene Fama** found multiple trading rules that worked in Professor Ernst's data set but not in withheld sample
- **Elroy Dimson, Paul Marsh, Mike Staunton (DMS)**: 2013 Credit Suisse yearbook demonstrated same thing globally

### DMS Valuation Study Methodology

- 20 nations dating back to 1900 + 3 global/regional indices
- Generated trading strategies at end of each year based on dividend yield
- Examined how those strategies performed over next 5 years
- Performed thousands of trial runs over entire 113-year period

### DMS Conclusion

> "We learn far less from valuation ratios about how to make profits in the future than about how we might have profited in the past."

- On average, devising trading strategies based on prior valuations added nothing going forward

## THE LAKE WOBEGON MIND

- 64% of Americans think they're going to heaven, only 0.5% plan on opposite direction
- **Business startup study**: >2/3 failed within 4 years, yet founders estimated overall success rate at ~50/50, own chances at ~70%
- Fully 1/3 of entrepreneurs were 100% certain they'd succeed
- **Recommendation**: fledgling businesspeople should "form relationships with outsiders who can provide objective assessments"

### Terry Odean's Driving Ability Survey

- **Behavioral economist Terry Odean**: handed questionnaire to Berkeley MBA class about driving ability relative to classmates
- Typically 1/4 rated themselves in top 10% of drivers
- Nearly all rated themselves above average (both mathematically impossible)

### The Speeding Ticket Story

- Odean asked one student who rated herself below average why
- She was going to rate herself top quarter, but remembered: prior 12 months had 3 speeding tickets, 2 accidents, license about to be revoked
- **Only one student rated himself bottom 10%**: foreign student who didn't drive

### Overestimating Financial Prowess

- Both individuals and professionals overestimate financial ability
- Nearly a quarter century ago, **Wall Street Journal** writer **Greg Ip** wrote classic article describing how recency and overconfidence combine to distort investor expectations

### The 1998 Market Crisis Study

- Summer 1998: stock markets reeled as sovereign bonds worldwide fell into default (same crisis that nailed Long-Term Capital Management)
- **Greg Ip** examined change in investor attitudes following market decline, tabulated investor expectations:

| EXPECTED RETURNS | JUNE 1998 | SEPTEMBER 1998 |
|------------------|-----------|----------------|
| Next 12 months, own portfolio | 15.2% | 12.9% |
| Next 12 months, market overall | 13.4% | 10.5% |

- Average small investor thought fall in prices heralded LOWER future returns
- Never mind: lower price for any asset class → higher future return (or at least lower loss)
- **Equally remarkable**: average investor thought he'd beat market by ~2%
- Since average investor gets market return minus expenses and transaction costs, will in fact get LESS than market return

### The Real-World Loss Problem

- Author neglected to ask whether readers had actually ever lost 15%, 25%, or 35% of portfolio
- Simply looking at table or running portfolio simulation ≠ facing real-world losses
- Stock market only rarely falls for no good reason
- **Bear markets almost always result from**: incipient financial system collapse, hyperinflation, or possibility of nuclear annihilation
- Fear of real geopolitical and economic catastrophe makes such times the most dangerous mountain passes on highway of riches

### The Pain Erasure Mechanism

- **Evolutionarily adaptive tendency**: erase physical and psychological pain
- We remember joys of decade-ago Italian vacation, downplay bumpy flight, food poisoning, loquacious Peoria dentist on Vatican tour
- **Financial equivalent**: mentally buffing up how easily you sailed through 2008-2009 stock market, whose considerable agita you've conveniently forgotten

## A HEDGE FUND IS THE INVESTMENT WORLD'S BIRKIN BAG

- Humankind is ape that imitates, tells stories, AND seeks status
- In scarce food/resources environment, only most capable survive and reproduce
- Human pecking order signals genetic fitness to prospective mates
- While connection between status and mate-finding has attenuated in modern world, evolutionary drive to seek/acquire status remains

### Karl Marx on Status

> "A house may be large or small; as long as the surrounding houses are equally small it satisfies all social demands for a dwelling. If a palace rises besides the little house, the little house shrinks into a hut."

### Cultural Definitions of Envy

- Old joke: US = neighbor's bigger car; England = neighbor having tea with Queen; France = neighbor's attractive lover; Russia = wishing neighbor's cow was dead
- **Investment world version**: envy of neighbor's glamorous stock or hedge fund
- Lusting after glamorous investments can damage finances at least as much as hankering after tony house, car, or lover

### Wealthy People and Access

- Wealthy often impair medical care by fragmenting with doctor shopping, extreme winding up with charlatan celebrity physicians
- Same true in finance: wealthy often have access to managers and vehicles unavailable to plain folks
- Particularly **hedge funds, limited partnerships**, and worst case **con artists** like **Bernie Madoff** (whose appeal rested on turning down prospective clients)

### Hedge Fund Fee Structure

- **"Two and twenty"**: 2% management fee + 20% of returns
- If hedge fund makes 10%/year, fees alone = 4%
- Just for starters — also incur significant transactional costs, usually packaged through advisors and/or "funds of funds" charging another layer of fees
- Typical hedge fund investor flies into 7%/year headwind (handicap not even Warren Buffett can overcome)

### Hedge Funds and Crowded Trade

- Suffer from same "crowded trade" phenomenon troubling commodities futures funds
- More assets thrown at given asset → higher price, lower future returns

### David Swensen and the Yale Model

- 2000: **Yale endowment manager David Swensen** wrote *Pioneering Portfolio Management*
- Proposed portfolio strategy radically different from conventional 60/40 stock/bond institutional model
- Deployed <50% in conventional publicly traded foreign/domestic stocks and bonds
- Invested rest in **hedge funds, private equity, private real estate, commodities futures**

### Yale's Brilliant Success

- **July 1987 - June 2007** (20 years): 15.6% annualized return (4.8% higher than S&P 500)
- Along the way, endowment experienced considerably less volatility than conventional portfolios
- Good news traveled fast: by 1997, Princeton, Harvard, Stanford had copied Yale's trajectory

### The Alternatives Bubble

- Most good investment ideas eventually get run into ground
- By mid-2000s, *tout le monde* adopted Yale model
- As of mid-2011: 823 large endowments tracked by **National Association of College and University Business Officers** invested average 53% in alternative investments

### Endowment Performance Reality

- **10 years ending mid-2011**: university endowments (on average) barely nudged out returns from simple 60/40 S&P 500/Barclays Aggregate Bond or Vanguard Balanced Index Fund (which mimics this mix)
- Things got worse: more money crowded into alternatives, endowments fell further behind
- **20-year period ending June 30, 2021**: average endowment returned 6.8% vs 7.5% for standard 60/40 global stock/bond indices mix

### Hedge Fund Industry Growth and Decline

- **1998-2012**: hedge fund assets grew from $300 billion to $3 trillion
- One rough estimate of "alpha" (excess returns) available to hedge fund managers = $30 billion
- Quarter century ago (1998): $30 billion = 10% of $300 billion managed (more than covered fees/expenses)
- 2012: $30 billion provided only 1% boost to $3 trillion under management (after fees, put clients seriously under water)

- Mix almost exactly reproduces hedge fund index behavior, EXCEPT:
- Hedge fund curve tilted upper-left to lower-right (bloated by 10x between 1998-2012)
