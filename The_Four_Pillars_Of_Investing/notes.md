- Fatal mistake: bouncing between failed schemes instead of managing risk
- Potholes: deflation, leverage, confiscation, chasing performance, overestimating risk tolerance, stockbrokers' conflicts, skewness, overconfidence, cable financial news

## Introduction: The Highway of Riches

**LTCM vs. Sylvia Bloom:**
- **LTCM**: hedge fund founded by **John Meriwether**, Nobel winners **Myron Scholes** and **Robert C. Merton**. 25:1 leverage, quadrupled money in 4 years, then imploded. A 4% asset price fall = 100% wipeout.
- **Sylvia Bloom**: legal secretary at NYC firm 1947-2014 (67 years). Estate: $9M+ (mostly stocks). Two-thirds to **Henry Street Settlement**. No one knew of her wealth.

**Bloom's Three Advantages:**
1. No leverage — could weather storms
2. Time — 67 years of compounding vs. LTCM's 4 years
3. Frugality without deprivation — dressed well, traveled, but saved consistently

> "Compound interest is the eighth wonder of the world." — Einstein (apocryphal)

> "To make money they didn't have and didn't need, they risked what they did have and did need." — Warren Buffett

- Bloom's niece **Jane Lockshin** regularly took her to lunch, always paid the bill — never knew her aunt was a millionaire
- 9/11 example: 84-year-old Bloom walked across Brooklyn Bridge, took bus home during snowstorm. Friend Paul Hyams: "Why?" She replied: "Where should I be?"
- Purpose of saving: to spend later — in Bloom's case, on charity

> "Investing, distilled to its essence, is the conveyance of assets from your present self to your future self."

- Financial highway strewn with black ice and potholes. Bloom drove slowly; LTCM put pedal to metal.
- Finance professionals deem light-on-stocks portfolios "suboptimal" — but suboptimal strategy that survives is preferable to optimal one that risks out

**Investing Is Half Math, Half Shakespeare:**
- **Robert Kaplan**: "Half of everything is geography; the other half is Shakespeare"
- Math = Mr. Spock. Shakespeare = fear, greed, irrationality, narratives over data
- Solution: master the **Four Pillars**

## PILLAR ONE: INVESTMENT THEORY

> "Risk and return go hand in hand."

- No way to avoid stock risk. No market-timing fairy. Cassandras abound; one or two call each crash by chance, then fare worse than coin flip
- S&P 500: peak late 2007 to low March 2009, fell 55.2%. By end 2022, gained 644% from that low
- **Equanimity**: ability to bear losses calmly. Most potent elixir: large pile of safe assets (Treasury securities)

> "It is from time to time the duty of the serious investor to accept the depreciation of his holdings with equanimity." — Keynes

**Life-Cycle Investing:** Key determinants of stock risk = investor's age + "burn rate" (portfolio spending rate)

## PILLAR TWO: INVESTMENT HISTORY

- Over decades: markets are orderly. Over several years: can go off rails
- Late 1990s: companies needed only eyeballs/clicks for ionospheric prices. Early 1930s: some companies sold below cash-on-hand value
- Finance is social science, not hard science — markets don't respond the same way to given circumstances
- **Investor unaware of financial history is irretrievably handicapped**
- Two concepts: (1) long-term returns/volatilities of asset classes, (2) **Mr. Market** (**Benjamin Graham**) suffers from severe bipolar disorder — swinging between extremes of euphoria and depression
- **1999 Dinner Party Test**: if transported to 1999 dinner party, topic A = stupendous tech gains. Easiest way to pick who got rich: give brief quiz on the 1929 crash. How was Goldman Sachs involved? Who was Samuel Insull?

## PILLAR THREE: INVESTMENT PSYCHOLOGY

- Stone Age brain: 300 generations after last ice age, we navigate financial world with risk horizon in decades, not seconds
- Investor's greatest enemy: the Stone Age face in the mirror
- **Skunk analogy**: evolved response doesn't work against "hunk of metal moving at 60 mph"
- Common mistakes: overconfidence (timing, picking, risk tolerance), overpaying for glamorous stocks, trading too much, seeking low-probability high payoffs (IPOs, lottery tickets)

## PILLAR FOUR: THE BUSINESS OF INVESTMENT

- Financial services industry = battlefield. Average stockbroker services clients like "Baby Face Nelson serviced banks"
- Shocking ignorance among advisors; no educational requirements for brokers/fund managers
- Industry moral code "would give tobacco industry a run for its money"
- **Good news**: Vanguard pioneered low-cost index funds; competitors had lunches eaten, decided to join it. Wide range of inexpensive mutual funds and ETFs now available. Vanguard became victim of own success (poorly handled flood of new customers), but now not the only choice.

## Author's Key Lessons (Two Decades)

1. **If you can't save, nothing else matters** — money's purpose is autonomy ("fuck-you money"), not stuff. Hedonic treadmill is the enemy.
2. **Treasury Bill Theory**: staying the course proportional to safe assets held. Biggest determinant of success = how you respond to worst times. T-bills and CDs see you through.
3. **Won the game? Stop playing.** At least 10 years' basic expenses in safe assets; 20-25 even better.
4. **SD is incomplete** — doesn't capture *when* losses occur. Corporate bonds same SD as Treasuries but far riskier (get clobbered in crises when you need cash most).
5. **Maximize odds of success, not returns** — funding retirement ≠ maximizing returns. More Sylvia Bloom, less Merton and Scholes.

## Chapter 1: NO BALLS, NO BLUE CHIPS

### Physicians Are Lousy Investors

- Author practiced medicine most of adult life — confirmed commonplace belief
- Two factors: physicians suffer from overconfidence, and don't approach finance with same rigor as medicine
- Finance is as serious a field as any science. Most physicians manage life savings without glance at financial equivalents.
- "Medical equivalent would be learning brain surgery by reading health section in *USA Today*"

### Napoleon's Egypt Analogy

- 1798: **Napoleon** invaded Egypt with rudimentary maps, no knowledge of climate/terrain. Disaster from start to finish.
- Most investors apply same degree of planning to investing — unaware of terrain, risk/reward relationship, portfolio design mechanics
- Like Napoleon's troops: doomed to fail

### The Risk Premium

> "Return and risk are joined at the hip."

**Coin-Flip Bond Example:**
- Treasury perpetuity: $1,000 face, 3% yield ($30/year)
- Coin-flip bond: heads=$60, tails=$0 (averages $30/year)
- Investors price coin-flip bond at $750 → yield rises to 4%
- Extra 1% = the **risk premium** (most important term in the book)

**Uncle Fred's Retirement Plan:**
- Option 1: safe 3% return → retire with $302,310 (35 years)
- Option 2: coin flip, 30% or -10% → massive variance. All heads = $162M; all tails = pittance.

### The Gordon Equation

- **Expected Return = Dividend Yield + Dividend Growth Rate**
- January 1926 test: predicted 6.6% (5.0% yield + 1.6% growth). Actual 1926-2021: 7.35% real. Gap explained by dividend multiple nearly quadrupling.
- **Realized Return = Fundamental Return + Speculative Return**
  - Speculative = annualized change in dividend multiple (unforecastable)
  - Short-term: speculative overwhelms fundamental (2008: multiple fell 42%; 1933: rose 66%)
  - Over 30-year periods, Gordon forecast converges with actual

**Forward-Looking (2022):**
- Dividend yield 1.7% + real growth 2.0% = **3.7% expected real return**

### International Stock Returns

- **Dimson, Marsh, Staunton (DMS)**: 122 years, 21 nations. Stocks > bonds > bills everywhere
- Top returners: South Africa, Australia, US, New Zealand, Sweden, Denmark
- English common-law institutions protect property rights better than language
- **China**: blistering growth but nominal 1.2% stock returns 1993-2021 (negative real)
- DMS 122-year equity risk premium: 5.0%, but 0.7% from multiple expansion → forward ERP ~4.3%

### Do Stocks Get Less Risky Over Time?

- Worst 30-year real return: 4.14% ($1 → $3.36). Best: 11.80% ($1 → $28.37)
- **Zvi Bodie** (1995): put options get MORE expensive over longer horizons, not less
- **Survivorship bias**: DMS includes only 21 nations whose markets survived (St. Petersburg 1917, Shanghai 1950 = "discontinuities" excluded). May overestimate future returns.

### Sequence-of-Returns Risk

- 1967-1996: 6.09% real return overall. First 15 years: 0.40%. Second 15: 12.10%
- **Mr. Forward vs. Mr. Reverse**: Forward won $149,436 vs $69,291 (1967 dollars)
  - High-return years compounded over much larger asset pile in second half
- Young savers should pray for early bear markets (cheap shares)
- **Retirees**: bad initial returns = devastating. Good initial returns = cruise control.

### Expected Return of Bonds

- **Treasury bonds**: yield ≈ return. TIPS yields: ~1.5% (2022), were negative (2021)
- **Corporate bonds (Baa)**: ~0.2% loss/year average, 2% yield spread over Treasuries
  - Net extra return after defaults: ~0.8%. Not worth it — losses are "lumpy" and cluster in crises
- **Ilmanen Risk**: corporate bonds hit discount rack exactly when you need cash (S&L crisis, dot-com, GFC, Covid)
- **Catastrophe bonds**: uncorrelated with crises → lower expected returns than corporates
- **Gold**: does well when stocks/bonds tank. ~Zero real return over millennia (ounce = fine toga in Rome, good suit today)

**Author's Four Books — Expected vs. Realized Returns:**

| Book (Year) | Expected Real Stock | Expected Real Bond | Expected 60/40 | Realized 60/40 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Intelligent Asset Allocator (2000) | 2.7% | 3.8% | 3.2% | 3.6% |
| Four Pillars (2002) | 3.4% | 3.0% | 3.2% | 4.9% |
| Investor's Manifesto (2010) | 3.6% | 1.8% | 2.9% | 5.5% |
| Four Pillars (2023) | 3.7% | 1.5% | 2.8% | N/A |

All prior forecasts were pessimistic — falling interest rates drove up both stock and bond prices.

### Chapter 1 Summary Points

- **Past four decades**: "One long beer and pizza party" (2022 saw some dieting)
- **Worst of times**: prices fall dramatically, expected returns must rise to compensate. Most purchases at such times do well. Unless they don't.
- **Maximize odds**: keep "sleeping money" in short-duration Treasuries and FDIC-guaranteed CDs

### Other Asset Classes

- **Only two forms**: debt (lending) or equity (owning)
- **Commodities**: futures = zero-sum with high costs. Contango subtracts several percent annually.
- **Real estate**: "owning property isn't so much investment as a job." Drug-addled tenants, management costs. By REIT-level diversification, you just own equities.
- **Cryptocurrency**: "money freed from the specter of government's prying eyes and printing presses." Can earn interest if "willing to take in yet more cryptocurrency" and risk founder is fraudster. Author prefers dollars/euros/yen/pounds. Tech may revolutionize finance (like stamped coins, paper money did), but "holding them in your safe or purse hasn't proved a paying proposition."

## Chapter 2: Odds and Ends

### Value vs. Growth Stocks

- Steady companies (food, medicine) = higher prices, lower expected returns
- Glamorous companies (Amazon) command premium; unglamorous (Caterpillar) demand higher return
- **"Bad companies" have higher returns** for three reasons:

**1. Risk differential:**
- 1929-32: large growth fell to 18¢/$1; large value to 12¢/$1
- 2007-09: growth to 55¢; value to 37¢

**2. Investors overestimate growth:**
- **Fuller, Huberts, Levinson** (1993): growth stocks (P/E 25.6) earned only 10% faster than market in year 1, fading to 1% by years 5-8. Value stocks closed gap by year 6.

**3. Asymmetric earnings surprises** (**Dreman and Berry**):
- Growth exceeds expectations: +6.63%. Disappoints: -18.49% (3x worse)
- Value exceeds: +20.05%. Disappoints: -4.19% (1/5 as bad)
- **Facebook/Meta** (2022): shares fell 26.4% in one day after missing estimates

### Three-Factor Model (Fama-French)

- **Rolf Banz** (1981): small-cap stocks had higher returns
- **Fama and French** (1992): three factors explain portfolio behavior:
  1. Market factor: 5.82% annualized, 18.47% SD
  2. Small company factor: 1.80%, 10.99% SD
  3. Value company factor: 3.37%, 12.23% SD
- Small/value factors are long/short portfolios; investors only get ~half the factor return

### The Disappearing Value Premium

- Value factor trending down since mid-1990s. Two explanations:
  1. **Crowded trade**: value became popular (Dimensional Fund Advisors, ETFs bid up prices)
  2. **Irrational selling**: investors dumped value for big tech growth (partially reversed 2022)

**Value Spreads (Growth:Value ratio):**

| Metric | Average 1951-2021 | 2000 | 2020 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Price/Book | 4.8 | 9.9 | 11.1 |
| Price/Earnings | 3.2 | 6.5 | 6.4 |
| Price/Cash Flow | 3.2 | 6.4 | 5.5 |

- Sorting by cash flow and earnings produces same pattern — price/book isn't obsolete

### International Factor Returns

- Developed foreign (mid-1990-2021): market 5.62%, small -0.44%, value 1.85%
- Emerging (mid-1989-2021): market 6.34%, small 2.17%, value 7.33%
- 10 years ending 2021: value negative in developed, positive in emerging

### The Factor Zoo

- **Campbell Harvey** (Duke): 400+ factors in top journals; many are "lucky findings"
- "Butter production in Bangladesh" predicted S&P 500 ("Stupid Data Miner Tricks")
- Only value factor has compelling data AND believable theoretical/behavioral explanation
- For most investors: total-market funds cost virtually nothing and are tax efficient

### Deep Risk: The Only Risk That Really Matters

- **Shallow risk**: temporary loss (recovers). **Deep risk**: permanent loss
- Risk Depth = Size × Duration
- Worst US deep risk: not Great Depression (recovered in 15.5 years) but **1941-1980** (40 years of inflation devastated bonds to 34 cents real value)

**Four Horsemen:**
1. **Inflation**: most common. Most nations redenominated currencies. Few currencies from 1900 survive.
2. **Confiscation**: depending on ideology, taxes = theft or club dues for functioning society. Only sure way to avoid: move yourself and assets abroad. **Gerard Depardieu** relocated from France to Russia for high marginal tax rate (2023: not many other rich Frenchmen want to join him). Most US citizens loath to renounce citizenship.
3. **Devastation**: war/revolution can destroy economy. Nuclear proliferation = worst case.
4. **Deflation**: before central banking, occurred fairly often. Now least likely scenario with monetary printing press. **Japan**: inflation barely positive (0.44% annually since 1990).

**Mitigating inflation** (most manageable):
- Short-duration fixed income (<5 years). TIPS/I bonds in taxable accounts.
- 2022: 30-year Treasury lost 30%. Inflation increases stock/bond correlation.
- **Weimar hyperinflation**: consumer prices ×trillion, equities still produced positive real returns

**Commodity producers in high inflation** (9 years since 1950, inflation >5%):
- Gold stocks: 3.25% real annualized. CRSP 1-10: -6.19% real
- Value stocks do well: more indebted (inflation erodes debt), cash flows sooner
- 1975-81: Fama-French US Large Value 20.98% vs. growth 12.15% annually

## Chapter 3: The Recent History of Randomovia

### Efficient Markets — Origin Story

**Louis Bachelier** (1900): obscure French mathematician, scion of Le Havre wine merchant family. Parents' early deaths forced him to take over father's business. Sold firm, moved to Paris, found employment on the Bourse, studied under **Henri Poincaré** at Sorbonne. Doctoral dissertation *Théorie de la spéculation* applied Brownian motion (random particle movement) to financial markets. Predicted market's random motion increases as square root of time — same relationship Einstein derived years later.

> "The mathematical expectation of the speculator is zero"

Chain: Bachelier → **Kolmogorov** → **Paul Lévy** → **Leonard Jimmie Savage** → **Paul Samuelson**

**Samuelson** (1974): "Challenge to Judgment" suggested index fund tracking S&P 500. Read by **John C. Bogle** → rest is history.

**Alfred Cowles 3rd**: wealthy grandson of *Chicago Tribune* owner. 1913 Yale graduate; tuberculosis sent him to Colorado sanatorium. Began dabbling in family finances during 1929 crash (lasted nearly 3 years, stocks down almost 90%). Enthusiastic reader of financial newsletters — none predicted it. Used Hollerith punch-card machine to crunch numbers. Found way to Yale professor **Irving Fisher**. 1933 article: "Can Stock Market Forecasters Forecast?" Studied 16 financial services, 20 insurance companies, 24 newsletters. Answer: no. Readers would have done better throwing darts. 1944 extension confirmed: forecasters could not forecast.

**Irving Fisher**: remembered for "stocks have reached what looks like a permanently high plateau" (fall 1929). Also creator of modern discounted dividend model.

**Eugene Fama**: working-class Boston, switched from French to economics. Hired as research assistant by **Harry Ernst** (Tufts) to find profitable trading rules. Found strategies that worked ex post always failed ex ante — when Ernst released full dataset, previously successful strategies failed. Career at Chicago → **Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH)**: markets incorporate all known information. Stock picking is expensive and futile; only surprises move prices. EMH consigned technical analysis to dustbin.

> "Technical analysts exist mainly to make astrologers look good."

**The Data-Mining Problem:**
- Stadium analogy: 10,000 people flip coins; flip tails, sit down. After 10 flips, ~10 remain standing by pure chance. Same with stocks and managers — some outperform by luck but can't repeat it.

### Measuring Fund Performance

- **Jack Treynor** and **William Sharpe**: performance must be risk-adjusted (stock exposure = stock risk = volatility)

> "The burden of proof may reasonably be placed on those who argue the traditional view—that the search for securities whose prices diverge from their intrinsic values is worth the expense required." — William Sharpe

- **Michael Jensen** (U of Rochester, 1968): acted on Sharpe's challenge. Before fees: 48 outperforming vs 67 underperforming. After fees: 39 vs 76.
- Only 1 fund beat market by >3%/year; 21 underperformed by >3%. Funds also carried 8.5% sales loads.
- Jensen's three conclusions: (1) average fund gets gross return ≈ market, (2) average investor gets market minus expenses, (3) chance explains "best" managers

### Performance Doesn't Persist

- Past superior performance does not persist
- Brief tiny persistence: top performers beat average by 0.25-0.5% next year (momentum), then nothing
- Less than mutual fund fees — futile strategy
- Figure 3.3: top-quartile Morningstar funds 1991-2020 showed zero evidence of skill
- 1996-2020: S&P 500 returned 9.56%, average fund 8.90%, past top-quartile 8.63%
- ~3% of funds disappear yearly (**survivorship bias**) — overstates performance ~0.5%
- Bottom-tier persistence exists: high fees + frenetic trading = highest costs year after year
- **"Performance comes and goes, but expenses are forever"**

> "Beware of past-performance 'proofs' in finance. If history books were the key to riches, the Forbes 400 would consist of librarians." — Warren Buffett

### Collective Wisdom of the Market

- **Francis Galton** (1906): crowd guessed ox weight off by single pound — better than best individual
- Thermonuclear warhead (1966) and USS *Scorpion* (1968): averaged expert estimates off by 200 meters

### Superstar Fund Manager Myth

- Humans wired to respond to narratives over dry numbers. Financial media circling for compelling stories that draw eyeballs and advertisers.
- **Jane Bryant Quinn** called it "financial pornography"
- Anything above the fold already impounded into prices

> "Something that everyone knows isn't worth knowing." — Bernard Baruch

**Cathie Wood / ARK Innovation ETF (ARKK):**
- 61.2% annualized 2017-2020; then -23.6% (2021), -67.0% (2022)
- **Amy Arnott** (Morningstar): "ARKK: An Object Lesson in How Not to Invest"
- Fund return since 2014 founding: 28%. Average dollar invested: only 9.8% (vs S&P 500's 14.4%) — investors joined late (2020-2021)
- Before ARK: underperformed at AllianceBernstein; cofounded Tupelo Capital (flamed out in tech crash)

**Gerald Tsai / Manhattan Fund:**
- 1952: **Edward C. Johnson II** hired young Chinese immigrant to Fidelity. Specialty: growth stocks (Xerox, IBM, Polaroid)
- Mid-1960s **Go-Go Years** mirrored late 1990s and recent big tech infatuation. Tsai was prototypical "gunslinger."
- 1962: fund gained 68%. 1965: 50% (vs S&P 12.4%). When **Edward C. Johnson III** joined, Tsai told he wasn't in line to run company.
- Left to found Manhattan Fund. 1968: ranked 299th of 305 funds. Just before bottoming, sold to **CNA Financial** for $30M.
- Real story: **asset bloat** — reputation attracted $1.6B, enormous for the time. **Impact cost** cratered performance: large fund forced to buy high, sell low on every trade.
- Went on to distinguished career, became chairman of Primerica.

> "I own last year's best-performing fund. The problem is I bought it this year." — Jonathan Clements

**William Miller / Legg Mason Value Trust:**
- Beat S&P 500 for 15 straight years (1991-2005). 1.75% expense ratio.
- Asset bloat: when sole manager held $750M; by 2006 had >$20B
- 1998-2008: lagged S&P by 4%/year. Anyone buying after 1993 did worse than index.

### Market Guru One-Hit Wonders

- **Joseph Granville**: lucky bear calls + showmanship. Jan 6, 1981: "sell everything." 1980-2005: lost 0.5%/year (vs S&P +11.9%)
- **Elaine Garzarelli**: predicted Oct 19, 1987 crash. Grossly underperformed next 3 years. Fired 1994.
- **Nouriel Roubini**: predicted 2008 crisis correctly. June 2009: "95% in cash." S&P 2009-2022: +459%.
- **Graham and Harvey**: reprised Cowles's work on 132 newsletters (326 strategies). Not one proved consistently correct. Granville newsletter: 4.9% annualized loss over 12.5 years (vs S&P 15.6% gain).
- **Andrew Metrick** repeated Cowles's work with advanced tools: scattered outperformers explained by chance.
- If you could successfully time the market: keep profits for yourself. Wouldn't sell information in newsletter for $250/year.

### Why Not Buy Stocks on Your Own?

- **Sharpe's arithmetic**: fund managers compete against each other → average = market minus expenses
- Retail investors = "playing tennis against invisible opponent; most of the time, it's Serena Williams"
- **Diversification problem (positive skewness)**: since 1926, majority of companies underperformed T-bills. Bottom 96% barely matched T-bill return. Just 4% of companies provided entire equity risk premium.
- Only 23% of random 5-stock portfolios beat market. 37% of 25-stock. 43% of 100-stock portfolios. Better to own the whole haystack.

### Peter Lynch and Magellan Fund

- Mid-1981 to mid-1990: 22.5%/year vs S&P 500's 16.53%
- Magellan was incubator fund (closed to public early, merged laggards into it)
- Asset bloat: <$100M (1977) → >$16B (1990). Forced into larger stocks.
- End of tenure: >1,700 holdings, effectively an expensive index fund
- Last 4 years: outperformed S&P by only 2%/year. Quit exhausted.

### Warren Buffett and Berkshire Hathaway

- 1965-2021: 20.21% annualized vs S&P 500's 10.52%
- Mesmerized by stock market at early age. Priceless exposure to **Benjamin Graham** at Columbia Business School (1950-51) — only A+ Graham ever gave
- Initially turned down for job at **Graham-Newman** (firm reserved analyst positions for Jews due to rampant anti-Semitism in investment business)
- Returned to Omaha, worked for father's brokerage. Graham later relented and hired him. After Graham's 1956 retirement, Buffett returned to Omaha, operated partnerships
- Early 1960s: began acquiring **Berkshire Hathaway** (failing textile manufacturer) at ~$10/share
- Three advantages over Lynch: (1) internal asset bloat only, (2) active management of companies, (3) insurance float as cheap leverage
- **Alpha declining**: 16.65% (1965-83) → 6.83% (1984-2002) → 2.17% (2003-21)
- 2003-2022: Berkshire 9.76% vs S&P 500 9.79% (essentially tied). But Buffett favors value stocks (rough sledding past few decades) — beats Russell 1000 Value at 8.83%.
- Even Buffett cannot defy gravitational law of asset bloat. Just as with Lynch: spectacular initial results sowed seeds of eventual fade.
- **Professor Samuelson**: one of EMH's first supporters, wrote plea for index funds — but recognized Buffett as exception. Bought Berkshire in 1970. After Bogle established first index fund (1976), stopped buying individual stocks. Also bought shares of mutual fund management company his secretary recommended ("better to own asset management company than be one of its customers — as there is only one place for a temperate man to be in a saloon, behind the bar and not in front of it").

**Wilmot H. Kidd III** — Central Securities: closed-end fund, beat S&P by 2.8%/year for ~50 years. Closed structure protected from inflows.

**Jim Simons** — Renaissance Technologies: Medallion Fund 66% gross (39% net) over 31 years. Closed to outside investors after 5 years. "The most talented investors quickly privatize their talents."

> "Superstar managers sow the seeds of their own destruction simply by growing their portfolios too large."

**The Shell Game Analogy:**
- 10 shells with payouts: $10K, $9K, $8K, $7K, $6K, $5K, $4K, $3K, $2K, $1K
- Index fund guarantees $8,000 payment
- Why gamble when you could lock in the high-probability outcome?
- Of tens of thousands of managers over decades, only the tiniest handful have shown indisputable skill
- **The skilled don't want your cash** — rational superinvestors manage only for themselves (Simons's Law)

## Chapter 4: The Perfect Portfolio

### Diversification: Uncle Fred's Two-Coin Toss

- One toss: 20% SD, 8.17% return. Two tosses on split halves: 14.1% SD, **9.08% return**
- Adding uncorrelated assets reduces risk AND increases return

### Standard Deviation's Limits

- **Fat tails**: Oct 19, 1987 was -20 SD event. Random walk probability: 3×10⁻⁸⁹. Power law predicts extreme events far better.
- **Fama and Mandelbrot** (mid-1960s): prices follow power law, not Gaussian
- SD misses Ilmanen risk: 2008 corporate bond mix lost 8.04% vs Treasury/stock mix losing 1.42%

**Senator Richard Burr's ATM story** (2008): after testimony from Paulson and Bernanke about frozen banking system, called wife: "Go to the ATM machine, and I want you to draw out everything it will let you take... I was convinced on Friday night that if you put a plastic card in an ATM machine the last thing you were going to get was cash."
- Author weathered 2000-2002 with equanimity, emotionally prepared for another halving. 2008 was different: to buy fire-sale stocks, had to sell bonds at a loss. "Few sensations as disturbing as realizing what you thought was money isn't."

> "Charlie Munger: the prime directive of compounding is to never interrupt it."

- **Buffett**: keeps ~20% of Berkshire in T-bills and cash

### Correlations

- Correlation +1.0: no diversification benefit. 0: significant benefit. -1.0: zero risk (too good to be true)
- S&P 500/REITs (1995-2002): 50/50 mix had higher return AND lower risk (cherry-picked low correlation)
- S&P 500/International correlations: risen to nearly 1.0 in recent decades. Diversification value eroding.

### Currency

- Short-term: currencies can devastate foreign returns (1980-85, dollar strengthened ~one-third)
- Long-term: currency effects wash out (mean reversion)
- **Stocks**: hold unhedged. **Bonds**: hedged foreign bonds ≈ US bonds, so foreign bonds aren't worth owning.

### Stock-Bond Correlation

- 1970s-80s (hawkish Fed): positive correlation (both fell together)
- 1990s-2021 (dovish Fed): negative correlation (bonds = safe haven when stocks fell)
- **2022**: pattern reverted. S&P fell 18%, long Treasury fell ~30%. Bonds failed as shelter.

### Contango and Negative-Correlation Assets

- **Precious metals mining**: 0.23 correlation, 5.90% return vs stocks 10.70%. "Gold bugs pay for protection."
- **VXX (volatility futures)**: -0.79 correlation. 2010-11: lost ~74% while S&P gained 8.4%. Bear market insurance isn't free.

### Does Diversification Work?

> "Diversification fails us just when we need it most." — Bruno Solnik

- All 9 stock asset classes fell >50% in 2007-09 GFC
- But **over decade (2000-09)**: US small value +121%, REITs +171%, emerging +162% despite S&P 500 -9%
- **Diversification worthless during bear markets, proves merit in recovery**
- **Japanese retiree example** (1990-2021): 0.53% nominal return in local currency (almost exactly inflation). Worst returns occurred early. With even moderate withdrawal rate, ran out of money within decade. Could same happen to Americans? Unlikely, but why take unnecessary risk when globally diversified portfolio hedges against it.

## Chapter 5: Asset Allocation, Part 1

- **No asset-allocation fairy** — no technique finds optimal future allocation
- **"Couch Potato Portfolio"** (50/50) coined by **Scott Burns** — served many well
- **Efficient frontier**: **Harry Markowitz** (1952) described **mean variance optimization (MVO)**

**MVO's problems:**
1. Hypersensitive to inputs (tiny return changes → wild allocation swings)
2. Mean reversion bias ("error maximizer")

> "I should have computed the co-variances... Instead, I visualized my grief if the stock market went way up and I wasn't in it—or if it went way down and I was completely in it. So I split fifty-fifty." — Markowitz

**The Torah of Asset Allocation**: rabbis **Shammai** struck questioner; **Hillel** answered: "That which is hateful to you, do not unto another. The rest is commentary." Financial Torah = the stock/bond percentage split.

### Ages of the Investor

**Young investor "Frida":**
- $50K salary, $10K retirement fund. Human capital (PV of wages) >$1M.
- In theory: cannot invest aggressively enough. **Ayres and Nalebuff**: recommend ~200% equity via LEAPS.

- Financial press labels 10% fall a "correction" and 20% a "bear market" — mindless numerology
- 10% falls: ~once/year. 20%: ~once/5 years. 50%: 3-4 times per century. 90%: once (1929-32).
- Most financial advisors encounter military veterans who faced death in combat with equanimity but threw up when portfolio lost 5%

- **Start low, go slow.** Takes a market cycle or two to determine real risk tolerance. Aim: not to get rich, but to not die poor.

**Risk Aversion Study** (**Barsky** et al): 50/50 chance of doubling income or cutting by one-third → RRA factor. Nearly two-thirds wouldn't accept even 20% income loss chance (RRA >4). Responses from undergrads ≠ actual behavior when nest egg halves.

### Retirees: The Most Important Number

**Retiree "Fritz":**
- $750K portfolio, $50K expenses, $20K Social Security/pension → $30K **residual living expenses (RLE)**
- 25 years × $30K = $750K needed. Burn rate = 4%. Can't afford risk.
- **TIPS ladder**: riskless inflation-adjusted payoff. Problems: no TIPS maturing 2034-39; longest maturity 30 years.
- **"3% is the new 4%"** for those approaching retirement
- Historical Bengen/Trinity data blessed investors with 7% real stock returns and 2% real bond returns — rested on dramatic increase in stock valuations over that period (unlikely to repeat)
- Only when withdrawal rate lowered to 3.17% do all eight portfolios clear the 30-year bar

**Burn Rate Analysis:**
- Trinity Study/Bengen rule (4% safe withdrawal): rested on 7% real stock returns from 1926-mid-1990s
- At adjusted lower returns (4.5% real stocks, 1.0% real bonds), three of eight starting dates failed 30 years
- Only at 3.17% withdrawal do all portfolios clear 30-year bar

**Burn rate guidelines:**
- >3.5%: in red zone. Allocate enough safe assets for minimum 20 years RLE. Defer Social Security to age 70 (best annuity money can buy). Invest rest conservatively with TIPS ladder matching outflows.
- 2-3.5%: probably in good shape. Should still defer Social Security to 70. Reasonable stock/bond mix (25/75 to 75/25) should service retirement needs fairly well.
- <2%: portfolio allocation barely matters vs heirs/charities. Especially true if Social Security and pension cover living expenses. "Beyond splashing out for beamer or occasional business-class seat, portfolio returns irrelevant to material circumstances."

**Annuity caveats:**
1. Carry credit risk of issuing insurance firm
2. State government backing funded by other insurance companies — severe crisis would cut through reserves
3. In 2008, US rescued AIG; since then debt/GDP ratio soared past 100% — questionable if Uncle Sam could save annuitants again
4. No annuities with true inflation protection available
5. Always "pay" to delay Social Security to 70 first before purchasing TIPS ladder or annuity

### LMP/RP Framework

- **Liability Matching Portfolio (LMP)**: safe assets covering 20-25 years of RLE
- **Risk Portfolio (RP)**: everything beyond LMP, for heirs/charities
- Psychological hack: two buckets separating bond (LMP) and stock (RP) holdings

**Wealthy retiree "Frank"**: $3M portfolio, needs $750K LMP → $2.25M RP in stocks (75/25 overall). **Pfau and Kitces**: "reverse glide slope" — increasing equity after retirement as LMP drawn down.

### Longevity Risk

- Deferred annuities: bad idea for several reasons:
  - No effective inflation rider — dollar purchasing power declined to $0.29 between 1966-1985
  - Credit risk of handing money to insurance company for decades before getting paid back
  - State guarantees behind policies can fail over 20-30 year horizons
- Better: reserve 10% stock assets as mental account for longevity insurance
- Long-term equity risk less threatening than inflation risk from nominal-dollar annuity
- Can deploy dividend stream into sequential 30-year TIPS auctions, or opportunistically exchange equity at high prices for 30-year TIPS
- **Deferring Social Security to 70**: best annuity money can buy, effectively solves longevity problem

### Middle (Preretirement) Years — Trickiest Game of All

- Decade before retirement: most difficult and uncertain investing phase
- Early years: rich wage stream + future human capital mitigates stock risk
- Later years: human capital depleted, sequence-of-return risk from both stocks AND bonds leaves little margin for error

**Three transition strategies:**
1. **Formulaic**: reduce equity as function of age (70/30 at 40 → 25/75 at 70, -1.5%/year)
2. **TIPS ladder approach** (**Michael Zwecher**, *Retirement Portfolios*): gradual switch from bond fund to TIPS ladder
3. **Market-responsive** (Bernstein's recommendation): if bull pushes portfolio over LMP "magic number" (20-25× RLE), start de-risking. Once you've won the game, why keep playing aggressively? Add TIPS, plain Treasuries, CDs to LMP bucket. Then free to add stocks to RP bucket.

### Demographic Roulette

- 1980 cohort: 19 years to reach LMP. 1949 cohort: 37 years (started investing at bull market start)
- Those who began working in 1930s/1970s (distressed markets) accumulated fastest
- Lucky but imprudent investor with favorable draw may never accumulate adequate LMP
- Prudent but unlucky investor may do everything right and never achieve security
- **"By saving hard and playing life-cycle game well, you stack odds in your favor"**
- Better to fly coach and buy clothes at Target (invested too conservatively) than live with your kids (invested too aggressively)

## Chapter 8: A Brief History of Financial Time

### Ancient Capital Markets

- First organized trade: Fertile Crescent (Mesopotamia grain for Persian Gulf metals)
- First organized long-distance trade: Fertile Crescent (Mesopotamia's grain for Persian Gulf's metals). Maritime expeditions recorded on durable clay tablets.
- Ancient loans often interest-free but with draconian collateral — enslavement of debtor's children or debtor himself
- Second century BCE: Mesopotamian legal code set maximum rates: 33.33% for grain, 20% for silver
- Height of Roman Empire: rates fell to ~4%
- Anthropological data: prehistoric annual rate ~100% (pig or sack of rice loaned paid back twice over a year later)
- As societies become prosperous, investment returns necessarily fall. A "wealthy" society = one with lots of capital to spare.

### Venice's Prestiti (1200s)

- First public debt markets: forced loans (prestiti) on wealthy citizens (1262)
- Paid semiannual 2.5% (5%/year). Never matured (perpetuity). Critically, could be transferred to others — became well-trusted, traded beyond Venetian borders.
- 5% usually below prevailing rate → forced purchase = tax on the wealthy
- Market value = annual interest ÷ prevailing rate. If rate rose to 10% due to war, prestiti value halved.
- **1377-80 war with Genoa**: prestiti fell to 19% of face value. Genoese penetrated Venetian Lagoon, captured Chioggia, blockaded Venice itself. 1380: Venetians launched daring counterblockade, forced retreat → dramatic recovery. Those who bought at worst point easily doubled money.
- Same pattern: Britain 1789-1814, US 1931-32 and 2008-09
- **March 9, 2009**: each S&P dollar grew to $7.33 by year-end 2022
- **"Best fishing usually done in roughest waters"** — but investing at such times = financial Russian roulette

### Secular Decline in Interest Rates

- **Paul Schmelzing** (Harvard): rates fallen from ~17% to ~zero over seven centuries (~0.6-1.8% per century)
- "Zero lower bound can be expected to become permanent"

**Three reasons:**
1. **Savings glut** (Bernanke): wealthier societies have more capital than need
2. **Longer life expectancies** (**Irving Fisher**: interest rate = "impatience"). Ancient/medieval populations: starving, poorly housed, short-lived → highly impatient for consumption → demanded high rates. Modern populations: better-fed, longer-lived, must squirrel away for retirements lasting longer than working careers. With apologies to **Samuel Johnson**: man to be hanged in fortnight demands high rate of return on savings.
3. **Lower transaction costs**: ancient investors had to find their own borrowers without modern communication — only high return justified the effort. Modern investors acquire minuscule slices of world's markets with few keystrokes. Should not expect to be as well rewarded for minimal effort.

### Early Stock Markets

- **Bazacle mills**, Toulouse (1071): originally floating mills on Garonne River — technological marvels (equivalent of today's Apple/Tesla). Companies merged into **Honor del Bazacle** (1372), shares began to trade without management approval. 1888: converted from milling to hydroelectric. Yielded ~5% dividend for centuries. 1946: French government nationalized, extinguished shares.
- **Dutch East India Company** (1602): founded **Amsterdam Stock Exchange**. Before 1800, only two stocks regularly traded there (Dutch East and West India companies).
- Equity risk premium uniformly ~4-5% over past century, but ~1% from multiple expansion → forward ~3-4%
- If SD of US stocks is ~16%, over next 30 years, 95% confidence limits around 3.5% central estimate are between -2.3% and +9.3% per year
- Modern investor should be philosophical: might envy Mesopotamian capitalist earning 20% on silver loan, but that world was dangerous and miserable. Better a world with low returns where starvation is largely banished and young people not struck down by bacterial diseases.

## Chapter 9: TOPS — Manias

- Three decades ago, author discovered **Charles Mackay's** 1841 *Memoirs of Extraordinary Popular Delusions and the Madness of Crowds*
- First three chapters (Mississippi Company, South Sea, tulip episodes) seemed irrelevant to well-behaved markets of early 1990s
- Next several years: Mackay's descriptions came to life. "Best investment author ever made."
- **Bernard Baruch**: Mackay saved him millions, wrote foreword to 1932 printing
- **Harry Truman**: "The only thing new in the world is the history you don't know"
- Einstein's compound interest quote is apocryphal, "but he could have said same about amnesia"
- Even mention of dot-com episode draws blank stares from audience below age 40

### Diving for Dollars (1687) — First Tech Bubble

- **1687**: New England sea captain **William Phips** docked in England with 32 tons of silver raised from Spanish pirate ship. Enriched himself, crew, backers beyond wildest dreams.
- Entrepreneurs floated shares in "diving engine" ventures — almost all patents worthless, submitted for express purpose of creating interest in company stock
- **Daniel Defoe** (of *Robinson Crusoe* fame) was treasurer of one company; insider knowledge didn't prevent one of most spectacular bankruptcies of the age
- No diving company turned profit; not immediately clear how any could access treasure-laden wrecks — all had "dubious business model"
- Eventual result: total loss for all

### Kindleberger's Count

- **38 boom-and-bust cycles** beginning 1618: 3 (17th c.), 7 (18th c.), 17 (19th c.), 11 (20th c.)

**Two reasons for post-1600 start:**
1. **Fractional reserve banking**: London goldsmiths issued certificates exceeding precious metal held. Leverage = ratio of paper to hard assets. 2:1 or below fairly safe.
2. **Scientific method**: **Francis Bacon's** *Novum Organum* (1620) → inductive process → **Royal Society** (Hooke, Boyle, Newton)

### Minsky's Instability Hypothesis

- Stability begets instability, instability begets stability
- Conservative bankers see cautious brethren's profits → lower lending standards → blowup → rediscover prudence → cycle anew (~once per decade)
- Modern central banking may have muted cycles OR made bubbles more frequent (quantitative easing)

### Human Nature and Bubbles

- **Mackay's** book recounted mass delusions beyond finance: religion, dress, health, facial hair, even murder by poison
- Recent **QAnon** belief system — conspiracy theory believed by as much as 20% of US population — shows we haven't outgrown this tendency

**The ape that imitates**: 50,000-100,000 years ago, humans departed Africa to inhabit all continents. Had to learn kayaks, buffalo hunting, poison blow guns. Rather than hardwire distinct abilities, evolution encoded general-purpose skill of imitation. Propensity also amplifies maladaptive behaviors — including financial manias.

**The ape that tells stories**: narratives trump facts and data. Preliminary studies: "the more compelling the story, the more it erodes critical-thinking skills." Inherent conflict: CNBC talking heads devise most compelling narratives possible; investors should intentionally avoid those narratives, rely only on data and analytical discipline.

### South Sea Bubble (1720)

- Final subscription priced at £1,000, sold out in under a day (par value £100, started at £130)
- Government bribed with under-the-table shares (including king's mistress) — turned out to be counterfeit
- Easy capital spawned dubious enterprises: settling Australia (half-century before Captain Cook), machine guns, transporting live fish to London
- Famous mythical company chartered "for carrying on an undertaking of great advantage but no one to know what it is"
- **Only 2 of 190 bubble companies succeeded**: insurance giants **Royal Exchange Assurance** and **London Assurance**
- **Prince of Conti** demanded gold/silver for paper money from **Law's Banque Royale** → precipitated bank run
- **Bubble Act** (June 1720): South Sea Company had Parliament require charters for new companies. Shut off fund flow from insurance companies lending to shareholders (especially **Sword Blade Company**, chartered only to make swords). Pricked its own bubble.
- **Isaac Newton** lost fortune: could calculate "motions of heavenly bodies" but not "the madness of the people"
- **Ernest Hemingway**: "Bankruptcy occurs in two ways. Gradually, then suddenly."

### Railway Mania (1840s)

- Until steam power: long-distance overland travel exclusively for the rich. Poor roads, dangerous, slow, extremely uncomfortable.
- Railroads made overland travel cheap, safe, rapid. "Jet airliner, PC, internet, espresso all rolled into one."
- **Fanny Kemble** described first trip on **George Stephenson's** *Rocket*: "swifter than the bird flies... the sensation of flying was quite delightful and strange beyond description"
- First line: Darlington-Stockton (1825). **Liverpool and Manchester Line** (1831) produced healthy dividends, soaring stock prices.
- **Queen Victoria's** first railway trip (1842) ignited popular enthusiasm. "Railway time" replaced "internet time" of 1990s. First time people measured distances in hours, not days.
- Late 1844: three largest companies paying 10% dividend. 16 new lines, 50 new companies. Offerings guaranteed 10% dividends, featured MPs and aristocrats on boards (paid with under-the-table shares).
- Late summer 1845: shares up 500%, 450+ new companies. Nearly 8,000 miles of new lines planned — four times existing trackage. Lines planned literally from nowhere to nowhere.
- **Walter Bagehot**: "John Bull can stand many things, but he cannot stand two percent" (low safe rates drive speculation)
- Mid-October 1845: all over. *Times of London* introduced word "bubble" into financial lexicon.

### The Roaring Twenties

- **Winston Churchill** reinstated gold standard at prewar £/$4.86 → overvalued pound → trade imbalance
- **Benjamin Strong** (Federal Reserve chairman) and **Montagu Norman** (Bank of England governor) were close personal friends. Strong, who dominated the Fed, threw gasoline on fire by lowering interest rates.
- Third ingredient: 20+ years since last crash (1907, stocks fell 48%) → not enough gray heads left to warn
- Final component: millions of ordinary citizens, completely ignorant of asset valuation, sucked in by watching friends/neighbors grow effortlessly rich
- Stocks rose 150%+ over 2.5 years before October 1929 "black days" → fell to one-tenth of peak within 3 years

### Four Signs of a Mania

1. **Speculation becomes everyday conversation** (South Sea: "nothing but South Sea Stock!")
2. **People quit jobs for full-time speculation** (1920s actress ran Park Avenue brokerage; dot-com day traders)
3. **Skeptics attacked viciously** (**Paul Warburg** warned of "orgy of unrestrained speculation"; met with anti-Semitic slaps. "You just don't get it" — five-word phrase of every modern bubble)
4. **Extreme predictions** (Fisher's "permanently high plateau"; **John McAfee**: Bitcoin to $500K or "I'll eat my dick on national television")

### How to Spot a Bubble

- **EMH believers recoil**: **Eugene Fama** — "The word 'bubble' drives me nuts, frankly"
- **William Goetzmann**: markets that doubled had only 7% chance of halving next year, 17% over five years. Bubbles are rare; most frothy markets not followed by crashes.
- Supreme Court Justice **Potter Stewart** (*Jacobellis v. Ohio*): "I shall not today attempt further to define... But I know it when I see it." Same applies to bubbles — can't model mathematically, but know one when we see it.

**Am I telling you to time the market? No.** Most sensible approach: ignore bubbles, stay the course with prudent mix of stock index funds and Treasuries. Rebalancing automatically mandates some selling during manias. Same for panics — mandates stock purchases. In both cases: ignore talkative neighbors, turn off CNBC, log off internet, keep tight grip on tiller.

### Post-Bubble Pattern

- **Edward Chancellor** (*Devil Take the Hindmost*): manias represent collective dream of boundless wealth; awakening produces revulsion, mass bankruptcy, pursuit/punishment of promoters
- Poet **Alexander Pope** to **Bishop Atterbury** (who had warned him): "The fate of the South-sea scheme has, much sooner than I expected, verified what you told me... it happens in the case of our death."

- 1720 England especially dire — sophisticated financial system just a generation old. Many worried about permanent plunge into medieval poverty.
- Minsky's criteria work in reverse: loss of faith in new technologies + liquidity contraction = catastrophe. By that point, knowledge that recoveries follow collapses has fallen victim to amnesia.

### Railway Aftermath

- **George Hudson**: charismatic entrepreneur, floated shares yielding generous dividends well before lines constructed. Payouts funded by new stock sales — a **Ponzi scheme**. Parliament outlawed in 1847.
- Bank of England administered coup de grace: raised discount rate from 3.5% to 5.0%
- Subscription leverage devastated: "Entire families were ruined. There was scarcely an important town in England but what beheld some wretched suicide. Daughters delicately nurtured went out to seek their bread."

- But left valuable infrastructure: combined with steamship to underpin England's 19th-century global military and economic dominance. Ruined investors = capitalism's unwitting philanthropists.

## Chapter 10: The Great Crashes (continued)

### October 1929 Crash

### Volatility Works Both Ways
- During severe bear markets, abnormally large **price increases occur nearly as often as large falls**
- Oct 30 (day after Black Thursday): market rose 12.3%. Oct 2008: rose >10% twice; worst down day <8%
- Volatility is volatility, whether upside or downside

### Benjamin Graham and Post-Crash Sentiment
- 1920s aftermath left Graham perplexed; result: manuscript with **David Dodd** → *Security Analysis* (published during Depression depths, still considered classic)
- **Lawrence Chamberlain** flatly stated in *Investment and Speculation* that only bonds suitable for investment
- **1940 Federal Reserve survey**: 90% of public opposed common stock purchases — attitude unchanged for a generation
- Graham's philosophy: investment must promise **safety of principal and adequate return** upon thorough analysis; everything else is speculative
- Introduced **amoral relativism**: no intrinsically "good" or "bad" stocks — at high enough price, best companies are speculative; at low enough price, worst companies could be sound
- Portfolio recommendation: most conservative 25% stocks, most aggressive 75%; implication: **50/50 split** (startling in Depression depths)

### 1932: The Bottom
- Aggregate stock market yielded **dividends of nearly 10%**, remaining above 6% for over a decade
- Almost all stocks below book value; one-third below one-tenth of book value
- A few sold for less than cash on hand; anyone buying considered certifiable

### Charlie Mitchell and National City Bank
- Country's "burnt customers" targeted **Charlie Mitchell**, president of National City Bank
- Mitchell's sales tactic: take neophyte to top of Bankers' Club — "Look down there. There are six million people with incomes that aggregate in the thousands of millions of dollars. They are just waiting for someone to come and tell them what to do with their savings."
- Brokers sold **$1.5 billion** in stocks/bonds from bank's investment arm
- **Ferdinand Pecora**, chief counsel of Senate Committee on Banking and Currency — humble Italian-American origin, connoisseur of criminal behavior
- Over eight days of testimony, Pecora methodically dismantled Mitchell
- Salesmen paid ~$25,000/year (30× typical salary); Mitchell paid >$1M/year (unheard-of)
- Bank employees bought stock at inflated prices from paychecks, then fired after paying off shares
- Mitchell not prosecuted (hadn't violated securities laws of the period); acquitted on all counts

### Four Pieces of Federal Legislation
- **Securities Act of 1933**: made stock/bond issuance more open/fair
- **Securities Act of 1934**: regulated trading, established Securities Exchange Commission
- **Investment Company Act of 1940**: allowed modern mutual fund industry
- These three made US securities markets **most tightly regulated in the world**
- **Glass-Steagall Act (1933)**: separated commercial/investment banking; eroded post-1980s; repealed 1999

### 1966-1982: The Long Bear Market
- Longest stretch of negative real returns in US stock history; aggravated by high inflation from oil crises
- Federal Reserve unwilling to tackle inflation until 1979 appointment of **Paul Volcker**
- **Nifty Fifty** bubble: McDonald's, Disney, Polaroid, HP, Xerox at P/E ratios approaching triple digits
- Disney P/E fell 76→11; Polaroid 90→16; HP 65→18; McDonald's P/E of 9 by 1980
- By end of 1974: average stock at **seven times earnings**; one-third cheaper than five times

### "The Death of Equities" — BusinessWeek, August 1979
- Dow at 875, six and a half years after topping 1,000 in Jan 1973; inflation ~9%
- Dollar invested 1973 purchased just **71 cents** of consumer goods (with reinvested dividends)
> "The death of equities looks like an almost permanent condition—reversible someday, but not soon."

- Gold: $35/oz (1968) → $500 (1979) → $800 peak (~$3,000 today's dollars)
- Real estate: those who bought houses in 1950s-60s sitting on real capital wealth; stocks dismissed as "paper assets"
- Late 1960s: >30% of households owned stock; 1970s-early 1980s: only 15%
- Young investors avoided stocks; only older investors who remembered 1932 knew stocks were cheap

> "Have you been to an American stockholders' meeting lately? They're all old fogies. The stock market is just not where the action's at."

- The sublime irony: older investors stuck with stocks weren't addled — they were the **only ones who remembered how to value stocks by traditional criteria**

### Lessons from the BusinessWeek Article
- Human nature: unduly influenced by **last 10-20 years' returns**
- Doubly true for bonds — before 2022 carnage, investors accustomed to four decades of falling rates; forgot "euthanasia of the *rentier*" between 1941-1980
- When recent returns very high or very low, **put faith in longest data series you can find**
- At time of article: stocks yielding >5%, earnings growing at real 2%/year = **7% expected real return**
- Between publication and end of 2022: S&P 500 actually returned **8% after inflation** (additional 1% from valuation recovery)

### Three Roadblocks to Buying at the Bottom
1. **Human empathy**: financially expensive emotion; most empathetic investors tend to be worst investors; Yiddish: the more of a *mensch* you are, more likely to lose discipline during bear market
2. **The "Rat Hole Problem"**: impossible to know where bottom is until far in rearview mirror
   - Example: buying S&P every 20% fall from Oct 2007 peak of 1,565 → purchases at 1,253, 1,002, 802 — would you have had moxie for that last purchase? Market fell further, bottomed March 9, 2009 at 677
   - Same exercise 1929-1932 yielded **nine successive purchases** barely missing tenth
> "Those with the enterprise haven't the money, and those with the money haven't the enterprise, to buy stocks when they are cheap." — Benjamin Graham
3. **Alarming narratives**: stocks don't get cheap in vacuum
   - 1930s and 2008-2009: economy appeared to plunge off cliff; Senator Burr instructed wife to withdraw as much cash as possible
   - 1979: savings corroded daily by inflation; bonds "certificates of confiscation"
   - March 2020: easy to imagine world economy devastated by lethal pandemic

### The Courage Required
- Holding and buying assets everyone else is running from takes more fortitude than many investors can manage
- Humans: the apes that imitate AND tell stories — understand world through **narratives, not data and facts**
- **Cheap stocks excite only the dispassionate, the analytical, and the long-lived** — manage two of three, you'll be well rewarded
- To make it through market bottoms, need **patience, cash, and courage** in that order
- Safe assets = concentrate of courage; despite low yield, fortitude they supply makes them arguably **highest-returning assets in portfolio**

## Chapter 11: Behavioral Finance (continued)

### Emotional Damage and Risk Tolerance
- Behavioral study: emotionally damaged subjects were MORE risk tolerant than controls — losses didn't impact their investment approach
- **System 1** generates irrational fear from normal market randomness — shouldn't care about bad day/year if long-term returns are good
- System 1 fixates on short-term losses, ignores the bigger risk: insufficient assets for long-term needs
- The investor who put $10,000 in a mutual fund in mid-1970s and forgot about it was shocked when October 19, 1987 crash dropped her account — to $179,623

### Loss Aversion: The 2:1 Ratio
- **Behavioral economics tenet**: losses impact emotionally ~**2× as much as gains**; $1 loss requires ~$2 gain to offset
- **Dow 1896-2018**: up 51.9% of days, down 46.6%, unchanged 1.5%
- If one losing day = two gaining days emotionally, investors feel bad about stocks watching CNBC
- Lengthening to monthly: 58.2% winning vs 41.8% losing — still doesn't hit 2:1
- Yearly: 81 winning, 42 losing — still falls short
- **Only checking portfolios every 2+ years** clears the 2:1 psychological hurdle
- **"Risk aversion myopia"**: investors behave as if time horizon is ~1 year

### Representativeness Heuristic
- **Good company ≠ good stock**: glamorous growth companies don't generate higher long-term returns; value companies do better because they must offer higher returns to attract investors
- **China 1993-2022**: economic growth often >10%/year, but MSCI China Index returned only **1.1%/year in USD** (less than US CPI's 2.4%)
- **Asian tigers** (Korea, Singapore, Malaysia, Indonesia, Taiwan, Thailand): positive real returns since 1988, but lower than US
- **UK**: no nation fell farther in relative position during 20th century (1900 ruled waves; 2000 open-air theme park with nuclear deterrent), yet equity returns **above developed-nation average** over 122 years
- **BRIC nations** (2007 investor rage) performed poorly since
- Why: **share dilution** (new shares constantly sold, especially in Asia) + **weak shareholder protections** in developing nations (particularly Chinese stocks)

### Eloquence and Forecasting
- Hypothesis: inverse/zero correlation between eloquence and forecasting ability
- Prominent finance academics tend to be below-average public speakers; market-timing stars deployed sparkling rhetoric on road to flameout
- Two mechanisms: (1) eloquence obscures heuristic errors; (2) best CNBC answer is "How the heck should I know?" — but that person won't get invited back
- **Tetlock's "symbiotic triangle"**: authoritative-sounding experts + ratings-conscious media + attentive public
> "The three principals—authoritative-sounding experts, the ratings-conscious media, and the attentive public—may thus be locked in a symbiotic triangle."

### WYSIATI: Recent Past as Enemy
- **June 30, 2020 yield curve**: 3-month T-bill yielded 0.16%, 5-year note 0.29% — only 13 basis points extra for 4.3% annual risk (SD)
- **0.03% return per 1% of risk** (extending bond maturity) vs stocks' **0.25% return per 1% of risk** — stocks offered ~8× better risk/reward
- **Bond duration**: always shorter than maturity because of coupons; 5-year note's duration in mid-2020 ≈ 5 years
- **First 4 months of 2022**: 5-year Treasury yield rose 1.66%, price fell ~8%
- Even if rates stayed same, buying Treasuries >few months' duration in mid-2020 made little sense (near-nonexistent coupon cushion)
> "More money has been lost reaching for yield than at the point of a gun." — Raymond DeVoe Jr.
- March 2023 update: inverted yield curve ~5% at 6 months; higher yield offers generous cushion nonexistent in mid-2020 "when rates at lowest point in 5 millennia"
- 2020 bond yields had fallen continuously for 4 decades; investors couldn't envision dramatic rate rise, let alone repeat of Keynes' "euthanasia of the rentier"
- **1999-2008**: worst 10-year stock return in US history; dramatic recovery (7× price increase off 2009 low by 2021) erased memory of painful equity losses — just in time for 2022's carnage in both stocks and bonds

### The Stationarity Fallacy
- **Shiller CAPE** (S&P 500 price ÷ 10-year inflation-adjusted earnings): end-2021 CAPE = 38.3 (vs 1999 peak of 44.2)
- What could be simpler? Buy when CAPE low, sell when high
- **Stationarity fallacy**: assumption that future CAPE values/relationships will look like prior ones
- Airfoils/circuits behave same way under same conditions; **stocks and bonds do not** — call it "the engineer's mistake" (Long-Term Capital Management)
- Pre-1955: every time dividend yield dipped below 4%, market suffered serious decline
- "Sell at 4%" rule kept investors out 90%+ of time after 1955, 100% after 1985; March 2009 bottom: S&P still yielded only 3.6%
- **DMS study** (Dimson, Marsh, Staunton — 20 nations, 113 years, thousands of trial runs): trading strategies based on prior valuations added nothing going forward
> "We learn far less from valuation ratios about how to make profits in the future than about how we might have profited in the past."

### The Lake Wobegon Mind (Overconfidence)
- 64% of Americans think they're going to heaven, only 0.5% plan on opposite direction
- Business startups: >2/3 failed within 4 years; founders estimated overall success at ~50/50, own chances at ~70%; 1/3 were 100% certain
- Recommendation: "form relationships with outsiders who can provide objective assessments"
- **Terry Odean**: Berkeley MBAs — 1/4 rated themselves top 10% drivers; nearly all above average
- Speeding ticket story: one student about to rate top quarter, then remembered 3 speeding tickets, 2 accidents, license about to be revoked
- Only student rating himself bottom 10%: foreign student who didn't drive

### Investor Overconfidence (Greg Ip, 1998)

### Hedge Funds: The Birkin Bag of Investing
- Humankind: the ape that imitates, tells stories, AND seeks status

### David Swensen and the Yale Model
- 2000: **David Swensen** wrote *Pioneering Portfolio Management*; deployed <50% in conventional stocks/bonds, rest in hedge funds, private equity, real estate, commodities
- **July 1987 - June 2007**: 15.6% annualized (4.8% > S&P 500) with less volatility
- Princeton, Harvard, Stanford copied by 1997
- By mid-2011: 823 large endowments averaged 53% in alternatives

- **20 years ending June 2021**: average endowment returned 6.8% vs 7.5% for standard 60/40 global index
- **1998-2012**: hedge fund assets grew $300B → $3T; estimated alpha ($30B) went from 10% of AUM to 1%; after fees, put clients seriously underwater

## Chapter 12: Behavioral Finance Summary

- Almost all stock market patterns are coincidence — **Fama** found historical patterns useless; market strategist's real job is to make palm reader look good
- **Myopic risk aversion**: System 1 defines risk as losing money now; real risk is insufficient assets over half a century
- **Deep risk** (Chapter 2's four horsemen): inflation, confiscation, devastation, deflation
- System 1 focuses on myopic, short-term market declines that almost always turn out temporary
- Combating myopic risk aversion is the most difficult emotional task facing any investor

**Two ways to combat myopic risk aversion:**
1. **Check portfolios infrequently** — behavioral research (lab and real world): investors who never look earn higher returns than frequent checkers; check 1-2× per year for fraud protection only
   - It's good that Zillow.com can't offer accurate daily house quotes
   - Graham observed during Depression: investors in obscure mortgage bonds (not quoted in newspapers) held on and did well; owners of frequently quoted corporate bonds were more likely to panic and sell
2. **Maintain pile of safe assets** — Treasuries, CDs, high-quality money market provide priceless equanimity
   - Newly retired: at least 5 years of RLE in safe assets (10 better, 20 not too much)
   - Young person's biggest "bond" = decades of future earnings; amount depends on job security
   - Young person must carry disability insurance; if dependents, life insurance too
- Don't become a whale — exclusive vehicles designed to bleed wealthy investors for commissions, transactional costs, fees
- **Overconfidence antidote**: "The market is much smarter than I will ever be. Millions of other investors are much better equipped than me."
- Most liberating aspect of indexing: earning market return outperforms majority of professionals engaged in expensive Sisyphean task of trying to exceed it

## Chapter 13: Your Broker Is Not Your Buddy

### The Churnovian Analogy
- Imaginary country **Churnovia** (north of Chapter 3's Randomovia): doctors have no firm educational requirements (cram for multiple-choice exam, take as many times as needed), owe no professional duty to patients, perform unnecessary surgeries for financial gain
- Disputes go to arbitration board chosen by **Churnovian Medical Association**
- International attorney: "I'd be a fool to take your case"
> Welcome, dear reader, to Churnovia.

### Harvard Study (Sendhil Mullainathan)
- Sent actors to brokers' offices posing as clients, each with list of current investments
- Quarter came with simple, low-cost indexed portfolios
- All but **2.4%** of brokers recommended selling index funds, switching to high-cost actively managed funds with front-end loads
- Study conclusion: "adviser self-interest plays an important role in generating advice that is not in the best interest of the clients"

### Three Key Problems with Brokers
1. **No educational requirements**: simply pass multiple-choice Series 7 exam; in many US states, harder to get licensed as manicurist
2. **No fiduciary duty**: only "suitability" standard for decades; firms relabeled brokers "investment advisors," conned SEC into Reg BI ("best interest") — mealy-mouthed structure falls far short of fiduciary
3. **Interests fundamentally opposed**: client minimizes fees; broker maximizes them
> "They take all the money and throw it up in the air. Everything that sticks to the ceiling belongs to the clients." — Fred Schwed
- Account agreements force disputes to arbitration before NYSE/FINRA (brokers' own trade groups)

### Charles Merrill's Vision and Betrayal
- **Charles Edward Merrill**: complex, self-aggrandizing, also visionary who nearly single-handedly pioneered financial services as public fiduciary around WWII
- Born 1885, entered brokerage after dropping out of Amherst; repulsed by corrupt 1920s climate
- Revolutionary reforms at merged **Merrill Lynch** (1939):
  - Paid brokers by **salary, not commission** (first since 17th-century London coffeehouses)
  - Minimum commissions; didn't charge for collecting dividends
  - Always disclosed company's interest in particular stock (unheard of elsewhere)
  - Analytic research replaced "hot tips"
- Died 1956: Merrill Lynch = nation's largest wire house — 122 offices, 5,800 employees, 440,000 customers
- **Donald Regan** (later US Treasury secretary) took over 1968; markets buoyant, brokers at other firms printing money on commissions; defections from Merrill mounted
- By 1971 Regan readopted commission practices; defections stopped, but clients turned back into cash cows
- Marked end of trail for full-service brokerage as socially useful enterprise
- Fell to **Jack Bogle** at Vanguard to champion inexpensive access

### Broker Practices (Pre-2010 Era)
- Spread between bid/ask extracted enormous toll, especially on municipal/corporate bonds (up to several percent per transaction)
- **Principal transactions**: brokerage sold from own inventory; client told "there is no commission" when actually paying substantial spread
- "Specials": undesirable securities firm underwrote, pushed by brokers with glowing research; brokers rewarded with bonuses and exotic vacations
- Stock recommendations: retail client is last in line after institutional players; prices already bid up
- 2017: only **6% of analyst ratings were "sell"**; during dot-com: just 1%
- Analysts feel immense pressure to recommend stocks their firms underwrite; "hold" is worst they give

### Things Improving
- Wire houses moved from commissions to fee-based (1%-2% AUM) — less extraction
- New breed sells complexity: rotating cast of overlapping funds and holdings
- **Robo-advisors**: 0.15%-0.50% of AUM — halfway between hands-on manager and DIY

### Advisor Selection Rules
- Avoid advisors deploying actively managed funds (particularly more than one per asset class)
- Avoid advisors recommending "alternative" asset classes (hedge funds, private equity, venture capital)
- Turn nose up at any fee approaching 1%; if comfortable executing trades, pay by the hour
- Have advisor sign **fiduciary oath** — if they hesitate, don't walk, run
- Don't become a "whale"

### Insurance: Buy to Insure, Not Invest
- Term life only if sole breadwinner lacks sufficient assets; same for disability
- Long-term care: most fraught area; dramatically increasing premiums, decreasing benefits; best case: self-insure
- Avoid: **variable universal life** (mind-bogglingly complex, extremely high expense)
- Avoid: **whole/universal life** (far more expensive than term; better to invest premium difference in index funds)
- Avoid at all costs: **fixed indexed annuities** — caps annual returns, siphons dividends, has termination penalties
- Fixed indexed annuities are stock index funds "in same way Don Corleone was Catholic"
- **Allan Roth's** six questions expose the flaws (caps as low as 4.5%, company can lower cap, excludes dividends, etc.)
- Insurance salespeople are masters of psychological manipulation

## Chapter 14: The Improving Mutual Fund Scene

### Early History
- Mutual funds first appeared late 18th-century Holland
- **Goldman Sachs** created disastrously leveraged investment trusts before 1929; demise spurred **Investment Company Act of 1940**
- Before 1950s: conservative, ~0.5% expenses, but 7%-9% **front-end sales load** destroyed the low cost
- Only few percent of $5B under management in **no-load funds**
- First no-load: **Scudder, Stevens & Clark** Balanced and Common Stock funds (1928-1929)

### Trap #1: Sales Loads
- Front-loaded (Class A), back-loaded (Class B), ongoing (Class C) — all go to broker
- Load funds on average **underperform** no-load funds even after load paid

### Gunning the Fund: Fidelity Select Technology
- Mid-1983: 162% trailing 12-month return; **Ned Johnson III** persuaded to cooperate with *Money* magazine cover story
- Manager **Michael Kassen** posed in shorts with squash racket in February Boston
- Fund tripled to $650 million in weeks; fire hose of inflows ballooned impact costs; tech collapsed
- **Mutual fund hierarchy of happiness**: fund family (collects 1%+ fees + 3% loads regardless) > manager > shareholders (last)

### Hot Money Dynamics
- Select Technology: nearly $1B in 1983-84 → $71M by 1989 → $5.2B during late-1990s bubble (beat S&P by 66% in 1998, 96% in 1999, quintupled to $5.2B) → tech collapse of 2000
- High past performance attracts naive cash at exactly the wrong time
- Share owners in red-hot funds buy high and sell low, earning much lower **internal rate of return** than the fund itself

### Investor-Fund Return Gap (Morningstar / Russell Kinnel)
- Typical investor earns **1%-1.5% less** than fund return
- Gap increases with volatility: sleepy large-cap value: 0.4%; large-cap growth: 3.4%; **technology: 13.4%**
- Morningstar "Buy the Unloved" (1994-2021): unloved categories returned **11.9%** vs loved **6.7%**

### Variable Annuities
- Mix insurance and investment; high sales fees + ongoing insurance charges; tax advantage rarely worth fees
- "These products are not bought—they are sold"
- Large chunk sold for retirement accounts where tax deferral is unnecessary
- Only consider for: investor <40, no tax-sheltered assets, wanting tax-inefficient exposure via low-cost provider
- If stuck with high-cost annuity: consider **1035 exchange** to Vanguard/Transamerica, Fidelity, or Dimensional

### John C. Bogle and the Birth of Vanguard
- Young Bogle at **Wellington Management** bought Nifty Fifty, partnered with hotshot Boston firm **Thorndike, Doran, Paine & Lewis**; Nifty Fifty collapse hit Wellington Fund hard; Boston partners **canned Bogle**
- By happy coincidence, Bogle had written **1951 Princeton senior thesis** on mutual fund business; few knew the **Investment Company Act of 1940** as well
- Act mandated fund directorship be separate from advisory companies; after months of acrimonious debate, Wellington Fund declared independence from Wellington Management
- **September 24, 1974**: **Vanguard** born with revolutionary "service corporation" owned by the funds themselves
- Since funds owned by shareholders, **fund shareholders were Vanguard's owners** — first and only truly "mutual" fund company; no incentive to milk customers
> "As there is only one place for a temperate man to be in a saloon, behind the bar and not in front of it." — Paul Samuelson

- Bogle moved shareholders from in front of the bar to behind it

### Bogle's Complex Character
- In 1960, young hotshot Bogle wrote article in *Financial Analysts Journal* under pseudonym **"John B. Armstrong"** ridiculing passive management
- After reading **Charles Ellis's "The Loser's Game"** (85% of pensions underperformed) and calculating by hand from *Weisenberger* that funds underperformed S&P 500 by 1.4%/year over 30 years, Bogle saw the light
- **Jason Zweig** to **Eric Balchunas**: "He was the ultimate insider. What happened is he got thrown out from the inner circle... He made a virtue out of necessity."

### FIIT: "Bogle's Folly"
- **1976**: launched **First Index Investment Trust** tracking S&P 500; initial underwriting: scant $11 million
- Several academically oriented types at Wells Fargo, American National Bank, Batterymarch had started small passive funds earlier, but **Glass-Steagall** forbade retail offerings
- **Ned Johnson**: "I can't believe the great mass of investors are going to be satisfied with just receiving average return. The name of the game is to be the best."
- **Sharpe's arithmetic**: over longer periods, ~1%/year expense advantage leaves only indexing advantage
> "Performance comes and goes, but expenses are forever." — Jonathan Clements

- Virtuous cycle: renamed **500 Index Fund** gathered more assets → lowered expense ratio → lengthened performance lead → attracted more assets
- Expense ratio: 0.66% (1976) → **0.04%** (year-end 2022); ETF class at 0.03%
- **1986**: Total Bond Index. **1992**: Total Stock Market Index
- **1988**: Fidelity introduced own index funds. **2018**: Fidelity launched **zero-expense** index funds
- By mid-2022: indexed assets roughly equal to actively managed
- **Big Three**: BlackRock (primary products passive), Vanguard, State Street — own about half of US stock market
- Bogle passed away 2019; transferred potential billions from his pockets to customers

### Active Fund Fairy Tales
- **Bear market myth**: S&P scorecard (2000-2002 and 2008): in 11 of 12 categories, **majority of active funds underperformed benchmarks**
- "Won't everyone index?" — what matters is % of **trading**, not assets; active managers trade ~10× more; if just 10% of funds run by active managers, they'd still do ~half the trading
- Mathematically impossible for active managers to thrive: if passive owns 99%, remaining 1% still identical to market; active managers still get market return minus expenses
- Political danger of Big Three → see **John C. Coates**, "The Future of Corporate Governance Part I: The Problem of Twelve"

### ETFs: Index Funds in Drag
- **1993**: State Street listed first ETF — ticker **SPY** (SPDR). 1993: 1 ETF; 2003: 276; 2021: **8,552** (98% passively managed)
- Three differences from closed-end funds: (1) overwhelmingly passive, (2) **authorized participants (APs)** keep price near NAV through arbitrage (buy ETF when at discount, sell underlying securities; reverse at premium), (3) ETF assets surpassed open-end
- **Bogle opposed ETFs** to his dying day — saw them as speculative vehicles, "like handing a match to an arsonist"
- He did his best to prevent Vanguard from offering them before company's bylaws forced him off board in 1999, on his seventieth birthday
- Bogle's departure allowed **Gus Sauter**, Vanguard's index fund engineer, to launch ETF program
- It's a good thing Bogle didn't get his way

### ETF Advantages
- Slightly lower expenses; commission-free at most brokerages
- **Tax advantage**: in-kind redemption avoids capital gains distributions (doesn't apply at Vanguard — patented method extends tax efficiency to open-end funds)
- **DFA products** now available to public as ETFs (previously required expensive advisor)
- Treat long-term shareholders more fairly by externalizing trading costs via bid/ask spread

### ETF Drawbacks
- **Bid/ask spread** can eat years of expense advantage; always use **limit orders**; avoid first/last 30 minutes of trading; spreads can vary greatly throughout day
- **Bond ETF liquidity mismatch**: during 2008 and March 2020, municipal/corporate bond ETFs traded at wide spreads; sellers got significantly lower prices than open-end fund holders
- **Paralysis of choice**: watching ticker bounce vs open-end fund's 4pm price
- ETFs can't close to new money (Vanguard closed Primecap, Fidelity closed Magellan; ETFs lack this mechanism)
- For long-term investor: not much practical difference — sandwich tastes same regardless of bag color

### Good Actively Managed Funds
- Indexing beats active because of **costs, not stock selection**
- Duke University study (Reinker and Tower): Vanguard's active funds underperformed passive by exactly the expense difference
- If can't resist: choose low expenses, low turnover, decades of history
- **Vanguard Wellington** (60/40), **Wellesley Income** (40/60), **Dodge & Cox** (founded 1931), **Vanguard Primecap**

## ASSEMBLING THE FOUR PILLARS: INVESTMENT STRATEGY

## Chapter 16: How Much Do You Need to Retire?

### Retirement Calculators Are Unreliable
- Life's vicissitudes make forecasting impossible; luck matters alongside hard work
- Calculators ignore caprice of human existence, provide false security
- **Roman Empire lasted only half a millennium** — 85-year life expectancy gives ~15% chance of societal collapse
- Most assume future returns as generous as past (likelihood: "between Santa Claus and Easter Bunny")

### Calculating Your Number
1. Calculate **annual residual living expenses (RLE)** — spending beyond Social Security/pension
2. Accumulate approximately **25 years of RLE**
3. Bracket with **large uncertainty (at least 2× both directions)**

**Example — Fritz (65-year-old retiree)**:
- Expenses + taxes $50K; SS + pensions $20K; **RLE shortfall $30K/year**
- Needs: 25 × $30K = **$750,000**
- All in today's dollars pegged to retirement moment; at 3% inflation over 24 years (age 89): spending $60K/year nominal; Rule of 72: 24×3=72

### How to Get There — Felicia's Example
- Age 25, 40 years to target, earns $70K inflation-adjusted

| Real Return | 0% | 1% | 2% | 3% | 4% | 5% | 6% |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Monthly Savings | $1,563 | $1,271 | $1,021 | $810 | $635 | $491 | $377 |

- Author estimates 60/40 return ~3%; 60/40 has ~9% annual SD; over 40 years, annualized SD = 1.4%
- **95% confidence interval**: realized return between +0.2% and +5.8% (3% ± [2 × 1.4%])
- At optimistic 4% real return: save **10% of pretax salary starting age 25**
- Start at 35: 18.5% at 4% real; 26% at 2% real
- **Sequence risk**: young saver wants bad returns followed by good; retired uncle bankrupted by reverse

### The Millionaire Next Door (Stanley and Danko)
- Most common car of wealthy: **Ford F-150** (not Mercedes)
- Average plumber retires sooner than average lawyer (who spends more keeping up with peers)
> "If you can't save, you're far less likely to get rich."

### Other Goals
- **Emergencies**: 6 months (secure government job) to 1-2 years (tenuous/retraining needed); retiree: 10+ years RLE in safe assets
- Keep emergency money in **taxable accounts** if young (tapping IRA before 59½ = large tax + penalty)
- **IRS Rule 72(t)**: periodic withdrawals from IRA at 55 under certain circumstances; consult accountant
- **House savings** (within 5 years): short-term bonds, CDs, money market; keep in taxable account
- **College savings**: 529 plans (tax-advantaged); max **30-40% stocks**, gradually shift to bonds; sell stocks for tuition in good years, bonds in bad

## Chapter 17: Rebalancing and Asset Allocation

### Psychological Benefits
- Rebalancing forces **buy-low/sell-high behavior** — selling assets with high recent returns (optimistic narratives) and buying battered assets (pessimistic narratives)
- Builds **"financial condition"** — not wealth, but strength of investment discipline and emotional stability
- Like physical conditioning, requires constant exercise

### Optimal Frequency
- Historical data favors rebalancing every **2-5 years**, not annually
- Stocks show **price momentum** under 1 year, **mean reversion** over 3-5 years
- **US vs Japan (1990s)**: extreme case — the longer you waited before selling US/buying Japanese, the better

### Calendar vs Threshold Rebalancing
- **Calendar**: specific date every 1-3 years
- **Threshold**: rebalance when asset exceeds defined bound (e.g., 25% trigger on 30% target = rebalance at 37.5% or 24%)
- Security returns behave **lognormally** (100% increase and 50% decrease both change price by factor of two)
- Design thresholds higher for volatile assets: **40-50% for emerging markets**
- Threshold requires spreadsheet prowess; both work, statistically impossible to prove one superior

### Tax Considerations
- Rebalance primarily in **tax-sheltered accounts** (no tax consequences)
- Taxable accounts: capital gains substantial in older portfolios with low basis
- Where possible, rebalance with fund distributions and withdrawals

### Dynamic Asset Allocation
- Definition: varying policy allocation with changing market conditions
- Occasionally adjusting slightly **opposite to very large valuation changes** can work
- **2000**: S&P at 30× earnings, 1% yield → author suggested reducing S&P, increasing undervalued assets
- **Dinner with John Bogle**: Bogle expected near-zero stock returns, determined to "stay the course," but leaned in and admitted he'd **reduced stock allocation by several percent**
- Warnings: most investors adjust based on economic/political conditions (fool's errand); even normal rebalancing can feel like "repeatedly lighting money on fire" during bear markets
- If attempting: make only **small, infrequent changes** opposite to large valuation shifts only

### Spending It Down
- **Delay Social Security to age 70** — nearly all retirees should; inflation-adjusted **7.3% annual payout** is superb investment (still excellent even for Warren Buffett)
- Low burn rate (<3.5%): maintain long-term allocation
- High stock allocations (>50%): unacceptably risky at any burn rate
- **Value averaging in reverse**: during high equity periods, sell equities; during bear markets, spend down bonds

### Fritz (4% burn rate) vs Frank (1% burn rate)
**Fritz** ($750K, $30K RLE, 4% burn rate = 1/25):
- Bad initial returns + 4% burn could extinguish any conceivable stock/bond mixture by age 80-85
- Essential first step: spend down savings to delay Social Security to 70
- Options: **TIPS ladder** (author's preference; main drawback: longest bond is 30-year, no maturities between 2034-2039) or **SPIA** (longevity protection but severe inflation risk + credit risk of issuer)
- SPIA inflation risk has no viable solution; escalator option reduces initial payout, works against you in high inflation

**Frank** ($3M, $30K RLE, 1% burn rate):
- Protected from all but societal catastrophes (Armageddon, revolution, environmental disaster)
- Should still defer Social Security to 70
- Could own all-stock portfolio; dividend stream alone covers RLE in all but 1930s-style bear
- Managing most assets for heirs/charities/Uncle Sam
- If sleeping at night more important than making spendthrift nephews rich: more conservative allocation

### Chapter 17 Summary
- Investment success revolves around **clearly defined allocation policy** through hell or high water
- Don't let perfect be enemy of good — "suboptimal" allocation you carry out beats optimal one you'll abandon
- Ranges from single target-date fund to dozen asset classes; **sticking with strategy matters far more than precise allocation**
- Rebalancing: reduces risk AND builds emotional fortitude — "fearful when others are greedy and greedy when others are fearful" (Buffett)
- Reducing equity in retirement depends primarily on burn rate

## Chapter 18: Nuts and Bolts

### The Shift from DB to DC Plans
- Past decades: companies gradually closed traditional **defined-benefit pensions**, replaced with **defined-contribution schemes** (most commonly **401(k)s**)
- Impact: offloaded shortfall risk from employers to employees — good for employers, bad for workers
- Saving, investing, and spending for retirement requires ability, knowledge, and self-discipline that strains credulity to expect of the average worker
- Analogy: "Imagine boarding airliner. Instead of heading to seat, flight attendant tells you pilots decided to stay home because of risk/stress. Turn left at door, you fly the plane."
- Employers loved DC plans: inexpensive to fund, effectively shielded from liability, paid scant attention to fund expenses
- Before 2005: typical plan had overt costs **at least 2%/year** before hidden administrative fees
- 1990s: fund offerings heavily weighted with large-cap glamour/tech funds; many participants selected highest-returning funds (often high-octane tech); did not turn out well
- Bottom of pile: teachers' 403(b) and government 457 accounts larded with eye-watering fees from insurance companies

### Improvement Drivers
- **Richard Thaler** (behavioral economist): target-date funds as default choice
- **BrightScope**: applied sunlight disinfectant to Byzantine fee structures
- **Jerome Schlichter** (tort attorney): dozens of high-profile cases against companies failing fiduciary responsibilities; **"Schlichterized"** = fearsome prospect for corporate legal departments
- Today: most plans offer decent low-cost index and target-date funds
- If stuck with bad plan: maximize employer match, save beyond in personal IRA; **roll to IRA upon termination**

### Personal Accounts: The Vanguard Effect
- **"The Vanguard Effect"** (Eric Balchunas): existential threat forced Fidelity, Schwab, wire houses to compete on price
- Fidelity has **zero expense ratio** funds; all three offer commission-free ETF trading
- Vanguard's mutual ownership returns **100% of share lending profits** to fund shareholders (Fidelity, Schwab, BlackRock don't)
- But Vanguard customer support has deteriorated (clerical errors, extended hold times)
- Schwab, BlackRock: publicly traded → conflicts between company and fund shareholders
- Fidelity: owned by Johnson family; private firms' ethics generally between mutualized and publicly traded

### Custodian Rules of the Road (Vanguard, Schwab, Fidelity)
1. **Open-end funds**: limit to custodian's own
2. **ETFs**: trade any company's commission-free online; **always place limit orders**; avoid first/last 30 minutes; pay attention to price spreads
3. **Treasuries**: purchase at auction commission-free at all three; confirm secondary market commission details
4. **Banking**: Fidelity/Schwab offer online banking; Vanguard does not; Vanguard charges $20 annual fee below certain asset levels

### Schwab's Hidden Revenue
- Default sweep yields **0.40%** — 3.7% below concurrent Vanguard Treasury money market
- **Allan Roth**: **134% of Schwab's 2019 earnings** came from net interest (paying customers pitiful yield, loaning out at higher rates); rest of company operations lost money
- Keep cash at minimum with T-bill ladder, short-term Treasury ETF (**iShares SGOV**: duration 0.1 years, approximates money market), or separate money market

### PFOF
- Schwab accepts payment for order flow; Fidelity and Vanguard do not

### Total Stock Market Funds
- All major companies offer near-zero expense index funds in open-end or ETF wrappers
- Fidelity's at zero; ETF class of Vanguard's biggest fund at 0.03%
- One fund sufficient for simpler allocations
- Open-end vs ETF: minimal long-term return difference; choice is trading psychology preference

### Tilting Toward Small Value
- Adding tilts quickly creates portfolio bloat (12+ equity asset classes before REITs, precious metals, energy)
- **Marginal benefits of extra splitting obey law of diminishing returns**
- Simple approach: add one small value fund to US and developed foreign total market
- **Modest tilt**: small value ~1/3 size of total market component
- **Bigger tilt**: equal weights to total market and small cap value
- **US value factor has been negative for nearly two decades**; value factor is risk factor — biggest risk is premium may never return
- Hold tilted assets in **tax-sheltered accounts**
- ETF "in-kind" redemption solves capital gains problem (fund distributes most appreciated shares without selling and capturing gains), but **IRS and legislators have eyes on this loophole** — survival not assured
- Before widespread ETF availability, open-end funds distributed large capital gains as stocks "grew out of" their categories — particularly problematic for small and value asset classes
- If tilting works, you'll feel vindicated; if not, prepare for "a barrage of I-told-you-sos"

### Specialty Funds
- **US REIT Index Funds**: multiple options from Dimensional, Fidelity, iShares, Schwab, SPDR, Vanguard; expense ratios 0.07%-0.39%
- **Global/International REITs**: iShares (REET), SPDR (RWO), Vanguard offerings; 0.12%-0.59%
- **Precious metals equity**: iShares MSCI Global Gold Miners (RING, 0.39%), VanEck Gold Miners (GDX, 0.51%)
- **Energy stocks**: Vanguard Energy Index Fund to specific oil/gas exploration; 0.10%-0.40%

### Bonds: The Sleep-at-Night Allocation
- **Stocks take risks; bonds maintain equanimity and let you sleep**
- Both corporations and governments can default; few surprises more alarming than discovering "safe" assets aren't
- **Most long-term return determined by behavior in worst 2% of the time**
- Municipal bonds: limit to **no more than 1/3 of fixed income**; never single-state only
- **Do not own municipal/corporate bonds in ETF** — liquidity mismatch during turbulence (2008, March 2020)
- Author "not wild" about bond index funds (~1/3 corporate/asset-backed without government guarantee)
- **Core recommendation**: center on **government-guaranteed debt** — CDs below $250K FDIC limit, Treasuries
- Ideally purchase Treasuries at auction; if too much work, Treasury ETF or fund (highly liquid, no mismatch problem)
- **TIPS ladder** with annual maturities matching living expenses = safest retirement spending strategy
- Currently no low-cost long-term TIPS vehicle; no maturities between 2034-2039 (problem slowly disappearing)

### When ETFs Go Bad: Thematic and Leveraged Funds
- Proliferation of hot-money vehicles: wound care, agribusiness, marijuana, crypto ETFs
- **Leveraged funds**: "variance drag" destroys returns (Chapter 1 math box)
  - **URE** (2× REIT daily return): −1.99%/year over 15 years vs VNQ's +6.22%
  - **SRS** (inverse 2× REIT): −34.57%/year ($1,000 → $1.72 over 15 years)
- Issuers claim "not designed for long-term"; **if lousy long-term, lousy short-term too** (−3.47% monthly)
- **Avoid all inverse and leveraged funds**

### Bond Fund Details
- **Short-Term Treasury Funds**: multiple options from Fidelity, iShares, Schwab, Vanguard; durations 0.1-2.7 years; expenses 0.03%-0.15%
- **Intermediate/Long-Term Treasury**: 5-17 year duration options; Vanguard intermediate/long-term have $50,000 minimum; expenses 0.03%-0.15%
- **TIPS Funds**: durations 2.5-20 years; providers include Fidelity, iShares, PIMCO, Schwab, SPDR, Vanguard; expenses 0.03%-0.20%
- **Short-Term Bond Index**: durations ~2.6-2.8 years; 56%-74% US government bonds; expenses 0.03%-0.07%
- **Total Bond Index**: durations 5.2-6.5 years; 47%-60% US government bonds; expenses 0.025%-0.06%
- **National Municipal Bond**: short/intermediate/long-term options; Vanguard has $50,000 minimums; expenses 0.07%-0.09%
- **Corporate Bond**: durations 2.8-13.0 years; expenses 0.04%-0.07%; some Vanguard funds have purchase fees 0.25%-1.0%

### Chapter 18 Summary
- Pick custodian(s): Vanguard, Fidelity, Schwab — each has downsides; landscape evolving
- ETFs vs open-end: personal preference; ETFs containing corporate/municipal securities may suffer wide spreads during extreme turbulence
- Tilting: may or may not offer higher returns; costs complexity + higher fees
- **Over the long haul, discipline to stick with chosen strategy is far more important than precise allocation**

## Chapter 19: A Final Word

### Pillar One: Investment Theory
- **Risk and return joined at the hip**; shouldn't own equities if fearsome losses upset you; desire safety = accept low returns
- Boring companies must offer higher returns than sexy ones; **promise of high returns with low risk = marker for fraud**
- Estimate long-term stock return: 2% earnings growth + 1.7% dividend = **~3.7%** real; **TIPS suggest ~1.5% real bond return**
- Market is terrifyingly smarter than wisest individual participants
- **Stock picking and market timing: expensive, risky, ultimately futile**; past performance not indicative of future returns — **not at all**
- Only small percent of stocks produce most of market return — **own the whole haystack**
- Can't identify future best portfolio; **most prudent course: widely diversified portfolio**

### Pillar Two: Investment History
- Markets regularly drift into la-la land on both sides
- New technologies → **hold tight to wallet**; sky falling = usually best time to buy
- While history doesn't repeat or rhyme, only so many plot trajectories; helps to realize you've seen the movie before

### Pillar Three: Investment Psychology
- **You are your own worst enemy** — overconfident about stock picking and risk tolerance
- **Fred Schwed**: some things cannot be conveyed to a virgin with abstractions
- Must not interrupt investment strategy to reap compound interest's bounty
- Social instincts corrode wealth; **successful investing is a solitary activity**
- Ignore last 5-10 years; focus on longest-term data; resist imagining patterns where there are none

### Pillar Four: Investment Business
- Most brokers service clients "same way Bonnie and Clyde serviced car dealerships"
- **99% of financial media is worse than worthless** (cable TV: 100%)
- Never forget: primary business of most fund companies is **collecting assets, not managing money**

### The Four Pillars as Framework
- Together the four pillars function like four legs of a chair — subtract even one, investment strategy comes to grief
- Principals of **Long-Term Capital Management** demonstrated even brilliant theory masters court disaster without knowledge of psychology, history, and business
- While you might manage finances with just this book, **you'd be foolish to do so** — it's a framework to continuously build upon

### The Overarching Message
- With relatively little effort, can design portfolio with wide diversification and minimal expense
- **It will prove superior to most professionally managed accounts**
- Don't need great intelligence and good luck
- Essential characteristics: **discipline and stamina to "stay the course"** (Jack Bogle)
- **Financial security rests on four pillars**: knowledge of theory, history, psychology, and business
- **Learn them well and use them wisely**
