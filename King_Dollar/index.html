Chapter 1: With Great Power

Blustein opens with a vivid scene from the pre-Civil War era: squads of bank “porters” toting bundles of checks and gold coins through New York’s streets, creating a chaotic and error-prone payments system. An 1858 account describes “sixty Porters were out at all once” with hundreds of bank drafts, crossing each other’s paths in utter confusion.

The solution came in 1853 when George Dummer Lyman—a bank clerk who had studied London’s Bankers’ Clearing House—organized the New York Clearing House: 52 banks, an oval table about 70 feet long, outside clerks rotating one position at the manager’s signal, settling all transactions in as little as six minutes. This institution was the embryonic ancestor of the massive dollar-based clearing infrastructure that now underpins global finance.

The chapter pivots to the modern equivalent: CHIPS (Clearing House Interbank Payments System), headquartered on the 17th floor of a Manhattan skyscraper, processing 540,000+ transactions worth $1.8 trillion per average day—about 95% of all cross-border dollar payments. SWIFT, by contrast, is merely a messaging system; Fedwire handles mostly domestic transfers.

Blustein’s critical insight: “When the stuff flowing through the pipes is the bulk of international movements in the dollar, and the pipes are located in a jurisdiction subject to US law, foreign banks and even whole governments need to take care lest they lose access to that plumbing. The clout the US government gains as a result is incalculable.”

He introduces his central metaphor from Spider-Man: “With great power comes great responsibility.” The dollar’s dominance gives the United States extraordinary abilities—to impose devastating financial sanctions, to borrow at scale, to serve as the world’s monetary anchor—but those abilities demand restraint and wisdom. He quotes Brazilian president Lula, baffled in Shanghai in April 2023: “Every night I ask myself why all countries have to base their trade on the dollar.”

By the numbers, the dollar’s supremacy is overwhelming. It is used in roughly 90% of foreign exchange trades. Over 75% of global trade is invoiced in dollars (96% in the Western Hemisphere). Approximately 60% of official foreign reserves are dollar-denominated. 60% of international loans and deposits are in dollars. Roughly 70% of international bonds and debt securities are dollar-denominated. These metrics haven’t meaningfully changed in decades, despite periodic prophecies of decline.

Blustein explains the three functions of money at the international level—medium of exchange, unit of account, store of value—and shows how “complementarity” and “network effects” create a self-reinforcing loop: exporters invoice in dollars, receive dollar payments, borrow in dollars, trade dollars on FX markets, creating more demand. Everybody uses dollars because everybody else does—like English as lingua franca.

He also provides a deep primer on the nature of money itself: the distinction between “public money” (Federal Reserve Notes) and “private money” (bank deposits created “as if by magic” when banks make loans), held together by the “singleness of money” principle—a dollar is always worth a dollar—underwritten by the Fed, FDIC, rule of law, and the government’s five state powers: the power to tax, legal tender laws, the Federal Reserve’s mandate, the public/private money framework, and the reserve settlement system.

The chapter establishes three responsibilities that accompany dollar power: (1) keep the dollar sound through monetary/fiscal discipline, (2) wield sanctions carefully to avoid driving countries toward alternatives, and (3) act as global lender of last resort via the Fed in crises. These responsibilities form the book’s organizing framework. It ends with a warning that will echo through every subsequent chapter: the only real threat to the dollar’s throne is “catastrophic missteps by the US government.”

Chapter 2: From Humble Origins to King of Currencies

Blustein opens with journalist Nellie Bly’s 1889 circumnavigation—she carried Bank of England notes and gold, not dollars, because just 135 years ago the dollar was virtually useless outside the United States. America’s banking system was primitive: the “Free Banking Era” (1837–1863) allowed states to charter banks freely, producing “wildcat banks” in remote areas where nobody could redeem their notes.

Andrew Jackson had killed the Second Bank of the United States in 1836, viewing it as elitist tyranny, and the country had no central bank from 1836 to 1913—the only major economy without one. Bank notes traded at varying discounts depending on distance from the issuing bank and perceived soundness.

The Civil War produced America’s first national currency: Treasury Secretary Salmon Chase created “greenbacks” (US Notes printed with green ink on the back), which depreciated as low as 35 cents on the gold dollar. The National Banking Act (1863) created nationally chartered banks issuing uniform notes. The Gold Standard Act of 1900 formally pegged the dollar at $20.67 per troy ounce—but even then, the British pound sterling ruled international finance and London was the world’s financial center.

The absence of a central bank made America uniquely vulnerable to panics. When Overend, Gurney & Co. collapsed in London in 1866, Walter Bagehot formulated the doctrine that a central bank must “lend freely in a crisis, at high rates, against good collateral.” America had no such institution. In 1907, J.P. Morgan personally organized a rescue—summoning bankers to his library, locking the doors, forcing them to pledge money. The spectacle of one private citizen saving the financial system spurred creation of the Federal Reserve (signed into law December 23, 1913).

World War I was the tipping point. Britain borrowed massively, including from the US; America emerged as the world’s largest creditor nation; New York began rivaling London. The 1944 Bretton Woods conference enshrined dollar dominance: 730 delegates from 44 nations gathered at a New Hampshire resort where Harry Dexter White (US Treasury) defeated John Maynard Keynes’s proposal for a new supranational currency called “bancor.”

Keynes’s plan was ambitious—a new unit for central banks to settle cross-border transactions, with allocations proportional to each country’s share of world trade. But Keynes himself admitted the idea was “complicated and novel and perhaps Utopian,” and it was shelved before delegates even arrived because it “contravened the interests of the United States.” White’s system pegged all currencies to the dollar, and the dollar to gold at $35/oz. The IMF and World Bank were created as institutional pillars. The system reflected raw American power: the US held three-quarters of the world’s monetary gold and produced the bulk of global output.

The chapter’s most important section covers the system’s collapse. Belgian economist Robert Triffin identified the fatal flaw in 1960: the US had to run persistent deficits to supply the world with dollars, but those very deficits would eventually undermine confidence in the dollar’s gold backing. France’s Giscard d’Estaing coined the term “exorbitant privilege”; de Gaulle demanded a return to the gold standard.

By 1971, US gold reserves covered only 25% of foreign-dollar liabilities. At a secret Camp David weekend (August 13–15, 1971)—“Don’t tell your wife where you’re going, or your secretary either”—Treasury Secretary John Connally laid out the plan: “We close the gold window.” Arthur Burns (Fed chair) agonized: “What a tragedy for mankind!” But ultimately gave “wholehearted support.”

Nixon announced the suspension on national television, interrupting Bonanza. Connally’s immortal line to European finance ministers: “The dollar is our currency, but your problem.” By spring 1973, all major currencies were floating freely. Remarkably, the dollar’s global dominance survived. Freed from gold, it became a pure fiat currency—yet remained indispensable. Charles Kindleberger decried “the Crime of 1971” and declared the dollar “finished as international money.” Blustein: “That was an early example of the dollar’s death being greatly exaggerated.”

Chapter 3: Maintaining the Throne, Through Thick and Thin

This chapter is structured around the crises that were supposed to destroy the dollar—and instead reinforced it. Blustein opens with a vivid scene at SAMA (the Saudi Arabian Monetary Agency) in the 1970s: a “dilapidated, fly-infested building” in Jeddah with leather-bound ledgers kept by hand and a single telex machine in a tiny triangular room—yet this primitive institution was managing an influx of “$100 million per day” in oil revenue after OPEC quadrupled prices.

Why did Saudi Arabia keep its wealth in dollars? David Mulford (SAMA adviser) explained: the team was tiny—“only six professionals confronted by a mountain of work”—and in most non-US markets, a $5–10 million trade was enough to move prices. Only the US Treasury market offered the depth needed for portfolios growing $5–10 billion every 30 days. By end of 1977, only ~6% of SAMA’s $60 billion was in non-dollar assets.

Kissinger and Treasury Secretary Simon sweetened the deal: SAMA could buy Treasuries directly (bypassing public auctions), while “joint commissions” in economics and defense led to $100+ billion in US weapons purchases. Meanwhile, the US bond market was exploding: by 1976, Treasury trading volume averaged $10.5 billion/day—triple the 1973 pace and far exceeding NYSE stock trading. Tom Wolfe’s bond-trading “masters of the universe” were making the dollar’s safe-haven status a self-fulfilling prophecy through sheer market depth.

The chapter’s centerpiece is Volcker’s revolution. By 1979, Burns’s and Miller’s failure to control inflation had eroded dollar confidence (CPI hit 10.75% annual rate). On October 6, 1979—his “Saturday Night Special”—Volcker announced a radical shift to money supply targeting. The night before, he had seemed “so tired and troubled” that his vice-chair was “worried about him.” When Senator Cranston entered the restaurant, Volcker’s smile froze: “In view of what we’re about to do tomorrow, we’d better be careful about the levity.”

Interest rates surged; auto dealers mailed the Fed keys to unsold cars; farmers circled headquarters on tractors. The toll was brutal but effective: by 1984, inflation was tamed. Volcker’s conquest imparted “sanctity to a foundational principle of the dollar’s international status—the independence of America’s central bank.”

But the strong dollar brought new problems: US exporters couldn’t compete, the current account deficit hit ~$120 billion by 1985. The Plaza Accord (September 22, 1985)—“the world’s best-kept secret”—saw G-5 nations agree to push the dollar down. David Mulford (now Treasury assistant secretary) was dispatched to negotiate secretly with counterparts, distributing a US draft communiqué at the start of sessions and collecting it at the end—no copies allowed. The result: a ~40% depreciation against the yen over two years.

The 2008 crisis was the ultimate paradox: the crisis was “Made in America”—yet the dollar rose 15% as the world scrambled for the only truly liquid safe asset. European banks had borrowed $2+ trillion in dollars for mortgage bets; when markets seized up, they desperately needed greenbacks.

The Fed opened swap lines that surpassed $500 billion, lending to 14 foreign central banks—including giving unlimited access to the ECB, BOJ, BOE, and SNB. The “dirty little secret,” revealed years later via Bloomberg FOIA litigation, was that foreign institutions like Barclays, Deutsche Bank, and UBS absorbed a majority of the funds. Geithner’s words at the October 2008 FOMC: “The privilege of being the reserve currency of the world comes with some burdens.”

The Fed’s willingness to act as global lender of last resort paradoxically reinforced the dollar’s centrality—a self-reinforcing dynamic no other currency could replicate. Post-crisis predictions of dollar decline flooded in: Zhou Xiaochuan revived Keynes’s bancor idea; Germany’s Steinbrück declared the US would “lose its superpower status”; Sarkozy insisted that “what was true in 1945 cannot be true today.” All these predictions proved wrong.

Chapter 4: Pretenders to the Throne

This sprawling chapter systematically evaluates every currency cited as a potential dollar replacement, and finds each one profoundly lacking.

The Euro. Blustein opens with his 2002 visit to Athens, where Greeks were delighted to trade the inflation-ravaged drachma for the euro. Executive Dimitri Papalexopoulos described pre-euro Greece: 17% average annual inflation, no long-term fixed rates, less than 1% real growth since 1980. In the eurozone, “we have a cost of capital roughly equal to that of the US.” By its tenth anniversary, the euro held 25% of global reserves; scholars Chinn and Frankel predicted it could “overtake the dollar as early as 2015.”

Euro-skeptics (Friedman, Feldstein, Dornbusch, Krugman) had warned that monetary union without fiscal union was unstable—members surrendered control over interest rates and exchange rates but kept separate budgets. When Greece revealed its deficit was triple previous estimates (October 2009), the crisis unfolded exactly as predicted: the “doom loop” between weak banks and weak sovereigns, bailouts overriding the Maastricht Treaty’s “no bailout clause,” contagion to Ireland (“Black Thursday”—a budget deficit of 32% of GDP), Portugal, Spain, Italy, Cyprus.

Draghi’s “whatever it takes” pledge in mid-2012 arrested the most virulent phase, but the structural flaw was laid bare: the eurozone lacks a single safe asset comparable to US Treasuries. German bunds may be as safe, but Germany doesn’t issue enough debt. “Investors weighing what sort of securities to keep in reserve for emergencies will tend to favor the ones that are both the safest and most liquid of all, meaning those of the United States.”

The Japanese Yen. In a delicious irony, the US actively pressured Japan to internationalize the yen in the 1980s. Treasury Undersecretary Beryl Sprinkel told his Japanese counterparts a Missouri farm story about cutting piglets’ tails: “We just hacked them off once up at the top!” Japan resisted with its “step by step” approach. The yen briefly looked like a contender as Toyota, Sony, and Toshiba seemed poised to overtake American industry.

But Japan’s bubble burst in the 1990s; deflation, zombie banks, and demographic decline (the world’s oldest population) rendered the yen a permanent non-contender. It peaked at 5.5% of global reserves in 2001, then declined. The BOJ bought so many government bonds that there was “very little trading in those bonds in recent years”—destroying liquidity and further diminishing the yen’s international appeal. Blustein: “Perhaps if Finance Ministry officials had taken the moral of Beryl Sprinkel’s piglet story to heart and dismantled their controls much earlier, dollar users would have had strong motivation to shift to yen. But the opportunity was missed.”

The SDR. The IMF’s synthetic reserve asset, created in 1969, has never gained traction—you can’t buy coffee with SDRs, and no private transactions are denominated in them. Zhou Xiaochuan’s 2009 proposal to expand the SDR’s role went nowhere. Tobias Unteroberdoerster, managing the system from the IMF’s twelfth floor, acknowledged that SDRs remain “a niche instrument.”

The Chinese Renminbi. This is the pretender Blustein takes most seriously—and demolishes most thoroughly. China’s GDP is second only to America’s, and Beijing has taken deliberate steps: establishing CIPS, opening “dim sum bond” markets in Hong Kong, winning SDR inclusion (2016). But the renminbi’s share of global payments remains ~4.5% and reserves barely 2%.

The fundamental obstacle is capital controls and authoritarian governance. Blustein traces the history of China’s “financial repression” model (keeping capital trapped at home so firms can borrow cheaply) and the catastrophic 2015–2016 capital flight, when $1 trillion fled the country and Beijing burned through $1 trillion in reserves to defend the currency.

Xi Jinping’s increasingly authoritarian direction—tech sector crackdown, disappearing business figures like Jack Ma, reneging on Hong Kong’s autonomy, arbitrary regulatory changes overnight—only worsened the trust deficit. For international investors, the question is simple: would you park your money in a system where the rules can change at an autocrat’s whim? For most of the world’s capital, the answer is no.

Chapter 5: Weaponization

Blustein traces how the dollar’s centrality was transformed from a passive advantage into an active weapon. The key insight: any bank that wants to clear dollar transactions must do so through CHIPS or Fedwire in New York, which puts it under US jurisdiction. Almost every cross-border payment passes through one of just fifteen megabanks.

The compliance machine—staffed by thousands of bankers scrutinizing transactions under threat of massive penalties—does the enforcement work automatically. “Most major companies will prefer to be over-compliant rather than risk being under-compliant,” creating a self-policing private-sector enforcement mechanism far more pervasive than any government agency could maintain alone.

The legal foundation is IEEPA (International Emergency Economic Powers Act, 1977), giving the president sweeping authority to block transactions during declared emergencies. Blustein traces sanctions from Woodrow Wilson’s advocacy for economic penalties through the League of Nations (“a nation boycotted is a nation that is in sight of surrender”), through the Bank Secrecy Act’s AML infrastructure, FATF international standards, and FinCEN’s reporting requirements.

The post-9/11 transformation made dollar weaponization truly potent. The Patriot Act’s Section 311 allowed Treasury to designate foreign financial institutions as “primary money laundering concerns,” effectively excommunicating them from the dollar system. Juan Zarate and John Taylor pioneered these techniques. The CIA’s secret access to SWIFT transaction data—revealed publicly in 2006 to European outrage—demonstrated the extraordinary surveillance capability that dollar centrality provides.

OFAC’s SDN (Specially Designated Nationals) list became “the financial equivalent of a death sentence.” In Latin America it was known as la lista Clinton—even Colombian banks wouldn’t touch anyone connected to it. The army of compliance officers that banks were forced to hire created a self-policing mechanism: HSBC increased compliance employees sevenfold to 8,300 in five years after its $1.25 billion fine for letting Mexican cartel funds flow through.

Chapter 6: “Open an Account on the Moon—We Can Go After That”

The chapter title comes from US negotiator Christopher Hill’s quip to his North Korean counterpart about OFAC’s seemingly limitless reach. Blustein delivers a gripping account of dollar weaponization in practice through three case studies.

The BDA Shockwave. Banco Delta Asia, a tiny family-run bank in Macao with 9 branches and fewer than $400 million in deposits, served as a conduit for North Korean illicit finance, including the famous “supernote” counterfeiting operation. When Treasury designated BDA under Section 311 in September 2005, panicked customers withdrew $133 million in six days. But the real shockwave rippled far beyond the bank itself—every financial institution in Asia scrambled to cut ties with anyone connected to North Korea.

Even Bank of China’s Macao branch froze North Korean accounts—despite Beijing’s desire to prop up the Kim regime. An inebriated North Korean negotiator confessed: “You Americans have finally found a way to hurt us.” The crucial principle: the US doesn’t need to sanction every bank. A single designation triggers cascading compliance reactions through the entire global banking system. The BDA action derailed the Six Party Talks—North Korea stormed out and tested its first nuclear device three months later. But the Treasury’s weapon had been tested against a nation-state, and its explosive potential was demonstrated.

The Iran Campaign. Stuart Levey (Treasury’s first undersecretary for terrorism and financial intelligence) refined the BDA approach into a “whisper campaign”—100+ private meetings with bank CEOs worldwide, warning about reputational and legal risks. Combined with escalating OFAC designations, criminal prosecutions of foreign banks (BNP Paribas paid $8.9 billion), CISADA (2010), and the Bank Markazi designation (2011), Iran was progressively isolated from the global financial system.

By mid-2013, Iran’s oil exports had plunged from 2.5 million barrels per day to 1 million. GDP shrank 6.6%, the rial fell ~80%, and prices soared. Iran returned to negotiations, elected Rouhani on a platform of lifting sanctions, and the 2015 JCPOA was signed—arguably the dollar weapon’s greatest success.

The Limits of Sanctions. North Korea’s elaborate evasion infrastructure—front companies like Diamond 8 Trading, “burner banks,” shell entities—showed that determined adversaries can find workarounds. Russia’s pre-invasion “Fortress Russia” preparations (SPFS messaging system, reserve hoarding, the Mir card network) demonstrated that countries can partially anticipate and prepare. China developed “burner banks”—small institutions like Bank of Kunlun in Xinjiang, used solely to buy Iranian oil, conducting no dollar clearing in New York, and thus impervious to SDN designation.

Most dangerously, Trump’s “sanctions addiction” raised alarm about overuse. Obama averaged ~500 new sanctions designations per year; Trump ramped to 600, then 900, then 1,200+. The Iran JCPOA withdrawal, Venezuela sanctions, the extraordinary sanctioning of the ICC prosecutor, the Deripaska/Rusal debacle that nearly wrecked global aluminum markets—when a single administration sanctions practically everyone, the weapon loses its precision and its credibility. Biden’s own October 2021 review concluded the US had overused sanctions and needed recalibration.

Chapter 7: Crypto, Libra, and the Central Bankers’ Wake-Up Call

Blustein delivers a succinct and devastating assessment of cryptocurrency’s failure to fulfill its original promise as an alternative to state-controlled money. Bitcoin, launched in 2009 with the utopian goal of creating a decentralized, censorship-resistant currency, has instead become a speculative asset—“digital gold” at best, a vehicle for scams at worst. Its 21-million-coin cap makes its monetary policy more rigid than the gold standard, since a real economy needs an elastic money supply that grows with production capacity.

Bitcoin’s price volatility makes it useless for everyday transactions—from $0.003 at the famous pizza purchase in 2010 to $20,000 by December 2017, then crashing below $100 and back up again. El Salvador’s adoption as legal tender proved to be an embarrassing flop. Mostly, crypto is a speculative tool with “prices depending solely on what others will pay”—unlike stocks (company profits), bonds (interest), or houses (utility).

The real alarm bell for central bankers came in June 2019 when Facebook announced Libra: a stablecoin backed by a basket of government securities and fiat currencies, designed to serve Facebook’s 2.4 billion users. Unlike Bitcoin, Libra threatened to actually work as money. Central bankers were horrified at the prospect of a Big Tech company issuing a private currency that could rapidly achieve global scale, undermining monetary sovereignty.

The backlash was swift and fatal. Congressional hearings savaged the project. Fed Chair Powell told Congress the project “cannot go forward” until serious concerns were addressed. Visa, Mastercard, PayPal, and other partners fled. Libra was renamed Diem in 2020 and finally dissolved in 2022, its technology sold to Silvergate Capital (which itself later collapsed).

But Libra’s lasting impact was to galvanize central banks into taking digital currency seriously. BIS general manager Carstens had derided CBDCs as “neither necessary nor desirable” in March 2019; by June he had reversed course, admitting Libra was “a wake-up call.” Over 100 countries began exploring CBDCs. China accelerated its e-CNY development. The “Libra moment” transformed digital currency from a fringe topic into a mainstream policy priority.

Chapter 8: To CBDC or Not to CBDC

This chapter explores whether Central Bank Digital Currencies will reshape the international monetary system. Blustein begins with China’s e-CNY—the most advanced major-economy CBDC—which emerged from the country’s remarkable fintech leapfrog: Alipay and WeChat Pay had already made China a largely cashless society (94% of online payments by 2020), and the PBOC wanted to ensure the state retained control of the monetary system, especially after Jack Ma criticized regulators and Xi canceled the Ant IPO.

Blustein argues that the e-CNY poses minimal threat to dollar dominance. A CBDC is essentially a technological upgrade to existing money—it doesn’t change the fundamental reasons people choose one currency over another. China’s capital controls, authoritarian governance, and lack of deep financial markets are the real barriers to the renminbi, and no digital wrapper can solve those problems. In practice, Chinese citizens themselves barely use it—South China Morning Post reporters found a drink shop employee saying, “You’re the first one to propose to use e-CNY,” and a government employee who transfers her salary to Alipay/WeChat: “Why should I bother to download another app?”

In the US, the CBDC debate became a culture-war battleground. Randal Quarles (Fed vice-chair for supervision) delivered his famous “parachute pants” speech, comparing CBDC enthusiasm to a passing 1980s fashion fad. DeSantis, Trump, and RFK Jr. attacked CBDCs as government surveillance tools. Trump promised to “never allow the creation” of a CBDC. Blustein’s rebuttal: this rhetoric is “based on exaggerated ideas about the extent of financial privacy that US citizens currently have”—all bank-linked transactions can already be traced with a warrant, and the Freedom Convoy asset freeze happened without any CBDC.

The chapter’s most important section covers stablecoins and the BIS’s “Future Monetary System” vision. Stablecoins (like Tether and USDC) are crypto tokens pegged to fiat currencies, but Blustein catalogs their flaws: the TerraUSD algorithmic collapse ($40 billion wiped out by Do Kwon’s “I don’t debate the poor on Twitter”), Tether’s questionable reserves and refusal of full audits, and the fundamental violation of the “singleness of money” principle. Moody’s Analytics documented 700+ depeggings in 2022 alone—a stablecoin that can wobble to $0.87 (as USDC did during SVB) is not real money.

Stablecoins also undermine sanctions enforcement: they are bearer instruments transferred without issuer verification, no AML/KYC at point of transfer. Russian dealer Yury Orekhov used USDT to buy Venezuelan oil: “USDT works quick... That’s why everyone does it now.”

The BIS alternative, championed by Hyun Song Shin, is tokenized deposits: bank deposits represented as digital tokens on programmable ledgers, settled in wholesale CBDC behind the scenes. This preserves the banking system (and therefore sanctions infrastructure), maintains FDIC-style protections, enables smart contracts and atomic settlement, and sidesteps the political firestorm over retail CBDC. JPM Coin was the first tokenized deposit—a private blockchain for B2B corporate transactions. Blustein is clearly sympathetic to this vision, which he calls the most promising path for the dollar’s digital future.

The chapter closes with mBridge, a BIS Innovation Hub project enabling real-time cross-border CBDC transfers between Thailand, Hong Kong, UAE, and China. A pilot settled $22 million+ in real transactions among 20+ banks in seconds—a glimpse of how the plumbing of international finance might eventually be rebuilt.

Chapter 9: The Fed, the Dollar, and Global Power

This sprawling chapter covers the threats to dollar credibility emanating from within America itself. It begins with Trump’s unprecedented attacks on Fed independence—publicly berating Powell, calling him an “enemy,” exploring whether a president could fire a Fed chair. If a president successfully subjugated the Fed, it would undermine the institutional credibility that makes the dollar trustworthy.

Blustein then provides a harrowing account of the Treasury market’s structural vulnerabilities. The $18 trillion market—the bedrock of global finance—has become fragile. Post-GFC bank regulations reduced dealers’ willingness to warehouse Treasuries, and the gap was filled by algorithmic traders (who provide liquidity only in calm markets) and hedge funds running massively leveraged “basis trades” at 50:1 or even 100:1 leverage. The October 2014 “flash rally”—yields swinging wildly with no obvious trigger—was an early warning sign.

In March 2020, when the pandemic triggered a “dash for cash,” the supposedly risk-free Treasury market seized up. Bid-ask spreads on 30-year bonds blew out to 32 times normal; some dealers refused to bid at all. Foreign central banks sold $109 billion from Fed custody accounts in March alone. Hedge funds’ leveraged basis trades unwound violently, forcing sales to meet margin calls.

The Fed intervened with what Quarles called a “damn close-run thing”—cutting rates to zero, committing to unlimited QE, and purchasing roughly $2 trillion in securities within two months. Swap lines were reopened to 14 central banks ($440 billion+ outstanding by end of April), and a novel FIMA repo facility was created for central banks excluded from the swap line network (India, China, Taiwan, Thailand).

The chapter then tracks the journey from rescue to inflation. William McChesney Martin’s famous 1955 metaphor—the Fed’s job is “to take away the punch bowl just as the party gets going”—frames the narrative. Powell’s August 2020 FAIT announcement (Flexible Average Inflation Targeting) meant the Fed would tolerate above-target inflation to make up for years of undershooting. This was informed by a 2019 “Fed Listens” tour in which community groups argued that tight labor markets help disadvantaged workers most.

But FAIT collided with massive fiscal stimulus: Biden’s $1.9 trillion American Rescue Plan on top of Trump’s CARES Act ($2.7T) and December 2020 package ($900B). Larry Summers warned (February 2021) the ARP was three times larger than the output gap, risking “inflationary pressures of a kind we have not seen in a generation.” He was right. Supply chain disruptions combined with demand surge from stimulus checks. “Team Transitory” insisted inflation was temporary; Powell’s pivot came November 30, 2021: “it’s probably a good time to retire the word ‘transitory.’” By March 2022, CPI hit +8.5% YoY—fastest since 1981.

The Fed hiked 11 times, including four consecutive 75bp increases—the most aggressive tightening since Volcker. The resulting dollar surge became a “wrecking ball” for the rest of the world: the yen fell 20%, the euro hit $0.96, sterling nearly hit parity at $1.03. Wheat costs in local currency spiked brutally: +106% Peru, +112% Egypt, +132% Pakistan, +176% Ethiopia.

Hélène Rey’s concept of the “global financial cycle” showed that US monetary policy transmits worldwide regardless of exchange rate regime. Brazil’s finance minister Mantega accused the Fed of waging a “currency war” via QE; Bernanke’s 2013 “taper tantrum” caused capital flight from the “fragile five.” Proposed remedies—Carney’s “synthetic hegemonic currency,” Ocampo’s IMF reserve asset—went nowhere. Connally’s 1971 quip remains apt: “The dollar is our currency, but it’s your problem.”

A silver lining: by 2019, emerging markets had learned from past crises—four-fifths of bonds now issued in local currency (versus one-fifth at the millennium)—and the widely predicted 2022–2023 emerging market debt crisis turned out to be a false alarm.

Chapter 10: Punches Landed, Punches Pulled

This chapter provides the most detailed account to date of the decision to freeze Russia’s foreign reserves after the February 2022 invasion of Ukraine—and the sobering aftermath that revealed the limits of financial warfare against a large economy.

The key advocate for targeting reserves was Canadian Deputy Prime Minister Chrystia Freeland, who had Ukrainian grandparents, spoke Ukrainian, held Harvard and Oxford degrees in Russian history, and had served as the Financial Times’ Moscow bureau chief before being personally banned from Russia. Freeland argued that Russia’s $640 billion reserve hoard was the real center of gravity—not SWIFT access, which banks could work around via email or fax.

On February 26, with Ukrainian PM Shmyhal calling from a Kyiv bunker, Freeland pushed through EU approval. Mario Draghi helped persuade Yellen. By February 28, OFAC had formally prohibited all transactions with Russia’s central bank. The initial impact was dramatic: the ruble lost 25% in a day, ATM queues formed across Moscow, Apple Pay and Google Pay stopped working on the metro, and Aeroflot was banned from European airspace. Western analysts predicted 50%+ inflation and 10-15% GDP contraction. French economy minister Le Maire crowed: “We will cause the collapse of the Russian economy.”

The collapse never came. Russia’s GDP shrank a mere 2% in 2022, and by 2023 its growth outpaced America’s. Several factors explained this resilience. Elvira Nabiullina had prepared the domestic payments system (the Mir card network had 100+ million cards). The West pulled its punches on secondary sanctions—Europeans flatly refused, still smarting from Trump’s Iran secondary sanctions—and carved out massive exceptions for Russian oil, gas, coal, fertilizer, and wheat exports. Russia redirected energy sales to India and China. Chinese manufacturers filled the import gap. Former Soviet republics became transshipment hubs for Western technology.

The $60/barrel oil price cap, imposed in late 2022, was initially effective but was soon evaded by Russia’s “shadow fleet” of 180-300+ aging tankers purchased by front companies, stripped of Western insurance, and used to transport crude outside the cap’s jurisdiction. By late 2023, Urals crude was fetching $74/barrel.

The Biden administration eventually tightened the screws. A December 2023 executive order authorized secondary sanctions on banks helping Russia buy military technology. A June 2024 expansion gave OFAC authority to target foreign financial institutions dealing with 4,500+ Russian entities. The G-7 engineered an ingenious compromise on the frozen reserves: a $50 billion loan to Ukraine backed by interest income from the ~€190 billion held at Euroclear in Belgium—using the reserves without technically confiscating them.

Blustein’s assessment is measured: the sanctions sent a “thundererous message of intolerance for Putinesque aggression” and will impose long-term costs through brain drain, foreign investment loss, and technological isolation. But they taught “a humbling lesson about the limitations of the tool when it comes to large economies, especially those that make lots of products that the world needs. The bigger the target, the harder it is to make it fall.”

The chapter’s second half debunks the “Anti-Dollar Axis” narrative that dominated 2022-2023 media coverage. Despite breathless headlines about BRICS currencies and the petroyuan, the data showed no convergence between the dollar and renminbi. RMB use remained confined to bilateral trade with China; its share of global payments stuck at 4.5% and reserves at 2%.

Economist Patrick Chovanec demolished the petroyuan theory: even if Saudi Arabia accepted renminbi for oil, what would it do with the proceeds? Chinese capital markets are “not nearly as large or liquid” as US markets, “permission is required to move large sums in and out,” and China itself would prefer to spend its dollar hoard on Saudi oil rather than use renminbi. The “rupee trap”—Russia accumulating billions in rupees from Indian oil sales that it couldn’t spend ($41.5 billion in exports versus $2.8 billion in imports)—illustrated the practical impossibility of de-dollarization for bilateral trade partners with unbalanced trade. Even Lavrov acknowledged the problem: “We need to use this money. But for this, these rupees must be transferred in another currency.”

Chapter 11: …Comes Great Responsibility

The final chapter is Blustein’s sustained argument for how the United States should steward the dollar’s power. He begins by making the affirmative case: dollar dominance is good for the world because it provides a convenient, trusted medium for international transactions and gives the US economic leverage that is preferable to military force. Dollar dominance doubts “should be put to rest”—based on the historical evidence and alternatives’ lack of credibility. The caveat: “barring catastrophic missteps by the US government.”

But the Spider-Man principle demands restraint. Blustein advances several arguments against cavalier sanctions use. First, humanitarian harm: Francisco Rodríguez’s research shows sanctions produce “consistently statistically significant adverse effects” on poverty, inequality, child mortality, and life expectancy. Iranian patients couldn’t get liver transplant drugs despite having OFAC licenses, because no bank would process the transaction (overcompliance).

Second, diplomatic damage: allies resent bullying (Europe’s fury over Trump’s Iran sanctions; Kenya’s Ruto demanding to know why dollars are used in Djibouti-Kenya trade). Third, collateral damage escalation: when the target can hit back, the stakes multiply exponentially.

The Taiwan scenario crystallizes this last point. A vivid April 2023 tabletop exercise on Capitol Hill simulated a 2027 Chinese invasion. Blue Team (House Select Committee members) faced Red Team (CNAS staffers), including one who warned: “We are going after companies like Apple, Dell, HP. You want a new iPhone? Guess what. You are not going to get it.”

Sanctions similar to the Russia package would devastate China—but China’s GDP is 10 times Russia’s, its four major banks rank among the world’s 30 systemically important institutions, and it dominates one-third of all global manufacturing sectors (two-thirds of lithium-ion batteries, four-fifths of solar panels, 86% of rare-earth minerals, 80% of computers, 67% of smartphones). An Atlantic Council/Rhodium Group study estimated $3 trillion in trade and financial flows at risk—nearly equivalent to the UK’s GDP. Decoupling from Chinese manufacturing? “Maybe in my grandchildren’s lifetimes, not mine,” Blustein writes at age 72.

On digital currency policy, Blustein is blunt: stop linking the dollar’s future to CBDCs or stablecoins. The e-CNY will not become a medium of exchange “in places like Kansas and Idaho.” The most promising path is the BIS vision of tokenized deposits, which preserve the banking system (and therefore sanctions infrastructure), maintain privacy protections, and offer genuine fintech innovation. Crypto should be regulated, not empowered—its original goal of disrupting banks would, if realized, weaken the very compliance infrastructure that makes sanctions possible.

Blustein is scathing about crypto’s capture of American politics. Trump’s 180-degree pivot from “Bitcoin seems like a scam” (2021) to “crypto capital of the planet” (Nashville, July 2024) was driven by millions in campaign contributions, not conviction. The Fairshake PAC raised $194 million by fall 2024—the largest single spending operation targeting anti-crypto politicians regardless of party. JD Vance holds bitcoin and is mentored by Peter Thiel, who told a Bitcoin 2022 conference that “central banks are bankrupt… we are at the end of the fiat money regime.” Trump’s family venture World Liberty Financial was co-founded by individuals with “very questionable business track records.”

On fiscal policy, Blustein rejects two wrong arguments. The “General Jack D. Ripper theory” (from Dr. Strangelove) claims losing dollar status means losing everything—but the dollar doesn’t actually give the US lower borrowing rates (10-year Treasury yields are higher than Japan, France, Germany, Canada, and even Greece). The opposite extreme—“deficits don’t matter”—is also wrong: CBO projects 6.3% of GDP average deficits through 2034 (likely optimistic), and with rates elevated after QT, massive borrowing will crowd out private investment and stunt productivity growth. Fitch downgraded US Treasury debt in August 2023, citing “steady deterioration in standards of governance.”

The book closes by identifying the true existential threats: not foreign rivals, but American self-inflicted wounds. A debt ceiling default. Presidential attacks on Fed independence going further than Trump’s 2018–2020 assaults. Rule of law eroded until international investors see “little difference between the impartiality of US and Chinese courts.” US-China tensions escalating until the world splits into two isolated economic blocs. A White House influenced by crypto ideologues who decide to “upend the fiat money system.”

“If US policy goes in those directions, dollar dominance will be lost or diminished. That will be the least of our worries.”

The final line: “Let us hope that, instead, the future brings ample grounds for raising a toast to the king’s reign: Long may it abide!”

Key Takeaways

Dollar dominance is structural, not decreed

The dollar’s supremacy rests on deep, liquid financial markets ($18T Treasury market), the rule of law, Fed credibility, and powerful network effects—not on any government mandate. Countries use dollars because other countries use dollars, and because there is nowhere else to park trillions of dollars in safe, liquid assets.

No rival is remotely close

The euro lacks fiscal union and a common safe asset. The yen is backed by a demographically declining economy. The renminbi has capital controls, an untrustworthy legal system, and a 4.5% share of global payments. Bitcoin failed as money. CBDCs are technological upgrades that don’t change currency fundamentals. Every few years produces a new round of “dollar is dying” predictions; every time, the data shows the opposite.

Sanctions are powerful but limited

Dollar weaponization brought Iran to the negotiating table (JCPOA) and sent a powerful message after Russia’s invasion of Ukraine. But the Russia case showed that large, commodity-exporting economies can partially absorb the blow—especially when the sanctioning coalition pulls its punches on secondary sanctions and energy carve-outs.

Overuse erodes the weapon

The Trump administration’s indiscriminate sanctions use (Iran withdrawal, Venezuela, ICC prosecutor) alarmed even allies and accelerated modest de-dollarization efforts. The Biden administration’s own review (October 2021) concluded the US had overused sanctions and needed recalibration. Daniel McDowell’s research shows targeted nations are trying their utmost to de-dollarize.

The biggest threat is American self-sabotage

Not China, not BRICS, not crypto: the dollar’s greatest vulnerability is catastrophic policy missteps by the US government—debt ceiling brinksmanship, attacks on Fed independence, erosion of rule of law, fiscal recklessness, and crypto-industry capture of regulatory policy.

The Fed is the dollar’s ultimate guarantor

In every modern crisis (2008, 2020), the Fed’s willingness to act as global lender of last resort—via swap lines and emergency facilities—paradoxically reinforced dollar supremacy. No other central bank can perform this function.

The Treasury market is dangerously fragile

The plumbing of the world’s most important financial market has become dependent on algorithmic traders (who vanish in stress) and hedge funds running 50:1 leveraged basis trades. The March 2020 near-collapse was a “damn close-run thing.”

Contrarian Ideas

Reserve freezing may backfire over time

Conventional wisdom says freezing Russia’s reserves was an unprecedented power play. But Blustein argues that by violating the sovereign immunity principle for central bank reserves, the West may have sent “shivers through finance ministries and central banks” worldwide—accelerating exactly the kind of defensive diversification it sought to prevent.

De-dollarization is real but irrelevant

China has increased the renminbi’s use in bilateral trade. But this is de-dollarization at the margins, not dethronement. RMB use remains confined to China’s bilateral trade partners; there is virtually no “third country trade” in renminbi. The metrics that actually matter (global reserves, global payments, trade invoicing) show no meaningful erosion.

The “exorbitant privilege” is not so exorbitant

Conventional wisdom says dollar dominance gives the US uniquely cheap borrowing. In fact, US 10-year Treasury yields in September 2023 were higher than those of Japan, France, Germany, Canada, Australia, and Greece. The US is not uniquely able to run current account deficits; Australia, Canada, and Britain all ran bigger deficits (as % of GDP) during 2010–2019.

SWIFT expulsion is mostly symbolic

De-SWIFTing Russia was hailed as a “nuclear option,” but Blustein argues it was far less impactful than the reserve freeze. Banks can send messages via email, phone, or fax; SWIFT is a messaging system, not a settlement system. The reserve freeze actually immobilized assets.

McDowell’s paradox: sanctions as a wasting asset

If dollar sanctions power is gradually eroding as countries develop alternatives, the argument for restraint (save the weapon for when you really need it) could equally support urgency (use it now while it’s still effective). Blustein finds this counterargument compelling.

Sanctions hurt the innocent more than the powerful

Rodríguez’s research shows sanctions consistently increase poverty, child mortality, and undernourishment in target countries. Elite targets (oligarchs, autocrats) are better positioned to evade; ordinary citizens bear the brunt. Private-sector overcompliance amplifies the damage—banks refuse legal humanitarian transactions rather than risk any connection to sanctioned entities.

Multipolar currency system might be better

Eichengreen argues that historically, having multiple major international currencies is the norm and is actually preferable: “Just like biodiversity makes for a more robust global ecology, a multipolar system will be more robust.” A world with viable alternatives to the dollar would create more safe havens during crises.

Allegories & Cultural References

Spider-Man: “With Great Power Comes Great Responsibility”

The book’s central organizing metaphor. Blustein uses it in the title of Chapter 1 and the title of Chapter 11, bookending the entire argument. The dollar’s extraordinary power—sanctions, reserve status, lender of last resort—demands proportionate restraint. When used irresponsibly, the curses against King Dollar are justified.

Nellie Bly’s Circumnavigation (1889)

Journalist Nellie Bly (real name: Elizabeth Cochrane) raced around the world in 72 days carrying Bank of England notes and gold—not dollars. Blustein uses this to dramatize how recently the dollar was a provincial irrelevance. Just 135 years ago, it was useless outside US borders.

Nixon Interrupts Bonanza (1971)

Nixon’s announcement of the gold window closure interrupted the popular TV Western. The juxtaposition of a cowboy show and the end of the global monetary order captures the surreal disconnect between ordinary American life and the tectonic shifts in international finance.

Beryl Sprinkel’s Piglet Tails

Treasury Undersecretary Sprinkel told Japanese counterparts a Missouri farm story about cutting piglets’ tails: “We just hacked them off once up at the top!”—urging Japan to liberalize its financial system in one swift move rather than incrementally. Japan chose “stride by stride” and missed the window. Blustein returns to the metaphor later: had Japan listened, the yen might have become a true rival.

Tom Wolfe’s “Masters of the Universe”

Bond traders in 1970s–80s Wall Street, immortalized in Bonfire of the Vanities. Blustein uses the reference to illustrate how the explosive growth of Treasury trading—$10.5 billion per day by 1976, dwarfing stock trading—built the deep, liquid market that makes the dollar indispensable.

Wile E. Coyote Moment

In 2007, with the dollar at all-time lows against the euro, Gisele Bündchen demanding euro payments, and Warren Buffett bearish, the long-predicted dollar collapse seemed imminent—“The Wile E. Coyote moment, it seemed, might finally be at hand.” Instead, the 2008 crisis sent everyone rushing back to the dollar. The cartoon reference captures the repeated pattern: the dollar runs off the cliff but never falls.

The “Sopranos State”

Political scientist Sheena Chestnut coined this term for North Korea—a state running criminal enterprises (counterfeiting, drugs, weapons) like a mafia family. Blustein uses it to frame the BDA sanctions story: Treasury targeted the Kim regime’s illicit finance the way law enforcement targets organized crime.

Quarles’s “Parachute Pants” Speech

Fed vice-chair Randal Quarles compared CBDC enthusiasm to 1980s fashion: Americans have “a penchant for getting carried away by novelty,” leading to “occasionally impetuous, deluded crazes or fads.” The analogy deflated the breathless urgency of the CBDC advocates and became the speech’s shorthand title.

General Jack D. Ripper (from Dr. Strangelove)

Blustein labels the extreme view that losing dollar dominance means losing everything—national security, borrowing capacity, superpower status—the “General Jack D. Ripper theory,” after Kubrick’s paranoid general who believed Communists were poisoning his “precious bodily fluids.” The analogy skewers the hysteria: dollar dominance matters, but losing it wouldn’t be as apocalyptic as the Ripper crowd imagines.

Gisele Bündchen and Jay-Z

In late 2007, the Brazilian supermodel demanded euro payments and Jay-Z flashed €500 notes in a music video. Blustein cites both as cultural markers of peak dollar pessimism—moments when even pop culture was declaring the dollar dead. The dollar then rose 15% during the ensuing crisis.

The Bitcoin Pizza

The famous 2010 transaction where 10,000 bitcoins bought two pizzas. Blustein notes the irony: the bitcoins didn’t even go to a pizzeria—someone in California called Papa John’s and paid by credit card. Even crypto’s foundational origin story depended on the traditional dollar payment system.

Proposals & Frameworks

The Spider-Man Framework for Dollar Stewardship

Blustein’s central argument, woven throughout the book: the dollar’s great power demands proportionate responsibility. Sanctions should only be deployed when (1) core US interests or those of allies are truly at stake, (2) the desired result is achievable, and (3) the action is coordinated with allies. Unilateral, indiscriminate sanctioning—the Trump-era pattern—erodes the weapon’s effectiveness and antagonizes allies.

Tokenized Deposits as the Digital Dollar Path

Rather than a retail CBDC (politically toxic) or stablecoins (volatile, poorly regulated, sanctions-undermining), Blustein endorses the BIS vision: tokenized bank deposits on programmable ledgers, settled in wholesale CBDC, maintaining the banking system as gatekeeper for compliance/sanctions enforcement while enabling smart contracts and real-time settlement.

Sanctions Restraint Doctrine

Drawing on McDowell’s Bucking the Buck: reserve dollar sanctions for “core interests” where the desired result aligns with US foreign policy goals and is reasonably achievable. Coordinate with allies. Accept humanitarian responsibility. Recognize that power used irresponsibly is power that will eventually be circumvented.

Treasury Market Structural Reform

Fix the fragile Treasury market structure that nearly collapsed in March 2020. SEC has approved centralized clearing for Treasury trades; proposals to modify bank regulations to encourage more Treasury holding are under discussion. The moral hazard from Fed rescues of leveraged basis trades demands structural, not just monetary, solutions.

The Three Pillars of Dollar Durability

Blustein’s implicit framework for why the dollar endures: (1) Institutional credibility—Fed independence, rule of law, property rights; (2) Market depth—no alternative offers comparable safe, liquid assets at scale; (3) Network effects—incumbency advantage reinforced by each crisis that the dollar-centered system survives.

A comprehensive examination of the US dollar’s global supremacy—how it rose to dominance, why no rival comes close to challenging it, how the United States has weaponized its centrality through financial sanctions, and the grave responsibilities that accompany such extraordinary power.
