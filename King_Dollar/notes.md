## Chapter 1: With Great Power (pages 13-30+)

### Opening: The New York Clearing House Origin Story
- Book opens with the logistical nightmare of 1850s NYC banking — "porters" carried bundles of checks and gold coins (specie) between banks on foot to settle mutual obligations
- An 1858 account describes "sixty Porters were out at all once" with hundreds of bank drafts, crossing each other's paths in utter confusion — the system was chaotic, error-prone, and dangerous
- George Dummer Lyman, a bank clerk, proposed a clearinghouse system in 1851, modeled on London's Bankers' Clearing House
- The New York Clearing House opened October 11, 1853: 52 banks, oval table about 70 feet long, clerks sitting inside and standing outside — could process all transactions in as little as six minutes
- This evolved into CHIPS (Clearing House Interbank Payments System), now headquartered on the 17th floor of a Manhattan skyscraper, processing 540,000+ transactions worth $1.8 trillion per average day

### The Dollar's Global Role — Key Statistics
- The dollar is the most important currency on the planet, used far more than any other for world payments, invoicing, borrowing/lending, reserves, and as a foreign policy weapon
- After Russia's February 2022 invasion of Ukraine, dollar-based sanctions were the most dramatic measure — cutting Russian banks/businesses from the dollar-based system
- Countries fearing sanctions are trying to build alternative financial mechanisms to bypass the dollar — so far largely ineffective

### CHIPS vs SWIFT vs Fedwire
- CHIPS is the main conduit for large-value international dollar transactions — handles 90%+ of world total, and ~95% of payments going through CHIPS begin or end outside the US
- Fedwire (Fed-owned) handles mostly domestic US interbank transfers
- SWIFT is a messaging system — it doesn't clear or settle transactions
- Key insight: "When the stuff flowing through the pipes is the bulk of international movements in the dollar, and the pipes are located in a jurisdiction subject to US law, foreign banks and even whole governments need to take care lest they lose access to that plumbing. The clout the US government gains as a result is incalculable."

### Lula's Bafflement (April 2023)
- Brazilian president Lula in Shanghai: "Every night I ask myself why all countries have to base their trade on the dollar... Who was it that decided that the dollar was the currency after the disappearance of the gold standard?"
- Blustein explains the currency hierarchy: Brazilian real, Armenian dram, Malaysian ringgit are little used outside their borders. International trade requires "hard" currencies — USD, EUR, JPY, GBP, CNY, CHF

### Dollar Dominance by the Numbers
- US economy = ~25% of global GDP, US trade = ~8% of world trade, yet dollar dominates transactions with no Americans involved
- 75%+ of global trade invoiced in dollars (96% in Western Hemisphere); even South Korea and Australia invoice 80-90% of exports in dollars
- ~60% of official foreign reserves are dollar-denominated (mostly US Treasuries)
- 60% of international loans and deposits are in dollars
- ~70% of international bonds and debt securities are dollar-denominated
- Dollar is used in ~90% of foreign exchange trades
- In FX swaps market: average churn = ~$5 trillion/day (GDP of entire world turned over every 14 days), mostly for hedging not speculation

### Three Functions of Money at the International Level
- Medium of exchange — dollar used for most cross-border trade payments
- Unit of account — dollar used for invoicing globally
- Store of value — dollar-denominated assets (Treasuries) are the primary reserve asset
- "Complementarity" — each function reinforces the others. Exporters invoice in dollars → receive dollar payments → borrow in dollars → trade dollars on FX markets → creates more demand
- "Network effects" — everybody uses dollars because everybody else does (like English as lingua franca)

### Why the Dollar Dominates: Fundamental Factors
- No treaty or accord formalizes dollar dominance (Bretton Woods pact broke down in early 1970s)
- Unrivaled depth, breadth, and liquidity of US financial markets — anyone can buy/sell Treasuries in large quantities without moving prices
- Crucial during financial crises — ability to unload holdings quickly without fire-sale losses
- America's legal/institutional infrastructure: property rights, enforceable contracts, rule of law, government transparency
- Other hard currencies have similar features but each has drawbacks limiting their global role

### Dollar Weaponization History
- Both Democratic and Republican administrations have exploited dollar dominance for national security — fighting terrorism, isolating rogue states
- 2022 against Russia: widely welcomed
- 2018 against Iran: controversial — Trump bullied allies into compliance, threatening to cut off any entity dealing with Tehran
- Foreign governments' "de-dollarization" efforts have generally proven ineffective

### What Makes a Dollar a Dollar? (Deep Dive into Money)
- Money is a "social construct" or "shared fiction" — value depends on willingness to accept it
- Historical examples: shells, beads, wampum, cigarettes in POW camps, paper checks during Irish bank strike (1970)
- The dollar is fiat currency (lost gold backing in 1971) — nobody is threatened with bodily harm for refusing it (unlike Kublai Khan's paper chao)
- But state power crucially underpins dollar confidence:

### Five State Powers Supporting the Dollar
1. **Power to tax** — government accepts dollars for taxes; IRS enforces with fines/imprisonment
2. **Legal tender laws** — "This note is legal tender for all debts, public and private" — courts enforce dollar obligations
3. **Federal Reserve** — mandated to keep prices stable and foster full employment; maintains purchasing power
4. **Public vs private money distinction** — paper bills are "public money" (Federal Reserve Notes); bank deposits are "private money" (created when banks make loans); both are interchangeable at par ($1.00 = $1.00) — this is the "singleness of money"
5. **Reserves/settlement medium** — banks settle with each other using reserves (electronic entries at the Fed, aka "central bank money"); FDIC guarantees deposits up to $250K to prevent panics

### Money Creation
- Banks create private money "as if by magic" when making loans — lending $100M to a company simply credits the company's account with $100M
- This is regulated (banks must have sufficient cushion to absorb losses) but crypto fans find it "especially galling"
- Jon Cunliffe (Bank of England deputy governor, 2021): most people don't understand the public/private money distinction, and the seamless interchangeability between the two "is not an accident — it is due to the credibility of the institutional framework"

### Historical Warning: Currency Dominance is Not Permanent
- Before the dollar: Byzantine solidus, Florentine florin, Venetian ducat, Dutch guilder, Spanish silver peso, British pound sterling — each reigned supreme until issuing state's clout faded
- Doom predictions for the dollar abound: Ruchir Sharma ("A Post-Dollar World Is Coming"), Zoltan Pozsar, Niall Ferguson (warning about China's digital payments), Stanley Druckenmiller (worried about losing reserve status within 15 years)
- Book *De-Dollarization* by Luft and Korin: dollar's current path is "a train wreck in the making"
- Blustein's thesis: these predictions are largely wrong — the dollar's foundations remain far stronger than critics acknowledge

### The Dollar Domestically: State Scaffolding
- The fiat-currency dollar rests on extensive state scaffolding domestically
- But internationally, the scaffolding depends on market forces — the question is how durable those market forces are
- This sets up the book's central investigation

### Book's Thesis and Chapter Roadmap (pages 30-35)
- Blustein uses Spider-Man's adage: "With great power comes great responsibility" — the dollar's power imposes three responsibilities:
  1. **Keep the dollar sound** — maintain its value through monetary/fiscal discipline
  2. **Wield sanctions carefully** — overuse could drive countries to alternatives
  3. **Act as global lender of last resort** — Fed must provide dollar liquidity in crises (as it did in 2008 and 2020 with swap lines)
- These responsibilities form the book's organizing framework
- Chapter roadmap: Ch 2-3 (history), Ch 4 (rivals: euro, yuan, etc.), Ch 5-6 (sanctions/weaponization), Ch 7-8 (crypto/CBDCs), Ch 9-11 (current threats and future)

## Chapter 2: From Humble Origins to King of Currencies (pages ~36-60+)

### Nellie Bly's 1889 World Tour — The Dollar Was Nothing
- Journalist Nellie Bly (real name: Elizabeth Cochrane) set out to beat Jules Verne's fictional 80-day circumnavigation in November 1889
- She carried Bank of England notes and gold — NOT dollars — because dollars were virtually useless outside the US
- Key insight: just 135 years ago, the dollar had zero international standing; its rise to dominance was not inevitable

### America's Primitive Banking System Before the Civil War
- Early US had no national currency — banks issued their own notes, creating chaos
- "Free Banking Era" (1837-1863): states chartered banks freely; many were "wildcat banks" (set up in remote areas so nobody could redeem notes)
- Bank notes traded at varying discounts depending on distance from issuing bank and perceived soundness
- Alexander Hamilton wanted a strong central bank (First Bank of the United States, 1791); Andrew Jackson killed the Second Bank in 1836, viewing it as elitist tyranny
- Result: America had no central bank from 1836 to 1913 — the only major economy without one

### Civil War: Birth of a National Currency
- Union desperately needed money; Treasury Secretary Salmon Chase created "greenbacks" — US Notes printed with green ink on the back
- Legal Tender Act (1862): greenbacks declared legal tender, but NOT convertible to gold
- Greenbacks depreciated significantly during the war (at worst, $1 greenback = $0.35 in gold)
- National Banking Act (1863): created nationally chartered banks that could issue uniform national bank notes, gradually driving state-issued notes out

### The Gold Standard Era
- After Civil War, US moved toward full gold convertibility
- Gold Standard Act of 1900 formally pegged dollar at $20.67 per troy ounce
- Gold standard gave international credibility — any country on gold had an automatic exchange rate with every other gold-standard country
- But the dollar still wasn't dominant — the British pound sterling ruled international finance
- London was the world's financial center; Bank of England was the linchpin of the global monetary system

### Bank of England and the "Lender of Last Resort" Concept
- Overend, Gurney & Co. collapse (May 1866): one of London's biggest discount houses failed, causing a massive panic
- Bank of England initially refused to help — 200+ companies collapsed in the aftermath
- Walter Bagehot, editor of *The Economist*, wrote *Lombard Street* (1873): argued the central bank MUST lend freely in a crisis, at high rates, against good collateral — "lender of last resort"
- This became the foundational principle of modern central banking
- America had NO such institution — making it uniquely vulnerable to panics

### JP Morgan and the Panic of 1907
- Without a central bank, the US relied on private individuals to stem financial panics
- JP Morgan personally organized a rescue in the Panic of 1907 — summoning bankers to his library, locking the doors, forcing them to pledge money to bail out failing institutions
- The spectacle of one private citizen saving the financial system was embarrassing; it spurred creation of the Federal Reserve
- Federal Reserve Act signed December 23, 1913 — gave America its first permanent central bank

### World War I: The Tipping Point
- Before WWI, British pound was the undisputed king of international currencies
- WWI devastated European economies — Britain borrowed massively, including from the US
- The US emerged as the world's largest creditor nation
- New York began rivaling London as a financial center
- The dollar started its ascent, though the pound remained dominant through the interwar period

### Bretton Woods Conference (July 1944)
- 730 delegates from 44 nations gathered at the Mount Washington Hotel in Bretton Woods, New Hampshire
- John Maynard Keynes (Britain) vs. Harry Dexter White (US) — the two architects of the postwar monetary order
- Keynes proposed "bancor" — a new supranational currency for international settlements; would prevent any single country from dominating
- White insisted on the dollar — backed by America's economic supremacy and gold reserves
- White won: the Bretton Woods system pegged all currencies to the dollar, and the dollar to gold at $35/oz
- Created the IMF (International Monetary Fund) and World Bank
- This formally enthroned the dollar as the world's reserve currency

### Keynes vs White: The Bancor Debate (pages 51-53)
- In two years of pre-Bretton Woods negotiations, Keynes and White agreed on basics: fixed but adjustable exchange rates, limited capital mobility
- Keynes's plan was far more ambitious — a new supranational currency called "bancor," issued by an International Clearing Union
- Bancor wouldn't be spendable by individuals — only a unit for central banks to settle cross-border transactions
- Each country would get bancors proportional to its share of world trade; exports would earn bancors, imports would cost bancors
- Key insight: Keynes himself called it "complicated and novel and perhaps Utopian"
- The plan was DOA — shelved before delegates even arrived at Bretton Woods because it "contravened the interests of the United States"
- White wanted the dollar to be "as good as gold"; the US held the whip hand since its GDP comprised the bulk of postwar world output — no global monetary system would work without American participation

### Bretton Woods System in Practice (pages 53-54)
- White's approach won: IMF created (no bancor, no Clearing Union), system of fixed exchange rates pegged to the dollar
- All IMF member currencies set "par values" against the dollar (e.g., Netherlands: 37.69 cents/guilder, Mexico: 20.6 cents/peso)
- Exchange rates had to stay within narrow bands; if they moved >1%, countries had to intervene (buy/sell currency)
- Countries in trouble could borrow dollars from the IMF to prop up their currencies
- US commitment: dollar pegged to gold at $35/oz — but this was a "quasi-gold standard" (only official entities could convert, not private citizens)

### Robert Triffin and the Triffin Dilemma (pages 54-56)
- Robert Triffin, Belgian-born economist (butcher's son, first in family to attend university), earned PhD at Harvard
- Passionate believer in international cooperation; favored a global currency like Keynes's bancor
- In the late 1950s at Yale, Triffin identified the fatal flaw in Bretton Woods — published *Gold and the Dollar Crisis* (1960)
- Read closely by JFK; Triffin testified to Congress calling for "fundamental reform" to avert an "imminent threat to the once mighty US dollar"
- The system worked in the 1950s because US manufacturers dominated global markets — massive demand for dollars to buy American goods
- Marshall Plan: US provided ~$13.5 billion in aid to Europe + hundreds of millions to Japan (>5% of US GDP, equivalent to ~$1.25 trillion today)
- US held three-quarters of world gold at Fort Knox — dollar's gold-backing appeared solid
- **The dilemma**: As Europe/Japan recovered and grew more competitive, they needed fewer dollars; meanwhile Americans were buying more imports and spending on Cold War military/foreign aid → "dollar glut" formed
- Too few dollars → global trade starved of the currency needed for cross-border commerce → stagnation
- Too many dollars → faith in the $35/oz gold convertibility undermined → confidence crisis
- "Sooner or later something had to give"

### "Exorbitant Privilege" and French Resentment (pages 56-57)
- Term coined in mid-1960s by Valéry Giscard d'Estaing (French finance minister): the US uniquely enjoyed "autonomy" — freedom from external constraints
- US could run "deficits without tears" (Jacques Rueff, adviser to de Gaulle)
- De Gaulle himself held a 1965 press conference demanding return to gold standard — international commerce "should rest... on an indisputable monetary basis... gold that has no nationality"
- **Balance of payments explained**: every country has inflows (exports, capital) vs outflows (imports, spending abroad); sustained deficits require painful adjustment — austerity, spending cuts, tax hikes
- But the US was exempt: since the dollar was THE international currency and dollar creation was at the Fed's whim, America didn't face the same constraint
- France had to ensure enough dollar reserves to defend the franc-dollar rate; the US had no such worry

### Bretton Woods Collapses (pages 57-60)
- Gold price spiked to $40/oz on the London market in October 1960 — alarm bells rang
- Fed + European central banks spent the 1960s trying to buttress the $35 peg: gold pooling, currency swap arrangements
- US pressured Germany and Japan (who depended on US nuclear umbrella) to NOT follow France in converting dollars to gold
- By late 1960s, measures were failing: LBJ's guns-and-butter spending (Vietnam + Great Society) without adequate taxes → inflation (CPI rose >4% in 1968, nearly 5.5% in 1969)
- Foreign central banks bought up excess greenbacks; US gold reserves dwindled to half the level of a decade earlier
- By 1969, speculators bid gold above $43/oz

### Paul Volcker and the Camp David Weekend (pages 59-61)
- Volcker, appointed Treasury undersecretary by Nixon in 1969 (a Democrat serving a Republican), had spent his career defending Bretton Woods stability
- But by 1971 the strains were acute: imports exceeded exports for the first time in the postwar era; US gold reserves covered only 25% of foreign-dollar liabilities (down from 80% under Kennedy)
- "None of us were ready to simply abandon the Bretton Woods framework" — Volcker's 2018 memoir
- **Camp David, August 13-15, 1971**: 15 senior US economic policymakers summoned in secret — "Don't tell your wife where you're going, or your secretary either"
- Nixon: "There are to be no calls out of here... Everyone here is to button his lip"
- Treasury Secretary John Connally laid out the plan, including: "We close the gold window" — stop converting dollars to gold at $35/oz
- Connally: "Anybody can topple us, anytime they want... We have left ourselves completely exposed"
- Arthur Burns (Fed chair) agonized — "What a tragedy for mankind!" — but ultimately gave "wholehearted support"
- Burns worried about losing "goodwill" of allies, especially with the 10% import tariff also being considered
- Connally's famous bluster: "So the other countries don't like it. So what?... Let 'em. What can they do?"

### Nixon's Sunday Night Address (pages 61-62)
- Nixon interrupted the TV show *Bonanza* to announce: import surcharge, temporary wage/price controls, and "suspension" of dollar-gold convertibility
- "I am determined that the American dollar must never again be a hostage in the hands of international speculators"
- Efforts to build a new fixed-rate system followed but failed; by spring 1973, all major currencies were floating freely
- Connally's immortal line to foreign finance ministers during negotiations: "The dollar is our currency, but your problem"
- **Bretton Woods was kaput** — the dollar became a pure fiat currency, supply governed solely by the Fed

### Post-Bretton Woods: Dollar as Pure Fiat (pages 62-63)
- How the Fed creates/destroys money: buys Treasury bills from banks → credits their reserve accounts (creates dollars); sells bonds → absorbs dollars (destroys them)
- Goal: calibrate money supply to produce interest rates fostering growth without inflation — too many dollars = inflation, too few = recession
- Capital mobility restrictions also loosened — 1970s opened floodgates for transnational capital flows
- Dollar initially tanked: by July 1973, fallen 31% vs mark, 26% vs yen, 23% vs Swiss franc
- Leonard Silk (NYT economics commentator, July 1973): "The dollar is regarded all over the world as a sick currency"
- Charles Kindleberger (MIT) decried "the Crime of 1971" and declared: "The dollar is finished as international money"
- Blustein: "That was an early example of the dollar's death being greatly exaggerated"

## Chapter 3: Maintaining the Throne, Through Thick and Thin (pages ~65-90+)

### Opening: The Saudi Arabian Monetary Agency (SAMA)
- Opens with a vivid scene: a "dilapidated, fly-infested building" in Jeddah was the repository for the world's most spectacular dollar cache during the 1973-74 oil shock
- SAMA (Saudi central bank) was primitive: leather-bound ledgers kept by hand, open-drain toilet flushed once daily, newspapers arrived days late after censorship, no phone calls abroad — only a single telex machine in a tiny triangular room
- David Mulford (hired from investment banking as SAMA adviser, 1975): described waiting 10-20 minutes for the telex to connect to New York/London/Tokyo
- Despite this primitiveness, SAMA was managing an influx of cash "the likes of which no country had ever seen" — $100 million/day in oil revenue
- Oil prices quadrupled: from <$3/barrel in early 1970s to >$11/barrel by March 1974 (OPEC + Arab embargo after Israel's October 1973 war with Egypt/Syria)

### Why Saudi Arabia Stayed in Dollars (pages 67-69)
- Dollar was facing its first major post-Bretton Woods challenge: inflation eroding its value (CPI: 11% in 1974; remained 5-8% through 1978)
- US trade balance turning negative; federal budget deficits deepening
- From March 1973 to October 1978: dollar depreciated 34% vs mark, 30% vs yen, 51% vs Swiss franc
- Yet SAMA kept the overwhelming bulk of holdings in dollar-denominated securities — WHY?
- **Reason 1: Logistical constraints** — "We had a small team totaling only six professionals confronted by a mountain of work" (Mulford); portfolio growing $5-10 billion every 30 days
- **Reason 2: Liquidity** — in most non-US markets, a currency trade of $5-10 million was enough to move prices; German Schuldschein, Japanese yen bonds, Dutch guilder bonds "were just not possible in the sizes common in the U.S. market"
- By end of 1977, only ~6% of SAMA's $60 billion reserves was in non-dollar currencies
- "There could hardly be a simpler and more straightforward illustration of the role that liquidity plays in the dollar's hegemonic status"

### The Petrodollar Deal (pages 67-68)
- Secretary of State Kissinger + Treasury Secretary William Simon launched efforts in 1974 to keep Saudi money in dollars
- Simon traveled to Saudi Arabia and negotiated a secret deal: SAMA could buy Treasury securities directly (bypassing public auctions), at the average auction interest rate, in exchange for promising substantial dollar investments
- Confidentiality was the "sine qua non" — a Treasury memo: "we have assured them that we will do everything in our power to comply with their desires"
- Kissinger exploited Saudi insecurity about foreign/domestic enemies: "joint commissions" in economics and defense → $100+ billion in US weapons purchases, training of Saudi armed forces, US Corps of Engineers building military bases on Saudi soil
- Often portrayed as cynical bargain: US military protection for Saudi oilfields in exchange for dollar loyalty — but Blustein says logistical/liquidity reasons mattered more than any formal deal

### The Bond Market Boom (pages 69-72)
- While SAMA was putting money into Treasuries, US bond markets were exploding in size and activity
- 1960s Wall Street had been "dull for bonds" — *Institutional Investor* ran a 1969 cover story with dinosaurs: "Can the bond market survive?"
- By mid-1970s: Treasury bond trading volume averaged $10.5 billion/day in 1976 — triple the pace of 1973, and far exceeding the $631 million daily NYSE stock trading volume
- Salaries and bonuses for bond traders skyrocketing; banks raided by Wall Street firms offering six-figure packages to traders in their twenties
- Trading floors at Salomon Brothers, Paine Webber, Aubrey Lanston — traders shouted across rows of desks, "barked over the phone with counterparts at rival firms"
- Tom Wolfe's *Bonfire of the Vanities* (1987): bond traders as "masters of the universe"
- **Why bonds boomed**: (1) massive federal deficits → Treasury flooding market with new IOUs, (2) computerization bringing efficiency to OTC bond trading, (3) interest rate volatility creating huge profit opportunities
- Bond spreads were razor-thin (1/128 to 1/32 of 1%) but on $100 million orders, even 1/128 = $7,812.50 commission
- Leverage: a dealer with $25 million capital might hold 20-100x that in bonds ($500M-$2.5B), borrowing from banks/money managers
- Key insight: this massive, liquid bond market gave dollar holders worldwide confidence they could always buy, sell, and park assets in the world's safest instruments

### "Petrodollar Recycling" (pages 72-73)
- Oil payments created a global dollar circulation loop: dollars paid for oil → flowed to SAMA/Gulf states → deposited in large banks → banks lent to borrowers worldwide (including developing countries needing dollars to buy oil)
- Eurodollar market (centered in London): banks of many nationalities accepting dollar deposits and making dollar loans globally
- Billions in loans went to Latin America and elsewhere — "would fall seriously into debt difficulties in the 1980s, but that's another story"
- Even without Bretton Woods, the dollar remained "perched high atop the international currency totem pole"
- Susan Strange (British scholar): closing the gold window was "a progression from exorbitant privilege to super-exorbitant privilege" — the US government could print money that others could not refuse to accept

### The Fed's Failure and Dollar Crisis (pages 73-74)
- By late 1970s, dollar's "unconstrained printing" was testing the patience of the Saudis and other holders
- Arthur Burns (Fed chair 1970-78): presented a stern anti-inflation face but "shrank from raising interest rates high enough" — yielded to Nixon's pressure for easy money before the 1972 election
- Burns admitted political pressure made him cave: "Every time the Fed squeezed credit... it repeatedly evoked violent criticism from both the executive establishment and the Congress"
- Burns's successor William Miller (former CEO of Textron) lasted only 17 months — widely regarded as ineffective
- Miller treated the FOMC like a corporate board — used an egg timer, limited remarks, sought quickest consensus between "hawks" (tighten credit) and "doves" (easier credit)

### 1979 Crisis: Dollar Collapse (pages 71-72)
- January 1979 Islamic Revolution in Iran → second oil shock → crude prices soared again
- US inflation jumped to 10.75% annual rate in first nine months of 1979; dollar plummeted anew
- OPEC countries rumbled about moving out of dollars — but SAMA non-dollar assets "never exceeded 15 to 20 percent of total holdings" (Mulford)
- Even with new Riyadh headquarters and a "wonderfully equipped trading room," currency diversification was "a constant priority" but logistically limited
- By fall 1979: gold leaped from ~$200/oz to >$400/oz; investors fled to precious metals, real estate, art — anything tangible
- At IMF/World Bank annual meeting in Belgrade, Yugoslavia, officials implored US for drastic action

### Volcker's Revolution (pages 72-76)
- Paul Volcker didn't drink much, but on October 5, 1979 (his second month as Fed chair), two associates talked him into wine at Chez Camille in Washington
- Volcker had seemed "so tired and troubled that frankly, we were worried about him" (Frederick Schultz, Fed vice chair)
- When Senator Alan Cranston entered the restaurant, Volcker's smile froze: "In view of what we're about to do tomorrow, we'd better be careful about the levity"
- **The "Saturday Night Special" (October 6, 1979)**: Volcker summoned an emergency FOMC meeting
- Radical new approach: instead of directly setting the federal funds rate, the Fed would target money supply growth (M1 — currency + checking deposits)
- This would cause interest rates to swing violently — but Volcker knew it would signal iron resolve
- Not a true "monetarist" — but the approach would shield the Fed from political blame ("we're not setting rates, we're controlling money supply")
- **Volcker's political genius within the FOMC**: the chair controls the agenda, directs staff, and derives power from "institutional loyalty and collegiality" — frequent dissenters "become isolated and lose influence"
- FOMC voted unanimously; doves insisted only on assurance that rates could go down as well as up

### The Volcker Shock's Toll (pages 76-78)
- Borrowing costs soared: prime rate jumped 6 percentage points in six months
- Fall 1980: despite approaching presidential election, Volcker raised the discount rate a full percentage point on September 25 — White House aides were "furious"; Carter blasted Fed for "ill-advised" policies but was powerless
- Reagan came in favoring slow money growth, but relations soured as tight credit persisted
- Fed policy became "a game of guts ball" — probing limits of society's tolerance for unemployment to break the inflation spiral
- Interest rates stayed high despite howls: auto dealers mailed the Fed keys to unsold cars; farmers circled headquarters on tractors
- **October 1982**: Volcker finally relented with "the doom and gloom speech" — acknowledged parallels to 1929; FOMC voted 9-3 for a more flexible, stimulative policy
- **Confrontation with Reagan team continued**: May 1983, Fed tightened again (Lyle Gramley correctly predicted stronger-than-expected recovery); December 1983, administration publicly called for easing — Volcker tightened instead
- Result: "Morning in America" — unemployment down, inflation down, interest rates declined. Perfect conditions for Reagan's 1984 reelection

### Solving One Problem, Creating Another (pages 78-80)
- Volcker's conquest of inflation imparted "sanctity to a foundational principle of the dollar's international status — the independence of America's central bank"
- But a new problem: the strong dollar. High yields + low inflation made Treasuries irresistible → dollar soared ~30% vs yen, 60%+ vs mark in first half of 1980s
- Strong dollar was "strength with a vengeance" — US exporters couldn't compete
- Lee Morgan (CEO, Caterpillar Tractor): led lobbying campaign citing dollar-yen rate as the reason for Caterpillar's loss of sales to Komatsu; 15,000 layoffs; resonated on Capitol Hill during Rust Belt unemployment crisis
- Stephen Marris (British economist, Institute for International Economics): kept retirement savings in German marks, "ready to be converted into pounds to buy a 34-foot cruising catamaran"
- Marris's 1985 book *Deficits and the Dollar: The World Economy at Risk*: "A hard landing has become inevitable for the dollar and the world economy"
- Current account deficit by 1985 was ~$120 billion (~3% of GDP) — US economy dependent on foreign capital to an unprecedented degree

### The Plaza Accord (September 1985) (pages 80-82)
- Treasury Secretary James Baker and deputy Richard Darman recognized the trading system could collapse if the dollar kept rising
- David Mulford (now Treasury assistant secretary for international affairs) dispatched to negotiate secretly with G-5 counterparts (Japan, West Germany, Britain, France)
- Clandestine meetings: Mulford distributed a US draft communiqué at the start of sessions and collected it at the end — no copies to leak
- September 22, 1985: "the world's best-kept secret" unveiled at New York's Plaza Hotel
- **Plaza Accord**: G-5 agreed to coordinated effort to push dollar down — "orderly appreciation of the main non-dollar currencies against the dollar" (word "orderly" insisted upon by Volcker)
- Central banks unloaded billions of dollars; Japan went furthest with plans for substantive policy changes
- Result: by end of October 1985, dollar down ~13% vs yen, 10%+ vs mark; over two years, total depreciation ~40% vs yen, 36% vs mark
- Helped rebalance US trade somewhat; current account deficit shrank in late 1980s/early 1990s

### "Declinist" Sentiment and the 1990s-2000s (pages 82-84)
- Despite Plaza Accord relief, "declinist" sentiment grew — especially after US foreign liabilities surpassed claims in 1986 (US became "debtor nation" instead of "creditor nation")
- Diane Kunz (Yale, *Foreign Affairs*, 1995): "The Fall of the Dollar Order: The World the United States Is Losing"
- *The Economist*: "the dollar's dominance is waning"
- Blustein: "That was only a foretaste of what was to come"

### China and "Bretton Woods II" (pages 84-87)
- October 1, 2004: Jin Renqing (China's finance minister) arrives at the Treasury building for first-ever Chinese invitation to a G-7 dinner — but goes to the wrong entrance; Secret Service blocks him until Treasury Secretary Snow personally escorts him in
- China's economic rise created enormous US-China trade imbalance — US imports exceeded exports by far more than in the 1980s
- Current account deficit peaked at ~$600 billion by 2004 (~6% of GDP, vs 3.4% peak in 1987)
- Asian nations ran massive surpluses (South Korea, Japan, China, Taiwan)
- **"Bretton Woods II"**: term used by observers who believed the US-Asian co-dependency was sustainable — Americans consumed, Asians exported and invested their dollar earnings in Treasuries/Fannie Mae/Freddie Mac, keeping US interest rates low → housing boom
- "Privilege at its most exorbitant" — foreigners handed Americans pieces of paper with dollar signs; US government and agencies printed voluminously; Americans lived well on low-cost products and low-interest loans
- Personal savings rate fell to ~0% by 2005 and stayed there for three years

### China's Renminbi Peg (pages 86-87)
- Renminbi fixed at 8.28 per dollar since 1994; China refused to let it appreciate even as surplus surged to >7% of GDP by 2005
- Feared unemployment in low-margin export sector if currency rose
- Asian countries also hoarded dollar reserves — memory of 1990s crises when countries ran short of dollars and faced humiliating IMF bailouts
- China's foreign reserves: quintupled from 2002 to $1.7 trillion by 2007
- China allowed renminbi to rise a little in 2005, but "only a little"
- US options limited — unilateral tariffs would violate trade rules and risk economic warfare

### The Doom Chorus (pages 87-88)
- Kenneth Rogoff (IMF chief economist): "The world is set to jump off the top of a waterfall without knowing how deep the water is below"; projected dollar must decline ~35%
- William Cline (*Washington Post*): "Sooner or later, the rest of the world will decide that the United States is no longer a safe bet for lending more money"
- Nouriel Roubini (NYU, February 2005): "There is a meaningful risk the Bretton Woods 2 system will unravel before the end of 2006" — predicted sharp dollar fall, rising rates, asset price collapse, global recession — earned nickname "Dr. Doom"

### The Trans-Atlantic Dollar Bomb (pages 88-89)
- While everyone focused on the Pacific imbalance, a "potentially even more pernicious" flow was crossing the Atlantic
- European banks (London, Frankfurt, Zurich — and German *Landesbanken*) poured hundreds of billions into US mortgage-backed securities, attracted by high yields
- Since they bought dollar-based assets but had no dollar deposits, they borrowed dollars short-term — from US banks or by issuing dollar commercial paper (30-180 day IOUs sold to US money market funds)
- Same model as Bear Stearns and Lehman Brothers: "funding real estate gambles by borrowing short term"
- Late 2007: Gisele Bündchen (Brazilian supermodel) wanted to be paid in euros instead of dollars — dollar hitting all-time lows vs euro and Canadian dollar
- Warren Buffett and Bill Gross (PIMCO) expressed bearish dollar sentiment
- "The Wile E. Coyote moment, it seemed, might finally be at hand"

### The Great Paradox: 2008 Crisis (pages 89-91)
- **The crisis was "Made in America" — yet the dollar ROSE 15% (April 2008 to March 2009)** against an inflation-adjusted index of other currencies
- "Call this counterintuitive, call it paradoxical, call it unfair, call it perverse, but the 'safe haven' became the currency of the country where the epicenter of the crisis was located"
- Same reason as SAMA in the 1970s: **liquidity** — Treasuries were the only investment anyone could buy or sell in massive quantities without moving prices
- Financial crisis = liquidity evaporates; markets "seize up"; everyone hoards the most liquid assets → Treasuries

### The Fed as Global Lender of Last Resort (pages 91-93)
- European banks had $2+ trillion in dollar-denominated liabilities — they'd borrowed roughly that amount to make what they thought were lucrative mortgage bets
- When markets seized up in 2007-2008, these banks desperately needed dollars to pay obligations
- ECB, Bank of England, Swiss National Bank could only print euros, pounds, Swiss francs — NOT dollars
- **Fed swap lines**: December 12, 2007 — Fed announced currency swaps with ECB, BOE, Swiss National Bank, Bank of Canada
- Mechanics: Fed sends dollars to foreign central bank (e.g., $5-10 billion to ECB) in exchange for equivalent in euros, for a fixed period (e.g., 90 days); foreign central bank lends dollars to its starving banks
- After Lehman (September 2008): swap lines expanded dramatically — total surpassed $500 billion
- More countries added: Australia, Denmark, Norway, Sweden, New Zealand
- **Four central banks (ECB, BOE, BOJ, SNB) got UNLIMITED swap authorization**
- Fed's alphabet soup of domestic liquidity programs: TAF, PDCF, TSLF, TALF, CPFF — all ran into the trillions
- **The dirty little secret**: foreign institutions (Barclays, RBS, Deutsche Bank, Dresdner Bank, West LB, UBS, Credit Suisse, Dexia) were absorbing "a majority of the funds on offer" — revealed only in 2010-2011 after congressional legislation and a Bloomberg FOIA lawsuit that went to the Supreme Court
- Fed kept it secret because Bernanke knew congressional controversy would erupt
- **Not illegal or improper**: foreign banks' US branches were legally domestic institutions; and it was in US self-interest to prevent a global firestorm from a foreign megabank default
- Fed followed Bagehot's principles: lender of last resort lends at interest, against good collateral — not giving money away

### Emerging Market Swap Lines and FOMC Debate (pages 91-93)
- Late October 2008: Fed agreed to swap lines with four "systemically important" emerging markets — Brazil, Mexico, Singapore, South Korea — $30 billion limit each
- FOMC pushback: Charles Plosser (Philadelphia Fed): "I don't know where we draw the lines" — others asked if any requests had been rejected (yes — some countries lacked "systemic importance")
- Plosser suggested IMF should handle this; IMF was reportedly planning a new type of liquidity loan
- Rebuttal from Bernanke and Nathan Sheets (Fed's top international staffer): IMF lacked the financial clout — its lending capacity was ~$250 billion; the $120 billion in swaps to four emerging markets alone was "essentially half of what the IMF could do"
- Timothy Geithner (NY Fed president): "The privilege of being the reserve currency of the world comes with some burdens... we have an interest in helping these countries mitigate the problems they face"
- Key insight: the Fed was the ONLY institution that could print dollars and funnel them where needed — this was an unprecedented role as international lender of last resort
- Plosser acquiesced, but the question of "where do we draw the lines" remains profoundly vexing — what happens with an "America First" president? What if the Fed is skittish about helping foreigners next time?

### Post-Crisis Backlash Against the Dollar (pages 93-95)
- Zhou Xiaochuan (People's Bank of China governor, March 2009): essay calling for international reserve currency "disconnected from economic conditions and sovereign interests of any single country" — reviving Keynes's bancor idea via SDRs
- UN commission of experts agreed: "A global reserve currency... is an idea whose time has come"
- European leaders showed "scant regard for the Fed's role in saving their banks" — vowed to fundamentally alter the monetary hierarchy
- Peer Steinbrück (German finance minister): "The United States will lose its superpower status in the world financial system"
- Nicolas Sarkozy (French president, November 2008): "I am leaving tomorrow for Washington to explain that the dollar... can no longer claim its lofty status. What was true in 1945 cannot be true today"
- Scholars: Jonathan Kirshner (Cornell) — "the financial crisis of 2008 has further weakened the dollar's footing"; Antonio Mosconi (Einstein Centre) — "the last convulsion of the international role of the dollar"

### "Exorbitant Privilege" vs "Exorbitant Burden" (pages 95-96)
- Michael Pettis (Peking University): dollar's preeminence was actually a BURDEN — "Americans were the ones who absorbed the bulk of both the excess capital inflows and the excess manufactured goods from the rest of the world... The consequences were the housing debt bubble and a displaced manufacturing base"
- Blustein's rebuttal: this is "more than a trifle overstated" — housing bubble would have happened regardless (unscrupulous lenders, Wall Street CDOs, complicit ratings agencies existed independently); foreign capital reduced US interest rates by only 0.5-0.9 percentage points; manufacturing decline was global (even Germany) due to automation/productivity
- But: the dollar's primacy DID make the crisis worse — US interest rates were lower, credit was more reckless, markets were less effective at disciplining excesses
- "This much is indisputable: the crisis laid bare serious drawbacks in a system dominated by a single currency. But what was the alternative?"
- Chapter 3 closes: four "pretenders to the throne" examined next — each suffered shortcomings "which sometimes materialized so rapidly as to leave their cheerleaders red-faced"

## Chapter 4: Pretenders to the Throne (pages ~98-145+)

### Pretender #1: The Euro

#### Blustein's 2002 Athens Trip (pages 98-100)
- Greeks were delighted with the euro in 2002 — drachma notes gone, replaced by same currency as Germany, France, Netherlands
- Dimitri Papalexopoulos (building-materials executive): pre-euro Greece had 17% average annual inflation over two decades, no long-term fixed interest rates (only one-year rates), <1% real growth since 1980
- Asked employees about "hurdle rate" for investments: "they asked me, 'what do you mean?'" — long-term project financing in drachma was impossible
- In the eurozone: "As unbelievable as it sounds to Greek ears, we have a cost of capital roughly equal to that of the US" — sparked explosion in business lending, mortgages, home buying
- "With the benefit of hindsight, we know that Greece would be the first of several countries to be stricken by a crisis that nearly ripped the eurozone apart"

#### Why the Euro Was Created (pages 100-101)
- Europe wanted a currency matching its economic heft; single market integration was impeded by multiple currencies with fluctuating exchange rates
- From European Coal and Steel Community → EEC → EU — customs/tariff barriers fell but currency risk remained
- A monetary union would eliminate exchange-rate uncertainty at a stroke

#### The Euro-Skeptics' Warning (pages 101-102)
- Major economists opposed: Milton Friedman, Martin Feldstein (Harvard), Rüdiger Dornbusch (MIT), Paul Krugman
- Core argument: joining a currency union means surrendering control over money supply, interest rates, and exchange rate — enormously important economic tools
- **The US comparison**: common currency works in America partly because of labor mobility — if one region slumps, workers move to booming areas; in Europe, people are "much more loath to seek work by moving many hundreds of miles away, where they may not be able to communicate with their neighbors"
- If a European country slumped while others boomed, it would have "very limited ways of generating recovery" without its own monetary policy
- Maastricht Treaty (1992): convergence criteria (low inflation, low rates, deficits <3% GDP) — skeptics said rules were insufficient
- Stability and Growth Pact (1997): threatened sanctions for violators — naysayers said rules "didn't cover enough crucial areas, and wouldn't be enforced anyway"

#### Euro-Optimists and Early Success (pages 102-103)
- Fred Bergsten (1997): predicted euro would reach "full parity" with dollar in 5-10 years — "The euro's rise will convert an international monetary system that has been dominated by the dollar since World War II into a bipolar regime"
- Robert Mundell (Nobel laureate): "the introduction of the euro will challenge the status of the dollar and alter the power configuration of the system"
- Early 2000s: markets increasingly treated eurozone member bonds as near-identical in creditworthiness
- By 2007: Greek 10-year bonds paying 4.29% vs Germany's 4.02% — a gap of just 0.27 percentage points
- Euro's 10th anniversary (late 2008/early 2009): celebrations coincided with dollar taking blame for the Global Financial Crisis
- Lucas Papademos (ECB vice president): "The euro has been a resounding success"
- *Wall Street Journal* editorial: "The single European currency... is a rare economic shining star of the past decade"
- Even British commentators wondered about ditching the pound for the euro

#### Euro at Its Peak (pages 103-104)
- By its first decade: euro held 25% of global foreign exchange reserves (vs ~65% for dollar)
- Eurozone output exceeded 16% of global GDP; ECB under Trichet was respected; low inflation; sophisticated financial markets with transaction costs rivaling the US
- Menzie Chinn & Jeffrey Frankel (2008 paper): "there now exists a credible rival for lead international currency, the euro... the euro could overtake the dollar as early as 2015"
- Parag Khanna (*NYT Magazine*, "Waving Goodbye to Hegemony"): Gulf exporters diversifying into euros, Ahmadinejad calling dollars "worthless," Chávez suggesting euros, "Gisele Bündchen demands to be paid in euros, while Jay-Z drowns in 500 euro notes in a recent video"
- "Within months of the euro's tenth anniversary, however, its major flaws would become glaringly evident"

#### The Eurozone Crisis (pages 104-108)
- Greece had borrowed €300+ billion (~100% of GDP) at low rates while skewing statistics
- **Intra-eurozone trade imbalances**: Germany surplus 6.1% of GDP (2006), Netherlands 8.2%; Greece/Spain/Portugal deficits 8-11% of GDP
- Northern surplus countries' capital flowed south, funding consumption/housing binges — German regional banks (*Landesbanken*) were among the most enthusiastic funders
- **October 2009 revelation**: Greece's budget deficit would be 12.5% of GDP — triple previous estimates
- **Greece's vicious cycle**: couldn't spend its way out (would worsen debt fears); couldn't cut taxes (would reduce revenue); couldn't devalue currency or pump money supply (ECB controlled those); caught in a debt trap
- By spring 2010: default and eurozone exit became subjects of serious speculation
- **May 7-9, 2010 marathon**: €110 billion bailout, overriding the Maastricht Treaty's "no bailout clause"; Greece accepted savage austerity (spending cuts, tax hikes, structural reforms); a "firewall" stabilization fund created; ECB began selectively buying bonds
- **Ireland — "Black Thursday" (September 30, 2010)**: Finance Minister Brian Lenihan revealed Anglo Irish losses were far worse than known; two-year bank guarantee meant the budget deficit would swell to 32% of GDP; debt-to-GDP ratio projected to leap from 25% (2007) to 98.6%
- Ireland's €85 billion rescue; then Portugal (spring 2011); second Greek bailout (2012) — biggest sovereign debt restructuring in history; crises in Italy, Spain, Cyprus
- **"Doom loop"**: banks losing confidence of depositors/creditors (worried about government support) ↔ governments suffering from fears that bank bailout costs would crush taxpayers — a self-reinforcing spiral unique to the eurozone because individual member states (not a central federal authority) were responsible for bank regulation and bailouts

#### Draghi's "Whatever It Takes" and the Euro's Structural Flaw (pages 108-109)
- Mid-2012: doom loop escalating → ECB president Mario Draghi pledged "whatever it takes to preserve the euro"
- Draghi vowed — over objections of high-ranking German policymakers — that ECB would buy UNLIMITED quantities of crisis-country bonds (conditional on strict reforms)
- This ended the most virulent phase; European Stability Mechanism created as permanent firefighting institution
- **But the structural flaw was exposed**: the eurozone lacks a single safe asset comparable to US Treasuries
- German *bunds* may be as safe as Treasuries, but Italian/Spanish/Greek bonds are NOT — yet all are denominated in euros
- "It is only natural that investors and central bankers around the world, weighing what sort of securities to keep in reserve for emergencies, will tend to favor the ones that are both the safest and most liquid of all, meaning those of the United States rather than eurozone nations"

### Pretender #2: The Yen (pages 109-114)

#### US-Japan "Yen-Dollar Talks" in the 1980s
- US Treasury officials traveled to Tokyo for "yen-dollar talks" — lavishly entertained with *kaiseki* dinners, sake, karaoke with geishas, all on Japanese government expense accounts
- **Key irony**: the US was actively ENCOURAGING Japan to internationalize the yen — "Treasury officials were not only willing to countenance another currency playing a global role similar to that of the greenback, they were insisting on it"
- Japan's "financial repression" model: government kept capital trapped at home so industrial firms could borrow cheaply; restricted cross-border money flows; discouraged foreigners from buying yen
- As late as 1970, almost no Japanese trade was invoiced in yen
- Progress was "glacial" — Japanese negotiators parried US proposals with "step by step" approach
- Treasury Undersecretary Beryl Sprinkel's piglet story: "I grew up in Missouri on a dirt farm... when piglets were born, we had to cut their tails off. When we cut them off we didn't cut them off one inch at a time!... We just hacked them off once up at the top" — shocked silence, then laughter; Oba declared Japan would go "stride by stride"

#### Japan's Economic Miracle and Yen Fears (pages 112-114)
- Toyota, Nissan, Honda invaded US auto market; Sony, Matsushita in electronics; Fujitsu, NEC in computers; Toshiba, Hitachi in heavy machinery — Japan seemed poised to overtake the US
- Books: *Japan as Number One*, *Trading Places: How We Allowed Japan to Take the Lead*
- 1984 yen-dollar pact liberalized Japan's financial system somewhat; yen-denominated exports rose from <30% to nearly 40% by 1991
- Plaza Accord (1985) explicitly called for yen appreciation
- To counter *endaka* (strong yen), Bank of Japan cut rates → stock/property bubble reached "stratospheric heights"
- Japanese multinationals shifted production overseas (North America, Europe, Southeast Asia) — 17,000 Matsushita workers in Malaysia donning company uniforms
- *Newsweek* (August 1991 cover): "Sayonara, America" — Asian yen bloc generating more growth than Europe or North America combined
- "Yen bloc" / "coprosperity sphere" fears widespread; Diane Kunz (1995): "The death of the dollar order will drastically increase the price of the American dream while simultaneously shattering American global influence"

#### Why the Yen Failed as a Rival (pages 114-115)
- By mid-1990s: Japan's bubble had burst → deflation, zombie banks saddled with bad loans
- 1996 "Big Bang" financial reforms couldn't overcome legacy of financial repression — banks "accustomed to being cossetted by the Finance Ministry" couldn't adapt
- Foreign financial firms left Tokyo for Hong Kong, Singapore, Shanghai
- Yen as reserve currency: peaked at 5.5% of global reserves (2001), declined to ~3% by 2016
- Even Japanese trade only 37% exports / 26% imports invoiced in yen
- Aging society, dwindling population, anemic growth
- BOJ bought so many government bonds (to fight deflation) that there's been "very little trading in those bonds in recent years" — destroying liquidity, further diminishing yen's international appeal
- Blustein: "Perhaps if Finance Ministry officials had taken the moral of Beryl Sprinkel's piglet story to heart and dismantled their controls much earlier, dollar users would have had strong motivation to shift to yen. But the opportunity was missed"

### Pretender #3: The SDR (pages 115-120)
- IMF headquarters in downtown Washington: hundreds of economists from dozens of countries, swiping ID badges through security gates
- Twelfth floor: Siddharth Tiwari's Finance Department — Tobias Unteroberdoerster manages "the world's least-well-known monetary system"
- SDRs: created 1967 as "paper gold" to supplement dollar reserves; technically a claim on IMF member currencies, not a currency itself
- SDR value = weighted basket: dollar (43.38%), euro (29.31%), renminbi (10.92%), yen (8.33%), pound (8.09%)
- Countries can exchange SDRs for hard currency through IMF "designation mechanism" — IMF identifies strong-economy countries willing to accept SDRs
- SDR allocations: 1970-72, 1979-81 (then nothing for nearly 30 years); 2009: $250 billion during GFC; 2021: $650 billion during COVID
- 2021 allocation controversy: distributed by IMF quota shares, so rich countries got most — US got $113 billion, Afghanistan got $450 million
- SDRs as share of global reserves: only about 3% — "pale imitation of the dollar"
- Zhou Xiaochuan's March 2009 essay: proposed expanding SDRs into a true international reserve currency — a "super-sovereign reserve currency"
- Zhou explicitly referenced Keynes's bancor and Triffin dilemma as justification
- Barry Eichengreen's response: SDR "doesn't work like a true currency" — can't buy coffee with it, can't pay taxes with it, no SDR-denominated bond market
- Zhou's proposal garnered serious academic interest but no political traction — IMF members (especially the US) had no incentive to create a rival to the dollar
- Unteroberdoerster: SDRs remain "a niche instrument... They are not the answer to the international monetary system's problems"

### Pretender #4: The Renminbi (pages 120-130)

#### China's Financial Repression (pages 120-123)
- Blustein's 1990s China visit: Foreign Exchange Certificates (FEC) — special currency for foreigners at luxury hotels, Friendship Stores
- "Amusing little relic of Maoist days" — two-tier currency system with FEC worth more than regular renminbi
- No. 1 Auto Plant in Changchun: liberated workers in drab, Soviet-style communist industrial city — "Freedom Auto Works" making Red Flag limousines for party leaders
- China's financial system: "financial repression" — state-owned banks funnel cheap savings from households to state-owned enterprises
- Household savings rates extraordinarily high (30-40%) partly because no social safety net; banks paid depositors far below market rates
- Central Huijin Investment Ltd.: government holding company controlling majority stakes in China's biggest banks
- Chinese banking law requires loans serve "the industrial policies of the State" — not market allocation
- Zhu Rongji's reforms (late 1990s): cleaned up bad loans, restructured banks, prepared for WTO entry
- WTO accession (December 2001): required opening financial markets — but China maintained capital controls and state bank dominance

#### Renminbi Internationalization Push (pages 123-126)
- Post-2008 financial crisis: China began actively promoting renminbi for international use
- "Dim sum bonds": renminbi-denominated bonds issued in Hong Kong — first step in creating offshore renminbi market
- Trade settlement: expanded renminbi use for cross-border trade, especially with Asian neighbors
- Renminbi appreciated ~25% against dollar from 2005-2015 (from ~8.3 to ~6.2)
- HSBC's Qu Hongbin (2012): predicted renminbi internationalization as "a financial revolution of truly epic proportions"
- Arvind Subramanian (Peterson Institute): argued "the renminbi could surpass the dollar" as dominant reserve currency by 2025
- IMF SDR basket inclusion: November 2015, renminbi added at 10.92% weight — symbolic recognition of China's growing financial importance
- This was a major political achievement for Beijing — signaled arrival as a "first-class" currency

#### The 2015-16 Crisis: Renminbi's Reckoning (pages 126-128)
- Summer 2015: Chinese stock market crash — Shanghai Composite fell ~40% from June peak
- August 2015: PBOC devalued renminbi by ~2% — triggered global panic, capital flight accelerated
- Capital outflows: ~$100 billion per month at peak; China burned through $1 trillion in foreign reserves (from $4T to $3T) defending the currency
- Capital controls reimposed: crackdown on outbound investment, limits on foreign currency purchases
- Renminbi share of global cross-border payments: peaked at 2.8% (August 2015), then declined to 1.8% by 2017
- The crisis exposed fundamental contradiction: China wanted renminbi internationalization but wouldn't give up capital controls or state-directed banking
- "You can't have a major international currency if you're going to maintain those kinds of controls"

#### Xi Jinping and Rule of Law Erosion (pages 128-129)
- Xi's consolidation of power: anti-corruption campaign doubled as political purge
- "Socialist rule of law" doctrine: law serves the Party, not independent judiciary
- Disappearances of billionaires (Jack Ma most prominent); arbitrary regulatory crackdowns on tech, education, gaming sectors
- Foreign investors increasingly nervous: "How can I trust my assets are safe in a system where the rules can change overnight?"
- This authoritarianism directly undermined renminbi internationalization — international currencies require trust in property rights, contract enforcement, rule of law
- CIPS (Cross-Border Interbank Payment System): China's alternative to SWIFT, launched 2015
- CIPS still tiny compared to SWIFT: ~3,000 participants vs SWIFT's 11,000+ institutions
- CIPS processes in hours what SWIFT does in minutes — infrastructure gap remains large

#### Chapter 4 Wrap-Up: Why the Dollar Keeps Winning (pages 129-130)
- Every challenger had fatal flaws: euro (no unified fiscal authority, existential crises), yen (financial repression, demographic decline), SDR (not a real currency), renminbi (capital controls, authoritarian governance)
- Dollar survived: Bretton Woods collapse, 1970s stagflation, Japan's rise, euro's creation, 2008 GFC, China's ascent
- Each crisis that should have weakened the dollar actually reinforced it — "flight to safety" effect
- Bernanke's 2016 blog post: "exorbitant privilege is not so exorbitant anymore"
  - Seigniorage from dollar dominance: only ~$20 billion/year
  - Safe haven status actually hurts US exporters (strong dollar = expensive exports)
  - "English is of considerably more benefit" to the US than dollar reserve status
  - Dollar dominance is more burden than benefit in many ways — but no one else wants the burden either

## Chapter 5: Weaponization (pages 128-145+)

### Trump, Iran, and the Power of Secondary Sanctions (pages 128-132)
- May 2018: Trump withdrew from JCPOA (Iran nuclear deal) over European allies' objections
- Key weapon: secondary sanctions — threatening to cut off from dollar system any foreign company doing business with Iran
- European companies capitulated despite EU opposition: Maersk (shipping), Peugeot (autos), Siemens (industrial), Total (oil) all abandoned Iran operations
- INSTEX (Instrument in Support of Trade Exchanges): EU's attempt to create a barter system to circumvent US sanctions — complete failure
  - Couldn't find anyone willing to run it (fear of US retaliation)
  - Only processed a handful of tiny humanitarian transactions
- Sanctions as "America's new preferred way of waging war" — less blood, less political cost than military action
- Growth of sanctions programs: from 4 sanctioned governments (pre-9/11) to 22+ by 2018
- Trump escalation: Obama averaged ~500 new sanctions designations/year; Trump ramped to 600, then 900, then 1,200+

### European Fury at Dollar Weaponization (pages 132-134)
- Heiko Maas (German Foreign Minister): called for "independent payment channels" free from US control
- Bruno Le Maire (French Finance Minister): "Europe must be a sovereign continent, not a vassal"
- Jean-Claude Juncker (European Commission President): called for euro to become global reserve currency to reduce dollar dependence
- Jacob Lew (Obama Treasury Secretary) and John Kerry (Obama Secretary of State): both warned that overuse of sanctions would drive allies to build alternatives, eventually undermining the very weapon
- Daniel McDowell's *Bucking the Buck*: "Every time Washington reaches for sanctions, it gives other countries one more reason to find alternatives"

### How Dollar Payments Actually Work: CHIPS as Chokepoint (pages 134-136)
- CHIPS (Clearing House Interbank Payments System): processes 95% of cross-border dollar transactions
- ~40 megabanks are direct members; "almost every cross-border payment passes through one of just fifteen banks"
- Correspondent banking explained: Bank A in Country X doesn't have dollars — uses a correspondent bank in the US as intermediary
- International payments don't actually "move" money — they're a series of debits and credits across correspondent accounts
- This architecture creates a single chokepoint: if a foreign bank loses access to its US correspondent, it's effectively cut off from the global dollar system
- The US government can compel any US bank to stop serving a foreign client — sanctions enforcement
- This is why secondary sanctions work: even if a European company doesn't care about US law, its bank does

### The Compliance Machine (pages 131-133)
- CHIPS doesn't screen transactions itself — banks do, spending billions annually on compliance departments (thousands of staff, expensive hardware/software)
- Private sector is "the principal point of both the spear and shield" — Adam M. Smith (Gibson Dunn, former Obama Treasury official)
- "Most major companies will prefer to be over-compliant rather than risk being under-compliant"
- A whole cottage industry emerged: consultancies, law firms, software analyzers, due-diligence investigators advising on sanctions risk
- Cross-border payment fees exist because intermediaries take cuts at each step — why fintech entrepreneurs target remittances
- But large payments (millions per transaction) require massive bank liquidity — *The Pay Off* example of Dutch brother unable to hold $100K for a sibling's transfer
- IEEPA (International Emergency Economic Powers Act, 1977): principal statutory basis for US sanctions
  - To trigger IEEPA's extreme powers (freezing assets), president only needs to identify a "national emergency"
  - "There's almost no jurisprudence... courts frequently finding that the administration's power under IEEPA is uniquely broad"

### History of Economic Sanctions (pages 133-137)
- Woodrow Wilson: envisioned economic sanctions as alternative to military force, via League of Nations
- Embargoes oldest form — first recorded: Athens, 432 BC; Jefferson's Embargo Act of 1807 backfired badly
- League of Nations mandated collective embargo against aggressors — Wilson called it a "terrible remedy"
- League embargo of Italy (1935, Ethiopia invasion): didn't stop Mussolini, but Italy suffered (61% export decline, 44% import drop, 25% lira depreciation, 1.7% GDP shrinkage)
- Nicholas Mulder's *The Economic Weapon*: Depression-era sanctions drove Germany/Japan toward autarky and eventually war
  - Japan's drive for Greater East Asia Co-Prosperity Sphere: needed raw materials blocked by sanctions
  - FDR froze Japanese assets July 1941, cut off oil → Japan attacked Pearl Harbor
- 1930s pattern: sanctions effective at inflicting economic damage, ineffective at changing behavior
- Cuba embargo (post-1959): hurt Cuba economically but didn't dislodge Castro
- Carter froze Iranian assets (1979), embargoed Soviet grain (1980) — neither achieved goals
- Panama (late 1980s): Noriega indicted for drug smuggling; dollar transfers banned → economy collapsed, banks shut → but Noriega only removed by US invasion (1989)
- 1990s resurgence: South Africa success (sanctions credited with ending apartheid, Mandela elected 1994)
- Post-Cold War unipolarity: UN Security Council no longer blocked by Soviet vetoes → UN sanctions expanded (Iraq, Yugoslavia, Rwanda)
- Helms-Burton Act (1996): secondary sanctions on foreign companies doing business in Cuba — huge backlash from Canada/Europe; Clinton deferred enforcement
- Richard Haass "Sanctioning Madness" (1997 *Foreign Affairs*): growing expert consensus sanctions were blunt and ineffective
- Hufbauer, Schott, Elliott: highlighted "black knights" providing economic lifelines to targets
- By late 1990s: sanctions in disrepute — Saddam Hussein entrenched despite crushing UN embargo, millions of Iraqis impoverished

### The Anti-Money Laundering Infrastructure (pages 137-141)
- FinCEN (Financial Crimes Enforcement Network): Treasury agency tracking domestic money flows
  - Banks must report transactions >$10,000; file "suspicious activity reports" (SARs) for anything fishy
  - Blustein's personal anecdote: briefed at FinCEN's Virginia "war room," they pulled up his ex-mother-in-law's entire financial profile
- War on drugs drove anti-money laundering expansion — drug kingpins need to legitimize proceeds (casino chip laundering example)
- Bank Secrecy Act (BSA, 1970): first big step — requires banks to monitor clients and file SARs
- 1986: US first country to outlaw money laundering itself
- FATF (Financial Action Task Force, 1989, Paris): G-7 created "soft law" body to standardize global AML rules
  - Not binding treaty, but enormous influence — banks worldwide must implement KYC (know your customer) rules
  - Post-9/11: expanded to counter-financing of terrorism (CFT)
  - FATF "noncomplier" blacklist = financial death — Nauru (early 2000s) virtually blockaded
  - HSBC fined $1.25 billion for letting Mexican drug cartel funds flow through (lowest-risk category for Mexican accounts)
- Clinton administration ramped up OFAC enforcement (1995): targeted Latin American drug trade
  - "Specially Designated Nationals" (SDNs) = *la lista Clinton* — financial untouchables
  - Even Colombian banks avoided anyone connected to SDN list
- Effectiveness questionable: "pretty useless" — smart lawyers/accountants circumvent with sub-$10K transactions, shell companies, trade-based laundering
- Bush administration (mid-2001): Treasury Secretary Paul O'Neill and Lawrence Lindsey (NEC) nearly rolled back AML rules — viewed as unconstitutional burden on banks
- Then 9/11 changed everything

### Post-9/11: OFAC and the Patriot Act (pages 141-144)
- John Taylor (Treasury undersecretary for international affairs): walked through tunnel to OFAC offices 10 days after 9/11
- Taylor was a free-market academic, ill-suited for financial warfare — but adapted quickly after seeing how 9/11 changed the world
- All 19 hijackers had opened US bank accounts in their own names (Sun Trust, Bank of America); entire operation cost ~$500,000 — too small to trigger SARs
- September 24, 2001: Bush Rose Garden announcement — executive order freezing assets of 27 individuals/organizations
  - "This morning, a major thrust of our war on terrorism began with the stroke of a pen"
  - "Money is the lifeblood of terrorist operations. Today, we're asking the world to stop payment"
- USA PATRIOT Act (October 26, 2001): massively expanded Treasury's powers
  - Could designate foreign jurisdictions, institutions, and whole classes of transactions as "primary money laundering concerns"
  - Threatened foreign banks from "high-risk" jurisdictions with loss of correspondent accounts at US banks
  - Even Switzerland, Luxembourg, Liechtenstein forced to increase compliance staffing and suspicious activity reporting
- Juan Zarate (*Treasury's War*, 2013): "We could prompt banks to make decisions to cut off banking relationships, isolating rogues from the international financial system — and we could rely on the business decision making of the banks themselves to do the heavy lifting"
- Section 311 of Patriot Act: Zarate's "Bad Bank Initiative" — could label banks as "primary money laundering concerns" and cut off all correspondent accounts
  - "No bank wanted to run the risk of being cut off from the US banking system"
  - 2003-2006: targeted banks in Syria, Lebanon, Cyprus, Burma, Latvia, Belarus
  - Section 311 against Ukraine spurred parliament to pass tighter AML laws within a month
  - Riggs Bank (DC, founded 1840s): fined $25M for lax handling of embassy transactions → sold to PNC of Pittsburgh
- Terrorist financing = "reverse money laundering" — clean money funneled to terrorists (opposite of drug money needing to be cleaned)

### SWIFT and the Treasury's Secret Intelligence (pages 144-150)
- SWIFT HQ: La Hulpe, Belgium — lakeside, near nineteenth-century chateau; neoclassical building with tight security
- 11,000+ banks in 200+ countries; ~45 million messages/day (2022)
- SWIFT codes (8-11 characters): identify sending/receiving banks — SWIFT doesn't hold money or move it, just transmits messages
- Pre-SWIFT (before mid-1970s): telex machines, prone to errors; 1973: 239 banks from 15 countries formed SWIFT under Belgian law
- First SWIFT message: May 9, 1977, sent by Prince (later King) Albert of Belgium
- Early 1990s: US Justice Dept and FATF approached SWIFT to subpoena messages — CEO Leonard "Lenny" Schrank deemed it "unthinkable" (privacy, European data protection)
- Post-9/11: Treasury summoned Schrank; General Counsel David Aufhauser "brazenly opened the meeting with a declaration: 'I want your data.' Without a pause, Schrank responded, 'What took you so long?'"
- Compromise reached: SWIFT would respond to targeted subpoenas only for terrorism-related transactions
- CIA facility analyzed SWIFT data; independent auditors oversaw the program; Aufhauser limited subpoenas to ~1/month
- Spring 2003: SWIFT board (chaired by Dutch banker Jaap Kamp) tried to suspend cooperation — flew to Washington, heard classified briefings from FBI director Robert Mueller about lives saved
- "Scrutineers" arrangement: SWIFT employees sat next to CIA analysts, could object to overreach
- June 2006: *New York Times* exposed the program — massive controversy
  - Bush administration furious; SWIFT feared it all along
  - But Treasury had already pivoted: created Office of Intelligence and Analysis (2004) — unique among world's finance ministries
- Stage set for bigger targets: next would come a full governing regime (Chapter 6 preview)

## Chapter 6: "Open an Account on the Moon — We Can Go After That" (pages ~150-165+)

### North Korea: The "Sopranos State" (pages 150-154)
- "Supernotes": nearly flawless counterfeit $100 bills, same ink and paper as genuine US currency
- 2002 classified briefing: Secret Service revealed the counterfeiters were agents of North Korea (DPRK)
- David Asher (State Dept senior adviser): "North Korea had become the world's best counterfeiter of 100 dollar bills... We even saw surveillance photographs of members of the ruling elite passing the notes themselves in banks and casinos"
- North Korea's criminal enterprises: heroin, cocaine, methamphetamines, weapons, counterfeit cigarettes, counterfeit Viagra
- Revenue financed luxuries for elites who controlled military/security apparatus → entrenched Kim Jong Il's rule
- Sheena Chestnut (political scientist): coined "Sopranos state" for North Korea

### The Road to Banco Delta Asia (pages 154-156)
- Post-Korean War: US had tried everything to squeeze Pyongyang — trade restrictions, travel bans
- 1990s collapse of Soviet Union worsened North Korea's destitution but didn't stop nuclear weapons progress
- Military option ruled out: retaliatory strike could decimate Seoul (~10 million South Koreans)
- Clinton administration: negotiated freeze on nuclear reactors in exchange for energy supplies
- Late 2002: intelligence revealed North Korea secretly enriching weapons-grade uranium — Six Party Talks began (2003) with US, Russia, Japan, South Korea, China
- Conventional sanctions useless: US-North Korean trade negligible, and China provided economic lifeline
- Treasury officials (Asher, Daniel Glaser — Zarate's deputy) pushed Section 311 as the answer
  - "I was going to all these inter-agency meetings... 'We have this section 311 that we can use.' Practically every time I would show up, I would have to start over, because people would say, 'What's 311? Can you explain that again?'"
- After months of inter-agency debate, White House gave the go-ahead

### The BDA Strike (pages 156+)
- Target: Banco Delta Asia (BDA) — tiny family-run bank in Macao (former Portuguese colony, Chinese gambling enclave)
- 9 branches, 30,000 customers, <$400 million in deposits
- Chosen because it facilitated North Korea's black-market enterprises, and its smallness minimized diplomatic blowback with China (bigger banks had tighter Chinese government ties)
- September 15, 2005: Treasury designated BDA as "primary money laundering concern" under Section 311
- BDA declared "a willing pawn for the North Korean government to engage in corrupt financial activities"

### The BDA Shockwave (pages 156-158)
- Within 6 days: panicked customers withdrew $133 million (one-third of deposits); Macao authorities seized the bank, froze $25M in North Korean accounts
- Far more stunning: banks across Asia voluntarily terminated all North Korean business — legitimate or not — fearing they'd be the next Treasury target
- Even Bank of China's Macao branch froze North Korean accounts — despite Beijing's desire to prop up the Kim regime
- "The mere issuance of a notification tainting a small bank in Macao... resulted in pariah status for North Korea throughout the global financial system"
- Zarate: "This was the moment of awakening to the power of the Treasury Department... This was the coming-of-age of a new era of financial pressure and warfare"
- Glaser: "Nobody was more confident about our ability than I was, and even I was taken aback at how impactful it was"
- Treasury's evidentiary standard was low: "80/20 rule" — only 80% confidence of wrongdoing needed, per O'Neill edict
- Christopher Hill told North Korean counterpart: "We have the capacity to go after your activities all over the world. If you want to go open an account on the moon, we can go after that"
- Inebriated North Korean negotiator confessed to Victor Cha (NSC): "You Americans have finally found a way to hurt us"

### BDA's Mixed Results (pages 158-159)
- BDA action derailed Six Party Talks — North Koreans stormed out, refused to negotiate until financial damage was reversed
- July 2006: North Korea test-fired missiles; three months later: first successful nuclear device test
- Internal Bush administration battle: State (Hill, Rice) vs Treasury — State saw BDA as impediment to disarmament; Treasury refused to backtrack
- Messy compromise: $25M in frozen funds released through complicated arrangement; hawks called it "capitulation"
- North Korea continued toward becoming a nuclear power
- But the Treasury's weapon had been tested against a nation-state — its explosive potential was demonstrated

### Stuart Levey's "Whisper Campaign" Against Iran (pages 159-162)
- Stuart Levey: first undersecretary for terrorism and financial intelligence (2004-2011)
- Unusual for Treasury: instead of meeting foreign government officials, Levey met bank CEOs — 100+ meetings worldwide
- Message: doing business with Iran was dangerous and should be terminated
- Iran context: terrorism list since 1980s, full US embargo mid-1990s, "axis of evil," IRGC was first foreign military section targeted by US sanctions
- Iran's oil revenue + agents in lax-governance countries allowed it to shrug off conventional sanctions
- Most worrisome: failure of UN/IAEA to stop Iran's nuclear weapons-grade fuel capacity
- Levey's "whisper campaign" (2006): toured Europe, Middle East, Asia warning bank CEOs that Iran was using the financial system illicitly — same playbook as North Korea
- September 2006: OFAC blacklisted Bank Saderat (3,400 branches outside Iran) — "significant facilitator" of Hezbollah, Hamas, Palestinian Islamic Jihad
- Next: Bank Sepah, Bank Melli, Bank Mellat also hit
- Levey's implicit threat: "Nice little bank you've got here — what a shame if something happens to it." Banks understood the BDA precedent.
- Levey: "Someone might say this was coercion, but we felt it was highlighting the way Iran was undermining the integrity of the international financial system"
- Blustein: "I have seen no credible evidence that Levey issued explicit threats" — he showed classified briefing material on Iran's front companies and obfuscation tactics
- By April 2008: Levey told Senate that "the world's leading financial institutions have largely stopped dealing with Iran, and especially Iranian banks, in any currency"
- By fall 2008: 80+ banks had curtailed Iran business — "Even banks in Muslim countries, from Bahrain to Malaysia... Most surprising has been the shift by several Chinese banks"
- Iranian officials furious: denounced Treasury for "assigning one of their Zionist deputies to halt the Iranian economy"
- Iranian companies increasingly struggled to obtain credit, forced to barter with foreign partners
- Obama kept Levey in his job; campaign resumed after September 2009 revelation of secret uranium-enrichment facility at Qom

### Criminal Prosecution and Congressional Escalation (pages 162-164)
- Manhattan DA Robert Morgenthau: criminally targeted foreign banks dealing with Iran — adding enforcement dimension Treasury couldn't
- 2009: Lloyds Bank fined $350 million for dollar transactions on behalf of Iranian front companies
- By 2016: nearly a dozen mostly-European banking giants settled — BNP Paribas, Credit Suisse, Commerzbank, Standard Chartered
- Banks had "stripped" Iranian references from payment instructions to evade US sanctions
- BNP Paribas: almost $9 billion fine — record criminal penalty at the time
- Banks also had to beef up compliance staff: HSBC increased compliance employees sevenfold to 8,300 in five years after 2012 settlement; hired Levey as chief legal officer
- CISADA (Comprehensive Iran Sanctions, Accountability, and Divestment Act): Congress gave Treasury power to bar US correspondent accounts for any foreign bank doing "significant" business with sanctioned Iranian entities — regardless of local law
- 2011: EU joined with oil embargo + forced SWIFT to kick major Iranian banks off its messaging network
- Late 2011: Bank Markazi (Iran's central bank) designated "primary money laundering concern" under Section 311
- Strategic loophole: foreign banks could be exempted from secondary sanctions if their home countries "significantly" decreased Iranian oil purchases

### Iran Sanctions: Success and Aftermath (pages 164-166)
- By mid-2013: Iran's Asian oil customers cutting purchases — Japan -45%, Taiwan -57%, India -30%, China -21%
- Iran's oil exports plunged: 2.5M barrels/day (2011) → 1M barrels/day (2013)
- Iranian economy: GDP shrank 6.6%, rial fell ~80%, prices soared for meat, grain, vegetables, fruit
- Iran returned to negotiations mid-2012; elected Hassan Rouhani (2013) on platform of getting sanctions lifted
- Mid-2015: JCPOA signed — Iran's nuclear facilities limited to non-military purposes
- Dollar weapon more conclusive than with North Korea — led to actual policy concessions
- US coordinated with allies: Obama administration ensured oil squeeze wouldn't cause global economic damage
- JCPOA didn't survive Trump — he reimposed sanctions, as covered at start of Chapter 5

### Countermeasures: Russia's "Fortress Russia" (pages 166-169)
- Sergey Glazyev (Putin adviser): "We will encourage everybody to dump U.S. bonds, get rid of dollars as an unreliable currency, and leave the U.S. market... The more aggressive the Americans are, the sooner they will see the final collapse of the dollar"
- 2014: Russia hit by financial sanctions after Crimea annexation — Obama's initial sanctions restrained (European allies anxious about disrupting Russian energy exports)
- Tougher steps followed: effectively blocked Russian firms from raising funds on international markets
- SWIFT disconnection discussed but discarded as "too extreme for such a major power"
- Sanctions + oil price decline → deep Russian economic slump → Putin ordered "Fortress Russia" strategy
- Fortress Russia elements: reduce foreign debt (public and private), disciplined fiscal/monetary policy, accumulate foreign reserves, reduce dollar's role in trade and finance
- SPFS (System for Transfer of Financial Messages): Russia's SWIFT alternative, launched November 2014, fully operational 2017
  - Few foreign institutions joined (mostly Belarus, former Soviet republics)
  - Domestic Russian banks could communicate without SWIFT
- Mir card network: replaced Visa/Mastercard for domestic consumer payments
- By 2019: Fortress Russia largely succeeded fiscally — budget surplus, public debt only 15% of GDP (vs EU average ~80%), reserves swelled to ~$542 billion
- But austerity caused real hardship: disposable incomes declined every year 2014-2017, only marginal recovery after

### Russia's De-Dollarization: Limited Results (pages 169-171)
- Central bank dollar holdings: fell from 40%+ (2013) → 24% (2020) → 16% (early 2022); euro overtook dollar as largest reserve holding — but central bank controls its own reserves, not private trade
- Russian firms reduced dollar invoicing by 20+ percentage points (2013-2020) — but dollar still ~60% of exports
- McDowell's data: Russia's exports to EU still ~40% dollar-denominated in 2020 (same as euro)
- Russia's imports remained heavily dollar-dependent
- Most telling: Russia-Turkey trade — dollar remained dominant currency in 2020 (same as 2013), despite both Putin and Erdogan pledging to use local currencies
- "Despite their mutual interest in achieving this outcome, de-dollarization remained elusive"
- Biggest success: Sino-Russian trade — euro surpassed dollar as main vehicle currency for Russian exports to China (Beijing sympathetic to less dollar-dominated system)

### Countermeasures: China's CIPS (pages 171-173)
- April 2012 (one month after Iranian banks kicked off SWIFT): People's Bank of China announced plan for its own cross-border payment network
- CIPS (Cross-Border Interbank Payment System): launched 2015 in Shanghai — combines messaging + clearing + settlement (unlike SPFS which is messaging-only)
- Potential threat to CHIPS as chokepoint: if substantial dollar transactions shifted to CIPS, US sanctions power would degrade
- But CIPS processes in renminbi, CHIPS in dollars — CIPS can only rival CHIPS if renminbi rivals dollar
- CIPS daily volume: ~650 billion yuan (~$90B) in early 2024 vs CHIPS's $1.8 trillion — dwarfed
- CIPS banks: ~1/7 the number using SWIFT; most CIPS transactions still involve SWIFT messaging
- Early 2024: ~1,500 financial institutions connected (including Citibank, Standard Chartered, BNP Paribas)
- Transaction value doubled from early 2020 to early 2023; 500+ banks joined in that period
- Rush Doshi (*The Long Game*): "CIPS is not yet ready to serve as an alternative messaging system for cross-border payments outside of China. Even so, that day will come"
- Gal Luft & Anne Korin (*De-Dollarization*): "CIPS and SPFS are nowhere near becoming as widely used as SWIFT... But today is today and tomorrow is tomorrow"
- Fundamental limit: renminbi's restricted use outside China (capital controls, no rule of law) keeps CIPS stunted — "as long as such concerns persist, the renminbi's internationalization will remain stunted relative to the dollar"
- "Burner banks": Chinese strategy — banks like Bank of Kunlun (Xinjiang, bought by China National Petroleum Corp 2009) used solely to buy Iranian oil, no dollar clearing in New York, impervious to SDN designation
  - Treasury sanctioned Bank of Kunlun 2012, but bank continued operating since it conducted no US dollar business
  - Russia used similar technique: small banks for arms trading to avoid sanctions targets on bigger banks
  - Limitation: Treasury intelligence can detect burner-bank networks and threaten secondary sanctions on anyone dealing with them
  - Bank of Kunlun stopped handling Iranian payments in 2018

### North Korea's Sanctions Evasion (pages 173-176)
- *Diamond 8*: tanker that made multiple voyages to North Korea (2019-2020), turning off tracking signals, transmitting false identity, registered in Seychelles under Indonesian company, linked to Tony Tung (Fujian oil trader)
- Part of elaborate network keeping North Korea supplied with oil despite UN sanctions (post-January 2016 hydrogen bomb test)
- North Korea's State Trading Companies (STCs): John Park (Harvard) and Jim Walsh (MIT) documented their rising sophistication in 2016 research
  - Interviewed former STC managers among 30,000 North Korean defectors in South Korea
  - Sanctions had "perverse and unanticipated effects by significantly improving North Korea's sanctions-busting proficiency"
  - Post-BDA: North Korea found new partners and instruments beyond Treasury's reach
  - After 2009 (Wen Jiabao's Pyongyang visit): deeper China-DPRK ties, expanded use of Chinese middlemen
  - Higher sanctions risk → higher fees → attracted larger, more sophisticated Chinese partners
  - STC managers moved to Chinese cities for extended periods, enrolled children in schools
  - Used third-party shell companies and Chinese brokers: "The suppliers assume they are doing business with a Chinese firm, not a North Korean one"
  - 900-mile China-North Korea border: goods smuggled in small consignments hidden in regular Chinese commercial flow — "hiding in the open"
- Marcus Noland and Stephan Haggard: North Korea is "the paradigmatic hard target" — illustrates that dollar weaponization, for all its potency, has limitations

### Trump's Sanctions Addiction (pages 176-179)
- Trump tweet (October 8, 2019, on Turkey/Syria): "I will totally destroy and obliterate the economy of Turkey. (I've done it before!)"
- Trump enjoyed signing sanctions executive orders, glowed for photographers, told aides he got "special gratification" from smacking down foreign antagonists as a "near-costless way"
- Iran: reimposed sanctions sent economy into reverse — oil exports cut in half, GDP contracted 12% (2018-2021), inflation skyrocketing, rial in free fall, middle class shrank from 45% to 30% of population
  - But Tehran refused to accede to Trump's "much more extreme demands"
  - Sanctions proved counterproductive: hardened anti-American sentiment, undermined moderate forces
  - Iranian student activist Shadee: "The past two presidential elections we told people, 'Go vote so we don't get sanctioned anymore'... But after everything we've been through because of Trump pulling out of the deal and reimposing sanctions... We have no arguments left. We've been rendered speechless"
- Venezuela: sanctions to topple Maduro failed — he clung to power; blamed for Venezuelan immigration flood
  - Thomas Shannon (undersecretary of State 2016-2018): "The sanctions were going to grind the Venezuelan economy into dust and have huge human consequences, one of which would be out-migration"
- Russia (Deripaska/Rusal, 2018): sanctioned oligarch's aluminum company → global aluminum prices surged 20%, Rusal operations in Ireland/Sweden halted, German automakers threatened
  - Treasury had to embarrassingly retreat, negotiate Deripaska's reduced stake in Rusal — "a textbook example of the collateral economic damage that can result from the dollar weapon's punishing impact"
- ICC prosecutor Fatou Bensouda: bank account frozen and barred from US transactions for investigating alleged US war crimes in Afghanistan — Biden administration reversed in 2021

### Chapter 6 Conclusion (pages 179-180)
- Dollar weaponization had come far since post-9/11 — from drug traffickers to OFAC to global nation-state targeting
- Obama and Trump administrations hit target countries hard
- Results often fell short: targets staunchly resisted changing policies and behavior
- But no gainsaying the US currency's "globe-girdling dominion"
- As 2010s ended and 2020s dawned: another phenomenon fueling downbeat conjecture about dollar's future — digitized currency (next chapter)

## Chapter 7: Crypto, Libra, and the Central Bankers' Wake-Up Call (pages ~181-196)

### Bitcoin and Crypto's Failures as Money (pages 181-184)
- Bitcoin's "creating money out of thin air" critique: "the air that Bitcoin is created from is much thinner" — can't pay IRS with it, legal tender only in El Salvador and Central African Republic, where "most ordinary people shun it"
- 21 million bitcoin cap: sounds anti-inflationary but actually makes Bitcoin's "monetary policy" more rigid than the gold standard — economy needs elastic money supply that grows with production capacity; fixed supply → deflation
- Altcoin proliferation (Dogecoin etc.): Bitcoin maximalists call them "shitcoins" — but their existence undermines Bitcoin's scarcity argument since anyone can create crypto tokens
- Bitcoin price volatility: $0.003 (pizza purchase, 2010) → $120 → $1,238 (2013) → below $100 → $20,000 (Dec 2017) — "nobody's idea of a proper medium of exchange"
- Even the famous pizza purchase: bitcoins went not to a pizzeria but to someone in California who called Papa John's and paid by credit card
- Ethereum (2013 white paper, launched 2015): Vitalik Buterin's blockchain for smart contracts and "atomic swaps" — self-executing agreements with no intermediaries
- MIT's Narula: programmable money examples — drug company paying for health data, crop insurance auto-paying farmers
- Real-world crypto use cases: Silk Road (illicit marketplace, shut down 2013, Ross Ulbricht sentenced to life); censorship resistance in Venezuela/Turkmenistan/Equatorial Guinea
- Mostly a speculative tool: "prices depending solely on what others will pay" — unlike stocks (company profits), bonds (interest streams), or houses (utility)
- Crypto Winter 2018: Bitcoin fell nearly two-thirds in first weeks of the year
- Then came Libra — jolting officials from "bemused detachment"

### Facebook's Libra (pages 184-188)
- June 2019: David Marcus (Facebook exec, digital payment veteran) unveiled Libra — "empower billions of people, reinvent money and transform the global economy"
- Permissionless, blockchain-based, stablecoin pegged to basket of currencies (like SDR) — backed by reserves of low-risk assets in dollar, euro, yen
- 2.5 billion Facebook users = instant potential user base unlike any currency
- Libra Association: 20+ companies/nonprofits — Visa, Mastercard, Vodafone, eBay, Spotify
- Stablecoins existed since 2014 (Tether being the biggest) — but Libra was different: consumer payments, not just crypto trading; multi-currency basket with serious pretensions as unit of account
- Government/central banker fury: "No private entity can claim monetary power, which is inherent to the sovereignty of nations" — joint French/German statement vowing to stop it
- If successful, Libra's reserve could reach $1 trillion+ — potential to destabilize global economy; easy cross-border payments would facilitate money laundering and sanctions evasion
- Trust problem: Sherrod Brown (Senate Banking Committee): "Do you really think people should trust Facebook with their hard-earned money?"
- October 2019: *WSJ* reported Libra was "on life support" — members fleeing anti-Facebook brush
- Zuckerberg grilled for 6 hours in House hearing; "bank the unbanked" argument fell flat (poverty isn't primarily a technical/banking access problem)
- Marcus's "internet of surveillance" warning about Chinese digital currency alternative backfired — *FT*'s Jemima Kelly: "That's right, Facebook — literally the poster-child for 'surveillance capitalism' — is going to save the world from the internet of surveillance"

### Libra → Diem → Death (pages 188-189)
- April 2020: scaled back — single-currency coins (Libra dollars, Libra euros) instead of basket; no longer permissionless; user vetting required
- May 2020: hired Stuart Levey (the Iran sanctions architect) as CEO; rebranded "Diem"
- Levey: "I wanted to create a safe variation of crypto — safe for consumers, safe from illicit finance, with safe reserves and customer money completely segregated"
- June 24, 2021: breakfast meeting between Fed chair Jerome Powell and Treasury Secretary Janet Yellen — Powell willing to allow Diem trials but needed administration support; Yellen had "deep reservations" and wouldn't give it
- Early 2022: Diem's assets sold to a California bank — project dead
- Legacy: "What if other Facebooks were out there?" — spurred central bankers to take digital currency seriously

### BIS and the CBDC Movement (pages 189-196)
- BIS (Bank for International Settlements): Basel, Switzerland — "central bank for central banks," founded 1930
- Cylindrical 1970s headquarters looks like "nuclear-plant cooling tower" or the mountain from *Close Encounters*
- ~600 staff; serves 63 central banks; monthly dinners for central bank heads (second Sunday of every month — tradition since 1930s)
- BIS history: accepted looted Nazi gold during WWII; subject of dark conjecture (*The Tower of Basel* book)
- BIS became world's foremost CBDC advocate — but reluctantly
- March 2019: BIS general manager Agustín Carstens (former Bank of Mexico governor) speech derided CBDCs as "neither necessary nor desirable"
  - "The important part of CBDC is not the 'D' for 'digital'... The important part is the 'CB' for 'central bank'"
  - Key concern: if ordinary people could deposit directly at central banks, it would force central banks into lending business — "bureaucratic government institutions" doing what commercial banks do better
  - Two-tier solution: customers get CBDC from commercial banks (like cash today) — but risk of "flight to safety" bank runs where depositors flee to 100% government-backed CBDC
  - Conclusion: "central banks are not seeing today the value of venturing into uncharted territory"
- Three months later (June 30, 2019): Carstens reversed course — "it is sooner than we think that there is a market and we need to be able to provide central bank digital currencies"
- Same day: BIS announced "Innovation Hub" headed by Benoît Cœuré (former ECB, French economist)
- Timing: Libra announced mid-June 2019 — BIS insists Innovation Hub was already in the works, but Carstens admitted Libra was "a wake-up call... We central bankers were in a comfort zone, but [Libra] showed we needed to change"
- Cecilia Skingsley (Innovation Hub head): "a lot of surprised faces" at BIS annual meeting about Libra — "There was a revelation. We are actually in a competitive business"
- Early CBDC movers: Sweden's Riksbank (e-krona, because cash use declining), Bahamas (Sand Dollar, financial inclusion for remote islands)
- The biggest CBDC project drawing global attention: China's (Chapter 8)

## Chapter 8: To CBDC or Not to CBDC (pages ~196-215)

### China's e-CNY (DC/EP) (pages 196-200)
- April 2020: leaked screenshot of digital yuan app interface — first public glimpse of China's CBDC
- Pilots in Shenzhen, Suzhou, Chengdu: Suzhou civil servants received half transport subsidies in e-CNY; Shenzhen lottery gave 50,000 residents "red envelopes" of 200 yuan (~$30) — 62,000+ transactions resulted
- PBOC had been researching digital currency since 2014
- Chorus of alarm from Western commentators:
  - Kumar & Rosenbach (*Foreign Affairs*): "China is years ahead... digital currencies will degrade the efficacy of U.S. sanctions"
  - Morgan Beller (*The Information*): "China Is Winning the Digital Currency War with the U.S."
  - Niall Ferguson (*Bloomberg*): e-CNY "includes an offensive capability to challenge the U.S. dollar's dominance in cross-border payments"
  - Michael Hasenstab (*FT*): "could be the key that accelerates the decline of the dollar's dominance"

### China's Fintech Leapfrog (pages 200-202)
- By 2020: China's retail payment technology far surpassed US/Europe/Japan
- Until ~2013: China's financial system was "antiquated" — most used cash, only 1 in 6 had credit cards, many merchants refused cards
- Lack of modern financial infrastructure → ideal environment for fintech leapfrogging
- Jack Ma (Alibaba/Ant) and Pony Ma (Tencent): built "super apps" Alipay and WeChat Pay
- Started with e-commerce payment need (Chinese consumers didn't trust online merchants, no Amazon-like credit card infrastructure)
- Yuebao (2013): Alipay money market fund offering 6%+ interest → forced banks to compete on rates
- By 2017: China ranked #1 in world for fintech adoption — Alipay/WeChat handled ride hailing, insurance, bill payment, restaurant reservations, everything; even beggars displayed QR codes

### Why China Actually Built e-CNY (pages 202-203)
- Government grew wary of Alipay/WeChat duopoly — 94% of online payments, Ant (900M users) + WeChat (800M users)
- Financial stability concerns; October 2020: Jack Ma criticized Beijing regulators → Ant IPO canceled on Xi Jinping's orders; Pony Ma's empire hit with similar regulations
- Chief motivation for e-CNY: rein in Alipay/WeChat, create government-controlled payment alternative, prevent loss of control over payments and credit allocation
- Secondary spur: Libra — Chinese officials even more alarmed than Western counterparts about Facebook's global digital currency threatening monetary sovereignty
- Martin Chorzempa (*The Cashless Revolution*): domestic control was the primary driver, not world monetary domination
- Privacy concern: e-CNY presents "a privacy nightmare... and the consequences to civil liberties could be dire" — "controllable anonymity" promises are cold comfort from a regime using facial recognition, geolocation, CCTV, WeChat censorship
- Rule-breakers already found unable to board trains/planes because payment methods suspended by officials

### e-CNY's Limited International Threat (pages 203-206)
- Biggest worry: authoritarian governments adopting similar CBDCs for surveillance — but not a direct threat to Americans/Westerners
- Alipay/WeChat accepted by merchants worldwide (for Chinese tourists) — but that's not financial-sector takeover; Alibaba/Tencent international expansion efforts have "mostly flopped" because their super-app model thrived in China's uniquely primitive pre-existing financial infrastructure
- e-CNY's international prospects: "marginal at best" — digitization changes nothing about renminbi's fundamental weaknesses (weak rule of law, capital controls)
- Even if PBOC let nonresidents use e-CNY abroad, host-country central banks would control whether foreign CBDCs are accepted domestically — multilateral agreements would protect national currencies
- The kicker: March 2023, *South China Morning Post* reporters visited Suzhou mall (pilot site for 3 years) — findings mirrored Bahamas Sand Dollar: hardly anyone used e-CNY
  - Drink shop employee: "You're the first one to propose to use e-CNY"
  - Another shop: customers used it "only about once a month"
  - Government employee Michelle Feng: required to open e-CNY wallet but transfers salary to Alipay/WeChat — "Why should I bother to download another app?"
  - Q3 2022: e-CNY transactions = barely 0.1% of Alipay/WeChat volume
- The Chinese themselves show little sign of using their CBDC

### US CBDC Debate: Quarles's "Parachute Pants" Speech (pages 206-210)
- June 2021: Fed vice chair Randal Quarles (conservative, Republican, Mormon) delivered the "parachute pants speech" — compared CBDC enthusiasm to 1980s fashion fad
- "Americans have a penchant for getting carried away by novelty, which can lead to 'occasionally impetuous, deluded crazes or fads'"
- Key arguments against US CBDC:
  - "The dollar is already highly digitized" — interbank check collection almost entirely electronic
  - Improvements underway: FedNow (launched mid-2023) for instant small-value payments
  - Dollar's global role "rests on a number of foundations... None of these are likely to be threatened by a foreign currency, and certainly not because that foreign currency is a CBDC"
  - Financial inclusion: unbanked households dropped from 8.2% (2011) to 5.4% (2019) — CBDC not clearly the best tool
  - Cyberattack risk: "far more entry points to a CBDC network" than existing Fed systems
  - Big Brother tradeoff: full transparency = AML compliance but privacy nightmare; full anonymity = privacy but money laundering risk — impossible to satisfy both
- Retail vs wholesale CBDC debates; bank run risk (deposits fleeing to 100% government-backed CBDC); negative interest rate possibility as monetary policy tool
- Governance questions: what can CBDC be used to pay for? John Paul Koning: defining "lawful" purchases is "much trickier than it sounds" — would the Fed need a "porn expert"?
- CBDC design questions remain deeply contentious among economists, technologists, and policymakers worldwide

### US CBDC Politics: From Wonky to Wacky (pages 210-213)
- Jerome Powell's mantra: "It is more important to get it right than to be first"

#### Trump vs. Fed Independence (pages 227-229)

#### Treasury Market Vulnerabilities (pages 229-233)
- US Treasury market: ~$18 trillion outstanding, ~$600 billion in daily trading volume — largest, most liquid securities market in the world
- But structure has become fragile: "flash rally" of October 15, 2014 — yields swung wildly with no obvious trigger
- Problem: post-GFC bank regulations (leverage ratio, Volcker Rule) reduced banks' willingness to act as market makers/dealers
- Banks used to warehouse Treasuries on their balance sheets, providing a buffer — now they hold minimal inventory
- Gap filled by principal trading firms (PTFs) — algorithmic, high-frequency traders who provide liquidity in normal times but vanish in stress
- Hedge funds: massive leveraged "basis trades" — exploiting tiny spread between cash Treasuries and futures, using 50:1 or even 100:1 leverage via repo
- This made Treasury market dependent on fair-weather liquidity providers who would flee at the first sign of trouble

#### COVID-19 March 2020: Treasury Market Near-Collapse (pages 233-237)
- March 2020: "dash for cash" — everyone tried to sell everything simultaneously as pandemic fears escalated
- Treasury market — supposedly the ultimate safe haven — seized up
- Bid-ask spreads on 30-year Treasuries blew out from ~1/32 of a point to over 1 full point (32x normal)
- Some dealers refused to bid at all — unprecedented for US government securities
- Algorithmic traders amplified volatility — their models not designed for pandemic-level dislocations
- Hedge funds' leveraged basis trades unwound violently — forced selling of Treasuries to meet margin calls
- Foreign central banks sold Treasuries to raise dollars for their own economies — $109 billion in March alone from Fed custody accounts
- The very assets that were supposed to be "risk-free" and "cash-like" became illiquid
- Fed response (March 15-23): cut rates to zero, announced QE purchasing Treasuries and MBS "in the amounts needed to support smooth market functioning"
- March 23 statement: "whatever it takes" moment — Fed committed to unlimited QE
- Purchased ~$2 trillion in securities within two months
- Randy Quarles: described it as "a damn close-run thing" — the financial system came terrifyingly close to complete breakdown

- Red Team (China): CNAS staffers, including Stacie Pettyjohn ("We are going after companies like Apple, Dell, HP. You want a new iPhone? Guess what. You are not going to get it.")
- Scenario all too plausible: Beijing has sworn to reunify Taiwan by force if necessary
- Sanctions similar to Russia package would devastate China — but shock waves would "pound the economies of virtually every nation on earth"
- China's GDP is 10x Russia's; its four major banks rank among world's 30 "systemically important" financial institutions
- China dominates ~1/3 of 120 global manufacturing sectors (>50% market share each): 2/3 lithium-ion batteries, 4/5 solar panels, 86% rare-earth minerals, 80% computers, 67% smartphones, pharmaceutical ingredients, purified neon for semiconductors
- Atlantic Council/Rhodium Group (June 2023): "Approximately $3 trillion in trade and financial flows" at risk from maximalist China sanctions — nearly equivalent to UK's GDP
- "Decoupling" from China? "Maybe in my grandchildren's lifetimes, not mine" (author, age 72) — no country matches China as manufacturer at scale (not because of cheap labor, but infrastructure, logistics, engineer networks, vendor ecosystems)
- Samsung moved phone production to Vietnam but phones still full of made-in-China components
- Deterrence value: making clear to Xi/Politburo that Taiwan invasion triggers massive sanctions "as well as" military response

#### Digital Currency Policy Prescriptions (pages 274-276)
- "A plea, for everyone involved in policy discussions about the digitization of money: Henceforth, please omit references to the international dominance of the dollar, or at least keep the issue in proportion"
- Dollar's salvation doesn't depend on a US CBDC or dollar stablecoins
- e-CNY is NOT going to become medium of exchange "in places like Kansas and Idaho" — but legitimate concern that autocratic countries will issue CBDCs "with Chinese characteristics" (surveillance features)
- US should accept responsibility to provide an alternative with free-speech and privacy protections — with "Spider-Man-like dutifulness"
- **Best path forward**: embrace BIS vision of tokenized deposits (from Chapter 8)
  - Tokenized deposits keep central bank money at the foundation
  - Greatest fintech innovation opportunities
  - Preserve privacy protections (same as regular bank deposits, subject to AML/KYC)
  - Fortify banks' role in payments → bolster sanctions power (sanctions work because banks scrutinize trillions of dollars under threat of severe penalties)
- Stablecoins: should play a role (competition is healthy, lifeline for people under oppressive regimes), but regulatory framework should prioritize tokenized deposits over crypto
- Crypto's original inspiration was to disrupt/bypass banks — but eliminating banks would be bad for sanctions power
- **Bleak prospects**: crypto industry flush with cash, buying political influence

#### Trump and Crypto Politics (pages 276-279)
- Bitcoin 2024 conference (Nashville, July 27, 2024): Trump promised to make US "crypto capital of the planet and the Bitcoin superpower of the world"
- Previous Trump positions: 2019 "I am not a fan of Bitcoin"; 2021 "seems like a scam... the currency of the world should be the dollar"
- Nashville promises: "strategic national bitcoin stockpile" (210,000 seized bitcoins, ~$14B), expanded bitcoin mining, fire Gary Gensler (SEC chair — crypto's bête noire)
- Attendees knew it was "pretty clearly pandering" — but campaign received millions from crypto tycoons
- **Fairshake PAC**: $194M raised by fall 2024 (largest single spending operation), targeting anti-crypto politicians regardless of party; helped defeat Rep. Katie Porter
- JD Vance: held $100K-$250K in bitcoin; mentor Peter Thiel (Bitcoin 2022: "central banks are bankrupt... we are at the end of the fiat money regime"); World Liberty Financial (WLF) — Trump family crypto venture with questionable co-founders (Chase Herro, Zachary Folkman)
- Trump on dollar: "if we lost the dollar as the world's currency... that would be the equivalent of losing a war" — would use tariffs to prevent de-dollarization ("I'll say, 'You leave the dollar, you're not doing business with the United States'")
- Author's concern: "I can easily imagine... a major increase in the number of unsuspecting Americans losing their savings in crypto schemes"; if "strategic national Bitcoin stockpile" established, taxpayer money props up an asset "supposedly inspired by libertarian ideals"
- Still: dollar dominance unlikely to be affected even by Trump's crypto policies — "America would become a shoddier place. But the reasons for its currency supremacy would remain fully intact"

#### Dollar Dominance and US Fiscal Policy (pages 280-284)
- US doesn't force other countries to use the dollar (tariff threats notwithstanding); actively encouraged yen and euro internationalization (Chapter 4)
- Multipolar currency regime might actually be good: more safe havens during crises, less unhealthy reliance on single currency
- Gourinchas (IMF chief economist), Hélène Rey, Barry Eichengreen: "new Triffin dilemma" + historical norm is multiple major international currencies; "Just like biodiversity makes for a more robust global ecology, a multipolar system will be more robust"
- **Two wrong arguments about dollar and fiscal policy**:
  1. **"General Jack D. Ripper theory"** (from *Dr. Strangelove*): if dollar loses dominance, US can't borrow → superpower status unaffordable → catastrophe
     - Wrong: dollar doesn't give US lower borrowing rates (10-year Treasury yield HIGHER than Japan, France, Germany, Canada, Australia, Greece in Sept 2023)
     - US not uniquely able to "live beyond its means" — Australia, Canada, Great Britain all ran bigger current account deficits (% of GDP) in 2010-2019
     - "Exorbitant privilege is not so exorbitant anymore" (Bernanke)
     - Dollar's global role does make exchange rate higher → hurts US manufacturers; Vance: reserve currency status = "massive tax on American producers"
  2. **"Deficits don't matter" theory**: dollar is world's safest asset → foreigners will always lend → Washington can spend freely
     - Author's evolution: skeptical in 1980s when deficit was 5% GDP; has come around that larger deficits are sustainable during crises (2008-2011 at 8-10% GDP; 2020-2021 at 12-15%)
     - BUT: CBO projects 6.3% of GDP average deficit 2025-2034 (likely optimistic; realistic range 6.5-10% of GDP)
     - Super-low rates are over; Fed in inflation-fighting mode; QE → QT; yields above 5% in early 2024
     - Large deficits with high rates → crowd out private investment → hurt productivity → long-term damage
     - Fitch downgraded US Treasury debt (August 2023): "steady deterioration in standards of governance"
     - Political system incapable of remedy: Republicans cut taxes when in power, block Democratic revenue increases; debt-limit standoffs; filibusters

#### Catastrophic Policy Missteps — The Real Danger (pages 284-286)
- Dollar survived Trump 1.0, will likely survive 2.0 — but possible catastrophic missteps:
  - Debt ceiling default: "a prolonged default would surely prove calamitous to the market for Treasury securities"
  - Presidential attacks on Fed independence going further than Trump 2018-2020
  - Rule of law eroded to point where international investors see little difference between US and Chinese courts
  - US-China tensions escalate → world splits into two isolated economic blocs (dollar vs. renminbi)
  - White House occupied by someone influenced by Peter Thiel who decides to "upend the fiat money system"
- "If US policy goes in those directions, dollar dominance will be lost or diminished. That will be the least of our worries"
- Closing: "the big question is whether the United States will act as a responsible steward of the great power that the dollar confers. If it doesn't, then curses against King Dollar may be justified. Let us hope that, instead, the future brings ample grounds for raising a toast to the king's reign: Long may it abide!"
