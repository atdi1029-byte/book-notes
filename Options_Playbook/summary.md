# The Options Playbook — Summary
By Brian Overby | TradeKing | 2009 | 373 pages

## One-Line Summary
A field manual for 40 options strategies, organized by complexity and market outlook, with every play defined by setup, break-even, max profit, max loss, time decay effect, and IV exposure.

## Core Argument
Options aren't mystical instruments reserved for Wall Street insiders — they're tools with defined risk profiles, and any trader who understands the Greeks, volatility, and time decay can use them intelligently. The key isn't memorizing formulas but knowing WHICH play to run for a given market outlook, and having an exit plan before you enter.

## The Greeks — Quick Reference
| Greek | Measures | Good for Buyers | Good for Sellers |
|-------|----------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Delta | Price sensitivity to stock move | High delta = more responsive | Lower delta = less exposure |
| Gamma | Rate delta changes | High gamma = accelerating gains | High gamma = accelerating losses |
| Theta | Daily time decay | Enemy | Best friend |
| Vega | Sensitivity to IV change | Want IV to rise | Want IV to fall |

## Key Concepts

**Implied Volatility regime determines strategy:**
- High IV → sell premium (iron condors, short straddles, covered calls)
- Low IV → buy premium (long calls/puts, straddles, back spreads)

**Time decay rule of thumb:**
- ATM options decay fastest near expiration (non-linear)
- 90-day ATM option at $1.70 → loses $0.30 in first month, $0.40 in second, $1.00 in final month

**1 Standard deviation = 68% probability range:**
- Formula for any time frame: Stock × IV × √(days/365)
- $50 stock, 20% IV, 45 days: SD ≈ $3.50

**Overby's 80% Rule:**
If you've captured 80%+ of max profit on a short strategy, close it. The remaining 20% isn't worth the risk.

**Position delta:**
- Options mimic shares: delta × 100 × contracts = equivalent share exposure
- 10 calls at delta 0.75 = +750 (acts like 750 shares long)

## Five Mistakes
1. No defined exit plan (upside + downside + time frame)
2. Doubling up to recover losses (compounds risk, not like stock averaging)
3. Trading illiquid options (wide bid-ask destroys returns)
4. Waiting too long to buy back short strategies (one day it bites you)
5. Legging into spreads (always enter as single order)

## Rolling Overview
Rolling = close existing short option + open new one at different strike/expiration.
- Covered call: Roll UP and OUT (higher strike, further expiration).
- Cash-secured put: Roll DOWN and OUT.
- Spread: Roll both legs simultaneously in single order.
- Caution: Rolling can compound losses. Know when to cut and run.
