# The Millionaire Mind — Compacted Notes (Chapters 1-5, First Half)

## Chapter 1: An Introduction to the Millionaire Mind

### Core Concept: Balance Sheet Affluent vs. Income Statement Affluent
- **Millionaire mind**: financially independent yet enjoy life — not "all work, no play"
- Most became millionaires from economic ground zero, one generation
- Live in fine homes with little/no debt — not credit junkies or highly leveraged
- **Balance Sheet Affluent**: assets greatly exceed liabilities, little/no credit balances
- **Income Statement Affluent**: big income, big home, big debt, little net worth

> "We [the lenders] own it all ... You [borrowers] just run these businesses for us." — Credit officer to Gene (Oklahoma salvage business owner)

> "I taught my sons and daughters that money is not their God. You control it ... not let it control you." — Millionaire respondent

### Research Foundation: 733 Millionaires Surveyed
- **Jon Robbin** (Harvard mathematician): expert in geodemography, built model estimating net worth for 226,399 neighborhoods nationwide
- Selected 2,487 target neighborhoods, randomly sampled 5,063 households
- 1,001 completed responses, **733 were millionaires** ($1M+ net worth)
- Nine-page questionnaire with **277 questions**
- Respondents lived in old, well-established, upper-middle-class neighborhoods (homes built 1950s, 1940s, or earlier)

### Demographic Sketch
**Traditional Family:**
- Average: 54-year-old male, married 28 years (1 in 4 married 38+ years)
- 92% married, 95% of married have children (average 3 children)

**Wealth & Artifacts:**
- Average net worth: $9.2M (median $4.3M), average income: $749K (median $436K)
- Never spent >$41K on automobile, >$4,500 on engagement ring, >$38 for haircut
- **61% never received any inheritance, financial gifts, or trust income**
- 97% homeowners, purchased current home average 12 years ago for $558,718 (median $435K)
- Current value: $1,381,729 (median ~$750K) — 61% live in homes valued >$1M
- Home constructed 40 years ago (median), 25% in homes built before 1936
- 53% have not moved in past 10 years, only 27% ever had home built for them

**Vocations:**
- 32% business owners/entrepreneurs, 16% senior corporate executives
- 10% attorneys, 9% physicians
- Business owners richest overall; senior executives often multimillionaires (16% of millionaires, 26% of decamillionaires)
- 50% of wives don't work outside home

**Education:**
- 90% college graduates, 52% hold advanced degrees
- Average undergrad GPA: **2.92** (on 4.0 scale), average SAT: **1190**
- Only ~50% of C students could recall SAT scores (actual average likely ~1090)

### Top Five Success Factors (Self-Reported)
1. **Integrity** — being honest with all people
2. **Discipline** — applying self control
3. **Social skills** — getting along with people
4. **A supportive spouse**
5. **Hard work** — more than most people

- **Luck ranks #27 out of 30 factors** (near bottom)
- Strong correlation between **willingness to take financial risk** and wealth level
- Risk = investing in **yourself** (career, professional practice, private business), NOT stock market gambling

### The 900 Club: Millionaires with SAT <1000
- 72% said school/college was: "Learning to fight for our goals because someone labeled us as having 'average or less ability.'"
- 93% indicated school/college was influential in determining "hard work was more important than genetic high intellect in achieving."

### Courage and Financial Risk
- **Positive correlation** between taking financial risk and net worth
- **Inverse correlation**: higher net worth = less likely to gamble
- Self-employment is risky, but millionaire mind-set: risky **not** to be self-employed
- 37% reduce fears/worries about critical financial decisions by calling upon **strong religious faith**

### Vocation is Foundation: The Fighter Pilot Analogy
- **Sergeant "Paule" Rossmann & Major Erich Hartmann** (WWII "Ace of Aces" with 352 victories, 80+ total)
- Rossmann's approach: "See and decide prior to firing" method — attack only when in **best possible position to win**
- Hartmann survived 1,425 combat missions, never even wounded

> "How else can we explain their ability to discover economic opportunities that most so-called geniuses were unable to see?"

- Really brilliant millionaires: selected vocation they **love** — one with few competitors but high profits

### Warning to the "Intellectually Gifted"
- Many A students assume superior intellect → superior income and wealth
- May select vocations filled with competitors who also have high analytical intellect
- "Just ask a sample of middle-aged MBAs how many dogfights they've been in in the past twenty years."

### Choice of Spouse

- 92% married, only 2% never married, ~2% currently divorced
- Decamillionaire households: only **~1 in 3** wives works outside home (vs. 70% for high-income non-millionaire couples)
- 80% believe "having a supportive spouse" was very important or important for economic success

- **Median outstanding mortgage:** just under $100,000 (~7% of current market value)
- 40% have no mortgage at all, less than 5% have outstanding balance of $1M+

### All As All Leaders?

**Fred Fiedler and Thomas Link research:**
> "Cognitive ability tests have been notoriously poor predictors of leadership performance.... Relations between intelligence and leadership and managerial performance ... accounting for less than 10 percent of the variance.... Even these low correlations are likely to be overestimates of the true relationship... Leader intelligence under certain conditions correlates negatively with performance."

- **90% of variation in leadership performance is NOT explained by standardized intelligence measures**
- Leadership qualities + tenacity can help you outpace all the whiz kids

  2. **9:00 AM arrival:** between 10:00-11:00 AM at office

**Author discovered schedule:**
- Entered computer center 2:00 AM to run large programs

- Stanley's books/papers completed faster with 3-4 hour daily writing sessions
- **Quality of writing matters, not ten-hour days**

## Profile: Donald and Winifred Sonner

### Early Life and First Fortune

- **Donald Sonner**, 64, head of Southern Bloomer Manufacturing, Bristol, Tennessee
- Never had traditional job working for others
- Became millionaire first time at age 24
- Has been "up and down the ladder many times" since
- Grew up on farm working "awful hard"

> "In those days we had to get our mail from a post office that was in a country store, and I would go to the store for the mail."

- Observed traveling peddlers with "more money than God" selling gloves, pipes, socks
- At 15, bought second-hand socks in North Carolina, couldn't sell to stores
- Sold socks at sawmill camps instead, made "pretty good profit"

> "I knew more what I was looking for."

### Building a Business on Seconds and Scrap

- Built fortune by finding unusual markets for less-than-top-quality goods
- **Business model**: "I'm in the junk business. I'm the guy that buys the seconds, the odd-lot cloth, out of textile mills. I'm their dumpster man."
- Capital advantage: has money to buy cloth whether moving it or not
- Last resort: turn unsold goods into rags and sell to industries

### The Gun Patch Innovation

- Nearly cornered market for gun-cleaning patches made from scrap cloth
- Made from scraps from cutting-room and sewing-room floors
- People initially laughed: "He can't last long"

> "But this was money from heaven: I had an endless supply. And it has grown into a substantial business for us."

### Prison Garment Market Discovery

- Began making underpants for prisoners after losing all his fortune
- Lost fortune meant no need for uniform, first-quality goods

> "There was a mental institution up the road and I said, 'They got women there.' They needed an item that would go through their big heavy steam laundry. By buying this odd-lot cloth, I could give them a lot for their money. And with this little operation, I could put a lot of sewing into it."

### Other Markets

- **Sawmills**: second-quality socks
- **Military**: gun patches
- **Prisons and mental institutions**: second-quality garments from odd-lot cloth

> "When you have something to sell, you've got to learn where there's a market."

### Partnership with Winifred

- **Winifred Sonner** runs business with him as great partner
- She spearheads marketing, he handles manufacturing

> "She's a gorgeous woman, but she's gorgeous in the inside too... and willing to fight to win."

- First wife was "a little country girl from the mountains"
- Made money selling women's apparel early on
- Money became important to first wife to point where "she was rotten with it"

> "Winifred and I are comfortable with each other regardless of where we are. We make a tremendous team. I don't know who's the boss, but there's a kind of a magic between us."

### Business Philosophy

> "You have to like what you're doing—like a rabbit dog. You chase this rabbit and forget about everything else but the rabbit. You have to live it, but you get enjoyment out of some success in it."

- Only one year of high school education

> "I do not have a great education: just a year of high school. You develop a feel for ideas that don't take much money. I don't think educational background in most cases is as important as the will to win."

### Frugal Lifestyle

- Watches costs carefully
- Doesn't try to impress others
- Could drive any car but drives '88 minivan (bought used in '90 for $5,000)
- Drives '82 Volvo (paid $3,000)

> "A self-made person is not a person generally who's trying to impress someone."

### Advice Against Borrowing

> "[Borrowing] a lot of money for a new business is the worst thing in the world. If you don't have any money, you learn how to do things without. If you have money, you make mistakes. The more money, the bigger the mistakes."

- Paying interest 24 hours/day, 7 days/week

> "If you don't have any banks breathing down your throat maybe you don't do as much—but it doesn't take so much to feed them."

### The Inventory Trap

> "Inventory is the death of a lot of small businesses. I always thought the produce business was good, because you either sell it or you smell it, so you learn to make it move."

- Must sacrifice, learn to do with less to grow business

> "You learn to do with less and, in turn, you grow the business. That's the reason second-and third-generation kids can't keep their parents' business growing. Their parents watched out for the pitfalls, and the kids don't have that experience."

### Career Choice Advice

> "You've got to do one of two things. Either have a master's degree in a good field or work like hell and use your mind to start something small."
