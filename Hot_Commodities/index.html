Glossary

Futures Mechanics

Futures contract: A standardized agreement to buy or sell a fixed quantity of a commodity at a predetermined price on a specific future date. Unlike a stock, you are buying a commitment, not an asset. Example: one NYMEX crude oil contract = 1,000 barrels at an agreed price for delivery in December.

Spot price: The current market price for immediate delivery of a commodity. If crude oil spot is $80/barrel today, that is what you pay right now.

Contango: When futures prices are higher than the current spot price — the typical "normal" state. Storage costs, financing costs, and insurance explain the premium. Rolling forward in contango costs money because each new contract is pricier.

Backwardation: When futures prices are lower than the spot price. Signals a current shortage — the market expects supply to ease later. Rolling forward in backwardation earns money (positive roll yield). Rogers uses a shift from contango to backwardation as a bullish signal.

Open interest: The total number of outstanding, unliquidated futures contracts from the previous close. Rising open interest alongside rising prices confirms conviction in a trend.

Margin: The deposit required to hold a futures position. Stocks require 50% by law; commodity futures can be as low as 2-10%. CBOT wheat margin in 2006 was $700 on a $22,500 contract — about 3%. This leverage amplifies both gains and losses.

Stop order: An instruction to automatically sell (or buy) once a price threshold is hit, limiting losses. In volatile markets, prices can gap through stops, executing at worse prices than intended.

Daily limit: Exchange-imposed cap on how much a price can move in a single session. Corn can move no more than 20 cents/contract per day. A "limit up" or "limit down" market halts trading entirely — trapping leveraged positions.

Roll yield: The return (positive or negative) earned by rolling an expiring futures contract into the next-month contract. In backwardation, rolling earns a premium. In contango, rolling costs a premium.

Open outcry: The traditional system of traders in colored jackets shouting bids and offers in a pit. The Chicago Board of Trade and CME operated this way for over a century.

Market Concepts

Hedging: Using futures to offset price risk in a physical commodity position. A chocolate maker buys cocoa futures to lock in costs. An oil producer sells oil futures to lock in revenues. Neither party expects to take or make physical delivery.

Going long: Buying a futures contract betting the price will rise. If you buy December cocoa at $1,320 and it rises to $1,360, you profit $40/ton on the contract.

Going short: Selling a futures contract you don't own, betting the price will fall. You profit by buying back cheaper later. In futures, every long position has a matching short — the market is always balanced.

Fungibility: Interchangeability. One gallon of heating oil is identical to any other gallon of the same grade. This makes commodity futures liquid, portable, and globally tradeable in a way that unique assets like paintings or stocks cannot match.

Clearinghouse: The intermediary guaranteeing every futures trade. Losers' daily losses are collected and paid to winners each night. If a trader can't cover a margin call, their position is closed immediately — preventing systemic risk.

Investment Concepts

RICI (Rogers International Commodity Index): A 36-component commodity index Rogers built in 1998 because he found all existing indexes flawed. Weighted by importance in actual global commerce rather than price or tradition. Returned +254% in its first eight years vs. +32% for the S&P 500.

CRB Index (Commodity Research Bureau): A widely followed commodity price index, but Rogers criticized it for equal-weighting all components — making crude oil worth the same as orange juice.

Hubbert's Peak: The theoretical point at which oil production peaks and then permanently declines, as originally predicted by geologist King Hubbert in 1956 for U.S. production (which peaked in 1973 exactly as he said).

Bull market signal: Rogers: a commodity bull ends when fundamental behavior changes — conservation, new supply comes online, stockpiles build. It does not end with a mild price correction.

CTA (Commodity Trading Adviser): A registered professional who manages or advises on commodity accounts. A middle option between self-directed trading and fully managed accounts.

Introduction: Lucky by Design

Jim Rogers grew up poor in Demopolis, Alabama, and landed a summer job on Wall Street in 1964 not knowing the difference between a stock and a bond. What he discovered changed his life: "someone on Wall Street would actually pay me for figuring out that a revolution in Chile would drive up the price of copper." That sentence captures everything Rogers would spend the next four decades doing.

After a scholarship to Oxford and a stint managing the post commander's money in the U.S. Army, Rogers returned to New York in 1968 with $600. He arrived at the tail end of the great postwar stock bull market — which meant he arrived with no loyalty to stocks and a willingness to look anywhere. When the commodity markets caught fire in the 1970s, he was ready. He bought his first CRB Commodity Yearbook in 1971 and began studying the charts the same way he had studied geopolitical events: as supply and demand puzzles waiting to be solved.

He co-managed the Quantum Fund with George Soros from 1968 to 1980 — a fund that reportedly returned 4,200% while the S&P 500 returned 47% over the same period. Rogers retired at 37. The Dow closed at 995 in 1966 and was back at 800 in 1982 — a 20% nominal decline after 16 years of the worst inflation in American history. When Rogers called the coming stock bull market in 1982 and everyone thought he was crazy, he was simply reading supply and demand: stocks were cheap, commodities were expensive and supply was building. In due time, Kellogg's stock went from $2 to $40.

By 1998 Rogers noticed an eerily familiar pattern — now in reverse. Most stocks were heading south while glamour tech names levitated. When he went back to his CRB Yearbooks he found commodities adjusted for inflation near their lowest levels since the Depression. Then Merrill Lynch announced it was exiting the commodities business. "I couldn't stop smiling." The last bulls were leaving.

He shopped every available commodity index and found them all useless: the CRB equally weighted crude oil and orange juice; the Dow Jones index hadn't been revised since the 1960s; Reuters' version hadn't been touched since the 1930s; the Goldman Sachs index was 65% hydrocarbons with weights that shifted unpredictably as prices rose. So Rogers built his own: the Rogers International Commodity Index, 36 commodities weighted by genuine importance in global commerce. It opened August 1, 1998, lost 11% that first year while Asia was in crisis — and Rogers was content. He then drove around the world for three years. When he returned after 9/11, the RICI was up 80%. By September 2006 it was up 254%.

The origin story he returns to again and again is 1973. A hedge fund manager had dismissed Rogers's oil thesis in the early 1970s — Rogers had explained that OPEC was established in 1960 to raise prices, that markets ignored them for a decade but supply-demand had finally tightened, that oil companies were genuinely undervalued. The manager didn't act. War broke out in 1973. Oil skyrocketed. The manager then said: "You sure were lucky." Rogers was astonished. He had done the homework. He had seen the imbalance. The war was an accident that accelerated an outcome the fundamentals already demanded. "When you've done your homework, once you recognize that supply and demand is totally out of whack, and you make your move, you are definitely going to get very lucky."

Chapter 1: The Next New Thing Is — Things

Rogers opens with a proposition that sounds almost provokingly obvious: the world is entering a commodity bull market, and it will last for years. The reason it sounds obvious is that he has spent thirty years watching people miss the previous ones — and he wants to make sure you don't miss this one.

He starts with the historical record. There have been three long commodity bull markets in the twentieth century: 1906–1923, 1933–1953, and 1968–1982. Each lasted an average of just over seventeen years. The new bull began in early 1999. The CRB Commodity Yearbook lists more than eighty actively traded commodities — oil, natural gas, wheat, corn, cotton, soybeans, aluminum, copper, silver, gold, cattle, hogs, pork bellies, sugar, coffee, cocoa, rice, wool, rubber, lumber. Natural resources are the largest nonfinancial market on the planet. Annual production of just the 35 most active commodities totals $2.2 trillion.

The supply story is the foundation of everything. No major oil field has been discovered in more than 35 years. The largest existing fields are 50 to 70 years old. The United States hasn't built a new oil refinery since 1976; the number of domestic refineries dropped from 321 to 149 between 1982 and 2004. Active U.S. drilling rigs fell from 4,530 at the end of 1981 to 1,201 in 2004. Metals are equally bare: virtually no new mine shafts have been opened worldwide in twenty years. Canadian mining CEOs told Rogers in 2004 that essentially no new mines were opening in the foreseeable future. Aluminum smelting capacity has been growing at half its historical rate since 1961.

To illustrate why supply takes so long to respond to price signals, Rogers tells the story of Oklahoma in his childhood. Driving down the highway he saw fires burning in open fields — farmers flaring natural gas. It seemed wasteful and strange. Only decades later did he understand: the 1956 Supreme Court ruling allowed the federal government to regulate gas prices, and Washington held them so artificially low that it was more economical to burn the gas than to produce and sell it. No one could make money exploring. Drilling companies went out of business. The same dynamic — government-suppressed prices destroying the productive infrastructure of an entire industry — has played out across dozens of commodities.

Demand is where the China thesis enters. Rogers visited China in 1999 and photographed a young woman on a motor scooter, talking on a cell phone, with colored hair. Armani had arrived in Shanghai. Prada and Louis Vuitton each had at least nine stores in China. Zegna had 29 shops since 1991. China had averaged 9% annual growth since 1980. It had already surpassed Japan as the world's number-two oil consumer and was the number-one consumer of copper and steel, having consumed one-third of global steel production in 2003 — more than the U.S. and Japan combined. China had moved from being a commodity exporter to a massive importer within a decade, now importing half its copper, three-fifths its iron ore, and one-third its oil.

The negative correlation between commodities and stocks is one of the book's core analytical insights. Rogers shows that for 130 years, as documented in Barry Bannister's study at Stifel Nicolaus, stocks and commodities have alternated leadership in cycles averaging eighteen years. The 1906–1923 commodity bull was matched by a stock market that went nowhere. The Roaring Twenties were a stock party during which commodity prices fell. The 1970s reversed it again. The mechanism is not mysterious: the Kellogg Company needs wheat, corn, sugar, and paper. When commodity prices are low, Kellogg's profit margins expand and its stock soars — from $2 to $40 by 1999. In the first year of the commodity bull, Kellogg's stock sank 50%.

The Yale study ("Facts and Fantasies About Commodity Futures," Gorton & Rouwenhorst, 2004) vindicated what Rogers had been arguing from experience. Since 1959, commodity futures had produced better annual returns than stocks, outperformed bonds by even more, with slightly less volatility than the S&P 500. Commodities were a better inflation hedge than either stocks or bonds. And the cumulative performance of futures was triple the performance of commodity-producing company stocks over the same period.

Rogers is careful to warn that bull markets have setbacks. During the 1968–1982 bull, the CRB rose 200% in the early years, then plunged 53% — then rose 22%, lost 15%, stalled for five years, then spiked 104% again. Gold rose from $35 to $200 in 1975 on anticipation of Americans being allowed to own it, then crashed back to $100 — before going up 8.5 times more to $870 in January 1980. Setbacks during a bull market are buying opportunities, not exits. The bull ends only when fundamental behavior changes: smaller cars, nuclear power, conservation, new fields coming online. Those days, in Rogers's view, are at least a decade away.

Chapter 2: But…

This is Rogers's mythbusting chapter — the objections every broker, relative, and dinner-party acquaintance raises when you mention commodities. He takes each one seriously and demolishes it with data.

The most common objection is the brother-in-law story: someone's relative lost his shirt speculating in soybeans. Rogers acknowledges this happens — but the culprit is leverage, not the asset. In the futures market you can buy $100 worth of soybeans for $5 of margin. If soybeans rise $5 you double your money; if they fall $5 you are wiped out. "Buy full price, cash on the barrelhead," Rogers says, and the volatility of soybeans looks just like IBM. The commodity wasn't dangerous. The margin was.

The technology objection — that computers or new inventions will make physical commodities obsolete — is one Rogers has heard every decade since he started on Wall Street. The Yankee clipper gave way to the steamship, the railroad, the automobile, the semiconductor, the internet. Through all of these revolutions came periodic multiyear commodity bull markets. The Hughes diamond drill bit was invented in the 1960s, revolutionizing oil drilling — and yet oil prices rose 1,000% in the 15 years from 1965 to 1980. Technology cannot feed you or keep you warm. You cannot make lead mines on a computer. Computers cannot make sugar grow faster than nature allows.

Many investors attributed the early 2000s commodity rally to dollar weakness or speculation. Rogers dispatches both. The dollar strengthened in spring 2004 and commodity prices kept rising. In the 1970s, inflation was running at 10% annually, the dollar was weak, the economy was in recession — and commodity prices kept rising. Speculators can only have short-term effects: if they artificially drive up oil, producers with excess supply will sell into the rally, driving prices back down. Only genuine supply-demand imbalances sustain long-term moves.

Stock brokers call commodities risky. The Yale study says otherwise. The volatility of commodity futures was slightly below that of the S&P 500 for the same period. Cisco, Yahoo!, and Microsoft were more volatile than soybeans, sugar, and metals during the 1990s. And buying commodity-producing companies introduces layers of extra risk: stock market sentiment, management quality, balance sheet problems, labor disputes, environmental litigation. Phelps Dodge (copper) fell during the early commodity bull because investors didn't trust copper, full stop. The Yale study showed futures outperformed the matching equities by 3x.

The "haven't I missed it?" objection gets Rogers's most detailed treatment. He uses lead mines as his case study. To bring a new lead mine to market, you must: find a deposit, raise capital, obtain government permits, survive environmental review, build a road through wilderness, create infrastructure, hire workers, begin mining, find smelter capacity (the last U.S. lead smelter was built in 1969), build a new one if none exists, survive another round of environmental opposition, and raise more capital. All of this takes years. Meanwhile, prices keep rising because the market knows new supply is years away. Every commodity has this lag structure: North Sea oil was discovered in 1969 and reached markets in 1977. Alaskan North Slope oil: discovered 1968, markets nine years later. A 1,200-pound steer takes two years from conception to slaughter. A coffee tree takes three to five years to bear fruit. That is why commodity bull markets last so long.

Chapter 3: Stepping Up to Commodities

Before Rogers explains how to invest in commodities, he insists you know yourself. The example he uses is himself. In 1980, he went short on oil: supplies were building, production was expanding, by 1978 supply had actually exceeded demand. The fundamentals were clear. Then Iraq invaded Iran. Two OPEC members at war. Rogers panicked and covered his short positions at higher prices — even though the fundamentals hadn't changed, even though the geopolitical shock was temporary. "I am the world's worst trader," he writes. "The best way for me to make money is to find something cheap that I like, take a position, and hold it for the long term."

He lays out a research framework built entirely around supply and demand. On the supply side: How much production exists worldwide? How many tons of reserves? Is production concentrated in areas of potential turmoil? What are existing inventories? How many mines exist? What is potential new supply over the next ten years? How long before new sources reach market? On the demand side: What is the commodity most used for? Which uses will continue and which might shift? What alternatives exist if prices get too high? What new technologies might require this commodity?

He offers four ways to invest. First: shares in commodity-producing companies or service companies. The risk here is all the non-commodity noise — management, balance sheets, stock market sentiment, government policy. Rogers thinks these risks are not worth it when you can simply own the commodity. Second: invest in commodity-rich countries. Canada and Australia have grown their economies during commodity booms; the Canadian dollar hit US$1.06 during the 1970s commodity bull, then collapsed to US$0.60 in the bear market. Chile is the world's leading copper exporter. But country risks — Venezuelan strongmen, Russian asset stripping, Nigerian corruption — add yet more noise.

Third: real estate in commodity-rich regions. Don't buy in New York or Boston, Rogers says — those cities are too dependent on financial services that suffer when commodity prices rise. Buy a lake house in Iowa or Nebraska, a property in Oklahoma oil country, a place in Montana or Colorado mining territory. When commodity prices rise, these regions prosper. Fourth, and Rogers's preferred option: buy commodities directly. "Copper doesn't care what Alan Greenspan and the Federal Reserve think." Copper will never go to zero. You can store gold bars in a bank vault; for larger, bulkier commodities, you use the futures markets.

On account types, Rogers provides a useful taxonomy. An individual (non-discretionary) account keeps you in control of every trade. A managed account gives a manager power of attorney. A CTA provides advice for a fee. Futures options — puts and calls — limit your risk to the premium paid, but Rogers warns: a Chicago Mercantile Exchange study from 1997–1999 found that more than 75% of all options held to expiration expired worthless. The odds favor sellers of options, not buyers. Commodity pools are limited partnerships; mutual funds (rare in commodities) include PIMCO's CommodityRealReturn Strategy Fund and Oppenheimer's Real Asset Fund. Finally, index investing — Rogers simply created his own.

Chapter 4: Stepping Into the Markets

Futures markets are older than you think. Japanese merchants were selling rice futures in the seventeenth century. American farmers drove crops to Chicago each fall by horse and wagon; too much supply at harvest meant prices crashed and grain rotted in the streets, while spring brought scarcity and unaffordable food. In 1848, eighty-two Chicago merchants founded the Chicago Board of Trade in a local flour store to solve this problem. The first forward contract on record (1851) was for 3,000 bushels of corn not for spot delivery. By 1865, CBOT had formalized standardized futures contracts.

The major exchanges Rogers covers: the Chicago Mercantile Exchange (CME, founded 1898, now the largest futures exchange in the U.S.); the New York Mercantile Exchange (NYMEX, established 1872 originally to trade butter and cheese, now the world's largest commodity-only exchange); the London Metal Exchange; the Shanghai Futures Exchange; the Bolsa de Mercadorias & Futuros (BM&F, Brazil's main exchange); and the Coffee Sugar & Cocoa Exchange (CSCE) in New York. At the turn of the twentieth century there were roughly 1,000 commodity exchanges in the U.S.; by Rogers's writing there were seven.

Every futures contract is defined by four standards: quantity (5,000 bushels of corn, 1,000 barrels of oil, 25,000 pounds of copper, 100 troy ounces of gold), description (heating oil vs. crude vs. natural gas), delivery date and location (predetermined months and delivery points), and payment terms (strictly cash, settled daily). The daily cash settlement is what gives futures their power — and their risk. Winners receive the daily gain in cash; losers must post additional margin by the close or their position is forcibly closed.

The speculator is "a dirty word in most circles," Rogers acknowledges, but in commodity markets the speculator is crucial. Wall Street banks, hedge funds, and private individuals willing to absorb price risk provide the liquidity and stability that volatile markets need. Fewer than 3% of futures contracts result in actual delivery — the rest are closed out before expiration. The speculator never touches or smells a commodity. Their willingness to hold risk is the grease in the system.

Rogers's own short-selling record is instructive. In 1987 he went short soybeans at over $9 per bushel, when everyone was buying madly. Prices fell and he profited. In 1980 he went short oil when the fundamentals clearly pointed to oversupply — then Iraq invaded Iran and he panicked, covering at higher prices and taking a loss. In hindsight, the short was correct: oil eventually sank. He also shorted University Computing at $48, covered at $72 for a loss, and watched the stock go to $96 — then plummet to $2. He was right on the direction but didn't have the nerve or capital to stay. "I don't like to sell something short unless it's unbelievably expensive."

The contango-versus-backwardation distinction is one of the book's more technical insights and one of its most useful. In a normal (contango) market, futures prices are higher than spot prices — the premium reflects storage, financing, and insurance costs. In backwardation, the futures price is below spot, usually signaling an acute current shortage. The "roll yield" in backwardation is positive — you earn money simply by rolling your position forward each month. In contango, rolling costs money. Rogers notes that when a commodity market shifts from contango to backwardation, it often signals the beginning of a powerful new bull move.

He also explains stop orders and daily limits with characteristic honesty about their failure modes. If you buy silver at $5.90 and set a stop at $5.80, you've theoretically capped your loss at 10 cents. But in a fast-moving market, prices can gap through the stop and execute far below where you expected. Daily trading limits are worse: corn can only move 20 cents per contract per day. If a weather event sends corn "limit up" for a week or two with no trades possible, an overleveraged position is simply trapped, hemorrhaging margin with no escape.

Chapter 5: Notes from the Wild, Wild East

Rogers traveled through China in 1999 as part of his round-the-world drive and came away with a conviction that would shape his entire investment thesis. He had seen many rapidly developing economies — South Korea, Thailand, Taiwan — but China's scale was different. One point three billion people industrializing simultaneously, with a government ruthlessly focused on economic growth, was a commodity demand event unlike anything in modern history.

China had become the world's number-one consumer of copper and steel, the number-two oil consumer after the United States, the number-one producer of TVs, and was rapidly becoming the world's leading internet user. It had moved from being a major commodity exporter to a massive importer within a decade: importing half its copper, three-fifths of its iron ore, one-third of its oil. Chinese recycling plants were being drained by European scrap dealers.

The dietary transformation was equally striking. Chinese people were eating more meat than ever before — still far below per capita levels in Europe and the U.S., but the direction was clear. More meat means more corn and soybean feed for cattle, sheep, and chickens. China was importing soybeans from as far away as Brazil and Australia. The developing Chinese sweet tooth meant rising sugar imports. Rogers saw these convergences not as abstractions but as commodity purchases that someone had to produce and ship.

On the yuan, Rogers was ahead of his time. He believed the dollar would continue weakening over the long term and that China, as the world's strongest creditor nation with enormous foreign exchange reserves, was in the same position in 2004 that the United States had been in in 1920. The U.S. dollar replaced the British pound as the world's reserve currency over the following thirty to forty years. Rogers owned Swiss francs, Singapore dollars, and Norwegian krone as alternatives while he waited. He expected the yuan — once China opened its capital account — to become one of the strongest currencies in the world.

He doesn't paper over the risks. China has had genuine setbacks. When the prime minister suggested slowing the economy in spring 2004, the prices of copper, gold, and oil all fell simultaneously. Asian and American stock exchanges fell. "When China sneezes the rest of the world will be reaching for aspirin." He compares China's trajectory to the United States in 1907 — a rising power that hit a devastating financial crisis — and to Japan in 1966, when Japan's financial system effectively collapsed just as it was becoming the world's richest country. Selling then would have been a catastrophic mistake.

China was also making deals across Africa, Latin America, and the Middle East to secure commodity resources — oil in Sudan and Angola, copper in Zambia, iron ore in Brazil. Rogers notes this without alarm: "I will let the politicians worry about that." His interest is in the commodity demand, not the geopolitics. And as for the Communist Party: Rogers considers it essentially a relic. A country where property owners demonstrate in Tiananmen Square over getting ripped off by developers, where foreign entertainers perform despite government dress-code "reviews," and where "To get rich is glorious" has replaced Marx — that is a country where capitalism has already won.

Chapter 6: Goodbye, Cheap Oil

Most people believe the 1973 oil crisis was caused by the Arab oil embargo. Rogers argues this is wrong, and getting it wrong leads investors to misunderstand oil markets fundamentally. The real story began years before the Yom Kippur War.

By the early 1970s, OPEC had been established for thirteen years with the explicit goal of raising oil prices. Markets had largely ignored them. But Nixon's 1971 price controls on oil were quietly destroying U.S. production by making it unprofitable to invest in exploration while simultaneously encouraging consumption. U.S. oil imports more than doubled between 1970 and 1973. By October 1973, it was already a seller's market. When Egypt and Syria attacked Israel on October 6 and OPEC announced a 70% price increase to $5.11 a barrel, the Saudi oil minister declared: "We are masters of our own commodity." He was wrong — the commodity was the real boss, and it had been tightening for years before a single shot was fired.

The cease-fire came within a week of OPEC's move. The embargo was relaxed by December and terminated by March 1974. But oil prices kept rising for the rest of the decade. OPEC "was not about to let politics get in the way of cashing in on those high prices." The war was an accelerant, not the cause. The fundamental supply-demand situation was what sustained the price increase for six more years.

Hubbert's Peak is the geological concept at the center of Rogers's oil argument. King Hubbert, a Shell Oil scientist, published a paper in 1956 predicting that U.S. oil production would peak by the early 1970s. Everyone laughed. By the time people were publicly mocking "Hubbert's Peak," it was already coming true. U.S. oil production peaked in 1973 and by 1981 the lower-48 states were pumping almost a third less oil. A series of geologists and oil analysts — Colin Campbell, Ken Deffeyes of Princeton, Henry Groppe after 50 years in the oil business, Matthew Simmons of Houston — were applying Hubbert's method globally, and most expected worldwide production to peak sometime between 2000 and 2015.

Matthew Simmons made the most alarming case. After reading hundreds of geological reports on Saudi fields, he concluded that Saudi Arabia's five "elephant" wells — discovered between 1940 and 1965 — had delivered 90% of all Saudi oil from 1951 to 2000, and they were peaking. The Ghawar field, source of 55–60% of all Saudi oil ever produced, was 90% depleted. No independent audit of Saudi fields had been conducted for years. Saudi Arabia had reported exactly 260 billion barrels of reserves every single year since 1988 — the same number in 2005 as in 1988, despite having produced 69 billion barrels in the intervening years. In 2005 they revised it up to 261 billion. The pattern looked less like careful accounting than political declaration.

The country-by-country tour of supply risk is sobering. Russia under Putin increased production 50% between 1999 and 2003, but the Yukos affair — Putin throwing the CEO in jail for tax fraud — underscored that property rights were illusory. Russian pipelines could handle only half of claimed production. Hundreds of millions in profits were ending up in Swiss bank accounts. The Caspian Sea region — Azerbaijan and Kazakhstan — had been ballyhooed since a $7.4 billion deal in 1994, but Rogers visited Baku in 1999 and saw only "outlaw capitalism": stripping assets instead of building infrastructure. The Tengiz field in Kazakhstan, discovered in 1979, was still producing less than 300,000 barrels per day almost thirty years later.

Nigeria had four oil refineries in 2000; not one was operating for lack of capital. Rogers witnessed parliamentary elections where MPs added $200 million to the national budget so each could buy a luxury car and take a $30,000 furniture allowance. Venezuela's Hugo Chavez had fired 18,000 oil company employees who struck in 2003, replacing them with soldiers — former managers said the 3-million-barrel-per-day capacity figure was wildly inflated. Canada's Athabasca tar sands contained potentially 1.6 trillion barrels of oil, but extracting them cost $23 billion and counting, employed 10,000 workers at premium wages, emitted 25% more greenhouse gases than conventional oil, and produced only 740,000 barrels per day. The biggest optimists hoped for an extra 1 million barrels by 2012. The world was using 85 million barrels per day.

Alternatives — solar, wind, bioenergy, geothermal — get a fair-minded review. Rogers acknowledges wind as the fastest-growing electricity source in the world, geothermal as a resource with extraordinary latent potential (the top six miles of earth's crust contain 50,000 times the energy of all known oil and gas reserves). But all of them were "still many dollars and many years away" from making a dent in oil prices for at least a decade. The best hedge against rising oil prices, he concludes, is to buy some oil. "Still a lot cheaper than a barrel of Perrier or Coca-Cola."

Chapter 7: Gold — Mystique vs. Fundamentals

Rogers opens the gold chapter by admitting his ambivalence. "Gold is not my favorite." He owns some; his baby daughter's portfolio includes it; it represents 3% of the RICI. But other commodities will do much better in this bull market, and gold's mystique — its ancient status as "the ultimate money" — tends to produce a certain type of unhinged investor who Rogers has little patience for.

The history is real and remarkable. Egyptians mined gold before 2000 B.C.; King Tutankhamun was buried in a 2,448-pound gold coffin. King Croesus of Lydia minted the first pure-gold coin in the sixth century B.C. Rome issued its first gold coin in 50 B.C. The Incas used gold to decorate virtually everything — each emperor took his gold to the tomb. Spain's Ferdinand and Isabella sent galleons to find Incan gold, funding Spain's golden age. Isaac Newton devoted twenty years after revolutionizing physics to alchemy, and later set the mint price for gold in Britain in 1717 — a price that lasted two hundred years until World War I.

But Rogers's argument is about supply and demand, not history. And by that measure, gold looks unattractive relative to other commodities. While exploration for almost every other metal was frozen, gold mining expanded. In 2003, searches for gold represented 75% of ongoing exploration by the world's largest mining companies. The "aboveground stocks" of gold — total gold ever mined and still held in some form — stood at 150,500 metric tons at end of 2003, with 61% of that mined since 1950. Central banks were sitting on over one billion troy ounces worth roughly $600 billion at 2006 prices — and they were actively selling.

The U.S. gold story is also a cautionary tale about government intervention in commodity prices. FDR banned gold exports in 1933, made it illegal for Americans to own gold, and fixed the price at $35 a troy ounce — essentially picking a round number. That price held from 1935 to 1970. At $35 there was no incentive to explore for gold; production kept declining while industrial demand for gold in dentistry and electronics quietly grew. By the 1960s, U.S. trade deficits meant dollars were piling up abroad; France turned up at Fort Knox waving dollar reserves and demanding gold. Nixon's 1971 decision to stop converting dollars to gold was a default, and it sent demand for gold soaring.

When Americans were finally allowed to own gold in 1974, the price shot to $200 — then crashed back toward $100 as foreigners dumped their gold onto eager American buyers. "The foreigners made a killing at our expense." Gold then went through the roof again: the Iranian Revolution, the hostage crisis, doubts about the dollar's survival. In January 1980 gold hit $850. Then Reagan's "morning in America," inflation was tamed, and gold entered a 25-year bear market. From 1980 on, worldwide gold production expanded for twenty consecutive years at record-high prices — everyone was looking — and over that same period gold lost 70% of its value. Too much supply.

Rogers's call is nuanced: the dollar will likely continue declining, and he can see gold climbing. Own some as insurance against genuine catastrophe. But don't make it your main commodity bet. The case for lead, sugar, and coffee is far stronger on supply-demand fundamentals. "The person who figures out how to turn gold into lead is the one who's going to get richer than Croesus."

Chapter 8: Lead — A Heavy Metal With the Potential to Fly

Lead is Rogers's textbook case for how a toxic reputation creates a commodity investment opportunity. Nobody wants to be in the lead business. Nobody wants to admit they invest in it. This near-universal aversion has starved the industry of capital for decades — which is precisely why it is interesting.

The history of lead in America involves paint, solder, leaded gasoline, bullets, and pipes. By the 1980s, government agencies had banned lead in paint, solders, and gasoline. Lawsuits followed. The Lead Industries Association, Inc., filed for bankruptcy in 2002 after 84 years due to "lack of insurance to cover litigation." In 2004, the U.S. Consumer Product Safety Commission recalled 150 million toy bracelets, rings, and necklaces from vending machines because they contained dangerous amounts of lead. This is not a commodity that generates cocktail party enthusiasm.

The supply picture is stark. U.S. mine production peaked in 1999 and fell to a six-year low of 440,000 metric tons in 2002. Seven lead mines in Missouri accounted for 96% of U.S. production. Only three smelters in the country were processing primary lead: one in Herculaneum, Missouri (112 years old); the Buick Mine and Mill in Missouri; and one in Montana. The Buick Mine and Mill was the most recently built smelter in the United States — constructed in 1969. No one had built a new one since. In 2002, a state study found that one-quarter of children living near the Herculaneum smelter had elevated blood lead levels; the EPA relocated approximately 100 families. In 2004, Doe Run closed its Viburnum No. 28 lead mine after 41 years of operation. Worldwide, lead production from mines fell 4% in 2001 and another 1.2% in 2002. The largest lead mine in the world — Cannington in Australia, owned by BHP Billiton — had opened in 1997 and was the only major new lead mine to open in decades.

Demand was rising, mainly from Asia. By 2000, 88% of apparent U.S. lead consumption was going into lead-acid storage batteries — for cars, forklifts, airport ground equipment, mining machinery, hospital power systems, and telecom networks. In 2004, only 4% of China's 1.3 billion people had cars. China made 4 million cars in 2003, up 750,000 from the previous year. Mercedes, BMW, and Cadillac had all arrived in Chinese showrooms. That 4% ownership rate represented an enormous demand trajectory for batteries. China was also the world's number-one producer of televisions — all of which used lead in their cathode-ray tubes. When digital technology replaced CRTs, Chinese consumers would be watching on computer monitors — which also use lead to block radiation.

Lead prices after zigzagging between 2000 and 2003 rose from roughly $500 per metric ton to approximately $1,500 by 2005 — triple the price, and well above the previous all-time high of $668 set in 1979. Rogers ends the chapter with what he calls the iron lesson: lead had lost two of its largest markets (paint and gasoline) in the previous twenty years. Yet prices were making all-time highs. "A huge bull market even when demand declines. The lesson is clear: Decreased demand or even a slowdown of economic growth does not necessarily mean a bear market — not if supplies are decreasing faster."

Chapter 9: Searching for the Next Sugar High

Rogers was sitting in a Paris hotel when he plucked a wrapped sugar cube from the bowl on the table and handed it to a French business journalist. "Put this in your pocket and take it home," he said, "because the price of sugar is going to go up five times in the next decade." Sugar was 5.5 cents per pound that day. The journalist pocketed the cube but, Rogers suspects, didn't act on the advice.

The previous sugar bull market gave Rogers his conviction. Between 1966 and 1974, sugar went from 1.4 cents to 66.5 cents per pound — a 45x rise. The French film "Le Sucre," starring Gérard Depardieu, was made about the chaos of that boom. By 1985, sugar had fallen back to 2.5 cents. Exchange seats at the New York Board of Trade were being given away. Rogers bought a cheap seat, leased it out for years, and eventually sold it when prices rose — a small illustration of how to benefit from commodity cycles even without buying the commodity directly.

The new supply story by the mid-2000s was compelling. In Brazil — the world's largest producer and exporter — ambitious farmers were switching to soybeans at $6 a bushel rather than growing sugar at 13.8 cents a pound. One-third of all American sugar-beet processing mills had shut down since 1996. The only producers staying in the game were the most efficient ones, propped up by government subsidies. The U.S. paid roughly $5 billion per year in sugar subsidies; the EU paid roughly $2 billion. These programs forced consumers to pay two to three times the world price and suppressed international prices by encouraging overproduction in high-cost countries. In 2004, the WTO ruled that EU subsidies violated international trade rules — a ruling that, if upheld, would lift global sugar prices by an estimated 20%.

Then came the Brazil-sugar-ethanol triangle, Rogers's most original insight on the commodity. Brazil was using half its sugar crop to produce ethanol. In 2005, 60% of new cars sold by Volkswagen, Fiat, and GM in Brazil were "flex-fuel" — able to run on either ethanol or gasoline. With oil at record highs, gasohol was 40% cheaper than gasoline, and Brazilian drivers were switching en masse. UNICA, the São Paulo Sugarcane Agro-Industry Union, predicted that 50% of the 2004–05 crop would end up in Brazilian gas tanks rather than on export markets. Brazil was exporting ethanol to the Caribbean, which redistilled it and shipped it to the United States at low duty. Sweden was a buyer. Japan, as a Kyoto signatory, was likely to become a larger ethanol customer.

China's role in the sugar story was equally powerful. China imported 672,000 tons of sugar in 2003, then announced it would import over 1.2 million tons in 2004 — its sugar-beet crop had fallen 50% and drought had hit sugarcane. China consumed about 7 kilograms of sugar per person per year, against the "globally accepted" norm of 25–30 kg and the U.S. figure of 45.3 kg (100 pounds). China was already consuming 11 million tons of sugar per year — more than the United States — and every percentage point of per capita consumption growth pointed to massive import demand. The correlation between increasing affluence and sugar consumption has held across every culture and every era.

When Rogers wrote, sugar was 85% below its all-time high; oil was at an all-time high. The Brazil-China-oil trifecta — ethanol diversion, Chinese demand, and subsidized supply erosion — pointed to one direction. Removing all trade distortions in sugar could lift global prices 47% over ten years, according to estimates Rogers cites. "In a bull market, nearly everything reaches its all-time high."

Chapter 10: Can Coffee Perk Up?

Rogers was puzzled by the line at Starbucks. Customers were paying $3 for a latte while complaining about the "high price of coffee." In reality, coffee prices in the early 2000s were at their lowest point in decades. The all-time contract low hit on December 2001: 41.50 cents per pound. The average retail can price at grocery stores was down 65 cents over the previous ten years.

Coffee has been, at certain times, the world's second most important cash commodity after crude oil. Its origins trace to Ethiopia before A.D. 1000, then to Arab cultivation — Christian monks dried the berries for wine; Arabs were drinking a roasted-bean beverage by the fifteenth century. Venetian traders brought it to Europe around 1600, Pope Clement VIII declared it "a good Christian beverage," and the first London coffeehouse opened in 1652. Dutch traders smuggled a coffee plant from the Arab port of Mocha to their colony in Java in 1690. A French naval officer stole a seedling and carried it to Martinique, from which it spread throughout tropical America. The Continental Congress declared coffee the national drink of the United States to protest the British tea tax.

The production basics matter for understanding the bull case. There are two species: robusta and arabica (milder flavor, preferred by Americans, half the caffeine). A coffee tree takes three to five years to start yielding and peaks in production around fifteen years, after which it is replaced. It takes 2,000 coffee cherries — 4,000 beans — to make one pound of roasted coffee. The average tree produces one to two pounds of roasted coffee per year. World production averaged about 106.6 million 60-kg bags per year over the decade before Rogers wrote. Brazil is the largest producer and exporter; Vietnam surpassed Colombia and Indonesia to become the world's number-two producer after production leaped by more than half in 1999–2000.

Weather is the wild card. Brazil has experienced seventeen "major freezes" in the twentieth century, roughly one every six years, and massive price spikes follow every frost announcement. The 1994 "double frost" sent coffee from about $1 per pound to nearly $3, followed by a 40% production decline. The 1975 devastating frost cut an estimated 28–30 million bag harvest to only 9.5 million bags, driving prices to an all-time high of $3.25 per pound in 1977. This asymmetry — a single weather event can destroy years of supply in weeks — is a structural feature of the coffee market.

The supply crisis Rogers describes was built from cumulative neglect. Between 1995–96 and 2002–03, world crops grew significantly in almost every year, averaging 106.6 million bags and reaching a record 120 million bags in 2002–03. With prices at historic lows, Brazilian farmers were leaving the coffee business to grow sugarcane for gasohol or soybeans for China. Those who remained couldn't afford to maintain trees or hire adequate labor. Colombian farmers were tearing out coffee trees and replacing them with coca plants. Then the USDA announced that 2003 stocks would be down 24% from the previous year-end. The 2003–04 harvest came in roughly 15% below the record 120-million-bag crop — partly due, as Rogers notes, "to the neglect of groves during the long period of weak prices."

The China and India angle on coffee is different from sugar or oil. China is a tea-drinking civilization; annual per capita coffee consumption was 0.20 kg — less than half a pound, compared to 10.1 kg in Switzerland. Starbucks had opened in China in 1999 and by the time Rogers wrote had over 184 stores, including 51 in Beijing and 72 in Shanghai. But even their cheapest drinks at $1.50 were still too expensive for most urban workers. Japan was the model case: a traditional tea culture that transformed over decades into the world's third-largest coffee consumer. Rogers says that transformation could happen in China and India — but it will take time. "All the classic elements are lining up for a bull market in coffee." When the next bull move happens, investors will talk about China and India demand. Rogers's advice: recognize the hysteria for what it is, take your profits, and look for value elsewhere.

Trading & Investing Lessons

Supply Is the Investable Variable, Not Demand

Rogers's framework consistently focuses on supply depletion as the more predictable and persistent variable. Demand can be estimated from macro trends (China's industrialization, growing middle classes), but the real money is in spotting when supply has been destroyed by low prices, neglect, regulation, or political risk. No new lead smelter in 35 years. No new oil refinery in nearly 30. These are investable facts that don't require macro forecasting — they just require reading industry data and knowing where to look.

Negative Correlation as Portfolio Architecture

The Yale study documented what Rogers had been living: commodities are negatively correlated with stocks and bonds over long periods. For a portfolio, this is valuable not just as an inflation hedge but as a genuine return source during stock bear markets. The 1970s, which destroyed stock portfolios, built commodity fortunes. Any period of sustained rising commodity prices will compress the profit margins of industrial companies — Kellogg's story in reverse. Owning commodities directly during such a period provides returns precisely when your equity book is suffering.

Know Yourself Before You Know the Market

Rogers's confession about panicking out of his oil short in 1980 is the most important investing lesson in the book. He was right about the fundamentals. He was wrong about his own temperament. The lesson isn't about oil — it's about position sizing and leverage. If you use margin, you invite panic. Rogers's solution: find something cheap, take a position, and hold for the long term without the leverage that forces premature exits.

Price Suppression Builds the Next Bull

Every commodity Rogers studies has the same pattern: an extended period of artificially low or economically depressed prices destroys the productive infrastructure of the industry. Exploration stops, mines close, trees are abandoned, workers leave. Then demand grows or a shock arrives, and the supply response takes years — because mines take years to open, trees take years to bear fruit, refineries take years to build. The price suppression period is where the bull market is manufactured. Investors who spot the depletion before the turn make the largest gains.

Bulls End in Hysteria, Not Valuation

Rogers's exit signal — farmers on Fortune covers, pork-belly pits on CNBC, supermarket shoppers talking about soybeans — is a behavioral indicator, not a valuation one. Trying to time the peak with fundamentals is difficult (oil production exceeded demand in 1978 but prices kept rising for two more years). The reliable signal is when the narrative reaches maximum saturation: when everyone knows about the bull market, when new supply is visibly ramping, when alternatives have genuinely arrived. That is the time to sell.

Contango vs. Backwardation as a Timing Tool

The roll yield structure of commodity futures provides an important timing signal that most equity investors ignore entirely. When a commodity market shifts from contango (normal, futures above spot) to backwardation (futures below spot), it signals an acute shortage and tends to coincide with the early stages of a powerful price move. Additionally, in backwardation you earn money simply by holding and rolling your position — the return structure is working for you rather than against you.

Key Takeaways

Commodity Bull Markets Are Long and Cyclical

Three bull markets in the twentieth century (1906–1923, 1933–1953, 1968–1982) each lasted around seventeen years. A new bull began in 1999. The structural supply depletion across energy, metals, and agricultural commodities — decades of underinvestment during a bear market — takes years to reverse. New mines, new oil fields, and new agricultural infrastructure don't appear overnight.

Buy the Commodity, Not the Company

The Yale study showed futures outperformed commodity-producing company stocks by 3x over 43 years. Companies carry management risk, political risk, balance sheet risk, and stock market sentiment risk. A commodity doesn't. And a commodity cannot go to zero — but Enron did.

China Is a Multi-Decade Demand Story

1.3 billion people industrializing, urbanizing, and adopting Western consumption patterns will require enormous quantities of every commodity for decades. China's dietary shifts alone — more meat, more sugar, more processed food — translate into demand for corn, soybeans, sugar, and coffee. This isn't a trade; it's a structural backdrop.

Government Suppression Destroys Supply

Every time governments have held commodity prices artificially low — natural gas in the 1950s and 60s, gold at $35 for 35 years, lead at 14 cents in the early 1970s — the suppressed price destroyed the productive infrastructure. When controls are removed, the bull market that follows can be violent because supply takes so long to rebuild.

Setbacks Are Buying Opportunities

During the 1968–1982 bull market, the CRB index fell 53% at one point. Gold fell 50% from $200 to $100 before going up 8.5x more. China tightening credit in 2004 sent copper and gold sharply lower. In each case, the underlying supply-demand imbalance was unchanged. The smart investor uses the setback to add exposure.

Read the Lag Time in Each Commodity

North Sea oil: discovered 1969, markets 1977. Alaskan North Slope: 1968 to 1977. A coffee tree: three to five years to fruit. A steer: two years from conception. A lead mine: potentially a decade from discovery to production. These lag times are the mechanical reason commodity bulls last as long as they do — and they are knowable in advance from reading the industry.

Contrarian Ideas

The 1973 Oil Crisis Had Little to Do With the Embargo

The canonical explanation of the 1973 oil shock — Arabs punishing the West for supporting Israel — is wrong, or at least incomplete. Supply had already tightened before the war, Nixon's price controls were suppressing production, and imports had doubled. The embargo ended in months but prices rose for six more years. The Arabs lifted the embargo not out of magnanimity but because they didn't want politics getting in the way of profits.

Gold Is Not the Best Commodity to Own in a Commodity Bull

The goldbug assumption — that gold is the ultimate inflation hedge and commodity play — misses the supply picture. While other commodities suffered from underinvestment, gold mining expanded. Central banks were selling. Aboveground stocks were enormous. Lead, sugar, and coffee all had better supply-demand setups than gold during the same period.

Buying Commodity Stocks Is Not the Same as Buying Commodities

Most investors who want commodity exposure buy Exxon or Phelps Dodge. Rogers argues this is fundamentally different — and inferior. You are now exposed to oil prices AND management quality AND balance sheets AND stock market sentiment AND government policy. The Yale study confirmed it empirically: futures outperformed the matching equities by 3x. Buy the thing, not the company that digs it up.

Technology Doesn't Eliminate Commodity Bull Markets

The most seductive objection to commodity investing is that technology will find substitutes and make physical commodities obsolete. But every technological revolution in the past 150 years — the railroad, the automobile, the semiconductor, the internet — has occurred alongside periodic commodity bull markets. Technology changes how we use resources; it doesn't eliminate the need for them.

A Commodity Losing Its Biggest Markets Can Still Bull

Lead lost paint and leaded gasoline — two of its largest historical demand sources — yet hit all-time price highs in 2005. The lesson cuts against naive demand analysis: if supply decreases faster than demand, prices rise regardless of demand trends. Supply is often the more important variable.

Proposals & Frameworks

The Rogers Research Framework

Before investing in any commodity, answer the supply questions: How much production exists worldwide? How many reserves remain? Is production in politically unstable areas? What are inventories? How many mines or production facilities exist? What new supply could come online in the next ten years? How long before it gets to market? And the demand questions: What is this commodity's primary use? Which uses are growing? What alternatives exist at higher prices? What new technologies require it? If you cannot answer these questions, don't invest.

The Four Commodity Investment Vehicles

Ranked by Rogers's preference: (1) Buy commodities directly via futures accounts — purest exposure, no company-specific risk. (2) Real estate in commodity-rich regions — indirect but durable exposure to regional prosperity. (3) Shares in commodity-rich countries whose currencies will rise with their resource wealth. (4) Shares in commodity-producing companies — the most diluted exposure, burdened with management, labor, and political risk. The Yale study confirmed the order: futures triple the returns of the underlying equities.

The Rogers Plan for Sugar Subsidies

Rogers proposes paying each of the approximately 5,000 American sugar producers $100,000 per year for life, a condo at the beach, and a Porsche — on the condition they never plant sugar again. Total cost: less than $5 billion per year, equal to the current subsidy, with a better outcome for consumers, the federal budget, and international sugar markets. He acknowledges this is unlikely to happen.

The RICI Construction Logic

Rogers's index includes 36 commodities weighted by their actual importance in global commerce — not by price appreciation (which would overweight recently risen commodities) and not equally (which makes crude oil equal to orange juice). The index is reviewed periodically. It includes rice (half the world eats it daily) and excludes items like hides and tallow (not traded on any major exchange). Energy is 44% because energy is 44% of global commodity commerce; precious metals are 7.1% because that reflects their actual economic weight.

Bull Market Exit Signals

Exit when: stockpiles of all commodities are rising; new oil reserves are being announced; wind farms are visible outside cities; new mines are coming online; farmers are featured on business magazine covers; commodity trading is featured on prime-time financial television; commodity prices appear in normal social conversation. The last leg of a bull market always ends in hysteria. Oil production exceeded demand in 1978 but prices kept rising until 1980 — the hysteria lag can last one to two years after fundamentals have already peaked.

What Didn't Fit

Nigeria's Dysfunction at Close Range

Rogers visited Nigeria in 2000 during parliamentary elections and witnessed first-hand the political economy at work. Members of parliament added $200 million to the national budget so each could purchase a luxury car and receive a $30,000 furniture allowance. Nigeria had four oil refineries and not one was operational for lack of capital — and the situation hadn't improved five years later. Rogers, who had traveled to 116 countries, said Nigeria had already achieved world-class distinction in one area: political corruption. "I doubt Nigeria will survive as a nation." The lesson for investors is that political corruption doesn't just waste money — it destroys the operational infrastructure that translates resource wealth into production. Nigeria's oil is real; Nigeria's capacity to extract and refine it reliably is not.

The Caspian Sea Letdown

The Caspian region — Azerbaijan and Kazakhstan — was supposed to be the next great oil frontier. A $7.4 billion deal with Western companies in 1994 generated enormous excitement. Rogers visited Baku in 1999 and saw only "outlaw capitalism": stripping available assets rather than building infrastructure. The Tengiz field in Kazakhstan had been discovered in 1979 — Russians didn't have the technology to develop it — and nearly thirty years later was producing less than 300,000 barrels per day. The Kashagan field was claimed to hold 40 billion barrels but only 6–9 billion were recoverable. Rogers's verdict: "I wouldn't invest a nickel there." Former Soviet Central Asia, he wrote, was "a disaster about to spiral into a catastrophe." The lesson is that reported reserves and actual producible reserves are entirely different numbers, and political and infrastructure context determines which one matters.

Lead's Ancient History and Modern Toxicology

Rogers engages with the Roman lead-poisoning myth that historians have debated for decades. Roman water systems used lead pipes (plumbarii = Roman plumbers, giving us the word "plumbing"). The architect Vitruvius actually warned around 90–20 B.C. that clay pipes were healthier than lead. But modern historical research suggests water moved too quickly through Roman aqueducts to pick up much lead contamination, and terra-cotta cisterns provided most drinking water. Romans did occasionally suffer lead toxicity — from boiling wine in lead cauldrons — but probably didn't die en masse from their plumbing. The Roman Empire's fall had more to do with "occupying too much territory too far away with too many troops," Rogers notes, adding: "a history lesson that the U.S., with troops now in more than 100 countries, might contemplate." Characteristically, a detour through Roman history lands as contemporary political commentary.

Congress's Lead Price Control Experiment

The interplay of government intervention and commodity price cycles gets specific illustration in lead. Congress passed the Lead and Zinc Mining Stabilization Program in 1961, paying miners when lead prices fell below 14.5 cents per pound. The program lasted until 1969. Price controls in the early 1970s held the price at roughly 14 cents per pound. When those controls were lifted in December 1973, the U.S. lead price moved almost straight up for the next six years, reaching a monthly high of 57–59 cents per pound in December 1979 — a four-fold increase. The pattern is identical to natural gas in the 1950s and gold from 1935 to 1970: suppressed prices prevent investment in production, so when controls are removed, there is nothing to prevent a violent price spike. The intervention intended to help buyers becomes the cause of the eventual price shock.

Coffee's Colonial Journey

Coffee's path from the highlands of Ethiopia to the lips of every American office worker is a story of colonial ambition and botanical theft. Dutch traders smuggled a coffee plant from the Arab port of Mocha to their colony in Java in 1690 — giving us the word "java." A French naval officer managed to steal a single seedling and carry it across the Atlantic to Martinique, from which it spread throughout tropical America. The Continental Congress, in an act of anti-British defiance in 1773 following the Boston Tea Party, declared coffee the national drink of the United States. The progression from Ethiopian monastery to French navy officer to revolutionary politics to Starbucks in Beijing spans eight hundred years and illustrates exactly the kind of long-arc narrative that Rogers believes matters for investors: the commodities we take for granted have reached us through chains of accident, ambition, and politics — and those chains are still being built.

Hot Commodities: How Anyone Can Invest Profitably in the World's Best Market
Jim Rogers · 2004 · 240 pages
A contrarian investment case for commodities at the dawn of a generational bull market, grounded in supply-demand fundamentals, the China demand shock, and validated by the landmark 2004 Yale academic study.

About the Author

Jim Rogers grew up in Demopolis, Alabama, the son of a family with modest means, and won a scholarship to Yale University before attending Oxford on a scholarship as well. He arrived in New York in 1968 with $600 and began working in finance, eventually co-founding the Quantum Fund with George Soros — one of the most successful hedge funds in history. From 1968 to 1980, the fund reportedly returned 4,200% while the S&P 500 returned 47%. Rogers retired at 37, financially independent, and began a second life as a global investor, motorcycle adventurer, and author.

He is perhaps as famous for his travels as his investments. In the 1990s he rode a motorcycle around the world; in 1999–2002 he drove a custom Mercedes through 116 countries and 152,000 miles with his partner Paige Parker (whom he married on January 1, 2000 in Iceland). That journey produced the book "Investment Biker" and its sequel "Adventure Capitalist," both documenting his boots-on-the-ground research method: visiting countries, talking to business owners, watching economies in real time. He later moved to Singapore with his family because he believed Asia would be the economic center of the twenty-first century.

Rogers has been a professor at Columbia University's business school and a frequent commentator on global markets. He created the Rogers International Commodity Index in 1998 and has been one of the most consistent and early advocates for commodity investing since the late 1990s. He is also the author of "A Bull in China" and "Street Smarts." His investment philosophy is simple and consistent: find something undervalued on supply-demand fundamentals, take a position, and hold it. He is as open about his failures — panicking out of his oil short in 1980, missing moves because he sold too early — as he is about his successes.

Why This Book Matters

Published in 2004 at the beginning of the commodity supercycle, Hot Commodities arrived when most professional investors had forgotten commodities existed. The decade from 1982 to 1999 was the greatest stock bull market in American history, and anyone who spent it in commodities was considered a crank. Rogers was not a crank — he was early, and he was right. By the time the book appeared, his RICI index had already returned 80% since its 1998 launch. By 2006 it was up 254%. Oil had not yet hit $100. Gold had not yet hit $1,000. The decade ahead would prove every major supply-depletion thesis in the book correct.

The book matters for a structural reason beyond its specific commodity calls: it teaches investors to think about the physical world rather than just financial markets. Stock investors obsess over earnings, multiples, and management. Rogers asks: how many mines are open? How old are the oil fields? How long does it take a coffee tree to bear fruit? These are not glamorous questions, but they are the questions that determine where commodity prices go over the next ten to twenty years. In a world where financialization had made most investors forget that physical commodities are the raw material of everything, Rogers's framework was genuinely corrective.

The book also marked a turning point in how academics and practitioners treated commodity investing. The Yale study that Rogers uses as his academic anchor was itself brand new in 2004 — it had never been rigorously established that commodity futures could be a core asset class with equity-like returns and lower volatility. That study, and Rogers's popularization of its findings, contributed to the wave of commodity ETFs, institutional commodity allocations, and commodity index funds that emerged over the following decade. Jim Rogers didn't just call the bull market in commodities — he helped build the infrastructure that allowed ordinary investors to participate in it.
