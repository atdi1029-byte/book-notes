# Hot Commodities: How Anyone Can Invest Profitably in the World's Best Market
**Jim Rogers | 2004 | 240 pages**

---

## Core Thesis

Commodities enter long-term bull markets every generation. Rogers identified the
new bull in 1998-1999 and argues it will last at least another decade. The
argument rests on two pillars: supply has been severely depleted (no new mines,
aging oil fields, underinvestment across the board) while demand is exploding
(China's industrialization, growing middle classes worldwide). The academic
case was finally validated by the Yale study (Gorton & Rouwenhorst, 2004),
which showed commodity futures have produced returns comparable to stocks
with less volatility — and triple the returns of commodity-producing company
stocks.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Three bull markets per century:** 1906–1923, 1933–1953, 1968–1982. A new
   one began in 1999. Each lasted ~17 years.

2. **Supply is the key variable.** When prices are low for extended periods,
   producers go out of business, exploration stops, infrastructure atrophies.
   The next demand increase finds empty shelves.

3. **Commodities and stocks are negatively correlated.** Low commodity prices
   = higher corporate profits = stock bull market. The reverse holds. Cheap raw
   materials fueled the 1980s-90s stock boom; rising commodity costs will eat
   into those profits.

4. **China is the demand shock.** 1.3 billion people industrializing rapidly.
   Already the world's No. 1 consumer of copper and steel, No. 2 oil consumer.
   Changed from commodity exporter to massive importer within a decade.

5. **Don't buy commodity stocks — buy commodities.** Yale study: futures
   produced triple the returns of commodity-producing equities over 43 years.
   Copper doesn't care what Alan Greenspan thinks. It can't go to zero.

6. **Price controls destroy supply.** Every suppressed commodity eventually
   erupts: natural gas flared because it was too cheap to produce; gold at $35
   for 35 years; lead at 14 cents while demand built. Removal of controls = bull.

7. **Bull markets end in hysteria, not logic.** The exit signal: pork-belly pits
   on CNBC, supermarket ladies talking soybeans, farmers on Fortune covers.
   Supply has caught up; fundamental shift in how people use energy has occurred.

8. **Know thyself.** Rogers panicked shorting oil when Iraq invaded Iran — right
   thesis, wrong nerves. Most people should use index funds or hold positions
   without leverage.

## Book Structure

- **Introduction:** Rogers's background, Quantum Fund, why he built the RICI
- **Chapter 1:** Bull thesis — supply depletion, China demand, historical cycles,
  negative stock correlation, warnings about setbacks
- **Chapter 2:** Myths debunked — brother-in-law, technology, weak dollar,
  broker risk, "haven't I missed it"
- **Chapter 3:** How to invest — research framework, four alternatives (company
  stocks, commodity-rich countries, real estate, commodities directly), account
  types
- **Chapter 4:** Futures markets primer — history, mechanics, hedging, going
  long/short, margins, contango vs. backwardation
- **Chapter 5:** China deep dive — why the growth is real, yuan as future
  reserve currency, downside risks
- **Chapter 6:** Oil — 1973 embargo real story, Hubbert's Peak, Saudi reserves
  debate, Russia, Caspian, Nigeria, Venezuela, alternatives still a decade away
- **Chapter 7:** Gold — why Rogers is skeptical (oversupply, high aboveground
  inventories, central banks selling), but owns some as insurance
- **Chapter 8:** Lead — textbook supply-demand case; toxic reputation = no new
  investment = coming shortage + China battery demand
- **Chapter 9:** Sugar — Brazil-China-oil trifecta; ethanol diversion; WTO
  subsidies ruling; historical 45x price rise 1966-1974
- **Chapter 10:** Coffee — all-time low 2001, farmers quitting, 3-5 year tree
  lag guarantees delayed supply response
- **Conclusion:** Yale study vindication; how to recognize the top

## The RICI

Rogers International Commodity Index (36 components):
- Energy 44% (crude oil, Brent, gasoline, natural gas, heating oil)
- Grains & Oilseeds 19.94% (wheat, corn, soybeans, rice, barley...)
- Industrial Metals 14% (aluminum, copper, zinc, lead, nickel, tin)
- Softs 9.96% (cotton, coffee, sugar, cocoa, wool, OJ)
- Precious Metals 7.1% (gold, silver, platinum, palladium)
- Livestock 3% (cattle, hogs)
- Other 2% (rubber, lumber)

Performance August 1998 – September 2006: RICI +254%, S&P 500 +32%,
Lehman Long Treasury Bond +67%.

## What Makes This Book Special

Rogers writes like the man who drove around the world in a car (he did —
116 countries, 152,000 miles). He visited Nigeria during elections and watched
MPs buy luxury cars with public money. He stood in Baku's "outlaw capitalism."
He talked to Canadian mining CEOs, read Matthew Simmons's Saudi field reports,
and handed sugar cubes to French journalists. The result is an investment thesis
grounded in boots-on-the-ground observation, not models.

The book is also deeply honest: Rogers admits he panicked on his own short
position when Iraq invaded Iran, that he's "the world's worst trader," and that
he can't guarantee any commodity will do well. The intellectual humility makes
the underlying conviction — commodities are entering a generational bull market
— far more credible.
