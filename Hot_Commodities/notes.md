# Hot Commodities: How Anyone Can Invest Profitably in the World's Best Market
**Jim Rogers | 2004 (UK paperback 2007) | 287 pages**

## INTRODUCTION (pp. 12–20)

Rogers's origin story: Poor kid from Demopolis, Alabama. Summer job on Wall
Street in 1964 knowing nothing — didn't know the difference between a stock
and a bond. Revelation: "someone on Wall Street would actually pay me for
figuring out that a revolution in Chile would drive up the price of copper."

Scholarship to Oxford (politics, philosophy, economics). Used scholarship money
to invest. After Oxford, U.S. Army, investing the post commander's money in
stocks for a tidy return. Returned to NY in 1968 with $600.

Arrived at the tail end of the post-WWII bull market in stocks. Learned the
primary lesson: buy value on the cheap. Because he had no loyalty to stocks, he
was willing to look anywhere. Noticed commodities in the bear market of the
1970s. Bought his first CRB Commodity Yearbook in 1971.

Studied commodity charts: sharp rises → analyzed why. Studied supply/demand
trends, aging mines, exploration activity, weather impacts. Historical knowledge:
the Civil War cut off cotton to England → English planted cotton everywhere →
price crashed. This pattern was essential to understanding 1970s commodity
rises.

Co-managed offshore hedge fund (not named but it was Quantum Fund with
Soros). Retired at 37 in 1980. Dow closed at 995 in 1966, was back at 800 in
1982 — 20% decline after 16 years (ignoring inflation which was the worst in
U.S. history). By 1979, Business Week: "Equities Are Dead!"

Rogers called the stock bull market in 1982 when everyone thought he was crazy.
Dow moved from 800 to 11,000 over next ~17 years. S&P and Dow rose 10x
during 1980s-90s. Nasdaq soared 25x from 1980 level.

In 1998, noticed most stocks heading south while glamour tech stocks (Cisco,
Nortel, JDS Uniphase, Microsoft) kept rising. Went back to CRB Yearbooks.
Found commodities incredibly undervalued — when adjusted for inflation,
approaching lows not seen since the Depression. Simultaneously, Merrill Lynch
announced it was exiting the commodities business. "I couldn't stop smiling."

**Creating the RICI:** Shopped existing indexes.
- CRB Futures Index: 17 components equally weighted → crude oil = orange
  juice. Rejected.
- Dow Jones Commodities Index: Editor of WSJ didn't even know his paper had
  one. Hadn't been revised since the 1960s.
- Reuters Commodity Index: Reuters executive had no idea his company had one.
  Hadn't been revised since the 1930s.
- Goldman Sachs Commodity Index (GSCI): 65% weighted toward hydrocarbons.
  Weights change based on price increases — unpredictable year to year.
- Journal of Commerce: Included hides and tallow (not traded on any exchange).
  Didn't include rice (half the world eats it daily). Too Americancentric.

Built his own: Rogers International Commodity Index (RICI). 36 commodities
reflecting global commerce. Opened August 1, 1998. Lost 11.14% end of 1998
— but he was content. Started three-year round-the-world drive (116 countries,
152,000 miles, married Paige Parker on Jan 1, 2000). When he returned after
9/11, RICI was up 80%. By September 2006, up 254%.

The 1973 oil story: Rogers explained to a hedge fund manager in the early 1970s
that oil and gas were being depleted, pipelines had few reserves, prices were
dirt cheap, buy oil companies. Man scorned advice. War broke out in 1973, oil
skyrocketed. Man said "You sure were lucky." Rogers was astonished. He had
analyzed: OPEC established in 1960 to raise prices — markets ignored them for
a decade, but by 1970s supply-demand balance had shifted back. The Arabs
called off the embargo by December 1973 because they didn't want to let
politics get in the way of high prices. Prices continued for years after.

**Core thesis:** When you've done your homework, when you recognize supply
and demand is totally out of whack, and you make your move, "you are
definitely going to get very lucky."

## CHAPTER 1: THE NEXT NEW THING IS—THINGS (pp. 21–46)

The commodities listed in CRB Yearbook's 80+ traded items: oil, natural gas,
wheat, corn, cotton, soybeans, aluminum, copper, silver, gold, cattle, hogs,
pork bellies, sugar, coffee, cocoa, rice, wool, rubber, lumber.

"You really cannot be a successful investor in stocks, bonds, or currencies
without understanding commodities." Commodities belong in every diversified
portfolio.

For most investors, commodities = scary story about brother-in-law who lost
his shirt in soybeans. Like Americans who fear foreign travel because of the
language barrier, investors fear commodities because they don't know them.

**Why cheap raw materials fueled the 1980s/90s stock boom:** Raw materials
were in a bear market → removed cost/margin pressures from companies →
higher profits. Smart investors who saw the commodity bear ending in late 1990s
knew the stock bull was also ending.

Natural resources = largest nonfinancial market on the planet. Annual
production of just 35 most active commodities = $2.2 trillion. Volume of
dollars traded on commodity exchanges = several times that of all U.S. stock
exchanges.

**Three long commodity bulls in the 20th century:** 1906–1923, 1933–1953,
1968–1982. Each lasted an average of just over 17 years. New bull began early
1999.

### Why Now? Supply-Demand Out of Whack

Oklahoma gas story: Rogers as a child saw fires in fields along Oklahoma roads.
Only decades later understood: 1956 Supreme Court ruled government could
regulate natural gas prices. Washington kept prices so cheap it was more
economical to "flare" (burn off) gas than produce it. No financial incentive to
explore. Drilling companies went out of business. This same dynamic played
out across commodities.

**Oil:** No major oil field discovered in more than 35 years. Largest discoveries
50-70 years old. U.S. production peaked early 1970s and declined — including
Alaska. North Sea peaked 1999. U.S. hasn't built new oil refinery since 1976.
Number of domestic refineries decreased more than half since 1982 (321 to
149). Active U.S. rigs: 4,530 at end of 1981 → 1,201 in 2004.

**Natural gas:** North American production not keeping pace with demand.
Known gas deposits getting old. Capital investment dollars absent for years.
Pipeline infrastructure incomplete (Canadian and Alaskan gas). Environmental
regulations limit drilling to 3.5 months/year in some cases. 7 LNG terminal
projects canceled due to environmental opposition.

**Metals:** Virtually no new mine shafts opened in 20 years worldwide.
Canadian mining CEOs in 2004 confirmed virtually no new mines opening in
near future. Aluminum smelting capacity growing at half its annual rate since
1961. 8 of 10 Pacific Northwest aluminum smelters idled/closed. Last lead
smelter built in U.S. in 1969. More than half of all mining exploration = for
gold alone.

**Sugar:** No one opening new sugar plantations. World sugar demand beginning
to exceed supply. Sugar also becoming energy commodity: ethanol. Brazil using
half its sugar crop to power cars. 60% of Brazil's new cars (2005) were flex-fuel.

**The China Effect:** Rogers in China in 1999, photographed young Chinese girl
on motor scooter talking on cell phone with colored hair. Armani in Shanghai.
Prada and Louis Vuitton with at least 9 stores each in China. Zegna with 29
shops since 1991. China: fastest growing economy since 1980, averaging 9%/yr.
Passed Japan as No. 2 oil consumer. No. 1 consumer of copper and steel.
Consumed one-third of global steel in 2003 — more than U.S. + Japan combined.
Imports half its copper, three-fifths its iron ore, one-third its oil.

Chinese sweet tooth: candy/confectioneries becoming fashionable gifts. Sugar
imports rising. China importing soy from as far away as Brazil and Australia.

**Ships shortage:** Not enough ships worldwide to ferry existing commodities
to destinations. Those operating using more expensive fuel.

In mid-1999, commodity prices adjusted for inflation = lower than at any time
since the Depression. Crude oil record high in 1970s would be over $90/barrel
in then-current dollars.

**Negative correlation with stocks:** Historical charts show stocks and
commodities as parallel lines going in separate directions. 1906-1923:
commodities hot, stocks nowhere. Roaring Twenties: stocks hot, commodities
cold. 1970s: hot commodities, cold stocks. 1980s-90s: opposite. Now turning
back to commodities.

Barry Bannister study (Stifel Nicolaus): for past 130 years "stocks and
commodities have alternated leadership in regular cycles averaging 18 years."

**Rogers's theory for negative correlation:** Kellogg Company needs wheat,
corn, sugar, paper. When commodity prices low → Kellogg makes bigger profits.
Kellogg stock went from $2 to $40 in 1999 during the commodity bear market.
First year of commodity bull market → Kellogg stock sank 50%. Kellogg's stock
did virtually nothing during the 1970s commodity bull market.

**Yale study ("Facts and Fantasies About Commodity Futures," Gorton &
Rouwenhorst):** Returns from commodity investments "negatively correlated"
with equity and bond returns. Commodities have been better hedge against
inflation than stocks and bonds for past 45 years.

**Commodity returns outpace inflation:** Inflation IS higher commodity prices.
Higher commodity prices are "the majorettes leading the musicians down the
street." Investing in commodities = beat the band.

### Warnings

**Warning 1: There Will Be Setbacks.** Last commodity bull (1968-1982):
commodities went up/down slightly for first 3 years, then spiked 200%+ on CRB,
went down, then up, then plunged 53% over next year. Gained 22% by 1977,
then lost 15%, then stalled for 5 years — before another 104% spike. Gold
went from $35 to $200 in 1975 (anticipation of Americans being allowed to
own it), then fell 50% back to $100. Then gold went up 8.5x more to $870 in
January 1980. When prices go down: "the smart investor will buy more."

**Warning 2: China Setbacks.** When China prime minister suggested slowing
economy in spring 2004, prices of copper, gold, oil fell. When China sneezes,
investors panic. But 1.3 billion Chinese aren't about to disappear. Buy more
commodities cheap during those setbacks.

**Consolidation vs. End of Bull Market:** A consolidation = temporary decrease
due to glitch in supply-demand. China tightening loans → crimp in metals prices,
but if worldwide supplies still low and demand high, prices rise again when
China restarts importing.

A bull market ENDS when fundamental changes in how we live occur. 1972:
Club of Rome predicted world running out of resources → oil spiked. But then:
Carter era sweaters and thermostats, smaller cars, Europeans switching to
nuclear power, North Sea and Alaska oil coming online. By 1978 oil production
exceeded demand for first time in years — major fundamental shift. (Prices
continued 2 more years: "the last leg of a bull market always ends in hysteria;
the last leg of a bear market always ends in panic.")

Signals bull is over: new oil reserves discovered, wind farms outside cities,
new mines coming online, stockpiles of all commodities rising. "Those days,
in my opinion, are a decade away, at least."

## CHAPTER 2: "BUT…" (pp. 47–54)

Common myths debunked:

**"My relative got wiped out."** He was inexperienced and buying on thin margin.
You can buy $100 of soybeans for $5 margin → if soybeans go up $5 you double,
but if they go down $5 you're wiped out. "Buy full price, cash on the barrelhead"
and the volatility is just like IBM.

**"Technology makes commodities obsolete."** Yankee clipper → replaced by
steamship → railroad (the original Internet). Telephone, radio, TV (more
"Internets"), automobile, semiconductor. Through all these revolutions came
periodic, multiyear commodity bull markets. Hughes diamond drill bit invented
in 1960s → explosion in oil drilling technology → oil prices still went up
1,000% in 15 years 1965-1980. Technology cannot feed us or keep us warm.
You can't make lead mines on a computer. Computers cannot make sugar grow
faster than nature allows.

**"It's just speculation and the lower dollar."** Dollar strengthened in spring
2004 and commodity prices kept rising. The 1970s: inflation 10%/yr, dollar
weak, economy in major recession — commodity prices kept rising. Speculators
can only have short-term effect: if they artificially drive up oil, producers with
excess supply will dump oil driving price back down.

**"My stock broker says it's risky."** More volatility in Nasdaq in recent years
than in any commodities index. Cisco, Yahoo!, Microsoft more volatile than
soybeans, sugar, metals. Yale study: "high risk" of commodities doesn't square
with facts. Average annual return on commodities index "comparable to return
on S&P 500." Volatility of commodities futures "slightly below" that of S&P
500 for same period. Stocks have "more downside risk relative to commodities."

Buying commodity-producing companies instead: "even riskier bet." Phelps
Dodge (copper) affected by stock market sentiment, balance sheet, management,
labor problems, environmental issues. Yale study: "the cumulative performance
of futures has been triple the cumulative performance of 'matching' equities."
And: commodities cannot go to zero, while Enron can (and did).

**"Haven't I missed out?"** Lead mine case study: Takes years — find deposit,
raise capital, government permission, environmental compliance, build road
through wilderness, create infrastructure, finally mine, then need smelter (last
U.S. lead smelter built 1969), face environmental opposition again, find money
to build it. Meanwhile prices keep rising because new supplies take so long.

Every commodity takes time: North Sea oil discovered 1969 → market 1977.
Alaska North Slope discovered 1968 → market 9 years later. A 1,200-lb steer
takes 2+ years from conception to slaughter. One coffee tree takes 3-5 years
to bear fruit. That's why bull markets in commodities last so long.

Investment in commodities has not just done better than commodity-producing
companies over 43 years — returns are triple those companies.

## CHAPTER 3: STEPPING UP TO COMMODITIES (pp. 55–71)

**Know thyself first.** Rogers panicked shorting oil in 1980 when Iraq invaded
Iran — covered his short positions at higher prices even though the poor
fundamentals hadn't changed. Everyone panics.

Rogers: "I am the world's worst trader. The best way for me to make money is
to find something cheap that I like, take a position, and hold it for the long
term."

**Choosing what to invest in:** CRB Yearbook lists 100+ commodities. RICI
has 36. Five sectors: Energy, Metals, Grains, Food/Fiber, Livestock. Start with
one or two sectors.

**Index criticisms:**
- Reuters-CRB 2005 revision: gives aluminum 600% greater weight than wheat.
  Aluminum is wonderful but most people never use it; nearly everyone uses
  wheat daily.
- Dow Jones-AIG: Natural gas weighting up 250% in recent years but production
  is declining. Cocoa disappeared from index. Has world stopped producing
  cocoa? Livestock was 26% of GSCI in 1993, 4% in 2005 — livestock
  production down 80-90%? No. All three indexes increase weightings as prices
  rise — not how real world works.
- RICI: attempts to balance according to importance in international commerce.

**Research framework — Supply questions:**
- How much production worldwide?
- How many tons of reserves?
- Is production in areas of potential turmoil?
- What are existing inventories?
- How many mines exist?
- What is potential supply over next 10 years?
- New sources of supply? Old mines expanding? When? Cost? Output?
- Time before new supplies get to market?

**Research framework — Demand questions:**
- What is commodity most used for?
- Which uses will continue?
- What alternatives exist if prices get too high?
- What new technologies might require this commodity?

If plastic pipes wipe out copper demand → smart investor recognizes: plastic is
an oil product → more plastic piping = more oil demand.

**Four investment alternatives:**
1. Shares in commodity-producing companies or service companies. Risks:
   stock market sentiment, government policies, management quality, balance
   sheets. "Why go to all the extra trouble and risk of buying stocks?" RICI up
   254% over 8 years; S&P up 32%; Lehman Long Treasury Bond up 67%.

2. Invest in commodity-rich countries. Canada and Australia have grown
   economies. Canadian dollar worth US$1.06 during 1970s commodity bull;
   declined to ~US$0.60 in subsequent commodity bear. Chile = world's leading
   copper exporter. But: country risks (Venezuela strongman, Russia stripping
   assets, Nigeria corruption, South Africa instability).

3. Real estate in commodity-rich areas. Don't buy in New York or Boston —
   too dependent on financial services. Buy lake house in Iowa, Nebraska; farm
   states; oil/gas areas (Oklahoma); mining states (Montana, Colorado).

4. Buy commodities directly. "Copper doesn't care what Alan Greenspan and
   the Federal Reserve think." Copper will never go to zero. Can store gold
   bars in bank vault; for larger commodities → futures markets.

**Account types:**
- Individual Account (Non-Discretionary): you control every trade
- Managed Account (Discretionary): manager has power of attorney
- Commodity Trading Adviser (CTA): provides advice for a fee
- Trading Futures Options: puts/calls, only risk is premium paid. Chicago Merc
  examined 1997-1999: 75%+ of all options held to expiration ended worthless.
  Odds are with sellers of options.
- Commodity Pool: limited partnership, diversified fund
- Mutual Funds: rare for commodities. PIMCO CommodityRealReturn Strategy
  Fund (PCRAX) $5B; Oppenheimer Real Asset Fund (QRAAX) $885M.
- Index Investing: Rogers creates own index fund.

## CHAPTER 4: STEPPING INTO THE COMMODITIES MARKETS (pp. 72–107)

**History of futures:** American farmers transported crops by horse and wagon
each fall. Too much supply → prices crashed, grain rotted in streets. Spring:
too little → unaffordable. 1848: 82 Chicago merchants founded Chicago Board
of Trade in a local flour store. 1851: earliest forward contract recorded (3,000
bushels of corn not for spot delivery). 1865: CBOT formalized with standardized
futures contracts.

Japanese were selling rice futures in the 17th century.

**The exchanges:** Chicago Mercantile Exchange (CME) founded 1898, now
largest futures exchange in U.S. New York Mercantile Exchange (NYMEX)
established 1872 to trade butter and cheese, now world's largest exchange
dealing only in commodities. At turn of 20th century ~1,000 commodity
exchanges in U.S.; now seven.

Also: London Metal Exchange (LME), Shanghai Futures Exchange, Bolsa de
Mercadorias & Futuros (BM&F, Brazil's main commodity exchange), Coffee
Sugar & Cocoa Exchange (CSCE) of NYBOT.

**Fungibility:** Unlike stocks, paintings, automobiles, stamps. One gallon of
heating oil = any other gallon. 5,000 bushels of wheat here = 5,000 bushels
there. That makes commodity futures portable, easily transferred, highly liquid.
"Copper is copper all over the world. If there is too much, the price will go
down. If there is too little, it will rise."

**Four standards in every futures deal:**
1. Quantity — 5,000 bushels corn, 1,000 barrels oil, 25,000 lbs copper, 100 troy
   oz gold
2. Description — heating oil vs. crude oil vs. natural gas
3. Delivery date and location — predetermined months and locations
4. Payment terms — strictly cash, settled daily

**Open outcry:** Traders in colored jackets screaming bids and offers in a pit.
"Controlled chaos and energy of hundreds of men in colored jackets waving
pieces of paper."

**Hedging:** Chocolate company owns cocoa futures to protect against Ivory
Coast civil war sending cocoa prices to the sky. Buy December cocoa futures
at $1,300/ton in June. If Ivory Coast erupts → price spikes, futures value rises,
offsets higher costs. If peace breaks out, price drops → hedger locked in at
$1,300, can buy in cash market at lower rate. "Having insurance doesn't prevent
bad things from happening."

Oil producer hedges: pumping sweet light crude with 1,000 barrels in the
ground. Price is $60/barrel now but uncertain in 6 months. Sell futures at
NYMEX locking in $60. Hedge against price falling before barrels are pumped.

**The speculator:** "A dirty word in most circles. But in the world of commodities
the speculator is crucial." The grease in the system. Wall Street banks, hedge
funds, private individuals — never have to touch or smell a commodity. Less
than 3% of futures contracts result in delivery. Speculators close out before
expiration dates. Their willingness to bid on contracts gives volatile markets
stability and liquidity.

**Going Long:** Buy futures contract, hold it, betting price will appreciate.
Cocoa example: speculator bids $1,315 for December cocoa, gets it at $1,320.
Next day violence in Ivory Coast, price shoots up $40. Can sell for profit or
wait.

**Going Short:** Sell high, buy low. Short selling: borrow assets (or in futures,
just put up collateral) and sell them, then buy back cheaper. In futures, short
position pairs with every long position.

Rogers shorting story: Went short soybeans in 1987 when prices over $9.00/
bushel, everyone buying madly. Prices fell. Lesson: short hysteria.

Shorted oil in 1980 — production and conservation building up supplies, by
1978 supplies exceeded demand but prices kept rising. Decided to bet against
oil. Then Iraq invaded Iran. Two OPEC members at war — wrong time to short
oil. Covered shorts as prices skyrocketed. In hindsight should have hung in —
oil was genuinely overpriced and eventually sank.

"I don't like to sell something short unless it's unbelievably expensive."
University Computing story: shorted at $48, covered at $72 taking a loss — stock
then went to $96, then plummeted to $2. Was right but didn't have guts or money
to stick. JDS Uniphase: went from $20 to $129 before plummeting toward zero.

When gold at $150 in 1970s, might have seemed expensive to short. But it went
to $860. When sugar at 40 cents, seemed expensive. Went to 66 cents before
falling back.

**Stop orders:** Set automatic sell trigger to limit losses. Buy silver at $5.90,
set stop at $5.80 (limit loss to 10 cents/oz). If price hits $5.80, broker executes
sell. Warning: in highly volatile market, prices can move so fast that desired
stop price may be impossible to execute. Also daily trading limits: corn can go
up or down no more than 20 cents/contract/day. If corn at $3.00 and rises to
$3.20, all trading stops. Can go "limit up" or "limit down" for a week or two
with no trades possible. "Woe to the investor who is overleveraged at times
like this."

**Margins:** Stock: at least 50% deposit required by law. Commodities: as low
as 2%, typically 5-10%. CBOT initial margin for wheat in 2006: $700 (~3% of
$22,500 contract). Corn: $450 (~4% of $11,250 contract). NYMEX oil margin
2006: $4,725 (~8% of $60,000+ contract).

Soybeans leverage example: Buy 5,000 bushels at $5/bushel ($25,000 total) for
$1,250 margin (5%). Price rises 10% → contract worth $27,500 → $2,500 profit
= tripling your money. But if price falls 10% → $2,500 loss, wiped out margin
plus in the hole $1,250. "Beans declined 10 percent, but you lost 200 percent."

"This is why that brother-in-law lost his shirt in soybeans."

Daily reckoning: winners collect from losers. If can't ante up for day's losses,
position closed immediately. Clearinghouse is in the middle of every trade —
guarantor.

**Decoding futures tables (WSJ):**
- COPPER-HIGH (CMX) 25,000 lbs; cents per lb
- Columns: Open, High, Low, Settle, Change, Lifetime High, Lifetime Low,
  Open Interest
- Open Interest = outstanding, unliquidated contracts from previous market close

**Contango vs. Backwardation:**
- Contango: futures price higher than spot price (typical — storage costs,
  financing costs factored in). Market is "normal."
- Backwardation: futures price lower than spot price. Usually means tight
  current supply (shortage now, expectation of more supply later).
- In a backwardated market, the "roll yield" is positive — rolling contracts
  forward earns money. In contango, rolling forward costs money.

Rogers on backwardation as signal: When markets shift from contango to
backwardation, it often signals the beginning of a really powerful bull market.

## CHAPTER 5: NOTES FROM THE WILD, WILD EAST (pp. 108–121)

### "Red Capitalism" — China's Rise

China has had fastest growing economy in world since 1980, averaging 9%+/yr.
Already the world's:
- No. 1 consumer of copper and steel
- No. 2 oil consumer (after U.S.)
- No. 1 producer of TVs
- No. 1 in cell phone use, rapidly No. 1 in internet use

Moved from major exporter to major importer of commodities. Imports half
its copper, three-fifths its iron ore, one-third its oil. Draining European
recycling plants of scrap.

### Growing Economy Needs Things

1.3 billion people to feed. Chinese eating more meat than ever (still lower per
capita than other Asian countries, Europe, U.S.). Need more corn feed for
cattle, sheep, chickens. Importing soy from Brazil and Australia. Developing
sweet tooth → sugar imports rising. Major exporter of commodities not long
ago; now massive importer.

### China vs. India

India also growing 8% in recent years (supposedly second-fastest behind China).
But Rogers is skeptical of India vs. China. China's infrastructure and governance
better equipped to sustain growth. South Korea, Japan (few commodities of
their own) competing with China for raw materials.

### The Yuan — Next World Currency?

Rogers believes dollar will continue weakening. China has strongest financial
position of any major country: creditor nation, enormous foreign exchange
reserves. Yuan is "blocked" — not freely tradeable yet — but if/when China
opens capital account, it will be the strongest currency in the world.

Rogers compares: The U.S. was in same position in 1920 as China in 2004.
U.S. dollar replaced British pound as world's reserve currency over next 30-40
years. Rogers owns Swiss francs, Singapore dollars, Norwegian krone as dollar
alternatives.

### Downside Risks

China has had serious setbacks before — 1907 Wall Street collapse parallels. In
1966, Japan's entire financial system collapsed just as it was rising to world's
richest country. Selling then would have been a massive mistake.

Hard landing scenarios: When China prime minister suggested slowing economy
in spring 2004, copper, gold, oil prices all fell. Asian and U.S. stock exchanges
fell. "When China sneezes the rest of the world will be reaching for aspirin."

### Imperial Ambitions?

China making deals in Africa, Latin America, Middle East to secure commodity
resources. Has large military. "I will let the politicians worry about that."

### Upside of the Downside

Any setback will be temporary. Communist Party is a relic already — "To get
rich is glorious." Chinese property owners demonstrating in Tiananmen Square
over getting ripped off by developers. Foreign entertainers perform in China
but "Relevant departments will carry out strict reviews of performance clothing"
— losing battle against Western influence. Best way to play China's rise: buy
commodities during corrections.

## CHAPTER 6: GOODBYE, CHEAP OIL (pp. 122–151)

### 1973 Oil Crisis — The Real Story

October 6, 1973: Egypt and Syria attacked Israel on Yom Kippur. U.S. airlifted
supplies to Israel. October 18: OPEC announced 70% price increase to $5.11/
barrel. Saudi oil minister: "We are masters of our own commodity." He was
wrong — the commodity was the real boss.

Critical insight: The oil embargo had little to do with high prices. In early 1970s,
BEFORE the war, oil supplies were already tighter than they'd been for decades.
Nixon administration put price controls on oil in 1971 → discouraged investment
in production/exploration while encouraging consumption. U.S. oil imports more
than doubled since 1970. By October 1973, it was already a seller's market.

Cease-fire declared within a week of OPEC's move. Embargo relaxed by
December, terminated by March. "OPEC was not about to let politics get in the
way of cashing in on those high prices."

The oil price kept rising for the rest of the decade — AFTER the war, AFTER
the boycott ended.

### Today's Oil Crisis (written 2004-2006)

Price crested $70 for first time ever in 2006; U.S. gasoline topped $3/gallon.
Chevron's Gulf of Mexico discovery in September 2006 — ballyhooed by press.
15 billion barrel estimate = only 6-7 months of world supply. Years away from
production. 

In real terms: old record high ($40/barrel in 1979) would be over $87/barrel in
2006 dollars. Even $70 wasn't expensive in historical adjusted terms.

Fall 2006 oil fundamentals:
- Supply: 85.5 million barrels/day — declining
- Demand: 84.8 million barrels/day — rising
- IEA 2007 forecast: Demand 86.4 mbd, Supply 85.5 mbd — still struggling
- OPEC already pumping at almost full capacity, only 500,000 barrels/day of
  "effective" idle capacity

### Hubbert's Peak

King Hubbert (Shell Oil scientist): 1956 paper predicted U.S. would peak as
oil producer by early 1970s. Everyone thought he was crazy. But by the time
people were mocking "Hubbert's Peak," it was coming true. U.S. oil peaked in
1973. By 1981, pumping almost a third less oil in lower 48 states.

New Hubberts predicting worldwide peak:
- Colin Campbell (retired British geologist): peaked around 2005
- Douglas-Westwood: "world is drawing down its oil reserves faster than ever"
- Ken Deffeyes (Princeton, updating Hubbert's work): not optimistic
- Henry Groppe (Houston, 50+ years in oil business): worldwide peak sometime
  between 2000-2005
- PFC Energy: peak production by 2015
- Matthew Simmons (Houston investment banker, energy adviser to Bush
  administration): Saudi fields have already peaked

Simmons's argument based on reading hundreds of geological reports:
- Saudi's five "elephant" wells (discovered 1940-1965) delivered 90% of all
  Saudi oil 1951-2000. They're peaking.
- Ghawar — largest oil reservoir in world, source of 55-60% of all Saudi oil
  ever produced — is 90% depleted.
- No independent audit of Saudi fields for years. Last field-by-field estimate
  for Ghawar was in 1975.
- "The technical revolution created a monstrous decline rate" — horizontal
  drilling and pressurization techniques drain fields faster.
- "We could be on the verge of seeing a collapse of 30-40% of their production
  in the imminent future."

Saudi reserves oddity: In 1988 Saudi Arabia announced 260 billion barrels.
Hasn't changed since — 260 billion barrels every year for 18 years even as they
produced 69 billion barrels. (In 2005 they said 261 billion.)

Counter-view: USGS and EIA comfortable with OPEC/Saudi numbers. Cambridge
Energy Research's Daniel Yergin: new "digital oil fields" will squeeze billions
more barrels out of currently infeasible deposits. EIA: production won't peak
until 2037-2047. (Yergin also predicted February 2003 that oil prices would
soon decline significantly — just before they doubled in 28 months.)

T. Boone Pickens: "We'll never be flush with oil again. The worldwide decline
will be so steep that whatever we put back will never allow us to catch up."

### Russia

Under Putin, massive investments in pumping; production increased 50% between
1999-2003. Russia claims 6% of world's reserves, pumps 10% of what world
needs. Potential 180 billion barrels if independent audits are right. BP bought
50% stake in Russian oil company. ExxonMobil was licensed to join Yukos in
$12 billion project.

But: Putin threw Yukos CEO in jail for tax fraud. Russian firms "backward in
oil technology." Russian pipelines can handle only half claimed production.
"Hundreds of millions of dollars in profits are ending up in places unknown
to investors" = Swiss bank accounts. Asset stripping. Rogers: "I've been
hearing how much things have improved in the former Soviet Union, but the
only changes I've seen there are for the worse."

### Caspian Sea (Azerbaijan, Kazakhstan)

Azerbaijan at turn of 20th century: half world's oil came from region. 1994: $7.4
billion deal with Western companies. Rogers in Baku 1999: "only capitalism I
saw was 'outlaw capitalism' — stripping all available assets instead of building
infrastructure." Tengiz field (Kazakhstan, 1979) — Russians didn't have the
technology to develop it. Almost 30 years later, pumping less than 300,000
barrels/day. Kashagan: 40 billion barrels claimed, only 6-9 billion recoverable.

"I wouldn't invest a nickel there." Former Soviet Central Asia republics: "a
disaster about to spiral into a catastrophe."

### West Africa (Nigeria, Angola)

Rogers in Nigeria in 2000 during parliamentary elections: witnessed first-hand
at least one distinction "in which Nigeria was already world-class: political
corruption." MPs increased budget by $200 million so each could buy a luxury
car and take a $30,000 furniture allowance. Nigeria has 4 oil refineries, not one
was operating for lack of capital in 2000 — and still not five years later. "I
doubt Nigeria will survive as a nation."

Angola: 30-year civil war. Luanda "a hellhole with streets named after Karl
Marx, Che Guevara, and Fidel Castro." War has ended, things improved, oil
production rising. Rogers bullish on Angola long-term.

### Venezuela

Hugo Chavez elected 1998. Former army officer, fan of Fidel Castro. 2003: fired
18,000 oil company employees who struck, replaced with soldiers. Ex-managers
say 3 million barrels/day capacity is inflated. U.S. imports more oil from
Venezuela than from Saudi Arabia. Chavez diverting oil money to social projects
causing slippage in capacity. "I wouldn't count on Venezuela to keep oil prices
down."

### Iraq

Pipelines sabotaged multiple times in 2006. Years before returning to 2.5 million
barrels/day. OPEC's June 2005 report on production: Iraq literally off the chart,
footnote says "Figures don't include Iraq."

### Canada (Tar Sands)

U.S. imports almost as much oil from Canada as from Saudi Arabia. Alberta
potentially 1.6 trillion barrels — but in Athabasca tar sands, 650 miles from
Arctic Circle. Must literally squeeze oil from stone. Cost: $23 billion and
counting. Shell's plant employs 10,000 at premium wages. Emits 25% more
greenhouse gases than Saudi oil. Produces 740,000 barrels/day total.
"Biggest optimists hope for an extra 1 million barrels/day by 2012 and 2 million
additional by 2018. The world uses 85 million barrels/day."

### Alternatives to Oil

**Solar:** Effective technology around for years. 83% of Israeli households had
solar collectors by 1994. Japan 4.2 million buildings using solar hot-water by
1992. High upfront costs. Solar Two (Barstow, CA): 10 megawatt capacity, $48.5
million to build. Japanese spent 10x as much as Americans to commercialize PV.

**Wind:** Fastest-growing electricity source in world, averaging 25% growth/yr
since 1990. Germany, U.S., Spain, Denmark, India = 84% of world wind energy.
Ford renovating Rouge assembly plant as "factory of the future." U.K. mandated
wind for significant % of energy by 2010. "You might consider windmill stock
instead of oil futures."

**Bioenergy:** Fourth-largest energy resource worldwide after coal, oil, natural
gas. 10 gigawatts of installed capacity in U.S. Biomass includes sugar and corn
for ethanol. Could replace petrochemicals.

**Geothermal:** Geothermal energy potential in top 6 miles of earth's crust =
50,000 times the energy of all known oil and gas resources in world. But only
8,000 megawatts of capacity in use worldwide. Philippines reduced foreign oil
dependence by half using geothermal since 1970s oil crisis.

All alternatives "still many dollars and many years away." Won't make so much
as a dent in oil price for at least a decade.

### Oil Price and the Economy

Bears say: recession will drive oil down. But: in 1970s, economies of U.S.,
Europe went sour — U.K. actually went bankrupt, IMF flew in to rescue British
government. Yet oil prices kept rising because supplies were so low.

The IEA estimated oil industry must invest $69 billion/year in exploration and
production during next decade. But oil producers more eager to acquire other
oil producers than spend money finding more oil. "Meanwhile, existing fields
continue to be depleted."

The best hedge against increased oil prices: buy some oil. "Still a lot cheaper
than a barrel of Perrier or Coca-Cola."

## CHAPTER 7: GOLD—MYSTIQUE VS. FUNDAMENTALS (pp. 153–165)

**Gold's ancient history:** Egyptians mined before 2000 B.C. King Tut buried in
2,448-pound gold coffin. King Croesus of Lydia minted first pure-gold coin in
sixth century B.C. Romans first gold coin 50 B.C. Incas used gold for decor
on virtually everything — each emperor took his gold to his tomb. Spain's
Ferdinand and Isabella sent galleons to find Incan gold → Spain's golden age.
Isaac Newton devoted 20 years after revolutionizing physics to alchemy (1666
onward). Newton later set the mint price for gold in Britain in 1717 — lasted
200 years until World War I.

**Rogers's honest position:** "Gold is not my favorite." Owns some personally.
Baby girl owns commodities including gold. 3% of RICI. "Other commodities
will do much better in this bull market."

**Goldbugs:** "A gloomy bunch" who buy gold as insurance against corporate
stupidity, real-estate crash, stock-market meltdown, dollar collapse. Following
an obsession is not the best investment philosophy. Many things have been "the
ultimate money" — cattle, ivory, sea shells, pretty beads.

When Rogers started on Wall Street in late 1960s: gold at $35/oz. Rose to
historic high of $850 in January 1980. Then for 25 YEARS was in a bear market.
"Gold has been worth a fraction of the price of a Mickey Mantle bubble gum
card and has been nowhere near as stable in price."

**The case against gold:**
- Unlike almost all other commodities, supply has NOT been allowed to dwindle.
  While most mining exploration was on hold, gold mining expanded.
- In 2003: search for gold = 75% of ongoing exploration by world's largest
  mining companies.
- Gold Fields Mineral Services: "aboveground stocks" at end of 2003 = 150,500
  tons. 61% mined since 1950.
- Central banks sitting on 1 billion+ troy ounces (~$600 billion at 2006 prices).
  U.S. holds most: 262 million troy ounces. Germany 112 million. France 97
  million.
- Central banks are selling their gold.
- No independent audit of Fort Knox for several decades.

**Supply/Demand for gold:**
- Supply: mined on every continent except Antarctica (international moratorium).
  Production fell 0.5% in 2004. South Africa 16%, U.S. 12%, Australia 11%,
  China 7.5%, Russia 6.2%, Canada 5.8%.
- Demand: jewelry down (especially in U.S.). Industry and dentistry about 11%
  of demand (~400 tons/year). Gold in computers (hair-thin wires connecting
  circuits to semiconductors), TVs, VCRs, phones (gold coating on contacts),
  dental work, medicine (treating rheumatoid arthritis since 1920s, cancer
  treatment via gold vapor lasers, surgical instruments). Worldwide average
  demand DOWN for 1999-2003 period across the board.

**U.S. Gold History:**
- 1900: U.S. backed currency with gold at $20.67/oz.
- FDR 1933: banned gold export, banned convertibility, made it illegal for
  Americans to own gold. Price moved to $35 — FDR just picked a round number.
- Gold stayed at $35 from 1935 to 1970. Undervalued → no incentive to explore.
  Gold production kept decreasing. Meanwhile more gold used in dentistry and
  electronics → demand rising → price rising.
- U.S. had trade deficits since 1950s, printing money. Run on Fort Knox. French
  turned up waving dollar reserves demanding gold.
- Nixon 1971: U.S. would no longer convert dollars to gold — defaulting on
  obligation. Demand for gold increased.
- 1974: Congress decided Americans were grown-up enough to own gold. Price
  shot up to $200 — then fell back toward $100 as foreigners dumped gold onto
  Americans. "The foreigners made a killing at our expense."
- After 1976: gold through the roof. Iranian Revolution 1979, hostage crisis,
  dollar doubts. January 1980: $850. Then Reagan, "morning in America,"
  inflation tamed, dollar survived.
- From 1980 onward: worldwide gold production expanded for 20 years straight.
  With record high prices everyone looking for gold. Over same period, gold lost
  70% of its value. Too much supply.
- Post-dot-com crash, post-9/11: Goldbugs multiply. Chinese allowed to own
  gold after decades-long ban. Japanese bank-account guarantees modified. Price
  rallied to $430 (16-year high), then to over $700 in 2006.

**Rogers's call:** Dollar likely to continue declining long-term. Can see gold
climbing. Own some as insurance. But too many other more attractive commodity
opportunities. "The person who figures out how to turn gold into lead is the one
who's going to get richer than Croesus — at least until the Apocalypse arrives."

## CHAPTER 8: A HEAVY METAL WITH THE POTENTIAL TO BE A HIGH FLIER (pp. 166–174)

**Lead's toxic reputation:**
Lead (plumbum in Latin). Roman water system pipes made of lead. "Plumbarii"
= Roman plumbers. Vitruvius (90-20 B.C.): "Water from clay pipes is much
more wholesome … because lead is found to be … hurtful to the human system."
Historical research shows water moved too quickly through aqueducts to be
contaminated; terra-cotta cisterns gave sweeter taste. Romans did get ill from
lead occasionally (boiling wine in lead cauldrons) but didn't die en masse.

Roman Empire fell more likely from "occupying too much territory too far away
with too many troops" — "a history lesson that the U.S., with troops now in
more than 100 countries, might contemplate."

**Lead in America:**
Used in ceramic glazes, pewter, brass, leaded glass, paints, pipes, bullets. After
WWI: electrical cable covers, solders, SLI (starting-lighting-ignition) batteries,
leaded gasoline. By 1980s: government agencies banned lead in paint, solders,
gasoline. Lawsuits followed. Lead Industries Association, Inc. filed for
bankruptcy in 2002 after 84 years due to "lack of insurance to cover litigation."

2004: U.S. Consumer Product Safety Commission recalled 150 million toy
bracelets, rings, necklaces from vending machines — contained dangerous
amounts of lead.

**Why Rogers is bullish on lead:**
- Classic supply-demand imbalance
- Mine and metal production going down every year worldwide
- Demand increasing, mainly in Asia and China (motor scooters and cars = more
  lead-acid batteries)
- No one wants to be in the lead business → underinvestment → coming shortage

**Supply:**
U.S. mine production peaked in 1999. 2002: fell to six-year low of 440,000
metric tons. 2003: 450,000 metric tons. Seven lead mines in Missouri = 96% of
U.S. production. Only three smelters processing primary lead in U.S.:
Herculaneum, Missouri (112 years old); Buick Mine and Mill, Missouri; one in
Montana. Buick Mine and Mill is most recent smelter built in U.S. — in 1969.

In 2002, state study found one-quarter of children living near Herculaneum
smelter had high blood lead levels. EPA relocated ~100 families. 2004: Doe Run
closed its Viburnum No. 28 lead mine after 41 years.

Worldwide lead production from mines down 4% in 2001, another 1.2% in 2002.
Australia largest producer (700,000 metric tons). China second (600,000 tons).
U.S. third (450,000 tons, declining steadily since 1999).

Largest lead (and silver) mine in world: Australia's Cannington mine (BHP
Billiton), opened 1997 — only major new lead mine in decades.

In summary: "Lead supplies are down worldwide."

**Demand:**
Lead now used in: vehicles' SLI batteries (70%+ of world's lead); non-SLI
batteries (forklifts, airport ground equipment, mining equipment, utility
load-leveling). Computer/telecom networks, hospital power systems. TV and
computer monitors (lead blocks radiation). Ammunition (3%), glass and ceramics,
casting metals, sheet lead for roofing. By 2000: 88% of apparent U.S. lead
consumption = lead-acid storage batteries.

China car story: In 2004, only 4% of China's 1.3 billion people had cars. 4
million cars made in China in 2003, up 750,000 from previous year. Mercedes,
BMW, Cadillac all in China. 4% ownership = enormous upside for batteries.
China is also No. 1 producer of TVs in world, sales rising 20% — all TVs use
lead in cathode-ray tubes (and when digital drives out CRTs, Chinese will watch
on computer monitors — which also use lead to block radiation).

"Worldwide demand for lead is up and increasing."

**Prices:**
After zigzagging 2000-2003, lead headed up from ~$500/metric ton to ~$1500
by 2005 — triple and way above previous all-time high of $668 in 1979.

Historical note: Congress passed Lead and Zinc Mining Stabilization Program
in 1961 paying miners when price dropped below 14.5 cents/lb (lasted until
1969). Government price controls in early 1970s kept price at ~14 cents/lb. When
controls lifted December 1973, U.S. price headed almost straight up for next
6 years to monthly high in December 1979 of 57-59 cents/lb.

**CODA — The Iron Lesson:**
Lead has lost two of its largest markets in past 20 years (paint and gasoline).
Yet prices are making all-time highs. "A huge bull market even when demand
declines. The lesson is clear: Decreased demand or even a slowdown of economic
growth does not necessarily mean a bear market — not if supplies are decreasing
faster."

## CHAPTER 9: SEARCHING FOR THE NEXT SUGAR HIGH (pp. 175–189)

**Rogers's sugar pitch to French journalist:**
In Paris hotel, plucked wrapped sugar cube from bowl. Handed to French
business writer: "Put this in your pocket and take it home, because the price of
sugar is going to go up five times in the next decade." Sugar was 5.5 cents/lb
that day. Previous record: from 1.4 cents in 1966 to 66.5 cents in 1974 — 45x
rise. Famous French film "Le Sucre" (starring Gérard Depardieu) about that
sugar boom. Writer pocketed the cube but likely didn't invest.

**Why sugar was cheap:**
By 1985 sugar had fallen to 2.5 cents. Exchange seats being given away at New
York Board of Trade. Rogers bought a cheap seat, leased it out, eventually sold
when prices rose — "another way of benefiting from the rise of commodity
prices." Sugar stayed in bear market for next 19 years.

**The supply story:**
In Brazil (world's largest producer and exporter), ambitious farmers would
rather grow soybeans at $6/bushel than sugar at 13.8 cents/lb. One-third of all
American sugar-beet processing mills shut down since 1996. Only most efficient
producers surviving; government subsidies often the only incentive keeping
American and European producers in the game.

International pressure building on U.S. and EU to end subsidies. U.S. pays ~$5
billion in sugar subsidies; EU pays ~$2 billion. Forces consumers to pay 2-3x
world price. 2004: WTO ruled EU subsidies encouraged overproduction and
artificially depressed international prices. Ruling appealed.

"The Rogers Plan" for U.S. sugar subsidies: offer 5,000 American sugar
producers $100,000/yr for life, condo at the beach, and a Porsche — on condition
they never plant sugar again. Would cost less than $5 billion/yr and everyone
would come out ahead.

**Brazil's transformation:**
In 1992: Brazil had 8.4% share of world sugar market. By 2004: 31%. Between
1984-2003: Brazilian production increased 144% (all after 1993-94). In 2002-03:
Brazil accounted for 43% of world raw-sugar exports (more than double 18 years
earlier). After devaluing currency in 1999, sugar more competitive as far away
as Asia.

**The Brazil-Sugar-Ethanol Triangle:**
Brazil uses half its sugar crop for ethanol. 2005: 60% of Brazil's new cars from
VW, Fiat, GM were "flex-fuel" running on ethanol. Government increased
ethanol percentage added to gasoline. With oil at record highs, gasohol 40%
cheaper than gasoline → Brazilian drivers shifted to fuel-efficient options.

UNICA (Sao Paulo Sugarcane Agro-Industry Union): predicted 50% of 2004-05
crop would end up in gas tanks. More sugar in gas tank = less for export.
Brazil exporting ethanol to Caribbean, which redistills and ships to U.S. at low
duty. Sweden is a customer. Japan (Kyoto signatory) likely to become bigger
ethanol customer.

**The China effect on sugar:**
China importing 672,000 tons in 2003, announced importing 1.2 million+ tons
in 2004. Sugar-beet crop down 50%, sugarcane down due to drought. China per
capita sugar consumption: 7 kg/yr vs. "globally accepted" 25-30 kg/yr (U.S.:
45.3 kg = 100 lbs/yr). China already consuming 11 million tons/year (U.S.:
slightly more than 9 million tons). Direct correlation between increasing affluence
and sugar consumption regardless of culture.

**Historical sugar price movements:**
- 1966-1974: 1.4 cents → 66.5 cents (45x rise)
  Cause: 4 straight record crops, then demand outpaced supply 1972. Industry
  users stockpiling. People grabbing sugar off restaurant tables for home use.
  Dinner guests bringing 5-lb bags of sugar instead of wine. Cyclamate pulled
  from market 1969 as potential carcinogen → consumers returned to real sugar.
- 1974-1976: prices crashed as everyone planted more sugar (record 1975-76 crop)
- 1979-1981: Australia reduced production 10%, Philippines typhoon, Brazil
  diverted sugar to ethanol. Soviet crop failed. Prices back to 40 cents in 1981
  (400% from 8 cents four years earlier).
- 1981-2003: bear market, high-fructose corn syrup replaced sugar in soft drinks
  (1983), aspartame approved 1981 → replaced sugar in diet products. By 1985:
  back to 2.5 cents.

**Forecast:**
Sugar was 85% below its all-time high; oil was at all-time high. The Brazil-
China-oil trifecta points to higher prices. WTO subsidies ruling (if upheld)
would lift global sugar prices 20%; removing ALL trade distortions could lift
prices 47% over 10 years. "In a bull market, nearly everything reaches its
all-time high."

## CHAPTER 10: CAN COFFEE PERK UP? (pp. 191–201)

Rogers puzzled by Starbucks customers sipping $3 lattes while complaining
about "high price of coffee." In early 2000s, coffee prices were at their lowest
in decades.

Coffee has been second most important cash commodity in the world (only
after crude oil) at certain times. But business "awful for coffee producers for
years." All-time contract low: 41.50 cents in December 2001. Average retail
can price at grocery store was DOWN 65 cents over 10-year period 1994-2004.

### Brief History

Ethiopian origins (archaeological evidence pre-A.D. 1000). From Ethiopia to
Arab world → first cultivated there. Christian monks dried berries for wine.
Arabs drinking roasted coffee beverage by 15th century. Venetian traders
brought to Europe ~1600. Pope declared it "a good Christian beverage." First
London coffeehouse 1652. French followed 1672.

Dutch traders smuggled coffee plant from Arab port of Mocha to Java colony
in 1690. French naval officer stole seedling → Martinique → spread throughout
tropical Americas. Continental Congress declared coffee national drink to protest
British tea tax.

**Coffee production basics:**
Two species: robusta and arabica (milder, preferred by Americans, half the
caffeine). Takes 3-5 years for plant to start yielding. Trees replaced after 15
years when production peaks. It takes 2,000 cherries (4,000 beans) to make
one pound of coffee. Average tree produces 1-2 lbs of roasted coffee per year.
Coffee transported in 60-kg bags.

World production: ~106.6 million bags/year average over past decade.

**Two harvesting methods:**
- Dry: cherries dried in sun, fruit and bean separated by mechanical huskers.
- Wet (better flavor, higher price): soaking cherries to remove skins.

**Weather vulnerability:**
Brazil had 17 "major freezes" in 20th century, one about every 6 years. Massive
price spikes follow frost news. 1994 "double frost" → coffee from ~$1/lb to
nearly $3. Followed by 40% production decline. 1975 devastating frost → harvest
estimated at 28-30 million bags produced only 9.5 million → prices hit all-time
high of $3.25/lb in 1977.

### Supply Crisis

Between 1995-96 and 2002-03: world crops grew significantly every year except
one, averaging 106.6 million bags/year. 2002-03: record 120 million bags.
September 2001: world inventories up 15% from previous year — almost 5-month
supply. All-time low December 2001: 41.50 cents.

Brazilian coffee farmers leaving business → growing sugarcane for gasohol or
soybeans for China. Those remaining couldn't afford to maintain trees or hire
enough labor. Colombia farmers tearing out coffee trees to replace with coca
plants.

2003: USDA announced stocks would be down 24% from previous year-ending
stocks of 28.4 million bags. 2003-04 harvest: ~15% less than previous year's
120 million bags — "in part caused by the neglect of groves during the long
period of weak prices." Brazilian arabicas down as much as 40%.

Brazil = biggest producer and exporter. Vietnam now No. 2 (surpassed Colombia
and Indonesia). Vietnam's production increased in 1999-2000 by more than half
(11 million bags = 10%+ of world total).

### Demand

U.S.: largest consumer, importing 21 million bags, consuming 27% of world
exports. Germany 17%, Italy/France/Japan 9% each. Only 20% of world
population drinks coffee — those in U.S., Europe, Japan.

World coffee demand increasing at 1.3%/year for past decade. But supplies
during same period increased 3.6%/year — hence the bear market.

2003-04 production fell to 108 million bags — threatening to drop below
consumption level.

### China and Coffee

China = inveterate tea drinkers. Annual per capita consumption: 0.20 kg
(less than half a pound). Swiss consume 50x as much (10.1 kg). Americans
drink 20 gallons/year.

Starbucks in China since 1999, now over 184 stores (51 in Beijing, 72 in
Shanghai). Cheapest coffee drinks at $1.50 still too expensive for most urban
workers. Japan = model case: traditional tea-drinker → now world's third-largest
coffee consumer. But that transformation took decades.

### Outlook

"All the classic elements are lining up for a bull market in coffee: low prices,
fewer growers, fewer trees, less coffee — and continued demand." Coffee trees
take 3-5 years to come to fruition — like a metal, not a typical crop. When
next bull move happens, investors will dream about China and India demand,
"why this move in coffee is different." That will be classic bull-market hysteria.
"Recognize it for what it is, take your profits, and look for value elsewhere."

## CONCLUSION (pp. 202–207)

Rogers wanted to write this book to "open the minds of investors to commodities."
He couldn't prove academically that commodities deserved a place in portfolios —
until the Yale study.

**Yale Study Summary ("Facts and Fantasies About Commodity Futures," Gorton
& Rouwenhorst, 2004):**
- Since 1959, commodity futures produced better annual returns than stocks,
  outperformed bonds even more, with LESS risk than stocks and bonds
- 1970s: commodities outperformed stocks; 1980s: exact opposite — confirming
  negative correlation
- Commodity returns positively correlated with inflation
- Volatility of commodity futures slightly BELOW volatility of S&P 500
- Cumulative performance of futures was TRIPLE that of commodity-producing
  companies' stocks
- Therefore commodities are not just good diversification — they offer better
  returns, with less risk

"Frankenstein is dead." Dismissing commodities will now be "liable to criticism
and reproach — backed up by a reputable academic study." Analogous to junk
bond study in late 1970s that gave credibility to high-yield bonds, or 1960s
study that helped reinvigorate the stock market.

**Warning about bull markets:**
- Not all commodities go up at same time
- In last long-term bull (began 1968): sugar peaked in 1974, commodity bull
  continued for rest of decade
- Every commodity guided by its own supply-demand dynamic
- Bull markets end in hysteria

"You will know the end of the bull market when you see it." Signs: increases
in production, decreases in demand. Remember: oil production exceeded demand
in 1978 but price skyrocketed for 2 more years. Academics were predicting $100
oil as late as 1980.

**When to get out:**
"Rich, smiling farmers and oil rigs on the covers of Fortune and Business Week.
CNBC's 'money honeys' broadcasting from the pork-belly pits in Chicago. The
ladies down at the supermarket talking about how they just made a killing in
soybeans. Small cars will be the norm, homes will be heated five degrees below
today's preferred room temperature, and there might be a wind farm on the
outside of town as far as the eye can see." That's when to exit.

"Those days, in my opinion, are a decade away, at least."
