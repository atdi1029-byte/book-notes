Preface

The book opens in the heat of the Arab Spring. On December 17, 2010, Mohamed Bouazizi, a street vendor in Tunisia, set himself on fire in protest against corruption and police harassment. Within weeks the revolution spread. President Ben Ali, who had ruled Tunisia since 1987, fled on January 14, 2011. A month later, on February 11, Hosni Mubarak was ousted in Egypt after nearly thirty years of iron-fisted rule.

The protestors of Tahrir Square were not demanding better economic advice. They were demanding political change. Noha Hamed, a twenty-four-year-old advertising worker, said it plainly: “We are suffering from corruption, oppression and bad education. We are living amid a corrupt system which has to change.” Mohamed ElBaradei, the former director of the International Atomic Energy Agency, compressed the formula into a tweet: “Tunisia: repression + absence of social justice + denial of channels for peaceful change = a ticking bomb.”

“They get it wrong not by mistake or ignorance but on purpose.” — Acemoglu & Robinson

Most academics and policy pundits diagnose poverty through three lenses: geography, culture, or ignorance. Egypt is poor because it is a desert, or because its people lack the right cultural values, or because its rulers simply do not know which policies to adopt. Acemoglu and Robinson reject all three. Egypt is poor, they argue, because it has been ruled by a narrow elite that organized society for its own benefit — a pattern visible in Mubarak’s estimated $70 billion personal fortune. The rich countries of the world became rich because their citizens overthrew the elites who controlled power and built societies where political rights and economic opportunity were broadly distributed.

1. So Close and Yet So Different

The city of Nogales is cut in half by a fence. To the north lies Nogales, Arizona, in Santa Cruz County: average household income around $30,000, most teenagers in school, most adults with high school diplomas, relatively healthy, with access to Medicare and a road network linking them to the rest of the United States. To the south lies Nogales, Sonora: household income roughly one-third the Arizona figure, high infant mortality, poor public health, roads in disrepair, rampant crime, and a daily reality of corruption and ineptitude.

The two sides share the same geography, climate, disease environment, and — crucially — the same ethnic background, food, and culture. The area was unified until the Gadsden Purchase of 1853, when Lieutenant N. Michler, surveying the new border, noted the “pretty little valley of Los Nogales.” What divides the two Nogaleses is not geography or culture but the international border itself, and the different institutions on each side.

The institutional divergence traces to the early colonial period. In 1516, Juan Díaz de Solís sailed into the Río de la Plata and was promptly killed by the Charrúas. Pedro de Mendoza founded Buenos Aires in 1534, but the local hunter-gatherers refused to provide food or labor, and within four years every Spaniard had abandoned the settlement for Asunción, where the sedentary Guaraní could be coerced. Buenos Aires was not resettled until 1580. The lesson was clear: colonists wanted others to do the work.

Hernán Cortés perfected the extraction playbook. Arriving at Tenochtitlan on November 8, 1519, he seized Emperor Moctezuma, looted the treasury, and melted the gold into bars. As Bernardino de Sahagún’s Florentine Codices recorded: “They took it all.” The encomienda system followed, granting indigenous populations to Spanish lords for tribute and forced labor. Bartolomé de las Casas, the Dominican priest who documented the horrors, described settlers in Nicaragua forcing labor “night and day without any rest whatsoever.”

Francisco Pizarro replicated the model in Peru. At Cajamarca on November 15, 1532, he captured the Inca emperor Atahualpa, extracted a ransom sufficient to fill one room with gold and two with silver, and then strangled him in July 1533. The mita system, revived by Viceroy Francisco de Toledo, forced one-seventh of all adult males within a 200,000-square-mile catchment area to work the silver mines of Potosí, whose population reached 160,000 by 1650 — larger than Lisbon or Venice. The mita was not abolished until 1825, and its legacy is visible today: the province of Acomayo, inside the old mita zone, is dramatically poorer than neighboring Calca, which lay outside it.

English colonization followed a different path because it had to. When the Virginia Company founded Jamestown in 1607, there was no gold, no dense settled population to coerce, and no centralized empire to hijack. Captain John Smith quickly realized that the Cortés model was impossible: “Victuals you must know is all their wealth.” Chief Wahunsunacock refused subordination: “If your King have sent me presents, I also am a King.” The Virginia Company tried barracks, work gangs, and executions, but colonists could simply run away into a continent with a population density of three-quarters of a person per square mile, compared to 400 in central Mexico.

Forced to innovate, the company introduced the headright system in 1618 — fifty acres per settler — and in 1619 established the General Assembly, which Acemoglu and Robinson call “the start of democracy in the United States.” Repeated elite attempts to impose extraction failed: Lord Baltimore’s manorial Maryland, John Locke’s Fundamental Constitutions of Carolina with hereditary “leet-men” — all collapsed because settlers had frontier alternatives. By the 1720s every colony had a governor-and-assembly structure.

“If your King have sent me presents, I also am a King, and this is my land.” — Wahunsunacock to John Smith

Mexico’s independence was the mirror image of America’s. The U.S. Constitution of 1787 grew from a century and a half of assembly government. Mexican elites, by contrast, declared independence in 1821 specifically to protect their colonial extractive institutions from Spain’s Cádiz Constitution of 1812. Iturbide crowned himself emperor. Between 1824 and 1867, Mexico had fifty-two presidents. Santa Ana alone served eleven times. By 1910, the United States had 27,864 banks with $27.3 billion in assets; Mexico had 42 banks, two of which controlled 60 percent of total banking. The contrast between Bill Gates, who built Microsoft through innovation and faced antitrust prosecution, and Carlos Slim, who captured the Telmex monopoly through political connections and delayed payment using the company’s own dividends, captures the institutional difference perfectly.

2. Theories That Don’t Work

Three grand theories have dominated thinking about why some nations are poor. The geography hypothesis, stretching from Montesquieu to Jeffrey Sachs, blames tropical climates, poor soils, and disease. But Nogales, North and South Korea, and East and West Germany all share geography yet diverge dramatically. The historical record delivers an even sharper rebuke: at the time of European contact, the Aztec and Inca civilizations in the tropics were far richer than the hunter-gatherers of temperate North America. Great tropical civilizations — Angkor, Vijayanagara, Aksum, the Indus Valley — flourished for centuries. Tropical diseases, Acemoglu and Robinson argue, are a consequence of poverty, not its cause. England was deeply unhealthy in the nineteenth century but invested in clean water, sewage systems, and public health services. Poor countries today could do the same — if their institutions permitted it.

Jared Diamond’s thesis in Guns, Germs, and Steel is more sophisticated but still insufficient. Diamond convincingly explains why the Spanish could conquer the Incas, but he cannot explain why the gap between Spain and Peru widened after both had access to the same technologies. A Spaniard today is roughly six times richer than a Peruvian. Diamond’s framework also cannot account for enormous variation within continents: Botswana and the Democratic Republic of Congo share a landmass but inhabit different economic universes. Maps of wild pig and aurochs ancestors show them distributed widely across Eurasia, undermining the claim that species availability explains institutional divergence. The Middle East led the Neolithic Revolution but was impoverished by Ottoman expansion — not by its geography.

The culture hypothesis, from Max Weber’s Protestant ethic onward, fares no better. Catholic France quickly matched Protestant England in industrial development. East Asian success stories have nothing to do with Christianity. The Kingdom of Kongo provides a devastating test case: when Portuguese missionaries brought the wheel and the plow, the Kongolese rejected both — but they eagerly adopted guns, which were useful for the slave trade. The problem was incentives, not culture.

The culture argument crumbles further under close examination. Nogales has the same culture on both sides of the fence. North and South Koreans shared an unprecedented cultural homogeneity before division at the 38th parallel — different “cultures” today are a consequence of institutional divergence, not its cause. The “English culture” argument is equally hollow: the United States, Canada, and Australia were English colonies, but so were Sierra Leone and Nigeria. The variation within former English colonies is as great as the variation across the entire world. Argentina and Uruguay have more European descent than the United States or Canada, yet their economic performance “leaves much to be desired.”

China’s trajectory demolishes the cultural explanation from yet another angle. Chinese poverty under Mao resulted from disastrous policies — the Great Leap Forward, the Cultural Revolution — not from Chinese culture. The moment Deng Xiaoping changed the institutional framework, growth exploded. If culture determined economic outcomes, the same Chinese people could not have produced both Mao’s catastrophe and Deng’s miracle within a single generation.

“Economics has gained the title Queen of the Social Sciences by choosing solved political problems as its domain.” — Abba Lerner

The ignorance hypothesis — that poor countries are poor because their leaders do not know the right policies — is the most dangerous of the three. Ghana’s Kwame Nkrumah was advised by Nobel laureate Arthur Lewis himself, yet he built a footwear factory more than 500 miles from the nearest tannery and a mango canning plant where mangoes do not grow. British economist Tony Killick documented project after project “whose viability was undermined by poor siting.” These were not mistakes of ignorance but calculated political decisions — projects located in the districts of political allies.

When Prime Minister Kofi Busia finally agreed to the economically correct policy of devaluation on December 27, 1971, the result was rioting in Accra and a military coup by Lieutenant Colonel Acheampong, who immediately reversed the devaluation. Busia understood the economics perfectly — he also understood the political gamble and lost. Deng Xiaoping’s reforms in China succeeded not because he received better economic advice but because he won a political struggle against the Gang of Four. The problem in poor countries is not that rulers lack knowledge — it is that they deliberately choose policies that benefit themselves at the expense of their citizens.

The chapter’s conclusion is devastating for the economics profession. If poverty is a political problem, then technical economic advice — however brilliant — will not solve it. As Abba Lerner observed, economics has prospered by choosing political problems that have already been solved. The truly hard problems — why Egypt is poor, why Sierra Leone has collapsed — require political transformation, not better policy papers.

3. The Making of Prosperity and Poverty

The Hwang Pyŏng-Wŏn brothers were separated during the Korean War in 1950 and reunited fifty years later. The brother who went south became a pharmacist, owned a car and a telephone. The brother who stayed north was a doctor but had no car, no phone, and arrived at the reunion in a threadbare borrowed coat, perpetually nervous. South Korea enjoys living standards comparable to Portugal or Spain. North Korea’s income is roughly one-tenth that level, akin to sub-Saharan Africa, and life expectancy is ten years shorter. The famous satellite photograph tells the story: the South blazes with light while the North is almost completely dark.

From this contrast, the authors build their central framework. Inclusive economic institutions secure private property for the majority of people, provide an unbiased legal system, offer public services that create a level playing field, and permit the entry of new businesses. Extractive economic institutions are designed to extract income and wealth from one subset of society to benefit another. Barbados in 1680 illustrates the distinction: its 175 largest sugar planters enjoyed strong property rights, but 39,000 of the island’s 60,000 inhabitants were slaves. Property rights for a few are not inclusive institutions.

Political institutions determine economic ones. Absolutist political institutions concentrate power narrowly and without constraints, producing extractive economics. Pluralistic institutions distribute power broadly and constrain its exercise. Two conditions are necessary for inclusive political institutions: pluralism and a sufficiently centralized state. Somalia has widely distributed political power but no centralization — the result is chaos, not inclusion. Max Weber defined the state as the entity with a “monopoly of legitimate violence,” and without that monopoly, property rights cannot be enforced.

The feedback loops between political and economic institutions create self-reinforcing cycles. Extractive political institutions generate extractive economics, which enrich the elite, who use that wealth to reinforce their political dominance. Inclusive political institutions produce inclusive economics, which distribute income more equitably, sustaining broad-based political participation. Combinations of extractive and inclusive tend to be unstable because one set of institutions pushes toward the other.

“Inclusive institutions redistribute not just income but power — a dictator may become worse off.” — Acemoglu & Robinson

Joseph Schumpeter’s concept of creative destruction sits at the heart of the argument. Sustained economic growth requires technological change, which inevitably replaces old industries, firms, and skills with new ones. The fear of creative destruction is often at the root of elite opposition to inclusive institutions — on the eve of the Industrial Revolution, European aristocracies whose income derived from land and monopolies stood to lose everything as factories drew labor and resources from the countryside. Mobutu Sese Seko’s Congo illustrates why elites rarely choose prosperity: his inner circle, the “Les Grosses Legumes,” grew fabulously rich while the country collapsed around them. Mobutu built a Concorde-capable airport at his jungle palace in Gbadolite and bought castles across Europe. Inclusive institutions would have redistributed not just his income but his power.

4. Small Differences and Critical Junctures

In 1346, the Black Death arrived in Europe from China via the Silk Road and wiped out roughly half the population. Boccaccio in Florence recorded that “death occurred within three days.” This was a critical juncture — a major event that disrupted the existing balance of power — and it produced diametrically opposite outcomes in Western and Eastern Europe.

In England, the sudden scarcity of labor gave peasants unprecedented bargaining power. Eynsham Abbey found that “scarcely two tenants remained.” The crown’s Statute of Laborers of 1351, which tried to freeze wages at pre-plague levels, simply did not work. In 1381, the Peasants’ Revolt led by Wat Tyler captured most of London. Feudal labor obligations dwindled, and an inclusive labor market began to emerge.

In Eastern Europe, the same plague and the same labor scarcity produced the opposite result. Lords in Poland, Prussia, and Hungary prevailed over weaker, less organized peasants and imposed a Second Serfdom far more intense than what had come before. In Korczyn, Poland, all peasant labor was paid in 1533; by 1600, nearly half was unpaid. In Mecklenberg, labor obligations rose from a few days per year in 1500 to three days per week by 1600. By 1600, Western Europe had a market economy; Eastern Europe had coerced serfs.

The explanation lies in small institutional differences that existed before the plague. Western European towns were stronger, peasants better organized, and commercial networks denser. These differences were modest — but the critical juncture of the plague amplified them into permanently divergent trajectories. The same mechanism operated in 1588, when England, France, and Spain were all absolutist monarchies. The key difference was that the English Parliament controlled taxation while the French Estates-General did not. Spain’s American gold funded the crown directly, allowing it to bypass representative institutions. In England, Elizabeth I could not monopolize Atlantic trade, so wealthy merchants with few ties to the crown emerged — and they demanded institutional change.

“Without this unlikely victory, the Glorious Revolution would never have got moving.” — Acemoglu & Robinson, on the 1588 Armada

History is deeply contingent. The defeat of the Spanish Armada in 1588 — attributable largely to bad weather and the Duke of Sidonia’s mistakes — opened the Atlantic to English merchants and set in motion the chain of events that led to the Glorious Revolution. Had the Armada succeeded, the entire trajectory of English and world history would have been fundamentally different. The authors also introduce Robert Michels’s “iron law of oligarchy” — the observation that revolutions often simply replace one set of extractors with another, as happened repeatedly in Egypt, Ethiopia, and most post-colonial states.

5. “I’ve Seen the Future, and It Works”

In 1921, the American journalist Lincoln Steffens returned from Moscow and declared: “I’ve seen the future, and it works.” For a time, he appeared to be right. Stalin’s forced collectivization of agriculture abolished private property and herded millions into collective farms. Probably six million people died of famine; hundreds of thousands more were murdered or banished. But between 1928 and 1960, Soviet national income grew at roughly six percent per year — arguably the fastest sustained spurt in history up to that point.

The growth was real but fundamentally different from growth under inclusive institutions. It was not driven by technological change but by the brute-force reallocation of labor from agriculture — where it was enormously unproductive — into industry, combined with massive capital accumulation directed by the state. Paul Samuelson’s economics textbook solemnly predicted that the Soviet Union would overtake the United States by 1984 (or at the latest 1997). By the 1970s, growth had essentially stopped.

The central planning system, Gosplan, generated perverse incentives that are blackly comic in retrospect. When targets were set in tons, factories produced steel sheets too heavy to use. When targets were set in area, sheets came out too thin. Chandeliers planned by weight were too heavy to hang from ceilings. Bonuses for exceeding targets created powerful disincentives to innovate, because innovation risked missing current targets, and any success would simply cause next year’s targets to be ratcheted up.

“You can move someone to a factory, but you cannot force people to think and have good ideas by threatening to shoot them.” — Acemoglu & Robinson

The regime dealt with inconvenient truths through violence. The 1937 census revealed a population of 162 million — far below Stalin’s publicly announced figure of 180 million, the gap reflecting the scale of famine and purges. Stalin had the census organizers arrested and shot. The next census, in 1939, dutifully “found” 171 million. Between 1940 and 1955, thirty-six million people were convicted of labor offenses — roughly one-third of the adult population. Fifteen million went to prison. A quarter of a million were shot. Another two and a half million languished in gulags.

The fundamental problem was that moving toward inclusive economic institutions would have jeopardized the Communist Party’s political monopoly. When Gorbachev finally began opening the system after 1987, the party crumbled. The Soviet experience demonstrates the central dilemma of extractive growth: it can deliver impressive results in the short run but cannot generate the sustained technological innovation that requires creative destruction.

The pattern recurs across history. Around 1620, King Shyaam forged the Kuba Kingdom in Central Africa, building a state, bureaucracy, legal system, and even trial by jury. Food production per capita doubled. But growth was limited: without creative destruction, the situation was essentially unchanged when the Belgians arrived in the late nineteenth century. The Maya city-states illustrate the darker end of extractive growth — Copán’s population peaked at 28,000 around AD 750, larger than contemporary London or Paris, then collapsed to 600 by AD 1200 as endless warfare between competing extractive elites destroyed the economic base.

The authors draw an explicit parallel to modern China, arguing that growth under the Chinese Communist Party resembles Soviet growth in the 1950s and 1960s — impressive catch-up driven by reallocation and capital accumulation, but unlikely to be sustained without the political transformation that would permit genuine creative destruction.

6. Drifting Apart

Medieval Venice was possibly the richest place in the world. Its population grew from 45,000 in 1050 to 110,000 by 1330 — as large as Paris and three times the size of London. The key innovation was the commenda, a rudimentary joint-stock company that allowed young men without capital to trade using the investments of wealthy partners. Government records reveal extraordinary social mobility: between 960 and 982, sixty-five to eighty-one percent of the names appearing in official documents were new. Venice was a place where talent could rise.

Political institutions kept pace: a Ducal Council after 1032, a Great Council after 1171, independent magistrates, courts, bankruptcy laws, and the beginnings of modern banking. But in 1297, the ruling families enacted La Serrata — “The Closure” — sealing the Great Council to outsiders and making incumbency hereditary. By 1315, the Libro d’Oro (Gold Book) had registered the permanent aristocracy. The economic serrata followed: the commenda was banned, trade was nationalized in 1314, and heavy taxes were levied on any remaining private commerce. Long-distance trade became the exclusive preserve of the nobility.

“Instead of pioneering trade routes, Venetians make pizza and blow colored glass for foreigners.” — Acemoglu & Robinson

Venice’s population shrank to 100,000 by 1500 and contracted further between 1650 and 1800 while the rest of Europe expanded. The story demonstrates a crucial insight: progress toward inclusive institutions can be reversed.

The Roman Republic tells a similar tale on a grander scale. Founded around 510 BC with cleverly designed inclusive institutions — elected magistrates, checks and balances, a separate Plebeian Assembly with veto power — Rome generated genuine economic growth. Archaeological evidence is abundant: Mediterranean shipwrecks peaked at around 180 near the time of Christ, Monte Testaccio is composed of roughly fifty-three million discarded amphorae, and Greenland ice cores show atmospheric lead peaking in the first century AD and not returning to that level until the thirteenth century.

But the republic collapsed under the weight of inequality and elite power struggles. Tiberius Gracchus proposed land reform in 133 BC and was clubbed to death by senators. His brother Gaius met the same fate. Julius Caesar crossed the Rubicon in 49 BC, and Augustus created the Empire. Under the emperors, institutions became increasingly extractive: Tiberius abolished the Plebeian Assembly, citizens received free wheat and circuses instead of political voice, and Constantine allowed landlords to chain their semi-serf coloni to the land. Shipwrecks fell from 180 to 20 by AD 500. Mediterranean trade may not have recovered to Roman levels until the nineteenth century.

Roman emperors actively blocked innovation. Tiberius reportedly killed an inventor of unbreakable glass “lest gold be reduced to the value of mud.” Vespasian refused a labor-saving device for transporting columns: “How will I feed the populace?” In an economy built on slavery, those actually doing the production had no incentive to innovate. Economies based on repression, the authors note, are “notoriously noninnovative.”

After the legions withdrew from Britain in AD 411, civilization collapsed with stunning speed. At Vindolanda, a Roman fort near Hadrian’s Wall, archaeologists found letters from a centurion named Candidus (AD 103) that reveal a monetary economy, financial services, roads, literacy, and a postal system. By AD 450, money had vanished, urban areas were abandoned, people had forgotten how to make mortar, and only crude handmade pottery remained. “Nobody wrote from Vindolanda anymore.” England was a historical backwater — while Egyptians built pyramids, the English built Stonehenge.

Ethiopia provides a parallel. The Kingdom of Aksum around 400 BC traded with India, Arabia, and Rome, and coined its own money. But when Arab expansion cut its trade routes in the seventh century, it developed a feudal gult system in which holders extracted one-half to three-quarters of agricultural output — even more extractive than English feudalism. Cut off from the processes that transformed Europe, Ethiopian absolutism went unchallenged for over a millennium.

7. The Turning Point

In 1589, William Lee invented the stocking frame, a machine that could mechanize textile production. He demonstrated it for Queen Elizabeth I, who refused to grant a patent: “Consider what the invention could do to my poor subjects. It would assuredly bring to them ruin.” James I also refused. The monarchs were not concerned about workers’ welfare — they feared political destabilization. This fear of creative destruction is why, the authors argue, there was no sustained economic growth between the Neolithic Revolution and the Industrial Revolution. Living standards barely changed: Diocletian’s Edict on Maximum Prices from AD 301 shows wages almost identical to those of seventeenth-century Italian unskilled workers.

The English path to the Industrial Revolution began with the Magna Carta in 1215, when barons forced King John to acknowledge that he must consult to raise taxes. The first elected Parliament followed in 1265. Tudor centralization under Henry VII and Henry VIII built a nascent bureaucratic state — the Dissolution of the Monasteries removed the Church as an independent power center — but this very centralization created demand for Parliament as a counterweight.

By 1621, the Stuart kings had granted seven hundred monopolies covering nearly every commodity: “A man lived in a house built with monopoly bricks, heated by monopoly coal, ate monopoly butter, monopoly currants, monopoly red herrings, monopoly salmon, and monopoly lobsters.” The Statute of Monopolies of 1623 prohibited new domestic monopolies, but Charles I continued to sell overseas monopolies, levy forced loans, and extend the “ship money” tax inland. When he was finally forced to recall Parliament in 1640 to fund a war against Scotland, the Long Parliament refused to dissolve, and the English Civil War began.

The Civil War of 1642 split along institutional lines: Birmingham, whose industries had weak monopoly protection, supported Parliament; Shrewsbury, whose merchants depended on Crown-protected monopolies, supported the king. Cromwell’s Roundheads won and Charles was executed in 1649. But Cromwell’s own dictatorship proved that simply removing the king was not enough. The monarchy was restored in 1660, and Charles II and James II immediately tried to reassert absolutism.

The decisive break came with the Glorious Revolution of 1688. Parliament invited William of Orange to replace James II, and the resulting Bill of Rights established four foundational principles: Parliament determines the succession; the monarch cannot suspend laws; no taxation without Parliamentary consent; no standing army without Parliamentary consent. Authority shifted permanently from crown to Parliament. Absolutism in England was over.

“Anybody could petition Parliament, and people did.” — Acemoglu & Robinson

The economic transformation was immediate and dramatic. Property rights were strengthened. The hearth tax was abolished in 1689. The Bank of England was created in 1694, launching a financial revolution. Records from C. Hoare’s bank (1702–1724) show that two-thirds of the biggest borrowers were merchants and businessmen, not aristocrats. The Royal African Company’s slave-trade monopoly was broken in 1698 after 135 petitions from interlopers gathered 8,000 signatures against the company’s 2,500. State capacity expanded: government expenditures reached ten percent of national income, and the excise tax bureaucracy grew from 1,211 officials in 1690 to 4,800 by 1780.

The Industrial Revolution that followed was made possible by these institutional foundations. By 1760, patented inventions surged. James Watt developed the separate condenser; Henry Cort revolutionized wrought iron; Abraham Darby pioneered coal smelting. The transformation of cotton production tells the story in numbers: spinning 100 pounds of cotton by hand required 50,000 hours; Arkwright’s water frame reduced this to 300 hours; the self-acting mule brought it to 135. Arkwright built one of the world’s first factories at Cromford in 1771, employing 600 workers. English exports doubled between 1780 and 1800.

The contrast with the Continent is telling. Denis Papin built a working steamboat in 1705 but boatmen’s guilds at Münden destroyed it, and Leibniz’s petition for safe passage was rebuffed. Papin died a pauper in an unmarked grave. In post-1688 England, such destruction of innovation was institutionally impossible. The men who drove the Industrial Revolution were overwhelmingly new men: James Brindley, the canal engineer, was a rural millwright; George Stephenson, creator of The Rocket, was the son of illiterate parents. Only one-fifth of leading cotton industrialists had any previous manufacturing experience.

Inclusive institutions did not prevent conflict, but they channeled it toward reform rather than repression. On August 16, 1819, sixty thousand protestors gathered at St. Peter’s Fields in Manchester demanding “No Corn Laws” and “Universal Suffrage.” Cavalry charged the crowd, killing eleven and wounding six hundred — the Peterloo Massacre. But in the long run, repression was not viable under inclusive institutions. The First Reform Act of 1832 enfranchised the industrial cities; the Corn Laws were repealed in 1846.

8. Not on Our Turf

The previous chapters established that absolutism blocks inclusive institutions. But absolutism is not the only barrier. Large parts of the world, especially in Africa, lacked even the minimal state centralization necessary for law, order, and secure property rights. Without a state that can enforce contracts, protect property, and maintain peace, economic development is impossible — regardless of how power is distributed.

Somalia illustrates this vividly. Somali society was organized into six clan families — Dir, Darod, Isaq, Hawiye, Digil, and Rahanweyn — each subdivided into diya-paying groups that settled disputes through “blood wealth.” The Hassan Ugaas lineage heer, recorded by a British district commissioner on March 8, 1950, specified twenty camels for murder by an outsider and thirty-three and a third for wounding. Leaders called sultans and elders had no real coercive power; every adult male had a say through informal councils. There was no written law, no police force, no legal system beyond Sharia and the informal heer codes.

The consequences were predictable. In early 1955, a wounded man triggered a chain of retaliations between the Habar Tol Ja’lo and Habar Yuunis clans near Domberelly. A botched blood-wealth ceremony led to a mistaken killing, then all-out warfare: thirteen Yuunis and twenty-six Tol Ja’lo died in forty-eight hours. The conflict continued for another year before British colonial officers brokered a settlement paid over three years. Blood wealth was always paid in the shadow of force — it usually just delayed the next eruption.

Political power was widely dispersed — almost pluralistic — but without a centralized state this did not produce inclusive institutions. Each clan rejected subjugation to any other, and the balance of military power made centralization impossible. Any clan attempting to centralize faced stiff resistance and would lose its existing power and privileges.

The Kingdom of Taqali in the Nuba Hills of Sudan illustrates a related dynamic. Taqali kings had access to Arabic writing but used it only for external diplomacy. Historian Janet Ewald, investigating in the late 1970s, discovered that citizens resisted writing because they feared the state would use it to claim land ownership and impose systematic taxation. Rulers, for their part, preferred oral interaction — it gave them maximum discretion. Written laws set benchmarks that were harder to reverse. Neither rulers nor ruled wanted the tools of state capacity, and without those tools, economic development was impossible.

“Political centralization is resisted for the same reason that absolutist regimes resist change: fear that it will reallocate political power.” — Acemoglu & Robinson

African societies did not use wheeled transport, plow agriculture, or writing — not because of cultural backwardness but because the politics of societies like Somalia and Taqali made these technologies dangerous to adopt. Writing could be used against you. Plows could create wealth that would be seized. The political environment stripped away the incentive to innovate.

The synthesis is crucial. Nations under extractive absolutist institutions — Spain, Ethiopia, the Ottoman Empire — choked incentives for growth. Austria-Hungary, Russia, and China took explicit steps to block industrialization. But societies that lacked even basic centralization — Somalia, Afghanistan, Haiti, Nepal — were equally unable to benefit from the Industrial Revolution. While industrialization was transforming England and spreading across Europe, Somalis were feuding and fending for their lives. Inclusive institutions require both pluralism and a centralized state.

The chapter draws a direct line to the present. Even today, the countries with the weakest states — Afghanistan, Haiti, Somalia — remain among the poorest on earth. Economic incentives are “all but destroyed” when the state cannot maintain rudimentary order. Societies that had already taken steps toward inclusive institutions, like the United States and Australia, or where absolutism was seriously challenged, like France and Japan, seized the opportunities of industrialization. Those trapped between absolutism and anarchy were left behind.

9. Reversing Development

European expansion simultaneously fueled inclusive institutions in Britain and sowed the seeds of underdevelopment across much of the rest of the world. The Moluccan Archipelago was the world’s only source of cloves, mace, and nutmeg. The Dutch East India Company (VOC), founded in 1602 with its own army and the power to wage war, realized that monopolizing supply was more profitable than competing on price. In 1621, Governor Jan Pieterszoon Coen sailed to the Banda Islands and massacred almost the entire population — approximately 15,000 people. The islands were divided into sixty-eight parcels, given to Dutchmen who imported slaves to work them. World spice supply fell by roughly sixty percent and the price of nutmeg doubled.

Even polities not directly colonized turned inward. Banten cut down its pepper trees; the ruler of Maguindanao forbade further planting. Burma moved its capital from the coast to inland Ava in 1635. Southeast Asian states became more absolutist and less commercial, unable to take advantage of the Industrial Revolution when it arrived.

The Atlantic slave trade was the most devastating instance of development reversal. In the sixteenth century, roughly 300,000 Africans were enslaved; in the seventeenth, 1.35 million; in the eighteenth, six million. In total, well over ten million Africans were shipped across the Atlantic. In Angola, Benin, Ghana, and Togo, cumulative slave exports exceeded the entire population as of 1400.

“Since this slave trade has been us’d, all punishments are changed into slavery.” — Francis Moore, 1730s

The slave trade fed on itself. By 1730, approximately 180,000 guns were being imported annually along the West African coast. British merchants alone sold 283,000 to 394,000 guns per year in the mid-eighteenth century, along with 22,000 tons of gunpowder. Polities like Oyo and Asante reorganized entirely around enslaving — eighty to ninety percent of Oyo’s exported slaves were war captives. The Oracle of Arochukwa operated a chilling partnership between priests and slavers: people “swallowed” by the oracle were led through a cave to waiting European ships. Patrick Manning estimates that Africa’s population should have been 46 to 53 million by 1850 but was roughly half that — the difference reflecting not just the eight million exported but the millions killed in internal warfare.

The British abolished the slave trade in 1807 and slavery itself in 1834, but the end of external demand did not reverse the damage. Slavery became more prevalent within Africa. Asante settled slaves on plantations producing gold, kola nuts, and food. In many West African kingdoms, well over half the population were slaves. Sierra Leone did not abolish slavery until 1928; Liberia had an estimated one-quarter of its labor force under coercion as late as the 1960s.

South Africa provides the most detailed case study of deliberate development reversal. Before the mineral discoveries, African farmers were dynamic and commercially oriented. Xhosa farmers readily adopted plows, wagons, and irrigation. By 1882, roughly 8,000 African farmers had purchased 90,000 acres. Stephen Sonjica, a self-made farmer, secretly saved eighty pounds to buy his own land. In 1869, prosperous African farmers donated money to the Lancashire Cotton Relief Fund — Africans helping poor English workers.

The discovery of diamonds at Kimberley in 1867 and gold at Johannesburg in 1886 changed everything. Mining magnates needed cheap labor. George Albu, chairman of the Association of Mines, asked in 1897: “Why should a nigger be allowed to do nothing?” The Natives Land Act of 1913 allocated eighty-seven percent of the land to Europeans, who comprised roughly twenty percent of the population. The advances of fifty years were systematically reversed: people gave up plows and reverted to hoes. Mining wages fell thirty percent between 1911 and 1921 and were still twelve percent lower in 1961 than in 1911. Hendrik Verwoerd declared in 1954: “There is no place for him in the European community above the level of certain forms of labour.” The dual economy ended in 1994, not through the natural development that Arthur Lewis had theorized, but through black political mobilization after Soweto in 1976.

10. The Diffusion of Prosperity

Australia followed a path remarkably parallel to the American colonies. When Captain Arthur Phillip’s fleet arrived at Sydney Cove in January 1788, the settlement was a penal colony. But as in Virginia, there was no indigenous population to exploit, no gold to seize, and forced labor quickly proved impractical. Punishment meant lashing or banishment to Norfolk Island, a thirteen-square-mile rock a thousand miles east of the mainland — but even this could not make convicts productive. Guards had to give convicts incentives: extra time to work for themselves, permission to sell what they produced, and land and full rights restored after completing sentences.

Henry and Susannah Cable, married convicts transported on the Alexander, brought the first civil case in Australian history when goods donated for their journey vanished during the voyage. Under British law, convicts could not own property or sue in court. But Judge Advocate David Collins issued a writ anyway, and Captain Sinclair was ordered to pay fifteen pounds. From that moment, Australian law diverged from British precedent. By 1809, Henry Cable owned nine farms totaling roughly 470 acres, shops, houses, the Ramping Horse hotel, and a ship trading in sealskins.

The ensuing political struggle between the squattocracy — wealthy sheep magnates like John Macarthur, who arrived as a soldier in 1790 and became the richest man in Australia — and the ex-convict majority, led by figures like William Wentworth, whose father had been an accused highway robber, produced increasingly inclusive institutions. Macarthur dismissed Wentworth’s supporters as men “sentenced to be hung” and a “son of two convicts.” But Wentworth’s newspaper The Australian won the argument. Governor Richard Bourke allowed ex-convicts to sit on juries in 1831. By 1842 a legislative council was created with two-thirds elected members. Victoria and Tasmania became the first places in the world to implement an effective secret ballot, still known internationally as “the Australian ballot.” By the 1850s, Australia had adult white male suffrage.

The French Revolution of 1789 destroyed absolutism across much of continental Europe. On August 4, the National Constituent Assembly abolished feudalism, serfdom, tax privileges, and guild restrictions. Despite decades of instability — the Terror, Napoleon’s dictatorship, restoration — the revolution was an irreversible step away from the ancien régime, culminating in the Third Republic of 1870, a parliamentary system similar to what the Glorious Revolution had created in England.

The revolution’s reach extended far beyond France. In Frankfurt’s Judengasse, a quarter-mile walled ghetto twelve feet wide, no more than 500 Jewish families were permitted to live, with a maximum of twelve marriages per year and both partners required to be over twenty-five. Mayer Amschel Rothschild, the leading dealer in coins and antiques, could not open a business outside the ghetto. French emancipation of Jewry in 1791 changed everything; the 1796 bombardment of Frankfurt demolished half the Judengasse. Full emancipation came in 1811 under a Napoleon-appointed Grand Duke. Mayer Amschel told his son: “You are now a citizen.” The Rothschild sons built the largest bank in nineteenth-century Europe.

“All citizens, without distinction of birth, are eligible to any office or dignity.” — Article 11, French National Constituent Assembly, 1789

Napoleon codified these reforms into the Code Napoleon — equality before the law, abolition of feudalism and guilds — and imposed it across every territory he conquered. In Nassau-Usingen, peasants had been subject to 230 different payments, dues, and services, including a “blood tithe” after animal slaughter and a bee tithe. In Cologne and Aachen, guilds blocked spinning and weaving machines. Napoleon swept all of it away. By the mid-nineteenth century, industrialization was underway in nearly all French-conquered areas; unconquered Austria-Hungary, Russia, Poland, and Spain remained stagnant.

Japan’s Meiji Restoration of 1868 provides the key non-Western example. In autumn 1867, Ōkubo Toshimichi of the Satsuma domain traveled to Edo and forged the secret Satcho Alliance with the Chōshū domain. On January 3, 1868, they declared the Meiji Restoration and launched sweeping reforms: feudalism abolished in 1869, three hundred fiefs surrendered to the government and turned into prefectures; equality of all social classes before the law; the samurai class dissolved; individual property rights established; the first railway from Tokyo to Yokohama; and by 1890 the first written constitution in Asia.

Japan succeeded where China failed because of a crucial structural difference. China had a centralized bureaucratic empire where opposition could not be institutionalized — the only way to change the system was mass rebellion, as in the Taiping Rebellion of 1850–1864. Japan’s feudal domains maintained enough independence to form a coalition against the shogun. When Commodore Matthew Perry’s warships appeared in Edo Bay in 1853, making clear that economic backwardness meant military vulnerability, the Satsuma and Chōshū domains could organize an overthrow. In China, the emperor was not overthrown until 1911, and conditions worsened under Mao’s communist revolution.

11. The Virtuous Circle

In 1722, groups of men with blackened faces — the “Blacks” — roamed southern England killing deer, burning haystacks, and destroying fences in protest against elite encroachment on traditional rights. William Cadogan had bought roughly a thousand acres near Windsor and evicted people from lands they had used for generations. In May 1723, the Black Act created fifty new capital offenses. Prime Minister Walpole personally pressed for convictions.

Yet when John Huntridge was tried in 1724, a jury of major landowners found him innocent despite Walpole’s prosecution. The historian E.P. Thompson captured the paradox: the rulers were “prisoners of their own rhetoric; they played games of power according to rules which suited them, but they could not break those rules or the whole game would be thrown away.” The rule of law — the principle that laws apply equally to all, including the powerful — is a “very strange concept in historical perspective,” made possible only by pluralistic institutions.

“My object is not to favour, but to put an end to such hopes… reforming to preserve and not to overthrow.” — Earl Grey, on the Reform Act

The virtuous circle operates through four reinforcing mechanisms: pluralism combined with the rule of law constrains power; inclusive economic institutions empower broader segments of society; independent media helps organize against threats; and inclusive economies generate urbanization, education, and innovation that make repression increasingly costly.

Britain’s democratic expansion illustrates the circle in action. The Luddite Riots, Peterloo Massacre, and Swing Riots created a consensus that only reform could prevent revolution. The First Reform Act of 1832 doubled the electorate from eight to sixteen percent. The Second Reform Act of 1867 gave working-class men the majority in urban constituencies. The Third Reform Act of 1884 extended the vote to sixty percent of adult males. Women achieved full equality in 1928. The Education Act of 1870 pushed school attendance from forty percent of ten-year-olds to one hundred percent by 1900.

In America, the virtuous circle manifested as trust-busting. John D. Rockefeller’s Standard Oil controlled eighty-eight percent of refined oil by 1890. J.P. Morgan and Andrew Carnegie created U.S. Steel in 1901 — the first billion-dollar corporation. The Sherman Antitrust Act of 1890, Roosevelt’s breakup of Northern Securities in 1902, and Taft’s dissolution of Standard Oil in 1911 demonstrated that inclusive institutions could discipline even the most powerful private interests. Woodrow Wilson warned: “If monopoly persists, monopoly will always sit at the helm of government.” Muckraking journalists — Ida Tarbell’s History of Standard Oil, Louis Brandeis’s Other People’s Money — mobilized public opinion and helped pass the Seventeenth Amendment requiring direct election of senators.

The ultimate test of the virtuous circle came in 1937, when Franklin Roosevelt attempted to pack the Supreme Court by adding up to six justices. Despite his enormous popularity, only forty percent of the public supported the plan. Louis Brandeis, who privately sympathized with Roosevelt’s objectives, spoke against it. The Senate voted 70–20 to strip the court-packing provisions.

The contrast with Argentina is sharp. Perón successfully impeached four of five Supreme Court justices in 1946. Menem expanded the court from five to nine in 1990. Every Argentine regime picks its own court. The difference is that in the United States, pluralistic institutions had created broad segments of society willing to defend judicial independence even against a popular president.

12. The Vicious Circle

Where the virtuous circle reinforces inclusion, the vicious circle perpetuates extraction through three distinct mechanisms. Sierra Leone after independence in 1961 illustrates the first: colonial extractive institutions captured by new rulers. Siaka Stevens won the 1967 election and promptly tore up the railway that connected the Mende-dominated south to the capital — an act reminiscent of Tsar Nicholas I fearing that railways would bring revolution to St. Petersburg. Marketing boards, the colonial tool for taxing farmers, became Stevens’s personal extraction machine: farmers sometimes received as little as ten percent of the world price for their crops, with ninety percent siphoned to the ruling elite.

Stevens emasculated the army and created paramilitaries whose acronyms told the truth: ISU (“I Shoot U”) and SSD (“Siaka Stevens’s Dogs”). Sam Bangura, the central bank governor who dared to criticize Stevens in 1980, was murdered — thrown from the top floor of a building onto the street below, which happened to be named Siaka Stevens Street.

Guatemala demonstrates the vicious circle’s most extreme form: the same families maintaining control for five centuries. On January 14, 1993, the President, Finance Minister, and Development Minister of Guatemala were all descended from sixteenth-century conquistadors. Sociologist Marta Casáus Arzú documented that twenty-two core families and twenty-six connected ones have controlled the country since 1531. The mechanisms of extraction evolved — encomienda, repartimiento, Consulado de Comercio, coffee plantations, vagrancy laws — but the extractors remained constant. Under Jorge Ubico (1931–1944), using the words “obreros,” “sindicatos,” or “huelgas” could land you in prison.

“The cow eats where it is tethered.” — Siaka Stevens

The U.S. South shows the vicious circle operating within a democratic nation. After the Civil War, slavery was abolished, but the planter elite “lost the war but would win the peace.” Jonathan Wiener found that of 236 planter families in five Alabama counties in 1850, 101 maintained their elite position in 1870. Jim Crow replaced slavery: vagrancy laws, anti-enticement statutes, poll taxes, and literacy tests. By 1900, the South was only 13.5 percent urbanized, compared to sixty percent in the Northeast.

The second mechanism is the iron law of oligarchy. In Ethiopia, Emperor Haile Selassie employed a special bearer to carry fifty-two pillows and was overthrown by the Derg military junta in 1974. By 1978, Mengistu Haile Mariam was sitting in a gilded armchair on Selassie’s throne. A former minister wrote: “We were supposed to have a revolution of equality; now he had become the new Emperor.” Mengistu used famine as a political weapon, reproducing the extractive institutions he had supposedly overthrown.

13. Why Nations Fail Today

In January 2000, the Zimbank national lottery in Zimbabwe drew a winning ticket bearing the name of President Robert Mugabe. The prize was Z$100,000 — roughly five times the annual per capita income. The rigged lottery was a small symptom of a comprehensively rigged state. By 2008, per capita income had fallen to half its 1980 level, unemployment reached ninety-four percent, and a cholera epidemic killed over 4,000 people. Mugabe had eliminated opponents — up to 20,000 killed in Matabeleland — merged opposition parties, rewritten the constitution to concentrate power, and after 2000 unleashed “war veterans” to occupy white-owned farms, destroying the agricultural base and triggering hyperinflation that rendered the Zimbabwean dollar worthless by January 2009.

Zimbabwe’s colonial origins explain the persistence. Cecil Rhodes’s BSAC expedition entered the Ndebele kingdom in 1890; by 1923, Southern Rhodesia had self-government under a white elite comprising roughly five percent of the population. Ian Smith declared independence in 1965, triggering UN sanctions and a guerrilla war. When ZANU and ZAPU negotiated the end of white rule, Mugabe simply took over the colonial extractive institutions — price regulations, marketing boards, state industries — and redirected them to benefit ZANU-PF. The iron law of oligarchy operated perfectly: Smith’s extractive regime was replaced by Mugabe’s equally extractive regime.

Sierra Leone’s civil war (1991–2001) confirmed that the country had been a failed state long before the Revolutionary United Front invaded from Liberia. Foday Sankoh, a former corporal who had trained at Qaddafi’s camp in Libya, crossed the border on March 23, 1991 with Charles Taylor’s men. Under Stevens’s successor Momoh, the state had disintegrated: national television stopped broadcasting in 1987 when the minister of information sold the transmitter; the radio tower collapsed in 1989; by the end of Momoh’s rule, he had stopped paying civil servants, teachers, and even paramount chiefs. Soldiers became “sobels” — soldiers by day, rebels by night. The RUF recruited child soldiers and amputated hands and ears as a terror tactic. Roughly 80,000 people died.

In Colombia, the state’s inability to project authority across its territory created space for both guerrillas and paramilitaries. After FARC kidnapped Jesús Castaño in 1981, demanding $7,500 ransom — the family mortgaged their farm and found the father’s corpse chained to a tree — his sons founded paramilitary groups that grew into the AUC, fielding 30,000 men by 2001. The Pact of Santa Fé de Ralito, a “refounding of the country” signed by paramilitary leaders and senators, revealed that roughly one-third of Colombia’s congressmen owed their 2002 election to paramilitary support. Salvatore Mancuso confirmed: “35 percent of the Congress was elected in areas where there were Self-Defense groups.” Some 4.5 million people were internally displaced and paramilitaries expropriated up to ten percent of all rural land.

Argentina’s decline illustrates how extractive political institutions can hollow out a wealthy country over generations. Simon Kuznets reportedly observed that there are four types of countries: developed, undeveloped, Japan, and Argentina. Before 1914, Argentina enjoyed fifty years of commodity-driven growth under extractive institutions, but this was growth without creative destruction — not sustainable. The El Corralito of December 2001 froze bank accounts; the January 2002 devaluation forcibly converted dollar accounts to pesos at the old one-to-one rate, effectively expropriating three-quarters of citizens’ savings. Every Argentine regime has packed or replaced the Supreme Court — Perón impeached four of five justices in 1946, Menem expanded it from five to nine in 1990 — ensuring that no institutional check survives a change of government.

North Korea’s 2009 currency reform wiped out citizens’ private wealth by limiting conversions to roughly $40 at black-market rates, while Kim Jong-Il spent $800,000 annually on Hennessy cognac alone and maintained a seven-story pleasure palace with a wave pool and karaoke bar. In Uzbekistan, cotton constitutes forty-five percent of exports, and 2.7 million children are pulled from school each harvest to pick it — earning roughly three cents per kilogram against a world price of $1.40. When the U.S. company Interspan built a successful tea business capturing thirty percent of the local market, President Karimov’s daughter Gulnora decided the tea industry looked promising: Interspan personnel were arrested, beaten, and tortured, and the company’s assets were seized. The Karimov family’s tea share jumped from two to sixty-seven percent.

In Egypt, privatization under Mubarak merely transferred state monopolies to thirty-two connected “whale” families. Ahmed Ezz controlled seventy percent of the country’s steel production while serving as chairman of the People’s Assembly Budget Committee and a close associate of Gamal Mubarak. Ahmed Zayat’s Luxor Group bought Al Ahram Beverages through connected privatization, then sold it to Heineken five years later for a 563 percent profit. Mubarak planned dynastic succession through his son Gamal — a plan cut short by the Arab Spring of 2011.

The chapter’s conclusion links all cases. Different histories produced different elites and different forms of extraction — communism in North Korea, crony capitalism in Egypt, paramilitary symbiosis in Colombia, colonial continuity in Zimbabwe — but the common thread is always the vicious circle of extractive institutions. The intensity of extraction correlates directly with the depth of poverty: North Korea’s total extraction produces near-total immiseration, while Argentina’s less extreme extraction produces merely chronic underperformance. Even when extractive institutions cause state collapse, as in Sierra Leone, the old extractive elite often returns — in 2007, Stevens’s old party the APC won power again, and his sons were made ambassadors.

14. Breaking the Mold

In September 1895, three Tswana chiefs — Khama, Bathoen, and Sebele — sailed from Bechuanaland to Britain to plead against incorporation into Cecil Rhodes’s British South Africa Company. They mounted a nationwide speaking tour, and Rhodes cabled in frustration: “I do object to being beaten by three canting natives.” They succeeded, and their nation’s trajectory would be fundamentally different from its neighbors.

Botswana’s success rests on preexisting inclusive traditions. The kgotla, or general assembly, allowed anyone to speak and could overrule the chief. A Tswana proverb captures the principle: kgosi ke kgosi ka morafe — “the king is king by the grace of the people.” At independence in 1966, Botswana had twelve kilometers of paved roads and twenty-two university graduates. It was one of the poorest countries on earth. Today it has the highest per capita income in sub-Saharan Africa, comparable to Estonia or Hungary.

“The king is king by the grace of the people.” — Tswana proverb

Seretse Khama, Botswana’s first president, made a crucial decision before the discovery of diamonds was announced: he changed the law so that all subsoil mineral rights were vested in the nation, not in individual tribes. In Sierra Leone, diamonds fueled civil war. In Botswana, diamond revenues built the state.

The U.S. civil rights movement broke the Southern vicious circle from within. On December 1, 1955, Rosa Parks was arrested in Montgomery, Alabama, sparking a bus boycott that lasted over a year. Economic logic was already weakening the old order: mass black outmigration reached 100,000 per year, and cotton harvesting was rapidly mechanizing. But the legal and political system resisted. Louisiana’s literacy test deemed a white applicant literate after he wrote “FRDUM FOOF SPETGH.” Strom Thurmond filibustered the 1957 Civil Rights Act for twenty-four hours and eighteen minutes. The Voting Rights Act of 1965 transformed the South: black voter registration in Mississippi jumped from five percent in 1960 to fifty percent by 1970. Southern per capita income, which stood at roughly half the national average in 1940, essentially converged with the rest of the country by 1990. Being part of the United States was decisive — the South was subject to federal law.

China’s economic transformation under Deng Xiaoping represents perhaps the most dramatic example of breaking the mold, but also its limits. Mao’s Great Leap Forward killed twenty to forty million people. Deng had long argued for pragmatism: “No matter whether the cat is black or white, if it catches mice, it’s a good cat.” Purged during the Cultural Revolution and exiled to a tractor factory in Jiangxi, he returned after Mao’s death in September 1976 to win a factional struggle against the Gang of Four. The Third Plenum of November–December 1978 shifted the party’s focus from class struggle to economic modernization. By 1983, the household responsibility system was universal, and by 1984 grain output was one-third higher than in 1978. But China’s political institutions remained extractive — the transformation was highly contingent, and the authors warn that sustained growth without political reform is unlikely.

15. Understanding Prosperity and Poverty

The final chapter synthesizes the argument at two levels. The first is the institutional interpretation: nations with inclusive economic and political institutions prosper; those with extractive institutions do not. The second is the historical mechanism: institutional drift creates small differences between similar societies, and critical junctures — the Black Death, Atlantic trade expansion, the Industrial Revolution — amplify these differences into permanently divergent trajectories. Growth under extractive institutions is possible but not sustained, because elites resist the creative destruction that threatens their power.

The China mirage dominates the contemporary debate. Dai Guofang started a steel company that competed with state-sponsored firms and was arrested in 2004, jailed for five years. Chen Yun’s metaphor captures the party’s philosophy: the economy is a bird, and the party’s control is the cage — “enlarge the cage but never unlock it.” Richard McGregor reports that every major state company CEO has a red phone on his desk — the party calls with orders. Chinese growth resembles Soviet growth in the 1950s and 1960s: impressive catch-up under extractive institutions. Liu Xiaobo was imprisoned and awarded the Nobel Peace Prize while behind bars.

“Enlarge the cage but never unlock it.” — Chen Yun, on China’s economy

Modernization theory — the idea that economic growth automatically produces democracy — is wrong. Germany and Japan were among the richest and most educated nations on earth before succumbing to Nazism and militarism. Argentina was one of the world’s wealthiest countries in the nineteenth century and became no more democratic for it. The causation runs from institutions to growth, not the reverse.

Nor can prosperity be engineered from outside. Zimbabwe declared its central bank independent in 1995. Inflation went from twenty percent to 230 million percent by 2008. The central bank governor knew exactly what had happened to Sam Bangura — compliance with Mugabe was, as one official put it, “a prudent choice for his personal health.” Microeconomic interventions fare no better: when the Seva Mandir NGO tried nurse attendance incentives in Udaipur, the local health administration deliberately sabotaged the scheme within a year. In Afghanistan, a village shelter program lost roughly twenty percent at every administrative layer until only oversized wooden beams arrived — too large to use, they were burned as firewood. Studies estimate that only ten to twenty percent of foreign aid reaches its intended target.

The path forward is empowerment. On May 12, 1978, workers at a Scânia truck factory in São Paulo went on strike — the first since strikes had been banned following the 1964 coup. The strike was led by Lula da Silva, then thirty-three years old. The Workers’ Party was founded in October 1979, fusing unions, intellectuals, social movements, and opposition politicians. Porto Alegre pioneered participatory budgeting. When Lula was elected president in 2002, poverty fell from forty-five to thirty percent and inequality dropped sharply. Brazil’s transformation was not engineered by economists or foreign aid — it was the consequence of groups building inclusive institutions from below.

Three factors facilitate empowerment: a centralized state that provides basic order, preexisting pluralistic traditions (even rudimentary ones like the kgotla), and civil society institutions including independent media. The Fujimori/Montesinos bribery records from Peru revealed that a Supreme Court judge cost $5,000 to $10,000 per month to bribe, but controlling a television station cost millions. A Chinese commentator summarized the extractive playbook: “Three principles: the Party controls the armed forces; the Party controls the cadres; the Party controls the news.” Meaningful change requires broad mobilization to transform extractive institutions into inclusive ones — not merely to seize them.

Trading & Investing Lessons

Extractive Institutions Are Value Traps

A country with extractive institutions can generate impressive short-term growth — the Soviet Union grew six percent annually for three decades, and China has dazzled for four. But this growth is reallocation, not innovation. In market terms, these are value traps: the headline numbers look compelling, but the underlying business model destroys long-term value. Investing in countries with extractive political institutions means betting that the current growth rate will persist without the creative destruction that sustains it. The Soviet economy collapsed; the authors predict China will slow for the same reasons.

Creative Destruction Is the Only Source of Compounding

Sustained wealth creation — whether for nations or portfolios — requires creative destruction. Old industries must die so new ones can emerge. The Industrial Revolution happened in England because inclusive institutions let innovators like Arkwright and Watt disrupt incumbents. In markets, the same principle holds: sectors that resist disruption (regulated monopolies, state-owned enterprises) may look stable but they compound poorly. The real returns come from economies and companies where innovation is rewarded, not suppressed.

Institutions Are the Macro Overlay

Before analyzing any country’s stocks, bonds, or currency, the first question should be: are the institutions inclusive or extractive? Argentina had one of the highest per capita incomes in the world in 1900 and has been a chronic underperformer since. Botswana was one of the poorest countries on earth in 1966 and has compounded wealth for six decades. The institutional framework is the single most powerful macro factor — more predictive than GDP growth, commodity prices, or central bank policy.

The Vicious Circle Is Negative Momentum

Extractive institutions create self-reinforcing feedback loops: elites extract wealth, use it to entrench power, extract more wealth. This is the institutional equivalent of negative momentum — once a country enters the vicious circle, each year makes the next worse. Zimbabwe, Sierra Leone, and Venezuela all show the pattern: each policy intervention (land seizures, nationalization, price controls) accelerates decline rather than reversing it. For investors, the signal is clear: when a country’s institutions are deteriorating, do not buy the dip. The fundamentals are getting worse, not better.

The Virtuous Circle Is Compounding

Inclusive institutions create the opposite feedback loop: rule of law attracts investment, investment creates a middle class, the middle class defends rule of law. This is how wealth compounds at the national level. The United States, Britain, Australia, and Botswana all demonstrate the pattern. For investors, the implication is to overweight countries where the virtuous circle is intact and institutions are strengthening — the institutional tailwind compounds just like interest.

Critical Junctures Are Regime Changes

The book’s critical junctures — the Black Death, Atlantic trade, the Industrial Revolution — are the macro equivalent of regime changes in markets. These are moments when small pre-existing differences get amplified into permanent divergences. For traders: the lesson is that positioning before a critical juncture matters enormously. Countries that entered the Industrial Revolution with inclusive institutions pulled permanently ahead; those that didn’t fell permanently behind. The modern equivalents — the AI revolution, the energy transition — will similarly reward countries whose institutions allow creative destruction and punish those that suppress it.

Key Takeaways

Institutions determine prosperity

The difference between rich and poor nations is not geography, culture, or the quality of economic advice. It is institutions — the rules that structure incentives, distribute power, and constrain elites. Inclusive institutions secure property rights, encourage competition, and allow creative destruction. Extractive institutions concentrate wealth and power in narrow hands.

Political institutions drive economic ones

Economic institutions do not arise in a vacuum. Those who hold political power shape the rules of the economic game. Inclusive political institutions — pluralistic and centralized — produce inclusive economics. Absolutist political institutions produce extractive economics.

Creative destruction is the mechanism of sustained growth

Technological innovation, by its nature, destroys old industries and threatens existing power structures. Elites under extractive institutions resist this. From Elizabeth I rejecting the stocking frame to Roman emperors killing inventors to Ming China turning away from the seas, the fear of creative destruction explains why most of human history saw no sustained economic growth.

Small differences are amplified by critical junctures

Societies drift apart through small, cumulative institutional differences. These differences become decisive during major disruptions: the Black Death, Atlantic trade, the Industrial Revolution. The same event can push similar societies in opposite directions.

Growth under extraction is possible but temporary

The Soviet Union, the Maya, the Kuba Kingdom, and modern China all demonstrate that extractive institutions can generate short-term growth through reallocation and capital accumulation. But without creative destruction, this growth eventually stalls.

The vicious and virtuous circles are self-reinforcing

Inclusive institutions create feedback loops that protect and extend inclusion. Extractive institutions create feedback loops that perpetuate extraction — through elite persistence, the iron law of oligarchy, and destructive infighting. Breaking out of a vicious circle requires broad political mobilization, not just a change of leadership.

Contrarian Ideas

Geography is irrelevant to modern prosperity

Conventional view: tropical climates, poor soils, and endemic diseases cause poverty. Counter-evidence: Nogales, North/South Korea, East/West Germany share geography but diverge dramatically. Tropical civilizations were once the richest on earth. Diseases are consequences of poverty, not causes.

Culture does not explain development

Conventional view: some cultures — Protestant, Confucian, Western — are more conducive to economic success. Counter-evidence: the Kongolese rejected plows but adopted guns; North and South Koreans share a culture but live in different worlds; “Chinese culture” produced both Mao’s catastrophe and Deng’s miracle. Incentives, not values, drive behavior.

Foreign aid cannot create prosperity

Conventional view: targeted aid, conditional lending, and technical assistance can lift nations out of poverty. Counter-evidence: only ten to twenty percent of aid reaches targets; conditional aid props up dictators; institutional engineering fails because formal independence (of courts, central banks) means nothing without political pluralism to enforce it.

Economic growth does not produce democracy

Conventional view: as countries get richer, they become democratic. Counter-evidence: Germany and Japan were rich and educated before fascism. Argentina was among the world’s wealthiest. China has grown rapidly with no democratic opening. The causation runs from institutions to growth, not the reverse.

China’s growth will not last

Conventional view: China will continue to grow and eventually democratize. Counter-evidence: Chinese growth resembles Soviet growth — reallocation and accumulation, not innovation. The party actively suppresses creative destruction (Dai Guofang case). Without political reform, growth rates will gradually decline.

Proposals & Frameworks

The Inclusive/Extractive Matrix

All institutions can be classified along two axes: economic (inclusive vs. extractive) and political (pluralistic vs. absolutist). Sustained prosperity requires inclusive institutions on both dimensions. Extractive-inclusive combinations are unstable and tend to resolve toward one pole. The matrix provides a diagnostic tool for any nation: identify where it sits and you can predict its trajectory.

Critical Junctures Framework

Major events (plague, trade routes opening, wars, revolutions) interact with pre-existing small institutional differences to produce divergent outcomes. Policy implication: the moments of institutional vulnerability — democratic transitions, economic crises, post-conflict periods — are when the trajectory of nations can shift. What matters is the breadth of the coalition that seizes the juncture.

Three Mechanisms of the Vicious Circle

(1) Elite persistence: the same families control institutions across centuries (Guatemala). (2) Iron law of oligarchy: new rulers reproduce the same extraction (Ethiopia, Sierra Leone). (3) Destructive infighting: competition to control extraction leads to civil war and state failure (Somalia, Congo). Each mechanism suggests different interventions.

Four Mechanisms of the Virtuous Circle

(1) Rule of law: pluralism constrains power. (2) Economic empowerment: inclusive economies create broad middle classes with political weight. (3) Independent media: free press organizes resistance to backsliding. (4) Positive feedback: urbanization, education, and innovation make repression increasingly costly.

What Didn’t Fit

The Gosplan Comedy of Errors

Soviet central planning produced absurdities that deserve more attention than the chapter prose could give. Steel sheet targets measured in tons yielded sheets too heavy for any practical use. Targets measured in area produced sheets too thin to be structural. Chandeliers planned by weight were too heavy to hang from ceilings. Only one of Gosplan’s five-year plans was ever labeled “final” — the rest remained “drafts” because the system could never actually compute what it promised to compute. Stalin called the plans “just the beginning,” using them primarily as tools to monitor political loyalty rather than allocate resources.

The Neolithic Revolution Reversed

The authors make a bold claim that challenges Jared Diamond directly: political hierarchy caused agriculture, not the reverse. Archaeological evidence from Natufian sites shows sedentary communities with clear social stratification before any farming. The Yir Yoront aboriginals of Australia, when given steel axes by missionaries, used the extra productivity not for more production but for more sleeping. Institutional context determines what people do with new technology — tools alone change nothing.

The Baldwyn Property Case

After the Glorious Revolution, the Baldwyn family invested £6,000 navigating the river Salwerpe. When Parliament later tried to transfer their rights to the Earl of Shrewsbury, the Baldwyns petitioned on the grounds that it would be a “dangerous consequence to take away any person’s right purchased under an act of Parliament.” Their rights were upheld — a small case illustrating the enormous shift in property security that made long-term investment possible in post-1688 England.

The Oracle of Arochukwa

In West Africa, the Aro people operated a notorious institution linking religion and slavery. People brought before the Oracle for judgment were “swallowed” by the spiritual power — in reality, led through a cave system to the riverbank, where European slave ships waited. The Oracle fused spiritual authority with the slave trade in a way that made both the institution and the trade nearly impossible to challenge from within the society.

Prosperous African Farmers Before the Land Act

Before South Africa’s deliberate destruction of African agriculture, black farmers were thriving commercially. By 1882, roughly 8,000 African farmers had purchased 90,000 acres of land. Stephen Sonjica secretly saved eighty pounds to buy his own farm. Perhaps most remarkably, in 1869 prosperous African farmers in the Cape Colony donated money to the Lancashire Cotton Relief Fund in England — Africans were literally helping poor English factory workers. The Natives Land Act of 1913 reversed all of this, confining Africans to thirteen percent of the land and destroying fifty years of agricultural progress.

Why Nations Fail: The Origins of Power, Prosperity, and Poverty
Daron Acemoglu & James A. Robinson
Crown Publishers, 2012 · 529 pages
A sweeping institutional theory of prosperity and poverty, arguing that nations succeed or fail based on whether their political and economic institutions are inclusive or extractive, supported by historical evidence spanning the Neolithic Revolution to the Arab Spring.

About the Authors

Daron Acemoglu is an Institute Professor at MIT, one of the most cited economists in the world, and a recipient of the John Bates Clark Medal (2005) and the Nobel Prize in Economics (2024, jointly with Robinson and Simon Johnson). Born in Istanbul to Armenian parents, he has published extensively on political economy, labor economics, and the role of institutions in development. His work combines rigorous econometric analysis with broad historical narrative.

James A. Robinson is a University Professor at the University of Chicago and the Reverend Dr. Richard L. Pearson Professor of Global Conflict Studies at the Harris School of Public Policy. A political scientist and economist who has conducted extensive fieldwork in Africa and Latin America, Robinson brings a comparative politics perspective to the institutional theory of development. Together with Acemoglu, he has shaped modern understanding of how political institutions determine economic outcomes.

Why This Book Matters

Why Nations Fail may be the most influential work of political economy published in the twenty-first century. It provides a unified framework for understanding the vast differences in living standards across the globe — a framework that is both intellectually rigorous and accessible to general readers. The book’s central insight — that institutions, not geography, culture, or ignorance, determine prosperity — has reshaped academic debates and policy conversations from the World Bank to national development agencies. Acemoglu was awarded the 2024 Nobel Prize in Economics for this body of work.

The book matters because it challenges comfortable assumptions. Foreign aid donors, World Bank economists, and development consultants have spent decades searching for technical solutions to poverty — the right macroeconomic policies, the right microeconomic interventions, the right institutions to transplant. Acemoglu and Robinson argue that this entire approach misses the point. Poverty is not a technical problem with a technical solution. It is a political problem that can only be solved through political transformation — the hard, contingent, often violent process of building inclusive institutions from the ground up.
