## PREFACE (pp. 1-6)

Written during Arab Spring — Mohamed Bouazizi's self-immolation
Dec 17, 2010 sparked revolution. Ben Ali (Tunisia, ruled since
1987) ousted Jan 14, 2011; Mubarak (Egypt, ~30 years) ousted
Feb 11, 2011. Average Egyptian income ~12% of American; life
expectancy 10 years shorter; 20% in dire poverty.

Egyptian protestors (Noha Hamed, Mosaab El Shami, Wael Khalil)
all focused on POLITICAL change first — corruption, oppression,
lack of political rights. ElBaradei tweet: "Tunisia: repression +
absence of social justice + denial of channels for peaceful
change = a ticking bomb."

Three conventional explanations rejected: (1) Geography —
Nogales disproves; (2) Culture — shared across borders;
(3) Ignorance — elites CHOOSE bad policies for self-enrichment.

THESIS: Egypt is poor because ruled by narrow elite organizing
society for own benefit (Mubarak's $70B fortune). Rich countries
became rich because citizens overthrew elites, created broadly
distributed political rights and economic opportunity. Britain
richer than Egypt because of 1688 revolution → politics →
economics → Industrial Revolution. Past Egyptian revolutions
failed because revolutionaries simply replaced old elites.

## CHAPTER 1: SO CLOSE AND YET SO DIFFERENT (pp. 7-56)

### Nogales Divided
Nogales, AZ: $30K household income, most teens in school, high
life expectancy, Medicare, law and order, democracy. Nogales,
Sonora: income ~1/3 of Arizona, high infant mortality, poor
roads, corruption, crime. Same geography, climate, ethnicity,
culture. Area unified until Gadsden Purchase of 1853. The BORDER
defines the difference — different institutions.

### Spanish Colonization Strategy
1516: Juan Díaz de Solís explored Río de la Plata, killed by
Charrúas. 1534: Pedro de Mendoza founded Buenos Aires — but
hunter-gatherers refused labor. Spaniards moved to Asunción
(sedentary Guaraní), adapted existing forced labor systems.
Buenos Aires abandoned, not resettled until 1580.

Cortés perfected the model: (1) capture indigenous leader,
(2) claim wealth, (3) coerce tribute/labor, (4) become new
elite. Nov 8, 1519: arrived Tenochtitlan, seized Moctezuma.
Sahagún's Florentine Codices: "They took it all" — gold melted
into bars. Military conquest completed 1521.

ENCOMIENDA system: grants of indigenous peoples to encomenderos.
Bartolomé de las Casas (Dominican priest, arrived Hispaniola
1502) documented horrors — Nicaragua settlers forced labor
"night and day without rest." In New Granada: King Bogotá
tortured to death for gold/emeralds.

Pizarro captured Inca emperor Atahualpa at Cajamarca Nov 15,
1532. Ransom: fill rooms with gold and silver. He did it.
Spanish strangled him July 1533. Temple of the Sun stripped.

MITA system: 1545 silver discovered at Cerro Rico, Potosí
(population 160,000 by 1650, larger than Lisbon/Venice).
Viceroy de Toledo's mita: one-seventh of males in ~200,000
sq mile catchment area worked mines. Abolished only 1825.
LEGACY VISIBLE TODAY: Acomayo (in mita area) much poorer than
nearby Calca (outside it) — inhabitants consume ~one-third less.

### Jamestown and English Colonization
English colonized North America as latecomers — "desirable"
parts already taken. 1607: Virginia Company founded Jamestown.
Captain John Smith realized Cortés/Pizarro model wouldn't work
— no gold, people couldn't be forced. "Victuals you must know
is all their wealth." Wahunsunacock refused subordination:
"If your King have sent me presents, I also am a King."

Virginia Company tried barracks/work gangs/execution — but
colonists could run away (population density: US at most 3/4
per sq mile vs central Mexico 400+ per sq mile). Starting 1618:
"headright system" (50 acres per settler). 1619: General
Assembly — "the start of democracy in the United States."

English elites repeatedly tried extractive institutions:
Lord Baltimore's manorial Maryland (1632); John Locke's
"Fundamental Constitutions of Carolina" with hereditary serfs
("leet-men") and aristocrats (landgraves/caziques) — "we may
avoid erecting a numerous democracy." ALL FAILED: impossible
to coerce settlers who had frontier options. By 1720s all
thirteen colonies had governor + assembly structures.

### Mexico's Independence vs. America's
US Constitution (1787) grew from 1619 General Assembly.
Mexico's independence was opposite: elites fled Cádiz
Constitution (1812) which threatened encomienda/forced labor.
Iturbide declared himself emperor 1821. Between 1824-1867:
FIFTY-TWO presidents, few constitutional. Santa Ana president
eleven times, lost Texas, presided over disastrous
Mexican-American War. Independence motivation was to PROTECT
colonial extractive institutions from reform.

### Banking: US vs Mexico
US (1818): 338 banks/$160M assets; by 1914: 27,864 banks/
$27.3B. Mexico (1910): only 42 banks, TWO controlled 60%.
Porfirio Díaz ruled ~34 years, violated property rights,
granted monopolies. Key contrast: US generals abided by
Constitution; Mexican strongmen only removed by force.

### Bill Gates vs Carlos Slim
Both world's richest — Gates through innovation (faced DOJ
antitrust); Slim through monopoly capture (Telmex privatized
1990, Slim's consortium won despite not highest bid, delayed
payment using Telmex's own dividends). Slim lost $454M lawsuit
in Dallas for violating US rules — "firms have to respect the
rules of the United States."

### Core Framework
Economic institutions shape incentives; political institutions
determine economic institutions. Those controlling politics
prefer extractive institutions. Rankings remarkably stable
going back 50-150 years. US citizen 7x as prosperous as
Mexican, 20x vs sub-Saharan Africa, 40x vs Mali/Ethiopia.

## CHAPTER 2: THEORIES THAT DON'T WORK (pp. 60-84)

### Geography Hypothesis — REFUTED
Montesquieu: tropical people lazy. Sachs: tropical diseases/
soils. DISPROVED by: Nogales, North/South Korea, East/West
Germany. Historical REVERSAL: Aztec/Inca civilizations RICHER
than North America at contact. Great tropical civilizations:
Angkor, Vijayanagara, Aksum, Indus Valley. Tropical diseases
are CONSEQUENCE of poverty, not cause.

Diamond's Guns Germs Steel: powerful for why Spanish conquered
Incas but can't explain modern inequality. After exposure to
all technologies, gap GREW (Spaniard now 6x richer than
Peruvian). Can't explain variation within continents. Maps show
pig/aurochs ancestors widely distributed across Eurasia.
Middle East led Neolithic Revolution but Ottoman expansion made
it poor — not geography.

### Culture Hypothesis — REFUTED
Weber's Protestant ethic: Catholic France quickly mimicked;
East Asian successes irrelevant to Christianity. Nogales: same
culture both sides. North/South Korea: common culture before
division. Kingdom of Kongo: Portuguese brought wheel/plow —
ALL adoption failed, but Kongolese quickly adopted GUNS for
slave trade. Incentives matter, not culture. "English" culture
can't explain why Sierra Leone differs from US. Chinese poverty
was Mao's policies, not Chinese culture.

### Ignorance Hypothesis — REFUTED
Ghana's Nkrumah: advised by Nobel laureate Arthur Lewis, built
absurd projects (footwear factory 500+ miles from tannery,
mango canning where mangos don't grow). He CHOSE bad policies
for political support. PM Kofi Busia knew devaluation was right
economics — signed IMF agreement Dec 27, 1971, result: rioting,
military coup. Deng Xiaoping's reforms were political revolution,
not better economic advice. "They get it wrong not by mistake
or ignorance but on purpose." Abba Lerner: "Economics has
gained the title Queen of the Social Sciences by choosing
solved political problems as its domain."

## CHAPTER 3: THE MAKING OF PROSPERITY AND POVERTY (pp. 85-113)

### The 38th Parallel
Hwang Pyŏng-Wŏn brothers: separated 1950, reunited 2000.
South = pharmacist with car/phone. North = doctor but no car,
no phone, threadbare borrowed coat, always nervous. South Korea:
living standards like Portugal/Spain. North: ~one-tenth, akin
to sub-Saharan Africa. Life expectancy TEN YEARS less. Satellite
image: North almost completely dark; South blazing with light.

### Inclusive vs Extractive Economic Institutions
INCLUSIVE: secure private property for MAJORITY, unbiased law,
public services, level playing field, new business entry.
EXTRACTIVE: designed to extract from one subset to benefit
another. Barbados 1680: 175 largest planters had property
rights but 39,000 of 60,000 population were slaves — NOT
inclusive.

### Inclusive vs Extractive Political Institutions
ABSOLUTIST: power narrow and unconstrained → extractive
economics. PLURALISTIC: power broadly distributed, constrained.
Two conditions for inclusive: (1) pluralistic (2) sufficiently
centralized state. Somalia: widely distributed power but NO
centralization = chaos. Weber: state = "monopoly of legitimate
violence."

### Feedback Loops
Extractive political → extractive economic → wealth to elites
→ reinforces dominance. Inclusive political → inclusive economic
→ equitable distribution → sustains inclusive political.
Combinations of extractive + inclusive generally UNSTABLE.

### Creative Destruction (Schumpeter)
Growth replaces old with new. FEAR of creative destruction
often at ROOT of opposition to inclusive institutions. On eve
of Industrial Revolution, European aristocracies' income from
land/monopolies was threatened by factories drawing resources
from countryside.

### Why Not Always Choose Prosperity?
Mobutu's Congo: "Les Grosses Legumes" became wealthy while
country declined. Mobutu built Concorde-capable airport at
Gbadolite, bought castles in Europe. Inclusive institutions
redistribute not just income but POWER — dictator may become
worse off.

## CHAPTER 4: SMALL DIFFERENCES AND CRITICAL JUNCTURES (pp. 114-141)

### The Black Death (1346)
From China via Silk Road, wiped out ~HALF of European
population. Boccaccio (Florence): "death occurred within three
days." CRITICAL JUNCTURE with divergent outcomes:

WESTERN EUROPE (England): Labor scarcity → peasants demanded
change. Eynsham Abbey: "scarcely two tenants remained."
Statute of Laborers (1351) tried to fix wages at 1347 levels —
DIDN'T WORK. 1381 Peasants' Revolt (Wat Tyler) captured most
of London. Feudal labor dwindled; inclusive labor market emerged.

EASTERN EUROPE: Same plague, same labor scarcity — but lords
prevailed (weaker towns, less organized peasants). SECOND
SERFDOM more intense: Korczyn, Poland: all work paid (1533) →
nearly half unpaid (1600). Mecklenberg: few days/year (1500) →
3 days/week (1600). By 1600: West in market economy; East =
coerced serfs.

### Small Differences That Matter
1588: England/France/Spain all absolutist. KEY difference:
English Parliament had taxation power; Estates-General didn't.
Spain: American gold funded Crown; trade monopolized. England:
Elizabeth couldn't monopolize Atlantic trade → wealthy merchants
with few Crown links → demanded institutional changes → critical
role in Civil War and Glorious Revolution. France: Fronde
(1648-52) but opponents less wealthy/numerous.

### Contingency of History
1588 Armada: bad weather + Sidonia's mistakes → English won
against all odds → Atlantic opened. WITHOUT this unlikely
victory, Glorious Revolution "would never have got moving."
Michels's "iron law of oligarchy": revolutions often replace
one tyranny with another. Botswana = rare post-colonial
exception under Seretse Khama.

### Understanding the Lay of the Land
Settler colonies: IR spread rapidly. French Revolution →
Western Europe converged with England. Latin America: Spanish
extractive institutions endured. Africa: least ready — slave
trade made institutions MORE extractive; colonial legacy worse
in 1960s than at start. China: Ming emperors turned from oceans
fearing creative destruction. Japan vs China: Tokugawa's
tenuous hold → 1853 Perry → Meiji Restoration → growth.
Ottoman Empire: 500 years absolutist, no private property in
land, tax farming (collectors took 1/2 to 2/3).

## CHAPTER 5: GROWTH UNDER EXTRACTIVE INSTITUTIONS (pp. 142-170)

### The Soviet Union
Lincoln Steffens after visiting Moscow: "I've seen the future,
and it works." Stalin collectivized agriculture — abolished
private property, herded people into collective farms.
Probably SIX MILLION died of famine, hundreds of thousands
murdered/banished. Between 1928-1960: national income grew
6%/year — probably fastest spurt in history up to then. Growth
was NOT technological change but REALLOCATING LABOR from
agriculture to industry + capital accumulation.

Samuelson's textbook predicted Soviet dominance: 1961 edition
said USSR would overtake US by 1984 or 1997. By the 1970s
growth had all but stopped. TWO REASONS: (1) lack of incentives,
(2) elites resist creative destruction.

### Gosplan and Perverse Incentives
Plans labeled "draft" — only ONE ever labeled "final." Stalin:
plans were "just the beginning." Gosplan's role: provide
information to monitor friends and enemies. Perverse incentives:
plan in TONS → sheet too heavy; plan in AREA → sheet too thin;
chandelier plan in tons → too heavy to hang. Bonuses created
DISINCENTIVES: innovation risked missing targets; output targets
ratcheted up if you exceeded them.

1937 census showed 162M (less than Stalin's announced 180M) —
reflected famines/purges. Stalin had organizers ARRESTED and
SHOT. Next census (1939) "found" 171M.

### Coercion as Substitute
Between 1940-1955: 36 MILLION people found guilty of labor
offenses (~one-third of adult population). 15 million to prison.
250,000 shot. Plus 2.5 million in gulags. "You can move someone
to a factory, but you cannot force people to think and have
good ideas by threatening to shoot them."

### Fundamental Problem
Soviet rulers would have had to abandon extractive economics,
but this would jeopardize political power. When Gorbachev
started moving away after 1987, Communist Party crumbled.

### Bushong vs Lele: The Kuba Kingdom
Kasai River divides Lele (poor, subsistence, inferior
technology, constant conflict) from Bushong (rich, market
exchange, sophisticated mixed farming with 2-3 maize harvests
vs Lele's one). Anthropologist Mary Douglas: "Everything the
Lele have or can do, the Bushong have more and can do better."
NOT geography or culture — Lele knew about Bushong tools.

Around 1620: King SHYAAM forged the Kuba Kingdom — built state,
bureaucracy, legal system, police, even trial by jury. Food
per capita DOUBLED. But growth was LIMITED: no creative
destruction, situation unchanged when Belgians arrived late
19th century.

### The Neolithic Revolution
~9500 BC: first domestic plants at Jericho/Tell Aswad.
Natufians were SEDENTARY before farming (gazelle teeth evidence:
killed in all seasons). Archaeological evidence shows HIERARCHY
BEFORE FARMING — graves with obsidian/shells vs graves without.
AUTHORS' ARGUMENT: institutional changes (political hierarchy)
CAUSED sedentary life and farming, not the reverse — challenging
Diamond's thesis. Yir Yoront aboriginals given steel axes →
used them for MORE SLEEPING, not more production.

### The Maya: Extractive Growth's Dark End
Maya city-states illustrate TWO limits: (1) no technological
progress, (2) political instability. Classic Era (AD 250-900):
~50 city-states, writing system, Long Count calendar. Copán:
dynasty from AD 426 (K'inich Yax K'uk' Mo') for 400 years.
Population peaked at 28,000 (~AD 750, larger than contemporary
London/Paris), fell to ~600 by AD 1200. Dated monuments
collapse from 40/year at peak to ZERO by 10th century.

Wealth led to CONSTANT WARFARE (Venus = celestial patron of
war, "star wars" glyphs). Overthrow of k'uhul ajaw model →
royal courts stopped, monuments abandoned, economy contracted.
Extractive institutions create incentives for OTHERS to fight
to become extractors — instability is INHERENT.

KEY WARNING: China under CCP is another example of extractive
growth, unlikely to sustain without political transformation.

## CHAPTER 6: DRIFTING APART (pp. 171-202)

### Venice: From Powerhouse to Museum
Medieval Venice possibly RICHEST PLACE in the world. Population:
45,000 (1050) → 70,000 (1200) → 110,000 (1330, as big as
Paris, 3x London). Key innovation: the COMMENDA (rudimentary
joint stock company). New names in government documents: 69%,
81%, 65% in AD 960/971/982 — massive upward social mobility.

Political reforms: Ducal Council (after 1032), Great Council
(after 1171), independent magistrates, courts, bankruptcy laws,
beginnings of modern banking.

LA SERRATA ("The Closure"), 1297: Great Council sealed to
outsiders, incumbents became hereditary aristocracy. 1315:
Libro d'Oro (Gold Book) registered nobility. Then ECONOMIC
Serrata: BANNED the commenda, nationalized trade (1314),
charged high taxes for private trade (1324). Long-distance
trade became preserve of nobility.

Decline: population shrank to 100,000 by 1500. Between
1650-1800: Venice's population CONTRACTED while Europe's
expanded. "Instead of pioneering trade routes, Venetians
make pizza and blow colored glass for foreigners."

### Rome: Republic to Empire
Roman Republic (~510 BC): cleverly designed inclusive
institutions — magistrates elected for a year, checks and
balances. Plebeians had own assembly, tribune with veto power,
won rights through "secession" (strike/withdrawal).

Archaeological evidence of growth: Mediterranean shipwrecks
peaked at 180 around Christ's birth. Monte Testaccio: ~53
MILLION discarded amphorae. Greenland ice cores: atmospheric
lead peaked 1st century AD, not matched until 13th century.

Tiberius Gracchus (133 BC): proposed land reform (300-acre limit
on public land). Senate blocked implementation, starved
commission of funds. Tiberius clubbed to death by senators.
Brother Gaius also murdered. Republic fell to Julius Caesar
(49 BC, crossed the Rubicon). Augustus created Empire.

Decline paralleled Maya: increasingly extractive. Emperor
Tiberius abolished Plebeian Assembly. Citizens got free wheat
and circuses instead of political voice. Coloni (semi-serfs):
Constantine (AD 332) allowed landlords to chain them.
Shipwrecks fell from 180 to 20 by AD 500. Trade may not have
recovered to Roman levels until 19th century.

EMPERORS BLOCKED INNOVATION: Tiberius killed inventor of
unbreakable glass "lest gold be reduced to the value of mud."
Vespasian refused labor-saving column transporter: "How will I
feed the populace?" Slavery meant those doing production had no
incentive to innovate — economies based on repression are
NOTORIOUSLY NONINNOVATIVE.

### Vindolanda and England's Collapse
Roman centurion Candidus (AD 103): letters reveal monetary
economy, financial services, roads, literacy, postal service.
After AD 411 Romans withdrew. By AD 450: money vanished, urban
areas abandoned, people FORGOT mortar, only crude handmade
pottery. "Nobody wrote from Vindolanda anymore." England was
historical BACKWATER — while Egyptians built pyramids, English
built Stonehenge (not large enough to house one of Khufu's
ceremonial boats).

### Feudalism's Consequences
Fall of Rome → feudal society. Two important by-products:
(1) SLAVERY DISAPPEARED from Europe — entire population reduced
to serfdom instead; (2) power vacuum allowed INDEPENDENT CITIES
to flourish. After Black Death, serfdom crumbled in West →
stage set for pluralistic society without slaves.

### Ethiopia: Parallel Feudalism
Kingdom of Aksum (400 BC): traded with India/Arabia/Rome,
coined money, comparable technology. Declined like Rome when
Arabs cut trade routes (7th century). Developed GULT system
(feudal land grants) — holders extracted 1/2 to 3/4 of
agricultural output (even more extractive than English
feudalism at ~1/2). But Ethiopia remained isolated, cut off
from processes that changed Europe — absolutism unchallenged.

### Key Chapter Argument
No simple cumulative process: (1) moves toward inclusion CAN BE
REVERSED (Venice); (2) small institutional differences are
EPHEMERAL. England — where decisive inclusive institutions
emerged — was a backwater where Roman hold was weakest. History
matters through INSTITUTIONAL DRIFT creating small differences
amplified by critical junctures.

## CHAPTER 7: THE TURNING POINT (pp. 203-260)

### William Lee's Stocking Frame (1589)
Lee mechanized textile production. Elizabeth I refused patent:
"Consider what the invention could do to my poor subjects. It
would assuredly bring to them ruin." James I also refused.
Both feared POLITICAL DESTABILIZATION, not workers' welfare.
Fear of creative destruction is why no sustained growth between
Neolithic and Industrial Revolutions.

Living standards barely changed: Diocletian's Edict on Maximum
Prices (AD 301) shows wages almost identical to 17th-century
Italian unskilled workers. Life expectancy from Natufian Abu
Hureyra to Ancient Rome to 17th-century England was similar.

### Magna Carta to Parliament
1215: Barons forced King John to sign Magna Carta at Runnymede
— king must CONSULT to raise taxes. Clause 61: 25 barons could
seize castles if king violated charter. John got Pope to annul
it, but its influence remained. First elected Parliament 1265.
Two key features: (1) represented broad set of interests
including gentry; (2) members consistently opposed monarchy.

### Tudor Centralization
Henry VII disarmed aristocracy after 1485. Henry VIII via
Thomas Cromwell: "revolution in government" — nascent
bureaucratic state. Dissolution of Monasteries removed Church
power. Centralization created demand for Parliament as
counterweight. Peasants' Revolt of 1381 and subsequent
insurrections redistributed power from elite to people.

### Stuart Monopolies and Conflict
By 1621: SEVEN HUNDRED monopolies covering nearly everything —
"a man lived in a house built with monopoly bricks... heated by
monopoly coal... ate monopoly butter, monopoly currants,
monopoly red herrings, monopoly salmon, and monopoly lobsters."
Statute of Monopolies (1623) prohibited new domestic monopolies.

Charles I declined to call Parliament after 1629, levied forced
loans, sold overseas monopolies, extended "ship money" inland.
1640: forced to call Parliament (couldn't fund Scottish war).
Short Parliament sat 3 weeks. Long Parliament refused to
dissolve. Civil War 1642: Birmingham (weak monopolies) supported
Parliament; Shrewsbury merchants (Crown-protected monopolies)
supported king. Cromwell's Roundheads won. Charles executed 1649.

### The Glorious Revolution (1688)
Cromwell's dictatorship → monarchy restored 1660 → Charles II
and James II tried absolutism again. 1688: Parliament invited
William of Orange. Bill of Rights established: (1) Parliament
determines succession; (2) monarch can't suspend laws;
(3) no taxation without consent; (4) no standing army without
consent. Authority switched to Parliament — end of absolutism.

### Economic Transformation After 1688
Property rights strengthened. Hearth tax abolished 1689,
replaced by land tax. Bank of England created 1694 → financial
revolution. C. Hoare's bank records (1702-1724): two-thirds of
biggest borrowers were merchants/businessmen, not aristocrats.

Royal African Company monopoly (slave trade, governed by
James II) challenged: 135 petitions from interlopers demanding
free trade vs Company's counterpetitions. 8,000 signatures vs
2,500. Monopoly ABOLISHED 1698. Petitioning was crucial
mechanism — "anybody could petition Parliament, and people did."

State capacity expanded: expenditures reached ~10% of national
income. Excise tax bureaucracy grew from 1,211 (1690) to 4,800
(1780). Supervisor Cowperthwaite (1710): traveled 290 miles,
visited 263 victualers, 71 maltsters, took 81 measurements —
surveillance exceeding what most poor countries achieve today.

### Transportation and Property Revolution
Baldwyn family invested £6,000 in river Salwerpe navigation
(1662). When Parliament tried to transfer rights to Earl of
Shrewsbury (1693), Baldwyn petitioned: "dangerous consequence
to take away any person's right purchased under an act of
Parliament." Rights upheld — property much more secure after
1688. Parliament passed hundreds of acts reorganizing land
property rights, enabling canal/road investment.

### The Industrial Revolution
By 1760: jump in patented inventions. James Watt's separate
condenser + "sun and planets" gear. Papin's steamboat (1705)
DESTROYED by boatmen's guild at Münden — Leibniz's petition
for safe passage rebuffed. Papin died a pauper in unmarked
grave. In post-1688 England, this couldn't happen.

Henry Cort (1780s): wrought iron techniques. Abraham Darby:
coal smelting (1709). Spinning: 50,000 hours by hand for 100
lbs cotton → Arkwright's water frame 300 hours → self-acting
mule 135 hours. Arkwright built one of world's first factories
at Cromford (1771), employed 600. Hargreaves's spinning jenny
(1764), Crompton's mule (1779), Cartwright's power loom (1785).
English exports doubled 1780-1800.

### New Men
James Brindley (canal engineer): born rural Derbyshire,
millwright. George Stephenson (The Rocket): son of illiterate
parents, started as coal mine engineman. Only one-fifth of
leading cotton industrialists had previous manufacturing
experience. Factory system was entirely new — newcomers were
needed.

### Peterloo and Reform
Aug 16, 1819: 60,000 protestors at St. Peter's Fields,
Manchester — "No Corn Laws," "Universal Suffrage." Cavalry
charged: 11 dead, ~600 wounded. BUT long-run repression was
not viable under inclusive institutions. First Reform Act 1832:
enfranchised Birmingham/Leeds/Manchester/Sheffield. Corn Laws
repealed 1846.

### Why England?
Glorious Revolution was shaped by: (1) political conflict
between absolutism and opponents; (2) emergence of broad
coalition for constitutional rule, not just replacing one
elite with another; (3) institutional drift after Black Death;
(4) Atlantic trade creating new merchant class (by 1686:
702 London merchants exporting to Caribbean, 1,283 importing;
ports like Bristol/Liverpool similarly vibrant). Same forces
at work in France/Spain/Portugal but kings there more able to
monopolize trade.

## CHAPTER 8: NOT ON OUR TURF: BARRIERS TO DEVELOPMENT

### Somalia and the Absence of State (pp. 260-266)
CORE ARGUMENT: Absolutism was not the ONLY barrier to inclusive
institutions — many parts of the world, especially Africa,
lacked even a minimal state for law and order, a prerequisite
for a modern economy.

SOMALIA: Organized into six clan families (Dir, Darod, Isaq,
Hawiye + Digil and Rahanweyn). Clans contain diya-paying groups
who pay/collect "blood wealth" as compensation for murder.
Leaders had no real power; political power widely dispersed
through informal councils. No written law, no police — Sharia
+ informal heer codes governed obligations.

SPECIFIC: Hassan Ugaas lineage heer recorded March 8, 1950 —
~1,500 men; 20 camels for murder by outsider, 33⅓ for wounding.
BLOOD FEUDING EXAMPLE (1955): Habar Tol Ja'lo and Habar Yuunis
clans near Domberelly — wounded man → retaliation → all-out war:
13 Yuunis and 26 Tol Ja'lo killed in 48 hours. War continued
another year.

### Why Political Centralization Failed
Centralizing meant some clans subject to others — they rejected
this. Balance of military power made centralization impossible.

KINGDOM OF TAQALI (Nuba Hills, Sudan): Kings had access to
Arabic writing but only used it for external diplomacy. Citizens
resisted writing fearing state would use it to claim land and
impose systematic taxation. Rulers also preferred oral interaction
— maximum discretion.

SYNTHESIS: Nations under extractive institutions (Spain, Ethiopia)
choked incentives. Austria-Hungary, Russia, Ottoman Empire, China
took EXPLICIT STEPS to block industry. But societies lacking even
centralization (Somalia, Afghanistan, Haiti) were equally
disadvantaged.

## CHAPTER 9: REVERSING DEVELOPMENT

### Spice and Genocide — The Moluccas (pp. 289-295)
MOLUCCAN ARCHIPELAGO: only producers of cloves, mace, nutmeg.
Nutmeg/mace grew ONLY in Banda Islands. VOC (Dutch East India
Company) founded 1602 — had own army, power to wage war.

Governor JAN PIETERSZOON COEN (1621): sailed to Banda and
MASSACRED almost entire population (~15,000 people). Islands
divided into 68 parcels given to Dutchmen with slaves.
RESULT: Dutch reduced world supply of spices ~60%, nutmeg
price doubled.

Even polities not directly colonized turned inward: Banten
cut down pepper trees; Maguindanao ruler "forbidden the
continued planting of pepper." De-urbanization, population
decline — Burma moved capital from coast to inland Ava (1635).
Southeast Asian states became MORE absolutist.

### Slavery and Africa (pp. 295-303)
KEY STATISTICS: 16th century ~300,000 slaves traded; 17th
~1,350,000; 18th ~6,000,000 across Atlantic. TOTAL: well over
10 million. In Angola, Benin, Ghana, Togo: cumulative exports
exceeded entire 1400 population.

GUNS FOR SLAVES: By 1730, ~180,000 guns imported/year along
West African coast. British alone sold 283,000-394,000 guns/year
(1750-early 19th c.), 22,000 tons of gunpowder.

TWO adverse processes: (1) polities became MORE absolutist,
organized around enslaving; (2) warring destroyed whatever
legitimate state authority existed. Francis Moore (1730s):
"Since this slave trade has been us'd, all punishments are
changed into slavery."

ORACLE OF AROCHUKWA: Priests in league with Aro slavers —
people "swallowed" by oracle were led through cave to waiting
European ships. OYO: 80-90% of slaves sold were war captives.

DEMOGRAPHIC IMPACT (Patrick Manning): population should have
been 46-53 million by 1850 — was about ONE-HALF. Not only 8
million exported but millions killed by internal warfare.

### Abolition Failed to End Slavery
1807: British banned slave trade; 1834: abolished slavery.
BUT: slavery became MORE prevalent WITHIN Africa. Asante:
slaves on plantations around Kumase. In West African kingdoms:
well over HALF of population were slaves. French records: 30%
enslaved in western Sudan by 1900. Sierra Leone: slavery
abolished only 1928. Liberia: one-quarter coerced as late as
1960s.

### South Africa: Creating a Dual Economy (pp. 303-315)
Sir Arthur Lewis (1955, Nobel 1979): "dual economy" model.
BUT: Transkei's backwardness was NOT natural — CREATED BY STATE
to produce cheap labor.

AFRICAN DYNAMISM BEFORE REVERSAL: Xhosa farmers readily adopted
plows, wagons, irrigation. By 1882: ~8,000 African farmers had
bought 90,000 acres. Stephen Sonjica: self-made farmer who
secretly saved £80. 1869: prosperous African farmers donated
money for Lancashire Cotton Relief Fund — Africans helping
poor English workers!

DESTRUCTION: George Albu (chairman, Association of Mines, 1897):
"Why should a nigger be allowed to do nothing?" NATIVES LAND
ACT OF 1913: 87% of land to Europeans (~20% of population),
13% to Africans. G. Findlay (1940): "Tribal tenure is a
guarantee that the land will never be properly worked... Cheap
labour must have a cheap breeding place."

Miners' wages FELL 30% (1911-1921); in 1961 still 12% LOWER
than 1911. Colour bar banned Africans from ALL skilled mining
jobs. Verwoerd (1954): "There is no place for him in the
European community above the level of certain forms of labour."
Dual economy ended 1994 through BLACK POLITICAL MOBILIZATION
after Soweto (1976).

## CHAPTER 10: THE DIFFUSION OF PROSPERITY

### Australia: From Penal Colony to Democracy (pp. 319-328)
January 1788: fleet under Captain Arthur Phillip at Sydney Cove.
Henry and Susannah Cable: married convicts who sued Captain
Sinclair for stolen goods — first civil case in Australia.
Under British law convicts couldn't own property, but Australian
courts let them. Henry Cable by 1809: owned 9 farms (~470
acres), shops, houses, hotel, a ship.

SQUATTOCRACY vs. EX-CONVICTS: John Macarthur (richest man,
sheep magnate) vs. William Wentworth (writer, explorer, led
ex-convict cause via The Australian newspaper). Victoria and
Tasmania: first places to introduce effective SECRET BALLOT —
still called "the Australian ballot." By 1850s: adult white
male suffrage.

### French Revolution and Its Export (pp. 329-340)
August 4, 1789: National Constituent Assembly abolished feudalism,
serfdom, tax privileges, guild restrictions. Article 11: "All
citizens, without distinction of birth, are eligible to any
office or dignity."

FRANKFURT'S JUDENGASSE: No more than 500 Jewish families,
confined to quarter-mile walled ghetto 12 feet wide. Max 12
marriages/year, both over 25. Mayer Amschel Rothschild couldn't
open business outside. 1791: French emancipated Jewry; 1796
bombardment demolished half of Judengasse. Full emancipation
1811 by Napoleon-appointed Grand Duke. Rothschild sons built
largest bank in 19th-century Europe.

Napoleon imposed Code Napoleon across conquered territories:
equality before law, abolition of feudalism/guilds. By mid-19th
century: industrialization underway in almost all French-conquered
areas; unconquered Austria-Hungary, Russia, Poland, Spain
remained stagnant.

### Meiji Restoration — Japan (pp. 339-344)
Satcho Alliance (Satsuma + Chōshū): January 3, 1868 declared
Meiji Restoration. REFORMS: Feudalism abolished 1869; equality
before law; samurai class abolished; property rights; first
railway Tokyo-Yokohama; by 1890 first Asian written constitution.

WHY JAPAN SUCCEEDED, CHINA DIDN'T: China had centralized
bureaucratic empire — opposition NOT institutionalized. Japan's
domains maintained independence, could overthrow shogun through
coalition. China only overthrew emperor in 1911, then worsened
under Mao.

## CHAPTER 11: THE VIRTUOUS CIRCLE

### The Black Act and Rule of Law (pp. 349-358)
1722: "Blacks" (men with blackened faces) across southern England
— killing deer, burning haystacks. William Cadogan bought ~1,000
acres near Windsor, evicted people. May 1723: Black Act created
50 new capital offenses.

John Huntridge trial (1724): jury of major landowners found him
INNOCENT despite Walpole's prosecution — upheld rule of law.
E.P. Thompson: rulers were "prisoners of their own rhetoric;
they played games of power according to rules which suited them,
but they could not break those rules or the whole game would be
thrown away."

### Four Mechanisms of Virtuous Circle
1. Pluralism + rule of law: constrains power
2. Inclusive economic → inclusive political: equitable income
   empowers broader segments
3. Independent media: helps organize against threats
4. Positive feedback: inclusive economy → urbanization, education,
   innovation → clamping down destroys these gains

### The Slow March of Democracy (pp. 359-364)
Luddite Riots (1811-16), Peterloo (1819), Swing Riots (1830) →
consensus that only reform prevented revolution. Earl Grey:
"My object is not to favour, but to put an end to such hopes...
reforming to preserve and not to overthrow." Chartist Stephens:
"universal suffrage... is a knife and fork question."

First Reform Act (1832): 8% → 16%. Second (1867): working-class
majority. Third (1884): 60% of adult males. Women fully equal
1928. Corn Laws repealed 1846; civil service opened 1871;
1906-14 Liberal reforms. Education Act 1870: 40% of 10-year-olds
→ 100% by 1900.

### Busting Trusts — America (pp. 365-372)
Rockefeller's Standard Oil: by 1890 controlled 88% of refined
oil. J.P. Morgan + Carnegie: U.S. Steel (1901) — first $1B corp.
People's Party got 8.5% in 1892. Sherman Antitrust Act (1890);
Roosevelt broke Northern Securities (1902); Taft broke Standard
Oil (1911); Wilson created FTC + Federal Reserve (1913-14).

Wilson: "If monopoly persists, monopoly will always sit at the
helm of government." MUCKRAKERS: Ida Tarbell's History of
Standard Oil, Louis Brandeis's Other People's Money → Seventeenth
Amendment (direct Senate elections, 1913).

### FDR's Court-Packing Failure (pp. 372-380)
1937: FDR proposed adding up to 6 justices. Only ~40% support.
Senate voted 70-20 to strip court-packing elements.

CONTRAST — ARGENTINA: Perón (1946) impeached 4 of 5 justices.
Menem (1990) expanded court from 5 to 9. Every regime picks own
court. KEY: In US, pluralistic institutions created broad segments
willing to defend Court's independence.

## CHAPTER 12: THE VICIOUS CIRCLE

### Sierra Leone: Extractive Continuity (pp. 382-393)
Independence 1961 → Siaka Stevens (APC) won 1967 → pulled up
railway to Mendeland (like Tsar Nicholas I fearing railways).
Marketing boards: farmers sometimes received as low as 10% of
world price — 90% extracted. Stevens emasculated army, created
paramilitaries: ISU ("I Shoot U"), SSD ("Siaka Stevens's Dogs").
Sam Bangura (central bank governor, 1980): criticized Stevens →
murdered, thrown from top floor onto Siaka Stevens Street.

DIAMONDS: De Beers got monopoly, created private army larger
than colonial government. Stevens nationalized 1970.
CONTRAST WITH AUSTRALIA: Gold (1851), also alluvial. Inclusive
model won — anyone could buy mining license.

### Guatemala: 500 Years of Same Families (pp. 393-400)
Jan 14, 1993: President, Finance Minister, Development Minister
ALL descended from 16th-century conquistadors. Marta Casaús Arzú:
22 core + 26 connected families have controlled Guatemala since
1531. Encomienda → repartimiento → Consulado de Comercio.

Coffee boom → 1871 "Liberal" coup — same families. Decree 177
(1877): repartimiento institutionalized. Libreta, vagrancy laws.
Jorge Ubico (1931-1944): banned words "obreros," "sindicatos,"
"huelgas." Form of extraction changed; extractive nature and
elite identity persisted.

### From Slavery to Jim Crow — U.S. South (pp. 399-407)
Civil War → slavery abolished → BUT Jim Crow replaced it.
Planters "lost the war but would win the peace." Jonathan Wiener:
of 236 planter elite (1850), 101 maintained position in 1870.
Alabama Black Code (1865): vagrancy + anti-enticement laws.
Poll taxes + literacy tests. 1900: 13.5% South urbanized vs
60% Northeast. Crumbled only after WWII + civil rights movement.

### The Iron Law of Oligarchy (pp. 407-414)
Michels's concept: new groups reproduce same extractive system.
ETHIOPIA: Haile Selassie → 1974 Derg coup → Mengistu. By 1978:
Mengistu in gilded armchair on Selassie's throne. "We were
supposed to have a revolution of equality; now he had become
the new Emperor." Used FAMINE as political weapon.

THREE FORMS: (1) Same elite persists (Guatemala, U.S. South);
(2) Iron law of oligarchy (Ethiopia, Sierra Leone, Congo);
(3) Extractive institutions breed infighting → civil wars.

## CHAPTER 13: WHY NATIONS FAIL TODAY

### Zimbabwe (pp. 418-420)
Jan 2000 lottery: winning ticket drawn for President Mugabe.
Prize: Z$100,000 (~5x per capita income). By 2008: per capita
income HALF of 1980 level. Unemployment: 94%. Cholera: 98,741
cases. Mugabe eliminated opponents (up to 20,000 killed in
Matabeleland). After 2000: land occupations → agricultural
collapse → hyperinflation → dollar abandoned Jan 2009.

### Sierra Leone Civil War (pp. 423-428)
March 23, 1991: Foday Sankoh crossed from Liberia with RUF.
Stevens's quote: "The cow eats where it is tethered." "Sobels"
= soldiers turned rebels. RUF recruited children, amputated
hands/ears. War ended 2001: ~80,000 dead. Sierra Leone was
already a failed state before RUF.

### Colombia (pp. 428-434)
FARC kidnapping → Castaño brothers founded paramilitaries → AUC
(30,000 men by 2001). Pact of Santa Fé de Ralito: ~1/3 of
congressmen owed 2002 election to paramilitaries. Mancuso:
"35 percent of the Congress was elected in areas where there
were Self-Defense groups." ~4.5 million internally displaced.

### Argentina's Decline (pp. 434-438)
El Corralito (Dec 2001): froze bank accounts. Kuznets: four
types of countries — developed, undeveloped, Japan, and Argentina.
Pre-1914: ~50 years of growth under extractive institutions
(commodity boom) — not sustainable. Power concentrated in
Perónist Party; Supreme Court packed/replaced by every regime.

### North Korea and Uzbekistan (pp. 438-446)
Nov 2009: North Korea currency reform wiped out citizens' wealth.
Kim Jong-Il's annual cognac budget: $800,000. UZBEKISTAN: Cotton
= 45% of exports. 2.7 million children emptied from schools for
harvest. Children paid ~$0.03/kg vs. world price ~$1.40/kg.
Karimov: 2005 Andijon massacre (~750 killed).

### Egypt: Crony Capitalism (pp. 447-449)
Ahmed Ezz: 70% of Egypt's steel, close to Gamal Mubarak.
Privatization: state monopolies → private monopolies enriching
32 connected "whale" families. Mubarak planned dynastic
succession; cut short by Arab Spring 2011.

## CHAPTER 14: BREAKING THE MOLD

### Botswana's Origins (pp. 455-464)
September 1895: Three Tswana chiefs (Khama, Bathoen, Sebele)
sailed to Britain to save states from Cecil Rhodes's BSAC.
Nationwide tour; Rhodes cabled: "I do object to being beaten
by three canting natives."

TSWANA INSTITUTIONS: Kgotla (general assembly) — anyone could
speak, assembly could overrule chief. Proverb: kgosi ke kgosi
ka morafe — "The king is king by the grace of the people."

At independence (1966): 12 km paved roads, 22 university
graduates — one of poorest countries in world. Now: highest per
capita income in sub-Saharan Africa. Seretse Khama changed law
before diamond announcement: all subsoil mineral rights vested
in NATION, not tribe. Diamond revenues → state building.

### Civil Rights — Breaking the Southern Vicious Circle (pp. 465-472)
Dec 1, 1955: Rosa Parks arrested. Bus boycott until Dec 20, 1956.
Louisiana literacy test: white applicant judged literate after
writing "FRDUM FOOF SPETGH." Ole Miss crisis (1962): 2,500
rioters → 20,000 troops. Thurmond filibustered 1957 Civil Rights
Act for 24 hours 18 minutes.

Voting Rights Act 1965: Mississippi black registration ~5% (1960)
→ 50% (1970). Southern per capita income: ~50% of U.S. average
(1940) → gap basically vanished by 1990. KEY: Being part of
United States was decisive — South subject to federal law.

### China's Rebirth (pp. 472-479)
Mao's Great Leap Forward (1958): 20-40 million deaths. Deng:
"No matter whether the cat is black or white, if it catches mice,
it's a good cat." Purged in Cultural Revolution, exiled to
tractor factory.

Mao died September 1976 → three factions. Nov-Dec 1978: Third
Plenum — focus shifted to economic modernization. 1983: household
responsibility system universal → 1984 grain output 1/3 higher.
China broke mold economically BUT political institutions remained
extractive — highly contingent.

## CHAPTER 15: UNDERSTANDING PROSPERITY AND POVERTY

### The Theory Summarized (pp. 480-484)
Two levels: (1) institutional interpretation (extractive vs.
inclusive); (2) how history shaped trajectories (institutional
drift + critical junctures). Growth under extractive institutions
possible but NOT sustained. Three major critical junctures:
Black Death, Atlantic trade, Industrial Revolution.

### Authoritarian Growth — The China Mirage (pp. 490-494)
Dai Guofang: started steel company competing with state firms →
arrested 2004, jailed 5 years. Chen Yun: "bird in a cage" —
enlarge but never unlock. Richard McGregor: red phone on desk
of every major state company CEO. Chinese growth resembles
Soviet growth (1950s-60s): catch-up under extractive institutions.
Liu Xiaobo: imprisoned, awarded Nobel Peace Prize.

### Critique of Modernization Theory (pp. 495-498)
Lipset: as societies grow, they become democratic. WRONG:
causation runs from institutions to growth. Germany/Japan were
rich/educated yet succumbed to Nazism/militarism. Argentina:
one of richest in 19th century → democracy no more successful.

### You Can't Engineer Prosperity (pp. 499-507)
Zimbabwe declared central bank independent 1995. Inflation:
~20% → 230 MILLION% (2008). Central bank governor knew what
happened to Sam Bangura — compliance was "prudent choice for
personal health."

Seva Mandir NGO: nurse attendance incentives in Udaipur. Within
a year: health administration deliberately undermined scheme.
Afghan village shelter: each layer took ~20% → only oversized
wooden beams arrived (used as firewood). Studies: only 10-20%
of aid reaches target.

### Empowerment: Brazil's Workers' Party (pp. 508-514)
May 12, 1978: Scânia truck factory strike — led by Lula da Silva,
age 33. Workers' Party launched October 1979. Porto Alegre:
"participatory budgeting." Lula elected president 2002. Poverty
fell from 45% to 30%; inequality fell sharply.

KEY: Brazil's rise was NOT engineered by economists or foreign
aid — consequence of groups building inclusive institutions from
below. CONTRAST VENEZUELA: democracy since 1958 but without
grassroots empowerment → Chávez → still extractive.

### What Facilitates Empowerment
Three factors: (1) centralized order; (2) preexisting pluralistic
institutions; (3) civil society institutions.

MEDIA: Fujimori/Montesinos bribery records — Supreme Court judge
cost $5,000-$10,000/month; TV stations cost MILLIONS. General
Bello: "If we do not control the television we do not do anything."
Chinese commentator: "Three principles: Party controls armed
forces; Party controls cadres; Party controls the news."

Meaningful change requires broad mobilization to TRANSFORM
extractive into inclusive institutions — not seize them.
