# More Money Than God — First Half Notes (Compacted)

## Introduction: The Alpha Game

### The Evolution from Jones to Asness

- **Alfred Winslow Jones** created the first hedge fund at age 48 in late 1940s—tramp steamer worker, Marxist Workers School in Berlin, anti-Nazi operative, married twice, befriended Dorothy Parker and Ernest Hemingway
- Raised **$100,000 in 1949** to create a "hedged fund" generating extraordinary profits through 1950s-60s
- **Clifford Asness** represents modern hedge fund manager: PhD in quantitative finance from University of Chicago, Goldman Sachs veteran, launched at age 31
- Raised **$1 billion** for startup, told *New York Times* "it doesn't suck" to be worth millions
- By 2007, **AQR Capital Management** ran **$38 billion** under Asness's leadership

### Jones's Four Pillars of Innovation

1. **Performance fees**: 20% of profits, incentivizing lieutenants
2. **Regulatory flexibility**: avoided red tape to shift methods as markets changed
3. **Long/short structure**: balanced promising shares with short selling of unpromising ones
4. **Leverage**: hedging allowed safe use of borrowed money to magnify bets

> "Jones had invented a platform for strategies more complex than he himself could dream of."

### The Wealth Explosion

- **J. Pierpont Morgan** died 1913 with **$1.4 billion** fortune (today's dollars)
- By 2000s, top hedge-fund managers surpassed Morgan's wealth in just couple years of trading
- **2006 earnings**: **Lloyd Blankfein** (Goldman CEO) earned $54 million, but bottom earner on *Alpha* top 25 made **$240 million**
- Top three hedge-fund moguls each earned over **$1 billion** that year
- Six Connecticut hedge-fund managers pocketed combined **$2.15 billion** in 2006

### The New Elite's Lifestyle

- **Ken Griffin** (Citadel): bought $50 million Bombardier Express jet with crib for two-year-old
- **Louis Bacon** (Moore Capital): acquired island in Great Peconic Bay, monitored mud turtle mating with transmitters
- **Steven Cohen** (SAC Capital): estate with basketball court, indoor pool, skating rink, two-acre golf course, van Gogh and Pollock paintings, Keith Haring sculpture

### Hedge Funds and Social Impact

- **George Soros**: charities fostered independent voices in ex-communist nations, funded laissez-faire economics rethink
- **Paul Tudor Jones**: created **Robin Hood**, first "venture philanthropies" fighting NYC poverty with demanding benchmarks
- **Bruce Kovner**: godfather of neoconservative movement, chaired American Enterprise Institute
- **Michael Steinhardt**: bankrolled efforts to create new secular Judaism

### The Efficient Market Debate

- **1960s-1980s academic consensus**: markets are efficient, prices follow random walk, hedge funds succeed mainly through luck
- Logic: if possible to know bond/equity would rise, smart investors would have pounced already
- Implication: professional money managers generally fail
- Successful funds "could drop their *h* and be called edge funds"

### Market Inefficiencies as Hedge Fund Edge

- **Liquidity imperfections**: 1970s-80s, big pension fund dumping large block couldn't find buyer without offering discount
- **Michael Steinhardt** made fortune systematically milking these discounts
- **Paul Tudor Jones** maintains he anticipated 1987 crash because colleague **Peter Borish** mapped 1980s market against 1929 charts
- But Borish admitted to massaging data; predicted crash would hit spring 1988 (actual: October 1987)
- "Genius does not always understand itself"

> "Out of all the research that we've done with top players, we haven't found a single player who is consistent in knowing and explaining exactly what he does." — tennis coach Vic Braden

### Academic Conversion to Market Inefficiency

- **Scott Irwin** (young economist): analyzed commodity price series from Indianapolis firm, proclaimed prices moved in trends
- **Commodities Corporation** hedge fund had analyzed same data years earlier, programmed computer to trade on it
- **1987 crash** underlined doubts: when market valuation changed 1/5 in single trading day, hard to believe valuation deserved deference

> "If the efficient markets hypothesis was a publicly traded security, its price would be enormously volatile. But the stock crashed along with the rest of the market on October 19, 1987." — Harvard economists **Andrei Shleifer** and **Lawrence Summers**, 1990

### The Legitimization Effect

- Acknowledgment of market inefficiency limits had profound effect on hedge funds
- Lines of academics queued to join them
- **Cliff Asness** at University of Chicago: thesis adviser was **Eugene Fama**, father of efficient-market hypothesis
- By 1988, Fama was leading revisionist charge with **Kenneth French**—discovered non-random patterns lucrative for traders
- **Myron Scholes** and **Robert Merton**: Nobel laureates signed up with **Long-Term Capital Management**
- **Andrei Shleifer**: helped create investment company **LSV** with two fellow finance professors
- **Lawrence Summers**: signed with **D. E. Shaw**, quantitative hedge fund

### Institutional Capital Flows to Hedge Funds

- Years after 1987 crash = inflection point
- Before: most hedge-fund money from rich individuals
- After: most money from endowments
- New wave led by **David Swensen** (Yale endowment boss)
- 2002: swashbuckling **Farallon** swooped into Indonesia, bought country's largest bank
- Endowments sought uncorrelated returns called **"alpha"**

### The Social Function of Alpha

- **Fama and French** research: unglamorous "value" stocks underpriced relative to overhyped "growth" stocks
- Hedge funds' function: correct inefficiencies
- **Cliff Asness** buying value stocks and shorting growth stocks = reducing unhealthy bias against solid firms
- **Michael Steinhardt** buying Ford stock after large-block sale dip = ensuring fair prices
- Statistical arbitrageurs like **Jim Simons** and **David Shaw** computerizing Steinhardt's art
- By flattening kinks in market behavior, hedge funds contributed to **"Great Moderation"**

### The Destabilization Question

**1994 bond market crisis**:
- Federal Reserve announced tiny 0.25% rise in short-term interest rates
- Bond market went into mad spin
- Leveraged hedge funds wrong-footed, began dumping positions furiously
- Turmoil spread from US to Japan, Europe, emerging world
- Several hedge funds sank; looked like **Bankers Trust** might be dragged down

**LTCM failure (1998)**: Terrified regulators rushed in to oversee burial

**East Asian crisis**: Malaysia's prime minister **Mahathir Mohamad** lamented:

> "All these countries have spent 40 years trying to build up their economies and a moron like Soros comes along with a lot of money to speculate and ruins things."

### Two Competing Views at Start of 21st Century

**View 1**: Hedge funds as stabilizing heroes who muscled inefficient prices into line

**View 2**: Hedge funds as weak links whose instability or wanton aggression threatened global economy

- Heart of matter: leverage
- Leverage gave hedge funds ammunition to trade in greater volume → render prices more efficient
- But leverage made hedge funds vulnerable to shocks
- In US and Britain: hedge funds' stabilizing impact received emphasis
- Elsewhere: risk of destabilizing panics got most attention

### The 2007-2009 Crisis and Its Lessons

- **July 2007**: credit hedge fund **Sowood** blew up; following month dozen quantitative hedge funds tried cutting positions simultaneously
- Following year: Lehman Brothers collapse left hedge funds with money trapped
- Hedge funds needed access to leverage, but nobody lent after Lehman shock
- Hedge funds built strategies on short selling, but governments imposed shorting restrictions
- Investors demanded capital back; some funds withheld it by imposing "gates"

### The Mistaken Conclusion

**What the cataclysm actually showed**:

1. **Central banks must steer economies differently**: target asset-price inflation, not just consumer-price inflation
   - Fed allowed borrowing binge because focused on consumer-price inflation
   - American households increased borrowing from 66% of GDP (1997) to 100% (decade later)
   - Housing finance companies sold unrepayable mortgages
   - Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac collapsed
   - Banks like Citigroup gorged on bad mortgage-backed securities

2. **Financial firms riddled with dysfunctional incentives**: clearest problem is "too big to fail"
   - Wall Street behemoths load up on risk expecting taxpayer bailouts
   - Institutions government rescued: commercial banks (Citigroup), former investment banks (Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley), insurers (AIG)
   - **Hedge funds made it through mayhem without direct taxpayer assistance**
   - Even LTCM 1998: Fed oversaw burial but provided no money to cover losses
   - **They are safe to fail, even if not fail-safe**

3. **Skewed incentives in traders' pay packages**:
   - Hedge funds have "high-water marks": if lose money one year, take reduced/no performance fees until earn back losses
   - Hedge-fund bosses mostly have own money in funds
   - Bank traders face fewer restraints
   - Average hedge fund borrows only 1-2x investors' capital; highly leveraged ones generally borrow <10x
   - Investment banks like Goldman or Lehman: leveraged **30:1** before crisis
   - Commercial banks like Citi: even higher

4. **Hedge fund structure promotes paranoid discipline**:
   - Banks = establishment institutions with comfortable bosses
   - Hedge funds = scrappy upstarts with bosses who think nothing of staying up all night
   - Banks collect savings with government deposit insurance
   - Hedge funds must demonstrate risk management before raising client money
   - Banks know liquidity crisis = access to central bank emergency lending
   - Hedge funds have no such safety net
   - Banks mark to market only when borrowers repay
   - Hedge funds mark portfolios to market → slight blips affect bottom line immediately

## Chapter 1: Big Daddy

### The Dawn of Hedge Funds

- 2003-2006: Top 100 hedge funds doubled to **$1 trillion**
- 1968 article cited fund manager who made **20% in a week, for seven weeks in a row**

> "If you really know what's going on, you don't even have to know what's going on to know what's going on... You can ignore the headlines, because you anticipated them months ago."

### Alfred Winslow Jones: The Founding Father

- Born **September 9, 1900** (9th hour, 9th day, 9th month)
- Son of expatriate American running **General Electric's** Australian operations
- Graduated **Harvard** 1923, worked as purser on tramp steamer, export buyer, statistician

### Berlin and Political Awakening

- Posted to **Berlin, December 1930** as vice-consul
- Germany: 8% output drop, 4.5 million unemployed
- **National Socialist Party** won 107 Reichstag seats
- Met **Anna Block** — Jewish banker's daughter, socialite, left-wing anti-Nazi activist
- Married Anna secretly; forced to resign State Department May 1932
- Returned to Berlin fall 1932 as "Richard Frost," working for **Leninist Organization**
- Moved to London 1933 as "H. B. Wood," lobbying British Labour Party for military action against Hitler

### Return to America and Academic Career

- Divorced Anna, returned to New York 1934
- Enrolled at **Columbia University** sociology graduate program
- Married **Mary Elizabeth Carter**
- 1937 honeymoon in war-torn Spain; hitchhiked to front lines with **Dorothy Parker**, met **Ernest Hemingway**

### Doctoral Work and Political Evolution

- Late 1930s: researched whether same calamity could befall America
- Decamped to **Akron, Ohio**; organized team for **1,700 field interviews**
- Statistical analysis concluded: **acute economic divisions did not carry over into polarized world-views**
- Repudiation of socialist assumptions from youth
- Thesis published as **Life, Liberty and Property** (1941)

### Launch into Journalism

- **Fortune** magazine published condensed version, offered Jones a job
- 1942 essay warned **Roosevelt's** economic statism needed dismantling post-war

> "As conservative as possible in protecting the free market and as radical as necessary in securing the welfare of the people."

### "Fashions in Forecasting" (1948)

- March 1949 Fortune essay gave Jones opportunity to turn mind to finance
- Attacked "standard, old-fashioned method of predicting the course of the stock market"
- Jones cited moments when stocks shifted sharply **without changed economic data**
- Money = medium through which greed and fear expressed themselves; **barometer of crowd psychology**

### The Feedback Loop Theory

- Investor emotions create trends in stock prices
- Stock market rise → investor optimism → further rise → more optimism
- Feedback loop drives prices up, creating **self-fulfilling trend**

### Ignoring Academic Economics

- Jones oddly ignorant of **Alfred Cowles** (father of statistical economics)
- Cowles published 1933 and 1944 studies: "Can Stock Market Forecasters Forecast?" — three-word abstract: **"It is doubtful"**
- Jones cited Cowles selectively
- Cowles concluded any appearance of patterns was **too faint and unreliable to be traded upon profitably**
- Forecasting a trend likely to destroy it

### Launching the First Hedge Fund (1949)

- By March 1949, launched world's first hedge fund
- Late forties, two children, expensive New York tastes
- Left **Fortune** staff hoping to launch new magazine; blueprints failed
- Raised **$60,000** from four friends, put up **$40,000** of own money

### Twenty-Year Track Record

- By 1968: cumulative return of **just under 5,000%**
- Investor who gave $10,000 in 1949 now worth **$480,000**
- Five years to 1965: returned **325%**, dwarfing 225% return on hottest mutual fund
- Performance rivaled even **Warren Buffett**

### The Innovation: Leverage and Short Selling

- Started in shabby office on Broad Street
- **True innovation: structure of fund**
- Standard practice: load up with stocks when market expected to rise, hold cash when expected to topple
- Jones improved: used **leverage** (borrowed to be 150% long) and **short selling**
- 1929 trauma gave both techniques bad name
- Jones's innovation: "**speculative means for conservative ends**"

> "You could buy more good stocks without taking as much risk as someone who merely bought."

### 1961 Prospectus Example

Two investors, each with **$100,000**:
- **Traditional investor**: $80,000 in best stocks, $20,000 in safe bonds
- **Hedged investor**: borrows $100,000 for total $200,000, buys $130,000 good stocks, shorts $70,000 bad stocks
- Hedged investor: superior diversification, **net $60,000** exposure vs $80,000

**Bull Market (20% rise)**:
- Traditional: **$24,000** gain
- Hedged: **$32,000** gain

**Bear Market (20% fall)**:
- Traditional: **-$8,000** loss
- Hedged: **$8,000** gain

Result defies basic investing rule: **higher returns by assuming higher risk**

### Understanding Volatility: The "Velocity" Concept

- Some stocks bounce more than others: different **volatilities**
- Jones measured volatility of all stocks — called it **"velocity"**
- **Sears Roebuck** since 1948: swings 80% as big as market average → relative velocity of **80**
- **General Dynamics**: relative velocity of **196**
- Must adjust position sizes for volatility to create true hedge

### Separating Alpha from Beta

- Jones distinguished money made through stock picking (**alpha**) from money made through market exposure (**beta**)
- Later became commonplace terminology
- Each evening looked up closing prices, noted in pencil in dog-eared leather book

### Anticipating Modern Portfolio Theory

- **1952**: **Harry Markowitz** published "Portfolio Selection"
- Jones's velocity calculations effectively controlled risk as Markowitz advocated
- **William Sharpe** developed variation: calculate **single correlation between each stock and market index**
- Precisely what Jones's velocity calculations were designed to do
- By 1963, Jones implementing Sharpe's advice for over a decade

### Anticipating Separation Theorem

- Jones anticipated **James Tobin** (Nobel Prize winner)
- **1958**: Tobin proposed **separation theorem**: investor's stock choice should be separate from risk appetite question
- Nine years before Tobin published, Jones was onto same point

### Secrecy and Avoiding Regulation

- Wanted to protect investment methods from competitors
- **Richard Valentine** (attorney at Seward & Kissel): realized if managers took share of investment profits, could be **taxed at capital-gains rate** (25% vs 91%)
- Jones charged investors **20% of upside**
- Claimed inspired by **Phoenician merchants** keeping fifth of profits from successful voyages
- Performance fee termed "**performance reallocation**"

### Escaping Regulatory Oversight

- Declined to register under Securities Act of 1933, Investment Company Act of 1940, Investment Advisors Act of 1940
- **Essential**: these laws restricted funds from borrowing or selling short
- To sustain idea funds were private: **never advertised publicly**
- 1961: set up second partnership rather than allow first to cross **100-member threshold**

### The Price of Secrecy

- Stealth allowed Jones escape regulatory oversight
- **Came at a price**: secrecy piques public curiosity
- "Wall Street's last bastions of secrecy, mystery, exclusivity, and privilege" (**John Brooks**)
- Thanks to pattern established early, **hedge funds forever mysterious, shadowy, resented**

### The Price of Ignoring Cowles

- Efforts to call overall market direction **failed as often as they succeeded**
- **1953, 1956, 1957**: Jones lost money on market calls
- **1960**: Cowles published update reversing earlier finding — **concluded trends did not exist after all**
- **August 1965**: net exposure of **minus 18%**, market embarked on hot rally
- **Jones never turned profit by betting the charts**

### The Real Source of Success

- Statistical methods revealed precisely how much money losing from bad market calls
- Funds still performed marvelously
- Having designed hedged portfolio, **needed to choose stocks to put inside it**

### The Model Portfolio System

- Jones knew he could not be great stock picker himself
- Starting early 1950s: invited brokers to run **"model portfolios"**
- Each man selected favorite shorts and longs, phoned in changes
- Jones used paper portfolios as source of stock-picking ideas
- Compensated brokers according to how well suggestions worked
- **Marvelous and unique**: getting brokers to phone in hot ideas before giving to others

### Competitive Edge in the 1950s

- 1950s Wall Street: **sleepy, unsophisticated place**
- Harvard investment course dubbed **"Darkness at Noon"** (unpopular lunchtime slot)
- Jones's method broke mold: **each stock picker for himself**
- Substituted **individualism for collectivism**, **adrenaline for complacency**
- Even in 1960s: convened remarkably few investment meetings
- Allotted each in-house manager segment of partners' capital, left manager to invest

### The Information Advantage

- 1950s-1960s: **information didn't reach everyone at once**
- Jones men hustled hardest
- **Alan Dresher** (Jones stock picker): had idea of going to SEC offices to read company filings **the moment they came out**
- Extraordinary: **he was all alone**

### Linking Compensation to Results

- When broker passed stock tip to normal mutual fund: **no connection between tip quality and broker payment**
- Jones meticulous in paying for good research ideas, **paid handsomely**
- Young broker could see salary double if model portfolio recommendations generated profits
- Each year successful segment managers given **extra capital to manage**
- Unsuccessful managers received **less capital to play with**
- Jones required **partners to have own capital in funds**

### Creating Competitive Multimanager Structure

- Without realizing significance, Jones created **competitive multimanager structure**
- Same structure reinvented 1970s by firm in Princeton, New Jersey
- Combination of **Darwinist individualism** and **top-down risk control** almost unique to Jones
- **Jones blew up institutional arrangements, created edge that brought in serious money**

### The Decline of A.W. Jones

- **Barton Biggs** persuaded Jones's longest-serving fund manager to quit and start rival fund
- Every great investor's edge eventually unravels
- By 1969, there were **200-500 hedge fund imitators**
- **The Economist** reported **$11 billion** under management — five times figure from two years earlier
- Term "hedge fund" (corrupted version of Jones's "hedged fund") entered Wall Street lexicon

### The Hedge Fund Boom of the Late 1960s

- **Fortune** profiled Jones in 1966 as "the best professional manager of investors' money"
- Jones himself earned "something in the millions"
- **New York** magazine proclaimed A.W. Jones was hedge funds' "big daddy"

### Regulatory Scrutiny

- 1968: **New York Stock Exchange** and **American Stock Exchange** began considering restrictions
- January 1969: **SEC** sent questionnaires to 200 hedge funds
- Complaints seemed curious: nobody explained why short selling might be pernicious
- Hedge funds were envied and adored while also being objects of irrational resentment

### Jones's Indian Summer and Decline

- 1966-1968: Jones's investors pocketed returns (after fees) of **26%, 22%, and 47%**
- But concealed trouble: losing their edge
- Stock pickers defected to set up rival firms
- Hedging principles no longer seemed relevant
- Pushed boundaries of leverage
- Velocity calculations fell by the wayside

### The End of the Go-Go Era (1969-1973)

- Jones had been lucky: opened hedged fund just as crash trauma wearing off
- 1950: only 1 in 25 American adults owned stocks; by late 1950s, 1 in 8 did
- S&P 500 rose from 15 at Jones's launch to peak of **108 in late 1968**
- **Jerry Goodman/The Great Winfield**: entrusted money to twentysomething managers with no memories and no fear

> "Show me a portfolio, I'll tell you the generation. You can tell the swinger stocks because they frighten all the other [older] generations." — The Great Winfield

### Jones's Fatal Flaw: The Multimanager Structure

- Multimanager structure empowered go-go segment managers, but wasn't designed to save Jones from sudden reversal
- More the market rose, more Jones's performance-tracking system rewarded aggressive segment managers
- May 1969: stock market started to fall, shedding quarter of value over next year
- Year ending May 1970: Jones lost **35%** of client money
- September 1970: Jones's 70th birthday party — fretted segment managers would resent spending
- After two decades of eminence, Jones's investment edge was gone

## Chapter 2: The Block Trader

### The Economic Watershed (1969-1973)

- 1969-1973 marked watershed for American economy
- Previous two decades: jobs plentiful, wages rose, finance quaintly stable
- Late 1960s: inflation tore this world apart
- Below 2% in first half of decade, hit **5.5% in spring 1969**
- 1971: **Nixon administration** abandoned gold standard
- Another round of monetary tightening; market crashed again in 1973-74

### The First Hedge Fund Era Ends

- Close of 1968 to September 30, 1970: **28 largest hedge funds lost two thirds of capital**
- Claim to be hedged turned out to be bald-faced lie
- By January 1970, only **150 hedge funds** remained (down from 200-500)
- Crash of 1973-74 wiped out most of the rest
- 1984: survey by **Tremont Partners** identified only **68 hedge funds**
- **A.W. Jones partnership** withered: from $100 million+ managed in late 1960s to **$35 million in 1973** and **$25 million** decade later

### The Rise of Steinhardt, Fine, Berkowitz & Company

- Adversity did not rule out success
- Dominant partner **Michael Steinhardt** became legend
- Raised by single mother in hardscrabble Brooklyn; father confrontational, addicted to gambling
- Admitted to **University of Pennsylvania** at 16; graduated at 19
- By mid-1960s (age ~25): "the hottest analyst on Wall Street"
- Short, barrel-chested, prone to terrifying outbursts

### Launching the Hedge Fund (1967)

- Steinhardt quit brokerage business in 1967, launching hedge fund with **Jerrold Fine** and **Howard Berkowitz**
- Equipped offices with pool table, proclaimed intellectual superiority of youth
- First full year (1968): loaded up on story stocks
- **King Resources**, **National Student Marketing**, technology companies with "Data" or "-onics"
- Year-end result: **up 84%**; each had become millionaires

### Surviving the 1969-70 Bear Market

- 1969: bull market ended; other go-go funds blew up
- **Fred Mates**, top-performing mutual-fund manager of 1968, found himself working as bartender
- Almost alone among gunslingers, Steinhardt, Fine, and Berkowitz sensed long postwar expansion had overreached
- Had read **Benjamin Graham**, who made fateful decision not to buy shares in **Xerox/Haloid**
- Haloid sextupled in next two years

### Actually Hedging

- Start of 1969: Steinhardt and friends shorted enough story stocks to balance long positions
- When S&P 500 fell **9%** that year, firm preserved all but sliver of capital
- Following year (S&P 500 dropped another 9%), troika actually made money
- Having survived 1969-70, firm went on offensive in 1971
- When **Fortune** published list of top 28 hedge funds that year, Steinhardt, Fine, Berkowitz was only one to have expanded
- Partnership racked up return of **361%** since opening in July 1967 — **36x better than stock market index**

### The Contrarian Turn (1972)

- 1972: young troika turned pessimistic again
- Edifice of America's postwar confidence seemed to be cracking:
  - Nixon administration covering up truth about failures in Vietnam
  - Covering up inflation with impractical wage-and-price-control program
  - America's finest companies covering up truth with accounting shenanigans
- **Jerry Fine** and **Howard Berkowitz** found red flags in footnotes regularly
- **Leonard Spacek** (chairman emeritus of **Arthur Andersen**): "financial statements are a roulette wheel"

### Positioned for the Crash

- Stock market trading at levels reflecting broad political and financial delusions
- 1972: positioned portfolio so short positions greatly outweighed longs, waited for crash

### The 1973-74 Payoff

- At first, it did not happen — market sailed along for rest of 1972
- Fund was down 2% in year to September, when S&P 500 rose 9%
- Then payoff came: S&P fell 2% in year to September 1973 and shocking **41%** the following year
- Steinhardt, Fine, Berkowitz racked up gains of **12% then 28%** after fees
- Old prejudice against short selling came back with vengeance
- Betting against American companies portrayed as one step away from treason
- Thinking back on that period, Steinhardt recalls: **"That was, for me, the height of professional satisfaction"**

### The Success Formula: Contrarianism

- Success demonstrated capacity for contrarianism that marked later hedge funds
- A.W. Jones invented hedge-fund structure to control market exposure
- Go-go imitators turned it into way of maximizing market exposure
- Troika made it into vehicle for betting against conventional wisdom

### Block Trading Ethics and Market Impact

- **Block trading collusion**: Brokers tipped Steinhardt about large institutional orders before execution
- **Will Weinstein** (Oppenheimer): "Everyone had a hundred behind it"
- Steinhardt's frank admission: "I was being told things other accounts were not being told. I got information I shouldn't have. It created a lot of opportunities for us."
- **28 years in markets, only 4 losing years** — probability of 1 in 11,000

### SEC Investigation and Regulatory Response

- **January 1970**: SEC alleged Steinhardt purchased Seaboard Corporation block to help broker boost price
- **1976**: Settled without admitting wrongdoing
- Regulators attempted reforms:
  - Created **"consolidated tape"** to print all stock transactions
  - Required bids and offers be similarly reported

### Economic Benefits of Hedge Funds

Two stabilizing contributions:
1. **Contrarianism dampened market swings**: Sold during 1972 bubble, went long at end of 1974 crash
2. **Monetary analysis innovation**: Applied analytical techniques to asset pricing

**Block trading as liquidity provision**: Institutional investors needed to trade large chunks of stock; Steinhardt provided liquidity

### Post-1987 Market Changes

- **Black Monday aftermath**: Big block-trading desks pulled back from business
- *New York Times* (December): 25,000-share blocks now disrupted prices as much as 100,000-share blocks had before
- SEC promised to investigate "troubling lack of block trading"
- Ironic reversal: Fast-trading hedge funds became preferable to their sudden absence

### Steinhardt's 1978 Sabbatical

- Fall 1978: took sabbatical to "shrink his waistline and expand his head"
- Skeptics doubted commitment
- Returned fall 1979, broke up with partners

## Chapter 3: Paul Samuelson's Secret

### Samuelson's Efficient Market Paradox

- **1967 congressional testimony**: **Paul Samuelson** cited PhD dissertation showing randomly chosen stock portfolios beat professionally managed mutual funds
- **1970**: Samuelson won Nobel Prize
- **1974**: "Most portfolio decision makers should go out of business—take up plumbing, teach Greek, or help produce the annual GNP"

> "Even if this advice to drop dead is good advice, it obviously is not counsel that will be eagerly followed."

But left room for exceptions: "People differ in their heights, pulchritude, and acidity. Why not in their P.Q. or performance quotient?"
- Exceptional investors wouldn't rent themselves cheaply — they'd form small partnerships to capture gains
- These would be **hedge funds**
- **1970**: Became founding backer of **Commodities Corporation**

### Commodities Corporation Origins

- Among first boutiques for hard-core "quants"
- Premise: Harness "large scale econometric analysis, impossible prior to introduction of computers"
- **Paul Cootner** (MIT colleague): contributed to efficient-market theory
- Hired Apollo project programmer — literally a rocket scientist
- Structured as corporation, but otherwise typical hedge fund
- **Samuelson's investment**: **$125,000** stake, became active board member

### F. Helmut Weymar — The Driven Leader

- **PhD dissertation**: anticipated cocoa price using historical data
- Tall, bespectacled, distinguished Northern European air
- Moved cities frequently as child — left him independent, determined to make money "beholden to nobody"

**Graduate student cocoa trade**: Built mathematical model of frozen-orange-juice industry predicting price would double
- Borrowed $20,000 to buy warehouse consignment
- Price shot up as predicted
- Local supermarkets refused to buy wholesale from student

**Nabisco cocoa trading**: Model predicted prices would fall, so Weymar bought cocoa futures by truckload
- Cocoa prices almost doubled after African crop failures
- Weymar sold stockpile at vast profit

> "This wasn't a period of maximum modesty or self-doubt in my life."

### Founding Commodities Corporation

- **Frank Vannerson**: bearded, subdued economics PhD
- PhD thesis on wheat market complemented Weymar's cocoa expertise
- Raised **$2.5 million** in start-up capital
- **Princeton, New Jersey farmhouse headquarters** — flowering trees, acres of lawn
- Informal, egalitarian office culture
- German shepherd **Cocoa** (lost leg to car collision)
- Seven professionals, six support staff
- Horseshoes at lunch, softball after markets closed
- Philosophy: beat market with computer models, math, superior information

### Team Composition — Fundamental Analysis Focus

Econometricians built models predicting where prices ought to be:
- **Vannerson**: Wheat
- **Paul Cootner**: Pork-belly market econometric model
- **Kenneth Meinken**: Soybeans and feed grains

**Weymar's cocoa model data**:
- Tracked correlation between weather and cocoa yields in Ivory Coast and Ghana
- **Hans Kilian** (German): toured African countryside in Land Rover, counted cocoa pods (25 per tree)
- Land Rover frequently broke down
- Battled to get Ghanaian currency to pay local workers

### The Corn Blight Fiasco (1971)

- **U.S. cornfields hit by fungal disease**
- Plant experts predicted reappearance following year on bigger scale
- Weymar and colleagues saw chance for edge

**Retained plant pathologist** at Rutgers University:
- Investigation conclusion: blight fright was overdone
- Traders built vast short positions in anticipation

**Friday night CBS News special**: Featured **Illinois state plant pathologist** — predicted **catastrophic corn harvest**

**Monday opening**: Corn futures jumped so steeply trading immediately suspended
- Couldn't exit positions until Tuesday
- **Firm's $2.5 million start-up capital reduced to $900,000**

**Resolution**:

### The Philosophy of Reflexivity

**Soros's central insight**: markets not efficient because **investors' perceptions affect underlying reality**

- Contrast with efficient-market theory: assumes underlying reality exists independently of investors' views

**Implications**:
- Investors who spot start of virtuous cycle can **ride trend profitably**

- Once Druckenmiller's trading demonstrated **power of speculators over governments**, risk of abuse became obvious
- French politicians' concerns about hedge funds amassing **dangerous and excessive power** not totally baseless

### The Policy Response: Nothing

**Clamping Down = Return to Bretton Woods**:
- Little was done to reduce danger
- Clamping down on speculators would require **taming cross-border money waves**
- Would involve **return to Bretton Woods and reimposition of capital controls**
- Most policy makers **viewed with horror**
- If free trade in goods/services beneficial, surely **free flows of capital good for same reason**
- Week after sterling crisis, **Spain and Ireland tried to dampen speculative attacks by restricting banks' freedom**
- **Controls quickly circumvented**

**Abolish National Currencies**:
- If capital controls off table, one remaining way to **prevent speculative attacks**—**abolish them**
- **"Speculation can be very harmful,"** Soros told interviewer
- Single European currency **"would put speculators like me out of business, but I would be delighted to make that sacrifice"**
- **Europe eventually unified currencies in 1999**, but not everybody learned
- **Emerging economies in Asia and Latin America** stuck with pegging policy
- Created **immense opportunities for hedge funds later in decade**

## Chapter 8: Hurricane Greenspan

### Michael Steinhardt's Shadowbanking Empire

**The Setup: Fed-Assisted Arbitrage**:
- December 1993: **Steinhardt Partners** up **$100 million in single day**
- Running **~$4.5 billion** in capital; 1991/1992 returns: **47%/48%** after fees
- Magazines reporting Steinhardt's **Picasso purchase (~$1 million)**
- Early 1990s: pioneering **early version of shadow banking system**
- Strategy involved **exploiting central bank policies** (**U.S. Federal Reserve**)

**How It Worked**:
- **1990-1991**: U.S. in recession following S&L crisis
- **Fed keeping short-term interest rates low** to stimulate economy
- Steinhardt could **borrow short-term money exceedingly cheaply, load up on longer-term bonds** yielding considerably more
- Risk: if **longer-term interest rates spiked up**, value of bonds would crater
- But **sluggish economy kept demand for capital low**
- **Longer-term interest rates fell** at start of 1990s
- Steinhardt handed **capital gain on bonds** on top of profits from gap between short/long-term rates

**Political Economy**:
- **John Major enriched Druckenmiller** to neutralize political rivals
- **Fed rewarded Steinhardt** trying to assist battered banking system
- By **increasing gap between short/long-term rates**, Fed made it **more lucrative for banks to engage in normal business**

**Steinhardt as Shadowbank**:
- Fed wanted to help banks, so Steinhardt **turned himself into shadowbank**
- Borrowed short, lent long—**just like any bank would**
- Difference: **Steinhardt bypassed tedious business** of hiring tellers/credit officers
- Borrowed from **brokerages (Goldman Sachs, Salomon Brothers)**, then lent by buying bonds
- **No bank infrastructure**—could **charge in/out as Fed's policies shifted**

**The Leverage Advantage**:
- Real banks faced **regulatory controls on lending**
- For every **$100 customer deposits**, had to set aside **~$10 in capital**
- Steinhardt had **no depositors, hence no "capital adequacy" rule**
- Could **borrow whatever brokers would lend**
- For every **$100 Steinhardt borrowed to buy U.S. government bonds**, could often get away with **as little as $1 in capital**

### Europe and the Bond Strategy Expansion

**1993: Taking Bond Strategy to Europe**:
- Steinhardt/fellow speculators took bond strategy to Europe
- **Exchange-rate mechanism crises over**, Europe headed for **monetary union**
- Process forcing **interest rates across continent to converge**
- Countries like **Spain and Italy** (weak inflation record) now subjected to **strict inflation-fighting rules**
- Interest rates began **falling toward Germany's**
- Steinhardt/traders bought **truckloads of Spanish and Italian bonds**
- Realized **capital gain as interest rates came down**
- **European statesmen made no secret** of plans for monetary union

### The Hedge Fund Boom (Early 1990s)

**Industry Growth**:
- **Hedge funds profited hugely** early 1990s
- Senior investment bankers **quit Goldman Sachs/Salomon Brothers** to get in on new game
- **Wall Street law firms** claimed to be **midwifing new hedge-fund partnerships at rate of two per month**
- Number of hedge funds: **over 1,000 (1992) → perhaps 3,000 (next year)**

**Fee Evolution**:
- **Dawn of industry**: **A.W. Jones** charged **no management fee**, asking only **20% profit share**
- **Second generation**: demanded **1% management fee + 20% profit share**
- **Early 1990s boom**: hot new funds demanded **"2 and 20"**

> "Perhaps never before in history have so few made so much money so fast" — *Forbes*

**Steinhardt's Market Dominance**:
- Steinhardt's energetic shadowbanking **mirrored by most other major funds**
- **April 1991 Treasury bond auction**: Steinhardt and **Bruce Kovner** bid for **$6.5 billion of $12 billion** paper
- Lent bonds to short sellers, bought them back again → ended with **$16 billion bonds** (considerably more than 100% of market)
- Bonds shot up in value, **short sellers tried to get out** but couldn't buy back paper

**Legal Settlements**:
- After **three years fighting in courts**, Steinhardt/Kovner settled without admitting guilt
- Steinhardt: **paid $40 million** compensation to short sellers
- Kovner: **paid $36 million**

### Shadowbanks Changing Monetary Policy

**Accelerating Policy Transmission**:
- As hedge funds responded to **Fed's low short-term rates** by aggressively buying longer-dated Treasuries, **link between short/long-term rates grew tighter**
- **Cut in Fed's rate** now **fed through a lot faster**
- **Could be good**: if Fed wanted to stimulate economy, helpful that hedge funds chased long bond rates downward
- **Could be dangerous**: bond market might respond quickly, but **real economy bound to lag**

**The Pyramiding Danger**:
- Bubbles threatened to be **newly scary**
- World in which hedge funds could **corner Treasury auction** = new kind of world built on **breathtaking leverage**
- **American system pyramiding debt upon debt**: government borrowed from hedge funds → hedge funds borrowed from brokers
- **If one player collapsed, rest could lose access to borrowed funds** → force them to dump assets fast
- **Bubble could burst instantly**

### January 1994: Steinhardt's Confidence

- Start of 1994: prospect not on anybody's mind
- January: authorized lieutenant to **buy staggering position in Canadian bonds**
- Added to bets in **U.S., Japan, Europe**
- Headed off to **China on another vacation**

### Alan Greenspan's Rate Hike (February 4, 1994)

**The Setup**:
- **January 21, 1994**: **Federal Reserve Chairman Alan Greenspan** visited White House
- Economy had **recovered, then grown smoothly for 34 consecutive months**
- Inflation quiescent, but **time to preempt resurgence with small rate hike**
- Aimed to pilot economy toward **"soft landing"**

**Gore's Objection**:
- **Vice President Gore** objected
- In past, small rate hike signaled **beginning of long series**
- If markets expected same, **long rates might shoot upward anticipating more tightening**
- **Bond market would crash**

**Greenspan's Reassurance**:
- Fed chairman spent career as **economic consultant in New York**
- More than most Washington figures, **understood markets**
- Focused above all on **reaction in stock market**
- Bond market seemed **secondary importance**

**The Theory**:
- Greenspan assured Gore: **long-term rates governed mainly by inflation expectations**
- If Fed raised short rates, would **signal authorities vigilant on price pressures**
- Result: **lower inflation expectations → lower long-term rates**
- Rate hike **should be bullish for bond market**

**The Reality: February 4, 1994**:
- Fortnight later: Greenspan presided over **Fed's interest-rate committee meeting**
- Proposed to **head off inflation** with gentle rate hike of **25 basis points** (3% → 3.25%)
- Greenspan instructed colleagues: **"If we do 50 basis points today, very high probability of cracking markets... very unwise procedure"**

**Market Reaction**:
- Greenspan's sense of stock market **right**
- After Fed announced quarter-point rate hike, **S&P 500 dipped**
- Greenspan's assurances about bond market **proved mistaken**
- Instead: **Fed's 25-basis-point tightening produced swift increase of same magnitude in ten-year Treasury rate**
- **Something mysterious was stirring**

### The Shadowbank Response: Opposite of Greenspan's Expectation

**The New Logic**:
- In era before shadowbanking, Greenspan's reassurances **might have proved justified**
- **New shadowbanks not as focused on inflation** as Fed chairman expected
- Way shadowbankers saw: **first Fed hike in half decade created uncertainty**
- Uncertainty = risk
- Because even **small fall in bond market could wipe out thin capital base** of leveraged hedge funds, **mere *possibility* of fall forced them to reduce risk**
- **New logic of leverage changed central-banking game**
- In response to rate hike, shadowbanks **dumped bonds, forced long-term interest rates up**—**opposite of what Greenspan expected**

**February 11: The Yen Shock**:
- Week after Fed's action (Friday, February 11): **another shock to bond market**
- **Trade talks between Clinton administration and Japan broke down**
- American side seeking revenge by **signaling stronger yen would be in order**
- Within week, **yen jumped 7% against dollar**
- Wrong-footing several hedge funds (including **Stan Druckenmiller**: **$8 billion bet against yen**, lost **$650 million in two days**)
- **Sheer scale of hedge-fund leverage ensured vicious chain reaction**
- Losses from yen shock forced hedge funds to **dump assets and raise capital**
- Over two weeks, **ten-year Treasury yield jumped more than quarter of percent**
- **World in which hedge funds traded everything = world of unpredictable connections**

### Europe: The Contagion Spreads

**February: Central Banks Push Rates Down**:
- Next victim: **Europe**
- February: central banks of **Germany, Britain, France, Belgium pushed short-term rates downward**

**Traders' Logic Asserts Itself**:
- In aftermath of yen shock, **traders' logic asserted itself once again**
- **European long-term interest rates spiked up**
- In fortnight: yield on **German 10-year bonds rose 37 basis points, Italy's rose 58, Spain's rose 62**
- Hedge funds lost money on U.S. Treasuries and yen
- Responding by **dumping European bonds**

**The Stampede and Margin Calls**:
- Once hedge funds **began to flee Europe**, stampede built on own momentum
- **Brokers that lent freely** suddenly reversed
- Rather than accepting **$1 million collateral per $100 million bonds**, brokers demanded **$3-5 million**
- To meet brokers' margin calls, hedge funds had to **liquidate holdings on grand scale**
- If **leveraged 100:1**, and broker demands extra **$4 million in margin**, you have to **sell $400 million bonds—quickly**
- **Scary pyramiding of debt** (fueled bond bubble in good times) now **accelerated implosion**

**March 1: Inflation Data Triggers More Selling**:
- Two weeks after yen shock (March 1): **more bad news**
- New data: **U.S. inflation more of threat than feared**
- **Bond markets in Japan and Europe cratered**
- Far from spooked by inflation, **Japan grappling with threat of *deflation***
- Yet **ten-year Japanese interest rates jumped 17 basis points**
- To raise capital, hedge funds **off-loaded estimated $60 billion European bond holdings**

### The Carnage: March 1994

**Major Casualties**:
- Frenzy of selling created **sharp losses across Wall Street**
- **Paul Tudor Jones**: spring 1994 fund **down sharply**
- Same for **Bruce Kovner and Louis Bacon**
- **David Gerstenhaber** (macro trader): **blew up spectacularly**
- **Proprietary trading desks did badly**: 1994, **Goldman Sachs experienced worst year in decade**
- **Insurance industry**: reckoned to have **lost as much money on bond holdings as damages from Hurricane Andrew**
- **"I'm starting to call this Hurricane Greenspan"** — insurance analyst

**Bankers Trust Near-Failure**:
- Few hours on March 2: **Bankers Trust teetered on brink of bankruptcy**
- Trading suspended on **New York Stock Exchange**
- **New York Fed president William McDonough** phoned top bankers to rally confidence
- Ultimately, **Bankers Trust survived**

### Michael Steinhardt: The Greatest Casualty

**The Losses**:
- **Greatest casualty of bloodbath: Michael Steinhardt**
- Returned from China vacation to find **U.S. bond market sold off**
- Biggest bets: **Japan, Canada, especially Europe** (accumulated **astonishing $30 billion bond portfolio**)
- Then **logic of deleveraging kicked in**
- Every bond market whipsawed
- For Steinhardt's **vast and leveraged portfolio**, each **basis-point move upward in European rates = $10 million loss**
- By end of February: **bled stunning $900 million**
- **Down almost 20%**

**The Liquidity Trap**:
- Steinhardt not merely misjudged **markets' direction**; misjudged **liquidity**
- In **boom years of early 1990s**, hedge funds encountered **no difficulty establishing vast positions in European bonds**
- Bought up **about half of Germany's government and government-guaranteed bonds**
- But when shock hit and **brokers issued margin calls**, hedge funds had to **sell out in rush**
- **Everybody scrambled to sell to everybody else**
- **Liquidity was gone. Nobody was buying.**

**The Personal Crisis**:
- In old days of equity block trading, **Steinhardt knew brokers personally**
- **Europe's bond markets different game**
- Steinhardt dealing with **brokers based continent away—anonymous voices in different time zone**
- **European bond brokers didn't know Steinhardt and he didn't know them**
- When liquidity crunch came, **not willing to help Steinhardt get out of positions**

**The Desperation**:
- Seated at **bow-shaped desk, staring at seven blinking computer screens**
- Witnessed decomposition of his fund
- **Desperate sensation of not being able to catch breath** (as though drowning)
- Ate little, slept less
- Known for **explosive blowups**, now took to **conferring in whispers**
- Traders fought to get out of positions
- But **prices kept falling like stones**
- Market seemed to consist exclusively of sellers

**The Fire Sale**:
- **John Lattanzio** (veteran Steinhardt lieutenant) marched to bond trading desk
- **"Just sell 'em!" Lattanzio barked. "Just sell 'em!"**
- **"I can't,"** trader answered flatly
- Seeing no other way forward, Steinhardt resolved to **dump bonds at any price**
- By end of **four-day selling burst in early March**, traders had **dumped $1 billion in European bonds**
- **Canadian central bank** would periodically call up nervously to ask whether Steinhardt's traders were done selling

**The Aftermath**:
- Steinhardt fallen into trap that would **become well known in new leveraged markets**
- Failed to sense when trade had become ***crowded***
- Looking back, Steinhardt conceded naïveté: **"Trade in European bonds was crowded, fact that totally passed me by"**
- When dust settled end of March, **Steinhardt's funds down 30%**
- About **$1.3 billion of capital vaporized**

### Askin Capital: The Fraud Collapses

**David Askin's Pitch**:
- Steinhardt's humiliation **quickly followed by another hedge-fund collapse**: **Askin Capital Management**
- **David Askin** launched business **1993 glory days**
- Created **$2.5 billion portfolio of mortgage securities**
- Little experience actually managing money
- Pitch: as **former head of mortgage research at Drexel Burnham Lambert**, could make money from mortgages **in any financial climate**

**The Mortgage Derivatives Strategy**:
- Claimed edge in **analyzing likelihood mortgage might be paid off early**
- Wall Street busy **slicing mortgages into exotic instruments**
- Interest payments and principal payment on mortgage cut into **two separate "strips"**: **interest-only strips (IOs)** and **principal-only strips (POs)**
- Askin dabbled in **IOs, POs, inverse IOs, inverse POs, even "forward inverse IO"**

**The Fraud**:
- Askin's pitch won over **respected names** (investors: **AIG, Rockefeller Foundation**)
- Sales pitch **fraudulent**
- Askin claimed funds **"market neutral"**
- Truth: **had little idea how clever investments might perform in crisis**
- Claimed to use **sophisticated modeling** (proprietary software)
- **No such model existed**

**The Collapse**:
- When Fed raised interest rates February, **Askin's funds began to fall in value**
- Surge in long-term rates **raised cost of mortgages, removed incentive for homeowners to refinance**
- Despite claims to **market neutrality**, Askin turned out to be **acutely exposed to duration change**
- For any given rise in long-term rates, **portfolio fell five times more than ordinary bond**
- First Askin **covered up discovery by misreporting results**
- Revolt within firm forced him to **come clean** (Friday, March 25): admitted funds **fallen 20% in February**
- Moreover, **assassination of Mexican presidential candidate (March 23)** ratcheted up panic

**The Final Days**:
- Morning of **Monday, March 28**: brokers began issuing **margin calls to Askin**
- **Bear Stearns** demanded **$20 million**
- **Kidder Peabody** wanted **$41 million**
- By afternoon Bear upped request to **$50 million**
- Next day **Salomon Brothers, Lehman Brothers, host of other creditors piled on**
- Soon clear: **Askin would have to sell huge portions of portfolio**
- **Team of Kidder mortgage traders** showed up uninvited, demanded to inspect books
- **Yet another lesson about leverage**: **Not only can cause fund to crash. Can complicate burial.**

**Wednesday, March 30: The Death Throes**:
- Sharks continued to circle
- Brokers issued **ever bigger margin calls**
- **Swiss investment-management firm Unigestion** ordered Askin to **liquidate positions**
- Askin contacted **18 dealers**, most bids **so low as to be worthless**
- Two of Askin's positions (**"forward inverse IOs"**) **failed to attract any bids at all**
- **Sheer complexity of instruments made them impossible to sell**
- **Nobody on Street knew how to value them**

**The Final Lowball Offer**:
- **3:45 PM Wednesday**: **Bear Stearns** offered to buy chunk of Askin's portfolio for **paltry $5 million**
- Bear demanded Askin **answer yes or no within 15 minutes**
- Askin prevaricated, asked for **extra 15 minutes**
- Ultimately refused
- Brokers gave up hope of **negotiated asset sale**, began to **take possession of whatever collateral they held**
- **Bond market reeled again** as remains of Askin's fund dismembered

### Clinton's Intervention (March 31, 1994)

**The President's Panic**:
- Panic in bond market reached **top echelons of government**
- Morning of **March 31, 1994**: as **Askin's brokers selling life out of Treasury market**, **Bill Clinton interrupted California vacation**
- President **schooled by advisers to view bond market with special awe**
- Previous year: raised taxes on theory **smaller federal budget deficit would reduce government's demand for capital**
- **Clinton didn't love this argument**—first reaction: denounce undue power of **"bunch of fucking bond traders"**
- As **Clinton's tax hike followed by fall in market interest rates** amid bond bubble of 1993, **advisers appeared vindicated**
- **Collapse of bond market early 1994** came as **rude shock**

**Rubin's Explanation**:
- Clinton phoned **Robert Rubin** (ex-Goldman Sachs chief, top economic adviser)
- President demanded to know **what was going on**
- For about half hour, **Rubin labored to explain why long-term interest rates soaring**
- Trends in growth or inflation **could not provide answer**
- **Bond market behaving in new ways**; Wall Street **changing faster than anyone noticed**
- Fed raised interest rates just twice (25 basis points each)
- Yet **bonds falling off cliff**
- **Nobody seemed to understand exactly what was going on**: not Federal Reserve chairman, not hedge-fund gurus, not even Rubin

**Clinton's Plea for Calm**:
- President finished telephone conversation, walked out to **face gaggle of reporters**
- **"No one believes there's serious problem with underlying American economy. Some corrective things will happen from time to time, but there's no reason for people to overreact."**

### The New Leveraged Reality

**Standard Response, Wrong Diagnosis**:
- President's plea **encapsulated response to financial panics standard for generation**
- Bond market confounded Clinton's gurus
- Events as disparate as **Japanese trade negotiations and Mexican assassinations** linked in terrifying ways by **tentacles of leverage**
- Best president could do: **plead for calm** (in way that *misconceived challenge* posed by hedge funds)
- Truth: in new leveraged world, **overreaction was inevitable**
- Whole point of leverage: **investors feel ripples from economy in magnified way**
- Forced to **keep fingers on hair trigger**
- **Overreaction becomes mandatory**

**The Bond Market Transformation**:
- Rubin correct: **Wall Street changed radically**
- As leverage multiplied investors' buying power, **sheer size of bond market transformed**
- **1981**: new public issues of bonds/notes (excluding Treasury securities) totaled **$96 billion**
- **By 1993**: offerings **multiplied thirteenfold to $1.27 trillion**
- **Askin and exaggerated analytic powers**, but other Wall Street firms **hiring physicists, equipping with supercomputers**
- **Small wonder government's power to influence and even understand markets was waning**
- **Deepening of bond markets weakened governments' ability to anticipate shifts in long-term interest rates**

### Two Policy Tracks: Monetary Policy and Financial Regulation

**Track One: Monetary Policy**:
- First: **monetary policy**
- Fed chairman conceded **bond market's reaction had been shock**
- Three weeks into mayhem (February 28): **Fed's interest-rate committee convened by conference call**
- **Alan Blinder**: lamented **bond market's capricious behavior**, denouncing exaggerated power of **"twenty-seven-year-olds in yellow suspenders"**

**Should Fed Target Asset Prices?**:
- Fed could have chosen to **redefine inflation-fighting mandate**
- Traditionally set interest rates **with view to keeping consumer prices stable**
- Question raised by **bond market collapse of 1994**: concerned **stability of asset prices**
- If bond market heads into record territory, shouldn't this be signal **credit too cheap**—time to **raise interest rates to deflate bubble**?
- Because Fed **targeting inflation rather than bond market**, allowed bubble to expand
- Then **Steinhardt blew up; Askin blew up**; more than **$600 billion knocked off value of U.S. securities**
- Another **$900 billion or so wealth destroyed in foreign bond markets**
- Over next decade, **Greenspan wrestled with this question**
- Ultimately deciding **using monetary policy to deflate bubbles = bad idea**
- **Bubbles hard to identify**; besides, even if central bank allowed bubble to expand, **better to clean up after it popped**

### Regulatory Response to the 1994 Bond Crisis

**Fed's Clean-Up-Afterward Philosophy**:
- Fed Chairman **Alan Greenspan** defended post-crisis cleanup over preemptive action
- After mid-2000s credit bubble, this approach proved disastrously costly
- Case for considering asset bubbles in monetary policy and requiring financiers to restrain leverage was belatedly vindicated

**Congressional Hearings on Hedge Funds**:
- March 1994 bond meltdown triggered regulatory scrutiny
- **Representative Henry González**, chairman of House Financial Services Committee, vowed hearings
- *Business Week* called hedge funds "rogue elephants: overleveraged, undersupervised, and disruptive to the markets"

> "Hedge funds are rogue elephants: overleveraged, undersupervised, and disruptive to the markets." — *Business Week*

**Robert Johnson's Counterattack**:
- **Robert Johnson**, former Soros economist, was now González's staff director
- Johnson sensed lawmakers were badmouthing industry without understanding it
- Johnson organized hearings to force lawmakers to articulate concrete proposals

**The April 13 Hearings**:
- Regulators had no concrete plans to stop imploding hedge funds from damaging creditors
- Resorted to "plan B": assert no action needed
- **Eugene Ludwig** (comptroller of currency): only eight national banks lent to hedge funds, most loans secured by collateral
- **Fed governor John LaWare**: banks and brokers wouldn't be foolish enough to lend excessively

**Soros Testifies**:
- **George Soros** was sole witness in second session
- Johnson's calculation proved right: House staff lacked firm grounds for treating hedge funds aggressively
- Soros lectured on reflexivity, advertised his book
- Soros: leverage could destabilize markets, but restrictions should apply to all financial actors—starting with big brokerages—not just hedge funds

> "Nowadays, the term is applied so indiscriminately. There is as little in common between my type of hedge fund and the hedge fund that was recently liquidated, as between the hedgehog and the people who cut the hedges in the summer." — George Soros, referring to Askin's implosion

**Regulatory Response Fizzles**:
- Clinton administration officials debated options but didn't push for action
- **William McDonough** manned phones to save Bankers Trust but did nothing afterward to rein in leverage
- When **Long-Term Capital Management** failed four years later, 1994 crisis "looked trivial"

### Michael Steinhardt's Exit

**The 1994 Losses and Retirement**:
- **Michael Steinhardt** flirted with retirement for years: "I don't feel what I do is profoundly virtuous"
- After bond-market humiliation, resolved to exit
- His nerve was shattered—frightened to take risks, temper went from bad to horrible
- Fired employees at lunchtime and invited them back next day
- Hung on through 1995, turning in surprisingly good **27% return** and recouping **$700 million** of losses
- Announced long-rumored retirement

**Steinhardt's Legacy**:
- Watershed moment: after 27 years, one of the **Big Three** was exiting
- **$1 invested with Steinhardt in 1967 would be worth $480 at closing (1996)**—**26x more than S&P 500 index**
- 1994 bond crash cost Steinhardt's funds **$1.5 billion**, but he'd earned more than twice that between 1991-1993

> "I've made my investors and myself more money than I ever conceived of as a kid." — Michael Steinhardt

- Retirement gave him time to dance with **Martha**, the elegant blue crane on his country estate

### Industry Response to 1994 Crisis

**Investor Withdrawals and Media Proclamations**:
- By September 1994, investors had pulled roughly **$900 million** from hedge funds
- Financial magazines noted indignantly that hedge funds weren't actually hedged
- *Forbes* proclaimed: "The hedge fund party is over"

**Macro Funds Return Capital**:
- **Paul Tudor Jones** handed back a third of his capital to investors
- **Bruce Kovner** returned two thirds in June 1995
- Both cited difficulty maneuvering with too much capital
- Macro investing contemptuously dubbed "leveraged directional speculation"

**Industry Resilience**:
- Similar backlash had buried hedge funds in early 1970s
- By mid-1990s, industry was more resilient: with exception of Steinhardt, famous funds forced to shrink but carried on
- Ecosystem of smaller players evolved with rich range of investment styles
- Average hedge fund decline in Q1 1994: **2.2%**—less than **3.3% decline** for average equity mutual fund
- Hedge funds had beaten S&P 500 over past five years and were considerably less volatile
- University endowments and sophisticated investors recognized hedge funds could diversify risks

**Two Verdicts on Hedge Funds**:
- **Regulators**: confronted worrisome questions but lacking theory of how to tame industry, ultimately chose to look away
- **Institutional investors**: reached critical verdict that despite 1994 turmoil, hedge funds promised irresistible risk-adjusted returns
- The two verdicts were effectively one: because markets aren't perfectly efficient, hedge funds raise difficult issues
- But by same token, market inefficiency allowed hedge funds to do well, and investors would line up to get into them
## Chapter 9: Soros Versus Soros

### Tiger's Palladium Play

- **Noril'sk**: mining city in Siberian Arctic built by Stalin with slave labor; perhaps 200,000 **zeks** (political prisoners) died building it
- By mid-1990s: 260,000 inhabitants, −40°C temperatures, sulfurous smog from Dickensian smelters
- 1990: **Paul Tudor Jones** and other hedge funders vacationed at fishing camp on nearby **Ponoi River**
- Before 1990, westerners barred from the **Kola Peninsula** — visitors resolved to buy the camp
- When negotiating fishing rights, heard story at Helsinki meeting about Noril'sk's creaking infrastructure on verge of collapse
- **Thorpe McKenzie** (former Robertson partner at Tiger) picked up his ears: mine contained more than half the world's palladium — breakdown would have global consequences
- McKenzie researched palladium: three uses — dentistry (stable), catalytic converters (growing due to environmental regulation), cell phones (new)
- Other major palladium suppliers in Africa faced own infrastructure challenges
- McKenzie concluded demand would outgrow uncertain sources of supply
- Bought metal for own account, passed word to Robertson
- By 1994 Tiger had bought $40 million worth of palladium

### Dwight Anderson's Siberian Investigation

- **Dwight Anderson**, young Tiger commodities specialist, dispatched to investigate
- Turbocharged young men Robertson hired had appetite for adventure
- Flew to Moscow (emerging as go-to town for Western deal makers), then ancient Aeroflot aircraft to Siberia
- Touched down in Noril'sk in half-light of bleak November day
- Airport littered with wrecked aircraft carcasses plundered for spare parts to keep rest of fleet going
- Anderson surprised next day at how well mine was working
- Unlike Appalachian coal mines or deep southern African shafts, Siberian approach was truly Soviet in scale
- Could drive underground in vast truck and tour bewildering warren of caverns
- Excavation scale exceeded anything needed to extract ore, but excess was a blessing
- If one tunnel or trolley system developed problem, several alternative ways to get ore out
- If mechanical drill broke, two defunct ones nearby could be plundered for spare parts (like abandoned planes at airport)
- Anderson concluded stories of imminent collapse were deliberately exaggerated
- Local officials understood rumors were bullish for palladium prices — since they all had stakes in mine's sales, seized every chance to tell visiting foreigners about imminent production breakdown
- Russians selling more palladium than they mined — ripping metal out of disused Soviet military equipment
- Plundering would have to stop eventually — by year 2000, no more missiles to scrap, palladium price would skyrocket
- Tiger held on to position even though logic for owning it had changed
- Position lost money for next three years, but then things got interesting

### Hedge Funds and Emerging Markets

- Before 1990: westerners barred from the **Kola Peninsula** — fishing or mine inspection alike
- East Asia and Eastern Europe allowed only a trickle of foreign money into equity/bond markets (too small for much opportunity)
- Around the turn of the decade, emerging markets came into their own
- Outstanding stock of developing-country bonds shot from ~$40 billion to $100 billion in one year; blistering growth continued through rest of decade
- Traditional asset managers warmed to peripheral economies gradually, but hedge funds moved fast — by early 1990s, filling coffers with exotic names like Peru and reaping healthy profits
- 1992: **Soros** and **Druckenmiller** launched **Quantum Emerging Growth** to take advantage of new frontier
- 1993: **Louis Bacon's Moore Capital** followed with specialized emerging-market vehicle
- Benefits: hedge funds willing to provide capital when others were not; once hedge funds led the way, other Western asset managers followed
- Foreigners generally sophisticated in capital choices — presence frequently boosted quality and quantity of capital
- Risks: could provide capital and juice growth for years, then yank money out and cause growth to crater
- As Britain discovered in 1992 and **Alan Greenspan** in 1994, deep and fast-moving capital markets could force wrenching movements in currencies and interest rates
- End of 1994: **Mexico** succumbed to full-blown currency crisis — dress rehearsal for far bigger drama conceived at Soros Fund Management

### Quantum's Performance and the Size Debate

- Under **Druckenmiller**: Quantum up 63% (1993), 4% (1994), 39% (1995)
- Sterling trade showed bulk could be weapon in facing down government but also showed Quantum was unnecessarily large
- Given fund's size, should have sold $15 billion sterling, but only able to sell $10 billion
- If fund had been one third smaller, would have aimed to sell $10 billion and actually sold that amount — percentage gain would have been third higher
- **Druckenmiller**: stellar performance number was main goal — earnings tied to it, ego invested in it
- **Soros**: calculation different — ego invested as much in size of empire as scale of returns
- Owning global firm made him global player, raising profile of writings and philanthropy
- "Known as only private citizen to have his own foreign policy"
- Reveled in fact that eminent statesmen beat path to his office

> "Would you like a word with him?" Soros asked Druckenmiller when Henry Kissinger was visiting. "Does he know anything?" Druckenmiller countered dismissively. "Oh, yes," Soros answered, finding way to vaunt political connections while not seeming in awe of Kissinger's star power. "I don't like him, but he does know things."

- Compromise: Soros Fund Management continued accumulating assets, but split among funds
- By 1996: just over half of $10 billion in three other funds:
  - **Quantum Emerging Growth**
  - **Quasar** (invested with outside hedge-fund managers)
  - **Quota** (managed by **Nick Roditi**, secretive London-based macro trader)
- Soros recruited **Arminio Fraga**, Princeton-trained Brazilian economist, just left deputy governor of Brazil's central bank
- When two men met in early 1993, Soros offered partnership within days
- Druckenmiller eyed Fraga skeptically: "How am I supposed to run this place when I can't choose who works for me?"
- Soros: "Fine. Let's see if he's any good."
- For next four years at Soros, Fraga performed benign function: financing emerging economies shunned by traditional investors
- Bought bonds of big Latin countries (Brazil, Venezuela), branched into exotica like Moroccan loans, bought absurdly cheap Brazilian utility shares

### Thailand Crisis: The Setup

- Late 1996: Fraga attended talk by **Stan Fischer** (IMF number two)
- Mood mostly upbeat: Mexico's currency had recovered, emerging markets booming
- Somebody asked: "Who do you think is the next Mexico?"
- Fischer: "I'm not sure there's another one out there at the moment. But I do see some imbalances in Asia. That might be interesting to look at."
- Comment "put a little light in my mind"
- Weeks later, Fraga read joint IMF-Federal Reserve paper **"The Twin Crises"**: laid out in terrifying detail how currency collapse could interact with collapse of banking system
- Casting mind back to Fischer's remarks, Fraga approached Druckenmiller: "Do you mind if I go and take a look at what is going on in Asia?" — "Sure. Go."
- January 1997: Fraga landed in Thailand — country fitted double-crisis model perfectly
- Thailand's exports hammered by rise of China as low-cost rival; currency linked to basket dominated by strong dollar
- Running large trade deficit but refusing to adjust — consuming more than it produced, paying difference with loans from foreigners
- Arrangement left Thais exposed: if foreigners tired of lending, country would have to export enough to cover import bill and repay outsiders
- To boost exports and cut imports, baht would have to fall sharply
- 1996: major Thai bank collapsed, raising doubts about wisdom of lending; central bank cut interest rates, dampening returns for foreign creditors
- Fragile banks and reliance on foreign capital coming together as IMF-Fed paper described
- Clear to Fraga this interaction could turn toxic: if withdrawal of foreign money drove Thailand to devalue, banks would be tipped from fragility into outright ruin
- Much foreign lending denominated in dollars, channeled into real estate and projects generating revenue in baht — if dollar jumped, debts impossible to service

### The Bank of Thailand Meeting

- Fraga, **David Kowitz** (Soros Asian equities expert), and **Rodney Jones** (Soros Hong Kong economist) granted audience with high-ranking central bank official
- Invoking own experience as deputy governor of Brazil's central bank, Fraga offered thoughts on Thailand's dilemma
- On one hand, government committed to defending exchange rate (keeping interest rates high)
- On other hand, wobbly banks made devaluation and lower rates attractive
- Fraga had mild manner; Brazilian background helped — seemed more like benign emerging-market peer than menacing Wall Street predator
- Official looked at Fraga and gave answer that was honest and naive
- Until now, he said simply, Thailand had accepted whatever interest rates needed to maintain peg
- But now priorities might have to shift: given growing bank troubles, getting rates down might matter more than defending currency level
- Official had conceded peg was unsustainable — shorting baht was no-brainer
- Fraga and colleagues could visualize suitcase full of money, cash spilling from seams
- To reel in prize, had to pretend they hadn't noticed it
- If host realized full power of his comment, could snatch suitcase back — central bank could hike rates or resort to administrative crackdown on foreign speculators
- Ever polite and self-effacing, Fraga nodded pleasantly and allowed discussion to move on
- Kowitz gently led conversation backward: "Excuse me. I'm out of my depth here. Can you just repeat what you said a few minutes ago, just to make sure I got it right?"
- Official repeated statement — Soros team got confirmation; host had confessed and reconfessed his nakedness

### Druckenmiller Approves the Trade

- Late January: Soros team sold short ~$2 billion worth of Thai currency
- Government sold reserves and raised interest rates 3 percentage points
- Soros team locked in baht loans at low rates before hike — could wait until July
- Why didn't Druckenmiller go bigger? Sterling trade was 2.5x capital; Thailand only a fifth
- Could have sold additional $23 billion of baht — but had limited appetite for emerging-market aggression
- Soros liked to say markets could swing like wrecking ball, laying waste to economies
- **Rodney Jones** challenged morality of speculation in developing countries: millions of innocents forced into poverty
- Defense: speculation could benefit poor societies as signal, not sledgehammer — alert governments to need for change
- In Thailand's case, signaling could avoid hardship: the more government procrastinated, the more brutal eventual collapse

### Thailand's Disastrous Defiance

- Rather than devalue sensibly, Thailand threw away foreign-currency reserves buying baht from Druckenmiller
- Thai press got wind of Jones's visit, spooked jittery local investors
- Jones visited late April: counted hundred cranes on Bangkok skyline, almost none working
- 87 out of 90 finance companies insolvent
- Loans-to-GDP ratio: 40% in late 1970s crisis → 140% now
- May 11: PM **Chavalit** appeared on TV vowing to support baht, then admitting he couldn't promise success
- Druckenmiller increased bet from $2 billion to $3.5 billion
- **Paul Tudor Jones** spoke with Druckenmiller several times daily; **Tiger** built $2 billion short
- Day Druckenmiller increased: Bank of Thailand used $6 billion of reserves

### Capital Controls and Counterattack

- May 15: Thai authorities forbade banks from lending baht to foreigners
- Tiger forced to renew short-term baht loans at vastly higher rates — cost hit $10 million *per day*
- Three weeks after second strike: Thai currency gained 10%, hedge funds booked ~$500 million in losses
- Regional newspapers reported gleefully hedge funds losing "battle of the baht"
- Prime minister called central bank to promise staff a victory party
- Druckenmiller cut position from $3.5 billion to $3 billion

### Jones Discovers the Real Reserve Position

- Central bank reported reserves of $30 billion — but had sold vast quantities in forward markets, not on balance sheet
- Maneuver fooled IMF inspectors and Thai government itself
- **Jones** pieced together: Bank of Thailand used $21 billion of reserves in May alone — two thirds of war chest
- Jones worked out anguish in memo: **"The Economics of Deflation: What Keynes Would Say to Thailand"**

> "For a country to choose sky-high interest rates in preference to devaluation was 'sheer lunacy.'" — Rodney Jones

- Invoked Keynes's reflections on folly going back to A.D. 274 (Emperor Aurelian's deflationary misery caused rebellion and 7,000+ deaths)
- Clamping down on baht loans to foreigners as effective as blocking up one hole in showerhead: only accelerated pace capital whooshed out through other openings

### Thailand Capitulates (July 1, 1997)

- Tuesday, July 1: **Tiger** unleashed $1 billion barrage of baht selling — Bank of Thailand ran out of reserves
- 4:30 A.M. Bangkok time: officials conceded no more ammunition
- Baht fell 32% over next three months
- Soros funds gained ~$750 million; Robertson gained ~$300 million
- Thailand's output collapsed 17% from peak, destroying businesses and plunging millions into poverty
- July 1 was also the day Britain ceded Hong Kong — "a new kind of imperialism"

### Soros in Hong Kong: Dual Persona

- Late September 1997: Soros flew to Hong Kong for World Bank/IMF meetings
- Malaysia's **Mahathir** called him "criminal" and "moron," called for ban on "unnecessary, unproductive and immoral" currency trading
- But Soros the statesman-philanthropist was toast of meetings — addressed packed auditorium

> "The main enemy of the open society, I believe, is no longer the communist but the capitalist threat."

- Soros wanted to be thinker, statesman — not neoimperialist and smasher of small currencies
- Told lieutenants time for shorting Asian currencies had passed
- Even though Mahathir's outburst triggered new sell-off, Soros not in mood to listen

### Indonesia: Soros's $400 Million Loss

- Soros team bought ~$300 million worth of Indonesian rupiah, believing turmoil spilled over without justification
- Indonesians had let currency fall 11% in August — rebound might be in offing
- After Fraga-Jones visit in October, increased to ~$1 billion
- **Tiger** dumped rupiah end of October; Soros team followed IMF consensus
- November 2: IMF announced $33 billion credit line — rupiah rallied briefly
- But cronies around ailing **Suharto** frustrated reform: corrupt banks impossible to close, central bank printing money for cronies
- December: Suharto rumored seriously ill; rupiah down 44% from early November — trade cost Soros $400 million
- **Rodney Jones** got data confirming central bank was printing money for Suharto's cronies — Soros team still stuck
- In aftermath of 1987 crash, Soros dumped positions as soon as they went wrong — but this time team seemed paralyzed
- Finance minister **Mar'ie Muhammad** dispatched to reassure foreign investors, met Soros team at Plaza Hotel
- Muhammad spent years as respected technocrat; now defending program in which money printed to pay Suharto's friends
- Placed in impossible position, Muhammad refused to meet their gaze, mumbling through evasive answers

> "Oh my God," Druckenmiller said after meeting. "I can't believe that we are long. I don't believe anything that guy said. I don't even believe he's from Indonesia."

- **Kowitz** suggested using rupiah to buy physical commodities like iron — Soros said "That's an interesting idea" in thick European voice; nobody followed up
- Not even when Indonesia's government, unable to print money fast enough, released plastic souvenir banknotes as legal currency

### South Korea: Jones's Prescient Call Goes Unheeded

- Mid-November 1997: **Jones** discovered South Korea's hidden crisis
- Korean banks financed 50+ Thai companies now going bust
- **Bombshell**: central bank misleading markets like Thailand — official reserves $57 billion, real number closer to $20 billion
- Dollar debts understated by **$60 billion**, much maturing within weeks
- Jones memo of November 17 warned Korea "in the late stage of the crisis"
- Central bank reserves falling $1 billion per day, down to $9 billion
- December 3: IMF record $55 billion bailout — inadequate, private sector obligations more than twice that size
- Won fell 60% from level at Jones's memo
- **Soros team took no action** — no won short
- Boss wanted to be statesman, not wrecker of nations

### Soros as Savior in South Korea

- January 1998: Soros traveled to Korea as guest of president-elect **Kim Dae-jung**
- Camera crews at airport; dined with Kim; visited industrialists; breakfasted with **Michael Jackson** (plotting to take over theme park from bust Korean underwear maker)
- Promised Quantum would invest if Korea reformed
- **KOSPI index** jumped 25% in ten days
- Asked about contrast between Mahathir and Kim: **"One of them must be wrong"**

### Russia: The Messiah Complex

- Since 1987, Soros ran **Open Society Institute** in Moscow — textbooks, grants to scientists
- **István Rév** (Hungarian historian): Soros drawn to Russia by same forces that fascinated Napoleon — "Its vastness, its historical challenge, its backwardness, its perpetually unfulfilled promise"
- Made it principle not to invest in Russia — didn't want philanthropy seen as Trojan horse
- Allowed Druckenmiller and team to take positions, but kept personal distance
- End of 1996: President **Yeltsin** reenergized reform; hedge-fund managers jetting to Moscow, going to Bolshoi Opera
- Portfolio investment increased from $8.9 billion (1996) to $45.6 billion (1997) — 10% of Russia's GDP
- Russian equity index nearly tripled in first nine months — hottest emerging market
- **Spring 1997: Soros cracked** — took astonishing $980 million gamble in **Svyazinvest** (Russia's state telephone utility)
- Went over heads of Druckenmiller and colleagues
- Joined consortium bidding for 25% of sprawling state-owned telecoms utility
- Russia had 19 phone lines per 100 people vs. 58 in U.S. — undeniable potential
- But $1 billion stake in state company not something you could dump easily if politics turned bad
- Entailed type of risk that seemed crazy to most foreigners — even amid torrent of portfolio investment, foreign direct investment never more than a trickle
- Also secretly lent Russian government several hundred million dollars so Yeltsin could pay back wages
- Had he not done so, Yeltsin's brittle legitimacy might have cracked, unpaid workers might have rioted

> "I deliberately chose to expose myself. To be a selfless benefactor was just a little too good to be true. It fed my self-image as a godlike creature... I have talked about my messianic fantasies; I am not ashamed of them." — Soros

- Svyazinvest victory was Pyrrhic — losing oligarchs owned newspapers/TV, released smear stories, three officials resigned
- Asia crisis contagion hit Russia: banks pulled loans, oil prices crashed
- April 1998: annualized GKO yield hit 30%; May: 70%
- Soon half the hedge funds in New York salivating over three-month bonds with double-digit yields
- West not going to let nuclear power like Russia default and descend into chaos (the argument went)
- **June**: Goldman Sachs underwrote $1.25 billion issue of Russian bonds — sold out within an hour

### Anderson's $4 Billion Metal Deal

- **Dwight Anderson** offered to buy Russia's **entire stock of nongold precious metals** — palladium, rhodium, silver
- Tiger would pay $4 billion, sell gradually, share profits (eventual ~$8 billion for Russia)
- Metal transported by armored train from Siberia, flown to Swiss vault
- Anderson flew to Moscow in July to meet senior central bank official; GKO yields jumped from 70% to more than 100%
- Anderson and counterpart got along well; Yeltsin apparently knew and approved
- Anderson felt deal of lifetime almost in bag — Soros and Napoleon could salivate over Russia's limitless romance, but Anderson about to show single hedge fund could buy treasure of country
- Then message from Moscow: **commission required** — two contracts, one official, one secret (payment to Cyprus bank)
- Russia's parliament and people would only see official version
- Anderson: "It's got to be one contract" — transparent, standing up to public scrutiny
- Silence from Moscow, then: "What about Gibraltar?"
- Anderson had to explain himself again — didn't matter if Cyprus, Gibraltar, or other haven; had to be disclosed, had to be one contract
- Messages bounced back and forth until clear agreement was impossible
- For Russians: no earthly reason to sell without private commission
- For Anderson: no way to salvage deal of the decade

## Chapter 10: The Enemy Is Us — Long-Term Capital Management

### LTCM: Origins and Culture

- Bank of China building in Hong Kong: heavy and squat, then face-lift from **I. M. Pei** (1990)
- Emerged as soaring, daring, giddy thing: slim stiletto-heeled goddess on catwalk
- Pei explained triangular towers evoked bamboo — through miracles of modern leverage, institutions growing as quickly as bamboo
- Thanks to same leverage, new financial palaces thin sided and hollow — unexpected sideways blow could topple them
- September 1997: **LTCM** threw party at Bank of China building — epitome of new financial engineering Pei's structure evoked
- Small team earned $2.1 billion the previous year
- Announced return of $2.7 billion to outside investors — in new world of leverage, didn't need client cash
- Could be ambitious and slender, like I. M. Pei creation
- Founder **John Meriwether**: one of first to see potential in financial engineering
- At **Salomon Brothers**, transformed trading group into "quasi-university environment"
- Recruited **Eric Rosenfeld** (Harvard), **Larry Hilibrand** (MIT), **Robert Merton**, **Myron Scholes** (future Nobel laureates)
- Mid-1980s: most Salomon partners had not gone to college, much less PhD program
- Firm's trading culture personified by **Craig Coats Jr.** — tall, charismatic, believed model for hero in *Bonfire of the Vanities*
- Coats ran government-bond trading old-fashioned way: firm belief in own instincts
- Remarkably quickly, superiority of Meriwether's professors became obvious
- By end of 1980s: small quasi faculty accounted for 90% of Salomon's profits
- Coats left after big loss; Meriwether elevated to vice chairman
- **Hilibrand** campaigned to shutter fancy internal catering, even suggesting investment banking division closed entirely — "what was purpose of 6,000 other employees?"
- Secret deal: professors got fixed 15% of their group's profits — "hedge fund within bank"

### LTCM Launch (February 1994)

- **Hilibrand** suggested closing investment banking entirely — "what was purpose of 6,000 other employees?"
- Push bore fruit in secret deal: to keep rocket scientists happy, Salomon granted fixed 15% of their group's profits
- By securing guaranteed performance fee, Meriwether created hedge fund within bank — creating hedge fund instead of bank merely step away
- 1991: Treasury bond scandal — trader **Paul Mozer** cheated repeatedly in auctions
- Meriwether (responsible for overseeing Mozer) resigned, paid $50,000 SEC fine
- Resolved to create **"Salomon without the bullshit"** — functions like marketing, clearing, settling outsourced
- No need to spread professors' trading profits through undeserving back-office departments
- February 1994: launched Long-Term Capital Management
- 8 members of Salomon brain trust joined; leased space at 600 Steamboat Road, Greenwich
- Same building as **Paul Tudor Jones**; showed up in golf shirts and chinos
- Bluefish jumping in Long Island Sound — quants would arm themselves with fishing rods and race out in hot pursuit

### October 1997: Nobel Prize and Expansion

- **Merton and Scholes** won Nobel Prize for option-pricing model
- LTCM expanded into equity arbitrage — not stock-picking but mathematical clarity
- Favorite: **Royal Dutch/Shell** — Dutch and British shares on same profits, British at discount
- Collected more dividends than paid out; hedged so indifferent to share price movement
- Biggest equity bet: sold options betting on market steadiness — **"central bank of volatility"**
- Five-year S&P 500 options priced at 19% implied volatility vs. typical 10-13%
- Value-at-risk: max likely daily loss $116 million; max 21-day loss $532 million
- Would take **ten-sigma event** (1 in 10^24) to put them out of business
- Twice-weekly risk meetings gamed out Wall Street crash or Japanese earthquake scenarios

> "We thought we were being conservative." — Eric Rosenfeld

### May-June 1998: First Warning Signs

- IMF bailout of Indonesia faltered; Japan in first recession since 1974; Russia tripled interest rates
- LTCM lost 6% in May, 10% in June — way beyond value-at-risk calculations
- Single percentage-point rise in S&P volatility cost $40 million; expected volatility jumped from 19% to 26%
- July: recovered, partners relaxed — dismissed spring as freak misfortune

### August 17, 1998: Russia Defaults

- LTCM had little direct Russia exposure, but indirect effects devastating
- **High-Risk Opportunities** hedge fund went bankrupt; rumors Lehman might fail
- Treasury-swap spread widened by eight basis points in one day (typically moved less than one)
- By end of Friday August 21: LTCM lost **$550 million total, 15% of capital**
- Partners on deferred vacation: **Meriwether** in China, **Rosenfeld** in Idaho
- Skeleton crew in Greenwich watched screens in wonder
- LTCM's confident assumptions burning: correlations now in lockstep, value-at-risk off by $400 million
- Most fundamentally: believed in corrective power of arbitrage — portfolio burning, traders waited in vain for market to spring back

### The "Pogo Moment": Reverse Arbitrage

- Russian blowup caused all main arbitrage players to lose money simultaneously
- Arbitrageurs lacked muscle to correct anomalies — faced margin calls forcing them to dump positions
- **Reverse arbitrage**: unwinding of each portfolio damaged all others — self-reinforcing cycle
- Meriwether's old Salomon group closing positions hammered LTCM
- Every trade went wrong in correlated way — not economically similar but similar in type of fund holding them
- **Rosenfeld**: failure to anticipate trader-driven correlation was central error
- LTCM's success created special vulnerability: army of imitators had pieced together strategy
- Superportfolio of disciples was even larger — **nobody to sell to**
- Like central bank — its trading presented predators with one-way bet

> "Where are you?" / "We're down by half." / "You're finished." / "What are you talking about? We still have two billion." / "When you're down by half, people figure you can go down all the way. They're going to push the market against you." — Vinny Mattone and Meriwether

### Scrambling for Capital

- By Labor Day: three out of four funds had lost money; LTCM lost 44% of capital ($1.9 billion)
- Calculated loss should occur less than once in lifetime of universe
- Rosenfeld phoned **Warren Buffett** to buy $5 billion merger-arb portfolio — refused
- Meriwether breakfasted with Soros/Druckenmiller: Soros offered $500 million if Meriwether raised another $500 million
- Approached **Prince Alwaleed**, **Michael Dell**; called **Herb Allison** (Merrill boss)

> "John, I'm not sure it's in your interest to raise money. It might look like you're having a problem." — Herb Allison

- Buffett reacted by ordering lieutenants to monitor Berkshire Hathaway's hedge fund exposure
- Salomon issued similar edicts — ceased extending loans to almost all hedge funds
- More LTCM scrambled to raise money, more panic spread — running on moving carpet

### September: Wall Street Turns Predator

- Meriwether reported losses in investor letter September 2 — reaffirmed faith in arbitrage, writing of "this unusually attractive environment"
- Letter leaked before last copy faxed; *WSJ* ran front-page story next day — whole world knew LTCM on deathwatch
- Every Wall Street player started trading against it
- Most junk bonds rallied early September, but bonds LTCM owned remained dead
- LTCM's hurricane bonds (securities selling hurricane risk to insurers) plummeted 20% — though hurricane probability utterly unaltered
- Europe: gap between government bonds and market interest rates widened in Britain and Germany for no fundamental reason besides Long-Term's betting

> "It's not about the market anymore; it's about you. If you're long, they're short. If you're short, they're long." — Jim Rickards, general counsel

### Goldman Sachs: Inspection and Alleged Front-Running

- Goldman demanded half of LTCM management company + full access to fund's strategies for $1 billion investment
- LTCM tried to get nondisclosure signed — Goldman refused
- Goldman's inspectors allegedly plugged oversized laptop into LTCM network and downloaded positions
- Goldman denied it, but proprietary trading desk soon selling positions resembling LTCM's
- Goldman trader in London: "If you think a gorilla has to sell, then you sure want to sell first"
- Corzine: "We had to protect our own positions. That part I'm not apologetic for"

### Federal Reserve Steps In

- Meriwether and partners decided time to inform **Federal Reserve** that failure was possibility
- **William McDonough** (head of New York Fed) on his way out of town
- Fell to **Peter Fisher** (number two) to wade into crisis
- Sunday, September 20: Fisher hitched ride in assistant's Jeep from New Jersey to Greenwich
- Expert financial plumber, unflappable, had way of cutting through complexity with simple propositions
- As Fisher listened to Long-Term's positions, no neat solutions came to mind
- LTCM so leveraged, held sprawling portfolio worth $120 billion — only beginning of challenge
- What rattled Fisher: portfolio included **extraordinarily concentrated bets**
- Figured Long-Term's position in British government bond futures ("gilts") might represent half of open interest in that market
- Similarly outlandish position in Danish mortgages; portfolio of equity options enormous
- Issue not simply LTCM's collapse causing broad market fall — Fisher worried **particular markets might cease trading altogether**
- According to Long-Term's calculations, seventeen biggest counterparties stood to lose $3 billion among them if wound up immediately
- If holdings liquidated in rush, losses considerably bigger
- Fisher presumed big Wall Street players had similar trades on own books — damage magnified by hit to shadow portfolio
- Speedy and costly liquidation seemed horribly likely
- Past financial collapses: regulators could take charge of remaining assets, avoid fire sale
- But Long-Term different: **hedge fund had no assets for regulators to seize** — all pledged as collateral
- If LTCM defaulted on one loan agreement, cross-default clauses would automatically trigger
- Closeout agreements would flutter from thousand fax machines; ownership transferred to brokers, which would dump rapidly
- Fisher confronted problem that would haunt regulators next decade: firms entangling themselves with dozens of partners can be too complex and intertwined to be buried easily

### Monday: LTCM Loses $550 Million More

- Markets tumbling; television commentators speculating Clinton's Monica Lewinsky deposition would rattle investors
- Fisher found this grimly amusing — truth was word was out about LTCM's rescue attempts
- Salomon reported Goldman "banging the shit" out of Long-Term in Tokyo
- Goldman reported Salomon doing same in Europe
- Attacks on equity options so ferocious that options prices implied crash every month
- End of Monday: lost $550 million, one third of equity — capital sunk below $1 billion for first time

### The Fed-Brokered Rescue

- Fisher stayed in touch with Goldman, Merrill, J.P. Morgan bosses — hoped for private-sector rescue
- Clear lone bank unable to perform role safely — purchaser would have to sell some holdings; knowing this, others would continue selling
- Solution: **consortium to purchase portfolio** — each would get piece small enough to hold; hyenas would stop feeding
- Hitch: traditional rivalries seemed to preclude alliance — unless Fed brokered one
- Fisher invited heads of big three banks to breakfast at Fed Tuesday morning; all three favored joint action
- By end of day: converged on view that money would have to be injected into Long-Term
- Lifting bits of portfolio out wouldn't work — LTCM's cat's cradle of trades too complicated
- Price tag rising: August needed $1 billion; three weeks later $2 billion; now **$4 billion injection** to stabilize
- Each bank had incentive to free ride — let rivals shoulder cost
- Fisher convened sixteen main counterparties Tuesday evening — impossible to conclude deal
- Blueprint: sixteen creditors kicking in $250 million each; inevitably smaller banks complained
- Around 11:00 P.M., with no resolution, Fisher suggested break until 10 A.M. next morning
- Wednesday negotiations no easier: **Lehman Brothers** pleaded balance sheet too fragile; two French banks offered only $125 million each
- Biggest shock: **Bear Stearns** (Long-Term's lead broker, earned millions in fees) adamantly refused to put up a single dollar
- **Philip Purcell** (Morgan Stanley): Bear's free riding "not *acceptable*"
- **David Komansky** (Merrill Lynch) exploded in Cayne's face
- Discussions grew so ugly Fisher began gaming out impasse consequences

> "I remember thinking, 'If this thing isn't going to work, I'm going to have to stand on the table and say, Don't touch your cell phones.'" — Peter Fisher

- Wednesday: eleven banks agreed to $300 million each; total **$3.625 billion**
- Meriwether and partners saw personal fortunes reduced to almost nothing

> "We lost money the old-fashioned way: through honesty and hard work." — Jim Rickards

### Post-LTCM: Lessons Unheeded

- Congressional hearings October 1; **Rep. Kanjorski** suggested enemies could harm U.S. by partnering with hedge funds

> "Individuals who lend money to others have a very important interest in getting that money back." — Alan Greenspan

- **Greenspan** fell back on idea private creditors would check excesses — didn't concede flaw until 2009; even then presented as though crisis of 2007-2009 came out of blue
- Good reasons for Greenspan's diffidence: clamping down on hedge funds wouldn't have ended dangerous trading
- Could not simply announce cap on leverage: ratio of borrowing to capital almost meaningless number — failed to capture whether portfolio hedged or exposed to risks via derivatives
- Could not cap value-at-risk: LTCM shown this measure misleading
- Frustrating truth: risks depended on constantly changing conditions — whether others mimicking trades, how liquid markets were, whether banks suffering from compromised immune systems
- Fed's **Peter Fisher** could see theoretical case for controls but seemed so unlikely government would get details right that he never went for action
- **Basel II** capital requirements (1998-2004) chewed up six years, ended in abject failure — deferred to banks' own risk models — "banks should self-regulate"
- European banks that adopted Basel II proved hopelessly fragile in 2007
- If regulators tried to control leverage of hedge funds and shadowbanks, might have done no better than Basel standards
- Culprits in 2007-2009 crisis: leveraged off-balance-sheet vehicles owned by banks, leveraged broker-dealers, leveraged insurer (AIG) — hedge funds were not the villains

> "We do not have the choice of accepting the benefits of the current system without its costs." — Alan Greenspan

- LTCM showed craziness of insuring world against volatility without capital reserve — AIG repeated same error over next decade
- LTCM exposed fallacy of diversification reducing risk to zero — repeated with mortgage securities
- Most fundamental: LTCM provided object lesson in dangers of leveraged finance
- World's response: not just to let leveraged trading continue, but **tolerate vast expansion**

## The Tech Bubble

### The Bubble as Test of Hedge Fund Philosophy

- Tech bubble tested two competing views: sophisticated traders enforce efficiency, or traders lack muscle and prefer riding trends
- If markets needed efficiency-enforcing arbitrage, the dot-com bubble was the ultimate test
- **Books-A-Million** jumped tenfold in three days after announcing website improvements (Nov 1998)
- **Priceline.com** gained 425% on first trading day — untested airline ticket site worth more than United, Continental, and Northwest combined
- Social justification for highly paid managers: they enforce efficient pricing and allocate capital to best uses, not to bubbly start-ups with bad business plans

### Tiger vs. the Bubble

- Tiger and Quantum had ~$20 billion at peak — tiny vs. $5 trillion NASDAQ cap
- Hedge funds too small to deflate bubble alone; heavy losses during waiting period could trigger redemptions
- **Robertson** declined to fight tech bubble in 1999 — lost money on shorts in 1998

### Tiger's Death Spiral

- Summer 1999: Tiger lost 7.3% first half; tech-heavy mutual funds up 25%+; day traders in kitchens outperforming Robertson's special-forces unit
- Tiger lost 17% in Q3 even as momentum surfers reaped extraordinary profits
- Yen losses (fall 1998) plus latest setbacks strained investor patience
- Net $3 billion withdrawn in six months to March; another $760 million end of Q2
- Hemorrhaging of investors continued: end of September, net $1.3 billion more yanked away
- More investors pulled out → more Robertson forced to liquidate holdings
- Once Wall Street knew Tiger was forced seller, predatory instincts returned
- Robertson reported Tiger's top ten stock positions monthly in investor letters — rivals front-ran him
- **June rumor**: Tiger facing $3 billion withdrawals at month-end triggered predatory sales spasm — Tiger lost $72 million in fifteen minutes on steady market day
- Unlike LTCM (brokers calling in loans), Tiger's danger was investors calling in equity
- In both cases, widespread knowledge of holdings contributed to troubles — lesson for those advocating hedge-fund transparency: publicity can destabilize markets
- Tiger fell from $21 billion peak (Aug 1998) to $9.5 billion just over a year later — $5 billion decline due to clients voting with wallets
- Not just money — Tiger was his family, personal network of power brokers and friends
- He'd run special-forces unit, led team-building excursions out west — not used to defeat or ignominious rout
- Low moments: Robertson called old lieutenant **John Griffin** (now running own hedge fund) — "John, can you believe that I got a letter from so-and-so who has been in Tiger since 1982? He says he's buying a house. He needs his money back. He's just lost faith in me."
- Robertson announced investors could redeem twice yearly rather than four times — mild restriction compared to later lockups, but backed down after protests
- Investors protested: proved loyalty keeping money through terrible year, why be chained to *Titanic* now? — Robertson conceded they had point
- Next generation learned: reduce mismatch between yankable capital and illiquid investments by permitting less frequent withdrawals
- Unable to lock in money, forced to sell — became sitting duck
- Rivals knew he had to unload, went short in anticipation
- Aug-Oct: rumors of Tiger's unraveling mounted
- **US Airways** short sales rose from 1.6 million to 3.8 million shares, driving battered stock down another tenth
- **Federal-Mogul** short sales leaped from 3.7 million to 6.5 million shares, forcing stock down 80%
- Robertson sold ~$40 billion of stocks and $60 billion of other positions over past year
- Some considered it a miracle Tiger was still standing
- **Philip Duff** (Tiger COO): "How many financial institutions could have a $100 billion downsizing of their balance sheet? Few of those would probably survive."
- End 1999: Robertson's tone telegraphed the end was coming
- Pleaded with investors — great technology can change lives without generating profits
- Pointed out managers of Tiger portfolio companies buying stock back (regarded as cheap); tech firm managers eagerly selling stakes in own enterprises
- "We are in wild runaway technology frenzy: meantime most other stocks are in a state of collapse"
- December: "I have never seen such a dichotomy. There will be a correction."
- Jan 2000: uncorked frustration at "irrational public" — "This is the public that in the month of December drove the unprofitable companies in the NASDAQ up some 38 percent. There has to be a day of reckoning."
- Day of reckoning came March 10, 2000 — NASDAQ crested, air whooshed out of bubble
- Too late — Robertson had made his decision to exit, too beaten to change it
- March 30 (NASDAQ already 15% off peak): broke news to investors
- After months assuring light at end of tunnel, confessed sick of waiting
- Rational valuation measures taken backseat to "mouse clicks and momentum" — no stomach for more punishment
- Market stayed irrational longer than he stayed solvent (Keynes warning)
- Time to bring curtain down on Tiger

### David Swensen: The Endowment Pioneer

- **David Swensen** of **Yale**: tall, angular, ascetic, cerebral — "stiff-backed midwesterner"
- Possessed above all by fierce sense of moral purpose
- Grew up River Falls, Wisconsin; founded recycling club through church group; mother and sister Lutheran ministers
- Ambition: follow state's progressive tradition, be elected to Senate
- After enrolling Yale's economics PhD program, took different turn
- Befriended future Nobel laureate **James Tobin**
- Got to know **Salomon Brothers** (provided market data for dissertation)
- Developed passion for finance and Yale environment
- 1980: Salomon immediately hired Swensen after completing doctorate
- Helped make financial history: played role in creation of first **currency swap** (deal between IBM and World Bank, 1981)
- 1982: lured to **Lehman Brothers** to run bank's fledgling swaps desk
- 1985: former professors lobbied him to take over Yale's troubled endowment
- Accepted happily, gave up investment-banking bonuses for book-lined office on campus (pay cut 80%)
- Years later, Wall Street admirer remarked he could have been billionaire running hedge fund
- "What's the matter with you?" / "A genetic defect"
- When Swensen arrived, more than four-fifths of assets in US stocks, bonds, cash — only one-tenth in "alternative investments"
- Resembled most other college endowments — to young man returning from innovative Wall Street world, seemed tame
- **Tobin**: portfolio diversification = one free lunch in economics
- Modern financial system: diversification should mean more than broad mix of US bonds and equities
- Assets like foreign equities, real estate, private equity, oil, gas, timber offered ways to add equity-type returns while diversifying risk substantially
- Swensen's interest in **"absolute return"** (over next years term entered investment lexicon, became synonym for hedge funds)
- Moralist in Swensen had no desire to help hedge-fund managers earn fortunes — but economist impressed by design of hedge-fund incentives
- Performance fees meant managers didn't get paid just for scale
- But recognized performance fees alone encourage too much risk — sought managers with own savings in funds
- What really interested Swensen: scale and source of hedge-fund profits — promised equity-sized returns uncorrelated with market index, offering free lunch of diversification

### Tom Steyer and Farallon

- 1987: two years after Swensen assumed helm at endowment, received visit from Yale alumnus at homecoming football game
- Visitor said he had small fund on West Coast; perhaps Yale might invest?
- Swensen and deputy **Dean Takahashi** listened to pitch: "We're not interested. We'll never be interested."
- The supplicant: **Tom Steyer**, hedge fund called **Farallon**
- Steyer: sweeping red-blond parting, jaunty sideburns, faded woven wristbands
- Though grown up in New York, exuded vibe of adopted home San Francisco
- Ebullient, funny, comfortable in own skin — could fill conversation with mental and athletic juice, running with ideas like soccer star
- Came equipped with another quality appealing to Swensen later: acute sense of right and wrong, colored everything from lifestyle to business approach
- Founded **Farallon** 1985; long after building it into one of world's biggest hedge funds, renowned for beat-up car, habit of flying commercial, utter indifference to fashion
- Office: desk in middle of open-plan hallway; behind it breathtaking panorama of San Francisco (but Steyer kept blinds down)
- Motivated partly by desire to escape Wall Street for West Coast life, partly by sense of justice
- Young analyst at **Morgan Stanley**: upset to discover investment-bank advisers paid for being wrong; sounding convincing mattered more than actually being right — objective simply extract fees from clients
- After stint at Stanford business school, worked at **Goldman Sachs** merger-arb unit under **Robert Rubin** (future Treasury secretary)
- Suited him better: Goldman got paid only when Goldman right (though distribution of profits among employees sometimes generated arguments)
- Way Steyer saw things, setting up independent fund = logical next step
- Had begun at firm that took no responsibility for bad investment calls
- Moved to firm that took responsibility collectively but didn't always recognize individual's contribution
- Now by starting freestanding fund, would be out on own — no buffer between quality of investment calls and rewards
- Rented cheap space in downtown San Francisco with couple desks
- Would ride elevator at 5:30 A.M., clutching coffee and doughnut, ready to analyze merger action at start of NY trading day
- When takeover bid announced, stock in target would move most of way to bid price — example: trading at $30, bid for $40, shoots to $38
- Presented Steyer with choice: if bought stock and merger consummated, pocket $2/share; if called off, forfeit $8 — knowing whether to risk $8 to make $2 required special skill
- Steyer pursued work with competitive passion that sometimes seemed overboard
- After taking losses in 1987 crash, started showing up at 3 A.M., accompanied by wife who feared for his stability
- Despite hit in 1987, did extremely well — buying target companies in deals that would be consummated, eked out profits month by month
- By shorting acquiring firms, hedged out risk from general market movements
- Toward end of 1980s, expanded horizons — partly survival strategy: takeover boom skidded to halt when junk-bond market collapsed 1989, leaving merger arbitrageurs with few mergers to analyze
- But also playing offense: junk-bond collapse created opportunity to apply analytical skills in different context
- Companies at center of junk-bond market filed for bankruptcy one by one
- Pension funds, mutual funds, other institutional investors = forced sellers (rules forbade holding bonds of companies in default) — compelled to concede bargains to nimble players like Farallon
- When **Drexel Burnham Lambert** (kingpin of junk-bond market) filed bankruptcy 1990, Steyer bought large slice of debt at cents on dollar
- When sold stake 1993, Farallon's portfolio chalked up 35% profit
- With Drexel transaction: scored dazzling double (profited from mergers made possible by Drexel's bonds, profited again from Drexel's implosion)
- Created what became known as **"event-driven" hedge fund** — specialized in events causing existing prices to be wrong, moments when disruption suddenly rendered market's settled view inoperative
- Moment before takeover bid: company's share price embodies verdict of investors who projected future earnings (price efficient, analyzed to death)
- Moment after takeover bid: old calculations scrambled — opportunity for those who can analyze quickly
- Swensen: "Great investors tend to have a 'screw loose,' pursuing the game not for profit, but for sport"

### The Fairman Incident and Yale Invests

- Even before Drexel coup, news of Steyer's performance reached ears of **David Swensen**
- Steyer making excellent money irrespective of whether stock market up or down — offered diversification
- To financial economist attuned to limits of efficient-market hypothesis, Steyer's success did not look merely lucky
- Two factors enough to make Swensen reconsider initial refusal to invest in Farallon
- Before went further, Swensen had to take measure of Steyer the man — wanted partners with integrity, wanted something more
- Beating market only possible for people with sort of obsessive passion
- Yale did due diligence, found Steyer had all qualities endowment could hope for
- Guy not running hedge fund because craved luxury (just had to look at office)
- Steyer insisted Farallon employees keep liquid savings in fund so would feel pain if lost money
- Also embraced convention of **"high-water mark"**: if fund was down, would take no further fees until earned money back for investors
- Fall 1989: Swensen flew to San Francisco, visited Farallon's scruffy office, approved of what he saw
- Over cheap lunch with Steyer and partner **Fleur Fairman**, Swensen repeated earlier verdict
- Hedge funds would stiff clients if strategies went wrong — rather than working without compensation to earn capital back (as high-water mark promised), hedge funds would simply close shop, reopen under new name with fresh set of investors
- "Look, the reason we don't want to do this honestly is in this format, if you lose money, you won't want to earn it back. You'll close down and start a new fund. That's the problem with the whole format."
- Steyer might have argued back, but Fairman beat him to it
- "That's a bunch of bullshit!" she exclaimed — Swensen could see she was furious
- "If you think that's who we are then we don't want your money anyway! You have no idea who we are! It's just ridiculous that you'd say that!"
- Better response than Swensen could possibly have wished — found integrity he sought
- Fairman took decency so seriously she flew off handle when questioned
- January 1990: Yale invested $300 million in Farallon (total capital to $900 million) — kick-starting gradual change in social impact of hedge funds
- By 1995: Yale allocation to hedge funds jumped to 21%
- July 2000-June 2003: S&P 500 lost 33% while HFR hedge fund index gained 10%; Yale up 20%
- Swensen's decisions generated **$7.8 billion** of Yale's $14 billion endowment
- By 2009: roughly half capital in hedge funds came from institutions, not individuals

### Hidden Risks in Event-Driven Funds

- Colorado episode exposed vulnerability in Farallon and ambitious bargain-hunting funds generally
- **Bargains lurk in quirky places**: junk-bond details, postcrisis Indonesia, rancher/farmer feuds in remote Colorado valley
- To invest successfully, need to understand terrain traps — young hedge funds sometimes lack manpower to survey adequately
- If Farallon spent more time in San Luis Valley, might have realized Gary Boyce was unsatisfactory partner
- In fast-growing fund (doubling assets every 4-5 years), hard to grow in-house expertise as fast as incoming capital
- Event-driven returns partly reflect willingness to buy **illiquid investments** — if you make a mistake, you can't get out
- In ordinary liquid markets: prices fairly efficient, second-guessing hard; in illiquid markets: bargains plenty but mistakes extremely costly
- Accounting quirk: illiquid assets don't have continually updated prices — funds **estimate** profits, potentially **smoothing** volatility
- Hard to know what illiquid asset is worth — lack continually updated price discovery from constant trading
- In some cases, funds may exaggerate returns (game not sustainable)
- Even honest funds "smooth" results: estimate illiquid asset value every few weeks; if rises 5% then falls back, recorded simply as flat — sharp volatility unreported
- Result: risk-adjusted returns look wonderful because some risk goes unreported
- In crisis: illiquid assets collapse hardest — investors crave easily sold securities, shun rest ruthlessly
- LTCM's apparently diverse portfolio concealed single bet that world would be stable — when wrong, unrelated positions collapsed simultaneously
- Many positions boiled down to premium for holding illiquid assets
- Event-driven funds may be taking concentrated bet on illiquid investments
- 1998: LTCM paid ultimate price for this risk; 2008: buyers of illiquid assets paid heavily again

### Tudor's Quantitative Trading Journey

- **Paul Tudor Jones** invested in computer-trading projects — early systems named **Madonna** and **Material Girl**
- Hired **Sushil Wadhwani** from Goldman (1995) — economist who taught at LSE, served on Bank of England monetary committee
- At Goldman, learned limits of pure economic thinking by rubbing shoulders with real traders

> "Identifying an illogical price anomaly was only the start of a trader's thought process; the next step was to identify a trigger."

- Triggers: upcoming election, psychological tipping point in charts, institutional investor behavior changes
- Wadhwani's mission: build machine that mimicked discretionary traders' eclectic thinking
- Created naive model combining economic indicators, institutional cash positioning, options signals
- Watched Jones and **Mark Heffernan** ("greatest discretionary trader in Goldman Sachs empire") trading, probed them on reasons for their moves
- Refined model by observing how they combined familiar factors in different ways
- Position sizing: calculated "z score" — higher confidence of winning = larger bet
- Model needed flexibility in *type* of trades, not just sizing
- In turbulence, weight sentiment measures (options market) more heavily; in recession, negative data points have larger impact
- Needed multiple programs: LTCM-style arbitrage for stable times, trend-following for unstable times
- Ideal: devise ways to shift between strategies automatically
- Left for Bank of England 1999 before fully building vision
- First version: **Techno-Fundamentals**, trend-following system, ran successfully from late 1997
- By 2008: more than fifty people working on computerized trading at Tudor, algorithms driving $3+ billion of $17 billion capital
- But systems not as original as **Jim Simons's Renaissance** — Tudor hired from financial establishment where others likely developed similar strategies
- Handful of prized experts moved among handful of firms — each move reduced odds any firm would build truly unique system

- Hired established scientists, not young quants or Wall Street veterans

### Peter Brown and Robert Mercer: Computerized Translation

- Came from **IBM's research center** in 1993; drove Medallion Fund success
- **Brown**: magnesium flare — slept five hours/night, riffed passionately on topics, rode unicycle around office
- **Mercer**: icy cold poker player, never recalled having nightmare, IBM boss called him automaton
- Before Renaissance, worked on cryptography
- Upended computerized translation: traditional programmers taught computers grammar rules
- Brown-Mercer: didn't speak French, got **Canada's parliamentary records** (thousands of paired French/English passages), told computer to figure out correlations
- Computer searched patterns: English word initially equally likely translatable into any of 58,000 French words; after checking paired passages, nearly 99% uncertainty eliminated
- Couplings emerged: *lait*/milk, *pourquoi*/why
- Other correlations softer — multiple algorithms needed, combing data repeatedly

> "Only in this way can one hope to hear the quiet call of *marqué d'un asterisque*/starred or the whisper of *qui s'est fait bousculer*/embattled."

- Results outclassed competing systems by wide margin
- Reception at translation experts meeting: "People were shaking their heads and spurting grunts of disbelief or even of hostility"
- "Where's the linguistic intuition?" — "Yes that's the point; there isn't any"

> "Every time I fire a linguist, my system's performance improves." — Fred Jelinek, IBM

- Used "expectations maximization algorithm" invented by **Leonard Baum** (same Baum who worked for IDA then Simons)
- Within years, statistical machine translation celebrated as intellectual revolution
- Lesson: computers better suited to learn without paying attention to rules — don't need grammar crutch

### Implications for Medallion

- **Tudor/Wadhwani**: trained machine to approach markets in manner making sense for humans
- **Brown-Mercer**: trained themselves to approach problems in manner making sense for computer
- **D. E. Shaw**: start with market theories, test against data (like teaching computer grammar)
- **Brown-Mercer**: feed data first, let computer find answers (like code crackers without grammar book)
- False pattern danger: computer may find fake ghosts — patterns existing by chance with no predictive value
- **Eric Wepsic** (statistical arbitrage at D. E. Shaw) example: **Super Bowl** — used to be said if original NFL team won, market would head upward
- As statistics, relationship might hold; as common sense, meaningless coincidence
- Dangerous to trade on statistical evidence unless intuitively explained
- D. E. Shaw in 1990s: detected curious correlations between cable, media, consumer electronics firms — once realized correlations reflected technology euphoria pushing into all these industries, seemed more tradable
- Signals based on intuition have further advantage: understanding why they work helps know when they might cease
- **Mercer** echoes Wepsic's wariness but adds crucial twist:
- "Some signals that make no intuitive sense do indeed work"
- "The signals that we have been trading without interruption for fifteen years make no sense. Otherwise someone else would have found them."
- It is the nonintuitive signals that often prove the most lucrative for Renaissance

- **Big Meeting** (Tuesday mornings): 90+ PhDs gathered; every refinement began with presentation, peer review, then Small Meeting
- **Brown and Mercer** decided green light; **Henry Laufer** retained veto right
- Pay tied to profits of entire firm, not subunit — collaboration reinforced
- Brown and Mercer created system allowing multiple programmers to work simultaneously (repeated IBM trick)
- Researcher could adapt in-house programming language to express new idea — neologisms useful in computing as in speech
- More finance went global, more statistics from foreign markets fed into system
- More business went digital, more data available — e-commerce sales, Web-surfing habits
- Old days: track stock price trade by trade; now see each bid and offer, including those never consummated

### The Russian Defection Crisis (2003)

- Renaissance instructed applicants: if joined, could never work elsewhere in finance
- Researchers required to invest 1/5 of pay in Medallion, locked up four years after departure
- Helped that firm based in quaint East Setauket — once kids in local schools, didn't want to go anywhere
- Recruited Soviet prodigies from **Institute of Physics and Technology in Moscow**
- Kids raised in failing political system likely to assume authority is corrupt — person's only obligation: look out for himself
- Kids separated from parents more likely to mature as hardened individualists
- Two Russians presented ultimatum: refused noncompete, demanded higher pay
- Simons refused; Russians left for rival hedge fund
- Remarkable: **Medallion's performance continued leaving rivals in dust**
- Like magician who drinks poison and survives
- Infrastructure as important as research: takes enormous time/money to set up systems absorbing trillion bytes daily
- While Russians struggling to create comparable platform, faculty at Renaissance moving on — each Tuesday brought new Big Meeting, fresh ideas

### Medallion as Formula One Race Car

- Fund closed to outside investors in 1993; by 2000s consisted almost entirely of employees' money
- Short-term signals: can't get too large (liquidity constraints)
- But existence had halo effect on industry — offset Long-Term Capital damage to black-box trading reputation
- 2005: launched **Renaissance Institutional Equities Fund** designed to absorb $100 billion
- Only way huge amount manageable: branch from short-term trading into more liquid longer-term strategies
- By summer 2007: raked in $25+ billion — one of world's largest hedge funds

## Chapter 14: Premonitions of a Crisis — Amaranth

### The Alpha Factory Bubble

- Scale and persistence of hedge funds' success transforming industry structure
- First generation seen as freakish geniuses with possibly lucky, certainly not reproducible returns
- By 2005: more than 8,000 hedge funds; old talk of luck and genius faded
- New lingo: at conferences from Phoenix to Monaco, consultants held forth about scientific product called **alpha**
- Alpha could be explained: strategies like merger arbitrage or statistical arbitrage delivered uncorrelated, market-beating profits in understandable, replicable, manufacturable way
- Era of manufacturer arrived — innovation and inspiration gave way to new alpha factory
- Opportunistic consultants staged workshops on how to open hedge fund; *Hedge Funds for Dummies* appeared
- Grandees with no known background in asset management (such as **Madeleine Albright**, former secretary of state) jumped into industry
- Frenzy recalled extremes of leveraged-buyout boom in 1980s or dot-com mania in 1990s
- Robertson's "Tiger seeds": after closing Tiger in 2000, turned offices into incubator for protégés
- Managed his money, benefited from coaching, used prestige of association to raise more capital
- Under old model Robertson maintained personal control; now let protégés run own shows — switched from inventing technique to franchising it
- Switch provided lucrative final chapter to illustrious career — by 2006 reinvented Tiger complex managing $16 billion
- **Multistrategy hedge funds**: purest factory expression — collected multiple alpha-generating strategies under one roof
- Blended together to diversify away risk; shifted capital among various styles according to market conditions
- Factories talked little about invention, a lot about process — viewed hedge funds less as vehicles for financial creativity than as financial products
- **Citadel** (Chicago) emerged as prime exponent: "to see if we can turn the investment process into widget making"
- **Ken Griffin**, Citadel's thirtysomething boss, keen consumer of management texts
- Staff sneaked glances at tomes on desk so they could brace for next six-step plan
- Pushed people out of company with mechanical determination
- Liked comparing hedge funds to buses: People get on, people get off, bus keeps rolling forward
- Griffin started trading convertible bonds from Harvard dorm room; by start of 2000 still running about $2 billion
- Age of manufacturer arrived, Citadel took off — assets swelled to $13 billion by 2007
- Could charge clients almost anything: expenses amounting to more than 5% of capital before 20% performance fee
- Let it be known Citadel would one day compete with Goldman Sachs and Morgan Stanley
- **Eton Park** (launched 2004 by ex-Goldman merger arbitrageur **Eric Mindich**): raised $3 billion before opening doors, managed $11 billion four years later
- Powerful logic to rush to bigness: small companies may excel at generating ideas, big companies excel at implementation
- Once hedge-fund industry progressed through garage-workshop phase, took sleek professional outfits to bring inventions to market
- State-of-the-art computers executing lightning trades, legal departments understanding rules in multiple countries, treasury departments negotiating best terms from brokers
- By 2007: funds managing $5 billion+ accounted for 60% of industry assets

### Amaranth: Rise and Fall

- **Nick Maounis**: convertible-arb specialist, launched September 2000
- Shape-shifting: merged from merger-arb → convertible-arb → bond trades → stat-arb → energy
- First three years: 22%, 11%, 17% returns
- Hollowness beneath appearance: Maounis had no clear edge in deciding which strategy to shift into — upped allocation to styles that worked recently

### Brian Hunter: The Star Trader

- Young Canadian, 6'5", **Calgary Flames** jersey, graduate math degree
- Earnest, soft-spoken, unfailingly calm
- Spotted anomaly: winter gas options strangely cheap — classic asymmetric trade
- Unlike oil (shipped in tankers), natural gas delivered in pipelines — supply routes can't easily change to fill shortages
- Loaded up on options: most he could lose was small cost of buying; if shock hit, could earn many times more
- From **Deutsche Bank** where trading group lost $51 million in one week (December 2003)
- Hunter's response nothing if not brazen: ascribed loss to "unprecedented" price moves and Deutsche's "well-documented" software problems, demanded bonus anyway
- Argued he personally earned $40 million for bank during 2003
- Deutsche fired him February 2004 — Amaranth promoted him

### Hurricane Katrina (2005) and Hunter's Ascent

- August/September 2005: **Katrina** and **Rita** devastated gas production
- Amaranth down 1% first half on sluggish strategies; up 21% by year-end (average hedge fund: 9%)
- Hunter and gas trades earned **$1.26 billion** — all of Amaranth's profits
- Hunter reportedly pocketed a tenth ($126 million)
- Assets swelled to ~$8 billion
- Christmas mailing: toy gasoline pumps, quoting Ben Franklin: "Energy and persistence alter all things"
- Seldom had one of Founders been taken so out of context

### The Risk Problem

- On any reasonable reckoning, Katrina and Rita constituted freak events
- Whereas Hunter ready to blame unforeseeable extremes for Deutsche Bank losses, happy to take credit when unforeseeable extremes made him hero
- Maounis grew increasingly enamored of young star — seemed to view Hunter as convertible arbitrage trader transported to different space
- Generating profits while taking little risk (no way winter gas would fall below summer gas price — potential losses appeared limited)
- Risk team only reinforced conviction — member assured Maounis Hunter was "greatest commodity trader he had ever witnessed"
- In April 2005, Maounis learned Hunter offered $1 million bonus to sign on with rival **SAC Capital**
- Feeling back against wall, Maounis took chance: promoted Hunter to cohead of energy desk, gaining authority to place own trades
- Pumped energy desk allocation from 2% to ~30% — effectively bet company on thirty-two-year-old trader barely one year with firm
- Hunter commuted to Calgary office in Ferrari (though sometimes snowy conditions forced use of Bentley)
- January-April 2006: trades earned ~$2 billion, driving nearly all Amaranth performance
- At time when average multistrategy hedge fund up just 5%, Amaranth up roughly six times more
- Maounis began to say that although failed to strike gold in statistical arbitrage, discovered another secret weapon just as potent
- End of February: Hunter held astonishing 70% of November 2006 NYMEX gas futures, about 60% of January 2007 contracts
- By around end of April: Amaranth owned upward of 100,000 NYMEX contracts — more than 40%+ of total outstanding for all months
- **Hunter was momentum trader who traded on his own momentum**
- His own money driving profits — spread between summer/winter gas widened from $1.40 to $2.20 as Hunter built positions
- Not clear strategy making money because market fundamentals on his side — by early 2006, gas output recovered from hurricane devastation; mild winter weather reducing demand
- By April: quantity of gas held in storage nearly 40% above previous five-year average
- Small market; main buyer of Hunter's positions was Hunter himself — nobody to sell to if needed out
- May 2006: team from **Blackstone** visited Hunter in Calgary — Blackstone ran one of longest-standing funds of funds, had $125 million invested, now having second thoughts
- Amaranth's risk department calculated most Hunter could lose in single month was $300 million (tolerable 3% of equity)
- But Hunter's own trading rendered assessment obsolete — plenty of room for spread to shrink disastrously
- Blackstone informed Amaranth it would withdraw capital at next opportunity; happy to pay penalty fee for short-notice redemptions
- Maounis finally reckoned with fact star trader overreached — told Hunter to cut back
- Easier said than done: nobody wanted to buy Amaranth's contracts

### Amaranth's Collapse (September 2006)

- May: down over $1 billion, nearly 4x risk department's estimate
- Late July: Hurricane rumors let Hunter jack up bets, triggering collapse of rival **MotherRock**
- August: Maounis gave WSJ interview praising Hunter's risk management; one veteran compared faith to "that of a jilted lover"
- **Thursday, September 14**: lost **$560 million in a single day**
- Saturday: investment bankers descended on Greenwich office, devouring Pop-Tarts in pantry
- Goldman and Merrill made offers, but **J.P. Morgan** torpedoed Goldman deal over clawback rights

### Ken Griffin and Citadel Step In

- Griffin working out at home when email arrived: Amaranth down 50% in a month
- Thought of Vinny Mattone's advice: "If you are down by half, you are not going to recover"
- **Charles Winkler** (ex-Citadel, now Amaranth COO): "We need a bridge loan and a couple hundred million"
- Griffin sent two lieutenants to Greenwich — "looked half the age of investment-banking rivals"
- **Bob Polachek** worked through night checking brokerage statements — no undiscovered holes
- 5:30 A.M.: sale went forward; J.P. Morgan and Citadel took ownership
- Citadel eventually earned **~$1 billion profit** — Griffin's plan to build next Goldman Sachs had taken significant step forward
- Amaranth's collapse confirmed hedge funds had entered **bubble territory** — grown too fast for available talent
- Under pressure to perform, funds granted inexperienced traders leeway to blow up spectacularly
- **Multistrategy format** made this danger especially acute — veterans like Druckenmiller and Bacon understood risk because they traded daily and their own savings/reputation at stake
- New alpha factories structured differently: believed in delegation — boss couldn't be expert in every risk
- Once risk decisions delegated down the chain, multistrategy funds contended with mild version of problematic incentives plaguing large banks
- Traders want bonuses; bonuses won by betting big; central risk department seldom controls wizards who acquire aura of invincibility
- **$6 billion of investors' equity** gone — larger than Long-Term Capital incineration
- Result: American taxpayers suffered no harm; no Fed crisis meeting; no damage to financial system
- Market had disciplined Amaranth while inflicting minimal damage on wider economy
- Hedge funds might occasionally ignite fires, but they could also be firefighters (Citadel's lightning purchase proved this)

> "What Brian is really, really good at is taking controlled and measured risk." — Nick Maounis, WSJ interview, one month before collapse

## Chapter 15: Riding the Storm

### Daniel Sadek and the Subprime Machine

- **Daniel Sadek**: born Lebanon 1968, scarred by civil war gunshot
- Landed in California at 18 — gas station, car salesman
- Discovered could get home-loan license without classes (1,500 hours needed for barber)
- By 2005: **Quick Loan** had 700 employees, top 50 subprime lender
- Marketing: "No income verification. Instant qualification!!"
- Bankrolled movie **Redline** — "action flick loaded with cars, chrome, and silicone"
- Cast demolished $2 million of Sadek's sports cars during filming
- Movie tagline: "Fear nothing; risk everything"

### Kyle Bass: Betting Against Sadek

- **Kyle Bass** (Dallas hedge fund): heard about Sadek's filmmaking — confirmed suspicion
- Between 2000-2005: volume of subprime loans **quadrupled**
- Sadek's attitude summed up by movie: catapulting Porsche Carrera GTs — "That's the guy you want to bet against"
- Bass and **Hayman Capital** figured out how mortgages were packaged into bonds — shorted large quantity

### Michael Litt's Epiphany

- **Michael Litt** (FrontPoint Partners): visited mortgage desk at Lehman Brothers
- New gigantic trading floor; traders teasing buddy — tailor measuring men for $6,000 suits, guy ordered only one
- On plane from London, read BIS report explaining how sophisticated finance suppressed volatility
- At 40,000 feet, looking at Greenland: volatility low because world awash with wild money
- Rebalanced FrontPoint's portfolio to get ready for shock

### John Paulson: The Perfect Short

- **John Paulson**: neither tall nor short, handsome nor plain, eager nor standoffish — "Mr. Average"
- Harvard Business School 1980 → management consultancy → **Odyssey Partners** (early hedge fund) → **Bear Stearns** (mergers/acquisitions)
- 1994: launched tiny hedge fund in Park Avenue building incubating several hedge-fund start-ups
- Grew capital from $2 million → $600 million (2003) → $4 billion (2005)
- Only 7 analysts on staff; amassed personal fortune in quietest way possible
- Loner and contrarian — no doubt of own ability, no need for affirmation
- Specialized in **merger arbitrage** focused on long odds: bet against mergers expected to be consummated (collecting modest premium), or in favor of ones where market might be shocked by surprise bid
- Also made money **calling turns in the cycle** — when economy booming, looked to go short, and vice versa
- Skill lay in detail of positions: **capital-structure arbitrage** started from fact various bonds issued by company might be treated differently in bankruptcy
- **Senior bonds**: first claim on company's remaining assets, paid back first
- **Junior bonds**: make do with whatever left over
- Long as company healthy, investors didn't focus on this dull legal nuance — senior and junior bonds traded at similar prices
- But if economy weakening, opportunity arose: short junior bonds that would get hit most
- Sometimes hedged position by going long senior bonds
- **Paolo Pellegrini**: tall, elegant Italian with heavy-framed glasses and perpetually amused twinkle — "I'm a romantic type"
- Spent years chasing fruitlessly offbeat ideas; when signed on as Paulson's analyst for financial companies (2004), realized for first time unconventional style was welcomed

### Mortgage Securities: The Dream Target

- Paulson felt economic cycle getting ready to turn downward
- Bonds bought at discount during previous recession now trading at silly heights
- Financial markets frothy thanks to long period of low interest rates
- Began hunting for **best way to bet against bubble** — wanted to find American capitalism's weakest spot
- Thing that would blow up loudest/fastest if economy slowed even a little
- **Dream target** would combine all standard vulnerabilities: cyclical industry, loaded with too much debt, debt sliced into senior/junior bonds
- Experimented with shorts on car-company bonds, shorted insurance company and couple of home lenders
- Risk: these all had value as franchises — buildings, brands, customer relationships — even if collapsed under debt weight, would probably still be worth something
- If going to be contrarian, wanted to short something that could be **totally wiped out**
- **Spring 2005**: hit on right target — **mortgage securities**
- Combined every imaginable charm for short seller: cyclical, mountains of debt, division into senior/junior tranches
- Lenders like Sadek generated mortgages sold to Wall Street banks; banks rebundled into CDOs sliced into layers
- BBB tranche only 1% thick — nonpayment rate of 6% would take it to zero
- No franchise value to worry about — pile of loans worth zero is simply zero
- **April 2005**: first bet — credit default swap on $100 million BBB subprime debt
- Paid **$1.4 million/year** for insurance; if wiped out, pocket full $100 million

### Pellegrini's Research: "Zero Would Mean Zero"

- Bought largest mortgage database in country with $2 million research budget
- First discovery: so long as house prices rose, homeowners bailed out by refinancing
- Mortgage industry argued 15% annual appreciation would continue; national slumps "never happened"
- Pellegrini: adjusted for inflation, there had been national slumps in 1980s and 1990s
- If house prices merely went flat: **default rate of at least 7%**, wiping out all BBB bonds
- **"Zero would mean zero"**

### The Risk-Reward Table

- If $600 million fund spent 7% on insurance:
  - Worst case: loss of $42 million
  - 30% default: gain **341%** ($2 billion)
  - 50% default: gain **568%** ($3.4 billion)
  - 80% default (Paulson considered highly likely): gain **909%** ($5.5 billion)
- When explained to investors: few thought he had gone crazy (909%? When did that ever happen?)
- Summer 2006: set up new fund, seeded with own money

### The ABX Index: "Go for the Jugular"

- July 2006: Wall Street created **ABX** (subprime mortgage index) — easy to buy insurance in size
- ABX trader called: "What's your picture?"
- "Five hundred million" — "Done" — "Another five hundred million" — "Done"
- Next day bought another $1 billion
- Paulson thought: **this is the holy grail** — remembered Soros: go for the jugular
- **"I'll do another three billion"** — silence, then trader agreed to another billion
- By calling around banks: positions totaling **$7.2 billion**

### February 7, 2007: The Tide Turns

- **New Century Financial** postponed earnings — some borrowers unable to make first payments
- **HSBC** set aside $10.6 billion in loan-loss reserves
- Morning of February 8: ABX slid 5 points — 1% decline = $250 million profit for Paulson
- **Netted $1.25 billion in single morning** — about as much as Soros earned from sterling

> "Is this a misprint? It's 6.6 percent, right, not 66 percent?" — incredulous client

- Summer: colleague whispered something in meeting; Paulson returned beaming
- **"We just got our marks for the day. We made a billion dollars"**
- Flagship fund gained 700% cumulative by end of 2007, net of fees
- Firm generated estimated $15 billion in profits; Paulson personally pocketed $3-4 billion — "the man who made too much"
- When Paulson recommended changes to Treasury secretary **Hank Paulson's** bank bailout plan, Congress gave him Soros-level deference
- Rep. **John Tierney** (MA): "I was thinking we probably had the wrong Paulson" in charge

### Paulson's Success in Context

- Paulson's memory of Soros's injunction to "go for the jugular" was more fitting than he realized
- Subprime bubble was 21st-century version of policy errors earlier hedge funds had exploited
- 1970s: incompetent central banks stoked inflation, allowing commodities traders to ride glorious trends
- 1990s: central banks committed to untenable exchange-rate pegs macro traders attacked gleefully
- By 2000s: inflation and unsustainable currency pegs were gone — passing of these follies made way for new one
- Because inflation vanquished, central banks felt free to stimulate with low interest rates, creating conditions for asset bubble
- Because exchange rates now stable, Wall Street emboldened to take other sorts of risk, leveraging up further
- Each new era brought fresh kind of blunder, creating fresh opportunity for traders too
- Heyday of macro hedge funds might be over, but new heyday of credit hedge funds had arrived
- **John Paulson was the new George Soros**

### Why Hedge Funds Beat Banks

- **2007**: hedge funds specializing in asset-backed securities **up 1% on average** — completely dodged subprime bullet
- Hedge funds as whole gained 10%
- Banks and investment banks bought subprime by bucketful: **Citibank** rescued by government, **UBS** needed Swiss lifeline, **Merrill** sold to Bank of America

**Four reasons banks fared worse:**

1. **Capital requirements became a crutch**: AAA ratings on toxic bonds (paid for by issuers) let banks hold without much capital — regulation substituted for analysis
2. **Hedge-fund incentives**: managers have own wealth in funds; bank prop traders don't risk personal savings, stock price diluted across divisions
3. **Multiple profit centers distracted executives**: Merrill sold $70 billion of CDOs earning $875 million in fees; scramble for commissions distorted investment choices; banks stuck with billions of unsold inventory when CDO demand collapsed
4. **Culture**: Hedge funds are paranoid, live and die by returns, run by charismatic founder who can flip positions; banks are complacent with multiple revenue streams and sticky capital. After 1998: hedge funds locked up investors longer and negotiated guarantees from brokers; banks trended opposite — equity capital fell by ~1/3 between mid-1990s and mid-2000s

### Bear Stearns: The Ironic Twist

- Bear had reputation as vigilant manager of trading risks — exactly the kind of institution that would not be expected to buy poisonous mortgage securities
- But by mid-2000s, emerged as #1 packager of mortgage-backed securities on Wall Street (up from #3 in 2000)
- To keep sausage factory going, Bear bought up subsidiaries that made subprime loans directly to home buyers (both in US and Britain)
- Expansion shifted managers' attention: less focused on what mortgages might be worth than on how to create lots of them
- Devised ambitious **"10 in 10" strategy**: asset management revenues would rise to 10% of Bear's total by 2010
- Never mind that Bear's asset-management subsidiary starting from position of insignificance
- Pursuit of fee income helped seal Bear's fate — hurriedly assigned unqualified executives to launch internal hedge funds loaded with subprime
- **Ralph Cioffi**: salesman who previously worked on Bear's sales desk, peddling mortgage-backed securities to institutional clients
- His plan for hedge funds: buy those same mortgage-backed securities and leverage them by astonishing **35 to 1**
- Sort of risky bet that made sense to deep-pocketed, fee-hungry parent — would have been less likely to fly with real hedge fund
- Cioffi himself was not sort of figure who could have launched own fund easily
- As salesman, had virtually no experience controlling portfolio risk — some Bear executives argued he should not be allowed to do so
- Bear COO **Paul Friedman**: "There were a fair number of skeptics internally who couldn't figure out how this guy — who was bright but had never managed money — was now going to be running money. He knew nothing about risk management, had never written a ticket in his life that wasn't someone else's money"
- Cioffi also short on managerial ability — failed to secure approval of fund's independent directors before buying securities from other Bear divisions
- **Paperwork in such disastrous state** that law firm had to be brought in to investigate
- Bear's internal funds were product of bank culture, not hedge-fund culture
- Launched by people seeded within large firm, not entrepreneurs; raised capital with parent's brand and network; knew parent might bail them out
- Bear's non-hedge-fund internal hedge funds collapsed in June 2007; Bear felt compelled to rescue them with emergency loans — vindicating the view that deep-pocketed parents reduce vigilance
- If you wanted reason why Bear's internal funds blew up, whereas most regular hedge funds avoided subprime trap, answer was culture

## Chapter 16: The 2008 Crisis

### The Asymmetrical Payout

- Policy makers estimated 50/50 odds Lehman failure would bring calm or chaos
- From trader's perspective: 50% chance of calm meant chaos virtually certain — asymmetrical payout meant rational hedge funds would bet aggressively on collapse
- Lehman was the daisy chain of interlocking transactions — symbolism of firm that survived Depression and world wars would trigger panic

> "You knew Lehman Brothers would be kickoff for a big down move. You knew that." — Paul Tudor Jones

### Lehman's Collapse and Frozen Assets

- News leaked Sunday lunchtime; American hedge funds realized London operation would declare bankruptcy under British law — segregated accounts frozen
- Hedge-fund lawyers rushed from Hamptons weekend homes, calling outside counsels on speed dial
- **Eric Rosenfeld** (LTCM veteran): "How could they do this?"
- **Tudor Jones** had $100 million frozen in Lehman London (withdrawal request arrived one day late)

### Tudor's Emerging-Market Disaster

- Tudor allowed emerging-market credit team to build giant portfolio: banks in **Kazakhstan, Russia, Ukrainian dairy farms**
- Once Lehman collapsed: emerging-market portfolio lost ~two-thirds of value, costing Tudor over $1 billion
- Loans utterly illiquid — nobody wanted to buy at any price
- Unlike futures trading (always had "stop" to exit), these loans had no exit

> "That was the worst moment of my whole life." — Paul Tudor Jones

- "I used to always think, 'How'd these guys in 1929 lose it all?'... I see how these guys in '29 got hurt now. They had things that they couldn't get out of."
- Tudor ended down 4% for year — forced to impose gates suspending withdrawals

### The End of the Investment-Bank Model

- Lehman and rivals borrowed billions in short-term money markets, used it to buy hard-to-sell assets
- When crisis hit, short-term lending dried up instantly — everyone could see investment banks might face crunch, and fear was self-fulfilling
- Commercial banks have government deposit insurance + Fed emergency lending; **investment banks had no safety net**
- Weekend of Lehman's fate: **Merrill Lynch** CEO **John Thain** shuttled between NY Fed and meetings with **Ken Lewis** (Bank of America)
- Negotiations culminated 1:00 A.M. Monday morning: Thain agreed to sell Merrill for $29/share
- Year earlier, Merrill rebuffed Bank of America offer worth $90/share — "as if Wal-Mart were buying Tiffany's"
- Bear, Lehman, Merrill gone — only **Morgan Stanley** and **Goldman Sachs** remained
- Both relied on short-term funding + extremely high leverage — vulnerable to bank run
- Stock prices permanently at top of CNBC screen in "death watch"
- **AIG** trouble made things worse: by writing **credit default swaps**, AIG sold protection against bond defaults without holding capital in reserve
- Created crazy risk-taking incentive: locate ambitious trading operation inside well-capitalized firm, imbue traders with invulnerability sense
- AIG's credit default swaps lost billions when default likelihood spiked post-Lehman
- Tuesday, September 16: government forced to rescue AIG, lending **$85 billion**
- Day after AIG rescue: rumors Morgan exposed to AIG mess drove Morgan stock down 42% by midday
- Hedge funds clamored to get assets out, desperate to avoid another Lehman-type trap
- **John Mack** (Morgan CEO) raged against short-selling conspirators — repeat of battles Bear Stearns and Lehman waged against hedge funds

### The Short-Selling Ban

- Following Lehman's summer path, Morgan and Goldman lobbied regulators to clamp down on short sales
- Awkward demand: Morgan and Goldman were short sellers themselves, built flourishing prime-brokerage business financing hedge fund short sales
- Frenzied Wednesday aftermath: both firms worked phones
- By end of day, NY senators **Schumer** and **Clinton** calling SEC chairman to demand short-selling ban
- Thursday: **SEC chairman Christopher Cox** expressed doubts but Treasury Secretary **Hank Paulson** insisted: "You have to save them now or they'll be gone while you're still thinking about it"
- ~1:00 P.M.: **Financial Services Authority** (London) announced 30-day ban on short selling of 29 financial firms
- Goldman trading floor: ~3 dozen traders stood, placed hands over hearts, sang "The Star-Spangled Banner" (playing over loudspeaker)
- That evening: SEC banned short selling of ~800 financial companies
- Brought Morgan and Goldman brief breathing room but amounted to **frontal government assault on hedge funds' viability**
- Stock-picking funds lost hundreds of millions: "We went from playing chess to rugby at halftime" (Tiger cub complaint)
- Claim ban protected financial system was stretch — included Internet incubators, retailers
- During good times, Morgan and Goldman reveled in not being deposit-taking banks subject to Fed oversight
- **Swift U-turn**: demanded to be swept under Fed's purview for guaranteed emergency lending access
- Evening of September 21: Fed extended protection to Morgan and Goldman — investment-bank vulnerability ended

### Ken Griffin's Probability Tree

- **Citadel** did NOT get Fed protection; government kicked it in teeth via short-selling ban
- Built giant portfolio of convertible bonds, hedged by shorting stocks — ban made hedging impossible
- Griffin's calculation: Morgan Stanley 50% survival; if down, Goldman 95% follows; if Goldman fails, Citadel nearly 100% collapse
- **Citadel survival odds: ~55%**

> "That's a pretty bad day — when you realize twenty years of your work comes down to whether or not some firm that you have no influence over fails." — Ken Griffin

### Citadel's Near-Death Experience

- Convertible bond portfolio devastated — short-selling ban made hedging impossible
- Demand for convertible bonds cratered; Citadel left with shocking losses
- End of September: main funds down 20% for month; as equity base shriveled, leverage ratio went up
- Since 1990 creation, Citadel grown from nothing to $15 billion assets, 1,400 employees — now survival in question
- **Two-front war**: if cut leverage by selling convertible holdings, rivals would see desperation and start squeezing portfolio
- If did nothing, would run out of cash and unable to meet margin calls
- Additional danger: if trading partners worried about survival, would mark down estimated value of derivatives contracts, forcing Citadel to cough up cash until coffers empty
- Like brokers did to Askin Capital, Long-Term Capital, and every failing institution since
- Jettisoned assets not part of main strategies, raising capital without telegraphing distress
- Closed derivatives with other firms, replacing with exchange contracts (exchange wouldn't squeeze)
- Where not possible to close derivatives, took comfort in state-of-the-art back office — unlike many hedge funds, maintained infrastructure to track daily value of every derivative
- Better understood what derivatives worth, harder for counterparts to push marks against it

### The Quant Quake of August 2007

- Second half of July: computerized equity trading systems stopped performing well, some losing money
- Quants discovered something disturbing: signals themselves no longer working
- Programs betting on momentum fizzling; programs betting momentum would reverse fizzling; programs betting cheap stocks would outperform expensive ones fizzling — **all fizzling at once**
- Someone (or several quants) liquidating holdings after losing money on mortgages, needing to raise cash
- Forced selling drove prices against anyone with comparable portfolio

#### Mike Mendelson and AQR
- **Mike Mendelson**, ex-Goldman quant, ran **AQR** (founded 1998 by **Cliff Asness**)
- AQR managed $10 billion in hedge funds + $28 billion traditional funds
- Asness contributed to academic literature on pricing anomalies
- Late July: AQR's losses modest, but Mendelson heard another big quant fund suffered bad loss
- Erring on side of prudence, trimmed leverage
- First days of August: models started working again; whoever liquidating must have stopped

#### Monday, August 6: The Storm Intensifies
- Mendelson at routine meetings, then checked performance on BlackBerry at Subway sandwich shop
- Numbers all red and large: AQR lost **tens of millions** in past three hours
- Realized quant deleveraging happening on monstrous scale — or several quants bailing at once
- **Leverage mechanics**: if fund has $100 capital supporting $800 positions, 5% loss leaves $60 capital and $760 positions
- Leverage rockets from 8:1 to 12:1 after 5% loss; another 5% drives leverage toward infinity — capital becomes negative
- **Death spiral**: Mendelson sketched hyperbolic curve on notepad showing how leverage accelerates upward
- Only way to survive: keep leverage on flat, left-hand portion of curve
- If AQR down 5%, might have to sell almost two-fifths of positions to keep leverage stable

#### The Meltdown Spreads
- Similar dramas at other quant funds — most were not like Simons's Medallion
- Trading well-known anomalies, not esoteric secrets — "There is no E=MC² under the hood" (Asness)
- Even Simons's large institutional fund traded on understood signals; everyone read same academic papers, made same bets (momentum, value)
- Normal times: crowding didn't matter; but crowding caused panic during selloff
- **Tuesday**: even more brutal — AQR's models lost money twice as quickly, firm dumped billions of positions
- Technology made liquidation faster; bad news: rivals could liquidate just as quickly
- Every quant firing torpedoes at every other quant; rumors of funds down 10%+ or worse
- At Renaissance campus (Long Island), **Jim Simons** huddled with lieutenants, tweaking model parameters like pilot navigating hurricane
- **Cliff Asness** (AQR founder) blew up in office, smashed computer screens
- **Ken Griffin** called, now with reputation for buying corpses out of car wrecks
- Asness: "I looked up and saw the Valkyries coming and heard the grim reaper's scythe knocking on my door. I did my best to run to the light"

#### Policy Makers in Parallel Universe
- Tuesday: Fed's interest-rate committee warned about inflation risks
- Wednesday: President Bush said markets would lead to "a soft landing"
- Thursday: tone changed — **BNP Paribas** suspended redemptions from three money-market funds, citing "complete evaporation of liquidity"
- **European Central Bank** responded with $131 billion emergency liquidity
- Fed chairman **Ben Bernanke** turned office into makeshift war room

#### Mendelson Sees Light at End of Tunnel
- Greenwich, Tuesday: Mendelson calling brokers and friends — anyone who might know how leveraged funds positioned
- Business dominated by handful: Simons's $25+ billion fund, **Highbridge Capital**, **D. E. Shaw**, **Barclays Global Investors**, **Goldman Sachs Asset Management**
- Wanted to know how much big players cut leverage — if down to flat part of hyperbolic curve, selling pressure would end
- Working phones all day Tuesday, reached buddy just after midnight — guy rushed back from vacation, exhausted, blabbering, at end of tether — about to liquidate whole book
- Mendelson ticked one firm off list, hit phones again next morning
- By Thursday evening: figured only one big player hadn't cut leverage
- Guessed it might be Goldman's **$5 billion Global Equity Opportunities Fund**
- Goldman executives decided fund's positions too big to sell, so leverage rocketed up as bets hammered
- When market opened Friday: fund either liquidates (hitting other quants for fifth straight day) or gets recapitalized by parent

#### Friday Morning: The Turn
- Mendelson couldn't care less about Fed's inflation stance — looking at own trading model
- Within minutes, generating profits — performance screamed Goldman subsidiary rescued, quant liquidations ended
- AQR began to releverage as quickly as possible; more it caught upswing, more it could make up for past four days
- Following Monday: Goldman announced publicly it recapitalized Global Equity fund with **$3 billion fresh money**
- Quant quake ended as abruptly as it started
- Friday and Monday: great days for models, most quants recouped at least part of losses
- Mendelson's verdict: "A bunch of us lost a bunch of money and had a really tiring week, which sucked, believe me. But I don't think it was a big public policy issue"

### Sowood Capital Collapse (July 2007)

- **Ken Griffin** of Citadel was vacationing in France when the subprime crisis escalated
- Friday, July 27, 2007: subprime troubles morphed into broader credit crisis
- **Sowood Capital Management**, $3 billion Boston hedge fund, caught fire and faced margin calls
- Boss **Jeffrey Larson** came from Harvard endowment — had run $3 billion of Harvard's $20 billion endowment
- Launched independent multistrategy fund Sowood in 2004 (70 employees)
- Had *avoided* subprime securities — sensed default rates rising, bought "senior" bonds and shorted riskier paper (capital-structure arbitrage like Paulson's)
- Subprime losses at Bear Stearns seemed like good news, not a threat
- Early July: injected another $5.7 million of personal savings into Sowood
- Third week of July: other leveraged players deleveraging after mortgage losses began dumping non-mortgage positions indiscriminately
- **High-quality bonds dumped first** when traders deleveraged — capital-structure arbitrage logic collapsed
- Larson sought emergency capital from Harvard; Harvard deemed it too risky
- Friday morning, July 27: news of Sowood's troubles spread, traders positioned for contagious spiral
- Margin calls forced Sowood to dump corporate bonds in fire sale, hitting other bond funds and driving market down
- Self-fulfilling fears: bonds fell hard that afternoon; even after market close, Sowood continued losing money in after-hours trading
- Sunday lunchtime: Larson called Ken Griffin — recalled how Citadel bought Amaranth's trading book the previous year
- By Monday 7 A.M., Griffin and team worked through the night and bought Sowood's entire trading book
- Larson told investors: "Citadel offered the only immediate and comprehensive solution"
- Sowood's funds down 57% and 53% for month; Harvard's endowment took $350 million hit
- Deal calmed fears of position dumping; bond market recovered 4%+ that day
- One hedge fund imploded, threatening systemic fire; another (Citadel) acted as fireman

### The Deathwatch Conference Call

- First weeks of October: Citadel fought two-front war — rumors surfaced at dizzying speed
- Hit with margin calls; Fed calling trading partners asking exposure sizes
- Truth: Fed calling around telling banks not to pull loans — whether this saved Citadel or fueled rumor mill debatable
- Some days in October, CNBC parked truck outside Citadel Center — new deathwatch beginning
- **Friday, October 24**: morning — **Dan Dufresne** (young Griffin lieutenant, head of treasury) set off to catch train to office
- Took call on cell from European bank: "Just so you know, there are rumors picking up momentum in Europe that the Fed is in your office in Chicago, organizing a liquidation of your assets"
- Dufresne decided to get cab — not discussing alleged demise on crowded commuter train
- Voice pressed: "I'm hearing from our guys in London that this is happening. Is it? I'm sure it's not, but you need to know that it's picking up speed"
- Dufresne assured contact just another rumor — talked ~10 minutes, got another call (same rumor)
- By time reached desk at Citadel Center: third and fourth calls
- Colleague **Gerald Beeson** in office early, peppered with questions from European trading desks since 5 A.M.
- Dufresne and Beeson suspected financial journalists gotten hold of rumor, bouncing it off everyone they knew
- Rumor spreading faster than Citadel could douse it
- Credit default swaps on Citadel bonds trading at distressed levels — signaling more trouble than Lehman on eve of bankruptcy
- **James Forese** (Citigroup's head of capital markets) placed call to Griffin
- According to rumors: Griffin visiting Fed in Washington looking for bailout
- After dialing Citadel, Forese put on hold for a minute, then Griffin picked up
- "You're calling me for one of three reasons. One, to see if I'm alive. Two, to see if we have any money..."
- Forese cut him off: "The reason I'm calling is to offer you help. If you need to liquidate portfolios and need someone to discreetly handle that, you know we would do that for you"
- "We're losing a lot of money. But we've got a lot of liquidity" — because Citadel locked up long-term funding, not facing margin calls
- Sold plenty of assets to raise cash — more proactive than Bear or Lehman in preventing leverage from spiraling
- Forese: "You guys are getting killed in the rumor mill"
- "I know. I can't get rid of the rumors" — no matter how many panicky callers Griffin's team assuaged, rumors growing more hysterical
- Last thing wanted: stage conference call appearing to confirm worst suspicions (Bear and Lehman had done calls, did no good)

### Citadel's Key Advantage: Matched Funding

- Unlike investment banks (short-term borrowing), Citadel's treasury matched loan maturities to asset liquidity
- Five-year bond issuance; bank loans locked in for up to a year
- State-of-the-art back office tracked daily value of every derivative
- Recalling LTCM's "Salomon without the bullshit," Citadel staffer joked LTCM partners were the bullshit

### The Bondholder Call

- **Forese** remembered Citadel issued five-year bonds — could convene phone call for bondholders
- Without signaling panic, doing call in that format would give message extra weight
- Griffin could get in trouble with regulators if gave bondholders anything other than truth — making assurances believable
- "That's a pretty good idea" — Griffin allowed; hung up and summoned lieutenants
- Around 10:30 A.M.: inner circle convened around whiteboard
- Somebody jotted down half-dozen points Citadel needed to get across
- Griffin wanted **Gerald Beeson** (super-charged COO) to do most talking
- Another note added to whiteboard: **"Speak slowly"**
- "How many call-in lines?" — When big company like Coca-Cola held quarterly call, usually ~250 lines
- "Five hundred" somebody ventured — "A thousand" Beeson countered
- 3:30 P.M. scheduled; well over 1,000 callers attempted to dial in — traders, investors, financial reporters
- **Culmination of Citadel deathwatch**, biggest financial drama since Lehman's failure
- On some Wall Street trading floors, call played over loudspeakers
- 25-minute delay arranging more lines; those who managed to get in treated to grating techno hold music
- Eventually Beeson began pitch: Yes, flagship funds down 35%, but firm long way from running out of cash
- Financing secured, untapped $8 billion credit line, no way would go under
- **Adam Cooper** (general counsel) held up sign in front of Beeson: "To call this a dislocation doesn't go anywhere near the enormity of what we've seen"
- Emphasized Citadel would still survive: "We will prosper in the new era of finance" — Griffin mustered, mustering all confidence he could manage
- After 12 minutes, call over
- Griffin got off phone, went to answer staff questions in town-hall meeting
- Beeson spent afternoon on series of calls with reporters, hammering home same message
- Next day: CNBC truck gone from usual position outside Citadel Center — fires doused (for time being)

### Citadel's Losses Continue

- End of year: two flagship funds down **55%**; $9 billion evaporated (equivalent of at least two Long-Term Capitals)
- But: investors risked capital, rewarded for years, forced to take losses — exactly as should happen
- **No taxpayer bailout; no systemic destabilization**
- Careful liquidity management substituted for Fed safety net
- Old-line investment banks built castles of leverage on short-term loans — whole edifice toppled
- Griffin shared paranoid culture of hedge funds, leveraged more cautiously — survived

### Industry-Wide Carnage

- Even worst period in history, hedge funds proved their worth
- End of 2008: most funds lost money; almost 1,500 went bust
- Industry down 19% — but S&P 500 fell twice as much
- Unlike banks, investment banks, home lenders — **hedge funds imposed no costs on taxpayers or society**
- Point largely lost amid crisis — bursting of hedge-fund bubble left no room to think about policy meaning
- Titans flown so high that spectacle of fall mesmerizing
- **Phil Falcone** (Harbinger Capital): returned 43% first half 2008, stalked *New York Times*
- September: lost >$1 billion in week thanks to Lehman collapse + SEC short-selling ban
- February 2007: alpha factory **Fortress** went public amid great fanfare, creating $10.7 billion paper wealth for helicopter-riding chiefs
- December 2008: assets collapsed by almost a third, forced to fire two dozen portfolio managers
- **John Meriwether** and **Myron Scholes** (LTCM veterans) set up new hedge funds in 1999 that did well for years — end of 2008 both near precipice
- **Chris Hohn** (Children's Investment Fund): down 42%, seemed to lose intellectual moorings: "Quite frankly activism is hard" — as though surprised by discovery
- Stunned by reversals, investors scrambled to get money out — hedge funds responded with gates suspending withdrawals
- Sometimes investors lucky to be taken prisoner: gates averted need for disastrous fire sales
- Other times outrage: one prominent fund charged departing investor fee, then blocked redemption, refused to return fee, carried on charging management fee
- **Farallon**: $36 billion → $20 billion; nimbler boutiques escaped with minor scratches
- **Rock Creek Capital** data: funds <$1 billion down relatively modest 12%; $1-10 billion down 16%; >$10 billion down 27%
- Larger funds tended to be more peremptory and arrogant — stuck in losing positions when liquidity dried up

## Conclusion: The Arguments Against Regulation

### The Case for Hedge Funds

- Manage risk better than rivals; recognize setbacks faster; liquidate before secondary effects hit system
- Government should encourage them, shifting risk away from too-big-to-fail institutions
- Chief prescription: **don't regulate**

### Performance Fees: Not as Perverse as Claimed

- 20% fee criticized since 1960s — allegedly promotes wild punts
- Academic criticism: incentive fees induce deceptively smooth returns concealing blowup risk
- Example: Fund takes in $100, invests in S&P, earns $5 selling options against collapse — if collapse occurs, fund wiped out; but over 5-10 years, fund appears to beat market by 5 points
- But: hedge-fund managers have own wealth in fund alongside clients
- Investment banks distributed **50% of net revenues** as salary/bonuses — far larger incentive for risk
- Bank compensation variable, set without shareholder consultation; hedge-fund fees fixed ahead of time

### Hedge Funds vs. Mutual Funds

- Actively managed mutual funds charge ~1% fee, delivering no alpha — **fee per unit of alpha is infinite**
- **Ibbotson/Chen/Zhu study** (1995-2009, corrected for survivorship and backfill bias):
  - Average hedge fund returned 11.4%/year, or 7.7% after fees
  - 7.7% included **3 percentage points of alpha** — real value despite fees

### Hedge Funds vs. Private Equity

- Both structured as private partnerships with leverage and performance fees
- Venture capital generates alpha of 4-5%/year; buyout funds generate returns little different from S&P 500
- Private equity demands capital locked up for up to a decade — clear disadvantage

### Do Hedge Funds Destabilize Markets?

- Most hedge funds stabilize: arbitrage funds and statistical arbitrage drive prices toward rational levels
- **Robertson-style stock pickers** move stocks closer to best estimates of value, allocating capital to firms that will use it productively
- Commodity traders responding to gluts/shortages stabilize markets — their responses generate price signals that force healthy adjustment
- Trend-following can contribute to overshooting (2007-2008 oil: $61 → $96 probably fair response to emerging markets demand, then $145 probably irrational)
- Currency traders sometimes send rational signals, sometimes drive currencies to irrational extremes
- **Could hedge-fund regulation dampen trend-following?**
- Restrictions would limit contrarian trend-bucking as well as trend-following trading — no data to prove net effect
- Hedge funds **more likely to be contrarian** due to structure:
  1. Free to go short as well as long
  2. Judged on absolute returns (mutual funds measured against consensus-reflecting indices)
  3. Performance fees provide resources and incentives for contrarian bets
- **Paul Tudor Jones** example: even self-described trend follower recognizes contrarian potential
- Knows investors react to information gradually — prices respond incrementally rather than in one jump
- Precisely because Jones understands momentum, he has knack for sensing when it's gone too far — he's the last person to exacerbate a trend
- Bet against trend before 1987 crash, against Tokyo 1990, declared summer 2008: **"Oil is a huge mania... It is going to end badly"** — weeks before bubble burst
- **European exchange-rate crisis**: Soros/Druckenmiller didn't simply lead attack pack — they bet French franc *would resist* devaluation pressure
- **East Asian crisis**: hedge funds helped precipitate Thailand devaluation (trade deficit made peg illogical)
- Rupiah crash driven by Indonesians rushing to expatriate money, not hedge funds — far from jumping on bandwagon, Soros team pushed back unsuccessfully
- Farallon helped begin Indonesia's turnaround with contrarian BCA purchase

> "The best way to dampen trend following is not to constrain hedge funds. It is to let them go about their business."

### The One Argument for Regulation: When Funds Get Too Large

- Case for hedge funds is case for **entrepreneurial boutiques**
- When funds cease being small enough to fail, regulation warranted
- ~5,000 hedge funds failed 2000-2009 without triggering taxpayer bailout — but LTCM serves as warning

**Three clues to systemic risk:**
1. Size of capital
2. Extent of leverage
3. Types of markets traded in

**LTCM**: under $5 billion capital but leveraged 25x = $120 billion portfolio + $1.2 trillion derivatives; traded illiquid, esoteric markets — combination triggered Fed intervention

**Amaranth** ($9 billion): normal leverage, trades on exchanges — no red flags; generated more headlines than system shocks

**Quant funds 2007**: $100+ billion capital leveraged ~8x = $800+ billion — but traded in superliquid equity markets; could cut leverage rapidly

**Citadel** ($13 billion, leveraged 11x): $145 billion portfolio — worrisomely large, but matched illiquid positions with long-term borrowing — survived

### Proposed Tiered Framework

- **Tier 1** ($120 billion leveraged assets): regulatory cross-examination of derivatives and liquidity
- **Tier 2** ($200 billion): scrutiny of leverage; extra capital padding if too thin
  - Cautious bar: at $200 billion, still considerably smaller than Bear Stearns ($350 billion assets, 2006)
- **Tier 3** (going public): more frequent regulatory examination
  - Presumption of competent risk management should be softened
- Would leave **nearly all hedge funds outside the net** — only 39 worldwide had capital over $10 billion (Jan 2009)
- Other ~9,000 left alone unless leverage got them over threshold
- Small number of genuine systemic risks handled differently — treated as though they were investment banks

### The Historical Parallel

- In 1949 when **Alfred Winslow Jones** launched his hedged fund, merchant banks (Goldman, Morgan Stanley, Lehman) were private operations deploying partners' capital — like today's hedge funds
- Managed risk ferociously (speculating with their own money); largely unregulated
- Over next half century: sold shares, opened global offices — not because sprawling public companies are better at risk, but because rewards to leaders were irresistible
- Every investment bank that went public unlocked millions; every expansion created fresh opportunity to risk shareholders' capital and collect 50% quasi performance fee
- **Incentives pushed investment banks to take ever greater risk** until they paid the price
- By end of 2008: Goldman and Morgan became bank holding companies, needing Fed backstop
- **Today, hedge funds are the new merchant banks**
- Same logic that tempted old merchant banks to go public will seduce some hedge funds
- When that happens, they will pose threat to financial system they've wrongly been accused of posing in the past
- **The wheel of Wall Street turns. Greed and risk are always with us.**
