# More Money Than God — Summary

**Author:** Sebastian Mallaby
**Pages:** 495
**Published:** 2010
**Category:** Narrative / Financial History

## One-Line Summary
The history of hedge funds from A.W. Jones's first "hedged fund" in 1949 through the 2008 financial crisis, told through the characters who built, broke, and remade the industry.

## Key Themes

1. **The Efficient Market Paradox**: Markets are not perfectly efficient — hedge funds exploit inefficiencies, but their very success attracts imitators who crowd trades and create new risks.

2. **Leverage as Double-Edged Sword**: Leverage gives hedge funds the ammunition to correct mispricings and earn outsized returns, but also makes them vulnerable to sudden shocks and liquidity crises.

3. **Government Failure Creates Opportunity**: Every generation of hedge funds profits from a different type of government error — 1970s inflation, 1990s currency pegs, 2000s housing bubble.

4. **Culture Beats Structure**: Hedge funds survived the 2008 crisis better than banks not because of better regulation but because of paranoid, entrepreneurial culture — managers with their own money at risk, no safety net, constant mark-to-market discipline.

5. **The Paradox of Growth**: Small hedge funds are innovative, nimble, and safe to fail. As they grow into alpha factories, they develop the same bureaucratic incentive problems as the banks they were supposed to replace.

## The Characters

- **Alfred Winslow Jones** — Former Marxist, tramp steamer worker, and Fortune journalist who invented the hedge fund at age 48 in 1949.
- **Michael Steinhardt** — Brooklyn-raised contrarian whose block-trading edge and explosive temper produced 26x S&P returns over 27 years.
- **Commodities Corporation** — Princeton farmhouse quant shop that spawned Bruce Kovner, Michael Marcus, Paul Tudor Jones, and Louis Bacon.
- **George Soros** — Hungarian refugee whose "reflexivity" philosophy led to the $1 billion sterling short that broke the Bank of England.
- **Stan Druckenmiller** — Soros's lieutenant who actually executed the sterling trade, then rode and crashed the tech bubble.
- **Julian Robertson** — Tiger Management's value investor who was right about the dot-com bubble but couldn't survive being early.
- **John Meriwether** — LTCM founder whose Nobel Prize-backed fund nearly took down the financial system in 1998.
- **Jim Simons** — Mathematician who built Renaissance Technologies into the most successful fund ever by treating markets like code-breaking.
- **David Swensen** — Yale endowment chief who channeled institutional money into hedge funds, transforming the industry.
- **John Paulson** — Quiet merger-arb specialist who made $15 billion betting against subprime mortgages.
- **Ken Griffin** — Citadel founder who survived 2008 with 55% losses but no taxpayer bailout, proving hedge funds are "safe to fail."

## The Argument

Mallaby's central thesis: hedge funds are the financial system's best-designed institutions. They manage risk better than banks because their managers have their own money at stake, they face no too-big-to-fail moral hazard, they mark to market daily, and their paranoid culture prevents complacency. The 2008 crisis proved this — hedge funds lost money but imposed no costs on taxpayers, while banks, insurers, and government-sponsored enterprises required massive bailouts. The prescription is not to regulate hedge funds more, but to encourage them as alternatives to systemically dangerous banks. The one exception: when individual funds grow so large and leveraged that they become too complex to fail (like LTCM), tiered regulation is warranted.
