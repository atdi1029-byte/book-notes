The Alpha Game

In 1949, a forty-eight-year-old former Marxist, tramp steamer worker, and Fortune magazine journalist named Alfred Winslow Jones scraped together $100,000 and invented something the world had never seen: a "hedged fund." He combined long positions in promising stocks with short sales of unpromising ones, used leverage to amplify both sides, and charged a 20% cut of the profits. He claimed inspiration from Phoenician merchants who kept a fifth of the gains from successful voyages. Over the next two decades, a $10,000 investment with Jones grew to $480,000 — a cumulative return of just under 5,000%.

Half a century later, Clifford Asness represented everything Jones's invention had become. A PhD in quantitative finance from the University of Chicago, a Goldman Sachs veteran, he launched his own fund at thirty-one, raised a billion dollars, and told the New York Times "it doesn't suck" to be worth millions. By 2007 his firm AQR Capital Management ran $38 billion. The distance between Jones's shabby office on Broad Street and Asness's empire measured the distance the industry had traveled.

The wealth was staggering. J. Pierpont Morgan died in 1913 with a fortune equivalent to roughly $1.4 billion in today's money. By the 2000s, the top hedge fund managers surpassed Morgan's lifetime fortune in a couple of years of trading. In 2006, Lloyd Blankfein, the CEO of Goldman Sachs, earned $54 million — but the bottom earner on Alpha magazine's top-25 hedge fund list made $240 million. The top three each made over a billion. Six managers in Connecticut alone pocketed a combined $2.15 billion.

The lifestyles matched the numbers. Ken Griffin of Citadel bought a $50 million Bombardier Express jet with a crib installed for his two-year-old. Louis Bacon of Moore Capital acquired an island in Great Peconic Bay and monitored mud turtle mating patterns with electronic transmitters. Steven Cohen of SAC Capital built an estate with a basketball court, indoor pool, skating rink, two-acre golf course, and paintings by van Gogh and Pollock. But the money also flowed outward. George Soros's charities fostered independent voices across the former communist world. Paul Tudor Jones created Robin Hood, one of the first "venture philanthropies," fighting New York City poverty with demanding performance benchmarks. Bruce Kovner became the godfather of the neoconservative movement and chaired the American Enterprise Institute.

From the beginning, the academic establishment insisted these fortunes were mostly luck. The efficient-market hypothesis, dominant from the 1960s through the 1980s, held that if it were possible to know a stock would rise, smart investors would already have bought it. Prices reflected all available information. Professional money managers generally failed to beat the index. Successful hedge funds, the logic went, "could drop their h and be called edge funds." But the 1987 crash began to change minds. When the market's valuation shifted by a fifth in a single trading day, it was hard to believe prices deserved deference.

"If the efficient markets hypothesis was a publicly traded security, its price would be enormously volatile. But the stock crashed along with the rest of the market on October 19, 1987." — Andrei Shleifer and Lawrence Summers

Lines of academics queued to join hedge funds. Eugene Fama, the father of the efficient-market hypothesis, was leading the revisionist charge with Kenneth French by 1988, discovering non-random pricing patterns. Nobel laureates Myron Scholes and Robert Merton signed up with Long-Term Capital Management. Lawrence Summers joined D. E. Shaw. Institutional money followed the professors. Before 1987, most hedge fund capital came from rich individuals. Afterward, endowments led by David Swensen at Yale poured in, seeking uncorrelated returns they called alpha.

The central question of the book emerged from this transformation. Hedge funds correct market inefficiencies — they buy underpriced value stocks and short overpriced growth stocks, ensuring capital flows toward productive uses. But the leverage that gives them ammunition to move prices also makes them vulnerable to shocks. The heart of the matter is leverage. It renders prices more efficient in calm times and threatens to destabilize everything when panic hits. Two views competed at the start of the twenty-first century: hedge funds as stabilizing heroes muscling inefficient prices into line, or hedge funds as weak links whose instability threatened the global economy.

The 2008 crisis seemed to settle the debate — but not in the way most people assumed. The institutions the government rescued were commercial banks like Citigroup, former investment banks like Goldman Sachs, and insurers like AIG. Hedge funds made it through the mayhem without direct taxpayer assistance. Even LTCM in 1998: the Federal Reserve oversaw the burial but provided no public money. Investment banks leveraged 30:1. Commercial banks went even higher. The average hedge fund borrowed only one or two times investors' capital. They were, in Mallaby's formulation, "safe to fail, even if not fail-safe."

Big Daddy

Alfred Winslow Jones was born on September 9, 1900 — the ninth hour of the ninth day of the ninth month. His father ran General Electric's Australian operations. Jones graduated from Harvard in 1923, worked as a purser on a tramp steamer, and eventually found himself posted to Berlin in December 1930 as an American vice-consul. Germany had lost 8% of its output and had 4.5 million unemployed. The National Socialist Party had just won 107 seats in the Reichstag. Jones met Anna Block, a Jewish banker's daughter and left-wing anti-Nazi activist. He married her secretly and was forced to resign from the State Department. He returned to Berlin under the alias "Richard Frost," working for a Leninist Organization, then moved to London as "H. B. Wood" to lobby the British Labour Party for military action against Hitler.

Back in New York by 1934, Jones divorced Anna, enrolled in Columbia University's sociology graduate program, married Mary Elizabeth Carter, and honeymooned in war-torn Spain in 1937 — hitchhiking to the front lines with Dorothy Parker and meeting Ernest Hemingway. His doctoral work took him to Akron, Ohio, where he organized a team for 1,700 field interviews to test whether American economic divisions could produce the political extremism he had witnessed in Germany. His conclusion was a repudiation of his youthful socialism: acute economic divisions did not carry over into polarized worldviews. The thesis was published as Life, Liberty and Property in 1941, and Fortune hired him.

It was a 1949 Fortune essay called "Fashions in Forecasting" that turned Jones from journalist to investor. Writing about stock market prediction, he attacked the standard methods and observed that money was a medium through which greed and fear expressed themselves — a barometer of crowd psychology. Investor emotions created feedback loops: a rising market bred optimism, which drove further buying, which confirmed the optimism. By March 1949, Jones had raised $60,000 from four friends and put up $40,000 of his own money to launch the world's first hedge fund.

Jones's true innovation was not what he bought but how he structured the buying. The standard approach was to load up on stocks when the market seemed bullish and hold cash when it looked bearish. Jones improved on this by using both leverage and short selling simultaneously. He borrowed money to be 150% long, then shorted stocks he considered overvalued, creating what he called "speculative means for conservative ends." A traditional investor with $100,000 might put $80,000 in good stocks and $20,000 in bonds. Jones's hedged investor would borrow another $100,000, buy $130,000 of good stocks, short $70,000 of bad ones, and end up with net market exposure of only $60,000 — less than the traditional investor, but with far more diversification and upside potential.

"You could buy more good stocks without taking as much risk as someone who merely bought." — A.W. Jones

Jones also anticipated ideas that academics would formalize years later. He measured the volatility of individual stocks — he called it velocity — and adjusted position sizes accordingly, a primitive version of what Harry Markowitz would publish in 1952 as "Portfolio Selection." He distinguished money made through stock picking (alpha) from money made through market exposure (beta), terms that later became standard. He anticipated James Tobin's separation theorem by nine years — the idea that your stock choices should be independent of your risk appetite.

Jones's attorney Richard Valentine discovered that if managers took a share of investment profits rather than a flat fee, the income could be taxed at the capital gains rate of 25% rather than the income rate of 91%. Jones charged 20% of the upside and called it a "performance reallocation." To avoid registration requirements that would have barred the fund from borrowing or selling short, Jones never advertised publicly and kept the partnership below 100 members. This secrecy had a price: it made hedge funds "forever mysterious, shadowy, resented."

The real source of Jones's returns was not his market timing — his efforts to call the overall direction of the market failed as often as they succeeded. In 1953, 1956, 1957, and 1960 he lost money on market calls. In August 1965 his net exposure was minus 18% just as the market embarked on a hot rally. His statistical methods revealed exactly how much he was losing from bad market calls, and yet the fund still performed marvelously. The secret was stock picking, and Jones knew he could not do it alone.

Starting in the early 1950s, he invited brokers to run "model portfolios" — each man selected favorite longs and shorts and phoned in changes. Jones used these paper portfolios as a source of ideas and compensated brokers according to how well their suggestions worked. In the sleepy, unsophisticated Wall Street of the 1950s, where Harvard's investment course was dubbed "Darkness at Noon" because it was relegated to an unpopular lunchtime slot, Jones's hustle was unmatched. Alan Dresher, one of his stock pickers, would go to the SEC offices to read company filings the moment they came out. He was all alone.

Without realizing its significance, Jones had created a competitive multimanager structure — individualism instead of collectivism, adrenaline instead of complacency. Each in-house manager got a segment of partners' capital and was left to invest it. Successful managers received more capital; unsuccessful managers received less. Partners had to keep their own money in the fund. But the structure had a fatal flaw: when the market turned, the performance-tracking system rewarded the most aggressive managers, amplifying the damage. By 1969 there were 200 to 500 hedge fund imitators. A 1966 Fortune profile called Jones "the best professional manager of investors' money," and New York magazine proclaimed him hedge funds' "Big Daddy." Then the go-go era ended. In the year ending May 1970, Jones lost 35% of client money. After two decades of eminence, the edge was gone.

The Block Trader

The crash of 1969-73 destroyed the first generation of hedge funds. The 28 largest lost two thirds of their capital. Their claim to be hedged turned out to be, in Mallaby's phrase, "a bald-faced lie." By 1984, a survey by Tremont Partners identified only 68 hedge funds in America. Jones's own partnership withered from over $100 million to $25 million. But adversity did not rule out success for everyone.

Michael Steinhardt was raised by a single mother in hardscrabble Brooklyn. His father was confrontational and addicted to gambling. Admitted to the University of Pennsylvania at sixteen and graduated at nineteen, by his mid-twenties Steinhardt was "the hottest analyst on Wall Street" — short, barrel-chested, and prone to terrifying outbursts. In 1967 he launched a hedge fund with Jerrold Fine and Howard Berkowitz. They equipped the office with a pool table and proclaimed the intellectual superiority of youth. Their first full year, loaded up on story stocks — King Resources, National Student Marketing, companies with "Data" or "-onics" in their names — they were up 84%. Each had become a millionaire.

When the bull market ended in 1969, nearly every other go-go fund blew up. Fred Mates, the top-performing mutual fund manager of 1968, ended up working as a bartender. Almost alone among the gunslingers, the troika sensed the long postwar expansion had overreached. They had read Benjamin Graham, who made the fateful decision not to buy shares in Haloid — which then sextupled as it became Xerox. The lesson cut both ways: great companies could be missed, but speculative manias always ended. At the start of 1969, Steinhardt and his partners shorted enough story stocks to balance their long positions. When the S&P 500 fell 9% that year, the firm preserved almost all of its capital. The following year, as the index dropped another 9%, they actually made money.

Positioned for the 1973-74 crash with short positions greatly outweighing longs, Steinhardt racked up gains of 12% and then 28% after fees while the S&P plummeted 41%. Old prejudices against short selling came back with a vengeance. Betting against American companies was portrayed as one step away from treason. Looking back, Steinhardt recalled: "That was, for me, the height of professional satisfaction." The success demonstrated a capacity for contrarianism that would mark all the great hedge funds to come. Jones had invented the structure to control market exposure. The go-go imitators had turned it into a way to maximize exposure. Steinhardt made it into a vehicle for betting against conventional wisdom.

Steinhardt's real edge, though, came from block trading. When a large institutional investor needed to sell a huge chunk of stock, there was often no buyer without a significant discount. Steinhardt systematically milked these discounts, providing liquidity that the market badly needed. Brokers tipped him about large institutional orders before execution. Will Weinstein of Oppenheimer: "Everyone had a hundred behind it." Steinhardt was blunt about the advantage: "I was being told things other accounts were not being told. I got information I shouldn't have. It created a lot of opportunities for us." Over 28 years in markets, he had only four losing years — a probability of roughly 1 in 11,000.

After the 1987 crash, when the big block-trading desks pulled back, the irony became acute. The New York Times reported that 25,000-share blocks now disrupted prices as much as 100,000-share blocks had before the crash. The SEC promised to investigate the "troubling lack of block trading." Fast-trading hedge funds, recently objects of suspicion, had become preferable to their sudden absence. The market needed them.

Paul Samuelson's Secret

In 1967, Paul Samuelson told Congress that a PhD dissertation had shown randomly chosen stock portfolios beat professionally managed mutual funds. By 1974, the Nobel laureate was telling portfolio managers they should "take up plumbing, teach Greek, or help produce the annual GNP."

"Even if this advice to drop dead is good advice, it obviously is not counsel that will be eagerly followed." — Paul Samuelson

But Samuelson left room for exceptions. Some people might have a higher "P.Q." — performance quotient. These exceptional investors wouldn't rent themselves cheaply. They'd form small partnerships to capture their gains. They would be hedge funds. In 1970, Samuelson put his money where his mouth was, becoming the founding backer of Commodities Corporation.

The firm was headquartered in a Princeton, New Jersey farmhouse — flowering trees, acres of lawn, horseshoes at lunch, softball after markets closed, a three-legged German shepherd named Cocoa. It was among the first boutiques for hard-core "quants," premised on harnessing large-scale econometric analysis. The founder, F. Helmut Weymar, had written his PhD dissertation on predicting cocoa prices using historical data. Before starting the firm, he had borrowed $20,000 to buy a warehouse full of frozen orange juice based on a mathematical model predicting the price would double. The price shot up as predicted, but local supermarkets refused to buy wholesale from a graduate student.

Early success was followed by near-death. In 1971, U.S. cornfields were hit by fungal disease. Weymar and his team hired a plant pathologist at Rutgers who concluded the blight scare was overdone. They built vast short positions. Then a Friday night CBS News special predicted a catastrophic corn harvest. Monday morning, corn futures jumped so steeply that trading was immediately suspended. The firm's $2.5 million in start-up capital was reduced to $900,000. At a July board meeting, the firm agreed to give Weymar one last chance — it would close if it lost another $100,000. The Rutgers pathologist was eventually proved right, but the experience taught humility: "The most dangerous people in the world are very smart traders who had never gotten their teeth kicked in."

The near-disaster triggered a crucial pivot. Frank Vannerson, a bearded, subdued economics PhD, had obtained historical commodity price data from an Indiana firm and spent a year analyzing daily prices for fifteen commodities. By 1970 he was satisfied that price trends genuinely existed and devised an automated trading system called the Technical Computer System — one of the first computerized trading programs in history. Weymar was initially skeptical, but the corn episode made the virtue of automation obvious: risk controls were programmed in from the start. After allocating more capital to the system, Commodities Corporation took off.

The firm's greatest contribution was the people it spawned. Michael Marcus, hired in 1974 without a doctorate (one of the first to break that barrier), increased his trading account by 2,500% over ten years. Marcus was intense and eccentric — he believed most foods contained poisons, once purified with a raw vegetable diet until he became so emaciated he hired a dietician-driver, owned roughly ten homes but sold some without spending a single night in them. His trading combined fundamentals with chart reading: "Like mounting a surfboard, ready for the wave." When Nixon's price controls fixed plywood at $110 per thousand square feet but builders were willing to pay much more, Marcus bought plywood futures by the truckload and virtually doubled his money.

In 1977, Marcus placed an ad for an assistant trader. It was answered by a Harvard PhD dropout who was driving a taxi part-time. Marcus called Weymar: "Helmut, I have in my office the next president of Commodities Corp." Bruce Kovner was tall, imposing, with a big head topped by thick hair, and possessed a striking intellectual range — he had been part of a circle of political scientists at Harvard that included James Q. Wilson and Daniel Patrick Moynihan. He had borrowed $3,000 against his MasterCard and turned it into $22,000 trading futures. He came to his interview having read Charles Mackay's Extraordinary Popular Delusions and the Madness of Crowds. Weymar hired him directly as a trader. Kovner averaged 80% annual gains over the next decade and eventually launched Caxton Corporation.

Kovner's most elegant innovation was the carry trade. In 1981, he and Roy Lennox noticed that currencies trading at a big discount in the forward market often had high interest rates. The traditional view was that a low forward rate signaled coming depreciation, but Kovner realized it more often reflected high interest rates — signaling the currency might rise, not fall. Spanish banks paying 7% interest meant the peseta was worth 7% more in the spot market than in the one-year forward. The carry trade raked in profits for the next decade.

By the end of the 1970s, Commodities Corporation was prodigiously successful. In 1980, its profits hit an astronomical $42 million. After $13 million in bonuses to 140 employees, it outearned 58 of the Fortune 500. The triumph was one of flexibility — moving beyond rigid econometric models to embrace trend following and trader intuition. Innovation in hedge funds, it turned out, depended less on grand academic breakthroughs than on humble trial and error. Years later, financial academia caught up with Vannerson's discovery: a 1986 Journal of Finance paper found that trend following in currency markets earned sizable profits. The academic examined the same price series Vannerson had worked on almost two decades earlier.

The Alchemist

George Soros arrived at the London School of Economics in 1949 as a young Hungarian refugee. He had survived the Nazi occupation of Budapest by separating from his family and assuming a Christian identity. He had witnessed corpses in the streets, mangled figures with bound hands and crushed heads. He had hawked jewelry to black-market dealers to help his family survive. In London he worked as a dishwasher, house painter, busboy, headwaiter, and lifeguard — reading Adam Smith, Thomas Hobbes, and Machiavelli between shifts. The philosopher Karl Popper inspired him most: a fellow Austrian refugee whose central contention was that humans cannot know truth with certainty. Soros named his future philanthropy the Open Society Institute after Popper's masterwork.

Soros left LSE with mediocre grades, sold handbags in northern Wales, and eventually landed a job with a Hungarian brokerage in London. In 1956 he moved to New York, spotted discrepancies between prices of the same security in different markets, and started making money as an arbitrageur. By 1969 he had persuaded the firm Arnold & S. Bleichroeder to let him manage a hedge fund called Double Eagle, named after the Habsburg symbol. It started with $4 million. First year: up 23%. Second year: up 44%. Third year: up 18% while the market fell 11%. By 1973, Soros set up his own firm and renamed it the Soros Fund, partnering with Jim Rogers, a brash young Yale and Oxford graduate from Alabama who rode motorcycles and made an odd couple with the intense, brooding Soros.

Soros's central intellectual contribution was the concept of reflexivity: the idea that investors' perceptions affect the underlying reality they are trying to evaluate. If investors believe a company will succeed, they rush to buy the stock, the stock price rises, the company finds it easier to raise capital, and the prophecy becomes self-fulfilling. This creates a feedback loop between perception and reality that the efficient-market hypothesis completely ignores. The trick for a trader is to spot the start of a virtuous cycle and ride it — and then to recognize when the cycle is reaching exhaustion.

The trade that made Soros a legend began not with Soros himself but with his lieutenant, Stan Druckenmiller. Previously running Duquesne Capital Management in Pittsburgh, Druckenmiller joined Soros Fund Management in 1988. By 1992 he was managing $3.5 billion and saw an irresistible opportunity in the European Exchange Rate Mechanism. Britain had joined the ERM in September 1990 at a rate of 2.95 deutsche marks to the pound. But German reunification had created inflationary pressures, forcing the Bundesbank to raise rates. Britain was in recession and desperately needed lower rates, not higher ones. British mortgage rates were not fixed — when the Bank of England raised rates, families felt the pinch immediately.

Druckenmiller saw the asymmetry: there was significant chance Britain would let sterling devalue and virtually no chance sterling would rise against the mark. He invested $1.5 billion in positions against the pound by the end of August. Then came the trigger. At a Bath meeting of European finance ministers in early September, British chancellor Norman Lamont banged his fist and demanded the Bundesbank lower its interest rates. Helmut Schlesinger, the Bundesbank president, was so shaken his first instinct was to walk out. Days later, Schlesinger declared publicly in Basel that he had "no confidence" in fixed relationships among European currencies and alluded to the unsoundness of the Italian lira. Soros was in the audience. He approached Schlesinger afterward and asked about the ECU. Schlesinger said he liked the concept but would prefer the name "mark." The message was clear: the Bundesbank would not sacrifice its inflation-fighting mission to save other countries' exchange rates.

Soros called Druckenmiller in New York: the lira was heading for a fall. Druckenmiller added a bet against the Italian currency. Then Robert Johnson, a currency expert, took a cab to Soros's run-down offices at 888 Seventh Avenue — duct tape on the carpet — and sat around a small conference table with Soros and Druckenmiller. Johnson estimated the most they could lose betting against sterling was half a percent, and the probable gain was fifteen to twenty percent, with a 90% probability on a three-month time frame. Druckenmiller wanted to build the position steadily. Soros looked puzzled. "That doesn't make sense," he said. If the risk-reward was that good, why not jump to $15 billion?

"Go for the jugular." — George Soros to Stan Druckenmiller

On the Tuesday evening before Black Wednesday, Druckenmiller and Soros got on the phones themselves, selling sterling to anyone who would buy. Banks getting vast sell orders alerted their own currency traders. The avalanche started. Louis Bacon called Druckenmiller late that night. Robert Johnson was beeped while asleep in his apartment around 2 AM. Scott Bessent arrived at the office shortly after Druckenmiller and could see the hulking outline of his boss in the dark room, lit only by the telephone. A disembodied eastern European accent filled the darkness: Soros urging Druckenmiller to leverage up and redouble the selling.

When London opened, the Bank of England intervened twice before 8:30 AM, each time buying £300 million. Absolutely no effect. By 8:40 AM the bank had purchased a total of £1 billion. Sterling refused to budge. Lamont told Prime Minister John Major the intervention was failing, but Major refused to authorize a rate hike, pleading for new economic data. At 11:00 AM, Lamont announced a two-percentage-point emergency rate increase. He walked to the Reuters screen to watch. The pound did not respond at all. The line on the screen remained totally flat. Lamont felt like a surgeon watching a heart monitor and realizing the patient had expired. A further 3% rate hike had equally no effect. At 7:30 PM, Lamont announced Britain's exit from the exchange-rate mechanism.

The Bank of England had spent $27 billion defending sterling. British taxpayers lost roughly $3.8 billion. Soros Fund Management's profit exceeded $1 billion. Bruce Kovner pocketed $300 million, Paul Tudor Jones $250 million, and the top seven U.S. bank currency desks shared $800 million. Soros became "the man who broke the Bank of England." But Druckenmiller's strategy went far beyond the sterling short. While selling the pound, he was buying British government bonds and equities, reasoning that ejection from the ERM would free Britain to cut interest rates. Those bets paid off handsomely, explaining why Quantum's 69% return in 1992 was followed by 63% in 1993. The Swedish krona, quietly shorted by Robert Johnson before Sweden's devaluation in November 1992, added upward of another billion. After the publicity from the sterling trade, Soros and Druckenmiller made sure nobody spoke publicly about Sweden.

Hurricane Greenspan

By December 1993, Michael Steinhardt was up $100 million in a single day. He was running roughly $4.5 billion, had returned 47% and 48% after fees in 1991 and 1992, and magazines were reporting his purchase of a Picasso. He had pioneered an early version of the shadow banking system: borrowing short-term money cheaply thanks to the Fed's low interest rates and loading up on longer-term bonds yielding considerably more. He was doing what any bank would do — borrow short, lend long — but without the tedious business of hiring tellers and credit officers. He bypassed the banking system entirely, borrowing from brokerages like Goldman Sachs and Salomon Brothers, then lending by buying bonds. For every $100 a real bank received in deposits, regulators required roughly $10 in capital. Steinhardt had no depositors and therefore no capital requirements. For every $100 he borrowed to buy government bonds, he could get away with as little as $1 in capital.

In 1993, Steinhardt and fellow speculators took this strategy to Europe, buying truckloads of Spanish and Italian bonds as interest rates converged toward Germany's in anticipation of monetary union. The number of hedge funds exploded from over 1,000 in 1992 to perhaps 3,000 a year later. Fees escalated from Jones's original 20% profit share with no management fee, to a 1% management fee plus 20%, to the "2 and 20" that hot new funds demanded.

"Perhaps never before in history have so few made so much money so fast." — Forbes

On January 21, 1994, Federal Reserve Chairman Alan Greenspan visited the White House to warn that the economy had grown smoothly for 34 consecutive months and it was time to preempt inflation with a small rate hike. Vice President Gore objected: a small hike would signal the beginning of a long series, bond markets would crash. Greenspan assured him that higher short-term rates would actually signal vigilance against inflation, lowering long-term rates. On February 4, the Fed raised rates by a gentle 25 basis points, from 3% to 3.25%. The stock market dipped as Greenspan expected. But the bond market did the opposite of what Greenspan predicted: a swift, equal increase in ten-year Treasury rates. Something mysterious was stirring.

In the era before shadow banking, Greenspan's logic might have held. But the new shadowbankers were not focused on inflation expectations. They focused on uncertainty. Leverage meant that even a small fall in bond prices could wipe out their thin capital base, so the mere possibility of a fall forced them to reduce risk. Shadowbanks dumped bonds, forcing long-term rates up — the opposite of what Greenspan expected. Then on February 11, trade talks between the Clinton administration and Japan broke down, the yen jumped 7%, and Druckenmiller lost $650 million in two days on an $8 billion bet against the yen. Losses from the yen shock forced hedge funds to dump assets everywhere. European long-term rates spiked. The stampede built on its own momentum as brokers issued margin calls, demanding $3-5 million in collateral per $100 million of bonds instead of the usual $1 million.

The greatest casualty was Steinhardt himself. He returned from a vacation in China to find his biggest bets — in Japan, Canada, and especially Europe, where he had accumulated an astonishing $30 billion bond portfolio — hemorrhaging money. Each basis-point rise in European rates cost him $10 million. By the end of February he had bled a stunning $900 million and was down almost 20%. Seated at his bow-shaped desk, staring at seven blinking computer screens, he witnessed the decomposition of his fund. He ate little, slept less. Known for explosive blowups, he now took to conferring in whispers. His veteran lieutenant John Lattanzio marched to the bond trading desk: "Just sell 'em! Just sell 'em!" The trader answered flatly: "I can't." In a four-day selling burst, they dumped $1 billion in European bonds. The Canadian central bank periodically called to ask nervously whether Steinhardt's traders were done selling.

"Trade in European bonds was crowded, fact that totally passed me by." — Michael Steinhardt

When the dust settled at the end of March, Steinhardt's funds were down 30%. About $1.3 billion had vaporized. The collapse was followed by the implosion of Askin Capital Management, a $2.5 billion fund run by David Askin, who claimed his exotic mortgage derivatives were "market neutral" and that proprietary software modeled their risk. No such model existed. When the Fed raised rates, his instruments fell five times harder than ordinary bonds. Askin covered it up by misreporting results, then came clean: down 20% in February. Brokers issued margin calls. When he tried to sell, two of his positions — "forward inverse IOs" — attracted no bids at all. Nobody on the Street knew how to value them.

The panic reached the top echelons of government. President Bill Clinton interrupted a California vacation, phoned Robert Rubin, and demanded to know what was happening. Rubin labored for half an hour to explain why long-term rates were soaring. Nobody seemed to understand. Clinton walked out to face reporters: "No one believes there's serious problem with the underlying American economy." The plea encapsulated the standard response for a generation — and misconceived the challenge. In the new leveraged world, overreaction was not a choice but a necessity. The whole point of leverage was that investors felt ripples in magnified form. They were forced to keep fingers on hair triggers. Overreaction became mandatory.

Steinhardt hung on through 1995, turning in a surprisingly good 27% return and recouping $700 million, then announced his retirement. After 27 years, $1 invested with him in 1967 would be worth $480 — 26 times more than the S&P 500. Retirement gave him time to dance with Martha, the elegant blue crane on his country estate.

Soros Versus Soros

In the mid-1990s, Tiger Management's Dwight Anderson flew to Noril'sk, a mining city in the Siberian Arctic built by Stalin with slave labor — perhaps 200,000 political prisoners died in its construction. At minus 40 degrees, Anderson touched down in half-light on an airport littered with wrecked aircraft carcasses plundered for spare parts. He was surprised to find the mine working better than expected: tunnels so vast you could drive through in a truck, with multiple alternative routes if any one section broke down. He concluded that stories of imminent collapse were deliberately exaggerated — local officials understood that rumors were bullish for palladium prices, since they all had stakes in the mine's sales. Tiger held on to its $40 million palladium position even as the investment thesis evolved.

The real drama played out in Southeast Asia. Late in 1996, Arminio Fraga, a Princeton-trained Brazilian economist at Soros Fund Management, heard Stan Fischer of the IMF suggest that "some imbalances in Asia" might be interesting to look at. Fraga flew to Bangkok in January 1997 and found a country that fitted the double-crisis model perfectly: exports hammered by China's rise, currency pegged to a strong dollar, running a large trade deficit, and banks that were fragile. At a meeting with a high-ranking Bank of Thailand official, Fraga listened as the man conceded that the currency peg might be unsustainable. The Soros team could visualize a suitcase full of money, cash spilling from the seams. To reel in the prize, they had to pretend they hadn't noticed it. Fraga nodded pleasantly and let the discussion move on. Then David Kowitz gently led the conversation backward: "Excuse me, can you just repeat what you said a few minutes ago?" The official repeated himself — he had confessed and reconfessed his nakedness.

The Soros team sold short roughly $2 billion of baht. Thailand fought back with capital controls, forbidding banks from lending baht to foreigners. Tiger's costs hit $10 million per day. Regional newspapers reported gleefully on hedge funds losing "the battle of the baht." The prime minister called the central bank to promise staff a victory party. But Rodney Jones, Soros's Hong Kong economist, discovered the central bank had burned through $21 billion of reserves in May alone — two thirds of its war chest. On July 1, 1997, Tiger unleashed $1 billion in baht selling. The Bank of Thailand ran out of ammunition at 4:30 AM. The baht fell 32% over three months. Soros funds gained roughly $750 million. Thailand's output collapsed 17% from its peak.

Soros the trader and Soros the statesman-philosopher were at war with each other. At the World Bank/IMF meetings in Hong Kong that September, Malaysia's Mahathir Mohamad called him a "criminal" and a "moron." But Soros the statesman was the toast of the meetings, telling a packed auditorium: "The main enemy of the open society, I believe, is no longer the communist but the capitalist threat." He told his lieutenants the time for shorting Asian currencies had passed — even as his team bought $1 billion in Indonesian rupiah, believing the turmoil had spilled over without justification. When Suharto's cronies frustrated reform and the central bank printed money to pay the president's friends, the rupiah collapsed. The trade cost Soros $400 million. His team met Indonesia's finance minister Mar'ie Muhammad at the Plaza Hotel. Muhammad refused to meet their gaze, mumbling through evasive answers.

"Oh my God. I can't believe that we are long. I don't even believe anything that guy said. I don't even believe he's from Indonesia." — Stan Druckenmiller

Rodney Jones discovered that South Korea's reserves were a fraction of what was reported — official number $57 billion, real number closer to $20 billion, with $60 billion in understated dollar debts. His memo of November 17 warned Korea was "in the late stage of the crisis." The Soros team took no action. No won short. The boss wanted to be a statesman, not a wrecker of nations. In January 1998, Soros traveled to Seoul as a guest of president-elect Kim Dae-jung. Camera crews greeted him at the airport. The KOSPI index jumped 25% in ten days. Asked about the contrast between Mahathir and Kim, Soros said: "One of them must be wrong."

The messianic impulse reached its zenith in Russia. Soros went over the heads of Druckenmiller and his entire team to take an astonishing $980 million gamble in Svyazinvest, Russia's state telephone utility. He secretly lent the Russian government several hundred million dollars so Yeltsin could pay back wages.

"I deliberately chose to expose myself. To be a selfless benefactor was just a little too good to be true. It fed my self-image as a godlike creature... I have talked about my messianic fantasies; I am not ashamed of them." — George Soros

The Enemy Is Us

John Meriwether had been one of the first to see the potential in financial engineering. At Salomon Brothers, he transformed his trading group into a "quasi-university environment," recruiting Eric Rosenfeld from Harvard, Larry Hilibrand from MIT, Robert Merton and Myron Scholes who would win the Nobel Prize. By the end of the 1980s, this small faculty accounted for 90% of Salomon's profits. Hilibrand once campaigned to close the investment banking division entirely: "What was the purpose of 6,000 other employees?" After a Treasury bond scandal forced Meriwether's resignation in 1991, he resolved to create "Salomon without the bullshit." In February 1994, he launched Long-Term Capital Management from a rented space at 600 Steamboat Road in Greenwich — the same building as Paul Tudor Jones. The quants showed up in golf shirts and chinos, and when bluefish jumped in Long Island Sound, they armed themselves with fishing rods and raced outside.

LTCM earned $2.1 billion in 1996 and announced the return of $2.7 billion to outside investors — in the new world of leverage, they didn't need client cash. Their biggest equity bet was selling options betting on market steadiness, effectively becoming the "central bank of volatility." Their value-at-risk calculations said it would take a ten-sigma event — a probability of roughly one in 10 to the 24th power — to put them out of business.

"We thought we were being conservative." — Eric Rosenfeld

On August 17, 1998, Russia defaulted on its debt. LTCM had little direct Russia exposure, but the indirect effects were devastating. By the end of Friday, August 21, the fund had lost $550 million — 15% of its capital. Partners were scattered on vacation: Meriwether in China, Rosenfeld in Idaho. Every trade went wrong in correlated fashion, not because the positions were economically similar but because the same type of fund held them all. LTCM's success had created a special vulnerability: an army of imitators had pieced together the strategy. The superportfolio of disciples was even larger, and there was nobody to sell to.

Vinny Mattone, an old bond trader, called Meriwether: "Where are you?" "We're down by half." "You're finished." "What are you talking about? We still have two billion." "When you're down by half, people figure you can go down all the way. They're going to push the market against you." By Labor Day, LTCM had lost 44% of its capital — $1.9 billion. The calculated probability of such a loss should have occurred less than once in the lifetime of the universe.

Rosenfeld phoned Warren Buffett. Refused. Meriwether breakfasted with Soros. Soros offered $500 million if Meriwether raised another $500 million. The more LTCM scrambled to raise money, the more panic spread. When Meriwether's investor letter leaked, the Wall Street Journal ran a front-page story and every player on Wall Street started trading against the fund. LTCM's hurricane bonds — securities that sold hurricane risk to insurers — plummeted 20%, though the probability of a hurricane was utterly unchanged.

"It's not about the market anymore; it's about you. If you're long, they're short. If you're short, they're long." — Jim Rickards, LTCM general counsel

Peter Fisher of the New York Fed drove from New Jersey to Greenwich and discovered that LTCM held a $120 billion portfolio with extraordinarily concentrated bets — perhaps half the open interest in British gilt futures, an outlandish position in Danish mortgages, enormous equity options. The issue was not just a broad market fall but that particular markets might cease trading altogether. Hedge funds had no assets for regulators to seize — all were pledged as collateral. If LTCM defaulted, cross-default clauses would trigger automatically, and closeout agreements would flutter from a thousand fax machines. Fisher brokered a consortium of eleven banks, each putting in $300 million for a total of $3.625 billion. Meriwether and his partners saw their personal fortunes reduced to almost nothing.

"We lost money the old-fashioned way: through honesty and hard work." — Jim Rickards

The Tech Bubble

The dot-com bubble was the ultimate test of whether sophisticated traders could enforce market efficiency. Books-A-Million jumped tenfold in three days after announcing website improvements. Priceline.com gained 425% on its first trading day — an untested airline ticket site was worth more than United, Continental, and Northwest combined. If hedge funds existed to ensure rational pricing, this was their moment.

They failed. Julian Robertson declined to fight the bubble directly, having lost money shorting tech in 1998. The NASDAQ was "a locomotive hurtling down the tracks" — certain to derail but on an unknown timetable. Robertson concentrated on traditional value: auto parts companies, power utilities. His massive position in US Airways, once worth $1.5 billion, imploded when machinists rejected a pay contract and work stoppages forced flight cancellations. Tiger couldn't sell without triggering a free fall. By the summer of 1999, day traders in their kitchens were outperforming Robertson's special-forces unit. Tiger lost $3 billion in net withdrawals over six months. Rivals knew Robertson was a forced seller and front-ran him, driving his known positions down further. When he reported his top ten stock positions in monthly investor letters, the market punished every one. In June, a rumor that Tiger faced $3 billion in month-end withdrawals triggered a predatory selling spasm — Tiger lost $72 million in fifteen minutes on an otherwise steady market day.

Robertson never stopped being right about fundamentals. In January 2000, he declared: "There has to be a day of reckoning." The day came on March 10. But it was too late — Robertson had made his decision to exit. On March 30, with the NASDAQ already 15% off its peak, he told investors that rational valuation measures had taken a backseat to "mouse clicks and momentum." He had no stomach for more punishment. The market had stayed irrational longer than he had stayed solvent.

Druckenmiller's ride was wilder. He started 1999 by shorting a dozen overvalued start-ups — "They'd close one day at a hundred and open at one forty." Within weeks the position cost Quantum $600 million. Down 18% by May, he hired Carson Levit to buy dot-com stocks, attended the Sun Valley tech conference, returned converted, and piled into "radioactive shit that I don't know how to spell." VeriSign, Qualcomm, Gemstar. By year-end, he had turned a losing year into a 35% gain — one of the great comebacks in hedge fund history. Then in February 2000, watching biotech firm Celera Genomics skyrocket, he dumped everything. "This is fucking nuts." Soros: "I'm really glad you're doing it." But the NASDAQ kept rising. Druckenmiller bought it all back. When the crash came on March 10, he was fully loaded. Quantum lost roughly a tenth of its capital. Druckenmiller left for a vacation in Florida.

"Money is supposed to be enjoyed, but if I can't enjoy two weeks with my kids, what's the point of it all? I'm tired, exhausted." — Stan Druckenmiller

On April 28, 2000, Soros convened a press conference. Quantum was down 21% for the year. Druckenmiller was leaving. Quantum would henceforth be managed as a sedate endowment. "We have come to realize that a large hedge fund like Quantum Fund is no longer the best way to manage money." Within one month, the two largest hedge funds in history had pulled down their shutters. But it was far too early to write the industry's epitaph.

The Yale Men

On June 1, 2001, 2,920 people filled the Jacob Javits Center for the Robin Hood Foundation gala conceived by Paul Tudor Jones. The charity had distributed $90 million fighting poverty. One guest paid $540,000 for lunch with a leading financial mogul. Another paid $260,000 for a part in Russell Crowe's A Beautiful Mind. Sonia Jones bid $420,000 for a yoga lesson with Madonna and Gwyneth Paltrow. Jerry Seinfeld urged the auction, cannons spewed confetti, Robert Plant strutted onto the dance floor. The evening pulled in $113.5 million.

The money flooding into hedge funds in the 2000s came largely because of one man: David Swensen of Yale. Tall, angular, ascetic, he had grown up in River Falls, Wisconsin, founded a recycling club through his church group, and originally intended to become a senator. After a PhD at Yale and a stint at Salomon Brothers where he helped create the first currency swap, he was lured back to run Yale's endowment in 1985, taking an 80% pay cut. A Wall Street admirer once remarked he could have been a billionaire running a hedge fund. "What's the matter with you?" "A genetic defect."

Swensen's key insight was that hedge funds offered equity-sized returns uncorrelated with the market index — the "free lunch" of diversification that James Tobin had described. The moralist in Swensen had no desire to enrich hedge fund managers, but the economist was impressed by the design of their incentives: performance fees meant they didn't get paid just for scale, and the best ones kept their own savings in the fund. By 1995, Yale's allocation to hedge funds reached 21%. Over the three years ending June 2003, the S&P 500 lost 33% while Yale gained 20%. Swensen's decisions generated $7.8 billion of Yale's $14 billion endowment. By 2009, roughly half the capital in hedge funds came from institutions rather than individuals.

The fund that proved Swensen's thesis was Farallon, run by Yale alumnus Tom Steyer. When Steyer first pitched Swensen in 1987, he was told: "We're not interested. We'll never be interested." Steyer persisted. Long after building Farallon into one of the world's biggest hedge funds, he was still renowned for his beat-up car, habit of flying commercial, and utter indifference to fashion. His office was a desk in the middle of an open-plan hallway. Behind it: a breathtaking panorama of San Francisco. He kept the blinds down. What converted Swensen was an incident at a 1989 lunch with Steyer and partner Fleur Fairman. Swensen voiced his concern that hedge funds would stiff clients if strategies went wrong — rather than working without compensation, they'd just close shop and reopen under a new name. "That's a bunch of bullshit!" Fairman exploded. "If you think that's who we are then we don't want your money anyway!" Swensen had found the integrity he was looking for. In January 1990, Yale invested $300 million in Farallon.

Farallon's most audacious deal was buying Bank Central Asia, Indonesia's largest bank, in November 2001. Andrew Spokes, a Goldman recruit, led due diligence in a country where the airport-to-taxi stretch was viewed as a "natural kill zone." The investment case was elegant: BCA was essentially a government bond fund with cheap retail deposits and a free option on Indonesia's recovery. Farallon won with a $531 million bid — "a hedge fund from latte land bought control of the top bank in the world's largest Muslim country." By 2006, BCA's share price was up 550%. Portfolio investment into Indonesia surged from $286 million the year before the purchase to $4 billion. An event-driven fund had created an event, helping turn the economic tide for 240 million people.

The Code Breakers

Jim Simons's Renaissance Technologies did something no other fund managed: it hired established scientists rather than young quants or Wall Street veterans, located itself on Long Island far from the hedge fund centers of Greenwich and Manhattan, and explicitly rejected ideas from academic finance. The faculty initially held a weekly journal club reading finance papers, then abandoned it as fruitless.

The key figures were Peter Brown and Robert Mercer, who came from IBM's research center in 1993 and drove the Medallion Fund's extraordinary success. Brown was a magnesium flare — sleeping five hours a night, riffing passionately on topics, riding a unicycle around the office. Mercer was icy cold, a poker player who never recalled having a nightmare. Before Renaissance, they had upended computerized translation at IBM. Traditional programmers taught computers grammar rules. Brown and Mercer — neither of whom spoke French — instead got Canada's parliamentary records (thousands of paired French and English passages) and told the computer to figure out the correlations. Where a linguist would painstakingly code rules, the computer simply searched patterns until nearly 99% of uncertainty was eliminated.

"Every time I fire a linguist, my system's performance improves." — Fred Jelinek, IBM

Applied to markets, this philosophy meant letting the data speak without imposing human theories. Where D. E. Shaw started with market theories and tested them against data — like teaching a computer grammar — Brown and Mercer fed data first and let the computer find answers, like code-breakers without a grammar book. The danger was false patterns. Mercer acknowledged the risk but added a crucial twist: "Some signals that make no intuitive sense do indeed work. The signals that we have been trading without interruption for fifteen years make no sense. Otherwise someone else would have found them." It was the nonintuitive signals that proved most lucrative.

The Renaissance campus featured a gym, tennis courts, a pond with goldfish, and an article posted on an office door: "Why Most Published Research Findings Are False." Scientists roamed freely. Mirrors were positioned at corners so you could see if Brown was coming on his unicycle. Every refinement began with a presentation at the Tuesday morning Big Meeting of 90+ PhDs, followed by peer review and a smaller decision meeting. Pay was tied to the profits of the entire firm rather than any subunit, ensuring collaboration. By the 2000s, Medallion was closed to outside investors and consisted almost entirely of employee money. It was, in effect, a Formula One race car — faster than anything else on the track, but not designed to carry passengers.

Amaranth

By 2005, more than 8,000 hedge funds operated worldwide. The old talk of genius and luck had faded, replaced by the language of manufacturing. At conferences from Phoenix to Monaco, consultants held forth about a scientific product called alpha that could be explained, replicated, and produced at scale. Hedge Funds for Dummies appeared. Madeleine Albright, the former secretary of state, jumped into the industry. The era of the alpha factory had arrived.

Amaranth embodied the factory's hollowness. Founded in 2000 by convertible-arbitrage specialist Nick Maounis, it shape-shifted from one strategy to another — merger-arb, convertible- arb, bonds, stat-arb, energy — without any clear edge in deciding which strategy to pursue. The star was Brian Hunter, a six-foot-five young Canadian who commuted to the Calgary office in a Ferrari (switching to the Bentley on snowy days). Hunter had been fired from Deutsche Bank after his trading group lost $51 million in one week. His response was brazen: he ascribed the loss to "unprecedented" price moves, demanded his bonus anyway, and pointed out he had personally earned $40 million for the bank that year.

In August 2005, Hurricanes Katrina and Rita devastated natural gas production. Hunter's gas trades earned $1.26 billion — all of Amaranth's profits for the year. He reportedly pocketed $126 million. The Christmas mailing included toy gasoline pumps quoting Benjamin Franklin: "Energy and persistence alter all things." Seldom had one of the Founders been taken so out of context.

Maounis pumped the energy desk's allocation from 2% to roughly 30% of the fund, effectively betting the company on a thirty-two-year-old trader who had been with the firm barely a year. By February 2006, Hunter held 70% of November NYMEX gas futures and about 60% of January contracts. He was a momentum trader who traded on his own momentum — his buying drove the spread between summer and winter gas from $1.40 to $2.20. The fundamentals were moving against him: gas output had recovered from the hurricanes, winter weather was mild, and storage was nearly 40% above the five-year average. When a team from Blackstone visited Hunter in Calgary and examined the real risks, they withdrew their $125 million.

On Thursday, September 14, 2006, Amaranth lost $560 million in a single day. Investment bankers descended on the Greenwich office, devouring Pop-Tarts in the pantry. Ken Griffin of Citadel was working out at home when the email arrived: Amaranth down 50% in a month. He sent two lieutenants who "looked half the age of the investment-banking rivals." By 5:30 AM, they had verified there were no hidden holes. Citadel and J.P. Morgan took ownership of the portfolio. Citadel eventually earned roughly $1 billion in profit. Six billion dollars of investors' equity was gone — larger than LTCM's incineration. But the result was very different: no taxpayer harm, no Fed crisis meeting, no damage to the financial system. The market had disciplined Amaranth while inflicting minimal collateral damage.

"What Brian is really, really good at is taking controlled and measured risk." — Nick Maounis, WSJ interview, one month before the collapse

Riding the Storm

Daniel Sadek was born in Lebanon in 1968, scarred by a civil war gunshot wound. He landed in California at eighteen, worked as a gas station attendant and car salesman, and discovered he could get a home-loan license without any classes — you needed 1,500 hours of training to become a barber. By 2005, his company Quick Loan had 700 employees and ranked among the top 50 subprime lenders. Its marketing: "No income verification. Instant qualification!!" Sadek bankrolled a movie called Redline — "an action flick loaded with cars, chrome, and silicone." The cast demolished $2 million of his sports cars during filming. The movie's tagline: "Fear nothing; risk everything."

John Paulson was Daniel Sadek's opposite in every way — neither tall nor short, handsome nor plain, eager nor standoffish. "Mr. Average." He had grown his hedge fund from $2 million to $4 billion through quiet, meticulous merger arbitrage, with only seven analysts on staff. He specialized in betting the long odds: against mergers expected to succeed, or in favor of surprise bids the market didn't see coming. His deeper skill lay in capital-structure arbitrage — the insight that a company's senior and junior bonds would behave very differently in bankruptcy.

In the spring of 2005, Paulson found the dream target. Mortgage securities combined every charm a short seller could want: a cyclical industry loaded with mountains of debt, sliced into senior and junior tranches. The BBB tranche of a typical subprime CDO was only 1% thick — a nonpayment rate of just 6% would wipe it out completely. And unlike a company, a pile of worthless loans had no franchise value. Zero would mean zero. His analyst Paolo Pellegrini, a tall, elegant Italian with an amused twinkle, bought the largest mortgage database in the country and proved that if house prices merely went flat — not even fell — the default rate would reach at least 7%, wiping out all BBB bonds.

The risk-reward was absurd. A $600 million fund spending 7% on insurance would lose $42 million in the worst case. If defaults hit 80% — which Paulson considered highly likely — the gain would be 909%. When he explained this to potential investors, few thought he was sane. When Wall Street created the ABX index in July 2006, making it easy to bet against subprime at scale, Paulson remembered Soros's injunction: go for the jugular. "What's your picture?" the trader asked. "Five hundred million." "Done." "Another five hundred million." "Done." Next day, another billion. By calling around the banks, he amassed positions totaling $7.2 billion.

On February 7, 2007, New Century Financial postponed earnings. HSBC set aside $10.6 billion in loan-loss reserves. The next morning, the ABX slid five points. Each 1% decline was worth $250 million to Paulson. He netted $1.25 billion in a single morning — about as much as Soros earned from sterling. An incredulous client called: "Is this a misprint? It's 6.6 percent, right, not 66 percent?" One summer day, a colleague whispered something during a meeting. Paulson returned beaming: "We just got our marks for the day. We made a billion dollars." The flagship fund gained 700% by the end of 2007. The firm generated an estimated $15 billion in profits. Paulson personally pocketed $3 to $4 billion — "the man who made too much."

Paulson's subprime bet was the twenty-first-century version of the policy errors earlier hedge funds had exploited. In the 1970s, incompetent central banks stoked inflation, handing commodity traders glorious trends. In the 1990s, unsustainable currency pegs gave macro traders irresistible targets. Each new era brought a fresh kind of blunder, creating fresh opportunity. The heyday of macro hedge funds might be over, but a new heyday of credit hedge funds had arrived. John Paulson was the new George Soros.

The 2008 Crisis

Policy makers estimated 50/50 odds that Lehman Brothers' failure would bring calm or chaos. From a trader's perspective, those odds made chaos virtually certain — the asymmetrical payout meant rational hedge funds would bet aggressively on collapse. News leaked on a Sunday lunchtime. American hedge funds realized Lehman's London operation would declare bankruptcy under British law, freezing segregated accounts. Lawyers rushed from Hamptons weekend homes. Eric Rosenfeld, the LTCM veteran, said: "How could they do this?" Tudor Jones had $100 million frozen in Lehman London — his withdrawal request had arrived one day late.

"You knew Lehman Brothers would be kickoff for a big down move. You knew that." — Paul Tudor Jones

Tudor had allowed his emerging-market credit team to build a giant portfolio of loans to banks in Kazakhstan, Russia, and Ukrainian dairy farms. Once Lehman collapsed, the portfolio lost roughly two thirds of its value, costing Tudor over $1 billion. The loans were utterly illiquid. "That was the worst moment of my whole life," Jones said. "I used to always think, 'How'd these guys in 1929 lose it all?' I see how these guys in '29 got hurt now. They had things that they couldn't get out of."

The crisis destroyed the investment-bank model overnight. Merrill Lynch CEO John Thain negotiated through the night and agreed to sell Merrill to Bank of America for $29 per share. A year earlier, Merrill had rebuffed an offer worth $90 — "as if Wal-Mart were buying Tiffany's." Morgan Stanley and Goldman Sachs, the last survivors, were both vulnerable to bank runs. John Mack of Morgan raged against short-selling conspirators. By Thursday, New York senators Schumer and Clinton were calling the SEC chairman to demand a short-selling ban. Treasury Secretary Hank Paulson insisted: "You have to save them now or they'll be gone while you're still thinking about it." On Goldman's trading floor, three dozen traders stood, placed their hands over their hearts, and sang "The Star-Spangled Banner" as the ban was announced over the loudspeaker.

Ken Griffin of Citadel received no such protection. The government had kicked him in the teeth with the short-selling ban, making it impossible to hedge his giant convertible-bond portfolio. His probability tree: Morgan Stanley had 50% survival odds; if Morgan fell, Goldman followed with 95% certainty; if Goldman failed, Citadel was nearly 100% certain to go under. Overall survival odds: roughly 55%.

"That's a pretty bad day — when you realize twenty years of your work comes down to whether or not some firm that you have no influence over fails." — Ken Griffin

On October 24, Dan Dufresne, Griffin's young head of treasury, was heading for the train when a European bank called: rumors were spreading that the Fed was in Citadel's office organizing a liquidation. Dufresne took a cab instead — not a conversation for a crowded commuter train. By the time he reached his desk, three and four calls were coming in. Credit default swaps on Citadel bonds were trading at levels signaling more trouble than Lehman on the eve of bankruptcy. James Forese of Citigroup called Griffin directly. "You're calling me for one of three reasons. One, to see if I'm alive..." Forese offered to handle discreet liquidation. Griffin: "We're losing a lot of money. But we've got a lot of liquidity." Forese suggested a bondholder conference call — the format gave assurances extra weight because Griffin could face regulatory trouble for lying. The call was scheduled for 3:30 PM. Well over a thousand callers tried to dial in. On some Wall Street trading floors, it was played over loudspeakers. The hold music was grating techno.

After a 25-minute delay arranging more lines, Gerald Beeson delivered the pitch: yes, down 35%, but the firm was a long way from running out of cash. Financing was secured. An untapped $8 billion credit line stood behind them. Someone held up a sign: "To call this a dislocation doesn't go anywhere near the enormity of what we've seen." Griffin mustered all the confidence he could manage: "We will prosper in the new era of finance." The call lasted twelve minutes. The next day, the CNBC truck was gone from its usual position outside Citadel Center. The fires were doused — for the time being. By year-end, Citadel's flagship funds were down 55%. Nine billion dollars had evaporated, equivalent to at least two Long-Term Capitals. But there was no taxpayer bailout, no systemic destabilization. Investors had risked their capital, been rewarded for years, and were now forced to take their losses. Exactly as it should work.

The Case for Hedge Funds

Even in the worst period in their history, hedge funds proved their worth. The industry lost 19% in 2008, but the S&P 500 fell twice as much. Almost 1,500 funds went bust. Unlike banks, investment banks, and home lenders, hedge funds imposed no costs on taxpayers or society. The point was largely lost amid the crisis, but the policy implications were profound: hedge funds manage risk better than their rivals, recognize setbacks faster, and liquidate before secondary effects hit the system. The chief prescription was counterintuitive: don't regulate.

Hedge fund managers have their own wealth at risk alongside clients. Investment banks distributed 50% of net revenues as salary and bonuses — a far larger incentive to gamble with other people's money. Bank compensation was variable and set without shareholder consultation. Hedge fund fees were fixed ahead of time and backed by high-water marks: if a fund lost money, it earned no performance fees until it had earned the losses back. Actively managed mutual funds, by contrast, charged roughly 1% per year and delivered no alpha at all — their fee per unit of alpha was literally infinite.

Do hedge funds destabilize markets? Most stabilize them. Arbitrage funds drive prices toward rational levels. Stock pickers allocate capital to firms that will use it productively. Commodity traders responding to gluts and shortages generate price signals that force healthy adjustment. Trend following can contribute to overshooting — oil rising from $61 to $96 in 2007 was probably rational, but the spike to $145 probably was not. But hedge funds are more likely to be contrarian than traditional managers: they can go short as well as long, they are judged on absolute returns rather than benchmark- relative performance, and their performance fees give them both the resources and the incentive to bet against the crowd.

The one argument for regulation concerned size. When individual funds grew so large and leveraged that they became too complex to fail, they warranted scrutiny. LTCM had under $5 billion in capital but was leveraged 25 to 1, creating a $120 billion portfolio plus $1.2 trillion in derivatives, trading in illiquid, esoteric markets. Amaranth at $9 billion had normal leverage and traded on exchanges — no red flags. The proposal was tiered: at $120 billion in leveraged assets, regulatory examination of derivatives and liquidity; at $200 billion, scrutiny of leverage and extra capital requirements; for any fund going public, more frequent examination. This would leave nearly all hedge funds outside the net — only 39 worldwide had capital over $10 billion as of early 2009.

In 1949, when Jones launched his hedged fund, the merchant banks — Goldman, Morgan Stanley, Lehman — were private operations deploying their partners' capital, much like today's hedge funds. They managed risk ferociously because they were speculating with their own money. They were largely unregulated. Over the next half century, they sold shares, expanded globally, and unlocked millions for their leaders. But every expansion created fresh opportunity to risk shareholders' capital and collect what amounted to a 50% quasi-performance fee. The incentives pushed investment banks to take ever greater risk until they paid the price. By the end of 2008, Goldman and Morgan Stanley became bank holding companies, needing the Fed's safety net to survive. Today, hedge funds are the new merchant banks. The same logic that tempted old merchant banks to go public will seduce some hedge funds. When that happens, they will pose the threat to the financial system they have wrongly been accused of posing in the past.

Key Takeaways

Markets Are Not Efficient

Every chapter provides evidence that prices deviate from rational values — Jones profited from stock picking, Steinhardt from block-trading discounts, Soros from currency pegs maintained by politics rather than economics, Paulson from mortgage securities priced as if home prices could never decline nationally.

Leverage Is the Industry's Central Paradox

Leverage gives hedge funds the firepower to correct mispricings and earn outsized returns. It also makes them vulnerable to sudden shocks and forces hair-trigger responses. The 1994 bond crisis, LTCM, and the 2008 meltdown all demonstrated how leverage amplifies both the signal and the noise.

Government Failure Creates Each Generation's Opportunity

The 1970s delivered inflation for commodity traders. The 1990s delivered currency pegs for macro traders. The 2000s delivered housing bubbles for credit traders. Each era's dominant policy error creates the next era's dominant hedge fund strategy.

Culture Matters More Than Regulation

Hedge funds survived 2008 better than banks because of paranoid, entrepreneurial culture: managers with their own money at risk, no safety net, constant marking to market. Banks had deposit insurance, central bank backstops, and complacent bureaucratic structures.

Small Is Beautiful, Big Is Dangerous

Nearly 5,000 hedge funds failed between 2000 and 2009 without triggering a single taxpayer bailout. The danger arises only when individual funds grow so large and complex that their failure threatens the system — as LTCM demonstrated in 1998.

Contrarianism Requires Capital

Robertson was right about the tech bubble but ran out of capital before the market proved him right. Paulson was right about subprime but needed patient investors willing to tolerate years of small insurance premiums. Being right eventually is not enough if you can't survive being early.

Transparency Can Be Destabilizing

LTCM's leaked positions allowed rivals to trade against it. Tiger's monthly disclosure of top holdings enabled predatory front-running. The lesson complicates calls for hedge fund transparency: in a leveraged world, publicity can turn a stressed fund into a dead one.

Incentive Design Is Everything

Jones's performance fee and partnership structure aligned interests. Bank traders risked shareholders' capital while collecting huge bonuses. AIG sold insurance without capital reserves. Bear Stearns launched internal hedge funds staffed by salesmen who had never managed risk. Every crisis in the book traces back to misaligned incentives.

Contrarian Ideas

Hedge Funds Are Safer Than Banks

The conventional view blames hedge funds for financial instability. Mallaby argues the opposite: hedge funds absorb risk that would otherwise sit in too-big-to-fail institutions, and they fail without requiring taxpayer bailouts.

Don't Regulate Hedge Funds

Against the post-crisis regulatory impulse, Mallaby argues that regulating most hedge funds would be counterproductive — it would constrain the contrarian trading that keeps markets efficient while doing nothing to prevent the real culprits (overleveraged banks and insurers) from causing damage.

Performance Fees Are Fair

The 20% fee sounds extortionate, but it aligns incentives far better than the 1% management fee charged by mutual funds that deliver no alpha. The fee per unit of alpha at a typical mutual fund is literally infinite.

Short Sellers Are Heroes, Not Villains

Short selling is consistently demonized as unpatriotic or destabilizing. But short sellers are the market's early warning system — they identify fraud, excess, and misallocation before anyone else.

The 2008 Crisis Was Not a Hedge Fund Crisis

The culprits were commercial banks, investment banks, government-sponsored enterprises, and insurers — all regulated institutions with implicit or explicit government backing. Hedge funds were casualties, not causes.

Allegories & Cultural References

The Phoenician Merchants

Jones claimed his 20% performance fee was inspired by ancient Phoenician sea merchants who kept a fifth of the profits from successful voyages — linking hedge fund compensation to the oldest risk-taking tradition in civilization.

The I. M. Pei Stiletto

LTCM's party at the Bank of China building in Hong Kong serves as metaphor for the fund itself: transformed from squat and heavy into a soaring, daring creation — slim and elegant through the miracles of modern leverage, but hollow-sided and vulnerable to any unexpected sideways blow.

Napoleon and Russia

Soros's fascination with Russia echoed Napoleon's: drawn by its vastness, its historical challenge, its perpetually unfulfilled promise. Both were ultimately humbled.

The Hyperbolic Curve

During the August 2007 quant quake, AQR's Mike Mendelson sketched a hyperbolic curve on a notepad showing how leverage accelerates toward infinity as losses mount — the visual representation of why margin-driven selling becomes a death spiral.

"Darkness at Noon"

Harvard's 1950s investment course was nicknamed after the Koestler novel — not for political content but because it was so unpopular it was relegated to a lunchtime slot. A marker of how marginal finance was in the academy before hedge funds made it glamorous.

Frameworks & Models

Jones's Four Pillars

Performance fees, regulatory flexibility, long/short structure, and leverage — the architectural blueprint that every subsequent hedge fund either adopted or reacted against.

Reflexivity

Soros's theory that investor perceptions create feedback loops with reality: rising prices breed optimism, which drives further buying, which confirms the optimism, until the cycle exhausts itself.

The Asymmetric Bet

The structure exploited by Druckenmiller (sterling), Fraga (baht), and Paulson (subprime): situations where the potential gain vastly exceeds the potential loss. When someone else — usually a government — is maintaining an artificial price, the downside is capped and the upside is enormous.

Carry Trade

Kovner's discovery that currencies trading at a forward discount often have high interest rates, signaling strength rather than weakness. Buy the high-yielding currency, collect the interest differential.

Capital-Structure Arbitrage

Paulson's technique of shorting junior bonds while sometimes going long senior bonds of the same issuer, exploiting the legal nuance that different tranches of debt receive very different treatment in bankruptcy.

Tiered Regulatory Framework

Mallaby's proposed solution: leave most hedge funds unregulated, but impose bank-style scrutiny on the handful whose leveraged assets exceed $120-200 billion or whose structures (going public) create too-big-to-fail dynamics.

More Money Than God: Hedge Funds and the Making of a New Elite

Sebastian Mallaby · 2010 · 495 pages

A sweeping narrative history of hedge funds from A.W. Jones's first "hedged fund" in 1949 through the 2008 financial crisis, told through the characters, trades, and crises that built, broke, and remade the industry — and arguing that hedge funds are the financial system's best-designed institutions.

About the Author

Sebastian Mallaby is the Paul A. Volcker Senior Fellow for international economics at the Council on Foreign Relations and a contributing columnist for the Washington Post. Born in Britain and educated at Oxford, he spent over a decade at The Economist, where he served as the magazine's Washington bureau chief and wrote its weekly editorial on international economic affairs. He later joined the Washington Post editorial board.

Mallaby's work sits at the intersection of financial history, policy analysis, and narrative journalism. His other books include The Man Who Knew: The Life and Times of Alan Greenspan (2016) and The World's Banker: A Story of Failed States, Financial Crises, and the Wealth and Poverty of Nations (2004), a biography of World Bank president James Wolfensohn. He has a particular gift for transforming complex financial mechanics into character-driven stories accessible to general readers.

Mallaby spent four years researching More Money Than God, conducting over 300 interviews with hedge fund managers, their associates, regulators, and academics. The book won the Financial Times and Goldman Sachs Business Book of the Year Award in 2010 and is widely considered the definitive history of the hedge fund industry.

Why This Book Matters

Published in 2010, just two years after the worst financial crisis since the Great Depression, More Money Than God arrived at a moment when the public was furious at Wall Street and eager to blame hedge funds for the catastrophe. Mallaby's meticulously researched argument — that hedge funds were casualties, not causes, of the crisis — was profoundly contrarian and deeply uncomfortable for politicians seeking easy villains. The book reframed the debate by showing that the institutions requiring taxpayer bailouts were regulated banks and insurers, while the unregulated hedge funds absorbed their losses privately.

Beyond its policy argument, the book is simply the best narrative history of hedge funds ever written. From Jones's post-Marxist intellectual journey to Soros's late-night phone calls during the sterling crisis to Steinhardt whispering at his trading desk as $1.3 billion evaporated, Mallaby captures the human drama behind the numbers with novelistic detail. The characters — brilliant, obsessive, flawed, sometimes monstrous — are rendered with the complexity they deserve.

Most importantly, the book's central insight has only grown more relevant: that financial innovation depends on incentive design, not regulation. The question of whether large financial institutions should be structured as partnerships with their own capital at risk or as publicly traded corporations gambling with shareholders' money remains the central fault line of financial policy. Every crisis since 2010 has reinforced Mallaby's argument that the answer lies not in more rules but in better structures.

What Didn't Fit

Mallaby's history of hedge funds reads like a thriller. Here are the scenes that did not make the chapter coverage.

Anderson's $4 Billion Russian Metals Deal

Dwight Anderson of Soros Fund Management nearly pulled off the deal of a lifetime: buying Russia's entire nongold precious metals stock for $4 billion. Anderson and his Russian counterpart got along well, and Yeltsin apparently knew and approved. Then a message arrived from Moscow: “commission required” — two contracts, one official, one secret with payment to a Cyprus bank. Anderson insisted it had to be one transparent contract. Silence, then: “What about Gibraltar?” Anderson explained himself again. The messages bounced back and forth until it became clear that no deal was possible without a bribe. For the Russians, there was no earthly reason to sell without a private commission. Anderson had to walk away from what would have been the single largest commodity trade in hedge fund history.

Goldman's Gorilla and the LTCM Inspection

When LTCM was desperate for capital, Goldman Sachs demanded half of LTCM's management company plus full access to the fund's strategies for a $1 billion investment. LTCM tried to get a nondisclosure signed; Goldman refused. Goldman's inspectors allegedly plugged an oversized laptop into LTCM's network and downloaded positions. Goldman denied it, but the proprietary desk soon began selling positions that bore a suspicious resemblance to LTCM's portfolio. A Goldman trader in London put it plainly: “If you think a gorilla has to sell, then you sure want to sell first.” Meanwhile Bear Stearns, LTCM's lead broker that had earned millions in fees, refused to contribute a single dollar to the rescue. David Komansky of Merrill Lynch exploded in Jimmy Cayne's face.

Farallon's Colorado Water Debacle

Tom Steyer's Farallon Capital invested in a plan to pump water from an aquifer beneath Colorado's San Luis Valley to supply Denver and Boulder. The local partner was Gary Boyce, a flamboyant horse trainer with big dreams. Farmers revolted, fearing their wells would run dry. A Colorado senator accused Yale — Farallon's biggest investor — of profiting at taxpayers' expense. An “unFarallon campaign” erupted on college campuses, with protesters in crony-capitalist costumes and a “transparency fairy” in a feathered mask. The lesson: if Farallon had spent more time in San Luis Valley, they might have realized Boyce was an unsatisfactory partner. In a fast-growing fund that doubles assets every four to five years, it is hard to grow in-house expertise as fast as incoming capital.

The Guillotine and the Anglo-Saxon Plot

After the 1994 bond crisis devastated European markets, politicians were incandescent. French finance minister Michel Sapin suggested guillotining troublemaking traders. Belgium's Willy Claes blamed Anglo-Saxon financiers for “plotting to divide Europe.” Soros was called to testify before Congressional hearings organized by Robert Johnson, where he lectured on reflexivity while legislators struggled to understand what hedge funds actually did. Macro funds began returning capital: Tudor Jones returned a third of his assets, Kovner returned two-thirds. The era of the lone macro genius was giving way to something more institutional.

Bear Stearns: 10-in-10 and 35-to-1 Leverage

Bear Stearns devised an ambitious “10 in 10” strategy: asset management revenues would rise to 10% of the firm's total by 2010, starting from insignificance. The pursuit of fee income sealed Bear's fate. They hurriedly assigned unqualified executives to launch internal hedge funds loaded with subprime mortgages. Ralph Cioffi, a salesman from Bear's sales desk, was put in charge. His plan: buy mortgage-backed securities and leverage them 35 to 1. It was the sort of bet that made sense to a deep-pocketed, fee-hungry parent company but would never have survived the scrutiny of a real hedge fund where the manager's own money was on the line. The difference between bank culture and hedge fund culture — between other people's money and your own — proved fatal.
