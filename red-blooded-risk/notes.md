## Chapter 1: What This Book Is and Why You Should Read It

### Risk, Danger, and Opportunity

**Risk** is something you dial up or down to accomplish a goal. **Danger** is one-sided negative. **Opportunity** is one-sided positive. Three tests distinguish them:

1. **Two-sidedness**: Risks have winning and losing outcomes. Dangers and opportunities have only one side.
2. **Measurability**: Risks are measurable; dangers/opportunities often are not.
3. **Control**: Risks refer to human interactions under our control; dangers/opportunities come from nature.

The distinction is in the eye of the beholder. NASCAR manipulating rules for an optimal number of fatal crashes treats danger as risk. The Ford Pinto memorandum mixed risk and danger.

### Red-Blooded Risk Management

**Thin-blooded** people treat risks as dangers and avoid them. **Hot-blooded** people treat risks as opportunities. **Cold-blooded** people treat both as risks. **Red-blooded** people feel excitement but understand that successful risk-taking is calculation, not instinct.

Red-blooded risk management uses three mathematical techniques to: turn any situation into a system with delineated risks/dangers/opportunities; optimize risks for the best outcome; arrange things so dangers and opportunities make maximum positive contributions.

This field emerged in the 1950s from rebels against conventional academic risk ideas. They sought wisdom from real risk-takers in disreputable places. In the 1970s they challenged conventional theory. By the 1980s they were on Wall Street, developing ideas between 1987-1992.

### The Rocket Scientists

These quants became known as "rocket scientists" — partly because several literally worked on rockets. The name captures their spirit: rigorous mathematical analysis tied firmly to physical reality, exploration, and adventure. Most were not true rocket scientists; they were "quants" or "financial engineers."

From 1982-1992, they **hollowed out the inside of Wall Street and rebuilt it** entirely on different principles. Few people on Wall Street noticed the fundamental change; they saw only minor external design changes and one day realized there was no more film to develop.

> "What I do isn't rocket science. Most of it is trivially simple and the rest is meticulous care rather than brilliance. But to be historically accurate, we're stuck with the term."

### Risk and Life

Risk-taking is a philosophy of life. Two sensible attitudes exist:

1. **Avoid risk** whenever possible, unless the potential payoff justifies it
2. **Embrace positive-edge opportunities** — risks that offer genuine advantage

Taking enough gambles ensures outcomes cluster around expected values: flip a coin a million times and you'll approach 50/50 heads and tails. The author describes himself as not taking huge risks but taking lots of risks. Success comes from learning this philosophical orientation and acting accordingly.

### Three Iron Rules for Risk Takers

- **Rule 1: Positive expectation** — must have an edge in all bets. A single $1 lottery ticket at $0.90 EV vs. $1.10 EV makes no practical difference; a million tickets makes all the difference.
- **Rule 2: Independent bets** — bets must be uncorrelated. Sometimes advantageous to flip coins for decisions to ensure randomness.
- **Rule 3: Proper bet sizing** — never lose so much you're taken out of the game, but bet very big when the right gambles come along.

No middle way: either avoid risk as much as practical or seek out as many risks as possible. Half bonds / half stocks still makes you a risk taker — just a low-risk one. Risk takers must enjoy volatility — there is no destination, you never stop gambling. Risk avoiders must learn to endure volatility to reach a planned destination.

### Play and Money

**Play** occurs within a delineated area isolated from the rest of the world. This isolation allows in-game events to be treated as risks. Play can have enormous real-world effect: elections, trials, wars, and markets are all games governed by rules. **Money** matters because optimizing risk requires goals and constraints measured in the same units.

### Bayesian Probability and the Numeraire Problem

**Bruno de Finetti's** example: what probability do you assign to life on Mars one billion years ago? Whatever price you'd buy/sell a security paying $1 if true — that price is your probability. Key insight from rocket scientists: the probability depends on the numeraire. Mars Expeditionary Currency (mecs) worth $1 today but $10 if life confirmed — priced in dollars, probability = 1%; priced in mecs, probability = 10%.

**Numeraire**: a unit of account assigning relative values. No perfect numeraire exists — just as there's no absolute frame of reference in physics. Rocket scientists' solution: probability distributions covering 95-99% of events, leaving 1-5% as undefined outcomes.

### Frequentism

Frequentism defines probability as long-term frequency. Fatal flaw: it can only tell us about probabilities created by the experimenter, not the ones we want to know. A frequentist testing at 5% can say fewer than 5% of rejected hypotheses will be true — but which 5% matters enormously.

**Levee example**: "1% chance this levee will be breached" sounds physical but contains implicit assumptions about the value of human life vs. property damage. Rejecting at 1% is consistent with 100 levees all having breach rejected — you can't distinguish certain-to-breach from certain-not-to-breach.

### Rationality

Classical economics separates decisions into **beliefs** (probability distribution) and **preferences** (utility function). But the separation is arbitrary: in real decisions (e.g., stop for gas on unfamiliar road?), people weigh probabilities and preferences simultaneously. Research shows many risk decisions can be modeled this way, but no single distribution+utility function explains all decisions even for one individual. Intelligent risk management must begin with a numeraire plus awareness that the numeraire doesn't cover all possible outcomes.

### Abraham Wald's Bomber Story (WWII)

Allied Air Force recommended armoring the most-hit areas of returning bombers. Statistician **Abraham Wald** put armor everywhere no damage was recorded. "The bombers hit in those places never came back." Using the obvious numeraire (recorded damage) led to exactly the wrong conclusion. This survivorship bias / wrong numeraire error is the single most common mistake in quantitative decision making.

### Bets

Traditional probability theory has you betting against yourself — "entirely pointless." Real bets require asking: who are you betting with? and who is doing the betting?

- Against an idiot: set price between your true belief and what you think they believe (maximize edge)
- Against an expert: set price at what you think they think (minimize expected loss)

**Frequentist NYC snow example**: "I reject snow in NYC tomorrow at 5% significance" is technically accurate (snow occurs <5% of days) but useless — you can only profit betting against someone who thinks NYC is in Australia. Real test: can you post odds on BetFair and take all comers?

Rocket scientists' own notion of probability: a distribution can only be defined with respect to a numeraire; cannot cover all possible outcomes; probabilities only defined up to a bid/ask spread. Unless someone can profit taking bets from everyone, no one is competent to define the probability of an event. Meaningful probabilities require either an active betting market or a hypothetical one with identified skilled bettors.

### Exponentials and Culture

Three reasons exponentials matter for risk:
1. **Sudden dramatic changes are usually exponentials** — small and unnoticed, then explosive growth hits a physical limit. People call this a Black Swan but the real story is the exponential buildup, not the collision with the limit.
2. **Exponentials trump risk** — **John Kelly** (1956) showed optimal exponential growth beats any other strategy. **Edward Thorp** named it "fortune's formula."
3. **Risk avoiders misuse exponentials** — CEOs targeting compound growth rates build exponentials that hit limits with unpredictable fallout.

Most difficult aspect of risk management: competition from older belief systems (superstition, religion, authority). Good risk decisions are paradoxically hard to defend — they produce alternating complacency and erratic-looking actions. Bad risk management is ingrained in social institutions.

The book represents consensus of successful modern quantitative risk takers in finance.

## Chapter 3: Pascal's Wager and the Seven Principles of Risk Management

### Pascal's Wager as a Risk Framework

Pascal's wager: cannot determine by reason whether God exists — must treat it as a bet. Any probability of infinite upside outweighs finite downside — belief is the dominant strategy. More realistic than dice: unknown outcomes, unknown probabilities, ability to gather information and influence outcomes.

Pascal's practical prescription: "be faithful, honest, humble, grateful, generous, a sincere friend, truthful." Key insight: we often don't know the stakes of the wager.

### The Asset Manager Analogy

An asset manager faces the same problem: known probabilities for normal returns; zero knowledge of crisis scenarios. Pre-1987 standard: set arbitrary limits on leverage, liquidity, counterparty quality, then maximize within those limits. Decisions made by intuition or (per **George Soros**) "by how much his back hurt."

### Principle I: Risk Duality

Split all risk analysis into two regimes: (1) **Normal events** — plentiful data, reliable quantitative conclusions; (2) **Abnormal events** — will occur, cannot be quantified, must survive and ideally exploit them.

Quantitative orthodoxy: ignore what you can't measure. **Nassim Taleb**: only tail events matter. Brown's position: both matter — you need the dual framework.

Brown's 1982 University of Chicago dissertation on this sat ignored until **October 19, 1987** crash. Market fell more than twice its previous single-day record. True surprise wasn't the size — surprise was the entire financial system realigning in a way no one predicted. Post-crash research: marketwide realignments happened once or twice a decade.

### Principle II: Valuable Boundary (VaR)

**Value at Risk (VaR)**, operationalized ~1992: 1% one-day VaR means the portfolio will lose more than VaR on 1 day in 100, assuming normal markets and no position changes. The 99 normal days provide data for optimization; the ~2-3 VaR break days per year are analyzed separately.

VaR must be set before trading begins and never adjusted afterward. Quality test: VaR breaks must be completely unpredictable. Development stole methods from sports betting point-spread setters; only gained acceptance by risking real money on it. Major discovery: risk in the well-behaved center of the distribution was far less understood than assumed.

> "VaR is the least you will lose on the worst 1 percent of your days, not the most you can lose."

VaR is not a measure of risk or worst-case loss. Institutional pressure for "rationality, control, auditability, explainability" has killed the value of VaR at many firms.

### Principle III: Risk Ignition

Traces back to **John Kelly** (1956): take the optimal amount of risk and you are guaranteed exponentially growing success. Taking less risk than optimal is not safer — it locks in a worse outcome. Taking more also leads to worse outcomes and often complete disaster.

#### Casino as Risk Ignition Model

Casino holds both sides of most bets plus an edge — gambling activity averages out quickly. Key distinction: quants focus on **edge** (fraction of each bet); casinos care about **hold** (fraction of chips bought that casino keeps) — edge and hold vary independently based on betting speed and bettor behavior. Casinos offer widely varying edges but strikingly similar holds. Optimal hold: low enough for high volume/growth, high enough to profit. Casinos return ~75% of losses as comps (food, lodging, entertainment, forgiven debts) to keep hold at optimal level.

**Nassim Taleb's casino example**: three biggest losses at a major casino one year were: (1) owner's daughter kidnapped for ransom, (2) employee hid mandatory tax forms under his desk for years → large government fine, (3) tiger mauled a star performer. None were quantifiable gambling risks — risk ignition works only inside the VaR limit.

> "Risk ignition is not simply a strategy pursued by a few hedge funds and quant proprietary traders. It drives the important changes in the world. It creates self-sustaining exponential growth."

Once quants learned to set VaR limits and provision for tail events, hedge funds went from producing moderately wealthy owners to **billionaires** — not because strategies changed but because risk management did. Financial institutions went from earning 15% of corporate profits to over 40%, doubling GDP share — attributed to improvements in risk management, not new markets.

### Principle IV: Money

Money and risk are inseparable. Inside the money economy, everything is measurable on a common scale. Outside: trust, loyalty, affection — not purchasable directly.

**Mountain climber vs. road builder**: Solo climber faces binary outcomes, sequential correlated risks. Road builder has continuous outcomes, independent subtasks — amenable to risk management and ignition.

Money's two features enabling ignition: (1) measures constraints and goals in the same units — partial wins reduce future constraints; winnings can be rebet, (2) captures value of any degree of success — not binary.

**True money** defined: single instrument functioning as medium of exchange + store of value + **numeraire** (standard of value). Economists emphasize the first two; Brown argues the numeraire function caused the explosion in value creation. Token and commodity money preceded true money by millennia. **Financial derivatives** are currently the most important form of money in advanced economies (few have noticed).

### Outside the VaR Boundary: Utility Theory and Its Problems

Early probability theorists: evaluate all gambles by **expected value** — anyone declining a positive EV bet is "irrational." Problem: a coin flip where heads triples your wealth and tails loses everything has positive EV = half your wealth, but most people rationally decline.

Fix: **utility theory** — assign utility to outcomes, evaluate on expected utility. Standard assumption: **declining marginal utility** — as you get richer, each extra dollar adds less utility → all risk is bad. Brown's critique: this was invented to salvage a mathematical assertion, never empirically verified, never theoretically justified — yet accepted as unquestioned truth.

Six extensions to rescue it: (1) allow current possessions to matter (endowment effect), (2) include utility from conforming to social norms, (3) make utility depend on past and anticipated future consumption (non-time-separable), (4) make utility depend on all possible outcomes not just expected utility, (5) don't collapse all consumption to a single numeraire, (6) recognize people have multiple natures with different utility functions. With extensions, utility theory becomes mathematically intractable — almost any behavior can be labeled rational utility maximization.

**Pascal's alternative — change of numeraire**: computed in "lives" rather than pleasure units. No God → one life; God exists → infinite potential lives. Pascal acknowledged you don't know now how you will feel about possible outcomes — utility theory requires knowing in advance how you rank all outcomes.

**The multiple-natures problem**: conventional analysis assumes a single utility maximizer — Pascal knew we have multiple natures. Parts of the brain seek short-term pleasure; others take longer views. If you tame your lusts, who exactly is happier? Your nature has changed. Organizational analog: evaluating proposals, we automatically adopt the company's viewpoint; decisions made for the organization, family, country — not just the individual self. Outside the VaR boundary, entirely unexpected choices force questions about who you really are and what you really want.

### Principle V: Evolution

Evolution is the premier example of good risk. Two caveats: mutation is almost always bad for the individual but optimal for the population; random variation is only half — the other half is natural selection.

A company surviving a series of crises has evolved into a better-constructed entity. The diverse, stress-tested company is preferable to the safe, untested one. Economists' counter-argument of directed diversity (breeding) only optimizes along foreseeable dimensions.

**Evolutionary view of stocks**: conventional wisdom says between two stocks with equal expected cash flows, prefer the less volatile one. Contrarian: stock in volatile company A (survived many crises) may be superior to safe, untested company B — A's stresses may have evolved characteristics that succeed in the future; B is narrowly positioned for past conditions. Need to identify the type of randomness: mismanagement vs. innovation-driven volatility.

Adding randomness to beliefs harnesses evolution. Religious scholar **Greg Barton**: the more you read, the less certainty you find; the most rigid views correlate with the least reading. Intense experiences (crises, foxholes) strip away weak, simplistic beliefs — natural selection applied to personal worldview.

### Principle VI: Superposition

Pre-1900s, no one could demonstrate true randomness existed. Quantum superposition: a particle acts as if under all cups at once until observed. Macroscopic analogy: a company with tight cost controls runs efficiently but only under prevailing price structure — small changes cause large dislocations. A looser company maintains diversity of approaches.

Same economy-wide: fully priced/marked-to-market economies are narrowly efficient but fragile; less integrated economies spawn innovation from overlooked assets.

### Principle VII: Game Theory

**Game theory**: mathematical study of uncertainty from rational actions of others. Key results: the most rational action is often to flip a coin; knowledge and freedom of action can be harmful — in a truel (three-person duel), the most skillful person often has the least chance of winning.

> Friedrich A. Von Hayek: "To be impartial means to have no answer to certain questions which, if we have to decide them, we decide by tossing a coin." — *The Road to Serfdom*, 1945

Game theory reverses causality: traditional economics says natural risk reduces expected utility and freedom of action increases it. Game theory: natural risk can be good; control can be bad. Real problems always have three dimensions: you, nature, and others — they cannot be separated.

A skilled poker player incorporates *your model of her* into her decision — even a perfect probability model loses money if you call on it, because she adjusts. The only counter: raise some of the time, injecting game-theory randomness — fundamentally different from roulette, where betting on odds wins in the long run.

**Black Wednesday (1992)**: British government tried to keep the pound above 2.778 DM. Spent £27B buying sterling, raised rates 500 bps during a recession. **George Soros** and **Stanley Druckenmiller** treated it as a heads-I-win-tails-I-break-even bet. Pound forced to 2.4 DM. Treasury lost £3.3B.

Contrast: **Paul Volcker** crushed inflation from 13.5% to 3.2% by raising rates to 20% and making markets believe he would go farther. Strong action is useless if everyone knows it represents the limit of your strength.

**Eisenhower** maintained more resources in reserve than committed to combat — game theory at theater scale. Germans committed everything to every battle, removing strategic ambiguity.

**Prisoner's Dilemma and focal points**: five blue buttons, one red — must press same button as partner to win $100. Game theory has no formal principle to distinguish the red button, yet everyone presses it — partnership reasoning. People are hardwired for cooperation and focal point reasoning, not rational self-interest.

## Chapter 4: The Secret History of Wall Street: 1654-1982

### The Rocket Scientists

The quants who came together on Wall Street in the 1980s eventually explained the modern concept of probability and reconstructed the global financial system. Motivation: make more money than any rational person could spend without wearing a tie or being polite to anyone they didn't like. Communicated via dial-up bulletin boards, sharing hand-typed data.

### Pascal and Fermat (1654)

The 1654 correspondence between **Blaise Pascal** and **Pierre Fermat** is the earliest tangible evidence of a fundamental shift — not a discovery but a change in social attitudes. Their problem: how to divide stakes in an interrupted game — a Bayesian degree-of-belief question, not frequentist.

Probability was created by confusing two concepts: (1) **frequency** (how often events recur in repeatable experiments) and (2) **degree of belief** (how strongly to hold a proposition). This distinction recognized repeatedly and ignored: **Frank Knight** (risk vs. uncertainty), **Donald Rumsfeld** (known unknowns vs. unknown unknowns). The frequentist/Bayesian split was never resolved theoretically until the early 1990s, accidentally, on Wall Street.

### Author's Intellectual Formation

Introduced to philosophy of probability by **W. V. O. Quine** at Harvard, 1974. **Fischer Black** gave references to **Ian Hacking**, **Steven Stigler**, **Lorenz Krüger** — historians rewriting the history of statistics. **John Tukey** and **Fred Mosteller**: exploratory data analysis — let data talk rather than test hypotheses. Author took courses from Mosteller. **Brad Efron**: strong influence (bootstrap methods). Fischer Black became advocate for *exploring* models rather than analyzing or believing them — a key practitioner stance.

### Poker and the Gambling Subculture

Author supported himself playing poker since age 15; in the 1970s this meant finding games, collecting winnings, and navigating cheating/robbery/arrest. Key gambling ethic: you are expected to bet a significant stake on any assertion you make — those who shoot their mouths off end up disgraced and broke; statements you do hear can be relied on ("you can bet on them"). Gambling culture enforces the fusion of frequency and degree of belief via social mores: if you assert a degree of belief, you bet on it; over many bets the frequency of wins should guarantee profit.

### Advantage Gamblers

Quants disaffected from academia found intellectual peers among **advantage gamblers** — specifically blackjack card counters. Advantage gamblers are **frequentists**: make money from superior prediction of repeatable-experiment frequencies; no social skills required, but required increasing deception to avoid casino countermeasures. References: **Ben Mezrich**, *Bringing Down the House* (2002).

Advantage gamblers' self-image: "undercover Robin Hoods" stealing back from casinos what casinos had stolen from everyone; viewed poker players like the author as closer to casino owners — "the sheriff of Nottingham than Robin Hood." In Reno 2009, news that a customer shot a pit boss in Atlantic City caused the advantage gambler table to erupt in raucous cheers loud enough to get them kicked out.

### Sports Betting

**Point spread** invented to equalize money bet on both sides, not to reflect true probability. Spreads set to balance action → easy for quants to find profitable opportunities.

**Pre-pre-opening line process (NFL)**: (1) insiders see line Sunday, fixed bets, line corrected; (2) Monday, quant bettors see it, smaller bets, adjusted; (3) Tuesday, opening line to public. Quants could progress from bettor → contract line-setter → bookmaking operations.

Sports-betting quants were **Bayesians** (degree-of-belief): a sporting event can't be repeated, so probability = subjective belief about outcome, shaped by how people bet.

Two strategies: (1) estimate true probability from first principles and beat the vigorish; (2) find which spreads are set too high/low relative to public demand without estimating true probability — same distinction as **value investing vs. momentum investing**.

Brown's personal specialty: **line changes** — distinguishing moves driven by new information vs. public sentiment swings. Example: Green Bay -3.5 over Chicago; Chicago QB confirmed to play → line moves to Green Bay +1.5. A bettor holding Green Bay can sell the bet (transfer for $1,000) rather than hedge at a locked-in $1,000 loss (and risk $22,000 double-loss on push). Brown profits if the move was sentiment-driven, not fundamental.

### Quants to Wall Street

~20 of ~100 rocket scientists arriving in the early 1980s came from gambling backgrounds. Common touchstone: **Edward Thorp's** *Beat the Dealer* (1962) and *Beat the Market* (1967). Thorp invented most standard hedge fund strategies in the mid-1960s. First to expand **John Kelly's** work.

### Four Parts of Finance

1. **Sales** — the dominant visible face; bond salesmen, stockbrokers, investment bankers, many "portfolio managers" (really asset gatherers). They write the official history. Job requires interpersonal skill, not intelligence; most have no idea of finance's economic function.
2. **Finance people** (information processing) — the vast majority: accountants, bank tellers, loan officers, insurance adjusters. Hundreds of billions of transactions/day. Each transaction is a fraud/error opportunity. Familiar to most people but rarely written about.
3. **Information hoarding** — from 1900-1970, banks monopolized financial information (creditworthiness, operating results, asset holdings) as a source of profit. Mortgage spread: 3% net revenue in 1960s → under 1% today due to **disintermediation**. Explosion in transaction volume more than offset narrowing margins.
4. **Real finance** (risk-taking) — the small group that actually makes bets; trading floors and hedge funds. Revolutionized after 1982 by rocket scientists. Basic structure now purely quantitative; 30 years ago purely qualitative.

### The Quant Revolution's Hidden Nature

Pre-1980: independent financial quant needed (a) data access and (b) trading accounts on fair terms — both controlled by information-hoarding banks. Had to work inside a bank to get either.

Critical mass of quants pooled information on dial-up bulletin boards — typed in price series, market conventions, trading rules; wrote handbooks on opening accounts and avoiding bank tricks. Result was superior to bank data: electronic, integrated, cleansed of errors. Most observers saw Wall Street changing and quants growing more important — but missed that quants were the driver, not the result. The change was to real finance (risk-taking), not to sales or information processing.

> "We still call it Wall Street but no one who was actually on Wall Street in 1980 would recognize any part of it — and none of it is located on physical Wall Street."

## Chapter 5: When Harry Met Kelly

### Kelly

Conventional wisdom: risk decisions driven by subjective preference. Kelly's 1956 result: there is a calculable optimal amount of risk that always does best in the long run. Beyond Kelly, more risk only increases probability of bad outcomes. Below Kelly, you're guaranteed to do worse.

**Illustration** (100 even-money bets, 60 wins guaranteed, adversary chooses order):
- Fixed bet of $24 → end with $1,480 (best fixed-bet outcome; anything ≥$25 and adversary front-loads losses, wiping you out)
- **Bet 20% of current bankroll each time**: order-independent — you never go broke, always end with the same amount. Each matched loss/win pair reduces bankroll by bet%² (20%² = 4%). 40 such pairs: $1,000 → $195; then 20 unmatched wins → **$7,490**
- Bet 10%: pairs cost 1% each; end at $4,501
- Bet 30%: pairs cost 9% each; end at $4,370
- **20% is the unique optimum** — provably no strategy does significantly better

When you only know the probability (not exact outcome count): if you face many bets over a lifetime, long-run results converge to expectation. Kelly betting is then optimal without knowing your utility function or future opportunities. Consistent risk-taking is as important as choosing the right level: most people take a few large concentrated risks (dominating life outcome) and avoid thousands of small positive-edge risks — then blame Black Swans for losses created by their own betting strategy.

### Harry

**Harry Markowitz**, University of Chicago graduate student, 1950s: asked "Why don't investors put all their money in the best stock?" Answer: "Too risky." Developed **Modern Portfolio Theory (MPT)**. Note: dissertation chairman **Milton Friedman** reportedly said it was "nice work, but not economics."

Harry's answer: diversify to lower risk, accepting lower expected return. Kelly's answer: putting 100% in one stock is **overbetting** — you lose access to all future positive-EV opportunities. Not about other simultaneous stocks; about future betting opportunities.

> "Because buying one stock virtually guarantees doing worse than buying a larger portfolio. It's not riskier; it's just worse."

Harry = diversification because of simultaneous opportunities. Kelly = bet sizing because of future opportunities. Both valid; mainstream finance developed Harry's ideas; quants developed Kelly's. MPT works for large liquid portfolios held 1-36 months; Kelly required for most hedge fund contexts.

Equal-weight portfolio of all 7 returned 34.4% — better than every individual commodity, even though portfolio avg return (1.8%) is lower than most individual averages.

### Markowitz + Kelly Combined

Markowitz tells you *which assets* to hold, not *how much leverage to take*. Same Sharpe ratio whether 10% in portfolio or 10:1 leveraged. But outcomes differ massively: 10% allocation → $1,132 end; 10:1 leverage → $2. Optimal Kelly leverage for this portfolio: **3.322 to 1**.

Progression: equal-weight $1,344 → Markowitz $2,924 → Kelly-optimal $8,145 (during period of generally declining real commodity prices). Harry and Kelly answer different questions; combined they're far more powerful.

> "The most important insight is that even with perfect foresight, risk still matters."

### Harry vs. Kelly: Core Distinction

**Markowitz**: risk is a cost; investors pay via lower expected return to reduce it. All hold the same portfolio (highest Sharpe ratio). **Kelly**: risk is not inherently bad; there is an optimal amount. Diversification is valuable not because it reduces risk but because it moves you toward optimal risk level.

For an active investor who can size accurately, the best portfolio is the one that can be sized most accurately — not necessarily the highest Sharpe or most diversified.

### Investment Growth Theory (IGT) vs. Modern Portfolio Theory (MPT)

| Dimension | MPT | IGT |
|---|---|---|
| Approach | Top-down | Bottom-up |
| Key variable | Correlation | Capital at risk, expected return, variance |
| Time | One-period | Dynamic, no horizon |
| Short positions/derivatives | Awkward | Natural |
| EMH parallel | Fair price | Capital allocated properly |
| Anticyclicality | Neutral to growth | Anticyclical |

MPT says correlation is paramount; investors barely thought about it before Markowitz. IGT's three key parameters match how investors actually think: capital at risk, expected return, variance.

IGT portfolio construction: analyze opportunities one at a time. Most attractive + lowest-risk → largest allocation. No gain from two stocks in the same industry. Far more valuable: different time scales, different kinds of risk. Typical IGT portfolio: few stocks + private equity + venture capital + real assets + HFT + relative value + global macro + high-grade bonds + distressed bonds. Result: **half-dozen to several dozen** assets (not hundreds). No two IGT investors hold the same portfolio — they seek bargains others haven't bought.

IGT explains the **endowment effect** (not irrational — you value owned assets differently because your portfolio allocation changes their risk profile), buy low/sell high preference, and the dividend puzzle. **IGT CAPM is anticyclical**: good news → current holders sell → company must pay out dividends or buybacks; growth requires recruiting new investors. MPT CAPM is growth-neutral: stock is equally good buy at $1, $10, or $1,000.

**Bubble/crash problem under MPT**: with index investors passive, valuations determined by battles between fundamental and momentum investors. Fundamental investors win → bounded upside. Momentum investors win → no natural limit. Even a few momentum victories can push market to economically unjustified bubble or crash.

### Diversification Math — The Concentrated Portfolio Defense

For N assets with same standard deviation and pairwise correlation rho: portfolio SD = sigma x sqrt(1/N + rho). As N approaches infinity, portfolio SD approaches 0 only if rho = 0.

For equities, typical rho ≈ **0.16** — can't get portfolio SD below ~40% of average stock SD no matter how many stocks you add. Picking 20 random stocks gets **90% of diversification benefit** of the full market. Picking stocks with low or negative correlations → full benefit with just **4-8 stocks**. Pre-MPT typical professional portfolio of 8-40 stocks was rational, not ignorant.

Author's critique: "a religious faith in diversification that mathematics could not compete with" — observed especially among actuaries.

### eRaider.com — The Concentrated Portfolio Experiment

Author's real-world attempt to exploit IGT principles (~1996-2000). 7-stock portfolio with low mutual correlations, lower SD than market. Key criteria: low institutional ownership, active/intelligent message boards, obvious shareholder value catalysts management could act on.

Co-conceived with **Martin Stoller** (Kellogg School of Business, Northwestern). Strategy: reach shareholders holding ≥20% of float via stock message boards. Team assembled: finance professors, accounting professors, SEC commissioner, industry experts, journalists. Accumulated 5% stakes before announcing (legal max before SEC filing).

Took **4 years** to get SEC approval — fund opened **March 10, 2000**. That day: NASDAQ hit all-time high of **5,048.62**. Over next 2.5 years NASDAQ fell to **1,114.11** and never broke 3,000 again. Fund converted to private hedge fund after two years. Martin Stoller was diagnosed with a brain tumor — he was the communications genius and public face.

Martin's father: **Phil Stoller**, famous securities swindler, subject of **Murray Teigh Bloom's** *Rogues to Riches: The Trouble with Wall Street* (1973).

Portfolio did outperform (lost less than market); achieved goals at 6 of 7 companies. Key lesson: **"No one wants to pay for activism"** (quote from Phil Goldstein) — shareholders encouraged, then sold on any price bounce activism produced. Internet investor base made it harder, not easier, to effect improvements.

### MPT Out in the World — Real-World Effects

**What MPT actually produced:**
- Pushed investors to highly diversified low-cost index funds
- Created consolidation of investment management (huge funds, huge companies)
- Made markets more cross-sectionally efficient (returns aligned with risk levels)
- But: index investors overweight overpriced assets and underweight underpriced ones by mathematical certainty — "the worst of both worlds" (noted by **Ken French** and **John Bogle** independently)
- Large diversified portfolios meant no investor oversight → fed bubbles/crashes
- MPT focus on monthly returns → underappreciation of long-term economics and short-term microstructure
- Suppressed hedge funds with theory that they "shouldn't be able to make money" → created massive opportunities as prices diverged from fundamental value

**What an IGT world would have produced instead:**
- Concentrated hedge fund strategies (only accessible to the rich)
- More small funds and companies; far less consolidation
- Markets more stable and closer to fundamental value in absolute terms
- Less efficiency in relative pricing across securities
- More scandals and blowups (more aggressive strategies)
- **Biggest problem**: lack of fairness — no low-cost average-return option for ordinary investors

> "Man does not live by MPT alone."

## Chapter 6: Exponentials, Vampires, Zombies, and Tulips

### Why Exponentials Are Central

Exponentials underlie most large positive and negative surprises. Things that don't change don't cause risk. Things that move at steady rates are manageable with deterministic rules. Exponentials can go from "too small to notice" to "too big to survive" before you can react — or from invisibly small to fulfilling your wildest dreams before competitors notice.

**Types of growth**:
- **Linear/arithmetic**: constant rate — distance doubles when time doubles
- **Polynomial**: rate increases with level — hurricane power proportional to wind speed cubed (doubling wind = 8x destruction)
- **Exponential**: rate proportional to *level* — the bigger it gets, the faster it grows; always outpaces polynomial eventually, but physical limits cap it. Common error: people use "exponential" to mean "very fast"

> "The trick is to find the exponential stuff when it's still slow. By the time it gets fast, it's too late."

Linear/polynomial growth gives warning — you can wait. Exponential does not. Risk management implication: linear risks can be monitored reactively; exponential risks must be identified before they accelerate. Real-world exponential: slow → suddenly accelerates → hits limit → appears as overnight success to observers.

### AIDS Case Study

HIV probably infected first human 1884-1924; first confirmed U.S. death 1968; not identified until 1981. 1981: 122 U.S. deaths; appeared to be quadrupling every year. With ~10-year latency, projections pointed to everyone dead by 2001. By the time noticed, growth rate was already slowing. First effective measures: late 1980s. Deaths peaked ~1995. Counterfactual: if action delayed until 1989, peak could have been >1 million/year instead of 50,000.

### The Negative Side of Exponentials

**Negative exponential**: rate of shrinking proportional to level — losses decelerate over time. **Moore's Law** (computing cost falls 50% every 18 months) is a negative exponential.

Betting *on* an exponential: if right, gains continuously accelerate; if wrong about direction (it's negative), losses slow down → **asymmetric payoff structure**. Betting *against* an exponential: if wrong, losses accelerate — very dangerous.

Internet in 1990: clearly exponential network effects, but unclear if it would hit limits before paying off. Regardless of probability assessment, the risk characteristics of being long were far better than being short. 100 bets on exponentials: can make money winning 1 and losing 99 if the one win is uncapped. 100 bets against exponentials: can lose money winning 99 and losing 1.

### Bubbles

**Bubble**: a self-fulfilling exponential — price rises → people buy → price rises more. Starts with true economic exponential → hits real limit → bubble investors keep pouring in. Many things are better after a bubble than before — "flip side of creative destruction." Net harm: loss aversion makes pain exceed pleasure; gains/losses distributed unfairly.

### Tulips — What Actually Happened

**Tulpenwoede** (tulipomania), Holland, early 1600s. Popularized by **Charles Mackay**, *Extraordinary Popular Delusions and the Madness of Crowds* (1841). People were NOT paying high prices for single flowers — they were paying for *rights to a new variety* (like movie distribution rights, not a ticket).

Tulip bulbs are exponential: each generates offspring at ~30%/year from healthy bulbs. Valuable coloration comes from **mosaic virus** infection — infected bulbs reproduce slowly (sickest = most valuable). Marketing mechanics: grower sells one bulb to celebrity at high price → celebrity displays publicly → artificial scarcity creates demand → price falls as supply grows over ~20 years.

Quantitative example: first flower worth $100, value declines 20%/year, supply triples first 6 years then grows 30%/year, 5% discount rate → initial variety worth **$200,000+**. After 2 years: 9 flowers worth $64 each; bulb worth $23,000. After 4 more years: 729 flowers worth $26 each; bulb worth $362.

Mackay confused two phenomena: (1) high prices for rare bulbs ca. 1610 and (2) the crash of February 1637. The famous horror stories were **17th-century advertising** — stories designed to dramatize a variety's value for illiterate audiences. 1610 high prices were rational: buyers made money because natural reproduction rate exceeded price decline.

> "There was no tulip bubble in 1610, just the normal operation of a business combining fashion and technology — high technology at the time."

Dutch flower business today is larger in real per-capita terms than in 1610; only consistently profitable agricultural business in Europe.

**February 3, 1637 crash**: market collapsed midwinter (all bulbs in frozen ground). Expensive bulbs fell 16%; cheap bulbs fell **95%**. Mackay's "widespread ruin" was myth — government propaganda pamphlets blaming speculators; no increase in bankruptcies; losses mainly among upper-middle-class merchants who had both bought and sold. **1635 Peace of Prague** gave Holland security and access to new European markets — legitimate economic reason for price increases.

**Quantitative sensitivity**: a 2% change in depreciation rate (20% → 18%) increases new-variety value 66% ($205K → $339K). For near-commodity bulbs already close to ordinary value, same change produces **568% price increase** ($1.15 → $7.70). Cheaper commodities have greater proportionate price sensitivity — explains why cheap bulbs crashed 95% while expensive ones fell only 16%.

### The Money Theory of Tulipomania

Core revisionist argument: tulip futures were a substitute currency in a capital-starved, inflation-ridden economy. Tulip bulbs: store of value, medium of exchange, portable. Tulip futures: unlimited supply creation, crude paper currency without a clearinghouse. Naturally expanding supply at ~30%/year — potentially matching economic growth without corrupt manipulation. Usury was technically illegal — rising tulip prices were a backdoor interest payment.

Why the crash: once supply growth exceeded economic growth, inflation resulted → bulbs lost monetary status. No central clearinghouse → cascade of unresolvable contracts.

Author sees money creation in all bubbles: Internet (stock option currency), housing 2007, 1850 North America. "If stagnation is the alternative to bubbles and crashes, I vote for volatility."

## Chapter 7: Money (Comic/Story)

Once money exists, behavior shifts from rational production to **arbitrage**: find what's cheap, convert to something expensive. Markets as emergent, self-organizing systems.

**Egyptian counterfactual**: if pharaohs had used competitive bidding instead of slave labor, innovative businesses would have sprung up and techniques spread globally. Instead, technology vanished and monuments decayed.

Governments were generally hostile to money until Romans discovered taxing and paying in money was more efficient. But Rome didn't understand money needs to complete a circuit. Paper money: wherever introduced, unprecedented steady rise in wages.

**Continental dollars (1775)**: Americans issued $250M, depreciating ~50%/year. Rapid depreciation stimulated an economy starved of money by British rulers. War without taxes caught governments' attention worldwide.

**Derivatives**: improved on paper money by giving price signals for all commodities at future times. Everywhere derivatives introduced, rate of wage increase exploded. Today: more transactions mediated by direct goods exchange and status rather than equal value.

## Chapter 8: The Story of Money: The Past

### Property, Exchange, and Money

#### The Endowment Effect / Loss Aversion

Classic experiment by **Daniel Kahneman, Jack Knetsch, and Richard Thaler**: coffee mugs with school logo ($6 at campus store). Buyers offered average **$2.87**; sellers demanded average **$7.12**. Same mug, radically different valuations based on ownership. Replicated across many settings, species (birds, insects). Explains **loss aversion**: losing something you have is more painful than gaining something equivalent is pleasurable.

#### Evolutionary Basis of Property

Loss aversion evolved to **reduce intraspecies fighting** and allow investment in territory/resources. Mechanism: incumbents value a resource more than intruders → intruders know fighting an incumbent is costly → fewer fights → more investment. **Free rider problem**: an individual who rationally balanced defense vs. retreat would benefit without paying the cost — but if too many free-ride, fighting increases.

Risk is deeply embedded in property: if outcomes were certain, the stronger party would just take; property rights require uncertainty. Evolution used asymmetric loss aversion to create the deterrent.

### Four Types of Human Exchange

|            | Reciprocal    | Nonreciprocal |
|------------|---------------|---------------|
| Symmetric  | Equal value   | Gambling      |
| Asymmetric | Gift          | Involuntary   |

Gift and gambling prefer luxury/symbolic goods. Equal value and involuntary exchange: goods are the entire point.

### Paleonomics

Upper Paleolithic (~50,000 years ago): emergence of long-distance exchange networks. Gambling and equal-value exchange allow individuals from different social bands to transact without complex rules.

**Token money → commodity money**: as exchanges become impersonal, token money emerges (seashells most common). Commodity money replaces tokens. Ideal: nonperishable, portable, divisible, desired universally → precious metals. Multiple forms: gold (international), silver (merchant), copper (retail). **Murray Gell-Mann anecdote** (1955): Archbishop of Canterbury's secretary insisted on guineas — 142 years after the last guinea was minted.

Two critical features of early money: (1) many different types for different exchange types; no government has successfully suppressed alternatives; (2) only a subset of goods can be exchanged for any given money type.

### What Money Does

Without good money, households must make almost everything themselves. More importantly: without good money there will be far less innovation — because risk cannot be managed. With money, constraints and goals both become money — enabling risk management and exponential growth.

**Potter's four-step growth model**: (1) arbitrage step: 50% margin → 50% growth per cycle; (2) bottleneck step: suppliers/customers saturate; (3) price signal step: signals radiate globally, calling in new suppliers and stimulating innovation; (4) scale/efficiency step: larger scale, labor specialization, knowledge spread.

### Money as Prerequisite for Kelly Betting

Without money, a business can't compute optimal scale or relate supply to demand. Egyptian pyramids: technically impressive but economically dead ends — no spin-off industries, no wages, no innovation feedback loop.

### Government and Paper

Romans: taxed and paid rather than ordering — worked while loot lasted. Fatal flaw: didn't understand money only works when it circulates. Response: debase currency + wage and price controls — mistakes every government has repeated.

After Rome's fall (476 AD), all gold/silver drained into Italy via Church and Venice. Paper money solved the circulation problem — not a promise for gold but a promise of future value. Result: steadily rising wages, unprecedented in history. Anyone can start a project; labor demand can outpace population growth.

### How a Bank Actually Works

A bank needs only the ability to make loans — no gold reserves, no equity capital, no marble columns. Lends paper; borrowers exchange paper for goods; sell products for paper; repay with interest. Most important credit webs are informal and invisible to economic statistics — power more innovation and lift more people to financial security than official bank loans, which mostly serve people who already have money.

> "Banks are institutions that lend money to people who don't need it."

Paper money's vulnerability: loss of confidence → accepted at discount (e.g., $105 paper buys what $100 silver does). Discount creates an effective interest rate on deposits. **Borrower floor**: discounted currency actually helps borrowers repay — if bank money is at 50% discount, a borrower who gets only $600 for spoiled flour can sell it for $1,200 in bank paper and repay a $1,000 loan with interest. Why accept dubious paper? Perishable goods sellers: paper beats nothing; diversified risk vs. trusting one buyer; "right-way risk" — bank failure correlates with bad markets, so goods have little value either way.

**Paper vs. metal — the biggest misunderstanding**: paper money is NOT a substitute for precious metal — it is a fundamentally different thing. Rules requiring paper to relate to gold/silver are designed to destroy true paper money and turn it into a token. Paper creates a richer, more nuanced price signal set: each bank's paper has its own interest rate, discount rate, and exchange rate vs. other paper monies — entirely new signals absent from metal.

A village economy has a **hard economy** (precious metal + solid credit, serving aristocracy) alongside a **soft economy** (paper + risky credit, funding new ideas). Soft economy can fund any persuasive person; poor people have more incentive to innovate than rich; most innovation comes from the soft economy. Successful innovation makes the paper good → virtuous cycle.

**Geographic spread signal**: when a local bank is in trouble, its notes spread outward (holders spend them elsewhere); neighboring regions discount the paper when they see net inflows → signal for arbitrageurs to buy cheap goods in the troubled area. **Macroeconomics fails here**: the virtue of paper money is how many types exist; treating it as a single money like silver coins misses everything.

### 1776: Continental Dollars

$250M issued, depreciated ~50%/year. NYC 8.875% sales tax at velocity of 20 erodes a dollar ~50% in 4 months — same rate. High depreciation doesn't stop monetary acceptance.

### Andrew Dexter

**Andrew Dexter**: America's first great overleveraged real estate developer. Built the **Boston Exchange Coffee House** ($500,000, 200-room hotel/exchange, among tallest buildings in the New World — burned down because no ladders reached the 7th floor). Also founded **Montgomery, Alabama**, reserving a plot for the state capitol (later built there) while Alabama wasn't yet a state.

His scheme: controlled two banks 600 miles apart — **Bank of Detroit** (frontier outpost, recently burned, no roads) and **Farmers Exchange Bank of Gloucester, Rhode Island**. Printed $100K from each; spent notes in between — notes would take years to return, most never would; effectively a zero-interest, no-collateral, no-doc loan. Worked because: money that traveled far enough lost its local suspicion — if Bank of Detroit notes made it to Boston, people assumed they must be good (social proof).

Failed when: construction overruns + disappointing revenue → bank couldn't redeem notes; no collateral → bankruptcy. Real problem: lack of transparency, not lack of capital — but the regulatory response restricted bank supply and kept money in the hands of the politically connected wealthy, not the innovative poor. Counterfeiters were folk heroes until the Civil War; bank robbers became folk heroes after.

### Government Capture of Paper Money

Governments gradually took over money creation, treated paper as "cheap precious metal everyone should pretend is real" — same mistake as debasement. Result: token money. Gained new debasement tools: interest rates and exchange rates. Destroyed the subtle local price signals that were the whole point.

## Chapter 9: The Secret History of Wall Street: 1983-1987

### Efficient Markets — The Real History

Common misunderstanding: efficient markets was NOT ivory-tower orthodoxy imposed on practitioners. Until the 1950s, almost no one believed markets were efficient — it was taken as obvious that professionals knew more than amateurs. What changed: academics started checking. Empirical evidence flooded in — professional investors did no better than random selection after fees. They tended to buy the same things (pushing prices up) and sell simultaneously (pushing them down). Annual market-beaters showed no persistence — being right one year predicted nothing for the next.

Key logical point: sum of all investors' returns before fees = the market return. If someone makes more, someone must make less. Fees charged by active management far exceed the maximum possible aggregate excess profit from beating the market.

**Earth analogy**: markets are efficient at large scales (like Earth being smoother than a billiard ball at cosmic scale), but exploitable inefficiencies exist at human scale — like mountains and oceans.

Social value of finance: the "making markets more efficient" justification is like rolling rocks downhill — it smooths the Earth but produces no useful work. Real finance is more like a **hydroelectric dam**: holds water back, moves away from equilibrium to generate value. "Finance is just another business, to be evaluated by how much it improves things for customers."

**Efficient markets does NOT mean rational investors** — the theory studies price movements, not investor psychology. Prices can be efficient even if all investors are ignorant and irrational. **Efficient markets does NOT mean prices are always right** — market price is right *on average*, which is much weaker but still a strong claim. Practical implication: if oil is $100/barrel, that's not necessarily right, but it's very hard to make money consistently buying or selling at $100.

Dangerous error: treating the average price as exactly right and building further computations on it. **Coin example**: two unbalanced coins (10% and 90% heads). Market prices security at $50. But a compound bet (two heads in a row) priced at $25 (= 50% x 50%) is wrong — actual probability is 41%. The market was right on average for a single flip, but "assuming it's exactly right" breaks down for derived calculations.

**Corporate contract example**: company with 100M shares, price rises $0.25 on major contract announcement → market says contract worth $25M. Counterparty offers $30M breakup fee. If market is exactly right: $25M < $30M → take the fee. But market is only right on average: contract could be worth $100M or -$100M. The fact the other party offers $30M suggests it's worth more.

Systematic anomalies: small-cap outperformance, price momentum, value effect (low P/E, low P/B outperformed), low-volatility advantage. None suppressed; collectively a tiny fraction of total returns. By 1980, ~25% of finance professors accepted anomalies as refuting efficient markets; by 1995, ~95%.

### Efficiency vs. Equilibrium

**Eugene Fama**: every test of efficiency is a joint test of efficiency and equilibrium. Two exploitation strategies:
1. **Markets inefficient but in equilibrium** (card counter): prices wrong but you can transact freely. Compute correct price, buy cheap, short expensive.
2. **Markets efficient but out of equilibrium** (sports bettor): prices right given structure, but transactions aren't happening at the price. Accommodate others' needs.

### Dual-Class Share Arbitrage

Setup: Company has Class A (public, 1 vote) and Class B (insider, 10 votes). Equal financial claims. Class B trades at discount to Class A despite superior voting rights (due to illiquidity). Trade: Buy 100K Class B at $49 ($4.9M), short 100K Class A at $51 ($5.1M) → $200K instant profit, near-zero net position. At 10% margin: $1M deposited, write check for $800K profit.

Risk: spread widens (Class A to $53, Class B stays $49). Mark-to-market loss of $200K. Broker raises margin from 10% to 20%. Must post more cash. True believers double their bet; weak hands close. Disaster scenario: other frequentists exit simultaneously, widening spread further, triggering more margin calls, forced liquidation at maximum loss. "Strongest weak hands" — the investor who endures the most pain without being able to hold on for the turnaround.

**Frequentists (card counters)**: dual-class is a pure arbitrage to hold forever; they bet against people (other investors cause the only real losses). Secretive, antisocial, long-only own capital. **Sports bettors**: call it a convergence trade; study spread history and bet on when it narrows. They care who is on the other side — understanding those people IS the strategy. More willing to reverse if they expect spread to widen.

Card counters: faith in the market, fear of people. Sports bettors: rely on predictable human behavior, fear market forces.

### Momentum / Managed Futures

**Managed futures** = best-known momentum strategy. Originated in commodity futures, now applied across all liquid asset classes. Momentum easiest to explain as **disequilibrium**: slow information flow into prices rather than market inefficiency.

**Gasoline crack spread example**: cold snap in February → refineries shift to heating oil → spring gasoline stocks fall → gasoline prices rise until Memorial Day. Crude oil barely affected (world supply is vast; refinery capacity is the bottleneck). Without financial markets: crack spread rises steadily Feb→June. With perfect markets: all futures immediately reprice in February. In reality: a few specialist fundamental investors notice first, rush to buy gasoline futures / short crude futures — before the cold snap actually occurs. Once most of the move has happened, remaining edge is too small vs. risk → specialists exit.

**Implication**: if net direction of crack spread over the last month has been up → more good news than bad → net positive info not yet fully incorporated → momentum signal is valid. Momentum requires careful calibration per market: what lookback period, what other signals help? Brown's sports-betting analogy: early-week line changes = news (bet with them); late-week/Friday changes = uninformed bettor flow (bet against them).

### GNMA and CMOs

Biggest sports-bettor opportunity in the 1980s: mortgage securities. **GNMA** (Government National Mortgage Association) issued securities backed by 30-year fixed-rate mortgages, backed by full faith and credit of U.S. government. Paid significantly higher yields than Treasuries due to **prepayment risk** and **adverse selection** — rates down → homeowners refinance → holders get cash back at worst time; rates up → fewer refinancings → holders get less cash than expected. Prepayment risk was smaller than yield difference implied → statistical arbitrage opportunity.

Brown's insight: divide GNMA cash flows into **tranches** with different risk/maturity profiles. Some investors want certainty (few bps over Treasuries), some want short duration, others want good returns. Result: keep 80% of spread vs. 20% as money manager; sell $1B in securities vs. struggling to raise $10M. Same logic as ETFs vs. open-end mutual funds — issuance beats management as a business.

**Salomon Brothers** issued the first **collateralized mortgage obligation (CMO)**; became dominant issuer.

### Pairs Trading

Find two similar stocks trading in stable ratio (e.g., **Pfizer** and **Merck**). July 1983-end 1985: Merck traded ~2.5x Pfizer price (range: 2.10-2.92). Buy cheap leg / short expensive leg when ratio diverges; reverse when it normalizes → consistent profits. Dual explanation: market inefficiency OR market equilibrium with slow price correction.

### Two Generations of Quants

**First generation** ("rocket scientists," ~100 people by mid-1980s): loose network, Kelly-based, getting rich exponentially slowly. Came to Wall Street to beat the Street. Gambling/trading background → risk takers by nature. Thought probabilistically about markets.

**Second generation** (post-mid-1980s, thousands globally): drawn by skyrocketing financial pay + end of Cold War military funding (freed Russian, Eastern European, later Chinese quants). NOT risk takers by nature; most had no interest in finance pre-Wall Street. **Financial engineers**, not rocket scientists. Came to praise the Street, not beat it — expected to be trained, then make incremental improvements for salary.

### Sharpe Ratios and Wealth

Sharpe ratio = (return - risk-free rate) / standard deviation. For Kelly investor: long-term growth ≈ Sharpe². Sharpe 1 → ~100% annual growth. Sharpe >1 strategies are self-limiting.

| Sharpe | Best suited for |
|--------|-----------------|
| 0.1 | Large institutions with cheap capital |
| 0.2 | Large institutions |
| 0.5 | Hedge fund strategies |
| >1 | Personal capital, event-driven |

Full Kelly betting is highly sensitive to fat-tail losses: add ONE loss that's 5x the bet → optimal bet drops from 20% to 11.6%, final wealth drops more than half. "Computing the correct bet if your model is perfect and then cutting it in half is not adequate risk management."

Low-Sharpe danger: combining many uncorrelated strategies creates high-Sharpe composite, BUT correlation spikes in crises. Depends on liquidity and good data — if these disappear, strategy breaks when needed most. Brown chose Sharpe ~0.5, moderate capacity, semiliquid securities.

### 1987

**October 19, 1987**: market fell ~25% in one day — all quants wiped out. The crash itself wasn't the surprise: first-gen quants were prepared for large stock market declines, knowing markets can move 2-3x any historical maximum. What got them: simultaneous host of other changes, not the market move itself.

By October 1987, quants had been on Wall Street 6-7 years; went from unknown outsiders to recognized (even "cool") players. Had attained high rank in institutions or were close to investing only their own money.

> "We were neither the first nor the last to confuse brains and a bull market."

Post-crash: witch hunt to blame quants — mostly landed on **portfolio insurance** quants from the sports-bettor camp. Brown's reaction: excited — "This was the game we had come to play." Immediately began revising models; a Yale presentation one month after the crash already contained half the ideas developed over the next five years. Burning question: how to run quant strategies without blowing up when the market does → answer came via series of accidents, including resolution of the 333-year-old frequentist/Bayesian confusion.

## Chapter 10: The Story of Money: The Future

### Futures Markets — Origins (~1850)

**Futures contract**: mutual promise to buy/sell a standardized amount and quality of a commodity at a specified future time, place, and price. Born in the northern Mississippi drainage basin ~1850; boards of trade appeared in major cities.

Three defining features:
1. Standardized amount, quality, time, and place
2. **Two contracts via a clearinghouse**: each party contracts with the clearinghouse, not each other — enables anonymous trading and easy exit (offset contracts cancel)
3. **Marked to market daily**: price changes settled in cash daily; by delivery date, contract written at current price, so default risk limited to one day's price move

Margin posted (~$500 on a $5,000 contract) protects clearinghouse against one day's default; as long as wheat doesn't move >$0.10/bushel in a day, the margin covers any loss.

### Farmers and Millers — Debunking the Myth

Standard textbook story (farmers hedge by selling futures, millers buy to lock in supply) is **completely false**. Farmers rarely participated; when they did, they were more likely to *buy* crops than sell. Millers use futures but generally sell crops, not buy.

The miller's real motivation is **financing, not hedging**. Futures contract = a borrowing mechanism — miller gets wheat now and promises to deliver wheat later. Eliminates money from the transaction → no inflation/deflation risk on the loan. Miller has no intention of delivering wheat to clearinghouse — he rolls the position, exactly like a bond borrower who refinances rather than repaying principal.

> "The futures contract is essentially a financing arrangement to the miller, not a risk reduction tool."

The point of a futures contract is the mark-to-market payments, NOT delivery — same as a bond's point being interest payments, not principal repayment. Miller's real concern: keeping machinery running with a secure wheat supply; price risk is secondary (competitors face the same price changes anyway).

### Money, New and Improved

**Derivatives improve on paper money**: instead of one interest rate and exchange rate per bank, there's a rate for every commodity, every economic interest, and every future delivery time period (Jan→Mar rate, Mar→Jun rate, etc.).

**Spread trades = derivative money in action**:
- Buy March wheat Chicago / sell March wheat Kansas City → you've lent transportation services
- Buy #1 wheat / sell #2 wheat → you've borrowed cleaning services
- You borrow/repay in precise future goods you need/can provide, not in money
- Chicago Board of Trade has quoted "impossible" spreads (flour→wheat, time-travel of crops) for 160 years

**Starting a business under three money regimes:**
- **Precious metal**: must have metal or find investor with metal
- **Paper money**: find a bank; less capital needed but still needs lending standards and net worth
- **Derivative money**: identify inputs you need and outputs you'll deliver; enter derivative contracts for both; receive upfront cash if future product value > input cost; no bank needed

**Energy sector example** (the first pure derivative businesses): entrepreneur wants a power plant; goes to derivatives dealer (not bank). Enters contract to receive 10 years of oil supplies + contract to sell 10 years of electricity = **"tolling" swap**. Receives cash upfront because electricity value > oil value. Uses cash to build plant and pay operating expenses. After 10 years, owns plant free and clear. If he fails to deliver electricity → plant seized by counterparty. Cash in the deal is essentially an accounting entry.

### A General Theory of Money

Three functions compared across precious metal, paper, and derivatives:

1. **Medium of exchange (clearing mechanism)**: derivatives' clearinghouse vastly superior — all circulation stays on the books, transactions always balance by rule, fraud requires corrupting the clearinghouse (easier to police than cash flows), losses tracked daily and limited to one day's move. Derivatives won't replace paper for small transactions (won't buy coffee with a futures contract), just as paper never replaced coins for tiny purchases — but derivatives are already the medium of exchange driving the economy.

2. **Store of value**: precious metal is very certain but expensive (must mine and lock up real resources). Paper money separates hedgers (hold cash, earn no interest, want liquidity) from speculators (deposit in bank, earn interest, bet on money's value). Derivatives: better because each user specifies exactly which future goods/services to hedge or bet on; daily mark-to-market payments protect value.

3. **Numeraire**: precious metal fails in uncertain world — no single unit prices items from different economic groups. Paper money improves if there's a diversity of banks. **Soviet Union example**: businesses reported profits in rubles but rubles were opinions, not actionable cash. Derivatives go one better — every transaction is actionable; each participant constructs a numeraire matching their specific real economic interests. **Numeraire for a derivatives dealer** = shares in the clearinghouse. For end users, numeraire = net product of economic activity (oil refiner: refined products minus crude). When your numeraire is your net economic product, you have no risk.

### Value and Money

Paper money's value = ability to pay taxes (like revenue-anticipation bonds). Derivative value = mark-to-market payments, backed by future goods/services in the contract itself.

**Anne and Anaïs currency bet**: USD/EUR 1-year forward $1.40/€1.00. Anne (USD-based) bets with Anaïs (EUR-based): if EUR > $1.40 in one year, Anaïs pays Anne €1.00; otherwise Anne pays $1.40. Both have positive expected value — because they use different numeraires, they value the same outcome differently and can arbitrage the valuation difference. Source of extra value: no goods created — Anne and Anaïs shift consumption across states of the world using different numeraires. Profit limit: ~5% of risk risked in one bet, up to ~10% with currency diversity. Derivatives more than double the value of underlying assets — more economic value added by futures markets than by all work making/processing the underlying commodities.

### Two Types of Speculators

**Value investors**: buy cheap relative to fundamental value, sell expensive; keep prices near fundamental value; provide liquidity (buy when others sell). **Momentum investors**: see where market is going, push it there faster (sometimes overshooting); behind bubbles and crashes; suck liquidity out of market; but without them no market — they provide the volatility that creates economic value for end users and break through entrenched myths to foster innovation. Economic value added by futures markets is proportional to price volatility.

### Debunking "Zero-Sum" and "Casino" Charges

Futures markets are zero-sum in accounting terms only — just like banks (every dollar in interest paid by a borrower = dollar received by depositor), yet banks add enormous value. Money itself is zero-sum: asset to owner, equal liability to everyone else. Since each user has a different numeraire, each can count a net profit in economic value — insisting futures markets are zero-sum misunderstands numeraires.

> "Of course they are casinos where speculators create risk. That's their function. If speculators went away or stopped creating risk, the markets would collapse."

### Clearinghouses and Their Failures

Structure: clearinghouse → clearing firms → FCMs → end users. Claim "no clearinghouse has ever gone broke" is misleading — they survive by bankrupting their customers.

**Paris Sugar Bourse (1905)**: first major example — clearing members on wrong side, set off-market settlement prices in their own favor. **Tino De Angelis (1962)**: floated soybean oil on seawater in tanks, used as collateral for gigantic futures positions; discovered, positions liquidated, prices crashed. **New York Produce Exchange** members were long and facing losses → declared contracts would settle at old high price, converting exchange losses to customer losses. Exchange had the "decency to go out of business." **London Metals Exchange**: did same thing twice in tin contracts — still operates. **NYMEX (1976)**: Maine potato contracts — still boasts of being "world's largest physical commodity futures exchange."

FCM risk: if your FCM or another FCM customer gets into trouble, you can be on the hook — like uninsured private banknotes. Solution needed: bankruptcy-like process with independent court-appointed administrator determining settlement prices — not dealer vote.

### Lehman Brothers and Cash

**Lehman Brothers** last filing before failure: $20B cash and equivalents vs. $263B short-term liabilities — cash irrelevant. $3B was short-term notes issued by a Lehman-related entity, guaranteed by Lehman, pledged to JPMorgan — Lehman effectively printed its own money (legal and common practice). Also had $336B noncash short-term assets — fate determined by quality and liquidity of these, not cash.

**How Lehman's Treasury desk actually worked**: quoted prices on all Treasury securities whether it owned them or not. Customers called to buy → Lehman sold; customers called to sell → Lehman bought. No cash or bonds changed hands at trade time. End of day: totaled bonds owned/owed, cash owned/owed, posted to customer accounts as virtual transactions. If Lehman held real bonds, it immediately lent them via repo. Any unwanted net exposure offset with a financial derivative.

Entire dealer system runs on **short-term repurchase agreement (repo)** financing: buy asset → immediately repo it to borrow purchase price. If Lehman could have netted: $336B - $263B = $73B, no crisis — but netting was incomplete. What mattered was ability to make mark-to-market payments and deliver repo assets.

> "Cash, in the sense of paper money issued by the government, is meaningless. The cash Lehman held belonged to others and could not have paid its liabilities anyway."

**Features of derivative money**: (1) virtual settlement — most transactions never involve physical cash; (2) minimal capital — only need enough to cover daily price changes on net positions; (3) risk-based capital rules — all firms that failed in 2008 were "well-capitalized" by government standards; (4) offsetting/netting — 2008 had significant but insufficient netting, which amplified Lehman contagion.

### Historical Arc: Money Phases

Barter → precious metal (freed world from civilization rise-fall cycles) → paper money (steadily rising wages) → derivative money (golden age of innovation).

19 of the 100 wealthiest Americans of all time built fortunes in the northern Mississippi drainage basin, 1850-1880: Rockefeller, Mellon, Carnegie, McCormick, Westinghouse, Pullman, Swift, Pulitzer, Hearst, Armour, Marshall Field. Region was sparsely populated in 1840. Internet billionaires built companies without profits — an opening battle for derivatives replacing paper money.

### The End of Paper

Paper money won't "lose its value" — just as gold didn't vanish when paper arrived — but its economic importance will shrink. Transitions underway: Internet bidding and future promises (airline seats, hotel rooms) → full derivative trading of future services. **BetFair** demonstrates large supply of sophisticated speculators willing to provide liquidity.

**Airline seat example**: seats have unpredictable value (empty = marginal cost only; full demand = 2-3x price); travelers have complex contingent plans; paper money pricing cannot allocate efficiently — a natural early transition to derivative trading. Internet economy: news, research (Wikipedia), entertainment increasingly free; open-source often better than commercial. Status goods (airline club memberships, hotel status): status becomes a kind of money.

### Robert Shiller's Vision: Human Capital Derivatives

**Robert Shiller** envisions derivatives for all major life decisions. Example: going to medical school → sell half the *cohort average* income of similar med school entrants (not personal income, which creates moral hazard). Buyer protected from individual motivation; seller protected from systemic income changes; dropout buys back the contract at gain or loss. Prices would be powerful career-choice signals; allows financing all kinds of ideas using future human capital.

Human capital exceeds all other assets in total value — without derivatives it remains sterile capital. End state: derivative purchase/sale may take money out of the equation altogether except for small incidental purchases; inflation becomes irrelevant to those fully hedged in real terms.

## Chapter 12: What Does a Risk Manager Do? — Inside VaR

### Professional Standards
- Financial risk management is quantitative with specific techniques — not vague worry
- Almost all financial risk managers use **VaR** as primary component; a vocal minority objects but still acknowledges importance of daily predictions verified against outcomes
- Author: strong VaR proponent — anyone who doesn't understand the orthodoxy is not qualified
- **CRO** of large institution is usually a bank executive, not a technical expert
- Best practice: separate risk management from trading; independent risk manager increasingly demanded by regulators

### Front Office
- **Front-office risk manager** employed inside each revenue-producing unit, reports through CRO (not business head) — independence emerged over past 15 years
- Recruited from the business, like coaches from former players
- In the 1980s, quants often chosen for this role; author ran a mortgage securities department and acted as his own risk manager

### Trading Risk
- Traditional job: watch traders for signs of too much or too little risk; set tiered limits
- Traders consistently under limits are investigated too — **seats are valuable; traders must take risk**
- "Putting trades in a drawer" (unreported trades) = grounds for immediate dismissal
- Risk manager listens to traders explain trades to judge *behavior*: "talking their book" (marshaling every argument) = sign of uncertainty → veto. Oddly weak case = may unconsciously want veto → also veto. Want: firm confidence plus realistic awareness of risks
- Good traders never tell you real reasons — they don't know them consciously — but good instincts can be trusted
- Value of experienced risk managers: longer market memory, can distinguish a bad week from worst event in history

### Quants on the Job
- Quants introduced systematic record-keeping — discovered even the best traders **bet more when wrong than when right**
- Traders almost always underbet vs. the **Kelly amount** yet occasionally made unjustifiable large bets
- **Jacob Brown's study** (in **Dylan Evans**'s *Risk Intelligence*): at world's largest online financial betting site, 90% bet randomly and lost; 10% had predictive edge — but virtually all would have done better betting constant size. When they bet more than average: lost more often. Some turned winning prediction percentages into net financial losses
- Three quant camps on desks in late 1980s:
  - **Frequentists** (card counters): overmanaged, treated traders like algorithms
  - **Bayesians** (sports bettors): best at analysis but lousy at communicating to traders
  - **Poker players** (author's type): best at getting traders to make right bets through influence, conversation, psychological framing

### Accuracy and Performance Ratios
- **Accuracy ratio**: fraction of winning trades. **Performance ratio**: avg gain ÷ avg loss
- Key insight: **the trader picks the performance ratio; the market gives the accuracy ratio**
- Three things a front-office risk manager does: (1) understand business, coach risk attitude, (2) keep exhaustive records and translate findings, (3) communicate risks to organization
- Three things NOT done: sniff out obscure risks, police traders, fight to keep risk down

### Middle Office
- Author invented the term **middle office** in early 1990s at **JPMorgan**
- Core problem: information flowed only one way (front to back office); back office absorbed all errors, learned nothing
- Investment bank internal numbers were "a joke"; commercial bank numbers were "merely bad"
- **JPMorgan** was the exception: commercial bank aggressive in trading; split off **Morgan Stanley** after Glass-Steagall (1932)
- Author spent early 1990s teaching risk management at JPMorgan — considers it his biggest contribution

### Back Office
- Back-office risk management born at JPMorgan, **Bankers Trust**, and **Citibank** — all commercial banks
- Most risk management jobs today are back-office
- **Operational risk** = everything other than market and credit risk; mostly concerned with dangers, not risks (Black Swan territory)

### Middle Office Function
- Two information loops: front → middle → back office, and back → middle → front
- Middle office concerned only with information important for **firmwide risk**
- Built from bottom-up (risk people) and top-down (CEOs wanting aggregated daily data)
- Regulators adopted it — Basel II/III based on middle-office concepts
- **SEC in 1998** ordered firms to include risk information in reports — but author has never met anyone who actually uses the VaR data in financial statements
- Daily process: issue VaRs for every unit, test predictions against actual P&L (2-3 day lag), tweak algorithms

### Looking Backward
- Common charge: risk managers are backward-looking — low-volatility periods produce low risk estimates, so positions are oversized when volatility spikes
- Author's counter: in 2006 *all* measures of risk were low, including forward-looking ones (**VIX**, correlations)
- Key insight: **when a crisis starts, the numbers will have already changed** — a solid balance sheet today tells little about recovery value in default

### Prediction: Two Approaches
- **Aggressive/precise**: short-term data, clear signals, wrong often
- **Cautious/robust**: long-term history, broad predictions, right often but less actionable
- Wall Street quant insight (late 1980s): use both together — precise guides action, robust is Plan B

> "The risk management rule is: if it's likely, exploit it; if it's plausible, hedge it; if it's possible, prepare for it; if it's impossible, you just bet everything you have or ever will have on that assessment."

### Risk Control — Three Tests
1. Can the business accurately estimate daily P&L volatility?
2. Can it tell you P&L conditional on market prices during the day?
3. Are estimated numbers consistent with actual cash flows from arm's-length transactions?

- Either bring businesses under risk control or shut them down — no middle ground
- **Residual risk** must be statistically independent of market factors and other businesses

### Beyond P&L — VaR Generalizes
- VaR applies to any objective, frequently-measurable variable — not just financial P&L
- Hospital example: track nightly patient deaths. Process of back-testing VaR improves data systems themselves
- Useful technique: let employees **bet on VaR breaks** ($1 to win $19) — reveals information unavailable through formal reporting

### Numbers — Pervasive Data Errors
- Author always checks numbers; finds they're frequently wrong — most people don't check
- Error rates by task type:
  - Manual number entry (waitress, retail): **1-5% error rate**
  - Simple calculations (adding a check): **5-20% error rate**
  - Pre-scanner supermarket checkout with ~50 items: about one error per transaction
- Misconception: "official" computer-printed institutional numbers are more likely correct — false
- **Adjustable-rate mortgage** interest computation error rate in early days: **>50%** (not zero today)
- Early payoff principal/interest calculation: more often wrong than right
- **Monroe Bond Calculator** (1980s): errors on the order of hundreds of dollars per $10M trade — back-office and salespeople didn't care; it wasn't their money
- **2000 U.S. Presidential election**: publicized vote-counting error rates — "no surprise to risk managers"
- How numbers survive being wrong: errors compound in large systems; only reality checking (reconciliation against independent systems) keeps them within tolerable range
- Firm's internal cash accounting vs. actual bank account: typically millions of dollars apart in large firms
- Reconciliation fixes discrepancies selectively (biased toward making firm look right) — does not improve accuracy, only shrinks discrepancy

> "To know whether you should rely on a number from a complex system, don't ask about the inputs or the processes; ask what it is checked against."

- If checked against nothing, or only against other outputs of same system: **worthless**
- If checked rigorously against objective reality: trustworthy

### Personal Origin Stories
- First job (1974): tennis player rankings — illegible score sheets, ambiguous names, impossible results. CEO sent first page without caveats. Lesson: sloppy numbers get used as if authoritative
- Election ballot counting (1974): company had only counted 200 nonrandomly sampled ballots (not originals), still managed to count them wrong. Flawed count allowed to stand
- Washington gas-rationing plan: **Ford** said 75% of tractors ran on diesel; **Dept. of Agriculture** said 25% — different definitions. The more he researched, the more he realized no rationing plan could work

### Waste
- Discussed with **Fischer Black**: informal estimate that **~95% of all work is wasted**
- People miss this because they evaluate only their own level: a truck driver paid $300 for a delivery considers work done. But some goods are defective, some won't sell, some will be moved back — 5% of goods might represent 100% of actual value created
- Accounting rules treat every expenditure as value creation — fiction
- Only reason useful fraction is as high as 5%: finance, social infrastructure, and technology
- Socialism's spurious appeal: noticing waste, thinking top-down decree can eliminate it — but refusing waste in one area generates much greater waste elsewhere
- **Martin Stoller** (eRaider.com partner): "the myth of efficient government" — not that government wastes money, but that people imagine large institutions as rational actors processing reliable inputs
- Local property tax data (2,000 residences, $200M assessed value): off by some percentage but bears relation to reality. Federal housing data (100M residences, $20T assessed value): completely meaningless

> "It is a myth to think of any large institution as a rational actor processing reliable inputs in a predictable way to decide on reasonable actions with predictable effects."

### Dietrich Dorner's Systems Experiments
- **Dietrich Dorner** (German psychology professor): experiments on how people manage systems
- **Meat locker experiment**: subject controls refrigeration dial to keep temperature in safe band — almost everyone fails
  - Mistake #1: keep turning dial up because temperature doesn't drop immediately — can't wait 5 minutes for feedback
  - Mistake #2: wait until temperature is right before turning down — then wildly overshoot cold, overcorrect warm, oscillate
  - Only people who succeed: those who apply explicit quantitative reasoning and have faith to stick with it
- **Virtual village experiment**: manage health care, irrigation, housing — almost everyone creates environmental disaster in a few virtual years; short-term corrections without considering long-term consequences — "top-down thinking leads to bottom-up failure"
- Author's freshman-year political simulation: professors and grad students with complete data and full knowledge of equations quickly generated global thermonuclear war — then claimed simulation was rigged rather than examining how rational decisions led to disaster

> "Given that humans demonstrably cannot manage simple virtual systems with complete information and power... you would have to be a complete idiot to expect them to manage complex real systems with noisy and out-of-date information and strictly limited power."

### Academic Data Failures
- Sticky-pricing research: more firms in an industry correlated with more frequent price changes — alleged evidence of price-fixing
- Later discovered: data artifact — more firms meant more sampled, more samples meant more detected changes; controlling for this, entire effect disappeared
- Publications not rescinded; PhDs not revoked; everyone moved on
- Conclusion: researchers never really believed their results — "their expertise was a fortress to be defended, not a tool to explore reality"

### Validation
- VaR's primary contribution is subjecting risk information to **objective validation**
- Some middle-office managers invented **KRIs** (key risk indicators) and **KPIs** (key performance indicators) — whatever you measure, people distort it
- Author's test for job applicants: name any computation you've done professionally for which you have objective evidence the result was accurate — most quantitative professionals cannot answer satisfactorily

### Book Value Breakdown
- Pre-1970: **book value** closely tracked stock price; price-to-book ratio near 1; bankruptcy recoveries near book value minus ~20% legal fees
- Post-1970s: relationship broke completely — today basically unrelated; half of bankruptcies produce near-zero creditor recovery
- Not only does net value vanish — assets turn out to be worth close to zero
- Accounting numbers still useful as indicators but have "long since lost any connection to tangible economic reality"

### Washington Poker as Reality Check
- Real reason for Washington trip: most cosmopolitan poker city in 1970s — players from all over the world
- Played with members of Congress, cabinet members, generals, senior diplomats — "not impressed"
- Smart, honest, hard-working — but none had data to know if what they were doing worked; none interested in falsification

> "I think anything bigger than a small town can be run efficiently only using quantitative methods, and those methods require constant, rigorous validation and openness to all challenge."

- **Diogenes** parallel (2,400 years ago): invented cynicism; searched for honest man; was a banker kicked out of Sinope for defacing coins. **Plato** defined man as "featherless biped" — Diogenes plucked a chicken and brought it to him
- Author felt kinship: went on search for honest quants willing to bet on their conclusions, like a sports betting line-setter

## Chapter 14: The Secret History of Wall Street: 1988-1992

### The 1987 Crash as Inflection Point
- **October 19, 1987**: stocks fell ~25%; Tuesday markets nearly didn't open; financial system survived through luck and "unsung minor heroism"
- By Thursday the financial landscape had changed dramatically — but only quants noticed
- **Fischer Black**: "Markets look a lot less efficient from the banks of the Hudson than from the banks of the Charles"

### Option Smile (Post-Crash)
- **Black-Scholes-Merton** (1973): dominant option pricing model; **Ed Thorp** had developed equivalent model six years earlier but used it privately to make money rather than publish
- Pre-crash: practitioners knew tail options were overpriced vs. model; attributed to unsophisticated investors overpaying for crash protection; pros sold protection and hedged profitably
- **By October 22, 1987**: flipped overnight — accepted that tail options *should* sell for more (they reflect real risk, not irrationality)
- **Option smile**: plotting option price / model price vs. exercise price produces a smile — OTM options on both ends expensive, ATM cheap
- **Skew**: smile is asymmetric — put side lifted more than call side; crash protection commands permanent extra premium
- Shift happened simultaneously in FX, interest rate, and commodity markets — no disruption in those markets, so it was a clear **ideological shift**, not a local market event
- Adjusting for smile/skew changes what an option *is*: can no longer be derived mathematically from the underlying, so it's no longer a "derivative" in the strict sense
- Two competing models: **local volatility** vs. **stochastic volatility** — gave opposite advice; neither resolved as of writing

### Mortgage Market Break
- Early 1980s CMO creation: ~4% annual profit (10% NPV of mortgage value) converting government mortgage securities into **collateralized mortgage obligations**
- Pre-crash mispricing: all government mortgage bonds priced similarly regardless of CMO desirability; the most CMO-attractive bonds should have been bid up
- October 19-22: "the government mortgage security curve snapped to a perfect fit" of interest rate modeler computations overnight — eliminated most mortgage arbitrage and compressed CMO issuance spreads permanently
- Unlike standard arbitrage (slow correction then equilibrium), these arbitrages persisted and grew as more quants piled in, then **all fixed at once**
- Dark interpretation: "the market seemed to have figured out a way first to blow up all the quants, and then move markets in the direction quants had been betting"

> "If I told you I thought that was true — that the market is sentient and decided to hurt quants — you would be justified in calling me paranoid. Well, I don't think I'm paranoid, but that's still the most natural way I remember 1987."

### Three Statistical Philosophies (Dissertation Context)
- **University of Chicago**, working for **Professor Craig Ainsley** on **conditional heteroskedasticity** — time series where volatility is unknown and changing (stock prices can be calm for months then suddenly move 2%+ daily)
- **Rob Engle** (frequentist, California professor) presented at Chicago; Craig attacked him ferociously, then pulled author aside: "That guy is way ahead of us; we have to drop everything so we can publish first"
- Engle published first, won **Nobel Prize** for **ARCH (autoregressive conditional heteroskedasticity)**; Craig's Bayesian version was arguably superior on some points but had fewer practical applications
- **Frequentists** (Engle/ARCH): assume well-defined underlying process with knowable long-run mean; stick with strategy forever given sufficient capital; leads to belief in market equilibrium; card counters
- **Bayesians** (Craig): start with prior, update to posterior on new data; market always efficient, continually improving knowledge; problem — no prior belief works in practice: weak priors miss opportunities, strong priors get wiped in crises
- **Nonparametric / data analysts** (author's camp): jackknifes, bootstraps, cross-tabulations; let data speak; minimal assumptions; poker players
- **Resampling**: author's approach — string together randomly selected past days (with replacement) to generate distribution of future outcomes; requires returns to be independent
- **Heteroskedasticity vs. fat tails**: with pure fat tails, extreme days are random in time; with heteroskedasticity, extreme moves cluster together — financial data showed far more heteroskedasticity than fat tails
- Author's dissertation criterion: parameters maximizing **Kelly bet growth rate**, not minimum squared error (frequentist) or consistent beliefs (Bayesian)
- Resampling outperformed both in author's tests; rejected by dissertation committee as not sufficiently related to prior literature; later validated by crash — except 1987 itself (sui generis; worked well in 2007-2009)

### Three Paths Post-1987
1. **Frequentist capital managers** (small hedge funds): calculated precise capital requirements (e.g., 1-in-1,000 chance of losing $1M+ → hold $1M capital = single-A rated bond). Layered capital structure: cheap AAA-like safe capital + expensive equity-like risky capital; charge each strategy its cost of capital, drop those that can't clear the hurdle
2. **Bayesian earnings modelers** (big banks): believed capital/market-value risk management was misguided — led to buying high, selling low. Risk management meant steering steady through storms. Example: a 10-year bank loan doesn't lose "value" when rates rise (hold to maturity, cash flows unchanged) — so marking to market was counterproductive
3. **Nonparametric value traders** (author's camp): daily marked-to-market value of opening positions. Most days resemble recent past, but with some probability of being entirely different; filled tail distribution with long-term history + cross-market examples + hypothetical scenarios. Stress scenario: loss = leverage squared regardless of positions held (reflecting 1987 pattern)

> "A stock doesn't know that you own it... But in October 1987, it seemed like the market knew exactly what you owned."

### Birth of VaR
- Late 1989/early 1990: risk focus shifted from extreme market crises to **interconnections among firms and events** — a series of scandals and bankruptcies causing cascading damage
- Key problem: **JPMorgan**, **Bankers Trust**, **Citibank** (nervous about FX) could not aggregate risk across trading desks
- Accounting was useless — most exposure was in derivatives, invisible to accountants
- Even visible positions: how do you compare owning $1B stocks vs. shorting $1B different stocks? Positions changed faster than accounting could track
- Senior management fear: all desks unknowingly on the same side of a bet; $10M or $100M could vanish mysteriously — what if tomorrow it's $10B?
- **Value camp** (author's) had the only universally comparable number: daily P&L on opening positions, available for every desk
- Problem: value camp lacked systems to aggregate across asset classes; each desk used different and sometimes inconsistent market parameters (commodity desk: oil futures and gasoline futures marked separately; difference ≠ input used by crack spread options desk)
- **Capital camp** had the right *metric* but not the measure: maximum loss at specified probability over specified horizon — testable, cross-desk
- Synthesis: value camp's measure + capital camp's metric = **Value at Risk (VaR)**
- Every day estimate the VaR; verify that losses exceed it on ~5% of days (or chosen confidence level) — not more, not less

> "It amuses me how casually people use the name VaR today without thinking that it makes no sense at all. Value isn't at risk... Nobody invented VaR. It was a hybrid stitched together out of existing parts in order to get a quick answer to a question quants didn't think was important."

### VaR Was Harder Than Expected
- First shock: computing VaR correctly was extremely difficult
- Gaussian approximations failed; nonparametric methods also failed
- Breaks were clustered in time and came disproportionately when VaR was low — the opposite of what you want
- E.g., breaks on 5% of days overall, but 15% of days following another VaR break, and only 2% more than four weeks after a break — systemically dangerous
- Most breaks came from **system and data errors**, not market movements; more time spent on approximations for missing data than on price dynamics
- Critical rule: **VaR is never recomputed** — the estimate set at start of trading is what gets tested, not a corrected ex-post value; risk management is about the number used for decisions
- Eventual solution: layered ad hoc methods combined with learning algorithms, not elegant theory
- Unexpected benefit: VaR could change suddenly, and those sudden changes were often useful early warnings — a risk measure needs to *surprise* you to be valuable

### Computing VaR — Three Methods

**Method 1: Historical Simulation**
- Back-testing: $1M in S&P 500 from 1930-present = 19,922 days → 1% VaR should produce 199 breaks; acceptable range 173-227
- Look back 1,000 days; set VaR halfway between 10th and 11th worst losses
- Produces exactly 1% breaks in-sample but **never passes a back-test**
- Fatal flaw: **heteroskedasticity** — stock volatility clusters, so look-back period often contains wrong regime
- Result: 314 breaks vs. expected 199 (57% too many)
- 23 of 314 breaks came day after another break → 7.3% break probability after a break (should be 1%)
- 136 breaks within 10 days of prior break → 6.8% conditional probability
- If 25+ days since last break, probability drops below 0.4%
- Average VaR on break days = $24,100 vs. average VaR of $26,500 — VaR is *lower* on break days, meaning it signals low risk when risk is highest. Worse than a constant number
- Most popular VaR for regulatory/reporting purposes anyway — easy to compute, objective, never surprises
- "You will be a popular risk manager, because you'll always tell people to do what they wanted to do anyway"
- **Procyclical effect**: takes too much risk after good times, cuts back after bad — exactly wrong

**Method 2: Parametric (Gaussian EWMA)**
- Assumes price changes drawn from **Gaussian (normal) distribution**
- **RiskMetrics** version: **EWMA** (exponentially weighted moving average) — updates daily: new estimate = 0.94 × yesterday's variance + 0.06 × today's move²; recent days weighted heavily, old days never fully forgotten
- Standard: VaR = 2.33 × √variance for 99%
- Result on S&P 500: **1,298 breaks** vs. expected 199 — catastrophically wrong
- Adds second failure on top of heteroskedasticity: **fat tails** — financial returns have far more extreme events than normal distribution predicts
- Day-after-break conditional: 154 breaks (11.7% chance vs. expected 1%)
- Can be fixed by setting VaR = 7.63 × √variance → gets right break count and reduces clustering; parametric responds to volatility faster than historical sim — but average VaR on break days ($22,300) still less than average VaR ($27,200)

**Method 3: Parametric + Scaling Factor**
- Simple fix: when VaR break occurs, **double the VaR estimate for next day** (100% scaling factor); each non-break day, multiply scaling factor by 0.94
- Key property needed: VaR must react violently to unusual events and settle moderately quickly afterward — more important than other algorithmic details
- Result: **188 breaks** (within 173-227 CI), randomly distributed in time, only 2 breaks day after a break (correct)
- Average VaR on break days = $27,500 vs. $25,100 average — *higher* on break days (directionally correct, unlike first two methods)
- All three back-tests must pass simultaneously — easy to cheat on any two

> "The only algorithms that work are based on simple principles — such as if anything happens inconsistent with yesterday's risk model, double whatever risk you assigned to that thing yesterday."

### Why This All Matters
- This was tested on the simplest possible case: one asset (S&P 500), continuous history, no trading — and standard methods still fail catastrophically
- Practical VaR involves hundreds to millions of market factors, interdependencies, position errors, instruments without price history
- These algorithms cannot be built in controlled systems, audited meaningfully, or explained. Only reason to trust them: they work, and nothing else does

> "Quantitative methods are extremely powerful and useful, but only when subjected to constant, rigorous validation."

### VaR Is Not a Risk Measure
- Only answers: "What is the amount we expect losses to exceed with 5% probability tomorrow, if markets are normal and we don't trade?"
- Says nothing about break days, abnormal markets, actual trading, or periods longer than one day
- Real value: **chain of command information flow** — each level speaks to adjacent level using VaR as common language

### VaR Goes Public
- **Till Guldimann** of JPMorgan released research paper in 1993 — first public introduction outside Wall Street's inner circle
- 1994: Morgan opened free database of correlations/volatilities for VaR computation → grew into **RiskMetrics** → purchased by **MSCI Barra**
- **JPMorgan CEO Dennis Weatherstone** wanted the **"4:15 report"** — daily firmwide risk summary available immediately after market close
  - Traders imagined: report profits relative to risk, keep limits if ratio good
  - CEOs imagined: look at number, order risk reduced if too high
  - Both unrealistic — VaR doesn't answer either question
- Reducing VaR doesn't obviously reduce risk — squeezing normal results might expand abnormal ones or vice versa
- Inside Wall Street, VaR benefits prompted quants to question the nature of risk:
  - Inside VaR boundary = frequentist domain (lots of data, historical frequency reliable)
  - Outside VaR boundary = no good data, probability is a matter of belief

## Chapter 16: Why Risk Managers Failed to Prevent the Financial Crisis

### The Risk Management Spectrum
- Firms that took risk management seriously **all survived**; among less-serious firms, total slackers did no worse than average achievers
- **Middle-office risk management only helps if done really well** — regulator-imposed minimum compliance is useless

### JPMorgan vs. Morgan Stanley
- JPMorgan ran below minimum risk 2000-2004, then at minimum 2005-2007 — quiet underachievers cause diffuse harm
- Morgan Stanley was next domino after Lehman/Merrill — any more risk would have been fatal
- Two firms were "a hairbreadth apart" in practice
- **Crisis was not caused by any one firm failing** — systemic

### Pre-Crisis Stress Tests
- Typical firm going into 2007 had credit stress event ~40% as bad as what actually occurred — stress test was not worst case, just a planning scenario
- Stress tests **did** flag danger to **AIG**, **Fannie Mae (FNMA)**, **Freddie Mac (FHLMC)**, and mortgage/municipal bond insurers
- They did **not** show significant danger to major commercial/investment banks
- Stress tests included housing price declines and subprime losses — firms reserved capital and bought insurance accordingly
- Crisis mechanism: as things worsened, firms cut exposures and increased hedges via **BBB spread trades**:
  - Buy protection on BBB-rated securities (first to signal credit crunch; junk defaults in good and bad times, but BBB spike = credit crunch signal)
  - Fund by selling protection on super-senior securities (AAA-rated, designed to survive even Great Depression repeat)
  - In cash flow terms this worked: BBB protection paid off, super-seniors stayed safe
- **What nearly killed firms was the leverage required**: $1B BBB protection funded by $10B super-senior protection sold → $11B notional derivatives position for $1B hedge
- In the crisis depths, no one cared about position quality — only leverage
- Many firms went broke holding positions that would eventually have paid off
- Author's diagnosis: firms bet we weren't heading into worse-than-Great-Depression AND that markets would remain somewhat liquid — the second bet nearly killed them

> "You can't make the financial system safe by making each institution safe; you can make it safe only by making the system robust to individual institution failure."

### What Regulators Should Do
- **Isolate essential financial infrastructure** from Wall Street risk-taking: no derivative bet should threaten small bank accounts, ATMs, or short-term business credit
- Absurd to guarantee bank deposits then let banks put that money in anything but Treasuries (or at most home mortgages and short-term business credit)
- **Reduce inter-firm linkages**: too many unrelated businesses under holding companies, too many offsetting transactions creating pure sloppiness
- **Slash reporting volume** — warehouses full of fiction — instead demand a few key pieces of validated data
- Require 95% one-day VaR estimates for all trading businesses, daily, before trading; validate that breaks are correct in number and independence
- **Stop bailouts**: they destroy healthy evolution and creative destruction; clog the system with "weeds that should have gone extinct years ago"

> "There should be a lot of financial firm failures. They should fail fast and often. The only safe system is one that allows failure."

### Managing Risk (Post-VaR)
After VaR, stress tests, scenario analysis, and hole-patching — risk management divides into two parts:
1. **Inside VaR boundary**: plenty of data, limited outcomes → conventional statistical analysis; goal = get risk to **Kelly levels**, no higher or lower
2. **Beyond VaR boundary**: the three unspeakable truths

- Kelly implementation is complex: Kelly is sensitive to low-probability bad events, requiring trans-VaR analysis to properly set wealth and variance parameters (using enterprise value and day-to-day variance will cause over-risking and failure)
- Risk manager is **not** a dictator — essential information exists in the executive suite (strategic plans, stakeholder attitudes) and in business units
- Must build systems to: (a) aggregate business unit risk into coherent picture for exec committee, and (b) translate exec decisions into meaningful instructions for frontline
- Analogy to **chief counsel**: brings legal information, participates in decisions, defers on business matters within legal bounds — but will not accept illegal decisions; "hired gun" vs. corporate counsel distinction
- Risk limits are the primary enforcement tool: objective, measurable, controllable by business unit heads, proportional to risk levels
- Supplemented by peer risk manager conversations — more nuanced than top-down limit blunt instrument

## Unspeakable Truth #1: Risk Managers Should Make Sure Firms Fail

- **"Fail often, fail fast, fail cheap"** — failure is not the problem; slow and expensive failure is
- *Cheap* is not measured by initial outlay — it means pulling the plug quickly, not bleeding money to prove you were right
- Historical analogy: fewer soldiers died in battle than were massacred fleeing battles fought too long after any hope of victory was gone
- **Firms should fail** — they exist to serve customers; if they don't, they should be replaced; "trying to run a firm to zero probability of failure guarantees a firm that does no good in life, lives too long, and does too much bad in death"
- "Too many firms failed to fail in 2004-2007, and clogged up the drain in 2008"
- **Evolutionary logic**: crises forge valuable parts; position firm so unfit parts slough off cleanly and innovations that worked well can flourish — must be arranged ahead of time (politics takes over once crisis passes)
- **Customer primacy in evolutionary terms**: fitness = value to customers (in finance, often a fiduciary duty)
  - Shareholder gains, employee welfare, creditor safety are secondary
  - Creditors get first lien and interest rate — that is their protection, not anyone's goodwill
- Crisis example: universal banks couldn't serve all masters simultaneously — trading desks, lending, asset management, and investment banking had directly conflicting customer obligations
- Every department should face competitive selection pressure — if IT spends twice what a vendor would charge, the firm's fitness degrades
- "Let things fail one at a time in good times rather than all at once in a crisis"

### Compensation as Natural Selection
- Quants' original insight: align interests by ensuring risk takers have **same fraction of wealth at stake** as the firm
- Mechanism: **out-of-the-money indexed stock options** that pay only if company significantly outperforms peers, with long vesting (5 years) and holding periods (additional 5 years after exercise) — spread deep into the org chart, not just top management
- Cash bonuses paid promptly and generously when the risk system confirms performance; payment timing mirrors position lifecycle (liquid traders paid fast; structured product creators paid years later)
- Compensation schemes work automatically — aligned incentives make monitoring unnecessary
- **Post-crisis regulatory blunder**: governments imposed compensation for future performance — exactly backwards
  - If bonus withheld unless firm survives 3 years → employee assumes firm will survive (why care about something already priced in?)
  - Correct: pay bonus only if firm fails → employee racks their brain for every way firm could fail and makes bets that pay off in that scenario; those bets might save the firm
- **Boat builder analogy**: pay builder only if boat doesn't sink → she builds shaky boat you never take out in bad weather, skips safety equipment. Pay only if boat sinks → she builds one you can take in storms with great safety equipment. Best: pay promptly as quality is revealed and validated

> "I'm sorry to say that your government chose to put you and your financial future on that first boat. As a professional risk manager, I advise you to learn how to swim."

## Unspeakable Truth #2: Good Stuff Beyond the VaR Limit

- Beyond VaR is also where **superposition** lives — a type of randomness where a variable takes all possible values at once

### The eRaider.com Case Study
- In 1995, everyone agreed Internet stocks were overpriced — rare moment of clarity. Red-blooded risk takers should be *creating and selling* Internet stocks
- NASDAQ went up 5x from 1995 to 2000, then fell back to ~1995 level by 2002
- **Amazon.com** IPO: ~$4 split-adjusted, rose to $113, fell under $6 — classic bubble arc
- Knowing the crash would happen was easy; *profiting* was hard — peak-to-trough Amazon fell ~95% but had multi-month >100% rallies on the way down. Any leverage would have wiped out even a correct short before being proven right
- **Timing is the killer** in bubble shorts — even certainty of outcome doesn't help if you can't survive the path
- **Superposition business**: build businesses that work under *both* "Internet stocks are worth a lot" AND "worth little" — such ideas cannot work under coin-flip probability analysis; each side rejects it for opposite reasons
- Author and **Martin Stoller** created **eRaider.com**: used bubble-era stock options (not IPO) to recruit 36 top business experts, built $20M-equivalent business for ~$1M. $5,000 printed 20,000 stock certificates on real banknote stock — accepted "like thousand-dollar bills"
- Post-bubble thesis: people would blame Wall Street analysts, want better corporate governance — eRaider positioned for that. Also held stocks calculated to perform well when bubble burst
- The idea ultimately failed, but the mechanism worked: volatility gave them a $20M business for $1M; the business model, not the superposition logic, was flawed

> "Success often comes from ideas that make sense under no single version of reality, but are calculated to exploit uncertainty and volatility."

### Superposition in Risk Management
- Risk managers encourage superposition ideas that add little in normal times but catch wind in trans-VaR events
- Accomplished by **giving permission to fail** — the most underappreciated power of the risk manager
- Pre-approved risk → failure tolerated; unapproved → not tolerated. Enables "fail fast" culture

## Unspeakable Truth #3: Risk Managers Create Risk

- Risk managers deliberately **add artificial risk** to improve outcomes
- Game theory and Monte Carlo analysis prove adding artificial randomness can improve outcomes

### The Standardization Problem
- Large organizations naturally standardize everything — increases efficiency but also **correlation**, creating fragility
- Standardization pathologies:
  - Easy to exploit by rogue employees/competitors (predictable = gameable)
  - Enemy of change — you won't notice better alternatives if you never try them
  - Single standardized systems make errors rarer but catastrophic when they occur
  - Inhibits adaptation when conditions change
- Adding **deliberate randomness** at micro level improves macro predictability: harder for competitors to exploit weaknesses, harder for employees to game system, more robust to single-event disasters, enables evolution
- Risk manager adds randomness through: campaigns against useless standardization, exposing people to how other organizations solve the same problem, tolerating employee turnover (costly but imports new ideas)
- Benefit of added randomness can be **quantified and balanced** against cost savings of standardization — not anarchy, but deliberate calibration

> "It is smart risk management to take fliers sometimes, even if they are completely at random."

### Three Arguments for Diversity
1. **Evolution** — customer-driven selection pressure kills worse ideas, rewards better ones
2. **Superposition** — mixed systems can do things no standardized system can
3. **Pure robustness** — random variation means one event can't bring whole system down

## Chapter 17: The Story of Risk

Visual history of risk in graphic format.

### Ancient World
- **Hammurabi's Code**: same punishments for theft, fraud, and accidental loss — no distinction between intentional and random harm
- Ancient gamblers could compute dice odds but no evidence probability applied to non-gambling decisions
- **Phoenicians**: exempted "bottomry" loans from usury laws if ship lost — possibly earliest **risk-adjusted return**

### The Probability Revolution
- **Jakob Bernoulli**, *Ars Conjectandi* (published 1713): first proof of **Law of Large Numbers**
- To be 99% sure frequency is between 24-26%, must repeat 4-flip experiment 12,354 times — the **frequentist limit**: probability as long-run frequency requires repeatable experiments

### Bruno de Finetti and Bayesian Probability
- **Bruno de Finetti** (1937): first fully coherent answer to non-repeatable events
- Force someone to name a price for a bet on a one-time event — that price reveals **subjective probability**
- Named Bayesian after **Thomas Bayes** but basic ideas formalized by **Pierre-Simon Laplace**; modern version by **Richard Cox** and de Finetti

### Two Schools and Their Practitioners
- **Frequentists**: became blackjack card counters, reclusive hedge fund managers; believed in market equilibrium
- **Bayesians**: gravitated toward sports betting, structured finance; believed in market efficiency
- **Abraham Wald** (two years after de Finetti): drew on both → decision theory, nonparametric statistics. Followers liked poker, became traders and PMs
- **All three traditions came together on Wall Street in 1987**

## Chapter 18: Frequency versus Degree of Belief

### The 1654 Fermat-Pascal Letters
- The interrupted game problem: two gamblers playing to 7 wins, one has 6 wins and other has 5 — how to divide the stake?
- **Fermat** (combinatorial): play out all 4 possible continuations → first player wins 3 of 4 → three-fourths
- **Pascal** (legalistic): start from tied = half; leader already owns half; next game contests other half → three-fourths
- Pascal's joy at both methods yielding same answer was the revolution
- Critical: neither mentions the probability of winning — Fermat is pure frequency, Pascal is pure belief

### Bernoulli's Ars Conjectandi and the Urn
- Draw 10 marbles from urn of 100: 9 red, 1 black. If ≤50 are red, max probability of drawing 9+ red out of 10 = 11/1024 = 1.07%

> "Randomness is something we create in order to learn about something deterministic. Just by stirring the marbles around so you pick at random, the marble you hold in your hand can tell you something about the marbles remaining in the urn."

- Key insight: the urn has a *deterministic* number of red marbles; randomness is deliberately introduced to enable inference

### Frequentist vs. Bayesian Formally
- Frequentist: "I can reject at 5% significance the null hypothesis" — says nothing about actual number of red marbles; accuracy claim is about long-run accuracy of many statements
- Bayesian: must have **prior belief**; update to posterior. Under uniform prior, probability of ≥50 red goes from 50% to 99.47% after observing 9 of 10 red
- Bayesian probability requires **moneylike** stakes — you must be able to divide and compare
- **Money fuses frequency and degree of belief** — Fermat and Pascal's original insight

### Why Both Schools Fall Short
- **Frank Knight** and **Keynes** distinguished uncertainty amenable to math vs. not — but assumed coin-flip logic was the only mathematical treatment
- Author: mainstream probability theorists are wrong because he tried this stuff and reports what works

### Black-Scholes: The Arbitrage Argument
- Stock at $70, worth $100 or $50 tomorrow; option to buy at $80. Option = 40 shares minus $2,000 = **$8/share**
- BSM backward arbitrage = forward stochastic PDE = CAPM equilibrium — all converge
- **Derivative** was coined ~1970 because price is *derivable* via arbitrage reasoning
- BSM is not a pricing model — it's a **conversion tool** (option price ↔ implied volatility), like solving yield-to-maturity from bond price
- **Vega**: sensitivity to implied vol — "not actually a Greek letter; some forgotten trader thought it was"

### Risk-Neutral Probability Distribution
- From option prices across strikes/expiries: extract exact distribution a risk-neutral investor places on stock
- Risk-neutral distribution = **degree of belief** (from prices); actual distribution = **frequency** (from history)
- First theory to relate degree of belief to frequency while keeping them distinct

### Change of Numeraire
- Expressing option in shares rather than dollars = **change of numeraire**
- Picking the right numeraire can transform a hard probability problem into one answerable from observation
- **Kraitchik's envelope paradox**: two envelopes with X and 2X. Apparent paradox (always switch) resolves when you recognize X as numeraire changes when you switch
- **Pascal's Wager** used same trick: switching numeraire makes infinite-payoff faith arguments appear reasonable

### Polling as a Case Study
- Political polling seems like pure objective probability (no money), but: can't get truly random samples; respondents lie or change minds
- Author's test for believing a pollster: would you bet at the odds they claim? If pollster says 60% Democrat → would you put $59 to $41?
- Need ~20,000 predictions (state assemblies, Congress, governors, president) for statistical power — not just one presidential race
- Must prespecify conditions under which model is invalid; post-hoc excuses don't count
- Must back-test: assign each prediction a bet sized by electoral population, check if the betting book was profitable

### Why Numeraire Choice Is Foundational
- Frequentist statistics: no logical bridge from frequency to belief without moneylike terms
- Bayesian statistics: insists on one universal numeraire (dollars), often wrong — money can't buy everything, people have multiple natures
- Statistics cannot settle arguments unless people agree on a numeraire first — "there are always statisticians on both sides of any lawsuit"
- Only trustworthy numeraire: one **validated by back-testing** and measurable in observable transactions

### The Middle-Layer Problem
- Post-WWII: advances in data processing outpaced advances in data validation
- Fields that developed a **middle layer** of statisticians between theorists and data collectors failed completely — the layer consumed resources and insulated both sides
- **Economics** is the prime example: theorists without data and data miners without theory both went broke
- **Finance** was the "bastard offshoot" that succeeded: huge monetary rewards to people who could bridge theory and data; survivors figured it out → field exploded in profitability, knowledge, and influence

## Chapter 19: The Secret History of Wall Street: 1993-2007

### Scale of Transformation
- 1980: investment banks were small private partnerships; commercial banks had 19th-century balance sheets; Glass-Steagall separated them; hedge funds were obscure; few individuals cared about Wall Street
- Triggering event: **rocket scientists** arrived ~1980, building on finance professors' work from 1950s-70s
- U.S. economy roughly **doubled** in real terms 1980-2007 (GDP, national income, nonfinancial corporate profits)
- Financial businesses **quadrupled** in real terms (revenue, profits, assets)
- Financial employment grew only slightly faster than population, but composition shifted: lower-wage service employees fell, higher-wage decision-makers increased dramatically
- Finance contracted geographically (neighborhood bankers, insurance agents, stockbrokers displaced by global institutions in NY and London) but expanded culturally (consciousness of finance penetrated homes, TV, computers)
- **Andrew Oldham**'s insight (1963): bad boys get more attention — he made the Rolling Stones scary. Finance took ~20 years to apply: "junk bonds" language, "hostile" raids, **George Soros** glorying in "attacking Britain" rather than explaining economic efficiency

### The Quant Revolution — Statistical Framework
- **VaR limit** = the region where the numeraire can be trusted; outside VaR, data can't be aggregated under normal statistical assumptions
- Wall Street quants **pre-identified the outlier region** — forced to by top-down regulatory demands, discovered how hard and useful it was

Standard quant process for financial trading:
1. Estimate **95% one-day VaR** every trading day (even if systems down)
2. Compare actual daily **P&L** against VaR prediction
3. Test for correct number of **VaR breaks** (independent in time and level)
4. Once VaR system is reliable, collect data *within* VaR limit for analysis
5. Investigate trans-VaR days; supplement with hypothetical scenarios and historical stress dates
6. Estimate **left tail distribution** (power law often appropriate); accept nonzero catastrophic failure probability
7. Analyze trans-VaR scenarios for non-P&L risks: **leverage, liquidity, counterparty credit, model risk, fraud**
8. Compute optimal **Kelly bet** from good within-VaR data + hypothetical tail; size up to Kelly while staying under trans-VaR destruction thresholds
- The **VaR back-test** (step 3) is the only objectively validated part of the process — without it, you can't know you're doing the right thing

### Universal Statistical Framework (Author's Synthesis)
Applies beyond finance to any domain:
- Define a clear set of predictions and a clear numeraire so predictions can be aggregated
- Estimate the range of outcomes where the numeraire is trusted (rule of thumb: ~30 breaks needed to estimate one parameter)
- **Validate rigorously**: breaks count, time distribution, breaks conditional on range estimate size
- Ideally: allow public betting against your range estimate
- Analyze within-range data with standard statistics; analyze outside-range data with judgment and imagination (numeraire breaks down here)
- Build a plan optimizing growth across both ordinary and out-of-region scenarios

> "Someone who can't predict the ordinary events can't have much useful to say about the entire distribution. Someone who could predict ordinary events but doesn't bother is missing a valuable tool to improve knowledge."

- Author claims this framework is "the first satisfying and complete answer to the problem that began with Pascal and Fermat in 1654"
- Took **Jakob Bernoulli** 35 years to formalize Pascal's ideas → author predicts rigorous statistical foundations should emerge ~2025

### Where Did the Money Come From?
- Global savings rates declined — Wall Street didn't grow because people saved more
- Key shift: defined-benefit → defined-contribution plans
- Real source: **quants created capital by redefining its basis** from cash/gold to *risk* — effectively doubled available capital worldwide
- True capital creation requires derivatives and securitization

### Where Did the Money Come From? (continued)
- **Debt monetization** (printing money instead of issuing bonds): author believes average effect is negative — people aren't fooled, they anticipate future printing and discount accordingly
- True capital creation requires derivatives and securitization — "it sounds like black magic. It's more or less magic, but it's not black. You get to keep your soul."

### Social Value of Finance
- Common answer (finance makes markets more efficient) is wrong — financial trading more likely to push markets away from efficiency; when misaligned, markets snap suddenly in the opposite direction traders were pushing
- **Real social value of finance is profit** — in honest finance, both sides of a transaction benefit AND the intermediary profits, creating genuine value that accumulates at strategic locations for further investment
- Quants entered a system where most financial profits were "taxes on real activities, protected by information hoarding and crony regulation" — they changed this by wanting to profit via value creation

### Three Camps of Quants — Recap
- **Capital camp** (frequentist): small hedge funds, VaR originators
- **Value camp** (nonparametric): trading desks, VaR users
- **Bayesian camp** (sports bettors): executive/staff positions in large firms, hitched to earnings-risk, left out of VaR revolution
- Bayesians: adaptive, no long run, accept changing conditions — some became "unreconstructed earnings-risk believers" who blamed mark-to-market for the crisis (author disagrees: refusal to accept honest marks *was* the problem)

### Risk-Based Capital and Basel
- **Bayesians** stole focus on capital from frequentists by reversing the idea: instead of capital held to offset risk, **risk would define capital** → "risk-based capital" born
- All financial institutions should hold capital proportionate to asset riskiness — this definition suspiciously resembled the earnings-volatility measure sports bettors had always favored
- **Reserves** (fraction of liabilities held as specific assets — originally gold, later central bank deposits): protect liquidity (short-term redemption)
- **Capital** (fraction of assets funded by equity or specific liabilities): protects long-term solvency
- Example: 10% reserve requirement on $100 deposit → $90 lent out, redeposited, re-lent → $100 deposit supports $1,000 in loans. 8% capital requirement on $100 loan → $8 must come from equity
- A bank can satisfy reserves but have negative capital (insolvent long-term) or satisfy capital but have no reserves (illiquid short-term)

**Basel I** (1988): **BIS** declared internationally active banks must hold capital ≥ 8% of assets
- Key flaws: no capital required for government bonds; "capital" could include certain liabilities
- **Incentive to pick riskiest assets within each bucket**: short-term senior loans to blue chips AND long-term subordinated loans to near-bankrupt firms both carried 100% risk weight, but the latter paid much higher interest → higher ROE
- Post-crisis leverage limits are just Basel I stated in reverse — "limiting leverage is a step backward in bank regulation"

**Basel II** (developed 1994-2005, implemented 2006-2009): capital requirement = 3× the 99%, 10-day VaR
- VaR as risk measure is theoretically crazy: determined by center of distribution, ignores tails; 99% 10-day VaR requires 30,000 trading days (119 years) of data for reliability
- VaR as risk measure was practically defensible: no better measure existed; forced banks to confront central risk; could be back-tested objectively
- **Basel use test**: banks would use same metrics for internal control and regulatory reporting
- Fatal decisions: **variance-covariance VaR** emphasized (assumes normal distribution — never back-tests well); **capital relief promised** to advanced banks; **forward-looking aspects jettisoned** (capital rules must be counter-cyclical but this was stripped to fit under the 8% bar)
- ~2004: market volatility dropped to historic lows during final implementation — Basel II requirements appeared adequate; everyone understood volatility would return but had too much momentum to stop
- Basel II not actually responsible for pre-crisis undercapitalization: banks weren't on Basel II yet; failures came from liquidity loss and market confidence

> "The capital number might as well be fiction. Never trust a complicated computation that cannot be independently validated against some objective standard."

### Capital Formation, Allocation, Price Discovery
- Three functions of financial markets: capital formation, capital allocation, price discovery
- Quants 1980-2007 doubled world capital without persuading anyone to give up existing assets — made beliefs about future value count as assets (**"the bezzle"** — **Galbraith**'s term: embezzlement creates capital since both parties think they have the money)
- **Securitization** = **disintermediation**: takes institutions out of finance, linking providers and users directly
  - Traditional savings bank: consumes half the mortgage interest before paying depositors; bailed out taxpayers ~$100B when rates rose
  - MBS pool: national diversification, standardized for liquid trading, borrowers pay less, investors earn more
- **"Slicing and dicing"** is a poor metaphor — author prefers "bathed and lathed" or "hewed and glued" (more like making plywood than cutting vegetables)
- **Credit derivatives** pulled credit risk from bonds/loans and packaged into diversified portfolios with predictable characteristics
- Root cause of every securitization disaster: **failure to sell the entire deal in true arm's-length transactions** — when margins compressed, structurers jammed unsalable pieces into managed accounts, lent buyers money to purchase them, or held internally with fake hedges; "always, always led to disaster"
- **Liquidity cascade**: 5-year investor benefits from monthly prices; monthly from daily; daily from 15-min; HFT trader at frontier has worst position — no reference prices, must trade in small batches — but by accepting that position, everyone else's liquidity improves
- Three great innovations (free capital, improved allocation, better price discovery) yet produced serial disasters: Drexel Burnham, Bankers Trust, Kidder Peabody, LTCM, 2007-2009, Orange County, Enron
- Author's position: good from financial innovation far exceeds damage

### Exonerating the Commonly Blamed
- **Greed** was not the cause — everyone in finance is "greedy" in some sense; no more greed on Wall Street than Main Street
- The "heads I win, tails the firm loses" accusation is sillier still: largest shareholders in all major firms were **employees**
- Three CEOs with over $1 billion at stake: **Hank Greenberg** (AIG), **Dick Fuld** (Lehman), **Jimmy Cayne** (Bear Stearns) — all failed. The people with most to lose were the ones who lost
- Decisions were reckless but not made recklessly — senior people worried about pride, friends, employees, customers, creditors

### The Root Cause: Risk Takers Were Chased Out
- **The biggest single reason things fell apart**: risk takers systematically displaced by **risk avoiders**
- Wall Street was built to be run by people with deep risk-taking experience — niche opportunities, failing fast, milking successes until limits, then finding new opportunities
- As Wall Street grew far bigger than imagined, people with no risk-taking backgrounds flooded in to "get rich safely"
- **Risk avoiders' tell**: better credentials, fewer failures (no risk = no failure), better dressed, better interpersonal skills — and they all agreed with each other
- Risk avoiders love steadily growing revenue — you can't get that from genuine risk-taking but can fake it by forcing exponential growth on ideas past their limit
- **Risk takers must be honest to survive** — it's bred in the bone. Risk avoiders think that as long as everyone is calm, the ship can't sink
- Risk avoiders eviscerated VaR by putting it into controlled systems, preferring predictability over information content

### Exodus of Quants
- First to leave: **card-counting frequentists** — started hedge funds, kept them small
- Next: most **poker players** — things got too regimented, couldn't support good ideas
- **Bayesian sports bettors** initially benefited (social skills) but were passed over for promotion; found natural home in tech start-ups in late 1990s
- By early 2000s, hard to find an original vintage quant on Wall Street
- **Key structural problem**: risk takers don't form consensus, while risk avoiders do — even if risk takers had been in majority, their disparate views couldn't penetrate formal decision-making

### Vision for the Future
- Author's preferred vision: smaller, diverse, innovative businesses — competing approaches to financial tasks, failures hurting only investors and employees
- A **robust payment-processing and short-term credit system** run by risk avoiders for risk avoiders — he calls these **"People's Banks" / "Volcksbanks"** (honoring **Paul Volcker**)
- His prediction: big institutions will reinvent themselves and retain control of essential financial infrastructure

## Chapter 20: The Secret History — The 2007 Crisis and Beyond

### Allegorical Fable (Graphic Novel)
Cast of characters representing risk-taking archetypes:
- **Red Blood** — original risk taker; serial failures, ignoring established wisdom; by 1993 on FX/options desk
- **Blue Blood** — credentialed risk avoider; takes over as CEO after scandal
- **Hot Blood** — most profitable trader, aggressive, over-limit
- **Cold Blood** — 5-year track record of steady growth, no losses — the risk-avoider's ideal
- **Blood Sucker** — risk manager hired to impose professional standards
- **Thin Blood** — impeccable credentials; "saw it all coming, nobody listened"
- **UN** — desk head who follows all rules

Key dynamics: Red runs the desk; Hot Blood makes most money but is over limit. When Blue takes over, Cold Blood is hired for "steadily growing profits with no losses" fitting the firm's strategic vision. Conflict: firm can't make quarterly earnings targets; Cold proposes adjusting model parameters after the quarter closes (fraud). Blood Sucker objects — warns of death spiral: earnings disappointment → stock falls → confidence loss → cost of capital rises → people leave → options lose value → earnings worse. "We're being taken over cheap by some bank with good risk management."

Board sides with Blue over Blood Sucker. UN put in charge ("he's a trader who really understands risk — he's never over limit"). Firm fails.

Blame assigned after failure: Blue (did everything according to best expert advice), Blood Sucker (imposed highest professional standards), Thin Blood (impeccable credentials, "I saw it all coming"), Cold Blood (possessed extraordinary vision, "would have worked but for irrational market events"), UN (followed all the rules). True blame falls on Red Blood — "long history of serial failures and ignoring established wisdom. Failed to start slowly and grow exponentially. Planned for success when she launched and failed fast when she failed." The risk taker, not the risk avoiders who destroyed the system.

### Postmortem Lessons

**Lesson 1: Large Diversified Regulated Financial Institutions Are Fatally Flawed**
- All either failed or would have failed without massive government intervention
- Governments cannot afford a second round of bailouts — wise course is to break them up or shut them down
- Author argues from insider experience: "Unless you have worked in the middle office of some of these institutions for over a decade... I don't think you can understand the deep design deficiencies"
- Reform to date has worked in the opposite direction of what's needed: should strengthen middle offices, hire back risk takers, embrace good risk, focus on customers — and allow some to fail

**Lesson 2: Leverage's Hidden Danger**
- Simple view: leverage multiplies gains and losses. Levering a safe stock 2:1 can be safer than holding a volatile unlevered stock
- What banks actually did: used leverage to *offset* risk (hedging), not just amplify it
  - Example: bank holds $1B in Treasuries, enters short futures contract to offset interest rate risk → 2:1 gross notional leverage but *less* risk than before
- New lesson from 2007: in severe credit crunch and liquidity crisis, even **good leverage** (the kind that offsets risk) could kill — counterparty risk, clearinghouse failures, basis risk all became lethal

**Frequentist Lesson: Signal vs. Fluctuation**
- U.S. Treasury bonds did great in the crisis — but that may not repeat
- People overcorrected: decided all illiquid investments were bad, without distinguishing between (a) investments supposed to be liquid that weren't vs. (b) investments everyone knew were illiquid
- Key insight: you get *overpaid* for taking on illiquidity; most investors pay too much for more liquidity than they need
- Subprime mortgages not inherently bad — long history of benefiting borrowers and lenders
  - "No-doc loans" (called "liar loans") actually have *less* fraud than conventional loans — prime borrowers lie systematically (borrowed down payments, double-counted assets, claimed fantasy bonuses)
  - **Conventional subprime** performed better than expected given housing decline
  - No-income-verification loans served undocumented immigrants and tax evaders with real cash income; 100% LTV loans made sense for young people with education debt but steady income
- Real problem: **NINJA loans** (no income, no job, no assets — "should be NINJNA") — multiple simultaneous underwriting flaws with no compensating strengths. Not overlooking one flaw; overlooking all flaws is wishful thinking
- Fraudulent **prime** loans more dangerous than subprime: losing 100% on a pool expected to have 0.1% losses is bigger shock than losing 30% on a pool expected to have 10% losses

**Poker Player Lesson: Was the Risk the Right Size?**
- Postmortem question: not "was the risk good or bad?" but **"was the risk the right size?"**
- Toy example: 10%, 20%, 30%, 40% returns over four years, then -50% in year 5 → overall 20% profit over 5 years
  - At half size: 19.5% total profit (slightly worse)
  - At twice size: wiped out (-100% in year 5)
  - **Optimal sizing**: 77% of actual risk taken → 23% return. Should have sized slightly smaller
- Real lesson: systematically asking whether more or less risk would have improved outcomes — not just reacting to the loss

### Risk-Growth Tradeoff
- Civilian employment rose ~4.0% from end of 1999 to Nov 2007 peak, then fell 5.9% by Dec 2009; net gain of only 1.4% over the whole period
- Key argument: a **lower-risk financial system** would have produced both smaller losses AND smaller gains — the two are inseparable
- Thought experiment: if lower risk cut recession job losses from 5.9% to 3.0%, but also cut monthly job growth in good times by 30%, you'd end up with *fewer* total jobs — not more
- To justify risk reduction: must believe you can cut >50% of losses while losing <30% of gains — a very high bar of skill
- Brown has never seen anyone credibly demonstrate the ability to eliminate losses with zero effect on gains
- There may be an **optimum level of ups and downs** higher than most people assume, for both individual happiness and business productivity

> "In the end, there is no magic to risk management. Risk managers cannot prevent bad things from happening... Nevertheless, it is a revolutionary advance in human organization."

## Appendix: A Risk Management Curriculum

### Problems with Existing Programs
- Existing degrees are largely repackaged portfolio math and regulatory reporting
- True risk management requires radical rethinking of probability theory and economics
- Required breadth: probability, economics, accounting, data processing, psychology, law, logic, history, philosophy

### #1 Hiring Priority: Data Reliability
- Most valuable skill: deep understanding of real financial data in a real firm
- Prerequisite: at least 2 years' experience in financial operations, control, or IT

### Curriculum Structure
**Statistics** (Theoretical Foundation):
- Standard statistics taught to non-statisticians is "worse than useless" for a risk manager
- Requires rigorous foundation from original sources in philosophy, mathematics, and logic of probability
- Breadth over depth: diverse knowledge of many approaches > expertise in one; "Until we really understand probability (if we ever do), we need to teach it broadly"

**People** (Behavioral & Group):
- Individual: behavioral studies, cognitive psychology, welfare economics (with caution — welfare econ has more nonsense than data-driven fields)
- Special focus on how people actually make risk decisions
- Law: useful for understanding how people deal with risk before and after the fact
- Group: history of risk-taking in wars, financial institutions, politics, professional gambling, venture capital, scientific research
- Visiting practitioners preferred over academics for group-level courses

**Risk Reporting** (Practical):
- Three kinds of risk reports: internal (front + back office), regulatory, and financial
- Analogous to Financial Statement Analysis in business school — best taught cross-disciplinarily
- Additional: law/regulation of risk, commercial risk management products, professional standards and ethics
- Recommend a program-long weekly course: case studies, lectures, projects, presentations from practicing risk managers

### Four Practical Requirements
1. **Extensive VaR Calculation**: build automated system posting VaR before midnight every night, even with missing data, illness, or system failures. Any objective daily data source works (parking receipts, rain in Central Park, letter frequency in *USA Today*). Must pass standard back-test; 80% VaR recommended
2. **Tracking Down Numbers**: find something like "proportion of US tractors running on gasoline" — statistics cited casually in news articles. Forces students to discover definitional problems in seemingly straightforward questions; develops skepticism about reported data
3. **Real Research in Non-Coin-Flip Risk Models**: statistical assistance on projects in evolutionary biology, quantum physics, behavioral game theory — fields that have advanced beyond 17th-century probability. Difference between homework and genuine research (unknown answer, real stakes) cannot be replicated in classroom
4. **Personal Risk-Taking Experience**: "Anyone who is not confident that he can walk into a casino and win has no business telling others how to manage risk." Students given $1,000 play money at **Iowa Electronic Markets** each quarter (real-money prediction markets on elections, weather, movie grosses); must double it by quarter's end or start over. The school offering this program must itself be willing to take risks

> "Who wants a risk management degree awarded by an institution afraid to deviate from staid consensus?"
