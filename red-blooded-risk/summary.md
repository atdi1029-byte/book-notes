# Red-Blooded Risk: The Secret History of Wall Street

**Author:** Aaron Brown
**Illustrated by:** Eric Kim
**Pages:** 461
**Category:** Economics / Risk Management

## Summary

Red-Blooded Risk is a sweeping intellectual history of quantitative risk management, told by one of the original "rocket scientists" who rebuilt Wall Street from the inside between 1982 and 2007. Aaron Brown traces the parallel histories of probability theory (from Pascal and Fermat through frequentists and Bayesians to modern VaR), the evolution of money (from precious metals through paper to derivatives), and the quant revolution that transformed global finance.

The book's central framework distinguishes four types of risk-takers: thin-blooded (avoid risk), hot-blooded (embrace risk recklessly), cold-blooded (calculate risk mechanically), and red-blooded (feel excitement but manage risk through rigorous calculation). Brown argues that red-blooded risk management, built on seven principles including Risk Duality, VaR boundaries, Risk Ignition via Kelly criterion, and evolutionary diversity, is the key to both personal and institutional success.

Brown provides deep technical content on Value at Risk computation, the Kelly criterion vs. Modern Portfolio Theory debate, the true economics of tulip mania, how derivatives function as a superior form of money, and why the 2007 financial crisis was caused not by excessive risk-taking but by risk avoiders displacing genuine risk takers from positions of power. The book features manga-style illustrations by Eric Kim that dramatize key concepts through recurring characters.

## Key Themes

- Risk vs. danger vs. opportunity: fundamentally different categories requiring different responses
- Kelly criterion as the provably optimal bet sizing strategy, complementing Markowitz's portfolio selection
- VaR as a practical boundary separating quantifiable risk from unknowable tail events
- Money evolution from barter to precious metals to paper to derivatives, each enabling new forms of risk management
- The quant revolution: how ~100 outsiders from gambling backgrounds rebuilt global finance between 1982-2007
- Risk avoiders destroying institutions by displacing risk takers and demanding predictable growth
- Three unspeakable truths: risk managers should help firms fail fast, exploit trans-VaR opportunities via superposition, and deliberately create artificial risk to improve robustness
