# Expected Returns: An Investor's Guide to Harvesting Market Rewards
**Antti Ilmanen | Wiley Finance | 2011 | 1,010 pages**

---

## What the Book Is

Expected Returns is the most comprehensive quantitative guide to investment returns ever written for practitioners. Ilmanen — formerly at Salomon Brothers, Brevan Howard, and advisor to Norway's sovereign wealth fund — spent 20 years assembling this encyclopedic work. The central question: where do investment returns come from, and can we estimate them in advance? The answer is a resounding yes, but only if you look beyond historical averages and use forward-looking indicators, multiple perspectives (asset classes, strategy styles, and risk factors), and disciplined behavioral skepticism about your own biases.

---

## Core Thesis

Traditional institutional investing is broken. The classic 60/40 portfolio puts over 90% of risk in equities and relies on backward-looking historical averages. Ilmanen proposes a radically expanded framework:

1. **Three perspectives simultaneously**: Asset class premia, strategy style premia, and underlying risk factor premia
2. **Forward-looking market indicators are better than historical averages**: Current yield, P/E, and spread encode current expectations more honestly than historical data
3. **Not all volatility is equal**: Timing of losses matters as much as magnitude; investments that lose in bad times deserve high required premia
4. **Carry, value, and momentum work everywhere**: Across asset classes, time periods, and geographies
5. **Prudent leverage is not the enemy**: Levering low-volatility assets beats owning high-volatility ones in every asset class studied

## Key Return Sources (10 Empirical Verdicts)

1. **High volatility/high beta?** No within asset classes (lottery preference + leverage constraints). Yes across asset classes.
2. **Illiquidity?** Yes — less liquid assets earn a genuine premium, but it's not constant.
3. **Fast growth?** No — over-extrapolating past growth is the most common investor error.
4. **Cheap assets (value)?** Yes — works whether irrational or rational risk compensation.
5. **Positive carry?** Yes — especially in cross-country trades. Poor timing of losses sustains the premium.
6. **Recent distress?** No for equities; Yes for "fallen angels" (forced institutional selling creates opportunity).
7. **Momentum?** Yes in the 6-12 month window. Chasing 3+ year winners invites reversal.
8. **Leverage?** Yes if prudent — enhances returns by monetizing diversification gains.
9. **Diversification?** Yes if combined with leverage to monetize risk reduction.
10. **Timing?** Yes in moderation — valuation signals have genuine medium-term predictive ability.

## Critical Numbers and Findings

**Equity Risk Premium (1900-2009):** 5.4% compound real return globally; 5.7% arithmetic; beat bonds by 3.7%/yr. But 2% annual EPS dilution from net equity issuance means realistic real EPS growth = ~1%.

**Bond Risk Premium:** Hockey-stick shape — rises steeply from 1yr to 2-5yr, then flat/declining. Short maturities (1-3yr) have SR > 1.0. 10-year Persistence Factor (PF) = 2.5 — 150% of carry earned as excess return. Survey-based BRP (not yield curve steepness) is the best ex ante measure.

**Credit Risk Premium:** Disappointing. IG corporates earned only 24-30 bp over Treasuries historically. Index investors captured only 21% of average 129 bp spread — lost the rest to forced selling of fallen angels, spread widening, and defaults clustering in bad times. Only two consistently good credit trades: front-end top-rated credits (SR > 1.0 pre-2008) and BB-rated fallen angels.

**FX Carry (G10, 1983-2009):** SR = 0.61 consistently across every decade; EM carry SR = 1.30. The forward premium puzzle: high-yield currencies typically APPRECIATE instead of depreciating (violating UIP). G5 PF coefficients all exceed 1.7, some reach 3.6.

**Equity Value (VMG, 1926-2009):** 4.1% annual geometric premium; SR ≈ 0.39. Style timing via earnings yield spread: next-year correlation = 0.48. January seasonal: 2.2% vs. 0.24% other months.

**Commodity Momentum:** S&P GSCI equal-weighted 4-sector portfolio: 7.08% annual excess return, SR = 0.50. Roll return is the key ex ante signal. Backwardated commodities earn significantly higher returns.

**Volatility Selling:** IV-RV spread averages 4%+ in S&P 500 options — persistent, rational compensation for negative skewness. SR ≈ 1.0 gross but collapsed from 1.3 to 0.4 after 2008.

**Hedge Funds (Ibbotson-Chen-Zhu 2010):** After survivorship and backfill bias correction (3-7% total overstatement), residual alpha = 3.0% but 3.8% captured by fees. Investor timing gap costs additional 3-7%.

**Private Equity:** Phalippou-Gottschalg (2009): after proper bias adjustment, PE lagged S&P 500 by ~3%/yr. After leverage risk adjustment, underperformance doubles to ~6%.

## Portfolio Construction Framework

**The Core Problem:** 60/40 portfolio = 95% of risk in equities. This is inefficient.

**Risk Parity Solution:** Equal volatility weights → SR improves from 0.38 to 0.40; equity market correlation falls from 0.96 to 0.76. Gains from risk parity rise with negative stock-bond correlation.

**Diversification Return Formula:** GM ≈ AM − Variance/2. A 50/50 heating oil/stocks rebalanced portfolio earned 11.0% GM vs. 8.2% and 6.8% for components (DR = ~3.5%). Requires rebalancing to constant weights — drift portfolios lose this gain.

**Grinold's Fundamental Law:** IR = IC × √Breadth. A modest skill with many uncorrelated bets beats high skill with few concentrated bets.

**Style Diversification vs. Asset Diversification:** Four strategy styles (equity value, FX carry, commodity trend, vol selling): combined SR = 1.2 vs. constituent average 0.6; inter-strategy correlation = −0.05. Four asset classes combined: SR barely improves; inter-asset correlation = 0.30. Style diversification is dramatically more effective.

**Super-Diversifier Pairs (negatively correlated):** Equities + Treasuries; Value + Momentum; Carry + Trend. Carry loses in 11 of 15 worst equity months; Trend profits in 13 of 15.

## The Case for and Against Leverage

Ilmanen's "most controversial recommendation": accept prudent leverage. Within every asset class, the most volatile assets consistently earn the LOWEST long-run returns — high-vol stocks, 30-yr Treasuries, CCC-rated bonds all disappoint. This is because leverage-constrained institutions overpay for embedded leverage. The solution: lever up low-vol assets. This monetizes diversification gains and avoids overpriced lottery assets. Post-2008 regulation will make this edge LARGER, not smaller.

Risky leverage: boosting leverage when risk premia fall; levering illiquid assets; borrowing that forces liquidations during crises.

Safe leverage: levering low-vol liquid assets via exchange-traded futures/options; risk parity.

## Behavioral Pitfalls

The book catalogs why investors consistently earn less than assets offer:
- **Excessive extrapolation:** Retail equity forecasts correlate 0.9 with trailing 12M returns
- **Institutional timing gap:** Dollar-weighted HF returns are 3-7% below time-weighted
- **Comfort traps:** Avoiding leverage → overpaying for embedded leverage (PE, structured products); staying equity-centric → forfeiting diversification; yield-chasing via complex structures → fees overwhelm yield
- **Loss aversion:** Hold losers too long, sell winners too early → disposition effect → momentum profits for contrarians
- **The objective/subjective paradox:** Subjective investor expectations are negatively correlated with objective market-based expected returns. The correlation is −0.9 for Ilmanen's "irrational expectations" composite

## Seasonal and Cyclical Patterns

**January Effect:** Credit premium: positive in January, net NEGATIVE in other months. Small-cap, high-vol stocks, FX carry all concentrate returns in January. Momentum loses in January. Bond term premium: only earned OUTSIDE January.

**Turn-of-Month:** ALL US equity excess returns 1926-2005 earned over 4-day window (last business day + first 3). Other days: slightly negative. This pattern held consistently in every decade.

**Business Cycle:** Equities perform best near cycle troughs (high expected returns after max pessimism). The 4-quadrant regime matrix shows: equities excel in disinflationary stable environments; bonds in disinflationary stagnation; commodities in inflationary booms. Equities paradoxically worst in inflationary booms — monetary tightening kills markets.

## The Big Picture: Secular Trends

The 1988-2007 era was exceptional: global inflation fell from 13% → 3.8%; financial sector leverage exploded from 9% → 115% of GDP; stock-bond correlation flipped from +0.4 → -0.1 (dramatically improving diversification). Five secular trends reversed after 2008: free market capitalism, disinflation, rising leverage, financialization, asset richening. Five continue: technology, rise of emerging economies, aging populations, globalization, environmental concerns.

Ilmanen's 2010 outlook: moderate returns from risky assets; China/EM secular handoff; medium-term inflation risk after near-term deflation; sovereign creditworthiness reassessment; "the riskless return in Treasuries has morphed into a returnless risk."

## The Carry Interpretation Chapter (Core Insight)

The book's most important theoretical finding: **carry measures better predict near-term asset returns than they predict future rate/price changes that would offset them.** This is empirically true in ALL asset classes — currencies, bonds, credit, equities, commodities, real estate, and volatility. The pure expectations hypothesis (UIP for FX, PEH for bonds) is soundly rejected. High-yield currencies APPRECIATE rather than depreciate; upward-sloping yield curves are followed by yield DECLINES (enriching bond holders). This means yield-seeking is genuinely rewarded, not just a carry-to-be-offset-by-capital-losses game.
