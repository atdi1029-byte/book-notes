### Chapter 1: Peter Brandt — Strong Opinions, Weakly Held

**Background:**
- Many Market Wizards failed initially — Brandt fits this pattern. Unusual: after a decade of spectacular success, he lost his edge, quit cold turkey for 11 years, then resumed for a second lengthy span of outstanding performance.
- Classical chart analysis based on Richard Schabacker's "Technical Analysis and Stock Market Profits" (1932), popularized by Edwards & Magee's "Technical Analysis of Stock Trends" (1948).
- Started as commodities broker in early 1970s during spiking inflation. Trading pits era — "bedlam of a ring of screaming brokers."
- 27-year career: initial 14-year span + current 13-year span, separated by 11-year hiatus.
- Average annual compounded return: 58%. But he notes this is inflated by very aggressive trading (53% annualized volatility).
- Sharpe ratio (1.11) understates his quality — Sharpe penalizes upside volatility equally with downside. His adjusted Sortino ratio (3.00) is nearly triple his Sharpe because his large gains far exceed his largest losses.
- Monthly Gain to Pain ratio: 2.81 — very high given career length.
- Not truly "unknown" — has Factor market letter and growing Twitter following. Several other traders in the book cited Brandt as an important influence. Schwager wanted him for the book regardless.

**Personal story:**
- Brandt was catalyst for Schwager writing this book. They are friends. Schwager drove to interview him in Tucson.
- 35 years earlier, Brandt had a terrible sleepwalking accident — fell nearly 20 feet from upper deck railing he mistook for a chair. Spent 40+ days in hospital in body cast. Half-dozen back surgeries since. Lives with constant pain, never complains, refuses pain medication.

**Interview — Early Life:**
- Grew up dirt poor, raised by single mom. Extremely entrepreneurial from age ~10: paper routes, collecting bottles, delivering shopper bills.
- Majored in advertising, loved it.
- Two things led to trading:
  1. Brother buying bags of silver coins at 20% premium in late 1960s-70s (asymmetric risk: max loss 20%, unlimited upside). By 1974 silver tripled from $1.50 to $4.50. Brother driving Mercedes-Benz.
  2. Met a soybean pit trader through sons' hockey. Visited Board of Trade, watched traders from restaurant above the pits. "Whoa! I want to do this."
- Was on fast track at 5th largest ad agency (accounts: Campbell, McDonald's). Created the McDonald's grab-a-bucket-and-mop commercial. Was present when Ronald McDonald was created.
- Liked advertising but hated corporate politics. Trading attracted him: knew how they did at end of each day, worked 9:30-1:15, making great money.

**Becoming a broker & early trading failures:**
- Joined Continental Grain (Conti Commodities) — first training program for commodity brokers, 8 people, 3 months. Took a pay cut from $28K/year to ~$1,300/month draw.
- Got clients by leveraging advertising connections — visited Campbell and McDonald's in person. Major food processors had been badly hurt by 1973-74 commodity bull market (unhedged). Brandt offered to train their purchasing agents in futures hedging.
- Started trading his own account ~1976. First trade: bought soybeans on his friend John's "bullish" tip. Market went up 5-6 cents then crashed. Lost $600. Key lesson: "bullish" and "bearish" don't mean anything without knowing the person's time horizon, what move they're looking for, and what would tell them they were wrong.
- Blew out 3-4 accounts over the next 3 years trading oats (tiny, low-volatility markets — the joke: "you knew you were in trouble when you were trading oats").
- Tried fundamentals (following Conti's morning speaker system), point-and-figure charting, seasonal patterns, spread trading — nothing worked.

**The breakthrough — Edwards & Magee:**
- A colleague (chartist) walked him to a bookstore and bought him Edwards & Magee's "Technical Analysis of Stock Trends." That book changed everything. "It put a lot of square pegs in square holes and round pegs in round holes." Gave him a framework for entries, stops, and targets.
- 1979: first account that gained traction. Still had too much volatility (hadn't worked out position sizing) but was moving steadily north.
- The broker who bought him the book was never a successful trader himself — ironic.

**Mentor — Dan Markey:**
- Sold his commercial hedging accounts to Dan Markey, his biggest mentor and "probably one of the best traders I ever met."
- Dan's philosophy: "I look at the markets through the lens of History 101, Economics 101, and Psychology 101." He was a position trader in grains, held for months. Intuitive — couldn't explain why he was right, but he was.
- Dan was buying into weakness, selling into strength — exact opposite of Brandt. But the lesson was risk management: Dan would probe the market for lows, get out of any trade at a loss by end of week, try again. He kept probing.
- Dan's quote: "There are two parts to a trade: direction and timing. If you're wrong about either one, you're wrong on the trade."
- Lesson from Dan: protect your capital and you'll always have another shot. "You had to protect your pile of chips."
- Another mentor's key advice: "Peter, you have to have an edge to make money, and a chart pattern does not give you an edge." Didn't make sense to Brandt at the time, but did 5 years later — chart patterns are visible to everyone and frequently fail.

**Factor Research & Trading:**
- 1980: incorporated Factor Research and Trading. Named "Factor" as an inside joke — he was translating his chart analysis into fundamental-sounding narratives for Campbell clients. Other traders called it "Brandt bullshit factor."
- Methodology evolution over time:
  - Eliminated "popcorn trades" — trades that pop up to a profit then fall back to where you entered
  - Charts became less reliable due to HFT creating volatility around breakout points
  - Shifted from 1-4 week patterns to 8-26 week patterns (more reliable)
  - Stopped trading symmetrical triangles and trendlines — only trades horizontal boundary breakouts now (rectangles, ascending/descending triangles)
  - Used to trade 30-35 patterns/month, now much more selective
  - Will take a second chance on a breakout (not same day) but never a third
  - "I trade price change, I don't trade price level" — doesn't mind buying at $1.50 after being stopped out twice at $1.20
  - Risk per trade now ~0.5% of equity (was up to 10% early in career — twentyfold reduction)
  - Wants stops at breakeven within 2-3 days of entry. Average loss: 23 basis points
  - Overnight stops: depends on market liquidity (yes for euro, no for Mexican peso; yes for gold, no for copper)

**First bad year & the decline:**
- 1988: first losing year after 9 good years. Only lost ~5%. Got sloppy — entered patterns too early, chased markets. Cause: complacency after being up 600% in 1987 (best year ever).
- 1989: got back on track by returning to basics.

**Key psychological insights:**
- "The big one is to forgive myself. I am going to make mistakes."
- "I now know that I am my own worst enemy and that my natural instincts will often lead me astray."
- "I am a glorified order enterer; I am not a trader." Edge comes from the process of disciplined order entry, not from prediction.
- "Most of my big profits have come from trades that were counterintuitive." Possible inverse correlation between how he feels about a trade and how it turns out. Example: wanted to be bullish grains for a year, but his best grain trade was shorting Kansas City wheat — went against his instincts because the chart demanded it.
- "I take pride in the fact that I can be wrong 10 times in a row. I understand that my edge comes from the fact that I have become so good at taking losses."
- "A losing trade doesn't imply you did anything wrong." You can do the right thing and still lose money — no direct feedback loop.
- "A year from now when I look back at the chart, will I be able to see in the chart the day and the price at which I took a position? If the answer is yes, then it is a good trade, regardless of whether it wins or loses."

**"Cosmic sovereignty":**
- Brandt attributes his early success largely to luck — he calls it "cosmic sovereignty." Started trading at the perfect time for chart trading. Markets in 1970s-80s had clean patterns. Had the right mentors, right firm, right accounts from advertising career. Bet 10-15% of account on Swiss franc and Deutsche mark positions — could have blown up but turned into a giant profit.
- "I can't take credit for any of that. None of that is due to my own intelligence or abilities."

**Commodities Corporation:**
- Recruited by Commodities Corporation (legendary prop firm in Princeton — seeded Michael Marcus, Bruce Kovner, etc.). Brandt flew to Newark, picked up by limousine, driven to "the castle."
- Started with $100K, eventually raised to $1M then $5M+.
- Major problem: never comfortable with size. First 100-contract bond order made him shake. "I was freaked out. I don't think I ever got over that."
- 3 years before he was comfortable with 100-lot positions.
- Larger size made him timid — started trading his equity rather than the market. "I started thinking in terms of dollars." Performance deteriorated from ~1991.
- By 1992: trading trailing off, not meeting benchmarks. Not taking losses per se, but performing near breakeven and barely using his allocation.
- Mutual decision to end the relationship.
- Continued trading own account for 2 more years but had become gun-shy. "The fun of trading had left me."
- 1995: closed his account entirely. Mixed feelings — relief and "I'm admitting failure."

**11-year hiatus (1995-2006):**
- Didn't have a futures account. Didn't have charting software. Didn't look at a single chart for 11 years.
- 2006: sitting at his desk, wife Mona standing beside him, thought of trading fluttered back. She hated the idea ("Really, you want to do that again?"). He said "I have to do it."
- Motivation: missed trading, and couldn't let failure be the last word.
- Came back to find electronic trading had replaced the pits. Got out his old time stamp machine — laughed at how archaic it was. Had a few good trades early on, charts still seemed to work.

**Managing other people's money (2013-2014):**
- 2013: worst year since resuming trading (-13% vs avg +49% in other years). Two reinforcing factors:
  1. Accepted investor money for the first time — "Once I found myself trading money for friends, all of a sudden, it was real money. It messed with my head."
  2. Drank the Kool-Aid that markets had permanently changed, tried adding indicators and mean reversion trades (exact opposite of his approach). Vicious cycle — kept trying different things out of desperation, turning a 3-4 month/5% drawdown into 18-month/17% drawdown.
- Own account traded more aggressively than investor accounts, so his own loss was larger (investors' worst loss ~10%).
- June 2014: returned all investor money. That month was the exact bottom of his drawdown. Believes he would have kept digging the hole deeper if he hadn't returned the money.
- Trading peers gave him the honest advice he needed: managing other people's money was messing up his trading.
- Key realization: "I had bought into the lie that I had to change because the old ways no longer worked. I finally realized I had to get back to basics."

**Equity tracking innovation:**
- After 2013-14, stopped looking at total account equity (which includes open trade profits). Now graphs equity based on closed trades only.
- Rationale: "Open trade profits aren't mine. They don't belong to me. So it doesn't matter if I lose it." This makes it easier to give winning trades room to run.
- Position management: once profits equal 1% of equity, takes half the position off. Then gives the other half much more latitude.

**Trade management details:**
- When remaining half gets within 30% of target, uses mechanical 3-day stop rule: Day 1 = high day of move. Day 2 = market closes below low of Day 1. Day 3 = market closes below low of Day 2 → exit. Days need not be consecutive.
- Developed this rule not because it's optimal, but because as a discretionary trader he hates indecision and regret — wanted an automatic rule.
- Initial risk ~0.5% equity. Target ~2% equity (4:1 reward/risk).
- Usually takes full profits at target. Exception: short positions may use very tight stop instead, because markets can collapse faster than they rise.
- Doesn't re-enter after taking profits — must be a brand-new trade setup.
- "I feel more comfortable playing between the 30-yard lines than trying to run a kickoff from the 10-yard line to the end zone."
- Trades stocks and futures identically using same chart methodology.

**"Ice line" concept:**
- From living on a Minnesota lake — ice freezes over, supports you, but if you fall through, the ice acts as resistance (can't get back up easily). In charts: a price breakout area where at least half the day's range is below the breakout level → the low of that day becomes a significant risk point (stop level). If less than half is below, use the prior day's low.

**Research on stop tightness:**
- Did research: giving market a bit more breathing room early on would increase overall returns. But his goal is to maximize profit factor (return/risk ratio), not raw return. A method with higher return-to-risk can always be scaled up via position size to beat a higher-return but lower-ratio method at the same risk level.

**Never uses trailing stops or pyramiding:**
- Trailing stops: "What sense does that make! You mean you want to be selling when you should be buying more?"
- Using open profits to add contracts: "the most asinine trading idea I ever heard. If you do that, you can be right on the trade and still lose money."
- His positions only get smaller, never bigger. Biggest position is always day one.

**Weekend/Friday rule (from Richard Donchian, 1970s):**
- If market closes at new high or low on Friday, move likely extends through Monday/early Tuesday.
- Brandt likes Friday breakouts because they align with completed chart pattern + Donchian rule.
- Conversely: liquidate any trade showing a net loss at Friday close. Friday close is the most critical price of the week — it's where people commit to holding over the weekend.
- He sometimes violates this rule but "the market usually slaps me in the face" when he does.

**Worst trade:**
- Long crude oil January 1991 when US attacked Iraq in First Gulf War. Crude closed ~$29 in NY, traded $2-3 higher on the Kerb (London after-hours) overnight. Next day opened $7 below NY close — a $10 swing. Lost ~14% of equity. Largest loss ever.
- Didn't wait for a rebound: "I don't sit and speculate with a loss. If you speculate with a loss to get less of a loss, you end up with more of a loss."

**Factor market letter:**
- Writes it for himself — "an audience of one." It's his way of preaching to himself the things he needs to be continually reminded of.
- Does all chart analysis and trading decisions between Friday close and Sunday evening opening. Rarely makes new trading decisions during the week.
- Doesn't trade intraday: "If I sit and watch the computer screen all day, I will sabotage myself." Ed Seykota quote: "Having a quote machine is like having a slot machine on your desk — you end up feeding it all day long."

**"Strong opinions, weakly held" philosophy:**
- The minute a trade reaches his pocket, it becomes a weakly held position. Will drop it like a hot potato.
- Can go from strong opinion to out of the market in one day.
- Twitter followers criticize him for changing views, but flexibility is a strength, not weakness.

**What still works in chart analysis:**
- Classical chart analysis (as Schabacker described) no longer works on its own. Large long-term patterns, trendlines, channels, symmetrical triangles — all broken.
- What works: shorter-term patterns (<1 year, preferably <26 weeks) with horizontal boundaries — H&S, ascending/descending triangles, rectangular consolidations.
- The patterns themselves don't provide edge — they merely identify points where risk/reward is asymmetric with close, meaningful stops.
- "Charts do not forecast. Charts will give you the idea of the path of least resistance."
- "All that charts do is give me a point at which I'm willing to take a bet."

**Dan Markey's "dominant fundamental factor" theory:**
- Over 1-5 years, one underlying fundamental factor drives a market. All other news is noise causing gyrations around the dominant trend. Most people don't know what the dominant factor is — and if they do, they're usually trying to fade it.
- Example: QE after the Great Recession was the dominant factor driving the stock bull market, but people kept saying "too much debt, have to short this market."

**Advice for aspiring traders:**
- "If you lost everything you put in, would it meaningfully change your lifestyle? If it would, don't trade."
- Don't trade if you're not a good problem solver.
- Don't trade if you need regular income — markets are not an annuity.
- Expect 3 years to even get a clue, 5 years to reach competency.
- Probability of earning a living as a trader: ~1%.
- "Learn not to take a loss personally. The markets don't care about who you are."
- Find your own way — copying someone else's trading style will never work because you won't have the conviction to survive drawdowns.
- "What is the pitch that I'm willing to swing on?" Define with high specificity what trade you'll take before worrying about sizing, leverage, scaling.
- When out of sync: cut size, don't change methodology. "Optimizing your trading approach for the last series of trades is not a solution."
- Don't confuse wanting to be rich with wanting to trade. "Unless you love the endeavor, you are unlikely to succeed."

**Winners vs losers:**
- Winners: respect risk, limit risk per trade, assume they'll be wrong, don't get too excited about wins or too bummed about losses.
- Losers: risk too much, no methodology, chase markets, FOMO, can't control emotions — wild swings between excitement and depression.

**Schwager's commentary:**
- Entry methodology (chart analysis) is actually the least important element for Brandt. Risk management is everything. Charts are merely a tool to identify points amenable to his risk management approach.
- Brandt's strategy: risk very little on each trade, restrict to setups with 3-4x reward/risk potential. Uses charts to find close, meaningful stops.
- Good trade ≠ winning trade. Brandt judges trades by whether they followed his methodology, not by P&L.
- Trading size matters psychologically even when markets are liquid. Brandt deteriorated at Commodities Corporation when size increased.
- Own account success ≠ managing money success. Guilt over losing others' money impairs decision-making.
- Biggest losses often follow biggest profits (complacency → sloppiness). Marty Schwartz: "My biggest losses have always followed my biggest profits."
- All profits come from ~10-15% of trades; the other 85-90% are throwaways. The goal is to make the 85% as close to net zero as possible.
- Patience is essential but not necessarily innate — Brandt is naturally impulsive but enforces patience through discipline.
- Keep trade logs categorized by type to know what actually works.

**Three Years Later (Updated Edition):**
- Became even more extreme on risk management since the interview.
- Reduced max risk per trade to 50-60 basis points (from ~100 bp).
- Bleeds off risk as quickly as possible — moves stops to breakeven often on the same day of entry. Goal: breakeven trade on half the position within a few days.
- Analyzed his trades (Pareto principle): almost all profits came from 15% of trades. Those 15% worked immediately — 14 of 17 best trades last year were profitable from day one. So tighter stops on the other 85% barely hurts the winners.
- "If a trade is going to work, I will know right away."
- Experimenting with adding a third tranche on trades triggered by weekly chart patterns (in addition to daily). Gives more leeway to the weekly-triggered tranche. Planning to switch entirely to weekly charts by 2025 (50th anniversary of first visiting CBOT) — promised wife and kids he'd be done with daily trading by then. Would accept 10-15%/year.
- "Chair-and-ottoman" pattern — Brandt's own invention. First saw it in sugar chart. Not in Edwards & Magee. Recent example: gold bottom Sep-Nov 2022 with bearish COT sentiment confirming.
- Favorite market if he could only trade one: Eurodollars (if not at zero rates). Also loves soybean oil and Japanese yen for their tendency to trend.
- Recent mistake: bought 5-year T-notes for good reason, then Fed spooked the market. Jammed his stop for emotional reasons rather than any rule. "It is much harder dealing with a winning trade than a losing trade." With a loss, the recipe is simple — don't want to be in it. With a winner, you can get spooked out for no good reason.
- Judges trades by whether he reacted emotionally vs followed his process, regardless of outcome. "I have more emotional and intellectual satisfaction over a trade that had a loss for which I did things right than over a trade that I accidentally made money out of by being stupid."

### Chapter 2: Jason Shapiro — The Contrarian

**Background:**
- Achieved consistent success by learning to do the exact opposite of what he did instinctively in his first decade. 30+ year career spanning own account, various asset management firms, and multiple CTA iterations.
- Managed assets from a few million to $600M. Now runs a one-man CTA, content not to expand.
- Track record from 2001. At 20% target volatility: 34% avg annual compounded return, 16.1% max drawdown. Adjusted Sortino ratio 2.83, monthly Gain to Pain ratio 2.45.
- Returns negatively correlated to stock, hedge fund, and CTA indexes — truly contrarian.
- Since resuming trading in 2016 after a hiatus, all performance stats have improved vs full career.

**How Schwager found him:**
- Email recommendation: "You absolutely must talk to Jason Shapiro." Shapiro initially declined ("I really don't want to be in a book"), then reconsidered, then declined again, then one minute later: "Ah, screw it, let's do it."

**Early life — the rebel:**
- "Terror show" as a teenager. Kicked out of three high schools for not doing work and insubordination. GPA: 1.7.
- Father backed out of parenting by age 15-16. Mother was a child psychologist — her approach: "let him be, eventually he'll grow up and find himself."
- Got into University of South Florida despite grades (test scores qualified him automatically). Placed on academic probation when they saw his transcript.
- Majored in finance. Turning point sophomore year: arrested for "drunken stupidity," spent night in Tampa holding pen. Thought "this is not what I want to be doing with my life." Got straight A's from that point on.
- Near-perfect GMAT score (missed one question). Applied to Harvard, Chicago, Wharton — all rejected for lack of work experience.

**Path to trading:**
- Graduated 1988 during Japan boom. Went to Japan, taught English for a year. Applied to HSBC in Hong Kong for their 5-year executive development program. Got hired.
- Wanted Treasury Department (trading) but program was for generalist bankers. Lasted about a year before being fired — "complete insubordination." Same reason as high school.
- While at HSBC, began trading Hang Seng futures through a broker on his American softball team. First book on trading: picked up Market Wizards in a Hong Kong bookstore. "I woke up the next morning and thought, I know what I want to do with my life now. This makes perfect sense to me."
- The Commodities Corporation traders (Jones, Kovner, Marcus) resonated most — super contrarians who didn't care what others thought.

**First trading career — making and losing fortunes:**
- Bought Hang Seng at ~4,000, it went to 7,000 within 6 months. "A great time to be a young, ignorant, and stupid trader." Like buying NASDAQ in 1999 — couldn't lose as long as you didn't go short.
- After HSBC firing: stayed in Hong Kong, worked at brokerage firms, traded all the time. Jacqui Chan (Chinese-Malaysian woman, former Morgan Stanley trader) funded his futures account with $100K. Ran it to $700K. Bought a Porsche. Planning how to live like a billionaire.
- Then the bull market ended. Blew out the entire account within 6 months. All he had left was the Porsche.
- Switched brokerage firms, rebuilt account. Made a lot of money on the Barings Bank/Nick Leeson collapse — Nikkei futures traded at 15-20% discount to cash when Singapore announced liquidation. Went long futures, short cash. Made ~20% in one day.
- Attended London Business School Masters in Finance. Spent 10% of time on school, 90% trading Hang Seng and S&P futures. Had broker fax charts daily (internet barely existed).
- Key experience: large Hang Seng position before month-long Africa vacation. Left stop orders with broker. Called from Africa 3 weeks later — Hang Seng was 15% higher. Made $300K while doing nothing. Confirmed Jesse Livermore's lesson: "You make your money in the sitting."

**LTCM and the European convergence trade:**
- Met head of global fixed income at Salomon Brothers through a business school professor's wife. Told him "I try to find out what everyone is doing and do the opposite." Salomon guy smiled at Shapiro's naïveté: "Sometimes everyone can make money" — pointed to European interest rate convergence trade (buy Italian bonds, sell German bonds, profit when euro forces rates to converge).
- But everyone (LTCM, Salomon, etc.) had the same trade in maximum size. When Russia defaulted August 1998, they had to sell their European convergence positions to cover Russian bond losses. No buyers. Trade blew up LTCM and Salomon.
- The trade was ultimately right (rates did converge), but the crowding destroyed the participants. Key lesson for Shapiro about what happens when everyone is on the same side.

**Second blowout:**
- Finished London Business School with $500K+ in his account. Moved to Thailand (Phuket, beach house, satellite dish). Blew up everything in 8 months.
- The mistake: US stock market going straight up, so he wanted to be the hero and short it. Kept shorting, kept getting stopped out. After Greenspan's "irrational exuberance" speech, market sold off, and he thought "Greenspan and I are the only ones who get it." But the S&P rallied back and closed higher that same day. He was "fucking dead."
- Fell into FOMO trap: after being short all that time, couldn't bring himself to go long when the short move finally happened.

**The turning point — writing it all down:**
- After losing everything, sat down and wrote pages of what worked and what didn't.
- Core realization: "Don't have an opinion. Should've been up, but it's down, so short it; should've been down, but it's up, so long it." When the tape tells you something, don't fight it — go with it.
- Also discovered the Commitment of Traders (COT) report as a tool. While running a CTA and trying to short the 1999 bubble, noticed the COT data never gave a sell signal until January 2000. "That is powerful data."

**COT Report — Shapiro's primary tool:**
- Weekly report from CFTC showing positions of speculators vs commercials. Commercials tend to be right (better informed); speculators tend to be wrong.
- Shapiro uses relative position levels (not absolute) — in markets like gold where commercials are always short, he looks at whether the short position is relatively small vs historical range.
- Doesn't trade on COT alone — needs price action confirmation + tight stops. COT sets up the trade, price confirms it.

**Career rollercoaster:**
- Moved to Hawaii, partnered in a new CTA. Never got anywhere with it — during 1998-99 NASDAQ bubble, telling investors "we made 12%" was unimpressive ("Yeah, I made 12% this week").
- 2000: moved to Chicago, joined Gelber Group as prop trader. Used COT data, did the opposite of what the other (incompetent) traders were doing. Made money while they all lost. Got fired at year-end anyway — "those guys hated me. I was essentially fading them."
- A few years later, the manager (Leno) was busted by FBI for insider trading.

**Helmut Weymar connection:**
- After Gelber, had no job, wife wanted divorce. Met a divorce lawyer whose wife was best friends with Helmut Weymar's wife (Weymar co-founded Commodities Corporation).
- Told the lawyer: "If he wants to be my divorce lawyer, he has to get his wife to introduce me to Helmut."
- Two days later, sitting in Weymar's office with the fireplace. Weymar gave him $2M, then Commodities Corporation (now Goldman-owned) gave another $2M. Started CTA with $4M, made 22% first year.
- Expected more money from Goldman/CommCorp — never came (political — Goldman people didn't want to take orders from departing Weymar).

**Cranmore Capital (David Reed):**
- Joined Cranmore Capital with $5M to manage. Reed's firm was entirely trend-following.
