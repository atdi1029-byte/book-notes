Peter Brandt: Strong Opinions, Weakly Held

Peter Brandt is the one trader in this book for whom the adjective “unknown” barely applies. Through his Factor market letter and a rapidly growing Twitter following, he has gained recognition from a segment of the trading community. His career spans twenty-seven years of active trading, split into an initial fourteen-year span and a current thirteen-year span, separated by an eleven-year hiatus during which he never once looked at a chart. His average annual compounded return of fifty-eight percent comes with remarkably controlled downside — his adjusted Sortino ratio of 3.00 is nearly triple his Sharpe ratio because his largest gains far exceed his largest losses.

Brandt grew up dirt poor, raised by a single mother, and started working at age ten delivering newspapers at 5 a.m. on Sundays and collecting bottles. Two things introduced him to trading. His brother bought bags of silver coins at a twenty percent premium in the late 1960s — a perfect asymmetric risk trade where the maximum loss was twenty percent but the upside was unlimited. By 1974 silver had tripled from $1.50 to $4.50, and his brother was driving a Mercedes-Benz. The second catalyst came through a hockey friendship — his sons played with the son of a soybean pit trader who invited Brandt to lunch at the Chicago Board of Trade. Watching the traders from the restaurant above the pits, Brandt thought: “Whoa! I want to do this.”

“I am a glorified order enterer; I am not a trader. My edge comes from the process.” — Peter Brandt

He was on the fast track at the fifth-largest advertising agency in the world — his accounts included Campbell and McDonald’s, and he was present when Ronald McDonald was created. But corporate politics grated on him, and trading offered something advertising never could: a clear daily scorecard and a 9:30-to-1:15 workday. He joined Continental Grain’s first commodity broker training program, taking a pay cut from $28,000 a year to roughly $1,300 a month. His first trade was a soybean contract bought on a friend’s bullish tip — it went up five cents, then crashed. He lost $600 and learned that “bullish” means nothing without knowing the person’s time horizon.

What followed was a grinding series of failures. Brandt blew out three or four accounts over the next three years, cycling through fundamentals, point-and-figure charting, seasonal patterns, and spread trading. The breakthrough came when a colleague walked him to a bookstore and bought him Edwards and Magee’s Technical Analysis of Stock Trends. That book gave him a framework — where to enter, where to place stops, and where the market might go. By 1979 his account was finally moving north. His mentor Dan Markey taught him that there are two parts to a trade — direction and timing — and if you’re wrong about either one, you’re wrong on the trade. Dan’s most important lesson was risk management: protect your pile of chips and you will always have another shot.

Brandt’s methodology evolved substantially over the decades. He eliminated what he calls “popcorn trades” — positions that pop up to a profit and then fall all the way back down. He shifted from one-to-four-week patterns to eight-to-twenty-six-week patterns because the longer formations proved more reliable. He stopped trading symmetrical triangles and trendlines entirely, focusing only on patterns with horizontal boundaries — rectangles, ascending triangles, descending triangles. His risk per trade shrank from as much as ten percent of equity in the early days to roughly half a percent now, a twentyfold reduction. He wants his stops at breakeven within two or three days of entry.

The career nearly ended in the early 1990s. Trading for Commodities Corporation in Princeton — the legendary firm that seeded Michael Marcus and Bruce Kovner — Brandt could never get comfortable with size. His first hundred-contract bond order made him shake. Larger positions made him timid, and he started thinking in dollar terms instead of market terms. By 1992 he was barely using his allocation. He quit entirely in 1995 and didn’t look at a chart for eleven years. In 2006, sitting at his desk with his wife Mona standing beside him, the thought of trading fluttered back into his mind. She hated the idea. He opened an account anyway.

“Most of my big profits have come from trades that were counterintuitive. I take pride in the fact that I can be wrong ten times in a row. My edge comes from the fact that I have become so good at taking losses.” — Peter Brandt

His worst year since returning came in 2013, when he accepted investor money for the first time and simultaneously drank the Kool-Aid that markets had permanently changed. He added indicators, tried mean reversion — the exact opposite of his approach. A standard three-month, five-percent drawdown turned into an eighteen-month, seventeen-percent drawdown. The moment he returned all investor money in June 2014, his drawdown bottomed. The lesson was brutal but clear: stick to what works, and don’t let managing other people’s money contaminate your process.

In the updated interview, Brandt reveals he has become even more extreme on risk management — reducing max risk per trade to fifty to sixty basis points and bleeding off risk as quickly as possible, often moving stops to breakeven on the same day. He discovered through analysis that almost all his profits came from fifteen percent of his trades, and those trades worked immediately — fourteen of seventeen top winners in his most recent year were profitable from day one. He has also started experimenting with a third tranche triggered by weekly chart patterns, preparing for a possible transition to weekly-only trading by 2025.

Jason Shapiro: The Contrarian

Jason Shapiro achieved consistent success by learning to do the exact opposite of what he did instinctively in his first decade of trading. His returns are negatively correlated to stock, hedge fund, and CTA indexes — a truly contrarian performance stream. At a twenty-percent target volatility, his average annual compounded return is thirty-four percent with a maximum drawdown of just 16.1%. His path to the interview was characteristically contrarian — he initially declined, then reconsidered, then declined again, and one minute later emailed: “Ah, screw it, let’s do it.”

Shapiro was kicked out of three high schools for insubordination. His mother, a child psychologist, had the wisdom to let him find himself. He graduated with a 1.7 GPA, got a near-perfect GMAT score, was rejected by Harvard, Chicago, and Wharton, taught English in Japan, got hired by HSBC in Hong Kong, and was fired within a year for — complete insubordination. While at HSBC, he began trading Hang Seng futures after meeting a broker on his American softball team. The first trading book he ever read was Schwager’s own Market Wizards, which he finished in one sitting and woke up knowing what he wanted to do with his life.

The next several years were a rollercoaster of making and losing fortunes. Jacqui Chan funded his account with $100,000 and he ran it to $700,000 — then the bull market ended and he blew out everything. He rebuilt, profited from the Barings Bank collapse by going long Nikkei futures at a fifteen-to-twenty percent discount to cash, attended the London Business School where he spent ninety percent of his time trading, and made $300,000 while on vacation in Africa by leaving a Hang Seng position to run without watching it. That experience confirmed Jesse Livermore’s lesson: you make your money in the sitting.

“Should’ve been up, but it’s down, so short it; should’ve been down, but it’s up, so long it.” — Jason Shapiro

After blowing out a second account in Thailand by stubbornly shorting the rising U.S. stock market, Shapiro sat down and wrote pages of what worked and what didn’t. He discovered the Commitment of Traders report as his primary tool, using it to identify extremes in speculator positioning and then trading against them. The COT didn’t give a sell signal on the stock market until January 2000, confirming its power. His methodology requires both positioning data AND confirming price action — COT sets up the direction, the market’s response to news confirms the timing.

Shapiro spent a month in a Buddhist monastery on Inle Lake in Burma, shaving his head and going out with monks every morning with alms bowls. Villagers with almost nothing would fill their bowls with rice and fish. The experience permanently changed his values. He drives a 1998 Toyota 4Runner and has no interest in material displays of wealth. His definition of happiness: believing that the future will be better than the past.

His key insight is about participation as the discounting mechanism. Everyone understands that the market is a discounting mechanism, but most people think the discount happens through price. Shapiro argues it happens through participation. When Amazon was at $700-800 and everyone called it a bubble, the people calling it a bubble didn’t own it — meaning the bullish fundamentals were not discounted at all. He even applies this to football betting: when twenty-five of his thirty guys pick the same team, the other team wins eighty percent of the time.

In the updated interview, Shapiro caught the 2022 bear market perfectly — speculators got super long in November 2021. He specifically remembers a CNBC perma-bear who had been bearish from the March 2020 bottom finally turning bullish, declaring he wanted to get long the Russell breakout. That was the exact top. The market reversed the very next day. He now runs a weekly market letter and YouTube videos, donating his half of the subscription revenue to charity.

Richard Bargh: The Importance of Mindset

Richard Bargh grew up on a Yorkshire dairy farm and at age four told his father he wanted a real job. At six he visited the one wealthy family in the village and told his mother he would buy that house one day. He was inspired to become a trader by the movie Rogue Trader about Nick Leeson. After earning a Masters in Applied Mathematics from Imperial College London, he failed to get a job at a pension company because he showed no interest in the work, and decided that if he was going to fail, he might as well fail at something he wanted to do.

He almost blew his trading firm interview by unconsciously putting his tie in his mouth while a partner was speaking. The firm hired him anyway on a second chance, attracted by his hunger more than his experience. His first year of live trading was agonizing — he made money on a simulator using an “open drive” pattern but never made a penny from intraday technicals once trading real money. He spent four years stubbornly trying to make technicals work when all his actual profits came from fundamental events.

Bargh had dealt with depression since secondary school and recognized early that trading would destroy him unless he became happy. He read Depressive Illness: The Curse of the Strong by Tim Cantopher, which described depressed people as quiet, ambitious, and hard-working — his personality exactly. He used brute-force self-talk on the bus home: “You have got to be happy!” He credits Ed Seykota’s Trading Tribe Process as an essential influence and runs the London TTP tribe.

“Mental capital is the most critical aspect of trading. What matters most is how you respond when you make a mistake, miss a trade, or take a significant loss.” — Richard Bargh

By mid-2013, Bargh was nearly finished — almost out of savings, parents threatening to cut support, only one month’s expenses remaining. His bosses consulted Amrit Sall, who told them Bargh was a hard worker who would make it. They gave him a salary. Then in November 2013, Mario Draghi announced a surprise ECB rate cut nobody expected until December. Bargh bought several hundred European short-term interest rate futures and made nearly £90,000 in one trade. His parents were visiting that night. He took them to dinner and told them what had happened. They couldn’t believe it.

His biggest innovation is in trade implementation. During the Brexit vote, instead of shorting the wildly volatile British pound directly, he bought U.S. Treasurys — a risk-off trade with much tighter stops and far less chance of a whipsaw knockout. The T-bonds rallied sixty to one hundred ticks and he made a hundred percent return on his account in one day. The way a trade idea is implemented can be more important than the trade idea itself.

He keeps a daily spreadsheet tracking focus, energy, risk management, ego, FOMO, and happiness rating, reviewing it weekly for patterns. When his mindset is off, he leaves work early or takes the next morning off. He trades from a state of calm and inner peace, and refuses to let a bad day or missed trade spiral into compounding mistakes. In the updated interview, his returns shifted from giant home runs to many moderate wins, with smaller position sizes and smaller drawdowns. His favorite technical setup: low volatility transitioning to high volatility, where tight stops in the quiet period allow large position sizes that benefit enormously from the subsequent expansion.

Amrit Sall: The Unicorn Sniper

Amrit Sall has one of the best track records Schwager has ever encountered. Over thirteen years, he achieved an average annual compounded return of 337% — not cumulative, annual. He had thirty-four days with returns greater than fifteen percent, three with returns exceeding one hundred percent, and only one double-digit loss day, which was caused by his computer powering down at a critical moment. His adjusted Sortino ratio of 17.6 is twelve times his Sharpe ratio — an extreme positive skew indicating that his large gains are tremendously greater and more frequent than his large losses. A colleague describes his trade execution as “a thing of beauty.”

Sall stumbled into economics at university because his grades weren’t good enough for business information systems. In retrospect, that failure was exactly what needed to happen. At the University of Reading, he fell in love with a simulated trading room and talked his way into a prop firm’s training program. Of twenty trainees, only two survived. He was profitable immediately, but his first year was a disaster — he made £150,000 by midyear, got cocky, and blew the remaining £50,000 in the second half. The firm’s £30,000 stop-out point was approaching, and Sall had no backup plan.

“90% of the time, the market is not going to provide any opportunities, and 10% of the time, I will make 90% of my profits.” — Amrit Sall

His philosophy crystallized around what he calls “unicorn” trades — opportunities where everything lines up perfectly and he goes aggressive, even during drawdowns. He only gets about ten a year, and he cannot afford to miss a single one. The defining trade of his career came on March 18, 2009, when the Fed expanded quantitative easing to include longer-term Treasury purchases for the first time. Sall had prepared in advance, asking his risk manager to double his T-note position limit from 300 to 600 contracts. The request was denied because he was in a drawdown. He bought 300 T-notes the instant the announcement hit and was out within minutes, having made over one million dollars in two to three minutes of trading.

His worst day came in June 2013 when Mario Draghi began answering a question about negative interest rates with “I told you we are technically ready…” and Sall went short the euro before Draghi finished: “…but we won’t pre-commit.” At that exact moment his computer powered down. By the time he got to the risk manager’s desk, the euro had soared and he had taken a twenty-four percent hit. He went to the bar that evening and called it a “celebration.” That counterintuitive reaction — treating yourself after a loss — is his method for breaking negative psychological spirals.

Sall draws heavily on the concept of flow states. He read that U.S. Navy SEALs use neurofeedback and biofeedback to access flow on demand in combat, and that the hardest part of a SEAL’s job is knowing when not to shoot. Before significant events, he uses breathwork and meditation to enter what he calls a “deep now” state where he reacts from the subconscious level representing ninety-five percent of the mind’s potential. In this state, he processes large amounts of information without hesitation, cuts losses without emotion, and runs winners without the impulse to snatch profits.

Sall’s self-description captures his approach perfectly: “My style has often been referred to that of a sniper. I am in a constant state of readiness waiting for that perfect shot. I don’t want to waste my bullets doing anything else because it will jeopardize my ability to take that perfect shot.” In the updated interview, he reveals building a team, developing proprietary models, and holding positions for weeks and months — while still using events to get short-term leverage. He also found information arbitrage opportunities like scraping Florida COVID data from the state website two to three minutes before Bloomberg reported it.

Daljit Dhaliwal: Know Your Edge

Daljit Dhaliwal’s initial passion was tennis, not trading. He was ranked about eightieth in the UK and beat a world-ranked player six-four, but when his coach told him there was no money in professional tennis and he’d have to start a new career in his thirties, he walked away. A soccer ankle injury in university sealed the decision. During the 2008 financial meltdown, markets captured his attention, and he applied to thirty trading positions. All rejected him except one prop firm. In the interview he told them: “If you just give me this job, I will be the hardest worker you will ever see.”

Dhaliwal’s nine-plus-year track record produced an average annual compounded return of 298%. His volatility is stratospheric at eighty-four percent, but that’s because of huge gains, not huge losses — his maximum drawdown is under twenty percent. He has been profitable in all years, ninety-five percent of quarters, and seventy percent of months. His methodology evolved constantly: from technical analysis to headline trading during the eurozone debt crisis to fading initial headline reactions once algorithms captured the first-move profits to macroeconomic models developed with a full-time research assistant.

“It’s not about being right; it’s about making money.” — Daljit Dhaliwal

His most painful trade came in December 2015 when the Financial Times accidentally published a pre-written story saying the ECB had left rates unchanged five minutes before the actual announcement that they had cut rates. Dhaliwal trusted the FT, went full size long euro and short Euro Stoxx, and went from up six figures to down six figures in seconds — a twenty percent account hit. The FT later issued a retraction explaining it was an editing error on pre-written stories. After that trade, he never entered a position without a stop and always took partial profits on quick gains.

Two figures were transformative influences. He won a charity auction lunch with Ray Dalio for $40,000 at an Italian restaurant in Manhattan. Dalio told him to go as far back as possible to test views — people get blindsided by things that have happened before because they overweight recent history. Peter Brandt told him it took eight to ten years to know his edge, prompting Dhaliwal to examine his own “best-dressed trades.” He discovered every big winner shared three characteristics: an unexpected event, harmony between short-term and long-term views, and a tendency to work immediately.

His drawdown management is systematic: cut size in half at five percent, half again at eight percent, stop trading entirely at fifteen percent. He maintains a daily trading journal recording not just analysis but feelings, and watches for the keywords FOMO and frustration as warning signs. His advice: “The one question every trader needs to be able to answer is: what is your edge? If you don’t have a clear answer, you won’t know which trades to focus on.”

John Netto: Monday Is My Favorite Day

John Netto ran a high-school bookmaking operation, lost everything he’d made over several years in a single weekend on an outlier event, and learned about tail risk before he ever traded a market. With poor grades and a 1.8 GPA, he joined the Marines on impulse when a Navy recruiter asked him. Almost nine years of service taught him discipline, the ability to function under pressure, and accountability. He became fluent in Japanese, served at the U.S. embassy in Tokyo, and planned an officer career until chronic knee injuries forced a disability discharge. His disability check became his trading stake.

Netto’s methodology combines three elements: the market narrative (the dominant fundamental regime driving prices), technical analysis for entry timing, and event trading through proprietary software called MPACT. For each scheduled event, he defines twenty to forty possible scenarios with predefined trade actions. MPACT reads the text of a Fed statement in milliseconds, scores it across four areas, and executes the appropriate trade automatically. The software took six years and a substantial portion of his net worth to build.

“Monday is my favorite day of the week. When you love what you do, you’re going to be successful.” — John Netto

His most provocative insight: emotions are your friend, not your enemy. While most trading advice says to eliminate emotions, Netto argues that because emotional trading consistently leads to bad outcomes, your own emotional extremes serve as a powerful contrarian indicator. When he caught himself feeling “animalistic greed” after shorting 200 E-mini S&P contracts with zero respect for risk, he covered immediately. The best positions, he says, are when he still feels some unease — if he exhales in relief, that’s a warning sign of a reversal.

Jeffrey Neumann: Penny Wise, Dollar Wise

Jeffrey Neumann started with a $2,500 insurance check from a hailstorm and turned it into $50 million in profits over seventeen years. His approach relies on being early — early in emerging sector themes, early in trendline breakouts, early in trying new products. He exploited a decimal pricing glitch to become a quasi-market-maker in penny stocks, buying at 7.01 cents and selling at 7.99 cents for thirteen percent per trade. He reached one million dollars before he understood trading at all.

The pivotal moment came when a chat room researcher named “songw” found a local newspaper story about a bill to increase ethanol content in gasoline from one percent to five percent. Most people focused on gasoline going from ninety-nine to ninety-five percent; songw reframed it as a four-hundred percent increase in ethanol demand. Three ethanol stocks went up over one thousand percent in ten trading days. Neumann sold the day the bill went to Congress. “Catalysts are over the second the public believes it is the beginning.”

His “four corners” methodology requires a right chart pattern, a clean share structure, the right sector, and a catalyst. He pyramids aggressively into positions, sometimes putting a third of his account into a single stock, but exits immediately if the stock doesn’t act right. His exit indicators are social — when friends at the bar talk about his stocks, when his golf buddy asks about Litecoin, when CNBC covers his sector, the trade is over. In the updated interview, he quintupled his career winnings during the pandemic era through masks, drones, SPACs, and crypto, and now believes lithium equities will be the biggest trade of his life.

Chris Camillo: Neither

Chris Camillo invented an entirely new class of market analysis that is neither fundamental nor technical — he calls it social arbitrage. At fourteen, he noticed that his local 7-Eleven had consolidated two full refrigerators of Snapple into half of one and asked his stockbroker brother how to profit from Snapple’s decline. That first put option trade was the seed of his entire career methodology: detecting change in consumer behavior before it shows up in financial data.

He founded TickerTags, a company that monitors social media mentions of over 250,000 curated word combinations linked to public companies. The key insight: conversational data is earlier than transactional data. People talk about buying something before they buy it. Credit card data shows what people bought yesterday; social media shows what they’re interested in today. He identified the La Croix trend years before Wall Street, spotted the Chipotle E. coli foot traffic collapse through mentions of “Chipotle” plus “lunch,” and caught the SeaWorld decline after Blackfish by monitoring sentiment that refused to normalize.

His most impressive trade was COVID in 2020 — he had one hundred percent conviction weeks before the market cracked and bought weekly puts on indexes, airlines, and casinos. He lost a couple hundred thousand dollars a week as puts expired worthless for two and a half weeks before the market finally broke. That year he made over $20 million in profits from a $7 million account.

Marsten Parker: Don’t Quit Your Day Job

Marsten Parker was a music major who taught himself programming and designed stock trading systems that delivered roughly twenty percent average annual returns over two decades — well over triple the S&P 500. He kept his day job throughout most of his career, and almost quit trading in early 2016 despite being more than $5 million ahead because cumulative profits must outpace taxes and withdrawals. His greatest lesson in adaptability: when his momentum systems stopped working, he had the flexibility to switch to their exact opposite — mean-reversion systems.

Michael Kean: Complementary Strategies

Michael Kean tripled the S&P 500’s performance in both return and return/risk terms by combining a sixty-percent long equity portfolio with forty percent devoted to short-term trading, primarily biotech catalyst shorts. He looks for stocks with upcoming binary events — drug trial results — where the news is already priced in or the trial is likely to fail. His biggest holding, JD.com, taught him the hardest lesson: after a Google deal announcement, the stock initially rallied then reversed sharply lower. He held anyway and watched it lose half its value. The worst mistake was ignoring that day’s counter-to-expected price action.

His approach to risk management at the portfolio level is elegant: the short-biased trading portion naturally hedges the long equity portion, creating a portfolio of inversely correlated components. His worst-ever loss came from straying from his methodology — in late 2014, up thirty-five percent with a month to go, he felt he had “earned the right to take more risk” and bought Russian stocks and MLPs he knew nothing about simply because they were down a lot. He surrendered seven percent in two weeks.

Pavel Krejčí: The Bellhop Who Beat the Pros

Pavel Krejčí is a bellhop from Prague whose long-only equity day-trading strategy has surpassed ninety-nine percent of professional managers. His high school economics teacher told him he would never do any job related to economics. He got a three in economics and a four in math on a scale where five is worst. He opened a stock account with $20,000, lost half of it, stopped trading for six months, borrowed $5,000 from his brother, and returned with a methodology he still uses today.

He trades stocks only on the day after earnings reports — roughly seven months a year concentrated in the four earnings seasons. His universe is two hundred to three hundred high-volume stocks. Ninety percent of his profits come from buying stocks in uptrends with bullish earnings reports. He enters within the first thirty minutes after the open, places a stop one second after entry at four to five percent below, and holds nothing overnight. His win rate is about sixty-five percent, and his average win is 1.5 times his average loss. He was up thirteen percent in 2008 with a long-only strategy while the S&P fell thirty-seven percent.

The book changed his life — for fifteen years he worked alone and spoke to nobody about trading. After publication, he connected with people worldwide and started managing money. His returns dropped to roughly ten percent annually in recent years due to COVID hospitalization, the birth of his daughter making him more risk-averse, and volatile markets unfavorable for his strategy. He has started adding short-selling for the first time. When asked the most important lesson he has learned: “How not to lose money. I always play defense; I don’t play offense.”

46 Market Wizard Lessons

The conclusion distills the interviews into forty-six universal trading lessons. The key themes: find a methodology that fits your personality, keep a trading journal and categorize trades, know your edge and stay within it, learn from mistakes, design asymmetric strategies, manage risk at both the individual trade and portfolio level, use stops at hypothesis-disproving points not monetary amounts, don’t wait for stops to be hit on lagging trades, take larger positions on high-conviction trades but never so large that fear interferes, stick to your methodology and avoid impulsive trades, guard against complacency after winning streaks, be observant of behavioral trends, and love what you do.

Several lessons are unique to this volume. The concept of treating yourself after a loss to break negative spirals. The idea that indirect trade implementation can be superior to the obvious direct trade. The warning that missed trades are often more expensive than losses because they shake confidence and cause overcompensating. And the most repeated theme of all: patience, both to wait for the right trade and to stay with a good one once you’re in it.

Trading & Investing Lessons

Risk Management Is Everything

Every trader in this book credits risk management, not entry methodology, as the primary driver of success. Brandt acknowledges that classical chart analysis has lost virtually its entire edge, yet still succeeds because charts are merely a tool for identifying asymmetric risk/reward entry points. Sall varies his risk between one and five percent depending on conviction. Dhaliwal cuts size systematically at drawdown thresholds. The lesson for any investor: the quality of your exits matters more than the quality of your entries.

Counter-to-Expected Price Action Signals Direction

When a market moves opposite to what news should dictate, it reveals the real direction. Dhaliwal’s Australian dollar trade — a spectacularly bullish employment report that was followed by a price breakdown below long-term support — is the purest example. For investors, this means watching how markets respond to news matters more than the news itself.

Position Sizing Should Vary by Conviction

Don’t take the same size on every trade. Sall goes maximum aggressive on his unicorn trades. Neumann puts a third of his account into his highest- conviction sector plays. The wide variation in position sizing is an essential factor in generating outsized returns. Conversely, trading too large for your comfort level leads to paralysis and missed opportunities.

The Danger of Managing Other People’s Money

Brandt’s worst year coincided with accepting investor money. Bargh was nearly destroyed by the financial pressure of having no salary. Own-account success does not automatically translate to managing money — guilt over losing others’ money impairs the decision-making process.

Drawdown Management Determines Survival

Every successful trader has a systematic plan for drawdowns — whether cutting size, tightening criteria, or stopping trading entirely. The worst action during a drawdown is changing your methodology to chase recent results. Brandt extended a three-month drawdown to eighteen months by trying mean reversion. The best action is often the simplest: cut size and wait for the market to align with your approach again.

Patience Is the Competitive Advantage

In a world of algorithmic speed, the advantage of the individual trader lies in patience — the ability to wait for the fat pitch. Sall makes ninety percent of his profits from ten percent of the time. Brandt’s profits come from ten to fifteen percent of his trades. Krejčí only trades during earnings seasons. The markets reward those who can sit still when there is nothing to do.

Key Takeaways

There Is No Single True Path

Approaches ranged from pure technical to pure fundamental to social media analysis to neither. The only common denominator is that each trader found an approach that fits their personality.

Asymmetry Drives Exceptional Returns

The best traders design their strategies so that average wins far exceed average losses, even with win rates below fifty percent. Sall is right only thirty to fifty percent of the time but earns eight times as much on winners as losers.

The Internal Game Is the Final Frontier

Three traders from the same firm independently arrived at the conclusion that mindset is the most critical factor once methodology is established. Flow states, meditation, journaling, and emotional awareness are not soft skills — they are the essential edge.

Your Methodology Will Need to Change

Markets evolve. Brandt’s classical chart patterns no longer work as standalone signals. Dhaliwal shifted from headline trading to fading headlines to macro analysis. Parker switched from momentum to mean-reversion. Adaptability is survival.

Failure Is the Tuition for Success

Nearly every trader blew up at least once before succeeding. Shapiro lost everything twice. Bargh nearly ran out of money. Neumann lost a year’s profits in a single day. What separates the winners is their response: analyze what went wrong, learn the lesson, and never repeat the same mistake.

Contrarian Ideas

Emotions Are a Signal, Not a Noise

Netto argues against the standard advice to eliminate emotions. Because emotional trading consistently loses money, monitoring your own emotional extremes provides a reliable contrarian indicator.

Treat Yourself After a Loss

Sall celebrates after bad days to break negative spirals — the exact opposite of the instinct to punish yourself by cutting spending.

Consistent Profitability Is a Trap

Aiming to make money every month leads to forcing marginal trades during dry periods. Sall observed that a common trait among failed traders was having a goal of making money every month.

Classical Chart Analysis No Longer Works

Brandt, one of the most successful chart traders alive, says that trading every chart pattern entry point Schabacker labeled would be extremely difficult today. Large long-term patterns, trendlines, and channels have lost their edge. What survives: shorter-term patterns with horizontal boundaries that define close, meaningful stops.

Allegories & Cultural References

The Sniper

Sall describes his trading style as that of a sniper — constant readiness, waiting for the perfect shot, refusing to waste bullets on anything less.

The Wildebeest Migration

Neumann identifies with David Tepper’s analogy of being the first wildebeest into the valley to get the fresh green grass, accepting the risk of a waiting lion.

Music Is the Space Between the Notes

Sall quotes Debussy to illustrate that trading success depends more on what you don’t do between the real trade opportunities than on the trades themselves.

The Ocean Analogy

Sall compares the surface turbulence of market volatility to the calm that exists below — every trader has the ability to dive down into that calm region.

Burn the Boats

Sall’s description of his early career when he had no backup plan: “If you want to take the island, burn the boats.”

Proposals & Frameworks

Brandt’s Trade Management System

Enter on horizontal boundary breakouts. Risk half a percent of equity per trade. Move stop to breakeven within two to three days. Take half off when profits equal one percent of equity. Use three-day mechanical stop rule near the target. Liquidate any trade at a loss on Friday’s close. Never use trailing stops or pyramid into positions.

Shapiro’s Contrarian Framework

Use the Commitment of Traders report to identify extremes in speculator positioning. Wait for price action that confirms a reversal despite news in the opposite direction. Place stops at the reversal day’s low or high. Exit when the COT-based oscillator returns to neutral.

Dhaliwal’s Drawdown Protocol

Cut position size in half at a five percent drawdown. Cut in half again at eight percent. Stop trading entirely at fifteen percent. Watch for warning signs: two-percent single-day loss, loss of significant open profits, or a high-conviction trade that ends as a wash.

Neumann’s Four Corners

Right chart pattern (downtrend line breakout). Clean share structure (few options/warrants, under 200 million shares). Right sector. Catalyst or story to move the stock.

Camillo’s Information Imbalance Model

Identify significant off-radar information. Assess whether it has been disseminated to the investment public. If undisseminated: check for extraneous factors. If none: trade the information imbalance. Exit at “information parity” — the moment the information hits the street regardless of P&L.

What Didn’t Fit

Brandt’s Sleepwalking Accident

Thirty-five years before the interview, Brandt got up in the middle of the night to use the bathroom and, while sleepwalking, climbed over the upper deck railing thinking it was a chair in the hallway. He fell nearly twenty feet and spent over forty days in a hospital wearing a body cast sandwiched between two mattresses. He has had half a dozen back surgeries since and lives with constant pain, never complains, and refuses medication. The physical resilience parallels his trading resilience.

Shapiro’s Marijuana Business

During his trading hiatus, Shapiro built about forty marijuana growing facilities in Rhode Island at $150-200K each, renting them to operators at $6,000 a month with six-year lease-to-own deals. Two-year payback. He didn’t care about the stigma — most people did, which created the barrier to entry he wanted.

Neumann’s Kenya Safari Text

While on safari in Kenya, Neumann received a BlackBerry text from a friend telling him his Spongetech stock had hit twenty-five cents. He bribed the reception desk to use their dial-up computer and sold his position at the dead top. Five minutes after he returned to his tent, his friend texted that the stock was at five cents. That was probably a $700,000 text message. Spongetech was later exposed as a massive pump-and-dump scheme by the SEC.

Bargh’s Tie-in-Mouth Interview

During his trading firm interview, Bargh unconsciously put his tie in his mouth while a partner was speaking — a childhood habit of concentration. After he left, the partner at the desk said “We can’t hire him.” The interviewing partner convinced them to give him a second chance. Bargh only found out years later when someone made a joke about it.

The “Brandt Bullshit Factor”

When Brandt was translating his chart analysis into fundamental-sounding narratives for Campbell clients, traders at the CBOT overheard him talking about anchovy problems off the coast as a reason to buy soybeans. They started calling it the “Brandt bullshit factor,” which is why he named his company Factor Research and Trading.

Camillo’s Michelle Obama Dress

Camillo missed the J.Crew trade triggered by Michelle Obama wearing a yellow J.Crew dress on Jay Leno. It was one of the most defining moments for the brand in a decade. He was so upset at himself for seeing but not observing the opportunity that he went on eBay, bought the dress, and keeps it in his closet as a permanent reminder of the missed trade.

Unknown Market Wizards by Jack D. Schwager · Harriman House, 2020 (Updated 2023) · 430 pages

The fifth Market Wizards volume profiles eleven solo traders who achieved extraordinary returns while operating in complete obscurity. Their approaches span technical analysis, fundamental events, contrarian positioning, social media arbitrage, and systematic programming. The universal thread: risk management, patience, self-awareness, and the relentless pursuit of a methodology that fits your personality.

About the Author

Jack D. Schwager is the author of the Market Wizards series, one of the most widely recognized collections of interviews with top traders. The series includes Market Wizards (1989), The New Market Wizards (1992), Stock Market Wizards (2001), Hedge Fund Market Wizards (2012), and Unknown Market Wizards (2020). He is also the author of A Complete Guide to the Futures Markets, Market Sense and Nonsense, and The Little Book of Market Wizards.

Schwager is a founding partner of FundSeeder Technologies, a platform that provides free performance analytics and verified track records for traders. He began his career as a research analyst for Commodities Corporation, the legendary Princeton firm that seeded many of the traders featured in his first book. He has a degree in economics from Brown University and a Masters in economics from Brooklyn College.

Why This Book Matters

In an era when algorithmic trading, artificial intelligence, and the proliferation of hedge funds have led many to question whether individual traders can still compete, Unknown Market Wizards provides an emphatic answer: yes, they can. The traders profiled here are not MIT quants with billion-dollar budgets. They are a bellhop, a former Marine, an ex-advertising executive, a music major, and a kid who started with an insurance check from a hailstorm. Their success demonstrates that the human elements of trading — pattern recognition, emotional discipline, patience, and adaptability — remain viable competitive advantages.

The updated edition, with follow-up interviews covering the pandemic, the inflation shock, and two bear markets, adds a rare dimension: accountability. Unlike most trading books that capture a moment in time, this edition shows whether these traders’ approaches survived the most turbulent period in recent market history. Almost universally, they did — reinforcing the book’s central message that sound trading principles are timeless even as markets change.
