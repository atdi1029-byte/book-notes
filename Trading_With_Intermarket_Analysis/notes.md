- His second book "Intermarket Analysis: Profiting from Global Market Relationships" (2004) is now required reading for CMT program
- Three major deflationary events in last 20 years: (1) Japanese stock peak 1990, (2) Asian currency crisis 1997-98, (3) housing collapse 2007

## Chapter 1: Intermarket Analysis: The Study of Relationships

### All Markets Are Related
- Intermarket analysis = study of how various financial markets relate to each other
- Departure from prior single-market approach where stock analysts ignored bonds, commodities, dollar
- Four asset classes: bonds, stocks, commodities, currencies
- Bond prices usually trend opposite direction of stocks
- Bond yields inversely correlated with bond prices — falling yields (rising bond prices) warn of stock trouble
- Figure 1.1: 10-year Treasury yield peaked in Jan 2000, eight months before S&P 500 peaked — classic leading indicator
- Bond market usually changes direction before stocks at major turning points

### Asset Allocation Strategies
- Asset allocation now includes commodities and currencies, not just bonds/stocks/cash
- Since 2002 (dollar decline began): commodities +64%, bonds +23%, stocks +9% over 10 years
- Main catalyst for commodity upturn was 32% drop in U.S. dollar
- Dollar and commodities trend in opposite directions (one of most reliable intermarket relationships)
- Figure 1.2: Dollar peak in 2002 coincided exactly with major commodity upturn (CRB Index)
- Commodity-linked currencies (AUD, CAD) benefited — Aussie dollar gained 101% vs 50% for euro over 10 years starting 2002

### ETFs Have Revolutionized Intermarket Trading
- ETFs made it easy to invest in commodities, currencies, foreign markets
- In 1990s, incorporating commodities/currencies required futures markets
- ETFs allow implementing sector rotation strategies easily
- Book relies heavily on ETFs to demonstrate intermarket interactions

### Sector Rotation and the Business Cycle
- Stock market has 10 sectors, ~90 industry groups
- At bull market starts: consumer discretionary, tech, transportation, small-caps lead
- Near market tops: energy stocks become leaders (dangerous warning sign)
- Money flows from energy into defensive sectors (consumer staples, health care, utilities) near tops
- Consumer discretionary leads early in expansion; energy leadership comes near end; consumer staples strongest during downturns

### Stocks Peak and Trough before the Economy
- 2000 stock top → recession following spring
- October 2007 top → recession that December
- Stock bottoms (2003, 2009) preceded end of recessions by a few months
- When stocks weaken → money rotates from stocks into bonds; at bottoms → opposite
- Bonds change direction first at tops and bottoms, stocks second, commodities third
- Bonds are an even earlier economic indicator than stocks

### The Role of Oil
- Rising oil has contributed to every U.S. recession in last 40 years
- Oil spikes preceded/accompanied stock drops in 1973 (Arab Oil Embargo → 50% loss in 1974), 1987, 1990, 1994, 2000
- Sharp oil drops are bullish — both Iraq wars (1991, 2003) saw oil drop → new bull markets
- Figure 1.3: Crude started climbing early 2007, S&P peaked October 2007; crude peaked July 2008 (9 months after stock top)
- Rising oil forces Fed to raise rates → weakens stocks and economy

### Advantages of Using Charts
- Intermarket work is market-driven, not theoretical
- Economic statistics are backward-looking; markets are forward-looking (discounting mechanisms)
- Markets anticipate economic trends 6-9 months into future
- Chartists trust forward-looking markets; economists rely on lagging indicators

### Viewing the Big Picture Is Important
- Charts are universal — apply to any market, any timeframe, anywhere in world
- Intermarket chartist can follow any market without being an expert
- Provides big-picture view vs tunnel vision of single-market analysts

### Intermarket Implications for Technical Analysis
- Intermarket work greatly expands usefulness of technical analysis
- Allows talking about inflation, deflation, interest rates, dollar, business cycle
- Consumer discretionary leads early in expansion; energy at end; staples during downturns
- Fed didn't acknowledge deflation threat until spring 2003 — markets spotted it years earlier
- Wall Street paid a big price ignoring chart signals in 2000 and 2007

### A New Dimension to Technical Work
- Intermarket analysis improves "peripheral trading vision"
- Like driving without mirrors — dangerous to trade without checking related markets
- Doesn't replace traditional TA; adds another dimension
- Even the Fed uses charts to look at financial markets for economic clues

### Intermarket Work Is an Evolutionary Step
- Technical analysis has enormous transferability between markets
- All global markets are linked; traders must account for these linkages
- TA can be applied to any market, any asset class, short-term or long-term

### Why Relationships Change
- Intermarket relationships are not static — most remain constant, some change
- Bond-stock relationship changed around 2000 (became inverse instead of positive)
- Deflation threat after 2007 housing collapse was worst since Great Depression
- Traditional fiscal/monetary measures didn't work as in past business cycles
- It's much harder to fight deflation than inflation
- After lowering short-term rates to zero, Fed had to resort to other measures (QE)
- Intermarket principles are guidelines, not rigid rules
- Correlations may weaken for short periods — tools exist to monitor correlation strength

### Intermarket Principles (Summary List)
- All global markets are linked
- Analysis of any one market should include analysis of the others
- Four asset classes: Stocks, Bonds, Commodities, Currencies
- Dollar and commodities trend in opposite directions
- Bond prices and commodities trend in opposite directions
- Since 1998, bond and stock prices have trended inversely
- Since 2008, stocks and commodities have been closely correlated
- Bonds usually change direction before stocks
- Stocks usually change direction before commodities
- Bond yields peak first at tops, stocks second, commodities last
- Those rotations are less reliable at bottoms than at tops
- All global stocks are closely correlated
- Rising dollar benefits U.S. stocks; weaker dollar favors foreign stocks
- Emerging markets closely tied to commodity trends

### Review of the Old Normal
- Main focus of book is "new normal" since 2000 (especially since 2008)
- But useful to first review "old normal" relationships from final three decades of prior century
- Stock and commodity prices have become closely correlated (new, since 2008)
- Bond and stock prices have trended in opposite directions since 1998 (reversed earlier relationship)

## Chapter 2: Review of the Old Normal (pages 36+)
- Reviews intermarket relationships during last three decades of 20th century
- 1980 commodity peak ended hyperinflationary 1970s
- Asian currency crisis 1997-98 introduced deflation threat for first time since 1930s
- Japanese stock market collapse in 1990 contributed to growing deflation threat

### 1980 Was a Key Turning Point
- Commodity bubble burst in 1980, ending hyperinflation of 1970s
- 1980 peak in commodities coincided with bottom in U.S. dollar
- Falling commodities + rising dollar → major bond upturn 1981 → stock bull market 1982
- That important turning point followed the traditional intermarket script very closely
- Figure 2.1: CRB Index peak (down arrow) in 1980 coincided exactly with Dollar Index upturn (up arrow)
- Rising dollar helped end the commodity inflationary spiral of the 1970s
- A rising dollar causes commodity prices to fall; falling commodities usually produce higher bond prices

### The End of the Inflationary 1970s
- 1970s: CRB Index appreciated 250%; bond yields rose 150%
- 1970s were bad for bonds AND stocks — DJIA started and ended the decade at same level (~1000)
- Mid-decade stagflation: 1973 oil spike (Arab Oil Embargo) → 50% stock market loss in 1974
- Stagflation = high inflation + economic stagnation
- Gold soared above $700/oz by end of 1970s; weak dollar contributed to upward spiral
- CRB fell from record high of 330, began 20-year decline losing half its value
- Gold fell from $700 to $250 (60% loss from 1980 peak) over those 20 years
- Within two years of 1980 commodity top, intermarket trends of 1970s completely reversed
- Next two decades favored paper assets (bonds, stocks) at expense of tangible assets (commodities)

### The 1987 Crash Reinforced Intermarket Trends
- Two factors supporting rising stocks after 1982: falling commodities (low inflation) and rising bonds (falling rates)
- April 1987: commodity prices spiked higher while bond prices tumbled
- Bond prices peaked four months before stocks — again demonstrating bonds change direction before stocks
- Figure 2.4: Commodity spike (up arrow) spring 1987 coincided with bond plunge; S&P crashed October 1987
- 1987 crash was global — demonstrated global stock markets are highly correlated during downturns
- During market downturns, rising dollar makes foreign stocks fall even faster than U.S. stocks

### The Two Iraq Wars
- August 1990: Iraq invades Kuwait → gold and oil surge, bonds and stocks tumble worldwide
- January 1991 (Desert Storm): all intermarket trends reversed — bonds and stocks resume uptrends, gold and oil fall
- March 17, 2003 (second Iraq war hostilities begin): U.S. stock market surged 8% (biggest weekly gain in 20 years), started 4-year bull run
- Gold fell 15%, crude oil tumbled 33%, dollar jumped 4%
- Bond prices turned down as massive switch from Treasuries back into stocks
- In both Iraq conflicts, the four financial markets did exactly what intermarket analysis predicted
- During 2002-2003 second Iraq crisis, bonds and stocks trended inversely — reflecting the post-1998 decoupling

### The 1994 Stealth Bear Market Follows Intermarket Script
- Called "stealth" because major stock indexes lost little more than 10%
- But underneath: small-caps lost 15%, transports fell 26%, utilities lost 34% peak to trough
- Transportation stocks especially vulnerable to rising oil (fuel costs)
- Rising oil → outsized losses in fuel-sensitive transports; rising rates (falling bonds) → huge losses in rate-sensitive utilities
- Commodity upturn first half 1993 → bond downturn second half 1993 → stocks peaked September 1993, fell five months later (February 1994)
- Bond market preceded the turn in stocks — once again
- After peaking, stocks and bonds fell together as commodities rose (old intermarket model held)
- Dollar fell throughout 1994 → contributed to commodity rise
- Commodity peak mid-1994 → bond bottom November 1994 → stocks turned up a month after bonds
- From Q4 1994 bottom, stocks began phenomenal bull run lasting until end of decade
- After 1994, rising dollar + falling commodities → rising bonds → rising stocks (intermarket textbook)
- Figure 2.6: Close correlation between Treasury bond prices and utility stocks during 1994-1995

### Echoes from the 1930s
- Starting 1998, word "deflation" heard for first time since 1930s
- Caused mainly by Asian currency crisis 1997-1998
- Also: Japanese deflation was starting to infect Japanese economy
- Within a couple years after 1998, global deflation spread from Asia (Asian contagion)
- THE KEY CHANGE: bond and stock prices became inversely correlated — what was good for bonds after 1998 became bad for stocks
- Bond yields turned down before stocks in 2000 and 2007 — early warning of stock market decline
- Commodities started to become more closely correlated with stocks (as in deflationary 1930s)
- This tighter link became even more pronounced after 2008 housing/financial collapse
- Both asset classes tied to global economic cycle: rising commodities (copper, oil) = economic strength = good for stocks
- First decade of new millennium: resurgence of hard assets (gold, commodities) largely from Fed weakening dollar to combat deflation
- In 1930s, dollar was devalued by taking U.S. off gold standard — same result: higher gold prices
- Contrary to popular belief, gold does very well in deflationary climate (1930s and post-2000)
- 1929-1932: stocks and commodities tumbled together; only Treasury bonds and gold-mining stocks bucked the trend

### The Deflation Scenario
- Murphy included "The Deflation Scenario" section in 1999 revision of "Technical Analysis of the Financial Markets"
- Asian currency collapse 1997 had especially depressing effect on global commodity markets
- CRB Index fell to lowest level in 20 years → disinflation turning into dangerous deflation
- KEY DISTINCTION: Disinflation (prices rise at slower pace) is good for bonds and stocks; Deflation (prices actually fall) is good for bonds but BAD for stocks
- In 1998: commodity prices fell sharply, bond prices surged (normal), BUT stocks fell while bonds rose (NEW — departure from old normal)
- Global flight to safety: money poured into U.S. Treasury bonds while stocks were sold worldwide
- A deflationary period usually favors bond prices over stocks

### The Japanese Bubble Bursts in 1990
- Japanese stock market bubble burst in 1990 → 13-year descent → deflationary spiral in world's second-largest economy
- Japanese deflation contributed to decoupling of bonds and stocks in the U.S. nearly a decade later
- "Decouple" = when normal relationship between two markets disappears or reverses

## Chapter 3: The 1997-1998 Asian Currency Crisis

### Overview
- Most important result: dramatic decoupling of bond and stock prices
- Market reactions during 1997-1998 were a dress rehearsal for the following decade
- Key lesson: falling bond yields (rising bond prices) after 2000 would NOT have the same positive effect on stocks as before 1998
- Two deflationary events of 1990s: Japanese stock collapse (1990) and Asian currency collapse (1997)
- Japanese deflation may help explain the persistent decline in U.S. bond yields

### The Asian Currency Crisis Starts in 1997
- Summer 1997: Thai baht starts to tumble → spreads to other Asian currencies
- Currency collapse → Asian stock price collapse → ripple effect globally
- Fears of global deflation → commodity prices into free fall → worldwide rotation out of stocks into Treasuries
- CRB Index fell to lowest level in 20 years (Figure 3.1)
- Asian central bankers raised interest rates to stabilize currencies → pushed Asian stocks into steep decline for at least a year

### Bonds and Stocks Start to Decouple
- Decoupling: bond and stock prices trend in opposite directions (instead of same direction)
- Second half 1997: U.S. stocks declined while Treasury bonds rose
- First half 1998: stocks rose while bonds declined
- Q3 1998 (height of crisis, July-October): Dow lost 20%, stocks fell sharply while Treasury bond prices soared
- During financial panics: investors take money out of stocks → put into U.S. Treasuries (safe haven)
- By end of 1998, crisis passed → complete reversal: bonds tumbled, stocks soared
- Bonds continued falling through all of 1999; stocks soared to record highs
- Figure 3.2: Bond prices rose and stocks fell during height of 1998 financial crisis

### 1997 and 1998 Were Only a Dress Rehearsal
- The 1998 decoupling was a dress rehearsal for the devastating bear market starting spring 2000
- During worst three stock market years since Great Depression: bond prices rose continuously while stocks fell
- Fed lowered rates 12 times over 18 months — no apparent effect on stocks
- Those who heeded 1998 warnings knew that rising bond prices (falling rates) don't necessarily help stocks in deflationary climate
- Figure 3.3: Before 1998, falling bond yields were positive for stocks; after 1998, falling bond yields became bad for stocks
- Bond yields still turned down ahead of stocks (maintained leading indicator role) — but new relationship meant bond YIELDS (not prices) became the leading indicator
- During economic downturn, Fed lowers rates to help stocks; during deflationary downturn, Fed policy becomes much less effective

### Intermarket Lessons of 1997 and 1998
- Need to monitor global markets — not just stocks, but currencies too
- Collapse in a relatively obscure Asian currency (Thai baht) eventually had dramatic effect on U.S. markets
- Flight out of stocks into bonds lasted 18 months
- Sector rotations influenced: investor funds rotated from economically sensitive stocks to defensive (consumer staples)
- Asian contagion collapsed commodity prices → intensified fears of global deflation
- Currency ETFs now make it much easier to track and participate in foreign currency trends
- Most important lesson: deflationary trends from Asia caused a major change in bond-stock relationship
- Rising bond prices no longer helped stock prices — rising bond market came at expense of stocks
- Falling bond yields now became bad for stocks (the new normal)

### The Asian Effect Overrides the Fed
- After 2000 stock bubble burst, Fed lowered rates 12 times over 18 months — didn't work
- Deflationary trends coming from Asia were the reason
- By 2002, Fed was using the "D word" (deflation) but only to deny it was a real threat
- Fine line between disinflation (prices rise slower — helps stocks) and deflation (prices actually fall — hurts stocks)

### Two Deflationary Events of the 1990s
- First: Japanese stock collapse 1990 → led to deflation lasting 20+ years
- Second: Asian currency crisis 1997-1998 → caused global commodity collapse
- Japanese stock collapse had more subtle but longer-lasting effect
- Asian crisis had more direct and immediate impact on financial markets
- Figure 3.4: Commodity prices and Japanese stocks both hit 20-year lows during 1998

### Deflationary Effect on Bond Yields
- Bond yields normally trend in same direction as commodity prices
- Bond yields had been dropping since 1981 (year after commodities peaked)
- 1993: bond yields fell to 6%; 1994: yields rallied sharply (contributing to stock decline)
- 1994-1997: yields trended sideways between 1993 low and 1994 high
- 1997: commodity prices started dropping during Asian crisis → yields started dropping again
- Within a year, long bond yield fell below 1993 low to reach lowest level in 20 years (same as CRB)
- Figure 3.5: Falling commodity prices helped push Treasury yield to new 20-year low during 1998

### Japanese Deflation and U.S. Interest Rates
- Japanese deflation had much bigger impact on U.S. interest rates than commonly credited
- Figure 3.6: Nikkei 225 and U.S. Treasury bond yield show remarkably close visual correlation since 1990
- Correlation of .75 between the two markets over this period
- Asian currency crisis had more dramatic short-term impact on U.S. bond yields
- Japanese deflationary effects since 1990 had more subtle but longer-lasting effect on U.S. rates

### Chapter 3 Summary
- Asian currency crisis was the key deflationary event, along with Japanese stock collapse
- Most important result: decoupling of bonds and stocks (inverse instead of positive correlation)
- Bond yields became the new leading indicator for stocks (instead of bond prices)
- These changes set the stage for the "new normal" that dominates markets since 2000

## Chapter 4: Intermarket Events Surrounding the 2000 Top

### Events Leading Up to the 2000 Top
- During 1999: stock market soared to new record high; bond prices suffered one of worst years in history
- Sharp rise in oil during 1999 pushed rates higher globally
- Asian market recovery in 1999 also pushed commodity prices (copper, aluminum) higher → heavy losses in bonds
- Rising commodity prices prompted Fed to start raising short-term rates mid-1999 → contributed to major stock top the following year
- Most stock market tops have been caused by Fed raising short-term rates to stem the rise in oil
- Rotation out of bonds initially helped stocks in 1999, but longer-lasting commodity effects were more damaging

### Crude Oil Triples in Price
- Oil tripled during 1999 — big reason rates rose and bonds fell that year
- Rising oil had positive effect on energy stocks (became market's strongest sector in 1999)
- Energy leadership is almost ALWAYS a warning sign that a stock bull market and economic recovery are nearing completion
- Groups hurt most by rising oil: fuel-intensive transportation stocks and interest rate-sensitive financial stocks
- When oil is rising, stocks tied to energy become stock market leaders
- TRADING SIGNAL: Leadership shift from energy stocks to consumer staples = threshold from late expansion to early contraction (happened spring 2000)
- Rising oil → Fed raises short-term rates → inverted yield curve → stock market top

### A Rise in Short-Term Rates Leads to an Inverted Yield Curve
- Fed started raising short-term rates summer 1999 (because commodity prices rising too sharply)
- By Q1 2000: Fed tightening led to inverted yield curve
- Inverted yield curve = short-term rates rise ABOVE long-term rates
- Normal yield curve: long-term rates higher than short-term rates
- The danger point: when Fed pushes short-term rates over long-term rates
- Official inverted yield curve: 2-year rate rises above 10-year rate
- Recessions of 1970, 1974, 1980, 1982, 1990 all preceded by inverted yield curves
- Stocks most vulnerable during inverted yield curve: those with highest P/E ratios (most expensive)
- In 2000: the overvalued stocks were dot-com/Internet/tech in the Nasdaq
- Bear market = market falls 20% from previous peak
- Downtrend = series of lower highs and lower lows
- Nasdaq peaked Q1 2000, lost 40% by May (official bear market)
- Figure 4.1: Nasdaq fell below its spring low, signaling major downtrend

### REITs Benefit from Falling Stocks
- REITs were top-performing group in April 2000 (as Nasdaq tumbled)
- REITs benefit during bear markets for three reasons:
  1. Pay high dividends (appealing when bond yields plunge)
  2. Low correlation with stock market (diversification value)
  3. Historically negative correlation to technology stocks (REITs rise when tech falls)
- Figure 4.2: REITs turned up just as Nasdaq peaked in spring 2000
- Homebuilding stocks also turned up — benefit from falling interest rates
- The bear market starting 2000 + aggressive Fed easing helped create the housing boom that lasted several years before bursting
- Housing stocks did NOT benefit from the next bear market (2007-2008) — in fact, housing collapse helped CAUSE it

### Consumer Staples Start to Outperform
- Consumer staples = defensive sector (beverages, food, tobacco, household products)
- People still buy these products in good times and bad — relatively resistant to business cycle
- Relative strength in consumer staples hints at economic slowing
- Relative strength = how a group is doing compared to benchmark (S&P 500)
- Other defensive groups that attract money during economic slowdowns: health care and utilities
- Even truer if stock decline is accompanied by falling interest rates (makes dividend-paying stocks more attractive)
- When bond yields fall sharply, investors favor dividend-paying stocks for higher yield
- Figure 4.3: Consumer staples rallied as Nasdaq tumbled during 2000
- DANGER SIGNAL: When energy AND consumer staples are the two strongest sectors simultaneously — that's what happened first half of 2000

### Market Lessons from 2000
- 2000 was textbook example of technical, intermarket, and economic analyses working together
- S&P 500 fell deeply below its 200-day moving average during second half of 2000
- 200-day MA divides major uptrends from major downtrends
- Significant drop below 200-day MA = warning that major decline has begun
- When the 200-day MA itself turns down = another clear sign major bear market has begun (happened Q4 2000)
- A downturn in the 200-day moving average is usually a warning of a more serious market drop
- Intermarket warnings had been flashing during second half of 1999: rising commodities, rising rates
- Result: Fed tightening → inverted yield curve Q1 2000 (had led to every recession since 1970)
- Sector rotations followed end-of-expansion pattern
- Wall Street and economic community ignored ALL warning signs — relied on outdated fundamental info
- Most important lesson: Stocks are a better indicator of the economy than the economy is of stocks

### Bonds, Stocks, and Commodities Peaked in the Proper Order
- CRITICAL PEAKING SEQUENCE: Bonds first → Stocks second → Commodities third
- They also tend to BOTTOM in the same order
- Bonds are leading indicator for stocks; stocks are leading indicator for commodities
- Stock prices usually peak 6-9 months before start of recession
- When commodities finally peak → usually a sign recession has already started
- 2000 sequence: Bond yields peaked January 2000 → Nasdaq peaked March, S&P peaked September → CRB peaked January 2001 (5 months after S&P)
- Figure 4.5: Bond yields peaked before stocks in 2000
- Figure 4.6: S&P 500 peaked five months before commodities
- Figure 4.7: All three markets on same chart — peaked in normal order (bonds first, stocks second, commodities last)
- BEFORE 1998: bond PRICES peaked before stocks. AFTER 1998: bond YIELDS have been peaking before stocks
- Official recession began March 2001 (two months after commodity peak)
- Same peaking order repeated during 2007-2008: yields peaked June 2007 (4 months before stocks peaked October), commodities peaked July 2008

### The 2002 and 2003 Bottoms Reverse Normal Order
- Normal BOTTOMING sequence: Bond yields first → Stocks second → Commodities third
- BUT in 2002-2003, it happened in REVERSE: Commodities bottomed first (start of 2002) → Stocks bottomed October 2002 → Bond yields didn't turn up until June 2003
- Figure 4.8: Three markets bottomed out of sequence during 2002-2003
- When something unusual happens in intermarket work, there's usually a reason
- The unusual bottoming order was partly because of the Fed

### The Fed Discovers Deflation during 2003
- Markets had been warning of growing deflationary threat since Asian crisis
- Markets are discounting mechanisms — had been trading on assumption deflation was real for several years
- Economic community (including the Fed) kept calling falling inflation/rates "good things"
- May 6, 2003: Fed statement acknowledged "the probability of an unwelcome substantial fall in inflation... exceeds that of a pickup in inflation" — first time since WWII that Fed feared deflation more than inflation
- Fed had been lowering rates since January 2001 → by May 2003, rates lowered 12 times to lowest level in 40 years (1.25%)
- Running out of ammunition with short-term rates
- Talk of Fed buying longer-maturity bonds to lower long-term rates → contributed to plunge in bond yields spring 2003
- Market reaction to deflation announcement: 10-year Treasury yield hit lowest level in 45 years
- But that plunge in yields was short-lived; by June, yields started climbing sharply
- Rise in yields helped by massive rotation out of bonds back into stocks (new bull market had started 3 months earlier)

### Commodities Turn Up during 2002
- Fed couldn't lower short-term rates much more → but could lower the dollar
- A falling dollar is one of the best cures for falling prices (boosts inflation)
- Fed suspected of allowing dollar to depreciate since start of 2002 to create inflation
- Confirmed when Treasury Secretary hinted U.S. government had abandoned "strong dollar" policy
- Traders took this as signal: government wanted dollar to fall to reflate the economy
- Since commodities are priced in dollars, a falling dollar causes commodity prices to increase
- Dollar peaked Q1 2002 → started major decline lasting through end of decade
- CRB Index turned up at exact same time dollar peaked
- The major bull market in commodities starting 2002 was a direct result of the Fed's weakening the dollar to battle deflation

## Chapter 5: The 2002 Falling Dollar Boosts Commodities

### Overview
- Dollar peak in 2002 led to major uptrend in commodities
- Gold experienced major upside breakout as dollar broke 7-year support line
- Gold ended a 20-year secular bear market just as stocks ended their secular bull market
- Commodities outperformed stocks for first time in two decades
- A peak in crude oil during March 2003 contributed to stock market upturn

### Commodities Inflate
- U.S. dollar hit final peak Q1 2002 → dropped sharply for rest of year and rest of decade
- CRB Index turned up at exact point dollar peaked (inverse relationship confirmed)
- Complete name: Thomson Reuters/Jefferies CRB Index
- Figure 5.1: Dollar formed head-and-shoulders top pattern (October 2000 to spring 2002)
- Head-and-shoulders top = three peaks with middle peak slightly higher → bearish pattern completed when trendline (neckline) under two reaction lows is broken
- Dollar fell below neckline April 2002 → began major decline
- Figure 5.2: CRB rose above its 200-day moving average April 2002 just as dollar was breaking down
- The longer a chart pattern has been forming, the more significant it becomes
- Figure 5.3: Dollar peak (down arrow) coincided exactly with CRB upturn (up arrow) during spring 2002
- CRB 2001 bottom was a successful retest of 1999 bottom → bullish double bottom pattern
- Double bottom = two prominent bottoms around same price level, completed when prices exceed middle peak

### Commodities Gain from Battle against Deflation
- Falling dollar is normally considered inflationary
- By end of 2002, CRB had risen to highest level in five years → evidence dollar decline was creating commodity inflation
- Government willing to devalue dollar → could only serve to boost commodity markets
- A Fed preoccupied with deflation would also be reluctant to raise interest rates → also bullish for commodities
- Commodity markets were big winners in the Fed's battle against deflation

### The Dollar Drop Leads to a New Bull Market in Gold
- Primary beneficiary of a falling dollar: gold and gold-mining shares
- Strong inverse relationship between dollar and gold (trend in opposite directions)
- Gold started rallying spring 2001, just as dollar was starting its peaking process
- Final dollar peak spring 2002 gave gold an even bigger boost → climbed back over $300 for first time in two years
- Gold-mining stocks rallied right along with bullion
- Gold benefited from BOTH falling dollar AND falling stock market

### Falling Stocks Are Also Good for Gold
- Gold peaked in 1980 at $700 → fell for 20 years
- Stock market bottomed 1982 → rose for two decades
- Gold and stocks usually trend in opposite directions
- S&P 500 peaked end of August 2000 → gold stocks bottomed within three months (November 2000)
- Gold turned up following April (2001) → bear market in stocks helped usher in new bull market in gold
- Gold does well during BOTH inflationary AND deflationary periods
- 1970s inflation: gold and mining shares soared
- 1929-1932 deflation: gold bullion was fixed price, but Homestake Mining gained 300% while stock market lost 90%
- Gold is considered an alternative to paper assets
- End of 20-year bull market in stocks coincided almost exactly with end of 20-year bear market in gold
- Dollar devaluation + worst bear market since Great Depression → gold became world's strongest asset for rest of decade
- Dollar was essentially devalued in 1933 when U.S. was taken off the gold standard

### Not a Lot of Alternatives
- After 20-year bull market in stocks ended, gold was one of few alternatives
- Stocks declining, interest rates at lowest in 40 years (fixed income unattractive), money market funds paying ~1%
- U.S. short-term rates lowest among major industrialized countries (except Japan at zero)
- Falling dollar made U.S. bonds and stocks less attractive to foreign investors
- Climate of falling stocks + falling dollar + historically low rates = perfect intermarket climate for gold

### Gold and the Dollar Experience Major Trend Changes
- KEY CHARTING TECHNIQUE: To validate a trend change, compare chart action of two related markets
- If gold is starting to rise, first check if dollar is starting to drop (confirms intermarket pattern)
- A minor trend change in one may not justify a major change in the other — magnitude should be similar
- Figure 5.4: Dollar broke its rising seven-year trendline in December 2002 — serious breakdown signal
- While daily charts are good for short-term trends, weekly and monthly charts are better for long-term trend analysis
- Figure 5.5: Gold rose above 1999 peak near $320 end of 2002 — five-year high = major upside breakout
- Upside breakout = price rises above a previous peak; the further back the peak, the more significant
- Both gold and dollar experiencing major trend changes at the same point in time, moving in opposite directions

### Shifting from Paper to Hard Assets
- Gold is often viewed as a proxy for the entire commodity sector
- Gold often changes direction before other commodities (due to stronger link to dollar)
- Gold surged over $700 in 1970s when commodities were in major uptrends; peaked 1980 as commodity bubble burst
- Commodity-related ETFs have made investing in gold/commodities much easier for average investor
- New millennium: gold and commodities attracting money for first time in 20 years — at expense of bonds and stocks

### The Stock Peak Coincides with Gold Bottom
- Gold prices trend in opposite direction of stocks
- Gold is a hedge against a falling stock market (whether threat is inflation like 1970s or deflation like 1930s)
- Gold peaked 1980 → 20-year bear market; Stocks bottomed 1982 → 20-year bull market
- S&P 500 peaked August 2000; gold stocks bottomed November 2000 (within 3 months); gold turned up April 2001
- 20-year bull market in stocks ended almost exactly as 20-year bear market in gold ended
- One reason Wall Street strategists are seldom bullish on gold: they know rising gold is usually bearish for stocks
- Figure 5.6: Gold bottom coincided with 2000 stock market top (20-year chart)

### Gold Breaks 15-Year Resistance Line
- Long-term charts most useful for spotting major trend changes

## Chapter 6: Asset Allocation Rotations Leading to 2007 Top

### Overview
- Relative strength ratio used to spot changes in leadership between asset classes
- Major asset allocation shift from paper to hard assets during 2002
- Bond/stock ratio tracked important changes in leadership during 2000, 2003, 2007, and 2009

### Relative Strength between Asset Classes / The Relative Strength Ratio
- Sector rotation = movement of funds into and out of various stock market sectors depending on business cycle stage
- Relative performance compares two asset classes (or markets) to determine which is stronger
- Relative strength ratio (RS line) = price of one market divided by price of another
- Rising RS line = numerator outperforming denominator
- Extremely useful for sector rotation strategies — better to be in sectors with rising ratios, out of falling ratios
- Any two markets can be compared with ratios
- Simple trendline analysis is usually sufficient to spot important turns in relative strength ratios

- CRB/S&P ratio (commodity/stock): falling for 20 years from 1980-2000 (stocks were stronger)

- 2007 stock market top led to financial meltdown threatening global financial system

- Four lagging groups with history of peaking first at market tops: small-cap stocks, financials, retailers, and transportation stocks (+ homebuilders)

- Stock market has history of peaking roughly six months ahead of economy

## Chapter 8: Intermarket Analysis and the Business Cycle

### The Four-Year Business Cycle
- American economy cycles through expansion and contraction averaging four years
- Stock market tends to bottom every four years (usually during midterm election) — the presidential cycle
- Last six four-year bottoms: 1990, 1994, 1998, 2002, 2006, 2010
- Earlier four-year bottoms: 1970, 1974, 1982, 1987
- Most bottoms occur in second half of year, coinciding with midterm congressional elections
- Stock Trader's Almanac: market performs better during second half of presidential terms
- Third year of president's term has historically seen strongest stock returns; fourth year second strongest
- First two years of a president's term are usually worst for stocks
- Rationale: presidents do everything to stimulate markets/economy in two years before next election
- Contraction phase often turns into recession (period of negative growth)
- Since WWII: 11 U.S. recessions, average duration 11 months
- Great Recession (Dec 2007 to June 2009) lasted 18 months — longest since Great Depression
- Last five recessions: stocks lost about a third on average; Great Recession: stocks lost half
- Stock market usually turns down before recession starts and turns up before recession ends

### The Business Cycle Explains Intermarket Rotation
- Business cycle explains chronological sequence among bonds, stocks, and commodities
- Near end of economic expansion: bonds first to turn down → stocks peak second → commodities third
- Happened during 2000-2001 and again during 2007-2008
- Order of troughs has been less reliable
- Before 1998: bond PRICES turned down before stocks and commodities; since 1998: bond YIELDS have been first to peak
- ASSET ALLOCATION BY BUSINESS CYCLE STAGE:
  - Economic strength → favor stocks
  - Economic weakness → favor bonds
  - Beginning of economic upswing → favor stocks
  - Latter part of expansion → favor commodities
  - Late expansion: inflation-sensitive groups (basic materials, gold, oil stocks) do well
  - Increased inflation pressures → Fed raises short-term rates → eventually hurts stocks and economy
- Falling dollar during 2002 caused commodities to bottom a year ahead of stocks
- Deflation scare spring 2003 caused bond yields to turn up three months AFTER stocks
- Traders usually rotate into commodities and their related stocks in the late stages of a bull market in stocks
- Energy stock leadership is almost always a bad sign for the rest of the market

### Lessons from 2000 and 2007
- 1999: oil tripled → Fed raised short-term rates → stock market top 2000 → recession 2001
- Commodities peaked start of 2001 (five months after stocks); bond yields peaked before stocks during 2007; commodities peaked mid-2008

### Oil Leads to Higher Rates from 2004 to 2006
- During 2004: oil rose above $40 for first time ever
- Fed embarked on series of short-term rate hikes lasting into 2006
- Rising oil + higher short-term rates → bond yields spiked 2005-2006 → helped end bull market in housing stocks
- Figure 8.2: Crude oil above $40 during 2004, then Fed raised short-term rates (up arrow) between 2004-2006
- TRADING APPLICATION: Knowing the normal peaking order offers advantages:
  - When bond yields peak → time to rotate from stocks into bonds
  - When stocks peak → commodities are alternate asset class to rotate into
  - Peaking process also alerts to likelihood of recession

### The 2001 Fed Easings Didn't Work
- After dot-com bust 2000: Fed lowered rates 12 times — didn't stem stock decline
- Bonds and stocks had completely decoupled (deflationary environment)
- Unusual ingredient: threat of global deflation not experienced since 1930s
- Current generation of investors/economists hadn't lived through that cycle → few recognized symptoms

### Comparisons to the 1920s and 1930s
- After inflationary WWI decade: commodities peaked 1920, initial drop, then flat through 1920s
- Commodities peaked 60 years later during 1980 (inflationary decade including Vietnam War)
- Bonds bottomed 1920 as commodities peaked; bonds bottomed 1981 one year after commodities peaked
- Stocks bottomed 1921 (one year after bonds turned up); stocks bottomed 1982 (one year after bonds)
- 1920s: bonds and stocks rose while commodities remained flat (similar to 1980-2000)
- Bond prices peaked 1928, a year before stocks → stock market peak 1929 → stock prices plunged while bond prices rose
- That major decoupling of bonds and stocks was caused by commodity action: starting late 1929, commodities (flat through 1920s) began major decline as stocks peaked
- Plunge in commodity prices turned benign disinflation into harmful deflation (similar to 1998)
- In deflationary climate: bond prices rise while stocks and commodities fall together
- Stocks and commodities peaked together 1929, bottomed together 1932, stayed closely linked through 1930s
- Quote from Murphy's 2004 book: "When deflation is the main threat, stocks and commodities become closely correlated... rising commodity prices are considered a plus for stocks and the economy"

### Rotating Asset Classes over Decades
- Rotating leadership between bonds, stocks, commodities over long periods:
  - WWI inflationary decade: commodities strongest
  - Disinflationary 1920s: leadership passed to stocks
  - Deflationary 1930s: bonds became strongest asset
  - Post-WWII to late 1960s: stocks resumed leadership
  - Inflationary 1970s: commodities back on center stage
  - After 1980 commodity peak: stocks and bonds came back into favor
  - Deflationary cycle starting late 1990s: shifted leadership from stocks back to bonds
- By end of 2011: long-term government bonds had gained 11.5% per year over prior three decades, beating S&P 500's 10.8%
- First time bonds outperformed stocks over a 30-year period since the Civil War
- Most of that generational bond strength took place after 2000 — reflecting global deflationary tendencies
- Bond cycles rarely last more than 30 years → suggests current cycle is nearing an end

### Lessons of Long Cycles
- Each asset class has long periods of outperformance lasting decades
- Early stages of long-term expansion: rising commodity prices are positive for stocks (a little inflation is good)
- When commodity prices SPIKE higher (1970s): little inflation turns to BIG inflation — bad for stocks
- Rising commodity prices can be good for stocks; SOARING commodity prices are bad
- Conversely, falling commodity prices can also be good stocks
- Commodity prices peaked in 1920 and 1980, remained relatively flat afterward → declining/flat commodities ushered in eras of disinflation → stocks did well (1920s, 1980-2000)
- COLLAPSING commodity prices are bad for stocks (1929, 1998) → turn disinflation into deflation

### The Kondratieff Wave
- Long cycle of economic activity lasting approximately 55-60 years
- Discovered in 1920s by Nikolai Kondratieff, Russian economist
- Tracked from 1789, found three major peaks with third in 1920
- Four Kondratieff Wave peaks over two centuries: 1816, 1864, 1920, 1980
- Each expansion led to burst of inflation (rising commodities and rates)
- All four inflations coincided with major American wars (War of 1812, Civil War, WWI, Vietnam War)
- Each burst of inflation ended with peak in commodity prices
- After peaking, commodities plateau for a decade or longer — stocks do especially well during this disinflation period
- 60 years between 1920 and 1980 commodity peaks fits model very closely
- DANGER POINT: when commodity prices end their plateau and start falling again → deflationary forces exert negative influence on stocks and economy (happened 1929 and 1998)
- Interest rates also fall during deflationary part of cycle
- 30-year drop in bond yields after 1980 matched by similar decline 1920-1940s

### Dividing a Lifetime Cycle into Seasons (Ian Gordon's framework)
- Spring (1949-1966): strengthening economy + benign inflation → stocks do well
- Summer (1966-1980): inflationary period with rising commodity and real estate values
- Autumn (starting 1980): greatest speculation in bonds, stocks, real estate + massive debt buildup
- Winter (starting 2000): deflation (collapse in commodity prices), debt repayment, stock/real estate plunge
- Best two defenses during Kondratieff winter: bonds and gold
- Gold and bonds have been the two strongest assets over the last decade
- Kondratieff Wave ~60 years = lifetime cycle (most people live through it only once → unprepared for onset)
- Winter can last ~15 years on average; after debt repaid and confidence restored → spring begins again

### Housing Is Interest Rate Sensitive
- Real estate/housing closely tied to interest rate direction
- Housing is countercyclical — moves opposite to normal business cycle
- Homebuilding strongest when rates are falling (generally weaker economy)
- Housing stocks turned up in 2000 and rose through bear market and recession — reacting to falling rates more than falling stocks
- Tight monetary policy restrains real estate
- Raising of short-term rates 2005-2006 helped end housing boom of prior decade
- Figure 8.3: Jump in bond yields 2005 coincided with peak in homebuilding stocks (Pulte Group)
- Homebuilding rally started during 2000 when bond yields plunged
- Breaking of three-year down trendline in bond yields (2005) raised fears of higher mortgage rates
- Mortgage rates are determined by Treasury bond yields

### Real Estate Doesn't Always Follow Rates
- 1930s: real estate collapsed despite falling long-term rates
- 1970s: housing boom occurred while long-term rates were RISING (inflationary impact overcame higher rates)
- 1980s: housing weakened as rates dropped
- Real estate's link to inflation appears greater than link to interest rates

### Real Estate Doesn't Always Follow Inflation
- Real estate is also considered an inflation hedge — often but not always the case
- Home prices fell 1925-1935 (Great Depression); didn't start rising until late 1940s
- Farm values plunged in 1930s as agricultural commodity prices collapsed
- Home values soared during inflationary 1970s, peaked with inflation early 1980s
- Farmland plunged after 1981 when agricultural commodities peaked
- Housing boom 1998-2005 couldn't be tied to inflation (inflation was low, deflation pressures emerging)
- None of the historical reasons (inflation, rates, business cycle) fully explain real estate cycles
- Real estate and housing appear to move according to a different cycle than other financial markets

### The 18-Year Real Estate Cycle
- Clarence Long discovered 18-year real estate cycle in 1940, tracking from 1870 to 1940
- 18 years peak to peak (or trough to trough)
- Three real estate cycles fit in one Kondratieff Wave
- Each real estate cycle encompasses four normal business cycles
- Kondratieff Wave and Long's 18-year cycle both turned down in late 1920s → convergence of two major cycles caused collapse in stock values, home/land values, commodities, interest rates
- 2000-2002 bear market: only Kondratieff Wave turned down; real estate cycle kept rising → cushioned economic downturn during 2001 recession
- Figure 8.4: Falling housing stocks made 2008 stock plunge worse than 2000
- Homebuilders rose during 2000-2002 bear market (because of falling rates) → cushioned economic downturn
- After collapse in housing stocks during 2006, the 2008 stock plunge was a lot more severe

### The Real Estate Peak Was Overdue
- 18-year cycles from 1927 peak and 1945 trough → major real estate upturns scheduled for 1970s and 1990s (both arrived on schedule)
- Major real estate peak scheduled for 1981 (arrived on time)
- Last major real estate trough: 1990-1992 → next cycle bottom scheduled 2008-2010
- The last real estate upswing lasted 13-15 years (unusually long) — probably because of unprecedented drop in long-term rates to lowest in half a century
- Ideal 18-year cycle: 9 up years followed by 9 down years
- Best way to measure health of housing industry: study the trend of homebuilding stocks
- Figure 8.5: Dow Jones Home Construction Index 2000-2012
- Rallied 2000-2005, turned down 2006 → bottomed late 2008/early 2009 → rose above falling trendline → trended sideways (bottoming pattern) → second bottom H2 2011 → rallied to 2010 high by Q1 2012
- Index exceeded 2010 peak June 2012 → established new uptrend → good news for housing
- The 2005 peak in housing and 2006 downturn: for first time since Great Depression, both 60-year Kondratieff Wave AND 18-year real estate cycle were falling together → convergence made downturns more severe and upswings less robust
- The good news: downturn in those longer economic cycles may be nearing completion

### Economic Cycles Set the Framework for Intermarket Work
- Not enough to just look at four-year business cycle — must put it in longer historical perspective
- Those who don't understand longer cycles remain baffled as to why last two business cycles have been so different
- Traditional fiscal and monetary steps to strengthen the business cycle have been mostly unsuccessful because of deflationary headwinds from longer cycles

## Chapter 9: The Impact of the Business Cycle on Market Sectors

### Sector Rotation within the Business Cycle
- Different market sectors do better at different stages of the business cycle
- Two goals: (1) anticipate which sectors to upgrade/downgrade based on business cycle, (2) use sector rotations to determine position of business cycle
- Money flows from sector to sector in repetitive pattern as economy goes from expansion to contraction

### Sector Rotations during 2000 Favored Contraction
- 2000: Oil spike in 1999 → Fed rate hikes → energy stock leadership → defensive rotation into consumer staples
- Energy and staples being the two top sectors during H1 2000 = bearish signal
- Market leadership by consumer staple stocks is almost always a negative warning sign for the stock market

### Sector Rotations during 2003 Favored Expansion
- 2003: Consumer discretionary and technology showed market leadership — bullish signal
- Consumer staples became weakest sector (sign of increasing investor confidence)
- Consumer discretionary strong + consumer staples weak = good news for stock market and economy

### Technology Leadership Is Another Good Sign
- October 2002 (market bottom) to June 2003: Nasdaq +37%, Russell 2000 Small Cap +24%, S&P 500 +19%
- Technology-dominated Nasdaq leading market higher = confirming sign of new bull market
- Nasdaq market is usually viewed as proxy for technology sector

### Smaller Stocks Lead at Bottoms
- Russell 2000 Small Cap Index in leadership role during 2003 — positive sign
- Smaller stocks usually lead at important market bottoms and when economy is coming out of recession
- Six recessions between 1960-1991: small stocks outperformed large in first year following each recession
- Same pattern following the two recessions after 2000

### Transportation Leadership
- Economically sensitive transports tend to lead at market bottoms (if economy is going to produce goods, it has to transport them)
- 1999: rising oil → energy stocks in leadership → transports weaken
- After economic slowdown: oil falls (as it usually does in recession) → energy stocks weaken alongside commodity → weaker energy + stronger transports = sign things are getting better

## Chapter 11: The Dollar and Commodities Trend in Opposite Directions

### Overview
- Inverse correlation between dollar and commodity prices = one of the most consistent and reliable intermarket relationships
- Close positive link between commodities and foreign currencies
- Correlation Coefficient measures relationship strength and when it's weakening
- Gold doesn't always act like other commodities (dual role as commodity AND currency)
- Gold isn't just world's strongest commodity — also world's strongest currency

### Both Markets Need to Be Analyzed Together
- In 20 years between 1970-1990: every important turn in commodity prices was either preceded by or coincided with a turn in the dollar (opposite direction)
- During bear market years of 1990 and 1994: falling dollar contributed to rising commodities which weakened bonds and stocks
- Dollar upturn after those bearish years pulled commodity prices lower → helped boost bonds and stocks
- It's a mistake to analyze one without the other

### The Rising Dollar Contributed to the 1997-1998 Commodity Collapse
- Dollar started rising 1995 (after stealth bear market of 1994), rallied through balance of decade
- By 1996: rising dollar having negative impact on commodities (started dropping)
- Dollar often changes direction before commodities — becomes valuable leading indicator
- Asian currency crisis 1997: collapse in Asian currencies pushed money into relative safety of dollar and Treasuries
- Dollar spike during 1997-1998 was major contributing factor to commodity plunge (intensified deflation fears)
- During times of global financial crisis: money usually flows to relative safety of U.S. dollar and Treasury bonds
- Fed adopted same plan used during 1930s to boost commodity prices (especially gold) by weakening the dollar
- Within five years of 1997 Asian crisis: Fed embarked on plan to weaken dollar and boost commodities

### The Falling Dollar from 2002 to 2008 Pushed Commodities Higher
- Dollar peaked 2002, started major decline lasting until 2008
- CRB Index turned up at exact point dollar peaked — falling dollar contributed to six-year commodity upturn
- Figure 11.2: Dollar peak led to commodity bottom during 2002; falling dollar contributed to commodity rise through mid-2008

### The Dollar Bottom during 2008 Contributed to Commodity Plunge
- Stocks peaked October 2007 → commodities kept rising until mid-2008 (falling dollar was main factor keeping commodity rally going)
- Dollar bottom mid-2008 → finally contributed to commodity price collapse
- Figure 11.3: CRB lost more than 50% of value from mid-2008 to start of 2009 (far outpaced 30% drop during 1997-1998)
- Although CRB drop (-50%) was far greater than dollar rise (+20%), the dollar bottom was a major contributing factor
- Deflationary implications of 2008 commodity collapse invoked comparisons to 1930s
- While commodity traders need to keep eye on dollar, dollar traders also need to keep eye on commodity markets

### Dollar Peaks in 2009 and 2010 Lifted Commodities
- Figure 11.4: Two dollar peaks during 2009 and 2010 coincided with two commodity bottoms
- First dollar peak (March 2009): coincided with CRB turning back up; also coincided with stock market bottom
- Second dollar peak (June 2010): led to another commodity upturn that lasted a year
- Dollar bottom in 2011 coincided with commodity peak
- In each instance: change in dollar direction coincided with change in commodity direction (opposite)

### The Dollar Bottom in 2011 Pushed Commodities Lower
- Dollar formed major bottom during spring 2011 → started to rally
- CRB Index peaked at same time → turned lower
- Figure 11.5: Dollar bottom during April-May 2011 coincided with commodity peak; both trends continued for rest of year
- Commodities lost about 20% from their spring peak by end of 2011

### Correlation Coefficient
- Correlation coefficient measures the degree of correlation between two markets
- Ranges from +1.0 (perfect positive correlation — move in same direction) to -1.0 (perfect negative correlation — move in opposite directions)
- Zero = no correlation
- Readings above +0.50 suggest strong positive correlation; below -0.50 suggest strong negative correlation
- Figure 11.6: Correlation coefficient between dollar and CRB ranged between -0.50 and -0.90 over most of last decade — confirming strong inverse relationship
- Useful for confirming normal intermarket relationships AND for spotting when those relationships are weakening
- Weakening correlation can be a sign that something is changing in the intermarket environment

### Gold Isn't Like Other Commodities
- Gold has a dual role: it's both a commodity AND an alternate currency
- Gold's closer link to the dollar (compared to other commodities) is due to its currency role
- Gold often leads other commodities at turning points because of its stronger dollar sensitivity
- Gold and foreign currencies both benefit from a falling dollar → they trend in the same direction
- But gold has historically done BETTER than foreign currencies during periods of dollar weakness
- Gold outperformed the euro (the dollar's main competitor) during the decade after 2002

### Commodities Are Linked to Foreign Currencies
- Commodities and foreign currencies both benefit from a falling dollar → positive correlation between them
- Commodity-linked currencies (AUD, CAD) get a double boost: from falling dollar AND rising commodity prices that boost their export-dependent economies
- Gold and foreign currencies both rise together when dollar drops, but not necessarily at same pace

### Gold Outperforms the Euro
- Over the decade from 2002-2012: gold vastly outperformed the euro
- Gold gained about 490% while the euro gained much less
- Gold's outperformance explained by its dual role as commodity AND currency → benefits from two tailwinds simultaneously
- When foreign currencies like the euro start to weaken (as happened during European debt crisis), gold can still rise while currencies fall → gold is seen as safer than any individual currency

### Gold Outpaces Other Commodities
- Gold has also outperformed the broader CRB commodity index by a wide margin since 2001
- Gold/CRB ratio rose steadily → gold gaining more than other commodities
- Further evidence of gold's dual role — it benefits from commodity tailwinds AND currency/safe-haven tailwinds

### Gold versus Foreign Currencies
- All three (gold, foreign currencies, commodities) benefit from falling dollar
- But gold benefits the most → outperforms both foreign currencies and other commodities
- Gold is the world's strongest commodity AND the world's strongest currency
- When both commodities and currencies are rising (falling dollar), gold gets a double benefit
- When commodities fall but currencies also fall (flight to safety), gold still holds up better than either

### The Dollar's Impact on Other Intermarket Trends
- Dollar direction has a ripple effect across all four asset classes:
  - Falling dollar → rising commodities → rising inflation pressures → falling bond prices → eventually hurts stocks
  - Rising dollar → falling commodities → lower inflation → rising bond prices → eventually helps stocks
- Dollar direction also influences:
  - Relative performance of U.S. vs foreign stocks
  - Direction of emerging market stocks (tied to commodities)
  - Gold and gold mining stocks
  - Energy stocks and basic materials
- The dollar is the starting point for much of intermarket analysis

## Chapter 12: Stocks and Commodities Become Highly Correlated

### Another Side Effect of the Deflationary Environment
- One of the most important intermarket changes since 2008: stocks and commodities have become highly correlated (moving in same direction)
- Before the deflationary era: stocks and commodities had a much weaker (or inverse) correlation
- The 2008 financial meltdown tightened the link between stocks and commodities because both became tied to the global economic cycle
- Rising commodities (like copper and oil) signaled economic strength → good for stocks
- Falling commodities signaled economic weakness → bad for stocks
- This is a "new normal" relationship that didn't exist before 1998

### Commodities Lost Half Their Value in Just Six Months
- From mid-2008 to early 2009: CRB Index lost more than 50% of its value
- That commodity collapse was far more severe than the 30% drop during 1997-1998
- Deflationary implications invoked comparisons to the 1930s
- Raised fears of another Great Depression

### Stock and Commodities Became Closely Correlated after 2008
- Figure 12.1: S&P 500 and CRB Index overlaid from 2007 to 2012 — the two lines track each other closely
- Before 2008: the correlation between stocks and commodities was much weaker
- After 2008: correlation coefficient between the two has remained consistently above +0.50 (often above +0.70)
- This new positive correlation is a direct result of the deflationary environment
- In a deflationary climate: rising commodity prices are considered a plus for stocks and the economy
- Falling commodity prices suggest economic weakness → bad for stocks
- This mirrors what happened during the deflationary 1930s when stocks and commodities also moved together

### Copper Influences Stock Market Direction
- Copper is often viewed as a leading indicator of economic activity ("Dr. Copper")
- Copper has many industrial uses → its price direction reflects global manufacturing demand
- Figure 12.2: Strong positive correlation between copper prices and S&P 500 since 2008
- When copper is rising → usually positive for stocks; when copper is falling → usually negative
- Copper can sometimes turn before the stock market → useful as a leading indicator

### The Silver/Gold Ratio Influences the Stock Market
- Silver is both a precious metal AND an industrial metal (unlike gold, which is primarily monetary)
- Silver/gold ratio = price of silver divided by price of gold
- Rising silver/gold ratio = silver outperforming gold = industrial demand is strong = bullish for stocks
- Falling silver/gold ratio = gold outperforming silver = safe-haven demand dominates = bearish for stocks
- Figure 12.3: Strong positive correlation between silver/gold ratio and S&P 500
- Silver/gold ratio is a useful indicator of risk appetite in the market

### Silver Stocks Led Commodity Stocks Lower during 2011
- During spring 2011: silver collapsed from near $50 to around $30
- Silver stocks (like SLV) led commodity stocks lower → preceded broader market weakness
- This was consistent with the silver/gold ratio signaling a shift toward risk aversion

### The Influence of Commodities on Sector Performance
- Rising commodities favor basic materials and energy sectors
- Falling commodities hurt those sectors but can benefit consumer-oriented sectors (lower input costs)
- The commodity/stock correlation means that rising commodity prices generally support a bullish sector rotation (into cyclicals and materials)
- Falling commodities support defensive rotation (into staples, healthcare, utilities)

### Commodities Led Stocks Lower during 2011
- Commodities peaked spring 2011 → led stocks lower
- The commodity peak preceded the stock market peak by a few weeks
- This was unusual because normally commodities peak AFTER stocks
- The strong correlation between the two meant that commodity weakness directly dragged stocks lower

### The Commodity Peak Also Influenced Sector Rotations
- The 2011 commodity peak caused a rotation out of basic materials and energy stocks
- Money flowed into defensive sectors (staples, healthcare, utilities)
- This defensive rotation was a warning of broader market weakness

### Gold Stocks versus Gold
- Gold miners (GDX) and gold bullion (GLD) generally trend in same direction
- But gold miners are also STOCKS → affected by stock market trends
- When stock market is falling, gold miners can fall even as gold bullion rises → miners are affected by equity risk
- Gold miners are stocks first and gold proxies second

### Gold Miners Are Stocks
- Gold miners belong to the stock market → influenced by stock market trends
- They provide leveraged exposure to gold prices (rising faster than gold when gold rises, falling faster when it falls)
- But stock market weakness can override gold strength → miners fall even when bullion holds up

### Gold Shares Underperform Bullion during 2011
- During 2011: gold bullion hit new record highs while gold mining stocks actually fell
- This divergence was due to the broader stock market weakness pulling miners down
- When gold miners underperform bullion, it's often a negative signal for both gold and stocks

### Gold and Miners Relink during July
- After diverging, gold miners and bullion re-established their normal positive correlation later in 2011
- This relinking often occurs after major market dislocations

### Dollar Direction Impacts Foreign Stocks
- Rising dollar → negative for foreign stock ETFs (because local currency returns are reduced when converted to dollars)
- Falling dollar → positive for foreign stock ETFs
- Currency trends impact foreign ETFs MORE than they impact the underlying local stock markets
- This means dollar direction should be a key factor when deciding between U.S. and foreign stock allocation

## Chapter 13: Stocks and the Dollar

### A Weak Historic Link between the Two
- Unlike the strong dollar/commodity inverse link, the dollar/stock relationship has been weaker and less consistent historically
- Before 2000: the correlation between dollar and stocks was relatively weak (sometimes positive, sometimes negative)
- Since 2000 (deflationary era): the relationship has become more consistently negative — falling dollar has tended to coincide with falling stocks, and vice versa
- But this negative correlation has not been as reliable as the dollar/commodity inverse link

### A Long-Term Comparison of Stocks and the Dollar
- Over very long periods: there's no consistent correlation between stocks and the dollar
- During the 1990s: both the dollar and stocks rose together (positive correlation)
- From 2002-2007: stocks rose while the dollar fell (negative correlation)
- The relationship changes over time depending on the dominant economic regime

### Stocks and the Dollar Become Negatively Correlated
- Since 2008: stocks and the dollar have developed a more consistently negative correlation
- When the dollar rises (risk-off/flight to safety): stocks tend to fall
- When the dollar falls (risk-on): stocks tend to rise
- This negative correlation is another manifestation of the deflationary/risk-on-risk-off environment
- In a risk-off environment: money flows into the dollar and Treasuries, out of stocks and commodities
- In a risk-on environment: money flows out of the dollar/Treasuries and into stocks and commodities

### The Commodity Impact on the Dollar-Stock Link
- The key connector between stocks and the dollar is COMMODITIES
- Since stocks and commodities are now closely correlated (post-2008), and commodities and the dollar are inversely correlated, it follows that stocks and the dollar are negatively correlated
- The chain: Falling dollar → Rising commodities → Rising stocks (and vice versa)
- The commodity link explains why the dollar-stock relationship has strengthened since 2008

### The Dollar Bottom during 2011 Hurts Stocks
- Dollar bottomed spring 2011 → started to rise
- At the same time: commodities peaked and stocks peaked
- All three relationships held: rising dollar, falling commodities, falling stocks
- Figure 13.1: Dollar bottom in spring 2011 coincided with stock market peak

### The Dollar Impact on Foreign Stocks
- A rising dollar is ESPECIALLY negative for foreign stocks priced in dollars
- Two forces hit foreign stock ETFs when dollar rises: (1) local stock prices may fall, and (2) currency translation makes it worse
- When dollar falls: foreign stock ETFs get a double boost (local prices rise + currency translation adds return)
- TRADING IMPLICATION: When dollar is falling → overweight foreign stocks; when dollar is rising → overweight U.S. stocks
- A rising dollar favors U.S. stocks; a weaker dollar favors foreign stocks

### Commodities Are Linked to Emerging Markets
- Emerging market economies are often heavily dependent on commodity exports
- Rising commodity prices → stronger emerging market economies → rising emerging market stocks
- Falling commodity prices → weaker emerging market economies → falling emerging market stocks
- Figure 13.2: Strong correlation between CRB Index and Emerging Market iShares (EEM)
- Emerging markets are a good way to play the commodity trend through equities

### China Influences Copper Trend
- China is the world's biggest consumer of copper and many other industrial commodities
- Chinese economic growth (or slowdown) has a major impact on commodity prices, especially copper
- Monitoring Chinese stock market can provide early clues about commodity direction
- When Chinese stocks weaken → often signals reduced commodity demand → negative for copper and other industrial commodities

### Chinese Stocks Influence the S&P 500
- Since 2008: Chinese stocks have led U.S. stocks at several important turning points
- Chinese market peaked before the S&P 500 during 2007
- Chinese market bottomed before the S&P 500 during 2008-2009
- The link runs through commodities: Chinese demand drives commodity prices, which influence the global economic outlook

### Europe Is Also Important
- European stock markets are closely correlated with U.S. stocks
- European economic weakness (particularly the sovereign debt crisis starting 2010) has had a negative impact on global stock markets
- European bank weakness is particularly concerning because of its interconnections with the global financial system

### Currency Trends Impact Foreign ETFs More
- Currency trends affect foreign ETFs more than they affect the underlying local stock markets
- When the dollar is rising sharply: foreign ETFs fall even if local stock markets are flat or slightly positive
- This is because the ETF price is converted back to dollars → strong dollar erodes dollar-denominated returns
- IMPORTANT for traders: Always consider the dollar trend when trading foreign stock ETFs

- Example of using chart support levels on foreign ETFs
- When a foreign ETF holds a prior support level → bullish; when it breaks below → bearish

### EMU iShares Diverge from Euro
- European stocks and the euro don't always move in lockstep
- During certain periods: European stocks can rise even as the euro falls (if ECB easing is boosting local stocks)
- The divergence can create opportunities for traders who monitor both

### EAFE and Emerging iShares Stabilize at End of 2011
- Both developed and emerging foreign ETFs formed potential bottoms near the end of 2011
- This stabilization coincided with a pause in the dollar rally

### Don't Forget about Canada
- Canadian market is heavily commodity-dependent (energy and mining)
- Canadian dollar (loonie) is closely correlated with commodity prices
- When commodities are rising: Canadian stocks and the loonie tend to outperform

### The Canadian Dollar versus the Euro
- Canadian dollar and euro both benefit from falling U.S. dollar
- But they don't always move together: Canadian dollar is more commodity-linked while euro is more tied to European economic conditions
- During European debt crisis: CAD held up better than euro because of Canada's commodity-export economy

### Canadian Markets and Commodities
- Toronto Stock Exchange (TSX) is one of the most commodity-sensitive stock markets in the world
- When commodities are rising: TSX tends to outperform S&P 500
- When commodities are falling: TSX tends to underperform

### How to Add the Americas to Your Foreign Portfolio
- For foreign stock exposure: consider both developed (EAFE) and emerging (EEM) markets
- Add Canada separately (not included in EAFE; small weight in VEU)
- Latin American exposure comes through emerging market ETFs or individual country funds (Brazil EWZ, Mexico EWW)

## Chapter 14: The Link between Bonds and Stocks

### The Two Markets Compete for Investor Funds
- Bonds and stocks are always competing for investor money
- When investors are optimistic → they favor stocks over bonds
- When investors are pessimistic → they favor bonds (especially Treasuries) over stocks
- The bond/stock ratio tracks these shifts in investor preference

### The Positive Correlation between Bond Yield and Stocks
- Since 1998: bond YIELDS and stocks have been positively correlated (rise and fall together)
- This is the "new normal" — before 1998, bond PRICES and stocks rose together
- Falling bond yields (rising bond prices) since 1998 have been BAD for stocks
- This is because falling yields in the deflationary era signal economic weakness, not easier monetary policy
- TRADING RULE: Watch bond yields — if yields are falling → be cautious on stocks; if yields are rising → more bullish on stocks

### Bond Yield Leads Stocks Lower during 2010 and 2011
- Bond yields peaked in early 2010 and again in early 2011 → in both cases, stocks peaked shortly afterward
- Bond yields continue to act as a LEADING INDICATOR for stocks
- The lead time has varied from a few weeks to several months
- Figure 14.1: Bond yield turning down before stock market during 2010 and 2011

### The Falling Bond Yield Boosts Dividend-Paying Stocks
- When bond yields fall: dividend-paying stocks become more attractive (higher relative yield)
- This benefits defensive sectors: utilities, consumer staples, healthcare, REITs
- Investors searching for yield rotate from bonds into dividend-paying stocks
- This is one of the reasons why defensive sectors outperform during periods of falling yields
- When bond yields fall sharply, investors favor dividend-paying stocks in the search for a higher yield

### Consumer Staples and Utilities Thrive on Rising Volatility
- During periods of market stress and falling yields: consumer staples and utilities tend to outperform
- These sectors benefit from: (1) defensive money flows, (2) yield attraction as bond yields fall, (3) lower beta (less volatile than market)

### Not All Bonds Are the Same
- Treasury bonds are the safe haven benchmark
- Corporate bonds are more closely tied to economic health of issuing companies
- High-yield (junk) bonds act more like stocks than bonds
- During risk-off periods: Treasuries rise while high-yield bonds fall
- The spread between Treasury yields and corporate/high-yield yields widens during stress
- Widening credit spreads = increasing fear in the market = negative for stocks

### Some Bond Prices Can Trend in Opposite Directions
- Treasury bonds and high-yield bonds can move in opposite directions
- During 2008 crisis: Treasuries soared while junk bonds plunged
- This divergence is a sign of extreme stress in the financial system
- Monitoring the relative performance of Treasury vs. junk bonds provides insight into market risk appetite

### Quantitative Easing
- QE = Fed buying bonds to push down long-term interest rates
- QE1 (2008-2010), QE2 (2010-2011): Fed purchased Treasury bonds and mortgage-backed securities
- Goal: lower long-term rates to stimulate borrowing and economic activity
- Side effect: weakened the dollar → boosted commodities and commodity-linked assets

### The Impact of Quantitative Easing on Bonds and Stocks
- Counterintuitively: during QE programs, bond YIELDS often rose (and bond prices fell)
- This is because QE boosted economic confidence → investors rotated from bonds into stocks
- When QE ended: yields often fell again (and bond prices rose) as the economic boost faded
- Stocks tended to rally during QE and weaken when QE ended
- "Don't fight the Fed" — when the Fed is actively easing, it tends to be positive for stocks

### Operation Twist
- Fed program in 2011-2012: selling short-term Treasuries and buying long-term Treasuries
- Goal: flatten the yield curve by pushing down long-term rates specifically
- Different from QE because it didn't increase the overall size of the Fed's balance sheet

### The Yield Curve
- Yield curve = comparison of short-term vs long-term interest rates
- Normal yield curve: long-term rates higher than short-term (upward sloping)
- Flat yield curve: short and long rates roughly equal
- Inverted yield curve: short-term rates higher than long-term → DANGER SIGNAL for economy and stocks
- Every recession since 1970 has been preceded by an inverted yield curve
- Steepening yield curve (long rates rising faster than short): generally positive for economy and stocks
- Flattening yield curve: generally a warning sign

### The Impact of Quantitative Easing on the Yield Curve
- QE programs tended to steepen the yield curve (by pushing short-term rates to zero while long-term rates held up or rose)
- A steeper yield curve is generally positive for banks (which borrow short and lend long)
- Operation Twist had the opposite effect: it flattened the curve by pushing down long-term rates

### Bond Yield and Stocks Diverge at the Start of 2012
- Early 2012: stocks started to rally while bond yields remained low
- This divergence was unusual given the positive correlation between the two since 1998
- It raised the question of whether the Fed's manipulation of rates was distorting the normal intermarket relationship

### TIPS and Gold Rise Together
- TIPS (Treasury Inflation Protected Securities) and gold both benefit from inflation expectations
- When inflation expectations rise: TIPS outperform regular Treasuries and gold tends to rise
- When inflation expectations fall: TIPS underperform and gold may weaken
- The TIPS/Treasury ratio is a useful measure of inflation expectations

### The Pendulum Swings Back to Stocks at the Start of 2012
- Early 2012 saw a rotation from bonds back into stocks
- Bond yields started to inch higher → positive sign for stocks
- Sector rotations also turned bullish: technology and consumer discretionary leading

### The Fed Launches QE3
- September 2012: Fed announced QE3 (open-ended bond buying of $85 billion per month)
- Unlike QE1 and QE2, QE3 had no set end date
- Initially positive for stocks and commodities, negative for the dollar

## Chapter 15: The Link between Bonds and Commodities

### One of the Traditional Relationships
- Bond prices and commodity prices normally trend in opposite directions — one of the oldest intermarket relationships
- Rising commodities → inflation pressures → bond prices fall (yields rise)
- Falling commodities → deflationary pressure → bond prices rise (yields fall)
- This inverse relationship has been one of the most reliable over the decades
- Bond yields and commodity prices normally trend in the SAME direction

### Bond and Commodity Prices Normally Trend in Opposite Directions
- Figure 15.1: Long-term chart showing Treasury bond price and CRB Index trending in opposite directions from 1980s through 2000s
- Commodity peak in 1980 → led to major bond bull market (falling yields, rising prices)
- Commodity peak in 2008 → bond prices surged as commodities plunged
- This inverse relationship is one of the foundations of intermarket analysis

### The Inverse Bond-Commodity Link between 2003 and 2006
- During 2003-2006: the inverse relationship held reasonably well
- Commodities were rising → bond prices were falling (yields rising)
- Figure 15.2: CRB and Treasury bond trending in opposite directions during 2003-2006

### Why They Changed during 2007
- During 2007: the normal inverse relationship started to break down
- Commodities kept rising (driven by falling dollar and emerging market demand)
- But bond prices ALSO rose (driven by flight to safety as subprime crisis unfolded)
- Both were rising for different reasons → the correlation weakened
- This was a sign that something unusual was happening in the financial system

### Copper versus Corn during 2002
- Different commodities sometimes move differently depending on the economic environment
- Industrial commodities (copper) are more sensitive to economic growth
- Agricultural commodities (corn) are more influenced by weather and supply factors
- This means the CRB Index can mask different dynamics within the commodity complex

### A Comparison of Copper and Treasury Bond Prices
- Copper and Treasury bonds often show a cleaner inverse relationship than the broader CRB
- Because copper is primarily an industrial metal, it's more directly tied to economic growth
- When copper is rising → economy is strengthening → bond prices typically fall
- When copper is falling → economy is weakening → bond prices typically rise

### The Copper Bottom during 2009 Contributed to the Bond Top
- Copper bottomed early 2009 (along with stocks) → started to rally sharply
- At the same time: Treasury bond prices peaked and started to decline
- Copper's upturn signaled economic recovery → money rotated from safe-haven bonds into riskier assets
- Figure 15.3: Copper bottom in early 2009 coincided with Treasury bond top

### The Thomson Reuters/Jefferies CRB Index
- The CRB Index has been restructured several times over the years
- Its composition can influence how well it correlates with other markets
- The current version is more heavily weighted toward energy than previous versions
- Some analysts prefer to use the equal-weighted commodity index or individual commodities for cleaner intermarket signals

### The CRB Index/Treasury Bond Ratio
- CRB/Treasury bond ratio is a useful tool for tracking the relative strength between commodities and bonds
- Rising ratio = commodities outperforming bonds = inflationary environment = generally bullish for stocks
- Falling ratio = bonds outperforming commodities = deflationary environment = generally bearish for stocks
- This ratio has been a reliable indicator of the inflation/deflation balance

### The Commodity/Bond Ratio Since 2008
- After 2008: the commodity/bond ratio fell sharply as the deflationary plunge hit commodities
- The ratio bottomed in early 2009 along with stocks and commodities
- Since then: the ratio has recovered but remained below its pre-2008 levels → suggesting deflationary headwinds still present

### The CRB/Bond Ratio Influences Stocks
- When the CRB/bond ratio is rising: stocks tend to do well (inflationary/growth environment)
- When the ratio is falling: stocks tend to struggle (deflationary/recessionary environment)
- The ratio acts as a barometer of the inflation/deflation balance, which directly impacts stock market performance

### The History of Commodity/Bond Ratio Influence on Stocks
- The positive correlation between the CRB/bond ratio and stocks has existed for decades
- During the 1970s: rising ratio (commodities outperforming bonds) corresponded with periods of stock strength within that inflationary era
- During the 1980s-1990s: falling ratio (bonds outperforming commodities) was associated with the great bull market in stocks (disinflation was positive)
- Since 2000: the ratio's influence on stocks has become more pronounced due to the deflationary environment

### The CRB/Bond Ratio Also Influences Sector Rotation
- Rising CRB/bond ratio favors: energy, basic materials, industrials (inflation beneficiaries)
- Falling CRB/bond ratio favors: utilities, consumer staples, healthcare (deflation beneficiaries)
- The ratio can be used to guide sector allocation decisions

### The CRB/Bond Ratio Also Influences Emerging Markets
- Emerging markets are heavily commodity-dependent
- When the CRB/bond ratio rises: emerging market stocks tend to outperform
- When the ratio falls: emerging markets tend to underperform
- This makes the CRB/bond ratio a useful tool for allocating between developed and emerging markets

### Commodity Inflation versus Bond Deflation
- The tension between commodity inflation and bond deflation has been a defining feature of the post-2008 environment
- Fed's QE programs were designed to boost commodity prices (reflation) while keeping bond yields low
- This created an artificial balance that could unwind if the Fed reduced its bond purchases

### Commodity and Bond Links to China and Japan
- China's commodity demand is a major driver of the CRB/bond ratio
- Strong Chinese growth → higher commodity prices → higher ratio → positive for stocks
- Japanese deflation has been a major force pushing bond yields lower globally
- The interplay between Chinese growth (inflationary) and Japanese deflation (deflationary) has been a key dynamic in global markets

### Chapter 15 Summary
- Bond prices and commodities normally trend in opposite directions
- The commodity/bond ratio is a useful barometer of inflation/deflation balance
- Rising ratio = growth/inflation = good for stocks; Falling ratio = deflation/recession = bad for stocks
- The ratio influences sector rotation and emerging market allocation
- China (commodity demand) and Japan (deflation) are major external drivers

## Conclusion

### Recap of Intermarket Principles
- All global markets are linked to each other
- The four asset classes: stocks, bonds, commodities, currencies
- Dollar and commodities trend in opposite directions (most reliable relationship)
- Bond prices and commodities trend in opposite directions
- Since 1998: bond and stock prices have trended inversely (bond yields and stocks positively correlated)
- Since 2008: stocks and commodities have become closely correlated
- Bonds usually change direction before stocks; stocks usually change direction before commodities
- Bond yields peak first at tops, stocks second, commodities last
- Those rotations are less reliable at bottoms than at tops
- All global stocks are closely correlated (especially during downturns)
- A rising dollar benefits U.S. stocks; a weaker dollar favors foreign stocks
- Emerging markets are closely tied to commodity trends

### The New Normal in Intermarket Relationships
- The most important intermarket change: the decoupling of bonds and stocks starting in 1998
- Before 1998: rising bond prices (falling yields) were GOOD for stocks
- After 1998: rising bond prices (falling yields) have been BAD for stocks
- This change was caused by the emergence of deflation as a major economic force
- In a deflationary environment: falling yields signal economic weakness, not easier money
- The second major change: stocks and commodities becoming closely correlated after 2008
- Both changes are consistent with what happened during the deflationary 1930s

### Fed Policy May Be Interfering with Normal Bond/Stock Relationship
- The Fed's unprecedented intervention (QE, zero interest rates) has distorted some normal intermarket relationships
- Bond yields may be artificially low due to Fed buying → making the yield/stock correlation less reliable
- Once the Fed stops manipulating rates: normal relationships may reassert themselves
- The Fed's actions have created uncertainty about which intermarket relationships will hold going forward

### The Fed Also Kept Bond Yields Low during the 1940s
- During WWII and its aftermath: the Fed kept long-term bond yields artificially low (similar to current policy)
- That period also saw distortions in normal intermarket relationships
- Once the Fed allowed rates to normalize in the early 1950s: normal intermarket patterns returned
- History suggests that current Fed intervention is temporary and normal relationships will eventually reassert

### Asset Allocation Strategies May Start Favoring Stocks
- After a decade of bonds outperforming stocks (2000-2011): the pendulum may be swinging back
- Bond yields near historic lows → limited upside for bonds (prices can't rise much more)
- Stocks still well below their 2007 peaks → potential for upside
- The beginning of a shift from bonds to stocks would be confirmed by a rising bond yield and a rising stock market (positive correlation)

### The Nasdaq/Bond Ratio May Be Bottoming
- The Nasdaq/Treasury bond ratio had been falling since 2000 (bonds outperforming tech stocks)
- By 2012: signs that this ratio was forming a potential bottom
- A turn up in this ratio would signal the start of a major shift from bonds into stocks (particularly tech)

### The Nasdaq Composite Index Hits a 12-Year High
- In 2012: the Nasdaq Composite hit its highest level since 2000
- Technology leadership was returning → a positive sign for the broader market
- Nasdaq leadership is typically associated with economic expansion and bull markets

### Banks Show New Leadership
- Banking stocks started to show relative strength in late 2011/early 2012
- Banks benefit from a steeper yield curve (borrow short, lend long)
- Bank strength is typically a positive sign for the economy and stock market
- Banks are in the financial sector, which typically leads during early stages of economic recovery

### Homebuilders Bottom
- As discussed in Chapter 8: homebuilding stocks formed a potential bottom during 2008-2011
- By 2012: homebuilders were starting a new uptrend → positive for the housing industry and economy
- The real estate cycle bottom appears to have occurred roughly on schedule (18-year cycle from 1990-1992 trough)

### Adding a New Dimension to Technical Analysis
- Intermarket analysis doesn't replace traditional technical analysis — it adds another dimension
- Traditional TA focuses on individual markets; intermarket analysis focuses on relationships between markets
- The combination of both approaches provides a more complete picture
- Intermarket analysis improves the chartist's "peripheral trading vision"
