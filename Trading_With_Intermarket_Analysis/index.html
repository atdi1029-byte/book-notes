Introduction

John J. Murphy submitted a paper to the Market Technicians Association in the late 1980s arguing that intermarket relationships existed between bonds, stocks, commodities, and currencies. It was rejected for “lack of proof.” Twenty years later, a poll of MTA members ranked intermarket analysis fifth out of fourteen technical disciplines. Murphy had spent two decades proving what the establishment initially dismissed.

This book is his third on the subject, following Intermarket Technical Analysis (1991) and Intermarket Analysis (2004). The earlier works documented the “old normal” — a world where rising bond prices helped stocks, where commodities moved independently, and where the dollar’s influence was relatively straightforward. Three deflationary shocks changed everything: the Japanese stock market collapse in 1990, the Asian currency crisis in 1997–98, and the housing collapse of 2007.

“The purpose of this book is to update those intermarket trends and to explain what has changed and what hasn’t.” — John J. Murphy

The key changes since 1998: rising bond prices no longer help stocks — they signal deflationary trouble. Since 2008, stocks and commodities have become tightly correlated, both tied to the global economic cycle. These shifts mirror the 1930s deflationary environment, making historical parallels not just academic but practically useful.

Murphy writes in charts. The book contains over 150 figures from StockCharts.com, most showing overlaid price series that make intermarket relationships visually obvious. His argument is that charts are forward-looking discounting mechanisms, while economic data is always backward-looking.

“Markets anticipated economic trends six to nine months into the future. Chartists trust forward-looking markets; economists rely on lagging indicators.” — John J. Murphy

Markets spotted deflation years before the Fed acknowledged it in 2003, and markets signaled the 2007–08 collapse while Wall Street dismissed the warnings.

Chapter 1: Intermarket Analysis — The Study of Relationships

Intermarket analysis begins with a simple premise: no market moves in isolation. Stocks, bonds, commodities, and currencies form an interconnected web where a change in one ripples through the others. Before Murphy’s work, stock analysts ignored bonds, commodity traders ignored currencies, and everyone ignored the relationships between them. That siloed approach left traders vulnerable to forces they couldn’t see coming.

The four asset classes follow a chronological sequence at major turning points. Bonds change direction first, stocks second, commodities third. The ten-year Treasury yield peaked in January 2000, a full eight months before the S&P 500 peaked that September.

“Bond market usually changes direction before stocks at major turning points.” — John J. Murphy

Bond yields peaked again in June 2007, four months before the October stock market top. This leading indicator relationship is one of the most reliable in intermarket work.

Figure 1.1. The 10-year Treasury yield peaked eight months before the S&P 500 during 2000 — bonds lead stocks at major turning points.

The dollar sits at the center of the intermarket web, and its relationship with commodities is the most consistent of all. Dollar and commodities trend in opposite directions. When the dollar peaked in 2002, commodities launched a major six-year rally. Over the following decade, commodities gained 64%, bonds 23%, and stocks just 9%. The main catalyst was a 32% drop in the dollar.

Figure 1.3. Crude oil started climbing early 2007 while the S&P peaked October 2007 — rising oil has contributed to every U.S. recession in forty years.
Figure 1.2. The dollar peak in 2002 coincided exactly with the CRB commodity upturn — the inverse dollar/commodity relationship is one of the most reliable in markets.

ETFs revolutionized the practical application of intermarket analysis. In the 1990s, trading commodities and currencies required futures markets. Now any investor can rotate between asset classes using SPDRs, iShares, and PowerShares products. Sector rotation strategies that once lived only in institutional research departments became accessible to anyone with a brokerage account.

The stock market itself contains ten sectors and roughly ninety industry groups, each responding differently to the business cycle. At bull market beginnings, consumer discretionary, technology, and small-caps lead. Near market tops, energy stocks become leaders — a dangerous warning sign. Money then flows into defensive sectors: consumer staples, healthcare, and utilities.

Oil has contributed to every U.S. recession in the last forty years. Rising oil preceded stock market drops in 1973, 1987, 1990, 1994, 2000, and 2007. Crude started climbing in early 2007, the S&P peaked that October, and crude itself didn’t peak until July 2008 — nine months after the stock top. Conversely, sharp oil drops are bullish: both Iraq wars saw oil plunge and new bull markets begin.

Murphy argues that intermarket analysis doesn’t replace traditional technical analysis but adds a crucial dimension to it. It’s like driving with mirrors — you can navigate without checking your blind spots, but sooner or later you’ll get hit by something you didn’t see. The Fed itself uses charts to read financial markets for economic clues, even if it won’t always admit it.

The chapter closes with a comprehensive list of intermarket principles: all global markets are linked; bonds lead stocks which lead commodities; dollar and commodities are inversely correlated; emerging markets are tied to commodity trends; and a rising dollar favors U.S. stocks while a weaker dollar favors foreign markets. These principles are guidelines, not rigid rules — correlations may weaken temporarily, and tools like the correlation coefficient can monitor their strength.

Chapter 2: Review of the Old Normal

The last three decades of the twentieth century followed an intermarket script that Murphy calls the “old normal.” It begins with the 1980 commodity peak, which ended the hyperinflationary 1970s. During that decade, the CRB Index appreciated 250%, bond yields rose 150%, and gold soared above $700. The Dow Jones Industrial Average started and ended the 1970s at roughly the same level — around 1,000 — making it a lost decade for stock investors.

Everything turned in 1980. Commodities peaked, the dollar bottomed, and within two years the entire intermarket landscape reversed. Falling commodities plus a rising dollar produced a major bond upturn starting in 1981, which in turn launched the great stock bull market of 1982. For the next twenty years, paper assets — bonds and stocks — dominated at the expense of tangible assets like gold and commodities. Gold fell from $700 to $250 over those two decades, losing 60% of its value.

Figure 2.1. The CRB commodity peak in 1980 coincided with the dollar upturn — ending the inflationary 1970s and launching two decades of paper asset dominance.
Figure 2.2. Bond prices and commodity prices trend in opposite directions — one of the oldest and most reliable intermarket relationships.

The 1987 crash reinforced these intermarket patterns. In April 1987, commodity prices spiked higher while bond prices tumbled. Bond prices peaked four months before the stock market crash that October, once again demonstrating that bonds change direction before stocks. The crash was global — stock markets fell worldwide, proving that during downturns, global correlations spike.

Figure 2.3. Bond prices peaked four months before the October 1987 crash — commodity spikes in spring 1987 pushed bonds down first, stocks followed.
Figure 2.5. How oil impacted stocks during the first Persian Gulf War — Iraq’s invasion pushed oil and gold up while stocks and bonds tumbled.

The two Iraq wars provided textbook intermarket confirmations. In August 1990, Iraq invaded Kuwait: gold and oil surged while bonds and stocks tumbled. When Desert Storm began in January 1991, every relationship reversed — bonds and stocks resumed uptrends, gold and oil collapsed. The second Iraq war in March 2003 produced the biggest weekly stock market gain in twenty years (8%), while gold fell 15% and crude tumbled 33%. In both conflicts, the four financial markets did exactly what intermarket analysis predicted.

The 1994 stealth bear market offers a subtler example. Major indexes lost only about 10%, masking the real damage underneath: small-caps fell 15%, transports 26%, and utilities 34%. The sequence was textbook — commodity upturn first half of 1993, bond downturn second half, stocks peaking September 1993 and falling by February 1994. The dollar fell throughout 1994, contributing to the commodity rise. When commodities peaked mid-1994, bonds bottomed in November, and stocks turned up a month later. From that Q4 1994 bottom, the stock market began its phenomenal bull run to the end of the decade.

The old normal worked beautifully for decades. But starting in 1998, the word “deflation” was heard for the first time since the 1930s. The Asian currency crisis and Japanese stock collapse introduced forces that would break the old correlations and create a new, more dangerous intermarket environment.

Figure 2.6. Close correlation between Treasury bond prices and utility stocks during 1994–1995 — rate-sensitive utilities move in lockstep with bonds.
Figure 2.4. Commodity spike spring 1987 coincided with bond plunge — the 1987 crash was a textbook intermarket event.

Chapter 3: The 1997–1998 Asian Currency Crisis

The most important result of the Asian currency crisis was the dramatic decoupling of bond and stock prices. Before 1997, bonds and stocks generally moved in the same direction — rising bond prices (falling yields) were good for stocks. The crisis changed that relationship, and it has never fully reverted.

It started with the Thai baht in the summer of 1997. The currency collapse spread to other Asian economies, then to their stock markets, then to global commodities, and finally to the rest of the world. The CRB Index fell to its lowest level in twenty years as deflation fears intensified. Money poured out of stocks and into the safety of U.S. Treasury bonds.

Figure 3.1. The commodity plunge to a 20-year low during 1998 raised fears of global deflation — falling commodities signaled something far worse than disinflation.

The decoupling showed up clearly in the data. During the third quarter of 1998, the Dow lost 20% while Treasury bond prices soared. This was the opposite of the old normal, where bonds and stocks moved together. By the end of 1998, the crisis passed and everything reversed: bonds tumbled while stocks soared into 1999. But the precedent was set.

“Those who heeded the warnings of 1998 knew that rising bond prices don’t necessarily help stocks in a deflationary climate.” — John J. Murphy

Murphy emphasizes a critical distinction: disinflation (prices rising at a slower pace) is good for both bonds and stocks. Deflation (prices actually falling) is good for bonds but bad for stocks. The Asian crisis pushed the global economy from benign disinflation toward dangerous deflation. The fine line between the two would define markets for the next decade.

The crisis also established that bond yields — not prices — became the relevant leading indicator for stocks. In the old normal, watching bond prices was sufficient. In the new normal, falling bond yields signal economic weakness and potential stock trouble, even though bond prices are rising. Bond yields still turned down before stocks at the 2000 and 2007 peaks, maintaining their leading indicator role.

Figure 3.3. Before 1998, falling bond yields were positive for stocks; after 1998, falling yields became bad for stocks — the defining change of the new normal.
Figure 3.4. Two deflationary events of the 1990s — commodity prices and Japanese stocks both hit 20-year lows during 1998.
Figure 3.5. Falling commodity prices helped push the Treasury yield to a new 20-year low during 1998.

Japanese deflation played a bigger role than commonly acknowledged. The Nikkei 225 and U.S. Treasury bond yields showed a remarkably close visual correlation since 1990, with a correlation coefficient of 0.75. Japanese deflationary effects since the 1990 stock collapse had a subtle but persistent downward pull on U.S. interest rates that lasted more than two decades.

The practical lesson: investors need to monitor global markets, including currencies in countries they might never trade directly. A collapse in the Thai baht eventually hit the S&P 500 through a chain of intermarket connections that traditional single-market analysis would never have detected.

Figure 3.2. Bond prices rose while stocks fell during the height of the 1998 financial crisis — the first major decoupling.
Figure 3.6. Nikkei 225 and U.S. Treasury yield showed remarkably close correlation since 1990 — Japanese deflation had a lasting effect on U.S. interest rates.

Chapter 4: Intermarket Events Surrounding the 2000 Top

The year 2000 stock market top was a textbook case of technical, intermarket, and economic analyses working together. Every warning sign was flashing, yet Wall Street and the economic community ignored them all. Murphy walks through the intermarket chain that led to the collapse of the dot-com bubble.

It began with oil tripling in price during 1999. Rising oil had a positive effect on energy stocks, which became the market’s strongest sector. Energy stock leadership is almost always a warning sign that a bull market and economic recovery are nearing completion. Rising oil forced the Fed to start raising short-term rates in the summer of 1999.

By the first quarter of 2000, Fed tightening produced an inverted yield curve — short-term rates rose above long-term rates. Every recession since 1970 had been preceded by an inverted yield curve. The stocks most vulnerable were those with the highest price-to-earnings ratios: the dot-com and technology stocks concentrated in the Nasdaq. The Nasdaq peaked in Q1 2000 and lost 40% by May.

Figure 4.1. The Nasdaq fell below its spring low, confirming a major downtrend — the burst of the dot-com bubble followed textbook intermarket warnings.
Figure 4.2. REITs turned up during 2000 as the Nasdaq peaked — REITs benefit from bear markets through high dividends and negative tech correlation.
Figure 4.3. Consumer staples rallied as the Nasdaq tumbled during 2000 — defensive sector leadership signals economic contraction.

As stocks fell, REITs became the top-performing group. REITs benefit during bear markets because they pay high dividends, have low correlation with stocks, and show historically negative correlation with technology stocks. Homebuilding stocks also turned up, benefiting from the falling interest rates that accompanied the bear market. The bear market starting in 2000 plus aggressive Fed easing helped create the housing boom that would eventually cause the next crisis.

Consumer staples started to outperform as the economy weakened. Staples are defensive — beverages, food, tobacco, household products that people buy regardless of economic conditions. When energy and consumer staples are simultaneously the two strongest sectors, it’s an especially bearish signal. That’s exactly what happened in the first half of 2000.

The S&P 500 fell deeply below its 200-day moving average during the second half of 2000. When the 200-day MA itself turned down, it confirmed that a major bear market was underway. The intermarket warnings had been flashing for over a year: rising commodities, rising rates, inverted yield curve, sector rotations following the end-of-expansion pattern.

“Most important lesson: Stocks are a better indicator of the economy than the economy is of stocks.” — John J. Murphy

The critical peaking sequence played out perfectly. Bond yields peaked January 2000, the Nasdaq peaked March, the S&P peaked September, and the CRB commodity index peaked January 2001 — five months after the S&P. Bonds first, stocks second, commodities third. The official recession began March 2001, two months after the commodity peak. The same sequence would repeat during 2007–08.

Figure 4.5. Bond yields peaked January 2000, eight months before the S&P 500 — the peaking sequence at work.
Figure 4.6. The S&P 500 peaked five months before commodities during 2000 — stocks lead commodities at tops.
Figure 4.7. All three markets peaked in the normal intermarket order — bond yields first, stocks second, commodities last — during the 2000–2001 cycle.

The 2002–2003 bottoms, however, reversed the normal sequence. Commodities bottomed first (start of 2002), stocks bottomed October 2002, and bond yields didn’t turn up until June 2003. Murphy notes that when intermarket relationships act out of sequence, there’s always a reason — in this case, the Fed’s unprecedented fight against deflation.

Figure 4.6. Stocks peaked before commodities during 2000 — the S&P led the CRB by five months.
Figure 4.7. All three markets peaked in the normal intermarket order during 2000 — bonds first, stocks second, commodities last.

Chapter 5: The 2002 Falling Dollar Boosts Commodities

The dollar peaked in the first quarter of 2002 and began a major decline that would last through the end of the decade. The CRB commodity index turned up at the exact point the dollar peaked. The dollar had formed a head-and-shoulders top pattern spanning October 2000 to spring 2002 — three peaks with the middle slightly higher, a classic bearish pattern completed when the neckline was broken in April 2002.

Figure 5.1. The dollar completed a head-and-shoulders topping pattern during 2002 — the neckline break in April launched a major decline lasting the rest of the decade.
Figure 5.3. The 2002 dollar peak coincided exactly with the major commodity bottom — a textbook inverse relationship.

The falling dollar was a deliberate weapon against deflation. The Fed suspected of allowing the dollar to depreciate since early 2002, and the Treasury Secretary hinted the U.S. had abandoned its “strong dollar” policy. Since commodities are priced in dollars, a falling dollar mechanically pushes commodity prices higher. The major bull market in commodities starting 2002 was a direct result of the Fed weakening the dollar to fight deflation.

Gold was the primary beneficiary. Gold started rallying in spring 2001 as the dollar began its peaking process, then surged past $300 when the dollar broke down decisively. Gold benefited from a double tailwind: the falling dollar and the falling stock market. Gold and stocks trend in opposite directions over long periods.

“One reason Wall Street strategists are seldom bullish on gold: they know rising gold is usually bearish for stocks.” — John J. Murphy

Gold peaked in 1980 as the 20-year stock bull market was about to begin, and the S&P 500 peaked in August 2000 within three months of gold stocks bottoming.

Figure 5.6. The gold bottom coincided with the 2000 stock market top — the end of the 20-year stock bull market marked the end of the 20-year gold bear market.

Gold does well in both inflationary and deflationary periods. During the 1970s inflation, gold soared. During the 1929–32 deflation, gold bullion was fixed-price, but Homestake Mining gained 300% while the stock market lost 90%. Gold is viewed as an alternative to paper assets, and as the world’s strongest currency — it outperformed the euro by a wide margin over the decade from 2002 to 2012.

The Dow/gold ratio provides a powerful long-term signal for shifts between paper and hard assets. The ratio peaked in 1966, fell through the inflationary 1970s, bottomed in 1980, then rose steadily to a record high near 40 during 2000. Within two years of the stock market peak, the ratio broke its 20-year rising trendline — signaling a generational shift in favor of gold and commodities versus stocks. From 2000 to 2011, the S&P lost 15% while gold gained 490%.

Figure 5.4. The dollar broke its rising seven-year trendline in December 2002 — a serious breakdown signal confirmed by gold’s simultaneous breakout.
Figure 5.8. The Dow/Gold ratio peaked in 2000 after a 20-year rise, then broke its long-term support — signaling a generational shift from stocks to gold.

Murphy uses a key charting technique throughout: to validate a trend change, compare the chart action of two related markets. If gold is starting to rise, check whether the dollar is starting to drop. If the magnitude of both moves is similar, the trend change is more credible. The dollar broke its seven-year rising trendline in December 2002 while gold simultaneously rose above a five-year resistance level — confirming each other’s new trend.

The oil peak in March 2003 — inflated by an Iraq war premium — provided the final piece. When the war started, crude tumbled 33% in a single week, and the S&P rose above a trendline drawn over nine months of highs, confirming a new bull market that would last until 2007. Just as rising oil hurts stocks, falling oil prices are usually good for the stock market.

Figure 5.10. Oil peaked at the start of the Iraq war in March 2003 and tumbled 33% — the stock market surged, confirming that sharp oil drops are bullish.
Figure 5.2. CRB rose above its 200-day moving average in April 2002 just as the dollar was breaking down.
Figure 5.5. Gold rose above its 1999 peak near $320 by end of 2002 — a five-year high representing a major upside breakout.
Figure 5.6. 20-year comparison showing gold bottom coinciding with the stock market top — the end of one secular trend marked the start of another.
Figure 5.7. Gold breaks 15-year resistance while stocks break 20-year support at the same time — each reinforced the other.

Chapter 6: Asset Allocation Rotations Leading to the 2007 Top

Murphy introduces the relative strength ratio as the primary tool for spotting leadership shifts between asset classes. The ratio divides one market’s price by another’s: a rising line means the numerator is outperforming. Simple trendline analysis on these ratios is usually sufficient to spot important turns.

The CRB/S&P ratio — commodities divided by stocks — had been falling for twenty years from 1980 to 2000, reflecting stock dominance. During 2002, this ratio broke a two-decade downtrend, signaling a generational shift from paper assets to hard assets. Between 2002 and 2008, commodity gains outpaced stock gains by a factor of seven to one.

Figure 6.2. The CRB/Treasury bond ratio also turned up during 2002 after declining for more than twenty years — commodities outperformed bonds for six years after.
Figure 6.1. The CRB/S&P ratio bottomed during 2000 and broke its 20-year downtrend in 2002 — signaling a generational shift from stocks to commodities.

The bond/stock ratio (Treasury price divided by S&P 500) tracks which asset investors are favoring. It turned up dramatically during the 2000 bear market as money rotated from stocks into bonds. It peaked near the end of 2002, turned down as the 2003 bull market began, and stayed down until mid-2007 when subprime problems first surfaced. When the ratio broke a down trendline extending back to its 2004 peak, it confirmed that investors were rotating back into bonds and out of stocks.

Figure 6.3. The bond/stock ratio tracked important changes in asset leadership during 2000, 2003, and 2007 — rising ratio favors bonds, falling ratio favors stocks.
Figure 6.5. The bond/stock ratio shifted back in favor of bonds during mid-2007 as subprime problems surfaced — breaking the down trendline confirmed the rotation.

The 2007 intermarket chain played out step by step. During the first half, stocks rose and bonds fell. In July, stocks fell and bonds turned up immediately. The proper action: rotate out of the market turning down (stocks) into the one turning up (bonds). The most important trend changes, Murphy argues, are usually the easiest to spot on a chart.

“The most important trend changes are pretty easy to spot — you just have to follow the charts.” — John J. Murphy

Falling U.S. rates hurt the dollar, which pushed gold to a record high. The two-year Treasury yield and the Dollar Index fell together during 2007, pushing the dollar to a record low. Gold and the dollar formed almost a perfect mirror image. By the first quarter of 2008, the dollar was at its record low and gold at its record high.

Figure 6.7. The 2-year Treasury yield and the Dollar Index fell together during 2007 into Q1 2008, pushing the dollar to a record low.
Figure 6.8. Gold and the dollar formed almost a perfect mirror image during 2007–2008 — the intermarket chain from falling rates to rising gold.

The three markets peaked in the right order again. Bond yields peaked June 2007, stocks peaked October 2007, and commodities peaked mid-2008. The U.S. recession started December 2007, two months after the stock peak. Any theory of “global decoupling” — the idea that foreign stocks were immune to U.S. problems — proved spectacularly wrong. The S&P lost 45%, EAFE iShares lost 50%, and Emerging Market iShares lost 55%.

Figure 6.9. Bond yields peaked June 2007, stocks peaked October 2007, commodities peaked mid-2008 — the same peaking sequence as 2000.
Figure 6.10. All global stock markets fell together during the 2008 bear market — there is no such thing as global decoupling.

Murphy gave a speech in Switzerland in January 2008 warning that U.S. problems would spread to Europe. He was greeted with skepticism. He turned out to be right, as he has been at most major turning points throughout his career. The intermarket playbook worked — again.

Figure 6.4. T-bond/stock ratio peaked in spring 2003 forming a double top — confirmed by breaking the rising trendline.
Figure 6.6. T-note price vs S&P 500 during 2007 — first half stocks rose and bonds fell; July stocks fell and bonds turned up immediately.

Chapter 7: Visual Analysis of the 2007 Market Top

This chapter combines traditional charting techniques with intermarket analysis to show how the 2007 top could have been anticipated. The S&P 500 had reached the same level where it peaked in 2000 — seven years earlier. That previous peak represented dangerous resistance, the price level where selling pressure tends to emerge. Technical traders often start taking profits when a market retests a prominent prior peak.

Market breadth — the number of stocks rising versus falling — provided unmistakable warnings. The NYSE advance-decline line peaked in June/July 2007, dropped into August, and when the S&P rallied to a new high in October, the AD line failed to confirm. This negative divergence meant the new stock high was supported by a shrinking number of stocks.

Figure 7.1. Monthly chart of the S&P 500 spanning 1998–2008 — the 2007 rally reached the same level as the 2000 peak, a dangerous resistance area.

Four groups with a history of peaking first drove the divergence: small-cap stocks, financials, retailers, and transportation stocks. None reached new highs in Q4 2007. At market tops, money rotates out of economically sensitive groups first — small caps peak before large caps, financials weaken as credit deteriorates, retailers reflect consumer stress, and transports suffer from rising energy costs.

Figure 7.3. The Dow Transports/Industrial ratio fell as crude oil rose during 2007 — rising fuel costs hammered transportation stocks, creating a Dow Theory warning.

The Dow Theory, one of the oldest pillars of technical analysis, originated with Charles Dow himself. In a healthy economy, industrial and transport stocks should rise together. During Q4 2007, the Dow Industrials hit a new high while transports were nearly 10% below their summer high. That divergence was a classic Dow Theory warning.

Figure 7.4. Dow Industrials hit a new high in October while Transports were 10% below their summer high — a textbook Dow Theory warning.

The Dow Transport peak in July 2007 preceded the start of the U.S. recession in December by exactly five months.

Consumers were squeezed from two directions: home prices falling for the first time in their lifetimes and energy prices soaring. Oil doubled from $50 to $100 during 2007, and the plunge in retail stock relative performance was the direct result. The financial community kept claiming rising oil wasn’t hurting consumers — the charts showed otherwise.

Figure 7.6. The PHLX Housing Index and retail/S&P ratio fell together from mid-2005 — housing weakness and retail decline were clearly linked on the charts.

Housing stocks had been broadcasting trouble since mid-2005 — a full two years before subprime problems surfaced in the summer of 2007. The PHLX Housing Index broke a three-year rising trendline during Q2 2006. When housing stocks peaked, retail relative strength peaked at the same time, confirming the link between housing weakness and consumer spending.

Figure 7.9. Housing stocks fell below chart support in spring 2006 — a rising trendline drawn under 2003–2005 lows was broken, confirming the downtrend.
Figure 7.11. The percentage of NYSE stocks above their 200-day moving average showed negative divergence — it dropped sharply mid-2007 while the S&P made new highs.

The warning was given one to two years early and largely ignored by the financial community.

“Charts are a shortcut form of fundamental analysis — markets discount fundamentals before they become generally known.” — John J. Murphy

Murphy introduces the percentage of NYSE stocks above their 200-day moving average as another breadth measure. Readings over 80% signal a dangerously overextended market. By August 2007, this indicator had fallen below 40%, putting most NYSE stocks in downtrends. The S&P’s October high was not confirmed — another negative divergence.

Why do breadth measures work so well at tops? Because the NYSE Composite and S&P 500 are capitalization-weighted. Small and midsize stocks dominate breadth measures but contribute little to headline indexes. The large-cap-dominated indexes that get media attention are usually the last to fall. Breadth indicators tell you what the rest of the market is doing before the blue chips catch up to reality.

Figure 7.3. Crude oil price vs Dow Transports/Industrial ratio showing the inverse relationship during 2007.
Figure 7.6. Retail relative strength ratio fell alongside the PHLX Housing Index — housing and retail weakness were closely linked.
Figure 7.7. Retail stocks: absolute price still rising but relative performance peaked in 2005 — relative performance changes direction before absolute.
Figure 7.8. PHLX Housing Index weekly chart showing the rising trendline break during Q2 2006 — a clear trend reversal.
Figure 7.10. Housing peak in 2005 gave warning a full two years before subprime became a household word.

Chapter 8: Intermarket Analysis and the Business Cycle

The American economy cycles through expansion and contraction averaging about four years, with stock markets tending to bottom during midterm election years — the presidential cycle. The six four-year bottoms from 1990 to 2010 all followed this pattern. The third year of a president’s term has historically produced the strongest stock returns, as presidents do everything possible to stimulate the economy before the next election.

Figure 8.1. Examples of four-year stock market bottoms — markets tend to bottom during midterm election years in the presidential cycle.

The business cycle explains why bonds, stocks, and commodities peak and trough in a specific order. Near the end of economic expansion, rising commodity prices force the Fed to raise rates, bonds turn down first, stocks follow, and commodities peak last as the recession begins. The proper asset allocation shifts with each stage: favor stocks during economic strength, bonds during weakness, and commodities in the late stages of expansion when inflation pressures build.

Murphy draws extensive parallels between the post-2000 era and the 1920s and 1930s. Commodities peaked in 1920 and 1980 after inflationary decades; bonds bottomed the following year in both cases; stocks bottomed one year after bonds in both cases. The 1920s saw bonds and stocks rise while commodities remained flat — similar to 1980–2000. Bond prices peaked in 1928, stocks peaked in 1929, and when commodities started falling sharply, benign disinflation turned to harmful deflation.

The Kondratieff Wave provides the longest cycle framework — roughly 55 to 60 years of economic activity. Four Kondratieff peaks have occurred over two centuries: 1816, 1864, 1920, and 1980. Each expansion led to a burst of inflation coinciding with a major American war (War of 1812, Civil War, WWI, Vietnam). The 60 years between the 1920 and 1980 commodity peaks fits the model closely.

“When deflation is the main threat, stocks and commodities become closely correlated… rising commodity prices are considered a plus for stocks and the economy.” — John J. Murphy

Ian Gordon divides the Kondratieff cycle into seasons. Spring (1949–1966): strengthening economy with benign inflation, stocks do well. Summer (1966–1980): inflationary period with rising commodities and real estate. Autumn (starting 1980): greatest speculation in bonds, stocks, and real estate, massive debt buildup. Winter (starting 2000): deflation, debt repayment, stock and real estate plunges. The best defenses during Kondratieff winter are bonds and gold.

The 18-year real estate cycle, discovered by Clarence Long in 1940, adds another layer. Three real estate cycles fit within one Kondratieff Wave. The last major real estate trough was 1990–92, putting the next cycle bottom around 2008–2010 — remarkably accurate. For the first time since the Great Depression, both the 60-year Kondratieff Wave and the 18-year real estate cycle were falling simultaneously after 2005, making the downturn far more severe than normal.

Housing is countercyclical — it moves opposite to the normal business cycle because it’s interest rate sensitive. Housing stocks turned up in 2000 and rose through the bear market, cushioning the recession. But after housing stocks collapsed in 2006, the 2008 stock plunge had no cushion. The jump in bond yields during 2005 coincided exactly with the peak in homebuilding stocks, as higher yields meant higher mortgage rates.

Figure 8.3. The jump in bond yields during 2005 contributed to the homebuilder top — rising rates hit the rate-sensitive housing sector first.
Figure 8.5. The Dow Jones Home Construction Index formed a bottoming pattern between 2008 and 2012, consistent with the 18-year real estate cycle.

Murphy argues that it’s not enough to watch the four-year business cycle alone. Longer cycles — the Kondratieff Wave, the 18-year real estate cycle — set the framework. Those who ignored these longer rhythms remained baffled by why traditional fiscal and monetary remedies were failing. The deflationary headwinds from the longer cycles overwhelmed the usual stimulative tools.

Figure 8.2. Short-term rates started rising during 2004 after oil hit a record high — the Fed response to rising energy costs.
Figure 8.4. Falling housing stocks made the 2008 stock plunge far worse than 2000 — homebuilders rose during the 2000 bear market but collapsed in 2006.

Chapter 9: The Impact of the Business Cycle on Market Sectors

Sector rotation is one of the most actionable concepts in the entire book. Money flows from sector to sector in a predictable pattern as the economy moves from expansion to contraction, and Murphy maps exactly which sectors lead at each stage.

At market bottoms and during early recovery, cyclicals and technology lead. The Nasdaq outperformed the S&P 500 by 50% to 30% during 2009, and small-caps gained 40% — classic signs of a new bull market. Technology leadership is confirmed when the tech-heavy Nasdaq outpaces the broader market. Small caps have outperformed large caps in the first year following every recession between 1960 and 2009.

Figure 9.3. Murphy’s sector rotation model showing how nine sectors perform throughout the business cycle — the most actionable framework in the entire book.

As the expansion matures, leadership shifts to basic materials and energy. Energy leadership signals that the economic expansion and bull market are nearing completion. Rising energy prices prompt the Fed to raise rates, which eventually hurts stocks and the economy. This pattern produced market tops in both 2000 and 2007.

The transition from expansion to contraction shows up when energy and consumer staples become the two strongest sectors simultaneously . Consumer staples — beverages, food, tobacco, household products — are things consumers need regardless of economic conditions. Their relative outperformance is almost always a negative warning sign for the stock market. The consumer staples/S&P ratio acts as an excellent contrary indicator: it rises during bear markets and falls during bull markets.

Figure 9.1. The consumer staples/S&P ratio peaked end of 2002 as the market bottomed, underperformed during the bull market, then rose sharply in H2 2007 as the market peaked.

The consumer discretionary/staples ratio is one of the most useful tools in the book. When discretionary outperforms staples, investors are optimistic — bullish for stocks. When staples outperform discretionary, investors are turning defensive — bearish. In April 2009, this ratio broke a trendline drawn along its 2007–2008 peaks, confirming the market bottom and implying the recession was nearing completion. The recession ended three months later.

Figure 9.4. The consumer discretionary/staples ratio bottomed in Q4 2008, formed a higher low in March 2009, and broke its downtrend in April — confirming the market bottom.

Murphy emphasizes that sector trends last only three to six months and need to be monitored at least weekly. As much as half a stock’s performance is tied to the group it belongs to.

“As much as half a stock’s performance is tied to the group it belongs to — find the leading groups first, then find the leaders within those groups.” — John J. Murphy

The general rule: buy the strongest stocks in the strongest sectors. Industry group leadership can be tracked using market carpets — heat maps where greener boxes indicate stronger performance and redder boxes indicate weakness.

Both absolute and relative performance matter, but they measure different things. Absolute performance tells you if a sector is rising or falling. Relative performance tells you how it compares to the rest of the market. Relative performance usually changes direction before absolute performance, making it a more sensitive early warning signal.

“Relative performance of a market group is often more telling than its absolute trend.” — John J. Murphy

The goal is to own stocks with positive performance on both measures.

The 2011 market provides a real-time example. Energy stocks started underperforming in April while defensive sectors turned up. Over the following six months, healthcare, staples, and utilities became the three strongest sectors, while materials and energy were the weakest. The S&P lost 19% between April and October while Treasury bonds gained 7%. The sector rotation model predicted the weakness months in advance.

Chapter 10: Exchange-Traded Funds

ETFs first appeared in 1993 with the launch of SPY (SPDR S&P 500), which remains the largest traded fund. Assets grew 30% per year from 2000 to 2010, reaching $1.5 trillion by 2012. ETFs combine the diversification of mutual funds with the tradability of individual stocks — they trade on exchanges throughout the day, can be sold short, and offer lower expenses and greater tax efficiency than mutual funds.

For intermarket analysis, the critical advantage of ETFs is access. Every market Murphy discusses can be traded with an ETF: domestic stock indexes via SPY and QQQ, nine sector SPDRs for rotation strategies, iShares for bond durations (IEF for 7–10 year, TLT for 20+ year), PowerShares for commodity baskets (DBC, DBA, DBE), GLD for gold, CurrencyShares for individual currencies, and UUP/UDN for dollar direction.

Murphy devotes attention to the distinction between Treasury and corporate bonds. Treasury bonds are the safe-haven benchmark. High-yield (junk) bond ETFs act more like stocks than bonds — they rise during risk-on environments and fall during stress. During 2008, Treasuries soared while junk bonds plunged. For safety, use Treasury ETFs; for economic optimism, corporate bond ETFs; high-yield bonds offer the most profit potential in good times but carry the most risk when things go bad.

Foreign stock ETFs deserve special attention because currency trends affect them more than they affect the underlying local markets. When the dollar is rising, foreign ETFs fall even if local stock markets are flat — the currency translation erodes dollar-denominated returns. EFA (EAFE developed markets) and EEM (emerging markets) are the primary benchmarks. Inverse and leveraged ETFs also exist but are best left to expert traders with short-term horizons, as they’re not suitable for long-term holdings.

Chapter 11: The Dollar and Commodities Trend in Opposite Directions

The inverse correlation between the dollar and commodity prices is the most consistent and reliable relationship in intermarket analysis. In the twenty years between 1970 and 1990, every important turn in commodity prices was either preceded by or coincided with a turn in the dollar in the opposite direction. It’s a mistake to analyze either market without the other.

Figure 11.1. The rising dollar contributed to the commodity collapse during 1997–98 — the Asian crisis pushed money into the dollar and out of commodities.
Figure 11.2. The falling dollar from 2002 to mid-2008 pushed commodities higher — the inverse relationship held for six years.
Figure 11.3. The dollar bottom during 2008 contributed to the commodity plunge — the CRB lost more than 50% of its value in just six months.

The correlation coefficient between the dollar and CRB ranged between -0.50 and -0.90 over most of the last decade, confirming the strong inverse relationship. This metric is useful for both confirming normal relationships and spotting when they’re weakening. A reading moving toward zero would suggest something is changing in the intermarket environment.

Gold isn’t like other commodities because it has a dual role as both commodity and alternate currency. Gold’s closer link to the dollar stems from its currency role, and it often leads other commodities at turning points because of greater dollar sensitivity. Gold outperformed both the euro and the broader CRB Index by wide margins over the decade from 2002 to 2012 — it gained 490% while other commodities gained far less. Gold is the world’s strongest commodity and the world’s strongest currency.

Figure 11.8. Gold outpaced the euro from 2008 through 2011 — gold’s dual role as commodity and currency gives it a double tailwind.
Figure 11.9. Gold significantly outperformed the broader CRB commodity index since 2001 — especially after mid-2008 when all other assets fell.

Commodity-linked currencies like the Australian and Canadian dollars get a double boost from a falling U.S. dollar: the direct currency effect plus rising commodity prices that strengthen their export-dependent economies. The Aussie dollar gained 101% versus just 50% for the euro over ten years starting 2002, reflecting Australia’s commodity export economy.

The dollar’s direction creates a ripple effect across all four asset classes. Falling dollar leads to rising commodities, rising inflation pressures, falling bond prices, and eventually hurts stocks. Rising dollar leads to falling commodities, lower inflation, rising bond prices, and eventually helps stocks. The dollar is the starting point for much of intermarket analysis.

Chapter 12: Stocks and Commodities Become Highly Correlated

One of the most important intermarket changes since 2008 is that stocks and commodities have become highly correlated, moving in the same direction. Before the deflationary era, their correlation was weak or inverse. After the 2008 financial meltdown, both became tied to the global economic cycle — rising commodities like copper and oil signal economic strength and are good for stocks, while falling commodities signal weakness and drag stocks lower.

Figure 12.1. Commodities lost half their value during the last half of 2008 — the plunge tightened the correlation between stocks and commodities.
Figure 12.2. The CRB Index and S&P 500 tracked each other closely from 2006 to 2011 — the new positive correlation is a defining feature of the deflationary era.

Copper — often called “Dr. Copper” for its economic sensitivity — shows a strong positive correlation with the S&P 500 since 2008. Copper has many industrial uses, making its price direction a real-time gauge of global manufacturing demand. It can sometimes turn before the stock market, adding leading indicator value.

“Copper has many industrial uses, making its price direction a real-time gauge of global manufacturing demand.” — John J. Murphy

The silver/gold ratio provides another useful signal. Silver is both a precious metal and an industrial metal, so when silver outperforms gold, it means industrial demand is strong — bullish for stocks. When gold outperforms silver, safe-haven demand dominates — bearish for stocks. The ratio is a real-time measure of risk appetite.

This new correlation also means that commodity peaks now have a more direct impact on stocks. During spring 2011, commodities peaked and led stocks lower — unusual because normally commodities peak after stocks. But the strong post-2008 correlation meant commodity weakness directly dragged the S&P down.

Gold miners (GDX) illustrate a complication: they are stocks first and gold proxies second. When the stock market falls, gold miners can decline even as gold bullion rises. During 2011, gold bullion hit record highs while gold mining stocks actually fell, pulled down by broader equity weakness. When miners underperform bullion, it’s often a negative signal for both gold and stocks.

Chapter 13: Stocks and the Dollar

Unlike the strong dollar/commodity inverse link, the dollar/stock relationship has historically been weaker and less consistent. During the 1990s, both the dollar and stocks rose together. From 2002 to 2007, stocks rose while the dollar fell. Since 2008, however, the relationship has become more consistently negative — rising dollar (risk-off) coincides with falling stocks, and falling dollar (risk-on) with rising stocks.

The key connector is commodities. Since stocks and commodities are now closely correlated, and commodities and the dollar are inversely correlated, it follows that stocks and the dollar are negatively correlated. The chain: falling dollar leads to rising commodities, which leads to rising stocks. This chain explains why the dollar-stock relationship has strengthened since 2008.

Figure 13.3. The dollar and stocks moved in opposite directions during 2010–2011 — the negative correlation strengthened in the risk-on/risk-off environment.
Figure 13.4. The rising dollar since 2008 hurt foreign stocks more than U.S. stocks — currency translation compounds the damage.

A rising dollar is especially negative for foreign stocks priced in dollars. Two forces compound the damage: local stock prices may fall, and currency translation makes it worse. When the dollar falls, foreign ETFs get a double boost. Trading implication: overweight foreign stocks when the dollar is falling, overweight U.S. stocks when the dollar is rising.

Emerging markets are the most commodity-sensitive segment. Their economies depend heavily on commodity exports, so rising commodity prices boost their economies and their stock markets. The correlation between the CRB Index and Emerging Market iShares (EEM) is strong and consistent.

China influences the cycle from the demand side. As the world’s biggest consumer of copper and many industrial commodities, Chinese economic growth or slowdown has a major impact on commodity prices. Chinese stocks have led U.S. stocks at several important turning points since 2008 — peaking before the S&P in 2007 and bottoming before the S&P in 2008–09. The link runs through commodity demand.

Chapter 14: The Link between Bonds and Stocks

Bonds and stocks are always competing for investor money. Optimistic investors favor stocks over bonds; pessimistic investors do the opposite. Since 1998, bond yields and stocks have been positively correlated — they rise and fall together. This is the new normal: falling yields signal economic weakness and are bad for stocks, even though bond prices are rising.

Bond yields continue to act as a leading indicator, turning down before the stock market. During 2010 and again in 2011, bond yields peaked and then turned down, with stocks peaking shortly afterward. The lead time varies from a few weeks to several months, but the sequence is consistent.

Figure 14.2. Bond yields turned down before the stock market during both 2010 and 2011 — yields continue to act as a leading indicator for stocks.

When bond yields fall sharply, dividend-paying stocks become more attractive because they offer higher relative yield. This benefits utilities, consumer staples, healthcare, and REITs. These defensive sectors outperform during periods of falling yields for three reasons: defensive money flows, yield attraction, and lower volatility relative to the market.

Not all bonds behave the same way. Treasury bonds are the safe-haven benchmark, but high-yield (junk) bonds act more like stocks. During risk-off periods, Treasuries rise while junk bonds fall. Widening credit spreads — the gap between Treasury and high-yield yields — signal increasing fear and are negative for stocks.

“Don’t fight the Fed — when the Fed is actively easing, it tends to be positive for stocks.” — John J. Murphy

Quantitative easing created a counterintuitive dynamic: during QE programs, bond yields often rose because QE boosted economic confidence and investors rotated from bonds into stocks. When QE ended, yields fell again as the confidence faded. The Fed’s intervention may have been distorting normal intermarket relationships, raising questions about how reliable the yield/stock correlation would remain.

The yield curve remains one of the most reliable recession predictors. An inverted yield curve — short-term rates above long-term rates — has preceded every recession since 1970. A steepening curve is generally positive for the economy and for banks, which borrow short and lend long. Operation Twist in 2011–12 deliberately flattened the curve by pushing down long-term rates, which had the opposite effect of QE.

Chapter 15: The Link between Bonds and Commodities

Bond prices and commodity prices normally trend in opposite directions — one of the oldest intermarket relationships. Rising commodities create inflation pressure, which pushes bond prices down (yields up). Falling commodities create deflationary pressure, pushing bond prices up (yields down). Bond yields and commodity prices trend in the same direction, which is just the other side of the same coin.

Figure 15.1. Treasury bond and commodity prices trending in opposite directions from the 1980s through the 2000s — one of the foundations of intermarket analysis.
Figure 15.2. The inverse bond-commodity link held well between 2003 and 2006 as commodities rose and bonds fell.

The inverse relationship held well during 2003–2006 as commodities rose and bonds fell. It started to break down during 2007 when commodities kept rising (driven by the falling dollar and emerging market demand) while bond prices also rose (driven by flight to safety as the subprime crisis unfolded). When both markets rise for different reasons, the correlation weakens — a sign that something unusual is happening in the financial system.

Copper often shows a cleaner inverse relationship with Treasury bonds than the broader CRB Index. As a primarily industrial metal, copper is more directly tied to economic growth. Copper bottomed in early 2009 along with stocks, and at the same time Treasury bond prices peaked and started to decline as money rotated from safe havens into riskier assets.

The CRB/Treasury bond ratio serves as a barometer of the inflation/deflation balance. A rising ratio — commodities outperforming bonds — signals a growth/inflationary environment that’s generally positive for stocks. A falling ratio signals deflation/recession and is generally negative for stocks. The ratio also guides sector allocation: a rising ratio favors energy, materials, and industrials, while a falling ratio favors utilities, staples, and healthcare.

The tension between commodity inflation and bond deflation defined the post-2008 environment. The Fed’s QE programs were designed to boost commodity prices while keeping bond yields low — an artificial balance that could unwind if the Fed reduced its purchases. China (commodity demand, inflationary force) and Japan (decades of deflation, downward pressure on global yields) represented the two major external drivers pulling in opposite directions.

Conclusion

Murphy concludes by looking forward from 2012. After a decade of bonds outperforming stocks, the pendulum may have been swinging back. Bond yields near historic lows meant limited upside for bonds, while stocks remained well below their 2007 peaks with potential for gains. The Nasdaq hit a 12-year high in 2012, technology leadership was returning, banking stocks showed new relative strength, and homebuilders were forming a bottom consistent with the 18-year real estate cycle.

He also warns that the Fed’s unprecedented intervention has distorted some normal intermarket relationships. Once the Fed stops manipulating rates, normal relationships should reassert themselves — as they did in the early 1950s after a similar period of rate manipulation during and after WWII. History suggests the distortion is temporary.

His final advice is characteristically straightforward: trade trends, not opinions. Markets are forward-looking discounting mechanisms that know more than any individual analyst. Charts are the best way to see what the markets are saying, and intermarket analysis provides the framework for understanding why. The combination of charting skills and intermarket understanding gives the trader a significant edge.

Trading & Investing Lessons

Bond Yields as a Leading Indicator

The most reliable timing tool in the book is bond yield direction. Yields peaked before stocks in 2000 and again in 2007. When bond yields start falling after a sustained rise, it’s time to reduce stock exposure and increase bond allocation. This is not a contrarian signal — it’s the market’s forward-looking assessment of economic weakness arriving months before the data confirms it.

The Inverted Yield Curve as a Recession Alarm

Every recession since 1970 was preceded by an inverted yield curve. When the 2-year rate rises above the 10-year rate, reduce risk aggressively. The stocks most vulnerable are those with the highest valuations — in 2000, that meant tech stocks; the specific sector changes, but the principle doesn’t.

Energy Leadership as a Sell Signal

When energy stocks become market leaders, the economic expansion is nearing its end. When energy and consumer staples are simultaneously the two strongest sectors, it’s time to rotate into defensive positions or out of equities entirely. This signal flashed in both 2000 and 2007.

Dollar Direction as an Allocation Guide

Rising dollar: overweight U.S. stocks, underweight foreign stocks and commodities. Falling dollar: overweight foreign stocks, commodities, gold, and emerging markets. The dollar direction is the starting point for asset allocation decisions.

Ratio Analysis for Regime Detection

The Dow/gold ratio, CRB/S&P ratio, bond/stock ratio, and discretionary/staples ratio each signal important regime changes. When these ratios break long-term trendlines, it signals generational shifts in asset class leadership that last years, not months. The CRB/S&P ratio breaking its 20-year downtrend in 2002 was a signal to shift from paper to hard assets for the rest of the decade.

Sector Rotation as a Business Cycle Clock

Rather than waiting for economic data to confirm a recession, watch sector rotations. Small caps peaking, transports weakening, retail underperforming, and consumer staples gaining relative strength all point to a late-cycle economy. The sector rotation model gives you a 3-6 month head start on the economic data.

Key Takeaways

The Four-Asset Intermarket Web

Stocks, bonds, commodities, and currencies form an interconnected system. A change in one market creates ripple effects through the others. Never analyze a single market in isolation.

The Peaking Sequence Is Reliable

At market tops, bond yields peak first, stocks peak second, commodities peak third. This sequence held in 2000–01 and 2007–08. The bottoming sequence is less reliable.

Deflation Changed Everything

The shift from an inflationary to a deflationary environment after 1998 reversed the bond-stock relationship and created a new positive correlation between stocks and commodities. These changes mirror the 1930s.

Charts Beat Economics

Markets are forward-looking discounting mechanisms. Charts spotted deflation years before the Fed acknowledged it. Wall Street paid heavily for ignoring chart warnings in both 2000 and 2007.

Sector Rotation Reveals the Cycle

Sector leadership tells you where the economy is in the business cycle more reliably than economic statistics. The stock market is a better indicator of the economy than the economy is of stocks.

Gold Thrives in Extremes

Gold performs well during both inflation (1970s) and deflation (1930s, post-2000). It serves as an alternative to paper assets and benefits from a falling dollar and a falling stock market simultaneously.

ETFs Democratized Intermarket Trading

What once required futures accounts and institutional access can now be implemented with sector SPDRs, bond ETFs, commodity ETFs, and currency ETFs available in any brokerage account.

Contrarian Ideas

Falling Interest Rates Can Be Bad for Stocks

The conventional wisdom that lower rates help stocks only applies in a normal inflationary environment. In a deflationary climate, falling rates signal economic weakness, and the Fed’s rate cuts become increasingly ineffective. The Fed lowered rates twelve times after the 2000 crash with no apparent effect on stocks.

Economic Data Is Nearly Useless for Timing

Economists rely on backward-looking data that tells you where the economy has been, not where it’s going. The stock market peaked in October 2007; the official recession didn’t begin until December. By the time the data confirms a recession, stocks have already fallen significantly.

Global Decoupling Is a Myth

The theory that foreign stocks are immune to U.S. problems is wrong. Global stock correlations spike during downturns. In 2008, foreign stocks fell more than U.S. stocks, not less. The only safe havens during global downturns are Treasury bonds and gold.

Rising Commodity Prices Can Be Bullish

In the old normal, rising commodities were inflationary and bad for stocks. In the new deflationary environment, rising commodities signal economic recovery and are closely correlated with rising stocks. Context determines whether commodity strength is positive or negative.

Proposals & Frameworks

The Intermarket Principles

Murphy codifies a set of intermarket principles that serve as the book’s theoretical foundation: all global markets are linked; the four asset classes are stocks, bonds, commodities, and currencies; dollar and commodities trend in opposite directions; bond prices and commodities trend in opposite directions; since 1998, bond and stock prices have trended inversely; since 2008, stocks and commodities have been closely correlated; bonds usually change direction before stocks; stocks usually change direction before commodities.

The Sector Rotation Model

Murphy’s sector rotation model maps nine market sectors to business cycle stages: cyclicals and tech at bottoms, industrials in early recovery, materials and energy near tops, staples and healthcare and utilities in contraction. Energy and staples leading simultaneously equals maximum bearishness.

The Peaking/Bottoming Sequence

Bond yields peak first, stocks second, commodities third at market tops. The same sequence theoretically applies at bottoms, but has been less reliable. At bottoms, the sequence can reverse when unusual forces like Fed intervention are in play.

Ratio Analysis Framework

Divide any two markets to create a relative strength ratio. Apply trendline analysis to the ratio. A rising ratio means the numerator is outperforming. Key ratios: bond/stock (risk appetite), CRB/S&P (paper vs. hard assets), discretionary/staples (consumer confidence), Dow/gold (secular regime), silver/gold (industrial demand vs. safe haven).

Long Cycle Framework

Three nested cycles shape markets: the 4-year business cycle (presidential cycle), the 18-year real estate cycle (Clarence Long), and the 55–60-year Kondratieff Wave. When multiple long cycles turn down simultaneously, as after 2005, the damage is far more severe than a normal recession.

What Didn’t Fit

The 1994 Stealth Bear Market’s Hidden Damage

The 1994 bear market is called “stealth” because major indexes only lost about 10%, masking devastating losses underneath. Small-caps fell 15%, transports crashed 26%, and utilities plummeted 34%. Transportation stocks were especially vulnerable to rising oil prices — fuel costs cut directly into margins. Utilities, the most rate-sensitive sector, suffered as rising commodity prices pushed bond yields sharply higher. This episode demonstrates why headline indexes can be misleading and why breadth analysis and sector-level monitoring are essential. The intermarket sequence played out perfectly under the surface even though the S&P barely flinched.

The Fed’s Belated Deflation Acknowledgment

On May 6, 2003, the Fed issued a statement acknowledging for the first time since World War II that it feared deflation more than inflation. Markets had been trading on this assumption for years — the CRB had been signaling deflation since the Asian crisis. The Fed had lowered rates twelve times to the lowest level in forty years (1.25%) and was running out of ammunition. Talk of the Fed buying longer-maturity bonds to push down long-term rates contributed to a plunge in yields to 45-year lows. By June, the plunge reversed as a massive rotation out of bonds into stocks began — the new bull market had already started three months earlier, once again showing that markets lead economic policy rather than following it.

Gold Mining Stocks and the Homestake Mining Story

During the 1929–32 deflation, gold bullion was fixed at a government-set price, but Homestake Mining gained 300% while the stock market lost 90%. Gold mining stocks provided leveraged exposure to gold’s safe-haven status. Murphy uses this historical example to argue that gold isn’t just an inflation hedge — it’s a hedge against paper asset devaluation in any extreme environment. The Gold Miners ETF (GDX) gained more than 400% from 2000 to 2011 while the S&P 500 lost 15%. One reason Wall Street strategists are seldom bullish on gold: they know rising gold is usually bearish for the stocks they’re paid to promote.

Consumer Discretionary vs. Staples as a Real-Time Economic Gauge

The consumer discretionary/staples ratio deserves special attention because it does three things simultaneously when it turns: it tells you when to rotate between the two sectors, it confirms whether a market bottom or top has been completed, and it implies whether the recession is starting or ending. In April 2009, the ratio broke its downtrend trendline, and the recession ended three months later. Murphy makes the point that consumer spending represents 70% of the U.S. economy, so the trend of retail stocks — a subset of discretionary — is one of the most reliable real-time economic indicators available. The financial media kept claiming housing weakness wasn’t hurting the economy, but the retail/S&P ratio had been falling since mid-2005, telling a different story entirely.

Neural Networks and Quantitative Intermarket Models

Murphy briefly mentions using neural network software to quantify intermarket relationships. Neural networks can identify nonlinear relationships between markets that may not be visible on standard charts. He treats them as supplements to, not replacements for, visual analysis. The mention is brief but significant — it suggests that even a chart purist like Murphy recognized the potential for computational approaches to capture intermarket dynamics that the human eye might miss. These tools have evolved significantly since the book’s publication and are now central to many quantitative trading strategies.

About the Author

John J. Murphy is one of the most influential technical analysts in the history of financial markets. He served as a technical analyst for CNBC for seven years, bringing chart-based market analysis to a mainstream television audience at a time when most financial commentators relied exclusively on fundamental analysis. Before CNBC, he was director of Merrill Lynch’s Technical Analysis Futures Division, where he developed and applied intermarket techniques to institutional trading.

Murphy’s book Technical Analysis of the Financial Markets (1999) is widely considered the bible of technical analysis and is required reading for the Chartered Market Technician (CMT) program. His earlier work Intermarket Analysis (2004) is also part of the CMT curriculum. He founded MurphyMorris.com, which later merged with StockCharts.com, where he served as chief technical analyst.

With over forty years of experience in technical analysis, Murphy is credited with establishing intermarket analysis as a recognized branch of the discipline. His initial paper on intermarket relationships was rejected by the Market Technicians Association for lack of proof — but twenty years later, intermarket analysis ranked fifth out of fourteen technical disciplines in an MTA poll. His vindication was complete.

Why This Book Matters

Trading with Intermarket Analysis matters because it teaches a way of seeing that most market participants lack. The average investor watches one market — usually stocks — and tries to predict its direction using information from that market alone. Murphy demonstrates that the most powerful signals come from other markets entirely: bond yields peaking before stocks, the dollar turning before commodities, sector rotations shifting months before the economic data confirms a change. This broader vision doesn’t just improve timing; it changes the fundamental approach to risk management.

The book is also one of the clearest explanations of how the deflationary environment that began in the late 1990s broke longstanding intermarket rules and replaced them with new ones. Many investors and advisors still operate on the old assumption that falling rates help stocks. Murphy shows why that assumption is dangerous in a deflationary world and provides the visual evidence to prove it. For anyone trying to understand why the post-2008 market environment “feels different,” this book provides the structural explanation.

Perhaps most importantly, the intermarket framework gives traders a way to be early rather than late. When bond yields, dollar direction, and sector rotations align, they provide a forward-looking picture that economic data simply cannot match. The Fed didn’t acknowledge deflation until 2003; markets had been signaling it since 1998. Housing stocks peaked in 2005, a full two years before subprime became a household word. The chartist who follows intermarket signals is consistently months ahead of the consensus.
