# Trading with Intermarket Analysis — Summary
## John J. Murphy (2013, Wiley Trading, 352 pages)

### Core Thesis
All financial markets — stocks, bonds, commodities, and currencies — are interconnected. Understanding these relationships gives traders a significant edge by improving their "peripheral trading vision." The book is an updated guide to intermarket analysis reflecting the "new normal" that emerged after the 1997-98 Asian currency crisis and the 2008 financial meltdown.

### The Four Asset Classes and Their Relationships
1. **Dollar and Commodities**: Trend in opposite directions (most reliable relationship)
2. **Bond Prices and Commodities**: Trend in opposite directions
3. **Bond Yields and Stocks**: Positively correlated since 1998 (reversed from prior relationship)
4. **Stocks and Commodities**: Closely correlated since 2008 (new relationship)

### The "Old Normal" vs "New Normal"
- **Before 1998**: Rising bond prices (falling yields) were good for stocks. Stocks and commodities had a weak or inverse correlation.
- **After 1998**: Rising bond prices (falling yields) became bad for stocks — a sign of deflationary stress. The Asian currency crisis caused the decoupling.
- **After 2008**: Stocks and commodities became closely correlated, both tied to the global economic cycle. Rising commodities = economic strength = good for stocks.

### Critical Peaking Sequence
At market tops: **Bond yields peak first → Stocks peak second → Commodities peak third**. This occurred in both 2000 and 2007-2008. Bonds are a leading indicator for stocks; stocks are a leading indicator for commodities and the economy.

### Sector Rotation and the Business Cycle
- **Early recovery**: Consumer discretionary, technology, transports, small-caps lead
- **Full expansion**: Basic materials, industrials lead
- **Late expansion**: Energy becomes market leader (WARNING sign)
- **Contraction**: Consumer staples, healthcare, utilities lead
- **Key danger signal**: When energy AND consumer staples are the two strongest sectors simultaneously (happened in 2000 and 2007)

### Key Trading Signals
- Energy stock leadership = bull market nearing completion
- Inverted yield curve = recession coming (preceded every recession since 1970)
- Negative divergence in NYSE advance-decline line = market breadth deteriorating
- Small caps underperforming large caps = defensive rotation underway
- Falling dollar = bullish for commodities, gold, foreign stocks, emerging markets
- Rising dollar = bullish for U.S. stocks, Treasuries; bearish for commodities

### Historical Parallels
- The post-2000 deflationary environment mirrors the 1930s in many ways
- Kondratieff Wave (~60-year cycle) peaked in 1920 and 1980
- 18-year real estate cycle helps explain housing boom/bust timing
- Gold thrives in both inflationary (1970s) and deflationary (1930s, post-2000) environments

### ETFs as Implementation Tools
ETFs revolutionized intermarket trading by making all four asset classes — stocks, bonds, commodities, currencies — easily tradeable. Sector rotation strategies that once required futures markets can now be implemented with Select Sector SPDRs and similar products.

### Key Takeaways for Traders
1. Never analyze one market in isolation — always check related markets
2. Bond yields are the best leading indicator for stocks
3. Dollar direction is the starting point for much of intermarket analysis
4. Sector rotations reveal the business cycle stage better than economic statistics
5. Charts are forward-looking; economic data is backward-looking
6. Trade what the markets are telling you, not opinions or predictions
