Introduction

Gregory Zuckerman opens the book in a fish restaurant in Cambridge, Massachusetts, in September 2017, picking at a salad while a British mathematician named Nick Patterson warns him that nobody will talk. Zuckerman has spent years at the Wall Street Journal and knows how the game works — subjects come around eventually. Not these people. Renaissance Technologies is the most secretive firm on Wall Street, protected by iron-clad thirty-page nondisclosure agreements that bind even retirees. Even archrivals won’t return calls, as if Jim Simons were a Mafia boss they dared not offend.

The numbers justify the secrecy. Renaissance’s Medallion fund has averaged 66% annual returns before fees since 1988, generating over $100 billion in trading profits with roughly 300 employees. That performance dwarfs Warren Buffett, George Soros, Peter Lynch, Ray Dalio — anyone you can name. In recent years, Medallion has pulled in more than $7 billion annually in trading gains, more than Under Armour, Levi Strauss, Hasbro, and Hyatt Hotels combined. The average Renaissance employee holds nearly $50 million in the firm’s hedge funds.

“If we have enough data, I know we can make predictions.” — Jim Simons

The central paradox of the story is that Simons and his team shouldn’t have been the ones to crack the market. None of them took a finance class. Simons did theoretical math — the most impractical kind — and didn’t seriously trade until age forty. He set up shop in a sleepy Long Island town and hired mathematicians and physicists who knew nothing about investing, some outright suspicious of capitalism. Zuckerman compares them to a group of tourists discovering El Dorado on their first trip to South America, plundering the golden city while hardened explorers look on in frustration.

Beyond the money, the book explores what Simons’s revolution means for society. His methods anticipated Silicon Valley’s data obsession by three decades. But the story also raises troubling questions about senior executive Robert Mercer, arguably the person most responsible for Donald Trump’s 2016 election victory, and his daughter Rebekah, who played a key role in the Brexit campaign. The same mathematical genius that conquered markets also produced the funding behind Breitbart News and Steve Bannon.

Zuckerman conducted over 400 interviews with more than 30 current and former Renaissance employees — a remarkable feat given the firm’s legendary secrecy. Simons himself spent more than ten hours discussing certain periods, though he asked Zuckerman not to write the book and refused to discuss trading specifics. The result is the most comprehensive account of Renaissance ever published, and a story full of surprises: for all his insights and prescience, Simons was blindsided by much of what took place in his life. That may be the most enduring lesson of his remarkable story.

The book’s subtitle — “How Jim Simons Launched the Quant Revolution” — understates the scope. This is also a story about the nature of genius, the corrosive effects of wealth, the human need to find patterns in chaos, and the question of whether removing human judgment from consequential decisions makes the world better or worse. By early 2019, quant investors controlled roughly 30% of all stock trading, topping both individual investors and traditional fund managers. The world Simons built is now the world everyone inhabits.

Prologue: Fall 1990

The book opens with Jim Simons at fifty-two, sitting in his office on the thirty-third floor of a midtown Manhattan building, nearly 5’10″ with graying hair and craggy features that reminded friends of Humphrey Bogart. He has spent twelve years searching for a successful investing formula and the results so far are painful. His early trading relied on intuition — the ups and downs made him “sick to his stomach.” He has recruited two renowned mathematicians, but both partnerships crumbled amid losses and acrimony. At one point an employee worried Simons was contemplating suicide.

Simons chain-smoked three packs of Merit cigarettes a day, filling the office with a permanent haze. Staff manufactured excuses to keep meetings outdoors. In trading, as in mathematics, breakthroughs in midlife are vanishingly rare. He was attempting something the financial world considered not just unlikely but absurd: building a fully automated system that would beat the greatest living investors using nothing but math and computing power.

Now Simons is working with Elwyn Berlekamp, a game theorist from UC Berkeley, to build a computer model that would remove human emotion from investing entirely. Berlekamp is a remarkable figure: PhD at twenty-three, acclaimed codebreaker, groundbreaking university administrator who now needs a new challenge. When he discusses his trading methods with Berkeley business school graduates, they mock it as quackery. “Oh, come on. Computers can’t compete with human judgment,” one tells him.

Berlekamp approached the problem like the game theorist he was. Trading infrequently magnified the consequences of each decision — one bad call could wipe out months of gains. But trading constantly, hundreds of times a day, meant you only needed to be right slightly more than half the time. The casino analogy was irresistible: just as a casino profits from a slim edge across millions of bets, their fund could profit from a bare majority of winning trades. Meanwhile, Simons kept calling about gold and copper positions five to ten hours a week — the man who championed removing humans from trading couldn’t stop trading by hand.

“We’re gonna do things better than humans can.” — Elwyn Berlekamp

The context is 1990, the golden age of traditional investing. Soros, Lynch, and Bill Gross are producing enormous profits with intelligence, intuition, and old-fashioned research. Few people even use email. The internet browser hasn’t been invented. The idea that math professors using computers might trounce celebrated investors seems far-fetched if not outright ludicrous. But Simons is undeterred. He projects 80% returns for the next year. Berlekamp can’t handle the pressure and eventually quits. Simons tells a friend: “The hell with it, I’m just going to run it myself.”

Berlekamp sold his shares at six times what he had paid sixteen months earlier and returned to Berkeley. He later conceded: “It never occurred to me that we’d go through the roof.” With Berlekamp gone, Simons faced the defining choice of his career: shut down the experiment or take sole command of a system he still didn’t fully trust. He chose the latter. Everything that followed — the hiring of Henry Laufer, the recruitment from IBM, the rise of the most profitable fund in history — flowed from that stubborn moment of conviction.

Chapter One: Young Jimmy Simons

In 1952, fourteen-year-old Jim Simons works at Breck’s Garden Supply in Newton, Massachusetts, but he is so lost in thought he keeps misplacing everything. He gets demoted to sweeping floors — which he considers a stroke of luck, since it leaves him alone to ponder. When a couple asks about his plans, he tells them he wants to study mathematics at MIT. They burst out laughing. “They thought it was the funniest thing they had ever heard,” Simons later recalls.

Marcia and Matthew Simons welcomed their only child, James Harris, in spring 1938. Marcia funneled all her ambitions into her son: “She was ambitious for me. She saw me as her project.” His father Matty was one of ten children who hustled from age six, selling newspapers and hauling bags at train stations. Kind and soft-spoken, Matty worked as a sales manager for 20th Century Fox, bundling Shirley Temple films with four or five others and convincing theaters to buy the whole package. His father-in-law Peter Kantor lured Matty into a shoe factory with promises of an ownership stake that never materialized. Matty later told his son he wished he hadn’t abandoned a promising career — a lesson that deeply shaped Simons.

“The lesson was: Do what you like in life, not what you feel you ‘should’ do.” — Simons

The grandfather Kantor was a character: heavyset, flamboyant, drove Cadillacs, wore elevator shoes at five-foot-four, blew his wealth on horse races and paramours, and always kept $1,500 in his breast pocket. He was found dead with that exact amount, surrounded by Christmas cards from “dozens of appreciative female friends.” He taught his grandsons Russian phrases: “Give me a cigarette” and “Kiss my ass.”

Simons showed mathematical genius from the start. At age three he doubled numbers and figured out all powers of 2 up to 1,024. At four, riding in the car, he stumbled onto Zeno’s Paradox — if you keep halving the gas in a tank, it should never run out. He spent long stretches sitting with his thoughts, once climbing a tree in nursery school just to ponder. At MIT he blasted through his degree and at twenty-three experienced an existential crisis: “Is this it?” He loved math but needed adventure. He tried starting a floor tile factory in Bogotá, went back to Harvard as a professor, but was secretly under financial pressure — borrowing money and persuading his parents to mortgage their home.

Friends Jim Harpel and Simons sneaked into burlesque shows at the Old Howard Theatre; Harpel’s father showed up one evening and quipped: “Not much bird watching around here.” A doctor once advised young Jimmy to pursue medicine instead: “You can’t make any money in mathematics.” Simons wanted to try anyway.

His first wife Barbara put it best: “Jim understood at an early age that money is power. He didn’t want people to have power over him.” He rarely wore socks, even in frigid winters: “I just decided it takes too much of my time to put them on.”

Chapter Two: The IDA Years

In 1964, Simons quit Harvard to join the Institute for Defense Analyses in Princeton, doubling his salary. The IDA was an idea factory for Cold War codebreaking, staffed by roughly twenty-five PhD holders hired for brainpower and creativity rather than specific expertise. All employees shared the same title — technical staff member — and routinely shared credit. Afternoon rituals included chess, puzzles, Go, and champagne toasts after solving problems. One cryptologist was discovered living in the computer room in his bathrobe after being thrown out of his home. Another typed with bare, smelly toes. Simons threw dinner parties with rum-heavy Fish House Punch and hosted high-stakes poker that lasted until morning.

The IDA shaped Simons in two critical ways. First, it taught him to build mathematical models that discern patterns in seemingly meaningless data — exactly what he would later do with financial markets. He helped develop an ultrafast code-breaking algorithm that earned him a personal visit to Washington for thanks from the Defense Department. Second, it established the culture he would replicate at Renaissance: hire brilliant people, give them freedom, and let ideas emerge.

Lenny Baum, who would become Simons’s first investing partner, developed a credo that captured the IDA spirit: “Bad ideas is good, good ideas is terrific, no ideas is terrible.” Baum also developed the Baum-Welch algorithm for analyzing hidden Markov processes — one of the twentieth century’s notable advances in machine learning, enabling Google’s search engine and the first effective speech recognition.

At twenty-eight, Simons conceived an electronic stock trading company called iStar. The plan was accidentally left on a Xerox machine and discovered by deputy director Lee Neuwirth. It never raised money, but the ambition was there. Simons also produced his most important mathematical work during this period — the Simons equation, which proposed a uniform solution through six dimensions. Three Italians including Fields Medal winner Enrico Bombieri confirmed his counterexample in dimension seven, establishing Simons as one of the world’s preeminent geometers.

With Baum and colleagues, Simons wrote a classified paper proposing a stock-trading system using hidden Markov models that claimed annual gains of at least 50%. Rather than using economics to identify market states, they used pure math to determine which states best fit the pricing data. The poker analogy was apt: you don’t need to know why your opponent is glum, just identify his moods and adjust your strategy.

Simons’s time at the IDA ended dramatically. When the New York Times published a pro-Vietnam War piece by General Maxwell Taylor, Simons wrote a six-paragraph rebuttal. A Newsweek stringer then asked about Defense Department employees opposed to the war. Simons unwisely said he would only do personal math research until the war ended. His boss Henry Leibler delivered the news: “You’re fired.” Simons protested that he was a permanent member. Leibler replied: “The only difference between a permanent member and a temporary member is a temporary member has a contract. You don’t.” Getting fired convinced Simons he needed to gain control over his own future.

He landed as chairman of the math department at Stony Brook University, where Governor Rockefeller was pouring $100 million into making it the “Berkeley of the East.” Simons recruited James Ax from Cornell by driving five hours and sending care packages filled with pebbles and climate reminders.

Chapter Three: Monemetrics and Baum

Simons named his first trading firm Monemetrics — combining “money” and “econometrics” — and recruited Baum to join him. Baum was a Brooklyn native, son of Russian Jewish immigrants, who had married Julia Lieberman, a woman who fled Czechoslovakia in 1941 on the last boat from Europe. Even more successful at codebreaking than Simons, most of Baum’s hundreds of papers remained classified. He fit the absentminded professor stereotype perfectly — once arriving at work with half a beard because he got distracted mid-shave.

Baum scrutinized currency data and determined the Japanese yen moved in steady lines, perhaps because the Japanese government intervened “in precise Japanese manner.” He built an algorithm to buy below the trend line and sell above it. The work was simple but instilled confidence. “Once I got Lenny involved, I could see the possibilities of building models,” Simons said. They named the fund Limroy — an amalgam of Lord Jim (the Conrad novel) and Royal Bank of Bermuda.

The British pound trade showed what Baum could do at his best. Lying on the beach in summer 1979, Baum mulled weakness in sterling. An expert who advised them made so much selling pounds he named his son Sterling. Baum raced to the office too late — Thatcher had already released the pound and it rose five cents. But Baum was unfazed: “That’s nothing! It’s going to go up fifty cents — maybe more!” He was right. The fund followed with similar wins in the yen, deutsche mark, and Swiss franc, piling up more than $43 million in profits between July 1979 and March 1982.

They hired Greg Hullender, a nineteen-year-old on the verge of getting kicked out of Caltech for running an unauthorized trading operation from his dorm room. He had turned $200 into $2,000 repackaging stock options for students. Simons offered $9,000 a year. Hullender thought it sounded like “the shadiest thing in the world,” but since he was about to be expelled, options were limited. He didn’t even have a driver’s license, so Simons lent him a bicycle.

Together they built the Piggy Basket system — named because live hogs were one of its components. Staffer Carole Alberghine climbed on sofas to update figures on graph paper hanging from the ceiling, eventually toppling and suffering a permanent injury. The system developed an appetite for potato futures, shifting two-thirds of their cash into Maine potatoes. The CFTC called: Monemetrics was close to cornering the global potato market. “They think we’re trying to corner the market on spuds!” Simons told Hullender with amusement.

The darkest moment came after bond trading losses. Hullender found Simons lying on his couch, musing about Joseph Conrad’s Lord Jim — a story of failure and redemption. “He had a really good death, though. Jim died nobly.” Hullender left the room wondering if his boss was contemplating suicide. But Simons emerged from the funk more determined than ever.

“I don’t want to have to worry about the market every minute. I want models that will make money while I sleep. A pure system without humans interfering.” — Jim Simons

The partnership with Baum eventually unraveled over their different temperaments. Baum was an optimist who liked to buy and hold; Simons wanted systematic discipline. Their fight over gold was legendary: Simons screamed “Sell the fucking gold, Lenny!” as gold soared past $800. When Baum finally sold, gold kept rising — but then the bubble burst. A catastrophic coffee trade and a bond market collapse triggered automatic clauses that forced liquidation. Baum returned to Princeton, competed in Go tournaments into his eighties, walked two miles to coffee shops to comfort homesick undergraduates, and passed away in 2017 at eighty-six.

Chapter Four: James Ax

James Ax was everything Baum was not: brilliant, angry, and chronically difficult. A Bronx native from Stuyvesant High School whose father abandoned the family when he was seven, Ax won the Cole Prize in number theory and became the youngest-ever full professor at Cornell at twenty-nine. He was also the kind of person who showed up to poker wearing a ski mask to conceal his expressions — and still lost, because he straightened up whenever he had a good hand.

At Stony Brook, Ax told colleagues that mathematicians do their best work by age thirty, and his own publication rate dwindled. A bitter divorce cost him custody of his sons — he would have no contact with them for fifteen years. He interrupted so frequently during meetings that colleague Leonard Charlap carried a bell to ring each time. He got into a fistfight with an associate professor and threatened another: “I’m going to do everything I can to ruin your career, fair or foul.” When Simons offered him a chance to build a mathematical trading system, Ax seized it like a lifeline.

Simons backed Ax to build a mathematical trading system. They set up Axcom Limited in Huntington Beach, California, where oil wells pumped in the parking lot. Ax considered sunspots and lunar phases as potential signals. He microwaved his underwear in the office (the microwave burst into flames). He staged intense weight-loss competitions with colleagues. But beneath the eccentricity, Ax and his data specialist Sandor Straus were building something important: early trend-following models using moving averages.

The critical hire was René Carmona, an expert in stochastic differential equations who proposed using nonlinear regressions — letting computers search historical data for similar trading environments and track what happened next. This was an early machine-learning system: the model generates predictions based on complex patterns humans can’t detect. When Simons saw the results, he blanched. He could understand linear models. This was a black box.

“Just follow the data, Jim. It’s not me, it’s the data.” — René Carmona

Ax increasingly isolated himself in a Malibu estate, going to the office once a week. He installed a salt lick in his yard to attract deer and spent long stretches staring from his window. Colleagues theorized he chose coastal homes to avoid dealing with people on at least one side. When the fund collapsed nearly 30% on a failed soybean cornering attempt, Simons’s accountant Mark Silber halted all trading. Ax was furious: “How can he stop me from trading?”

Berlekamp bought Ax’s stake in the firm. Ax retreated, wrote poetry, enrolled in screenwriting, completed a sci-fi thriller called Bots, and eventually tracked down the sons he hadn’t seen in fifteen years. “Hi. This is James Ax,” he said to his son Brian at Brown University. The boys forgave him. He died of colon cancer at sixty-nine. On his tombstone: the Ax-Kochen theorem formula.

Chapter Five: Berlekamp Joins

Elwyn Berlekamp was drawn to investing by John Larry Kelly Jr.’s paper on bet sizing: accurate information combined with properly sized wagers produces an edge. Berlekamp had zero interest in finance initially, but Kelly’s math was irresistible. At UC Berkeley he became an expert decoding digital information for NASA, cofounded combinatorial game theory, and stored five multicolored BIC pens in his front pocket.

In 1985, Eastman Kodak acquired Berlekamp’s block-codes firm, handing him a windfall of several million dollars. He bought top-rated municipal bonds, only to watch a rumor about changes to their tax status crush their value. Congress never acted, but the damage was done. Berlekamp called Simons for commodities advice. “I have just the opportunity for you,” Simons replied. Within months, Berlekamp was studying Axcom’s trading systems and seeing both their promise and their flaws.

On his first visit to Axcom, Jim Ax greeted him with an annoyed look: “If Simons wants you to work for us, he’ll have to pay for you. I know I’m not.” Berlekamp decided to stick around like George Costanza in Seinfeld — just never leave. He quickly saw that Axcom’s models didn’t size trades properly and held investments for dangerously long periods. Ax brushed off his recommendations as “tinkering.”

Berlekamp argued that holding positions for weeks was dangerous in volatile markets — frequency itself was a form of risk management. Ax cited the cost of rapid trading and noted that Straus’s intraday data wasn’t clean enough for short-term models. In October 1987, Axcom sidestepped Black Monday — Ax presciently bought Eurodollar futures as the Dow plummeted 22.6%. Word spread that Simons had math wizards attempting something genuinely new.

In March 1988, Simons shut down Limroy and launched Medallion — named for the prestigious math awards both Simons and Ax had received. Edward Thorp showed interest but canceled after visiting: “Going to their offices was like walking into a giant ashtray.” Simons was still chain-smoking three packs of Merit cigarettes a day.

Meanwhile, Ax had retreated to a seaside estate in Malibu, coming to the office just once a week. He installed a salt lick in his yard to attract deer and spent long stretches staring from his window. Colleagues theorized he chose coastal homes to avoid dealing with people on at least one side. An employee overheard someone asking Ax on the phone: “Okay, so what kind of cereal do you want me to bring?” The research suffered. Carmona drove weekly to bring food, books, and necessities, enduring conspiracy theories and grueling games of paddle tennis. When the fund collapsed nearly 30% after a failed soybean cornering attempt by an Italian conglomerate, Simons’s accountant Mark Silber halted all trading. Ax was furious: “How can he stop me from trading?”

Chapter Six: Berlekamp Takes Control

After buying out Ax, Berlekamp leased offices in the Wells Fargo Building near Berkeley and installed a satellite receiver atop Tilden Tower in Oakland. A month later, the Loma Prieta earthquake killed 63 people, collapsed shelves, and toppled the receiver. But Berlekamp had a key insight that would transform the fund: trading frequently reduces risk.

“If you trade a lot, you only need to be right 51 percent of the time.” — Berlekamp

Like a casino profiting from a slight edge across millions of bets, Medallion would trade so frequently that it could make money on a bare majority of trades. Berlekamp cut the average holding period from a week and a half to a day and a half. The team discovered trading anomalies: prices fell before key economic reports and rose right after. Mondays followed Fridays; Tuesdays saw reversions. Deutsche marks showed 20% correlation between consecutive periods. Some patterns were so faint the team called them ghosts — they kept reappearing with just enough frequency to be tradeable.

Sandor Straus’s obsessive collection of tick data — information most investors ignored — turned out to be a goldmine. With new MIPS computers powerful enough to parse it, Renaissance had data nobody else had cleaned or analyzed. They implemented with $27 million and began scoring profits almost every day.

The gains came so reliably that Simons revived an old IDA tradition: champagne after a big day. But $1 million days became routine, and the drinking got out of hand, so Simons raised the threshold — champagne only if returns rose 3% in a single day. One persistent failure was the Canadian dollar: every trade was a dud. A Chicago trader explained: “Don’t you know, Jim? Those guys are crooks.” Only three traders handled the exchange, and they shared information and front-ran Simons’s orders. The system immediately eliminated Canadian dollars. At Berkeley, business school graduates continued to mock Berlekamp’s methods: “We were viewed as flakes with ridiculous ideas.”

A near-death experience came when they discovered every position was held at Stotler Group, run by a top Chicago Board of Trade official rumored to have heavy debts. Simons’s advice was instant: “Elwyn, when you smell smoke, you get the hell out!” Months later Stotler filed for bankruptcy.

Medallion gained 55.9% in 1990, even as the broader market suffered from the Iraq-Kuwait invasion. When Simons called about gold and oil positions during the crisis, Berlekamp refused: “The system will determine what we trade.” This was the fundamental tension at the heart of Renaissance: Simons championed removing humans from trading, yet spent five to ten hours a week trading gold and copper by hand. Berlekamp eventually sold his shares at six times what he paid sixteen months earlier and returned to teaching. He died in 2019 at seventy-eight.

Chapter Seven: A History of Quant Trading

Zuckerman steps back to trace the centuries-long history of systematic trading. Babylonians recorded barley prices on clay tablets. In sixteenth-century Europe, Christopher Kurz forecasted spice prices using astrology and back-testing. In eighteenth-century Japan, Munehisa Homma invented candlestick charting for rice and created a mean-reversion strategy. Charles Dow created the Dow Jones Industrial Average and birthed technical analysis.

Along the way came a series of spectacular flameouts. William D. Gann, a Texas cotton rancher, turned $130 into $12,000 in a month using his “Law of Vibration” — yet died with a net worth of only $100,000. Gerald Tsai Jr., the most influential investor of the late 1960s, kept his Fidelity war room at 55°F to sharpen his staff, then was crushed in the 1969-70 bear market. Richard Dennis, the “Prince of the Pit,” codified his rules and shared them with recruits called “turtles” — he made $80 million in 1986 and was obliterated by the 1987 crash. The pattern was consistent: systematic traders kept building fortresses that the market eventually knocked down.

The modern story begins with Edward Thorp, who worked with Claude Shannon at MIT and embraced Kelly’s proportional betting system. Thorp’s bestselling Beat the Dealer outlined systematic gambling tactics, and he brought the same approach to Wall Street. His fund Princeton/Newport Partners attracted Paul Newman and averaged over 15% annually for nineteen years. Thorp was “surprised and encouraged by how little was known by so many” in finance.

At Morgan Stanley, Gerry Bamberger invented pairs trading in the early 1980s. Nunzio Tartaglia, a wiry astrophysicist from Brooklyn, renamed the group Automated Proprietary Trading and generated $50 million in annual profits. New hires included David Shaw and Robert Frey. But Morgan Stanley’s senior management was never comfortable with computer- driven trading and shut the group down — squandering “some of the most lucrative trading strategies in the history of finance.”

Shaw went on to found D. E. Shaw & Co. above a communist bookstore in Union Square. He hired English majors, chess masters, stand-up comedians, and a demolitions specialist. An early employee named Jeffrey Bezos wrote a business plan for an online bookstore on his laptop in a van while traveling — originally called “Cadabra” before being renamed Amazon.com because the first name sounded too much like “cadaver.”

The chapter’s cautionary tale is Black Monday — October 19, 1987, when the Dow plunged 23%, partly blamed on portfolio insurance triggering cascading computer selling. Benoit Mandelbrot argued markets have fractal patterns and deliver more unexpected events than models assume — a view later championed by Nassim Nicholas Taleb.

Chapter Eight: Henry Laufer’s Rise

Henry Laufer finished undergrad at City College and grad school at Princeton in two years each. A bookish introvert who carried a slide rule, he became Simons’s best partner yet. Laufer made two decisions that transformed Medallion. First, the fund would employ a single trading model rather than multiple models — allowing it to detect correlations across asset classes and making it easier to add new investments later. Second, Laufer split the trading day into five-minute bars, revealing nonrandom effects invisible at longer time scales.

The five-minute bars unlocked a world of patterns. Bar 188 in cocoa fell on nervous days. Bar 199 rebounded. Bar 50 in gold saw buying on inflation days. Combination effects emerged: paired investments like gold and silver moved together at specific times. Any signal needed a p-value under 0.01 to be included in the model. Laufer then built the betting algorithm — a dynamic, real-time program identifying optimal trades that was an early form of machine learning.

“Our system is a living thing; it’s always modifying.” — Jim Simons

The key recruit was Nick Patterson, a British cryptologist born in Bayswater with facial dysplasia — a rare disorder distorting the left side of his face, leaving one eye blind. Sent to Catholic boarding school, he evolved into “the school brain, a British stock character — seen as odd but useful, so they left me alone.” Patterson became a poker stud at a London coffee shop that turned out to be a front for an illegal gambling ring. The proprietor offered him a chess-hustle partnership. Patterson’s response: “Are you nuts? You can’t make any money in mathematics.”

Patterson joined the British government’s cryptography unit — the same one made famous by Alan Turing during World War II. He harnessed Bayes’ theorem, updating beliefs with new information, and solved a long-standing problem so valuable that top-secret documents were labeled “For US Eyes Only and for Nick Patterson” — “It was James Bond stuff.” When the pay scale elevated administrators above cryptologists, Patterson bristled: “It was the insult, not the money. I had to get out.” He joined the IDA, and when a colleague received permission to visit Russia, Patterson realized the Cold War was ending and his job would vanish. Simons called urgently: “We need to talk. Will you work for me?”

Patterson coined the name for transaction costs: The Devil. Knowing copper will rise from $3.00 to $3.10 is meaningless if your buying pushes the price to $3.05. Renaissance developed sophisticated approaches to direct trades across exchanges, reducing market impact, and began trading sixteen times a day. In 1994, Medallion soared 71% — in a year when the Fed surprised markets with rate hikes, causing deep losses for many.

Simons concluded Renaissance was exploiting the foibles of fellow speculators. Tversky and Kahneman’s work on irrationality, and Richard Thaler’s behavioral economics, explained why: loss aversion, anchoring, and the endowment effect cause investors to make similar mistakes repeatedly. It was no coincidence Medallion made its largest profits during extreme market turbulence.

“What you’re really modeling is human behavior. Humans are most predictable in times of high stress — they act instinctively and panic.” — Kresimir Penavic

Chapter Nine: The Stock Challenge

The 1990s belonged to star stock pickers. Peter Lynch at Fidelity’s Magellan grew the fund from $100 million to $16 billion, averaging 29% annually. His mantra was “know what you own.” He made a killing on Dunkin’ Donuts (“didn’t have to worry about low-priced Korean imports”) and rode Hanes to a 10x gain after his wife brought home a pair of L’eggs pantyhose. His most important tool was the telephone, not a computer. By 1990, one in every hundred Americans was invested in Magellan. “The computer won’t tell you if a business trend is going to last a month or a year,” Lynch insisted.

Bill Gross, the “Bond King,” had paid through business school with blackjack winnings using Thorp’s card-counting system. At PIMCO he became adept at predicting interest rates, wore custom-made shirts with a loose tie after yoga, and in 1995 made a huge wager on falling rates that generated 20% gains for the largest bond fund ever assembled.

Stanley Druckenmiller, a shaggy- haired Pittsburgh native who had dropped out of an economics PhD, took over Soros’s Quantum Fund at thirty-five. In 1992, Druckenmiller predicted Britain would break from the European Exchange Rate Mechanism. Soros said the position didn’t make sense — then reversed himself: “Trades like this only happen every twenty years or so.” He urged a much bigger bet. They shorted $10 billion of British pounds. On September 16, 1992, they earned over $1 billion in 24 hours. The Quantum Fund gained over 60% in 1993, controlling $8 billion.

Against these titans, the idea that math professors with computers might beat the market still seemed laughable. Lynch, Gross, Druckenmiller, and Soros were famous precisely because they relied on human judgment — reading annual reports, visiting factories, sensing market psychology. Computers were for back-office clerks, not portfolio managers.

Renaissance’s stock efforts, by contrast, were struggling badly. Robert Frey ran a statistical arbitrage strategy backed by Simons, using market-wide factors to identify stocks that had temporarily diverged from their historic relationships. The portfolio was market neutral — betting on relative movements, not direction. The models identified profitable trades consistently, but lost most of the profits in execution. Staffers complained the system was too sensitive to tiny fluctuations, buying and selling before shares had a chance to rise.

Frey compared himself to a chef with a delicious recipe who dropped most of the food on the way to the dinner table. “I wasn’t the best person to get the trains running on time,” he later admitted. Simons needed someone who could fix the plumbing — who could build a system that translated brilliant predictions into actual profits. He found them at IBM.

Chapter Ten: Brown and Mercer Fix It

Robert Mercer grew up fascinated by computers. At age ten, his father showed him the punch cards of an IBM 650; Robert created programs in an oversize notebook and carried it for years before touching an actual machine. At Kirtland Air Force Base, he loved the “solitude of the computer lab late at night, air-conditioned smell, sound of discs whirring.” He rewrote an electromagnetic fields program to run 100 times faster — and nobody cared. At IBM’s speech-recognition group, he was tall, fit, laconic, and ate potato chips in order of size: broken ones first, then smallest to largest.

Peter Brown came from a different world. His father Henry Brown created the world’s first money-market mutual fund. Peter stuffed envelopes as a teenager while Henry worked every day except Christmas and ate peanut butter sandwiches. Peter was the animated one — thick curly hair, infectious charm, but also brutally blunt. He once grabbed a marker from an intern’s hand during a whiteboard session and sneered: “This is kindergarten computer science!”

At IBM, they worked under Fred Jelinek, who modeled language as a game of chance: “Pie” is more likely to follow “apple” than “him.” The team used the Baum-Welch algorithm — coinvented by Simons’s former partner Lenny Baum — and Bayesian mathematics. Brown realized probabilistic models could translate text, using Canadian parliamentary proceedings as training data. Their advances laid the groundwork for Amazon Alexa, Apple Siri, and Google Translate. Brown also convinced IBM to hire a chess team; the machine was renamed Deep Blue after the original name “Deep Thought” drew awkward associations with the 1972 film Deep Throat. In 1997, millions watched Deep Blue defeat Garry Kasparov.

Frustration with IBM’s refusal to commercialize their advances simmered for years. Then tragedy struck. In late winter 1993, Mercer’s mother was killed and his sister injured when their car hit ice. That Easter, his father succumbed to a progressive illness. A few months later, Patterson called asking why Mercer hadn’t responded to a letter: “Just come and talk to me. What have you got to lose?” Brown looked at his newborn daughter, thought about Mercer struggling with college bills, and decided it might actually make sense to work in finance for a few years.

When Nick Patterson recruited them to Renaissance in 1993, Mercer was skeptical that hedge funds added anything to society. But Brown was enthused when he learned Simons’s original partner was the coinventor of the algorithm they used every day at IBM. The stock-trading system they inherited was brilliant at picking trades but terrible at executing them. Mercer diagnosed the problem: “There’s many a slip ’twixt the cup and the lip.”

They built an automated single system that programmed all limitations — broker leverage caps, unavailable shorts, risk parameters — into one self-correcting optimization loop running several times per hour. It was half a million lines of code, compared to tens of thousands in the old system. When David Magerman found a critical bug — the S&P 500 index was coded as a static figure from 1991 instead of an updating variable — the long losing streak ended overnight.

Chapter Eleven: The Medallion Powerhouse

By summer 1997, Medallion managed over $900 million, mostly in futures. The stock-trading profits were still tiny, but good enough to merge Nova (the stock fund) into Medallion, creating a single hedge fund trading almost every type of investment. Each week, Simons, Brown, and Mercer assigned themselves three peer-reviewed papers to read and present. After several hundred papers, they gave up — published tactics fell apart when tested. Brown concluded: “Any time you hear financial experts talking about how the market went up because of such and such — remember it’s all nonsense.”

Simons insisted that all staffers have full access to source code, readable in cleartext with no hidden corners. Even secretaries had access, which proved unwieldy. The single monolithic system meant researchers could wander into colleagues’ offices offering suggestions. Annual exotic vacations and tiny-lunchroom lunches built camaraderie. The peer pressure was intense: “If you didn’t make much progress, you’d feel pressure. That was how your self-worth was determined,” said Robert Frey.

The compensation structure was radical. Bonuses depended on how well the fund performed, not individual contributions. Glen Whitney explained: “Discover a predictive source, fix a bug, make the code run faster, get coffee for the woman down the hall with a great idea — bonuses depend on how well the fund performs, not if your boss liked your tie.” Top employees could invest their own money in Medallion, which was the biggest perk of all. Lifetime nondisclosure agreements and noncompetes reduced defection risk, though full source-code access meant any staffer could theoretically walk out with the firm’s secrets.

The culture was strikingly unwealthy. Visitor Peter Weinberger stood in the parking lot in 1996 and saw nothing but old Saturns, Corollas, and Camrys. At a 1997 lunch, someone asked whether anyone flew first class. The table went silent until one embarrassed mathematician admitted: “I do. My wife insists on it.” A visitor compared the atmosphere to “perpetual exam week.”

The contrast with Long-Term Capital Management proved instructive. LTCM, loaded with Nobel laureates and averaging nearly 50% in its first three years, calculated it was unlikely to lose more than $35 million in a single day. In August 1998, after Russia’s debt default, it dropped $553 million on a single Friday. Medallion gained 42% that year. Patterson drew the lesson: “LTCM’s basic error was believing its models were truth. We never believed our models reflected reality — just some aspects of reality.” Renaissance didn’t borrow as heavily, didn’t hire economists with preconceived theories, and never confused a useful approximation with the truth.

The three-step process for discovering trading signals was: identify anomalous patterns, ensure they’re statistically significant and nonrandom, and see if they could be explained. About 60% of investments with sudden big price changes would snap back — reversion was the most enduring signal. More than half the signals were nonintuitive. Brown explained: “If there were signals that made a lot of sense that were very strong, they would have long ago been traded out.” One defense against overfitting: researcher David Leinweber showed US stock returns could be predicted with 99% accuracy by Bangladesh butter production.

Chapter Twelve: Data Deluge

After 2000, Renaissance began ingesting one terabyte of new information annually: every trade order, earnings report, executive trade, news flash, and offshore insurance claim.

“There’s no data like more data.” — Robert Mercer

The stock trading bore striking similarities to speech recognition — the reason Renaissance raided IBM’s team. They developed super-short-term signals, executing 150,000 to 300,000 trades daily. After a 98.5% return in 2000, Medallion rose 33% in 2001 while the S&P gained just 0.2%. Global expansion followed — signals in Canada, Japan, the UK, France, Germany, Hong Kong. Combining foreign with US algorithms dropped the correlation to overall markets, pushing the Sharpe ratio from roughly 2.0 in the 1990s to approximately 6.0 by early 2003.

Basket options from Deutsche Bank and Barclays turbocharged returns. Medallion’s computers sent trading instructions sometimes every second. Competitors generated about $7 per dollar of cash; Medallion generated $12.50. During 2002’s downturn, that ratio hit $20 per dollar. By 2002 the fund managed $5 billion but controlled over $60 billion in positions. The tax benefits were enormous — options enabled long-term capital gains treatment even for trades lasting days. A 2014 Senate subcommittee found Renaissance had paid $6.8 billion less in taxes through these structures.

The wealth was becoming impossible to ignore. Scientists who once drove Corollas were buying mansions in the Renaissance Riviera near Old Field. Simons had a fourteen-acre estate overlooking Long Island Sound. Mercer built an estate with full-length portraits of his daughters and hired exterminators to kill thousands of bugs for his daughter Heather Sue’s blowout wedding. A refrigerator in the office displayed the fund’s latest annual return. Brown had mixed feelings about the wealth — he drove a Prius and wore clothing with holes. His son once visited Mercer’s mansion and asked: “Dad, don’t you and Bob do the same thing?”

The wealth brought internal tension. Foreign-born scientists hired from rival firms earned $40 million or more a year and complained about overpaid “deadwood.” Alexey Kononenko, a Ukrainian mathematician, regularly ignored assignments and skipped meetings about his behavior. Thirteen top executives gathered at Simons’s estate to decide what to do; nobody wanted to be there. Firing him risked a mass exodus — the nondisclosures were hard to enforce and Kononenko could return to Eastern Europe beyond American law. The consensus: he would stay and get a promotion. David Magerman was disgusted: “This is disgusting! If we don’t shut them down, I’m quitting.” He stayed anyway. The atmosphere took on a Lord of the Flies quality.

By 2003, Brown and Mercer’s stock-trading profits were twice Laufer’s futures returns. At lunch at Billie’s 1890, a wood-paneled saloon in Port Jefferson, Simons told them he was thinking about retiring. “You’ll take over. I want you to become co-CEOs.”

Chapter Thirteen: RIEF Launch

By 2004, Medallion’s Sharpe ratio hit 7.5. But the fund was bumping against its own success: it returned gains annually to employees to avoid getting too big, discarding longer-term signals in the process. Simons saw an opportunity. A new fund called RIEF (Renaissance Institutional Equities Fund) would use those discarded signals. Scientists determined they could beat the market by a few percentage points even at $100 billion.

Senior sales exec David Dwyer led campus tours that treated scientists like exotic creatures. “Rarely did they come and not invest,” he said. Mercer was kept away from investors for being unpredictable. Once asked how the firm made money, he said: “We have a signal. Sometimes it tells us to buy Chrysler, sometimes it tells us to sell.” Chrysler hadn’t existed since 1998. The endowment overlooked the flub and invested anyway.

The sales pitch conflated RIEF with Medallion: same firm, same researchers, same risk models. Investors figured RIEF would produce returns “somewhat close” to Medallion’s 38% annualized track record. Renaissance’s own disclaimers were buried in fine print. Meanwhile, an espionage drama played out. Two researchers, Alexander Belopolsky and Pavel Volfbeyn, had defected to Millennium Management after HR mistakenly gave them nondisclosure agreements but not noncompete contracts. They racked up $100 million in profits using a strategy an independent expert called “Henry’s signal” — developed by Laufer. “They stole from us!” Simons erupted. Millennium’s Israel Englander terminated them and paid Renaissance $20 million.

RIEF launched in summer 2005 and by spring 2007 held $35 billion — one of the world’s largest hedge funds. But the quant quake of August 2007 changed everything. A fire sale by an unknown fund triggered cascading losses across every quant strategy. Medallion lost more than $1 billion in a week. RIEF plunged $3 billion. Simons flew back from his mother’s funeral on his Gulfstream and overruled Brown’s instinct to let the models run: “Our job is to survive. If we’re wrong, we can always add later.”

The scene in the central conference room was extraordinary. Six or seven senior people debated while staffers in the open atrium studied faces entering and leaving, gauging desperation levels. Brown urged: “This is an opportunity!” Henry Laufer agreed: “Trust the models — let them run.” Simons shook his head. He was scared banks would sell Medallion’s positions out from under them. That Thursday, Renaissance began reducing equity positions. Over at Morgan Stanley, Peter Muller’s PDT fund lost $600 million in two days. The losses were twenty standard deviations from average — most researchers had never come close to experiencing anything like it.

“You believe in the system, or you don’t.” — Senior Renaissance scientist

Senior scientists were furious — not about the losses, but about Simons interfering with the system. One emailed: “You’re dead wrong.” But Simons stood firm: “I’d make the same decision again.” The human toll was real: salesman Mark Dwyer had sold his home and invested the proceeds in Medallion, borrowing from Deutsche Bank to amplify his position. He was down nearly $1 million in a week; his Crohn’s disease flared from the stress. By late 2007, growing market turbulence aided Medallion’s signals. The fund ended the year up 86%. RIEF, however, kept struggling through the 2008 crisis, dropping 17% while the S&P fell 37%. Investors fled. By the time the dust settled, RIEF was down to less than $5 billion.

Chapter Fourteen: Spending the Fortune

By 2010, Simons was worth roughly $11 billion. He owned a 220-foot yacht called Archimedes — a $100 million vessel with a dining room for twenty, a fireplace, Jacuzzi, and grand piano. His $50 million Fifth Avenue apartment put him next door to George Soros.

After a family member was diagnosed with autism in 2003, Simons convened a roundtable and made a $100 million commitment, becoming the largest private donor to autism research. The Simons Simplex Collection gathered genetic samples from thousands of families and identified over 100 genes. He founded Math for America in 2004, offering $15,000 annual stipends to a thousand top math and science teachers in New York City.

“Instead of beating up the bad teachers, we focus on celebrating the good ones. We give them status and money, and they stay in the field.” — Simons

The autism research grew from personal tragedy. In 2003, Simons’s third-eldest son Nicholas drowned while freediving in Bali. He had been working on a hydroelectric project in Kathmandu, planning a medical clinic in Nepal, and was weeks from starting organic chemistry and medical school. His diving partner went ashore for five minutes to fix a fogged mask; Nicholas was found on the sea floor. Simons told Robert Frey: “My life is either aces or deuces. I don’t understand.” His earlier son Paul had also died young. The family traveled to Nepal and funded a maternity ward at a Kathmandu hospital, then established the Nick Simons Institute for rural health care. Simons gave over $300 million to Stony Brook University and sometimes told Marilyn: “I feel irrelevant.” He found philanthropy as challenging as mathematics and finance.

In 2010, Simons turned seventy-two and handed the reins to Brown and Mercer. Medallion managed roughly $10 billion with approximately 45% average annual returns after fees since 1988 — more than doubling Buffett’s 20%. The Economist declared: “There’s Renaissance Technologies, and then there’s everyone else.” The firm employed about 250 staffers, including over 60 PhDs in AI, quantum physics, computational linguistics, statistics, and number theory. Astronomers proved especially capable — they were accustomed to scrutinizing large, confusing data sets.

During the 2008 crisis, all eyes turned to John Paulson, whose colleague Paolo Pellegrini developed a chart showing the housing market was 40% overpriced. Paulson purchased credit default swaps and harvested a $20 billion windfall. David Einhorn accused Lehman Brothers of accounting tricks and was vindicated when Lehman went bankrupt. Simons was called to testify before a House committee but was something of an afterthought — his quant models were too hard to understand, his methods too difficult to replicate. The lesson seemed obvious: one could outsmart the market, it just took diligence, intelligence, and gumption.

Mercer offered the most honest assessment of their edge: “We’re right 50.75 percent of the time. But we’re 100 percent right 50.75 percent of the time. You can make billions that way.” The firm’s real advantage wasn’t any single signal but the mathematical relationships between forces — multidimensional anomalies others missed. A senior staffer explained: “The inefficiencies are so complex they are, in a sense, hidden in the markets in code. RenTec decrypts them.” The contradictions of Simons’s later years were hard to miss. Renaissance had spent years legally converting short-term trading profits into long-term capital gains, saving billions in taxes — yet Simons decried insufficient government spending on basic education. He hired waves of top scientists while lamenting the talent that private industry siphoned from schools. RIEF, the fund meant to showcase Renaissance’s methods for outside investors, dropped 17% during the 2008 crisis while the S&P fell 37%, then lost another 6% in 2009 as the market soared 26.5%. Investors fled. “No client on earth would touch it,” one salesman admitted. Medallion, meanwhile, kept minting money for insiders.

Chapter Fifteen: The Political Aftermath

Bob Mercer was always an odd duck. Silver-haired with wire-rimmed glasses, he preferred cats to humans, owned a $2.7 million model train, collected machine guns including an AR-18 from The Terminator, and once declared he thought he would live forever. At lunch he baited liberal colleagues with incendiary views: Clinton was a “rapist” and “murderer,” the gold standard should return, government spent too much on African Americans. He brought research claiming radiation extended the lives of Hiroshima survivors. Most colleagues dismissed him as a provocateur — annoying but harmless.

After Obama’s 2008 election, Mercer began making serious political donations. He funded biochemist Arthur Robinson, who collected urine vials for longevity research and published a newsletter claiming climate science was a hoax. In 2011, the Mercer family met Andrew Breitbart and Steve Bannon, purchasing nearly 50% of Breitbart News for $10 million. Daughter Rebekah became the family’s public face — a Stanford biology and math graduate who homeschooled four children and spent $28 million on a seventeen-bedroom triplex at Trump Place.

When Ted Cruz dropped out of the 2016 race, the Mercers pivoted to Trump. On August 13, after a devastating New York Times story on campaign chaos, Rebekah confronted Trump at East Hampton: “No, it’s not bad — it’s over. Unless you make a change. Bring in Steve Bannon and Kellyanne Conway.” Bannon took an Uber to Bedminster. After Trump finished golf, hot dogs, and ice cream, Bannon told him: “No doubt you can win.”

On election night, Republican data projected no more than 204 electoral votes. Trump threw his flip phone: “What a waste of time and money.” Mercer arrived in a posh three-piece gray suit; Bannon joked someone had invited Rich Uncle Pennybags from Monopoly. At 1 a.m., the AP called the race. Meanwhile, Simons was sipping red wine with Abe Lackman: “We were pretty depressed.”

The aftermath tore at Renaissance. Simons was now the most important supporter of the Democratic Party’s super PAC, having donated over $27 million to Democratic causes. David Magerman, a registered Democrat and longtime Renaissance programmer, confronted Mercer directly. In a remarkable exchange, Mercer argued African Americans had been better off before the Civil Rights Act of 1964, which had “infantilized” them by making them dependent on government. Magerman screamed: “Bob — they had to use separate bathrooms and water fountains!”

In February 2017, Magerman went to the Wall Street Journal: “I’m ready to take action. Enough is enough.” He was immediately suspended. At a poker tournament at the St. Regis hotel, Rebekah confronted him: “Karma is a bitch. You’re pond scum. You’ve been pond scum for twenty-five years.” Renaissance fired him in April.

Protesters gathered outside Mercer’s home. Death threats forced security hires. Two hundred scientists endorsed a letter urging the American Museum of Natural History to remove Rebekah from its board. Renaissance’s morale deteriorated. On a crisp October morning, Simons dropped by Mercer’s office: “I think it’s best if you stepped down.” Mercer looked sad and hurt but accepted without protest. He resigned as co-CEO on November 2, 2017.

Chapter Sixteen: Rise of Quant Investing

In late December 2018, Jim Simons is at the Beverly Hills Hotel, worth $23 billion, watching the S&P 500 suffer its worst December since 1931. He picks up the phone and calls Ashvin Chhabra at Euclidean Capital: “Should we be selling short?” The irony is thick — the pioneer of algorithmic trading, the man who built models to remove human emotion from investing, is calling for gut-instinct advice during a market panic. Chhabra hesitates. A day later, stocks firm. The collapse is over.

By 2019, the traditional approach to investing is in retreat. Actively managed funds hold only half the money in stock mutual funds, down from 75%. The stars have fallen: John Paulson suffered deep losses after his 2007 triumph, David Einhorn lost clients, Bill Gross saw his returns slip, and even Warren Buffett’s Berkshire Hathaway trailed the S&P 500 over five, ten, and fifteen years.

Quant investors now control roughly a third of all stock-market trades, doubled since 2013. The spoils are enormous: Simons made an estimated $1.5 billion in 2018, the founders of Two Sigma earned $700 million each, Ray Dalio made $1 billion. Ken Griffin of Citadel spent $238 million on a New York penthouse — the most expensive home ever sold in the United States.

Years after Renaissance adopted machine learning, the rest of the industry followed. Two Sigma deployed 100 teraflops of computing power across 11 petabytes of memory. Firms now ingested satellite images of parking lots, executive tone on earnings calls, Amazon reviews, and cell phone data tracking which store aisles shoppers lingered in. A Renaissance specialist observed: “Instead of the hit-and-miss strategy of trying to find signals using creativity and thought, now you can just throw a class of formulas at a machine- learning engine.” In May 2010, the flash crash sent the Dow plummeting 1,000 points in minutes, stoking fears about automated trading — though many quant strategies actually helped stabilize markets by buying when stocks got cheap.

But the quant revolution has limits. Returns for most quant hedge funds have been underwhelming — about 4.2% annually compared to 3.3% for the average hedge fund. Market data is always changing, unlike physics, and pricing histories are limited. Renaissance remains the stunning exception, profiting on barely more than 50% of trades but doing so with absolute consistency across tens of thousands of positions.

“The approach is scientific. We use very rigorous statistical approaches to determine what we think is underlying.” — Jim Simons

Medallion ended 2018 with roughly 45% gains while the S&P dropped more than 6%. Renaissance assets surged past $60 billion. Simons celebrated his eightieth birthday with a lecture series on physics and a nighttime cruise on Archimedes around Manhattan. “I promise not to turn eighty again,” he told guests.

Epilogue

In his final years, Simons channeled his fortune into the problems that fascinated him most. Louis Reichardt — the first American to climb both Everest and K2 — was hired to improve the lives of people with autism. A repository from 2,800 families was closing in on drugs for potential treatment. The Simons Foundation became the nation’s second-largest private research funder, with a $450 million annual budget.

The Simons Observatory in Chile’s Atacama Desert, 17,000 feet up in clear dry skies, searches for evidence of the Big Bang and cosmic inflation. It is led by David Spergel and, fittingly, Brian Keating — the son of Simons’s early partner James Ax. Simons simultaneously funds the competing bouncing model theory of Paul Steinhardt: “It’s always been aesthetically pleasing to me to think time has gone on forever.”

Most mornings Simons woke around 6:30, walked Central Park, and exercised with a trainer. On daylong hikes he usually led the way, leaving young staffers huffing and puffing behind him. He had switched to electronic cigarettes but kept a pack of beloved Merits tucked in his breast pocket. Asked about his neighbor George Soros, he allowed: “I suppose he’s worth listening to, though he sure talks a lot.” He spent $20 million in 2018 backing Democratic candidates, helping the party regain control of the House.

David Magerman filed two lawsuits against Renaissance and in 2018 reached an amicable settlement that preserved his right to invest in Medallion. He adopted a new cause: combating social media companies, giving nearly $500,000 to a coalition lobbying to break up Facebook. Peter Brown, now sole CEO, still kept his manic pace, sleeping on a Murphy bed at the office and leaning increasingly on other senior staffers. Medallion ended 2018 with roughly 45% gains while the S&P dropped more than 6%. Renaissance assets surged past $60 billion.

In March 2019, Simons addressed hundreds at MIT. On Mercer: “I think he’s a little crazy. But he’s extremely bright. I couldn’t fire him because of his political beliefs.” His life lessons were simple: work with the smartest people you can, be persistent, be guided by beauty. He looked at Marilyn and a grandson in the front row.

“We’ve had a lot of luck.” — Jim Simons

After the ovation, Simons extended a modest wave and, walking slowly, made his way out of the hall, family close behind.

Key Takeaways

Hire for Brainpower, Not Expertise

Renaissance never hired anyone from Wall Street. Instead they recruited mathematicians, physicists, astronomers, linguists — people who could recognize patterns in complex data. The firm’s motto might as well have been: smart people will figure out the problems.

Remove Human Emotion from Decisions

The core lesson of Simons’s career is that human judgment, intuition, and emotion are liabilities in investing. Every time Simons or his partners traded on instinct — gold with Baum, gold and oil during the Gulf War, shorting stocks in December 2018 — the results were worse than trusting the model.

Frequency Reduces Risk

Berlekamp’s casino insight: if you trade enough, you only need to be right slightly more than half the time. Medallion makes money on barely 50.75% of trades, but it makes that bet thousands of times a day.

Culture Is the Moat

A single monolithic system, full code access for everyone, bonuses tied to fund performance rather than individual contributions, lifetime NDAs, and the ability to invest personal money in Medallion — this package kept top talent and prevented the information leaks that destroyed competitors.

Data Quality Matters More Than Quantity

Sandor Straus spent years obsessively collecting and cleaning tick data that everyone else ignored. That data advantage persisted for decades.

Markets Are Exploitable Because Humans Are Irrational

Renaissance’s profits come largely from exploiting behavioral biases — loss aversion, anchoring, herding — that cause investors to make predictable mistakes, especially during periods of extreme stress.

Contrarian Ideas

You Don’t Need to Understand Why

Simons never required that trading signals make intuitive sense. More than half of Medallion’s signals were nonintuitive. As Simons put it: “I don’t know why planets orbit the sun. That doesn’t mean I can’t predict them.”

Finance Knowledge Is a Handicap

Renaissance deliberately avoided hiring anyone with Wall Street experience. The firm’s edge came from treating markets as a pattern-recognition problem, not a financial one. Economists and MBAs brought preconceived notions that got in the way.

The Best Investors Are Not Investors

The most successful money-making machine in history was built by people who didn’t care about business, never took a finance class, and in some cases were suspicious of capitalism itself.

Transparency Strengthens Rather Than Weakens

Giving every employee full access to source code seems like a security nightmare, yet it was central to Renaissance’s advantage. It enabled cross-pollination of ideas and let anyone spot bugs.

Models Should Never Be Fully Trusted

Despite building the most successful model in history, Simons learned repeatedly that blind faith in any model is dangerous. The dot-com crash, the quant quake, and RIEF’s struggles all reinforced the lesson: “Never put your full faith in a model.”

Frameworks & Models

Hidden Markov Models

Markets exist in underlying “states” that can be identified mathematically without understanding the economics. The Baum-Welch algorithm estimates probabilities within these hidden sequences.

The Three-Step Signal Process

1) Identify anomalous patterns in historic pricing data. 2) Ensure they are statistically significant, consistent over time, and nonrandom. 3) See if the behavior can be explained in a reasonable way.

Kelly Criterion

Accurate information + properly sized wagers = sustainable edge. Berlekamp brought Kelly’s bet-sizing formula to Medallion, ensuring trades were scaled to maximize long-term growth.

Five-Minute Bars

Laufer’s innovation of splitting the trading day into five-minute intervals revealed patterns invisible at longer time scales — the granularity that unlocked Medallion’s edge.

The Single Monolithic System

Rather than letting researchers run independent models, Simons insisted on one shared system. This enabled cross-asset correlations and prevented internal competition from degrading overall performance.

Gregory Zuckerman · Portfolio/Penguin · 2019 · 353 pages

About the Author

Gregory Zuckerman is a special writer at the Wall Street Journal, where he has worked for over two decades covering hedge funds, investment banking, and Wall Street dealmaking. He is the author of The Greatest Trade Ever (2009), about John Paulson’s subprime bet, and The Frackers (2013), about the shale oil revolution. Zuckerman has won multiple journalism awards including the Gerald Loeb Award and the New York Press Club Award.

For this book, Zuckerman conducted over 400 interviews with more than 30 current and former Renaissance employees — a remarkable feat given the firm’s legendary secrecy. Simons himself spent more than ten hours discussing certain periods with Zuckerman, though he asked the author not to write the book and refused to discuss trading specifics. Why This Book Matters

This is the definitive account of the most successful money-making operation in history. Medallion’s 66% average annual return over three decades — before fees — is a record that no other investor, fund, or institution has come close to matching. The book matters because it shows that the quant revolution Simons pioneered has fundamentally reshaped not just finance but the broader relationship between data, algorithms, and human decision-making across every industry.

It also serves as a deeply human story about the costs of extraordinary success: children lost, marriages broken, partnerships destroyed, a firm nearly torn apart by the political activities of its own executives. The central tension — between trusting the system and trusting your instincts — runs through every chapter and remains unresolved even at the end. Simons built the ultimate machine for removing human judgment from investing, and yet he never fully trusted it himself.

For anyone interested in how data, algorithms, and machine learning are reshaping every industry — not just finance — this is essential reading. Simons’s story is the origin story of the world we now inhabit.
