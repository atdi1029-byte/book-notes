## Introduction

### Renaissance's Unprecedented Success

- **Renaissance Technologies Medallion fund**: 66% average annual returns, over $100 billion in trading profits
- Outperforms Buffett, Soros, Lynch, Cohen, Dalio — recent years: $7B+ annually in trading gains
- Just ~300 employees vs. thousands at competitors
- **Jim Simons net worth**: ~$23 billion; average Renaissance employee: nearly $50 million in firm's hedge funds

### The Revolution Simons Pioneered

- **Decision to use data and mathematics** instead of intuition/instinct/traditional research
- By early 2019: **quant investors control ~30% of stock trading**, topping individual and traditional investors
- Methods embraced across industries three decades before becoming mainstream

> "If we have enough data, I *know* we can make predictions." — Jim Simons

### Broader Impact and Controversy

- Subsidizes thousands of public-school math/science teachers, developing autism treatments, expanding understanding of origins of life
- Senior executive **Robert Mercer** most responsible for Trump 2016 victory — plucked Steve Bannon and Kellyanne Conway from obscurity
- Mercer's daughter Rebekah: key role in Brexit campaign

### The Core Paradox

> **Simons and team *shouldn't* have been the ones to master the market**

- Never took finance class, didn't care about business; until age forty, only dabbled in trading
- Did **theoretical math** (most impractical kind); firm in sleepy North Shore Long Island town
- Hired mathematicians/scientists who **don't know anything** about investing — some outright suspicious of capitalism

> "It's as if a group of tourists, on their first trip to South America, with a few odd-looking tools and meager provisions, discovered El Dorado and proceeded to plunder the golden city, as hardened explorers looked on in frustration."

### Author's Research

- Over 400 interviews with 30+ current/former Renaissance employees
- Simons spent 10+ hours discussing certain periods but asked author not to write book, refused to discuss trading

> "For all his insights and prescience, Simons was blindsided by much that took place in his life. That may be the most enduring lesson of his remarkable story."

## Prologue: Fall 1990

- Simons at age 52, office on 33rd floor midtown Manhattan — nearly 5'10", graying, craggy features, reminded friends of **Humphrey Bogart**, heavy smoker
- Spent twelve years searching for successful investing formula
- Early trading relied on intuition — ups and downs made him "sick to his stomach"
- At one point, employee worried Simons was contemplating suicide
- Recruited two renowned mathematicians — **partnerships crumbled amid losses and acrimony**

### Partnership with Elwyn Berlekamp

- Working with **Elwyn Berlekamp**, game theorist, to build computer model removing emotion from investing
- PhD at 23, acclaimed code-breaker, renowned mathematician, groundbreaking university administrator — needed new challenge

> "In trading, as in mathematics, it's rare to achieve breakthroughs in midlife."

- Called Berlekamp repeatedly about gold prices; Berlekamp continued teaching at UC Berkeley
- When Berlekamp discussed trading with Berkeley business school graduates, they mocked methods as "quackery"

> "Oh, come on. Computers can't compete with human judgment." — Berkeley graduate
> "We're gonna do things *better* than humans can." — Berlekamp

### The Golden Age of Traditional Investing

- **Soros, Lynch, Bill Gross** producing enormous profits with intelligence, intuition, old-fashioned research
- Few used email in 1990, internet browser not invented, algorithms barely known
- Idea that math professors might use computers to trounce celebrated investors "seemed far-fetched if not outright ludicrous"
- Simons urged Berlekamp: "Next year, I know, we can be up 80 percent"
- Berlekamp eventually quit — Simons told friend: "The hell with it, I'm just going to run it myself."

## Chapter 2: Institute for Defense Analyses (1964-1968)

- **1964**: Quit Harvard to join IDA in Princeton; salary doubled
- IDA taught Simons to develop mathematical models to discern patterns in seemingly meaningless data
- Created algorithms for computer testing; **awful at designing programs** — relied on in-house programmers

### IDA Culture

- All employees given same title: *technical staff member*; routinely shared credit
- ~25 employees with doctorates; hired for brainpower, creativity, ambition rather than specific expertise
- Assumption: researchers would find problems and be clever enough to solve them
- **Lenny Baum** developed credo: "Bad ideas is good, good ideas is terrific, no ideas is terrible"
- **Lee Neuwirth** (deputy director): "It was an idea factory"
- Afternoon tea with chess, puzzles, Go; champagne toasts after solving problems
- Simons and Barbara threw dinner parties with rum-heavy Fish House Punch; high-stakes poker lasting until morning

### Early Trading Ideas at IDA

- **28-year-old Simons** conceived electronic stock trading company called **iStar**
- Plan left on Xerox machine, discovered by deputy director **Neuwirth** — failed to raise money

### Minimal Varieties Research — Breakthrough

- Conversations with **Frederick Almgren Jr.** (Princeton) helped achieve breakthrough
- Created **Simons equation** — uniform solution through six dimensions, proposed counterexample in dimension seven
- Three Italians including **Fields Medal winner Enrico Bombieri** confirmed counterexample
- **1968**: Published "Minimal Varieties in Riemannian Manifolds" — **established Simons as one of world's preeminent geometers**

### Stock-Trading System at IDA

- With **Baum** and colleagues: developed trading system in classified paper "Probabilistic Models for and Prediction of Stock Market Behavior"
- **Proposed method claiming annual gains of at least 50%**
- Searched for "macroscopic variables" to predict short-term behavior; posited market had as many as eight underlying "states"
- **Used hidden Markov model** — didn't try to identify states using economics; used math to determine states best fitting pricing data
- **Poker analogy**: don't need to know *why* opponent is glum, just identify moods and adjust strategy

> "He was a terrific listener... If there was a pony in your pile of horse manure, he would find it." — Neuwirth on Simons

### Vietnam War and Getting Fired

- *New York Times* published opinion piece by **General Maxwell D. Taylor** arguing US winning war
- Simons wrote six-paragraph letter opposing war: "It would make us stronger to rebuild Watts than to bomb Hanoi. It would make us stronger to construct decent transportation on our East Coast than to destroy all bridges in Vietnam."
- Stringer for *Newsweek* contacted Simons about Defense Department employees opposed to war
- Simons said he'd only do personal math research until war ended — this was not actually formalized, probably shouldn't have shared
- **"I was twenty-nine. No one had ever asked to interview me. And I was a wise guy."**
- Leibler gave Taylor heads-up; returned with disturbing news
- **Leibler**: "You're fired." — **Simons**: "You can't fire me, I'm a *permanent* member."
- **Leibler**: "The only difference between a permanent member and temporary member is a temporary member has a contract. You don't."
- Three days later LBJ announced halting bombing — Leibler told Simons not to bother returning
- By then had three young children; getting fired convinced him he needed to gain control over future

## Launching Monemetrics

### Simons Names His Firm

- **Monemetrics** — combining "money" and "econometrics"
- Would hire team to identify trends, develop mathematical formulas to profit

### Recruiting Lenny Baum

- Born 1931, son of Russian Jewish immigrants in Brooklyn; 6 feet tall, barrel chest
- Met **Julia Lieberman** at Brighton Beach — she fled Czechoslovakia in 1941 on last boat from Europe
- **Even more successful at codebreaking than Simons** — credited with IDA's most important still-classified achievements

#### The Baum-Welch Algorithm

- Developed algorithm to analyze **hidden Markov processes** — estimates probabilities within complex sequences with little information
- **One of twentieth century's notable advances in machine learning** — enabled first effective speech recognition and **Google's search engine**
- Most of Baum's hundreds of papers remained classified
- Fit stereotype of absentminded professor — once came to work with half a beard (distracted while shaving)
- Suffered from **cone-rod dystrophy**; ball hit him in head at tennis net
- Remained upbeat: "Let the problem be. It will solve itself."

### Baum Joins Simons (1977)

- Baum scrutinized currency data, determined **Japanese yen** moved in steady lines — perhaps due to Japanese government intervening "in precise Japanese manner"
- Developed algorithm directing purchases below trend line, sales above
- Simple work, but instilled confidence: "Once I got Lenny involved, I could see the possibilities of building models"

### Building the Fund

- Fund called **Limroy** — amalgam of Lord Jim (Conrad novel) and Royal Bank of Bermuda
- Baum would share in 25% cut from all trading profits
- **James Ax** convinced similar models could trade commodities (wheat, soybeans, crude oil)

### The British Pound Trade

- Summer 1979: Baum lying on beach, mulled weakness in British pound
- Expert who advised them made so much selling pounds he **named son Sterling**
- Baum raced to office: "Thatcher is sitting on the pound. She can't hold it down much longer."
- Simons: "It's too bad you didn't come in earlier. Thatcher stood up... The pound just rose five cents."
- Baum unfazed: "That's nothing! It's going to go up fifty cents—maybe more!"
- Baum was right — kept buying, currency kept soaring; followed with yen, deutsche mark, Swiss franc

### Greg Hullender: The Caltech Kid

- **19-year-old** on verge of getting kicked out of Caltech — caught running unauthorized trading operation from dorm room
- Turned $200 into $2,000 in days with stock options; began repackaging options for students: "It was like my own stock exchange"
- Simons offered **$9,000/year salary** — Hullender: "Jim's firm sounded like the shadiest thing in the world." But: "I was in my dorm room waiting to get kicked out—it's not like I had a lot of options"
- Didn't have driver's license, so Simons lent bicycle

### The Piggy Basket System

- Simons bought stacks of books with commodity/bond/currency prices going back decades (some before WWII)
- Hullender surreptitiously carried reels to **Grumman Aerospace** at midnight to convert on supercomputer
- Staffer **Carole Alberghine** climbed on sofas to update figures on graph paper hanging from ceiling — toppled, suffered permanent injury
- System had **live hogs as component** — named **"Piggy Basket"**; used **linear algebra** to produce trade recommendations

> "There's a pattern here; there *has* to be a pattern." — Jim Simons

### The Potato Debacle

- System developed appetite for **potatoes** — shifted two-thirds of cash into Maine potato futures
- **CFTC** called: Monemetrics close to cornering global market for potatoes
- "They think we're trying to corner the market on spuds!" — Simons told Hullender with amusement
- Closed positions at millions in losses; lost confidence in system

### Lord Jim and the Dark Moment

- After bond trading losses, Hullender found Simons lying on couch: "Sometimes I look at this and feel I'm just some guy who doesn't really know what he's doing."
- Simons talked about Lord Jim — failure and redemption, then: "He had a really good death, though. Jim died nobly."
- *Wait, is Simons contemplating suicide?* — Hullender worried

### New Determination: Pure Systems

- Simons emerged from funk more determined than ever to build **high-tech trading system guided by algorithms**

> "I don't want to have to worry about the market every minute. I want models that will make money while I sleep. A pure system without humans interfering." — Jim Simons

- Hullender quit 1980 to go back to school; revealed to colleagues he was gay — "Some things are more important than money"

### Return to Traditional Trading

- With Piggy Basket malfunctioning, Simons and Baum drifted to traditional style
- Consulted with economist **Alan Greenspan**; hired Parisian to translate obscure French financial newsletter
- Set up red phone for urgent financial news; sent office manager **Penny Alberghine** racing to find them
- "Come back in! Wheat's down thirty points!" — Alberghine screamed once
- "Investors are getting eleven and seven-*fucking*-eighths!" — Simons; when young employee gasped: "I know—that *is* an impressive rate!"

### Baum's Success and Buy-and-Hold Philosophy

- Making so much money on intuition that systematic trading seemed wasteful
- "Why do I need to develop those models? It's so much easier making millions in the market than finding mathematical proof." — Baum to daughter
- Optimist by nature; liked to purchase investments and sit on them — courage needed to hold
- "If I don't have a reason for doing something, I leave things as they are and do nothing." — Baum
- More than **$43 million in profits between July 1979 and March 1982** — nearly doubled original stake
- Correctly identified 1982 historic bottom of US stock market
- Purchased six-bedroom, turn-of-the-century home on Long Island Sound
- Once-relaxed Baum turned serious and intense; daughter Stefi: "He was like a different person"
- 1982: Matty Simons told Stony Brook mathematician at Nathaniel's bar mitzvah: "I liked to say, 'My son, the professor,' not 'My son, the businessman.'"

### The Gold-Coffee Disasters

- Fall 1979: both purchased gold futures at ~$250/ounce; gold soared past $700
- Simons dumped position; Baum couldn't bear to sell
- Simons's friend mentioned wife (jeweler) selling gold cuff links: "There's a *line* to sell gold?"
- "Sell the fucking gold, Lenny!" — Simons; Baum refused
- "Jim nagged me. But I couldn't find any specific reason or news for action, so I did nothing." — Baum
- Gold past $800; finally Simons pressed phone to Baum's ear: "Tell him you're selling, Lenny!" — "Alright, alright."
- Gold raced past $865, Baum bitterly complained Simons cost him serious money; then bubble burst — gold under $500
- "He had the buy-low part, but he didn't always have the sell-high part." — Simons
- **Coffee disaster**: Colombian from E. F. Hutton claimed insights; prices dropped 10% — Baum couldn't sell, asked Simons to do it for him; "the dumbest thing I ever did"

### Bond Market Collapse and Baum's Departure (1983-1984)

- 1983: Baum moved to Bermuda; idyllic weather reinforced bullish instincts
- Purchased tens of millions in US bonds — ideal if rates declined as **Paul Volcker** predicted
- But panic selling overcame bond market amid surging Reagan-era bond issuance
- "Lighten up, Lenny. Don't be stubborn." — Simons; Baum maintained typical equanimity
- Huge yen wager also backfired; "This cannot continue!" — Baum, staring at screen
- When positions plummeted 40%, triggered automatic clause forcing Simons to sell all holdings
- **Sad denouement to decades-long relationship**
- Baum ultimately proved prescient on rates; returned to Princeton
- As grew older: focused on prime numbers and unsolved **Riemann hypothesis**; competed in Go tournaments
- In his eighties: walked two miles to coffee shops, comforting homesick undergraduates
- **Summer 2017: Baum passed away at age 86**; children published his latest mathematics paper posthumously

### Impact on Simons

- Losses left deep scars; halted trading, instituted rule: **no performance results until end of month**
- Contemplated giving up trading; "There's no rhyme or reason" — told friend

### James Ax's Background

- Acclaimed mathematician with chiseled looks, biting humor, chronically angry
- Bronx native, **Stuyvesant High School**; father abandoned family when Ax was seven
- Suffered from **Crohn's disease**; earned PhD at Berkeley (1961)
- Collaborated with **Simon Kochen** to solve **Emil Artin's** fifty-year-old conjecture

### Move to Stony Brook and Deterioration

- Told colleague mathematicians do best work by age thirty; publication rate dwindled

- At meetings, interrupted so frequently colleague **Leonard Charlap** carried a bell to ring each time
- Got into fistfight with associate professor; threatened colleague: "I'm going to do everything I can to ruin your career, fair or foul"

### Joining Simons's Trading Venture

- **1979**: Joined Simons in strip-mall office near pizza parlor
- Early work: **trend-following** with moving averages; data riddled with errors

### Sandor Straus: The Data Purist

- PhD from Berkeley, **subpar mathematician**, under $20,000 salary at Stony Brook
- Simons offered to double salary; Straus requested leave of absence rather than resigning
- Directed to dig up historic information — pieced together custom database from magnetic tapes (**Dunn & Hargitt**)
- Discovered **tick data** feed; wrote program on **Apple II** to collect and store it
- Became obsessed: checking prices against yearbooks, *WSJ* archives; saw himself as **explorer on trail of untold riches**

> "I'm not going back to that computer center." — Straus

### Henry Laufer Joins

- Well-regarded Stony Brook mathematician; explored functions of complex numbers
- Created simulations testing **mean reversion** strategies
- Ax had difficulty sharing credit; Simons refused to speak to Laufer for six months (Laufer barely noticed)

### Move to California — Axcom Limited (1985)

- Ax and Straus settled in **Huntington Beach, California**; established **Axcom Limited**
- Simons received 25% of profits; Ax and Straus split remaining 75%
- Oil wells pumped in parking lot; used stair crawler for **VAX-11/750**, forklift for refrigerator-sized **Gould superminicomputer**
- By 1986: trading **twenty-one different futures contracts**
- Ax considered **sunspots, lunar phases**; Straus's AccuWeather cousin reviewed Brazilian weather for coffee prices — waste of time
- Ax staged intense **weight-loss competitions**; placed underwear in office microwave to dry (microwave burst into flames)
- Simons still **chain-smoking three packs of Merits a day** — staff manufactured excuses to keep him outside

### Stochastic Equations and René Carmona

- Ax concluded needed **stochastic differential equations** expert; recruited **René Carmona** (41, later Princeton professor)
- Carmona's key insight: needed **nonlinear regressions** capturing complex market relationships
- Have computers search for similar trading environments in past, track subsequent price reactions
- Straus began to **model data** — making educated guesses about gaps in historical data
- Proposing **early machine-learning system**: model generates predictions based on complex patterns humans can't detect
- Elsewhere, statisticians using similar **kernel methods**

### Simons Resists the "Black Box"

- When shared with Simons, he blanched — linear equations generated trades he could understand; Carmona's results came from running programs for hours, letting computers dig through patterns
- "I can't get comfortable with what this is telling me. I don't understand why [the program is saying to buy and not sell]." — Simons
- "It's a black box!" — Simons said with frustration
- Carmona: "Just follow the data, Jim. It's not me, it's the data."
- Ax: "It works, Jim. And it makes rational sense... humans can't forecast prices."
- In his head, Simons all-in on models; his heart wasn't quite there yet
- "Jim liked to figure out what the model was doing. He wasn't super fond of the kernel." — Straus
- Eventually incorporated **higher dimensional kernel regression** — worked best for trending models
- Some weekly stock data went back to **1800s** — would later help profit from market collapses and unexpected events

- Berlekamp had zero interest in finance initially but Kelly's portfolio theory intrigued him

### Berlekamp's Background

- 1964: Accepted UC Berkeley teaching job; became expert decoding digital info for NASA (Mars, Venus)
- Cofounded **combinatorial game theory**; wrote **Algebraic Coding Theory**; created **Berlekamp's algorithm** for cryptography
- Met Simons at IDA but didn't hit it off: "Jim had an insatiable urge to do finance and make money. He likes action."

### Eastman Kodak Windfall (1985-1986)

- Company acquired Berlekamp's block codes firm — windfall of several million
- Bought top-rated municipal bonds; rumor of tax-status change crushed value (Congress never acted)
- Called Simons for commodities advice; Simons: "I have just the opportunity for you"

### Berlekamp's Critique

- Discovered other problems: held investments for weeks/months, dangerous in volatile markets

### Limroy → Medallion (March 1988)

- Shut down Limroy, launched **Medallion** — named for prestigious math awards each had received
- **Edward Thorp** showed interest but canceled: "going to their offices was like walking into a giant ashtray"

### Ax's California Isolation

- Never truly enjoyed company of others; rented seaside estate in Malibu; went to office just once a week
- After marrying accountant **Frances**, became even less inclined to travel
- Employee overheard saying to Ax: "Okay, so what kind of cereal do you want me to bring?"
- **Carmona** drove weekly to bring food, books, necessities; endured conspiracy theories and grueling paddle tennis

- Installed salt lick in yard to attract deer; spent long stretches staring from window
- Drove white Jaguar; fell headfirst from mountain bike, prompting emergency brain surgery

- Fund down nearly 30%; soybeans collapsed when Italian conglomerate's cornering attempt failed
- Simons's accountant **Mark Silber** ordered halt to all trading based on longer-term signals
- Ax furious: "How can he stop me from trading? He *can't* close me down!"
- Straus didn't want to pick sides — Ax screamed: "How can you be so disloyal!"

### Berlekamp's Buyout (Summer 1989)

- "Why don't I buy your stake in the firm?" — Berlekamp to Ax
- Berlekamp owned 40%, Straus and Simons 25% each, Ax retained 10%
- Ax holed up for months; eventually wrote poetry, enrolled in screenwriting, completed sci-fi thriller *Bots*
- Tracked down son **Brian** at Brown University after **fifteen years** of silence: "Hi. This is James Ax."

## Chapter 6: Berlekamp Takes Control

### 1980s Context

- **Michael Milken** pocketed >$1B (1983-87) before jail; **Ivan Boesky** exchanged briefcases of $100 bills
- Berlekamp: anomaly academic with little use for rumors/tips; bore no resemblance to Wall Street masters
- Valued extreme diets, grueling bike rides — looked emaciated; stored 5 multicolored BIC pens in front pocket

> "Elwyn was a little disheveled, his shirttail out and wrinkled... But he was so smart, I saw past the quirks." — Langdon Wheeler

### Move to Berkeley (September 1989)

- Leased offices in **Wells Fargo Building** near UC Berkeley campus
- Arranged satellite receiver atop **Tilden Tower** in Oakland for up-to-the-minute prices
- Month later: **Loma Prieta earthquake** killed 63; shelves collapsed, satellite receiver toppled

### Implementing Short-Term Trading

- Key insight: buying/selling infrequently magnifies consequences of each move; lots of trades reduces risk
- **Casino analogy**: just as casinos profit from slightly more than half of bets, Medallion wanted to trade so frequently it could score big making money on bare majority

> "If you trade a lot, you only need to be right 51 percent of the time." — Berlekamp

### Discovering Trading Anomalies

- Prices often fell before key economic reports, rose right after (not always, but enough to trade)
- **Laufer** discovered **day-of-week patterns**: Monday followed Friday; Tuesday saw reversions
- **Twenty-four-hour effect**: previous day's trading predicts next day
- **Weekend effect**: buy late Friday if uptrend, sell early Monday
- **Deutsche marks**: 20% correlation between consecutive periods (vs. 10% other currencies, 7% gold, 1% stocks)

> "The time scale doesn't seem to matter. We get the same statistical anomaly." — Berlekamp

- Central banks extend currency trends by slowing abrupt moves; behavioral persistence: "People persist in their habits longer than they should."
- Some patterns so faint team called them **ghosts** — kept reappearing with enough frequency to be worthy

### Straus's Data Advantage

- For years collected **tick data** most investors ignored; new MIPS computers could now parse it all
- "We realized we had been saving intraday data... more reliable and plentiful than what others were using." — Straus

### Implementation: Late 1989

- Laufer split trading day into **five-minute bars** — improved computers made analyzing small slices feasible

### Behavioral Economics Connection

- **Tversky and Kahneman** explored irrationality; **Richard Thaler** spurred **behavioral economics** (both later won Nobel Prizes)

- Consensus emerged: investors act more irrationally than assumed, repeatedly making similar mistakes
- No coincidence Medallion made largest profits during extreme market turbulence — continued for decades
- Simons hadn't set out to exploit biases; over time came to believe errors/overreactions at least partially responsible for profits

> "What you're really modeling is human behavior. Humans are most predictable in times of high stress—they act instinctively and panic." — Kresimir Penavic

## The Three-Step Trading Signal Discovery Process

### By 1997: Statistical Significance Framework
1. Identify anomalous patterns in historic pricing data
2. Ensure **statistically significant**, **consistent over time**, and **nonrandom**
3. See if pricing behavior could be **explained in reasonable way**

- **Enduring success**: betting on retracements — ~60% of investments with big sudden price changes would snap back
- More than half of signals were **nonintuitive** — couldn't fully understand
- "Volume divided by price change three days earlier, yes. But not stock tickers starting with letter *A*"
- Brown: "If there were signals that made a lot of sense that were very strong, they would have long-ago been traded out"
- **Overfitting danger**: David Leinweber determined US stock returns predicted with 99% accuracy by Bangladesh butter production, US cheese production, sheep population
- Renaissance solution: limit money on head-scratching signals, work to develop understanding over time
- Over time, frequently discovered reasonable explanations — gave Medallion leg up on firms that had dismissed phenomena
- Settled on mix: **sensible signals**, **surprising trades with strong statistical results**, **a few bizarre signals so reliable they couldn't be ignored**
- **Medallion increasingly relied on strategies system taught itself** — form of machine learning
- Consistent winner might automatically receive more cash without anyone approving shift or being aware

## Chapter Sixteen: The Rise of Quantitative Investing

### December 2018: Simons's Moment of Doubt
- Worth ~$23 billion; called **Ashvin Chhabra** at **Euclidean Capital** about shorting stocks for protection
- The quant pioneer relying on gut instinct after market turbulence

### Decline of Traditional Active Management
- Actively managed funds held only 50% of stock-mutual fund money (down from 75%)
- Other 50% in **index funds** and passive vehicles acknowledging difficulty of beating market
- Failed stars: **Paulson** (deep losses after 2007 triumph), **Einhorn** ("King David" lost clients), **Bill Gross** (PIMCO returns slipped), **Buffett** (trailed S&P 500 over 5, 10, 15 years)
- Information asymmetry disappeared: any financial figure available instantly via keystroke, captured by machines
- Example: August 2018, **Geron Corporation** shares soared 25% after **Johnson & Johnson** job listing suggested regulatory approval — only those with tech to scour listings instantly benefited
- **Quant investors**: ~1/3 of all stock-market trades (doubled since 2013)
- **2018 earnings**: Simons $1.5B, **Two Sigma** founders $700M each, **Ray Dalio** $1B
- **Ken Griffin** (Citadel): $238M NYC penthouse (most expensive US home ever sold)
- Alternative data: satellite images, executive tone on calls, parking-lot traffic, Amazon reviews, cell phone data on store aisles

### Machine Learning Spreads
- Years after Renaissance adopted machine learning, others embraced these methods
- **Two Sigma**: 100+ teraflops, 11 petabytes memory

> "Instead of the hit-and-miss strategy of trying to find signals using creativity and thought, now you can just throw a class of formulas at a machine-learning engine." — Renaissance specialist

### Limitations
- Not easy to process information and discover signals in noisy data
- Quant hedge funds gained 4.2%/year average vs 3.3% for average hedge fund (5 years to spring 2019, excluding Medallion)
- Information always changing (unlike physics); limited pricing histories

> "Say you're trying to predict how stocks will perform over a one-year horizon. Because we only have decent records back to 1900, there are only 118 nonoverlapping one-year periods to look at in the US." — **Richard Dewey**

### Flash Crash and Systemic Risks
- **May 6, 2010**: Dow plummeted 1,000 points in minutes ("flash crash")
- Automated trading scary like autopilot/self-driving cars despite safety improvements
- But so many quant varieties impossible to generalize; some buy when stocks cheap, helping stabilize
- Humans prone to fear, greed, panic; machines could make markets **more** stable

### Renaissance 2019: Why They Succeed
- **Medallion**: 66% average annual gains before fees since 1988, 39% after fees
- Managed $65 billion; sometimes 5% of daily stock-market trading volume
- Renaissance studies the past, confident investors make similar future decisions

> "The approach is scientific. We use very rigorous statistical approaches to determine what we think is underlying." — Simons

- Renaissance profits on barely >50% of trades — sign of difficulty beating market

- **Elwyn Berlekamp**: if up to him, stocks would have numbers, not names

> "I don't deny that earnings reports and other business news surely move markets. The problem is that so many investors focus so much on these types of news that nearly all of their results cluster very near their average." — Berlekamp
