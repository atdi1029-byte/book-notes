# The Lessons of History — Notes
## Will and Ariel Durant (1968, ~100 pages)

Distillation of the Durants' 11-volume *Story of Civilization*
(~10,000 pages spanning 5,000 years of history). Pulitzer Prize
winners. Written as 13 interconnected essays examining what
history teaches about human nature, society, and civilization.

---

## I. Hesitations

- The historian faces the question: what use have your studies
  been? Can we derive from history any guidance for our present
  condition, any guard against surprise?
- "Most history is guessing, and the rest is prejudice." Even
  the historian who tries to rise above partiality betrays his
  secret predilections in his choice of materials and adjectives.
- Our conclusions from past to future are made hazardous by the
  acceleration of change. Charles Péguy (1909): "the world
  changed less since Jesus Christ than in the last thirty years."
- An element of chance enters history — Alexander drank himself
  to death (323 BC) and let his empire fall apart; Frederick the
  Great was saved by a Czar infatuated with Prussian ways.
- Historiography cannot be a science — it is an industry
  (ferreting facts), an art (establishing order), and a philosophy
  (seeking perspective). "History smiles at all attempts to force
  its flow into theoretical patterns or logical grooves."
- Since man is a composite — spore of species, member of family,
  unit in economy, citizen in state — we may examine history
  through biology, ethnology, psychology, morality, religion,
  economics, politics, and war.
- "Only a fool would try to compress a hundred centuries into a
  hundred pages of hazardous conclusions. We proceed."

## II. History and the Earth

- Human history is brief in cosmic time. First lesson of history:
  modesty. A comet could end it all.
- Geography is "the matrix of history." Rivers drew settlers:
  Egypt = "gift of the Nile," Mesopotamia built between rivers.
  India = Indus and Ganges; China = great rivers.
- Mediterranean dominated 2,000 years (Salamis 480 BC to
  Spanish Armada 1588). After Columbus and Vasco da Gama (1492),
  Atlantic nations rose, Mediterranean cities declined.
- The airplane will again alter the map of civilization. When
  sea power gives way to air power, "we shall have seen one of
  the basic revolutions in history."
- The influence of geographic factors diminishes as technology
  grows. "Man, not the earth, makes civilization."

## III. Biology and History

- History is a fragment of biology. All our economic competition,
  strife for mates, hunger, love, grief, and war are akin to what
  hides under fallen trees or in the waters.
- **Three biological lessons of history:**
  1. **Life is competition.** "Peaceful when food abounds, violent
     when the mouths outrun the food." Cooperation is real but
     mostly a tool of competition — we cooperate within groups
     to compete with other groups. "War is a nation's way of
     eating."
  2. **Life is selection.** Inequality is natural and inborn; it
     grows with civilization's complexity. "Nature smiles at the
     union of freedom and equality in our utopias." Freedom and
     equality are "sworn and everlasting enemies." Leave men
     free, inequalities multiply geometrically.
  3. **Life must breed.** Nature has a passion for quantity as
     prerequisite to selection of quality. She likes large litters.
     A high birth rate usually accompanies low civilization, and
     vice versa.
- Malthus (1798): without periodic checks (famine, pestilence,
  war), birth rate would outstrip food supply. The 19th century
  seemed to refute him, but Malthus would say this merely
  postpones the calamity.
- Birth control's historical role: Julius Caesar (59 BC) offered
  rewards to Romans with many children. Augustus renewed this
  campaign with like futility. Birth control spread in upper
  classes while immigrant stocks replenished the population.
- "The birth rate, like war, may determine the fate of
  theologies." The superior fertility of Catholics may cancel
  the Protestant Reformation and French Enlightenment. "There
  is no humorist like history."

## IV. Race and History

- The Durants examine and systematically dismantle racial
  theories of civilization:
  - **Gobineau** (1853-55): Aryan race superior to all.
  - **Houston Stewart Chamberlain** (1899): narrowed creative
    race to Teutons. Claimed Dante was German, thought he
    heard German accents in St. Paul's epistles.
  - **Madison Grant** (1916): "Nordics" were the creative branch.
- The Durants' rebuttal: A Chinese scholar would note China
  produced the most enduring civilization (2000 BC to present).
  A Mexican scholar could point to Mayan, Aztec, Incan cultures.
  Hindu temples of Madras and Trichinopoly are among the most
  impressive structures on earth. The Khmers built Angkor Wat.
- The Semites built Babylonia, Assyria, Syria, Palestine,
  Phoenicia, Carthage, and Islam. The Jews gave the Bible and
  Christianity, and much of the Koran.
- The ancient cultures of Egypt, Greece, and Rome were products
  of geographical opportunity and economic/political development,
  not racial constitution.
- "It is not the race that makes the civilization, it is the
  civilization that makes the people." The Englishman doesn't
  make English civilization as much as it makes him.
- Racial antipathies are generated predominantly by differences
  of acquired culture — language, dress, habits, morals, religion.
  "A knowledge of history may teach us that civilization is a
  co-operative product, that nearly all peoples have contributed
  to it."

## V. Character and History

- "Society is founded not on the ideals but on the nature of man,
  and the constitution of man rewrites the constitutions of states."
- **Table of Character Elements:** Six positive and six negative
  instincts: Action/Sleep, Fight/Flight, Acquisition/Avoidance,
  Association/Privacy, Mating/Refusal, Parental care/Filial
  dependence. Each generates habits and feelings.
- Human nature has shown little alteration in recorded history.
  "The Greeks of Plato's time behaved very much like the French
  of modern centuries; and the Romans behaved like the English."
- Means and instrumentalities change; motives and ends remain
  the same: to act or rest, acquire or give, fight or retreat,
  mate or reject.
- The "great man" regains his place as a formative force. He
  is not the god Carlyle described — he grows out of his time
  and land. But he is not merely an effect. Churchill's eloquence
  was worth a thousand regiments; Napoleon's tactics won
  campaigns and established states.
- History in the large is the conflict of minorities; the
  majority follows the innovating minority, applauds the victor,
  and supplies the human material of social experiment.
- "Out of every hundred new ideas ninety-nine or more will
  probably be inferior to the traditional responses which they
  propose to replace."
- The conservative who resists change is as valuable as the
  radical who proposes it. "Out of this tension, as out of the
  strife of the sexes and the classes, comes a creative tensile
  strength."

## VI. Morals and History

- Morals are the rules by which a society exhorts its members
  to behavior consistent with its order, security, and growth.
- Moral codes differ because they adjust to historical and
  environmental conditions. Three stages of economic history —
  hunting, agriculture, industry — each demanded different virtues.
- **Hunting stage:** Pugnacity, brutality, greed, and sexual
  readiness were advantages. "Probably every vice was once a
  virtue."
- **Agricultural stage:** Industriousness replaced bravery; thrift
  replaced violence; peace replaced war. Children became economic
  assets. Early marriage, monogamy, multiple children. This code
  produced "some of the strongest characters in history."
- **Industrial stage:** The Industrial Revolution shattered the
  agricultural moral code. Men left home and family. Children
  were no longer economic assets. Marriage delayed. City offered
  "every stimulus and facility for sex." Authority of father
  and mother lost through individualism of industry.
- Sin has flourished in every age. History's consolation: our
  generation hasn't rivaled homosexualism in ancient Greece/Rome
  or Renaissance Italy. Prostitution has been "perennial and
  universal."
- "We must remind ourselves again that history as usually written
  (peccavimus) is quite different from history as usually lived."
  Behind the red facade of war and politics were millions of
  orderly homes, devoted marriages. "Who will dare to write a
  history of human goodness?"
- We cannot be sure that moral laxity is a herald of decay.
  Civilizations decay "quite leisurely" — 250 years after moral
  weakening began in Greece, Hellenic civilization still produced
  masterpieces.

## VII. Religion and History

- "Even the skeptical historian develops a humble respect for
  religion." It has functioned in every land and age: comforting
  the suffering, disciplining the young, conferring meaning on
  the lowliest existence.
- "It has kept the poor (said Napoleon) from murdering the rich."
- Religion and morals: "Heaven and utopia are buckets in a well:
  when one goes down the other goes up; when religion declines
  Communism grows."
- Religion originated in fear of hidden forces — earth, rivers,
  oceans, trees, winds, sky. Priests used these fears to support
  morality and law. Every civilization told its people that moral
  codes were dictated by gods: Thoth for Egypt, Shamash for
  Babylonia, Yahveh for the Jews, Egeria for Rome.
- The Church served the state but claimed morality should stand
  above power. The Emperor Henry IV recognized this by
  submitting to Pope Gregory VII at Canossa (1077).
- The dream broke under nationalism, skepticism, and human
  frailty. The Church stooped to fraud, profiting from the
  mythical "Donation of Constantine" and forged "False Decretals."
- Does history support belief in God? If God means a supreme
  being both intelligent and benevolent, "the answer must be a
  reluctant negative." History remains a natural selection of
  the fittest. "The universe has no prejudice in favor of Christ
  as against Genghis Khan."
- The decline of religious belief began with Copernicus (1543).
  By 1611 John Donne mourned "new philosophy calls all in doubt."
  The Protestant Reformation, deistic movement, higher criticism
  of the Bible, growing acquaintance with other religions,
  exposure of religious frauds — all eroded faith.
- Yet religion has a habit of resurrection. Ikhnaton tried to
  destroy Amon's religion; within a year of his death it was
  restored. Buddha founded a religion without a god; after his
  death Buddhism developed a complex theology with gods, saints,
  and hell.
- "Puritanism and paganism alternate in mutual reaction in
  history." Probably our excesses will bring another reaction.
- Joseph de Maistre: "I do not know what the heart of a rascal
  may be; I know what is in the heart of an honest man; it is
  horrible."
- "As long as there is poverty there will be gods."

## VIII. Economics and History

- "History, according to Karl Marx, is economics in action."
- The Durants partly agree: the Delian Confederacy's money built
  the Parthenon; the Medici financed the Renaissance; the French
  Revolution came because the middle classes had risen to economic
  leadership and itched for political power.
- But Marx underestimated noneconomic incentives: religious
  fervor (Moslem armies), nationalistic ardor (Hitler's troops,
  Japanese kamikazes), mob fury (Gordon riots 1780, Paris
  massacres 1792).
- The concentration of wealth is natural and inevitable, a
  result of the concentration of ability. It recurs regularly in
  history. Democracy, allowing most liberty, accelerates it.
- When concentration reaches an explosive point, history resolves
  it by legislation redistributing wealth or by revolution
  distributing poverty.
- **Solon's reforms in Athens (594 BC):** Devalued currency,
  reduced debts, ended imprisonment for debt, established
  graduated income tax. Within a generation all agreed his
  reforms saved Athens from revolution.
- **Rome:** The Senate's uncompromising course when wealth
  concentrated led to 100 years of class war and civil war
  (Gracchi → Marius → Catiline → Caesar → Augustus).
- **The US (1933-52, 1960-65):** Followed Solon's peaceful
  methods, accomplished moderate redistribution. Upper classes
  "cursed, complied, and resumed the concentration of wealth."
- "We conclude that the concentration of wealth is natural and
  inevitable, and is periodically alleviated by violent or
  peaceable partial redistribution. In this view all economic
  history is the slow heartbeat of the social organism, a vast
  systole and diastole of concentrating wealth and compulsive
  recirculation."

## IX. Socialism and History

- The struggle between socialism and capitalism is part of the
  historic rhythm of wealth concentration and dispersion.
- **Sumeria (2100 BC):** State-organized economy, land as crown
  property, rations from royal storehouses, tens of thousands of
  clay tablets tracking deliveries.
- **Babylonia (1750 BC):** Hammurabi's code fixed wages and
  charges.
- **Ptolemaic Egypt (323-30 BC):** State owned soil, managed
  agriculture, nationalized production of oil/salt/papyrus/textiles,
  controlled all commerce, monopolized banking. At first,
  prosperity. Then pharaohs took to "drink and venery,"
  bureaucratic corruption, strikes, moral disintegration. Egypt
  fell to Roman rule.
- **Diocletian's Rome (AD 301):** Price controls, maximum wages,
  extensive public works, free food. The bureaucracy grew
  expensive and corrupt. Romans fled to the barbarians to escape
  taxes. Medieval serfdom began.
- **China — Szuma Ch'ien (145 BC):** State accumulated stockpiles,
  manipulated prices. **Wang Mang (AD 9-23):** Nationalized land,
  ended slavery, made loans at low interest. The rich plotted his
  fall; rebellion, slain. **Wang An-shih (1068-85):** Pervasive
  governmental control. Bureaucratic corruption and drought
  brought the Son of Heaven to dismiss him.
- **Incas of Peru (13th c.):** Longest-lasting socialist regime.
  State directed all agriculture, labor, trade. System endured
  until Pizarro's conquest (1533).
- **Jesuit settlements in Uruguay (1620-1750):** 150 Jesuits
  organized 200,000 Indians into a socialistic society. Natives
  were "docile and content."
- **Modern socialism:** Marx and Engels gave the movement its
  Magna Carta (Communist Manifesto, 1847) and Bible (Das Kapital).
- The Russian Revolution succeeded because the Czarist government
  was defeated and disgraced by war. Communism was a war economy,
  surviving through continued fear of war.
- Meanwhile capitalism undergoes a correlative process: limiting
  acquisition through semi-socialistic legislation and the
  welfare state. The Hegelian dialectic suggests a synthesis of
  capitalism and socialism.
- "The fear of capitalism has compelled socialism to widen
  freedom, and the fear of socialism has compelled capitalism to
  increase equality. East is West and West is East, and soon
  the twain will meet."

## X. Government and History

- "The first condition of freedom is its limitation; make it
  absolute and it dies in chaos."
- Power naturally converges to a center — hence the
  centralization of power in monarchy by Richelieu/Bismarck over
  feudal barons. A similar process centered power in the US
  federal government.
- Monarchy is the most natural kind of government (authority of
  the father, chieftain). If judged by prevalence and duration,
  it wins over democracy, which has been a "hectic interlude."
- Rome's greatest achievement: the Pax Romana (30 BC - AD 180),
  under what was effectively monarchical rule. Five "good
  emperors" (Nerva through Marcus Aurelius) were chosen by
  adoption, not birth — perhaps history's finest succession.
- Most governments have been oligarchies — "ruled by a minority,
  chosen either by birth, as in aristocracies, or by a religious
  organization, as in theocracies, or by wealth, as in
  democracies."
- Aristocracy at its best: a nursery of statesmanship, repository
  of culture, manners, and taste. At its worst: monopolized
  privilege, myopic exploitation, blind addiction to ancestral ways.
- Does history justify revolutions? In most instances the effects
  would have come through gradual economic developments. "The
  only real revolution is in the enlightenment of the mind and
  the improvement of character."
- **Athenian democracy:** After Pericles' death, class warfare
  between oligarchs and democrats was waged with assassination,
  civil war, and violence. Plato described democracy degenerating
  into anarchy, then dictatorship. "The Platonic wheel had come
  full turn."
- **Roman democracy:** Wealth corrupted elections; when money
  failed, murder was available. Caesar won, established popular
  dictatorship; aristocrats killed him; Augustus ended democracy.
- American democracy had a wider base: British heritage of law,
  Protestantism opening mental liberty, abundance of free land.
  Jefferson was "as skeptical as Voltaire and as revolutionary as
  Rousseau."
- Democracy is the most difficult form of government since it
  requires the widest spread of intelligence. "You mustn't
  enthrone ignorance just because there is so much of it."
- All deductions made, democracy has done less harm and more
  good than any other form of government.
- Warning: if our economy of freedom fails to distribute wealth
  as ably as it creates it, "the road to dictatorship will be
  open to any man who can persuasively promise security to all."

## XI. History and War

- "War is one of the constants of history, and has not diminished
  with civilization or democracy." In 3,421 years of recorded
  history, only 268 have seen no war.
- "Polemos pater panton" (Heracleitus) — war, or competition,
  is the father of all things.
- The causes of war are the same as causes of competition among
  individuals: acquisitiveness, pugnacity, pride.
- The state has our instincts without our restraints. It
  acknowledges no substantial restraints because there is no
  superstate to offer it basic protection.
- In the 17th-18th centuries, war was a contest of aristocracies.
  In the 20th century, communication, transport, weapons, and
  indoctrination made war a struggle of peoples.
- The generals and rulers have smiled at philosophers' dislike of
  war. "What but the victory of Charles Martel at Tours (732)
  kept France and Spain from becoming Mohammedan?"
- The philosopher answers: somewhere, sometime, we must challenge
  a thousand evil precedents and dare to apply the Golden Rule
  to nations, as Buddhist King Ashoka did (262 BC).
- "Magnanimity in politics is not seldom the truest wisdom, and
  a great empire and little minds go ill together" (Edmund Burke).
- The general smiles: "You have forgotten all the lessons of
  history." A world order will come not by agreement but through
  decisive victory. "Perhaps we are now restlessly moving toward
  that higher plateau of competition; we may make contact with
  ambitious species on other planets or stars; soon thereafter
  there will be interplanetary war. Then, and only then, will we
  of this earth be one."

## XII. Growth and Decay

- Civilization = "social order promoting cultural creation."
  Political order secured through custom, morals, and law;
  economic order through continuity of production and exchange;
  cultural creation through freedom and facilities.
- Why do civilizations die? Some (Virgil, Nietzsche) imagined
  eternal recurrence. "There is nothing so foolish but it can
  be found in the philosophers."
- History repeats itself, but only in outline and in the large.
  "There is no certainty that the future will repeat the past.
  Every year is an adventure."
- **Saint-Simon** divided history into "organic" periods (unified
  belief, building) and "critical" periods (doubt, individualism,
  destroying). **Spengler** saw civilizations as organisms with
  independent life spans — centripetal organization → centrifugal
  disorganization.
- States most likely arose through conquest, not social contract.
  The conqueror's decrees were the first laws.
- Growth may be stimulated by challenge (external invasion,
  drought). A challenge successfully met raises the temper of
  a nation and makes it abler.
- Causes of decay: exhausted soil, changed trade routes,
  excessive taxes discouraging investment, loss of foreign markets,
  concentration of wealth disrupting the nation in class war.
- As inequality grows, a cultured minority faces an uncultured
  majority. The majority's ways spread upward — "internal
  barbarization."
- As education spreads, theologies lose credence. The moral code
  loses aura as divine surveillance is removed. An unmoored
  generation surrenders to luxury, corruption, and disorder.
- Do civilizations die? "Again, not quite." Greek civilization
  is not really dead — Homer has more readers now than in his
  own day. "This selective survival of creative minds is the
  most real and beneficent of immortalities."
- Civilizations are "the generations of the racial soul." As
  life overrides death with reproduction, an aging culture hands
  its patrimony to its heirs across the years and seas.

## XIII. Is Progress Real?

- Against nations, morals, and religions rising and falling, the
  idea of progress "finds itself in dubious shape."
- Science is neutral: "it will kill for us as readily as it will
  heal." Francis Bacon's "Knowledge is power" now seems
  inadequate — the Middle Ages, stressing mythology and art,
  may have been wiser than we who "repeatedly enlarge our
  instrumentalities without improving our purposes."
- Has there been progress in philosophy since Confucius? In
  literature since Aeschylus? Is our music more profound than
  Palestrina? Our architecture compared to the Parthenon?
- **Defining progress:** Not as increase in happiness (our
  capacity for fretting is endless), but as "the increasing
  control of the environment by life."
- Progress need not be continuous or universal. At any time some
  nations progress while others decline.
- If the prolongation of life indicates better control of
  environment, then longevity (tripled in 300 years) proves
  progress. "If undertakers are miserable, progress is real."
- Would the Founding Fathers reproach us as ingrates for our
  blindness to our good fortune — living under constitutions
  giving habeas corpus, trial by jury, religious and intellectual
  freedom, emancipation of women?
- "We should not be greatly disturbed by the probability that our
  civilization will die like any other." Perhaps new civilizations
  should take fresh forms.
- A great civilization does not entirely die — *non omnis moritur*.
  The elements of civilization (language, writing, art, song,
  agriculture, family, social organization, morality, charity,
  teaching) have been tenaciously maintained through the perilous
  passage from one civilization to the next. "They are the
  connective tissue of human history."
- If education is the transmission of civilization, we are
  unquestionably progressing. Our finest contemporary achievement
  is our unprecedented expenditure of wealth and toil in providing
  higher education for all.
- "If progress is real despite our whining, it is not because we
  are born any healthier, better, or wiser than infants were in
  the past, but because we are born to a richer heritage, born
  on a higher level of that pedestal which the accumulation of
  knowledge and art raises as the ground and support of our being."
- "History is, above all else, the creation and recording of that
  heritage; progress is its increasing abundance, preservation,
  transmission, and use."
- Final words: For those who study history not merely as a
  warning but as an encouraging remembrance of generative souls,
  "the past ceases to be a depressing chamber of horrors; it
  becomes a celestial city, a spacious country of the mind,
  wherein a thousand saints, statesmen, inventors, scientists,
  poets, artists, musicians, lovers, and philosophers still live
  and speak, teach and carve and sing."
