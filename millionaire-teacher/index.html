Rule 1: Spend Like You Want to Grow Rich

In 2004, an American woman in Singapore drove the latest Jaguar — a $250,000 car on that island — lived in a massive house, and wore an elegant Rolex. Her husband was an investment banker. She hired Andrew Hallam to tutor her son for $150 an hour. The check bounced. Then it bounced again. And again, with “as much regularity as a Kathmandu power outage.” The woman who looked like a millionaire couldn’t reliably cover $150.

This is the central lesson of Rule 1: looking rich and being rich are two completely different things. You can lease a Porsche, borrow for a flashy home, and take five-star vacations. You’ll look wealthy. But you’ll be living on an umbilical cord of bank loans and credit cards — one layoff away from financial ruin. The Hippocratic Rule of Wealth is simple: above all, do no harm. Build assets, not debts. Spend far less than you make and intelligently invest the difference.

“Instead of looking where you think the grass is greener, admire your own yard.” — Andrew Hallam

Most people are trapped on what Hallam calls the hedonic treadmill — a new dining table demands matching plates, which demand a new couch, which demands new carpet, which demands a bigger entertainment system. Like Sisyphus rolling his boulder uphill, consumers who get close to paying off debts reward themselves by adding more. In 2010, the average U.S. household carried $7,490 in credit-card debt. In Nevada, 66% of homeowners owed more on their mortgages than their homes were worth.

Thomas Stanley’s research in The Millionaire Next Door reveals the truth behind actual wealth. Most homes valued above $1 million are owned by non-millionaires with bloated mortgages. Meanwhile, 90% of real millionaires — people with a net worth above $1 million — live in homes valued under $1 million. The median car price paid by American millionaires in 2009 was $31,367. Their most popular brand? Toyota. Warren Buffett’s most expensive car ever was a $55,000 Cadillac. The average decamillionaire paid $41,997.

Russ Perry, a millionaire mechanic Hallam worked with at a bus depot at age 20, crystallized this thinking. Russ judged financial intelligence by car choice. Assets appreciate — houses, stocks. Liabilities depreciate — cars. His rule: never buy new. Buy low-mileage Japanese cars after someone else absorbs the depreciation, drive them carefully, and sell for the same price or more. Hallam took this to heart, buying cars for $1,500–$5,000 and often profiting $800–$1,000 per vehicle. He once drove a 12-year-old Toyota van 8,000 miles to Guadalajara and back, then sold it for $500 more than he paid.

“If you’re truly wealthy, then there’s nothing wrong with blowing money on the odd luxury item. But if you’re trying to become wealthy, and you make those kinds of purchases, you’ll never get there. Never.” — Russ Perry

Hallam’s own story proves the principle. His dad was a mechanic supporting four kids on one salary. At 15, Hallam bought his own clothes. At 16, his own car. After university, teaching seventh grade, he ate potatoes, pasta, and free clams dug from the beach with a retired neighbor named Oscar. Dinner cost under $1. He lived on 30% of his salary and threw 70% at debt reduction. He house-sat for snowbirds, never turned on the heat — his father visited one December and stayed at a hotel the next time — and eventually rented a basement suite 35 miles from school, commuting by bike through rain and sleet. A colleague saw him stuffing a PowerBar at a gas station and said, “We should really start a collection for you at the school.” Meanwhile, his investment portfolio could have bought a brand-new sports car.

The Chinese proverb says wealth doesn’t last more than three generations: one builds it, one maintains it, one squanders it. Stanley’s research confirms this — adults who receive financial gifts end up 43% less wealthy than same-income peers who received nothing. The exception? School teachers and college professors, who apparently know what to do with the money. Financial handouts hinder wealth-building because they disconnect effort from reward. Hallam never received free money, so every purchase triggered the mental calculation: “Do I want to mop floors and stack 50-pound sacks of potatoes to pay for this $10 item?”

Rule 2: Use the Greatest Investment Ally You Have

Compound interest is the concept Albert Einstein supposedly called the eighth wonder of the world. The math is simple — you earn interest on your interest — but the results over decades are staggering. $100 invested at 10% becomes $259 in 10 years, $1,745 in 30 years, and $1,378,061 in 100 years. The U.S. stock market has averaged over 9% annually for the past 90 years, including every crash from 1929 to 2008.

To make this real, Hallam tells the story of Star and Lucy, a parable he calls “The Bohemian Millionaire.” Star grows up on a bohemian island, collecting aluminum cans with her mother Autumn from age 5. Autumn invests Star’s daily $1.45 in the stock market. Star never earns much — she sells handmade crafts — but her mother keeps sending $45 a month throughout her life. Lucy, Star’s friend, becomes an investment banker in New York, drives a BMW, dines at gourmet restaurants, and doesn’t start saving until 40, when she finally puts away $800 a month.

At 65, they compare notes. Star invested a total of $32,400 and has $1,050,180. Lucy invested $240,000 — nearly eight times more — and has only $813,128. Both averaged 9% returns. The entire difference is time. Lucy invested more money but started 35 years later, and compounding couldn’t close the gap.

“Few people despise debt as much as I do. But once you’re debt-free, there’s no feeling like it.” — Andrew Hallam

Before investing, pay off credit-card debt. Most cards charge 18–24% interest. Paying that while hoping to earn 10% in the stock market is, as Hallam puts it, “as absurd as bathing fully clothed in Vaseline and traveling home on the roof of a bus.” Paying off 18% debt is equivalent to a guaranteed, tax-free 18% return. Nobody promising that kind of return is legitimate — think Bernie Madoff.

Hallam explains how stocks actually work through a Willy Wonka analogy. Wonka starts a small chocolate shop, needs money to expand, and sells shares on the New York Stock Exchange. Investors become part-owners. As the factory grows and profits increase, the company pays dividends and the share price rises. Montgomery Burns buys $1,000 of Wonka stock at $10 a share, earns a $50 dividend (5%) and watches the share price climb to $10.50 (another 5%). Total return: 10%. Burns didn’t become Springfield’s richest man by trading frequently — he held for decades.

The practical takeaways: track your expenses for three months to discover where money actually goes. Transfer investment money on payday, not at month’s end. When your salary increases by $1,000, add at least half to investments. Start the Noah Principle immediately — don’t wait for the perfect time, because it never comes.

Rule 3: Small Percentages Pack Big Punches

The 44 Wall Street Fund was the top-ranked fund of the 1970s, beating the S&P 500 for eleven straight years. Then it became the worst performer of the 1980s, losing 73% of its value. Its brand was destroyed, and it was quietly merged into the Cumberland Growth Fund, then the Matterhorn Growth Fund. Today it’s as if the fund never existed. The Lindner Large-Cap Fund beat the S&P every year from 1974 to 1984, then returned just 4.1% annually for the next eighteen years while the index returned 12%. It too was swallowed by a merger.

This is survivorship bias in action. Failed funds vanish from the record, inflating the apparent success rate of active management. When you account for fees, survivorship bias, and taxes, the average U.S. actively managed fund underperformed the S&P 500 by 4.8% annually from 1984 to 1999. The industry carries five hidden costs that drag returns like a swimmer towing carpet through the water:

  • Expense ratios — a $30 billion fund costs investors ~$450 million per year (1.5%), sifted out whether the fund makes money or not
  • 12B1 fees — up to 0.25%, paying for advertising to attract new investors (current investors subsidize the marketing)
  • Trading costs — averaging 0.2% annually, invisible on statements
  • Sales commissions (loads) — as high as 6% upfront, requiring a 6.1% gain just to break even
  • Tax inefficiency — active funds trade their entire portfolio annually (100% turnover), triggering short-term capital gains taxes

A Bogle Financial Markets Research Center 15-year study found that an actively managed fund matching the S&P 500’s performance would need to beat the index by 16.2% over 15 years to match an index fund’s after-tax return — a virtually impossible hurdle.

The futility of picking winning funds is well documented. Morningstar’s five-star rating system identifies funds with great track records, but those ratings predict nothing about future performance. Hulbert’s Financial Digest tested the strategy of continuously buying Morningstar’s top-rated funds from 1994 to 2004: $100 became $165 after taxes. A broad stock market index turned $100 into $271 — 64% more. Yet over 90% of mutual fund money pours into Morningstar’s top-rated funds.

In July 1993, The New York Times launched a 20-year contest pitting high-profile financial advisers against the S&P 500. After just seven years, the index had turned $50,000 into $188,755. The best adviser managed $155,095. The worst, $111,815. The paper quietly discontinued the contest — perhaps the advisers grew tired of the humiliation.

“Adding high expense ratios, 12B1 fees, trading costs, sales commissions, and taxes to your investment is a bit like a boxer standing blindfolded in a ring and asking his opponent to hit him five times on the jaw before the opening bell.” — Andrew Hallam

Even the legends agree. Peter Lynch, who averaged 29% annually managing Fidelity’s Magellan Fund, said publicly: “The public would be better off in an index fund.” Bill Miller, whose Legg Mason Value Trust beat the S&P for 15 straight years, told Money magazine: “A significant portion of one’s assets in equities should be comprised of index funds.” Ted Aronson, who actively manages over $7 billion, invests his own taxable money in index funds: “After tax, active management just can’t win.”

Globally, the cost problem is even worse. An Oxford University Press study found that Canada has the most expensive mutual funds in the world at 3.00% total estimated expenses. The Netherlands is cheapest at 0.82%. The U.S. sits at 1.53%. Three groups will tell you active funds beat indexes: financial advisers who need the commission, PR professionals who write the fund company’s marketing materials, and prideful investors who can’t admit their advisers put self-interest first.

Rule 4: Conquer the Enemy in the Mirror

A mutual fund that averaged 10% annually from 1980 to 2005 should have turned $50,000 into $541,735. But John Bogle’s research showed the average investor in those funds earned only 7.3% — turning $50,000 into just $291,047. The gap cost them $250,688. The reason: emotional behavior. When funds had bad years, investors reduced contributions or fled. When funds soared, they piled in. This buy-high, sell-low cycle is the most expensive mistake in investing.

“After nearly 50 years in this business, I do not know of anybody who has done it successfully and consistently. I don’t even know anybody who knows anybody who has done it successfully and consistently.” — John Bogle, on market timing

Dollar-cost averaging eliminates this problem mechanically. Investing equal amounts monthly regardless of market conditions means you buy fewer shares when prices are high and more when prices are low. If you invested $100 a month into a 10% fund for 25 years, you’d actually average 10% or better. Most investors do the opposite — what Hallam calls “nutty behavior.”

Hallam’s most vivid metaphor is the dog on a leash. His dog Sue was hyperactive — on runs with a long rope, she darted wildly in every direction but ultimately couldn’t cover ground faster than her owner. The stock market works the same way: the dog is the stock price, the owner is business earnings. Short-term, the dog bolts ahead or lags behind. Long-term, it must move with the owner. When Coca-Cola’s stock price rose 966% from 1988 to 1998 while profits grew only 294%, the dog had raced ahead of its owner. The stock price in 2011 was still below its 1998 peak — the leashed dog always returns.

Jeremy Siegel at the Wharton School studied every stock market movement above 5% since 1885 and couldn’t find logical explanations for most of them. Missing just the best 10 trading days between 1982 and 2005 drops your return from 10.6% to 8.1%. Missing the best 50 drops it to 1.8%. Those moves are unpredictable — taking money out risks missing them entirely.

After September 11, 2001, markets dropped 20%. Hallam dumped money into stocks “like a crazed shopper at a going-out-of-business sale.” The money he invested in September 2001 was up 55% by January 2011 including dividends. Warren Buffett once asked shareholders: if you plan to eat hamburgers your whole life but don’t own cattle, should you want beef prices to be higher or lower? Most investors get this backwards — they cheer rising prices for stocks they’re still buying. A falling market is a sale at the supermarket, and young investors should celebrate it.

Rule 5: Build Mountains of Money with a Responsible Portfolio

Just eating Brussels sprouts doesn’t make a balanced diet, and a total stock market index alone isn’t a balanced portfolio. When the market drops 50%, a 100% stock portfolio drops 50%. Only an irresponsible portfolio falls that far. Bonds are the parachute. A government bond is simply a loan you make to the government — your money is safe as long as the government can pay, plus you receive annual interest. Short-term bonds (1–3 years) protect against inflation because when they expire, you reinvest at current rates.

The rule of thumb: your bond allocation should roughly equal your age. A 30-year-old holds 30% bonds; a 50-year-old, 50%. Government employees with guaranteed pensions can afford less. Bonds aren’t just a drag on returns — they’re the ammunition you use to buy stocks when they go on sale. When markets crash, you sell bonds and buy cheap stocks. When markets soar, you sell stocks and buy bonds. This rebalancing is what pension funds and university endowments do — and it forces the discipline of buying low and selling high.

Scott Burns, a former Dallas Morning News columnist, popularized the Couch Potato Portfolio: split money equally between a total stock market index and a total bond index, rebalance once a year. From 1986 to 2001, this 50/50 split averaged 10.96% annually. During the brutal 2008 crash, the Couch Potato dropped only 20.4% while the average actively managed fund dropped 29.1% and the average balanced fund dropped 28%. From 2006 to 2011 — a period including the worst crash since the Depression — $10,000 in the Couch Potato grew to $12,521.

The cowboy investor who holds 100% stocks for the extra return pays a steep price for marginal gain. Daniel Solin’s data from 1973 to 2004 shows 60% stocks/40% bonds earned 10.49% annually while 100% stocks earned 11.19% — just 0.7% more. But the worst 12-month stretch for the cowboy was a 20.15% drop versus only 9.15% for the balanced investor. In Canada, Ian McGugan demonstrated that a rebalanced combination of indexes with bonds actually beat 100% stock returns from 1975 to 2010 — bonds aren’t for wimps.

A 30-year-old American’s simple portfolio: 30% short-term government bond index, 35% U.S. total stock market index, 35% total international stock market index. Add fresh money monthly to whichever component has fallen behind. Once a year, rebalance back to your target. This takes ten minutes and beats 90% of professionals over time.

Rule 6: Sample a Round-the-World Ticket to Indexing

Rule 6 introduces real people — with their real names and real portfolios — who built indexed accounts in four different countries. The message: this works everywhere, not just in America.

Kris Olson, a 40-year-old American doctor who does volunteer medical work in Sumatra and Kenya, had triplets in 2006 and suddenly faced three college educations. He’d been buying actively managed mutual funds for years and recognized the fees were “flushing money down the toilet.” He called Vanguard, opened an account over the phone, and split his money: 35% U.S. bond index, 35% U.S. stock market index, 30% international stock market index. From January 2006 to January 2011, his portfolio gained 30.70% — beating the Fidelity Balanced Fund (19.40%), T. Rowe Price Balanced Fund (24.02%), and American Funds Balanced Fund (13.13%). His annual rebalancing took ten minutes.

For Americans who want it even simpler, Vanguard Target Retirement Funds are single products that blend stock and bond indexes, automatically rebalance, and gradually shift toward bonds as you age. Pick the fund whose bond allocation matches your age, not the one with your actual retirement date. The Target Retirement 2015 fund beat all three major balanced funds from 2006 to 2011 with a turnover rate of 19% versus 122% for Fidelity’s balanced offering.

Keith Wakelin, a landscaper and ultra-distance runner in British Columbia, followed the MoneySense couch potato formula: 20% international stock index, 20% Canadian stock index, 20% U.S. stock index, 40% Canadian bond index. His total return from 2005 to 2011 was 28.5%, beating four of Canada’s five major bank balanced funds. For Canadians, TD Bank e-Series index funds charge just 0.3–0.5% versus 2.5% for the average Canadian fund. Walking into a bank branch will get you steered toward expensive products instead — order online.

Gordon Cyr and Seng Su Lin, teachers in Singapore, built a five-component portfolio through DBS Vickers: 20% Singapore bonds, 20% Singapore stocks, 20% Canadian bonds, 20% Canadian stocks, 20% world stock index. Gordon had previously been trapped by Zurich International Life Limited, an offshore investment company that locked him into costly actively managed funds with confusing statements and heavy withdrawal penalties. Neerav Bhatt, a 28-year-old Australian blogger, discovered Vanguard Australia and its Life Strategy Funds — complete portfolios in a single product with tiered fees that decrease as your account grows.

Rule 7: Peek Inside a Pilferer’s Playbook

Financial advisers have four standard pitches against index funds, and Hallam dismantles each one. First: “Active managers can sell before markets drop.” A Standard & Poor’s study showed the vast majority of actively managed funds lost to indexes during the 2008 crash — the worst in recent memory — proving managers couldn’t dodge the bullet. Second: “Index funds give average returns.” Only true in a fantasy where fund companies, researchers, brokers, and the government all work for free. In reality, costs make the average active fund a below-average investment. Third: “We can show you funds that beat the index.” Past winners rarely repeat. Even Morningstar’s own research director admitted: “I don’t think you’d want to pay much attention to Morningstar’s ratings either.” Fourth: “We can time the market.” Financial advisers are salespeople, not market experts. Suze Orman calls them “pin-striped suited salespeople.”

The training gap is real. Certified financial planner certification requires fewer than six months of academic training and one year of sales experience. Daniel Solin quotes a broker’s description of training: “Study and take the Series 7 exams. I spent three weeks learning to sell.” William Bernstein recommends reading Burton Malkiel’s A Random Walk Down Wall Street and John Bogle’s Common Sense on Mutual Funds — after those two books, “you will know more about finance than 99 percent of all stockbrokers.”

Hallam tells the story of visiting a bank with his friend Dave Alfawicki in 2004, where a young adviser revealed she’d completed her entire investment certification course in two weeks and knew nothing about investing before it. When Hallam’s mother opened an account requesting 50% bonds, the adviser confessed the bank’s internal strategy: first, push expensive “fund of funds” products on uninformed clients. If the client seems smarter, offer in-house actively managed funds. Under no circumstances offer index funds unless the client explicitly demands them. Three years later, a different representative called claiming her 50% bond portfolio was “too risky” — a transparent ploy to move her into profitable products.

When Google employees became overnight millionaires after the 2004 IPO, waves of financial planners from JPMorgan Chase, UBS, and Morgan Stanley descended on the campus. Google’s leadership put them on hold and instead brought in William Sharpe (Nobel laureate), Burton Malkiel, and John Bogle to educate employees first. Sharpe: “Don’t try to beat the market.” Malkiel: build index portfolios. Bogle: advisers are a “giant fleecing machine.” When the planners finally arrived, Google staff were armed to the teeth and easily fended them off.

“The investment business is a giant scam. It deletes billions of dollars every year in transaction costs and fees. Most people think they can find fund managers who can outperform, but most people are wrong. You should simply hold index funds. No doubt about it.” — Jack Meyer, Harvard University Endowment Fund

Even the best in the business can’t beat indexes consistently. A FutureMetrics study of 192 major U.S. corporate pension plans from 1988 to 2005 found fewer than 30% outperformed a simple 60/40 index portfolio. The smartest pension fund managers already know this: Washington state indexes 100% of its stock market assets, California 86%, New York 75%, and Connecticut 84%.

Rule 8: Avoid the Seduction of Sexier Strategies

Daryl Klein promised investors 54% annual returns through something called Insta-Cash Loans. It was a Ponzi scheme — he paid “interest” using new investors’ deposits. When it collapsed in 2006, investors lost everything. Some had remortgaged their homes. Bernie Madoff ran the same scam on a larger scale, absconding with $65 billion. His victims included Kevin Bacon, Kyra Sedgwick, and Steven Spielberg. The lesson is simple: when someone makes lucrative promises, walk away. Nobody guarantees 54% returns — ever.

Investment newsletters are almost as bad. The Gilder Technology Report claimed 155% returns over three years, but $40,000 invested in its four recommended tech stocks in 2000 — Nortel, Lucent, JDS Uniphase, Cisco — became $1,140 by 2002. Recovery would require a 3,400% gain. A 12-year study by John Graham and Campbell Harvey tracked 15,000 newsletters and found 94% went out of business. Only 10 out of 160 tracked by Hulbert’s Financial Digest beat stock market indexes over a decade — odds of less than 7%.

Gold makes for dramatic bar graphs but terrible long-term investments. $1 invested in gold in 1801 was worth $73 by 2011. $1 invested in U.S. stocks was worth $10.15 million. Gold jumps up and down like a kid on a pogo stick but after inflation gains no long-term elevation.

Fast-growing economies don’t produce better stock returns. William Bernstein showed that China’s 9.61% GDP growth from 1988 to 2008 produced negative 3.31% stock returns, while the U.S. grew at 2.77% and returned 8.8%. David Swensen of Yale confirmed that developed market stocks outperformed emerging markets from 1985 to 2006. Emerging markets “rise like rockets, crash like meteorites, before rising like rockets again.”

Hedge funds charge 2% annual fees plus 20% of profits. A study by Burton Malkiel and Robert Ibbotson found fewer than 25% of hedge funds lasted a full eight years. The 75% that died don’t appear in performance databases, and back-fill bias inflated reported returns by 7.3% annually. Investment magazines are no better — their content is driven by advertisers. When Hallam wrote a 2005 article for MoneySense mentioning Russ Perry’s opinion against buying new cars, a major automobile manufacturer threatened to pull advertising if similar content appeared again.

“The best is the enemy of good. Investors who aren’t satisfied with a good plan — like indexing — may strive for something they hope will be ‘best.’ But that path’s wake is filled with more tragedies than successes.” — Voltaire (1764)

Rule 9: The 10% Stock Picking Solution

If stock picking is an itch you must scratch, limit it to 10% of your portfolio. The other 90% stays in index funds. Even with the best tools, most stock pickers eventually lose to indexes after transaction costs and taxes. But for those who want to try, Hallam offers a framework learned from years in an investment club.

First, prefer simple businesses. Peter Lynch’s advice: buy a business any idiot can run, because eventually one will be running it. Bill Gates argued that tech companies should carry lower valuations than old economy businesses because tech faces “complete changes in the rules.” Jeremy Siegel confirmed this in The Future for Investors: old economy stocks like Exxon Mobil, Johnson & Johnson, and Coca-Cola beat tech stocks when dividends are reinvested, because higher dividend yields create a snowball effect of compounding.

Use the price-to-earnings ratio to judge value, not share price. A $5 stock with a P/E of 25 is more expensive than a $100 stock with a P/E of 2. Compare a stock’s earnings yield (earnings divided by total market value) against the 10-year government bond yield. If the stock yields less than the bond, the risk isn’t worth taking. Hallam’s club bought Johnson & Johnson in 2010 at an earnings yield of 7.9% versus a 2.52% bond — well compensated for the added risk.

Look for businesses that can increase the price of their products with inflation. Dell makes computers that get cheaper every year — its profit margin fell from 6.34% to 4.08%. Coca-Cola and PepsiCo raise prices because of brand loyalty. Look for low debt: if a firm’s average three-year net income exceeds its total long-term debt, it’s conservatively financed. Coca-Cola could pay off its entire debt in 9 months. Starbucks had none. Look for high return on total capital — 15% or better sustained over 10 years. Fewer than 5% of businesses qualify: Colgate Palmolive, Heinz, Microsoft, PepsiCo, Johnson & Johnson.

Philip Fisher, author of Common Stocks and Uncommon Profits, pioneered the practice of kicking tires — visiting customers, asking competitors about strengths and weaknesses. Hallam does this for his club’s holdings: he wanders into construction sites asking what fastening brackets they use, checking whether the businesses he owns actually have loyal customers on the ground. Finally, demand honest management — look for high insider ownership (executives owning 10%+ of the stock) and reasonable executive pay relative to industry peers.

Hallam’s club bought Schering Plough in 2003 at $15.24 when the stock had been hammered from $40 after its blockbuster allergy drug Claritin lost patent protection. At $40, the earnings yield was just 3.8% — less than government bonds. At $15, it jumped to 7%. They sold at $25 in 2008 when the earnings yield had fallen below the bond rate again. A 64% profit. The rule: sell when the business changes fundamentally or when the price becomes grossly expensive relative to earnings.

Key Takeaways

Costs destroy returns more than bad stock picks

The five hidden costs of active management — expense ratios, 12B1 fees, trading costs, sales commissions, and tax inefficiency — collectively drag returns by 4–5% annually. Over a lifetime, this costs the average investor more than half their potential retirement portfolio.

Time beats money

Star invested $32,400 over 60 years and ended with $1,050,180. Lucy invested $240,000 over 25 years and ended with $813,128. Starting early with small amounts overwhelms starting late with large amounts because compound interest is exponential, not linear.

Behavior is the biggest variable

Mutual funds averaged 10% from 1980 to 2005 but the average investor earned only 7.3% because of emotional buying and selling. The gap — $250,688 on a $50,000 investment — came entirely from human psychology, not market performance.

Bonds are ammunition, not dead weight

A rebalanced portfolio with bonds can actually beat 100% stocks over long periods because it forces the discipline of buying low and selling high. The Couch Potato Portfolio dropped only 20.4% in 2008 while managed funds dropped 28–29%.

The industry is designed to extract your wealth

Financial advisers complete their certification in weeks, are trained primarily as salespeople, and earn nearly $150,000 per year — more than most lawyers or doctors. Their incentive structure rewards selling expensive products. Even Google’s management recognized this and armed employees with academic lectures before letting advisers near them.

A three-fund portfolio beats 90% of professionals

Domestic stock index, international stock index, bond index. Rebalance annually. Ten minutes of work. This beaten-path approach requires no expertise, no market forecasting, and no adviser fees — and it outperforms the vast majority of managed accounts over any period exceeding five years.

Contrarian Ideas

Falling markets are good news for young investors

Convention: market crashes are disasters. Hallam: they’re sales at the supermarket. If you’re buying stocks for the next 20 years, you want lower prices today, not higher ones. Buffett’s hamburger analogy proves the point — you don’t cheer when beef prices rise if you’re buying burgers for life.

Financial gifts to children make them poorer

Thomas Stanley’s research shows adults who receive financial handouts end up 43% less wealthy than peers who earned their own money. The exception — teachers and professors — reinforces that financial literacy, not money, is the gift that matters.

Fast-growing economies produce poor stock returns

China grew 9.61% annually from 1988 to 2008 and produced negative stock returns. The U.S. grew 2.77% and returned 8.8%. Developed markets consistently outperform emerging markets despite slower GDP growth.

The best fund managers recommend against their own industry

Peter Lynch, Bill Miller, Ted Aronson, and Jack Meyer — some of the most successful active managers in history — all recommend index funds for individual investors. They know the industry’s costs make it a losing proposition for most people.

Frameworks & Proposals

The Nine Rules of Wealth

  1. Spend like a millionaire — frugally
  2. Start investing early, after clearing high-interest debt
  3. Buy low-cost index funds, not actively managed funds
  4. Understand market psychology — don’t fall victim to fear and greed
  5. Build a balanced portfolio with stocks and bonds
  6. Create indexed accounts regardless of where you live
  7. Learn to fight an adviser’s sales rhetoric
  8. Avoid investment schemes, newsletters, gold, and hedge funds
  9. If you must pick stocks, limit to 10% of your portfolio

The Couch Potato Portfolio

50% total stock market index, 50% total bond index. Rebalance once a year. Averaged 10.96% annually from 1986 to 2001. Survived the 2008 crash with only a 20.4% drop versus 29% for managed funds.

Age-Based Bond Allocation

Bond percentage ≈ your age. Adjust down if you have a guaranteed pension. Three-fund split: bond index + domestic stock index + international stock index. Monthly contributions go to whichever component is below target. Annual rebalance to restore percentages.

Stock Picking Checklist (for the 10%)

  • Simple business any idiot can run
  • Can increase product prices with inflation
  • Low or no long-term debt (payable within one year of earnings)
  • Return on total capital above 15% sustained over 10 years
  • High insider ownership (10%+) and reasonable executive pay
  • Earnings yield exceeds 10-year government bond yield
  • Sell only when the business changes or price becomes grossly expensive

Millionaire Teacher: The Nine Rules of Wealth You Should Have Learned in School
Andrew Hallam · John Wiley & Sons · 2011 · 210 pages
A high school English teacher who became a debt-free millionaire in his 30s reveals the nine rules for building wealth on a middle-class salary — primarily through frugal living, index fund investing, and behavioral discipline.

About the Author

Andrew Hallam is a high school English teacher who became a debt-free millionaire in his 30s while earning a middle-class salary. Born in Canada, he taught in British Columbia before moving to Singapore American School, where he taught international students and held free financial seminars for colleagues. His personal investing history spans over 20 years, and he runs an investment club that practices the value-investing principles he describes in Rule 9.

Hallam became a regular contributor to MoneySense magazine after editor Ian McGugan discovered his financial writing. His articles covered index investing, frugal living, and personal finance strategies. He is also a competitive long-distance runner, a trait that shows up throughout the book — wealth-building, like distance running, rewards sustained effort over time rather than sprinting and burning out.

The book was endorsed by Burton Malkiel (who called it the best guide for newbie investors), Larry Swedroe, Bill Schultheis, and Michael O’Higgins. Hallam’s credibility comes not from Wall Street credentials but from the fact that he built his wealth using exactly the strategies he teaches — on a teacher’s salary, with his own money.

Why This Book Matters

Millionaire Teacher matters because it demolishes the most profitable lie in personal finance: that you need professional help to invest well. The financial services industry extracts hundreds of billions annually from individual investors through fees, commissions, and products designed to benefit sellers, not buyers. This book arms ordinary people with the knowledge to opt out entirely.

What makes it especially effective is the messenger. Hallam isn’t a finance professor or a hedge fund manager — he’s a schoolteacher. His proof of concept is his own life: a mechanic’s son who ate clam pasta, biked to work, and retired wealthy in his 30s. The international scope — real portfolios from the U.S., Canada, Singapore, and Australia — makes the advice genuinely global, not just another American investing book.

The book also serves as a behavioral intervention. Most investing failures aren’t intellectual — people know they should save more and spend less. The failures are emotional: chasing hot funds, fleeing crashes, trusting charming salespeople. By combining concrete portfolio instructions with vivid stories of what happens when people get it wrong, Hallam doesn’t just tell you what to do — he inoculates you against the psychological traps that prevent most people from actually doing it.

What Didn't Fit

Hallam fills his book with personal stories and specific calculations that make abstract financial principles concrete. Here are the best ones that did not make the chapter coverage.

NBA Players: 60% Broke in Five Years

Sixty percent of NBA players go broke within five years of retirement, despite earning millions during their careers. The Toronto Star reported this in 2008, and Hallam uses it as the ultimate proof that income has almost nothing to do with wealth. High schools do not prepare students for the financial world, and even massive earnings cannot compensate for the absence of the spending discipline that Rule 1 demands. If multimillionaire athletes cannot hold onto their money, the problem is clearly behavioral, not mathematical.

Julie and Tom's Tracking Experiment

Julie and Tom were a couple who believed they were already living frugally. Hallam challenged them to track every dollar they spent for three months. They were shocked by how much was bleeding out through Starbucks, golf, and restaurant tabs — expenses that felt trivial one at a time but added up to a staggering monthly total. After one year of conscious spending, they doubled their investment contributions. After two years, they tripled their original savings rate. Their reaction: “We didn't know where that money was going each month. It doesn't feel like we live any differently than three years ago, but the deposits in our investment account don't lie.”

Raymond James and the Chocolate Cake

Hallam's wife Pele was “helped” by a Raymond James adviser who sold her actively managed mutual funds and charged an additional 1.75% wrap fee on top of the funds' own hidden expenses. Over five years, her $200,000 account would have been $20,000 better off with a simple index fund. Hallam compared the adviser to Jesse James — “servicing my wife the way the infamous Jesse James used to service train passengers — by taking the money and running.” His broader analogy: the average financial adviser is as good for your long-term wealth as a chocolate cake diet is for your long-term health. The taste buds enjoy thirty seconds of sweetness while the body absorbs years of damage.

Hallam's Nortel Confession

In 1999, Hallam bought Nortel Networks shares despite knowing almost nothing about the company. Friends were making easy money on Internet stocks and he did not want to be left behind. He eventually read Nortel's annual report and discovered the company had been losing more money every year since 1996. He did not care — the stock price was rising. He paid $83 per share, watched it rise to $118 (a 42% profit), recognized the quick gain, and sold. Then the price rose to $124 and an analyst suggested $150. He bought back at $120. Gravity struck: the price dropped to $100, then $80, then $50. He sold at $48, losing almost half his investment. He was lucky compared to friends who never sold — those shares are now worth pennies. The lesson: he bought a stock he should never have owned because the business had not made a profit in years.

SmartMoney's April 2009 Advice

SmartMoney magazine published its April 2009 issue one month after the stock market hit its crisis low. Instead of recommending stocks at generational bargain prices, the magazine recommended bonds and gold — exactly what terrified readers wanted to hear. Over the next two years, stocks returned 69–70%. The five recommended bond funds averaged 32.8%. Gold gained 46%. Magazines sell what scared people want — chicken soup for the fearful investor — not what actually builds long-term wealth. The timing could not have been worse, and the lesson could not be clearer: financial media optimizes for subscription revenue, not reader returns.
