# Millionaire Teacher — Summary

**Author:** Andrew Hallam
**Published:** 2011, John Wiley & Sons
**Pages:** ~210

## Overview

A high school English teacher who became a debt-free millionaire
in his 30s shares nine rules for building wealth on a middle-class
salary. Written for financial beginners, the book makes a powerful
case for index fund investing, frugal spending habits, and
behavioral discipline — all illustrated through personal stories,
real people's portfolios, and vivid analogies.

## The Nine Rules

**Rule 1: Spend Like You Want to Grow Rich**
Wealth isn't what you earn — it's what you keep. Real millionaires
drive Toyotas ($31,367 median car), not Jaguars. Hallam biked
70 miles to work, ate clam pasta for under $1, and never turned
on the heat. Thomas Stanley's research: 80%+ of millionaires are
first-generation rich, and financial gifts to children produce
43% less wealth than earning it yourself.

**Rule 2: Use the Greatest Investment Ally You Have**
Compound interest over time. The Star vs. Lucy story: $45/month
from age 5 beats $800/month from age 40 by $237,000. Start early,
pay off credit card debt first, and invest consistently.

**Rule 3: Small Percentages Pack Big Punches**
Index funds beat actively managed funds after fees, taxes, and
survivorship bias. Five hidden costs drag active funds down:
expense ratios, 12B1 fees, trading costs, sales commissions,
and tax inefficiency. Nobel laureates, Peter Lynch, and Bill
Miller all recommend indexing. NYT's 7-year contest: S&P 500
crushed every professional adviser.

**Rule 4: Conquer the Enemy in the Mirror**
Investors sabotage themselves through emotional buying and selling.
John Bogle's data: mutual funds averaged 10% but investors earned
only 7.3% due to chasing performance. Think of stocks as businesses
(the owner) with prices on a leash (the dog). The leashed dog
always returns to the owner. Dollar-cost average, never time
the market, and celebrate falling prices.

**Rule 5: Build Mountains of Money with a Responsible Portfolio**
Bonds are the parachute. Bond allocation ≈ your age. Rebalance
annually by buying the laggard. The Couch Potato Portfolio
(50/50 stocks/bonds) averaged 10.96% from 1986-2001 and dropped
only 20.4% in 2008 vs. 29.1% for average actively managed funds.
Canadian rebalanced portfolios actually beat 100% stock returns
from 1975-2010.

**Rule 6: Sample a Round-the-World Ticket to Indexing**
Real portfolios from real people: Kris Olson (US/Vanguard),
Keith Wakelin (Canada/ETFs), Gordon Cyr & Su Lin (Singapore/DBS
Vickers), Neerav Bhatt (Australia/Vanguard Life Strategy). Each
beat the majority of balanced mutual funds in their country.
Vanguard Target Retirement Funds are the simplest option.

**Rule 7: Peek Inside a Pilferer's Playbook**
Financial advisers are salespeople, not experts. Four common
pitches debunked. Google armed employees with lectures from
Sharpe, Malkiel, and Bogle before letting advisers near them.
Pension fund managers (the best in the business) can't beat
indexed portfolios 70% of the time. The average broker's
training: 2 weeks of sales technique.

**Rule 8: Avoid the Seduction of Sexier Strategies**
Ponzi schemes (Daryl Klein, Bernie Madoff), investment
newsletters (94% fail within 12 years), gold ($1 in 1801 =
$73 by 2011 vs. $10.15 million for stocks), hedge funds (75%
die within 8 years, back-fill bias inflates returns 7.3%),
emerging markets (fast GDP growth ≠ better stock returns),
and investment magazine advice (driven by advertisers).

**Rule 9: The 10% Stock Picking Solution**
If you must pick stocks, limit to 10% of portfolio. Look for
simple businesses, low debt, high return on total capital (15%+),
pricing power, and honest management. Compare earnings yield to
10-year bond yield. Coca-Cola, Johnson & Johnson, and old economy
stocks beat tech over the long haul. Sell only when the business
changes or becomes grossly overpriced.

## Key Themes

- **Costs are the enemy**: Fees, commissions, and taxes destroy
  returns more than bad stock picks
- **Behavior beats brains**: Discipline and consistency matter
  more than intelligence or expertise
- **Simplicity wins**: A three-fund portfolio rebalanced annually
  beats 90% of professionals
- **Time is the weapon**: Starting early with small amounts
  overwhelms starting late with large amounts
- **The industry is conflicted**: Advisers profit from complexity;
  investors profit from simplicity

## Style Classification

**Practical finance** — actionable investing guidance with a
conversational tone. Mix of personal anecdotes, specific numbers,
comparison tables, and "do this" advice. Follows the practical
finance template: prose → bullets → tables → insight rhythm.
