## The Hippocratic Rule of Wealth

### Do No Harm

- Large paycheck ≠ wealth. Living like Persian royalty while collecting paychecks doesn't make you rich.
- **Hippocratic oath for wealth**: Above all, do no harm. In an era of instant gratification, it's easy to destroy your financial future.
- Build assets, not debts. Spend far less than you make and intelligently invest the difference.
- Most people fail to differentiate between "wants" and "needs"

### The Consumption Treadmill

- **Hedonic treadmill**: New dining table → matching plates → new couch → new carpet → entertainment system → home renovation → Hawaii trip
- Like **Sisyphus** rolling a boulder uphill, only to have it roll back down. Consumers who get close to paying off debts reward themselves by adding more debt.
- Buying after saving (instead of credit) seems "1950s" to modern consumers. Result: mountains of personal debt.

### The Debt Crisis

- 2010: Average U.S. household had **$7,490 in credit-card debt** (*The Wall Street Journal*)
- 2011: **23% of Americans** owed more on mortgages than homes were worth
- Nevada: **66% of homeowners** underwater on mortgages
- **Thomas Stanley** research: Most U.S. homes valued at $1 million+ (as of 2009) were owned by non-millionaires with large mortgages and expensive tastes
- **90% of actual millionaires** (net worth >$1M) lived in homes valued under $1 million
- True wealth = ability to live well without your job

## Compound Interest—The World's Most Powerful Financial Concept

### The Magic Einstein Referenced

> "Compound interest is the eighth wonder of the world. He who understands it, earns it; he who doesn't, pays it." — **Albert Einstein** (attributed)

- **Compound interest** is simple: earning interest on your interest over time
- $100 at 10% interest becomes $110 after year one, then $121 after year two (you earn interest on the $10 gain)
- Long-term compounding produces exponential growth few people grasp intuitively

### Compound Growth Examples

$100 invested at 10% annually grows to:
- $161.05 after 5 years
- $259.37 after 10 years
- $672.74 after 20 years
- $1,744.94 after 30 years
- $11,739.08 after 50 years
- $78,974.69 after 70 years
- $204,840.02 after 80 years
- $1,378,061.23 after 100 years

- Someone investing at 19 who lives to 90 has 71 years of compounding
- You don't need to be a vampire to benefit—patient investors with normal lifespans reap enormous rewards

### The Inspirational Realities of Starting Early

- **Pay off high-interest debt first** (car loans, credit cards) before investing
- Once debt-free, apply the **Noah Principle**: start immediately, regardless of age
- Money not spent on expensive cars, gadgets, and credit-card payments compounds dramatically if invested patiently
- **The longer money is invested in stocks, the lower the risk** due to historical mean reversion

### Historical Stock Market Returns

- U.S. stock market has generated returns exceeding **9% annually** over the past 90 years
- This includes the crashes of 1929, 1973–1974, 1987, and 2008–2009
- **Jeremy Siegel**, Wharton finance professor, author of *Stocks for the Long Run*: dominant historical markets like the U.S. aren't the only source of long-term returns
- England's stock market returns since 1926 have been similar to the U.S. despite shrinking global importance
- Germany's long-term stock market performance rivals the U.S. despite two devastating world wars

### Diversification is Key

- Don't pick one country's stock market—own an interest in all the world's stock markets
- Diversification + early investing = exponential wealth growth
- Starting young is the single greatest advantage

## Gifting Money to Yourself

### Julie and Tom's Expense Tracking Experiment (2005)

- **Julie** and **Tom**, private school teachers without pensions, wanted to know how much to save for retirement
- Author suggested doubling their current savings rate
- Julie thought it was attainable; Tom thought it was crazy
- Author gave them a 3-month tracking assignment:

  2. Calculate their average monthly living costs at the end

### The Awakening

- Julie was shocked by spending on eating out, clothes, and Starbucks
- Tom was shocked by spending on beers at the clubhouse while golfing
- **Tom's insight**: "I knew I had to write those purchases down at the end of the day, which acted as an accountability measurement. So I started spending less."

### Financial Efficiency

- Writing down expenses reveals two things:
  1. How much you actually spend per month (shows how much you can invest)
  2. Creates accountability, which naturally reduces spending

### Pay Yourself First

- Subtract average monthly expenses from income to determine investable amount
- **Transfer investment money on payday**, not at month's end
- Otherwise, you might not have enough left after "a few too many nights out"
- Author's wife made this mistake before marriage: invested whatever was left at month/year end
- When she switched to automatic transfers on payday, she doubled her investment rate

### Julie and Tom's Results

- After one year: doubled their investment contributions
- After two years: tripled their original savings rate
- Their reaction: "We didn't know where that money was going each month. It doesn't feel like we live any differently than three years ago, but the deposits in our investment account don't lie. We've tripled our savings."

### Bonus Tip

- When salary increases by $1,000 in a given year, add at least half of it to investments
- Put the rest in a separate account for something special
- This way, you get rewarded twice for the salary increase

## How and Why Stocks Rise in Value

### Where Does Stock Market Money Come From?

- Author averaged 10% per year for 20 years on stock investments
- Some years dropped significantly, some years earned much more than 10%

### The Willy Wonka Analogy

- **Willy Wonka** (from Roald Dahl's *Charlie and the Chocolate Factory*) starts a small chocolate shop
- Dreams of ice cream that doesn't melt, gum that never loses flavor, chocolate that could make the devil sell his soul
- Willy needs money to expand: larger building, more workers, faster machinery
- He hires someone to approach the **New York Stock Exchange** to find investors
- Investors buy parts of his business, called **shares** or **stock**
- Willy is no longer sole owner, but by selling shares he raises capital to build a larger, more efficient factory
- Increased production increases profits because he can make more treats faster

### Going Public

- Willy's company is now **public**: shareholders can sell their stakes to other willing buyers
- When a publicly traded company has shares on a stock market, trading activity has negligible effect on the business
- Willy focuses on making chocolate; shareholders don't interfere with day-to-day operations

### Dividends and Profits

- Shareholders wanted more than certificates from the NYSE—they wanted to share in business profits
- The **board of directors** (voted in by shareholders) decided to give owners an annual percentage of profits as a **dividend**
- Willy's factory sold $100,000 worth of chocolate annually
- After taxes, wages, and maintenance, the factory made $10,000 annual profit
- Board decided to pay shareholders $5,000 of that $10,000 profit and split it among them (the **dividend**)
- The remaining $5,000 is reinvested back into the business for bigger machinery, wider advertising, faster chocolate production, generating higher future profits

### Share Price Dynamics

- Reinvested profits made Willy's business more lucrative
- The Chocolate Factory doubled profits to $20,000 the following year and increased dividend payouts
- Other potential investors noticed and wanted to buy shares too
- More buyers than sellers created demand, causing share prices on the NYSE to rise
- **If more buyers than sellers, share price rises; if more sellers than buyers, share price falls**

### Stock Price Fluctuations

- Share prices fluctuate over time: sometimes climbing, sometimes falling, depending on investor sentiment
- Good news about the company increases public demand for shares, pushing up the price
- Bad news causes pessimism, causing the share price to fall

### Long-Term Growth

- Over the long term, when a company increases profits, the stock price generally rises along with it

### Two Ways Shareholders Make Money

1. **Dividends**: cash payments to shareholders (usually four times per year)
2. **Capital appreciation**: stock price increases substantially on the market; shareholders can sell shares for profit

### Example: Montgomery Burns Invests in Willy Wonka

- **Montgomery Burns** buys $1,000 of Willy Wonka stock at $10/share
- After one year, if share price rises to $10.50, that's a 5% increase ($10.50 is 5% higher than $10)
- If Burns receives a $50 dividend, that's an additional 5% return (5% of his $1,000 investment)
- Total potential return: 5% from share price increase + 5% from dividend = **10% annual profit**
- The 5% from price appreciation is only "realized" if Burns sells the shares

### The Value of Holding Long-Term

- Montgomery Burns didn't become the richest man in Springfield by buying and selling Willy Wonka shares quickly
- Studies show investors who buy and sell shares repeatedly don't make profits as high as investors who hold shares long-term
- Burns held shares for many years; sometimes share price rose, sometimes it fell
- But the company kept increasing profits, so share price increased over time
- Annual dividends plus rising stock price coupled with dividends earned Burns an average potential return of 10% per year

### The Mistake of Stock Picking

- Burns also bought shares in Homer's donuts and Lou's bar—both failed, and Burns lost money
- What drove Burns crazy: missing shares in Bart's joke-store company, **Bart's Barf Gags**, which quadrupled in just four years

## The Solution: Own Every Stock

- The best way to invest in the stock market is to **own every stock in the market**, rather than trying to follow Burns's strategy and guess which stocks will rise
- Sounds impossible to buy virtually every stock, but it's made easy by purchasing a single product that owns every stock within it
- **Start early enough** and you can invest half of what your neighbors invest over your lifetime and still end up with twice as much money
- For patient investors, the aggregate returns of the world's stock markets have dished out phenomenal profits

### Historical Returns

- U.S. stock market averaged **9.96% annually from 1920–2010**
- Some periods grew faster, some slower
- Invest early and frequently; the odds are high you'll slowly grow very wealthy

## Survivorship Bias and the Mutual Fund Graveyard

### The 44 Wall Street Fund Saga

- **44 Wall Street Fund**: top-ranked fund of the 1970s, beat S&P 500 for 11 straight years
- Success was temporary: became worst-performing fund in 1980s, lost 73% of value
- Brand destroyed, merged into **Cumberland Growth Fund** (1993), then **Matterhorn Growth Fund** (1996)
- Today it's as if the fund never existed

### The Lindner Large-Cap Fund Collapse

- **Lindner Large-Cap Fund**: beat S&P 500 every year from 1974-1984
- Next 18 years (1984-2002): returned just 4.1% annually vs 12% for S&P 500
- Dismal record erased when merged into **Hennessy Total Return Fund**

### The Survivorship Bias Problem

- Most books claim index funds beat 80%+ of actively managed funds over 10+ years
- **But they don't account for survivorship bias or taxes**
- Failed funds disappear from records, inflating the apparent success rate of active funds
- When accounting for fees, survivorship bias, and taxes: average U.S. actively managed fund underperformed S&P 500 by **4.8% annually from 1984-1999**

## Holes in the Hulls of Actively Managed Mutual Funds

Five factors drag down returns: expense ratios, 12B1 fees, trading costs, sales commissions, and taxes.

> "Buying these products over an investment lifetime can be like entering a swimming race while towing a hunk of carpet through the water."

### 1. Expense Ratios

- Hidden fees pay salaries of highly-paid analysts/traders, office costs, computers, paper, electricity
- Fund company owners receive profits from expense ratios
- **Example**: $30 billion fund costs investors ~$450 million/year (1.5% of assets)
- Money is sifted out whether fund makes money or not
- Not itemized on statements, invisible to most investors

### 2. 12B1 Fees

- Roughly 60% of U.S. fund companies charge 12B1 fees
- Can cost up to 0.25%, or $75 million/year for a $30 billion fund
- Pay for marketing: magazine/newspaper/TV/online advertising
- Current investors subsidize attracting new investors
- "Like a masked phantom pulling money from wallets every night"
- Not itemized on statements

### 3. Trading Costs

- Fund's trading costs fluctuate based on how much buying/selling managers do
- According to **Lipper** (global research company): average actively managed stock fund accrues trading costs of **0.2% annually** ($60 million/year on $30 billion fund)
- These costs aren't the only invisible albatrosses

### 4. Sales Commissions

- Many funds charge **load fees**: percentage up front to buy (goes to salesperson) or fee to sell
- Can be as high as 6%
- Financial advisers love selling "loaded funds" for the commission kick
- **Math trap**: 5.75% sales fee requires 6.1% gain just to break even the following year
- Losing 50% in one year means you need to **double your money** the next year just to get back to even
- Advisers choosing loaded funds "put a whole new spin on 'Piggy Bank'"

### 5. Taxes

- Over 60% of money in U.S. mutual funds is in taxable accounts
- When actively managed fund makes money, investors pay taxes on gains if held in taxable account
- Fund managers buy/sell stocks frequently, triggering **realized capital gains**
- More trading = less tax efficient
- Average actively managed fund trades every stock during an average year (**"100 percent turnover"**)
- Triggers short-term capital gains tax (hefty penalty in U.S.)

### Index Funds Are Tax-Efficient

- Total stock market index fund has virtually no trading
- Gains don't generate taxable hit unless investor sells the fund at higher price
- Rather than paying high capital gains tax yearly, index investor defers gains
- Allows for significantly higher compounding profits

### The Tax Efficiency Gap

**Bogle Financial Markets Research Center** 15-year study (1994-2009):
- Actively managed funds were dramatically less tax efficient than stock market index
- **Example**: Fund matching S&P 500 performance from 1994-2009
  - Before taxes: would have averaged 6.7% per year
  - After taxes: active fund would need to beat index by **16.2% over 15 years** to match index fund after-tax return
  - This assumes fund turnover equaled average actively managed fund turnover

> "Adding high expense ratios, 12B1 fees, trading costs, sales commissions, and taxes to your investment is a bit like a boxer standing blindfolded in a ring and asking his opponent to hit him five times on the jaw before the opening bell."

## Rule 4: Conquer the Enemy in the Mirror

### When a 10 Percent Gain Isn't a 10 Percent Gain

- Most investors sabotage their own returns through emotional behavior

### Dollar-Cost Averaging: The Disciplined Approach

- If you invested $100/month into a 10% fund for 25 years, you'd average 10% or more

- Most investors do the opposite — they exhibit **"nutty behavior"**

## RULE 5: Build Mountains of Money with a Responsible Portfolio

### The Brussels Sprouts Analogy

### What Are Bonds?

**Financial bonds explained:**
- Loan you make to government or corporation
- Money is safe as long as entity can pay back, plus annual interest
- Safest: **first-world government bonds** from high-income industrial countries
- Riskier: strong **blue-chip businesses** (Coca-Cola, Wal-Mart, Johnson & Johnson)
- Higher interest = higher risk of default
- Best safety: **short-term government bonds or high-quality corporate bonds**

### Why Short-Term Bonds?

- **Inflation risk with long-term bonds**
- Example: 4% bond over 10 years loses money if inflation runs 6% annually
- Bond pays 4%, but breakfast cereal price rises 6% = net loss
- **Short-term bonds (1-3 years) protect against inflation**
- When short-term bond expires, reinvest at higher rates if inflation rises
- Short-term government bond index handles this automatically

### Bond Mechanics (Half-Page Nutshell)

**Five-year government bond example:**
- Loan government $10,000
- Government promises $10,000 back plus guaranteed interest
- $500 annual interest payments at 5% rate
- Can sell before maturity, but price fluctuates daily
- Might get $10,500 or $9,500 if selling early

**Bond price movements:**
- **When inflation/interest rates rise, bond prices fall**
- Example: bought bond at 5% interest when inflation was 3%
- Inflation jumps to 5% — no new investor wants 5% bond with 5% inflation
- Bond price must drop to $9,500 to attract buyers
- New investor gets same $500 interest but pays less upfront
- **When inflation drops, bond prices rise** — institutional traders rush to buy

### Bond Index Funds Beat Actively Managed Bond Funds

- **Costs matter even more in bond world**
- 2003-2008 data from **John C. Bogle**, *Common Sense on Mutual Funds*:
  - Actively managed bond fund with sales load: 3.7% annual return
  - Actively managed bond fund without sales load: 4.9% annual return
  - **U.S. government bond index: 7.1% annual return**
- Active bond management slashes returns due to hidden fees
- Bond index provides greater statistical chance of success alongside domestic and international stock indexes

**Common sense adjustments:**
- 50-year-old government employee with guaranteed pension can take more risk (less than 50% bonds)

## Hedge Funds—The Rich Stealing from the Rich

### The Upside (Theoretical)

- **Hedge funds** = investment vehicle for wealthy accredited investors
- Minimal regulations allow betting against currencies or shorting stock market
- Can invest heavily in few individual stocks without regulatory egg-basket limits
- If manager's big bets pay off, investors reap rewards

### The Downside (Reality)

**High Fees:**
- Typical hedge fund charges **2% annual expense ratio** (one-third more than average US mutual fund)
- Plus **20% of investors' profits** as additional fee
- "License to print money off the backs of those hoping for high rewards"

**Voluntary Reporting Creates Bias:**
- Hedge funds voluntarily report results — *The Economist* tracks average unaudited returns
- Only surviving funds report; failed funds don't get crunched into averages

**Survivorship Bias + Back-Fill Bias:**
- **Burton Malkiel** (Princeton) and **Robert Ibbotson** (Yale) study (1996-2004): fewer than **25% of hedge funds lasted full eight years**
- 75% mortality rate — would you pick from group with such failure rate?
- Surviving funds report results; constantly dying funds aren't factored in
- Small new funds that succeed grow large enough to add performance figures to databases; unsuccessful ones disappear before reaching database threshold
- This "back-fill bias" inflated reported hedge fund returns by **7.3% annually** (Malkiel and Ibbotson)

**Tax Inefficiency:**
- Remarkably inefficient after taxes due to trading frequency
- Never know ahead of time which funds survive vs. die painful deaths

> "Hedge funds are like hedgehogs. Nice to look at from afar, but you really don't want to get too close to their spines."

- Better off in total stock market index fund

**Final thought from Voltaire (1764):**

> "The best is the enemy of good. Investors who aren't satisfied with a good plan—like indexing—may strive for something they hope will be 'best.' But that path's wake is filled with more tragedies than successes."

### What's a Price-Earnings Ratio?

**P/E ratio** indicates how cheap or expensive a stock is.
Stock price alone is irrelevant—a $5 stock can be more expensive than a $100 stock.

**Example**: Two businesses each generating $1 million annual profits
- Business One: 5 million shares at $5 = $25 million total cost
- Business Two: 20,000 shares at $100 = $2 million total cost
- Business One trades at 25X earnings (P/E = 25)
- Business Two trades at 2X earnings (P/E = 2)

Business One is far more expensive than Business Two.

**Tech stocks vs. old economy stocks** (January 2011 data):
- Tech stocks generally have higher P/E ratios
- Examples: Apple (22), Oracle (23), Qualcomm (26), Agilent (22), Google (25)
- Old economy: Coca-Cola (19), Wal-Mart (13), GE (20), Altria (13), J&J (12)

People willing to pay more for tech stock excitement—despite tech producing lower aggregate returns than old economy stocks when dividends are reinvested.

**Jeremy Siegel** (*The Future for Investors—Why the Tried and True Beats the Bold and New*):
Wharton professor's exhaustive research shows old economy stocks beat new economy (tech) stocks when dividends are reinvested.
Higher dividend payouts for old economy stocks mean more shares automatically purchased through reinvestment.
Snowball effect: new shares purchased with dividends generate further dividends.

Historical profitable stocks (past 50 years): Exxon Mobil, Johnson & Johnson, Coca-Cola
NOT: IBM, Texas Instruments

### Efficient Businesses Make Dollars and Sense

Choose between two businesses each generating $1 billion net income (past 3 years average).
Both growing earnings at same rate, same debt level, both in industries where goods will be used for many years, both can increase product prices with inflation.

Difference:
- Business A generates $1B profits off $10B in plants/machinery/assets
- Business B generates $1B profits off $5B in plants/machinery/assets

Which are you more comfortable with?

**Answer: Company B** (more efficient)
- Generates $1B from $5B in assets/materials = 20% return on total capital ($1B ÷ $5B = 0.20)
- Company A has 10% return on total capital ($1B ÷ $10B = 0.10)

**Return on total capital** measures how efficiently a business uses both shareholders' capital and debt to produce income.
Value of a company ultimately rests on its proven historical ability to earn significant and reliable profit on money invested in business.

Recommend serious stock pickers order subscription through **Value Line** investment-research provider.
Gives access to thousands of businesses around world.
Use portfolio screens to find companies with highest rates of return on total capital, then narrow down to see which have earned those returns consistently.

Single great year or creative accounting won't necessarily be sustainable going forward.
Want durable businesses with long histories of efficiency.

As of October 2010, analyzing more than 2,000 businesses in Value Line survey: fewer than 10% had returns on total capital exceeding 15%.

Refining search further for 10-year track record averaging 15% on total capital: only 5% of 2,000+ businesses fit.

Include: TJX Companies, Weight Watchers, Garmin, Colgate Palmolive, Coach, Stryker, Heinz, Microsoft, Coca-Cola, PepsiCo, Johnson & Johnson, Starbucks.

Using Value Line's stock screen, can find nearly 100 other businesses with 10-year track records averaging 15%+ on total capital.

Insane ritual burns through company cash—consists of buying high and selling low.

**Philip Fisher** (*Common Stocks and Uncommon Profits*): Late author devised pre-Internet system of kicking tires of companies.
Visited customers of businesses he liked, asked questioning their competitors as well.

> "What are the strengths and weaknesses of your competitors?" and "What should you be doing (but are not yet doing) to maintain your competitive advantage?"

Key isn't walking into public relations department asking these questions.
Get in on ground floor where products are being created, sold, or distributed, and ask there.

Internet can be great source of information, but can make people lazy—tempting us to skip getting "hands-on" feel for businesses.

**Construction site example**: Author often wanders in, asks what fastening/construction brackets they're using.
Simpson Manufacturing: business investment club owns shares in.
Always curious to see who's using the products, what they like/dislike about them.
If hear Simpson, Simpson, Simpson, how easy representatives are to work with, how great products are = established ground-floor information might not find on Internet.

As a business owner, important to know your company well.
Don't experiment with shortcuts—could end up getting lost.

### Set Your Price

Once decided which stocks look good, get them at right price.
Think of yourself as business owner buying entire company.

**Example company**:
- Trades at $26/share
- 740 million shares in company
- Entire business worth roughly $19.2 billion

Past three years average net income: $598 million (posted $672M in 2007, $525M in 2008, $598M in 2009).

If we owned entire company, paid $19.2B for it, want to know what our return on investment would be annually if we averaged $598M/year in net profits.

Dividing $598M by $19.2B = return (earnings yield) of 3.1%.

Makes sense from business perspective: if you bought entire business for $19.2B and made $598M after expenses and taxes, you would have made 3.1% on your $19.2B.

Is that a good deal? Depends on alternatives.

Compare yield from your stock with yield on 10-year government bond.
No stock is as safe as government bonds (at least highly developed countries don't go bust).
Would be silly to take on risk involved in buying stock if it yields less than risk-free bond.

Since future earnings on any stock are uncertain, should make sure any shares you buy yield a bit more than 10-year bond.
Extra yield compensates for risk you're embracing in buying the stock.

How much yield you should demand = matter of judgment.
- If company growing rapidly, may be willing to buy when average of past 3 years earnings works out to slightly more than equivalent of 10-year bond yield
- If firm growing slowly, might not want to buy until satisfied it will provide at least tenth more in earnings than 10-year government bond
- If bond yielding 5%, would demand at least 5.5% yield from stock before willing to purchase

**Johnson & Johnson example (2010)**:
- Club bought shares at $57/share
- Past 3 years net income averaged $12.64B
- Multiplying by number of shares in existence, calculate cost to buy entire company: roughly $160B
- Dividing average 3-year net income ($12.64B) by cost of total company ($160B) = annual earnings yield of 7.9%

Comparing with 10-year U.S. government bond yield (2.52%): well compensated for added risk of owning stock instead of bond, so bought shares.

## Selling Stocks

Stockowners should hold companies for long periods, but instances when wise to sell:

1. If company deviated from core business
2. If stock was grossly overpriced

First reason self-explanatory: if company's ability to make chocolate is legendary but decided to switch gears to pursue space tourism (something no track record in), might be wise to bail.

Second reason requires some judgment and math.

### When We Sold Schering Plough

**Schering Plough** (can no longer be purchased, Merck bought them 2009):
- Met investment club's purchase requirements in 2003
- Paid $15.24/share
- Blockbuster allergy medication Claritin losing patent protection
- Other companies could sell generic version for fraction of cost
- Price hammered from ~$40/share (2002) down to slightly more than $15/share (2003)
- Felt Wall Street's reaction to Claritin patent overdone and highly emotional

Prior to price drop, despite being great business, buying at $40/share would have been taking huge risk because bond yield would have been just 3.8%.
Less than what government bond was paying at the time, plus added risk of looming Claritin patent expiration.
Didn't expect Wall Street to hammer stock all the way down to $15.

At $40/share (earning yield 3.8%), not interested.
When earnings yield more than doubled, much more interested.

Earnings levels for Schering Plough in three years before purchase:
- 2001: $1.58
- 2002: $1.34
- 2003: $0.31

Average earnings for previous 3 years: $0.75/share.
At $15.24, that represented earnings yield of 7%.
Bought first shares and hoped price would fall further.

By 2008, Schering Plough's price risen to $25/share.
Earnings yield based on previous 3 years (2005, 2006, 2007) gave business earnings yield of just 3% annually.
Below interest yield on 10-year government bond (which paid roughly 4% at the time), so sold shares at $25.

64% profit over 3 years might sound impressive, but also could view as disappointment.
Investing easier if businesses you buy (at good prices) grow at pace relative to earnings growth.
Then, if business doesn't deviate from business model and most reasons you bought in first place still apply, can keep holding shares as they grow long-term while earning healthy dividends along the way.

Rarely sell individual stocks.
Many stocks eventually sold went on to new highs without us.
Could count Schering Plough as example—Merck bought them out for $28/share (12% higher than when author's club sold).

Generally, fewer trades you make in investment account, more money you'll make.
Whether mutual fund manager or personal stock picker, lower trades equate to lower costs and taxes—generally higher returns.

Committing to stocks for long period requires knowing as much about companies as possible.
To ensure highest odds of familiarity, may want to choose simple, predictable businesses that are efficiently run and likely to stand test of time.
Also consider what a financial tsunami could do to your businesses.
Low-debt levels can be solid foundations—especially during tough times.

When found business you want to buy, analyze price as if buying entire business.
Return you make highly dependent on price you pay.
But even with best stock-picking tools, odds are high that eventually most stock pickers will lose to market-tracking indexes, especially after factoring in transaction costs and taxes.

Fun to fight the tide.
But you should invest bulk of your money intelligently with diversified account of indexes.

## The Nine Rules of Wealth Checklist

People can pass by financial train wrecks or teach strategies to empower good financial decisions.
Most people spend too much on material things, invest inefficiently, allow fear and greed to manipulate wealth levels.

With luck, principles in this book will become mandatory high school curriculum.
Would go long way to ensure people invest responsibly, force financial service industry to limit gouging individual investors.

No matter your age and wealth level, can build financial security using nine rules:

**1.** Spend like a millionaire (or less) if you want to become rich.

**2.** Start investing as early as possible—after paying off credit card debt and any other high-interest loans.

**3.** Invest in low-cost index funds instead of actively managed funds. Nobody can consistently pick "winning" actively managed funds ahead of time.

**4.** Understand stock market history and psychology so you don't fall victim to craziness that infects every investing generation (often more than once).

**5.** Learn to build a complete, balanced portfolio with stock and bond index funds that will easily beat most of the pros.

**6.** Create indexed accounts no matter where you live.

**7.** Learn to fight an adviser's sales rhetoric.

**8.** Avoid investment schemes and scams that tickle your greed button.

**9.** If you must buy common stocks, do it with a small percentage of your portfolio and pick a mentor such as Warren Buffett.
