Chapter 1: The Golden Rules of Trading

Imagine you're sixteen years old and you just bought your first stock. You found a company called Poloron on a magazine's "best growth" list and put your entire $800 into it at $8 a share. The stock climbs to $20. You're thrilled. Then it starts falling. You have no plan for when to sell. You watch it drop back to $8. You actually buy more as it falls, thinking it'll bounce. This actually happened to Van Tharp, and the five mistakes he made in that single trade became the foundation for ten rules that the rest of this book is built on.

The first four rules all come down to one old piece of trading wisdom: cut your losses short and let your profits run. But Tharp makes it specific. Rule 1: before you buy anything, decide exactly how much you're willing to lose on it. If you buy a stock at $50 and decide you'll sell if it drops to $45, your risk is $5 per share. That $5 is called "1R" — one unit of risk. Everything in this book is measured in units of R.

Rule 2: measure all your results in R-multiples. If you risked $5 and made $15, that's a 3R winner. If you risked $5 and lost $5, that's a 1R loser. Rule 3: never let a loss grow bigger than 1R. If you set a $5 stop, honor it. Moving your stop further away because you "feel like it'll come back" is how 1R losses become 4R disasters. Rule 4: your average winner needs to be bigger than 1R. If your average win is 2R and your average loss is 1R, you can be wrong more than half the time and still make money.

Here's the tricky part: your brain is wired to do the exact opposite. Psychologists Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky (who won a Nobel Prize for this work) showed that humans naturally hold onto losers longer — because selling means admitting you were wrong — and sell winners too quickly, because taking a small profit feels like being "right." Every profitable trading rule fights against your natural instincts. That's why rules matter more than feelings.

Rules 5 through 10 build the analytical framework. Understand your trading system as a collection of R-multiples (some positive, some negative). The average of all those R-multiples is your "expectancy" — how much you earn per dollar risked, on average. Design your trading objectives before you start position sizing. Calculate something called the System Quality Number (more on that in Chapter 3). And learn to recognize what kind of market you're in, because some systems work great in trending markets and terribly in choppy ones.

The rules aren't complicated. The challenge is following them when your emotions scream at you to do the opposite. That's the tension at the heart of this entire book: the math is simple, but the psychology is hard. Ed Seykota, one of the greatest traders in history, breaks it down as 60% psychology, 30% position sizing, and only 10% the system itself. The system barely matters compared to how you manage your risk and your head.

Most traders spend 90% of their time on the 10% that matters least.

The Poloron story isn't just autobiography. Each of Tharp's five mistakes maps to a specific rule. No stop = rule 1 violated. No trailing stop = rule 2 violated. All-in bet = rule 7 violated. Adding to losers = rule 3 violated. No plan = rules 6 and 10 violated. He started the book with his own failure because the rules only make sense when you see what happens without them.

Think of it this way: if someone told you "always wear a seatbelt," you'd nod and maybe forget. But if they told you about the time they went through a windshield because they weren't buckled up, you'd remember. That's what the Poloron story does for trading rules. The specifics make the abstract concrete.

Chapter 2: What Is R, and Why Does It Matter?

Let's make this dead simple. You buy a stock at $50. You decide that if it drops to $45, you're out — you were wrong. The difference between $50 and $45 is $5. That $5 is your R. It's how much you lose per share if the trade doesn't work. If you bought 200 shares, your total risk is $1,000 (200 × $5). That's your 1R for this trade.

Now everything gets measured in terms of that R. If the stock goes to $65 and you sell, you made $15 per share on $5 of risk. That's a 3R winner. If you had a trailing stop that got you out at $60, that's a 2R winner. If you ignored your stop and the stock dropped to $40, that's a 2R loser (you lost $10 when you should have only lost $5). The beauty of R-multiples is that they work the same whether you're trading a $10 stock, a $100 stock, soybean futures, or euros against dollars. Everything becomes comparable.

Here's a real example from the book. Buy a stock at $8 with a stop at $6. R = $2 per share. The stock climbs to $28 and you sell. Profit = $20 per share ÷ $2 risk = a 10R winner. That's a fantastic trade. Now imagine you had a 25% trailing stop: when the stock hit $20, your stop moved up to $15. If it pulled back and hit $15, you'd exit with $7 profit on $2 risk = 3.5R. Still great. Without any stop? You might ride it all the way back down to $8 and make nothing. The trailing stop is what turns a ride into a profit.

The traditional way to calculate how good a system is uses this formula: (average profit × win probability) minus (average loss × loss probability). The problem is that gives you a dollar amount, which doesn't tell you how much you're earning per dollar risked. Tharp fixes this by dividing by the average risk. The result is expectancy in R-units. His sample system has an expectancy of 0.966R — meaning for every dollar you risk, you expect to earn about 97 cents over many trades. Not per trade, not immediately — over many trades. It's an average, and individual trades will swing wildly around it.

The standard deviation tells you how wildly. Tharp's sample system has a standard deviation of 3.14R. That means individual trades will regularly come in 3R above or below the average. You might have a stretch of −5R losers followed by a +12R winner. Both are normal for this system. The question is: can your account survive the losers long enough to capture the winners? That's what position sizing answers, and why R-multiples are the foundation for everything that follows.

The key insight is that R is decided before you enter the trade. Once you know your R, you know your maximum loss. You know what a "good" trade looks like (2R+ winner) and what a disaster looks like (anything bigger than 1R loss, which means you didn't honor your stop). The system for how much to trade — position sizing — is impossible without knowing R first. And if you can't define R for a particular trade, Tharp's advice is simple: don't take the trade.

A useful mental model: think of R as your "unit of measurement" for trading, like inches are for length. Saying "I made $5,000 on that trade" is like saying "that building is tall." Saying "I made 5R on that trade" is like saying "that building is 50 stories." The second version tells you something meaningful because it's relative to a known standard. Every trade in your history, across every instrument, can be expressed in R, making them all directly comparable.

The entire book rests on three assumptions: you can define R before entering, you will honor your stop losses, and you will let winners run as multiples of R. If you can't do these three things consistently, no position sizing method will save you. The math doesn't work without disciplined execution of the basics.

Chapter 3: How Good Is Your Trading System, Really?

Most people judge trading systems by win rate. "I win 80% of my trades" sounds impressive. "I only win 10% of my trades" sounds terrible. Tharp sets up a devastating demonstration by presenting six systems and asking readers to rank them. Almost everyone picks System 3-3, which wins 90% of the time. The punchline: System 3-3 loses money over time. Its rare losers are so large (10R) that they wipe out all the small 1R winners. Meanwhile, System 3-5, winning only 10% of the time, has the best expectancy of all (4.10R per trade) because its rare winners are enormous 50R payoffs that dwarf the steady 1R losses.

Win rate alone is like judging a basketball player only by how often they shoot. A player who takes 100 shots and makes 90 of them sounds great — until you learn that all 90 made shots were layups worth 2 points each (180 total) and the 10 misses were three-pointers that cost the team 10 possessions each. Meanwhile, the player who makes only 10 of 100 shots but each make is a three-pointer worth 50 points (500 total) is far more valuable. In trading, the size of your wins and losses matters at least as much as the frequency.

So if win rate is misleading, is expectancy the right measure? Better, but still not enough. Expectancy tells you the average R per trade but ignores how bumpy the ride is. System 3-5 has 4.10R expectancy — amazing — but also enormous variability. In 10,000 simulations, some paths show devastating drawdowns. System 3-6 has the second-best expectancy (1.04R) but an average drawdown of 53.4R — meaning your account would regularly lose more than 53 times your per-trade risk. Most people would quit long before the positive expectancy materialized.

Tharp's solution is the System Quality Number (SQN). The formula: expectancy divided by standard deviation of R, multiplied by the square root of the number of trades. In plain English: how big is your edge compared to how bumpy the ride is, adjusted for how many trades you have? It's essentially a statistical test asking: "Is this edge real, or could it just be random noise?"

The ranking flips completely when you use SQN instead of win rate or expectancy. System 3-2 — the boring one with only 0.15R expectancy but very consistent results — ranks first because its low variability makes its small edge reliable. Think of it like a salary versus freelancing: a $60K salary (low return, low variance) might be "better" than freelancing that averages $100K but swings between $0 and $300K, depending on your definition of "better." SQN captures this intuition mathematically.

A critical warning: SQN inflates with more trades because the formula includes the square root of N. A system with 198 trades might show SQN 5.19, but normalized to 100 trades it drops to 3.68. Always compare systems at the same trade count (Tharp recommends capping at 100). And with very small samples, the bar is higher: 10 trades needs SQN above 3.50 to be meaningful.

One beautiful counterintuitive finding: adding huge winners to a system actually hurts its SQN. If your system grinds out 1.5R winners regularly, adding a couple of 30R lottery tickets inflates the standard deviation so much that the SQN drops by 20%. The steadiest edge — not the flashiest — makes the best system. This has real implications: if you're designing a trading system, optimize for consistency over home runs.

Think of SQN as the "signal-to-noise ratio" of your trading system. A high SQN means the signal (your edge) is loud relative to the noise (random variation). A low SQN means you can barely tell if you have an edge at all. Position sizing decisions depend entirely on this number, because a system with a weak, noisy edge can only be traded with tiny positions, while a strong, clear edge opens up more aggressive options.

Chapter 4: What Could Actually Happen Next?

Here's a trap that catches almost every trader: you backtest your system over 20 years of data, see a beautiful equity curve, and conclude "this is what will happen." Wrong. That beautiful curve is one possible path through those trades. If the same trades had arrived in a different order, the equity curve could look completely different. Maybe the backtest put all the losers early and the big winners late, making the curve look like a hockey stick. Rearrange those trades and you might get a devastating drawdown in the middle that would make you quit.

Monte Carlo simulation solves this problem. Think of your trade results as marbles in a bag — some winning marbles, some losing marbles. A backtest draws them out in one specific order. A Monte Carlo simulation draws them out in random orders thousands of times, showing you the full range of possible outcomes. After 10,000 simulations of a 130-trade system, you learn things like: the average drawdown is 30.5R (thirty times your per-trade risk), the worst observed drawdown reached 162R, you'll probably see 6 losing trades in a row on average (with a possible maximum of 15), and there's a 97.6% chance of at least breaking even.

Those numbers are what you actually need for position sizing. If you know your worst likely drawdown is 60R, you can work backward: "I can tolerate a 25% account decline, so I should risk 25% ÷ 60 = about 0.4% per trade." That's a concrete, actionable number derived from statistical analysis, not from a gut feeling or a single backtest.

Tharp introduces six market types: markets can trend up, go sideways, or trend down, and each can be either quiet (small daily moves) or volatile (big daily swings). That gives you six environments: up-quiet, up-volatile, sideways-quiet, sideways-volatile, down-quiet, and down-volatile. A system with an overall SQN of 1.94 — mediocre — might be SQN 4.32 in up-quiet markets and −1.13 in sideways-volatile ones. That's not a mediocre system; it's an excellent system with a filter. If you only trade it in quiet trending markets, it's superb. If you trade it in all conditions, it's barely profitable.

This is like knowing that your car gets 40 mpg on the highway and 15 mpg in stop-and-go city traffic. The car isn't "bad" — it's being used in the wrong conditions. Knowing your system's market-type profile tells you when to press the gas and when to park.

The S&P 500 from 1995 to 2005 spent its bull market years (pre-2000) going up 70% of the time in mostly quiet conditions. After 2000, it was down or sideways 67% of the time. Traders who kept running their bull-market system into the bear market wondered why it stopped working. It didn't stop working — the environment changed. Different weather needs different clothes.

Correlation risk is the hidden danger. You might think 20 different stock positions means diversification. During a crash, they all move together. Steve Sjuggerud puts it perfectly: "All ships fall with the tide." The 1987 crash (S&P down 20% in one day) and 9/11 (12%+ in a week) show that diversification disappears exactly when you need it most. Position sizing needs to account for the worst case, not the average case.

The bottom line: before you trade any system, simulate it thousands of times, know your likely drawdown ranges, understand which market types suit it, and account for the fact that your "diversified" positions might all move against you simultaneously. This preparation is what separates informed trading from sophisticated gambling.

Chapter 5: The Psychological Traps That Kill Traders

Your brain is a survival machine that evolved to keep you alive on the savanna, not to manage a trading portfolio. French economist George Anderla calculated that all human knowledge doubled in the 1,500 years from Jesus to Leonardo da Vinci. It doubled again by 1750. Again by 1900. Again by 1960. Now it doubles in under a year. Your conscious mind can only process about seven things at once. To cope, your brain takes shortcuts: it generalizes, deletes information, and distorts what remains. These shortcuts kept your ancestors alive. In trading, they'll bankrupt you.

Locus of Control Bias: About 30% of stock market books have "picking" in the title. We believe success comes from choosing the right stock, just like lottery players believe picking their own numbers gives them better odds. It doesn't. In trading, you don't control which stocks go up. You control your exits. Your stop-loss caps your risk at 1R. Your trailing stop lets winners run. The entry is the least important decision you make.

Need to Be Right Bias: School taught you that 70% correct is passing and 94% is an A. In trading, 40% correct can be wildly profitable if your winners are big enough. But being "wrong" 60% of the time feels like failure, so traders hold losers (to avoid admitting they're wrong) and cut winners (to lock in the feeling of being right). This is exactly backward from what works.

Percent Gain Bias: "XYZ went up 150%!" screams the newsletter. You imagine your whole portfolio up 150%. But if you risked 1% of your equity on that trade with proper position sizing, the stock going up 150% made your account go up maybe 3.5%. And the newsletter isn't telling you about the seven losers that went alongside those three winners. When you add them all up — in R-multiples, not percentages — the newsletter's net result might be negative. Always ask: "What's the R-multiple of the complete track record?"

Authority Bias: "Pick Stocks Like Warren Buffett" by Warren Boroson. "How to Pick Stocks Like Warren Buffett" by Timothy Vick. Buffett wrote neither of these books. We defer to famous names and high-paid analysts, forgetting that the only authority that matters is your own system's data.

Prediction Bias: Traders think they need to understand why markets move to trade profitably. They don't. A trend follower only needs to know that big trends happen occasionally and catching them pays for the small losses in between. A value investor only needs to know when something is undervalued and when it stops being so. At a 1997 conference, Tharp said: "People don't make money by predicting markets. They make money by cutting losses short and letting profits run."

Law of Small Numbers: You find six chart patterns that worked perfectly. You declare you have a system. But out of 300 consolidation periods, over 7,000 instances of those patterns did nothing — they "worked" about 3% of the time. Your brain found a pattern in random noise and declared it meaningful. Even if the pattern is real, you still need a stop-loss, an exit plan, and position sizing before it's a tradeable system.

The scary thing about these biases is that knowing about them doesn't make them go away. They're hardwired. The solution isn't awareness — it's rules. Systematic, mechanical rules that override your emotional impulses. That's what the rest of this book provides: specific mathematical frameworks that produce position sizing decisions without consulting your feelings.

Chapter 6: Why Smart People Still Lose Money

Ralph Vince ran an experiment that should be posted on every trading desk in the world. He gave 40 PhDs a computer game: 60% chance of winning, 40% chance of losing, win or lose exactly the same amount each time, $10,000 starting capital, 100 rounds. This is a game that beats every casino in Las Vegas. How many of the 40 PhDs made money? Two. Two. Ninety-five percent of brilliant, educated people lost money in a game that was rigged in their favor. The only variable was how much they bet each round.

Here's how the typical PhD lost. Start betting $1,000 per round. Lose three in a row (odds: about 6%, so it'll happen). Account drops from $10,000 to $7,000. Think "I'm due for a win" (you're not — each round is independent). Bet $3,000. Lose again. Account drops to $4,000. Now you need to more than double your money just to get back to where you started. The gambler's fallacy — the belief that past losses make future wins more likely — is the most expensive cognitive error in human history.

The recovery asymmetry explains why going broke is so much easier than getting rich. If you lose 20% of your account, you need a 25% gain to recover. Lose 50% and you need 100%. Lose 75% and you need 300%. Lose 90% and you need 900% — essentially impossible.

This is why position sizing exists: to prevent you from ever digging a hole too deep to escape. A system with positive expectancy will make money if you survive long enough. Position sizing is the survival mechanism. The Market Wizards all say the same thing. Bruce Kovner: "Undertrade, undertrade, undertrade. Whatever you think your position ought to be, cut it at least in half." Larry Hite: "Never risk more than 1% of your total equity in any one trade." These aren't theoretical positions. These are rules that produced hundreds of millions in profits.

Here's a story that drives it home. A player of Tharp's "Secrets of the Masters" trading game knew the exact odds. He started with $10,000 and ran it up to $1,045,960 on 23 consecutive winners — odds of about 8 in a million. On trade 24, he bet $1,000,000 of his $1,045,960. It was his first loss. He ended up roughly a million dollars in debt. He won 28 out of 31 trades and went broke. A winning streak doesn't change the odds. It changes how you feel about the odds, which is infinitely more dangerous.

In a 60% win-rate system over 100 trades, there's a 100% probability of hitting at least four losses in a row. Average longest streak: five. Maximum observed: fourteen. For a 50% system: average streak of six, maximum of nineteen. These aren't edge cases. They're mathematical certainties. Your position sizing must survive the longest streak you'll encounter, or you'll go broke during an event that was guaranteed to happen eventually.

The definition Tharp settles on is precise: a "low-risk idea" is one with positive long-term expectancy, traded at a risk level small enough to survive the worst short-term outcome so you can realize the long-term edge. Position sizing is not about maximizing returns. It's about keeping you in the game long enough for your edge to work.

One more practical point: the Kelly Criterion says the optimal bet in a 60/40 game is 20% of your equity. But "optimal" here means "maximizes long-term growth rate" — with hair-raising drawdowns along the way. In real trading with its uncertainties, the practical maximum is 3-4%. If you have $5,000, that means $150-200 per trade. At that level, you can barely trade one position at a time. Small accounts face a structural disadvantage that position sizing can manage but not eliminate.

Chapter 7: The One Formula You Actually Need

Three hundred people sit in a room playing the same trading game. They receive the same trades in the same order from the same system. Starting equity: $100,000 each. At the end, some are bankrupt and some have over a million dollars. The only difference is how much each person chose to risk on each trade. That's the marble game experiment, and it proves that position sizing — the "how much" decision — is the dominant factor in trading outcomes.

The academic version comes from Brinson, Singer, and Beebower, who studied 82 portfolio managers over ten years and found that 91%+ of performance variance came from asset allocation — the institutional version of "how much do I put in each bucket?" The conclusion is the same at every level: the quantity decision matters more than the selection decision.

Tharp's universal formula for position sizing is beautifully simple: P = C / R. P is how many units (shares, contracts) you buy. C is your "cash at risk" — the percentage of your account you're willing to risk, times your account size. R is the risk per unit — the distance from your entry to your stop, times the unit size.

Let's walk through an example. You have a $30,000 account. You're willing to risk 2%. C = $30,000 × 2% = $600. You want to buy a stock at $50 with a stop at $45, so the risk per share is $5. R = $5. P = $600 / $5 = 120 shares. The total position costs $6,000 but you're only risking $600 — because if the stock hits your stop, you lose $5 × 120 = $600. The formula told you exactly how many shares to buy to risk exactly 2% of your account.

For futures: same account, 2% risk. Soybean contract at $5.20 per bushel with a 20-cent stop and 5,000 bushels per contract. R = $0.20 × 5,000 = $1,000. C = $600. P = 600 / 1,000 = 0.6 contracts. You can't trade 0.6 contracts — round down to zero. The formula just told you that this trade is too risky for your account size. Better to learn that from math than from a margin call.

The "account size" in the formula depends on which equity model you choose. There are three: Core Equity (starting capital minus what's already committed to open trades — most conservative), Total Equity (cash plus current market value of all open positions — most aggressive), and Reduced Total Equity (core plus any profits locked in by trailing stops — middle ground). Think of it like budgeting: core equity is like spending only from your savings account, total equity is like counting your investment portfolio's current value as spendable, and reduced total is somewhere in between.

Most of the book uses total equity as the default. The choice matters because a $100,000 account with $30,000 in open positions has core equity of $70,000 but total equity of $130,000 if those positions are currently profitable. At 2% risk, that's $1,400 versus $2,600 per trade — almost double. The more aggressive model compounds faster during winning streaks but also accelerates losses during drawdowns. Match the choice to your risk tolerance.

The beauty of P = C / R is that it works for anything. Stocks, futures, forex, options, crypto. Three variables, one formula, universal application. If you remember nothing else from this book, remember this formula and use it before every trade. It takes thirty seconds and it's the single most important calculation in all of trading.

Chapter 8: The Five Basic Ways to Size a Position

All five models are tested on the same system so we can see the pure effect of the sizing method: Donchian's 55-day channel breakout with a 21-day trailing stop, run on ten commodities from 1981 to 1991 with $1 million starting equity. Same trades, same exits, different sizing = different results. That's the experimental design, and it's rigorous.

Model 1: One Unit per Fixed Amount of Money. Buy one futures contract for every $50,000 of equity. It's the most common default because it's the simplest. Problem: a Treasury bond contract ($116K notional) and a corn contract ($12K notional) are treated identically, creating an 85/15 portfolio imbalance the model can't see. At $100K per contract: $5M profit, 37% max drawdown. At $20K per contract: negative $1.7M, 112% drawdown, 402 rejected trades. The model breaks completely when you push it.

Model 2: Equal Units. Divide capital into 5-10 equal slices. Better than Model 1 but still blind to the fact that different markets move different amounts per day. Model 3: Percent Margin. Size based on exchange margin requirements. The problem is that margins are set by exchanges for regulatory purposes and have nothing to do with actual risk. It's like setting your speed based on the speed limit when you should be driving based on road conditions.

Model 4: Percent Volatility. Now we're getting somewhere. This model sizes positions inversely to their volatility — if a market moves $3 a day and your equity allows $1,000 of daily movement, you buy 3.3 contracts. High-volatility markets get smaller positions; low-volatility markets get larger ones. At 2% volatility: $236 million in gains! But also 69-86% annual drawdowns. The model works beautifully because it sizes aggressively into trending markets, but the drawdowns would psychologically destroy most traders.

Model 5: Percent Risk (also called Fixed Fractional). This is the professional's choice. Instead of sizing by volatility, you size by the distance to your stop — your actual risk. At 2.5% risk on $50K equity with $1,000 stop per contract: buy 1.25 contracts, round to 1. At 5% risk: $5.6M profit. Compared to percent volatility at 2%: $236M. Percent risk is far more conservative but far safer.

The trade-off cannot be escaped: the model that makes the most money also produces the worst drawdowns. The model that lets you sleep at night leaves the most profit on the table. There is no free lunch. Every dollar of additional expected return costs you a corresponding increase in drawdown risk.

Think of the five models as a progression from dumb to smart. Model 1 ignores everything. Model 2 adds equal weighting. Model 3 uses a bad proxy (margin). Model 4 sizes by market behavior (volatility). Model 5 sizes by actual risk (your stop distance). Each step adds information and produces better risk-adjusted results. Most professionals land on Model 5 and then customize it with the advanced models from later chapters.

The key takeaway: even among just five models, the same system produces wildly different outcomes. The system doesn't change. The entries don't change. The exits don't change. Only the sizing changes, and it changes everything. That's why Tharp says 90% of your results come from position sizing.

Chapter 9: More Tools for Special Situations

The five core models handle most situations, but real-world trading throws curveballs. What if all your positions are in the same sector? What if you trade for a bank and don't know your equity? What if you trade both long and short? This chapter provides seven additional models for these specific problems.

Group Control: Limit your total risk in any one correlated group. During a financial crisis, five bank stocks don't give you five independent positions — they give you one giant bet on banks. Group control caps how much risk you can concentrate in any single sector. Think of it as "don't put all your eggs in one basket" applied to position sizing.

Portfolio Heat: Track total open risk across all positions. If you have ten trades each risking 2%, your portfolio heat is 20%. Most professionals cap this at 20%. But if those ten positions are correlated (like ten tech stocks during a bubble burst), your effective heat could be much higher than the sum suggests. This model forces you to think about aggregate risk, not just per-trade risk.

Equity Crossover: This one is clever. Treat your account equity like a stock chart. If your equity curve drops below its own moving average, stop trading or reduce size. A declining equity curve means either your system stopped working or you're in a hostile market type. Either way, shrinking your positions makes sense. When your equity recovers above its average, resume full sizing. It's a meta-system — a system for your system.

Position Sizing for Bank Traders: If you're a prop trader and don't know how much equity you're managing, ask one question: "How much could I lose before I'd be fired?" That's your effective equity. Apply percent risk to that number. Bankers Trust tried this in Australia in the 1990s and it worked beautifully — until the New York office underperformed and nobody got bonuses regardless of individual results. Perfect risk management destroyed by broken incentives. The bank was eventually acquired by Deutsche Bank.

Long versus Short: Different risk parameters for long and short trades. Shorts have theoretically unlimited risk (a stock can go to infinity) and fight the market's natural upward drift. You might risk 2% on longs but only 1.5% on shorts to account for this asymmetry.

Asset Allocation Model: For investors (not traders) who want position sizing to flow from their allocation framework. Decide "30% bonds, 50% stocks, 20% alternatives" first, then size individual positions within each bucket.

The key insight: these models are guardrails, not optimizers. They don't help you find the "best" position size — they prevent specific failure modes. Professional traders typically use several at once: percent risk as the primary sizing method, with portfolio heat, group control, and equity crossover as secondary constraints. Belt and suspenders.

Chapter 10: The Experiment That Proves Everything

This is the chapter that makes the entire book's case in a single experiment. Take twelve commodities from 1985 to 1995. Enter trades based on a coin flip — literally random. Exit with a trailing stop set at three times the market's recent volatility. Start with $1 million. The system is profitable about 80% of the time despite random entries, because the exit strategy captures trends while cutting losses.

The baseline: one unit per trade, no position sizing at all. Final equity: $1,382,853. Then apply position sizing models to the same random entries and same exits. Here's what happens:

One unit per $100,000 of equity: $23.76 million. Percent risk at 2.5%: $75.74 million. Tom Basso's scale-out rules: $78.65 million. Percent volatility at 2.5%: $154 million. Percent risk plus William Eckhardt's scaling-in method (used by the Turtles): $640 million. Same coin flip. Same exits. Position sizing alone created a 1,700-times difference in final equity.

But here's what the chart doesn't show: the $640 million model had an 82.2% maximum drawdown. The $75.74 million model dropped from $15.15 million to $7.9 million at one point. Most traders would quit during either of those drawdowns, giving up the eventual profits. The model that produces the most money also requires the most pain tolerance.

The random entry part is what makes this experiment so powerful. If a coin flip with proper exits and position sizing beats most professional money managers, then the obsession with finding the perfect entry signal is a colossal waste of time. Entries don't matter. Exits matter. Position sizing matters. Everything else is noise.

This is the experiment that should end every argument about whether position sizing matters. Same trades, same exits, 1,700 times difference in results. The "how much" question isn't a small factor in trading — it's the dominant factor.

The Eckhardt scaling method is worth understanding: start at 2.5% risk. When the trade moves in your favor enough that the initial risk is reduced by 50%, add a new position equal to half the original. Do this up to five times, creating a maximum position of 350% of the original, capped at 50% portfolio heat. You're adding to winners, not losers — your biggest positions are in your best trades. This is anti-Martingale at its most refined.

No model is "best." Each represents a point on the return-versus-drawdown continuum. If you can't handle more than 25% drawdowns, you'd lose money on every model tested here except the baseline (one unit, no sizing). The best model is the one you can execute consistently through the inevitable drawdowns — and only you know which one that is.

Chapter 11: What Do You Actually Want?

"I want to make as much money as possible" is not an objective. It's avoidance disguised as ambition. Tharp says at least 50% of system design should be spent on defining objectives. A real objective sounds like: "I want to grow my account by 100% over the next year while never experiencing a drawdown exceeding 25%." That's specific enough to build a position sizing strategy around.

Why does this matter? Because different objectives require different position sizes. If you want maximum average return, math says to risk 30% per trade. But at 30% risk, you have a 99.8% chance of ruin. The average return is high because one simulation in ten thousand hits $31.8 billion, which pulls the average up while 9,998 others go bankrupt. "Optimal" for maximum average return is "guaranteed ruin" for any individual trader.

Tharp tests six different definitions of "optimal" on the same system. Maximum mean return: 30% risk, 99.8% ruin. Maximum median return: 4.2% risk, 28% ruin. Greatest probability of hitting 300% return: 2.4% risk, 10.3% ruin. Less than 1% chance of ruin: 1.0% risk, 47.3% chance of reaching the goal. Tharp's personal preference — maximum spread between objective probability and ruin probability: 1.8% risk, 76.4% chance of reaching the goal, 4.9% ruin.

Think of it like speed limits. "What's the fastest I can drive?" is 200 mph on the autobahn. "What's the fastest I can drive while keeping my chance of dying below 1%?" is maybe 75 mph on a highway with a seatbelt. The "optimal speed" depends entirely on how you define optimal. And "maximum speed" is a death wish.

A fascinating finding: if you're willing to tolerate bigger drawdowns, every metric improves. Change the failure threshold from 25% drawdown to 50%, and Tharp's preferred risk level rises from 1.8% to 3.0%, with the probability of reaching the objective rising from 76.4% to 87.5%. Your drawdown tolerance is the single most important input to your position sizing decision. More tolerance = more flexibility = better expected outcomes. But only if you actually have that tolerance when the drawdown hits.

This chapter reframes the entire position sizing question. It's not "what's the best model?" It's "what model matches my specific objectives and my actual tolerance for pain?" Two traders with identical systems should use completely different position sizing if one can tolerate 50% drawdowns and the other panics at 15%. The math is the same for both. The inputs are different because they want different things.

System quality determines how much flexibility you have. An SQN 2 system has limited options — even the best sizing approach gives only a 79.6% chance of reaching a 300% target. An SQN 5 system would offer dramatically more room, achieving higher goals at lower ruin probabilities. This is why system quality comes before position sizing: the better your system, the more position sizing options open up to you.

The practical takeaway: before you decide how much to risk per trade, write down three things. What return do you want? What's the maximum drawdown you can genuinely tolerate (not hope to tolerate — actually tolerate)? And what's your system's SQN? With those three inputs and the tables in this book, you can look up your appropriate risk percentage directly. No guessing needed.

Chapter 12: Playing with the House's Money

Have you ever been at a casino and mentally separated your original money from your winnings? "I started with $200 and I'm up $300, so I'll bet bigger with the $300 because it's 'free money.'" Financially, that's irrational — a dollar is a dollar regardless of where it came from. Psychologically, it's one of the most powerful tools in trading.

The Market's Money approach separates your starting capital from your profits and applies different risk rules to each. Risk 1% of your original $100,000 = $1,000 per trade. After accumulating $25,000 in profits, risk 4% of those profits = $1,000 more. Your total risk doubles, but half comes from money the market "gave" you. If a drawdown wipes out the profits, you drop back to risking only 1% of your original capital. You've lost some of the market's money, not your own. The psychological impact of this separation is enormous — traders using this approach are more willing to stay in trades and tolerate drawdowns.

Five "reset methods" determine when you reclassify profits as "your money": Percentage gain — reset at 100% annually. Calendar date — reset every December 31. Monetary goal — reset when you've saved enough for a down payment or paid off a mortgage. Trade count — reset after 1,000 trades. Mathematical formula — continuously adjust based on a running average of recent equity changes. Each has trade-offs between simplicity and responsiveness.

Scaling in — adding to winning positions — is the natural companion to market's money. The Turtles' method (from William Eckhardt): when a trade moves in your favor enough to cut your initial risk in half, add a half-sized position. Do this up to five times. Your biggest positions end up in your best trades during your best periods. During drawdowns, there's nothing to add to because nothing is moving favorably. The system automatically concentrates capital in the right places at the right times.

The compound effect of combining market's money with scaling is powerful. Profits grow the base position size (market's money). Winning positions attract additional units (scaling in). The result: maximum exposure during winning streaks, minimum exposure during drawdowns. This expansion-contraction cycle is the anti-Martingale principle at its most refined — the opposite of the gambler's fallacy of betting big after losses.

The mathematical formula method deserves a closer look because it's the most adaptive. Define a base (starting equity), calculate a moving average of recent equity changes (say, the change over the last four trades divided by four), then set risk as 1% of base plus 10% of the moving average, capped at 4% total. As profits accumulate, the formula automatically increases risk capacity. During drawdowns, it automatically contracts. No manual intervention needed.

A word of caution: the psychological separation between "my money" and "the market's money" is a useful illusion. It's not mathematically real — losing $25,000 of "the market's money" hurts your account exactly as much as losing $25,000 of "your money." But if the illusion helps you trade more rationally, hold positions longer, and tolerate necessary drawdowns, then it's doing its job. Trading psychology is about managing your emotions so they don't override your system, and the market's money concept is one of the best tools for that purpose.

The practical question of when to reset matters. Calendar resets are simple but arbitrary. Goal-based resets align with your life. The formula approach adjusts continuously but is harder to implement. Pick the one that matches your personality: if you like simplicity, use calendar dates. If you like control, use the formula. All work; none is best.

Chapter 13: The Turbo Button (Fixed Ratio Sizing)

Imagine you have $25,000 and you're trading one contract. With percent risk at 2%, you'd stay at one contract until your account doubled. That could take years. Fixed Ratio Position Sizing (FRPS), developed by Ryan Jones, offers a shortcut: instead of maintaining a fixed percentage, you add one unit when your equity grows by a specific dollar amount (called the "delta") times your current number of units. Going from 1 to 2 units requires gaining one delta. Going from 2 to 3 requires two deltas. Going from 10 to 11 requires ten deltas. The scaling is fast early and slows down as positions grow.

For small accounts, this is transformative. A $25,000 account with a $2,500 delta goes from 1 to 2 contracts after gaining $2,500, from 2 to 3 after gaining another $5,000, from 3 to 4 after $7,500 more. The acceleration at the beginning can compress years of slow compounding into months. But there's a catch — and it's a big one.

Tharp tests FRPS across six systems with varying quality. For the excellent system (SQN 5.04), the most aggressive FRPS setting produces average equity of $4.49 million versus $332,000 at a flat 0.5% risk. FRPS turbocharges a good system. But for a weak system (SQN 0.20, positive but barely), the same setting produces 82% ruin. For a system with negative expectancy masked by a high win rate: 90.4% ruin. FRPS is a magnifying glass — it amplifies whatever the system produces, whether that's profits or destruction.

The critical number: 40% of FRPS configurations result in ruin when the delta is chosen randomly. That's terrifying. The fix: don't choose delta randomly. Simulate your system 100 times across 300 trades, find the average maximum drawdown, and set delta to half that value. With this discipline, FRPS works well for strong systems and shows no advantage over simpler percent risk for weak ones. The delta must be anchored to your system's actual drawdown characteristics, not to wishful thinking.

An enhanced version gives the best results of all: use FRPS when your account is small (it scales faster), then switch to percent risk when percent risk offers a larger position than FRPS (which happens naturally as the account grows). This hybrid produces average equity of $5.3 million versus $2.7 million for FRPS alone, and $12.87 million when the maximum drawdown estimate is intentionally aggressive (with a 4.6% ruin trade-off). The logic is elegant: FRPS is the turbo for small accounts, percent risk is the highway cruiser for large ones.

Tharp's personal verdict on FRPS is cautious. It's not intuitive. It's complex when simpler alternatives exist. It carries high bankruptcy risk during price shocks. Among his contacts, only Chris Anderson (the PhD who developed the simulator) actually uses it. Prerequisites: SQN of at least 2.5, hundreds of simulations to find maximum drawdown, and a paper-trading circuit breaker if equity drops half the historical maximum drawdown. If any of this sounds like too much work, percent risk is simpler, nearly as effective, and far less likely to blow up.

The dampening factor controls how fast you scale back down during drawdowns. At 100% dampening, you step down exactly as fast as you stepped up. At 50% dampening, you step down twice as fast — more protective but slower to recover. The choice requires simulation, not gut feeling.

Bottom line: FRPS is the turbo button. In the right car (good system) on the right road (favorable market), it's exhilarating. In the wrong car (weak system) on the wrong road (hostile market), it's a death trap. Know your system before you press it.

Chapter 14: How to Not Go Broke

Money managers live and die by one number: their worst peak-to-trough drawdown. An account that goes $50K → $80K → $60K has a 25% drawdown from peak. Nobody remembers the 60% gain. Everyone remembers the 25% drawdown. This chapter provides six models specifically designed to keep drawdowns within bounds that won't destroy your career or your psychology.

Model 17: SQN-Based Risk Tables. The most practical tool in the book. Tharp ran 10,000 simulations for each of seven systems at risk levels from 0.2% to 10%, creating lookup tables that any trader can use. Want less than 1% chance of a 25% drawdown with an SQN 4 system? Maximum risk: 2.4%. With an SQN 1 system? Only 0.2%. The tables are simple: find your SQN, pick your drawdown tolerance, read your risk limit.

The correlation warning is critical. If you have 10 positions and they're all correlated (like 10 tech stocks), divide the table's number by 10. An SQN 4 system with a 25% ruin level and 10 correlated positions: 2.4% ÷ 10 = 0.24% per position. Not 2.4%. Correlation destroys the diversification assumption that most traders rely on, and it appears exactly when you need diversification most — during crises.

Model 18: Two-Tier Sizing. Conservative on your base capital, aggressive on profits. Risk 0.4% normally (1.4% chance of ruin). When equity grows 40% above starting capital, switch to 1.2% risk. If the worst drawdown at the aggressive level hits 40%, your account drops to 84% of starting capital — still above the 75% ruin threshold. The math is designed so even the worst outcome at the aggressive tier doesn't destroy your original stake.

Model 19: Multiple Tiers. Instead of one big jump, gradually increase: start at 0.4%, add 0.2% every 5% equity growth, up to 1.2% max. Step down during drawdowns with a dampening factor. Smoother than two-tier, less psychologically jarring.

Model 20: Maximum R-Drawdown. Simulate your system, find that 10% of simulations show a 60R drawdown. Want to limit real drawdowns to 25%? Risk = 25% / 60R = 0.4% per trade. Clean math, no guessing. The five steps: define target drawdown, simulate R-drawdowns, pick probability level, divide, adjust for correlation.

Model 21: Tom Basso's Real-Time Scaling. Monitor your total open risk every minute (Basso literally did this by computer). If four gold contracts create $12,000 of open risk but your limit is $6,480, sell two contracts. Important: do NOT raise your stop to reduce risk while keeping the contracts. That merges exit decisions with sizing decisions, which must stay separate.

Model 22: Basso-Schwager Rebalancing. Study of 79 CTAs: monthly rebalancing across three managers reduced max drawdown from 34% to 28% while giving up less than 1% annual return. The rebalanced group could be leveraged 1.2x to produce 15.3% return at the same drawdown. Application for individuals: run 5+ uncorrelated systems, rebalance monthly.

The theme across all six models: drawdown management is position sizing's primary job. Making money is secondary. If you avoid ruin, a positive-expectancy system will eventually make money. If you don't avoid ruin, it doesn't matter how good your system is — you'll be wiped out before the math saves you.

Chapter 15: Methods That Will Destroy You

This chapter is a field guide to position sizing methods that sound smart and will bankrupt you. There are nine of them — four Martingale models and five other dangerous approaches. More position sizing methods exist that can destroy you than methods that can help you, and the dangerous ones often sound the most mathematically sophisticated.

Martingale strategies all share one premise: bet more after losing because a win is "due." In a casino doubling system, twelve consecutive losses on a $5,000 account require a $4,096 bet with only $905 remaining. "But twelve losses in a row almost never happens!" True — for any specific twelve trades. Over a career of thousands of trades, it's mathematically certain. Professional gamblers all say the same thing: don't use Martingale strategies. They don't work.

Larry Williams' version (Model 24) adds one unit after every loss and subtracts one after every win. In a 30% win-rate system over 100 trades (30 wins, 70 losses), position size grows to 41 units. Tharp calls it "probably one of the most dangerous and foolhardy method of position sizing I've ever seen." A more "conservative" version only increases after three consecutive losses, but simulations show that streaks of twelve or more are virtually certain over any five-year period.

Intuitive Sizing (Model 27): Risk more on trades you "feel good about." Psychological research shows zero correlation between trader confidence and trade outcome. Your gut feeling is noise, not signal.

Joe Ross Method (Model 28): Open at maximum size (5 contracts), sell 60% when profitable. Every loss hits at 100% size. Every big winner is captured at only 40%. It's the position sizing version of "cut profits short and let losses run." Structurally backwards.

Kelly Criterion (Model 30): Designed for coin flips, not trading. At 25% Kelly on a 50% win system, you'll eventually see 10-20 losses in a row, which at 25% risk is catastrophic. Kelly was built for binary outcomes (win/lose fixed amount). Trading has R-multiple distributions (variable wins and losses), which Kelly can't handle properly.

Optimal f (Model 31): Ralph Vince's method finds the fraction of your largest historical loss to bet per trade. Vince said anyone not using it "belongs on a psychiatrist's couch." Three problems: it assumes your worst loss has already happened (spectacularly dangerous), it considers only single-trade losses (not streaks), and it's unnecessarily complex. Tharp's simulation of a weak system at optimal f shows 42.5% ruin probability and negative median equity. The "optimal" fraction leads to ruin nearly half the time.

The chapter's final scorecard: 31 total models times 3 equity models = 93 position sizing approaches. Nine of the 31 are paths to ruin. That's 29% of all methods. Before using any position sizing approach, verify it's in the "safe" category. If it increases position size after losses (Martingale) or sounds too mathematically elegant to question (Kelly, optimal f), it's probably in the dangerous 29%.

Chapter 16: Putting It All Together

The final chapter is an interview with Dr. Chris Anderson, the electrical engineer whose work underpins much of the book. Anderson developed the Monte Carlo simulator, validated Fixed Ratio Position Sizing, and pointed Tharp toward the System Quality Number. His engineering background — radar systems for decision-making in noisy environments — maps perfectly to trading: extract signal (your edge) from noise (random variation) and make decisions accordingly.

Anderson's five elements of trading success summarize the entire book in five sentences. One: understand what you want and what you'll tolerate. Two: pick a system that matches. Three: verify the system is stable enough for the edge to work. Four: position size to reach your goals without crossing your pain threshold. Five: know when conditions change enough to stop. Most failures happen because of mismatches between these elements — wanting aggressive returns with conservative drawdown tolerance, or using a trending system in a choppy market.

The marble bag metaphor is the clearest explanation of simulation in the book. Your backtest is one draw from a bag of marbles. Maybe you drew the winning marbles first and it looked amazing. Simulation draws thousands of random orders to show you the full range of what's possible — from best case to worst case. The Coefficient of Variation (standard deviation ÷ expectancy × 100) tells you how bumpy the ride will be. A lower CV means smoother equity curves and faster recovery from drawdowns.

Running multiple systems provides three benefits: diversification (if one edge breaks, others continue), frequency (more trades = faster drawdown recovery), and the simple fact that trading frequency dramatically affects how long drawdowns last. A system trading 12 times a month might need two years to recover from a drawdown. At 50 trades a month, recovery happens in months. Each system must be independently strong and uncorrelated, with separate position sizing.

When bringing a new system online, bet tiny at first. Anderson suggests 0.6% risk initially, growing only after proven live profitability. Paper trading isn't enough — the psychology of real money changes everything. But you don't need to bet big to prove a system works. Start small, grow with demonstrated results, and only increase aggression after the data supports it.

Tharp's personal minimum: a 3:1 reward-to-risk ratio and drawdown duration under 2.5 months. If a system doesn't meet these thresholds, it doesn't get capital, period. Having clear standards like this prevents the "maybe this one will work" rationalization that leads traders to fund systems their own data says are marginal.

The book's final message is appropriately humble: these tools — SQN, R-multiples, Monte Carlo simulation, the CPR formula, the 31 models — are frameworks for making better decisions under uncertainty. They're not guarantees. Markets change, systems degrade, and your psychology remains the weakest link. But a trader who has honestly assessed their objectives, calculated their SQN, simulated their drawdowns, and chosen a position sizing method that matches their actual tolerance for pain has a structural advantage over the vast majority of market participants who trade on gut feel, indicator worship, and hope.

Determine your objectives and System Quality Numbers. Use the guidelines in this book to pick the right strategy. And remember Ed Seykota's breakdown: 60% psychology, 30% position sizing, 10% system. You just learned the 30%. The 60% is up to you.
