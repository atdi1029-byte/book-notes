Glossary

Core Concepts

R-Multiple: Any trade's profit or loss expressed as a multiple of its initial risk. A $500 profit on a trade where the stop-loss risked $100 per share is a 5R winner. Normalizes results across instruments and timeframes.

Initial Risk (R): The amount you stand to lose per unit if your stop is hit. Buy at $50 with a stop at $45 means R = $5/share. This is the fundamental unit of measurement for everything in the book.

Expectancy: The mean of your R-multiple distribution — how much you can expect to earn per dollar risked over many trades. A system with 0.50R expectancy earns fifty cents for every dollar put at risk, on average.

Expectunity: Expectancy multiplied by opportunity (number of trades). A 0.60R system trading 100 times per year produces 60R of expectunity annually.

System Quality Number (SQN): A t-score measuring whether your system's expectancy is significantly different from zero. Formula: (Expectancy / Std Dev of R) × √N. Rates from <1.0 (hard to trade) up to 7.0+ (Holy Grail).

Position Sizing: The algorithm that tells you "how much" or "how many" to trade per position. Not diversification, not risk control, not money management — specifically the quantity decision. Coined by Tharp in Trade Your Way to Financial Freedom.

Position Sizing Models

Percent Risk (Fixed Fractional): Risk a fixed percentage of total equity on each trade. At 2% risk on a $100K account with a $5 stop, you'd buy 400 shares ($2,000 / $5).

Percent Volatility: Size positions inversely to their volatility (typically ATR). High-volatility instruments get smaller positions, equalizing fluctuation exposure across the portfolio.

Fixed Ratio Position Sizing (FRPS): Developed by Ryan Jones. Increase unit size when equity grows by (current units × delta). Scales up fast initially, then decelerates. Requires careful delta selection based on simulated maximum drawdown.

Portfolio Heat: Total open risk across all positions at any given time. Generally should not exceed 20%. With correlated positions, effective heat is much higher than the sum of individual position risks.

CPR Formula: P = C / R. Cash at risk (C) = percentage of equity × account size. Risk per unit (R) = distance from entry to stop × unit size. Position size (P) = number of units to buy.

Equity Models

Core Equity: Starting equity minus initial allocations of all open positions. Most conservative — each new position is sized off a shrinking base, and equity only adjusts when positions close.

Total Equity: Cash plus current market value of all open positions. Most aggressive — rising positions immediately enable larger new positions.

Reduced Total Equity: A hybrid that adds locked-in profits from trailing stops back to core equity. Changes only when stops are moved, creating a middle ground between core and total equity.

Risk Concepts

Anti-Martingale: Increase position size when winning, decrease when losing. Every recommended model in this book is anti-Martingale. Compounds winners while shrinking during drawdowns.

Martingale: Increase position size after losses, decrease after wins. Based on the gambler's fallacy that losses make wins more likely. All four Martingale models in Chapter 15 are classified as dangerous.

Kelly Criterion: W − [(1 − W) / R], where W = win probability and R = average win/loss ratio. Designed for binary outcomes, not R-multiple distributions. Can grossly overestimate appropriate position size.

Optimal f: Ralph Vince's method of finding the fixed fraction of your largest historical loss to bet on each trade to maximize Terminal Wealth Relative. Assumes worst loss has already occurred — Tharp considers this a fatal flaw.

Peak-to-Trough Drawdown: Maximum decline from highest equity peak to lowest subsequent trough before a new high. Money managers carry the label of their worst drawdown for their entire career.

Chapter 1: The Golden Rules of Trading

Van Tharp's trading education began in 1962 with a single stock purchase that taught him everything about what not to do. He bought Poloron, a mobile home manufacturer, at $8 per share after spotting it on a Fortune magazine top-growth list. The stock climbed beautifully to $20, and young Tharp watched his paper profits pile up with no plan for what to do next. When the price began falling, he made every classic mistake in sequence: he had no stop-loss, so there was no predetermined exit point. He had no trailing stop, which would have locked in a 3.5R profit from the $20 peak. He had put his entire $800 into the trade, violating any reasonable position sizing rule. And when the stock dropped, he added to the losing position rather than cutting it. Five mistakes on one trade, and each one would become the basis for a golden rule.

The first four golden rules are the old trading maxim restated in precise, measurable language. Rule one: never open a position without knowing your initial risk — the amount you lose per unit if wrong. In the Poloron case, R was $2 per share. Rule two: define all profits and losses as R-multiples. A $14 gain on $2 risk is a 7R winner. Rule three: limit losses to 1R or less, because moving stops against you creates 4R catastrophes that destroy system expectancy. Rule four: make sure your average profit is bigger than 1R. One 10R win plus nine 1R losses still nets a profit. These four rules encode the entire logic of profitable trading into a framework anyone can follow.

"Cut your losses short and let your profits run."— The Golden Rule summary of rules 1–4

Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky won the 2002 Nobel Prize partly for demonstrating that human psychology naturally produces the exact opposite of these four rules. Their Prospect Theory shows that people become more risk-tolerant when losing — holding losers far past their stops to avoid the pain of admitting failure — and less risk-tolerant when winning, cutting profits short to lock in the pleasure of being right. A trader with a $50 stock and a $45 stop watches it drop to $45, doesn't sell, sees it fall to $35, and absorbs a 3R loss that should have been 1R. On the profit side, that same trader with a trailing stop at $49.50 on a stock at $55 sells for thirty cents of "profit" to feel right, missing a subsequent rally to $75. The golden rules are simple. The psychology that fights them is not.

Rules five through ten build the quantitative framework. Rule five says to understand your system as an R-multiple distribution whose mean is your expectancy and whose standard deviation measures variability. Rule six demands that you design core trading objectives — stated as profit goals or runoff points — before touching position sizing. Rule seven is the question Ed Seykota calls the most important in all of trading: "How much should I invest?" Seykota's breakdown of trading success — 60% psychology, 30% position sizing, and only 10% the system itself — became the organizing principle of Tharp's entire career.

Rule eight introduces the System Quality Number, telling traders to calculate their SQN and simulate 100 or more random R-multiple samples to evaluate their position size under realistic conditions. Rule nine addresses macro factors — knowing what kind of market environment you're in and having a way to exploit or hedge it. Rule ten ties everything together: master all ten tasks of trading. These rules aren't aspirational. They're the minimum viable framework for any trader who wants to survive long enough for the math to work in their favor.

The trailing stop concept deserves special emphasis because it's where most traders fail. A 25% trailing stop on a stock that runs from $8 to $20 sets the exit at $15 — locking in $7 of profit on $2 of risk, a 3.5R winner. Without the stop, a trader watches the stock retreat from $20 to $12 to $8 and ends with nothing. With a stop that gets moved against you (the opposite of trailing), a planned 1R loss becomes a 3R or 4R disaster. The golden rules say: define your stop (rule 1), let it trail upward (rule 2), never move it against you (rule 3). Three rules that produce 3.5R winners instead of 0R or −3R losers.

The Poloron story works as a teaching device because every mistake maps to a specific rule. No initial risk parameter violates rule one. No trailing stop violates rule two. Risking the entire $800 account violates rule seven. Adding to a loser violates rule three. Having no plan, no rules, and no discipline violates rules six and ten. Tharp could have started the book with theory, but he chose to start with a failure story from his own life — because the rules only make sense when you understand the specific ways their absence destroys trading accounts.

The chapter also introduces the concept that will thread through the rest of the book: the mean of your R-multiple distribution is your expectancy, and expectancy per dollar risked is the only honest measure of a trading system's quality. A system with a positive expectancy will make money over time if position sizing allows you to survive the inevitable drawdowns. A system with negative expectancy will lose money no matter how clever the position sizing. Everything that follows in the next 380 pages is built on this foundation.

Chapter 2: Risk (R) and R-Multiples

Risk, as Tharp defines it, is not volatility, not beta, not the VIX. Risk is the specific, controllable amount of money you lose per unit if your trade is wrong. Buy a stock at $50 with a stop at $40 and your risk is $10 per share — that is 1R. Buy 100 shares and your total risk is $1,000. This simplicity is the point. A soybean futures contract at $5.20 per bushel with a ten-cent stop and a 5,000-bushel contract has R = $500 per contract. A stock bought at $8 with a $6 stop that climbs to $28 before you sell has produced a 10R winner. Every trade, every instrument, every timeframe can be reduced to this single unit of measurement, and that reduction is what makes systematic position sizing possible.

The power of R-multiples becomes clear through worked examples. Consider a $50 stock with a stop at $40: R = $10 per share. If you buy 100 shares, your total risk is $1,000 and the position costs $5,000 — but only the $1,000 is actually at risk. If the stock rallies to $70, you've made $20 per share on $10 of risk, a 2R winner. If a 25% trailing stop kicks in at $52.50 from a $70 peak, you capture a 1.25R gain. The R-multiple strips away the noise of price levels, position sizes, and dollar amounts to reveal what actually happened: how much did you make or lose relative to what you risked? That ratio is all that matters for evaluating whether a trade was good or bad.

The R-multiple framework exposes a critical error in the traditional expectancy formula. The textbook version — average profit times win probability minus average loss times loss probability — gives you an average profit in dollar terms, but tells you nothing about profit per dollar risked. Tharp corrects this by dividing the result by average risk, yielding expectancy in R-units. Using his sample trades from Table 2-1, the system shows an expectancy of 0.966R with a standard deviation of 3.14. That 0.966R means you can expect to earn about ninety-seven cents for every dollar you risk, on average, over many trades.

"Your expectancy is the average of the R-multiples (both positive and negative) of your system."— Van Tharp

The standard deviation of 3.14 tells you that individual trades will vary wildly around that average — and that variability is where position sizing either saves you or destroys you. A system with 0.96R expectancy and 3.14 standard deviation will produce individual trades ranging from large losses to enormous gains, and the sequence in which those trades arrive determines whether your account survives long enough to realize the positive expectancy. Two systems can have identical expectancy but wildly different standard deviations, and the one with lower variability will be far easier to trade psychologically and far safer to size aggressively.

The total risk concept links R-multiples to position sizing through a simple relationship. If your system risks $10 per share and you buy 100 shares, your total risk is $1,000 or 1R for the position. All your profits and losses for that trade are then expressed as multiples of that $1,000. A $3,000 profit is 3R. A $500 loss (if your stop was tighter than expected or you got partial fills) is 0.5R. This normalization means you can compare a $10 stock trade, a soybean futures contract, and a forex position on equal footing — all in units of R.

A 25% trailing stop on a stock that runs from $8 to $20 sets the stop at $15, locking in a $7 profit on $2 of initial risk — a 3.5R gain. Without the trailing stop, you watch profits evaporate. With a rigid stop that gets moved against you, a planned 1R loss becomes a 4R catastrophe that requires multiple winners just to recover. The math is unforgiving: if your average loss balloons from 1R to 2R because you keep moving stops, a system with 0.96R expectancy drops to approximately zero expectancy, and all the position sizing in the world cannot save a system with no edge.

The variability dimension is what separates this chapter from a basic lesson on stop-losses. Everyone knows to cut losses; the insight here is that the distribution of your R-multiples — not just the average — determines what position sizing strategies are available to you. A system with 2R expectancy but 15R standard deviation is mathematically superb but practically untradeable for most people, because the journey from starting capital to long-run profit will pass through drawdowns that feel like ruin. The standard deviation, not just the expectancy, determines your reality.

The entire system of position sizing taught in this book rests on the assumption that you can define R before entering, that you will honor your stops, and that you will let your winners run as multiples of that initial risk. If you cannot determine initial risk before entering — common in options, some forex strategies, and illiquid markets — Tharp's framework still applies, but you must impose an artificial worst-case stop to create an R value. Without a defined R, position sizing is impossible, and without position sizing, trading outcomes are determined by luck rather than by any mathematical edge.

Chapter 3: Evaluating the Quality of Your Trading System

Tharp builds his argument for the System Quality Number through a brilliant pedagogical sequence. He presents six sample trading systems and asks the reader to rank them. System 3-3 has a 90% win rate, and most people pick it immediately. System 3-5 wins only 10% of the time and looks terrible at first glance. The reveal is devastating: System 3-3 has a negative expectancy of −0.10R — it loses money over time despite winning nine out of ten trades, because its rare 10R loser wipes out all those small winners. System 3-5, with its lonely 10% win rate, has the highest expectancy of all at 4.10R, because its rare winners are 50R monsters that dwarf the steady stream of 1R losses.

"If you picked System 3-3 as your favorite, then you picked a system that will lose money in the long run." — Van Tharp

The six systems span the full spectrum of trading profiles. System 3-1 wins only 20% of the time but produces big 10R winners against 1R losses, a classic trend-following profile. System 3-2 is the steady grinder: 50% win rate with modest 1.3R wins against 1R losses, generating 75 trades per month with low variance. System 3-4 mimics a real-world trend follower with 17.6% wins and a nasty mix of 5R losses alongside 25R winners across 12 monthly trades. System 3-6 is the wild card: 33.3% wins with both large losses (10R) and large gains (30R) creating enormous variability across 35 monthly trades. Each system teaches something different about the relationship between win rate, payoff ratio, variance, and practical tradeability.

Win rate is the most naive ranking method, but expectancy alone isn't much better. Tharp calls it "one of the most naive ways to evaluate systems" because it ignores both frequency and variability. Expectunity — expectancy times number of trades — is better still, producing rankings like 3-5 at 61.5R per month and 3-3 at −6.0R per month. But even expectunity misses the critical dimension of drawdown. When Tharp runs 10,000 Monte Carlo simulations of 100 trades for each system, System 3-6 (second-best expectancy at 1.04R) shows an average drawdown of 53.4R and a maximum drawdown of 199R — near-ruinous territory. System 3-2, with the lowest positive expectancy at 0.15R, has an average drawdown of only 8.7R. High-variance, high-expectancy systems produce catastrophic drawdowns that force traders to quit before the math works out.

The System Quality Number resolves this by incorporating all three dimensions: expectancy, variability, and sample size. The formula — expectancy divided by standard deviation, multiplied by the square root of the number of trades — is mathematically equivalent to a t-score testing whether expectancy is significantly different from zero. Using trades-per-month as N, the ranking flips completely: System 3-2 (0.15R expectancy, the lowest positive system) ranks first at SQN 1.13 because its extremely low variance makes its small edge reliable and tradeable. When normalized to 100 trades, System 3-5 jumps to the top at 2.60, reflecting the power of its enormous expectancy over a larger sample.

Tharp provides a rating scale that becomes a reference throughout the book. An SQN below 1.0 is probably untradeable. Between 1.7 and 2.0 achieves statistical significance. A score of 3.0 to 5.0 is excellent, 5.0 to 7.0 is superb with few systems achieving it, and 7.0 or above is what Tharp calls Holy Grail territory. Real-world examples include Steve Sjuggerud's True Wealth newsletter at approximately SQN 3, and Ken Long's ETF workshop systems scoring above 5. Tharp's own simple efficiency system produced an SQN of 4.08 over 23 trades, though it degraded in sideways and volatile markets — a preview of the market-type analysis that comes in Chapter 4.

A critical warning on SQN inflation: the formula includes the square root of N, so larger trade counts mechanically inflate the score. A system with 198 trades, mean R of 0.39, and standard deviation of 1.06 shows SQN 5.19 — "Superb." But normalized to 100 trades, the score drops to 3.68 — merely "Good." Tharp's rule: always cap N at 100 for apples-to-apples comparisons. With small samples, the bar is higher: 10 trades requires SQN above 3.50, and 20 trades requires above 3.00, just to have confidence the edge is real and not an artifact of randomness.

The chapter introduces seven model systems (SQN1 through SQN7) that serve as reference points for every position sizing test in the rest of the book. They range from SQN1 (expectancy 0.76R, standard deviation 7.54, 22% win rate — a classic trend-following profile with few big winners and many small losses) to SQN7 (expectancy 3.42R, 90% win rate, SQN 6.99 — near-Holy Grail). Between these extremes, every position sizing model is tested, and the results consistently show that system quality determines what position sizing strategies are even viable.

One counterintuitive finding deserves attention: adding very large R-multiple winners actually hurts your SQN. Adding two 30R winners to a good system reduced its SQN by 20% because the standard deviation ballooned from approximately 4.5 to 5.99. The huge winners pull the distribution wider, making the edge less reliable even though the average return increased. To improve SQN, add consistent small winners rather than lottery-ticket trades. This has profound implications for system design — the steadiest edge, not the flashiest, produces the most tradeable system. A system that grinds out 1.5R winners at a 65% clip will always be easier to position size than one that produces occasional 50R home runs mixed with long losing streaks.

Chapter 4: What Can I Expect in the Future?

Backtesting is a necessary evil and a dangerous seduction. Optimizing parameters until the equity curve looks smooth is what Tharp calls a "prescription for disaster" — even twenty years of historical data is just one sample path from a universe of possibilities. The solution is Monte Carlo simulation, which takes your R-multiple distribution and randomly reorders the trades thousands of times to generate a probability distribution of outcomes. Instead of asking "what happened?" the simulation asks "what could happen?" — and the difference between those two questions is the difference between overconfidence and preparedness.

Running Monte Carlo simulation on a sample system of 130 trades produces sobering numbers. The average peak-to-trough drawdown is 30.5R, the maximum observed drawdown reaches 162R, the average streak of consecutive losses is 6 with a maximum of 15, and the probability of breaking even or better is 97.6%. The 95th percentile drawdown duration is 9.6 months, and the average yearly gain is 96R with a gain-to-drawdown ratio of 3.1. A single backtest shows you one path; the simulation shows you the full terrain — including the valleys you'll have to cross. Any trader who sizes their positions based on the backtest's best path is guaranteed to be underprepared for the simulation's worst.

Before trading any backtested system, Tharp poses six essential questions. Is my sample representative of what I can expect going forward? Is the system valid, or did I overfit parameters? What drawdowns and returns can I realistically expect? What market types does it work in? Am I assuming one trade at a time when I'll actually run correlated portfolios? And how should I position size given my specific objectives? Most traders skip directly from backtesting to live trading, answering none of these questions. The simulation forces honest answers to at least questions three and four.

Perhaps the most valuable concept in this chapter is market-type analysis. Tharp defines six market types along two dimensions: direction (up, sideways, down) and volatility (quiet, volatile). A system with an overall SQN of 1.94 — looking mediocre — might score above 4.0 in quiet trending markets and −1.13 in sideways volatile markets. That system isn't mediocre; it's excellent with a filter. The market-type breakdown for one sample system shows SQN 4.32 in up-quiet conditions, 4.45 in down-quiet, and −1.13 in sideways-volatile — a system that's superb in trending environments and destructive in choppy ones.

The seven model systems (SQN1 through SQN7) are introduced here and serve as reference points for every position sizing test in the remainder of the book. They range from SQN1 (0.76R expectancy, 7.54 standard deviation, 22% win rate) to SQN7 (3.42R expectancy, 4.89 standard deviation, 90% win rate, SQN 6.99). The range is deliberate: by testing every position sizing model against systems spanning the full quality spectrum, Tharp can show exactly when each model works, when it fails, and why. A poor system has almost no safe position sizing options; a superb system makes almost any approach viable.

Using S&P 500 data from 1995 to 2005, Tharp shows that secular bull markets were up 70% of the time with mostly quiet conditions, while the post-2000 secular bear was down or sideways 67% of the time. Down periods were almost always volatile; up periods were mostly quiet. The mean weekly price range was 3.3% with a standard deviation of 1.85%. A trader who knows which market type suits their system can sit on the sidelines during hostile environments rather than grinding through drawdowns. The implication: you need at least 30 trades from each of the six market types — a minimum of 180 trades total — for a truly representative sample of system performance.

Correlation risk lurks beneath the surface of any multi-position portfolio. Twenty simultaneous positions may appear diversified but move together during a crash — Steve Sjuggerud captures this with "all ships fall with the tide." Price shock risk compounds the problem: October 1987 saw the S&P drop 20% in a single day, and September 11, 2001 produced a 12% or greater weekly decline. Leveraged positions can be completely wiped out in these events regardless of stops, because the market gaps past your exit level. No simulation can fully capture tail risk, but knowing it exists should make any trader more conservative in their position sizing than the simulation alone suggests.

"Congratulations! You are now thinking in terms of probabilities and statistics." — Van Tharp

The chapter's summary crystallizes the entire first section of the book into six steps. Every trade needs a predetermined worst-case exit. Trading results are a set of R-multiples. The system is an R-multiple distribution with mean (expectancy) and standard deviation (variability). Evaluate with SQN. Understand performance across all six market types. And simulate to forecast drawdowns, losing streaks, and gain ranges. A trader who has completed these six steps knows more about their system than 95% of market participants know about theirs — and they haven't even started position sizing yet. That comes in Part II.

Chapter 5: Are You Doomed to Failure?

Before getting to the mechanics of position sizing, Tharp devotes an entire chapter to the psychological biases that prevent traders from implementing what they learn. The information overload is staggering: French economist George Anderla calculated that the total stock of human knowledge doubled in the 1,500 years from Jesus to Da Vinci, then again by 1750, again by 1900, again by 1960, and now doubles in under a year. The conscious mind processes only seven (plus or minus two) chunks of information at a time. To cope, the brain generalizes, deletes, and distorts — survival mechanisms in daily life that become fatal errors in trading.

"Money is made through the personal application of these principles. If you try to project what you learn outside of yourself onto the market, you will not be able to apply any of the principles taught in this book." — Van Tharp

The Locus of Control bias makes traders believe the key to success is picking the right stock. About 30% of all stock market books have "picking" in the title. Lottery players believe choosing their own numbers gives them an edge — the same illusion drives stock selection obsession. Mutual funds stay 95% or more invested because their mandate is defined as picking, not managing risk. The remedy is recognizing that you control your exits, not the market's direction. Stops cap losses at 1R while trailing stops let profits run to multiples of R. The entry is the least important part of any trade.

The Need to Be Right bias runs even deeper. Education trains us to equate being wrong with failure — a 70% score passes, 94% earns an A. In trading, a 40% win rate can be wildly profitable if average winners are large enough. But the psychological pain of being "wrong" on six out of ten trades leads to the classic destructive pattern: holding losers beyond stops because selling would confirm being wrong, and cutting winners short because taking a small profit feels like being "right." A trader buys a stock at $50 with a stop at $45. It drops to $45, and instead of selling, they hold. It falls to $35 — a 3R loss on what should have been 1R. On the winning side, they have a trailing stop at $49.50 on a stock at $55 and sell for thirty cents of "profit," missing a rally to $75. The need to be right on individual trades systematically destroys the R-multiple distribution.

The Percent Gain bias distorts how people evaluate newsletters and stock tips. A headline screaming "XYZ went up 150%" makes you visualize your entire portfolio gaining 150%. In reality, if you risked 1% of your equity on that trade with a 25% trailing stop, a move from $10 to $25 with a stop at $18.75 produces a 3.5R profit — a 3.5% gain on equity, not 150%. Tharp shows a newsletter example with ten recommendations: three winners (6R, 2R, and 8R) and seven losers averaging 2R. The net result is negative 1R, but the advisor markets only the winners. The solution is demanding all claims be translated into R-multiples and asking for the complete track record, not the highlight reel.

The Prediction and Understanding bias convinces traders they must understand why markets move in order to trade profitably. Elliott Wave Theory is cited as an extreme case of manipulating price bars to manufacture explanatory theories. At a behavioral finance conference in Frankfurt in 1997, Tharp offered his counter-thesis: "People don't make money by predicting markets. They make money by cutting losses short and letting profits run and by using proper position sizing." You don't need to understand the market. You need to understand your concept: a trend follower needs to know that big trends occur occasionally and that catching them is worth enduring many small losses. A value investor needs to know when something stops being undervalued. Neither requires a theory of why the market does what it does.

The Law of Small Numbers lets traders find six chart patterns that worked and declare they have a system. Out of 300 consolidation periods, 7,124 or more occurrences produced nothing — the signal worked roughly 3% of the time, but the six successes created a vivid mental model of a reliable pattern. Even a genuinely valid pattern still needs a worst-case stop, an exit plan, and position sizing before it's a tradeable system. Belief Perseverance compounds the damage: once convinced a pattern works, traders actively avoid disconfirming evidence. Arbitrageurs who made fortunes continue trading broken strategies until they lose everything, because acknowledging the strategy failed feels like admitting a personal failure.

Repetition bias creates conviction through frequency of exposure. "Stock picking is important" appears across CNBC, Bloomberg, Fox Business, and 158 or more Amazon books on the subject. Authority bias defers to famous investors, spawning titles like How to Pick Stocks Like Warren Buffett by Timothy Vick and Pick Stocks Like Warren Buffett by Warren Boroson — neither written by Buffett himself. The Wanting Lots of Facts bias affects roughly 75% of people who have a sensory/detail orientation. They demand data before acting, making them ideal targets for Holy Grail indicator salesmen who show fifty cherry-picked chart examples and sell $3,000 software. The victims never see failure examples, don't know the exit rules, and don't understand that position sizing drives results far more than any indicator ever could.

Each bias is a specific, identifiable reason why traders fail to implement the position sizing rules that follow. The chapter concludes with Representation bias — the reminder that a price chart represents the market but is not the market, and that every transformation layered on top (trendlines, moving averages, Fibonacci levels) moves further from reality. Recognizing these biases won't eliminate them — they're hardwired into human cognition — but naming them makes them observable, and observable biases can be managed through systematic rules that override emotional impulses.

Chapter 6: The Most Important Factor (Besides You)

The most devastating experiment in the book comes from Ralph Vince, who gave forty PhDs a computer game with a 60% win rate, fixed 1R wins and losses, and $10,000 in starting capital over 100 trials. This is a game that beats any Las Vegas casino. The result: only two of the forty made money. Ninety-five percent of highly educated people lost money in a game mathematically guaranteed to produce profits, and the sole variable was position sizing. The experiment didn't test intelligence or market knowledge — it tested whether smart people could manage the simple question of how much to bet. They couldn't.

The typical failure path follows the gambler's fallacy with mathematical precision. Start betting $1,000 per trial. Lose three in a row — equity drops to $7,000. Convince yourself you're "due" for a win and bet $3,000. Lose again — equity drops to $4,000. Now you need a 150% gain just to get back to breakeven. The probability of four consecutive losses in a 60/40 game is only 2.56% per any given sequence of four trials, but over 100 trials it's virtually certain to occur at least once. The Kelly Criterion says the optimal bet in this exact game is 20% of equity, yet even at that mathematically "optimal" level, the ride is psychologically brutal. In real trading with more uncertainty, the practical maximum is 3-4%.

"A low-risk idea is an idea with a long-term positive expectancy that's traded at a risk level to allow for the worst possible occurrence in the short term so that you are able to realize the long-term positive expectancy." — Van Tharp

Position sizing is the mechanism that converts a positive-expectancy system into a low-risk idea. The recovery asymmetry makes this urgent: a 20% drawdown requires a 25% gain to recover, 40% requires 66.7%, 50% requires 100%, and 75% requires a staggering 300%. At 90% drawdown, you need a 900% gain — statistically improbable for virtually any system. When you risk too much and suffer a large loss, compounding switches from ally to enemy, and the hole becomes mathematically inescapable. This asymmetry is the deepest reason why conservative position sizing outperforms aggressive sizing over the long run: avoiding large drawdowns is more valuable than capturing large gains.

The Market Wizards are unanimous on this point, and Tharp quotes them extensively. Bruce Kovner: "Undertrade, undertrade, undertrade is my second piece of advice. Whatever you think your position ought to be, cut it at least in half." Larry Hite: "Never risk more than 1% of your total equity in any one trade. By risking 1%, I am indifferent to any individual trade. Keeping your risk small and constant is absolutely critical." Richard Dennis: "Minimize your losses and try to preserve capital for those very few instances where you can make a lot in a very short period of time." These are not theoretical positions — these are the rules that produced hundreds of millions in trading profits.

The Secrets of the Masters trading game drives the psychological point home. A tester who knew the exact odds started with $10,000 and built his account to $1,045,960 on 23 consecutive winners — odds of about eight in a million. On trade 24, he risked $1,000,000 of his $1,045,960 balance. It was his first loss. He ended approximately one million dollars in debt despite winning 28 out of 31 trades. A long winning streak doesn't change the underlying probabilities; it changes your perception of them, which is far more dangerous.

The losing streak probabilities are sobering even for good systems. In a 60% win system over 100 trades, there is a 100% probability of encountering at least a four-loss streak. The average longest streak is five, and maximum observed streaks reach fourteen. For a 50% win system, the average longest streak is six with maximum observed streaks of nineteen. These aren't edge cases — they're statistical certainties that will occur in the normal course of trading. Any position sizing strategy that can't survive the longest expected losing streak is guaranteed to fail, regardless of the system's long-run expectancy.

The connection between account size and position sizing viability adds a practical dimension. A $5,000 account risking the maximum practical 3-4% has only $150-200 per trade — effectively unable to trade most instruments. The optimal bet size in a simple 60/40 game is 20% (Kelly Criterion), but in real trading with its uncertainty and variable payoffs, even 3-4% is aggressive. Small accounts face a structural disadvantage: position sizing constraints limit them to one or two positions at a time, eliminating diversification benefits entirely.

Tharp defines position sizing formally as "that portion of your trading system that tells you 'how many' or 'how much.' How many units of your investment should you put on at a given time? How much risk should you be willing to take?" He considers it, aside from personal psychological issues, the most critical concept any trader needs to tackle. The definition is deliberately narrow: position sizing is not diversification, not hedging, not asset allocation. It is specifically and only the quantity decision — how many shares, how many contracts, how many units. Everything else in risk management flows from getting this single decision right.

Chapter 7: CPR for Traders and Investors

The marble game experiment strips away every variable except position sizing and psychology. Three hundred people receive the same sequence of R-multiple draws from System 3-1. Same entries, same exits, same trade outcomes in the same order. Starting from $100,000, final equities ranged from zero to over one million dollars. The only differences were how much each person risked per trade and the psychological decisions that drove those choices. This isn't a theoretical exercise; it's a direct demonstration that the "how much" decision dominates all others.

The academic confirmation comes from Brinson, Singer, and Beebower's landmark 1991 study in the Financial Analysts Journal: examining 82 portfolio managers over ten years, they found that more than 91% of performance variance was attributable to asset allocation — the institutional version of "how much." The marble game proves the same thing at the individual trader level. Three hundred people, same trades, same sequence — and the outcomes spread from zero to a million. If that doesn't convince a trader that position sizing matters more than entries, nothing will.

Three components interact to determine position sizing outcomes. First, the trader's objectives: "not going broke" produces completely different behavior from "win at any cost." Second, psychology: beliefs and emotions determine which objectives actually drive behavior under pressure. Third, the position sizing method itself — the specific algorithm chosen from the 30-plus models and 90-plus combinations with equity models available, plus scaling in and out. The interaction of these three components explains why two traders using the same model on the same system can produce vastly different results. The method is the same; the psychology and objectives are different.

The CPR formula is Tharp's universal position sizing equation: P = C / R. Cash at risk (C) equals your chosen risk percentage times your account size. Risk per unit (R) equals the distance from entry to stop times the contract or share size. Position size (P) is simply C divided by R. The formula is elegant in its simplicity and universal in its application. It works identically whether you're trading stocks, futures, forex, or options, and its three variables make it impossible to hide from the risk implications of any trade.

The worked examples make the formula concrete. A $30,000 account risking 2% on a stock at $50 with a $5 stop: C = $600, R = $5, P = 120 shares. The total position costs $6,000 but only $600 is actually at risk. For futures, a soybean contract with $500 in account risk and a $1,000-per-contract stop yields P = 0.5 — you cannot even enter the trade, and the formula tells you so before you lose money discovering it. A forex trade on a $200,000 account at 2% risk with a 78-pip stop on a $100,000 contract: C = $4,000, R = $780, P = 5 contracts. In each case, the formula translates abstract risk tolerance into a specific number of units to trade.

Three equity models determine what number goes into the "account size" variable. Core Equity is the most conservative: starting equity minus the initial allocation of all open positions. Each new trade is sized off a shrinking base that only resets when positions close. If you start with $50,000 and allocate 10% per trade, your first position is sized on $50,000 (allocating $5,000), your second on $45,000 (allocating $4,500), and your third on $40,500. Core equity is associated with the Market's Money concept: risk minimal original capital, then risk profits more aggressively once positions are profitable.

Total Equity is the most aggressive: cash plus the current market value of all open positions. Rising positions immediately enable larger new positions, creating a feedback loop that accelerates gains during winning streaks but also accelerates losses during drawdowns. Tom Basso used this model to maintain constant risk as a percentage of true portfolio value. Reduced Total Equity splits the difference, adding locked-in profits from trailing stops back to core equity. If you've moved your stop to lock in $11,000 of profit on a position, that $11,000 enters your equity calculation even though the position is still open. The risk ranking from most to least conservative is core, reduced total, then total equity. The book uses total equity as its default.

The choice of equity model interacts with the position sizing model to create the actual risk profile. A 2% risk model on core equity is significantly more conservative than the same 2% on total equity, because core equity shrinks with each open position while total equity grows with profitable ones. This interaction produces the book's taxonomy: three equity models times 31 position sizing models equals 93 different approaches, plus infinite variations from scaling in and out. The taxonomy sounds overwhelming, but the CPR formula remains constant through all of them — only the inputs change.

Chapter 8: Core Position Sizing Models

All five core models are tested against the same baseline system: Donchian's 55-day channel breakout with a 21-day trailing stop, run across ten commodities from 1981 to 1991 with $1,000,000 starting equity. This constant backdrop isolates the pure effect of position sizing choices. By holding the system constant and varying only the sizing method, Tharp eliminates every confounding variable and lets the models speak through their results alone.

Model 1, Units per Fixed Amount of Money, is the most common default — one futures contract per $50,000 of equity. Its advantage is simplicity: it never rejects a trade for being too risky. Its fatal disadvantage is that all contracts are not alike. A Treasury bond futures contract has $116,000 in notional value and a daily range of roughly $2,325, while a corn contract has only $12,000 notional and $550 per day. Trading one of each creates an 85/15 portfolio imbalance that the model cannot see. The results expose the danger: at $100,000 per contract, the system earns $5 million with a 36.9% maximum drawdown. At $20,000 per contract, it loses $1.7 million with a 112% drawdown and 402 rejected trades due to insufficient margin. To keep drawdowns below 50%, you need at least $70,000 per contract.

Model 2, Equal Units / Equal Leverage, divides capital into five to ten equal slices — a step up from Model 1 but still blind to volatility differences between instruments. It produces approximately equal dollar exposure but equal dollars don't mean equal risk when one market moves four times as much per day as another. Model 3, Percent Margin, sizes by exchange margin requirements — limiting margin allocation to a percentage of equity per trade. The problem is that margin requirements fluctuate daily, are set arbitrarily by exchanges for regulatory purposes, and bear no systematic relationship to actual position risk. Using margin as a proxy for risk is like using the speed limit as a proxy for road conditions.

Model 4, Percent Volatility, is the first genuinely sophisticated approach. It equalizes market fluctuation exposure by sizing inversely to volatility. If a contract's daily range is $3 and your equity allows $1,000 of daily fluctuation, you take 3.3 contracts. The typical limit is 10% total portfolio volatility allocation. At 2% volatility allocation, the 55/21 breakout system generates a staggering $236 million in gains — but with drawdowns reaching 69-86% annually. The volatility model produces enormous returns precisely because it sizes aggressively into trending markets where breakouts occur, but the drawdowns require extraordinary psychological resilience and the willingness to watch equity drop by two-thirds without abandoning the system.

Model 5, Percent Risk (Fixed Fractional Position Sizing), is the workhorse of professional trading. Instead of sizing by volatility, it sizes by the distance to your stop — the actual risk of the trade. At 2.5% risk on $50,000 equity with a $1,000 stop per contract, you trade 1.25 contracts (rounded down to one). The calculation is a direct application of CPR: C = $1,250 (2.5% of $50,000), R = $1,000, P = 1.25, rounded to 1. At 5% risk the system generates $5.6 million, while the percent volatility model at 2% produces $236 million. Percent risk is far more conservative but far safer.

This is the central trade-off of position sizing, and it cannot be escaped. The model that makes the most money also demands the highest tolerance for drawdowns. The model that keeps you sleeping at night also leaves the most profit on the table. There is no free lunch — every dollar of additional expected return comes with a corresponding increase in drawdown risk. Each trader must find their own point on this continuum based on their personal objectives, their system's SQN, and their honest assessment of how much pain they can endure without abandoning the system.

The five core models form a progression from naive to sophisticated. Model 1 ignores everything except equity. Model 2 adds equal weighting. Model 3 uses a poor external proxy. Model 4 sizes by market behavior (volatility). Model 5 sizes by actual risk (distance to stop). Each step adds information and produces better risk-adjusted outcomes. Model 5 is where most professional systematic traders eventually settle, and the remaining chapters expand the toolkit with more specialized approaches built on this foundation.

The results table for Model 1 alone demonstrates the stakes. At $100,000 per contract: $5 million profit, 18.2% annual gain, 36.9% drawdown. At $50,000: $19.3 million, 32.3% gain, 61% drawdown. At $40,000: $27.5 million, 36.5% gain, 69.7% drawdown. At $30,000: $30.9 million, 38% gain, 80.5% drawdown. At $20,000: negative $1.7 million, 112% drawdown, 402 rejected trades. The curve is nonlinear — returns increase modestly as you size up, but drawdowns explode. The model breaks completely at $20,000 per contract, producing margin calls and ruin. The table is a miniature version of the book's central argument: more risk doesn't produce proportionally more return, but it does produce proportionally more drawdown.

Chapter 9: More Position Sizing Models

Beyond the five core models, Tharp catalogs seven additional approaches for specific situations and institutional constraints. These aren't improvements on the core models — they're tools for problems the core models don't address, like correlated positions, institutional mandates, and traders who don't control their own capital. Each solves a real problem that practitioners encounter, and several can be layered on top of the core models rather than replacing them.

Group Control (Model 6) limits exposure to any single sector or correlated group. During the 2008 financial crisis, holding five different bank stocks felt diversified until all five dropped 80% together. Group control caps total risk within any correlated cluster — energy stocks, grain futures, tech companies — so that a sector-wide move can't wipe out the portfolio. Portfolio Heat (Model 7) extends this to the entire portfolio, tracking total open risk across all positions with a typical cap of 20%. It's the aggregate version of individual percent risk, and Tharp considers it one of the most important monitoring metrics available. If you have ten positions each at 2% risk but all are correlated, your effective portfolio heat is closer to 20% than the 2% your individual sizing suggests.

Long versus Short (Model 8) position sizing applies different risk parameters to long and short trades, recognizing that short positions have theoretically unlimited risk and different behavioral dynamics. A trader might risk 2% on longs but only 1.5% on shorts to account for the upward drift of equity markets and the potential for infinite loss on a short that gaps higher. Equity Crossover (Model 9) reduces or eliminates trading when the equity curve drops below its own moving average — a meta-system that treats your account equity as a price chart. If your equity curve is trending down, you're either in a hostile market type or your system has degraded, and either way reducing position size is prudent.

Asset Allocation to Determine Position Sizing (Model 10) works for investors who want their position sizing to flow from their asset allocation framework rather than the other way around. Instead of sizing each trade individually, you determine what percentage of the portfolio belongs in each asset class and then size positions to fill that allocation. Position Sizing for Portfolio Managers (Model 11) handles the constraint that most managers must remain 95% or more invested with no discretion to go to cash. Their clients hired them to be invested, not to time the market, so the manager's job is to select the best positions within their mandate and size them relative to each other rather than relative to a cash alternative.

Model 12 addresses Professional Traders Who Don't Know How Much Equity They Have — bank and prop traders who trade the firm's capital without visibility into the total pool. The solution is elegant: ask one simple question. How much could you lose before you'd be fired? That number becomes your effective equity for sizing purposes, and you apply percent risk or percent volatility to it as if it were your own account. The Bankers Trust case study from mid-1990s Australia illustrates the institutional failure mode. They implemented this plan beautifully for their Australian traders, but when New York traders underperformed, nobody in any office got bonuses regardless of individual results. The motivation destruction was total — why manage risk carefully if your compensation depends on people you've never met? The bank was eventually acquired by Deutsche Bank, and all the training was lost.

Equity Crossover (Model 9) deserves special attention because it's the only model that treats your account equity as a tradeable instrument. When your equity curve drops below its own moving average, you reduce or eliminate trading entirely. The logic is sound: a declining equity curve means your system is either in a hostile market type or has degraded, and either way, continuing to trade at full size is throwing money at a proven problem. When the equity curve recovers above its average, you resume full position sizing. The model adds no additional risk during good times and automatically protects capital during bad times.

The Models 6 through 12 are less about finding the optimal position size and more about preventing specific failure modes. Group control prevents correlation disasters. Portfolio heat prevents aggregate overexposure. Long/short differentiation prevents asymmetric risk blindness. Equity crossover prevents trading during system degradation. Each is a guardrail rather than an optimizer, and professional traders typically use several simultaneously — applying percent risk as the primary sizing method while monitoring portfolio heat, group exposure, and equity trend as secondary constraints.

The bank trader model also highlights an underappreciated truth: most institutional money is managed by people who have no framework for the "how much" decision. Portfolio managers are required to be nearly fully invested. Bank traders don't know their effective equity. Hedge fund traders often size by gut feel or by the Kelly Criterion (which Tharp will demolish in Chapter 15). The gap between what this book teaches and what the industry practices is enormous, and that gap is both a source of frustration and a competitive advantage for anyone willing to do the work.

Chapter 10: Comparing the Impact of Various Models

The comparison test is the most striking demonstration in the entire book. Six position sizing models are applied to a system with random entries — a literal coin flip determining whether to go long or short — across twelve commodities from 1985 to 1995. Exits use a three-times-volatility trailing stop recalculated daily. The system is profitable approximately 80% of the time despite random entries, because the exit strategy captures trends while cutting losses. If nothing else sticks from this book, this experiment should: entries don't matter nearly as much as exits and position sizing.

Starting from $1,000,000 with $100 per trade in slippage and commissions, the baseline of one unit per trade produces a modest $382,853 net profit with a 4.72% maximum drawdown over 1,306 trades across eleven years. This is the control group — no position sizing at all beyond the decision to take one unit per signal. Every other model uses the same 1,306 trades, the same entry signals, the same exits. The only variable is how many units to take per trade.

Position sizing alone then causes final equity to vary from $382,000 to $640 million — a 1,700-times difference using identical entry signals and identical exits. One unit per $100,000 reaches $23.76 million with a 39.3% maximum drawdown. Percent risk at 2.5% hits $75.74 million with a 44.92% drawdown. Tom Basso's scale-out rules produce $78.65 million with a 44.3% drawdown. Percent volatility at 2.5% reaches $154 million with a 77.8% drawdown. And percent risk with William Eckhardt's scaling-in method peaks at $640 million with an 82.2% maximum drawdown.

The Eckhardt scaling method, used by the Turtles, starts at 2.5% risk and adds positions equal to half the original size when the initial risk has been reduced by 50%, up to five times, creating a maximum position 350% of the original — capped at 50% portfolio heat. Of thirty scale-out variants tested, twenty-seven improved performance over the base model. The 2.5% percent risk model's psychological challenge is worth noting: between February and May 1989, equity dropped from $15.15 million to $7.9 million. Most traders would have quit during that decline, missing the majority of the eventual $75.74 million in total gains. The model works mathematically but only if the trader can survive the drawdowns psychologically.

"Perhaps you can now begin to understand why 90% of your system results are really due to position sizing." — Van Tharp

The critical lesson isn't that one model is inherently better. It's that every model represents a specific point on the return-versus-drawdown continuum, and the choice must be driven by the trader's objectives, not by chasing maximum returns. If you cannot tolerate drawdowns exceeding 25%, you would have lost money on every model except the baseline one-unit-per-trade approach. The model that produces $640 million also produces an 82% drawdown that would cause virtually any trader or investor to quit.

The random-entry test also proves something profound about the structure of markets themselves. Trends exist. If they didn't, a coin-flip entry with a volatility-based trailing stop would lose money from transaction costs. Instead, it's profitable 80% of the time. The exit captures the trend; position sizing amplifies it. Entries are just the mechanism for getting on board — and a random mechanism works about as well as a sophisticated one, because the real value is in staying with trends (via trailing stops) and sizing positions to survive the inevitable periods when trends don't materialize.

The key lesson from the comparison isn't about any single model. It's that no model is inherently "better" — the impact magnitude is the point. If you cannot tolerate drawdowns exceeding 25%, you would have lost money on every model except the baseline one-unit-per-trade. The models that produce hundreds of millions also produce drawdowns that would cause any normal person to abandon ship. A model you abandon during a 40% drawdown produces worse results than a conservative model you stick with through a 10% drawdown. The best model is the one you can execute consistently, and only you know which one that is.

The chapter closes by pointing toward Part III, where position sizing moves from "how much impact does it have?" to "how do I use it to meet my specific objectives?" The comparison test answered the first question definitively: position sizing is the dominant variable. The remaining chapters answer the second, providing specific frameworks for translating objectives into risk percentages, choosing between models, and avoiding the dangerous approaches that sound sophisticated but lead to ruin.

Chapter 11: Meeting Your Objectives

At least 50% of system design should be spent on defining objectives, and "I want to make as much as possible" does not qualify. That statement is avoidance masquerading as ambition. Real objectives specify a target return, an acceptable drawdown, or both. With an SQN above 3.0 and appropriate position sizing, specific objectives become achievable — but you have to know what they are before you can design a sizing strategy to reach them. The purpose of position sizing, Tharp repeats throughout, is to meet your objectives. Not to maximize returns. Not to minimize risk. To meet your specific objectives.

The test system used throughout Part III has an expectancy of 0.75R, an SQN of 2.05 on 100 trades per year, and a distribution that mirrors a realistic trading system: 2% chance of a 20R winner, 3% chance of a 10R winner, 20% chance of a 2R winner, 30% chance of a 1R winner, 40% chance of a 1R loser, and 5% chance of a 5R loser. This isn't a superb system — it's solidly "good" on the SQN scale, the kind of system many competent traders actually operate. The question is what position sizing can achieve with this raw material.

The "optimal" position size turns out to be a meaningless concept when defined as the one that maximizes average ending equity. Simulating at 16% risk per trade produces the highest average ending equity of $216 million, with a maximum of $31.8 billion — but the minimum ending equity at that risk level is $32,286. A math professor explains the paradox: the largest risk percentage before guaranteed bankruptcy will always produce the highest mean equity in simulations, because rare astronomical wins (like 100 consecutive 20R trades) dominate the average while being irrelevant to any individual trader's experience. One simulation path hits $31.8 billion; 9,998 others go bankrupt. The average looks amazing. The median is devastation.

"The purpose of position sizing is to meet your objectives and once you've determined your objectives, you can design a position sizing algorithm that's optimal for doing just that." — Van Tharp

The six competing definitions of "optimal," tested on System 11-1 with 10,000 Monte Carlo runs and a goal of 300% return against a failure threshold of 25% drawdown, tell the complete story. Maximum mean return at 30% risk produces a 99.8% probability of ruin and a negative median ending equity. Maximum median return at 4.2% risk gives a 69% probability of reaching the target with 28% ruin. Greatest probability of achieving the objective comes at 2.4% risk: 79.6% success with 10.3% ruin. Less than 1% chance of ruin requires dropping to 1.0% risk, where the objective probability falls to 47.3% but the median gain is still 95.6%.

Tharp's personal preference is the "maximum spread" definition at 1.8% risk: 76.4% probability of achieving the objective with only 4.9% probability of ruin, producing a 206% median gain. The gap between the math professor's "optimal" (30% risk, 99.8% ruin) and Tharp's practical optimal (1.8% risk, 4.9% ruin) could not be wider. This table should be posted above every trader's monitor — it proves that "optimal" has no meaning without specifying optimal for what.

Changing the failure criterion reveals another critical relationship. When failure is defined as a 50% drawdown instead of 25%, optimal risk levels increase across all definitions. The "maximum spread" risk rises from 1.8% to 3.0%, and the probability of achieving the objective rises from 76.4% to 87.5%. Willingness to tolerate larger drawdowns allows more aggressive sizing and improves nearly every outcome metric. This isn't a free lunch — you're accepting more pain for more return — but it demonstrates that the drawdown tolerance you set is the single most important parameter in your position sizing decision, more important even than the model you choose.

The interaction between drawdown tolerance and system quality creates a two-dimensional decision space. A superb system (SQN 5+) with high drawdown tolerance (50%) can size aggressively and achieve ambitious objectives with high probability. A mediocre system (SQN 2) with low drawdown tolerance (25%) is constrained to conservative sizing with modest objectives. Most traders overestimate both their system quality and their drawdown tolerance, leading to position sizes that are too large for their actual situation. The cure is honesty on both dimensions, backed by simulation data rather than wishful thinking.

The chapter also examines the relationship between expectancy, win rate, and position sizing flexibility. At SQN 2.05, System 11-1 has limited flexibility — the maximum probability of reaching its objective is 79.6%, and every increase in aggression carries significant ruin risk. A system with SQN 5.0 would have dramatically more room to maneuver, achieving higher objectives at lower ruin probabilities. This is why Tharp insists that system quality comes before position sizing: the better your system, the more position sizing options are available to you, and the more likely those options are to succeed.

Chapter 12: Market's Money Methods

The core insight of the Market's Money approach is separating your starting capital from your profits and treating them with different risk parameters. You might risk 1% of your original $100,000 capital while risking 4% of accumulated profits. The psychological logic is powerful: losing profits hurts less than losing original capital, so you can be more aggressive with money you've already "won" without the psychological pain of drawdown on your core equity. The practical result is faster compounding during winning streaks while preserving the ability to restart near your original capital if a drawdown wipes out the more aggressively traded portion.

The concept is associated with the Core Equity model from Chapter 7. Start with $100,000 and risk 1% per trade on your base capital — that's $1,000 of initial risk. After accumulating $25,000 in profits, you now risk 4% of those profits ($1,000) while keeping the base risk unchanged. Your total risk per trade doubles, but half of it comes from profits rather than original capital. If a drawdown wipes out the profits, you're back to your original 1% risk level on your original capital, having lost only money that the market "gave" you. The psychological separation between "my money" and "the market's money" is not mathematically meaningful — a dollar is a dollar — but it's psychologically powerful enough to change actual trading behavior for the better.

Model 13, Using Your Optimal Target Risk Percentage, takes the optimal risk level from Chapter 11's simulations and applies it to the market's money concept. Model 14 formalizes the Market's Money Methods with five specific reset rules that define when profits get reclassified as "your money." Percentage gain resets at a fixed return target like 100% annually. Calendar date resets on December 31 or quarterly. Monetary goal resets when you hit a specific dollar target like paying off a mortgage. Trade count resets after a specified number of trades.

The mathematical formula approach is the most sophisticated of the five reset methods. It calculates a moving average of recent equity changes and allocates risk as a combination of base risk on starting equity plus a larger percentage of the running average of recent profits, capped at a maximum total risk percentage. The formula: BASE = starting equity, MM = (current equity minus equity four trades ago) divided by four. Risk allocation becomes 1% of BASE plus 10% of MM, up to 4% total. As equity grows, the formula automatically increases risk capacity, and it contracts during drawdowns without the abrupt transitions of the simpler methods.

Model 15 covers Scaling In Techniques, which are complementary to market's money methods. William Eckhardt's approach, used by the Turtles, adds to winning positions as the initial risk is reduced. When a trade moves in your favor and the distance from current price to your stop has shrunk to half the original, you add a new position equal to half the original size. This can be repeated up to five times, creating a maximum position of 350% of the original. The key insight is that you're adding to winners, not losers — the position grows only when the market confirms your thesis, and each addition is sized conservatively relative to the proven profit cushion.

The interaction between market's money and scaling produces a compound acceleration effect. As profits accumulate, base position sizes grow (market's money). As winning positions extend, additional units are added (scaling in). The combination means that your largest positions are in your best trades during your best periods — exactly when you want maximum exposure. During drawdowns, both mechanisms contract simultaneously: market's money reduces base sizes and scaling opportunities disappear because positions aren't moving favorably. This natural expansion-contraction cycle is the anti-Martingale principle in its most refined form.

The chapter distinguishes between three different philosophies of profit treatment. Some traders view all equity as a single pool and size accordingly (total equity model). Others treat profits as a separate bucket to be risked more aggressively (market's money). Still others lock in profits periodically and restart from a new base (the reset methods). Each philosophy produces different psychological and mathematical outcomes, and the choice should match the trader's relationship with risk. A trader who can tolerate large drawdowns on total equity doesn't need the psychological cushion of market's money. A trader who freezes up after losing original capital probably does.

The practical question of when to reset is as important as the method itself. Calendar resets are simple but arbitrary — markets don't respect December 31. Monetary goal resets align with real-life financial objectives but may not reflect trading conditions. The mathematical formula approach is the most adaptive, adjusting continuously based on recent performance, but it's also the most complex to implement. Tharp doesn't recommend one over another; the choice depends on whether you prioritize simplicity, alignment with life goals, or responsiveness to market conditions.

Chapter 13: Fixed Ratio Position Sizing

Fixed Ratio Position Sizing, developed by Ryan Jones, represents a fundamentally different approach to scaling. Instead of maintaining a fixed percentage of equity at risk, you increase your unit size by one when equity grows by the product of current units and a fixed increment called the delta. The scaling is rapid at first — going from one to two units requires gaining only one delta — but decelerates as position size grows, since going from ten to eleven units requires gaining ten deltas. This concave scaling curve means small accounts can increase position size very quickly, which makes FRPS attractive to traders starting with limited capital.

The chapter is the longest and most detailed in the book, testing FRPS across six systems ranging from negative expectancy to superb. The systems span the quality spectrum: System 13-1 with 0.15R expectancy and SQN 0.42, System 13-2 at 1.25R and SQN 1.72, System 13-3 with only 0.04R but an 80% win rate and SQN 0.20, System 13-4 at 1.20R and SQN 5.04, System 13-5 with negative expectancy of −0.08R but a 77% win rate, and System 13-6 at −0.07R. Delta values tested are $1,000, $2,000, and $5,000, with initial bet sizes of $500, $1,000, and $2,000. The combinatorial testing is exhaustive.

The results are stark and carry the chapter's central warning. For System 13-4 (the excellent system), the most aggressive FRPS setting produces average equity of $4.49 million versus $332,000 at a flat 0.5% risk — a massive outperformance. But for System 13-3 (positive but weak, SQN 0.20), the same aggressive setting produces 82% ruin. For System 13-5 (negative expectancy masked by a high win rate), the ruin rate is 90.4%. The $1,000 initial bet with $1,000 delta on System 13-3 hits 95.9% ruin. FRPS is a magnifying glass: it amplifies whatever the system produces, whether that's profits or losses.

Overall, approximately 40% of FRPS model configurations result in ruin when delta is selected without reference to the system's maximum drawdown. With a delta of $5,000, no system fails — but the gains are too slow to justify the complexity. The sweet spot requires calibration based on simulation, which brings us to the chapter's core contribution: using simulated maximum drawdown to select the delta. Run approximately 100 simulations of 300 trades, use the average maximum drawdown, and set delta to half that value. With this discipline, FRPS performs well for strong systems and shows little difference from percent risk for weak ones.

An enhanced version — switching from FRPS to percent risk whenever percent risk offers a larger position — produces the best results of all. Average equity rises to $5.3 million versus $2.7 million for the baseline, and to $12.87 million when the maximum drawdown estimate is intentionally underestimated (with a 4.6% ruin trade-off). The gain-to-drawdown ratio for the best enhanced configuration reaches 380.9 versus 143.7 for the baseline. The key insight is that FRPS is most powerful at the beginning when accounts are small, while percent risk takes over as accounts grow — the switch happens naturally when percent risk becomes the larger bet.

"When you have a good system, the results suggest that risking the most will give the best results. Furthermore, switching to a fixed percentage only when it is a larger risk than FRPS clearly gives better results." — Van Tharp

Tharp's personal assessment is cautious despite the promising results. FRPS is not intuitively logical, is complex when simpler alternatives exist, and carries high bankruptcy risk during price shocks. Among Tharp's contacts, only Chris Anderson actually uses FRPS in practice. The prerequisites for using it are demanding: system SQN of at least 2.5, hundreds of simulations to establish maximum drawdown, delta set to half the peak (not average) maximum drawdown, and a paper-trading circuit breaker that triggers when equity drops half the historical maximum drawdown. If you don't have the stomach or the infrastructure for these safeguards, percent risk is simpler, nearly as effective, and far less likely to produce catastrophic outcomes.

The FRPS advantages and disadvantages are clearly stated. Small accounts benefit enormously from the rapid early scaling. With delta based on worst historical drawdown and a good system, it works well. It can achieve profit objectives better than simple percent risk when delta exceeds $2,000. But the disadvantages are serious: very complex with no intuitive feel, requires extensive historical testing and simulation, dangerous with poor systems, carries bankruptcy potential during price shocks, and may offer no advantage over simpler scaling or market's money methods. Tharp requires SQN of at least 2.5 before anyone should attempt FRPS.

The chapter also introduces a dampening factor for scaling back down. When equity drops by a prior increment, you reduce by one unit. At 50% dampening, you drop one unit when equity falls by half the distance of the most recent up-increment. At 100% dampening, you step back down exactly as fast as you stepped up. This asymmetry is crucial: stepping down faster than you stepped up means your position size shrinks rapidly during drawdowns, limiting damage but also limiting recovery speed. The dampening choice, like every other parameter in FRPS, requires simulation-based calibration rather than intuition.

Chapter 14: Position Sizing Methods to Help You Avoid Ruin

Money managers carry the label of their worst peak-to-trough drawdown for the rest of their careers, regardless of overall performance. An account that goes from $50,000 to $80,000 to $60,000 has a $20,000 decline — 25% from peak — that feels like a devastating loss even though the account is still up 20% from where it started. Clients don't remember the 60% gain. They remember the 25% drawdown. Most of the job of limiting these drawdowns falls on position sizing strategy, not the trading system itself, and this chapter provides six specific models for keeping drawdowns within tolerable bounds.

Model 17 uses the SQN to determine maximum allowable risk, and the resulting ruin tables are the book's most practical reference tool. Running 10,000 simulations of 100 trades at risk levels from 0.2% to 10% for all seven SQN systems produces a matrix that any trader can use as a lookup table. At less than 1% chance of ruin with a 25% drawdown level, an SQN 4 system allows 2.4% portfolio heat. An SQN 1 system allows only 0.2%. An SQN 7 system can handle up to 9.8% at a 50% drawdown tolerance. At 10% ruin tolerance, the numbers roughly double: SQN 4 can handle 4.8%.

Correlated positions dramatically compress allowable heat, and this is where most traders underestimate their risk. Ten correlated positions at SQN 4 with a 25% ruin level allow only 0.3% per position — not the 2.4% that a single uncorrelated position would permit. During a crisis, positions that seemed independent suddenly move together, and the trader's actual portfolio heat jumps from the intended 24% (ten positions at 2.4%) to an effective 24% concentrated in a single correlated bet. Very low drawdown tolerance combined with multiple positions demands either a superb system, very few simultaneous positions, or approximately 0.2% individual risk — often all three.

Two-Tier Position Sizing (Model 18) pursues two objectives simultaneously: never reaching ruin on core equity while using profits aggressively to reach a growth target. Using System 13-2 as the example with a 300% gain target and 25% drawdown failure threshold, the base level risks 0.4% with only a 1.4% chance of ruin. When equity grows 40% above starting capital — say from $100,000 to $140,000 — you switch to 1.2% risk. If the worst-case drawdown then hits 40%, the account drops to $84,000, still above the $75,000 ruin threshold. The math is designed so that even the worst outcome at the aggressive tier doesn't trigger ruin on the original capital.

Multiple Tier (Model 19) refines this with gradual increments rather than a single jump. Start at 0.4% risk, add 0.2% every 5% equity increase, up to a maximum of 1.2%. A dampening factor controls how fast you step back down during drawdowns: 100% dampening means you step down exactly as fast as you stepped up, while 50% dampening steps down twice as fast. The gradual approach avoids the psychological shock of a sudden risk increase and creates a smoother transition between conservative and aggressive modes.

Model 20 uses Maximum R-Drawdown from simulations to set position size directly. System 13-2 shows a median maximum drawdown of 38R, with 10% of simulations reaching 60R and 1% reaching 93R. To limit real drawdowns to 25% with 10% probability: divide 25% by 60R to get 0.4% risk per trade. For 1% probability: divide 25% by 93R to get 0.27%. The five-step process is clean: define worst-case drawdown, simulate R-drawdowns, choose probability level, divide, and adjust for correlation. This converts "how much should I risk?" from an opinion into a calculation.

Model 21, from Tom Basso, monitors open risk and open volatility in real time — Basso did this every minute by computer — and scales out when thresholds are exceeded. Four gold contracts at $440 with a $410 stop create $12,000 of open risk. If 3% of $216,000 equity is only $6,480, you must reduce to two contracts. Crucially, you do not raise the stop to keep the contracts — that merges exit and sizing decisions, which must remain separate. Open volatility monitoring works similarly: when total ATR exposure across positions exceeds a set percentage of equity, you reduce the most volatile position. Five corn contracts whose volatility jumps from 8 to 20 cents per bushel create $5,000 of exposure against a $4,500 limit, requiring the sale of one contract.

Model 22, the Basso-Schwager rebalancing technique, applies a form of Martingale logic across managers rather than within a single system. Tom Basso studied 79 CTAs and all 79,079 three-manager combinations from 1983 to 1993. Static allocation produced 13.27% annual return with 34.26% maximum drawdown. Monthly rebalancing — systematically moving money from winners back to losers across the three managers — produced 12.62% return with only 28.29% drawdown. The rebalanced group could be leveraged 1.211 times to match the static drawdown while producing 15.28% annualized return. Jack Schwager's follow-up showed that five low-correlation CTAs reduced risk by 38.6%, and ten CTAs reduced it by 45.3%. The application for individual traders: run five or more uncorrelated systems, rebalance monthly, and weight initial allocation by SQN.

Chapter 15: Position Sizing Strategies to Avoid

Every Martingale strategy shares the same fatal premise: increase position size after losses on the assumption that a win becomes more likely. In a casino doubling system starting at $1, five consecutive losses require a $32 bet, ten losses require $1,024, and twelve losses on a $5,000 account leave you with $905 and needing a $4,096 bet. The math is merciless and the outcome is certain: given enough time, any Martingale system encounters a streak long enough to exceed the account's capacity. The only question is when, not whether.

"Professional gamblers all say 'Don't use Martingale strategies because they don't work.'"— Van Tharp

Larry Williams' Model 23 increases commitment when recent win rate falls below the expected baseline — if the system wins 60% but recent trades show 40%, double up. Tharp raises three objections: you may not know the true baseline (if you think 65% but it's 55%, you increase size at exactly the wrong time), market conditions may have changed making your system obsolete, and each trade's probability is independent of the streak. Model 24, also from Williams, increases position size by one unit after every loss and decreases after every win. In a 30% win-rate system over 100 trades with 30 wins and 70 losses, position size grows to 41 units — Tharp calls it "probably one of the most dangerous and foolhardy methods of position sizing I've ever seen presented anywhere."

Model 25 is a more conservative version that only increases after three consecutive losses, but Monte Carlo simulations of 5,000 iterations across 300 trades at 40% win rate show that streaks of seven are near-certain in any given year, streaks of nine are also near-certain, and over a five-year period streaks of twelve or more are virtually guaranteed. Ten instances of 23 consecutive losers appeared across the simulations. Any Martingale designed around a single year's worst streak will be destroyed by the multi-year streaks that inevitably come. Model 26, Regression toward the Mean Position Sizing, risks 10% after a 20R drawdown — all share the same three fatal flaws: overestimated baseline performance, unchanged per-trade probabilities, and sensitivity to parameter settings.

The five non-Martingale dangerous models are equally instructive. Intuitive Position Sizing (Model 27) risks more on trades you "feel strongly" about. Psychological research shows zero correlation between trader confidence and trade outcome, making intuitive sizing mathematically equivalent to random sizing with an overconfidence bias. The exception: if you have 30 or more documented trades empirically showing that high-confidence calls outperform, you can conservatively increase from 1% to 1.5-2% — but the data must exist, not the feeling.

The Joe Ross Method (Model 28) opens at maximum size (five contracts) and sells 60% when profitable enough to cover initial risk, then raises stops to breakeven on the remainder. The fatal flaw is structural: every loss hits at 100% size while every big winner is captured at only 40%. It's the position sizing equivalent of cutting profits short and letting losses run. The Kelly Criterion (Model 30), adapted by Edward Thorp from J.L. Kelly's 1956 Bell System paper, was designed for binary outcomes and can grossly overestimate appropriate sizes for R-multiple distributions. At 25% Kelly on a 50% win system, losing ten to twenty trades in a row is a statistical certainty over time.

Optimal f (Model 31), from Ralph Vince, iteratively finds the fixed fraction of your largest historical loss that maximizes Terminal Wealth Relative. Vince himself declared that anyone not trading optimal f "belongs on a psychiatrist's couch rather than in the markets." The method's three fatal flaws: it assumes you've already experienced your worst loss (spectacularly dangerous), it considers only single- trade losses rather than losing streaks, and its iterative procedure is unnecessarily complex. Tharp's simulation of a system with SQN 0.21 shows that at optimal f of 15%, the probability of ruin is 42.5% and the median ending equity is negative. To maximize probability of reaching the objective requires 13.8% risk — producing a 29% chance of success against a 38% chance of ruin.

Percent Risk Based on Winning Percentage (Model 29) uses the formula F = p − (1 − p) where p is win probability, assuming payoff ratios of exactly 1. At 80% win rate it recommends 60% risk; at 60% it recommends 20%. But payoff ratios are never 1 in real trading, you don't know your true accuracy, and a high win-rate system can have negative expectancy — System 3-3 proved that. This model sounds rigorous but builds on assumptions that are never met in practice.

The chapter's final tally: four Martingale models (23-26) and five other dangerous models (27-31) that should never be used. Combined with the 22 viable models from earlier chapters, the book's total reaches 31 models times three equity models, yielding 93 different position sizing approaches. Nine of the 31 are explicitly marked as paths to ruin. The warning is clear: more position sizing methods exist that can destroy you than methods that can help you, and the dangerous ones often sound the most mathematically sophisticated.

Chapter 16: Putting It All Together

The final substantive chapter is an interview with Dr. Chris Anderson, a PhD in electrical engineering from NC State (1990) who specializes in radar and optical systems for complex decision-making in random environments. Anderson's contributions to this book are foundational: he validated Fixed Ratio Position Sizing under certain assumptions, originated thinking about drawdowns in R-multiple terms, pointed Tharp toward the System Quality Number, and developed the Monte Carlo simulator used throughout. The interview format allows Anderson to explain these tools in his own words, and his engineering perspective brings a different clarity than Tharp's psychological approach.

Anderson's five crucial elements of trading success provide a concise summary of everything the book teaches. First, understand your objectives — what you want and what drawdown you'll tolerate. Second, pick a system matching your wants and comfort levels. Third, choose a system stable enough to let its edge work over time. Fourth, position size to achieve your objectives without crossing your pain threshold. Fifth, determine when market conditions have changed enough to stop trading the system. Most traders fail not because they can't find an edge, but because of mismatches between what they want, what their system provides, and how they react when the inevitable drawdowns arrive.

The marble bag metaphor crystallizes the simulation approach more clearly than any formula. A backtest produces one outcome — one specific sequence of drawing marbles from a bag. Maybe you drew all the winning marbles first and the equity curve looked spectacular. Maybe a different order would have produced a devastating drawdown. Simulation draws marbles in random orders thousands of times to show the full distribution of possible outcomes. Running 5,000 simulations on a system with ten 1.5R winners and ten 1R losers produces forty or more distinct equity paths, some reaching positive 40R and others negative 20R, all from the same underlying system.

The Coefficient of Variation — standard deviation times 100 divided by expectancy — emerges as a key metric for comparing systems. A system with 0.8R expectancy and 4.82 standard deviation has a CV of 602.5%, meaning extreme variability relative to its edge. A system with 0.45R expectancy and 1.49 standard deviation has a CV of 331% — smoother, more predictable, faster to recover from drawdowns. Smaller CV doesn't necessarily mean more profitable in the long run, but it means a smoother ride that's psychologically sustainable. Since the biggest risk to any trading system is the trader abandoning it during a drawdown, CV may be the most important practical metric after SQN.

Three reasons justify running multiple systems: diversification against any single edge breaking, increased trading frequency while maintaining quality, and the dramatic impact of frequency on drawdown recovery time. A system trading 12 times per month may need 25 to 200 trades to recover from a long drawdown — that's months or years. At 50 trades per month, recovery comes much faster. Each system must be independently strong and uncorrelated, with separate position sizing per system to prevent concentration risk.

Anderson distinguishes between the psychological and technical dimensions of trading failure. Most traders fail due to psychological issues rather than technical ones. Two specific problems dominate: the inability to cut losses short, leading to outsized R-multiple losers that destroy the distribution, and mismatches between what traders want and what their system can realistically provide. A trader who wants 100% annual returns with less than 10% drawdowns has incompatible objectives, and no position sizing method can resolve a contradiction. The technical tools — SQN, simulation, R-multiples — exist to make the psychology manageable by replacing vague fears with specific numbers.

When bringing a new system online, Anderson recommends starting with minimal position sizes and growing with winnings. Using a system with 0.25R expectancy and typical 9R drawdown, the initial risk might be only 0.6% to minimize ruin probability. After demonstrated live profitability, shift to 4% risk — accepting 40% ruin probability but targeting 155% median return. This graduated approach validates the system with real money before committing significant capital. Tharp's personal standard requires a minimum 3:1 reward-to-risk ratio before considering any system and drawdown duration preferably under 2.5 months.

"Determine your objectives and System Quality Numbers; then use the guidelines in this book to determine what strategy is right for you." — Chris Anderson

The interview closes with Anderson emphasizing that the tools in this book — SQN, Monte Carlo simulation, R-multiple analysis — are not guarantees. They're frameworks for making better decisions under uncertainty. Markets change, systems degrade, and the trader's psychology remains the weakest link in any chain. But a trader armed with these tools, who has honestly assessed their objectives and their tolerance for pain, has a structural advantage over the vast majority of market participants who trade on gut feel, indicator worship, and hope. The book's final message: determine your objectives and System Quality Numbers, then use the guidelines to determine what strategy is right for you.

Trading & Investing Lessons

Position Sizing Is the Leverage Dial, Not the Entry Signal

The random-entry experiment proves that a coin flip with a good exit and proper position sizing can be profitable 80% of the time. Most traders spend 90% of their energy on entries and 10% on position sizing; this book argues the ratio should be inverted. When evaluating any trading system or newsletter, the first question should be "what is the R-multiple distribution?" not "what is the win rate?"

The Recovery Asymmetry as Risk Ceiling

A 50% drawdown requires a 100% gain to recover. A 75% drawdown requires 300%. This isn't a theoretical concern — it's the mathematical reason why maximum risk percentages exist. Any position sizing model that allows drawdowns beyond 50% is functionally equivalent to ruin for most traders, because the psychological and mathematical barriers to recovery become insurmountable.

SQN as System Due Diligence

Before allocating capital to any strategy, compute its SQN from at least 100 trades. Below 1.7 isn't statistically significant. Between 2.0 and 3.0 is good but requires conservative sizing. Above 3.0 opens up more aggressive sizing options. This single number encapsulates system quality more completely than win rate, profit factor, or Sharpe ratio, and it directly maps to position sizing decisions through the ruin tables.

Drawdown Duration Matters More Than Depth

System 3-4 has a 95% drawdown duration of 83.2 months — nearly seven years. SQN3 has a duration of only 3.6 months. Most traders abandon systems during multi-year drawdowns regardless of the system's long-run edge. When choosing between systems with similar SQN scores, prefer the one with shorter drawdown durations, because you're more likely to actually follow it.

Correlated Positions Multiply Risk Silently

Ten positions at 2% risk each don't create 20% portfolio heat if all ten move together during a crisis. The ruin tables show that ten correlated SQN 4 positions at a 25% ruin level allow only 0.3% per position — not the 2.4% that a single position would permit. Correlation is invisible during calm markets and lethal during turbulent ones.

Market's Money as Psychological Armor

Treating profits as "the market's money" and risking them more aggressively creates a psychological separation that helps traders stay in trades longer and tolerate drawdowns better. The mathematical result is faster compounding during winning periods without the emotional devastation of giving back original capital during drawdowns.

Key Takeaways

The How-Much Decision Dominates Everything

Random entries with proper position sizing and exits produced $640 million from $1 million. The same entries without position sizing produced $382,000. The entry signal is essentially irrelevant compared to position sizing and exit strategy. This is the book's central proof point, demonstrated empirically rather than theoretically.

Optimal Position Size Depends on Your Definition of Optimal

Six definitions of "optimal" produce wildly different risk percentages on the same system, from 0.8% to 30%. Maximum mean return requires 30% risk and produces 99.8% ruin. Maximum probability of achieving objectives requires 2.4% risk. The definition you choose reveals your actual risk tolerance.

Win Rate Is a Trap

A 90% win-rate system can have negative expectancy and lose money over time. A 10% win-rate system can have the highest expectancy of any system tested. Evaluating systems by win rate alone is the most common and most costly mistake in trading.

Ninety-Five Percent of PhDs Lost Money in a Winning Game

The Ralph Vince experiment with 40 PhDs playing a 60% win-rate game is the most devastating demonstration that position sizing — not intelligence, not knowledge, not even edge — determines whether you make or lose money.

Nine Models Should Never Be Used

Four Martingale models (23-26) and five other dangerous models (27-31) including Kelly Criterion, Optimal f, and Intuitive Sizing are specifically identified as paths to ruin. The book doesn't just teach what works — it warns against what destroys.

Your System's SQN Determines Your Position Sizing Options

A poor system (SQN 1) at 25% drawdown tolerance with less than 1% ruin probability can risk only 0.2% per trade. A superb system (SQN 7) at the same tolerance can risk 9.8%. System quality doesn't just affect returns — it constrains the entire range of position sizing strategies available to you.

Contrarian Ideas

Entries Are the Least Important Part of Trading

The entire industry is built around entry signals, indicators, and stock picking. Tharp proves that random entries with good exits and position sizing outperform most actively managed funds. The massive infrastructure of Wall Street research, recommendations, and predictions is essentially irrelevant to trading success.

High Win Rates Are a Red Flag

Rather than signaling a good system, a very high win rate often indicates hidden tail risk. System 3-3's 90% win rate masks a negative expectancy that guarantees long-term losses. Systems that "feel good" because they win frequently are often the most dangerous to trade with real money.

Adding Huge Winners Hurts System Quality

Counterintuitively, adding two 30R winners to a good system reduced its SQN by 20% because the standard deviation exploded. The best system improvements come from adding consistent small winners, not chasing lottery-ticket trades. The steadiest edge, not the flashiest, produces the most tradeable system.

Kelly Criterion and Optimal f Are Dangerous

Two of the most academically respected position sizing methods — both backed by rigorous mathematics — can lead directly to ruin when applied to real trading. Kelly was designed for binary outcomes. Optimal f assumes your worst loss has already occurred. Both produce ruin probabilities exceeding 40% in Tharp's simulations.

Most Traders Are Too Smart to Succeed

The PhD experiment proves that intelligence doesn't help. Sensory/detail-oriented traders (75% of the population) are specifically vulnerable to indicator salesmen because they demand data before acting — and the data they're shown is always cherry-picked.

Proposals & Frameworks

The SQN Rating Scale

A universal system evaluation framework: below 1.0 is probably untradeable, 1.7+ is statistically significant, 2.0-3.0 is good, 3.0-5.0 is excellent, 5.0-7.0 is superb, and 7.0+ is Holy Grail. Cap N at 100 for comparisons. Every position sizing decision in the book flows from where your system falls on this scale.

Six Market Types Model

Markets exist in six states: up-volatile, up-quiet, sideways-volatile, sideways-quiet, down-volatile, and down-quiet. Every system should be evaluated separately in each type, requiring at least 30 trades per type (180 minimum total). A system with SQN 1.94 overall can be SQN 4.32 in up-quiet conditions and −1.13 in sideways-volatile — making market-type classification essential for knowing when to trade and when to sit out.

The CPR Universal Sizing Formula

P = C / R. Three variables, one formula, applicable to any instrument in any market. Cash at risk, risk per unit, position size. The simplicity is intentional — any formula complex enough to confuse you is complex enough to be applied incorrectly under the stress of live trading.

Five-Step Ruin Avoidance Process

Define worst-case drawdown to avoid, simulate system to determine R-drawdown probabilities, choose R-drawdown at acceptable probability level, divide target drawdown percentage by that R value to get position size percentage, and adjust for correlated positions. This converts an abstract "how much should I risk" question into a concrete mathematical procedure.

31 Models × 3 Equity Models = 93 Position Sizing Approaches

The comprehensive taxonomy of every position sizing method, from the simplest (one unit per fixed amount) to the most sophisticated (multi-tier with dampening factors), combined with three equity models, creates a decision matrix that covers virtually every possible approach. Nine are explicitly identified as dangerous, leaving 66 viable combinations for traders to match against their systems and objectives.

The book doesn't just assert this; it proves it with Monte Carlo simulations, comparative model testing, and exhaustive data across multiple system types.

The practical framework is immediately actionable. Any trader can calculate their R-multiples, compute their SQN, look up their maximum risk percentage in the ruin tables, and implement the CPR formula tomorrow. The book also does something rare in trading literature: it explicitly warns against popular methods. The Kelly Criterion and Optimal f chapters, demolishing two academically prestigious approaches with simulation evidence, save readers from expensive mistakes that sound mathematically rigorous but perform catastrophically in practice.

Perhaps most importantly, the book reframes what position sizing is for. It's not about maximizing returns. It's about meeting your specific objectives — whatever those are — while keeping the probability of ruin below your personal tolerance. This reframing, from optimization to objective-matching, is the conceptual breakthrough that makes the rest of the book's technical content usable. A trader who understands this will never again ask "what's the best position sizing model?" and will instead ask "what position sizing model matches my system, my objectives, and my psychology?" That question, properly answered, is worth the entire book.
