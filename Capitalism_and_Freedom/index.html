Key Takeaways

  1. Economic and political freedom are inseparable: No society with broad political freedom has existed without something comparable to a free market. Democratic socialism is impossible because centralized economic control inevitably concentrates political power.
  2. The market enables “unanimity without conformity”: Markets work like proportional representation—each person gets what they want without forcing others to conform. Political channels require yes/no decisions that strain social cohesion.
  3. Government’s proper role is limited: Rule-maker and umpire, not player. Protect freedom, enforce contracts, foster competition, handle genuine neighborhood effects—but every expansion carries the danger of becoming irreversible.
  4. The Federal Reserve caused the Great Depression: The Fed, created to prevent banking panics, produced the worst banking panic in American history through acts of commission and omission, allowing the money stock to fall by one-third from 1929 to 1933.
  5. Rules beat discretionary authority: Legislative rules for monetary policy (steady money supply growth of 3–5% annually) prevent the concentration of power that discretionary authority inevitably creates—paralleling the logic of the First Amendment.
  6. Floating exchange rates preserve freedom: Fixed rates and exchange controls lead inevitably to import rationing and authoritarian spirals. Freely floating rates are the only fully automatic, freedom-preserving adjustment mechanism.
  7. The Keynesian multiplier is empirically wrong: Government spending of $100 adds “just about $100 to income”—entirely absorbed by government, with private expenditure unchanged. The multiplier is closer to 1 than the theoretical 3.
  8. School vouchers would reduce class distinctions: The current system stratifies education by neighborhood wealth. Vouchers would give poor families genuine school choice and inject competitive pressure that rewards teaching merit over seniority.
  9. Occupational licensing is the new guild system: Over 80 occupations licensed by state law, with pressure invariably from practitioners, not the public. The AMA functions as “perhaps the strongest trade union in the United States.”
  10. The negative income tax is superior to the welfare state: A single program setting an income floor through the tax system would replace dozens of special measures, direct help specifically at poverty, preserve work incentives, and cost far less.
  11. Capitalism reduces discrimination: The impersonal market separates economic efficiency from irrelevant personal characteristics. Discrimination persists most in monopolistic sectors and least where competition is greatest.
  12. Corporate “social responsibility” is subversive: In a free economy, business has one responsibility—increase profits within rules of open competition. The doctrine transforms businessmen into unelected civil servants.
  13. Graduated taxes create more inequality than they cure: A flat rate of 23.5% would yield the same revenue as the 20–91% graduated structure. High rates encourage avoidance and protect existing wealth from newcomers.
  14. Government reforms produce effects opposite to those intended: Railroad regulation protected railroads; public housing worsened housing; agricultural programs hurt impecunious farmers; Social Security increased need for direct relief.
  15. Ideas matter when crisis arrives: Books “keep options open until circumstances make change necessary.” The core function is developing alternatives until “the politically impossible becomes politically inevitable.”

Contrarian Ideas

  1. Fair employment laws and right-to-work laws are identical in principle: Both interfere with freedom of voluntary contracts. The ACLU will fight to the death to protect a racist’s right to preach segregation, but will jail him if he refuses to hire a Negro—an utter contradiction.
  2. Private monopoly may be the least evil: In a dynamic society, monopoly conditions change; public regulation and public monopoly outlive their justification. The ICC was created to regulate railroad monopoly but became a protector of incumbents against new competitors.
  3. National parks should be privately operated: Unlike city parks, national parks have limited entrances where tolls are feasible—no neighborhood-effect justification for public provision exists.
  4. Roosevelt’s gold nationalization was equivalent to Castro’s land seizure: “No difference in principle between this nationalization of gold at artificially low price and Fidel Castro’s nationalization of land and factories at artificially low price.”
  5. Discrimination is a “taste” that markets penalize: A person refusing to hire based on race limits his own range of choice and pays higher costs—exactly like a country imposing tariffs on itself.
  6. Medical licensure reduces both quantity and quality of care: It creates alternative professions of possibly lower quality (osteopathy), featherbedding, reduced experimentation, and difficulty suing for malpractice.
  7. Inherited wealth and inherited talent are ethically identical: There is no greater ethical justification for high returns to someone who inherits a peculiar voice than to someone who inherits property. The distinction is untenable.
  8. The Keynesian multiplier is closer to 1 than 3: Deficit spending is “not demonstrated true by logical considerations alone, has never been documented by empirical evidence, and is inconsistent with relevant empirical evidence.”
  9. Compulsory Social Security is paternalism for adults: If a man knowingly prefers to live for today and deliberately chooses a penurious old age, by what right do we prevent him? “Humility is the distinguishing virtue of the believer in freedom; arrogance, of the paternalist.”
  10. Government is the most unstable component of the economy: The most unstable element of postwar national income is federal expenditure itself—and its instability doesn’t even offset other movements.

Author’s Key Proposals

  1. Monetary Rule: Legislate steady growth of money supply at 3–5% annually, eliminating Federal Reserve discretion. Convert monetary policy from a threat to freedom into a pillar of free society.
  2. Floating Exchange Rates: Abandon fixed exchange rates and gold convertibility entirely. Let currency prices move freely to clear the market—the only fully automatic, freedom-preserving mechanism.
  3. Unilateral Free Trade: Follow Britain’s corn law repeal. Eliminate tariffs by one-tenth annually over ten years; raise import quotas 20% annually. Reciprocal negotiation fosters the erroneous view that tariffs help the imposing country.
  4. School Voucher System: Parents receive vouchers redeemable at any approved school—public, private, religious, or for-profit. Government sets minimum standards but does not operate schools.
  5. Negative Income Tax: Replace all welfare programs with a single income-floor mechanism through the tax system. At 50% subsidy rate: person with no income receives $300; every extra dollar earned means more money available.
  6. Flat-Rate Income Tax: Replace the graduated 20–91% structure with a flat rate (~23.5%) on broadly defined income, eliminating percentage depletion, tax-exempt securities, and special capital gains treatment.
  7. Abolish Corporate Income Tax: Require corporations to attribute undistributed earnings to stockholders for individual taxation—few measures would do more to invigorate capital markets and promote competition.
  8. Equity Investment in Human Capital: Government finances vocational training in exchange for a specified percentage of future earnings above a base amount, combined with income tax at minimal administrative cost.
  9. Eliminate Occupational Licensing: Replace with voluntary certification that informs consumers without restricting practice. Even medicine should require only certification, not licensing.
  10. End Fourteen Unjustifiable Activities: Agricultural price supports, tariffs, output controls, rent/price controls, minimum wages, detailed industry regulation, FCC broadcasting control, compulsory Social Security, restrictive licensing, public housing subsidies, peacetime conscription, national parks, postal monopoly, publicly owned toll roads.

Prefaces (2002, 1982, 1962)

The Triumph of Economic Freedom

Between the 1962 publication and the 1980s, the climate of opinion shifted dramatically from hostility toward free-market ideas to respectability. Government spending nonetheless exploded: from 26% of national income in 1956 to 36% by 2000. Thatcher and Reagan rode the wave of changing opinion but couldn’t reverse spending growth.

The 1989–92 collapse of the Soviet Union was the decisive natural experiment. Smaller-scale precedents had already confirmed the verdict: Hong Kong/Taiwan vs. mainland China, West Germany vs. East Germany, South Korea vs. North Korea. Hayek’s Road to Serfdom was vindicated.

China under Deng Xiaoping introduced market reforms that made Chinese citizens “freer and more prosperous than under Mao—freer in every dimension except the political.” The East Asian tigers proved market-oriented development works where central planning and foreign aid failed.

One Major Revision

Friedman proposed replacing the economic/political freedom dichotomy with a trichotomy: economic freedom, civil freedom, and political freedom. Hong Kong before the handover showed that economic freedom is necessary for civil and political freedom, but political freedom is not necessary for economic and civil freedom.

The Role of Ideas in Crisis

“There is enormous inertia—a tyranny of the status quo—in private and especially governmental arrangements. Only a crisis produces real change. When that crisis occurs, the actions that are taken depend on the ideas that are lying around.”

Barry Goldwater was overwhelmingly defeated in 1964; Ronald Reagan overwhelmingly victorious in 1980—“two men with essentially the same program and the same message.” What changed was not the arguments but the experience: Russia and China “gone sour,” Vietnam disillusioned intellectuals, Great Society programs “turning to ashes,” and inflation hit pocketbooks.

At a late-1960s debate at the University of Wisconsin, Friedman’s opponent Leon Keyserling read Chapter 2’s list of fourteen unjustified government activities to mock Friedman as reactionary. When he reached point 11—conscription—the audience burst into applause and he lost them entirely. Conscription was the only item actually eliminated.

Introduction

Kennedy’s famous “Ask not” line reflects paternalism in both halves: “what your country can do for you” treats government as patron and citizen as ward; “what you can do for your country” treats government as master and citizen as servant. A free man sees government as instrumentality—the true question is “what can I and my compatriots do through government” while preventing it from becoming a Frankenstein.

Two Constitutional Principles

Limited scope: Government protects freedom, enforces contracts, fosters competition—but any expansion is fraught with danger. Dispersed power: Better at county than state, state than federal. Local dissatisfaction allows mobility; federal-level dissatisfaction leaves “few alternatives in this world of jealous nations.”

Great advances never came from centralized government: Columbus, Newton, Shakespeare, Edison, Florence Nightingale—all products of individual genius and a social climate permitting variety. Government uniform standards could improve many lives at any moment but would “replace progress by stagnation, substitute uniform mediocrity for variety essential for experimentation.”

The book’s major theme: competitive capitalism as both a system of economic freedom and a necessary condition for political freedom. The term “liberalism” rightfully belongs to this viewpoint. Nineteenth-century liberalism emphasized freedom and free trade; twentieth-century “liberalism” shifted to welfare and equality, reviving the state paternalism classical liberalism opposed.

Chapter I: The Relation between Economic Freedom and Political Freedom

The common belief that politics and economics are separate is a delusion. Democratic socialism is impossible: a socialist society cannot guarantee individual freedom. Economic freedom is both an end in itself and a means to political freedom.

No society with a large measure of political freedom has existed without something comparable to a free market. Capitalism is necessary but not sufficient: Fascist Italy, Spain, pre-war Japan, and Tsarist Russia had private enterprise without political freedom. Yet even in those societies, citizens had more freedom than in totalitarian states.

Economic Power as Check on Political Power

Competitive capitalism separates economic from political power, enabling each to offset the other. Economic power can be widely dispersed (many millionaires in one economy), but political power is harder to decentralize. If economic power joins political power, concentration is almost inevitable.

Freedom to Advocate Radical Change

Can men openly advocate capitalism in a socialist society? All jobs are under political authority, and advocacy requires financing—renting halls, printing pamphlets, buying radio time. But all resources are government-controlled. The crucial role of inequality of wealth and the patron: radical movements have always been funded by wealthy converts (Engels, Vanderbilt Field, Anita McCormick Blaine). The market breaks the vicious circle by enabling financing through profitability.

“From 1933 to the outbreak of World War II, Churchill was not permitted to talk over the British radio, which was a government monopoly, because the BBC held his views to be too controversial.”

The Hollywood blacklist failed precisely because the market made it costly: at least 15% of films were written by blacklisted members under pseudonyms. Producer Frank King: “We have an obligation to our stockholders to buy the best script we can.” The impersonal market separates economic activities from political views—no one who buys bread knows the farmer’s politics.

Chapter II: The Role of Government in a Free Society

The market permits “unanimity without conformity”—a system of proportional representation where each person gets what they want. Political channels require substantial conformity: decisions are yes/no, and using them strains social cohesion. Majority rule is an expedient, not a principle.

Government as Rule-Maker and Umpire

Society is like a game requiring acceptance of rules and an umpire. Absolute freedom is impossible with imperfect men—freedoms can conflict. Complex examples: freedom to combine vs. freedom to compete; property rights as complex social creations (airplane rights, patents, riparian rights).

Monopoly: Three Bad Alternatives

When markets can’t function due to monopoly: private monopoly, public monopoly, or public regulation. All are bad. Friedman concludes private monopoly may be the least evil because in a dynamic society, monopoly conditions change—and public regulation outlives its justification.

“The ICC was established to protect the public from exploitation by the railroads. After the introduction of trucking, the ICC became the means whereby the railroads could protect themselves from competition by trucks.”

Neighborhood Effects

Actions imposing costs/benefits on third parties that can’t be charged justify government intervention—but the argument cuts both ways. City parks justify public provision (impossible to charge beneficiaries); national parks do not (entrances are few, tolls feasible). Every government intervention to overcome neighborhood effects introduces new neighborhood effects.

Fourteen Unjustifiable Government Activities

Agricultural price supports, tariffs, output controls, rent/price controls, minimum wages, detailed industry regulation (ICC, banking), FCC broadcasting control, compulsory Social Security, occupational licensing, public housing subsidies, peacetime conscription, national parks, prohibition on private mail carrying, publicly owned toll roads.

Chapter III: The Control of Money

An automatic commodity (gold) standard is neither feasible nor desirable: it requires enormous resource costs and the mythology to sustain it no longer exists. Even the nineteenth-century gold standard was highly managed—gold accounted for only 10–20% of the U.S. money stock from 1879–1913.

Comparing the pre-Fed era (1879–1914) to the post-Fed era reveals the latter as decidedly more unstable in money stock, prices, and output. The three most severe contractions in American history—1920–21, 1929–33, 1937–38—all occurred under the Fed.

The Great Depression: A Man-Made Catastrophe

The October 1929 stock market crash was neither the start nor the major cause. The peak came months earlier, partly due to tight Fed policy to curb “speculation.” The first year of decline was severe but showed no signs of catastrophe.

November 1930: Bank failures triggered runs. The failure of the Bank of the United States (over $200 million in deposits; its name led many to think it was official) was critical. The Fed “failed the test miserably”—it did nothing. This was a failure of will, not power.

September 1931: When Britain went off gold, the Fed raised the discount rate more sharply than in its entire history—arresting the gold drain but triggering spectacular bank failures. One in ten banks suspended; deposits fell 15%.

From July 1929 to March 1933, the money stock fell by one-third. It is “literally inconceivable” that income could have declined by over one-half and prices by over one-third without this monetary collapse. The system established to prevent suspension of convertibility instead let nearly one-third of banks fail.

Rules Instead of Authorities

The case for rules parallels the First Amendment: voting on free speech case-by-case, the majority would deny it in most instances. But voting on free speech as a bundle, an overwhelming majority supports it. The same logic applies to monetary policy.

“Friedman’s proposed rule: legislate that the total money stock must grow at a specified annual rate of 3–5%. The precise definition and rate matter far less than making a definite choice and sticking to it.”

Chapter IV: International Financial and Trade Arrangements

The most serious short-run threat to economic freedom is exchange controls to “solve” balance of payments problems. Starting with foreign exchange controls leads inevitably to import rationing and domestic production controls. Full exchange controls were invented by Hjalmar Schacht in early Nazi Germany.

Gold as Commodity Price Support

U.S. gold policy is functionally a price support program. When gold was set at $35/ounce in 1934, this was above market price—producing a “surplus.” Now prices have doubled while gold stays at $35, creating a “shortage”—exactly as rent ceilings produce housing shortages.

The 1933–34 nationalization of private gold holdings was a fundamental departure from liberal principles. There is “no difference in principle between this nationalization of gold at artificially low price and Fidel Castro’s nationalization of land and factories at artificially low price.”

Four Mechanisms for Adjustment

Reserves (temporary), domestic deflation (painful), direct controls (authoritarian), and exchange rate adjustment (the only fully automatic, freedom-preserving mechanism). Freely floating rates would prevent balance-of-payments problems from arising, enable free trade, and make transparent the fallacy of protectionist “low wages” arguments.

The Case for Unilateral Free Trade

Reciprocal tariff negotiation is the wrong procedure: it fosters the erroneous view that tariffs help the imposing country. The U.S. should follow Britain’s nineteenth-century corn law repeal—raise all quotas 20% annually, reduce all tariffs by one-tenth annually over ten years.

“We believe in freedom and intend to practice it. No one can force you to be free. That is your business. But we can offer you full co-operation on equal terms to all. Our market is open to you.”

Chapter V: Fiscal Policy

Three failed justifications for government spending: pump priming (abandoned after the 1937–38 contraction), secular stagnation (discredited but programs persist), and balance wheel (spending rises in recessions, never falls in expansions, and programs take effect after recessions pass).

The most unstable component of postwar national income is federal expenditure itself—and its instability doesn’t even offset other movements.

Demolishing the Keynesian Multiplier

The “widely held view” that deficit spending is necessarily expansionary “cannot be demonstrated true by logical considerations alone, has never been documented by empirical evidence, and is inconsistent with relevant empirical evidence.”

Problem 1: If government provides something people were buying privately (park admission), the spending adds nothing—it just diverts private expenditure. Only spending on something utterly useless avoids diversion, revealing the “limited intellectual content” of make-work programs.

Problem 2: The Keynesian multiplier of 3 works only under two extreme assumptions that no established economist accepts: either the liquidity trap or complete interest-rate insensitivity of investment.

Friedman’s empirical finding: a $100 increase in government expenditure adds “just about $100 to income”—all absorbed by government, with private expenditures unchanged. The proper fiscal policy: plan expenditures based on what the community wants, set tax rates to cover spending on average, avoid erratic changes.

Chapter VI: The Role of Government in Education

Neighborhood effects justify government involvement (democratic society requires minimum literacy and shared values) but not government administration. Paternalism is inescapable for children.

The Voucher System

Government subsidy of education is justifiable, but nationalization of the “education industry” is not. Parents receive vouchers redeemable at any approved school. Government’s role is limited to minimum standards. Precedents: the GI Bill, British local authority payments, French state subsidies.

Vouchers would reduce class distinctions: under the current system, low-income families face a double penalty—they cannot afford to move to expensive neighborhoods with “good” schools AND pay for private alternatives. Modern urban reality stratifies education by neighborhood wealth; the myth of the small town with one school for rich and poor is obsolete.

The Teacher Salary Problem

The core issue: salaries are too uniform and rigid. Poor teachers are grossly overpaid, good teachers grossly underpaid. Bureaucratic salary schedules based on seniority and credentials (not merit) are the inevitable result of government administration.

The Equity Investment Proposal

Vocational training is investment in human capital with returns captured by the individual—no neighborhood effects justify subsidy. But human beings cannot serve as collateral in a non-slave state, creating a capital market imperfection.

Friedman’s solution: government finances training in exchange for a specified percentage of future earnings above a base amount. Payments are combined with income tax at minimal administrative cost. This makes equality of opportunity a reality “not by impeding competition, destroying incentive, dealing with symptoms… but by strengthening competition, making incentives effective, eliminating causes of inequality.”

Chapter VII: Capitalism and Discrimination

Capitalism’s development coincided with major reductions in discrimination against religious, racial, and social groups. Jews survived medieval persecution by operating in market sectors; Puritans and Quakers accumulated market funds to migrate to the New World; post-Civil War Southern states never imposed barriers to Negro property ownership because “the strong belief in private property overrode the desire to discriminate.”

The bread purchaser doesn’t know whether wheat was grown by a white, Negro, Christian, or Jew. A businessman expressing preferences unrelated to productive efficiency imposes higher costs on himself. Discrimination in a free market is a “taste” that the market penalizes.

Fair Employment Practices Legislation

FEPC clearly interferes with freedom of voluntary contracts. The grocery store owner in a prejudiced neighborhood may simply be transmitting community tastes, not exercising his own prejudice. Friedman distinguishes positive harm (physical force) from negative harm (inability to find contracts)—there is no case for government to prevent the second type.

“Is it any more desirable that momentary majorities decide what characteristics are relevant to employment than that they decide what speech is appropriate?”

The ACLU position is contradictory: defending racist speech but criminalizing discriminatory hiring. Right-to-work laws and FEPC are identical in principle—both interfere with employment contracts—yet supporters of each oppose the other.

Segregation in Schooling

Because schooling is government-operated, the state must choose between enforced segregation or enforced integration—both bad. The solution: school vouchers. Virginia adopted a voucher plan to avoid integration, but the first request was a parent transferring a child from a segregated to an integrated school because it was educationally better.

“The difference between the result and the intention is one of the primary justifications of a free society.”

Chapter VIII: Monopoly and the Social Responsibility of Business and Labor

The most important fact about enterprise monopoly: its relative unimportance from an economy-wide view. Four million separate enterprises, 400,000 new ones born each year, one-fifth of workers self-employed. Of the private sector, at most one-quarter is monopolistic, at least three-quarters competitive.

Monopoly in Labor

Unions raised wage rates for 10–15% of workers by 10–15%, meaning 85–90% of workers had wages reduced by about 4%. Since unions have been strongest among already high-paid groups, they have made working-class incomes more unequal.

The Three Sources of Monopoly

Technical (natural): Single enterprise more efficient than many. Friedman advocates private unregulated monopoly as least evil—dynamic changes will undermine it over time.

Government assistance (the most important source): Tariffs foster domestic collusion; Britain was protected from widespread monopoly by free trade. The corporate tax structure encourages earnings retention, giving established corporations advantages over new enterprises. Special union immunities from antitrust.

Private collusion: Generally unstable without government help—takes only one “chiseler” (actually a public benefactor) to break a cartel. Antitrust laws’ major contribution is ruling out obvious collusive devices.

Social Responsibility

In a free economy, business has one social responsibility: increase profits within rules of open competition. The doctrine is fundamentally subversive: if businessmen have social responsibility beyond profits, they become unelected civil servants.

The 1962 steel price episode: President Kennedy’s threats (antitrust suits, tax audits) demonstrated how much power for a police state already existed. Corporate charitable contributions are inappropriate—they prevent stockholders from deciding how to dispose of their own funds and move society toward a corporate state.

Chapter IX: Occupational Licensure

Overthrow of the medieval guild system was an indispensable step in the rise of Western freedom. Yet by 1952, over 80 occupations were licensed by state law—from pharmacists to threshing machine operators, egg graders, guide dog trainers, tree surgeons, and “hypertrichologists” (who remove excessive hair in Connecticut). Pressure invariably comes from practitioners, not the public.

Three Levels of Intervention

Registration: list names, no denial of right to practice. Certification: government certifies skills but doesn’t prevent uncertified practice (like CPA). Licensing: must obtain license or face jail. Friedman finds it “difficult to see any case where licensure rather than certification can be justified.”

Barbering: A Case Study

A Maryland law required barbers to study “bacteriology, histology of the hair, skin, nails, muscles and nerves, structure of the head, face and neck, elementary chemistry relating to sterilization.” In 1929, no state required barber college graduation; now states require 1,000+ hours of “theoretical” instruction.

Medical Licensure: The AMA as Trade Union

The AMA is “perhaps the strongest trade union in the United States.” The Council on Medical Education approves schools; state lists are identical. In the 1930s, the Council wrote to schools that they were admitting too many students—every school reduced admissions the following year.

After Hitler came to power (1933), the number of foreign-trained physicians admitted to practice in the U.S. in the following five years was the same as in the five years before—despite a tremendous outflow from Germany. The threat of additional physicians led to tighter requirements.

Friedman argues licensure reduces both quantity and quality: it creates alternative professions of possibly lower quality, encourages featherbedding, reduces experimentation, and makes malpractice suits harder. Without AMA monopoly, “medical teams”—department stores of medicine combining practitioners of different skill levels—would have emerged.

Chapter X: The Distribution of Income

The ethical principle of free market society: “to each according to what he and the instruments he owns produces.” Payment according to product serves an allocative function—without it, mutually beneficial exchanges won’t occur.

Inequality from Tastes for Uncertainty

If individuals with equal endowments enter a lottery voluntarily, the resulting inequality is required to let them make the most of their initial equality. The girl who tries to become a movie actress rather than a civil servant is deliberately choosing a lottery. Redistributing after the event denies the opportunity.

Inherited Wealth vs. Inherited Talent

The distinction is untenable. Is there greater ethical justification for high returns to someone who inherits a peculiar voice than to someone who inherits property? A parent can pass wealth by financing CPA training, setting up a business, or creating a trust fund—the effect is identical though the income is classified differently.

Facts of Income Distribution

The more capitalistic a country, the smaller the fraction paid for capital and the larger for human services—in India/Egypt, about half is property income; in the U.S., roughly one-fifth. Capitalism leads to less inequality than alternative systems. The great achievement of capitalism is not accumulation of property but opportunities for people to develop their capacities.

Graduated Taxes Failed

A flat rate of 23.5% on presently defined taxable income would yield the same revenue as the 20–91% graduated structure. Graduated taxes are less burdensome on being wealthy than becoming wealthy—they protect existing holders from newcomer competition.

Chapter XI: Social Welfare Measures

Public Housing

Dwelling units destroyed far exceeded new units constructed—net effect raised persons per dwelling unit. Income limitations concentrated “broken” families. Cash grants would have spread families through the community, avoiding concentration effects.

Minimum Wage Laws

Effects precisely opposite those intended: the state can legislate a minimum wage but cannot require employers to hire at that wage. Those rendered unemployed are precisely those who can least afford to lose income.

Farm Price Supports

Benefits inversely related to need. Main effect: kept more people on farms, making output larger rather than income per farmer higher. Consumer pays twice—taxes and higher food prices.

Old Age and Survivor’s Insurance

Three analytically separable elements: compulsory purchase of annuities, nationalization of annuity provision, and income redistribution. Each is independently unjustifiable.

The paternalistic justification: people are shortsighted, “we” know better. But this is paternalism for responsible adults—“the thoroughgoing paternalist is an opponent on grounds of principle, not a well-meaning but misguided friend. He basically believes in dictatorship, benevolent perhaps, majoritarian perhaps, but dictatorship nonetheless.”

“Humility is the distinguishing virtue of the believer in freedom; arrogance, of the paternalist.”

Chapter XII: The Alleviation of Poverty

Western countries enormously reduced absolute poverty through free enterprise over two centuries. Private charity was most robust during the laissez-faire era; governmental welfare expansion coincided with decline in private charitable activities.

The Negative Income Tax

If income falls below exemptions/deductions, the person receives a subsidy rather than paying tax. At 50% subsidy rate with $600 exemption: person with no income receives $300. An extra dollar earned always means more money available—work incentive is preserved.

Cost analysis (1961): Government spent ~$33 billion on direct welfare. This could provide $6,000 per unit to the lowest 10%, or $2,000 per unit to the lowest third. Even supplementing just the lowest 20% would cost less than half of current spending. Current programs far exceed what is needed to alleviate poverty.

Two principles: help people as people, not as members of occupational or age groups; operate through the market, not distort it. The major danger: the system converts from majority helping an unfortunate minority to majority imposing taxes for its own benefit.

Liberal vs. Egalitarian

The liberal believes in equal rights and equal opportunity, not material equality of outcome. The egalitarian goes further: defending taking from some to give to others on grounds of “justice.” At this point, equality conflicts with freedom—one cannot be both an egalitarian and a liberal.

Chapter XIII: Conclusion

Friedman catalogs reforms that produced effects opposite to those intended:

  • Railroad regulation: quickly became an instrument for railroads to protect themselves from competition
  • Income tax: enacted at low rates to redistribute, became a facade of loopholes; a flat 23.5% yields the same revenue
  • Monetary reform: the Fed bears primary responsibility for converting a serious contraction into the Great Depression
  • Agricultural programs: wasted public funds, distorted resources, did little for impecunious farmers
  • Public housing: worsened housing conditions, contributed to juvenile delinquency
  • Social security: enacted to eliminate need for direct relief, yet relief rolls grow

The central defect is not faulty details but seeking through government to force people to act against their own immediate interests. This is countered by the strongest creative force: millions of individuals promoting their own interests and living by their own values.

“Concentrated power is not rendered harmless by the good intentions of those who create it.”

Two threats to freedom: the external (hostile foreign powers) and the internal—far more subtle—men of good intentions impatient with the slowness of persuasion. Freedom can be preserved if we awake to the threat and persuade fellow citizens that free institutions offer a surer route than the coercive power of the state.

Dicey: “The beneficial effect of State intervention, especially in the form of legislation, is direct, immediate, and visible, whilst its evil effects are gradual and indirect, and lie out of sight… The mere decline, therefore, of faith in self-help—and that such a decline has taken place is certain—is of itself sufficient to account for the growth of legislation tending towards socialism.”

Capitalism and Freedom · Milton Friedman · 1962 · 224 pages

Based on lectures delivered at Wabash College in 1956, this book argues that competitive capitalism is both a system of economic freedom and an indispensable condition for political freedom. Friedman systematically examines monetary policy, fiscal policy, education, discrimination, occupational licensing, income distribution, and social welfare—proposing market-based alternatives to government intervention in each domain.
