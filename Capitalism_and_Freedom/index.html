Key Takeaways

  1. Economic and political freedom are inseparable: No society with broad political freedom has existed without a substantially free market. Capitalism is necessary—though not sufficient—for political freedom. Democratic socialism is a contradiction in terms.
  2. Government is the greatest threat to freedom: The concentration of power, even by well-intentioned people, is inherently dangerous. Men of good will seeking to reform others through state power pose a subtler threat than external enemies.
  3. Markets enable “unanimity without conformity”: The market allows each person to get what they want without forcing agreement, while political channels require yes/no decisions that strain social cohesion.
  4. The Federal Reserve caused the Great Depression: The Fed’s failure to provide liquidity during bank runs (1930–33) converted a severe but manageable contraction into the worst economic catastrophe in American history. The money stock fell by one-third.
  5. Rules beat discretion in monetary policy: A legislated rule requiring steady 3–5% annual growth in the money stock would prevent both inflationary and deflationary disasters better than human judgment.
  6. School vouchers would help the poor most: Government should subsidize education through vouchers redeemable at any school, not operate schools directly. The current system stratifies education by neighborhood wealth.
  7. Occupational licensing is medieval guild regulation reborn: Licensing invariably serves producer interests, not consumer protection. The AMA functions as the strongest trade union in America.
  8. The Keynesian multiplier is empirically wrong: Government deficit spending adds roughly $1 of income per $1 spent—no multiplier effect—because it crowds out private spending dollar-for-dollar.
  9. Floating exchange rates preserve freedom: Fixed rates lead inevitably to exchange controls, import rationing, and authoritarian economic management. Floating rates are the only freedom-preserving adjustment mechanism.
  10. Graduated income taxes create arbitrary inequality: A flat 23.5% rate would yield the same revenue as the 20–91% graduated structure. High rates protect existing wealth from newcomer competition through avoidance devices.
  11. The negative income tax is superior to welfare: A single program providing cash subsidies to those below an income floor would replace dozens of inefficient programs at lower cost while preserving work incentives.
  12. Discrimination is a “taste” best fought by markets, not law: The impersonal market punishes discriminators with higher costs. Fair employment laws interfere with contract freedom and are identical in principle to laws requiring discrimination.
  13. Corporate “social responsibility” is subversive: In a free economy, business has one responsibility—earn profits within rules of open competition. The social responsibility doctrine turns businessmen into unelected civil servants.
  14. Reform programs consistently produce opposite effects: Railroad regulation protected railroads, public housing worsened housing, Social Security didn’t eliminate relief, agricultural programs didn’t help poor farmers.
  15. Ideas lying around determine crisis responses: The function of intellectual work is to keep alternatives alive until “the politically impossible becomes politically inevitable.”

Contrarian Ideas

  1. The Hollywood blacklist proved markets protect freedom: At least 15% of films were written by blacklisted writers under pseudonyms because producers had a profit motive to buy the best scripts regardless of politics.
  2. School vouchers would reduce, not increase, class segregation: Current public schools are stratified by neighborhood wealth; vouchers would give poor families genuine choice. Chicago’s legally integrated schools are as segregated as Southern ones.
  3. Medical licensing reduces both quantity and quality of care: It blocks entry, creates inferior alternative professions (osteopathy, chiropractic), restricts experimentation, and makes malpractice suits harder.
  4. Roosevelt’s gold nationalization was morally equivalent to Castro’s land seizure: Both confiscated private property at artificially low prices—“no difference in principle.”
  5. Fair employment laws and segregation laws are identical in principle: Both use state power to override voluntary contracts based on racial characteristics. The ACLU position—defending free speech but not free hiring—is “utterly contradictory.”
  6. Compulsory Social Security is paternalistic dictatorship: If a man knowingly prefers to live for today and chooses a penurious old age, “by what right do we prevent him?”
  7. Corporate charity undermines capitalism: Tax-deductible corporate contributions let managers dispose of stockholders’ money, realizing the “separation of ownership and control” that critics attack.
  8. The Fed era is more unstable than the pre-Fed era: The three worst contractions in American history (1920–21, 1929–33, 1937–38) all occurred under the Federal Reserve and were directly caused by its actions.
  9. Unilateral tariff elimination helps the eliminating country: Reciprocal negotiation fosters the false view that tariffs help the imposing country. The U.S. should follow Britain’s corn law repeal.
  10. Marx accepted capitalist ethics: The claim that labor is “exploited” only follows if you accept the capitalist principle that factors are entitled to what they produce—the very ethic Marx purported to reject.

Author’s Key Proposals

  1. Monetary rule: Legislate steady 3–5% annual money stock growth; abolish Federal Reserve discretion entirely.
  2. School vouchers: Replace government-operated schools with vouchers redeemable at any approved institution—public, private, religious, or for-profit.
  3. Floating exchange rates: Abandon fixed rates and gold convertibility for freely floating currencies to prevent the authoritarian spiral of exchange controls.
  4. Unilateral free trade: Eliminate all tariffs and quotas over ten years without waiting for reciprocity—following Britain’s corn law repeal.
  5. Flat income tax: Replace graduated rates (20–91%) with a flat rate (~23.5%) on broadly defined income; eliminate loopholes, deductions, and special treatment.
  6. Abolish corporate income tax: Require corporations to attribute undistributed earnings to stockholders for individual taxation, invigorating capital markets.
  7. Negative income tax: Replace all welfare programs with a single cash transfer to those below an income floor, preserving work incentives.
  8. Equity investment in education: Government finances vocational/professional training in exchange for a percentage of future earnings above a base amount.
  9. Eliminate occupational licensing: Replace licensing with voluntary certification; abolish medical licensure specifically.
  10. End fourteen government programs: Agricultural supports, tariffs, rent controls, minimum wage, conscription, FCC broadcast control, public housing, compulsory Social Security, and six others.

Prefaces (2002, 1982, 1962)

The Triumph of Economic Freedom (1962–2002)

Between 1962 and the 1980s, the climate of opinion shifted dramatically from hostility toward free-market ideas to respectability. Government spending nonetheless exploded: from 26% of national income in 1956 to 36% by 2000. Thatcher and Reagan rode the wave of changing opinion but couldn’t reverse spending growth.

The 1989–92 collapse of the Soviet Union was the decisive natural experiment. Smaller-scale precedents had already confirmed the verdict: Hong Kong/Taiwan vs. mainland China, West Germany vs. East Germany, South Korea vs. North Korea. Hayek’s Road to Serfdom was vindicated.

China under Deng Xiaoping introduced market reforms that made Chinese citizens “freer and more prosperous than under Mao—freer in every dimension except the political.” The East Asian tigers proved market-oriented development works where central planning and foreign aid failed.

One Major Revision Proposed

Replace the economic/political freedom dichotomy with a trichotomy: economic freedom, civil freedom, and political freedom. Hong Kong before the handover showed that economic freedom is necessary for civil and political freedom, but political freedom is not necessary for economic and civil freedom.

What Changed? Experience, Not Theory (1982 Preface)

The intellectual climate shifted not because of new arguments but because reality discredited the alternatives: Russia and China “gone sour,” British Fabian socialism lost influence, Vietnam disillusioned Kennedy/Johnson supporters, Great Society programs “were turning to ashes,” and inflation hit pocketbooks.

Barry Goldwater was overwhelmingly defeated in 1964; Ronald Reagan overwhelmingly victorious in 1980—“two men with essentially the same program and the same message.” The difference was the climate of opinion.

“Actions that are taken depend on the ideas that are lying around. That, I believe, is our basic function: to develop alternatives to existing policies, to keep them alive and available until the politically impossible becomes politically inevitable.”

The book originated in lectures at Wabash College (June 1956) sponsored by the Volker Foundation. Intellectual debts owed to Frank Knight, Henry Simons, Lloyd Mints, Aaron Director, Friedrich Hayek, and George Stigler at the University of Chicago.

Introduction

Kennedy’s famous line reflects paternalism in both halves: “what your country can do for you” treats government as patron and citizen as ward; “what you can do for your country” treats government as master and citizen as servant. A free man sees government as instrumentality—the true question is “what can I and my compatriots do through government” while preventing it from becoming a Frankenstein.

Two Constitutional Principles for Limited Government

Limited scope: Government protects freedom, enforces contracts, fosters competition—but any expansion is fraught with danger. Dispersed power: Better at county than state, state than federal. Local dissatisfaction allows mobility between communities; federal-level dissatisfaction leaves “few alternatives in this world of jealous nations.”

Great advances never came from centralized government: Columbus, Newton, Shakespeare, Edison, Florence Nightingale—all were products of individual genius, minority views, and a social climate permitting variety. Government uniform standards would “replace progress by stagnation, substitute uniform mediocrity for variety essential for experimentation.”

“Our problem today is not to enforce conformity; it is rather that we are threatened with an excess of conformity. Our problem is to foster diversity.”

Chapter I: The Relation between Economic Freedom and Political Freedom

The common belief that politics and economics are separate is a delusion. Democratic socialism is impossible: a socialist society cannot guarantee individual freedom. Economic freedom is both an end in itself and a means to political freedom.

No society with a large measure of political freedom has existed without something comparable to a free market. Capitalism is necessary but not sufficient: Fascist Italy, Spain, pre-war Japan, and Tsarist Russia had private enterprise without political freedom. Yet even in those societies, citizens had more freedom than in totalitarian states.

Economic Power as Check on Political Power

Competitive capitalism separates economic from political power, enabling each to offset the other. Economic power can be widely dispersed (many millionaires), but political power is harder to decentralize. If economic power joins political power, concentration is almost inevitable.

Freedom to Advocate Radical Change

Can men openly advocate capitalism in a socialist society? All jobs and resources are under political authority. The crucial role of inequality of wealth and the patron: radical movements have always been funded by a few wealthy converts (Engels, Vanderbilt Field, Anita McCormick Blaine). The market enables financing through profitability rather than ideological persuasion.

From 1933 to WWII, Winston Churchill was not permitted to speak on British radio because the BBC was a government monopoly and his views on Hitler’s Germany were “too controversial.”

The Hollywood Blacklist

The formal blacklist failed precisely because the market made it costly: at least 15% of Hollywood films were written by blacklisted members under pseudonyms. Producer Frank King: “We have an obligation to our stockholders to buy the best script we can.” The impersonal market separates economic activities from political views—no one who buys bread knows the farmer’s politics.

Chapter II: The Role of Government in a Free Society

The market permits “unanimity without conformity”—a system of proportional representation where each person gets what they want. Political channels require substantial conformity: decisions are yes/no, and using them strains social cohesion. Fundamental value differences are seldom resolved at the ballot box—“only by conflict.”

Government as Rule-Maker and Umpire

Society is like a game requiring acceptance of rules and an umpire. Government’s basic roles: modify rules, mediate differences, enforce compliance. Absolute freedom is impossible with imperfect men—freedoms can conflict.

Monopoly: Three Bad Alternatives

When markets can’t function due to monopoly, three alternatives exist: private monopoly, public monopoly, or public regulation. All are bad. Friedman concludes private monopoly may be the least evil because monopoly conditions change—and public regulation outlives its justification.

The ICC was justified when railroads had natural monopoly. After roads and air transport emerged, the ICC didn’t disappear—it became an agency protecting railroads from trucks, then protecting existing truckers from new entrants.

Neighborhood Effects

Actions imposing costs/benefits on third parties justify government intervention—but the argument cuts both ways. City parks justify public provision (impossible to charge beneficiaries); national parks do not (entrances are few, tolls feasible). Every government intervention introduces new neighborhood effects—plus the critical one of threatening individual freedom.

Fourteen Unjustifiable Activities

Agricultural price supports, tariffs, output controls, rent/price controls, minimum wage, detailed industry regulation (ICC, banking), FCC broadcast control, compulsory Social Security, occupational licensing, public housing subsidies, peacetime conscription, national parks, prohibition on private mail carrying, and publicly owned toll roads.

Chapter III: The Control of Money

An automatic commodity standard (gold) is neither feasible nor desirable: it requires enormous resource costs and the mythology needed to make it work no longer exists. Even the 19th-century gold standard was highly managed—gold accounted for only 10–20% of the U.S. money stock from 1879–1913.

The Federal Reserve’s Failure

Comparing 1879–1914 (pre-Fed) to 1914 onward reveals the post-Fed era as decidedly more unstable. The three most severe contractions—1920–21, 1929–33, 1937–38—all occurred under the Fed and were directly attributable to its actions.

The Great Depression: A Man-Made Catastrophe

The 1929 stock market crash was neither the start nor the major cause. November 1930: The failure of the Bank of the United States (over $200 million in deposits; its name led many to think it was official) triggered runs. The Fed “failed the test miserably”—doing nothing to provide liquidity.

September 1931: When Britain went off gold, the Fed raised the discount rate more sharply than in its entire history—arresting the gold drain but triggering spectacular bank failures. One in ten banks suspended operations.

From July 1929 to March 1933, the money stock fell by one-third. It is “literally inconceivable” that money income could have declined by over one-half and prices by over one-third without this monetary collapse.

Rules Instead of Authorities

The case for legislative rules parallels the First Amendment: voting on free speech case-by-case, the majority would deny it in most instances. But voting on free speech as a bundle, an overwhelming majority supports it. The same logic applies to monetary policy.

“What we need is not a skillful monetary driver of the economic vehicle continuously turning the steering wheel to adjust to the unexpected irregularities of the route, but some means of keeping the monetary passenger who is in the back seat as ballast from occasionally leaning over and giving the steering wheel a jerk that threatens to send the car off the road.”

Friedman’s proposed rule: Legislate that the total money stock must grow at a specified annual rate of 3–5%. The precise rate matters far less than making a definite choice and sticking to it.

Chapter IV: International Financial and Trade Arrangements

The most serious short-run threat to economic freedom is adopting exchange controls to “solve” balance of payments problems. Starting with foreign exchange controls leads inevitably to import rationing and domestic production controls. Full exchange controls were invented by Hjalmar Schacht in the early years of the Nazi regime.

Gold as Commodity Price Support

U.S. gold policy functions as a price support program, not a monetary system. When gold was set at $35/ounce in 1934, it was above market price—producing a “surplus.” As prices doubled and tripled while gold stayed at $35, the “shortage” appeared—exactly as rent ceilings produce housing shortages.

The 1933–34 nationalization of private gold holdings was a fundamental departure from liberal principles. There is “no difference in principle between this nationalization of gold at artificially low price and Fidel Castro’s nationalization of land and factories at artificially low price.”

Four Mechanisms for Adjustment

(1) Reserves: Drawing down gold—clearly temporary. (2) Domestic deflation: Painful and politically unacceptable. (3) Direct controls: The authoritarian path. (4) Exchange rate adjustment: The only fully automatic, freedom-preserving mechanism.

Freely floating exchange rates would prevent balance-of-payments problems from arising and make transparent the fallacy of protectionist “low wages” arguments. Japanese workers’ lower wages reflect lower productivity; tariffs lower both countries’ living standards.

Unilateral Free Trade

Reciprocal tariff negotiation is the wrong procedure: it fosters the erroneous view that tariffs help the imposing country. The U.S. should follow Britain’s corn law repeal and unilaterally eliminate trade barriers over ten years.

Chapter V: Fiscal Policy

Three justifications for government spending have each failed: pump priming (abandoned after 1937–38), secular stagnation (discredited but programs persist), and the balance wheel (spending rises in recessions, never falls in expansions).

Ironically, the most unstable component of postwar national income is federal expenditure itself—and its instability doesn’t even offset other movements.

Demolishing the Keynesian Multiplier

The “widely held view” that deficit spending is necessarily expansionary “cannot be demonstrated true by logical considerations alone, has never been documented by empirical evidence, and is inconsistent with relevant empirical evidence.”

Problem 1: If government provides something people were buying privately, the spending adds nothing—it just diverts private expenditure. Problem 2: The Keynesian multiplier of 3 works only under two extreme assumptions no established economist accepts.

Friedman’s empirical finding: a $100 increase in government expenditure adds “just about $100 to income”—no multiplier. Private expenditures are unchanged and likely smaller in real terms.

The proper fiscal policy: plan expenditures based on what the community wants done through government. Set tax rates to cover spending on average. Avoid erratic changes. The government should be a stable participant, not a “skillful driver.”

Chapter VI: The Role of Government in Education

Two grounds justify government involvement in education: neighborhood effects (democratic society requires minimum literacy and shared values) and paternalism (children cannot make decisions for themselves). But neither justifies government administration of schools.

The Voucher System

Parents receive vouchers redeemable at any approved school—public, private, religious, for-profit. Government’s role is limited to setting minimum standards. Precedents: the GI Bill, British local authority payments, French state subsidies for non-state schools.

The argument that private schools exacerbate class distinctions “points in the opposite direction.” Low-income families face a double penalty: they cannot afford expensive neighborhoods with “good” schools AND pay for private alternatives. Vouchers would give poor families genuine choice.

“If one were to seek deliberately to devise a system of recruiting and paying teachers calculated to repel the imaginative and daring and self-confident and to attract the dull and mediocre and uninspiring, he could hardly do better than imitate the system of requiring teaching certificates and enforcing standard salary structures.”

Vocational Education: The Equity Investment Proposal

Vocational training is investment in human capital with returns captured by the individual—no neighborhood effects justify subsidy. But human beings cannot serve as collateral in a non-slave state, creating a capital market imperfection.

Friedman’s solution: Government finances training in exchange for a specified percentage of future earnings above a base amount. This makes equality of opportunity a reality “not by impeding competition, destroying incentive, dealing with symptoms… but by strengthening competition, making incentives effective, eliminating causes of inequality.”

Chapter VII: Capitalism and Discrimination

Capitalism’s development coincided with major reductions in economic discrimination. Jews survived medieval persecution by operating in market sectors; post-Civil War Southern states imposed many legal restrictions on Negroes but never barriers to property ownership—the strong belief in private property overrode the desire to discriminate.

How Free Markets Fight Discrimination

The bread purchaser doesn’t know the farmer’s race or religion. A businessman expressing preferences unrelated to productive efficiency imposes higher costs on himself; discriminatory individuals are driven out by those without such preferences.

Friedman defines discrimination as a “taste”—the appropriate recourse is persuasion, not coercion. Fair employment laws clearly interfere with freedom of voluntary contracts.

“Is it any more desirable that momentary majorities decide what characteristics are relevant to employment than that they decide what speech is appropriate?”

Segregation in Schooling

Because schooling is government-operated, the state must choose between enforced segregation or enforced integration—both bad solutions. The answer: school vouchers that eliminate government operation. Virginia adopted a voucher plan to avoid integration, but the first voucher request was a parent transferring a child from a segregated to an integrated school because it was educationally better.

“The difference between the result and the intention is one of the primary justifications of a free society.”

Chapter VIII: Monopoly and the Social Responsibility of Business and Labor

The most important fact about enterprise monopoly: its relative unimportance from an economy-wide view. Four million separate enterprises, 400,000 new ones born each year, one-fifth of workers self-employed. Nutter and Stigler studies show at most one-quarter of the private sector is monopolistic.

Monopoly in Labor

Unions raised wage rates for 10–15% of workers by 10–15%, meaning 85–90% of workers had wages reduced by about 4%. Since unions have generally been strongest among already high-paid groups, they have made working-class incomes more unequal.

Three Sources of Monopoly

  1. Technical (natural monopoly): Friedman advocates private unregulated monopoly as least evil—dynamic changes undermine natural monopoly over time, and regulation is exceedingly difficult to reverse.
  2. Government assistance (most important source): Tariffs, corporate tax structure encouraging earnings retention, special union immunities from antitrust. Britain was protected from monopoly by free trade; monopoly became serious only after free trade was abandoned.
  3. Private collusion: Generally unstable without government assistance. Takes only one “chiseler” (actually a public benefactor) to break a cartel.

Social Responsibility of Business

In a free economy, business has only one social responsibility: increase profits within rules of open competition, without deception or fraud. The social responsibility doctrine is fundamentally subversive—if businessmen have obligations beyond profits, they become unelected civil servants.

“By pursuing his own interest, he frequently promotes that of the society more effectually than when he really intends to promote it. I have never known much good done by those who affected to trade for the public good.” —Adam Smith

Corporate charitable contributions are inappropriate: the corporation is an instrument of its stockholders. Permitting corporate charitable deductions moves toward a corporate state.

Chapter IX: Occupational Licensure

The overthrow of the medieval guild system was an indispensable early step in the rise of Western freedom. Recent retrogression: by 1952, more than 80 separate occupations licensed by state law—from pharmacists to egg graders, tree surgeons, and hypertrichologists.

Three Levels of Restriction

Registration: List names in official register; no denial of right to practice. Certification: Government certifies skills but doesn’t prevent uncertified practice (e.g., CPA). Licensing: Must obtain license or face fine/jail. Friedman finds it difficult to see any case where licensing rather than certification is justified.

Medical Licensure: The AMA as Trade Union

The AMA is “perhaps the strongest trade union in the United States.” The Council on Medical Education approves medical schools; state approved-school lists are identical with the Council’s list. In the 1930s, the Council wrote to schools that they were admitting too many students; every school reduced admissions.

After Hitler came to power (1933), the number of foreign-trained physicians admitted to the U.S. in the five years after was the same as in the five years before—despite a tremendous outflow of physicians from Germany. The threat of additional physicians led to stringent tightening of requirements.

Does Licensing Raise Standards?

Friedman argues it does not: (1) it creates alternative professions of possibly lower quality (osteopathy, chiropractic), (2) fewer physicians means more untrained practice, (3) restricting to orthodox members reduces experimentation, (4) monopoly makes malpractice suits harder.

“I am myself persuaded that licensure has reduced both the quantity and quality of medical practice… I conclude that licensure should be eliminated as a requirement for the practice of medicine.”

Without AMA monopoly, “medical teams” (department stores of medicine) would have developed—long-lived organizations with strong reputational incentives, combining practitioners of different skill levels.

Chapter X: The Distribution of Income

The ethical principle of free market society: “To each according to what he and the instruments he owns produces.” Payment according to product is instrumentally necessary for efficient allocation—unless each receives what they add, mutually beneficial exchanges won’t occur.

Inequality from Tastes for Uncertainty

If individuals with equal endowments voluntarily enter a lottery with unequal prizes, the resulting inequality is required to let them make the most of their initial equality. The girl choosing to become a movie actress rather than a civil servant is deliberately entering a lottery.

Inherited Wealth vs. Inherited Talent

The widely-argued distinction is untenable. Is there greater ethical justification for high returns to someone who inherits a peculiar voice than to someone who inherits property? A parent can pass wealth by financing CPA training, setting up a business, or creating a trust fund—the income is classified differently but the effect is identical.

Facts of Income Distribution

The more capitalistic a country, the smaller the fraction paid for capital and the larger for human services. In India/Egypt, about half is property income; in the U.S., roughly one-fifth. Capitalism produces less inequality than alternative systems and substitutes temporary for permanent inequality.

“The chief characteristic of progress and development over the past century is that it has freed the masses from backbreaking toil and has made available to them products and services that were formerly the monopoly of the upper classes.”

Flat Tax Proposal

A flat 23.5% rate on presently defined taxable income would yield the same revenue as the current 20–91% graduated structure. Graduated taxes are less burdensome on being wealthy than becoming wealthy—they protect existing wealth holders from newcomer competition.

Chapter XI: Social Welfare Measures

Public Housing

Dwelling units destroyed far exceeded new units constructed—net effect raised persons per dwelling unit. Income limitations concentrated “broken” families; schools can absorb a few problem children but not large numbers. Cash grants would have spread families through the community, avoiding concentration.

Minimum Wage Laws

Effects precisely opposite those intended: the state can legislate a minimum wage but cannot require employers to hire at that wage. Those rendered unemployed are precisely those who can least afford to lose income.

Old Age and Survivor’s Insurance (OASI)

Three analytically separable elements: (1) compulsory purchase of annuities, (2) nationalization of annuity provision, (3) income redistribution. All three are unjustifiable.

The paternalistic justification: people are shortsighted, “we” know better than “they” what is in their own good. The thoroughgoing paternalist is an opponent on grounds of principle—he basically believes in dictatorship, benevolent perhaps, but dictatorship nonetheless.

“Humility is the distinguishing virtue of the believer in freedom; arrogance, of the paternalist.”

If only 1% would become public charges without Social Security, why restrict freedom of the other 99%? The Great Depression that made the argument plausible was itself a consequence of government mismanagement.

Chapter XII: The Alleviation of Poverty

Western countries have enormously reduced absolute poverty through free enterprise over two centuries. Private charity was most robust during the laissez-faire era; governmental welfare expansion coincided with decline in private charitable activities.

The Negative Income Tax

If income falls below exemptions/deductions, the person receives a subsidy rather than paying. At 50% subsidy rate with $600 exemption: person with no income receives $300. An extra dollar earned always means more money available—preserving work incentives.

Government spent ~$33 billion on direct welfare programs (1961). $33 billion could provide $6,000 per unit to the lowest 10%. Current programs far exceeded what was needed to alleviate poverty. The negative income tax could replace the entire host of special measures at lower cost.

Liberal vs. Egalitarian Philosophy

The liberal believes in equal rights and equal opportunity, not material equality of outcome. The egalitarian defends taking from some to give to others on grounds of “justice.” At this point, equality conflicts with freedom—one cannot be both an egalitarian and a liberal.

Chapter XIII: Conclusion

Friedman catalogs reforms that produced effects opposite to those intended: railroad regulation protected railroads; the income tax became a facade of loopholes; the Federal Reserve produced the worst banking panic in American history; agricultural programs distorted resource use without helping poor farmers; public housing worsened conditions for the poor.

The central defect is not faulty details but seeking through government to force people to act against their own immediate interests. This is countered by one of the strongest creative forces: millions of individuals promoting their own interests and living by their own values.

“Concentrated power is not rendered harmless by the good intentions of those who create it.”

Two threats to freedom: the external threat from hostile foreign powers, and the far more subtle internal threat from men of good intentions who wish to use state power to reform us, impatient with the slowness of persuasion.

A. V. Dicey: “The beneficial effect of State intervention, especially in the form of legislation, is direct, immediate, and visible, whilst its evil effects are gradual and indirect, and lie out of sight… The mere decline, therefore, of faith in self-help—and that such a decline has taken place is certain—is of itself sufficient to account for the growth of legislation tending towards socialism.”

But there is hope: “There is much ruin in a nation” (Adam Smith). The basic structure of values and interwoven network of free institutions will withstand much. Freedom can be preserved if we awake to the threat and persuade fellow citizens that free institutions offer a surer route to their ends than the coercive power of the state.

Capitalism and Freedom · Milton Friedman · 1962 · 224 pages

Based on lectures delivered at Wabash College in 1956, this foundational text of free-market liberalism argues that competitive capitalism is both a system of economic freedom and an indispensable condition for political freedom. Friedman proposes radical reforms—school vouchers, floating exchange rates, a flat tax, the negative income tax, and the abolition of occupational licensing—many of which eventually became mainstream policy decades later.
