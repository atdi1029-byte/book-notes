## Prefaces (2002, 1982, 1962)

### The Triumph of Economic Freedom (1962-2002)
- Between 1962 publication and the 1980s, the climate of opinion shifted dramatically from hostility toward free-market ideas to respectability. Government spending nonetheless exploded: from 26% of national income in 1956 to 36% by 2000. Thatcher and Reagan rode the wave of changing opinion but couldn't reverse spending growth.
- The 1989-92 collapse of the Soviet Union was the decisive natural experiment. Smaller-scale precedents had already confirmed the verdict: Hong Kong/Taiwan vs. mainland China, West Germany vs. East Germany, South Korea vs. North Korea. Hayek's *Road to Serfdom* was vindicated.
- China under Deng Xiaoping introduced market reforms that privatized agriculture, dramatically increased output, and made Chinese citizens "freer and more prosperous than under Mao—freer in every dimension except the political." The East Asian tigers proved market-oriented development works where central planning and foreign aid failed.

### One Major Revision Proposed
- Replace the economic/political freedom dichotomy with a trichotomy: **economic freedom, civil freedom, and political freedom**. Hong Kong before the handover showed that economic freedom is necessary for civil and political freedom, but political freedom is not necessary for economic and civil freedom—and under some circumstances political freedom can inhibit economic and civic freedom.

### What Changed? Experience, Not Theory
- The intellectual climate shifted not because of new arguments but because reality discredited the alternatives: Russia and China "gone sour," British Fabian socialism lost influence, Vietnam disillusioned Kennedy/Johnson supporters, Great Society programs "were turning to ashes," and inflation hit pocketbooks.
- Barry Goldwater was overwhelmingly defeated in 1964; Ronald Reagan overwhelmingly victorious in 1980—"two men with essentially the same program and the same message." The difference was the climate of opinion.

### The Role of Ideas in Crisis
- Books "keep options open until circumstances make change necessary." There is enormous inertia—a tyranny of the status quo—in social arrangements. When crisis produces change, "actions that are taken depend on the ideas that are lying around." The core function of intellectual work is to "develop alternatives to existing policies, to keep them alive and available until the politically impossible becomes politically inevitable."

### Draft Debate Anecdote
- At a late-1960s debate at University of Wisconsin, Friedman's opponent Leon Keyserling read Chapter 2's list of unjustified government activities to mock Friedman as reactionary. When he reached point 11—conscription—the audience burst into applause and he lost them. Conscription was the only item from the list of 14 actually eliminated. Most other items moved further from Friedman's principles.

### Origins and Acknowledgments
- The book originated in lectures at Wabash College (June 1956) sponsored by the Volker Foundation. Intellectual debts owed to Frank Knight, Henry Simons, Lloyd Mints, Aaron Director, Friedrich Hayek, and George Stigler at the University of Chicago. Wife Rose Friedman assembled the manuscript; children Janet and David's "willingness to accept nothing on faith" forced clearer exposition.

## Introduction

### Kennedy and the Free Man's Perspective
- Kennedy's famous line reflects paternalism in both halves: "what your country can do for you" treats government as patron and citizen as ward; "what you can do for your country" treats government as master and citizen as servant. A free man sees government as instrumentality—the true question is "what can I and my compatriots do **through government**" while preventing it from becoming a Frankenstein.

### Two Constitutional Principles for Limited Government
- **Limited scope**: Government protects freedom, enforces contracts, fosters competition—but any expansion is fraught with danger. Relying primarily on voluntary cooperation ensures the private sector checks government power.
- **Dispersed power**: Better at county than state, state than federal. Local dissatisfaction allows mobility between communities; federal-level dissatisfaction leaves "few alternatives in this world of jealous nations." Centralization's attraction—enabling more effective "good" legislation—ignores that those controlling power today may not tomorrow, and one person's good is another's harm.

### Freedom as the Engine of Progress
- Great advances never came from centralized government: Columbus, Newton, Shakespeare, Edison, Florence Nightingale—all were products of individual genius, minority views, and a social climate permitting variety. Government uniform standards in housing, nutrition, or schooling could improve many individuals' living at any moment, but would "replace progress by stagnation, substitute uniform mediocrity for variety essential for experimentation."

### Liberalism Reclaimed
- The book's major theme: competitive capitalism as both a system of economic freedom and a necessary condition for political freedom. The term "liberalism" rightfully belongs to this viewpoint. 19th-century liberalism emphasized freedom, laissez faire, and free trade; 20th-century "liberalism" shifted to welfare and equality as alternatives to freedom, reviving the state paternalism classical liberalism opposed.

## Chapter I: The Relation between Economic Freedom and Political Freedom

### The Indivisibility of Economic and Political Freedom
- The common belief that politics and economics are separate is a delusion. Democratic socialism is impossible: a socialist society cannot guarantee individual freedom. Economic freedom is both an end in itself (comparable in direct importance to political freedom) and a means to political freedom.
- Examples of economic freedom being indistinguishable from political freedom: British exchange controls preventing foreign travel, compulsory retirement programs consuming 10% of income, occupational licensing restricting career choice, the Amish having livestock sold at auction to satisfy Social Security levies, a Californian jailed for selling Alka Seltzer below "fair trade" price.

### Historical Evidence
- No society with a large measure of political freedom has existed without something comparable to a free market. The typical state of mankind is tyranny, servitude, and misery. The 19th and early 20th century Western world was a striking exception, and political freedom came with free market development.
- Capitalism is necessary but not sufficient for political freedom: Fascist Italy, Spain, pre-war Japan, and Tsarist Russia had private enterprise without political freedom. Yet even in those societies, citizens had more freedom than in totalitarian states like Soviet Russia or Nazi Germany.

### Economic Power as Check on Political Power
- Competitive capitalism separates economic from political power, enabling each to offset the other. The market eliminates a major source of coercive power by removing economic organization from political control. Economic power can be widely dispersed (many millionaires in one economy), but political power is harder to decentralize (a fixed total to distribute). If economic power joins political power, concentration is almost inevitable.

### Freedom to Advocate Radical Change
- A critical test of freedom: can men openly advocate capitalism in a socialist society? In socialism, all jobs are under political authority, and advocacy requires financing—renting halls, printing pamphlets, buying radio time. But all resources are government-controlled. The crucial role of **inequality of wealth and the patron**: radical movements have always been funded by a few wealthy converts (Engels, Vanderbilt Field, Anita McCormick Blaine). The market breaks the vicious circle by enabling financing through profitability rather than ideological persuasion.

### Winston Churchill and the BBC
- From 1933 to WWII, Churchill was not permitted to speak on British radio because the BBC was a government monopoly and his views on Hitler's Germany were "too controversial." A leading citizen, MP, and former cabinet minister was silenced by government broadcasting control.

### The Hollywood Blacklist
- The formal blacklist of suspected Communist writers was "unfree" as a collusive arrangement using coercive means. But it failed precisely because the market made it costly: at least 15% of Hollywood films were written by blacklisted members under pseudonyms. Producer Frank King: "We have an obligation to our stockholders to buy the best script we can." Commercial incentive protected individuals' freedom to work.

### McCarthyism and the Private Market
- The Fifth Amendment appeal would have been "hollow mockery" without alternative to government employment. The private market, especially its most competitive sectors (small business, trade, farming), provided refuge. The impersonal market separates economic activities from political views—no one who buys bread knows the farmer's politics.

## Chapter II: The Role of Government in a Free Society

### Market vs. Political Channels
- The market permits "unanimity without conformity"—a system of proportional representation where each person gets what they want. Political channels require substantial conformity: decisions are yes/no, and using them strains social cohesion. The wider the range of activities covered by the market, the fewer issues require political decisions. Fundamental value differences are seldom resolved at the ballot box—"only by conflict."

### Majority Rule as Expedient
- Unanimity is the ideal but impractical. Majority rule is an expedient, not a principle. For trivial matters, bare plurality suffices; for deeply felt issues, even bare majority won't do. Constitutional provisions represent self-denying ordinances to refrain from majority rule on certain issues—reached consensus about acceptable means.

### Government as Rule-Maker and Umpire
- Society is like a game requiring acceptance of rules and an umpire. Government's basic roles: modify rules, mediate differences, enforce compliance. Absolute freedom is impossible with imperfect men—freedoms can conflict. Complex examples: freedom to combine vs. freedom to compete (U.S. tradition favors open entry; Continental tradition allows cartels); property rights as complex social creations (airplane rights, patents, riparian rights).

### Monopoly: Three Bad Alternatives
- When markets can't function due to monopoly, three alternatives exist: private monopoly, public monopoly, or public regulation. All are bad. Henry Simons, observing U.S. regulation, preferred public monopoly; Walter Eucken, observing German public monopoly, preferred regulation. Friedman concludes private monopoly may be the least evil because in a dynamic society, monopoly conditions change—and public regulation/monopoly outlives its justification.
- **Railroad example**: The ICC was justified when railroads had natural monopoly. After roads and air transport emerged, the ICC didn't disappear—it became an agency protecting railroads from trucks, then protecting existing truckers from new entrants.

### Neighborhood Effects
- Actions imposing costs/benefits on third parties that can't be charged justify government intervention—but the argument cuts both ways and has been used to rationalize almost every conceivable intervention. City parks justify public provision (impossible to charge beneficiaries); national parks do not (entrances are few, tolls feasible). Highways involve high collection costs for general roads (gasoline tax is cheaper), but long-distance turnpikes with limited access should be privately owned.
- Every government intervention to overcome neighborhood effects introduces new neighborhood effects by failing to charge or compensate properly—plus the critical neighborhood effect of threatening individual freedom.

### Paternalism
- Freedom is tenable only for responsible individuals. Paternalism is inescapable for madmen and children—but it's the most troublesome ground for government action because it means some decide for others. Children are "responsible individuals in embryo" whose ultimate rights must be protected. No formula tells us where to stop; we must rely on fallible judgment and free discussion.

### List of Unjustifiable Government Activities
- Fourteen current activities that cannot be justified by Friedman's principles: (1) agricultural price supports, (2) tariffs and import restrictions, (3) output controls, (4) rent and price controls, (5) minimum wage and interest rate ceilings, (6) detailed industry regulation (ICC, banking), (7) FCC control of radio/TV (implicit censorship), (8) compulsory Social Security, (9) occupational licensing beyond tax receipts, (10) public housing subsidies, (11) peacetime conscription, (12) national parks, (13) prohibition on private mail carrying, (14) publicly owned toll roads.

## Chapter III: The Control of Money

### Why Not a Gold Standard?
- An automatic commodity standard is neither feasible nor desirable: it requires enormous resource costs and the mythology needed to make it work no longer exists. Even the 19th-century gold standard was highly managed—gold accounted for only 10-20% of the U.S. money stock from 1879-1913, with the rest being silver, fiduciary currency, and bank deposits.

### The Federal Reserve's Failure
- Comparing 1879-1914 (pre-Fed) to 1914 onward reveals the post-Fed era as decidedly more unstable in money stock, prices, and output. The three most severe contractions in American history—1920-21, 1929-33, 1937-38—all occurred under the Fed and were directly attributable to its acts of commission and omission.

### The Great Depression: A Man-Made Catastrophe
- The October 1929 stock market crash was neither the start nor the major cause of the Depression. The peak of business came months earlier, partly due to tight Fed policy to curb "speculation." The first year of decline was severe but showed no signs of catastrophe.
- **November 1930**: Bank failures triggered runs on banks. The failure of the Bank of the United States (over $200 million in deposits; its name led many to think it was official) was critical. The Fed, created precisely to handle such situations, "failed the test miserably"—it did nothing to provide liquidity. This was a failure of will, not power.
- **Early 1931**: Signs of recovery appeared, but the Fed took mild deflationary action. When renewed bank failures erupted, the Fed stood idle. When Britain went off gold (September 1931), the Fed raised the discount rate more sharply than in its entire history—arresting the gold drain but triggering spectacular bank failures. One in ten banks suspended operations; deposits fell 15%.
- **1932**: A $1 billion bond purchase program slowed the decline but was too little, too late. When the Fed relapsed into passivity, collapse resumed, terminating in the Banking Holiday of 1933—every bank officially closed for over a week. The system established to prevent suspension of convertibility instead let nearly one-third of banks fail, then welcomed the most sweeping suspension in history.
- From July 1929 to March 1933, the money stock fell by one-third. It is "literally inconceivable" that money income could have declined by over one-half and prices by over one-third without this monetary collapse.

### Rules Instead of Authorities
- The case for legislative rules over discretionary authority parallels the First Amendment: voting on free speech case-by-case, the majority would deny it in most instances. But voting on free speech as a bundle, an overwhelming majority supports it—because each person feels more strongly about losing his own right in the minority than denying others' rights when in the majority. The same logic applies to monetary policy: individual decisions miss cumulative effects.
- **Price level targeting is the wrong rule**: it states objectives the Fed cannot directly achieve, leaving too much discretion.
- **Friedman's proposed rule**: Legislate that the total money stock (currency outside banks plus all bank deposits) must grow at a specified annual rate of 3-5%. The precise definition and rate matter far less than making a definite choice and sticking to it. This converts monetary policy from a threat to freedom into a pillar of free society.

## Chapter IV: International Financial and Trade Arrangements

### The Threat of Exchange Controls
- The most serious short-run threat to economic freedom is adopting exchange controls to "solve" balance of payments problems. Starting with foreign exchange controls leads inevitably to import rationing, domestic production controls, and an endless authoritarian spiral. Full exchange controls were invented by Hjalmar Schacht in the early years of the Nazi regime.

### Gold as Commodity Price Support
- The U.S. gold policy is functionally a price support program, not a monetary system. It differs from wheat supports in three ways: it pays the support price to foreign producers too, sells only to foreign purchasers, and the Treasury creates money to pay for gold (so expenditures never appear in the budget).
- When gold was set at $35/ounce in 1934, this was above market price—producing a "surplus." Now prices have doubled and tripled while gold stays at $35, creating a "shortage"—exactly as rent ceilings produce housing shortages.

### Roosevelt's Destruction of Property Rights
- The 1933-34 nationalization of private gold holdings at an artificially low price was a fundamental departure from liberal principles. There is "no difference in principle between this nationalization of gold at artificially low price and Fidel Castro's nationalization of land and factories at artificially low price." The abrogation of gold clauses in contracts—declaring valid agreements invalid for one party's benefit—was equally destructive.

### Four Mechanisms for Balance of Payments Adjustment
1. **Reserves**: Drawing down gold or foreign currency reserves—clearly temporary
2. **Domestic price adjustment**: Deflation at home to make exports cheaper—the gold standard mechanism, but painful and politically unacceptable
3. **Direct controls**: Tariffs, quotas, capital controls—the authoritarian path
4. **Exchange rate adjustment**: Letting currency prices move to clear the market—the only fully automatic, freedom-preserving mechanism

- Freely floating exchange rates would prevent balance-of-payments problems from arising, enable free trade, and make transparent the fallacy of protectionist "low wages" arguments. The exchange rate settles where U.S. exports equal imports in value. Japanese workers' lower wages reflect lower productivity; tariffs don't protect American workers—they lower both countries' living standards.

### The Case for Unilateral Free Trade
- Reciprocal tariff negotiation is the wrong procedure: it ensures slow pace and fosters the erroneous view that tariffs help the imposing country. The U.S. should follow Britain's 19th-century corn law repeal and unilaterally eliminate trade barriers—raise all quotas 20% annually, reduce all tariffs by one-tenth annually over ten years.

## Chapter V: Fiscal Policy

### Three Failed Justifications for Government Spending
1. **Pump priming** (New Deal): Temporary spending to restart the economy—abandoned after the 1937-38 contraction showed it didn't work
2. **Secular stagnation** (post-1937): Economy "mature," requiring permanent deficits—thoroughly discredited by analysis and experience, but programs enacted in its name persist
3. **Balance wheel** (recent): Government offsets private spending fluctuations—but the wheel is unbalanced: spending rises in recessions, never falls in expansions, and programs take effect after recessions pass

### The Federal Budget as Destabilizer
- Ironically, the most unstable component of postwar national income is federal expenditure itself—and its instability doesn't even offset other movements. The government should achieve reasonable stability in its own spending before lecturing the private sector.

### Demolishing the Keynesian Multiplier
- The "widely held view" that deficit spending is necessarily expansionary "cannot be demonstrated true by logical considerations alone, has never been documented by empirical evidence, and is inconsistent with relevant empirical evidence."
- **Problem 1—What does government spend on?** If government provides something people were buying privately (park admission), the spending adds nothing—it just diverts private expenditure. Only spending on something utterly useless avoids diversion, which reveals the "limited intellectual content" of make-work programs.
- **Problem 2—Where does government get the money?** To separate fiscal from monetary effects, assume government borrows (no money creation). The Keynesian multiplier of 3 works only under two extreme assumptions: either people are utterly indifferent between bonds and money ("liquidity trap"), or borrowers are so stubborn no interest rate rise cuts their spending. No established economist accepts either extreme, yet the multiplier conclusion requires one of them.
- Under the rigid quantity theory extreme (amount of money people want to hold depends only on income): government spending is completely offset by reduced private spending. Interest rates rise enough to choke off private expenditure dollar-for-dollar. Income doesn't rise at all.
- **Friedman's empirical finding**: Results "strongly suggest" the outcome is closer to the quantity theory extreme. A $100 increase in government expenditure adds "just about $100 to income"—all absorbed by government spending, with private expenditures unchanged and likely smaller in real terms due to price effects.

### The Proper Fiscal Policy
- Plan expenditures based on what the community wants done through government, with no regard to year-to-year stability. Set tax rates to cover planned spending on average. Avoid erratic changes in either. The government should be a stable, predictable participant in the economy, not a "skillful driver" constantly adjusting.

## Chapter VI: The Role of Government in Education

### Two Grounds for Intervention
- **Neighborhood effects**: Democratic society requires minimum literacy and shared values. Education benefits society beyond the individual, and specific beneficiaries cannot be identified to charge. This justifies government involvement but not government administration.
- **Paternalism**: Children cannot make decisions for themselves; the family is the basic social unit.

### The Voucher System
- Government subsidy of education is justifiable, but nationalization of the "education industry" is not. The alternative: parents receive vouchers redeemable at any approved school (public, private, religious, for-profit). Government's role is limited to setting minimum standards—content requirements and sanitary conditions. Precedents include the U.S. GI Bill, British local authority payments, and French state subsidies for non-state schools.

### Why Vouchers Would Reduce Class Distinctions
- The argument that private schools exacerbate class distinctions "points in the opposite direction." Under the current system, low-income families with gifted children face a double penalty: they cannot afford to move to expensive neighborhoods with "good" schools AND pay for private alternatives. The current system is dominated by the myth of the small town with one school for rich and poor alike, but modern urban reality stratifies education by neighborhood wealth. Vouchers would give poor families genuine choice.

### The Teacher Salary Problem
- The core issue is not that salaries are too low on average but that they are too uniform and rigid. Poor teachers are grossly overpaid, good teachers grossly underpaid. Bureaucratic civil-service salary schedules based on seniority and credentials (not merit) are the inevitable result of government administration. Competition under a voucher system would reward merit and attract ability.

### Historical Context
- A century ago, the public school system was arguably justified: transportation was limited (making the technical monopoly argument strong), mass immigration required a "melting pot" for common language and values, and no administrative machinery existed for cash grants. Today the problem is excess conformity, not insufficient conformity, and modern governmental machinery makes voucher administration feasible.

### Higher Education
- The case for nationalization is even weaker: there is less agreement on content, no technical monopoly (given distances students travel), and the "common core of values" argument doesn't hold. State universities' main competitive advantage is lower tuition through subsidies, creating "unfair" competition with private institutions. Any subsidy should go to individuals via scholarships tenable at any institution, with government schools required to compete on equal terms.

### Vocational and Professional Education: The Equity Investment Proposal
- Vocational training has no neighborhood effects comparable to general education—it is investment in human capital, with returns captured by the individual. Outright government subsidy is inappropriate because it leads to overinvestment (individuals bear no costs but reap all returns) and arbitrary income redistribution from taxpayers to the subsidized.
- **The capital market imperfection**: Human beings cannot serve as collateral in a non-slave state. Physical capital can be mortgaged and sold; human capital depends on the borrower's cooperation. Fixed money loans with only future earnings as security involve too much risk. High nominal interest rates to compensate for defaults conflict with usury laws.
- **Friedman's equity investment proposal**: Government offers to finance training in exchange for a specified percentage of future earnings above a base amount for each $1,000 received. Payments are combined with income tax at minimal administrative cost. The base is set at estimated earnings without specialized training; the fraction is calculated to make the project self-financing. This makes individuals bear the full cost while enabling those currently shut out by capital market imperfections to invest in themselves.
- The current system's restriction of expensive training to those whose parents can finance it creates "non-competing groups" sheltered from competition, perpetuating inequalities. The equity investment approach would make equality of opportunity a reality "not by impeding competition, destroying incentive, dealing with symptoms... but by strengthening competition, making incentives effective, eliminating causes of inequality."
