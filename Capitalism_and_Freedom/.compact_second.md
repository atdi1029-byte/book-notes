## Chapter VII: Capitalism and Discrimination

### Historical Evidence: Capitalism Reduces Discrimination

- Capitalism's development coincided with major reductions in economic discrimination against religious, racial, and social groups
- Contract arrangements replacing status arrangements freed serfs in the Middle Ages; Jews survived by operating in market sectors despite official persecution; Puritans and Quakers accumulated funds in markets to migrate to the New World
- Post-Civil War Southern states imposed many legal restrictions on Negroes but never barriers to property ownership -- the strong belief in private property overrode the desire to discriminate, making capitalism a major source of opportunity for Negroes
- General pattern: discrimination persists most in monopolistic areas; least discrimination where greatest competition
- Despite this record, minority groups frequently advocate fundamental alterations to capitalism, attributing residual restrictions to the market rather than recognizing it as the major factor enabling restrictions to be as small as they are

### How Free Markets Separate Economics from Discrimination

- The bread purchaser doesn't know whether wheat was grown by a white, Negro, Christian, or Jew -- the market separates economic efficiency from irrelevant characteristics
- A businessman expressing preferences unrelated to productive efficiency imposes higher costs on himself; in a free market, discriminatory individuals are driven out by those without such preferences
- Common fallacy: the person who discriminates incurs no costs and only imposes costs on others -- this is as wrong as the fallacy that a country doesn't hurt itself by imposing tariffs
- A person objecting to buying from or working alongside a Negro limits his own range of choice, must pay higher prices or receive lower returns

### Defining Discrimination as "Taste"

- Hard to see that discrimination has meaning other than a "taste" one doesn't share -- we don't call it discrimination when someone pays more to hear one singer over another
- Friedman's personal view: color of skin or religion of parents is no reason to treat someone differently; a man should be judged by what he is and does
- But in a society based on free discussion, the appropriate recourse is to persuade people their tastes are bad -- not to use coercive power to enforce one's own attitudes on others

### Fair Employment Practices Legislation (FEPC)

- FEPC clearly interferes with freedom of voluntary contracts by subjecting them to state approval
- Grocery store example: if a neighborhood has strong aversion to Negro clerks, requiring the store to hire one reduces business, imposes losses on the owner, and may close the store -- the owner may simply be transmitting community tastes, not exercising his own prejudice
- Friedman distinguishes two kinds of harm: (1) positive harm through physical force or coercion, and (2) negative harm from inability to find mutually acceptable contracts -- there is no case for using government to avoid this second type
- Slippery slope: if the state can say individuals may not discriminate by color, it can equally say they must discriminate -- Hitler's Nuremberg laws and Southern segregation laws are similar in principle to FEPC
- Any minority counting on specific majority action to defend its interests is short-sighted; without a general self-denying ordinance against government intervention, majorities will use power to give effect to their own prejudices

### Fair Speech vs. Fair Employment

- The ACLU position is utterly contradictory: favoring both free speech and fair employment laws
- The ACLU will fight to the death to protect a racist's right to preach segregation on a street corner, but will favor putting him in jail if he refuses to hire a Negro

> "Is it any more desirable that momentary majorities decide what characteristics are relevant to employment than that they decide what speech is appropriate?"

### Right-to-Work Laws

- Right-to-work laws and FEPC are identical in principle: both interfere with freedom of employment contracts
- Despite this identity, almost 100% divergence of views -- those favoring FEPC oppose right-to-work and vice versa
- As a liberal, Friedman opposes both
- In competitive markets, closed shops could arise naturally if employees preferred them; but in practice, the closed shop is almost always a symbol of monopoly power
- The right approach is more effective antitrust action in labor, not right-to-work laws

### Segregation in Schooling

- Because schooling is primarily government-operated, the state must explicitly choose between enforced segregation or enforced integration -- both are bad solutions
- The solution: school vouchers that eliminate government operation of schools and permit parents to choose
- Virginia adopted a voucher plan (to avoid compulsory integration), but Friedman predicts effects very different from intentions -- the first voucher request was a parent transferring a child from a segregated to an integrated school simply because it was educationally better

> "The difference between the result and the intention is one of the primary justifications of a free society."

- Chicago, with laws requiring integration, has public schools probably as thoroughly segregated as most Southern cities -- a voucher system there would likely decrease segregation and widen opportunities

## Chapter VIII: Monopoly and the Social Responsibility of Business and Labor

### Two Meanings of Competition

- In ordinary discourse, competition means personal rivalry; in economics, it means the opposite -- impersonal markets where no one participant determines terms of access
- The wheat farmer doesn't feel personal rivalry with his neighbor-competitor; all take prices as given by the market
- Monopoly exists when an individual or enterprise has sufficient control to determine significantly the terms on which others shall have access

### Two Problems of Monopoly for Free Society

1. Limitation on voluntary exchange through reduction in available alternatives
2. The issue of "social responsibility" -- a monopolist is visible and has power, making it easy to argue he should further socially desirable ends, yet widespread application of such doctrine would destroy free society

### The Extent of Monopoly

- The most important fact about enterprise monopoly: its relative unimportance from an economy-wide view -- four million separate enterprises, 400,000 new ones born each year, one-fifth of workers self-employed
- Nutter and Stigler studies (1939): roughly one-quarter of the economy governmentally operated/supervised; of the remaining three-quarters, at most one-quarter monopolistic, at least three-quarters competitive
- The governmentally operated sector has grown greatly; within the private sector, monopoly has not increased and may have decreased
- Why monopoly seems more important than it is: (1) confusion between absolute and relative firm size, (2) monopoly is more newsworthy -- wholesale trade is twice as important as auto production but highly competitive, hence draws no attention, (3) general bias toward big over small, (4) overemphasis on manufacturing (one-quarter of output) where monopoly is more prevalent

### Monopoly in Labor

- Labor unions include roughly a quarter of the working population, but this greatly overestimates their impact on wage structure; many unions are utterly ineffective
- Friedman's rough estimate: unions raised wage rates for 10-15% of workers by 10-15%, meaning 85-90% of workers had wages reduced by about 4%
- Since unions have generally been strongest among already high-paid groups, they have made working-class incomes more unequal by reducing opportunities for the most disadvantaged
- Coal industry example: when the Guffey Coal Act was declared unconstitutional, John L. Lewis and the United Mine Workers stepped in -- by calling strikes whenever coal stockpiles grew large enough to threaten prices, Lewis controlled output and prices, with gains divided between operators and employed miners

### Government and Government-Supported Monopoly

- Government support for cartels has grown much more rapidly than direct government monopoly: ICC, agricultural programs, FCC, Federal Power Commission, Civil Aeronautics Board
- Texas Railroad Commission (nothing to do with railroads) enforces oil output restrictions -- keeping oil wells idle to maintain prices is featherbedding identical to paying coal firemen on diesel locomotives, yet oil industry representatives loudest in condemning labor featherbedding are deafeningly silent about their own
- NYC taxi medallions selling for $20,000-$25,000; Philadelphia $15,000 -- arbitrary limitations restricting freedom and promoting waste

### Patents and Copyrights

- Strong prima facie case for property rights: without protection, inventors cannot capture compensation, eliminating innovation incentive
- Costs: many beneficial innovations (e.g., the supermarket concept) are not patentable, so the patent system diverts activity toward patentable inventions and away from non-patentable innovations; trivial patents often used as collusion devices to maintain private monopoly arrangements
- Patent/copyright terms (e.g., 17 years) are matters of expediency, not principle; shorter periods might be preferable but more study needed

### The Three Sources of Monopoly

1. **Technical (natural monopoly)**: single enterprise more efficient than many (telephone, water) -- Friedman advocates private unregulated monopoly as least of three evils (vs. regulated or government-operated), because dynamic changes will undermine natural monopoly over time and regulation is exceedingly difficult to reverse
2. **Government assistance** (most important source): tariffs foster monopoly by making domestic collusion easier than international -- Britain was protected from widespread monopoly by free trade in the 19th century, and monopoly became a serious problem only after free trade was abandoned post-WWI; the corporate/individual income tax structure encourages retention of earnings because even if internal return is lower than what stockholders could earn externally, the tax saving makes retention profitable -- this is a great source of strength for established corporations over new enterprises and a major reason for post-WWII horizontal diversification; special union immunities from antitrust laws, with different standards applied to violence in labor disputes vs. other circumstances
3. **Private collusion**: Adam Smith noted "People of the same trade seldom meet together, even for merriment and diversion, but the conversation ends in a conspiracy against the public" -- but private cartels are generally unstable and brief without government assistance; higher prices attract outsiders and create incentives for each participant to undercut; takes only one "chiseler" (actually a public benefactor) to break a cartel; antitrust laws' major contribution is less through actual prosecutions than ruling out obvious collusive devices and reaffirming that combinations in restraint of trade are unenforceable in courts -- European courts enforce such agreements, which is why cartels are more stable and widespread in Europe

### Appropriate Government Policy

- First necessity: eliminate measures directly supporting monopoly; even-handed antitrust enforcement on enterprises and labor alike
- Corporate tax should be abolished; corporations should attribute undistributed earnings to stockholders for tax purposes -- few measures would do more to invigorate capital markets and promote competition
- Drastic scaling down of highly graduated income tax rates plus elimination of avoidance devices

### Social Responsibility of Business and Labor

- In a free economy, business has only one social responsibility: increase profits within rules of open and free competition, without deception or fraud

> "By pursuing his own interest, he frequently promotes that of the society more effectually than when he really intends to promote it. I have never known much good done by those who affected to trade for the public good." -- Adam Smith

- The doctrine is fundamentally subversive: if businessmen have social responsibility beyond profits, they become civil servants -- and in a democracy, civil servants are chosen by election, not by private groups
- 1962 steel price increase: President Kennedy's public display of anger and threats (antitrust suits, examination of tax reports) demonstrated how much power for a police state already existed in Washington
- Voluntary price/wage controls, if effective, would produce shortages, rationing, and eventually destroy the free-enterprise system
- Corporate charitable contributions are inappropriate use of corporate funds -- the corporation is an instrument of its stockholders who own it; if a corporation makes a contribution, it prevents individual stockholders from deciding how to dispose of their own funds
- The major complaint against modern business (separation of ownership and control) becomes reality when corporations are permitted to make charitable/educational contributions with tax deductions -- this undermines the individualistic society and moves toward a corporate state

## Chapter IX: Occupational Licensure

### Historical Context and Scope

- Overthrow of the medieval guild system was an indispensable early step in the rise of Western freedom
- Recent retrogression: increasing tendency to restrict occupations to state-licensed individuals
- By 1952, more than 80 separate occupations licensed by state law -- from pharmacists and architects to threshing machine operators, egg graders, guide dog trainers, tree surgeons, and hypertrichologists (who remove excessive hair in Connecticut)
- Pressure for licensure invariably comes from members of the occupation itself, never from the public

### Control by Members of the Occupation

- 75% of occupational licensing boards composed exclusively of licensed practitioners, often nominated directly by occupational associations
- Result: essentially medieval guild regulation by the state
- When fear of communism swept the country, Texas required pharmacist license applicants to swear they were not Communist Party members -- relationship to public health "somewhat obscure"
- Indiana required professional boxers and wrestlers to swear they were not subversive; a junior high music teacher forced to resign as Communist had difficulty becoming a piano tuner in D.C.

### Barbering: A Case Study in Absurdity

- A Maryland law (struck down by courts, though similar statutes upheld elsewhere) required neophyte barbers to receive formal instruction in "the scientific fundamentals for barbering, bacteriology, histology of the hair, skin, nails, muscles and nerves, structure of the head, face and neck, elementary chemistry relating to sterilization and antiseptics"
- In 1929, not one of eighteen representative states required graduation from a barber college; today states typically insist on graduation from a barbering school providing not less than one thousand hours of instruction in "theoretical subjects," followed by apprenticeship
- The licensing of barbering is far from trivial -- it is already a serious infringement on individual freedom and threatens to become much more with continual pressure to extend it

### Why Licensing Proliferates

- Producer groups are more concentrated politically than consumer groups -- barbers have intense interest in barbering regulation, but customers spend only a minor fraction of income in barber shops and won't testify against licensing at the legislature
- The puzzle is not why we have so many silly licensure laws, but why we don't have far more
- The only way to offset special producer interests: establish a general presumption against state intervention, putting the burden of proof on those who would depart from it -- the same argument that justifies a Bill of Rights and rules for monetary/fiscal policy

### Registration vs. Certification vs. Licensing

- **Registration**: list names in official register; no denial of right to practice
- **Certification**: government certifies skills but doesn't prevent uncertified practice (e.g., CPA designation)
- **Licensing**: must obtain license to practice; anyone without license subject to fine or jail
- Friedman finds it difficult to see any case where licensure rather than certification can be justified -- certification provides needed information; if consumers still choose uncertified practitioners with full knowledge, that is their business

### Medical Licensure: The AMA as Trade Union

- The American Medical Association is "perhaps the strongest trade union in the United States"
- Control mechanism: the Council on Medical Education and Hospitals approves medical schools; state approved-school lists are identical with the Council's list
- 1930s: Council wrote to medical schools that they were admitting too many students; every school reduced admissions the following year
- Lawyers were less successful at restriction because many state legislators graduated from unapproved night law schools -- voting to restrict practice to approved-school graduates would be "voting that they themselves were not qualified"

### Quality as Rationalization

- The profession uses "quality" to justify restriction, but during the Depression the rationalization was explicit: if "too" many are let in, incomes will fall so low that practitioners will resort to unethical practices -- arguing the only way to maintain ethics is keeping incomes at standards "adequate to the merits and needs of the medical profession"
- Friedman finds this "objectionable on both ethical and factual grounds" -- extraordinary that medical leaders should publicly proclaim they must be paid to be ethical; "there seems little correlation between poverty and honesty"
- The "Cadillac lawyers" analogy: at a meeting discussing admission, a colleague argued it would be absurd if the auto industry said no one should drive a car below Cadillac standard; an audience member rose and approved: "the country cannot afford anything but Cadillac lawyers!" -- this attitude (only "optimum" quality acceptable) always leads to restrictive policies
- Council recommended citizenship as a requirement for medical practice -- inconceivable how this relates to medical performance
- After Hitler came to power (1933), the number of foreign-trained physicians admitted to practice in the US in the five years after was the same as in the five years before -- despite a tremendous outflow of physicians from Germany; the threat of additional physicians led to stringent tightening of requirements

### AMA Control Over Medical Organization

- Licensure gives the profession indirect control over hospital admission (hospitals need interns; internships must be in "approved" hospitals; approved list = Council's list)
- AMA has consistently opposed group practice and prepaid medical plans -- particularly prepaid group practice because it eliminates discriminatory pricing
- Only a few group practices survived, notably in D.C. where they won an antitrust suit against the AMA

### Does Licensure Raise Standards?

Friedman argues it does not, for four reasons:

1. Block to entry creates alternative professions of possibly lower quality (osteopathy, chiropractic)
2. Fewer physicians means more untrained practice and featherbedding -- trained physicians spend time on tasks technicians could perform
3. Restricting practice to orthodox members reduces experimentation; advances often come from "crackpots and quacks"
4. Monopoly makes malpractice suits harder -- physicians won't testify against colleagues who control their hospital privileges

> "I am myself persuaded that licensure has reduced both the quantity and quality of medical practice... I conclude that licensure should be eliminated as a requirement for the practice of medicine."

### Hypothetical Free Market Medicine

- Without AMA monopoly, "medical teams" (department stores of medicine) would have developed -- long-lived organizations with strong reputational incentives, combining practitioners of different skill levels, using technicians for appropriate tasks
- Individual practice would continue alongside, just as small stores exist alongside department stores
- The impossibility of any individual conceiving all possibilities is the great argument against professional monopolies and for the market's tolerance of diversity

## Chapter X: The Distribution of Income

### The Ethics of Distribution

- Central element in 20th-century collectivist sentiment: belief in equality of income as a social goal
- The ethical principle of free market society: "To each according to what he and the instruments he owns produces"
- Payment according to product and equality of treatment are partly compatible: if one person prefers leisure and another marketable goods, equal money payments would create unequal outcomes in a more fundamental sense

### Inequality from Tastes for Uncertainty

- Lottery analogy: if individuals with equal endowments voluntarily enter a lottery with unequal prizes, the resulting inequality is required to let them make the most of their initial equality
- Redistributing income after the event is equivalent to denying them the opportunity to enter the lottery
- This extends beyond literal lotteries: the girl who tries to become a movie actress rather than a civil servant is deliberately choosing to enter a lottery; so is the person investing in penny uranium stocks rather than government bonds

### Initial Differences in Endowment

- Much of the inequality that raises difficult ethical issues reflects initial differences in endowment, both of human capacities and of property
- The widely-argued distinction between inequality from personal endowments vs. inherited property is untenable
- Is there any greater ethical justification for high returns to someone who inherits a peculiar voice than to someone who inherits property? Sons of Russian commissars surely have higher income expectations than sons of peasants -- is this more or less justifiable than the son of an American millionaire?
- A parent can pass wealth by financing a child's CPA training, setting them up in business, or creating a trust fund -- the child has higher income in each case, but income is classified as "human capacities," "profits," or "inherited wealth" respectively; there is no ethical basis for distinguishing among these categories
- It seems illogical to say a man is entitled to what he has produced but not entitled to pass it to his children -- to say he may use income for riotous living but may not give it to heirs; surely the latter is one way to use what he has produced

### Four Robinson Crusoes

- One lands on a large fruitful island, three on tiny barren ones; would the three be justified in compelling him to share?
- Same situation differently: you find a $20 bill while walking with three friends -- would they be justified in compelling you to share?
- Most readers say yes to the first and no to the second, revealing the inconsistency in egalitarian intuitions

### Distribution According to Product: Instrumental, Not Ethical

- The operative function is allocative, not distributive: unless each receives what they add to the product, mutually beneficial exchanges won't occur
- Payment according to product is necessary for efficient resource allocation without compulsion
- Inequality also provides independent foci of power offsetting political centralization, "patrons" to finance unpopular ideas, and sponsors for experimental products

### Marxist Criticism Accepts Capitalist Ethics

- Marx argued labor was exploited because it produced the whole product but received only part -- but this value judgment follows only if one accepts the capitalist ethic that labor is entitled to what it produces
- The Marxist syllogism has an unstated shift in meaning: "present and past labor produce the whole product; present labor gets only part" -- the logical conclusion is "past labor is exploited," with the inference that past labor should get more, "though it is by no means clear how, unless it be in elegant tombstones"

### Facts of Income Distribution

- The more capitalistic a country, the smaller the fraction of income paid for capital and the larger the fraction for human services -- in India/Egypt, about half is property income; in the US, roughly one-fifth
- The great achievement of capitalism has not been accumulation of property but opportunities it offered people to develop their capacities -- yet enemies castigate it as materialist
- Capitalism leads to less inequality than alternative systems: less in Western countries than in India or status societies; likely less than in communist countries; and inequality has diminished over time with capitalist development
- Statistical data on income distribution is misleading: cannot segregate equalizing differences (a baseball player's short career requires higher annual pay); the most important factor reducing inequality is the changing distribution of families by number of children (average declined, very large families virtually eliminated)

### Two Kinds of Inequality

- **Temporary inequality**: income mobility where positions change year to year -- a sign of dynamic change and equality of opportunity
- **Permanent inequality**: rigid status society where families stay in the same position -- characteristic of non-capitalist societies
- Competitive free-enterprise capitalism substitutes temporary for permanent inequality, undermining status and introducing social mobility

> "The chief characteristic of progress and development over the past century is that it has freed the masses from backbreaking toil and has made available to them products and services that were formerly the monopoly of the upper classes."

### Have Graduated Taxes Reduced Inequality?

- Friedman's judgment: graduated taxes had minor effect narrowing differences between income groups but introduced essentially arbitrary inequalities of comparable magnitude within income classes
- Two reasons taxes failed: (1) high taxes discouraged entry into highly taxed activities, raising pre-tax returns; (2) stimulated extensive tax avoidance (percentage depletion, tax-exempt bonds, capital gains treatment, expense accounts)
- A flat rate of 23.5% on presently defined taxable income would yield the same revenue as the current 20-91% graduated structure (1959 data)
- Graduated taxes are less burdensome on being wealthy than becoming wealthy -- they protect existing wealth holders from newcomer competition

### Friedman's Flat Tax Proposal

- Flat-rate tax on income above an exemption, with income defined very broadly
- Abolish corporate income tax; require corporations to attribute earnings to stockholders
- Eliminate percentage depletion, tax-exempt securities, special capital gains treatment, numerous current deductions
- The better approach: remove government-created inequalities (monopoly privileges, tariffs, licensing) rather than alleviating symptoms through taxation

> "The distribution of income is still another area in which government has been doing more harm by one set of measures than it has been able to undo by others."

## Chapter XI: Social Welfare Measures

### Public Housing

- The neighborhood effect argument for public housing (slums impose higher fire and police costs) actually argues for higher taxes on housing that adds to social costs -- but this would bear on low-income people; the real argument is that public housing helps the poor
- Why not cash subsidies instead? Families could spend cash as they choose; they are never worse off with cash and better off if other needs are more important; public housing can only be justified on paternalistic grounds that families "need" housing more than other things but would spend money unwisely
- Actual effects opposite those intended: dwelling units destroyed far exceeded new units constructed -- net effect raised persons per dwelling unit
- Once adopted, public housing was dominated by special interests wanting blighted areas cleared, not by concern for the poor
- Income limitations for subsidized rentals concentrated "broken" families (divorced/widowed mothers with children); schools can absorb a few "problem" children but not large numbers -- in some projects, broken families are a third or more of total
- Cash grants would have spread families more thinly through the community, avoiding concentration effects

### Minimum Wage Laws

- Effects precisely opposite those intended: the state can legislate a minimum wage but cannot require employers to hire at that wage
- Those rendered unemployed are precisely those who can least afford to lose income
- Support comes partly from Northern trade unions and firms threatened by Southern competition

### Farm Price Supports

- Benefits inversely related to need (proportional to amount sold on market); impecunious farmers sell less and get larger fractions from own-use products that don't qualify
- Main effect: kept more people on farm than would otherwise stay, making farm output larger rather than income per farmer higher
- Consumer pays twice: taxes for payments and higher food prices
- Cotton example: high prices encouraged other countries to expand production; then the US sold overseas at low prices, imposing heavy losses on producers we had encouraged to expand

### Old Age and Survivor's Insurance (OASI)

Three analytically separable elements: (1) compulsory purchase of annuities, (2) nationalization of annuity provision, (3) income redistribution

**Income redistribution in OASI:**
- Those at advanced age receive greater benefits than taxes could have purchased; those entering young will receive decidedly less
- The tax is a flat rate on earnings up to a maximum, constituting a larger fraction of low incomes
- No grounds to justify taxing the young to subsidize the old regardless of economic status

**Against nationalization:**
- If government can undersell private competitors through economies of scale, it will get business without compulsion; if it cannot, the economies aren't present
- Technical complexity means laymen are incompetent to judge; bulk of "experts" become employees of the nationalized system, inevitably favoring expansion
- Congressional control becomes essentially impossible -- proposals rubber-stamped, able and ambitious administrators expand agency scope unchecked

> "I conclude that the case against the nationalization of the provision of annuities is exceedingly strong, not only in terms of liberal principles but even in terms of the values expressed by proponents of the welfare state."

**Against compulsory purchase:**
- The paternalistic justification: people are shortsighted and improvident, "we" know better than "they" what is in their own good; we cannot persuade them individually, but 51% can compel all -- this is paternalism for responsible adults, not children or the insane
- The thoroughgoing paternalist is an opponent on grounds of principle, not a well-meaning but misguided friend -- he basically believes in dictatorship, benevolent perhaps, majoritarian perhaps, but dictatorship nonetheless
- The liberal must believe in freedom of individuals to make their own mistakes: if a man knowingly prefers to live for today and deliberately chooses a penurious old age, by what right do we prevent him?

> "Humility is the distinguishing virtue of the believer in freedom; arrogance, of the paternalist."

- The liberal alternative justification: the improvident will impose costs on others as public charges -- but if only 1% would become charges, why restrict freedom of the other 99%?
- The Great Depression, which made the argument plausible, was itself a consequence of government mismanagement -- OASI is a cure for a very different malady
- Private arrangements have altered greatly over time: children once were the major means of old-age provision; as community became affluent, more people accumulated property or acquired private pension rights; recent pension plans over and above OASI have accelerated this trend
- Compulsory purchase has deprived all citizens of control over a sizable fraction of income, inhibited competition in annuity sales, and given birth to a large bureaucracy showing tendencies of growing by what it feeds on -- all to avoid the danger that a few might become public charges

## Chapter XII: The Alleviation of Poverty

### The Case for Government Action

- Western countries have enormously reduced absolute poverty through free enterprise over two centuries
- Private charity was most robust during the laissez-faire era; governmental welfare expansion coincided with decline in private charitable activities
- Neighborhood effect argument: everyone benefits from poverty alleviation regardless of who pays; in large, impersonal communities, private charity alone may fail to coordinate

### Principles for Poverty Programs

1. Help people as people, not as members of occupational groups, age groups, or industries -- the poor farmer should be helped because he is poor, not because he is a farmer
2. Operate through the market, not distort or impede market functioning

### The Negative Income Tax

- Mechanism: if income falls below exemptions/deductions, the person receives a subsidy (negative tax) rather than paying
- At 50% subsidy rate with $600 exemption: person with no income receives $300
- Sets a floor below which no one's net income can fall

**Advantages:**
- Directed specifically at poverty; provides help as cash (most useful form)
- Doesn't eliminate work incentive -- an extra dollar earned always means more money available
- Could replace the host of special measures; makes costs explicit; fits into existing tax system

**Cost analysis (1961):**
- Government spent ~$33 billion on direct welfare programs
- $33 billion could provide $6,000 per unit to the lowest 10%, or $2,000 per unit to the lowest third
- Even supplementing just the lowest 20% would cost less than half of current spending
- Current programs far exceed what is needed to alleviate poverty

**Major disadvantage:**
- Danger that the system converts from majority helping an unfortunate minority to majority imposing taxes for its own benefit

### Liberal vs. Egalitarian Philosophy

- The liberal believes in equal rights and equal opportunity, not material equality of outcome
- The egalitarian goes further: defends taking from some to give to others on grounds of "justice"
- At this point, equality conflicts with freedom -- one cannot be both an egalitarian and a liberal

## Chapter XIII: Conclusion

### The Record of Government Intervention

Friedman catalogs reforms that produced effects opposite to those intended:

- **Railroad regulation**: quickly became an instrument for railroads to protect themselves from competition, at consumer expense
- **Income tax**: enacted at low rates to redistribute income, became a facade of loopholes; a flat 23.5% rate would yield the same revenue as the 20-91% graduated structure
- **Monetary reform**: intended to promote stability, the Federal Reserve bears primary responsibility for converting a serious contraction into the Great Depression catastrophe -- the system created to prevent bank panics produced the most severe banking panic in American history
- **Agricultural programs**: wasted public funds, distorted resource use, imposed controls on farmers, interfered with foreign policy, did little to help the impecunious farmer
- **Public housing**: worsened housing conditions for the poor, contributed to juvenile delinquency, spread urban blight
- **Social security**: enacted to eliminate need for direct relief, yet relief rolls grow and direct assistance spending mounts

### Why Reforms Failed

- Not accidental or a matter of faulty details -- the central defect is seeking through government to force people to act against their own immediate interests
- This is countered by one of the strongest creative forces: millions of individuals promoting their own interests and living by their own values
- "Interests" include the whole range of values people hold dear -- Germans who lost lives opposing Hitler were pursuing their interests; capitalist societies are less materialistic than collectivist ones precisely because freedom permits full scope for non-material values

### The Invisible Hand vs. the Visible Hand

- Progress in the US is the product of individual initiative through the free market; government measures have hampered, not helped
- The invisible hand has been more potent for progress than the visible hand for retrogression -- affordable only because of extraordinary market fecundity

### Two Threats to Freedom

1. **External**: the obvious threat from hostile foreign powers
2. **Internal** (far more subtle): men of good intentions who wish to use state power to reform us, impatient with the slowness of persuasion

> "Concentrated power is not rendered harmless by the good intentions of those who create it."

### Hope for the Future

> "There is much ruin in a nation." -- Adam Smith

- The basic structure of values and interwoven network of free institutions will withstand much
- Freedom can be preserved if we awake to the threat and persuade fellow citizens that free institutions offer a surer route to their ends than the coercive power of the state

> A. V. Dicey: "The beneficial effect of State intervention, especially in the form of legislation, is direct, immediate, and visible, whilst its evil effects are gradual and indirect, and lie out of sight... The mere decline, therefore, of faith in self-help -- and that such a decline has taken place is certain -- is of itself sufficient to account for the growth of legislation tending towards socialism."
