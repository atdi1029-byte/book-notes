## Chapter 18: Nemesis

### The Ohio Antitrust Challenge (1889-1892)
- Ohio AG **David K. Watson** discovered Standard Oil's trust deed in a bookstore appendix — Ohio Standard had transferred control to out-of-state New York trustees, violating its charter
- May 1890: Watson filed **quo warranto** petition seeking dissolution
- **Mark Hanna** pressured Watson directly, warning Standard men "are pretty much all Republicans and have been most liberal in their contributions to the party"
- Successor **Frank Monnett** alleged Watson was offered bribes up to $100,000 to drop the case

### The 1892 Ruling and Reorganization
- March 2, 1892: Ohio Supreme Court ruled Standard Oil must renounce the trust
- Standard counsel **Samuel C. T. Dodd** was dismissive: "The only effect will be to inconvenience us a little." He and Rockefeller had pre-planned a response using an 1889 New Jersey law permitting holding companies
- March 10: Dodd announced trust dissolution; certificates exchanged for shares in 20 constituent companies. Rockefeller held 256,854 of 972,500 shares
- The overhaul was shadow play: the executive committee at 26 Broadway was formally dissolved, but members simply became presidents of the twenty affiliates — "gentlemen upstairs" in Room 1400. Nobody switched seats at the lunch table
- **Standard Oil (New Jersey)** became the de facto holding company; 17 individual stockholders controlled majorities in all twenty companies

### The Panic of 1893
- June 1893 stock-market crash triggered what was called the Great Depression until the 1930s; Erie and Northern Pacific railroads failed; mass unemployment sharpened class tensions
- **Henry Clay Frick** ordered Pinkertons to fire on Homestead workers — Rockefeller sent a congratulatory telegram
- Standard Oil thrived — demand for illuminating oil and lubricants was inelastic. Rockefeller saw this as proof cooperation was superior to competition

> "We held things together so steady that our fortunate laboring men got their pay, though in other concerns many of them were compelled to go, and without bread." — Rockefeller

- The **Populist Party** called for graduated income tax, government ownership of railroads; **General Jacob Coxey's** Army marched on Washington; Pullman strike under **Eugene Debs** was crushed by troops
- Rockefeller reportedly began sleeping with a revolver

### The Automobile Horizon and Competitors
- Standard spotted the automobile's potential early. **Karl Benz** patented a car in 1886; **Henry Ford** tested a two-cylinder auto at 30 mph in 1893
- The auto would be a godsend: as electrification spread, gasoline demand from motorcars more than compensated for kerosene's decline
- The **Mellon family** sold their Crescent Pipe Line to Standard in August 1895
- Independent producers rallied via the **Producers' and Refiners' Oil Company**; Standard-allied railroads harassed pipe-laying crews with scalding steam and boiling water
- 1895: 30 independents merged into the **Pure Oil Company** — Standard's first enduring domestic competitor

### Rockefeller's Fortune and Standard as Financial Empire
- By mid-1890s, ~$3 million/year in dividends; $24 million invested outside oil across 16 railroads, 9 real-estate firms, 6 steel companies, 9 banks
- Standard had been self-financing since the early 1880s — never beholden to Wall Street bankers. Journalist **John Moody**: "The Standard Oil Trust was really a bank of the most gigantic character"
- **National Transit Company** alone kept $40 million on deposit; Standard made large loans to banks, brokerage houses, railroads

### William Rockefeller and National City Bank
- William parlayed Standard Oil of New York into Wall Street prominence — the street John viewed as sinful
- 1884: Met **James Stillman** on the Chicago, Milwaukee and St. Paul Railroad board; Stillman recruited William as director of **National City Bank**; by 1891 it was nicknamed "the Oil Bank"
- Stillman-William rapport transferred to children: daughters married William's sons — producing a line of Stillman-Rockefellers central to National City Bank (today's **Citigroup**)
- John had serious reservations about Stillman; when Stillman made derogatory remarks at 26 Broadway, Junior rose: "Mr. Stillman, you can say those things to my father but you can't say them to his son. Good day."
- John's line always identified with **Chase**; William's with Citigroup

### The 1893 Panic — Rockefeller as Rescuer
- Rockefeller provided almost $6 million to 58 individuals and firms turned away by banks
- Treasurer **William T. Wardwell** shut down the lending window on the company's own founder — refused more credit because collateral was collapsing
- When Captain Vandergrift telegraphed from Pittsburgh that his trust company faced ruin, Rockefeller replied: "Check for one million dollars is on the way"
- After frantic negotiations, Wardwell boosted credit line to $2.8 million in exchange for a lien on his quarterly Standard Oil dividend of $775,000
- In the 1894 gold crisis, Stillman cabled $20 million from Europe (half from Standard Oil connections) to rescue the Treasury — Morgan "took the pose of savior" and assumed all credit
- The episode soured Rockefeller on Stillman, believing he had used Standard's $5 million to buy bargain stocks; Rockefeller said: "We will do nothing. But we shall not forget it!"

### Henry Demarest Lloyd and *Wealth Against Commonwealth* (1894)
- Lloyd called Rockefeller "a Czar of plutocracy"; friends included Clarence Darrow, Jane Addams, Eugene Debs
- His book drew on George Rice, Lewis Emery, and government records; omitted all names to avoid libel. Four printings within a year; Louis Brandeis praised it; Edward Everett Hale called it the most important book since *Uncle Tom's Cabin*
- Key flaws: falsely described Rothschilds as Standard's agents; wrongly accused Rockefeller of rigging shortages (Standard maintained dominance by keeping prices low)
- Central enduring argument: "Liberty produces wealth, and wealth destroys liberty"
- Rockefeller's response: Standard paid "no more attention to all this nonsense than an elephant might be expected to pay to a tiny mosquito"

### Rockefeller's Retirement (1895-1897)
- By 1895, age 56, began fading into retirement; sat for the haunting Eastman Johnson portrait
- Business "had ceased to amuse him, it lacked freshness and variety" (Gates)
- September 1897: officially walked away. Succeeded by **John D. Archbold** — jovial, pugnacious, who gave the trust a more defiant, combative tone
- Fatal misstep: never publicly announced retirement, retained titular presidency of Standard Oil of New Jersey
- When he stepped down worth ~$200 million; thanks to the internal-combustion engine, fortune soared to $1 billion by 1913 — "surely history's most lucrative retirement"

### Henry Flagler's Diverging Path
- By the 1890s, Rockefeller sensed Flagler had become a slave to fashion and ostentation, betraying their shared puritanical creed
- Flagler's first wife Mary died May 1881; he married **Ida Alice Shourds** in 1883, a former actress with incendiary temper
- Built the Ponce de Leon Hotel in Saint Augustine, the Alcazar Hotel; merged railroads in 1892 and planned a line down Florida's coast to Key West (realized 1912)
- Sold 2,500 shares of Standard Oil stock to Rockefeller for $375,000 — right before the auto boom sent those shares soaring
- Rockefeller on Flagler's gambit: "Think of pouring out all that money on a whim. But then Henry was always bold"
- Ida Alice's mental decline: pathologically obsessed with Mary Lily Kenan, obtained a Ouija board — prelude to full breakdown. The Rockefellers were scandalized by her sybaritic displays

## Chapter 19: The Dauphin

### Junior at Brown University (1893-1897)
- **John D. Rockefeller Jr.** — "prototype of the poor little rich boy," solitary childhood, overdeveloped conscience
- Chose Brown over Yale; entered during industrial slump and anti-Rockefeller rhetoric
- President **E. Benjamin Andrews** praised Junior directly; Junior wrote an essay favoring profit-sharing for laborers
- Made Phi Beta Kappa; observed Baptist austerity — no drinking, smoking, cards. Campus legend: soaked apart stuck stamps, pressed his own trousers, recorded every expense

> "Here comes Johnny Rock, reeking with virtue and without one redeeming vice!" — Brown classmate

- Became more sociable; as class president persuaded classmates to forgo alcohol at the class supper

### Meeting and Courting Abby Aldrich
- At a sophomore dance, met **Abby Aldrich**, daughter of Senator **Nelson Aldrich** — too terrified to dance with her but immediately smitten
- Abby: tall, voluptuous, confident, not awed by his name or money
- Senator Aldrich: chairman of Senate Finance Committee, confirmed protectionist, amassed $16 million, sailed a 200-foot yacht
- Junior prayed daily for divine guidance four years; Alta developed excessive attachment to her brother and treated Abby as a rival
- Cettie likened Senior to God and Junior to the Christ child: "You can never forget that you are a prince"
- February 1901: after a six-month trial separation, Cettie gave categorical verdict: "Of course you love Miss Aldrich. Why don't you go at once and tell her so?"
- Wedding October 9, 1901 at Warwick; muckraker Phillips: "the chief exploiter allied by marriage with the chief schemer"
- Cettie developed chills and missed the ceremony — exact repeat of her flight at Junior's Brown dance

### Junior Enters 26 Broadway (1897)
- Started October 1, 1897; $6,000 salary — a "disguised allowance"
- Senior gave no instructions whatsoever: "Father never said a word to me about what I was to do"
- First major assignment: supervise transport of a granite obelisk for the family burial plot — so large it required two freight cars
- **Frederick T. Gates** provided the mentorship Senior withheld; under Gates's tutelage, Junior joined boards of U.S. Steel, National City Bank, and Standard Oil while still in his twenties

### The Stock Market Disaster
- Wall Street operator **David Lamar** cultivated George Rogers as a go-between; Junior took a gigantic stake in U.S. Leather believing he was acting with trader James R. Keene
- Keene knew nothing; Lamar was liquidating as fast as Junior bid up — Junior lost nearly $1 million
- Senior listened quietly, conducted thorough calm inquiry, then: "All right, I'll take care of it, John" — no reproach, no homily

> "Never shall I forget my shame and humiliation as I went up to report the affair to Father." — Junior

- Chernow: "The true lesson lay in what he did not say. Rockefeller sensed that his insecure son had castigated himself so unmercifully that bitter reproaches were superfluous." The incident reinforced Junior's innate conservatism
- Junior brooded over inadequacy; went after work to West Fifty-fifth Street stables to furiously chop firewood to purge tension. Brown classmate Henry Cooper advised: "I truly think it would do you good to take up smoking an occasional cigarette"

## Chapter 20: The Standard Oil Crowd

### Frederick T. Gates — Character and Background
- Antithesis of Rockefeller: florid, melodramatic, flamboyant; could talk for hours "as if delivering a fiery sermon or Shakespearean soliloquy"; threw feet on desk, jabbed finger through cigar smoke; colleague said he had "a voice that thundered out of Sinai"
- Self-described: "eager, impetuous, insistent, and withal exacting and irritable"
- Born 1853, son of impecunious Baptist minister; quit school at fifteen to help pay family debts; attended University of Rochester; entered Rochester Theological Seminary
- First pastorate Minnesota 1880; young bride **Lucia Fowler Perkins** died from massive internal hemorrhage after sixteen months — eroded Gates's faith, sparked deep skepticism about American doctors (with far-reaching impact on Rockefeller philanthropy in medicine)
- Became a biblical modernist; came to believe "Christ neither founded nor intended to found the Baptist Church, nor any church"
- 1888: **George A. Pillsbury** (state's wealthiest Baptist) confided he had incurable disease; Gates devised matching-grant structure and performed so brilliantly he left ministry entirely for the American Baptist Education Society
- Began working with Rockefeller 1891; Gates combined "moral passion with great intellect"; salary rose from $4,000/year to $32,000 by 1902
- Deliberately kept away from Standard Oil matters — "The oil companies didn't like him" (Junior)

### Rockefeller's Use of "Fresh" Men
- Surrounded himself in early 1890s with brand-new men who could defend his past with "total sincerity — and total ignorance"
- Rockefeller revealed opinion of employees only by adding or subtracting duties — never explicit praise or blame
- Gates on Rockefeller: "His psyche was like a set of Chinese boxes: If you penetrated the outer wall, then you faced another wall, then another, ad infinitum"

### The Passive Investor — Colby & Hoyt Swindles
- Paradox: mastermind of Standard Oil was a passive, easily hoodwinked investor outside his own industry
- By 1890, earning $10M/year but deferring credulously to church friends **Colgate Hoyt** and **Charles Colby**, fellow Fifth Avenue Baptist congregants
- Hoyt and Colby induced him to pour millions into companies he knew only from misleading statements — took minority stakes believing partners invested equivalent amounts
- Gates inspected investments under guise of philanthropic tours: Alabama iron furnace was a fraud — "thinly veiled real-estate pump scheme"; Wisconsin iron works losing $1,000/day while Rockefeller had been told it earned $1,000/day; **San Miguel Consolidated Mines** in Telluride were phantoms. Denver mining engineer: "Do you mean to tell me that John D. Rockefeller has invested money in that Damned Swindle!!"

> "He was deeply agitated and, had I not been able to give him the most positive assurances, would have been incredulous." — Gates, on delivering bad news

- After the fiascos, Rockefeller moved Gates into 26 Broadway with unrestricted access; Gates found "some twenty sick and dying corporations, every one in red ink"
- **George Rogers** urged creation of executive committee: Gates (investments), Starr Murphy (legal), Rogers (office) — each paid $10,000/year. Rogers: "This will seem very high but it will be considerably cheaper than being robbed as you have been"
- Hoyt and Colby had quietly bailed out; Rockefeller made Gates president of virtually all of them — overnight, the ex-minister ran two railroads plus mines, timber, and manufacturing
- Gates personally invested and cashed out $500,000 profit in 1902
- In 1917, asked by B.C. Forbes to name the greatest businessman he'd ever known, Rockefeller skipped Flagler, Archbold, Ford, Carnegie — named **Frederick T. Gates**: "He combines business skill and philanthropic aptitude to a higher degree than any other man I have ever known"

### Rockefeller's Investment Philosophy
- Most sacred rule: never disturb Standard Oil stock; always maintained cash of at least $10 million
- Born contrarian: bought in declining markets, sold in rising ones; accumulated positions buying each eighth-point decline, unwound selling each eighth-point rise — averaged cost over extended periods
- Refused to give stock tips after being sued twice for incorrect advice
- Employed double sets of brokers: primary broker placed orders through dozens of secondary brokers ignorant of his identity
- In later life preferred "sure, steady gains to speculative killings"
- Security: minimum two people present whenever the safe housing securities was opened; **Starr Murphy** once had to bring $60 million in securities to Pocantico totally unguarded
- 1910: When **Henry Clay Frick** advised buying 50,000 shares of Reading Railroad, the moment Frick left, Rockefeller ordered liquidation of his 47,500 shares, selling final 2,500 at the stock's peak
- As a lender: preferred large sums backed by first-class securities; Gates: "Never have I known Mr. Rockefeller to call a private loan, foreclose a private mortgage, or oppress a debtor"
- Triumph: $6M loan to fledgling **General Motors** in 1906; notes repaid in shares that soared from 200 to 1,500
- Disaster: buried $40M in Missouri Pacific stock through George Gould — "as much as Gould himself had"
- J.P. Morgan relationship hostile: Morgan repeatedly assigned Rockefeller small portions of mediocre issues; after Morgan Sr. died 1913: "we have had sufficient experience with the House of Morgan in the role of 'pack horses' for their poor investments"

### The "Standard Oil Crowd" and Wall Street
- Late 1890s newspapers portrayed a shadowy cabal: Rogers, Stillman, and William Rockefeller
- Public assumed John D. masterminded it; in reality he was largely absent and opposed their tactics
- Rockefeller (1906): "It has been said that I control all the banks, all the trust companies, all the insurance companies, even all the railroads. Will you believe me when I say that I do not own a controlling interest in any bank, trust company or insurance company?"
- **Amalgamated Copper scandal** (1899): Stillman, William Rockefeller, and Rogers secretly acquired Anaconda Copper for $39M via National City Bank loan, sold to public as Amalgamated for $75M — $36M profit on watered stock. Crowds mobbed National City Bank on May 4, 1899
- John D. put not a dime in; Lawson conceded this in a footnote — but the Rockefeller name was splashed across every page
- Rogers on Rockefeller: "No man has done his business properly who has missed a single dollar he could have secured... it is a religion with us all"
- John D. suffered in silence knowing that speaking up would require repudiating his brother
- William Rockefeller's lifestyle: cocktails, gambling, fast-trotting horses, opera, yachts; Rockwood Hall had 204 rooms, gardens by Frederick Law Olmsted; co-founded Jekyll Island Club with J.P. Morgan — "One Hundred Millionaires" resort off Georgia
- Gave little to charity; deaf ear to University of Chicago appeals; left ~$200M
- John stayed aloof from William's deals — "No, that is William's" ended all discussion
- The bank that descended from Standard Oil: **Equitable Trust** → merged with Chase after 1929 crash → became the fortress of Rockefeller-family finance

### Henry H. Rogers — "Hell Hound Rogers"
- Rose from impecunious boyhood to veritable czar of Staten Island; expected to succeed Rockefeller, bristled when Archbold was chosen
- Rogers and **Mark Twain**: when Twain's publishing house went bankrupt, Rogers wrote an $8,000 check, managed creditors, saved copyrights. Twain: "He's a pirate all right, but he owns up to it and enjoys being a pirate"
- Paid for much of **Helen Keller**'s education at Radcliffe; established an annuity for lifelong security
- 1907 board meeting: Rogers sank millions in Virginia Railway, sought Standard relief; Rockefeller blocked it. Rogers slammed fist, threatened to sell all his Standard stock; Rockefeller placidly: "What is your price?" Rogers handed over his entire block — surrendering a vast fortune

### The Mesabi Range — Last Great Investment
- Rockefeller stumbled into owning most iron ore on the Mesabi Range — his last monumental business project
- Mesabi ore was fine and powdery, scoopable by steam shovels at a fraction of underground costs; commercial viability unproven
- The **Merritt family** ("seven men of iron") borrowed recklessly to build a railroad to Lake Superior; the 1893 panic savaged iron prices. Workers with drawn pistols forced their way into their Duluth office demanding unpaid wages
- Rockefeller applied the Lima oil playbook: swoop in with cash while Carnegie and Schwab scoffed. Carnegie: "Rockefeller's reputation now is one of the poorest investors in the world"
- Initial $100,000 loan consumed eight years; by October 1893, grown to nearly $2 million with no guarantee of commercial value

> "I had to loan my personal securities to raise money, and finally we were compelled to supply a great deal of actual cash, and to get it we were obliged to go into the then greatly upset money market and buy currency at a high premium." — Rockefeller

- Gates matured enormously during the crisis, given extraordinary authority with $100,000 at his discretion
- Early 1894: Merritts sold 90,000 shares at $10/share; Gates: "It is, in my opinion, the opportunity of a lifetime"
- As steelmakers adapted to cheap Mesabi ore, stock soared. Merritts sued — Duluth jury found for the Merritts, overturned on appeal. Settled for $525,000 with public retraction
- Recruited **Samuel Mather** at a ten-minute predinner chat to build twelve ore-carrying ships — the largest ever on the Great Lakes. Mather pretended to need only one or two so contractors underbid; when all contracts were awarded simultaneously, yards were stunned
- Where schooners charged $4.20/ton, Rockefeller's fleet carried ore at 80 cents/ton; loading rate: 10,000 tons every six hours
- Built up 56 steel vessels under the **Bessemer Steamship Company** — world's largest ore-carrier fleet. Forced Carnegie to organize competing Pittsburgh Steamship Company
- Consistently hired for general ability over specific expertise: Gates nominated his uncle **LaMont Montgomery Bowers** to run the fleet — a man who had never been on a ship. Bowers was later assigned to Colorado Fuel and Iron — bringing lasting disgrace to the Rockefeller name

### Carnegie-Rockefeller Deal and U.S. Steel
- December 1896: Carnegie agreed to consume minimum 600,000 tons/year from Rockefeller mines at 25 cents/ton; non-aggression clause on Mesabi. Carnegie boasted he got ahead of Rockefeller; in fact it was his belated correction of his own blunder
- Consolidated stock bought at $10 in 1894 hit $60 in 1899, $70 in 1900, $100 in 1901
- 1896 election: Standard Oil gave $250,000 to McKinley; **Mark Hanna** proclaimed Sherman Antitrust Act would never thwart monopolies under Republicans; 198 trusts created 1898-1902
- Morgan created U.S. Steel to prevent Carnegie from diversifying — and feared Rockefeller might build steel mills. Morgan disliked Rockefeller, who had self-financed through retained earnings. Contrasts: Morgan — sybaritic, theatrical, insider; Rockefeller — ascetic, disciplined, Baptist outsider

> "For Rockefeller, Morgan embodied all the sins of pride, luxury, and arrogance."

- Morgan referred Junior, 27, to negotiate. Junior to Morgan: "I did not come here to sell. I understood you wished to buy." Frick served as honest broker, visiting Pocantico secretly after dark
- While negotiations dragged, Gates commissioned new Mesabi survey maps revealing undiscovered ore deposits — information known only to the Rockefellers, pivoting them subtly toward selling
- Total payout: $80 million for Consolidated stock + $8.5 million for 56 vessels; Gates estimated $55 million was pure profit. Gates demanded recognition: "'Thank you' is not enough, Mr. Rockefeller"
- Carnegie received $300M in bonds — the zenith of his wealth; Rockefeller was "just warming up"
- Morgan's unspoken revenge: assigned Rockefeller inferior positions in weaker bond syndicates thereafter

### The Frank Rockefeller / Corrigan Disaster
- Frank remained VP of Standard Oil of Ohio but suffered alcoholism, gambling, violent rages; John covertly funded his losses
- **James Corrigan** sold refineries to John for 2,500 shares of Standard Oil; after 1893 panic borrowed more; Frank's debts to John exceeded $800,000
- Rockefeller offered market price for Corrigan's shares; within a month Standard stock zoomed from $168 to $185. Corrigan concluded he'd been swindled
- Court arbitrators found Rockefeller blameless. Frank had caskets of his deceased children disinterred from the family plot: "Not one of my blood will ever rest upon land controlled by that monster"
- Despite attacks, John continued bailing Frank out secretly. Final meeting: "Frank, I'll always be a brother to you." They never spoke again
- Rockefeller's lesson to Junior: "Never lend money to your friends; it will spoil your friendships"

## Chapter 21: The Enthusiast

### Retirement and Staggering Wealth
- Average American earned under $10/week; Rockefeller's income was ~$10 million per year before income taxes
- Of more than $250 million in Standard Oil dividends 1893-1901, over a quarter went to Rockefeller
- One periodical calculated his wealth appreciated $55 million in nine months as Standard Oil shares soared
- Unlike other wealthy contemporaries (J.P. Morgan), showed no wanderlust, collected no art, cultivated no fancy connections
- Ida Tarbell branded him a "social cripple" with inferiority complex; Chernow argues it connoted mental health — he needed no outward validation
- "Only fools get swelled up over money"

### Bicycle, Golf, and Systematic Play
- 1890s Cleveland was seized by a bicycle craze; Rockefeller joined with boyish elan — bought corded knickerbocker suits, alpine hats, cloth leggings
- Gates watched him teach himself to turn around without dismounting: started with wide circles, narrowed until almost circling the rear wheel
- Applied industrial method: broke cycling into components, perfected each. When engineer said no practicable grade existed, Rockefeller replied "Nothing is impossible," buried himself in civil-engineering books, calculated a 3% grade, and rode straight up
- 1899, Lakewood: friend **Elias Johnson** persuaded him to try golf; after a few tips drove three balls 100+ yards. "Is that all there is of it?"
- Secret lessons from pro Joe Mitchell while hiding from Cettie in the bushes; weeks later feigned ignorance, then drove 160 yards straight. Cettie: "John, I might have known it. You do things better and more easily than anyone else"
- Eerily accurate swing — "the slowest back-swing I ever saw"
- Dissected his game like a manufacturing process: had a photographer film his swing for time-and-motion analysis, hired a boy to say "Keep your head down," had caddy nail his foot with a wire croquet wicket to fix stance
- Golf unlocked a social side suppressed during Standard Oil years; strict taboo: no business or charity discussed on the course
- Dr. Biggar credited golf with rejuvenating him: "His skin, which was formerly pallid, is now firm and ruddy"
- 1905 account book: personal expenses $525,211, of which $27,537 (~$450K in 1996 dollars) went to golf alone

> "We should not rejoice in the downfall of others, but I slaughtered four men at golf on Saturday last. This was very wrong, and of course I will never do it again." — to daughter Bessie

### Pocantico Hills
- During 1893 panic bought 400 acres in Pocantico Hills, North Tarrytown, just south of William's Rockwood Hall
- Attracted by natural beauty, not elite neighbors; Kykuit Hill (Dutch for "lookout") offered views of the Hudson and Catskills
- No grand plan: "I have no scheme whatever in reference to this new property, further than to own it"
- By 1900: 1,600 acres; eventually 3,000 acres threaded by miles of winding roads
- Craved seclusion; hired Frederick Law Olmsted's firm for landscaping, then took over himself — planted up to 10,000 young trees at a time (eventually ran world's largest nurseries)
- When neighbor Thomas Birdsall refused to sell, Rockefeller surrounded his property with the largest cedar trees available, casting it into perpetual gloom — Birdsall caved
- By 1901, hired golf architect William Dunn to plot a 12-hole course
- Played golf in December snow by having workmen with horses and snowplows clear five fairways overnight: "We never had a finer game"
- Kept 400-500 acres fenced and guarded (after bomb threats); public allowed to hike the rest without cars
- Believed in dignity of manual labor; led work gangs, surveyed roads until dark: "I am thinking of moving that hillock. Offhand, I would say there are just about 650,000 cubic feet of dirt here"
- Paternalistic boss: 300 mostly Black and Italian workmen; outlawed profanity, tried to shut down Tarrytown's lone tavern; never yelled

### Lakewood, New Jersey — Winter Golf Retreat
- Found solution for Pocantico's short golf season at tony Lakewood resort
- Acquired the Ocean County Hunt and Country Club — he called it "Golf House," surrounded by 75 acres of spruce and fir; allowed golf nearly 10 months/year
- **William James** was a frequent Lakewood visitor; found Rockefeller "a man 10 stories deep, and to me quite unfathomable... accused of being the greatest villain in business, a hater of cities and lover of the open air"
- James urged Rockefeller to abandon silence and combat Tarbell's attacks by letting the public know him

### Health Philosophy and Longevity
- Longevity as religious duty: "I hope you will take good care of your health. This is a religious duty"
- Reduced everything to rigid routine — 9 hours daily sleep including long post-lunch siesta
- Credited longevity to "almost reckless independence in determining for myself what to do and the rigid adhering to regulations which give me the maximum of rest"
- Low pulse as point of pride: "that indicates a capacity for enduring and retaining one's balance"
- Medical rationale for emotional control: anger "produces in the blood a lot of toxins"; worry "causes a greater strain upon the nerves than hard work"
- Devotee of osteopathy; lobbied to protect osteopaths from mainstream medicine — irony: his own philanthropy had funded the medical establishment attacking them

> "Listen to that, doctor. They say I control all the oil in the country and I haven't enough even to oil my own joints." — to his osteopath as vertebrae cracked

- Plain diet: peas, beans, rice, fish, brown bread, baked potatoes twice a day; chewed each bite ten times; lingered at table an hour after dinner for digestion
- Post-dinner Numerica game (competitive solitaire with square counters — Baptist prohibition on cards); distributed a dime to winner, nickels to losers; grew so skilled he usually won
- **Dr. Hamilton F. Biggar**: homeopathic physician and golfing crony; patients included McKinley and Mark Hanna. Publicly prophesied Rockefeller would live to 100
- Paradox: the philanthropist who funded the Rockefeller Institute for Medical Research was personally devoted to homeopathy and smoked mullein leaves in a clay pipe for respiratory problems
- Harvard president Eliot told Gates most Harvard doctors considered Biggar inept

### Alopecia — Physical and Psychological Impact
- Hair loss began earlier than believed: ordering hair restorative by 1886 (age 47); full alopecia (total body hair loss) hit in 1893 amid digestive crisis
- By March 1901 (per Cettie's memo book), moustache and all body hair gone — sudden, startling transformation
- Appeared to age a generation overnight: skin parchment-dry, head large and bumpy; Charles Schwab (new president of U.S. Steel) didn't recognize him at a Morgan dinner — Rockefeller had to introduce himself
- Coming at dawn of muckraking era, the hairless appearance devastated his public image — looked like "a hairless ogre"
- Wore a black skullcap giving him the look of a Renaissance prelate
- Biggar's hair-restoration regimen: phosphorus six days a week, sulfur on the seventh — failed
- Bought a wig, nervously tested it first at Euclid Avenue Baptist Church; when reception was warm, became almost boyishly elated
- Eventually wore rotating wigs of different lengths to simulate hair growing and being cut; had wigs styled for golf, church, short walks
- Never found the ideal: Paris maker's springs pushed through hair; Cleveland maker's fabric shrank and slid across his bald pate

### Cettie's Decline
- By early 1890s complained of "a general state of prostration"; ailments: asthma, colitis, eye and spine problems
- Up to mid-1890s, John confided business details to her (including Mesabi ore); then his letters became empty and platitudinous — deliberately tiptoeing around stress out of respect for her health
- April 1904 (height of Ida Tarbell's series): suffered likely a mild stroke, left nearly paralyzed; John took her to Forest Hill, read aloud from *With God in the World* by Bishop Brent
- Family projected stoic patience; outsiders saw a demanding invalid — nurses crept on eggshells, required to serve hot milk at exactly 11 AM
- Clung ever more to religion; wrote to children in elevated sermon language; "never seemed to dawn on her to encourage her children to have a good time"
- **Bessie Rockefeller Strong**: married philosopher Charles Augustus Strong in 1889. In 1902, age 36, suffered a stroke or cardiac event. Santayana: "She was always, as they put it, in delicate health, which was a euphemism for not being in her right mind." Lapsed into childlike behavior, babbling in French; addressed William James in childlike French. Charles — brilliant, cold — found caretaking intolerable; sailed with Bessie to southern France in 1904

### Edith Rockefeller McCormick
- Family changeling: recalcitrant, headstrong, voracious reader, entertained religious doubts — "Reading has always been more important to me than eating"
- 1893: both Bessie and Edith sent to **S. Weir Mitchell's** Hospital for Nervous Diseases in Philadelphia; Mitchell was a patrician neurologist specializing in female nervous disorders
- November 1895: married **Harold McCormick** of Chicago, son of Cyrus McCormick (developer of the mechanical reaper, founder of what became International Harvester)
- Press labeled Edith "Princess of Standard Oil" and Harold "Prince of International Harvester"; she kept her name as Edith Rockefeller McCormick
- In Chicago, became everything Rockefeller tried to suppress: vanity, ostentation, narcissism — moved into grand mansion at 1000 Lake Shore Drive, entertained 200 guests with menus in French, gilded-silver service from the Bonapartes, Napoleon's royal chairs
- Assembled a $2 million string of pearls, a Cartier necklace with 10 emeralds and 1,657 diamonds, a cape of 275 animal skins
- Declared the Rockefellers descended from the noble La Rochefoucaulds
- 1908: Rockefeller scolded Harold for borrowing: "I find that Alta and John's expenses have been less than one-third of what yours have been"
- Winter 1900-01: grandsons Jack and Fowler caught scarlet fever at Pocantico; Rockefeller offered a New York physician $500,000 to save both. Jack died January 2, 1901 at nearly four — prompting founding of John McCormick Institution of Infectious Diseases
- August 1902: J.P. Morgan partner George Perkins merged McCormick firms into **International Harvester** controlling 85% of farm equipment; Rockefeller eventually invested $25-30M with loans reaching $60M

### Alta Rockefeller
- Kind, obedient; suffered partial deafness from scarlet fever
- At 19, infatuated with 47-year-old widower pastor — Rockefeller delivered ultimatum forcing him out
- Founded **Alta House** in Cleveland — settlement house for poor Italian immigrants; Rockefeller funded it for 20 years
- Married **Ezra Parmalee Prentice** — cold perfectionist who translated *Treasure Island* into Latin and required children to converse in Latin

## Chapter 22: Avenging Angel

### Ohio Antitrust (Monnett) and Rockefeller's Testimony
- AG **Frank Monnett** charged Standard had never complied with 1892 dissolution order; $27 million in trust certificates remained unredeemed
- October 1898 testimony: five hours, conceded nothing. *World* headline: "Rockefeller Imitates a Clam"
- Two weeks after Ohio Supreme Court ordered books produced: 16 boxes of company records incinerated by Standard employees
- Senator **Joseph B. Foraker** was on Standard's payroll; Archbold disbursed $44,500 in lobbying fees in 1900 alone
- June 1899: Standard became a full holding company — Standard Oil of New Jersey — parent controlling 19 large and 22 small companies

### Standard Oil at Peak Power
- 84% of all petroleum products sold in America; pumped a third of crude — the highest percentage ever reached
- New uses multiplied: oil stoves, parlor lamps, varnish, paraffin wax as insulator, fuel oil for British Navy (1903)
- Henry Ford's first vehicle: Standard Oil salesman Charlie Ross stood by with a can of Atlantic Red Oil. Registered automobiles: 800 in 1898, 8,000 in 1900. Wright brothers at Kitty Hawk (1903): gasoline supplied by Standard Oil
- Russia temporarily surpassed the U.S. as world's largest producer, capturing 35% of world market; Royal Dutch, Shell, Burmah Oil expanding
- Sir **Marcus Samuel** of Shell held secret talks at 26 Broadway; instead signed with **Henri Deterding** of Royal Dutch, creating a major rival
- 1900: **Waters-Pierce** (Standard's Texas subsidiary) ousted for violating antitrust law
- 1901: Spindletop gusher spawned 500 new companies; by 1905 Texas = more than a quarter of U.S. crude. Hostility prevented Standard's re-entry; **Gulf Oil** and **Texaco** rose from this vacuum
- Chernow: by the early 1900s, the antitrust cases were "fast becoming superfluous" — the monopoly was crumbling on its own

### Theodore Roosevelt
- McKinley's assassination brought Roosevelt, 43, to the White House. Rockefeller called him "the shrewdest of politicians"
- Roosevelt distinguished "bad trusts" from "good trusts"; singled out Standard Oil as emblematic of the abusive type
- February 1902: launched antitrust suit against Northern Securities (Morgan railroad holding company)
- Early 1903: pushed Bureau of Corporations to investigate trusts. Standard lobbied six senators to kill it; Junior sent telegrams at Archbold's prodding. Roosevelt leaked the telegrams, creating national uproar
- The scandal convinced Junior he was "too squeamish for a business career"

### Ida Minerva Tarbell — Origins and Investigation
- Born 1857, 30 miles from where Drake struck oil; grew up in Oil Regions surrounded by derricks, tanks, refineries
- Father Franklin crafted hemlock-bark vats, then converted to barrel making; down the hill from the Tarbells lived young Henry Rogers
- In 1872, fifteen-year-old Ida witnessed the South Improvement Company tear apart Titusville; her father joined vigilantes sabotaging SIC tanks. Standard symbolized "the triumph of grasping men over decent folk"
- Enrolled at Allegheny College in 1876 — sole girl in the freshman class. Decided never to marry; "walked through life in a shining moral armor"
- 1891: moved to Paris; struggled on "the ragged edge of bankruptcy"; read Lloyd's book and rediscovered Rockefeller as author of her father's ruin. Father Franklin was slowly dying of stomach cancer while she wrote the series
- **Samuel McClure** — described by Kipling as "a cyclone in a frock coat" — assigned her; projected three installments, stretched to nineteen (Nov 1902-1904), then two-volume book. *McClure's* circulation grew from 24,500 to 375,000
- Brother **William Walter Tarbell** was treasurer of Pure Oil Company — steered Rockefeller enemies to her and vetted manuscripts, a partnership never publicly acknowledged
- Father Franklin warned: "Don't do it, Ida — they will ruin the magazine"
- Standard Oil's response: "haughty, self-defeating silence." Rockefeller: "If she is right we will not gain anything by answering, and if she is wrong time will vindicate us" — a tactical blunder that implicitly validated her portrait
- **Henry Rogers** met Tarbell through Mark Twain; for two years she periodically visited 26 Broadway, whisked in by back doors. Rogers's motive: deflect attention from his own misdeeds and enjoy seeing sanctimonious Rockefeller attacked. Junior later told Allan Nevins that Rogers "was secretly glad to see Rockefeller attacked"
- Flagler also spoke candidly off the record: Rockefeller was "the biggest little man and the littlest big man he ever knew" — capable of giving $100,000 to charity then haggling over a ton of coal

### Tarbell's Assessment — Strengths and Flaws
- Strengths: clear chronology, made convoluted oil industry comprehensible, exposed the rebates and drawbacks system as irrefutable mechanism of Standard's rise, documented pipeline monopoly abuses, taut spare language that made readers boil with righteous anger

> "There was not a lazy bone in the organization, not an incompetent hand, nor a stupid head." — Tarbell on Standard Oil
> "They had never played fair, and that ruined their greatness for me." — Tarbell

- Did NOT argue for breaking up all trusts; pleaded only for free competition; devoted one chapter to "The Legitimate Greatness of the Standard Oil Company"
- Weaknesses: the SIC was initiated by railroads, not Rockefeller; she romanticized Oil Creek drillers who themselves tried forming cartels and happily took rebates; made Standard Oil and Rockefeller interchangeable while Flagler (who negotiated secret freight contracts) escaped essentially unscathed. Ultimately "a superior screed masquerading as sober history"
- The Widow Backus case — her most famous charge was least deserved: Backus's plant was primitive, operated at a loss, appraised at $71K by her own negotiator; Standard paid ~$79K. Brother-in-law testified Rockefeller paid "3 times the value." Backus invested proceeds and died worth ~$300,000
- Tarbell debunked one myth: the Buffalo refinery bombing allegation against Rogers — "no refinery was burned in Buffalo" — but the story lived on via Josephson's *Robber Barons*

### Tarbell's Surveillance of Rockefeller
- Research assistant **John M. Siddall** used a teenager assigned to burn Standard Oil records monthly; had a friend impersonate a Sunday-school teacher to infiltrate Rockefeller's church picnic
- Tarbell and Siddall attended Euclid Avenue Baptist Church (June 14, 1903) — the only time she ever stood in Rockefeller's presence. Tarbell: "There was an awful age in his face — the oldest man I had ever seen, I thought, but what power!"
- Siddall noted Rockefeller quietly distributed charity envelopes to needy congregants each Sunday — revising his portrait: "In one part is legitimate business, in another corrupt business, in another — somewhere in his being — religious experience and life"

### Tarbell Exposes Big Bill
- April 1903: researcher Siddall learned from Rockefeller's brother-in-law that Bill was still alive — family had led public to believe he was long dead
- **Joseph Pulitzer** offered $8,000 for information; dispatched reporter Slaght on a nationwide manhunt
- Bill had settled in Freeport, Illinois as **Dr. William Levingston**; at 94, fell and broke his arm — revealing to wife Margaret that her husband was a bigamist with five children, one among the world's richest men
- Bill died nominally May 11, 1906; buried in an unmarked grave in a plain unvarnished box. Margaret paid gravediggers $3 but couldn't afford the extra $1 for a brick vault
- February 2, 1908: *World* front page: "Secret Double Life of Rockefeller's Father"

### Frank Rockefeller's Interview with Tarbell
- January 1904: Frank, chewing tobacco and spewing bile, told Tarbell he'd wanted to kill John "a hundred times"
- Called Cettie "narrow-minded, stingy and pious"; accused her of stage-managing charity for religious PR
- Chernow: Tarbell should have treated Frank's lunacy as a red flag

### The Toll on the Family
- While articles ran, virtually the entire family was struck by ailments: Junior gripped by migraines and insomnia, Bessie suffered a stroke, Cettie semiparalyzed, Edith fled to Europe in depression
- Tarbell's character study (1905) abandoned clinical detachment: called Rockefeller "a living mummy," his piety "a hypocritical facade"

## Chapter 23: Faith of Fools

### The Dual Rockefeller Problem
- Had Rockefeller died in 1902, he'd be known only as a rapacious businessman — but just as muckrakers taught the public he was the devil incarnate, he was turning to philanthropy
- "We are almost forced to posit, in helpless confusion, at least two Rockefellers" — his good side was every bit as good as his bad side was bad
- He experienced no sense of discontinuity; did not view philanthropy as atonement. Churchill: "The founder of the Standard Oil Company would not have felt the need of paying hush money to heaven"
- Believed Standard Oil had done more good than his philanthropy ever could
- Regarded his fortune as a public trust; Gates helped redirect from partisan causes toward programs with universal appeal
- Fundamental paradox: the philanthropies were both extremely powerful and inhibited from exercising that power — any boasting would "lend color to the suspicion that gifts are not free from the taint of self-seeking"
- Rockefeller never used wealth to alleviate poverty directly; scorned social welfare. His model was prevention over relief: "Instead of giving alms to beggars, if anything can be done to remove the causes which lead to the existence of beggars, then something deeper and broader will have been accomplished"
- The social-gospel movement (peak 1900-1920) united social reform with moral uplift — perfect synthesis for a Baptist who wanted to transition from denominational giving to secular, ecumenical causes

### The Rockefeller Institute for Medical Research (RIMR)
- Rockefeller himself furnished the original idea — around 1894, countering Harper's proposal for a medical school with a novel concept: a department devoted purely to research
- Summer 1897, Catskills: Gates read **William Osler's** *Principles and Practice of Medicine* with a medical dictionary at his side; appalled that symptoms were described for many diseases but cures identified for only four or five
- Gates drafted memo citing European precedents: Pasteur Institute (Paris, 1888) and Koch Institute (Berlin, 1891)
- American medical schools at the time: mostly commercial operations taught by moonlighting doctors; many did not require a college degree
- When Gates hired Starr Murphy to canvass physicians, many were skeptical the country had enough scientific talent; recommended small grants to individual labs instead
- Rockefeller let the memo marinate two years — recognized medical research was safe, universally popular, noncontroversial

> "John, we have *money*," he told his son, "but it will have value for mankind only as we can find able *men* with ideas, imagination and courage to put it into productive use."

- RIMR founded June 1901 in a Lexington Avenue loft; initial pledge $200,000 over ten years. Key innovation: scientists, not lay trustees, controlled expenditures — revolutionary at the time
- **Dr. William H. Welch** — first dean of Johns Hopkins Medical School, trained in Germany — recruited protege **Simon Flexner** as first director. Flexner hesitated months; Gates admitted he had "the faith of fools"
- 1903: bought 13 acres on the East River for $660,000 — then bleak with breweries and slaughterhouses; now Rockefeller University
- Rockefeller did not visit the RIMR for years; when Junior coaxed him to ride a taxi there, he sat outside: "I can see the outside" — briefly went in, never returned
- Despite detachment, Gates kept him personally briefed; Gates reported seeing "the tears of joy course down his cheeks" over the institute's possibilities
- Winter 1904-05: meningitis epidemic killed 3,000 New Yorkers. Flexner developed horse serum treatment distributed free as public service until 1911 — saved hundreds or thousands of lives before antibiotics arrived
- Flexner recruited "prima donnas" including Paul Ehrlich, Jacques Loeb, Hideyo Noguchi
- **Dr. Alexis Carrel** (French-born) pioneered rejoining severed blood vessels; his 1909 operation saved a premature infant by connecting its leg vein to its father's artery — Rockefeller told this story at dinner parties for years
- Carrel won the Nobel Prize in 1912 — first ever awarded to a researcher in America
- Rockefeller retained personal faith in homeopathy even as he funded the world's most sophisticated scientific medicine — deeply ironic. Through his own philanthropies, did more than anyone to destroy homeopathy in America
- Total gifts: $61 million. By the 1970s, faculty included 16 Nobel Prize winners. Name changed to **Rockefeller University** in 1965

> "When history passes its final verdict on John D. Rockefeller, it may well be that his endowment of research will be recognized as a milestone in the progress of the race." — Winston Churchill

## Chapter 24: The Millionaires' Special

### Southern Education and Black Colleges
- April 1901: chartered train of millionaires toured black colleges; Junior called it "the most instructive experience of my life"
- Key stats: 50% illiteracy for southern blacks; Kentucky was the only southern state with compulsory attendance laws
- Senior's interest dated to 1882 when Spelman Seminary was in a leaky church basement

### The General Education Board (GEB)
- Feb 1902: Junior chaired meeting; originally "Negro Education Board," given neutral name
- Senator Aldrich shepherded federal charter through Congress, January 1903
- Nine-tenths of GEB money went to white schools — "a sorry sequel for a foundation that was supposed to be called the Negro Education Board"
- 1907: $32 million gift — "the largest sum ever given by a man in the history of the race"
- GEB realized southern education couldn't improve without stronger local economies; by 1912, more than 100,000 farms had changed cultivation methods through GEB/USDA joint work

### The Hookworm Campaign
- **Dr. Charles Wardell Stiles** discovered that "lazy" poor whites were actually hookworm victims; the cure cost fifty cents in salts and thymol
- Gates: "This is the biggest proposition ever put up to the Rockefeller office"
- JDR pledged $1 million; dispensaries grew from 2 counties in 1910 to 208 within three years; nearly half a million people cured
- In 1913, Rockefeller Foundation sent the campaign to 52 countries on 6 continents

### The Flexner Report and Medical Education
- **Abraham Flexner** surveyed all 155 medical schools; only 23 required more than high school. One dean produced a pulse-taking device as his "physiology lab"
- The 1910 report: "most pitiless indictment of medical education ever printed"; over 100 schools perished; homeopathic schools among major casualties
- Gates devoured the report; asked Flexner how much to transform American medicine. Flexner: "It might cost a billion dollars." Gates: "All right, we've got the money."
- By time Flexner left GEB (1928): $78 million distributed for medical education; total GEB lifetime: $130 million

### University of Chicago — Frustrations
- Rockefeller's massive endowment deterred local giving; press portrayed it as "Rockefeller's hobbyhorse"
- Spendthrift president Harper ignored deficit warnings; Rockefeller reluctantly covered gaps each year
- December 1910: JDR made final $10 million, bringing total to $35 million; declared the university "the property of the people"
- Gates's seminal argument: a donor's highest ideal is to give birth to an institution that enjoys life totally independent of him

### The "Tainted Money" Controversy
- March 1905: Rockefeller's $100,000 gift to Congregational missions. Rev. **Washington Gladden** dubbed it "tainted money" — phrase fixed permanently in American political lexicon
- Mark Twain satirized the affair: open letter from Satan: "three-fourths of the support of the great charities has been conscience-money"

## Chapter 25: The Codger

### Rockefeller in Retirement — Wealth and Frugality
- Annual untaxed income: $58 million in 1902 (~$1 billion today); spent only $439,000 on household
- Exchanged token gifts with Cettie ($500 birthday gift despite her $1M+ bond portfolio); Junior ecstatic over $11 worth of asparagus from Pocantico
- Estates ran as internal market: Pocantico "sold" trees to Lakewood at $1.50-$2.00 (cost: 5-10 cents); per-capita food costs audited across all houses
- Assumed every tradesman was padding bills; stopped mid-walk to watch for idling workers; adopted strict 10% tip policy after porters took him at his word when he said "take what you deserve"
- Fought doctors' bills ferociously: reduced Dr. Smith's $3,000 bill to $500 under threat of litigation; Dr. Allen's $350/day beaten down to $75: "I believe it my duty to a good many people who have been blackmailed by doctors to stand a trial"
- Core dogma: prices should reflect market values, not the buyer's ability to pay; nothing offended him more than a wealth premium

### The Daily Schedule
- Goal: live to 100. 6 AM rise; 8:45 numerica; 9:15 correspondence (2,000 letters daily); 10:15 golf; lunch; motoring; formal dinner; 10:30 PM sleep
- Observer Inglis: "something bordering on the superhuman — perhaps the inhuman"

### Junior's Psychological Burden and Standard Oil
- Junior's conduct governed by "purpose of rehabilitating his father's public reputation" (Gates)
- His need to vindicate Senior was partly love, partly self-interest: to give away Rockefeller money with clear conscience, he had to convince himself it was earned fairly
- Children: Babs (1903), John III (1906 — "Richest Baby in History"), Nelson (1908 on Senior's birthday — regarded as omen of dynastic destiny)
- Junior discovered Standard Oil's routine secret political payoffs — campaign cash to party bosses including Mark Hanna and Cornelius Bliss who came "at the back door, hat in hand"
- Protested to Archbold who dismissed it as what all big corporations did
- In 1903 lobbied father-in-law Senator Aldrich to appoint Senator Boies Penrose to Senate Finance Committee — in later years regarded this as one of the few times his ethical compass failed
- Began attending only a third of board meetings, distancing himself from Archbold
- Ida Tarbell's expose and Junior's awareness of Standard Oil's corruption pushed him into nervous breakdown in late 1904
- Press ridiculed him as weak, prudish, neurasthenic — mocked whether he tipped (giving barber a nickel made headlines)

### Junior's Bible Class and the Brown Rose Speech
- Took over men's Bible class from Charles Evans Hughes; attendance quadrupled to 500
- February 1902 Brown YMCA speech: used American Beauty rose — bred through "constant, painful pruning" — to justify consolidation; the metaphor haunted him for decades
- Senior: "I would rather see my son doing this work than see him a monarch on his throne"

### The Wealth Transfer and Building Kykuit
- Before 1911, only token transfers; first gift was 100 shares of Standard Oil of New Jersey (1903)
- January 1910: Senior asked what Junior spent the previous year; Junior computed it to the decimal: $65,918.47
- Three-story Georgian manor at Pocantico; Rockefeller built a turntable contraption to track sunlight angles hourly
- Landscape architect Bosworth quoted $30,000; final bill: $750,000 — more than house and furnishings combined
- At peak: 75 houses, 70 miles of private roads; in 1929 paid $700,000 to buy entire village of East View to reroute a railroad

## Chapter 26: The World's Richest Fugitive

### Standard Oil and the Antitrust Drive
- Standard executives smothered Roosevelt with campaign money despite loathing him: $100,000 from Henry Rogers
- Attorney General Knox heard Roosevelt dictating a letter ordering return of Standard Oil's funds; Knox: money was already spent. Roosevelt: "Well, the letter will look well on the record, anyhow"
- After he won, boardroom acknowledged "the worst investment they had ever made"
- Frick on Roosevelt: "We bought the son of a bitch, but he wouldn't stay bought"
- February 1905: House unanimously urged investigation, triggered by Kansas oil boom
- Roosevelt signed the Hepburn Act (1906) — broader ICC power, pipelines placed under ICC domain; Bureau of Corporations report highlighted Standard's collusion: "The Standard Oil Company has benefited enormously up almost to the present moment by secret rates"
- Roosevelt's double game: publicly called Standard directors "the biggest criminals in the country" while privately receiving Archbold and Rogers cordially at the White House
- Rogers: "We will see Standard Oil in hell before we will let any set of men tell us how to run our business" — catastrophic hubris at the moment they needed diplomacy
- Standard Oil treated the federal government as "a meddlesome, inferior power" — the hubris that would be its downfall

### Rockefeller as Fugitive
- Missouri AG **Herbert Hadley** proved Waters-Pierce and Republic Oil were secret Standard subsidiaries
- Process server leaped onto the running board of Rogers's departing car and flung a subpoena before jumping off
- The telltale cheese episode: Rockefeller had his favorite cheese shipped daily; local hack driver told reporters: "Them cheeses, I would recognize anywhere"
- Rockefeller converted Pocantico into a fortress; fled by boat to Lakewood with floodlights on all approaches
- August 1906: dictated a resignation letter; Archbold blocked it: "if any of us had to go to jail, he would have to go with us!"

### The Image Problem and *Random Reminiscences*
- Standard was caught in a transitional media moment — new newspaper chains and magazines could saturate the country; the corporation had no PR apparatus
- Standard's halfhearted PR: secretly paid $15,000/year to economist George Gunton; hired first publicist **Joseph I.C. Clarke**, editor of the *New York Herald*
- Clarke invited reporters to golf outings — invariably favorable. Rockefeller to Harold McCormick: "They say they did not know me before, and seem entirely friendly"
- Published memoirs in *The World's Work* (safe venue — editor Walter Page was a GEB member); seven articles ghostwritten by Doubleday, assisted by Starr Murphy
- Gates persuaded Rockefeller to expand the Widow Backus section: "I doubt if any single libel has done more harm"
- Tone: avuncular, easygoing — "I live like a farmer, playing golf, planting trees." Core ideology: cooperation over competition; "the greatest obstacle to American progress is the willingness of so many men to invest their time in multiplying competitive industries"
- Tarbell's response: "He is more like Bernard Shaw's Napoleon — great, because for himself he suspended the ordinary laws of conventionality and morality... He is a mastodon of mental machinery. And would you ask a steam plow for pity?"
- "Clearly, the lady had not mellowed."

### The William Hoster Encounter
- *New York American* reporter booked the same ocean crossing to France (1906)
- Shocked: the sickly ogre of press caricature was "a tall, broad-shouldered, robust man, with ruddy complexion and altogether vigorous manner"
- Rockefeller cavorted on ship: danced, wore a harlequin costume at the captain's dinner, charmed children
- When confronted, Rockefeller acknowledged some responsibility for his press treatment: "I suppose there may be something in what you say"
- On wealth rumors: "Nothing like it — not by one-third. I want to make clear the injury done to me by these persistent stories that I am worth a billion dollars"
- Invited Hoster to accompany the party to Compiegne — astonishing reversal after a lifetime of shunning reporters
- "They will know me better when I am dead, Mr. Hoster. There has been nothing in my life that will not bear the utmost scrutiny"

### Mark Twain Luncheon (May 1908)
- Twain introduced Rockefeller to forty magazine publishers at the Aldine Club
- Twain's tribute: "Mr. Rockefeller got up and talked sweetly, sanely, simply, humanly... when he sat down all those men were his friends"
- An unlikely triumph — but arrived too late; the political assault on Standard Oil headed inexorably to its finale

### Family Afflictions (1906) and Bessie's Death
- May 1906: Rockefeller catalogued family troubles — Edith ill, Junior still weak from breakdown, Alta recovering from surgery, Cettie down with pneumonia: "no one family has a monopoly of the ills of life"
- Bessie most worrisome: moved to Cannes in 1904 with husband Charles Strong; childlike behavior, babbling in French, morbid poverty delusions despite $404,489 net worth
- Spring 1906: Rockefeller and Cettie traveled to France for seven weeks — extraordinary for these provincials — to stay at Chateau des Avenues, Compiegne
- George Santayana was visiting; found Rockefeller wearing "a pepper and salt wig decidedly too small for him"
- Returned to America July 1906; left Bessie in France against his strong wish
- November 13, 1906: word from France of paralytic stroke; next day: "Bessie Passed Away at Two O'Clock This Morning Without Suffering"
- Rockefeller's reply: "We all send love. All is well with dear Bessie. Command us for any service. Father."
- Charles Strong refused to return to America; became permanent expatriate. Rockefeller cut off gifts a year later; feared granddaughter Margaret would be deprived of religion
- By ghastly coincidence, news arrived as the government began prosecuting Standard Oil

## Chapter 27: Judgment Day

### The Federal Antitrust Suit
- November 18, 1906: government filed in Missouri to dissolve Standard Oil under the Sherman Antitrust Act
- Defendants: Standard Oil of NJ, 65 subsidiaries, and individual chieftains including John, William, Flagler, Payne, Archbold, Rogers
- Charges: monopolizing oil industry, conspiring to restrain trade via rebates, pipeline monopoly abuse, predatory pricing, industrial espionage, secret ownership of ostensible competitors
- 1907 report: Standard still refined 87% of all kerosene — more than twenty times the nearest competitor, Pure Oil
- Standard officials remained delusionally sanguine; Rockefeller privately told Archbold it was "a flimsy vendetta worked up by Roosevelt"
- Why Standard singled out: Morgan's firms (U.S. Steel, International Harvester) treated more leniently because they cooperated; Standard had stonewalled. Archbold had been "foolishly antagonistic" while Morgan was wise
- Seven federal suits plus six state suits simultaneously; Ohio grand jury brought 939 indictments
- 444 witnesses, 11 million words of testimony, 1,374 exhibits, 12,000 pages across 21 thick volumes — "Never before in the history of the United States had there been so far-reaching a struggle between industry and government"

### The Chicago Rebate Case and Judge Landis
- Standard of Indiana accused of taking illegal rebates from Chicago and Alton Railroad
- Judge **Kenesaw Mountain Landis**: forty-one, gaunt, later first baseball commissioner
- Rockefeller evaded subpoena; fled to Pittsfield, Massachusetts. July 6, 1907: arrived at federal building; hundreds of spectators; zealous onlookers tore buttons from his coat
- Fifteen-minute appearance: played confused old dotard; in exchange gained immunity from criminal prosecution — a PR victory

### The $29.24 Million Fine
- August 3, 1907: Landis read his verdict before over a thousand spectators; called Standard "no better than a common thief"
- Fine: $29.24 million ($457 million in 1996 dollars) — $20,000 for each of 1,462 carloads of oil; maximum penalty
- Scale: could build five battleships; fill 177 flatcars with silver dollars; roughly half the money coined annually by the federal government
- Rockefeller had just announced a $32 million gift to the GEB — possibly in anticipation
- Rockefeller was on the golf course when a messenger sprinted across the fairway with a yellow envelope
- Read the verdict without flinching, handed the boy a dime, pocketed the message, said: "Well, shall we go on, gentlemen?"
- Then hit an excellent drive of ~160 yards; only when pressed: "Twenty-nine million, two hundred and forty thousand, the maximum penalty, I believe. It is your honor. Will you gentlemen drive?"
- Shot nine holes in fifty-three — his best score ever
- Mark Twain: the fine reminded him of "the bride's words the next morning: 'I expected it but didn't suppose it would be so big'"
- "Judge Landis will be dead a long time before this fine is paid" — proved accurate
- July 1908: federal appeals court overturned the fine and severely reprimanded Landis for "abuse of judicial discretion"; Standard found not guilty
- Roosevelt furious: called the decision a blow to "the cause of civilization"

### The Panic of 1907
- Underlying conditions: easy money, manic speculation in copper, mining, and railroad shares; trust companies exploiting loopholes
- Standard Oil shares crashed from 500 to 421 the week after the Landis fine, triggering broader slump
- Late October: Knickerbocker Trust collapse; Morgan rushed back from an Episcopal convention; Treasury Secretary Cortelyou placed $25 million in government funds at Morgan's disposal
- When Gates heard of the collapse, he called Rockefeller at Pocantico; Rockefeller, standing in his bathrobe, called Melville Stone of the Associated Press and declared he would "give half of all he possessed to maintain America's credit" — an unprecedented statement: a single citizen promising to bail out Wall Street
- Deposited $10 million in National City Bank (deepest gold reserves during the panic)
- "They always come to Uncle John when there is trouble"
- October 24: returned to 26 Broadway for the first time in years; offered services to Morgan; his millions formed part of Morgan's $25 million fund keeping the stock exchange open
- While Morgan was the impresario, Rockefeller provided more private money than anyone else
- Also bought $4.5M of International Harvester stock from the cash-strapped McCormicks; extended $7M loan to brother William
- The panic briefly made Rockefeller seem civic-minded and earned lavish praise — but an off-the-record comment criticizing Roosevelt's "runaway policy" was published, aggravating the president
- Standard Oil tried a bold peace overture: open its books if the government withdrew the suit. Roosevelt refused: "If we have a criminal case against these men, I should be very reluctant to surrender it"

### Archbold's Bribery Network and the Hearst Letters
- Archbold put Senator Foraker on payroll ($44,000 in six months); used certificates of deposit rather than checks — "the instrument of choice for political bribery"
- Letters pilfered by Archbold's butler's son and office boy, sold to Hearst's *New York American*
- September 1908: Hearst read letters aloud in Columbus, Ohio, creating a national sensation
- Foraker and Sibley were driven from public life

### Junior's Break from Standard Oil and the Progressive Turn
- The Archbold/Hearst scandal confirmed Junior's doubts; "It was the political contributions that focussed the whole thing" as to whether he should resign
- January 11, 1910: Junior quietly retired as a director of Standard Oil. Also resigned from U.S. Steel — kept only Colorado Fuel and Iron (ironically the one that would defile his name)
- After leaving Standard Oil, connected with reformers Jacob Riis and Lillian Wald
- January 1910: appointed foreman of special grand jury investigating white slavery in New York flourishing under Tammany Hall protection. Tammany expected him to be weak; he extended jury from one to six months, issued fifty-four indictments
- Set up **Bureau of Social Hygiene** (1913) — studied urban ills for 25 years; key figure identified: **Raymond B. Fosdick**, later president of the Rockefeller Foundation

### The Supreme Court Decision — May 15, 1911
- Chief Justice White: Standard Oil given six months to spin off subsidiaries
- Rockefeller was golfing at Pocantico; told priest: "Buy Standard Oil" — sound advice
- Sent partners a whimsical message: "Dearly beloved, we must obey the Supreme Court. Our splendid, happy family must scatter"
- The "rule of reason" doctrine: not every combination was illegal, only unreasonable ones — a partial victory for reformers at best

### Dissolution as Rockefeller's Luckiest Break
- Converted JDR from a mere millionaire ($300M net worth) to history's first near-billionaire
- As owner of ~25%, received stake in Standard Oil of NJ plus each of 33 new companies
- Hidden assets quickly emerged: Standard had been extremely conservative in capitalization; antitrust litigation had depressed shares (bounced back post-ruling); automobile euphoria created enormous growth expectations
- Between Jan-Oct 1912: Standard of NJ: 360 to 595; Standard of NY: 260 to 580; Standard of Indiana: 3,500 to 9,500
- JDR's net worth reached $900 million in 1913 — more than the total U.S. national debt ($1.2B); federal spending was a mere $715M
- Roosevelt: "No wonder that Wall Street's prayer now is: 'Oh Merciful Providence, give us another dissolution'"
- Successor companies: Standard of NJ became **Exxon**; Standard of NY became **Mobil**; Standard of Indiana became **Amoco**; Standard of California became **Chevron**. Three of the Seven Sisters dominating world oil
- Rockefeller quietly worked to undermine dissolution: suggested officers meet at 26 Broadway daily; companies continued dividing country into 11 marketing territories
- Morgan: "How the hell is any court going to compel a man to compete with himself?"
- Post-dissolution, younger innovators got chances: **Dr. William M. Burton** of Standard of Indiana patented the "cracking" process in 1913 — refining crude to yield far higher gasoline percentage
- Under Rockefeller, kerosene prices stayed low; under Archbold, domestic prices rose while foreign prices were depressed. Return on assets: 11-17% in JDR's last decade; 21-27% under Archbold. "He was a much less clever monopolist than his mentor"

## Chapter 28: Benevolent Trust

### Retirement Life and the Automobile
- Kept a Peerless auto at Pocantico as early as 1904; prized the 1918 Crane-Simplex touring car
- 1912: Black Hand threats; installed alarm system — button under pillow made inconspicuous lights twinkle in trees

### The Rockefeller Foundation
- Gates's dramatic 1906 warning: "Your fortune is rolling up like an avalanche! You must distribute it faster than it grows! If you do not, it will crush you and your children"
- Rockefeller had recognized the need as early as 1901: "Let us erect a foundation, a trust, and engage directors who will make it a life work to manage this business of benevolence properly"
- June 29, 1909: Rockefeller signed over 73,000 shares (~$50 million) to three trustees: Junior, Gates, Harold McCormick
- Mission deliberately vague: "to promote the well being of mankind throughout the world" — intended to free the foundation from the founder's influence
- Federal charter battle lasted three years; Attorney General Wickersham protested that power vested in a small body controlling $100M for "indefinite objects" might be "corrupt in its influence"
- Rockefeller offered extraordinary concessions: board appointments subject to veto by president, chief justice, and presidents of Harvard, Yale, Columbia, Johns Hopkins, and Chicago. Legislators began demanding grants for their districts — Rockefeller called it "blackmail"
- 1913: abandoned federal pursuit, obtained New York State charter (same legislature had chartered Carnegie Corporation at $125M with little protest)
- Between 1856-1909, had given $157.5 million; gave $100 million to Foundation in first year plus $82.8 million by 1919 — by 1919 had already given what Carnegie gave in his entire lifetime ($350M)
- The 1913 charter also insulated wealth from the newly ratified Sixteenth Amendment (income tax, top rate 6%)
- Rockefeller: "When a man has accumulated a sum of money within the law, the Government has no right to share in its earnings"

### Gates's Departure and Foundation Structure
- By summer 1909, Gates suffered nervous strain after 20 years; by August 1912 tendered resignation from business to focus on philanthropies
- Rockefeller tried to keep him: "Shall we not, dear friend, continue along life's pathway together?"
- Gates held out for $60,000 compensation vs. Rockefeller's offered $25,000 — grievance dating to the 1901 Mesabi sale

### Foundation Operations
- First trustees' meeting May 19, 1913 at 26 Broadway; Junior elected president; Senior nominated as trustee but never sat in on a single meeting
- Structure effectively a Rockefeller family holding company: 2 family members, 3 staffers, 4 drawn from Rockefeller philanthropies
- Oriented toward science as objective and above politics; Junior in 1917 on medicine: "This is a field in which there can be no controversy"
- Shunned humanities, social sciences, and arts for years as "too subjective"
- Senior retained right to designate $2M of income annually — financing Baptist missionary work and even Charles Davenport's Eugenics Record Office
- Maiden action June 1913: took antihookworm campaign global to 52 countries under **Wickliffe Rose** via new International Health Commission
- Rose's greatest success: yellow fever — nearly wiped out in South and Central America by late 1920s; Foundation-sponsored vaccine ready by 1937 (six researchers died contracting it); millions of doses saved American soldiers in WWII
- Rose realized real long-term threat was lack of trained personnel — shifted to building public-health infrastructure: gave $6 million to Johns Hopkins for a school of hygiene and public health (opened 1918), similar gift to Harvard in 1921; spent $25 million total creating such schools from Calcutta to Copenhagen
- China received more Foundation money than any country except the U.S.; built **Peking Union Medical College** (1921) — 59 buildings on 29 acres, roofed with jade-green tiles ("the Green City"); later nationalized by Communists but introduced a generation of Chinese doctors to modern medicine
- By the 1920s, the Rockefeller Foundation was the largest grant-making foundation on earth
- Of $530 million given in his lifetime, $450 million went into medicine
- Redefined philanthropy: previous benefactors donated to named buildings; Rockefeller's was oriented toward creation of knowledge — more impersonal but far more pervasive

## Chapter 29: Massacre

### Colorado Fuel and Iron
- CFI disaster dated to 1902: flush with Mesabi profits, Rockefeller bought 40% of stock and 43% of bonds for $6 million at George Gould's suggestion. Gates later learned Gould had been tipped that management was "rotten" — executives were "liars, swindlers, and thieves"
- Colorado's largest employer: 24 coal mines providing coke for its own steel mills; never paid a dividend
- Junior inherited his father's doctrine: unions were frauds. Rockefeller Sr. on unions: "It is all beautiful at the beginning... But soon the real object shows itself — to do as little as possible for the greatest possible pay"
- Bowers and CFI president Welborn used spies, detectives, fired union sympathizers; simultaneously raised wages 10% and introduced 8-hour day as paternalistic inoculation
- Junior's governance model — absentee ownership, monitoring via ledger statistics — was "sadly deficient"; the Rockefellers were "all-powerful yet passive amid a spiraling crisis"
- Jan 1910: explosion killed 79 men; Colorado Bureau of Labor Statistics charged "cold-blooded barbarism" — Junior's next letter to Bowers didn't mention it, discussed only stock growth
- Colorado coalfields: dangerous mines, armed guards, desolate conditions; in 1913 alone, 464 men killed or maimed; workers from 32 countries speaking 27 languages

### The Strike and Ludlow Massacre
- September 26, 1913: 9,000 workers struck; evicted from company homes, pitched tent colonies
- Baldwin-Felts detectives brought in; Albert Felts designed the "Death Special" — armored vehicle with two machine guns
- Junior cheered Bowers: "Whatever the outcome, we will stand by you to the end"
- April 6, 1914 congressional testimony: Junior admitted he'd done nothing to end the strike, hadn't visited Colorado in ten years, didn't know of valid grievances — submitted all with pride
- **April 20, 1914**: militiamen unleashed machine-gun fire on Ludlow tent colony all day; drunken guardsmen swept through with torches. Two women and eleven children hiding in a bunker were asphyxiated
- **Helen Keller**: "Mr. Rockefeller is the monster of capitalism." **Upton Sinclair** threatened to indict Junior for murder
- A bomb assembled by Wobblies exploded on Lexington Avenue — widely believed destined for Junior's town house

### Mackenzie King and the Colorado Industrial Plan
- **William Lyon Mackenzie King**: Canadian wunderkind, Canada's first deputy minister of labor at 25, then minister of labor
- June 1914: Rockefeller Foundation invited King to head new Department of Industrial Relations
- King initially petrified: "Once associated with the Rockefeller concern, my future in politics would be jeopardized"
- King and Junior: near-identical temperament — short, stocky, prudish, fervent, devotedly read the Bible; both idealized their mothers. Junior considered King's arrival "heaven-sent deliverance"; within a year: "I feel I have found in you the brother I have never had"
- King's candor was unique: bluntly warned Rockefeller philanthropic work could be destroyed by Ludlow backlash; urged Junior to recognize "it was absolutely necessary to take the public into one's confidence"
- King devised company union plan — organized labor scorned it as "another paternalistic trick"; CFI management resisted too
- Senior, moved by the reform effort, bequeathed Junior another 80,000 shares of CFI stock — effective control

> "I wish I had had you the thirty or forty years I was in business to advise me on policies." — Rockefeller Sr. to King

### Junior's Walsh Commission Testimony (January 1915)
- King coached Junior; **Ivy Lee** insisted he enter City Hall through the front door — ending "the rear door philosophy"
- Junior endorsed labor's right to organize and capital's right to resist; buttonholed Mother Jones, invited her to his office
- Junior's mea culpa on the stand: admitted he had taken "too narrow a view of a director's responsibilities" — the public confession Senior could never have made
- Lippmann called Junior "a careful, plodding, essentially uninteresting person" — missing how bravely he had managed to appease both a venomous public and an all-powerful father

### The Colorado Trip (September 1915)
- Postponed by Cettie's death; made in September as an act of atonement
- Refused weapons, bodyguards, police escort. Visited the Ludlow site marked by two railroad ties nailed into a black cross
- Bought denim overalls at a company store, descended a coal shaft, lunched on beefsteak and beans
- At a mining camp schoolhouse: suggested clearing the floor for a dance; grabbed a miner's wife and danced with every woman present. Abby wrote: "From the papers I gather your dancing has been one of your greatest assets"
- October 2: 2,404 of 2,846 CFI workers voted for his plan; 2,000 boycotted out of disdain
- Had to force out L.M. Bowers; Junior to resistant manager: "Paternalism is antagonistic to democracy" — a sharp break from his father's orthodoxy
- Company union outlawed by Wagner Act in 1935; UMW won recognition in 1933
- After Colorado, Junior became evangelical voice for improved labor relations; sold U.S. Steel stock in 1920 strike when management refused to end twelve-hour, seven-day weeks
- Mackenzie King: elected Liberal Party leader 1919, prime minister 1921, served record twenty-two years — helping forge the modern Canadian welfare state

### Ivy Lee — Pioneer of Corporate PR
- Lee's philosophy: tell the truth fully and frankly. Junior: "This is the first advice I have had that does not involve deviousness"
- Lee's actual conduct: smeared Mother Jones as former brothel madam, blamed Ludlow on an overturned stove
- Carl Sandburg: "Ivy Lee—Paid Liar"; Upton Sinclair: "Poison Ivy"

## Chapter 30: Introvert and Extrovert

### Cettie's Death (March 12, 1915)
- Wheelchair-bound from at least 1909; ailments: lumbago, pleurisy, congestive heart failure, bladder problems
- Senior was infinitely patient — at dinner parties would slip upstairs with a flower and amusing talk — but refused to modify seasonal rotation of homes
- Cettie's diary: "John Sr. is at Pocantico coming down Sundays"

> "People tell me I have done much in my life. I know I have worked hard. But the best thing I ever accomplished was to win Cettie Spelman. I have had but one sweetheart and am thankful to say I still have her." — Rockefeller at church

- March 12, 1915: expired at 10:20 AM with sister Lute beside her; Senior at Ormond Beach returned to breakfast openly weeping — a sight Junior and Abby had never seen
- Stared long at her body at Pocantico: "she triumphed gloriously when the end came, and to the last view her face bore that angelic radiance"
- Cuyahoga County tax dispute: declared Rockefeller a 1913 resident because Cettie's illness kept him past tax-listing day; assessed $1.5 million. Ohio governor threatened subpoena
- Could not bury Cettie without facing subpoena; casket stored four and a half months in the Archbold mausoleum, guarded by two armed men
- Secret transfer during a hailstorm: undertaker swapped caskets for an empty one, drove out the gate, loaded into a baggage car amid an electrical storm. Lowered beside Eliza at Lake View Cemetery with gap left for Rockefeller between them
- Senior relished the intrigue: "To plan and carry out the removal without the public discovering a thing was a source of satisfaction to him"
- Cettie's wardrobe probate: most costly item a seal coat at $150; wedding ring of 1864 valued at $3
- 1918: Senior gave $74 million for the **Laura Spelman Rockefeller Memorial** — his last major philanthropic commitment. Under **Beardsley Ruml**, it poured nearly $50 million into social science research; Hutchins of Chicago: it "did more than any other agency to promote the social sciences in the United States"

### Edith, Jung, and the Zurich Saga
- Edith sailed to Zurich in April 1913 with son Fowler and daughter Muriel; settled into the Hotel Baur-au-Lac — stayed eight years
- **Carl Jung** diagnosed her with "latent schizophrenia"; confirmed by her dream of a tree struck by lightning and split in two
- Travel phobia so severe even brief train trips were unbearable; chauffeur Ammann described her as vain, narcissistic, haughty, brutal with servants
- Jung's cures: board a train and go as far as possible (if she leapt off in terror, Ammann raced the Rolls to next station); also had her kneel and scrub hotel floors and walk hatless in rain to conquer haughtiness
- Harold resigned as treasurer of International Harvester to pursue Jungian psychology: "I am trying to learn to *think*"
- Twelve-year-old Muriel and son Fowler both herded into analysis with Jung associates
- Edith missed Harold's brother Cyrus's wedding and failed to attend Cettie's funeral; Rockefeller's patience broke
- Harold to his mother admitted Edith still suffered agoraphobia, had not left hotel grounds for nearly a year — "hardly a glowing testimonial to Jung's method"
- Edith knew her father's emotional defenses intimately: "There is warmth and love in your heart when we can get through all the outside barriers which you have thrown up to protect yourself"
- Jung visited Kykuit in October 1912; dismissed Rockefeller as "narrow, empty, and sanctimonious": "Rockefeller is really just a mountain of gold, and it has been dearly bought"
- Edith put up $120,000 for a Psychological Club in Zurich; sponsored translations of Jung's work significantly expanding his influence
- **Sigmund Freud** (estranged from Jung) suspected Jung was chasing Rockefeller money: "Jung has gone to America again, to see a Rockefeller woman, so they say"
- Edith patronized **James Joyce** — set up anonymous bank account of 1,000 francs monthly; when Joyce discovered her identity, she insisted he undergo analysis (he refused); credit cut after 18 months. Joyce's biographer noted the sadistic society woman Mrs. Mervyn Talboys in *Ulysses* "may owe something to Edith Rockefeller McCormick"
- Used Jungian license for infidelity; Austrian **Edwin Krenn** entered her life — believed him to be architect of genius
- Harold in Chicago was pursued by Polish singer **Ganna Walska** — "gold digger wrapped in exotic mystery"
- Harold filed for divorce; Edith signed a harsh settlement: no alimony, paid Harold $2.7 million. Rockefeller sent Harold a $1,000 Christmas check the same day
- By early 1920, Edith's debts had ballooned to $812,000; Rockefeller bailed her out with Standard Oil stock
- Back in Chicago: Jungian practice drawing 100 patients; hosted seances; announced she was the reincarnated spirit of Tutankhamen's child bride; deposited $5.23 million in trust with Krenn. Flagship venture: Edithon, 1,500-acre millionaires' haven on Lake Michigan. Firm collapsed in 1929 crash
- Never expressed remorse for deserting her father during the last twenty years; "long been liberated from such outmoded concepts"

## Chapter 31: Confessional

### Late-Life Liberation
- After Cettie's death, Rockefeller seemed "lighter and more ebullient, more Bill's son than Eliza's" — freed from the ordeal of her illness
- **Fanny Evans**, buxom cousin from Strongsville, Ohio (30 years his junior) became housekeeper and chief companion; they engaged in wry banter
- Swiss valet **John Yordi** managed his diet, entertained him on the organ (hymns, naturally)
- Preferred Florida in winter; consulted the U.S. Weather Bureau to confirm Ormond Beach got more sunshine than Augusta, Georgia
- Bought **The Casements** in September 1918 — had an associate buy it to keep the price down
- Known to townspeople as "Neighbor John"; attended Ormond Union Church every Sunday, slipping the pastor an envelope covering salary and operations for the year
- At Ormond Beach, "for the first time developed true friends, not just golf cronies"
- Closest companion: Civil War general **Adelbert Ames** — wounded at Bull Run, four years older. Amused that Rockefeller insisted on switching to old golf balls at water hazards: "They must be very rich!" Rockefeller said of players who used new balls
- **Henry Ford** dropped by unannounced: "As soon as I saw his face I knew what had made the Standard Oil Company"
- **Will Rogers** breakfasted at The Casements twice; when given a souvenir dime: "after the company this little dime has been keepin', I'm afraid it's gonna be plumb lonesome in my pocket"

### The Dime-Dispensing Ritual
- Rockefeller's idea, not Ivy Lee's; Lee made it a public trademark
- Estimated 20,000 to 30,000 coins distributed; served as social lubricant for a man uncomfortable in public

### The Inglis Biography Project
- After Ludlow, Junior and Lee revived the idea of an authorized biography
- Startling confession from Rockefeller on the SIC: "Most of what my son knows of this situation is his memory of what he has read in [Tarbell's] book" — Junior had been kept deliberately ignorant of Standard Oil's most controversial episodes
- Lee recruited **William O. Inglis**, a genial *World* editor who golfed with celebrities
- Interviews ran November 1917 to December 1920 — roughly an hour daily; produced 480,000 words of verbatim transcript
- Inglis read passages from Lloyd and Tarbell (which Rockefeller claimed never to have read); Rockefeller would recline with eyes shut as if asleep, then pop open and deliver exact responses
- The Rockefeller that emerged: "alternately wry and genial, fiery and sardonic" — an articulate man who had worked out elaborate justifications he had never shared
- Chernow: "in this confession-box setting, Rockefeller was often voluble rather than candid; the habit of secrecy was too deeply ingrained. He voiced no regrets"
- On Lloyd: "reckless, hysterical, and inaccurate"
- On Tarbell: "much more dangerous... she makes a pretence of fairness... and beneath that pretence she slips into her 'history' all sorts of evil and prejudicial stuff"
- Privately spewed rage: "They are a stench today in the nostrils of all honest men and women. They are a poison"
- Refused to acknowledge the 1911 dismemberment; when Inglis offered to read the Supreme Court opinion aloud: "No; I have never heard the decision read. I shirked it; left it to the lawyers"
- Insisted cooperation had triumphed over competition — ironic given the 1914 Clayton Antitrust Act and 1915 FTC enshrining competition as America's economic religion
- WWI gave convenient vindication: government urged Standard Oil companies to pool efforts; Standard of NJ supplied one-fourth of Allied oil needs. Lord Curzon: "The Allied cause had floated to victory upon a wave of oil"
- Donated $70 million to the war cause, including $22 million from the Foundation to rescue Belgium from famine
- Inglis finished a "sanitized, adulatory" biography in 1924; circulated to William Allen White who called it "too toadying." Even Tarbell (now more conservative, had written laudatory biography of Judge Gary) called it "evasive and one-sided." Junior consigned it to the archives permanently

### Portrait and Sculpture
- Junior proposed commissioning **John Singer Sargent** for five portraits; Rockefeller's bookkeeper instinct: "What about Kohlbach? The price seems very, very high"
- Sargent's 1917 Ormond Beach portrait: blue serge jacket, white vest, slacks — stressing simplicity. Sargent found Rockefeller "most like an old medieval saint with a great deal of intellect... the fine, keen ascetic type, and his expression of benevolence"
- Sargent recommended sculptor **Paul Manship**, who executed two busts: one with eyes lifted heavenward (saintly), one with stern face and tight lips (hard) — together a composite portrait of a man "forever torn between heaven and earth, earthly gain and eternal salvation"
- Manship: "If he'd lived in the Middle Ages, he'd have been Pope at Rome"
- Manship noted Rockefeller believed "the fortune that he had acquired had been given to him as a responsibility, that he must not do with it except for the good of man"

## Chapter 32: Dynastic Succession

### The Father-Son Relationship
- **H. L. Mencken** dismissed Junior: "He is attended to simply because he happens to be the son of old John... he has never said anything beyond the talents of a Rotary Club orator"
- Despite mutual devotion, separated by deep emotional reticence. Junior: "Neither Father nor I had the temperament which gives itself freely. We talked about whatever we had to talk over — never discursively"
- Senior once told Inglis: "I must say that I have never had time to become really acquainted with my son"
- Senior announced during a golf game: "My greatest fortune in life has been my son"

### The Chinese Porcelains Episode
- Junior became enthralled by J.P. Morgan's Chinese porcelains at the Metropolitan Museum; after Morgan's death, dealer Joseph Duveen offered them for $1 million
- Junior wrote his father requesting a loan — like "a trembling, sweaty schoolboy"
- Senior refused; Junior pushed back (unusually, now past forty): "I have never squandered money on horses, yachts, automobiles or other foolish extravagances. A fondness for these porcelains is my only hobby"
- Senior relented and gave the money outright; without this moment of rebellion, Junior might never have created The Cloisters or Colonial Williamsburg

### The Great Wealth Transfer
- Trigger: Ludlow proved Junior had fortitude; inheritance tax hikes in 1916-17 clinched the decision
- Rockefeller acted with "electrifying speed" once decided — consistent with his lifelong pattern of slow deliberation followed by decisive action
- March 13, 1917: 20,000 shares of Standard Oil of Indiana — largest intrafamily transfer in history
- February 1920 note: "Dear Son: I am this day giving you $65,000,000 par value of United States Government First Liberty Loan 3½% bonds. Affectionately, Father."
- Before 1917 Senior had given $275M to charity, $35M to children; between 1917-1922 gave $200M to charity and $475M to children (nearly all to Junior)
- Junior's net worth: from $20M to ~$500M in five years — more than the combined total given to RIMR, GEB, Foundation, and Memorial ($447M)
- Sisters Alta and Edith each received only $12M trust funds — stark contrast
- Senior kept ~$20-25M to "play the stock market"

> "What a providence that your life should have been spared to take up the responsibilities as I lay them down!" — Senior to Junior

### Junior and Abby's Family
- Six children: Babs, John III, Nelson, Laurance (1910), Winthrop (1912), David (1915)
- 10 West Fifty-fourth Street: nine-story mansion — rooftop squash court, art gallery, music room, infirmary
- Summer retreat: The Eyrie in Seal Harbor — expanding to 107 rooms, 44 fireplaces, 2,280 windows
- Junior donated thousands of acres for **Acadia National Park** (first in the East); charted 57 miles of auto-free carriage roads with stone bridges
- Abby: liberal Republican, supported Planned Parenthood, United Jewish Appeal, National Women's Trade Union League
- Abby to sons: "It is to the everlasting disgrace of the United States that horrible lynchings and brutal race riots frequently occur in our midst"
- To Laurance (age 13): "It makes life too easy; people become self-indulgent and selfish and cruel"
- Co-founded **Museum of Modern Art** (1929) with Lillie Bliss and Mary Sullivan; bequeathed 181 artworks in 1935; January 1936 *Time* cover named her "the outstanding individual patron of living artists in the U.S."
- Children received 30-cent allowance; required to spend 1/3, save 1/3, donate 1/3; earned pocket money at Pocantico: killing flies (10 cents per 100), buffing shoes, trapping attic mice (5 cents/mouse)
- Nelson: "I can honestly say that none of us has ever had a feeling of actually being rich"
- Junior was forbidding; Abby served as interpreter, defusing tensions, tidying account books before the weekly paternal audit
- Senior in sharp contrast: beloved, playful grandfather — "a wit, a clown, an ace raconteur." John III: "He loved to tell jokes... He was warm, friendly, and accessible, and he never preached"

### Religious Evolution
- After Cettie's death, Junior moved toward open, interdenominational Christianity
- 1920: spearheaded Inter-church World Movement — ecumenical twelve-city fundraising tour. Fiasco: raised only $3 million (one-third from Rockefellers)
- December 1917 Baptist Social Union speech — near heresy: "A life, not a creed, would be its test; what a man does, not what he professes"
- By mid-1920s, openly doubted literal Bible interpretation; attacked fundamentalists' "narrow and medieval creed" during Scopes monkey trial era
- Southern Baptist Convention reaffirmed Genesis creation account (1926) — direct rebuke of Rockefeller liberalism
- Recruited **Harry Emerson Fosdick** — controversial modernist preacher nearly tried for heresy; denied virgin birth, inerrant Bible. Famous exchange:
  - Fosdick: "Because you are too wealthy, and I do not want to be known as the pastor of the richest man"
  - Junior: "Do you think more people will criticize you for my wealth, than will criticize me for your theology?"
- Donated $10 million to build **Riverside Church** at Morningside Heights; Gothic design inspired by Chartres
- Instead of saints: chancel screen lined with scientists, doctors, reformers — Pasteur, Nightingale, Lincoln; archivolts featured Confucius, Buddha, Mohammed, Darwin, Einstein
- By 1935, Junior made his last annual gift to Northern Baptist Church — cited "tendency inherent in denominations to emphasize form instead of substance"

### Conservation and Cultural Projects
- Grand Tetons: 1924 trip with Abby; Junior struck by epiphany to preserve the view. Bought 33,562 acres through front group **Snake River Land Company**; local opposition (cattlemen, hunters) blocked transfer for decades; only in 1943 did FDR create Jackson Hole National Monument; merged into Grand Teton National Park in 1950
- Also funded Shenandoah, Great Smoky Mountains national parks; gave $1 million to stop redwood logging at Bull Creek Flat
- **Colonial Williamsburg**: met Rev. Goodwin at Phi Beta Kappa banquet 1924; resisted two years; estimated $5 million, eventually spent $55 million. Rockefeller involvement concealed — Goodwin referred to patron as "Mr. David." Junior: "No scholar must ever be able to say we have made a mistake." Roosevelt opened it to public in 1934
- **The Cloisters**: Junior's love of medieval art led him to sculptor George Grey Barnard; Metropolitan Museum took entire collection with Rockefeller money; building incorporated cloisters from five French monasteries. Junior donated his prized Unicorn Tapestries — supreme sacrifice. Underwrote more than 90% of all art displayed
- MoMA: deepest marital friction — Junior banished Abby's modern art to upper-floor gallery. Despite loathing it, became chief benefactor ($6M endowment); Nelson became president at 22. Senior's and Junior's homes at 10 West 54th Street eventually razed to build museum and Abby Aldrich Rockefeller Sculpture Garden — "the ultimate affront" for Junior
- Key contrast: Junior's conservationist impulse (preserving the past) was opposite to Senior's forward-looking scientific philanthropic spirit

## Chapter 33: Past, Present, Future

### Deaths of Siblings
- **Frank Rockefeller**: never reconciled; on his deathbed (stroke, early 1917) his greatest fear was that John might come see him; died April 1917. John canceled his dead brother's outstanding loans after the funeral
- **William Rockefeller**: died June 1922 from pneumonia after throat cancer diagnosis; left ~$200M ($1.8B today), eclipsing Payne Whitney and Thomas Fortune Ryan; gave almost nothing to charity despite John's urging; estate split among four children

### Nostalgia and Late-Life Personality
- By 1922 had lost parents, four siblings, wife, eldest daughter, two grandchildren, and most old associates
- Annual pilgrimages with William to the Finger Lakes boyhood region; on last visit to the Moravia house, bowed his head: "Farewell old home!" — days later it burned from a faulty chimney
- After Cettie's death, became "positively ribald"; developed lady friend Mrs. Ira Warner; notorious for roving hands under car blankets. Tom Pyle: "I never decided whether different women received different treatment or whether some found it acceptable to be pinched by a ninety-year-old multimillionaire"
- Chernow: "As if living his life backward, Rockefeller belatedly entered adolescence in his ninth and tenth decades" — attaining the carefree childhood denied to him
- Owned 60 stylish suits, several hundred ties; changed outfits three times a day
- On 80th birthday: asked how much a Rolls-Royce cost, took the $14,000 check instead; credited good health to golf and "a daily tablespoon of olive oil"
- Weight dropped below 100 pounds; "a wizened little man, no taller than his son"
- Growing detachment from material things — observed an elaborate yacht, mused on its pretension, then: "Wasn't that a beautiful rain we had last night?"

### Image Rehabilitation in the 1920s
- By his ninetieth birthday (1929), an American legend — filmed slicing oversize cake, walloping golf balls, leading hymn sing-alongs
- Image shift: the Roaring Twenties worshipped money; his depredations seemed half-forgotten; first-generation industrialists now had "heroic sheen" next to salaried managers
- Biggest driver: public associated him with philanthropy far more than Standard Oil
- Even Hearst press: "The Rockefellers have given away more money and to better advantage than anybody else in the world's history"
- Pulitzer's *World* (1923): "doubtful whether any private individual has ever spent a great fortune more wisely"

### Junior's Continued Burden
- Annual income fluctuated $35-57 million; diverted 30-40% to charity = avg $11.5M/year, more than the Rockefeller Foundation's annual grants
- Gates resigned (diabetes) and died Feb 1929 — catastrophic loss of vision and tenacity. Starr Murphy died 1921; replaced by **Thomas Debevoise** ("the Prime Minister")
- Key new adviser: **Raymond B. Fosdick** — met Junior in 1913, served as civilian aide to Pershing in WWI, became trusted friend and eventual biographer
- Junior checked into Battle Creek Sanitarium of Dr. John H. Kellogg in late 1922 — tormented by headaches, nervous exhaustion, temporary deafness. For twelve years, seldom went more than two days without excruciating headache
- Gates on Junior: "He would have preferred to cut loose from his father's fortune and make for himself a wholly independent career. But he was an only son, the heir of colossal wealth, dedicated from his birth to overwhelming burdens, not to be evaded"
- 1929 consolidation: Laura Spelman Memorial and GEB programs absorbed into Rockefeller Foundation
- Junior's $28 million gift created International Education Board to grant fellowships in natural sciences globally
- France trip, June 1923: Junior shocked by Versailles decay — offered PM Poincare $1 million to refurbish the roof/gardens and restore Reims cathedral

## Chapter 34: Heirs

### McCormick Grandchildren — Troubles
- Senior's worst fears about grandchildren materialized in the 1920s, especially with the McCormicks
- **Fowler**: doted on by Senior; became friend, acolyte, and traveling companion of Carl Jung (called Jung "a God figure"); married **Anne "Fifi" Stillman** — striking redhead divorcee, 18 years his senior; Senior heartsick
- **Muriel**: headstrong; returned Senior's birthday check as "materialistical"; adopted stage name Nawanna Micor, studied opera with Ganna Walska, tried Hollywood
- **Mathilde** at 17 decided to marry Swiss riding master **Max Oser**, 45-year-old widower; Edith tried to frighten her with tales of McCormick hereditary mental illness: "Two died young and two are insane." Senior graciously received them at Lakewood for his 90th birthday

### Margaret Strong de Cuevas
- **Margaret Strong** (Bessie's daughter) grew up in lonely atmosphere with invalid father Charles Strong, close friends with George Santayana
- Married **George de Cuevas** in Paris (1927) — styled as Spanish nobleman but actually Chilean banking family
- In his will, Rockefeller left most of remaining estate to Margaret — tribute to her and her sainted mother Bessie

### Junior's Children
- **Babs**: Jazz Age rebel — flapper outfits, sports car, Harlem jazz clubs. Offered $2,500 + car if she didn't smoke before 21; started at 15. Saw Junior as "a man incapable of enjoying himself." Called day after wedding "her first day of freedom"
- **John III**: tightly wound, shy; "inherited Eliza's puritan conscience without Big Bill's saving levity." Diary: "I have no personal attraction. Nobody wants to sit next to me at the table." At Princeton, didn't own a car; allegedly turned away cashing a check (the owner had accepted checks from George Washington and Julius Caesar). Chaired Lincoln Center, Population Council, Asia Society. Suffered intermittent agoraphobia like aunt Edith
- **Nelson**: named after Senator Aldrich; inherited charm and craving for publicity. At Lincoln School, told a new teacher "you're new here and I've been around for quite a while." Senior thesis denied Standard Oil drove competitors out unfairly — coached by Inglis. Governor of New York on great-grandmother Eliza's Bible; VP under Ford. Divorced first wife 1962; many felt it killed presidential ambitions. On meeting **Mahatma Gandhi**: "He showed no interest in me whatever"
- **Laurance**: most physically resembled Senior; shed boyhood religious beliefs under rational scrutiny. Became youngest NYSE member; joined syndicate with Captain Eddie Rickenbacker to buy Eastern Airlines; took stake in McDonnell Aircraft (surged on WWII contracts); founded Rockresorts; major conservationist
- **Winthrop**: chubby, maladroit; bore the brunt of Nelson and Laurance's cruelty. Expelled from Yale. "I had mastered only two subjects: how to smoke and how to drink." Found belonging working as roustabout at Humble Oil in Texas — "men working with their hands, producing something real." Winthrop uniquely perceived Senior's duality: "He was warm, human and real — and yet this other quality was there." Elected Arkansas governor in 1966 — first Republican in 94 years
- **David**: Senior adored him — told valet David most resembled him. Harvard cum laude; doctorate at Chicago with free-market conclusions; agreed the 1911 breakup was counterproductive. Dedicated career to Chase Manhattan Bank — "the first member of the family since Grandfather who has had a regular job"

### Wealth Transfer to Third Generation
- 1934 trusts: Abby received $18.3M; each older child ~$12M; total transferred $102 million
- Between 1917-1960, Junior gave away $537M directly plus $540M through organized philanthropies

## Chapter 35: See You in Heaven

### Rockefeller Center
- Origin: 1928, Met Opera wanted to abandon old quarters; site between 48th-51st and 5th-6th (owned by Columbia University), then a sleazy district of speakeasies and pawnshops
- **Otto Kahn** convinced Junior he could render community service and profit simultaneously
- Junior authorized a $3M+/year lease without consulting a lawyer — impulsive, out of character
- Met Opera withdrew after demanding Junior pay half its new $8M building; Junior suddenly held 229 shabby brownstones in worsening Depression
- By spring 1930: paid out $10 million; liable for $4 million more; rentals covered barely a tenth of costs
- Bold pivot: personally finance office buildings and round up tenants. "I walk the floor nights wondering where I'm going to get the money"
- Naming: advisers including Ivy Lee, Nelson, and John Todd convinced him "Rockefeller Center" was the most potent marketing tool — proof of how far the family image had advanced since the muckraker era
- Tenants: RCA, NBC, RKO — 1 million sq ft at $3M/year. Broke ground July 1931 on fourteen projected buildings; employed 75,000 unionized workers during Depression
- Architecture: **Wallace Harrison** used European modernism; Art Deco Indiana limestone towers, 850 feet high; critics carped at inception, later called it the world's finest skyscraper ensemble
- Diego Rivera mural controversy: commissioned for RCA lobby; painted Lenin's face into the mural; refused to delete it; paid in full and dismissed; mural reluctantly destroyed. Junior to Senior: "It was for this reason primarily that Rockefeller Center decided to destroy it"
- RCA Building completed 1933; family offices moved from 26 Broadway to floor 56 (Room 5600) — seat of the Rockefeller empire henceforth
- By 1939: transformed from Depression-era joke into one of the era's outstanding business triumphs
- Senior's indifference: "He probably never set foot in Rockefeller Center" — absorbed with his own earlier creations, tended to screen out what he hadn't initiated

### The 1929 Crash and Depression Era
- During the 1920s boom, Rockefeller played the market with "guilty thrill" — hid trading activity from Junior; having given most wealth to Junior, would borrow up to $20 million and occasionally cadge loans from his son
- After the crash, Ivy Lee scripted a press release after Rockefeller bought a million shares of Standard of NJ: "These are days when many are discouraged. In the ninety years of my life, depressions have come and gone. Prosperity has always returned, and will again." Eddie Cantor's riposte: "Sure, who else had any money left?"
- Junior's net worth slashed from nearly $1B to under $500M by 1934; annual income fell from $56.7M peak to $16.5M
- Under Roosevelt, Rockefeller nudged into praising "the courage and progressive leadership of President Roosevelt"; started giving nickels instead of dimes as austerity symbol. Quickly soured on the New Deal; when Social Security passed in 1935, sure it would "destroy America's moral fiber"

### Death of Edith and Banking
- Edith diagnosed with breast cancer 1930; sold pearls and emeralds to Cartier for ~$1M; begged Junior for loans. Died August 25, 1932 at the Drake Hotel. Left five-twelfths of estate to Edwin Krenn (more than to any of her three children). Joyce on her death: "She was very kind to me at a difficult moment"
- Banking consolidation: Equitable Trust merged with Chase National — creating the world's largest bank, "the Rockefeller Bank"

### Death (May 23, 1937)
- By his nineties weighed under 100 pounds; abandoned Kykuit permanently; settled at The Casements with oxygen canisters
- No more golf, no garden walks; discarded expensive wigs forever; rode stationary bicycle daily for muscle tone
- July 8, 1935 (96th birthday): insurance paid $5 million face value; only 1 in 100,000 lived that long
- Still enjoyed Hollywood movies (especially Jean Harlow); listened to sermons on bedside radio when too weak for church
- To Henry Ford departing one day: "Good bye, I'll see you in heaven." Ford: "You will if you get in"
- Death remained the one subject he would never discuss about himself. Junior: "Never did he speak of death in relation to himself; rather did he speak always of life, of activity, of accomplishment"
- Saturday May 22: paid off mortgage of Euclid Avenue Baptist Church; that afternoon bantered chivalrously with Mrs. Evans in the sunlight
- 4:05 AM, May 23: lapsed into coma and died in his sleep. Official cause: sclerotic myocarditis. Six weeks short of 98
- Private funeral at Ormond Beach; casket escorted by motorcycle guard to railroad station, then by private car to Pocantico
- At Tarrytown, Junior and his five sons waited on the platform in identical homburgs
- May 25: Fosdick delivered eulogy; Dr. Archer Gibson played the Kykuit pipe organ. Worldwide, employees of all Standard Oil successors observed five minutes of silence
- May 27: returned to Cleveland; lowered between Eliza and Cettie at Lake View Cemetery — for fear of vandals, placed in bombproof tomb sealed by heavy stone slabs

### The Estate and Legacy
- Left only $26.4 million — most as U.S. Treasury notes. Retained one share of Standard Oil of California marked "Certificate No. 1" for sentimental reasons
- In his will, left most of remaining estate to **Margaret Strong de Cuevas** — a surprise; tribute to her sainted mother Bessie
- Saudi Arabia and Kuwait oil tapped within a year of his death — ensuring petroleum's preeminence
- Sixty years later, four successor firms — Exxon, Mobil, Amoco, Chevron — ranked among the fifty largest companies on earth
- Descendants worth more than $6.2 billion in 1996; JDR paid $317 million in taxes over his lifetime
- Newspaper obituaries dwelled on the benign philanthropist, not the ferocious trust king. Attorney Samuel Untermyer: "Next to our beloved President, he was our country's biggest citizen"
- Depression-era revival of Rockefeller-baiting (*Robber Barons* by Josephson) countered by WWII-era rehabilitation (*Allan Nevins* two-volume authorized biography). Pattern: "Rockefeller was always either lionized or vandalized according to the temper of the times"

### Chernow's Final Verdict
- "An amalgam of godliness and greed, compassion and fiendish cunning" — personifying the ambiguous Puritan heritage of America
- As businessman: perfected a monopoly demonstrating efficiency of large-scale business; charted the way for the modern multinational corporation; exposed manifold abuses of untrammeled power; proved the fragile nature of free markets, forcing government to specify rules for competition
- As philanthropist: the fiercest robber baron became the foremost philanthropist; accelerated shift from personal charity to institutionalized promotion of knowledge; set the benchmark for professionalism in the foundation field
- Chernow's closing judgment: so much good had unexpectedly flowered from so much evil that God might even have greeted him on the other side, as the titan had so confidently expected all along
- Junior's epitaph: "There was only one John D. Rockefeller."
