## Foreword

### Chernow's Thesis: The Elusive Titan

- **John Davison Rockefeller, Sr.** — most paradoxical figure in American
  history: rapacious creator of **Standard Oil** and wizened dime-dispensing
  philanthropist. "A master of disguises," spending his life "camouflaged
  behind multiple personae and shrouded beneath layers of mythology."
- Public image fractured: the corporate pirate vs. the newsreel saint
- Earlier in the century books appeared nearly one per year, yet he lived
  "behind the walls of his estates and the frosted-glass doors of his office"

### Why Prior Biographies Failed

- **Muckrakers** (**Henry Demarest Lloyd**, **Ida Tarbell**) submerged
  personality in corporate machinations
- **Allan Nevins** (1950s two-volume biography) tried vindication but
  Rockefeller "vanishes for pages at a time"
- **H. G. Wells**: "The life history of Rockefeller is the history of the trust"
- Pre-mid-century works ignored: his bigamist snake-oil father, marriage,
  daughters, political views, forty-odd years of retirement, and
  **John D. Rockefeller Jr.'s** role

### Chernow's Research Breakthrough

- Visit to **Rockefeller Archive Center** in **Sleepy Hollow, New York**
  yielded a **1,700-page verbatim transcript** of interviews by
  **William O. Inglis** (1917-1920) — authorized biography never published
- Transcripts revealed: analytic, articulate, even fiery; dry midwestern wit
- Also accessed 20,000 pages of letters from associates (dating to 1877),
  papers of Tarbell (Drake Well Museum, Titusville), Lloyd (Wisconsin),
  Nevins (Columbia)

### Chernow's Interpretive Stance

- Rockefeller was "an implausible blend of sin and sanctity"
- **Evangelical Baptist faith** is "the passkey that unlocks many mysteries
  of his life" — religious and acquisitive natures intimately related
- Timely: in an era of falling trade barriers, his story returns to when
  "industrial capitalism was raw and new in America, and the rules of the
  game were unwritten"
- Key epigraphs: **Bertrand Russell** compared him to Bismarck. **William
  James** called him "a man 10 stories deep... flexible, cunning, quakerish."
  **Winston Churchill** credited his research endowments as milestones of
  human progress.

## Prelude: Poison Tongue

### The Inglis Interviews (1917-1920)

- Setting: **Kykuit**, Georgian mansion in **Westchester County's Pocantico
  Hills**; Rockefeller in his late seventies, reluctantly agreeing to please
  his son who wanted to cleanse the family name
- Format: Inglis read passages from Lloyd and Tarbell; Rockefeller refuted
  paragraph by paragraph
- Coached by publicist **Ivy Lee**, never used "trust," "monopoly," or
  "cartel" — preferred "cooperation"
- Dismissed free-market ideology with contempt: called proponents "virtuous
  academic Know-Nothings about business"
- **Striking omission**: never once alluded to the 1911 dismemberment of
  Standard Oil — talked as if the old monolith still stood
- Called critics "croakers, howlers, grumblers, blackmailers, pirates,
  wolves, freebooters"

### The Tarbell Flashpoint

- **Ida Tarbell's** "cool, clear-eyed investigative prose had turned his
  name into a byword for corporate greed"
- His deepest rage triggered not by Standard Oil exposé but by her 1905
  "Character Study" of his father: "the poison tongue of this poison woman"
- The outburst revealed buried pain; he quickly recovered: "I am grateful
  that I do not cherish bitterness even against this 'historian,' but pity"

### Family Tree

- **William Avery Rockefeller** (1810-1906) × **Eliza Davison** (1813-1889)
- **John D. Rockefeller, Sr.** (1839-1937) married **Laura Celestia Spelman**
  (1839-1915), September 9, 1864
- Children: Bessie, Alice (died young), Alta, Edith,
  **John D. Rockefeller Jr.** (1874-1960)
- JDR Jr. married **Abby Greene Aldrich**; children included **Nelson**,
  **David**, Laurance, Winthrop, John III

## Chapter 1: The Flimflam Man

### Rockefeller Ancestry

- Around 1723, **Johann Peter Rockefeller** emigrated from Rhine valley to
  Pennsylvania. **Godfrey Rockefeller** (JDR's grandfather): feckless,
  addicted to drink; wife **Lucy Avery** was tall, fiery Baptist, better
  educated, descended from **Edmund Ironside** (English king, 1016)
- JDR on Lucy: "My grandmother was a brave woman. Her husband was not so
  brave as she." Godfrey established an equation between bonhomie and lax
  character. Lucy had ten children; third was **William Avery Rockefeller**

### Big Bill's Arrival in Richford

- **"Big Bill"**: nearly 6 feet, broad chest, auburn beard, brash charm,
  sworn foe of conventional morality by his twenties
- Famous ruse: posed as a **deaf-mute peddler** with slate reading "I am
  deaf and dumb" — extracted town secrets and softened marks. When Mrs.
  Smith marveled at his "recovery of speech": "Yes, I'm somewhat improved"
- Narrowly exposed at **Deacon Wells's** home; first act in Richford was to
  flash the slate asking "Where is the house of Godfrey Rockefeller?" —
  immediately revealing his fakery

### Bill Meets Eliza Davison

- **Eliza Davison**: slim, spirited, flaming red hair, blue eyes — pious,
  self-contained antithesis of Bill. Mother died when Eliza was twelve
  (from a pill by a traveling doctor)
- So taken by the deaf-mute act she exclaimed: "I'd marry that man if he
  were not deaf and dumb." Father **John Davison** — prudent Scotch-Irish
  Baptist, 150 acres — strongly opposed the match
- Bill had been keeping **Nancy Brown** as housekeeper; paid her ~$400 when
  he pursued the richer Davison daughter
- **February 18, 1837**: Bill (27) and Eliza (24) wed against father's wishes

### The Household at Richford

- Bill brought Nancy Brown into the house — children alternated between wife
  and mistress: 1838: Lucy (Eliza's). Nancy's **Clorinda**. **July 8, 1839**:
  **John Davison Rockefeller** born in a bare bedroom 8 × 10 feet. Nancy's
  **Cornelia**. JDR sandwiched between two illegitimate half-sisters
- JDR's generation: Carnegie (1835), Jay Gould (1836), J.P. Morgan (1837)
- Eliza's brothers forced Bill to expel Nancy; Clorinda died young;
  Cornelia became a schoolteacher with telling resemblance to Bill

### Bill's Life on the Road

- Departed furtively under cover of night, returned weeks/months later,
  flinging pebbles at windows. Arranged store credit: "Give my family
  anything they want while I'm away"
- **Eliza's response**: extreme frugality — "Willful waste makes woeful
  want" — the direct source of JDR's legendary thrift
- Bill styled himself a "botanic physician"; sold purplish berries as
  "sovereign remedy for stomach troubles." Townspeople named him
  **"Devil Bill"** — gambling, horse theft, desperado rumors
- After one long absence, rode in on magnificent carriage, diamonds
  glittering, settled the tab with large bills. Eliza remained loyal

### JDR's Early Memories

- Only surviving Richford memory: the brook. Notably edited out father and
  inebriated grandfather — early sign of **self-protective suppression of
  unpleasant memories**. "I shudder to think of what I should have been if
  I had remained in Richford all my life."

## Chapter 2: Fires of Revival

### Move to Moravia (~1843)

- Moravia: 30 miles north; more settled, temperance and antislavery
  sentiment. Bill paid $1,000 for 92 acres sloping to **Owasco Lake**
  (Finger Lakes). JDR: "How the wind used to roar among the hemlocks!"
- Bill ran a **legitimate logging business** — guided gangs by starlight,
  floated logs to Auburn. Helped select school site; stocked lake with
  pickerel; headed local temperance committee. JDR: "He'd get a thing done
  while his neighbors were beginning to talk about it"
- Moravia revealed Bill's underlying craving for respectability

### Family Sorrows

- 1843: **Mary Ann** born (Bill absent). Two years later, twins: **Frank**
  (healthy) and **Frances** (died before age 2, ~70 doctor visits). Eliza
  sent seven-year-old John to pick stones so he wouldn't see burial — JDR
  later exhibited unacknowledged dread of death

### John's Early Character

- Grim, expressionless face; "always thinking." Slow learner but patient
  and persistent — terrific head for math. Extraordinary capacity to
  **pinpoint goals without impulsiveness**: at checkers, studied every
  move: "I'll move just as soon as I get it figured out. You don't think
  I'm playing to get beaten, do you?"
- Only submitted to games where he dictated the rules. Dry wit — "He would
  plague us all with his jokes, always with a straight, solemn face"

### Early Entrepreneurial Instincts

- Age seven: bought candy by the pound, resold to siblings at profit.
  Dropped earned coins into a blue china bowl. **First business coup**:
  shadowed turkey hen, raided nest, raised chicks for sale, expanded next
  year. "To this day, I enjoy the sight of a flock of turkeys"

### Religion and the Baptist Church

- Bill never joined a church but offered John $5 to read the Bible — an
  **unintentional association between God and money**. Eliza found in the
  Bible a balm for her troubled spirit
- "I was trained from the beginning to work and to save. I have always
  regarded it as a religious duty to get all I could honorably and to give
  all I could."
- **Low-church Baptists**: opposed vain display but did not prohibit wealth
  accumulation. The **Second Great Awakening** (1800-1830s) made upstate NY
  the **"Burned-Over District."** **Charles Grandison Finney**: most
  celebrated revivalist. Baptists departed from strict Calvinism: *all*
  souls could be saved — free agency, self-reliance

### Eliza's Character and Influence

- Rudimentary education; showed coolheaded bravery — once sang loudly to
  deter thieves while ill. Stern discipline: birch switch, strapped
  children to apple tree ("I'm doing this in love"). Once punished JDR for
  another's misdeed: "Never mind, we have started in on this whipping, and
  it will do for the next time"
- JDR inherited her slow metabolism, narrow face, piercing eyes, understated
  authority. Key admonition: **"We will let it simmer"** — used throughout
  his career. Drew closest to JDR, accelerating his maturity at the cost
  of spontaneous joy
- **Bertrand Russell**: "What he said, what he thought, and what he felt,
  came from his mother, but what he did came from his father"

### Bill's Talents and Dark Side

- Superb athlete (jumped backward over fence), ventriloquist (half-dozen
  voices), animal trainer, crack shot (200 paces), hypnotist
- **Love of money**: stashed cash in bureau drawer, kept four-gallon pail
  of gold pieces, once wore tablecloth stitched from banknotes. "The old
  man had a passion for money that amounted almost to a craze." Bill's
  mistrust of banks passed directly to JDR
- Business ethics: "you were entitled to outwit the other fellow by any
  means, fair or foul." Dropped JDR from high chair: "Remember, never
  trust anyone completely, not even me"

### The Rape Indictment (1849)

- July 26, 1849: Bill indicted for assaulting **Anne Vanderbeak**
  (housemaid). Never appeared in court. The Hewitt brothers (whose sister
  Charlotte Bill had seduced) sat on the jury
- Scandal ended Bill's truce with John Davison, who sued for $1,210.75,
  had goods attached, modified will to put Eliza's inheritance in trustees'
  hands. Bill abandoned family; 1850 resettled them in **Owego**
- Having a notorious father bred in JDR a **reflexive habit of secrecy**,
  contempt for idle chatter, defiant attitude toward strangers

### Move to Owego (1850)

- **Owego** — county seat of Tioga County, on a bend of the Susquehanna;
  imposing courthouse, well-stocked library, renowned school
- Erie Railroad arrived June 1, 1849 — ending isolated self-contained
  economies and opening towns to national markets; JDR later called trains
  conveyances that "would figure so largely in his own exploits"
- Eliza increased dependence on JDR: "I was taught to do as much business
  at the age of ten or eleven as it was possible for me to do"

### Owego Academy (1852)

- **Owego Academy** under **Dr. William Smythe**; notable alumni: **Thomas
  C. Platt** (Republican boss) and **Washington Gladden** (anti-Standard
  preacher). JDR: plodding, unremarkable. Tutor **Susan La Monte**: "There
  was nothing about him to make anybody pay especial attention to him"
- Class photo exclusion: suits too shabby — JDR never expressed resentment
- Oddly self-possessed adult demeanor; aware of girls but tightly controlled
  (knowing his father's history)
- At ~age thirteen, by the Susquehanna: "Some day, sometime, when I am a
  man, I want to be worth a-hundred-thousand-dollars. And I'm going to be,
  too — some day"
- Context: **California Gold Rush** (1848) — **Mark Twain** identified it as
  the watershed that "sanctified a new money worship"

## Chapter 3: Bound to Be Rich

### Move to Strongsville, Ohio (1853)

- Bill swept family to **Strongsville**, ~12 miles from Cleveland. Dumped
  them at sister's house paying $300/year — profound humiliation. Bill
  forming "a new romantic attachment." Cousin Billy Humiston described JDR
  as "sanctimonious"

### Central High School, Cleveland (1854-1855)

- Entered age fifteen. On slavery: "a violation of the laws of our country
  and the laws of our God." Signed essays **"John D."** Nicknames:
  **"Old Pleased-Although-I'm-Sad"** and **"the Deacon."** Teacher: "the
  coldest blooded, the quietest and most deliberate chap"
- Fantastic mind for numbers; loved **music**, practiced piano up to six
  hours daily. Close friend **Mark Hanna** (later senator): "sane in every
  respect save one — he is money mad!"
- Already lending to **his own father** at interest, age 15

### Big Bill's Double Life: The Levingston Identity

- Bill ran a **full double life**: Dr. William A. Rockefeller at home;
  Dr. William Levingston away. Set up in **Niagara, Ontario**. Around 1852
  met **Margaret Allen** (~17) in Norwich, Ontario; Bill was 42
- **June 12, 1855**: Bill married Margaret — clandestine bigamous life
  lasting to his death. Just weeks before, told John he was **not to go to
  college**. John dropped out ~May 1855 — Bill cutting first-family
  expenditures for second family and grooming John as **surrogate father**

## Chapter 4: The Bookkeeper

### Commercial College and Job Hunt (1855)

- **$40** for three months at **E. G. Folsom's Commercial College**:
  double-entry bookkeeping, penmanship, banking, commercial law
- Targeted only firms with **high credit ratings**, made rounds six days a
  week for six weeks. "I was working every day at my business — the
  business of looking for work." When he exhausted his list, started over

### September 26, 1855 — "Job Day"

- Walked into **Hewitt and Tuttle**, commission merchants. **Isaac L.
  Hewitt**: "We'll give you a chance." Three months unpaid; first pay:
  **$50**; raised to **$25/month**. Honored September 26 as **"Job Day"**
  for life — more than his birthday. "All my future seemed to hinge on
  that day"

### The Sacred Ledger

- Bought a small red book for a dime: **Ledger A**. Wept showing it fifty
  years later. **Max Weber**: "rational bookkeeping" integral to
  capitalism's spirit. **Schumpeter**: double-entry bookkeeping
  capitalism's "towering monument"
- "I had a passion for detail which afterward I was forced to strive to
  modify"

### Work Habits at Hewitt and Tuttle

- Reviewed every invoice; caught a ship captain's unwarranted damage claims.
  One-man collection agent — sat outside debtors' homes, pale and patient.
  Dreamed about unpaid accounts decades later. Exposure to **transportation
  economics** was foundational for Standard Oil's railroad negotiations.
  Compulsive orderliness a reaction to disorderly father and childhood

### Money and Charity from Day One

- **Ledger A**: ~**6% of wages to charity** in first year; by 1859
  surpassed **10%**. Ecumenical: gave to a black man to buy wife out of
  slavery, black church, Methodist church, Catholic orphanage
- Read *Extracts from the Diary of Amos Lawrence* — wealthy textile
  manufacturer who gave away $100,000+. Resolved to one day give "crisp
  bills" like Lawrence
- When given a raise: **"I felt like a criminal"** — religious scruples
  about coveting money. Started at 6:30 AM; pledged not to be seen after
  10 PM — couldn't obey

### Erie Street Baptist Mission Church

- Church as substitute family. Baptized fall 1854. Sunday-school teacher,
  trustee, volunteer janitor: swept halls, washed windows, snuffed candles.
  Favored hymn: "I've Found a Friend"
- Church mortgage crisis: stationed himself at the door, buttonholed every
  congregant for specific pledges; raised full **$2,000**. Age 20, the
  second most important member. "Perhaps nothing in his early life so
  foreshadowed his unswerving pursuit of business goals"
- **Henry Ward Beecher**: "where you find the most religion you find the
  most worldly prosperity." Rockefeller: **"God gave me my money."**
  Doctrine of **stewardship**: wealthy man as temporary trustee

### Founding Clark and Rockefeller (April 1, 1858)

- **Maurice B. Clark**, 28, Englishman — classmates at Folsom's. Each
  invested $2,000. JDR saved $800 in three years; Bill "loaned" $1,000 at
  10% (above prevailing rate). April 1, 1858: **Clark and Rockefeller** at
  32 River Street. "It was a great thing to be my own employer." Fell to
  his knees that night and implored the Lord's blessing
- At 95, still told grandchildren: "Oh how blessed the young men are who
  have to struggle for a foundation and a beginning in life"

## Chapter 5: The Auction

### Pre-Drake Oil and Drake's Discovery

- Petroleum oozed from **Oil Creek**, PA — Seneca Indians used it. Around
  1850, **Samuel Kier** marketed **Kier's Rock Oil** for liver complaints.
  Illumination crisis: whale fisheries couldn't meet demand. **George
  Bissell** organized **Pennsylvania Rock-Oil Company**; **Professor
  Benjamin Silliman Jr.** of Yale authenticated it in 1855 report
- **Edwin Drake**: former railroad conductor, dispatched to Titusville
  December 1857. **August 28, 1859**: oil bubbled up. His achievement:
  figuring out how to extract commercial quantities systematically.
  Triggered stampede; within a year, dozen refineries on Oil Creek
- Cleveland: one day's travel from Titusville, well-positioned with
  businessmen already refining illuminating oil from bituminous coal

### Rockefeller's Entry into Oil

- Viewed Drake's discovery spiritually: "these vast stores of wealth were
  the gifts of the great Creator." This unswerving faith enabled him to
  persist where others faltered
- Key link: **Samuel Andrews**, self-taught English chemist, distilled
  Cleveland's first kerosene in 1860. Andrews pitched Clark and Rockefeller
  for backing; Clark refused, Andrews went to JDR directly
- **Andrews, Clark and Co.** formed 1863: $4,000 for half the capital.
  Refinery at **Kingsbury Run** christened **Excelsior Works**. November
  1863: **Atlantic and Great Western Railroad** arrived — direct access to
  NYC and PA oil fields. Ability to ship by water *or* rail gave leverage
  for **preferential freight rates**
- By mid-1863, Cleveland had **twenty refineries**. Low entry barrier: as
  little as $1,000. Maurice Clark: "John had abiding faith in two things —
  the Baptist creed and oil"

### The Oil Regions

- Wild frontier: **"Sodden Gomorrah"** overrun with prostitutes,
  cardsharpers. Transport brutal: teamsters charged $3-$4/barrel to
  railhead. The **Pennsylvania barrel = 42 gallons** (standard to this day)
- The **Empire Well** (September 1861): first great gusher, 3,000
  barrels/day, crashing prices to **10 cents/barrel**. Price volatility
  extreme: 10¢-$10/barrel in 1861. The **"rule of capture"**: neighbors
  could drain your oil
- **Key insight**: searching for oil was unpredictable; *refining* was
  methodical — the point of maximum leverage

### The Clark Brothers Conflict

- Clark dismissed JDR as "a mere bookkeeper." Brother James tried physical
  intimidation; JDR: "you can knock my head off but you can't scare me."
  James boasted of swindling people — JDR audited expenses closely
- Core disagreement: **Rockefeller wanted to borrow heavily and expand**;
  Clarks favored caution. Clark furious when JDR borrowed $100,000

### The 1865 Auction

- Rockefeller secretly allied Sam Andrews, lined up bankers, provoked a
  quarrel. Clark threatened dissolution; JDR raced to the *Cleveland
  Leader* to publish notice — Clarks stunned
- **February 1, 1865**: auction at Rockefeller's home. Bidding rose from
  $500 to $72,000; Clark: "I'll go no higher, John." Purchase price:
  **$72,500**. At 25, JDR controlled **Cleveland's largest refinery**, 500
  barrels/day — twice the nearest rival
- "It was the day that determined my career. I felt the bigness of it, but
  I was as calm as I am talking to you now"

### Laura Celestia Spelman ("Cettie")

- Met during Central High School. Practical-minded: taking commercial
  courses. Father **Harvey Buel Spelman**: Ohio legislator, progressive
  school creator. Mother **Lucy Henry Spelman**: uncompromising abolitionist,
  ran Underground Railroad station; **Sojourner Truth** spent days with them
- Spelmans went bankrupt in 1851. Cettie: valedictorian, speech **"I Can
  Paddle My Own Canoe."** Attended **Oread Collegiate Institute**
  (Worcester, MA). Rockefeller proposed "in a business-like way"; spent
  $118 on diamond ring (wildly out of character)
- **September 8, 1864**: married. **Worked the morning of his wedding day**;
  told foreman: "Treat them well, but see that they work." Spent $15.75 on
  wedding ring. "The Sponge" in the Oil Regions

## Chapter 6: The Poetry of the Age

### Gilded Age Context

- Post-Civil War: most fertile era for "schemers and dreamers, sharp-elbowed
  men." **Mark Twain** and **Charles Dudley Warner**: *The Gilded Age*.
  **The Great Barbecue** dominated by **Jay Cooke**, **Vanderbilt**,
  **Gould**, **Drew**, **Fisk**. First transcontinental railroad 1869
- **Kerosene** emerged as economic staple: extended workday, lit rural
  darkness. **Congressman James Garfield** (1865): "Oil, not cotton, is
  King now." By 1866, **two-thirds of Cleveland kerosene went overseas**

### Expansion: Standard Works & Vertical Integration

- December 1865: **Standard Works** with brother William — combined with
  Excelsior made JDR Cleveland's leading refiner. Foreman **Ambrose
  McGregor**. Catchphrase: **"That's right, eternal vigilance!"**
- Barrel-making: under $1 (vs. $2.50 from coopers). Dried oak in kilns.
  Expanded by-products: benzine, paraffin, petroleum jelly. Refineries
  operated full capacity even when demand slackened
- Refiners lived in terror of fire — **Mark Hanna's** refinery burned to
  the ground 1867. Gasoline was a useless waste product; refiners let it
  run into rivers. Rockefeller kept horses and hose carts ready

### William Rockefeller in New York (1866)

- Dispatched to oversee exports. European buyers were true price arbiters —
  "sat there like a lot of vultures." William's defining trait: **absolute
  reliability**. Contrasting temperament: bluff, intuitive vs. John's
  analytic reserve

### Capital: Dependence on Banks

- Wall Street saw oil as risky; refused to finance. JDR "almost on our
  knees" at banks. Key tactic: never appear eager to borrow. When offered
  $50,000 while desperately seeking $15,000, stalled: "can you give me
  twenty-four hours to think it over?" **Key principle**: always moved into
  battle backed by abundant cash

### Henry Morrison Flagler — The Indispensable Partner

- **Henry Morrison Flagler** (b. 1830): nine years older, son of
  impecunious Presbyterian pastor. Dropped out at 14; married into
  **Harkness** family. Civil War blunder: salt company in Saginaw went
  bankrupt. Saginaw salt companies formed a **cartel** — direct precedent
  for Standard Oil strategy
- Daily walks built seamless partnership. Back-to-back desks; collective
  letter-writing — passed drafts until "not one syllable more" than needed.
  Flagler could "teach the fine points of contract law to lawyers"
- Through Flagler: **$100,000** from **Stephen V. Harkness** (fortune from
  inside tip on 1862 liquor tax). Irony: temperance advocate received key
  capital from liquor gains
- March 4, 1867: **Rockefeller, Andrews and Flagler** — JDR was
  twenty-seven. Flagler: "a friendship founded on business was superior to
  a business founded on friendship." Mastermind of railroad negotiations —
  "the single most controversial aspect of Standard Oil history"

### Transportation and the Rebate System

- Transportation costs: the decisive competitive variable in oil. Cleveland's
  edge: multiple competing routes. Rockefeller and Flagler played three
  railroads against each other. Even manipulated **Jay Gould** — who called
  Rockefeller "the highest genius for constructive organization"

### The Lake Shore Deal (1868)

- Flagler cut deal with **General J. H. Devereux** (Lake Shore Railroad):
  rates cut to **$1.65/barrel** vs. listed **$2.40**. In exchange,
  Rockefeller promised 60 carloads daily. Railroads acquired a **vested
  interest in oil monopoly**
- **Tarbell** called it "original sin." Chernow: before his first rebate,
  JDR was already the world's largest refiner. Scale enabled the deal.
  Deals were **oral, never written**. JDR summoned **Commodore Vanderbilt**
  (age 74) to *come to him*
- Rebates were **industry-wide**: PA RR had granted thousands before.
  Railroad trackage doubled to 70,000 miles in eight years. JDR's defense:
  "Who can buy beef cheapest — the housewife, the steward, or the
  commissary for an army?"

## Chapter 7: Millionaires' Row

### 424 Euclid Avenue (1868)

- **"Millionaires' Row"**: home to **Henry B. Payne**, **Amasa Stone**,
  **John Hay**. JDR's $40,000 house deliberately modest — more money on
  stables (100+ feet long, elaborate chandeliers). "I hate frills"
- **Passion for trotters**: $10,000-$12,500 thoroughbreds; stockholder in
  **Cleveland Driving Park Company**. Racing style: patient, coaxing

### Domestic Life

- Opposite of philandering father. Social circle: family, business, church.
  Temperance absolute. Telegraph wire between home and office; napped daily
  after lunch. Despite calm: "For years on end I never had a solid night's
  sleep, worrying about how it was to come out"

### The Children

- **Bessie** (1866), **Alice** (1869, died age 1), **Alta** (1871),
  **Edith** (1872), **John Jr.** (1874). Delivered by **Dr. Myra Herrick**,
  Cleveland's first woman doctor. JDR: affectionate, playful father
- **"Make-believe market economy"**: 2¢ killing flies, 10¢ sharpening
  pencils, 5¢/hour practicing instruments, 2¢/day abstaining from candy.
  Shared one bicycle among four. John Jr. wore dresses until age 8.
  Morning prayers; penny fine for latecomers. Sunday heavily regimented.
  Cards forbidden; one piece of cheese daily to teach self-restraint

### Cettie Rockefeller

- More pinchpenny than John — patches on clothes, said a woman needed only
  two dresses. Narrowed intellectually after marriage. Their congruence
  left no critical voice to check business excesses

### Big Bill's Wandering Life

- Bill and **Margaret** in Illinois as **"Dr. William Levingston"** —
  notorious quack. John maintained "frosty civility." Eliza rotated among
  children's homes; grandchildren knew only grandfather "lived an odd life
  somewhere out West"

## Chapter 8: Conspirators

### Oil Industry Crisis

- By 1870, refining capacity **triple** crude pumped; 90% operating in the
  red. Market not self-correcting. JDR diagnosed "ruinous competition" and
  envisioned a giant cartel

### Formation of Standard Oil (January 10, 1870)

- **Standard Oil Company (Ohio)**: JDR president, William VP, Flagler
  secretary/treasurer. Capitalized at **$1 million**. Already 10% of
  American refining. Leading men received **no salary** — profit from
  shares only. Paid **105% dividends** first year

### The South Improvement Company (SIC)

- Originated with **Tom Scott** (Pennsylvania Railroad). Presented by
  **Peter H. Watson**, November 30, 1871. Alliance of three railroads and
  select refiners. **Rebates up to 50%** PLUS **"drawbacks"** — rebates on
  every barrel shipped by *rivals*. Also intelligence on competitors.
  "An astonishing piece of knavery"
- Rockefeller's group controlled 900 of 2,000 shares. His letters to Cettie
  contradict later claims: "Indeed the project grows on me." No qualms:
  "It was right before me and my God"

### The SIC Leak (February 1872)

- Substitute freight agent promulgated rates prematurely. **3,000** stormed
  **Titusville Opera House**. Young **Archbold** denounced "the great
  anaconda." JDR crowned **"the Mephistopheles of Cleveland."** Death
  threats; revolver by bed. A fourteen-year-old **Ida Tarbell** watched —
  formative moment

### The Cleveland Massacre (Feb 17 - March 28, 1872)

- Swallowed **22 of 26 Cleveland competitors** — six in one 48-hour period.
  Started at top: **Clark, Payne & Co.** ($400,000); **Colonel Oliver H.
  Payne** thunderstruck by Standard's profits. Standard pitch: "absolute
  control... turn over your refinery to my appraisers"
- Paid typically a **quarter of original costs**. Often bought plants to
  **shut them down**. **Isaac Hewitt** (original boss) sold for $65,000;
  JDR: "I have ways of making money you know nothing about"
- **Perjured himself** in Hanna lawsuit. **Ella Grant Wilson**: her father
  "went almost insane... left his church and never entered a church
  afterward"

### Ideological Defense of Monopoly

- "The day of combination is here to stay. Individualism has gone, never to
  return." Surprisingly, a **refutation** of social Darwinism: Standard was
  the antidote. God was "an honorary shareholder." Standard: "an angel of
  mercy, reaching down from the sky"

---

## Chapter 9: The New Monarch

### Pittsburgh Plan (1872)

- **National Refiners' Association** — Titusville rejected as "old SIC in
  disguise." Eyewitness on JDR: "He took me all in, saw just how much
  fight he could expect from me, and I knew it." Dissolved June 24, 1873.
  Lesson: only **outright ownership** would work

### Panic of 1873

- **Jay Cooke** failed; crude crashed to 48 cents/barrel. JDR slashed
  dividends, acquired rivals at distress prices

### Secrecy

- Acquired companies kept original names, stationery; no mention of
  Standard Oil. Internal correspondence in code. Warned new partners not to
  flaunt wealth: "keep this contract secret even from your wife"

### Pittsburgh and Philadelphia (1874)

- Saratoga Springs summit with **Charles Lockhart** and **William G.
  Warden**. Warden discovered JDR could manufacture kerosene below his own
  costs. Standard absorbed more than half of Pittsburgh's capacity. Key
  recruits: **Charles Pratt** (Astral Oil), **Henry H. Rogers** (versatile
  executive, later patented naphtha separation)

### Completing the Monopoly

- May 1875: covertly bought **J. N. Camden** in West Virginia. Camden
  worked for JDR while B&O president Garrett thought he was fighting
  Standard. By late 1877: **~90% of U.S. oil refining** — age 38, five
  years after Cleveland Massacre

### Railroads, Tank Cars, Pipelines

- Built own tank-car fleet; forced **leakage allowance** on tanks that
  didn't leak — 62 gallons gratis per car. **Rutter Circular** (Sept 1874)
  equalized freight rates, destroying Oil Creek's advantage
- **Daniel O'Day** launched pipeline network; by summer 1874, Standard
  controlled nearly one-third of crude flow

## Chapter 10: Sphinx

### The Sphinx Persona

- Arrived **9:15 sharp**, immaculately dressed. "Wraithlike" movements.
  Trained face into **stony mask**. "Success comes from keeping the ears
  open and the mouth closed." When angry, grew eerily quiet
- Hired talented people "as found, not as needed." "The ability to deal
  with people is as purchasable a commodity as sugar or coffee, and I pay
  more for that ability." Once trusted: "throw them in and make them sink
  or swim"
- Control mechanism: **the ledger book**. "I charted my course by figures,
  nothing but figures." **Little red notebook** on facility tours: "I have
  seen the sweat start out on the foreheads"

### The One-Drop-of-Solder Story

- "How many drops of solder on each can?" "Forty." "Have you tried
  thirty-eight?" 38 leaked; **39 became the standard**. Saved $2,500 first
  year, compounded to "many hundreds of thousands"

### Sam Andrews Buyout

- Andrews said **$1 million**; JDR had check next morning. Flipped shares
  to **Vanderbilt** for $300,000 profit. Had Andrews held: worth **$900
  million by 1930s**. Andrews built 100-room "Andrews's Folly." "Before
  selling he was sore at John. After selling he was sore at himself"

---

## Chapter 11: The Holy Family

### Forest Hill Estate

- 1873: 79 acres four miles east. Grew to 700+ acres, 136 employees;
  racetrack, two lakes, 20 miles of roadway. Tarbell mocked it; JDR loved
  function over refinement

### John D. Rockefeller Jr.

- Born January 29, 1874. Sickly; "shy, ill-adjusted and frail." JDR:
  "a beloved companion. He had a genius with children." No corporal
  punishment. Money lesson: when Junior proposed lump-sum church giving,
  JDR made him calculate 11 cents interest lost

### Religion and Church

- **Euclid Avenue Baptist Church**: plain brownstone, lower-middle-class,
  chosen deliberately. JDR covered half the budget. Sunday-school
  superintendent 1872-1905. Friday prayer meetings for 40 years. "I believe
  it is a religious duty to get all the money you can, fairly and honestly;
  to keep all you can; and to give away all you can"

### Rockefeller Siblings

- **Lucy**: died 1878, age 40. **Mary Ann**: reclusive, funereal black.
  **Frank**: aped Big Bill — drinker, choleric. Married **Helen Scofield**
  (daughter of Cleveland Massacre target). 1876: testified against John.
  Repeatedly rescued from bankruptcy. 1886: made VP of Standard Oil of Ohio
  — then enforced monopoly policies he'd excoriated. Gradually shunted
  aside; "retained because his name was Rockefeller"

## Chapter 12: Insurrection in the Oil Fields

### Bradford Boom and Immediate-Shipment Crisis (1878)

- Bradford, PA fields opened 1875; prices collapsed. O'Day built tankage to
  4.5 million barrels. Standard accepted oil only for immediate shipment,
  quoted crude 20% below. Masked midnight marches "in regular ku-klux
  fashion." Standard: $60 dividend on $100 par

### The Empire Transportation Company War

- **Tom Scott** used **Empire** (500 miles pipeline, 1,000 tank cars).
  JDR's ultimatum; idled Pittsburgh refineries; enlisted Erie and NY
  Central. **Great Railroad Strike of 1877**: Pittsburgh alone — 500 tank
  cars, 120 locomotives, 27 buildings torched. Scott capitulated: fire sale
  for **$3.4 million**. JDR raced bank to bank: "I must have all you've
  got!" Standard became **"evener"** of railroad pool

### The Tidewater Pipe Line Battle

- **Byron Benson**: revolutionary 110-mile pipeline from Bradford to
  Williamsport. Standard's obstruction: bought "dead lines," warned
  manufacturers, scare stories. **May 28, 1879**: oil crossed the mountains.
  But within a year, Tidewater joined Rockefeller's pool. By 1882:
  **Standard 88.5% / Tidewater 11.5%**

### Legislative Corruption

- Flagler wired $10,000 to Albany lobbyists. **Camden** monitored federal
  bills in Standard code. NY state senator **A. N. Cole** wrote asking for
  "attorneys" paid in government bonds lest cash "be construed into
  corruption"

### Clarion County Indictments (1879) and Hepburn Committee

- Nine officials indicted — Rockefeller began "a thirty-year career as a
  fugitive from justice." Christmas 1879: Standard renounced (or seemed to)
  secret rebates; criminal cases scuttled
- **Hepburn Committee**: NY Central had **6,000 secret contracts**. Called
  Standard "a mysterious organization whose members decline giving a history
  lest testimony be used to convict them of a crime"

## Chapter 13: Seat of Empire

### Rockefeller at 40

- Standard Oil stock worth **$18 million** (~$265M in 1996). "As lean as a
  grey-hound" with early stress symptoms

### Move to New York (1883-84)

- **Benjamin Brewster**: "You can't have one head in Cleveland and another
  in New York." Home: **4 West Fifty-fourth Street** — brownstone purchased
  from **Arabella Worsham** (mistress of **Collis Huntington**). Moorish
  salon, Turkish bath, paintings by Corot, Meissonier. Site later became
  MoMA sculpture garden

### Standard Oil Colony on Fifth Avenue

- Flagler took SE corner of 54th Street; William NE corner; Brewster next
  door. JDR never acquired cosmopolitan tastes; built ice-skating rink,
  raced horses in Central Park. Took the Sixth Avenue elevated train to
  work for a nickel each morning. Completely avoided Gilded Age scene —
  no costume balls, theater, opera, clubs

### 26 Broadway (May 1, 1885)

- Built on site of **Alexander Hamilton's** former home. World's most
  famous business address — Standard Oil's name didn't appear outside.
  Nine stories, granite, secret-rim locks. "A cave for pirates, a den for
  the cutthroats of commerce"
- Each noon: executive committee lunch on top floor. Directors in silk hats;
  **Charles Pratt** held head of table; Flagler to his right; then JDR

### Consensus Leadership and the Trust (1882)

- Canvassed everyone's opinion first; no initiatives opposed by board.
  Never said "I" — preferred "we." **Standard Oil made few major strategic
  missteps**
- **1882 Standard Oil Trust Agreement**: drafted by **Samuel C. T. Dodd**
  and Flagler, kept secret six years. Nine trustees received stock of
  Standard of Ohio and 40 other companies. JDR held one-third; trust
  certificates worth **$19 million**. **$70 million enterprise** controlling
  90% of refineries and pipelines
- Committee system: executive committee approved all expenditures above
  $5,000. Shut down 30+ obsolete refineries; concentrated >25% of kerosene
  in three monster plants: Cleveland, Bayonne, Philadelphia

### Family Life in New York

- Household: "a tranquil island of missionary work, temperance meetings,
  and prayer vigils." Bessie at **Vassar**. Junior at **Cutler School**
  (grade average 98.1 at 13, then nervous collapse). Custom **Browning
  School** built around Junior
- 1887 European trip: wired secretary from 460 miles out; fired tour guide;
  declined then accepted papal audience. Trunk of begging letters

### Philanthropy

- Core: **wholesale giving** over scattershot. Matching grants. Supported
  **Edward Judson** and social gospel. $40,000 toward **Judson Memorial
  Church**
- **Spelman Seminary**: 1882, committed to black women's school. **Packard**
  and **Giles** opened in Atlanta church basement (1881), eleven students.
  JDR: **"Are you going to stick?"** Named it Spelman. Later: **Martin
  Luther King Jr.'s** mother and grandmother were alumnae

## Chapter 14: The Puppeteer

### Global Dominance

- **70% of American oil** went abroad. Distributed cheap lamps and wicks
  gratis in Asia. One traveler in 1874 saw Standard kerosene in ancient
  Babylon and Nineveh

### The Electric Light Threat

- **Thomas Edison** announced practical lightbulb fall 1878; by 1879 burned
  100 hours straight. September 4, 1882: Edison threw switch in **J. P.
  Morgan's** office. By 1885 only 250,000 lightbulbs — not enough to
  displace kerosene quickly; Rockefeller had time to adapt
- Natural gas: **J. N. Pew** piped it to Pittsburgh 1883. Standard secretly
  controlled gas companies in Titusville, Oil City, Buffalo and thirteen
  other localities. Detroit franchise: Standard furnished $15,000 cash and
  $10,000 in gas stock to sway politicians

### Russian Threat and International Competition

- **Robert Nobel** built pipeline and world's first oil tanker (*Zoroaster*)
  at Baku. **Rothschilds** built refineries. Standard created **Anglo-
  American Oil Co.** (Britain), **Deutsche-Amerikanische** (Germany).
  Retained **~80% of world markets**
- **Royal Dutch** (1884, Sumatra). **Marcus Samuel** cut delivery via Suez
  from 4 months to 1; renamed **Shell** (1897). Standard spread antisemitic
  rumors about "Hebrew influence"

### Rockefeller as "Master Puppeteer"

- Only Standard figure with no operational responsibility. Replied in
  **opaque letters** devoid of names. Chernow: "He knew everything"
- Predatory pricing: high in monopoly markets, slashed where independents
  appeared. Sold **a quarter of all oil at cost**. Never sought 100% —
  kept ~10% for political prudence. Intelligence network tracked every
  barrel; station managers expected **85% market share**
- Marketing empire: eleven districts. Sold stoves and lamps at cost.
  Chess, Carley and Company threatened grocers: "buy our oil or we start a
  grocery store and sell at cost." **George Rice** driven from Anniston,
  Alabama by sustained below-cost selling
- **Henry Demarest Lloyd**: *Atlantic Monthly* 1881 "Story of a Great
  Monopoly" — six printings. Popularized **octopus** nickname
- **William G. Warden** letter (May 1887): "We are quoted as the
  representative of all that is evil... None of us would choose such a
  reputation." JDR sent a bland note and never addressed it again

## Chapter 15: Widow's Funeral

### Big Bill's Later Life

- Recruited **Dr. Charles H. Johnston** as fake **Native American** — paid
  Bill $1,000 tuition for apprenticeship in fraud. Bill pulled $200/day
- 1881: John bought Bill **160-acre ranch, Park River, ND** — condition:
  never bring Margaret. Injured, Bill blurted his real identity to Johnston

### Eliza's Death (1889)

- Died March 28, age 76 — never knowing Bill had a second wife. John
  instructed preacher to announce she died a widow. Forced Bill to sign
  documents as "William Avery Rockefeller, widower" — blocking Margaret
- September 1902 Forest Hill party: Bill at 92, 250 pounds, gouty — won
  turkey shoot handily, taunted John: "I suppose he is a good Baptist, but
  look out how you trade with him"

### Frank Rockefeller

- "Hot-tempered and vindictive... Sometimes I thought he was insane." Lost
  up to $100,000 in Chicago oil speculations. John pulled him from
  bankruptcy repeatedly. Made VP of Standard Oil of Ohio — enforced policies
  he'd denounced. Gradually sidelined

## Chapter 16: A Matter of Trust

### Lima Crude Breakthrough

- No major oil outside PA for 25 years. **Archbold** (1885): "I'll *drink*
  every gallon west of the Mississippi!" JDR pointed skyward: **"The Lord
  will provide"**
- May 1885: Lima, Ohio — high sulfur, "smells like polecats." Hired
  **Herman Frasch** to fix it. Board deadlock (**Charles Pratt** resisting).
  JDR pledged **$3 million of own funds**. Oct 13, 1888: Frasch succeeded.
  Standard placed test labs in every refinery
- Moved into production: $22 million in two years. By 1891 controlled a
  quarter of all production. **Standard Oil of Indiana** at Whiting
  (36,000 barrels/day). **Dr. William M. Burton** later discovered
  "cracking" — essential for auto age

### Standard Oil's Financial Returns

- **Flagler** testified: average 13% on net assets (understated). **Bureau
  of Corporations** calculated **19% return from 1882 to 1896**. Dividends
  sometimes declared at 200% — misleading because real capital ~10x
  official capitalization. January 1885 "200% dividend" = ~20% on real
  capital

### Politics and Legislation

- **Henry B. Payne** Senate affair (1884): Ohio became "Coal-oil
  Legislature." **Camden**: "Politics is dearer than it used to be"
- **Interstate Commerce Act** (1887): Standard publicly complied;
  **Colonel Thompson** found workarounds immediately
- **Sherman Antitrust Act** (July 2, 1890): President **Taft** later
  identified Standard as chief cause. Nearly toothless initially —
  paradoxically increased concentration via mergers
- Chernow: Standard taught that "free markets, if left completely to their
  own devices, can wind up terribly *un*free"

## Chapter 17: Captains of Erudition

### Burden of Wealth

- Net worth ~**$150 million**; income **$750/hour**. **Gates**: "hunted,
  stalked and hounded almost like a wild animal." One steamer: **5,000
  begging letters from Europe**

### University of Chicago

- **Augustus H. Strong** pitched NYC Baptist university; JDR resisted.
  **Thomas W. Goodspeed** and **William Rainey Harper** (prodigy: college
  at 10, B.A. at 14, Ph.D. at 18) proposed Chicago instead
- **Frederick T. Gates** recruited as ABES executive secretary; championed
  Chicago. JDR's **$600,000 pledge** (May 1889), conditional on $400,000
  from others. At Tremont Temple, clergymen waved handkerchiefs
- **Carnegie** (June 1889): "The man who dies thus rich dies disgraced."
  Gates faulted Carnegie for vanity
- Harper accepted presidency February 1891 after JDR consented to extra
  million for Morgan Park Seminary. **120+ faculty appointments**: raided
  Ivy League, netted nine presidents. Faculty: **John Dewey**, **George
  Herbert Mead**, **Albion Small**, **Thorstein Veblen**

### Hiring Gates (March 1891)

- JDR in broken health: "The pressure of these appeals has become too great
  for endurance." Donations: $124,000 (1889) → **$1.35 million** (1892)

### Health Crisis (1889-1892)

- **Pratt** died 1891; **Flagler** distracted. **Dr. Biggar**: "close to
  the edge of a breakdown." Eight months at Forest Hill. Contemplated
  **retirement** at fifty. Stopped going to 26 Broadway by **1897**

### University Opens (October 1, 1892)

- JDR refused to attend. **750 students**, one-fourth women. Beside the
  **1893 Columbian Exposition**. From a Ferris wheel: the fair and the
  university Standard Oil profits built
- Harper's spending violated every understanding. **Panic of 1893**: JDR
  transferred $500,000. **October 1895**: $3 million endowment pledge.
  The football story: Gates told coach **Amos Alonzo Stagg** at halftime
  (trailing 12-10); captain roared "Three million dollars!" — Chicago won
  22-12; students sang: "There was a man sent from God whose name was John"

### First Campus Visit (July 1897)

- "It is the best investment I ever made in my life... The good Lord gave me
  the money, and how could I withhold it from Chicago?"

### Academic Freedom — Bemis Controversy

- 1895: **Edward Bemis** dismissed after attacking Standard Oil's gas
  company. No evidence JDR directly ordered it. **Veblen** published
  *Theory of the Leisure Class* at Chicago (1899)

### Harper's Fiscal Defiance

- Violated every written understanding; letters of remonstrance
  "mysteriously disappeared." By 1897: **$200,000 deficit**. Gates
  delivered humiliating rebuke; transcript deposited in Rockefeller's safe.
  Harper reportedly circumvented the ban on asking for money by *praying
  aloud* for funds in Rockefeller's presence
