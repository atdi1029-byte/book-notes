## The Ludic Fallacy: Games vs. Real Risk

### The Casino and the Unknown Unknown
- Taleb discusses a conference at a casino where he prepared remarks on **Black Swans** and the limits of predictability
- **Ludic fallacy** (from Latin *ludus*, games): the error of confusing game-like, calculable risks with real-world uncertainty
- In a casino, probabilities are **known, Gaussian, and computable**—odds are defined in advance; "36 black" pops up 95% of the time
- **Real life has different risks**: odds are unknown, sources of uncertainty are undefined, outcomes follow **scalable, wild randomness** (not bell curves)
- Casino's four largest losses fell **completely outside their risk models**: tiger attack on performer (Siegfried & Roy), dynamite threat by disgruntled contractor, tax-fraud filing by employee, kidnapping of owner's daughter

### The Error of Applying Gaming to Reality
- **Economists draw false distinction** between **Knightian risks** (computable) and **Knightian uncertainty** (non-computable)
- People trained in probability theory often mistake games for the real world—dice, coin flips, bell curves are "laboratory contraptions," absent from life
- **Confirmation error** (from Chapter 5): we use games as proof that probability theory works, then claim this generalizes
- We **overestimate luck in games** while **underestimating it in life**
- Casinos spent hundreds of millions on surveillance and probability models—yet their actual risks came from Black Swans outside the models

### Classical Thinking on Uncertainty
- **Simon Foucher** (1673, *Dissertation on the Search for Truth*): teaches the art of doubting; humans are "dogma-prone from our mother's wombs"
- **Cicero** wrote that probability remains "fuzzy throughout," and certainty-seeking is a vice—skepticism, not calculation, is the path
- Before Enlightenment "scientific" mentality, people understood: **you cannot compute uncertainty**
- Modern education (college, PhDs in probability theory) trains minds to accept the ludic fallacy uncritically

## Wrapping Up Part One: The Cosmetic Rises to the Surface

### Why We Miss Black Swans
- All Part One topics reduce to **one single problem**: the gap between what we know and what we assume we know
- **Bildungsphilisters** (Nietzsche's term): learned blue-collar thinkers who confuse disciplines as separate, when they're all the same problem
- We naturally favor the **tangible, visible, sensational, narrated**—not the abstract, invisible, quiet
- We confirm visible events that happened, while **ignoring what could have happened but didn't** (silent evidence)
- **Platonification**: mistaking well-organized knowledge for reality; we become blind to what exists outside our models
- This is why we fail to see Black Swans: "the ones that did not happen were too abstract"

### The Problem of Information
- **Humans are naturally shallow** with abstract matters—we lack context; randomness and uncertainty are abstractions
- **Silent evidence problem**: the dark side of the moon is harder to see; **shining light on the unseen costs energy**—both computationally and mentally
- We don't recognize what we don't see, and we're unaware of this blindness

### Distance from Primates: The Virtue of Shallow Thinking
- Throughout history, cultures created distinctions between "higher" and "lower" forms—Greeks vs. barbarians, English gentlemen vs. workers, Nietzsche's Übermensch
- **Modern stoic virtue**: a dignified system determining elegance through idle pursuits, not manual labor; separates results from efforts
- Taleb's proposal: to reach a "higher form of life," **shut down television, minimize news, ignore blogs**, train reasoning to control decisions, learn to **distinguish the sensational from the empirical**
- Avoid **"tunneling"**—narrow focus on one thing
- **Prediction is the real test of understanding**, not narration (we're good at explaining what happened; terrible at predicting what will)

## Part Two: We Just Can't Predict

### The Three Black Swans
- Computer, Internet, laser: all **unplanned, unpredicted, unappreciated upon discovery**
- We have a retrospective illusion of planning (master plans that didn't exist)
- We continue to use forecasting tools that **exclude rare events**, making the world seem more "usual" than it is
- Prediction is institutionalized despite our **appalling record**

### The Berra-Hadamard-Poincairé-Hayek-Popper Conjecture
- **Yogi Berra**: "It is tough to make predictions, especially about the future"
- **Jacques Hadamard, Henri Poincaré, Friedrich Hayek, Karl Popper**: all examined humanity's **structural, built-in limits** to predicting
- As models improve and complexity increases, the role of Black Swans grows—prediction becomes **harder, not easier**
- Quote (attributed to Berra): "The future ain't what it used to be"

## Chapter 10: The Scandal of Prediction

### Sydney Opera House: A Monument to Our Failure
- Built to open in 1963 at AUS$7 million cost; actually opened >10 years later at AUS$104 million
- Australians built a "symbol of epistemological arrogance"—a monument to failure in predicting and planning
- Planning failures exist in the Soviet Union, wars, all major historical events—but Sydney Opera House is the "mildest illustration"
- This chapter has two themes:
  1. **Epistemic arrogance**: we know we don't know much, but have a habit of knowing "a little bit more than we actually do"
  2. **Implications for all prediction activities**

### Why We Don't Discuss Prediction Failures
- **The scandal of prediction**: we predict constantly yet never discuss our record; we don't examine how we always miss big events
- We "tunnel" while looking into the future, acting as if the future is usual when **it is not a Platonic category**

### Epistemic Arrogance: The Catherine II Experiment
- **Epistéme** (Greek): knowledge; **epistemic arrogance** = hubris concerning the limits of knowledge
- Knowledge *does* grow, but **confidence grows faster**, making increased knowledge paradoxically increase confusion, ignorance, conceit
- **Experiment**: pick random numbers (e.g., lovers of Catherine II, population of Rajastan); ask people to estimate ranges with 98% confidence; expect only 2% to fall outside
  - Harvard Business School students: 45% error rate (not 2%)
  - Later studies across populations: typically 15-30% error, not 2%
  - **MBAs are particularly arrogant**; janitors and cabbies are more humble
- People are 22x overconfident about what they know
- **Epistemic arrogance bears a double effect**: we overestimate what we know AND underestimate uncertainty by compressing the range of possible uncertain states

### Uncertainty and Real Life
- In real life, you don't know the odds; you must discover them
- **Any decision about the future is infected by epistemic arrogance**—we underestimate possibility of straying from initially envisioned course
- Example: divorce rates are 33-50%, yet divorced people claim "we got along so well" (not forecasting the gap)
- **Confirmation bias** (Chapter 5) + **underestimation of luck** combine to create chronic underestimation of uncertainty

### Information Toxicity: More Data ≠ Better Decisions
- **Trained professionals (economists, forecasters, finance PhDs) are MORE affected by knowledge impediments** than laypeople
- **Aristotle Onassis** example: Greek shipping magnate became wealthy not through desk work but through charm and people skills; his main tool was a notebook (not analysis)
- **Additional knowledge of business minutiae can be useless or toxic**
- Stuart Oskamp (1965 study): psychologists given successive case files on patients showed *increased* confidence but *no* increase in diagnostic accuracy—more information just made them more confident in the original diagnosis
- **Paul Slovic** (bookmakers & horse races): bookmakers given 10 most useful variables predicted well; given 10 more, accuracy didn't increase but confidence *soared*
- **"More is better" is a middlebrow belief**—often false; more information produces more noise and mistaken certainty

### Fire Hydrant Thought Experiment
- Show a blurry image of a fire hydrant, then sharpen it in steps; group seeing fewer intermediate steps recognizes it *faster*
- **More hypotheses → more false paths → worse recognition**
- **Theory stickiness**: once we produce a theory, we rarely change minds (confirmation bias + belief perseverance)
- Listening to hourly news is worse than reading a weekly magazine (longer intervals allow information filtering)

### The Expert Problem
- **The expert problem** has two forms:
  1. **Mild case**: arrogance with some competence
  2. **Severe case**: arrogance mixed with incompetence ("the empty suit")
- **Experts who tend to be experts**: livestock judges, astronomers, test pilots, soil judges, chess masters, physicists, mathematicians, accountants, grain inspectors, photo interpreters, insurance analysts (bell-curve statistics)
- **Not experts**: stockbrokers, clinical psychologists, psychiatrists, college admissions officers, court judges, councilors, personnel selectors, intelligence analysts, economists, financial forecasters, finance professors, political scientists, "risk experts," Bank for International Settlements staff, International Association of Financial Engineers staff, personal financial advisers
- **Pattern**: things that **move** require knowledge; things that **don't move** seem to have experts
- **Things that move are often Black Swan-prone** → experts are narrowly focused ("tunnel"), which works when Black Swans aren't consequential

### Experts Don't Know What They Don't Know
- **Lack of knowledge + delusion about knowledge quality go together**—same process makes you less knowledgeable AND more satisfied
- **Robert Trivers** (evolutionary psychology): humans good at predicting power balance (raids/wars); poor at large-scale wars (new to human heritage); we misestimate duration and power (Lebanon war, Vietnam, Iraq)
- **Greeks vs. Persians**: ancestral wars; humans good at predicting. **Modern large-scale wars**: Black Swan territory; we're terrible

### Prediction vs. Accuracy: Economic Forecasting
- Economists produce abundant forecasts but **never check how they fared after formulation**
- "Outlook for 200X" annual booklets are published without post-mortems
- **Data available**: economic variables, market calls, TV "expert" commentary
- Can test: compare economists (in suits, prestigious institutions, seven-figure earners) to **Mikhail the cabdriver** (control)—would cabdriver beat professional economists? Likely yes on many metrics
- Most economists **earn seven figures, rank as stars**, but "are foolish enough to publish" their projections for posterity
- **No consequential difference** between economist and non-expert forecasters; or economists are worse than random chance on many variables

## Key Concepts

- **Ludic fallacy**: mistaking game probabilities for real-world uncertainty
- **Knightian risks** vs. **Knightian uncertainty**: computable vs. non-computable
- **Epistemological arrogance**: confidence grows faster than knowledge
- **Silent evidence**: events that didn't happen are invisible to analysis
- **Prediction records**: universally terrible; never audited; continually trusted
- **Information toxicity**: additional data increases confidence without improving accuracy
- **Expert paradox**: domains without Black Swans have real experts; domains with Black Swans don't
- **Theory stickiness**: once formed, beliefs resist contrary evidence
