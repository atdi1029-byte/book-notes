Introduction

The central claim of this book is deceptively simple: trade wars between nations are not really about trade, and they are not really between nations. They are the international expression of class conflicts happening inside countries. When the rich in China, Germany, or any other surplus economy take too large a share of national income, their workers cannot afford to buy what they produce. The resulting gluts of savings and manufactured goods must go somewhere, and that somewhere has been the United States, which absorbs the world's excess at the cost of lost factories, swollen debts, and recurring financial crises.

Klein and Pettis frame this as an optimistic argument. The Chinese are not evil. The Germans are not scheming. The Americans are not lazy. The problems of the past few decades stem from massive transfers of income to the rich and the companies they control — transfers that distort not just the domestic economy of each surplus country but the entire global economy. If the diagnosis is correct, the cure does not require a zero-sum confrontation between nations. It requires each country to address its own internal distribution of income.

"A global conflict between economic classes within countries is being misinterpreted as conflicts between countries with competing interests." — Klein & Pettis

The danger, they warn, is a repetition of the 1930s. The breakdown of the international economic and financial order in that decade undermined democracy, encouraged virulent nationalism, and ultimately led to war, revolution, and genocide. The mechanisms are structurally identical: when purchasing power is systematically transferred from workers to elites, the elites' excess savings flood across borders, destabilizing the countries that absorb them. The book traces this mechanism through China, Germany, and the United States with relentless specificity.

The argument is built on a framework from international macroeconomics that most people never encounter: the identity between the current account balance and the financial account balance. A country's trade surplus or deficit is not a scorecard of competitiveness. It is the mechanical consequence of how income is distributed within that country, how much is saved versus spent, and how financial flows move across borders. Once you see trade through this lens, the entire debate about tariffs, currency manipulation, and bilateral deficits looks like treating symptoms while ignoring the disease.

The book proceeds through three detailed case studies — China, Germany, and the United States — showing how the same mechanism produces surpluses in the first two and deficits in the third. China uses financial repression and the hukou system to suppress household income. Germany uses wage stagnation and constitutional fiscal austerity to starve domestic demand. Both countries transfer income from workers to elites, producing more than they can consume and flooding the rest of the world with excess savings and manufactured goods. The United States, as the issuer of the world's reserve currency, is forced to absorb the surplus — not as a privilege but as a burden that has hollowed out American manufacturing and inflated dangerous debt bubbles.

Klein and Pettis are explicit about what they are not arguing. They do not oppose trade. They do not advocate autarky or protectionism. They believe the gains from international commerce are real and substantial. What they oppose is a system in which the gains are captured by elites in surplus countries while the costs are borne by workers in both surplus and deficit countries. The solution is not to close borders but to fix the internal distribution of income that makes trade destructive rather than beneficial.

The book draws heavily on economic history, tracing the pattern back through the Poyais fraud of the 1820s, the Baring Crisis of 1890, the collapse of the gold standard in the 1930s, the Bretton Woods system, the Asian Financial Crisis of 1997-98, and the global financial crisis of 2008. In each case, the same structural dynamic repeats: surplus capital accumulates in one part of the world, the financial system creates assets to absorb it, lending standards deteriorate, bubbles inflate, and crises follow. The specifics change but the mechanism is invariant.

Chapter 1: From Adam Smith to Tim Cook — The Transformation of Global Trade

The story of international trade begins with Adam Smith and David Ricardo, whose theories of absolute and comparative advantage promised that free trade would make everyone richer. Ricardo's famous example of English cloth and Portuguese wine showed that even if one country was better at producing everything, both countries would benefit from specializing and trading. For two centuries, this idea has been the intellectual foundation of the global trading system.

Klein and Pettis do not dispute that trade creates wealth. What they challenge is the assumption that the gains from trade are distributed fairly within each country. Ricardo himself acknowledged that trade would create losers — workers in industries that could not compete — but he assumed they would find new employment quickly enough that the disruption would be manageable. That assumption has not always held.

The chapter traces the tension between free trade and protectionism through American history. Alexander Hamilton's 1791 Report on the Subject of Manufactures argued that the United States needed to protect its infant industries from British competition, and Congress responded with the Dallas Tariff of 1816. Friedrich List, the German economist who studied American industrialization firsthand, carried the infant-industry argument back to Europe and gave it theoretical rigor. The debate has never been settled: it resurfaces whenever a country's workers bear the costs of integration while its elites capture the gains.

The transformation of global trade in the late twentieth century changed the nature of the debate. The old model — countries trading finished goods across borders — gave way to global supply chains, where a single product might cross dozens of borders before reaching the consumer. Apple's iPhone is the canonical example: designed in California, assembled in China from components manufactured in Japan, South Korea, Taiwan, and Germany. The final assembly in China adds only a tiny fraction of the total value, yet the entire device shows up in trade statistics as a Chinese export to the United States.

This distortion matters because it inflates bilateral trade deficits and fuels political anger that may be misdirected. The US-China trade deficit looks enormous in gross terms but is much smaller when measured by value added. Meanwhile, the real action — the decisions about where to invest, where to hire, and how to divide the profits — happens inside multinational corporations whose interests do not align neatly with any single country.

The chapter also examines how the shipping container revolution, pioneered by Malcom McLean in the 1950s, slashed transportation costs by more than 90 percent and made global supply chains physically possible. Before containerization, shipping costs were high enough to function as a natural tariff. After it, the cost of moving goods across oceans became trivial compared to the cost of labor, making it rational for companies to chase the cheapest workers anywhere on the planet.

The rise of trade in intermediate goods — components rather than finished products — has made traditional trade statistics nearly useless for understanding who benefits from trade. By some estimates, roughly half of all international trade now consists of intermediate goods moving within supply chains. This means that tariffs on Chinese exports can raise costs for American manufacturers who depend on Chinese-made components, while doing nothing to address the underlying savings imbalances that drive the trade deficit.

"The real conflict is not between American workers and Chinese workers but between workers everywhere and the financial and business elites who benefit from the existing arrangement." — Klein & Pettis

The chapter sets up the book's central intellectual move: shifting the conversation from trade flows to capital flows, from competitiveness to income distribution, and from bilateral balances to the global savings glut. Every chapter that follows is an application of this framework to a specific country or mechanism.

Chapter 2: The Growth of Global Finance

If Chapter 1 reframes trade, Chapter 2 reframes finance. The explosive growth of cross-border capital flows since the 1980s has transformed the global economy in ways that most people — including most policymakers — do not fully appreciate. Before the 1980s, international finance was a relatively modest affair: countries traded goods, settled their balances, and occasionally borrowed from one another. By the 2000s, gross cross-border financial flows dwarfed trade flows by a factor of ten or more.

Klein and Pettis trace this transformation to the deregulation of capital markets in the United States and the United Kingdom during the Thatcher-Reagan era. The removal of capital controls, the liberalization of banking, and the rise of new financial instruments created a world in which money could move across borders almost instantaneously and in almost unlimited quantities. This was supposed to improve the allocation of capital: savings from rich countries would flow to developing countries where returns were higher, financing growth and convergence.

The reality has been nearly the opposite. Capital has consistently flowed from poor countries to rich ones — from China and the oil-exporting states to the United States and Western Europe. This is not because returns are higher in rich countries. It is because governments in surplus countries systematically suppress domestic spending and accumulate foreign financial assets, while the US financial system stands ready to create whatever assets those savers want to buy.

The chapter documents how the Poyais fraud of the 1820s — in which the Scottish adventurer Gregor MacGregor sold bonds for a fictional Central American country — illustrates a pattern that has repeated itself for two centuries. Whenever surplus capital accumulates in one part of the world and needs somewhere to go, the financial system produces assets to absorb it, regardless of whether those assets represent genuine economic value. The same dynamic drove the Baring Crisis of 1890, the emerging-market lending boom of the 1970s, and the subprime mortgage bubble of the 2000s.

A key insight is that international capital flows are not driven primarily by interest rate differentials or expected returns. They are driven by structural savings imbalances within countries. When China's government suppresses household consumption, the resulting surplus must be exported as capital. When Germany's corporate sector slashes wages and hoards profits, those profits must find borrowers somewhere. The capital does not flow because the destination offers attractive opportunities. It flows because it has nowhere else to go.

The authors draw a sharp distinction between "Scroogeville" — an economy where the rich save too much and the poor cannot spend enough — and a healthy economy where savings are channeled into productive domestic investment. In Scroogeville, the excess savings create a structural current account surplus that must be absorbed by the rest of the world, typically through debt bubbles and financial crises in the receiving countries.

The chapter also examines Peter Navarro's claim that bilateral trade deficits mechanically reduce GDP, showing that this view confuses accounting identities with causal mechanisms. A trade deficit is not a subtraction from output. It is the consequence of a particular pattern of spending, saving, and financial flows that may or may not be beneficial depending on the circumstances. The obsession with bilateral balances distracts from the real issue: why are some countries structurally incapable of spending what they earn?

"Whenever surplus capital accumulates in one part of the world and needs somewhere to go, the financial system will produce assets to absorb it, regardless of whether those assets represent genuine value." — Klein & Pettis

Chapter 3: Saving, Investment, and Imbalances

This chapter is the theoretical backbone of the book. Klein and Pettis lay out the macroeconomic identities that connect income distribution within a country to its trade balance with the rest of the world. The argument is built on a simple accounting fact: a country's current account balance equals the difference between what it produces and what it spends, which in turn equals the difference between national saving and national investment. If a country saves more than it invests domestically, the surplus must flow abroad as a current account surplus.

The critical question is what determines a country's saving rate. The conventional view holds that saving is a cultural trait — Asians save because of Confucian values, Germans save because of Protestant thrift, Americans spend because of consumerist culture. Klein and Pettis demolish this view with a simple observation: a country's saving rate is determined primarily by its income distribution. Rich people save a much larger share of their income than poor people. When income shifts from workers to the wealthy, the national saving rate rises mechanically, regardless of culture.

This has profound implications. China does not have a high saving rate because the Chinese are culturally thrifty. It has a high saving rate because the Chinese government has systematically transferred income from households to the state and to businesses through financial repression (below-market interest rates on deposits), wage suppression, and the hukou system (which denies migrant workers access to social services). Chinese household consumption as a share of GDP is extraordinarily low — not because Chinese families choose not to spend, but because they retain far less of what they produce than families in any comparable economy.

Germany tells a parallel story. German household consumption has been suppressed by stagnant wages, the elimination of the net wealth tax, a regressive inheritance tax system that exempts family-owned businesses, and the Schuldenbremse (debt brake) that constitutionally prohibits the government from borrowing to invest. The German corporate sector has been running a current account surplus of more than 2 percent of GDP since the early 2000s, hoarding profits rather than investing them domestically.

The chapter introduces the concept of balance of payments mechanics. When a country has a current account surplus, it must by definition have a financial account deficit — meaning it is sending capital abroad. Those capital outflows must be absorbed by deficit countries, which are forced to borrow more, invest more, or consume more than they otherwise would. The surplus country's internal decision to suppress wages creates an obligation on the rest of the world to absorb the excess.

Klein and Pettis walk through the algebra carefully. A country's current account balance can be decomposed into the saving-investment balances of three sectors: households, businesses, and the government. Changes in any one sector are often offset by changes in the others, which is why policy interventions that target only one sector (such as cutting the government deficit) often fail to change the overall current account balance. Japan is the proof: its government runs deficits of 6 percent of GDP and its household saving rate is near zero, yet it consistently runs current account surpluses because Japanese corporations save even more.

The concept of the "Scroogeville" allegory is developed fully here. Imagine a small economy where one person owns everything and saves 100 percent of income above subsistence. The workers earn just enough to survive and save nothing. Total production exceeds total consumption. The surplus must either be invested domestically (but there are no profitable opportunities because the workers can't buy anything) or exported. If exported, some other economy must absorb the excess by borrowing. This is the mechanism by which class war within one country becomes trade war between countries.

"A country's saving rate is determined primarily by its income distribution, not by cultural values or individual choices." — Klein & Pettis

The chapter concludes by addressing a common objection: don't deficit countries benefit from cheap capital? Sometimes, if the capital finances productive investment. But when the capital flows are driven by savings gluts rather than genuine opportunities, the receiving country's financial system is forced to create assets to absorb the inflows — lowering lending standards, inflating bubbles, and ultimately setting the stage for a crisis. The cheap capital is not a gift. It is a burden that the deficit country had no say in accepting.

Chapter 4: From Tiananmen to the Belt and Road — Understanding China's Surplus

China's transformation from a poor, agrarian economy into the world's largest manufacturer is one of the great achievements of the modern era. But Klein and Pettis argue that the same policies that powered China's rise also created the massive savings imbalances that now distort the global economy. The chapter traces how Deng Xiaoping's reforms, the trauma of Tiananmen Square, and the priorities of the Chinese Communist Party combined to produce an economy where workers retain an abnormally small share of national income.

After Tiananmen, the party leadership made a calculated decision: economic growth would be the source of legitimacy, but political liberalization was off the table. Growth would be driven by investment and exports, not by household consumption. The tools were financial repression — the government set interest rates on bank deposits well below the rate of inflation, effectively taxing savers to subsidize borrowers (state-owned enterprises and real estate developers). Chinese households lost roughly 4 percent of GDP per year through this hidden tax, equivalent to a transfer of about $500 billion annually from savers to the state-favored corporate sector.

The hukou system — a legacy of the Mao era that ties social benefits to a person's registered place of residence — amplified the suppression of household income. Roughly 290 million migrant workers live and work in cities where they cannot access public schools, hospitals, or pensions because their hukou is registered in a rural village. These workers respond rationally by saving as much as possible to self-insure against risks that the state covers for urban residents. The result is an artificially elevated household saving rate that depresses consumption and inflates the national surplus.

Labor organizing is another casualty. Independent trade unions are illegal in China. The only permitted union, the All-China Federation of Trade Unions, is controlled by the party and has historically sided with employers. Workers who attempt to organize independently face harassment, detention, and imprisonment. Without collective bargaining power, wages have consistently grown more slowly than productivity, transferring an ever-larger share of output to corporate profits and government revenue.

The chapter documents the extraordinary scale of China's investment boom. Gross fixed capital formation reached 46 percent of GDP by 2011 — a level without historical precedent for a major economy. The Soviet Union peaked at about 33 percent; Japan peaked at about 36 percent. Much of this investment has been wasteful: ghost cities, redundant infrastructure, and industrial overcapacity in steel, aluminum, cement, glass, and solar panels. The problem is not that China invested too much but that the investment was financed by suppressing household consumption rather than by genuine corporate profits or voluntary savings.

The Belt and Road Initiative represents China's attempt to export its surplus savings and excess industrial capacity to the developing world. By financing infrastructure projects in Central Asia, Southeast Asia, Africa, and Latin America, China can keep its factories running even as domestic demand stagnates. But Klein and Pettis note the uncomfortable parallel with earlier episodes of surplus recycling: the British lending boom to Latin America in the 1820s, the German bank lending to Southern Europe in the 2000s, and the American recycling of petrodollars in the 1970s all ended badly for both lenders and borrowers.

Xi Jinping's administration has paid lip service to rebalancing the economy toward consumption, but the structural reforms needed — hukou abolition, financial liberalization, labor rights, SOE dividend distributions — threaten powerful vested interests. As long as China can dump its excess savings in the United States and other deficit countries, the pressure to reform remains manageable. The class war within China — between the party-state-corporate elite and ordinary workers — is being exported as a trade war with the rest of the world.

"Chinese government policies — persecuting labor organizers, offering cheap bank loans to real estate developers — have caused American manufacturing workers to lose their jobs." — Klein & Pettis

The numbers are stark. Chinese household consumption fell from about 46 percent of GDP in 2000 to just 35 percent by 2010, the lowest level for any major economy in modern history. It has recovered slightly since then, but remains far below the global average of about 60 percent. The gap — roughly 15-20 percentage points of Chinese GDP — represents the income that has been transferred from workers to the state and to the corporate elite. That gap is the source of China's trade surplus, and it is the reason American factories have closed.

Chapter 5: The Fall of the Wall and the Schwarze Null — Understanding Germany's Surplus

Germany's path to becoming the world's second-largest surplus economy was entirely different from China's, yet the destination is remarkably similar. Where China used financial repression and political authoritarianism, Germany used reunification shock, labor market deregulation, and constitutional fiscal austerity. The result in both cases was a massive transfer of income from workers to capital owners, depressed domestic consumption, and a structural current account surplus that the rest of the world was forced to absorb.

The story begins with the fall of the Berlin Wall in 1989 and the chaotic reunification that followed. Helmut Kohl insisted on a 1:1 exchange rate between the East German mark and the Western deutschmark, overruling the Bundesbank's recommendation of 2:1. His motivation was political: to stem the flood of East Germans migrating westward. "If the deutschmark doesn't come to us, we go to it" had become a popular slogan in the East. But the generous exchange rate, combined with aggressive wage hikes pushed by Western trade unions to prevent factory relocations, made East German industry uncompetitive overnight. Only 8 percent of East German firms were judged "viable" after reunification. Manufacturing output collapsed by two-thirds.

The Treuhandanstalt — the agency charged with privatizing East German state-owned enterprises — became a symbol of the destruction. Initial optimism about East German assets vanished as it became clear that most were worth only "their real-estate or scrap value." Instead of generating revenue, the privatization program cost the government hundreds of billions of deutschmarks. By 1992, almost half of unified Germany's unemployed were in Eastern states despite those states containing less than 19 percent of the total population. Six percent of the East German population had migrated to the West by 1994.

Germany's economy stagnated through the 1990s, earning the label "the sick man of Europe." The Bundesbank raised interest rates to fight reunification-era inflation, reaching nearly 9 percent in 1992 — the highest since the postwar central bank's creation in 1948. The government slashed infrastructure spending by nearly 20 percent between 1992 and 1998 to comply with the Maastricht Treaty. Construction activity fell relentlessly for more than a decade. Germany grew at just 1.3 percent per year from 1991 to 1997 — the worst sustained slowdown since the 1950s.

The response came in two waves. First, Gerhard Schröder's Red-Green coalition cut the top income tax rate from 53 to 42 percent, slashed the corporate profit tax to about 39 percent, and — most significantly — eliminated the capital gains tax on corporate cross-holdings. Then came Agenda 2010 and the Hartz Reforms, named after Peter Hartz, Volkswagen's HR chief. Hartz IV cut unemployment insurance to twelve months for workers under fifty-five (from thirty-two months previously) and threatened to strip benefits from anyone who rejected a job offer for low pay. The effect was to terrify German workers into accepting wage cuts. Real hourly pay and benefits fell roughly 5 percent between 2001 and 2007 after inflation. The average German household's income was slightly lower in 2013 than it had been in 1999.

The political fallout was dramatic. Oskar Lafontaine, Schröder's own former finance minister, quit the SPD to lead a new left-wing party that eventually became Die Linke (The Left), which vowed to repeal Hartz IV — legislation it called a law that "legally decreed poverty." In a darkly comic twist, Peter Hartz himself was forced to leave Volkswagen after sex and bribery scandals. The SPD lost North Rhine-Westphalia, which it had governed continuously since 1966, and Schröder engineered early elections. The result was a grand coalition under Angela Merkel that preserved Agenda 2010 and Hartz IV intact.

The consequences for income distribution were severe. The capital share of net value added in German nonfinancial businesses rose from about 25 percent in the mid-1990s to 36 percent by 2007. Union membership plunged: the share of workers covered by collective bargaining fell from over 80 percent to less than 45 percent. Income concentration at the top decile surpassed the levels of Germany's industrialization period of 1871-1913. The median German household is far poorer than the median Spanish household and only about as wealthy as the median Greek or Polish household — even though the average German is about 50 percent richer than the average Italian. The explanation: wealth in Germany is extraordinarily concentrated, with 90 percent of all businesses (and more than half of corporate cash flow) controlled by family dynasties that pass them from generation to generation largely exempt from inheritance tax.

The domestic weakness was exported through Germany's banks. Between 2002 and 2008, German residents spent €702 billion less than they earned. Of the €2.7 trillion in cumulative financial outflows, more than €1.6 trillion were attributable to banks buying foreign bonds and making foreign loans. The state-owned Landesbanken played a disproportionate role: after losing their government guarantees in 2005, they were "woefully unprepared" for open competition and poured money into whatever foreign borrowers would take it. The lending was concentrated in Ireland, Italy, and Spain — countries that would become the epicenters of the euro crisis.

"Despite following a radically different path than China, Germany ended up in a surprisingly similar place. Class wars were won by the rich at the expense of everyone else." — Klein & Pettis

After the crisis, Germany doubled down. The Schuldenbremse (debt brake), enshrined in the constitution in 2009, prohibited the federal, state, and municipal governments from running meaningful budget deficits under almost any circumstances. Since 1998, government capital spending has not been enough to maintain existing infrastructure — net public investment has been consistently negative. About half of Germany's roads and bridges need repair. The Leverkusen Bridge over the Rhine had to be closed to trucks in 2012 and will need total rebuilding. Fewer than 3 percent of Germans have broadband speeds of 100 megabits per second. Finance Minister Wolfgang Schäuble's obsession with the schwarze Null (black zero — a balanced budget) was so complete that his finance ministry staffers dressed in black and posed in a circle to bid him farewell when he retired. Through the Fiscal Compact of 2012, Germany imposed this model on all of Europe, effectively turning the entire euro area into a massive surplus bloc worth roughly 4 percent of GDP.

The euro area crisis countries — Spain, Greece, Italy, Portugal, Ireland, Cyprus, Slovenia — were forced to shift from a combined current account deficit of 6.5 percent of GDP to a surplus of 2 percent between 2008 and 2016. Real domestic spending plunged by 11 percent in Spain, 9 percent in Italy and Portugal, 14 percent in Cyprus, and 13 percent in Slovenia. Greece's domestic consumption and investment shrank by a third. As of 2019, Italy's real consumption and investment remained 8 percent below 2008 levels. Greece remained in depression. Der Spiegel characterized Germany's approach as "pedagogical imperialism."

Chapter 6: The American Exception — The Exorbitant Burden and the Persistent Deficit

The United States shares many of the same domestic pathologies as Germany and China: rising inequality, a corporate investment bust, stagnant wages, and jobs relocated abroad. Germany had the Hartz Reforms; America had Clinton-era welfare cuts and Bush tax cuts. Germany has the Schuldenbremse; America has the Tea Party, the debt ceiling, and sequestration. Yet Germany became the world's largest surplus country while the United States remains the world's largest deficit country. The explanation lies not in American profligacy but in the unique features of the American financial system and the dollar's role as the world's reserve asset.

Klein and Pettis make a striking argument: based on its domestic fundamentals alone, the United States should have been running surpluses. The share of national income going to the top 10 percent rose from 30 to 40 percent. The richest 1 percent's share of total wealth soared from 22 to 42 percent, with almost all of the increase concentrated in the top 0.1 percent. About 70 percent of all earnings from asset ownership now flow to the richest 1 percent, up from 35 percent in the late 1970s. Since the highest earners save roughly 40 percent of their income, this concentration should have pushed the aggregate saving rate up, producing surpluses just as it did in Germany and China.

Instead, the household saving rate fell from about 10 percent of disposable income in the late 1970s to barely 3 percent on the eve of the financial crisis, because the vast majority of Americans ended up saving even less — or outright dissaving — to compensate. The bottom half of the income distribution experienced essentially no income growth after taxes, inflation, and government benefits since the late 1970s. The paradox is explained by the financial system's response to the flood of foreign capital: it lowered borrowing costs, inflated asset prices, loosened lending standards, and created the conditions for ordinary Americans to spend beyond their incomes.

The chapter demolishes the bipartisan consensus that the US current account deficit is caused by fiscal profligacy. Robert Skidelsky, George Shultz, Martin Feldstein, Jason Furman, and even Joseph Stiglitz have all blamed government deficits for the trade gap. Klein and Pettis show, with decades of data, that this is simply wrong. The combined government budget balance almost perfectly mirrors the private sector's behavior: when the government tightens, the private sector loosens, and vice versa. The current account deficit has remained remarkably stable at 2-3 percent of GDP through radically different fiscal regimes.

The real driver is foreign demand for US assets. Since the late 1990s, foreign governments and their reserve managers have poured trillions of dollars into American financial assets — Treasury bonds, agency debt, bank deposits — not because they sought high returns but because they wanted safety. These "uneconomic" capital flows were as large as America's entire current account deficit between 2001 and 2014. The money was not invited; it was pushed in by the structural savings gluts of the surplus countries.

The chapter traces the dollar's journey from the gold standard through Bretton Woods to the present system. At Bretton Woods in 1944, John Maynard Keynes proposed an "international clearing bank" using a new currency called the bancor that would penalize surplus and deficit countries equally. The United States vetoed the proposal, insisting that the dollar be the anchor of the system with no mechanism to force surplus countries to adjust. Valéry Giscard d'Estaing, France's finance minister, famously labeled this arrangement America's "exorbitant privilege." Klein and Pettis argue it was actually an exorbitant burden.

The Triffin Dilemma, identified by Belgian-American economist Robert Triffin in the late 1950s, predicted exactly what happened: the US government was issuing more dollar obligations than its gold reserves could support, making the system inherently unstable. The United States lost nearly 10,000 metric tons of gold — just over half its total holdings — between 1957 and 1968. When Richard Nixon closed the gold window in August 1971, the Bretton Woods system was finished. But the dollar's role as the world's reserve asset survived and even intensified, especially after the Asian Financial Crisis of 1997-98 traumatized a generation of policymakers into hoarding dollar reserves.

The safe asset shortage is the mechanism that links foreign reserve accumulation to the American housing bubble. Foreign central banks spent roughly $4.1 trillion buying dollar-denominated assets between 1998 and 2008, absorbing all new Treasury bonds and much of the debt issued by Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac. But it was not enough. The world's savers wanted to buy trillions of dollars of low-risk bonds that did not exist. Wall Street responded: "When the ducks are quacking, feed them." Between 2001 and 2007, financiers produced about $2.5 trillion of private-label mortgage-backed securities based on loans that did not conform to normal underwriting standards. By the peak in 2006, the typical private-label mortgage had a down payment worth just 3 percent of the home's appraised value.

"Rather than an exorbitant privilege, as the French wrongly described it, the dollar's primacy has been an exorbitant burden." — Klein & Pettis

The irony is that despite the explosion of mortgage debt, America did not actually experience an economic boom. Real per-person household consumption grew more slowly from 2000 to 2006 than from 1947 to 2000. Private employment fell 3 percent between 2000 and 2003 and never grew fast enough to keep pace with the expanding population. Manufacturing production, excluding distortions from semiconductor productivity estimates, fell 6 percent and barely recovered. More than 80 percent of the decline in private-sector employment between 2000 and 2003 was directly attributable to the manufacturing sector, concentrated in places most exposed to competition from countries where workers were paid far less. A Fed study published in 2017 found that "the displacement of domestic production by imports fueled demand for credit to substitute for lost wages." The debt bubble and the deindustrialization were two sides of the same coin.

Since 2008, the burden has only shifted in form, not in magnitude. Reserve managers were replaced by European savers after 2014, as the ECB's asset purchase program hoovered up European government bonds (just 15 percent of German government bonds were held by private investors by 2018) and pushed Europeans to buy about €1.5 trillion in foreign bonds, including roughly $800 billion in US bonds. Personal consumption per person is more than 12 percent below the pre-1998 trend. Government investment spending is less than half what it was in the mid-2000s. The manufacturing trade deficit (excluding refined petroleum) exceeds 4 percent of GDP — the highest since the nineteenth century. The current account deficit persists at 2-3 percent of GDP not because Americans spend too much but because the rest of the world continues to save too much and dump its excess in America.

Conclusion: To End the Trade Wars, End the Class Wars

The book's conclusion restates the thesis with the force of a closing argument. Trade war is not a conflict between countries. It is a conflict between bankers and owners of financial assets on one side and ordinary households on the other — between the very rich and everyone else. Rising inequality has produced gluts of manufactured goods, job loss, and rising indebtedness. For decades, the United States has been the largest single victim, absorbing the rest of the world's excess output and savings at the cost of deindustrialization and financial crises. This is America's exorbitant burden.

Klein and Pettis draw a startling analogy: the United States, the United Kingdom, Canada, and Australia — all of which absorb surplus production in exchange for taking on debt — "therefore resemble the imperial colonies of Europe of the late nineteenth century." Back then, subject peoples were forced to buy Europe's excess production in exchange for taking on unneeded debt. Today, the mechanism is economic rather than military, but the structure is the same. The modern regime depends on the English-speaking countries' political commitment to open markets. In democracies, people have the option to change their mind.

The 2016 US presidential election showed the cracks. All major candidates disavowed the Trans-Pacific Partnership. Bernie Sanders warned it would "throw American workers out on the street." Donald Trump pulled out of the TPP and imposed tariffs on everything from Korean washing machines to nearly all of America's imports from China, doubling customs revenue between 2017 and 2019. But tariffs, the authors argue, are ineffective at best and harmful at worst. They treat the symptom — the bilateral deficit — while leaving the disease — the internal class wars that produce the savings gluts — untouched.

The book's prescriptions flow directly from the diagnosis. For the United States: shift the burden of absorbing foreign inflows from the private sector to the federal government by issuing Treasury debt as needed, investing in infrastructure, lowering payroll taxes, and expanding the social safety net. For China: reform the hukou system, expand the social safety net, allow workers to organize, force state-owned enterprises to pay dividends into a social wealth fund, and prop up the yuan to shift purchasing power from exporters to consumers. For Europe: federalize fiscal policy, reform inheritance and property taxes, lower VAT, raise minimum wages, and turn the European Investment Bank into the continent's main infrastructure lender.

The alternatives, Klein and Pettis warn, are far worse. If surplus countries do not reform, the United States will eventually refuse to continue absorbing global imbalances. Without a new set of rules, global trade will become unstable and increasingly contentious — resembling the anarchy and violence that characterized trade from the 1600s through the first half of the twentieth century. The authors call for a modern equivalent of Keynes's bancor: a symmetrical mechanism that forces both surplus and deficit countries to adjust.

The deeper problem, Klein and Pettis argue, is that globalization and rising inequality have reinforced each other in a vicious cycle. Businesses across the world use international competition as an excuse to push for lower wages, weaker environmental and safety regulations, preferential tax regimes, and regressive transfers. Squeezing ordinary households has apparently been much easier than increasing productivity, investing in infrastructure, and improving health and education. But the strategy is self-defeating: depressing wages leads to lower consumption and higher indebtedness, both of which are ultimately self-limiting. It is not a coincidence that throughout modern history, high levels of income inequality have coincided with soaring levels of debt.

Demand for goods and services has become the world's scarcest and most valuable resource, with the United States playing the role of swing producer. "Competitiveness" has become a euphemism for pushing wages down, either directly or through currency depreciation and weaker social safety nets. The fetish of competitiveness has generated a global spending shortage. Trade wars are an almost inevitable consequence of globalization as it has been practiced — not because trade is bad, but because the gains have been hoarded by elites while the costs have been socialized across ordinary workers in every country.

An American withdrawal from global trade — the gradual closure of the world's largest consumer market and largest capital market — would impose significant costs on the rest of the world and eventually on the United States itself. Without agreeing on a new set of rules, global trade will become unstable and increasingly contentious as countries try to shift the burden of adjustment elsewhere. Instead of the historically anomalous period of peaceful globalization in the second half of the twentieth century, the world would resemble the anarchy — and potentially the violence — that characterized trade from the 1600s through the first half of the twentieth century.

"Peoples who fundamentally share common interests are being set against each other because the ultrarich have been successfully waging a class war against everyone else." — Klein & Pettis

The book ends with a call for hope grounded in historical precedent. At Bretton Woods, the Allies created a new system of rules for the global economy. At home, governments strengthened social democracy by guaranteeing basic living standards. Their solutions were imperfect — that is why we are where we are — but they were based on the values of egalitarianism, global cooperation, and peace. "The peoples of the world deserve a comparable response to the challenges of today." If redistribution is done right, the authors argue, it would lead not to lower output but to higher output: transferring income to ordinary households means more consumption and more investment, because the rich cannot possibly spend all they earn. The trade wars would end because the class wars would end.

Trading & Investing Lessons

Surplus Countries as Value Traps

Germany's persistent trade surplus and depressed domestic spending mirror the structure of a value trap: an asset that looks cheap on fundamentals but cannot unlock its value because of structural impediments. German equities have chronically underperformed despite strong exports because domestic demand is systematically suppressed. When analyzing economies or sectors, look for similar patterns: high savings rates driven by inequality rather than genuine thrift signal suppressed demand and poor internal returns.

Reserve Accumulation as a Leading Indicator

The pace of foreign reserve accumulation by surplus-country central banks is a powerful leading indicator for US interest rates and asset bubbles. When reserve managers are aggressively buying US assets, borrowing costs fall and credit conditions loosen — regardless of what the Fed is doing. Monitoring SAFE, the Bank of Japan, and the sovereign wealth funds of the Gulf states gives an edge in anticipating liquidity conditions.

Currency Pegs Create Fragility

The book's analysis of the Asian Financial Crisis and the euro crisis reinforces a timeless trading lesson: fixed exchange rate regimes that suppress internal adjustment create hidden fragility. The longer the peg holds, the larger the imbalances that build up beneath it. When the peg finally breaks — as it did for the UK in 1992, Thailand in 1997, and arguably the euro periphery in 2010 — the reversal is violent and offers asymmetric trading opportunities.

The Fiscal Policy Red Herring

One of the book's most actionable insights for macro trading is that fiscal policy does not drive the US current account balance. Government tightening is offset by private sector loosening and vice versa. Traders who bet on dollar strength or weakness based on fiscal deficit projections are missing the real driver: foreign capital flows. Watch the financial account, not the budget.

Deindustrialization as Regional Risk

The link between import competition and local credit expansion — "the displacement of domestic production by imports fueled demand for credit to substitute for lost wages" — has direct implications for regional real estate and municipal credit analysis. Places most exposed to foreign competition are also most exposed to credit bubbles and subsequent defaults.

The Exorbitant Burden Trade

If the US ever moves to restrict capital inflows — through a market access charge, capital controls, or a reformulated international monetary system — the dollar would weaken substantially, US manufacturing would revive, and long-duration Treasuries would sell off as the safe-asset premium unwinds. This is a low-probability, high-impact scenario worth monitoring through legislative proposals and Treasury rhetoric.

Key Takeaways

1. Trade wars are class wars in disguise

Rising inequality within surplus countries transfers income from spenders (workers) to savers (elites), creating structural surpluses that must be exported, forcing deficit countries to absorb excess production and capital through debt bubbles and deindustrialization.

2. Income distribution determines trade balances

A country's saving rate — and therefore its current account balance — is primarily a function of how income is distributed, not cultural values or individual choices. Shift income to the rich and the saving rate rises mechanically.

3. The "exorbitant privilege" is actually an exorbitant burden

The dollar's role as the world's reserve asset forces the United States to absorb the excess savings of the rest of the world, displacing American production, inflating debt bubbles, and subsidizing foreign exporters at the expense of American workers.

4. Fiscal policy does not drive the current account

Decades of US data show that changes in the government's budget balance are almost perfectly offset by opposite changes in the private sector. The current account is determined by foreign capital flows, not domestic fiscal decisions.

5. China and Germany took different paths to the same destination

China used financial repression, the hukou system, and labor suppression. Germany used reunification shock, the Hartz Reforms, and constitutional fiscal austerity. Both transferred income from workers to elites, suppressed domestic consumption, and generated massive surpluses that distorted the global economy.

6. Safe asset scarcity caused the housing bubble

Foreign central banks absorbed all new Treasury bonds and most GSE debt between 1998 and 2008. The remaining demand for safe US assets was met by Wall Street creating $2.5 trillion in private-label mortgage-backed securities based on loans that should never have been made.

7. Tariffs cannot fix the problem

As long as the internal class wars in surplus countries suppress domestic consumption, those countries must run surpluses. Tariffs shift the composition of trade but not the aggregate balance, and risk retaliation that damages both sides.

8. The solution is ending inequality, not restricting trade

Redistribution from elites to ordinary households in the surplus countries would increase both consumption and investment, eliminate the savings gluts, and make trade wars unnecessary.

Contrarian Ideas

The US trade deficit is not caused by American overspending

Consensus: Americans consume too much and save too little. Reality: American domestic demand has been exceptionally weak since the financial crisis, with personal consumption per person more than 12 percent below the pre-1998 trend. The deficit persists because foreign savers force capital into the US, and the financial system adjusts to absorb it.

Germany's export success is a symptom of failure, not strength

Consensus: Germany's trade surplus proves its economy is competitive and well-managed. Reality: Germany's surplus is the consequence of domestic weakness — stagnant wages, collapsing investment, decaying infrastructure. Germany's share of world exports did not increase during the period when its surplus exploded; the surplus came from imports falling even faster than export growth.

Government deficits do not cause trade deficits

Consensus: the "twin deficits" hypothesis holds that budget deficits cause current account deficits. Reality: throughout US history, changes in the fiscal balance have been almost perfectly offset by opposite changes in private-sector behavior. The current account deficit expanded to 4 percent of GDP in the late 1990s precisely when the government budget was in surplus for the first time since the 1950s.

Being the world's reserve currency is a curse, not a blessing

Consensus: the dollar's reserve status gives America an "exorbitant privilege." Reality: it imposes an exorbitant burden by forcing the US to absorb excess savings from the rest of the world, displacing manufacturing, inflating bubbles, and leaving American workers worse off.

Allegories & Cultural References

The Scroogeville Allegory

An economy where one person saves everything and workers earn bare subsistence. Production exceeds consumption; the surplus must be exported. The foreign country that absorbs it must borrow. Class war within one country becomes trade war between countries.

The Poyais Fraud

Gregor MacGregor's 1820s scam selling bonds for a fictional Central American country illustrates how surplus capital will always find assets to buy — even fictitious ones. The pattern recurs: Baring Crisis, petrodollar recycling, subprime MBS.

The Don Quijote Airport

Spain's Ciudad Real airport, built for 10 million passengers per year despite being more than two hours from Madrid, symbolizes the wasteful investment that inevitably accompanies capital inflows from surplus countries. Ireland's abandoned golf courses tell the same story.

The Imperial Colony Analogy

Klein and Pettis compare modern deficit countries to nineteenth-century colonies forced to buy Europe's excess production in exchange for unneeded debt. Today's mechanism is economic, not military, but the structure is identical.

Proposals & Frameworks

The Savings-Glut Framework

Trade imbalances are not caused by differences in competitiveness, fiscal policy, or cultural values. They are caused by the distribution of income within countries. When income shifts to entities that save more (the rich and the companies they control), national saving rises, consumption falls, and the surplus must be exported. The current account balance is the dependent variable; income distribution is the independent variable.

The Exorbitant Burden Framework

The reserve currency issuer does not benefit from its status. It is forced to absorb the excess savings of the rest of the world through some combination of deindustrialization, debt bubbles, and lower domestic production. The financial system responds to the inflows by creating assets (lowering standards, inflating prices) until the savings are absorbed. The process continues until the reserve country can no longer bear it.

The Symmetrical Adjustment Proposal

Revive Keynes's principle: surplus and deficit countries should bear equal responsibility for adjustment. Mechanisms could include capital controls, market access charges on foreign investment, expanded Fed swap lines to reduce the need for reserve accumulation, and international agreements penalizing persistent surpluses.

What Didn't Fit

The Neuer Markt Collapse

Germany's answer to the Nasdaq was the Neuer Markt, launched by the Frankfurt-based Deutsche Börse in 1997. During the bubble, shares listed on the Neuer Markt outperformed the Nasdaq-100 by a factor of four. At its peak in March 2000, the companies were worth nearly €234 billion. By 2002, they had lost 95 percent of their value amid scandals involving insider trading, stock-price manipulation, and falsified earnings. Deutsche Börse shut down the exchange that September. The episode illustrates Germany's brief moment of financial optimism before the long austerity that followed. It also shows that the German corporate debt binge of the late 1990s — current account deficit ballooning from 2 to 7 percent of GDP — was no more sustainable than the American version.

Indonesia's Rupiah Catastrophe

The Asian Financial Crisis hit Indonesia harder than any other country. The rupiah lost 80 percent of its value between January 1997 and July 1998. Real output per person fell more than 14 percent. The chaos toppled the Suharto regime, which had ruled since 1967, and led to the independence of East Timor, occupied since 1975. Average living standards did not return to their pre-crisis peak until the mid-2000s. The IMF's conditions were often counterproductive — including the notorious demand that Indonesia end its clove monopoly in cigarette manufacturing, a demand that had nothing to do with the financial crisis. The humiliation drove a generation of Asian policymakers to accumulate the massive dollar reserves that would later distort the American economy.

The Peter Bofinger Defense

The German economist Peter Bofinger mounted a cogent defense of Kohl's 1:1 exchange rate for reunification, arguing that after accounting for the social security contributions East Germans would not have to pay, the labor cost gap at 1:1 was close to the productivity gap. Any other exchange rate, Bofinger argued, "would have strongly increased the movement of workers from East to West Germany" because Eastern living standards would have collapsed by more than half. The West German unions' strategy of pushing Eastern wages to parity destroyed this logic by making Eastern labor costs 50 percent higher than Western levels within two years.

Houses as ATMs

A 2005 study by Alan Greenspan and Fed economist James Kennedy tracked how much purchasing power American households were "extracting" from their homes. In the 1990s, Americans boosted disposable income by 2-3 percent per year through home equity, mostly from capital gains on sales. Between 2004 and mid-2006, home equity withdrawals boosted disposable income by roughly 10 percent — about $1 trillion per year. Over the entire bubble (2002-2007), Americans extracted $4.7 trillion in wealth from their homes. Housing wealth rose less than $2 trillion while home values increased roughly $7 trillion; the difference was debt. From many Americans' perspective, rising home prices were doing the saving for them, freeing up cash from meager wages to buy goods and services.

Book Details

Trade Wars Are Class Wars: How Rising Inequality Distorts the Global Economy and Threatens International Peace
Matthew C. Klein & Michael Pettis
Yale University Press, 2020
285 pages
A rigorous reframing of international trade conflicts as the global expression of domestic class wars, showing how rising inequality in China, Germany, and the United States has produced the savings gluts, debt bubbles, and political anger that define our era.

About the Authors

Matthew C. Klein is a financial journalist and economics writer who has worked at the Financial Times, Bloomberg, and Barron's. He co-authors the The Overshoot newsletter and has written extensively about international capital flows, trade imbalances, and the structural forces that shape the global economy. His journalistic approach brings accessibility to what could otherwise be impenetrable macroeconomic theory.

Michael Pettis is a professor of finance at Peking University's Guanghua School of Management and a senior fellow at the Carnegie Endowment for International Peace. He has spent more than two decades living in China and analyzing its economy from the inside, making him one of the most respected foreign voices on Chinese macroeconomics. Before academia, Pettis spent years on Wall Street, including stints at Bear Stearns and as a managing director at Credit Suisse. His previous books include The Great Rebalancing and Avoiding the Fall. He also owns a Beijing nightclub and music label, making him perhaps the only economist equally at home in a derivatives trading room, a Chinese university lecture hall, and a punk rock venue.

Why This Book Matters

Trade Wars Are Class Wars was published in 2020, but it reads like a prophecy written for 2025 and beyond. Every major economic development since its publication — the intensification of US-China tensions, the Trump tariffs, Europe's energy crisis and continued fiscal orthodoxy, the reshoring movement, the debate over industrial policy — confirms the book's central framework. The class wars Klein and Pettis identified have only deepened, and the trade wars they predicted would follow have only escalated.

The book matters because it provides the only framework that coherently explains why the world's largest deficit country and the world's largest surplus countries are all experiencing the same domestic pathology — rising inequality, stagnant wages, decaying infrastructure — despite being on opposite sides of the trade balance. The answer is not that one side is cheating and the other is losing. The answer is that elites in all these countries are winning the same class war, and ordinary workers everywhere are paying the price. Until that war ends, the trade wars cannot end either.

For investors, policymakers, and citizens trying to understand why the global economy feels broken, this is the most important book of the decade.
