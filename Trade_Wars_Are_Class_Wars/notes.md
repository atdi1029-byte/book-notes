## INTRODUCTION

### Core Thesis
Rising inequality WITHIN countries heightens trade conflicts BETWEEN them.
This is an optimistic argument: not a zero-sum conflict between nations
or economic blocs. Chinese and Germans are not evil. The problems of
the past few decades stem from massive transfers of income to the rich
and the companies they control.

### The Mechanism
- A global conflict between economic classes WITHIN countries is being
  misinterpreted as conflicts BETWEEN countries with competing interests
- The danger: repetition of the 1930s breakdown of international
  economic/financial order that undermined democracy and encouraged
  virulent nationalism → war, revolution, genocide

### China Case
- Chinese gov't policies: persecute labor organizers, offer cheap bank
  loans to real estate developers → American manufacturing workers lose jobs
- Systematic transfers from Chinese workers to Chinese elites distort
  the Chinese economy by strangling purchasing power and subsidizing
  production at expense of consumption
- This distorts the global economy: gluts of manufactured goods, bidding
  up prices of stocks/bonds/real estate
- Chinese underconsumption destroys jobs elsewhere; inflated asset values
  lead to cycles of booms, busts, debt crises
- Chinese workers/retirees are also harmed: underpaid relative to value
  of production, over-taxed, can't afford goods/services, polluted air/water
- Between 2002–2010: voters in US congressional districts competing with
  Chinese imports elected increasingly extreme representatives (both left/right)
- Trump won 89 of 100 counties most affected by Chinese import competition
  in 2016 Republican primaries
- Some estimates: Trump would have lost general election without trade-induced
  radicalization of voters in Michigan, Pennsylvania, Wisconsin

### Europe Case
- German companies slash wages → German gov't cuts welfare → Spaniards
  get housing bubble
- Europe, not China, has become the biggest threat to world economy
- Since 2010, household spending in euro area grew at barely half the rate
  of overall production
- Before 2012, Europe as a whole was not unbalanced relative to rest of
  world — imbalances within certain countries (Germany) were absorbed by
  crisis countries (Spain, Greece, Italy, Ireland, Portugal, Baltics)
- Spain had second-biggest trade deficit in world (behind US); Greece
  (11M people) had fifth biggest
- Germany's pathologies: rising inequality, depressed consumption,
  systematic underinvestment — preview of what came for rest of continent
- Nationalists stoke ethnic prejudice to sidestep fundamental economic issues
- German politicians demanded Greece repay debts (many purchased by
  German banks during boom) by selling islands
- Greek gov't revived demands for Nazi reparations
- Dijsselbloem (Dutch FM, Eurogroup president) blamed crisis on people
  who "spend all the money on drinks and women"

### Historical Parallel: Hobson and Imperialism
- John A. Hobson (1902, "Imperialism: A Study"): surplus capital that
  cannot find sound investments within the country → central explanation
  for American and European imperialism
- Income concentration gave the rich "an excess of consuming power which
  they cannot use" at expense of everyone else
- "Consumption alone vitalizes capital and makes it capable of yielding profits"
- Rich savers had to search abroad for "new areas for profitable investment
  and speculation" → encouraged political expansion
- Hobson's solution: change income distribution. "The home markets are
  capable of indefinite expansion" if income is "properly distributed"
- "Whatever is produced in England can be consumed in England"
- Hobson's argument went unheeded. 12 years later → WWI
- In 1920s: rich Americans were source of the glut, Europeans forced to absorb it
- Kenneth Austin (US Treasury): Hobson's insight applies to modern China,
  Japan, Germany — US acting as sink for foreign gluts
- Late 19th century, 1920s, and today: same harm from extremely unequal
  income distributions spread through global trade/financial systems

## CHAPTER 1: From Adam Smith to Tim Cook — The Transformation of Global Trade

### Early Trade History
- International trade used to be simple: high transport costs + political
  constraints limited flow of finished goods and raw materials
- Late 18th/early 19th century: British argued for removing tariffs
  (specialization); Americans and Germans proposed protecting infant industries
- End of Napoleonic Wars + steam engines + telegraph → trade boom until
  early 1870s → ended with Panic of 1873 (world's first synchronized
  global financial crisis)
- Late 1880s–WWI: high imperialism, increased trade within larger blocs
  even as it limited trade between them
- World wars + Great Depression + revolutions crushed international trade
  to lowest level since late 18th century — but also caused massive
  redistribution of wealth → income more equally distributed within rich
  countries than ever before
- Container shipping by late 1990s transformed trade: companies spread
  complex manufacturing supply chains across multiple countries
- Popular understanding of trade still based on obsolete 18th-century models

### Pins, Cloth, and Wine — The Classical Trade Theorists
- Adam Smith's pin factory: "eighteen distinct operations" to make one pin.
  Workers become thousands of times more productive by specializing.
  Smith (1776): "It is the maxim of every prudent master of a family never
  to attempt to make at home what it will cost him more to make than to buy"
- David Ricardo: born half century after Smith. Jewish financier who
  became so wealthy betting correctly on Battle of Waterloo that he bought
  a seat in Parliament. Published "On the Principles of Political Economy
  and Taxation" (1817)
- Ricardo's comparative advantage: Portuguese workers could make BOTH
  cloth and wine more efficiently than English. But Portuguese earned
  relatively higher profits on wine, English on cloth. Specialization
  benefits both — even when one country is more productive in everything
- CRITICAL CAVEAT: Ricardo assumed capital would NOT flow across borders.
  "Most men of property" would be "satisfied with a low rate of profits
  in their own country, rather than seek a more advantageous employment
  for their wealth in foreign nations." Carrier pigeons and sailing ships
  couldn't coordinate cross-border production. Wars were common.
- What people forget: Ricardo realized that WITHOUT these primitive
  conditions, it would be "advantageous to the capitalists of England
  [that] the wine and the cloth should both be made in Portugal" — i.e.,
  capital would flow to the most productive location and England would
  lose both industries
- Ricardo's case for free trade DEPENDED on investors' unwillingness to
  move money abroad. As technology improved and costs collapsed, those
  assumptions broke down completely

### Cultural Reference: Port Wine
- "One happy consequence of those difficulties was the invention of Port
  wine by British entrepreneurs to solve the twin problems of never-ending
  conflicts with France and the spoilage of conventional Portuguese wine
  during the long journey to England"

### Hamilton, List, and the "American System"
- George Washington and Alexander Hamilton: manufacturing capacity was
  a NATIONAL SECURITY imperative. America isolated, vulnerable to naval
  embargoes, far from allies
- Washington (Jan 8, 1790): "A free people ought not only to be armed,
  but disciplined... Their safety and interest require that they should
  promote such manufactures as tend to render them independent of others
  for essential, particularly military supplies"
- Hamilton's "Report on the Subject of Manufactures" (1791): founding
  document of the developmental state. Manufacturing would "diversify
  the industrious pursuits of the citizenry," raise agricultural
  productivity, encourage investment in machines
- Hamilton's key insight: countries could only capture productivity gains
  from division of labor by REJECTING it at the international level.
  Internal economic diversity was incompatible with national specialization.
  A rebuttal to Ricardo before comparative advantage was even written
- Hamilton wanted tariffs on foreign manufactures + elimination of tariffs
  on raw material imports (copper, sulfur, silk) → make American goods
  cheap relative to imports. Goal: promote entrepreneurship, grow "infant"
  industries that would eventually mature and not need protection
- Hamilton expected European capital to flow TO America: "a very material
  difference in profit" would "produce a transfer of foreign capital to
  the United States" — opposite of Ricardo's assumption
- Dallas Tariff (1816): duties as high as 30% on manufactured imports

### Friedrich List and "The National System"
- Friedrich List: German who moved to Pennsylvania in 1825 after persecution
  for "seditious activities." Had tried to create German customs union
- List saw US as model for German unification. Wrote letters defending
  "the American System," criticizing Smith's "erroneous" theories for
  ignoring "the fracture of the human race into nations"
- List wanted "political economy" not "cosmopolitical economy"
- "The National System of Political Economy" (1841): "free competition
  between two nations which are highly civilized can only be mutually
  beneficial in case both of them are in a nearly equal position of
  industrial development"
- List: "Consumption begets therefore production, as much as production
  begets consumption" — virtuous circle of wages → demand → more production
- List warned: "industry, left to itself, would soon fall to ruin, and
  a nation letting everything alone would commit suicide"
- List's limits: thought Latin America, China, India, Spain, Russia would
  FAIL to benefit from his program — lacked "certain stock of freedom,
  of security, of instruction." Criticized Spain: "the priesthood consumes
  the fat of the land and nourishes vicious indolence"
- US levied tariffs of ~45% on manufactured goods from 1870-1913
- By WWI eve, most European countries charging 20% tariffs on manufactures
  (exceptions: UK and Netherlands)

### High Imperialism and the Open Door
- World trade shrank relative to output starting in 1870s — wouldn't
  recover to that level for 100 years
- Britain's solution: massive colonial portfolio. Empire would be sink
  for exports + source of raw materials. Territories not allowed to
  develop using List's "National System"
- Scramble for Africa: Berlin Conference 1884-85 to prevent military
  clashes between European powers. By WWI eve, all of Africa except
  Ethiopia and Liberia under European control
- Japan fought China for Taiwan/Korea (1890s); Russia-Japan war (1904-05)
  over Korea and Manchuria — first war between mechanized armies
- US approach different: Open Door Policy. Confident merchants, uninterested
  in colonial dependencies. Focused on expanding domestic market via
  Manifest Destiny. First foreign branch of a US bank not established
  until just before WWI (Buenos Aires)

### From World Wars to World Order
- US profited as supplier to WWI belligerents: exported more than twice
  what it imported → "largest favorable trade balance that has ever existed
  in any country" (economist John H. Williams, 1921)
- US refused to build new order — feared Europeans wanted to constrain
  US power. Focused on getting Britain/France to repay wartime loans
  while hoarding gold → international trade fell from 27% of global
  output (1913) to 20% (1923-28)
- Great Depression: trade collapsed to 11% of world economy by 1932
- Smoot-Hawley Tariff Act (1930): punitive American taxes → global
  retaliation → competitive currency devaluations, rising tariffs,
  deglobalization
- KEY LESSON of Smoot-Hawley: countries with large trade surpluses have
  them ONLY because they cannot consume all they produce — makes them
  extremely vulnerable to decline in international trade
- Barter agreements: most notorious between Nazi Germany and Soviet Union
- French exports to colonies jumped from 19% to 28% (1929-1938); Italian
  exports to African possessions from 2% to 23%; Japanese exports to
  imperial possessions from 35% to 63%
- WWII: US became "Arsenal of Democracy" (FDR). US exports grew 45%
  between 1938-1941. Japan lost access to US imports → invaded Southeast
  Asia for oil, rubber, supplies
- Bretton Woods (1944): 44 countries, shortly after Normandy landings.
  Goal: prevent return to 1920s/30s economic anarchy. Harry Dexter White
  and John Maynard Keynes — architects. Neither wanted to bring back
  capital mobility that had distorted prewar trade
- ITO proposed but died in 1950 (US objected to jurisdiction over domestic
  policy). GATT thrived instead under US leadership. US dominated world
  trade: 35% of all manufacturing exports
- Despite liberalization, trade didn't return to pre-1929 importance.
  Cross-border flows wouldn't reach 1870s zenith until the 1970s
- Decolonization + Cold War + import substitution limited trade growth

### Container Shipping Revolution
- 1950s shipping: era of jet plane and rocket ship, but moving goods was
  SLOWER and MORE EXPENSIVE than 19th century
- Pre-container: cold-rolled steel, coffee beans, and clothes traveled
  together. Loading/unloading employed tens of thousands of longshoremen.
  One week to load a cargo ship, one week to unload, one week to reload
  for return. 2/3 of shipping cost = time in port, not the ocean voyage
- Shipping from US to Europe: sailing Brooklyn to Bremerhaven only ~10
  days, but total shipment took MONTHS
- Longshoremen unions "relentlessly creative at finding ways to reduce
  efficiency" — prepackaged bundles would be unpacked then repacked by
  workers charging by the hour plus overtime
- Containerization: standardized metal boxes, interchangeable across all
  transport modes. Cut time in port from days to hours. Cut opportunities
  for theft (explains why Scotch whisky exporters were early adopters)
- Ships now carry 10x as many containers as 1960s but spend far less
  time in port
- Vietnam War as catalyst: Saigon port even worse than Brooklyn. Corrupt
  Vietnamese generals stole desirable items. US Army hired Malcom McLean
  (original innovator of container shipping) to build container port at
  Cam Ranh Bay. Within months, solved the army's logistics problem
  AND showed the world what containerization could do. (McLean also
  profited by having ships pick up Japanese goods for the return trip)
- 1970s: shipping companies embraced containers, borrowed heavily for
  new vessels. Glut of capacity + oil price quadrupled → severe recession,
  many lines went bankrupt. By 1980s, stabilized
- By late 1980s: deregulation + falling costs → increasingly made sense
  to spread production across the world. Smith's specialization applied
  at a scale he never imagined

### How Global Value Chains Distort Bilateral Trade Data
- Detroit-Windsor (20-minute drive): Great Lakes motor vehicle complex
  may be first recognizably modern global value chain. Trade between
  Canada and Michigan > trade between Canada and China
- Three cross-border manufacturing networks centered on: US, Germany,
  China (previously Japan until ~2007)
- Trade of intermediate inputs = more than HALF of all international trade.
  Finished goods/services = only one-third. (Energy and metals = rest)
- US exports to Canada and Mexico ≈ US exports to EU, China, Japan, Korea
  combined. But much of the VALUE comes from elsewhere
- Seatbelt example: fibers manufactured in Mexico, woven/dyed in Canada
  (water abundance), sent to Mexico to sew, installed in US plant.
  Nearly half of content of motor vehicles imported from Mexico was
  originally made in the US
- China electronics (2007): ostensibly exported $290B of "computer,
  electronic, optical" products, but ~$120B (40%) of value came from
  Korea, Japan, Taiwan. Even now, ~1/3 of China's imports from Korea/
  Taiwan originated elsewhere. Taiwanese academics: standard figures
  overstate Taiwan-China trade by factor of THREE
- For US: imports overstated by ~16%, exports overstated by ~20%.
  Chinese imports AND exports both overstated by ~30%
- Nearly half of exports sold by Germany's eastern neighbors = foreign
  components. More than half of motor vehicles produced by German car
  companies made OUTSIDE Germany. ~1/3 of Germany's own automotive
  export value comes from neighbors
- Port distortion: US exports land at Antwerp/Rotterdam before moving to
  France/Germany/Italy. Similarly HK/Singapore for East Asia
- Strange result: US reports $151B in exports to Belgium, Netherlands,
  HK, Singapore (2018) — more than exports to China ($121B), nearly as
  much as France+Germany+UK combined ($161B). These are artifacts of
  flawed reporting, not real economic indicators

### How Corporate Tax Avoidance Distorts Trade Data
- "People who in previous eras might have written symphonies or designed
  cathedrals have instead saved companies hundreds of billions of dollars
  in taxes by shifting trillions of dollars of intangible assets across
  the world" — great cultural reference/allegory
- US income tax (1913): assessed nothing on money earned abroad. By 1950s,
  companies started relocating to exploit lower foreign tax rates
- Kennedy admin proposed "capital export neutrality" — pay US taxes
  regardless of structure. Alternative: "capital import neutrality" —
  territorial system where each country taxes its own
- Revenue Act of 1962: split the difference. "Active business" profits
  from foreign factories untaxed as long as reinvested abroad. "Passive"
  income (royalties, licenses, dividends) taxed at US rates (Subpart F)
- Treasury Decision 8697 (1996): "check-the-box" rule. Supposed to
  simplify things → instead opened massive loopholes. Income from
  royalties/licenses could now be treated as "active." IRS recognized
  the problem, proposed new rule, but POLITICAL INTERFERENCE blocked fix
- Once Subpart F neutered: "creatives in law and accounting departments"
  exploited "intangibility" — patents occupy no physical space, can be
  moved anywhere by filling out forms
- The scheme: set up subsidiary in corporate tax haven → sell it the
  patents → subsidiary licenses patents to rest of company → collects
  large cut of global sales as "royalties" → profits shifted from
  high-tax to low-tax jurisdictions
- US effective corporate tax rate: dropped from >35% (mid-1990s) to ~30%
  (early 2000s) to ~26% (mid-2010s). 2017 tax law pushed below 20%
- ~40% of ALL profits earned by multinationals outside home markets
  shifted from high-tax (China, France, Germany, Japan, US) to low-tax
  (Caymans, Ireland, Singapore) jurisdictions

### The Apple/iPhone Case Study (Tim Cook reference from chapter title)
- Apple makes few goods itself — Foxconn assembles iPhones from components
  Apple doesn't produce. Most value captured by Apple (shareholders +
  American workers who designed software/product/operations)
- iPhone should generate: exports for component makers (Korea, Japan,
  Taiwan), imports/exports for assembler (China), exports for software
  (US). Instead, much of Apple's American value counted as export from
  corporate tax haven
- Foxconn's Zhengzhou plant (half of all iPhones): technically in a
  "bonded zone" — not in China for customs purposes. Apple buys finished
  phones from Foxconn BEFORE they enter China, sells to Irish subsidiaries,
  which sell to the world at hefty margin
- Apple paid only ~18% effective tax rate (2017 fiscal year)
- Microsoft: effective rate also ~18% (2015-17), attributed only 12% of
  total profits to US sales. "Foreign earnings taxed at lower rates"
  shaved 19 percentage points off US rate
- Google: also ~18% effective rate

### Creative Taxation — Other Examples
- Pharma: R&D costs billions, manufacturing cost trivially low. Move
  patents offshore → huge tax savings. J&J: ~17% effective rate,
  "international operations" shaved 17 points off headline rate
- Starbucks: Dutch subsidiary collected 6% of all non-US sales as
  "royalty" for using company's "intellectual property." UK operation
  "consistently lost money." Dutch subsidiary collecting all royalty
  income ALSO claimed it made no money — despite having only "some
  office space and a few dozen employees"
- 2/3 of FDI income from American multinationals officially came from
  7 tiny tax havens: Bermuda, Caymans, Ireland, Luxembourg, Netherlands,
  Singapore, Switzerland
- US direct investments abroad consistently yield ~4% more than foreign
  investments in the US (tax-driven distortion)
- 3/4 of net income from foreign subsidiaries of American corporations
  attributed to handful of tiny tax havens

### Ireland as Tax Haven Hub
- Cork: Apple's European HQ since 1980 (~6,000 workers). Pfizer,
  GlaxoSmithKline, J&J also there. Dublin: Facebook, Google, Microsoft
- Official corporate tax rate: 12.5% (one of world's lowest)
- But real trick: operations "registered" in Ireland can be "tax-resident"
  in Caymans/Bermuda (rate = 0%). "Double Irish" + Dutch sandwich =
  effective rate near zero
- 2018: Irish subsidiaries of US corporations generated $53B in profits
  ≈ same as US subsidiaries in Canada ($31B), China ($13B), Japan ($13B)
  COMBINED
- Dutch subsidiaries: $87B ≈ Australia ($10B) + Brazil ($4B) + UK ($47B) +
  France ($2B) + Germany ($7B) + HK ($8B) + Mexico ($9B) combined
- 7 corporate tax havens responsible for $324B in US direct investment
  income (2018)

### The Hidden Stash
- 1998-2017: US companies in 7 tax havens "earned" and "reinvested" >$2.1T
  in profits. Rest of world: earned and reinvested <$1.5T — difference
  of ~$640B
- Money ended up back in US as fixed-income investments (despite being
  "foreign" for tax purposes)
- Apple 2017 annual report: financial assets "held by foreign subsidiaries"
  yet invested in "dollar-denominated holdings"
- Microsoft: "investments are predominantly U.S. dollar-denominated
  securities" but 96% of financial assets "held by our foreign
  subsidiaries and would be subject to material repatriation tax effect"
- 2012-2017: US companies reinvested ~$1.2T in main tax havens. Same
  period: US bonds held by Caribbean, Ireland, Luxembourg, Netherlands,
  Singapore, Switzerland increased by... $1.2T. Not a coincidence
- Irish residents ostensibly owned >$688B in US Treasury/agency/corporate
  debt (end of 2017), up from just >$200B (start of 2012)
- Apple + Microsoft alone could explain nearly half of total increase in
  reported Irish holdings of US bonds
- 2017 tax law allowed repatriation: companies withdrew $319B from tax
  havens in 2018 → $256B decline in US bonds held by residents of
  those same havens (Nov 2017 – Jun 2018)

## CHAPTER 2: The Growth of Global Finance

### Three Mental Models of Trade-Finance Relationship
1. Finance = mostly trade finance. Financial transactions driven by
   production/transport costs. Trade imbalances self-correcting via
   Ricardian comparative advantage. Global economy benefits.
2. Finance = rational investment seeking most productive opportunities.
   Capital flows from rich → developing economies. Trade surpluses for
   rich, deficits for developing. Economy benefits because helps
   convergence. (Describes 19th century reasonably well)
3. Finance driven by wide variety: rational investment, speculation,
   capital flight, fads, panics, mercantilism, desire for safety.
   Trade imbalances caused by this mix. Connection to prosperity is
   ACCIDENTAL. If driven by anything other than rational investment,
   trade imbalances probably DETRACT from growth and distort societies.
   → THIS IS THE MODEL THAT BEARS CLOSEST RELATION TO REALITY

### Cross-Border Finance: Scale
- 1855: cross-border financial claims = just 16% of one year's global output
- 1870: jumped to 94%
- Today: OVER 400%
- Growth occurred in cycles of booms and busts

### The Pattern of Lending Booms
- Every international lending boom preceded by same phenomena:
  1. Structural change expands definition/amount of money → rapid credit
  2. Asset boom encourages increasingly risky behavior (more leverage)
  3. Some event sets off fashion for foreign securities → money pours
     across borders into risky developing countries
- Recipients have little in common besides being remote from financial
  centers. Boom ends when sudden expansion of lending ends even more suddenly

### Proof: Synchronized Credit Cycles Disprove Standard Theory
- If rational investment theory correct, distribution of cross-border
  lending should look random (each country evaluated individually).
  Instead: history replete with SYNCHRONIZED credit cycles
- UK-US example: 18th-19th century business cycles tightly linked.
  Correlation stronger with FINANCIAL CONDITIONS (Bank of England gold
  reserves) than with underlying trade (cotton supply)
- When BoE comfortable with gold reserves → both countries expand
- When BoE pulling gold back → both countries contract
- "Economic crises and panics in the United States seemed to be linked
  more to British gold imbalances than anything else"

### The First Global Credit Boom: The 1820s
- Post-Napoleonic Britain: rapid growth, technological progress (trains,
  steamships, gas lighting, textiles). Rich English savers eager for
  profitable outlets after decades of wartime uncertainty
- Resulting frenzy spread to most far-fetched projects — including at
  least one loan to a country that DIDN'T EXIST
- Friedrich Engels called its collapse the "first general crisis"
- Four key changes in British financial conditions:
  1. 700M-franc indemnity payment on France (Treaty of Paris) → Baring
     Brothers arranged French rentes → cash flooded London markets
  2. British gov't converted perpetual bonds from 5% to 4% coupons →
     investors received £2.8M in cash (had to find new investments)
  3. Parliament allowed provincial banks to print money (1822) → banks
     "swamped the whole country with paper money"
  4. End of Napoleon's Continental System blockade → recovery of European
     export markets + collapse of military spending → Bank of England
     bullion reserves surged from £4M (1821) to £14M (late 1824)

### Cultural Reference/Allegory: The Kingdom of Poyais
- Most entertaining transaction of the 1820s boom: £200,000 offering by
  the kingdom of Poyais — a FICTITIOUS Central American country
- "Self-appointed king" Sir Gregor MacGregor: Scottish adventurer who
  fought alongside Simón Bolívar (has a statue in Caracas)
- Bond actually traded up before investors realized there was no such
  country. MacGregor had invented his kingdom and fooled bankers into
  underwriting and promoting growth prospects for an imaginary borrower

### The Boom-Bust Pattern
- 1822: £2M loan to new republic of Colombia (negotiated by Francisco
  Antonio Zea). Half immediately exchanged at 20% discount against
  inflated claims. Colombia received very little actual cash
- But the loan was great success for INVESTORS — bankers made large profits
  on limited risk. British investors buying 6% foreign bonds at 80-84 cents
  vs 4% on their own government perpetuals → Latin American loan market took off
- Chile (£1M), Peru (£450K), Denmark (£2M), Russia (£6.5M) all came to market
- 50+ stock companies organized in 1824-25 for sole purpose of operating
  in Latin America (authorized capital >£35M, mostly not put up)
- Latin American consumption grew strongly, imports from England doubled
  by 1825 in just four years
- By 1825: many investments of questionable value — common pattern when
  small countries try to absorb large financial inflows. Not enough
  productive opportunities → excess credit finances boondoggles, consumer
  imports, or weapons for civil wars
- Bank of England tightened: bullion fell from £14M (1824) to £2M (late 1825).
  Raised discount rate to bring gold home
- Rising rates → financing costs on commodities surge → prices decline →
  banks that lent against coffee, tin, iron, sugar, cotton in trouble →
  cotton trading firm failures (Nov 1825) →
  BoE tightened further to protect liquidity. Mid-December: two major
  London banks (agents for dozens of Yorkshire textile provincials)
  collapsed → general panic
- 76 of 806 banks in England and Scotland closed permanently during crisis.
  Walter Bagehot: BoE itself nearly forced to suspend payments. Remaining
  banks called in loans → forced industrial companies to cut production/
  lay off workers
- Summer 1826: crisis spread to Berlin, Amsterdam, St. Petersburg, Vienna,
  Rome, Paris. Collapsed European demand crushed Latin American commodity
  prices and export revenues
- Mid-1825 to end-1828: zero new foreign loans in British market
- By 1829, every Latin American borrower except Brazil had defaulted
  (Brazil needed emergency £800K loan in 1829 to make interest payments)
- "British financial press was soon filled with anger and recrimination"
- In retrospect: classic speculative bubble. Took a full generation before
  Latin American borrowers regained full access to international markets

### The 1830s and the Second International Lending Boom
- Just ten years after 1820s crash, British capital markets afflicted by
  another speculative fever — this time focused on the United States
- Unlike most cycles (many lenders, many borrowers), the 1830s/40s was
  driven by tight links between single lender (Britain) and single
  borrower (US)
- By 1830: recovery underway. Bumper harvests drove down grain prices →
  lifted purchasing power. Bank Act of 1826 permitted new banks of issue
  (notes worth ≥£5). Until 1833, only 34 new banks; between 1833-35,
  34 more; in 1836 alone, 42 new banks of issue
- US: Andrew Jackson shifted deposits from Second Bank to politically
  connected "pet" banks → rapid banking expansion. State banks: 329
  (1829, $110M capital) → 506 (1834) → 788 (1837, >$500M capital)
- Federal government selling huge amounts of public land → speculators
  bought with borrowed money → "effectively converting unimproved land
  into mortgages" → credit creation drove up asset prices on both sides
  of Atlantic
- US trade deficit ballooned from avg $2M/yr (1823-30) to avg $24M/yr
  (1831-36)
- July 1836: Jackson's "specie circular" — all land purchases in physical
  gold/silver only. Bank lending suddenly limited to actual bullion on hand
- Bank of England simultaneously raised discount rate: 4% → 4.5% → 5%
- BoE directors decided to stop lending to British banking houses with
  "excessive" American exposure → "ham-fisted announcement precipitated
  a global credit contraction"
- Cotton price (America's principal export) dropped 30% in early 1837
- American banking houses in New Orleans and New York began closing.
  British banks, suffering own slowdown, cut off all further lending
- Panic pushed British private banks to pull gold from BoE. Reserves
  dropped from £14M to £2.4M. BoE had to apply for extraordinary
  £2M gold loan from Bank of France
- By 1842 (depression bottom): Pennsylvania — one of richest states and
  heaviest borrowers — suspended interest payments. Arkansas, Florida,
  Illinois, Indiana, Maryland, Michigan, Mississippi already defaulted
- States alone (excluding private borrowers) defaulted/rescheduled $120M
- Mississippi, Arkansas, Florida: "with carefully rationalized arguments,
  simply repudiated the debt outright"

### Cultural Reference: Rothschild's Fury
- James Mayer de Rothschild (head of French branch of House of Rothschild)
  reportedly told a visiting US Treasury representative: "you may tell
  your government that you have seen the man who is the head of the
  finances of Europe, and that he has told you that you cannot borrow
  a dollar, not a dollar"
- "The fury and sense of betrayal of English investors led to an outpouring
  of hatred and scorn for the American rascals, and English literature is
  the richer for the many scathing diatribes that followed"

### The "First Global Financial Crisis": 1873
- Long expansion from late 1850s through US Civil War and German/Italian
  unification. Imports/exports more than doubled in UK, US, France,
  Austria, Belgium
- Financial innovation: Salmon P. Chase marketed bonds to middle-class
  Americans (with Jay Cooke & Co.) to finance Civil War. France created
  first universal investment banks: Crédit Industriel et Commercial (1859),
  Crédit Lyonnais (1863), Société Générale (1864). Paris rivaled London
  for international loans by mid-1860s
- Germany: Gründerzeit (founders' era) — so many joint-stock companies
  created 1866-73 they still exist today. Austrian bank capital: 190M
  gulden (1866) → 508M gulden (end 1872). 15 more banks with 72M paid-up
  capital in first 3 months of 1873 alone
- Most important trigger: 5-billion-franc indemnity on France after losing
  Franco-Prussian War (1870-71). France paid it off in just 3 years →
  transfer of wealth equal to 20-25% of EITHER country's GDP
- German banks funded by French reparations → mortgage banks → construction
  boom + "notorious stock swindles" that came close to bringing scandal
  to Bismarck's government
- In US: speculative frenzy in railway stocks/bonds. Jay Gould and
  "Diamond" Jim Brady became notorious figures. British investors bought
  £57M of Latin American bonds in the entire 1860s, then scooped up
  £59M in just first 3 years of 1870s
- Carlos Marichal (Mexican economic historian): "all the Latin American
  states were besieged by the European moneylenders"
- New German Empire thrown into massive trade deficit as valuable currency
  drove manufacturers out of global markets (masked by property/financial
  booms)
- Crisis began: Vienna stock market crash (May 8, 1873). Spread to NYC:
  Jay Cooke & Company — America's largest private bank and financial
  agent to the US government — forced to close (Sept 18) because of
  Northern Pacific Railway bonds
- Jay Cooke's closing broke NYSE. Sellers poured in, speculators forced
  to liquidate. Banks suspended payments. NYSE closed, didn't reopen
  until month's end. US entered 5 years of what was then called
  "the Great Depression"
- October: crisis returned across Atlantic to Germany. November: England.
  BoE raised discount rate to fight gold rush, worsening downturn.
  Russia and Scandinavia sucked in. France least affected (losing the
  war and paying reparations had kept it out of the boom!)
- Banks worldwide collapsed, survivors hoarded gold. Global credit
  contraction → international borrowers couldn't raise funds → defaults.
  Chile avoided defaulting on foreign bonds only by liquidating its
  banking system and losing all its gold → declared currency no longer
  convertible to bullion (1878)

### The Baring Crisis and the First Lending Boom of the 20th Century
- Argentina: economically insignificant through 1820s-60s. By late 1870s:
  emerged as major exporter of wheat, leather, chilled meat. Under
  General Julio Argentino Roca: reformed currency/banking, borrowed
  heavily for infrastructure and military
- By 1889: 40-50% of all British funds invested outside home market were
  in Argentina
- Late 1880s: President Miguel Juárez Celman's inflationary policies
  alarmed investors. Baring Brothers tried to place £2M offering for
  Buenos Aires Water and Drainage Company — could sell only £150,000
  to investors, had to hold the rest itself
- Argentina converted peso-denominated cedulas (mortgage bonds, largest
  component of foreign financing) into gold-denominated cedulas — risky
  strategy (Mexico did something similar in 1994 with dollar-denominated
  debt)
- BoE raised discount from 3% to 6% (beginning to October 1889) to
  discourage gold outflows. New Argentine loans declined: £23M → £12M
  (1889) → £5M (1890)
- July 1890: Argentine finance minister resigned over Celman's monetary
  policy → exchange rate crisis → inflation spike → cost of servicing
  gold cedulas soared → attempted military coup → Celman fled
- Argentine securities dropped below 40 cents on the dollar
- November 1890: losses nearly bankrupted Baring Brothers. BoE assembled
  consortium of private banks to rescue Baring (echoed in 1998 when Fed
  organized bailout of LTCM after Russia's default)
- Rescue insufficient → broader financial panic. British lenders slashed
  foreign lending → international panics for 3 years

### The 1920s "Dance of the Millions" and the Interwar Period
- 1910s: US commercial banks established first overseas branches (including
  Argentina). Unusually, this lending boom didn't end in large-scale
  defaults because WWI boosted commodity export revenues. Only countries
  that defaulted (Russia, Mexico, Ottoman Empire) did so after revolutions
  or invasions
- During WWI: Americans lent to Europeans to buy US food/manufactures.
  After war: lent to Germans to pay reparations to France to service
  debts to UK to repay debts to US — circular lending strategy
- Dawes Plan (1924): Americans lent to Germany → comfortable with
  international lending → loan market took off
- In Latin America: rapid lending expansion called "the dance of the
  millions" — drove up prices of Latin loans, ships, commodities, US stocks
- 1929 crash + US banking collapse → abrupt end. Temporary explosion of
  lending in first half of 1930 → too little, too late. Peripheral
  countries: couldn't raise US money, couldn't earn commodity revenues
  → massive defaults

### The Less-Developed-Country Lending Boom (1970s-80s)
- Roots: 1950s, Soviet Union moved dollar deposits to Swiss banks to
  protect from confiscation → Eurodollar market born. European and
  Japanese banks lent these dollars outside US, creating parallel
  dollar-denominated system connected to but distinct from regulated
  New York system
- Great Inflation: US banking regulations (since 1930s) limited interest
  rates on deposits. By late 1960s-70s, accelerating inflation meant
  savers LOSING money in accounts. Solution: commercial paper sold to
  money-market mutual funds → replicated bank accounts with higher yields
  → gave European/Japanese banks access to dollars for lending
- Oil price: $2 → nearly $40/barrel over 1970s (supply disruptions,
  dwindling US production). Oil exporters flooded with dollars, couldn't
  spend fast enough → deposited in Eurodollar market → banks funded
  lending boom to Latin America, Soviet bloc, even North Korea
- Initially successful. After surviving first oil shock, LDCs responded
  to credit influx with faster growth and surging consumer imports.
  Within a few years, world's largest banks had lent multiples of their
  equity to developing countries
- Killed by sharp, deliberate monetary contraction: Fed (1980-82) to end
  Great Inflation. Interest rates soared — West German chancellor
  Helmut Schmidt: real interest rates at highest levels "since the birth
  of Jesus Christ"
- Debt servicing costs soared while commodity export revenues collapsed.
  Banks unwilling to make new Eurodollar loans. Beginning with Mexico
  (August 1982), LDCs began asking for debt relief → "lost decade" of 1980s

### The European Banking Glut and the Crisis of 2008
- Most significant financial development: launch of euro (1999). Dozen
  separate currencies → one. Foreign exchange risk eliminated (even as
  default risk unchanged)
- European banks suddenly liberated to lend across continental monetary
  union. French banks → Italian/Greek borrowers. Spanish banks → French/
  German savers. Debt owed by euro-area residents to residents of OTHER
  euro countries increased by >€8 TRILLION between 2002-2008
- European banks expanded outside Europe, especially to US. By 2007:
  >40% of all bank credit to US nonbank private sector came from lenders
  headquartered OUTSIDE the US, mostly from Europe
- European banks had "effectively become American banks" — borrowed
  dollars by issuing short-term debt to Americans, used proceeds to buy
  US mortgage bonds
- Deutsche Bank underwrote MORE subprime mortgage bonds than Goldman
  Sachs, Bank of America, Citigroup, J.P. Morgan, or Countrywide
- European investors owned ~20% of all US asset-backed securities on
  eve of crisis
- During 2008 panic: most of Fed's emergency loans went to banks OUTSIDE
  the US, mainly in Europe
- Hyun Song Shin (economist): "the U.S.-dollar denominated assets of
  banks outside the United States are comparable in size to the total
  assets of the U.S. commercial banking sector, peaking at over $10
  trillion prior to the crisis"
- Fig 2.2: Foreign banks provided comparable credit to US nonfinancial
  sector as US-chartered banks (~$3T vs ~$6T at peak, but foreign banks
  grew faster 2005-2008)
- Same essential pattern as 1820s: structural change in banking regulation
  → boom in lending from starting market to rest of world. Changing
  European financial conditions affected US. Then US mortgage defaults
  redounded back to Europe

### Chapter 2 Conclusion
- International flows don't consist mostly of trade finance. Not driven
  primarily by rational investment seeking productive opportunities.
  Instead: driven by changes in credit conditions and speculative sentiment
- To understand international capital flows → must understand what drives
  SAVINGS. Savings rate determined NOT by cultural factors or attitudes
  toward thrift, but by how income is distributed domestically

## CHAPTER 3: Saving, Investment, and Imbalances

### The Scarcity vs. Abundance Paradox
- When resources scarce (most of human history): suppressing consumption
  (saving) is NECESSARY to fund investment and raise living standards.
  Growing populations consumed any additional output → capped living
  standards
- When resources abundant (modern era): trying to save by consuming less
  is WASTEFUL and counterproductive. People left idle, fields fallow,
  factories deteriorate. Cutting consumption simply leads to lower
  production. Excess capacity DISCOURAGES new investment → lower living
  standards
- "High-savings model" = the original version of trickle-down growth

### The Accounting Identities
- Global demand = Global production
- Demand = Consumption + Investment
- Production = Consumption + Savings
- Domestic demand = GDP + Imports - Exports
- Exports - Imports = Domestic saving - Domestic investment
- KEY: Trade imbalances = difference between domestic saving and investment

### When Trade Imbalances Help vs. Hurt
- HELPFUL: countries with few domestic investment opportunities (mature
  demographics, tech frontier) export surpluses to countries that NEED
  capital goods/infrastructure. Without this, famine: Soviet Union
  1929-33 and China 1958-62 — both committed to rapid industrialization
  while cut off from world trade
- HARMFUL: when imports crowd out domestic production. People in certain
  countries spending too little and saving too much — NOT because
  households are thrifty or governments prudent. Because ELITES transfer
  wealth/income from people who would SPEND (workers, pensioners) to
  people who SAVE (the rich). Forces untenable choice on rest of world:
  absorb the glut or endure a slump from insufficient global demand

### Two Development Models: High Savings vs. High Wages
- Investment = GDP + Imports - Consumption - Exports
- HIGH-SAVINGS MODEL: forces ordinary people to spend less so government/
  businesses can spend more. Consumption squeezed to pay for productive
  investment in infrastructure and capital goods (not monuments/military).
  Raises living standards even as workers' share of output declines.
  "The original version of trickle-down growth"
- Requires authoritarian political culture or high degree of centralization
- British example: aristocratic landowners used state power to evict
  subsistence farmers → enclosed estates → boosted agricultural profits
  → displaced peasants flooded cities → rising numbers limited bargaining
  power → real wages didn't rise despite rising output per hour → boosted
  manufacturer profits → reinvested in more capacity
- 1740: just 4% of British production saved. By 1820s: national saving
  rate 14% → Britain an industrial superpower exporting excess output
- But Britain also used HIGH-WAGE elements: workers earned higher pay
  than rest of Europe. High productivity + favorable business climate
  pulled in capital from abroad. Dutch estimated to have paid for ~1/3
  of Britain's total investment in 18th century (Dutch capital attracted
  by British tariffs and what would now be called "IP theft")

### The American Development Story
- Pre-Civil War South: "exceptionally cruel form of agrarian feudalism."
  Cotton, tobacco, cash crops for British manufacturers. Extreme wealth/
  income inequality reinforced by brutal subjugation → crushed consumption
- But planters had little interest in economic development. Instead of
  buying capital goods, spent surpluses buying MORE enslaved workers and
  land. Contributed to industrialization only INDIRECTLY: trapped behind
  high tariffs → forced to buy Northern manufactures. Southern exports
  (generated by slave labor) helped pay for Northern imports of advanced
  European technology/machines → boosted the North's manufacturing capacity
- The North: HIGH-WAGE model. Abundant land, liberal institutions, "Yankee
  ingenuity" → highest pay in the world + rapid living standard increases.
  High birth rates + immigration → US population: 4M (1790) → 40M (1870)
  → 80M (1900). Protective tariffs biased market toward American goods
- Combined result: investments in America incredibly attractive for
  European savers (especially British). Foreign saving supplemented
  domestic saving → lifted US investment without depressing consumption.
  Until end of 19th century, US consistently imported more goods than
  it exported even as manufacturing output soared
- Anglo-American relationship in 19th century = Anglo-Dutch in 18th:
  transfer of excess output from smaller but more advanced society to
  larger one undergoing rapid industrialization. Same forces attracted
  millions of immigrants; economists estimate value of human capital
  inflow worth several times the value of conventional foreign investment
- North's victory in Civil War + westward expansion solidified high-wage
  commitment + increased industrial potential. Rising inequality after
  Civil War eventually depressed consumption relative to production →
  more investment funded internally → by early 20th century, America
  became a net exporter

### America's Influence on Germany and Japan
- Friedrich List had explicitly argued America was model for unified
  German economy. Erasmus Peshine Smith published "Manual of Political
  Economy" (1853) — perhaps most important theoretical defense of
  America's developmental state
- Smith saw abolitionism, protectionism, and mass immigration as common
  program opposed to free trade and slavery. America's high wages (product
  of high tariffs, abundant land, human liberty outside the South) caused
  exceptional productivity → forced businesses to invest in capital
  equipment → rapid population growth expanded domestic market
- Smith's arguments found ready audience in Japan. Shogunate had been
  forced to accept disadvantageous commercial treaties when American
  naval ships arrived at Tokyo Bay in 1853 (couldn't levy tariffs >5%).
  Smith was invited to advise new Meiji government (1871) and became
  influential adviser
- Japan adopted the American System: internal improvements (roads,
  railroads), "model factories" (echoing Hamilton) for ships and military
  kit. Economic historian Kenichi Ohno: these factories "had strong
  demonstration effects on emerging Japanese entrepreneurs" and trained
  engineers who later established other factories. Paid for with land
  taxes and forced loans from rich Japanese
- Japan had large trade deficit — imported almost all advanced machinery
  (railroad engines, electric generators) plus raw materials like cotton

### The Soviet High-Savings Extreme
- Stalin: manufacturing capacity as national security imperative — echoing
  Washington 150 years earlier. But unlike Americans, developed capacity
  WITHOUT trade deficits funded by foreign investment
- Bolshevik coup (1917) + repudiation of foreign debts → pariah state.
  Also opposed to foreign investors on ideological grounds
- Exports in 1920s-30s: base metals, gold, grain. Mining required many
  workers, supplied cheaply by gulag system of forced labor camps
- Peasants: had fought and won separate revolution against old landlords
  after 1917, keeping surplus they generated. Lenin's New Economic Policy
  accommodated these "agrarian capitalists" — likened it to Treaty of
  Brest-Litovsk (temporary expedient)
- By late 1920s: Stalin chose collectivization — violently enslaved
  Soviet peasantry. Even though agricultural output was LOWER under state
  ownership, government had total control and could extract substantial
  surplus by squeezing peasants
- Grain exports soared in early 1930s even as tens of millions starved.
  By end of 1930s: typical Soviet got fewer calories from grain than in
  prerevolutionary period, but far more calories from vodka
- Humanitarian catastrophe enabled imports of industrial equipment from
  main trade partner: Nazi Germany. Ideological differences insignificant
  vs complementary economic needs — both international pariahs cut off
  from global markets. Nazi Germany traded advanced manufactures for raw
  materials (mirrored 19th-century imperial Germany/tsarist Russia trade)
- Germans willing to supply technology to "hated communists" because they
  thought Soviets couldn't modernize fast enough to become military threat.
  Soviets held opposite view (thought Nazis would focus on fighting West)
  → happy to provide raw materials to pay for industrial transformation
- By WWII eve: Stalin achieved indigenous industrial base. Without it,
  could not have defeated the Germans. But enormous self-imposed cost:
  much of population conscripted into forced labor, tens of millions
  starved, soldiers had modern tanks and aircraft but lacked basic goods
  like boots and radios. "Soviet Union's experience demonstrated both
  the triumph and the limitations of the high-savings growth model"

### Japan's Postwar Social Contract
- More humane variant of high-savings model. Workers, businesses,
  government agreed on social contract for rapid growth
- Workers: agreed not to strike, limited wage increase requests
- Businesses: agreed to reinvest profits in domestic capacity and
  technological improvements, refrain from excessive executive pay
- Government: supported companies with cheap loans at expense of regular
  savers + import restrictions to protect domestic market
- Handful of conglomerates dominated economy in oligopoly at consumers'
  expense. Abroad, these same companies competed ferociously for market
  share → forced efficiency and innovation
- Japanese workers, consumers, retirees all subsidized industrial
  development by taking home lower share of national output than Western
  counterparts, using financial system designed to transfer purchasing
  power from households to businesses
- Japan's exports consistently exceeded imports despite rapid growth
- Problem: as Japan converged to Western living standards, institutional
  biases kept investing even after best projects completed. By early
  1980s, mechanisms to constrain household spending and subsidize
  corporations had outlived their usefulness. Incremental gains from
  each additional investment fell while constraints on households remained
- Japanese society eventually adjusted in 1990s and 2000s, but
  unnecessarily painfully: business investment plunged, unemployment rose,
  household saving rate dropped to zero to compensate for weak wage growth.
  Could have been avoided by abandoning development model earlier

### Korea: Both Models in One Country
- After 1948 division: until 1970s, communist North appeared more
  successful than market-oriented South (which had copied Japanese model)
- Over time: Soviet system's advantage (rapid physical capital development)
  outweighed by disadvantages of low productivity, inability to innovate,
  systemic malinvestment
- South Korea's conglomerates (chaebol), consciously echoing Japanese
  models, had to upgrade technology to compete globally
- South Korea living standards: 1/10 of US (1970) → ~half of US (after
  1997 crisis) → ~70% of US (2016) — comparable to Japan, NZ, France
- One of few countries to transition successfully from poor to rich

### 19th Century Summary
- In 19th century: America's high-wage model was complementary to
  Britain's surpluses. World prospered because recipients of resources
  generally made best use of them, and excess production came from
  richest/most advanced societies
- By end of 20th century: things reversed. Poor countries often
  subsidizing consumption of the rich — at the expense of both

### Excess Savings and the Great Glut
- Scarcity stopped being serious problem in rich world near last quarter
  of 20th century. "Making things has become easier and cheaper than
  ever before. Shortages have been replaced with gluts"
- Two basic inputs (labor, capital) both abundant for decades:
  - Unemployment systematically higher since 1970s than before. Even
    more extreme accounting for part-time employment and rising share of
    working-age people who neither work nor attend school
  - US manufacturing capacity utilization: 83% avg (1948-1979), 80% avg
    (1980-1999), 75% avg (since 2000). Manufacturing capacity shrunk
    slightly since 2008, but output shrunk even more
- Fig 3.1: US manufacturing capacity utilization rate trending down
  across all three periods

### Corporate Behavior Has Changed
- Traditionally: business sector should spend MORE expanding capacity
  than it generates in cash flow (difference covered by household savings)
- In past few decades: business sectors in many countries now spend LESS
  than they generate in cash flow. Surpluses either distributed to
  shareholders (US) or retained by companies (Germany, Japan, South Korea)
- Implication: far fewer worthwhile investment opportunities in rich world.
  Those remaining (infrastructure, housing) hampered by political
  constraints, not excessive cost of capital

### Prices Confirm the Glut
- Capital equipment prices in US: dropped 30% since 1991 in absolute terms
- Durable consumption goods (cars, appliances, furniture): dropped >36%
  since peak in 1995
- Clothing and footwear: lower than in mid-1980s
- Since 1990, most US inflation from: healthcare (including prescriptions),
  housing, education — all sectors where government tightly regulates
  supply and heavily subsidizes demand. Rest of economy: flat prices
- Manufactured goods cost relative to average disposable income: collapsed
  >90% since late 1940s, most decline since mid-1980s
- Fig 3.2: manufactured goods price index ÷ avg disposable personal
  income (Jan 1947 = 100): from 100 → below 10

### Financial Market Confirmation
- Inflation-adjusted long-term interest rates falling steadily since
  early 1980s (Volcker disinflation). Real borrowing costs below long-term
  growth forecasts, near zero
- Corporate valuations: steadily increased (especially in rich world)
- Credit spreads: exceptionally tight outside financial crisis period
- Private equity: "burdened with trillions of dollars of funding that
  they have not been able to deploy"
- Bond/loan covenants: steadily loosened in favor of borrowers
- "The ability to make productive investments may once have been
  constrained by access to material resources, but that era ended
  decades ago"

### The Savings Paradox
- Standard economics has difficulty with the "great glut" because textbook
  definition of "saving" = "not consuming" — think of Joseph and the
  Pharaoh hoarding grain in seven fat years for seven lean years
- Biggest mistake: thinking higher saving CAUSES additional investment.
  In scarcity conditions, yes. But nothing automatic about it. When
  conditions don't apply, higher saving simply means lower living standards
- Better to think of it IN REVERSE: more investment leads to more saving.
  Worthwhile projects generate additional output → most consumed (lifting
  living standards) + some used for further investment → total savings
  INCREASE even as consumption rises relative to production
- Trying to promote investment through higher saving is often
  counterproductive. Spending less on consumer goods/services → unless
  investment immediately rises to offset → less total production AND
  lower total saving → which discourages new investment

### Cultural Reference/Allegory: "Scroogeville"
- When a society — "call it Scroogeville" — increases its saving rate,
  it consumes less relative to production. Three possible global outcomes:
  1. Investment rate in Scroogeville rises
  2. Investment rate in rest of world rises
  3. Saving rate in rest of world falls
- These equal four scenarios:
  1. Productive investment rises globally (good but LEAST LIKELY in
     today's developed world — not constrained by capital costs)
  2. Wasteful investment increases globally (UNEQUIVOCALLY BAD)
  3. Consumption outside Scroogeville rises (could be good OR dangerous
     depending on how financed)
  4. Production outside Scroogeville falls (UNEQUIVOCALLY BAD)

### Income Distribution Is the Key
- Most people spend close to everything they earn. The rich do not.
  "There is only so much a person can consume, no matter how expensive
  his tastes"
- Give most people an extra dollar → spent buying goods/services. Give
  a rich person an extra dollar → used to accumulate additional assets
- Rising inequality → value of assets contingent on continued spending
  increases by people with progressively lower income shares → only way
  to make this work: rising debt
- Economists Kumhof, Rancière, and Winant: "almost perfect relation
  between rising income concentration in the United States in the 1920s
  and rising U.S. household indebtedness in the years before the Great
  Depression"
- Marriner Eccles (FDR's Fed chairman): "By taking purchasing power out
  of the hands of mass consumers, the savers denied to themselves the
  kind of effective demand for their products that would justify a
  reinvestment of their capital accumulations in new plants"

### Excess Saving Leads to Wasteful Investment
- The saving rate BY ITSELF is meaningless. What matters: amount of
  unconsumed output relative to supply of worthwhile investment
  opportunities
- Saving is excessive when real resources diverted from immediate human
  needs to develop wasteful investments (hard to distinguish "worthwhile"
  from "wasteful" except in retrospect)
- Excess saving necessarily leads to wasteful investment because it
  encourages overbuilding AND because suppressing consumption reduces
  viability of otherwise worthwhile projects
- "Building truck factories or apartment complexes or power plants is
  not 'investing' in any meaningful sense if nobody ends up buying more
  trucks, living in the apartments, or needing the extra electricity.
  It is just waste"

### IMF Data: Global Saving/Investment Stable Since 1980
- World's saving and investment rates have been stable as share of
  global output since 1980. Dramatically higher saving in some places
  (especially China) offset by much lower saving elsewhere
- Implication: globally, no shortage of funding for worthwhile investment
  projects. Had there been a shortage, increased saving would have been
  matched by increased investment. Instead: places that squeezed
  consumption simply forced production to fall elsewhere → significant
  consequences for trade

### China vs. Germany vs. US
- China: investment rate soared since 1980s but NOT by enough to offset
  even larger relative decline in consumption. Until 2008: surge in
  production vs domestic demand → rest of world forced to absorb
  difference. Since 2008: surplus shrank because investment share of
  GDP increased. But consumption share barely rose → saving rate HIGHER
  now than when China had trade surpluses worth 10% of output
- Germany: surplus is function of weak domestic demand. Both consumption
  and investment have grown sluggishly for ~30 years. Production weak too
  but marginally faster than domestic demand. Germany's saving rate and
  investment rate not unusual individually — but the GAP between them
  is almost unprecedented for a country its size
- US: domestic demand weak, but output growth even worse. Foreign saving
  crowded out US domestic production as trade deficit. Spain's massive
  deficit caused by investment boom (no meaningful changes to production
  or consumption) — subsequent shift to surplus caused by investment bust,
  not household saving changes. Greece: investment boom AND bust amplified
  by consumption boom and bust

### Three Explanations for Linked Surpluses and Deficits
1. Changes within China/Germany/surplus countries caused domestic demand
   to fall relative to production → forced rest of world to spend more
   (CORRECT — first option is best explanation)
2. Changes within US/Spain/Greece/deficit countries caused output to fall
   relative to spending → forced rest of world to produce more
3. Hermes (Greek god of commerce) or Lakshmi (Indian counterpart) has
   been "extraordinarily busy spending all of his time managing trade,
   investment, and savings, country by country, with such precision that
   at every single point in time, by an astonishing coincidence, all the
   saving rates and all the investment rates in hundreds of more-or-less
   autonomous entities in the world balance out perfectly" (satirical)

### The Mechanisms of Wealth Transfer
- Chinese hukou (household registration) system deprives hundreds of
  millions of urban workers access to government benefits for which they
  pay tax
- German companies refuse to invest domestically despite rising
  profitability and regular losses on investments abroad
- These choices mechanically suppress consumption and investment relative
  to production → forces rest of world to spend more than they produce

### The Balance of Payments
- Current account = Financial account + Statistical discrepancy
- Current account = Household saving + Corporate profits + Taxes
  − (Household investment + Business investment + Government spending)
- Financial account = Foreigners buying local assets − Locals buying
  foreign assets
- Financial account = Private sector financial account + Change in
  central bank reserves
- "There is no way to save without someone else dis-saving"

### Good vs. Bad Imbalances
- GOOD: 19th-century US importing British capital → boosted investment
  far above what domestic saving alone could achieve, without squeezing
  American workers. Also: South Korea (1948 to Asian Financial Crisis 1997)
  — consistently imported more than exported, transitioned from poor to
  rich. Norway: imported massive amounts in 1970s for offshore oil
  development → repaid obligations → amassed large stock of foreign
  assets from hydrocarbon profits
- BAD: Germany since 1999. Private sector spent €5.1 TRILLION acquiring
  foreign assets. But those assets grew to only €4.8 trillion — a
  valuation loss of 7% across nearly two decades (subprime mortgages,
  Greek sovereign debt)
- Hünnekes, Schularick, and Trebesch (2019): "Germany could have become
  about 2 to 3 trillion Euros richer [between 2009 and 2017] had its
  returns in global markets corresponded to those earned by Norway or
  Canada." Poor returns caused by "remarkable inability to pick the
  right stocks and bonds"
- German domestic assets returned 2.4 percentage points MORE per year
  than foreign assets (1999-2017). Since 2009: gap widened to 5
  percentage points. €1M invested in German assets (1999) → ~€2M by
  2017. Same amount in actual German foreign investments → <€1M
- Germans invested €2.6T more abroad than non-Germans invested in
  Germany (1999-2018). But net foreign asset position grew only €1.9T
  → net loss of 29%. Would have been far better off investing at home

### Pull vs. Push — How to Tell the Difference
- PULL (deficit country pulls in capital): interest rates RISE as
  imbalance grows. Turkey example (2010-2018): current account deficit
  ~6% of domestic income, funded by borrowing. Turkish lira fell by
  half in real terms. Dollar-denominated business loan rates rose from
  ~4% to 6%. Lira-denominated rates soared from 9% to 28%. Eventually
  foreign revulsion slashed spending → forced into current account
  surplus by mid-2019
- PUSH (surplus country pushes capital out): interest rates FALL or
  stay stable in deficit country. Spain example: mid-1990s current
  account nearly balanced, benchmark rates >5% real. From then until
  2008: current account deficit widened to ~10% of GDP. Yet real interest
  rates dropped BELOW 0. Currency couldn't move (euro). Real effective
  exchange rate rose 18% (wages/prices rose faster than neighbors).
  Cost of capital was plunging even as Spain borrowed more and more.
  Spain wasn't pulling money in — overwhelmed by flood of foreign
  investment into its banks, bonds, real estate. House prices more than
  doubled (2001-2007). Once foreigners stopped pushing → Spanish spending
  rapidly contracted below incomes → surplus, but at cost of crushing
  unemployment

### Cultural Reference: Lottery Winners and Capital Inflows
- "Lottery winners often do worse in life than if they had bought a
  losing ticket. The sudden infusion of money acts like a hit of cocaine
  and distorts behavior in similarly unhealthy ways. Something similar
  happens to countries on the receiving end of unsolicited financial
  inflows"
- "Few societies have been able to absorb sudden, large sums of capital
  from abroad without experiencing soaring debt, asset bubbles, and
  economic crises"

### Why It Is Better to Give Than to Receive: The German Empire in the 1870s
- Rich historical case study of how capital inflows harm recipients
- For most of its history: Germany divided into many small states with
  distinct identities, political forms, religious traditions
- Prussia: second-most populous state in post-Napoleonic German
  Confederation. Began realizing nationalists' dream by provoking and
  winning short wars: Denmark (1864, "liberated" Schleswig-Holstein),
  Austria (1866, annexed hostile neighbors + created North German
  Confederation)
- Louis-Napoléon (Napoleon III): "afflicted by gallstones and a weakness
  for mistresses, was prone to strategic errors and presumptuous
  pronouncements." Tried to buy Luxembourg from Netherlands, tried to
  annex Belgium — both violated treaty commitments
- Spanish throne crisis (1868-70): France and Prussia each proposed
  candidates. Drove French to make unreasonable demands → declared war
  on Prussia (July 1870). Within six weeks, Louis-Napoléon captured at
  Battle of Sedan. Third Republic continued war but eventually yielded
- Bismarck's challenge: keep France down long enough for Germany to
  consolidate. Solution: 5 billion gold franc indemnity — equal to
  roughly 1/4 of France's economic output in 1870. Hoped to cripple
  French reconstruction for generations. German armies would occupy
  France's industrial heartland until paid
- Other side: transfer to German economy equal to ~1/5 of its domestic
  production over 3 years

### Counterintuitive Outcome: The Indemnity Hurt Germany
- France raised the money surprisingly easily — French savers had ample
  resources. France shifted to trade surplus, stopped investing abroad,
  freed up income to buy government bonds. French economy struggled
  initially but new debt didn't cripple it
- Charles Kindleberger (financial historian): French indemnity expanded
  the GLOBAL money supply. German money supply grew as gold flowed in,
  but this was NOT offset by equal reduction in France's money supply
  (French gov't bonds became moneylike liquid assets). Transfer resulted
  in sudden, substantial increase in global moneylike assets
- Germany absorbed financial inflow worth ~8% of GDP each year for 3 years.
  Much allocated to military costs (fortifications on French border,
  veterans' pensions) → boosted purchasing power → trade deficit through
  import boom
- German coal miners in Ruhr: hourly pay jumped 60% between 1871-1873 →
  German exports uncompetitive in global markets. Gold flowed back from
  Bank of Prussia to Bank of France. Balance of payments always balances
- Ludwig Bamberger (parliamentarian, Deutsche Bank cofounder) warned:
  hold unspent funds in gold or foreign assets. Advice ignored →
  invested in German state bonds and railroad bonds → "frenzy of
  investment at home and abroad in which a substantial share of the
  inflows from France was wasted"
- Arthur Monroe (economist): government investments "released on the
  German market, within about two years, a sum nearly three times as
  great as the total monetary stock of the country and considerably
  greater than the combined debt of all the German states, including
  debts incurred for railroad building"
- German economy responded like every other economy that receives large
  financial inflows: rapid growth immediately (>6% annually), then
  after 1874, shrank for three straight years. Banking boom in Germany
  and Austria → bust once reparations stopped. German banknotes grew
  >12% annually (1871-1874), then shrank 10% annually until 1878
- By 1876: situation so dire that German manufacturers pushed for
  protective tariffs (adopted 1879) to compensate for trade changes
  wrought by indemnity. Some in France even believed Berlin might send
  the money back

### Navarro's Error: Why Bilateral Flows Obscure Sources of Imbalances
- Franco-German experience: while gold flow pushed Germany into trade
  deficit and France into surplus, this was NOT matched by corresponding
  change in trade balance BETWEEN France and Germany. Germans bought
  imports from all over the world; France exported more globally. The
  bilateral financial flow affected overall trade, but bilateral trade
  impact was insignificant
- KEY LESSON: countries that spend more than they earn are NOT responsible
  for the current account deficits of their trade partners (regardless
  of bilateral data). America's bilateral surplus with Australia doesn't
  explain Australia's overall CA deficit — both countries spend more than
  they earn
- Netherlands and Singapore: despite large bilateral US surpluses
  (explained by corporate tax avoidance + port transshipment), both
  "consistently spend far less than they earn — both in absolute terms
  and relative to their incomes." Among largest contributors to
  America's CA deficit

### Peter Navarro's Flawed Framework
- Trump's trade adviser. Believes bilateral trade deficits matter and
  map onto bilateral financial flows. WSJ column (2017): China's
  bilateral surplus enables it to "buy up America's companies,
  technologies, farmland, food-supply chain — and ultimately [control]
  much of the U.S. defense-industrial base"
- BUT: data shows Americans invested ~$46B INTO China (2015-2019) while
  Chinese sold $380B of US assets → $430B in NET financing went FROM
  the US to China (not the other way). If bilateral financial flows
  mirrored bilateral trade as Navarro believes, US would have had a
  CA SURPLUS with China — instead, bilateral CA deficit >$1.5 TRILLION
- ~2/3 of US financial account surplus came from euro-area residents
  (2015-2019): invested $976B more in US than Americans invested in
  Europe. But bilateral CA deficit with Europe was just $116B. ~$860B
  in European funding went to US, then was spent in China and elsewhere
- Mexico: bilateral CA deficit ~$350B (2015-2019). But Mexico itself
  consistently runs one of world's largest CA deficits (~2% of GDP).
  Mexico's large bilateral trade surplus with US is consequence of
  location next to world's largest consumer market — American, European,
  Japanese manufacturers built factories in Mexico to ship components
  north. Only ~60% of value of US imports from Mexico comes FROM Mexico
- Navarro's proposal to force Mexico to buy more US goods would likely
  INCREASE the overall US CA deficit (redirected Mexican spending = less
  income for third countries = less demand for US exports)
- "Penalizing Mexican exports would also be counterproductive" — would
  make Mexico less attractive to foreign investors, increasing relative
  attractiveness of American manufacturing assets → narrower bilateral
  deficit with Mexico at cost of wider overall American deficit

### Chapter 3 Conclusion
- "The answers have nothing to do with cultures of thrift or profligacy.
  Instead, they have everything to do with the distribution of income
  and the structure of the global monetary system"

## CHAPTER 4: From Tiananmen to the Belt and Road — Understanding China's Surplus

### Overview
- Chinese economy grown at breakneck pace for four decades. Initially
  because Maoism replaced by Deng Xiaoping's moderate reformism (1978).
  After century of war and repression, entrepreneurial energies finally
  allowed to flourish → significant gains in living standards in first
  decade after 1978 reforms
- Also produced difficult political problems → suppressed in exchange
  for new model generating rapid growth at all costs
- Biggest mistake analysts make: conflating nearly four decades since
  Deng into a single, consistent growth model. Much more useful to think
  of FOUR VERY DIFFERENT STAGES (the last just beginning)

### China's Development Model (post-1989)
- Like Britain centuries earlier: workers flowed from countryside to
  cities (new opportunities + local governments confiscated/appropriated
  land). New urban workers systematically underpaid relative to value of
  production → surplus funded physical capital investment
- Like 19th-century Americans: attracted foreign technology/expertise by
  promising high profits + large domestic market access
- Like Japan and South Korea: funneled household savings to preferred
  companies through state-controlled banking system. Discriminatory
  regulations + moral suasion favor domestic "champions" over non-Chinese
- Worked reasonably well for many years (despite environmental damage).
  BUT high-savings model has long outlived its usefulness
- Premier Wen Jiabao (March 2007): "There are structural problems in
  China's economy which cause unsteady, unbalanced, uncoordinated and
  unsustainable development"
- Until financial crisis: costs passed to rest of world through rising
  trade surpluses and massive financial outflows. Mid-2000s: Chinese
  residents (mostly state-affiliated) buying >$700B/year in foreign assets
  → absorbed by US and other rich countries at cost of falling
  manufacturing capacity + rising household indebtedness
- After 2008: collapsing export demand forced choice — let production
  fall (unemployment) or boost domestic demand (investment or consumption)
- Government chose investment: massive infrastructure + housing boom →
  magnified imbalances while shifting them inward. Sustained growth but
  at cost of nearly unprecedented surge in Chinese indebtedness.
  Unproductive investments failed to pay for themselves
- Fig 4.1: China's total debt owed by nonfinancial sector: ~100% of GDP
  (1996) → ~250% of GDP (2018)
- Danger: having reached limits of debt-funded investment growth,
  government may attempt to shift costs back to rest of world through
  trade surpluses and financial outflows
- Only way to prevent: rebalance so household consumption prioritized
  over investment. Means reversing all mechanisms transferring purchasing
  power from workers/retirees to companies/government — reforms "at least
  as dramatic and politically difficult as the reforms implemented by
  Deng Xiaoping beginning in 1978"
- Problem: "vested interests" (Premier Li Keqiang's term) — years of
  state-sponsored income concentration created potent group that will
  fiercely resist reforms shifting spending power back to consumers.
  Can Communist Party reform without losing political monopoly?

### The First Stage: "Reform and Opening Up" (late 1970s-1989)
- End of 1970s: Maoist policies had severely constrained productive
  capacity. Economy contracting even as population swelled. Living
  standards <8% of developed world. Baby boom (1950s-60s) would raise
  working-age share by ~20 percentage points (early 1970s to mid-2000s)
- 1950-1977: real output per person grew avg 2.5%/yr (vs Japan at 7%
  over same period). After 1977: only THREE years below 7% GDP growth
  (1982: 5.2%, 1990: 4.0%, 1991: 3.8%)
- Deng's reforms: relaxed laws preventing unplanned economic activity,
  reduced central planning in favor of local planning, let farmers keep
  surplus after selling minimum quota to state. "Big push" investment
  + expensive military budgets abandoned for "go slow" approach favoring
  domestic consumption
- Credit allocation decentralized: within 3 decades, ~60% of all new
  lending came from local provincial institutions (up from ~0% at start)
- Deng's challenge: reform rigid institutions without undermining
  Communist Party or fomenting opposition. Gradualist approach kept many
  old elites in positions of power → planned economy and nascent market
  interacted in unpredictable ways
- Heavy industry liberalized more slowly than farming/light manufacturing
  → imbalances between rural and urban economies → rapid food price
  inflation in late 1980s → lowered urban living standards
- Barry Naughton (economist): "a sense that the government was failing
  to honor a kind of implicit social compact with urban residents"
- Workers and students demanded greater political rights → Tiananmen
  Square protests → Communist Party violently suppressed prodemocracy
  movement (June 4, 1989)
- Deng's opponents: felt vindicated, attempted to reverse reformist
  program. Economy grew at slowest rate since 1978. Weak growth
  undermined their claims → gave Deng chance to reinvigorate
  liberalization with "harder political edge"
- Deng's famous Southern Tour (1992): organized primarily to overwhelm
  continued elite resistance to reform

### Stage Two: The Chinese Development Model (post-1989)
- After 1989: shifted from merely eliminating growth constraints to
  ACTIVELY implementing policies to generate rapid growth. In absence
  of political reform, Communist Party would command legitimacy through
  presiding over remarkable living standard increases → would also
  generate imbalances (especially the extraordinarily low share of
  national income retained by ordinary households)
- Alexander Gerschenkron ("Economic Backwardness in Historical
  Perspective," 1951): developing countries face two constraints:
  1. Uncertain property rights, noncredible legal systems → can't
     generate enough domestic savings
  2. Private sector fails to direct investment into productive projects
- Gerschenkron's solution: state intervention. Amass resources from
  private sector → build infrastructure and manufacturing. Suppress
  household consumption → investment rises. Workers/retirees taxed to
  subsidize investment directed by central authorities — "a development
  strategy naturally suited to authoritarian regimes that do not need
  to be held accountable to their voters"
- Nearly every investment-driven growth miracle has followed this: USSR
  under Stalin (1930s), Maoist China (Great Leap Forward, 1958-62, failed),
  Brazil (military dictatorship, 1960s-70s, mixed results). Japan is
  only major democracy to have implemented it; South Korea's iteration
  (during military dictatorship) was most successful

### The Transfer Mechanisms (post-1989)
- Fig 4.2: After 1989, household consumption share of GDP plummeted from
  ~50% → ~35% while fixed investment + net exports rose from ~30% → ~50%
- Government began implementing variety of mechanisms to transfer spending
  power from ordinary Chinese to subsidize domestic investment and
  foreign consumption. Rapid growth MASKED the scale of transfers —
  household income grew rapidly in absolute terms even as share of
  economy shrank
- Decline in consumption relative to GDP was substantially larger than
  rise in investment → China generated massive CA surplus reaching ~10%
  of national output by 2007-08

### Mechanism 1: Currency Manipulation
- China had been devaluing yuan against dollar since early 1980s
  (old official price was arbitrarily high). 1994: abandoned gradual
  approach, devalued by a third (5.8 → 8.7 yuan per dollar). By mid-1995:
  hard peg at 8.3 yuan/dollar, rigidly maintained until mid-2005
- Yuan became progressively undervalued as Chinese productivity grew
  much faster than US productivity while exchange rate stayed fixed →
  made Chinese exports increasingly cheap for foreigners AND deprived
  Chinese consumers of ability to buy what their labor had earned
- Transfer: from Chinese consumers → profits of manufacturing operations
  (including joint ventures with American, European, Japanese companies)
- By time China allowed gradual yuan appreciation (2005): CA surplus
  already worth 6% of GDP
- PBOC had to spend trillions of dollars to maintain exchange rate during
  peg years and limit appreciation after 2005. Between 1998-2008: China
  had $1.4T cumulative CA surplus + $500B cumulative financial account
  SURPLUS (foreigners eager to invest in China, but Chinese private sector
  blocked from investing abroad). Balancing term: $1.9T growth in
  PBOC foreign exchange reserves — equivalent transfer of wealth from
  Chinese households to owners of Chinese export manufacturing
- BIS estimate: China's inflation-adjusted exchange rate depreciated
  ~20% between 1998-2007. Yuan didn't consistently surpass 1998 level
  until 2011
- Fig 4.3: Real trade-weighted yuan (Jan 1998 = 100): fell from 100
  to ~83, recovering to 100 only by ~2011

### Mechanism 2: Regulatory Transfers
- Property rights often ignored: local governments seize valuable land
  from households to sell to developers. Expropriations encouraged by
  central government's incentive system for local party officials →
  prioritized reported output growth above all → also encouraged ignoring
  pollution and environmental degradation to attract business investment
- "Ordinary Chinese had their wealth and health taken from them to
  benefit elites"
- Share of Chinese GDP consumed by Chinese households fell by 15
  percentage points between late 1980s and bottom in 2010. As of 2018:
  Chinese households still consume <40% of output — lower ratio than
  every other major economy in the world, by far

### Mechanism 3: Financial Repression (most important, least appreciated)
- Gradual elimination has only PARTIALLY reversed China's domestic
  imbalances. Few ways to save in China other than government-run bank
  deposits. Interest rates on deposits set extraordinarily low (especially
  relative to growth). Lending rates also below market but high enough
  above deposit rates to guarantee bank profit
- Borrowing limited by regulators, steered toward favored borrowers →
  cheap financing for those with political connections at expense of
  ordinary savers/borrowers without connections
- Financial system produced massive, sustained transfer from Chinese
  people to large manufacturers, infrastructure developers, real estate
  developers, and provincial/municipal governments. Transfer worth ~5%
  of Chinese GDP each year between 2000 and start of interest rate
  liberalization (~2013)
- With such cheap capital: entities with privileged loan access embarked
  on massive investment spree with little concern about project quality

### Mechanism 4: "Comparative Advantage of Lower Human Rights" (Qin Hui)
- Chinese historian Qin Hui's phrase. Gives bosses edge in bargaining
  over wages and benefits
- Labor organizing and adversarial unions ILLEGAL in China. Those who try
  to help improve working conditions (lawyers, students) often arrested
  for "threatening the social order"
- Political prisoners: either unpaid or underpaid workers in everything
  from shoemaking to electronics assembly
- Workers at nonfinancial corporations in China: paid only 40% of value
  of what they produce. In most other countries: labor share ~70%

### Mechanism 5: The Hukou System
- Originally Maoist-era system to keep workers on farms. Limits rights
  of Chinese to move and settle outside birthplace
- Local governments stopped enforcing laws preventing rural migrants from
  holding city jobs (good for business) — but migrants remain under
  constant threat of eviction (especially in largest, richest cities)
  → relatively compliant workforce
- Functions as REGRESSIVE TAX: workers pay into national social security
  (health, education, pension) regardless of where they live, but can
  only receive benefits where officially registered. Curtails social
  spending for hundreds of millions of poor Chinese even as their taxes
  support relatively rich local governments
- China's official tax system also regressive: personal income tax
  collects only ~1% of GDP, vs consumption + social security taxes at ~14%.
  Low earners often face HIGHER tax rates than the rich

### Result: Highest Saving Rate in History
- Subsidizing production at expense of workers/savers → China's saving
  rate reached highest level ever recorded in history
- NOT because households were thrifty — but because income shifted from
  higher-consuming households to lower-consuming sectors (corporations,
  government)
- These savings financed massive investment program in infrastructure
  and manufacturing directed by central authorities
- When it works: powerful development model producing rapid growth even
  with substantial hidden taxes/transfers (living standards still soared
  in absolute terms)
- Process transformed relationship between Beijing and country's elite:
  powerful new group emerged that benefited disproportionately from these
  policies → the direct beneficiaries of measures used to subsidize
  investment by constraining household income/consumption → "access to
  extraordinarily cheap credit quickly becoming the most valuable asset
  in China"

### Stage Three: From High Investment to Overinvestment (late 1990s onward)
- China's underlying economic potential continued to expand (rising
  productivity, high investment, continued rural-to-urban migration).
  But actual output grew MUCH MORE than underlying potential
- Until 2008: difference absorbed abroad through rising trade surpluses.
  After 2008: government ramped up domestic investment further (without
  commensurate increase in profitable projects) → sharp increase in
  domestic debt burden
- KEY CONCEPT: In China, GDP growth rate is an INPUT, not an output.
  Set early in the year as target. Local governments generate enough
  economic activity to fill gap between private sector output and target
- Two critical differences from normal economies:
  1. Local governments NOT subject to hard budget constraints — can
     engage in near-unlimited nonproductive economic activity because
     they control most credit creation within banking system
  2. Banks don't have to write down loans to projects that can't service
     debt → allows them to extend as much new credit as needed to hit
     GDP target
- Until mid-1990s: system worked because shortage of infrastructure/
  manufacturing capacity was so large that almost any investment increased
  productivity more than its cost. Best financial system = one that
  expanded most rapidly = LEAST discriminating in project selection
- By late 1990s: years of rapid investment made it increasingly difficult
  to find obviously productive investments. Cheap credit from captive
  household savings subsidized investments that added less value than
  they truly cost → China reached saturation point
- HSBC (2012): China's capital stock per worker just 6% of US level →
  concluded "China needs to invest more, rather than less." But this
  assumes every country should invest to the same per-capita level
  regardless of institutions
- Institutional quality matters more than capital stock level: high
  corruption, vague regulations, arbitrary rule are natural obstacles.
  America's forgiving bankruptcy system fosters entrepreneurship vs
  more hidebound European societies
- "Societies are not poor because they lack buildings, machines, and
  other forms of physical capital. Rather, they usually lack capital
  because they are poor, and they are poor because their institutions
  prevent them from absorbing investment and labor as productively"
- Harry X. Wu (economist): estimates China's underlying productivity
  has NOT improved since early 1990s. Output increased only because
  more machines and workers used to make more stuff → explains why
  Chinese economy unable to grow without relying on either foreign
  demand or rapid increases in domestic indebtedness
- Fig 4.4: China's estimated underlying productivity (1980 = 100):
  rose from 100 to ~155 by late 2000s, then declined to ~130 by 2016

### The Vested Interest Trap
- Financial system remained designed to expand investment as rapidly as
  possible, regardless of whether individual projects economically
  justifiable. Costs so heavily subsidized by hidden transfers from
  households → no mechanism to constrain or identify wasted investment
- Anyone who could plausibly claim to be satisfying central government's
  development objectives got access to cheap credit → as they borrowed
  and built, vested interests became increasingly powerful politically
  and increasingly determined to maintain access to low-cost loans
- "China's politics distorted its economy, which has distorted its
  politics even more"
- Would have been better to revise investment-led growth model as soon
  as widespread misallocation became apparent. But "what typically happens
  is that the vested interests who benefit from that growth model delay
  meaningful adjustments" — same in every historical precedent. Necessary
  adjustment postponed until debt becomes serious problem

### Government Response: Constraining Credit
- Recently: central government has begun grappling with problems by
  constraining credit growth. PBOC's preferred measure: "aggregate
  financing to the real economy." During boom: broad credit grew >20%/yr.
  2016: grew 15%. 2018: grew <10%
- Fixed asset investment growth: slowed from ~26%/yr (2005-11) to 10%
  (2015) to just 6% (2018)
- Real progress, but growth in Chinese debt continues to exceed growth
  in debt capacity. Government still building elaborate subway stations
  in desolate marshlands to sustain spending rather than raising household
  income and creating incentives for small businesses

### Will China's Imbalances Shake the World Again?
- China's annual CA surplus: peaked at ~$420B (2008), contracted to
  ~$190B (first half 2019). Looks like rebalancing. But closer
  examination: fragile and prone to reverse
- China's trade surplus in MANUFACTURED GOODS: already far larger than
  2008, both in absolute terms and relative to rest of world's economic
  output. "The glut of excess Chinese production has only gotten worse
  and the burden imposed on China's trade partners to absorb this glut
  has only gotten bigger"
- Fig 4.5: China's manufactured goods trade surplus relative to rest of
  world's GDP: rose from ~0.4% (mid-1990s) to ~1.5% (2015), still ~1.2%
- Manufacturing exports have become LESS important to Chinese economy
  (30% of GDP in 2007-08, now just 18%) — but China's growing share
  of global output means any change looks different from world's vs
  China's perspective
- China's imports of manufactures SHRANK: from 23% of Chinese GDP (2004)
  to <10% today. Both component imports (domestic suppliers increasingly
  capable) and finished goods imports (9% of GDP in 2004 → <5% today)
- "Made in China 2025" agenda: explicitly aimed at accelerating import
  substitution
- "Stories about low-wage Chinese workers merely assembling advanced
  components made elsewhere are, at best, unrepresentative of the situation"
  — 2/3 of value of advanced manufactured exports came from abroad in
  early 2000s; today most of the value comes from Chinese labor and capital

### China's Incompatibility with Rules-Based Trade
- Since joining WTO (2001): China has scrupulously adhered to WTO
  commitments. BUT "the Chinese economy may be fundamentally incompatible
  with the spirit of any rules-based trading system because the Chinese
  party-state has enormous power to tell companies what to do"
- Communist Party cells embedded in most Chinese companies, even
  subsidiaries of non-Chinese firms. Executives are party members
  (eligible for promotions/favors, vulnerable to discipline)
- Milhaupt and Zheng: identified 95 of top 100 private firms and 8 of
  top 10 Internet firms whose founders/controllers are party-state members
- "Little autonomy from discretionary state intervention in business
  judgment" — "the state exercises significant extra-legal control rights
  over private firms"
- No need for tariffs when executives can simply be told to pick Chinese
  suppliers. Result: "a modernized version of Friedrich List's National
  System adapted for an age when explicit tariffs are considered outmoded"

### Disguised Capital Flight via "Travel Services"
- China's reported imports of travel services: ballooned from $102B
  (2012) to $277B (2018). But exports of travel services stagnated.
  Increase not matched by actual tourism data
- NY Fed (2019): China's balance of payments data overstated true trade
  deficit in travel services by "about $85 billion in 2018"
- Likely explanation: much of "travel" is actually capital flight —
  visiting US to buy life insurance, housing, or exchange high-priced
  jewelry for dollars. Sharp increase in "tourism spending" occurred
  shortly after start of Xi Jinping's anticorruption campaign
- Fig 4.7: Money leaving China (USD billions/yr): total outflows peaked
  at ~$700B (2015-16), combining direct investment abroad, "net errors
  and omissions" (= capital flight), and "tourism"
- Capital controls progressively tightened 2016-17. "Many of the Chinese
  executives who had demonstrated a particularly strong appetite for using
  their companies to borrow domestically to buy foreign assets have since
  been arrested, while others ended up dying under unusual circumstances"

### Belt and Road Initiative
- Best understood as way to manage tradeoffs of internal rebalancing,
  NOT as strategic plan for territory/military bases
- Pre-2008: China dealt with overcapacity by exporting purchasing power
  and goods to Americans/Europeans while accumulating trillions in US/
  European financial assets and financing rising debt in rest of world.
  Became unsustainable when American/European borrowers reached debt
  capacity limits
- Post-2008: adapted by boosting domestic investment at cost of rising
  domestic debt. Also proved unsustainable
- BRI promise: create new demand for Chinese exports of manufactured
  goods and construction services in Southeast Asia, South Asia, Africa,
  Eastern Europe, Latin America. Chinese banks lend to foreign governments
  → those governments hire Chinese firms to build ports, railroads,
  electric grids, coal plants, telecoms
- Problems: Chinese lenders have little incentive for due diligence →
  raft of bad debts by recipient governments. Chinese firms have little
  concern for environmental impact. Political/cultural insensitivities
  create frictions. Total addressable market of BRI countries far smaller
  than North America/Europe — difficult to imagine BRI substituting for
  loss of traditional export markets

### Stage Four: The Spirit of '78
- Beijing's three difficult options: rising debt, rising unemployment,
  or wealth transfers from elites to ordinary households
- Government can continue to prop up growth as long as banks can finance
  additional investment — but there is a point past which rising domestic
  indebtedness can no longer be traded for lower unemployment
- Warning signs already showing in provinces: Wei Chen, Xilu Chen, Chang
  Tai-Hsieh, and Zheng Song (economists, 2019) found officials in
  Liaoning, Inner Mongolia, and Tianjin systematically overstated capital
  investment since 2008. Preferred to LIE about numbers rather than use
  cheap credit to boost spending. "Local leaders in these three provinces
  were recently arrested in corruption crackdowns, and one of the
  official accusations was that these leaders had overstated local GDP"
- Implication: Chinese economy may have rebalanced toward consumption
  MORE than official data imply. But debts probably closer to 300% of
  national income rather than official 250% → quality of past investments
  even worse → closer to reaching debt capacity limit

### Why Reform Today Is Harder Than 1978
- When Deng began: debt levels were low. Eliminating constraints on
  productive activity → increased productivity showed up immediately
  as higher growth
- High debt levels change the impact of productive reforms in three ways:
  1. High debt creates uncertainty about cost distribution (default/
     repayment) → everyone changes behavior to avoid bearing costs:
     rich try to take money out of country, businesses cut investment,
     workers become uncooperative, middle-class savers buy hard assets.
     "Many of these financial distress processes have already started"
  2. In the past, provincial governments hit GDP targets by borrowing
     in excess of real growth capacity. Without cheap borrowing, GDP
     would have grown much slower → growth rates will slow significantly
     once debt stabilizes. Moreover, debt already so high that priority
     should be REDUCING it, not merely stabilizing → what had been growth
     impulse must now move into reverse
  3. Banking system has not recognized economic losses → GDP has been
     substantially overstated by amount of bad loans. Many investments
     fail to support future consumption/production capable of justifying
     cost → reduce long-term growth by adding bad debt. Costs will
     necessarily lower future GDP by amount past GDP was overstated

### What Reforms Are Needed
- Third Plenum (October 2013) provided blueprint. Progress made on:
  interest rate liberalization, environmental protection, health
  insurance, one-child policy
- Household consumption has begun to rise relative to total production
  (but remains far below early 2000s level as share of Chinese economy)
- Next crucial step: transfer substantial wealth/income from elites
  (especially provincial/municipal governments and state-owned entities)
  to households. Means: land reform, hukou reform, tax reform,
  privatization, legalization of unions
- Safe prediction: household income growth will substantially exceed
  GDP growth over next decade or two. Progressive wealth transfers
  could maintain rapid growth of Chinese living standards even as
  investment growth slows to zero or turns negative. Household income/
  consumption might grow 5-6% annually while average GDP growth slows
  to 3-4%
- If opposition to transfers forces government to use credit as substitute
  for reform → risk is hitting debt capacity constraint → growth slows
  much more dramatically, economy might even shrink. US precedent:
  1929-33, total production fell 26%, household consumption dropped 18%.
  Better model: Japan after 1990 (GDP growth slowed to near zero but
  household consumption grew ~2% thanks to steady decline in household
  saving rate)

### Chapter 4 Closing
- "One way or another China will rebalance its economy — all imbalances
  eventually reverse themselves — but the specific path depends on how
  the political system interacts within several competing constraints"
- "The adjustment period that has followed every growth miracle has always
  overturned expectations, especially the most seemingly obvious and
  widely accepted ones, and has always been far more difficult
  economically than even the greatest pessimists had feared. It is
  safe to assume that this will happen again"

## CHAPTER 5: The Fall of the Wall and the Schwarze Null — Understanding Germany's Surplus

### Overview
- By end of 1989: >100 million people liberated from communist regimes.
  Integration into Western Europe's capitalist economy transformed
  German society and politics
- Reunification proved traumatic for many Germans in both East and West.
  Poverty and insecurity rose. Workers at top saw income gains even as
  most others experienced outright wage cuts
- National income shifted from workers to capital owners. Tax cuts for
  high earners, absence of meaningful inheritance tax, weakened social
  benefits → purchasing power shifted toward rich households and businesses
  that spend far less than they earn
- "Despite following a radically different path than China, Germany ended
  up in a surprisingly similar place. Class wars were won by the rich
  at the expense of everyone else"
- Unable to absorb all it produced → surplus had to go somewhere.
  Before 2008: excess savings went to rest of Europe as loans from
  German banks → forced people in Spain, Greece, Italy, elsewhere to
  borrow more than they could reasonably afford. "That turned out badly
  for both the creditors, who have lost hundreds of billions of euros
  on bad assets, and for the debtors, who have since suffered levels
  of unemployment unprecedented in modern European history"
- After 2008: Germany's net financial outflows persisted because policy
  choices reinforced weakness in domestic spending. Most important:
  government's "fanatical opposition to public borrowing" — the
  Schuldenbremse (debt brake). Increasing fiscal rectitude has more
  than offset gradual rebalancing in private sector
- German government's zeal in imposing its economic model on neighbors
  has magnified Germany's surplus into even bigger European surplus

### The End of Communism in Europe
- Revolution began in Poland (1980): workers at Gdansk Shipyard formed
  Solidarity, independent labor union. Communist government imposed
  martial law (1981). Unlike in China, repression failed
- By 1988: waves of strikes and growing support → Polish government
  forced to negotiate. Round Table Agreement (April 5, 1989): legalized
  independent trade unions, scheduled free elections

### Reunification and the Currency Question
- July 1, 1990: Western deutschmark replaced East German mark
- Bundesbank wanted 2:1 exchange rate to prevent inflation and preserve
  East German industry. Kohl insisted on 1:1 for political reasons —
  to discourage mass migration from East ("If the deutschmark doesn't
  come to us, we go to it" was a popular East German slogan)
- Peter Bofinger's defense of 1:1: after accounting for social security
  contributions East Germans wouldn't pay, gap in labor costs at 1:1
  was close to gap in worker productivity. Any other exchange rate
  "would have strongly increased the movement of workers from East
  to West Germany" because living standards in the East would have
  collapsed by more than half

### The Wage Problem
- West German unions pushed aggressive wage hikes in East to prevent
  employers from relocating factories there. Goal: parity by mid-1990s
- East German workers went along — no stake in enterprise profitability,
  assumed Western executives from Treuhandanstalt knew what they were doing
- By 1991: labor costs in East Germany (hourly pay / hourly output)
  had soared >50% above West German levels
- Result: only 8% of East German firms were "viable" after union
- East German manufacturing output collapsed by two-thirds between
  end of 1989 and beginning of 1991
- By 1992: almost half of unified Germany's unemployed were in
  Eastern states (despite <19% of total population)
- Irony: job losses precipitated the very westward migration the
  high-wage policy was meant to prevent — 6% of East German population
  moved West by 1994

### Treuhandanstalt and Privatization
- Initial optimism about East German assets vanished — most worth only
  "their real-estate or scrap value"
- Privatization ended up costing government hundreds of billions of DM
  instead of generating revenue
- By 1995: almost all Treuhandanstalt companies sold or parceled out;
  unrealized losses absorbed by federal government
- About half of East German workers employed by West German/foreign
  businesses; 20% at companies founded after reunification; most of
  rest had lost their jobs

### Social Consequences
- Many Eastern workers became dependent on handouts or forced into
  low-paying jobs. Disproportionately women moved West; men without
  advanced schooling left behind, resentful
- West German living standards ~8% lower by early 2000s than they
  would have been without reunification
- Despite costs, reunification largely succeeded: East German living
  standards almost completely converged with West (life expectancy,
  manufacturing output doubled since early 1990s, lower living costs
  attracted artists/others to Eastern cities)

### The Sick Man of Europe
- West German economy grew 5.7%/year from beginning of 1989 to start
  of 1991. Then: unified Germany grew only 1.3%/year through end of 1997
  — worst sustained slowdown since the 1950s
- Causes: austere policy stance. Inflation rose from 1.5% (mid-1980s)
  to ~5% (early 1990s). Bundesbank raised discount rate from 2.5%
  (end of 1987) to almost 9% (mid-1992) — highest since 1948
- Maastricht Treaty (1992): government cut infrastructure spending by
  nearly 20% between 1992 and 1998
- Construction activity fell relentlessly for >decade after 1994 peak
- Consumption grew ~2%/year above inflation only because household
  saving rate dropped
- Fig 5.1: German construction activity plunged from 1991 to ~2006

### Eastern European Competition
- Liberation of Eastern Europe: 100 million new customers BUT also
  tens of millions of low-cost workers fluent in German
- Typical German manufacturing worker earned ~9x typical Slovakian
  manufacturing worker (year 2000)
- German companies moved jobs and production to Central/Eastern Europe
- EU accession process began end of 1997; most joined by 2004,
  Bulgaria/Romania by 2007
- German car companies: production in Germany barely grew (4.9M → 5.1M
  units), but total production doubled to 10M+ by moving plants abroad.
  By 2017: 16.5M vehicles, 10.8M made outside Germany. ~50% of German
  motor vehicle output value from imported components

### Wage Suppression
- Threat of relocation gave employers powerful tool: if unions pushed
  for higher pay, businesses would simply move a few hundred miles east
- Median manufacturing wage in former West Germany grew just 5% total
  (inflation-adjusted) between 1991 and 2000
- Union membership plunged by a third in the 1990s
- "Opening clauses" let local union reps negotiate bespoke company-level
  deals, watering down industrywide collective bargains
- Unions' priority: preserve jobs at cost of forgoing pay raises
- Between 1993-1997: German manufacturing employment fell 15%
- Unemployment rose from ~5% (1991) to nearly 10% (end of 1997)
- Three million full-time jobs vanished; only one million part-time added
- Real value of money spent on pay and benefits essentially flat
  between 1995 and 2011

### Roman Herzog's Speech (April 26, 1997)
- Germany in danger of being left behind in "a great, global race"
- Germans "ask too much of the government" and "subscribe to the myth
  that its resources are inexhaustible"
- Called for "a new social contract for the future" — cut social
  entitlements, deregulation, lower taxes, lower wages
- "The world is on the move; it will not wait for Germany"
- Surprise: the most left-wing government in Federal Republic history
  would implement his program

### Schröder's SPD Government (1998)
- SPD's best performance since 1972; center-left coalition won >51%
  of Bundestag seats
- Schröder had promised he didn't deserve a second term if unemployment
  still >3.5 million by next election (2002)
- Signature policy: massive tax cuts. Top income tax: 53% → 42%.
  Corporate profit tax: ~52% → ~39%. Eliminated capital gains tax on
  corporate cross-holdings (previously taxed at 53%)
- These mainly benefited the rich; insufficient to stimulate domestic
  spending or investment

### The Neuer Markt Bubble
- Frankfurt's Deutsche Börse launched Neuer Markt in 1997 — Germany's
  answer to Nasdaq
- During bubble: outperformed Nasdaq-100 by 4x. At peak (March 2000):
  companies worth nearly €234 billion
- By 2002: companies had lost 95% of value. Exchange shut down Sept 2002
- Scandals: insider trading, stock-price manipulation, falsified earnings
- German corporate debt rose 25% between 1997 and 2001
- Current account deficit ballooned from 2% to 7% of GDP by 2000
- After bubble burst: investment collapsed. Building activity plunged
  23% between mid-2000 and trough in 2006. Business investment net of
  depreciation fell >60%. Two million full-time jobs vanished

### Euro Area Constraints
- Germany joined euro area January 1, 1999 → lost independent monetary
  policy. ECB set rates for euro area as a whole
- ECB president Wim Duisenberg, asked about pressure for looser policy
  (April 2001): "I hear, but I do not listen"
- Maastricht Treaty + Stability and Growth Pact (1997): limited
  government borrowing space. Irony: German negotiators had demanded
  those rigid fiscal rules
- Real domestic demand fell ~3% from end-2000 to trough in 2004.
  Spending on consumption and investment didn't return to previous
  level until end of 2006
- Result: massive current account shift. Through 1990s: household
  surpluses (~4% GDP) offset by corporate and government deficits
  (~2.5% GDP each). By 2004: current account surplus ~5% of GDP
- Fig 5.2: Domestic production vs domestic demand diverged sharply
  after 2000

### Export Competitiveness Myth
- Germany's share of world exports was SAME in 2004 as in 1998
- Germany's share of intra-European trade rose <1 percentage point
  in 2000s (Czechia, Netherlands, Poland rose by comparable amounts)
- Real German export growth SLOWED: 9%/year (1994-2000) → 4%/year
  (2001-2004)
- Surplus explained by even greater slowdown in imports — Germans
  spent fewer euros on imports in 2004 than in 2000 despite inflation
  and rising oil prices

### Agenda 2010 and the Hartz Reforms
- Schröder launched "Agenda 2010" speech to Bundestag on March 14, 2003
  (eve of Iraq War). Linked domestic reform to foreign policy independence
- Core idea: "Individual responsibility" would trump collective provision;
  "nobody will be allowed to live off the community"
- Hartz IV (named after Peter Hartz, VW HR chief and Schröder's friend):
  unemployment insurance would stop after 12 months for under-55s
  (previously 32 months). Rejecting a job offer for low pay → lose welfare.
  Retirement ages would rise
- Deeply unpopular. SPD internal revolt led by young Andrea Nahles
  (later party leader in 2018). Mass protests summer 2004
- Despite opposition, most Germans supported the changes — but this
  majority was naturally center-right, not SPD's base

### Political Fallout
- East Germans who'd flocked to SPD in 2002 felt betrayed
- Oskar Lafontaine (Schröder's 1998 finance minister) quit SPD to lead
  new western left-wing party (WASG) allied with eastern PDS
- WASG/PDS became Die Linke (The Left) — vowed to repeal Hartz IV,
  "which legally decreed poverty"
- Peter Hartz himself forced to leave VW after sex/bribery scandals
- May 2005: SPD lost North Rhine-Westphalia (ruled continuously since
  1966). Schröder engineered early election for September 2005
- Die Linke won 9% of seats from both blocs. Result: grand coalition
  CDU/CSU + SPD under Chancellor Angela Merkel. Grand coalition ensured
  survival of Agenda 2010 and Hartz IV

### Impact of Hartz IV
- Meager wage growth and underinvestment were consequences of 1990s
  elite choices, not welfare cuts per se
- But Hartz IV reinforced weakness: "moved the system from one that
  protected living standards indefinitely to temporary protection of
  living standards followed by much lower income with strict conditions"
  (economist Christian Odendahl)
- Workers at risk of poverty: 5% (2005) → 10% (2015), driven by shift
  to low-paying part-time work
- Since mid-1990s: more than ALL net increase in German employment
  came from self-employed and part-time workers
- Full-time employment fell for a decade, remains lower than 1995
- Nearly 30% of all German jobs now part-time (~double the early 1990s)
- Germans aged 55-64 with jobs: <40% (1990s) → >70% (now) — many of
  these would previously have taken early retirement
- Fig 5.3: Employment rose but hours worked FELL — the "employment
  miracle" was really a part-time shift
- Share of Germans unable to meet unexpected expense: 25% (2005) →
  41% (one year after Hartz IV). As of 2017: >30%
- Marcel Fratzscher (German Institute for Economic Research): Agenda
  2010's impact was more psychological "turning point" than any specific
  policy — "reflected in the actions of employers, employer associations,
  and labor unions"
- Workers accepted real wage cuts. Hourly pay and benefits fell ~5%
  between 2001 and 2007 (inflation-adjusted)
- Average German household income slightly lower in 2013 than in 1999

### Verteilungskampf and Germany's Excess Saving
- Capital share of net value added in nonfinancial businesses: ~25%
  (mid-1990s) → 36% (2007). Labor share declined ~12 percentage points
- Bundesbank: companies boosted profitability by "wage moderation,"
  reducing "non-core payments," and shifting "production activities
  requiring a low-skilled workforce to foreign countries with a more
  favorable (wage) cost structure"
- ~2/3 of total increase in Germany's national income (2000-2007)
  came from capital income growth, not rising employee compensation
- Fig 5.4: Net operating surplus as % of net value added rose from
  ~22% (1991) to peak ~36% (2007)
- German companies got away with wage cuts because they could sell
  to foreigners — avoid moribund domestic market
- Corporate sector: average current account deficit ~2% GDP (1991-1999)
  → surplus >2% GDP consistently since early 2000s

### Wealth Inequality
- Average German ~50% richer than average Italian, 2x average Spaniard
- BUT median German household far POORER than median Spanish household;
  only about as wealthy as median Greek or Polish household
- Low-income Germans have less net wealth than low-income Estonians
  and Hungarians. Many have no assets or owe debts exceeding assets
- Only 10% of German households directly own listed company shares;
  only 13% own mutual funds
- 90% of all German businesses (>50% of corporate cash flow) are
  family-owned, passed generation to generation, mostly exempt from
  inheritance tax as long as jobs preserved for 7 years after handover
- Effective inheritance tax: ~1% on inheritances >€10M vs ~14% on
  inheritances €100K-€200K
- Net wealth tax abolished in 1997 (constitutional court ruling 1995
  that it treated residential property differently from other assets)
- Abolishing wealth tax raised effective return on saving for the rich →
  encouraged them to save more, spend less
- Property taxes based on assessments from 1964 (West) or 1935 (East),
  not current market values — favors wealthy property owners
- Only 44% of German households own their main residence — one of
  lowest shares in rich world. Median homeowner with mortgage has 14x
  net wealth of median renter; without mortgage, 26x
- A third of German homeowners own multiple rental properties → rising
  rents systematically transfer income from poor to rich (unlike in
  other countries)
- Union membership dropped another 25% from 2000 to 2010.
  Workers covered by collective bargaining: >80% (mid-1990s) → <45% today
- "Income concentration at the top decile in Germany today is even
  greater than during the industrialization period of 1871-1913"
  (Fig 5.5: share of national income going to top 10%)

### How Income Concentration Depressed Spending
- Bundesbank (2007): >25% of total increase in personal saving rate
  since 2000 explained by income redistribution alone — "The
  distribution of income has shifted in favor of sections of the
  population that tend to save more"
- Poor Germans save nothing (need every euro); rich Germans save >40%
  of income
- Two additional Bundesbank explanations for "exceptional" and "very
  unusual" weakness in household consumption:
  1. Workers' income stagnation ("decisive countermeasures" by companies
     against "competition from emerging economies") convinced Germans
     they needed to save more for the future
  2. Germans saving more because "households have become more acutely
     aware of the strains on the public social security systems" —
     compensating for anticipated pension cuts ("permanent corrections
     to current pension entitlements" = massive anticipated wealth loss)

### How Germany's Domestic Weakness Contributed to the Euro Crisis
- Had Germany been a CLOSED economy: moribund investment, tight budgets,
  falling wages would have forced down domestic spending AND limited
  corporate profits. Keynes's "paradox of thrift" — no trade surplus,
  no impact on other countries
- But Germany was open: >25% of value generated by workers/capital
  sent abroad before 2008, mostly to European neighbors
- Profits rose as costs (wages) held steady and export revenues rose
  with global growth. Germany's increasingly unequal income distribution
  transferred purchasing power from German workers to consumers in
  the rest of the world
- IMF July 2019 Country Report on Germany: "almost perfect relation
  between changes in the share of income going to the wealthiest
  households" and changes in current account balance

### German Banks as the Transmission Mechanism
- 2002-2008: German residents spent €702 billion less than they earned
  (cumulative current account surplus)
- German residents bought €2.7 trillion in foreign assets; foreigners
  bought €2.0 trillion in German assets. Direct investment = just 15%
  of cross-border flows and 22% of overall surplus
- Germany's precrisis surplus recycled abroad by Germany's BANKS:
  of €2.7 trillion in financial outflows, >€1.6 trillion attributable
  to banks buying foreign bonds and making foreign loans
- Banks raised ~€900 billion from foreigners (Jan 2002 - Sep 2008)
  for net outflow of €739 billion. Banks alone = >100% of net
  financial outflows from Germany
- Big internationally focused banks (Deutsche, Dresdner, Commerzbank)
  accounted for just 31% of growth in foreign lending
- STATE-OWNED Landesbanken played much larger role, especially after
  losing government guarantees on debt in 2005 — without state support,
  loans to safe domestic borrowers at low rates no longer worthwhile.
  "Woefully unprepared" to invest abroad, they searched for opportunities.
  Between Jan 2005 and Oct 2008: Landesbanken = 46% of total growth
  in German bank lending to foreigners
- German bank lending concentrated in Ireland, Italy, and Spain —
  consistently the largest lenders to what became Europe's crisis
  countries

### The European Borrowing Boom
- Spanish banks: owed €300B to rest of world (2002) → €800B (mid-2008)
- Other Spanish businesses and households: €160B → €650B
- In 6 years: >€1 trillion flowed into Spanish private-sector credit
- Spain's net obligations: €250B (2002) → €900B (eve of crisis)
- Spanish consumption + investment grew ~30% more than output (2001-2007).
  Trade deficit widened from 2% to 6% of GDP. Not a competitiveness
  issue — Spanish exports grew in lockstep with GDP
- Italy: gross external debt nearly doubled (€1T → €1.9T)
- Portugal: external debt doubled (€160B → €340B)
- Greece: debts tripled (€100B → €330B)
- Ireland: most extreme — residents quadrupled debts to foreigners
  (€450B → €1.8T) thanks to "absurdly oversized banks"
- Total: euro area crisis countries took on ~€4 trillion in additional
  foreign debt between 2001 and 2008
- Debt binge funded some worthwhile projects (Spain's high-speed rail,
  Athens subway) but much wasted on boondoggles (Don Quijote Airport
  built for 10M passengers/year despite being >2 hours from Madrid;
  Irish countryside blighted by abandoned golf courses)

### The Crisis Response: "Pedagogical Imperialism"
- Spending booms were "almost inevitable consequences of the financial
  inflows coming into the crisis countries from the surplus countries,
  particularly Germany" — nothing to do with culture, weather, or
  Catholic vs Protestant differences
- Crisis countries lacked standard tools: couldn't raise interest
  rates during boom (euro), couldn't depreciate currency or do central
  bank bond-buying during bust
- Consensus German view: crisis caused by profligacy of borrowers.
  Recklessness of German lenders and structural inequities of German
  economy not considered significant
- Der Spiegel characterized German approach as "pedagogical imperialism"
  — rest of Europe should follow Hartz IV welfare cuts and Germany's
  constitutional commitment to fiscal restraint

### The Schuldenbremse (Debt Brake)
- Old rule (Article 115, Basic Law, 1969-2010): government borrowing
  limited to investment spending, with allowances for "disturbances"
- New rule: constitutional limit on borrowing at ALL levels of
  government — Federation, Länder, municipalities, off-budget entities,
  social security funds — must balance budgets on average
- Much more stringent: no exceptions for infrastructure investment,
  no using asset sale revenues for spending
- Only fiscal flexibility: allow automatic stabilizers to operate
  (but can't EXPAND them). Emergency exceptions require special
  Bundestag vote + "binding amortization plan"
- IMF estimated German budget deficit was consistently 2-3% of national
  output (1993-2010) after accounting for business cycle → debt brake
  would have required several percentage points of GDP in higher taxes
  or lower spending EVERY YEAR
- Finance ministry (2009): government would have borrowed just €55B
  (2000-2008) under debt brake vs actual €240B excess of spending
  over tax receipts. No analysis of impact on households or businesses

### Infrastructure Decay
- Since 1998: government capital spending has NOT been enough to
  maintain existing infrastructure. Net spending (after depreciation)
  consistently NEGATIVE
- Municipalities alone have €160B investment backlog. About half of
  roads and bridges need repair
- Leverkusen Bridge (crosses Rhine between Cologne and Düsseldorf):
  closed to trucks in 2012, degraded so much it needs total rebuilding.
  New bridge won't open until 2020+
- A40 Bridge near Duisburg: repeatedly shut to truckers for safety
- Germany has notoriously slow internet: <3% have broadband ≥100 Mbps;
  only 1% have fiber — comparable to Italy, far worse than Portugal,
  Spain, Eastern Europe. Mobile broadband: equivalent to Turkey,
  far below Poland or Spain

### The Schwarze Null (Black Zero)
- January 13, 2015: finance ministry announced "no new borrowing was
  required" in all of 2014. Finance Minister Wolfgang Schäuble's
  prize: the schwarze Null
- Budget moved further into surplus since then, even as infrastructure
  continues to deteriorate. Surplus now ~2% of GDP
- 2011-2015: government borrowed €142 billion LESS than allowed under
  its own debt brake rules
- Schäuble's obsession with black zero was "overwhelming logic" —
  staffers dressed in black, posed in big circle to wish him farewell
  after 2017 retirement
- Saving rationale (preparing for aging/declining population) doesn't
  justify current policy: repaying debt at average real interest rate
  far below zero only makes sense if expected return on investment
  in German economy were "deeply negative"

### Exporting the Model: Fiscal Compact
- May 2010: Europeans led by Germany committed to resolving euro crisis
  with "fiscal consolidation"
- By 2012: entire euro area accepted Treaty on Stability, Coordination
  and Governance (Fiscal Compact) — effectively imposed Germany's debt
  brake on all of Europe. Governments must run balanced budgets or
  surpluses "in almost all circumstances." Must seek approval from
  rest of Europe "on their public debt issuance plans." Failure →
  sanctions

### Europe Becomes Like Germany
- Pre-crisis: euro area aggregate current account ≈ zero (surpluses
  in Germany/Netherlands offset by deficits in Spain/Greece)
- 2008 crisis forced large changes in spending. Euro area private
  sector surplus ~5 percentage points of GDP higher than pre-2008.
  Initially offset by government fiscal deficits (governments borrowed
  when households/businesses wouldn't)
- 2011-12: ECB failed to support governments under speculative attack →
  sovereign crisis → embrace of fiscal austerity. Government deficits
  forced to close → rest of world must absorb European surplus
- Crisis countries shifted from deficit of ~6.5% of GDP to surplus of
  ~2% of GDP (2008-2016). Real domestic spending plunged: ~11% in
  Spain, ~9% in Italy/Portugal, ~14% in Cyprus, ~13% in Slovenia.
  Greece: domestic consumption and investment shrunk by A THIRD.
  As of 2019: Italy real consumption + investment still 8% below
  2008; Greece remains in depression
- No rebalancing within Europe: surplus countries (Germany, Netherlands)
  did NOT increase spending. Instead, rest of world (Africa, Middle
  East, India, Indonesia, Latin America, UK, US) forced to absorb
  European financial outflows through rising trade deficits
- Euro area as a whole now has massive external surplus: ~4% of GDP
- Countries that adopted Germany's model experienced rising inequality,
  declining purchasing power. VAT rates rose while corporate income
  taxes and wealth taxes fell. "Wealthy Italians and Spanish lost
  very little (perhaps even increased their income) while lower
  earning Italians and Spaniards have lost a great deal" (Zsolt Darvas)
- Europe remains committed to "misguided fetishes of competitiveness
  and balanced budgets." Governments would rather keep domestic demand
  permanently suppressed than risk ending up like Greece
- Fig 5.7: Birth of the "euroglut" — crisis countries shifted from
  deficit to surplus, Germany/Netherlands surplus persisted, entire
  euro area went to ~4% surplus
- Euro area too big to foist its internal distortions on others without
  creating larger global imbalances. "The United States will have to
  reprise its role as the ultimate source of global demand"

## CHAPTER 6: The American Exception — The Exorbitant Burden and the Persistent Deficit

### Overview
- US has been world's "indispensable spender" for decades — countries
  that save too much and spend too little have been accommodated by
  Americans who borrow to spend more than they earn
- American experience shares much with Germany: rising inequality,
  income shift from labor to capital, investment collapse after tech
  bust, jobs relocated abroad (Mexico/China vs Eastern Europe),
  Clinton welfare reforms + Bush tax cuts parallel Schröder/Hartz
- Yet Germany became world's largest SURPLUS country while US remains
  world's largest DEFICIT country. Why?
- Answer: specific features of US financial system — flexibility, size,
  concern for foreign investor rights. US is issuer of world's premier
  safe asset. Dollar is plentiful, easy to trade, free of default risk.
  US economy is large, diversified, and open

### The Exorbitant Burden
- Since late 1990s: excess savings enter US primarily when foreign
  governments buy US government financial assets — acquiring reserves
  at expense of domestic spending (transfer from consumers to export
  industry owners)
- Purchases motivated by desire to AVOID RISK, not earn high returns,
  no matter how low the yield
- These "uneconomic" capital flows were as large as America's ENTIRE
  current account deficit between end of 2001 and end of 2014
  (Fig 6.1: cumulative transactions since Jan 1995)
- Since 2014: Europeans have been biggest buyers of US assets
- Profit-seeking savers would NOT have enabled this — US was not an
  especially profitable investment destination. Indiscriminate inflows
  inflated American purchasing power, forced current account deficit
  wider. Imports displaced American productive capacity, debt
  substituted for lost income
- US financial system RESPONDED to the foreign money by creating new
  assets, lowering lending standards, inflating asset prices, keeping
  dollar elevated even as deficit ballooned

### The Mystery of America's Missing Surplus
- US SHOULD have been running surpluses based on domestic fundamentals:
  - Top 10% share of national income: 30% → 40% (after taxes/transfers)
  - Ultra-high earners (top 1%) drove most of the increase
  - Bottom half: essentially NO income growth since late 1970s
    (after taxes, inflation, government benefits)
  - Wealth held by richest 1%: 22% → 42%; almost all increase
    attributable to richest 0.1%
  - ~70% of all earnings from assets now go to richest 1%
    (up from 35% in late 1970s)
- Highest earners save ~40% of income → aggregate saving SHOULD rise
- Instead, household saving rate FELL: ~10% (late 1970s) → 5% (late
  1990s) → barely 3% (eve of financial crisis) because vast majority
  ended up saving even LESS. Post-crisis: reverted to 6-7%

### Policy Parallels with Germany
- Dramatic reductions in top tax rates gave high earners motive to
  push for more pay. Changes to regulatory treatment of stock buybacks
  and LBOs enabled outsized executive/financier incomes
- Effective capital gains tax fell >10 percentage points (mid-1990s
  to mid-2000s)
- Regressive payroll taxes effectively replaced corporate profit taxes
  (early 1950s to end of 1980s)
- Welfare cut in 1990s (similar to Hartz IV). Private-sector
  unionization collapsed. Jobs relocated abroad

### Corporate Investment Bust (Parallel to Germany)
- After two decades flat, real capital spending by US companies grew
  20%/year between 1994 and 2000. Durable goods manufacturing capacity
  expanded 10%/year (1995-2000) vs 3%/year (1967-1994)
- Then: corporate bankruptcies + ~40% decline in share prices.
  Net business investment fell >50% between 2000 and 2003
- American business sector current account: swung from −5% of GDP
  to surplus >1% of GDP. Companies chose to invest less, control
  costs, hoard profits
- Since 2000: durable goods manufacturing capacity grew just 1%/year;
  overall US manufacturing capacity grew just 10% total in ~20 years
- Employee compensation as share of corporate net value added fell
  9 percentage points (2001-2007). Investor share rose 6 points
- Between end of 2000 and mid-2006: just 37% of increase in corporate
  net value went to workers (slightly less than went to investors)
- Post-crisis: business investment spending in 2017 still 2% LOWER
  than in 2000 (after inflation and depreciation). Except for shale
  producers and a few tech startups, most US companies generate far
  more cash than they need for R&D and investment
- Nonfinancial businesses paid nearly 18% of net value to creditors
  and shareholders (2010-14) — investors did better than at any point
  since 1920s

### Is Fiscal Policy to Blame?
- Bipartisan consensus: US current account deficit caused by profligate
  government spending. Skidelsky (2005), Shultz & Feldstein (2017),
  Jason Furman, even Stiglitz blame fiscal deficits
- Authors argue this is WRONG: government budget balance almost
  perfectly mirrors private sector behavior. Tax hikes → private sector
  saves less; spending cuts → same. National saving rate stays roughly
  constant regardless of fiscal policy
- Japan example: personal saving rate near zero, government deficit
  ~6% of GDP for decades, yet MASSIVE current account surpluses.
  Because corporate margins are high and Japanese companies extremely
  reluctant to invest domestically — corporate surplus outweighs
  government + household deficits

### Historical Evidence (Fiscal Policy Doesn't Drive Current Account)
- Fig 6.2: US fiscal policy does NOT drive current account balance
- 1983-1985: current account went from balanced to −3% GDP. But budget
  deficit actually SHRANK (8% → 6.5% GDP). Private sector surplus
  plunged from 8% to 3.5%
- 1987: fiscal deficit shrank to 5% GDP, offset by private surplus
  declining below 2%
- 1992: recession → fiscal deficit 7.5% GDP, but current account
  deficit <1% because private sector surplus returned to ~7% GDP
- Mid-1990s: fiscal deficit shrank to 4% (smallest since 1970s), but
  current account deficit GREW to 1.5% because private surplus collapsed
  to 2.5%
- Late 1990s: government budget reached balance for first time since
  1950s. Current account deficit EXPANDED to 4% GDP because private
  sector transformed surplus into 4% deficit (corporate investment binge)
- 2000-2003: government surplus → 7% deficit. But private sector swung
  from 4% deficit to 2.5% surplus → current account barely changed
  (4% → 4.5% of GDP)
- 2003-2006: current account deficit grew to >6% GDP. Government
  deficit shrank to 3.5% because private sector switched from 2.5%
  surplus to 2.5% deficit
- 2006-2009: government deficit exploded by ~10 percentage points.
  But private sector swung by 13 points → current account deficit
  actually SHRANK to <3% of GDP
- Post-2017 tax cuts: government deficit widened to ~7% GDP. Impact
  on current account "negligible" because private surplus rose
  commensurately to 4.5% of GDP

### The Real Explanation: Reserve Assets and the Dollar
- Choices of US households, businesses, government are NOT ENOUGH to
  explain persistent deficits. Whenever one sector cuts spending,
  another steps in. Better to think of current account as the
  INDEPENDENT variable — financial inflows from abroad determine
  the deficit, and US sectors adjust in response
- Key difference between America and Germany: robust foreign appetite
  for US assets, explained by dollar's special role as world's
  reserve asset

### Understanding Reserve Assets
- Money hierarchy: reserve assets at top (unquestionable "money-ness").
  Creditors accept lower forms when optimistic, demand higher forms
  when nervous
- Gold was ultimate reserve asset for centuries: hard to destroy,
  supply changes little, value independent of creditworthiness
- Banks issued notes redeemable for gold → profited by issuing more
  notes than gold they held → vulnerable to runs when too many tried
  to redeem at once
- Central banks emerged as lenders of last resort: could lend gold
  to solvent-but-illiquid private banks, print their own notes backed
  by gold. Credibility of gold pegs shifted from private lenders to
  national governments
- By 19th century: Bank of England obligations became reserve assets
  "almost as good as gold." Gold standard had effectively become paper
  standard
- Pre-WWI: Bank of England held only 3.4% of world's gold, <5% of
  notes backed by gold. Yet governments held British pounds as reserves.
  Pound sterling was dominant reserve currency (Japan held almost all
  reserves as UK government/bank financial claims)

### Interwar Gold Standard Failure
- WWI: all major powers except US suspended gold convertibility
  (printing money for war). Genoa Conference (1922): agreed to
  restore gold standard
- Gold standard was SUPPOSED to make persistent imbalances impossible:
  surplus countries receive gold → credit expands → prices rise →
  exports become expensive → surplus self-corrects. Deficit countries
  lose gold → credit tightens → prices fall → exports become cheap
  → deficit self-corrects
- Restored standard failed because France and US GAMED IT: both
  sterilized gold inflows (prevented gold from expanding domestic
  money supply). France reset franc much lower than prewar → suppressed
  imports. US dollar effectively depreciated relative to Europeans.
  Both ran massive surpluses, hoarding gold
- Deficit countries (UK, Germany) honored gold commitments at expense
  of domestic economy. By Great Depression: unsustainable
- UK left gold September 1931. US broke dollar link in 1933. France
  waited until 1936. Germany kept peg to gold until defeat by Allies
  — at cost of death penalty for paying foreign debts in hard currency.
  Nazi regime resorted to barter trade (advanced manufactures for
  raw materials, often from Soviet Union)

### Bretton Woods and the Dollar Standard
- July 1944: 720 delegates from 44 Allied countries at Bretton Woods
- Keynes (representing Britain) proposed "international clearing bank"
  using "bancor" — penalized BOTH surplus and deficit countries equally.
  Excess bancor balances would be confiscated. Goal: symmetrical
  adjustment, balanced trade
- US VETOED Keynes's plan. Americans wanted dollar as currency of
  international trade and finance, with fixed exchange rates. No
  mechanism to distribute adjustment burden — deficit countries must
  cut spending; surplus countries face NO pressure to adjust.
  IMF would offer temporary emergency loans to defend dollar pegs
- At the time, this seemed good for US: didn't want to be penalized
  for surpluses, expected postwar demand for US productive capacity
  to rebuild Europe/Asia
- System encouraged central banks to hold US government obligations
  as reserve assets (replacing gold). For US Federal Reserve: easy —
  reserve asset WAS the dollar, could create as many as needed. For
  all others: tension between international and domestic priorities,
  resolved by accumulating dollar-denominated asset stockpiles
- Valéry Giscard d'Estaing labeled this America's "exorbitant privilege"
- Europeans accepted because they had protection: dollars convertible
  to gold at $35/ounce

### "Our Currency, but Your Problem" (The Triffin Dilemma)
- Robert Triffin (Belgian-American economist, Harvard): by late 1950s,
  warned Bretton Woods was inherently unstable
- Dollar obligations growing faster than gold supply. US government
  was like a bank issuing more notes than gold it held — always
  vulnerable to a run. Testimony to Congress (October 1959): "We have
  been lending long—and even given funds away—while borrowing short
  and losing gold"
- Throughout 1950s: US had current account surplus ~1% of GDP, but
  financial outflows exceeded inflows. Balance of payments balanced
  only because Americans sold claims on their gold reserves
- By end of 1950s: foreigners began converting dollar reserves to
  physical gold. US gold reserves fell ~15% between end of 1957
  and end of 1959
- Triffin's dilemma: unsustainable model of global finance OR
  perpetually strangled credit
- Kennedy's candidacy made it worse (promised social spending, tax
  cuts, military rearmament). Gold reserves fell another 9% in 1960.
  London gold price spiked to $40/oz (Oct 1960) vs official $35

### Measures to Preserve Bretton Woods
- Fed sent reps to Bank for International Settlements (Basel) to
  convince Europeans to keep reserves in dollars
- London Gold Pool: US + 7 allies fixed gold at $35/oz
- IMF given new powers to lend gold to US
- Fed established currency swap lines with foreign central banks
- Treasury issued "Roosa bonds" (denominated in foreign currencies
  like DM) to discourage Europeans from calling in gold
- Congress passed "interest equalization tax" to discourage Americans
  from investing abroad
- Foreign aid recipients told to spend dollars on American exports
- These measures bought another decade. But total: US lost nearly
  10,000 metric tons of gold reserves (just over half of total
  holdings) between end of 1957 and March 1968. France + UK got
  about half; rest to Austria, Belgium, Netherlands, Spain, Switzerland

### End of Bretton Woods
- March 1968: Gold Pool collapsed, couldn't maintain $35. Two-tiered
  market: central banks at $35, everyone else at market price
  ($43/oz by 1969)
- August 1971: gold at $40, Bundesbank let DM float, threats to
  American gold holdings, rising unemployment, deteriorating trade
  balance → Nixon ended convertibility of dollars into gold.
  Bretton Woods was over
- System could have lasted longer if foreigners hadn't converted to
  gold. Problem was inflation: US consumer prices rose ~40% between
  1958 and end of 1970 while official gold price stayed at $35

### Post-Bretton Woods: "Our Currency, but Your Problem"
- Treasury secretary John Connally to European counterparts: the dollar
  was "our currency, but your problem"
- De Gaulle complained Americans could fight wars in Southeast Asia
  and fight poverty at home without raising taxes or cutting spending
- German leaders blamed American profligacy for accelerating price
  increases in late 1960s; responded rationally by replacing dollars
  with gold until they brought down the system
- Had US done better job keeping inflation under control (perhaps by
  tolerating higher unemployment), Europeans might never have broken
  Bretton Woods. World might have gradually embraced global dollar
  standard with Fed setting monetary policy for entire planet
- Instead: monetary anarchy. Exchange rates swung wildly in 1970s-80s.
  Europeans hated this so much they deliberately re-created fixed
  exchange rate system → eventually the euro (a Gaullist rebuke to
  American economists who pushed floating currencies)
- Yet Europeans exacerbated the flaws: sold exports to booming US
  market, depended on American military spending. European welfare
  states might not have been affordable without strong external demand
  and depressed defense budgets
- Foreign demand for US assets only GREW after 1971, increasingly
  distorting both global and American economies

### Excess Savings, Mercantilism, and Bretton Woods II
- Most nations rejected free-floating currencies. Many pegged to dollar
  or DM to stabilize inflation and encourage foreign investment/exports
- Most international trade in manufactured goods denominated in dollars
  even when transactions don't involve the US → "Bretton Woods II"
- Governments with pegged currencies lacked reserves to defend their
  pegs — money buying safe assets was money NOT spent on roads and
  hospitals. Systematic mismatch between reserve supply and money
  linked to those assets (just like pre-WWI gold standard)
- George Soros: made greatest trades betting on collapse of fragile
  exchange rate regimes; described self-reinforcing process as
  "reflexivity"

### The Asian Financial Crisis (1997-98)
- Indonesia, South Korea, Malaysia, Philippines, Thailand had attracted
  large financial inflows. All growing rapidly, maintained stable
  dollar pegs. Banks and businesses borrowed extensively in foreign
  currencies to bid up domestic assets (short-term debt)
- Small changes in sentiment → self-reinforcing cascade of falling
  asset prices, currency depreciation, rising real interest rates
- Indonesia hit hardest: rupiah lost 80% of value (Jan 1997 - Jul 1998).
  Real output per person fell >14%. Chaos → collapse of Suharto regime
  (ruled since 1967) and independence of East Timor (occupied since 1975).
  Average living standards didn't return to pre-crisis peak until mid-2000s
- Thailand and Malaysia: real living standards fell 12% and 10%
  respectively. Korea and Philippines fared relatively better
- IMF conditions: often counterproductive. Government spending cuts
  and tax increases didn't restore confidence but did crimp spending.
  Raising interest rates made locals MORE desperate to get money out.
  IMF later admitted "programs were initially less successful than hoped"
- Notorious example: IMF demanded Indonesia end clove monopoly in
  cigarette manufacturing — had nothing to do with financial crisis
- Result: generation of policymakers vowed never to repeat it →
  unprecedented scale of foreign reserve accumulation

### Post-Asian Crisis Reserve Accumulation
- Pre-crisis: governments owned ~$970 billion in dollar-denominated
  reserves (large chunk held by Europeans and Japan)
- By mid-2008: $5.2 trillion (mostly held by governments of poor
  countries). By beginning of 2019: ~$8 trillion
- Necessary tradeoff: reduction in domestic spending. Central banks
  and sovereign wealth funds bought foreign assets with purchasing
  power that would otherwise have been used for imports → governments
  increased wealth at expense of ordinary households
- "Regardless of intent": combination of large surpluses, state-sponsored
  purchases of foreign assets, and managed exchange rates = economically
  equivalent to mercantilism via currency manipulation

### China: The Most Consequential Practitioner
- Pre-Asian crisis: China's SAFE owned ~$100 billion in reserves
  (country at Honduras's level of development). Capital account
  mostly closed
- Many within Chinese government had planned to gradually liberalize
  and let yuan float. Indonesia's collapse changed everything —
  Chinese leaders horrified that seemingly stable authoritarian regime
  fell because private sector ran into debt trouble
- Solution: maintain exchange rate peg, prevent yuan from appreciating.
  Cheap yuan transferred income from consumers/workers to government
  and export-oriented companies, discouraged domestic spending,
  minimized reliance on foreign financing
- Between 1996 and eve of global financial crisis: SAFE spent $1.8
  trillion accumulating foreign reserves (~2/3 invested in US).
  After crisis: another $2 trillion to prevent further appreciation

### Oil Exporters
- Outside East Asia: major reserve buyers were commodity exporters
  (Arabian Peninsula, Norway, Russia)
- China's rapid industrialization was windfall: oil prices rose from
  $20-30/barrel (1999-2003) to $75 (2006) to >$130 (summer 2008)
- Combined current account surplus of energy exporters: ~$90 billion
  (2002) → >$600 billion (2008). Averaged >$500 billion/year at
  $110/barrel (2011 to mid-2014)

### Dollar Appreciation and Trade Distortion
- These unsolicited state-sponsored inflows explain why US current
  account deficit expanded while dollar APPRECIATED and real interest
  rates FELL — opposite of what would happen if Americans were actively
  trying to attract foreign money
- Late 1990s: current account deficit 1.5% of GDP, real Treasury
  yield ~4%. By 2005: deficit 6% of GDP, real yield <2%, dollar
  ~15% MORE valuable than mid-1990s
- Dollar appreciated >20% against trading partners' currencies between
  start of 1997 (eve of Asian crisis) and beginning of 2002. Even
  after declining from then until 2008, consistently remained far
  above 1988-96 average

### The Safe Asset Shortage
- Foreign central banks and reserve managers spent ~$4.1 trillion
  buying dollar assets between start of 1998 and mid-2008 — distorted
  US economy and sowed seeds of financial crisis
- Two linked problems: (1) extra demand for dollar assets had to be
  matched by additional supply — Americans had to create >$4 trillion
  in safe financial obligations; (2) governments accumulated reserves
  by suppressing domestic spending → exacerbated global glut of
  manufactured goods → someone had to absorb excess production to
  prevent global depression → dollar's preeminence meant Americans
  absorbed both excess capital AND excess manufactured goods
- Consequences: housing debt bubble + displaced manufacturing base
- Rather than an exorbitant PRIVILEGE, the dollar's international
  status imposed an exorbitant BURDEN
- Best response (in retrospect): federal government should have
  borrowed as much as necessary to accommodate inflows, spent the
  money supporting US manufactured goods, infrastructure, reversing
  income concentration, reducing poverty. No housing bubble, debt
  borne by entity best able to bear it

### What Actually Happened: The Housing Bubble
- Federal government debt available for purchase rose just $1.3 trillion
  (start of 1998 to mid-2008). Foreign investors bought >$1.4 trillion
  of it — more than all new Treasury bonds issued
- NOT nearly enough to satiate foreign demand for safe US assets
- Reserve managers turned to Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac debt (GSEs).
  Government "agency securities" outstanding grew >$5 trillion (1998-2008),
  mostly issued by Fannie and Freddie. Foreign investors bought ~$1.5
  trillion. US banks, insurers, pension funds bought more.
  Remaining ~$1.2 trillion of foreign reserve demand went to bank
  deposits and "occasionally more exotic assets"
- Even without Asian/Middle Eastern reserve managers hoovering up
  Treasuries and GSE debt: fundamental shortage of safe assets.
  Pension funds and life insurers worldwide needed more fixed income.
  US corporate debt grew just $200 billion (2002-2007)
- Wall Street saying: "When the ducks are quacking, feed them."
  Between 2001 and mid-2007: financiers produced ~$2.5 trillion of
  private-label MBS (based on nonconforming mortgage loans).
  Plus ~$650 billion in structured finance products derived from MBS.
  Value has since shrunk by $2.8 trillion
- Fig 6.3: Residential mortgages held in private-label conduits peaked
  at ~$2.5 trillion in 2007, now back to late-1990s levels

### More Credit Supply Leads to Lower Credit Standards
- Voracious demand for low-risk US fixed income + government's failure
  to issue enough debt → massive expansion of mortgage credit supply
- Problem: most people who could afford a mortgage already had one
- Bankers' solution: lower standards until anyone could get one.
  Credit histories ignored, documents falsified. Biggest increases
  in debt driven by people with worst credit scores in poorest
  neighborhoods — also responsible for overwhelming majority of
  eventual losses
- Down payment requirements collapsed: by peak (2006), typical
  private-label mortgage had down payment worth just 3% of appraised
  value. Some originators offered liens to COVER the down payment
  or lent more than home's value
- Rising house prices reinforced declining standards (and vice versa):
  most rapid price increases in zip codes with worst credit scores
  and highest poverty rates

### Houses as ATMs
- Greenspan and Fed economist James Kennedy (2005 study): Americans
  boosted disposable income by 2-3%/year in 1990s by tapping home
  wealth (mostly from capital gains on sales)
- 2004 to mid-2006: home equity withdrawals boosted disposable income
  by ~10% (~$1 trillion/year). Total extracted (2002-2007): $4.7
  trillion in wealth from homes
- Housing wealth rose <$2 trillion while home values increased ~$7
  trillion (difference = debt)
- Many Americans had negative saving rates in 2000s — from their
  perspective, rising home prices were doing the saving for them
- Fig 6.4: home equity withdrawal relative to disposable income peaked
  ~10% in 2005-2006, crashed to near zero by mid-2008
- By mid-2008: Americans stopped extracting. Debt repayment became
  priority. Spending cuts → job losses → more defaults → falling
  prices → tightening standards → "self-reinforcing processes on the
  way up become even more self-reinforcing on the way down"

### No Boom Despite the Bubble
- Despite mortgage debt explosion, America did NOT experience an
  economic boom (unlike Greece, Ireland, Spain)
- Inequality, anemic corporate investment, relatively tight fiscal
  policy all dampened domestic demand
- Private fixed investment below 2000 peak until 2014. No consumption
  boom either: real per-person household spending grew slower in
  2000-2006 than in 1947-2000
- Private sector employment fell 3% (2000-2003), never grew enough
  to keep pace with expanding population. Fed worried about deflation
- Current account and trade deficits expanded as domestic production
  grew even less than anemic domestic demand

### Manufacturing Devastation
- US manufacturing production rose just 10% between peak in mid-2000
  and mid-2006 (when current account deficit peaked). But distorted
  by semiconductor productivity estimates — exclude semiconductors
  (<4% of manufacturing workers): output fell ~6% (2000-2003), barely
  recovered by end of 2006
- Most categories (motor vehicles, machinery, metal products, food,
  plastics, furniture) flat. Textiles and apparel shrank ~30%
- American DEMAND for manufactured goods did grow, but essentially all
  growth satisfied by foreign production
- >80% of private-sector employment decline (2000-2003) directly
  attributable to manufacturing sector. Concentrated in places most
  exposed to competition from countries where workers paid far less
  (especially China)
- Nothing inherently wrong with US manufacturers opening operations
  in China — problem was that Chinese workers couldn't consume
  additional US imports, breaking link between rising trade and
  rising living standards
- Age-adjusted share of Americans with a job never surpassed 2000 peak.
  Drop particularly severe for those without college degrees
- Disability payments helped displaced workers make ends meet but
  couldn't replace lost income. Impact magnified throughout affected
  communities: men without jobs couldn't find women to marry, driven
  to drinking, drugs, suicide. Crime rates slightly increased in
  places most exposed to import competition even as crime fell
  nationally
- Fed study (2017): "The displacement of domestic production by imports
  fueled demand for credit to substitute for lost wages"
- Dollar overvaluation savaged America's terms of trade: dollar
  appreciated >20% (1997-2002), remained far above 1988-96 average
  even after declining. Fig 6.5: trade-weighted dollar index

### The Exorbitant Burden Since 2008
- Financial crisis should have led to rebalancing: US shifting from
  deficit to surplus through falling imports and rising exports.
  Domestic production would have increased faster than demand, debts
  repaid, other countries would bear burden of sustaining global spending
- Instead: US current account deficit persisted at 2-3% of GDP.
  Cannot be explained by excessive spending — American domestic demand
  has been EXCEPTIONALLY WEAK since crisis
- Personal consumption per person >12% lower than pre-1998 trend
- Business investment finally higher than 2000 peak, but home building
  at levels of early 1990s recession
- Government investment spending less than half of mid-2000s level
- Share of working-age Americans with a job: roughly at 2007 level,
  significantly below 2000
- Fig 6.6: real household consumption per person diverged dramatically
  from 1947-1998 trend after the crisis
- Manufacturing output and capacity BOTH lower than 2008 peak as of
  end of 2018. Manufacturing employment still down ~10% from 2006
- Trade deficit in manufactured goods (excluding refined petroleum):
  >4% of GDP — highest since 19th century
- Overall current account deficit kept in check only by transformation
  of US oil industry (shale) and rising software exports
- Persistence can ONLY be explained by excessive saving abroad and
  US role in absorbing those savings. "It is not Americans who have
  decided to borrow too much" — as long as there are Americans willing
  to borrow, the financial system will find them and lower rates/
  standards until loan targets are met

### How Financial Inflows Force US Savings Down
- Net inflows → dollar more expensive → household purchasing power
  rises at expense of export revenues/incomes → less saving through
  combination of higher consumption and lower production
- Cheap imports displace workers → unemployed have NEGATIVE saving
  rate (still consume with no income) → mechanically lowers national
  saving rate
- Lower employment → additional government borrowing for fiscal
  transfers → consumption rises, savings decline
- Fed tries to reduce unemployment → lower interest rates, looser
  credit → encourages borrowing
- Foreign inflows + Fed response → asset bubbles (real estate, stocks)
  → people feel richer, spend more out of current income

### Post-2014 Shift: Europeans Replace Reserve Managers
- Until 2014: reserve managers (China, oil exporters) were main funders
  of US current account deficit
- 2009 to mid-2014: Americans consistently bought more assets abroad
  than private investors bought in US. Without foreign government
  purchases, US would have had cumulative current account SURPLUS
  of ~$1 trillion (~1.2% of GDP/year). Reserve managers buying at
  annual rate equivalent to ~3.6% of US GDP
- After mid-2014: reserve managers no longer the main funders.
  China's reserves fell (offset by rich households/businesses investing
  abroad). Oil producers ran down reserves as prices collapsed.
  But Asian governments (Japan, South Korea, Singapore, Taiwan,
  Thailand) continued accumulating through government-backed pensions
  and life insurance
- Brad Setser (Council on Foreign Relations): cumulative state-sponsored
  purchases of US financial assets roughly zero since start of 2014
- NEW source: Europeans. Euro area members suppressed domestic demand
  to satisfy balanced budget ideology. ECB's asset purchase program
  bought trillions of euros of European bonds → just 15% of German
  government bonds held by private investors (beginning of 2018)
- European savers bought ~€1.5 trillion in foreign bonds (mid-2014
  to end of 2018), including ~$800 billion in US bonds. Net flow from
  euro area = roughly equivalent to total US current account deficit
  over that period
- Unreciprocated: contributed to ~20% rise in real value of dollar
  (mid-2014 to beginning of 2016). US exports of capital goods and
  motor vehicles fell; imports up ~20%
- "It is as if the 1960s have returned"

### Keynes's Revenge
- Conventional wisdom that it's GOOD to be reserve currency issuer
  is "a misconception grounded in psychology rather than economics"
- For >60 years, US has satiated savers in rest of world at expense
  of own workers. Dollar's primacy has been an exorbitant BURDEN,
  not privilege
- Keynes understood this: UK was <10% of world economy after WWI
  yet committed to unreasonably strong exchange rate in 1920s to
  protect value of sterling assets held by savers in India and
  Commonwealth. British exports ~25% lower than in 1913, artificially
  cheap imports displaced domestic production, nearly a decade of
  extremely high unemployment. "Issuing one of the world's major
  reserve currencies was no privilege for them"
- 1930-32: British public voted to place domestic concerns above
  international responsibilities. Abandoned gold, devalued, lowered
  interest rates, imposed tariffs → quickly closed living standards gap
- Similarly, US made a choice to tie international financial system
  to dollar after WWII. At first not a burden (US was ~half of
  global output). By 1971: untenable, Nixon broke link to gold.
  But from 1990s: rest of world decided dollar was international
  reserve asset regardless of formal commitments → financial inflows
  accommodated by US political/financial system "with disastrous
  consequences for Americans"
- When foreign savers buy US assets: current account deficit rises
  through combination of lower income from deindustrialization and
  higher spending on imports. Unless US government absorbs inflows
  by issuing debt, private sector must absorb by selling assets,
  issuing equity, or borrowing — "there is no other plausible outcome"
- Keynes's bancor would have imposed symmetrical discipline on surplus
  AND deficit countries. He lost the argument at Bretton Woods, but
  his analysis remains sound. "It is time to try again"

## CONCLUSION: To End the Trade Wars, End the Class Wars

### Core Restatement
- Trade war is NOT a conflict between countries. It is a conflict
  between bankers/owners of financial assets on one side and ordinary
  households on the other — between the very rich and everyone else
- Rising inequality has produced gluts of manufactured goods, job
  loss, and rising indebtedness — "an economic and financial perversion
  of what global integration was supposed to achieve"
- US has been largest single victim: absorbing the rest of the world's
  excess output and savings at cost of deindustrialization and
  financial crises = America's "exorbitant burden"
- All peoples suffer: US financial system and consumer market function
  as "safety valve for exploitation elsewhere." Rich in Europe,
  China, and other surplus economies can squeeze workers and retirees
  confident they can sell wares, earn profits, park savings in safe
  assets

### The Colonial Analogy
- US, UK, Canada, Australia — all of which absorb surplus production
  in exchange for taking on debt — "therefore resemble the imperial
  colonies of Europe of the late nineteenth century"
- Back then: subject peoples forced to buy Europe's excess production.
  Today: similar situation except the modern regime depends on
  English-speaking countries' POLITICAL commitment to open markets.
  "This is a choice, but in democracies, the people have the option
  to change their mind"

### Political Consequences
- 2016 election: all major US presidential candidates disavowed TPP.
  Bernie Sanders: would "throw American workers out on the street."
  Even Larry Summers (former Treasury secretary): "more globalization"
  was unnecessary
- Trump pulled out of TPP, imposed tariffs on everything from Korean
  washing machines to nearly all Chinese imports. Customs revenue
  doubled (end 2017 to mid-2019). Many leading Democrats also support
  China tariffs
- But tariffs are ineffective at best, harmful under certain conditions.
  Capital controls becoming more popular: New Zealand banned nonresidents
  from buying residential property; Australia limits foreign buyers;
  Canadian local governments taxing foreign housing purchasers
- July 31, 2019: bipartisan bill introduced directing Federal Reserve
  to force current account deficit to zero via "market access charge"
  on foreign investment

### Why Americans Have Tolerated It
- When Bretton Woods was constructed, US economy was about equal in
  size to entire rest of world. Today: <25% of global output.
  Rest of world 3x bigger relative to US
- America has far less capacity to absorb world's savings imbalances.
  As US share shrinks, burden rises. System will break down "as a
  matter just of arithmetic"
- Why tolerated so long: America's own class wars. Plenty of Americans
  have prospered producing financial assets to accommodate excess
  savings. World's preference for American markets and dollar inflates
  incomes of financiers who control access to those markets — as well
  as their political clout
- US Treasury's approach to international finance driven by what made
  sense for major commercial and investment banks. Interests of
  everyone else "largely ignored, if not outright opposed by
  counterproductive commitments to maintain a strong dollar"
- Justified on grounds that deregulating capital and increasing its
  mobility → best investment outcomes → wealth trickles down.
  In reality: international capital flows driven by speculation,
  investment fads, capital flight, and reserve accumulation, not sober
  investment decisions
- American companies adapted by relocating production to countries
  where workers are underpaid, selling goods back to US consumers at
  higher margins
- US officials willing to sacrifice domestic industry for geopolitical
  reasons during Cold War — repeatedly negotiated trade agreements
  Commerce Department officials privately argued were disadvantageous
- Paul Volcker: "The top dog pays the price"

### Globalization and Inequality Reinforce Each Other
- World's rich benefit at expense of workers because interests of
  American financiers are complementary to interests of Chinese and
  German industrialists. Both complement interests of wealthiest
  throughout world, even from poorest countries
- "The modern surplus countries do not need colonies to absorb their
  excess production because they can work with bankers, their willing
  collaborators in the deficit countries"
- Businesses use international competition as excuse to push for
  lower wages, weaker regulations, preferential tax regimes.
  "Squeezing ordinary households has, apparently, been much easier
  than increasing productivity, investing in infrastructure, and
  improving health and education"
- Unsustainable: depressing wages → lower consumption + higher
  indebtedness, both self-limiting. "It is not just a coincidence
  that throughout modern history, high levels of income inequality
  have coincided with soaring levels of debt"
- Demand for goods and services has become world's scarcest resource.
  US plays role of "swing producer." "Competitiveness" has become
  euphemism for pushing wages down → "beggar thy neighbor" → global
  spending shortage → trade wars are "an almost inevitable consequence
  of globalization as it has been practiced"

### What Should America Do?
- Cannot unilaterally address inequality, raise living standards, AND
  stabilize current account deficit without restricting foreign
  investment. Reducing inequality → higher consumption + investment →
  but some of increased spending absorbed by foreign producers →
  current account deficit actually increases
- Short term: shift burden from private sector to federal government.
  Government should absorb unwanted financial inflows. Issue Treasury
  debt as needed to accommodate foreign savers. Lower payroll taxes,
  larger standard deductions, better social safety net (especially
  health) → necessary budget deficits + ameliorate unequal income
  distribution
- Even better: government absorbs foreign flows by DIRECTLY investing
  in infrastructure (public transit, green energy). Many years of
  austerity → large backlog of worthwhile projects. Infrastructure
  investment would generate debt-servicing capacity exceeding cost →
  no higher overall debt burden (debt rises, but GDP rises more)
- Federal spending could sustain demand for American manufactures
  even while domestic market swamped by gluts from abroad. Defense
  procurement easiest approach, but other measures too
- Accommodate legitimate desire of foreign governments for crisis
  self-insurance WITHOUT having them accumulate dollar reserves.
  Make it easier to borrow dollars from Fed: permanent institutional
  swap lines (not just temporary ones) with wider field of eligible
  borrowers → reduce foreign reserve demand for US assets

### What Should the Surplus Countries Do?
- "German policymakers often insist that Germany's surpluses are the
  reward for superior production techniques, this is total nonsense.
  The reward a country receives for superior productivity is higher
  imports through improving terms of trade"
- Persistent surpluses are "almost always the consequence of highly
  unbalanced distributions of income in favor of businesses and the
  rich"

### China Prescriptions
1. Reform and eventually eliminate hukou system
2. Expand social safety net, guarantee retirement income security
   including health care
3. Make it easier for workers to organize and negotiate
4. State-owned enterprises should pay higher dividends, distributed
   to households through dedicated social wealth fund
5. Tighter environmental regulations
6. Reform tax system: lower burden on poor/middle, raise on highest
7. Continue propping up yuan value (including by selling foreign
   reserves) to shift purchasing power from exporters to consumers
- All except last were listed in Third Plenum reforms (October 2013),
  even supported by former central bank governor. But ferociously
  opposed by powerful vested interests. As long as China can dump
  excess savings in US, tempting to postpone

### Europe Prescriptions
- Euro area now world's biggest source of global imbalances
- Pre-2008: crisis countries' borrowing offset German stagnation.
  Post-crisis: governments forced to retrench → higher taxes, more
  unemployment, higher poverty, worse infrastructure, lower living
  standards. Interest rates near zero on even longest maturities
- Goals: reduce overall private surplus AND expand aggregate budget
  deficit
- Europe's large private surplus is function of rising inequality →
  reversible through straightforward policies: higher taxes on high
  earners, lower social security taxes, lower VAT, stronger safety
  nets, higher minimum wages
- Germany specifically: reform inheritance tax (discourage concentration
  in family-owned businesses), update property tax to reflect current
  values. Could shift from 2% surplus to 4% deficit by lowering taxes,
  improving income security, investing in roads, bridges, high-speed
  rail, broadband, green energy
- Netherlands: help struggling households escape massive housing debt
  by lowering income and value-added taxes, loosening bankruptcy laws
- Individual country efforts not enough — Europe's balance has shifted
  since 2012 because of changes in crisis countries (Greece, Italy,
  Spain), which based on economic conditions should be running the
  biggest budget deficits
- Most practical solution: FEDERALIZE European fiscal policy.
  National governments spend less, tax less, borrow less (honoring
  treaty commitments). European Investment Bank becomes main
  infrastructure funder across borders. Common deposit insurance and
  bank resolution (savings at Greek banks = savings at German banks).
  New central euro area treasury: takes over unemployment reinsurance,
  retirement security, backstops EIB, issues debt as desirable as
  US Treasuries, levies common taxes (crack down on corporate profit
  shifting, target net wealth of richest euro area residents)

### If Surplus Countries Follow These Prescriptions
- Living standards would rise worldwide. Indebtedness would fall.
  Rising consumption would encourage business investment. Transferring
  income to ordinary households → more consumption AND more investment.
  "Redistribution, in this case, would lead to higher output"
- Open system preserved. Trade conflicts would disappear as each
  country's internal class conflicts were peacefully resolved
- "This is our preferred outcome. The alternatives are far worse."
  At minimum: US refusal to continue absorbing global imbalances.
  Without new rules: global trade becomes unstable, increasingly
  contentious, potentially violent — resembling anarchy of trade
  from 1600s through first half of 20th century
- "What we are suggesting may seem difficult, but it has been done
  before. At Bretton Woods, the Allies created a new system of rules
  for the global economy and international finance. At home, governments
  strengthened their social democracy by guaranteeing basic living
  standards and improving the security of workers and retirees...
  Their solutions were imperfect—that is why we are where we are—
  but they were based on the values of egalitarianism, global
  cooperation, and peace. The peoples of the world deserve a
  comparable response to the challenges of today."
