# Trade Wars Are Class Wars — Summary
## Matthew C. Klein and Michael Pettis (2020)

### Core Thesis
Trade wars between nations are not what they appear. They are the international manifestation of class wars *within* nations. When elites in China, Germany, and other surplus countries systematically transfer income from workers to themselves and the companies they control, they create economies that produce more than they can consume. The resulting gluts of savings and manufactured goods must go somewhere — and that somewhere has overwhelmingly been the United States, which absorbs the world's excess production and savings at the cost of deindustrialization, debt bubbles, and financial crises.

### The Mechanism
Rising inequality within countries → workers can't afford to buy what they produce → excess savings and production must be exported → surplus countries run persistent trade surpluses → deficit countries (especially the US) are forced to absorb the excess through some combination of higher borrowing, lower production, and financial bubbles. This is not a zero-sum conflict between nations — it is a conflict between the rich and everyone else that plays out on a global stage.

### Three Case Studies

**China (Ch 4):** The Chinese government systematically transfers income from households to the state and export-oriented businesses through financial repression (below-market interest rates on savings), the hukou system (denying migrant workers social services), suppression of labor organizing, cheap loans to developers, and an undervalued currency. The result: Chinese workers retain far less of what they produce than workers in comparable economies, Chinese consumption is artificially depressed, and the surplus floods global markets.

**Germany (Ch 5):** After reunification, Germany underwent a brutal transformation. The 1:1 currency conversion, aggressive Eastern wage hikes, Treuhandanstalt privatization failures, and the "sick man of Europe" period led to the Hartz Reforms and Agenda 2010 under Schröder. The result was a massive shift of income from labor to capital, collapsing union coverage (80% → <45%), stagnant real wages for over a decade, and a constitutional "debt brake" (Schuldenbremse) that starved public investment. Germany's surplus is not the reward for competitiveness — it is the consequence of domestic weakness. German banks recycled the surplus abroad, inflating debt bubbles in Spain, Ireland, Greece, and Italy. After the euro crisis, Germany's "pedagogical imperialism" imposed the same model on all of Europe, turning the entire euro area into a massive surplus bloc (~4% of GDP).

**United States (Ch 6):** America shares many of Germany's domestic pathologies — rising inequality, corporate investment bust, jobs relocated abroad, welfare cuts. Yet the US runs persistent deficits, not surpluses. The difference: the dollar's role as the world's reserve asset. Foreign governments and savers pour trillions into US financial assets not for returns but for safety, inflating American purchasing power, suppressing borrowing costs, displacing manufacturing, and ultimately creating the conditions for the housing bubble and financial crisis. What the French called America's "exorbitant privilege" is actually an "exorbitant burden."

### Key Arguments
- **Fiscal policy doesn't drive the US current account.** Decades of data show that when the government tightens, the private sector loosens (and vice versa). The current account deficit is determined by foreign capital inflows, not domestic fiscal decisions.
- **The gold standard, Bretton Woods, and the dollar system** all suffered from the same fundamental problem: no mechanism to force surplus countries to adjust. Keynes's "bancor" proposal would have imposed symmetrical discipline. The US vetoed it. The consequences have been playing out ever since.
- **The Asian Financial Crisis of 1997-98** traumatized a generation of policymakers into hoarding dollar reserves — creating the massive capital flows that inflated the US housing bubble.
- **"Competitiveness" is a euphemism** for pushing wages down. The fetish of competitiveness has generated a global spending shortage, making demand the world's scarcest resource and the US its "swing producer."

### Prescriptions
- **US:** Shift burden of absorbing foreign inflows from private sector to federal government. Issue Treasury debt as needed. Invest in infrastructure. Lower payroll taxes, expand safety net. Permanently expand Fed swap lines to reduce foreign reserve demand.
- **China:** Reform hukou, expand social safety net, allow labor organizing, SOE dividends to households via social wealth fund, prop up yuan to shift purchasing power to consumers.
- **Europe:** Federalize fiscal policy. Higher taxes on the rich, lower VAT, reform German inheritance and property taxes, invest in infrastructure, create common deposit insurance and a central euro area treasury.
- **The alternative:** If surplus countries don't reform, the US will eventually refuse to absorb global imbalances. Without new rules, global trade becomes unstable and potentially violent — resembling the anarchy of the 1600s through 1940s.

### Bottom Line
"Peoples who fundamentally share common interests are being set against each other because the ultrarich have been successfully waging a class war against everyone else." The solution is not tariffs or trade deals — it is ending the class wars within each country that produce the imbalances in the first place.
