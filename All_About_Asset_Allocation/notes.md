## FOREWORD (William Bernstein) — pp. 8-9
- Alfred Cowles III, heir to Chicago Tribune fortune, was badly hurt by 1929 crash. Asked: how could the nation's best analysts be so uniformly wrong?
- Cowles founded the Econometric Society with foremost economists; along with Benjamin Graham, began collecting/analyzing financial data rigorously. "Took investing away from astrologers and charlatans and gave it to astronomers and physicists."
- Bernstein's warning: when you pick up a financial magazine, watch CNBC, or call your broker, you've traveled back to the pre-1929 era — "the financial equivalent of betting the farm on the daily horoscope."
- Key insight: the object of the game is NOT to get rich — it's to not get poor. Concentration maximizes both chance of riches AND chance of cat-food-cuisine retirement.
- Bernstein praises Ferri for providing both blueprints (which asset classes, how much) AND construction materials (which funds, from whom). But no book can give you the nerve to stick to the plan when "30 stories up in the naked girders in a howling wind."

## INTRODUCTION — pp. 11-19
- Opening questions: "Is asset allocation dead?" "Has the past decade changed how I should invest?" Answers: No. Perhaps. Yes.
- 2000s decade shocks: tech bubble deflation → 9/11 → two wars → housing collapse → financial crisis. All forced investors to question Modern Portfolio Theory (MPT).
- Ferri's diagnosis: people seek certainty, which makes them prey for charlatans (gold bugs, China real estate bulls, Madoff-style fraudsters). "Everyone selling a product has an answer to volatility… and most are selling products you'd best avoid."
- Core philosophy: the future is unknowable, so no single sector can be counted on. Spread risk across many asset classes. Keep costs low. Control what you can control.
- Four lessons: (1) Allocate across multiple asset classes to reduce risk. (2) Invest broadly within each class to eliminate single-security risk. (3) Keep costs low including taxes. (4) Rebalance periodically.
- Three types of asset allocation:
  1. **Strategic asset allocation** — buy, hold, and rebalance. No market predictions needed. This is the book's focus.
  2. **Tactical asset allocation** — active changes based on short-term forecasts (earnings, rates, economic outlook, technicals).
  3. **Market timing** — all-in or all-out decisions on asset classes.
- Ferri on tactical/timing: "just about every unbiased academic study ever conducted shows this type of advice is no better than flipping a coin."
- Total risk concept: portfolio risk is not just market risk. Job security, family health, divorce, debt — all affect how you react to portfolio drawdowns. A person who sold stocks at the 2009 bottom during a divorce/family crisis was invested above her real risk tolerance, not her stated one.
- Rebalancing is counterintuitive: sell what went up, buy what went down. Only investors who rebalanced religiously in early 2009 captured the recovery gains by year-end.

## CHAPTER 1: Planning for Investment Success — pp. 21-40
**Key Concepts:** Investment planning is critical. Asset allocation is the key element. Discipline and commitment required. No shortcuts.

- Success hinges on three steps: (1) develop a prudent investment plan, (2) fully implement it, (3) maintain it with discipline. "If you create a good plan and follow it, your probability of financial freedom increases exponentially."
- Plan A vs Plan B: Most investors use Plan A (chase what looks good, sell what's down). Plan B is buy, hold, and rebalance regardless of near-term prospects. Academic research shows Plan A investors tend to sell investments that outperform what they buy next.
- Ferri estimate: Less than 10% of all investment plans are fully implemented AND maintained long enough with enough discipline to work. Most fall apart from procrastination, distraction, or outside life stresses.
- **Investment Policy Statement (IPS):** Write your plan down. Include financial needs, goals, asset allocation, investment choices, and the reasoning. "I guarantee that you will not be making many snap investment decisions if you have the discipline to read your IPS before you make any change."
- **No shortcuts:** Financial education is not taught in schools, trade schools, law schools, or medical schools. People learn by expensive trial and error. Common aftermath: become either too conservative or too aggressive. The Enron example — rank-and-file employees lost retirement savings concentrated in company stock/bonds. Same pattern with GM, Lehman, AIG, Bear Stearns, Chrysler.
- **The investment pyramid (Figure 1-1):** Five levels:
  1. Cash/liquid (checking, savings, money market) — 3-4 months if single, 6-12 if family, 24 months at retirement
  2. Liquid long-term discretionary (mutual funds, ETFs, CDs, bonds, retirement accounts, annuities accumulating)
  3. Less liquid long-term discretionary (home, other real estate, businesses, collectibles, hedge funds, venture capital)
  4. Non-discretionary assets (employer pension, restricted stock options, Social Security, annuities paying out)
  5. Speculative capital (trading accounts — "price-trend bets with short holding periods")
- **On professional advice:** Barrier to entry is extremely low — no finance degree required, just reading/writing English, no felonies, pass a simple exam. "It takes about as much effort to become a registered broker as for a 16-year-old to get a driver's license." WSJ publishes long lists weekly of disciplined brokers/advisors. Good advisors exist but separating them from bad ones is hard.
- **On active management:** Like running a marathon in muddy boots. Information is equally available to all investors by law. "What everyone already knows is not worth knowing." Charlie Munger said Buffett succeeds because he has a rare natural gift to sift thousands of data points for the one or two that are market-moving relevant.
- **Morningstar 5-Star trap:** Cash flow studies show vast majority of new mutual fund contributions flow into recently 5-Star-rated funds. Those ratings don't persist. A rush of new money forces the manager to change strategy — the very thing that earned the rating. "In the active fund business, nothing fails like success." — John Bogle
- **Greenspan quote (1998):** "No matter how skillful the trading scheme, over the long haul, abnormal returns are sustained only through abnormal exposure to risk."
- **Ibbotson/Kaplan study (2000):** More than 90% of a portfolio's long-term variation in return is explained by its asset allocation — not by manager skill in picking stocks or timing markets.
- **Brinson/Hood/Beebower studies (1986, 1991):** Same conclusion confirmed on 91 large U.S. pension plans from 1974-1983. 90%+ of long-term return characteristics and risk are determined by asset allocation.
- **Von Clausewitz quote:** "The greatest enemy of a good plan is the dream of a perfect plan." Analysis paralysis is real — at some point you must implement.
- **Boring is good:** No home run investments, no exciting trading stories. But boring asset allocation beats active strategies over a lifetime. Warren Buffett has publicly stated many times that index funds are the best way for most individuals to own common stocks.

## CHAPTER 2: Understanding Investment Risk — pp. 42-56
**Key Concepts:** Returns are directly related to risk. No risk-free investments after taxes and inflation. Practitioners = volatility. Individuals = losing money.

- Risk-return relationship is "one of the few laws of economics that has stood the test of time." Uncertainty of income stream drives expected return — the higher the uncertainty, the higher the required return.
- **T-bills: the myth of "risk-free":** 2007 example — nominal T-bill return 4.7%, after 25% tax = 3.5%, inflation 4.1% → investors LOST 0.6% purchasing power. Cumulative from 1955: $100 fell to $75.26 by 1980, took 21 years to recover, and by 2009 was back down to $93.40. "For more than 30 years we have been paying our government to borrow money from us."
- **TIPS and I-bonds:** Protect from inflation but not taxes. Even TIPS fluctuate — Barclays TIPS Index fell 2.4% in 2008.
- **Multiple definitions of risk:**
  - Academics: volatility of price/return (standard deviation)
  - Pension funds: not having enough to meet future retirement obligations
  - Mutual fund managers: underperforming other managers with same objective
  - Individuals: losing money (most direct and emotionally powerful)
- **1987 crash lesson:** S&P 500 was up 36% through September, then crashed 9% on Friday Oct 16 + 23% on Black Monday Oct 19. Yet by year-end the S&P 500 was up 5.1%. "No investor who had money in a diversified stock portfolio for the entire year in 1987 lost money, but that is not what people remember. We only remember how bad it felt to lose money on Black Monday."
- **Standard deviation explained:** Investment with 5% avg annual return and 10% standard deviation → 68% chance annual return falls between -5% and +15%.
- **Rolling volatility figures (Figure 2-4):** Small stocks avg SD ~20% ±8%; large stocks ~15% ±5%; long-term corp bonds ~8% ±3%; T-bills ~0.2% avg. Order of volatility is remarkably consistent over decades.
- **Volatility kills compounded returns (Table 2-2):** Four portfolios, each with 5% simple average return but different standard deviations (0%, 5%, 10%, 15%). Portfolio A (0% SD) = 5.0% compound return. Portfolio D (15% SD) = 3.9% compound return. Same average, 1.1% lower compound return due to volatility. And the damage is exponential — each 5% increase in SD doesn't cost the same amount; it costs more as SD rises.
- **Key insight:** Any strategy that lowers portfolio return volatility WITHOUT lowering the simple average return will INCREASE the compounded return. This is how diversification creates return.
- **Behavioral benefit:** Reducing volatility also helps investors stay committed — they're less likely to panic-sell. "The chance of making an emotional sell decision goes up as the value of a portfolio goes down."

## CHAPTER 3: Asset Allocation Explained — pp. 57-60+ (continues)
**Key Concepts:** Diversification reduces large-loss risk. Rebalancing contains risk. Correlations not static. Low correlation preferred but hard to find.

- **Harry Markowitz (1952):** 25-year-old grad student at University of Chicago. 14-page paper "Portfolio Selection" published in Journal of Finance. Initially considered "too basic" — so intuitive his professors didn't think it would matter. Eventually earned him the Nobel Prize in Economics (technically "Bank of Sweden Prize in Economic Sciences in Memory of Alfred Nobel").
- **Markowitz's key insight:** Risk of each individual investment is less important than HOW all investments work together to reduce overall portfolio risk. Used mathematicians' formulas to quantify the risk-return relationship achieved through diversification.
- **Two types of stock risk (Figure 3-1):**
  1. Systematic risk (beta/market risk) — cannot be diversified away
  2. Unsystematic risk (unique/company-specific risk) — reduced as you add more stocks across sectors
- **Efficient market theory (EMT) → Modern portfolio theory (MPT):** EMT = markets efficiently price securities based on known information, so a market portfolio is most efficient. MPT = study of efficient allocation of asset classes within a portfolio. Took until late 1970s to be practically implemented due to computing power requirements.
- **Rebalancing explained:** What separates asset allocation from simple diversification. When short-term volatility throws a portfolio off its target weights, rebalancing systematically sells what has outperformed and buys what has underperformed — the mechanized version of discipline.

## CHAPTER 3 (continued): Asset Allocation Explained — pp. 61-80

- **Rebalancing mechanics:** Sell over-target, buy under-target. 50/50 stock-bond example: after 1 year at 60/40 → sell 10% stocks, buy 10% bonds. Can also rebalance when adding/withdrawing money or reinvesting dividends.
- **Regression to the mean:** Ferri's reasoning for why rebalancing works — all broad asset classes tend to return to their historic risk profiles. Highly controversial, but Ferri observes it happening. "Rebalancing forces the sale of a small portion of the asset class that has gone up in value and forces a small extra purchase in the asset class that did not perform as well. This means selling some of the winners and buying more of the losers... selling high and buying low."
- **Rebalancing math (Table 3-1):** Two investments, each with 3.9% compounded return over 2 years. "Let it ride" portfolio = 3.9% return. Rebalanced portfolio = 5.0% compounded — 1.1% bonus purely from eliminating return volatility through rebalancing. "This 'free lunch' from rebalancing is the essence of modern portfolio theory."
- **Calendar vs. percentage rebalancing:** Calendar (annual) vs. percentage trigger (rebalance when X% off target). Percentage may deliver slightly better performance, but not worth the extra monitoring time for individuals. Annual rebalancing is simple, cost-effective, and more likely to actually get done.
- **Correlation:** Range from +1 (perfect positive) to -1 (perfect negative). Positive ≥ +0.3 / Negative ≤ -0.3 / Noncorrelated = between +0.3 and -0.3. Key: diversification benefit from owning noncorrelated or low-positive-correlation assets.
- **Critical truth about correlation:** "I will state right now that there are no negatively correlated asset classes." Not permanently. Even commodities and gold, which sometimes show negative correlation to stocks, produce no real return over inflation. Shorting S&P 500 cancels gains.
- **1990s diversification illusion:** Many investors owned multiple growth funds and thought they were diversified. When tech/telecom collapsed 2000-2002, all fell simultaneously because all were weighted in same sector stocks. "Quantity diversification is not the same as quality diversification."
- **Correlations are dynamic, not static (Figure 3-9):** Rolling 60-month correlation between 5-year T-notes and S&P 500 shifted dramatically over 50 years. During 1990s: positive. During 2000-2009: negative. Average for 20-year period = 0. "Past correlations are not a reliable indicator of future correlations."
- **Decade-by-decade stock/bond MPT benefit (Table 3-4):** When correlation was highly negative (1950-59, 2000-09) → highest portfolio risk reduction AND highest return boost from rebalancing. When correlation was most positive (1990-99) → lowest MPT benefit but benefit still existed.
- **Key decade study:** During 2000-2009 (worst decade for stocks in 60 years), the 50/50 stock-bond portfolio still had a solidly positive return. "Asset allocation is for patient people."
- **Important insight on prediction:** Over any 10-year period, it is easier to predict portfolio risk than portfolio return. Only after ~30 years does a portfolio return prediction reliably align with risk.
- **Asset allocation is not infallible:** Cannot eliminate all risk. Broadly diversified portfolios still lose money sometimes — 50/50 stock-bond lost money in 11 of 60 years (roughly 1 in 5). Worst year: 2008 at -11.9%. Best: 1954 at +27.7%. Single-year losses are not failures — abandoning the plan is the failure.
- **On negatively correlated investments:** Only worth holding if they earn positive real return. "You cannot eat lower risk." Finding TWO negatively correlated classes that BOTH earn positive real returns over inflation = "basically impossible."

## CHAPTER 4: Multi-Asset-Class Investing — pp. 83-100+
**Key Concepts:** Owning more asset classes is better. Each unique class reduces risk. Choose classes with positive real returns and lower correlation. Can select good allocation but not perfect one.

- **The Talmud (circa 1200 BC–500 AD):** "Let every man divide his money into three parts, and invest a third in land, a third in business, and a third let him keep in reserve." Ferri: today that means stocks, bonds, and land.
- **Northwest quadrant:** Adding unique asset classes pushes the efficient frontier toward lower risk + higher return. The northwest quadrant is "investment utopia" — but no fixed combination of assets consistently achieves it. Can only be known in retrospect.
- **March 2009 cautionary tale:** Had you allocated out of stocks in March 2009 because gurus predicted more losses, you would have missed the 60% gain for the rest of the year. "Regular rebalancing means selling a percentage of an asset class that has outperformed and buying more of one that didn't."
- **International stocks:** Roger Gibson (Journal of Finance, 1999): "Diversification across two major forms of equity investing with dissimilar patterns of returns further reduces equity risk." Foreign stocks offer: (1) lower correlation to U.S. markets, (2) currency hedge against declining dollar.
- **International allocation sweet spot (Figure 4-2):** 30% international = ~0.5% extra return over U.S. stocks with NO additional risk. Beyond 70% international = mostly risk with minimal extra return.
- **John Bogle vs. international:** Bogle questioned wisdom of owning international stocks after U.S. outperformed by 8% annually in the 1990s. Ferri respectfully disagreed — "a single 10-year period is not a reason to be out of international stocks for a lifetime." By end of 2009, Bogle changed his view and accepted up to 20% in international index funds.
- **Corporate bonds:** Correlation with Treasury bonds averaged ~+0.8 over long term — generally move together. But during 2007-2009, correlation turned distinctly negative → strong diversification benefit. Treasuries outperformed corporates in 9 of 25 years from 1985-2009 despite corporates having higher yields.
- **Multi-asset-class example (5 classes):** 70% S&P 500 + 15% Pacific + 15% Europe for equity; 50% Treasuries + 50% corporates for bonds. Result: 0.4% higher annualized return vs. 2-class portfolio. A 40% stock multi-asset portfolio beat a 50% stock two-class portfolio on both return AND risk.
- **Wesley Branch Rickey analogy:** Baseball innovator who broke the color barrier. Said "Baseball is a game of inches." Ferri: "Asset allocation is a game of basis points. A basis point is 0.01%. If you add 10 bps to your annual return without taking more risk, it makes a huge difference over decades."
- **1980s:** Multi-asset-class investing worked beautifully — extra 0.8% return, negligible extra risk.
- **1990s:** Multi-asset worked poorly — lower returns than U.S.-only, though also lower risk. Led investors to question international.
- **2000s:** Multi-asset = opposite of 1990s. Considerable return increase vs. U.S.-only. International came back dramatically.
- **Bottom line:** Multi-asset-class investing requires a long-term commitment. "Once you go down this road, it is for life." Modern portfolio theory reduces the number and size of unpleasant outcomes, but does not eliminate them.

## CHAPTER 4 (continued) + CHAPTER 5: A Framework for Investment Selection — pp. 101-120

**Chapter 4 final points:**
- Five critical warnings about asset class discovery:
  1. Nearly impossible to find low-cost investable classes consistently negatively correlated
  2. Correlations change often — "don't trust any report that says 'the correlation is X'" by the time it's printed it may have changed
  3. During extreme volatility, positive correlation spikes across everything (2007-2009: commodities touted as safe haven — they CRASHED more than stocks in 2008)
  4. Sept 11, 2001: all global stock markets fell 5%+ — no amount of global stock diversification helped
  5. More asset classes = better, but diminishing returns after ~12 different investments, and maintenance cost rises
- Computer optimization critique: computers assume past = future, don't know your job security, health, family situation, whether Social Security will exist in 25 years. "A computer model may be mathematically correct based on very limited facts, but the answer is not going to work if the allocation does not fit who you are."

**Chapter 5: A Framework for Investment Selection**

- **Four-step asset allocation process:**
  1. Determine portfolio risk level based on financial needs and tolerance → equity vs. fixed-income split
  2. Analyze asset classes: unique risk, expected return, past correlation, tax efficiency
  3. Choose securities (low-cost index funds and ETFs) to represent each class
  4. Implement fully and rebalance occasionally
- **Three criteria for asset class inclusion:**
  1. Fundamentally different from other classes in portfolio
  2. Expected to earn return > inflation rate over time
  3. Accessible through low-cost diversified fund
- **Stocks vs. bonds — fundamentally different:** Stocks = part ownership, entitled to earnings/dividends/appreciation, LAST in liquidation. Bonds = loan with obligation to pay interest + principal regardless of profits, with bond covenant spelling out terms.
- **Commodity critique:** "A barrel of oil does not swell into two barrels of oil, and an ounce of gold does not multiply into two ounces of gold. No commodity pays one penny in interest or dividends." Commodities have historically zero real return above inflation. Price volatility alone doesn't generate returns — only cash flows do (dividends, interest, rent, scarcity value).
- **Diversification ≠ quantity:** 1990s example: investors owned multiple growth funds thinking they were diversified. All were heavily in tech/telecom stocks. When sector collapsed 2000-2002, all fell together. "Diversification does not simply mean owning several mutual funds with different names."
- **Rolling vs. static correlation:** Single long-term average correlation is "rather useless" and gives false impression correlation doesn't change. Rolling correlation reveals the true shifting nature. Any investment with NO consistently high positive correlation with other portfolio holdings provides diversification benefit.
- **On hedge funds, private equity, venture capital, fund-of-funds:** High fees, illiquid, tax-inefficient, loosely regulated, secretive holdings, "oversold by Wall Street brokers who say you are a very special investor." Ferri: "Stay away."
- **U.S. = 45% of world's tradable securities** (as of 2009) and getting smaller every year as emerging markets (China, India, Brazil, Eastern Europe) grow.
- **Chapter 6 preview:** U.S. stocks have produced ~6% real compounded returns. Best combination: broad index fund + small-cap value.

## CHAPTER 6: U.S. Equity Investments — pp. 120-140+
**Key Concepts:** ~6% real compounded returns. Market can be subdivided into many categories. Best mix: broad index fund + small-cap value.

- **U.S. stock market returns:** Broad market returned 10.9% annually 1950-2009. Beat 6.1% T-notes and 3.8% inflation. But 15 years from 1968-1982, real return barely above inflation. 2000-2009: U.S. stocks LOST 2.9% annually in real terms — worst since 1930s Depression.
- **Market structure:** 26,000+ U.S. company stocks issued, but less than 20% qualify for NYSE or Nasdaq (size, price, liquidity requirements). Remainder = OTC Bulletin Board or Pink Sheets.
- **Wilshire 5000:** First U.S. index capturing return of entire U.S. listed market. Originally 5,000 stocks when launched 1974, number varies today. Excludes bulletin board stocks.
- **Morningstar Style Box:** 9-box grid (3 sizes × 3 styles). Covers ~1,600 stocks = 97% of market value but only 30% of companies. Ferri adds 4th size category (microcap) to capture 99% of market. Large cap = 70% of market value from just 241 stocks (5% of companies). Microcap = 3,400+ stocks but only 3% of market value.
- **Microcap premium (Banz & Reinganum, late 1970s):** Microcap stocks had long-term returns ~5%/year HIGHER than large-cap. Not just explained by volatility — something else was happening. Extra return = payment for liquidity risk. Sometimes moved in opposite direction from large-caps → diversification benefit.
- **Microcap practical reality:** No true, broadly diversified, low-cost microcap fund available. Very difficult market to index. Must find fund with weighted avg market cap ≤$200M, at least 300 companies, expense ratio < 1%, no commission. "Good luck!"
- **Fama-French Three Factor Model (1992):** Published in Journal of Financial Economics, "The Cross-Section of Expected Stock Returns." Three risk factors explain 95% of a diversified portfolio's return:
  1. **Beta (market risk)** — ~70% of return explained by broad market movement
  2. **Size factor** — small stocks generate higher returns, represent unique non-diversifiable risk
  3. **Value factor (BtM)** — value stocks (low price/book) generate higher returns, unique non-diversifiable risk
- **Fama-French implication:** 95% of a portfolio's return comes from exposure to these three risk factors, NOT stock selection. Devastating blow to active managers.
- **Value premium confirmed across indexes (30 years, 1979-2009):**
  - Large-cap value outperformed large-cap growth with LESS standard deviation
  - Small-cap value outperformed small-cap growth by an even larger margin with less risk
  - Value premium appears in all global markets too
- **"Growth fund + value fund" trap:** Holding 50% Russell 3000 Growth + 50% Russell 3000 Value = almost exactly same risk/return as just owning the Russell 3000 index. Zero benefit from splitting. "A better strategy is to hold the total market and add small cap and value based on total risk you are seeking."
- **Why value stocks outperform:** No consensus explanation. Possibilities: overly pessimistic earnings outlooks, undercoverage by Wall Street analysts (they chase banking fees, not unglamorous companies). Fama-French: value stocks must have more fundamental economic risk that doesn't show up in standard deviation — that's why returns are higher.
- **Small-cap value:** Represents ~3% of broad market cap. Has BOTH size premium AND value premium → deviates substantially from broad market year-over-year.

## CHAPTER 6 (continued) + CHAPTER 7: International Equity Investments — pp. 141-160

**Chapter 6 wrap-up:**
- Small-cap value rolling 36-month correlation with total market avg ~+0.8 but varies. Late 1990s: low correlation as growth/tech dominated. After 2000: small value catapulted above large growth. Investors who abandoned small value for tech growth "watched their money evaporate during the tech wreck."

- Caution: "The news is out about the former excess returns from size and style investing." Risk premiums may be smaller going forward.

**Chapter 7: International Equity Investments**

- **Five extra risks of international investing:** (1) Currency risk, (2) Political risk, (3) Trading/custody risk (exchange restrictions on non-domestic investors), (4) Regulatory risk (weak judicial systems), (5) Information risk (weak corporate disclosure)
- **Currency effects:** U.S. dollar trending lower for many decades. 1995-2001: strong dollar → U.S. stocks outperformed. After 2001: weak dollar → international outperformed. Key insight: "No one knows which country will outperform the global markets in the near term, or whether the U.S. dollar will strengthen or weaken."
- **ADRs:** American Depositary Receipts — how large foreign companies list in U.S. markets. U.S. bank converts foreign shares to dollar-denominated ADRs.
- **MSCI EAFE:** Morgan Stanley Capital International Europe, Australasia, Far East Index. ~1,000 large-company stocks from 23 developed markets. Think of it as "the international S&P 500 for all developed countries except U.S. and Canada." Float-adjusted. Historical return: 9.5% net of foreign withholding taxes (1970-2009) vs. S&P 500 9.9%.
- **EAFE underperformed U.S. 1970-2001** — but "the return on EAFE was very good; the problem was that the return on U.S. stocks was great." EAFE overtook U.S. market after 2001.
- **Correlation rising:** EAFE correlation with U.S. total market increased from ~+0.5 in 1998 to ~+0.8 by 2009. Partly due to euro introduction in 1999 linking European economies. Also 2007-2009 crisis spiked correlations. Ferri expects correlations to fall again as economies diverge post-recovery.
- **Japan dominance:** By 1988, Japanese stock boom pushed Pacific Rim to 70% of EAFE. Then Japan bear market + Europe bull market pushed Europe back to 70%+ by 1990s.
- **Splitting EAFE into Europe + Pacific Rim:** 50% Europe + 50% Pacific Rim, rebalanced annually = 10.1% vs. 9.5% EAFE → 0.6%/year improvement from rebalancing the two volatile regions against each other.
- **Why invest internationally?** Mainly for currency diversification. Also security diversification. Not because one region consistently outperforms — capital flows freely in developed markets, erasing arbitrage opportunities quickly.
- **Canada (~6% of international market, ~3% of global):** Finance, energy, basic materials. Natural resources exposure = hedge against rising commodity prices.
- **Emerging markets:** MSCI Emerging Markets Index covers 22 investible countries. Market-weighted (dominated by 1-2 countries at times). DFA created equal-weighted alternative → 0.5%/year higher returns than MSCI EM since 1999. Far more volatile than U.S. or EAFE.
- **Developed vs. Emerging:** Developed = GDP per capita > ~$10,000/year + deep/mature securities markets (Australia, Germany, Japan, UK). Emerging = less-developed, moving toward free markets (China, India, Turkey, Eastern Europe). Frontier markets = earliest stage (Bulgaria, Argentina, Vietnam, Kuwait) — < 1% of global market cap.

## CHAPTER 7 (continued) + CHAPTER 8: Fixed-Income Investments — pp. 161-185

**Chapter 7 wrap-up:**
- International size and value premiums confirmed by multiple independent studies. DFA indexes 1999-2009: international size premium = 3.9%/year, value premium = 4.7%/year. MSCI EAFE data going back to 1975: 2%+/year for each premium.

- EAFE vs. separate regional funds: EAFE is convenient but has no built-in rebalancing — weight swings wildly between Europe and Pacific Rim. Better to hold Europe + Pacific Rim separately and rebalance annually.

**Chapter 8: Fixed-Income Investments**

- **Bond market structure:** Five major categories: (1) government (T-bills, notes, bonds, agency, munis), (2) corporate (investment-grade and high-yield/junk), (3) mortgages (GNMA, FHLMC, FNMA), (4) asset-backed securities, (5) foreign bonds.
- **Morningstar Fixed-Income Style Box:** 9-box grid with horizontal axis = maturity (short/intermediate/long) and vertical axis = credit quality. Two main risk factors: interest-rate risk and credit risk.
- **Maturity structure:** Short ≤ 3 years avg. Intermediate = 4-9 years. Long = 10+ years. Normal yield curve: 10-year T-note yields more than 1-year T-bill. Inverted curve (T-bills > T-notes) = signals economic slowdown. Average spread between 1-year and 10-year Treasuries = ~0.9%. Was 3%+ for much of 2009.
- **Credit risk tiers:** Government bonds lowest risk. Investment grade = BBB/Baa or higher. High-yield/junk = BB/Ba or lower. Rating cut → price falls.
- **Credit spread behavior:** Aaa-Baa spread widens in recession, narrows in recovery. Some say corporate bond risk = equity risk in disguise — not entirely correct.
- **Barclays Capital U.S. Aggregate Bond Index:** 8,400+ bonds. Average maturity ~6.8 years. 70%+ in Treasuries, agencies, government-backed mortgages. Remainder = investment-grade corporates and Yankee bonds. TIPS NOT included.
- **High-yield bonds:** Add default risk on top of credit risk. Should earn higher returns than investment grade. High-yield prices led stock market at both crisis peaks and recovery (widened in Oct 2007 when stocks hit all-time high; started recovering Nov 2008, 5+ months before stocks turned). Ferri: "Never buy individual high-yield bonds — high trading costs and lack of investment information." Use a low-cost diversified fund.
- **TIPS (Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities):** Introduced 1997. Par value + interest payments adjust with CPI. "Inflation risk-free" among all fixed-income. But: (1) traditional Treasuries already embed expected inflation, so TIPS only helps if ACTUAL inflation exceeds expectations; (2) TIPS taxed on BOTH real income AND inflation adjustment (the government taxes the inflation it creates). Key: 10-year nominal Treasury yield minus 10-year TIPS yield = market's 10-year inflation forecast. At end of 2009: 2.23% expected inflation (up from historic low of 0.25% in 2008).
- **iBonds:** Similar to TIPS but: sold at face value in small denominations ($50-$5,000), tax-deferred until cashed in or 30 years, tax-free for education. Limits: $5,000/person/year; penalty = 3 months earnings if cashed before 5 years.
- **Foreign bonds:** Global bond market ~$65 trillion (roughly equal to global stock market). Half dollar-denominated, half foreign currency. Ferri skeptical: "I am not a big foreign bond advocate." Fees for foreign bond funds 2-3x higher than U.S. bond funds, which negates much or all of the currency diversification benefit.

**Chapter 8 wrap-up:**

**Chapter 9: Real Estate Investments**

- **Real estate returns (Dimson study, Brandes/Prudential):** Long-term U.S. real estate returns roughly on par with U.S. stock market since 1930s. Income return (rent) = very consistent at ~7%/year ±1%. Capital appreciation = variable but typically just above inflation.
- **Home ownership returns:** ~3%+ per year nominal = roughly inflation rate. NOT a superior investment — provides a place to live + modest real appreciation. Ferri: "Home prices are down to where they should have been all along without the bubble. I would not be afraid to buy a home at 2010 prices."
- **Three reasons for 2000-2003 housing surge:** (1) Flight to tangible assets after stock market crash, (2) record-low interest rates + new mortgage products requiring little money down, (3) easy government qualification rules + major tax benefits.
- **Ferri self-assessment:** In the 2005 first edition, predicted gradual housing price decline. "For once in my professional career I was right about the short-term direction of a market, although I was wrong in my forecast that the price decline would be gradual. Prices fell like a rock."
- **REITs (Real Estate Investment Trusts):**
  - Congress authorized 1960. Required: 75% assets in real estate, 75% income from rents, 90%+ of taxable income paid as dividends.
  - Tax law change 1992 allowed pension trusts to invest → market exploded from $5.5B in <60 REITs to $250B in 100+ REITs by 2009.
  - Three types: Equity (own properties — only type Ferri recommends), Mortgage (commercial loan financing = more like bonds), Hybrid (both).
  - REIT price-only tracks roughly inflation. TOTAL return (dividends reinvested) ≈ U.S. stock market long-term.
  - Correlation: Was high before 1992 tax law change. Then fell through 2001 (briefly negative). Then rose again, becoming highly correlated with U.S. equities by end of 2000s.
  - Optimal allocation from efficient frontier = 40% REITs, but Ferri recommends only 10% because only ~100 REITs trade on U.S. exchanges — narrow sector, < 3% of market cap.
  - Investable commercial real estate ~$4 trillion total; only ~7% is in publicly traded REITs.
  - Home equity: Keep OUTSIDE investment asset allocation. Can't truly know market value, and can't rebalance against other assets.

**Chapter 10: Alternative Investments (intro, pp. 209-210)**

- "Stocks and bonds are for investing; commodities are for speculating."
- Cash flow drives real returns. Commodities produce NONE. "A ton of copper today will be a ton of copper 10 years from now and 100 years from now, and a ton of copper pays no dividends or interest."
- Hedge funds: private investment accounts, mostly unregulated, very expensive (performance bonus + management fee), illiquid (may require months or years to withdraw), truly skilled managers don't need your money — "you will not gain access to their fund unless you have many millions."
- "When in doubt, stay out."

## CHAPTER 10: Alternative Investments — pp. 209-235

- **Commodities categories:** Energy (crude oil, natural gas), Industrials (copper, steel, cotton), Precious metals (gold, silver), Livestock, Grains/oilseeds (corn, wheat), Softs (cocoa, coffee, sugar).
- **Supply-and-demand cycle always corrects:** High wheat prices → farmers grow more wheat. High steel → more mining. High oil → more pumping, exploration, alternative fuels. "By definition, a commodity suggests abundance."
- **Gold and inflation (thousands of years of data):** Gold and inflation are "basically one and the same." Big spikes followed by big drops. "Gold is a store of wealth that people hoard during bad economic times and sell when money is being made in other assets." In general, longer a global expansion runs → more gold prices fall.
- **CRB Spot Index (inflation-adjusted, 1947-present):** Has fallen ~1%/year in real terms. Commodities boom occurs every ~30 years, with prices falling to a lower real level at the end of each boom.
- **Futures mechanics:** Contango = futures price > spot price (buyers lose on roll). Backwardation = futures price < spot price (buyers gain on roll). Oil was in backwardation ~57% of time 1983-2007. Then went contango in 2006. USO ETF launched April 2006 at $68.25, fell to $52.01 one year later (-23.8%) while crude oil itself only fell 6.5% — the extra loss came from negative roll yield.
- **Commodities total return index = spot change + roll yield + T-bill interest on 95% cash.** Ferri: "You make little to no money over T-bills if you do not time commodity purchases and sales correctly."
- **Commodities in portfolio:** Low correlation with stocks and bonds (good for diversification). But: fail to earn real returns over inflation AND tend to spike in positive correlation with stocks exactly when you don't want them to (e.g., 2008: commodities crashed WITH stocks).
- **Three major commodity indexes:**
  1. Reuters-CRB: equal-weighted 22 commodities
  2. S&P GSCI: market-value weighted, energy ~70%+ of index
  3. DJ-UBS: market-weighted with cap of 33% per group, 15% per component, reweighted annually
- **Commodity fund costs:** Management fees + potentially 5.75% sales charge. ETFs help but still expensive vs. stock/bond index funds. Tax-inefficient: futures gains = ordinary income. Gold/precious metals = taxed as collectibles at higher capital gains rate.
- **Hedge funds — the full case against:**
  - High fees: avg 1.5%/year + 20% performance incentive
  - No regulation → no required disclosure → numerous data biases: survivorship bias (closed funds excluded), selection bias (bad quarters not reported), backfill bias (simulated historical returns added), pricing bias (managers value own illiquid securities)
  - After correcting for all biases: hedge fund returns ≈ intermediate-term government BONDS, not stocks — but with much GREATER risk
  - Performance persistence short-term only; disappears over multiyear periods (Agarwal & Naik, Georgia State/London Business School)
  - Hedge fund closure rate: 2%/year in early 1990s → 12%+ by 2007-2009
  - Fund of funds: adds ANOTHER layer of fees + performance incentive on top of underlying fund fees. "After adding up all the fees and incentives... the cost can easily eat up 50 percent of the profits."
  - "If you still believe that hedge funds are a good idea, please stop believing it."
- **Collectibles:**
  - Mei Moses Fine Art Index: return close to U.S. stocks since 1954, slightly higher volatility
  - PCGS CU3000 Coin Index: 181-fold increase 1970-1989, then crashed 75% in 5 years. Long-term = roughly stock market returns but without accounting for acquisition, storage, insurance costs
  - "Collect what you enjoy collecting, and perhaps you will even make money doing it."

## CHAPTER 11: Realistic Market Expectations — pp. 237-260

- **Returns vs. volatility predictability:** Market volatility is MORE predictable over time than market returns. Standard deviations of asset classes remain relatively stable decade to decade; returns do not. Highest volatility periods = lowest return periods.
- **1980-2009 retrospective:** U.S. stocks returned 11.2%/year. 5-year T-bonds returned 8.4%/year. Inflation 3.5%. Real stock return >7%, real bond return ~5%. "There is little chance of those returns happening over the next 30 years." Reason: inflation was double-digit in 1980 and has fallen to low single digits. High starting inflation → higher nominal returns. Low starting inflation → lower nominal returns.
- **Ferri's conservative stock return estimate:** 5% real per year going forward.
- **T-bill reality check:** Long-term avg nominal return = 5.1%. Inflation = 3.8%. Real return = 1.3%. But income taxes are due on 5.1% (not 1.3%). At 25% tax rate, after-tax real T-bill return = **0%**. "A return from T-bills of zero percent after taxes and inflation is not surprising and should be expected."
- **Stacking risk premiums model:**
  - Base: T-bill return = expected inflation + real risk-free rate
  - + Term risk premium (longer maturity = higher expected return, ~0.5% per 3-year duration increase based on TIPS curve)
  - + Credit risk premium (investment grade: ~0.5-1.5% over Treasuries; high yield: 1.5-3%+)
  - + Equity risk premium (~2-3% annualized over long-term corporate bonds)
  - + Small-cap value premium (~3-5% annualized over total market, avg 5% historically but Ferri uses conservative 3%)
- **Economic factor (GDP) model:**
  - Equity return = earnings growth + cash dividends + valuation change
  - Earnings growth highly correlated (+0.9) with GDP per capita growth
  - Fed target real GDP growth = ~3%/year
  - 10% of corporate GDP flows through to earnings (consistent over time)
  - Dividend payout <30% of earnings, dividend yield ~2% at end of 2009
  - P/E ratio: assumed constant at 18x (normal for low-inflation period) for forecasting
- **Bond return formula:** Fixed-income return = yield at purchase + change in yield (driven by inflation changes, real risk-free rate changes, and credit spread changes)
- **Interest rates track inflation (Figure 11-11):** From early 1960s to early 1980s, inflation soared to 15%, causing massive bond selloffs. From 1982 on, inflation fell → historic bond rally. By 2008, inflation turned negative → T-bond yields hit 60-year lows.
- **Ferri's 30-year return forecast (Table 11-5, assuming 3% inflation):** Conservative estimates. Ferri: "A forecast should always err on the conservative side. It is wiser to expect lower returns and be pleasantly surprised rather than relying on a rosy forecast and running out of money."
- **Commodities exception:** High volatility does NOT predict high returns for commodities. No cash flow = no return premium. "The present value of $0 in cash flow is $0. Commodities and gold are worth only what you will be able to sell them for in the future. Historically, this has been the inflation rate."

## CHAPTER 12: Building Your Portfolio — pp. 261-285+

- **Four life stages:** Early Savers (20-39), Midlife Accumulators (40-59), Transitional Retirees (60-79), Mature Retirees (80+)
- **Portfolio limit:** No more than 12 different funds in a portfolio — diminishing returns and higher costs after that point.

**Stage 1: Early Savers (20-39)**
- Savings habit > investment method at this stage. Save 10% of earnings from day one.
- Emergency fund: 6 months of living expenses.
- Don't invest 100% in stocks (two reasons): (1) Almost no one can actually handle 100% stocks through a bear market — many who thought they could sold at the bottom in 1987 and 2000-2003; (2) A 20% bond allocation allows you to BUY stocks when they're down.
- Counterintuitive finding: Many young workers actually underinvest in equities due to misunderstanding risk. TIAA-CREF data: surprisingly high % of young workers put retirement assets in low-interest fixed accounts.
- Recommended median: 70% stocks / 30% bonds. Range: 60-80% stocks.

**Stage 2: Midlife Accumulators (40-59)**
- Emergency fund: 12 months (growing family = more unexpected needs).
- Speculative investments should wind down. "It is dangerous to think that you will find a way to make money by speculating at this point in your life if you did not make any money by speculating when you were young."
- **Liability matching framework:**
  1. Estimate future living expenses
  2. Estimate noninvestment income (Social Security + pension)
  3. Identify the income gap
  4. Determine how much to accumulate: expect max 5% withdrawal rate → need 20× annual income gap
  5. Design savings/investment plan to reach that target
- If in 30%+ combined tax bracket: consider municipal bonds in taxable accounts.
- Recommended median: 60% stocks / 40% bonds.

**Stage 3: Transitional Retirees (60-79)**
- Move from accumulation to distribution phase.
- Keep 1-2 years living expenses in cash/short-term bond fund to manage cash flow jitters.
- **Social Security + pension income:** Don't try to calculate their present value or call them "bonds." Instead, use the "liability reduction method" — subtract their cash flows from retirement expenses, then invest to cover the remaining gap.
- Safe withdrawal rate: ~4%. Adjust based on: (a) age of retirement, (b) bequest goals, (c) expected active vs. passive retirement years.
- Person at age 65 likely to live 20+ more years (avg life expectancy 86, per IRS Publication 590). Portfolio still needs growth.
- Recommended median: 50% stocks / 50% bonds.

**Stage 4: Mature Retirees (80+)**
- 2 years in cash/short-term bonds. No speculative investments.
- Average life expectancy of 65-year-old today: 86 years (10 years longer than in 1940). Society of Actuaries tables now go to age 120.
- Consolidate accounts with 1-2 custodians (Schwab, Vanguard, Fidelity). Write down where all documents are. Write a portfolio management statement in your own words.
- Key decision: Is remaining portfolio for you, or will most go to heirs? If heirs, should be invested based on THEIR ages and needs, not yours. Could mean a MORE aggressive allocation than you'd use for yourself.

**"Your Age in Bonds" Rule & the "Allocation Age" Modification**
- Classic heuristic: put your age in bonds (e.g., at 65 → 65% bonds). Simple, easy to implement.
- Ferri & Israelsen (Financial Planning Magazine, Nov 2009) refined this with "allocation age" — adjust chronological age up or down based on life situation.

  2. Two 55-year-old women: one with pension + Social Security covering all expenses → allocation age 70 (can take less risk); other with no pension, part-time work, chronically ill spouse → allocation age 40 (needs growth). Same birthday, completely different allocations.

## CHAPTER 13: How Behavior Affects Asset Allocation Decisions — pp. 296-315

**Core premise:** MPT assumes rational investors who maximize returns for given risk. Reality: investors are emotional, irrational, and systematically biased.

**Key behavioral researchers:** Daniel Kahneman, Meir Statman, Richard Thaler, Robert Shiller, Amos Tversky.

**Overconfidence bias:**
- Investors overestimate their ability to pick stocks and time markets.
- Studies (Barber & Odean): investors who trade most frequently underperform by the widest margin.
- Overconfident investors: disproportionately male, highly educated, high-income, professionals.
- Vanguard study: 85% of 401(k) participants classified themselves as "unskilled" investors — but many still over-trade.
- Women's 401(k) accounts outperform men's: women maintain plans, don't trade emotionally.
- Most ironic finding: worst-performing 401(k) accounts belong to highest-paid, most-educated employees who thought they could beat the market.

**Loss aversion:**
- Kahneman & Tversky (Prospect Theory): $1 loss feels ~2× as painful as $1 gain feels good.
- Result: investors sell winners too early (lock in gain) and hold losers too long (avoid realizing loss).
- For asset allocation: people accept too little risk to avoid the pain of losses, even when that means failing to meet long-term goals.

**Recency bias:**
- Investors extrapolate recent trends. After a 3-year bull market: "stocks always go up." After a bear: "this is different, I need to get out."
- Causes investors to pile into asset classes at peaks and flee at troughs — the opposite of rebalancing logic.

**Stress-testing your portfolio:**
- Ferri method: use ACTUAL historical fund data for a 12-month period matching your portfolio's allocation.
- Example: woman with $300K IRA, 70/30 portfolio. 2000-2002 stress test showed losses that eventually triggered panic below her tolerance. Portfolio restructured to 40/60 — one she could actually maintain through downturns.
- "The key is to find the asset allocation you can live with, not the one that maximizes theoretical return."
- VIX as fear gauge: above 30 = elevated fear; VIX hit ~80 in October 2008 (all-time high).

**Risk avoidance vs. risk tolerance:**
- Two separate questions: (1) How much risk can you tolerate without panicking? (2) How much risk do you actually need?
- Don't take risk you don't need. If you've already won the game (accumulated enough), stop playing with money you can't afford to lose.
- "Risk avoidance: don't take risk you don't need. Risk tolerance: know your pain threshold and stay under it."

## CHAPTER 14: When to Change Your Asset Allocation — pp. 316+

**Three legitimate reasons to change allocation:**
1. Financial goal is within reach — reduce risk when you no longer need to grow
2. You realize you won't need all the money — invest for heirs' needs (their age/timeline, not yours)
3. You realize your tolerance was lower than you thought — emotional reality exposed by an actual bear market

**Bear market near retirement — what to do:**
- Don't increase risk (chasing returns to recover)
- Don't decrease risk (locking in losses and missing recovery)
- Options: save more, work longer, or spend only portfolio income
- "The worst time to make a major asset allocation decision is during extreme market conditions."

**Daily volatility creates "terrifying moments" missed in annual data:**
- 1987: Black Monday, -20% in a single day → but annual S&P 500 return for 1987 was +5.1%
- The annual number is calm; the intraday experience is not. This is why investors panic even in "good" years.

**Practical "I can't sleep" protocol — three-step equity reduction:**
1. Run the "income reality check" — portfolio income (dividends + interest) covers expenses even if prices fall. This calmed many clients through 2008.
2. If still anxious: permanently reduce equity by 10% (e.g., 60/40 → 50/50). Stay there through the bottom and beyond. Do NOT return to prior risk level.
3. If still anxious after first 10% cut: reduce another 10%. "Once you get to this level of risk, stay there, even when the market recovers."
- "Emotional investing is a lose-lose-lose proposition. Panic sellers almost always capitulate near a market bottom, then lose again when they don't get back in during the recovery."

## CHAPTER 15: Fees Matter in Asset Allocation Planning — pp. 318-332

**The cost drag example (Table 15-1):**
- 24-year-old, $36K salary, saves 10%, 3% annual raise, retires at 65, withdraws 4%
- Market earns 7.5% gross; low-cost fund = 7.0% net (0.5% ER); high-cost = 6.0% net (1.5% ER)
- **Result: low-cost fund accumulated 32% more over 40 years — a difference of $472,000+**
- That extra $472K generates ~$19,000/year more income at 4% withdrawal rate

**Fund fee landscape (2009 Morningstar data):**
- 20,000+ mutual funds with min purchase <$10K
- Average annual expense ratio across ALL funds: 1.4%
- Only ~4,500 funds were both no-load AND no 12b-1 fee
- Only ~6% of all funds had expenses below 0.5%/year
- Vanguard average: less than 0.3%
- Warren Buffett (2004 Berkshire annual report): "All they had to do was piggyback Corporate America in a diversified, low-expense way. An index fund they never touched would have done the job."
- Fund companies hide costs via "soft dollars" and "rebates" — backroom deals with brokers not reported to investors

**Taxes as an investment expense:**
- Four taxable events: (1) interest/dividend income, (2) short-term cap gains distributions, (3) long-term cap gains distributions, (4) personal sale of shares
- ETFs tax advantage: managers can rid portfolio of low-cost shares by distributing to authorized participants → fewer cap gains distributions than traditional mutual funds
- Municipal bond interest: federally tax-free (some exceptions for AMT-subject bonds)

**Asset location to reduce taxes (placement strategy):**
- Put HIGH-tax investments in tax-deferred/tax-free accounts: corporate bonds, CDs, agency bonds, REITs, high-turnover funds, commodities funds
- Put LOW-tax investments in taxable accounts: low-turnover equity index funds, broad market ETFs, municipal bonds
- Caution: location strategies make rebalancing harder; tax rates change; behavioral trap of comparing accounts separately rather than holistically

**Tax swapping (tax loss harvesting):**
- Sell a losing fund → buy a similar-but-not-substantially-identical fund → capture the loss
- Example: sell VTI (tracks MSCI U.S. Broad Market) → buy IWV (tracks Russell 3000). Near-identical tracking, different enough to avoid wash sale rule.
- Tax lot tracking: buy $3K/quarter → each quarter = different cost basis → can designate specific lots when selling
- Example savings: selling the losing July lot (−$300 loss) vs. letting FIFO apply (+$240 gain) = $189 difference in taxes at 35% bracket
- Consult tax advisor — IRS hasn't formally defined "substantially identical" for ETFs/mutual funds

**Dollar cost averaging + tax lots:**
- Invest fixed amount quarterly (not irregularly). Creates clean, distinct tax lots.
- Makes tax swapping much simpler and more effective.

**Why index funds fit asset allocation perfectly (3 reasons):**
1. Low tracking error — data in asset allocation models comes from market indexes; index funds deliver those actual returns
2. Low expense ratios — best index funds: ~0.1%/year vs. industry avg 1.4%. (Warning: some "index" funds charge >1.0% — check before buying)
3. Low tax liability — low turnover → few capital gains distributions; ETF structure eliminates even more

**Hiring a low-fee advisor:**
- Ferri's fee at Portfolio Solutions LLC: 0.25% of AUM (minimum quarterly fee per household)
- Benefits: planning + implementation, consistency of strategy, circuit breaker during panic, ongoing management
- Red flags: commission-based "advisors" are salespeople by definition — not unbiased
- Key checks: verify background at SEC.gov; demand itemized fees in writing; no conflicts with investment products used
- Common advisor-fee range: 0.25%–1.0%+ of assets; higher end is rarely justified
