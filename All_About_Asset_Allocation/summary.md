# All About Asset Allocation — Summary
**Richard A. Ferri, CFA | 2nd Edition, 2010 | McGraw-Hill | 367 pages**

---

## Core Thesis

Asset allocation — how you divide money across asset classes — determines the vast majority of your long-term portfolio return and risk. Getting this right, implementing it fully, and maintaining it with discipline is worth more than any hot stock tip or market call. The future is unknowable; diversification across many asset classes with low costs is the only rational response.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Asset allocation drives ~93% of portfolio return variability** (Brinson, Hood, Beebower 1986 and follow-on studies). Security selection and timing account for the remaining scraps.

2. **Three allocation strategies:** Strategic (buy-hold-rebalance, the book's focus), Tactical (short-term forecasts), Market Timing (all-in/out). Every unbiased academic study shows tactical and timing are no better than flipping a coin.

3. **Fama-French Three-Factor Model:** Beta + Size + Value explain 95% of diversified portfolio returns. Small-cap and value stocks earn higher returns over time — compensation for higher volatility, not magic.

4. **Risk stacking:** Expected return = T-bill rate + term premium + credit premium + equity premium + size premium + value premium. Each layer adds return and risk. Understand what you're buying.

5. **Rebalancing is the only free lunch:** Sell what went up, buy what went down. Counterintuitive but documented. Investors who rebalanced in early 2009 captured the full recovery by year-end. Annual calendar rebalancing > reactive rebalancing.

6. **Correlation is the engine of diversification.** Assets with low or negative correlation reduce portfolio volatility without sacrificing return. But correlations are unstable — especially during crises when all risky assets move together. This is why you need uncorrelated asset types, not just different labels.

7. **Four asset class selection criteria:** (1) Real expected return above inflation, (2) Low or varying correlation with existing portfolio, (3) Low-cost available vehicles, (4) Fundamental economic differences from other classes.

8. **Life-cycle allocation by stage:**
   - Early Savers (20-39): 70/30 stock-bond median
   - Midlife Accumulators (40-59): 60/40 median, liability matching
   - Transitional Retirees (60-79): 50/50 median, income gap analysis
   - Mature Retirees (80+): 40/60 median, consolidate and document

9. **"Allocation age" > chronological age:** Adjust your bond allocation based on pension income, bequest goals, health, employment stability. Two 55-year-olds can rationally have allocation ages of 40 and 70.

10. **Behavioral finance reality check:** Overconfident investors trade too much and underperform. Loss aversion makes people sell at bottoms. Recency bias pushes people in at peaks. Women outperform men in 401(k)s because they maintain plans. The highest-paid, most-educated employees have the worst-performing accounts.

11. **Stress-test before committing:** Model your portfolio using ACTUAL historical data through a real bear market period. Find your true pain threshold, not your stated one. If you can't sleep, run the income-reality-check → reduce equity 10% → repeat.

12. **Fees compound in reverse:** 1% extra annual expense = 32% less wealth over 40 years ($472K+ for a median worker). Only ~6% of all mutual funds charge under 0.5%/year. Vanguard average: 0.3%. Industry average: 1.4%.

13. **Asset location matters:** Tax-inefficient assets (corporate bonds, REITs, high-turnover funds) belong in tax-deferred accounts. Low-turnover equity index funds and municipals belong in taxable accounts. But beware: location complicates rebalancing.

14. **Tax swapping (loss harvesting):** Sell a losing fund, buy a similar-but-not-identical fund, capture the tax loss while staying invested. Track tax lots via dollar-cost averaging to maximize harvesting opportunities.

15. **Only three legitimate reasons to change allocation:** Financial goal is in reach (de-risk), you won't need all the money (invest for heirs), your real tolerance proved lower than believed. Never change allocation out of fear mid-bear-market.

## Thematic Breakdown

### Part I: Philosophy and Evidence
Ferri grounds everything in academic evidence. Brinson et al. on asset allocation dominance. Fama-French on size and value. Every chapter ties back to: costs low, diversify broadly, hold through cycles.

### Part II: The Asset Classes
Methodical coverage of every major class — U.S. equities (micro/small/mid/large, value/growth), international (developed, emerging, EAFE), fixed income (T-bills, T-bonds, TIPS, corporate, high-yield, municipal, foreign), real estate (REITs, commercial, home), and alternatives (commodities, hedge funds, collectibles). Each analyzed for: return history, volatility, correlation with other classes, and recommended implementation vehicles.

### Part III: Building the Portfolio
Life-cycle framework with specific stock/bond targets at each stage. Liability matching for retirees. The "allocation age" modification to the "age in bonds" heuristic. Behavioral finance traps and how to avoid them.

### Part IV: Maintenance and Costs
Rebalancing mechanics. When (and why) to change allocation. Chapter 15's devastating arithmetic on fees — the most actionable chapter in the book for most readers.

## Contrarian Ideas

- **Don't try to value Social Security and pensions as bonds.** Use the "liability reduction method" — subtract those cash flows from your expenses, then invest to cover the remaining gap. Calling them "bonds" distorts your allocation math.
- **Young workers often under-invest in equities,** not over-invest. TIAA-CREF data showed surprising concentrations in low-interest fixed accounts by young workers who confused "safe" with "low volatility."
- **100% stocks is usually wrong for young investors** — not because of risk tolerance but because you need some bonds to BUY stocks when they're cheap. The 20% bond "dry powder" position outperformed 100% stocks in many scenarios.
- **Hedge funds: the research is devastating.** Backfill bias, survivorship bias, pricing bias, and high fees render most academic studies of hedge fund performance garbage. After real costs, most underperform.
- **Home ownership is NOT an investment.** It's consumption that provides housing services. Real home appreciation since 1900 is near zero after inflation. REITs are the real estate investment vehicle.
- **The Morningstar 5-Star trap:** New cash floods 5-star funds after the rating is earned, forcing strategy changes. "In the active fund business, nothing fails like success." — Bogle

## Author Background

Richard A. Ferri, CFA, is the founder of Portfolio Solutions, LLC, a low-cost investment management firm based in Troy, Michigan. Before founding Portfolio Solutions, he spent years as a stockbroker and active trader — an experience that converted him to passive, evidence-based investing. He is the author of six books including *All About Index Funds* and *The ETF Book*. His firm charges 0.25% of AUM, a figure he cites in the text as a benchmark for what "fair" advisory fees look like.
