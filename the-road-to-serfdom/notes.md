## Editorial Foreword

### Publication History and Editorial Context

- This definitive edition is part of *The Collected Works of F. A. Hayek*, originally planned as 22 volumes, now anticipated at 19
- **Bruce Caldwell** became general editor in 2002 after **Stephen Kresge** and **W. W. Bartley III**
- *The Road to Serfdom* has gone through numerous editions: 1944 original, 1956 with new foreword, 1976 with new preface, 1994 50th anniversary edition with **Milton Friedman** introduction
- British edition (March 1944) vs. American edition (September 1944) — American version chosen for this text because it's clearer (explicitly says "England" rather than "this country")
- Editorial policy: texts published in corrected, revised, annotated form; "American English" used throughout (differs from other volumes using British English)
- Hayek's quotations sometimes misquote originals — only noted when meaning would be affected

### Publication Challenges

- University of Chicago Press director **Joseph Brandt** didn't expect big sales for a British-focused book
- Hoped to get **Walter Lippmann** to write foreword (he estimated 2,000-3,000 copies if he did, otherwise 900)
- Lippmann was too busy; **Wendell Wilkie** (whose *One World* was a bestseller) also declined
- **John Chamberlain** (*New York Times* book review editor) ultimately recruited for the job
- Since 1944, University of Chicago Press estimates 350,000+ copies sold; Routledge added thousands more (no reliable count)
- Significant *samizdat* circulation behind Iron Curtain during Cold War

## Introduction

### Reception: Praise and Condemnation

- Not everyone liked the book — intelligentsia in the U.S. greeted it with condescension and vitriol
- **Isaiah Berlin** (British Embassy diplomat) wrote in April 1945 he was "still reading the awful Dr. Hayek"
- Economist **Gardiner Means** told William Benton of *Encyclopaedia Britannica* after 50 pages: "couldn't stomach any more"
- Philosopher **Rudolf Carnap** wrote to **Karl Popper**: "I was somewhat surprised to see your acknowledgement of von Hayek. I have not read his book myself; it is much read and discussed in this country, but praised mostly by the protagonists of free enterprise and unrestricted capitalism, while all leftists regard him as a reactionary"

> "Those who, like Carnap, have not read Hayek but think that they already know what he is all about should be prepared for some surprises."

- Those on the left should preview chapter 3 (government intervention Hayek accepted in 1944)
- Those on the right should examine his distinction between liberal and conservative in 1956 foreword to American paperback

### Prelude: The British, Naziism, and Socialism

#### Hayek's Arrival at LSE (1931)

- **Friedrich A. Hayek** came to London School of Economics in early 1931 from Vienna to deliver lectures on monetary theory
- Published as *Prices and Production* (1931)
- Topic was timely — Britain's stagnant 1920s economy had worsened with the depression
- Offered visiting professorship beginning Michaelmas (fall) 1931, appointed to **Tooke Chair of Economic Science and Statistics** a year later
- Would remain at LSE until after the war

#### Summer 1931: Crisis and Controversy

- Summer 1931 was traumatic for Britain and Europe
- Financial crises on Continent led to gold drain in Britain, ultimately collapsing Labour government
- Gold standard abandoned, protectionist tariffs imposed in autumn
- August 1931: Hayek caused stir with first half of review of **John Maynard Keynes**'s *A Treatise on Money*
- Drew heated reply from Keynes — their battle would occupy little of Hayek's attention during 1931-32 academic year

#### 1933 Inaugural Lecture: Attacking Socialist Planning

- By 1932, Hayek had secured his chair
- March 1, 1933 inaugural lecture turned to new subject: Why were economists increasingly regarded by public as out of step with the times?
- **Hayek's answer**: Public opinion unduly influenced by earlier generation of economists who criticized *theoretical* approach to social sciences, undermining credibility of *economic reasoning*
- Once accomplished, people felt free to propose utopian solutions to depression that serious economics would show were infeasible
- Cited new enthusiasm for socialist planning in Britain as example of such misguided ideas
- **The culprits**: **German Historical School economists**, advisors to **Bismarck** in 19th century

### The German Historical School Connection

- Choice of German Historical School economists was significant — they had been chief rival of Austrian School of Economics before WWI
- German Historical School economists were conservative imperialists, cheerleaders for strong German Reich, opponents of German social democracy
- Also architects of numerous social welfare reforms
- Bismarck embraced these reforms to undermine socialists and strengthen the Empire
- Hayek hoped audience would see parallels to 1933 — only a month before **Adolf Hitler** became Chancellor

#### The Reichstag Fire and Hitler's Rise

- Within days of becoming Chancellor, Hitler convinced President Hindenburg to sign decree prohibiting meetings and publications endangering public security
- Aimed squarely at communists and socialists
- Morning before Hayek's address: Reichstag building set on fire and burned
- Nazis blamed communists, used it to justify further repression
- Half century before, Bismarck had used an attempt on the Emperor's life to put his own anti-socialist laws in place

#### Deterioration in Germany (1933)

- After Hayek's speech, situation in Germany continued to deteriorate
- March: wholesale arrests of communists, harassment of social democratic leadership
- Opposition newspapers closed, constitutional protections swept away
- Notorious "enabling law" passed giving Hitler virtual dictatorial powers
- April 1: nationwide boycott against German Jews
- Later in month: action against trade unions began
- May: students on university campuses held book-burning celebrations
- **May 10, 1933**: Berlin Opernplatz book-burning — martial songs and speeches broadcast live across Germany

### The "Nazi-Socialism" Memo (Spring/May 1933)

- Hayek's criticisms of socialism in his address were not well received
- One intelligent student told him the sole purpose was to destroy his reputation by taking "clearly anti-socialist position"
- Even more disquieting: interpretation of events in Germany emerging among British intelligentsia
- Prominent members of German industrial class initially supported Hitler's rise
- Nazi persecution of the left led many in Britain to see Nazism as either:
  1. Capitalist-inspired movement, OR
  2. Last-ditch attempt by bourgeoisie to deny inevitable triumph of socialism

#### Lord Beveridge's Interpretation

- **Lord Beveridge** (Hayek's director at LSE) was propagating this interpretation
- Very special situation arose in England by 1939: people seriously believed National Socialism was a capitalist reaction against socialism

> "A very special situation arose in England, already in 1939, that people were seriously believing that National Socialism was a capitalist reaction against socialism. It's difficult to believe now, but the main exponent whom I came across was Lord Beveridge. He was actually convinced that these National Socialists and capitalists were reacting against socialism. So I wrote a memorandum for Beveridge on this subject, then turned it into a journal article."

#### Hayek's Counter-Argument

- Hayek wrote memo titled "**Nazi-Socialism**" (reproduced for first time in this volume's appendix)
- **Rebuts the standard account**: National Socialism is NOT "a genuine socialist movement"
- In support: notes antagonism to liberalism, restrictive economic policy, socialist background of some leaders, antirationalism
- Nazi success NOT due to reactionary desire to return to prewar order
- Rather: represented culmination of antiliberal tendencies grown since Bismarck's time
- Socialism and Naziism both grew out of antiliberal soil that German Historical School economists had tended
- Added chilling warning: many other countries were following same process of development
- Contended: "the inherent logic of collectivism makes it impossible to confine it to a limited sphere" — hinted at how collective action must lead to coercion

#### From Memo to Magazine Article to Pamphlet

- Hayek ultimately turned 1933 memo into magazine article (April 1938): "**Freedom and the Economic System**"
- Following year: expanded version as public policy pamphlet (1939)
- Comparing the two reveals accretion of ideas that would later appear in *The Road to Serfdom*

### Development of Key Arguments (1938-1939)

#### 1938 Version: The Fatal Flaw of Socialist Planning

- 1938 version continued to stress links between fascism and socialism
- Began expanding on what he saw as fatal flaw of socialist planning: "presupposes a much more complete agreement on the relative importance of the different ends than actually exists, and that, in consequence, in order to be able to plan, the planning authority must impose upon the people that detailed code of values which is lacking"
- Followed with fuller exposition: even democratic planning, if successfully carried out, eventually requires authorities to use variety of means (propaganda to coercion) to implement the plan

#### 1939 Version: New Ideas Added

- Drew contrast between central planning and planning of a general system of rules under liberalism
- Noted how price system is mechanism for coordinating knowledge
- Made several observations concerning economic policy under liberal regime
- All these ideas would be incorporated into *The Road to Serfdom*

### The Socialist Calculation Debate

- Some arguments developed in 1930s fighting battles against socialist planning
- Other arguments not actually new at all
- Another debate on feasibility of socialism took place immediately following WWI
- **Ludwig von Mises** (Hayek's mentor) contributed key argument
- This earlier controversy in mostly German-language publications
- When Hayek encountered similar arguments in England (academic colleagues and press favoring planning), decided to educate them about earlier discussion
- **1935**: Published edited volume *Collectivist Economic Planning: Critical Studies on the Possibilities of Socialism*

#### The Volume's Contents

- Contained translations of articles by others, including von Mises's seminal piece "**Economic Calculation in the Socialist Commonwealth**"
- Introductory and concluding essays by Hayek
- In former: reviewed earlier Continental debates on socialism
- In concluding essay "**The Present State of the Debate**": identified and assessed more recent proposals
- Among them: idea of reintroducing competition within a socialist state, dubbed "**pseudo-competition**" by Hayek, later called "**market socialism**"

#### Socialist Responses

- Drew response from socialist camp
- Most prominent: Polish émigré economist **Oskar Lange**
- Defense of market socialism in journal article later reprinted in book: *On the Economic Theory of Socialism*
- Hayek would respond in turn to Lange and **H. D. Dickinson** in a book review a few years later

### The LSE Classroom Battle (1933-1937)

- Three essays constitute written record of early economic arguments against socialism
- Battle also taking place in classrooms (and doubtless senior commons room)
- Beginning 1933-34 summer term: Hayek offered class "**Problems of a Collectivist Economy**"
- Socialist response immediate: next year students could enroll in "**Economic Planning in Theory and Practice**" (taught first by **Hugh Dalton**, later years by **Evan Durbin**)
- According to LSE calendar, 1936-37 summer term: students could hear Hayek 5-6 pm and Durbin 6-7 pm each Thursday night
- This may have proved too much: next year classes placed in same time slot on successive days (Durbin Wednesdays, Hayek Thursdays)

### The Broader Context: Scientific Planning in 1930s Britain

- By time WWII began, Hayek had criticized (in book, journal, classroom) variety of socialist proposals
- *The Road to Serfdom* in many respects continuation of this work
- Important to recognize it also goes beyond academic debates
- By end of decade: many other voices calling for transformation (sometimes radical) of society
- Few held corporatist view of good society bordering on fascism
- Others sought middle way
- Still others avowedly socialist
- One thing all agreed on: *scientific planning* necessary if Britain was to survive

#### Webb and Mannheim: Champions of Planning

- Fabian socialists **Sidney and Beatrice Webb**: *Soviet Communism: A New Civilization?*
- Praised "**Cult of Science**" discovered on visits to Soviet Union
- Held out hope that scientific planning on massive scale was appropriate medicine for Britain's recovery from depression
- Sociologist **Karl Mannheim** (fled Frankfurt 1933, ultimately gained LSE position): warned only by adopting comprehensive system of economic planning could Britain avoid fate of central Europe
- For Mannheim, planning was inevitable; only question: totalitarian or democratic?
- These economists joined by other highly respected public intellectuals (natural scientists to politicians)

#### The Ambiguity of "Planning"

- Planning was the word on everyone's lips, but very few clear about what it entailed
- Situation well captured by Hayek's friend and LSE colleague **Lionel Robbins** (1937):

> "Planning" is the grand panacea of our age. But unfortunately its meaning is highly ambiguous. In popular discussion it stands for almost any policy which it is wished to present as desirable.... When the average citizen, be he Nazi or Communist or Summer School Liberal, warms to the statement that "What the world needs is planning," what he really feels is that the world needs that which is satisfactory.

- Robbins's passage suggests planners to be found all along political spectrum
- Sorting out exactly what planning implied for a complex society was to be yet another major theme in Hayek's coming work

### Hayek's New Project: Abuse of Reason (1939)

- By 1939, most elements for Hayek's book were present, but form not yet in place
- When not fighting against socialist planners, Hayek spent much of rest of 1930s exhausting himself writing and rewriting major theoretical work in economics
- Ultimately published 1941 as *The Pure Theory of Capital*
- That project finally winding down in August 1939
- In letter to old university friend **Fritz Machlup**: spoke of new project through study of relationship between scientific method and social problems
- Would provide systematic investigation of intellectual history and reveal fundamental principles of social development of last 100 years (from **Saint-Simon** to **Hitler**)
- This was to become Hayek's **Abuse of Reason project**, from which would emerge *The Road to Serfdom*

## Hayek's War Effort

### September 1939: Offering Services

- September 1, 1939: Germany invaded Poland
- Two days later: England and France declared war
- Within a week: Hayek sent letter to director general of British Ministry of Information
- Offered services to aid with propaganda campaign directed at German-speaking countries
- Enclosed memo with suggestions about how to proceed
- Proposed campaign with historical dimension demonstrating principles of liberty England and France stood for were same as those enunciated by great German poets and thinkers of past
- Show these had been eclipsed by "distorted view of history, on which they have been brought up during the last sixty years" (since Bismarck's time)
- Hayek's efforts had little effect — staff member dated December 30th said offer to help was politely but firmly turned down

### War Teaching and Writing

- Once war began in earnest (May 1940): most LSE colleagues called to duty in various government departments
- As naturalized British subject (1938), as émigré Hayek not offered post
- Spent war teaching classes and writing
- Clearly frustrated British government had no place for him
- Complained in letter to Machlup: "getting really annoyed by the refusal to use a person like myself on any useful work"
- By this time, Hayek's intellectual history was well under way

### Outlining the Book to Machlup

- In letter to Machlup, provided outline of the book
- Noted "[t]he second part would of course be an elaboration of the central argument of my pamphlet on Freedom and the Economic System"
- First part: "**Hubris**"
- Second part: "**Nemesis**"

### Working on Abuse of Reason (1940)

- Hayek worked on Abuse of Reason project for rest of 1940
- Completing number of historical chapters, beginning others on methodology
- Toward end of year: began transforming last part of book into what would become *The Road to Serfdom*
- Initially envisioned as "sixpence Penguin volume"
- Why abandon larger historical endeavor (never completed the Abuse of Reason project) to focus on shorter, more popular, "political" tract?
- Probably never have definitive answer, but certain plausible reasons stand out
- Were Allies to lose war, western civilization in Europe itself would be the cost
- Hayek also worried about what would transpire if Allies *won*

## Reception and Critical Response

### Reception in England vs. United States

- **Barbara Wootton** praised the book in England with "courteous and frank" work, mentioned in Hayek's 1956 foreword
- Labour Party politicians in Britain took Hayek's views seriously and responded substantively
- **Different American reception**: **Herman Finer's** *Road to Reaction* called it "the most sinister offensive against democracy to emerge from a democratic country for many decades"
- Finer accused Hayek of anti-democratic sentiment, claiming "the idea *that democracy is dangerous and ought to be limited*" was the essence of Hayek's argument
- Finer found "thoroughly Hitlerian contempt for the democratic man" in Hayek's work
- **George Soule** labeled Hayek "the darling of the Chamber of Commerce"
- Left-leaning *PM* newspaper exposé claimed business interests promoted the book's "selling"
- *Look* and *Reader's Digest* treatments "gave big business a wonderful opportunity to spread distrust and fear of the New Deal"

### The 1948 Postscript and Foreword

- Press sent Hayek **Finer's book** in December 1945, asked if he wanted to add reply chapter
- Hayek worked on postscript on and off for years; 1948 draft exists in archives
- **Not included in final foreword** — Hayek's response focused on explaining different receptions in England vs. US rather than attacking critics
- Key explanation: different experiences with socialism in the two countries

> "It is hard to imagine that Hayek's book would have become so widely known, remembered decades after its initial publication, had it not been for the *Reader's Digest* condensation."

- **Antony Fisher**, Institute of Economic Affairs founder, was inspired by the condensation to "wage the war of ideas" after reading it and meeting Hayek at LSE (summer 1945)
- Condensation turned book into symbol for admirers and critics alike
- **John Scoon**: "People still tend to go off half-cocked about it; why don't they *read* it and find out what Hayek actually says!"

### Prominent Criticisms Assessed

#### Historical Accuracy

- **Frank Knight** objected: German history far more complicated than Hayek portrayed
- Socialist policies since Bismarck comprised only one element in Germany's trajectory
- Hayek's defense: *The Road to Serfdom* was intended as final section of much larger project tracing gradual decline of liberalism across countries
- Publishing as separate piece was "akin to offering a punch line without the joke"
- The book confronted the widespread claim that National Socialism was the logical/historical next phase of collapsing capitalism
- Hayek's point (now evident): fascism and communism represent totalitarian systems with more in common with each other than with liberal free market democracies

#### Vagueness on Alternatives

- Complaint: long on criticism, short/vague on proposed alternatives
- **Alvin Hansen** (*The New Republic*): Hayek distinguished "good planning" from "bad planning" but didn't explain how to draw the line
- **John Maynard Keynes** read the book en route to Bretton Woods, wrote it was "a grand book" with moral/philosophical agreement, but "not only in agreement with it, but in a deeply moved agreement"
- Keynes continued: "You admit here and there that it is a question of knowing where to draw the line. You agree that the line has to be drawn somewhere, and that the logical extreme is not possible. But you give us no guidance whatever as to where to draw it."

#### Response Through Later Works

- Hayek took these criticisms to heart
- **Two further contributions** refined and extended arguments:
  1. ***The Constitution of Liberty***: laid out philosophical foundations of liberal constitutionalism — private sphere of individual activity defined, state granted monopoly on coercion but constitutionally limited by rule of law
  2. ***Law, Legislation, and Liberty***: lamented western democracies circumventing liberal constitutionalism by passing coercive legislation under guise of achieving social justice but actually serving special interests; included unique proposal for legislative reform

#### Market Socialism

- Third complaint: argument against socialism incomplete by failing to address "market socialism"
- **Evan Durbin** (LSE colleague) chided Hayek in *Economic Journal* for "only one reference to the work of those of us who are both practicing economists and also Socialists"
- **Durbin's "democratic socialism"**: "rigid programme of production" not needed; only that "the final responsibility for taking economic decisions is transferred from the private company or group of shareholders to the representatives of the community"
- Variant of **market socialism** (also "competitive socialism") articulated by **Oskar Lange** in *On the Economic Theory of Socialism*
- Hayek had already criticized this doctrine in 1940 review of Lange volume
- That review became the long footnote on market socialism in *The Road to Serfdom*

### Market Socialism Explained

- **Attractive because** appears to combine best parts of rival systems: efficiency of market-based system + egalitarian policies promoting social justice, all within democratic polity
- Critics of capitalism but accept perfectly competitive markets have desirable efficiency characteristics
- **Key claim**: real world markets don't resemble perfect competition — atomistic competition disappeared with cartels/monopolies in late 19th century
- Contemporary capitalism lacks beneficial aspects of competition while retaining defects
- **Planned market socialist economy** would restore true competition with all benefits while correcting social injustices of unfettered capitalism
- **Lange's blueprint**: free markets for consumer goods and labor, but (due to public ownership of means of production) no market for productive resources
- **Central Planning Board** would provide prices, adjusting them up/down using "trial-and-error" method depending on revealed shortages/surpluses

### Hayek's Critique of Market Socialism

- **Key complaint**: Lange neglected to say how often prices would need adjustment
- Even with quick adjustments, Hayek thought system would always be playing catch-up relative to market adjustments, thus less efficient
- Famous quote: "it is difficult to suppress the suspicion that this particular proposal has been born out of an excessive preoccupation with problems of the pure theory of stationary equilibrium"
- Hayek's later work on how markets coordinate social and economic activity in a world where knowledge is dispersed would become central contribution to economics

#### Lange's Response (July 31, 1940)

> "I do not propose price fixing by a real central planning board, as a practical solution. It was used, in my paper, only as a methodological device to show how equilibrium prices can be determined by trial and error even in the absence of a market in the institutional sense of the word. Practically, I should, of course recommend the determination of the prices by a thorough market process whenever this is feasible."

- Hayek inferred Lange had basically accepted his criticisms about *practical feasibility*
- Though Durbin thought differently, Hayek felt market socialism was theoretical exercise not to be taken seriously as practical proposal

### Why Hayek Relegated Market Socialism to a Footnote

- Did not write *The Road to Serfdom* with theoretical economists like Lange or Durbin in mind
- Unlike such economists, most advocates of "planning" hadn't begun thinking through what planned society meant
- For them, planning was (as **Robbins** put it) a panacea
- Vague but widespread sentiment for which book was meant to be antidote
- Hayek trying to show planning "might sound good in theory, but would not work in practice (or, at least, not unless the western democracies were prepared to accept severe constraints on personal liberty of the sort on display in the systems against which they currently were fighting)"
- Market socialism only a theoretical dream; details of argument against it would be out of place in general book
- Economist readers already aware of arguments he'd made in 1940

### Hayek's Irritation with Durbin

- Evident in unpublished 1948 postscript:

> "Mr. Durbin... is especially pained that I have not taken more seriously and devoted no more than a note to the interesting schemes of a competitive socialism which have been put forward in recent years in a number of learned books and articles. I am quite ready to discuss their theoretical merits and have in fact done so at some length in an article quoted in the footnote just referred to. And I shall be very glad to examine these plans further as soon as there are any signs that they are taken seriously by, and exercise any practical influence on the politics of, the socialist parties. But I have yet to find any socialist party willing to consider using competition as the method for organizing economic activity, and until this is the case I cannot see that anyone but the specialist need be bothered with the objections to those ingenious schemes."

- Added that he'd have more confidence in genuineness of desire to reconcile freedom and socialism if **Oskar Lange** hadn't become spokesman for Russian point of view at UN
- Durbin now member of Socialist British Government "doing most of the things of which he apparently disapproves"

### Durbin's Fate and Lange's Trajectory

- **Durbin died in drowning accident 1948** — may explain why passage never included in foreword
- **Lange's accommodation** to political realities in native Poland increased over time
- Went on to write apologetics for Stalin
- Renounced earlier views on market socialism, forbid their republication in Polish

### Market Socialism's Persistence

- Dreams never die despite Lange and Durbin being gone
- Most recent revival after collapse of Soviet bloc in last decade of 20th century
- Longevity easy to explain: for those seeking middle way, market socialism is ideal system
- **Recent bolstering**: economics of information (incentive problems), public choice analysis (political obstacles)
- Hayek's 1948 position: until real-world example of "ingenious scheme" forthcoming, best considered theoretical construct of interest only to specialists with no particular relevance for actual world

### The "Inevitability Thesis" or "Slippery Slope"

- Hayek claimed to have said: once society engages in planning, it's doomed to end up in totalitarian state
- **Durbin**: Hayek believed "any departure from the practice of free enterprise, any hope that reason and science may be applied to the direction of economic activity, any attempt at economic planning, must lead us remorselessly to serfdom"
- If Durbin's statement seems stark, he wasn't alone — interpretation spanned ideological spectrum from **Barbara Wootton** to **George Stigler**
- **Paul Samuelson** diagrammed it in economics text: political freedom on one axis, economic freedom on other, movement down curve (slippery slope) from high to low levels of both being Hayek's prediction: "government modification of laissez faire must lead *inevitably* to political serfdom"

### Hayek's Denial of Inevitability

- Interpretation occurred despite frequent protests
- 1976 preface: "It has frequently been alleged that I have contended that any movement in the direction of socialism is bound to lead to totalitarianism. Even though this danger exists, this is not what the book says."
- Private letter to **Paul Samuelson**:

> "I am afraid in glancing through the 11th edition of your *Economics* I seem to have discovered the source of the false allegation about my book *The Road to Serfdom* which I constantly encounter... [Y]ou assert that 'each step away from the market system and towards the social reform of the welfare state *is inevitably* a journey that must end in a totalitarian state' and that 'government modification of market laissez faire must lead *inevitably* to political serfdom.'

> How anyone who has read my book can in good faith say this when ever since the first edition I say right at the beginning... 'Nor am I arguing that these developments are inevitable. If they were, there would be no point in writing this. They can be prevented if people realize in time where their efforts may lead.'"

### What "Inevitability" Actually Means

- Hayek's letter to Samuelson rules out one interpretation: NOT making historical claim that no matter what future moves in Britain/America, socialist future ending in totalitarianism was inevitably coming
- This kind of inevitability thesis was what Hayek criticized in "Scientism and the Study of Society" — historicism, belief in historical laws allowing prediction of necessary future

### The Warning Interpretation

- **More plausible reading**: warning that unless we change our ways, we are headed down road to serfdom
- Part of Hayek's intent to issue warning
- Particular fear: might embark on path without realizing it
- Speech before Economic Club of Detroit: "the danger is the greater because we may choose the wrong way, not by deliberation and concerted decision, but because we seem to be blundering into it."
- Chapter 4 title makes clear: some opponents claimed planning was "inevitable," that unless we embraced "planning for freedom" we were headed toward totalitarianism
- Hayek hoped to stand such argument on its head: planning itself constituted significant step toward totalitarian state

### The Logical Argument Interpretation

- Yet another way to read Hayek: offering *logical* rather than *historical* argument
- Recognized "liberal socialists" value freedom of choice and honoring of individual preferences
- **Denied** they could maintain those values *and still carry out their proclaimed program of extensive central planning*
- "socialism can be put into practice only by methods which most socialists disapprove"
- Even if begun as "liberal socialist" experiment, full-scale planning requires planning authorities take over all production decisions
- To make any decisions, would need to exercise more and more political control
- Can't separate control of economy from political control
- **Hayek's logical argument against planning** articulated in 1939 "Freedom and the Economic System":

> "In the end agreement that planning is necessary, together with the inability of the democratic assembly to agree on a particular plan, must strengthen the demand that the government, or some single individual, should be given powers to act on their own responsibility. It becomes more and more the accepted belief that, if one wants to get things done, the responsible director of affairs must be freed from the fetters of democratic procedure."

### Testing Hayek's Logical Thesis

- Countries Hayek most concerned about (Western European democracies and United States) did not go to complete central planning or full nationalization
- Britain had movement in this direction after war, reached high point by late 1940s (only ~20% of industry nationalized)
- **Those seeing Hayek as predicting inevitable trend** would view this history as refuting his claim
- **Those seeing him as providing warning** might thank him for saving them from disaster
- **Confronting logical argument**: subsequent paths of western European democracies not really tests of thesis
- Many developed substantial welfare states; Hayek spoke about separate dangers in later writings
- **Existence of welfare states** (whatever successes/failures) does not undermine Hayek's logical argument: **welfare state is not socialism**

### The Proper Test

- Ask: How many actually existing, real-world political systems have fully nationalized means of production AND preserved both economic efficiency and freedom of choice over goods and occupations?
- Count them up
- Compare with number that nationalized means of production and turned to extensive planning/control with curtailment of individual liberties
- If one agrees this is right test, **Hayek's position fully vindicated**: full socialism *can* only be put into practice by using methods most socialists would disapprove

## The Continuing Relevance of The Road to Serfdom

- Reading/rereading will be pleasurable for some, induce apoplexy in others
- Continues to be lightning rod and Rorschach test
- Elements of general treatise but also (as Hayek admitted) "tract for the times"
- Modern readers unfamiliar with Third Reich history may stumble over names like **Julius Streicher** or **Robert Ley**
- Who recalls **Sir Richard Acland's** "Forward March" movement or Temporary National Economic Committee?
- Editor provided brief notes for context

### Timeless Ideas

- **Immediate objective**: persuade British audience that heritage of liberal democracy under rule of law should be viewed as national treasure rather than object of scorn
- Still-vital roadmap for organizing society rather than embarrassing relic
- Message invites more than occasional reexamination

### Dangers in Times of War

- **Warning about dangers war poses for established civil societies**
- During such times hard-won civil liberties most likely to be all-too-easily given up
- Politicians instinctively recognize seductive power of war
- Times of national emergency permit invocation of common cause and purpose
- War enables leaders to ask for sacrifices
- Presents enemy against which all segments of society may unite
- True of real war, but savvy politicians find it effective to invoke war metaphors in many contexts
- War on drugs, war on poverty, war on terror — examples from recent times
- **More worrisome**: none has logical endpoint; each may be invoked forever

### Hayek's Specific Fear

- For war to be fought effectively, power and size of state must grow
- No matter what rhetoric politicians employ, they and bureaucracies love power
- Power never easily surrendered once won
- Though eternal vigilance is sage advice, "wartime" (or when politicians try to convince it is) is when those valuing preservation of individual liberty must be most on guard

### Power of Ideas

- Clear recognition throughout all Hayek's work
- Perhaps **John Maynard Keynes** said it best in closing chapter of *The General Theory*:

> "the ideas of economists and political philosophers, both when they are right and when they are wrong, are more powerful than commonly understood. Indeed the world is ruled by little else. Practical men, who believe themselves quite exempt from any intellectual influences, are usually the slaves of some defunct economist. Madmen in authority, who hear voices in the air, are distilling their frenzy from some academic scribbler of a few years back. I am sure that the power of vested interests is vastly exaggerated compared with the gradual encroachment of ideas."

- Hayek would have offered immediate assent
- Keynes's passage carries implication: those who fail to understand origins of ideas do so at their peril
- Given many years Hayek spent diligently toiling as perennial advocate of causes most contemporaries thought lost, anachronistic, or return to reaction, perhaps no person better represents notion of power of ideas in twentieth century than **F. A. Hayek**

## Foreword to the 1956 American Paperback Edition

### Reception in England vs. America

- Book written in England during WWII, primarily for English socialists who had left-leaning sympathies
- Hayek witnessed firsthand the origins of totalitarian movements in Europe before the war
- English reception: thoughtful, examined seriously despite political disagreements (except some Labour politicians who attacked it as written by a "foreigner")
- American reception: vastly different, more polarized
  - Rejected by first three publishers
  - Sold at unprecedented rate for academic book when published
  - Received both lavish praise and passionate hatred
  - American socialists dismissed it as "malicious and disingenuous" without examining the argument

> "Contrary to my experience in England, in America the kind of people to whom this book was mainly addressed seem to have rejected it out of hand as a malicious and disingenuous attack on their finest ideals; they appear never to have paused to examine its argument."

### Why the Different Reactions?

- **Timing difference**: In England (and Europe), socialist ideas had been tested for 40-50 years; disillusionment already set in
- **In America**: Socialist planning ideas were only 10-15 years old (New Deal era), enthusiasm still fresh and "unsoiled by practical experience"
- What Europeans had learned through bitter experience was still "the glittering hope of a better world" to American radicals

### American Context in the 1940s

- **1934 National Planning Board** devoted attention to planning examples from Germany, Italy, Russia, and Japan
- Planning advocates in America vehemently denied connection between planning and totalitarianism
- Suddenly became fashionable to deny planning inspiration came from Russia
- **Alvin Hansen** and other prominent economists advocated planning, held up Russia as model

> "In 1934 the newly established National Planning Board devoted a good deal of attention to the example of planning provided by these four countries: Germany, Italy, Russia, and Japan."

- Planning advocates claimed Italy, Russia, Japan, and Germany "all reached totalitarianism by very different roads"

### The Century of Socialism (1848-1948)

- **Hot socialism** (state ownership of means of production) probably ended around 1948
- Many illusions discarded by socialist leaders themselves
- Socialist name lost much attraction in the US
- **New threat**: "Welfare State" replaced socialism as reform goal
  - Hodgepodge of inconsistent ideals
  - Many measures that "in the long run cannot be reconciled with the preservation of a free society"
  - Not all aims are impracticable, but danger in choosing efficient tools incompatible with freedom

### The Persistent Danger

- **Administrative coercion** increasingly replacing rule of law modifications
- **Direct state controls and monopolistic institutions** replacing market-based financial incentives
- Legacy of socialist period influencing policy for years to come
- Understanding *why* and *how* certain economic controls destroy freedom is critical

> "Just because in the years ahead of us political ideology is not likely to aim at a clearly defined goal but toward piecemeal change, a full understanding of the process through which certain kinds of measures can destroy the bases of an economy based on the market and gradually smother the creative powers of a free civilization seems now of the greatest importance."

### Warning About "Liberal" in America

- Hayek uses "liberal" in nineteenth-century sense (current in Britain)
- **In American usage**: "liberal" came to mean advocacy of government control (nearly opposite of original meaning)
- This linguistic shift helped camouflage leftist movements
- **True liberalism** ≠ conservatism
  - True liberalism: denial of all privilege
  - Conservatism: defender of established privilege, leans on government power, nationalistic, anti-intellectual
- Conservative tendencies often closer to socialism than true liberalism

> "I am still puzzled why those in the United States who truly believe in liberty should not only have allowed the left to appropriate this almost indispensable term but should even have assisted by beginning to use it themselves as a term of opprobrium."

### The British Labour Government Experience (1945-1951)

- Six years of socialist government after *The Road to Serfdom* published
- **Main thesis confirmed**: Most important change is *psychological*, not immediate institutional
  - "an alteration in the character of the people"
  - "This is necessarily a slow affair, a process which extends not over a few years but perhaps over one or two generations"
- Strong tradition of political liberty is no safeguard if danger comes from gradual undermining by new institutions

> "The important point is that the political ideals of a people and its attitude toward authority are as much the effect as the cause of the political institutions under which it lives."

### Evidence of Character Change in Britain

- **1947 Economic Survey**: Prime Minister claimed government must "lay down the economic task for the nation" and determine "what the objectives of policy ought to be"
- **Six months later**: Same government forced to restore **conscription of labor** (peacetime)
- Power unused doesn't diminish significance—authorities having power changes behavior even without using it

> "It is rather difficult to see how the government could have persisted in its illusions when in the same document it claims that it was now for 'the Government to say what is the best use for the resources in the national interest' and to 'lay down the economic task for the nation: it must say which things are the most important and what the objectives of policy ought to be.'"

### Sociological Evidence of Regulation's Impact

- Wartime regulations made permanent by Labour government affected mental attitudes of youth
- Activities require referencing "book of words" first before acting
- "Time-budget of an ordinary city youth" shows waking hours predetermined by directives
- Young people acclimatized to state control, seldom plan independent enterprises
- Seeking "escape and recuperation in an absence of discipline as complete as they can make it"

> "The young people] are obliged to stomach so much external and, as it seems to them, meaningless control that they seek escape and recuperation in an absence of discipline as complete as they can make it."

### Tocqueville's Prophecy

- Hayek compares British trajectory to **Tocqueville's** prediction of "new kind of servitude"

> "After having thus successively taken each member of the community in its powerful grasp, and fashioned him at will, the supreme power then extends its arm over the whole community. It covers the surface of society with a network of small complicated rules, minute and uniform, through which the most original minds and the most energetic characters cannot penetrate to rise above the crowd."

- Power "does not destroy, but it prevents existence; it does not tyrannize, but it compresses, enervates, extinguishes, and stupefies a people, till each nation is reduced to be nothing better than a flock of timid and industrial animals, of which government is the shepherd."

### The Totalitarian Danger Remains

- Hayek never accused socialist parties of deliberately aiming at totalitarianism
- **Core argument**: "unforeseen but inevitable consequences of socialist planning create a state of affairs in which, if the policy is to be pursued, totalitarian forces will get the upper hand"
- Socialism requires methods most socialists disapprove
- Old socialist parties inhibited by democratic ideals, lacked ruthlessness for their chosen task

> "I explicitly stress that 'socialism can be put into practice only by methods of which most socialists disapprove' and even add that in this 'the old socialist parties were inhibited by their democratic ideals' and that 'they did not possess the ruthlessness required for the performance of their chosen task.'"

- British socialists in 1920s *less* ruthless than German Social Democrats
- German Social Democrats never approached totalitarian planning as closely as British Labour government

### Post-War Developments

- **Ivor Thomas** (former Labour party member): "from the point of view of fundamental human liberties there is little to choose between communism, socialism, and national socialism. They all are examples of the collectivist or totalitarian state . . . in its essentials not only is completed socialism the same as communism but it hardly differs from fascism."

- Growth of arbitrary administrative coercion
- Destruction of **Rule of Law** for reasons discussed in chapter 6
- **Lord Chief Justice** warning (25 years before): "New Despotism" now established fact, per *The Economist*

> "The 'New Despotism' of which a Lord Chief Justice had warned Britain as long as twenty-five years ago is, as *The Economist* recently observed, no longer a mere danger but an established fact."

- Despotism exercised by "thoroughly conscientious and honest bureaucracy" for country's good
- Nevertheless arbitrary government, free from parliamentary control
- Transition to dictatorship "would be easy, swift, and could be accomplished with complete legality"

### Recommended Reading

- **John Jewkes**, *Ordeal of Planning* (London: Macmillan, 1948)
  - "Best discussion known to me of a concrete instance of the phenomena discussed in general terms in this book"
  - Detailed analysis of British Labour government's economic policies
  - "Spells out a lesson which is of significance far beyond Great Britain"

### Future of Socialism in Britain

- Unlikely another Labour government would resume large-scale nationalization
- Defeat of systematic socialism gives breathing space to re-examine ambitions
- Without revised social aims, likely to "continue to drift in the same direction in which outright socialism would merely have carried us a little faster"
- Must discard dangerous parts of "socialist inheritance" to preserve freedom

> "Without such a revised conception of our social aims, we are likely to continue to drift in the same direction in which outright socialism would merely have carried us a little faster."

## Preface to the 1976 Edition

### Reflections on Success and Reception

- Written 1940-1943 during Hayek's work in economic theory, unexpectedly became starting point for 30+ years of new work
- Annoyance with misinterpretation of Nazi movement in English "progressive" circles sparked the project
- Book became political tract despite Hayek being an economist — social scientists made him uncomfortable about straying beyond his technical competence
- One philosopher wrote to another reproaching him for lauding "this scandalous book, which 'of course [he] had not read'"

### Subsequent Work and Clarification

- Later works attempted to fill gaps: *The Constitution of Liberty* (1960) restated classical liberalism more coherently
- *Law, Legislation, and Liberty* (three volumes, first in 1973) provided Hayek's own answers to questions raised by earlier restatement
- Now proud of the book rather than apologetic — surprised by how much early insight later work confirmed
- Simple, nontechnical introduction to "one of the most ominous questions which we have to solve"

### Terminological Changes Since 1940s

- **"Socialism"** in 1940s meant unambiguously: nationalization of means of production and central economic planning
- Sweden today is less socialistic than Great Britain or Austria, though commonly regarded as more socialistic
- Socialism has come to mean chiefly: extensive redistribution of incomes through taxation and welfare state institutions
- Effects discussed in book are brought about more slowly, indirectly, imperfectly through welfare state
- **Ultimate outcome tends to be the same**, though process differs from what Hayek originally described

### Clarifications and Corrections

> "It has frequently been alleged that I have contended that any movement in the direction of socialism is bound to lead to totalitarianism. Even though this danger exists, this is not what the book says."

- Book contains a warning: unless we mend principles, unpleasant consequences will follow which advocates don't want
- Hayek admits he **under-stressed the significance of communism in Russia** — pardonable when Russia was wartime ally
- Had not fully freed himself from "current interventionist superstitions" and made concessions now considered unwarranted
- Still regarded it as rhetorical question whether Rule of Law prevailed in Germany under constitutional constraints
- Later discovered **Hans Kelsen** and **Harold J. Laski** (influential socialist lawyers/political scientists) maintained precisely that Rule of Law did prevail

### Increased Alarm Since Writing

- Study of contemporary trends has increased alarm and concern
- Influence of socialist ideas and naive trust in good intentions of totalitarian power holders has "markedly increased" since the book was written
- No longer resents being known for this "pamphlet" rather than strictly scientific work
- After 30+ years of further study, genuinely believes insights help avoid grave dangers

## Introduction

### The Experience of Repeated History

> "Few discoveries are more irritating than those which expose the pedigree of ideas." —Lord Acton

- Contemporary events differ from history: we don't know results they will produce
- Looking back we assess past significance and trace consequences
- **We cannot get a glimpse of what lies ahead** — would be different if we could live through events a second time
- Fortunate that man cannot have this experience; history knows no laws it must obey

### The Author's Unique Perspective

- Hayek's experience: **"twice living through the same period"** — twice watching very similar evolution of ideas
- Half his adult life in Austria (close touch with German intellectual life), half in US and England
- Became convinced forces destroying freedom in Germany are **"also at work here"** and less understood than in Germany
- Moving between countries allows twice watching similar phases of intellectual development
- Hearing opinions/measures for second time that he first met 20-25 years ago gives them new meaning as **symptoms of a definite trend**

### Germany as Warning

> "It is necessary now to state the unpalatable truth that it is Germany whose fate we are in some danger of repeating."

- Danger not immediate; conditions in England/US still remote from recent Germany
- Yet the road is long, and **difficult to turn back once one advances**
- Not Hitler's Germany that England/US resemble, but the **current of ideas** during/after WWI and present democracies
- Same determination to use wartime organization for peacetime creation
- Same contempt for 19th-century liberalism, spurious "realism," cynicism, fatalistic acceptance of "inevitable trends"
- **Nine out of ten lessons** reformers want us to learn from war are precisely the lessons Germans did learn — which produced the Nazi system

### The Trend Away from Liberalism

- Socialist policy of Germany was held up by progressives as example to imitate before the war
- Sweden more recently the model to which progressive eyes were directed
- Memory reaches back to how deeply German thought/practice influenced ideals and policy in England and US for at least a generation before WWI
- England/US increasingly convinced that at least some forces destroying freedom in Germany are at work in their countries too
- **Character and source of danger less understood** than in Germany

### The Tragedy of Good Intentions

> "The supreme tragedy is still not seen that in Germany it was largely people of good will, men who were admired and held up as models in the democratic countries, who prepared the way for, if they did not actually create, the forces which now stand for everything they detest."

- Chance of averting similar fate depends on facing danger and revising cherished hopes if they prove to be the source of danger
- Few signs we have intellectual courage to admit we may have been wrong
- Few ready to recognize: **rise of fascism and nazism was reaction against socialist trends** of preceding period but a **necessary outcome of those tendencies**
- Truth most were unwilling to see even when similarities between communist Russia and National Socialist Germany were widely recognized
- Many who think themselves superior to nazism and sincerely hate its manifestations **work for ideals whose realization would lead straight to the abhorred tyranny**

### Parallels Are Not Inevitable

- Not arguing these developments are inevitable — if they were, no point in writing
- They can be prevented if people realize in time where their efforts may lead
- Until recently, little hope that making people see the danger would be successful
- Now time seems ripe for fuller discussion — problem more widely recognized, special reasons make it imperative to face issues squarely

### Understanding the Enemy

- This will enable us to understand our enemy and the issue at stake
- Cannot be done satisfactorily without recognition of positive ideals for which we are fighting
- Not enough to give firm beliefs to resist enemy propaganda (blatant and subtle forms)
- Insufficient when countering propaganda among people in countries under Axis control (where effect won't disappear with Axis defeat)
- Not enough to show others what we fight for is worth their support, or to guide building of new world safe against old dangers

### Democratic Insecurity Before the War

- Democracies showed inner insecurity and uncertainty of aim in dealings with dictators before war
- Not less in propaganda attempts and discussion of war aims
- Can be explained only by **confusion about their own ideals** and nature of differences separating them from enemy
- Misled by refusing to believe enemy was sincere in profession of some beliefs shared
- Left and Right both deceived believing National Socialist party was in service of capitalists and opposed to all socialism

### The Development Leading to Totalitarianism

- Many features of Hitler's system have been **recommended for imitation** from unexpected quarters, unaware they're integral parts of that system
- Dangerous mistakes made because **we do not understand the opponent** we are faced with
- Almost as if we don't want to understand development producing totalitarianism — such understanding might destroy dearest illusions we are determined to cling to

### Anglo-Saxon Intellectual Debt to Germany

- Theory that Germans as such are inherently vicious is **hardly tenable and not creditable** to those who hold it
- Dishonors long series of Anglo-Saxon thinkers who during last hundred years gladly took over what was best in German thought
- Forgets that 80 years ago **John Stuart Mill** drew inspiration for *On Liberty* more from two Germans than any other men: **Goethe** and **Wilhelm von Humboldt**
- Forgets two most influential intellectual forebears of National Socialism were **Thomas Carlyle** and **Houston Stewart Chamberlain** (a Scot and an Englishman)
- In cruder forms, this view adopts worst features of German racial theories

### The Real Question About Germany

- Problem is not why Germans as such are vicious (congenitally probably no more than other peoples)
- Question: determine circumstances during last 70 years that made possible progressive growth and ultimate victory of particular set of ideas
- Why in the end this victory brought most vicious elements among them to the top
- **Mere hatred of everything German** instead of particular ideas now dominating Germans is very dangerous
- Blinds those who indulge in it against real threat
- To be feared this attitude is **kind of escapism** — unwillingness to recognize tendencies not confined to Germany
- Reluctance to re-examine and discard beliefs we've taken over from Germans and by which **we are still as much deluded as the Germans were**

### The Danger of Blaming Only German Character

- Doubly dangerous: contention that only peculiar wickedness of Germans produced Nazi system likely to become excuse for **forcing on us the very institutions which have produced that wickedness**

### Socialist Exiles and the Spread of Ideas

- Interpretation about to be proffered differs from that given by most foreign observers and majority of exiles from Germany/Italy
- If interpretation is correct, explains why almost impossible for person who holds now prevalent socialist views to see events in proper perspective
- **Superficial and misleading view**: sees in National Socialism merely a reaction fomented by those whose privileges/interests were threatened by advance of socialism
- Was naturally supported by all those who, though at one time active in movement of ideas leading to National Socialism, stopped at some point and were forced into conflict with Nazis to leave their country
- They were numerically the only significant opposition to Nazis means no more than that in wider sense practically **all Germans had become socialists** and liberalism in old sense had been driven out by socialism
- Conflict between National Socialist "Right" and "Left" in Germany is **kind of conflict that will always arise between rival socialist factions**

### The Paradox of Socialist Refugees

- Interpretation means, however, that many socialist refugees, in clinging to their beliefs, are now (though with best will in the world) **helping to lead their adopted country the way which Germany has gone**

### Anglo-Saxon Friends Shocked by Refugee Views

- Hayek knows many Anglo-Saxon friends sometimes shocked by semi-Fascist views occasionally expressed by German refugees
- Genuinely socialist convictions of these refugees could not be doubted
- While observers put this down to others' being Germans, true explanation: **they were socialists whose experience had carried them several stages beyond that yet reached by socialists in England and America**

### German Socialism and Prussian Tradition

- True, of course, that German socialists found much support in their country from certain features of **Prussian tradition**
- Kinship between Prussianism and socialism (in which both sides in Germany gloried) gives additional support to main contention
- Would be mistake to believe **specific German rather than socialist element produced totalitarianism**

- Was from masses, not classes steeped in Prussian tradition and favored by it, that National Socialism arose

## Chapter One: The Abandoned Road

> "A program whose basic thesis is, not that the system of free enterprise for profit has failed in this generation, but that it has not yet been tried." —F. D. Roosevelt

### The Unexpected Turn of Civilization

- When civilization takes unexpected turn — instead of continuous progress we expect, we find ourselves threatened by evils associated with past ages of barbarism — we naturally blame anything but ourselves
- Have we not all striven according to our best lights?
- Have not many finest minds worked to make this a better world?
- Have not efforts and hopes been directed toward greater freedom, justice, and prosperity?
- If outcome so different from aims — if instead of freedom and prosperity, **bondage and misery** stare us in the face — clear sinister forces must have foiled our intentions
- We are victims of some evil power which must be conquered before we can resume the road to better things

### Common Ground in Diagnosis

- However much we differ when we name the culprit (wicked capitalist, vicious spirit of particular nation, stupidity of elders, social system not yet fully overthrown) — we all are, or at last were until recently, **certain of one thing**: the **leading ideas which during the last generation have become common to most people of good will** and have determined major changes in our social life **cannot have been wrong**
- Ready to accept almost any explanation of present crisis except one: that **present state of the world may be result of genuine error on our own part** and that pursuit of some of our most cherished ideals has apparently produced results utterly different from those expected

### The Crisis Before and During War

- While energies directed to bring this war to victorious conclusion, sometimes difficult to remember that even before war the values for which we are now fighting were threatened here and destroyed elsewhere
- Though for time being different ideals represented by hostile nations fighting for existence, must not forget: **conflict has grown out of struggle of ideas within what, not so long ago, was a common European civilization**
- Tendencies which have culminated in creation of totalitarian systems were not confined to countries which have succumbed to them

### Germany, Italy, Russia as Products of Shared Thought

- Somewhat difficult to think of Germany, Italy, or Russia not as different worlds but as **products of development of thought in which we have shared**
- At least so far as our enemies are concerned, easier and more comforting to think they are entirely different from us and what happened there cannot happen here
- Yet history of these countries in years before rise of totalitarian system showed few features with which we are not familiar
- **External conflict is result of transformation of European thought** in which others have moved so much faster as to bring them into irreconcilable conflict with our ideals, but which has not left us unaffected

### The Unforeseeable Results of Ideas

- That **change of ideas and force of human will have made the world what it is now**, though men did not foresee results
- That no spontaneous change in facts obliged us to adapt our thought is perhaps particularly difficult for Anglo-Saxon nations to see
- Just because in this development they have, fortunately for them, **lagged behind** most European peoples
- Still think of ideals which have guided us for past generation as ideals only to be realized in future
- Not aware how far in last 25 years they have already transformed not only world but also our own countries

### The Illusion of Recent Governance

- Still believe that until quite recently we were governed by what are vaguely called **19th-century ideas** or principle of laissez faire
- Compared with some other countries, and from point of view of those impatient to speed up change, there may be some justification for such belief
- But although until 1931 England and America had followed only slowly on path on which others had led, **even by then they had moved so far** that only those whose memory goes back to years before last war know what a liberal world has been like

### The Magnitude and Direction of Change

- Crucial point of which our people are still so little aware is not merely magnitude of changes which have taken place during last generation
- But the fact that they mean a **complete change in direction of evolution of our ideas and social order**
- For at least 25 years before specter of totalitarianism became real threat, we had progressively been **moving away from basic ideas on which Western civilization has been built**

### The Shock of Totalitarianism

- That this movement on which we have entered with such high hopes and ambitions should have brought us face to face with totalitarian horror has come as **profound shock to this generation**
- Still refuses to connect the two facts
- Yet this development merely **confirms warnings of fathers of liberal philosophy** which we still profess
- Have progressively abandoned that **freedom in economic affairs without which personal and political freedom has never existed in the past**

### The Warning of Tocqueville and Acton

- Although we had been warned by some of greatest political thinkers of 19th century, by **Tocqueville** and **Lord Acton**, that **socialism means slavery**
- We have steadily moved in direction of socialism
- Now that we have seen new form of slavery arise before our eyes, we have **so completely forgotten the warning** that it scarcely occurs to us that the two things may be connected

### The Break with Western Civilization's Evolution

- How sharp a break not only with recent past but with whole evolution of Western civilization the modern trend toward socialism means becomes clear if we consider it not merely against background of 19th century
- But in **longer historical perspective**
- Rapidly abandoning not views merely of **Cobden and Bright**, of **Adam Smith** and **Hume**, or even of **Locke** and **Milton**
- But one of salient characteristics of Western civilization as it has grown from foundations laid by Christianity and Greeks and Romans

### The Assault on Individualism

- Not merely 19th- and 18th-century liberalism, but **basic individualism inherited from Erasmus and Montaigne, from Cicero and Tacitus, Pericles and Thucydides**, is progressively relinquished

### The Counter-Renaissance

- Nazi leader who described National Socialist revolution as **counter-Renaissance** spoke more truly than he probably knew
- Was decisive step in destruction of that civilization which modern man had built up from age of Renaissance
- Which was, above all, an **individualist civilization**
- Individualism has bad name today, term has come to be connected with **egotism and selfishness**
- But individualism of which we speak in contrast to socialism and all other forms of collectivism has **no necessary connection with these**

### The True Meaning of Individualism

- Only gradually in course of this book shall we be able to make clear contrast between two opposing principles
- But essential features of that individualism which, from elements provided by Christianity and philosophy of classical antiquity, was first fully developed during Renaissance and has since grown into what we know as Western civilization — are:
  - **Respect for individual man qua man** — recognition of his own views and tastes as supreme in his own sphere, however narrowly circumscribed
  - Belief it is desirable that men should develop their own individual gifts and bents
- "Freedom" and "liberty" now words so worn with use and abuse that one must hesitate to employ them to express ideals for which they stood during that period
- **"Tolerance"** is perhaps only word which still preserves full meaning of principle
- Which during whole of this period was in ascendant and which only in recent times has again been in decline, **to disappear completely with rise of totalitarian state**

### The Transformation from Hierarchy to Individual Choice

- Gradual transformation of rigidly organized hierarchic system into one where **men could at least attempt to shape their own life**
- Where man gained opportunity of **knowing and choosing between different forms of life**
- Closely associated with **growth of commerce**
- From commercial cities of northern Italy new view of life spread with commerce to west and north
- Through France and southwest of Germany to Low Countries and British Isles
- Taking firm root wherever there was no despotic political power to stifle it

### Commerce and Individual Liberty

- In Low Countries and Britain it for long time enjoyed its fullest development
- For first time had opportunity to **grow freely** and to become foundation of social and political life of these countries
- From there in late 17th and 18th centuries it again began to spread in more fully developed form to West and East, to New World and to center of European continent
- Where devastating wars and political oppression had largely submerged earlier beginnings of similar growth

### The Direction of Social Development

- During whole of this modern period of European history the general direction of social development was one of **freeing individual from ties which had bound him** to customary or prescribed ways in pursuit of his ordinary activities
- Conscious realization that **spontaneous and uncontrolled efforts of individuals were capable of producing complex order** of economic activities could come only after this development had made some progress
- Subsequent elaboration of consistent argument in favor of economic freedom was outcome of free growth of economic activity which had been **undesigned and unforeseen by-product of political freedom**

### The Marvelous Growth of Science

- Perhaps greatest result of unchaining of individual energies was **marvelous growth of science** which followed march of individual liberty from Italy to England and beyond
- That inventive faculty of man had been no less in earlier periods is shown by many highly ingenious automatic toys and other mechanical contrivances constructed while industrial technique still remained stationary
- By development in some industries which, like mining or watch-making, were not subject to restrictive controls
- But few attempts toward more extended industrial use of mechanical inventions, some extraordinarily advanced, were promptly suppressed
- Desire for knowledge was stifled, so long as dominant views were held to be binding for all
- Beliefs of great majority on what was right and proper were allowed to bar way of individual innovator

### Industrial Freedom and Scientific Progress

- Only since industrial freedom opened path to free use of new knowledge
- Only since **everything could be tried — if somebody could be found to back it at his own risk** — and, it should be added, as often as not from outside authorities officially entrusted with cultivation of learning
- Has science made great strides which in last 150 years have changed face of world

### The Enemy's Recognition of Western Individualism

- As is so often true, nature of our civilization has been seen more clearly by its enemies than by most of its friends
- **"The perennial Western malady, the revolt of the individual against the species"** as that 19th-century totalitarian, **Auguste Comte**, has described it
- Was indeed the force which built our civilization
- What 19th century added to individualism of preceding period was merely to make all classes conscious of freedom
- To develop systematically and continuously what had grown in haphazard and patchy manner
- To spread it from England and Holland over most of European continent

### The Unprecedented Results

- Result of this growth surpassed all expectations
- Wherever barriers to free exercise of human ingenuity were removed, man became rapidly able to satisfy ever widening ranges of desire
- While rising standard soon led to discovery of very dark spots in society, spots which men were no longer willing to tolerate
- There was probably **no class that did not substantially benefit from general advance**
- Cannot do justice to this astonishing growth if we measure it by our present standards (which themselves result from this growth and now make many defects obvious)

### The Success Beyond Wildest Dreams

- To appreciate what it meant to those who took part in it, must measure it by hopes and wishes men held when it began
- There can be no doubt that its success surpassed man's wildest dreams
- That by beginning of 20th century the **workingman in Western world had reached degree of material comfort, security, and personal independence** which hundred years before had seemed scarcely possible

### The New Sense of Power

- What in future will probably appear most significant and far-reaching effect of this success is **new sense of power over their own fate**
- Belief in unbounded possibilities of improving their own lot which success already achieved created among men
- With success grew ambition — and **man had every right to be ambitious**
- What had been inspiring promise seemed no longer enough
- Rate of progress far too slow
- Principles which had made this progress possible in past came to be regarded more as **obstacles to speedier progress**, impatiently to be brushed away
- Than as **conditions for preservation and development** of what had already been achieved

### Liberalism Not a Stationary Creed

- Nothing in basic principles of liberalism to make it stationary creed
- No hard-and-fast rules fixed once and for all
- Fundamental principle that in ordering of our affairs we should make **as much use as possible of spontaneous forces of society**
- Resort **as little as possible to coercion**, is capable of infinite variety of applications
- All difference between deliberately creating system within which competition will work as beneficially as possible
- And passively accepting institutions as they are
- Probably nothing has done so much harm to liberal cause as wooden insistence of some liberals on certain rough rules of thumb
- Above all principle of laissez faire
- Yet, in sense, this was necessary and unavoidable

### The Need for Rough Rules

- Against innumerable interests which could show that particular measures would confer immediate and obvious benefits on some
- While harm they caused was much more indirect and difficult to see
- Nothing short of some hard-and-fast rule would have been effective
- Since strong presumption in favor of industrial liberty had undoubtedly been established
- Temptation to present it as rule which knew no exceptions was too strong always to be resisted

## Chapter 1 (continued): The Abandoned Road

### The Paradox of Liberal Success

- Liberal progress was necessarily slow, requiring gradual improvement of institutional frameworks through deepening understanding of social forces
- The liberal attitude toward society resembled a gardener tending a plant—one must understand structure and function before effectively nurturing growth
- Nineteenth-century economic policy principles were admittedly crude, requiring continued refinement in areas like monetary systems and monopoly control
- **The irony:** liberalism's very success became the cause of its decline—as prosperity increased, people grew impatient with remaining evils and unwilling to tolerate what now seemed both unbearable and unnecessary

> "It might even be said that the very success of liberalism became the cause of its decline. Because of the success already achieved, man became increasingly unwilling to tolerate the evils still with him which now appeared both unbearable and unnecessary."

### The Erosion of Liberal Belief

- By turn of the century, belief in basic tenets of liberalism was increasingly relinquished
- Past achievements came to be seen as secure and imperishable possessions, acquired once and for all
- People fixated on new demands, whose rapid satisfaction seemed barred by adherence to old principles
- The shift: from adding to or improving existing machinery to **completely scrapping and replacing it**
- As hope centered on something entirely new, interest in understanding how the free system actually worked declined—along with awareness of what depended on its existence

### The Engineering Mindset and Social Problems

- Hayek criticizes the uncritical transfer of habits from technological problems to social problems
- The natural scientist and engineer's habits of thought were imposed on spheres where inappropriate
- These habits discredited past study of society that didn't conform to engineering prejudices and imposed ideals of organization unsuitable to social domains
- **The trend:** complete reversal from individualist tradition that created Western civilization—an entire abandonment of that tradition

### Germany as Intellectual Center (post-1870)

- Around 1870, the direction of idea-travel reversed: English ideas stopped spreading eastward, began retreating
- From 1870 onward, **Germany became the center** from which ideas destined to govern the twentieth century spread east and west
- Key German thinkers exported: **Hegel, Marx, List, Schmoller, Sombart, Mannheim**—whether socialism in radical form or merely "organization" and "planning"
- German ideas and institutions were readily imported and imitated everywhere

> "For over two hundred years English ideas had been spreading eastward. The rule of freedom which had been achieved in England seemed destined to spread throughout the world. By about 1870 the reign of these ideas had probably reached its easternmost expansion."

- **Karl Mannheim** quoted: "We have never had to set up and direct the whole system of nature as we are forced to do today with society.... Mankind is tending more and more to regulate the whole of its social life, although it has never attempted to create a second nature."

### Germany's Lead in Socialist Development

- Although socialism didn't originate in Germany, it was **perfected there** during last quarter of nineteenth and first quarter of twentieth century
- Germany had considerable lead in development of theory and practice of socialism
- A generation before socialism became serious issue elsewhere, Germany had large socialist party in parliament
- Most doctrinal development carried on in Germany and Austria—even Russian discussion largely continues where Germans left off
- Most English and American socialists remain unaware that problems they're discovering were thoroughly discussed by German socialists long ago

### The Intellectual Influence and Its Ironies

- German influence supported by extraordinary reputation German thinkers had earned during preceding hundred years when Germany was integral member of common European civilization
- Soon served to assist spreading German ideas **directed against foundations of that civilization**
- Germans spreading these ideas were fully aware of the conflict: what had been common heritage of European civilization became to them "Western" civilization—where "Western" meant west of the Rhine
- "Western" in this new sense meant liberalism, democracy, capitalism, individualism, free trade, internationalism, peace

### Western Self-Doubt and German Import

- Despite growing German contempt for "shallow" Western ideals, West continued importing German ideas
- People of the West were induced to believe their own former convictions had merely been rationalizations of selfish interests
- Free trade portrayed as doctrine invented to further British interests
- Political ideals of England and America dismissed as hopelessly outmoded and shameful

## Chapter 2: The Great Utopia

> "What has always made the state a hell on earth has been precisely that man has tried to make it his heaven." —**Hölderlin**

### Socialism's Displacement of Liberalism

- Socialism displacing liberalism as doctrine of progressives doesn't mean people forgot warnings of great liberal thinkers about collectivism's consequences
- Extraordinary thing: **same socialism** recognized as gravest threat to freedom quite openly began as reaction against liberalism of French Revolution, yet gained acceptance under flag of liberty
- Rarely remembered now that socialism in beginnings was frankly authoritarian
- French writers who laid foundations of modern socialism had no doubt ideas could only be put into practice by strong dictatorial government

### Early Socialist Authoritarianism

- To early socialists, socialism meant attempt to "terminate the revolution" by deliberate reorganization of society on hierarchical lines and imposition of coercive "spiritual power"
- Where freedom was concerned, founders made no bones about intentions
- Freedom of thought regarded as root-evil of nineteenth-century society
- First of modern planners, **Saint-Simon**, predicted those who didn't obey his proposed planning boards would be "treated as cattle"

### Democratic Socialism's Emergence

- Only under influence of strong democratic currents preceding revolution of 1848 did socialism begin allying itself with forces of freedom
- Took new "democratic socialism" long time to live down suspicions aroused by antecedents
- Nobody saw more clearly than **Tocqueville** that democracy as essentially individualist institution stood in irreconcilable conflict with socialism

> "Democracy extends the sphere of individual freedom; socialism restricts it. Democracy attaches all possible value to each man; socialism makes each man a mere agent, a mere number. Democracy and socialism have nothing in common but one word: equality. But notice the difference: while democracy seeks equality in liberty, socialism seeks equality in restraint and servitude." —**Tocqueville** (1848)

### The Promise of Freedom

- To allay suspicions and harness craving for freedom, socialism increasingly made use of promise of "new freedom"
- Coming of socialism was to be leap from realm of necessity to realm of freedom
- Was to bring "economic freedom" without which political freedom already gained was "not worth having"
- Only socialism capable of effecting consummation of age-long struggle for freedom

### The Redefinition of Freedom

- Subtle change in meaning of word "freedom" important for making argument sound plausible
- To great apostles of political freedom, word meant freedom from coercion, freedom from arbitrary power of other men, release from ties leaving individual no choice but obedience
- New freedom promised was to be freedom from necessity, release from compulsion of circumstances inevitably limiting range of choice
- Before man could be truly free, "despotism of physical want" had to be broken, "restraints of economic system" relaxed

### Freedom as Power

- Freedom in this new sense merely another name for power or wealth
- Yet promises of new freedom often coupled with irresponsible promises of great increase in material wealth in socialist society
- Not from absolute conquest of niggardliness of nature was economic freedom expected
- **Real promise:** great existing disparities in range of choice of different people were to disappear
- Demand for new freedom thus only another name for old demand for equal distribution of wealth
- New name gave socialists another word in common with liberals, which they exploited to the full
- Although word used in different sense by two groups, few noticed and still fewer asked whether two kinds of freedom could really be combined

### Socialist Propaganda and the Belief in Freedom

- No doubt promise of greater freedom has become one of most effective weapons of socialist propaganda
- Belief that socialism would bring freedom is genuine and sincere
- Would only heighten tragedy if it proved what was promised was Road to Freedom but in fact High Road to Servitude
- Promise of more freedom responsible for luring more and more liberals along socialist road, blinding them to conflict between basic principles of socialism and liberalism
- Often enabling socialists to usurp very name of old party of freedom
- Not surprising idea of socialism's leading to opposite of liberty should appear inconceivable to intelligentsia who embraced it as apparent heir of liberal tradition

### Recent Warnings and Unexpected Testimonies

- In recent years, old apprehensions of unforeseen consequences of socialism have been strongly voiced from most unexpected quarters
- Observer after observer impressed with extraordinary similarity in many respects of conditions under "fascism" and "communism"
- More and more people began asking whether these new tyrannies were not outcome of same tendencies

### Max Eastman on Stalinism

- Even communists shaken by testimonies like that of **Max Eastman**, Lenin's old friend
- Found himself compelled to admit "instead of being better, Stalinism is worse than fascism, more ruthless, barbarous, unjust, immoral, antidemocratic, unredeemed by any hope or scruple"
- "Better described as superfascist"
- When same author recognizes **Stalinism *is* socialism** in sense of being inevitable although unforeseen political accompaniment of nationalization and collectivization he had relied upon as part of plan for erecting classless society, conclusion achieves wider significance

### W.H. Chamberlin's Verdict

- **W.H. Chamberlin**, twelve years in Russia as American correspondent, saw all ideals shattered
- Summed up conclusions from studies in Russia, Germany, and Italy: "socialism is certain to prove, in the beginning at least, the road NOT to freedom, but to dictatorship and counter-dictatorships, to civil war of the fiercest kind. Socialism achieved and maintained by democratic means seems definitely to belong to the world of utopias."

### F.A. Voigt on Marxism and Fascism

- **F.A. Voigt**, British writer, many years observing developments in Europe as foreign correspondent
- Concluded "Marxism has led to Fascism and National Socialism, because, in all essentials, it IS Fascism and National Socialism."

### Walter Lippmann on Planned Society

- **Walter Lippmann**: "the generation to which we belong is now learning from experience what happens when men retreat from freedom to a coercive organization of their affairs. Though they promise themselves a more abundant life, they must in practice renounce it; as organized direction increases, variety of ends must give way to uniformity. That is the nemesis of the planned society and the authoritarian principle in human affairs."

### Peter Drucker on Marxism's Failure

- **Peter Drucker**: "The complete collapse of the belief in the attainability of freedom and equality through Marxism has forced Russia to travel the same road toward a totalitarian, purely negative, non-economic society of unfreedom and inequality which Germany has been following. Not that communism and fascism are essentially the same. Fascism is the stage reached after communism has proved an illusion, and it has proved as much an illusion in Stalinist Russia as in pre-Hitler Germany."

### The Intellectual History of Nazi and Fascist Leaders

- Significant is intellectual history of many Nazi and Fascist leaders
- Everyone watching growth of these movements in Italy or Germany struck by number of leading men who began as socialists and ended as Fascists or Nazis
- From **Mussolini** downward (not excluding **Laval** and **Quisling**), many began as socialists and ended as Fascists or Nazis
- True of leaders and even more true of rank and file
- Relative ease with which young communist could be converted into Nazi or vice versa was generally known in Germany, best of all to propagandists of the two parties

### The Common Enemy and Shared Characteristics

- True that in Germany before 1933, and Italy before 1922, communists and Nazis/Fascists clashed more frequently with each other than with other parties
- Competed for support of same type of mind, reserved for each other hatred of heretic
- Practice showed how closely related they are
- To both, real enemy the man with whom they had nothing in common and whom they could not hope to convince—the liberal of old type
- While to Nazi the communist, and to communist the Nazi, and to both the socialist, are potential recruits made of right timber (though listened to false prophets), both know there can be no compromise between them and those who really believe in individual freedom

### Eduard Heimann on "The Rediscovery of Liberalism"

- Professor **Eduard Heimann**, one of leaders of German religious socialism: "Hitlerism proclaims itself as both true democracy and true socialism, and terrible truth is there is grain of truth for such claims—infinitesimal grain, to be sure, but at any rate enough to serve as basis for fantastic distortions"
- "Hitlerism even goes so far as to claim role of protector of Christianity, and terrible truth is even this gross misinterpretation is able to make some impression"
- "But one fact stands out with perfect clarity in all the fog: **Hitler has never claimed to represent true liberalism. Liberalism then has the distinction of being the doctrine most hated by Hitler.**"
- Hatred had little occasion to show itself in practice merely because, by time Hitler came to power, liberalism was to all intents and purposes dead in Germany
- **It was socialism that had killed it**

### The Connection and Democratic Belief

- While to many who watched transition from socialism to fascism at close quarters the connection between two systems has become increasingly obvious, in democracies majority still believe socialism and freedom can be combined
- So little is problem yet seen, so easily do most irreconcilable ideals still live together, that we can hear contradictions like "individualist socialism" seriously discussed

### The Task Ahead

- If this is state of mind drifting us into new world, nothing can be more urgent than seriously examining real significance of evolution that has taken place elsewhere
- Although conclusions will only confirm apprehensions others have already expressed, reasons why this development cannot be regarded as accidental will not appear without rather full examination of main aspects of transformation of social life
- **Democratic socialism, the great utopia of the last few generations, is not only unachievable, but that to strive for it produces something so utterly different that few of those who now wish it would be prepared to accept the consequences**
- Many will not believe until connection has been laid bare in all its aspects

## Chapter 3: Individualism and Collectivism

> "The socialists believe in two things which are absolutely different and perhaps even contradictory: freedom and organization." —**Élie Halévy**

### The Confusion About Socialism

- Before progressing with main problem, confusion largely responsible for drifting into things nobody wants must be cleared up
- Confusion concerns nothing less than concept of socialism itself
- May mean, and often used to describe, merely ideals of social justice, greater equality, and security—ultimate aims of socialism
- But means also particular method by which most socialists hope to attain these ends and which many competent people regard as only methods by which they can be fully and quickly attained
- In this sense socialism means **abolition of private enterprise, of private ownership of means of production, and creation of system of "planned economy" in which entrepreneur working for profit is replaced by central planning body**

### Two Types of Socialists

- Many call themselves socialists who care only about first, who fervently believe in those ultimate aims of socialism but neither care nor understand how they can be achieved
- Merely certain they must be achieved, whatever the cost
- But to nearly all those to whom socialism is not merely hope but object of practical politics, characteristic methods of modern socialism are as essential as ends themselves
- Many people who value ultimate ends of socialism less than socialists refuse to support socialism because of dangers to other values they see in methods proposed by socialists
- **Dispute about socialism has thus become largely dispute about means and not about ends**

### The Dual Meaning of "Economic Planning"

- Confusion further increased by common practice of denying that those who repudiate the means value the ends
- Situation still more complicated by fact that same means—"economic planning"—can be used for many other purposes
- Must centrally direct economic activity if we want to make distribution of income conform to current ideas of social justice
- "Planning" therefore wanted by all those who demand "production for use" be substituted for production for profit
- But such planning no less indispensable if distribution of incomes is to be regulated in way which appears opposite of just
- Whether we wish more good things go to some racial elite, Nordic men, or members of party or aristocracy, methods we shall have to employ are same as those which could insure egalitarian distribution

### Socialism and Collectivism

- May seem unfair to use term "socialism" to describe methods rather than aims, to use for particular method a term which for many stands for ultimate ideal
- Probably preferable to describe methods which can be used for great variety of ends as **collectivism** and to regard socialism as species of that genus
- Yet although to most socialists only one species of collectivism will represent true socialism, must always be remembered socialism is species of collectivism and therefore everything which is true of collectivism as such must apply also to socialism
- Nearly all points disputed between socialists and liberals concern methods common to all forms of collectivism and not particular ends for which socialists want to use them
- All consequences with which we shall be concerned follow from methods of collectivism irrespective of ends for which they are used
- Must also not be forgotten that socialism is not only by far most important species of collectivism or "planning" but that it is socialism which has persuaded liberal-minded people to submit once more to that regimentation of economic life which they had overthrown because, in words of **Adam Smith**, it puts governments in position where "to support themselves they are obliged to be oppressive and tyrannical"

### The Meaning of "Collectivism"

- Difficulties not yet over if we agree to use term "collectivism" so as to include all types of "planned economy," whatever end of planning
- Meaning becomes somewhat more definite if we make clear we mean that sort of planning which is necessary to realize any given distributive ideals
- But as idea of central economic planning owes appeal largely to very vagueness of meaning, essential we should agree on precise sense before discussing consequences

### "Planning" in Common Usage

- "Planning" owes popularity largely to fact everybody desires we should handle common problems as rationally as possible and that, in so doing, we should use as much foresight as we can command
- In this sense everybody who is not complete fatalist is planner, every political act is (or ought to be) act of planning, and there can be differences only between good and bad, between wise and foresighted and foolish and shortsighted planning
- Economist, whose whole task is study of how men actually do and how they might plan their affairs, is last person who could object to planning in this general sense
- **But it is not in this sense that our enthusiasts for planned society now employ this term**, nor merely in this sense that we must plan if we want distribution of income or wealth to conform to some particular standard

### The Liberal vs. The Collectivist Plan

- According to modern planners, and for their purposes, it is not sufficient to design most rational permanent framework within which various activities would be conducted by different persons according to their individual plans
- This liberal plan, according to them, is no plan—and indeed, not plan designed to satisfy particular views about who should have what
- What our planners demand is **central direction of all economic activity according to single plan, laying down how resources of society should be "consciously directed" to serve particular ends in definite way**

### The Core Dispute

- Dispute between modern planners and opponents is therefore *not* dispute on whether we ought to choose intelligently between various possible organizations of society
- Not dispute on whether we ought to employ foresight and systematic thinking in planning our common affairs
- **Dispute about what is best way of so doing**
- Question is whether for this purpose it is better that holder of coercive power should confine himself in general to creating conditions under which knowledge and initiative of individuals are given best scope so that *they* can plan most successfully
- Or whether rational utilization of our resources requires *central* direction and organization of all our activities according to some consciously constructed "blueprint"
- Socialists of all parties have appropriated term "planning" for planning of latter type, and it is now generally accepted in this sense
- Though meant to suggest this is only rational way of handling our affairs, it does not, of course, prove this
- Remains point on which planners and liberals disagree

### Opposition to Central Planning is Not Laissez Faire

- Important not to confuse opposition against this kind of planning with dogmatic *laissez faire* attitude
- Liberal argument is in favor of making best possible use of forces of competition as means of coordinating human efforts, not argument for leaving things just as they are
- Based on conviction that, where effective competition can be created, it is better way of guiding individual efforts than any other
- Does not deny, but even emphasizes, that in order competition should work beneficially, carefully thought-out legal framework is required and that neither existing nor past legal rules are free from grave defects
- Nor does it deny that where impossible to create conditions necessary to make competition effective, we must resort to other methods of guiding economic activity
- Economic liberalism is opposed, however, to competition's being supplanted by inferior methods of coordinating individual efforts
- Regards competition as superior not only because it is in most circumstances most efficient method known but even more because it is only method by which our activities can be adjusted to each other without coercive or arbitrary intervention of authority
- Indeed, one of main arguments in favor of competition is that it dispenses with need for "conscious social control" and that it gives individuals chance to decide whether prospects of particular occupation are sufficient to compensate for disadvantages and risks connected with it

### Competition and Coercion

- Successful use of competition as principle of social organization precludes certain types of coercive interference with economic life
- But admits of others which sometimes may very considerably assist its work and even requires certain kinds of government action
- But there is good reason why negative requirements, points where coercion must not be used, have been particularly stressed
- Necessary in first instance that parties in market should be free to sell and buy at any price at which they can find partner to transaction and that anybody should be free to produce, sell, and buy anything that may be produced or sold at all
- Essential that entry into different trades should be open to all on equal terms and that law should not tolerate any attempts by individuals or groups to restrict this entry by open or concealed force
- Any attempt to control prices or quantities of particular commodities deprives competition of its power of bringing about effective coordination of individual efforts, because price changes then cease to register all relevant changes in circumstances and no longer provide reliable guide for individual's actions

### Restrictions on Methods of Production Compatible with Competition

- Not necessarily true, however, of measures merely restricting allowed methods of production, so long as these restrictions affect all potential producers equally and are not used as indirect way of controlling prices and quantities
- Though all such controls of methods of production impose extra costs (i.e., make it necessary to use more resources to produce given output), they may be well worth while
- To prohibit use of certain poisonous substances or to require special precautions in their use, to limit working hours or to require certain sanitary arrangements, is fully compatible with preservation of competition
- Only question here is whether in particular instance advantages gained are greater than social costs which they impose
- Nor is preservation of competition incompatible with extensive system of social services—so long as organization of these services is not designed in such way as to make competition ineffective over wide fields

### Positive Requirements for Competition

- Regrettable, though not difficult to explain, that in past much less attention has been given to positive requirements of successful working of competitive system than to these negative points
- Functioning of competition not only requires adequate organization of certain institutions like money, markets, and channels of information—some of which can never be adequately provided by private enterprise
- But depends, above all, on existence of appropriate legal system, legal system designed both to preserve competition and to make it operate as beneficially as possible
- By no means sufficient that law should recognize principle of private property and freedom of contract; much depends on precise definition of right of property as applied to different things
- Systematic study of forms of legal institutions which will make competitive system work efficiently has been sadly neglected
- Strong arguments can be advanced that serious shortcomings here, particularly with regard to law of corporations and of patents, not only have made competition work much less effectively than it might have done but have even led to destruction of competition in many spheres

## Chapter 3 (continued): Individualism and Economic Order

### When Competition Needs Supplementing

- Some fields lack conditions for effective competition—where benefits/damages can't be effectively priced
- Examples: signposts, roads themselves, harmful effects of deforestation, smoke/noise from factories
- Where price mechanism fails, **direct regulation by authority** becomes necessary
- But this doesn't prove we should suppress competition where it *can* work

### The Proper Role of State Activity

> "To create conditions in which competition will be as effective as possible, to supplement it where it cannot be made effective, to provide the services which, in the words of Adam Smith, 'though they may be in the highest degree advantageous to a great society, are, however, of such a nature, that the profit could never repay the expense to any individual or small number of individuals'—these tasks provide, indeed, a wide and unquestioned field for state activity." (**Adam Smith**)

- Competition requires an **intelligently designed legal framework**—not laissez-faire
- Preventing fraud and deception remains "by no means yet fully accomplished"
- No rational system would have the state do nothing

### The Turn Against Competition

- Framework for competition was incomplete when states abandoned it for a **different principle**
- Question shifted from "making competition work" to "displacing it altogether"
- Modern planning movement is **against competition as such**—a rallying flag for old enemies of competition
- **Socialist propaganda** has made planning respectable among liberals, dulling suspicion of anti-competitive schemes
- What unites Left and Right socialists: **common hostility to competition** and desire for directed economy
- Terms "capitalism" and "socialism" obscure rather than clarify the transition

### The Intermediate Stage: Syndicalism

- Universal struggle against competition produces something worse than either planning or liberalism
- **Syndicalist/corporative organization**: competition suppressed, planning left to **independent monopolies** of separate industries
- People united in hostility to competition but agree on little else
- This destroys competition industry by industry, putting consumers at mercy of **joint monopolist action** of capitalists and workers
- Though this state exists in many fields, it's unstable and unjustified
- Independent planning by monopolies produces effects **opposite to planning's aims**
- Only alternative: **state control of monopolies**—which must be increasingly complete and detailed

### Britain's Pre-War Drift

- A 1939 weekly magazine noted British leaders were "growing accustomed to thinking in terms of national development by controlled monopolies"
- War greatly accelerated this process
- Grave defects and dangers increasingly obvious

### The Appeal of the Middle Way

- Complete centralization still appalls most people due to difficulty and horror of total direction
- People still believe in a **middle way** between atomistic competition and central direction
- Nothing seems more plausible than "judicious mixture" of competition and planning
- **But competition cannot be combined with planning** to any extent without ceasing to operate as effective guide
- "Planning" in small doses can't produce thoroughgoing effects
- Both become poor, inefficient tools if incomplete
- Result worse than either system used consistently
- **Planning and competition can be combined only by planning *for* competition, not planning *against* competition**

### Critical Distinction

- The book's criticism is directed solely at **planning against competition**—planning to be substituted for competition
- As "planning" has become synonymous with the former, Hayek will use the term simply as planning
- Notes this leaves opponents a good word meriting better fate

- Hayek's paper "Socialist Calculation: The Competitive 'Solution'" in *Economica* (1940)—critiques "competitive socialism" proposals by **H. D. Dickinson** and **Oskar Lange**/**Fred M. Taylor**

## Chapter 4: The "Inevitability" of Planning

> "We were the first to assert that the more complicated the forms assumed by civilization, the more restricted the freedom of the individual must become." —**Benito Mussolini**

### The Myth of Technological Inevitability

- Few planners claim central planning is desirable—most say we have **no choice**
- Myth: competition spontaneously eliminated by technological changes beyond our control
- This argument rarely developed at length, taken as established fact by repetition
- **But it's devoid of foundation**—tendency toward monopoly is product of **opinions fostered for half a century**, not objective facts

### The Concentration of Industry Doctrine

- Most frequent argument: technological changes made competition impossible in increasing fields
- Only choice: control by private monopolies vs. government
- Derives from **Marxist doctrine** of "concentration of industry"
- Now found in circles unaware of its Marxist origins

### Historical Evidence Against Inevitability

- Growth of monopoly over last 50 years is historical fact
- But is it **necessary consequence** of technology or result of **policies pursued**?
- **Temporary National Economic Committee** study on *Concentration of Economic Power*:
  - Greater efficiency of large-scale production "finds scant support in any evidence that is now at hand"
  - Detailed monograph: advantages of large establishments not demonstrated
  - Economies of size exist invariably, but optimum efficiency reached long before major supply subjected to control
  - **Monopoly frequently product of factors other than lower costs**
  - Attained through collusive agreement, promoted by public policies
  - When these agreements invalidated/policies reversed, **competitive conditions can be restored**

### Historical Timing Reveals Policy, Not Technology

- If technological, monopoly growth should appear first in most advanced economies
- **Actually appeared first** in comparatively young industrial countries: **United States and Germany** (late 19th century)
- In **Germany** especially (model country of "necessary evolution of capitalism"):
  - Growth of cartels/syndicates since 1878 **systematically fostered by deliberate policy**
  - Not only protection but direct inducements and ultimately **compulsion** used
  - First great experiment in "scientific planning" and "conscious organization of industry"
  - Created giant monopolies represented as inevitable 50 years before same done in Britain
  - Due to influence of **German socialist theoreticians**, particularly **Sombart**
  - "Inevitable development" of competitive system into "monopoly capitalism" became widely accepted

### Germany vs. United States

- US protectionist policy made similar development possible, seeming to confirm generalization
- But **Germany's development came to be regarded as representative** of universal tendency
- Became commonplace to speak—quoting **Reinhold Niebuhr**—of "Germany where all the social and political forces of modern civilization have reached their most advanced form"

### The British Case Proves Deliberate Policy

- Little inevitability evident; result of **deliberate policy** becomes clear examining England
- Until 1931 and **general protection** policy, British industry as competitive as any time in history
- Despite incompatible wage/money policies in 1920s, years up to 1929 compare favorably with 1930s for employment/activity
- Only since transition to protection has monopoly growth proceeded at amazing rate, transforming British industry
- To argue technological progress during this period created necessities is absurd
- Technological necessities operating in Germany in 1880s/1890s made themselves felt in Britain in 1930s—"not much less absurd than the claim... that Italy had to abolish individual freedom before other European people because its civilization had marched so far in advance"

### Theory vs. Practice in England

- Thesis that change in opinion/policy merely follows facts could be given appearance of truth
- Nation followed at distance intellectual developments elsewhere
- Could argue monopolistic organization grew despite public opinion favoring competition
- **But in Germany**: deliberate policy, undertaken in service of planning ideal
- Germans (and imitators) merely following course 19th-century thinkers, particularly Germans, mapped out
- Intellectual history of last 60-80 years is **perfect illustration** that in social evolution **nothing is inevitable but thinking makes it so**

### Alternative Interpretation: Complexity Argument

- Modern technological progress makes planning inevitable could mean:
- **Complexity of modern civilization** creates new problems inadequately solved by competition
- Modern town problems (e.g., public utilities) not uppermost in planners' minds
- What they suggest: increasing difficulty of obtaining **coherent picture** of complete economic process makes **coordination by central agency** indispensable to prevent chaos

### Misapprehension of Competition's Function

- Argument based on **complete misapprehension** of how competition works
- Far from appropriate only to simple conditions, complexity of division of labor under modern conditions makes **competition the only method** of adequate coordination
- Efficient control/planning would be no difficulty if single person/board could survey all relevant facts
- Only as factors become so numerous that **synoptic view impossible** does decentralization become imperative
- Once decentralization necessary, problem of **coordination arises**
- Leaves separate agencies free to adjust to facts only they know, yet brings mutual adjustment
- Because nobody can consciously balance all considerations, coordination effected by **arrangements conveying information**—what's required is apparatus of registration automatically recording effects of individual actions
- This apparatus is the **price system** under competition

### Price System as Information Network

- Price system enables entrepreneurs to adjust activities by watching movement of comparatively few prices
- Engineer watching hands of few dials adjusts to those of fellows
- Price system fulfills function **only if competition prevails**—individual producer must adapt to price changes, cannot control them
- More complicated the whole, more dependent on division of knowledge coordinated by impersonal mechanism
- The **price system** is this mechanism for transmitting relevant information

### Competition vs. Conscious Planning

- No exaggeration: if relied on conscious central planning, industrial system would never have reached differentiation, complexity, flexibility attained
- Compared with decentralization plus automatic coordination, central direction is **incredibly clumsy, primitive, limited in scope**
- Division of labor reached extent making modern civilization possible not because consciously created but because **man stumbled on method** by which division could be extended far beyond limits of planning
- Further growth of complexity makes it **more important than ever** to use technique not dependent on conscious control

### Technology Requires Monopoly Argument

- Another theory connects monopoly growth with technology, using **opposite arguments**
- Contends not that modern technique destroys competition but that **impossible to use new possibilities unless monopoly conferred**
- Argument not necessarily fraudulent—critical reader will suspect
- Often used merely as special pleading by interested parties
- Even more often based on confusion between technical excellence from narrow engineering viewpoint and desirability from society's viewpoint

### Limited Force of the Argument

- Some instances where argument has force
- Conceivable British automobile industry might supply cheaper/better car if everyone made to use same kind
- Or electricity for all purposes cheaper than coal/gas if everybody made to use only electricity
- In such instances **at least possible** we might be better off preferring no choice
- But **no individual ever gets choice**—alternative is either all same cheap car (or only electricity) or choice at much higher price
- Whether true in instances given unknown
- Must be admitted **possible** that by compulsory standardization or prohibition of variety beyond certain degree, abundance might be increased sufficiently to compensate for restriction of consumer choice
- Even conceivable new invention beneficial but could be used only if many/all made to avail themselves at same time

### Freedom vs. Immediate Gain

- Whether such instances of great/lasting importance, certainly not instances where legitimately claimed **technical progress makes central direction inevitable**
- Would merely make necessary to choose between gaining particular advantage by compulsion and not obtaining it
- Or in other instances, obtaining later when technical advance overcome difficulties
- True in such situations **may have to sacrifice possible immediate gain** as price of freedom
- But avoid necessity of making future developments dependent upon knowledge particular people now possess
- By sacrificing possible present advantages, **preserve important stimulus to further progress**
- Though short run price for variety/freedom may be high, **in long run material progress will depend on this very variety**

### Technology as Power, Not Compulsion

- In much current discussion, technological progress presented as if **outside us** compelling use in particular way
- True inventions given tremendous power
- **Absurd** to suggest must use power to destroy most precious inheritance: **liberty**
- Means if want to preserve it, **must guard more jealously than ever**, prepared to make sacrifices
- Nothing in modern technological developments forces toward comprehensive planning
- Great deal in them makes infinitely more dangerous the power planning authority would possess

### Why Experts Support Planning

- Movement toward planning result of deliberate action, no external necessities force us
- Worth inquiring why so large proportion of **technical experts** in front rank of planners
- Explanation closely connected with important fact critics should keep in mind: **little question almost every technical ideal could be realized within comparatively short time if made sole aim of humanity**
- Infinite number of good things highly desirable and possible, but cannot hope to achieve more than few within lifetime
- Or can hope to achieve only very imperfectly
- **Frustration of ambitions in own field** makes specialist revolt against existing order
- All find it difficult to see things left undone which everybody must admit both desirable and possible
- Cannot all be done at same time
- Can be achieved only at sacrifice of others
- Falls outside any specialism
- Can be appreciated only by painful intellectual effort
- More painful/more difficult as forces to see against wider background objects to which most labors directed, balance against others outside immediate interest

### Every Specialist a Potential Planner

- Every one of many things which in isolation would be possible to achieve in planned society creates **enthusiasts for planning**
- Feel confident they will be able to instill into directors their sense of value of particular objective
- Hopes of some undoubtedly fulfilled—planned society would further some objectives more than present
- Foolish to deny instances of planned/semiplanned societies furnish illustrations of good things owed entirely to planning
- Magnificent motor roads in Germany/Italy instance often quoted (though not kind of planning not equally possible in liberal society)
- **Equally foolish** to quote such instances of technical excellence in particular fields as evidence of general superiority of planning
- More correct to say such extreme technical excellence **out of line with general conditions is evidence of misdirection of resources**
- Anyone driven along famous German motor roads and found amount of traffic less than on many secondary road in England can have little doubt that, so far as peace purposes concerned, little justification for them
- Not case where planners decided in favor of "guns" instead of "butter"
- But by our standards little ground for enthusiasm

### The Illusion of Unified Values

- Illusion of specialist that in planned society he would secure more attention to objectives for which he cares most is **more general phenomenon** than term "specialist" suggests
- In predilections/interests all in some measure specialists
- All think our personal order of values not merely personal but that in free discussion among rational people we would convince others ours is right one
- Lover of countryside wants traditional appearance preserved, blots made by industry removed
- Health enthusiast wants picturesque but unsanitary old cottages cleared
- Motorist wishes countryside cut up by big motor roads
- Efficiency fanatic desires maximum specialization and mechanization
- Idealist for development of personality wants to preserve as many independent craftsmen as possible
- All know aim can be fully achieved **only by planning**—and all want planning for that reason
- But adoption of social planning can only bring out concealed conflict between aims

### Planning's Strength: Frustrated Single-Mindedness

- Movement for planning owes present strength largely to fact that, while in main still ambition, **unites almost all single-minded idealists**
- All men/women devoted lives to single task
- Hopes place in planning result not of comprehensive view of society but of very limited view, often great exaggeration of importance of ends place foremost
- Not to underrate great pragmatic value of this type in free society like ours—makes them subject of just admiration
- But would make very men most anxious to plan society **most dangerous** if allowed to do so
- Most intolerant of planning of others
- From saintly/single-minded idealist to fanatic often but step
- Though resentment of frustrated specialist gives demand for planning strongest impetus, could hardly be more unbearable—and more irrational—world than one in which most eminent specialists in each field allowed to proceed unchecked with realization of ideals
- Nor can "coordination" as some planners imagine become new specialism
- **Economist last to claim** he has knowledge coordinator would need
- Plea for method which effects coordination **without need for omniscient dictator**
- Means precisely retention of impersonal, often unintelligible, checks on individual efforts—those against which all specialists chafe

## Chapter 5: Planning and Democracy

> "The statesman who should attempt to direct private people in what manner they ought to employ their capitals, would not only load himself with a most unnecessary attention, but assume an authority which could safely be trusted to no council and senate whatever, and which would nowhere be so dangerous as in the hands of a man who had folly and presumption enough to fancy himself fit to exercise it." —**Adam Smith**

### Collectivism's Common Feature

- Common feature of all collectivist systems: **deliberate organization of labors of society for definite social goal**
- Present society lacks "conscious" direction toward single aim
- Activities guided by whims/fancies of irresponsible individuals
- Always been main complaint of socialist critics

### The Basic Conflict

- Puts basic issue clearly
- Directs to point where conflict arises between **individual freedom and collectivism**
- Various collectivisms (communism, fascism, etc.) differ in nature of goal toward which they want to direct society's efforts
- All differ from liberalism/individualism in wanting to **organize whole of society** and all resources for unitary end
- Refuse to recognize autonomous spheres where ends of individuals supreme
- Short: **totalitarian** in true sense—describing unexpected but inseparable manifestations of what in theory we call collectivism

### The Problem of Defining "Social Goal"

- "Social goal" or "common purpose" usually vaguely described as "common good," "general welfare," "general interest"
- Does not need much reflection to see **terms have no sufficiently definite meaning** to determine particular course of action
- Welfare/happiness of millions cannot be measured on single scale of less and more
- Welfare of people, like happiness of man, depends on great many things that can be provided in infinite variety of combinations
- Cannot be adequately expressed as single end but only as **hierarchy of ends**
- Comprehensive scale of values in which every need given its place

### No Complete Ethical Code Exists

- To direct all activities according to single plan presupposes **every one of our needs given rank** in order of values which must be complete enough to decide among all courses planner must choose
- Presupposes existence of **complete ethical code** in which all different human values allotted due place
- Conception of complete ethical code unfamiliar, requires imagination to see what involves
- Not in habit of thinking of moral codes as more/less complete
- Fact we constantly choosing between different values without social code prescribing how ought to choose does not surprise us, does not suggest moral code incomplete
- But where all means to be used are **property of society** and to be used in name of society according to unitary plan, "social" view about what ought to be done must guide all decisions
- In such world should soon find **moral code full of gaps**

### The Absence of a Common Moral Framework

- Not concerned whether would be desirable to have such complete ethical code
- May merely be pointed out: up to present growth of civilization accompanied by **steady diminution of sphere** in which individual actions bound by fixed rules
- Rules of common moral code progressively become fewer and more general in character
- From primitive man (bound by elaborate ritual in almost every daily activity, limited by innumerable taboos, who could scarcely conceive of doing things differently from fellows), morals have more/more tended to become merely **limits circumscribing sphere** within which individual could behave as he liked
- Adoption of common ethical code comprehensive enough to determine unitary economic plan would mean **complete reversal** of this tendency

### Planning Requires Moral Answers That Don't Exist

- Essential point: **no such complete ethical code exists**
- Attempt to direct all economic activity according to single plan would raise innumerable questions to which answer could be provided only by moral rule
- But to which **existing morals have no answer** and where exists no agreed view on what ought to be done
- People will have either no definite views or conflicting views on such questions
- Because in free society we have lived there been no occasion to think about them, still less form common opinions

### The Impossibility of Comprehensive Planning

- Not only do we not possess all-inclusive scale of values: would be **impossible for any mind to comprehend** infinite variety of different needs competing for available resources, attach definite weight to each
- For our problem minor importance whether ends for which any person cares comprehend only his own individual needs or include needs of closer or even more distant fellows
- Whether egoistic or altruistic in ordinary senses
- Point which so important is **basic fact**: **impossible for any man to survey more than limited field**, be aware of urgency of more than limited number of needs
- Whether interests center round own physical needs or whether takes warm interest in welfare of every human being he knows, ends about which he can be concerned will always be only infinitesimal fraction of needs of all men

### Individualism Based on Limited Knowledge

- This fundamental fact on which whole **philosophy of individualism** based
- Does not assume man is egoistic or selfish or ought to be
- Merely starts from indisputable fact that **limits of powers of imagination** make it impossible to include in scale of values more than sector of needs of whole society
- Since scales of value can exist only in individual minds, nothing but **partial scales** of values exist—inevitably different and often inconsistent with each other
- From this individualist concludes individuals should be allowed, within defined limits, to follow own values and preferences rather than somebody else's
- Within these spheres **individual's system of ends should be supreme** and not subject to dictation by others
- This recognition of individual as ultimate judge of his ends, belief that as far as possible own views ought to govern his actions, forms **essence of individualist position**

### Social Ends as Coinciding Individual Ends

- View does not exclude recognition of social ends
- Or rather of coincidence of individual ends which makes it advisable for men to combine for their pursuit
- But limits such common action to instances where **individual views coincide**
- What called "social ends" for it merely identical ends of many individuals—or ends to achievement of which individuals willing to contribute in return for assistance they receive in satisfaction of own desires
- Common action thus limited to fields where people agree on common ends
- Very frequently these common ends will not be ultimate ends to individuals but **means** which different persons can use for different purposes
- People most likely to agree on common action where common end not ultimate end to them but means capable of serving great variety of purposes

### Organizations Have Limited Spheres

- When individuals combine in joint effort to realize ends they have in common, organizations they form (state much more powerful than any others) given **own system of ends** and own means
- But any organization thus formed remains one "person" among others
- In case of state much more powerful than any others, yet still with **separate and limited sphere** in which alone its ends supreme
- Limits of this sphere determined by extent to which individuals agree on particular ends
- Probability they will agree on particular course of action necessarily decreases as scope of such action extends
- Certain functions of state on exercise of which there will be practical unanimity among citizens
- Others on which there will be agreement of substantial majority
- And so on, until come to fields where, although each individual might wish state to act in some way, there will be almost as many views about what government should do as there are different people

### The Limits of Voluntary Agreement

- Can rely on voluntary agreement to guide action of state only so long as confined to spheres where agreement exists
- But not only when state undertakes direct control in fields where there is no such agreement is it bound to suppress individual freedom
- Can unfortunately not indefinitely extend sphere of common action and still leave individual free in own sphere
- Once communal sector (in which state controls all means) exceeds certain proportion of whole, **effects of actions dominate whole system**
- Although state controls directly use of only large part of available resources, effects of decisions on remaining part of economic system become so great that indirectly controls almost everything

## Continuation: Planning and Democracy (pp. 129-135)

### State Control Over Economic Life in Germany

- By 1928, German authorities (central and local) controlled over 53% of
national income directly
- Indirectly controlled almost the entire economic life of the nation
- When the state directs so much economic activity, the "social scale of
values" must encompass practically all individual ends

### The Fatal Flaw of Democratic Planning

- People may agree on adopting planning for "common welfare" without
actually agreeing on what ends the plan should serve
- Agreement exists only on the **mechanism**, not on the **goals**
- Analogy: like agreeing to take a journey together without deciding the
destination—may end up somewhere no one wanted to go
- Planning forces agreement on everything because it cannot confine
collective action to tasks with consensus
- This is the core feature determining the character of a planned system

### Parliament's Inability to Plan

- **Sidney and Beatrice Webb** complained nearly 50 years ago about "the
increased incapacity of the House of Commons to cope with its work"
- **Professor Laski** elaborated: parliamentary machine unsuited to pass
complicated legislation rapidly
- Labour Government would confine Commons to "ventilation of grievances"
and general principles
- Bills would be general formulae giving wide powers to government
departments
- Powers exercised by Order in Council, attacked by vote of no confidence
- **Donoughmore Committee** reaffirmed necessity of delegated legislation
- Laski raised (but left unanswered) whether socialist government can
"risk the overthrow of its measures as a result of the next general
election"

### Why Parliaments Cannot Plan

- The fault is not with representatives but with the task itself
- They are asked to produce agreement on **everything**—the whole
direction of national resources
- System of majority decision unsuited for this
- **No reason why there should be a majority in favor of any one positive
course** when number of alternatives is legion
- Every member might prefer some particular plan to no plan, yet no one
plan may be preferable to a majority

### The Impossibility of Piecemeal Planning

- Cannot achieve coherent plan by voting clause by clause
- Economic plan must have a **unitary conception** to deserve the name
- Even if parliament could step-by-step agree on some scheme, it would
satisfy nobody
- A complex whole with carefully adjusted parts cannot be achieved through
compromise between conflicting views
- As impossible as planning a military campaign by democratic procedure
- Delegation to experts becomes inevitable

### Planning Requires Choosing Between Conflicting Ends

- A general in charge of military campaign has a single end to which all
means must be devoted
- No limitation of means, no need to balance different independent aims
- But the ends of an economic plan **cannot be defined apart from the
particular plan itself**
- The essence of the economic problem: choosing between conflicting or
competing ends—different needs of different people
- Which ends conflict, which must be sacrificed—these are the
**alternatives between which we must choose**
- Only experts who know all the facts can decide which ends to prefer
- They alone can impose their scale of preferences on the community

### The Real Meaning of Delegation

- Not merely technical detail is delegated
- True problem: in economic legislation the interests to be reconciled are
so divergent that no agreement likely in democratic assembly
- Delegation objectionable when it gives power to make arbitrary decisions
in particular cases ("judging the case on its merits")
- This means giving authority power to make what are, to all intents and
purposes, **arbitrary decisions with force of law**

### The Cry for an Economic Dictator

- **Élie Halévy** observed that influential public men in England (Lord
Eustace Percy, Sir Oswald Mosley, Sir Stafford Cripps) all agreed: "We are
living in economic chaos and we cannot get out of it except under some kind
of dictatorial leadership"
- The "composite photograph" has grown considerably since

### Germany Before Hitler

- Before Hitler, Germany had reached a stage where it had to be governed
dictatorially
- Democracy had broken down
- Sincere democrats like **Brüning** no more able to govern democratically
than **Schleicher** or **von Papen**
- Hitler did not destroy democracy; he took advantage of its decay
- At the critical moment, he seemed the only man strong enough to get
things done

### Karl Mannheim's False Reassurance

- **Karl Mannheim** argues that so long as democracy retains ultimate
control, essentials of democracy are not affected
- Claims democratic sovereignty can be boundlessly strengthened by plenary
powers without renouncing democratic control

> "The only way in which a planned society differs from that of the
nineteenth century is that more and more spheres of social life, and
ultimately each and all of them, are subjected to state control. But if a
few controls can be held in check by parliamentary sovereignty, so can
many... In a democratic state sovereignty can be boundlessly strengthened
by plenary powers without renouncing democratic control." —**Karl
Mannheim**

### The Vital Distinction Mannheim Overlooks

- Parliament can control tasks where it has agreed on the aim and merely
delegates working-out of detail
- **Entirely different** when delegation occurs because there is no real
agreement on ends
- When planning body must choose between conflicting ends, parliament is
not even aware of the conflict
- No majority can agree on alternative plan
- Parts objected to can almost always be represented as essential parts of
the whole
- Parliamentary discussion becomes ineffective
- At best reduced to choosing the persons who have practically absolute
power
- Tends toward **plebiscitarian dictatorship**: head of government
confirmed by popular vote but has all powers to ensure vote goes his way

## Chapter Six: Planning and the Rule of Law (pp. 136-148)

### The Rule of Law Defined

> "Nothing distinguishes more clearly conditions in a free country from
those in a country under arbitrary government than the observance in the
former of the great principles known as the Rule of Law." —**Karl
Mannheim**

- Government bound by rules fixed and announced beforehand
- Makes it possible to foresee with fair certainty how authority will use
coercive powers
- Individuals can plan their affairs based on this knowledge
- Discretion left to executive organs wielding coercive power should be
reduced as much as possible
- Under Rule of Law, government prevented from stultifying individual
efforts by **ad hoc** action
- Individual free to pursue personal ends within known rules of the game

### Two Types of Government Action

1. **Permanent framework of laws**: productive activity guided by
individual decisions; government fixes conditions under which resources may
be used
2. **Central direction**: government directs use of means of production to
particular ends

- First type can be made in advance as **formal rules** not aimed at
particular people
- Intended to be useful to unknown people for purposes they will decide
- Long-term rules that cannot be known to assist particular people more
than others
- Like instruments of production helping people predict behavior of those
they collaborate with

### Planning Cannot Be Governed by Formal Rules

- Planning authority cannot confine itself to providing opportunities
- Must provide for actual needs as they arise and choose deliberately
between them
- Cannot tie itself down to general and formal rules preventing
arbitrariness
- Must constantly decide questions by formal principles only
- Must set up distinctions of merit between needs of different people
- Decisions on how many pigs to raise, which coal mines to operate, shoe
prices—these cannot be deduced from formal principles or settled long in
advance
- Depend on circumstances of the moment
- Somebody's views must decide whose interests are more important
- These views become part of the law of the land, **a new distinction of
rank imposed by the coercive apparatus of government**

### Formal Law vs. Substantive Rules

- Distinction is like laying down a Rule of the Road (Highway Code) vs.
ordering people where to go
- Or providing signposts vs. commanding which road to take
- Formal rules tell people in advance what action the state will take in
certain situations, defined in general terms, without reference to time,
place, or particular people
- Refer to typical situations useful for variety of individual purposes
- Knowledge that state will act in definite way provided as a means for
people to use in making their own plans

### Why Formal Rules Must Remain Ignorant of Particular Effects

- We do **not** know their concrete effect
- We do **not** know what particular ends these rules will further or
which particular people they will assist
- They are given the form most likely to benefit all people affected by
them
- This is the most important criterion of formal rules
- No choice between particular ends or particular people
- **We just cannot know beforehand by whom and in what way they will be
used**

### The Rationale of Ignorance

- In our age with passion for conscious control, may seem paradoxical that
we should know less about particular effects under formal rules
- Yet this is the rationale of the great liberal principle of the Rule of
Law
- **The apparent paradox dissolves when we follow the argument further**

### Two Arguments for the Rule of Law

**Economic argument:**
- State should confine itself to establishing rules applying to general
types of situations
- Allow individuals freedom in everything depending on circumstances of
time and place
- Only individuals concerned can fully know these circumstances and adapt
actions to them
- If individuals are to use their knowledge effectively, they must be able
to predict actions of the state
- But if state actions are to be predictable, they must be determined by
rules fixed independently of concrete circumstances
- Cannot be foreseen nor taken into account beforehand
- Particular effects will be unpredictable
- If state were to direct individual's actions to achieve particular ends,
its action would have to be decided on basis of full circumstances of the
moment and therefore be unpredictable
- The more the state "plans," the more difficult planning becomes for the
individual

**Moral/political argument:**
- If state can exactly foresee effects of its actions, it can leave those
affected no choice
- State chooses between different ends
- To create new opportunities open to all, precise results cannot be
foreseen
- General rules must be intended to operate in circumstances which cannot
be foreseen in detail
- Their effect on particular ends or particular people cannot be known
beforehand
- Only in this sense possible for legislator to be impartial
- To be impartial means to have no answer to certain questions—to decide
by tossing a coin
- In a world where everything was precisely foreseen, state could hardly
do anything and remain impartial

### The State Cannot Be Impartial When Effects Are Known

- Where precise effects of government policy on particular people are
known, government cannot be impartial
- Must take sides, impose its valuations upon people
- Instead of assisting them in advancement of their own ends, choose the
ends for them
- As soon as particular effects are foreseen, law ceases to be mere
instrument used by people and becomes instrument used by lawgiver upon
people
- State ceases to be piece of utilitarian machinery and becomes a "moral"
institution
- "Moral" here means institution that imposes on members its views on all
moral questions
- In this sense the Nazi or any other collectivist state is "moral," while
the liberal state is not

### The Problem of "Fairness" in Planning

- Contention: economic planner need not be guided by individual prejudices
but could rely on general conviction of what is fair and reasonable
- Those with experience of planning in particular industry find no
insuperable difficulty arriving at decision all immediately interested will
accept as fair
- **This proves nothing**: selection of "interests" concerned when
planning confined to particular industry
- Those most immediately interested not necessarily best judges of
interests of society as a whole
- When capital and labor in industry agree on policy of restriction and
thus exploit consumers, no difficulty about division of spoils
- Loss divided between thousands or millions usually either disregarded or
inadequately considered

### No General Principle of Fairness Can Decide Planning Questions

- Must apply "fairness" to questions where gains and losses are seen
equally clearly
- Such instances readily recognized that no general principle such as
fairness can provide an answer
- When we must choose between higher wages for nurses or doctors, more milk
for children or better wages for agricultural workers, employment for
unemployed or better wages for employed—nothing short of complete system of
values in which every person or group has a definite place is necessary

### The Decline of the Rule of Law: Delegation and Discretion

- As planning becomes more extensive, necessary to qualify legal provisions
by reference to what is "fair" or "reasonable"
- Means leaving decision of concrete case to discretion of judge or
authority
- Could write history of decline of Rule of Law, disappearance of
**Rechtsstaat**, in terms of progressive introduction of vague formulae
into legislation and jurisdiction
- Increasing arbitrariness and uncertainty of law and judicature
- Consequent disrespect for law and judicature, which could not but become
instrument of policy
- This process had been going on steadily in Germany for some time before
Hitler and policy well advanced toward totalitarian planning

### Planning Requires Deliberate Discrimination

- Planning necessarily involves deliberate discrimination between
particular needs of different people
- Allowing one man to do what another must be prevented from doing
- Must lay down by legal rule how well off particular people shall be and
what different people are to be allowed to have and do
- Means return to rule of status, reversal of "movement of progressive
societies" which **Sir Henry Maine** described as "movement from status to
contract"

### Rule of Law vs. Substantive Equality

- Rule of Law, more than rule of contract, is the true opposite of rule of
status
- Rule of Law = rule of formal law, absence of legal privileges of
particular people designated by authority
- Safeguards equality before the law, which is the opposite of arbitrary
government
- **Formal equality before the law is in conflict, and in fact
incompatible, with any activity of government deliberately aiming at
material or substantive equality** of different people
- Any policy aiming directly at substantive ideal of distributive justice
must lead to destruction of Rule of Law
- To produce same result for different people, necessary to treat them
differently
- To give different people same objective opportunities is not to give them
same subjective chance

### Economic Inequality and the Rule of Law

- Cannot be denied that Rule of Law produces economic inequality
- All that can be claimed: this inequality is not designed to affect
particular people in a particular way
- Very significant that socialists (and Nazis) have always protested
against "merely" formal justice
- Always objected to law which had no views on how well off particular
people ought to be
- Always demanded "socialization of the law," attacked independence of
judges, supported movements like **Freirechtschule** which undermined Rule
of Law

### Universal Application More Important Than Content

- For Rule of Law to be effective, more important that rule be applied
always without exceptions than what this rule is
- Often content of rule is of minor importance, provided same rule
universally enforced
- Example: does not matter whether we all drive on left or right, so long
as we all do the same
- Important thing is that rule enables us to predict other people's
behavior correctly
- Requires it should apply to all cases—even if in particular instance we
feel it to be unjust

### The Abuse of "Privilege"

- Conflict between formal justice and formal equality before the law, and
attempts to realize ideals of substantive justice and equality, accounts
for widespread confusion about concept of "privilege"
- Application of term "privilege" to property as such would indeed be
privilege if, for example, landed property were reserved to members of
nobility
- Also privilege if right to produce or sell particular things reserved to
particular people designated by authority
- But to call private property as such, which all can acquire under same
rules, a privilege because only some succeed in acquiring it, is depriving
the word "privilege" of its meaning

### Laissez Faire Is a Misleading Description

- Unpredictability of particular effects is the distinguishing
characteristic of formal laws
- Helps clear up confusion about nature of liberal system
- Question whether state should or should not "act" or "interfere" poses
false alternative
- Term "laissez faire" is highly ambiguous and misleading
- Of course every state must act and every action interferes with
something
- Important question: whether individual can foresee action of state and
make use of this knowledge as datum in forming own plans
- Result: state cannot control use made of its machinery
- Individual knows precisely how far he will be protected against
interference from others
- Or whether state is in position to frustrate individual efforts

### Liberal Principles and State Action

- State controlling weights and measures (or preventing fraud and deception
in any other way) is certainly acting
- State permitting use of violence, for example by strike pickets, is
inactive
- In first case state observes liberal principles
- In second case it does not
- Similarly with most general and permanent rules state may establish
regarding production (building regulations, factory laws)
- May be wise or unwise in particular instance
- Do not conflict with liberal principles so long as intended to be
permanent and not used to favor or harm particular people
- True that in these instances there will be short-run effects on
particular people which may be clearly known
- With this kind of laws the short-run effects are in general not (or at
least ought not to be) the guiding consideration
- As immediate and predictable effects become more important compared with
long-run effects, we approach the border line where distinction, however
clear in principle, becomes blurred in practice

### Historical Development of the Rule of Law

- Consciously evolved only during liberal age
- One of its greatest achievements, not only as safeguard but as legal
embodiment of freedom
- **Immanuel Kant** (and **Voltaire** before him): "Man is free if he needs
to obey no person but solely the laws"
- As vague ideal existed at least since Roman times
- During last few centuries never been so seriously threatened as today
- Idea that there is no limit to powers of legislator is in part a result
of popular sovereignty and democratic government
- Strengthened by belief that so long as all actions of state are duly
authorized by legislation, Rule of Law will be preserved
- **This is completely to misconceive the meaning of the Rule of Law**
- Has little to do with whether all actions of government are legal in
juridical sense
- May well be that Hitler has obtained his unlimited powers in strictly
constitutional manner
- Whatever he does is therefore legal in juridical sense
- But who would suggest for that reason that Rule of Law still prevails in
Germany?

### Planning Destroys the Rule of Law

- To say that in planned society Rule of Law cannot hold is not to say
actions of government will not be legal or that such society will
necessarily be lawless
- Means only that use of government's coercive powers will no longer be
limited and determined by pre-established rules
- Law can, and to make central direction of economic activity possible
must, legalize what to all intents and purposes remains arbitrary action
- If law says that such a board or authority may do what it pleases,
anything that board or authority does is legal—but its actions are
certainly not subject to Rule of Law
- By giving government unlimited powers, most arbitrary rule can be made
legal
- In this way **a democracy may set up the most complete despotism
imaginable**

### Planning Requires Powers That Cannot Be Stated in Generic Form

- If law is to enable authorities to direct economic life, must give them
powers to make and enforce decisions in circumstances which cannot be
foreseen and on principles which cannot be stated in generic form
- Consequence: as planning extends, delegation of legislative powers to
diverse boards and authorities becomes increasingly common
- **Lord Hewart** case before WWI: Mr. Justice Darling said "Parliament had
enacted only last year that the Board of Agriculture in acting as they did
should be no more impeachable than Parliament itself"—this was still rare
thing
- Has since become almost daily occurrence
- Constantly the broadest powers are conferred on new authorities which,
without being bound by fixed rules, have almost unlimited discretion in
regulating this or that activity of the people

### The Rule of Law Implies Limits to Legislation

- Restricts it to kind of general rules known as formal law
- Excludes legislation either directly aimed at particular people or
enabling anybody to use coercive power of state for such discrimination
- Means not that everything is regulated by law, but on the contrary, that
coercive power of state can be used only in cases defined in advance by the
law and in such a way that it can be foreseen how it will be used
- Particular enactment can infringe Rule of Law
- Anyone ready to deny this would have to contend that whether Rule of Law
prevails today in Germany, Italy, or Russia depends on whether dictators
have obtained their absolute power by constitutional means

### Constitutional Safeguards

- Whether main applications of Rule of Law are laid down in bill of rights
or in constitutional code, or whether principle is merely firmly
established tradition, matters comparatively little
- Whatever form it takes, any such recognized limitations of powers of
legislation imply the recognition of the inalienable right of the
individual, inviolable rights of man

### H. G. Wells and the Contradiction of Planning with Rights

- Pathetic but characteristic that leading advocate of most comprehensive
central planning like **H. G. Wells** should at same time write ardent
defense of rights of man
- Individual rights Mr. Wells hopes to preserve would inevitably obstruct
the planning which he desires
- To some extent he seems to realize the dilemma
- Provisions of his proposed "Declaration of the Rights of Man" so hedged
about with qualifications that they lose all significance

#### Wells's Declaration: Right to Buy and Sell

- Proclaims every man "shall have the right to buy and sell without any
discriminatory restrictions anything which may be lawfully bought and sold"
- Which is admirable
- But immediately proceeds to make whole provision nugatory by adding that
it applies only to buying and selling "in such quantities and with such
reservations as are compatible with the common welfare"
- Since all restrictions ever imposed upon buying or selling anything are
supposed to be necessary in interest of "common welfare," there is really
no restriction which this clause effectively prevents and no right of
individual that is safeguarded by it

#### Wells's Declaration: Right to Employment

- Declaration states every man "may engage in any lawful occupation" and
"he is entitled to paid employment and to a free choice whenever there is
any variety of employment open to him"
- Not stated who is to decide whether particular employment is "open" to a
particular person
- Added provision that "he may suggest employment for himself and have his
claim publicly considered, accepted or dismissed" shows Mr. Wells thinking
in terms of authority which decides whether a man is "entitled" to
particular position
- Which certainly means the opposite of free choice of occupation

#### Wells's Other Freedoms in a Planned World

- How in a planned world "freedom of travel and migration" is to be secured
when not only means of communication and currencies are controlled but also
location of industries planned
- How freedom of press is to be safeguarded when supply of paper and all
channels of distribution are controlled by planning authority

## Chapter 6 (continued): Planning and the Rule of Law

### Progressive Rejection of Individual Rights

- Socialist reformers since early days have attacked the "metaphysical" idea of individual rights
- In a rationally ordered world, they claim, there will be no individual rights but only individual duties
- Progressives now treat protection of individual rights as reactionary
- **The Economist** (1939) exemplified this shift: argued democratic governments must have plenary powers *in posse* without restrictive penumbra of individual rights
- Economic control advocates necessarily hold this view—incompatible with Rule of Law preservation

### Economic Planning in Central Europe: Minority Oppression

- Central European countries demonstrated how economic control enables ruthless discrimination against national minorities
- Formal recognition of minority rights loses all significance under complete economic control
- Industries concentrated in hands of particular minorities enabled policies ostensibly against industries but actually targeting ethnic groups
- "Government control of the development of industries" became tool of oppression without infringing statutory minority protections

### Rule of Law Defined

- **A.V. Dicey** (*Introduction to the Study of the Law of the Constitution*, 1915): Rule of Law means absolute supremacy of regular law as opposed to arbitrary power
- Excludes arbitrariness, prerogative, or wide discretionary authority
- In England: established tradition; in Germany: *Rechtsstaat* concept
- Older, wider meaning: rule or reign of law (discussed in **Henry Maine**'s *Ancient Law*)
- **Carl Schmitt** (Nazi legal theorist) opposed liberal *Rechtsstaat* to National Socialist ideal of *gerechte Staat* ("just state")—arguing substantive justice necessarily implies discrimination between persons
- Schmitt also promoted *Freirechtsschule* (legal realism): instinct rather than rule-following as basis of judicial interpretation

### Liberty Versus Law—A False Dichotomy

- Conflict not between liberty and law, as often misconceived
- Real conflict: between different kinds of law
- **Rule of Law**: general principles laid down beforehand—"rules of the game" enabling individuals to foresee state coercion and plan accordingly
- **Arbitrary Authority**: gives government power to do what it thinks fit
- Rule of Law cannot be preserved in democracy deciding every conflict by merits rather than rules
- **John Locke**: state of nature described as "perfect freedom"—men form civil societies and submit to laws to preserve liberty and property

### Rule of Law in Criminal Justice

- Criminal law principle: *nulla poena sine lege* (no punishment without pre-existing law)
- **Bill of attainder** example: Parliament resolved Bishop of Rochester's cook should be boiled to death without clergy—violated Rule of Law despite being law
- Law must exist as general rule before individual case arises
- Cannot be preserved in totalitarian regimes

> "No 'crime' must remain without punishment, whether the law explicitly provides for it or not. The rights of the state do not end with punishing law breakers. The community is entitled to whatever may seem necessary to the protection of its interests—of which observance of the law, as it stands, is only one of the more elementary requirements." —**E.B. Ashton** on fascist principle (*The Fascist: His State and His Mind*)

### H.G. Wells and the Planners' Vision

- **H.G. Wells** (1866-1946): science fiction author, social satirist, progressive activist
- "Declaration of the Rights of Man" (1939, *Current History*): drafted vision later incorporated into Universal Declaration of Human Rights (1948)
- Wells asked comprehensive planning questions but provided little answer, like other planners
- Planners show more consistency in rejecting individual rights, insisting on duties instead

## Chapter 7: Economic Control and Totalitarianism

> "The control of the production of wealth is the control of human life itself." —**Hilaire Belloc**

### The Planners' Consolation: Economic Dictatorship Only

- Most serious planners acknowledge directed economy requires dictatorial lines
- Complex interrelated activities must be directed by single expert staff
- Ultimate responsibility in hands of commander-in-chief whose actions not fettered by democratic procedure
- **Stuart Chase**: assures us "political democracy can remain if it confines itself to all but economic matters"
- Planners argue: by giving up freedom in less important (economic) aspects, we gain greater freedom for higher values
- If economic activities concerned only inferior sides of life, we should endeavor to relieve ourselves from material ends, leaving them to utilitarian machinery

### The Erroneous Belief in Separate Economic Ends

- Power over economic life wrongly assumed to be power over secondary matters only
- No such thing as purely economic ends separate from other ends of life
- Apart from miser, ultimate ends of reasonable beings never economic
- Strictly speaking: no "economic motive" but only economic factors conditioning striving for other ends
- "Economic motive" means desire for general opportunity, power to achieve unspecified ends
- **Money**: offers widest choice in enjoying fruits of our efforts—one of greatest instruments of freedom ever invented
- Money opens astounding range of choice to poor man—greater range than generations ago was open to wealthy
- In existing society money opens range greater than available to wealthy in past

### Socialists' Proposal to Displace Pecuniary Motive

- If rewards displaced by "noneconomic incentives": public distinctions, privileges, positions of power, better housing/food, opportunities for travel/education
- This would mean recipient no longer allowed to choose
- Whoever fixed the reward determines not only size but particular form

### Economic Control as Control of Means for All Ends

- Economic gain/loss where it is still in our power to decide which needs shall be affected
- Economic matters affect only "fringe," "margin" of our needs
- Many things more important than economic gains/losses: stand high above amenities and necessities affected by economic ups and downs
- "Filthy lucre"—whether economically worse or better off—seems of little importance
- Economic planning cannot seriously interfere with more basic values of life

### The Crucial Error

- Economic values less important because in economic matters we are free to decide what is more/less important
- In present society it is *we* who solve economic problems of our lives
- To be controlled in economic pursuits means to be always controlled unless we declare our specific purpose
- When we declare specific purpose we must get it approved

### Who Decides: Individual or Planner?

- Question raised by economic planning: whether we shall satisfy what we regard as more/less important needs in way we prefer
- Or whether this is decided by the planner
- Economic planning would not affect merely marginal needs
- It means we as individuals should no longer be allowed to decide what we regard as marginal

### Authority Controls Allocation of Limited Means

- Authority directing all economic activity controls allocation of limited means for all our ends
- Whoever has sole control of means must determine which ends to be satisfied, which not
- This is the crux: economic control not merely control of sector of human life separable from rest
- It is control of means for all our ends
- Whoever controls means must determine which ends served, which values rated higher/lower—what men should believe and strive for

### Central Planning Decides Relative Importance of Needs

- Central planning means economic problem solved by community instead of individual
- Must also be community, or representatives, who decide relative importance of different needs
- So-called economic freedom planners promise: we are to be relieved of necessity of solving our own economic problems
- Since under modern conditions we are dependent on means fellow-men provide, economic planning would involve direction of almost whole of our life
- Hardly an aspect, from primary needs to relations with family/friends, from nature of work to use of leisure, over which planner would not exercise "conscious control"

### Power Over Private Lives

- Planner's power over private lives no less complete if he chose not to exercise it by direct control of consumption
- Although planned society might employ rationing, power over private lives does not depend on this
- Would be hardly less effective if consumer nominally free to spend income as pleased
- Source of this power: control over production
- In planned society authority would possess control over all consumption

### Freedom of Choice Rests on Competition

- Our freedom of choice in competitive society: if one person refuses to satisfy wishes, we can turn to another
- Face a monopolist we are at his mercy
- Authority directing whole economic system would be most powerful monopolist conceivable
- Need not fear such authority would exploit power like private monopolist
- It would have complete power to decide what we are given and on what terms

### Distribution, Discrimination, and Authority Power

- Authority would not only decide what commodities/services available and in what quantities
- Could direct distribution between districts and groups
- Could discriminate between persons to any degree it liked
- If we remember why planning advocated, can there be much doubt this power would be used for ends authority approves and to prevent pursuits of ends it disapproves?

### Control of Production and Prices: Almost Unlimited Power

- Power conferred by control of production and prices almost unlimited
- In competitive society: prices we pay for thing, rate at which we can get one thing for another, depend on quantities of other things of which by taking one we deprive other members of society
- Price not determined by conscious will of anybody
- If one way of achieving our ends proves too expensive, we are free to try other ways
- Obstacles not due to someone's disapproving of our ends but to fact same means also wanted elsewhere

### Authority Watches Over Ends Pursued

- In directed economy, where authority watches over ends pursued, certain authority would use powers to assist some ends and prevent realization of others
- Not our own view but somebody else's of what we ought to like or dislike would determine what we should get
- Since authority has power to thwart any efforts to elude guidance, it would control what we consume almost as effectively as if directly told how to spend income

### Consumers and Producers: Will of Authority Shapes Daily Lives

- Not only as consumers but even mainly in that capacity would will of authority shape and "guide" our daily lives
- Would do so even more in our position as producers
- Two aspects of our lives cannot be separated
- As for most of us time we spend at our work is large part of whole lives, and as our job usually also determines place where and people among whom we live, some freedom in choosing our work is probably even more important for our happiness than freedom to spend income during hours of leisure

### Limited Freedom of Occupation in Best of Worlds

- Even in best of worlds freedom of occupation will be very limited
- Few people ever have abundance of choice of occupation
- What matters: we have some choice, not absolutely tied to particular job chosen for us or which we may have chosen in past
- If one position becomes intolerable or if we set our heart on another, there is almost always a way for able, some sacrifice at price of which he may achieve his goal
- Nothing makes conditions more unbearable than knowledge no effort of ours can change them
- Even if we should never have strength of mind to make necessary sacrifice, knowledge we could escape if only strove hard enough makes many otherwise intolerable positions bearable

### State Action Can Help: But Not "Planning"

- Not to say all is for best in present world, or much could not be done to improve opportunities of choice open to people
- State can do great deal to help spreading of knowledge/information and to assist mobility
- Kind of state action which really would increase opportunity almost precisely opposite of "planning" now generally advocated and practiced
- Planners promise free choice of occupation will be scrupulously preserved or even increased
- But if they promise more than they can possibly fulfill, they must control entry into different trades/occupations, or terms of remuneration, or both

### Control of Entry and Remuneration

- In almost all known instances of planning, establishment of such controls and restrictions among first measures taken
- If such control universally practiced and exercised by single planning authority, one needs little imagination to see what would become of "free choice of occupation" promised
- "Freedom of choice" would be purely fictitious, mere promise to practice no discrimination where in nature of case discrimination must be practiced
- All one could hope: selection made on what authority believed to be objective grounds

### Planning Authority Fixes Remunerations

- Little difference if planning authority confined itself to fixing terms of employment and tried to regulate numbers by adjusting terms
- By prescribing remuneration, it would no less effectively bar groups of people from entering many trades than by specifically excluding them
- Rather plain girl badly wanting to become saleswoman, weakly boy set his heart on job where weakness handicaps him
- Apparently less able or suitable not necessarily excluded in competitive society if they value position sufficiently
- Will frequently be able to get start by financial sacrifice and later make good through qualities which at first are not obvious

### Authority Fixes Remunerations for Whole Category

- When authority fixes remunerations for whole category and selection among candidates made by objective test, strength of desire for job will count for very little
- Person whose qualifications not of standard type, or temperament not of ordinary kind, will no longer be able to come to special arrangements with employer whose dispositions will fit in with his special needs
- Person who prefers irregular hours or even happy-go-lucky existence with small and perhaps uncertain income to regular routine will no longer have choice
- Conditions will be without exception what in some measure they inevitably are in large organization—or rather worse, because no possibility of escape
- We shall no longer be free to be rational or efficient only when and where we think it worth while
- We shall all have to conform to standards which planning authority must fix to simplify task

### To Make Task Manageable: Reduce Diversity

- To make immense task manageable, it will have to reduce diversity of human capacities and inclinations to few categories of readily interchangeable units
- Deliberately disregard minor personal differences

### Man Becomes Mere Means

- Although professed aim of planning would be that man should cease to be mere means
- In fact—since impossible to take account in plan of individual likes and dislikes—individual would more than ever become mere means, used by authority in service of such abstractions as "social welfare" or "good of community"

### Everything Has a Price in Competition

- That in competitive society most things can be had at price—though often cruelly high price we have to pay—fact importance of which can hardly be overrated
- Alternative not complete freedom of choice but orders and prohibitions which must be obeyed and, in last resort, favor of mighty

### Cash Nexus Reproach

- Significant of confusion prevailing that it should have become cause for reproach that in competitive society almost everything can be had at price
- If people who protest against having higher values of life brought into "cash nexus" really mean we should not be allowed to sacrifice our lesser needs to preserve higher values
- This demand must be regarded as rather peculiar, scarcely testifies to great respect for dignity of individual
- That life/health, beauty/virtue, honor/peace of mind can often be preserved only at considerable material cost, and that somebody must make choice, is as undeniable as that we all are sometimes not prepared to make material sacrifices necessary to protect higher values against all injury

### Example: Automobile Accidents

- We could reduce automobile accidents to zero if willing to bear cost—if in no other way—by abolishing automobiles
- Same true of thousands of other instances in which we constantly risking life/health and all fine values of spirit, and of our fellow-men, to further what we at same time contemptuously describe as our material comfort
- Nor can it be otherwise, since all our ends compete for same means
- We could not strive for anything but these absolute values if they were on no account to be endangered

### People Want Choice Made for Them by Others?

- That people should wish to be relieved of bitter choice which hard facts often impose upon them not surprising
- Few want to be relieved through having choice made for them by others
- People just wish choice should not be necessary at all
- They are only too ready to believe choice not really necessary, that it is imposed upon them merely by particular economic system under which we live
- What they resent: there is an economic problem

### False Hope of "Potential Plenty"

- In their wishful belief there is really no longer economic problem people have been confirmed by irresponsible talk about "potential plenty"
- Which, if it were fact, would indeed mean there is no economic problem making choice inevitable
- Although this snare has served socialist propaganda under various names as long as socialism existed, still as palpably untrue as when first used over hundred years ago
- In all this time not one of many people who have used it has produced workable plan of how production could be increased so as to abolish even in western Europe what we regard as poverty—not to speak of world as whole
- Reader may take it that whoever talks about potential plenty is either dishonest or does not know what he is talking about
- Yet it is this false hope as much as anything which drives us along road to planning

### Popular Movement Still Profits by False Belief

- While popular movement still profits by false belief
- Claim that planned economy would produce substantially larger output than competitive system is being progressively abandoned by most students of problem
- Even good many economists with socialist views who have seriously studied problems of central planning now content to hope planned society will equal efficiency of competitive system
- They advocate planning no longer because of superior productivity but because it will enable us to secure more just and equitable distribution of wealth

### Only Serious Argument for Planning: Distribution

- This indeed only argument for planning which can be seriously pressed
- Indisputable that if we want to secure distribution of wealth which conforms to some predetermined standard
- If we want consciously to decide who is to have what, we must plan whole economic system
- Question remains whether price we should have to pay for realization of somebody's ideal of justice is not bound to be more discontent and more oppression than was ever caused by much-abused free play of economic forces

### Dangerous Illusion of Return to Pre-Laissez Faire Ties

- We should be seriously deceiving ourselves if for these apprehensions we sought comfort in consideration that adoption of central planning would merely mean return, after brief spell of free economy, to ties and regulations which have governed economic activity through most ages
- That therefore infringements of personal liberty need not be greater than they were before age of laissez faire
- This is dangerous illusion

### Even in Periods of Furthest Regimentation

- Even during periods of European history when regimentation of economic life went furthest, it amounted to little more than creation of general and semipermanent framework of rules within which individual preserved wide free sphere
- Apparatus of control then available would not have been adequate to impose more than very general directions
- Even where control most complete it extended only to those activities of person through which he took part in social division of labor
- In much wider sphere in which he then still lived on his own products, he was free to act as he chose

### Situation Now Entirely Different

- Situation now entirely different
- During liberal era progressive division of labor has created situation where almost every one of our activities is part of social process
- Development which we cannot reverse, since only because of it we can maintain vastly increased population at anything like present standards
- But in consequence, substitution of central planning for competition would require central direction of much greater part of our lives than was ever attempted before
- Could not stop at what we regard as our economic activities, because we are now for almost every part of our lives dependent on somebody else's economic activities

### Passion for "Collective Satisfaction of Our Needs"

- Passion for "collective satisfaction of our needs," with which our socialists have so well prepared way for totalitarianism
- Which wants us to take our pleasures as well as necessities at appointed time and in prescribed form
- Partly intended as means of political education
- Also result of exigencies of planning, which consists essentially in depriving us of choice, in order to give us whatever fits best into plan and that at time determined by plan

### Political Freedom Meaningless Without Economic Freedom?

- Often said political freedom meaningless without economic freedom
- True enough, but in sense almost opposite from that in which phrase used by our planners
- Economic freedom which is prerequisite of any other freedom cannot be freedom from economic care which socialists promise us
- Which can be obtained only by relieving individual at same time of necessity and of power of choice
- Must be freedom of our economic activity which, with right of choice, inevitably also carries risk and responsibility of that right

## Chapter 8: Who, Whom?

> "The finest opportunity ever given to the world was thrown away because the passion for equality made vain the hope for freedom." —**Lord Acton**

### Competition as "Blind" Justice

- Significant that commonest objection to competition: it is "blind"
- Not irrelevant to recall that to ancients blindness was attribute of their deity of justice
- Although competition and justice may have little else in common, much in commendation of competition as of justice that it is no respecter of persons
- Impossible to foretell who will be lucky ones or whom disaster will strike
- Rewards and penalties not shared out according to somebody's views about merits or demerits of different people but depend on their capacity and their luck
- As important as that in framing legal rules we should not be able to predict which particular person will gain and which will lose by their application

### Choice Not Between Absolute Standard and Accident

- Choice open to us not between system in which everybody will get what he deserves according to some absolute and universal standard of right
- And one where individual shares determined partly by accident or good or ill chance
- But between system where it is will of few persons that decides who is to get what
- And one where it depends at least partly on ability and enterprise of people concerned and partly on unforeseeable circumstances
- Less relevant because in system of free enterprise chances not equal, since such system necessarily based on private property and (though perhaps not with same necessity) on inheritance
- With differences in opportunity which these create

### Strong Case for Reducing Inequality of Opportunity

- Strong case for reducing this inequality of opportunity as far as congenital differences permit
- As possible to do so without destroying impersonal character of process by which everybody has to take his chance
- No person's view about what is right and desirable overrules that of others

### Poor in Competitive Society More Free

- Fact that opportunities open to poor in competitive society much more restricted than those open to rich does not make it less true
- That in such society poor are much more free than person commanding greater material comfort in different type of society
- Although under competition probability that man who starts poor will reach great wealth much smaller than true of man who has inherited property
- Not only possible for former, but competitive system only one where it depends solely on him and not on favors of mighty
- Where nobody can prevent man from attempting to achieve this result

### Overlooked Patent Fact

- It is only because we have forgotten what unfreedom means that we often overlook patent fact
- That in every real sense badly paid unskilled worker in this country has more freedom to shape his life than many small entrepreneur in Germany or much better paid engineer or manager in Russia
- Whether question of changing job or place where he lives, of professing certain views or of spending leisure in particular manner
- Although sometimes price he may have to pay for following his inclinations may be high, and to many appear too high
- There are no absolute impediments, no dangers to bodily security and freedom, that confine him by brute force to task and environment to which superior has assigned him

### Ideal of Justice of Most Socialists

- That ideal of justice of most socialists would be satisfied if merely private income from property were abolished
- Differences between earned incomes of different people remained what they are now is true
- What these people forget: in transferring all property in means of production to state, they put state in position whereby its action must in effect decide all other incomes
- Power thus given to state and demand that state should use it to "plan" means nothing else than that it should use it in full awareness of all these effects

### Power Merely Transferred to State Erroneous

- To believe that power which is thus conferred on state is merely transferred to it from others is erroneous
- It is power which is newly created
- Which in competitive society nobody possesses
- So long as property divided among many owners, none of them acting independently has exclusive power to determine income and position of particular people
- Nobody tied to any one property owner except by fact that he may offer better terms than anybody else

### Who Can Doubt Racial/Religious Minority Would Be Freer?

- Who can seriously doubt that member of small racial or religious minority will be freer with no property
- So long as fellow-members of community have property and are therefore able to employ him
- Than he would be if private property were abolished and he became owner of nominal share in communal property?
- Or that power which multiple millionaire, who may be my neighbor and perhaps my employer, has over me is very much less than that which smallest *fonctionnaire* possesses
- Who wields coercive power of state and on whose discretion it depends whether and how I am to be allowed to live or to work?
- Who will deny that world in which wealthy are powerful is still better world than one in which only already powerful can acquire wealth?

### Max Eastman Rediscovering Truth

- Pathetic, yet at same time encouraging, to find as prominent old communist as **Max Eastman** rediscovering this truth:

> "It seems obvious to me now—though I have been slow, I must say, in coming to conclusion—that institution of private property is one of main things that have given man that limited amount of free-and-equalness that Marx hoped to render infinite by abolishing this institution. Strangely enough Marx was first to see this. He is one who informed us, looking backwards, that evolution of private capitalism with its free market had been precondition for evolution of all our democratic freedoms. It never occurred to him, looking forward, that if this was so, these other freedoms might disappear with abolition of free market."

### No Reason Why Planner Should Determine Incomes?

- Sometimes said, in answer to such apprehensions, there is no reason why planner should determine incomes of individuals
- Social and political difficulties involved in deciding shares of different people in national income so obvious that even most inveterate planner may well hesitate before he charges any authority with this task
- Probably everybody who realizes what it involves would prefer to confine planning to production, to use it only to secure "rational organization of industry," leaving distribution of incomes as far as possible to impersonal forces

### Although Impossible to Direct Industry Without Exercising Influence on Distribution

- Although impossible to direct industry without exercising some influence on distribution
- Although no planner will wish to leave distribution entirely to forces of market
- They would probably all prefer to confine themselves to seeing that distribution conforms to certain general rules of equity and fairness
- That extreme inequalities avoided, that relation between remuneration of major classes just
- Without undertaking responsibility for position of particular people within their class or for gradations and differentiations between smaller groups and individuals

### Close Interdependence Makes It Difficult to Stop Planning Where We Wish

- We have already seen that close interdependence of all economic phenomena makes it difficult to stop planning just where we wish
- That once free working of market impeded beyond certain degree, planner will be forced to extend controls until they become all-comprehensive
- These economic considerations, which explain why it impossible to stop deliberate control just where we should wish, strongly reinforced by certain social or political tendencies whose strength makes itself increasingly felt as planning extends

### Once Position of Individual Determined by Authority

- Once it becomes increasingly true, and is generally recognized, that position of individual determined not by impersonal forces, not as result of competitive effort of many
- But by deliberate decision of authority, attitude of people toward their position in social order necessarily changes
- There will always exist inequalities which will appear unjust to those who suffer from them
- Disappointments which will appear unmerited, and strokes of misfortune which those hit have not deserved
- When these things occur in society which is consciously directed, way in which people will react will be very different from what it is when they are nobody's conscious choice

### Inequality More Readily Borne If Impersonal

- Inequality undoubtedly more readily borne, and affects dignity of person much less, if it is determined by impersonal forces than when it is due to design
- In competitive society it is no slight to person, no offense to his dignity, to be told by any particular firm that it has no need for his services or that it cannot offer him better job
- True that in periods of prolonged mass unemployment effect on many may be very similar
- There are other and better methods to prevent that scourge than central direction
- Unemployment or loss of income which will always affect some in any society certainly less degrading if it is result of misfortune and not deliberately imposed by authority
- However bitter experience, it would be very much worse in planned society

### In Planned Society: Individuals Must Decide Whether Person Needed

- There individuals will have to decide not whether person is needed for particular job but whether he is of use for anything, and how useful he is
- His position in life must be assigned to him by somebody else

### People Will Not Submit to Suffering Result of Authority Decision

- While people will submit to suffering which may hit anyone, they will not so easily submit to suffering which is result of decision of authority
- May be bad to be just cog in impersonal machine; but infinitely worse if we can no longer leave it, if we are tied to our place and to superiors who have been chosen for us
- Dissatisfaction of everybody with his lot will inevitably grow with consciousness that it is result of deliberate human decision

### Government Cannot Refuse Responsibility for Fate or Position

- Once government has embarked upon planning for sake of justice, it cannot refuse responsibility for anybody's fate or position
- In planned society we shall all know that we are better or worse off than others, not because of circumstances which nobody controls
- And which it is impossible to foresee with certainty, but because some authority wills it
- All our efforts directed toward improving our position will have to aim, not at foreseeing and preparing as well as we can for circumstances over which we have no control
- But at influencing in our favor authority which has all power
- Nightmare of English nineteenth-century political thinkers, state in which "no avenue to wealth and honor would exist save through government" would be realized in completeness which they never imagined
- Though familiar enough in some countries which have since passed to totalitarianism

### Problem of Due Station Becomes Central Political Problem

- As soon as state takes upon itself task of planning whole economic life, problem of due station of different individuals and groups must indeed inevitably become central political problem
- As coercive power of state will alone decide who is to have what, only power worth having will be share in exercise of this directing power
- There will be no economic or social questions that would not be

### Lenin's "Who, Whom?" and the Problem of Power

- **Lenin** introduced to Russia the phrase "who, whom?"—during Soviet rule, this became shorthand for the universal problem of socialist society
- Central question: Who plans whom? Who directs and dominates whom? Who assigns people their station in life and who receives their due allotment?
- These issues become the central political questions, to be decided solely by supreme power
- **Harold Lasswell** (American political scientist) expanded this to: "who gets what, when, and how"—the core problem of all government

### Two Fundamental Distinctions

- First: Particular measures may be taken without knowing how they will affect particular individuals—thus without aiming at such effects
- Second: The extent of government activities determines whether everything a person gets depends on government, or whether people retain some independence
- This is **the whole difference between a free and a totalitarian system**

### The Impossibility of "Greater Equality" as a Planning Principle

- **Complete equality** would provide definite guidance to the planner and give clear meaning to distributive justice
- But no socialist movement has ever gained substantial support by promising absolute equality
- What socialism actually promised: not absolutely equal distribution, but "greater equality"
- **Crucial distinction**: While absolute equality would clearly determine the planner's task, the desire for "greater equality" is merely negative—just an expression of dislike for the present state
- "Greater equality" is no more definitive than phrases like "common good" or "social welfare"
- It provides no help in deciding between the merits of particular individuals or groups

### The Collapse of Moral Standards Under Planning

- Most people find it difficult to admit we lack moral standards to settle distributive questions better than competitive markets do
- Our notions of "just price" or "fair wage" derive from competitive regimes and would disappear with competition
- "Just price" or "fair wage" means either: (1) the customary price/wage people have come to expect, or (2) the price/wage without monopolistic exploitation
- Exception: socialist doctrine once claimed workers deserved the "full produce of their labor"—but this is now rejected as based on erroneous interpretation
- If individual workers don't get their full product, and returns from capital are divided among all workers, the same basic problem remains: **how to divide it**

### The Planner's Dilemma: Ends Determine Incomes

- The planner must decide both what quantities to produce AND what constitutes a "fair wage"
- If fewer architects are wanted, and the need can be met by those willing to stay at lower remuneration, the "fair" wage will be lower
- In deciding the relative importance of different ends, **the planner also decides the relative importance of different groups and persons**
- Since people aren't merely means, he must consciously balance the importance of different ends against the effects on different people
- This means the planner **exercises direct control over the conditions of different people**

### Income Variation Within and Between Occupations

- Income differences within a trade or profession are as great as those between propertied and propertyless classes
- Example: differences between most/least successful doctor, architect, writer, movie actor, boxer, jockey are as great as between propertied and propertyless
- Differences between more/less successful plumber, market gardener, grocer, or tailor are equally vast
- No amount of standardization by creating categories can eliminate the necessity of discrimination between individuals

> "A fixed rule, like that of equality, might be acquiesced in, and so might chance, or an external necessity; but that a handful of human beings should weigh everybody in the balance, and give more to one and less to another at their sole pleasure and judgment, would not be borne unless from persons believed to be more than men, and backed by supernatural terrors." —**John Stuart Mill** (nearly a hundred years ago)

### The Problem Emerges When Planning Is Actually Attempted

- These difficulties don't lead to open clashes when socialism is merely an aspiration of a limited, homogeneous group
- They surface when socialist policy is attempted with support of many different groups composing the majority
- The burning question becomes: **which set of ideals shall be imposed upon all** by making the country's resources serve a particular vision?
- Successful planning requires creating a common view on essential values
- This restriction of freedom regarding material things directly touches **spiritual freedom**

### Education, Indoctrination, and the Socialist Roots of Totalitarianism

- Socialists traditionally hoped to solve this problem through **education**
- But knowledge cannot create new ethical values—no amount of learning makes people hold the same views on moral issues
- What's required is not rational conviction but **acceptance of a creed** to justify a particular plan
- Socialists recognized this early: the task required general acceptance of a common **Weltanschauung** (worldview)
- To produce a mass movement with a single worldview, socialists created most of the **instruments of indoctrination** later used by Nazis and Fascists

### Nazis and Fascists Borrowed Socialist Methods

- In Germany and Italy, Nazis and Fascists had little to invent
- Usages of new political movements had already been introduced by socialists
- The idea of a political party embracing all activities from cradle to grave, claiming to guide views on everything, making all problems questions of party Weltanschauung—**first put into practice by socialists**
- **Austrian socialist writer** noted with pride that the movement created "special organizations for every field of activities of workers and employees"
- It was **socialists**, not Fascists, who:
  - Collected children from tenderest age into political organizations to make them "good proletarians"
  - First organized sports, games, football, hiking in party clubs to prevent infection by other views
  - Insisted party members distinguish themselves by modes of greeting and forms of address
  - Created devices for supervision of private life
- **Balilla**, **Hitlerjugend**, **Dopolavoro**, **Kraft durch Freude** (political uniforms and military party formations)—all little more than imitations of older socialist institutions

### The Rise of Rival Socialist Movements

- So long as socialist movement is bound up with one particular group (usually skilled industrial workers), creating a common view on status is comparatively simple
- As socialism advances, it becomes evident that income and position are determined by the coercive apparatus of the state
- People can only maintain/improve position as members of an organized group capable of influencing or controlling the state machine
- This creates a **tug-of-war between pressure groups**
- Not necessarily to the advantage of the poorest—older socialist parties, representing particular groups, had designed ideology to appeal to manual workers in industry
- Their success creates a powerful countermovement by large propertyless classes who find their relative status threatened by advance of elite industrial workers

### Socialist Theory Ignored the New Middle Class

- Socialist theory based everywhere on division of society into two classes: capitalists and industrial workers
- Socialism counted on rapid disappearance of old middle class
- **Completely disregarded the rise of a new middle class**: clerks, typists, administrative workers, schoolteachers, tradesmen, small officials, lower ranks of professions
- For a time these classes provided many labor movement leaders
- But as their position deteriorated relative to industrial workers, ideals guiding them lost appeal
- While all "socialists" in disliking capitalism and wanting deliberate wealth-sharing, their ideas of justice proved very different from older socialist parties

### The Appeal of Fascism and National Socialism

- Old socialist parties' methods (raising relative economic position) cannot secure support of all
- Rival socialist movements arise appealing to those whose relative position is worsened
- Statement that fascism and National Socialism are "middle-class socialism" has truth—but in Italy and Germany supporters were economically hardly middle class any longer
- It was largely **a revolt of a new underprivileged class against the labor aristocracy** created by industrial labor movement
- Key factor: **envy of the unsuccessful professional man, university-trained engineer or lawyer**, and the "white-collared proletariat" toward engine drivers, compositors, and trade-union members whose income was many times theirs
- Average Nazi movement member in early years was poorer than average trade-unionist or older socialist party member—gained poignancy from having often seen better days and living in surroundings recalling the past

### "Class Struggle à Rebours"

- Expression "**class struggle à rebours**" (class struggle in reverse) current in Italy during fascism's rise points to important aspect
- Conflict between Fascist/National Socialist and older socialist parties must largely be regarded as **conflict between rival socialist factions**
- No difference about the state assigning each person their proper place—but profound differences about what those proper places should be

### Old Socialists Faced Resentment

- Old socialist leaders, who regarded parties as natural spearhead of future general socialism, found it difficult to understand that resentment of large poor classes should turn against them
- While organized labor in particular industries had found it easy to reach understanding with employers, very large classes were left out
- To them, more prosperous sections of labor movement seemed to belong to the exploiting rather than exploited class

### Lower Middle Class Resentment

- Resentment of lower middle class (from which fascism and National Socialism recruited) intensified by the fact that education and training made them aspire to directing positions
- Regarded themselves as entitled to be members of the directing class
- Younger generation, spurning independent positions involving risk, flocked to salaried positions promising security
- They demanded a place in organized society yielding income and power their training entitled them to
- While believing in organized society, they expected a place very different from what labor-ruled society seemed to offer
- Quite ready to take over older socialism's methods but intended to employ them in service of a different class
- Movement attracted all who, while agreeing on desirability of state controlling economic activity, disagreed with ends for which the industrial workers' aristocracy used their political strength

### Tactical Advantages of the New Socialist Movement

- Labor socialism grew in a democratic and liberal world, adapting tactics to it and taking over many liberal ideals
- Protagonists still believed that creation of socialism would solve all problems
- **Fascism and National Socialism** grew out of experience of an increasingly regulated society awakening to the fact that democratic and international socialism aimed at incompatible ideals
- Their tactics developed in a world already dominated by socialist policy and its problems
- They had no illusions about:
  - Possibility of a democratic solution to problems requiring more agreement than can reasonably be expected
  - Capacity of reason to decide questions of relative importance of wants of different men/groups
  - The formula of equality providing an answer
- They knew the strongest group which rallied enough supporters in favor of a new hierarchical order, and which frankly promised privileges to the classes to which it appealed, was likely to obtain support of all disappointed because promised equality merely furthered a particular class's interest
- Above all, successful because they **offered a theory, or Weltanschauung**, which seemed to justify the privileges they promised

### Cultural shift in values

- Anticapitalist propaganda has fundamentally altered values before institutions have changed
- Commercial enterprise now viewed as disreputable; profit-making seen as immoral

## Chapter 10: Why the Worst Get on Top

> "Power tends to corrupt, and absolute power corrupts absolutely." — **Lord Acton**

### The consoling myth

- Belief that totalitarianism's repellent features are accidental — due to historical chance of blackguards and thugs establishing the regime
- If the Streichers, Killingers, Leys, Heines, Himmlers, and Heydrichs came to power in Germany, this proves German character vicious — not that totalitarian systems inherently elevate such people
- False hope: couldn't the same system be run by decent people for the common good?

### The dangerous assumption

- Not all good people are democrats or want to share in government
- Many would prefer to entrust power to someone they think more competent
- Nothing dishonorable in approving dictatorship of the good
- Totalitarianism can be a powerful system for good or evil — depends on the dictators
- Those fearing it might be run by bad men might forestall this danger by seeing it established by good men

### Why this won't work

- Strong reasons exist why **worst features of totalitarian systems are not accidental but phenomena totalitarianism is certain to produce**
- Just as democratic statesmen planning economic life will face the alternative of assuming dictatorial powers or abandoning plans, totalitarian dictators must choose between disregarding ordinary morals or failure
- The unscrupulous and uninhibited are more likely to be successful in societies tending toward totalitarianism

### The moral atmosphere under collectivism

- Debate about collectivism's "moral basis" misses the point
- Question is not whether existing morals demand collectivism, but **what moral views will be produced by a collectivist organization**
- Interaction between morals and institutions may produce ethics altogether different from moral ideals that led to the demand for collectivism
- No reason any system should necessarily enhance attitudes serving its designed purpose
- Ruling moral views will depend partly on qualities leading individuals to success in a collectivist system and partly on requirements of totalitarian machinery

### Pre-totalitarian stage: demand for action

- General demand for quick and determined government action dominates
- Dissatisfaction with slow, cumbersome democratic procedure makes action for action's sake the goal
- Man or party who seems resolute enough "to get things done" exercises greatest appeal
- "Strong" means not numerical majority but solid support inspiring confidence in ability to carry out whatever wanted

### The new military-style party

- Central European socialist parties familiarized masses with semi-military organizations designed to absorb private life
- All that was needed: carry the principle further, seek strength not in occasional election votes but in absolute, unreserved support of smaller, thoroughly organized body
- Chance of imposing totalitarian regime depends on leader collecting a group prepared to voluntarily submit to totalitarian discipline they'll impose by force on the rest

### Why socialists were reluctant

- Socialist parties had strength to get anything if they'd used force, but were reluctant
- They set themselves a task only the ruthless ready to disregard accepted morals could execute
- Characteristic: in both Germany and Italy, fascism's success was preceded by socialist parties' refusal to take government responsibilities
- Socialists unwilling to employ methods to which they'd pointed the way
- Still hoped for miracle of majority agreeing on particular plan for organizing whole society
- Others learned: in planned society, question is not what majority agrees but what largest single group with sufficient agreement can enforce

### Three negative selection principles

**First principle: The lowest common denominator**

- Higher education and intelligence = more differentiated views and tastes, less agreement on particular value hierarchy
- To find high degree of uniformity and similarity of outlook, must descend to regions of lower moral and intellectual standards where more primitive and "common" instincts/tastes prevail
- Largest group with very similar values = people with low standards
- "Mass" in derogatory sense — least original and independent
- If potential dictator relied only on those whose primitive instincts are very similar, numbers would give insufficient weight

**Second principle: The docile and gullible**

- Will obtain support of all the docile and gullible who have no strong convictions of their own
- Prepared to accept ready-made system of values if drummed into their ears sufficiently loudly and frequently
- Those whose vague, imperfectly formed ideas are easily swayed, whose passions and emotions are readily aroused

**Third principle: Hatred as unifying force**

> "It seems to be almost a law of human nature that it is easier for people to agree on a negative program—on the hatred of an enemy, on the envy of those better off—than on any positive task."

- Contrast between "we" and "they" — common fight against outsiders — essential ingredient in any creed solidly knitting together a group for common action
- Consequently always employed by those seeking support of policy
- Unreserved allegiance of huge masses benefits from leaving them greater freedom of action than any positive program
- Enemy may be internal (Jew, kulak) or external
- Seems indispensable requisite in totalitarian leader's armory

### Anti-Semitism and anticapitalism

- In Germany: Jew became the enemy (later replaced by "plutocracies")
- No less result of anticapitalist resentment on which whole movement was based than selection of kulak in Russia
- In Germany and Austria: Jew regarded as representative of capitalism because traditional dislike of large classes for commercial pursuits left these more accessible to excluded group
- Old story: alien race admitted only to less respected trades, then hated for practicing them
- German anti-Semitism and anticapitalism spring from same root
- Great importance for understanding what happened, often grasped by foreign observers

### Collectivism's nationalist tendency

- To treat universal tendency of collectivist policy to become nationalistic as due entirely to securing unhesitating support would neglect another important factor
- May be questioned whether anyone can realistically conceive of collectivist program other than in service of limited group
- Collectivism can exist only as some kind of particularism — nationalism, racialism, or classism
- Belief in community of aims and interests with fellow-men presupposes greater similarity of outlook and thought than exists between men merely as human beings

### Collectivism as particularism

- If "community" or state prior to individual, if they have ends independent of and superior to individuals', only those working for same ends can be regarded as community members
- Person respected only as member of group, only if and in so far as he works for recognized common ends
- Derives whole dignity only from membership, not merely from being a man
- Concepts of humanity and internationalism are entirely products of individualist view of man
- No place for them in collectivist system of thought

### Factors strengthening particularism

**Desire for group identification with superiority**

- Community of collectivism can extend only as far as unity of purpose exists or can be created
- Desire of individual to identify with group often result of feeling of inferiority
- Want will be satisfied only if membership confers some superiority over outsiders
- Sometimes violent instincts individual must curb within group can be given free range in collective action toward outsider — further inducement for merging personality in that of group

**Reference to Reinhold Niebuhr**

> "There is a profound truth expressed in the title of Reinhold Niebuhr's *Moral Man and Immoral Society*—however little we can follow him in the conclusions he draws from his thesis."

- Niebuhr: "an increasing tendency among modern men to imagine themselves ethical because they have delegated their vices to larger and larger groups"
- Acting on behalf of group frees people of many moral restraints controlling behavior as individuals within group

### Planners' antagonism toward internationalism

- Definitely antagonistic attitude most planners take toward internationalism explained by: in existing world, all outside contacts of a group are obstacles to their effectively planning the sphere in which they can attempt it
- Therefore no accident that editor of comprehensive collective study on planning discovered "most 'planners' are militant nationalists" (**Findlay MacKenzie**)

### Early Fabian imperialism

- Nationalist and imperialist propensities of socialist planners not always as flagrant as with **Webbs** and early Fabians
- With Webbs, enthusiasm for planning characteristically combined with veneration for large, powerful political units and contempt for small state
- Historian **Élie Halévy** on the Webbs forty years ago: their socialism was profoundly antiliberal

> "They did not hate the Tories, indeed they were extraordinarily lenient to them, but they had no mercy for Gladstonian Liberalism. It was the time of the Boer War and both the advanced liberals and the men who were beginning to form the Labour Party had generously sided with the Boers against British Imperialism, in the name of freedom and humanity. But the two Webbs and their friend, Bernard Shaw, stood apart. They were ostentatiously imperialistic. The independence of small nations might mean something to the liberal individualist. It meant nothing to collectivists like themselves."

- **Sidney Webb**: "the future belonged to the great administrative nations, where the officials govern and the police keep order"
- **George Bernard Shaw** (quoted by Halévy): "the world is to the big and powerful states by necessity; and the little ones must come within their border or be crushed out of existence"

### Power as collectivism's goal

- Passages provide characteristic example of that glorification of power which easily leads from socialism to nationalism
- Profoundly affects ethical views of all collectivists
- So far as small nations' rights concerned, **Marx** and **Engels** little better than most consistent collectivists
- Views occasionally expressed about Czechs or Poles resemble contemporary National Socialists'

### Individualist social philosophers vs. collectivists

- To great individualist social philosophers of 19th century (**Lord Acton**, **Jacob Burckhardt**), down to contemporary socialists like **Bertrand Russell** who inherited liberal tradition, power has always appeared the arch-evil
- To strict collectivist it is goal in itself
- Not only (as **Russell** described) desire to organize social life according to unitary plan springs largely from desire for power
- Even more: outcome of fact that to achieve their end, collectivists must create power — power over men wielded by other men — of magnitude never before known
- Success will depend on extent to which they achieve such power

### The illusion of extinguishing power

- Many liberal socialists guided by tragic illusion: by depriving private individuals of power they possess in individualist system, and transferring this power to society, they can thereby extinguish power
- What all who argue this way overlook: by concentrating power so it can be used in service of single plan, it is not merely transferred but infinitely heightened
- By uniting in hands of some single body power formerly exercised independently by many, an amount of power is created infinitely greater than any that existed before
- So far-reaching as almost to be different in kind
- Entirely fallacious when sometimes argued that great power exercised by central planning board would be "no greater than the power collectively exercised by private boards of directors"

### Power in competitive vs. planned society

- In competitive society, nobody can exercise even a fraction of power which socialist planning board would possess
- If nobody can consciously use the power, it is abuse of words to assert it rests with all capitalists put together
- Play upon words to speak of "power collectively exercised by private boards of directors" so long as they do not combine to concerted action
- Would mean end of competition and creation of planned economy
- To split or decentralize power is necessarily to reduce absolute amount of power
- Competitive system is only system designed to minimize by decentralization the power exercised by man over man

### Economic power centralized becomes political power

- Separation of economic and political aims is essential guaranty of individual freedom
- Consequently attacked by all collectivists
- Must now add: "substitution of political for economic power" now often demanded means necessarily the substitution of power from which there is no escape for a power which is always limited
- What is called economic power, while it can be instrument of coercion, in hands of private individuals is never exclusive or complete power, never power over whole life of person
- But centralized as instrument of political power it creates a degree of dependence scarcely distinguishable from slavery

### The collectivist moral system

- From two central features of every collectivist system (need for commonly accepted system of ends of the group + all-overriding desire to give group maximum power to achieve these ends), grows a definite system of morals
- On some points coincides, on others violently contrasts with ours
- Differs in one point making it doubtful whether we can call it morals: **does not leave individual conscience free to apply its own rules**, does not even know any general rules which individual is required or allowed to observe in all circumstances
- Makes collectivist morals so different from what we have known as morals that we find it difficult to discover any principle in them

### Collectivist ethics vs. individualist ethics

- Difference of principle very much same as already considered in connection with Rule of Law
- Like formal law, rules of individualist ethics (however unprecise in many respects) are general and abstract
- Prescribe or prohibit general type of action irrespective of whether in particular instance ultimate purpose is good or bad
- To cheat or steal, torture or betray confidence held to be bad, irrespective of whether in particular instance any harm follows or any high purpose for which such act may have been committed can alter the fact it is bad
- Though we may sometimes be forced to choose between different evils, they remain evils

### "The end justifies the means"

- Principle that end justifies means is in individualist ethics regarded as denial of all morals
- In collectivist ethics it becomes necessarily the supreme rule
- Literally nothing which consistent collectivist must not be prepared to do if it serves "the good of the whole"
- "Good of the whole" is to him the only criterion of what ought to be done
- **Raison d'état** (reason of state) — collectivist ethics has found its most explicit formulation
- Knows no other limit than that set by expediency — suitability of particular act for end in view
- What raison d'état affirms with respect to relations between different countries applies equally to relations between different individuals within collectivist state
- No limit to what its citizen must be prepared to do, no act which his conscience must prevent him from committing, if necessary for end which community has set itself or which his superiors order him to achieve

### Absence of absolute formal rules

- Absence of absolute formal rules in collectivist ethics does not mean there are not some useful habits of individuals which collectivist community will encourage and others it will discourage
- Will take much greater interest in individual's habits of life than individualist community
- To be useful member requires very definite qualities which must be strengthened by constant practice
- Reason we designate these qualities as "useful habits" and can hardly describe them as moral virtues: **individual could never be allowed to put these rules above any definite orders or let them become obstacle to achievement of any of particular aims of his community**
- Only serve to fill gaps which direct orders or designation of particular aims may leave, but can never justify conflict with will of authority

### German virtues vs. English virtues

- Differences between virtues which will continue to be esteemed under collectivist system and those which will disappear well illustrated by comparison of virtues which even their worst enemies admit Germans (or "typical Prussian") possess, and those of which they are commonly thought lacking and in which English people used to pride themselves as excelling
- Few will deny Germans on whole industrious and disciplined, thorough and energetic to degree of ruthlessness, conscientious and single-minded in any task they undertake
- Possess strong sense of order, duty, and strict obedience to authority
- Often show great readiness to make personal sacrifices and great courage in physical danger
- All these make German efficient instrument in carrying out assigned task
- Carefully nurtured in old Prussian state and new Prussian-dominated Reich

### What Germans lack

- "Typical German" often thought to lack: individualist virtues of tolerance and respect for other individuals and their opinions, of independence of mind and uprightness of character and readiness to defend one's own convictions against superior
- Germans themselves, usually conscious they lack it, call *Zivilcourage* (civil courage)
- Of consideration for weak and infirm, of that healthy contempt and dislike of power which only an old tradition of personal liberty creates
- Deficient also in most of those little yet important qualities which facilitate intercourse between men in free society: kindliness and sense of humor, personal modesty, and respect for privacy and belief in good intentions of one's neighbor

### Social virtues flourish in individualist society

- After what we have already said it will not cause surprise that these individualist virtues are at same time eminently social virtues
- Smooth social contacts and which make control from above less necessary and at same time more difficult
- Virtues which flourish wherever individualist or commercial type of society has prevailed and which are missing according as collectivist or military type of society predominates
- Noticeable difference between various regions of Germany as it has now become of views which rule in Germany and those characteristic of West
- Until recently, in parts of Germany which have been longest exposed to civilizing forces of commerce, old commercial towns of south and west and Hanse towns, general moral concepts probably much more akin to those of Western people than to those which have now become dominant all over Germany

### Masses not devoid of moral fervor

- Would be highly unjust to regard masses of totalitarian people as devoid of moral fervor because they give unstinted support to system which to us seems denial of most moral values
- For great majority opposite is probably true: **intensity of moral emotions behind movement like National Socialism or communism can probably be compared only to those of great religious movements of history**
- Once you admit individual is merely means to serve ends of higher entity called society or nation, most features of totalitarian regimes which horrify us follow of necessity
- From collectivist standpoint: intolerance and brutal suppression of dissent, complete disregard of life and happiness of individual, are essential and unavoidable consequences of this basic premise
- Collectivist can admit this and at same time claim his system superior to one in which "selfish" interests of individual are allowed to obstruct full realization of ends community pursues

### German philosophers on personal happiness

- When German philosophers again and again represent striving for personal happiness as itself immoral and only fulfillment of imposed duty as praiseworthy, they are perfectly sincere, however difficult this may be to understand for those who have been brought up in different tradition

### Where there is one common end, no room for general morals

- Where there is one common all-overriding end, there is no room for any general morals or rules
- To limited extent we ourselves experience this in wartime
- But even war and greatest peril had led in democratic countries only to very moderate approach to totalitarianism, very little setting-aside of all other values in service of single purpose
- Where few specific ends dominate whole of society, inevitable that occasionally cruelty may become duty
- Acts which revolt all our feeling (shooting of hostages, killing of old or sick, compulsory uprooting and transportation of hundreds of thousands) should become instrument of policy approved by almost everybody except victims
- Suggestions like "conscription of woman for breeding purposes" can be seriously contemplated
- Always in eyes of collectivist a greater goal which these acts serve and which to him justifies them because pursuit of common end of society can know no limits in any rights or values of any individual

### Leaders must be unscrupulous

- While for mass of citizens of totalitarian state it is often unselfish devotion to ideal (although one repellent to us) which makes them approve and even perform such deeds, this cannot be pleaded for those who guide its policy
- To be useful assistant in running totalitarian state, not enough man should be prepared to accept specious justification of vile deeds
- Must himself be prepared actively to break every moral rule he has ever known if seems necessary to achieve end set for him
- Since it is supreme leader who alone determines ends, his instruments must have no moral convictions of their own
- Must, above all, be unreservedly committed to person of leader
- Next to this, most important thing: they should be completely unprincipled and literally capable of everything
- Must have no ideals of their own they want to realize, no ideas about right or wrong which might interfere with intentions of leader

### Little to attract the morally good

- Thus in positions of power little to attract those who hold moral beliefs of kind which in past have guided European peoples
- Little which could compensate for distastefulness of many particular tasks, and little opportunity to gratify any more idealistic desires, to recompense for undeniable risk, sacrifice of most pleasures of private life and of personal independence which posts of great responsibility involve
- Only tastes which are satisfied: taste for power as such and pleasure of being obeyed and of being part of well-functioning and immensely powerful machine to which everything else must give way

### The path to power in totalitarian state

- Yet while there is little likely to induce men who are good by our standards to aspire to leading positions in totalitarian machine, and much to deter them, there will be special opportunities for ruthless and unscrupulous
- Jobs to be done about badness of which taken by themselves nobody has any doubt, but which have to be done in service of some higher end, and which have to be executed with same expertness and efficiency as any others
- As there will be need for actions which are bad in themselves, and which all those still influenced by traditional morals will be reluctant to perform, readiness to do bad things becomes path to promotion and power
- Positions in totalitarian society in which it is necessary to practice cruelty and intimidation, deliberate deception and spying, are numerous
- Neither Gestapo nor administration of concentration camp, neither Ministry of Propaganda nor SA or SS (or their Italian or Russian counterparts), are suitable places for exercise of humanitarian feelings
- Yet it is through positions like these that road to highest positions in totalitarian state leads
- Only too true when distinguished American economist concludes from brief enumeration of duties of authorities of collectivist state: "they would have to do these things whether they wanted to or not: and the probability of the people in power being individuals who would dislike the possession and exercise of power is on a level with the probability that an extremely tender-hearted person would get the job of whipping-master in a slave plantation."

### Conclusion: Selection and moral deformation

- Cannot exhaust this subject here
- Problem of selection of leaders closely bound up with wide problem of selection according to opinions held
- Rather according to readiness with which person conforms to ever changing set of doctrines
- Leads us to one of most characteristic moral features of totalitarianism: its relation to, and effect on, all virtues falling under general heading of truthfulness

## Chapter 11: The End of Truth

### Propaganda and Totalitarian Mind Control

- Totalitarian effectiveness requires people to believe in system goals, not just obey them
- Beliefs must be "chosen for the people and imposed upon them" yet become genuine beliefs
- **Gleichschaltung**: coordination of all propaganda to serve same goal differentiates totalitarian from competitive propaganda
- Under single control of information, propagandist can "mold minds in any direction he chooses"
- Even intelligent, independent people cannot escape influence if isolated from alternative information sources

> "It is significant that the nationalization of thought has proceeded everywhere pari passu with the nationalization of industry." —**E. H. Carr**

### Moral Consequences Beyond Coercion

- If propaganda only indoctrinated values, problem would be whether code is good or bad
- Totalitarian moral code is "not likely to appeal to us" — enforced inequality, disappearance of humanitarian elements, disrespect for human life and the weak
- But deeper moral consequence: totalitarian propaganda **destroys respect for truth itself**

### Propaganda Must Extend to Facts

- To justify official values, propaganda must make assertions about causal connections between means and ends
- Distinction between ends and means "is never so clear cut" as general discussion suggests
- People must agree not only with ultimate aims but also with **views about facts** on which measures are based
- Planner cannot create comprehensive value code in advance; discovers conflicts as he goes
- Planning authority must rationalize prejudices with theories that become "integral part of governing doctrine"

### Creation of Official Myths

- Totalitarian leader may be guided by instinctive preferences, not conscious theory
- Example: dislike of Jews combined with romantic yearning for "tall blond man, the 'aristocratic' figure"
- **Pseudoscientific theories** provide rational justification for shared prejudices
- **Blut und Boden** ("blood and soil"): expresses ultimate values plus "whole host of beliefs about cause and effect"
- **Plato's "noble lies"**, **Sorel's "myths"**, Nazi racial doctrine, Mussolini's corporative state theory — all serve same purpose
- Particular views elaborated into scientific theories to justify preconceived opinions

### Perversion of Language

- Most effective technique: persuade people new values are same as old values, "not properly understood or recognized before"
- Use old words but change their meaning
- **"Liberty"** worst sufferer — used freely in totalitarian states
- **"New Liberties for Old"** — liberty destroyed in name of new freedom

> "Naturally the advent of planned freedom does not mean that all [sic] earlier forms of freedom must be abolished." —**Dr. Karl Mannheim**

- **"Collective freedom"** = unlimited freedom of planner, not members of society
- "Confusion of freedom with power carried to the extreme"
- Same perversion happens to "justice," "law," "right," "equality"
- Whole language becomes despoiled, words become empty shells used solely for emotional associations

### Silencing Criticism

- Plan itself must become "sacrosanct and exempt from criticism"
- Official creed comprises all views about facts on which plan is based
- Public criticism must be suppressed because it weakens public support

> "Whilst the work is in progress, any public expression of doubt, or even fear that the plan will not be successful, is an act of disloyalty and even of treachery because of its possible effects on the will and on the efforts of the rest of the staff." —**Sidney and Beatrice Webb** on Russian enterprises

- When doubt concerns whole social plan, not just particular enterprise, must be treated as sabotage
- Schools, press, radio, motion pictures used exclusively to spread views strengthening belief in authority's decisions
- Situation permanently same as wartime in some fields: everything causing doubt suppressed
- No field where systematic control of information will not be practiced

### Totalitarian Control of Science

- Even abstract sciences not exempt from control
- Disciplines dealing with human affairs (history, law, economics) cannot allow disinterested search for truth
- Vindication of official views becomes sole object
- These become "most fertile factories of official myths"

### Examples of Scientific Control

- **Theory of relativity**: attacked as "Semitic attack on foundation of Christian and Nordic physics" OR opposed for conflict with dialectical materialism
- **Mathematical statistics**: attacked because "form part of class struggle on ideological frontier"
- **Journal for Marxist-Leninist Natural Sciences**: "We stand for Party in Mathematics"
- **German physicist Lenard**: summarized lifework as *German Physics in Four Volumes*
- Totalitarianism condemns activity done "for its own sake" — science, art, chess must serve conscious social purpose

> Official exhortation to chess players: "We must condemn once and for all the formula 'chess for the sake of chess' like the formula 'art for art's sake.'"

### Science Must Serve Official Doctrine

- Science must serve truth, not produce unforeseeable results outside planner's philosophy
- Nazi minister of justice: every scientific theory must ask "Do I serve National Socialism for the greatest benefit of all?"

### Truth Becomes Official Decree

- "Truth" no longer something to be found by individual conscience
- Becomes "something to be laid down by authority"
- Must be believed "in interest of unity of organized effort"
- May be altered as exigencies of organized effort require
- General intellectual climate: complete cynicism about truth, loss of meaning of truth
- Disappearance of spirit of independent inquiry and belief in rational conviction
- Differences of opinion become political issues decided by authority

### Intellectual Climate of Oppression

- Not unique to established totalitarian systems
- Found among intellectuals who embraced collectivist faith in liberal countries
- British scientific writer defended Inquisition as "beneficial to science when it protects a rising class"
- Practically indistinguishable from Nazi views leading to book burning and persecution of intelligentsia

### The Argument Against Freedom of Thought

- Some intellectuals argue no real freedom of thought exists anyway
- Opinions and tastes shaped by propaganda, advertising, example of upper classes
- Therefore we should "deliberately turn thoughts of people in what we think is a desirable direction"
- Great majority rarely think independently, accept ready-made views
- But this doesn't justify any group claiming right to determine what people ought to think
- Shows "complete confusion of thought"

### Why Freedom of Thought Matters

- Value not that everybody can think independently
- Value: **any cause or idea may be argued by somebody**
- As long as dissent not suppressed, someone will query ruling ideas and test them against argument and propaganda
- Interaction of individuals with different knowledge and views constitutes life of thought
- Growth of reason is social process based on differences
- Cannot be predicted which views will assist growth
- To "plan" or "organize" growth of mind sets bounds to development, produces stagnation

### Collectivism's Self-Contradiction

- Collectivist thought starts out to make reason supreme
- Ends by destroying reason by misconceiving process on which growth depends
- Paradox: collectivist doctrines demand "conscious control" or "conscious planning"
- This necessarily leads to demand that mind of some individual should rule supreme
- Only individualist approach recognizes **superindividual forces** guiding growth of reason
- Individualism = attitude of humility before social process
- Tolerance to other opinions
- Exact opposite of intellectual **hubris** at root of demand for comprehensive direction

## Chapter 12: The Socialist Roots of Naziism (continued)

### The Intellectual Origins of National Socialism

- **Plenge** and **Lensch** provided leading ideas for National Socialism, particularly for **Oswald Spengler** and **Arthur Moeller van den Bruck**
- Spengler's *Prussianism and Socialism* (1920) expressed ideas widely held by German socialists
- Central thesis: "Old Prussian spirit and socialist conviction, which today hate each other with the hatred of brothers, are one and the same"
- German liberals were seen as representatives of Western civilization—"the invisible English army" left after Napoleon's defeat at Jena
- Reformers like **Hardenberg** and **Humboldt** were condemned as "English"—liberalism was the foreign spirit to be expelled by German revolution beginning in 1914

### Three Forms of Western Existence

> "The three last nations of the Occident have aimed at three forms of existence, represented by famous watchwords: Freedom, Equality, Community. They appear in the political forms of liberal Parliamentarianism, social Democracy, and authoritarian socialism."

- **Spengler's framework**: English structure based on distinction between rich and poor; Prussian structure based on command and obedience
- Class distinction fundamentally different in the two countries
- Spengler argued that since Bismarck, Germany had progressively assumed more socialist forms of economic organization

### The Prussian Idea and State Socialism

> "In Prussia there existed a real state in the most ambitious meaning of the word. There could be, strictly speaking, no private persons. Everybody who lived within the system that worked with the precision of a clockwork, was in some way a link in it."

- The Prussian idea requires all wages and salaries fixed by the state
- State administration of all property becomes a salaried function—the *Beamtenstaat*
- **Central question**: "Is in the future trade to govern the state, or the state to govern trade?"
- Spengler: "Prussianism and Socialism combat the England in our midst"

### Moeller van den Bruck and the Third Reich

- **Moeller van den Bruck** proclaimed WWI as war between liberalism and socialism
- "We have lost the war against the West. Socialism has lost it against Liberalism"
- Liberalism was the archenemy—German youth now "turns with nausea, with wrath" from this foreign philosophy
- Liberalism seen as "foreign, more repugnant, more opposed to its philosophy" than any other ideology
- German youth recognized the liberal as *the archenemy*
- The Third Reich was designed to give Germans "a socialism adapted to their nature and undefiled by Western liberal ideas"

### The Broader Pattern: 1922 Onward

- By 1922, detached observers noted "a peculiar and, on a first glance, surprising phenomenon" in Germany
- The fight against capitalistic order seen as "continuation of the war against the Entente with the weapons of the spirit and of economic organization"
- A return to "the best and noblest traditions" of the German people
- Fight against liberalism united socialists and conservatives in one common front
- The **German Youth Movement** was almost entirely socialist in inspiration and outlook

### Die Tat Circle and Ferdinand Fried

- In the late twenties until Hitler's rise, circle of young men gathered around journal *Die Tat*
- Led by **Ferdinand Fried**, became chief exponent of this tradition in intellectual sphere
- Fried's *Ende des Kapitalismus* (1931) was characteristic product of *Edelnazis* group
- Particularly disgusting because of its resemblance to contemporary English and American literature
- Same drawing together of socialists of Left and Right
- Nearly the same contempt for all that is liberal in the old sense
- "Conservative Socialism" and "Religious Socialism" were slogans preparing atmosphere for National Socialism's success

### The Question of Inevitability

- Had "conservative socialism" with "weapons of spirit and economic organization" nearly succeeded before the real war began?
- This intellectual movement was not isolated—it represented a dominant trend
- The fusion of socialism and nationalism was completed in the German Youth Movement
- *Die Tat* circle led by Fried became chief exponent until Hitler's advent

## Chapter 13: The Totalitarians in Our Midst

### The False Sense of Security

> "When authority presents itself in the guise of organization, it develops charms fascinating enough to convert communities of free people into totalitarian States." —*The Times* (London)

- The magnitude of totalitarian outrages has strengthened assurance "it cannot happen here"
- The gulf separating us from Nazi Germany seems so immense that nothing there appears relevant
- **But**: fifteen years ago the possibility would have seemed just as fantastic to Germans themselves and hostile foreign observers
- The relevant comparison is not present Germany but **Germany of twenty or thirty years ago**

### England's Drift Toward German Patterns

- Many features once regarded as "typically German" are now equally familiar in England
- Most significant: increasing similarity between economic views of Right and Left
- Common opposition to liberalism has replaced liberal consensus that once dominated English politics
- **Harold Nicolson** noted that during last Conservative government, "the most gifted backbenchers were all socialists at heart"
- Many socialists have more sympathy with Conservatives than with Liberals
- Related features: increasing veneration for the state, admiration of power and bigness for bigness' sake
- Enthusiasm for "organization" of everything (now called "planning")
- Inability to leave anything to "the simple power of organic growth" (deplored by von Treitschke sixty years ago)

### The Eclipse of Liberal England

- How far England has traveled on the German path is evident from extraordinary vividness of discussions during WWI comparing British and German political/moral views
- British public then had truer appreciation of differences than now
- While once proud of distinctive tradition, few political views then regarded as characteristically English are not now "half-ashamed, if they do not positively repudiate them"
- The more typically English a writer on political/social problems appeared then, the more forgotten in his own country today
- Examples: **Lord Morley**, **Henry Sidgwick**, **Lord Acton**, **A. V. Dicey**—admired worldwide as examples of liberal England's political wisdom, now largely obsolete Victorians
- While no lack of sympathetic treatment of Bismarck in contemporary English literature, **Gladstone** rarely mentioned by younger generation without sneer over his Victorian morality and naïve utopianism

### Keynes on German Economic Thought (1915)

- Hayek quotes Lord Keynes describing 1915 "nightmare" in typical German work of that period
- German author's view: "even in peace industrial life must remain mobilised"
- This means "the militarisation of our industrial life"
- Individualism must come to an end absolutely
- System of regulations must be set up for maximum degree of efficiency (*Leistungsfähigkeit*)
- Influence on individual advantage only indirect
- Nation will grow into "closed unity" and become what **Plato** declared it should be—*Der Mensch im Grossen*
- Coming peace will strengthen idea of State action in industry
- Foreign investment, emigration, industrial policy treating whole world as market are "too dangerous"
- Old order of industry based on Profit is dying
- New Germany of twentieth-century: Power without consideration of Profit
- Goal: "to make an end of that system of Capitalism, which came over from England one hundred years ago"

> "Except that no English author has yet to my knowledge dared openly to disparage individual happiness, is there a passage in this which is not mirrored in much of contemporary English literature?"

### Totalitarian Ideas Spreading in Democracies

- Not merely the ideas which prepared totalitarianism in Germany, but many principles of totalitarianism itself exercise increasing fascination in other countries
- Few people ready to swallow totalitarianism whole
- But scarcely a leaf out of Hitler's book which somebody in England or America has not recommended
- This applies particularly to people who are undoubtedly Hitler's moral enemies because of one special feature in his system
- Anti-Semitism of Hitler has driven from his country many confirmed totalitarians of the German type

### The Similarity of English and German Political Literature

- No general description can adequately convey similarity of much current English political literature to works which destroyed belief in Western civilization in Germany
- Similarity is more of temper/approach than specific arguments
- Similar readiness to break all cultural ties with past
- Willingness to stake everything on success of a particular experiment
- Most works preparing way for totalitarian course in democracies are products of sincere idealists and men of considerable intellectual distinction

### Professor E. H. Carr's Realism

- **E. H. Carr's** books on *Twenty Years' Crisis* and *Conditions of Peace* show marked influence of specific German ideas
- Carr confessed himself adherent of "the 'historical school' of realists which had its home in Germany and can be traced through the great names of Hegel and Marx"
- A realist is one "who makes morality a function of politics" and "cannot logically accept any standard of value save that of fact"

- Old morals with their "abstract general principles" must disappear
- "The empiricist treats the concrete case on its individual merits"
- Nothing but expediency matters

- Carr would presumably argue that different views then professed in Britain were merely product of British hypocrisy

### Carr's Future Society: Totalitarian Planning

- From glimpses of future society Carr contemplates, appears to be quite on totalitarian model
- Sometimes one wonders whether resemblance is accidental or deliberate
- Does Carr realize when he asserts "we can no longer find much meaning in the distinction familiar to nineteenth-century thought between 'society' and 'state,'" this is precisely the doctrine of **Carl Schmitt**, leading Nazi theoretician of totalitarianism?
- Or that view "the mass production of opinion is the corollary of the mass production of goods" and "the prejudice which the word propaganda still exerts in many minds today is closely parallel to the prejudice against control of industry and trade" is really an apology for Nazi-style opinion regimentation?

### Carr's Rejection of Nineteenth-Century Liberalism

- In *Conditions of Peace*, Carr answers question concluding last chapter with emphatic affirmative:

> "The victors lost the peace, and Soviet Russia and Germany won it, because the former continued to preach, and in part to apply, the once valid, but now disruptive ideals of the rights of nations and laissez faire capitalism, whereas the latter, consciously or unconsciously borne forward on the tide of the twentieth century, were striving to build up the world in larger units under centralized planning and control."

- Carr completely adopts German battle cry of socialist revolution of East against liberal West in which Germany was leader
- "The revolution which began in the last war, which has been the driving force of every significant political movement in the last twenty years . . . a revolution against the predominant ideas of the nineteenth century: liberal democracy, national self-determination and laissez faire economics"
- "It was almost inevitable that this challenge to nineteenth-century beliefs which she had never really shared should find in Germany one of its strongest protagonists"
- With fatalistic belief of every pseudo-historian since Hegel and Marx, development is represented as inevitable: "We know the direction in which the world is moving, and we must bow to it or perish"

### Economic Fallacies Supporting Inevitability

- Conviction that trend is inevitable characteristically based on familiar economic fallacies
- Presumed necessity of general growth of monopolies from technological developments
- Alleged "potential plenty" and all other popular catchwords appearing in works of this kind
- Professor Carr is not an economist, and his economic argument will not bear serious examination
- His belief that importance of economic factor in social life is rapidly decreasing doesn't prevent him from basing all predictions about inevitable developments on economic arguments
- Or from presenting as main demands for future "the reinterpretation in predominantly economic terms of the democratic ideals of 'equality' and 'liberty'"

### Carr's Contempt for Liberal Economics

- Carr's contempt for all ideas of liberal economists rivals that of any German writers quoted in last chapter
- Even takes over German thesis, originated by **Friedrich List**, that free trade was policy dictated solely by special interests of England in nineteenth century
- Now, however, "the artificial production of some degree of autarchy is a necessary condition of orderly social existence"

### Carr on War as Social Solidarity

- After all this, hardly surprised to find characteristic section headed "The Moral Function of War"

### The Scientific Organization of Society

- Perhaps not yet given enough attention to one feature of intellectual development in Germany during last hundred years now appearing in almost identical form in English-speaking countries: **scientistic agitation for "scientific" organization of society**
- Ideal of society organized "through and through" from top has in Germany been considerably furthered by unique influence which scientific and technological specialists were allowed to exercise on formation of social and political opinions
- Few people remember that in modern history of Germany, political professors have played role comparable to that of political lawyers in France
- Influence of these scientist-politicians of late years not often on side of liberty
- "Intolerance of reason" so frequently conspicuous in scientific specialist
- Impatience with ways of ordinary man so characteristic of expert
- Contempt for anything not consciously organized by superior minds according to scientific blueprint were phenomena familiar in German public life for generations before they became of significance in England

### Germany's Shift from Humanities to Realities (1840-1940)

- Perhaps no other country provides better illustration of effects on nation of general and thorough shift of greater part of its educated class from "humanities" to "realities" than Germany between 1840 and 1940

### German Intellectuals' Submission to National Socialism

- Way in which, in the end, with few exceptions, German scholars and scientists put themselves readily at service of new rulers is one of most depressing and shameful spectacles in whole history of rise of National Socialism
- Well known that particularly scientists and engineers, who had so loudly claimed to be leaders on march to new and better world, submitted more readily than almost any other class to new tyranny

### Julien Benda's Prophetic Warning

- Role which intellectuals played in totalitarian transformation of society was prophetically foreseen in another country by **Julien Benda**
- *Trahison des clercs* (1927) assumes new significance when re-read fifteen years after it was written

> "Superstition of science held to be competent in all domains, including that of morality; a superstition which, I repeat, is an acquisition of the nineteenth century. It remains to discover whether those who brandish this doctrine believe in it or whether they simply want to give the prestige of a scientific appearance to passions which they perfectly know are nothing but passions. It is to be noted that the dogma that history is obedient to scientific laws is preached especially by partisans of arbitrary authority. This is quite natural, since it eliminates the two realities they most hate, i.e., human liberty and the historical action of the individual."

### C. H. Waddington's *The Scientific Attitude*

### Central Authority Must Control Everything

- International authority can contribute to prosperity if it **keeps order** and creates conditions for independent development
- But impossible to be just or let people live independently if central authority **doles out raw materials, allocates markets, approves spontaneous efforts**
- Every action requires central sanction

### Economic vs. Political Powers

- These difficulties cannot be met by conferring **"merely" specific economic powers** on international authorities
- Belief this is practical rests on fallacy that economic planning is merely technical task by experts
- Any international economic authority could exercise **most tyrannical and irresponsible power** imaginable
- Example: exclusive control of essential commodity (air transport) = far-reaching power over any authority
- No "technical necessities" could justify actions; humanitarian arguments about ill-favored groups could not be questioned
- **Comprehensive monopolies** recognized by national governments but subject to none = worst conceivable rackets

### Raw Materials Control: Political Domination

- Consider proposals for **conscious control of essential raw materials** (gasoline, timber, rubber, tin)
- Controller decides whether to increase supply (lowering producer incomes) or allow new industries to start
- Controller "protects" standards of specially entrusted groups, depriving others of their best chance to improve
- **Control of all essential raw materials** = no new industry, no new venture without permission from controllers

### Market and Investment Control = Veto Power

- Same applies to international "sharing" of markets and control of investment/natural resources development
- Pose as hard-boiled realists but regard **"utopianism"** as more practicable for intimate interference
- Once undreamed-of power given to international government (represented as incapable of enforcing simple Rule of Law), this power will be used **unselfishly** to command consent
- **Illusion**: nations might abide by formal rules agreed upon, but will **never submit to direction** of planning — never agree on rank-ordering of needs and rate of advancement

### Not a Technical Task but Comprehensive Power

- If people understood proposals mean transferring such powers to international authority, they'd realize they've delegated **most comprehensive power over their lives**, not merely a technical task

### The Realist Camouflage: Hegemony by One Power

- "Realists" advocating schemes believe great powers won't submit to superior authority but will use "international" authorities to **impose their will on smaller nations**
- By camouflaging planning authorities as "international," condition for international planning becomes practicable: **done by one single predominant power**
- This wouldn't alter the fact that for smaller states it means **complete subjection to external power** with no resistance possible

### Professor Carr and the Nazi Precedent

- Passionate advocates of centrally directed economic New Order (like **Professor Carr**) show complete disregard for individuality and small-nation rights
- Carr's views represent trend toward totalitarianism in England
- His colleague asked: **"If the Nazi way with small sovereign states is indeed to become the common form, what is the war about?"**
- Recent utterances in *London Times* and *New Statesman* about these questions have caused disquiet among smaller Allies

### The Right Conclusion: Restraint, Not Direction

- Small states are right: cannot hope for order or peace if states regain unfettered sovereignty in economic sphere
- But this does **not** mean new superstate must direct nations how to use their resources
- Means: **power to restrain nations from harmful action**, set of rules defining what states may do, authority to enforce rules
- Powers needed are mainly **negative**; must be able to say "No" to restrictive measures

### The Opposite is True: Political Authority Above Economic Interests

- Widely believed: need international economic authority while states retain political sovereignty
- **Opposite is true**: need superior **political power** to hold economic interests in check, not more power for economic authorities
- International political authority without power to direct people what they must do, but able to **restrain harmful action**
- Need: authority not mixed up in the economic game, can truly **hold the scales** in conflicts between economic interests

### Powers Needed: Ultra-Liberal "Laissez Faire" State

- Powers for international authority: not new powers of recent states but **minimum powers** to preserve peaceful relationships
- Essentially powers of **ultra-liberal "laissez faire" state**
- More essential at international level than national level: powers **strictly circumscribed by Rule of Law**
- Need becomes greater as states become **units of economic administration** rather than supervisors; friction arises between states as such

### Federation: Strictly Defined Powers, Limited Democracy

- Form of international government transferring strictly defined powers to international authority while countries remain responsible for internal affairs: **federation**
- Must not let propaganda for "Federal Union" obscure that federation = **only form** creating international order without undue strain on legitimate independence
- Federation = **democracy with definitely limited powers**

### International Law Needs Enforcement Power

- In calling rules of international behavior "international law," we expressed a pious wish
- To prevent killing, we don't issue declaration that killing is undesirable — we give authority power to prevent it
- No international law without power to enforce it
- Obstacle to creating international power: idea it needs **practically unlimited powers** of modern state

### Division of Power Limits the Whole

- With division of power under federal system, this is **not necessary**
- Division would limit power of **whole as well as individual states**
- Many kinds of planning now fashionable would become **altogether impossible** — but this is an **advantage**
- Federation prevents most harmful planning while leaving way free for desirable planning

### Federation Prevents Restrictionism, Allows Local Planning

- Federation can prevent most forms of **restrictionism**
- Confines international planning to fields where true agreement can be reached among **immediately concerned**, not all affected
- Desirable planning effected **locally without restrictive measures**; left to those best qualified
- Hope: within federation, **centralization process may reverse**, devolution of powers from state to local authorities becomes possible

### 19th-Century Liberals Advocated Federation

- Idea of world finding peace through **absorption of states in large federated groups**, ultimately one federation, was ideal of **almost all liberal thinkers of 19th century**
- **Tennyson**: vision of "battle of the air" followed by **federation of the people** after last great fight
- Final achievement of federal organization = next great step in civilization
- 19th-century liberals may not have been fully aware **how essential** federal organization was as complement to their principles
- Few did not express belief in it as ultimate goal
- Only with 20th century approach did **Realpolitik** regard these hopes as unpracticable and utopian

### Small States and Local Democracy

- **No accident**: more beauty and decency in life of small peoples, more happiness/content in proportion as they avoided centralization
- Democracy/freedom cannot foster growth if all power/important decisions rest with organization **too big for common man to survey**
- Democracy never worked well without great local self-government
- Responsibility learned in familiar affairs; awareness of neighbor's needs (not theoretical knowledge) guides action
- Where political scope becomes so large that necessary knowledge is possessed **exclusively by bureaucracy**, creative impulses of private persons flag

### Experience of Small Countries

- Experience of **Holland and Switzerland** contains much from which even fortunate larger countries like Great Britain can learn
- We gain if we create a world **fit for small states to live in**

### Small States Need Rule of Law

- Small states can preserve independence in international and national sphere only within **true system of law**
- Guarantees: certain rules invariably enforced, authority with enforcement power cannot use it for any other purpose
- Supernational authority must be **very powerful** for enforcing common law but constitution designed to **prevent international/national authorities from becoming tyrannical**

### Never Prevent Abuse if We Don't Limit Power

- We shall **never prevent abuse of power** if not prepared to limit power occasionally preventing its use for desirable purposes
- Great opportunity at war's end: victorious powers, by submitting to a system of rules they have power to enforce, acquire **moral right to impose same rules on others**

### International Rule of Law as Safeguard

- International authority effectively limiting state powers over individuals = **best safeguard of peace**
- International Rule of Law must safeguard against **tyranny of state over individual** as much as tyranny of new superstate over national communities
- Neither omnipotent superstate nor loose association of "free nations" but **community of nations of free men**

### Goal: Community of Free Nations

- Goal: have long pleaded it had become impossible to behave in international affairs as desirable because **others would not play the game**
- Coming settlement = opportunity to show we are sincere, prepared to accept **same restrictions on freedom** we think necessary to impose on others

### Federation: Extremely Difficult Task

- Federal principle may prove **best solution** to world's most difficult problems
- But application is task of **extreme difficulty**; not likely to succeed if overambitious
- Strong tendency to make new international organization **all-comprehensive and world-wide**
- Imperative need for some comprehensive organization (new League of Nations)

### Danger of Overambition

- Great danger: if new world organization is charged with all desirable tasks, it will **not be adequately performed**
- My conviction: such ambitions were root of League of Nations' weakness — in attempt to make it world-wide, it had to be made **weak**
- Smaller, more powerful League might have been better instrument to preserve peace

### British Empire and Western Europe Federation

- Believe considerations still hold; degree of cooperation achievable between British Empire and western Europe nations would **not be possible on world scale**
- Comparatively close association of federal union represents will **not be practicable** beyond perhaps even as narrow a region as western Europe, though may be possible to gradually extend

### Regional Federations and Larger Associations

- With formation of regional federations, possibility of **war between blocs** remains
- To reduce risk, must rely on larger and looser association
- Point: need for some other organization should not obstruct **closer association** of countries more similar in civilization, outlook, standards

### Preventing Future Wars

- Must aim at preventing future wars as much as possible
- Must not believe we can create permanent organization making **all war impossible**
- Not only unsuccessful but would spoil chances of success in more limited sphere
- Measures to make war altogether impossible may be **worse than war itself**
- If we can reduce friction risk likely to lead to war, this is probably all we can reasonably hope to achieve

## Chapter 16: Conclusion

### The Book's Purpose and Limitations

- Book does not sketch detailed program for future society
- On international affairs, went little beyond essential critical task
- May soon be called to create framework for future growth
- Must be beginning of new, long, arduous process to create a world "very different from that which we knew during the last quarter of a century"

### Against Detailed Blueprints

- Doubtful whether detailed blueprint of internal order would be useful or competent at this stage
- Important thing now: agree on certain principles and free ourselves from past errors
- Before war, reached stage where clearing away obstacles more important than devising machinery for "guiding" and "directing"
- Need to release creative energy of individuals rather than plan progress
- First need: free ourselves from **contemporary obscurantism** that persuades us past actions were wise or inevitable

> "We shall not grow wiser before we learn that much that we have done was very foolish."

### The Courage to Start Fresh

- Must have courage to make new start—even if means *reculer pour mieux sauter* (stepping back to leap forward)
- Not those who believe in inevitable tendencies who show courage
- Not those who preach "New Order" as projection of last forty years' tendencies
- Those crying loudest for New Order are "most completely under the sway of the ideas which have created this war and most of the evils from which we suffer"
- Young generation right to have little confidence in ideas ruling most elders
- Young mistaken to believe these are still liberal ideas of nineteenth century

### Defending True Liberalism

- Younger generation "hardly knows" nineteenth-century liberal ideas
- Cannot possess power to go back to reality of nineteenth century, but have opportunity to realize its ideals—"and they were not mean"
- Little right to feel superior to grandfathers
- Twentieth century, not they, made mess of things
- If first attempt to create world of free men failed, must try again

> "The guiding principle that a policy of freedom for the individual is the only truly progressive policy remains as true today as it was in the nineteenth century."

### Footnote on *Reculer pour mieux sauter*

- Phrase means recommendation to back up a little before renewing attack
- American equivalent: "Drop back and punt"

### Nazi-Socialism (Spring 1933)

### National Socialism as Genuine Socialist Movement

- Recent events in Germany incomprehensible unless treated as "culmination of tendencies which date back to a period long before the Great War"
- Superficial to consider forces dominating Germany as reactionary (wanting return to 1914 order)
- Persecution of Marxists/democrats obscures fundamental fact: **National Socialism is genuine socialist movement**
- Leading ideas are "final fruit of the anti-liberal tendencies which have been steadily gaining ground in Germany since the later part of the Bismarckian era"
- Led majority of German intelligentsia first to "socialism of the chair" then to Marxism (social-democratic or communist form)

### Why Socialist Character Went Unrecognized

- Alliance with nationalist groups representing great industries and landowners
- This proves these groups too "have, at least partly, been mistaken as to the nature of the movement"
- Many capitalists themselves strongly influenced by socialistic ideas, lack sufficient belief in capitalism to defend it with clear conscience
- German entrepreneur class showed "incredible short-sightedness in allying themselves with a movement of whose strong anti-capitalistic tendencies there should never have been any doubt"

### Nazi Opposition to Established Socialists

- Nazi opposition to established socialist parties gained them sympathy of entrepreneurs
- Opposition only to very small extent directed against their economic policy
- What Nazis objected to: internationalism and cultural programme aspects still influenced by liberal ideas
- Accusations most effective in propaganda: corruption, nepotism, "alleged alliance with 'the golden International of Jewish Capitalism'"

### The 25 Points Programme

- Nationalists could hardly have advanced fundamental objections to economic policy of other socialist parties
- Their own programme differed only in that "its socialism was much cruder and less rational"
- Famous **25 points by Herr Feder** (one of Hitler's early allies)
  - Repeatedly endorsed by Hitler
  - Recognized by National-Socialist party by-laws as "immutable basis of all its actions"
  - Circulating throughout Germany in hundreds of thousands of copies
  - Full of ideas resembling early socialists
- **Dominant feature: fierce hatred of anything capitalistic**
  - Individualistic profit seeking
  - Large scale enterprise, banks, joint-stock companies, department stores
  - "International finance and loan capital"
  - System of "interest slavery"
- Abolition of these described as "[indecipherable] of the programme, around which everything else turns"
- Masses of German people, already under influence of collectivist ideas, "responded so enthusiastically"

### Genuine Anti-Capitalistic Attack

- Violent anti-capitalistic attack is genuine—not mere propaganda
- Clear from personal history of intellectual leaders and general *milieu* from which movement springs
- Not denied that many young men prominent in movement previously been communists or socialists
- Literary tendencies made German intelligentsia ready to join new party
- Common characteristic of politically influential writers (many free from party affiliations): **anti-liberal and anti-capitalist trend**
- Groups around review **"Die Tat"** made phrase **"the end of capitalism"** accepted dogma to most young Germans

### Anti-Liberal and Anti-Rational Character

- Movement more anti-liberal than anything else
- Closely connected: anti-rational, mystical and romantic sentiment growing for years among youth
- Protest against **"liberal intellectualism"** voiced by Berlin University students not isolated aberration but "true expression of feeling of great masses"
- Anti-rational tendencies in art and literature converged—often to amazement of originators—in Nazi movement
- Main influence destroying belief in universality and unity of human reason: **Marx's teaching** of class-conditioned nature of thinking
  - Difference between bourgeois and proletarian logic
  - Needed only to be applied to other social groups (nations, races) to supply weapon against rationalism
- Completely permeated German thought
- Past few years: promoted as **"sociology of knowledge"** to rank of new branch of learning
- From this intellectual relativism (denied existence of truths recognizable independently of race, nation, or class) only step to position putting sentiment above rational thinking

### Anti-Liberalism Leading to Universal Compulsion

- Anti-liberalism and anti-rationalism intimately bound up
- Easy to understand and inevitable: if rule by force by privileged group to be justified, its superiority must be accepted (cannot be proved)
- Less easily understood but of immense importance: **anti-liberalism, when confined to economic field, today has sympathy of almost all rest of world**
- Leads inevitably to reign of universal compulsion, intolerance, suppression of intellectual freedom
- **Inherent logic of collectivism**: impossible to confine it to limited sphere
- Beyond certain limits, collective action in interest of all only possible if all coerced into accepting as common interest what those in power take it
