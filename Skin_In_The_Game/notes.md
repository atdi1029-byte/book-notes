# Skin in the Game — Nassim Nicholas Taleb (2018)
# Raw Notes

## BOOK 1: INTRODUCTION

### Prologue, Part 1: Antaeus Whacked

**Core thesis** — Skin in the Game covers four intertwined topics:
a) Uncertainty and reliability of knowledge (BS detection)
b) Symmetry in human affairs (fairness, justice, reciprocity)
c) Information sharing in transactions
d) Rationality in complex systems and the real world

Skin in the game is NOT just about incentives or having a share of
benefits. It's about symmetry — having a share of the HARM, paying a
penalty when something goes wrong. This single idea ties together
incentives, used car buying, ethics, contract theory, learning,
Kantian imperative, municipal power, risk science, option theory,
theology, and more.

Key quote: "Don't tell me what you 'think,' just tell me what's
in your portfolio."

**Antaeus myth** — Antaeus was a giant (son of Gaea/Earth and Poseidon)
who forced passersby to wrestle, crushing them to build a temple of
skulls. He was invincible because he drew strength from contact with
the earth. Hercules defeated him by lifting him off the ground. Lesson:
you cannot separate knowledge from contact with the ground. Contact
with the real world = skin in the game. Greeks called it *pathemata
mathemata* — "guide your learning through pain."

**Libya vignette** — Interventionistas (Bill Kristol, Thomas Friedman, etc.)
promoted Iraq invasion 2003, then removal of Libyan leader 2011.
Result: Libya now has slave markets. They supported "moderate rebels"
who became al-Qaeda. Same al-Qaeda originally created to fight Soviets.
Pattern: they satisfy the objective of "removing a dictator" like a
doctor injecting cancer cells to improve cholesterol readings, then
claiming victory when patient dies with good cholesterol numbers.

Three flaws of interventionistas:
1) Think in statics not dynamics (can't think in second steps)
2) Think in low, not high, dimensions (reduce multidimensional
   problems to single metrics like cholesterol)
3) Think in terms of actions, never interactions

The downside doesn't affect the interventionist — he continues from
his suburban house with two-car garage. Innocent people — Ezidis,
Christians, Mandeans, Syrians, Iraqis, Libyans — pay the price.

Principle of intervention = first do no harm (*primum non nocere*).
Those who don't take risks should never be involved in making decisions.

**Warlords are still around** — Historically all warlords were warriors
themselves. Societies were run by risk takers, not risk transferors.
Roman emperor Julian the Apostate died fighting on the Persian frontier
*while emperor* — spear lodged in his chest. Less than a third of Roman
emperors died in their beds. Emperor Valerian was captured and used as
a human footstool by the Persian Shapur. Last Byzantine emperor
Constantine XI removed his purple toga and charged Turkish troops
facing certain death.

British Royal family sent Prince Andrew to front lines in Falklands
1982 — noblesse oblige. You can't be a lord if you aren't a lord.

**The Bob Rubin Trade** — Robert Rubin collected $120M+ from Citibank
in the decade before the 2008 crash. When the bank went insolvent
and was rescued by taxpayers, he didn't write any check — invoked
"Black Swan" as excuse. Heads he wins, tails he shouts "Black Swan."
Risk was transferred to taxpayers (schoolteachers, supervisors,
nutrition advisors). Government interference, not markets, enables
this — through bailout mechanisms.

Post-2008, risk-taking moved toward hedge funds — owner-operators
with at least half their net worth in funds, personally going down
with the ship. Regulations after 2008 avoided requiring skin in the
game despite thousands of pages of new rules.

"Bureaucracy is a construction by which a person is conveniently
separated from the consequences of his or her actions."

"Decentralization is based on the simple notion that it is easier
to macrobull***t than microbull***t."

**Systems learn by removing** — Absence of skin in the game has both
ethical AND epistemological effects. Risk transfer impedes learning.
"You will never fully convince someone that he is wrong; only
reality can."

"The curse of modernity is that we are increasingly populated by a
class of people who are better at explaining than understanding."

Learning in biology = intergenerational selection imprinted at cellular
level. Skin in the game is more filter than deterrence. Evolution
requires risk of extinction.

"There is no evolution without skin in the game."

Systems learn by removing parts, via negativa. Bad pilots end up at
the bottom of the Atlantic. Bad drivers are in the cemetery.
Transportation got safer not because individuals learn, but because
the system does — by filtering.

"Skin in the game keeps human hubris in check."

### Prologue, Part 2: A Brief Tour of Symmetry

**From Hammurabi to Kant** — Skin-in-the-game symmetry was the
principal rule for organized society, predating human settlement.
It exists even in the animal kingdom. The idea of law = fixing
imbalances and remedying asymmetries.

**Hammurabi's Code** — Posted on basalt stele ~3,800 years ago in
Babylon, 282 laws. Central theme: establishing symmetry. If a builder
builds a house that collapses and kills the owner, the builder shall
be put to death. If it kills the owner's son, the builder's son shall
be put to death. The builder has skin in the game.

Best place to hide risks = "in the corners" — rare events far away
in time/place so blowups happen when you're gone. Old alcoholic
English banker's career advice: "I give long-term loans only. When
they mature I want to be long gone. And only reachable long distance."
He survived by changing country every five years, wives every ten,
banks every twelve. Swiss were first to claw back past bonuses (2008).

*Lex talionis* ("eye for an eye") comes from Hammurabi — metaphorical
not literal. Talmudic discussion in *Bava Kamma*: if followed literally,
a one-eyed person blinding a two-eyed person pays only half; the
blind go scot-free. The tort system (thanks to Ralph Nader) imposes
*some* penalty — enough to protect consumers without literal amputation.

Taleb lectured French financiers 300 feet from the Hammurabi stele
in the Louvre — right after Ben Bernanke spoke (epitome of no skin
in game) — and nobody in the room knew about Hammurabi's connection
to banker accountability. Four millennia ago we were more sophisticated.

**Table 1: Evolution of Moral Symmetry**
- Hammurabi/Lex Talionis: "An eye for an eye"
- Leviticus 19.18: "Love your neighbor as yourself"
- Silver Rule: (implied — do not do to others...)
- Golden Rule (Matthew 7:12): "Do unto others as you would have
  them do unto you"
- Kant's Universal Law (1785): "Act only in accordance with that
  maxim through which you can at the same time will that it will
  become a universal law"

**Silver beats Gold** — The Silver Rule ("Do not treat others the way
you would not like them to treat you") is MORE robust than the Golden
Rule. Why? First, it tells you to mind your own business — don't
decide what's "good" for others. We know what is bad with much more
clarity than what is good. Via negativa (acting by removing) is more
powerful and less error-prone than via positiva (acting by addition).

The idea is fractal — works at all scales: individuals, families,
tribes, nations. Isocrates (5th century BC Athenian orator) applied
it to international relations: "Deal with weaker states as you think
it appropriate for stronger states to deal with you."

First Amendment = silver rule symmetry: you can practice your religion
so long as you allow me to practice mine. The gravest threat to
democracy is limiting speech because it might hurt feelings.

**Fuhgetaboud Universalism** — Kant's categorical imperative
("Behave as if your action can be generalized to everyone in all
places, under all conditions") sounds great on paper but is
disastrous in practice. We are local and practical animals.
The small is not the large; the tangible is not the abstract.
Micro works better than macro. The general and abstract tend to
attract self-righteous psychopaths (the interventionistas).

Kant didn't get the notion of scaling. The deep message of the book:
the danger of universalism taken two or three steps too far —
conflating the micro and the macro. Same problem as Black Swan's
*Platonification* — missing central but hidden elements when
transforming a thing into an abstract construct.

**II. From Kant to Fat Tony** — In New Jersey, symmetry means (in
Fat Tony's terms): *don't give crap, don't take crap*. More
practically: "Start by being nice to every person you meet. But
if someone tries to exercise power over you, exercise power over him."

Fat Tony = Incerto character, opposite of State Department analyst.
Calm, unfazed, wealthy from helping "the suckers" separate from
their funds. He generically calls them "the suckers."

Options link: In an option, one person (buyer) has the upside,
the other (seller) has liability for the downside. Disruption of
this symmetry = explosive situation (2008 crisis). Bankers' profits
accrued to them, losses quietly transferred to society.

**Crook, Fool, or Both** — "Avoid taking advice from someone who
gives advice for a living, unless there is a penalty for their
advice." Two types without skin in the game: the fool (takes risks
he doesn't understand, mistakes luck for skill) and the crook
(transfers risks to others via warped incentives). Economists
focus only on the crook.

Agency problem (principal-agent): vendor in one-shot transaction
can hide information. Insurance: you know more about your health
than insurers → adverse selection. Filters: high deductibles.

Fools of randomness are purged by reality (evolutionary filtering).
"We may not know beforehand if an action is foolish — but reality
knows."

**Causal Opacity and Preferences Revealed** — Fat Tony's motto:
"You do not want to win an argument. You want to win." Focus on
what people DO, not what they SAY. Revealed preferences > stated
preferences. A diamond (onerous to buyer) is more convincing
commitment than verbal promise.

"Forecasting (in words) bears no relation to speculation (in deeds)."
What matters in life isn't how frequently you're right about
outcomes, but how much you make when you're right. Being wrong,
when it's not costly, doesn't count — similar to trial-and-error.

The *inverse problem* in mathematics: harder to reverse-engineer
than to engineer. We can only run processes forward. Skin in the
game solves the Black Swan problem: what has survived has revealed
its robustness. Without skin in the game, we fail to get the
*Intelligence of Time* (Lindy effect): time removes the fragile
and the life expectancy of the nonfragile lengthens with time.

Rationality defined: a practice that has worked for a long time
cannot be called irrational even if we don't understand why.
If something *stupid* works (and makes money), it cannot be *stupid*.

"What is rational is what allows the collective — entities meant
to live for a long time — to survive." Some "overestimation" of
tail risk is NOT irrational — there are risks we cannot afford to
take (ergodic dimension, Chapter 19).

**Skin in the Game, but Not All the Time** — Focus on those who are
*professionally* slanted — causing harm without accountability by
the structure of their occupation. The professionally asymmetric
person is rare but causes a lot of problems. Most people (bakers,
plumbers, taxi drivers, accountants) pay a price for their mistakes.

**III. Modernism** — *Intellectualism*: the belief that one can
separate an action from its results, theory from practice, and fix
complex systems via top-down hierarchical approaches.
*Scientism*: naive interpretation of science as complication rather
than a skeptical enterprise. Replacing natural processes that have
survived trillions of stressors with peer-reviewed journal findings
that may not survive replication is neither science nor good practice.

"Those who talk should do and only those who do should talk."
Game theorist Ariel Rubinstein: do your theories, don't tell people
in the real world how to apply them.

Stage light example: speakers are uncomfortable because light beams
into their eyes, hindering concentration. Practice continues because
those who lecture don't work on lighting and light engineers don't
lecture.

Metro North train example: renovated trains got modern amenities but
the designer (who doesn't ride or doesn't read with coffee) tilted
the flat ledge where commuters put their coffee cups. Now it's
impossible to put a cup on it. Architects build to impress other
architects → irreversible structures that don't serve residents.

"Things designed by people without skin in the game tend to grow
in complication (before their final collapse)."
"Non-skin-in-the-game people don't get simplicity."

**I Am Dumb Without Skin in the Game** — *Pathemata mathemata*
reversed: learning through thrills and pleasure. People have two
brains — one when there's skin in the game, one when there's none.
Drug addicts with dull intellect are capable of most ingenious
tricks to procure drugs — but in rehab, their miraculous powers
disappear.

Taleb's confession: he never cared about mathematics until a friend
at Wharton told him about financial options. The combination of
trading and probability, with risk on the line, made his second
brain manifest. He knew in his guts that bell curve theories had
errors in the tails. As traders, the math fit their problem like a
glove, unlike academics inventing models from thin air.

**Regulations vs. Legal Systems** — Two ways to protect citizens
from corporate predators: (1) regulations — but these restrict
freedom, lead to regulatory capture, and parasites benefit from them.
Once in, they never leave. (2) Legal liability and lawsuits —
Anglo-Saxon approach: "if you harm me, I can sue you." Common law
is built bottom-up via trial and error — about the spirit.
Regulation is about the letter, rigid.

Deontic libertarianism: prefer freedom + civil responsibility +
penalty for harm. Freedom is one's first most essential good,
including freedom to make mistakes (that harm only you).

**IV. Soul in the Game** — Skin in the game is ultimately about
honor as an existential commitment. "If you do not take risks for
your opinion, you are nothing."

Honor means there are things you would categorically NEVER do
regardless of material rewards. And things you would DO
unconditionally regardless of consequences. Duels robbed us of
Pushkin, Galois, and many more — living as a coward was simply
no option. Spartan mother: "With it or on it" (return with your
shield or don't come back alive).

Modernity destroys this: people who work for disgusting lobbies
justify it with "I have children to put through college." People
fit ethics to their profession rather than finding a profession
that fits their ethics.

Beyond skin in the game: having your skin in OTHER people's game —
sacrifice for the collective. But also artisanal honor.

**Artisans** — "Anything you do to optimize your work, cut corners,
or squeeze more efficiency out of it will eventually make you
dislike it." Artisans have their soul in the game. They do things
for existential reasons first, commercial later. They combine art
and business. They have sacred taboos — things they wouldn't do
even if it markedly increased profitability.

*Compendiaria res improbitas, virtusque tarda* — the villainous
takes the short road, virtue the longer one. Cutting corners is
dishonest.

Fay Weldon paid by Bulgari to weave product recommendations into
her novel → disgust from literary community. Jerzy Kosinski caught
subcontracting book sections → ostracism. Alexandre Dumas ran a
workshop of 45 ghostwriters (joked he read "some" of his own books)
but that's the exception.

Yossi Vardi's advice: don't have an assistant. An assistant suspends
your natural filtering, forces you to do only things you enjoy.
Via negativa: maximal free time, not maximal activity.
"Having an assistant (except for the strictly necessary) removes
your soul from the game."

**Entrepreneurs** — Heroes who fail for the rest of us. But many
mistaken for having skin in the game: their real aim is to cash out
or go public. True value of the company and its long-term survival
are of small relevance — pure financing scheme. Identify them by
their ability to write a convincing business plan.

"The skills at making things diverge from those at selling things."

Companies beyond entrepreneur stage start to rot — mortality of
cancer patients comes from assignment of time-defined duties. Change
assignment or company → "It's not my problem anymore."

**Eponymy** — Products/companies that bear the owner's name convey
valuable messages — they have something to lose. Paul Wilmott
putting his name on a mathematical finance journal = commitment to
quality. "Egomaniac" is good for the product.

**Citizenship de Plaisance** — Many wealthy people living in the US
avoid becoming citizens — permanent residence is a free option
(right, not obligation). Reason: taxes. Once a US citizen, you pay
worldwide income tax even living overseas, and it's not easily
reversible. Other countries (France, UK) allow exemptions for
tax-haven residents. This invites "buying" citizenship via
investments + minimum residence, then living tax-free.

A country should not tolerate fair-weather friends. Taleb's parents
are French, making naturalization easy, but he refused — it felt
offensive without emotional attachment via skin in the game. Only
passport he'd consider: Greek/Cypriot (ancestral bond to
Hellenistic world). Instead, he came to the US and embraced it
as commitment and identity. Many made fun of him for not taking
residence in Cyprus/Malta for lower taxes. "If I want to lower
taxes, I am obligated to fight for it... and to not run away."

**Heroes Were Not Library Rats** — If you want classical values
(courage, stoicism), read the texts themselves: Seneca, Caesar,
Marcus Aurelius. Or commentators who were doers (Montaigne). Avoid
intermediaries. "Studying courage in textbooks doesn't make you
any more courageous than eating cow meat makes you bovine."

Heroes of history were people of deeds endowed with risk-taking
spirit. Career professors teaching stoicism almost never get it.
Classicists who know Alexander, Cleopatra, Caesar, Hannibal in
intimate detail can't produce "a shade of intellectual valor."
Academia and journalism = refuge of the *stochastophobe tawker*
(voyeur who wants to watch but not take risks).

**Soul in the Game and Protectionism** — Workers have an artisan
in them. Some protectionism has a strong rationale. It's not
irrational to leave money on the table for personal preference —
economics academia, which promotes self-interest, cannot explain
its own existence on self-interest alone. A shoemaker in
Westchester County wants to BE a shoemaker — even if outsourcing
to China and switching professions would be "economically
optimal." People want their soul in the game.
Decentralization and fragmentation improve people's connection
to their labor.

**Skin in the Ruling** — Story of the corrupt Persian judge
Sisamnes (from Herodotus): flayed alive by King Cambyses for
violating rules of justice. His son then dispensed justice from
his father's chair, upholstered with the flayed skin — literally,
skin in the game.

### Prologue, Part 3: The Ribs of the Incerto

Each Incerto book emerged from the previous one's ribs. *Black
Swan* was occasional discussion in *Fooled by Randomness*;
*Antifragile* was adumbrated in *The Black Swan*; *Skin in the
Game* was a segment of *Antifragile*: "Thou shalt not become
antifragile at the expense of others."

**The Bob Rubin Trade expanded** — Whenever bonus period (yearly)
mismatches blowup frequency (every ~10 years), agents have
incentive to play the risk-transfer game. Progressive
accumulation of Black Swan risks → systemic blowup.

**The Road** — Ethics side straightforward (Fat Tony–Isocrates
asymmetry). Tort law: Taleb accumulated close to 20 volumes on
his coffee table, but found it so dull he could read only 7 lines
per sitting. Law cannot be playful. Topics that match his
naturalistic filter: pagan theology, religious practices,
complexity theory, ancient/medieval history, probability, risk
taking.

Taleb learned Akkadian to recite Hammurabi's law with Semitic
phonetics — soul in the game for his own book.

**An Enhanced Detector** — After *Antifragile*, Taleb thought of
retiring but a mathematical brain teaser led to five years of
compulsive math practice. Result: oversensitive BS detector.
"Seeing the psychologist Steven Pinker making pronouncements about
things intellectual has a similar effect to encountering a drive-in
Burger King while hiking in the middle of a national park."

**Book Reviewers** — Many are honest, but industry has fundamental
conflict with public. Book editors and risk-takers can detect
what's interesting — nondoers chronically cannot. Reviewers
can't judge books that are meant to be *reread* (learning rooted
in repetition and convexity). *Mishnah* in Hebrew = "doubling."
Books should serve readers, not critics. Michiko Kakutani (NYT)
and David Runciman (Guardian) example of unchecked reviewer power.

**Organization of the Book** — Taleb's own roadmap:
- Book 2: Deeper exposition of symmetry/agency
- Book 3: Minority rule (how small segment inflicts preferences)
- Book 4: Dependence and modern slavery (employees vs contractors)
- Book 5: Risk-taking and appearances; IYI; inequality; Lindy
- Book 6: Consequential hidden asymmetries (BS detection,
  journalists, virtue, peace)
- Book 7: Religion, belief, skin in the game (revealed preferences)
- Book 8: Rationality and risk (ergodicity, precautionary principle)

## BOOK 2: A FIRST LOOK AT AGENCY

### Chapter 1: Why Each One Should Eat His Own Turtles

**Turtle origin story** — Group of fishermen caught many turtles.
After cooking, turtles were much less edible than expected. Few
members wanted to eat them. Mercury (god of commerce, thieves,
luck) happened by; they offered him the turtles. Detecting he was
invited only to relieve them of unwanted food, he forced them ALL
to eat the turtles — establishing the principle: you must eat
what you feed others.

**A Customer Is Born Every Day** — "Beware of the person who gives
advice, telling you that a certain action on your part is 'good
for you' while it is also good for him, while the harm to you
doesn't directly affect him."

Lecture agent anecdote: Agent sent letter with ~10 questions
designed to make Taleb's life "better" — really to offload the
gritty work while agent collected fees. Six years later, Taleb
got letter from foreign tax authorities. Agent's reply: "I am not
your tax attorney."

Rule: What's presented as "good for you" is typically good for
the *other* party. Upright traders deal with those who admit
they're selling for their own benefit ("Do you have an ax?").

**White shoe investment bank** — Salespeople wore "black shoes" to
unload "stuffed" inventory (securities traders wanted to dump).
Couldn't sell to other professionals (they'd smell it). Sold to
the "buy side" — clients, especially unsophisticated ones.
Salesmen paid "points" (variable percentage) that increased with
eagerness to dump. One expert salesman: "If I buy the client,
someone from a New Jersey finance department who buys suits at a
department store, a bottle of $2,000 wine, I own him for months.
I can get $100,000 profits. Nothing in the *mahket* gives you
such return."

Top salesman arrived in chauffeured Rolls Royce. Asked if
customers got upset: "Rip them off, don't tick them off."
"Remember that every day a new customer is born."

**The Price of Corn in Rhodes** — Ancient ethics debate from
Cicero's *De Officiis*: Two Stoics — Diogenes of Babylon and his
student Antipater of Tarsus. Scenario: merchant brings corn to
Rhodes during famine, knowing more ships are coming. Must he
disclose? Diogenes: disclose only what civil law requires.
Antipater: disclose everything, beyond the law.

Traders had their own code: "stuffing" to other professional
traders = ostracism. But doing it to anonymous market/"the Swiss"
(random suckers) was permissible. *Relational* vs *transactional*
ethical wall — like domestic animals (protected) vs cockroaches.

Antipater's position is more robust — invariant to time, place,
participants. "The ethical is always more robust than the legal.
Over time, it is the legal that should converge to the ethical,
never the reverse." "Laws come and go; ethics stay."

Investment banks played on regulations, found loopholes.
Counterintuitively, more regulations = easier to make money.

**Equality in Uncertainty** — Core concept: *gharar* in Sharia law
(Islamic transaction law). Taleb views Sharia as "a museum of the
history of ideas on symmetry in transactions" — intersection of
Greco-Roman law, Phoenician trading rules, Babylonian legislation,
Arab tribal customs. *Gharar* = both uncertainty and deception;
Taleb's interpretation: *inequality of uncertainty*. "No person
in a transaction should have certainty about the outcome while
the other one has uncertainty." If one party has certainty = Sharia
violation. But weak asymmetry (like inside information giving an
edge) is acceptable since both parties face uncertainty about the
future price.

**Rav Safra and the Swiss** — Jewish ethics on transparency closer
to Antipater than Diogenes. Not just transparency of merchandise
but of *intentions*. Rashi relates: Rav Safra, a Babylonian scholar
AND active trader, was praying when a buyer offered a price for
his goods. He didn't reply (praying), so buyer raised the price.
Rav Safra had no intention of selling above the initial offer and
felt bound to honor the original price. Most shame-free policy:
maximal transparency, even of intentions.

But the story doesn't tell if the buyer was a "Swiss" (outsider
to whom ethical rules might be relaxed). The more confined ethics
are, the less abstract, the better they work. Nietzsche: "Sympathy
for all would be tyranny for thee, my good neighbor." Nietzsche is
the one person Fat Tony said he would never debate.

**Members and Non-Members** — Things don't "scale" and generalize.
A country is not a large city; a city is not a large family; the
world is not a large village. Athenians applied "democracy" only
to citizens, not slaves or metics (H-1B visa equivalent).
Theodosius's code deprived Roman citizens who married "barbarians"
of legal rights. Jewish ethics: thick and thin blood — "we are all
brothers, but some are more brothers than others."

Roma people have strict rules toward Gypsies, others toward
*unclean* non-Gypsies (*payos*). David Graeber observed even
Goldman Sachs acts like a communist community from *within*.

There IS a limit from scaling — "the general kills the particular."
Being tribal is not bad — work in fractal harmonious relations
between tribes. American-style federalism is ideal.

Yaneer Bar-Yam showed "better fences make better neighbors."
Scaling matters. Putting Shiites, Christians, Sunnis in one pot
singing "Kumbaya" around a campfire has failed.

People get along better as neighbors than as roommates. In
Taleb's ancestral village, people attend funerals, help out,
care about neighbors even if they hate each other's dogs. Ethics
is fundamentally local.

Modernity put in our heads two units: individual and universal
collective. But my skin lies in a family, community, tribe,
fraternity — not the universal.

**Non Mihi Non Tibi, Sed Nobis** — Elinor Ostrom's finding:
below a certain community size, people act as collectivists,
protecting the commons (tragedy of the commons solved). The
commons cannot be too large — it's like a club. Groups behave
differently at different scales. Municipal ≠ national.

Skin-in-the-game definition of commons: "a space in which you
are treated by others the way you treat them, where everyone
exercises the Silver Rule."

**Are You on the Diagonal?** — Geoff and Vince Graham saying:
"I am, at the Fed level, libertarian; at the state level,
Republican; at the local level, Democrat; and at the family
and friends level, a socialist."

Switzerland's political system: neither left nor right but
governance-based. Works because of scaling (federation).
Hans Gersbach organized workshop on rewarding/punishing
politicians whose interests diverge from constituents'.

**All (Literally) in the Same Boat** — Greek *Synkyndineo* =
"taking risks together" — opposite of risk transfer. Required
in maritime transactions. *Acts of the Apostles*: ship from
Sidon to Crete hits storm → they throw corn overboard.

Rhodian Law (~800 BC): when merchandise is thrown overboard
to lighten a ship, all owners proportionally share the loss —
not just the specific owner. Same with desert caravans: stolen
merchandise costs split among all merchants.

*Comppericlity* (risk sharing) vs *incompericlity* (Bob Rubin
risk transfer).

**Talking One's Book** — TV roundtable about Microsoft. Taleb's
turn: "I own no Microsoft stock, I am short no Microsoft stock,
hence I can't talk about it." Repeated his dictum: "Don't tell
me what you think, tell me what you have in your portfolio."

Two types: (1) buy stock, THEN comment on it (disclosing) —
user is the most reliable advocate. (2) Buy stock to trumpet
it, then sell — market manipulation. We removed journalists'
skin in the game to prevent manipulation, but without it they
imitate each other → monoculture and collective mirages.

**A Short Visit to the Doctor's Office** — Medicine is
apprenticeship-based, grounded in experience. But doctors face
skin-in-the-game distortions: "The legal system and regulatory
measures are likely to put the skin of the doctor in the wrong
game."

Every metric is gameable. Cancer doctor: tradeoff between laser
surgery (precise but worse 5-year stats) vs radiation therapy
(better 5-year survival but creates second tumors, worse 20-year
survival). Window = 5 years → incentive to choose radiation.
"A doctor is pushed by the system to transfer risk from himself
to you, and from the present into the future."

## BOOK 6: DEEPER INTO AGENCY

### Chapter 7: Inequality and Skin in the Game

**Two Types of Inequality** — Static inequality (snapshot
of who has what right now) vs. dynamic inequality (how people
move between levels over time). These are completely different
animals. Americans tolerate inequality because they believe
it's dynamic — the rich earned it and could lose it.
Europeans focus on static inequality and resent it.

Joan C. Williams's insight: the working class doesn't resent
the rich — they resent professionals. "The class of people
who suggest remedies do not fall prey to them." The liberal
professional class creates policies that affect others but
not themselves. A mandarin class.

**Ergodicity** — The central concept. An ensemble probability
(what happens across many people at one point in time) differs
from time probability (what happens to one person over time).
100 people going to a casino: one may go bust. One person
going to a casino 100 days in a row: will almost certainly
go bust. The ensemble average shows the casino profitable;
the time series shows the gambler ruined.

"The difference between 100 people going to a casino and
one person going to a casino 100 times, i.e., between
(path dependent) and conventionally understood probability.
The first is concerned with a collection of people, the
second with a single person through time."

The distinction matters enormously because economists
typically use ensemble averages when they should use time
averages. A strategy that looks good on average across
populations can be ruinous for an individual facing
sequential decisions with absorbing barriers (bankruptcy,
death).

**Absorbing Barriers** — An absorbing barrier is a point
you cannot come back from (death, bankruptcy, ruin). If
you face even a small probability of ruin on each round,
over enough rounds you WILL hit it. "No probabilistic
advantage is meaningful if there is a risk of total ruin."
This invalidates most cost-benefit analyses that ignore
tail risks.

The key insight: you need to survive FIRST, then optimize.
Never cross a road with a blindfold for a marginal efficiency
gain, no matter what the expected value calculation says.
Survival is not negotiable.

**Piketty's Capital Critique** — Thomas Piketty's *Capital
in the 21st Century* claimed the rich get richer (r > g).
Taleb says Piketty confuses the process with the result.
The same person doesn't stay in the top bracket — there's
constant turnover. In dynamic inequality, the *slot* stays
rich but the *person* occupying it rotates. Forbes 400:
most drop out within 30 years.

Furthermore, Piketty doesn't account for the absorbing
barrier — wealthy families that go bust. If you properly
account for the trajectory of individual families (not
static snapshots), inequality is far more dynamic than
Piketty admits. Taleb had a mathematical paper showing
this — if you account for ergodicity, r > g doesn't
produce the permanent aristocracy Piketty fears.

**Cobbler Envies Cobbler** — Aristotle said envy operates
within peer groups, not across distant classes. A cobbler
envies another cobbler, not the king. "People can stand
inequality if they are given a chance at mobility."
Americans accept CEOs making millions IF they believe their
children could get there. If mobility freezes, tolerance
for inequality collapses.

The dangerous inequality isn't income — it's *cronyism*.
When the rich get rich through rent-seeking (bailouts,
regulatory capture, government contracts) rather than
risk-taking, that's when skin in the game is violated
and society rightfully revolts.

**Vertical Socialization** — Taleb values communities where
the rich and poor interact daily (vertical socialization)
over segregated communities where classes never meet. In
his childhood Lebanon, the wealthiest people in town lived
next to workers. American suburbs segregate by income.
This segregation makes inequality more visible and bitter.

**Data Shmata** — Warning about empirical research on
inequality. Most studies use static snapshots (Gini
coefficients at a point in time). A country where people
rotate through income quintiles has the same Gini as one
where positions are frozen — but they're vastly different
societies. "Do not use statistics on income brackets to
discuss inequality — the same person does not stay in the
same bracket."

**Steven Pinker Critique** — Pinker's *Better Angels of Our Nature*
claims decline of violence and attributes it to modern institutions.
Taleb and Pasquale Cirillo scrutinized his data and found he either
didn't understand his own numbers or had a story in mind and kept
adding charts. "Statistics isn't about data but distillation, rigor,
and avoiding being *fooled by randomness*." His state-worshipping
IYI colleagues found it impressive — for a while.

**Ethics of Civil Service** — Worse than inequality: non-risk-takers
getting rich from public service. Obama accepted $40M+ for memoirs
on leaving office. His supporters defended him while criticizing
rich entrepreneurs. "Those who did not earn the money via commerce
were illogically exempt." Rich people entering public office show
some evidence of competence; public people becoming rich is the
opposite dynamic.

*"It is downright unethical to use public office for enrichment."*
Public servants should pledge to never earn more than a set amount
from private sector afterward — the rest goes to taxpayer. You don't
become a Jesuit priest because Goldman Sachs may hire you later.

Civil servants in sensitive areas (agro-alimentary, finance,
aerospace, Saudi-related) leave to join the industries they
regulated. Implicit bribe: act friendly to banking, then go to
J.P. Morgan for a multiple of your salary. Regulators have
incentive to make rules complex so their expertise sells later.
Tim Geithner helped bankers get bailouts, let them pay largest
bonus pool in history *after* the crisis (2010, using taxpayer
money), then got a multimillion-dollar financial job as reward.

**Domain Dependence of Expertise** — Electrician, dentist, London
cabby, algebraic geometer are real experts. Journalist, State
Department bureaucrat, clinical psychologist, macroeconomist are
not. "Who is the real expert? Who decides who is and who is not
an expert? Where is the meta-expert?" Answer: time. The Lindy
effect.

### Chapter 8: An Expert Called Lindy

**The Lindy Effect** — Lindy's is a deli in New York, now a
tourist trap, famous for cheesecake. Physicists and mathematicians
who hung out there noticed that Broadway shows lasting 100 days
had a future life expectancy of 100 more. Shows lasting 200 days
had 200 more. The heuristic: for nonperishable things, life
expectancy *lengthens* with survival time.

Taleb and Iddo Eliazar formalized the Lindy effect through
fragility theory. Fragility = sensitivity to disorder. A porcelain
owl wants tranquility — shocks of larger intensity hurt
disproportionally more (nonlinear response). Time is equivalent
to disorder; survival = ability to handle disorder.

*"That which is fragile has an asymmetric response to volatility
and other stressors, that is, will experience more harm than
benefit from it."*

Volatility and time are the same thing in probability. The only
effective judge of things is time — ideas, books, car models,
scientific theories. "Books written by the current hotshot Op-Ed
writer at The New York Times may get some hype at publication
time... but their five-year survival rate is generally less than
that of pancreatic cancer."

**Who Is the "Real" Expert?** — Lindy answers *quis custodiet
ipsos custodes* (who guards the guard). Survival will. Things
that survive hint they have robustness — conditional on exposure
to harm. Without skin in the game, fragility mechanisms break:
things survive for no reason then collapse causing collateral harm.

Two ways things handle time: (1) aging/senescence (biological
clock) and (2) hazard rate (accidents). Perishable things (humans)
age. Nonperishable things (ideas, technologies) can be Lindy.
A 20-year-old crocodile has 40 more years; a 40-year-old also
has 40 more. Ideas work this way.

*"That which is 'Lindy' is what ages in reverse, i.e., its life
expectancy lengthens with time, conditional on survival."*

We solve the "turtles all the way down" problem — fragility is
the expert, hence time and survival. Periander of Corinth (2500
years ago): *"Use laws that are old but food that is fresh."*
Alfonso X of Spain: *"Burn old logs. Drink old wine. Read old
books. Keep old friends."*

**Do We Need a Judge?** — Taleb held a quarter academic position
for most of his career. A department head warned him: "Life is
about peer assessment." Taleb's disgust: businessmen as risk
takers are NOT judged by other businessmen, only by their
accountant. A pit trader's wisdom: "If people over here like
you, you are doing something wrong."

*"You can define a free person precisely as someone whose fate
is not centrally or directly dependent on peer assessment."*

As an essayist, judged by readers — but not today's readers.
Future readers. Time counts. "I could fool my accountant with
steady earnings with a lot of hidden risk, but time will
eventually reveal them."

**Tea with the Queen** — Peers devolve into honors, Nobels,
Davos invitations, tea with the Queen. Rich people whose lives
revolve around trying to save the world — broadcasting virtue.
But they can't influence Lindy. "Contemporary peers are valuable
collaborators, not final judges."

**Institutions** — Worse than peer assessment: bureaucratization
creates university administrators as arbiters who judge via
external signals. "Prestigious" publication = currently powerful
people happy with your work, not Lindy-compatible. Social science:
citation rings, macroeconomics can be nonsense. Twenty people
set up an academy saying crazy things accepted by the collective
= "peer-reviewing" department.

*"Academia has a tendency, when unchecked (from lack of skin
in the game), to evolve into a ritualistic self-referential
publishing game."* Wittgenstein: in philosophy, the winner is
the one who finishes last. "Anything that smacks of competition
destroys knowledge."

Gender studies, psychology — ritualistic publishing diverges
from real research. Knowing "economics" means knowing theories
(mostly nonsense) not real economic activity. Parents save for
children to be taught post-colonial critique of quantum mechanics.

**Against One's Interest** — Most convincing statements are where
one has maximal skin in the game (stands to lose). Showing off
is human, fine as long as substance exceeds it. Weight research
that contradicts peers and entails reputational harm. "Someone
with a high public presence who is controversial and takes risks
for his opinion is less likely to be a bull***t vendor."

**Soul in the Game** — Research should be deprostitutionalized:
do it on your own time, derive income elsewhere. Genuine
researchers should have real-world day jobs — lens maker, patent
clerk, Mafia operator, professional gambler, firefighter,
lighthouse keeper. Taleb spent 23 years in a highly stressful
profession writing his first three books at night — this
eliminated tolerance for career-building research. Science is
a minority rule: a few run it, others are back-office clerks.

**Science Is Lindy-Prone** — Popper's falsification is Lindy-
compatible. But Taleb modifies Popper: replace "true" with
"useful," "not harmful," "protective to users." Ideas survive
not in the truth game but the harm game — an idea is a good
risk manager if it doesn't harm holders and favors survival.
Superstitions surviving centuries led to protective actions.
Paul Feyerabend: anything goes in the process — but not with
the test of time.

**Grandmother vs. Researchers** — Grandmother's advice works
90% of the time. Psychologists/behavioral scientists: less than
10%, unless it was already covered by grandmother and the
classics. Replication crisis: of 100 psychology papers in
"prestigious" 2008 journals, only 39 replicated. Fewer than
10 are truly robust. Grandmother's "irrational" warnings aren't
irrational — most of what's called "irrational" comes from
misunderstanding probability.

Books of ancients survived by Lindy AND the populations reading
them survived. Everything in social science/psychology has to be
Lindy-proof — have antecedent in classics (Cicero, Seneca,
Marcus Aurelius, Epictetus, La Rochefoucauld, Montaigne).

**Ancient Wisdom Tour** — Cognitive dissonance = Aesop's sour
grapes (from Assyrian Ahiqar). Loss aversion = Livy's "Men feel
the good less intensely than the bad." Via negativa = Ennius/
Cicero's "The good is not as good as the absence of bad." Skin
in the game = Yiddish: "Your fingernail can best scratch your
itch." Time discounting = "Bird in hand better than ten in tree."
Madness of crowds = Nietzsche (from Plato). Less is more =
Publilius Syrus. Overconfidence = Theognis of Megara.

Paradox of progress: the NY banker and Greek fisherman story —
banker proposes scheme to make millions and vacation in Greece;
fisherman already vacations in Greece. Montaigne's version:
Cyneas tells King Pyrrhus "But you are already there — why take
more risks?"

### Chapter 9: Surgeons Should Not Look Like Surgeons

**Looking the Part** — Two surgeons: first is refined, silver-
rimmed glasses, Ivy League diplomas, movie-casting perfect.
Second looks like a butcher — overweight, large hands, uncouth
speech, gold tooth, strong New Yawk accent, maybe went to a
local college. Taleb would take the butcher every time. Why?
The one who *doesn't* look the part, conditional on having made
a successful career, had much more to overcome. Reality is
blind to looks — skin in the game filtered out incompetence.

When results come through reality (not committees/evaluators),
image matters less. CEOs of corporations look the part because
image matters in hierarchical "job evaluation" systems —
executives are actors, different from entrepreneurs.

Success *in spite of not looking the part* is potent information.
Reagan was a former actor; Obama was even more of an actor (Ivy
League education as image builder). The *millionaire next door*
doesn't look rich. A Lebanese village neighbor, modest life,
left estate of $100 million — 100 times what expected.

"Next time you randomly pick a novel, avoid the one with the
author photo representing a pensive man with an ascot standing
in front of wall-to-wall bookshelves."

Flipping the argument: skilled thieves at large don't look like
thieves — those who do are more likely to be in jail.

*"In any type of activity or business divorced from the direct
filter of skin in the game, the great majority of people know
the jargon, play the part, and are intimate with the cosmetic
details, but are clueless about the subject."*

**The Green Lumber Fallacy** — From *Antifragile*: a fellow made
a fortune trading green lumber without knowing what it was (freshly
cut wood, not lumber painted green). The person who knew every
detail went bankrupt. What you need to know in practice doesn't
match what looks important intellectually. "In any activity,
hidden details are only revealed via Lindy."

Never hire a well-dressed trader. *"Hire the successful trader,
conditional on a solid track record, whose details you can
understand the least."* Terry B.'s investment class: polished
speaker (soup kitchen of business) vs. incomprehensible butcher-
type (centimillionaire). Jimmy Powers — Brooklyn street gangster,
college dropout, successful trader: "We did this and then did
that, badaboom, badabing, and then it was all groovy." "The
people you understand most easily were necessarily the bull***tters."

**Best-Dressed Business Plan** — Literature should not look like
literature. Georges Simenon learned from Colette to strip prose
to the core. Business plans are useful narratives for convincing
suckers. Microsoft, Apple, Facebook, Google were started by people
with skin and soul in the game who grew organically — funding
was not the prime source of creation.

*"Never hire an academic unless his function is to partake of
the rituals of writing papers or taking exams."*

**A Bishop for Halloween** — Mediterranean societies: highest-
ranking person has most skin in the game (economic risk-taking).
Anglo-Saxon culture transferred martial values to commerce.
Arabic culture also honors risk-taking. But history shows
societies where intellectuals were on top (Hindus/Brahmans,
Celts/druids, Chinese/scholars, postwar France) — they
maintained power through complex rituals, mysteries, and focus
on the cosmetic. The bishop on rollerblades would no longer be
a bishop. Decorative is fine for religion; science and business
must not be decorative.

**The Gordian Knot** — Never pay for complexity when all you need
is results. Alexander the Great at Gordium: oracle said whoever
untied the impossible knot would rule Asia. Alexander drew his
sword and cut it in half. No "successful" academic or IYI would
follow such a policy. Medicine: aspirin or good night's sleep
better than brain surgery for a headache, though the latter
appears "more scientific."

**Overintellectualization of Life** — Gigerenzer and Brighton:
baseball player catches a ball using the "gaze heuristic" (fix
gaze, run, keep angle constant) — NOT by solving differential
equations as Dawkins claims. Religious "beliefs" are mental
heuristics solving problems without the agent knowing how.
People paid to find complicated solutions have no incentive to
implement simple ones. The remedy itself has a skin-in-the-game
problem.

**Gold and Rice** — Golden Rice: genetically modified to include
vitamins. Taleb's team showed: (1) transgenics not same category
as traditional crossbreeding; (2) patent increase in systemic
risk; (3) no proper risk study; (4) GMOs brought hidden
environmental risk (higher pesticide killing microbiome). "Why
don't we give these people rice and vitamins separately?"
GMOs lost because an intransigent minority stood against them.

**The Compensation** — Evaluation forms create distortions. In
*The Black Swan*, Taleb tore up his evaluation form (asking
percentage of profitable days) in front of the big boss.
Russian Roulette makes money 5/6 times. Banks lose less than
1/100 quarters but then lose more than they ever made.

**Education as Luxury Good** — Harvard is like a Vuitton bag for
the new Asian upper class. Student loans transfer to bureaucrats,
real estate developers, tenured professors of disciplines that
wouldn't otherwise exist. "The level of (formal) education in
a country is the result of wealth" — not the cause.

**BS Detection Heuristic** — Hire the person with the least
label-oriented education, conditional on equal skills. If a
discipline's value depends on school prestige, it's BS. Math
on arXiv is fine; academic finance needs "prestigious" journal.

**Real Gyms Don't Look Like Gyms** — Fancy gym machines look
scientific but are scientism. Users become pear-shaped with
non-transferable skills. The barbell — simplest, cheapest —
recruits your entire body. Weight lifting phenomenology: 2500+
years old (Lindy). "All you need are shoes to run outside
and a barbell with weights." Gains come from working the tails
of the distribution, close to your limit.

### Chapter 10: Only the Rich Are Poisoned

**Constructed Preferences** — When people get rich, they shed
skin-in-the-game-driven preferences, substituting constructed
preferences that complicate their lives and trigger misery.
These constructed preferences serve those selling to them —
a skin-in-the-game problem.

**Michelin Restaurant** — Taleb's dinner at a Michelin-starred
restaurant: microscopic ingredients, contrasting tastes forcing
concentration like an entrance exam, sommelier's bull***t about
paired wine. "You were not eating, rather visiting some type
of museum." Left starving. Would have preferred pizza with
fresh ingredients at 1/20th the price.

*Venenum in auro bibitur* — "Poison is drunk in golden cups"
(Seneca's Thyestes). The rich are natural targets for scams
via complications. Financial consultants, diet advisors,
exercise experts, lifestyle engineers, breathing specialists —
an entire industry meant to swindle you. Hamburgers are
vastly tastier than filet mignon due to higher fat content.

**Large Funeral Homes** — People happier in close barrio-style
neighborhoods. Big bucks pressure move to outsized, impersonal,
silent mansions far from neighbors. Historically, mansions
teemed with servants, butlers, musicians. Now they're funeral
homes on Sunday evening. Vauvenargues: some things can be too
large for your heart. Rome was easy to love as a village,
harder as an empire.

**Conversation** — Being rich requires hiding your money (and
erudition) to have real friends. Classical art of conversation
(Castiglione's *Book of the Courtier*): people must be equal.
Hierarchy kills conversation. "You'd rather have dinner with
your friends than with your professor."

**Nonlinearity of Progress** — Sophistication can cause
"negative utility." Choice between $200 French experience and
$6.95 pizza: Taleb would pay $200 for the pizza plus $9.95
Malbec. Inverted U curve: level beyond which wealth causes
incremental harm. "If wealth is giving you fewer options
instead of more (and more varied) options, you're doing it
wrong." As society gets richer, someone will try to sell you
something until the point of degradation.

### Chapter 11: Facta non Verba (Deeds Before Words)

**An Offer Very Hard to Refuse** — The Godfather's horse head
scene. The executive was made an offer he could not refuse.
"It was a threat, and not an empty threat."

**Two Types of Terrorists** — Category error: (1) indiscriminate
terrorists (Al Qaeda, ISIS, "moderate rebels" in Syria) who kill
civilians for effect, seeking paradise; (2) strategic political
assassination groups (IRA, Algerian independence fighters, French
Resistance, Shiite organizations). The ancestry of the second
group traces to the Judean Sicarii (named for their daggers)
during Roman times, and through them to the Order of the Assassins.

**The Assassins** — Actual assassination was low on their agenda.
They understood non-cheap messaging. They preferred to *own*
their enemies. "The only enemy you cannot manipulate is a dead
one." In 1118, Sultan Ahmad Sanjar of the Seljuk Turkish Empire
woke with a dagger firmly planted beside his bed. A letter:
the dagger in hard ground was preferable to the alternative
(his soft breast). The Hashishins/Assassins made him aware of
the need to leave them alone. Sultan Sanjar had previously
snubbed peace negotiators, so they moved to phase two. They
convinced him his life was in their hands — and he didn't have
to worry if he did the right thing. "Sanjar and the Assassins
had a happy life ever after."

No explicit verbal threat was issued. *"Verbal threats reveal
nothing beyond weakness and unreliability."*

Assassins were an 11th-14th century Shiite sect, violently
anti-Sunni, often allied with Knights Templar, sharing values
of sparing the innocent. They sent same message to Saladin
(cake he was about to eat was poisoned... by themselves).
Their ethic: political assassination prevents war; dagger-
by-your-bed threats are even better for bloodless control.

**Assassination as Marketing** — In politics, make it look
intentional (opposite of criminal hits where you want it to
look accidental). War numbers historically inflated by both
sides: Mongols and their victims both had incentive to
exaggerate. Gene transfer happens by migrations and climate,
not war. Hama "massacre" of 1982: documented casualties at
least an order of magnitude lower than reported — numbers
swelled from 2,000 to 40,000 without new information.

**Assassination as Democracy** — If democracy doesn't fully
deliver governance (cronyisms, covert legal corruption), what
works is increased turnover at the top. Russian Constitution:
"Absolutism tempered by assassination." Today's politicians
have no skin in game, stay on the job far longer than historical
rulers. Mitterrand reigned 14 years with more power than most
French kings. Roman mechanism: bad emperors (Caligula, Caracalla,
Elagabalus, Nero) ended murdered by Praetorian Guard or by
suicide. Less than a third of emperors in first 400 years died
naturally.

**The Camera for Skin in the Game** — You no longer need horses'
heads. Take pictures of unethical people — silently, without
verbal threat. NYC subway: rude man insulted Taleb, Taleb pulled
out cell phone and photographed him — man "freaked out and ran
away." Parking spot thief: photographed license plate, man
rapidly drove away. Mountain cyclists on banned trail: Taleb
calmly took a dozen pictures — they never returned. But warns:
web-shaming is more powerful than past reputational blots. Ring
of Gyges (Plato's *Republic*): do people behave well because
they're watched, or because of their character? Socrates says
character. Virtue goes beyond pleasing watchers.

### Chapter 12: The Facts Are True, the News Is Fake

**How to Disagree with Yourself** — Summer 2009: Taleb had an
hour-long discussion with David Cameron at the Royal Society
for the Arts about making society robust to Black Swans.
59 minutes summarized by press from a tangential 20-second
comment taken in reverse of intended meaning — called a

weaken Cameron by demonizing Taleb. *The Guardian* tried to
tone down his correction into a "disagreement" with what *he*
said — they wanted him to disagree with himself.

**Information Doesn't Like to Be Owned** — Journalism isn't
Lindy compatible. In Ancient Rome, information transmitted
organically via marketplaces, barbers, pubs, coffee houses.
Taleb's grandmother had her "rounds" of condolences in
Beirut's Greek Orthodox community and knew everything.
Unreliable people carried less weight. The one-sided media
era (mid-20th century to 2016 election) was an aberration.
Social networks returned information to its natural two-way
format — Lindy strikes. Journalists worry about opinions of
other journalists (not readers) — opposite of restaurants
where owners worry about customers.

**The Ethics of Disagreement** — Criticize what a person *meant*,
not what they *said*. The charlatan (e.g. Sam Harris) defends
by focusing on specific statements, not the broader position.
"Give me a few lines written by any man and I will find enough
to get him hung" — Richelieu/Voltaire/Talleyrand. Popper started

dismantling. Aquinas's *Summa Theologica*: "Questio," then
"Objections," then "Sed Contra." Straw man = theft. Principle
of charity (understand a message as if you were its author)
is Lindy — Isaiah 29:21 warns against making "a man an
offender for a word." Calumny was severe crime in Babylon.

## BOOK 8: RISK AND RATIONALITY

### Chapter 14: Peace, Neither Ink nor Blood

**Interventionistas Disrupt Peace** — Wanting to get involved
in other people's affairs "in order to help" disrupts peace-
making mechanisms inherent in human affairs. If IYIs hadn't
gotten involved, the Israeli-Palestinian problem would have
been solved. "When you leave people alone, they tend to settle
for practical reasons."

**Arabs Fighting to the Last Palestinian** — Arab states prodded
Palestinians to fight for principles while potentates sat in
carpeted palaces with well-stocked refrigerators. Had
Palestinians settled in 1947, better off. 1948 war. Had they
settled then, better off. 1967 war. 1992 Oslo (from the top,
bureaucratic ink). "No peace proceeds from bureaucratic ink.
If you want peace, make people trade."

**Mars vs. Saturn** — D.C. pundits see everything as geopolitics
(Iran vs. Saudi Arabia, U.S. vs. Russia). During the Lebanese
war, local conflict metamorphosed into "Israel vs. Iran" problem.
War journalists got information from other war journalists.
Real people on the ground want bread, beer, and good weather
at family picnics — commonalities and peace, not conflicts.
Pro-Israeli governments (Morocco, Egypt, Saudi) have hostile
populations toward Jews; Iran's population is tolerantly pro-
Western. Relations between countries differ from relations
between governments.

**Where Are the Lions?** — South African safari: in a week
Taleb saw one lion (causing traffic jam) but thousands of
peaceful animals — giraffes, elephants, impalas. The image
of the one majestic lion dominated his memory. Same with
history: we mistake the lurid for the empirical. Very few
predators vs. collaborative animals. History written about
wars, not peaceful coexistence.

**History Seen from the Emergency Room** — History is largely
peace punctuated by wars, not wars punctuated by peace.
Availability heuristic makes us overestimate conflict. Four
problems with historians: (1) "Overfitting" — extracting too
much *via positiva* and not enough *via negativa*. (2) Confusing
frequency with intensity — five centuries of Italian "warfare"
before unification, but 600,000+ Italians died in WWI during
"stability," orders of magnitude more than all prior conflicts
combined. Bob Rubin trade: infrequent losses look stable until
they wipe you out. (3) Representativeness — historians prefer
stories of conflict over organic collaboration among merchants,
barbers, doctors, money changers, plumbers. French *Annales*
school understood history is the whole life of an organism, but
failed to change neighboring disciplines. (4) War accounts are
fraught with overestimation biases — the lurid rises from
account to account.

Historians derive knowledge from books, not real life. State
Department employees are students of these scholars. Reading
archives in Yale Library doesn't fit the temperament of a debt
collector for the Mafia or a pit speculator. Arabs in Spain,
Turks in Byzantine Empire — from geopolitics, a tug-of-war;
from real life, merchants doing business. "Better the turban
of the Turk than the tiara of the Pope" — Grand Duke Lucas
Notaras. Taleb's own existence as Greek-Orthodox Byzantine rite
under Islam witnesses such collaboration. Lebanese civil war:
except near the Green Line, it didn't feel like war.

Recommended history: Paul Veyne/Duby/Aries's *A History of
Private Life*, Le Roy Ladurie's *Montaillou Village Occitan*,
Braudel's *The Mediterranean*.

### Chapter 15: They Don't Know What They Are Talking About
When They Talk about Religion

**Words Mean Different Things** — "Mathematicians think in
objects and relations, jurists in constructs, logicians in
operators, and... fools in words." Different people mean
different things by "religion": for early Jews/Muslims, it
was law (*din* = law in Hebrew and Arabic). For Romans, social
events and festivals (*religio* vs *superstitio*). Early
Christianity (via Augustine) stayed relatively away from law.
Theodosius's code: "Christianized" with short intro, rest
remained identical to pagan Roman legal reasoning.

Christian Aramaic uses *din* for religion and *nomous* (Greek)
for law. Jesus: "Give to Caesar what belongs to Caesar" —
separated holy and profane. Islam and Judaism have no such
marked separation.

For most Jews today, religion = ethnocultural (without the
law), a nation. For Orthodox/Catholic Christians = aesthetics,
pomp, rituals. For Protestants = belief without aesthetics.
For Buddhists/Shintoists/Hindus = practical/spiritual
philosophy, code of ethics.

**Salafism as Political System** — In Serbia, Croatia, Lebanon,
religion means one thing at peace and quite different at war.
"Christian minority" in the Levant just means "secular" (wanting
church/state separation). EU bureaucrats treat Salafism as just
a religion — it's actually an intolerant political system that
rejects Western institutions. "Cancer must be stopped before
it becomes metastatic." Salafism resembles atheistic Soviet
Communism: both have all-embracing control over human activity.

**Belief vs. Belief** — Some beliefs are decorative, some
functional (help survival), others literal. The Salafi takes
beliefs literally; the Christian takes them metaphorically.
Religions that evolved (Christianity, Judaism, Shiite Islam)
did so by moving away from the literal. Gibbon: Roman modes
of worship "were all considered by the people, as equally true;
by the philosopher, as equally false; and by the magistrate,
as equally useful."

**Libertarianism and Church-Free Religions** — Julian the
Apostate tried to revive paganism after Constantine but made a
fatal error: imagining paganism needed church-like structure.
Paganism was by definition distributed. Same applies to
libertarianism — cannot be pigeon-holed into a political
party. Central belief: substitute rule of law for rule of
authority. Libertarianism is a movement, not a party.

### Chapter 16: No Worship Without Skin in the Game

**Belief Requires an Entry Fee** — Breaking the Greek-Orthodox
Lenten fast (no animal products): once you pay the price, you
feel entitled to celebrate Easter. Pascal's wager flaw: belief
cannot be a free option — symmetry between what you pay and
receive. Skin-in-the-game rules hold in our rapport with gods.

**The Gods Do Not Like Cheap Signaling** — The altar in Saint
Sergius church in Maaloula (Aramaic-speaking town) has a drain
for blood — recycled from pre-Christian pagan temple. Early
Christians were sort of pagans; before the Council of Nicea
(4th century) Christians commonly recycled pagan altars and
shared places of worship. *Madbah* (altar in Levantine/Aramaic)
= "ritual slaying by cutting the guttural vein." *Qorban*
(Semitic for "getting closer to God") originally done via
sacrifice, still used as word for sacrament.

Imam Hussein, son of Ali, offered himself as sacrifice:
"Let me be the *qorban* for you — the supreme offering."
Followers show literal skin in the game on Ashoura through
self-flagellation.

In the pagan world, no worship without sacrifice. Burnt
offerings were precisely burnt so no human would consume them
(though high priest got his share). Physical sacrifice applied
to Temple of Jerusalem. Hebrews 9:22: "Without shedding of
blood is no remission." Christianity replaced sacrifice with
Christ's simulacrum — wine representing blood, flushed in
*piscina* (the drain), exactly as in Maaloula.

Abraham and Isaac: the mother of all unconditional gifts to
God — sacrifice your son, not a reciprocal deal. Maimonides:
God didn't immediately proscribe animal sacrifice because it
was contrary to human nature; instead "transferred to His
service that which had served as worship of created beings."
*"Love without sacrifice is theft"* (Procrustes).

The strength of a creed rests not on "evidence" of its gods'
powers, but on evidence of skin in the game of its worshippers.

### Chapter 17: Is the Pope Atheist?

**Talk Is Just Talk** — When John Paul II was shot (1981),
rushed to Gemelli University Polyclinic — best doctors in
Italy. At no point did anyone consider taking him to a chapel
first for divine intercession. His predecessors (Roman
emperors) had similar policy: treatment first, theology after.

Imagine a powerful atheist head suffering similar health
crisis: would arrive at same hospital, receive same
"well-wishes" (their version of "prayers"). Atheists would
be less colorfully dressed, vocabulary less ornamental, but
actions nearly identical.

Differences between Pope and atheist of equivalent rank
concern non-life-threatening matters: certain bedroom
activities, etc. Many atheists engage in yoga, sit in concert
halls in awed silence, spend time in what a Martian would
recognize as ritualistic gestures.

**Religious in Words** — Most Christians (like Taleb, an
Orthodox Christian) do not act differently from atheists on
central medical, ethical, and decision-making matters.
"Their decisions on central matters are indistinguishable
from those of an atheist."

Two categories: (1) *atheists in actions, religious in words*
(most Orthodox and Catholic Christians); (2) *religious in
actions, religious in words* (Salafi Islamists, suicide
bombers). Nobody is atheist in both actions and words —
everyone has rituals, respect for the dead, superstitions,
or belief in the miraculous powers of the state.

Rationality resides in what you do, not what you think or
"believe." Rationality is about survival.

## CHAPTER 18: HOW TO BE RATIONAL ABOUT RATIONALITY

### Rory Sutherland and the Swimming Pool Problem
Taleb's friend Rory Sutherland points out that on a
risk-adjusted basis, swimming pools are more dangerous
than going to restaurants — yet people would never
intuitively feel that way. The point: our intuitions about
risk are adapted for survival, not for abstract calculation.
Our ancestors didn't need to calculate — they needed to
avoid ruin.

### Belief as Instrument, Not End Product
The key distinction: beliefs should be judged by what they
lead you to DO, not whether they are "true" in some
abstract, propositional sense. Eyes don't give us the
"truth" — they give us the representation most useful for
survival. There are optical illusions everywhere. The
columns of the Parthenon look straight but are actually
curved — the architects compensated for optical distortion.
The representation that works is the one that matters.

### Ergodicity First
"Survival comes first, truth, understanding, and science
later." You can't learn anything if you're dead. This means
that any rule of thumb, heuristic, or "bias" that helps you
survive is *rational* in the deepest sense, even if it looks
irrational to a classroom statistician.

### Bias-Variance Tradeoff (Figure 3)
Taleb presents a shooting-target diagram: a biased shooter
who clusters shots in one tight spot near (but not on) the
bullseye actually gets CLOSER to the target than an unbiased
shooter with high variance who scatters shots everywhere. In
decision-making, a "biased" heuristic that is consistent and
low-variance can outperform an "unbiased" but noisy formal
model. Sometimes being systematically off in a predictable
way is better than being randomly correct on average.

### Gigerenzer, Simon, Binmore
Taleb draws on three thinkers:
- **Herbert Simon**: bounded rationality — humans optimize
  within the constraints of limited information, time, and
  computational power. Rationality is always bounded.
- **Gerd Gigerenzer**: ecological rationality — heuristics
  aren't failures of rationality but evolved tools adapted
  to specific environments. Fast-and-frugal works.
- **Ken Binmore**: revealed preferences — "Judging people by
  their beliefs is not scientific." "There is no such thing
  as the 'rationality' of a belief, there is rationality of
  action." "The rationality of an action can be judged only
  in terms of evolutionary considerations."

### Religion as Tail Risk Management
When viewed through the lens of survival rather than truth,
religion looks deeply rational. Religious rules are not
about metaphysics — they're about behavioral constraints
that help populations survive. Kashrut (Jewish dietary law)
comprises about 500 interdicts — an enormous behavioral
burden. But those populations survived, and those that
didn't follow similar rules often didn't. The rules don't
need to be "true" in a propositional sense; they need to
work in a survival sense.

### "Tawk" and Cheap "Tawk"
There's a crucial difference between decorative beliefs and
beliefs that map to action. Decorative beliefs are things
you say at cocktail parties. Action-mapping beliefs are
things you'd bet on, risk something for. "How much you truly
'believe' in something can be manifested only through what
you are willing to risk for it."

### Lindy on Rationality
The ancients had a word: *sophrosyne* — a combination of
precaution, self-control, and temperance. "What is rational
is that which allows for survival." Not what sounds clever,
not what publishes well, not what wins debates — what
survives.

### The Nondecorative in the Decorative
What looks decorative or irrational often has a hidden
survival function. Jewish kashrut appears to be religious
decoration — but the 500 dietary interdicts have survived
for millennia precisely because populations that followed
them survived. "Rationality does not depend on explicit
verbalistic explanatory factors; it is only what aids
survival, what avoids ruin."

The chapter's devastating conclusion: "Not everything that
happens happens for a reason, but everything that survives
survives for a reason."

## CHAPTER 19: THE LOGIC OF RISK TAKING

### The Casino Thought Experiment — Ergodicity Explained
The core concept of the book's final chapter. Imagine two
scenarios:
1. **Ensemble probability**: 100 people go to a casino.
   Some win, some lose, one goes bust. You can calculate the
   "average" outcome across the group. The person who goes
   bust is just one data point out of 100 — the "average"
   gambler does okay.
2. **Time probability**: ONE person goes to the casino 100
   days in a row. On day 28, that person (Taleb's fictional
   cousin "Theodorus Ibn Warqa") goes bust. He hits the
   "uncle point" — complete ruin. There is no day 29.

These two probabilities are NOT the same. Ensemble
probability says "on average, gambling is fine." Time
probability says "eventually, the gambler is ruined."
The difference is ergodicity. A process is ergodic when
the time average equals the ensemble average. Most of
life is NOT ergodic — you can't recover from ruin.

### "Never Cross a River If It Is on Average Four Feet Deep"
A river that averages four feet but varies from one to
ten will drown you at the deep parts. The average is
meaningless if the variance can kill you. This is the
deepest problem with economists using ensemble averages
to evaluate individual outcomes.

### Who Got It Right
Taleb credits several thinkers who understood ergodicity
before the mainstream:
- **Ole Peters and Murray Gell-Mann**: their paper
  formalizing the distinction between ensemble and time
  probability
- **Claude Shannon**: information theory pioneer who
  understood sequential betting
- **Ed Thorp**: the card counter who beat Las Vegas and then
  beat Wall Street
- **J.L. Kelly**: the Kelly criterion for optimal bet sizing
  (maximize geometric growth, not arithmetic return)
- **Harald Cramér**: early probability theorist

### Mark Spitznagel and Universa
Taleb's business partner Mark Spitznagel runs Universa
Investments on exactly this principle — you can afford
many small losses as long as you never face ruin. The
strategy of tail-risk hedging is entirely about survival
across time, not about any single-period return.

### Repetition of Exposures
The critical point about risk: the probability of ruin
increases with repetition. Smoking ONE cigarette is
relatively harmless. But the damage comes from smoking
tens of thousands over a lifetime. Russian roulette has
a 5/6 survival rate — impressive! But play six times
and you're likely dead. "The risk of ruin does not change
one iota" with each pull — but the *cumulative* risk of
ruin across repeated plays trends toward certainty.

### "Loss Aversion" Doesn't Exist
Kahneman and Tversky's famous "loss aversion" — people
feeling losses roughly twice as much as equivalent gains —
is not a behavioral bias at all. It is a residual of
ergodicity. If you can be ruined, then losses and gains
are NOT symmetric. A loss that can bankrupt you is not the
mirror image of a gain of equal size. "Loss aversion" is
actually correct risk assessment for someone who can't
recover from ruin. Behavioral economists got it backwards.

### Who Is "You"? — The Inverted Pyramid
Risk assessment changes depending on WHO we're protecting.
Taleb draws a hierarchy (inverted pyramid):
1. **You** (individual survival)
2. **Family** (extended unit)
3. **Tribe/Community**
4. **Humanity**
5. **Ecosystem**

The worst case isn't just your death — it's your death
PLUS everyone else's. Each layer up is more important
than the layer below. "I have a finite shelf life,
humanity should have an infinite duration." "I am
renewable, not humanity or the ecosystem."

This is why the precautionary principle applies to
systemic and existential risks (nuclear, GMO, climate)
even if the per-event probability looks small. We aren't
protecting one person playing one round — we're
protecting the entire system across all future time.

### Courage and Precaution Aren't Opposites
Aristotle's *sophrosyne* (prudence/temperance) and
*phronesis* (practical wisdom) are complementary, not
contradictory, to courage. "Courage is when you sacrifice
your own well-being for the sake of the survival of a
layer higher than yours." A soldier who risks his life
for his country is being courageous AND rational — he's
putting a lower layer (himself) at risk to protect a
higher layer (tribe/nation).

### Warren Buffett's High Filter
Buffett famously says no to almost everything. This isn't
timidity — it's tail-risk awareness. By saying no to 99%
of opportunities, you protect yourself from the one that
could ruin you. The filter is a survival mechanism.

### Love SOME Risks
The book does NOT argue for risk avoidance. It argues for
risk *selection*. Convex tinkering — taking many small
risks with limited downside and unlimited upside — is the
engine of progress and discovery. Risk and ruin are
different things. "Never confuse risk with ruin." Take
risks that can't kill you. Avoid risks that can.

### Mediocristan vs Extremistan — All Risks Are Not Equal
"Never compare a multiplicative, systemic, and fat-tailed
risk to a non-multiplicative, idiosyncratic, and
thin-tailed one." The common argument "more people die
from bathtub drownings than terrorism" is a category error.
Bathtub drownings are Mediocristan — thin-tailed, individual,
bounded by the Chernoff bound. The probability of bathtub
deaths doubling next year is essentially zero. Terrorism is
Extremistan — multiplicative, potentially systemic. The
probability of terrorism deaths doubling (or multiplying by
1000x) is nonzero. "Your bathtub is not trying to kill you."
Paul Embrechts and extreme value theorists understand this —
they study what happens in the tails, classifying by the
generalized extreme value distribution. "Things are a lot —
a lot — clearer in the tails."

### Chapter 19 Summary Maxims
- "One may be risk loving yet completely averse to ruin."
- "In a strategy that entails ruin, benefits never offset
  risks of ruin."
- "Ruin and other changes in condition are different animals."
- "Every single risk you take adds up to reduce your life
  expectancy."
- "Rationality is avoidance of systemic ruin."

The book's final message is crystallized: the gap between
risk and ruin is where all of life happens. Take risks,
have skin in the game, expose yourself to upside — but
never, ever risk total ruin. "Survival is the ultimate
performance measure."

## EPILOGUE: WHAT LINDY TOLD ME

Taleb reflects on reaching the end of the Incerto — five
books written over more than two and a half decades. Seeing
his whitish beard in a restaurant mirror, he recognizes a
Lindy lesson: with age comes less need to prove, less to
explain, less to theorize. Someone in the restaurant says
"It is what it is," and the phrase stays with him.

He offers a Lindy-tested wisdom about beards and aging:
"When the beard (or hair) is black, heed the reasoning,
but ignore the conclusion. When the beard is gray, consider
both reasoning and conclusion. When the beard is white,
skip the reasoning, but mind the conclusion."

The book closes with a long *via negativa* maxim — defining
skin in the game by what cannot exist without it:

"No muscles without strength, friendship without trust,
opinion without consequence, change without aesthetics,
age without values, life without effort, water without
thirst, food without nourishment, love without sacrifice,
power without fairness, facts without rigor, statistics
without logic, mathematics without proof, teaching without
experience, politeness without warmth, values without
embodiment, degrees without erudition, militarism without
fortitude, progress without civilization, friendship
without investment, virtue without risk, probability
without ergodicity, wealth without exposure, complication
without depth, fluency without content, decision without
asymmetry, science without skepticism, religion without
tolerance, and, most of all: nothing without skin in
the game."

The book is dedicated to "Two men of courage: Ron Paul,
a Roman among Greeks; Ralph Nader, Greco-Phoenician saint."

## GLOSSARY — KEY TERMS

- **Rent Seeking**: using protective regulations to derive
  income without adding anything to economic activity
- **Revelation of Preferences**: actions are observables,
  thoughts are not; Fat Tony says "tawk is always cheap"
- **Regulatory Capture**: regulations gamed by agents,
  diverging from original intent
- **Scientism**: belief that science looks like science,
  emphasis on cosmetic aspects over skeptical machinery
- **Naive Rationalism**: belief we have access to what makes
  the world work and that what we don't understand doesn't
  exist
- **Intellectual Yet Idiot**: "an idiot"
- **Pseudo-rationalism**: focusing on rationality of belief
  rather than consequences
- **Agency Problem**: misalignment of interest between agent
  and principal
- **Bob Rubin Trade**: payoff in skewed domain where benefits
  are visible/rewarded and detriment is rare/unpunished
- **Interventionista**: someone who causes fragility because
  he thinks he understands; not exposed to skin in the game;
  usually lacks sense of humor
- **Green Lumber Fallacy**: mistaking the source of important
  knowledge for another, less visible one
- **Lecturing-Birds-How-to-Fly Effect**: inverting the arrow
  of knowledge to read academia -> practice
- **Lindy Effect**: nonperishable things gain life expectancy
  with every additional day of survival
- **Ergodicity**: when ensemble probabilities equal time
  probabilities; absence of ergodicity means risk properties
  don't transfer from observed probability to time-domain
  payoff
- **Mediocristan**: thin-tailed, no single observation can
  meaningfully affect the aggregate
- **Extremistan**: fat-tailed, a single observation can
  conceivably impact the total
- **Minority Rule**: asymmetry by which preferences of an
  intolerant minority dictate the total
- **Via Negativa**: focus on what something is NOT; subtraction
  not addition; works better in domains with multiplicative
  side effects
- **Scalability**: qualities change when entities get larger;
  argument for localism over globalism
- **Intellectual Monoculture**: convergence to "bien pensant"
  mode; penalty for divergence via labels
- **Virtue Merchandising**: debasing virtue by using it as
  marketing; similar to simony and indulgences
- **Golden Rule** (symmetry): Treat others as you'd want to
  be treated
- **Silver Rule** (negative golden rule): Do not do to others
  what you would not like done to you — prevents busybodies
- **Principle of Charity**: represent opponent's argument as
  accurately as you'd want yours represented
