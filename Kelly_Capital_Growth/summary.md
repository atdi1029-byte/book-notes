# The Kelly Capital Growth Investment Criterion — Summary

**Editors:** Leonard C. MacLean, Edward O. Thorp, William T. Ziemba
**Year:** 2011 | **Pages:** 1142 | **Format:** Academic anthology (54 papers)

## What This Book Is

A comprehensive collection of the most important papers on the Kelly criterion — the mathematical formula for optimal bet sizing that maximizes long-term wealth growth. The book traces Kelly's idea from its origins in information theory (1956) through its application to blackjack, horse racing, sports betting, and the stock market. It includes the full academic debate between Kelly advocates and critics (especially Paul Samuelson), and culminates with Edward Thorp's definitive practical guide.

## The Core Idea

**The Kelly criterion says: bet a fraction of your wealth equal to your edge divided by your odds.** For even-money bets, this simplifies to f* = p - q, where p is your probability of winning and q is your probability of losing. For stocks, the continuous version is f* = (expected return - risk-free rate) / variance.

This fraction maximizes the long-term compound growth rate of your wealth. It also minimizes the expected time to reach any wealth goal. Any other fixed-fraction strategy will eventually fall behind Kelly with probability approaching 1.

## Key Practical Takeaways

### 1. Half Kelly Is the Sweet Spot
Full Kelly is mathematically optimal but psychologically brutal. Half Kelly gives you 75% of the growth rate with dramatically less risk. The chance of ever losing half your bankroll drops from 50% (full Kelly) to just 12.5% (half Kelly). Most experienced practitioners — including Thorp himself — recommend half Kelly or nearby.

### 2. Overbetting Is Catastrophically Worse Than Underbetting
If you bet more than twice the Kelly fraction, your wealth goes to zero almost surely. The growth function is parabolic: undershooting Kelly costs you a modest amount of growth, but overshooting costs you everything. When in doubt, bet less.

### 3. Your Edge Estimate Is Almost Certainly Too High
Forecasts of excess return tend to be inflated by data mining, non-stationarity, success attracting competition, and plain optimism. Since overbetting is catastrophic, you should assume your true edge is lower than your estimated edge and size accordingly. This is another argument for fractional Kelly.

### 4. Mean Estimates Matter 100x More Than Covariance Estimates
The Chopra-Ziemba result shows that for aggressive investors (low risk aversion), errors in estimating expected returns are 100x more costly than errors in estimating covariances. Conservative investors are more balanced. Implication: spend your time getting the direction and magnitude of expected return right, not perfecting your correlation matrix.

### 5. Rebalancing Creates Free Growth
A fixed-mix portfolio that regularly rebalances to target weights generates excess growth of (eta - eta^2) * sigma^2 / 2 over buy-and-hold. This "volatility pumping" effect means rebalancing isn't just risk management — it's a source of return. Higher volatility makes rebalancing more valuable, not less.

### 6. Negative Correlation Is Gold
When two positions are negatively correlated, the optimal Kelly fraction for each increases dramatically. At correlation = -1 (perfect hedge), you can bet as much as you want — it's riskless arbitrage. This is the mathematical foundation of hedged strategies: buy the underpriced thing, short the overpriced thing, and size the position aggressively because the risks cancel.

### 7. Transaction Costs Determine Whether Edge Becomes Profit
The racetrack chapters show this vividly: a 1% increase in track take cuts profits by 17-33%. The same principle applies to trading: commissions, slippage, and market impact can consume a theoretical edge entirely.

### 8. Kelly Works — The Track Record Is Real
- Thorp: 20% annual return over 28.5 years, ~$80 billion in total trades
- Medallion Fund: 21-99% annual returns, zero losing years (1993-2005)
- HZR racetrack system: 11.4% return on $162,477 wagered across 2,192 races
- Thorp's sports betting: $123,000 profit from $50,000 bankroll in 4.5 months

## The Samuelson Debate — Resolved

Paul Samuelson argued that Kelly is only optimal if your utility function happens to be logarithmic — and most people's isn't. He's technically correct. But the practical resolution is: (1) Kelly's dominance properties hold regardless of utility function — it WILL have more wealth than any other strategy eventually, (2) the "eventually" might be very long, and (3) fractional Kelly lets you dial between maximum growth and maximum safety to match your actual risk tolerance. Use full Kelly if you have infinite patience and iron nerves. Use fractional Kelly if you're human.

Traders and investors who want to understand position sizing at its deepest level. Skip the proofs and focus on: Part I (Kelly's original paper + Thorp's blackjack application), Part III Ch 24 (good/bad properties), Part VI Ch 54 (Thorp's definitive practical guide), and Part VI Ch 53 (Medallion Fund).
