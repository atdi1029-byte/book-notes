What Is the Kelly Criterion?

Imagine you have a coin that lands heads 53% of the time. You can bet any amount you want on each flip, and you get even money when you win. How much should you bet?

If you bet everything every time, you will eventually go broke — one losing flip wipes you out. If you bet almost nothing, you will grow your money painfully slowly. Somewhere between reckless and timid lies the sweet spot that makes your wealth grow as fast as possible without risking ruin. That sweet spot is the Kelly criterion.

John L. Kelly Jr. was a physicist at Bell Labs in 1956. He was thinking about information theory — how to transmit signals over noisy phone lines — when he realized the same math applies to gambling and investing. The rate at which you can grow your wealth is mathematically identical to the rate at which you can transmit information through a noisy channel. Claude Shannon, the father of information theory and Kelly's colleague, immediately saw the investment implications.

The formula is stunningly simple for even-money bets: bet a fraction of your bankroll equal to your edge. If you win 53% of the time, your edge is 53% minus 47% = 6%. So bet 6% of your current wealth on each flip. Not 5%. Not 7%. Exactly 6%. This fraction maximizes your long-run compound growth rate and minimizes the time it takes to reach any wealth goal.

Where It All Started: Bernoulli and the St. Petersburg Paradox

In 1738, Daniel Bernoulli posed a puzzle that had stumped mathematicians for 30 years. His cousin Nicolas had invented a game: flip a fair coin repeatedly. If it lands heads on the first flip, you win $2. If tails then heads, you win $4. Tails-tails-heads, $8. The payoff doubles with each flip. The expected value of this game is literally infinite — $1 + $1 + $1 + ... forever. Yet no rational person would pay more than about $20 to play it. Why?

Bernoulli's answer was revolutionary: people don't value money linearly. The jump from $1,000 to $2,000 feels much bigger than the jump from $1,000,000 to $1,001,000. He proposed that people value the logarithm of their wealth, not the wealth itself. Under log utility, the St. Petersburg game has a finite expected value (about $2.88). Mystery solved — at least for two centuries.

Bernoulli also introduced a second idea that turned out to be even more important: the marginal utility of wealth is inversely proportional to your current wealth. If you have $100, an extra dollar is worth 1/100th of a "utility unit." If you have $10,000, it is worth 1/10,000th. This seemingly simple observation leads directly to the Kelly criterion when you apply it to repeated bets. Because each dollar matters less as you get richer, the optimal strategy naturally scales your bets proportionally to your current wealth.

The Kelly Formula: Edge Over Odds

Kelly's formula takes different forms depending on the bet structure. Here are the versions you will actually use:

Even-Money Bets

When you win the same amount you risk (like a coin flip at even odds): f* = p − q, where p is the probability of winning and q is the probability of losing. If you win 55% of the time, bet 10% of your bankroll. If you win 51%, bet 2%. The formula simply says: your bet size equals your edge.

Uneven Payoffs

When the payoff differs from the risk (like a trade where you risk $1 to make $2): f* = (bp − q) / b, where b is the payoff-to-risk ratio. If you have a 40% chance of winning a 2-to-1 payoff (risk $100 to make $200), then f* = (2 × 0.40 − 0.60) / 2 = 10%. Bet 10% of your capital.

Stock Market (Continuous Version)

For continuously traded securities: f* = (m − r) / s², where m is the expected return (drift), r is the risk-free rate, and s is the standard deviation (volatility). Using rough historical numbers for the S&P 500 (m = 11%, s = 15%, r = 6%), the Kelly fraction is (0.11 − 0.06) / 0.0225 = 2.22. That means Kelly says to leverage 222% — put 222% of your wealth into stocks. This is why almost nobody uses full Kelly in practice. More on that shortly.

Why Kelly Works: The Five Properties

Leo Breiman, a brilliant statistician, proved three fundamental theorems about Kelly in 1961 that gave it a rigorous foundation. Combined with Kelly's original work and later additions by Thorp, the strategy has five remarkable properties:

1. It Maximizes Long-Run Growth Rate

No other strategy produces a higher expected compound growth rate. Period. This is a mathematical fact, not an opinion. The Kelly fraction f* uniquely maximizes E[log(W_n/W_0)], which is the expected logarithm of your wealth ratio after n trials. Since log is a concave function, there is exactly one peak, and Kelly sits on it.

2. It Never Goes Broke

Because you always bet a fraction of your current wealth, you can never lose everything. If you have $1,000 and bet 6%, you risk $60. If you lose, you have $940, and your next bet is 6% of $940 = $56.40. Your bets shrink with your losses and grow with your wins. This is fundamentally different from fixed-dollar betting, which can drive you to zero.

3. It Beats Every Other Strategy Eventually

Given enough time, the ratio of Kelly wealth to any other strategy's wealth approaches infinity with probability 1. Not "likely." Not "on average." Almost surely. This is Breiman's famous result. The Kelly bettor's wealth will exceed any competitor's wealth by an arbitrarily large multiple if you wait long enough.

4. It Minimizes Time to Any Goal

Want to double your money? Triple it? Grow it tenfold? Kelly minimizes the expected number of trials to reach any preassigned wealth goal. No other strategy gets you there faster on average.

5. It Adapts to Changing Probabilities

If the odds change from bet to bet (as they do in blackjack when the card count shifts, or in markets when volatility changes), Kelly adjusts automatically. You simply recalculate f* with the new probabilities on each trial. Thorp proved this works even when the game is non-stationary.

These properties sound too good to be true. And in practice, they come with a catch — a very big catch that brings us to the most important practical insight in the entire book.

Half Kelly: The Practical Sweet Spot

Here is the dirty secret of Kelly: full Kelly is terrifying to live with. The math guarantees long-run optimality, but the short run can be stomach-churning. With full Kelly on a 53% coin, there is a 50% probability that at some point in your career you will lose half of your peak wealth. Not a typo. Fifty percent chance of a 50% drawdown. Most people cannot handle that.

Edward Thorp, the man who literally invented practical Kelly betting, has this to say about it: "Most cautious gamblers or investors who use Kelly find the frequency of substantial bankroll reduction to be uncomfortably large." This is the voice of experience — Thorp has been using Kelly since 1962.

"My gambling and investment experience, as well as reports from numerous blackjack players and loams, suggests that most people strongly prefer the increased safety and psychological comfort of 'half Kelly' (or some nearby value), in exchange for giving up 1/4 of their growth rate." — Edward O. Thorp

Half Kelly means betting exactly half the Kelly fraction. If Kelly says bet 6%, you bet 3%. The tradeoffs are remarkable:

Growth rate: 75% of full Kelly. You give up only one-quarter of your growth. Risk (standard deviation): 50% of full Kelly. Your ride is twice as smooth. Chance of ever losing half your bankroll: drops from 50% to just 12.5%. Time to reach a goal: takes about 1.33 times as long.

That is an extraordinary bargain. You trade a modest reduction in growth speed for a massive reduction in pain. In Thorp's words, you give up "1/4 of your growth rate" for "increased safety and psychological comfort." Almost everyone who has actually used Kelly in practice — as opposed to merely theorizing about it — gravitates toward half Kelly.

The Catastrophe of Overbetting

This is the single most important lesson in the entire 1,142-page book: overbetting is catastrophically worse than underbetting. The Kelly growth curve is a parabola — it rises to a peak at f* and then falls off. If you undershoot f* by a little, you lose a little growth. If you overshoot f* by the same amount, you lose the same amount of growth. So far, symmetric.

But here is the killer: if you bet more than twice the Kelly fraction (f > 2f*), your expected growth rate goes negative. Your wealth will go to zero. Not slowly. Not eventually. Surely. You will be ruined.

Think about what this means. If the true Kelly fraction is 5% and you bet 11%, you are past the danger point. Your wealth will oscillate wildly and trend toward zero. You might have a fantastic run first — Kelly overbettors can experience thrilling temporary gains — but the math is relentless. Over enough bets, you will lose almost everything.

This is why estimation error is so dangerous. If you think the Kelly fraction is 10% but it is actually 5%, and you bet your estimated 10%, you are betting at 2f* — right at the edge of the ruin zone. Since estimation errors almost always overestimate the edge (due to data mining, wishful thinking, and survivorship bias), the real-world risk of overbetting is much greater than the risk of underbetting.

"Using too large an f* and overbetting is much more severely penalized than using too small an f* and underbetting." — Edward O. Thorp

Why Your Edge Is Probably Smaller Than You Think

Thorp identifies five reasons why your estimated edge (m_e) is almost certainly higher than your true edge (m_t):

1. Regression to the Mean

Forecast excess returns tend to be too high. If you backtested a system and found a 15% edge, the real edge going forward is probably lower. The best performers in any sample tend to be lucky, not just skilled, and their future performance regresses toward the average.

2. Data Mining

If you test enough strategies on historical data, some will look fantastic by pure chance. A system that "worked" because you searched through 100 variations and picked the best one has a built-in upward bias. The true edge may be a fraction of what the backtest showed.

3. Non-Stationarity

Markets change. What worked from 2015 to 2020 may not work from 2020 to 2025. Volatility changes, correlations shift, regulations evolve. Systems that worked in the past may have lower true edge going forward.

4. Success Attracts Capital

If your edge is real and you make money consistently, other people notice. They copy your strategy. Money floods into the same trades. The edge gets competed away. The drift from m_e toward zero means your current Kelly fraction is probably too large.

5. Rule Changes

Changes in commissions, margin regulations, insider trading laws, and market structure can eliminate edges overnight. Your estimated edge from last year may already be gone.

The practical conclusion: always assume your true edge is smaller than your estimated edge, and size your positions accordingly. Half Kelly provides a natural buffer. At half Kelly, even if your true edge is only half your estimate, you break even on growth. At full Kelly with the same error, you would be losing money.

The Chopra-Ziemba Insight: What Matters Most

Vijay Chopra and William Ziemba published a landmark finding in 1993 that every portfolio manager should know: errors in estimating expected returns are far more damaging than errors in estimating covariances or variances.

How much more? It depends on your risk tolerance, and the numbers are striking:

For aggressive investors (low risk aversion): mean errors are 100 times more important than covariance errors and 22 times more important than variance errors.

For moderate investors: mean errors are about 7 times more important than covariance errors.

For conservative investors: the three types of errors are roughly equally important.

What does this mean in practice? Stop spending all your time building perfect correlation matrices and start spending it on getting your expected return estimates right. If you are an aggressive trader — which Kelly investors tend to be — a 10% error in your expected return estimate does as much damage as a 1,000% error in your covariance estimate. The return estimate is everything.

This also explains why fractional Kelly is so important. Since return estimates are the primary input to Kelly sizing, and since those estimates are notoriously unreliable, you should be deeply skeptical of any calculated Kelly fraction and dial it down accordingly.

Fractional Kelly: Dialing the Risk Knob

Fractional Kelly means using some fraction c of the full Kelly bet (where 0 < c < 2). Think of c as a risk knob that goes from 0 (no bet) to 2 (double Kelly, the suicide zone). Here is what happens as you turn the knob:

c = 0.25 (quarter Kelly): Growth rate is 43.75% of full Kelly. Very smooth ride. Takes 2.29x as long to reach goals. Good for extremely uncertain edges.

c = 0.50 (half Kelly): Growth rate is 75% of full Kelly. Chance of halving your bankroll: 12.5%. Takes 1.33x as long. The popular choice.

c = 0.75 (three-quarter Kelly): Growth rate is 93.75% of full Kelly. Nearly full speed with meaningfully less risk. Takes 1.07x as long.

c = 1.0 (full Kelly): Maximum growth rate. Chance of halving your bankroll: 50%. Minimum time to goals. Requires accurate edge estimates and strong nerves.

c = 1.5: Growth rate drops to 75% of full Kelly — same as half Kelly! But with MUCH more risk. You are giving up growth and getting nothing in return.

c = 2.0: Growth rate is zero. You are treading water while taking maximum risk.

c > 2.0: Growth rate is negative. Ruin is certain.

The key insight is the asymmetry. Below full Kelly, the growth curve is gently sloped — you can reduce your bet substantially with only modest growth penalties. Above full Kelly, the curve drops steeply. At c = 1.5, you have the same growth as c = 0.5 but much more risk. The parabola punishes aggression far more than it punishes caution.

Volatility Pumping: Free Money from Rebalancing

One of the most counterintuitive results in the entire book comes from Thomas Cover's universal portfolio and the work on volatility-induced growth: regularly rebalancing a portfolio to fixed target weights creates excess return out of thin air.

Here is a striking example from the book. Take two assets that are both losing money individually — say, each declining by 1% per year on average. If they are volatile and not perfectly correlated, a portfolio that rebalances between them can actually make money. The rebalancing systematically buys low (the asset that dropped more) and sells high (the asset that rose more), capturing the volatility as profit.

The formula for this excess growth is: (eta − eta²) × sigma² / 2, where eta is the portfolio weight and sigma is the asset's volatility. This is maximized when eta = 0.5 (equal weighting) and increases with volatility.

What does this mean for a trader?

Rebalancing is not just risk management — it is a source of return. When your DCA portfolio drifts from target weights, rebalancing back to targets is not merely prudent — it is actively profitable. The more volatile the assets, the more you gain from rebalancing. And equal weighting is optimal when you have no strong view on which asset will outperform.

Mulvey et al. confirmed this with real-world data: equal-weighted rebalanced portfolios consistently outperformed cap-weighted buy-and-hold indices across emerging markets, developed markets, and S&P 500 sectors.

Correlation and Hedging: Why Pairs Trades Work

Thorp provides the mathematical foundation for why hedged and pairs-trading strategies can be sized so aggressively. The key insight: negative correlation between two bets dramatically increases the optimal Kelly fraction for each.

For two independent bets with equal edge m, the Kelly fraction for each is: f* = m / (1 + m²). This is slightly less than the single-bet Kelly (f* = m) because both bets are competing for the same bankroll.

But as the correlation between the two bets becomes negative, something magical happens: the optimal fraction increases. At correlation = −1 (perfect negative correlation), the pair becomes a riskless arbitrage, and the optimal bet is as large as you can make it. The growth function increases without limit because there is no risk.

This is exactly the principle behind Thorp's investment strategy. In 1983, his partnership bought $330 million of "old" AT&T stock and simultaneously sold short $332.5 million of "new" AT&T plus the seven regional telephone companies. The long and short sides were nearly identical, achieving a correlation close to −1. The position size was enormous — one of the largest block trades ever on the New York Stock Exchange — because the hedging virtually eliminated risk.

For traders: When you find a genuine pairs trade or hedged position, Kelly tells you to size it much larger than an unhedged directional bet. The lower the residual correlation between your long and short legs, the bigger you should go. This is why convertible bond arbitrage, statistical arbitrage, and merger arbitrage funds use significant leverage — the hedging justifies it mathematically.

The Racetrack: Kelly's First Real-World Laboratory

Before Wall Street, Kelly-style betting was tested at the racetrack. Donald Hausch, William Ziemba, and Mark Rubinstein developed a system (called HZR or Dr.Z) in the early 1980s that exploited a specific inefficiency: the place and show pools at North American racetracks are systematically mispriced.

The win pool — where most money goes — is roughly efficient. Favorites and longshots get priced about right. But the place pool (horse finishes 1st or 2nd) and show pool (1st, 2nd, or 3rd) have persistent biases. Favorites are underbet in place and show relative to their true probability of finishing in the money.

The HZR system uses no handicapping — no opinions about which horse is best. It only uses the publicly displayed odds from the toteboard, applies the Kelly criterion to determine whether the expected return exceeds a threshold (typically 14-20% above breakeven), and calculates the optimal bet size.

The results across five tracks and 2,192 races: 531 system bets were made. 59% won outright. Weighted by bet size, 71% won. Total money wagered: $162,477. Total profit: $18,505. Rate of return: 11.4%. This against a track take (the house commission) of 14-18%.

In a live 1980 test at Exhibition Park in Vancouver, starting with $2,500, the system made 22 bets over 9 racing days and ended with $3,716 — a profit of $1,216 or 48.6%. The probability of achieving this result through random betting was 3 × 10&sup5;, effectively ruling out luck.

The trading parallel is powerful: a market can be efficient in one dimension (win/directional price discovery) but inefficient in another (place/show, options pricing, spreads). Edge doesn't require predicting direction — it requires finding mispricing and sizing correctly. And transaction costs (the track take, commissions, slippage) are the #1 determinant of whether theoretical edge becomes real profit. A 1% increase in track take cut profits by 17-33%.

Blackjack: Where Thorp Proved Kelly Works

Edward Thorp didn't just theorize about Kelly — he was the first person to apply it in the real world. In 1962, he published Beat the Dealer, which proved that blackjack could be beaten by counting cards. Shannon introduced him to Kelly's paper at MIT in 1960, and Thorp immediately saw how to use it for bet sizing.

The application is elegant. In blackjack, the player's edge changes from hand to hand as cards are dealt from the shoe. When many small cards have been played and the remaining shoe is rich in tens and aces, the player has a positive edge — perhaps 1-2%. When the shoe favors the house, the player has a negative edge.

Kelly says: on each hand, bet a fraction of your bankroll equal to your current edge (adjusted down for the variance of blackjack payoffs, which is higher than a simple coin flip because of splits, doubles, and blackjack bonuses). In favorable situations, bet big. In unfavorable ones, bet the minimum.

There is a complication unique to blackjack: you must make minimum "waiting bets" during unfavorable periods in order to stay at the table and catch the favorable ones. These waiting bets have negative expectation and drag down your overall growth rate. Thorp showed that the optimal Kelly fraction for favorable hands decreases as the ratio of favorable-to-unfavorable hands decreases. If you only see one favorable hand for every 10 unfavorable ones, your optimal bet in favorable situations is much smaller than if the ratio were 1-to-1.

The deeper lesson for traders: if you spend most of your time in losing positions (waiting bets) to catch the occasional winner, the winners need to be proportionally smaller than a pure Kelly analysis would suggest. Your true Kelly fraction incorporates the cost of waiting.

Sports Betting: $50K to $173K in Four Months

In 1993, Thorp was approached by a computer science PhD who had developed a sports betting system. Thorp was convinced by the analysis, put up $50,000 as a test bankroll, and used Kelly sizing.

Over 101 days of betting in the first four and a half months of 1994, the results were spectacular: $123,000 in profit — about $68,000 from Type 1 sports and $55,000 from Type 2. The expected profit based on their model was about $89,000, so they outperformed expectations by roughly $34,000.

Thorp notes this overperformance may not be pure luck: "Our expectation estimates from the model were deliberately chosen to be conservative. The reason is that using too large an f* and overbetting is much more severely penalized than using too small an f* and underbetting."

The typical edge was about 6%, so total action was about $2 million. They placed 5 to 15 bets per day, ranging from a few hundred dollars to several thousand each. The bankroll was never more than a few thousand dollars below breakeven at its worst point.

They stopped sports betting despite its success for three reasons: (1) it required someone physically present in Nevada, (2) transporting large amounts of cash between casinos was risky, and (3) it wasn't competitive with their Wall Street operations, which offered much larger capacity.

Wall Street: The Biggest Game

Applying Kelly to the stock market introduces new complexities. Unlike a coin flip or a blackjack hand, a stock has continuous outcomes — it can go up or down by any amount. The return distribution is not binary but approximately lognormal. And instead of individual bets, you are managing a portfolio of many simultaneous positions with complex correlations.

The continuous version of Kelly for a single stock is: f* = (m − r) / s², where m is the expected annual return, r is the risk-free rate, and s is the annual standard deviation.

For the S&P 500 with rough historical numbers (m = 11%, s = 15%, r = 6%), this gives f* = 0.05 / 0.0225 = 2.22. Full Kelly says to lever up 222%. But this would be insane in practice. In the October 1987 crash, the S&P dropped 23% in a single day. At 2x leverage, your portfolio drops 46%. At 2.22x leverage, you'd be down about 51% and facing a margin call that forces you to sell at the worst moment.

This is why Thorp and everyone else who actually manages money uses fractional Kelly in markets. The stock market is not a coin flip — it has fat tails, crashes, and regime changes that the continuous model doesn't capture. Full Kelly in stocks is a recipe for blowup.

For a portfolio of securities, the Kelly-optimal allocation follows a familiar form: F* = C¹ × (M − R), where C is the covariance matrix, M is the vector of expected returns, and R is the risk-free rate. This is the inverse-covariance-weighted excess return vector — the same formula that appears in mean-variance optimization but with a specific interpretation: it maximizes the log-growth rate of the portfolio.

The Samuelson Debate: Is Kelly Really Optimal?

No discussion of Kelly is complete without Paul Samuelson's objections. Samuelson, the first American to win the Nobel Prize in Economics, was Kelly's most prominent critic. He wrote his famous 1979 paper entirely in one-syllable words to make his point as clearly as possible.

Samuelson's core argument: Kelly maximizes E[log(W)], which is only optimal if your utility function is U(W) = log(W). Most people's utility is NOT logarithmic. A more risk-averse investor (whose utility curve is more concave than log) should bet less than Kelly. A less risk-averse investor might bet more. Kelly is not universally optimal — it is optimal for one specific preference function.

He also attacked what he called the "False Corollary": just because Kelly almost certainly accumulates more wealth in the long run does NOT mean its expected utility is higher for non-log investors. A highly risk-averse investor might prefer a lower-growth, lower-variance path even though it leads to less wealth.

The resolution: Samuelson is technically correct. But the editors (MacLean, Thorp, Ziemba) argue his criticism is "largely irrelevant for practical investors." Here is why:

First, fractional Kelly addresses the utility objection directly. If your utility is more concave than log, use less than full Kelly. The fractional Kelly framework lets you choose any point on the growth-vs-security tradeoff. You are not forced to use full Kelly any more than you are forced to drive at the car's top speed.

Second, the dominance properties hold regardless of utility. Kelly WILL have more money eventually. The debate is about whether "eventually" matters to you, given your time horizon and risk preferences. For long-term compounders (endowments, family offices, young investors with decades ahead), Kelly's properties are compelling. For short-term investors or retirees, less so.

Third, the practical dangers that Samuelson highlights — wild short-term swings, the possibility of losing 98% of your wealth on a single play with a 14% edge — are all addressed by using fractional Kelly rather than full Kelly.

The Medallion Fund: Kelly at Industrial Scale

Jim Simons and the Renaissance Technologies Medallion Fund represent arguably the most successful application of Kelly-style thinking in investment history. Ziemba notes that he taught Simons about the Kelly criterion in 1992.

The numbers are staggering. From January 1993 through April 2005:

Annual returns (net of fees): 39%, 71%, 38%, 31%, 21%, 42%, 25%, 99%, 31%, 29%, 25%, 28%. Zero losing years. Only 17 losing months out of 148. Only 3 losing quarters out of 46.

The fund's Sharpe ratio was 1.68 on an annual basis. Its Downside Symmetric Sharpe Ratio (which better captures the asymmetric return distribution) was an extraordinary 26.4 on an annual basis — the highest Ziemba had ever seen, exceeding even Princeton-Newport's 13.8 during 1969-1988.

$100 invested in January 1993 would have grown to approximately $5,000 by April 2005 — a 50x multiple in just over 12 years. And this is after the fund's eye-watering fees: 5% management plus 44% of profits. Gross returns were even higher.

The fund was closed to all outside investors except about six original ones, plus Renaissance employees. It managed about $5 billion and ran hundreds of short-term strategies simultaneously, many entering and exiting positions within seconds. The Kelly criterion helped determine not just individual position sizes but the overall allocation of capital across strategies.

Thorp's Track Record: 28 Years of Proof

Edward Thorp's personal investment record is the longest continuous track record of Kelly-style investing in existence. Starting in November 1969 with a convertible hedging partnership, through May 1998 — twenty-eight and a half years — his results speak for themselves:

Annual compound return: approximately 20%. Annual standard deviation: approximately 6%. Correlation with the market: approximately zero ("market neutral").

$10,000 invested in 1969, compounded tax-exempt, would have been worth $18 million by 1998.

During this period, Thorp estimates he made about $80 billion in total purchases and sales — roughly 1.25 million individual "bets" averaging about $65,000 each, with hundreds of positions open at any given time.

His strategies were primarily convertible bond arbitrage and statistical arbitrage — both hedged strategies where negative correlation between long and short legs allowed Kelly to justify larger positions. A notable competitor in the early days was Harry Markowitz, the Nobel laureate who created modern portfolio theory. After 20 months, Thorp's fund was up 39.9% while the Dow Jones Industrial Average was up only 4.2%. Markowitz eventually left, but Thorp persisted for decades.

Drawdown-Aware Kelly: Protecting Capital in Crashes

One of the most practical additions to Kelly theory comes from Mulvey, Pauling, and Madey's Dynamic Portfolio Tactics (DPT) system. Standard Kelly assumes you care only about long-run growth. But real investors care about drawdowns — they cannot tolerate losing 40% of their wealth even if the long-run math says they will recover.

DPT modifies the Kelly objective function by adding a drawdown penalty. When your current wealth drops below your peak (high-water mark) by more than a threshold, you increase your effective risk aversion, automatically reducing position sizes.

The researchers found that two thresholds worked well: D1 = 12% and D2 = 16%. When drawdown exceeds 12%, modestly reduce risk. When it exceeds 16%, reduce further. This creates a system that grows aggressively during good times but automatically shifts to capital preservation during bad times.

The results (1999-2009) across the S&P 500, EAFE, and emerging markets: the DPT combined strategy achieved 25.25% annual geometric return with only 12.99% maximum drawdown. Compare this to the S&P 500's −2.26% annual return and 50.80% maximum drawdown over the same period. The strategy gave up some upside during bubble years but massively protected capital during the 2008 crash.

The 2008 crash taught a vivid lesson: most of the damage was concentrated in just three months (September through November). Traditional annual or quarterly rebalancing was far too slow to respond. The DPT system, rebalancing every five trading days, detected the regime change quickly and de-risked before the worst of it.

For a trader: consider building automatic risk reduction into your position sizing. When your equity curve drops more than 12% from its peak, cut your position sizes. When it drops more than 16%, cut further. This is not abandoning Kelly — it is extending Kelly to account for the reality that your edge estimate becomes less reliable during crisis periods when volatility spikes and correlations break down.

The Practical Rules: A Trader's Cheat Sheet

After 1,142 pages and 54 academic papers, here is what matters for someone who trades and invests:

Rule 1: Find the Edge First

Kelly tells you how much to bet. It does NOT tell you what to bet on. The fundamental problem is finding positive expectation opportunities. Without a genuine edge, no position sizing formula can help you. "The central problem for gamblers is to find positive expectation bets" — Thorp.

Rule 2: Use Half Kelly

Full Kelly is for textbooks. Half Kelly gives 75% of the growth rate with dramatically less risk and a massive buffer against estimation error. When in doubt, go even lower. Quarter Kelly (43.75% growth, 25% risk) is appropriate when your edge estimate is highly uncertain.

Rule 3: Never Go Past Full Kelly

This is the one rule that is absolute. Betting more than Kelly reduces your growth rate. Betting more than 2x Kelly guarantees ruin. There is never a reason to bet more than f*.

Rule 4: Assume Your Edge Is Smaller Than You Think

Data mining, non-stationarity, competition, and overconfidence all bias your edge estimate upward. Downgrade your estimated edge by at least 25-50% before sizing positions. This is equivalent to using quarter-to-half Kelly on your raw estimate.

Rule 5: Focus on Expected Returns, Not Correlations

Chopra-Ziemba shows that errors in expected returns are 100x more costly than errors in correlations for aggressive investors. Get your directional view right. The correlation matrix is secondary.

Rule 6: Rebalance Regularly

Rebalancing is not just housekeeping. It creates excess growth (volatility pumping) that increases with asset volatility. Equal-weight rebalanced portfolios consistently outperform cap-weighted buy-and-hold.

Rule 7: Hedge to Increase Position Size

Negative correlation increases the Kelly fraction. If you can find a genuine hedge (pairs trade, convertible arb, long/short positions), Kelly justifies larger positions because the risks partially cancel. Thorp's career was built on this principle.

Rule 8: Cut Size When Losing

When your equity curve drops more than 12% from its peak, reduce position sizes. At 16% drawdown, reduce further. This is drawdown-aware Kelly, and it dramatically improves risk-adjusted returns during crisis periods.

Rule 9: Transaction Costs Kill

A 1% increase in transaction costs can cut profits by 17-33%. Factor all costs (commissions, slippage, funding, market impact) into your edge calculation before applying Kelly. Many "edges" disappear once costs are properly accounted for.

Rule 10: The Long Run May Be Very Long

Kelly dominates other strategies eventually, but "eventually" can mean thousands of trials. With a 2% edge on even-money bets, it takes about 385 bets just to expect to double your money. At 10 bets per day, that is 38 trading days. But the variation around this expectation is enormous. You need patience and faith in the math to ride out the inevitable losing streaks.

About the Editors

Leonard C. MacLean is a professor at Dalhousie University in Halifax, Canada. His research focuses on capital growth theory, stochastic programming, and the growth-security tradeoff in portfolio management. He has co-authored numerous papers with Thorp and Ziemba on fractional Kelly strategies and optimal capital growth with constraints.

Edward O. Thorp is the father of practical Kelly betting. A mathematics professor at MIT and UC Irvine, he pioneered card counting in blackjack (Beat the Dealer, 1962), co-invented the first wearable computer (with Shannon), created the first quantitative hedge fund (Princeton-Newport Partners, 1969), and compiled a 28.5-year track record of approximately 20% annual returns with near-zero correlation to markets. He made approximately $80 billion in total trades and was the first to use the Kelly criterion for real-world gambling and investing.

William T. Ziemba is alumni professor emeritus at the University of British Columbia and visiting professor at MIT Sloan. The most prolific contributor to Kelly criterion literature, he co-developed the HZR racetrack betting system, taught Jim Simons about Kelly (1992), and has published extensively on stochastic optimization, asset-liability management, and the evaluation of great investors. He is the author of Beat the Racetrack and Invest Japan.

Why This Book Matters

Position sizing is the most neglected topic in trading and investing education. Bookstores overflow with books about what to buy — which stocks, which sectors, which indicators, which patterns. Almost none address the equally important question of how much to buy. This anthology is the definitive collection on that question. It gathers 70 years of thinking — from information theory to financial economics — into a single volume and resolves the major academic debates with practical conclusions backed by extraordinary real-world evidence.

The book matters because it contains the actual intellectual toolkit used by some of the greatest investors of the modern era. Thorp's 28-year track record, Simons's Medallion Fund, and Buffett's concentrated portfolio all reflect Kelly-style thinking about position sizing and capital growth. The principles are universal: they apply equally to a $1,000 crypto portfolio and a $5 billion hedge fund. Find your edge, size your bets proportionally, never overbet, and compound relentlessly.
