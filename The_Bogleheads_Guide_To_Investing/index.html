Key Takeaways

  1. Live below your means. Only 5% of Americans become financially independent by age 65. The difference is choosing to be a Saver rather than a Borrower or Consumer. Financial freedom begins with spending less than you earn.
  2. Start saving and investing as early as possible. Compound interest is the most powerful force in investing. A 25-year-old investing $100/month at 10% will have $632,000 by age 65. Waiting until 35 cuts that to $226,000. Time is more important than timing.
  3. Pay yourself first. Have savings automatically deducted from your paycheck before you ever see it. If you wait to save what's left over, there won't be anything left over.
  4. Eliminate high-interest debt before investing. Paying off an 18% credit card is equivalent to earning an 18% guaranteed, tax-free return. No investment can consistently match that.
  5. Build an emergency fund. Keep 3–6 months of living expenses in a safe, liquid account before investing in the market. This prevents being forced to sell investments at the worst possible time.
  6. Understand what you're buying. Stocks are ownership shares in businesses. Bonds are IOUs from governments or corporations. Know the risk/return characteristics of each before investing.
  7. Index funds should be the core (or all) of your portfolio. Over time, 80% of actively managed funds underperform their benchmark index. Indexing guarantees you the market return minus tiny costs, which beats most professional investors.
  8. Keep costs ruthlessly low. Every dollar paid in fees, commissions, and expense ratios is a dollar less in your pocket. A 1% annual difference in costs over 30 years can reduce your final wealth by 25–30%. You can't control returns, but you can control costs.
  9. Asset allocation is the most important investment decision you'll make. How you divide your money among stocks, bonds, and cash accounts for roughly 94% of variation in portfolio returns. Get this right and the rest is details.
  10. Diversify broadly. Own the whole haystack instead of searching for the needle. Diversify across asset classes, geographies, and time (dollar-cost averaging). Invest approximately 20% of equities in international stocks.
  11. Minimize taxes. Place tax-inefficient investments (bonds, REITs, actively managed funds) in tax-sheltered accounts (IRAs, 401(k)s). Place tax-efficient investments (index funds, tax-managed funds) in taxable accounts.
  12. Never try to time the market. Market timing requires being right twice (when to get out and when to get back in). The cost of being out of the market during just a few of its best days is devastating. Stay invested.
  13. Don't chase past performance. Last year's winning fund is more likely to be next year's loser. Fund companies heavily advertise recent winners while quietly closing recent losers (survivorship bias).
  14. Rebalance your portfolio periodically. When market movements push your allocation away from your target, rebalance to maintain your intended risk level. This forces you to buy low and sell high systematically.
  15. Tune out the noise. The financial media, Wall Street marketing machine, and investment gurus exist to make money FROM you, not FOR you. All forecasting is noise.
  16. Master your emotions. Greed and fear are the investor's worst enemies. The average mutual fund investor underperforms the market by nearly 4% per year — almost entirely due to emotional decision-making (buying high, selling low).
  17. Protect your assets with proper insurance. Insure only against catastrophes you can't afford. Carry high deductibles. Buy term life insurance, not whole life. Don't mix insurance with investing.
  18. Plan for your money to outlive you. Withdraw no more than 4% of your portfolio's initial value per year (inflation-adjusted). Keep fixed expenses low in retirement and maintain flexibility.
  19. Get your estate planning in order. Have a will, consider a living trust, set up powers of attorney, and write an advance health care directive. Don't let procrastination leave your family unprotected.
  20. The Bogleheads' investment philosophy works. It's based on decades of academic research, not Wall Street marketing. The strategy is boring by design. Boring is beautiful when it comes to investing.

Thematic Breakdowns

The Power of Simplicity

The central thesis: effective investing is incredibly simple — create a diversified asset allocation plan, invest in low-cost index funds, rebalance periodically, stay the course. This simplicity is a problem for Wall Street (boring advice doesn't sell products) but a gift for investors.

Costs Are the Enemy

Fees, commissions, expense ratios, taxes, and inflation are the five horsemen of portfolio destruction. Unlike returns, costs are predictable and controllable. The book's single most actionable message: keep costs as low as possible, always.

Behavioral Finance

Perhaps the most important theme. Even investors who know the right strategy often fail because their emotions — greed, fear, overconfidence, loss aversion, herd mentality — cause them to deviate from the plan. The average investor's underperformance is primarily behavioral, not informational.

The Wall Street Machine

An entire industry exists to convince you that investing is complicated and that you need their expensive help. The uncomfortable truth: the investment industry's interests are fundamentally misaligned with yours. They profit from complexity, activity, and your attention. You profit from simplicity, inactivity, and ignoring them.

Time and Compound Interest

The book repeatedly emphasizes starting early. The magic of compounding — earning returns on your returns — makes time the most valuable asset an investor has. Every year of delay has an outsized cost.

Risk Management

Risk is not just about losing money — it's also about not having enough. Too conservative an allocation can be just as dangerous as too aggressive. The book advocates age-appropriate risk taking, proper insurance, and diversification as the keys to managing risk.

Contrarian Ideas

  1. Common life principles FAIL in investing. "Hire experts" (most underperform), "you get what you pay for" (higher fees mean lower returns), "past performance predicts the future" (it doesn't). The rules that work everywhere else are inverted in investing.
  2. Doing nothing beats doing something. In most areas of life, taking bold action is rewarded. In investing, the biggest returns come from doing as little as possible: buy index funds and don't touch them.
  3. Intelligence is no advantage. The Mensa Investment Club returned 2.5% vs the S&P 500's 15.3%. Two Nobel Prize winners created LTCM and blew up. Overconfidence born of intelligence is one of the worst investment handicaps.
  4. Women are better investors than men. Not because they know more, but because they trade less, take fewer risks, and don't let ego drive decisions.
  5. The "boring" strategy wins. Exciting investments (hot stocks, hedge funds, complex strategies) almost always underperform a simple three-fund index portfolio. Excitement in investing is a cost, not a benefit.
  6. Spending less is more important than earning more. The book's first chapter focuses not on investing but on financial lifestyle. The Savers beat the Borrowers and Consumers not through higher returns but through higher savings rates.
  7. You may not need a financial advisor. And if you do, a fee-only advisor who charges by the hour is far superior to one who earns commissions on products sold to you. Most investors can manage a simple index fund portfolio themselves.
  8. Conventional withdrawal wisdom may be wrong. For those wanting to leave a legacy, spending down tax-deferred accounts (IRAs) first — rather than taxable accounts first — can save heirs significant taxes through the stepped-up cost basis on inherited taxable assets.

Author's Key Proposals

  1. The Three-Fund Portfolio: A total stock market index fund, a total international index fund, and a total bond market index fund — held in appropriate proportions for your age and risk tolerance — is all most investors need.
  2. Bond allocation roughly equal to your age: A 30-year-old holds ~30% bonds, a 60-year-old holds ~60% bonds. Simple rule of thumb that increases safety as you age.
  3. Use Vanguard (or similar low-cost providers): Vanguard's unique mutual ownership structure means the fund company is owned by its fund shareholders, eliminating the conflict of interest inherent in for-profit fund companies.
  4. Max out tax-advantaged accounts first: 401(k) (especially to get the full employer match), IRAs, HSAs — before investing in taxable accounts.
  5. Dollar-cost average: Invest a fixed amount at regular intervals regardless of market conditions. This automatically buys more shares when prices are low and fewer when high.
  6. Rebalance annually or at 5% threshold: When any asset class drifts 5% or more from its target, rebalance. Or simply rebalance once a year at a set date.
  7. 4% withdrawal rule in retirement: Withdraw no more than 4% of your portfolio's starting value annually, adjusted for inflation. Or withdraw a maximum of 5% of current value if you prefer a fixed percentage.
  8. Buy term life insurance: Never whole life, universal life, or variable life. Insurance is for protection, investing is for wealth building. Don't mix the two.
  9. Build a comprehensive estate plan: Will + living trust + powers of attorney + advance health care directive + letter of instruction. Don't leave these decisions to the state.

Foreword & Introduction

Bogle compares the Bogleheads to the American associations described by Alexis de Tocqueville in Democracy in America (1840) — voluntary groups of citizens combining for mutual benefit. Two defining characteristics of Boglehead culture:

  1. Rationality — common sense, intolerant of illogic, preference for facts over hyperbole
  2. Caring — eager to help all investors without compensation or bias
"Choose a sound financial lifestyle. Start early and invest regularly. Know what you're buying. Preserve your buying power. Keep costs and taxes low. Diversify your stock portfolio." — John C. Bogle

Bogle draws parallels to Benjamin Franklin's The Way to Wealth (1757), noting that sound saving and investing principles are eternal. The Bogleheads' message is contrarian: common life principles (hire experts, you get what you pay for, past performance predicts future results) fail in investing.

Bogleheads follow the investment philosophy of John C. "Jack" Bogle, founder of The Vanguard Group. He created the first index fund for retail investors (initially mocked as "Bogle's Folly") — now the largest mutual fund in the world. The Bogleheads community began in 1998 on Morningstar's Vanguard Diehards message board, later moving to bogleheads.org, getting 50,000+ visits and 1,500+ posts daily.

Chapter 1: Choose a Sound Financial Lifestyle

"Drive-in banks were established so most of the cars today could see their real owners." — E. Joseph Grossman

The stark reality: Of 100 young Americans starting at age 25, by age 65 only one will be rich and four financially independent. The remaining 95 will be unable to self-sustain their lifestyle.

Three Financial Lifestyles

1. The Borrowers (Bill & Betty Borrower) — Live on credit cards, interest-only mortgages, leased cars. "Forget about tomorrow, let's live for today." Building negative wealth (debt), not assets. One job loss or illness away from financial ruin. In Texas: "big hat, no cattle."

2. The Consumers (Chad & Cathy Consumer) — Spend with a paycheck mentality. Buying decision: "Can we afford the monthly payments?" Pass up 401(k) matching and Roth IRAs for immediate gratification. Without a cash cushion or long-term plan, a rented lifestyle that ends when work ends.

3. The Keepers (Ken & Kim Keeper) — Focus on net worth, not net income. First thing every paycheck: minimum 10% saved and invested off the top. They enjoy life but with a long-term plan. The Keepers will always have more than they need.

Three Steps Before You Start Investing

1. Graduate from the Paycheck Mentality to the Net Worth Mentality

Society confuses income with wealth — they are separate measures. Income = how much you earn; Wealth = how much you keep. Net worth = total assets minus total debts. Calculate it annually.

2. Pay Off Credit Card and High-Interest Debts

Paying off credit cards is the highest, risk-free, tax-free return you can earn. Example: $8,000 balance at 18.9% with $160/mo minimum = 8 years to pay off, $7,000+ in interest = $15,000 for $8,000 of goods. No federal limit on credit card interest rates.

3. Establish an Emergency Fund

Readily accessible cash for emergencies. For most people: 6 months' living expenses. Keep in safe, liquid accounts: bank savings, credit union, money market funds.

Chapter 2: Start Early and Invest Regularly

"Adding time to investing is like adding fertilizer to a garden: It makes everything grow." — Meg Green, CFP

The Magic is in the Compounding

Rule of 72: Divide 72 by annual return to find how many years to double your money. 8% return → doubles every 9 years. 12% return → doubles every 6 years.

Einstein called compound interest "the greatest mathematical discovery of all time."

Eric Early vs. Larry Lately: Eric invests $4,000/year for 10 years (age 25–35), then stops. Total invested: $40,000. Larry invests $4,000/year for 30 years (age 35–65). Total invested: $120,000. At 8% return: Eric = $629,741; Larry = $489,383. Eric invested one-third the money but ended with 29% more because of a 10-year head start.

Saving is the Key to Wealth

The hardest part: acquiring the habit of saving. When you earn a dollar, try to save a minimum of 20 cents. Reducing spending is more efficient than earning more (every dollar saved avoids ~$1.40 pre-tax earning needed).

"The problem with getting rich quick is you have to do it so often." — Jason Zweig

Research proof (Venti & Wise, NBER 2000): The only significant factor determining wealth at retirement: some people choose to save more than others. Not health, luck, inheritance, or investment returns.

Finding the Money to Invest

  • Pay Yourself First: Take savings off the top of every paycheck. Minimum 10%.
  • Create a Tax-Free Fortune for $15/Day: Cut $15/day in spending → ~$5,500/year into a Roth IRA. A 25-year-old doing this at 8% for 40 years → $1,625,149 tax-free.
  • Don't Drive Yourself to the Poor House: New mid-priced car costs ~$2,500/year more than a 3-year-old used car. A 19-year-old buying used cars for life and investing the savings at 8% → $1,129,750 by age 65.
"When a man with experience meets a man with money, the man with money gets the experience, and the man with experience gets the money." — Old racetrack proverb

Not All Debt Is Bad Debt

Low-interest loans for homes, education, or starting a business = "good debt." Key: keep interest rates low, preferably tax-deductible, and borrow only when expected payoff exceeds cost of borrowing.

"What most young people don't understand is that SAVING is more important in the beginning than finding the best performing investment." — Eric Haban, Boglehead

Chapter 3: Stocks and Bonds

"Only buy something that you'd be perfectly happy to hold if the market shut down for 10 years." — Warren Buffett

Stocks

Stocks = ownership interest in a corporation. Returns come from: (1) dividends and (2) price appreciation. Don't invest all funds in a single company — if it goes bankrupt, you lose everything.

Bonds

A bond = an IOU / promissory note that pays interest until maturity, when face value is returned. Short-term: 1 year or less. Intermediate-term: 2–10 years. Long-term: 10+ years.

Treasury Issues

Safest bonds — backed by full faith and credit of U.S. government. Interest exempt from state and local taxes. T-Bills (13–52 weeks), T-Notes (2–10 years), T-Bonds (10+ years), TIPS (inflation-protected).

U.S. Savings Bonds (I Bonds & EE Bonds)

Tax-deferred for up to 30 years; exempt from state/local taxes. Can be used tax-free for qualified educational expenses (must be registered in parent's name). EE Bonds guaranteed to double in 20 years.

Corporate Bonds

Credit ratings: S&P AAA (highest) → BBB (lowest investment grade). Below BBB- = "junk bonds." Higher credit rating → lower yield; lower rating → higher yield (compensation for risk).

Municipal Bonds

Issued by state/local governments; normally free from federal tax, often also state tax. Can be triple tax-free. Worth considering in higher tax brackets (25%+).

Maturity and Duration

Duration measures bond/fund price sensitivity to interest rate changes. Bond prices move opposite to interest rates (seesaw analogy). Example: 4.3-year duration → 1% rate increase ≈ 4.3% price decline.

Why Invest in Bonds?

Bonds have low correlation to stocks — stabilizing force in a portfolio. 2008 bear market: equity funds lost 30–60%; Vanguard Total Bond Market Index Fund gained 5.05%.

Bogle's rule of thumb: Hold your age in bonds (20-year-old = 20% bonds, 50-year-old = 50% bonds).

Chapter 4: Mutual Funds, ETFs & Annuities

"I've found that when the market's going down and you buy funds wisely, at some point in the future you will be happy." — Peter Lynch

Mutual Funds

Pool money from many investors to buy securities. Governed by the Investment Company Act of 1940. Indexing replicates a benchmark at low cost. Active management picks stocks hoping to beat the benchmark at higher costs — very few consistently outperform.

10 Advantages of Mutual Funds: Diversification, professional management, low minimums, no-load options, liquidity, automatic reinvestment, convenience, customer service, record keeping, and variety.

Funds of Funds & Target-Date Funds

Vanguard LifeStrategy series: 4 funds from Growth (80/20) to Income (20/80). Target-date funds automatically become more conservative over time — no need to rebalance yourself.

Annuities

Fixed Annuities: Similar to bank CD but issued by insurance company. Often use "teaser" high initial rates, then drop. "Gotcha" surrender fees can last 10+ years.

Variable Annuities: Sub-accounts cloned from mutual funds with insurance wrapper. Expenses dramatically higher: many charge over 2.5% annually vs. Vanguard at 0.17%. Never put a variable annuity inside a tax-deferred account — "like wearing two raincoats when one will suffice."

Immediate Annuities: Lump sum → guaranteed income stream for life. You cannot outlive the income. No surrender charges, but you can't normally get your money back.

Bottom line: Most annuities are sold, not bought. Always fund 401(k), IRA, Roth IRA before considering an annuity.

Exchange-Traded Funds (ETFs)

Mutual funds that trade like stocks. Often very low expense ratios. Best for: Large, one-time purchases held long-term. Not suited for frequent small purchases — use index mutual funds instead. Using ETFs for day trading will likely hurt returns.

Chapter 5: Inflation-Protected Bonds

"Control your destiny or somebody else will." — Jack Welch

Inflation = a silent thief that steals purchasing power. 3% inflation over 40 years: $1,000 today → need $3,262 for same goods. 4% over 40 years: $1,000 → need $4,801.

I Bonds

"I" stands for Inflation; U.S. Savings Bonds backed by full faith and credit of U.S. government. Risk-free with guaranteed real return. Yield = fixed rate (stays for 30 years) + variable inflation adjustment (recalculated twice annually). Tax-deferred up to 30 years; exempt from state/local taxes.

TIPS (Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities)

Also U.S. Treasury-issued, inflation-protected. Rate set by marketplace at auction. Best location: Tax-deferred accounts (IRA, 401k) to avoid "phantom income" tax issue — you pay taxes on inflation-adjusted principal increase each year, even though you don't receive that money until maturity.

I Bond vs. TIPS: For practical purposes, nearly a toss-up. I Bonds offer more flexibility and tax deferral; TIPS offer higher guaranteed rates and work best in tax-deferred accounts.

Chapter 7: Index Funds

"Most of us have no chance of being as good as the average in any pursuit where others practice and hone skills for many, many hours. But we can be as good as the average investor in the stock market with no practice at all." — Jeremy Siegel

How Investing Is Different from Most of Life

Common life principles that fail in investing:

  • "Don't settle for average" → In investing, settling for the index beats 80% of pros
  • "Listen to your gut" → Your gut is the worst investing advisor
  • "Hire an expert" → You may get less than you pay for
  • "You get what you pay for" → Higher-cost funds do NOT perform better
  • "If there's a crisis, take action!" → Taking action in a downturn is usually the worst move
  • "History repeats itself" → Past performance does NOT predict future performance

Dalbar study (1993–2012): S&P 500 averaged 8.21%/year. The average equity fund investor earned only 4.25% — nearly half — due to market timing and chasing hot funds.

Seven Advantages of Index Funds

  1. No sales commissions — Load funds charge 4–6% upfront; index funds are no-load
  2. Low operating expenses — 1% difference compounded over 20 years on $10,000 at 10% = $11,133 difference
  3. Tax efficiency — Low turnover means fewer taxable capital gains distributions
  4. No need to hire a money manager — Saves 0.75–3.0% per year
  5. Higher diversification, less risk — Total Stock Market = entire U.S. equity market
  6. Doesn't matter who manages the fund — Active funds underperform their index in all categories
  7. No style drift or tracking errors — Index funds always match their benchmark
"In school, it takes a lot of work to earn an A. In investing, if you know nothing and buy index funds, you have a 100 percent chance of being a B investor. In a world where most investors get a D or worse, B is beautiful."

What the Experts Say About Indexing

"Most investors, both institutional and individual, will find that the best way to own common stocks is through an index fund that charges minimal fees." — Warren Buffett
"An index fund dooms you to mediocrity? Absolutely not: It virtually guarantees you superior performance." — William Bernstein
"I love index funds." — William F. Sharpe, Nobel Laureate

How to buy: Only consider no-load funds with expense ratios of 0.5% or less. Prefer index mutual funds over ETFs for regular investing. Recommended firms: Vanguard (preferred), Fidelity, T. Rowe Price, USAA, Charles Schwab.

Chapter 8: Asset Allocation

"The most fundamental decision of investing is the allocation of your assets: How much should you own in stocks? How much should you own in bonds? How much should you own in cash reserve?" — Jack Bogle

Asset allocation = dividing investments among different asset classes to minimize risk and maximize return for an efficient portfolio.

The Efficient Market Theory (EMT)

Key history: 1900 — Louis Bachelier planted the seeds. Alfred Cowles reviewed 7,500+ financial forecasts: "Can Stock Market Forecasters Forecast? It is doubtful." 1960s — Eugene Fama: stock prices are very efficient. 1973 — Burton Malkiel: "short-run changes in stock prices cannot be predicted."

"I know of no serious academic who would disagree with the thrust of EMT: The stock market sets a high hurdle that few investors can leap." — Jack Bogle

Modern Portfolio Theory

Harry Markowitz realized that a mixture of volatile noncorrelated securities could result in a portfolio with lower volatility and possibly higher return. Nobel Prize in 1990.

The Brinson, Hood, Beebower Study

Asset allocation determined 93.6% of the variability of pension plan returns. Active management cost the average fund a 1.10% reduction in return vs. just buying and holding an index.

Chapter 9: Costs Matter

"The shortest route to top quartile performance is to be in the bottom quartile of expenses." — Jack Bogle

Every dollar paid in commissions, fees, and expenses is one dollar less received from the investment. Total costs in the U.S. equity market are estimated at $300 billion annually.

Fees Covered by the Prospectus

"Death by a thousand fees isn't going to show up in a quarterly fund statement." — Stephen Schurr

Front-End Loads: 4–6% commission deducted upfront. $10,000 with 5% load → only $9,500 actually invested. Avoid load funds.

Back-End Loads (CDSL): Commission paid when you sell, typically declining over time. Funds with CDSL also charge annual 12b-1 fees.

No-Load Mutual Funds still have operating expenses: purchase fees, exchange fees, management fees, 12b-1 fees, and other expenses. Total Annual Fund Operating Expense (Expense Ratio) = management fees + 12b-1 fees + other expenses. Always read the fund prospectus.

Fees Not Covered by the Prospectus

Hidden Transaction Costs (caused by fund turnover) can easily exceed the expense ratio. These include brokerage commissions, bid-offer spreads, soft-dollar arrangements, and market impact costs. A $500M fund with 80–100% turnover could lose 3–5% per year to market impact alone.

Wrap Fees: Brokerage firm charges 2%+ on top of underlying fund expenses. Paying twice for professional management. Avoid the wrap.

Financial Research Corporation studied 11 common predictors of future mutual fund performance. Conclusion: The expense ratio is the only reliable predictor of future mutual fund performance.

Chapter 10: Mutual Fund Taxation

"For all long-term investors, there is only one objective — maximum total return after taxes." — Sir John Templeton

The Devastating Impact of Taxes

Charles Schwab study (1963–1992): $1.00 invested in U.S. stocks grew to $21.89 in a tax-deferred account vs. $9.87 in a taxable account — less than half.

How Mutual Funds Are Taxed

Stock Dividends: "Qualified" dividends taxed at preferential rates (0%, 15%, or 20%) — far lower than ordinary income rates. Bond dividends taxed at ordinary income rates (up to 39.6%). Tax-savvy investors: stocks in taxable accounts, bonds in tax-deferred accounts.

Capital Gains: Short-term (held ≤12 months): taxed at ordinary income rate. Long-term (held >12 months): taxed at max 15%. Hold mutual funds for more than 12 months to qualify for lower rates.

Tax Efficiency Ranking (Most → Least Efficient)

  1. Low-yielding cash, money market
  2. Tax-managed stock funds
  3. Total-market stock index funds
  4. Mid-cap and small-cap index funds
  5. Active stock funds
  6. Balanced funds
  7. Taxable bond funds
  8. REIT funds
  9. High-yield bond funds

Tax-Loss Harvesting

Example: You have a $2,000 gain and a fund with $6,000 loss: (1) Sell the losing fund before year-end, (2) Use $2,000 loss to offset the gain, (3) Use $3,000 to reduce ordinary income, (4) Carry forward remaining $1,000. Wash sale rule: Must wait 31 days before repurchasing the same fund.

"Of all the expenses investors pay, taxes have the potential for taking the biggest bite out of total returns." — The Vanguard Group

Tax-Sheltered Accounts

401(k) Plans: Pretax contributions (limit: $17,500; $23,000 with catch-up for 50+). Benefits: automatic withholding, employer matching, portability, creditor protection. If your 401(k) has poor options: invest up to company match, max out IRA, then contribute remaining to 401(k).

Asset Placement for Maximum After-Tax Return

The rule: Place most tax-inefficient funds in tax-deferred accounts; put tax-efficient funds in taxable accounts. Placing bonds in tax-deferred and stocks in taxable — vs. the reverse — produced $119,021 more after 30 years on a $100,000 portfolio.

14 Tax-Savvy Ideas

  1. Use tax-advantaged accounts (401(k), 403(b), IRAs, 529 plans)
  2. Buy fund shares after the distribution date
  3. Place tax-inefficient funds in retirement accounts
  4. Use tax-managed or tax-efficient index funds in taxable accounts
  5. Avoid balanced funds in taxable accounts
  6. Keep taxable fund turnover low
  7. Avoid short-term gains — hold >12 months
  8. Sell losing shares before year-end (tax-loss harvest)
  9. Sell profitable shares after the new year (delay tax)
  10. Determine best tax-basis method before selling
  11. Consider municipal bonds and U.S. Savings Bonds for taxable accounts
  12. During low-income years, convert to Roth
  13. Donate appreciated securities to charities
  14. Leave appreciated taxable assets to heirs (stepped-up basis)

Chapter 12: Diversification

"Diversification is a protection against ignorance." — Warren Buffett

WorldCom, Enron, and many dot-com companies destroyed employees who had most of their retirement in company stock. Diversify your portfolio! Diversification offers two benefits: (1) reduces risk, (2) can increase return through noncorrelation.

Correlation

Correlation coefficient ranges from +1.0 (perfect positive) to -1.0 (perfect negative). Investments that zig when others zag provide the best diversification. Owning multiple funds with R-squared near 1.0 provides no additional diversification.

"Fifteen stocks is not enough. Thirty is not enough. Even 200 is not enough. The only way to truly minimize the risks of stock ownership is by owning the whole market." — William Bernstein

Simplest approach: Vanguard Total Stock Market Index Fund — own nearly the entire U.S. market. Add international stock funds and bond funds of various types. Low R-squared funds offer the most diversification benefit (REITs at 0.12, Total Bond Market at 0.10).

Past Performance Does Not Predict Future Performance

75% of mutual fund investors mistakenly use short-term past performance as their primary reason for buying a fund. Morningstar stars are not predictive: five-star equity funds earned 5.7% vs. the Wilshire 5000's 10.3%.

Graham & Harvey study: Tracked 15,000+ market-timing predictions by 237 newsletter writers (1980–1992). Conclusion: "There is no evidence that newsletters can time the market." By the end of the study, 94% of the newsletters had gone out of business.

Chapter 15: Managing a Windfall

"If only God would give me some sign … a clear sign! Like making a large deposit in my name at a Swiss bank." — Woody Allen

More than 75% of windfalls are squandered. NBC News reported 70%+ of lottery winners exhaust their fortunes within three years.

Four Steps to Manage a Windfall

1. Deposit the Money and Leave It for Six Months. Emotions are temporary and usually subside within six months. Exceptions: take 1–2% to celebrate, pay off high-interest debts, pay any taxes due.

2. Know What You Can Buy. A $1 million windfall is NOT a ticket to being a millionaire — after taxes you may have ~$600,000. Rule of thumb: you can spend ~$5,000/year per $100,000 of invested capital.

3. Make a Wish List. Create three goal lists: short-term (within a year), intermediate (5 years), long-term. Rank goals by importance. Align spending with goals.

4. Get Professional Help. Start with a CPA who does NOT sell investment products. Get advice and insurance from different sources to avoid conflicts.

Chapter 16: Do You Need an Advisor?

"I helped put two children through Harvard — my broker's children." — Michael LeBoeuf

The broker's interests and yours aren't aligned. Most Bogleheads discovered that brokers sold them high-cost, loaded mutual funds and expensive annuities that earned big commissions but weren't in the client's best interest.

How Advisors Are Paid

Commission-based advisors: Paid when they sell you products — creates conflict of interest. Say "No, thanks."

Fee-only advisors (AUM): Charge a percentage of assets (0.25%–2%+). Even 2% on a bond fund yielding 4% consumes 50% of your return.

Fee-based ≠ Fee-only: "Fee-based" is an ambiguous term often used by salespeople who also earn commissions.

One-time fee or hourly rate: Best for DIY investors — advisor works for you since you're paying the entire bill. Interests are aligned.

Two most highly regarded designations: CFA (Chartered Financial Analyst) and CFP (Certified Financial Planner). Generic titles like "financial advisor" or "financial consultant" require NO special education or certification.

Chapter 17: Rebalancing

"Foolproof systems don't take into account the ingenuity of fools." — Gene Brown

Rebalancing = bringing your portfolio back to your target asset allocation after market forces have changed the percentages. It controls risk, forces "sell high, buy low," and takes advantage of reversion to the mean.

Portfolios that were never rebalanced had the lowest Sharpe ratios (worst risk-adjusted returns). All rebalancing methods outperformed never-rebalancing on a risk-adjusted basis.

When to Rebalance

Time-based: Rebalance at fixed intervals (annually, 18 months). Morningstar found 18-month intervals reaped similar benefits to more frequent rebalancing with lower costs.

Expansion bands: Create a window (±5 percentage points from target). If target equity is 60%, rebalance when equities fall below 55% or rise above 65%.

How to Rebalance

  • Sell overperforming assets and buy underperforming ones
  • Direct new contributions into underweight asset classes
  • Redirect mutual fund distributions to underweight funds
  • Rebalance tax-deferred accounts first — no tax consequences
  • Tax-loss harvest in taxable accounts before Dec 31
  • Pick a memorable rebalance date (birthday, New Year)
  • Simplest solution: Own a target-date fund that automatically rebalances

Chapter 18: Tune Out the Noise

"A fake fortuneteller can be tolerated. But an authentic soothsayer should be shot on sight." — Lazarus Long

All media have one goal: attract and hold an audience to sell advertising. When it comes to investing, this often conflicts with your interests.

The Great Wall Street Marketing Machine

The sales pitch: "Invest with us because we know how to beat the market." Investment pros have an 80% chance of underperforming the market over the long haul. The industry spends billions on propaganda to convince the public otherwise.

"Wall Street wants you to believe they are there to make money for you, but their true purpose is to make money from you." — Rick Ferri

The Three Biggest Lies — Wall Street Version

  1. "It's a stock picker's market" — The longer the time period, the greater the odds favor indexing.
  2. "The trend is your friend" — There has never been a 15-year period when stocks lost money. The only long-term trend was up.
  3. "We can predict the future" — Historical patterns amount to little more than selective perception.
"I'd compare stock pickers to astrologers, but I don't want to badmouth astrologers." — Eugene Fama

Separating Soothsayers from Truth Sayers

1. All Forecasting Is Noise. If you woke up with a one-year gift of stock market prophecy, would you publish a newsletter? Of course not — you'd keep quiet and invest.

2. Listen to the Helpers; Ignore the Hustlers. Follow writers like Jason Zweig (WSJ) and Jane Bryant Quinn. Avoid radio infomercials, "free" investment seminars, and market-beating newsletters.

"Mutual funds reporters lead a secret investing life. By day we write 'Six Funds to Buy NOW!' … By night, however, we invest in sensible index funds." — Anonymous, Fortune

3. Be a Bona Fide Skeptic. The best antidote to noise is knowledge based on empirical research by competent, unbiased parties with no interest in selling products.

Chapter 19: Mastering Your Emotions

"You aim for the palace and get drowned in the sewer." — Mark Twain

While most people are very emotional about acquiring money, behaving emotionally about money is a recipe for losing it. In the late 1960s, psychologists Amos Tversky and Daniel Kahneman pioneered behavioral economics, classifying the mental shortcuts (judgmental heuristics) people use to make quick economic decisions.

Greed and Fear

The Dalbar study (1993–2012): the average mutual fund investor underperformed the S&P 500 by 3.96% per year. Much of this was caused by investor behavior — excitedly chasing performance (buying high) and panicking (selling low).

"We have met the enemy, and he is us." — Pogo

Ego and Overconfidence

May be the biggest return killer. Three cautionary tales:

  1. The Beardstown Ladies: 14 grandmothers claimed 23.4% returns. Actual return: only 9.1%.
  2. Mensa Investment Club: During a period when S&P averaged 15.3%, Mensa (top 2% IQ) averaged only 2.5%.
  3. LTCM: Two Nobel Prize-winning economists who believed they could eliminate risk. Controlled $1.25 trillion leveraged. Required a $3.6 billion bailout.

Women tend to earn better returns than men because they trade less and take more prudent risks.

Loss Aversion

We feel the pain of a $100 loss twice as much as the benefit of a $100 gain. People who keep all money in CDs after a crash may think they're risk-free, but after taxes and inflation, they're actually losing purchasing power.

"A cat who sits on a hot stove will never sit on a hot stove again, but he will probably never sit on a cold one either." — Mark Twain

Other Emotional Traps

Paralysis by Analysis: With thousands of funds, some people never invest. Every day you don't invest is a day less of compounding.

The Endowment Effect: Overvaluing what we already own. Invest no more than 10% in employer's stock.

Following the Herd: Following the herd in investing is following it to the slaughterhouse.

Mental Accounting: A tax refund isn't "found money" — it's an interest-free loan you gave the government.

Anchoring: Holding a losing stock until it returns to "your price." "My mind is made up, don't confuse me with the facts."

Financial Negligence: Procrastination is the biggest detriment to financial success.

Keeping Emotions in Check

  1. Write down your major financial goals with dates and dollar amounts
  2. Practice the techniques in this book — pay off debt, invest in index funds, keep costs low, rebalance, stay the course
  3. Forget the notion that investing is supposed to be fun and exciting
  4. Don't expect to be perfectly rational all the time — learn from mistakes
  5. If you want thrills, take max 5% as a "casino account" — if you lose it all, it's gone forever
"Everyone is a fool for at least five minutes a day. Wisdom consists of not exceeding the limit." — Elbert Hubbard

Chapter 20: Making Your Money Last

"I've got all the money I'll ever need, if I die by four o'clock." — Henny Youngman

The Retiree's Spending Dilemma

Two ways to go wrong: (1) Overspending early — running out of money later, (2) Underspending — depriving yourself of goods and experiences. The story of Jacob Leeder: drove a 1984 Oldsmobile, lived modestly, never took vacations. His estate: approximately $36 million.

"The pride of dying rich raises the loudest laugh in hell." — John W. Foster

Ensuring Income for Life

  1. Keep fixed living expenses low. No enormous mortgage, expensive car payments, or credit card debt. Low overhead gives flexibility.
  2. Have a viable way to earn income if needed. Part-time work keeps you productive, mentally sharp, and reduces portfolio withdrawals.
  3. Delay retirement. Every year you delay = one more year to save, one more year of compounding.
  4. Wait to draw Social Security. Delaying from age 62 to full retirement provides 33% larger pension. Waiting until 70 garners 8% more per year above full retirement age.
  5. Consider an immediate annuity for those aged 75+.

Withdrawal Guidelines

Most credible studies conclude you can withdraw 4 to 6% per year with a good chance of not exhausting the portfolio. Above 6%, odds of survival diminish rapidly.

  • Inflation-adjusted: Begin at no more than 4% of portfolio's beginning value, increasing annually with cost of living
  • Fixed percentage: Withdraw max 5% of current value per year (be prepared for pay cuts in down years)

Longevity planning: For a married couple at 65, there's a 45% chance one will live to 90. Half of all retirees live longer than their life expectancy.

Chapter 21: Insurance

"Insurance is the business of protecting you against everything, except the insurance agent." — Evan Esar

Three Key Rules for Being Properly Insured

  1. Only insure against big catastrophes you can't afford out of pocket
  2. Carry the largest possible deductibles you can afford
  3. Only buy from the best-rated insurance companies (A. M. Best rating A or better)

Life Insurance

Buy term insurance. It's basic pay-as-you-go, no-frills, the cheapest way. Salespeople push whole life because they pocket 50–100% of the first year's premium on cash-value policies. Don't mix insurance with investing.

Disability Coverage

Your most valuable financial asset is your future earning power. A 35-year-old has a 20% chance of becoming disabled before age 65. More than half of personal bankruptcies were caused by illness and medical bills — 75% of those bankrupted had health insurance.

Property & Liability

Home/Renter's: Insure for replacement cost, not purchase price. Auto: Consider dropping comprehensive and collision on older cars. Liability umbrella: Purchase at least $1 million or enough to cover total net worth.

Long-Term Care

Nursing home costs well over $100,000/year in some areas. 600 out of 1,000 people will require a nursing home stay. Those with $200,000–$2 million in liquid assets should seriously consider it. Buy before age 60 — waiting until 70 means premiums are 2.5x higher.

Chapter 22: Estate Planning

"Rich people plan for three generations. Poor people plan for Saturday night." — Gloria Steinem

Essential Documents

A Will

Names the legal guardian for minor children. Instructs your executor on asset distribution. Without a will (intestate), the court decides everything.

Living Trust

A revocable trust avoids probate — trust assets don't go through court, affairs stay private, disposition starts immediately. You serve as your own trustee and retain full control.

Powers of Attorney

Durable power of attorney for finances (manages financial affairs if incapacitated) and durable power of attorney for health care (makes medical decisions on your behalf).

Advance Health Care Directive

Conveys your specific wishes regarding medical treatments and life-prolonging efforts. The Terry Schiavo case illustrates the importance of putting wishes in writing.

Stepped-Up Cost Basis

Heirs receive inherited assets at their value at time of death. Strategy: Consider spending down tax-deferred accounts (IRAs) first and leaving appreciated taxable assets to heirs — they get the step-up and avoid capital gains taxes. This is the opposite of conventional wisdom.

Gifting

An individual can give $14,000 per year to an unlimited number of people with no tax consequences. Couples can give double. No limit on tuition or medical bills paid directly to the institution, or gifts to charities.
