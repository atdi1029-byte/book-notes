Key Takeaways

  1. People are creatures of emotion, not logic.

    Carnegie’s foundational insight: humans are “creatures bristling with prejudices and motivated by pride and vanity.” Every principle in the book flows from this understanding. Rational arguments rarely change minds — only emotional satisfaction does.

  2. The deepest human craving is to feel important.

    John Dewey called it “the desire to be important.” William James called it “the craving to be appreciated.” This hunger drove Lincoln from a log cabin to the presidency, Dickens from a rat-infested warehouse to literary immortality, and Dillinger from obscurity to Public Enemy Number One. The difference between a Rockefeller and a criminal is simply how they satisfy this craving.

  3. Criticism is futile.

    Ninety-nine times out of a hundred, people don’t criticize themselves regardless of how wrong they are. Criticism puts people on the defensive, wounds their pride, and makes them justify themselves rather than change. Even Al Capone considered himself a public benefactor. Lincoln nearly died in a duel before learning this lesson — then never criticized anyone again.

  4. Appreciation — not flattery — is the master key.

    Charles Schwab earned the first million-dollar salary in American business not because he knew the most about steel, but because he could arouse enthusiasm through sincere appreciation. Flattery is insincere, from the teeth. Appreciation is sincere, from the heart. The difference is everything.

  5. See the world through others’ eyes.

    Henry Ford’s secret: “the ability to get the other person’s point of view and see things from that person’s angle as well as from your own.” Ninety percent of people ignore this ninety percent of the time. Don’t talk about what you want — bait the hook to suit the fish.

  6. You can’t win an argument.

    If you lose, you lose. If you win, you still lose — you’ve made the other person feel inferior and earned their resentment. The only victory is the one you avoid. Carnegie learned this from thousands of arguments in his courses: nine times out of ten, both sides end more firmly convinced they were right.

  7. Let the idea be theirs.

    Colonel Edward House planted ideas in President Wilson’s mind casually, then heard Wilson present them as his own at dinner. House never corrected him — he wanted results, not credit. The sketch artist who brought unfinished drawings for the buyer to complete sold every single one after three years of failure.

  8. Names are sacred.

    Jim Farley could call fifty thousand people by their first names and rode that skill to becoming Postmaster General. Andrew Carnegie named his steel works after railroad presidents to win contracts, and named a merged company after his rival George Pullman to close the deal. A person’s name is the sweetest sound in any language.

  9. Praise specifically, not generically.

    Don’t say “you’re good.” Point out exactly what they did well and why it matters. A struggling print shop worker became a loyal employee when his manager specifically described why his work was superior. Generic praise feels hollow; specific praise feels genuine.

  10. Give people a reputation to live up to.

    A fourth-grade teacher told the school’s worst-behaved boy: “Tommy, I understand you are a natural leader. I’m going to depend on you to help make this the best class.” He couldn’t let her down. A Belgian servant girl named Marie was told she had “treasures within her” — she transformed completely and married well. People become what you tell them they are.

Contrarian Ideas

  1. Never correct someone, even when they’re factually wrong.

    At a London banquet, a man attributed a Shakespeare quotation to the Bible. Carnegie corrected him. His friend — a Shakespeare scholar — kicked him under the table and sided with the wrong man. Privately: “Why prove to a man he is wrong? Is that going to make him like you?” Carnegie concludes that being right is worthless if it costs you the relationship.

  2. Admit fault even when you’re not entirely wrong.

    When a policeman caught Carnegie walking his dog off-leash, Carnegie preemptively condemned himself so thoroughly that the officer had no choice but to defend him: “Well, a little dog like that isn’t going to harm anybody.” Self-criticism disarms opponents because the only way they can maintain importance is by forgiving you.

  3. Listening is more powerful than speaking.

    Carnegie spent an entire dinner party listening to a botanist talk about exotic plants without contributing a word. The botanist told the host Carnegie was “the most interesting conversationalist.” This inverts the conventional wisdom that charisma means being fascinating — it means being fascinated.

  4. Technical knowledge matters far less than people skills.

    A Carnegie Foundation study found that only 15% of financial success comes from technical knowledge — 85% comes from skill in human engineering. Even in fields like engineering, the ability to deal with people determines who rises and who doesn’t.

  5. “But” destroys praise.

    Saying “We’re proud of you, but if you worked harder on algebra…” makes the child question whether the praise was sincere. Replace “but” with “and”: “We’re proud of you, and by continuing these efforts, your algebra can improve too.” One word changes everything.

  6. The desire to excel trumps money as a motivator.

    Frederic Herzberg studied thousands of workers and found the most motivating factor is not pay, benefits, or conditions — it’s the work itself. Charles Schwab wrote a “6” in chalk on the factory floor (the day shift’s output) and walked away. By morning, the night shift had replaced it with a “7.” Competition — the desire to excel — transformed a lagging mill into the top producer.

Carnegie’s 30 Principles

Part One — Fundamental Techniques in Handling People

  1. Don’t criticize, condemn, or complain.
  2. Give honest and sincere appreciation.
  3. Arouse in the other person an eager want.

Part Two — Six Ways to Make People Like You

  1. Become genuinely interested in other people.
  2. Smile.
  3. Remember that a person’s name is the sweetest sound in any language.
  4. Be a good listener. Encourage others to talk about themselves.
  5. Talk in terms of the other person’s interests.
  6. Make the other person feel important — and do it sincerely.

Part Three — How to Win People to Your Way of Thinking

  1. The only way to get the best of an argument is to avoid it.
  2. Show respect for the other person’s opinions. Never say “You’re wrong.”
  3. If you are wrong, admit it quickly and emphatically.
  4. Begin in a friendly way.
  5. Get the other person saying “yes, yes” immediately.
  6. Let the other person do a great deal of the talking.
  7. Let the other person feel that the idea is theirs.
  8. Try honestly to see things from the other person’s point of view.
  9. Be sympathetic with the other person’s ideas and desires.
  10. Appeal to the nobler motives.
  11. Dramatise your ideas.
  12. Throw down a challenge.

Part Four — Be a Leader

  1. Begin with praise and honest appreciation.
  2. Call attention to people’s mistakes indirectly.
  3. Talk about your own mistakes before criticising the other person.
  4. Ask questions instead of giving direct orders.
  5. Let the other person save face.
  6. Praise the slightest improvement and praise every improvement.
  7. Give the other person a fine reputation to live up to.
  8. Use encouragement. Make the fault seem easy to correct.
  9. Make the other person happy about doing the thing you suggest.

How This Book Was Written — And Why

First published in 1937 with a 5,000-copy print run, the book became an overnight sensation that has never gone out of print. Translated into nearly every written language, it reached over ten million readers in thirty-six languages. Carnegie constantly revised the material until his death in 1955; his wife Dorothy Carnegie later updated it with contemporary examples while preserving his “brash, breezy” conversational style. The book was dedicated to Homer Croy — “a man who doesn’t need to read it.”

In the first 35 years of the twentieth century, over 200,000 books were published in America — most “deadly dull,” many financial failures. The president of a major publishing house admitted the company lost money on seven of every eight books after seventy-five years in business. Carnegie had been teaching business professionals since 1912, initially just public speaking. He gradually realized adults needed even more training in getting along with people.

A Carnegie Foundation study found that even in technical fields like engineering, only 15% of financial success comes from technical knowledge — 85% comes from skill in human engineering. A University of Chicago survey of adults revealed that their second biggest interest after health was understanding and getting along with people. Yet no college offered a practical course on human relations, and no working manual existed.

Carnegie’s research was exhaustive: he hired a trained researcher for eighteen months in libraries, read newspaper columns, family court records, old philosophers, new psychologists, over 100 biographies of Theodore Roosevelt alone, and interviewed Marconi, Edison, FDR, James Farley, Owen D. Young, Clark Gable, and Mary Pickford. The book started as rules on a postcard, grew to a leaflet, then booklets — over fifteen years of experiment and research.

The results were transformational. An employer with 314 employees who had driven, criticized, and condemned workers for years reported: “No one greeted me. My employees actually looked the other way. But now they are all my friends and even the janitor calls me by my first name.” A Harvard graduate declared he learned more in fourteen weeks than in four years at Harvard. Carnegie himself admitted: “I wrote the book yet found it difficult to apply everything. Easier to criticize than understand. Easier to find fault than praise.”

Carnegie’s honest admission: “I wrote the book yet found it difficult to apply everything. Easier to criticize than understand. Easier to find fault than praise. More natural to talk about what you want than what others want.” His nine suggestions for getting the most from the book include: develop a deep desire to learn, read each chapter twice, stop frequently to reflect, mark and underscore, review monthly, apply rules at every opportunity, gamify learning (offer a dollar when someone catches you violating a principle), conduct weekly self-examinations, and keep a record of triumphs with specific names, dates, and results. A Wall Street banker with little formal schooling became one of America’s most important financiers by devoting each Saturday evening to a ruthless process of self-examination, asking himself: “What mistakes did I make? What did I do that was right — and how could I have improved? What lessons can I learn?”

Carnegie closes the introduction with a declaration: “Remember that you are not merely trying to acquire information. You are attempting to form new habits. Ah yes, you are attempting a new way of life.” As Herbert Spencer put it: “The great aim of education is not knowledge but action.” And this is an action book.

Part One: Fundamental Techniques in Handling People

Chapter 1: If You Want to Gather Honey, Don’t Kick Over the Beehive

Principle: Don’t criticize, condemn, or complain.

Carnegie opens with a parade of criminals who never blamed themselves. “Two Gun” Crowley, surrounded by police with machine guns, wrote while bleeding: “Under my coat is a weary heart, but a kind one — one that would do nobody any harm.” At Sing Sing’s death house: “This is what I get for defending myself.” Al Capone considered himself a public benefactor: “I have spent the best years of my life giving people the lighter pleasures, helping them have a good time, and all I get is abuse.” Dutch Schultz believed it too. Sing Sing warden Lewis Lawes confirmed: virtually no criminal considers himself a bad man. Ninety-nine times out of a hundred, people don’t criticize themselves for anything, no matter how wrong it may be.

If hardened criminals won’t criticize themselves, what hope do we have that gentle criticism will change the ordinary people we deal with? Carnegie cites B.F. Skinner — animals rewarded for good behavior learn faster than those punished — and Hans Selye: “As much as we thirst for approval, we dread condemnation.” George B. Johnston, a safety coordinator, enforced hard hat rules through authority — workers complied when he was present but removed their hats the moment he left. When he changed approach, asking if the hats were uncomfortable and reminding workers pleasantly that the hats protected them from injury, compliance increased permanently with no resentment.

The centerpiece is Lincoln’s evolution. As a young Illinois lawyer, Lincoln ridiculed a vain politician named James Shields in an anonymous newspaper letter. Shields challenged him to a duel — they met on a Mississippi River sandbar, seconds away from death before intermediaries intervened. It was the most lurid incident of Lincoln’s life. From that moment, he almost never criticized anyone again. During the Civil War, he bore McClellan, Pope, Burnside, Hooker, and Meade — generals who bungled catastrophically — without public condemnation.

The most powerful illustration: after Gettysburg, Lincoln was furious that Meade let Lee escape. He wrote a devastating letter — then never sent it. It was found among his papers after his death. He had realized that Meade, having seen as much blood as he had, might not be so eager to attack either.

The Roosevelt-Taft split is instructive. Roosevelt supported Taft for president in 1908. After returning from abroad, Roosevelt denounced Taft’s conservatism, formed the Bull Moose party, split the Republican vote, and delivered the most disastrous defeat in Republican history. Taft said tearfully: “I don’t see how I could have done any differently.” Criticism didn’t change Taft — it only made him justify himself.

Mark Twain wrote devastating letters when angry (“The thing for you is a burial permit”) — but his wife secretly lifted them from the mail. The writing let him blow off steam without sending them. Thomas Hardy quit writing fiction forever after bitter criticism. Thomas Chatterton was driven to suicide by it. Carnegie himself nursed a grudge for ten years after author Richard Harding Davis sent a cutting note.

Carnegie also tells the story of test pilot Bob Hoover, whose plane nearly crashed when a mechanic fueled it with jet fuel instead of gasoline. The mechanic was sick with guilt. Hoover put his arm around the man’s shoulder: “To show you I’m sure that you’ll never do this again, I want you to service my F-51 tomorrow.”

The chapter closes with W. Livingston Larned’s classic essay “Father Forgets” — a father reflects on spending the entire day criticizing his young son for dirty shoes, messy face, and poor table manners. That evening, the boy timidly throws his arms around the father’s neck. The father’s agonized realization: “I was measuring you by the yardstick of my own years.”

Chapter 2: The Big Secret of Dealing with People

Principle: Give honest and sincere appreciation.

Carnegie lists eight basic human wants — health, food, sleep, money, the hereafter, sex, children’s well-being — and then the one almost never satisfied: a feeling of importance. This gnawing, unfaltering human hunger distinguishes us from animals. The rare individual who honestly satisfies it will hold people in the palm of their hand.

George Washington wanted to be called “His Mightiness, the President.” Columbus pleaded for “Admiral of the Ocean and Viceroy of India.” Catherine the Great refused letters not addressed to “Her Imperial Majesty.” Victor Hugo aspired to have Paris renamed in his honor. Shakespeare sought a coat of arms for his family. Carnegie’s own father bred prize Duroc-Jersey hogs and displayed blue ribbons on white muslin when visitors came — the hogs didn’t care about ribbons, but Father did.

The difference between a Rockefeller and a Dillinger? Simply how they satisfied this craving. Rockefeller got his feeling of importance by giving money for a hospital in Peking. Dillinger got it as a bandit and killer — “I’m Public Enemy Number One.”

A psychiatric hospital physician told Carnegie about a woman whose husband refused to love her — she went insane and in imagination divorced him, married into English aristocracy, and insisted on being called “Lady Smith.” The doctor said: “If I could stretch out my hand and restore her sanity, I wouldn’t do it. She’s much happier as she is.” If some people go insane to get their feeling of importance, imagine what miracles honest appreciation can achieve.

Charles Schwab earned the first million-dollar salary in American business (1921, when $50 a week was well-off) — picked by Andrew Carnegie to lead U.S. Steel at age thirty-eight not because he knew the most about steel, but for his ability to enthuse people:

“I consider my ability to arouse enthusiasm among my people the greatest asset I possess, and the way to develop the best that is in a person is by appreciation and encouragement. There is nothing else that so kills the ambitions of a person as criticisms from superiors. I never criticize anyone. I believe in giving a person incentive to work. So I am anxious to praise but loath to find fault. If I like anything, I am hearty in my approbation and lavish in my praise.”

Andrew Carnegie praised associates publicly and privately. His self-written epitaph: “Here lies one who knew how to get around him men who were cleverer than himself.”

When Rockefeller’s partner Edward Bedford lost a million dollars in South America, Rockefeller praised him for saving 60% of the investment: “That’s splendid. We don’t always do as well as that upstairs.”

A study of runaway wives found the primary reason was “lack of appreciation.” Florenz Ziegfeld, the great Broadway producer, took “drab little creatures” and made them feel beautiful — raised chorus girl salaries from $30 to $175 a week, sent telegrams on opening night, gave American Beauty roses. He transformed them through “sheer power of his gallantry and consideration.”

A blind Detroit schoolboy named Stevie Morris was praised by his teacher for his remarkable hearing — compensating for his blindness. That appreciation changed his life. He became Stevie Wonder.

Supervisor Pamela Dunham of New Fairfield, Connecticut, had a janitor doing poor work, jeered at by employees. She praised him occasionally for good work in front of others. His job improved daily — “honest appreciation got results where criticism and ridicule failed.”

Carnegie tells the transformative story of a self-improvement class where a husband was asked to list six things his wife could improve. He reversed the exercise — sent six red roses with a note: “I love you the way you are.” His wife nearly cried. The other women called it “the most considerate thing I have ever heard.”

The crucial distinction: appreciation is sincere and from the heart; flattery is insincere and from the teeth. As Carnegie defines it: “Flattery is telling the other person precisely what he thinks about himself.” General Obregon warned: “Don’t be afraid of enemies who attack you. Be afraid of the friends who flatter you.”

We spend 95% of our time thinking about ourselves — when we stop to think of the other person’s good points, cheap flattery becomes unnecessary. Emerson put it simply: “Use what language you will, you can never say anything but what you are.”

Chapter 3: He Who Can Do This Has the Whole World with Him

Principle: Arouse in the other person an eager want.

Carnegie’s fishing analogy: “Personally I am very fond of strawberries and cream, but I have found that for some strange reason, fish prefer worms.” Don’t think about what you want — bait the hook to suit the fish.

Andrew Carnegie’s nephew trick is delightful. His sister-in-law worried that her Yale boys weren’t writing home. Carnegie wagered $100 he’d get replies without asking. He sent chatty letters mentioning in a postscript that he was enclosing five dollars for each — but neglected to enclose the money. Replies came immediately.

Stan Novak’s kindergarten story is charming. His son Tim was kicking and screaming about starting kindergarten. Instead of forcing him, the family sat around the kitchen table finger-painting, singing songs, listing fun things about school. Tim started peeping around the corner, begging to join. Next morning: Tim was sitting asleep in the living room chair “waiting to go to kindergarten. I don’t want to be late.”

A father couldn’t get his three-year-old to eat. Scolding, nagging: “Mother wants you to eat this.” The boy paid as much attention as a fleck of sand on the beach. The father’s realization: no trace of horse sense to expect a three-year-old to react to a thirty-year-old’s viewpoint. He asked himself: what does this boy want? The child was being bullied daily by a bigger kid who stole his tricycle. Father explained eating would make him strong enough to “wallop the daylights out of” the bully. The boy devoured spinach, sauerkraut, and salt mackerel.

Another father couldn’t stop bed-wetting. Scolding, spanking, shaming failed. What did the boy want? Pajamas like Daddy (not a nightgown like Grandmother) and his own bed. Mother took him to the department store; the salesgirl coached: “Young man, what can I show you?” The boy stood taller. On delivery day he shouted: “Daddy! Come upstairs and see my bed that I bought!” Pride was involved — and pride stopped the problem.

K.T. Dutschmann’s three-year-old refused breakfast cereal. Scolding, pleading, coaxing failed. One morning she put the girl on a chair and let her make the cereal herself. “Oh, look, Daddy, I am making the cereal this morning.” The child ate two helpings — she had achieved a feeling of importance and found an avenue of self-expression. William Winter: “Self-expression is the dominant necessity of human nature.”

Carnegie tears apart a radio company’s letter: every line says “we desire,” “we want,” “our accounts.” Nobody cares what the company desires — the reader is worried about their own mortgage, garden bugs, blood pressure, and missed bus.

Barbara Anderson wrote to twelve banks not about what she wanted (a job) but how her ten years of experience could help them meet their goals. Eleven of twelve invited her for interviews. She had a choice of offers because she didn’t state what she wanted — she wrote how she could help them.

Carnegie also demonstrates with a hotel ballroom negotiation. The hotel wanted to triple his lecture room rent after tickets were printed. Carnegie didn’t argue about what he wanted. He drew a line down a sheet of paper: “Advantages” vs. “Disadvantages” for the hotel.

Advantage for the hotel: rent the ballroom for dances and conventions at higher rates. Disadvantage for the hotel: lose educated, cultured lecture crowds worth $5,000 or more in free newspaper advertising. The increase dropped from 300% to 50% — without Carnegie saying a word about his own desires.

Michael E. Whidden of Shell Oil couldn’t get an older manager to clean up his service station. Instead of pleading, he invited the manager to visit the newest Shell station. The manager was so impressed he cleaned up his own station. Both benefited. Owen D. Young captured the principle: “People who can put themselves in the place of other people, who can understand the workings of their minds, need never worry about what the future has in store for them.”

Part Two: Six Ways to Make People Like You

Chapter 1: Do This and You’ll Be Welcome Anywhere

Principle: Become genuinely interested in other people.

You can make more friends in two months by becoming interested in other people than in two years trying to get others interested in you. Dogs prove this — Carnegie’s childhood dog Tippy (killed by lightning) taught him friendship by instinct. A dog makes a living by giving nothing but love. No ulterior motives, no hidden agendas — just genuine affection. That’s why dogs are universally loved.

Alfred Adler wrote: “It is the individual who is not interested in his fellow men who has the greatest difficulties in life and provides the greatest injury to others. It is from among such individuals that all human failures spring.”

The New York Telephone Company studied five hundred conversations and found the word “I” was used 3,900 times. When you look at a group photograph, whose face do you look for first? People are not interested in you or me — they are interested in themselves.

Howard Thurston, the acknowledged dean of magicians for forty years — sixty million paid admissions, two million dollars in profit — never thought of his audience as “a bunch of suckers.” His success came not from superior knowledge (hundreds knew as much about magic) but from genuine interest in people. Before every show: “I love my audience. I love my audience.” Theodore Roosevelt greeted servants by name, asked kitchen maids about their families, and ate their corn bread — the White House head usher Ike Hoover, who served forty years there, said his visit was “the only happy day we had in nearly two years, and not one of us would exchange it for a hundred-dollar bill.”

A sales rep named Edward Sykes lost a drugstore account in Hingham, Massachusetts. On his next visit, instead of approaching the owner directly, he talked to the soda clerk and sales clerk first. Hours later, the soda fountain boy told the owner Sykes was “one of the few salespeople who bothered to say hello” to store employees. The owner agreed — and became a loyal customer once more.

The Brooklyn Institute wanted distinguished authors — Kathleen Norris, Fannie Hurst, Ida Tarbell, Rupert Hughes — to lecture for free. They sent letters signed by 150 students expressing genuine admiration and asking for guidance. The authors liked being asked; they traveled to Brooklyn to give a helping hand.

C.M. Knaphle tried for years to sell fuel to a chain-store organization. Instead of selling, he asked the executive to help with a debate by defending chain stores. The executive talked for an hour and forty-seven minutes, eyes glowing, proud of the service he rendered to communities. At the door: “Please see me again later in the spring. I should like to place an order with you for fuel.” Knaphle reflected: “I had made more headway in two hours by becoming genuinely interested in him and his problems than I could have made in ten years trying to get him interested in me and my product.”

Charles R. Walters needed a confidential report from a bank president. He remembered the president’s secretary collected stamps for her twelve-year-old son. He sent stamps from the bank’s foreign department — letters from seven continents. The president radiated smiles and good will, spent half an hour on stamps, then devoted over an hour giving Walters all the information he needed — and called in subordinates and telephoned associates for more.

The most moving story: ten-year-old Martin Ginsberg was in the hospital welfare ward, alone on Thanksgiving Day. Major surgery was imminent. His father was dead, his mother on welfare and couldn’t visit. A student nurse heard his sobbing. She told him she was lonely too, brought two trays of turkey dinner with all the fixings, and stayed on her own time until almost 11 PM playing games. “Many Thanksgivings have come and gone since I was ten, but one never passes without me remembering that particular one and my feelings of frustration, fear, loneliness and the warmth and tenderness of the stranger that somehow made it all bearable.”

Most remarkable: after WWI, Kaiser Wilhelm was universally despised. One small boy wrote a sincere letter saying he would always love Wilhelm as his Emperor. The Kaiser invited the boy to visit; the boy came with his mother — and the Kaiser married her.

Publilius Syrus wrote in 100 BC: “We are interested in others when they are interested in us.”

Chapter 2: A Simple Way to Make a Good First Impression

Principle: Smile.

Charles Schwab’s smile was worth a million dollars. A smile says: “I like you. You make me happy. I am glad to see you.” Dogs are universally loved for exactly this reason — they’re so glad to see you.

William Steinhardt, a New York stockbroker married eighteen years, was “one of the worst grouches who ever walked down Broadway.” He seldom smiled at his wife, barely spoke before leaving for work. One morning he forced himself: “Good morning, my dear.” She was bewildered, then shocked. He smiled at the elevator operator, the subway cashier. Everyone smiled back. Complaints became easier at work. “These things have literally revolutionized my life.”

The effect of a smile is powerful even when unseen. Telephone companies teach “phone power” — smile when talking on the phone because your smile comes through in your voice. Robert Cryer recruited a Ph.D. candidate who chose his smaller company over larger ones. The candidate’s explanation: “Your voice sounded as if you were glad to hear from me… that you really wanted me to be part of your organisation.”

William James observed that action and feeling go together: “The sovereign voluntary path to cheerfulness, if our cheerfulness be lost, is to sit up cheerfully and to act and speak as if cheerfulness were already there.” Happiness doesn’t depend on outward conditions but on inner ones — not what you have, who you are, where you are, or what you’re doing, but what you think about it.

Shakespeare put it plainly: “There is nothing either good or bad, but thinking makes it so.” Lincoln agreed: “Most folks are about as happy as they make up their minds to be.” Carnegie saw crippled boys on crutches at a Long Island Railroad station; the man in charge told him: “When a boy realizes he is going to be a cripple for life, he is shocked at first; but after he gets over the shock, he usually becomes as happy as normal boys.”

Senora Maria Gonzalez worked in a closed-off office in Guadalajara, Mexico, envying others’ shared camaraderie. After weeks of looking away shyly, she told herself: “Maria, you can’t expect those women to come to you.” She put on her brightest smile and said “Hi, how are you today?” to each person. Smiles and hellos returned immediately. Acquaintances developed, some became friendships.

An ancient Chinese proverb states: “A man without a smiling face must not open a shop.” A woman at a New York dinner party spent a fortune on sables, diamonds, and pearls but had done nothing about her face — which radiated sourness and selfishness. The expression on your face is far more important than the clothes on your back. Carnegie adds his own definition of a smile: “It costs nothing, but creates much. It enriches those who receive, without impoverishing those who give.”

Chapter 3: If You Don’t Do This, You Are Headed for Trouble

Principle: Remember that a person’s name is the sweetest and most important sound in any language.

Jim Farley — orphaned at ten when his father was killed in a horse accident, the family left with a few hundred dollars of insurance, no high school education — could call fifty thousand people by their first names. Before Roosevelt’s 1932 campaign, he wrote hundreds of letters, traveled twenty states, twelve thousand miles in nineteen days. After returning, he sent personalized letters to thousands: “Dear Bill,” “Dear Jane,” signed “Jim.” That skill made him Postmaster General and Democratic National Committee chairman by age forty-six.

Andrew Carnegie discovered the power of names at age ten. He caught a mother rabbit with babies but had nothing to feed them. He told neighborhood children: pull clover and dandelions, and I’ll name the bunnies after you. It worked perfectly. In business, he named his steel works after Pennsylvania Railroad’s president to win rail contracts, and merged with Pullman by suggesting the company carry Pullman’s name.

Napoleon III’s technique when he didn’t catch a name: “So sorry, I didn’t get the name clearly. How is it spelled?” He repeated it during conversation, associated it with features, wrote it down, then tore up the paper. Most people don’t remember names because they don’t invest the effort to concentrate, repeat, and fix them in mind.

Sid Levy had a customer named Nicodemus Papadoulos who broke down in tears when Levy pronounced his name correctly: “In fifteen years nobody has ever made the effort to call me by my right name.” P.T. Barnum offered his grandson $25,000 to carry the family name. Benton Love of Texas Commerce Bancshares put it bluntly: “The executive who tells me he can’t remember names is telling me he can’t remember a significant part of his business.”

Franklin D. Roosevelt took time for everyone. When W.F. Chamberlain delivered a special Chrysler car to the White House, FDR called him by name, showed genuine interest, and days later sent an autographed photo and a personal thank-you note. Most people don’t remember names because they don’t take the time to concentrate, repeat, and fix the name in their mind. “Good manners,” said Emerson, “are made up of petty sacrifices.”

Chapter 4: An Easy Way to Become a Good Conversationalist

Principle: Be a good listener. Encourage others to talk about themselves.

At a New York dinner party, Carnegie listened intently for hours while a distinguished botanist discussed exotic plants, experiments, indoor gardens, and potato facts. He violated etiquette by ignoring everyone else at the table. He contributed almost nothing. The botanist told the host Carnegie was “most stimulating” and “the most interesting conversationalist” — though Carnegie had said hardly anything at all. As Jack Woodford observed: “Few human beings are proof against the implied flattery of rapt attention.” Harvard president Charles W. Eliot described the art of listening: “not mere silence, but a form of activity.” He sat erect, hands joined, faced his interlocutor, and seemed to hear with eyes as well as ears.

The New York Telephone Company sent a “troubleshooter” to deal with a vicious customer who cursed, raved, threatened, refused to pay his bills, wrote to newspapers, filed complaints, and started lawsuits. The troubleshooter simply listened — saying “yes” and sympathizing — for nearly three hours across four visits. The customer became a charter member of the “Telephone Subscribers’ Protective Association” — the only member besides the troubleshooter. He had wanted a feeling of importance, and listening gave it to him.

Julian Detmer of the Detmer Woollen Company listened patiently to an angry customer who traveled all the way to Chicago over a small debt dispute. He didn’t interrupt. The customer talked himself out. He returned later, placed the largest order the company had ever received, and named his son Detmer — remaining a friend and customer for twenty-two years.

Edward Bok, a poor Dutch immigrant boy who washed bakery windows after school with only six years of formal education, became one of America’s most successful magazine editors. His method: he wrote to famous people asking about their childhoods. He wrote General Garfield (running for President); Grant drew a map and invited fourteen-year-old Bok to dinner. Soon he was corresponding with Emerson, Holmes, Longfellow, Mrs. Abraham Lincoln, Louisa May Alcott, General Sherman, and Jefferson Davis — visiting many as a welcome guest in their homes. All because he listened with genuine fascination.

During the Civil War, Lincoln asked an old friend to travel from Springfield to Washington. He talked for hours about the Emancipation Proclamation, reading letters and newspaper articles — then sent his friend home without ever asking for his opinion. He had wanted a sympathetic listener to unburden himself. “He seemed to feel easier after that talk.”

Carnegie’s recipe for being despised: “Never listen to anyone for long. Talk incessantly about yourself. If you have an idea while the other person is talking, don’t wait — bust right in and interrupt.” People are a hundred times more interested in themselves than in you. A person’s toothache means more to them than a famine killing millions.

Chapter 5: How to Interest People

Principle: Talk in terms of the other person’s interests.

Everyone who visited Theodore Roosevelt was astonished at the breadth and depth of his knowledge. Whether the visitor was a cowboy, a Rough Rider, a New York politician, or a foreign diplomat, Roosevelt knew exactly what to say. The secret: whenever he expected a visitor, he sat up late the night before reading about the subject his guest treasured most. The royal road to a person’s heart is to talk about what they treasure most.

Yale professor William Lyon Phelps remembered visiting his Aunt Libby at age eight. A middle-aged New York lawyer spent the entire evening talking to young Phelps about boats — a subject the lawyer cared nothing about. Aunt Libby’s explanation: “He is a gentleman. He saw you were interested in boats, and he talked about the things he knew would interest and please you. He made himself agreeable.” Phelps never forgot.

Edward Chalif needed a corporate president to fund a Scout trip to Europe. Instead of talking about Scouts, he asked to see the president’s famous million-dollar cancelled check. The president gladly showed it, told the story behind it — and funded not one boy but five, plus Chalif, extended the trip to seven weeks, and provided letters of introduction to European branch presidents who met them in Paris. “Yet I know if I hadn’t found out what he was interested in, and got him warmed up first, I wouldn’t have found him one-tenth as easy to approach.”

Henry Duvernoy of Duvernoy and Sons tried to sell bread to a certain New York hotel for four years — called weekly, went to the same social affairs, even took rooms in the hotel. Total failure. Then he discovered the manager belonged to the Hotel Greeters of America and was its president nationally and internationally. He talked about the Greeters; the manager talked for half an hour, vibrant with enthusiasm. The Greeters were his hobby, the passion of his life. Days later, the steward called: “I don’t know what you did to the old boy, but he sure is sold on you.” Duvernoy reflected: “I had been drumming at that man for four years — trying to get his business — and I’d still be drumming at him if I hadn’t finally taken the trouble to find out what he was interested in.”

Chapter 6: How to Make People Like You Instantly

Principle: Make the other person feel important — and do it sincerely.

Carnegie at the post office noticed a bored clerk weighing envelopes with mechanical indifference. He found something genuine to admire: “I certainly wish I had your head of hair.” The clerk beamed, became pleased, and a warm conversation followed. An audience member later asked: “What did you want to get out of him?” Carnegie’s answer: nothing. If appreciation requires getting something in return, you’ve failed. He predicts the man went home happy, told his wife, and looked in the mirror. The little courtesies oil the monotonous grind of everyday life: “I’m sorry to trouble you,” “Would you be so kind as to—?”, “Won’t you please?”, “Thank you.”

Philosophers have agreed for thousands of years — Zoroaster in Persia, Confucius in China, Lao-tse in the Valley of the Han, Buddha on the Ganges, Jesus in Judea: treat others as you want to be treated.

Hall Caine, a blacksmith’s son with only eight years of schooling, wrote to poet Dante Gabriel Rossetti praising his artistic achievements. Rossetti was delighted, invited Caine to London as secretary. Through this connection Caine met literary artists, gained encouragement, and launched a career that made him the richest literary man of his time, with a multimillion-dollar estate at Greeba Castle on the Isle of Man. “Such is the power, the stupendous power, of sincere heart-felt appreciation.”

Edward E. Harriman wanted a job from inaccessible business maverick R.J. Funkhouser, who rose from poverty to riches and protected himself with a dedicated secretary. Harriman studied the secretary’s interests first, then told her he had a proposition that might translate into financial and political success for Funkhouser. She arranged the meeting. Harriman framed every qualification as a contribution to Funkhouser’s success: “Mr. Funkhouser, I believe I can make money for you.” He was hired immediately and worked there for over twenty years.

Instructor Ronald Rowland invited a quiet, shy student named Chris into the advanced class. Chris was stunned: “Who me, Mr. Rowland? Am I good enough?” He walked out “seemingly two inches taller.” Rowland made a sign for his classroom: “YOU ARE IMPORTANT.” Almost all people feel superior to you in some way. The sure way to their hearts: let them realize, subtly and sincerely, that you recognize their importance. Emerson: “Every man I meet is my superior in some way. In that, I learn of him.”

Part Three: How to Win People to Your Way of Thinking

Chapter 1: You Can’t Win an Argument

Principle: The only way to get the best of an argument is to avoid it.

Carnegie was an inveterate arguer in youth. He studied logic, competed in debates, even planned to write a book on the subject. After listening to thousands of arguments and watching their effects, he concluded there is only one way to get the best of an argument: avoid it entirely. Avoid it like rattlesnakes and earthquakes.

Truck salesman Patrick O’Haire won every argument with customers who criticized White Motor Company trucks. “I often walked out of an office saying: ‘I told that bird something.’ Sure I had told him something, but I hadn’t sold him anything.” His transformation: if a buyer said “White truck is no good,” he agreed that the competitor made fine trucks, then simply talked about White’s good points. He became a star salesman.

Tax consultant Frederick Parsons argued with a government inspector for an hour over $9,000. The longer they argued, the more stubborn the inspector became. Parsons changed tactics: “I suppose this is a very petty matter compared with the really important decisions you’re required to make. I’ve had to get my knowledge from books. You are getting yours from the firing line of experience.” The inspector softened, talked about his work, told about his children. Three days later: the tax return was accepted exactly as filed.

Lincoln reprimanded a young army officer for arguing with an associate: “No man who is resolved to make the most of himself can spare time for personal contention. Still less can he afford to take the consequences, including the vitiation of his temper and the loss of self-control.” Buddha said: “Hatred is never ended by hatred but by love.” Misunderstanding is never ended by argument but by tact, diplomacy, conciliation, and a sympathetic desire to see the other person’s viewpoint.

Opera tenor Jan Peerce, married nearly fifty years, said: “My wife and I made a pact a long time ago, and we’ve kept it no matter how angry we’ve grown with each other. When one yells, the other should listen — because when two people yell, there is no communication, just noise and bad vibrations.”

Carnegie includes a nine-point framework for handling disagreements: welcome it (“when two partners always agree, one of them is not necessary”), distrust your first instinct, control your temper, listen first, look for areas of agreement, be honest about your own errors, promise to think it over, thank opponents sincerely, and postpone action to give both sides time to think through the problem. In preparation for the next meeting, ask yourself: Could my opponents be right? Is there truth in their position? Will my reaction drive them away or draw them closer? Is this difficult situation an opportunity for me?

Chapter 2: A Sure Way of Making Enemies — And How to Avoid It

Principle: Show respect for the other person’s opinions. Never say “You’re wrong.”

Theodore Roosevelt confessed that being right 75% of the time would reach his highest expectations. Yet people routinely tell others they’re wrong — a direct blow at their intelligence, pride, and self-respect that makes them want to strike back.

Benjamin Franklin’s transformation is the chapter’s centerpiece. A blundering youth whose opinions had “a slap in them for everyone,” a Quaker friend told him: “Ben, you are impossible. Your friends find they enjoy themselves better when you are not around.” Franklin forbade himself words like “certainly” and “undoubtedly,” replacing them with “I conceive” and “it so appears to me at present.” Despite being a poor speaker, he “generally carried my points” and became one of America’s greatest diplomats.

Carnegie warns: never begin by announcing “I am going to prove so-and-so to you.” That’s tantamount to saying: “I’m smarter than you are.” This arouses opposition before you even start. As Alexander Pope wrote: “Men must be taught as if you taught them not / And things unknown proposed as things forgot.” Galileo agreed: “You cannot teach a man anything; you can only help him to find it within himself.”

Carnegie’s drapery story is wonderfully self-aware. He hired a decorator for expensive draperies. When a friend said his decorator had overcharged him, Carnegie immediately defended himself. When a different friend admired the draperies, Carnegie voluntarily admitted: “Well, to tell the truth, I can’t afford them myself. I paid too much. I’m sorry I ordered them.” When handled gently, we may admit mistakes to others and even take pride in our frankness. But not if someone tries to ram the fact down our throats.

A young New York attorney argued Lustgarten v. Fleet Corporation before the U.S. Supreme Court. When a justice said the statute of limitations in admiralty law was six years, the attorney bluntly corrected him. He was technically right — but a hush fell on the court, “the temperature seemed to drop to zero.” He had made the “enormous blunder of telling a very learned and famous man that he was wrong.”

James Harvey Robinson explains in The Mind in the Making: most of our “reasoning” consists of finding arguments for going on believing as we already do. The little word “my” is the most important in human affairs — we resent any imputation that our watch is wrong, our car shabby, or our conception of the canals of Mars is subject to revision. Harold Reinke, a Dodge dealer in Billings, Montana, switched from arguing with hard-boiled customers to saying: “Our dealership has made so many mistakes that I am frequently ashamed. We may have erred in your case. Tell me about it.” The result: disarmed customers, several thanked him for understanding, two brought in friends to buy new cars.

Lumber salesman R.V. Crowley watched a customer’s inspector reject a shipment as below grade. Instead of quoting grading rules, Crowley asked friendly questions in a “very friendly, cooperative spirit” about why certain pieces were rejected. The inspector’s whole attitude changed — he eventually admitted the mistake was on their end, accepted the entire lot, and paid in full. “A little tact, and the determination to refrain from telling the other man he was wrong, saved my company a substantial amount of cash.”

Katherine Allred, an industrial engineering supervisor, tried to change an incentive system by telling management they were wrong. She failed completely. After Carnegie’s course, she asked where they felt problems were, discussed each point, and let them develop her system themselves. “They enthusiastically accepted it.” General Robert E. Lee once spoke glowingly of a bitter enemy. When challenged, Lee replied: “The president asked my opinion of him; he did not ask for his opinion of me.”

Chapter 3: If You’re Wrong, Admit It

Principle: If you are wrong, admit it quickly and emphatically.

Carnegie walked his Boston bulldog Rex off-leash in Forest Park. A mounted policeman confronted him: “What do you mean by letting that dog run loose? Don’t you know it’s against the law?” Carnegie promised to obey — did for a few times, then took a chance again. Same policeman on horseback. This time, instead of waiting for the officer to speak, Carnegie beat him to it: “Officer, you’ve caught me red-handed. I’m guilty. I have no alibis, no excuses. You warned me last week that if I brought the dog out here again without a muzzle you would fine me.” The officer responded in a soft tone: “Well, now, I know it’s a temptation to let a little dog like that have a run out here when nobody is around.” Carnegie: “Sure it’s a temptation, but it is against the law.” The officer’s only option to maintain his own importance was magnanimity: “Well, a little dog like that isn’t going to harm anybody. I’ll tell you what — you let him run over the hill where I can’t see him, and we’ll forget all about it.”

Commercial artist Ferdinand Warren used the same technique on a petulant, scolding art editor — a buyer who “was always delighted to find fault with some little thing.” Warren recently delivered a rush job; the editor phoned immediately, demanded Warren come in, was hostile and gloating. Warren’s response: “If what you say is true, I am at fault and there is absolutely no excuse for my blunder.” Warren criticized his own work so thoroughly that the editor reversed course, defended Warren’s work, took him to lunch, gave him a cheque and another commission. “My eagerness to criticise myself took all the fight out of him.”

Bruce Harvey of Albuquerque incorrectly authorized full wages to an employee on sick leave. When he discovered the error, he walked into his boss’s office and said it was his fault. The boss exploded — blamed the personnel department. Harvey repeated: it was my fault. The boss exploded again about accounting carelessness. Harvey again: it was my fault. Finally the boss looked at him and said: “Okay, it was your fault. Now straighten it out.” The error was corrected quietly, nobody got into trouble, and the boss had more respect for Harvey ever since.

The most powerful example: Robert E. Lee after Pickett’s Charge at Gettysburg. Lee could have found alibis — division commanders had failed, the cavalry hadn’t arrived. Instead, as beaten troops struggled back, he rode out alone: “All this has been my fault. I and I alone have lost this battle.” Few generals in history have shown such courage.

Elbert Hubbard, one of the most original authors who ever stirred up a nation, frequently aroused fierce resentment with his stinging sentences. But he turned enemies into friends with remarkable skill. When an irritated reader attacked him, Hubbard responded: “Come to think it over, I don’t entirely agree with it myself. Not everything I wrote yesterday appeals to me today. I am glad to learn what you think on the subject. The next time you are in the neighbourhood you must visit us and we’ll get this subject threshed out for all time. So here is a handclasp over the miles.”

A Chinese father, estranged from his son for years, struggled with the cultural tradition that elders never apologize first. In Chinese culture the older person cannot take the first step; the father felt young people should have respect for elders. After taking Carnegie’s course, he addressed the class: “Dale Carnegie says, ‘If you are wrong, admit it quickly and emphatically.’ It is too late for me to admit it quickly, but I can admit it emphatically. I wronged my son. He was right in not wanting to see me and to expel me from his life. I may lose face by asking a younger person’s forgiveness, but I was at fault and it is my responsibility to admit this.” The class applauded and gave full support. Next class: the father told how he went to his son’s house, asked for and received forgiveness, and was now embarked on a new relationship with his son, daughter-in-law, and grandchildren he had at last met.

Carnegie’s conclusion: when right, try to win people gently and tactfully to your way of thinking. When wrong — and that will be surprisingly often — admit mistakes quickly and with enthusiasm. An old proverb captures it: “By fighting you never get enough, but by yielding you get more than you expected.”

Chapter 4: Begin in a Friendly Way

Principle: Begin in a friendly way.

Lincoln said: “A drop of honey catches more flies than a gallon of gall.” Aesop told the same story 2,600 years ago: the wind bet the sun it could remove a man’s coat faster. The harder the wind blew, the tighter the man clutched his coat. The sun smiled kindly — and the man willingly removed it. Gentleness and friendliness are always stronger than fury and force.

Daniel Webster never used high-pressure methods. He prefaced arguments with: “It will be for the jury to consider,” or “This may, perhaps, be worth thinking of,” or “Here are some facts that I trust you will not lose sight of.” No attempt to force opinions — this friendly approach helped make him famous.

Engineer O.L. Straub wanted his rent reduced from a landlord other tenants considered impossible. Everyone tried confrontation and failed. Straub greeted the landlord warmly at the door, praised how he ran the building, and expressed a genuine desire to stay another year. The landlord — who had never received such a reception from a tenant — was completely disarmed. He vented about problem tenants (fourteen letters of complaint from one, snoring neighbor complaints), then not only offered a rent reduction without Straub even asking but inquired what decorating he could do. “If I had tried to get the rent reduced by the methods the other tenants were using, I am positive I should have met with the same failure they encountered.”

Dean Woodcock, a Pittsburgh electric company superintendent, saw his crew repairing equipment on a pole — dozens of people watching what looked like a two-person job. A photographer appeared with a camera, ready to capture what looked like wasteful overstaffing. Woodcock approached in a friendly manner, explained this was first-time work and many executives were observing. The photographer put away his camera and thanked Woodcock. A friendly approach saved the company from embarrassing publicity.

Gerald H. Winn of Littleton, New Hampshire, had a flooded basement with over $2,000 in damage from a subdivision owner’s neglected storm drain. Instead of confrontation, Winn talked about the owner’s West Indies vacation first, then casually mentioned the “little” water problem. The owner quickly agreed to pay for all damage and install the drain. “Even though it was the fault of the owner, if I had not begun in a friendly way, there would have been a great deal of difficulty.”

Chapter 5: The Secret of Socrates

Principle: Get the other person saying “yes, yes” immediately.

A “No” response creates physical withdrawal in the entire organism — glandular, nervous, muscular. Saying “Yes” puts the body in a forward-moving, accepting attitude. The Socratic method works on this principle: ask questions opponents must agree with, winning one admission after another until they embrace conclusions they would have denied minutes earlier.

Bank teller James Eberson had a customer who refused to fill out an account form. Instead of ultimatums, Eberson asked: “Wouldn’t you like the bank to transfer money to your next of kin at your death?” “Yes, of course.” One yes-getting question at a time, the customer softened and happily answered everything. “I found that by getting him to say ‘yes, yes’ from the outset, he forgot the issue at stake.”

Westinghouse salesman Joseph Allison tried for thirteen years to sell motors to a difficult customer. The chief engineer finally objected: “Your motors are too hot. I can’t put my hand on them.” Allison agreed one hundred percent and got “yes, yes” responses:

“The NEMA regulations say a motor may run 72 degrees above room temperature. Correct?” — “Yes.” “What’s the temperature of the mill room?” — “About 75 degrees.” “Seventy-five plus seventy-two is 147. Wouldn’t you scald your hand under hot water at 147 degrees?” — “Yes.” “Wouldn’t it be wise to keep your hands off those motors?” — “Well, I guess you’re right.”

Result: a $35,000 order for the ensuing month. “It took me years and cost me countless thousands in lost business before I learned that it is much more profitable to look at things from the other person’s viewpoint and try to get that person saying ‘yes, yes.’”

Eddie Snow of Oakland wanted to rent a bow for his visiting brother. The store clerk didn’t argue. Instead, he asked a series of yes-getting questions: Had Eddie rented before? “Yes.” Probably paid $25-$30? “Yes.” Was he the kind of person who liked to save money? “Yes.” Then explained bow sets for sale at $34.95 — only $4.95 more than a single rental. Eddie’s chain of “yes” responses led to a purchase, and he became a regular customer.

A Chinese proverb five thousand years old: “He who treads softly goes far.”

Chapter 6: The Safety Valve in Handling Complaints

Principle: Let the other person do a great deal of the talking.

Most people trying to win others over talk too much themselves. Let the other person talk — they know more about their business and problems than you do. Ask questions. If you disagree, don’t interrupt. They won’t pay attention while they still have ideas crying for expression.

A textile representative arrived at a crucial conference with severe laryngitis — he could hardly whisper. He faced a room full of decision-makers: a textile engineer, the purchasing agent, the sales director, and the president. He wrote on a pad: “Gentlemen, I have lost my voice. I am speechless.” The president said: “I’ll do the talking for you.” He exhibited the samples, praised their merits, became the representative’s advocate. A lively discussion arose about each sample. Result: a contract for over half a million yards of upholstery fabrics — $1.6 million — the largest single order the company ever received. “I know I would have lost the contract if I hadn’t lost my voice, because I had the wrong idea about the whole proposition.”

Barbara Wilson and her teenage daughter Laurie tried lectures, threats, and punishments — the relationship deteriorated. Finally Wilson asked “Why, Laurie, why?” and just listened. The girl’s thoughts poured out. Wilson realized she had never listened — always talked and ordered. Laurie needed a confidante. The relationship transformed completely.

Charles T. Cubellis landed a Wall Street job by spending hours researching the firm’s founder before his interview. During the meeting, he said: “I should be mighty proud to be associated with an organization with a record like yours.” The founder talked for a long time about his early struggles — starting with $450, working twelve-to-sixteen-hour days, fighting discouragement. Cubellis barely spoke. The vice president later said: “I think this is the person we are looking for.”

La Rochefoucauld warned: “If you want enemies, excel your friends; but if you want friends, let your friends excel you.” When friends excel us, they feel important. When we excel them, they feel inferior and envious. A personnel agency worker named Henrietta initially had no friends among colleagues because she bragged daily about placements and new accounts. After Carnegie’s course, she talked about herself less and listened to associates boast about their achievements. Now she has friends — and they share in her triumphs willingly.

Chapter 7: How to Get Cooperation

Principle: Let the other person feel that the idea is theirs.

Colonel Edward House wielded enormous influence with President Wilson by planting ideas casually: “I learned the best way to convert him to an idea was to plant it in his mind casually, so as to get him thinking about it on his own account.” Days later, Wilson would present the idea as his own at the dinner table. House never claimed credit — he wanted results.

Sketch artist Eugene Wesson called on one buyer for three years without a sale. Then he brought unfinished sketches and asked the buyer to help complete them. All were accepted: “I urged him to give me his ideas. This made him feel that he was creating the designs. And he was. I didn’t have to sell him. He bought.”

An X-ray equipment manufacturer wrote to a Brooklyn hospital: “Our machines are not perfect. We know that, and we want to improve them. Could you find time to look them over and give us your ideas?” The doctor felt important, cancelled a dinner engagement to examine the equipment. The more he studied it, the more he liked it — the idea of buying was entirely his own. Nobody had sold him — he had sold himself.

Paul M. Davis wanted a Civil War battlefields vacation; his wife Nancy wanted a western tour. Their daughter Anne had just completed U.S. history in junior high. Davis asked Anne how she’d like to visit the places she’d learned about. She said she’d love to. Two evenings later, Nancy announced the summer vacation would be eastern states. Nancy thought it was her idea. A great trip for everyone.

Automobile showroom manager Adolph Seltz had discouraged salespeople. He called a meeting and asked them what they expected from him, then asked what he had a right to expect from them. Replies came fast — loyalty, initiative, teamwork. One volunteered fourteen hours a day. They had made a moral bargain with Seltz, and sales increased phenomenally. Emerson captured the principle: “In every work of genius we recognise our own rejected thoughts; they come back to us with a certain alienated majesty.”

Chapter 8: A Formula That Will Work Wonders for You

Principle: Try honestly to see things from the other person’s point of view.

Carnegie initially ordered boys in the park to put out campfires with authority — they obeyed sullenly and probably rebuilt them. His improved approach: “Having a good time, boys? What are you going to cook? I loved building fires when I was a boy. But you know they’re dangerous here. Won’t you please rake the leaves away?” The boys cooperated willingly — they saved face and felt respected.

Sam Douglas criticized his wife’s lawn care for four years — it ruined their evenings. After Carnegie’s course, he joined her in pulling weeds and complimented the lawn’s appearance. Happier life for both. Elizabeth Novak was six weeks late on a car payment and received a nasty phone call. Instead of becoming defensive, she apologized sincerely and looked at it from the creditor’s viewpoint. His tone changed immediately; he volunteered a payment plan without her asking.

Dean Donham of Harvard: “I would rather walk the sidewalk in front of a person’s office for two hours before an interview than step into that office without a perfectly clear idea of what I was going to say and what that person was likely to answer.” If you get only one thing from this entire book — an increased tendency to think always in terms of the other person’s point of view — it may easily prove to be one of the stepping-stones of your career.

Chapter 9: What Everybody Wants

Principle: Be sympathetic with the other person’s ideas and desires.

Carnegie offers a magic formula: “I don’t blame you one iota for feeling as you do. If I were you I would undoubtedly feel just as you do.” You can say this with complete sincerity — because given their body, temperament, and experiences, you would feel the same.

The determinism argument is striking: if you had inherited Al Capone’s body, temperament, mind, environment, and experiences, you would be exactly what he was. Three-fourths of the people you meet are hungering for sympathy. Give it to them and they will love you.

Carnegie made a factual error on a radio broadcast, saying Louisa May Alcott lived in Concord, New Hampshire (actually Massachusetts). A Colonial Dame in Philadelphia sent a scathing letter. Carnegie called, apologized, and sympathized with her viewpoint. Because he sympathized, she began sympathizing with him — hostility transformed into friendliness. He got infinitely more fun out of making her like him than he would have from telling her off.

President Taft handled an ambitious mother who lobbied for weeks to get her son appointed to a position. She secured the aid of Senators and Congressmen and came to see Taft personally. But the position required technical qualifications, and Taft followed the Bureau head’s recommendation and appointed someone else. The woman wrote an accusatory letter. Taft’s strategy: he put the letter in a drawer and waited two days before responding. In his response, he expressed disappointment, explained the technical requirements, and expressed hope her son would accomplish what she hoped in his current position. She apologized for writing as she had. Later, when the appointment fell through, Taft appointed her son instead — and gave a musicale at the White House within two days. The first to greet him were this husband and wife, who had been at death’s door just days before.

Sol Hurok, America’s top impresario for half a century, managed Chaliapin, Isadora Duncan, and Pavlova. His first lesson was the necessity for “sympathy, sympathy, and more sympathy” with their idiosyncrasies. When Chaliapin called at noon claiming his throat was “like raw hamburger,” Hurok rushed over: “What a pity! My poor fellow. Of course you cannot sing. I will cancel the engagement. It will only cost you a couple of thousand dollars, but that is nothing compared to your reputation.” By 7:30 PM, Chaliapin always sang.

Piano teacher Joyce Norris had a student named Babette with exceptionally long fingernails — a serious handicap for playing. She didn’t mention them initially, not wanting to discourage a beginner. After the first lesson: “Babette, you have attractive hands and beautiful fingernails. If you want to play as well as you are capable of, you would be surprised how much quicker and easier it would be if you trimmed your nails shorter.” The following week: nails trimmed — the first time Babette had ever cut them. Norris implied “I sympathise with you — I know it won’t be easy, but it will pay off.”

Jay Mangum needed an eight-hour escalator shutdown at a Tulsa hotel, but the manager would only allow two hours. Instead of arguing, Mangum sympathized: “Rick, I know your hotel is quite busy and you would like to keep the shutdown to a minimum. I understand your concern. However, if we do not do a complete job now, your escalator may suffer more serious damage and cause a much longer shutdown.” The manager agreed to eight hours — won over without rancor.

Chapter 10: An Appeal That Everybody Likes

Principle: Appeal to the nobler motives.

J. Pierpont Morgan observed that a person usually has two reasons for doing something — one that sounds good and a real one. Strategy: appeal to the one that sounds good, because all of us are idealists at heart.

Hamilton Farrell had a tenant threatening to break his lease with four months remaining. Instead of citing the contract, he said: “I’ve sized you up as a man of your word. I’m willing to take a gamble. Think it over. If you still intend to move, I’ll accept your decision as final. For after all, we are either men or monkeys — and the choice usually lies with ourselves!” The tenant concluded the only honorable thing was to stay.

Lord Northcliffe found a newspaper using a picture he didn’t want published. He didn’t say “I don’t like it.” He said: “My mother doesn’t like it.” Rockefeller Jr. told photographers: “You’ve got children yourselves. It’s not good for youngsters to get too much publicity.” Both appealed to nobler motives.

Cyrus H.K. Curtis, a poor boy from Maine who launched the meteoric career of owning The Saturday Evening Post and Ladies’ Home Journal, couldn’t afford to pay contributors standard magazine prices. His solution: appeal to nobler motives. He persuaded Louisa May Alcott to write for him at the flood tide of her fame by offering to send a check for $100 not to her, but to her favorite charity.

Six automobile customers who refused to pay disputed service bills were handled by James Thomas. The credit department’s first approach failed completely: they told customers bluntly they’d come to collect past-due bills, made it plain the company was absolutely right, and intimated they knew more about automobiles than customers ever could. Result: argument. Thomas tried a different approach. He visited each customer, explained he’d come to find out what the company had done or failed to do, listened sympathetically, then said: “I know it is safe for me to ask you to adjust it, just as you would if you were the president of my company. I am going to leave it all up to you. Whatever you say goes.” Five of six gave the company the best of it. Appealing to their self-image as fair people accomplished what threats and arguments could not.

Chapter 11: The Movies Do It. TV Does It. Why Don’t You Do It?

Principle: Dramatise your ideas.

The Philadelphia Evening Bulletin was rumored to have too much advertising and too little news. Solution: they clipped all reading matter from one edition and published it as a book called One Day — 307 pages. Dramatised the fact more vividly than any statistics could.

James Boynton tried to present cold cream market research through tables and statistics — the prospect argued about methodology. Second meeting: he brought thirty-two jars of competitors’ cold cream to the executive’s office, each with a tag itemizing investigation results. No argument, an hour-long friendly discussion. Same facts, but dramatised. “I was presenting the same facts this time. But this time I was using dramatisation, showmanship — and what a difference it made.”

A manufacturer of rat poison gave dealers a window display featuring two live rats. Sales zoomed to five times the normal rate. NCR salesman Jim Yeamans told a grocer “You are literally throwing away pennies every time a customer goes through your line” — then threw a handful of actual pennies on the floor. The grocer immediately ordered new machines.

Joe Fant couldn’t get his five-year-old and three-year-old to pick up toys. He invented a “train”: Joey was the engineer (Captain Casey Jones) on his tricycle, Janet’s wagon was attached as the caboose, and she loaded all the “coal” (toys) every evening. Brother drove her around the room. Room cleaned up without lectures, arguments, or threats.

Mary Catherine Wolf needed a meeting with her impossibly busy boss. Requests through the secretary went nowhere. She wrote a formal letter with a fill-in-the-blank appointment form and put it in his in-basket at 11 AM. She got his completed form back by 2 PM. They met that afternoon, talked over an hour, and resolved all problems. The old-time lover, when proposing, didn’t just use words of love — he went down on his knees to show he meant what he said. Showmanship.

Chapter 12: When Nothing Else Works, Throw Down a Challenge

Principle: Throw down a challenge.

Charles Schwab’s lagging mill couldn’t meet quota despite coaxing, pushing, swearing, and threats. Schwab asked the day shift foreman: “How many heats did your shift make today?” “Six.” Without another word, Schwab picked up a piece of chalk and wrote a big “6” on the factory floor, then walked away.

When the night shift came in, they saw the 6 and asked what it meant. Competition ignited. Next morning, the night shift had rubbed out the 6 and replaced it with a big 7. The day shift came in, saw the 7, and worked furiously. They left a 10. The mill went from lagging behind to becoming the top producer in the plant. Schwab’s insight: “The way to get things done is to stimulate competition. I do not mean in a sordid money-getting way, but in the desire to excel.”

Frederic Herzberg studied thousands of workers and confirmed: the most motivating factor is not money, conditions, or benefits — it is the work itself. What every successful person loves is the game — the chance for self-expression, to prove their worth, to excel. This is what makes foot-races, hog-calling contests, and pie-eating competitions. Harvey Firestone agreed: “I have never found that pay and pay alone would either bring together or hold good people. I think it was the game itself.”

New York Governor Al Smith offered Lewis E. Lawes the warden position at Sing Sing — the most dangerous prison in America, where previous wardens lasted only three weeks. It was a dangerous political appointment. Smith: “Young fellow, I don’t blame you for being scared. It’s a tough spot. It’ll take a big person to go up there and stay.” Lawes accepted the challenge, became the most famous warden of his time. His book 20,000 Years in Sing Sing sold hundreds of thousands of copies and inspired movies. His prison reform work “humanised” criminals.

Without a challenge, Theodore Roosevelt would never have been President. Opposition discovered he was no longer a legal New York resident after returning from Cuba. Thomas Collier Platt threw down the gauntlet: “Is the hero of San Juan Hill a coward?” Roosevelt stayed in the fight — and changed his life and the nation’s future. As the motto of the King’s Guard in ancient Greece declared: “All men have fears, but the brave put down their fears and go forward, sometimes to death, but always to victory.”

Part Four: Be a Leader — How to Change People Without Giving Offence or Arousing Resentment

Chapter 1: If You Must Find Fault, This Is the Way to Begin

Principle: Begin with praise and honest appreciation.

Calvin Coolidge told his secretary: “That is a pretty dress you are wearing. You are a very attractive young woman.” She blushed. Then: “Don’t get stuck up. From now on, I wish you would be a little more careful with your punctuation.” The psychology was superb — easier to hear unpleasant things after genuine praise.

William McKinley had to reject a prominent Republican’s campaign speech — a speech the man thought rivalled Cicero. McKinley didn’t say it was terrible. He said: “My friend, that is a splendid speech, a magnificent speech. No one could have prepared a better one. There are many occasions on which it would be precisely the right thing to say, but is it quite suitable to this particular occasion?” Then he helped rewrite it — the man became an effective campaign speaker.

Lincoln’s letter to General Hooker (April 26, 1863) is one of his most famous documents — the second most famous Lincoln letter after the Mrs. Bixby condolence letter, written during the darkest period of the Civil War when eighteen months of defeats had demoralized the nation, soldiers were deserting, and Republican senators wanted Lincoln out.

Lincoln praised Hooker before criticizing his grave faults. He acknowledged Hooker as brave and skillful, approved his confidence, recognized that ambition within reasonable bounds does good. But: Hooker had thwarted General Burnside and done great wrong to his country and his brother officer. He had said the army and government needed a dictator. “Of course, it was not for this, but in spite of it, that I have given you command. Only successful generals can set up as dictators. What I now ask of you is military success, and I will risk the dictatorship.”

He closed with devastating precision: “Neither you nor Napoleon, if he were alive again, could get any good out of an army while such spirit prevails in it, and now beware of rashness. Beware of rashness, but with energy and sleepless vigilance, go forward and give us victories.”

Dorothy Wrublewski, a branch manager at Fort Monmouth Federal Credit Union, had a young teller trainee who was excellent with customers — accurate, efficient, outgoing — but slow balancing out at end of day. The head teller wanted to fire her. Wrublewski observed the next day, praised her friendliness and accuracy with customers, and only then suggested they review the balancing procedure together. Once the teller realized her manager had confidence in her, she easily followed suggestions and mastered the function.

W.P. Gaw needed overdue deliveries from a bronze subcontractor. Instead of demanding, he praised the man’s unique name (the only person in Brooklyn with it), admired his factory as the cleanest he’d ever seen, praised his inventions, and had lunch together — never mentioning the real purpose. After lunch, the subcontractor volunteered: “You can go back with my promise that your material will be fabricated and shipped, even if other orders have to be delayed.” Beginning with praise is like a dentist using Novocain: the patient still gets drilled, but the pain is killed.

Chapter 2: How to Criticise — and Not Be Hated for It

Principle: Call attention to people’s mistakes indirectly.

Charles Schwab saw employees smoking under a “No Smoking” sign. He walked over, handed each man a cigar: “I’ll appreciate it, boys, if you will smoke these on the outside.” They knew he knew they’d broken the rule — and admired him for saying nothing about it. John Wanamaker saw a customer waiting at an unattended counter while salespeople huddled and talked. He quietly slipped behind the counter and served the customer himself.

Orlando Mayor Carl Langford claimed an “open-door” policy, but citizens were blocked by secretaries. His solution: he removed the door from his office. The aides got the message.

The single most actionable insight in the entire book: change “but” to “and.” “We’re proud of you, Johnnie, but if you worked harder on algebra…” makes the child question whether the praise was real. Replace with “and”: “We’re proud of you, and by continuing these efforts, your algebra can improve too.” Praise is accepted, behavior is guided, no sting.

Lyman Abbott, about to preach from Henry Ward Beecher’s famous pulpit, polished his sermon like Flaubert and read it to his wife. She didn’t say it was terrible — she merely remarked that “it would make an excellent article for the North American Review.” Abbott saw the point, tore up the manuscript, and preached without notes. She had praised it and subtly suggested it wouldn’t work as a speech.

Marge Jacob had construction workers leaving lumber debris in her yard. She and her children picked up and piled it neatly. Next morning she told the foreman: “I’m really pleased with the way the front lawn was left last night; it is nice and clean and does not offend the neighbours.” From then on, workers cleaned up after themselves every day.

Master Sergeant Harley Kaiser needed reserve noncommissioned officers to get regulation haircuts — but reserves are civilians most of the time and resent being told what to do. As an old-time regular-army sergeant he could have yelled and threatened. Instead: “Gentlemen, you are leaders. You will be most effective when you lead by example. You know what the army regulations say about haircuts. I am going to get my hair cut today, although it is still much shorter than some of yours. You look at yourself in the mirror, and if you feel you need a haircut to be a good example, we’ll arrange time for you to visit the post barbershop.” Several looked in the mirror that afternoon and went to the barbershop. No orders given.

Chapter 3: Talk About Your Own Mistakes First

Principle: Talk about your own mistakes before criticising the other person.

Carnegie’s nineteen-year-old niece Josephine, fresh from high school with zero business experience, became his secretary. When Carnegie started to criticize her first mistakes, he stopped himself:

“Just a minute, Dale Carnegie. You are twice as old as Josephine. You have had ten thousand times as much business experience. How can you possibly expect her to have your viewpoint, your judgement, your initiative — mediocre though they may be? And just a minute, Dale, what were you doing at nineteen? Remember the asinine mistakes and blunders you made?”

After honest self-reflection, Josephine’s batting average at nineteen was better than his had been. So his approach: “You have made a mistake, Josephine, but the Lord knows, it’s no worse than many I have made. You were not born with judgement. That comes only with experience, and you are better than I was at your age. I have been guilty of so many stupid, silly things myself, I have very little inclination to criticise you or anyone.” It is not nearly so difficult to listen to a recital of your faults if the person criticizing begins by humbly admitting he, too, is far from impeccable.

E.G. Dillistone, an engineer in Brandon, Manitoba, had a new secretary who made two or three spelling mistakes per page. Simply pointing out errors didn’t improve her proofreading. His approach: he sat with her when a letter with errors arrived and said, “Somehow this word doesn’t look right. It’s one of the words I always have had trouble with. That’s the reason I started this spelling book of mine.” He opened his thumb-index book to the appropriate page. The frequency of errors dropped significantly.

Clarence Zerhusen of Timonium, Maryland, discovered his fifteen-year-old son experimenting with cigarettes. Instead of threats and lectures, he told David about his own addiction — how he’d started at the same age and couldn’t stop despite knowing it was harmful. David decided on his own not to smoke until graduation — and never started. Zerhusen himself quit smoking as a result of the conversation.

Prince von Bulow handled a crisis with Kaiser Wilhelm II — who had made outrageous public statements in England that set the entire continent buzzing with fury — by lavishing praise on the Kaiser’s natural science knowledge while humbly admitting his own ignorance: “I am shamefully ignorant of all branches of natural science, have no notion of chemistry or physics, and am quite incapable of explaining the simplest of natural phenomena. But in compensation, I possess some historical knowledge and perhaps certain qualities useful in politics, especially in diplomacy.” The Kaiser beamed, shook hands repeatedly, and exclaimed: “Haven’t I always told you that we complete one another famously? We should stick together, and we will!” If humility can turn a furious Kaiser into a staunch friend, imagine what it can do in daily life.

Chapter 4: No One Likes to Take Orders

Principle: Ask questions instead of giving direct orders.

A colleague worked three years in Owen D. Young’s office and never heard him give a direct order. Young said: “You might consider this” or “Do you think that would work?” or “Maybe if we phrased it this way it would be better.” This saved people’s pride, gave them a sense of participation, and encouraged cooperation instead of rebellion.

A vocational school student illegally parked his car, blocking the shop entrance. One instructor stormed over in an arrogant tone, threatened to drag the car out with a chain. The student and entire class turned against him for the rest of the term. A friendly question — “Whose car is blocking the driveway?” — would have solved the problem without resentment. People are more likely to accept a decision if they had a part in making it.

Ian Macdonald received an impossible-deadline order at his Johannesburg plant. Instead of pushing workers to speed up, he called a meeting, explained the situation, then asked: “Is there anything we can do to handle this order? Can anyone think of different ways to process it?” Employees generated ideas themselves and approached the work with a “We can do it” attitude. The order was produced and delivered on time.

Chapter 5: Let the Other Person Save Face

Principle: Let the other person save face.

General Electric needed to remove Charles Steinmetz from heading the calculating department — he was a genius but a terrible administrator. They couldn’t offend him. Solution: they gave him the new title “Consulting Engineer of General Electric Company.” Same work he loved, someone else ran the department. Steinmetz was happy.

We ride roughshod over others’ feelings — finding fault, issuing threats, criticizing in front of others — without considering the hurt to their pride. A few minutes’ thought, a considerate word, a genuine understanding of the other person’s attitude would go so far toward alleviating the sting.

Accountant Marshall Granger had to let seasonal employees go after tax season. Old approach: blunt dismissal (“Sit down, Mr. Smith. Season’s over, no more assignments”). Effect: disappointment, feeling “let down,” no love for the firm. New approach: called each person individually, acknowledged specific good work, said: “You’ve got the stuff — going a long way wherever you’re working. This firm believes in you, we’re rooting for you, don’t want you to forget it.” People felt better about being let go and returned willingly when needed.

Anna Mazzone made a serious planning error on her first major project. Her boss’s response: thanked her, said errors were normal on new projects, and expressed confidence that it was lack of experience — not ability — that caused the failure. “I left that meeting with my head up in the air and with the determination that I would never let that boss of mine down again.”

A vice president publicly berated a production supervisor with aggressive questions. The supervisor became evasive; the VP lost his temper and accused him of lying. Working relationship destroyed in minutes. The supervisor — a good worker — left for a competitor months later.

Saint-Exupéry captured it perfectly: “I have no right to say or do anything that diminishes a man in his own eyes. What matters is not what I think of him, but what he thinks of himself. Hurting a man in his dignity is a crime.”

Chapter 6: How to Spur People On to Success

Principle: Praise the slightest improvement and praise every improvement. Be “hearty in your approbation and lavish in your praise.”

Enrico Caruso’s first teacher told him he couldn’t sing — his voice sounded “like the wind in the shutters.” His mother, a poor peasant, praised him, told him she knew he could sing, and went barefoot to save money for lessons. He became the greatest opera singer of his age. Charles Dickens mailed his first manuscript at night so nobody would laugh at him. When one editor praised his work, Dickens wandered the streets with tears of joy. Without that encouragement, he might have spent his life in rat-infested factories.

H.G. Wells was a London boy, clerk in a dry-goods store, up from 5 AM to 2 AM — fourteen hours of despised drudgery. After two years he couldn’t stand it, tramped fifteen miles to talk to his mother, frantic, pleaded, wept, swore he’d kill himself if he had to remain. He wrote a pathetic letter to his old schoolmaster declaring he was heartbroken and no longer wanted to live. The schoolmaster gave him praise, assured him he was intelligent, fitted for finer things, and offered him a job as teacher. That praise changed the future of English literature — Wells went on to write innumerable bestsellers and made over a million dollars.

John Ringelspaugh of Rocky Mount, North Carolina, applied Skinner’s principle to his children. Parents’ communication had become nothing but yelling — children got worse, so did the parents. He decided to praise instead of harp on faults. Hard to find things to praise initially, but he found something each day. Within one to two days the really upsetting behaviors stopped. Other faults disappeared. Children began capitalizing on the praise, going out of their way to do things right — the result of praising the slightest improvement rather than condemning everything wrong.

Keith Roper of Woodland Hills, California, had a new employee struggling at the print shop. The supervisor was upset and considering termination. Roper personally visited the shop, told the young man how pleased he was with his work, pointed out exactly why it was superior and how important his contribution was to the company. Complete turnaround within days. The young man told co-workers about the conversation — that someone in the company appreciated good work. He became a loyal, dedicated worker. Roper didn’t just flatter with “You’re good.” He specifically pointed out how the work was superior — singled out specific accomplishments.

The key: praise must be specific. Don’t say “you’re good.” Point out exactly what they did well and why it matters. Only specific praise feels genuine. As William James wrote: “Compared with what we ought to be, we are only half awake.” One of the powers we habitually fail to use is the magic ability to praise people and inspire them with a realization of their latent possibilities. “Praise is like sunlight to the warm human spirit; we cannot flower and grow without it.”

Chapter 7: Give a Dog a Good Name

Principle: Give the other person a fine reputation to live up to.

If you want to improve a person in a certain respect, act as though that trait were already their outstanding characteristic. Shakespeare: “Assume a virtue, if you have it not.” Give them a fine reputation to live up to and they will make prodigious efforts rather than see you disillusioned. Samuel Vauclain, president of Baldwin Locomotive Works, observed: “The average person can be led readily if you have his or her respect and if you show that you respect that person for some kind of ability.”

When mechanic Bill’s work declined at Henry Henke’s truck dealership, Henke called him into the office: “Bill, you are a fine mechanic. You have been in this line of work for many years. You have done many jobs and gotten many compliments about your good work. Yet lately the time you take to complete jobs has been increasing and your work has not been up to your old standards. Because you have been such an outstanding mechanic in the past, I felt sure you would want to know how I feel about this.” Bill assured the boss the work was not out of his range of ability and promised to improve. He did — he had a reputation to live up to, and he lived up to it.

Georgette Leblanc told her cross-eyed, bandy-legged scullery maid: “Marie, you do not know what treasures are within you.” Marie repeated it to herself, began caring for her appearance, and within two months announced her marriage: “I’m going to be a lady.” A single sentence changed her life.

Mrs. Ruth Hopkins, a fourth-grade teacher, received the school’s most notorious troublemaker — mischievous, fights, fresh to teachers, getting worse each year. His only redeeming quality: he could learn rapidly. Hopkins looked straight at Tommy: “I understand you are a natural leader. I’m going to depend on you to help me make this the best class in the fourth grade this year.” She reinforced this over the first few days by complimenting everything he did. With that reputation to live up to, even a nine-year-old couldn’t let her down — and he didn’t.

Dr. Martin Fitzhugh wrote a note to his charwoman Bridgit: “My dear Bridgit, I see you so seldom, I thought I’d take the time to thank you for the fine job of cleaning you’ve been doing.” Next day: mirror-like desk, shiniest chrome cup holder he’d ever seen. Extra time taken? None. She had been given a reputation to live up to.

Chapter 8: Make the Fault Seem Easy to Correct

Principle: Use encouragement. Make the fault seem easy to correct.

Carnegie’s bachelor friend, age forty, took dancing lessons. His first teacher told him: “You’re all wrong. You’d have to forget everything and begin all over.” He quit. The second teacher praised what was right and minimized errors: “You have a natural sense of rhythm. You really are a natural-born dancer.” He knew he was fourth-rate, but deep down he liked to think maybe she meant it. She gave him hope — and he improved rapidly. Tell someone they’re stupid and doing everything wrong — you destroy every incentive to improve. Use the opposite: be liberal with encouragement, make the thing seem easy, express faith in their ability.

Lowell Thomas gave Carnegie confidence at bridge. Carnegie knew nothing about the game — it was a black mystery. Thomas said: “Dale, it is no trick at all. There is nothing to bridge except memory and judgement. You’ve written articles on memory. Bridge will be a cinch for you. It’s right up your alley.” Almost before he realized it, Carnegie found himself at a bridge table for the first time — all because he was told he had a natural flair and the game was made to seem easy.

Ely Culbertson came to America in 1922 and tried teaching philosophy, selling coal, selling coffee — all failures. He played some bridge but it never occurred to him to make a profession of it. Then he met a bridge teacher named Josephine Dillon, fell in love, and married her. She noticed how carefully he analysed cards and persuaded him he had potential genius at the card table. That encouragement — and that alone — caused him to make bridge his profession. His books were translated into a dozen languages and sold over a million copies.

The most moving example: Clarence Jones’s fifteen-year-old son David, who had been labeled “brain-damaged” and “Frankenstein” after a childhood car accident left a scar on his head. Two years behind his age group, only in seventh grade, couldn’t multiply, added on his fingers, could barely read. School administrators decided he couldn’t function normally.

Jones discovered David loved working on radio and TV sets. He pointed out that David needed math to qualify for technician training. Using flash cards, Jones made a big deal of every card David got right, celebrated each milestone — hugs, dancing jigs. He timed each session with a stopwatch. First night: 52 minutes. Then 48, 45, 44, 41. He promised David that when he could do all cards in 8 minutes with no errors, they’d stop. Within a month: all cards perfect in under 8 minutes.

David made a fantastic discovery: learning was easy and fun. Algebra became easier when you could multiply. He brought home his first B in math. Reading improved. Natural talents in drawing emerged. For a science exhibit, he built a complex model demonstrating the effect of levers — requiring drawing, model-making, and applied mathematics. It won first prize at his school and third prize in all of Cincinnati. The kid who had been told his brains leaked out of the cut on his head never failed to make the honour roll through high school, and was elected to the national honour society.

Chapter 9: Making People Glad to Do What You Want

Principle: Make the other person happy about doing the thing you suggest.

In 1915, Wilson determined to send a personal peace emissary to counsel the warlords of Europe. Secretary of State William Jennings Bryan longed to go — he saw a chance to perform a great service and make his name immortal. But Wilson appointed Colonel Edward House instead. House had the thorny task of breaking the news to Bryan. His approach: he told Bryan that the President thought it unwise for anyone to go officially, and that Bryan’s going “would attract a great deal of attention” and people would wonder why he was there. The implication: Bryan was too important for the job. Bryan was satisfied.

Wilson’s failure with the League of Nations illustrates the principle in reverse. Despite brilliance at making individuals feel important, Wilson refused to include prominent Republicans — Elihu Root, Charles Evans Hughes, Henry Cabot Lodge — at the peace conference. He took unknown men from his own party instead, snubbed the Republicans, wouldn’t let them share ownership of the League. He failed to make the other side happy about cooperating. Result: wrecked his career, ruined his health, shortened his life, kept America out of the League, and altered the course of world history.

Napoleon created the Legion of Honour, distributed 15,000 crosses, made eighteen generals “Marshals of France,” and called his troops the “Grand Army.” Critics said he gave “toys” to war-hardened veterans. Napoleon: “Men are ruled by toys.” Gunter Schmidt in West Germany had an employee who kept mis-tagging prices. Reminders and confrontations failed. He appointed her “Supervisor of Price Tag Posting” for the entire store. The new title transformed her attitude completely. Mrs. Ernest Gent of Scarsdale couldn’t stop boys from destroying her lawn. Nothing worked. She made the worst offender her “detective” in charge of keeping trespassers off. He threatened to brand anyone who trespassed. Problem solved.

Dale Ferrier of Fort Wayne couldn’t get his son to pick up pears from the yard. Instead of confrontation, he made it a deal: one dollar per bushel picked, minus one dollar per pear left behind. His son not only picked every pear but monitored carefully to avoid missing any.

Carnegie’s six guidelines for effective leadership: be sincere (don’t promise what you can’t deliver), know exactly what you want the other person to do, be empathetic (ask what they really want), consider the benefits they’ll receive, match those benefits to their wants, and frame your request to show personal benefit to them. A Shortcut to Distinction

Lowell Thomas’s biographical essay reveals Carnegie’s extraordinary origin story. In January 1935, 2,500 people packed the Hotel Pennsylvania ballroom during the Depression for Carnegie’s course. Over fifteen thousand business people had already been trained. Major corporations — Westinghouse Electric, McGraw-Hill, Brooklyn Union Gas, New York Telephone — conducted the training in their own offices.

Carnegie was born on a Missouri farm, ten miles from the nearest railway — he never saw a streetcar until age twelve. Picked strawberries and cut cockleburs for five cents an hour. The family’s luck was relentless — the ‘102’ River rose and drowned the corn, swept the hay; hogs sickened from cholera; the cattle market collapsed; the bank threatened foreclosure. They sold out and bought a farm near State Teachers’ College at Warrensburg. Carnegie couldn’t afford one dollar a day for board. He stayed on the farm, commuted three miles on horseback daily, milked cows, cut wood, fed hogs, studied Latin by coal-oil lamp until his eyes blurred, and woke at 3 AM to nurse pedigreed Duroc-Jersey pigs — putting piglets in a basket behind the kitchen stove to keep them warm.

Ashamed of his poverty, his tight coat and too-short trousers, he developed an inferiority complex — then found his leverage. No flair for athletics, he decided to win speaking contests instead. Spent months preparing, practiced riding to college, milking cows, mounted a hay bale in the barn and harangued frightened pigeons. Initial defeats made him consider suicide. Then suddenly he began to win — not one contest, but every speaking contest in college. Other students asked him to train them; they won also.

After graduation, he sold correspondence courses to Nebraska and Wyoming ranchers. Despite boundless energy, he couldn’t make a go of it. In Alliance, Nebraska, he threw himself on his hotel bed mid-day and wept in despair. He resolved to go to Omaha — no money for a railroad ticket, so he traveled by freight train, feeding and watering two carloads of wild horses for his passage. He sold bacon, soap, and lard for Armour and Company across the South Dakota Badlands — covered his territory by freight train, stage coach, and horseback, slept in pioneer hotels with muslin-sheet partitions, played poker with Indians, learned money collection. Within two years, he boosted the worst territory (#25) to first place among twenty-nine routes. Armour offered a promotion; he refused.

He tried acting, toured the country, recognized he’d never be a Booth or Barrymore. He sold Packard automobiles — dreadfully unhappy, had to scourge himself to work daily. Then he stumbled into teaching public speaking at YMCA night schools. The YMCA refused his two-dollar-per-night salary — he taught on commission. Within three years they were paying him thirty dollars per night. His course spread to Philadelphia, Baltimore, London, and Paris.

Carnegie reviewed his education and realized public speaking had given him the most of any subject — confidence, courage, poise, and the ability to meet and deal with people in business. More valuable than all other college courses combined. His philosophy: almost anyone can speak acceptably if they have self-confidence and an idea boiling within. The way to develop self-confidence is to do the thing you fear and get a record of successful experiences behind you. His main job was never teaching speaking — that was incidental — it was helping people conquer their fears and develop courage. The course evolved into a striking combination of public speaking, salesmanship, human relations, and applied psychology. Post-graduation loyalty was remarkable: one Philadelphia group of nineteen members met twice monthly for seventeen years. A student commuted weekly from Chicago to New York for the course.

How to Win Friends & Influence People by Dale Carnegie · First published 1937, revised edition · 237 pages

The foundational self-help book on human relations, distilled from Carnegie’s 24 years of teaching adults. Every principle flows from one meta-insight: people are creatures of emotion, not logic, driven by a desperate craving to feel important. The person who satisfies this hunger — through sincere appreciation, genuine interest, and seeing the world through others’ eyes — can accomplish almost anything with people.
