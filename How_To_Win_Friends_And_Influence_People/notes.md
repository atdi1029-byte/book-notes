## Front Matter

First published **1937** with a 5,000-copy print run, the book became an overnight sensation that has never gone out of print. Translated into nearly every written language, it reached over **10 million readers** in 36 languages. Carnegie constantly revised the material until his death in 1955; his wife **Dorothy Carnegie** later updated it with contemporary examples while preserving his "brash, breezy" conversational style.

Carnegie wrote as he spoke: "intensively exuberant, colloquial, conversational." The book functioned as a textbook for his Effective Speaking and Human Relations courses, which are still used today.

Dedicated to **Homer Croy** — "a man who doesn't need to read it." Editorial assistance from **Arthur R. Pell, Ph.D.** and consultant **Dorothy Carnegie**.

### How This Book Was Written — And Why

In the first 35 years of the 20th century, over 200,000 books were published in America — most "deadly dull," many financial failures. The president of a major publishing house admitted the company lost money on 7 of every 8 books after 75 years in business.

Carnegie had conducted educational courses for business professionals since 1912. Initially just public speaking, he gradually realized adults needed **even more** training in getting along with people.

> "I wish a book such as this had been placed in my hands twenty years ago! What a priceless boon it would have been."

A **Carnegie Foundation study** found that even in technical fields like engineering, only **15% of financial success** comes from technical knowledge — **85% comes from skill in human engineering**.

> "The ability to deal with people is as purchasable a commodity as sugar or coffee. And I will pay more for that ability than for any other under the sun." — **John D. Rockefeller**

A University of Chicago and YMCA survey of adults in Meriden, Connecticut ($25,000 budget, two years, 156 questions) revealed that adults' **second biggest interest** after health was understanding and getting along with people. Yet no college in America offered a practical course on human relations.

The world's outstanding authority on adult education confirmed: "I know what those adults want. But the book they need has never been written."

**Carnegie's exhaustive research:**
- Read newspaper columns, magazine articles, family court records, old philosophers, new psychologists
- Hired trained researcher for 1.5 years in libraries
- Read biographies from **Julius Caesar to Thomas Edison**
- Read over **100 biographies of Theodore Roosevelt** alone
- Interviewed: **Marconi, Edison, FDR, James Farley, Owen D. Young, Clark Gable, Mary Pickford, Martin Johnson**

> "This book wasn't written in the usual sense of the word. It grew as a child grows. It grew and developed out of that laboratory, out of the experiences of thousands of adults."

Started as rules on a postcard, grew to a larger card, then a leaflet, then booklets — over **15 years of experiment and research**.

### Transformational Results

**Employer with 314 employees** who had driven, criticized, and condemned workers for years:

> "When I used to walk through my establishment, no one greeted me. My employees actually looked the other way when they saw me approaching. But now they are all my friends and even the janitor calls me by my first name."

- Salespeople sharply increased sales and opened new accounts previously solicited in vain
- An executive at **Philadelphia Gas Works** was saved from demotion and instead promoted
- Spouses reported homes "much happier" since training began
- A sophisticated art dealer couldn't sleep for three nights, shaken by realization of own mistakes
- A Harvard graduate declared he learned **more in 14 weeks** than in **4 years at Harvard**

> "Compared to what we ought to be, we are only half awake. We are making use of only a small part of our physical and mental resources." — **Professor William James of Harvard**

> "The sole purpose of this book is to help you discover, develop and profit by those dormant and unused assets."

> "Education is the ability to meet life's situations." — **Dr. John G. Hibben**, former president of Princeton

> "The great aim of education is not knowledge but action." — **Herbert Spencer**

> "And this is an action book." — Carnegie

### Nine Suggestions for Getting the Most from This Book

1. **Deep desire to learn** — the one indispensable requirement; a vigorous determination to increase ability to deal with people

2. **Read each chapter twice** — first for a bird's-eye view, then reread thoroughly

3. **Stop frequently to reflect** — ask how and when to apply each suggestion

4. **Mark and underscore** — four-star suggestions: underscore every sentence or mark with "****"

5. **Review monthly** — an insurance manager read all contracts every month for 15 years. Carnegie himself had to keep reviewing his own public speaking book to remember what he'd written. "The rapidity with which we forget is astonishing."

6. **Learn by doing** — apply rules at every opportunity; only knowledge that is **used** sticks in your mind

> "If you teach a man anything, he will never learn." — **Bernard Shaw**

Carnegie's honest admission: he wrote the book yet found it difficult to apply everything. Easier to criticize than understand. Easier to find fault than praise. More natural to talk about what you want than what others want.

7. **Gamify learning** — offer a dollar when someone catches you violating a principle

8. **Weekly self-examination** — a Wall Street banker with little formal schooling became one of America's most important financiers. Devoted each Saturday evening to "illuminating process of self-examination and review." Three questions:
   - "What mistakes did I make that time?"
   - "What did I do that was right — and in what way could I have improved my performance?"
   - "What lessons can I learn from that experience?"

Initially made him very unhappy and astonished at his own blunders. Over years, blunders became less frequent.

> "It helped me improve my ability to make decisions — and it aided me enormously in all my contacts with people. I cannot recommend it too highly."

9. **Keep a record of triumphs** — be specific: names, dates, results. Will inspire greater efforts. Fascinating to review years later.

> "Remember that you are not merely trying to acquire information. You are attempting to form new habits. Ah yes, you are attempting a new way of life."

# Part One: Fundamental Techniques in Handling People

## Chapter 1: If You Want to Gather Honey, Don't Kick Over the Beehive

**Principle: Don't criticize, condemn, or complain.**

### The Self-Justification of Criminals

**"Two Gun" Crowley**, notorious NYC killer, surrounded by police with machine guns, wrote while bleeding: "Under my coat is a weary heart, but a kind one — one that would do nobody any harm." At Sing Sing's death house: "This is what I get for defending myself."

**Al Capone** regarded himself as a public benefactor: "I have spent the best years of my life giving people the lighter pleasures, helping them have a good time, and all I get is abuse."

**Dutch Schultz** told a newspaper he was a public benefactor — and believed it.

**Lewis Lawes** (Sing Sing warden): Few criminals regard themselves as bad men — they rationalize and justify their antisocial acts.

> "Ninety-nine times out of a hundred, people don't criticize themselves for anything, no matter how wrong it may be."

### Why Criticism Fails

**Criticism is futile** because it puts people on the defensive and makes them strive to justify themselves. It wounds pride, hurts sense of importance, arouses resentment.

**B.F. Skinner**: Animals rewarded for good behavior learn much faster than animals punished for bad behavior — the same applies to humans.

**Hans Selye**: "As much as we thirst for approval, we dread condemnation."

**George B. Johnston**, safety coordinator, enforced hard hat rules through authority — workers complied then removed hats when he left. Changed approach: asked if hats were uncomfortable, reminded workers pleasantly that hats protected them from injury. Result: increased compliance with no resentment.

### Theodore Roosevelt vs. Taft

Roosevelt left the White House in 1908, supported Taft. After returning, he denounced Taft's conservatism, formed the Bull Moose party, split the Republican vote. Taft and the GOP carried only Vermont and Utah — the most disastrous defeat in Republican history.

Taft said tearfully: "I don't see how I could have done any differently from what I have." Criticism didn't persuade Taft he was wrong — only made him justify himself.

### Lincoln's Evolution on Criticism

**Early career:** As a young lawyer in Springfield, Illinois, Lincoln attacked opponents in published letters. In autumn 1842, he ridiculed vain politician **James Shields** in an anonymous letter in the *Springfield Journal*. Shields challenged Lincoln to a duel — they met on a sandbar in the Mississippi River, nearly fought to death before seconds intervened.

Most lurid personal incident in Lincoln's life. **From that time on, he almost never criticized anybody for anything.**

**Civil War:** Appointed generals who bungled — McClellan, Pope, Burnside, Hooker, Meade — while half the nation condemned them. Lincoln held his peace: "with malice toward none, with charity for all." Favorite quotation: "Judge not, that ye be not judged." To critics of southern people: "Don't criticize them; they are just what we would be under similar circumstances."

### The Meade Letter — Lincoln's Restraint

**Battle of Gettysburg** (July 1863): Lee trapped between swollen Potomac and Union Army. Lincoln ordered Meade to attack immediately — golden opportunity to end war. Meade called a council of war (violating orders), hesitated, procrastinated, refused to attack. Lee escaped.

Lincoln was furious: "Great God! What does this mean? We had them within our grasp, and had only to stretch forth our hands and they were ours."

Lincoln's letter to Meade (never sent — found among papers after death):

> "I do not believe you appreciate the magnitude of the misfortune involved in Lee's escape. He was within our easy grasp, and to have closed upon him would, in connection with our other late successes, have ended the war... Your golden opportunity is gone, and I am distressed immeasurably because of it."

Lincoln's likely reasoning: "Maybe I ought not to be so hasty... if I had been up at Gettysburg, and if I had seen as much blood as Meade has seen during the last week, and if my ears had been pierced with the screams and shrieks of the wounded and dying, maybe I wouldn't be so anxious to attack either."

**Learned by bitter experience that sharp criticisms and rebukes almost invariably end in futility.**

> "When dealing with people, let us remember we are not dealing with creatures of logic. We are dealing with creatures of emotion, creatures bristling with prejudices and motivated by pride and vanity."

### Other Examples

**Mark Twain** wrote stinging letters when angry — "The thing for you is a burial permit. You have only to speak and I will see that you get it" — but his wife secretly lifted them out of the mail. The writing let him blow off steam without sending them.

**Carnegie's personal lesson:** Young Carnegie wrote to famous author **Richard Harding Davis** with a note ending "Dictated but not read" (trying to seem important). Davis returned the letter: "Your bad manners are exceeded only by your bad manners." Carnegie resented it sharply — ten years later, the hurt persisted. Resentment from criticism can rankle across decades.

**Thomas Hardy**: bitter criticism caused him to give up writing fiction forever.
**Thomas Chatterton**: criticism drove the English poet to suicide.
**Benjamin Franklin**: "I will speak ill of no man, and speak all the good I know of everybody."

> "Any fool can criticize, condemn and complain — and most fools do. But it takes character and self-control to be understanding and forgiving."

> "A great man shows his greatness by the way he treats little men." — **Carlyle**

### Bob Hoover's Choice

Famous test pilot returning from air show — both engines stopped at 300 feet. WWII propeller plane fueled with jet fuel instead of gasoline. Managed emergency landing, plane badly damaged but no injuries. Mechanic sick with agony, tears streaming.

Hoover didn't scold. Put arm around mechanic's shoulder: "To show you I'm sure that you'll never do this again, I want you to service my F-51 tomorrow."

### "Father Forgets" — W. Livingston Larned

Classic piece reprinted worldwide. A father reflects on criticizing his young son throughout the day: scolding about face-dabbing, unclean shoes, table manners. That evening, the boy runs in timidly and throws arms around father's neck.

Father's realization: "I was measuring you by the yardstick of my own years."

> "Instead of condemning people, let's try to understand them. Let's try to figure out why they do what they do. That's a lot more profitable and intriguing than criticism; and it breeds sympathy, tolerance and kindness."

> "To know all is to forgive all." — **Dr. Johnson**

## Chapter 2: The Big Secret of Dealing with People

**Principle: Give honest and sincere appreciation.**

### The Only Way to Get Anyone to Do Anything

Only one way to get anybody to do anything: **make the other person want to do it**. You can force cooperation through threats — but these methods have sharply undesirable repercussions. The only way to influence people is by giving them what they want.

**Sigmund Freud**: Everything springs from two motives — the sex urge and the desire to be great.

**John Dewey** (America's most profound philosopher): The deepest urge in human nature is **"the desire to be important."**

**William James**: "The deepest principle in human nature is the craving to be appreciated" — not wish, not desire, not longing, but **craving**.

### The Eight Basic Human Wants

1. Health and preservation of life
2. Food
3. Sleep
4. Money and the things money will buy
5. Life in the hereafter
6. Sexual gratification
7. Well-being of our children
8. **A feeling of importance**

Almost all wants are gratified except one: the desire to be important. This gnawing, unfaltering human hunger distinguishes us from animals. The rare individual who honestly satisfies this heart hunger will hold people in the palm of their hand.

### Historical Drive for Importance

Carnegie's father bred prize Duroc-Jersey hogs and displayed blue ribbons on white muslin when visitors came. **Hogs didn't care about ribbons — but Father did.** Without this urge for importance, civilization would have been impossible.

- **Lincoln**: poor grocery clerk studying law books bought for fifty cents from a barrel
- **Dickens, Christopher Wren, Rockefeller**: all driven by desire for importance
- **George Washington**: wanted to be called "His Mightiness, the President"
- **Columbus**: pleaded for "Admiral of the Ocean and Viceroy of India"
- **Catherine the Great**: refused letters not addressed to "Her Imperial Majesty"
- **Victor Hugo**: aspired to have Paris renamed in his honor
- **Shakespeare**: tried to procure a coat of arms for his family

**Dillinger vs. Rockefeller**: Rockefeller got his feeling of importance by giving money for a hospital in Peking. Dillinger got it as a bandit and killer — "I'm Public Enemy Number One." **The one significant difference: how they got their feeling of importance.**

A psychiatric hospital head physician told Carnegie about a woman whose husband refused to love her. She went insane — in imagination, divorced him, married into English aristocracy. The doctor said: "If I could stretch out my hand and restore her sanity, I wouldn't do it. She's much happier as she is."

**The lesson**: If some people go insane to get their feeling of importance, imagine what miracles honest appreciation can achieve.

### Charles Schwab — First Million-Dollar Salary

**Charles Schwab**: first person in American business paid over $1 million/year (1921, when $50/week was well off). Picked by Andrew Carnegie to be first president of United States Steel at age 38 — not because he knew the most about steel.

> "I consider my ability to arouse enthusiasm among my people the greatest asset I possess, and the way to develop the best that is in a person is by appreciation and encouragement."

> "There is nothing else that so kills the ambitions of a person as criticisms from superiors. I never criticize anyone. I believe in giving a person incentive to work. So I am anxious to praise but loath to find fault. If I like anything, I am hearty in my approbation and lavish in my praise."

**Andrew Carnegie** praised associates publicly and privately. Self-written epitaph: "Here lies one who knew how to get around him men who were cleverer than himself."

### Rockefeller's Appreciation

When partner **Edward T. Bedford** lost $1 million in South America, Rockefeller praised him for saving 60% of the investment: "That's splendid. We don't always do as well as that upstairs."

### The Hunger for Appreciation

Study of **runaway wives**: primary reason was "lack of appreciation." People neglect to express appreciation to spouses after years of marriage.

Self-improvement class story: husband asked to list six things wife could improve. He reversed the exercise — sent six red roses with note: "I love you the way you are." Wife nearly in tears. Other women called it "the most considerate thing I have ever heard."

**Florenz Ziegfeld** (Broadway producer) gained fame "glorifying the American girl." Took "drab little creatures" and made them feel beautiful: raised chorus girl salaries from $30 to $175/week, sent telegrams on opening night, gave American Beauty roses. Made them beautiful through "sheer power of his gallantry and consideration."

**Stevie Morris**: blind Detroit schoolboy whose teacher appreciated his remarkable hearing (compensating for blindness). This appreciation changed his life — became **Stevie Wonder**.

### Appreciation vs. Flattery

**The difference is simple:**
- Appreciation: sincere, from the heart, unselfish, universally admired
- Flattery: insincere, from the teeth, selfish, universally condemned

> "Flattery is telling the other person precisely what he thinks about himself."

**General Obregon's** warning: "Don't be afraid of enemies who attack you. Be afraid of the friends who flatter you."

> "Use what language you will, you can never say anything but what you are." — **Emerson**

We spend 95% of our time thinking about ourselves. When we think of others' good points, no need for cheap flattery.

### Application

Supervisor **Pamela Dunham** (New Fairfield, Connecticut) had a janitor doing poor work, jeered at by employees. Praised him occasionally for good work in front of others. Job improved daily — "honest appreciation got results where criticism and ridicule failed."

> "I shall pass this way but once; any good, therefore, that I can do or any kindness that I can show to any human being, let me do it now. Let me not defer nor neglect it, for I shall not pass this way again."

> "Every man I meet is my superior in some way. In that, I learn of him." — **Emerson**

## Chapter 3: He Who Can Do This Has the Whole World with Him

**Principle: Arouse in the other person an eager want.**

### The Fishing Analogy

> "I often went fishing up in Maine during the summer. Personally I am very fond of strawberries and cream, but I have found that for some strange reason, fish prefer worms."

Don't think about what you want — bait the hook to suit the fish.

**Lloyd George** (Britain's WWI Prime Minister) stayed in power when Wilson, Orlando, and Clemenceau were forgotten. Secret: "learned early in life that it was necessary to bait the hook to suit the fish."

**Talking about what we want is childish and absurd.** You are eternally interested in what you want — no one else is. **Only way to influence people: talk about what THEY want and show them how to get it.**

> "Action springs out of what we fundamentally desire... First, arouse in the other person an eager want. He who can do this has the whole world with him. He who cannot walks a lonely way." — **Harry A. Overstreet**

### Andrew Carnegie's Nephew Trick

Poverty-stricken Scotch lad who started at two cents an hour and gave away $365 million — learned early that the only way to influence people is to talk in terms of what they want.

Sister-in-law worried sick: two boys at Yale who neglected to write home. Carnegie wagered $100 he could get a reply without asking. Wrote chatty letter, mentioned casually in postscript: sending each one a five-dollar bill. **Neglected to enclose the money.** Replies came immediately thanking "Dear Uncle Andrew."

### Stan Novak's Kindergarten Story

Son Tim kicking and screaming about kindergarten. Instead of banishing: "If I were Tim, why would I be excited?" Whole family finger-painted on kitchen table, sang songs, listed fun things. Tim peeping around corner, begging to participate.

**Next morning:** Tim sitting asleep in living room chair "waiting to go to kindergarten. I don't want to be late."

**Before persuading:** pause and ask "How can I make this person want to do it?"

### The Hotel Ballroom Negotiation

Hotel wanted to triple Carnegie's lecture room rent after tickets were printed. Carnegie didn't argue about what he wanted. Drew line: "Advantages" vs. "Disadvantages" for the hotel.

- **Advantage for hotel:** Rent ballroom for dances/conventions at higher rates
- **Disadvantage for hotel:** Lose educated, cultured lecture crowds worth $5,000+ in free newspaper advertising

**Result:** Rent increase only 50% instead of 300% — without Carnegie saying a word about his own desires.

### Henry Ford's Secret

> "If there is any one secret of success, it lies in the ability to get the other person's point of view and see things from that person's angle as well as from your own."

So simple, so obvious. Yet 90% of people ignore it 90% of the time.

### Bad Letters Dissected

Carnegie tears apart a radio company letter: every line says "we desire," "we want," "our accounts." Nobody cares what the company desires — the reader is worried about their own mortgage, garden bugs, blood pressure, missed bus.

**A. Zerega's Sons** wrote to **Edward Vermylen** about late deliveries, describing their own problems first and mentioning the customer's benefit last. Vermylen's reaction: "The whole effect is one of raising a spirit of antagonism rather than of cooperation."

Carnegie rewrote it from the customer's perspective: "Your company has been a good customer for fourteen years... we're eager to give you the speedy service you deserve."

### Barbara Anderson's Bank Letters

Wrote to banks not about what she wanted (a job) but how her ten years of experience could help them meet their goals. **11 of 12 banks invited her for interviews.** She had a choice of offers because she didn't state what she wanted — she wrote how she could help them.

### The Underweight Boy

A father couldn't get his three-year-old to eat. Scolding, nagging: "Mother wants you to eat this." The boy paid as much attention as a fleck of sand on the beach.

Father's realization: no trace of horse sense to expect a three-year-old to react to a thirty-year-old's viewpoint. "What does that boy want?"

The boy was bullied daily by a bigger kid who stole his tricycle. Father explained eating well would make him strong enough to "wallop the daylights out of" the bully. The boy devoured spinach, sauerkraut, and salt mackerel.

### The Bed-Wetting Problem

Same boy's bed-wetting: scolding, spanking, shaming failed. Parents asked: "How can we make this boy want to stop?"

**What he wanted:** pajamas like Daddy (not nightgown like Grandmother), and his own bed.

Mother took him to department store. Salesgirl coached: "Young man, what can I show you?" Boy stood taller. On delivery day he shouted: "Daddy! Come upstairs and see my bed that I bought!"

**Pride was involved.** His bed. His pajamas. He wanted to act like a man. And he did.

### K.T. Dutschmann's Breakfast Solution

Three-year-old daughter refused breakfast cereal. Scolding, pleading, coaxing failed. One morning: put her on chair, let her make the cereal herself.

"Oh, look, Daddy, I am making the cereal this morning." Ate two helpings — she had achieved a **feeling of importance** and found an avenue of self-expression.

> "Self-expression is the dominant necessity of human nature." — **William Winter**

### Not Manipulation

Each party should gain. The Vermylen letter: both sides gained. The bank and Mrs. Anderson: both gained. This is about seeing others' perspectives and creating mutual benefit.

**Michael E. Whidden** (Shell Oil) couldn't get an older manager to clean up his service station. Instead of pleading, he invited the manager to visit the newest Shell station. The manager was so impressed he cleaned up his own station. Both benefited.

> "People who can put themselves in the place of other people, who can understand the workings of their minds, need never worry about what the future has in store for them." — **Owen D. Young**

# Part Two: Six Ways to Make People Like You

## Chapter 1: Do This and You'll Be Welcome Anywhere

**Principle: Become genuinely interested in other people.**

### The Dog's Lesson

Dogs are the greatest winners of friends — genuine affection without ulterior motives. A dog makes a living by giving nothing but love. Carnegie's childhood dog **Tippy** (killed by lightning) taught him friendship by instinct.

You can make more friends in **two months** by becoming interested in other people than in **two years** trying to get others interested in you.

### Self-Centeredness: The Fatal Error

People are not interested in you or me — they are interested in themselves.

**New York Telephone Company study**: the word **"I" was used 3,900 times in 500 telephone conversations.**

Group photographs: whose picture do you look for first?

> "It is the individual who is not interested in his fellow men who has the greatest difficulties in life and provides the greatest injury to others. It is from among such individuals that all human failures spring." — **Alfred Adler**

### Howard Thurston: Genuine Interest

Acknowledged dean of magicians — 40+ years traveling, 60+ million paid admissions, $2 million profit. Success not from superior knowledge — hundreds knew as much about magic.

Two unique factors: master showmanship and **genuine interest in people**. Before every show: "I am grateful because these people come to see me. They make it possible for me to make my living." Never said "there is a bunch of suckers."

> "I love my audience. I love my audience." — **Howard Thurston**

### George Dyke: New Life at 72

Retired from service station at 70. Boredom of retirement led him to talk with accomplished fiddlers, learn their backgrounds. Became known as **"Uncle George, the Fiddle Scraper from Kinzua County"** — creating a new life through sustained interest in other people.

### Theodore Roosevelt's Servants

Even his servants loved him. Valet **James E. Amos** wrote *Theodore Roosevelt, Hero to His Valet*. Roosevelt called servants' families by name, greeted cottage neighbors from afar ("Oo-oo-oo, Annie?").

At White House: greeted all old servants by name, including scullery maids. Asked kitchen maid **Alice** if she still made corn bread, then went to his office eating it to tell President Taft.

Head usher **Ike Hoover** (40 years at White House): "It is the only happy day we had in nearly two years, and not one of us would exchange it for a hundred-dollar bill."

### Edward M. Sykes: The Drugstore Lesson

Sales rep lost Johnson & Johnson account at a Hingham drugstore. Next visit, he **talked to soda clerk and sales clerk first** before approaching the owner. Hours later, the soda fountain boy told the owner Sykes was "one of the few salespeople who bothered to say hello" to store employees. Owner agreed, remained loyal customer.

### Brooklyn Institute Letters

Wanted distinguished authors (**Kathleen Norris, Fannie Hurst, Ida Tarbell, Rupert Hughes**) to lecture. Sent letters signed by 150 students expressing admiration and asking for advice. Authors liked being asked — they traveled to Brooklyn to give a helping hand.

### The Kaiser's Little Boy

After WWI, Kaiser Wilhelm was universally despised. One small boy wrote a sincere letter saying he would always love Wilhelm as his Emperor. The Kaiser invited the boy to visit; the boy came with his mother — and the Kaiser married her.

### C.M. Knaphle: The Chain Store Miracle

Tried for years to sell fuel to a chain-store organization. Instead of selling, he asked the chain-store executive to help with a debate by defending chain stores. The executive talked for **one hour and forty-seven minutes**, eyes glowing, proud of the service he rendered to communities.

At the door: "Please see me again later in the spring. I should like to place an order with you for fuel."

> "I had made more headway in two hours by becoming genuinely interested in him and his problems than I could have made in ten years trying to get him interested in me and my product."

### Charles R. Walters: The Stamp Collector

Needed confidential report from a bank president. Remembered the president's secretary collected stamps for her twelve-year-old son. Sent stamps from the bank's foreign department — letters from seven continents.

The president **radiated smiles and good will**, spent half an hour on stamps, then devoted over an hour giving Walters all needed information — and called in subordinates and telephoned associates for more.

> "We are interested in others when they are interested in us." — **Publilius Syrus** (100 BC)

### Martin Ginsberg: Thanksgiving

Ten years old, welfare ward, major surgery, Thanksgiving Day. Father dead, mother on welfare, couldn't visit. A student nurse heard his sobbing, told him she was lonely too, brought two trays of turkey dinner with all the fixings, and stayed on her own time until almost 11 P.M. playing games.

> "Many Thanksgivings have come and gone since I was ten, but one never passes without me remembering that particular one and my feelings of frustration, fear, loneliness and the warmth and tenderness of the stranger that somehow made it all bearable."

## Chapter 2: A Simple Way to Make a Good First Impression

**Principle: Smile.**

A woman at a New York dinner party spent a fortune on sables, diamonds, and pearls but had done nothing about her face — which radiated sourness and selfishness. The expression on your face is **far more important than the clothes on your back**.

### Charles Schwab's Million-Dollar Smile

Schwab told Carnegie his smile had been worth a million dollars. His personality, charm, and ability to make people like him — and one of the most delightful factors was his **captivating smile**.

Actions speak louder than words. A smile says: "I like you. You make me happy. I am glad to see you." Dogs are a hit because they're so glad to see us. A baby's smile has the same effect.

> "People who smile tend to manage, teach and sell more effectively, and to raise happier children. There's far more information in a smile than a frown. That's why encouragement is a much more effective teaching device than punishment." — **Professor James V. McConnell**

A large New York department store employment manager: would rather hire a sales clerk without grade school education with a pleasant smile than a doctor of philosophy with a somber face.

### The Unseen Smile: Phone Power

The effect of a smile is powerful even when unseen. Telephone companies teach "phone power" — smile when talking on the phone. Your smile comes through in your voice.

**Robert Cryer** was recruiting a Ph.D. candidate who had offers from larger companies. The candidate explained his choice: "Your voice sounded as if you were glad to hear from me... that you really wanted me to be part of your organisation."

### William B. Steinhardt: The Grouch Transformed

New York stockbroker, married 18 years, seldom smiled at wife, spoke barely two dozen words before leaving for business. "One of the worst grouches who ever walked down Broadway."

Decided to try smiling for a week. Next morning: "Bill, you are going to wipe the scowl off that sour puss of yours today." At breakfast: "Good morning, my dear" — wife bewildered, shocked.

Smiled at elevator operator, doorman, subway cashier. Soon everyone smiled back. Complaints became easier at work. Eliminated criticism, gave appreciation instead. His office partner's clerk admitted Steinhardt had been a terrible grouch until recently.

> "These things have literally revolutionized my life. I am a totally different man, a happier man, a richer man, richer in friendships and happiness — the only things that matter much after all."

### William James on Action and Feeling

Action and feeling go together. By regulating our actions (under direct control of the will), we indirectly regulate our feelings.

> "Thus the sovereign voluntary path to cheerfulness, if our cheerfulness be lost, is to sit up cheerfully and to act and speak as if cheerfulness were already there."

### Happiness and Inner Conditions

Happiness doesn't depend on outward conditions — depends on **inner conditions**. Not what you have, who you are, where you are, or what you're doing. It's **what you think about it**.

> "There is nothing either good or bad, but thinking makes it so." — **Shakespeare**

> "Most folks are about as happy as they make up their minds to be." — **Abraham Lincoln**

Carnegie saw crippled boys on canes and crutches at Long Island Railroad station. Man in charge: "When a boy realizes he is going to be a cripple for life, he is shocked at first; but after he gets over the shock, he usually becomes **as happy as normal boys**."

### Senora Maria Gonzalez: The Smile Breakthrough

Worked in a closed-off office in Guadalajara, Mexico, envying others' shared camaraderie. After weeks of looking away shyly, she said: "Maria, you can't expect those women to come to you." Put on brightest smile, said "Hi, how are you today?" to each person. Smiles and hellos returned immediately. Acquaintances developed, some became friendships.

> "A man without a smiling face must not open a shop." — Ancient Chinese proverb

> "It costs nothing, but creates much. It enriches those who receive, without impoverishing those who give."

## Chapter 3: If You Don't Do This, You Are Headed for Trouble

**Principle: Remember that a person's name is to that person the sweetest and most important sound in any language.**

### The Jim Farley Story

Orphaned at 10, father killed by horse accident, family left with few hundred dollars insurance. No high school education — yet received four honorary college degrees by age 46 and became **Postmaster General** and **Democratic National Committee chairman**.

Secret: could call **fifty thousand people** by their first names. Systematically gathered names, family details, business info, political opinions. Filed facts mentally, recalled them a year later.

Before Roosevelt's 1932 campaign: wrote hundreds of letters, traveled twenty states, twelve thousand miles in nineteen days. After returning, sent personalized letters to thousands: "Dear Bill," "Dear Jane," signed "Jim."

> "The average person is more interested in his or her own name than in all the other names on earth put together."

**Sid Levy's** customer **Nicodemus Papadoulos** broke down in tears when Levy pronounced his name correctly: "In fifteen years nobody has ever made the effort to call me by my right name."

### Andrew Carnegie's Name Strategy

Called the **Steel King** — yet knew little about steel manufacturing. Real genius: handling people. By age 10, discovered the importance people place on their own name.

**The rabbit story:** As boy in Scotland, caught mother rabbit with babies but nothing to feed them. Told neighborhood children: pull clover and dandelions, and he'd **name the bunnies in their honor**. Strategy worked perfectly.

**Business applications:** Named **Edgar Thomson Steel Works** after Pennsylvania Railroad president to win rail contracts. Competing with George Pullman, Carnegie proposed a merger and asked what to name it. Pullman immediately suggested "the Pullman Palace Car Company, of course." Deal closed — became industrial history.

While personally in charge, **no strike ever disturbed Carnegie's flaming steel mills**.

### The Power of Names

- **P.T. Barnum** offered grandson **$25,000** to carry the name "Barnum" Seeley
- Libraries, museums, university buildings bear names of major donors
- **Benton Love** (Texas Commerce Bancshares chairman): "The executive who tells me he can't remember names is telling me he can't remember a significant part of his business"

### Remembering Names: Techniques

Most people don't remember names because they don't take time to concentrate, repeat, and fix names in mind.

**Napoleon III's** technique: if he didn't catch a name — "So sorry, I didn't get the name clearly" or "How is it spelled?" Repeated it during conversation, associated with person's features. For important names: wrote on paper (eye + ear impression), then tore it up.

**Franklin D. Roosevelt** took time for everyone. When **W.F. Chamberlain** delivered a special Chrysler car to the White House, FDR called him by name, showed genuine interest, and days later sent an autographed photo and thank-you note.

> "To recall a voter's name is statesmanship. To forget it is oblivion."

> "Good manners are made up of petty sacrifices." — **Emerson**

## Chapter 4: An Easy Way to Become a Good Conversationalist

**Principle: Be a good listener. Encourage others to talk about themselves.**

### The Botanist Story

Distinguished botanist at a New York dinner party. Carnegie listened intently for hours while the man discussed exotic plants, experiments, indoor gardens, potato facts. Carnegie violated etiquette by ignoring everyone else.

Botanist told host Carnegie was "most stimulating" and "most interesting conversationalist."

> "An interesting conversationalist? Why, I had said hardly anything at all."

Carnegie had simply **listened with genuine interest**. That kind of listening is one of the highest compliments.

> "Few human beings are proof against the implied flattery of rapt attention." — **Jack Woodford**

> "There is no mystery about successful business intercourse... Exclusive attention to the person who is speaking to you is very important. Nothing else is so flattering as that." — **Charles W. Eliot**, former Harvard president

**Eliot's** listening: "not mere silence, but a form of activity." Sat erect, hands joined, faced his interlocutor, seemed to hear with eyes and ears. Listened with his mind.

### Retail Failure and Success

**Mrs. Henrietta Douglas** (Chicago) bought a coat at special sale, found tear in lining. Clerk refused to listen: "All sales are final." Department manager intervened, listened attentively, offered to repair, replace, or refund. Listening saved a long-term patron.

### The Chronic Kicker

**New York Telephone Company**: vicious customer cursed, raved, threatened, refused to pay, wrote newspapers, filed complaints, started suits. Company sent a "troubleshooter" who **listened** for nearly three hours across four visits, saying "yes" and sympathizing.

Customer became charter member of the "Telephone Subscribers' Protective Association" — only member besides the troubleshooter. He had wanted a **feeling of importance**, and listening gave it to him.

### Julian F. Detmer: Twenty-Two-Year Friendship

**Detmer Woollen Company** founder: angry customer came to Chicago over a small debt dispute. Detmer listened patiently, didn't interrupt, let customer talk himself out. Thanked him for coming.

Customer returned later, placed the largest order ever, and **named his son Detmer** — remaining a friend and customer for twenty-two years.

### Edward Bok: From Poverty to Publishing

Poor Dutch immigrant boy, washed bakery windows after school. Only six years of schooling but became one of America's most successful magazine editors. His method: wrote to famous people asking about their childhoods.

Wrote **General Garfield** (running for President); Grant drew a map and invited fourteen-year-old Bok to dinner. Soon corresponding with: **Emerson, Holmes, Longfellow, Mrs. Abraham Lincoln, Louisa May Alcott, General Sherman, Jefferson Davis**. Visited many as welcome guest in their homes.

### Lincoln: The Need for a Listener

During the Civil War, Lincoln asked an old friend to come from Springfield to Washington. Talked for hours about the Emancipation Proclamation, went over every argument, read letters and newspaper articles — then sent his friend home **without asking for his opinion**.

He had wanted merely a friendly, sympathetic listener to unburden himself. "He seemed to feel easier after that talk."

### The Recipe for Being Despised

> "Never listen to anyone for long. Talk incessantly about yourself. If you have an idea while the other person is talking, don't wait for him or her to finish: bust right in and interrupt in the middle of a sentence."

People are a hundred times more interested in themselves than in you. A person's toothache means more to them than a famine killing millions. A boil on their neck interests them more than forty earthquakes in Africa.

## Chapter 5: How to Interest People

**Principle: Talk in terms of the other person's interests.**

### Theodore Roosevelt's Preparation

Everyone visiting Roosevelt was astonished at the range of his knowledge. Whether visitor was cowboy, Rough Rider, politician, or diplomat — Roosevelt knew what to say. Secret: whenever he expected a visitor, he sat up **late the night before reading about the subject** his guest treasured most.

The royal road to a person's heart: talk about what they treasure most.

### William Lyon Phelps: The Gentleman's Art

Yale professor, essayist. At age eight, visiting Aunt Libby Linsley, a middle-aged New York lawyer spent the evening talking to young Phelps about boats — a subject the lawyer cared nothing about.

Aunt's explanation: "He is a gentleman. He saw you were interested in boats, and he talked about the things he knew would interest and please you. He made himself agreeable."

> "I never forgot my aunt's remark."

### Edward L. Chalif: The Boy Scout Miracle

Needed president of one of America's largest corporations to fund a Scout trip to Europe. Instead of talking about Scouts, he asked to see the president's famous million-dollar cancelled check (later cancelled and framed). The president gladly showed it. Chalif admired and asked how it came to be drawn.

Result: president funded not one boy but **five boys plus Chalif**, gave a **thousand-dollar letter of credit**, extended the trip to **seven weeks**, gave letters of introduction to European branch presidents who met them in Paris.

> "Yet I know if I hadn't found out what he was interested in, and got him warmed up first, I wouldn't have found him one-tenth as easy to approach."

### Henry G. Duvernoy: Four Years of Failure

Duvernoy and Sons (wholesale baking, New York). Tried to sell bread to a certain hotel for **four years** — called weekly, went to same social affairs, even took rooms in the hotel. Failed.

Discovered the manager belonged to the **Hotel Greeters of America** and was its president nationally and internationally. Talked about the Greeters; the manager talked for half an hour, vibrant with enthusiasm. The Greeters were his hobby, the passion of his life.

Days later, steward called: "I don't know what you did to the old boy, but he sure is sold on you."

> "I had been drumming at that man for four years — trying to get his business — and I'd still be drumming at him if I hadn't finally taken the trouble to find out what he was interested in."

### Edward E. Harriman's Strategy

Wanted job from inaccessible business maverick **R.J. Funkhouser**, who rose from poverty to riches and protected himself with a dedicated secretary. Harriman studied the secretary's interests first, told her he had a proposition that might translate into financial and political success for Funkhouser. She arranged the meeting.

## Chapter 6: How to Make People Like You Instantly

**Principle: Make the other person feel important — and do it sincerely.**

### The Postal Clerk's Hair

Carnegie at the post office, noticed a bored clerk. Found something genuine to admire: "I wish I had your head of hair." The clerk beamed, became pleased, conversation turned warm. Carnegie predicts the man went home happy, told his wife, looked in mirror.

An audience member asked: "What did you want to get out of him?" Carnegie's answer: **Nothing.** If appreciation requires getting something in return, you've failed.

### The Universal Law

**John Dewey**: desire to be important is the deepest urge. **William James**: the deepest craving. This urge differentiates us from animals and made civilization possible.

Philosophers have agreed for thousands of years:
- **Zoroaster** — Persia, 2,500 years ago
- **Confucius** — China, 2,400 years ago
- **Lao-tse** — Valley of the Han
- **Buddha** — Holy Ganges, 500 years before Christ
- **Jesus**: "Do unto others as you would have others do unto you"

### Small Courtesies = Good Breeding

"I'm sorry to trouble you," "Would you be so kind as to —?", "Won't you please?", "Thank you." These phrases oil the monotonous grind of everyday life — hallmark of good breeding.

### Hall Caine's Literary Success

Son of a blacksmith, only eight years of schooling. Wrote to poet **Dante Gabriel Rossetti** praising his work. Rossetti: delighted, invited Caine to London as secretary. Caine met literary artists, gained encouragement, launched career. Died the richest literary man of his time — multimillion-dollar estate at **Greeba Castle**, Isle of Man.

> "Such is the power, the stupendous power, of sincere heart-felt appreciation."

### The Shy Boy Named Chris

Instructor **Ronald J. Rowland** — student Chris: very quiet, shy, lacking self-confidence. Rowland invited him into the advanced class. Chris: "Who me, Mr. Rowland? Am I good enough?" Answer: "Yes, Chris, you are good enough."

Chris walked out "seemingly two inches taller," bright blue eyes, positive voice. Rowland made a sign for his classroom: **"YOU ARE IMPORTANT."**

### The Unvarnished Truth

Almost all people feel superior to you in some way. Sure way to their hearts: **let them realize in a subtle way that you recognize their importance, and do it sincerely.**

> "Every man I meet is my superior in some way. In that, I learn of him." — **Emerson**

### The Funkhouser Job Interview

**Edward E. Harriman** facing inaccessible **R.J. Funkhouser**: "Mr. Funkhouser, I believe I can make money for you." Framed every qualification as a contribution to Funkhouser's success. Hired immediately, worked 20+ years, both prospered.

> "Talking in terms of the other person's interests pays off for both parties."

# Part Three: How to Win People to Your Way of Thinking

## Chapter 1: You Can't Win an Argument

**Principle: The only way to get the best of an argument is to avoid it.**

### The Sir Ross Smith Banquet

London banquet honoring **Sir Ross Smith** (Australian ace, flew halfway around world in 30 days). A man attributed a Shakespeare quotation to the Bible. Carnegie corrected him. Shakespeare scholar **Frank Gammond** kicked Carnegie under the table and sided with the wrong man.

Privately, Gammond admitted: "*Hamlet*, Act Five, Scene Two. But we were guests at a festive occasion. **Why prove to a man he is wrong? Is that going to make him like you?**"

> "Always avoid the acute angle." — **Frank Gammond**

### Carnegie's History as an Arguer

Inveterate arguer in youth — argued with brother about everything "under the Milky Way." Studied logic, debating contests in college, taught debating in New York, planned to write a book on the subject. Listened to thousands of arguments, watched their effects.

**Conclusion**: only one way to get best of argument — **avoid it**. Avoid like rattlesnakes and earthquakes.

**Nine times out of ten**, an argument ends with each contestant more firmly convinced they're right.

You can't win because:
- If you lose, you lose
- If you win, you lose — you make opponent feel inferior, wound their pride, earn their resentment

> "A man convinced against his will / Is of the same opinion still."

### Patrick J. O'Haire — The Truck Salesman

Little education, had been a chauffeur. Selling trucks without success. Continually arguing with prospects who criticized White Motor Company trucks. "Pat saw red and was right at the customer's throat."

Won many arguments: "I often walked out of an office saying: 'I told that bird something.' Sure I had told him something, but I hadn't sold him anything."

**Transformed approach**: If buyer says "White truck is no good" — agree that the competitor makes fine trucks. Then stop arguing. Begin talking about White's good points. No room for argument if you agree. Became star salesman.

> "As I look back now I wonder how I was ever able to sell anything. I lost years of my life in scrapping and arguing. I keep my mouth shut now. It pays."

> "If you argue and rankle and contradict, you may achieve a victory sometimes; but it will be an empty victory because you will never get your opponent's good will." — **Ben Franklin**

Which would you rather have: an academic victory or a person's good will? You can seldom have both.

> "Here lies the body of William Jay, / Who died maintaining his right of way — / He was right, dead right, as he sped along, / But he's just as dead as if he were wrong."

### Frederick S. Parsons — The Tax Inspector

Income tax consultant, disputing with government tax inspector for an hour over $9,000. Inspector: cold, arrogant, stubborn. "Reason was wasted and so were facts." The longer they argued, the more stubborn the inspector became.

Parsons changed tactics: "I suppose this is a very petty matter in comparison with the really important and difficult decisions you're required to make. I've made a study of taxation myself. But I've had to get my knowledge from books. You are getting yours from the firing line of experience."

Inspector straightened up, leaned back, talked for a long time about his work and clever frauds uncovered. Tone became friendly, told about his children. Three days later: decided to leave the tax return exactly as filed.

A traffic inspector's story reinforces this. He tried confrontation with a combative park visitor — the situation escalated. But when another officer acknowledged the visitor's importance and expertise, the man's demeanor shifted entirely. He became sympathetic and cooperative. The lesson: the combative person wanted a **feeling of importance**. Arguing denied him that; acknowledging him granted it.

As long as the inspector argued, the man got his feeling of importance by loudly asserting authority. As soon as his importance was admitted and the argument stopped, he became sympathetic and kindly.

**Lincoln** reprimanded a young army officer for arguing:

> "No man who is resolved to make the most of himself can spare time for personal contention. Still less can he afford to take the consequences, including the vitiation of his temper and the loss of self-control. Yield larger things to which you show no more than equal rights; and yield lesser ones though clearly your own. Better give your path to a dog than be bitten by him in contesting for the right. Even killing the dog would not cure the bite."

**Buddha**: "Hatred is never ended by hatred but by love." Misunderstanding is never ended by argument but by **tact, diplomacy, conciliation, and a sympathetic desire to see the other person's viewpoint**.

**Nine Ways to Handle Disagreement** (from *Bits and Pieces*):

1. **Welcome the disagreement** — "When two partners always agree, one of them is not necessary." Disagreement may prevent a serious mistake.

2. **Distrust your first instinctive impression** — Your first reaction in a disagreeable situation is defensive. Be careful. Keep calm. May be at your worst, not your best.

3. **Control your temper** — You can measure the size of a person by what makes them angry.

4. **Listen first** — Give opponents a chance to talk. Let them finish. Don't resist, defend, or debate. This builds bridges of understanding, not barriers of misunderstanding.

5. **Look for areas of agreement** — When you've heard opponents out, dwell first on the points where you agree.

6. **Be honest** — Look for areas where you can admit error and say so. Disarms opponents, reduces defensiveness.

7. **Promise to think over opponents' ideas and study them carefully** — And mean it. Opponents may be right. Easier to agree to think about their points than to move ahead and find yourself where "you wouldn't listen."

8. **Thank your opponents sincerely for their interest** — Anyone who disagrees is interested in the same things you are. Think of them as wanting to help you; may turn opponents into friends.

9. **Postpone action to give both sides time to think through the problem** — Suggest a new meeting later when all facts may be brought to bear. In preparation, ask yourself:
   - Could opponents be right? Partly right?
   - Is there truth or merit in their position?
   - Is my reaction one that will relieve the problem or just relieve my frustration?
   - Will my reaction drive opponents away or draw them closer?
   - Will my reaction elevate the estimation good people have of me?
   - Will I win or lose? What price will I pay if I win?
   - Is this difficult situation an opportunity for me?

**Jan Peerce** (opera tenor, nearly 50 years married):

> "My wife and I made a pact a long time ago, and we've kept it no matter how angry we've grown with each other. When one yells, the other should listen — because when two people yell, there is no communication, just noise and bad vibrations."

## Chapter 2: A Sure Way of Making Enemies — And How to Avoid It

**Core principle: Show respect for the other person's opinions. Never say, "You're wrong."**

**Theodore Roosevelt** confessed that being right 75% of the time would reach his highest expectations. On Wall Street, being right 55% makes a fortune. Yet people routinely tell others they're wrong — a direct blow at their **intelligence, judgement, pride, and self-respect** that makes them want to strike back, not change their minds.

Carnegie warns: "Never begin by announcing 'I am going to prove so-and-so to you.' That's tantamount to saying: 'I'm smarter than you are.'" This arouses opposition before you even start.

> "Men must be taught as if you taught them not / And things unknown proposed as things forgot." — Alexander Pope

> "You cannot teach a man anything; you can only help him to find it within himself." — Galileo

> "Be wiser than other people if you can; but do not tell them so." — Lord Chesterfield

**The Magic Phrase**: "Well, now, look. I thought otherwise but I may be wrong. I frequently am. And if I am wrong, I want to be put right. Let's examine the facts."

**Harold Reinke** (Dodge dealer, Billings, Montana): Switched from arguing with hard-boiled customers to saying: "Our dealership has made so many mistakes that I am frequently ashamed. We may have erred in your case. Tell me about it." Result: disarmed customers, several thanked him for understanding, two brought in friends to buy cars.

**Supreme Court blunder**: A young New York attorney argued *Lustgarten v. Fleet Corporation* before the U.S. Supreme Court. When a justice said the statute of limitations in admiralty law was six years, the attorney bluntly corrected him: "Your Honour, there is no statute of limitations in admiralty." He was technically right — but "a hush fell on the court... the temperature seemed to drop to zero." He made the "enormous blunder of telling a very learned and famous man that he was wrong."

**James Harvey Robinson** (*The Mind in the Making*): Most of our "reasoning" consists of finding arguments for going on believing as we already do. The little word **"my"** is the most important in human affairs — we resent any imputation that our watch is wrong, our car shabby, or our conception of the canals of Mars is subject to revision.

**Carl Rogers** (*On Becoming a Person*):

> "I have found it of enormous value when I can permit myself to understand the other person... Our first reaction to most statements is an evaluation or judgement, rather than an understanding of it. Very rarely do we permit ourselves to *understand* precisely what the meaning of the statement is to the other person."

**Carnegie's draperies lesson**: He hired a decorator for draperies and was dismayed by the high bill. Friend #1 said: "What? That's awful. He put one over on you." — Carnegie immediately defended himself. Friend #2 admired the draperies and wished she could afford such quality — Carnegie voluntarily admitted: "Well, to tell the truth, I can't afford them myself. I paid too much. I'm sorry I ordered them." When handled gently, we may admit mistakes to others and even take pride in our frankness. But not if someone tries to ram the fact down our throats.

**Benjamin Franklin's transformation**: A blundering youth whose opinions "have a slap in them for everyone," he was told by a Quaker friend: "Ben, you are impossible. Your opinions have a slap in them for everyone who differs with you. They have become so offensive that nobody cares for them. Your friends find they enjoy themselves better when you are not around."

Franklin's rule: **forbear all direct contradiction**. He forbade himself words like "certainly" and "undoubtedly," replacing them with "I conceive," "I apprehend," or "it so appears to me at present."

> "I soon found the advantage of this change... The modest way in which I propos'd my opinions procur'd them a readier reception and less contradiction... For these fifty years past no one has ever heard a dogmatical expression escape me."

Result: despite being a poor speaker, subject to much hesitation in his choice of words, Franklin "generally carried my points" through diplomatic language. He became "one of the most able, suave and diplomatic men in American history."

**Horace Greeley** (Civil War editor): Believed he could drive Lincoln into agreement through argument, ridicule, and abuse. Waged a bitter campaign month after month, year after year. Wrote a brutal, sarcastic personal attack on Lincoln the very night Booth shot him. Result: ridicule and abuse never made Lincoln agree with Greeley once.

**Katherine A. Allred** (industrial engineering supervisor, yarn-processing plant, Kings Mountain, NC): Needed to change the incentive system for operators producing more than 12 yarn varieties.

**First attempt**: Told management in detail how they were wrong, showed where they were being unfair. Result: "I failed miserably! I had become so busy defending my position on the new system that I had left them no opening to graciously admit their problems on the old one."

**Second attempt** (after Carnegie's course): Asked where they felt problems were, discussed each point, asked opinions on the best way to proceed. "With a few low-keyed suggestions, at proper intervals, I let them develop my system themselves." Result: "They enthusiastically accepted it."

> "I am convinced now that nothing good is accomplished and a lot of damage can be done if you tell a person straight out that he or she is wrong. You only succeed in stripping that person of self-dignity and making yourself an unwelcome part of any discussion."

**R.V. Crowley** (lumber salesman, New York): Customer called saying lumber shipment was "entirely unsatisfactory" — 55% below grade, refused to accept. Old approach would have been quoting grading rules, trying to convince the inspector he was wrong. Crowley's key insight: "Lumber inspectors are like baseball umpires. Once they make a decision, they never change it."

New approach: walked to the lumber car being unloaded, asked inspector to continue sorting, watched without objection, asked friendly questions in a "very friendly, cooperative spirit" about why certain pieces were rejected. His only stated reason: "so that we could give his firm exactly what they wanted in future shipments." Inspector's whole attitude changed — he admitted not being experienced on white pine, began asking Crowley questions. Eventually felt guilty rejecting pieces, saw the mistake was on their end for not specifying the grade needed. He went through the entire carload again, accepted the whole lot, and the company received a check in full.

> "In that one instance alone, a little tact, and the determination to refrain from telling the other man he was wrong, saved my company a substantial amount of cash, and it would be hard to place a money value on the good will that was saved."

> "I judge people by their own principles — not by my own." — Martin Luther King Jr.

**General Robert E. Lee** once spoke in glowing terms about an officer under his command. Another officer was astonished: "General, do you not know that the man of whom you speak so highly is one of your bitterest enemies who misses no opportunity to malign you?" Lee replied: **"Yes, but the president asked my opinion of him; he did not ask for his opinion of me."**

> "Agree with thine adversary quickly." — Jesus (2,000 years ago)

**King Akhtoi of Egypt** (4,200 years before Christ): counselled his son, "Be diplomatic. It will help you gain your point."

## Chapter 3: If You're Wrong, Admit It

**Core principle: If you are wrong, admit it quickly and emphatically.**

Carnegie walked his Boston bulldog **Rex** unleashed in Forest Park (rarely met anyone). A mounted policeman confronted him: "What do you mean by letting that dog run loose without a muzzle and leash? Don't you know it's against the law?" Carnegie replied softly that he didn't think Rex would do any harm. The policeman: **"You didn't think! You didn't think! The law doesn't give a tinker's damn about what you think. That dog might kill a squirrel or bite a child."** Threatened: if caught again, "you'll have to tell it to the judge."

Carnegie promised to obey — did for a few times, then took a chance again. Same policeman on horseback. **Instead of waiting for the officer to speak**, Carnegie beat him to it:

> "Officer, you've caught me red-handed. I'm guilty. I have no alibis, no excuses. You warned me last week that if I brought the dog out here again without a muzzle you would fine me."

The policeman responded in a soft tone: "Well, now, I know it's a temptation to let a little dog like that have a run out here when nobody is around." Carnegie: "Sure it's a temptation, but it is against the law." Policeman: "Well, a little dog like that isn't going to harm anybody." Carnegie: "No, but he may kill squirrels." Policeman: "Well now, I think you are taking this a bit too seriously. I'll tell you what you do. You let him run over the hill there where I can't see him — and we'll forget all about it."

**Key insight**: The policeman, being human, wanted a feeling of importance. When Carnegie condemned himself, the only way the officer could nourish his self-esteem was to take a magnanimous, forgiving attitude.

> "If we know we are going to be rebuked anyhow, isn't it far better to beat the other person to it and do it ourselves? Isn't it much easier to listen to self-criticism than to bear condemnation from alien lips?"

**Ferdinand E. Warren** (commercial artist): Used this technique to win over a petulant, scolding art editor — a buyer who "was always delighted to find fault with some little thing." Recently delivered a rush job; the editor phoned immediately, demanded Warren come in, was hostile and gloating.

Warren's response: "Mr. So-and-so, if what you say is true, I am at fault and there is absolutely no excuse for my blunder. I have been doing drawings for you long enough to know better. I'm ashamed of myself."

The editor immediately started defending Warren: "Yes, you're right, but after all, this isn't a serious mistake. It is only—"

Warren interrupted: "Any mistake may be costly and they are all irritating."

The editor tried to break in, but Warren kept criticising himself. "For the first time in my life, I was criticising myself — and I loved it."

Result: the editor protested, praised Warren's work, took him to lunch, gave him a cheque and another commission.

> "My eagerness to criticise myself took all the fight out of him. He ended up by taking me to lunch; and before we parted, he gave me a cheque and another commission."

**Bruce Harvey** (Albuquerque, NM): Incorrectly authorized full wages payment to an employee on sick leave. When he discovered the error, brought it to the employee's attention and explained the correction would reduce the next paycheck by the entire overpayment. The employee pleaded this would cause serious financial hardship — could it be repaid over time? Harvey explained this would require the supervisor's approval, which "would result in a boss-type explosion."

Harvey realized the whole mess was his fault. He walked into the boss's office and told him he'd made a mistake, gave him the complete facts. Boss exploded — it was the personnel department's fault. Harvey repeated: it was MY fault. Boss exploded again about accounting carelessness. Harvey again: it was MY fault. Each time he reiterated that it was his fault. **Finally the boss looked at him and said: "Okay, it was your fault. Now straighten it out."** Error corrected quietly, nobody got into trouble. The boss has had more respect for Harvey ever since.

**Robert E. Lee at Gettysburg**: **General George E. Pickett** himself was picturesque — long auburn hair, wrote ardent love-letters on the battlefield. Troops cheered as he rode off toward Union lines that tragic July afternoon. Pickett's Charge was the most brilliant and picturesque attack in the Western world — but it was doomed. Lee had failed; he could not penetrate the North.

Lee could have found a score of alibis: division commanders had failed him, the cavalry hadn't arrived to support the infantry attack. Instead, as beaten and bloody troops struggled back to Confederate lines, Robert E. Lee rode out to meet them **alone** with a self-condemnation little short of sublime:

> "All this has been my fault. I and I alone have lost this battle."

Few generals in all history have had such courage and character.

> "Any fool can try to defend his or her mistakes — and most fools do — but it raises one above the herd and gives one a feeling of nobility and exultation to admit one's mistakes."

**Chinese father-son reconciliation** (told by Michael Cheung, Hong Kong instructor): Chinese culture presents special problems with this principle. A middle-aged class member had been estranged from his son for many years — the father had been an opium addict (now cured). In Chinese tradition, the older person cannot take the first step. The father felt young people should have respect for elders; he was right not giving in to his desire; the son must come to him.

In an early session, he told the class about grandchildren he'd never seen, how much he desired to be reunited with his son. Classmates (all Chinese) understood his conflict between desire and tradition.

**At the end of the course**, the father again addressed the class: "I have pondered this problem. Dale Carnegie says, 'If you are wrong, admit it quickly and emphatically.' It is too late for me to admit it quickly, but I can admit it emphatically. I wronged my son. He was right in not wanting to see me and to expel me from his life. I may lose face by asking a younger person's forgiveness, but I was at fault and it is my responsibility to admit this."

The class applauded and gave full support. Next class: the father told how he went to his son's house, asked for and received forgiveness, and was now embarked on a new relationship with his son, daughter-in-law, and grandchildren he had at last met.

There is a degree of satisfaction in having the courage to admit errors. Not only does it clear the air of guilt and defensiveness, but it often helps solve the problem created by the error.

**Elbert Hubbard** — one of the most original authors who ever stirred up a nation — frequently aroused fierce resentment with his stinging sentences. But he turned enemies into friends with remarkable skill. Example response to an irritated reader:

> "Come to think it over, I don't entirely agree with it myself. Not everything I wrote yesterday appeals to me today. I am glad to learn what you think on the subject. The next time you are in the neighbourhood you must visit us and we'll get this subject threshed out for all time. So here is a handclasp over the miles."

**Conclusion**: When right, try to win people gently and tactfully to our way of thinking. When wrong — and that will be surprisingly often — admit mistakes quickly and with enthusiasm.

> "By fighting you never get enough, but by yielding you get more than you expected." — Old proverb

## Chapter 4: Begin in a Friendly Way

**Core principle: Begin in a friendly way.**

**Daniel Webster** never used high-pressure methods. He prefaced arguments with: "It will be for the jury to consider," or "This may, perhaps, be worth thinking of," or "Here are some facts that I trust you will not lose sight of." No attempt to force opinions — this friendly approach helped make him famous.

**O.L. Straub** (engineer): Wanted rent reduced from a hard-boiled landlord who other tenants considered impossible. Other tenants tried confrontation and failed. Straub's strategy: greeted the landlord warmly at the door, praised how he ran the building, expressed genuine desire to stay another year. The landlord had never received such a reception from a tenant — was completely disarmed. He then vented about problem tenants (fourteen letters of complaint from one, snoring neighbor complaints). Result: offered a rent reduction without Straub even asking, then asked what decorating he could do.

> "If I had tried to get the rent reduced by the methods the other tenants were using, I am positive I should have met with the same failure they encountered. It was the friendly, sympathetic, appreciative approach that won."

**Aesop's fable**: The wind bet the sun it could remove a man's coat faster. The harder the wind blew, the tighter the man clutched his coat. The sun smiled kindly — the man pulled off his coat. Gentleness and friendliness are always stronger than fury and force.

> "A drop of honey catches more flies than a gallon of gall." — Lincoln

**Dean Woodcock** (Pittsburgh electric company superintendent): His crew was repairing equipment on a pole — dozens of people watching what looked like a two-person job. A photographer appeared with a camera. It looked like wasteful overstaffing. Woodcock approached the photographer in a friendly manner, explained this was first-time work for his department and many executives were interested in observing. Assured him this wasn't normal conditions. The photographer put away his camera and thanked Woodcock. A friendly approach saved the company from embarrassing publicity.

**Gerald H. Winn** (Littleton, NH): Basement flooded with over $2,000 damage from a subdivision owner's neglected storm drain. Instead of confrontation, Winn talked about the owner's West Indies vacation first, then casually mentioned the "little" water problem. Owner quickly agreed to pay for all damage and install the drain. "Even though it was the fault of the owner, if I had not begun in a friendly way, there would have been a great deal of difficulty."

**F. Gale Connor** (Lutherville, MD): Recurring car service problems. Realized reasoning or shouting wouldn't work. Approached the agency owner, praised competitive prices and outstanding reputation friends had told him about. Owner personally involved himself, lent Connor his own car during repairs.

Aesop (Greek slave, 600 BC) taught truths about human nature as valid in Boston and Birmingham today as in ancient Athens.

## Chapter 5: The Secret of Socrates

**Core principle: Get the other person saying "yes, yes" immediately.**

Don't begin by discussing points of disagreement. Emphasize the things you agree on — both striving for the same end, only the method differs.

**Professor Overstreet**: A "No" response creates physical withdrawal — the entire organism (glandular, nervous, muscular) gathers into a condition of rejection. Saying "Yes" puts the organism in a forward-moving, accepting, open attitude. More "yeses" at the outset means more likelihood of capturing attention for the ultimate proposal.

**James Eberson** (bank teller, NYC): Customer refused to fill out account form. Instead of ultimatums, Eberson asked: "Wouldn't you like the bank to transfer money to next of kin at your death?" — "Yes, of course." One yes-getting question at a time, the customer softened and happily answered everything. "I found that by getting him to say 'yes, yes' from the outset, he forgot the issue at stake."

**Joseph Allison** (Westinghouse Electric): Tried to sell motors to a difficult prospect — previous salesmen had called for ten years without selling anything. Allison called steadily for three more years, finally sold a few motors. Expected an order for several hundred more. Then the chief engineer refused: "Your motors are too hot. I can't put my hand on them."

Allison agreed 100% and got "yes, yes" responses:

- "You must have motors that won't run any hotter than standards set by the National Electrical Manufacturers Association. Isn't that so?" — **"Yes"**
- "The NEMA regulations say a properly designed motor may have a temperature of 72 degrees Fahrenheit above room temperature. Is that correct?" — **"Yes, that's quite correct. But your motors are much hotter."**
- "What is the temperature of the mill room?" — **"About 75 degrees"**
- "If the mill room is 75 degrees and you add 72 to that, that makes 147 degrees Fahrenheit. Wouldn't you scald your hand if you held it under hot water at 147 degrees?" — **"Yes"**
- "Wouldn't it be a good idea to keep your hands off those motors?" — **"Well, I guess you're right."**

Result: $35,000 order lined up for the ensuing month.

> "It took me years and cost me countless thousands of dollars in lost business before I finally learned that it doesn't pay to argue, that it is much more profitable to look at things from the other person's viewpoint and try to get that person saying 'yes, yes.'"

**Eddie Snow** (Oakland, CA): Wanted to rent a bow for his visiting brother. Store clerk said they no longer rented bows. Instead of arguing, the clerk asked a series of yes-getting questions: Had Eddie rented before? "Yes." Probably paid $25-$30? "Yes." Was he the kind of person who liked to save money? "Yes." Then explained bow sets for sale at $34.95 — only $4.95 more than a single rental. Eddie's chain of "yes" responses led to a purchase and he became a regular customer.

**The Socratic method**: The "gadfly of Athens" — one of the greatest philosophers who changed the whole course of human thought 2,400 years ago — didn't tell people they were wrong. He asked questions opponents had to agree with, kept winning admissions until they embraced conclusions they would have denied minutes earlier.

> "He who treads softly goes far." — Chinese proverb (5,000 years of studying human nature)

## Chapter 6: The Safety Valve in Handling Complaints

**Core principle: Let the other person do a great deal of the talking.**

Most people trying to win others over talk too much themselves. Let them talk — they know more about their business and problems than you do. Ask questions. If you disagree, don't interrupt. They won't pay attention while they still have ideas crying for expression.

**G.B.R.** (textile manufacturer's representative): Arrived at a crucial conference with severe laryngitis — could hardly whisper. Faced a room full of decision-makers: a textile engineer, the purchasing agent, the sales director, and the president. Wrote on a pad: "Gentlemen, I have lost my voice. I am speechless."

The president said: "I'll do the talking for you." He exhibited G.B.R.'s samples, praised their good points. A lively discussion arose about the merits of each sample — the president acting as G.B.R.'s advocate. Result: contract for over half a million yards of upholstery fabrics ($1,600,000) — the biggest single order the company had ever received.

> "I know I would have lost the contract if I hadn't lost my voice, because I had the wrong idea about the whole proposition. I discovered, quite by accident, how richly it sometimes pays to let the other person do the talking."

**Barbara Wilson** and her teenage daughter Laurie: Lectured, threatened, punished — all efforts failed, the relationship deteriorated. Finally asked "Why, Laurie, why?" — and just listened. The girl's thoughts poured out. Wilson realized she had **never listened** — always talked and ordered. Laurie needed a mother as confidante, an outlet for confusion about growing up. From that point, the relationship transformed completely.

**Charles T. Cubellis** (Wall Street job interview): Spent hours researching the founder before the interview. During: "I should be mighty proud to be associated with an organisation with a record like yours." The founder talked for a long time about early struggles — starting with $450, 12-16 hour days, fighting discouragement. Cubellis barely spoke. The vice president said: "I think this is the person we are looking for."

**Roy G. Bradley** (Sacramento, CA): A discouraged prospect, Richard Pryor, came to Bradley's brokerage office. Bradley mentioned one advantage — being an independent contractor. As Pryor talked about it, he talked himself out of each negative thought. Bradley just listened. Pryor became an outstanding representative.

> "If you want enemies, excel your friends; but if you want friends, let your friends excel you." — La Rochefoucauld

When friends excel us, they feel important. When we excel them, they feel inferior and envious.

**Henrietta G** (Midtown Personnel Agency, NYC): Initially had no friends among colleagues because she bragged daily about placements, new accounts, and accomplishments. They resented her instead of sharing her triumphs. After course suggestions: talked about herself less, listened more to associates. They also had things to boast about and were more excited about telling her their achievements. Now she only mentions her accomplishments when asked.

## Chapter 7: How to Get Cooperation

**Core principle: Let the other person feel that the idea is his or hers.**

We all have more faith in ideas we discover ourselves than in ideas handed to us on a silver platter. No one likes to feel they are being sold something or told to do a thing. We prefer to feel we're buying of our own accord or acting on our own ideas. We like to be consulted about our wishes, wants, thoughts.

**Adolph Seltz** (Philadelphia automobile showroom manager): Faced discouraged, disorganized salespeople. Called a meeting and urged them to tell him what they expected from him. Then: "I'll give you all these qualities you expect from me. Now I want you to tell me what I have a right to expect from you." Replies came fast: loyalty, honesty, initiative, optimism, teamwork, eight hours a day of enthusiastic work. One salesperson volunteered to work fourteen hours a day. The people had made a moral bargain with Seltz — consulting them about their wishes and desires was the key. Sales increase was phenomenal.

**Eugene Wesson** (sketch artist): Called on one NYC stylist once a week for three years — 150 failures. New approach: brought unfinished sketches and asked the buyer for help finishing them. Left the sketches, returned for suggestions, finished them according to the buyer's ideas. All were accepted, plus scores of additional commissions.

> "I realised why I had failed for years to sell him. I had urged him to buy what I thought he ought to have. Then I changed my approach completely. I urged him to give me his ideas. This made him feel that he was creating the designs. And he was. I didn't have to sell him. He bought."

**Paul M. Davis** (Tulsa, OK) and the family vacation: Davis wanted to visit Civil War battlefields and Independence Hall. His wife Nancy wanted a western tour — New Mexico, Arizona, California. Their daughter Anne had just completed a U.S. history course in junior high. Davis asked Anne how she'd like to visit the places she'd learned about. She said she'd love to. Two evenings later, Nancy announced the summer vacation would be eastern states. Nancy thought it was her idea. A great trip for everyone.

**Colonel Edward M. House** wielded enormous influence while Wilson occupied the White House. Wilson leaned on House for secret counsel more than on his own cabinet members.

House's method (revealed to Arthur D. Howden Smith, quoted in *The Saturday Evening Post*):

> "After I got to know the President, I learned the best way to convert him to an idea was to plant it in his mind casually, but so as to interest him in it — so as to get him thinking about it on his own account. The first time this worked it was an accident. I had been visiting him at the White House and urged a policy on him which he appeared to disapprove. But several days later, at the dinner table, I was amazed to hear him trot out my suggestion as his own."

House didn't interrupt or claim credit — he was too adroit for that. He wanted **results**, so he let Wilson feel the idea was his. He gave Wilson public credit for his ideas.

**Ralph Waldo Emerson** (*Self-Reliance*): "In every work of genius we recognise our own rejected thoughts; they come back to us with a certain alienated majesty."

**X-ray equipment sale**: A hospital in Brooklyn was building the finest X-ray department in America. Dr. L was overwhelmed by sales representatives praising their own equipment. One manufacturer took a different approach and wrote: "Our machines are not perfect. We know that, and we want to improve them. Could you find time to look them over and give us your ideas?" Dr. L felt important, cancelled a dinner engagement to examine the equipment. The more he studied it, the more he liked it — the idea of buying was his own.

**New Brunswick fishing trip**: Carnegie was overwhelmed by scores of letters from camps. One owner did something clever: sent names and phone numbers of previous guests. Carnegie called one he knew, got a firsthand account, and wired his reservation. Others tried to sell him — that one let him sell himself.

> "The reason why rivers and seas receive the homage of a hundred mountain streams is that they keep below them. Thus they are able to reign over all the mountain streams." — Lao-tse

## Chapter 8: A Formula That Will Work Wonders for You

**Core principle: Try honestly to see things from the other person's point of view.**

There is always a reason why someone thinks and acts as they do. Ferret out that hidden reason and you have the key to their actions, perhaps their personality. Ask: "How would I feel, how would I react if I were in his shoes?"

> "Success in dealing with people depends on a sympathetic grasp of the other person's viewpoint." — Kenneth M. Goode, *How to Turn People Into Gold*

**Sam Douglas** and the lawn: Douglas criticized his wife for working too hard on the lawn — it never looked good enough despite her efforts over four years. Each criticism ruined the evening. After Carnegie's course, he realized she enjoyed the work and might appreciate compliments instead. He joined her in pulling weeds, complimented how fine the lawn looked. Result: happier life for both.

**Dr. Gerald S. Nirenberg** (*Getting Through to People*):

> "Cooperativeness in conversation is achieved when you show that you consider the other person's ideas and feelings as important as your own."

Carnegie and the park fires: Initially ordered boys to put out fires with authority — they obeyed sullenly with resentment, probably rebuilt fires and wanted to burn the whole park. Improved approach: "Having a good time, boys? What are you going to cook? I loved to build fires myself when I was a boy. But you know they are dangerous here... Won't you please rake all the leaves away right now — and cover it with dirt before you leave?" Boys cooperated willingly — **they saved face and felt respected**.

**Elizabeth Novak** and the car payment: Six weeks late, received a nasty phone call. Instead of becoming defensive, she looked at it from the creditor's viewpoint. Apologized sincerely, remarked she must be his most troublesome customer. His tone changed immediately — he shared stories of truly rude customers, then volunteered a payment plan without her even asking.

> "I would rather walk the sidewalk in front of a person's office for two hours before an interview than step into that office without a perfectly clear idea of what I was going to say and what that person was likely to answer." — Dean Donham, Harvard

If you get only one thing from this entire book — an increased tendency to think always in terms of the other person's point of view — it may easily prove to be one of the stepping-stones of your career.

## Chapter 9: What Everybody Wants

**Core principle: Be sympathetic with the other person's ideas and desires.**

**The magic formula**: "I don't blame you one iota for feeling as you do. If I were you I would undoubtedly feel just as you do." You can say this with 100% sincerity. Three-fourths of the people you meet are hungering and thirsting for sympathy. Give it to them, and they will love you.

The determinism argument: if you had inherited Al Capone's body, temperament, mind, environment, and experiences, you would be exactly what he was. People who come to you irritated, bigoted, unreasoning deserve very little discredit for being what they are. "There, but for the grace of God, go I."

Carnegie's **Louisa May Alcott mistake**: On a broadcast, he mistakenly said Alcott lived in Concord, New Hampshire (actually Massachusetts). A Colonial Dame in Philadelphia sent a scathing letter. Carnegie called, apologized, sympathized with her viewpoint. Because he sympathized with her, she began sympathizing with him — hostility transformed into friendliness. He got infinitely more fun out of making her like him than he would have from telling her off.

**President Taft and the ambitious mother**: She lobbied for weeks to get her son appointed to a position. Secured the aid of Senators and Congressmen, came to see Taft personally. The position required technical qualification — Taft followed the Bureau head's recommendation and appointed someone else.

The mother wrote an accusatory letter. **Taft's strategy**: put the letter in a drawer and waited two days before responding. In his response: expressed disappointment but explained the technical qualifications requirement, expressed hope her son would accomplish what she hoped in his current position. She apologized for writing as she had.

**Follow-up crisis**: The appointment was not confirmed by the Senate. The woman was bedridden with serious stomach cancer, apparently worsened by disappointment. Taft wrote to the husband sympathizing, hoping the diagnosis was inaccurate, expressing hope that withdrawing his first choice and appointing her son would restore her health. He gave a musicale at the White House within two days — and the first to greet him were this husband and wife, who had been *in articulo mortis* (at death's door) just days before.

**Jay Mangum** and the hotel escalator: Mangum's elevator-escalator maintenance company had a contract for a Tulsa hotel. The hotel manager wouldn't allow a shutdown longer than 2 hours. The repair needed at least 8. Instead of arguing, Mangum sympathized: "Rick, I know your hotel is quite busy and you would like to keep the escalator shutdown time to a minimum. I understand your concern about this. However, if we do not do a complete job now, your escalator may suffer more serious damage and cause a much longer shutdown." The manager agreed 8 hours was more desirable — won to Mangum's way of thinking without rancor.

**Joyce Norris** and the long fingernails: Her student Babette had exceptionally long fingernails — a serious handicap for piano. Norris didn't mention them initially, not wanting to discourage. After the first lesson: "Babette, you have attractive hands and beautiful fingernails. If you want to play as well as you are capable of, you would be surprised how much quicker and easier it would be if you trimmed your nails shorter." Following week: nails trimmed — the first time Babette had ever cut them. Norris implied "I sympathise with you — I know it won't be easy, but it will pay off."

**Sol Hurok** (America's top impresario for half a century): Handled Chaliapin, Isadora Duncan, Pavlova. First lesson: the necessity for **sympathy, sympathy, and more sympathy** with their idiosyncrasies. When Chaliapin called at noon saying his throat was "like raw hamburger," Hurok rushed to his hotel: "What a pity! My poor fellow. Of course, you cannot sing. I will cancel the engagement. It will only cost you a couple of thousand dollars, but that is nothing compared to your reputation." By 7:30 PM, Chaliapin always consented to sing.

> "Sympathy the human species universally craves. The child eagerly displays his injury; or even inflicts a cut or bruise in order to reap abundant sympathy. 'Self-pity' for misfortunes real or imaginary is practically a universal practice." — Dr. Arthur I. Gates

## Chapter 10: An Appeal That Everybody Likes

**Core principle: Appeal to the nobler motives.**

**J. Pierpont Morgan**: A person usually has two reasons for doing something — one that sounds good and a real one. The person will think of the real reason on their own. But all of us, being **idealists at heart**, like to think of motives that sound good. Strategy: appeal to the noble motive.

**Hamilton J. Farrell** and the disgruntled tenant: A tenant of the Farrell-Mitchell Company threatened to move. The lease had four months to run; the tenant served notice he was vacating immediately. These were expensive winter months — the apartment would be difficult to re-rent before fall.

The traditional approach would be to point out the lease terms and threaten to collect the full balance. Farrell chose differently:

"Mr. Doe, I have listened to your story, and I still don't believe you intend to move. Years in the renting business have taught me something about human nature, and I sized you up in the first place as being a man of your word. In fact, I'm so sure of it that I'm willing to take a gamble."

"Here's my proposition. Lay your decision on the table for a few days and think it over. If you come back between now and the first of the month and tell me you still intend to move, I will accept your decision as final. But I still believe you're a man of your word and will live up to your contract. For after all, we are either men or monkeys — and the choice usually lies with ourselves!"

The tenant came back when the new month arrived, paid his rent, and stayed. He had concluded the only honorable thing to do was live up to the lease.

**Lord Northcliffe** found a newspaper using a picture he didn't want published. Didn't say "*I* don't like it." Instead: "My mother doesn't like it." Appealed to the nobler motive of respect for motherhood.

**Rockefeller Jr.** wanted photographers to stop snapping his children. Didn't say "I don't want their pictures published." Said: "You know how it is, boys. You've got children yourselves. It's not good for youngsters to get too much publicity."

**Cyrus H.K. Curtis** (poor boy from Maine): Starting his meteoric career owning *The Saturday Evening Post* and *Ladies' Home Journal*, he couldn't afford to pay contributors standard magazine prices. Solution: appealed to their nobler motives. Persuaded **Louisa May Alcott** to write for him at the flood tide of her fame by offering to send a check for $100 not to her, but to her favorite charity.

**Automobile bill collection** (James L. Thomas): Six customers refused to pay service bills. Each claimed some charge was wrong (though all had signed for the work). Credit department's first approach failed completely:
1. Told customer bluntly they came to collect a long past-due bill
2. Made it plain the company was absolutely right, customer absolutely wrong
3. Intimated the company knew more about automobiles than the customer could ever hope to know
4. Result: They argued.

**Thomas's new approach**: Visited each customer, explained he'd come to find out what the company had done or failed to do. Made clear that until hearing the customer's story, he had no opinion to offer. Told the customer he was the authority on his own car. Let the customer talk, listened with interest and sympathy. Then appealed to nobler motives:

"First, I want you to know I also feel this matter has been badly mishandled. You've been inconvenienced and annoyed by one of our representatives. That should never have happened. I'm sorry. As I sat here and listened to your side of the story, I could not help being impressed by your fairness and patience. Because you are fair-minded and patient, I am going to ask you to do something for me. Here is your bill; I know it is safe for me to ask you to adjust it, just as you would do if you were the president of my company. I am going to leave it all up to you. Whatever you say goes."

Result: One customer refused to pay a penny of the disputed charge; **five of six gave the company the best of it**. All six paid within two years. The company delivered new cars to all six customers.

> "Experience has taught me that when no information can be secured about the customer, the only sound basis is to assume that he or she is sincere, honest, truthful and willing to pay the charges, once convinced they are correct." — James L. Thomas

## Chapter 11: The Movies Do It. TV Does It. Why Don't You Do It?

**Core principle: Dramatise your ideas.**

Merely stating the truth isn't enough — it must be made **vivid, interesting, dramatic**. Movies and television do it. You must too.

**Philadelphia Evening Bulletin** crisis: Malicious rumour that the paper had too much advertising and too little news. Solution: clipped all reading matter from one regular edition, published it as a book called *One Day* — 307 pages of content. Dramatised the fact more vividly than any statistics could.

**James B. Boynton** and the cold cream report: Had to present a lengthy market report for a leading cold cream brand. The firm had finished an exhaustive study, and the data was needed immediately. The prospective client was one of the biggest, most formidable men in advertising.

**First approach failed**: Boynton got sidetracked into a futile discussion of investigation methods. Argued back and forth about who was right. Won the point to his own satisfaction, but time was up, the interview was over — hadn't produced results.

**Second meeting**: Instead of tabulations and data, brought **32 jars** of competitors' cold cream to the executive's office while he was on the phone. Each jar had a tag itemizing trade investigation results — the tags told the story briefly and dramatically. No argument, a friendly conversation developed, an hour-long discussion (versus 10 minutes the first time).

> "I was presenting the same facts this time that I had presented previously. But this time I was using dramatisation, showmanship — and what a difference it made."

A manufacturer of rat poison gave dealers a window display featuring **two live rats**. The week they were shown, sales zoomed to five times the normal rate. Dramatisation in action.

**Jim Yeamans** (NCR salesman, Richmond, VA): Told neighborhood grocer "You are literally throwing away pennies every time a customer goes through your line" — then **threw a handful of pennies on the floor**. The grocer quickly became attentive. Got an order to replace all old machines.

**Joe B. Fant Jr.** and the children's train: Had difficulty getting his 5-year-old boy and 3-year-old daughter to pick up toys. Solution: invented a "train." Joey was the engineer (Captain Casey Jones) on his tricycle. Janet's wagon was attached as the caboose — she loaded all the "coal" (toys) every evening. Brother drove her around the room. Room cleaned up without lectures, arguments, or threats.

The old-time lover, when proposing, didn't just use words of love — he went down on his knees to show he meant what he said. Showmanship.

**Mary Catherine Wolf**: Needed a meeting with her impossibly busy boss. Requests through the secretary went nowhere. She wrote a formal letter with a fill-in-the-blank appointment form. Put it in his in-basket at 11 AM; got his completed form back by 2 PM. Met that afternoon, talked over an hour, resolved all problems.

## Chapter 12: When Nothing Else Works, Throw Down a Challenge

**Core principle: Throw down a challenge.**

**Charles Schwab's mill productivity story**: The mill manager couldn't get his workers to meet quota despite coaxing, pushing, swearing, and threatening to fire them. The mill was lagging behind. Schwab went to the day shift foreman and asked simply: "How many heats did your shift make today?" Answer: "Six."

Without another word, Schwab picked up a piece of chalk and **wrote a big "6" on the factory floor**, then walked away.

When the night shift came in, they saw the 6 and asked what it meant. The day shift had made six heats. Competition ignited. Next morning, the night shift had rubbed out the 6 and replaced it with a big **7**. The day shift came in, saw the 7, and worked furiously. They left a **10**. The mill went from lagging behind to becoming **the top producer in the plant**.

> "The way to get things done is to stimulate competition. I do not mean in a sordid money-getting way, but in the desire to excel." — Schwab

The challenge appeals to people of spirit. Without one, **Theodore Roosevelt** would never have been President. Opposition discovered he was no longer a legal New York resident after Cuba. Thomas Collier Platt threw down the gauntlet: "Is the hero of San Juan Hill a coward?" Roosevelt stayed in the fight — changed his life and the nation's future.

> "All men have fears, but the brave put down their fears and go forward, sometimes to death, but always to victory." — Motto of the King's Guard in ancient Greece

**Lewis E. Lawes** and Sing Sing: New York Governor Al Smith offered Lawes the warden position at the most dangerous prison — previous wardens lasted only three weeks. It was a dangerous political appointment. Smith: "Young fellow, I don't blame you for being scared. It's a tough spot. It'll take a big person to go up there and stay." Lawes accepted the challenge, became the most famous warden of his time. His book *20,000 Years in Sing Sing* sold hundreds of thousands of copies. His prison reform work "humanised" criminals and inspired movies.

**Frederic Herzberg** (behavioural scientist): Studied the work attitudes of thousands of people — from factory workers to senior executives. What he found:

- The most motivating factor is **not** money, good working conditions, or fringe benefits
- It's **the work itself** — if the work is exciting and interesting, the worker looks forward to it and is motivated to do a good job
- What every successful person loves: **the game** — the chance for self-expression, the chance to prove his or her worth, to excel, to win
- This is what makes foot-races, hog-calling contests, and pie-eating contests
- **The desire to excel. The desire for a feeling of importance.**

> "I have never found that pay and pay alone would either bring together or hold good people. I think it was the game itself." — Harvey S. Firestone

## Chapter 1: If You Must Find Fault, This Is the Way to Begin

**Core principle: Begin with praise and honest appreciation.**

**Calvin Coolidge** complimented his secretary: "That is a pretty dress you are wearing this morning, and you are a very attractive young woman." She blushed. Then: "Now, don't get stuck up. I just said that to make you feel good. From now on, I wish you would be a little more careful with your punctuation." Obvious perhaps, but the **psychology was superb** — easier to listen to unpleasant things after hearing praise.

**William McKinley** (1896 campaign): A prominent Republican read him a campaign speech he thought rivalled Cicero. McKinley couldn't use it as written. Instead: "My friend, that is a splendid speech, a magnificent speech. No one could have prepared a better one. There are many occasions on which it would be precisely the right thing to say, but is it quite suitable to this particular occasion?" Then helped him rewrite it — the man became an effective speaker.

**Lincoln's letter to General Hooker** (April 26, 1863) — the second most famous Lincoln letter after the Mrs. Bixby condolence letter. Written during the darkest Civil War period — 18 months of defeats, the nation demoralized, soldiers deserting, Republican senators wanting Lincoln out.

Lincoln **praised Hooker before criticising his grave faults**:

> "I have placed you at the head of the Army of the Potomac. Of course, I have done this upon what appears to me to be sufficient reasons, and yet I think it best for you to know that there are some things in regard to which I am not quite satisfied with you."

Lincoln acknowledged Hooker as brave and skillful, approved his confidence, recognized that ambition within reasonable bounds does good. **But**: Hooker had thwarted General Burnside and done great wrong to his country and his brother officer. He had said the army and government needed a dictator — "Of course, it was not for this, but in spite of it, that I have given you command. Only successful generals can set up as dictators. What I now ask of you is military success, and I will risk the dictatorship."

> "Neither you nor Napoleon, if he were alive again, could get any good out of an army while such spirit prevails in it, and now beware of rashness. Beware of rashness, but with energy and sleepless vigilance, go forward and give us victories."

**Dorothy Wrublewski** (branch manager, Fort Monmouth Federal Credit Union): A young teller trainee was excellent with customers — accurate, efficient, outgoing — but slow balancing out at end of day. The head teller wanted to fire her. Wrublewski observed the next day, then praised her friendliness, accuracy, and outgoing manner with customers. Only then suggested they review the balancing procedure together. Once the teller realized the manager had confidence in her, she easily followed suggestions and mastered the function.

**W.P. Gaw** and the bronze subcontractor: Instead of demanding overdue deliveries, Gaw praised the man's unique name (only person in Brooklyn with it), admired his factory as the cleanest he'd seen, praised his inventions. Had lunch together — **not a word about the real purpose**. After lunch, the subcontractor volunteered: "You can go back with my promise that your material will be fabricated and shipped, even if other orders have to be delayed."

> Beginning with praise is like a **dentist using Novocain** — the patient still gets drilling, but the Novocain is pain-killing.

## Chapter 2: How to Criticise — and Not Be Hated for It

**Core principle: Call attention to people's mistakes indirectly.**

**Charles Schwab** saw employees smoking under a "No Smoking" sign. Didn't point to the sign or say "Can't you read?" Walked over, handed each man a cigar: "I'll appreciate it, boys, if you will smoke these on the outside." They knew he knew they'd broken a rule — and admired him for saying nothing about it.

**John Wanamaker**: Toured his Philadelphia store, saw a customer waiting at a counter while salespeople huddled at the far end laughing and talking. Didn't say a word — quietly slipped behind the counter, served the customer himself, and handed the purchase to the salespeople to wrap.

**Mayor Carl Langford** (Orlando, FL): Claimed an "open-door" policy, but citizens were blocked by secretaries and administrators. Solution: **removed the door from his office**. The aides got the message — truly open administration ever since.

**The power of one word**: Changing "but" to "and" = difference between failure and success. "We're really proud of you, Johnnie, for raising your grades, *but* if you had worked harder on algebra..." makes Johnnie question the sincerity of the praise. Replace with: "We're proud of you, Johnnie, *and* by continuing the same efforts next term, your algebra grade can be up with all the others." Praise accepted, behavior guided, no sting.

**Lyman Abbott** and Henry Ward Beecher's pulpit: Abbott polished his sermon like Flaubert, read it to his wife. She didn't say it was terrible — merely remarked "it would make an excellent article for the *North American Review*." Abbott saw the point, tore up the manuscript, and preached without notes. She praised it **and** subtly suggested it wouldn't work as a speech.

**Marge Jacob** (construction cleanup): Workers left lumber debris in her yard. She and her children picked up and piled it neatly. Next morning she told the foreman: "I'm really pleased with the way the front lawn was left last night; it is nice and clean and does not offend the neighbours." From then on, workers cleaned up after themselves every day.

**Master Sergeant Harley Kaiser** (542nd USAR School): Needed reserve noncommissioned officers to get regulation haircuts — but reserves are civilians most of the time and resent being told what to do. As an old-time regular-army sergeant he could have yelled and threatened. Instead:

> "Gentlemen, you are leaders. You will be most effective when you lead by example. You must be the example for your men to follow. You know what the army regulations say about haircuts. I am going to get my hair cut today, although it is still much shorter than some of yours. You look at yourself in the mirror, and if you feel you need a haircut to be a good example, we'll arrange time for you to visit the post barbershop."

Result: several looked in the mirror, went to the barbershop that afternoon, and received regulation haircuts. No orders given.

## Chapter 3: Talk About Your Own Mistakes First

**Core principle: Talk about your own mistakes before criticising the other person.**

Carnegie and his niece **Josephine** (19, fresh high school grad, zero business experience): She became one of the most proficient secretaries west of Suez, but initially was "susceptible to improvement." When Carnegie started to criticise her, he stopped himself:

"Just a minute, Dale Carnegie; just a minute. You are twice as old as Josephine. You have had ten thousand times as much business experience. How can you possibly expect her to have your viewpoint, your judgement, your initiative — mediocre though they may be? And just a minute, Dale, what were you doing at nineteen? Remember the asinine mistakes and blunders you made?"

After honest self-reflection: Josephine's batting average at 19 was better than his had been. So when calling attention to her mistakes: "You have made a mistake, Josephine, but the Lord knows, it's no worse than many I have made. You were not born with judgement. That comes only with experience, and you are better than I was at your age. I have been guilty of so many stupid, silly things myself, I have very little inclination to criticise you or anyone. But don't you think it would have been wiser if you had done so and so?"

> Not nearly so difficult to listen to a recital of your faults if the person criticising begins by humbly admitting he, too, is far from impeccable.

**E.G. Dillistone** (engineer, Brandon, Manitoba): New secretary made 2-3 spelling mistakes per page. Merely pointing out errors didn't improve her proofreading. His approach: sat with her when a letter with errors arrived and said, "Somehow this word doesn't look right. It's one of the words I always have had trouble with. That's the reason I started this spelling book of mine." Opened his thumb-index book to the appropriate page. "I'm very conscious of my spelling now because people do judge us by our letters and misspellings make us look less professional." Result: frequency of errors significantly reduced.

**Prince von Bulow and Kaiser Wilhelm II** (1909): The Kaiser made outrageous public statements in England — he alone saved England from Russia and France, his campaign plan enabled Lord Roberts to defeat the Boers. Entire continent buzzed with fury. The Kaiser panicked, asked von Bulow to take the blame. Von Bulow refused, the Kaiser exploded: "You consider me a donkey!" Von Bulow knew he should have praised before condemning — but it was too late. He did the next best thing: praised after criticising.

> "I'm far from suggesting that. Your Majesty surpasses me in many respects; not only, of course, in naval and military knowledge, but above all, in natural science. I have often listened in admiration when Your Majesty explained the barometer, or wireless telegraphy, or the Roentgen rays. I am shamefully ignorant of all branches of natural science, have no notion of chemistry or physics, and am quite incapable of explaining the simplest of natural phenomena. But in compensation, I possess some historical knowledge and perhaps certain qualities useful in politics, especially in diplomacy."

The Kaiser beamed. Von Bulow had praised him, exalted him, and humbled himself. The Kaiser could forgive anything after that. Shook hands several times, later exclaimed: "Haven't I always told you that we complete one another famously? We should stick together, and we will!"

> If a few sentences humbling oneself and praising another can turn a haughty, insulted Kaiser into a staunch friend, imagine what humility and praise can do in daily contacts.

**Clarence Zerhusen** (Timonium, MD): Discovered 15-year-old son experimenting with cigarettes. Instead of threats, told David about his own addiction — started at the same age, couldn't stop. David decided not to smoke until graduation, and never started. Zerhusen himself quit smoking as a result of the conversation.

## Chapter 4: No One Likes to Take Orders

**Core principle: Ask questions instead of giving direct orders.**

**Owen D. Young**: A colleague worked three years in the same office and never heard him give a direct order. He used: "You might consider this," "Do you think that would work?" "Maybe if we phrased it this way it would be better." Saved pride, encouraged cooperation instead of rebellion. Let people learn from mistakes.

**Dan Santarelli** (vocational school teacher, Wyoming): A student illegally parked his car, blocking the entrance to the shop area. An instructor stormed over in an arrogant tone, threatened to wrap a chain around the car and drag it out. The student's response: resentment. The entire class turned against the instructor and made his life difficult for the rest of the term.

Alternative approach: a friendly question — "Whose car is blocking the driveway?" — followed by a suggestion about other cars needing to get in and out. The student would have gladly moved the car, no resentment.

Asking questions makes orders more palatable. People are more likely to accept a decision if they had a part in making it.

**Ian Macdonald** (Johannesburg manufacturing plant): Received a large special order with an impossible deadline. Instead of pushing workers and insisting they speed up, he called a meeting. Explained the situation, then asked questions:
- "Is there anything we can do to handle this order?"
- "Can anyone think of different ways to process it through the shop?"
- "Is there any way to adjust our hours or personnel assignments?"

**Employees generated ideas themselves**, approached the work with a "We can do it" attitude. The order was accepted, produced, and delivered on time.

## Chapter 5: Let the Other Person Save Face

**Core principle: Let the other person save face.**

**Charles Steinmetz** at General Electric: A genius in electricity but a failure as head of the calculating department. GE needed to remove him from that role but didn't dare offend him — he was indispensable and highly sensitive. Their solution: gave him a new title — **Consulting Engineer of General Electric Company**. Same work he loved, but someone else heads the department. Steinmetz was happy. GE maneuvered their most temperamental star without a storm — by **letting him save face**.

We ride roughshod over others' feelings — finding fault, issuing threats, criticising in front of others — without considering the hurt to their pride. A few minutes' thought, a considerate word, a genuine understanding of the other person's attitude would go so far toward alleviating the sting.

**Marshall A. Granger** (certified public accountant): Seasonal business — must let people go after tax season. Old approach: blunt dismissal ("Sit down, Mr. Smith. Season's over, no more assignments, you understood employment was temporary"). Effect: disappointment, feeling "let down," no love for the firm. New approach: called each person individually, acknowledged specific good work, said: "You've got the stuff — going a long way wherever you're working. This firm believes in you, we're rooting for you, don't want you to forget it." Result: people felt better about being let go, returned willingly when needed.

**Fred Clark** (Harrisburg, PA): A vice president publicly berated a production supervisor with aggressive questions. The supervisor became evasive to avoid embarrassment, the VP lost his temper, accused him of lying. **Working relationship destroyed** in a few brief moments. The supervisor — a good worker — left the firm months later and went to a competitor.

**Anna Mazzone** (marketing specialist, food packer): Made a serious planning error on her first major test. Had to redo the entire test. No time to discuss with her boss before the meeting. Terrified of public humiliation, resolved not to cry despite expecting the men to say women can't handle management.

Her boss's response: thanked her, remarked that errors are normal on new projects, expressed confidence that it was lack of experience — not lack of ability — that caused the failure. Assured her (in front of colleagues) that he had faith in her.

> "I left that meeting with my head up in the air and with the determination that I would never let that boss of mine down again."

> "I have no right to say or do anything that diminishes a man in his own eyes. What matters is not what I think of him, but what he thinks of himself. Hurting a man in his dignity is a crime." — Antoine de Saint-Exupery

## Chapter 6: How to Spur People On to Success

**Core principle: Praise the slightest improvement and praise every improvement. Be "hearty in your approbation and lavish in your praise."**

Animal trainers have used this principle for centuries. **Pete Barlow** (dog trainer): the moment a dog showed the slightest improvement, he patted, praised, gave meat, and made a big to-do. Why not use the same technique with people?

**Enrico Caruso** (opera singer): A boy working in a Naples factory who longed to sing. His first teacher discouraged him: "You can't sing. You haven't any voice at all. It sounds like the wind in the shutters." His mother — a poor peasant — praised him, told him she knew he could sing, went barefoot to save money for his music lessons. That mother's praise and encouragement changed his life. He became the greatest opera singer of his age.

**Charles Dickens**: Early 19th-century London. Everything against him — four years of school, father in jail for debts, knew the pangs of hunger. Job pasting labels in a rat-infested warehouse, slept in a dismal attic with two boys from the slums. So little confidence in his ability to write that he mailed his first manuscript at night so nobody would laugh at him. Story after story was refused. Finally, one was accepted. He wasn't paid a shilling, but **one editor had praised him**. That recognition thrilled him so that he wandered the streets with tears rolling down his cheeks. That encouragement changed his whole life — without it, he might have spent his entire life in rat-infested factories.

**H.G. Wells**: London boy, clerk in a dry-goods store, 5 AM to 2 AM (fourteen hours), despised it. After two years couldn't stand it, tramped fifteen miles to talk to his mother. Frantic, pleaded, wept, swore he'd kill himself if he had to remain. Wrote a pathetic letter to his old schoolmaster declaring he was heartbroken and no longer wanted to live. The **schoolmaster gave him praise**, assured him he was intelligent, fitted for finer things, and offered him a job as teacher. That praise changed the future of English literature — Wells went on to write innumerable bestsellers and made over a million dollars.

**B.F. Skinner**: When criticism is minimized and praise emphasized, good things are reinforced and poor things atrophy for lack of attention.

**John Ringelspaugh** (Rocky Mount, NC): Applied Skinner's principle to his children. Parents' communication had become nothing but yelling — children got worse, so did parents. Decided to **praise instead of harping on faults**. Hard to find things to praise initially, but found something each day. Within 1-2 days the really upsetting behaviors stopped, other faults disappeared. Children began capitalizing on praise, going out of their way to do things right. Result of praising the slightest improvement rather than condemning everything wrong.

**Keith Roper** (Woodland Hills, CA): A new employee was struggling. The supervisor was upset and considering termination. Roper personally visited the print shop, told the young man how pleased he was with his work. Pointed out **exactly why** it was superior and how important his contribution was to the company.

Result: complete turnaround in attitude within days. The young man told co-workers about the conversation — that someone in the company appreciated good work. He became a loyal, dedicated worker.

Roper didn't just flatter with "You're good." He **specifically pointed out how the work was superior** — singled out specific accomplishments. Praise must be **specific and sincere** to be meaningful. Everyone wants to be praised, but only specific praise feels genuine.

> "Compared with what we ought to be, we are only half awake. We are making use of only a small part of our physical and mental resources. Stating the thing broadly, the human individual thus lives far within his limits. He possesses powers of various sorts which he habitually fails to use." — William James

You possess powers you habitually fail to use. One of those powers is the **magic ability to praise people and inspire them with a realization of their latent possibilities**. Abilities wither under criticism; they blossom under encouragement.

> "Praise is like sunlight to the warm human spirit; we cannot flower and grow without it." — Jess Lair

## Chapter 7: Give a Dog a Good Name

**Core principle: Give the other person a fine reputation to live up to.**

If you want to improve a person in a certain respect, **act as though that trait were already their outstanding characteristic**. Shakespeare: "Assume a virtue, if you have it not." Give them a fine reputation to live up to and they will make prodigious efforts rather than see you disillusioned.

**Samuel Vauclain** (president of Baldwin Locomotive Works):

> "The average person can be led readily if you have his or her respect and if you show that you respect that person for some kind of ability."

When a good worker begins doing shoddy work, firing or berating doesn't solve the problem — it just causes resentment.

**Henry Henke** (truck dealership, Lowell, IN): A mechanic's work had become unsatisfactory. Instead of bawling him out or threatening him, Henke called him into the office for a heart-to-heart talk:

"Bill, you are a fine mechanic. You have been in this line of work for many years. You have done many jobs and gotten many compliments about your good work. Yet lately the time you take to complete jobs has been increasing and your work has not been up to your old standards. Because you have been such an outstanding mechanic in the past, I felt sure you would want to know how I feel about this, and perhaps jointly we could find some way to correct the problem."

Bill replied he hadn't realized he'd been falling down, assured the boss the work was not out of his range of ability, and promised to improve. He did — he became a fast, thorough mechanic again. He had a **reputation to live up to**, and he lived up to it.

**Georgette Leblanc** (*Souvenirs, My Life with Maeterlinck*): The story of a humble Belgian Cinderella. The servant girl "Marie the Dishwasher" was a scullery assistant — cross-eyed, bandy-legged, poor in flesh and spirit.

One day, while Marie was holding a macaroni plate, Leblanc said point-blank: **"Marie, you do not know what treasures are within you."**

Marie waited some moments, put the dish on the table, and sighed: "Madame, I would never have believed it." She didn't doubt, didn't ask a question. She went back to the kitchen and **repeated what was said** to herself. A force of faith took hold — no one made fun of her, she was given consideration.

The most curious change was in Marie herself: she began taking care of her face and body so carefully that youth seemed to bloom and modestly hide her plainness. Two months later, she announced her marriage to the nephew of the chef: **"I'm going to be a lady"** — and thanked Leblanc. A small phrase had changed her entire life.

**Mrs. Ruth Hopkins** (fourth-grade teacher, Brooklyn, NY): The school's most notorious "bad boy," Tommy T., was assigned to her class. His third-grade teacher had constantly complained to colleagues, principal, and anyone who'd listen. Mischievous, discipline problems, fights, teased girls, fresh to the teacher — getting worse with each year. Only redeeming feature: **ability to learn rapidly and master school work easily**.

Mrs. Hopkins greeted new students with little personal comments. When she came to Tommy, she looked straight into his eyes: **"Tommy, I understand you are a natural leader. I'm going to depend on you to help me make this class the best class in the fourth grade this year."**

She reinforced this over the first few days by complimenting everything he did, commenting on how it showed what a good student he was. **With that reputation to live up to, even a nine-year-old couldn't let her down — and he didn't.**

**Bill Parker** (food company sales, Daytona Beach, FL): Manager of a large independent food market turned down his new product line. Bill brooded all day, returned that evening: "Jack, since this morning I realized I hadn't given you the entire picture. I would appreciate some of your time to tell you about the points I omitted." Then: **"I have respected the fact that you are always willing to listen and are big enough to change your mind when the facts warrant a change."** With that reputation to live up to, Jack couldn't refuse another hearing.

**Dr. Martin Fitzhugh** (dentist, Dublin): Patient pointed out a dirty cup holder. Wrote his charwoman **Bridgit** a note: "My dear Bridgit, I see you so seldom, I thought I'd take the time to thank you for the fine job of cleaning you've been doing. By the way, since two hours twice a week is a very limited amount of time, please feel free to work an extra half hour from time to time if you feel you need to do those 'once-in-a-while' things like polishing the cup holders." Next day: mirror-like desk, shiniest chrome cup holder he'd ever seen. Extra time taken? **None at all.** She had been given a fine reputation to live up to.

## Chapter 8: Make the Fault Seem Easy to Correct

**Core principle: Use encouragement. Make the fault seem easy to correct.**

Carnegie's bachelor friend (age 40) took dancing lessons. First teacher: "You're all wrong. You'd have to forget everything and begin all over." He quit. Second teacher praised what was right, minimized errors: "You have a natural sense of rhythm. You really are a natural-born dancer." He knew he was fourth-rate, but deep down liked to think maybe she meant it. **She gave him hope and he improved.**

> Tell a child, spouse, or employee they're stupid and doing everything wrong — you've destroyed almost every incentive to improve. Use the opposite: be liberal with encouragement, make the thing seem easy, express faith in their ability.

**Lowell Thomas** (superb artist in human relations): Gave Carnegie confidence, inspired him with courage and faith. Carnegie was invited to sit in a friendly bridge game Saturday night — he knew nothing about bridge, it was a black mystery. Thomas said: "Dale, it is no trick at all. There is nothing to bridge except memory and judgement. You've written articles on memory. Bridge will be a cinch for you. **It's right up your alley.**"

Almost before he realized it, Carnegie found himself at a bridge table for the first time — all because he was told he had a natural flair and the game was made to seem easy.

**Ely Culbertson** (bridge expert): Came to America in 1922, tried teaching philosophy and sociology — couldn't. Tried selling coal — failed. Tried selling coffee — failed. Played some bridge but it never occurred to him he'd make a profession of it. Poor card player, very stubborn, asked so many questions and held post-mortem examinations that no one wanted to play with him.

Then he met a pretty bridge teacher named **Josephine Dillon**, fell in love, married her. She noticed how carefully he analysed cards and persuaded him he had **potential genius at the card table**. That encouragement — and that alone — caused him to make a profession of bridge. His books were translated into a dozen languages and sold over a million copies.

**Clarence M. Jones's son David** (Cincinnati, OH): In 1970, 15-year-old David came to live with his father after a rough life. Head cut open in a car accident at age 2, parents divorced at 4, spent most school years in Dallas in special classes for slow learners. Possibly because of the scar, school administrators decided he was brain-injured and couldn't function normally. Two years behind his age group, only in seventh grade, didn't know multiplication tables, added on fingers, could barely read.

**One positive point**: David loved working on radio and TV sets, wanted to be a technician. His father encouraged this but pointed out he needed math to qualify for training.

Jones obtained four sets of flash cards — multiplication, division, addition, subtraction. Put correct answers in a discard pile; missed cards went into a repeat pile until none were left. **Made a big deal out of each card David got right**, particularly cards missed previously. Timed each session with a stopwatch.

First night: 52 minutes. Second night: 48. Then 45, 44, 41, then under 40. Father and mother celebrated each milestone — hugs, dancing jigs. He promised David that when he could get all cards correct in 8 minutes with no errors, they'd quit doing them every night. It seemed impossible. But by the end of the month: all cards perfect in under 8 minutes.

When David made small improvements, his father would ask him to do it again — **capitalizing on the momentum of success**. David made a fantastic discovery: **learning was easy and fun**. Naturally, algebra became easier when you can multiply. He brought home his first B in math (never happened before). Then other changes came with unbelievable rapidity — reading improved, natural talents in drawing emerged.

For a school science exhibit, David chose a highly complex series of models demonstrating the effect of levers — requiring skill in drawing, model making, and applied mathematics. It took first prize in the school science fair, entered the city competition, and won **third prize for all of Cincinnati**.

The kid who had flunked two grades, been called "brain-damaged" and "Frankenstein," told his brains had leaked out of the cut on his head — suddenly discovered he could really learn and accomplish things. From that quarter through high school, he **never failed to make the honour roll** and was elected to the national honour society.

> "Once he found learning was easy, his whole life changed."

## Chapter 9: Making People Glad to Do What You Want

**Core principle: Make the other person happy about doing the thing you suggest.**

Back in 1915, America was aghast as Europe slaughtered each other on an unprecedented scale. Wilson determined to send a personal representative — a peace emissary — to counsel the warlords of Europe.

**William Jennings Bryan** (secretary of state, peace advocate) longed to go. He saw a chance to perform a great service and make his name immortal. But Wilson appointed his intimate friend and adviser, **Colonel Edward M. House**.

House had the thorny task of breaking the unwelcome news to Bryan. Bryan was "distinctly disappointed" — he had planned to do this himself. House's approach: told Bryan the President thought it unwise for anyone to go officially, and that Bryan's going "would attract a great deal of attention" and people would wonder why he was there. The implication: Bryan was **too important** for the job. Bryan was satisfied.

**Colonel House**, adroit and experienced in the ways of the world, was following an important rule of human relations: **always make the other person happy about doing the thing you suggest**.

**Wilson also made McAdoo** feel honored when inviting him to the cabinet — made it seem like McAdoo would be doing Wilson a favor by accepting, not the other way around.

**Wilson's failure with the League of Nations**: Despite his skill at making individuals feel important, Wilson failed catastrophically on the world stage. He refused to include prominent Republicans (**Elihu Root, Charles Evans Hughes, Henry Cabot Lodge**) at the peace conference. Took unknown men from his own party instead, snubbed the Republicans, wouldn't let them share ownership of the League. He failed to make the other side happy about cooperating. Result: wrecked his career, ruined his health, shortened his life, kept America out of the League, and altered the course of world history.

**Dale O. Ferrier** (Fort Wayne, Indiana) — getting his son to pick up pears: Instead of confrontation, made it a deal: "$1 per bushel picked, minus $1 per pear left behind." Son not only picked every pear but monitored carefully to avoid missing any.

**Refusing speaking invitations gracefully**: Don't just say you're too busy. Express appreciation, regret inability to accept, **suggest a substitute speaker**. The other person doesn't feel unhappy about the refusal — their attention is redirected constructively.

**Napoleon**: Created the Legion of Honour, distributed 15,000 crosses, made 18 generals "Marshals of France," called troops the "Grand Army." Critics said he gave "toys" to war-hardened veterans. Napoleon: **"Men are ruled by toys."**

**Gunter Schmidt** (West Germany): Employee kept mis-tagging prices. Reminders and confrontations failed. Solution: appointed her **Supervisor of Price Tag Posting** for the entire store. New title changed her attitude completely.

**Mrs. Ernest Gent** (Scarsdale, NY): Boys destroying her lawn. Nothing worked. Made the worst offender her "detective" in charge of keeping trespassers off. He threatened to brand anyone who trespassed. Problem solved.

**Six guidelines for effective leadership**:
1. **Be sincere** — don't promise what you can't deliver; focus on their benefits, not yours
2. **Know exactly** what you want the other person to do
3. **Be empathetic** — ask what they really want
4. **Consider the benefits** they will receive
5. **Match those benefits** to their wants
6. **Frame your request** to show personal benefit to them

**Example contrasts**:
- **Order form**: "John, we have customers coming tomorrow and I need the stockroom cleaned out. Sweep it, put stock in neat piles, polish the counter."
- **Benefit form**: "John, we have a job that should be completed right away. If it is done now, we won't be faced with it later. I'm bringing customers tomorrow. If you could sweep out the stockroom, put stock in neat piles, and polish the counter, it would make us look efficient and you will have done your part to provide a good company image."

Won't always get favorable reactions. But you are **more likely to change attitudes this way** than by not using these principles. Even a **10% improvement** makes you 10% more effective as a leader.

## A Shortcut to Distinction

*Biographical essay by Lowell Thomas (1936)*

**January 1935**, Hotel Pennsylvania, New York: 2,500 people packed the ballroom during the Depression for Carnegie's course. Over 15,000 business people had already been trained. Major corporations — Westinghouse Electric, McGraw-Hill, Brooklyn Union Gas, New York Telephone, American Institute of Electrical Engineers — conducted the training in their own offices.

A survey by the University of Chicago, YMCA, and American Association for Adult Education revealed that adults' prime interest was health; second was **developing skill in human relationships** — getting along with and influencing people. Not high-sounding psychology, but practical suggestions they could use immediately in business, social contacts, and the home.

**The shocking gap**: The survey found that **no working manual existed** to help solve daily human relationship problems. Volumes on Greek, Latin, and higher mathematics — topics adults don't care about — but nothing on the one subject with a veritable passion for guidance.

Carnegie was born on a **Missouri farm**, 10 miles from the railway — never saw a streetcar until age 12. Picked strawberries and cut cockleburs for 5 cents an hour. The family's hard luck was relentless: the '102' River rose, drowned the corn, swept the hay; hogs sickened from cholera; the cattle market collapsed; the bank threatened foreclosure. They sold out and bought a farm near State Teachers' College at Warrensburg, Missouri.

Carnegie couldn't afford $1/day for board. Stayed on the farm, commuted 3 miles on horseback daily. Milked cows, cut wood, fed hogs, studied Latin by coal-oil lamp until his eyes blurred. Woke at 3 AM to nurse pedigreed Duroc-Jersey pigs — put piglets in a basket behind the kitchen stove, fed them a hot meal, returned them to warmth. Only a half-dozen isolated students couldn't afford to board in town. Ashamed of poverty, his tight coat and too-short trousers, he developed an inferiority complex — then found his leverage in **public speaking contests**.

No flair for athletics — decided to win speaking contests instead. Spent months preparing, practiced riding to college, milking cows, mounted a hay bale in the barn and harangued frightened pigeons. Initial defeats made him consider suicide. Then **suddenly began to win** — not one contest, but every speaking contest in college. Other students asked him to train them; they won also.

After graduation: sold correspondence courses to Nebraska and Wyoming ranchers. Despite boundless energy, couldn't make a go of it. In Alliance, Nebraska, threw himself on his bed mid-day and wept in despair. Resolved to go to Omaha — no money for a railroad ticket, so he travelled by **freight train**, feeding and watering two carloads of wild horses for his passage.

Sold bacon, soap, and lard for **Armour and Company** across the South Dakota Badlands — cow and Indian country. Covered his territory by freight train, stage coach, and horseback. Slept in pioneer hotels with muslin-sheet partitions. Played poker with Indians, learned money collection. Within two years, boosted the worst territory (#25) to first place among 29 routes. Armour offered promotion: "You have achieved what seemed impossible." He refused, resigned, studied at the American Academy of Dramatic Arts, toured the country acting. Recognized he'd never be a Booth or Barrymore. Sold Packard cars — dreadfully unhappy, had to scourge himself to work daily. Resigned to teach public speaking at YMCA night schools.

The YMCA refused his $2/night salary. He taught on commission. Within three years they were paying him **$30 per night**. His course spread to Philadelphia, Baltimore, London, Paris. He wrote *Public Speaking and Influencing Men in Business*, adopted by the YMCA, American Bankers' Association, and National Credit Men's Association.

Carnegie reviewed his education: **public speaking** had given him the most — confidence, courage, poise, the ability to meet and deal with people in business. More valuable than all other college courses combined.

Carnegie's philosophy: almost anyone can speak acceptably if they have self-confidence and an idea boiling within. The way to develop self-confidence: **do the thing you fear** and get a record of successful experiences behind you. He forced each class member to talk at every session. The audience was sympathetic — all in the same boat. Constant practice develops courage, confidence, and enthusiasm that carry over into private speaking.

His main job was not teaching public speaking (that was incidental) — it was **helping people conquer fears and develop courage**.

The course started as merely public speaking but evolved into a striking combination of **public speaking + salesmanship + human relations + applied psychology**. Students were experienced business people, many hadn't seen a classroom in 30 years, and they wanted results they could use the next day. Carnegie was a "slave to no hard-and-fast rules" and developed a course "as real as the measles and twice as much fun."

> "All people can talk when they get mad. Hit the most ignorant man in town on the jaw and knock him down, he would get on his feet and talk with an eloquence that would have rivalled William Jennings Bryan at the height of his career."

Post-graduation loyalty was remarkable. Graduates formed clubs that met fortnightly for years. One Philadelphia group: 19 members meeting twice monthly for **17 years**. Class members frequently travelled 50-100 miles to attend. One student commuted weekly from **Chicago to New York** for the course.

> "The average person develops only 10% of his latent mental ability." — William James (Harvard)

Carnegie helped business men and women develop their latent possibilities — creating one of the most significant movements in adult education.

*Lowell Thomas, 1936*
