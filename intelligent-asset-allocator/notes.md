## Preface

- **July 1993**: Bernstein discovered *Wall Street Journal* article on asset allocation performance 1973-92 from **T. Rowe Price** research
- Various fixed mixes of U.S. large/small stocks, foreign stocks, U.S. bonds outperformed single components (including professional managers) with significantly lower risk

### The "Simpleton's Portfolio"

- Equal 25% allocation: U.S. large stocks, U.S. small stocks, foreign stocks, U.S. high-quality bonds
- Higher return and much lower risk than S&P 500 alone
- **S&P 500 outperformed 75% of professional money managers** over same period
- Bernstein built spreadsheet models going back to **1926**, collected data across multiple historical epochs
- **Critical assumption**: portfolios were **rebalanced** periodically

### Historical Evidence: Asset Divergence

- **1987**: U.S. large stocks +5.23%, small stocks -9.3%, foreign stocks +24.93%
- **1992**: U.S. small stocks +23.35%, foreign stocks -11.85%
- **1995-1998**: unprecedented returns for biggest U.S. growth stocks, battered almost everything else
- These wild divergences are precisely why allocation matters more than picking

### Asset Allocation vs. Stock Picking

- **Market timing and stock/fund picking are nearly impossible long-term**
- Far more important to get right proportion of asset classes than to pick "best" stocks

> "Nobody consistently calls the market, and almost nobody picks stocks or mutual funds with any persistent skill."

### Gary Brinson Studies (Late 1980s)

- **Gary Brinson** and colleagues studied 82 large pension funds
- **Asset allocation accounted for over 90% of return variability** among funds
- Less-than-10% from market timing and stock/bond selection
- Some observers suggest 90% too high--perhaps only 50%--but misses the point: nobody achieves long-term success at timing/selection

> "In other words, asset allocation policy was 10 times as important as stock picking and market timing combined."

> "Asset allocation is the only factor affecting your investments that you can actually influence."

### Media Focus on Wrong Things

- Enormous ink/airtime wasted on analysts' predictions and stock recommendations
- **Louis Rukeyser's *Wall Street Week* (1994)**: even Brinson himself spent airtime on market timing, almost no attention to asset allocation
- Gambling instinct ingrained in human nature--few resist speculation

### Three Lessons from History

1. **Stocks are riskier than cash**
2. **In the future, stocks will probably have higher returns than cash, but not by as much as in the past**
3. **Portfolio diversification reduces risk**
4. **Index your investments wherever you can**

### Efficient Frontier Website (1996)

- Placed first edition online at **efficientfrontier.com**
- Reaction exceeded expectations--small investors hungry for portfolio theory
- **Stronger emphasis on indexing** in print edition--active management is "sucker's game"
- Small stocks, REITs, foreign stocks: active managers' outperformance is **illusory**
- **Proliferation of inexpensive indexed investment vehicles**--even smallest investors can build portfolios as efficiently as biggest players

## Chapter 1: General Considerations

### Uncle Fred's Pension Plan

- 30 years old, work until 65, $5000/year contributed
- **Option 1**: CDs at 3% annualized = $302,310 (only $107,436 at 3% inflation)
- **Option 2**: Coin toss each year--heads = +30%, tails = -10%

### Analyzing the Coin Toss

- Thought of losing 10% of hard-earned retirement money is too much to bear
- All 35 tails = pittance; all 35 heads = bankrupt Uncle Fred with $162,000,000
- Over long period, half heads/half tails: **1.3 x 0.9 = 1.17** per two-year cycle
- Annualized return: **8.17%**, clearly superior to 3%
- Need **12 heads and 23 tails** (very unlikely) to come out worse than Option 1
- **Binomial distribution** (Figure 1-1): odds of <13 heads = less than 5%
- Order of tosses matters: 16 heads then 19 tails = behind; 27 tails then 8 heads = ahead (both extremely unlikely events)

### Average vs. Annualized Return

- Average of +30% and -10% = +10% (arithmetic average)
- Annualized return = 8.17% (geometric--the return you must earn each and every year to equal the series)
- Example: stock worth $10, doubles (+100%) first year to $20, loses 50% next year back to $10
- Average return: 25%. Annualized return: **zero**. Made no money.
- **Annualized return always less than average return**
- Coin toss: average 10%, annualized 8.17%
- **You pay bills with annualized return, not average return**--why annualized returns so important

### First Fundamental Law of Investing

> **In the long run you are compensated for bearing risk. Conversely, if you seek safety, your returns will be low.**

- Coin toss nearly identical to choice between "safety" of T-bills and "gamble" of common stocks
- 3% CD option is most truly frightening--**will almost certainly live golden years in poverty**
- Coin toss closely approximates returns/risks of common stocks (1926-1998 stock return: **11.22%**)
- More importantly, **riskiness of coin toss and common stocks are nearly identical**
- Promise of excessive returns with low risk = investment fraud signal

### The Standard Deviation

- **Standard deviation (SD)** measures "scatter" of returns--the primary risk metric
- Tool: **Texas Instruments BA-35** calculator (~$20)

### SD Benchmarks by Asset Class

| Asset Class | SD Range |
|-------------|----------|
| Money market (cash) | 2-3% |
| Short-term bond | 3-5% |
| Long-term bond | 6-8% |
| Domestic stocks (conservative) | 10-14% |
| Domestic stocks (aggressive) | 15-25% |
| Foreign stocks | 15-25% |
| Emerging markets | 25-35% |

> "Anytime a salesperson attempts to sell you a security, ask what its standard deviation of annual returns is. If he doesn't know, don't even think about buying it."

### Interpreting SD

- **68%** of time: return within +/-1 SD of mean
- **1-in-6** chance: loss worse than (mean - 1 SD)
- **1-in-44** chance: loss worse than (mean - 2 SD)
- **1-in-740** chance: loss worse than (mean - 3 SD)
- Example: Latin American fund, 15% expected return, 35% SD: 1-in-6 chance of >20% loss, 1-in-44 chance of >55% loss

> "One sign of a dangerously overbought market is a generalized underappreciation of the risks inherent in it."

### Other Risk Measures

- Returns don't follow perfect **normal distribution**--approximate **lognormal distribution** with skew and kurtosis
- **Semivariance** (mean variance of downside events only) proposed as alternative--in practice yields similar results
- **Long Term Capital Management**: simple SD/variance calculation would have warned of trouble years before collapse
- Other measures: probability of nominal loss, inflation-adjusted loss, "loss standard deviation," probability of underperforming index or T-bills
- All individual risk measures called **"utility functions"**

## Chapter 2: Risk and Return

### Individual Asset Classes: 1926-1998

- **Data source**: **Ibbotson** monograph, *Stocks, Bonds, Bills, and Inflation* ("the SBBI")--gold standard for U.S. asset returns
- 20-30 years minimum for reliable expected return estimates; 5-10 years sufficient for risk

| Asset | Ann. Return | SD | Worst Year | 1929-32 Return |
|-------|------------|-----|------------|----------------|
| T-bills (30-day) | 3.77% | 3.22% | 0.00% | +9.49% |
| 5-yr Treasuries | 5.31% | 5.71% | -5.13% | +20.27% |
| 20-yr Treasuries | 5.34% | 9.21% | -9.19% | +19.73% |
| Large stocks (S&P) | 11.22% | 20.26% | -43.35% | -64.23% |
| Small stocks | 12.18% | 38.09% | -59.12% | -87.98% |

- T-bills barely exceed 3.08% inflation; best attribute: keep pace with inflation long-term (though 1970s were exception)
- 5-yr notes carry **interest rate risk**: fixed coupon bonds decline when rates rise; longer maturity = worse damage
- Seven negative years in 73 years; worst loss 2.65% in 1994. Real return ~2%
- 20-yr bonds: return almost identical to 5-yr notes despite much higher risk (20 losses in 73 years, one nearly 10%)
- **Paradox**: "excess risk" of long Treasuries disappears in diversified portfolio
- **Nonsystematic risk**: diversifiable portion; **Systematic risk**: non-diversifiable portion
- Insurance companies favor long bonds to match long-lived fixed liabilities
- Precious metals stocks: real long-term returns only a few percent, SD ~30%
- Large stocks real return >8%: wealth doubles every 9 years; $10K for newborn = $470K after 50 years
- 1929-1932 inflation-adjusted loss: nearly two-thirds of value; can lose >40% in bad year
- Small stocks (bottom 20% NYSE by market cap) real return >9%: $10K = $785K after 50 years; 1929-32 loss over 85%--"industrial grade" risk

### Time Horizon and Risk

- **30-year rolling returns** (large stocks): not a single period <8%
- Risk of underperforming T-bills: 1-yr 36%, 5-yr 22%, 10-yr 13%, 20-yr 6%, 30-yr 3%, 40-yr 1%
- But 5% difference between highest/lowest 30-yr returns compounds to nearly 4x wealth difference
- When measuring **SD of end wealth**, stocks become **riskier** with time
- Most relevant risk: likelihood of running out of money

### Everybody's Grandchildren Ought to Be Rich

- **John J. Raskob**, *Ladies Home Journal*, summer 1929: $15/month in stocks = $80,000 in 20 years
- Claimed >25% annualized--absurd bubble-era projection
- Yet: $15/month from Jan 1926 to Dec 1994: common stocks $2,462,295; small stocks **$11,730,165**
- Novices buy during bull markets, lack risk appreciation, sell at the worst moment

> "I do not feel that selling at very low prices is a remedy for having failed to sell at high ones. ... I would say that it is from time to time the duty of the serious investor to accept the depreciation of his holdings with equanimity." --**John Maynard Keynes**

### 1970-1998 Asset Classes

- **1970 chosen** as starting point: high-quality data, includes 1973-74 bear market (worst since Depression)
- Data accessible from **Morningstar** for nominal fee
- New asset classes: **REITs** (equity only, excluding mortgage REITs), **European stocks** (Morgan Stanley Capital Indexes), **Pacific Rim**, **Japanese**, **precious metals**, **international small stocks** (Dimensional Fund Advisors index)
- International small index only UK/Japan before 1988--use with caution
- Precious metals data estimated from Morningstar mutual fund data (1970-75: Van Eck Gold Fund proxy)
- REIT data from **NAREIT**, only to 1971
- *Long-term returns for REITs and precious metals highly suspect, should not be used for planning*--main value as inflation hedges
- Nominal returns 4% higher for bonds, 1.5% for small stocks, 2.5% for large stocks in 1970-98 vs. 1926-98
- But inflation 5.23% (1970-98) vs. 3.1% (1926-98), so real returns roughly equal for large stocks
- **1929-1932 bear market worse than 1973-1974** in real terms (despite deflation vs. inflation)
- Risk-return relationship: good correlation overall; two notable exceptions: **precious metals** and **Pacific Rim** (returns not commensurate with risks)

### The Problem with Historical Returns

- Extrapolating past into future is hazardous
- **Bond returns**: good approximation = the **coupon**. 1926 AAA corporate coupon 4.9%, actual 1926-98 return 5.77%
- 1958-1983: brutal bond bear market, rates rose from <3% to >14%; 20-yr Treasuries returned only 3.5% for 50 years (1934-83)
- In 1984, 14% coupon forecast 12.85% return over next 15 years--far better than historical average would suggest

### Stock Returns: Discounted Dividend Method

- **Discounted dividend method**: most time-honored approach for estimating future stock returns
- Over long periods, all companies go bankrupt--stock value = **discounted value** of all future **dividends**
- Formula: Return = dividend yield + dividend growth rate + multiple change
- Historical: 4.5% yield + 5% growth + 1.7% multiple change = **11.2%** (matches actual 11.22%)
- **Multiple change**: stocks sold for 22x dividends (4.5% rate) in 1926, now 77x dividends (1.3% rate)
- **Future estimates** (as of writing): 1.3% yield + 5% growth + 0% multiple change = **6.3%**
- Long bonds yielding ~6%, so stock return only slightly higher than bond return
- In 1998, expected return of corporate bonds briefly exceeded that of stocks
- **Real earnings growth**: Dow nominal ~5%/yr, real (inflation-adjusted) only **~2%/yr**
- Illogical to expect broad stock index to significantly exceed "natural" real growth rate of 2%

> **Benjamin Graham**: "In the short run the stock market is a voting machine, but in the long run it is a weighing machine. What it weighs are earnings."

### Expected Returns for Other Asset Classes

- European/Japanese stocks: about same as U.S.
- U.S. small stocks: somewhat higher
- Pacific Rim/emerging markets: 3-4% yield, possibly higher growth, much higher risk
- **REITs**: greatest anomaly--yielding 8.8%, should have higher returns than S&P even with no earnings growth
- Use **real (inflation-adjusted)** returns for planning

### Table 2-3: Expected Asset Class Characteristics

| Asset | Expected Real Return | Worst Bear Market Loss |
|-------|---------------------|----------------------|
| T-bills | 0-3% | None |
| High-quality bonds | 3% | 0% (short) / 10% (long) |
| Large stocks (U.S./foreign) | 4% | 40-50% |
| REITs, small stocks, emerging mkts | 6% | 50-60% |
| Precious metals | 0-4%* | 50-60% |

- 50/50 stock-bond example: 25% small (6%) + 25% large (4%) + 50% bonds (3%) = **4% real return**
- **Rule of 72**: return rate x years to double = 72

### Chapter 2 Summary

1. Risk and reward inextricably intertwined
2. Longer holding = less chance of poor result
3. SD measures risk
4. Historical returns (>20 yrs) are decent guide to future; study investment history

## Chapter 3: The Behavior of Multiple-Asset Portfolios

### Uncle Fred's Two-Coin-Toss Offer

- Divide pension in half, separate independent coin toss for each
- Four outcomes: HH (+30%), HT (+10%), TH (+10%), TT (-10%)
- Account factor: 1.3 x 1.1 x 1.1 x 0.9 = 1.4157
- Annualized return: **9.08%** (nearly 1% higher than single toss's 8.17%)
- SD reduced to **14.14%** (from 20% single toss)

> Dividing your portfolio between assets with uncorrelated results increases return while decreasing risk.

- **Uncorrelated**: result of first toss has no influence on second
- Perfectly correlated = same as single toss; perfectly **inversely** correlated = always 10%, zero risk
- In real world, meaningful negative correlations are never seen long-term

### Example 1: Stock/Bond Model

- "Stock" = +30%/-10% coin toss; "Bond" = +10%/0% coin toss
- Must **rebalance** portfolio back to chosen combination at year-end
- If bond returns +10% and stock returns -10%, must sell some bond and buy more stock
- Why rebalance? (1) increases long-term return while reducing risk, (2) prevents all-stock drift, (3) instills discipline to **buy low, sell high**
- 75/25 stock/bond: annualized **7.70%**, SD **15.05%**
- Return only 0.47% less than 100% stock, risk decreased by ~5%
- One-quarter of risk eliminated at cost of only one-seventeenth of return

### The Remarkable Left Side of the Graph

- Right side unsurprising: adding bonds to stock dramatically reduces risk with small return loss
- Left side truly remarkable: starting from 100% bonds, adding small amounts of stock **increases** return AND **decreases** portfolio risk slightly
- "Minimum risk" portfolio = about 7% stock
- 12% stock = same risk as all-bond portfolio
- Phenomenon encountered repeatedly in portfolio behavior study

> **Thus, the investor whose sole aim is to minimize risk must of necessity own some stock.**

### Example 2: Two Identical Uncorrelated Assets (Uncle Fred's Two-Coin Redux)

- Both assets A and B: +30%/-10%; each asset's return independent of the other
- Assets have same return and SD, so 75/25 A/B behaves same as 25/75
- Risk lessens and return increases as you move toward 50/50 mix
- Most gain in return vs. risk from first shift (100% to 75/25)
- Trip from 75/25 to 50/50 produces much less additional benefit
- 65/35 mix differs from 50/50 by <0.1% return and 0.5% SD

### Three Key Points

1. Two similar uncorrelated assets in fixed rebalanced mix: reduces risk AND increases return (rebalancing forces buy low/sell high)
2. If similar returns/risks and poorly correlated: optimal mix close to 50/50
3. **Plenty of margin for error**--off 10-20% from best allocation costs little. Sticking to policy through thick and thin matters more than picking "best" allocation

### Dealing with More Than Two Assets

- Two uncorrelated assets: 4 time periods. Three assets: 8 periods. Four: 16.
- Difficult to find two uncorrelated assets; **practically impossible to find three**; **absolutely impossible to find more than three** mutually uncorrelated
- Portfolio of two assets: one correlation. Three assets: three correlations. Four: six. Ten assets: 45.
- Same reason big offices have messier politics than small ones

### Correlation Coefficient

- Ranges from -1 to +1: +1 perfectly correlated, 0 uncorrelated, -1 perfectly inversely correlated
- Real assets are almost always **imperfectly** correlated
- Calculated via spreadsheet: Excel CORREL function
- Visualized with scatter plots: if perfectly correlated, all points on straight line; if uncorrelated, widely scattered
- **S&P 500/U.S. Small Stock**: correlation .777 (high--poor return for one invariably associated with poor return for other)
- **S&P 500/EAFE**: correlation .483 (moderate--meaningful diversification)
- **Japan Small/REIT**: correlation .068 (near zero--"scattergram" with no discernable pattern)

### Chapter 3 Summary

1. **Correlation of assets** is central to portfolio theory--lower = better
2. Diversifying among uncorrelated assets reduces risk and increases return; requires periodic **rebalancing**

## Chapter 4: The Behavior of Real-World Portfolios

### Return-vs-Risk Plots and the Efficient Frontier Curve

- Stock/bond mixes 1926-1998 form a **return-versus-risk plot**: upward = higher return, rightward = higher risk
- Starting from 100% bonds: adding first 15% stock increases return dramatically with almost no added risk
- Path curves right past 50/50: further stock increases add considerable risk for modest return gains
- **Bulge in curve** = diversification benefit; more bulge = better
- From 100% stock end: adding small amounts of bonds dramatically reduces risk with minimal return sacrifice
- Figure 4-1 (stock/20-yr bonds) resembles coin-toss model from Chapter 3--simple model accurately captures real-world behavior
- Figure 4-2: three bond maturities mixed with stocks--**5-year notes superior** across most risk levels
- T-bills only desirable at very low risk levels
- **Mean-variance analysis** also confirms short-term bond superiority
- General rule: stick to bond maturities of **6 months to 5 years** for risk-diluting portion
- Small stocks and long-term corporate bonds behave nearly identically to large stocks and 20-year Treasuries

### Risk Dilution

- Two methods to reduce risk: (1) substitute less risky assets, (2) replace stock with short-term bonds
- **Risk dilution** = method 2; preserves stock strategy undisturbed
- Conservative portfolios almost always require small exposure to very risky assets
- High-risk vs. low-risk portfolios differ mainly in **broad stock/bond allocation**, not stock composition

### Foreign Assets: The Case for International Diversification

- All-U.S. database has only two broad uncorrelated categories: stocks and bonds
- > "The essence of effective portfolio construction is the use of a large number of poorly correlated assets."
- **Foreign securities necessary** for many imperfectly correlated components

### EAFE Index: Three Time Periods

- Most widely used foreign index: **Morgan Stanley EAFE** (Europe, Australasia, Far East)
- **1969-1988**: "Talk about a free lunch!" EAFE returns ~2% higher than comparable U.S. equity. Adding 30% EAFE = 2% more return with almost no extra risk
- **1979-1998**: "Goodbye free lunch." EAFE decreased both risk and return. By 2000, conventional wisdom: "Stay at home for higher returns"

> "Diversify abroad only at your peril. And, if you must invest abroad, do so only where you can drink the water."

- **1969-1998** (full 30 years): S&P 12.67%, EAFE 12.39%--nearly identical. Very "bulgy" plot; portfolios up to 80% EAFE had higher returns than either alone; up to 40% EAFE had less risk than either alone
- No question international diversification worked superbly over full 30 years

### The Danger of Recency

- **Recency** = single biggest investing mistake--extrapolating recent trends indefinitely
- 1970s: only real estate and gold seemed viable. Late 1980s: Japanese prowess seemed dominant. Both proved disastrous.

> "A recurring theme in these pages is that you try as hard as you can to identify the diverse strains of current financial wisdom in order that you may ignore them."

### Beyond the Modern Era

- **Will Goetzmann** and **Philippe Jorion** (UC Irvine): studied post-1920 returns outside U.S.
- U.S. had world's highest equity returns (~8% over inflation)
- **Survivorship bias**: 1930 biggest bourses were Berlin, Cairo, Buenos Aires--gone by 1960
- Global GDP-weighted portfolio returned ~1% less than domestic, but with much lower SD
- Main advantage of international diversification: **decreased risk**, not increased return

### Uncle Fred's Advice: Overlapping Periods Analysis

- Figure 4-6: two overlapping 20-year periods (1969-88 thin sail, 1979-98 thick sail) with three mixes each
- 1988 sentiment favored foreign equity; 1998 favored domestic--**recency in action**
- "Since you can't predict equity returns, why don't you try splitting the difference?"
- 50/50 domestic/foreign line much closer to best-performing line than worst in both periods
- For entire 30-year period, 50/50 mix is itself the "best" equity asset
- Almost all foreign advantage 1969-1988 came from **currency gain** (yen and European currencies rising)
- Decade ago: overly optimistic about foreign diversification. Today: overly pessimistic. Foreign stocks belong in everyone's portfolio.

> "The real purpose of portfolio analysis is not to find the 'best' asset mix. Rather, it is to find a mix that will not be too far off the mark under a wide variety of circumstances."

### Small vs. Large Stocks

- 73-year small-stock premium still ~1%, but S&P recent dominance questions it (recency again)
- No question small stocks are **more risky** than large stocks
- Figure 4-7: small/large stock mixes with 5-yr Treasuries nearly overlap
- Current data (1926-98): 50/50 small/bonds mix has almost same risk and return as 100% large stocks
- Small-stock "potency" ~1.5x S&P: more bang for buck in both risk and return
- Most common international small-cap index: **Dimensional Fund Advisors** index (back to 1970)
- Figure 4-8 (U.S./international small stocks, 1970-98): extremely "bulgy" diversification curve

**The Dark Side of International Small Stocks:**
- 1970-1989 returns: S&P 11.55%, EAFE 16.26%, U.S. small 11.82%, International small **26.14%**
- 1990-1998 returns: S&P 17.89%, EAFE 5.29%, U.S. small 13.56%, International small **-1.08%**
- Staggering returns first period, truly awful second period
- When foreign stocks do well, foreign small do especially well, and vice versa
- **Tracking error**: can you handle temporarily doing poorly while others do well?

### The Efficient Frontier

- **David Wilkinson** generated 800 random portfolios of seven assets (1992-96): cloud of risk/return
- Vertical line at 15% SD: some portfolios return 6%, others 14%--obviously want to be on top
- Upper left edge = **efficient frontier**--most return for given risk OR least risk for given return
- Central concept to portfolio theory, but also source of mischief
- **"There is no Santa Claus"**: next year's efficient frontier nowhere near last year's
- Like trying to generate power by placing battery where lightning last struck
- Anyone claiming "on the efficient frontier" also talks to Elvis and the Easter Bunny
- Efficient frontier 1992-96: heavy S&P/European; 1970-96: heavy Japanese/U.S. small/precious metals
- If you calculated efficient frontier for first half (1970-83) and used for second half (1984-96): would have gotten your head handed to you
- Over short periods, precise stock allocation matters greatly; over very long periods, becomes less important

### Market Timing is a Fool's Errand

- Institutional money managers spend large sums on macro/political/market analyses to guess which assets will perform best
- **It's a fool's errand**--markets have already impounded this information into current price
- U.S. economy strongest? Whole world knows it--that's why $100 buys only $8-$15 of earnings on Wall Street vs. Seoul/Hong Kong/Sao Paulo
- Think recency: impressive guy in $2000 suit spinning plausible patter probably had father pushing Krugerrands in 1979, older brother urging Japanese stocks in 1989

> "Something that everyone knows isn't worth knowing." --**Bernard Baruch**

### The Importance of Rebalancing

- Target composition = **policy allocation**
- If asset does extraordinarily well, must sell and reinvest in poor performers to return to target
- Requires enormous discipline and patience--doing opposite of what investment world is doing
- Psychologist friend: effective way of becoming "contrarian"--always moving opposite direction of crowd
- Great buying opportunities (U.S. stocks 1974, Japanese 1970) felt like throwing money down rat hole

### Small Investor's Three Advantages over Professionals

1. **Aggressive rebalancing** without client complaints (analogy: driving wrong way up one-way street--difficult with own vehicle; nearly impossible when chauffeuring Rolls Royce with owner squawking at every pothole)
2. **Access to small stocks** (large institutions effectively barred--can only buy so much thinly traded small company before price bid too high)
3. **No pressure after bad quarters** (even **Joe DiMaggio** had slumps; **Warren Buffett** occasionally gets burned; most successful allocations underperform S&P ~4 years out of 10)

> "This extremely successful allocation underperformed the S&P in 12 out of 27 years--44% of the time."

### Track Record of Experts

- Famous analyst "credited with calling the crash" in 1987: ran mutual fund that grossly underperformed, finally folded
- Another analyst on well-known TV program: runs funds with mediocre performance
- Newsletter writer on prime-time TV: recommendations analyzed by respected academics--would have lost **5.4% per year annualized over 13 years**
- Just as compulsive unskilled gambler occasionally beats house, so too "professionals" occasionally best patient individual investor
- **Small investor with fixed asset allocation policy has unfair advantage over professional**
- Object: develop long-term strategy to become casino owner, not the mark

### Chapter 4 Summary

1. Adding small amount of stock to bond portfolio increases return while actually reducing risk slightly
2. International diversification works over long periods--decreased risk is primary benefit
3. Sticking to allocation through volatility more important than picking perfect mix

## Chapter 5: Optimal Asset Allocations

### Three Types of "Optimal"

1. **Future optimal**: unknowable
2. **Hypothetical optimal**: from assumed inputs
3. **Historical optimal**: poor guide for future

> "You cannot know future optimal portfolio composition any more than you can sprout wings and fly."

### Recap of Key Principles

- Long-term returns and risks well known, but actual returns can deviate significantly over 10-20 years
- Effective diversification increases return while reducing risk through periodic **rebalancing**
- Asset allocation accounts for most performance differences among managers
- Market timing and security selection are difficult to impossible; nearly irrelevant
- Cannot predict future, so find allocation that does reasonably well under wide range of circumstances
- Sticking to target allocation through volatility more important than picking the right one

### Mean-Variance Optimization

- **Harry Markowitz** developed **mean-variance analysis** (Nobel Prize)
- **Mean-variance optimizer (MVO)**: software that computes optimal portfolios
- Requires three inputs: (1) return, (2) SD, (3) correlations for all assets
- Now available for under $100; example: **MVOPlus** by **Efficient Solutions**
- **Table 5-1**: optimizer inputs for 1970-96 (nine assets, returns, SDs, correlations for 27 periods)
- **MVO disadvantage**: single-period technique, doesn't account for rebalancing (but easy to adjust after)
- **Corner portfolios**: define efficient frontier via critical-line technique (10 portfolios from minimum-variance to maximum-return)
- Corner 1: minimum-variance (92.5% T-bills, 7.5% other). Corners 7-8: reasonable risk range for most investors
- Geometric return (what you "eat") vs. arithmetic return; difference = variance drag ~ (SD)^2/2

### Fatal Weakness of Historical Optimization

- Portfolio 7 (1970-96): one-third stock (almost entirely U.S. small, Japanese, precious metals), two-thirds 5-yr Treasuries
- These had highest returns for that period--optimizer's fatal weakness: **overly fond of assets with recent high returns**
- Can manipulate optimizer to produce almost any portfolio by adjusting return inputs by a few percent
- Assets exhibit **mean reversion** over long periods--stellar 10-year performance often followed by below-average next 10
- Optimizers called **"error maximizers"**

> "If you can predict the optimizer inputs well enough to come close to the future efficient frontier, then you don't need an optimizer in the first place."

**Five-year period test (1970-1998)** -- optimized each 5-yr period, tested next:

### More Bad News

- Well-diversified portfolio doesn't eliminate risk--economic catastrophes do not respect national borders
- 1929-32 and 1973-74 involved all markets; damage varied only by degree
- If one asset has SD of 20%, two completely uncorrelated: SD 14.1%. Four uncorrelated: 10%
- Nearly impossible to find three mutually uncorrelated assets
- Maximum risk reduction from diversification: about **one-quarter to one-third**
- **Below-average returns have higher correlation** than above-average ("negative semicorrelations" > "positive semicorrelations")
- Actual correlation in severe bear markets is higher than raw coefficient suggests
- **Bruno Solnik**: "Diversification fails us just when we need it most"
- Oct 19, 1987 and fall 1990: all world stock indexes suffered losses despite low normal correlations
- **Simple portfolio backtesting**: valuable supplement to MVO--see how portfolio responds in actual bear markets

### Sovereign Risk

- **Sovereign risk**: assets expropriated by foreign government or lost in war
- Pre-WWII: Germany and Egypt were two major capital markets--one destroyed by war, one nationalized
- Latin American nations defaulting on debt with clocklike regularity past century
- Mathematical impact: 50% capital loss once in 70 years lowers long-term return by only 1.0%
- Japanese/German capital disappeared at start of WWII but spectacular returns earned in four decades following 1945

### Globalization and Correlation

- Argument: globalization has eliminated international diversification value; markets increasingly correlated
- **U.K.-U.S. correlation by period**: 1919-37 (.66), 1938-56 (.26), 1957-75 (.74), **1976-94 (.18)** -- lowest in most recent period
- No pattern of increasing correlation despite globalization
- S&P 500/EAFE rolling 3-year correlation (1969-98): varies widely, no clear-cut increase
- Exception: increasing correlations among European markets over past two decades

### Rebalancing Bonus

- More important than risk reduction: the **rebalancing bonus**--extra return from rigorous rebalancing
- Not only pecuniary but also psychological: profiting by moving opposite to market gains healthy self-reliance and scorn for sentiment
- **Distrust of sentiment and "expert opinion" is investor's most useful tool**
- Limited exposure to any segment: if only 5-10% in emerging markets, inevitable 30-50% loss won't hurt much
- Gains in other areas make up for part or all of loss
- Low exposure makes you eager to rebalance, thus "buying cheap"

### Small Stocks: International Diversification

- **Table 5-3**: Correlations of global large and small stocks (1990-98)
- Example Japan-U.S.: large stocks .310, small stocks .195
- Small stock correlation consistently less than large stocks for each pair
- Particularly dramatic for U.S.-U.K. and U.S.-Continental European correlations
- Despite individual nations' small stock indexes being more volatile: **DFA global large index SD = 14.37%; global small index SD = 13.46%** (1990-98)
- **Real risk of small stocks**: tracking error--propensity for considerably lower or higher returns than large
- **Handle large-vs-small dilemma**: (1) guard against recency, (2) hedge bets with large and small, (3) own all **"four corners"** of global equity: large domestic, large foreign, small domestic, small foreign

### Three-Step Asset Allocation Approach

**Step 1: How many asset classes?**
- Law of diminishing returns: most diversification from first several
- **Level-one palette**: U.S. large (S&P 500), U.S. small (CRSP 9-10/Russell 2000/S&P 600), foreign (EAFE), U.S. short-term bonds
- If equal division among these four, will outperform overwhelming majority of professionals over decades
- **Level-two palette**: adds foreign large, emerging markets, foreign small, REITs, (optional: precious metals, international bonds). For individuals serious about full diversification benefit.
- **Level-three palette**: equity divided by nationality x size x value/growth
- **Growth**: Microsoft, Wal-Mart, Amazon.com--rapidly growing, selling for 35x+ earnings
- **Value**: General Motors, Kmart, J.P. Morgan--poor growth prospects, sell cheaply
- 10 regions x (large vs. small) x (value vs. growth) = 40 possibilities, plus sectors (REITs, precious metals, natural resources, utilities), plus bonds per nation
- Can buy emerging markets small-company fund or individual foreign companies as **American Depositary Receipts (ADRs)**
- Not recommended for those who don't truly enjoy investing

**Step 2: How conventional?**
- U.S. large stocks are "the market"--all investors compare to S&P 500 benchmark
- **Conventional portfolio**: 60% U.S. large, 20% each U.S. small and foreign (low tracking error)
- **"Equal mix" (coward's portfolio)**: equal-weighted across all three stock classes (higher tracking error)
- **Table 5-4**: six 5-year periods starting 1969--long-term returns very similar for all three
- Coward's portfolio underperformed S&P 500 by 4%/yr in 1969-73 and 1989-93; over 8% in last 5-yr period
- Conventional portfolio lagged S&P by considerably less
- **Tracking error**: how much temporary underperformance bothers you
- As portfolios increase in complexity, tracking error becomes more pronounced
- International small stocks lagged S&P by 19%/yr since 1990 despite outstanding 30-year performance
- More exotic asset classes = higher tracking error. Doesn't mean lower returns, just very different behavior

**Step 3: Risk tolerance (stock/bond split)**
- Beginning of millennium: stock returns at best only slightly higher than bonds
- 1920 DJIA earnings $9.12/share; 1998: $378.06 = 4.89%/yr nominal, real only ~2%
- S&P 500 yield 1.3% + 2% real growth = <3.5% expected real stock return
- **TIPS**: government-guaranteed 4.1% real yield
- Aggressive: 75% stocks max; moderate: 50/50; conservative: 30% stocks

> "The aggressiveness of your portfolio is reflected in your overall stock and bond mix, not in the kinds of equity you hold."

### Sample Portfolios

**The Madonna Portfolio** (bold, unconventional, high risk):
- 10% each: S&P 500, U.S. small, REITs, intl large, intl small, emerging markets, precious metals
- 30% U.S. short-term bonds

> "Not recommended for all but the hardiest of souls and most independent of thinkers."

**The Gap Portfolio** (complex, conventional, low risk):
- Different answers: complexity high (dozen+ classes), conventionality high (minimize tracking error), risk tolerance low (don't want >6% net worth loss in a year)
- From **DFA** "moderate balanced" strategy, 40/60 stocks/bonds
- 15 asset classes including value/growth splits
- 8% U.S. large growth, 8% U.S. large value, 4% U.S. small growth, 4% U.S. small value, 4% REIT, 4% intl large value, 2% intl small growth, 2% intl small value, 1.2% EM large growth, 1.2% EM large value, 1.6% EM small growth
- Bonds: 15% each of 1-yr corporate, 2-yr global, 5-yr U.S. govt, 5-yr global
- Quite conventional: 28/12 domestic/foreign split, much heavier large-cap than small-cap
- Return rarely varies more than half-dozen percent from domestic 40/60 S&P 500/T-bill mix

### Three Key Decisions Restated

- First: number of asset classes (more = better diversification but more tracking error and work)
- Second: tolerance for **tracking error** (low = keep foreign and small-cap stocks low)
- Third: stock/bond mix based on risk tolerance (75% stocks aggressive, down to 25% least aggressive)

### Chapter 5 Summary

1. Impossible to forecast future optimal portfolios by any technique
2. Widely diversified global portfolio of small and large stocks should have favorable risk/return
3. Allocation depends on: tracking error tolerance, number of asset classes, risk tolerance

## Chapter 6: Market Efficiency

> "There are two kinds of investors: those who don't know where the market is headed, and those who don't know that they don't know."

- Third type: **investment professionals** who know they don't know but whose livelihood depends on appearing to know
- Western culture relies on scientific method: beliefs should be **falsifiable** (testable)
- Skill defined by **persistence** of performance over time
- Baseball .300 hitters consistently outperform .260 average over multiple seasons by wide enough margin to prove skill
- "Hot hand" in basketball fails statistical analysis: recent high shooting percentage doesn't predict future
- Humans see patterns where none exist--critical insight for finance

### Michael Jensen's Landmark Study (1968)

- Examined mutual fund performance 1945-1964 in *Journal of Finance*
- **No evidence of persistence** of fund performance
- Dozens of subsequent studies confirm: tiny persistence exists, but after expenses investors still underperform

### DFA Study (1970-1998)

- Examined top 30 diversified mutual funds for sequential 5-year periods
- **Table 6-1**: top funds from first period underperformed S&P 500 in subsequent period
- In two of five examples, top funds actually underperformed their peers as well
- Performance resembles "chimpanzees throwing darts at the stock page"
- Success or failure is purely random affair
- Successful managers get interviewed in *Money*, *The New York Times*, assets balloon--temporarily
- Laws of chance eventually catch up: handsome prince managing funds turns out to be just another hairy simian

### Math Details: Statistical Testing for Skill

- Mean batting average .260, SD among hitters .020
- **Standard error (SE)** for 10-year period: 0.020/sqrt(10) = .0063
- **In sample** vs **out of sample**: picked from large group after the fact (probably luck) vs. identified ahead of time (probably skill)
- Same logic applies to evaluating money managers

### Robert Sanborn's Oakmark Fund

- **Robert Sanborn**, **Oakmark Fund**: 1991-98 annualized 24.91% vs. 19.56% S&P
- 1992: beat benchmark by 41.28%

**Year-by-year performance:**
| Year | Return vs S&P | Assets |
|------|--------------|--------|
| 1992 | +41.30% | $328M |
| 1993 | +20.40% | $1.21B |
| 1994 | +2.00% | $1.63B |
| 1995 | -3.10% | $3.30B |
| 1996 | -6.70% | $4.19B |
| 1997 | -0.80% | $7.30B |
| 1998 | -24.90% | $7.67B |

- Pattern: investors chase performance, more and more get lower returns
- **Dollar-weighted returns underperformed S&P by 7.55%/yr**--most investors hopped on after best returns occurred
- **Alpha**: excess return added by manager after accounting for market exposure, company size, value orientation
- **Regression analysis**: manager's returns fitted alongside market factors/sectors
- Oakmark's alpha first 29 months: truly spectacular, p = .0004 (less than 1-in-2000 chance due to luck)
- Last 29 months: equally impressive but in wrong direction
- Mr. Sanborn "modestly skilled" (not derogatory--99% of managers show no skill) but overwhelmed by **impact-cost drag** of managing billions of new assets
- Human nature: find patterns where none exist, find skill where luck more likely
- **Successful actively managed funds sow seeds of their own destruction**

### Why Fund Managers Do So Poorly: The Zero-Sum Game

- Fund manager performance does not persist, stock picking return is zero
- Managers *are* the market--impossible for all to perform above mean
- "Wall Street, unfortunately, is not Lake Wobegon, where all the children are above average"

### The Four Layers of Mutual Fund Costs

| Cost Layer | Large Cap | Small/Foreign | Emerging |
|-----------|-----------|---------------|----------|
| Expense ratio | 1.30% | 1.60% | 2.00% |
| Commissions | 0.30% | 0.50% | 1.00% |
| Bid-ask spread | 0.30% | 1.00% | 3.00% |
| Impact costs | 0.30% | 1.00% | 3.00% |
| **Total** | **2.20%** | **4.10%** | **9.00%** |

- **Expense ratio**: advisory fees + admin (does NOT include commissions; only reported since 1996 SEC requirement)
- **Bid-ask spread**: stock always bought slightly higher than sold. Microsoft 0.15% vs. Officeland (tiny company) 7.7%. Range 1-4% for foreign stocks.
- **Market-impact costs**: selling large blocks drives down price; buying drives up. First approximation: assume equal to spread. Not problem for small investors, real headache for large funds.
- Net active management drag: large-cap ~1.5%, foreign/small ~3.3%, emerging **~8%**
- High returns of recent years masked staggering costs

### The Information Age Paradox

- Before **Securities Act of 1933**: most basic financial information closely guarded
- **Benjamin Graham** wrote *Security Analysis*: getting company data required train rides and sweet-talking secretaries
- Now every aspect of company finances immediately available to anyone with computer and modem
- Data immediately discounted into security's price, no further profit from acting on it

### The January Effect

- **January effect (JE)**: small-company stocks' higher returns almost entirely concentrated in January
- **Table 6-3** (Ibbotson data, 1926-94): domestic stocks divided into deciles by NYSE size
- Decile 10 (smallest): January excess 10.28%, whole year 7.82%--January excess larger than annual!
- **Problem 1**: magnitude equals bid-ask spread for smallest stocks (~10%)--simple act of buying and selling eliminates benefit
- **Problem 2**: JE no longer exists (Figure 6-1: 10-year rolling average of small-cap premium faded into insignificance)
- Once profitable strategies discovered, acted upon by investment community, bidding up prices, eliminating excess return

### The Indexing Solution

- **Charles Ellis**: money manager, writer, financial elder statesman
- Famous 1972 *Financial Analysts Journal* article: **"The Loser's Game"**
- Compared professional investing to amateur tennis: amateur who simply returns ball with minimum fancy moves usually wins
- In both amateur tennis and investing, success less about winning than avoiding losing
- Easiest way to lose: incur high costs by trading excessively
- Index = simply buy and hold entire market, minimizing all four cost layers

**Index Fund Expenses:**

| Cost Layer | Large Cap | Small/Foreign | Emerging |
|-----------|-----------|---------------|----------|
| Expense ratio | 0.18% | 0.20% | 0.57% |
| Commissions | 0.01% | 0.10% | 0.10% |
| Bid-ask spread | 0.02% | 0.15% | 0.40% |
| Impact costs | 0.02% | 0.15% | 0.40% |
| **Total** | **0.23%** | **0.60%** | **1.47%** |
| **Indexing Advantage** | **1.97%** | **3.50%** | **7.53%** |

### Math: Active Manager Headwind

- Random scatter of active manager returns: ~8% SD in any year
- Over 25 years: SD = 8%/sqrt(25) = **1.6%**
- Large-cap index advantage (~1.6%) = +1 SD: should beat **84%** of active funds
- Small/foreign advantage (~3.2%) = +2 SD: should beat **97%** of active funds over 25 years
- Emerging-markets advantage: should best virtually all active peers

### Theoretical vs. Practical Indexing Advantage

- This is *theoretical* advantage; at least some active expenses spent on research (shown to be of some benefit)
- Research expenses almost never completely pay for themselves
- Key: research expense results in turnover, which increases total expense through commissions, spreads, impact costs

### Small-Cap Indexing & Dunn's Law

- Compare 15-year 8th percentile ranking of Vanguard 500 to DFA 9-10 Small Company Fund's 57th percentile ranking
- No coincidence — 15-year returns of tracking indexes: 17.91% (S&P) vs. 9.17% (small cap)

- Example: 1992-1994, small stocks outperformed large by 7.59% annually
  - Vanguard 500 ranked only 46th percentile
  - DFA 9-10 Small Company ranked 13th percentile

**Dunn's Law** (after **Steve Dunn**):

> "When an asset class does relatively well, an index fund in that class does even better."

- Mechanism: index fund takes full brunt of asset-class performance, while active managers drift into other size categories
- Past 15 years of large-cap dominance: most active small-cap managers owned some midsize and large stocks, helping vs. small-cap index
- Reverse true for large-cap active managers: frequently own stocks smaller than S&P 500, hurting performance vs. S&P 500
- Result: large-cap indexing looks better than it actually is; small-cap indexing looks worse

  - Yet not one diversified international fund had higher return over same period (expense advantage)

### Small-Cap Growth Exception

- One area where indexing may fail: small-cap growth stocks
- Data supports notion that research on these highly entrepreneurial companies may pay for itself
- Small-cap indexing strategy necessarily sells most rapidly appreciating stocks as they grow beyond size borders (when these actually have highest returns going forward)
- Small-cap growth has poor long-term returns regardless — best to avoid entirely, active or indexed

### Survivorship Bias

- Looking at performance of surviving funds only gives overly optimistic picture — worst funds killed off or merged
- **Burton Malkiel** (*A Random Walk Down Wall Street*): survivorship effect adds ~1.5% to reported average fund category performance
- May be as much as 3% for some categories (particularly small stocks)
- Screened Morningstar Principia (Nov 1999): 213 domestic small-cap funds with 5-year records, avg return 12.19%
- Index funds slightly higher (Vanguard Small Cap 13.64%, DFA 9-10 13.10%)
- True average including dead funds: probably 9%-10% range — indexing wins decisively

### Indexing and Tax Efficiency

- Index funds allow capital gains to grow largely undisturbed
- Active managers churn portfolios, generating unnecessary capital gains
- Small investors using active managers tend to turn over funds every few years when outperformance disappears
- **Caveat**: small-cap index funds have higher turnover (stocks "graduate" out) — generates high capital gains distributions
- Solution: "tax managed" small-cap and large-cap index funds now available

> "For the taxable investor, indexing means never having to say you're sorry."

### The Real Money: Pension Funds

- Mutual funds manage ~$5 trillion; pension funds manage ~$10 trillion (the "real" money)
- **Investment Company Institute data (1998)**: only 7% of mutual fund assets indexed vs. 34% of pension assets from 200 largest U.S. corporations
- Pension fund management involves four players: sponsors (corporations), employees/beneficiaries, fund managers (compete fiercely — 0.02% of $10B = $2M annually), consultants
- Like mutual fund managers, pension managers "throw projectiles at a stock list" — no consistent skill
- Some get lucky, attract consultants' attention, get hired; laws of probability take over, they underperform, get sacked
- Cycle costs ~1% of $10 trillion in pension expenses annually ($100 billion)
- **Piscataqua Research** (1987-1996): only 8% of nation's largest pension plans beat an indexed 60/40 mix

### Expert Opinion & Market Efficiency

- **David Dreman** (*Contrarian Market Strategy*): tracked expert opinion back to 1929 — underperformed market with 77% frequency
- Consensus/"expert" opinion underperforms ~75% of the time
- Dreman argues this is powerful evidence against efficient market hypothesis
- **Market efficiency**: public information already priced in; futile to analyze based on public info
- Markets often **overreact** to events (falling too much on bad news, rising too much on good)

- Why aggregate professional managers fail: they **are** the market

### Mr. Market and Valuation

- **Ben Graham's Mr. Market** analogy (from *The Intelligent Investor*): manic-depressive store owner marks prices up in manic phase, down in depressed phase
- Smart townspeople learned to buy during depressed periods, only necessities during manic periods
- Financial markets are equally irrational — intelligent investor stocks up when prices low, lightens when high
- Most investors (especially professionals) do the opposite
- Graham's women's magazine article: buy stocks like groceries (bargains), not like perfume
- Key for small investor: know how cheap or expensive the "tomatoes" are
  - 25 cents/pound = bargain; $3/pound = robbery

### Not Quite a Random Walk

- **Random walk**: yesterday's, last month's, or last year's return conveys **no information** about future returns
- Test for randomness: look for "autocorrelations" in price changes
  - **Positive autocorrelation** = momentum (above/below average returns tend to repeat)
  - **Negative autocorrelation** = mean reversion
  - **Zero** = random walk

**S&P 500 monthly returns (1926-1998)**: autocorrelation of **0.081**
- More than 2x random-walk SD (0.034); odds of occurring randomly: <1 in 100
- Conclusion: U.S. security prices exhibit **some momentum** over one-month periods

**Campbell, Lo, and MacKinlay (CLM) data (1962-1984)** from *The Econometrics of Financial Markets*:

| | Daily | Weekly | Monthly |
|---|---|---|---|
| Large stocks (value-weighted) | 0.176 | 0.015 | 0.043 |
| Small stocks (equally weighted) | 0.350 | 0.203 | 0.171 |

- Large stocks: high statistical significance for daily momentum, not longer periods
- Small stocks: demonstrate momentum over days, weeks, months
- Daily small-stock autocorrelation (0.350): many securities don't trade every day — big market move followed by appropriate moves next day in untreated stocks
- **CLM finding**: when looking for momentum in individual stocks, **none found** — charting individual stock prices is likely wasting time
- Cross autocorrelations: rise/fall in large stocks usually followed by rise/fall in small stocks

### What Momentum Means for Investors

- Most impressive autocorrelations (~0.2) explain no more than 4% of next period's change (R-squared)
- For taxable investor: **totally irrelevant** — advantage obliterated by capital gains from high-frequency trading
- For tax-sheltered allocator: **do not rebalance too frequently**
- If asset prices trend over long periods (months, 1-2 years), frequent rebalancing unfavorable
- Example: Japanese and U.S. markets both exhibited impressive momentum (opposite directions) since 1989 — rebalancing as little as possible from U.S. to Japan more advantageous
- **Practical rule: rebalance no more than once per year**

### Momentum and Mean Reversion: Two Sides of Same Coin

- Momentum investing and fixed-asset allocation with contrarian rebalancing = complementary
- Momentum in foreign/domestic equity classes results in periodic overvaluation and undervaluation
- Eventually long-term **mean reversion** corrects excesses
- **Eugene Fama** (2 decades ago): powerful case that security prices unpredictable
- **Burton Malkiel**: introduced "random walk" into popular investing lexicon
- In truly random-walk world, no advantage to portfolio rebalancing — you profit only when frogs turn into princes
- **Ken Fisher** calls this change in asset desirability resulting in short-term momentum and long-term mean reversion the "**Wall Street Waltz**"
- Understanding momentum's implications for rebalancing and asset behavior improves allocation skill

> "As much as it pains me to admit it, momentum exists."

2. Nobody can time the market
3. Because of 1 and 2, futile to select money managers based on past performance
4. Because of 1, 2, and 3, most rational way to invest in stocks: **low-cost passively managed vehicles (index funds)**

## Chapter 7: Odds and Ends

### Value Investing

- Can market segments outperform or underperform relative to their risk?
- Already shown: precious metals/collectibles (trifling return vs. very high risk); long-term bonds (same return as short-term but much higher risk)
- Already know company **size** predicts returns: small stocks outperform large (at cost of higher risk)
- Stocks outperform almost all assets long-run: buying a piece of growing economy
- Technological advances of 20th century created enormous wealth: air transport, radio, TV, automobiles, computers
- **Fatal investor extrapolation**: most profitable stocks must come from companies with hottest products — "**growth companies**"

### Three Valuation Measures

1. **P/E ratio**: price per $1 of earnings
   - P/E 30 = expensive; P/E 10 = cheap
   - Problem: earnings unstable, corporate accountants can "fiddle" with them
   - **Ben Graham**: earnings useful only when averaged over several years

2. **P/B ratio**: price relative to book value (net assets)
   - Example: ABC Airlines — assets $2B, liabilities $1B, outstanding stock $2B → P/B = 2
   - P/B < 1 = cheap; P/B > 5 = expensive
   - Book value very stable — accountants usually no need to fudge

3. **Dividend yield**: dividend ÷ stock price
   - High yield = cheap; low yield = expensive
   - Large, slowly growing companies historically paid >5%
   - Small/rapidly growing companies often pay no dividends

**Historical ranges (U.S. market, 1926-1998)**:
- P/E: typically 7-20, average ~14
- P/B: typically 1-3, average 1.6 (recently ballooned to ~8)
- Dividend yield: typically 2.5%-7%, average 4.5% (currently record low 1.3%)

Long-term returns usually higher when valuations cheap, lower when expensive — but practical usefulness questionable.

### Studies on Value Investing

**Paul Miller (Drexel & Co.), July 1936 - June 1964** — buying 10 lowest P/E stocks of Dow 30:

| Group | Annualized Return | SD | Losing Years | Losses >10% | Worst Year |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| All 30 Dow | +6.54% | 16.3% | 10 | 5 | -20.9% |
| Highest 10 P/E | +1.50% | 15.7% | 12 | 7 | -24.6% |
| **Lowest 10 P/E** | **+12.18%** | 21.1% | **7** | **2** | -30.5% |

- Returns are price change only (not including dividends, which would add ~5%)
- Higher SD of low-P/E stocks mostly due to very large gains in several years
- Low-P/E stocks **least risky** by total losing years and losses >10%

**Dow Dividend Strategy**: buy 5 highest-yielding Dow stocks
- Highest yielding (cheap): **Philip Morris**, **J.P. Morgan**, **Minnesota Mining**, **Chevron**, **Eastman Kodak** — "terrible" companies
- Lowest yielding (expensive): **Wal-Mart**, **McDonald's**, **IBM**, **Disney**, **American Express** — "good" companies
- The terrible ones outperform

**Fama and French** (*Journal of Finance*, June 1992):
- Stock returns July 1963 - Dec 1990
- All variation in return explained by just **two factors**: company size and P/B
- Cheapest tenth (lowest P/B): **19.6% annually**
- Most expensive tenth: **7.7% annually**
- Smallest cheapest stocks: **23% annually**
- P/E useful but **not nearly as useful as P/B** — after accounting for P/B, P/E had no predictive value

### Why Bad Companies Are Good Stocks

- Bad companies are cheap; management may turn things around; even if performance worsens, price probably won't drop much further
- Good companies are expensive; expected to "grow to the sky"; when they stop growing (inevitable), market takes them "out and shot"
- **David Dreman**: beautifully documented this phenomenon
  - Largest price movements occur when earnings greatly exceed or fall short of analysts' expectations
  - What really matters: whether earnings higher or lower than "Street" anticipates
  - Value stocks fall much less when earnings disappoint; rise more when earnings exceed expectations

> "Good companies are generally bad stocks, and bad companies are generally good stocks."

- **Tom Peters**, *In Search of Excellence* (1982): identified "excellent" companies using objective criteria
- **Michelle Clayman** (Oklahoma State): compared stock performance of "excellent" vs. "unexcellent" companies using same criteria
- Five years after book: **unexcellent companies outperformed excellent by 11% per year**
- Unexcellent companies considerably cheaper by P/E, P/B, and dividend criteria
- Psychologists call this error "**representativeness**"

Why value strategies still work decades after discovery: cheap companies are dogs — **most people cannot bring themselves to buy them**
- **Ben Graham** wrote *Security Analysis* 50 years ago
- Graham's disciples among most successful money managers: **Warren Buffett** is one of wealthiest men in world
- Everyone wants Amazon.com, Microsoft, Intel, AOL; no one wants Woolworth's

### Value vs. Growth: The Data

- **Robert A. Haugen** (*The New Finance: The Case Against Efficient Markets*):
  - Mid-1993: top 20% by P/E (growth) had P/E of 42.4, earnings yield 2.36%
  - Bottom 20% (value) had P/E of 11.93, earnings yield 8.38%
  - To come out ahead with growth, earnings must grow 3x larger than value — **they never even get close**
  - Each dollar invested in growth produces **less than half** the long-term earnings of value stocks

> "In the short term the markets are a voting machine, but in the long run they are a weighing machine. And what they are weighing is earnings." — Ben Graham

- **Josef Lakonishok** et al. (*Journal of Finance*, Dec 1994): confirmed low P/B and P/E superiority; also found **sales growth** affected returns — most rapidly growing companies had lowest returns
- **Earnings growth exhibits random-walk behavior** — this year's growth stock quite likely to become next year's value stock (at great cost to shareholders)

### Fama-French View vs. Lakonishok View

- **Fama and French** (devout efficient marketeers): higher value returns must be due to increased risk
  - Value stocks are "sick companies" — weak profitability, earnings growth, balance sheets
  - Economic downturn may bankrupt them; increased risk demands higher returns
  - If both Kmart and Wal-Mart offered same return, who would want Kmart?
- **Lakonishok** et al.: higher returns come **without higher risk** — present convincing evidence
  - **Wilshire Associates data** (Morningstar database, 1978+): for each company size, value portfolio returns several points more with **considerably lower** SD
  - During bull markets growth beats value; during bear markets value stocks lose much less
  - End of day: value stock returns may be superior simply because of more benign bear-market performance
  - Value stocks seem to outrank growth **precisely because they are less risky**

### The Three-Factor Model

**Fama-French framework** — any stock asset class earns four returns:
1. **Risk-free rate** (T-bill rate)
2. **Market-risk premium** (stock market exposure)
3. **Size premium** (small-company stocks)
4. **Value premium** (value stocks)

**Premiums (36-year data)**:
- Market premium: **5.65%** annualized, positive 78% of rolling 5-year periods
- Size premium: **1.71%**, positive only 53% of the time
- Value premium: **3.77%**, positive **87%** of the time (most consistent)

- Fama and French researched all three premiums extensively — proven across U.S. markets over long periods and in many other countries
- Likely fourth premium: **momentum** (nature of risk yet to be determined)
- Three-factor model useful for **evaluating money managers**: statistical techniques attribute returns to each risk factor vs. skill
- Most top-ranked managers' performance comes from **factor exposure**, not actual skill
- Balance factors with employment risk: employees of cyclical "value" companies should be wary of value portfolios (recession hits both job and portfolio); workers with recession-resistant jobs (**letter carriers**, **repo men**) better positioned for value stocks

### "New Era" Investing (Late 1990s)

- Stock valuations far higher than ever: dividend yields 1.3% (normally 3-7%), P/B 8 (normally 1-3), P/E ~30 (normally 10-20)
- Mathematics still matters: long-run equity return = dividend rate (1.3%) + earnings growth (5%) = **6.3%** expected
- No accelerating earnings trend visible in Dow earnings over 80 years
- 1830-1860 saw steam engine and telegraph (most transformative inventions in history), speed of cross-country transport increased by order of magnitude — U.S. equity return: only **4.2%**
- New-era arguments appeared before: 1926-1929 and late 1960s — both times old valuation methods declared obsolete

> "Seventy years ago few investors understood that excessive trading undermines profits... Americans have learned to buy and hold."

- **Ben Graham's** 1934 *Security Analysis* describes 1920s frenzy — just change "100 times earnings" to "100 times sales" for late 1990s parallel

**Recent asset-class returns (1989-1998)**:
- Barra Large Cap Growth: 21.35%; large value: 16.67%; 9-10 small-cap: 13.2%; EAFE: 5.54%
- Past decade convinced many: large beats small, growth beats value, domestic beats foreign
- More complete historical data shows **none of these assumptions is true**

**Kenneth French extreme quintile data (July 1927 - March 1998)**:
- Small value: **17.47%**; Small growth: **2.18%**
- Large value: **13.99%**; Large growth: **10.04%**
- Miserable small growth returns: author's theory is "**lottery ticket premium**" — investors accept poor returns for slim chance of finding next Microsoft

### Dow 36,000 Critique

- **James Glassman** and **Kevin Hassett** (*Dow 36,000*): Dow at 11,000 is "ridiculously undervalued," fair value about 36,000
- Based on **Discounted Dividend Model** (formulated 1938 by **John Burr Williams**):
  - PRP = div/(DR - g); Dow dividends ~$150, 6% growth assumption, 5.5% DR
  - Growth rate (6%) > DR (5.5%) = **infinite value**; lower growth to 5.1% → Dow 37,500
  - Formula: 150/(.055 - .051) = 150/.004 = 37,500
- Model "like balancing elephant on fence post" — small input changes produce massive swings
  - Move denominator numbers wrong way by 1% → Dow PRP of **6,250**
  - Slightly more optimistic → **Dow PRP of infinity**

**GH argument on risk**: stock market less risky than long Treasuries long-term
- Since 1926 worst 30-year annualized return: stocks 8.47%, Treasuries only 1.53%
- Short-term view: worst one-year: stocks -43.35%, bonds -7.78%

**GH argument on risk**: stock market actually less risky than long Treasury bond (long-term data)
- Since 1926 worst 30-year annualized return: stocks 8.47%, Treasuries only 1.53%
- But short-term view differs dramatically: worst one-year returns stocks -43.35%, bonds -7.78%
- October 19, 1987 got everyone's attention
- Controversy depends on whether investors experience risk as short-term or long-term phenomenon

**Evidence against GH claim that investors have lengthened risk horizons**:
- Trading volume steadily increasing; average mutual fund turnover up from 30% to 90%+
- West Coast discount brokerage survey: average annual portfolio turnover 75%
- Only 7% of mutual fund investments indexed
- Market declines of 1987, 1990, 1997 produced dramatic mutual fund outflows (not buy-and-hold behavior)
- **Shlomo Bentzarti and Richard Thaler** (*Quarterly Journal of Economics*, 1993): average investor risk horizon = **just one year**

**Redeemable bond thought experiment**: imagine 30-year Treasury that government can redeem at par anytime
- Would have higher price/lower yield (immunized against short-term shock)
- On "GH planet" where investors only care about long-term return, this would be priced identically to conventional 30-year bond
- The fact it wouldn't be tells us investors DO care about short-term risk

**Fundamental problem — DR = expected return**: if true DR is 5.5% and Dow correctly priced at 36,000, future stock return also 5.5%; real return ~3% — why invest when **TIPS** guarantee 4% real?
- At 5.5% DR, cost of capital is dirt-cheap — corporations won't be careful spending it (free-spending dot-coms not encouraging)
- At Dow 36,000: even 1% risk premium increase → Dow declines by **two-thirds**
- If GH right about correction past 36,000, subsequent stock ownership risks increase dramatically
- GH authors themselves hold ~20% bonds

**Paradigm shifts can happen**: 1958, first time stock yields fell below bond yields — universal outcry, stock market never looked back. Author wouldn't dismiss *Dow 36,000* out of hand, but **skepticism warranted**.

### Hedging: Currency Effects on Foreign Holdings

- Foreign holdings face intrinsic security risk + **currency fluctuation risk**
- Currency risk can be hedged via forward contracts in futures market
- **Hedged bond**: considerably lower risk than unhedged

**Foreign stocks more complicated than bonds** — 20 years ending Oct 1999:
- Hedged European stocks (MSCI-Europe) SD: 14.92%
- Local return = what European investor would see in native currency
- Past decades extremely turbulent in foreign exchange: monthly European currency returns SD 10.44%
- Dollar-denominated (unhedged) European SD: 16.25% (only slightly higher than hedged)
- Currency return uncorrelated with stock return — in short periods, hedging actually **increased** portfolio risk

**Empirical analysis (Nov 1979 - Oct 1999)** — three assets compared:
- Hedged MSCI-Europe: return 13.43%
- Unhedged MSCI-Europe: return 15.18% (currency return boosted)
- Domestic U.S. (CRSP 1-10): return 17.21% (1980s-1990s domestic much higher)
- During this period: hedging and foreign exposure both highly detrimental
- Best portfolio: almost exclusively domestic
- Adjusted analysis: hedged portfolios had both lower return AND lower risk than unhedged, but difference was very narrow (less than dozen basis points)
- Conclusion: hedging European stocks was roughly a wash in this period

**Forward premium mechanics**:
- Spot vs. forward contract rates (six-month forward):
  - Pound: spot $1.6239, forward $1.6245, premium 0.04%
  - Yen: spot $0.009758, forward $0.010052, premium 3.01%
  - Deutsche mark: spot $0.5219, forward $0.5288, premium 1.32%
- When you hedge: "sell short" forward rate, buy back later at spot
- If rates don't change: earn profit = forward premium
- In effect: being paid to hedge some currencies (present interest rates lower by forward premium)
- Unstable currency with high interest rates: forward premium highly negative = high hedging expense
- Forward rate seems predictive of future exchange rate — **it isn't**
  - Yen forward premium 3% over six months: says "don't worry about low yield, you'll make up difference with currency appreciation" — on average this doesn't happen
- Global bond managers made excess returns buying unhedged high-yield bonds of developed nations when currencies failed to depreciate as forecast
- Market inefficiency probably due to governments as major currency players (primary goal: currency defense, not profit)
- **Jeremy Siegel** (*Stocks for the Long Run*): 1910 pound worth $4.80, fallen to one-third; might think hedging would have increased returns — **wrong**. Almost all that period British rates higher than U.S., hedging costs were considerable — better off not hedging

**Hedging and short-term performance**: degree of hedging strongly affects short-term performance
- Don't be upset if fund has bad year because completely hedged with falling dollar (or vice versa)
- As long as funds stick to hedging policies, will be rewarded when currency pendulum swings (almost always does)

**Practical decisions**: all recommended foreign stock index funds are unhedged; only low-cost foreign bond funds are hedged. Cannot predict which approach will work in advance.

### Dynamic Asset Allocation

- **Dynamic asset allocation**: varying policy allocation because of changing market conditions
- Why relax discipline of fixed allocations? Isn't this tantamount to market timing?
- Adherence to fixed policy allocation with periodic rebalancing is **hard enough** — takes years to become comfortable, many lose nerve
- **Cannot pilot modern jet fighter before mastering trainer** — should not attempt dynamic allocation before mastering fixed
- Previous edition (1995): changing stock/bond allocation opposite direction of P/B produced slight improvement in risk-adjusted return — no longer true (P/B-sensitive investor would have completely exited stocks)

**P/B vs. 5-year forward returns**: although scatter exists, strong tendency for high returns at low starting P/Bs, low returns at high starting P/Bs
- Most remarkable: lower boundary forms quite straight line (minimum expected return for given P/B)
  - P/B 1.0 → ~13% return seems assured
  - P/B 1.25 → 7.5%
  - P/B 1.5 → ~2%
- Good reminder: when stocks get expensive, future returns likely to decline; when very cheap, likely generous
- **"Overbalancing"**: when you rebalance, you purchase more of an asset that declined (gotten cheaper); when you actually increase target weighting when price declines, simply rebalancing in more vigorous form
  - Increase target allocation very slightly (perhaps 0.1%) for every 1% asset falls in value (vice versa)
- **Examples**: two years ago 6% emerging markets appropriate, perhaps 8% not inappropriate with recent carnage; three years ago 40% S&P 500, perhaps 35% not bad idea in bubbly environment
- Most investors change allocations based on economic/political conditions — **poor approach**
- Author's opinion: changes purely market-valuation driven quite likely to increase return
- Rebalancing requires nerve and discipline; overbalancing requires even more — very few investors (small or institutional) can carry it off

### Behavioral Finance

- Overarching premise of book: rational investors faced with modern investing facts will make logical decisions
- **Problem: human beings are not rational**
- Past few decades: explosion in **behavioral finance** field — study of logical inconsistencies and foibles plaguing investors
- Three phenomena worth discussing: **overconfidence**, **recency**, **risk aversion myopia**

#### Overconfidence (Lake Wobegon Effect)

- **Garrison Keillor**, *A Prairie Home Companion*: "all the children are above average"
- **Greg Ip** (*Wall Street Journal*, Sept 14, 1998): after summer 1998 decline, investors' expected returns:

| | June 1998 | Sept 1998 |
|---|---|---|
| Own portfolio, next 12 months | 15.20% | 12.90% |
| Market overall, next 12 months | 13.40% | 10.50% |

- Average investor thinks they'll beat market by ~2% — mathematically impossible for the average
- 82% of U.S. drivers consider themselves in top 30% for safety
- 81% of new business owners think they'll succeed, but only 39% think peers will
- More complex the task, more inappropriately overconfident we are
- Longer feedback loop between actions and calibration = greater overconfidence
- Meteorologists, bridge players, ER physicians: generally well-calibrated; **most investors are not**

#### Recency

- Tendency to overweight recent data, underweight older (more comprehensive) data
- After prices fell summer 1998, investors expected **lower** returns — irrational since cheaper prices mean higher future returns
- Gold coin example: buy for $300, friend buys for $250; friend earns higher return — obvious, yet investors expect lower returns after price declines
- Explains why most investors are **"convex" traders** (term from **William Sharpe** and **Andre Perold**): buy as prices rise, sell as they fall
- **Sharpe and Perold** profound point: in world populated by convex traders, advantageous to be concave (vice versa)
- Because of recency, overwhelming majority of equity investors are convex → **rational investor is concave**
- Bond investors less subject to recency (falling bond prices make current yield more immediately attractive)

#### Risk Aversion Myopia

- **Risk aversion myopia**: overemphasis on possibility of short-term loss
- Human beings experience risk in short-term (as it should be for survival)
- State of nature: ancestors' ability to focus on risks of the moment had greater survival value than long-term strategic analytic ability
- Visceral obsession with here-and-now of rather less value in modern society, particularly investing
- After looking at long-term superiority of stocks, question is why anyone owns bonds at all — answer is risk aversion myopia

### Getting Started: Dollar Cost Averaging vs. Value Averaging

**Recommendation**: wade in slowly — takes time to accommodate ups and downs and convince yourself rebalancing is good

**DCA**: invest same amount regularly. Example: $100 invested 3x at $10, $5, $15
- Purchased 10 shares at $10, 20 shares at $5, 6.67 shares at $15 = 36.67 total shares
- Average cost $8.18/share — lower than simple average of $10 because more shares purchased at lower price
- DCA is not a free lunch: buying 20 shares at $5 took great fortitude — "point of maximum pessimism"
- Think of buying stocks October 1987, junk bonds January 1991, or emerging markets October 1998
- Real risk: entire buy-in during powerful bull market followed by prolonged drop

**Value Averaging** (**Michael Edleson**): superior method
- Instead of blindly adding $100/month, draw target value path increasing by $100/month
- Aim at $100 in account January, $200 February, etc.
- If fund declines, MORE than $100 required; if fund rises, less required; if fund rises greatly, nothing added
- Planning $3,600 over 3 years: may not complete in exactly 36 months
  - Markets generally up → may need 3-6-9 extra months
  - Bear market → will run out of money before 36 months

**VA advantages**:
1. Investor buys many more shares at low points than high points → higher returns
2. Gives experience investing during times of market pessimism and fear
3. Mandates larger amounts at market bottoms than tops (unlike DCA's fixed amount)
4. Can think of it as combination of DCA and rebalancing
5. Works in reverse: retirees sell more at tops, less at bottoms, stretching assets further

- No reason you must use DCA or VA — if well-acclimated to risk after years, can plunge right in
- Monthly funding over three years merely an example — can use quarterly, weekly, or even daily
- Recommend minimum 2-3 year funding period
- If market history is any guide, should have authentic bear market (or at least correction) during this time
- Will enable you to test resolve with relatively small mandated infusions
- Ultimately convince yourself of the value of rebalancing

**If taxable/tax-sheltered halves get out of kilter** (e.g., dramatic bull market in one asset while another falls):
- In tax-sheltered: can sell one fund and purchase another to rebalance
- In taxable: selling shares to rebalance has serious tax consequences — probably should be avoided
- Best approach: avoid reinvesting distributions as slow "safety valve" for overpriced assets

### Rebalancing

**Tax-sheltered**: can rebalance as often as you wish (no tax consequences)
- **Rebalancing bonus**: excess return from buying low and selling high that rebalancing forces — the only consistently effective method of market timing
- Optimal frequency hinges on finding interval where aggregate asset correlations lowest and annualized variances highest
- Asset variances and correlation coefficients differ depending on return intervals used (daily, weekly, monthly, quarterly, annually)
- Probably no way to predict optimal rebalancing period in advance for given portfolio
- **General rule**: long intervals preferred due to momentum phenomenon
  - Asset-class returns have slight tendency to trend — best to let run its course before rebalancing
- Example: U.S./Japan portfolio (former nearly straight up, latter nearly straight down since 1989) — rebalancing as rarely as possible (perhaps every 10 years) would have been best
- Rebalance every year or two and you won't go far wrong

**Taxable**: rebalance as sparingly as possible — good case for **never** rebalancing
- Selling triggers capital gains taxes at federal and state level
- Buying and selling taxable funds can be accounting nightmare (Vanguard and few others provide year-end share-tracking reports)
- Even most tax-efficient index funds have some year-end distributions — if fund exceeds policy target, avoid reinvesting distributions; take cash and redirect
- Fine to add frequently to taxable fund; sell at most once per year
- Accountant's opinion should definitely be sought

## Retirement: The Biggest Risk of All

### Duration Risk Explained

- **Duration**: time for reinvested income to overcome initial price shock from yield change
- Duration always < maturity for coupon-bearing bonds; = maturity for zero-coupon
- Technical definition: ratio of price-to-yield change (30-year bond decreases 10.96% per 1% yield increase)

**Examples**:
- 1-year T-bill at 5%: if yields rise to 10%, immediate 4.55% loss, but still gets full 5% holding to maturity; break-even at 1 year (= duration)
- 30-year 5% Treasury: if yields jump to 10%, drops to ~53 cents on dollar (happened 1967-1979); reinvesting coupons at higher yield → break-even in **10.96 years** (duration)

> "After 10.96 years, our unlucky bondholder is better off for the fall in price because of the rise in yield."

### Duration Applied to Stocks

- Stocks currently yielding ~1.3%; if stocks decline 75%, absolute dividend stays same but now reinvesting at yield 4x higher (5.2%)

**Duration scenarios at 1.3% stock yield**:
- 25% decline → 63-year duration
- 50% decline → 51 years
- 75% decline → 33 years
- 90% decline → only 19 years

**1929-1945 reality check**:
- $1 invested Labor Day 1929 → 16.6 cents by Independence Day 1932
- Recovered to par by end of January 1945 — less than 13 years after bottom
- Dividend yield 2.6% in Sept 1929; earnings growth only 1.8% for 30 years after
- Had crash not occurred, stocks would have returned 4.4%/year
- Break-even: January 1952 (22 years) — almost exactly duration model prediction

**Today's market more frightening than 1929**: 75% decline at 2.6% 1929 yield = 19 years duration; same decline at current 1.3% yield = **33 years duration**

> "The lower the yield, the higher the market price; the longer the duration, the greater the risk."

### Shortening Duration Through Contributions

- Monthly contributions effectively increase portfolio yield
- $10K initial with no additions + 75% decline at 1.3% yield: 33-year break-even
- Same + $200/month additions: break-even in **just over 11 years**
- Retirees face opposite problem: cannot shorten duration, can be devastated if duration > retirement horizon

### By Life Stage

- **Retired/living off savings**: neither enough time to clear duration hump nor ability to contribute
- **Boomer still saving**: likely plenty of time
- **Twenty-something**: get down on knees and pray for a market crash

Two types of investment pain: short-term (brutal bear markets) and long-term **shortfall risk** (running out of money before death).

### Retirement Calculations

- Assume $40K annual need beyond Social Security
- Simplify using **real** (inflation-adjusted) returns; reasonable estimate: ~4% real return
- Required savings: $40,000/0.04 = **$1,000,000** (maintain principal indefinitely)
- Willing to expire with zero: **$691,681** for 30 years (annuity calculation)
- 4% return is **market** return — fund expenses must be subtracted
- Typical 401(k) with 1-2% total expenses: may need up to 2x savings ($40K/0.02 = $2M)

> "2% of additional costs translates into a doubling of your retirement savings requirement."

### Sequence-of-Returns Risk

- Retirement calculators assume same return each year — reality varies dramatically
- **Order of good and bad years matters enormously**

**Uncle Fred's coin toss** (−10% or +30%): compounded avg 8.17% over 30 years, starting $1M:
- Alternating heads/tails: withdraw $81,700/year and keep $1M principal intact
- **15 straight tails first**: can withdraw only **$18,600/year**
- **15 straight heads first**: can withdraw **$248,600/year**
- In saving phase: much better off having bad years early, bull market later

### Trinity University Study

- **Philip L. Cooley, Carl M. Hubbard, Daniel T. Walz**: only **4-5% withdrawal rate** had reasonable expectation of success (dying without debt)

### 1966-1995 Stress Test

- Acid test: retiring Jan 1, 1966 through Dec 31, 1995
- 1966-1982: S&P 500 return 6.81% = identical to inflation → real return **zero** for 17 years
- 1983-1995: spectacular recovery, bringing 30-year real return to 5.4%
- Tested portfolio of 80% S&P + 20% U.S. small stocks mixed with 5-year Treasuries
- Various stock/bond mixes (100/0, 75/25, 50/50, 25/75, 0/100) at withdrawal rates of 4%, 5%, 6%, 7%
- All amounts in inflation-adjusted 1966 dollars
- All-stock portfolio = most volatile line; thinner lines = less stock
- At 7% withdrawal: all portfolios eventually run out
- At 4% withdrawal: most portfolios survive with significant remaining assets
- Higher bond allocation smoothed path but produced lower terminal wealth
