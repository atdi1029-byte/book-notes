## Chapter 15: What Killed Civic Engagement? Summing Up

### Suspect #1: Family Structure Change

**Evidence of family breakdown:**
- Century-long divorce rate increase (accelerated mid-1960s to mid-1970s, then leveled)
- Rise in single-parent families
- One-person households more than doubled since 1950 (rising widows living alone)
- **General Social Survey**: Married adults fell from 74% (1974) to 56% (1998)
- Adults with children at home fell from 55% to 38%
- **Census Bureau**: Married-with-kids households (archetypal Ozzie and Harriet) dropped from 40% (1970) to 26% (1997)

**Impact on civic engagement: Minimal.** Marriage and children do change social network types (more community organizations, less informal socializing), but only two organizational affiliations are strongly related to marital/parental status:
1. Church and youth-related activities
2. School affairs (PTA, Scouts, public meetings on school matters)

Parents more likely to volunteer than demographically matched singles/childless. Church/youth activities are the two most common volunteering sites in America.

**Activities NOT boosted by marriage/parenthood:**
- Marriage and children negatively correlated with sports, political, cultural, business/professional groups, service clubs, ethnic organizations, neighborhood associations, hobby groups
- Married people attend fewer club meetings than singles
- Married people slightly more likely to give/attend dinner parties, entertain at home, take active role in local organizations
- Married people less likely to spend time informally with friends and neighbors
- Marriage rate decline should have moved social activities from home to public settings
- No generic effects on civic engagement from marriage decline
- Interest in politics slightly higher among single/childless adults than married parents
- Having kids more important than marriage per se for local involvement (leadership, meetings, volunteering)—but the effect doesn't extend beyond school/youth activities

**Divorce and civic engagement:** Divorce negatively related to religious organizations but unrelated (positively or negatively) to other civic involvement. Divorced people (vs. never married) don't entertain friends less, volunteer less, attend club meetings less, work on fewer community projects. Actually sign slightly more petitions, attend more public meetings, write Congress more. Divorce itself not seriously implicated in the general civic disengagement trend.

### The Family-Religion Link

- Traditional family down, religious engagement down (a little)—but causal direction unclear
- Could be: family dissolution → lower religious involvement
- Could be: lower religious involvement → greater acceptance of divorce/non-traditional family forms
- Decline in family obligations should have freed up time for more social/community involvement

**Hypothetical scenario**: Statistically holding traditional family structure constant and giving extra weight to married people and parents:
- Might produce more religious participation
- Would produce more school and youth group involvement
- Volunteering would modestly increase
- **However**: No effect on secular organizations (Kiwanis to NAACP to AMA)
- No halt to decline in political activities (voting, party work)
- Would decrease time with friends and neighbors even more
- **Conclusion**: Apart from youth/church engagement, none of the major declines in social capital can be accounted for by traditional family structure decline. Important reasons for concern about erosion of traditional family values, but civic disengagement is not among them.

### Suspect #2: Race and the Civil Rights Revolution

Race fundamentally connected to American social history. Decline in social connectedness and trust began after greatest successes of civil rights revolution (1960s). Timing suggests possibility of civic "white flight"—legal desegregation led whites to withdraw from community associations. Controversial and cannot be settled definitively here.

**Three key facts:**

1. **Racial differences in associational membership not large until 1980s.** Controlling for education and income, blacks belonged to more associations than whites (more likely to belong to religious and ethnic organizations, no less likely for any other type). Chapter 8 showed racial differences in social trust very large even controlling for education, income, etc.—reflecting real experiences over many generations, not collective paranoia.

2. **Erosion of social capital affected all races.** Inconsistent with the "white flight" thesis. African Americans dropping out of religious/civic organizations at least as rapidly as whites. Sharpest drop in civic activity between 1970s and 1990s was among college-educated African Americans. Among whites, pace of disengagement uncorrelated with racial intolerance or support for segregation. Avowedly racist/segregationist whites no quicker to drop out than tolerant whites. Decline in group membership essentially identical among whites who favor segregation, oppose it, and blacks.

3. **Generational differences hard to reconcile with white flight theory.** Why should disengagement be hardly visible among Americans who came of age in the first half of the century (when society was objectively more segregated and subjectively more racist) than among those who came of age in the 1960s-1970s? If racial prejudice were responsible, disengagement should be especially pronounced among the most bigoted individuals and generations. But it's not.

**Conclusion**: Evidence not conclusive but shifts burden of proof. Reversing civil rights gains would do nothing to reverse social capital losses.

### Suspect #3: Big Government and the Welfare State

Timing of downturn in social connectedness suggests to some observers: the cause is big government "crowding out" private initiative.

**Some government policies destroyed social capital:**
- Slum clearance policies (1950s-1960s) replaced physical capital but destroyed social capital by disrupting existing community ties
- Certain social expenditures and tax policies may have created disincentives for civic-minded philanthropy

**However, harder to see which government policies are responsible for decline in** bowling leagues, family dinners, literary clubs.

**Empirical approach**: Among U.S. states, differences in social capital appear essentially uncorrelated with welfare spending or government size measures. Citizens in free-spending states no more engaged than in frugal ones. Cross-nationally, social trust and group membership positively correlated with government size—social capital highest in big-spending welfare states of Scandinavia. This simple analysis cannot tell us the causal direction, but it is not easily reconciled with the notion that government undermines social capital.

**Trends in American government size (Figure 78):**
- Only two things really changed: defense spending generally declined (1951-1998); state and local spending rose steadily (1947-1975)
- Two things have NOT really changed: size of federal domestic spending (averaged 2.2% of GNP late 1940s and late 1990s; 2.7% at peak mid-1960s); relative size of federal vs. state/local spending (last 25 years)
- Social capital increased a lot 1947-1965, decreased a lot 1965-1998
- Figure 78 seems inconsistent with any theory blaming decline on either big government or relative government size

### Suspect #4: Capitalism and the Market

Social critics long feared capitalism would undermine social ties (Georg Simmel, Karl Marx argued market capitalism created "cold society" devaluing human ties to mere commodities). Problem: this generic theory explains too much. America epitomized market capitalism for several centuries during which social capital went through great swings. **A constant can't explain a variable.**

**More valid version—corporate delocalization:** Gradual nationalization and globalization of economic structures. As Wal-Mart replaces the corner hardware store and Bank of America takes over First National Bank, incentives for business elites to contribute to community life atrophy.

**Charles Heying** showed how corporate delocalization in the last third of the 20th century stripped Atlanta of civic leadership—social cohesion and civic commitment rose from 1930s to peak in 1960s, then declined to 1990s. Very much the same trajectory as other measures of social capital. Suggestive evidence of similar trends in Chicago, Philadelphia, Dayton, Shreveport. One of Boston's top developers complained about the demise of "the Vault" (celebrated cabal of local business leaders):

> "Where are the power elite when you need them? They're all off at corporate headquarters in some other state."

Global economic transformations having important impact on community life, but the link is most direct regarding larger philanthropic and civic activities. Less clear why corporate delocalization should affect readiness to attend church socials, have friends over for poker, or even vote for president. Nevertheless, the connection is worth exploring.

## SECTION FOUR: So What?

## Chapter 16: Introduction

### The Civic Nostalgia Market

By virtually every conceivable measure, social capital eroded steadily and sometimes dramatically over the past two generations. Most Americans didn't need charts to know something bad was happening. On the eve of the millennium, market for civic nostalgia hotter than for blue-chip stocks.

**Examples:**
- Tom Brokaw's book profiling heroic WWII generation got mixed reviews but was a runaway best-seller
- In Los Angeles: movement to rename LAX after actor Jimmy Stewart (military hero who brought civic heroes Jefferson Smith and George Bailey to the silver screen)
- American nostalgia in the late 20th century: not run-of-the-mill rosy remembrance but an attempt to recapture when public-spiritedness really did carry more value and communities really did "work"

### How Social Capital Works

**1. Resolving Collective Action Problems:** People often might all be better off if they cooperate, with each doing her share. But each individual benefits more by shirking. If everyone thinks others will do the work, nobody participates and all are worse off. Social norms and the networks that enforce them provide the mechanism—these are "collective-action problems," "the prisoner's dilemma," "the free-rider problem," "the tragedy of the commons."

**2. Greasing the Wheels:** Where people are trusting and trustworthy, subject to repeated interactions with fellow citizens, everyday business and social transactions are less costly. No need to spend time and money making sure others will uphold their end. **Nobel Prize-winning economist Kenneth Arrow**:

> "Virtually every commercial transaction has within itself an element of trust, certainly any transaction conducted over a period of time. It can be plausibly argued that much of the economic backwardness in the world can be explained by a lack of mutual confidence."

**3. Widening Awareness of Interconnected Fates:** People with active and trusting connections develop or maintain character traits good for the rest of society. Joiners become more tolerant, less cynical, more empathetic. Without connections, people are unable to test the veracity of their views and more likely swayed by worst impulses. Not coincidental that random acts of violence tend to be committed by "loners."

**4. Information Conduits:** Networks serve as conduits for helpful information. Many Americans—perhaps most—get jobs through personal connections. If we lack social capital, our economic prospects are seriously reduced even if we have lots of talent and training.

**5. Psychological and Biological Processes:** People rich in social capital cope better with traumas and fight illness more effectively. Social capital appears to be a complement, if not substitute, for Prozac, sleeping pills, antacids, vitamin C:

> "Call me [or indeed almost anyone] in the morning" might actually be better medical advice than "Take two aspirin."

### Does Social Capital Really Matter?

**Yes** — impressive and growing body of research suggests civic connections help make us **healthy, wealthy, and wise.** Living without social capital is not easy — whether one is a villager in southern Italy, a poor person in an American inner city, or a well-heeled entrepreneur in a high-tech industrial district.

To believe social capital benefits individuals and communities, must first understand how it works its magic. High levels of trust and citizen participation operate through a variety of mechanisms to produce socially desirable outcomes. The mechanism(s) at work vary by circumstance and outcome.

### The PTA Example

Bob and Rosemary Smith want to improve their shambles of a public school. Their ability to start a PTA depends on: existence of other concerned parents, and likelihood the association will be effective. Social capital is the key: the more they know and trust neighbors, the greater their ability to recruit reliable members. In cohesive neighborhoods, individuals more easily learn who can be counted on and can make better use of moral suasion.

Once the PTA succeeds (seventeen active parents): belonging inculcates civic skills (designing projects, giving presentations, lobbying officials). PTA establishes norms of commitment. Connections strengthen norms of reciprocity and mutual concern. If Bob loses his job: fifteen adults for employment leads. If Rosemary starts a lobbying group: fifteen potential lobbyists. At minimum: another couple for Friday night movies. All gains—civic skills, social support, professional contacts, volunteer labor, moviegoing partners—arose because they wanted computers in their kid's school.

**Community connectedness not just about warm fuzzy tales of civic triumph.** In measurable and well-documented ways, social capital makes an enormous difference in our lives.

### Geographic Pattern of Social Capital

**Social Capital Index** combined fourteen indicators (Table 4) from variety of sources:

**Community organizational life**: Served on committee (r=0.88), served as officer (r=0.83), civic organizations per 1,000 pop. (r=0.78), club meetings attended (r=0.78), group memberships (r=0.74)

**Engagement in public affairs**: Presidential election turnout 1988/1992 (r=0.84), attended public meetings (r=0.77)

**Community volunteerism**: Nonprofits per 1,000 pop. (r=0.82), community projects (r=0.65), volunteer work (r=0.66)

**Informal sociability**: Visiting friends (r=0.73), entertaining at home (r=0.67)

**Social trust**: "Most people can be trusted" (r=0.92), "Most people are honest" (r=0.84)

These fourteen indicators sufficiently intercorrelated to tap a single underlying dimension. Differences among states substantial (ratios of roughly 3:1):
- Social trust: 17% (Mississippi) to 67% (North Dakota)
- Associational memberships per capita: 1.3 (Louisiana, North Carolina) to 3.3 (North Dakota)
- Presidential election turnout: 42% (South Carolina) to 69% (Minnesota)
- Nonprofits per 1,000: 1.2 (Mississippi) to 3.6 (Vermont)
- Club meetings per year: 4 (Nevada) to 11 (North and South Dakota)
- Volunteering rate: 5 times/year (Nevada, Mississippi, Louisiana) to twice that (Utah)
- Attended public meeting: 10% (Georgia, New York) to 32% (New Hampshire)

**Geographic pattern** (Figure 80): Primary high-pressure zone centered over headwaters of Mississippi and Missouri Rivers along Canadian border. Primary low-pressure area centered over Mississippi Delta through former Confederacy. California and mid-Atlantic near national average.

### Historical Roots: Tocqueville's Observation

**Tocqueville** observed precisely the same pattern in the 1830s:

> "As one goes farther south [from New England], one finds a less active municipal life; the township has fewer officials, rights, and duties.... Most of the immigrants who founded the northwestern states came from New England, and they brought the administrative habits of their old home to the new."

Interstate differences astonishingly similar to "state political culture" as drawn from the 1950s by **Daniel Elazar**, who traced them to 19th-century immigration patterns. One surprisingly strong predictor of social capital in any state in the 1990s: fraction of population of Scandinavian stock.

**Even more striking**: Spatial correlation between low social capital at end of 20th century and slavery in first half of 19th century. More virulent the system of slavery then, less civic the state today. **Slavery was a social system designed to destroy social capital** among slaves and between slaves and freemen. Well-established networks among the oppressed would have raised risk of rebellion. Egalitarian bonds of sympathy between slave and free would have undermined the system's legitimacy. After emancipation, dominant classes continued to inhibit horizontal social networks. Not happenstance that lowest levels of community-based social capital found where a century of plantation slavery was followed by a century of Jim Crow.

> **Inequality and social solidarity are deeply incompatible.**

If regional profiles represent long-standing traditions, then more plausible that social capital is cause, not merely effect, of contemporary social circumstance.

### Five Illustrative Fields

This section considers five illustrative fields in which social capital matters:
1. Child welfare and education
2. Healthy and productive neighborhoods
3. Economic prosperity
4. Health and happiness
5. Democratic citizenship and government performance

Evidence that social capital makes us smarter, healthier, safer, richer, and better able to govern a just and stable democracy. Evidence drawn from work of scholars in many disciplines, seeking analytic leverage by comparing differences across the fifty states. Evidence is neither exhaustive nor conclusive—scholars in medicine, criminology, economics, urban sociology, and state politics are actively exploring correlates and consequences. But the evidence shows that in measurable ways it matters that social capital has declined, and in many domains important to Americans, more social capital would improve things.

## Chapter 18: Safe and Productive Neighborhoods

### Criminology and "Social Disorganization" Theory

Scholars, especially criminologists, have long puzzled over why some neighborhoods have more vandalism, graffiti, street crime, gang scuffles—and why characteristics persist over decades despite population turnover. Beginning in the 1920s, criminologists developed "ecological" theories focused on "social disorganization": communities with high population turnover, anonymous neighbors, ethnically mixed groups, rare local organizations, disadvantaged youths trapped in subcultures cut off from the adult world.

**Robert J. Sampson** summarizing empirical studies: Even controlling for poverty and other factors, communities characterized by (a) anonymity and sparse acquaintanceship networks, (b) unsupervised teenage peer groups and attenuated control of public space, (c) weak organizational base and low social participation face increased risk of crime and violence. Example: living in a high-mobility neighborhood doubles risk of becoming a crime victim vs. a stable neighborhood.

### Jane Jacobs and Social Capital

**Jane Jacobs** (1961 classic): **"Social capital"** (a term she helped invent) differentiates safe from unsafe cities. Cities configured to maximize informal contact produce safer streets, better child care, happier residents. Regular contact with local grocers, families on front stoops, priests walking blocks, street fairs, conveniently traversed parks create a sense of continuity and responsibility:

> "The sum of such casual, public contact at a local level—most of it fortuitous, most of it associated with errands, all of it metered by the person concerned and not thrust upon him by anyone—is a feeling for the public identity of people, a web of public respect and trust, and a resource in time of personal and neighborhood need."

### State-Level Analysis: Homicide

Figure 84 shows states with more social capital have dramatically fewer murders. Inverse relationship "astonishingly strong—as close to perfect as one might find between any two social phenomena." Many reasons why high-social-capital states might have low homicide (wealthier, better educated, less urban, more egalitarian), but further analysis controlling for these factors: the relationship is real.

Social capital about as important as poverty, urbanism, racial composition. **More important than** education level, single-parent households, and income inequality in predicting murders (1980-95). Correlation remains strong when controlling for statewide levels of fear about crime—implies the causal arrow runs from social capital to crime.

### The Southern Violence Puzzle

Murder rates much higher in former Confederacy since before the Civil War—roughly 2x North during 1980s-90s. Regional distinction found among both whites and blacks. Many interpretations: psychological, cultural, societal. Regional difference persists even controlling for race, age, inequality, urbanization, education, poverty.

Competing explanations: "southern world view that defines the social, political, and physical environment as hostile" or "culture of honor" in dueling. But Figure 84 suggests social capital may be the missing link. Once differences in social capital are taken into account, the hoary regional difference vanishes. South is no more violent than expected given its well-established social-capital deficit. Explanation accounts for differences both between and within North and South. **Lethal violence is endemic wherever social capital is deficient.**

### Pugnacity

DDB Needham surveys: "I'd do better than average in a fist fight"—38% of Americans agree. Men 2x as likely as women (53% to 26%), but women slowly narrowed the gap. Nearly half of Louisiana, West Virginia, New Mexico residents agree vs. less than a third in South Dakota, Maine, Iowa, Minnesota, New Hampshire, Nebraska. Pugnacity strongly correlated with low social capital. Perhaps in the absence of community connectedness, a self-help system of enforcing social order has emerged.

### Neighborhood Effects

Studies use sophisticated techniques exploring "neighborhood effects" on child abuse, dropout rates, teen pregnancy, drug use. Unifying premise: behavior depends not only on own characteristics but on neighbors'. Despite criticism about clustering of like-minded people, the sheer number and diversity of studies finding neighborhood effects has been persuasive.

- **Boston**: Kids whose neighborhood peers use drugs, commit crimes, befriend gang members are substantially more likely to do so, regardless of initial proclivities
- **Chicago**: Young black men in neighborhoods with white-collar professionals 3x more likely to graduate high school than comparable young men in less educated neighborhoods
- "My fate depends on not only whether *I* study, stay off drugs, go to church, but also on whether *my neighbors* do these things"

**How it works**: Presence of social capital allows enforcement of positive standards, access to mentors, role models, educational sponsors, job contacts outside the neighborhood. Absence leaves kids to own devices—most likely to act on shortsighted or self-destructive impulses.

### The Inner-City Crisis

Many students of urban life have commented on the flight of jobs and middle-class families—drain of human, financial, and social capital.

**William Julius Wilson** (*The Truly Disadvantaged*, 1987):

> "The basic thesis is not that ghetto culture went unchecked following the removal of higher-income families in the inner city, but that the removal of these families made it more difficult to sustain the basic institutions in the inner city... And as the basic institutions declined, the social organization of inner-city neighborhoods likewise declined."

**Elijah Anderson** on Philadelphia: Steady erosion of "moral cohesion." Male "old heads"—"a man of stable means who was strongly committed to family life, to church, and, most important, to passing on his philosophy... to young boys he found worthy"—have lost prestige as legitimate jobs disappeared and illicit activity proved lucrative. Community "mothers" who once occupied porch stoops now "overwhelmed by a virtual proliferation of 'street kids'—children almost totally without parental supervision." These women no longer enjoy the informal permission they once had to intervene.

> "As family caretakers and role models disappear or decline in influence, and as unemployment and poverty become more persistent, the community, particularly its children, becomes vulnerable to a variety of social ills, including crime, drugs, family disorganization, generalized demoralization, and unemployment."

### Chicago Neighborhoods Study (Sampson, Raudenbush, and Earls)

Based on extensive survey and crime data: Two characteristics—**mutual trust and altruism among neighbors** and **willingness to intervene when children misbehave**—went a long way to explain why some neighborhoods were less crime-prone. A neighborhood's **"collective efficacy"** was a better predictor than poverty or residential instability of victimization risk.

**British Crime Data (Sampson and Groves)**: Areas where people connected through tight bonds of friendship and looser acquaintanceship ties, and active in local committees and clubs, have fewer muggings, assaults, burglaries, auto thefts. **Most interesting finding**: Poorer, less stable areas have higher crime rates not simply because of poverty per se. Rather, they have higher crime in large part because adults don't participate in community organizations, don't supervise teenagers, and aren't linked through networks of friends. **Young people rob and steal not only because they are poor, but also because adult networks and institutions have broken down.**

### Family Social Capital and Ripple Effects

Just as neighborhoods can affect families, families can affect neighborhoods. In economists' terms, family social capital has "positive externalities," spilling out of home and into streets. Northern California scholars: Presence of lots of stable families is associated with lower lawbreaking, not because adults serve as role models or supervisors, but because adults rear well-adjusted kids. "Good families" have a ripple effect by increasing the pool of "good peers." If youthful troublemaking is like a communicable disease (behavioral chicken pox spreading through high schools and friendship groups), stable families provide vaccines that reduce the number of contagious kids capable of infecting others.

**The integration paradox**: Integration of families into neighborhoods may not always be beneficial. If neighborhood norms are at odds with what Anderson calls "decent" values, then families who become enmeshed may run afoul of their own better natures. Northern California high school study: "parents knowing parents" was a strong indicator of child's classroom engagement and refusal to use drugs—but only where engagement and substance abuse were not already problems. In troubled areas, social integration of parents actually exacerbated the problems of living with poorly adjusted peers. **In other words, social integration into a community of bad actors may not produce good results.**

### Gangs as Misguided Social Capital

In settings lacking mainstream organizations, youths create their own social capital as gangs. **Ruth Horowitz** on Latino gangs in Chicago:

> "Whoever has cash pays for the beer; no questions are asked, no accounts are recorded. The same is true of small loans and meals. The more serious mutual obligations... are often continued over an extended period."

Gang life involves reciprocal obligations and norms that serve members' interests—similar in structure to bowling teams. Former Los Angeles gang member **Sanyika Shakur**: "If you leave and are successful, you're obligated. You have double indemnity: you have the cultural obligation, and you have the 'hood obligation... the cultural obligation: If you don't come back and contribute, you'll get your 'guild pass' revoked. Then you have the responsibility for the 'hood thing, which can get you murdered if you don't come back."

Some gangs are hierarchical drug enterprises; others closer to mutual aid societies based on horizontal bonds defended to the death. External effects typically harmful to bystanders. Gangs serve as social institutions where mainstream organizations are absent or debilitated—used by ward politicians for organizing, crime syndicates for entry-level employees, community groups for volunteer labor/money/protection.

**Washington, D.C. case**: Women activists in public housing used drug gangs as benefactors—the gang provided money for children's programs, and activists reciprocated by ensuring organizing proceeded without street interference. Where constructive social capital and institutions wither, gangs emerge to fill the void.

**Carol Stack's** *All Our Kin* (1960s): Classic study of black families in "the Flats"—"richly textured depiction of a wealth of social capital among the struggling poor." Dense exchange networks of goods, resources, child care among kin and non-kin. High trust required—gifts rarely repaid immediately. Members monitored each other for shirking. Recent studies show these networks less dense than Stack found, but reciprocity systems persist as an important, often overlooked asset.

### Community Policing

Police departments in the 1980s-90s implemented "applied social capitalism." **Chicago CAPS** (Wesley Skogan and Susan Harnett) created uniform opportunities for participation. **Boston's 10-Point Coalition**: Only local ministers had the connections and trust to broker working partnerships between police and public. Crime drops in 1990s big cities may be partly due to residents learning to capitalize on local social capital stocks.

### Social Capital and Disadvantaged Areas

Social capital is "far more often than not" a good thing for disadvantaged neighbors. Where lacking, effects of poverty, unemployment, and family breakdown are magnified. Preliminary evidence: social trust, organizational participation, and neighborhood cohesion help break the link between economic disadvantage and teenage troublemaking.

**The vicious circle problem**: "Neighborhood crime watch" programs most likely to succeed in middle-class, stable neighborhoods that already benefit from trust and networks. This creates a vicious circle: low trust/cohesion → higher crime → even lower trust/cohesion. Inner cities need the virtuous circle (existing social capital facilitates more) but face the vicious circle instead. Social-capital-intensive strategies may help "unwind" the negative spiral but are challenging.

### Why Social Capital Matters More for the Poor

Poor people lack economic and human capital, making social capital disproportionately important. Erosion affected affluent suburbs and inner-city Detroit equally in degree, but impact was greater in the inner city, which lacks cushioning of other capital forms. School shootings in suburban/rural communities: reminder that breakdown of community in privileged settings means affluence and education alone are insufficient. Had the "culture of suburbia" and middle-class white communities attracted equal scholarly attention, we could draw a more balanced assessment.

## Chapter 19: Economic Prosperity

### Social Capital and Employment

People from well-to-do families with valuable social ties succeed not just because richer/better educated, but because they can ply their connections. Conversely, individuals from socially isolated areas are held back by lack of social ties for a "hand up."

**Mark Granovetter's "Strength of Weak Ties" (1970s):** Counterintuitive: casual acquaintances more important than close friends/family for job searches. Close ties ("strong ties") likely know the same people and opportunities. Distant acquaintances ("weak ties") link to unexpected opportunities. Finding replicated and expanded by researchers interested in social mobility. Weak ties have especially strong impact on people at margins of mainstream institutions.

**Ethnic immigrant communities**: Employers rely on employees to recruit/train new workers. Social-capital approach speeds training, improves morale, enhances loyalty. Chinese "rag trade" in New York: solidaristic hiring practices boosted immigrants' wages to level of similarly skilled whites. Immigrant networks provide financing via **rotating credit associations** (members make regular contributions to common fund available to each in rotation)—widespread where formal credit institutions unwilling/unable to provide credit to small borrowers.

**Korean business owners**: ~70% used debt financing; 41% from family, 24% from friends (vs. 37% from financial institutions).

**Broader evidence**: 85% of young men in one survey used personal networks to find employment (vs. 54-58% using state agencies/newspapers). Los Angeles: two-thirds of white and black women landed positions with help of someone in the firm—most help came from people outside their own neighborhoods. Roughly half of Americans get jobs through a friend or relative.

**Church attendance**: One of the strongest predictors of whether inner-city black youths become gainfully employed—religious beliefs have almost no impact, suggesting it's the social networking aspect, not the religious aspect, behind economic success.

### Beyond Employment

**Ronald Burt**: Social and organizational ties in a business executive's Rolodex at least as important as educational qualifications. Studies from Albany to Singapore, Dresden to Detroit: at all levels of social hierarchy and all parts of economy, social capital is a powerful resource for occupational advancement, social status, economic rewards—**perhaps even more important than human capital** (education and experience).

**Brian Uzzi** (Chicago banking): Firms that embed commercial transactions with lenders in social attachments receive lower interest rates on loans.

**Buying and selling**: We prefer to deal with people we know. **DiMaggio and Louch**: "People who transact with friends and relatives report greater satisfaction with results than people who transact with strangers."

Social capital matters because our networks connect us to potential economic partners, provide high-quality information, and vouch for us. For many white-collar jobs, **connections = access** to other people and institutions—which is what actually gets us hired. Social networks have undeniable monetary value.

### The Problem: Social Capital Absent Where Needed Most

**Chicago** (Wilson): Blacks in extreme poverty substantially less likely to have a current partner or best friend. Those they have are less likely to have completed high school or have steady work. Lower volumes of social capital.

**Red Hook, Brooklyn**: Deterioration of neighborhood associations inhibited growth of social networks just as employers were making most hires through "word of mouth."

**Los Angeles County**: Neighborhood poverty kept workers' wages down not because they lacked transportation to well-paying jobs, but because they lacked access to networks that could tell them about good opportunities. Social contacts extremely lucrative in poverty.

**Atlanta study**: Each employed person in one's social network increases annual income by $1,400. Networks more lucrative for whites. Blacks who gain job information from contacts outside their neighborhood earn more than those relying on neighbors—suggests **bridging social capital may be the most lucrative form** for the disadvantaged.

### Potential Downsides

Scholars of ethnic "niche" economies questioned whether tight bonds might restrict growth and mobility. Ethnic enclaves provide start-up capital and customers, but pressures of solidarity can drag down individuals and businesses that succeed "too much" or try to expand beyond ethnically based markets. Less successful members sometimes take excessive advantage of bonds of obligation felt by more successful ones—fast-rising entrepreneurs face excessive demands for jobs, money, favors from struggling family and neighbors. To realize full potential, entrepreneurs may need to reach beyond their own groups and forge ties to the broader world—customers, financial institutions, civic associations. Where social capital is not productive, it must be sought elsewhere.

Tight networks may also be exploited commercially. **Amway and similar businesses** rely on quasi-independent agents who call upon friends/neighbors to buy and sell products—some view this as anathema to the tacit norms of reciprocity and altruism governing good social relations. These exceptions aside, most researchers agree social capital helps individuals prosper. The real debate: how big a role it plays relative to human or financial capital.

### Housing Values and Neighborhood Stability

**Pittsburgh study**: Other things equal, neighborhoods with high social capital far less likely to decline than low-social-capital areas. Areas where residents vote, sustain vibrant neighborhood associations, feel attached to the neighborhood, see it as a good place to live → other people want to move in, housing values remain comparatively high. Positive impact held even after accounting for proximity to downtown, racial composition, residents' socioeconomic status. **Homeowners who are also good neighbors take their social capital to the bank.**

### Social Capital and Neighborhood Economic Health

Social capital can help neighborhoods (and even entire nations) create wealth. It happens in many different ways.

**Housing Values**: Pittsburgh study: Other things equal, neighborhoods with high social capital far less likely to decline than low-social-capital areas. Areas where residents vote, sustain vibrant neighborhood associations, feel attached to the neighborhood → other people want to move in, housing values remain comparatively high. Positive impact held even after accounting for proximity to downtown, racial composition, residents' socioeconomic status. **Homeowners who are also good neighbors take their social capital to the bank.**

### Regional Economic Growth

At local/regional level, mounting evidence that social capital among economic actors can produce aggregate economic growth. Not saying more bowling leagues and PTAs will necessarily cause the town economy to prosper. Saying: under certain conditions, cooperation among economic actors might be a better engine of growth than free-market competition.

**Case Study 1: Tupelo, Mississippi.** In 1940, one of poorest counties in poorest state—no exceptional natural resources, university, or highways. 1936: ravaged by fourth deadliest tornado in U.S. history. 1937: only significant factory closed after divisive strike. **George McLean** (university-trained sociologist and native son) returned to run the local newspaper. Through exceptional leadership, united business and civic leaders around the idea: the town and Lee County would never develop economically until they had developed as a community.

McLean persuaded local business leaders and farmers to pool money to buy a siring bull—start of a lucrative dairy industry. To create a less hierarchical social order: the town's elite Chamber of Commerce was disbanded and **Community Development Foundation** was opened to everyone. Set to work improving local schools, starting community organizations, building a medical center, establishing a vocational education center. Businesses welcomed only if they paid high wages to all employees and shared this as a goal. Rural Development Councils set up in outlying areas to encourage self-help collective action.

Over the next fifty years, Tupelo became a national model of community and economic development. Since 1983: Lee County added 1,000 industrial jobs/year, garnered hundreds of millions in new investment, produced arguably the best school system in Mississippi, constructed a world-class hospital, kept unemployment and poverty rates well below state (sometimes national) averages. Community's success based on unwavering commitment: citizens would not benefit individually unless they pursued goals collectively. Unthinkable today to enjoy social prominence in Tupelo without also getting involved in community leadership.

**Case Study 2: Silicon Valley vs. Route 128.** Silicon Valley's success due to horizontal networks of informal and formal cooperation among nominally competing companies. Leaders shared information, problem-solving techniques, and beers after work. Developed trade associations, conferences, and **Homebrew Computer Club** (hobbyists' group whose ranks produced leaders of 20+ computer companies). High job turnover reinforced the value of personal relationships—"a colleague might become a customer or competitor; today's boss could be tomorrow's subordinate." When economic condition slipped in early 1990s, businesses created **Joint Venture: Silicon Valley** under the San Jose Chamber of Commerce—a nonprofit networking organization enhancing public-private cooperation.

Route 128 did NOT develop such interfirm social capital. Maintained traditional norms of corporate hierarchy, secrecy, self-sufficiency, territoriality. Employees rarely went out after work with one another or with people from other firms. Route 128's "I'll succeed on my own" philosophy is largely responsible for its poor economic performance relative to Silicon Valley's.

> "The contrasting experience of Silicon Valley and Route 128 suggests that industrial systems built on regional networks are more flexible and technologically dynamic than those in which experimentation and learning are confined to individual firms."

**Francis Fukuyama**: Economies with high social trust will dominate the 21st century. When we can't trust employees or market players, we squander wealth on surveillance, compliance, insurance, legal services. **Walter Powell and Jane Fountain**: Social networks are "key enablers" of innovation, as important as physical and human capital, particularly in rapidly evolving fields.

### Research Caveats

Understanding linkages between social capital and economic performance is a lively field. Would be premature to claim too much or describe exactly when networks boost aggregate productivity. Research on social capital and development in the Third World appearing rapidly (South Africa, Indonesia, Russia, India, Burkina Faso). Rich work under way on how Americans might improve poorest communities by enabling investment in social capital.

Links at the individual level are understood—you will benefit from a richer social network. Not yet entirely clear whether this merely reflects grabbing a larger share of a fixed pie, or whether if we all have richer networks, we all gain. Early returns encourage the view that social capital of the right sort boosts economic efficiency.

## Chapter 20: Health and Happiness

### Social Capital and Health: Overview

Of all domains, importance of social connectedness best established in health and well-being. **Emile Durkheim** (*Suicide*): Self-destruction is a sociologically predictable consequence of degree of integration—rarer among married people, in tight-knit religious communities, in times of national unity, more frequent when rapid change disrupts the social fabric.

Dozens of studies from Alameda (California) to Tecumseh (Michigan) established beyond reasonable doubt that **social connectedness is one of the most powerful determinants of our well-being.** The more integrated we are, the less likely we experience colds, heart attacks, strokes, cancer, depression, premature death. Protective effects confirmed for close family ties, friendship networks, participation in social events, and affiliation with civic associations. Both "machers" and "schmoozers" enjoy remarkable health benefits.

**James House** and colleagues: **Positive contributions to health made by social integration rival in strength the detrimental contributions of well-established biomedical risk factors** like cigarette smoking, obesity, elevated blood pressure, and physical inactivity. Evidence for health consequences of connectedness is as strong today as was evidence for health consequences of smoking at time of the first surgeon general's report. If trends in social disconnection are as pervasive as argued, "bowling alone" represents one of the nation's most serious public health challenges.

### Five Mechanisms

1. **Tangible assistance**: Social networks furnish money, convalescent care, transportation—reducing psychic and physical stress
2. **Safety net**: If you go to church regularly and slip in the bathtub and miss a Sunday, someone more likely to notice
3. **Reinforcing healthy norms**: Socially isolated people more likely to smoke, drink, overeat, engage in health-damaging behaviors
4. **Political organization**: Cohesive communities best able to organize politically to ensure first-rate medical services
5. **Physiological triggering**: Social isolation has measurable biochemical effects—isolated animals develop more extensive atherosclerosis (hardening of arteries); among both animals and humans, loneliness decreases immune response and increases blood pressure

**Lisa Berkman** (leading researcher): Social isolation is "a chronically stressful condition to which the organism responds by aging faster."

### Evidence: Individual and Community Level

Studies documented strong correlations at community level. Others zeroed in on individuals in natural settings and experimental conditions, carefully accounting for confounding factors. Many studies are longitudinal—checking on people over years. Researchers showed social isolation *precedes* illness, ruling out the possibility that isolation caused the illness.

Over the last twenty years, more than a dozen large studies in the US, Scandinavia, and Japan: **People who are socially disconnected are between two and five times more likely to die from all causes** compared with matched individuals who have close ties with family, friends, and community.

**Harvard School of Public Health Study** (170,000 individuals, all fifty states): Found (as expected) that African Americans, uninsured, overweight, smokers, low income, and less educated are at greater risk. Also found **astonishingly strong relationship between poor health and low social capital.** Moving from a high-social-capital state to one with very little increased chances of poor health by roughly 40-70%. Researchers concluded: **moving to a high-social-capital state would do almost as much good as quitting smoking.**

### Roseto, Pennsylvania

Small Italian American community, subject of nearly forty years of in-depth study beginning in the 1950s. Medical researchers noticed a happy but puzzling phenomenon: Compared with residents of neighboring towns, Rosetans just didn't die of heart attacks. Age-adjusted heart attack rate less than half that of neighbors; over seven years not a single resident under 47 died of heart attack.

Researchers looked for usual explanations: diet, exercise, weight, smoking, genetic predisposition—none held the answer. Indeed, Rosetans actually more likely to have some risk factors than people in neighboring towns.

Researchers began to explore Roseto's social dynamics. Town founded in the 19th century by people from the same southern Italian village. Through local leadership, immigrants created mutual aid society, churches, sports clubs, labor union, newspaper, Scout troops, park and athletic field. Residents developed a tight-knit community where conspicuous displays of wealth were scorned and family values and good behaviors reinforced. Rosetans learned to draw on one another for financial, emotional, and other forms of support. By day they congregated on front porches to watch comings and goings; by night they gravitated to local social clubs.

In the 1960s, researchers began to suspect **social capital (though they didn't use the term) was key to Rosetans' healthy hearts.** They worried that as socially mobile young people rejected tight-knit Italian folkways, heart attack rates would rise. Sure enough, by the 1980s Roseto's new generation of adults had a heart attack rate above that of neighbors in a nearby and demographically similar town.

Roseto is particularly vivid and compelling, but numerous other studies supported the intuition that social cohesion matters—not just in preventing premature death, but also in preventing disease and speeding recovery. Long-term California study: people with fewest social ties have highest risk of dying from heart disease, circulatory problems, and cancer (in women), even after accounting for health status, socioeconomic factors, and use of preventive care. Other studies linked lower death rates with voluntary group membership, cultural activities, church attendance, phone calls and visits with friends, and general sociability—holding parties, attending union meetings, participating in sports, being in cohesive military units. Connection persisted even controlling for social class, race, gender, smoking, obesity, lack of exercise. Not simply that healthy, privileged people happen to be more engaged and live longer. The broad range of illnesses affected and the fact that the link is tighter with death than with sickness suggest the effect operates at a fundamental level of general bodily resistance.

### The Bottom Line

> **As rough rule of thumb: if you belong to no groups but decide to join one, you cut your risk of dying over the next year in half.** If you smoke and belong to no groups, it's a toss-up statistically whether you should stop smoking or start joining. These findings are heartening—it's easier to join a group than to lose weight, exercise regularly, or quit smoking.

### Individual Health and Social Networks

**Carnegie Mellon University**: People with more diverse social ties get fewer colds—by no means a unique finding. Stroke victims with strong support networks functioned better after the stroke and recovered more physical capacities than those with thin networks. Older people involved with clubs, volunteer work, and local politics consider themselves in better general health than uninvolved people, even after accounting for socioeconomic status, demographics, level of medical care use, and years of retirement.

**What these studies tell us**: Social engagement actually has an independent influence on how long we live. Not simply that healthy, health-conscious, privileged people (who might happen to be more socially engaged) tend to live longer. The broad range of illnesses shown to be affected by social support, and the fact that the link is even tighter with death than with sickness, tend to suggest that the effect operates at quite a fundamental level of general bodily resistance.

### Declining Self-Reported Health

Same period witnessed significant decline in self-reported health, despite tremendous gains in medical diagnosis and treatment. By many objective measures (including life expectancy), Americans are healthier than ever before. But self-reports indicate we are feeling worse. Self-reports closely linked to social connectedness—precisely less connected Americans who are feeling worse. Facts alone do not prove we are suffering physically from our growing disconnectedness, but taken with more systematic evidence, this is another link in the argument.

### Depression and Social Connectedness

In any given year 10% of Americans suffer from major depression—fourth largest disease burden. Low levels of social support directly predict depression, even controlling for other risk factors. High levels speed recovery. Social support buffers us from daily stresses. Face-to-face ties more therapeutic than geographically distant ones. Same decades that social connectedness declined, depression and suicide increased—with deep generational roots.

### Life Satisfaction and Happiness

People with close friends and confidants, friendly neighbors, supportive coworkers less likely to experience sadness, loneliness, low self-esteem. Married people consistently happier than unattached, all else equal. In study after study, people report that good relationships—far more than money or fame—are prerequisites for happiness.

> **Single most common finding from half a century's research on correlates of life satisfaction, not only in the US but around the world: happiness is best predicted by breadth and depth of one's social connections.**

**Marriage**: Controlling for education, age, gender, income, and civic engagement, marriage's effect on contentment equals moving ~70 percentiles up the income hierarchy (15th to 85th). "Getting married is the 'happiness equivalent' of quadrupling your annual income."

**Education**: Four additional years = happiness equivalent of roughly doubling annual income.

**Civic connections**: Regular club attendance, volunteering, entertaining, or churchgoing = happiness equivalent of getting a college degree or more than doubling income.

> "Civic connections rival marriage and affluence as predictors of life happiness."

**Diminishing returns** (Figure 88): Biggest happiness gains between "never" and "once a month." Little gain after one meeting per three weeks; after fortnightly encounters, additional interaction shows negative correlation. Church attendance the exception—more frequent = higher happiness through weekly.

### Happiness Decline Since the 1960s

Since the Fab Four era, life satisfaction among adult Americans declined steadily. Half the decline = financial worries; half = declines in social capital (lower marriage rates, decreasing connectedness). Slump greatest among young and middle-aged adults (20-55). People over 55 actually happier than their age cohort a generation ago.

**Martin Seligman's diagnosis**: Modern society encourages belief in personal control and autonomy over commitment to duty and communal enterprise. This heightens expectations, leaving people unprepared for life's inevitable failures. Social capital safety nets (families, churches, friends) no longer strong enough to cushion the fall.

> "In our personal lives as well as in our collective life, we are paying a significant price for a quarter century's disengagement from one another."

## Chapter 21: Democracy

### Historical Foundations

**Oscar Wilde**: "The trouble with socialism is that it would take too many evenings." Central question: How many evenings does liberal democracy take?

Two views of democratic citizenship: (1) performing public duties (voting), (2) widespread participation in private voluntary groups. **Thomas Jefferson** (1816): Proposed ward republics small enough for every citizen to attend meetings in person—handling schools, poor relief, police, roads.

> "Making every citizen an acting member of the government... will attach him by his strongest feelings to the independence of his country."

**Tocqueville**: Local civic activity served as handmaiden of national democratic community:

> "If it is proposed to make a road cross the end of his estate, he will see at a glance that there is a connection between the small public affair and his greatest private affairs; and he will discover... the close tie that unites private to general interest."

**John Stuart Mill**: Without shared participation, citizens think of collective interests only in competition; a neighbor becomes "only a rival." The engaged citizen "is made to feel himself one of the public, and whatever is for their benefit to be for his benefit."

**John Dewey**: "Democracy must begin at home, and its home is the neighborly community." **Robert Westbrook**: "Only in local, face-to-face associations could members of a public participate in dialogues with their fellows."

### The Founders' Skepticism

**James Madison**: Called groups organized around particular interests "mischiefs of faction." Fear: elected representatives would sacrifice the common good for pet projects of the few. Must be tolerated for liberty but controlled. **Michael Schudson**: Founders "were far from sharing a pluralist vision, still attached as they were to the notions of consensus, property, virtue, and deference that came naturally to them."

### How Associations Serve Democracy

**External effects**: Express interests, protect from abuses of power. Political information flows through social networks. **Tocqueville**: "When some view is represented by an association, it must take clearer and more precise shape." Without associations, individuals have limited ability to be heard unless rich or famous (**Amy Gutmann**). East Germany democracy movement: recruitment through friendship networks; informal bonds more important than ideology.

**Internal effects—schools for democracy**: Members learn to run meetings, speak in public, write letters, organize projects, debate issues. **William Muraskin** on Prince Hall Masonry: training in leadership roles, bourgeois social roles, bringing leadership potential to fruition.

For working-class Americans, voluntary associations and churches offer the best civic skill-building opportunities—even better than the workplace. Two-thirds+ of members in religious, literary, youth, fraternal/service organizations exercise civic skills like giving presentations or running meetings.

**Churches' egalitarian role**: One of the few vital institutions where low-income, minority, disadvantaged citizens can learn politically relevant skills and be recruited into political action. Without them, class bias in politics would be much greater.

### Deliberative Democracy

Recent theoretical attention to the promise and pitfalls of "deliberative democracy." Some argue voluntary associations best enhance deliberation when they are microcosms of the nation (economically, ethnically, religiously). Others argue even homogeneous organizations enhance deliberative democracy by making public interactions more inclusive—e.g., minority groups pushing for nondiscrimination regulations, ethnic curricula, government board representation.

### Civic Virtues Learned Through Association

**Follow-up study of high school seniors**: Regardless of social class or academic background, those in voluntary associations far more likely to vote, participate in campaigns, discuss public issues two years post-graduation.

**Trustworthiness**: Repeated interactions reduce likelihood of shirking or cheating. **Reciprocity**: As shown in Chapter 7, more civic engagement leads to more concern for the generalized other (volunteering, blood donation, charity).

**Democratic deliberation**: Reciprocity = willingness of opposing sides to agree on ground rules for mutual accommodation after sufficient discussion, even when they don't agree on outcomes. Regular connections don't ensure empathy, but social isolation virtually guarantees its absence.

### Critics of Pluralism

**Three major concerns:**

1. **Interest groups distort decision-making**: From Theodore Lowe's *End of Liberalism* (1960s) to Jonathan Rauch's *Demosclerosis* (1990s)—specialized lobbies paralyze officials, stifle reforms. Reminiscent of Madison's "mischiefs of faction." Bargaining leads to greatest goodies for best-organized few.

2. **Associational bias favors the privileged**: People with education, money, status far more likely to benefit. Social capital benefits those who already have stock to trade. Virtually every study confirms this class bias.

> "The flaw in the pluralist heaven is that the heavenly chorus sings with a strong upper-class accent." — **E.E. Schattschneider**

3. **Political polarization**: Americans at political poles more engaged; moderates have dropped out. Data on ideological self-identification: Self-described middle-of-the-roaders ~one-half as likely to participate in public meetings, local organizations, political parties as in the mid-1970s. "Moderate" liberals/conservatives declined by one-third. Declines smallest among "very" liberal or conservative. **Civic disengagement exacerbates the "faction" problem**: More moderate voices have fallen silent.

### Psychic Engagement and Political Discussion

Social capital's role in politics: most political discussions happen informally—dinner table, office water cooler. We form views through interchange with friends and neighbors in civic networks.

**Cathy J. Cohen and Michael C. Dawson**: Informal networks not available to everyone. African Americans in poverty clusters suffer from dearth of political information and opportunity. Detroit study: even non-destitute residents in high-poverty neighborhoods far less likely to attend church, belong to voluntary organizations, attend public meetings, talk politics than similar people in advantaged neighborhoods. Inner-city neighborhoods often lack institutions to mobilize citizens.

**Vicious cycle**: People don't participate because they're not mobilized; not mobilized, they can never savor the fruits of participation.

### Citizenship by Proxy?

**James T. Hamilton study**: Neighborhoods where people owned homes and voted were (holding other factors constant) less likely to get hazardous waste plants than neighborhoods where people rented and rarely voted. Hazardous waste companies locate where they expect least locally organized opposition. Civic disengagement undermines neighborhood empowerment—and vice versa.

**Michael Schudson**: Large national organizations (AARP, Audubon, NAACP) can represent interests efficiently—"This is not Tocquevillian democracy," but citizens may get the same civic payoff for less hassle.

**Theda Skocpol's counterargument**: In classic civic America, ordinary people could interact alongside the privileged, exercise influence. Recent times: old civic America bypassed by professionally dominated advocacy groups rarely attached to real memberships.

> "Ideas of shared citizenship and possibilities for democratic leverage have been compromised in the process."

**Peter Skerry**: National organizations dominated by headquarters staff pulled toward wishes of major patrons (foundations, government agencies). Rely on media strategies emphasizing threats from "enemies"—giving us politics of posturing and confrontation rather than reasoned debate.

### Social Capital and Government Performance

**Italian Regional Government Study**: Beginning 1970, Italians established a nationwide set of powerful regional governments. Twenty new institutions virtually identical in form, but social, economic, political, cultural contexts differed dramatically (preindustrial to postindustrial, Catholic to Communist, feudal to modern). Like a botanist studying genetically identical seeds in different plots.

**Outcomes**: Some dismal failures (inefficient, lethargic, corrupt). Others remarkably successful (innovative day care programs, job training centers, investment promotion, environmental standards, family clinics, managing public business efficiently).

**Seemingly obvious factors proved irrelevant**: Organization too similar; party politics made little difference; affluence had no direct effect; social stability not correlated with good government. **Best predictor**: Strong traditions of civic engagement—voter turnout, newspaper readership, membership in choral societies, literary circles, Lions Clubs, soccer clubs.

**Civic communities** (e.g., Emilia-Romagna, Tuscany): Many active community organizations. Citizens engaged by public issues, not by patronage. Trust one another to act fairly and obey the law. Leaders relatively honest, committed to equality. Social and political networks organized horizontally, not hierarchically. Value solidarity, civic participation, integrity. **Democracy works.**

**Uncivic regions** (e.g., Calabria, Sicily): Characterized by **incivisme** (French term). The very concept of citizenship is stunted. Meager engagement in social and cultural associations. Public affairs = somebody else's business (*i notabili*—the bosses, the politicians). Laws made to be broken—fearing others' lawlessness, everyone demands sterner discipline. Trapped in interlocking vicious circles; nearly everyone feels powerless, exploited, unhappy. Representative government less effective than in civic communities.

**Astonishingly deep historical roots**: Traditions traced back nearly a millennium to 11th-century communal republics in Florence, Bologna, Genoa. Core of civic heritage: rich networks of organized reciprocity—guilds, religious fraternities, tower societies in medieval communes; cooperatives, mutual aid societies, choral societies in the 20th century.

**How it works**: Demand side—citizens in civic communities expect better government and (through their own efforts) get it. Supply side—social capital lowers transaction costs and eases collective action dilemmas. Police close more cases when citizens monitor neighborhoods. Schools teach better when parents volunteer. When community involvement is lacking, burdens on government employees are much greater and success more elusive.

### US State-Level Evidence

**Daniel Elazar's political cultures (1950s)**:
- **Traditionalistic** (South): Elites resistant to innovation
- **Individualistic** (Mid-Atlantic, western states): Strong parties, professional politicians, economic growth focus
- **Moralistic** (Northeast, upper Midwest, Pacific Northwest): "Good government," issue-based campaigning, social innovation prized

Elazar's political-culture map looks much like the distribution of social capital (Figure 80). Traditionalistic states have lowest social capital; individualistic, moderate; moralistic, highest. Correlation strikingly large.

Social capital-rich "moralistic" states: unusually innovative in public policy, maintain merit systems governing government hiring, more issue-oriented politics, apparently less corrupt.

**Municipal level**: Cities with institutionalized neighborhood organizations (Portland OR, St. Paul MN): more effective at passing proposals local people want, higher levels of support for and trust in municipal government. High grassroots involvement blunts patronage politics and secures fairer distribution of federal community development grants.

### Tax Compliance

Tax system legitimacy turns on belief we all do our share. Yet IRS cannot audit everyone—rational citizens have every reason to believe that if they pay their share, they subsidize cheaters. Recipe for disillusionment.

State-level analysis: where citizens view others as basically honest, tax compliance is higher than in low-social-capital states. Controlling for per capita income, income inequality, racial composition, urbanism, education levels, **social capital is the only factor that successfully predicts tax compliance.** Individual taxpayers who believe others are dishonest or distrustful of government are more likely to cheat. Willingness to pay my share depends crucially on perception that others are doing the same.

> In community rich in social capital, government is "we," not "they." I pay my taxes because I believe most other people do, and I see the tax system as basically working as it should.

**Figure 89**: Tax Evasion Is Low Where Social Capital Is High—shows strong correlation between Social Capital Index and low IRS criminal referrals/convictions (1982-97).

### Public Broadcasting and Collective Action

Public broadcasting = classic public goods problem. I obtain the benefit whether or not I pay; my contribution alone is unlikely to keep the station on air. Why should any rational, self-interested listener send a check?

**Answer**: In social-capital-rich communities, civic norms sustain expanded sense of "self-interest" and firmer confidence in reciprocity. Communities ranking high on turnout and social trust provide significantly higher contributions (controlling for education, affluence, race, tax deductibility, public spending). If social capital diminishes, more of us tempted to "free-ride"—not merely by ignoring appeals to "viewers like you," but by neglecting myriad civic duties that allow democracy to work.

Military units more effective when bonds of solidarity high. Communities with strong networks better at confronting unexpected crises. Collective action requires actions that violate immediate self-interest and assume neighbors will act collectively too. Modern society is replete with opportunities for free-riding and opportunism. Democracy doesn't require that citizens be selfless saints, but in many modest ways it assumes most of us much of the time will resist temptation to cheat.

> Social capital, the evidence increasingly suggests, strengthens our better, more expansive selves. The performance of our democratic institutions depends in measurable ways upon social capital.

## The Paradox of Social Capital and Tolerance

### America's Growing Tolerance (1950s-1990s)

America in the 1990s far more tolerant than the 1950s-1970s. All 21 tolerance questions in Table 7 moved in more tolerant direction:
- 1956: 50% of whites favored separate schools; 1995: only 4%
- 1963: 45% would move if blacks moved next door; 1997: 1%
- 1973: 20% had someone of another race to dinner; 1996: 42%
- 1963: 61% supported laws banning interracial marriage; 1998: 11%
- 1973: 45% favored banning homosexuality books from libraries; 1998: 26%
- 1975: 50% agreed "most men better suited emotionally for politics"; 1999: <25%
- Interracial social bridges strengthening even as most social capital declined

### Does Tolerance Require Disengagement?

**Table 8: Four Types of Society**

|  | Low Social Capital | High Social Capital |
|---|---|---|
| **High tolerance** | *Individualistic*: You do your thing, I'll do mine | *Civic community* (Salem without "witches") |
| **Low tolerance** | *Anarchic*: War of all against all | *Sectarian community* (Salem with "witches") |

**Key insight**: Tolerance and social capital are NOT opposite ends of a single continuum—four logically possible types exist. Cell (3)—high social capital plus high tolerance—is achievable.

**Evidence**: Individuals more engaged with communities are *more* tolerant than stay-at-home neighbors, not less. Correlation is positive even controlling for education. Especially strong link for gender and race: more community involvement = more open to gender equality and racial integration. Social joiners more tolerant of dissent and unconventional behavior. Pattern discovered during repressive McCarthy period, confirmed repeatedly since. Exception: religious involvement in fundamentalist churches linked to intolerance. **No empirical study confirms a supposed link between community involvement and intolerance.** George Babbitt's bigotry would have been worse if not exposed to Zenith's community life.

**State-level** (Figure 91): Most tolerant communities = places with greatest civic involvement. Communities where residents bowl alone = least tolerant places in America. Holds controlling for education, income, urbanism.

### Generational Dynamics

Most changes in both tolerance and engagement over last 30 years traceable to generational succession. But the dividing lines differ:

**Tolerance**: Almost entirely due to replacement of less tolerant people born in first half of 20th century by more tolerant boomers and Gen X. **James C. Davis**: People born in the 1970s-1980s are no more tolerant than parents born in the 1940s-1950s. The generational engine producing greater tolerance halted with the boomers.

**Engagement**: Something in the second half of the century made people less civically engaged. Late X'ers much less engaged than early boomers. Biggest losses still lie ahead.

**The 1940-45 cohort**: Liberal communitarians par excellence. They inherited parents' sense of community but discarded intolerance. Successfully passed tolerance to children but failed to transmit communitarian habits. The liberal communitarian cultural matrix from which the civil rights movement emerged was already fading by the 21st century.

**Key conclusion**: Social capital and tolerance have a symbiotic, not inverse, relationship. The generational roots of growing tolerance and declining engagement are quite distinct. No reason to assume community engagement must have illiberal consequences.

## Social Capital and Equality

**Empirical reality**: Community and equality are mutually reinforcing, not incompatible.

**Historical correlation**: Social capital and economic equality moved in tandem through most of the 20th century. 1950s-1960s: America more egalitarian in wealth/income distribution than in more than a century—same decades = high point of social connectedness. Record highs in equality and social capital coincided. Circumstantial evidence points to the WWII epoch as key.

**Reversal around 1965-70**: Last third of 20th century saw growing inequality AND eroding social capital. Gap between rich and poor increasing for nearly 3 decades (longest sustained increase in at least a century). First sustained decline in social capital in at least that long.

**State-level**: American states with highest social capital = most characterized by economic equality (Figure 92). In high-social-capital states, people from different classes equally likely to attend public meetings; in low-social-capital states, civic life monopolized by haves (Figure 93).

**Three causal interpretations**: (1) Social capital helps produce equality—historically the main weapon of have-nots. "Solidarity forever" = strategically sensible for minorities/working class. (2) Equality fosters social capital—great disparities inimical to widespread participation. (3) External forces cause both (e.g., WWII's leveling effects).

**False optimism**: To console ourselves that collapse of community brought a more liberal, egalitarian America. **False pessimism**: To refrain from rebuilding community for fear it leads to intolerance and injustice.

## The Deepest Paradox: Fraternity at War with Itself?

Fraternity most easily created *in opposition to* something else and most natural within homogeneous groups. Race = the most important embodiment in contemporary America. Legacy of slavery and Jim Crow thwarted connections across races. Civil rights movement aimed at destroying exclusive, nonbridging forms of social capital.

**The busing controversy**: Both sides were fundamentally concerned about social capital (though no one used that language). Proponents: Only through racially integrated schools could America generate sufficient social capital—familiarity, tolerance, solidarity, trust, habits of cooperation, mutual respect—across the racial divide. Opponents: In most parts of America, neighborhood schools provided a unique site for building social capital—friendship, habits of cooperation, solidarity. **Tragic conclusion: Both sides were probably right.**

**Political scientist Eileen McDonagh's formulation**: "Is it better to have neighborhoods legally restricted on basis of race, but with everyone having everyone else over for dinner, or is it better to have neighborhoods unrestricted on basis of race, but with very little social interaction going on between neighbors?" This was the dilemma embodied in the busing controversy. If we ignore it, efforts to reinvigorate community may simply lead to a more divided society.

### What Kind of Social Capital Do We Need?

**For different problems, different scopes**: Small children needing stimulation and structure = a little "familism" (bonding) goes a long way. Improving public schools = social capital at the community level—residential communities (neighborhood school model) or communities of like-minded families (charter school model). Deciding welfare policy or improving the quality of public debate = social capital of the most broad and bridging kind.

- Strong ties with intimate friends → chicken soup when you're sick
- Weak ties with distant acquaintances → more likely to produce job leads

**From the collective point of view**: The scope of social capital we need depends on the scale of problems we face. **For our biggest collective problems we need precisely the sort of bridging social capital that is toughest to create.** This challenge is central as we turn to the most important question: So what do we do now?

## Chapter 23: Lessons of History: The Gilded Age and the Progressive Era

### Historical Parallels

Over the last 3 decades, social, economic, and technological changes rendered obsolete significant stock of America's social capital. Growing deficit threatens educational performance, safe neighborhoods, equitable tax collection, democratic responsiveness, even health and happiness. Key question: Is erosion ineluctable or can we do something? History instructs—unexpectedly relevant and optimistic lessons from the **Gilded Age and Progressive Era.**

### The Gilded Age Transformation (1870-1900)

In 3-4 decades after the Civil War: Industrial Revolution, urbanization, massive immigration. Millions left family and friends on farms for Chicago, Milwaukee, Pittsburgh; millions more left Polish shtetls or Italian villages for the Lower East Side. Classic symptoms of social-capital deficit: crime waves, urban degradation, inadequate education, widening inequality, "saturnalia" of political corruption.

**Technological revolution:**
- U.S. Patent Office: 118,000 inventions in 8 decades up to 1870; next 4 decades at nearly 20x the rate
- Steel: 77,000 tons (1870) to 11.2 million tons (1900)
- Factories: 140,000 (1865) to 512,000 (1900); typical New England mill 200-300 workers (1865), first Ford plant 15,000 (1915)
- Rail network: 53,000 to 193,000 miles (1870-1900)
- Railroad and telegraph transformed America from "island communities" to integrated economic unit

**Journalist Mark Sullivan** on electricity:

> "Electricity was streaking up and down the country, literally like lightning—wires to provide it with a pathway were everywhere being extended, like long nerves of new growth."

### Corporate Revolution and Inequality

**1897-1904 merger wave**: Standard Oil, General Electric, Du Pont, U.S. Steel, American Tobacco, Nabisco. In relationship to economy size, not rivaled until megamergers of the 1990s.

**Glenn Porter**: "For the first time, whole industries came to be identified with names of powerful individuals—Vanderbilt, Rockefeller, Morgan, Carnegie, Duke, Swift, Armour."

Per capita wealth increased ~60%; real per capita GNP rose 133%. But gains not evenly spread. **Spaur** (1896): 1% owned >50% of national wealth; 44% of families at bottom owned only 1.2%. Not until final decades of 20th century would inequality widen as it did during the 19th.

Several severe recessions with unemployment surpassing 16% (1873-77, 1893-97). Prosperity after 1897 produced a society confident enough to contemplate large-scale reform.

### Population Growth, Urbanization, and Immigration

- National population: 40 million to 76 million (1870-1900); cities tripled to 30 million
- Chicago: barely a village in 1860, population 2.2 million by 1910
- 12 million immigrants 1870-1900 (more than in previous 2.5 centuries); nearly 13 million more in following 14 years
- By 1890: Immigrant adults outnumbered native adults in 18 of 20 cities with population >100,000
- By 1890: cacophony of strange tongues triggered debate about "Americanization" and ethnic identity

### Urban Crisis and Corruption

Cities "esthetically repulsive, commercially spastic, culturally balkanized, morally depraved, medically lethal" (**Kirschner**). **Steffens**: "The misgovernment of the American people is misgovernment *by* the American people." Boss Tweed paid $179,729.60 for three tables and forty chairs.

**Economic inequality**: Robber barons accumulated unprecedented wealth while most Americans lacked protections. Industrial trusts transformed economic power into political power. Workers faced violence and recurrent depressions.

### Economic Inequality and Political Alienation

Robber barons (Rockefeller, Morgan, Carnegie) accumulated unprecedented wealth while most Americans lacked protections against railroad exploitation, expensive credit, price deflation. Industrial trusts transformed economic power into political power. **Steven Diner**: Middle-class Americans watched trusts manipulate Congress and suppress dissent; government appeared captive of special interests.

### Race and Exclusion

After Reconstruction (1877), southern whites violently excluded blacks. **Plessy v. Ferguson** (1896): "separate but equal." Segregation spread from railcars to streetcars, hospitals, jails, theaters. 1880s lynchings averaged one every other day. 1890-1908: virtually all southern states disenfranchised African Americans—electoral participation fell 62% average (100% in North Carolina, 99% Louisiana, 98% Alabama, 83% Florida). Northern civic institutions also excluded working-class people, African Americans, Jews, Catholics. **American Protective Association** (founded 1887) claimed 2.5 million members (7% of adults) by 1894.

### From Social Darwinism to Social Reform

**Gilded Age ideology**: Social Darwinism—survival of fittest with minimal government interference. Critics gradually gained the upper hand. **Muckraking journalism** triggered a philosophical shift:
- **Jacob Riis**: *How the Other Half Lives* (1890)—slum tenements
- **Lincoln Steffens**: *Shame of the Cities* (1904)—urban corruption
- **Ida Tarbell**: *McClure's* (1904)—Standard Oil depredations
- **Upton Sinclair**: *The Jungle* (1905)—immigrant laborer abuses

Progressive intellectuals articulated yearning for community values of small-town life, nostalgia in the face of materialism, individualism, and "bigness."

**Henry Adams** later wrote of his own boyhood: "The American boy of 1854 stood nearer the year 1 than to the year 1900."

### New Consumer Culture and Gender Changes

New affluence combined with ingenuity to produce a culture of leisure and materialism. Phonograph and movies (1896-1902) portended radical transformation of mass leisure. By 1908, NYC alone had >600 five-cent storefront movie theaters ("nickelodeons"). Harvard philosopher **William James** bemoaned "moral flabbiness born of exclusive worship of bitch-goddess SUCCESS."

Other cultural changes were more progressive: As the Industrial Revolution advanced, women assumed new public roles, demanded the vote, got advanced education, increasingly worked alongside men. During the Gilded Age, women began to break out of the traditional "proper sphere," joining local reform efforts under the banner of "municipal housekeeping"—laying groundwork for the "new woman" of the Progressive Era.

### Technology and Community Debate

**Optimists** like William Allen White: "Electric wire, iron pipe, street railroad, newspaper, telephone have made us all one body.... It is but the dawn of a spiritual awakening." **Herbert Croly**: New media would allow active citizenry to "meet" despite distance—sounds timely for the Internet era.

**Cautious voices**: **Dewey**: "The Great Society created by steam and electricity may be a society but it is no community." **Mary Parker Follett**: "Real solidarity will never be accomplished except by beginning somewhere the joining of one small group with another." Follett sought community centers integrating churches, trade associations, lodges, youth groups. **Robert Park**: "In politics, religion, art and sport we are represented now by proxies where formerly we participated in person."

### The Progressive Vision

**Lippmann** (1914): "We have changed our environment more quickly than we know how to change ourselves."

Progressive thinkers came from small towns, recognized both oppressive features ("small-town herd") and virtues (community rooted in interpersonal ties). **William Allen White**: "Reading of a wedding or the birth of a child, we have that neighborly feeling that breeds the real democracy." Small-town neighborliness was informal mutual aid—social capital in pure form. **Jane Addams**: Village gossip kept men informed about who needed help, enabled "the good lying next at hand."

Impersonal market ties had replaced sturdier bonds of family, friendship, small-town solidarity. European theorists echoed the same concern: **Sir Henry Maine** (status vs. contract), **Ferdinand Tonnies** (Gemeinschaft vs. Gesellschaft), **Emile Durkheim** (mechanical vs. organic solidarity).

**Booth Tarkington** (Pulitzer Prize novelist) on late-19th-century Indianapolis:

> Not quite so long ago as a generation, there was no panting giant here... there was but a pleasant big town of neighborly people who had understanding of one another.... No one was very rich; few were very poor; the air was clean, and there was time to live.

**Charles Horton Cooley** (1912): "In our own life the intimacy of the neighborhood has been broken up by the growth of an intricate mesh of wider contacts which leaves us strangers to people who live in the same house ... diminishing our economic and spiritual community with our neighbors."

**Middle-class flight**: During the Gilded Age, the middle class fled immigrant cities to suburbs, retreated into tight neighborhood enclaves, dismissed municipal politics, allowed industrial capitalism to proceed unchecked. **Jeffrey A. Charles**: "By the turn of the century ... sociability alone appeared to be an inadequate response to the feeling of crisis that gripped the middle class.... Social redemption required a new type of cooperative activism ... serving the community."

Progressives too practical to seek return to a pastoral age. They admired past virtues but understood we could not go back. Outlook was **activist and optimistic, not fatalist and despondent.** Herbert Croly did not believe the future would take care of itself—**neither should we.**

**Woodrow Wilson** (1912): "We have come upon a very different age from any that preceded us.... To-day, the everyday relationships of men are largely with great impersonal concerns, with organizations, not with other individual men. Now this is nothing short of a new social age."

**IT WAS, IN SHORT, A TIME VERY LIKE OUR OWN:**
- Technological advance and unparalleled prosperity, but nostalgic for integrated connectedness
- New communications modes promised new community, but thoughtful observers worried those forms would be fool's gold
- Optimism from economic advances battled pessimism from intractable social ills
- New concentrations of wealth and corporate power raised questions about democracy's meaning
- Massive urban concentrations of impoverished ethnic minorities posed questions of social justice
- Comfortable middle class torn between seductive escape and deeper demands of redemptive solidarity
- Immigration changed America's complexion, seemed to imperil *unum* in *pluribus*
- Materialism, political cynicism, spectatorship over action seemed to thwart idealistic reformism
- Older social connections abraded—even destroyed—by technological, economic, social change
- Path from the past could not be retraced; few saw the path to a better future clearly
- Complacency bred of technological prowess succeeded by dissatisfaction, civic inventiveness, organized reform efforts fueled by both discontent and hopefulness

### Seeds of Progressivism

**Richard McCormick**: Amid hard times of the 1890s depression, Americans questioned whether democracy and economic equality were possible in industrial society. Hundreds poured energies into settlement houses. A new generation of ministers aligned churches with the disadvantaged. Municipal reform entered a new phase. Women's clubs turned from discussing literature to addressing social problems. Seeds of Progressivism were planted during the depression of the 1890s.

### The Associational Boom (1870-1920)

Late 19th century saw a veritable "boom" in association building. **1876**: Henry Martyn Robert published *Robert's Rules of Order.* Per capita density of associations rose sharply through the second half of the 19th century. Shortly after the turn of the century, density began to stagnate—followed by a long period of infilling as organizations added membership ranks.

**Theda Skocpol**: Half of all the largest mass membership organizations in two centuries of American history were founded 1870-1920. Of 506 contemporary associations in *Encarta 2000*, almost twice as many founded 1890-1920 as 1960-1990. Age distribution implies organizational entrepreneurs at the turn of the century were more prolific or successful than in our own time.

**Hard to name a major civic institution not invented in these decades**: Red Cross, NAACP, Knights of Columbus, Hadassah, Boy Scouts, Rotary, PTA, Sierra Club, Audubon Society, ABA, Farm Bureau Federation, Big Brothers, League of Women Voters, Teamsters, Campfire Girls.

### Fraternal Organizations

**W.S. Harwood** (1897): "Every fifth, or possibly every eighth, man you meet is identified with some fraternal organization." **1910**: conservatively one-third of all adult males were members (**David Beito**). Fraternalism: reaction against individualism and anomie. Provided material benefits (life/health insurance) and social solidarity. Mutual aid based on reciprocity—today's recipient, tomorrow's donor. Geographically extended structures facilitated coinsurance for natural disasters. Values: mutualism, self-reliance, thrift, leadership, self-government. Encompassed both middle-class and working-class; typically segregated by race and gender but comparable organizations served African Americans and women.

### Women's Associations

Spontaneous grassroots crusade of 1873-74 reinvigorated temperance → **WCTU** under **Frances Willard** adopted "Do Everything" policy (prison reform, youth groups, kindergartens, labor reform). **General Federation of Women's Clubs** (1890): food inspection, housing codes, workplace protection. **National Association of Colored Women's Clubs** (1896): barred by segregation from GFWC. **Rheta Childe Dorr**: "Home is not contained within the four walls of an individual home. Home is the community."

### Immigrant, Ethnic, and African American Associations

Emigration devalues social capital—most connections left behind. Immigrants conserve capital via chain migration and benevolent societies for mutual aid. Chinese tong member: "We are strangers in a strange country. We must have an organization to control our country fellows and develop our friendship." **1910**: Two-thirds of all Poles belonged to at least one of ~7,000 Polish associations. Similar for Jews, Slovaks, Croats.

**W.E.B. Du Bois** emphasized importance of black secret societies in *The Philadelphia Negro*. The church played a unique role—**Evelyn Higginbotham**: it "housed a diversity of programs including schools, circulating libraries, concerts, restaurants, insurance companies, vocational training, athletic clubs."

### Religious Revival and Social Gospel

Religion played a substantial role in civic revitalization despite an increasingly secular industrial culture. **Salvation Army** (evangelical Protestant movement) spread from Britain to America (1880), ministering to unchurched urban poor with missionary zeal and mass marketing (marches, brass bands, "Hallelujah lassies").

The era of the **Social Gospel** and **"muscular Christianity"**: Turn-of-the-century effort by liberal Protestant theologians and ministers to bring pressing social problems (urban poverty) to attention of middle-class parishioners. A reaction against individualism, laissez-faire, inequality; an attempt to make religion relevant to new social/intellectual circumstances.

**E. Brooks Holifield** on "the social congregation": In the late 19th century, thousands of congregations transformed into centers for worship AND dozens of activities (Sunday school, concerts, church socials, women's meetings, youth groups, girls' guilds, boys' brigades, sewing circles, benevolent societies, temperance societies, athletic clubs, scout troops). **Henry Ward Beecher** advised Yale seminarians to "multiply picnics"—congregations proceeded beyond picnics to gymnasiums, parish houses, camps, baseball teams, military drill groups. Protestant congregations by 1923 gave 25-35% of offerings to missions and benevolent causes (up from 14-18% at the turn of the century).

**Chautauqua movement** (founded 1874, upstate New York): Originally a summer institute for Methodist Sunday school teachers. Spawned nationwide series of extension schools, study groups, tent lecture circuits (speakers ranged from Eugene Debs to Warren Harding). By 1919: "one out of every eleven persons in the country attended a lyceum or Chautauqua program every year." Radio and later TV would provide more alluring entertainment, though less opportunity for grassroots, cross-class civic deliberation.

Catholics more sympathetic to the plight of the poor (more Catholics belonged to laboring classes). The church played a special role in the black community. **Evelyn Higginbotham**: The black church "housed a diversity of programs including schools, circulating libraries, concerts, restaurants, insurance companies, vocational training, athletic clubs—all catering to a mass population much broader than the membership of individual churches." Socially reformist Christianity was central inspiration for much social activism. At the **1912 Progressive convention**, delegates broke into emotional chorus of "Onward, Christian Soldiers!"

### Youth Organizations (1901-1910)

**Extraordinary burst of creativity**: Most nationwide youth organizations founded in a single decade—Boy Scouts, Girl Scouts, Campfire Girls, 4-H, Boys Clubs, Girls Clubs, Big Brothers, Big Sisters, American Camping Association. Reformers devoted energies to creating constructive paths for young people. **Marketing genius**: Combined enduring social values (trustworthy, loyal, helpful) with pure fun of camping, sports, play.

Kindergartens and high schools became recognizable elements of public schooling. Playgrounds spread rapidly; **Playground Association of America** founded 1906. Public recreation centers aimed to involve entire families in wholesome leisure.

**Child protection**: New York Society for Prevention of Cruelty to Children (1874) spawned similar organizations—by 1908, fifty-five local societies. Progressives didn't long nostalgically for "lost social control"—they blazed constructive new paths.

### Settlement Houses

**Hull House** (Jane Addams, 1889): Six by 1891, seventy-four by 1897, ~400 by 1910. Idealistic young middle-class men and women lived in urban slums for years. Activities: education, debate societies, lecture series, bathing/sanitation training, manual skills, kindergartens, daycare, art galleries, coffeehouses, gymnasiums, nurseries.

**Ironically, greatest long-term effect on service givers, not recipients.** Jane Addams hoped firsthand contact would give meaning to young college graduates. Leaders produced: **Florence Kelley**, **Eleanor Roosevelt**, **Gerard Swope** (GE president), **Walter Sherman Gifford** (AT&T president). **Richard McCormick**: Settlements served as training grounds for Progressive social reform across all domains.

### Kindergarten Movement

First American kindergarten by **Elizabeth Palmer Peabody** (Boston). By late 1870s: spreading rapidly; by 1908: 400+ kindergartens run by women's clubs, temperance groups, churches. Inspired by innovative German educational philosophy encouraging childhood creativity. Volunteer organizers sought to provide wholesome education for immigrant children AND influence child-rearing techniques. Grew adult connectedness through mothers' clubs, sewing clubs. Eventually incorporated into public school system, but important residue remained. **National Congress of Mothers** (1897) → **Congress of Mothers and Teachers** (1924) → **PTA**.

### Organized Labor

The era in which organized labor became a serious force. **Knights of Labor**: premise that workers of all types should be in "one big union"—boomed from 28,100 (1880) to 729,000 (six years later), then collapsed by 1894 due to internal conflicts. Leading role taken by **American Federation of Labor** and craft/industrial unions.

**Union membership 1897-1904**: Nearly quadrupled from 3.5% to 12.3% of nonagricultural workforce—a more durable expansion than previous waves. **Historians Cochran and Miller**: Unions were part of members' social lives, not merely economic tools. Labor unions were embedded in clubs, lodges, fraternal orders. Mutual benefit policies provided security. Union socials, dances, picnics, lectures offered "stimulating leisure-time activity."

**National Consumers League** (1899, founded by **Florence Kelley**): Enabled middle-class women shoppers to boycott firms failing to provide decent working conditions for women employees—bridging class lines through consumer activism.

### Political Achievements

Most thoroughgoing renovation of public policies and institutions in American history, all introduced and diffused nationwide in a few short decades:

**Electoral reforms**: Secret ballot (1888, Kentucky); popular initiative and referendum (1898, South Dakota); presidential primary elections (1900, Minnesota); city manager system (1903, Galveston); direct election of senators (1913); women's suffrage (1893 Colorado; 1920 U.S. Constitution). Most intense period of local administrative reform in U.S. history.

**National-level reforms**: Federal Reserve (1913), income tax (1913), Bureau of the Budget (1921). First consumer protection legislation: Food and Drug Administration, federal meat inspection (1906), Federal Trade Commission (1914). First environmental legislation: national forest system (1905), national park system (1913). Departments of Commerce and Labor (1913). Strengthened antitrust regulations (1903); child labor laws (1916); eight-hour day for railways (1916); workmen's compensation (1916). First federal regulation of telecommunications (1910). Federal campaign finance regulation (1907). Hardly any area of public policy left untouched.

Not all reforms proved as successful as advocates expected. Few in retrospect look positively pernicious. Taken as whole: an impressive achievement within a constitutional system built to thwart radical change. Rested on broad-based, grassroots, nationwide political movement sweeping both major parties. Political mobilization drew on energies and organizations created during social-capital building of previous decades.

### From Social Networks to Political Action

Wave began with inward-oriented organizations (leisure, self-help). Late 19th/early 20th century: gradually turned to community issues, then political reform. **Investment in social capital was not an alternative to, but a prerequisite for, political mobilization and reform.**

### Critiques and Cautions

**Technocratic elitism**: Progressives favored expert solutions that demobilized participation. Electoral turnout began descent from 1896. **Philip Ethington**: "Saddest perhaps is the deep and enduring damage done to democracy by her closest friends."

**Social control debate**: Did middle-class reformers organize to control working-class immigrants? Or strengthen immigrant communities? Probably elements of both. **Communitarian excess**: William Dudley Foulke suggested the draft be used for public service after WWI, even proposing mandated marriage and childbearing for the community's sake—illustrating the risk of "big brother-ism, American style."

**Most troubling—racial exclusion**: Jim Crow legalized (1896), second KKK founded (1915). Not all civic innovations were beneficent. Emphasis on community may shift balance from bridging toward bonding social capital—**one of the most instructive lessons from that earlier era.**

### Progressivism as Civic Communitarian Reaction

**Peter Levine**: Movement so broad (Upton Sinclair and J. Edgar Hoover, W.E.B. Du Bois and Robert Taft) it "can hardly be called a movement at all." Core interpretation: Progressive Era represented a civic communitarian reaction to the ideological individualism of the Gilded Age. Began with social goals broader and more immediate than political goals.

**Frederick Law Olmsted** (Central Park designer): Parks and recreation overcome isolation and suspicion:

> "Playgrounds... everywhere produce the same social results. That is, they bring about fine community spirit, awaken civic consciousness and co-operation, and make for a whole-souled companionship instead of individualism and isolation."

Important goal was to strengthen social capital—though they didn't use this terminology. The term itself was invented by Progressive Era educator **L.J. Hanifan** regarding community centers.

**Progressive method** (**Richard McCormick**): Typically began by organizing a voluntary association, investigating the problem, gathering facts, analyzing via social sciences. Proposed solution was popularized through education and moral suasion campaigns. If it seemed to work, taken over by government as public function.

### Geographic Distribution and Multi-Class Character

Major cities were part of the process, but much creative action was in heartland communities. Association building actually began in small heartland towns, not cosmopolitan metropolises. The high school movement spread most rapidly in small Midwest/West towns.

**Link and McCormick**: "Progressivism was the only reform movement ever experienced by the whole American nation."

Progressivism evaded simple "top down" or "bottom up" classification. Some organizations (4-H, Grange) were federal government creations. Others sprang from local initiatives. **Lateral diffusion** of initiatives from one community to another. 1907: Civic organizations in Rochester, NY established "social centers" in public schools for publicly funded popular debate. By 1916: the movement had spread across the country.

### Lessons for Today

Institutions formed 1880-1910 lasted nearly a century. In those few decades, voluntary structures of American society assumed their modern form. The trends toward disengagement in Section II register the decay of that very structure over the last third of the 20th century.

Progressives correctly diagnosed the deficit without seeking reactionary return. Tempting in 1890 to say "Everybody back to the farm"—they resisted. Similarly tempting now to say "Would all women please report to the kitchen, and turn off the TV?" Social dislocation can easily breed reactionary nostalgia.

**We desperately need an era of civic inventiveness** to create renewed institutions—the 21st-century equivalent of Boy Scouts, settlement houses, NAACP. What we create may look nothing like Progressive institutions (just as theirs weren't carbon copies of earlier small-town folkways). Hard to imagine a time without Boy Scouts, but a century ago the idea of beanies, merit badges, and the Scout's oath must have seemed fanciful. Some solutions may seem initially preposterous. Should be wary of straining civic inventiveness through conventional filters. **Willingness to err—and then correct our aim—is the price of success in social reform.** Specific Progressive reforms may no longer be appropriate, but the practical, enthusiastic idealism of that era—and its achievements—should inspire us.

## Chapter 24: Toward an Agenda for Social Capitalists

### Arts as Bridge-Building

Goal: ensure by 2010, Americans participate in (not merely consume) cultural activities. Art especially useful in transcending conventional social barriers. Social capital often a valuable by-product of cultural activities whose main purpose is purely artistic.

**Examples**: **Liz Lerman's Dance Exchange** (unemployed shipyard workers and white-collar professionals in Portsmouth, N.H.); **Roadside Theater** (celebrating traditions in declining Appalachian towns); Museum of the National Center for African American Artists (Boston—diverse groups parading 20-foot fish sculptures); **Toni Blackman's Freestyle Union** (D.C.—hip-hop and improvisational poetry attracting people from all walks of life); Baltimore Museum of Art "Freestyle Thursdays"; Chicago's **Gallery 37** (diverse young artists building connections); **David Simpson** (community theater bridging loggers and environmentalists). Many produce great art, but all produce great bridging social capital.

### Politics and Government

> **Nowhere is the need to restore connectedness, trust, and civic engagement clearer than in the now often empty public forums of our democracy.**

**Campaign finance reform** should increase importance of social capital (time-based participation) and decrease financial capital (check-based). Since time is more equally distributed than money, privileging time-based participation would reverse growing inequality in politics.

**Decentralization**: Government authority should be decentralized as far as possible to bring decisions to smaller, local jurisdictions, while recognizing and offsetting potential negative effects on equality and redistribution. Liberals alert to benefits of social capital should be readier to transfer authority downward in exactly the same measure that compassionate conservatives should be readier to transfer resources from have to have-not communities. Neighborhood councils have worked in cities like Minneapolis, Portland, and Seattle, creating new social capital in the form of potluck dinners, community gardens, and flea markets. But deft design is needed to ensure the balance between bridging and bonding does not tip too far toward urban fragmentation.

**Social-capital impact statements**: Policy designers of whatever partisan persuasion should become more social-capital-savvy, seeking to do minimum damage to existing stocks while looking for opportunities to add new ones. The idea of a "social-capital impact statement" for new programs—less bureaucratic than environmental impact statements but equally effective at calling attention to unanticipated consequences. Example: Greatest damage to social capital in inner-city Indianapolis in the last half century was the unintended disruption of neighborhood networks when neighborhoods were pierced by **Interstate 65** in the early 1960s. **Stephen Goldsmith's** Front-Porch Alliance, created more than a quarter century later, was a worthy restoration effort, but Goldsmith himself would say it would have been better to avoid the damage in the first place.

### Avoiding False Debates

1. **Top-down vs. bottom-up**: Roles of national and local institutions must be complementary; neither alone can solve the problem.
2. **Government as problem or solution**: It can be both. Many creative social capital investments (county agents, 4-H, community colleges, March of Dimes) were government policy results. Hard to imagine meeting challenges without using government.
3. **Individual vs. institutional change**: Both needed. America's major civic institutions are somewhat antiquated a century after most were created. They need reform to invite more active participation. But reform won't happen unless citizens resolve to become reconnected.

> **Henry Ward Beecher's advice** a century ago to "multiply picnics" is not entirely ridiculous today. We should do this, ironically, not because it will be good for America—though it will be—but because it will be good for us.

### The Saguaro Seminar

Thirty-three accomplished thinkers and doers meeting regularly to develop actionable ideas. Members included **Xavier de Souza Briggs**, **E.J. Dionne**, **Amy Gutmann**, **Barack Obama**, **Ralph Reed**, **George Stephanopoulos**, **William Julius Wilson**, among others from diverse backgrounds and political perspectives.

### Project Origins and Reception

Work began in 1992 while completing *Making Democracy Work* (twenty-year Italian government study). Fresh from stint as dean of the Kennedy School of Government, focused on problems of American democracy. Key insight: Italian research showing democracy depended on social capital had implications for contemporary America.

**Academic collaboration (1992-1994)**: **Joel Orlen** and the American Academy of Arts and Sciences convened workshops on social capital with core team: **Peter B. Evans**, **Susan Pharr**, **Theda Skocpol**. Generous funding from Carnegie Corporation, Ford Foundation, Rockefeller Foundation. Three journalist acquaintances (**David Boldt**, **Jonathan S. Cohn**, **Paul Solman**) pressed to carry discussion beyond academic circles.

**Breakthrough moment (spring 1994)**: Over breakfast, friend **Peter Ackerman** mentioned trends in league bowling seemed to fit the evolving generalization. A few weeks later, **Harold A. Pollack** ("research team") had rounded up enough data on PTA membership and fraternal organizations to move beyond anecdote.

January 1995: Abridged version published in *Journal of Democracy*. Until then Putnam was "an obscure academic" despite publishing scores of books over three decades. Suddenly invited to Camp David, lionized by talk-show hosts, featured with wife Rosemary in *People* magazine. He had "unwittingly articulated an unease that had already begun to form in the minds of many ordinary Americans."

**Academic vs. public audiences**: A striking distinction emerged. Academics *always* skeptical—wanted to know if disengagement was really true, asked about new social movements, the Internet, 12-step groups—but *never* commented on what to do if it were true. Public audiences *never* asked whether it was true (it rang true to their experience) but *always* deeply concerned about how to fix the problem. Public audiences asked tougher questions about solutions.

> "Pretty heady stuff, but it has kept me away from my computer, where I'm supposed to be working out a fuller version.... We may be running a risk of our marketing operation getting too far out in front of our product development." (Letter to friends, February 1995)

### Research Breakthroughs

**Most commonly cited criticism**: By focusing on declining membership in formal groups, Putnam may have ignored offsetting increases in informal connectedness. Alleged counterexamples (soccer matches, softball games) turned out mostly illusory after hard investigation. But it left open the possibility that overlooked forms of social capital were expanding. Colleagues **Kristin Goss** and **Steve Yonish** spent hundreds of hours in resourceful but usually fruitless research on this question.

**First major discovery**: The **Roper Social and Political Trends archive** (410,000+ respondents)—**Henry Brady** (Survey Research Center, UC Berkeley) and **Steve Yonish** (Harvard) spent a year yielding an archive of unparalleled value. Massive new evidence impressed even skeptics, confirming civic disengagement.

**Second major discovery** (mid-1997): **DDB Needham Life Style survey archive**, found through a footnote in a graduate critique by **Dhavan Shah** (student of **William Wells** at University of Minnesota). Steve Yonish gained access with help from **Jim Crimmins**, **Chris Callahan**, **Marty Horn**, **Doug Hughes** (DDB Needham), and **Sid Groeneman** (Market Facts). A truly unique resource tracking picnics and card games. New data suggested "Bowling Alone" might have **understated** the depth and breadth of social transformation. Validating and analyzing new evidence added two years to the project but deepened confidence.

Author's confidence was shaken when economist **John Helliwell** discovered the published General Social Survey data was flawed. Correcting computational errors diminished the apparent decline in formal group membership. Consolation: the error was discovered before critics found it.

### The Saguaro Seminar Formation

Conversations with **Lew Feldstein** (president of New Hampshire Charitable Foundation) about practical implications led to the formal Saguaro Seminar on Civic Engagement in America. Five-year collaboration became "one of the most enjoyable and productive" of Putnam's professional life. Feldstein brought practical idealism and skills in planning/managing an incipient social movement that the author lacked. He helped shift the focus from "what has happened" to "what we should do about it."

Name origin: southwestern saguaro cactus grows unseen for decades before flowering—"suitably apt metaphor for social capital."

By 1997, recruited a blue-ribbon group of civic leaders and scholars with **Tom Sander** and **Chris Gates** (president of National Civic League). Major funders: Carnegie Corporation, Lilly Endowment, MacArthur Foundation, Mott Foundation, Surdna Foundation, Rockefeller Brothers Fund, Rockefeller Foundation.

## Appendix I: Measuring Social Change

### Primary Strategy: Triangulation

**Core principle**: No single source of data is flawless, but the more numerous and diverse the sources, the less likely they could all be influenced by the same flaw. Two independent strands of evidence are better than one; more than two are better still, especially if they have different imperfections.

### Organizational Records

Firmest indicators—assiduous record keeping over decades more reliable than recollections. But limitations: organizations have life cycles independent of community vitality; nothing can be inferred from birth of new organizations unless we also examine death rate of older ones. One scholar estimates 80% of all community groups represent social "dark matter"—without formal structure, invisible to conventional chroniclers.

### Social Surveys

Well-designed polls provide "social time-lapse photography." Surveys can illuminate dark matter—not merely voting but chatting with neighbors, not merely membership but poker games. Key requirements: questions must be unchanging; change measured at multiple points becomes exponentially more reliable. In assessing social change, two observations are better than one, but many is much better than two.

**Cautions**: "Order effects" (answers depend on question order), "house effects" (different organizations get different results). Number of memberships a poll uncovers is heavily dependent on number of probes—"As many as you'd like, if I ask hard enough." Must cast the net widely—if bowling were replaced by softball, we'd miss the offset. Must match measurement periods to hypotheses about timing: "What's changing over what period?" Fair test requires data spanning three to five decades.

### Primary Survey Sources

1. **National Election Studies (NES)**: Biennial since 1952, focused on elections
2. **General Social Survey (GSS)**: Roughly biennial since 1974—group membership, church attendance, social trust
3. **Roper Social and Political Trends**: ~10x per year, 1973-1994, archive of 410,000+ respondents
4. **DDB Needham Life Style**: Annual since 1975, 87,000+ respondents covering social, economic, political themes
5. **Americans' Use of Time** (John Robinson, University of Maryland): Time diaries from 1965, 1975, 1985, 1995

**DDB methodology**: Each year since 1975, DDB Needham advertising agency commissions Market Facts to question a national panel about consumer preferences and life-style issues. Written questionnaires mailed to ~5,000 respondents; usable responses from 3,500-4,000 respondents. Response rate at final stage (70-80%) typically higher than conventional random samples.

**DDB limitations**: Until 1985, only married households included. Mail panel approach underrepresents high school dropouts (5% too few), single respondents (10% too few), racial minorities (half as many), and adults under 25. Sample reasonably represents middle 80-90% of American society.

**DDB validation**: Compared with GSS across a dozen diverse questions (feminism, marijuana, financial worries, smoking, church attendance, etc.)—levels, trends, and demographic correlates virtually indistinguishable despite marked differences in sampling (random vs. quota), questioning (interviews vs. mail), and question wording. **Steven Yonish and Putnam**: In every case, the two surveys are essentially equivalent.

Example: According to GSS, probability that a specific demographic profile (35-year-old single white mother with two years of college in New England) favored marijuana legalization was 35%; DDB said 38%—within sampling error. Controlling simultaneously for year of survey, year of birth, marital status, employment, education, income, race, region, and residence type, GSS says women attend church exactly 5.3 more times per year than men; DDB says 4.8—again within sampling error.

Additional validation: Comparison between two most widely reported consumer confidence surveys (Michigan and Conference Board)—one using random sampling, the other mail panels—showed very similar long-run changes (R² = .55 over three decades).

### Frequency Conversion Algorithm

Both GSS and DDB Needham surveys typically ask respondents to estimate frequency of various activities (e.g., church attendance), but use slightly different categories. Putnam converted raw data into estimated annual frequencies using a standardized algorithm. Reasonable observers might differ over what "several times a year" means quantitatively, but basic results are not sensitive to the exact integers assigned.

### Americans' Use of Time Archive

Another valuable source: the **Americans' Use of Time** project, managed by Professor **John Robinson** of University of Maryland. Based on careful time diaries kept by national samples in 1965, 1975, 1985, and 1995. One major advantage: it begins in 1965, just about when various forms of social capital began to decline. However, the 1965 sample excluded rural and retired families. Putnam adjusted 1965 figures using observed differences in 1975 and 1985 between the full national sample and the subset that would have been included within the 1965 frame. These adjustments account for minor discrepancies between Robinson and Godbey's published results and figures reported in this book.

### Absolute vs. Relative Measurement

Absolute numbers can be misleading. If total vote rises 5% while voting-age population rises 10%, participation has fallen. If membership in the Grange falls 5% while the number of farmers falls 50%, involvement per farmer has risen. Should generally consider "market share": what proportion of the eligible population takes part?

**Controlling for education** is controversial: education is usually the single most important predictor of many forms of social participation—from voting to chairing local committees to hosting dinner parties. Educational levels rose very sharply during the period of interest. So it seems logical to control for education—if vocabulary skills were falling despite rising education, we'd look for some other depressing factor (like TV).

**Counterargument**: If more people have college degrees, the sociological significance of the credential may have been devalued. Social status is associated with education, but we wouldn't assume more education means more total status in America.

**Core issue**: Whether my propensity to participate rises or falls when those around me become more educated. Evidence from this research strongly suggests effects are typically **absolute, not relative**—my education increases my participation, and your education does not lower mine. Under these circumstances, controlling for rising education would be appropriate. But doing so amplifies declines, so the more conservative course is NOT to control. **Putnam generally does not control for education, stacking the cards against his hypothesis. Evidence presented may well understate the gross decline.**

### Statistical Controls for Cause and Effect

Virtually every generalization in the book has been subjected to detailed statistical analysis controlling simultaneously for age (or year of birth), gender, education, income, race, marital status, parental status, job status, and size of community. Where relevant, also controlled for year of survey, region, financial worries, homeownership, residential mobility, commuting time, general leisure activity, time pressure, health, and other factors. Graphs typically present data without multivariate controls, but underlying relationships have been tested for spuriousness.

## Appendix III: Rise and Fall of Civic and Professional Associations

### Key Patterns

**Golden Age: 1945-1965** — Most civic groups experienced peak membership in this period. The pattern of rise and fall is remarkably consistent across diverse types of organizations—fraternal, service, women's, youth, professional, and recreational.

**Youth Organizations**: 4-H peaked 1950 at 180/1,000 rural youth, declined 26%. Boy/Girl Scouts peaked late 1960s-early 1970s. The timing of peak and decline varies slightly by type but clusters in the mid-1960s to early 1970s.

**Women's Organizations**: AAUW peaked 1955 at 53/1,000 college-educated women, down 84% by 1997. Business and Professional Women peaked 1956, down 84%. GFWC peaked 1930s-1950s, down 79%. League of Women Voters peaked 1960s, down 42%. These declines occurred despite dramatic increases in the educated female population that should have been the constituency for such groups.

**Fraternal Organizations**: American Legion peaked 1945 at 274/1,000 wartime veterans, down 47%. Odd Fellows peaked 1920s at 54/1,000, catastrophic 94% decline—a century-long erosion. Elks peaked 1970s, down 46%. Masons peaked at roughly the same time, with similar declines.

**Service Clubs**: Rotary down 25%, Lions down 55%, Kiwanis down 46% from 1960s peaks. These declines are measured as market share (per capita), not absolute numbers—absolute membership may have held steadier even as the eligible population grew.

**Bowling Leagues**: American Bowling Congress peaked 1979 at 83/1,000 adult men, down 72%. Women's Bowling Congress peaked 1980s, down 71%. The iconic example of the book's thesis—more Americans were bowling than ever, but league bowling (the social form) collapsed.

**Professional Associations**: AMA peaked 1950s-1960s at 75% of physicians, down to 40%. More varied trajectories—only newer groups (accountants) or ethnically specific groups (Hadassah, Knights of Columbus) maintained rates. The pattern is less uniform in professional associations, where specialization and alternative organizations may have absorbed some members.

**PTA**: Peaked 1960s at 48/1,000 families with children, down 60%. Perhaps the most symbolically important decline, given PTA's central role in connecting families to public schools and to one another.

**Overall pattern**: The near-universality of the decline across such diverse organizations—with different missions, constituencies, and organizational structures—strongly suggests a common underlying cause rather than organization-specific explanations.
