# Bowling Alone — Compacted Notes (Chapters 1-15)

## Chapter 1: Thinking about Social Change in America

### The Puzzle: Declining Civic Organizations (1990s)

Putnam opens with a catalogue of withering organizations across America:
- **Glenn Valley, PA Bridge Club**: 40+ members for half a century, broke up after 1990
- **Little Rock Sertoma Club**: ~50 weekly luncheon members (mid-1980s) → 7 regulars a decade later
- **Roanoke, VA NAACP**: Active since 1918, membership fell from ~2,500 to a few hundred; 1998 president contest drew only 57 voters
- **VFW Post 2378, Berwyn, IL**: Once a bustling working-class country club, by 1999 struggling to pay taxes. Tom Kissell: "Kids today just aren't joiners"
- **Dallas Charity League**: Met every Friday for 57 years, held last meeting April 30, 1999; average age 80+
- **Vassar alumnae book sale, DC**: Closed 51st annual sale (1999); co-chair noted volunteers "in their sixties, seventies, and eighties. They're dying, and they're not replaceable"
- **Tewksbury Memorial HS marching band**: 40 new uniforms purchased (1999), only 4 students signed up; 20 years earlier, 80+ members

> "The puzzle: In the last several decades of the twentieth century all these community groups and tens of thousands like them across America began to fade."

### The Civic Vitality of the 1950s-60s

The 1960s seemed threshold of expanded involvement. Churches and synagogues were packed — more Americans worshiping together than decades earlier, possibly more than ever. A 1958 University of Chicago study warned "the most dangerous threat hanging over American society is the threat of leisure." *Life* magazine (Feb 1964) celebrated Americans' "glut of leisure":

> "As a matter of fact, mankind now possesses for the first time the tools and knowledge to create whatever kind of world he wants.... Not only are Americans flocking into bowling leagues and garden clubs, they are satisfying their gregarious urges in countless neighborhood committees to improve the local roads and garbage collections."

**Political engagement was rising**: Robert E. Lane (1959) noted the ratio of political activists to population had "generally increased over the past fifty years." Daniel Bell and Virginia Held: "there is more participation than ever before in America." Voting turnout rose 1.6% every four years from 1920-1960; straight-line projection suggested ~70% by the bicentennial.

**Trust peaked**: In 1964, trust in neighbors reached **77%** (up from 66% during/after WWII). Gallup found desire for children to go into politics nearly doubled. The civic-minded WWII generation (JFK's cohort) was picking up the torch of leadership.

**But the era wasn't perfect**: Legal segregation, gender inequality (Betty Friedan not yet published), environmental degradation (Rachel Carson just exposing it), grinding rural poverty, infant mortality at 26 per 1,000 (44 for Black infants — nearly 4x worse than end of century). Nevertheless, engagement in community affairs and sense of shared identity had never been greater in modern America, and education — "by far the best predictor" of civic engagement — was rapidly expanding. The demographic promise of the baby boom meant swelling membership rolls ahead when boomers reached peak "joining" years.

**Doug McAdam** on the "can do" generation:

> "We were a 'can do' people, who accomplished whatever we set out to do. We had licked the Depression, turned the tide in World War II, and rebuilt Europe after the war.... Freedom Summer was an audacious undertaking consistent with the exaggerated sense of importance and potency shared by the privileged members of America's postwar generation."

### Introduction of "Social Capital"

**Social capital**: connections among individuals — social networks and norms of reciprocity and trustworthiness that arise from them. Closely related to "civic virtue" but emphasizes civic virtue is most powerful when **embedded in dense networks of reciprocal social relations**. Physical capital = physical objects; human capital = properties of individuals; social capital = connections among individuals. A society of many virtuous but isolated individuals is NOT rich in social capital.

Term independently invented at least **6 times** in the 20th century:
1. **L. J. Hanifan** (1916): West Virginia school supervisor urging community involvement

> "those tangible substances [that] count for most in the daily lives of people: namely good will, fellowship, sympathy, and social intercourse among the individuals and families who make up a social unit.... The individual is helpless socially, if left to himself.... If he comes into contact with his neighbor, and they with other neighbors, there will be an accumulation of social capital."

2. **1950s**: Canadian sociologists on suburban club memberships
3. **1960s**: **Jane Jacobs** on urban neighborliness
4. **1970s**: **Glenn Loury** on the social legacy of slavery
5. **1980s**: **Pierre Bourdieu** and **Ekkehart Schlicht** on social/economic resources in networks
6. **1980s**: **James S. Coleman** on social context of education

### Individual vs. Collective Faces

**Private face**: "Networking" — most get jobs because of whom we know, not what we know (**Ronald Burt**: executives with bounteous Rolodex files enjoy faster career advancement). **Claude S. Fischer**: "Social networks are important in all our lives, often for finding jobs, more often for finding a helping hand, companionship, or a shoulder to cry on."

**Public face**: Social capital has "externalities" — a well-connected individual in a poorly connected society is not as productive as a well-connected individual in a well-connected society. If neighbors watch each other's homes and lower crime, I benefit even if personally away.

**Networks sustain norms of reciprocity**:
- **Specific reciprocity**: I'll do this if you do that
- **Generalized reciprocity**: I'll do this without expecting anything specific back, confident someone will return the favor — the Golden Rule
- Gold Beach, Oregon Fire Department T-shirt: "Come to our breakfast, we'll come to your fire" — specific reciprocity, but onlookers smile recognizing underlying generalized reciprocity (firefighters will come even if you don't)

A society with generalized reciprocity is more efficient — money more efficient than barter. Trustworthiness lubricates social life. Frequent interaction among diverse people → norms of generalized reciprocity. Dense social ties facilitate gossip and reputation cultivation — essential foundation for trust. This is why the **diamond trade** (extreme fraud possibilities) concentrates in close-knit ethnic enclaves. Economic and political dealing embedded in dense networks → incentives for opportunism and malfeasance reduced.

### Bridging vs. Bonding Social Capital

**Most important distinction**:
- **Bonding** (exclusive): inward-looking, reinforcing homogeneous groups (ethnic fraternal orgs, country clubs, church-based women's reading groups). Good for undergirding specific reciprocity, mobilizing solidarity, furnishing start-up financing/markets/labor for local entrepreneurs. Good for **"getting by"**
- **Bridging** (inclusive): outward-looking, encompassing diverse social cleavages (civil rights movement, ecumenical orgs, many youth service groups). Better for linkage to external assets and information diffusion. Crucial for **"getting ahead"**

**Mark Granovetter**: "Weak" ties (distant acquaintances in different circles) actually more valuable than "strong" ties when seeking jobs or political allies.

Bonding = sociological **superglue**; Bridging = sociological **WD-40**. Groups can do both simultaneously: **Knights of Columbus** bridged ethnic cleavages while bonding along religious/gender lines. Internet chat groups bridge geography, gender, age while being homogeneous in education and ideology. Bonding and bridging are "more or less" dimensions, not either-or categories.

> Thomas Greene (1829, New Bedford lyceum): "We come from all the divisions, ranks and classes of society... While we mingle together in these pursuits, we shall learn to know each other more intimately; we shall remove many of the prejudices which ignorance or partial acquaintance with each other had fostered.... We may return to our homes and firesides with kindlier feelings toward one another, because we have learned to know one another better."

### American Cultural Tension

"Social capital" is new language for a very old debate about community vs. individualism. Historical oscillation from Pilgrims' escape to Emerson's "Self-Reliance," Thoreau's "Civil Disobedience," Whitman's "Song of Myself."

> "Our national myths often exaggerate the role of individual heroes and understate the importance of collective effort." — Putnam

**David Hackett Fischer**: Paul Revere's alarm succeeded only because of networks of civic engagement — towns had well-organized local militia. The myth of rugged individualism strikes a powerful inner chord but misleads.

**Contemporary civic malaise**: 1987 survey: 53% of boomers thought parents' generation better at "being a concerned citizen"; 77% said nation worse because of "less involvement in community activities." 1999: Two-thirds said civic life had weakened; 80% said there should be more emphasis on community.

### Book Structure and Method

Putnam's method: **"count things"** to curb nostalgia. Two-source rule — never report anything unless two independent sources confirm it. Like meteorologists measuring global warming, making imperfect inferences from available evidence. American history is a story of ups and downs — not just downs — of civic engagement, collapse and renewal. Within living memory, bonds were becoming stronger, not weaker.

### Illustrative Story: John Lambert and Andy Boschma

**October 29, 1997**: John Lambert (64) and Andy Boschma (33) knew each other only through bowling league at Ypsi-Arbor Lanes, Ypsilanti, Michigan. Lambert had been on kidney transplant waiting list for three years. Boschma unexpectedly offered to donate a kidney. The newspaper photograph revealed Boschma is white and Lambert is African American — they bowled together. "In small ways like this, and in larger ways, too, we Americans need to reconnect with one another." That is the simple argument of this book.

## Chapter 2: Political Participation

### Voting Decline

- **1960**: 62.8% voted (Kennedy vs. Nixon)
- **1996**: 48.9% voted — roughly a quarter decline over 36 years
- Outside the South, the slide since 1960 is the **longest decline in American history**
- Registration requirements relaxed (motor voter cost states $100 million), yet turnout declined — even facing lower hurdle, fewer make the jump
- Jim Crow laws (introduced 1890s) artificially depressed national averages for 70 years; the 1965 Voting Rights Act gave America credit for including southern blacks, obscuring that fewer of those who always had the right were exercising it
- **Not in nearly two centuries have so many American citizens freely abstained from voting**

### Voting Decline Is Almost Entirely Generational

**Intracohort change** (individual behavior shifts, can happen quickly and reverse quickly — like SUV fad) vs. **intercohort change** (generational replacement, slower, harder to reverse — like changing sexual mores). Example: Much change in sexual mores was intercohort — stricter beliefs gradually replaced by later generation with more relaxed norms, even though relatively few adults actually changed their views.

- Very little net decline in voting attributable to individual change; virtually all is generational
- Baby boomers and their children have been less likely to vote throughout their lives, whatever their station and level of interest
- **"Every year the Grim Reaper removes another swath of the most politically engaged generation"** in the American electorate
- This explains why decline continues ineluctably despite efforts like motor voter registration

### Political Knowledge and Interest

- Americans today know about as much civics as grandparents despite much more education — "surprising that civics knowledge has not improved accordingly"
- **Average college graduate today knows little more about public affairs than the average high school graduate in the 1940s**
- Post-baby boom generations substantially less knowledgeable despite proliferating information sources
- News and information gap first opened with boomers in 1970s, widened considerably with Gen X
- Daily newspaper readership among under-35s: two-thirds (1965) → one-third (1990)
- TV news viewership same age group: 52% → 41%
- Interest in current events gradually ebbed by roughly 20% over a quarter century (1974-1998, Roper)
- Decline is generation-specific, arguing against view that public affairs have simply become boring

### Cooperative Activities Decline Most

Activities requiring coordination declined most sharply — "If I'm the only member of a committee, it's not a 'committee.'" **Table 1** (1973-74 to 1993-94, Roper surveys):
- Served as officer of club/organization: **-42%**
- Worked for political party: **-42%**
- Served on committee for local organization: **-39%**
- Attended public meeting on town/school affairs: **-35%**
- Attended political rally or speech: **-34%**
- Participated in at least one of twelve activities: **-25%**
- Wrote letter to paper: -14%
- Wrote article for magazine/newspaper: -10%

Political systems need both articulation of grievances (individual) AND coalition-building (collective). Activities that bring citizens together and embody social capital declined most rapidly.

- **Utterly uninvolved rose**: From 46% (1973) to 64% (1994) engaged in none of twelve activities — 32 million fewer Americans involved
- **Active citizens halved**: Those doing 3+ activities dropped from 20% to 11%
- Decline appeared in all demographics — men/women, blacks/whites, cities/suburbs/rural, all regions, upper/lower class
- **Absolute declines greatest among educated**: college-educated public meeting attendance fell from 34% to 18%; **relative declines worse for less educated**

> "We remain, in short, reasonably well-informed spectators of public affairs, but many fewer of us actually partake in the game."

### Political Alienation

- 1960s: Three in four trusted government to do right most of the time; one in four felt "people like me don't have much say"
- 1990s: Three in four didn't trust government
- April 1966 (Vietnam, race riots): 66% rejected "people running the country don't really care"
- December 1997 (longest peace/prosperity period): 57% endorsed the same cynical view
- Today's cynicism may be more accurate, but it undermines motivation for involvement
- Declining electoral participation "merely the most visible symptom of broader disengagement from community life" — like a fever, even more important as sign of deeper trouble than as malady itself. **"It is not just from the voting booth that Americans are increasingly AWOL"**

## Chapter 3: Civic Participation

> "Americans of all ages, all stations in life, and all types of disposition are forever forming associations.... Nothing, in my view, deserves more attention than the intellectual and moral associations in America." — **Alexis de Tocqueville**

### The Paradox: Association Explosion vs. Membership Collapse

National nonprofit organizations tripled (10,299 in 1968 to 22,901 in 1997), but most lack mass membership. Median membership fell from ~10,000 (1962) to 1,000 (1988). **Organizations tripled; average membership one-tenth as large** — proliferation of letterheads, not grassroots participation boom.

**Geographic concentration tells the story**: Old organizations headquartered in heartland (Boy Scouts in Irving, TX; Knights of Columbus in New Haven; Lions in Oak Brook, IL; NAACP in Baltimore). New organizations clustered in Washington, DC — AARP at 6th and E Streets (not Florida/Arizona where constituents are); Children's Defense Fund, Common Cause, NOW, Greenpeace, National Wildlife Federation, Friends of the Earth. **Rotary alone has nearly twice as many chapters as all eighty-three public-interest groups combined.**

Of eighty-three public-interest groups founded in early 1970s, two-thirds had no local chapters at all; another 12% had no more than twenty-five chapters nationwide. These groups speak faithfully on behalf of citizens but are not really composed of citizen members like church congregations or reading groups.

### Tertiary Associations

- **AARP**: 400,000 members (1960) to 33 million (mid-1990s), but less than 10% belong to local chapters. "Grassroots activities on life support" per AARP staff. Founded as mail-order insurance firm; more in common with commercial mail-order organizations than face-to-face associations.
- For vast majority of "tertiary" association members, membership = writing a check or reading a newsletter. Few attend meetings; many organizations never have meetings at all.
- Bond between NWF/NRA members more like two Yankees fans on opposite coasts — shared symbols/leaders/common enemy, but unaware of each other's existence
- **Veterans' organizations** nearly tripled in the *Encyclopedia* (1980-1997), yet membership among American men and women *fell* by 10%. **Trade unions**: Encyclopedia listings grew 4%; fraction of employees belonging plummeted 35%+. More organizations ≠ more members.

### Chapter-Based Organizations: The Real Story

**Figure 8** reveals the pattern across 32 national chapter-based associations (1900-1997):
- Long upward wave 1900-1960s; more women in women's clubs, more rural residents in Grange, more youths in Scouts

> "As the decades passed, America seemed more and more to fit Tocqueville's description."

- **Great Depression**: Sharp dip — League of Women Voters, Elks, Moose, Knights of Columbus membership cut in half (1930-35). Youth organizations (4-H, Boy Scouts, Girl Scouts) immune to economic distress affecting adults.
- **WWII boom**: Two decades following 1945 = one of most vital periods of community involvement in American history. "Market share" for all thirty-two organizations skyrocketed. Encompassed virtually every organization — "old-fashioned" (Grange, Elks) to newer service clubs (Lions, League of Women Voters).
- **Decline begins**: Membership rates plateaued 1957, peaked early 1960s, sustained decline from 1969. Rates more than doubled 1940-45 to peak, then slightly less than halved by 1997.
- **NAACP**: Spiked sharply during WWII, collapsed early 1950s, regained highest levels early 1960s, then stagnated and slumped from 1970s onward.
- Very different organizations — different constituency, age, leadership — entered rough water at about the same time.

### PTA: Dramatic Rise and Fall

- Percentage of parents who joined more than doubled (1945-1960) — continuing uninterrupted growth since founding (1910)
- Average annual addition: 1.6% of all American families with kids (400,000 families/year)
- **Shocking reversal (1960)**: Average annual loss of 250,000 families/year
- Membership returned to 1943 levels, utterly erasing postwar gains
- Between 1990 and 1997, PTA lost half a million members even though families with children grew by 2 million and public school enrollment grew by 5 million
- One of most impressive organizational success stories in American history — "easy to sneer at cookies, cider, small talk in cynical era," but membership betokened commitment to participate in practical, child-focused form of community life

### Active Participation: Dramatic Collapse

- **Leadership roles sliced by more than 50%** (1973-1994): number of men and women taking any leadership role in any local organization
- Dismaying trend **accelerated after 1985**: ten short years (1985-1994), active involvement fell 45% — **nearly half of America's civic infrastructure obliterated in barely a decade**
- **Club meeting attendance** (DDB Needham, 87,000+ Americans): 12/year (1975-76) → 5/year (1999), a 58% drop
- 1975-76: 64% attended at least one club meeting; 1999: only 38% did

> "In short, in the mid-1970s nearly two-thirds of all Americans attended club meetings, but by the late 1990s nearly two-thirds of all Americans *never* do."

### Time Diary and Consumer Spending Evidence

- Time devoted to organizational activity: **3.7 hours/month (1965)** → **2.9 (1975)** → **2.3 (1985 and 1995)**
- Average day 1965: 7% spent time in community organization; 1995: 3%
- Nearly half of 1960s Americans invested time weekly in clubs/associations; less than one-quarter in 1990s
- Decline attributable to **generational replacement** — each successive generation investing less
- Among college graduates: average club meetings/year fell **55%** (thirteen to six)
- If current rate continues, clubs would become **extinct in less than 20 years**
- Consumer spending: 6 cents of every leisure dollar on club/fraternal dues (1929); post-WWII dip to 4 cents (TV arrival), recovery to 5 cents by end of 1950s; fell from 5 to 3 cents over last three decades — down 40% from postwar peak

**Summary**: First two-thirds of 20th century: involvement rose steadily (except Depression). Last third: only mailing list membership expanded — creation of "tertiary" associations whose members never meet. Face-to-face involvement plummeted. Formal membership edged down 10-20%; **active involvement collapsed** at astonishing rate, more than halving most participation indexes. Many Americans claim "membership" but stopped attending meetings, serving as officers, doing committee work — despite rapid increases in education.

## Chapter 4: Religious Participation

### America's Religious Exceptionalism

- US one of most religiously observant countries; most God-believing, religion-adhering, fundamentalist, and religiously fecund country
- **Phillip Hammond**: Religious adherence grew steadily from 17% (1776) to 62% (1980)
- **"The church is people"** (Rev. Craig McMullen, Dorchester Temple Baptist Church, Boston) — relationships, not buildings

### Churches as Social Capital Repositories

- Nearly half of all associational memberships are church-related; half of all personal philanthropy and volunteering occurs in religious context
- Churches provide incubator for civic skills: speeches, run meetings, manage disagreements, bear administrative responsibility
- Churchgoers substantially more likely to be involved in secular organizations, vote, participate politically, have deeper informal social connections
- **Religiosity rivals education** as powerful correlate of civic engagement
- Regular church attendees talk with **40% more people daily**
- 75-80% of church members give to charity vs. 55-60% of nonmembers; 50-60% volunteer vs. 30-35%
- Even excluding religious causes, active religious involvement among strongest predictors of both philanthropy and volunteering
- **Kenneth Wald**: "Religious ideals are potentially powerful sources of commitment and motivation" — but social ties in religious communities at least as important as beliefs per se
- American religious communities spend **$15-20 billion annually** on social services
- **Partners for Sacred Places**: 93% of older urban congregations provide community services; 80% of beneficiaries not congregation members
- **Industrial Areas Foundation** (most successful grassroots community organizing model) rooted in local parishes

> "[T]he Black church functioned as the institutional center of the modern civil rights movement.... Churches provided the movement with an organized mass base; a leadership of clergymen largely economically independent of the larger white society and skilled in the art of managing people and resources."

- **C. Eric Lincoln**: Black church "has been and is for Black America the mother of our culture, the champion of our Freedom, and hallmark of our civilization"

### Trends: Rise and Fall

- Survey evidence and denominational reports generally consistent: rise in church membership from 1930s to ~1960, plateau, then long slow slump of ~10%
- "No religion" rose from 2% (1967) to 11% (1990s)
- Church attendance: roughly 40-45% claim attendance in given week; most reasonable summary: declined modestly but unmistakably by 10-12% over last quarter century, slump more marked mid-1980s to mid-1990s
- Overreporting possibly as high as 50%; may be higher today than a generation ago — actual decline may be worse
- **Church-related social activities declined even faster**: 1950s roughly 1 in 4 belonged to church groups; by late 1980s/1990s cut to 1 in 8. University of Michigan-NORC study: 50% decline (1957-1976). GSS: 20%+ decline (1974-1996).
- **Time spent on religion**: 1 hour 37 minutes/week (1965) → 1 hour 7 minutes (1995). Fraction spending *any* time on religion fell by nearly one-half.
- Over last 3-4 decades: ~10% less likely to claim church membership; actual attendance/involvement fell 25-50%
- In all three domains (politics, organizations, religion): more demanding form of involvement showed greater decline. "Seen from without, institutional edifice appears virtually intact... When examined more closely: decay has consumed load-bearing beams of civic infrastructure."

### Generational and Denominational Shifts

- Decline largely attributable to generational differences — any given cohort hasn't reduced observance, but more recent generations less observant than parents
- **Wade Clark Roof**: Two-thirds of boomers dropped out of religious tradition; less than half returned. Even in forties and fifties, boomers remain less involved than middle-aged people a generation ago.
- College freshmen "never" attending church: 9% (1966) → 18% (late 1990s); "none" as religious preference: 7% → 14%
- High schoolers attending weekly: 40% (late 1970s) → 32% (early 1990s)
- Country becoming divided into two groups: devoutly observant and entirely unchurched, fewer moderates — sociological substratum of "culture wars"

**Denominational shifts**: Mainline Protestant denominations (Methodist, Presbyterian, Episcopal) heavily lost "market share"; evangelical groups (Southern Baptist, Pentecostal, Assemblies of God) grew but barely matched population growth. Growth at both ends of spectrum; middle collapsed. Self-identified Protestants fell by 12-15 percentage points (nearly one-fifth) in last third of 20th century. Catholic share rose, partly through Hispanic immigration (now one-quarter of American Catholics). "No religion" roughly quadrupled.

> **Wade Clark Roof and William McKinney**: "Large numbers of young, well-educated, middle-class youth... defected from the churches.... In this climate of expressive individualism, religion tends to become 'privatized,' or more anchored in the personal realms."

**Privatized religion** may be morally compelling but embodies less social capital. More people "surfing" from congregation to congregation. "Believers perhaps, but 'belongers,' not."

### Evangelical Social Capital: Inward, Not Outward

- Evangelicals attend church more, give 2.8% of income (vs. 1.6% for mainline), have more close friends in same congregation
- **George Marsden**: "The fundamentalist churches offer far stronger community to their members... some of the most cohesive non-ethnic communities in America"
- But social capital invested at home: among evangelicals, church attendance **not** correlated with community organization membership
- Most evangelical volunteering supports congregational life — teaching Sunday school, singing in choir — not extending to broader community
- Mainline Protestants and Catholics more likely to transfer civic skills to wider community
- **Robert Wuthnow**: "Mainline Protestant churches encourage civic engagement in the wider community, whereas evangelical churches apparently do not"
- During civil rights era: black civic engagement positively correlated with mainline black churches, negatively with black fundamentalist denominations
- Fact that evangelical Christianity rising and mainline falling means religion less effective as foundation for "bridging" social capital

> Wuthnow: "Religion may have a salutary effect on civil society by encouraging its members to worship... But religion is likely to have a diminished impact on society if that is the only role it plays. What interested Tocqueville about voluntary organizations was... their ability to forge connections across large segments of the population, spanning communities and regions."

## Chapter 5: Connections in the Workplace

### Union Membership Decline

- Economic shift from industrial to service economy explains only **one-quarter** of union decline
- All structural changes (industry mix, women's employment, regional shifts) account for **barely half**
- Within specific jobs: manufacturing -62%, mining -79%, construction -78%, transportation -60%, service sector -40%
- Only government briefly resisted (1962-79 after Kennedy legalized collective bargaining for executive branch)
- Comprehensive study: "virtually all the decline in unionization between 1977 and 1991 seems to be due to decline in demand for union representation" — fewer workers want to join
- Not primarily about public disgust (resentment actually declined); core issue: skepticism about "membership" itself, not just "union"

> "The young worker thinks primarily of himself. We are experiencing the cult of the individual, and labor is taking a beating preaching the comfort of coalition." — **Peter Pestillo**, labor economist

### Professional Associations: Growth Masked by Expansion

- Absolute membership grew impressively (AMA: 126,042 in 1945 → 296,637 in 1995; ABA: 34,134 → 357,933; IEEE: 111,610 in 1963 → 242,800)
- But "market share" declined: AMA peaked 1959, ADA/AIA peaked 1970, ABA peaked 1977; American Nurses Association cut from 18% → 9%
- American Institute of Architects membership more than doubled (1970-1997), but fraction of architects who were members fell 41% → 28%
- Even specialized subfield associations stagnated — specialization hypothesis doesn't hold
- Follows same pattern: growth during first two-thirds of century, stagnation and decline during last third

### The Workplace Hypothesis

**Hypothesis**: Perhaps friendships simply moved from front porch to water cooler. More Americans working outside home (67% in 1997 vs. 59% in 1950). **Arlie Russell Hochschild**: Workplace increasingly serves as sanctuary from stresses of marriage, children, housework.

**Alan Wolfe** interviews: Jeremy Toole: "These days people get about 90 percent of their social connections from the workplace." Diana Hamilton: "People's lives revolve around their work." Elizabeth Tyler: "I feel very much like I belong to a community of work."

**Evidence of workplace social capital**: 9 in 10 workers agree "I look forward to being with the people I work with." About half have at least one close personal tie at work. Over half of all conversations took place in workplace. Workplace much more diverse racially and politically than most other settings.

**Three major counterarguments**:

1. **No evidence of increase**: No evidence whatever that socializing in workplace has actually increased — some indirect evidence suggests the opposite direction
2. **Glass half-empty**: Co-workers account for less than 10% of our friends; workplace ties tend to be casual, not deeply supportive. When asked to list closest friends, less than half put even one co-worker. Neighbors more likely to appear than coworkers.
3. **Workplace restructuring damages ties**: Downsizing, contingent work, restructuring. **Peter Cappelli**: "The old employment system of secure, lifetime jobs with predictable advancement and stable pay is dead." Fastest-growing 1980s industries: "outplacement" services ($35M in 1980 → $350M in 1989). Nearly **30% of workers** have nonstandard jobs (part-time, temps, contractors). Part-time workers have only two-thirds as many work friends. Three-quarters of independent contractors have no regular work colleagues.

> "World War II veterans joining IBM were instructed to consult with their wives before taking the job, because 'once you came aboard you were a member of the corporate family for life.'"

**Charles Heckscher** (interviews with restructured firms): Most common reaction: "Put your head down." Workers: "We're all alone out here. It's been very stressful." "Rather than turning on each other, most people drifted apart, becoming more isolated."

**Job satisfaction declined**: 1955, 44% preferred hours on job; 1999, barely one-third. Roper: "completely satisfied" fell from 46% (mid-1970s) → 36% (1992). Two-thirds of employers monitor voice mail, email, phone calls, computer files, or videotape workers (1999 AMA survey). Rights of free speech and privacy "insecure in the workplace."

> **Alan Wolfe**: "Because we form such ties to promote the highly secular activities of getting and spending, friendships and connections developed at work are generally assumed to have a more instrumental character: we use people, and they use us.... the instrumental character of the latter cannot be an adequate substitute for the loss of the former."

**For the one American adult in three who is not employed, workplace ties are nonexistent. The workplace is not the salvation for our fraying civil society.**

## Chapter 6: Informal Social Connections

### Family Dinners and Socializing Decline

- Family togetherness declined across all forms (1976-1997, Roper polls of families with children 8-17):
  - Vacationing together: 53%→38%
  - Watching TV together: 54%→41%
  - Attending religious services together: 38%→31%
  - "Just sitting and talking" together: 53%→43%

> "It is hard not to read these figures as evidence of rapidly loosening family bonds."

### The Decline of Schmoozing

- Going to bars/nightclubs/taverns declined 40-50% (mid-1970s to late 1990s) — three independent survey series confirm
- Full-service restaurants fell by one-quarter; bars and luncheonettes cut in half; fast-food outlets doubled
- Unlike neighborhood bars where "everybody knows your name," fast-food patrons are anonymous — **Ray Oldenburg's "great good place"** disappeared. Americans chose to "grab a bite and run rather than sit a while and chat"

### The Collapse of Card Playing

- 1940: Playing cards in 87% of homes (vs. 83% radios, 36% telephones)
- Mid-20th century: one pack sold each year for every two Americans aged 14+
- 1958: 35 million Americans played bridge (nearly one-third of adults); millions in regular card clubs; 1961 Nebraska survey: nearly 1 in 5 adults in regular foursomes
- Card game sales track civic involvement precisely: steady growth 1890s-1920s, Depression slump, explosive post-WWII growth, then precipitous decline
- Mid-1970s: ~40% played monthly (card players to moviegoers 4:1)
- 1981-1999: frequency dropped from 16x/year to 8x/year
- 500 million card games played annually but falling by 25 million/year
- If one conversation per ten games: **50 million fewer "microdeliberations" about community affairs annually**
- Average age of ACBL members: 64 and rising — concentrated in retirement communities, "sociological equivalent of isolated ecological niches where endangered species often make a last stand"
- Substitutes are solitary: Internet bridge focused "entirely on the game itself, with very little social small talk"; megacasinos feature "acres of lonely 'players' hunched in silence over one-armed bandits"

### Neighborhood and Informal Ties

- "Spend a social evening with someone who lives in your neighborhood" (GSS 1974-1998):
  - Married people: ~30x/year → ~20x/year (one-third decline)
  - Single people: ~50x/year → ~35x/year
  - Compared with mid-1950s, neighborhood ties perhaps **less than half as strong**
- Sending greeting cards declined 15-20%; predates Internet by a decade; at any given age, Americans send fewer now than a generation ago — generational pattern
- Neighborhood watch groups: "frail replacement for vanished social capital — sociological AstroTurf, suitable only where the real thing won't grow." We invest more in guns, dogs, and locks than in social capital for crime defense.
- **Barrett A. Lee**: neighborhood organizations first appeared near end of 19th century and were well represented prior to the Great Depression — not newcomers
- Organized social life at neighborhood level (street carnivals, picnics, potlucks, dances) **much more vibrant in first half of 20th century** than in its waning years

**Time diary studies (1965-1995)**:
- % recording any informal socializing on a diary day: 65% (1965) → 39% (1995)
- Average daily time on informal socializing: 85 minutes → 57 minutes
- NPD Group (1992-1998): Largest changes were worship and visiting friends — both fell 20%+
- Decline uniform across all demographics: women and men, all ages, all classes, all regions, big cities and small towns, married and single

**The Education Paradox**: Schmoozing is higher among single and childless people; their numbers rose. Should have increased informal socializing — opposite of what we found. We might have expected the real-life equivalent of *Cheers* and *Friends* to replace civic organizations. Instead: "we witnessed the decline of the latter without compensating increase in the former." **"Something else must be even more powerfully depressing the rate of schmoozing."**

### Sports: From Doing to Watching

- Participation fell 10-20% as fraction of population in most sports; team sports hit hardest
- Softball dropped by roughly one-third (mid-1980s to late 1990s) — four data sources agree
- New sports (in-line skating, snowboarding) not as social; most dramatic increases in "in-home" activities (treadmills)
- **Declines sharpest among the young**: exercise class attendance among 20-somethings halved (8x/year to 3x); doubled among 60+. **Twin trends: rising activity among older, falling among younger** — same generational profile as social/political participation
- Even youth soccer stalled in 1990s after 1980s boom; Title IX gains didn't offset general decline
- Health clubs: despite "all the rage" image, **three times as many Americans play cards regularly as visit a health club**. Only among single, 20-something college graduates are health club visits more common than card games — only 1 in 15 adults falls into that category

**Bowling**: Most popular competitive sport in America; bowlers outnumber joggers 2:1, soccer players 3:1, tennis 4:1. Solidly middle-American across gender, class, age.
- 1980-1993: total bowlers increased 10%, league bowling decreased by **more than 40%**
- Peak mid-1960s: 8% of men, 5% of women were bowling team members
- League bowlers consume **3x as much beer and pizza** as solo bowlers — the social interaction and civic conversations over beer and pizza are what solo bowlers forgo
- If decline continues, league bowling would vanish entirely within first decade of new century

**Spectator sports** attendance nearly doubled per capita since 1960s. Playing musical instruments cut from 6x/year (1976) to 3x/year (1999); households with someone who plays fell from 51% (1978) to 38% (1997).

> "In football, as in politics: watching a team play is not the same thing as playing on a team."

## Chapter 7: Altruism, Volunteering, and Philanthropy

### Social Networks Drive Giving

- **"Someone asked me"** is the most common answer for why people volunteer; "Nobody asked" is why blood donors don't give
- Fund-raising = friend-raising: more social networks → more recruitment
- **Volunteering fosters more volunteering**: youth who volunteered are half again as likely to donate as adults and twice as likely to volunteer later. Receiving help makes people more likely to help others — ripple effect.
- Tocqueville's **"habits of the heart"**: giving, volunteering, and joining are mutually reinforcing
- Blood donation fostered by both churchgoing and club attendance: attenders donate at ~16% vs. non-attenders ~11%

### Philanthropy: The Rise and Fall

- Per capita giving nearly doubled 1960-1995 ($280 → $522 in 1993 dollars)
- But our income also rose — real per capita spending on flowers/seeds nearly tripled; recreation nearly quadrupled
- **Philanthropy relative to income**: in 1990s Americans donated smaller share than any time since 1940s
- **As share of income, declined 29%** (2.26% in 1964 → 1.61% in 1998)
- 1960: gave ~$1 per $2 spent on recreation; 1997: gave <$.50 per $2

**Parallel with social capital, not economy**:
- During Great Depression (1929-39): income fell 3%, but charity rose 25%+
- Next two decades (1939-60): income soared 74%, charity rose at same slow pace as during Depression
- After 1960: through booms and busts, generosity steadily shriveled
- **Waxing and waning of generosity tracks social capital, not financial capital**

**Religious and secular giving both declined**:
- Protestant "benevolences" (external charity) fell 38% since 1968; congregational finances down only 12% — growing slice of shrinking pie consumed internally
- Evangelicals give larger fraction of income but their per member contribution fell even faster than mainline (**John and Sylvia Ronsvalle**)
- Catholic giving: slashed 59% (1960-1989)
- United Way: now less than half the 1960 level, reached level not seen since early in century
- Self-reported giving: nearly half contributed monthly (1980s) → barely one in three (prosperous mid-1990s)

**If generosity matched 1960 levels**: +$4B/year for United Way, +$20B for congregations, +~$50B total

**Actual explanation**: breadth and simultaneity of post-1960 declines suggests wider social change, not foibles of particular organizations. After years of rising generosity, Americans became steadily more tight-fisted precisely when we also disengaged from social life of communities.

## Chapter 8: Reciprocity, Honesty, and Trust

### Generalized Reciprocity

- **David Hume's farmer parable**: mutual labor depends on confidence — both lose harvests without mutual confidence
- **Michael Taylor**: individual act combines short-term altruism + long-term self-interest in vague expectation of future help
- Norm of generalized reciprocity fundamental to civilized life; all prominent moral codes contain equivalent of Golden Rule. Ironic perversion: "Do unto others before they do unto you"
- **Tocqueville**: American democracy worked because Americans pursued **"self-interest rightly understood"**
- Examples: raking leaves before they blow onto neighbor's yard, lending dime for parking meter, caring for child of crack-head one flight down
- When we can relax guard, **transaction costs** reduced; trusting communities have measurable economic advantage; background stress may explain why life expectancy enhanced in more trustful communities
- **"Generalized reciprocity is a community asset, but generalized gullibility is not"** — trustworthiness, not simply trust, is the key ingredient. **Trust in government is not the same thing as social trust** — they must be kept distinct.

### How Trust Is Maintained

**Legal system**: courts and law enforcement — but if needed for simplest agreements, escalating costs preclude cooperation. **Diego Gambetta**: "Societies which rely heavily on use of force likely to be less efficient, more costly, and more unpleasant."

**Social fabric**: Dense networks make reputations worth more than gains from treachery. If collaborators are members of tightly knit community, they'll encounter each other again — or hear through grapevine. **Honesty encouraged by dense social networks.**

### Thick Trust vs. Thin Trust

- **Thick trust**: in longtime corner store owner (Max at corner store you've known for years)
- **Thin trust**: in "the generalized other" — even more useful, extending radius of trust beyond personal knowledge
- As social fabric becomes threadbare, effectiveness in transmitting reputations declines, power to undergird norms of honesty enfeebled
- **Wendy Rahn and John Transue**: social trust = "standing decision" to give most people the benefit of the doubt
- Trusting citizens volunteer more, give more to charity, participate more, serve on juries, give blood more, comply with tax obligations, more tolerant of minority views
- **Experimental evidence**: people who believe others are honest are themselves less likely to lie, cheat, or steal
- **Honesty, civic engagement, and social trust are mutually reinforcing**

### Four Decades of Dwindling Trust

- "Most people can be trusted": ~55% (1960) → ~35% (late 1990s) among adults
- Among high school students: ~55% (1975) → ~25% (mid-1990s) — even sharper
- Perceptions of honesty/morality: 50% affirmed (1952) → 27% (1998)
- Social trust rose from mid-1940s to mid-1960s, peaking 1964; then long decline
- Most decline attributable to **generational succession**: replacement of ~80% trust generation with barely ~40% trust generation — at century's end, each individual cohort almost as trusting as ever, but the replacement effect drives aggregate decline
- Trust patterns reflect realistic assessment: blacks less trusting, financially distressed less, big-city dwellers less, crime victims less. Small-town advantages genuine: car dealers in small towns perform far fewer unnecessary repairs; cheating condemned 3x more in small towns.

### Crime and the Rise of Lawyering

- Crime rates rose sharply in mid-1960s, just as social capital/trust began declining. Crime may be symptom of weakened social control.
- America had **fewer lawyers per capita in 1970 than in 1900** — two world wars, Depression, New Deal, transformation from rustic to metropolitan nation — none raised lawyering ratio
- After 1970: ratio of lawyers exploded, more than doubling in 25 years
- Legal profession grew **3x faster than professions as a whole** after 1970; by 1995 **34% more lawyers than doctors** (in 1970, 3% fewer); 1 lawyer per 2.1 engineers (1970: 1 per 4.5)
- Largest increase: **"preventive lawyering"** — beginning ~1970, informal understandings no longer seemed adequate. Spouses, neighbors, business partners, parents and children all abruptly began demanding to "get it in writing." Suddenness and timing uncannily similar to other social capital measures.

> **Marc Galanter**: "Like the provider of artificial hormones that supplement the diminished supply coursing through the body, the lawyer contrives enforceability to supplement the failing supply of reciprocity, moral obligation, and fellow-feeling.... Lawyers contrive to provide 'artificial trust.'"

- Even trust among lawyers impaired: as stability of social networks declined, lawyers worry less about reputation for honesty
- **One of most revealing indicators of fraying social fabric**: we rely increasingly on formal institutions to accomplish what we used to accomplish through informal networks reinforced by generalized reciprocity — that is, through social capital

## Chapter 9: Against the Tide? Small Groups, Social Movements, and the Net

### Small Groups

- **Robert Wuthnow**: 40% of Americans claim involvement in a small group; ~half are church-related (whose decline documented in Ch. 4). Nearly 5% in self-help groups; nearly as many in book/hobby clubs.
- Reading groups: despite claims of boom, as many Americans involved in 1960s-1970s as late 1990s. Proportion of single female college graduates actually fell from 1 in 3 to 1 in 4 (1974-1994). Historically, reading circles emerged in second half of 19th century, eventually giving birth to the suffrage movement and Progressive Era civic initiatives.
- Self-help groups (AA and 130+ analogues): ~2% currently active, ~3% lifetime; all participants outnumbered 2-to-1 by dropouts from league bowling. More than 60% have professional leaders.
- Support groups substitute for other intimate ties: participation 2-4x higher among divorced/single people
- Membership in self-help groups **completely unrelated** to any other form of group affiliation — alone among 22 types

> Wuthnow: "[T]he kind of community [these small groups] create is quite different.... The social contract binding members together asserts only the weakest of obligations. Come if you have time. Talk if you feel like it. Respect everyone's opinion. Never criticize. Leave quietly if you become dissatisfied.... We can imagine that they really substitute for families, neighborhoods, and broader community attachments that may demand lifelong commitments, when, in fact, they do not."

### Social Movements

- Social networks are quintessential resource of movement organizers
- Friendship networks (not environmental sympathies) explain who protested after Three Mile Island; social ties (not ideals) explain who was recruited to **Freedom Summer**
- Movements also *create* social capital — Freedom Summer "forged lifelong identities and solidarities" (**Doug McAdam**)
- **Kenneth Andrews**: Mississippi civil rights infrastructure affected African American political power for decades
- Whether among gays in San Francisco or evangelicals on the Mall, **act of collective protest itself creates enduring bonds of solidarity**

### Environmental Movement: Direct Mail, Not Engagement

- Membership exploded: 125,000 (1960) → 6.5 million (1990)
- Crucial innovation: **direct mail**, not deeper civic consciousness
- 1965: Audubon mailed 1 million invitations; by 1990: Greenpeace mailed **48 million letters annually**
- Izaak Walton League (no direct mail): 51,000 (1960) → 50,000 (1990); Sierra Club (with direct mail): 15,000 (1960) → 560,000 (1990)
- Direct-mail recruits: drop out more readily, participate in fewer activities, hold more extreme views, feel less attachment
- **Greenpeace** tripled to 2,350,000 (1985-1990), then lost **85% in 8 years** — no old-fashioned chapter-based org lost that much in three or four decades from postwar peak
- Members of local Legion post kept there by **personal ties among the guys**, not patriotism — tensile strength of newer organizations much weaker
- **Christopher Bosso**: supporters are less "members" than **"consumers of a cause"** — "today's hot product is tomorrow's remaindered bin"
- More than half of EDF "members": "I don't really think of myself as a member; the money I send is just a donation"
- NRA: membership tripled 1977-1996, but annual renewal rate barely 25%
- NARAL: half don't self-describe as members; 2/3 never encouraged friends to join; **John McCarthy**: members "did not talk with their friends about membership"
- **Sierra Club** survey: only 13% ever attended a single meeting
- **Audubon in Texas**: only 3-4% of 28,000 members active; comparison: every week twenty times as many Texans gather for lunch at Rotary clubs

### Pro-Life vs. Pro-Choice: Ground War vs. Air War

- **Pro-life**: thousands of church-based grassroots organizations; National Right to Life Committee: 13 million members, 7,000 local chapters
- **Pro-choice**: lacks preexisting social infrastructure; NARAL tripled to 500,000 then plunged to 190,000; only 3-5% did more than write a check
- Pro-life "ground war" adapted for social-capital-rich environment; pro-choice "air war" adapted for social-capital-poor environment
- Existence of well-developed national organization using "air war" is **sign not of presence of grassroots engagement, but of its absence**

### Religious Conservatives: The Real Grassroots Movement

- **Christian Coalition**, **Promise-Keepers**, **Operation Rescue**, **Moral Majority**: each claimed millions of active participants
- Post-WWII: center of gravity of evangelicalism moved from rural fringe toward middle-class suburbs
- **Robert Wuthnow**: Pre-1974, evangelicals less disposed to political participation; post-1974, more involved
- **Christian Smith**: "Which Christian tradition is actually doing the work of trying to influence American society? It is the evangelicals who are most walking their talk."
- Religious contacts had demonstrable impact on voting; most important predictor was social engagement in religious community, not demography or theology
- Evangelical activists: 60-70% attended church more than once a week (vs. <5% of other Americans); 3-5 times more active in all forms of civic/political life
- Created the **largest, best-organized grassroots social movement** of last quarter century

### Ballot Initiatives, Protests, and Countertrends

- Ballot initiatives skyrocketed in last third of century, but **California Commission**: "qualifying is thus no longer so much a measure of citizen interest as it is a test of fundraising ability"
- Protest participation: ever been in a march rose from 7% (1978) to 10% (1994), but explained by departure of pre-1960s non-protesters, not new protesters — marchers have **steadily and rapidly grayed**
- Promise-Keepers "Stand in the Gap" (1997): half a million men on Mall; organization virtually collapsed six months later
- **David Meyer and Sidney Tarrow**: "movement society" hypothesis ultimately concedes actual contentious action "seems to be more circumscribed than it was two decades ago"

### The Internet

- Timing: cannot be the cause — voting, giving, trusting began declining while Bill Gates was in grade school. By the time Internet reached 10% of adults (1996), decline had been underway for a quarter century.
- Early users: once controlling for education, indistinguishable from nonusers in civic engagement
- Computer-mediated communication transmits much less nonverbal information; humans remarkably effective at sensing mendacity through facial/vocal expressions — emoticons provide only "faintest trace"
- Experiments: richer media = more sociable, trusting encounters; computer-based groups find it harder to reach consensus, feel less solidarity
- **Flaming**, cheating, reneging more common; "easy in, easy out" relationships discourage social capital
- **Nohria and Eccles**: widespread CMC will require *more frequent* face-to-face encounters
- Social capital may be a *prerequisite for*, rather than a *consequence of*, effective CMC
- **Cyberbalkanization risk**: place-based heterogeneity replaced by interest-based homogeneity; filtering reduces serendipitous connections
- **Barry Wellman study**: Internet helps maintain ties over distances but physical proximity still matters — scholars who see each other often email more. Internet reinforces existing relationships rather than substituting for them.

> "The Internet will not *automatically* offset the decline in more conventional forms of social capital, but it has that potential."

**Four clearest countertrends**: youth volunteering, telecommunications/Internet, evangelical conservative grassroots activism, self-help groups. None outweighs the broader evidence of disengagement.

## SECTION THREE: Why?

## Chapter 10: Introduction

### The Mystery

In the first two-thirds of the 20th century, Americans were increasingly active civically — churches, union halls, bowling alleys, clubrooms, dinner tables. Year by year: more charitable giving, more community projects, increasingly trustworthy behavior. Then mysteriously and simultaneously began doing all those things less often.

**Current state**: Still more engaged than citizens in many other countries, but compared to our own recent past: less connected. We remain interested, critical spectators — "kibitz, but we don't play." Maintain facade of formal affiliation but rarely show up. Invented new ways of expressing demands that demand less of us. Social networks atrophied — thin, single-stranded, surf-by interactions replacing dense, multistranded bonds. **Morris Janowitz**: "communities of limited liability." Grassroots groups replaced by staff-led interest groups; place-based social capital supplanted by function-based. The decline's suddenness, thoroughness, and unexpectedness constitute an intriguing mystery. Like *Murder on the Orient Express*: more than one perpetrator.

**Education paradox**: Education is the single most powerful predictor of civic engagement. Four additional years = 30% more political interest, 40% more club attendance, 45% more volunteering. College graduates 2x as likely to serve as officer, attend public meeting, write Congress. Educational levels rose massively (HS graduates: 41% in 1960 → 82% in 1998; college degrees: 8% → 24%). Yet civic engagement declined at every educational level — among college graduates, organizational membership declined ~30%.

**Civic disengagement is an "equal opportunity affliction"**: declined among women and men, coasts and heartland, blacks and whites, small towns and metros, affluent and impoverished, Republicans and Democrats. Levels differ, but trends downward very similar everywhere. Example: 1974-1994, 18% of whites attended public meetings vs. 13% of blacks, but each race's rate cut in half. More people in rural Vermont in 1999 than metro Boston, but *fewer* than in 1959.

### Tests for Valid Explanations

1. Is the factor correlated with civic engagement?
2. Is the correlation spurious?
3. Is the factor changing in the relevant way?
4. Could the factor be the *result* rather than the *cause*?

## Chapter 11: Pressures of Time and Money

### Time Pressure: Weak Suspect

- We *feel* busier — proportion "always feeling rushed" jumped by more than half since mid-1960s
- But **McGrattan and Rogerson**: aggregate work hours roughly constant since WWII — the half century during which civic engagement first ballooned then shriveled
- **Robinson and Godbey**: 6.2-hour-per-week *gain* in free time 1965-1995 (4.5 hours women, 7.9 men — less housework, fewer children, earlier retirement)
- Harris polls: median "free time" remained rock-steady at 19-20 hours/week over last quarter century
- Redistribution problem: college-educated lost free time; less educated gained. Hours-per-week gap: 6 hours (1969) → 13 hours (1998). "Working class" less work, "leisure class" less leisure.
- Married couples average 14 more hours/week at work in 1998 vs. 1969 — for well-educated middle-class parents whose energies provided disproportionate civic infrastructure, the time bind is real
- Free hours not necessarily the *same hours* — coordinating schedules more burdensome, consistent with collective forms declining faster

**Key finding**: Heavy time demands NOT associated with less civic involvement, even among people with identical education/income. Employed people more active civically than those outside the labor force. Among workers, longer hours often linked to *more* engagement. **Verba**: amount of free time has little effect on whether someone becomes civically active. Only thing busy people do less: dinner with families. Time diary studies: harder workers forgo *ER* before Red Cross and *Friends* before friends. **Busy people give up TV watching — the activity most lethal to community involvement.**

### Financial Pressures: Also Weak

- Financial worries powerfully depress social involvement — even activities with no financial cost
- Only leisure activity positively correlated with financial anxiety: watching TV
- Great Depression triggered only significant interruption in rising civic tide; contrary to expectations that unemployment would radicalize victims, jobless became passive and withdrawn
- Comparing people with identical income/education: financially most worried third attend only two-thirds as many club meetings
- But: civic decline appeared before economic troubles of 1970s and continued through booms of mid-1980s and late 1990s. Economy went up and down, but social capital only went down.
- Among least financially troubled third: club meetings 13/year → 4/year
- Among most affluent 1-in-18: home entertaining 17 → 10, club attendance 13 → 5
- At most, financial anxiety might account for 5-10% of total decline
- **Pressures of time and money are supporting actors, but neither easily cast in the lead**

### Women's Employment: Modest Effect

- Women working outside home doubled: fewer than 1-in-3 (1950s) → nearly 2-in-3 (1990s)
- Work has **two opposing effects**: increases opportunity (exposure to larger networks) but decreases time
- Working women belong to slightly more voluntary associations; working for political party faded more rapidly among men than women
- But full-time employment cuts home entertaining ~15%, visiting ~25%, volunteering ~50%; husbands of full-time working women also less likely to attend church, volunteer, entertain

**Choice vs. Necessity** (DDB Needham data): *Virtually all the increase in women's full-time employment* driven by **financial necessity**, not personal fulfillment. Full-time by necessity rose from 21% to 36% of all women; full-time by choice steady at ~11%.

**Three major findings** (Figure 49):
1. All women more engaged than men regardless of work status
2. Full-time work sharply reduces engagement whether by choice or necessity — sharp falloff
3. **Part-time workers by choice** show highest engagement — a "golden mean" for balancing family, community, and self

> "One practical way to increase community engagement in America would be to make it easier for women (and men too) to work part-time if they wished."

**Critical finding**: Best guess: women's workforce entry + financial distress together account for **less than one-tenth** of total decline. **Central exculpatory fact**: civic engagement diminished almost equally for women and men, working or not, married or single, financially stressed or comfortable. Affluent housewives: higher *level* but virtually equal *declines*. Bachelors: similar declines in club meetings, visiting friends, community projects. **The emergence of two-career families played a visible but quite modest role.**

## Chapter 12: Mobility and Sprawl

### Mobility: Simply Wrong

- Residential stability strongly associated with civic engagement — recent arrivals less likely to vote, know neighbors, belong to organizations
- People expecting to move: 20-25% less likely to attend church, volunteer, work on community projects
- Homeowners much more rooted and more involved in community affairs
- Higher residential turnover → less friendly inhabitants, higher crime, lower school performance
- **But mobility has NOT increased**: 20% moved yearly (1950s) → 16% (1990s); homeownership rose to record 67% (1999)
- Average adult lived in same locality 22 years in 1968 and essentially unchanged three decades later
- **This theory is simply wrong**

### Sprawl: Real but Limited

- Small-town/rural America: 44% of population (1950) → 20% (1996)
- Suburbs: 23% (1950) → 49% (1996)
- Metropolitan residents 10-40% less engaged across all measures — even controlling for age, gender, education, race, marital status, job, finances, homeownership, region
- **Not self-selection**: "Metropolitans are less engaged because of where they are, not who they are."
- Yet at least until recently, urbanization had no deleterious effect: Americans moved into cities throughout first two-thirds of century while civic engagement was rising. Declines virtually identical everywhere.

> **Mark Twain (1867)**: "[New York] is a splendid desert — a domed and steepled solitude, where a stranger is lonely in the midst of a million of his race."

**Initial postwar suburban enthusiasm**: Park Forest, IL (**William Whyte**) — "hotbed of participation" with 66 adult organizations. **Herbert Gans**: Levittowners were "hyperactive joiners." Americans seemed to rediscover small-town civic virtues.

**Later suburban fragmentation**: Collectively heterogeneous but individually homogeneous "lifestyle enclaves." Gated communities: 15% of Orange County, CA developments in 1983 → doubled within 5 years. **Eric Oliver**: "Greater social homogeneity, lower political involvement." Suburbanites kept to themselves, asking little of neighbors and expecting little in return — what **M. P. Baumgartner** called "moral minimalism."

> **Kenneth T. Jackson**: "[A] major casualty of America's drive-in culture is the weakened 'sense of community'.... our lives are now centered inside the house... the social intercourse that used to be the main characteristic of urban life has vanished.... There are few places as desolate and lonely as a suburban street on a hot afternoon."

> **Lewis Mumford** (60+ years ago): "Suburbia is a collective effort to lead a private life."

**Edge cities** (**Joel Garreau**): More commuting suburb-to-suburb. Segregatory zoning excluded gathering places from residential areas. Malls: "carefully designed for one primary, private purpose — to direct consumers to buy." One Californian: "I live in Garden Grove, work in Irvine, shop in Santa Ana, go to the dentist in Anaheim, my husband works in Long Beach, and I used to be president of the League of Women Voters in Fullerton."

### The Automobile's Dominance

- One car per household (1969) → nearly two (1995); by 1990 more cars than drivers
- Americans average **72 minutes every day** behind the wheel — more than cooking/eating, 2x time with kids
- Private cars = 86% of all trips; 2/3 alone
- 80-90% drove to work alone by late 1990s (up from 64% in 1980); carpooling cut in half since mid-1970s
- Average commuting trip grew 37% longer in miles (1983-1995); commuters crossing county lines tripled (1960-1990)
- Annual congestion delay: 16 hours/driver (1982) → 45 hours (1997)
- 45% of drivers (61% of those 18-24) agreed "driving is my time to think and enjoy being alone"

**Three reasons sprawl hurts civic engagement**:
1. **Time**: Each additional 10 minutes of commuting cuts community involvement by 10% — more important than almost any demographic factor except education. Even non-commuters in high-commute communities suffer the "civic penalty" — a classic externality.
2. **Social segregation**: Homogeneous enclaves reduce incentives for bridging social capital. **Sprawl especially toxic for bridging social capital.**
3. **Disrupted boundedness**: Work-based ties compete with place-based ties. Spatial fragmentation between home and workplace bad for community life. **Verba and Nie** (1967): "Communities that appear to foster participation — the small and relatively independent communities — are becoming rarer and rarer."

**Sprawl civic penalty**: roughly 20% on most measures, but accounts for **less than one-tenth** of total decline. Civic disengagement perfectly visible in small towns untouched by sprawl.

## Chapter 13: Technology and Mass Media

### The Transformation of Communications and Leisure

Two especially relevant effects of technology:
1. **Individualization**: From fixed-program concerts to hi-fi Walkman CD; from handful of TV channels to exploding choice
2. **Private consumption**: From baseball park and dance hall to living room screen

> **T. S. Eliot** on television: "It is a medium of entertainment which permits millions of people to listen to the same joke at the same time, and yet remain lonesome."

TV reached 75% household penetration in just **7 years** — faster than telephone (67 years), automobile (52 years), or radio (14 years).

### Newspapers and Civic Engagement

> **Tocqueville**: "Only a newspaper can put the same thought at the same time before a thousand readers.... So hardly any democratic association can carry on without a newspaper."

- Newspaper readers join more, attend more meetings, vote more, volunteer more, trust more — even controlling for demographics
- Readership plunging: 1.3 papers per household (1948) → down 57% by 1998 — despite education rising 50%
- Decline is **generational**: 3 in 4 pre-1929 Americans read daily; only 1 in 4 Gen X'ers. Each generation replaces readers with nonreaders.
- TV and newspapers are complements, not substitutes: TV news viewers more likely to read newspapers
- Network news audience: 60% (1993) → 38% (1998); average nightly news viewer age 57 vs. 42 for prime-time. Half watch with remote in hand.
- Internet emerged as supplement, not substitute; Americans relying primarily on Internet for news actually **less civically involved**

### Television: The 800-Pound Gorilla

- 1950: barely 10% of homes had TV; 1959: 90% — fastest technology diffusion ever
- Per capita viewing grew 17-20% in 1960s, 7-8% more in 1970s, another 7-8% to late 1990s
- Average American watches roughly **4 hours per day** — highest in the world
- TV absorbs **40% of free time** in 1995 (up one-third from 1965)
- Between 1965-1995: gained 6 hours/week leisure; **spent almost all 6 hours watching TV**
- 3/4 of homes had 2+ sets; 77% of sixth-graders had TV in bedroom (up from 6% in 1970)

> **Robinson and Godbey**: "Television is the 800-pound gorilla of leisure time."

> **Kunstler**: "The American house has been TV-centered for three generations.... the life of the house correspondingly turns inward, away from its neighbors.... The outside world has become an abstraction filtered through television."

**Shift to habitual viewing**: Late 1970s selective viewers outnumbered habitual 3:2; by mid-1990s proportions reversed. Americans born before 1933: 43% selective; born after 1963: only 23%. **Habitual TV watching is a habit acquired in childhood.** Husbands and wives spend 3-4x as much time watching TV together as talking; 6-7x as much as in community activities. At least half watch by themselves. 81% report most evenings they watch TV. Two-thirds of kids say TV usually on during meals. By century's end, TV omnipresent.

### TV's Impact on Civic Engagement

**Each additional hour per day = roughly 10% reduction** in most forms of civic activism (controlling for poverty, age, education). If Americans spent 1 hour more/day in 1995 vs. 1965, that alone might account for **perhaps one-quarter of the entire drop**.

**"Television is my primary form of entertainment"** is the **single most consistent predictor** of civic disengagement — more powerful than education, income, generation, commuting, or any other factor tested.

- TV minimalists (definitely disagree): volunteer 9x/year, 18 letters, 9 club meetings, church 27x
- TV maximalists (definitely agree): volunteer 4x, 12 letters, 5 club meetings, church 19x
- TV particularly toxic for **collective activities**: cuts meetings/leadership by 40%; cuts individual activities by 10-15%
- Nothing is more broadly associated with civic disengagement than TV entertainment dependency
- **International evidence**: negative correlation appears across many countries; as TV spread, contacts with relatives, friends, neighbors reduced

**The Amish verdict**:

> "They would destroy our visiting practices. We would stay at home with the television or radio rather than meet with other people. The visiting practices are important because of the closeness of the people."

### The Canadian Natural Experiment

**Tannis MacBeth Williams**: Three isolated Canadian communities — **Notel** (no TV), **Unitel** and **Multitel** (had TV). Notel was typical except for valley location blocking reception.

> "There was a significant decline in Notel following the introduction of television, but no change in either Unitel or Multitel."

Clear evidence that introduction of television deflated participation in community activities — affecting those central to community activities, not just peripheral members.

### Three Mechanisms

1. **Time competition**: TV is the only leisure activity that inhibits other activities. **Rolf Meyersohn's "the more, the more" pattern** applies to everything except TV. One hour less daily TV = civic-vitamin equivalent of 5-6 more years of education (**Brehm and Rahn**). 1970s Roper: 47% watching more TV; 38% spending less time visiting — Americans "cocooning."

2. **Psychological effects**: TV is relaxing, passive, habit-forming, surprisingly unsatisfying — about as enjoyable as housework in time diaries. Creates pseudopersonal connections (**Meyrowitz**, **Hart**: TV creates false sense of companionship, making people *feel* engaged without *being* engaged). **Kubey and Csikszentmihalyi**: heavy viewers find it less rewarding; "heavy viewing may reinforce intolerance for daily moments not chocked full of sight and sound." DDB Needham "malaise" index: TV dependence as disruptive as financial anxiety. 1977 Detroit Free Press: only 5 of 120 families willing to give up TV for $500; those who did experienced anxiety, depression. One woman: "It was terrible. We did nothing — my husband and I talked." **Iyengar**: prevailing TV coverage leads viewers to attribute societal problems to individuals, shrinking own responsibility.

3. **Programmatic content**: News/educational programs attract civic audiences; soap operas, action dramas, reality TV attract least civic. Market segmentation — 500-channel cable and "target marketing" — reduces TV's role as shared "water-cooler" culture. Top-rated program's audience share shrank from two-thirds (1950s) to one-third (mid-1990s). TV encourages **materialist values** (**Gerbner**). "Parallel play" — watching alone in presence of others — a fairly primitive stage of social development.

> "At minimum: television and electronic cousins are willing accomplices in civic mystery, and more likely than not, they are ringleaders."

## Chapter 14: From Generation to Generation

### Age as Key to the Puzzle

- Age second only to education as predictor of civic engagement
- Middle-aged and older more active, generous, trusting, engaged
- **Life cycle effects** (everyone goes through) vs. **generational effects** (permanent cohort differences)
- Eyesight = life cycle (deteriorates for everyone); military service = generational (80% of 1920s men vs. 10% of 1960s men served)
- Life cycle effects: individuals change but society doesn't. Generational effects: society changes even though individuals don't. **"Precisely because the rhythm of generational change is slower paced, it is more nearly inexorable."**

### Expected Life Cycle Booms That Never Materialized

- Boomers should have produced waves of PTA membership in 1970s-80s, rising church membership, civic profusion in 1990s, boom in volunteering/philanthropy as they retire in 2010s
- **None materialized**. Expected upswings swamped by unexpected generational downswings.
- Even in tiny, civic-minded hamlets of pastoral Vermont, town meeting attendance fell nearly half between early 1970s and late 1990s
- **"This civic 'dog that didn't bark' is important clue"**

### Table 3: Generational Disengagement Accelerates

Decline from 1970s to 1990s in participation in any of 12 civic activities:
- Over-60: fell **11%**
- 45-59: fell **22%**
- 30-44: fell **32%**
- Under-30: fell **44%**

Pattern applies to virtually every form: newspaper reading, petitioning, writing letters, contacting Congress, church attendance, union membership, party work. Among over-sixty: activities almost as common in 1990s as 1970s. Among youngest: half as common. **More recent the cohort, more dramatic its disengagement.**

### The Long Civic Generation (born 1910-1940)

- **Core**: born 1925-1930 — grade school during Depression, WWII in high school (or on battlefield), first voted 1948/1952, set up housekeeping in 1950s, first TV in late twenties
- Exceptionally civic despite only one-quarter going beyond high school — "self-made citizens"
- Members belong to almost twice as many associations as grandchildren (1.9 vs. 1.1)
- More than twice as likely to trust others (50% vs. 20%)
- Vote at nearly double rate (80-85% vs. 45-50%)
- Nearly twice as likely to attend church regularly (45% vs. 25%)
- Almost three times as likely to read daily newspaper (75% vs. 25%)
- **Charles Tilly** (born 1928): "We are the last suckers"

> "Like Minerva's owl that flies at dusk, we come to appreciate how important the long civic generation has been to American community life just as its members are retiring."

At 1960 Kennedy-Nixon election: long civic generation comprised two-thirds of electorate. By 1992: share cut in half. Boomers and X'ers tripled from 1 in 4 to 3 in 4 adults. Generational replacement is **"the single most important explanation for the collapse of civic engagement."**

### The Graying of Civic America

- Early 1970s: people 60+ provided 12% of local organization officers, 20% of volunteers, 24% of club meeting attendance
- Mid-1990s: rose to 20%, 35%, 38% — though seniors' population share barely budged
- Mid-1970s: people 45+ accounted for one-third of town meeting participants; twenty years later: one-half
- Creates potential for self-disenfranchisement of younger groups

### Baby Boomers (1946-1964)

- Best-educated generation in history, first exposed to TV throughout their lives
- **Paul Light**: Average boomer by age 16 watched 12,000-15,000 hours of TV — "equivalent of 24 hours a day for 15 to 20 solid months." "There can be little doubt that television reduced the baby boom's contact with its peers and parents."
- Indelibly marked by civil rights, assassinations, Vietnam, Watergate — distrustful of institutions
- Less knowledgeable about politics than parents at comparable age (**Michael Delli Carpini**)
- Slow to marry, quick to divorce; 96% raised in religious tradition, but 58% abandoned it; less than half returned
- Less comfortable in bureaucracies, more insistent on autonomy; grew up in uniformity, emphasized individualism
- Crowded schools reduced social learning opportunities; diminished expectations due to competitive pressures within large cohort
- More **libertarian**: less respect for authority, religion, patriotism; more **tolerant** of racial, sexual, political minorities
- **Cheryl Russell**: "free agents"

### Generation X (1965-1980)

- Erosion of social capital began before any X'er was born — they cannot be blamed; X reflects continuation of post-WWII generational course
- UCLA survey of college freshmen: financial well-being "very important": ~40% (late 1960s) → 75% (1998); politics/environment: dropped to 26%/19%
- Trust collapsed: high school seniors "most people can be trusted": 61% (boomers 1976) → 23% (late X'ers 1995)
- Political discussions among high school students only half as common; student election participation: ~75% → 20%
- Collective action even more foreign to X'ers than boomers; emphasis on personal/private over public/collective
- Shaped by uncertainty: slow growth, inflation, children of divorce explosion; no collective success stories
- **"Second consecutive generation of free agents"**

### Depression, Suicide, and Social Isolation

- Depression struck earlier and more pervasively in each generation born after 1940
- Of Americans born before 1955: only 1% suffered major depression by age 75; born after 1955: 6% by age 24
- **Seligman**: "The rate of depression over the last two generations has increased roughly tenfold"
- Youth suicide (ages 15-19): more than quadrupled (1950-1995) — while suicide among older groups actually declined
- As long civic generation aged (reducing traditionally high elderly suicide), less-integrated boomers/X'ers raised traditionally low youth rates. Americans born in 1920s-30s: half as likely to commit suicide as people that age at midcentury. Born in 1970s-80s: three or four times more likely.
- DDB Needham: adults under 30 ranking high on malaise: 31% (1975-76) → 45% (1998-99); adults 60+ slipped from 33% to 30%
- 1940s-50s: younger people were happier than older; by 1999: younger were unhappier
- Average teenager spends 3.5 hours alone each day — more than with family or friends
- Seligman traced depression epidemic to "rampant individualism" coupled with weakened commitment to institutions; close-knit Amish community spared

> **Seligman**: "Individualism need not lead to depression as long as we can fall back on large institutions — religion, country, family.... in a self standing alone without the buffer of larger beliefs, helplessness and failure can all too easily become hopelessness and despair."

### Countervailing Trend: Youth Volunteerism

- Record 74% of college freshmen volunteered during last year of high school (1998 vs. 62% in 1989)
- Regular volunteerism (1 hour/week) up: 42% vs. 27% in 1987
- Not yet clear if driven by graduation requirements or genuine civic renewal
- More optimistic interpretation: forty-year trend toward disengagement may be at last bottoming out

### War as Civic Catalyst

**WWII's extraordinary civic burst**: 16 million served (including 6 million volunteers); 80% of men born in 1920s. Millions of front windows displayed blue stars (son in service) and gold stars (lost loved one). Draft decisions in hands of thousands of lay boards.

**Home front mobilization**:
- War Bond campaigns: Batman on comic covers; Betty Grable auctioned nylons for $40,000; Marlene Dietrich toured Ohio in a Jeep; 25 million workers in payroll savings; 1944 E-bond sales absorbed 7.1% of after-tax personal income
- Scrap drives: 400,000 tons of rubber in four weeks — 6 pounds for every American; Boy Scouts at filling stations
- Volunteer defense corps: 1.2 million (early 1942) → 12 million (mid-1943); 16,000 Chicago block captains took oath; Hannibal, MO meeting packed 4,000 inside, 15,000 outside
- Red Cross volunteers: 1.1 million (1940) → 7.5 million (1945)
- Nearly 20 million Victory Gardens yielding 40% of all vegetables
- **Gary, Indiana eighth-graders** (two-year period): taught infant care, collected 500,000 pounds of wastepaper, sold $40,000/month in war stamps, sponsored Clean Plate campaign, participated in War Bond and tin can drives
- Junior Leagues converted to Civilian Defense Volunteer Offices; by end of 1943: 4,300 CDVOs nationwide

War fostered equality: top 1% wealth share fell from 31% to 23% (1939-1945); top 5% income share from 28% to 19%. Celebrities served — DiMaggio, Clark Gable, William McChesney Martin, FDR's four sons. **"Most leveling event in American economic history."** Important contrast with Vietnam's social inequality in military service.

**Lloyd Warner**: found sense of "unconscious well-being" because "everyone is doing something to help in the common desperate effort in a cooperative rather than a private spirit."

> Kennedy (1946): "Most of the courage shown in the war came from men's understanding of their interdependence on each other.... We must work together.... We must have the same unity that we had during the war."

### Twin Explanations: War and Television

- Long civic generation was **last cohort to grow up without television**
- More fully any generation was exposed to TV in formative years, the lower its civic engagement as adults; they also watch differently — more habitually, more mindlessly
- Effect of TV (Chapter 13) and effect of generation (this chapter) are in some respects opposite sides of same coin
- **Roughly half of overall decline in social capital can be traced to generational change**
- To say disengagement is largely generational merely reformulates the puzzle: roots of lonely bowling probably date to the **1940s and 1950s**, not 1960s-1980s

### Two Types of Forces

**Society-wide forces** (affected all ages): Especially detrimental to private socializing (card playing, entertaining). Allure of electronic entertainment likely explanation.

**Generational forces**: Especially affected public engagement (religion, trust, voting, news, volunteering). Operated through generational succession — more gradual, less visible.

Combined effects on activities buffeted by both (club meetings, family dining, local leadership) produced the **most dramatic changes**: 60% fall in club meetings, 53% fall in leadership, 60% increase in families dining apart.

### Shift from Communal to Material Values

- **Roper polls** (1975-1996): "A lot of money" as "good life": 38% → 63%; "contribute to society": 38% → 32%
- Vacation homes: 19%→43%; second color TV: 10%→34%; second car: 30%→45%
- Happy marriage: 84%→80%; children: 74%→72%; interesting job: 69%→61%
- Much of growth in materialism attributable to generational replacement
- Gen X'ers nearly half as likely as pre-1946 cohort to feel belonging to neighborhood, church, local community, or organizations
- Even for younger cohort, **kith and kin twenty times more important than cyber-friends**

## Chapter 15: What Killed Civic Engagement? Summing Up

### Suspect #1: Family Structure Change — Minimal Impact

- Married-with-kids households: 40% (1970) → 26% (1997); married adults: 74% (1974) → 56% (1998); one-person households more than doubled since 1950
- But marriage/parenthood boost only **two** specific affiliations: church and youth-related activities
- Marriage **negatively correlated** with sports, political, cultural, business, hobby, neighborhood, service groups
- Married people attend **fewer** club meetings than singles; less likely to spend time informally with friends/neighbors
- Divorce unrelated to most civic involvement; divorced actually sign more petitions, attend more public meetings, write Congress more
- Holding traditional family structure constant would produce more church/youth involvement but **decrease** time with friends even more; no effect on secular organizations (Kiwanis to NAACP to AMA); no halt to political activity decline
- **Verdict**: Apart from church/youth, none of major declines attributable to family decline. Important reasons for concern about family erosion, but **civic disengagement is not among them.**

### Suspect #2: Race and Civil Rights Revolution — Not Guilty

Three key facts:
1. Controlling for education/income, blacks belonged to **more** associations than whites (not less)
2. African Americans dropped out of organizations **at least as rapidly** as whites; sharpest drop among **college-educated African Americans**
3. Among whites, pace of disengagement **uncorrelated** with racial intolerance or support for segregation — avowedly racist whites no quicker to drop out than tolerant whites

Generational differences hard to reconcile: disengagement barely visible among Americans who came of age when society was objectively more segregated and subjectively more racist.

> Evidence suggests reversing civil rights gains would do nothing to reverse social capital losses.

### Suspect #3: Big Government/Welfare State — Not Guilty

- Some policies destroyed social capital (1950s-60s slum clearance disrupted community ties)
- But harder to see which policies caused decline in bowling leagues, family dinners, literary clubs
- Among U.S. states, social capital essentially uncorrelated with welfare spending or government size
- Cross-nationally, social capital **highest** in big-spending Scandinavian welfare states
- Federal domestic spending averaged 2.2% of GNP in both late 1940s and late 1990s (peaked 2.7% mid-1960s)
- Social capital increased 1947-1965 and decreased 1965-1998 — **inconsistent with any theory blaming big government**

### Suspect #4: Capitalism and the Market — Partially Guilty

- Generic market capitalism = a constant over several centuries that can't explain a variable
- But **"corporate delocalization"** has stripped communities of civic leadership
- As Wal-Mart replaces corner hardware, Bank of America takes over First National, incentives for business elites to contribute atrophy
- **Charles Heying**: Atlanta's elite social cohesion/civic commitment rose from 1930s, peaked 1960s, then declined — same trajectory as social capital. Similar trends in Chicago, Philadelphia, Dayton, Shreveport.
- Boston developer on demise of "the Vault": "Where are the power elite when you need them? They're all off at corporate headquarters in some other state."
- Link most direct regarding larger philanthropic/civic activities; less clear why it would affect church attendance, poker, or voting
- Nevertheless: **connection between civic disengagement and corporate disengagement worth exploring**
