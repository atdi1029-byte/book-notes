Key Takeaways

  1. Social capital is collapsing across all dimensions. Membership in civic organizations fell 40%+, voting declined 25%, trust in others dropped from 55% to 35%, family dinners and informal socializing plummeted, volunteering shifted from communal projects to individual acts, and philanthropy as a share of income fell 29% between 1964 and 1998.
  2. The decline began abruptly in the mid-1960s. After a century of rising civic engagement that peaked around 1964, virtually every measure of social capital reversed course simultaneously — voting, club membership, churchgoing, entertaining at home, trust in neighbors, political interest, philanthropy, and community projects all began steady, synchronized declines.
  3. Social capital has measurable effects on everything. States with high social capital have better schools, lower crime, better health outcomes, higher economic growth, more effective government, and longer life expectancy. The correlation between social trust and nearly every measure of civic virtue is overwhelming.
  4. Generational replacement explains roughly half the decline. The “long civic generation” (born 1910–1940), shaped by the Depression and WWII, remained exceptionally engaged throughout their lives. Each successive generation — boomers, Gen X — has been progressively less civically engaged at every stage of life.
  5. Television accounts for roughly a quarter of the decline. Heavy TV watchers are less engaged across all age groups, but the effect is strongest for those who grew up with television. Each additional hour of TV per day corresponds to measurable reductions in voting, volunteering, club attendance, and visiting friends. TV privatizes leisure time.
  6. It is not just less joining — it is less informal connection. Beyond formal organizations, Americans spend dramatically less time with friends and neighbors, have fewer dinner parties, play cards less, visit relatives less frequently, and express less trust in their fellow citizens. The entire social fabric has frayed.
  7. Bonding vs. bridging social capital. Bonding (exclusive, inward-looking ties like ethnic clubs) provides “sociological superglue” for getting by. Bridging (inclusive, outward-looking ties like civic groups) provides “sociological WD-40” for getting ahead and generating broader identities. America is losing both.
  8. Work pressure and sprawl are minor culprits (~10% each). Despite popular narratives, time diary studies show Americans actually have more free time than in the 1960s. Two-career families and commuting contribute modestly but cannot explain the breadth or timing of the collapse.
  9. Big government and the welfare state are not to blame. The size of government relative to GDP barely changed from 1965 to 2000, and cross-national evidence shows social capital is highest in big-spending Scandinavian welfare states. Civic engagement and government size are uncorrelated or positively correlated.
  10. The decline is not a steady historical trend. Social capital rose dramatically from the 1900s through the early 1960s — the Progressive Era, the New Deal, and the postwar period all saw surging civic engagement. The current collapse represents a reversal of that upward trend, not some inevitable feature of modernity.
  11. Inequality and social solidarity are deeply incompatible. The lowest social capital today is found in states with the most virulent slavery in the 19th century and Jim Crow politics in the 20th. Slavery was a social system designed to destroy horizontal networks of reciprocity.
  12. History offers hope. The Gilded Age (1870–1900) faced the same challenges — technological disruption, urbanization, mass immigration, political corruption. The Progressive response was a “boom” in association-building that created nearly every major civic institution we know today. We need a similar era of civic inventiveness.

Contrarian Ideas

  1. Americans have more free time, not less. Time diary studies show free time rose roughly six hours per week for men and stayed flat for women from 1965 to 1995, contradicting the ubiquitous “time crunch” narrative. Busy people are actually more civically engaged — they sacrifice television before civic participation. Sidney Verba’s study found free time had “little or no effect” on whether someone became civically active.
  2. Volunteering rose, but became fragile. Total volunteering increased slightly in recent decades, but the increase was entirely among seniors over 60 exploiting newfound health and free time, not embedded in reinforcing organizational networks. Younger volunteers are sporadic and lack the reinforcing community ties that made earlier volunteering self-sustaining. The new volunteering is individual, not communal.
  3. Education increases engagement, but educated boomers are less engaged than uneducated pre-1946 cohorts. A college-educated baby boomer is less civically active than a high-school-educated member of the “long civic generation.” Generational effects swamp education effects — despite the fact that education is by far the strongest predictor of civic engagement at the individual level.
  4. Women’s entry into the workforce did not cause the decline. Women working outside the home are actually more engaged in most civic activities, controlling for other factors. The two-career family contributed modestly (~10%), but the narrative that feminism killed civic life is empirically false. Civic disengagement was almost equally great among affluent housewives and bachelors — groups entirely unaffected by two-career pressures.
  5. Small-town trust advantages are real, not paranoia. Urban dwellers express less trust than small-town residents, and this reflects actual experience — crime is higher, cheating is more common, and strangers are less helpful in cities. The trust gap between cities and small towns is not irrational; it is calibrated to real differences in social environments.
  6. War mobilized civic engagement. WWII fostered social solidarity through shared adversity, economic leveling (the top 1%’s wealth share fell from 31% to 23%), and mass participation — 16 million served, 12 million home-front volunteers, 20 million Victory Gardens. This is not an argument for war, but it explains why the “long civic generation” was so exceptionally engaged and why the search for “moral equivalents of war” is urgent.

Allegories & Cultural References

The Bowling Kidney Story. John Lambert (64, white, retired) and Andy Boschma (33, Black, accountant) knew each other only through a bowling league in Ypsilanti, Michigan. When Lambert needed a kidney, Boschma donated one of his. The book’s central metaphor — casual social ties across racial lines produce extraordinary acts of generosity that formal institutions cannot replicate.

The Diamond Trade. The diamond trade concentrates in close-knit ethnic enclaves where reputation enforces honesty without lawyers. Illustrates how dense social networks reduce transaction costs and enforce norms more efficiently than legal systems.

Sociological Superglue & WD-40. Putnam’s metaphor for bonding vs. bridging social capital. Bonding capital (superglue) holds homogeneous groups together for “getting by”; bridging capital (WD-40) lubricates connections across social cleavages for “getting ahead.”

Paul Revere’s Ride. Revere’s alarm succeeded not because of individual heroism but because of networks of civic engagement in Middlesex villages (David Hackett Fischer). Even iconic American individualism depended on preexisting social capital.

Murder on the Orient Express. Agatha Christie’s mystery where every suspect did it. Putnam’s analogy for the causes of civic decline: there is more than one perpetrator. The task is to sort ringleaders from accomplices.

The Civic Dog That Didn’t Bark. Expected life-cycle upswings in civic engagement as boomers matured never materialized — no PTA boom, no church surge. Sherlock Holmes allusion confirming that the decline is generational, not life-cycle — boomers never “grew into” civic habits.

Minerva’s Owl. Hegel’s image of wisdom arriving at dusk. We appreciate the long civic generation’s importance only as its members retire and die, too late to learn from them directly.

The Canadian “Notel” Experiment. Three Canadian towns — Notel (no TV), Unitel, and Multitel — compared before and after TV’s introduction. Near-experimental evidence that television causes civic disengagement: participation declined significantly in Notel after TV arrived, with no change in control towns.

The Roseto Study. Italian American community in Pennsylvania with heart attack rates less than half those of neighboring towns, despite more risk factors. Tight-knit community (mutual aid, churches, front-porch socializing) produced measurable health benefits; when young people abandoned communal folkways in the 1980s, heart attack rates rose above neighbors’.

Tupelo, Mississippi. One of the poorest counties in the poorest state in 1940. Under George McLean’s leadership, an inclusive Community Development Foundation replaced the elite Chamber of Commerce. Fifty years of sustained investment in social capital produced 1,000 industrial jobs annually, hundreds of millions in investment, and the best schools in Mississippi.

Silicon Valley vs. Route 128. Silicon Valley built horizontal networks of informal cooperation among nominally competing companies; the Homebrew Computer Club produced leaders of 20+ firms. Route 128 (Boston) maintained corporate hierarchy and secrecy. Social capital is a competitive economic advantage — open, networked regions outperform closed, hierarchical ones.

The Gilded Age Parallel. The period 1870–1900 faced the same challenges as 1965–2000: technological disruption, urbanization, immigration, political corruption, widening inequality. The Progressive Era response was a “boom” in association-building that created nearly every major civic institution we know today. History shows civic reinvention is possible.

Ground War vs. Air War. Pro-life movement (13 million members, 7,000 local chapters, church-based grassroots) vs. pro-choice NARAL (direct mail, 3–5% did more than write a check). A well-developed national organization using “air war” techniques is a sign not of grassroots engagement but of its absence.

The Busing Dilemma. Proponents argued integrated schools build bridging capital across racial divides; opponents argued neighborhood schools build bonding capital within communities. Putnam’s tragic conclusion: “Both sides were probably right.” Highlights the genuine tension between bonding and bridging social capital.

Carol Stack’s All Our Kin. Classic study of dense exchange networks among Black families in inner-city Midwest — goods, resources, child care shared among kin and non-kin. Social capital among the poor is disproportionately important because they lack economic and human capital; its erosion hits hardest where other cushions are absent.

Author’s Key Proposals

  1. Measure and track social capital systematically. Build comprehensive, ongoing national indicators of civic engagement, trust, and social networks — what gets measured gets managed. The fourteen-indicator Social Capital Index across all fifty states demonstrated that rigorous measurement reveals patterns invisible to casual observation.
  2. Design institutions to foster bridging social capital. Focus on creating opportunities for Americans to connect across lines of race, class, generation, and ideology — not just bonding within homogeneous groups. Bridging capital is harder to create but more desperately needed for the biggest collective problems.
  3. Adapt civic institutions to new realities. Do not assume 1950s organizational models will work today. Experiment with forms of engagement compatible with two-career families, dispersed communities, and digital natives. America’s major civic institutions are somewhat antiquated a century after most were created — they need reform to invite more active participation.
  4. Resist naive technological solutionism. The Internet and “virtual communities” may complement face-to-face ties, but early evidence suggests they are poor substitutes for real-world social capital. Computer-mediated communication strips away the nonverbal cues essential for trust, risks cyberbalkanization, and may displace rather than supplement in-person contact.
  5. Reverse the trend toward ever-larger, centralized institutions. Smaller, more local organizations (schools, businesses, governments) foster more engagement and accountability than distant bureaucracies and global corporations. Decentralize government authority as far as possible — neighborhood councils in Minneapolis, Portland, and Seattle created new social capital through potluck dinners, community gardens, and flea markets.
  6. Find “moral equivalents of war.” The shared adversity and egalitarian mobilization of WWII forged the long civic generation. We need non-martial equivalents — perhaps environmental challenges, public health crises, or infrastructure renewal — that create the same sense of common purpose without the destruction.
  7. Cultivate civic skills early through service learning. Youth who volunteer are far more likely to volunteer and donate as adults; habits formed in “formative years” shape lifelong patterns of engagement. Service learning (community service coupled to classwork) is more effective at inculcating civic habits than standalone service — by 1999, roughly 57% of students participated in community service but only 30% in service learning.
  8. Reconnect civic engagement with self-interest rightly understood. Tocqueville’s insight — Americans looked out for neighbors not from selfless altruism but from enlightened self-interest. Henry Ward Beecher’s century-old advice to “multiply picnics” is not entirely ridiculous. We should do this not because it would be good for America — though it would be — but because it would be good for us.

Relevance to Trading & Investing

  1. Trust is economic infrastructure. Kenneth Arrow (Nobel laureate): “Much of the economic backwardness in the world can be explained by lack of mutual confidence.” Social capital is not a luxury but a foundation for markets. High-trust regions have measurable economic advantages through lower transaction costs.
  2. Networks beat résumés. Most Americans get jobs through personal connections, not formal credentials. “It’s not what you know, it’s who you know” is empirically true. Financial capital and human capital are insufficient without social capital.
  3. Regional inequality reflects social capital gaps. States with high social capital (Minnesota, North Dakota) have stronger economic growth, better educational outcomes, and more effective institutions than low social capital states (Mississippi, Louisiana). Social capital is a leading indicator of economic health.
  4. Corporate delocalization destroys value. As businesses replace local ties with distant headquarters, they lose embedded knowledge, relationships, and long-term commitment to communities. Short-term shareholder value may rise, but systemic fragility increases.
  5. Thick vs. thin trust in markets. Dense social networks sustain reputations and enforce norms without legal systems — the diamond trade, ethnic business enclaves, and small-town commerce all rely on “thick trust.” Modern anonymous markets require “thin trust,” which is harder to sustain and more fragile to shocks.
  6. Information cascades through networks. Communities with dense social capital mobilize information faster — early-stage opportunities, warnings about threats, and diffusion of innovations all depend on social networks, not just formal channels.
  7. Silicon Valley vs. Route 128. Silicon Valley’s success was built on horizontal networks of informal cooperation among nominally competing companies. Route 128 (Boston) maintained traditional norms of corporate hierarchy and secrecy — and performed far worse economically. Social capital is a competitive advantage.
  8. Social capital’s volatility. The abrupt reversal in the mid-1960s shows social capital can collapse rapidly once tipping points are reached. Just as markets can shift from stability to crisis, social systems can undergo phase transitions.

Chapter 1: Thinking about Social Change in America

The Puzzle: Civic Collapse in the 1990s

Across America, civic organizations that had thrived for decades suddenly withered. The Glenn Valley PA Bridge Club went from 40+ members to dissolution. The Little Rock Sertoma Club fell from 50 members to 7. The Roanoke NAACP dropped from 2,500 to a few hundred. The Dallas Charity League, after 57 years of Friday meetings, held its final gathering in April 1999 — the average age of its remaining members was 80+. Even the Tewksbury High School marching band went from 80+ musicians to just 4 sign-ups. As one Vassar alumnae book sale co-chair put it: “They’re dying, and they’re not replaceable.”

“The puzzle: In the last several decades of the twentieth century all these community groups and tens of thousands like them across America began to fade.”

The Optimism of the Early 1960s

Membership rolls had lengthened steadily for as long as anyone could remember. A 1958 study had warned that “the most dangerous threat hanging over American society is the threat of leisure.” Voting turnout was rising at 1.6% per presidential election cycle, projected to reach 70% by 1976. Trust in neighbors peaked at 77% in 1964, up from 66% during and after WWII. Americans who wanted their children to enter politics nearly doubled in a decade. Education — by far the best predictor of civic engagement — was expanding rapidly with baby boomers approaching college.

“We were a ‘can do’ people, who accomplished whatever we set out to do. We had licked the Depression, turned the tide in World War II, and rebuilt Europe after the war.” — Doug McAdam

The era was not perfect — segregation by race was legal, segregation by gender was the social norm, and infant mortality stood at 26 per 1,000 (44 per 1,000 for Black infants). But engagement in community affairs and the sense of shared identity and reciprocity had never been greater in modern America.

Social Capital Defined

Social capital = connections among individuals — social networks and the norms of reciprocity and trustworthiness that arise from them. Analogous to physical capital (tools) and human capital (education), social contacts affect the productivity of individuals and groups. The term was independently invented at least six times in the 20th century: by L. J. Hanifan (1916), a West Virginia school reformer who defined it as “good will, fellowship, sympathy, and social intercourse”; by Jane Jacobs in the 1960s studying neighborliness in the metropolis; by Glenn Loury in the 1970s on the social legacy of slavery; by Pierre Bourdieu in the 1980s on social and economic resources in networks; and by James S. Coleman in the 1980s on the social context of education.

Social capital has a private face (networking for jobs, career advancement) and a public face (externalities that benefit the wider community). A neighborhood watch group reduces crime for everyone on the block, even uninvolved residents.

Reciprocity: The Engine of Social Capital

Putnam distinguishes between specific reciprocity (“I’ll do X if you do Y”) and generalized reciprocity (“I’ll do X confident that someone will reciprocate down the road” — the Golden Rule). A society with generalized reciprocity is more efficient, just as money is more efficient than barter. Dense social ties reduce incentives for opportunism — which is why the diamond trade concentrates in close-knit ethnic enclaves where reputation enforces honesty without lawyers.

Bridging vs. Bonding: The Most Important Distinction

Bonding social capital is exclusive and inward-looking, reinforcing homogeneous groups — ethnic fraternal organizations, country clubs. It is good for “getting by” — providing solidarity, start-up financing, psychological support. Bridging social capital is inclusive and outward-looking, crossing social cleavages — the civil rights movement, youth service groups. It is crucial for “getting ahead” — linking to external assets and diffusing information. Mark Granovetter’s insight: “weak” ties (distant acquaintances) are more valuable than “strong” ties for finding jobs and political allies. In Putnam’s memorable formulation: bonding is sociological superglue; bridging is sociological WD-40.

The Bowling Story

On October 29, 1997, John Lambert (64, retired) and Andy Boschma (33, accountant) knew each other only through a bowling league in Ypsilanti, Michigan. When Lambert needed a kidney, Boschma offered one of his. The newspaper photo revealed they were different races — but they bowled together. “In small ways like this, and in larger ways, too, we Americans need to reconnect with one another.” That is the simple argument of this book.

Chapter 2: Political Participation

Voting Decline: The Most Visible Symptom

In 1960, 62.8% of Americans voted in the Kennedy-Nixon election. By 1996, turnout had fallen to 48.9% — the lowest in nearly two centuries of free elections. Presidential participation declined roughly 25% over 36 years, with off-year and local elections declining similarly. The decline occurred despite lower barriers — “motor voter” registration laws, relaxed residency requirements, and $100 million spent to swell voter rolls.

The Decline Is Generational, Not Individual

Virtually all the net decline in voting is attributable to generational replacement, not individuals changing their behavior. Putnam draws a crucial distinction: intracohort change (individuals shifting together) is fast and reversible — like SUV fads. Intercohort change (generational replacement) is slow but nearly inexorable — like changing sexual mores. Baby boomers and their children have been less likely to vote throughout their entire lives than their parents and grandparents. As Putnam puts it: “Every year the Grim Reaper removes another swath of the most politically engaged generation in the American electorate.”

Cooperative Activities Declined Most

Roper surveys from 1973–74 to 1993–94 showed dramatic declines in cooperative political activities: serving as officer of a club or organization fell 42%, working for a political party fell 42%, serving on a committee for a local organization fell 39%, and attending public meetings on town or school affairs fell 35%. But writing letters to papers or Congress declined only 14–23%. The key pattern: activities requiring coordination with others (embodying social capital) declined most; individual expressive acts were more stable. Utterly uninvolved Americans rose from 46% to 64%, while active citizens (3+ activities) were cut in half, from 20% to 11%.

Political Knowledge Paradox

Americans score similarly on civics tests as their grandparents — despite having four more years of average formal education. The average college graduate today knows little more about public affairs than the average high school graduate in the 1940s. Daily newspaper readership among those under 35 plummeted from 67% in 1965 to 33% in 1990. Post-baby boom generations are substantially less knowledgeable despite a proliferation of information sources.

Trust in government collapsed in parallel: in the 1960s, 75% trusted the government to do right most of the time; by the 1990s, 75% did not. In April 1966 — during Vietnam and race riots — 66% rejected the view that “people running the country don’t really care what happens to you.” In December 1997 — during peace and prosperity — 57% endorsed that same cynical view.

“We remain, in short, reasonably well-informed spectators of public affairs, but many fewer of us actually partake in the game.”

Chapter 3: Civic Participation

“Americans of all ages, all stations in life, and all types of disposition are forever forming associations.... Nothing, in my view, deserves more attention than the intellectual and moral associations in America.” — Alexis de Tocqueville

The Paradox: More Organizations, Fewer Members

National nonprofit organizations tripled from 10,299 (1968) to 22,901 (1997), but median membership shrank from roughly 10,000 to 1,000 — a proliferation of letterheads, not grassroots participation. Old organizations were headquartered in the heartland (Boy Scouts in Irving, TX; Lions in Oak Brook, IL; American Legion in Indianapolis). New organizations clustered around Capitol Hill — AARP, NOW, Greenpeace, Common Cause — professionally staffed advocacy operations, not member-centered associations. Of 83 public-interest groups founded in the early 1970s, two-thirds had no local chapters at all. Rotary alone had nearly twice as many chapters as all 83 combined.

“Tertiary” Associations: Membership Means Writing a Check

AARP grew from 400,000 members in 1960 to 33 million by the 1990s, but fewer than 10% belonged to local chapters; grassroots activities were “on life support even during maximum membership growth.” Greenpeace became the largest environmental organization through aggressive direct mail, mailing 48 million letters annually — but when leadership cut back mailings, membership hemorrhaged 85% by 1998. The bond between members was like the bond between two Yankees fans on opposite coasts — shared symbols, not awareness of each other’s existence.

Active Involvement Collapsed Faster Than Nominal Membership

Leadership roles were cut by more than 50% between 1973 and 1994. The dismaying trend accelerated after 1985 — active involvement fell 45% in just ten years. Club meeting attendance dropped from an average of 12 per year in 1975 to 5 in 1999. Those attending at least one meeting fell from 64% to 38%.

“In the mid-1970s nearly two-thirds of all Americans attended club meetings, but by the late 1990s nearly two-thirds never do.”

Time diaries showed Americans’ investment in organizational life falling from 3.7 hours per month (1965) to 2.3 hours per month (1995). Among college graduates, club meetings per year fell 55%, from 13 to 6. Consumer spending on club and fraternal dues dropped from 6 cents per dollar of leisure spending in 1929 to 3 cents by 1997. Four independent data streams converge: active involvement fell by more than half in the last decades of the 20th century.

Chapter 4: Religious Participation

Churches as Social Capital Powerhouses

The United States is one of the most religiously observant countries in the world. Religious adherence grew from 17% in 1776 to 62% in 1980. Nearly half of all associational memberships are church-related; half of all philanthropy and half of all volunteering occur in a religious context. Churches provide an incubator for civic skills — speeches, meetings, managing disagreements, administrative responsibility. Religiosity rivals education as the most powerful correlate of civic engagement; regular attendees talk with 40% more people daily. Some 75–80% of church members give to charity versus 55–60% of nonmembers; 50–60% volunteer versus 30–35%.

“The Black church functioned as the institutional center of the modern civil rights movement.... Churches provided the movement with an organized mass base; a leadership of clergymen... skilled in the art of managing people and resources.”

Three to Four Decades of Erosion

Church membership rose from the 1930s to roughly 1960, then entered a long slow slump of approximately 10%. Those claiming “no religion” quadrupled from 2% (1967) to 11% (1990s). Church attendance rose sharply after WWII but declined roughly one-third between the late 1950s and late 1990s. Church-related social activities (Sunday schools, Bible study, socials) fell from about 25% membership in the 1950s to roughly 12.5% by the 1990s — a 50% decline. Time diaries showed Americans devoted only two-thirds as much time to religion in 1995 as in 1965 (1 hour 37 minutes per week down to 1 hour 7 minutes). Across all three domains — politics, organizations, and religion — the more demanding the form of involvement, the greater the decline.

The Evangelical Paradox: Strong Bonding, Weak Bridging

Mainline Protestants lost massive market share while evangelical and fundamentalist groups grew. Evangelical churches build extraordinarily strong internal communities — more regular attendance, more generous giving (2.8% versus 1.6% of income), more close friendships within the congregation. But evangelical social capital is invested inward, not outward: church attendance among evangelicals is not correlated with membership in community organizations. As Robert Wuthnow found: “Mainline Protestant churches encourage civic engagement in the wider community, whereas evangelical churches apparently do not.” The shift from mainline to evangelical Christianity means churches are less engaged with the wider community — reinforcing rather than counterbalancing the plunge in secular social connectedness.

Chapter 5: Connections in the Workplace

Union and Professional Association Decline

The shift to a service economy explains only one-quarter of union decline. All structural changes combined account for barely half. Unionization fell sharply within every industry — manufacturing down 62%, mining down 79%, construction down 78%. The core issue was skepticism about “membership” itself: “The young worker thinks primarily of himself” (Peter Pestillo). Professional associations doubled in raw numbers but market share peaked and reversed — the AMA peaked in 1959, the ABA in 1977, and architects’ membership rate fell from 41% to 28% despite doubling in absolute terms.

Has Social Capital Moved to the Office?

More Americans were working outside the home (67% in 1997 versus 59% in 1950), and the workplace was increasingly described as a “sanctuary” from domestic stress. 1990s management trends like “quality circles” and “team building” seemed to foster connectedness. But three counterarguments destroy the hypothesis: (1) No data showed workplace socializing had actually grown. (2) Co-workers accounted for fewer than 10% of close friends, and fewer than half of workers listed even one co-worker as close. (3) Downsizing and restructuring became routine even in boom times — half of firms laid off workers in 1993–94, “outplacement” services grew from $35 million to $350 million in a decade, and the most common reaction was to “put your head down” and drift apart.

“World War II veterans joining IBM were instructed to consult with their wives before taking the job, because ‘once you came aboard you were a member of the corporate family for life.’” — That contract is dead.

Nearly 30% of all U.S. workers had contingent or nonstandard jobs (temps, part-timers, contractors), all of which inhibit durable social ties. Job satisfaction fell: 44% preferred hours on the job in 1955 versus barely 16% in 1999. The workplace is not the salvation for our fraying civil society.

Chapter 6: Informal Social Connections

Family Togetherness Declining

Roper polls of families between 1976 and 1997 showed steady declines: vacationing together fell from 53% to 38%, watching TV together from 54% to 41%, and “just sitting and talking” from 53% to 43%. As Putnam notes, it is hard not to read these figures as evidence of rapidly loosening family bonds.

The Death of Schmoozing

Going out to bars and nightclubs declined 40–50% from the mid-1970s to the late 1990s. Full-service restaurants fell by one-quarter. Bars and luncheonettes were cut in half. Meanwhile, fast-food outlets doubled. Unlike the neighborhood bar where “everybody knows your name,” fast-food patrons are anonymous — the disappearance of what Ray Oldenburg called the “great good place.” Social evenings with neighbors fell roughly one-third for married people; neighborhood ties in the 1990s were perhaps less than half as strong as in the mid-1950s. Americans invested more in guns, dogs, and locks than in social capital for crime defense.

Card Playing: A Social Barometer

In 1940, playing cards were found in 87% of American homes (versus 83% with radios and 36% with telephones). By 1958, 35 million Americans played bridge. Then card playing plunged from 1981 to 1999: frequency halved from 16 times a year to 8. At the current decline rate, Putnam calculated it would disappear entirely within two decades. Roughly 500 million card games a year were declining by 25 million annually — if one conversation per ten games touched on community affairs, that meant 50 million fewer “microdeliberations” about civic life each year. The average age of Contract Bridge League members reached 64 and was rising; to 1990s students, “bridge” sounded as antique as “whist.”

Time Diary Evidence: The Full Picture

The fraction recording any informal socializing on a diary day fell from 65% (1965) to 39% (1995). Average daily socializing time dropped from 85 minutes to 57 minutes. The decline was universal: down among men and women, all ages, all classes, all regions, cities and suburbs and small towns, married and single.

From Doing to Watching

Sports participation declined 10–20% across the board — softball, tennis, volleyball, football, bicycling, skiing, hunting, and fishing all fell, with softball dropping by one-third in 15 years. Declines were sharpest among the young: exercise class attendance among 20-somethings halved while it doubled among those over 60. Bowling was the most popular competitive sport in America, with more bowlers than joggers and golfers combined. Total bowlers increased 10% from 1980 to 1993, but league bowling plummeted 40%. League bowlers consumed three times as much beer and pizza as solo bowlers — the money was in social interaction, not just rolling balls. Sports spectatorship nearly doubled since the 1960s while playing instruments fell from 30% to 20% of the population. The pattern across sports, culture, and politics: we watch more and do less.

Chapter 7: Altruism, Volunteering, and Philanthropy

Social Networks Drive Giving

“Someone asked me” is the most common reason people volunteer. “Nobody asked” is why blood donors stop giving. Fund-raising equals friend-raising: the more involved in social networks, the more likely to be recruited. Youth who volunteered were half again as likely to donate as adults and twice as likely to volunteer later — Tocqueville’s “habits of the heart.”

Philanthropy: A Four-Decade Decline

Per capita giving nearly doubled between 1960 and 1995 in absolute terms, but as a share of income, generosity steadily declined since 1961, entirely erasing postwar gains. Total giving fell from 2.26% of national income in 1964 to 1.61% in 1998 — a 29% relative decline. In 1960, Americans gave roughly $1 per $2 spent on recreation; by 1997, they gave less than 50 cents. During the Great Depression, income fell 3% but charity rose 25%+ — generosity tracks social capital, not financial capital.

If giving had matched 1960 levels, it would have added $4 billion per year to United Way, $20 billion per year to congregations, and $50 billion per year overall. Protestant giving per member fell sharply; Catholic giving as a share of income fell 59% from 1960 to 1989. United Way contributions dropped to less than half the 1960 level. Self-reported giving showed that nearly half contributed in the previous month in the 1980s, versus barely one-third by the mid-1990s.

Chapter 8: Reciprocity, Honesty, and Trust

Generalized Reciprocity: The Lubricant of Social Life

Generalized reciprocity combines short-term altruism with long-term self-interest: I’ll help now, confident someone will reciprocate down the road. Tocqueville called this “self-interest rightly understood” — not impossibly idealistic selflessness, but enlightened mutual aid. Trusting communities have measurable economic advantages, for the same reason money is more efficient than barter. Dense social networks foster honesty through reputation — which is why the diamond trade concentrates in close-knit enclaves. But as Putnam notes, “generalized reciprocity is a community asset, but generalized gullibility is not” — trustworthiness, not simply trust, is the key ingredient.

Thick Trust vs. Thin Trust

Thick trust is trust in people you know personally — Max at the corner store. Thin trust is trust in “the generalized other” — even more useful because it extends the radius of trust beyond people you can know personally. As the social fabric becomes threadbare, its power to undergird thin trust is enfeebled.

Four Decades of Dwindling Trust

On the standard survey question “Most people can be trusted,” the affirmative response fell among adults from roughly 55% in 1960 to 35% in the late 1990s. Among high school students, the drop was even more dramatic: from 55% in 1975 to 25% in the mid-1990s. Trust rose from the mid-1940s to the mid-1960s (peaking in 1964), then reversed into long decline. The decline was generational: the generation with a trust quotient of roughly 80% was being replaced by one with barely 40%, even though each individual cohort’s trust level barely changed.

The Rise of Lawyers as Artificial Trust

America had fewer lawyers per capita in 1970 than in 1900 — two world wars, the Depression, and the New Deal had not raised the lawyering ratio. But after 1970, lawyers per capita more than doubled in 25 years. By 1995, there were 34% more lawyers than doctors (versus 3% fewer in 1970). The largest driver was “preventive lawyering” — informal understandings no longer seemed adequate. Spouses, neighbors, and business partners all began demanding to “get it in writing.”

“Like the provider of artificial hormones that supplement the diminished supply... the lawyer contrives enforceability to supplement the failing supply of reciprocity, moral obligation, and fellow-feeling.” — Marc Galanter

This explosion in formal legal recourse may be one of the most revealing indicators of the fraying social fabric — we rely on law to do what informal networks reinforced by generalized reciprocity once accomplished.

Chapter 9: Against the Tide? Small Groups, Social Movements, and the Net

Small Groups: A Quiet Revolution?

Robert Wuthnow reported that 40% of Americans claimed involvement in a regular small group providing support and caring. About half were church-related (Sunday school, Bible study); roughly 5% were self-help groups (AA and 130+ analogues). Reading circles had deep American roots stretching back to post-Civil War self-improvement societies, but numbers were no higher than in the 1960s–70s. Self-help participation ran about 2–3% of adults — all self-help participants combined were outnumbered two-to-one by dropouts from league bowling alone.

Wuthnow’s verdict was telling: small groups create real care and connection but “the social contract binding members together asserts only the weakest of obligations. Come if you have time. Talk if you feel like it. Never criticize. Leave quietly if you become dissatisfied.”

Social Movements: Ground War vs. Air War

The 1960s produced social movements — civil rights, women’s, environmental, gay rights — that permanently expanded the repertoire of civic engagement. Social movements both consume and produce social capital: preexisting networks recruit participants, and collective action forges lifelong bonds. But national “social movement organizations” increasingly became professionalized advocacy operations with paid staffs, direct-mail fundraising, and Washington offices.

The environmental movement membership explosion was revealing: national organizations grew from 125,000 members in 1960 to 6.5 million by 1990. The crucial innovation was direct mail, not deeper civic consciousness — Greenpeace mailed 48 million letters annually, then lost 85% of members when mailings slowed. Sierra Club had only 13% of members who ever attended a single meeting; every week, 20 times more Texans gathered at Rotary clubs than at Audubon Society chapters.

Pro-Life vs. Pro-Choice: The Social Capital Difference

The contrast was stark. The pro-life movement had 13 million members and 7,000 local chapters, built on church-based grassroots networks — a “ground war” adapted to social-capital-rich environments. Pro-choice NARAL tripled to 500,000 then plunged to 190,000 in two years; only 3–5% ever did more than write a check — an “air war” adapted to social-capital-poor environments. The existence of a well-developed national organization using air war techniques is a sign not of grassroots engagement, but of its absence.

Religious Conservatives: The Real Countertrend

Religious conservatives created the largest, best-organized grassroots social movement of the last quarter century. Before 1974, evangelicals were less politically active than average; after 1974, more. Evangelical activists attended church more than once a week (60–70% versus less than 5% of other Americans) and were 3–5 times more active in virtually all civic and political activities. The most important predictor of mobilization was social engagement in religious community, not ideology or demographics.

The Internet

The Internet could not have caused civic decline — the decline was underway for 25 years before 10% of adults were online. Early users were no more or less civically engaged than nonusers. Putnam identified four serious challenges: the digital divide (access concentrated among educated, affluent, white males); loss of nonverbal cues (face-to-face interaction transmits volumes of information essential for trust); cyberbalkanization (interest-based communities replacing place-based heterogeneity with homogeneity); and displacement (potentially substituting for rather than complementing face-to-face contact).

“Social capital may turn out to be a prerequisite for, rather than a consequence of, effective computer-mediated communication.”

Chapter 10: Introduction to Part III — Why?

The first two-thirds of the 20th century saw rising civic engagement across all domains. Then came a mysterious simultaneous reversal — thin, surf-by interactions replacing dense, multistranded bonds. The decline was an equal-opportunity affliction: down among men and women, rich and poor, Black and white, cities and small towns, all regions, married and single. No demographic “hot spot” — the levels differed by group but the trends were virtually identical.

The education paradox was particularly striking: education is the strongest predictor of civic engagement, and educational levels rose massively (college degrees tripled from 1960 to 1998). Yet civic engagement declined even faster among the educated. Whatever the cause, it overwhelmed education’s powerful positive effect. Like Agatha Christie’s Murder on the Orient Express: there was more than one perpetrator. The task was to sort ringleaders from accomplices.

Chapter 11: Pressures of Time and Money

Time Pressure: A Red Herring

The proportion who “always feel rushed” jumped by more than half since the mid-1960s — but aggregate work hours had been roughly constant since WWII. Time diary studies showed a 6.2-hour-per-week gain in free time from 1965 to 1995 (Robinson and Godbey), due to less housework and earlier retirement. Paradoxically, busy people were more civically engaged: employed people were more active than those outside the labor force; longer hours correlated with more, not less, community involvement. They sacrificed TV before civic participation. Sidney Verba’s study found free time had “little or no effect” on whether someone became civically active.

Financial Anxiety: Supporting Actor, Not Lead

Financial anxiety powerfully depressed civic engagement — the jobless became passive and withdrawn; the financially worried third attended only two-thirds as many club meetings. The only leisure activity positively correlated with financial anxiety was watching TV. But civic decline began before the 1970s economic troubles and continued through 1980s and 1990s booms; declines were virtually as great among the affluent as among the poor. Even among the most fortunate Americans, entertaining fell from 17 times a year in 1975 to 10 in 1999. At most, financial anxiety accounted for 5–10% of the total decline.

Women’s Employment: A Modest Role

The fraction of women working outside the home doubled from the 1950s to the 1990s; virtually all the increase was driven by financial necessity, not personal fulfillment. Full-time work reduced club attendance, church attendance, home entertaining, visiting friends, and volunteering. But civic disengagement was almost equally great among affluent housewives and bachelors — groups unaffected by two-career pressures. Best estimate: women’s employment plus financial distress together accounted for less than one-tenth of total decline.

Chapter 12: Mobility and Sprawl

Residential Mobility: Exonerated

Americans are nomadic — roughly one in five move each year — but residential mobility has not increased. It actually slightly declined from 20% in the 1950s to 16% in the 1990s. Homeownership rose to an all-time record of 67% in 1999. This theory is simply wrong — mobility cannot explain civic decline because it has not increased.

Suburbanization: The Real Story

Where we live changed dramatically: small towns and rural areas fell from 44% of the population in 1950 to 20% in 1996, while suburbs grew from 23% to 49%. Metro area residents showed 10–20% fewer group memberships, club meetings, and church attendance, and were 30–40% less likely to serve as officers or attend public meetings. This was not self-selection — comparing identical demographics, metropolitans were less engaged because of where they were, not who they were.

Initial post-WWII suburbs were hyper-civic: Park Forest was a “hotbed of participation” (per William Whyte), Levittown was full of “hyperactive joiners” (per Herbert Gans). Later suburbs fragmented into lifestyle enclaves — collectively heterogeneous, individually homogeneous. Gated communities doubled from 15% to 30% of projects in Orange County within five years. As Lewis Mumford observed: “Suburbia is a collective effort to lead a private life.” Malls replaced Main Street as public space but were “carefully designed for one primary, private purpose — to direct consumers to buy.”

The Car and the Commute

Cars per household nearly doubled from 1969 to 1995. By 1990, America had more cars than drivers. Americans averaged 72 minutes every day behind the wheel; 80–90% drove to work alone. Commuting across county lines tripled from 1960 to 1990; congestion-related delay rose from 16 to 45 hours per year per driver.

Each additional ten minutes in daily commuting time cuts involvement in community affairs by 10 percent — fewer meetings, fewer petitions, less volunteering, less church attendance.

Commuting’s civic penalty extended even to non-commuters in high-commute communities — a synergistic effect with negative externalities. Sprawl disrupted community “boundedness”: work-based ties competed with place-based ties rather than reinforcing them. Overall, the “sprawl civic penalty” of roughly 20% on most measures probably accounted for less than one-tenth of total disengagement.

Chapter 13: Technology and Mass Media

The Transformation of Leisure

Electronic technology enabled two fundamental shifts: the individualization of entertainment (from communal music halls to private Walkmans and cable TV) and the privatization of consumption (from baseball parks to living rooms). As T.S. Eliot remarked of radio: “It is a medium of entertainment which permits millions of people to listen to the same joke at the same time, and yet remain lonesome.”

Newspapers: The Civic Medium in Decline

Newspaper readers were the machers and schmoozers of civic life — they joined more, voted more, volunteered more, visited friends more, and trusted more. But circulation plunged 57% per household from 1948 to 1998, despite education rising 50%. The decline was generational: 3 in 4 Americans born before 1929 read daily papers versus just 1 in 4 Gen X’ers — each generation replaced readers with nonreaders.

Television: The 800-Pound Gorilla

TV reached 90% of American homes in just seven years — the fastest technology diffusion ever recorded. The average American watched roughly four hours per day, representing approximately 40% of free time (the highest viewership in the world). Between 1965 and 1995, Americans gained about six hours per week of leisure time and spent almost all six hours watching TV. Multiple sets proliferated: three-quarters of homes had more than one, and 77% of sixth-graders had a TV in their bedroom by 1999. Viewing became increasingly solitary and habitual — by the mid-1990s, habitual viewers (turn on first, then look for something to watch) outnumbered selective viewers, reversing the pattern of the late 1970s.

The Single Most Consistent Predictor

Agreement with the statement “Television is my primary form of entertainment” was the single most consistent predictor of civic disengagement among all factors tested — including education, generation, income, and commuting. TV minimalists volunteered 9 times per year versus maximalists’ 4; attended 9 club meetings versus 5; went to church 27 times versus 19. Each additional hour of TV per day correlated with roughly a 10% reduction in most forms of civic activism. TV was particularly toxic for collective activities (meetings cut by 40%) compared to individual ones (letters cut 10–15%).

Three Mechanisms of TV’s Impact

1. Time displacement: TV is the only leisure activity that inhibits other leisure activities, violating the usual “the more, the more” pattern. One hour less daily TV equaled the civic equivalent of five to six more years of education.

2. Psychological effects: TV induced passivity, drowsiness, and low alertness. Heavy viewers experienced a kind of addiction — demanding major bribes to give up TV yet reporting it less satisfying than other activities or even work. TV dependence correlated with physical malaise (headaches, insomnia, indigestion) as strongly as financial anxiety.

3. Pseudopersonal connection: TV created a false sense of companionship — viewers felt intimate, informed, and engaged without being engaged. Like “parallel play” in children, where kids play side by side but do not actually interact.

The Canadian Natural Experiment

A study comparing three Canadian towns — “Notel” (no TV) versus “Unitel” and “Multitel” (with TV) — provided near-experimental evidence. When TV was introduced to Notel, community participation significantly declined with no change in the control towns. Clear causal evidence that TV drives disengagement.

“We can almost always tell if a change will bring good or bad tidings. Certain things we definitely do not want, like the television and the radio. They would destroy our visiting practices.” — Amish community member

Channel proliferation (19 channels in 1985 to 49 in 1997) destroyed shared “water cooler” culture: the top-rated show drew two-thirds of viewers in the 1950s, barely one-third by the mid-1990s. TV encouraged materialist values: heavy-viewing adolescents wanted high-status, easy, well-paid jobs. At minimum: television and its electronic cousins are willing accomplices in the civic mystery, and more likely than not, they are ringleaders.

Chapter 14: From Generation to Generation

The Generational Key

Age is second only to education as a predictor of civic engagement. But the crucial distinction is between life cycle effects (temporary, leaving society unchanged) and generational effects (permanent, transforming society). Expected life cycle upswings in civic engagement as baby boomers matured never materialized — no PTA boom, no church surge, no civic profusion. It was “the civic dog that didn’t bark.”

The Long Civic Generation (Born 1910–1940)

The core cohort, born roughly 1925–1930, attended grade school during the Depression, experienced WWII in high school or on the battlefield, and saw their first TV in their late twenties. Since national polling began, they had been exceptionally civic — voting, joining, reading, trusting, and giving more than any subsequent generation, despite having only one-quarter with education beyond high school. At their peak in 1960, they comprised two-thirds of the electorate; by 1992, they were cut in half as boomers and X’ers replaced them. Generational replacement was “the single most important explanation for the collapse of civic engagement over the last several decades.”

“Like Minerva’s owl that flies at dusk, we come to appreciate how important the long civic generation has been to American community life just as its members are retiring.”

Baby Boomers (1946–1964)

The first generation exposed to TV throughout their lives — by age 16, the average boomer had watched 12,000–15,000 hours of television. Indelibly marked by the upheavals of the 1960s, they were distinguished more by what they rejected (institutions, mainstream politics) than what they embraced. Slow to marry, quick to divorce; 96% raised religious but 58% abandoned faith; less comfortable in bureaucracies. Putnam called them “free agents”: tolerant, cynical, laid-back, individualistic — at high social cost.

Generation X (1965–1980)

The erosion of social capital began before any X’er was born — they continued a trend, they did not start it. Extremely individualistic, never connected to politics, shaped by uncertainty (slow growth, the divorce explosion, no collective triumphs). “Having lots of money” was rated important by 46% of high school seniors in 1976, rising to 70% by 1990. Trust collapsed: “most people can be trusted” fell from roughly 61% among boomers of 1976 to 23% among late X’ers of 1995.

Depression, Suicide, and Social Isolation

Depression struck earlier and more pervasively in each successive generation born after 1940; the depression rate increased roughly tenfold over two generations. Teen suicide (ages 15–19) quadrupled from 1950 to 1995, while suicide among older groups declined. The average teenager in the 1990s spent 3.5 hours alone daily, with fewer and weaker friendships than 1950s teens. Martin Seligman observed that the depression epidemic spared the close-knit Amish — “rampant individualism” coupled with weakened institutional commitments was the explanation.

World War II: The Forge of Civic America

WWII directly touched nearly everyone: 16 million served (80% of men born in the 1920s); 12 million home-front volunteers; the Red Cross grew from 1.1 million to 7.5 million; nearly 20 million Victory Gardens yielded 40% of vegetables grown. War bonds, scrap drives, blackout patrols, and block captains created massive voluntary mobilization that formed civic habits lasting decades. WWII was probably the most leveling event in American economic history: the top 1%’s wealth share fell from 31% to 23%; the top 5%’s income share dropped from 28% to 19%. TV and generational effects were likely opposite sides of the same coin — the long civic generation was the last to grow up without TV.

Chapter 15: What Killed Civic Engagement? Summing Up

Suspect #1: Family Structure — Mostly Exonerated

Married-with-kids households dropped from 40% (1970) to 26% (1997), but marriage and parenthood mainly boosted church and youth activities only. Married people actually attended fewer club meetings than singles. Holding traditional family structure constant would explain more religious and PTA participation but no effect on secular organizations or political activities.

Suspect #2: Race and Civil Rights — Not Guilty

African Americans dropped out of organizations at least as rapidly as whites. The pace of white disengagement was uncorrelated with racial attitudes. Generational patterns were incompatible with this theory: disengagement was minimal among those who grew up in the more segregated era. Reversing civil rights gains would do nothing to reverse social capital losses.

Suspect #3: Big Government — No Evidence

Social capital was highest in big-spending Scandinavian welfare states. Among U.S. states, civic engagement was uncorrelated with welfare spending or government size. Federal domestic spending was essentially unchanged (2.2% of GNP in both the late 1940s and late 1990s). A constant cannot explain a change.

Suspect #4: Capitalism and the Market — Too Generic, But...

Market capitalism had existed for centuries during which social capital went through great swings. But “corporate delocalization” — Wal-Mart replacing corner stores, Bank of America absorbing First National — stripped communities of civic leadership. The social cohesion of Atlanta’s elite peaked in the 1960s then declined, following the same trajectory as other measures.

The Real Culprits (Approximate Shares)

  • Generational change (especially TV’s role in shaping post-WWII cohorts): ~50%
  • Television and electronic entertainment: ~25% (overlaps with generational)
  • Suburban sprawl and commuting: ~10%
  • Time and money pressures (two-career families): ~10%
  • Other factors (corporate delocalization, family change): remainder

Chapter 16: So What? Social Capital and Community

Social Capital Makes Us Healthy, Wealthy, and Wise

Americans sensed at a visceral level that social bonds were disintegrating — the market for civic nostalgia boomed (Tom Brokaw’s WWII book, airport renamings). Social capital works through five mechanisms: (1) resolving collective action problems, (2) reducing transaction costs through trust, (3) widening awareness of interconnected fates, (4) serving as information conduits, and (5) improving health through psychological and biological processes.

“Virtually every commercial transaction has within itself an element of trust.... much of the economic backwardness in the world can be explained by a lack of mutual confidence.” — Kenneth Arrow (Nobel laureate)

The Social Capital Index Across 50 States

Putnam combined fourteen indicators into a single index. The differences were substantial: social trust ranged from 17% in Mississippi to 67% in North Dakota. The geographic pattern was striking: high social capital clustered along the Canadian border, centered over the headwaters of the Mississippi and Missouri Rivers. Low social capital centered over the Mississippi Delta, extending through the former Confederacy. The pattern had deep historical roots — Tocqueville had noted the same north-south gradient in the 1830s. The strongest predictor of a state’s social capital was the fraction of its population of Scandinavian stock.

Most striking: the spatial correlation between low social capital today and slavery in the 19th century. Slavery was “a social system designed to destroy social capital.” Inequality and social solidarity are deeply incompatible.

Chapter 18: Safe and Productive Neighborhoods

Criminology and Social Disorganization Theory

Since the 1920s, criminologists developed “ecological” theories linking crime to neighborhood characteristics: high population turnover, anonymous neighbors, ethnic fragmentation, sparse local organizations, and disadvantaged youth in subcultures cut off from the adult world. Robert J. Sampson found that even controlling for poverty, communities with anonymity and sparse acquaintanceship networks, unsupervised teenage peer groups, and a weak organizational base faced increased crime risk. Living in a high-mobility neighborhood doubled the risk of victimization versus a stable one.

Jane Jacobs and Social Capital in Cities

Jane Jacobs (1961) argued that cities configured to maximize informal contact produced safer streets, better child care, and happier residents. Regular contact with the local grocer, families on stoops, the priest walking blocks, street fairs, and parks created what she called “a web of public respect and trust, and a resource in time of personal and neighborhood need.” Higher levels of social capital, all else equal, translate into lower crime.

State-Level Evidence: Homicide and Social Capital

States with more social capital had dramatically fewer murders — the inverse relationship was “astonishingly strong.” Social capital was more important than education levels, single-parent household rates, and income inequality in predicting murders from 1980 to 1995. Once differences in social capital were accounted for, the infamous Southern regional difference in murder rates vanished entirely. “Lethal violence is endemic wherever social capital is deficient.”

Gangs as Misguided Social Capital

Where mainstream organizations were absent, youth created their own social capital in the form of gangs. Gang life involved reciprocal obligations and norms similar to bowling teams — lending money, buying rounds, serious mutual obligations spanning years. The external effects were typically harmful, but gangs served as social institutions where mainstream ones had been debilitated.

The Vicious Circle and Community Policing

Social capital was often lacking where needed most, creating a vicious circle: low trust led to higher crime, which led to even lower trust. “Neighborhood crime watch” programs were most likely to succeed in middle-class neighborhoods that already had trust and networks. Community policing represented “applied social capitalism” — fighting crime by building partnerships between law enforcement and community. Boston’s 10-Point Coalition, brokered by local ministers, succeeded because only ministers had the connections and trust (social capital) to make it work.

Poor people lack economic and human capital, making social capital disproportionately important to their welfare. Erosion affected Grosse Pointe and inner-city Detroit equally in degree, but the impact was far greater in the inner city, which lacked the cushion of other capital forms.

Chapter 19: Economic Prosperity

Social Connections and Employment

Mark Granovetter’s groundbreaking 1970s research found that casual acquaintances (“weak ties”) were more valuable for job searches than close friends or family. Distant acquaintances linked people to unexpected opportunities. “Weak ties” had especially strong impact on the fortunes of people at the margins of mainstream economic institutions. Roughly one-half of people got their jobs through a friend or relative.

Ethnic Networks as Economic Engines

Ethnic immigrant communities used social capital intensively: employers relied on employees to recruit and train new workers; immigrants accessed financing through family gifts and rotating credit associations. Among Korean business owners, roughly 70% used debt financing — 41% got money from family, 24% from friends (versus 37% from financial institutions). Church attendance was one of the strongest predictors of whether inner-city Black youth became gainfully employed — it was the social networking aspect of churchgoing, not religious beliefs, behind economic success.

Social Capital Beyond Employment

Ronald Burt found that social ties embodied in a business executive’s Rolodex were at least as important as educational qualifications and experience. Brian Uzzi (studying Chicago banking) found that firms embedding commercial transactions with lenders in social attachments received lower interest rates on loans. DiMaggio and Louch found that people who transacted with friends and relatives reported greater satisfaction.

Silicon Valley vs. Route 128

Silicon Valley’s success was due to horizontal networks of informal and formal cooperation among nominally competing companies. Leaders shared information, problem-solving techniques, and beers after work. The Homebrew Computer Club (a hobbyists’ group) produced leaders of 20+ companies. Route 128 (Boston) maintained traditional norms of corporate hierarchy, secrecy, and self-sufficiency — and performed far worse economically.

“The contrasting experience of Silicon Valley and Route 128 suggests that industrial systems built on regional networks are more flexible and technologically dynamic than those in which experimentation and learning are confined to individual firms.”

Tupelo, Mississippi: A Case Study

In 1940, Tupelo was one of the poorest counties in the poorest state. Through George McLean’s exceptional leadership, business and civic leaders united around the idea that economic development required community development. The elite Chamber of Commerce was disbanded; an inclusive Community Development Foundation was opened to everyone. Over fifty years: 1,000 industrial jobs were added annually, hundreds of millions invested, and arguably the best schools in Mississippi were built. Residents invested in social capital and reaped tangible economic returns.

Chapter 20: Health and Happiness

Social Capital and Health: The Evidence

The importance of social connectedness for health was the best-established of all social capital’s effects, traceable to Emile Durkheim’s Suicide in the 19th century. Dozens of studies from Alameda (California) to Tecumseh (Michigan) found that social connectedness is one of the most powerful determinants of well-being. More integrated people were less likely to experience colds, heart attacks, strokes, cancer, depression, and premature death of all sorts.

The Magnitude of the Effect

James House and colleagues found that the positive health contributions of social integration rivaled in strength the detrimental contributions of smoking, obesity, elevated blood pressure, and physical inactivity. Over the previous twenty years, more than a dozen large studies showed that people who are socially disconnected are between two and five times more likely to die from all causes. Lisa Berkman found that social isolation was “a chronically stressful condition to which the organism responds by aging faster.”

The Roseto Study

Roseto was a small Italian American community studied for nearly 40 years. Rosetans’ age-adjusted heart attack rate was less than half that of neighbors; not a single resident under 47 died of a heart attack over a seven-year period. The usual explanations (diet, exercise, genetics) did not hold — Rosetans actually had more risk factors. The key: a tight-knit community with mutual aid societies, churches, sports clubs, and front-porch socializing. By the 1980s, as young people rejected the tight-knit folkways, heart attack rates rose above those of their neighbors.

Harvard School of Public Health Study

A survey of nearly 170,000 individuals across all fifty states found that moving from a state with high social capital to one with low social capital increased chances of poor health by roughly 40–70%. Moving to a high-social-capital state would do almost as much good as quitting smoking.

“As a rough rule of thumb, if you belong to no groups but decide to join one, you cut your risk of dying over the next year in half.”

Life Satisfaction and Happiness

The single most common finding from half a century’s research: happiness is best predicted by the breadth and depth of one’s social connections. Marriage was the happiness equivalent of quadrupling annual income (moving from the 15th to the 85th percentile). Regular club attendance, volunteering, entertaining, or church attendance was the happiness equivalent of getting a college degree or more than doubling income. Since the 1960s, life satisfaction declined steadily — half due to financial worries, half due to declining social capital.

Chapter 21: Democracy

Classical Democratic Theory

Oscar Wilde: “The trouble with socialism is that it would take too many evenings.” How many evenings does liberal democracy take? Jefferson (1816) proposed ward republics small enough for every citizen to attend meetings in person — “Making every citizen an acting member of the government.” Tocqueville argued that local civic activity served as handmaiden of national democratic community. John Stuart Mill warned that without shared participation, citizens think of collective interests only in competition. John Dewey: “Democracy must begin at home, and its home is the neighborly community.”

Associations as Schools for Democracy

Civic associations served as “schools for democracy” where members learned to run meetings, speak in public, write letters, organize projects, and debate issues. For working-class Americans, voluntary associations and churches offered the best opportunities to exercise civic skills — even better than the workplace. Churches played an egalitarian role: one of the few institutions where low-income, minority, and disadvantaged citizens could learn politically relevant skills.

Italian Regional Government Study

In 1970, Italy established 20 identical regional governments in radically different social and economic contexts. Some became dismal failures (inefficient, corrupt); others remarkably successful (innovative programs, satisfied constituents). The best predictor of success: strong traditions of civic engagement — voter turnout, newspaper readership, choral societies, Lions Clubs, soccer clubs. These civic traditions could be traced back nearly a millennium to 11th-century communal republics in Florence, Bologna, and Genoa.

Tax Compliance and Social Capital

Controlling for per capita income, inequality, race, urbanism, and education, social capital was the only factor that successfully predicted tax compliance. Where citizens viewed others as basically honest, tax compliance was higher. In a community rich in social capital, government was “we,” not “they.”

The Tolerance Paradox

America became far more tolerant from the 1950s to the 1990s while simultaneously becoming less connected. But the evidence showed that individuals more engaged with communities were more tolerant, not less. The most tolerant communities in America were the places with the greatest civic involvement; communities where residents “bowled alone” were the least tolerant places. No empirical study confirmed a supposed link between community involvement and intolerance.

Political Polarization

Self-described middle-of-the-roaders became one-half as likely to participate in public meetings and organizations between the 1970s and 1990s, while “very” liberal or conservative citizens barely declined. Civic disengagement exacerbated the “faction” problem as moderate voices fell silent, leaving only ideological extremes in the public square.

Chapter 22: Social Capital, Equality, and the Dark Side

Social Capital and Equality: Complementary, Not Warring

The empirical reality: community and equality are mutually reinforcing, not mutually incompatible. The 1950s and 1960s were the period when America was most egalitarian in wealth and income distribution in more than a century — the same decades that marked the high point of social connectedness. The reversal around 1965–70 brought growing inequality and eroding social capital simultaneously. American states with the highest social capital were the states most characterized by economic equality.

Three causal interpretations: (1) Social capital produces equality — the main weapon of have-nots who lack other forms of capital. (2) Equality fosters social capital — great disparities of wealth and power are inimical to widespread participation. (3) External forces cause both — like the leveling effects of massive (and victorious) war. The evidence powerfully contradicted the view that community engagement must amplify inequality.

The Dark Side: Fraternity at War with Itself

Fraternity is most easily created in opposition to something or someone else and most natural within socially homogeneous groups. Race remained the most important embodiment of ethical crosscurrents around social capital in America. The parts of the US where social trust was lowest were the places where slavery and racialist policy had been most entrenched historically. The civil rights movement had aimed at destroying certain exclusive, non-bridging forms of social capital — racially homogeneous schools and neighborhoods.

The Busing Dilemma

Proponents argued that only through racially integrated schools could America generate bridging social capital across racial divides. Opponents argued that neighborhood schools provided unique sites for building bonding social capital. Putnam’s tragic conclusion: “Both sides were probably right.” As Eileen McDonagh asked: “Is it better to have neighborhoods legally restricted on the basis of race, but with everyone having everyone else over for dinner, or unrestricted on the basis of race, but with very little social interaction between neighbors?”

False optimism: To console ourselves that the collapse of community has brought us a more liberal, egalitarian America. False pessimism: To refrain from rebuilding community for fear it will lead to intolerance and injustice.

Chapter 23: Lessons of History — The Gilded Age and the Progressive Era

The Historical Parallel

The Gilded Age (1870–1900) was a time very like our own: technological advance, unparalleled prosperity, but nostalgia for integrated connectedness; new communications promising new community, but thoughtful observers worried those forms would be “fool’s gold.” In the three to four decades after the Civil War, the Industrial Revolution, urbanization, and massive immigration transformed American communities — millions left family and friends on the farm for Chicago and Pittsburgh, or left Polish shtetls and Italian villages for the Lower East Side.

The symptoms were eerily familiar: crime waves, degradation in cities, inadequate education, a widening gap between rich and poor, and a “saturnalia” of political corruption. Patents were generated at nearly 20 times the rate of the previous 80 years. Steel production mushroomed from 77,000 tons in 1870 to 11.2 million in 1900. Per capita wealth increased roughly 60%, but the gains were not spread evenly: the top 1% owned more than 50% of national wealth, while the bottom 44% owned only 1.2%.

The Associational Boom (1870–1920)

Americans responded with a veritable “boom” in association building. Per capita density of voluntary associations rose sharply. Half of all the largest mass membership organizations in two centuries of American history were founded between 1870 and 1920 (Theda Skocpol). Of all 506 “societies and associations” listed in Encarta 2000, almost twice as many were founded from 1890 to 1920 as from 1960 to 1990. It is hard to name a major mainline civic institution in American life today that was not invented in these few decades: the Red Cross, NAACP, Knights of Columbus, Hadassah, Boy Scouts, Rotary, PTA, Sierra Club, Audubon Society, American Bar Association, Farm Bureau, League of Women Voters, Teamsters.

Fraternal Organizations and Women’s Associations

By 1897, “Every fifth, or possibly every eighth, man you meet is identified with some fraternal organization.” By 1910, conservatively one-third of all adult males were members. Fraternalism represented a reaction against individualism and anomie; it provided material benefits (life and health insurance) and social solidarity and ritual. They “successfully created vast social and mutual aid networks among the poor.”

Women built parallel power: the WCTU under Frances Willard adopted a “Do Everything” policy encompassing prison reform, kindergartens, and labor reform. The General Federation of Women’s Clubs campaigned for food inspection, housing codes, safer drinking water, workplace protection, and services for the poor, sick, and children. The National Association of Colored Women’s Clubs, barred by segregation from the GFWC, supported nurseries, kindergartens, and homes for unwed mothers.

Settlement Houses and Youth Organizations

An extraordinary burst between 1901 and 1910 produced most nationwide youth organizations in a single decade — Boy Scouts, Girl Scouts, Campfire Girls, 4-H, Boys Clubs, Big Brothers and Big Sisters. Settlement houses multiplied from Hull House in 1889 to six by 1891, seventy-four by 1897, and roughly 400 by 1910. Ironically, their greatest long-term effect was on service givers, not recipients — settlements produced leaders like Florence Kelley, Eleanor Roosevelt, and presidents of GE and AT&T.

The Progressive Method

Progressives typically began by organizing a voluntary association, investigating a problem, gathering facts via social sciences, proposing solutions via education and moral suasion — and if it worked, the effort was taken over by government. Investment in social capital was not an alternative to, but a prerequisite for, political mobilization and reform. Political achievements included: the secret ballot (1888), popular initiative and referendum (1898), presidential primaries (1900), direct election of senators (1913), women’s suffrage (1920), the Federal Reserve (1913), the income tax (1913), the FDA, FTC, national parks, child labor laws, and the eight-hour day.

Lessons for Today

The institutions formed between 1880 and 1910 lasted nearly a century; the trends in Part II registered the decay of that very structure over the last third of the 20th century. Progressive leaders correctly diagnosed the problem but resisted reactionary nostalgia. Just as “Life was much nicer in the village” was wrong then, “Life was much nicer in the fifties — would all women please report to the kitchen and turn off the TV” was wrong now. We desperately need an era of civic inventiveness to create renewed institutions for reinvigorated civic life. What we create may look nothing like Progressive institutions — but willingness to err, and then correct our aim, is the price of success in social reform.

Chapter 24: Toward an Agenda for Social Capitalists

Arts as Bridge-Building

The goal: ensure Americans participate in (not merely consume) cultural activities — group dancing, songfests, community theater, rap festivals. Art is especially useful in transcending conventional social barriers; social capital is often a valuable by-product of cultural activities. Liz Lerman’s Dance Exchange built community through modern dance, bringing together unemployed shipyard workers and white-collar professionals. Roadside Theater dramatized local stories in declining Appalachian towns to restore community confidence. Toni Blackman’s Freestyle Union used hip-hop, rap, poetry, and improvisation to attract people from all walks of life. Many produced great art, but all produced great bridging social capital — “in some respects an even more impressive achievement.”

Politics and Government

Campaign finance reform should aim at increasing the importance of social capital (time-based participation) and decreasing the importance of financial capital (check-based participation) in elections. Government authority should be decentralized as far as possible to bring decisions to local jurisdictions — neighborhood councils in Minneapolis, Portland, and Seattle created new social capital through potluck dinners, community gardens, and flea markets.

Policy designers should become “social capital-savvy” — seeking to do minimum damage to existing stocks even as they added new ones. Interstate 65 pierced Indianapolis neighborhoods in the 1960s, disrupting social networks for decades — a “social-capital impact statement” could have prevented this. Many of the most creative investments in social capital (county agents, 4-H, community colleges, March of Dimes) were direct results of government policy.

Service Learning and Youth Engagement

Service learning (community service coupled to classwork) was believed more effective at inculcating civic habits than standalone service. By 1999, roughly 57% of students in grades 6–12 participated in community service, but only 30% in service learning specifically. Follow-up studies showed that regardless of social class, high school seniors in voluntary associations were far more likely to vote, participate in campaigns, and discuss public issues two years after graduation.

The Final Word

America’s major civic institutions were somewhat antiquated a century after most were created — they needed reform to invite more active participation. But institutional reform would not work unless citizens resolved to become reconnected with friends and neighbors. Henry Ward Beecher’s century-old advice to “multiply picnics” was not entirely ridiculous. We should do this, ironically, not because it would be good for America — though it would be — but because it would be good for us.

Methodological Appendix: Key Points

Triangulation Strategy

Putnam’s primary strategy was to triangulate among as many independent data sources as possible. No single source of data was flawless, but the more numerous and diverse the sources, the less likely they could all share the same flaw. Two independent strands of evidence are better than one, and more than two are better still, especially if they have different imperfections.

Key Data Sources

The four primary survey series were: the General Social Survey (since 1972), Roper Social and Political Trends (1973–94, with 410,000+ respondents), DDB Needham Life Style (since 1975, 87,000+ respondents), and National Election Studies (since 1952). Organizational records provided long-term data but missed informal activity and new organizations — one scholar estimated that 80% of community groups were “dark matter,” invisible to conventional chroniclers. The Americans’ Use of Time Project provided careful time diaries from national samples in 1965, 1975, 1985, and 1995.

The Education Paradox

Education was the most important predictor of civic participation, and educational levels rose sharply during the period of interest. Evidence suggested that the effects of education on participation were absolute, not relative — my education increased my participation, and yours did not lower it. Putnam generally did not control for rising education, which meant the evidence presented may well have understated the gross decline in civic engagement.

Rise and Fall of Civic Organizations

The golden age was 1945–1965, when most civic groups experienced peak membership rates. Catastrophic declines followed: Odd Fellows down 94%, American Bowling Congress down 72%, AAUW down 84%, Business and Professional Women down 84%. Service clubs (Rotary, Lions, Kiwanis) showed moderate declines of 25–55%. Only newer professional groups or ethnically specific groups (Hadassah, Knights of Columbus) maintained or grew membership rates.
