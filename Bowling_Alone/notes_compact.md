# Bowling Alone — Compacted Notes

## Chapter 1: Thinking about Social Change in America

### The Puzzle: Civic Collapse in the 1990s

- Across America, civic organizations that had thrived for decades suddenly withered: Glenn Valley PA Bridge Club (40+ members → dissolved), Little Rock Sertoma Club (50 → 7), Roanoke NAACP (2,500 → few hundred), Dallas Charity League (57 years of Friday meetings, final meeting April 1999, average age 80+), Tewksbury HS marching band (80+ → 4 sign-ups)
- Vassar alumnae book sale co-chair: "They're dying, and they're not replaceable"

> "The puzzle: In the last several decades of the twentieth century all these community groups and tens of thousands like them across America began to fade."

### The Optimism of the Early 1960s

- Membership rolls had **lengthened steadily** for as long as anyone could remember
- 1958 study warned "the most dangerous threat hanging over American society is the threat of leisure"
- Voting turnout rose at 1.6% per presidential election cycle; projected to reach 70% by 1976
- **Trust in neighbors** peaked at 77% in 1964 (up from 66% during/after WWII)
- Americans who wanted children to enter politics nearly doubled in a decade
- Education — **by far the best predictor** of civic engagement — was expanding rapidly with baby boomers approaching college

> "We were a 'can do' people, who accomplished whatever we set out to do. We had licked the Depression, turned the tide in World War II, and rebuilt Europe after the war." — Doug McAdam

### The Era Wasn't Perfect

- Segregation by race (legal) and gender (social) was the norm; infant mortality 26/1,000 (44/1,000 for Black infants)
- But **engagement in community affairs and sense of shared identity/reciprocity had never been greater** in modern America
- Prospects for broad-based civic mobilization seemed bright

### Social Capital Defined

**Social capital = connections among individuals** — social networks and norms of reciprocity and trustworthiness that arise from them. Analogous to physical capital (tools) and human capital (education), social contacts affect the productivity of individuals and groups.

- Term independently invented **at least 6 times** in the 20th century:
  - **L. J. Hanifan** (1916): West Virginia school reformer — "good will, fellowship, sympathy, and social intercourse"
  - **Jane Jacobs** (1960s): neighborliness in the metropolis
  - **Glenn Loury** (1970s): social legacy of slavery
  - **Pierre Bourdieu** (1980s): social/economic resources in networks
  - **James S. Coleman** (1980s): social context of education

- **Private face**: Networking for jobs, career advancement — "social networks are important in all our lives" (Claude S. Fischer)
- **Public face**: Externalities benefit the wider community — crime reduction from neighbors watching each other's homes benefits even uninvolved residents

### Reciprocity: The Engine of Social Capital

- **Specific reciprocity**: I'll do X if you do Y
- **Generalized reciprocity**: I'll do X confident that someone will reciprocate down the road (the Golden Rule)
- Society with generalized reciprocity is **more efficient** — like money vs. barter
- Dense social ties reduce incentives for opportunism — why the **diamond trade** concentrates in close-knit ethnic enclaves

### Bridging vs. Bonding: The Most Important Distinction

- **Bonding** (exclusive): Inward-looking, reinforces homogeneous groups — ethnic fraternal orgs, country clubs. Good for **"getting by"** — undergirding solidarity, providing start-up financing, psychological support
- **Bridging** (inclusive): Outward-looking, crosses social cleavages — civil rights movement, youth service groups. Crucial for **"getting ahead"** — linking to external assets, diffusing information
- **Mark Granovetter**: "Weak" ties (distant acquaintances) more valuable than "strong" ties for jobs and political allies
- Bonding = **sociological superglue**; Bridging = **sociological WD-40**
- Groups can bond along some dimensions while bridging across others (Knights of Columbus: bridged ethnic lines, bonded along religious/gender lines)

### American Cultural Tension: Community vs. Individualism

- Oscillation between community and individualism is a perennial American theme — from Pilgrims to Emerson's "Self-Reliance" to Thoreau's "Civil Disobedience"
- Paul Revere's alarm succeeded because of **networks of civic engagement** in Middlesex villages, not individual heroism (David Hackett Fischer)
- At century's end: 77% said nation worse because of "less involvement in community activities"; 80% said more emphasis should be placed on community

### Putnam's Method and Thesis

- **Not arguing** steady decline throughout history — American civic engagement has ups AND downs; within living memory, bonds were strengthening
- Method: **triangulation** — "two source" rule, never reporting anything without independent confirmation
- Seeking evidence across as diverse a range as possible — if transformation is broad and deep, it should show up everywhere

### The Bowling Story

- **October 29, 1997**: John Lambert (64, retired) and Andy Boschma (33, accountant) knew each other only through bowling league in Ypsilanti, Michigan. Lambert needed a kidney; Boschma offered one of his. The newspaper photo revealed they were different races — but they bowled together
- "In small ways like this, and in larger ways, too, we Americans need to reconnect with one another." That is the simple argument of this book.

## Chapter 2: Political Participation

### Voting Decline: The Most Visible Symptom

- **1960**: 62.8% voted (Kennedy vs. Nixon) → **1996**: 48.9% (lowest in nearly two centuries of free elections)
- Presidential participation declined ~25% over 36 years; off-year and local elections down similarly
- Decline occurred **despite lower barriers** — "motor voter" registration, relaxed requirements, $100M spent to swell voter rolls
- Outside the South, the slide since 1960 is the **longest decline in American history**

### The Decline Is Generational, Not Individual

- **Virtually all** the net decline in voting is attributable to generational replacement, not individuals changing behavior
- **Intracohort change** (individuals shifting together) is fast and reversible — like SUV fads
- **Intercohort change** (generational replacement) is slow but **nearly inexorable** — like changing sexual mores
- Baby boomers and their children have been **less likely to vote throughout their entire lives** than their parents/grandparents
- "Every year the Grim Reaper removes another swath of the most politically engaged generation in the American electorate"

### Voting as Proxy for Broader Disengagement

- Voters vs. demographically matched nonvoters are more likely to: give to charity, volunteer, serve on juries, attend school board meetings, cooperate with fellow citizens
- Recent evidence: **the act of voting itself encourages volunteering** and other good citizenship
- Declining electoral participation is "even more important as a sign of deeper trouble in the body politic than as a malady itself"

### Cooperative Activities Declined Most

**Roper surveys, relative change 1973–74 to 1993–94:**
- Served as officer of club/organization: **−42%**
- Worked for political party: **−42%**
- Served on committee for local organization: **−39%**
- Attended public meeting on town/school affairs: **−35%**
- Writing letters to paper or Congress: only −14% to −23%

- **Key pattern**: Activities requiring coordination with others (embodying social capital) declined most; individual expressive acts were more stable
- Utterly uninvolved Americans rose from 46% to 64% (32 million fewer engaged); active citizens (3+ activities) cut in half from 20% to 11%

### Political Knowledge Paradox

- Americans score similarly on civics tests as their grandparents — despite **four more years of average formal education**
- **Average college graduate today knows little more about public affairs than the average high school graduate in the 1940s**
- Daily newspaper readership among under-35s: **67% (1965) → 33% (1990)**
- TV news viewership for same group: **52% → 41%**
- Post-baby boom generations are **substantially less knowledgeable** despite proliferation of information sources

### Political Alienation

- 1960s: 75% trusted government to do right most of the time; by 1990s: 75% did NOT
- April 1966 (Vietnam, race riots): 66% *rejected* "people running the country don't really care what happens to you"
- December 1997 (peace and prosperity): 57% *endorsed* that same cynical view

> "We remain, in short, reasonably well-informed spectators of public affairs, but many fewer of us actually partake in the game."

## Chapter 3: Civic Participation

> "Americans of all ages, all stations in life, and all types of disposition are forever forming associations.... Nothing, in my view, deserves more attention than the intellectual and moral associations in America." — Alexis de Tocqueville

### The Paradox: More Organizations, Fewer Members

- National nonprofit organizations tripled from 10,299 (1968) to 22,901 (1997), but **median membership shrank from ~10,000 to 1,000** — proliferation of letterheads, not grassroots participation
- **Old organizations headquartered in heartland** (Boy Scouts in Irving TX, Lions in Oak Brook IL, American Legion in Indianapolis); **new organizations cluster around Capitol Hill** (AARP, NOW, Greenpeace, Common Cause) — professionally staffed advocacy organizations, not member-centered associations
- Of 83 public-interest groups founded in early 1970s, **two-thirds had no local chapters at all**

> Rotary alone has nearly twice as many chapters as all eighty-three public-interest groups combined.

### "Tertiary" Associations: Membership Means Writing a Check

- **AARP**: 400,000 members (1960) → 33 million (1990s), but <10% belong to local chapters; "grassroots activities on life support even during maximum membership growth"
- **Greenpeace**: Became largest environmental org through aggressive direct mail; when leadership cut back mailings, membership **hemorrhaged 85%** by 1998
- Bond between National Wildlife Federation members is like bond between two Yankees fans on opposite coasts — shared symbols, not awareness of each other's existence

### Chapter-Based Organizations: The Real Story

**Long upward wave (1900–1960s)**: Membership in 32 national chapter-based associations roughly doubled from 1940-45 to peak; more women in women's clubs, more youth in Scouts, more Jews in Hadassah/B'nai B'rith, more men in service clubs

**The decline**: Membership rates **plateaued 1957, peaked early 1960s, sustained decline began 1969**, then slightly less than halved from peak to 1997

- **PTA**: One of the most impressive organizational success stories — near-exponential growth 1910–1960, then **shocking reversal**. Membership per 100 families with children dropped from ~50 (early 1960s) to <20 (early 1980s). Between 1990–1997, PTA lost 500,000 members even as families with children grew by 2 million
- Very different organizations — different constituency, age, leadership — **entered rough water at about the same time**

### Active Involvement Collapsed Faster Than Nominal Membership

- **Leadership roles cut by more than 50%** (1973–1994): men and women taking any leadership role in any local organization
- Dismaying trend **accelerated after 1985** — active involvement fell 45% in just ten years (1985–1994)
- **Club meeting attendance**: 12/year average (1975) → 5/year (1999); those attending at least one meeting fell from 64% to 38%

> "In the mid-1970s nearly two-thirds of all Americans attended club meetings, but by the late 1990s nearly two-thirds never do."

- **Time diaries**: Americans' investment in organizational life fell from 3.7 hrs/month (1965) → 2.3 hrs/month (1995)
- Nearly half of Americans in the 1960s invested some time each week in clubs; less than one-quarter in the 1990s
- Decline attributable to **generational replacement** — each successive generation investing less
- Among college graduates, club meetings/year fell **55%** (from 13 to 6)
- **Consumer leisure spending on club/fraternal dues**: 6 cents per dollar (1929) → 5 cents (1958 peak) → 3 cents (1997)
- Four independent data streams converge: **active involvement fell by more than half** in the last decades of the 20th century

## Chapter 4: Religious Participation

### Churches as Social Capital Powerhouses

- US is one of the most religiously observant countries in the world; religious adherence grew from 17% (1776) to 62% (1980)
- **Nearly half** of all associational memberships are church-related; half of all philanthropy and half of all volunteering occur in religious context
- Churches provide an incubator for civic skills — speeches, meetings, managing disagreements, administrative responsibility
- **Religiosity rivals education** as the most powerful correlate of civic engagement; regular attendees talk with **40% more people daily**
- 75-80% of church members give to charity vs. 55-60% of nonmembers; 50-60% volunteer vs. 30-35%
- Churches provided the organizational base for abolition, temperance, civil rights, and right-to-life movements

> "[T]he Black church functioned as the institutional center of the modern civil rights movement.... Churches provided the movement with an organized mass base; a leadership of clergymen... skilled in the art of managing people and resources."

### The Decline: Three to Four Decades of Erosion

- **Church membership**: Rose from 1930s to ~1960, then long slow slump of ~10%; "no religion" quadrupled from 2% (1967) to 11% (1990s)
- **Church attendance**: Rose sharply after WWII, declined roughly one-third between late 1950s and late 1990s; overreporting may understate the decline
- **Church-related social activities** (Sunday schools, Bible study, socials): Fell from ~25% membership (1950s) to ~12.5% (1990s) — a 50% decline
- **Time diaries**: Americans devoted only two-thirds as much time to religion in 1995 as in 1965 (1hr 37min/week → 1hr 7min); fraction spending *any* time on religion fell nearly one-half
- In all three domains (politics, organizations, religion): **the more demanding the form of involvement, the greater the decline**

### Generational Replacement Drives the Decline

- Each generation is less religiously observant than the previous one; boomers dropped out at high rates, and even those who returned never closed the gap with their parents
- **Wade Clark Roof**: Two-thirds of boomers reared in religious tradition dropped out; less than half returned
- College freshmen claiming "no" religious preference: 7% (1966) → 14% (1997)
- Country becoming polarized: more devout AND more unchurched, while the middle collapses — the "culture wars" substratum

### The Evangelical Paradox: Strong Bonding, Weak Bridging

- Mainline Protestants lost massive market share; evangelical/fundamentalist groups grew (latest reenactment of a recurring pattern: Methodists overtook Episcopalians, now fundamentalists overtake Methodists)
- Evangelical churches build **extraordinarily strong internal communities** — more regular attendance, more generous giving (2.8% vs. 1.6% of income), more close friendships within congregation
- But evangelical social capital is invested **inward, not outward**: church attendance among evangelicals is NOT correlated with membership in community organizations
- Mainline Protestants transfer civic skills to wider community; evangelicals tend not to
- **Robert Wuthnow**: "Mainline Protestant churches encourage civic engagement in the wider community, whereas evangelical churches apparently do not"

> "Religion is likely to have a diminished impact on society if [worship, family time, and moral lessons are] the only role it plays. What interested Tocqueville about voluntary organizations was… their ability to forge connections across large segments of the population."

### Summary: Religion's Entry in the Social Capital Ledger

1. Religion remains a central fount of American community life — both directly (social services) and indirectly (nurturing civic skills, encouraging altruism)
2. Religious participation mirrors secular trends: flowering through the 1960s, then fading; decline is generational
3. The shift from mainline to evangelical Christianity means churches are **less engaged with wider community** — trends in religious life reinforce rather than counterbalance the plunge in secular social connectedness

## Chapter 5: Connections in the Workplace

### Union and Professional Association Decline

- Economic shift to services explains only **one-quarter** of union decline; all structural changes account for barely half. Unionization fell sharply *within* every industry (manufacturing −62%, mining −79%, construction −78%)
- Core issue: skepticism about **"membership" itself**, not just unions — "The young worker thinks primarily of himself" (Peter Pestillo)
- Professional associations doubled in raw numbers but **market share peaked and reversed** (AMA peaked 1959, ABA peaked 1977, architects' membership rate fell from 41% → 28% despite doubling in absolute terms)

### The Workplace Hypothesis: Has Social Capital Moved to the Office?

- More Americans working outside home (67% in 1997 vs. 59% in 1950); workplace increasingly a "sanctuary" from domestic stress (Arlie Russell Hochschild)
- 1990s management trends ("quality circles," "team building") seemed to foster connectedness
- **Three counterarguments destroy the hypothesis:**
  1. **No evidence of increase**: No data showing workplace socializing has actually grown; indirect evidence suggests the opposite
  2. **Glass half-empty**: Co-workers account for <10% of close friends; fewer than half of workers list even one co-worker as a close friend; Americans' most important personal networks are **not** centered in workplace
  3. **Restructuring damages social ties**: Downsizing became routine even in boom times (half of firms laid off workers in 1993-94). "Outplacement" services grew from $35M → $350M (1980-89). Most common reaction: "put your head down" and drift apart

> "World War II veterans joining IBM were instructed to consult with their wives before taking the job, because 'once you came aboard you were a member of the corporate family for life.'" — That contract is dead.

- Nearly **30% of all U.S. workers** have contingent/nonstandard jobs (temps, part-timers, contractors) — all inhibit durable social ties, and that fraction is rising
- Job satisfaction fell: 44% preferred hours on job (1955) → barely 16% (1999)
- Two-thirds of employers monitor employee communications; free speech/privacy rights are "insecure in the workplace"
- **The workplace is not the salvation for our fraying civil society**

## Chapter 6: Informal Social Connections

### Family Togetherness Declining

- Roper polls of families (1976-1997): Vacationing together 53% → 38%, watching TV together 54% → 41%, "just sitting and talking" 53% → 43%

> "It is hard not to read these figures as evidence of rapidly loosening family bonds."

### The Death of Schmoozing

- Going out to bars/nightclubs **declined 40-50%** from mid-1970s to late 1990s; full-service restaurants fell by one-quarter; bars and luncheonettes cut in half; fast-food outlets doubled
- Unlike neighborhood bars where "everybody knows your name," fast-food patrons are anonymous — the disappearance of Ray Oldenburg's "great good place"
- Social evenings with neighbors: married people from ~30x/year → ~20x/year (one-third decline); neighborhood ties in the 1990s perhaps **less than half as strong** as the mid-1950s
- We invest more in **guns, dogs, and locks** than in social capital for crime defense

### Card Playing: A Social Barometer

- 1940: Playing cards found in 87% of American homes (vs. 83% radios, 36% telephones); 1958: 35 million Americans played bridge
- Card playing plunged 1981-1999: frequency halved (16x/year → 8x/year); at current decline rate, would **disappear entirely within two decades**
- 500 million card games/year declining by 25 million/year — if one conversation per ten games, that's **50 million fewer "microdeliberations"** about community affairs annually
- Average age of Contract Bridge League members: 64 and rising; to 1990s students, "bridge" sounded as antique as "whist"
- Substitutes are solitary: megacasinos with "acres of lonely players hunched in silence over one-armed bandits"; internet bridge with "very little social small talk"

### Time Diary Evidence: The Full Picture

- % recording *any* informal socializing on a diary day: **65% (1965) → 39% (1995)**
- Average daily socializing time: **85 minutes → 57 minutes** — only two-thirds as much
- Decline universal: down among men and women, all ages, all classes, all regions, cities/suburbs/small towns, married and single

### From Doing to Watching

- Sports participation down 10-20% (softball, tennis, volleyball, football, bicycling, skiing, hunting, fishing); softball **dropped by one-third** in 15 years
- Declines **sharpest among the young** — exercise class attendance among 20-somethings halved while it doubled among 60+
- **Bowling**: Most popular competitive sport in America. Bowlers outnumber joggers/golfers 2:1, soccer 3:1, tennis 4:1. Total bowlers increased 10% (1980-93) but **league bowling plummeted 40%**. League bowlers consume **3x as much beer and pizza** as solo bowlers — the money is in social interaction
- Sports spectatorship nearly doubled since 1960s; playing instruments fell from 30% → 20% of population
- **Pattern**: In sports, culture, and politics alike — we watch more and do less

### Summary

- Evidence across a very wide range shows **striking diminution of regular contacts with friends and neighbors** over several decades
- We spend less time in conversation over meals, exchange visits less often, engage less in leisure activities that encourage casual social interaction
- It is not merely civic activities but also **informal connecting** that has declined

## Chapter 9: Against the Tide? Small Groups, Social Movements, and the Net

### Small Groups: A Quiet Revolution?

- **40% of Americans** claim involvement in a regular small group providing support/caring (Robert Wuthnow)
- ~Half are church-related (Sunday school, Bible study); ~5% in self-help groups (AA and 130+ analogues); comparable numbers in book clubs
- Reading circles have deep American roots — from post-Civil War self-improvement to the suffrage movement — but numbers today are **no higher than the 1960s-70s**
- Self-help participation rate: ~2-3% of adults — all self-help participants combined are outnumbered 2-to-1 by dropouts from league bowling alone
- Wuthnow's verdict: Small groups create real care and connection but "the social contract binding members together asserts only the weakest of obligations. Come if you have time. Talk if you feel like it. Never criticize. Leave quietly if you become dissatisfied."

### Social Movements: Ground War vs. Air War

- The 1960s produced social movements (civil rights, women's, environmental, gay rights) that permanently expanded the repertoire of civic engagement — boycotts, protest marches, sit-ins became standard across the political spectrum
- Social movements both **consume and produce** social capital: preexisting networks recruit participants, and collective action forges lifelong bonds (Freedom Summer veterans carried that vision into every subsequent movement)
- But national "social movement organizations" increasingly became **professionalized advocacy operations** — paid staffs, direct-mail fundraising, Washington offices
- **Environmental movement**: Membership in national orgs exploded from 125,000 (1960) to 6.5 million (1990). The crucial innovation was **direct mail**, not deeper civic consciousness — Greenpeace mailed 48 million letters annually, then **lost 85% of members** when mailings slowed
- Direct-mail recruits "drop out more readily, participate in fewer activities, and feel less attachment" — they are **consumers of a cause**, not members of a community
- Sierra Club: only 13% of members ever attended a single meeting; every week, 20x more Texans gather at Rotary clubs than at Audubon Society chapters

### Pro-Life vs. Pro-Choice: The Social Capital Difference

- **Pro-life**: 13 million members, 7,000 local chapters, built on church-based grassroots networks — a "ground war" adapted to social-capital-rich environments
- **Pro-choice**: NARAL tripled to 500,000 then plunged to 190,000 in two years; only 3-5% ever did more than write a check — "air war" adapted to social-capital-poor environments
- The existence of a well-developed national organization using air war techniques is **a sign not of grassroots engagement, but of its absence**

### Religious Conservatives: The Real Countertrend

- Religious conservatives created **the largest, best-organized grassroots social movement** of the last quarter century
- Post-WWII evangelicalism moved from rural fringe to middle-class suburbs; National Association of Evangelicals tripled membership 1940s-1970s
- Before 1974, evangelicals were *less* politically active than average; after 1974, *more* — including voting, joining political groups, writing officials
- Evangelical activists attend church more than once a week (60-70% vs. <5% of other Americans); are 3-5x more active in virtually all civic and political activities
- The most important predictor of mobilization is **social engagement in religious community**, not ideology or demographics

### Telecommunications and the Internet

- **Telephone** (cautionary tale): reinforced existing local ties rather than transforming them — 40-50% of calls within a 2-mile radius; "one does not meet new friends on the telephone" — the phone facilitated schmoozing but didn't create new social capital
- **Internet**: Cannot have *caused* civic decline (decline was underway for 25 years before 10% of adults were online). Early users were no more or less civically engaged than nonusers
- **Potential benefits**: Low-cost, egalitarian communication; asynchronous contact; mobilization (Nobel Prize–winning landmine campaign organized from rural Vermont via Internet)
- **Four serious challenges:**
  1. **Digital divide**: Access concentrated among educated, affluent, white males — may reinforce rather than bridge existing inequalities
  2. **Loss of nonverbal cues**: Face-to-face interaction transmits volumes of nonverbal information essential for trust; computer-mediated communication leads to more flaming, cheating, and depersonalization
  3. **Cyberbalkanization**: Interest-based communities replace place-based heterogeneity with homogeneity — knowing and caring more about less
  4. **Displacement**: May substitute for face-to-face contact rather than complement it

> "Social capital may turn out to be a prerequisite for, rather than a consequence of, effective computer-mediated communication."

- History of the telephone suggests the Internet will complement, not replace, face-to-face communities — scholars who see each other often also email more

## Chapter 10: Introduction to Part III — Why?

- First two-thirds of 20th century: rising civic engagement across all domains. Then a mysterious simultaneous reversal — **thin, surf-by interactions replacing dense, multistranded bonds**
- Decline is an **equal-opportunity affliction**: down among men and women, rich and poor, black and white, cities and small towns, all regions, married and single. No demographic "hot spot" — the *levels* differ by group but the *trends* are virtually identical
- **Education paradox**: Education is the strongest predictor of civic engagement, and educational levels rose massively (college degrees tripled 1960-1998). Yet civic engagement declined even faster among the educated — whatever the cause, it overwhelmed education's powerful positive effect
- Like Agatha Christie's *Murder on the Orient Express*: more than one perpetrator. The task is to sort ringleaders from accomplices

## Chapter 11: Pressures of Time and Money

### Time Pressure: A Red Herring

- Proportion who "always feel rushed" jumped by more than half since the mid-1960s — but **aggregate work hours have been roughly constant since WWII** (McGrattan & Rogerson)
- Time diary studies show a 6.2 hours/week **gain** in free time 1965-1995 (Robinson & Godbey) due to less housework and earlier retirement
- Paradoxically, **busy people are MORE civically engaged**: employed people more active than those outside the labor force; longer hours correlate with more (not less) community involvement. They sacrifice TV before civic participation
- Sidney Verba study: free time has "little or no effect" on whether someone becomes civically active

### Financial Anxiety: Supporting Actor, Not Lead

- Financial anxiety powerfully depresses civic engagement — jobless become passive and withdrawn; the financially worried third attend only two-thirds as many club meetings. Only leisure activity *positively* correlated with financial anxiety: watching TV
- But: civic decline began before 1970s economic troubles and continued through 1980s/1990s booms; declines are virtually as great among the affluent as among the poor
- Even among the most fortunate Americans, entertaining fell from 17x/year (1975) to 10x/year (1999)
- **At most, financial anxiety accounts for 5-10% of total decline**

### Women's Employment: A Modest Role

- Fraction of women working outside home doubled (1950s to 1990s); virtually all the increase was driven by **financial necessity, not personal fulfillment**
- Full-time work reduces club attendance, church attendance, home entertaining, visiting friends, and volunteering — regardless of motivation
- **Part-time work by choice** appears to be the "golden mean" for civic engagement — highest levels of community involvement
- But: civic disengagement is **almost equally great among affluent housewives and bachelors** — groups unaffected by two-career pressures
- Best estimate: women's employment + financial distress together account for **less than one-tenth** of total decline

## Chapter 7: Altruism, Volunteering, and Philanthropy

### Social Networks Drive Giving

- **"Someone asked me"** is the most common reason people volunteer; **"Nobody asked"** is why blood donors stop giving
- Fund-raising = friend-raising: the more involved in social networks, the more likely to be recruited
- Youth who volunteered are half again as likely to donate as adults and twice as likely to volunteer later — Tocqueville's **"habits of the heart"**

### Philanthropy: A Four-Decade Decline

- Per capita giving nearly doubled 1960-1995 in absolute terms, but **as a share of income, generosity has steadily declined since 1961**, entirely erasing postwar gains
- Total giving fell from 2.26% of national income (1964) to 1.61% (1998) — a 29% relative fall
- 1960: gave ~$1 per $2 spent on recreation; 1997: gave <$.50 per $2
- During the Great Depression: income fell 3% but charity *rose* 25%+ — **generosity tracks social capital, not financial capital**
- If giving matched 1960 levels: +$4B/year for United Way, +$20B/year for congregations, +$50B/year total
- Protestant giving per member fell sharply; Catholic giving as share of income fell **59%** (1960-1989); United Way contributions now less than half the 1960 level
- Self-reported giving: nearly half contributed in previous month (1980s) → barely one-third (mid-1990s)

## Chapter 8: Reciprocity, Honesty, and Trust

### Generalized Reciprocity: The Lubricant of Social Life

- **Generalized reciprocity**: Short-term altruism + long-term self-interest — I'll help now, confident someone will reciprocate down the road
- Tocqueville's **"self-interest rightly understood"**: not impossibly idealistic selflessness, but enlightened mutual aid
- Trusting communities have measurable economic advantages — same reason money is more efficient than barter
- Dense social networks foster honesty through reputation — why the diamond trade concentrates in close-knit enclaves
- **"Generalized reciprocity is a community asset, but generalized gullibility is not"** — trustworthiness, not simply trust, is the key ingredient

### Thick Trust vs. Thin Trust

- **Thick trust**: In people you know personally (Max at the corner store)
- **Thin trust**: In "the generalized other" — even more useful because it extends the radius of trust beyond people you can know personally
- As the social fabric becomes threadbare, its power to undergird thin trust is enfeebled

### Four Decades of Dwindling Trust

- Standard survey: "Most people can be trusted" — adults: **~55% (1960) → ~35% (late 1990s)**; high school students: **~55% (1975) → ~25% (mid-1990s)**
- 1952: Americans split 50-50 on whether society was morally upright; 1998: by 3-to-1, Americans believe society is less honest
- Trust rose from mid-1940s to mid-1960s (peaking 1964), then reversed into long decline
- Decline is **generational**: the generation with a trust quotient of ~80% is being replaced by one with barely ~40%, even though each individual cohort's trust level barely changed

### The Rise of Lawyers as Artificial Trust

- America had **fewer lawyers per capita in 1970 than in 1900** — two world wars, the Depression, the New Deal, none raised the lawyering ratio
- After 1970: lawyers per capita **more than doubled** in 25 years; by 1995, 34% more lawyers than doctors (3% fewer in 1970)
- Largest driver: **"preventive lawyering"** — informal understandings no longer seemed adequate; spouses, neighbors, business partners all began demanding to "get it in writing"

> "Like the provider of artificial hormones that supplement the diminished supply... the lawyer contrives enforceability to supplement the failing supply of reciprocity, moral obligation, and fellow-feeling." — Marc Galanter

- This explosion in formal legal recourse may be **one of the most revealing indicators** of the fraying social fabric — we rely on law to do what informal networks reinforced by generalized reciprocity once accomplished

## Chapter 12: Mobility and Sprawl

### Residential Mobility: Exonerated

- Americans are nomadic (~1 in 5 move each year), but residential mobility has **not increased** — slightly *declined* from 20% (1950s) to 16% (1990s)
- Homeownership rose to all-time record 67% (1999); average adult lived in same locality ~22 years, unchanged over three decades
- **Verdict: This theory is simply wrong** — mobility can't explain civic decline because it hasn't increased

### Suburbanization: The Real Story

- **Where** we live changed dramatically: small towns/rural fell from 44% (1950) → 20% (1996); suburbs grew from 23% → 49%
- Metro area residents (both city and suburb) show **10-20% fewer** group memberships, club meetings, church attendance; **30-40% less likely** to serve as officers or attend public meetings
- This is **not self-selection** — comparing identical demographics, metropolitans are less engaged because of *where they are*, not *who they are*

### From Civic Suburbs to Privatized Enclaves

- Initial post-WWII suburbs were **hyper-civic**: Park Forest ("hotbed of participation" per William Whyte), Levittown ("hyperactive joiners" per Herbert Gans)
- Later suburbs fragmented into **lifestyle enclaves** — collectively heterogeneous, individually homogeneous; gated communities doubled from 15% to 30% of projects in Orange County within 5 years
- **Eric Oliver** found greater social homogeneity = lower political involvement — suburbanization reduces local conflicts that engage citizenry

> "Suburbia is a collective effort to lead a private life." — Lewis Mumford

- Malls replaced Main Street as public space but are "carefully designed for one primary, private purpose — to direct consumers to buy"
- Lives increasingly traced in **"suburban triangles"** — home to work to shop to home

### The Car and the Commute

- Cars per household nearly doubled (1969-1995); by 1990, America had **more cars than drivers**
- Average trip to work increased 26%, shopping trips 29%; vehicle occupancy fell from 1.9 → 1.6 (passenger commuting down 50%)
- Americans average **72 minutes every day** behind the wheel; 80-90% drive to work alone (up from 64% in 1980)
- Commuting across county lines **tripled** (1960-1990); congestion-related delay rose from 16 → 45 hours/year per driver

> **Each additional ten minutes in daily commuting time cuts involvement in community affairs by 10 percent** — fewer meetings, fewer petitions, less volunteering, less church attendance

- Commuting's civic penalty extends **even to non-commuters** in high-commute communities — a synergistic effect with negative externalities
- Sprawl disrupts community "boundedness": work-based ties now compete with place-based ties rather than reinforcing them

### Sprawl's Overall Impact

- Three reasons sprawl hurts: **(1)** takes time, **(2)** social segregation reduces bridging capital, **(3)** disrupts community boundedness (most powerful)
- The "sprawl civic penalty" (~20% on most measures) probably accounts for **less than one-tenth** of total disengagement — visible but not sufficient alone

## Chapter 13: Technology and Mass Media

### The Transformation of Leisure

- Electronic technology enabled two fundamental shifts: **(1)** individualization of entertainment (from communal music halls to private Walkman/cable), **(2)** privatization of consumption (from baseball parks to living rooms)

> "It is a medium of entertainment which permits millions of people to listen to the same joke at the same time, and yet remain lonesome." — T.S. Eliot

### Newspapers: The Civic Medium in Decline

- Newspaper readers are **machers and schmoozers** — they join more, vote more, volunteer more, visit friends more, trust more
- Circulation plunged **57%** per household (1948-1998) despite education rising 50%
- Decline is **generational**: 3 in 4 born before 1929 read daily papers vs. just 1 in 4 Gen X'ers — each generation replaces readers with nonreaders
- TV news viewers are *more* civic than average but less so than newspaper readers — TV and newspapers are complements, not substitutes

### Television: The 800-Pound Gorilla

- TV reached 90% of homes in just **7 years** (fastest technology diffusion ever recorded)
- Average American watches ~**4 hours/day** — roughly **40% of free time** (highest viewership in the world)
- Between 1965-1995: Americans gained 6 hours/week leisure time and **spent almost all 6 hours watching TV**
- Multiple sets proliferated: 3/4 of homes had more than one; **77% of sixth-graders** had TV in bedroom by 1999
- Viewing became increasingly **solitary and habitual**: by mid-1990s, habitual viewers (turn on first, then look) outnumbered selective viewers — the reverse of late 1970s

### TV as Primary Entertainment: The Single Most Consistent Predictor

- **"Television is my primary form of entertainment"** — agreement with this statement is the **single most consistent predictor of civic disengagement** among all factors tested (including education, generation, income, commuting, etc.)
- TV minimalists volunteer **9x/year** vs. maximalists **4x**; attend **9 club meetings** vs. **5**; church **27x** vs. **19x**
- Each additional hour of TV/day = roughly **10% reduction** in most forms of civic activism
- TV particularly **toxic for collective activities** (meetings cut by 40%) vs. individual ones (letters cut 10-15%)

### Three Mechanisms of TV's Impact

**1. Time displacement**: TV is the *only* leisure activity that inhibits other leisure activities (violating the usual "the more, the more" pattern); one hour less daily TV = civic equivalent of **5-6 more years of education**

**2. Psychological effects**: TV induces passivity, drowsiness, low alertness; heavy viewers experience a kind of addiction — demand major bribes to give up TV yet report it less satisfying than other activities or even work. TV dependence correlates with physical **malaise** (headaches, insomnia, indigestion) as strongly as financial anxiety

**3. Pseudopersonal connection**: TV creates false sense of companionship — we *feel* intimate, informed, and engaged without *being* engaged; like "parallel play" in children (side by side but not interacting)

### The Canadian Natural Experiment

- **Notel** (no TV) vs. Unitel/Multitel (TV): when TV was introduced to Notel, community participation **significantly declined** with no change in control towns — clear experimental evidence that TV causes disengagement

> "We can almost always tell if a change will bring good or bad tidings. Certain things we definitely do not want, like the television and the radio. They would destroy our visiting practices." — Amish community member

### Content Matters — But Entertainment Dominates

- News/educational TV viewers are *more* civic; action dramas, soap operas, reality TV viewers are *less* civic
- But program types most associated with civic isolation constitute a **massive and growing share** of programming
- Channel proliferation (19 channels in 1985 → 49 in 1997) destroyed shared "water cooler" culture: top-rated show drew 2/3 of viewers in 1950s, barely 1/3 by mid-1990s
- TV encouraged **materialist values**: heavy-viewing adolescents want high-status, easy, well-paid jobs

**At minimum: television and its electronic cousins are willing accomplices in the civic mystery, and more likely than not, they are ringleaders.**

## Chapter 14: From Generation to Generation

### The Generational Key

- Age is **second only to education** as predictor of civic engagement; but the crucial distinction is between **life cycle effects** (temporary, society unchanged) and **generational effects** (permanent, society transformed)
- Expected life cycle upswings in civic engagement as baby boomers matured **never materialized** — no PTA boom, no church surge, no civic profusion: "the civic dog that didn't bark"

### The Long Civic Generation (Born 1910-1940)

- Core cohort born ~1925-1930: attended grade school during the Depression, experienced WWII in high school or on the battlefield, saw first TV in their late twenties
- Since national polling began, **exceptionally civic** — voting, joining, reading, trusting, giving more than any subsequent generation, despite having **only one-quarter** with education beyond high school
- At their peak (1960), comprised two-thirds of the electorate; by 1992, cut in half as boomers and X'ers replaced them
- **Generational replacement is "the single most important explanation for the collapse of civic engagement over the last several decades"**

> "Like Minerva's owl that flies at dusk, we come to appreciate how important the long civic generation has been to American community life just as its members are retiring."

### Baby Boomers (1946-1964)

- **First generation exposed to TV throughout their lives** — by age 16, average boomer watched 12,000-15,000 hours of TV
- Indelibly marked by 1960s upheavals; distinguished more by what they **reject** (institutions, mainstream politics) than what they embrace
- Slow to marry, quick to divorce; 96% raised religious but 58% abandoned faith; less comfortable in bureaucracies
- **"Free agents"**: tolerant, cynical, laid-back, individualistic — high social cost

### Generation X (1965-1980)

- Erosion of social capital began **before any X'er was born** — they continued a trend, didn't start it
- Extremely individualistic; never connected to politics; shaped by uncertainty (slow growth, divorce explosion, no collective triumphs)
- "Having lots of money" rated important rose from 46% (1976) to 70% (1990) among high school seniors
- Trust collapsed: "most people can be trusted" — from ~61% (boomers of 1976) to **23%** (late X'ers of 1995)

### Depression, Suicide, and Social Isolation

- Depression struck **earlier and more pervasively** in each successive generation born after 1940; depression rate increased roughly **tenfold** over two generations
- Teen suicide (ages 15-19) **quadrupled** 1950-1995, while suicide among older groups *declined* — crossing trajectories as the long civic generation aged
- Average teenager in 1990s spent **3.5 hours alone daily**, with fewer and weaker friendships than 1950s teens
- Martin Seligman: depression epidemic **spared the close-knit Amish** — "rampant individualism" coupled with weakened institutional commitments is the explanation

### World War II: The Forge of Civic America

- WWII directly touched nearly everyone: **16 million served** (80% of men born in 1920s); 12 million home-front volunteers; Red Cross grew from 1.1M to **7.5 million**; nearly 20 million Victory Gardens yielded 40% of vegetables grown
- War bonds, scrap drives, blackout patrols, block captains — massive voluntary mobilization created civic habits that lasted decades
- WWII was **probably the most leveling event in American economic history**: top 1% wealth share fell 31% → 23%; top 5% income share 28% → 19%
- Every major association sharply expanded "market share" between mid-1940s and mid-1960s
- Kennedy's "Ask not what your country can do for you" rang true to former Gary eighth-graders who had already been civic since childhood

### Summary: Half the Decline Is Generational

- About **half of overall decline** in social capital traceable to generational change
- TV and generational effects are likely **opposite sides of the same coin** — the long civic generation was the last to grow up without TV
- Materialism soared: "lot of money" as part of "the good life" rose from 38% (1975) to **63%** (1996); communal values declined
- Youth volunteerism uptick in 1990s may signal the trend is **at last bottoming out**

## Chapter 15: What Killed Civic Engagement? Summing Up

### Suspect #1: Family Structure — Mostly Exonerated

- Married-with-kids households dropped from 40% (1970) to 26% (1997), but marriage/parenthood mainly boost **church and youth** activities only
- Married people actually attend **fewer** club meetings than singles; divorce is unrelated to most civic involvement
- Holding traditional family constant: more religious/PTA participation, but no effect on secular organizations, political activities, or Kiwanis-to-NAACP decline; would **decrease** time with friends even more

### Suspect #2: Race and Civil Rights — Not Guilty

- African Americans dropped out of organizations **at least as rapidly** as whites; pace of white disengagement was **uncorrelated** with racial attitudes
- Generational patterns incompatible: disengagement minimal among those who grew up in the *more* segregated era
- **Reversing civil rights gains would do nothing to reverse social capital losses**

### Suspect #3: Big Government — No Evidence

- Social capital **highest** in big-spending Scandinavian welfare states
- Among U.S. states: civic engagement **uncorrelated** with welfare spending or government size
- Federal domestic spending essentially unchanged (2.2% of GNP in late 1940s and late 1990s) — a constant can't explain the decline

### Suspect #4: Capitalism and the Market — Too Generic, But...

- Market capitalism has existed for centuries during which social capital went through great swings — **a constant can't explain a variable**
- However, **"corporate delocalization"** (Wal-Mart replaces corner store, Bank of America takes over First National) stripped communities of civic leadership — social cohesion of Atlanta's elite peaked in 1960s then declined, same trajectory as other measures

### The Real Culprits (Approximate Shares)

- **Generational change** (especially TV's role in shaping post-WWII cohorts): ~**50%** of the decline
- **Television and electronic entertainment**: ~**25%** (overlaps with generational)
- **Suburban sprawl and commuting**: ~**10%**
- **Time and money pressures** (two-career families): ~**10%**
- Other factors (corporate delocalization, family change): remainder

## Chapter 16: Introduction — So What?

### Social Capital Makes Us Healthy, Wealthy, and Wise

- Market for civic nostalgia booming (Tom Brokaw's WWII book, airport renamings) — Americans sense at a visceral level that social bonds are disintegrating
- Social capital works through **five mechanisms**: (1) resolving collective action problems, (2) reducing transaction costs through trust, (3) widening awareness of interconnected fates, (4) serving as information conduits, (5) improving health through psychological/biological processes

> "Virtually every commercial transaction has within itself an element of trust.... much of the economic backwardness in the world can be explained by a lack of mutual confidence." — Kenneth Arrow (Nobel laureate)

### The Social Capital Index Across 50 States

- Fourteen indicators combined into single index; differences substantial (social trust: 17% in Mississippi vs. 67% in North Dakota)
- **Geographic pattern**: High social capital along Canadian border (headwaters of Mississippi/Missouri); low in former Confederacy, radiating outward from Mississippi Delta
- Pattern has deep historical roots — Tocqueville noted the same north-south gradient in the 1830s
- Strongest predictor of state social capital: fraction of population of **Scandinavian stock**
- Most striking: spatial correlation between low social capital today and **slavery** in the 19th century — "a social system designed to destroy social capital"
- **Inequality and social solidarity are deeply incompatible**
- Historical continuity of these patterns makes causation more plausible — if social capital were merely an effect of current conditions, it would be more mutable

### The 14 Indicators and State-Level Variation

- Combined index from 14 measures across five domains: community organizational life (committee service, officer roles, civic orgs per capita, club meetings, group memberships), public affairs engagement (voter turnout, public meeting attendance), volunteerism (nonprofits per capita, community projects, volunteer work), informal sociability (visiting friends, home entertaining), and social trust ("most people can be trusted," "most people are honest")
- All 14 intercorrelated tightly enough to form a single underlying dimension
- State ratios roughly 3:1 between highest and lowest: memberships per capita 1.3 (Louisiana) to 3.3 (North Dakota); club meetings 4/year (Nevada) to 11 (Dakotas); public meeting attendance 10% (Georgia, New York) to 32% (New Hampshire)
- Differences enabled natural experiments: compare quality of life across states with different social capital levels while controlling for other factors

## Chapter 18: Safe and Productive Neighborhoods

### Criminology and Social Disorganization Theory

- Since the 1920s, criminologists developed "ecological" theories linking crime to neighborhood characteristics: high population turnover, anonymous neighbors, ethnic fragmentation, sparse local organizations, disadvantaged youth in subcultures cut off from the adult world
- **Robert J. Sampson**: Even controlling for poverty, communities with (a) anonymity and sparse acquaintanceship networks, (b) unsupervised teenage peer groups, and (c) weak organizational base face increased crime risk
- Living in a high-mobility neighborhood **doubles** risk of victimization vs. a stable one

### Jane Jacobs and Social Capital in Cities

- **Jane Jacobs** (1961): Cities configured to maximize informal contact = safer streets, better child care, happier residents
- Regular contact with local grocer, families on stoops, priest walking blocks, street fairs, parks → "a web of public respect and trust, and a resource in time of personal and neighborhood need"
- Higher levels of social capital, all else equal, translate into lower crime

> "The sum of such casual, public contact at a local level ... is a feeling for the public identity of people, a web of public respect and trust, and a resource in time of personal and neighborhood need."

### State-Level Evidence: Homicide and Social Capital

- States with more social capital have dramatically fewer murders — the inverse relationship is "astonishingly strong"
- Social capital is **more important** than education levels, single-parent household rates, and income inequality in predicting murders (1980-95)
- **The Southern violence puzzle**: Once differences in social capital are accounted for, the infamous regional difference in murder rates **vanishes entirely** — the South is no more violent than expected given its social-capital deficit
- **"Lethal violence is endemic wherever social capital is deficient"**
- Pugnacity ("I'd do better than average in a fist fight"): 38% of Americans agree; nearly half in Louisiana/West Virginia/New Mexico vs. less than a third in Dakota/Maine/Iowa/Minnesota — strongly correlated with low social capital

### Neighborhood Effects on Crime and Delinquency

- Dozens of studies find "neighborhood effects" — a person's behavior depends not only on own characteristics but on neighbors' behavior too
- **Boston**: Kids whose neighborhood peers use drugs, commit crimes, befriend gang members are more likely to do so regardless of initial proclivities
- **Chicago**: Young black men in neighborhoods with white-collar professionals 3x more likely to graduate high school than comparable men in less educated neighborhoods
- **"My fate depends on not only whether I study, stay off drugs, go to church, but also on whether my neighbors do these things"**

### How Social Capital Works in Neighborhoods

- **Positive side**: Social capital provides enforcement of norms for youth, access to mentors and role models, job contacts, emotional/financial support, political leverage for community institutions
- **Negative side**: Where norms and associations are absent, kids are left to own devices — most likely to act on shortsighted or self-destructive impulses
- **Northern California finding**: Parents knowing child's friends' parents was strong indicator of good outcomes — *but only in areas where norms were already positive*. In troubled areas, social integration of parents actually **exacerbated** problems — **integration into a community of bad actors may not produce good results**

### Gangs as Misguided Social Capital

- In settings where mainstream organizations are absent, youth create their own social capital in form of gangs
- Gang life involves **reciprocal obligations** and norms similar to bowling teams — lending money, buying rounds, serious mutual obligations spanning years
- External effects typically harmful to bystanders — gangs serve as social institutions where mainstream ones are debilitated
- **Washington, D.C.**: Women activists in public housing used drug gangs as benefactors — gangs funded children's programs, activists ensured organizing proceeded without street interference

### Carol Stack's *All Our Kin*: Social Capital Among the Poor

- Classic study of black families in inner-city Midwest: dense exchange networks of goods, resources, child care among kin and non-kin
- High trust required — gifts rarely repaid immediately; networks enforced through monitoring and sanctions against shirkers
- Recent studies suggest these networks are not as dense or effective as Stack found in the late 1960s — inner cities have less social capital than they once did

### The Vicious Circle and Community Policing

- Social capital often lacking where needed most, creating a **vicious circle**: low trust → higher crime → even lower trust
- "Neighborhood crime watch" programs most likely to succeed in middle-class neighborhoods that already have trust and networks
- **Community policing** (1980s-90s) = "applied social capitalism" — fighting crime by building partnerships between law enforcement and community
- **Chicago CAPS program**: Created uniform opportunities for participation across community segments
- **Boston's 10-Point Coalition**: Partnership brokered by local ministers — only ministers had the connections and trust (social capital) to make it work

### Why Social Capital Matters More for the Poor

- **Poor people lack economic and human capital**, making social capital disproportionately important to their welfare
- Erosion affected Grosse Point and inner-city Detroit equally in degree, but **impact** far greater in inner city, which lacks the cushion of other capital forms
- Late-1990s school shootings in suburban/rural communities: reminder that breakdown of community in privileged settings means affluence and education alone are insufficient to prevent collective tragedy

## Chapter 19: Economic Prosperity

### Social Connections and Employment

- **Mark Granovetter** (1970s): Casual acquaintances ("weak ties") are more valuable for job searches than close friends/family — distant acquaintances link to unexpected opportunities
- "Weak ties" have especially strong impact on fortunes of people at margins of mainstream economic institutions
- Roughly **one-half** of people get jobs through friend or relative

### Ethnic Networks as Economic Engines

- Ethnic immigrant communities use social capital intensively: employers rely on employees to recruit/train new workers; immigrants access financing through family gifts and **rotating credit associations**
- **Korean business owners**: ~70% used debt financing; 41% got money from family, 24% from friends (vs. 37% from financial institutions)
- **Church attendance** is one of the strongest predictors of whether inner-city black youth will become gainfully employed — it's the **social networking aspect of churchgoing**, not religious beliefs, behind economic success

### Social Capital Beyond Employment

- **Ronald Burt**: Social ties embodied in a business executive's Rolodex are at least as important as educational qualifications and experience
- **Brian Uzzi** (Chicago banking): Firms that embed commercial transactions with lenders in social attachments receive **lower interest rates** on loans
- **DiMaggio and Louch**: People who transact with friends and relatives report greater satisfaction than those who transact with strangers

### Where Social Capital Is Absent

- **Chicago (Wilson)**: Blacks in extreme poverty substantially less likely to have a current partner or best friend; friends less likely to have completed high school or held steady work
- **Red Hook, Brooklyn**: Deterioration of neighborhood associations inhibited growth of social networks just as employers were making most hires through word of mouth
- **Atlanta study**: Each employed person in one's social network increases annual income by **$1,400**
- Networks more lucrative for whites than minority groups; among disadvantaged, **"bridging" social capital may be the most lucrative form**

### Regional Economic Success Stories

**Tupelo, Mississippi**: In 1940, one of the poorest counties in the poorest state. Through **George McLean's** exceptional leadership, united business and civic leaders around the idea that economic development required community development. Elite Chamber of Commerce disbanded; inclusive Community Development Foundation opened to everyone. Over fifty years: 1,000 industrial jobs added annually, hundreds of millions in investment, arguably the best schools in Mississippi. **Residents invested in social capital and reaped tangible economic returns.**

**Silicon Valley vs. Route 128**: Silicon Valley's success due to **horizontal networks** of informal and formal cooperation among nominally competing companies. Leaders shared information, problem-solving techniques, and beers after work. The **Homebrew Computer Club** (hobbyists' group) produced leaders of 20+ companies. Route 128 (Boston) maintained traditional norms of corporate hierarchy, secrecy, self-sufficiency — and performed far worse economically.

> "The contrasting experience of Silicon Valley and Route 128 suggests that industrial systems built on regional networks are more flexible and technologically dynamic than those in which experimentation and learning are confined to individual firms."

### Potential Downsides

- Tight ethnic networks can restrict growth — pressures of solidarity can drag down individuals who succeed "too much"
- Exploitative networks: **Amway** and similar businesses rely on agents to recruit friends/neighbors, potentially violating norms of reciprocity
- Most researchers agree social capital helps individuals prosper; debate is how big a role it plays relative to human or financial capital

## Chapter 20: Health and Happiness

### Social Capital and Health: The Evidence

- Importance of social connectedness for health is best established of all social capital's effects, traceable to **Emile Durkheim's** *Suicide* (19th century)
- Dozens of studies from Alameda (California) to Tecumseh (Michigan): **social connectedness is one of the most powerful determinants of well-being**
- More integrated people are less likely to experience colds, heart attacks, strokes, cancer, depression, and premature death of all sorts

### The Magnitude of the Effect

- **James House** and colleagues: Positive health contributions of social integration **rival in strength the detrimental contributions of** smoking, obesity, elevated blood pressure, and physical inactivity
- Evidence for health consequences of social connectedness is as strong today as was evidence for smoking's health consequences at the time of the first surgeon general's report
- Over last twenty years, more than a dozen large studies: **people who are socially disconnected are between two and five times more likely to die from all causes**

### Five Mechanisms

1. **Tangible assistance**: Money, convalescent care, transportation — reduces psychic and physical stress
2. **Safety net**: If you go to church regularly and slip in the bathtub and miss a Sunday, someone notices
3. **Reinforcing healthy norms**: Socially isolated people more likely to smoke, drink, overeat
4. **Political organization**: Socially cohesive communities organize for first-rate medical services
5. **Physiological triggering**: Social capital may stimulate immune systems and buffer stress

### Biochemical Evidence

- Animals in isolation develop more extensive atherosclerosis than less isolated animals
- Among both animals and humans, loneliness **decreases immune response and increases blood pressure**
- **Lisa Berkman**: Social isolation is "a chronically stressful condition to which the organism responds by aging faster"

### The Roseto Study

- Small Italian American community studied for nearly 40 years beginning in the 1950s
- Rosetans' age-adjusted heart attack rate was **less than half** that of neighbors; not a single resident under 47 died of a heart attack over a seven-year period
- Usual explanations (diet, exercise, genetics) didn't hold — Rosetans actually had *more* risk factors
- The key: tight-knit community with mutual aid society, churches, sports clubs, front-porch socializing
- By the 1980s, as young people rejected tight-knit folkways, heart attack rates **rose above** those of neighbors

### Harvard School of Public Health Study

- Survey of nearly 170,000 individuals across all fifty states
- **Moving from a state with high social capital to one with low social capital** increased chances of poor health by roughly **40-70%**
- **Moving to a high-social-capital state would do almost as much good as quitting smoking**

> "As a rough rule of thumb, if you belong to no groups but decide to join one, you cut your risk of dying over the next year in half."

### Depression and Social Connectedness

- In any given year, 10% of Americans suffer from major depression — fourth largest disease burden
- Low levels of social support directly predict depression; high levels speed recovery
- Face-to-face ties seem more therapeutic than geographically distant ties
- Coincidence of declining social connection and rising depression/suicide has deep generational roots — the most disconnected generations suffer most from "Agent Blue"

### Life Satisfaction and Happiness

- **Single most common finding from half a century's research**: Happiness is best predicted by breadth and depth of one's social connections
- **Marriage = happiness equivalent of quadrupling annual income** (moving from 15th to 85th percentile)
- **Regular club attendance, volunteering, entertaining, or church attendance = happiness equivalent of getting a college degree or more than doubling income**
- **Diminishing returns**: Biggest gains between "never" and "once a month"; little gain after ~fortnightly encounters. Church attendance is the exception — more is better at least through weekly

### Life Satisfaction Decline

- Since the 1960s, life satisfaction has declined steadily; half due to financial worries, half due to declining social capital
- Slump greatest among young and middle-aged adults; people over 55 are actually **happier** than their age cohort a generation ago
- **Martin Seligman**: Modern society encourages belief in personal control/autonomy over commitment to duty and community — heightens expectations, leaves people unprepared for inevitable failures, with weakened social capital safety nets

> "In our personal lives as well as in our collective life, we are paying a significant price for a quarter century's disengagement from one another."

## Chapter 21: Democracy

### Classical Democratic Theory

- **Oscar Wilde**: "The trouble with socialism is that it would take too many evenings." How many evenings does liberal democracy take?
- **Jefferson** (1816): Proposed ward republics small enough for every citizen to attend meetings in person — "Making every citizen an acting member of the government"
- **Tocqueville**: Local civic activity serves as handmaiden of national democratic community — only through personal stake in small public affairs do people discover the close tie between private and general interest
- **John Stuart Mill**: Without shared participation, citizens think of collective interests only in competition; a neighbor becomes "only a rival"
- **John Dewey**: "Democracy must begin at home, and its home is the neighborly community"

### Associations as Schools for Democracy

- Civic associations serve as **"schools for democracy"** — members learn to run meetings, speak in public, write letters, organize projects, debate issues
- For working-class Americans, voluntary associations and churches offer the best opportunities to exercise civic skills — even better than the workplace
- Churches play an **egalitarian role**: One of few institutions where low-income, minority, disadvantaged citizens learn politically relevant skills; without them, class bias in politics would be much greater

### External Effects on Government

- **Voice amplification**: Without associations willing to speak up, individuals have limited ability to be heard unless rich or famous
- **East Germany example**: Democracy movement recruited through friendship networks; informal bonds more important than ideology or formal organizing
- Political information flows through social networks; association membership clarifies views and unites energies

### Critics of Pluralism: Three Concerns

1. **Interest groups distort decision-making**: From Theodore Lowi's *End of Liberalism* to Jonathan Rauch's *Demosclerosis* — specialized lobbies paralyze officials and stifle reforms; "greatest goodies for best-organized few"
2. **Associational bias favors the privileged**: "The flaw in the pluralist heaven is that the heavenly chorus sings with a strong upper-class accent" — **E.E. Schattschneider**
3. **Political polarization**: Self-described middle-of-the-roaders became **one-half as likely** to participate in public meetings and organizations (1970s to 1990s), while "very" liberal or conservative barely declined — **civic disengagement exacerbates the "faction" problem** as moderate voices fall silent

### Citizenship by Proxy?

- **Michael Schudson**: Large national groups (AARP, Audubon) may represent interests efficiently — citizens "hire" them as they hire mechanics
- **Theda Skocpol's counterargument**: In classic civic America, millions of ordinary people could interact with each other and exercise influence; now bypassed by professionally dominated advocacy groups with members "worthy of the name" who are rarely more than check-writers
- **Peter Skerry**: These organizations are pulled toward wishes of major patrons (foundations, government agencies), rely on media strategies emphasizing threats, giving us politics of posturing and confrontation rather than reasoned debate

### Italian Regional Government Study

- 1970: Italy established 20 identical regional governments in radically different social/economic contexts
- Some became dismal failures (inefficient, corrupt); others remarkably successful (innovative programs, satisfied constituents)
- **Best predictor of success**: Strong traditions of civic engagement — voter turnout, newspaper readership, choral societies, Lions Clubs, soccer clubs
- Civic traditions traced back nearly **a millennium** to 11th-century communal republics in Florence, Bologna, Genoa
- Core of civic heritage: guilds, religious fraternities, tower societies → cooperatives, mutual aid societies, neighborhood associations, choral societies

### How Social Capital Affects Government: Demand and Supply

- **Demand side**: Citizens in civic communities expect better government and (through their own efforts) get it — hazardous waste companies locate where they expect least organized opposition
- **Supply side**: Social capital lowers transaction costs and eases collective action — police close more cases, schools teach better, child welfare does better "family preservation" when community involvement exists
- **Elazar's political cultures**: "Moralistic" states (highest social capital) are most innovative, have merit systems, issue-oriented politics, and appear less corrupt

### Tax Compliance and Social Capital

- **Controlling for per capita income, inequality, race, urbanism, and education, social capital is the ONLY factor that successfully predicts tax compliance**
- Where citizens view others as basically honest, tax compliance is higher
- In a community rich in social capital, government is "we," not "they" — social capital reinforces government legitimacy

### The Tolerance Paradox

- America became far more tolerant (1950s-1990s) while less connected — does tolerance require disengagement?
- **Evidence says no**: Individuals more engaged with communities are *more* tolerant, not less — correlation is positive even controlling for education
- Most tolerant communities in America = places with greatest civic involvement; communities where residents "bowl alone" = least tolerant places
- **No empirical study confirms supposed link between community involvement and intolerance**
- Generational dividing line for tolerance ≠ dividing line for engagement — the 1940-45 cohort was both the most engaged and successfully passed tolerance (but not communitarian habits) to their children

## Chapter 22: Social Capital, Equality, and the Dark Side

### Social Capital and Equality: Complementary, Not Warring

- Empirical reality: community and equality are **mutually reinforcing, not mutually incompatible**
- 1950s-1960s: America more egalitarian in wealth/income distribution than in more than a century — same decades = high point of social connectedness
- Reversal around 1965-70: last third of 20th century brought growing inequality AND eroding social capital simultaneously
- **American states with highest social capital = states most characterized by economic equality**
- In high-social-capital states, people from different social classes are equally likely to attend meetings and lead organizations; in low-social-capital states, civic life is monopolized by the haves

### Three Causal Interpretations

1. **Social capital produces equality**: The main weapon of have-nots who lack other forms of capital — "Solidarity forever" is strategically sensible
2. **Equality fosters social capital**: Great disparities of wealth and power are inimical to widespread participation
3. **External forces cause both**: Like the leveling effects of massive (and victorious) war
- Evidence powerfully contradicts the view that community engagement must amplify inequality

### The Dark Side: Fraternity at War with Itself

- Fraternity is most easily created *in opposition to* something or someone else; most natural within socially homogeneous groups
- Race = the most important embodiment of ethical crosscurrents around social capital in America
- Parts of the US where social trust is lowest today = places where slavery and racialist policy were most entrenched historically
- Civil rights movement aimed at destroying certain exclusive, non-bridging forms of social capital — racially homogeneous schools, neighborhoods

### The Busing Dilemma

- **Proponents**: Only through racially integrated schools could America generate bridging social capital across racial divides
- **Opponents**: Neighborhood schools provided unique sites for building bonding social capital
- **"Both sides were probably right"** — a tragic conclusion
- **Eileen McDonagh's formulation**: "Is it better to have neighborhoods legally restricted on the basis of race, but with everyone having everyone else over for dinner, or unrestricted on the basis of race, but with very little social interaction between neighbors?"

### What Kind of Social Capital Do We Need?

- For small children: A little "familism" (bonding) goes a long way
- For improving public schools: Need community-level social capital
- For our biggest collective problems: Need precisely the sort of **bridging social capital that is toughest to create**

> **False optimism**: To console ourselves that collapse of community has brought us a more liberal, egalitarian America. **False pessimism**: To refrain from rebuilding community for fear it will lead to intolerance and injustice.

## Chapter 23: Lessons of History — The Gilded Age and the Progressive Era

### The Historical Parallel

- The Gilded Age (1870-1900) was **a time very like our own**: technological advance, unparalleled prosperity, but nostalgia for integrated connectedness; new communications promising new community, but thoughtful observers worried those forms would be "fool's gold"
- In 3-4 decades after the Civil War: Industrial Revolution, urbanization, massive immigration transformed American communities — millions left family and friends on the farm for Chicago, Pittsburgh, or left Polish shtetls and Italian villages for the Lower East Side
- Classic symptoms of social-capital deficit: crime waves, degradation in cities, inadequate education, widening gap between rich and poor, "saturnalia" of political corruption

### Technological and Economic Revolution

- Patents generated at nearly **20x** the rate of the previous 80 years; steel production mushroomed from 77,000 tons (1870) to 11.2 million (1900); factories nearly quadrupled
- Railroad network grew from 53,000 to 193,000 miles (1870-1900)
- First great merger wave (1897-1904): Standard Oil, GE, Du Pont, U.S. Steel — in proportion to total economy, not rivaled until megamergers of 1990s
- Per capita wealth increased ~60%; real GNP rose 133% — but gains not spread evenly: 1% owned >50% of national wealth, bottom 44% owned only 1.2%

### Urbanization and Immigration

- National population nearly doubled (40M to 76M, 1870-1900); city population tripled (10M to 30M)
- Chicago: barely a village in 1860, population of 2.2 million by 1910
- Nearly 12 million immigrants arrived 1870-1900 (more than in the previous 2.5 centuries); by 1890, immigrant adults outnumbered native adults in 18 of 20 largest cities
- Cacophony of strange tongues triggered national debate about "Americanization" — similar to today's "multiculturalism" and "English only" debates

### Urban Crisis and Political Corruption

- Cities were "esthetically repulsive, commercially spastic, culturally balkanized, morally depraved, medically lethal, socially oppressive, and politically explosive" — **Don Kirschner**
- Machine politics provided access for immigrants but also bred rampant corruption (Boss Tweed: $179,729.60 for three tables and forty chairs)
- **Lincoln Steffens**: "The misgovernment of the American people is misgovernment *by* the American people"
- Robber barons (Rockefeller, Morgan, Carnegie) transformed economic power into political power
- Racial segregation intensified: **Plessy v. Ferguson** (1896) endorsed Jim Crow; 1880s lynchings averaged one every other day; 1890-1908 virtually all southern states disenfranchised African Americans (electoral participation fell 62% average)

### The Technology-Community Debate

- **Optimists**: New communications would expand altruism and unify society — "the dawn of a spiritual awakening" (**William Allen White**)
- **John Dewey**: "The Great Society created by steam and electricity may be a society but it is no community"
- **Mary Parker Follett**: "Real solidarity will never be accomplished except by beginning somewhere the joining of one small group with another"
- Progressives worried professionalization would replace participation with spectatorship — **"In politics, religion, art and sport we are represented now by proxies where formerly we participated in person" (Robert Park)**

### Progressive Vision: Small-Town Values in Industrial Society

- Progressive thinkers recognized both the oppressive features ("small-town herd") and virtues (community rooted in interpersonal ties) of small-town life
- **Jane Addams**: Village gossip kept people informed about who needed help, enabled "the good lying next at hand"
- Impersonal market ties were replacing sturdier bonds of family, friendship, small-town solidarity
- **Walter Lippmann** (1914): "We have changed our environment more quickly than we know how to change ourselves"
- But Progressives were **too practical to seek return to a smaller, simpler age** — they understood we could not go back. The issue: not "modernity, yes or no?" but how to reform institutions

### The Associational Boom (1870-1920)

- A veritable **"boom" in association building**: Americans created and joined unprecedented numbers of voluntary associations — per capita density rose sharply through the second half of the 19th century
- **Half of ALL the largest mass membership organizations in two centuries of American history** were founded 1870-1920 — **Theda Skocpol**
- Of all 506 "societies and associations" listed in *Encarta 2000*, almost twice as many were founded 1890-1920 as 1960-1990
- **Hard to name a major mainline civic institution in American life today that was not invented in these few decades**: Red Cross, NAACP, Knights of Columbus, Hadassah, Boy Scouts, Rotary, PTA, Sierra Club, Audubon Society, American Bar Association, Farm Bureau, League of Women Voters, Teamsters

### Fraternal Organizations

- By 1897: "Every fifth, or possibly every eighth, man you meet is identified with some fraternal organization" (**W. S. Harwood**)
- By 1910: conservatively one-third of all adult males were members — **David Beito**
- Fraternalism represented reaction against individualism and anomie; provided material benefits (life/health insurance) AND social solidarity and ritual
- "They successfully created vast social and mutual aid networks among the poor"
- Comparable organizations for African Americans and women served the same functions

### Women's Associations

- 1873-74 spontaneous crusade reinvigorated the temperance movement → **WCTU** under **Frances Willard** adopted "Do Everything" policy (prison reform, kindergartens, labor reform)
- **General Federation of Women's Clubs** (1890): Campaigned for food inspection, housing codes, safer drinking water, workplace protection, services for poor/sick/children
- **National Association of Colored Women's Clubs** (1896): Barred by segregation from GFWC; supported nurseries, kindergartens, homes for unwed mothers
- Suffrage movement was merely the most visible culmination of feminist organizing at the turn of the century

### Immigrant and African American Associations

- Immigrants rationally strove to conserve social capital via **chain migration** and **benevolent societies** for mutual aid
- **1910**: Two-thirds of all Poles in America belonged to at least one of ~7,000 Polish associations; similar figures for Jews, Slovaks, Croats
- African Americans: released from bondage, facing social dislocation, founded associations in great numbers (1870-1900)
- **W. E. B. Du Bois**: Emphasized importance of black secret societies providing "pastime from monotony of work, field for ambition and intrigue, chance for parade, insurance against misfortune"
- Church played unique role in social-capital formation within the African American community

### Religious and Social Gospel Revival

- **Social Gospel**: Turn-of-the-century effort by liberal Protestant theologians to bring urban poverty to attention of middle-class parishioners
- "The social congregation" — thousands of congregations transformed into centers for worship AND activities: Sunday school, sports, women's groups, youth brigades, sewing circles, benevolent societies
- **Chautauqua movement** (founded 1874): By 1919, "one out of every eleven persons in the country attended a lyceum or Chautauqua program every year"
- **Evelyn Higginbotham**: Black churches "housed a diversity of programs including schools, circulating libraries, concerts, restaurants, insurance companies, vocational training, athletic clubs"

### Youth Organizations and Settlement Houses

- **Extraordinary burst** (1901-1910): Most nationwide youth organizations founded in a single decade — Boy Scouts, Girl Scouts, Campfire Girls, 4-H, Boys Clubs, Big Brothers/Sisters
- **Settlement houses**: Hull House (Jane Addams, 1889) → six by 1891, seventy-four by 1897, ~400 by 1910
- Ironically, greatest long-term effect was on **service givers, not recipients** — settlements produced leaders like Florence Kelley, Eleanor Roosevelt, and GE/AT&T presidents

### Labor Unions as Social Capital

- Union membership nearly quadrupled (1897-1904) from 3.5% to 12.3% of nonagricultural workforce
- Unions were embedded in clubs, lodges, fraternal orders; provided mutual benefit policies; union socials and picnics offered "stimulating leisure-time activity"

### The Progressive Method and Political Achievements

- Typically began by organizing a voluntary association, investigating a problem, gathering facts via social sciences, proposing solutions via education and moral suasion — if it worked, taken over by government
- **Earlier inward-oriented phase of social networks paved the way for later outward-oriented phase of political action** — **investment in social capital was not an alternative to, but a prerequisite for, political mobilization and reform**
- Political achievements: secret ballot (1888), popular initiative/referendum (1898), presidential primaries (1900), direct election of senators (1913), women's suffrage (1920), Federal Reserve (1913), income tax (1913), FDA, FTC, national parks, child labor laws, eight-hour day — hardly any area of public policy left untouched

### Critiques and Cautionary Tales

- **Technocratic elitism**: Progressives favored "expert" solutions that demobilized public participation; electoral turnout began its descent from 1896 and hasn't recovered
- **Social control debate**: Did middle-class reformers organize associations to exert control over "rambunctious" immigrants, or to strengthen their communities?
- **Excessive communitarianism**: Communitarian impulses could go too far — **William Dudley Foulke** suggested the draft be used to compel marriage and teetotalism
- **Most troubling**: Racial segregation and exclusion were central to the Progressive Era agenda — Jim Crow was legalized (1896), the second KKK was founded (1915); not all "civic innovations" were beneficent

### Lessons for Today

- Institutions formed 1880-1910 lasted nearly a century; trends in Section II register the decay of that very structure over the last third of the 20th century
- Progressive leaders correctly diagnosed the problem but **resisted reactionary nostalgia** — just as "Life was much nicer in the village" was wrong then, "Life was much nicer in the fifties — would all women please report to the kitchen and turn off the TV" is wrong now
- **We desperately need an era of civic inventiveness** to create renewed institutions for reinvigorated civic life
- What we create may look nothing like Progressive institutions — but **willingness to err — and then correct our aim — is the price of success in social reform**

## Chapter 24: Toward an Agenda for Social Capitalists

### Arts as Bridge-Building

- Goal: ensure Americans **participate in** (not merely consume) cultural activities — group dancing, songfests, community theater, rap festivals
- Art is especially useful in transcending conventional social barriers; social capital is often a valuable by-product of cultural activities
- **Liz Lerman's Dance Exchange**: Built community using modern dance, bringing together unemployed shipyard workers and white-collar professionals
- **Roadside Theater**: Dramatized local stories in declining Appalachian towns to restore community confidence
- **Toni Blackman's Freestyle Union**: Uses hip-hop/rap poetry/improvisation to attract people from all walks of life
- **David Simpson**: Used community theater to build bridges between loggers and environmentalists
- Many produce great art, but all produce great bridging social capital — **"in some respects an even more impressive achievement"**

### Politics and Government

- **Campaign finance reform** should aim at increasing importance of social capital (time-based participation) and decreasing importance of financial capital (check-based participation) in elections
- Government authority should be **decentralized as far as possible** to bring decisions to local jurisdictions — neighborhood councils in Minneapolis, Portland, Seattle created new social capital (potluck dinners, community gardens, flea markets)
- Policy designers should become **"social capital-savvy"** — seek to do minimum damage to existing stocks even as they add new ones
- Example of damage: Interstate 65 pierced Indianapolis neighborhoods in the 1960s, disrupting social networks for decades — a **"social-capital impact statement"** could have prevented this
- **Avoid false debates**: "Top-down vs. bottom-up" (need both), "government as problem vs. solution" (it can be both), "individual vs. institutional change" (need both)
- Many of the most creative investments in social capital (county agents, 4-H, community colleges, March of Dimes) were direct results of government policy

### The Saguaro Seminar

- Thirty-three accomplished thinkers/doers meeting regularly to develop actionable ideas for increasing Americans' connectedness
- Diverse participants including **Barack Obama**, **Ralph Reed**, **Jim Wallis**, **George Stephanopoulos**, **William Julius Wilson**, **Amy Gutmann**, **E. J. Dionne**, **Martha Minow**
- Named after the saguaro cactus that grows unseen for decades before flowering — "a suitably apt metaphor for social capital"

### Service Learning and Youth Engagement

- **Service learning** (community service coupled to classwork) believed more effective at inculcating civic habits than standalone service
- 1999: ~57% of students in grades 6-12 participated in community service, but only 30% in service learning specifically
- Follow-up studies of high school seniors: regardless of social class, those in voluntary associations far more likely to vote, participate in campaigns, discuss public issues two years post-graduation

### The Final Word: Individual and Institutional Change

- America's major civic institutions are somewhat antiquated a century after most were created — they need reform to invite more active participation
- But institutional reform will not work unless citizens resolve to become reconnected with friends and neighbors
- **Henry Ward Beecher's** century-old advice to "multiply picnics" is not entirely ridiculous today
- We should do this, ironically, not because it will be good for America — though it will be — **but because it will be good for us**

## Methodological Appendix: Key Points

### Triangulation Strategy

- Primary strategy: triangulate among as many independent data sources as possible
- Core principle: No single source of data is flawless, but the more numerous and diverse the sources, the less likely they could all share the same flaw
- **Two independent strands of evidence are better than one, and more than two are better still, especially if they have different imperfections**

### Key Data Sources

- **Organizational records**: Long-term but miss informal activity and new organizations; one scholar estimates 80% of community groups are "dark matter" — invisible to conventional chroniclers
- **Social surveys**: Can illuminate "dark matter" but require identical questions repeated many times; two observations are better than one, but many is much better than two
- **Four primary survey series**: General Social Survey (since 1972), Roper Social and Political Trends (1973-94, 410,000+ respondents), DDB Needham Life Style (since 1975, 87,000+ respondents), National Election Studies (since 1952)
- **Americans' Use of Time Project**: Careful time diaries kept by national samples in 1965, 1975, 1985, and 1995

### The Education Paradox

- Education is the most important predictor of civic participation, and educational levels rose sharply during the period of interest
- Evidence suggests effects of education on participation are **absolute, not relative** — my education increases my participation, and yours does not lower it
- **Conservative approach**: Putnam generally does NOT control for rising education, which means the evidence presented **may well understate** the gross decline in civic engagement

### Appendix III: Rise and Fall of Civic Organizations

- **Golden Age: 1945-1965** — Most civic groups experienced peak membership rates
- **Catastrophic declines**: Odd Fellows down 94%, American Bowling Congress down 72%, AAUW down 84%, Business and Professional Women down 84%
- **Service clubs** (Rotary, Lions, Kiwanis) showed moderate declines (25-55%)
- Only newer professional groups or ethnically specific groups (Hadassah, Knights of Columbus) maintained or grew membership rates
