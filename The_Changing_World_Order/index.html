Introduction

A few years before writing this book, Ray Dalio noticed three enormous forces converging that he had never seen in his lifetime but that had appeared many times throughout history. The first was the creation of enormous debts paired with zero or near-zero interest rates, which led to massive money printing across the three major reserve currencies. The second was the largest wealth, political, and values gaps within countries— especially the United States—in roughly a century. The third was the rise of China to challenge the existing world power and its established order. Together, these three forces most closely resembled the period from 1930 to 1945.

The COVID-19 pandemic added another dimension. Acts of nature—diseases, famines, floods—had devastated civilizations repeatedly throughout history, but because they hadn’t happened in living memory, most people treated them as impossibilities rather than eventualities. Dalio’s core insight was that history moves through relatively well-defined life cycles. Each generation transitions into the next, and the aggregate of these individual stories produces a larger pattern—the Big Cycle of rises and declines that has governed empires for thousands of years.

“All the empires and dynasties I studied rose and declined in a classic Big Cycle that has clear markers that allow us to see where we are in it.”—Ray Dalio

The Big Cycle swings between two states. Peaceful, prosperous periods of creativity and productivity last roughly five times longer than the destructive periods of depression, revolution, and war that punctuate them. The destructive periods act like cleansing storms, ridding the system of its weaknesses and excesses— especially excessive debt—and producing a new beginning on sounder footing. After the conflict resolves, a new world order emerges. Within this overarching cycle sit nested ones: long-term debt cycles of roughly 100 years, short-term debt cycles of about eight years, and even shorter expansion-recession pulses.

The origin of this framework was personal. At age 22, clerking on the floor of the New York Stock Exchange on August 15, 1971, Dalio watched President Nixon announce on television that the United States would renege on its promise to let paper dollars be exchanged for gold. He expected stocks to crash the next morning. Instead, the market jumped roughly 4% while the dollar plummeted. He had never experienced a currency devaluation. Digging into history, he found dozens of cases with identical dynamics. It took several more painful surprises before he committed to understanding every major economic and market move over the last several centuries across all major countries.

“We are like ants preoccupied with our jobs of carrying crumbs in our very brief lifetimes instead of having a broader perspective of the big-picture patterns and cycles.” —Ray Dalio

From that broader perspective, only a limited number of personality types travel a limited number of paths, producing a limited number of situations that repeat throughout time. Only the clothes, languages, and technologies change. The research behind this book examined the rises and declines of every major empire and currency over the last 500 years, with particular focus on the three reserve currency empires: the Dutch Empire and guilder, the British Empire and pound, and the American Empire and dollar. China received the deepest treatment, with its history traced back to 600 CE.

Five background studies led Dalio here. First, his decades-long work on money and credit cycles revealed that central bank actions in 2008 mirrored the 1930s, widening the wealth gap and fueling populism. Second, a 2014 productivity study showed American leading indicators—education quality, indebtedness—declining relative to China and India. Third, Trump’s 2016 election highlighted how wealth and values gaps produce populism, as they had in the 1930s. Fourth, dividing the US economy into quintiles revealed dramatic differences between the top 40% and bottom 60%. Fifth, 37 years of direct engagement with China showed it becoming an effective competitor in production, trade, technology, and geopolitics.

Chapter 1: The Big Cycle in a Tiny Nutshell

The biggest thing affecting most people in most countries through time is the struggle to make, take, and distribute wealth and power. Throughout history, people with wealth have owned the means of production and worked with people who have political power in a symbiotic relationship. The forms evolved—from monarchs and nobles as landowners to capitalists and elected officials— but the dynamics have remained identical. Over time, a small percentage gains exceptional wealth and power, becomes overextended, encounters bad times that hit the least wealthy hardest, and fights produce revolutions or civil wars that establish a new order. No system of government, no economic system, no currency, and no empire lasts forever, yet almost everyone is surprised when they fail.

Productivity—output per person—is the most important force causing the world’s total wealth, power, and living standards to rise over time. It is driven by learning, inventiveness, work ethic, and economic systems. The broader shift from slow to fast productivity gains came from Gutenberg’s printing press in the mid-fifteenth century, which led to the Renaissance, the Scientific Revolution, the Enlightenment, and then capitalism and the First Industrial Revolution in Britain. Since then, wealth and power have come primarily from the combination of education, inventiveness, and capitalism.

“Throughout time, the formula for success has been a system in which well-educated people, operating civilly with each other, come up with innovations, receive funding through capital markets, and own the means by which their innovations are turned into production.” —Ray Dalio

Empire strength is measured through eight key determinants that form a causal chain: education leads to innovation and technology, which drives competitiveness and trade, building economic output and military strength, which enables a financial center and ultimately reserve currency status. Critically, reserve currency status lags behind the other determinants—the habit of using a currency persists long after the strengths that made it dominant have eroded. This lag is what makes the decline so dangerous: the country looks stronger than it actually is.

In the rise phase, countries have low indebtedness, small wealth gaps, strong leadership, good education including character and civility, and a peaceful world order. The Dutch at their peak invented a quarter of all major world inventions, created the first publicly listed company and the first stock exchange, and essentially invented capitalism as we know it. Financial centers follow empires: Amsterdam gave way to London, which gave way to New York, with Shanghai now developing.

At the top, the seeds of decline are already embedded in success. Workers earn more and become expensive. Other countries copy the methods. People stop working as hard and pursue leisure. Financial bubbles form. Wealth gaps self-reinforce as the rich influence politics and give privileges to their children. The reserve currency grants what Valéry Giscard d’Estaing called an “exorbitant privilege”— the ability to borrow far more than fundamentals justify, which boosts short-term power while weakening long-term finances.

“When borrowing and spending are strong, the empire appears very strong, but its finances are in fact being weakened because the borrowing sustains the country’s power beyond its fundamentals.” —Ray Dalio

The decline comes “gradually and then suddenly.” Debts grow too large. An economic downturn makes it impossible to borrow more. The government prints money. The currency devalues. Political extremism rises— populism of the left demands redistribution while populism of the right fights to maintain it. Capital flight accelerates. Productivity collapses, shrinking the pie and intensifying conflict. A rising rival exploits the domestic weakness. When all four forces align—indebtedness, civil war at home, war abroad, and loss of faith in the currency—a change in the world order is at hand.

Yet the destructive periods, while terrible, are typically shorter than people expect— roughly two or three years. History shows that the majority of people stay employed in depressions, are unharmed in shooting wars, and survive natural disasters. Tom Brokaw called those who endured the 1930–45 period “the Greatest Generation.” The post-war order created at Bretton Woods in 1944—including the United Nations, World Bank, and IMF—was designed by the United States, which held two-thirds of the world’s gold and produced roughly half of world output.

Chapter 2: The Determinants

The model is built from studying eleven leading empires over the last 500 years, the twenty most important countries of the last century, and major Chinese dynasties spanning 1,400 years. At its core are two components: existing conditions (the domestic and world orders) and the timeless forces that cause those conditions to change. Most people focus on existing conditions; the focus should be on the forces, because determinants lead to effects that become subsequent determinants, forming self-reinforcing chains.

The framework operates at four levels of detail. The Big Three cycles are finances, internal order, and external order. When all three are simultaneously good, a country is strong and rising; when all three are bad, it is weak and declining. The Big Five adds innovation and acts of nature. The Eight Powers adds education, cost competitiveness, military strength, trade, economic output, financial center strength, and reserve currency status. The Full Eighteen further includes geography, resource-allocation efficiency, infrastructure, character, governance, and wealth gaps.

Among the inherited determinants, geography matters enormously. The United States and China both have large land masses bounded by natural barriers, which promotes unity and a shared language and culture. Europe’s many natural boundaries produced divisions into different states with different languages. Natural resources are important but should not be overvalued relative to human capital— every commodity has declined in inflation-adjusted value over time. The most vulnerable position is high reliance on one or a few commodities.

Acts of nature have been civilization’s deadliest force. The Black Death killed 75 to 200 million people around 1350. Smallpox killed over 300 million in the twentieth century alone—double the number killed in wars. These catastrophes arrive unexpectedly and act as stress tests, revealing underlying strengths and weaknesses that were invisible during prosperous times.

“If we can understand those determinants, we can understand how the machine works and anticipate what will likely be coming at us next.” —Ray Dalio

Human capital is the most sustainable capital because inherited assets eventually deplete, while human capital can regenerate indefinitely. Elon Musk and Steve Jobs built companies that dwarfed incumbents with far more physical resources. Small countries like the Netherlands, Singapore, and Switzerland achieved extraordinary wealth through human capital alone. Culture drives outcomes—individuals like Jesus, Confucius, Mohammed, and Marx conveyed approaches captured in foundational texts that shaped how entire civilizations organize themselves.

The multigenerational psychological cycle runs through five stages, tracking the old proverb “shirtsleeves to shirtsleeves in three generations.” Stage 1: poor, think poor— subsistence economy, nobody lends, money valued highly. The Asian Tigers and then China exemplify countries here. Stage 2: rich, still think poor— the most productive phase, with hard work, high savings, and rising incomes. Stage 3: rich, think rich— priorities shift to savoring finer things, arts and sciences flourish. The US occupied this phase from 1950 to 1965; China is entering it now. Stage 4: poorer, still think rich— debts rise, productivity slows, but spending continues. Bubbles form. Stage 5: poor, think poor again— bubbles burst, currencies weaken, the country competes against cheaper rivals in earlier stages.

Openness to global thinking is one of the most reliable leading indicators of strength. History is littered with countries that isolated themselves—the late Ming Dynasty closing its doors, Tokugawa Japan’s Sakoku policy—and fell behind in technology with catastrophic consequences. Leadership matters tremendously: in each generation, a few hundred people across government, science, and commerce made all the difference. The course of history is shaped by their unique abilities and decisions.

Class struggles follow a recurring pattern. For almost all recorded history, very small ruling classes controlled most wealth and power. Aristotle observed in Politics that the poor and rich quarrel, and whichever side wins treats political supremacy as the prize of victory. The English barons forced King John to sign the Magna Carta in 1215 after a civil war over taxes. The global exploration era and the invention of capitalism shifted wealth from landowning nobles to capitalists and elected representatives. Political swings between left and right operate in roughly ten-year cycles.

Chapter 3: Money, Credit, Debt, and Economic Activity

Understanding money and credit is the biggest missing piece for even the most renowned historians, heads of state, and central bankers trying to understand how history works. All entities have money coming in and money going out. One entity’s spending is another’s income; one entity’s debt is another’s asset. Debt eats equity—creditors get paid ahead of owners. But central banks can feed the debt by printing money, and there is no fixed amount of money and credit in existence.

“Having a reserve currency is great while it lasts because it gives a country exceptional borrowing and spending power and significant power over who else in the world gets the money and credit needed to buy and sell internationally.” —Ray Dalio

But reserve currency status sows the seeds of its own loss. It enables overborrowing, and the eventual money printing to service that debt debases the currency. Meanwhile, countries without reserve currencies that borrow in dollars can go bankrupt when they cannot earn enough dollars to service their debts. The biggest current problem is that for many people, companies, nonprofits, and governments, incomes are low relative to expenses and debts are enormous relative to assets. It often doesn’t seem that way because they spend a lot and look rich while going broke.

The financial economy and the real economy are related but distinct. The real economy is the supply and demand of actual goods and services. The financial economy is central banks adjusting money and credit to raise or lower demand. Creating money does not create wealth—a house that doubles in price from money printing is the same house. Money is stimulative when given out and depressing when it must be repaid. Short-term debt cycles last roughly eight years. Most people have experienced enough of these to think the system will always work this way. The long-term debt cycle plays out over 50 to 75 years, containing six to ten short-term cycles within it. Because crises at this scale happen only once in a lifetime, almost nobody expects them.

The long-term debt cycle moves through six stages. Stage 1 is hard money, where gold settles claims without requiring trust. Stage 2 introduces claims on hard money—banks store gold and issue paper notes. Stage 3 sees debt multiply as holders lend their paper claims for interest and banks relend at higher rates. Everyone is happy: asset prices and production rise. Stage 4 arrives when holders try to convert their claims into actual money and discover there isn’t enough—a run occurs, and the central bank faces a choice between letting money drain out or printing. They always print.

Stage 5 is fiat money— paper currency with no hard asset backing. On August 15, 1971, Nixon announced the dollar’s delinking from gold. The United States had spent heavily on both military and social programs, financing it all by borrowing. Claims on gold far exceeded actual reserves. The resulting fiat system produced 1970s inflation and the 1980s debt crisis. Stage 6 is the flight back to hard money—overprinting causes people to flee into gold, stocks, foreign currencies, and real assets. Governments typically outlaw gold ownership and impose foreign exchange controls. Eventually debt is wiped out, and money is linked back to gold or a hard currency, as happened after the Weimar Republic and Argentina’s late-1980s crisis.

Governments have four levers for reducing debt: austerity (too painful to sustain), defaults (deflationary and devastating), tax increases (politically difficult), and printing money (the most common path because the harmed parties are hardest to identify). When holders of debt realize what is happening, they sell debt assets and shift wealth into better stores of value: gold, certain stocks, and countries not having these problems. The big blowup happens roughly every 50 to 75 years. The Old Testament prescribed a Year of Jubilee every 50 years when all debts were forgiven. Ironically, the closer people are to the blowup, the safer they feel.

“All currencies devalue or die, and when they do, cash and bonds are devalued or wiped out.” —Ray Dalio

Chapter 4: The Changing Value of Money

Of roughly 750 currencies that have existed since 1700, only about 20% remain, and every surviving one has been substantially devalued. Some were completely wiped out in countries that experienced hyperinflation or lost wars. Others merged, as European currencies did into the euro. Going back to 1850, the currencies of most major countries would be unrecognizable— Germany might use gulden or thaler, Japan might use koban or ryo.

Six major devaluation periods mark the modern era. The 1860s US Civil War saw Lincoln suspend gold convertibility and print “greenbacks.” The US returned to the gold standard in the mid-1870s, and most currencies remained fixed to gold until World War I. Heavy war borrowing during 1914–18 shattered the system. Germany’s Weimar Republic produced the most iconic hyperinflation in history. The 1930s depression triggered global money printing and competitive devaluations. Money and debt were completely wiped out for the losers of World War II and severely devalued for the winners except the United States, which accumulated two-thirds of the world’s government-held gold. The 1968–73 period broke the dollar-gold link, and since 2000, money’s value has steadily fallen relative to gold.

Historical returns reveal the danger of holding cash. From 1850 to the present, average annual real return on interest-earning cash was 1.2%, versus 0.9% for gold. But from 1912 onward— the fiat era—real returns on cash fell to negative 0.1% while gold rose to 1.6%. In Germany, due to hyperinflation, real cash returns averaged negative 18% per year. Positive real returns on cash occurred in only half of countries. After 1971, central banks had unconstrained ability to print, producing high inflation and low real rates. Gold surged until Paul Volcker raised interest rates significantly above inflation in 1980–81, causing gold to fall until 2000. Since then, renewed money printing has supported gold again.

Currency devaluation and loss of reserve currency status are related but distinct phenomena. Reserve currency loss is the product of chronic large devaluations, typically occurring when a country has already lost economic primacy to a rising rival, carries growing debts monetized by its central bank, and suffers a weakening currency in a self-reinforcing spiral. Net central bank reserves start falling years before the actual devaluation. Runs on the currency typically coincide with debt problems related to wartime spending. The key variable is how much economic and military power a country retains at the moment of devaluation—more power means more willing holders. The Dutch guilder collapsed massively and quickly during a steep national decline. The British pound declined more gradually, through two major devaluations. The US dollar has had two abrupt devaluations (1933, 1971) plus gradual erosion since 2000 but has not yet lost its reserve status.

The post-1971 shift to fiat money gave central banks unconstrained ability to create money. Gold appreciation surged until Volcker raised rates above inflation in 1980–81, then gold fell for two decades. Since 2000, central banks have pushed rates down, printed money, and bought financial assets on a massive scale—supporting gold once again. The pattern is clear: during periods of money devaluation, hard money and hard assets rise in value relative to cash. The key question for investors late in the debt cycle is whether the interest rate being paid on debt more than compensates for the devaluation risk they face. At the time of writing, the answer across major economies was unambiguously no.

The gold standard era before World War I (roughly the 1870s to 1914) was the only extended period when most major currencies maintained stable purchasing power. This stability came at a cost—governments could not print money to cushion downturns, producing severe deflationary depressions (the Panic of 1873, the Panic of 1893, the Panic of 1907). But for savers who held through, real returns on cash were genuinely positive. The shift away from gold removed the constraint on money creation and transferred wealth systematically from savers to borrowers across every subsequent generation.

“History has shown that there are very large risks in holding interest-earning cash currency as a storehold of wealth, especially late in debt cycles.” —Ray Dalio

Chapter 5: Internal Order and Disorder

How people are with each other is the primary driver of the outcomes they get. The internal cycle moves through six stages over roughly 100 years, with short-term debt cycles and political swings nested within it. Stage 1 establishes a new order after revolution. Stage 2 builds the systems and bureaucracies. Stage 3—the sweet spot—brings peace and prosperity. Stage 4 produces the excesses. Stage 5 brings bad finances and intense conflict. Stage 6 erupts into civil war or revolution, resetting to Stage 1.

At the time of writing, Dalio placed the United States in Stage 5: bad financial conditions and intensifying conflict, though still possessing great strengths in technology and military that are declining on a relative basis. China was in the rising phase. The US had 60 to 80 percent of economic red flags present, giving roughly a one-in-six chance of severe internal conflict. When the flags exceed 80 percent, the odds of civil war or revolution rise to one in three.

Stage 1’s consolidation of power involves the “purge”—the fight among the revolution’s winners to eliminate opposition. France’s Reign of Terror, Russia’s Red Terror, and China’s Anti-Rightist Campaign were all brutal examples. The best consolidators— Tang Taizong, Caesar Augustus, the US Founding Fathers—established orders lasting centuries. Stage 2 requires “civil engineers,” leaders completely different from revolutionary fighters. Winston Churchill and Mao Zedong were brilliant generals and lousy civil engineers. Konrad Adenauer, Lee Kuan Yew, and Deng Xiaoping exemplified the type needed to build lasting prosperity.

Stage 3 is the sweet spot where conditions improve for almost everyone. Incomes exceed expenses, savings finance investment, and successful entrepreneurs become heroes. Stage 4 produces the bubble: debt growth outpaces cash flows, investors assume the future will resemble the past, and leveraging spirals push asset prices ever higher. Spending shifts from investment to consumption. Military costs grow as global interests expand. Wealth gaps widen. History’s decadent leaders— Emperor Nero, Louis XIV, the Ming’s Wanli Emperor— withdrew from governing to pursue luxury while their empires rotted.

Stage 5’s toxic mix combines a country in bad financial shape, large wealth gaps, and a severe economic shock. The single most reliable leading indicator of civil war or revolution is bankrupt government finances combined with big wealth gaps. When a government with large deficits can print money, it always does, because printing is less painful than the alternatives. This drives investors to flee the currency, depreciating it further. When it cannot print—like US states versus the federal government—it must raise taxes and cut services, which is equally intolerable.

The late stages are poisoned by mutually reinforcing pathologies. Decadence becomes grotesque. Bureaucracy makes even obviously good decisions impossible—American education funding is a prime example, where the Constitution does not make education a federal responsibility, children in richer areas receive vastly better schooling, most agree this is unfair, yet the structure is nearly impossible to change. Populism surges as ordinary people feel abandoned by elites. Media trustworthiness collapses— a 2019 Gallup poll showed only 13% of Americans have “a great deal” of trust in media, down from 72% in 1976. Rules are abandoned in favor of passion. People dying in fighting is the marker signaling progression to Stage 6.

“When causes people are passionately behind are more important to them than the system for making decisions, the system is in jeopardy.” —Ray Dalio

Civil wars are incredibly brutal because they are fights to the death where everyone must pick a side and moderates disappear. The Russian Revolution of 1917 built a communist order lasting 74 years. Japan’s Meiji Restoration of 1866–69, triggered by American gunboats forcing the country open, produced a progressive new regime that introduced universal education and adopted capitalism. Revolutionary leaders are typically well-educated and middle-class—Danton, Robespierre, Lenin, and Mao all studied law or economics—supported initially by liberal aristocrats before evolving into brutal revolutionaries.

Democracy faces particular risks during times of great conflict. It requires consensus but produces fragmented decision-making. Leaders are selected through popularity contests without thoughtful capability review. The biggest risk is that ineffectiveness leads to revolution by populist autocrats. Historical breakdowns include Athens, the Roman Republic, the Weimar Republic, and 1920s Italy, Japan, and Spain. Federalist democracies face additional stress as conflicts between states and central government intensify—a marker to watch for in the United States.

Chapter 6: External Order and Disorder

International relations are governed far more by raw power than by law. All governance needs effective laws, enforcement, adjudication, and consequences, but these either do not exist or are not effective between countries. Attempts like the League of Nations and United Nations largely failed because they lack more wealth and power than the most powerful countries they are meant to constrain.

“The international order follows the law of the jungle much more than it follows international law.” —Ray Dalio

Five types of conflict escalate along a continuum: trade and economic wars (tariffs and restrictions), technology wars (protecting or sharing innovations), geopolitical wars (territory and alliances), capital wars (sanctions and financial exclusion), and military wars. Once military conflict begins, all four other dimensions are weaponized to the maximum. The first four evolve as intense competitions over years or decades before the shooting starts.

The two things one can be most confident about regarding war are that it will not go as planned and that it will be far worse than imagined. The greatest risk exists when two powers have roughly comparable military strength and irreconcilable existential differences. At the time of writing, the most potentially explosive situation was between the US and China over Taiwan. The choice facing opposing countries—fight or back down— is agonizing. Fighting costs lives and money; backing down costs status and invites further aggression.

The 1930s offer a detailed roadmap of how economic war becomes hot war. Roosevelt responded to the Great Depression with massive spending, a top marginal tax rate raised from 25% to 75%, and budget deficits that quintupled. The stock market returned over 200% and the economy grew at 9% real from 1933 to 1936. But a Fed tightening in 1936 pushed the fragile economy back into recession. Meanwhile, Hitler secretly announced expansion plans in November 1937, annexed Austria and part of Czechoslovakia, and invaded Poland on September 1, 1939. Japan expanded into China beginning in 1937, killing an estimated 200,000 civilians at Nanking alone.

Roosevelt escalated through the Export Control Act of 1940, moved the Pacific Fleet to Hawaii, and passed the Lend-Lease Act in March 1941, effectively ending American neutrality. By July 1941, the US had frozen all Japanese assets, closed the Panama Canal to Japanese ships, and embargoed oil—cutting off three-fourths of Japan’s trade and 80% of its oil. Japan calculated it would run out of oil in two years. Pearl Harbor followed on December 7, 1941.

“In war one’s ability to withstand pain is even more important than one’s ability to inflict pain.” —Ray Dalio

Wartime economies require near-total government control: rationing, production controls, price and wage controls, import and export restrictions, capital controls, and debt monetization. Top marginal tax rates were staggering:

Beyond taxes, governments imposed sweeping controls. Gold becomes the only trusted medium of exchange between non-allied countries. After the Battle of Midway in 1942, Allied equities rallied continuously while German and Japanese markets eventually closed and were virtually wiped out. Protecting wealth during wartime is extremely difficult—traditionally safe investments become unsafe, capital mobility is restricted, and redistribution takes priority over protection.

Chapter 7: Investing in Light of the Big Cycle

All investments are exchanges of a lump-sum payment today for expected future payments. Those future cash flows are determined by just four factors: growth, inflation, risk premiums, and discount rates. Central governments determine where money goes through taxing and spending but cannot create money. Central banks create money and credit but cannot control where it flows in the real economy. Portfolio construction should be well-diversified across these four drivers, tilted tactically based on current conditions.

Most investors base their expectations on the US and UK experience since the 1950s. That is survivorship bias. Not many stock and bond markets survived World War II. Looking at these two countries during this particular period means looking at uniquely blessed nations during the best part of the Big Cycle. In the 35 years before 1945, virtually all wealth was destroyed or confiscated in most countries. In some, capitalists were killed or imprisoned when capital markets and capitalism failed. Worst-case portfolio losses in a 60/40 stock-bond portfolio: Russia 1900–1918 lost 100%, China 1930–50 lost 100%, Germany 1903–23 lost 100%, Japan 1928–48 lost 96%, France 1930–50 lost 93%. Only the US, Canada, and Australia avoided sustained complete losses.

The big capital markets cycle mirrors the debt cycle. In the upwave, financial wealth and obligations rise relative to tangible wealth until the promises cannot be met. A run on the bank occurs, money is printed, financial wealth is devalued, and the cycle resets. When there is a lot of financial wealth relative to real wealth, the real returns of financial assets—especially cash and debt—are terrible. At the time of writing, US bond yields were near their lowest ever, with negative real yields. US bonds required roughly 45 years to return $100, Japanese bonds 150 years, and European bonds likely never. The logical question becomes: why not buy anything that keeps pace with inflation instead?

“One of the most important questions investors need to regularly ask themselves is whether the amount of interest that is being paid more than makes up for the devaluation risk they face.”—Ray Dalio

The fifty years before World War I offered a cautionary parallel. Almost no conflict between major powers, the greatest innovation and productivity growth ever seen, enormous wealth and prosperity, globalization at new highs— global exports rose by several multiples. Going into 1900, things looked spectacular except that wealth gaps and resentments were growing and debts were large. Between 1900 and 1914, conditions worsened and international tensions increased. Even with those warning signs, it would have been nearly impossible to predict World War I confidently. The lesson: the best part of the cycle feels permanently good, and the transition comes faster than anyone expects.

During periods of wealth destruction, normal investment rules break down completely. Wealth confiscation, capital controls, and stock market closures become common whenever large wealth gaps coincide with internal conflict. Deaths as a percentage of population reached 15% in 1940s Germany, 13% in 1940s Russia, and 4% in 1940s Japan. Even in the United States, which suffered no invasion, investors underperformed stocks by roughly 1.5% per year from 2000 to 2020 through behavioral errors—buying high and selling low—and lost approximately 25% of real equity returns to taxes in any twenty-year period.

In the rising parts of the capital markets cycle, stocks and bonds go up as interest rates decline. More money chasing assets raises demand and lowers risk premiums. Investments look increasingly attractive even as their expected future returns decrease. More outstanding claims relative to what those claims represent means more risk, but conditions seem good and memories of crises fade. When the cycle reverses and real returns of financial wealth turn negative— especially for cash and debt—hard money and hard assets rise in value relative to paper. The five hundred years of data across dozens of countries tells the same story: these cycles repeat for basically the same reasons, and the terrible periods are always more than matched by the terrific upswings that follow the painful transition.

Chapter 8: The Last 500 Years

The world in 1500 was incomparably different from today. Travel covered roughly 25 miles per day on horseback—Apollo astronauts reached the moon faster than a traveler could get from Paris to Rome. Countries as we know them did not exist; territories were run by families whose kingdoms fought constantly for wealth and power. Religions and religious leaders held far more power than they do today, while science did not exist. Serfdom persisted in most of Western Europe until the 1300s and 1400s. The Habsburg dynasty controlled Spain and the Holy Roman Empire, making it the Western world’s most powerful entity. The Ming Dynasty in China was the most advanced empire on earth, with a population roughly double Europe’s, the world’s largest navy, and a standing army of one million.

“That was the lay of the land in 1500. The world order was about to change in very big ways.” —Ray Dalio

Two patterns characterize the 500 years since: several revolutions in thinking that produced tremendous progress, and many cycles of peace and prosperity alternating with depressions and wars. The Commercial Revolution shifted wealth from agriculture to trade, centering on Italian city-states where Venice pioneered checks and balances in governance and Florence’s gold florin became an accepted global currency. The Renaissance modeled its thinking on ancient Greece and Rome, pivoting toward logical reasoning. The Reformation challenged papal monopoly after Martin Luther published his Ninety-Five Theses in 1517, triggering wars that killed millions and ultimately undermined the Holy Roman Empire.

The Peace of Westphalia in 1648 established geographic borders and sovereign rights, essentially inventing countries as we know them. One quarter of Europe’s population had died. The Dutch emerged as the leading global economic power. The invention of capitalism followed quickly: the Dutch East India Company became the world’s first publicly listed company in 1602, and the Amsterdam Stock Exchange opened the same year—over one in fifty Dutch adults owned shares. These innovations, combined with the Bank of England’s first government bond in 1694, shifted real GDP growth from slow to fast.

The Scientific Revolution saw Francis Bacon pioneer the scientific method, Copernicus and Galileo expand knowledge of the cosmos, and Newton formulate the laws of motion. Britain’s First Industrial Revolution made it the first country to sustain widespread productivity improvement. The Enlightenment applied scientific method to human behavior— Adam Smith in economics, John Locke and Montesquieu in political philosophy—producing the intellectual foundation for overthrowing monarchies. Napoleon converted revolutionary chaos to order, expanded his empire with military brilliance, overreached with the Russian invasion, and failed. The Congress of Vienna in 1814–15 created a new order with balance-of-power concepts that lasted roughly a century.

The Second Industrial Revolution of the late nineteenth century—railroads, electricity, telephones, interchangeable parts— primarily benefited the United States and Germany. It produced the Gilded Age with enormous wealth alongside enormous wealth gaps. The invention of communism in 1848 was a direct reaction to capitalism’s inequalities and would eventually produce revolutions in Russia and China that reshaped the twentieth century. The Age of Exploration that preceded all of this had begun with Henry the Navigator sponsoring Portuguese voyages, Spain conquering the Aztec and Incan empires, and the flow of silver from the Americas doubling prices across Europe every few decades—a vivid reminder of how monetary shifts can produce economic consequences that seem unimaginable based on recent experience.

China’s trajectory during these five centuries is especially instructive. The Ming Emperor Yongle sent Zheng He on seven “treasure voyages” with the world’s largest navy, but subsequent emperors ended the voyages and turned inward. Japan followed a similar path with its Sakoku policy of isolation. Both countries paid catastrophic prices for closing themselves off, falling behind in technology while European powers advanced. The contrast between China’s dominance in 1500— population 100–130 million, the most sophisticated civilization on earth—and its humiliation by British gunboats just three centuries later illustrates how quickly isolation can destroy even the greatest empire’s advantages.

Chapter 9: The Dutch Empire

The Dutch rise was driven by two critical innovations: uniquely effective sailing ships and capitalism itself. Income per capita rose to more than double that of most European powers. Dutch literacy rates reached twice the world average. The Dutch East India Company controlled roughly a third of all global trade. Amsterdam became the world’s most important financial center despite a total population of only one to two million people. At their peak, the Dutch produced approximately 25% of all major world inventions.

“In short, the Dutch were superbly educated people who were very hardworking and inventive.” —Ray Dalio

The Bank of Amsterdam, founded in 1609, solved the chaos of roughly 800 different coins circulating across Europe, many debased or counterfeit. It created the bank guilder—paper money backed by hard currency—which became the first true reserve currency. Baltic and Russian trade relied solely on guilders for pricing and settlement. The bank’s integrity and the nation’s commercial dominance made Amsterdam the center of global finance for over a century.

The Thirty Years’ War (1618–48) pitted Catholic Habsburgs against Protestant states, though alliances followed money and geopolitics more than theology—Catholic France under Cardinal Richelieu allied with Lutheran Sweden. The Habsburgs lost. The Peace of Westphalia expanded sovereignty, weakened religious authorities, and created modern nationalism. The Dutch emerged relatively unscathed financially and entered their Golden Age.

The classic reversal followed. Educational and technological edges eroded. The Dutch became wealthy and uncompetitive—wages rose, work ethic softened, military costs grew. The Industrial Revolution, centered in Britain, shifted the balance of power. Yet even during decline, roughly 40% of global trade continued to settle in Amsterdam using bank guilders, granting the Dutch the “extraordinary privilege” of accumulating enormous debts. Reserve currency usage lagged behind declining fundamentals, as it always does.

The Dutch fought a series of costly wars: the Eighty Years’ War against Spain (1566–1648), four Anglo-Dutch Wars over trade dominance (1652–1784), and the Franco-Dutch War (1672–78). The Glorious Revolution of 1688 placed William III of Orange on the English throne, which seemed beneficial but had devastating second-order consequences: Dutch merchants shifted operations to London, and English merchants gained access to Dutch trade networks, hastening London’s rise as the international finance center.

The killing blow came with the Fourth Anglo-Dutch War (1780–84). The English delivered a massive blow to the Dutch Navy, seizing Caribbean and East Indies territories. The Dutch East India Company lost half its ships and borrowed heavily from the Bank of Amsterdam. The bank created more paper money than it could back—a classic central bank move to print. Precious metal accounts plummeted from 17 million guilder in March 1780 to just 300,000 guilder by January 1783 as holders demanded their gold and silver back. By 1795, the French revolutionary government overthrew the Dutch Republic. By 1799, the Dutch East India Company’s charter expired, its stock worthless. The guilder, once the world’s most trusted currency, had died.

Chapter 10: The British Empire

Britain’s rise rested on a political revolution that subordinated monarchy to law. The English Civil War led to King Charles I being tried and executed. The Glorious Revolution established Parliament’s supremacy, allowing a moderately meritocratic selection of leaders—key statesmen like Pitt the Elder, Gladstone, and Disraeli came from merchant families rather than aristocracy. Francis Bacon’s vision of a society based on reason and science shaped the Enlightenment. Literacy rates rose. The Bank of England, founded in 1694, standardized government debt and drove bond yields down dramatically.

The Industrial Revolution married a well-educated population, a culture of inventiveness, access to capital, and England’s endowments of iron and coal. The shift from rural to urban, from agrarian to industrial, was transformative. Key inventions arrived in rapid succession: the steam engine (1712), the flying shuttle (1733), the spinning jenny (1764), the power loom (1785), and the steam locomotive (1801). At its peak, Britain produced 20% of world output, controlled 40% of global exports, governed 20% of the world’s land mass and 25% of its population. London became the financial center, and the pound sterling became the reserve currency.

“Every power has its time in the sun. But all eventually decline.” —Ray Dalio

France, despite being the center of Enlightenment learning, missed its chance. John Law’s Mississippi Company bubble in 1716–20 destroyed confidence in paper money. A series of costly wars drained the treasury. Extreme inequality at the court of Louis XVI— Marie Antoinette’s Hamlet near Versailles while bread prices soared— made revolution inevitable. The French Revolution produced the Reign of Terror (20,000–30,000 executed), hyperinflation of the assignat, and eventually Napoleon, who stabilized the economy but overreached with his Russian invasion. Britain won through superior financial strength—lending money to coalition forces.

After the Congress of Vienna in 1814–15, no power benefited more than Britain. Its main rivals were weakened, and the equilibrium allowed it to avoid expensive conflicts near home—a policy of “splendid isolation” that set the stage for the Imperial Century. Britain expanded into Canada, Australia, India, and Africa, and even where it did not take formal control, it intervened for trade access, as with the Opium Wars against China.

Three classic factors drove the decline. First, the Second Industrial Revolution (synthetics, petroleum, electricity, automobiles) benefited the US and Germany more, and Britain failed to reorganize its industries to compete. Second, inequality became extreme—the top 1% owned over 70% of all wealth, the top 10% owned 93%. The Labour Party rose as a significant force, and 25% of eligible men joined unions by 1911. Third, Germany unified under Bismarck’s brilliant diplomacy, implemented mandatory education, invested heavily in research universities and industrial capacity, and by 1900 its GDP surpassed Britain’s. Germany dominated Nobel Prizes from 1900 to 1913. Kaiser Wilhelm II set off an arms race that culminated in the explosion of 1914.

World War I killed 8.5 million soldiers and 13 million civilians. Russia devolved into revolution. The Spanish flu killed 20 to 50 million more. The Treaty of Versailles created an unsustainable order of reparations that made a second war inevitable. The Roaring Twenties produced a boom fueled by speculation and debt, which burst when the Fed tightened in 1929. World War II finished what the first started— the US gained enormously while Britain ended with large debts, an expensive empire, more competitive rivals, and a population with vast wealth gaps.

The pound’s loss of reserve currency status took roughly 20 years. One-third of international trade was still denominated in sterling in 1960. But smart investors recognized the contrast between British and American finances. A 1947 attempt to restore convertibility collapsed within weeks as the pound came under massive selling pressure. A 30% devaluation in 1949 restored competitiveness temporarily, but recurring balance-of-payments crises forced another devaluation in 1967, and the deutschemark replaced the pound as the world’s second reserve currency.

Chapter 11: The American Empire

The United States went through the usual revolution-and-consolidation process in a uniquely peaceful way through negotiation and near-total respect for agreements. Rapidly improving education preceded big rises in innovation, technology, and competitiveness. After the Civil War, the Second Industrial Revolution produced enormous wealth alongside enormous wealth gaps. The Progressive Era brought trust busting and federal income taxes via a constitutional amendment in 1913. New York became the world’s leading financial center.

“Still, the cycle needn’t transpire this way if countries in their rich and powerful stages stay productive, earn more than they spend, make the system work well for most of their populations, and figure out ways of creating and sustaining win-win relationships with their most significant rivals.” —Ray Dalio

The dollar’s path was not straightforward—in its first century the US financial system was completely underdeveloped, with no central bank and eight significant banking panics in New York alone between 1836 and 1913.

The Federal Reserve was created in 1913 after the Panic of 1907. US stock market capitalization had surpassed Britain’s by 1910, and US Steel became the world’s most valuable company. World War I transformed the landscape: the US maintained gold convertibility while European currencies devalued. Allied war debts boosted the dollar further. The Roaring Twenties ended when the Fed tightened in 1929. World War II made the US the decisive winner—it sold and lent during the war, fighting took place far from US territory, and by war’s end America had accumulated two-thirds of all government-held gold. The Marshall and Dodge Plans rebuilt Europe and Japan while strengthening the dollar’s position and American geopolitical influence.

The American Golden Age from 1945 to 1966 saw cheap veteran mortgages drive a housing boom, Marshall Plan exports fuel growth, and a multidecade stock market bull run reinforce New York’s dominance. The country had enough money for education, technologies that put men on the moon, and more. Dalio started investing in 1961 at age twelve, born at exactly the right time and place. But “guns and butter” fiscal policy—simultaneously funding the space program, the War on Poverty, and Vietnam— never accounted for costs. By the late 1960s, real GDP growth was near zero, inflation hit 6%, and short-term rates reached 8%. US banking went global, expanding from 13 banks with foreign branches in 1965 to 79 by 1970.

The classic run on gold arrived in 1971. Claims vastly exceeded reserves. Americans traveling in Europe had difficulty exchanging dollars. On August 15, Nixon defaulted on the gold commitment, and the world shifted to an unanchored fiat system. Paul Volcker—Nixon’s undersecretary at the delinking—later headed the Federal Reserve from 1979 to 1987. The 1970s produced an inflation psychology where Americans bought anything to get ahead of rising prices. Gold went from $35 (the 1944 fixed price) to $850 by 1980. Social turmoil compounded the economic crisis: Vietnam, oil embargoes, Watergate, 52 hostages held in Tehran for 444 days.

Volcker’s decision to constrain money growth in October 1979 drove interest rates to what Helmut Schmidt called the highest “since Jesus Christ.” The resulting tightness broke the backs of debtors and produced the worst downturn since the Great Depression. Reagan imposed fiscal discipline; Thatcher did the same in Britain. Mexico defaulted in August 1982. Dalio had anticipated the debt crisis and profited at first, then bet on a depression that never came—he had to let everyone at Bridgewater Associates go and borrow $4,000 from his father. He later called it the best thing that ever happened to him. The key insight: when debts are denominated in currencies that central banks can print, debt crises can be well-managed.

The period from the 1980s to 2008 was a disinflationary boom: falling inflation, falling rates, rising stocks. The Soviet Union collapsed in 1991, unable to afford its empire against Reagan’s arms-race spending. Globalization flourished. Three cycles peaked—the 2000 dot-com bubble, the 2007 housing bubble, and the 2019 pre-COVID peak. Throughout, China’s rise and technology’s replacement of workers widened wealth gaps. The US-China trade symbiosis was, as Dalio put it, “a hell of a ‘buy now, pay later’ deal”—China manufactured cheaply and lent Americans the money to buy the goods, despite earning roughly 40% of American incomes.

The 2008 crisis pushed rates to zero for the first time since the Great Depression. The Fed printed money and bought financial assets, which raised prices and benefited those rich enough to own them. Wealth gaps grew to their largest since the 1930–45 period. Trump’s 2016 election reflected populist revolt. COVID in March 2020 triggered unprecedented fiscal and monetary intervention. Dalio’s model placed the US roughly 70% through its Big Cycle—not yet in Stage 6 where active fighting begins, but with internal conflict high and rising, the country split almost 50-50 on seemingly irreconcilable lines. Three markers to watch: rules being disregarded, both sides emotionally attacking each other, and blood being spilled.

Chapter 12: China’s Rise

Dalio brought nearly 40 years of direct experience to this chapter. Many urged him not to publish it given US-China tensions—any compliment to China alienates American readers while criticism infuriates Chinese ones. He refused to stay silent because the relationship is too important to leave unexamined by those who know both countries well. He judged China through the lens of the 18 determinants and eight measures of power, not through ideology.

China has stood among the most powerful empires for most of recorded history, with a steep exception from roughly 1840 to 1950. Since Deng Xiaoping’s reforms in 1978, economic competitiveness and trade took off. By the time of writing, China was roughly tied with the US in trade, economic output, and innovation, with military and education rising quickly. Reserve currency and financial center status lagged but were growing.

The major dynasties followed the Big Cycle with remarkable consistency. The Tang Dynasty (618–907), under Emperor Taizong, unified China, fostered education and trade, and produced roughly 150 years of prosperity before declining through corruption and natural disasters. The Song Dynasty (960–1279) created the most innovative and dynamic economy in the world, reforming the examination system and inventing the first paper money—and also the first to overprint it, nearly tripling the money supply between 1209 and 1231. Kublai Khan’s Yuan Dynasty governed well initially but collapsed after less than a century from corruption and failed succession. The Ming Dynasty expanded trade with Europe but eventually closed its doors, believing it needed nothing from the outside world—a decision that drove China’s decline relative to Europe.

Three philosophies shaped Chinese governance. Confucianism emphasizes harmony through hierarchy, loyalty, education, and meritocracy. Legalism demands autocratic obedience. Taoism seeks harmony with nature’s laws. As Wang Qishan, vice president of China, explained the core difference: Americans hold the individual above all else, while Chinese place family and collective above everything. America is bottom-up, optimized for the individual; China is top-down, optimized for the collective. The Chinese word for “country” combines the characters for “state” and “family.”

China’s decline from 1800 to 1949 followed the classic pattern: the Qing Dynasty became decadent and weak, Western powers became strong, the financial system broke down, and massive rebellions erupted. The British East India Company smuggled opium from India to pay for tea and silk. The First Opium War (1839–42) ended with the Treaty of Nanking, giving Britain Hong Kong and opening ports by force. After the Boxer Rebellion in 1901, foreign powers demanded reparations equivalent to 18,000 tons of silver. This combination of weak leadership, unsound finances, internal rebellion, foreign exploitation, and natural disasters produced the Century of Humiliation that still shapes Chinese thinking today.

Three phases mark the modern rise. Mao (1949–1976) consolidated power and built the institutional foundation but ran a strict communist system that produced catastrophic results. Deng Xiaoping (1978–2012) launched “reform and opening up” with a three-part 70-year plan. He reformed governance from single-leader rule to collective leadership through the Politburo Standing Committee, introduced term limits, and relied on Lee Kuan Yew of Singapore for advice. Dalio’s first visit in 1984 found per capita income below $200 per year. He gave out $10 calculators that even the highest-ranking officials thought miraculous. He predicted skyscrapers would replace the hutongs below CITIC’s tenth floor; his Chinese hosts said he didn’t understand China.

The stock market’s creation was driven by reformers working from a dingy hotel with inadequate funding but a clear mission. Tiananmen Square in 1989 set back international relations but did not stop the reforms. Most of Dalio’s Chinese friends eventually concluded the government made the right call—their greatest fear was revolutionary disorder. The economy continued growing strongly, and relations with the West improved to their best level ever.

Chapter 13: US-China Relations and Wars

The emergence of US-China conflicts follows the classic pattern: prosperity financed by debt leads to a bubble, the bubble bursts, money printing benefits the wealthy, wealth gaps widen, populism and nationalism surge. The era of peace, prosperity, and globalization began to wane, giving way to an era of conflicts between rich and poor within countries and between the rising power and the dominant one. Dalio identified seven types of war between the US and China: trade, technology, geopolitical, capital, military, culture, and the war with ourselves.

The trade war centers on three criticisms of China: limiting market access to protect domestic industries, extracting technology from foreign companies through government support, and intellectual property theft (one in five North American companies in a 2019 survey reported their IP stolen by Chinese firms). The most dangerous escalation would be cutting off essential imports—the US restricting China’s oil and technologies, China withholding rare earth elements. Both sides are shifting toward decoupling, with China pursuing a “dual circulation” strategy.

The technology war is far more serious, because whoever wins the technology war probably wins the military war and all other wars. The US leads in advanced AI chips but trails in 5G. China produces roughly eight times as many STEM graduates. Its resources devoted to technology far exceed America’s. Tencent and Alibaba rank among the world’s ten largest tech companies. China hosts 40% of the world’s largest civilian supercomputers. The combination of big data, big AI, and big computing will produce superior decision-making— and China collects vastly more data per person from a population four times larger. If the US shuts off Chinese access to essential technologies, that signals a major step toward shooting war.

Taiwan is the most dangerous sovereignty issue and probably the only plausible trigger for military conflict between the two greatest powers. China is now stronger militarily in that region. An American failure to fight for Taiwan would signal the decline of the US Empire in the Pacific, much as Britain’s loss of the Suez Canal signaled the end of British power in the Middle East—and the end of the pound as a reserve currency. Dalio believed the US would probably lose a fight for Taiwan, and such a war would be deeply unpopular with the American public.

The capital war is constrained by the absence of an attractive reserve currency alternative to the dollar. China’s clearing system remains underdeveloped, and money does not flow freely across its borders. But history shows that when currencies are no longer desired and no attractive alternatives exist, they still devalue—capital flows into gold, commodities, stocks, and property instead. The culture war reflects irreconcilable differences: Chinese culture is top-down and collectivist; American culture is bottom-up and individualist. The Houston Rockets incident of 2019, when a general manager tweeted support for Hong Kong protests, illustrated the gulf— Americans saw a free speech issue while Chinese held the entire league responsible.

“The main thing to realize and accept is that the Chinese and Americans have different values.” —Ray Dalio

The most important war is the war with ourselves. A country’s internal strength is the factor most within its control and the most measurable through the 18 determinants. The internal wars and challenges in both China and the US are more important than the external ones. What makes a great empire is a reinforcing chain: strong education produces civility, good resource allocation, competitiveness, income growth, investment, productivity, inventions, military strength, trade dominance, and ultimately reserve currency status and financial center preeminence. Even small countries can become leading world powers if they manage themselves well.

Chapter 14: The Future

Simple extrapolation suggests China gaining 25–30% in relative power over the next twenty years while the US gains roughly 15%. But paradigm shifts make the future fundamentally different from the past. Standing in 1750, monarchies and agriculture seemed permanent, life expectancy was stuck at around 30 years, and concepts like capitalism and American global leadership were unimaginable. The Digital Revolution shattered many well-established beliefs. Dalio’s principle: bet on the upside from productivity improvements, but not so aggressively that cycles knock you out of the game.

Five determinants carry the biggest impact. Innovation is the most powerful: humanity’s inventiveness is accelerating, and quantum computing combined with AI will be to traditional computing what the computer was to the abacus. Human lifespan will likely increase by 20–25% or more. The debt and money cycle is the biggest driver of economic ups and downs. Promises in reserve currencies are too large to be paid in hard money, so money will probably be printed. The reserve currency will likely decline analogously to past declines—slowly for a long time, then very quickly, because the currency’s decline rate outpaces the interest paid to debt holders.

The internal order cycle shows the US in Stage 5 and China in Stage 3. A 2019 Pew survey found that 55% of Republicans and 47% of Democrats viewed the other side as “more immoral.” Fifteen percent of Republicans and 20% of Democrats thought the country would be better off if large numbers of the other side “just died.” Dalio estimated a roughly 30% chance of severe US internal conflict within ten years—dangerously high. The external order cycle puts US-China conflict at one standard deviation above normal. Graham Allison’s Destined for War found that nearly equal powers with irreconcilable differences led to military war in 12 of 16 historical cases. Dalio’s net probability of a major war within ten years: 35%.

Acts of nature represent the final wild card. Global temperature data shows warming at an accelerating pace. Extreme environmental events rose from fewer than 50 per year in 1970 to roughly 200 per year by 2020. The Notre Dame Global Adaptation Index identifies the most vulnerable nations as the Philippines, Indonesia, Vietnam, Bangladesh, and Thailand. India faces massive vulnerability due to geography and agricultural dependence. Humanity will likely respond slowly and reactively rather than proactively.

Looking ahead, three critical cycles within cycles converge. The short-term debt cycle (roughly eight years) suggests a downturn around the time of the next US presidential election. The internal political cycle exacerbates tensions with each election. And US-China conflicts continue escalating, with both sides pursuing five-year plans for greater self-sufficiency. Taiwan and the East and South China Seas present what Dalio called a “significant risk approaching conflict between an unstoppable force and an immovable object.”

Dalio’s core principles for navigating uncertainty begin with knowing all possibilities, thinking about worst-case scenarios, and eliminating intolerable ones. After his 1982 mistake nearly destroyed Bridgewater, he maintained a permanent “end of the world” portfolio covering depressions, devaluations, revolutions, wars, and pandemics. Second, diversify: reducing risk by up to 80% without reducing upside. The Chinese say a smart rabbit has three burrows. Third, defer gratification. Fourth, triangulate among the smartest people possible to stress-test your thinking.

“Whatever success I’ve had has been more due to my knowing how to deal with what I don’t know than anything I know.” —Ray Dalio

Key Takeaways

Empires Rise and Fall in a Predictable Big Cycle

Every empire follows the same arc: strong education produces innovation, which drives trade and economic output, building military strength and reserve currency status. The cycle lasts roughly 250 years, give or take 150. No system of government, no currency, and no empire lasts forever, yet almost everyone is surprised when they fail because the cycle exceeds one human lifetime.

Three Forces Are Converging Now

Massive debt requiring money printing, the largest internal wealth and values gaps in a century, and a rising power challenging the dominant one. Together, these three forces most closely resemble the 1930–1945 period. The US is roughly 70% through its Big Cycle, in Stage 5 of the internal order. China is in Stage 3, the peace and prosperity sweet spot.

Reserve Currency Status Lags Behind Fundamentals

The habit of using a currency persists long after the strengths that made it dominant have eroded. This lag makes the decline especially dangerous because the country looks stronger than it actually is. When the fall comes, it follows a pattern: slowly for a long time, then very quickly, as currency decline outpaces interest payments.

The Internal War Matters Most

The greatest war any country faces is the war with itself. Internal strength is the factor most within a country’s control and the most measurable. Bankrupt government finances combined with big wealth gaps is the single most reliable leading indicator of civil war or revolution. The US has 60–80% of red flags present.

Innovation Is the Only Sustainable Advantage

Humanity’s inventiveness is the most powerful force driving long-term progress. Whoever wins the technology war probably wins the economic and military wars. Quantum computing combined with AI will be to traditional computing what the computer was to the abacus. But the returns to innovation depend on how governments divide the productivity profits.

Prepare for What You Cannot Predict

Diversify across countries, currencies, and asset classes. Maintain a permanent “end of the world” portfolio. Bet on the upside from productivity improvements but never so aggressively that cycles knock you out of the game. A smart rabbit has three burrows.

Contrarian Ideas

The US Is in Late-Stage Decline, Not a Temporary Rough Patch

Conventional belief: America’s problems are cyclical and self-correcting. Dalio’s counter: the US matches 60–80% of the historical red flags for severe internal conflict, sits roughly 70% through the Big Cycle that has ended every previous empire, and has structural problems (unresolvable wealth gaps, unreformable bureaucracies, unsustainable debts) that have never been reversed by any empire at this stage without a painful restructuring.

China’s System Is More Adaptive Than You Think

Conventional belief: authoritarian systems are rigid and destined to fail. Counter: China has the longest continuous governance tradition on earth and has navigated Big Cycles repeatedly, collapsing and reconstituting itself multiple times. Its current system combines top-down control with market dynamism, collective over individual optimization, and multigenerational planning horizons that democracies cannot match. Eight times as many STEM graduates, four times the population collecting vastly more data for AI training.

Bonds Are the Most Dangerous Asset Class

Conventional belief: bonds are the safe part of a portfolio. Counter: holding debt late in the long-term debt cycle is holding a ticking time bomb. At the time of writing, US bonds required 45 years to return $100, Japanese bonds 150 years, European bonds likely never. All currencies devalue or die—of 750 currencies since 1700, only 20% survive, all devalued. Real returns on cash since 1912: negative.

Democracy Has a Structural Weakness

Conventional belief: democracy is inherently resilient. Counter: democracy requires consensus but produces fragmented decision-making. Leaders are selected through popularity contests. The biggest risk is that ineffectiveness drives revolution by populist autocrats, exactly as happened in Athens, Rome, Weimar Germany, and 1920s–30s Italy, Japan, and Spain.

Frameworks

The Big Cycle

Empires rise and fall over roughly 250 years through alternating periods of peaceful prosperity (5:1 ratio) and destructive transitions. Three nested cycles drive it: the long-term debt cycle (~100 years), the internal order cycle (~100 years), and the external order cycle. When all three align badly, the empire declines.

The Eight Determinants of Power

Education → Innovation → Competitiveness → Trade → Economic Output → Military Strength → Financial Center → Reserve Currency Status. Causal chain flows left to right. Reserve currency lags all others.

The Six Stages of the Long-Term Debt Cycle

Hard money → Claims on hard money → Increased debt → Debt crisis and defaults → Fiat money → Flight back to hard money. The cycle takes 50 to 75 years. Central banks always choose to print rather than allow deflationary collapse.

The Six Stages of Internal Order

New order consolidates power → Systems and bureaucracies built → Peace and prosperity (sweet spot) → Great excesses → Bad finances and intense conflict → Civil war or revolution. Duration: roughly 100 years. US currently in Stage 5.

The Multigenerational Psychological Cycle

Five stages: poor and think poor → rich but still think poor (most productive) → rich and think rich (arts flourish) → poorer but still think rich (bubbles form) → poor and think poor again. Duration matches the long-term debt cycle.

Four Market Determinants

All markets are driven by growth, inflation, risk premiums, and discount rates. Build portfolios diversified across these four drivers, tilted tactically based on where the cycle stands.

The Changing World Order: Why Nations Succeed and Fail by Ray Dalio (2021, 525 pages). A data-driven examination of the repeating Big Cycle that has governed the rise and decline of every major empire over the last 500 years, with particular focus on the converging forces threatening the current American-led order.

About the Author

Ray Dalio founded Bridgewater Associates in 1975, building it into the world’s largest hedge fund. His investment approach is rooted in understanding how economic and political machines work through studying historical patterns and building systematic models. Bridgewater’s flagship Pure Alpha fund has been one of the most successful in history, and Dalio’s principles-based approach to management and decision-making has influenced organizations far beyond finance.

Dalio’s engagement with China spans nearly four decades, beginning with his first visit in 1984 when per capita income was below $200 per year. He helped advise on the creation of Chinese financial markets, sent his son to live and study in Beijing, and developed relationships at every level of Chinese society and government. This unusual dual perspective— deep insider knowledge of both American and Chinese systems—is central to the book’s authority.

His previous books include Principles (2017) and How Countries Go Broke (2025), which extends the debt cycle analysis from this book into a detailed nine-stage framework. Dalio has committed to giving away the majority of his wealth through philanthropic efforts focused on education, ocean exploration, and microfinance.

Why This Book Matters

Most people plan their lives and investments based on the conditions they have experienced, which represents only a tiny fragment of what history has produced. This book demolishes that narrow frame by showing that the forces shaping our world today—unsustainable debt, widening inequality, great power rivalry— have appeared many times before and have always produced dramatic transitions. The value lies not in prediction but in pattern recognition: understanding where we are in the cycle changes how you invest, where you live, and how you prepare for what comes next.

The book’s most powerful contribution is making the case that the current American-led world order is not permanent, not special, and not exempt from the forces that ended every previous order. By quantifying empire strength across 18 determinants for 11 major countries, Dalio transforms vague anxieties about “American decline” into a measurable framework. The US scores highest overall but is declining on nearly every metric, while China is rising on nearly every one. Whether this transition happens peacefully or violently is the defining question of our era.

Published in 2021, the book’s predictions are now testable. Dalio estimated a major war probability of 35% within ten years and pointed to the 2026 timeframe as a critical risk window. Whether his specific forecasts prove accurate, the framework for thinking about these risks— rooted in 500 years of data across dozens of empires—is a tool that will remain useful long after the specific numbers are outdated.
