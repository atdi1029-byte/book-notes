## Introduction

### The Core Premise

**Ray Dalio** observed three major forces never seen in his lifetime but common in history:

1. **The Long-Term Debt and Capital Markets Cycle** — Huge debts, zero/near-zero interest rates, massive money printing in the three major reserve currencies
2. **The Internal Order and Disorder Cycle** — Largest wealth, political, and values gaps in ~100 years, especially in the US
3. **The External Order and Disorder Cycle** — China rising to challenge the US and existing world order

Most analogous period: **1930-1945**. The **COVID-19 pandemic** added another dimension — acts of nature (diseases, famines, floods) must be considered alongside depressions and wars.

### The Big Cycle Framework

> "All the empires and dynasties I studied rose and declined in a classic Big Cycle that has clear markers that allow us to see where we are in it."

The Big Cycle swings between:
1. **Peaceful/prosperous periods** of creativity and productivity (longer, ~5:1 ratio)
2. **Depression/revolution/war periods** of fighting over wealth and power (shorter, transitional)

Destructive periods act like cleansing storms — rid weaknesses and excesses (like too much debt), produce a new beginning on sounder footing. After conflict resolves, a **new world order** emerges.

Nested cycles within: long-term debt (~100 years), short-term debt (~8 years), shorter expansion/recession periods.

> "The swinging of conditions from one extreme to another in a cycle is the norm, not the exception."

> "When the cycles align, the tectonic plates of history shift, and the lives of all people change in big ways."

The really big boom and bust periods come ~once in a lifetime, so they surprise people who haven't studied history across generations. It was rare for a country in a rare century to not have at least one boom/prosperous period AND one depression/civil war/revolution period. Great empires typically lasted ~250 years (give or take 150).

### The 1971 Lesson and Archetypical Template Method

Age 22, clerking on the floor of the **New York Stock Exchange**. Sunday night, August 15, 1971 — President **Richard Nixon** announced the US would renege on its promise to allow paper dollars to be turned in for gold. Expected stocks to dive Monday morning; instead stock market jumped ~4% as the dollar plummeted. Hadn't experienced a currency devaluation before. Dug into history — saw many cases with similar effects.

Took a few more painful surprises to realize: needed to understand all the big economic and market moves in the last 100+ years across all major countries. From that learning came an **archetypical sequence** of how rises and declines typically happen. With that template: can watch events relative to archetype, bet on what typically comes next if on track, course correct if deviating.

> "I believe that the reason people typically miss the big moments of evolution coming at them in life is because they experience only tiny pieces of what's happening. We are like ants preoccupied with our jobs of carrying crumbs in our very brief lifetimes instead of having a broader perspective of the big-picture patterns and cycles."

From this perspective: only a limited number of personality types going down a limited number of paths, leading to a limited number of situations producing a limited number of stories that repeat over time. Only the clothes, languages, and technologies change.

### Background Studies Leading to This Book

1. **Money and Credit Cycles** — Studied the long-term debt/capital markets cycle (50-100 years). Central bank actions in 2008 (creating money, buying financial assets) mirrored 1930s — widened wealth gap → populism and conflict.

2. **Productivity and Structural Reform (2014)** — Developed 10-year growth estimates for 20 largest economies. Saw US leading indicators (education quality, indebtedness) worsening relative to **China** and **India**.

3. **Populism (2016)** — After **Trump** election. Highlighted how wealth/values gaps in the 1930s resemble today. Populists of left and right are more nationalistic, militaristic, protectionist, confrontational.

4. **The Two Economies** — Divided US economy into quintiles. Dramatic differences between top 40% and bottom 60%. Led to "Why and How Capitalism Needs to Be Reformed."

5. **China's Rise** — 37 years of direct engagement. Led China to become effective competitor with US in production, trade, technology, geopolitics, and world capital markets.

### Historical Research Scope

Looked at rises and declines of all major empires/currencies over the last 500 years. Focused most closely on three reserve currency empires:
1. **US Empire / US dollar** (current)
2. **British Empire / British pound** (previous)
3. **Dutch Empire / Dutch guilder** (before that)

Also studied Germany, France, Russia, Japan, China, India — China received most attention (history back to 600 CE). Used 30-year moving averages for long-term trends, unsmoothed for close-up developments. The book sacrifices precision for clarity.

## Chapter 1: The Big Cycle in a Tiny Nutshell

### Core Discovery

> "Biggest thing affecting most people in most countries through time: the struggle to make, take, and distribute wealth and power."

> "I'm on a mission to figure out how the world works and to gain timeless and universal principles for dealing with it well. It's both a passion and a necessity for me."

Throughout time and all countries: people with wealth own means of production, work with people who have political power in **symbiotic relationship**. Form evolved (monarchs/nobles as landowners → capitalists/elected or autocratic officials) but dynamics stayed the same. Over time, a small percentage gains exceptional wealth/power → becomes overextended → bad times hit the least wealthy hardest → conflicts produce revolutions/civil wars → new world order → cycle repeats.

No system of government, no economic system, no currency, and no empire lasts forever — yet almost everyone is surprised and ruined when they fail.

### Evolution, Cycles, and the Bumps Along the Way

> "Evolution is the upward movement toward improvement that occurs because of adaptation and learning. Around it are cycles."

> "Human productivity is the most important force in causing the world's total wealth, power, and living standards to rise over time."

**Productivity** (output per person) driven by learning, inventiveness, work ethic, economic systems. Has risen at different rates for different people, always for same reasons. The shift from slower to faster productivity gains came from **Gutenberg's printing press** (mid-15th century, already in use in China for centuries) → broader learning → **Renaissance** → **Scientific Revolution** → **Enlightenment** → **capitalism** → **First Industrial Revolution in Britain**.

The broader shift moved wealth/power from **agriculture-based** (landownership = principal source of power, monarchs/nobles/clergy working together) to **industry-based** (inventive capitalists created/owned means of production, worked with government). Since the Industrial Revolution: wealth/power come primarily from combination of **education, inventiveness, and capitalism**, with those who run governments working with those who control most wealth and education. How evolution happens also continues to evolve: agricultural land was worth most → machines and what they produced → now **digital things** (data, information processing) evolving to become worth most — creating fight over who obtains data and how they use it.

> "While significant, because these learnings and productivity improvements are evolutionary, they don't cause big abrupt shifts. The big abrupt shifts come from booms, busts, revolutions, and wars, which are primarily driven by cycles."

### The Formula for Success Throughout Time

> "Throughout time, the formula for success has been a system in which well-educated people, operating civilly with each other, come up with innovations, receive funding through capital markets, and own the means by which their innovations are turned into the production and allocation of resources, allowing them to be rewarded by profit making."

However, capitalism has created wealth/opportunity gaps and overindebtedness that lead to economic downturns, revolutions, and wars. How bad these periods get depends on how strong countries are — those with large savings, low debts, and strong reserve currencies can endure better.

### The Classic Money and Credit Cycle

The 1930-45 period: economy fell ~10%, stock market fell ~85%. Part of a cycle happening since recorded history — too much debt → credit collapse → central bank prints money → creates new monetary system.

**FDR's response**: Stock market and economy bottomed the day newly elected President **Franklin D. Roosevelt** announced he would default on the government's promise to let people turn in money for gold, and the government would create enough money/credit. Took **3.5 years** from October 1929 crash. Three-year recovery typical.

The 1907-19 period followed the same pattern: **Gilded Age** (US) / **Belle Epoque** (continental Europe) / **Victorian Era** (Britain) → debt bubble → **Panic of 1907** → wealth redistribution → WWI → **Spanish flu** → **Treaty of Versailles** (1919) → new world order → Roaring '20s boom → 1930-45 period.

> "These periods of destruction/reconstruction devastated the weak, made clear who the powerful were, and established revolutionary new approaches to doing things that set the stage for periods of prosperity that eventually became overextended."

### Historical Empire Trajectories

Chart shows 11 leading empires over 500 years: US, UK, Netherlands, China, Germany, France, Spain, Japan, Russia, India, Ottoman Empire.

- **China**: Dominant for centuries (consistently most powerful before ~1850), entered steep decline in 1800s
- **Netherlands**: Small country became world's reserve currency empire in 1600s
- **UK**: Peaked in 1800s
- **US**: Rose over last 150 years, particularly during/after WWII
- **Current moment**: US in relative decline, China rising again

Extended to 600 CE: China almost always the most powerful pre-1500. Also prominent: Middle Eastern caliphates, French, Mongols, Spanish, Ottomans.

Important caveat: leading powers weren't necessarily the "best-off" countries. Little correlation between wealth/power and happiness. "Boutique countries" (Switzerland, Singapore) achieve great wealth without becoming biggest empires.

### Eight Determinants of Wealth and Power

Composite index = roughly equal average of 18 measures of strength. Eight key determinants:
1. **Education**
2. **Innovation and technology**
3. **Competitiveness**
4. **Economic output**
5. **Share of world trade**
6. **Military strength**
7. **Financial center strength**
8. **Reserve currency status**

Causal chain: Education → innovation → trade/military → economic output → financial center → reserve currency. **Reserve currency lags** — habit of usage outlasts the strengths that made it common.

> "Strengths and weaknesses are mutually reinforcing."

### The Rise Phase

Characteristics: low indebtedness, small wealth/values/political gaps, people working together, good education/infrastructure, strong leadership, peaceful world order. Strong education includes character, civility, work ethic — taught in families, schools, religious institutions.

Innovation flourishes — the **Dutch at peak** invented quarter of all major inventions, ships for global trade, **capitalism as we know it**. Created first publicly listed company (**Dutch East India Company**) and first stock market. Must be open to best thinking in the world.

Financial centers: **Amsterdam** → **London** → **New York** → **Shanghai** (developing). All greatest empires used **capitalist approach** — even China (state capitalism). Needs developing capital markets (lending, bond, stock markets).

Reserve currency status: enables borrowing more at lower rates. All greatest empires followed this path.

### The Top and Decline Phases

**At the top**, seeds of decline embedded in success:
- Workers earn more → become expensive → less competitive
- Other countries copy methods/technologies (British hired Dutch ship designers to build cheaper, better ships)
- People don't work as hard, pursue leisure, become decadent
- Values change from those who fought for wealth to those who inherited it
- Financial bubbles form from betting on good times continuing
- Wealth gaps self-reinforce; rich influence politics, give privileges to children
- As long as living standards of most people still rising, resentments don't boil over

**Reserve currency gives "exorbitant privilege"** (term coined by **Valery Giscard d'Estaing**) — borrows more, boosts short-term power but weakens long-term finances.

> "When borrowing and spending are strong, the empire appears very strong, but its finances are in fact being weakened because the borrowing sustains the country's power beyond its fundamentals."

Empire becomes unprofitable: costs of maintaining/defending exceed revenue. Example: British Empire became massive, bureaucratic, lost competitive advantages as Germany soared → expensive arms race → world war. Richer countries borrow from poorer savers (US started borrowing from China in 1980s when per capita income was 40x China's).

**The decline** comes "gradually and then suddenly":
- Debts too large + economic downturn → can't borrow → must default or print money
- Nearly always prints money → devalues currency → raises inflation
- Political extremism: populism of left (redistribute wealth) and right (maintain it) — "anti-capitalist phase"
- Capital flight → taxes on rich rise → rich flee → tax revenue falls → hollowing out
- Country outlaws capital flight → those seeking to get out panic
- Productivity collapses → shrinks pie → more conflict → populist leaders → civil war/revolution

> "The decline phase typically comes from internal economic weakness together with internal fighting, or from costly external fighting, or both."

**External decline**: Rising rival exploits domestic weakness. Defending against rivals requires great military spending even as domestic economy deteriorates. No viable system for peacefully adjudicating international disputes. Hard choice: fight (risk losing) or retreat (shows weakness).

> "When all of these forces line up — indebtedness, civil war/revolution at home, war abroad, and a loss of faith in the currency — a change in the world order is typically at hand."

### What Are Destruction/Reconstruction Periods Like?

Without minimizing the suffering: history shows typically the majority of people stay employed in depressions, are unharmed in shooting wars, and survive natural disasters. Some described these periods as bringing important good things — drawing people closer, building strength of character, learning to appreciate basics. **Tom Brokaw** called people who went through 1930-45 "the Greatest Generation." Economic destruction and war periods typically don't last very long — roughly **two or three years**. Rarely gets all three types of big crises (economic, revolution/war, natural disaster) at same time.

> "Humanity's power to adapt and quickly get to new and higher levels of well-being is much greater than all the bad stuff that can be thrown at us."

### The 1945 World Order

Created: **Bretton Woods** monetary system (1944), **United Nations** (New York), **World Bank** and **IMF** (Washington). US was richest (two-thirds of world's gold), dominant economic power (~half of world production), strongest military (nuclear monopoly + strongest conventional forces).

At time of writing, 75 years later — classically approaching end of long-term debt cycle with large debts and typical monetary policies not working well. Politically fragmented central governments giving out a lot of money they are borrowing; central banks printing to monetize government debt. Big wealth/values gaps, rising world power competing, and a pandemic.

Bad periods of destruction/restructuring: typically **10-20 years**. Peaceful periods: typically **40-80 years**. Both with large variations. Historical transitions (Dutch → British, British → US) always involved: debt crisis, internal revolution (peaceful or violent), external war, currency breakdown, new domestic and world order.

## Chapter 2: The Determinants

### The Perpetual-Motion Machine

Model based on studying 11 leading empires of last 500 years, 20 most important countries of last 100 years, and major Chinese dynasties over 1,400 years. Also glanced at Roman, Greek, Egyptian, Byzantine, Mongol, Han, Sui, Arab, Persian empires.

Two core components: (1) existing conditions (domestic/world orders), (2) timeless and universal forces causing changes. Determinants lead to effects that become subsequent determinants, forming chains. Most people focus on existing conditions; the focus should be on the timeless forces that produce change.

> "If we can understand those determinants, we can understand how the machine works and anticipate what will likely be coming at us next."

Methodology: examines history both quantitatively (measuring conditions to objectively determine cause/effect) and qualitatively (not everything can be measured). Example: lot of debt + tight money = debt crisis. When three big cycles align badly → typically leads to decline.

> "While I measure and describe the determinants discretely, they are not separate. They interact with each other and blend together, typically reinforcing each other and the whole cycle's rise and fall."

### The 3, The 5, The 8, and The 18

**Three Big Cycles:**
1. Good/bad finances (capital markets cycle)
2. Internal order/disorder (wealth/values gaps)
3. External order/disorder (competitiveness between powers)

When all three simultaneously good → country strong and rising. When all three simultaneously bad → country weak and declining.

**Big Five** adds: 4. Innovation/technology, 5. Acts of nature

**Eight Powers:** Education, Innovation & Technology, Cost Competitiveness, Military Strength, Trade, Economic Output, Markets/Financial Center, Reserve Currency Status

**Full 18** adds: Geology/Geography, Resource-Allocation Efficiency, Acts of Nature, Infrastructure/Investment, Character/Civility/Determination, Governance/Rule of Law, Gaps in Wealth/Opportunity/Values

Exercise: rank each country 1-10 for each attribute. Higher total = greater probability of rising.

### Dynamics to Track (Qualitative Indicators)

- Self-Interest: Country vs. Individual orientation
- Desire to Gain Wealth/Power: Important vs. Not important
- Learning from History: Extensive vs. Limited
- Multigenerational Psychological Cycle: Strong vs. Decadent
- Decision-Making Time Frame: Long-Term vs. Short-Term
- Leadership: Competent vs. Weak
- Openness to Global Thinking: Open vs. Closed
- Culture: Productive vs. Unproductive
- Class Relationships: Cooperative vs. Divisive
- Political Left/Right: Moderate vs. Partisan
- Prisoner's Dilemma: Cooperative vs. At War
- Relationships: Win-Win vs. Lose-Lose
- Balance of Power: Favorable vs. Unfavorable
- Peace/War Cycle: Peace vs. War

### Inherited Determinants

**Geography**: US and China — large land bounded by natural barriers → unity, shared language/government/culture. Europe — many natural boundaries → divisions into different states with different languages/governments.

**Geology**: Natural resources important but shouldn't be overvalued vs. human capital. Every commodity has declined in inflation-adjusted value over time with big cycles. Middle Eastern wealth may fall as world turns from fossil fuels. Most vulnerable position: high reliance on one or few commodities.

**Acts of Nature**: **Black Death** killed 75-200 million (~1350). **Smallpox** killed 300+ million in 20th century (double war deaths). Droughts and floods caused massive famines. Catastrophes tend to come unexpectedly, act as stress tests revealing underlying strengths/weaknesses.

**Genealogy**: Evidence indicates only ~15% or less of behavioral variations between populations could potentially be explained by genetic differences. Relatively minor determinant compared to other influences.

### Human Capital Determinants

> "Human capital is the most sustainable capital because inherited assets that are drawn down eventually disappear, whereas human capital can exist forever."

Humans who generate more revenue than they expend = good human capital. Societies without it either drawing down resources or getting deeper into debt. Examples of human capital superiority: **Elon Musk/Tesla** vs. GM/Ford/Chrysler, **Steve Jobs/Bill Gates** vs. IBM. Allowed small countries (**Netherlands, England, Switzerland, Singapore**) to achieve great wealth.

### Key Human Nature Determinants

**Self-Interest**: Which "self" matters most (individual → family → tribe → state → country → empire → humankind) drives behaviors. When people willing to die for country, country more likely protected. Before ~1650, tribes and states more important than countries. World now moving from globalist to nationalist. US losing cohesion — people migrating to states aligning with preferences.

**Drive to Gain Wealth/Power**: Real wealth (house, car) has intrinsic value; financial wealth (bonds, stocks) has none. Being continuously productive matters more than expropriation — Russia post-1917 became poor quickly. Spending on investment/infrastructure (vs. consumption) → greater productivity. Wealth = power through symbiotic relationship between wealth-holders and political power-holders. **British East India Company (1717)** brought together financial capital, commercial capabilities, military capabilities — forced India's Mughal emperor to trade, first step toward British colonization.

> "When an individual, organization, country, or empire spends more than what it earns, misery and turbulence ahead."

**Capital Markets**: How well developed determines a country's success.

**Learning from History**: Chinese excellent at this. Most people don't study beyond their own experience. Peace/boom at beginning of cycle is opposite to war/bust at end — what people face later in lives more likely opposite than similar to what they encountered earlier.

**Multigenerational Psychological Cycle**: "From shirtsleeves to shirtsleeves in three generations." ~Length of typical long-term debt cycle. Five stages:

- **Stage 1**: Poor, think poor — subsistence, no debt (nobody wants to lend), value money highly. "Early-stage emerging countries." Evolution to next stage typically ~one generation. Examples: **Asian Tigers** (Hong Kong, Singapore, Taiwan, South Korea), then China.
- **Stage 2**: Rich, still think poor — most productive phase. Still work hard, save a lot, invest efficiently. Parents educate children well. Rapidly rising income and productivity growth simultaneously. Inflation not a problem, country becomes more competitive. Signs: gleaming new cities, high savings rates, rising foreign exchange reserves. "Late-stage emerging countries" — big countries emerging as great world powers.
- **Stage 3**: Rich, think rich — labor expensive but productivity sustains living standards. Priorities shift from working/saving to savoring finer things. Arts and sciences flourish. Reinforced as new generation (didn't experience bad times) becomes larger percentage. Signs: reduced work hours (six-day to five-day week), luxury spending increases. "Peak health countries." US 1950-1965. China now moving into it. Almost always become world economic/military powers.
- **Stage 4**: Poorer, still think rich — debts rise relative to income. People from first two stages died off. Workers earn/spend a lot → expensive → slower real income growth but reluctant to constrain spending. Lower savings, increase debts. Reduced investment in infrastructure/R&D slows productivity. Rely on reputation rather than competitiveness. Run "twin deficits." Bubbles form. "Early declining countries."
- **Stage 5**: Poor, think poor — bubbles burst, private debts grow, negative cycle. Government debt/deficits grow, central bank printing increases. Low real interest rates, weak currencies, poor economic conditions. Compete with less expensive countries in earlier stages. "Clearly declined countries." Romans, Greeks never recovered; Chinese have a few times.

**Short-Term Gratification**: Human propensity to choose short-term enjoyment over long-term well-being naturally exaggerates highs and lows. Pulls good times forward at expense of future, most classically by creating debt boom/bust cycle. Governments especially vulnerable because: (a) politicians motivated to prioritize near-term, (b) don't like facing limitations and difficult financial trade-offs (e.g., choosing military vs. social programs), (c) politically threatening to tax people.

**Humanity's Inventiveness**: Main driver of productivity. Capacity to invent solutions proven far more powerful than all problems combined. Knowledge gained more than lost — advances more in spurts and sputters than in cycles with downs. Spurts during upward swings of Big Cycle (Renaissance periods of great creativity); sputters during downward swings. In market-based system, most powerful way to innovate = bring new ideas to market, commercialize, profit. Marketplace incredibly efficient at weeding out bad ideas, pricing good ones.

> **Innovation + Commercial Spirit + Thriving Capital Markets = Great Productivity Gains = Increases in Wealth and Power**

### Culture, Openness, and Leadership

**Culture**: "Culture is destiny." Cultural differences — how people believe they should be with each other — matter enormously. Individuals like **Jesus, Confucius, Mohammed, Buddha, Mahavira, Guru Nanak, Plato, Socrates, Marx** conveyed approaches captured in foundational texts: Hebrew Bible, New Testament, Talmud, Quran, I Ching, Five Books and Four Classics, Analects, Upanishads, Bhagavad Gita, *Meditations*, *Republic*, *Wealth of Nations*, *Das Kapital*. These plus discoveries of scientists, artists, politicians, diplomats, investors, psychologists encountering realities and adapting → determine a people's culture.

**Openness to Global Thinking**: Good leading indicator of strength because isolated entities miss out on world's best practices and don't face off against world's best competitors. History littered with cases of isolated countries — sometimes by choice to protect cultures (late Tang, late Ming, early PRC periods in China; Edo period in Japan), sometimes due to natural disasters and internal fighting. Both lead to falling behind in technology with terrible consequences. One of most common reasons for empires and dynasties failing.

**Leadership**: Everything influenced by people in leadership positions. Life like game of chess or Chinese board game Go — every move helps determine outcome, some players know how to make better moves. In reading history: course changed by uniqueness of relatively few people in key areas. In each generation, roughly few hundred people made all the difference — in government, sciences, finance/commerce, arts. Studying what these key people were like and what they did in different situations helps understand how the perpetual-motion machine works.

### Class Struggles and Political Cycles

For almost all recorded history, very small ruling classes controlled most wealth/power. Those with wealth influence those with power and vice versa. All internal orders run by certain classes in symbiotic relationships — aligned not to disrupt the order but struggle with each other and with non-elites.

> "The poor and the rich quarrel with one another, and whichever side gets the better, instead of establishing a just or popular government, regards political supremacy as the prize of victory." — **Aristotle**

**England ~1200**: Nobles vs. monarchy over taxes. **King John** wanted more tax, nobles wanted to give less. Civil war → nobles won → first Parliament → **Magna Carta** (1215). Law didn't matter much relative to power → more fighting → new Magna Carta under **Henry III** (1225). Struggles continued constantly.

**15th-17th centuries**: Global exploration and colonialism (Portuguese, Spanish), invention of capitalism (stocks and bonds) and labor-saving machines fueling Industrial Revolutions (Dutch, then British). Shifted wealth FROM landowning nobles + monarchies TO capitalists + elected representatives or autocratic leaders. Almost all countries made these shifts — some peacefully, most painfully.

**France 17th-18th centuries**: King ruled with three classes — clergy (2%), nobility (2%), commoners (98%). First two had more votes than commoners. **Ancien regime** overthrown via **French Revolution** (began May 5, 1789).

**Political Left/Right Cycle**: Swings in roughly **10-year cycles**. Right: self-sufficiency, hard work, limited government, private sector > public sector. Left: helping each other, government support, sharing wealth. Both have blind spots.

> "The greatest challenge for policy makers is to engineer a capitalist economic system that raises productivity and living standards without worsening inequities and instabilities."

**Balance of Power Cycle**: Step 1: Formation of alliances ("enemy of my enemy is my friend"). Step 2: War to determine winners/losers (only when roughly equal power + existential differences; **mutually assured destruction** creates standoffs; 2020 US election example — roughly equal power, irreconcilable differences, fought within constitutional rules). Step 3: Fighting among the winners ("purge" stage — **Reign of Terror**, **Red Terror**, US-Soviet post-WWII split, Chinese communists vs. nationalists). Step 4: Peace and prosperity. Step 5: Increasing conflict leads to revolutionary changes.

> "Those societies that draw on the widest range of people and give them responsibilities based on their merits rather than privileges are the most sustainably successful."

Current class dynamics: AI and information technology developers evolving to gain more wealth and power than other classes.

## Chapter 3: The Big Cycle of Money, Credit, Debt, and Economic Activity

> "If you don't understand how money and credit work, you can't understand how the system works."

Understanding money/credit was "the biggest missing piece" for even the most renowned historians, heads of state, and central bankers in understanding history.

### Basic Financial Realities

All entities have money in (revenue) and money out (expenses). One entity's spending = another's income; one entity's debts = another's assets. **Debt eats equity** — creditor gets paid ahead of owner. But **central banks can feed debt by printing money**.

No fixed amount of money and credit in existence — central banks can easily create them. When created and spent, goods/services/assets go up in price. Also creates debt that must be repaid → money, credit, debt, and economic activity are inherently cyclical.

**Reserve currencies**: Not all money governments print is of equal value. At time of writing, world's dominant reserve currency is **US dollar** (US Federal Reserve). Much less important: **euro** (European Central Bank). **Japanese yen, Chinese renminbi, British pound** relatively small, though renminbi quickly growing.

> "Having a reserve currency is great while it lasts because it gives a country exceptional borrowing and spending power and significant power over who else in the world gets the money and credit needed to buy and sell internationally."

However, having reserve currency typically sows seeds of its own loss — allows overborrowing, creation of lots of money to service debt debases currency. Non-reserve currency countries often need dollars for debt service — when they can't earn them, they can go bankrupt.

**The biggest current problem**: For many people, companies, nonprofits, and governments, incomes are low relative to expenses, and debts/liabilities (pensions, healthcare, insurance) are very large relative to assets. Often doesn't seem that way because they spend a lot and look rich while going broke.

### Financial Economy vs. Real Economy

> "There is both a financial economy and a real economy. Though they are related, they are different."

**Real economy**: supply/demand of goods/services. **Financial economy**: central banks adjust money/credit to raise/lower demand. Can't do it perfectly → short-term debt cycles.

> "You cannot create more wealth simply by creating more money and credit. To create more wealth, you have to be more productive."

Creating money/credit doesn't increase actual wealth — house price going up from money printing means same house, calculated wealth up but actual wealth unchanged. Money is stimulative when given out, depressing when repaid.

Central banks vary costs and availability of money/credit to control markets and economy. When economy growing too fast → make money scarce and expensive (encourages lending over spending). When too little growth → make money cheap and plentiful (encourages borrowing and investing). These variations cause prices/quantities of goods/services/assets to rise and fall.

> "Think of the central bank as having a bottle of stimulant it can inject into the economy as needed."

Short-term debt cycles ~8 years ("the business cycle"). Most people have seen enough to know what they're like, mistakenly think they'll go on working this way forever.

> "I distinguish them from the long-term debt cycle, which typically plays out over 50 to 75 years (and so contains about six to 10 short-term debt cycles). Because the crises that occur as these long-term debt cycles play out happen only once in a lifetime, most people don't expect them."

Current long-term cycle designed at **Bretton Woods (1944)**, began 1945.

### What Is Money

**Money**: medium of exchange and storehold of wealth. Difference between money and debt: money settles claims; debt is a promise to deliver money.

> "Most money and credit have no intrinsic value. They are just journal entries in an accounting system that can easily be changed."

Lending is good for society when used productively. But periodically the system breaks down — currency supply "monetized," values fall or are destroyed.

### The Long-Term Debt Cycle (Six Stages)

**Stage 1: Hard Money** — No trust required for exchange. "Gold is the only financial asset that isn't someone else's liability." When countries at war and no trust, can still pay in gold.

**Stage 2: Claims on Hard Money** — Credible parties (banks, temples in China) put hard money in safe place, issue paper claims. "Linked currency system." People treat claims as if they are money.

**Stage 3: Increased Debt** — Holders of paper claims loan them to banks for interest. Banks lend to others at higher rate, making profit. Borrowers get buying power they didn't have. Whole society likes it — asset prices and production rise. Everyone happy — lending and borrowing booms repeatedly. Claims on money multiply far beyond actual hard money in bank.

> "Trouble approaches when there isn't enough income to service one's debts, or when the amount of claims people are holding in the expectation that they can sell them to get money to buy goods and services increases faster than the amount of goods and services by an amount that makes the conversion impossible."

**Stage 4: Debt Crises, Defaults, Devaluations** — Holders of debt try to convert to real money/goods → "run" occurs. Central bank faced with choice: allow money outflow (raises rates, worsens problems) or print money. Inevitably breaks link, prints, devalues. **Beautiful deleveraging**: buys enough debt to ease depression without triggering inflation. Private banks must default or get bailed out; central banks can devalue claims (pay back 50-70%) if debt in national currency. If debt denominated in foreign currency, even central banks must default.

**Stage 5: Fiat Money** — Paper currency with no hard asset backing. The 1971 dollar delinking: **August 15, 1971**, Nixon on TV. Pre-1971 US spent heavily on "guns and butter" (military + social programs), financed by borrowing. Claims on gold far exceeded actual gold reserves. Astute investors turned in claims. Led to dollar-based fiat system → 1970s inflation → 1980s debt crisis.

> "When credit cycles reach their limit, it is the logical and classic response for central governments and central banks to create a lot of debt and print money."

Happened in **2008** (rates hit 0%), **2020** (COVID), **1929-32**. Money printing speed: 2008 crisis took two months from crash to printing; 2020 took just weeks. At time of writing, debt/money creation at levels greater than any time since WWII.

**Stage 6: Flight Back to Hard Money** — Overprinting causes selling of debt assets, fleeing to gold, silver, stocks, foreign currencies. Governments outlaw gold ownership, impose foreign exchange controls, eliminate ability to transact in foreign currencies. Eventually debt wiped out, government links money back to gold or hard currency. Example: after Weimar Republic destruction, money became backed by gold-denominated assets and land. After Argentina crisis (late 1980s), money linked to dollar.

### Four Levers for Reducing Debt

1. **Austerity** (deflationary, too painful, doesn't last)
2. **Debt defaults/restructurings** (deflationary, painful — one person's debts are another's assets)
3. **Transfers/raising taxes** (politically challenging but more tolerable)
4. **Printing money and devaluing** (most expedient, least understood, most common — hard to identify harmed parties)

> "When governments print a lot of money and buy a lot of debt, they cheapen both, which essentially taxes those who own it."

When holders realize what's happening, they sell debt assets and borrow money to get into debt they can pay back with cheap money. They shift wealth into better storeholds: **gold, certain stocks, another country not having these problems**. Central banks continue printing while outlawing flow into inflation-hedge assets, alternative currencies, and alternative places.

**Reflation dynamics**: Either stimulates new money/credit expansion that finances economic expansion (good for stocks), or devalues money producing monetary inflation (good for inflation-hedge assets: gold, commodities, inflation-linked bonds). Early in long-term debt cycle (low debt, room to lower rates) → strong likelihood of credit/economic growth. Late in cycle (large debt, little room to stimulate) → much greater likelihood of monetary inflation with economic weakness.

> "All currencies devalue or die, and when they do, cash and bonds are devalued or wiped out."

Holding debt late in cycle = holding ticking time bomb. Big blowup happens roughly **every 50-75 years**. **Old Testament**: Year of Jubilee every 50 years, debts forgiven. Ironically, the closer people are to the blowup, the safer they feel.

## Chapter 4: The Changing Value of Money

### All Currencies Devalue or Die

Of ~750 currencies since 1700, only ~20% remain, and all have been devalued. Some completely wiped out (countries with hyperinflation/lost wars), some merged (European currencies → euro), some remain but devalued (pound, dollar). If you went back to 1850: Germany would use gulden or thaler, Japan might use koban or ryo.

Currency devaluation measured against: gold (timeless alternative currency), consumer price baskets (buying power), other currencies, stocks.

### Six Major Devaluation Periods

1. **1860s US Civil War**: Suspended gold convertibility, printed "greenbacks" to monetize war debts
2. **Mid-1870s**: US returned to gold peg. Most currencies fixed against gold until WWI. Exceptions: Japan (silver-linked until 1890s), Spain (frequently suspended convertibility)
3. **WWI (1914-18)**: Heavy borrowing to fund war. **Paris Peace Conference (1918)** attempted new order around League of Nations but couldn't forestall debt crises from war indemnities and inter-Allied war debts
4. **Post-WWI (1918-23)**: **Germany — most iconic hyperinflation** (Weimar Republic). **Spanish flu** 1918-20. Virtually every country devalued except US. Led to new world order → Roaring '20s → debt/asset bubbles → large wealth gaps
5. **1930s-WWII**: 1930-33 global debt crisis → money printing → competitive devaluations in virtually every country. Post-war: money/debt completely wiped out for losers (Germany, Japan, Italy, China), severely devalued for winners (UK, France). US gained wealth in gold form during war
6. **1968-73**: Dollar-gold link broken (most importantly 1971). Claims on gold far exceeded gold available. Led to fiat system → big increase in dollar-denominated money/credit → 1970s inflation → 1980s debt crisis
7. **2000-present**: Money value fallen relative to gold due to money/credit creation and low rates. Free-floating system — devaluation more gradual and continuous

### Historical Returns

**1850-present**: Average annual return of interest-earning cash: **1.2%**; gold: **0.9%**. Positive real return in about half of countries. Germany: total wipeout twice. Most real returns came during prosperity periods on gold standards (Second Industrial Revolution).

**1912-present (fiat era)**: Real return for bills: **-0.1%**; gold: **1.6%**. Positive real return in only half of countries. Over 2%/year loss in France, Italy, Japan; over **18%/year in Germany** due to hyperinflation.

> "History has shown that there are very large risks in holding interest-earning cash currency as a storehold of wealth, especially late in debt cycles."

After 1971 shift to fiat: central banks had unconstrained ability to create money → high inflation, low real rates. Big gold appreciation until 1980-81 when interest rates raised significantly above inflation. Gold fell until 2000. Since 2000: central banks pushed rates down, printed money, bought financial assets — supporting gold.

### Currency Devaluation vs. Reserve Currency Status Loss

Key distinction: not the same, though both caused by debt crises. Loss of reserve currency status is product of chronic large devaluations. Typically occurs when:
- Already established loss of economic/political primacy to a rising rival (Netherlands → UK, UK → US)
- Large and growing debts monetized by central bank printing
- Weakening currency in self-reinforcing run that can't be stopped

All major economies examined experienced more claims on central bank than hard currency available to satisfy them. Net central bank reserves start falling **prior** to actual devaluation, sometimes years ahead (UK suspended convertibility in 1947 ahead of 1949 devaluation; US suspended in 1971). Runs on currency typically occur alongside significant debt problems, often related to wartime spending (Fourth Anglo-Dutch War for Netherlands, World Wars for UK, Vietnam War for US). Central banks initially let short-term rates rise, but too economically painful, so they quickly capitulate and increase money supply.

Key variable: how much economic and military power country retains at time of devaluation — more power = more willing savers to continue holding money there.

**Dutch guilder**: massive and relatively quick collapse (<decade) during steep national decline
**UK pound**: more gradual, took two devaluations, assets significantly underperformed US assets
**US dollar**: two big abrupt devaluations (1933, 1971) plus gradual post-2000, but hasn't lost reserve status

## Chapter 5: The Big Cycle of Internal Order and Disorder

> "How people are with each other is the primary driver of the outcomes they get."

> "The biggest thing affecting most people in most countries through time is how people struggle to make, take, and distribute wealth and power."

### The Six Stages of the Internal Cycle

1. **New order begins, new leadership consolidates power**
2. **Resource-allocation systems and bureaucracies built/refined**
3. **Peace and prosperity** (sweet spot)
4. **Great excesses in spending/debt, widening gaps**
5. **Very bad financial conditions, intense conflict**
6. **Civil wars/revolutions** → back to Stage 1

> "As with cancer, it is best to stop the progress before getting into the later stages."

Typical duration: ~100 years (give or take a lot). Within each: short-term debt cycles (~8 years), political cycles moving control between right and left. Different countries in different stages simultaneously — this is the primary determinant of world order. How well people facing circumstances understand and adapt affects outcomes within range of possibilities. Different cultures have different approaches. Leaders and cultures who understand and adapt produce much better outcomes.

> "Every country is now going through them, and many of them are in different stages. For example, China and India are in very different stages than the United States and most European countries."

At time of writing, **US in Stage 5**: bad financial conditions and intensifying conflict while still having great strengths (technology, military) declining on relative basis. **China in "The Rise" phase.** US has 60-80% of economic red flags present → ~**1-in-6 chance** of severe internal conflict. When >80%: ~**1-in-3 chance** of civil war or revolution.

### Stage 1: Consolidation of Power

Revolutions come in two parts: (1) fight to bring down existing leaders, (2) "purges" — fight among winners and removal of opposition. Intensity varies, usually proportional to intensity of revolution.

Examples of brutal post-revolution periods: **Reign of Terror** (France, post-1789), **Red Terror** (Russia, post-1917), **Anti-Rightist Campaign** (China, post-1949). China's Cultural Revolution happened **17 years after** Communist Party took power.

At best: like period after US Civil War or during peaceful Roosevelt revolution of 1930s.

Best leaders: "consolidators of power" — **Tang Taizong, Caesar Augustus, US Founding Fathers, Napoleon, Bismarck**.

### Stage 2: Building Systems ("Early Prosperity")

System/institution building paramount. Must produce prosperity for most people, especially the large middle class. **Aristotle** (*Politics*): "Those states are likely to be well-administered in which middle class is large."

Best leaders: "civil engineers" — very different from revolutionary fighters. Winston Churchill and Mao were great "inspirational generals" and lousy "civil engineers." Examples: **Konrad Adenauer** (Germany), **Lee Kuan Yew** (Singapore), **Deng Xiaoping** (China).

Extraordinary leaders who took countries through Stages 6, 1, and 2 at scale:
- **Tang Taizong** (~150 years of peace, China became world's largest/strongest)
- **Caesar Augustus** (~200 years of peace, Rome became world's largest empire)
- **Genghis Khan** (century+ of prosperity, though failure to establish succession produced civil wars)

### Stage 3: Peace and Prosperity ("Mid-Prosperity")

> "I also call this phase 'mid-prosperity.' It is the sweet spot of the internal order cycle."

Conditions improving for almost everyone. Most of next generation better off than most of prior generation. Wide access to education, merit-based placements. Successful entrepreneurs and inventors become heroes. Debt growth fuels productivity/income growth, making debts easy to service. Incomes exceed expenses, savings exceed liabilities, savings financing investment in future.

Examples: Victorian Era, German Empire late 1800s (rapid industrialization, quickly strengthening economy).

Best leaders: "inspirational visionaries" — **William Gladstone** (strict budget controls, supported general population, "The People's William"), **Otto von Bismarck** (united 39 states into Germany, navigated international relations brilliantly, avoided debilitating wars), **Lee Kuan Yew** (Singapore 1959-2015, created principles for long-term success), **John F. Kennedy** (inspired moon mission, civil rights, contained opposition), **Deng Xiaoping** (transitioned communist system to productive state capitalism, "it is glorious to be rich," "doesn't matter if cat is black or white as long as it catches mice," reduced poverty, increased life expectancy).

### Stage 4: Period of Excesses ("Bubble Prosperity")

Debt growth outpaces future cash flows' capacity to service debts. Bubbles created because investors/business leaders/financial intermediaries/policymakers assume future will resemble past — mistakenly believe investments that have risen are good rather than expensive. Borrowing to buy drives up prices, reinforcing bubble process. As assets rise in value, net worth and spending-to-income levels rise, increasing borrowing capacity, supporting the leveraging-up spiral.

Shift from investment to consumption — more on luxury goods, less on profitable investments. Reduced infrastructure/R&D slows productivity, leads to aging cities. Heavy military spending to expand/protect global interests. Balance of payments deterioration. Large wealth/opportunity gaps emerge.

Examples: Japan 1988-90, US 1929, US 2006-07, Brazil/Latin American commodity producers 1977-79.

Decadent leaders: **Emperor Nero** (confiscated land for expansive palace), **Louis XIV** (expanded Versailles while people suffered), **Ming's Wanli Emperor** (withdrew from governing to build his own immense tomb).

### Stage 5: Bad Financial Conditions and Intense Conflict

**Classic toxic mix**: (1) country/people in bad financial shape (big debt and non-debt obligations), (2) large income/wealth/values gaps, (3) severe negative economic shock.

> "Single most reliable leading indicator of civil war or revolution is **bankrupt government finances combined with big wealth gaps**."

Financial problems hit private sector first, then public sector. Government's financial condition matters most — governments never let private sector's problems sink entire system. When government runs out of buying power, collapse occurs. But on the way to collapse: lot of fighting for money and political power.

Classic marker: government has large deficits creating more debt than buyers (other than central bank) willing to buy. Two paths:

1. **Can print money** (have central bank): Always do so because less painful than alternatives. Leads investors to flee money/debt being printed, depreciates currency.
2. **Can't print money**: Must raise taxes/cut spending. Drives those with money to leave. Paying more taxes and losing services is intolerable.

Late-cycle debt dynamic playing out at US state and federal levels — main difference: states can't print money, federal government can. Federal Reserve printing a lot, buying federal debt, financing government spending much bigger than intake.

> "Places (cities, states, countries) that have largest wealth gaps, largest debts, and worst declines in incomes are most likely to have greatest conflicts."

Connecticut example: highest per capita income, largest wealth/income gap, one of largest per capita debt and unfunded pension obligations. Haves and have-nots focused on own lives, little contact. Both more likely to have critical, stereotypical impressions of each other. COVID-inflicted health and budget shocks brought terrible conditions to surface.

> "Averages don't matter as much as the number of people who are suffering and their power."

> "Raising taxes and cutting spending when there are large wealth gaps and bad economic conditions, more than anything else, has been leading indicator of civil wars or revolutions."

History shows restructuring/devaluing previously created debt and non-debt obligations helps — classic in Stages 5 and 6. Once restructuring reduces debt burdens (painful at time), rebuilding can begin.

> "Debt and money created must be used to produce productivity gains and favorable returns on investment, rather than just given away."

> "History shows lending and spending on items that produce broad-based productivity gains and returns on investment that exceed borrowing costs result in living standards rising with debts being paid off."

Historical examples of successful investment: education at all levels (including job training), infrastructure, research yielding productive discoveries. Examples: Tang Dynasty, many Chinese dynasties, Roman Empire, Umayyad Caliphate, Mughal Empire, Meiji Japan, China's educational development over last couple decades. Long lead times, but improvements in education/infrastructure were essential behind virtually all empires' rises. Declines in quality almost always ingredients behind empires' declines.

**Additional risk factors**:

- **Decadence**: begins fashionable in Stage 4, becomes grotesque by Stage 5. Debt-financed, worsens financial conditions. Reduces productivity.
- **Bureaucracy**: While early in internal order cycle bureaucracy is low, it is high late in cycle, making sensible decision making more difficult. Things get more complex until even obviously good things can't be done. Example: US education funding — Constitution doesn't make education federal responsibility, funding comes from local taxes, children in richer towns in richer states receive much better education. Obviously unfair and unproductive, most agree children should have equal opportunities, but structure so ingrained it's nearly impossible to fix without revolutionary reinvention.
- **Populism/Extremism**: political and social phenomenon appealing to ordinary people who feel concerns not addressed by elites. Typically develops when there are wealth/opportunity gaps, perceived cultural threats, and "establishment elites" not working for most people. More extreme than moderates, confrontational rather than collaborative, exclusive rather than inclusive. 1930s: left = communism, right = fascism. More recently in US: **Donald Trump** (2016, populism of right), **Bernie Sanders, Elizabeth Warren, Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez** (populism of left). **Joe Biden** election may reflect desire for less extremism.
- - **Class Warfare**: Intensifies in Stage 5, becomes dangerous in Stage 6. Classic marker: demonization of other classes, producing scapegoat classes commonly believed to be source of problems. Leads to drive to exclude, imprison, or destroy them (happens in Stage 6). Examples: Nazi treatment of Jews, Chinese minorities in non-Chinese countries during stress, UK Catholics in stressful periods, rich capitalists commonly demonized.
- **Loss of Truth in Media**: Those fighting typically work with media to manipulate emotions, gain support, destroy opposition. Media goes wild like vigilantes — people tried/found guilty without due process. 1930s populists took control of media, established "ministers of propaganda." **2019 Gallup**: Only **13% of Americans** have "a great deal" of trust in media (down from **72% in 1976**). Dramatically decreased trustworthiness plagued former icons: *The Wall Street Journal* and *The New York Times*. Politically motivated, sensationalistic stories commercially rewarding when media business in financial trouble. **Martin Baron** (executive editor, *Washington Post*): "If you have a society where people can't agree on basic facts, how do you have a functioning democracy?" Even very capable and powerful people now too afraid of media to speak up about important matters or run for public office.
- **Rule-Following Fading**: Legal/police systems used as political weapons. Private police systems form (Nazi paramilitary before coming to power, KKK). Protests become increasingly violent — not always clear line between healthy protest and beginnings of revolution. Both giving freedom to protest and suppressing protests are risky. People dying in fighting is marker signifying progression to Stage 6. Late in Stage 5: reason abandoned in favor of passion, winning at all costs becomes the game.

> "When causes people are passionately behind are more important to them than the system for making decisions, the system is in jeopardy."

> "Watch populism and polarization as markers. In Stage 5, moderates become minority. In Stage 6, they cease to exist."

> "When in doubt, get out — if you don't want to be in civil war or war, you should get out while getting is good."

**Crossing from Stage 5 to Stage 6**: Occurs when system for resolving disagreements goes from working to not working. When one is in Stage 5 (like US is now), biggest question is how much the system will bend before it breaks. Democratic system allows population to do pretty much whatever it decides, produces more bending — regime changes can happen peacefully. But democracy also shown to break down in times of great conflict.

**Democracy's weakness**: Requires consensus but produces fragmented decision making. Leaders selected via popularity contests without thoughtful capability review. Biggest risk: ineffectiveness → revolution led by populist autocrats who represent large segments wanting a strong leader to get control of chaos. Historical breakdowns: Athens (400s-300s BCE), Roman Republic (century before 27 BCE), Weimar Republic (1920s), Italy/Japan/Spain (1920s-30s → fascism).

During times of great conflict, federalist democracies have conflicts between states and central government — would be a marker to watch for in the US.

### Stage 6: Civil Wars

> "Civil wars inevitably happen, so rather than assuming 'it won't happen here,' it is better to be wary of them."

> "Civil wars and revolutions inevitably take place to radically change the internal order. They include total restructurings of wealth and political power."

**Russian Revolution** (1917): broke existing system, built communist order lasting **74 years** (1917-1991). Eventually entered Stage 5 in late 1980s with attempted **perestroika** (restructuring), which failed; collapsed 1991.

**Japan's Meiji Restoration** (1866-69): three-year revolution triggered by Japan being closed off and failing to advance. Americans forced Japan to open, revolutionaries defeated ruling **shogun**, overturned old four-class system (military, farmers, artisans, merchants). New regime was relatively progressive: universal elementary education for boys and girls, adopted capitalism, opened to outside world. Meiji order lasted **76 years** (1869-1945) — eventually became decadent, overextended, fought expensive wars.

Most archetypal shifted power left to right, but many shifted right (Germany, Spain, Japan, Italy in 1930s; fall of Soviet Union; 1976 Argentina coup). Revolutionary leaders typically well-educated, middle-class: **Danton, Marat, Robespierre** (French Revolution, all lawyers/bourgeois), **Lenin** (studied law), **Mao** (studied law/economics), **Zhou Enlai** (scholarly middle-class). Initially supported by liberal aristocrats like **Marquis de Lafayette**. They evolve from idealistic intellectuals to brutal revolutionaries.

Civil wars are incredibly brutal because they are fights to the death — everyone forced to pick a side, moderates lose out. Best leaders for this stage: "inspirational generals" — strong enough to marshal support and brutal enough to do whatever necessary to win.

During civil wars: acute money shortages → capital controls (controls on movements to other jurisdictions, currencies, hard-to-tax assets like gold). Internal disorder attracts external enemies — splits people, is financially taxing, demands attention leaving less for other issues. Main reason internal and external wars tend to come close together.

> "Almost all civil wars have had some foreign powers participating in attempt to influence the outcome."

Post-revolution outcomes: While extremely painful, they often lead to restructurings that, if done well, can establish foundation for improved future results. What the future looks like depends on how next steps are handled.

## Chapter 6: The Big Cycle of External Order and Disorder

### International Relations = Law of the Jungle

Relationships between people/orders work basically same ways internally and externally, but international relations driven much more by raw power. All governance needs effective laws, enforcement, adjudication, consequences — these either don't exist or aren't effective between countries.

Attempts (**League of Nations, United Nations**) largely failed — organizations lack more wealth/power than most powerful countries.

> "The international order follows the law of the jungle much more than it follows international law."

### Five Types of International Conflict

1. **Trade/economic wars** — tariffs, import/export restrictions
2. **Technology wars** — which technologies shared vs. protected (national security)
3. **Geopolitical wars** — territory, alliances, negotiations and commitments
4. **Capital wars** — sanctions, cutting off money/credit, limiting capital market access
5. **Military wars** — actual shooting and deployment

Once military war begins, all four other dimensions weaponized maximally. First four evolve over time as intense competitions.

### Cycles of Internal and External Disorder

All factors that drive internal and external cycles tend to improve and worsen together. When things get bad, more things to argue over → greater inclination to fight. That's human nature, and why we have the Big Cycle.

> "All-out wars typically occur when existential issues are at stake and they cannot be resolved by peaceful means. The wars that result make it clear which side gets its way and has supremacy in subsequent matters."

Three big cycles of rising/declining conflict in Europe since 1500, averaging ~150 years each. Renaissance/Enlightenment/Industrial Revolution sowed seeds for Thirty Years' War, Napoleonic Wars, World Wars. China's "Century of Humiliation" (1840-1949) because Western powers and Japan exploited China.

### Key Principles About War

> "The two things about war that one can be most confident in are 1) that it won't be planned and 2) that it will be far worse than imagined."

Greatest risk: both parties have **roughly comparable military powers** and **irreconcilable existential differences**. Most potentially explosive at time of writing: US and China over **Taiwan**.

The choice that opposing countries face — fight or back down — is very hard. Both costly: fighting in lives and money, backing down in loss of status (shows weakness, leads to reduced support).

**Why "stupid" wars happen**: (a) prisoner's dilemma — both sides need assurance other won't try to kill them; (b) tit-for-tat escalation — like game of chicken, push too far → head-on crash; (c) perceived costs of backing down for declining power; (d) misunderstandings when decisions must be fast.

> "Winning means getting the things that are most important without losing the things that are most important, so wars that cost much more in lives and money than they provide in benefits are stupid."

> "Skilled collaborations to produce win-win relationships that both increase and divide up wealth and power well are much more rewarding and much less painful than wars."

Seeing things through adversary's eyes and clearly identifying/communicating red lines are keys to avoiding war. Leaders must be truthful and thoughtful — worst is when leaders are untruthful and emotional, worse still when they take over the media.

> "Have power, respect power, and use power wisely."

Having power is good — power wins out over agreements, rules, and laws all the time. Important to respect power — not smart to fight a war one will lose. Using power wisely doesn't necessarily mean bullying; includes recognition that generosity and trust produce win-win relationships fabulously more rewarding than lose-lose ones. Often using "soft powers" preferable to "hard powers." Power usually best handled like a hidden knife brought out only in event of a fight.

Financial strength to outspend rivals is one of the most important strengths — how US beat Soviet Union in Cold War. Country must provide "guns" (military) and "butter" (domestic spending) for 200-300 years. None forever. Generally desirable to have power, also desirable to not have power one doesn't need — maintaining it consumes resources.

### The 1930s-40s: From Economic War to Hot War

**Soviet Union**: recovering from 1917-22 revolution, lost war to Germany, costly war with Poland, 1921 famine, political purges. **China**: civil war, poverty, famine 1928-30. US and USSR droughts in 1930s triggered further suffering.

**Roosevelt's response (1929-1936)**:
- First 100 days: massive spending, jobs programs, unemployment insurance, Social Security, labor/union-friendly programs
- **"Soak the Rich Tax" (1935)**: top marginal income tax to **75%** (vs. 25% in 1930); by 1941: **81%**, top corporate rate **31%** (from 12%)
- Budget deficits: ~1% GDP → ~4% GDP
- **1933-1936**: Stock market returned **over 200%**, economy grew at ~**9%** real
- **1936 Fed tightening** caused fragile economy to fall back into recession, raising tensions

**Spanish Civil War**: populists of left (communists) vs. right (fascists) → brutal war. Right-wing **Franco** (with Hitler support) succeeded in purging left-wing opposition.

> "During periods of severe economic distress and large wealth gaps, there are typically revolutionarily large redistributions of wealth."

When done peacefully: large tax increases on rich and big increases in money supply. When done violently: forced asset confiscations. US and UK: redistributions of wealth maintained capitalism/democracy. Germany, Japan, Italy, Spain: they did not.

> "Before there is a shooting war there is usually an economic war."

~10 years of economic, technological, geopolitical, and capital wars before all-out wars declared. Common tactics when weaponized:
- **Asset freezes/seizures**: Preventing enemy from using/selling foreign assets (current US sanctions on Iranian Revolutionary Guard; initial US freeze against Japan in WWII)
- **Blocking capital markets access**: Preventing country from accessing capital markets (1887 Germany banned purchase of Russian securities/debt to impede military buildup; US threatening to do this to China)
- **Embargoes/blockades**: Blocking trade in goods/services (US oil embargo on Japan; France's blockade of UK in Napoleonic Wars)

**Why wars are uncertain**: (a) Rival powers only go to war when powers roughly comparable (otherwise suicidal for weaker). (b) Too many possible actions/reactions to anticipate. Only known at outset: will probably be extremely painful and possibly ruinous. Smart leaders typically only enter if pushed into fighting or losing by backing down.

**Hitler's expansion (1937-41)**: November 1937 secretly met with top officials to announce expansion plans to gain resources and unite Aryan race. Annexed **Austria**, seized part of **Czechoslovakia** with oil resources. Europe and US watched warily, not wanting another war after WWI devastation. **September 1, 1939**: Germany invaded Poland — the moment came for the Allies. Quickly captured **Denmark, Norway, Netherlands, Belgium, Luxembourg, France**. Strengthened alliances with **Japan and Italy** (common enemies, ideologically aligned). Seizing oil-rich territory allowed conserving existing oil while gaining new resources. War with Soviets inevitable; **June 1941**: invaded Russia despite non-aggression pact → extremely costly war on two fronts.

**Japan's Pacific expansion**: 1937 expanded occupation of China, brutally taking Shanghai and Nanking. **Estimated 200,000 Chinese civilians killed** at Nanking alone. US remained isolationist but provided **Chiang Kai-shek's** government with fighter planes and pilots — putting toe in war.

**Roosevelt's escalation**: Secretary of War **Henry Stimson** initiated aggressive sanctions → **Export Control Act of 1940** (restricted iron/steel exports to cut Japan off from resources). Mid-1940: moved US Pacific Fleet to Hawaii. **Lend-Lease Act (March 1941)**: allowed lending/leasing war supplies to countries "vital to the defense of the United States" (Great Britain, Soviet Union, China). Good for US geopolitically and economically — made money selling weapons/food while allies struggled. Great Britain running out of gold, so US allowed postponed payment. **Although not outright declaration of war, Lend-Lease effectively ended United States' neutrality.**

> "When countries are weak, opposing countries take advantage of their weaknesses to obtain gains."

Japan seized Southeast Asian colonies from France, Netherlands, Great Britain — overstretched by fighting in Europe. September 1940: invaded French Indochina. 1941: seized oil reserves in Dutch East Indies.

**July-August 1941**: Roosevelt froze all Japanese assets, closed Panama Canal to Japanese ships, embargoed oil/gas. Cut off **three-fourths of Japan's trade** and **80% of its oil**. Japan calculated it would run out of oil in two years.

**Pearl Harbor (December 7-8, 1941)**: Japan launched coordinated attacks on US military forces at **Pearl Harbor** and **Philippines**. Most optimistic Japanese leaders believed US would lose because: fighting on two fronts, its individualistic/capitalist system inferior to authoritarian/fascist systems, and greater Japanese willingness to endure pain and die.

> "In war one's ability to withstand pain is even more important than one's ability to inflict pain."

### Wartime Economic Policies

Classic wartime controls: rationing, production controls, price/wage controls, import/export restrictions, capital controls, debt monetization, autocratic governance.

Government controls nearly everything as country shifts resources from profit-making to war-making: what can be produced, what can be bought/sold (rationing), imports/exports, prices/wages/profits, access to financial assets, ability to move money out of country.

**Top marginal tax rates**: US **94%**, UK **98%**, Germany **60%**, Japan **74%**. Market closures, asset price controls, asset ownership restrictions, FX controls, limits on new issuance, limits on corporate profits — all "Yes" across Allies and Axis.

**Gold is the coin of the realm during wars** — money/credit not accepted between non-allied countries.

Market behavior: German equities outperformed early. After **1942 Battle of Midway**, Allied equities rallied continuously. German and Japanese markets closed at war's end, didn't reopen for ~5 years, **virtually wiped out**.

**Protecting wealth in war is difficult**: Normal economic activities curtailed, traditionally safe investments not safe, capital mobility limited, high taxes imposed. Protecting wealth of those who have it is not a priority relative to redistribution. Investment advice: sell out of all debt, buy gold. Wars financed by borrowing and printing → devalues debt and money. Justifiable reluctance to accept credit.

> "Every power has its time in the sun. But all eventually decline."

> "Still, the cycle needn't transpire this way if countries in their rich and powerful stages stay productive, earn more than they spend, make the system work well for most of their populations, and figure out ways of creating and sustaining win-win relationships with their most significant rivals."

## Chapter 7: Investing in Light of the Big Cycle

### Dalio's Investment Philosophy

> "The game I play for handling both my life and my career is to try to figure out how the world works, develop principles for dealing with it well, and then place my bets."

Of all factors driving Big Cycles, the **greatest contentious factor was the debt and capital markets cycle** — the "Big Investing Cycle." Need to understand cycles well enough to tactically move or diversify portfolio.

### Four Market Determinants

> "All markets are primarily driven by just four determinants: growth, inflation, risk premiums, and discount rates."

All investments are exchanges of lump-sum payments today for future payments. Future cash payments determined by growth and inflation. Risk premium = risk taken vs. having cash in hand. Present value of future payments determined by discount rate.

Central governments determine where money goes (tax and spend, can't create money). Central banks create money/credit (can't determine where it flows in real economy). Example: when growth and inflation too low, central banks create more money/credit → economic growth picks up, then inflation with a lag. When constrained, both slow down.

Portfolio goal: well-diversified across these four drivers (higher/lower growth, rising/falling inflation), tactically tilted based on what's happening. Building blocks broken down by country, environmental bias, sectors, individual companies.

### The Survivorship Bias Problem

Most investors base expectations on US/UK experience since 1950s. **That is survivorship bias.** Not many stock/bond markets survived WWII. Looking at US and UK = looking at uniquely blessed countries in uniquely peaceful/productive time = best part of the Big Cycle.

**In 35 years before 1945**: virtually all wealth destroyed or confiscated in most countries. In some countries many capitalists killed or imprisoned due to anger when capital markets and capitalism failed. Regular cycles: boom periods (Second Industrial Revolution, Gilded Age in late 19th/early 20th centuries) → rising internal and international conflict (1900-10) → great conflict and economic bust (1910-45). Now more aligned with late-cycle bust and restructuring periods than early-cycle boom and building periods.

**50 years before WWI**: almost no conflict between major powers, greatest innovation/productivity growth ever, enormous wealth and prosperity. Globalization at new highs — global exports up several multiples. Going into 1900, things looked great except wealth gaps and resentments increasing and debts large. Between 1900 and 1914, conditions worsened and international tensions increased. Would have been nearly impossible to predict WWI confidently even while seeing warning signs.

### Worst-Case Portfolio Losses by Country (60/40 stock/bond)

- **Russia 1900-1918**: -100% (Civil War, communist rule, debt repudiation)
- **China 1930-1950**: -100% (WWII, communist takeover)
- **Germany 1903-1923**: -100% (Weimar hyperinflation)
- **Japan 1928-1948**: -96%
- **Austria 1903-1923**: -95%
- **France 1930-1950**: -93%
- **Italy**: multiple catastrophic periods (-87%, -84%, -72%)
- **India 1955-1975**: -66%
- **Spain 1962-1982**: -59%
- **US, Canada, Australia**: only major countries without sustained complete losses

Wealth confiscation, capital controls, stock market closures common during periods with large wealth gaps and internal conflict. **Deaths as % of population**: Germany 15% (1940s), Russia 13% (1940s), Japan 4% (1940s).

**US investors**: underperformed stocks by ~**1.5%/year** (2000-2020) due to buying high/selling low. Lost ~**25% of real equity returns** to taxes in any 20-year period.

### The Big Capital Markets Cycle

In upwave: debt increases, financial wealth/obligations rise relative to tangible wealth until promises can't be met → "run on the bank" → printing money → devaluation → financial wealth goes down relative to real wealth → cycle begins again. The anti-capital, anti-capitalist part of the cycle continues until opposite extreme is reached, then reverses — when peace and prosperity return, financial assets have excellent real returns.

In the rising parts: stocks/bonds go up as interest rates go down. More money raises demand for financial assets, lowers risk premiums. Investments seem more attractive even as future expected returns go down. More outstanding claims relative to what there are claims on = more risk, but conditions seem good and memories of crises fade.

> "When there is a lot of financial wealth relative to real wealth it reverses and real returns of financial wealth, especially cash and debt assets, are bad."

Interest rates must stay low to provide relief to debtors and stimulate more debt growth. Classic late-cycle part of long-term debt cycle.

> "During periods of the devaluation of money, hard money and hard assets rise in value relative to cash."

At time of writing: US bond yields near lowest ever, real yields negative. US bonds: wait ~**45 years** to get $100 back. Japanese bonds: **150 years**. European bonds: **likely never**. Chinese bonds: **30 years**.

> "Rather than get paid less than inflation, why not instead buy stuff — any stuff — that will equal inflation or better?"

> "One of the most important questions investors need to regularly ask themselves is whether the amount of interest that is being paid more than makes up for the devaluation risk they face."

Looking globally back 500 years and in China back 1,400 years: same cycles occur repeatedly for basically the same reasons. The terrible periods prior to 1945 are typical of late Big Cycle transitions — while terrible, they were more than matched by terrific upswings after painful transition from old to new order. Because these things happened many times before: can't invest without protections against them.

## Chapter 8: The Last 500 Years in a Tiny Nutshell

Part I described how the perpetual-motion machine works. Part II shows what this machine produced over 500 years. This chapter sets the stage for remaining chapters on Dutch, British, American, and Chinese cases.

### The World in 1500

**Much "bigger"**: travel ~25 miles/day on horseback. Apollo astronauts traveled to moon/back faster than a traveler from Paris to Rome in 1500. Europe, Russia, China were separate worlds. States that seem tiny in retrospect didn't seem that way at time.

**Countries didn't exist** — territories run by families (kingdoms/dynasties that almost constantly fought neighbors for wealth and power). Because ruling order centered around families, lands could be inherited if rulers died without closer relatives. Arranged marriages kept empires in tighter family groups.

**Religions and religious leaders much more powerful; science didn't exist.** Elites: monarchs (divine power), clergy (represented divine), landowning nobles (oversaw peasants, treated largely like oxen). Though Europeans and Chinese on opposite sides of world with virtually no contact, they operated essentially same way — though China's institutions were bigger, more developed, less religious.

**Much less egalitarian**: ideas of equal treatment and judgment by people didn't exist. **Serfdom** (peasants as property) in most of Western Europe until 1300s-1400s. Rights remained weak until Enlightenment in 1700s.

### Europe's Empires in 1500

- **Habsburg dynasty**: controlled Spain + Holy Roman Empire (parts of Netherlands, Belgium, Italy, Germany, Austria) — Western world's most powerful
- **Valois/Bourbon dynasty**: controlled France, main Habsburg rival → lot of fighting
- **Tudor dynasty**: controlled England, growing in strength
- **Florence, Venice, Milan** (often republics with prominent families): most financial, commercial, intellectual, artistic innovation in Europe at 1500. Very rich, played central role in shaping Western world for centuries
- **Papal States**: Catholic Church acquired vast wealth from tithes and peasant labor on church lands
- **Rurik/Romanov dynasty**: Russia, peripheral power, seemed remote
- **Ottoman Empire**: centered in Constantinople (conquered 1453)
- Additionally, hundreds/thousands of family-run states across Europe, constantly fighting neighbors

### China's Ming Dynasty in 1500

**Most advanced and powerful empire in world.** Population 100-130 million vs. Europe's 50-55 million. **Confucian scholars** near top of social hierarchy — needed to study Confucianism and pass highly competitive exams. Political decisions based on ruler's interpretations of Confucian ideals.

**Neo-Confucianism** (dominant at time) shifted toward more rational, philosophical, humanistic thinking — key reason China pulled so far ahead of Europe in Middle Ages. Scholars and scientists had significant power → remarkable technological advances: gunpowder, printing press, architecture. Literacy rates extremely high. Advanced medicine — widespread smallpox vaccination program, centuries before Europe. Relatively sophisticated financial system with early corporations, banks, history of using printed money, relatively sophisticated financial markets. Militarily: largest navy in world, standing army of one million.

> "Of all the civilizations of premodern times, none appeared more advanced, none felt more superior than that of China." — **Paul Kennedy**, *The Rise and Fall of the Great Powers*

> "Ironically and typically, the enormity of the Ming Dynasty's wealth and power is one possible explanation of what eventually led to its fall."

Believing they didn't need anything else, the emperors ended exploration, closed doors, retired to pleasure, turned government over to ministers and eunuchs → dysfunctional infighting, corruption, weakness, vulnerability. Shift from pragmatic scientific study to pedantic scholarship helped drive China's decline relative to Europe.
## Chapter 8: What Has Happened Since 1500 (continued)

### Global Landscape in 1500

- **Asia fragmented**: India divided among several kingdoms including **Delhi Sultanate** and **Hindu Vijayanagara Empire**. **Mughal Empire** began conquest of India in 1520s. Japan divided, in civil war, isolated — not a power of note. **Ottoman Empire** controlled Middle East; key rival was **Safavid dynasty of Persia**.
- **Americas**: Largest empires were **Aztec Empire** (capital **Tenochtitlan**, probably larger population than any European city) and **Incan Empire**. European arrival devastated both; colonies emerged including seedlings of the United States (276 years later).
- **Africa**: Vast continent, three times bigger than Europe, divided into dozens of kingdoms. Biggest in 1500 was **Songhai Empire** in West Africa (center of trade and Islamic scholarship).

> "That was the lay of the land in 1500. The world order was about to change in very big ways."

### Key Revolutions and Ages Since 1500

Two patterns over 500 years: (1) several revolutions in ways of thinking led to tremendous evolution and progress; (2) many cycles of peaceful/prosperous periods alternating with depressions and wars that marked ends of old orders and beginnings of new ones.

### The Commercial Revolution (1100s-1500s)

Evolution from agriculture-based economy to trade in variety of goods. Centered in **Italian city-states** by 1500. Two enabling factors: (1) wars between Christian Europe and Ottoman Empire slowed land trade, creating opening for maritime trade; (2) Italian city-states developed **republican governments** modeled after Roman Republic.

**Venice** exemplified this — governance designed with checks and balances. Leader (the **doge**) could not name successor, family restricted from government. New doges chosen by vote via committees. Italians produced well-functioning **capital markets** supported by advances in **bookkeeping** and impartial contract-enforcement institutions.

**Florence's gold florin** became accepted as global currency. **Venice established a perpetual bond** (5% coupon, early 12th century) — centuries without defaults gave lenders confidence. Venice eventually defaulted ~1500 after losing wars. Liquid bond markets caught on in Netherlands and UK.

### The Renaissance (1300s-1600s)

New way of thinking modeled after ancient Greeks and Romans. Big pivot toward **logical reasoning** instead of divine intention. Self-reinforcing cycle: peaceful periods where creativity and commerce reinforce each other. Driving force: people like the **Medici**, merchants and bankers who supported arts and science. **Printing press** (mid-15th century) spread ideas rapidly.

Many European Renaissance innovations had already been in place in China for centuries — printing press, scientific method, meritocratic placements. China's **Neo-Confucianism** was China's Renaissance. Luminaries like **Shakespeare**, **Francis Bacon**, **Descartes**, **Erasmus** had broad impact.

### The Age of Exploration and Colonialism (1400s-1700s)

Europeans traveled the world seeking wealth, creating first widespread contact between peoples. **Henry the Navigator** sponsored earliest Portuguese voyages. Spain followed, conquering **Aztec** and **Incan** empires. Spain's **Habsburg Empire** controlled profitable silver mines — known as **"the empire on which the sun never sets"** (expression later used for British Empire).

**Spanish Price Revolution**: Flow of silver to Europe doubled prices every few decades — "a reminder of how big shifts can have economic impacts that seem unimaginable based on one's most recent experiences."

**China's exploration and pullback**: **Ming Emperor Yongle** sent **Zheng He** on seven "treasure voyages" with the world's largest and most sophisticated navy. But Ming emperors ended voyages and pulled inward. Result: **Age of Isolationism** in China and Japan (**Sakoku**). For centuries, both moved toward distance and isolation from foreigners.

### The Reformation (1517-1648)

**Martin Luther** published ***Ninety-Five Theses*** (1517), challenging papal monopoly. Church sold **"indulgences"** and governed sizable share of modern Italy (the **Papal States**). Wars of Religion intertwined with wars against existing elites — ~3 million died in French civil war; contributed to extended civil war in UK. Undermined Holy Roman Empire and Habsburgs. Left Germany with deep divisions through the Thirty Years' War.

### New World Order: Peace of Westphalia (1648)

Most important breakthroughs: **establishment of geographic borders and sovereign rights** of people within those borders. "Invented countries as we know them." One quarter of Europe's population died from combat, disease, or starvation. **Dutch emerged as leading global economic power.**

> "Wars are so much more terrible than even those who are eager for them can imagine, they lead to treaties that redefine the order and are followed by periods of peace."

### Invention of Capitalism (1600s)

**Dutch East India Company**: world's first publicly listed company (1602). **Amsterdam Stock Exchange**: first stock exchange globally (August 1602). Over 1 in 50 Dutch adults owned shares. World's first transnational corporation with shareholders, board of directors, corporate logo. Along with **Bank of England's** first government bond issuance (1694), these innovations shifted real GDP growth from slow to fast.

### The Scientific Revolution (1500s-1600s)

Extension of Renaissance reasoning and Reformation's drive to question authority. **Francis Bacon** pioneered scientific method. **Copernicus** and **Galileo** expanded knowledge of solar system. **Isaac Newton's laws of motion**. **Royal Society** (UK, 1660) promoted exchange of ideas. Newton was its president 1703-1727.

### The First Industrial Revolution (1700s-1800s)

UK pioneered machine-based manufacturing — first sustained, widespread period of productivity improvement in thousands of years. Power moved to central government bureaucrats and capitalists who controlled means of production. British Empire approached ~20% of world output. UK overtook Netherlands in output per capita ~1800.

### The Enlightenment and Age of Revolutions (1600s-1700s)

Scientific method applied to human behavior. **Adam Smith** in economics, **John Locke** and **Montesquieu** in political philosophy. Movement toward overthrowing monarchies. Included American, French, Spanish, German revolutions. **Napoleon**: classic benevolent dictator who converted chaos to order, expanded empire with military prowess, overreached and failed.

### Napoleonic Wars (1803-1815) and Western Powers in Asia

Britain and allies defeated Napoleon. **Congress of Vienna** created new order with balance of power concepts. **Pax Britannica** followed. British and Western powers brought gunboats to India, China, Japan. Chinese lost to British. **US Commander Matthew Perry** sailed into Tokyo Bay 1853. Led to **Meiji Restoration** (Japan, successful) and **Self-Strengthening Movement** (China, unsuccessful — Century of Humiliation).

### Second Industrial Revolution (1850s-early 1900s)

Centered on railroads, electricity, telephones, interchangeable manufacturing parts. Primarily benefited US and Germany. Produced **Gilded Age** with great wealth and wealth gaps. **Invention of Communism (1848)**: reaction against capitalism's wealth gaps — led to revolutions in Russia and China in 20th century.

## Dutch Rise: Innovation and Capital Markets

### Key Dutch Innovations

- **Dutch East India Company**: world's first mega-corporation, ~1/3 of global trade
- Income per capita rose to 2x+ most European powers; Dutch literacy rates twice world average
- Produced ~25% of all major world inventions at peak
- **Amsterdam became world's most important financial center** with only 1-2 million population
- Two critical inventions: (1) uniquely effective sailing ships, (2) capitalism

> "In short, the Dutch were superbly educated people who were very hardworking and inventive"

### The Capital Markets Cycle

**The Dutch invented capitalism as we know it.** Before Dutch innovations, collective public ownership via equity markets did not exist. **Dutch East India Company**: world's first publicly listed company (1602). Clear rules on ownership and transfer. Early 1700s peak: dividends = nearly $1 of every $100 of Dutch GDP.

### Bank of Amsterdam (1609)

Prior to Dutch banking: chaotic situation with ~800 different coins circulating (many debased/counterfeit). **Bank of Amsterdam** established to protect creditors from unreliable commodity money. Created **bank guilder backed by hard currency = Type 2 money** (first true reserve currency). Baltic and Russian trade relied solely on guilders for pricing and settlement.

### Thirty Years' War (1618-48) and Peace of Westphalia

Catholic side (Habsburg Austria, Spain, Papal States) vs Protestant side (German nobles, Denmark, Netherlands, Sweden, France). Money and geopolitics mattered more than religion — Catholic France (led by **Cardinal Richelieu**) allied with Lutheran Sweden. Habsburgs lost. Peace of Westphalia expanded sovereignty, created nationalism, made religious authorities less powerful. Dutch benefited from stability; less financially hurt than other powers.

### Dutch Golden Age and Decline

Classic reversal: educational/technological edge eroded, became uncompetitive, Industrial Revolution (centered in Britain) shifted power in 1700s. Dutch became extremely wealthy → less competitive → wages higher → overextended militarily.

**Reserve currency persistence**: Even during decline, ~40% of global trade settled in Amsterdam using bank guilders — gave Dutch "extraordinary privilege" of getting into lots of debt.

**Key Dutch wars**:
- **Eighty Years' War (1566-1648)**: Protestant Netherlands revolt against Catholic Spain; full independence realized 1648
- **First Anglo-Dutch War (1652-54)**: England's Navigation Act (1651) required American goods carried by English ships; trade war stalemate
- **Dano-Swedish War (1657-60)**: Sweden threatened profitable Baltic routes; Dutch defeated Swedish
- **Second Anglo-Dutch War (1665-67)**: Another trade dispute; Dutch victory
- **Franco-Dutch War (1672-78) and Third Anglo-Dutch War (1672-74)**: Fought over trade; Dutch foiled French conquest at tremendous cost
- **Fourth Anglo-Dutch War (1780-84)**: British retaliation for Dutch support of American Revolution; significant defeat, ended guilder as reserve currency

**Glorious Revolution (1688)**: **William III of Orange** married Mary II (daughter of unpopular English king), invaded England, took power. Short-term Dutch benefit from having ally on British throne, but second-order consequences: Dutch merchants shifted operations to London, hastening London's rise as international finance center. Alliance gave English merchants access to Dutch trade. William III died heirless 1702; direct connection broken. Dutch provinces splintered.

### British Advantages: Industrial Revolution

Mid-18th century: Dutch Empire no longer world's leading empire. **Britain learned from Dutch innovations** and invested in education. Coupled with capitalism: standardizing inputs, moving production to factories, transformative new inventions. British became more productive, captured more trade, built military might.

### The Fourth Anglo-Dutch War and Classic Bank Run

1770s: English began interfering with Dutch shipping, escalating after Dutch traded arms with American Revolution colonies. **1781: English delivered massive blow to Dutch Navy in Caribbean**, seizing territories in Caribbean and East Indies. Dutch East India Company lost half its ships and key trade route access → borrowed heavily from Bank of Amsterdam to survive.

**Financial losses → classic central bank move to print more money.** Bank of Amsterdam created more paper money (loans to Dutch East India Company) → clear there wouldn't be enough gold/silver to cover all paper claims → classic "run on the bank." Bank's precious metal store exhausted; guilder supply soared as demand fell.

**Accounts backed by coins plummeted from 17 million guilder (March 1780) to 300,000 guilder (January 1783)** as gold/silver coin owners demanded them back. Bank deposits traded at large discounts to guilder coins.

Bank run marked end of Dutch Empire and guilder as reserve currency:
- 1791: Bank taken over by City of Amsterdam
- 1795: French revolutionary government overthrew Dutch Republic
- 1796: Dutch East India Company nationalized (stock worthless)
- 1799: Charter expired

**Currency exchange rates**: Guilder remained stable through most of decline era, then: (A) stable through Golden Age, (B) Fourth Anglo-Dutch War → money printing/initial pressure, (C) brief stabilization at French Revolution start, (D) French overthrew Dutch — accounts basically wiped out.

Classic monetary transition: Type 1 (precious metal) → Type 2 (paper linked to metal) during financial stress/military conflict — risky because decreased currency trust. Dutch East India Company returns: like most bubble companies, great at first, attracted more investors even after fundamentals weakened; ultimately failed fundamentals and excessive debt broke it. British East India Company returns far exceeded Dutch; Dutch government bond returns terrible relative to British.

## Chapter 11: British Empire Rise and Decline

### The Rise (Starting ~1600)

**Political foundation**: **English Civil War** led to **King Charles I** tried and executed. Monarchy replaced by **Commonwealth under Oliver Cromwell**. **Glorious Revolution** established rule of law over monarchy. Strong Parliament allowed moderately meritocratic selection — key statesmen from merchant families: **Pitt the Elder, Pitt the Younger, Robert Peel, Gladstone, Disraeli**.

**Enlightenment ideas**: Shaped by **Francis Bacon (1561-1626)** — society based on reason and science, government power from the people. Improvements in basic education (literacy rates rose), first encyclopedias/dictionaries printed en masse.

**Financial strength**: Created powerful centralized fiscal authority. By 18th century, tax burden in Britain almost twice that of France. **Bank of England (1694)** standardized and increased liquidity of government debt. Bond yields fell drastically over early 1700s.

### The Industrial Revolution

Well-educated population + culture of inventiveness + capital + England's **iron and coal** endowments. Shift from rural/agrarian to urban/industrial. UK overtook Dutch as preeminent economic power ~1750.

**Timeline**: steam engine (1712), silk factory (1719), flying shuttle (1733), spinning jenny (1764), water frame (1769), power loom (1785), steam locomotive on wheels (1801), on rail (1816), railway Manchester-Liverpool (1825).

UK produced 20% of world's income at peak, controlled 40% global exports, 20% land mass, 25% population. UK's military (especially navy) secured control over global trade routes. **London became world's financial center; pound sterling became reserve currency.** Trade with China (seeking tea) sowed seeds of **Opium Wars** and China's Century of Humiliation.

### Why Not France?

France was center of Enlightenment learning (**Voltaire, Montesquieu**). Books on arts/science published in Paris doubled 1720-1780. But:

**John Law's Mississippi Company bubble**: Created national bank with paper money (1716). **Company of the West** (Mississippi Company) with monopoly rights in French Louisiana. Stock soared, became bubble, burst — paper money and shares rapidly lost value. People fled to hard currency coinage.

**Costly wars**: League of Augsburg (1688-97), Spanish Succession (1701-14), Austrian Succession (1740-48), **Seven Years' War** (1756-63, also French and Indian War), American Revolution (1775-83) — all cost more than they brought in. Without modern financial system, France relied on burdensome taxes.

**Extreme inequality**: Court of **King Louis XVI** infamous for frivolous spending — **Marie Antoinette's Hamlet** near Versailles. American Revolution deficits ~2-3% of GDP. Bad harvests 1788-89 caused soaring bread prices. **Estates General** (1789): Third Estate (98% of population) formed own assembly.

**Revolution, Terror, Hyperinflation**: Republic declared 1791. **Louis XVI** executed January 1793. **Reign of Terror**: 20,000-30,000 executed. **Assignat** experienced hyperinflation by 1795. Government destroyed money-printing presses in ceremony (1796).

**Napoleon**: Classic hero rising from chaos. 1799 coup; emperor with dictatorial powers until 1814. Stabilized economy, professionalized government. Overreached with Russia invasion. Britain won through greater financial strength — lent money to coalition forces.

### Congress of Vienna (1814-15)

**Quadruple alliance** (Britain, Austria, Prussia, Russia) reorganized world order. Created checks/balances lasting ~100 years. Like US after WWI/WWII, UK didn't seek significant new territories. Consolidated weak states (Italy, Germany, Low Countries) to counterbalance France. Treaties of Paris contained but didn't destroy France.

> Kissinger: "It may not have fulfilled all the hopes of an idealistic generation, but it gave this generation something perhaps more precious: a period of stability."

### British Power Approaches Its Peak

No power benefited more from new stability. Not only were Britain's main rivals weakened, but power equilibrium allowed UK to avoid expensive conflicts close to home — policy known as **"splendid isolation,"** set stage for **"Imperial Century."** UK expanded: Canada, Australia, India, large portions of Africa. Even where not explicitly taking control, able to intervene for trade access (e.g., **Opium Wars** ensuring UK's ability to export opium to China).

**Peak (~1870)**: 20% world income, 40% global exports, 20% land mass, 25% population. UK's share of global exports peaked ~1850 at about 40%. 1850-1914: ~60% of global trade denominated in pounds. **English Rothschild bank** made first major government loan to Prussia (1818). Bank of England operated as "lender of last resort." After 1870: persistent trade deficit funded by returns on overseas investments — income from surpluses funded increasing share of global cross-border investment.

**Three classic factors of decline**:

1. **Declining competitiveness**: Second Industrial Revolution (synthetics, petroleum, electricity, telephones, automobiles) benefited US and Germany more. UK failed to reorganize industries. Sizable increase in output per worker in US/Germany while UK didn't keep up.

2. **Rising inequality**: **Top 1% owned over 70% of all wealth; top 10% owned 93%.** Peak in wealth gap coincided with peak in empire ~1900. Reform bills expanded voting; public pensions, medical/unemployment insurance, free school lunches introduced. 25% of eligible men were union members by 1911. **Labour Party** became significant force. First national coal miners' strike (1912).

3. **Rise of rivals, particularly Germany**: Unified by **Bismarck's** brilliant diplomacy. Mandatory primary education from 1860s. Three new research universities. Government provided credit to corporations, grants to inventors, rebates on industrial equipment imports. Manufacturing output: 5% to 13% of world share (1860-1900). **By 1900, Germany's GDP surpassed Britain's.** Dominated Nobel Prizes (1900-1913). **Wilhelm II** (emperor 1888) set off arms race — forced Bismarck to resign, adopted world power policy. **1914: powder keg exploded into all-out war.**

### WWI and Its Aftermath

**8.5 million soldiers, 13 million civilians killed.** Russia devolved into revolution (1917). Spanish flu killed 20-50 million. More people died as percentage of European population than in Napoleonic Wars or Thirty Years' War.

**Paris Peace Conference/Treaty of Versailles (1919)**: Victors (US, Britain, France, Japan, Italy) carved up losing powers' territories, forced reparations payable in gold. Created unsustainable order making second war inevitable. Term **"new world order"** coined for **Woodrow Wilson's** vision (League of Nations). Debt burdens caused inflationary depression in Germany (1920-23). US benefited geopolitically — played key role in shaping new order, though remained more isolationist. Most countries endeavored to restore gold convertibility; currency stability came only after sharp devaluations.

**Roaring '20s**: Period of peace and prosperity fueled by great innovations and productivity. Capital markets boom produced big debts and big wealth gaps. Lot of debt created (promises to deliver paper money convertible to gold) to buy speculative assets, particularly stocks. **Fed tightened 1929**, bubble burst, global Great Depression began — brought economic suffering to virtually all nations, led to fighting over wealth within and between countries.

**Great Depression**: Brought economic suffering to virtually all nations, led to fighting over wealth within and between countries. Large wealth gaps led to rise of populism/extremism. US/UK maintained capitalism/democracy. Germany/Japan/Italy/Spain: populist dictators seized control, sought empire expansion. Classic 10-year skirmishing period before hot war (economic sanctions, trade wars, military buildups).

**WWII**: Even more costly than WWI. Germany/Japan lost; US/UK/Soviet Union won — though economically UK and Soviet Union lost too. US gained enormously in relative wealth. GDP per capita in Germany/Japan fell by at least half. US sold and lent extensively before and during war; fighting took place off US territory; US deaths comparatively low. US accumulated two-thirds of all government-held gold.

### British Decline After 1945

**Allied victory produced tremendous shift**: US emerged as dominant empire. UK left with large debts, expensive empire, more competitive rivals, population with big wealth gaps. **Pound lost status over 20 years**: one-third of international trade still in sterling in 1960. Smart investors recognized contrast between UK's and US's financial conditions.

**1947 convertibility crisis**: When partial convertibility introduced July 1947, pound came under massive selling pressure. **PM Clement Attlee** called for wartime sacrifice spirit. Run on pound accelerated. Convertibility suspended by end of August.

> Attlee: "We are engaged in another battle for Britain. This battle cannot be won by the few."

**1949 devaluation**: Pound devalued 30% against dollar. Competitiveness returned, full convertibility restored by mid-to-late 1950s. But recurring balance of payments strains. **Devalued again 1967.** Deutschemark replaced pound as world's second reserve currency. Sterling Area countries unwilling to hold reserves in pounds unless UK guaranteed dollar value.

### Formation of European Union

**Robert Schuman** (born German citizen, became French when Alsace-Lorraine returned after WWI) and **Konrad Adenauer** (centrist mayor driven from political life by Nazis, sent to concentration camp 1944) sought to make war "materially impossible."

**European Coal and Steel Community** → customs union (1957, **Treaty of Rome**) → opened borders (1985, **Schengen Agreement**) → political/economic union (1992, **Maastricht Treaty**) creating euro and EU citizenship. Integration of 27 member states (400+ million people) puts EU on similar standing to other great powers.

**EU's current challenges**: Large debt, weak economy, internal conflicts, weak military. Wealth inequalities fueled populism; UK left. Europe (source of greatest global powers 15th-20th centuries) slipped to secondary power status.

## Chapter 11: US Rise and Decline

### The Rise

US went through usual revolution/post-revolution process in uniquely peaceful way through negotiations and near-total respect for agreements. Rapidly improving education preceded big rises in innovation, technology, and competitiveness. After Civil War: **Second Industrial Revolution** — classic time when capitalism produced both great wealth and big wealth gaps. Progressive Era: trust busting, federal income taxes (constitutional amendment, 1913). **New York became world's leading financial center.**

### Dollar's Long Ascent

Dollar's path far from straightforward. In first century, financial system completely underdeveloped — no central bank, no lender of last resort. **Eight significant banking panics in New York alone (1836-1913).** Only two US banks in top 20 globally in 1913 (numbers 13 and 17; British banks held nine slots). **Federal Reserve** created 1913 after 1907 debt bust. US stock market capitalization surpassed Britain by 1910. **US Steel** (founded 1901) became most valuable company. Dollar remained world's leading reserve currency throughout entire period from post-WWII to present — **forceful demonstration of benefits of having currency that most of world's debts are denominated in.**

### WWI to WWII

US was not in WWI for most of it — only major country maintaining gold convertibility. European currencies devalued against dollar. Allied wartime debts owed to US boosted dollar. **Roaring '20s**: lot of debt created (promises to deliver paper money convertible to gold) to buy speculative assets. **Fed tightened 1929**, bubble burst, Great Depression.

**Post-WWII**: US came out the big winner — sold/lent during war, fighting off US territory, comparatively low deaths. Accumulated two-thirds of all government-held gold (equivalent to eight years of imports). **Marshall and Dodge plans** rebuilt Europe/Japan — good for devastated nations, US economy, US geopolitical influence, and dollar's reserve status.

### Post-War Geopolitical System

US, Russia (USSR), Great Britain emerged as great powers — US clearly richest and most powerful. Germany/Japan/Italy largely destroyed; Great Britain essentially bankrupt; France devastated. China in civil war. Good cooperation with Russia initially, but two greatest powers with opposing ideologies soon entered "cold" war.

> "Every nation must choose between alternative ways of life... One way of life is based upon the will of the majority... free institutions, representative government, free elections... The second way of life is based upon the will of a minority forcibly imposed upon the majority."

NATO formed 1949 (12 countries); SEATO 1954 (US, UK, Australia, France, New Zealand, Philippines, Thailand, Pakistan); Warsaw Pact 1955 (8 Soviet-aligned countries). **Nuclear weapons** gave big negotiating chip — today 11 countries have or are on brink of having them. US fought Korean War (1950s), Vietnam (1960s-70s), Gulf Wars (1990, 2003), Afghanistan (2001-2021) — costly in money, lives, and public support.

For Soviet Union, spending enough to compete militarily and maintain empire pushed it into bankruptcy.

**Future military conflicts**: Next high-stakes conflict will be very different due to new technologies. Classically, country that wins wars spends more, invests more, and outlasts opposition. **Biggest military risk for leading powers: losing economic and technology wars** — military spending takes government money away from social programs, and military technologies go hand in hand with private sector technologies.

**Current military assessment**: US remains strongest overall but not dominant in all regions. Significant chance US would lose wars against China and Russia in their geographic areas of strength. Most worrying: **forceful move by China to bring Taiwan under its control.**

### Post-War Monetary and Economic Systems

One system for US-led camp, one for Soviet-led camp (built around ruble, which nobody wanted). Representatives of 44 countries gathered at **Bretton Woods, New Hampshire** (1944) to create system linking dollar to gold and other currencies to dollar. Dollar became world's leading reserve currency — natural because two World Wars made US richest country by far. By end of WWII, US had amassed greatest gold/money savings ever — about two-thirds of all government-held gold, equivalent to eight years of import purchases.

How it worked: within countries, people used government-controlled paper money. When buying from another country, exchanged own currency for other country's currency with help of central banks that settled in gold. Central banks shrank gold holdings relative to dollar debt (1945-1971) because dollar-denominated debt was considered as good as gold, convertible to it, and earned interest.

**IMF and World Bank** designed to support new global financial system. **New York as new global financial center.** Most important elements of geopolitical order: **United Nations** headquartered in New York, with Security Council dominated by war's victors.

### American Golden Age (1945-1966)

Post-war recession brief: unemployment doubled to ~4%, ~20 million needed employment outside military. Removal of rationing laws fueled consumer spending surge. Cheap veteran mortgages → housing boom. Marshall/Dodge plans fueled exports. Stocks cheap, dividend yields high → multidecade bull market reinforced NYC's financial center dominance.

Enough money for education, technologies that allowed US to go to the moon, and more. Dalio started investing 1961 (age 12). Born at right time/place — US leading manufacturing country, labor valuable, most adults could get good jobs, kids could get college education, majority middle class, majority happy. **Stock market hit high 1966, marking end of good times for 16 years, though nobody knew it then.**

**US banking went global**: 13 banks with foreign branches (1965) → 79 (1970) → 757 branches. "Guns and butter" fiscal policy — never thought about costs of space program, War on Poverty, Vietnam War. By late 1960s: real GDP growth ~0%, inflation ~6%, short-term rates ~8%.

### 1970s: Balance of Payments Crisis

**Classic run on gold**: Claims on gold vastly exceeded reserves. When no interest rate is high enough to make sense to hold debt rather than turn paper money in for gold, run on bank occurs. Summer 1971: Americans traveling in Europe had difficulty exchanging dollars. **August 1971: Nixon defaulted on gold commitments**, offering paper money instead. World moved to **unanchored fiat monetary system (Type 3).**

**Paul Volcker** was Nixon's undersecretary when link severed; headed Federal Reserve 1979-1987. Did more to shape dollar-based monetary system than anyone.

Inflation psychology: Americans bought things to "get ahead of inflation" — gold, waterfront properties. Gold went from $35 (1944 fixed) to $850 (1980). Social turmoil: Vietnam, oil embargoes, Watergate, **52 hostages in Tehran (444 days)**. By decade's end: real GDP growth ~2%, inflation ~14%, short-term rates ~13%.

**Petrodollar recycling**: Deficit dollars deposited in American banks, lent to Latin American/emerging countries. Savings and loan associations borrowed short to lend long. Inflation came in two big waves bracketed by periods of extreme monetary tightness, steep stock market declines, and deep recessions. **Early in 1970s, most Americans had never experienced inflation, so weren't wary of it and allowed it to blossom. By decade's end, traumatized by it and assumed it would never go away.** By end of 1970s: gold had surged, commodities returned ~15% annualized, but high inflation wiped out modest 5% annual nominal return for stocks and 4% for treasuries.

"This shift in leadership, while seemingly insignificant at time, would germinate into biggest single force to shape 21st century."

Move to monetary tightness broke backs of debtors, drove world economy into worst downturn since Great Depression. **Reagan elected 1980** — perceived as conservative who would impose discipline where needed; G7 countries made analogous conservative shifts. **Thatcher** in UK. Both had landmark fights with labor unions. Mexico defaulted August 1982 — interestingly, US stock market rallied in response.

> "Economics and politics have swings between the left and the right... When there is a great matter of one extreme, one should expect a comparable move in the opposite direction."

Best thing that happened: changed approach to decision making. ### 1980s-2008

Disinflationary boom: falling inflation/rates, stock boom in US. At same time, inflationary depressions hit debt-burdened emerging economies that lacked ways to bail themselves out. Debt restructuring progressed slowly from 1982 until **Brady Plan** (**Nicholas Brady**, US Treasury secretary) created agreements through early 1990s, ending "lost decade" for these countries. **This whole 1971-91 up-and-down debt cycle profoundly affected just about everyone in world** — result of going off gold standard, inflation that followed, and crushing it through tight monetary policy.

In markets, big cycle showed as: (a) soaring inflation-hedge assets and bear markets in bonds in 1970s, (b) bone-crushing monetary tightening 1979-81 making cash best investment, and (c) big bull markets in bonds and stocks with emerging market debt restructuring through 1980s.

**Soviet Union collapsed 1991** — couldn't afford empire, economy, and military in face of Reagan's arms-race spending. Apparent that communism had failed everywhere. World entered very prosperous period of globalization and free-market capitalism.

Three economic cycles peaked: 2000 (dot-com), 2007 (housing bubble), 2019 (pre-COVID). Period also saw rise of China, advances in technologies that replaced people — good for corporate profits but widened wealth/opportunity gaps. Countries and borders faded in importance — goods produced wherever most cost-effective. Lower/middle-income workers in developed countries suffered; workers in productive emerging countries saw big relative gains. **Accurate to say: Chinese workers and machines replaced middle-class American workers.**

**US-China trade symbiosis**: Chinese manufactured cheaply, loaned US money to buy goods. "A hell of a 'buy now, pay later' deal." Shocking that Chinese (earning ~40% of Americans) lending money to Americans. China joined WTO 2001 — now larger trading partner than US for ~70% of WTO member countries.

> "Most people pay attention to what they get and not where the money comes from to pay for it, so there are strong motivations for elected officials to spend a lot of borrowed money and make a lot of promises."

**Long-term debt cycle (1945-2008)**: Throughout, whenever Fed wanted economy to pick up, lowered rates and made money/credit more available. This increased stock/bond prices and demand. How it was done until 2008: interest rates cut, debts increased faster than incomes to create unsustainable bubbles. Changed when 2008 bubble burst and rates hit 0% for first time since Great Depression. Three monetary policies: (1) interest rate cuts (MP1), (2) quantitative easing — printing money to buy bonds (MP2), (3) fiscal-monetary coordination where government does debt-financed spending and central bank buys that debt (MP3).

### 2008-2020: Money-Financed Boom

2008 debt crisis led to interest rates hitting 0%. Three main reserve currency central banks (led by Fed) moved to printing money and buying financial assets. Prices rose — helpful for economy but particularly beneficial for those rich enough to own financial assets. Money did not trickle down proportionately. Wealth/income gaps grew to largest since 1930-45 period. Borrowed money essentially free — investment/corporate borrowers took advantage to drive stock prices and profits up.

**Trump elected 2016**: blunt-speaking capitalist/populist of right, led revolt against establishment "elites" by promising to support people with conservative values who had lost jobs. Cut corporate taxes, ran big budget deficits that Fed accommodated. While debt growth financed relatively strong growth, further widened wealth and values gap — leading "have-nots" to become increasingly resentful of "haves."

Conflicts with China over trade, technology, geopolitics, and capital intensifying. **Economic sanctions like those used in 1930-45 period being used or put on table.**

US government took massive debt, gave money to people and companies; Fed printed money and bought debt at unprecedented scale. Other central banks followed similar playbook. Central bank balance sheets expanded to levels near/beyond wartime records.

Fed's 2020 move parallels: **Roosevelt's March 1933**, **Nixon's August 1971**, **Volcker's August 1982**, **Bernanke's November 2008**, **Draghi's July 2012**. This has become standard operating procedure for central banks — will persist until it no longer works.

## Chapter 12: China's Rise

### Dalio's Approach

Nearly 40 years of direct experience. First trip 1984. Many urged him not to publish this chapter given high US-China tensions — any compliment to China alienates US readers; criticism infuriates Chinese. Dalio refused to stay silent: **US-China relationship is too important to leave unmentioned** by those who know both countries well. Not a scholar of Chinese culture or language, but has numerous direct encounters at every level from lowest to highest ranks.

Not ideological — practical approach like a doctor relying on logic and cause/effect. Urges readers to look past caricatured pictures painted by biased parties and shed stereotypes about "old communist China" — they're wrong. **Triangulate** with people who've spent significant time in China. Judges China through lens of **18 determinants** and **eight key measures of power**: education, competitiveness, innovation/technology, trade, economic output, military, financial center status, reserve currency status.

### China's 1,400-Year Power History

China historically stood among most powerful empires. Notable exception: ~1840-1950 steep decline. Seven of eight powers hit lowest points in 1940-50 period. Since then, most improved gradually until ~1980, then economic competitiveness and trade took off after Deng's reforms. Not coincidence: 1984-2008, debt growth in line with economic growth (very strong). After 2008, debts rose relative to incomes. **Xi Jinping** (2012) improved debt/economic management dramatically.

**Current position**: Roughly tied with US in trade, economic output, innovation/technology. Strong and quickly rising in military and education. Emerging but lagging as reserve currency and financial center.

### Major Chinese Dynasties

**Tang Dynasty (618-907)**: Considered by many the high point of Imperial China. Emperor **Taizong** unified China, established stable government, fostered quality education and trade. Prosperity lasted ~150 years. Declined: governance quality fell, fragmentation, internal conflict, financial deterioration, natural disasters.

**Song Dynasty (960-1279)**: Most innovative and dynamic economy in the world. Emperor **Taizu** centralized governance, reformed imperial examination system. Immense scientific/technological advancement. Invented first commodity-backed paper money — also first to overprint and devalue it: money supply nearly tripled 1209-1231. **Huizi notes**: market value fell over 90% between 1195 and 1230s. Conquered by **Mongol leader Kublai Khan**.

**Yuan Dynasty (1279-1368)**: Kublai Khan governed well initially, encouraged education. Created paper currency (**Marco Polo** marveled at it), then overprinted it. Corruption, expensive wars, failed succession structure led to collapse after less than a century.

**Ming Dynasty (1368-1644)**: Founded by **Hongwu Emperor** (born in poverty, rose to great general). Consolidated power in 14-year purge (~30,000 executions). Built meritocratic society. Expanded trade with Europe bringing silver. Failed monetary/fiscal management, **Little Ice Age** caused agricultural disaster, collapsed 1644.

**Qing Dynasty (1644-1912)**: Maximum territorial expansion, governing one-third of world's population. Three long-ruling emperors brought extended prosperity. Europeans arrived — **Opium Wars** began Century of Humiliation. **Taiping Rebellion** and other uprisings. Collapsed 1912.

### Chinese Philosophies and Governance

**Confucianism**: Harmony through hierarchy. Family roles extend to ruler/subjects. Values loyalty, education, meritocracy.
**Legalism**: Autocratic conquest, strict obedience. Western equivalent: fascism.
**Taoism**: Harmony with nature's laws, balance of yin and yang.

Core difference (per **Wang Qishan**, VP of China): Americans hold individual above all; Chinese put family/collective above everything. **America: bottom-up, optimized for individual. China: top-down, optimized for collective.** Chinese word "country" = characters for "state" and "family."

Chinese military philosophy: ideal way to win war is not fighting but developing power until simply displaying it causes opponent to capitulate. Few wars fought outside China; most fought for control of the giant plain within natural borders (mountains and seas).

China's broader economy went from primarily agricultural through various manufacturing incarnations (Bronze Age, Iron Age), developed trading through **Silk Road**, gave rise to rich merchant class. Private entrepreneurial businesses produced cycles in which wealth disparities grew until governments expropriated and redistributed wealth. China experienced debt cycles for same reasons described elsewhere: stable periods, bubble periods, crisis periods, and inflationary/hyperinflationary periods when money was printed.

**Tributary system**: If China was more powerful (typically the case), less powerful states paid "tribute" with gifts/favors and received peace guarantees, recognition, and trading opportunities. Subordinate countries maintained their customs.

### China's Monetary History

China went through full range of money/credit cycles. Currency mostly metal (copper, silver) despite inventing paper money in 9th century. Experienced repeated cycles: hard currency → paper backed by hard currency → unbacked paper → overprinting/collapse → return to hard currency.

**Tang Dynasty**: "Flying cash" — earliest non-hard money instruments (like checks) circulated by merchants.
**Song Dynasty**: First commodity-backed paper money. Also first to overprint and devalue — money supply nearly tripled between 1209 and 1231.
**Yuan and Ming**: Both created paper currencies, overprinted them, saw currency collapse.
From mid-14th century to ~1933: returned to metal coins. **Fabi** replaced yuan in 1935 (move from currency they couldn't print to one they could), experienced hyperinflation. **Renminbi** introduced after PRC founded (1949).

China never had global reserve currency — remarkable given its power through history. Reasons: (1) in years prior to frequent oceanic travel, no such thing as global reserve currency; (2) China never became extensive enough that large portion of world wanted to hold its promissory notes; (3) being commercial was not culturally valued (**Confucian ideology** placed merchants below scholars); (4) lesser capital market development — while ahead during Song Dynasty, by 1600s far behind Europe; (5) isolationist policies; (6) weaker corporate law, businesses tended to be family-owned; (7) state support for private commerce inconsistent across dynasties.

### Decline 1800-1949

China's post-1800 decline began when: (a) Qing Dynasty became decadent/weak, (b) British and other Western countries became strong, (c) financial/monetary system broke down under unpayable debts and money printing, (d) massive domestic rebellions and civil wars erupted.

**British East India Company** wanted tea, silk, porcelain but had nothing Chinese wanted — had to pay in silver. When silver ran out, smuggled opium from India. **First Opium War (1839-42)**: technologically superior British Navy won, imposed treaty giving Britain Hong Kong and opening ports. Eventually lost northern China to Russia/Japan, Taiwan to Japan.

> "It is easy to see why Mao saw capitalism as a system in which companies pursued profits through imperialism — enriching greedy elites while exploiting workers."

Qing government borrowed heavily from foreigners to fight internal rebellions. After **Boxer Rebellion** (1901): foreign powers demanded equivalent of ~18,000 tons of silver in reparations, structured around 40-year debt guaranteed by tariff income on ports they controlled. Combination of: (1) weak leadership, (2) unsound finances, (3) internal rebellions, (4) fighting foreigners, (5) natural disasters — produced mutually reinforcing decline: **Century of Humiliation**.

**Marx's dialectical materialism**: system for producing change through "contradictions" of "opposites" that produce "struggles" leading to progress. Process similar to Dalio's own iterative principles approach. Capitalism produces: (a) more productivity long-run, (b) big wealth differences, (c) capital markets that overextend then break down.

### Three Phases of China's Rise (1949-Present)

**Phase 1 — Mao (1949-1976)**: Consolidated power, built institutional foundation, ruled as communist emperor. Strict communist system with government ownership and tight bureaucratic controls. End of WWII led to repatriation of most foreigners (except Hong Kong and Taiwan) and civil war between communists and capitalists.

**Phase 2 — Deng and Successors (1978-2012)**: **"Reform and opening up."** Three-part 70-year plan: (1) double incomes by 1980s end, (2) quadruple GDP per capita by 2000 (achieved 1995, five years early), (3) reach medium-developed levels by 2050. **"Socialist market economy" / "socialism with Chinese characteristics."**

Deng reformed governance: shifted from single-leader to **Politburo Standing Committee** voting. 1982 constitution: term limits (two five-year terms), collective leadership, greater freedoms to "seek truth from facts." Relied on **Lee Kuan Yew** of Singapore for advice. Lee Kuan Yew said without hesitation Deng was greatest leader of 20th century.

**Dalio's first China visit (1984)**: Invited by **CITIC** (China's only "window company"). Per capita income <$200/year. People smart and civilized; poverty widely shared. Goal heard often: **"break the iron rice bowl"** — no longer provide demotivating guaranteed employment/basic benefits; replace with incentive-based compensation. **Globalization** helped — world wanted to include China. Deng was eager learner; relied on **Lee Kuan Yew** of Singapore and other **"Asian Tiger"** leaders for advice.

Operating from dingy hotel, lacked adequate funding. Had clear mission, smart people, open-mindedness for rapid learning. "Not a job but noble mission." Over decades they built Chinese financial markets among world's largest.

**Tiananmen Square (1989)**: Movement to democratize China grew into demonstrations. Leadership split on how to handle it. Deng sided with conservatives' crackdown. **Madame Gu** (close CITIC friend whose brother was China's minister of defense): idealistic Mao follower in early years, lost husband to Cultural Revolution persecution, rose to senior CITIC job — cried at prospect of return to those terrible old days.

Most Chinese friends eventually believed government made right move — greatest fear was revolutionary disorder. Over next decade, economy continued strong growth; relations with West became better than ever.

**Globalization era**: **Globalization** (helped China immensely) can be said to have begun 1995 with formation of **WTO** (epoch ended with election of **Donald Trump** in 2016). China joined **WTO in 2001** — position in world trade soared. 2001: US had more trade than China with 80% of WTO members. Now: China larger trading partner for ~70% of those countries.

Symbiotic US-China relationship developed: Chinese manufactured cheaply, loaned Americans money to buy goods. "A hell of a 'buy now, pay later' deal." Shocking that Chinese (earning ~40% of Americans) lending money to Americans — reflection of how deeply Americans willing to get into debt to finance overconsumption and how much more Chinese valued saving. Emerging countries wanting to save in bonds of leading reserve currency can lead those countries to become **overindebted**.

From 1978-1997, Chinese economy grew at average 10%/year, sextupling in size while experiencing average inflation rate of just 8%. Reserves grew from $4 billion to nearly $150 billion (inflation-adjusted: over $250 billion). 1978: reserves covered 60% of annual imports. 1998: covered more than 125% of imports (nearly 800% of foreign debt service).

**Successors continue reforms (1997-2008)**: **Jiang Zemin** and **Hu Jintao** continued on Deng's basic path through many ups and downs (though more ups than downs). 1997 **Asian financial crisis**: **Zhu Rongji** (bold reformer at top of party) led successful debt/corporate restructuring — sales of unprofitable state-owned enterprises, building exports and foreign exchange reserves, crackdown on corruption, further market development. Extremely controversial, hurt people who benefited from old system — took courage, intelligence, support from top to execute. **China's 1992 "triangular" debt crisis**: Arose from five major government-owned banks lending to large, inefficient, unprofitable state-owned enterprises. Used best practices ("bad banks") modified for Chinese environment.

**Why 1978-2008 worked so well**: (1) World in peace/prosperity phase where globalization and capitalism flourished — beliefs that goods/services should be produced wherever most cost-effective, free flows of talented people, nationalism is bad, global equal opportunity good; (2) Deng swung pendulum from communist/isolationist policies that worked terribly to market/capitalist and open-door policies that worked terrifically, leading China to learn, attract foreign capital, become giant exporter and big saver.

Post-war periods of peace/prosperity typical when leading power isn't threatened and emerging countries aren't yet threatening — can learn from each other in symbiotic way, using capital markets to mutual benefit, until emerging power becomes strong enough to threaten. **These circumstances lifted most boats, especially boats of global elites.** China rose to nearly comparable power to US; together they created most of world's new wealth and new technologies. Europe (source of greatest global powers 15th-20th centuries) became relatively weak. Japan and Russia became secondary powers.

**Belt and Road Initiative**: extends through Central Asia (Kazakhstan, Pakistan, Tajikistan, Afghanistan) to Europe, through Arabian Peninsula and South Asia into Mediterranean and Africa. **Amounts invested are enormous — largest such program since Marshall Plan.** While appreciated by recipient countries, sparked resentments: some countries having problems repaying loans, US seeing China's soft power lessen American influence.

## Chapter 13: US-China Relations and Wars

### The Emergence of US-China Conflicts (2008-Present)

Classic pattern: periods of prosperity financed by debt growth lead to debt bubbles and large wealth gaps. In US, bubble burst 2008 (as in 1929). Interest rates pushed to 0% (as 1931). Central banks printed money and bought financial assets after 2008 (like 1934). Benefited "haves" more than "have-nots." Wealth gaps grew wider still. Those whose jobs taken by Chinese and immigrants began to rise up against globalization's elites. **Populism and nationalism grew around world** (as in 1930s).

> "The era of peace, prosperity, and globalization began to wane, giving way to an era of conflicts between the rich and the poor within countries and between the rising country (China) and the dominant world power (the US)."

**November 2008**: G20 countries gathered in Washington DC, agreed to jointly stimulate via fiscal/monetary policies. Debt growth in China significantly faster than economic growth (2009-2012).

### Current Positions

US in late-cycle decline: too much debt, must monetize it, pays negative real interest rates. Owes other countries (including China) a lot of money. Dollar-denominated debt shares disproportionately large relative to: (a) percentage international investors would hold to balance portfolios, (b) sizes of reserve holdings appropriate for trade/capital flow needs, (c) size of US economy relative to others. Those most responsible for determining holdings shares not inclined to increase shares — in fact considering reducing, which if happens will require larger purchases by Fed.

China experienced classic collapse then revolutionary reforms producing great advances. US has military bases in 70+ countries — uneconomical to maintain. Most Americans don't know where Taiwan is or how to spell it. Challenge for Chinese: lack of continuity of policies in US arising from seemingly whimsical shifts in what matters to American public.

> "All countries' success depends on sustaining the strengthening forces without producing the excesses that lead to countries' declines."

### Seven Types of Wars

1. Trade/economic  2. Technology  3. Geopolitical  4. Capital  5. Military  6. Culture  7. War with ourselves

These are interrelated extensions of one bigger evolving conflict. Time is on China's side. Need to watch "red lines" and "trip wires."

### Trade/Economic War

Three criticisms of China: (1) government limits market access, protects domestic industries; (2) government support extracts technologies from foreign companies; (3) intellectual property theft (one in five North America-based companies in 2019 CNBC Global CFO Council survey claimed IP stolen by Chinese companies). Both parties agree on being hawkish on China. Most dangerous escalation: cutting off essential imports (US cutting China's oil/technologies; China cutting rare earth elements, excluding companies like General Motors). Both shifting to decoupling — **"dual circulation"** (China). China's rate of reducing dependencies will be much greater over next 5-10 years.

> "When things are going well it is easy to keep the moral high ground. However, when the fighting gets tough, it becomes easier to justify doing that which was previously considered immoral."

### Technology War

**Much more serious** — whoever wins technology war probably wins military and all other wars. US ahead in advanced AI chips, behind in 5G. US tech market cap ~4x China's (understates China's strength — excludes Huawei, Ant Group). **Tencent and Alibaba** are 7th and 8th largest tech companies globally. **40% of world's largest civilian supercomputers** in China. China leads in some AI/big data, quantum computing, fintech dimensions. STEM graduates ~8x US. Resources poured into tech far greater than US — venture capitalists and government providing "virtually unlimited amounts."

**China will probably advance technologies faster**: big data + big AI + big computing = superior decision making. Collecting vastly more data per person, have 4x as many people. US relative position declining — China that marveled at handheld calculators 37 years ago.

**If US shuts off Chinese access to essential technologies, that signals major step toward shooting war.** **Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company** dynamic especially important. In 5-10 years China poised to be much more independent. There is a tech decoupling underway that will have huge impact.

### Geopolitical War

**Sovereignty probably China's biggest issue** — mainland, Taiwan, Hong Kong, East/South China Seas. Century of Humiliation motivates complete sovereignty. Chinese also believe US would topple CCP if it could — intolerable existential threat.

**Taiwan: most dangerous sovereignty issue.** China now stronger militarily in that region. US not fighting would be great geopolitical win for China — would signal **decline of US Empire in the Pacific** like **British loss of Suez Canal** signaled end of British Empire in Middle East. In British case it signaled end of pound as reserve currency. Dalio believes US would probably lose fight for Taiwan. War with China over Taiwan would be very unpopular in US.

> "I would worry a lot if we were to see a 'Fourth Taiwan Strait Crisis.'"

**Belt and Road Initiative**: countries wrestling with US vs China alignment. Most say China more important economically; US has military edge but doubts about US willingness to fight. Chinese generally want tributary-like relationships with non-rival countries.

### The Capital War

No attractive world reserve currency alternative to dollar currently exists. China's clearing system underdeveloped, money doesn't flow freely in/out. But history shows: when currencies aren't desired AND no attractive alternatives exist, currencies still devalue — capital flows into other investments (gold, commodities, stocks, property).

If US-China capital war intensifies, Chinese currency/capital market development would be detrimental to US, beneficial to China. Without US attacks, Chinese markets will develop quickly to compete with US. US policymakers must decide: disrupt this evolutionary path aggressively OR accept evolution leading China to become stronger and less vulnerable to US pressure.

### Military War

Impossible to visualize — warfare types include nuclear, biological, cyber, chemical, space, untested. **China militarily stronger than US in East/South China Seas**; US stronger globally. All-out conflict would be World War III, much deadlier than WWII. China's military improvement extremely fast; some imagine broad superiority in 5-10 years. Hottest spots: Taiwan, East/South China Seas, North Korea. Time on China's side — in China's interest to delay war.

> "When countries have big internal disorder, it is an opportune moment for opposing countries to aggressively exploit their vulnerabilities."

### Culture War

**Chinese culture**: top-down, collective interest, filial respect, each person knows role. **American culture**: bottom-up, personal freedom, individualism, revolutionary thinking. Both deeply embedded, neither will give up values. Chinese believe capable leaders choosing is preferable (like **Plato's view**). Chinese governance like big multigenerational companies.

**Houston Rockets example (2019)**: GM **Daryl Morey** tweeted support for Hong Kong protests. Americans: free speech issue. Chinese: held whole league responsible, dropped NBA games, demanded Morey fired. Irreconcilable difference: free speech importance vs organization responsible for individual actions.

> "The main thing to realize and accept is that the Chinese and Americans have different values."

**Chinese leadership style when in superior position**: (a) relative positions clear, (b) subordinate obeys, (c) punishment for disobedience. COVID mask example: Chinese accepted government direction, Americans fought it — affected case numbers and economic impact.

### War With Ourselves

Greatest war is internal — most control over own strength/weakness. 18 determinants are measurable. Internal wars more important than external in both countries.

> "As for the case at hand, the internal wars and challenges in both China and the US are more important and bigger than external wars and challenges."

Internal wars include: political wars within leadership at all government levels, wars between factions (rich/poor, rural/urban, conservatives/progressives, ethnic groups), demographic changes, climate change. Fortunately, the most important forces are **within our control and measurable** — allows seeing how we're doing and making corrections.

What makes a great empire (condensed chain): strong education (knowledge, skills, character, civility, work ethic) → improved civility and low corruption → people working well together → good resource allocation (improved by openness to global thinking) → greater global competitiveness → strong income growth → increased investment in infrastructure/education/R&D → rapidly increasing productivity → valuable inventions → strong military protecting trade routes → preeminent currency/equity/credit markets → **reserve currency status** → world-leading financial center. Even small-population empires become leading world powers if they manage themselves well.

> "By and large we will get what we deserve. As Churchill said, 'Deserve Victory!'"

## The Future: Looking Ahead

### The Limits of Extrapolation

Simple extrapolation suggests major power shifts over next 20 years: China up 25-30%, US up ~15%. But **paradigm shifts** make the future fundamentally different. Standing in 1750, monarchies and agriculture seemed permanent, life expectancy stuck at ~30 years — capitalism, democracy, and US global leadership were unimaginable. **Digital Revolution** shattered many well-established beliefs (e.g., negative bond yields were once thought impossible). Good indicators help distinguish real paradigm shifts from temporary fads.

> "While extrapolating the past is generally a reasonable thing to do, also be prepared to be surprised because the future will be much different than you expect it to be."

Dalio's key lesson from 50 years investing: identifying, understanding, and adapting to paradigm shifts is essential, even when you can't fully anticipate them. Post-1945 trends create false confidence — most people extrapolate positive trends and ignore darker historical patterns.

Cycles barely register compared to mega-macro trends of human progress, but **they damage and kill large numbers of people**. Major historical crises: Panic of 1819, Great Depression, WWI, WWII, 2008 financial crisis, COVID-19. Dalio's principle: **bet on upside from productivity improvements, but not so aggressively that cycles knock you out of the game.**

### Five Key Determinants with Biggest Impact

1. **Innovation**
2. **Debt/money/capital market cycle**
3. **Internal order and disorder cycle**
4. **External order and disorder cycle**
5. **Acts of nature**

Determinants sometimes rise/fall together (mutually reinforcing), sometimes one country's gain is another's loss. New technologies raise all living standards (positive-sum for humanity) but advantage countries better at inventing (zero-sum in competitive position). Military strength benefits some at expense of others (zero-sum or negative-sum).

### Innovation — Most Powerful Determinant

Humanity's inventiveness accelerating. Key inventions list: telephone (1876), electric light (1879), combustion engine (1885), airplane (1903), antibiotics (1928), computer (1939), nuclear weapons (1945), internet (1983), iPhone (2007), CRISPR (2012). **Quantum computing with AI**: will be to traditional computing what computer was to abacus. Human lifespan likely to increase 20-25%+.

**Human-computer collaboration**: computers have superior memory, data processing speed, no emotional mistakes; humans provide imagination, intuition, common sense, value judgments, emotional intelligence. **Quantum computing with AI** will provide humanity with vastly more power to see, understand, and shape things.

**Investment implications**: Companies making innovations are best bets, BUT returns depend on how governments divide productivity profits. If financially overextended with large wealth gaps, creates headwinds. Price matters: can lose money in great companies (too expensive) or make money in bad ones (too cheap). Downsides exist: technologies for harm develop alongside advances.

**Innovation gauge**: combination of external rankings, innovation per capita measures, and absolute share of key metrics (researchers, R&D, patents, Nobel Prizes, venture capital). **US at top** with marginal lead over China (second, rising fast). China's position rising while US steady. **Whoever wins technology war usually wins economic and military wars.**

> "Inventiveness and increases in living standards will probably get a lot better a lot faster — if humanity doesn't kill itself first."

### Debt/Money/Capital Market Cycle

Biggest driver of economic ups and downs. Promises in reserve currencies too large to be paid in hard money. Money will probably be printed to service debts. Wealth probably increasingly redistributed. **Big risk is NOT default but that creditors hold assets devalued by inflation.** Reserve currencies decline slowly then very quickly — guilder and pound both plunged during geopolitical crises.

Most probably will **decline analogously to past reserve currency declines: slowly for a long time, then very quickly**. Reserve currencies live on long after fundamentals cease to justify prominence (deeply entrenched), then abruptly plunge when fundamentals make holding debt a bad deal. Why fall happens fast: currency's decline rate outpaces interest paid to debt holders; net losses lead to selling, causing more losses (self-reinforcing).

Dalio believes great **wealth transfer from creditors to debtors** is coming (like biblical Jubilee described in Chapter 3). Financial conditions easy to assess (like assessing people/companies): check surpluses vs. deficits, assets vs. liabilities, debt currency. These long-term drivers make it easy to see which countries/currencies are vulnerable. **Anticipating exactly when the big fall happens is difficult.**

**Debt burden gauge** based on: (a) debt vs asset levels, (b) surpluses/deficits, (c) debt service vs GDP, (d) own vs foreign currency, (e) own vs foreign holders, (f) credit rating. Index excludes reserve currency status — shows exposure if that status were lost. US debt burdens high but in dollars (can print) — reduces default risk but increases devaluation risk. **If US lost reserve currency status, serious financial trouble.** Russia/Germany strongest (least indebted). China in middle — debts moderately high, mostly in own currency, mostly held by Chinese.

### Internal Order/Disorder Cycle

> *Romance of the Three Kingdoms*: "The empire long divided must unite and long united must divide. Thus it has ever been."

Key principle: **peace is good, war is costly.** When parties cooperate and compete well without wasting resources fighting, productivity and living standards rise. When conflict and fighting dominate, they waste resources and destroy more than they produce.

> "Power rules and tests of power are the ways one learns who rules."

**US in Stage 5** (bad finances, intense conflict). **China in Stage 3** (peace and prosperity). Changes can happen quickly (Arab Spring, Hong Kong, Syria/Afghanistan, Peru/Chile protests leading to revolutionary changes). **Odds of US Stage 6 within 10 years: ~30%** — dangerously high.

US polarization (2019 Pew survey): 55% Republicans/47% Democrats view other side as "more immoral." 61% Republicans/54% Democrats said other party doesn't share their values. 80% Democrats think Republican Party taken over by racists; 82% Republicans think Democratic Party taken over by socialists. 15% Republicans/20% Democrats thought country better off if large numbers of other side "just died." Nearly half of Republican parents (third of Democratic) displeased if child married from other party (compared to ~5% for both in 1960).

**Historical comparison**: Overall conflict as high as late 1960s but meaningfully less. "Political conflict" index highest since early 1920s (post-war recession and massive labor unrest). Comparable periods: 1900s-1910s (Progressive movement, backlash against robber barons), 1860s (Civil War). Risks high but not unprecedentedly so — still scary because world's leading power on the brink. Signs in US: some contesting election validity, willingness to fight for aims — bears watching.

US ~70% through big cycle. History shows reversing decline very difficult — requires undoing so many things already done. If spending > earnings and liabilities > assets, can only reverse by working harder or consuming less. Question: whether Americans can face challenges honestly and adapt.

**Can US reverse its decline?** Key questions: Will we modify capitalism to both increase pie size (productivity) and divide it well? With new technologies making employing people increasingly unprofitable: should we invest in people to make them productive even when uneconomic? What if our international competitors choose not to?

US has longest-lasting constitutional system — makes it less likely to be abandoned but more traumatic if it is. Greatest challenges are internal: can it remain strong and united, or will it continue to allow division and internal struggles to lead to decline? US has shown great capacity to bend without breaking in its 245-year history.

### External Order/Disorder Cycle

Greatest conflict between US and China — 1 standard deviation above normal. US-China in four types of war (trade, technology, capital, geopolitical) but not military. **These four types precede military wars by 5-10 years.** Major empires at war a little more than half the time since 1500; military wars started ~once every 10 years.

Easy to see when internal order threatened: when close elections adjudicated and losers respect decisions (order respected); when power fought over and grabbed (signals revolutionary change risk). Stage 6 most dysfunctional and harmful, but dysfunction increases in stages leading up to it — conflicts exist throughout society, not just government.

**Graham Allison** (*Destined for War*): nearly equal powers with irreconcilable differences led to military war in 12 of 16 cases; military buildups associated with major wars 80-90% of cases. **Dalio's net probability of big war in next 10 years: 35%** — essentially a wild guess but dangerously high.

**Main seemingly irreconcilable difference: Taiwan.** Only possible trigger for military war between two greatest powers in next 10 years. Both sides have capacity for mutually assured destruction — should prevent hot war for foreseeable future, unless unexpected technological breakthrough gives asymmetrical advantage.

> "It is impossible to confidently say who the winners and losers in the next big war would be."

### Acts of Nature and Climate Change

Droughts, floods, pandemics historically inflicted more harm on people than people inflicted on themselves — killed millions, disrupted economies, contributed to falls of many empires and dynasties. Major historical events: Spanish flu, Indian famines (multiple), Cocoliztli epidemics, Russian famine, French famine, China's Great Leap Forward famine, COVID-19, HIV/AIDS.

**Temperature and CO2 data** show clear global warming at accelerating pace — current warming is unusually rapid compared to long-term historical record. **Extreme environmental events: <50/year (1970) to ~200/year (2020)**, trending higher. Dollar cost of catastrophes (inflation-adjusted) also trending higher with extreme spikes (Hurricane Katrina, Japan earthquake, Hurricanes Harvey/Irma/Maria).

> "It is pretty clear to me that humanity and natural evolution together are doing great damage to the environment that will be very costly in both money and quality of life."

**Notre Dame-Global Adaptation Index (ND-GAIN)** quantifies country vulnerability. Most vulnerable: Philippines, Indonesia, Vietnam, Bangladesh, Thailand. India faces massive vulnerability (-2.4 score) due to geography and agricultural dependence. Nordic countries, Canada, Russia benefit (negative scores). Impact differs by location, climate, and industries. Change is slow, steady, well-telegraphed — allows adaptation and innovation. Expects humanity will respond **"slowly and reactively"** rather than proactively.

### The Next 10 Years (from 2021)

**Most important dynamics** — three critical cycles within cycles within cycles:

**1. Short-term debt/economic cycle** (~8 years typically): Central banks stimulate → create money/credit → attempt slowdown → never get timing right → bubbles, busts, repeat. Recent cycles began: April 2020 (largest ever stimulus), 2008, 2001, 1990, 1982, 1980, 1974, 1970, 1960. Current situation: enormous stimulus, limited slack, moderate to strong bubble signs, high interest rate sensitivity. Dalio's estimate: next downturn ~4 years from publication. More worried about excess money printing and loss of value than downturn itself.

> "Based on my estimates, there is a significant chance the next downturn will come around the time of the next presidential election in the US."

**2. Internal political cycle**: US congressional elections every 2 years, presidential every 4. China changes every 5 and 10 years, no term limit. Internal order/disorder typically lags economic cycle (people less confrontational in good times).

**3. Escalating US-China conflicts**: Both preparing with ~5-year plans for greater self-sufficiency and preparedness. Taiwan and East/South China Seas: **"significant risk approaching conflict between unstoppable force and immovable object."** China = unstoppable force; US = immovable object. Other nations (Russia, India, Japan, Korea, European/Middle Eastern powers) play important roles. Alliances likely to harden over next 5 years.

> "These things point toward the next big risk point being around five years from this writing, give or take a couple of years."

### Timing Uncertainty

> "There is nothing precise about the timing of these cycles."

Like hurricane seasons — know they're likely, prepare, watch for storms emerging. Trend and fundamentals suggest storms getting stronger. Unknown >> known. History told precisely, future exactly opposite.

> "For an investor, understanding history accurately is of no use relative to being a bit more right than wrong about the future."

### Dalio's Core Principles for Dealing with Uncertainty

> "Whatever success I've had has been more due to my knowing how to deal with what I don't know than anything I know."

Betting on future = betting on probabilities, and nothing is certain — not even the probabilities themselves. Non-investors also place bets via life decisions. Must know how to place bets assuming likely to be wrong a lot.

1. **Know all possibilities, think worst-case scenarios, eliminate intolerable ones.** Most important: don't get knocked out of the game. Learned from **1982 big mistake** that nearly broke him. Now maintains "end of the world" portfolio (covers depressions, devaluations, revolutions, wars, pandemics, health problems, death). Liberating and exciting to operate knowing worst-case covered — gives safety, freedom, ability for great results.
2. **Diversify.** If bunch of attractive but unrelated bets, can reduce risk up to 80% without reducing upside. Actually old, well-established life strategy. Chinese saying: **"a smart rabbit has three burrows"** — three places to go if one becomes dangerous. This principle saved many lives when things got bad.
3. **Deferred over immediate gratification.**
4. **Triangulate among smartest people possible.** Tag along with smartest people to stress test thinking and learn.

> "It is through these principles that I got so much upside with relatively little downside and a steadily improving future, albeit with bumps along the way."

**For policy makers**: Use gauges or make your own statistics to (1) measure health of your country and others, (2) see if improving or worsening, (3) make changes to determinants to get better future.

> "May the Force of Evolution be with you."

### How to Read the Framework

Leadership can't be measured objectively — "how can you measure whether Donald Trump was a good leader or a bad one?"

### Battle Between Innovation and Multiple Challenges

**Two main conclusions**: (1) Humanity's inventiveness will probably lead to great advances while debt/economic cycle, internal order cycle, external order cycle, and acts of nature will almost certainly pose problems — core tension: "there will be a battle between humanity's inventiveness and these other challenges." (2) Very different conditions exist within and between countries — will determine which rise/decline and how.

### Self-Reinforcing Cycles

Key principle: determinants reinforce each other in cycles. Stronger education → stronger incomes (positive). Weakening trade → higher indebtedness (negative). Creates Big Cycles when determinants align. When weak and weakening, empires decline. When strong and strengthening, empires rise.

**Triple threat decline pattern**: When (1) finances deteriorating + (2) high internal conflict (wealth/values gaps) + (3) challenged by strong foreign rivals → mutually reinforcing decline. Deteriorating finances prevent satisfying domestic needs AND financing war.
