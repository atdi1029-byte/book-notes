## Preface

### Systematic Trading and Investing

- **Robert Carver** admits he's bad at making financial decisions — becomes emotional, irrational despite being knowledgeable about finance
- Spent 20 years investing his own money and nearly a decade managing funds for large institutions
- Gap between theory and practice: knows what to do, but fear and greed cloud judgment when clicking the trade button
- Solution: **systematize financial decision making** to remove emotion and commit to a consistent strategy

### The Promise vs. Reality of Trading Systems

- Many authors offer trading systems that require subjective interpretation — not truly systematic
- Some systems are dangerous: trade too quickly, too expensively, in excessive size
- This book teaches how to develop your own trading system tailored to your needs

### Three Example Trader Types

**Asset Allocating Investor:**
- Allocates funds amongst and within different **asset classes**
- Uses systematic methods to avoid short-term fads
- Usually don't use leverage; uses unleveraged passive **exchange traded funds (ETFs)**
- Carver's own portfolio includes basket of ETFs managed using asset allocating investor principles

**Semi-Automatic Trader:**
- Makes own educated guesses but wants systematic **framework** for position and risk management
- Comfortable with **leverage** and investing with **derivatives**
- Active in equity index and commodity spread bet markets

**Staunch Systems Trader:**
- True believer in benefits of fully systematic trading
- Uses systematic **trading rules** to forecast price changes
- Can use **very simple trading rules** or sources of **alternative beta**
- Carver trades over 40 futures contracts with fully systematic set of eight trading rules

### Book Structure

**Part One — "Theory":** Theoretical basis for systematic strategies
**Part Two — "Toolbox":** **Back-testing** and **portfolio optimization** — powerful but dangerous when misused
**Part Three — "Framework":** Complete and extendable **framework** for creating systematic strategies
**Part Four — "Practice":** Three uses of framework for each trader type

## Introduction

### The Barclays Trade

- **23 January 2009** — Carver managing portfolio of systematic trading strategies for large hedge fund in London
- Research showed historically when people's fears confirmed by terrible economic numbers was often best time to buy
- Careful analysis: banks hardest hit by financial crisis should rebound most
- Particularly attracted to **Barclays** — balance sheet in relatively good condition
- GDP figures worse than expected: falling by 1.5%
- **Barclays** dropped 15% almost immediately to lowest level Carver had ever seen
- Emotions took over: quickly changed orders to only 1% of portfolio at risk
- Bought Barclays for 53p per share; sold for average of £2.50 each few months later
- **One of the biggest mistakes of his investing career** — had planned carefully, then let emotions win

> "I had planned carefully and meticulously, done everything right, and then at the last moment let my emotions get the better of me."

### September 2008 — Lehman Day

- US government trying to rescue **Lehman Brothers**, insurer **AIG**, mortgage agencies **Freddie Mac** and **Fannie Mae**
- Computer trading systems ran smoothly while markets in savage crisis
- Colleague showed live estimate of day's profitability
- **First time in firm's history showing ten digit number** — made over a billion dollars in single day
- Computer system stuck to preprogrammed trading rules while terrified humans discussed closing it down

> "Our computer system had stuck to its preprogrammed set of trading rules and mechanically exploited the market moves almost to perfection, whilst terrified humans had discussed closing it down."

### Why System Trade Now

- Institutional investors moved from expensive **active management** to cheaper **passive management**
- Low inflation active management fees of 1%+ are intolerable drag on performance
- **Passive indexing** is effectively systematic trading, albeit very simple
- Hedge fund returns separate into: **Beta** (market tracking), **Alpha** (skill), **Alternative beta**

## Chapter One: The Flawed Human Brain

### The Death of Rational Economic Man

- Classical finance (1950s) assumed purely rational actors leading to perfectly efficient markets
- From 1980s onward, psychologists found the economist's framework highly amusing
- Our brains carry **baggage from the distant past** — hardwired for survival where quick thinking beat slow consideration
- Created **behavioural finance** with its unifying model: **prospect theory**

### Why We Run Losses and Stop Out Profits

- **Prospect theory** explains investor mistakes — most people show greatest reluctance to take losses, described as **"get-evenitis"** by **Hersh Shefrin**
- Taking losses is psychologically painful; conversely, when positions rise we eagerly take profits
- Both effects contradict classical financial theory
- Back-tested an "early profit taker" vs "early loss taker" on 31 futures contracts: early loss taker beat early profit taker in 27 of them

> "Unfortunately taking small profits and letting losses run is almost always a bad idea."

### Gamblers Anonymous

- Addictive gambling factors: illusion of control, near misses, frequent small prizes, rapid continuous flow
- Illusion of control almost absent when investing systematically
- Near miss and rapidity factors clearly more associated with very frequent **day trading**
- Only small percentage of day traders manage to cover costs and make reasonable income

> "Their frantic churning of trades is not so very different from the addict spending hours in front of a slot machine."

### Sticking to the Plan — Commitment Mechanisms

- No point running systematic trading strategy unless you can stick to it
- Author calls process of interference **meddling** — due to biggest **cognitive bias** of all: **overconfidence**
- We think being cleverer and knowing more implies better decisions — but usually don't, thanks to cognitive biases

**Odysseus and Victor Niederhoffer:**
- Earliest commitment mechanism: **Odysseus** ordered crew to stuff ears with beeswax, tie him to mast to hear Sirens without danger
- **Victor Niederhoffer** locked himself inside racquetball court, issued instructions to assistant and future wife **Susan**: "Do not listen to my entreaties if I wish to double further. If losses reach 50%, reduce positions by one-half."

> "'Do not listen to my entreaties if I wish to double further. If the losses reach 50% of the winnings, reduce my positions by one-half. If I beg to be released, sell everything out.'"

- Victor immediately called to override — Susan closed entire position and Victor lived to fight another day

### Trading Systems Must Be Objective

- Many systems aren't objective and require discretion
- **Technical analysts** rarely agree on which patterns exist in particular chart
- Creating purely objective system is powerful commitment mechanism
- Only objective systems can produce **back-tests**

### Automation — The Dog Joke

- Old joke: best systematic trading setup consists of computer, man and dog
- Computer runs fully automated strategy, man feeds dog, and dog bites man if he touches computer
- Full automation is problematic for **discretionary traders**, though they could automate position and risk management
- System which is fully automated but not completely trusted is potentially lethal

### Three Pitfalls: Over-fitting, Overtrading, Over-betting

**Over-fitting:**
- **Narrative fallacy**: our brains see patterns where there are none
- Trading rules selected through **fitting** can be highly tuned to one set of market conditions

**Overtrading:**
- **Illusory superiority**: nearly all drivers consider themselves above average
- Both amateur investors and professional managers trade too frequently
- Extra costs from frequent trading require overconfidence to overcome

**Over-betting:**
- If ten times **leveraged**, 10% chance of losing half your capital on any given day
- Over betting is endemic amongst amateur investors with easy access to leverage through derivatives

### Standard Deviation Concept

- **Standard deviation** = measure of risk; **volatility** used as shorthand
- One unit = one **sigma**
- Annualise daily: multiply by √256 = 16; divide by 16 to go from annual to daily
- Average daily returns annualise by multiplying by 256
- Equities: ~20% annualized standard deviation
- Bonds: ~1.5% (two year) to ~8% (ten year)

### Gaussian Normal Distribution

- If returns are Gaussian normal, one sigma = ~68% of observations, two sigma = ~95%
- Example at 200% annualized std dev: 12.5% daily std dev, ~2.5% chance of >24.2% daily loss
- Normal distribution underestimates extreme losses: 1914-2014 Dow fell >4 sigma ~30 times vs once-per-century theoretical expectation

## Chapter Two: Systematic Trading Rules

### What Makes a Good Trading Rule

**Ideas First vs. Data Mining:**
- Two approaches: **data mining** (find profitable patterns) vs. **ideas first** (develop hypothesis, then test)
- **Leda Braga**, head of Systematica:

> "There's a creative moment when you think of a hypothesis, maybe it's that interest rate attracts currency rates. So we think about that first before mining the data."

- Ideas first: less fitting risk, smaller subset tested, simpler rules
- Data first: reduces bias toward known patterns, may discover novel strategies
- Author favors ideas first for intuitive, transparent rules

**Explainable Profits:** Understanding why a rule makes money indicates whether profits will continue. Beware **narrative fallacy**.

**Simplicity:** More parameters = less explainable, more **over-fitting** risk.

**Systematisable:** Not all trading styles suit systematisation — esoteric technical analysis, **merger arbitrage**, strategies using Google/Twitter sentiment lack sufficient historical data.

### When Trading Rules Don't Work

**Never Really Worked:** Could be **over-fitted**, rely on forward-looking data, suffer **survivorship bias**, underestimate costs, have too brief history

**The World Changes:** **Relative value** strategies: if all systematic traders use same rules, returns vanish. **Trend followers** face synchronized crashes.

### Why Certain Rules are Profitable

**Risk Premia:**
- Markets analogous to insurance: buying protection = lower returns; selling insurance = **risk premium**
- Equities earn premium over bonds; longer bonds over shorter; **equity value** strategies
- **John Paulson** made billions in 2006 buying cheap credit default swap insurance on risky mortgages

**Sharpe Ratio (SR):**
- SR = mean return / standard deviation (annualized)
- ~256 business days, so annualized SR ≈ 16 × daily SR
- Assumes symmetric gains/losses; useless for comparing assets with **skew**

**Skew:**
- **Negative skew**: higher chance of large down move (equities mildly negative)
- **Positive skew**: large up moves more likely (safe havens like gold, Swiss francs)
- October 1987: stock market fell 20% in single day (~20 sigma event)
- VIX/V2TX futures: buying = highly positive skew, selling = negative skew
- People overestimate rare probabilities, overpaying for disaster insurance

**Leverage:**
- Classical theory assumes free borrowing, but many investors cannot or will not borrow
- High SR but low volatility assets remain unloved by those requiring high returns who can't borrow
- Lucky leveraged investors outperform long-run but crisis creates death spiral — forced sales at worst prices

> "Is leverage dangerous? Not always. Leverage alone is a poor measure of risk, as varying amounts make sense for different assets. What makes it deadly is the potential for large unexpected losses."

- **Greek bonds example (2007-2010):** relative value trades buying/selling five-year bonds issued by Greece earned steady <1% return with almost zero volatility; in early 2010 the relationship collapsed — classic negative skew, steady gains tricked into thinking position was low risk

**Liquidity and Size:**
- Smaller companies and less liquid assets offer premia
- Premia vary over time — mortgage-backed derivatives: liquid with premium in 2006; untradeable in 2007

**When Others Have to Trade:**
- **FX carry**: borrow low interest rate currencies (Switzerland), invest in high rate currencies — consistently profitable until policy abandoned (January 2015); sudden losses = decidedly negative skew
- End-of-year trading for taxation/window dressing (seasonal equity effects)
- Insurance companies buying rare long maturity bonds to hedge liabilities
- Liquidity providers capturing spread as market makers — negative skew territory

**Barriers to Entry:**
- High frequency strategies require expensive co-located servers; returns compensate for investment
- Investing in private firms requires lengthy due diligence — returns compensate for effort, not skill

**Behavioral Effects:**
- Early loss taker (trend following) profitable due to prospect theory
- **Self-fulfilling prophecy**: Fibonacci numbers, technical levels may persist if enough traders use them

**Pure Alpha:**
- **Warren Buffett, John Templeton, Peter Lynch**: possibly rare **alpha** or very lucky
- Unlikely their methods can be systematized; pure skill holders adapt in ways rules cannot

### Trading Styles Classification

**Static vs. Dynamic:**
- Static: buy and hold (1st) → rebalanced to equal cash (2nd) → **risk parity** equal risk (3rd) → constant expected risk allocation (4th)
- **Asset allocating investor** runs 4th degree static portfolio
- Dynamic: actively trade, earn additional returns from buying/selling

**Risk Concept (Donald Rumsfeld framework):**
- Known Knowns, Known Unknowns, **unpredictable risks** (Unknown Unknowns)
- Predictable risk = recent historic standard deviation of daily percentage price changes
- Unpredictable risk = incorrect estimates, changing levels, wrong models (especially skew, non-Gaussian)

**Apple/Microsoft example:**
- 3rd degree: 50% cash in each (same initial std dev)
- Apple iPhone recall → price crashes → Apple std dev doubles → two-thirds portfolio risk from Apple
- 4th degree: sell Apple, buy Microsoft to restore equal risk allocation

**Volatility Standardisation:**
- Compares returns of different assets with same expected risk
- Benefits: dynamic portfolios with equal risk contribution; apply same trading rule to different assets; easily combine rules

**Contrarians vs. Market Followers:**
- **Mean reversion** / **relative value** = contrarians: buy low after falls, sell after rises → negative skew
- **Trend following** = market followers: close losing positions (early loss taker) → positive skew

**Skew and Trading Style:**
- Positive skew: frequent small losses, infrequent large gains (buying insurance, trend following, **John Paulson**, Nassim Taleb)
- Negative skew: frequent small gains, infrequent large losses (selling insurance, FX carry, LTCM 1998, Nick Leeson $1B loss selling straddles January 1995)

**Crowded Trades:**
- Deadly when majority of market has same bet — leveraged strategies going wrong create cascading liquidation
- After long stable period, trades get swamped → profits shrink → apparent risk drops → leverage increases → music stops
- **Long Term Capital Management** (1998), **Quant Quake** (August 2007)

### Trading Speed

**Very Slow (months to years):** Rules involve **mean reversion**; profits can't be statistically distinguished from noise — avoid

**Medium (hours/days to months):** Most attractive via **law of active management**; accessible to wider population; manual system on daily data works well

**Fast (microseconds to one day):** Very high raw SR but costs chew up profits; requires co-located servers, automated software; outside this book's scope

### Achievable Sharpe Ratios

- Single equity: SR ~0.15
- 20+ diversified equities same country: SR ~0.20
- Global equity index: SR ~0.25
- Multi-asset portfolio (equities, bonds, commodities): SR ~0.40
- Dynamic trading rules, single instrument: SR ~0.40
- Diversified futures system: SR ~0.50 to 1.0 (back-tested)
- Semi-automatic trader, single instrument: SR ~0.25; diversified: SR ~0.50
- **SR consistently above 1.0 rarely achieved, even by sophisticated institutional investors**

**Dangerous path 1 — Hidden Negative Skew:**
- Imaginary strategy: 100%/65% alternating returns for 20 years → SR 4.6
- Year 21: minus 100%, lose entire investment
- Even after blowup, 21-year SR is still an "excellent" 1.7
- **Long Term Capital Management** also had SR ~4.6 before its 1998 blowup

**Dangerous path 2 — Trading Too Quickly (Law of Active Management):**
- Monthly holding, SR 0.40 → daily holding SR 1.8 → hourly SR 5.2 (theoretical, pre-cost)
- But costs matter enormously: trading every couple of days could theoretically give SR >1.0 on futures
- For expensive spread bets, impossible to trade quickly and profitably
- Only futures are cheap enough for faster trading strategies

## Chapter Three: Fitting

### Joe and the 50 Rules

- At **Aqueduct Capital**, boss proudly introduced Joe: "He's only been here a month and he's already come up with 50 new trading rules profitable in back-tests!"
- Discovery of apparently profitable rules sifted from thousands of possibilities is incredibly dangerous
- Firm was liquidated a few months later

### Ideas First Testing Process

- **Fitting** involves selecting one or more **trading rules** from candidates
- Example: **mean reversion** rule on GBP/USD — buy if 5% below yearly average, sell at 5% above
- Test initial rule; if good enough, proceed to **calibration** (test variations like 3%, 6%, 10% or different lookback periods)
- Choose most profitable variation using **Sharpe ratio (SR)**

### Back-Testing Methods

**In Sample (BAD):** Use all data to fit, then test on same data → extremely optimistic, favors complex over-fitted rules

**Half Out of Sample:** Split data; fit on first half, test on second → wastes half data

**Expanding Window (PREFERRED):** Fit on growing window of past data, test on next unseen period — no cheating, nothing wasted

**Rolling Window:** Same but discard old data beyond fixed window — picks up structural changes but needs long window

### When Fitting Goes Bad

- Selected best **variation** from early loss taker rule on CME gold futures, 90 variations, one-year rolling window

**Three approaches tested:**
1. Picking the *best* variation each year: SR 0.07
2. Picking a *random* variation each year: SR 0.20
3. Keeping *all* variations equally weighted: SR 0.33

**Why fitting failed:**
1. Choosing just one variation = overconfidence
2. One year of data wholly insufficient
3. Testing far too many variations

### The Multiple Testing Problem

**TABLE 3: Bad rules slipping through when testing many rules**

| Rules tested | Min SR 0.5 | Min SR 1.0 | Min SR 2.0 |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | <1 | <1 | <1 |
| 5 | 1.4 | <1 | <1 |
| 10 | 3 | 1.5 | <1 |
| 50 | 16 | 8 | 1.2 |
| 100 | 30 | 16 | 2.3 |

**TABLE 4: Required SR threshold to avoid bad rules (by years of data)**

| Rules tested | 1 year | 5 years | 10 years | 30 years |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | 1.5 | 0.7 | 0.5 | 0.4 |
| 5 | 2.3 | 1.1 | 0.8 | 0.5 |
| 10 | 2.8 | 1.2 | 0.8 | 0.6 |
| 50 | 3.4 | 1.5 | 1.0 | 0.6 |
| 100 | 3.4 | 1.5 | 1.1 | 0.7 |

### How Much History to Determine if a Rule is Good?

- Uses **T-Test** with threshold of **two sigma** (2.5% chance of false positive)

**TABLE 5: Years needed to confirm profitability**

| True SR | 0.2 | 0.3 | 0.4 | 0.5 | 0.7 | 1.0 | 1.5 | 2.0 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Years needed | 45 | 37 | 33 | 20 | 10 | 6 | 3 | 1.4 |

- Average trading rule for one instrument has realistic SR ~0.3 → need nearly **40 years** of history

### How Long to Distinguish One Rule from Another?

- Two rules with SR 0.30 and 0.80, uncorrelated: takes **30 years** to distinguish
- Only highly correlated rules (0.95+) with large SR differences (1.0) can be distinguished in under 3 years

### Should You Fit Separately Per Instrument?

- Carver's hedge fund reorganized by asset class; teams convinced they needed market-specific rules

> "This is a classic example of the narrative fallacy, a cognitive bias we have seen before. To our human minds it makes sense to have different stories, and so different trading rules, for different instruments."

- Pooling data across instruments gives more history, improves ability to distinguish rules
- Diversification amongst instruments is preferable to rule diversification

### Rules for Effective Fitting

1. **Keep it simple** — complex methods make over-fitting harder to detect
2. **Fewer alternatives** — reducing the pool is crucial
3. **Ban time machines** — always use rolling or expanding out of sample fitting
4. **Don't drop rules casually** — need ~30 years to differentiate most rules
5. **Pool data across instruments** — only fit individually if statistically significant difference exists
6. **Compare apples with apples** — negative skew rules have flatteringly high SR
7. **Future won't be like the past** — many asset classes did extraordinarily well over last 40 years due to falling inflation

### How Carver Chooses His Rules

1. Come up with small number of **trading rules** to exploit each market idea
2. Select a few **variations** examining behavior (trading speed, correlation) — not performance
3. Allocate **forecast weights** taking SR uncertainty into account

- In his own portfolio: 8 rules from 5 themes
- If starting from scratch: just **trend following** and **carry** rules
- Average **correlation** between different rules on same instrument is higher than between instruments trading same rule
- Adding new instruments has far more benefit than adding rules

**Variations:** need fast, slow, and 2-3 in between per rule. Drop if >95% correlation, too expensive to trade, or extremely slow. **Never drop purely on performance.**

**Don't look at returns yet:** reserve performance data for forecast weights. Pre-selecting good rules contaminates the back-test.

## Chapter Four: Portfolio Allocation

### Optimising Gone Bad

- **Harry Markowitz** single period optimisation: finding max risk-adjusted return using estimated means and correlations
- Results in extreme, unstable weights (100% NASDAQ in tech boom, then 0% forever after)

> "When I asked one of these rocket scientists what they thought about the extreme portfolio weights they'd found I often got a shrug. 'These are what the optimizer came up with.'"

**Equal weights make sense when:** same volatility (always true for **volatility standardised** assets), same SR, same correlation. Otherwise: larger weights for higher SR and more diversifying (lower correlation) assets.

- With 15 years of data, can't distinguish Sharpe ratios between assets — but **can** distinguish different correlations
- Classic optimiser uses only average SR and correlation, ignoring uncertainty in estimates

### Bootstrapping

- Repeat optimisation many times over different parts of data, average the resulting weights
- Average of many optimisations → less extreme than single optimisation
- Noisy data → weights approach equal; significant differences → reflected in average

**TABLE 7: Three-asset portfolio comparison**

| Asset | Equal | Single period | Bootstrapped |
|---|---|---|---|
| US 20 year bond | 33% | 68% | 53% |
| S&P 500 | 33% | 32% | 27% |
| NASDAQ | 33% | 0% | 20% |

### Handcrafting Method

- Carver's simpler alternative: construct portfolio bottom-up by forming groups of similar assets
- Only needs correlation estimates (don't need to be precise)

> "I remember once being told 'Optimisation is more of an art than a science.' This never seemed particularly satisfying."

**TABLE 8: Group weights for handcrafting**

| Composition | Weights |
|---|---|
| One asset | 100% |
| Two assets | 50% each |
| Same correlations | Equal weights |
| 3 assets (corr 0.0, 0.5, 0.0) | 30%, 40%, 30% |
| 3 assets (corr 0.0, 0.9, 0.0) | 27%, 46%, 27% |
| 3 assets (corr 0.5, 0.0, 0.5) | 37%, 26%, 37% |
| 3 assets (corr 0.9, 0.0, 0.9) | 39%, 22%, 39% |
| 3 assets (corr 0.5, 0.9, 0.5) | 29%, 42%, 29% |
| 3 assets (corr 0.9, 0.5, 0.9) | 42%, 16%, 42% |

Process: group most correlated assets → set within-group weights from table → set across-group weights → multiply through levels.

**Three-asset example (US bonds, S&P 500, NASDAQ):**
- Group 1 (bonds): 100%. Group 2 (equities): 50% each.
- Across groups: 50% each.
- Final: 50% bonds, 25% S&P 500, 25% NASDAQ.

**16-asset portfolio example:**
- 11 equities: 3 UK banks (Barclays, HSBC, RBS), 2 UK retailers (Tesco, Sainsbury), 3 US banks (JP Morgan, Citigroup, Bank of America), 3 US retailers (Safeway, Walmart, Costco)
- 5 bonds: 2 UK govt bonds (5yr, 10yr), 3 US bonds (2yr, 20yr, 30yr)
- Four levels of grouping: equity sector within country → country within asset class → asset class → overall
- US bonds are interesting: 2yr/20yr correlation 0.5, 2yr/30yr 0.5, 20yr/30yr 0.9 → row 10 of table 8 → weights 42% (2yr), 29% (20yr), 29% (30yr)
- Most groups just use equal weights (same industry/country = similar correlations)
- Equal weights would give 6.25% each; handcrafting beats this for unbalanced portfolios

### Are We Cheating?

- Handcrafting can't easily be repeated for rolling out of sample back-test — you fit one single in-sample set of weights
- But handcrafted weights are less extreme than single period optimisation and remain relatively stable

> "Using in sample handcrafting rather than rolling out of sample bootstrapping gave an insignificant advantage (Sharpe ratio of 0.54 rather than 0.52). In comparison in sample single period optimisation produced an unrealistically high SR of 0.84"

- When using single period method for rolling out of sample fit, results were worse (SR of 0.3)

### Incorporating Sharpe Ratios

**TABLE 12: Adjustment factors for handcrafted weights**

| SR difference from average | Column A (certain, 10+ years) | Column B (uncertain, 10+ years) | Column C (<10 years) |
|---|---|---|---|
| -0.50 | 0.32 | 0.65 | 1.0 |
| 0 | 1.00 | 1.00 | 1.0 |
| +0.50 | 1.83 | 1.35 | 1.0 |

- Column A for cost adjustments (known with certainty)
- Column B for historical SR estimates
- Column C: <10 years data → don't adjust at all

**Steps:** handcraft base weights → get SRs → compute difference from group average → get multiplier → multiply → normalise to 100%

**Three-asset SR-adjusted example:**
- Historic SRs: NASDAQ 0, S&P 500 0.5, bonds 0.75
- Level 1 (equities): average SR 0.25; NASDAQ -0.25 from avg → multiply by 0.85; S&P +0.25 → multiply by 1.15
- Result: NASDAQ 42%, S&P 58%
- Level 2: equities avg SR 0.25, bonds 0.75; avg 0.50; equities -0.25 → 0.85; bonds +0.25 → 1.15
- Final: NASDAQ 18%, S&P 24%, bonds 58%

> "When I bring in Sharpe ratio estimates, handcrafting up-weights better performing assets and produces similar results to bootstrapping, but does not result in extreme portfolios like single period optimisation"

| Method | Factor |

| Bootstrapping, in sample | 60% |

## Chapter Five: Framework Overview

### A Bad Example

Typical flawed system: buy when 20 day MA crosses 40 day; 3% capital per trade; trailing stop at 3% loss.

**Problems:**
1. **Position sizing is arbitrary** — 3% makes no sense across different instruments, account sizes, time periods
2. **Stop loss based on capital is incorrect** — should be based on market volatility, not personal pain threshold

> "The solution is to separate out the components of your system: trading rules (including explicit or implicit stop losses), position sizing, and the calculation of your volatility target"

### Why a Modular Framework?

- **Trading rules** = engine; **framework** = chassis/drivetrain translating forecasts into positions
- **Flexibility:** can adapt for any trading rule, discretionary forecasts, or static allocation
- **Transparency:** each component explained — build understanding and confidence
- **Well-defined interfaces:** forecast of +1.5 has consistent meaning regardless of style or instrument
- **Getting the boring bit right:** framework handles position/risk management correctly for any rule

### Framework Components

1. **Instruments** — what you trade
2. **Forecasts** — predictions from trading rules (expected absolute value of 10; capped at ±20)
3. **Combined forecasts** — weighted average of multiple forecasts per instrument
4. **Volatility targeting** — how much overall risk
5. **Scaled positions** — position size from risk, forecast confidence, volatility target
6. **Portfolios** — combining trading subsystems with **instrument weights**
7. **Speed and Size** — trading costs and capital constraints

## Chapter Six: Instruments

### Necessities

- **Data availability:** minimum daily prices; automated strategies need live ticks; fundamental strategies need additional data
- **Minimum sizes:** JGB future >$1M per contract; Berkshire A shares >$100K; reduces granularity to binary positions
- **Understanding price drivers:** don't trade from ivory tower; know why prices move

> "More buyers than sellers is not an answer!" — Carver

- Swiss franc peg removal example: avoided EUR/CHF because market controlled by central bank, not normal historic factors

### Standard Deviation Requirements

- Instruments with extremely low risk (pegged currencies) should be excluded:
  1. Risk returns to normal sharply, creating losses
  2. Need excessive leverage
  3. Limit overall system risk
  4. More costly to trade

### Instrument Choice Factors

- **Diversification:** as large and diversified as possible; avoid highly correlated instruments
- **Costs:** cheaper is better; expensive instruments unsuitable for fast trading
- **Liquidity:** less liquid = more expensive; can reduce quickly during stress (CDS markets 2008)
- **Skew:** positive skew rules on negative skew assets alleviate danger

### Access

- **Exchange vs OTC:** exchange preferred — Swiss franc meltdown showed OTC brokers had severe difficulties while futures market operated normally
- **Cash vs derivative:** derivatives provide straightforward leverage; different tax treatments (UK spread bets = gambling, tax free)
- **Funds:** ETFs and passive index trackers useful when leverage can't be used; active funds have higher fees, hard to prove **alpha**

## Chapter Seven: Forecasts

### What Makes a Good Forecast

**Sergei's Wisdom** (experienced trader on sizing positions):

> "You need to know three things. First, *how much do you like this trade*? That comes from here," pointing to his heart. "Second thing, *how much can you afford to lose*? That is down here, in your gut." "Last thing, *how risky is it*? You calculate that here," tapping his forehead.

**Key properties of forecasts:**
1. **Scaled, not binary:** positive = buy, negative = short; closer to zero = smaller expected move. Binary systems cost more (full reversal trades).
2. **Proportional to risk-adjusted return:** use **volatility standardisation**. If Bund expected 2% return / 8% vol (0.25 ratio) and Schatz 1% / 2% (0.5 ratio), Schatz forecast should be twice Bund's.
3. **Consistent scale:** expected absolute value of 10. So +10 = average buy, -10 = average sell, +5 = weak buy, -20 = very strong sell.
4. **Capped at ±20** for five reasons:
   - **Risk control**: prevent one trading rule variation dominating returns
   - **Limited data**: absolute forecasts >20 appear ~5% of time → not enough evidence they're correct
   - **Extremes often different**: markets trend but after very sharp falls, "dead cat bounces" likely; dividend yields >50% = probably going bankrupt
   - **Higher realized volatility**: very strong forecast may just reflect low std dev period; Japanese bonds January 2013 had strong positive forecasts due to low vol → when PM Abe unveiled reforms, vol spiked and prices dropped fast; capping contained the damage
   - **Limited downside**: only ~5% of forecasts affected, so return reduction from capping is small

### Discretionary Trading (Semi-Automatic Trader)

|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| -20 | -15 | -10 | -5 | 0 | 5 | 10 | 15 | 20 |

- Don't change forecast once bet is open — risk of meddling
- Use systematic trailing stop loss to close positions

## Chapter Eight: Combined Forecasts

### Combining with Forecast Weights

- Use **weighted average** of individual forecasts; weights positive, sum to 100%
- Example: crude oil with +15 (EWMAC) and -10 (Carry), 50/50 weights → combined = 2.5

### Handcrafted Forecast Weights Example

**TABLE 17: Example with EWMAC (3 variants) + Carry**

| Variation | Within-rule weight | Rule weight | Final weight |
|---|---|---|---|
| EWMAC 16,64 | 42% | 50% | 21% |

| Carry | 100% | 50% | 50% |

### Forecast Diversification Multiplier

- Combined forecast will have smaller scale than individual forecasts (same portfolio diversification effect)
- Must multiply by **forecast diversification multiplier** to restore expected absolute value of 10
- Perfectly correlated → multiplier = 1.0
- Correlation 0.5 → multiplier = 1.15
- Correlation 0.0 → multiplier = 1.41

**TABLE 18 highlights:**
- 2 assets, 0.0 correlation: 1.41
- 10 assets, 0.0 correlation: 3.2
- 50+ assets, 0.0 correlation: 7.1
- Perfect correlation (1.0): always 1.0

- Floor negative correlations at zero
- **Maximum diversification multiplier: 2.5** (to avoid binary trading)
- Cap combined forecasts at ±20

### Combined Forecast Process

1. Individual forecasts (expected absolute value ~10, capped ±20)
2. Apply **forecast weights** (positive, sum to 100%)
3. Calculate raw combined forecast (weighted average)
4. Apply **forecast diversification multiplier** (at least 1.0, max 2.5)
5. Cap rescaled combined forecast at ±20

## Chapter Nine: Volatility Targeting

### The Importance of Risk Targeting

- Most important decision when designing a trading system
- Nearly all amateur traders lose money because **positions are too large**
- Painful losses are main reason traders meddle with systems
- Must understand: (1) your system's likely performance and skew, (2) your own risk tolerance

### Setting a Volatility Target

- **Percentage volatility target** × **trading capital** = **cash volatility target**
- Annualise daily by multiplying by 16 (√256)
- Volatility target is the long-term average of expected, predictable risk — not maximum loss

### Four Considerations

1. **How much can you lose?** — never put in more than you can afford to lose
2. **Can you cope with the risk?** — can you face regularly losing 5-20% of capital in a day?
3. **Can you realise that risk?** — limited by leverage availability and instrument volatility
4. **Is this level right for your system?** — match to expected SR and skew

### Can You Cope with the Risk?

- Say you put $100,000 capital and run with 200% volatility target
- Could you cope with losing $20,000 in one day? That would happen every month.

**TABLE 20: Losses at different volatility targets ($100K capital, SR 0.5, zero skew)**

| At 200% vol target | At 50% vol target |
|---|---|
| Worst daily loss/month: $20,000 | $5,000 |
| Worst weekly loss/year: $55,000 | $14,000 |
| Worst monthly loss/10 years: $80,000 | $32,000 |
| 10% chance cumulative loss ≥: $62,000 | — |

**Negative skew** (TABLE 21, 50% vol target, skew -2): individual losses bigger ($6,100/month worst day vs $5,000) but cumulative losses smaller ($7,400 at 10% vs higher).

### Leverage Dangers

- Swiss franc January 2015: natural risk ~1% annual; 50× leverage needed for 50% vol target
- Actual move >16% — only ≤7× leverage survived
- Diversifying across instruments helps
- Exclude low-volatility instruments requiring insane leverage

### Kelly Criterion and Optimal Bet Size

- Naively if expecting profit, should bet maximum — but compounding changes everything
- Example: expected average 100% per trade. Lose 90% first trade, make 190% next → only 29% of cash left despite 100% average
- Optimal bet = quarter of original size
- **Kelly criterion**: set **volatility target** equal to expected **Sharpe ratio (SR)**
- Invented by physicist **John Kelly** in 1956; maximises geometric mean (compound returns)
- SR 0.5 → 50% vol target is optimal across all skew levels
- Table 24: SR 2.0 → 200% vol target → 400% expected return (wildly overconfident)
- **Fortune's Formula** by **William Poundstone** — excellent readable book on Kelly

> "My experience has been that most cautious ... investors who use Kelly find the frequency of substantial bankroll reductions to be uncomfortably large." — **Ed Thorpe**

### Recommended Volatility Targets

**Carver's personal system:** 45 instruments, 8 rules, back-tested SR ~1.0 → advocates maximum **37% volatility target** (not Kelly's 100%)

**Rationale for Half-Kelly:**
1. Realized SR never matches back-test (over-fitting, chapters 3-4)
2. Asset returns lower than historical (chapter 2)
3. Apply 0.75 ratio to back-tested SR (out of sample bootstrap rule of thumb)
4. Absolute maximum SR for staunch systems traders: 1.0
5. Full Kelly too aggressive — Table 23: even with correct 50% Kelly target, 10% chance of losing half capital after 10 years
6. **Half-Kelly**: 1.0 × 0.75 × 0.5 = **37%**

**TABLE 25: Staunch Systems Traders (zero/positive skew)**

| Realistic SR | Vol target |
|---|---|
| 0.25 | 12% |
| 0.50 | 25% |
| 0.75 | 37% |
| 1.0+ | 50% |

**Negative skew:** halve targets (SR 0.50 → 12%, SR 1.0 → 25%)

**Asset Allocating Investors:** SR 0.20 → 10%, SR 0.40 → 20% (often can't reach even these without leverage)

**Semi-Automatic Traders:** SR 0.20 maximum safe achievable → **no more than 25% volatility target**

### Adjusting for Skew

- Negative skew: penalty for risk much greater; figure 21 shows flattened performance curve
- Many systems with negative skew have fantastic back-test SR — run at half usual risk
- Positive skew or zero skew: standard targets apply

### When to Change Target

- Don't change percentage volatility target — risk of meddling
- Reducing when distrusting system, increasing when confident → poor timing
- Exception: grossly miscalculated risk tolerance after first big loss → one-off change, only downwards
- Better: start with lower trading capital, gradually increase; keeps percentage target fixed while cash volatility grows

### Rolling Up Profits and Losses

- Percentage target stays fixed; trading capital changes → cash target adjusts
- Lose $2K on $100K → new target = 30% × $98K = $29,400 (don't maintain old higher level)
- Kelly says roll up profits too: make $3K → 30% × $103K = $30,900
- Compounding returns increases wealth faster
- **Checking frequency:** automated high-leverage system: hourly; manual trading: daily minimum; using leverage: at least weekly

### What Percentage of Capital Per Trade?

Traditional money management allocates percentage per trade. Can infer **percentage volatility target** from holding period and positions held.

**TABLE 27: Implied volatility from holding period and bet size (assumes avg 2 positions)**
- 1 day holding, 10% per trade → 160% vol (!)
- 1 week, 2.5% per trade → 40%
- 6 weeks, 10% per trade → 41%
- 3 months, 2.5% per trade → 7%

Carver's introductory "bad example" system: ~1 week holding, 10% capital → 80% vol target. If Kelly optimal, SR must be ≥1.6, implying 256% annual returns — serious overconfidence.

## Chapter Ten: Position Sizing

### Instrument Block and Block Value

- **Instrument block**: basic trading unit (1 share, 1 futures contract, minimum spread bet)
- **Block value**: monetary gain/loss from 1% price change per instrument block

**Examples:**
- Apple at $400: 1 share block, block value = $4 (1% of $400)
- 100 Apple shares: block value = $400
- UK FTSE spread bet at £10/point, FTSE at 6500: 1% = 65 points = £650 block value
- WTI crude oil future (1000 barrels): at $75, block value = $750
- Eurodollar future ($1M, 3-month rate): 1% price rise → block value = $2,450
## Chapter Ten: Position Sizing (continued - Worked Example)

### Price Volatility

- 1% fall in quoted price causes pain equal to **block value** per **instrument block** held
- Need to calculate expected **standard deviation** of daily instrument percentage returns = **price volatility**
- Example: average equity ~1% daily volatility, German 2-year Schatz bond ~0.02% daily volatility

### Measuring Recent Standard Deviation

Three methods to calculate recent standard deviation:

1. **Eyeball the chart** — Fine for **semi-automatic traders** who stare at charts
2. **Precise calculation over moving window** — Short window (10 days): noisy but reactive. Long window (100 days): smoother but slower
3. **Exponentially weighted moving average (EWMA)** — Default look-back: 36 days (equivalent to 25-day simple MA). Appendix D has calculation details

**Research findings:**
- Almost no performance difference for look-back periods from 6 months to 1 year
- Default 25 business days (5 weeks), corresponds to industry standard
- **RiskMetrics** system uses EWMA with 25-day simple MA equivalent half-life

**Caution:** Would be disaster during Credit Default Swaps crisis (early 2007), zero interest rate Eurodollar futures, or Swiss franc FX (January 2015). Calm periods produce dangerously large positions from low volatility.

### Instrument Currency Volatility

- Instrument currency volatility = block value x price volatility
- Example: oil futures with block value $750, price volatility 1.333% = $997.50 per day

### Instrument Value Volatility

- Needed when trading in different currency from trading capital
- Instrument value volatility = instrument currency volatility x exchange rate
- Example: UK investor, crude oil: $997.50 x 0.67 USD/GBP = £668,325

### Volatility Target and Position Risk

- **Daily cash volatility target** = annualized target / 16
- Example: £1,000,000 annual target -> £62,500 daily
- **Volatility scalar** = daily cash volatility target / instrument value volatility
- Example: £62,500 / £668,325 = 93.52 contracts

### From Forecast to Position

**Subsystem position** = volatility scalar x forecast / 10

- Forecast +10 = 93.52 contracts (full position)
- Forecast -6 = (93.52 x -6) / 10 = **-56.11 contracts** (short)
- Forecast -5 = 46.76 contracts
- Forecast -20 = sell 187.04 blocks
- **Asset allocating investors** with constant forecast +10: position always equals volatility scalar

### Worked Example

**Starting conditions:** £1M annualized volatility target, WTI Crude oil futures, Forecast: -6

1. Daily cash volatility target: £1,000,000 / 16 = £62,500
2. Price volatility: 1.33% of $75 price
3. Block value: 1000 barrels @ $75 -> 1% move = $750
4. Instrument currency volatility: $750 x 1.33 = $997.50
5. Exchange rate: USD/GBP ~0.67
6. Instrument value volatility: $997.50 x 0.67 = £668,325
7. Volatility scalar: £62,500 / £668,325 = 93.52 contracts
8. Subsystem position: (-6 x 93.52) / 10 = **short 56.11 contracts**

## Chapter Eleven: Portfolios

### The Diversification Advantage

> "Diversification is the only free lunch in investment."

- Allocating across asset classes can double expected Sharpe ratio
- Individual investors typically hold too few assets (often <5 shares)
- Within portfolio, use **instrument weights** to allocate capital

### Instrument Weights

- **Asset allocating investors** and **staunch systems traders** allocate trading capital between subsystems relating to fixed set of instruments
- Both approaches allocate to subsystems, not directly to instrument positions
- Use **handcrafting** or **bootstrapping** to determine weights
- Need correlations: estimate from back-test or use tables 50-55 (appendix C)

**Critical distinction:** Correlations needed are between **trading subsystem returns**, not underlying instrument returns.

> "A good approximation is that the correlation between subsystem returns will be 0.70 of the correlation of instrument returns."

- Static asset allocating investors: use instrument return correlations without 0.7 adjustment
- Don't adjust instrument weights for different Sharpe ratios between subsystems

### Example: Handcrafting Instrument Weights (S&P 500, NASDAQ, 20-year bond)

- Group one (bonds): 100% in 20-year bond
- Group two (equities): 50% each S&P/NASDAQ
- Top level: 50% bonds, 50% equities
- **Final weights:** Bond 50%, S&P 25%, NASDAQ 25%

### Semi-Automatic Trader Weights

- No fixed portfolios; equal allocation between concurrent bets
- Instrument weight = 100% / maximum number of bets
- Maximum shouldn't exceed 2.5x average number of bets expected

### Instrument Diversification Multiplier

- Diversified portfolios of volatility-standardized assets have lower expected standard deviation than individual assets
- Apply **instrument diversification multiplier** to ensure overall portfolio has right risk level

**For asset allocators/staunch systems traders:** Calculate from correlation matrix of trading subsystem returns

**For semi-automatic traders:** = maximum number of bets / average number of bets

> "It's possible to get very high values for the diversification multiplier... However in a crisis such as the 2008 crash, correlations tend to jump higher exposing us to serious losses."

- **Limit multiplier to absolute maximum of 2.5**

### Position Inertia

- Avoid trading until target position >10% away from current position
- Example: Italian government bond futures, desired unrounded position 133.48 (rounds to 133)

- Significantly reduces trading costs with negligible effect on pre-cost performance

## Chapter Twelve: Speed and Size

### Speed of Trading

How fast depends on: (1) expected price behavior, (2) cost of each trade.

> "Is it wise to trade this rule? Personally I think it is very unwise indeed."

- Pre-cost SR 0.5 with 20% volatility target: 30% expected returns, minus 20% annual costs = just 10% net
- **Overtrading** = result of overconfidence

### Calculating Cost of Trading

**Execution cost:** Half usual bid-offer spread for smaller traders. Example: Euro Stoxx spread 1 point, execution cost = 0.5 points.

**Types of cost:**
- Expected execution cost per block (half spread)
- Fee per ticket (£5-£15 typical UK retail)
- Fee based on trade size (e.g., €3/contract)
- Percentage value fee (UK stamp duty 0.5%, 0.1% typical commission)
- Holding costs (futures rolling, fund annual charges) — relatively small, ignored for speed decisions

### Standardized Cost Measurement

**Standardized cost** = (2 x C) / (16 x ICV)
- C = cost to trade one block
- ICV = daily instrument currency volatility
- Measures how much of your annualized raw Sharpe ratio you lose per round trip
- Effective cost of trading is higher for instruments with lower price volatility

**Example: Euro Stoxx futures**
- Price volatility: ~1.5%/day, each 1% move = €337
- Daily std dev: 1.5% x €337 = €506 (ICV)
- Annualized: €506 x 16 = €8,096
- Cost of trading: €8/block (€5 execution + €3 brokerage)
- Standardized cost: (2 x €8) / €8,096 = **0.002 SR units**

**Example: FTSE 100 spread bet (£1/point)**
- Price volatility 0.75%/day, FTSE at 6600 -> block value £66
- ICV: 0.75% x £66 = £49.50
- Half typical spread of 4 points = £4 cost (no fees/taxes)
- Standardized cost: (2 x £4) / (16 x £49.50) = **0.01 SR**
- ~10x more expensive than FTSE 100 future (unsurprising for retail product)

**Benchmarks:**
- Most liquid futures (FTSE 100): ~0.001 SR
- Australian interest rate futures: ~0.03 SR
- Spread bets on major indices: ~0.01 SR

> "Spread bets generally have higher trading costs than futures, and are OTC traded rather than on exchange. On the upside... they offer tax free returns under current UK legislation."

### Introducing Turnover

- **Turnover**: number of round trips (buy then sell) per year
- Turnover of 1 = one buy and one sell of average-sized position per year
- Cost in SR terms = standardized cost x turnover

**Sources of turnover (descending importance):**
1. Forecast changes (main source)
2. Instrument currency volatility changes
3. Trading capital changes
4. Exchange rate movements
5. System parameter changes (minimal)

### Setting a Speed Limit

> "I wouldn't trade a strategy which paid two-thirds of its profits out as costs... I personally think it's foolhardy to pay more than a third of your expected profits in trading costs."

- **Staunch systems traders**: max 0.13 SR/year on costs (1/3 of 0.40 SR)
- **Semi-automatic traders / asset allocators**: max 0.08 SR/year
- Speed limit = max SR cost / standardized cost per instrument

**Day trading requirements:**
- 500+ round trips annually
- Standardized cost must be ~0.00025 SR
- Need to get execution costs to zero (consistently hit mid-price or better)

> "This capability is relatively rare in practice, which is why the vast majority of amateur day traders are unprofitable."

### Forecast Weights and Trading Costs

- Unless faster rules give pre-cost SR advantage compensating for higher costs, weight toward slower rules
- Author never found consistent evidence that faster trading rules have higher pre-cost SR
- If handcrafting: assume 100% SR for all trading rules, but account for different trading costs
- With speed limit applied, adjustments never larger than 0.95 or 1.05

### Slower Estimation of Price Volatility

- After forecasts, next turnover source is adjusting positions as price volatility changes
- For zero-turnover 'no-rule' with default 5-week look-back: turnover of 1.6 -> 1.6 x 0.08 = 0.13 SR (violates 0.08 limit)
- With 20-week look-back: turnover of 0.40 -> 0.40 x 0.08 = 0.032 SR (well within limit)
- Semi-automatic traders: don't adjust instrument currency volatility estimate unless changed >25%

### Instrument Weight Considerations

- Academic research: extra costs of less liquid instruments often outweighed by higher profits
- Author's research: more expensive instruments perform somewhat better after costs if traded slowly (<15 round trips/year)
- Unless bootstrapping tells you differently, assume same post-cost SR for all trading subsystems

### Trading with Large Capital

- If typical order size > usual depth at inside spread, can't assume half-spread execution
- Three options: (1) single market order eating through order book, (2) passive limit order risking price drift, (3) algorithmic execution in chunks
- None guarantees 0.5 points cost; need serious research on execution costs

### Trading with Less Capital

- Problem: minimum block sizes prevent fractional positions
- Example: €20,000 volatility target -> S&P position always 0 or 1 contract regardless of forecast
- "Your risk is much 'lumpier' than if you could trade fractional contracts"
- Extreme examples: Berkshire Hathaway A at $220,000/share, Japanese 10-year bond at $1.3M
- Even without large minimum: impractical to buy ETFs in blocks <100 with per-ticket cost of €4

**Maximum position** = 2 x volatility scalar x instrument weight x instrument diversification multiplier
- (Asset allocators with constant +10 forecast: use 1x instead of 2x)

- Recommend maximum possible position >= 4 instrument blocks
- Options if too low:
  - Increase weight to instrument (only in moderation)
  - Reduce overall portfolio size by cutting number of instruments
  - Remove instrument entirely if unreasonably large
  - Live with it (author has ~6 futures in 40+ portfolio with max positions <4)
- Build most diversified portfolio possible without maximum position problems
- At least one instrument from each major asset class

## Chapter Fourteen: Asset Allocating Investor

### Profile

- Don't forecast asset prices; seek returns through diversified portfolios using 'no-rule' trading (constant +10 forecast)
- Access assets via ETFs
- Example: €10,000,000 notional trading capital

### Volatility Target Calculation

- Constrained by lack of leverage rather than loss tolerance
- **Desired leverage factor**: 90% for investors who don't use leverage
- Initial guess = lowest annualized price volatility of any instrument (6.88% for inflation linked bond)
- Calculate positions, sum total value, find realized leverage
- Adjust: achievable target = initial guess x (desired leverage / realized leverage)
- Result: 6.88% x (90% / 82.6%) = **7.5%** percentage volatility target
- Annualized cash volatility target: €750,000
- Expected returns: 0.4 x 7.5% = 3.0% + risk free rate

### Cost Expectations

- Turnover: 0.4 round trips/year (using 20-week volatility look-back)
- Standardized cost: 0.08 SR (most expensive instrument)
- Annual cost: 0.4 x 0.08 = 0.032 SR = 0.24% annual performance drag
- ETF holding costs: 0.05% (S&P tracker) to 0.55% (EM equities)

### Portfolio Construction via Handcrafting

- 10 instruments for tractability
- Constraints: max 40% bonds/equities, max 30% EM equities, max 25% inflation linked bonds
- First level: within region (equal weight per country)
- Second level: within asset class (bonds 25% EM, 25% inflation linked, 50% developed; equities 30% EM, 70% developed)
- Top level: 40% bonds, 60% equities
- **Instrument diversification multiplier**: 1.61

### Using Performance Predictions

- Translate predictions into annualized Sharpe ratios using current price volatility estimate
- Example: "Euro Stoxx will hit 3,000 in 12 months" = 8% rise; with 16% annualized volatility = 8%/16% = SR 0.50
- Adjust handcrafted instrument weights using SR adjustment table (column B 'Without certainty')
- Example: average SR 0.20 across portfolio, expected Euro Stoxx SR 0.50 -> 0.3 outperformance -> increase weight by 17%
- Renormalize weights to 100% after adjustment
- Don't need to change instrument diversification multiplier when adjusting weights
- Adjustments should be small and infrequent (each adjustment is additional trading = additional cost)

### Weekly Rebalancing Process

1. Get account value, calculate current trading capital
2. Cash volatility target = percentage volatility target x current capital; divide by 16 for daily
3. Get latest prices for all instruments
4. Update FX rates
5. Calculate instrument value volatility (20-week MA look-back)
6. Subsystem position = volatility scalar (constant forecast +10)
7. Optional: get performance predictions, translate to SR, adjust instrument weights
8. Portfolio instrument position = subsystem position x weight x diversification multiplier (1.61)
9. Round to nearest instrument block (100 shares)
10. Issue trades if current position >10% from target (position inertia)

## Epilogue: What Makes a Good Systematic Trader?

**Humble:** Underestimate intelligence, skill and luck. Don't try anything too clever.

**Simple trading rules:** Not over-fitted or even fitted at all. Handcrafting is both simple and effective.

**Sceptical:** Don't trust anybody: not broker, trainer, author (including Carver himself). Don't trust back-tests, especially if over-fitted on rolling out of sample basis. Future unlikely to be as good as past. Maximum expected SRs: highly diversified staunch systems unlikely to beat 1.0, semi-automatic max 0.5, asset allocators max 0.4. If making steady gains with few losses: good chance of negative skew trading style that will eventually blow up.

**Thoughtful:** Know why you might be making money and why you might not. Understand markets and trading rules.

**Thrifty:** Know trading costs precisely. Stick to speed limits: never set turnover so you spend more than 1/3 of pessimistic expected SR on trading costs per trading subsystem. Don't make broker or market makers wealthy.

**Nervous:** Only commit trading capital you can afford to lose. Use Half-Kelly: maximum percentage volatility target should be half what you pessimistically expect SR to be. At some point some instruments or trading rules will go badly wrong; limit exposure by trading most diversified set possible. Stay away from low volatility instruments (expensive to trade and dangerous).

**Diligent:** Best systematic traders are diligent when creating systems but lazy when running them. Hard work creating safe system you're comfortable with, then don't change it. Don't be tempted to meddle, improve, or risk manage. Time-consuming activities usually destroy performance.

**Lucky:** Even doing everything right, could still be unprofitable if chance turns against you. Can't entirely eliminate risk from investing. Should quantify it and make sure you can cope with likely downside.

> "So it only remains for me to say: Good Luck!"

## Glossary (Selected Key Terms)

- **Alpha**: Returns from manager's skill beyond market beta
- **Alternative beta**: Beta requiring active trading (e.g., equity value premium); doesn't require skill
- **Bootstrapping**: Portfolio optimization accounting for uncertainty in historical data
- **Carry**: Trading strategy profiting from yield/funding cost differential if prices unchanged
- **Combined forecast**: Forecast after combining multiple trading rules and multiplying by forecast diversification multiplier
- **Data mining**: Fitting to historical data; running thousands of back-tests guarantees finding apparently profitable rules that fail in future
- **Diversification multiplier**: Multiplies portfolio returns to restore standard deviation to same level as underlying assets; always >= 1
- **Dynamic**: Strategy with active buying/selling to express opinions on risk-adjusted returns (vs static)
- **EWMA**: Exponentially Weighted Moving Average; 36-day look-back equivalent to 25-day simple MA

## Appendix B: Trading Rules

### The A and B System

- Two simple rules used as examples: **early profit taker** (A=5, B=20) and **early loss taker** (A=20, B=5)
- Author does NOT recommend for actual trading
- Standard position size: $100,000 / instrument value volatility
- Profit target: reverse if price moves >A deviations from entry
- Trailing stop loss: reverse if price moves >B deviations from extreme since entry
- If A > B: early loss taker, exploits large trends, frequent stop-outs
- If B > A: early profit taker, frequent profits, suffers on large adverse moves

> "Notice that this is a complete trading system, not just a trading rule, since it tells you exactly what size positions to take."

### The EWMAC Rule

- Forecast = difference between fast and slow EWMA, standardized by price standard deviation, scaled by forecast scalar
- Look-back pairs (fast:slow ratio fixed at 4:1): 2/8, 4/16, 8/32, 16/64, 32/128, 64/256

**Decay parameter:** A = 2 / (L+1)

**EWMA formula (recursive):** Today's EWMA = (A x P_today) + (E_yesterday x (1-A))

**Raw crossover** = E_fast - E_slow (positive = uptrend, negative = downtrend)

- Divide by standard deviation of daily price changes (in price points)
- Multiply by forecast scalar
- Cap at +/-20

**Adjacent look-backs have correlation of 0.90** (Table 57); intervening values exceed 0.95 cutoff

**Forecast scalars (Table 49):**
- EWMAC 2,8: 10.6
- EWMAC 4,16: 7.5
- EWMAC 8,32: 5.3
- EWMAC 16,64: 3.75
- EWMAC 32,128: 2.65
- EWMAC 64,256: 1.87

### The Carry Rule

**Net expected return** varies by asset class:

**Equities (cash or margin):**
- Dividend yield (expected annual, or historic dividend / current price) minus funding cost (interest on cash, or margin borrowing cost)

**Equities (CFD):**
- Dividend yield minus average of long funding cost and short funding received

**FX (cash):**
- Foreign currency interest minus domestic borrowing cost

**Spread bets (FX or equities):**
- (Spot level - spread bet level) / time to maturity, annualized
- Assumes financing priced into spread bet vs spot relationship

**Futures (not trading nearest contract - preferred):**
- (Current contract price - nearer contract price) / distance between contracts, annualized
- Example: trading June 2017 Eurodollar, nearer = March 2017; distance = 0.25 years

> "If you are already trading the nearest contract you obviously can't use this method."

**Futures (nearest contract - approximation):**
- (Next contract price - current contract price) / distance between contracts, annualized

**Forecast calculation:**
- Raw carry = net expected return in price units / annualized standard deviation of returns
- Annualized std dev = daily std dev (in price points) x 16
- Raw carry is effectively annualized Sharpe ratio (expected return / std dev)
- **Forecast** = 30 x raw carry (capped at +/-20)
- Forecast scalar of 30 from appendix D technique across large markets
- Turnover rule of thumb: 10 round trips/year (with weekly forecast updates)

## Appendix C: Portfolio Optimisation

### Bootstrapping Details

1. Choose random subset of past returns
2. Calculate correlation and average returns from subset
3. Run normal optimization
4. Record instrument weights
5. Repeat multiple times
6. Average all weights for each asset

**Block bootstrapping:** Use consecutive returns (not random individual days) to preserve time-series dependence. Only randomness is choice of starting date. Block length needs to be long enough to capture time dependence.

**Sample length:** Rule of thumb = 10% of history available (e.g., 30 years -> 3-year blocks). Academic research and author's analysis: small benefit (0.05 SR improvement) from using multiple years vs only a month.

**Iterations:** 100 sufficient; almost no value from 200 vs 100 unless block size very small relative to history.

**Does it work?** Bootstrapping definitely superior to single period optimization and equal weights. Most consistent method with less variability across different data sets.

> "Personally I also find bootstrapping to be as good or better than other, relatively complex, methods."

## Appendix D: Framework Details

### Rescaling Forecasts

- Run back-test measuring average absolute forecast values across instruments
- Forecast scalar = 10 / average absolute value
- Example: average absolute value 0.3 -> scalar = 33.33
- If average absolute forecast varies significantly across instruments, trading rule is badly specified

### Diversification Multiplier Formula

- Given N items with correlation matrix H and weights W summing to 1:
- **Diversification multiplier** = 1 / sqrt(W x H x W')
- Floor negative correlations at zero before calculation
- Spreadsheet: `1/SQRT(MMULT(TRANSPOSE(F1:F3), MMULT(A1:C3, F1:F3)))`

### Price Volatility from Historic Data

**Moving average method:**
- Calculate daily percentage returns: (today - yesterday) / yesterday
- Standard deviation of last 25 returns = price volatility

**EWMA method:**
- Smoothing parameter A = 2 / (1 + L), where L = look-back days
- Default L = 36 days -> A = 0.054 (equivalent to 25-day simple MA)
- Calculate squared returns
- Recursive variance: V_today = (A x return^2) + ((1-A) x V_yesterday)
- Volatility = sqrt(variance)
