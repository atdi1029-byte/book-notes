The Big Idea

Robert Carver is a smart guy who used to manage billions of dollars at one of the world's biggest hedge funds. He knows finance inside and out. And yet, when it came time to actually click the "buy" button, his emotions regularly sabotaged him.

His worst example: in January 2009, he spotted that Barclays bank shares had crashed to their lowest price ever. His research said this was the perfect buying opportunity. But panic took over. Instead of investing 10% of his portfolio as planned, he only put in 1%. The stock quintupled. He made money, but left a fortune on the table because fear overrode his analysis at the last second.

Meanwhile, months earlier during the Lehman Brothers collapse, the hedge fund's computer system — which followed pre-set rules with zero emotion — made over a billion dollars in a single day while the humans around it were debating whether to shut everything down.

The lesson: knowing the right answer is not enough. You need a system that prevents you from second-guessing yourself. That is what this entire book is about — building a framework that makes financial decisions for you, so your emotions never get a vote.

Without a system, emotions sit between your analysis and your actions.

Chapter 1: Why Our Brains Fail Us

Economists used to assume people made perfectly rational decisions. Psychologists demolished that idea. Our brains evolved for a world of predators and food scarcity, not stock markets. We carry mental shortcuts that helped our ancestors survive but make us terrible investors.

The biggest problem is called "get-evenitis" — our deep reluctance to accept losses. When a stock drops, we hold on hoping it will recover. When it rises, we sell too quickly to lock in the win. Both instincts are backwards. Carver tested this on 31 different futures markets: cutting losses early and letting winners run beat the opposite approach in 27 of them. Trading against your gut is almost always the right move.

Trading also triggers the same brain circuits as gambling addiction. Day trading is especially dangerous because it combines all the addictive elements: the illusion of control, near misses, rapid feedback, and small frequent rewards. Only a tiny fraction of day traders cover their costs.

Knowing all of this intellectually does not fix the problem. You need what Carver calls a "commitment mechanism" — something that physically prevents you from interfering. Think of Odysseus tying himself to the mast so he could hear the Sirens without steering toward them. Or the trader Victor Niederhoffer, who locked himself in a racquetball court and gave his assistant strict orders to execute the plan no matter how much he begged to override it.

Three specific mistakes destroy trading systems: fitting rules too tightly to past data (so they only work in hindsight), trading too often (racking up costs), and betting too big (so one bad day wipes you out). The entire framework in this book is designed to prevent all three.

Chapter 2: What Trading Rules Actually Do

A trading rule is just a recipe that tells you when to buy, sell, or do nothing. Good rules start with an idea about why markets behave a certain way, then test that idea against historical data. Bad rules start by mining data for patterns and hoping they are not random noise.

Why does trend following work? Because of the emotional biases from Chapter 1. People hold losers too long and sell winners too fast, which means price movements tend to continue longer than they "should." As long as humans have these biases, momentum strategies should keep earning money.

Where do trading profits come from? Several places. Stocks earn more than bonds because they are riskier — you get paid for accepting that risk. Longer-term bonds pay more than short-term bonds for the same reason. Some currencies pay higher interest rates, rewarding you for holding them. People overpay for lottery- ticket investments (big potential upside), which means the opposite bet is profitable on average.

How do you measure whether a rule is working? The Sharpe ratio divides your return by your risk. A single stock might have a Sharpe ratio of 0.15. A diversified portfolio of stocks and bonds might reach 0.40. Carver's own system, trading over 40 markets with eight rules, backtests at 1.0 — and he considers that an upper bound for anyone. If your backtest shows a Sharpe ratio of 2.0 or higher, you have almost certainly fooled yourself.

Sharpe ratios above 1.0 should be treated with extreme suspicion.

Chapter 3: The Danger of Curve Fitting

Shortly after leaving his hedge fund, Carver visited a trading firm called Aqueduct Capital. The boss proudly introduced a young analyst who had discovered 50 profitable trading rules in his first month using backtesting software. The firm was liquidated a few months later.

The problem is simple: if you test enough random ideas, some will look profitable purely by luck. Test 100 worthless rules and you will still find about 16 that appear to have a Sharpe ratio above 0.5. With only one year of data, a rule needs a Sharpe ratio above 3.4 before you can trust it — and almost nothing legitimately achieves that.

How long does it take to know if a rule truly works? A rule with a Sharpe ratio of 0.5 needs about 20 years of data to confirm. At 0.3 — typical for a single market — you need nearly 40 years. Even telling two decent rules apart takes about 30 years. The practical lesson is devastating: you almost never have enough data to know which rule is best. So do not try too hard. Pick a few sensible rules based on logic, not performance, and live with the uncertainty.

Carver's approach barely fits at all. Come up with a small number of ideas. For each idea, select a few speed variations based on how they behave (not how profitable they look). If starting from scratch, just use two rules: trend following and carry. Adding new markets to trade matters far more than inventing new rules.

Chapter 4: Building a Portfolio Without a PhD

The standard textbook approach to building a portfolio is called Markowitz optimization. You feed in expected returns and correlations, and the math spits out the "perfect" allocation. The problem? It produces absurd results. Carver shows an example where the optimizer puts 68% in bonds and 0% in NASDAQ — extreme weights driven by tiny, unreliable differences in estimated returns.

One fix is called bootstrapping: run the optimization hundreds of times on different random subsets of your data and average the results. The extremes cancel out and you get much more sensible weights. But even this is overkill for most people.

Carver's preferred approach is "handcrafting": grab a pencil and the back of an envelope. Group similar assets together. Two assets in a group? Split 50/50. Three assets? Give more weight to whichever is most different from the others. Carver built a simple lookup table that gives you the weights based on how correlated the assets are. The results are nearly as good as the sophisticated math but take seconds instead of hours and never produce crazy allocations.

Always discount your backtested results. In-sample optimization: expect only 25% of the returns to survive in real trading. Handcrafted weights without performance adjustments: 70%. This "pessimism factor" is critical. If you skip it, you will take on more risk than your system can support.

Chapter 5: How the Framework Fits Together

Think of a trading system like a car. Most people obsess over the engine — the trading rules that tell you when to buy and sell. But the engine is useless without a chassis, transmission, and brakes. The framework is everything else: how you size positions, manage risk, and build portfolios.

The modular framework: rules feed forecasts, which combine with risk targets to produce actual trade sizes.

A typical bad system says "buy when the 20-day average crosses above the 40-day average" and "risk 3% of capital per trade." Why 3%? Does that make sense when markets are calm? During a crisis? For a retiree versus a 25-year-old? The framework separates these decisions so each one is handled correctly and independently.

Carver designed three versions of the same framework for three types of people: the hands-off investor who just wants a diversified portfolio, the part-time trader who has opinions but wants discipline, and the full-automation trader who lets computers do everything. They all use the same chassis with different engines.

Chapter 6: Picking What to Trade

Not everything is suitable for trading. You need reliable price data, manageable minimum sizes, and a genuine understanding of what moves the price. The Japanese government bond future costs over a million dollars per contract — it simply does not exist for individual traders.

Stay away from instruments with abnormally low volatility. When a currency is pegged (artificially held at a fixed price), it looks calm for years, inviting huge leveraged bets. Then the peg breaks and the explosion is catastrophic. In January 2015, the Swiss franc surged 16% in a single session when the central bank removed its peg. Some brokers went bankrupt.

Always prefer exchange-traded products over private (OTC) ones. During the Swiss franc crisis, OTC brokers stopped accepting orders and re-marked fills retroactively. Futures exchanges operated normally. The ideal instrument has low correlation with everything else in your portfolio, low trading costs, and deep liquidity.

Chapter 7: Forecasts — How Much Do You Like This Trade?

A forecast is your system's opinion about a trade, expressed on a consistent scale. The number 10 means "average bullish." The number 20 means "maximum conviction buy." Negative numbers mean sell. Zero means no opinion. The key insight is that all forecasts use the same scale regardless of which market or which rule produced them.

Why cap forecasts at 20? Because extreme readings are rare (only about 5% of the time), so there is very little evidence they are reliable. Very strong forecasts often coincide with artificially low volatility that is about to spike. And capping prevents any single bet from dominating your entire portfolio risk. The cost of capping is tiny — the protection is enormous.

For part-time traders who make their own calls: translate your opinion into this same scale. "Weak buy" = 5. "Sell" = -10. "Very strong buy" = 20. Once you open a position, never change your forecast. Let a mechanical stop loss handle the exit instead. This prevents the emotional meddling the whole system is designed to avoid.

For hands-off investors: use a constant forecast of 10 for everything. You are simply saying "I believe markets will continue to earn their long-term returns." The framework handles everything else.

Chapter 8: Blending Multiple Opinions

When you run multiple trading rules on the same market, they will sometimes disagree. Maybe your trend-following rule says "buy crude oil" while your carry rule says "sell." You blend them using a weighted average.

The weights come from handcrafting, just like portfolio weights. Group similar rules together. In Carver's system, carry gets 50% and three speeds of trend following share the other 50%. The middle-speed trend variation only gets 8% because it is highly correlated with its neighbors — it adds almost no new information.

Blending reduces the strength of the combined signal. Two rules averaging 10 each might produce a combined forecast of only 7 because they partly cancel out. You multiply by a "diversification multiplier" to restore the signal to its proper scale. This multiplier is capped at 2.5 — any higher and you would effectively have a binary system that is always screaming "maximum buy" or "maximum sell," which defeats the purpose of scaled forecasts.

Chapter 9: How Much Risk Can You Handle?

This is the single most important decision in the entire book. Nearly all amateur traders lose money because their positions are too large. The "volatility target" is how much you expect your portfolio to swing in a year, expressed as a percentage of your total capital.

Imagine you have $100,000 and set a 200% volatility target. Your worst day each month would cost about $20,000. Could you sleep through that? Most people cannot. At 50% volatility, the worst monthly day drops to $5,000 — still painful, but survivable.

The optimal bet size comes from something called the Kelly criterion, invented in 1956. It says your volatility target should equal your expected Sharpe ratio. But full Kelly is terrifyingly aggressive — even with perfect estimates, there is a 10% chance of losing half your money over ten years. Carver uses Half-Kelly: take your pessimistic Sharpe ratio estimate and cut it in half. His own system (backtested Sharpe of 1.0, adjusted down to 0.75 for pessimism, then halved) runs at 37% volatility.

Carver's own risk calculation: start with the backtest, apply pessimism, then halve for safety.

The golden rule: never increase your volatility target percentage. The temptation to bet bigger during winning streaks is pure ego. If you realize you set the target too high after your first painful loss, you may reduce it once — but only downward, never back up.

Chapter 10: Turning Opinions Into Trade Sizes

Position sizing connects your forecast to an actual trade. The math is straightforward once you break it into steps. First, figure out how much money one unit of an instrument moves per day (its "volatility"). Then figure out how much your total portfolio should move per day (your risk target). Divide the second by the first to get how many units to hold.

Adjust that number for your forecast strength. If your forecast is 10 (average conviction), hold the full amount. If your forecast is 5 (weak), hold half. If your forecast is -20 (maximum sell), go short at double the base size. The formula is: position = base size × forecast ÷ 10.

Do not round to whole numbers yet — that happens at the portfolio level. And measure volatility using recent data (roughly a 25-day lookback), not long-term averages. This ensures your positions automatically shrink when markets get wild and grow when markets calm down. It is a built-in safety mechanism.

The danger is calm markets. When measured volatility drops abnormally low, position sizes balloon. The Swiss franc before its peg broke and credit default swaps before the 2008 crisis both invited enormous positions that blew up spectacularly. Capping forecasts at 20 helps, but the real protection is avoiding instruments that require extreme leverage.

Chapter 11: Putting It All Together in a Portfolio

Diversification is the only genuine free lunch in investing. Spreading money across unrelated markets can nearly double your risk-adjusted returns. Yet most individual investors hold fewer than five stocks.

You combine your trading subsystems using "instrument weights" — what percentage of risk goes to each market. Use the same handcrafting method from Chapter 4: group correlated instruments, allocate within groups, then across groups.

Because diversification makes the overall portfolio less volatile than its individual pieces, you need to scale positions up with a "diversification multiplier" so the portfolio actually reaches your risk target. But cap this multiplier at 2.5 because during crises (like 2008), all correlations spike toward 1.0 and the diversification benefit temporarily vanishes.

A clever trick called "position inertia" saves a lot of money. Do not trade unless your target position has moved more than 10% from your current position. If you hold 133 contracts and the target moves to 134, the rounding noise does not justify paying the spread twice. This dramatically cuts costs with almost zero impact on performance.

Chapter 12: Speed, Cost, and Account Size

Trading costs are the one thing you can measure with certainty. Returns are uncertain; costs are not. The key metric is "standardized cost" — how much of your Sharpe ratio you lose per round trip of buying and selling. The cheapest futures cost about 0.001 per round trip. Spread bets cost about 0.01. Individual stocks cost even more.

Carver's speed limit rule: never spend more than one-third of your expected Sharpe ratio on costs. If you expect a Sharpe of 0.40, your total annual costs must stay below 0.13. This puts a hard cap on how frequently you can trade. With cheap futures, you might manage a holding period of about one week. With spread bets, more like six weeks. Day trading is virtually impossible for anyone paying normal spreads.

Small accounts face the opposite problem: minimum position sizes force you into lumpy, all-or-nothing bets. If the minimum trade is one futures contract worth $100,000 and your account is $50,000, you can either be 100% in or 100% out — no fine-tuning allowed. Carver recommends your maximum possible position should be at least four times the minimum trade size. If you cannot reach that threshold, either trade fewer markets or drop the problematic instrument entirely.

Chapter 13: The Part-Time Trader

The semi-automatic trader makes their own buy/sell decisions but wraps them in the framework for everything else: position sizing, stop losses, risk management. Carver's example uses £100,000 in trading capital and spread bets on equity indices and currencies.

The key innovation: once you open a bet, you never change your mind about it. No upgrading your forecast because the position is losing. No taking profits because it feels good. Instead, a mechanical trailing stop loss closes the position when the price moves too far against you — measured in volatility units, not fixed percentages. This gives you the positive skew of trend following: small losses, occasional large gains.

No profit targets. Carver's research shows that taking profits early hurts because most markets trend, and you end up exiting winning trades too soon. Only stop losses, never profit targets. Maximum of four bets open at once, each consuming about 1.25% of capital in daily risk. The daily work is minimal: check stop levels, update volatility estimates, execute any required trades.

Chapter 14: The Hands-Off Investor

The asset allocating investor does not try to predict prices at all. Every instrument gets a constant forecast of 10 — "I believe markets go up over time." The entire system reduces to portfolio construction with volatility-adjusted position sizing.

Without leverage, your volatility target is limited by the least volatile thing in your portfolio. Carver's example with ten ETFs across stocks and bonds achieves about 7.5% annual volatility — modest but achievable without borrowing. Expected returns of 3% per year with total cost drag of just 0.24%, which beats nearly all active fund managers.

The beauty shows during crises. In a trading diary from July through October 2008, the system mechanically sold equities as their volatility spiked and bought bonds as their prices rose. No human judgment required. No panic selling at the bottom. The framework simply maintained equal risk allocation across all instruments, which meant automatically reducing exposure to whatever was blowing up.

Chapter 15: The Full Automation Trader

The staunch systems trader uses multiple trading rules across multiple markets with zero human intervention. Carver's example uses $250,000 across six futures contracts spanning bonds, stocks, volatility, currencies, and commodities, with four trading rule variations: carry plus three speeds of trend following.

The backtested Sharpe ratio after costs is 0.53. After applying pessimism (multiply by 0.75) and Half-Kelly (divide by 2), the volatility target is 20%. The instrument diversification multiplier is 1.89. All instruments use the same rules because there is not enough data to justify treating different markets differently.

A trading diary from October through December 2014 shows the system holding large long positions in US treasuries (strong momentum plus positive carry), a short position in equity volatility futures (negative carry offset by trend signals), and smaller positions elsewhere. As profits accumulated, trading capital grew and all positions widened slightly — the Kelly- inspired roll-up mechanism at work.

Epilogue: What Makes a Good Systematic Trader?

Carver closes with seven qualities. Be humble — assume you are less smart, less skilled, and less lucky than you think. Be simple — use rules that have not been over-fitted and weights you could calculate on a napkin. Be skeptical — do not trust backtests, brokers, or authors (including Carver). Be thoughtful — understand why your system might be making money. Be thrifty — know your costs and never break the speed limit. Be nervous — only risk money you can afford to lose, use Half-Kelly, and diversify relentlessly. Be diligent when designing the system but lazy when running it — do the hard work upfront, then leave it alone.

The entire philosophy boils down to this: you are probably not as good at trading as you think you are, and that is fine. Build a framework that does not require brilliance, only discipline. Then stop touching it.

Key Takeaways (Plain English)

Your brain is the problem, not the market

You know what to do. You just cannot do it when money is on the line. Build a system that removes your finger from the trigger.

Simple beats clever

Two trading rules and 20 markets will beat 50 rules and 5 markets. Diversification across instruments is the real prize. More rules just means more opportunities to fool yourself.

You cannot test your way to success

It takes 20-40 years to know if a single rule is genuinely profitable. Stop optimizing and start accepting uncertainty.

Never bet more than half of what math says is optimal

Full Kelly maximizes long-term growth but produces terrifying drawdowns. Half-Kelly gives you 75% of the growth with far less pain.

Costs are certain; returns are not

Never spend more than a third of your expected returns on trading costs. This simple rule eliminates most unprofitable strategies before they start.

Design with energy, run with laziness

All the intellectual effort goes into building the system. Once it is running, the best thing you can do is nothing. Every intervention is a potential mistake.
