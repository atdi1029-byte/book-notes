Introduction

Robert Carver opens with a confession: he is terrible at making financial decisions. Not because he lacks knowledge — he spent nearly a decade running systematic strategies at one of the world's largest hedge funds. He is terrible because he is human. Humans get scared at exactly the wrong moment and greedy at exactly the wrong moment. The entire book exists to solve that problem: build a set of rules, commit to following them, and stop letting your emotions sabotage your money.

The story that drives this home happened in January 2009. Carver had done meticulous research showing that bank stocks were about to rebound. He identified Barclays as the best candidate — its balance sheet was stronger than competitors. The stock crashed 15% in a single day to its lowest level ever, which was exactly what his analysis predicted would be the best buying opportunity. He had planned to put 10% of his portfolio in. At the moment of truth, he panicked and put in only 1%. He bought at 53 pence and later sold at £2.50. The trade made money — but ten times less than it should have.

"I'm terrible at making financial decisions. The reason: I'm a human being." — Robert Carver

Compare that to what happened in September 2008, when Lehman Brothers collapsed. The hedge fund's computer system had no feelings. While every human in the building was discussing whether to shut it down, the computer stuck to its rules and traded the chaos. That single day, the fund made over a billion dollars — the first ten-digit profit in its history. The computer didn't know it was supposed to be scared.

This is the core argument of the book: you don't need to be smarter — you need to be more disciplined. And since humans are constitutionally incapable of discipline under financial stress, you need a system that forces it. Think of it like an autopilot. The pilot knows how to fly, but the autopilot doesn't get nervous in turbulence.

Carver builds his framework for three different kinds of people. The asset allocating investor doesn't try to predict anything — they just want a smart way to divide their money across stocks, bonds, and other assets. The semi-automatic trader makes their own predictions but uses the system for everything else — how much to bet, when to stop out. The staunch systems trader lets the computer make every decision, including what to buy and sell. The book shows you how to build each one, step by step.

You don't need to pick one type and stick with it. Carver himself runs all three: he holds ETFs as an asset allocator, trades UK stocks as a semi-automatic trader, and runs over 40 futures contracts with fully automated rules. The framework is the same for all of them — only the "engine" changes.

The broader point is that the investment industry has been moving this direction anyway. Pension funds are leaving expensive active managers (who charge 1%+ fees for returns that rarely beat a simple index) and going passive. Passive indexing is just very simple systematic trading. The question isn't whether to be systematic, but how sophisticated your system should be.

Most of the "alpha" that hedge funds claim to have is actually "alternative beta" — returns from well-known strategies like trend following or carry trading that require active management but not special genius. You can capture these yourself with the right setup. That's what this book teaches.

Chapter 1: The Flawed Human Brain

Before getting into the technical framework, Carver explains why you need one. The short answer: your brain is wired to be bad at investing. Not because you're stupid, but because evolution optimized human brains for survival on the savannah, not for managing a stock portfolio. The snap judgments that helped your ancestors avoid lions actively hurt you when you're deciding whether to sell a losing position.

The most damaging bias is called "get-evenitis" — the desperate need to avoid locking in a loss. When a stock drops 20%, it feels terrible to sell and make the loss "real." So you hold on, hoping it recovers. Meanwhile, when a stock goes up 15%, you rush to sell and pocket the gain before it disappears. This is exactly backwards. Carver tested this: he ran a "cut losses quickly" strategy against a "take profits quickly" strategy across 31 different futures markets. The loss-cutting strategy won in 27 out of 31. Your instinct to hold losers and sell winners is almost always wrong.

Think about it like this. Imagine you have two restaurant gift cards. One is worth $100 at a great restaurant. The other is worth $100 at a terrible restaurant you hate. A rational person would use the good one and throw away the bad one. But psychologically, people use the bad one first — they don't want to "waste" it, even though eating bad food is the real waste. That's get-evenitis applied to dinner.

Trading also triggers gambling addiction pathways in your brain. Day trading is especially dangerous because it hits all four addiction factors: you feel like you're in control (you're not), you experience near-misses that keep you hooked (almost profitable!), you get frequent small wins (dopamine hits), and the action is rapid and continuous (slot machine pacing). Only a tiny fraction of day traders make money after costs. The rest are essentially feeding slot machines.

So how do you fight your own brain? You need what psychologists call a commitment mechanism — something that physically prevents you from doing the stupid thing your emotions demand. The oldest example is from Greek mythology: Odysseus wanted to hear the Sirens' song without steering his ship onto the rocks, so he had his crew tie him to the mast and plug their own ears with beeswax. He could hear the music but couldn't act on the impulse to steer toward it.

Without a system, emotions override good analysis. A systematic framework removes the override.

A more modern example: trader Victor Niederhoffer locked himself inside a racquetball court and told his assistant Susan, "If I call and beg you to change my positions, do not listen. If losses reach 50%, cut everything in half. If I beg to be released, sell everything." Sure enough, he called almost immediately to override. Susan hung up and closed the positions. He lived to trade another day. The lesson: even the most experienced traders need physical barriers between their emotions and their money.

"If I call and beg you to change my positions, do not listen. If losses reach 50%, cut everything in half. If I beg to be released, sell everything." — Victor Niederhoffer to his assistant

Three traps destroy trading systems before they even start. Over-fitting means your rules are tuned to past data so precisely that they don't work on new data (like memorizing answers to last year's exam instead of learning the material). Overtrading means trading too often, paying more in costs than you earn in profits. Over-betting means putting too much on each trade — with 10x leverage, a 10% drop wipes out half your money.

Finally, Carver introduces two technical concepts you'll need throughout the book. Standard deviation is just a measure of how much prices jump around — think of it as the "usual range of daily movement." Stocks typically move about 1.25% per day (20% per year). Bonds are calmer. You convert between daily and yearly by multiplying or dividing by 16 (the square root of 256 trading days). And the normal distribution — the classic bell curve — seriously underestimates extreme events. Between 1914 and 2014, the Dow Jones had about 30 crashes that the bell curve says should happen once per century. Real markets have fat tails that make "impossible" events routine.

Chapter 2: Trading Rules

A trading rule is simply a formula that tells you when to buy, when to sell, and how strongly. But not all rules are created equal. The chapter explains which ones are worth using and — more importantly — why they make money, because understanding the "why" is what gives you confidence to stick with a rule during the inevitable losing streaks.

There are two ways to find trading rules. Ideas first means you start with a theory about how markets work ("prices tend to keep moving in the same direction") and then test whether the data supports it. Data first means you let a computer scan millions of patterns and find what worked historically. Carver strongly prefers ideas first. If you understand why a rule makes money, you can judge whether the reason will persist. If you just found a pattern in data, you have no idea whether it was real or a coincidence.

So where does the money actually come from? The most reliable source is risk premiums. Think of it like insurance. When you buy car insurance, you pay a small regular premium. The insurance company collects that premium from millions of people and occasionally pays out a big claim. In financial markets, the "insurance seller" earns a steady return for taking on risk. Stocks earn more than bonds because they're riskier. Long bonds earn more than short bonds. High-interest currencies earn a "carry" premium over low-interest ones. These premiums exist because risk is real, and someone has to be compensated for bearing it.

Another source of profit is behavioral effects. Remember get-evenitis? Because most people hold losers and sell winners, prices tend to keep moving in the same direction longer than they "should." This is why trend following works: you buy things that are going up and sell things that are going down, capturing the momentum that human psychology creates. It's profitable precisely because most people are doing the opposite.

Realistic return expectations. If someone promises a Sharpe ratio above 1.0, they're either lying or hiding catastrophic risk.

The Sharpe ratio measures how much return you get per unit of risk. Think of it as a report card grade for your strategy. A single stock gets about 0.15. A diversified mix of stocks, bonds, and commodities gets about 0.40. The absolute best that even sophisticated hedge funds achieve sustainably is about 1.0. If someone shows you a Sharpe ratio above 2.0, they have almost certainly found a pattern in past data that won't repeat, or they're hiding the risk of a catastrophic blowup.

This brings us to skew, the concept that separates surviving traders from blowups. Imagine two strategies. Strategy A makes a small loss most days but occasionally hits a huge winner (like buying lottery tickets). Strategy B makes a small profit most days but occasionally suffers a devastating loss (like selling insurance). Both might have the same average return, but they feel completely different. Strategy A is "positive skew" — trend following and buying options work this way. Strategy B is "negative skew" — carry trading and selling options work this way.

The danger of negative skew is that it looks great until it doesn't. Long Term Capital Management had an apparent Sharpe ratio of 4.6 — impossibly good — because it was earning small steady profits while accumulating massive hidden risk. In 1998, the hidden risk materialized and the fund nearly destroyed the global financial system. Nick Leeson's billion-dollar loss at Barings Bank was the same story: selling options that looked like free money until the market moved against him. Whenever a strategy looks "too good to be true," the most likely explanation is hidden negative skew.

"Whenever a strategy looks too good to be true, the most likely explanation is hidden negative skew — small steady profits masking catastrophic risk." — Robert Carver

The other trap is trading too fast. In theory, trading every hour produces a higher Sharpe ratio than trading monthly because you make more decisions. In practice, each trade costs money (broker fees, bid-ask spreads), and at high frequency those costs devour your profits. Only the cheapest futures markets can support trading frequencies above a few trades per week, and even then it's marginal. Day trading with retail products like spread bets is mathematically impossible to do profitably — the costs simply exceed any realistic edge.

Chapter 3: Fitting

Fitting is the process of choosing which trading rules to use based on how they performed in the past. It sounds completely sensible — of course you want rules that worked before! — but it's actually one of the most dangerous things you can do. This chapter explains why, using stories and numbers that will make you deeply suspicious of any back-test you ever see again.

Carver visited a trading firm called Aqueduct Capital where the boss proudly introduced a young analyst: "He's only been here a month and he's already come up with 50 new trading rules that are profitable in back-tests!" Carver kept a straight face. The firm was liquidated a few months later. The problem: when you test 50 rules, some will look profitable by pure chance, even if none of them actually work. It's like flipping a coin 50 times and being impressed when you get 5 heads in a row. It would have been more surprising if you didn't find some apparent pattern.

This is called the multiple testing problem, and the numbers are devastating. If you test 100 completely random rules (rules that have zero actual ability to predict markets), you will still find about 16 that pass a reasonable quality threshold. Two of them will look spectacular. If you only have one year of data, you need a rule to look incredibly profitable (Sharpe ratio above 3.4) before you can trust it wasn't just luck. With 30 years of data, the bar drops to about 0.6 — still a very high standard.

Test 100 random rules and 16 will pass your quality filter by pure luck. This is why back-tests are dangerous.

How long do you need to tell if a rule genuinely works? The answer is depressing. A rule that truly earns a Sharpe ratio of 0.3 (typical for one instrument) needs about 40 years of data before you can statistically confirm it's real. Even distinguishing a good rule (SR 0.8) from a mediocre one (SR 0.3) takes about 30 years if they're unrelated. The practical lesson: you almost never have enough data to definitively say one rule is better than another.

Carver tested fitting directly on gold futures. He tried 90 variations of a single rule with one year of rolling data. Three approaches: pick the best variation each year (Sharpe ratio: 0.07), pick a random variation (0.20), or just use all 90 equally weighted (0.33). The "smart" approach — picking the best — performed worst! Chasing the best-performing rule actually destroyed value because one year of data is nowhere near enough to identify the genuine winner.

"Pick 100 trading rules at random. Sixteen will pass your quality tests. Two will look spectacular. None of them actually work." — Robert Carver on the multiple testing trap

The safe approach is almost comically simple: don't fit. Choose rules based on how they behave (how fast they trade, how they correlate with other rules), not how well they performed. If you must compare rules, use data the rules haven't seen ("out of sample"). And keep the number of rules small. Carver's own system uses just 8 rules, and he says if starting from scratch, he'd use only 2: a trend-following rule and a carry rule. Adding more instruments (things to trade) helps far more than adding more rules.

At his hedge fund, Carver watched teams reorganize by asset class, each convinced they needed market-specific rules — one set for bonds, another for equities, another for commodities. This is the brain's pattern-matching instinct run amok. Our minds crave stories, and "bonds need special rules because they respond to central banks" is a better story than "use the same rule everywhere." But pooling data across all instruments gives you more evidence, which is exactly what you need when it takes 40 years to prove a rule works.

Chapter 4: Portfolio Allocation

How should you divide your money across different investments? This sounds like it should have a precise mathematical answer, and in theory it does — it's called Markowitz optimization, named after the Nobel Prize-winning economist who invented it in the 1950s. In practice, it produces answers that are absurdly extreme and wildly unstable.

Here's the problem. The optimizer needs two inputs: how good each investment's returns are (Sharpe ratio) and how much they move together (correlation). Correlations are relatively stable and can be estimated well. But return estimates are incredibly noisy. With 15 years of data, you literally cannot tell whether stocks perform better than bonds in a statistically meaningful way. The optimizer treats these noisy guesses as absolute truth and bets heavily on tiny, meaningless differences. The result: a portfolio that puts 68% in bonds, 32% in one stock index, and 0% in another, and then completely reverses itself a few years later.

Think of it like asking a GPS for directions when you're not sure whether you're in New York or Boston. The GPS gives you incredibly precise turn-by-turn directions — but they're for the wrong city. Precision with bad inputs produces confident nonsense.

"The optimizer treats noisy guesses as absolute truth and bets heavily on tiny, meaningless differences." — Robert Carver on Markowitz optimization

Bootstrapping is the first fix. Instead of running the optimizer once, you run it hundreds of times on different random slices of your historical data and average the results. The crazy allocations (68% bonds one time, 10% bonds another) cancel each other out, and you're left with something reasonable. It's like asking 100 people for directions and taking the average — the individual errors wash out.

Even simpler is Carver's signature method: handcrafting. Group similar investments together (US stocks with European stocks, government bonds with corporate bonds). Give equal weight within each group unless correlations differ significantly. Then set group-level weights. You can do the whole thing on the back of an envelope. For three assets — bonds, S&P 500, and NASDAQ — the handcrafted result is 50% bonds, 25% S&P, 25% NASDAQ. The fancy bootstrapping method gives 53%/27%/20%. The simple method is nearly as good.

Handcrafting extends to complex portfolios too. With 16 assets (11 stocks and 5 bonds), Carver groups them at four levels: sector within country, country within asset class, bonds versus equities, and overall. At each level, the group weights come from a simple lookup table based on correlations. Equal weights are the default unless correlations tell you to deviate. The US bond weights are interesting: the 2-year bond has moderate correlation (0.50) with both the 20 and 30-year bonds, while the 20 and 30-year are highly correlated (0.90). Result: the 2-year gets 42% and the others split the remaining 58%, because the 2-year adds more diversification.

You can adjust weights for return estimates, but only carefully. Carver provides a table of conservative multipliers. If you have more than 10 years of data and a reliable cost estimate, you can tilt modestly toward better-performing assets. With less than 10 years of data — don't adjust at all. The uncertainty is too large. This is one of the book's recurring themes: when you don't know something precisely, act as if everyone is equal rather than making big bets on noisy estimates.

Finally, always discount your back-test. If the optimizer says your portfolio should earn a Sharpe ratio of 1.0, the realistic expectation ranges from 25% of that (if you used in-sample single-period optimization) to 75% (if you used out-of-sample bootstrapping). Handcrafting without return adjustments: expect about 70%. The gap between back-tested and realized returns is one of the most important numbers in all of quantitative finance.

Chapter 5: Framework Overview

Now Carver starts building the actual system. Think of a trading system like a car. Most people obsess over the engine — which trading rules to use, which indicators to follow, which signals to trust. But the engine is only one component. You also need a chassis (how to size your positions), a gearbox (how to combine multiple signals), brakes (when to stop trading an instrument), and a speedometer (how fast you can trade without destroying your profits with costs). The framework is everything except the engine.

Carver opens with a deliberately terrible trading system to show what happens without a framework: "Buy when the 20-day moving average crosses above the 40-day. Buy 10 Eurodollar contracts. Set a stop loss at 3% of capital." Every piece of this is broken. Why 10 contracts? That's way too many for a small account and meaningless for a large one. Why 3% stop loss? That's based on your personal pain threshold, not on how volatile the market is. In a calm market, you'll never hit it; in a crisis, you'll hit it every day.

The framework separates these concerns into seven independent modules that snap together like building blocks. Each module does one job and passes its output to the next. If something goes wrong, you can identify exactly which module is the problem without rebuilding everything from scratch.

Seven modules, each doing one job. The same framework works for all three trader types — only module 2 changes.

The key insight is the interface between the engine and the framework: a single number called the forecast. It's scaled so that +10 means "average buy signal" and -10 means "average sell signal," capped at ±20 for safety. It doesn't matter whether that number came from a computer algorithm, your personal judgment, or a constant "always buy" rule. The framework treats all forecasts identically. This is what makes the same framework work for a passive retiree and an active futures trader.

The three trader types plug into the framework differently. The asset allocating investor sets every forecast to a constant +10 (always mildly bullish on everything) and lets the framework handle diversification and sizing. The semi-automatic trader replaces the constant with their own judgment (-20 to +20) and adds a systematic stop loss. The staunch systems trader lets computer algorithms generate all the forecasts. Same chassis, different engines.

Why go through all this trouble? Because the "boring" parts of trading — how much to bet, when to adjust positions, how to handle costs — are actually where most of the value (and most of the danger) lives. A mediocre trading rule with excellent position sizing will outperform a brilliant rule with terrible sizing every time. Carver's framework gets the boring bits right so you can focus your energy on the parts that actually require judgment.

The framework also makes the system transparent. If your portfolio loses money, you can trace exactly why. Was it the forecast that was wrong? Was volatility underestimated? Were costs too high? Were the instrument weights unbalanced? In a black-box system, you just see the loss and have no idea what went wrong, which makes you anxious, which makes you meddle, which makes things worse.

"A mediocre trading rule with excellent position sizing will outperform a brilliant rule with terrible sizing every time." — Robert Carver

Chapter 6: Instruments

Before building a trading system, you need to decide what to trade. This sounds obvious, but the choice matters more than most people realize. Picking the wrong instruments can make your system expensive, dangerous, or simply impractical — regardless of how good your rules are.

Every instrument needs three things. First, data: you need at least daily price history, and ideally decades of it (since it takes decades to validate trading rules). Second, manageable sizes: if the minimum trade is $1 million (like Japanese government bond futures), most individuals simply can't trade it. Third, understanding: you need to know what makes the price move. "More buyers than sellers" is not an answer, Carver warns. If you don't understand whether an instrument responds to interest rates, earnings, commodity supply, or central bank policy, you can't evaluate whether your trading rules make sense for it.

Instruments with very low natural volatility are surprisingly dangerous. When a price is artificially stable — like the Swiss franc while the central bank was holding it at a fixed level — your system calculates that the risk is low and takes an enormous position. Then the stability breaks. In January 2015, the Swiss central bank suddenly stopped defending the currency peg. The franc jumped over 16% in one session. OTC brokers couldn't process orders. Some went bankrupt. The futures exchange, by contrast, operated normally throughout. Carver had avoided this instrument entirely because he recognized the price was controlled by a single actor, not normal market forces.

The choice between different types of financial products matters enormously. Futures are the cheapest to trade, operate on regulated exchanges, and have transparent pricing — but their minimum sizes can be large. Spread bets (popular in the UK) are tax-free and have small minimums, but they're more expensive and traded over-the-counter (meaning your broker is the counterparty, not a regulated exchange). When the Swiss franc blew up, spread bet firms stopped taking orders and retroactively changed fill prices. Futures exchanges processed everything normally.

ETFs (exchange traded funds) provide cheap access to whole asset classes without leverage. They're ideal for the asset allocating investor who doesn't want to manage margin accounts. The cheapest S&P 500 tracker charges 0.05% per year. The most expensive emerging market ETFs charge 0.55%. Either way, it's far cheaper than paying an active manager 1-2% for returns that probably won't beat the index anyway.

Diversification is the most important factor in instrument selection. A portfolio of five similar tech stocks is far less diversified than a portfolio containing one stock, one bond, one commodity, one currency, and one volatility instrument. Carver's own futures portfolio spans over 40 instruments across all major asset classes. For a smaller account, even six futures — one from each class — captures most of the diversification benefit.

The golden rule: build the most diversified portfolio possible without running into problems with minimum trade sizes or excessive costs. Start with one instrument from each major asset class. If your capital allows, add more from each category. But the first instrument in each class provides the biggest bang for the buck — adding a second US stock index after you already have one helps much less than adding your first bond or commodity.

Avoid active managed funds entirely. They charge higher fees, rarely demonstrate persistent skill after costs, and you lose the ability to apply the systematic framework to their holdings. A cheap index ETF does the same job for a fraction of the cost and lets you maintain full control over position sizing and rebalancing.

Chapter 7: Forecasts

The forecast is the bridge between your opinion (or your algorithm's opinion) and the framework that sizes your position. Think of it as a dial that goes from -20 (maximum bearish — sell as hard as possible) through 0 (neutral — no opinion) to +20 (maximum bullish — buy as hard as possible). The number 10 means "average strength buy signal." The number -5 means "mildly bearish." Every forecast in the system runs on this same scale, regardless of what generated it.

Why this particular scale? Because it lets one formula size positions for every instrument and every rule. If crude oil has a forecast of +10 and German bonds have a forecast of +10, the framework gives both positions the same amount of risk (measured in daily volatility). This works because forecasts are volatility-standardized — they measure expected returns relative to expected risk, not in raw dollar terms. A $10 move in oil is very different from a $10 move in bonds, but a "10" forecast means the same thing for both.

Carver illustrates with a story from early in his career. He asked an experienced trader named Sergei how to size a position on German bond futures. Sergei pointed to his heart ("How much do you like this trade?"), then his gut ("How much can you afford to lose?"), then his forehead ("How risky is it?"). Cryptic, but exactly right. The forecast captures the heart — your conviction. The volatility target captures the gut. And price volatility captures the forehead. All three combine in the position-sizing formula.

Why cap at ±20? Five reasons. First, it prevents any single position from taking over your portfolio — a forecast of +100 would mean a position ten times the normal size, which is terrifying. Second, extreme forecasts happen rarely (about 5% of the time), so there isn't enough data to know if they're actually right. Third, markets behave differently at extremes — after a massive crash, a "dead cat bounce" is common, so a huge negative forecast might be wrong. Fourth, extreme forecasts often appear when measured volatility is abnormally low, meaning the position is actually much riskier than it looks. Fifth, the cost of capping is tiny since it only affects 5% of signals.

For semi-automatic traders who make their own calls, opinions translate directly: "Very strong sell" = -20, "Sell" = -10, "Weak buy" = +5, and so on. The critical discipline: never change your forecast after you enter a trade. When a position is losing, you desperately want to reduce it. When it's winning, you want to add more. Both impulses are exactly the emotional sabotage the framework is designed to prevent. Instead, a systematic trailing stop loss handles exits. It tracks the best price since entry and closes the position if the price falls a set distance below that peak.

For asset allocating investors, the forecast is the simplest thing in the world: +10 for everything, always. This says "I believe markets will continue earning their historical risk premium, but I have no idea which direction any particular asset will move." It sounds almost embarrassingly simple, but it captures the one thing we know is true about markets: over long periods, diversified risk-bearing is rewarded.

The technical detail behind forecast scaling: each trading rule produces raw output (say, the difference between a fast and slow moving average). You divide by the instrument's price volatility to get a risk-adjusted number, then multiply by a forecast scalar that ensures the average absolute value comes out to 10. The scalar is calculated from back-test data: if the average raw forecast is 0.3, the scalar is 10 / 0.3 = 33.3. This calibration step is what makes forecasts comparable across all instruments and rules.

"A forecast of +10 means exactly the same thing whether it comes from a computer algorithm, your personal judgment, or a constant 'always buy' rule." — Robert Carver

Chapter 8: Combined Forecasts

If you're running more than one trading rule on the same instrument, they will sometimes disagree. Your trend-following rule might say "oil is going up" while your carry rule says "oil is expensive." The combined forecast is simply a weighted average of all the individual forecasts. It nets out the disagreement and produces a single number that reflects your overall conviction.

Here's a concrete example. Crude oil has three EWMAC (trend following) variations and one carry rule. The trend rules together get 50% weight and carry gets 50%. If the EWMAC composite says +15 (strong buy) and carry says -10 (sell), the weighted average is: (0.5 × 15) + (0.5 × -10) = +2.5. That's a very weak buy signal — your rules basically disagree, so the system takes only a tiny position. If both rules agreed and said +15, the combined forecast would be +15 — a strong, confident position.

This is one of the most elegant features of the framework. When your rules agree, you take big positions. When they disagree, you take small ones. No human judgment is needed to navigate the disagreement — the math handles it automatically.

There's a technical wrinkle: combining forecasts shrinks the scale. Two rules that each average |10| will produce a combined forecast that averages less than 10, because they partially cancel each other. The forecast diversification multiplier corrects for this. With two perfectly correlated forecasts (they always agree), the multiplier is 1.0 — no correction needed. With two uncorrelated forecasts, it's 1.41. With ten uncorrelated forecasts, it's 3.2. The multiplier restores the combined forecast to an average absolute value of 10 so position sizing works correctly.

Carver caps the multiplier at 2.5 to avoid a dangerous edge case. With many uncorrelated rules, the multiplier could grow so large that the combined forecast is almost always pegged at +20 or -20. This effectively turns your carefully scaled system into a binary "all long" or "all short" switch, which is exactly what the scaled forecast was designed to avoid. Binary switches mean full reversals, full reversals mean maximum trading costs.

The weights between rules follow the same handcrafting logic as portfolio weights. Group similar rules together (all trend-following variations in one group, carry in another). Within groups, give more weight to variations that are less correlated with their neighbors. Across groups, default to equal weight unless you have strong evidence (decades of data) that one type of rule is genuinely better.

Faster rules generate more trading, which costs more. Unless faster rules consistently produce better returns before costs — and Carver has never found evidence that they do — you should tilt slightly toward slower rules. In practice this adjustment is tiny (factors of 0.95 to 1.05), because the cost differences between adjacent-speed rules are small.

For the asset allocating investor and the semi-automatic trader, this chapter is mostly irrelevant. If you run only one rule (or one human judgment) per instrument, there is nothing to combine. Only the staunch systems trader with multiple automated rules needs forecast combination.

Chapter 9: Volatility Targeting

This is the most important chapter in the book. If you get everything else right but set your risk level wrong, you will either blow up (too much risk) or earn trivial returns (too little). The volatility target is the single number that controls how much risk your whole system takes. It's expressed as the expected annualized standard deviation of your portfolio returns as a percentage of your capital.

What does that mean in real money? Say you have $100,000 and set a 50% volatility target. That means you expect your portfolio to fluctuate by about $50,000 per year (one standard deviation). On a typical bad day, you might lose $3,125 (that's $50,000 / 16). On the worst day of a typical month, you could lose $5,000. On the worst week of a typical year, about $14,000. These numbers should make you physically uncomfortable — if they don't, you probably haven't thought about them hard enough.

Your volatility target determines the size of your worst days. Pick a number where the losses are painful but survivable.

Now for the math of optimal bet sizing. The Kelly criterion, invented by physicist John Kelly in 1956, says your optimal volatility target equals your expected Sharpe ratio. If your strategy earns SR 0.50, Kelly says target 50% volatility. It maximizes long-term compound growth. The problem is that full Kelly is terrifyingly aggressive. Even if your estimate is perfectly correct, there's a 10% chance you'll lose half your money over ten years.

Why? Because compounding punishes losses more than it rewards gains. Here's a simple example: imagine you expect an average return of 100% per trade. You bet everything. First trade: lose 90%. You now have $10,000 from your original $100,000. Second trade: gain 190%. Your $10,000 becomes $29,000. The average return across both trades was 100% per trade. But you lost 71% of your money. The optimal bet would have been only a quarter of your capital. This is why Kelly feels so aggressive — it optimizes for the long run, but the short-run volatility is brutal.

Carver's solution is Half-Kelly: set your target at half the optimal level. And since your expected Sharpe ratio is almost certainly overestimated (back-tests are always too optimistic), discount it first. Carver's own system has a back-tested SR of 1.0, which he discounts to 0.75 (25% pessimism), then halves for safety: 0.75 × 0.50 = 0.375 = 37% volatility target. That's what he actually runs with real money. For most people, the target should be even lower: 10-25% depending on strategy type and Sharpe ratio.

If your strategy has negative skew (frequent small gains, occasional huge losses), halve the target again. A strategy with SR 0.50 and zero skew targets 25%. The same strategy with skew of -2 targets only 12%. Negative skew makes bad days much worse than the average statistics suggest, and the extra safety margin is essential for survival.

"Even if your estimate is perfectly correct, there's a 10% chance full Kelly will cost you half your money over ten years." — Robert Carver on why Half-Kelly is safer

A final critical rule: never raise your volatility target. The temptation is strongest when things are going well — you're making money, your confidence is high, maybe you should take more risk? This is precisely when a correction is most likely. Start with a low target and a small amount of capital. Gradually add capital as you gain confidence. The percentage target stays fixed; only the absolute dollar amount grows.

Chapter 10: Position Sizing

Position sizing is where everything connects. Your forecast says how bullish or bearish you are. Your volatility target says how much total risk you want. Now you need to figure out the actual number of shares, contracts, or pounds-per-point to trade. The math is a chain of simple steps, each one converting one measurement into the next.

Start with the instrument block: the smallest unit you can trade. One share of Apple. One futures contract of crude oil. One pound per point on a spread bet. Then calculate the block value: how much money a 1% price move generates per block. If Apple trades at $400, a 1% move is $4 — that's the block value per share. For crude oil futures (1,000 barrels at $75/barrel), a 1% move of $0.75 per barrel across 1,000 barrels = $750 per contract.

Next, measure price volatility: how much the price typically moves in a day, expressed as a percentage. Stocks average about 1.25% per day. Bonds are much calmer. Multiply block value by price volatility to get instrument currency volatility — the dollar risk of holding one block for one day. For crude oil: $750 × 1.33% = $997.50 per contract per day.

If you trade in a different currency from the instrument, convert using the exchange rate to get instrument value volatility. A UK trader holding crude oil futures: $997.50 × 0.67 (dollars per pound) = £668 per contract per day. This is the number that feeds into position sizing.

Each step converts one measurement into the next. The same chain works for any instrument in any currency.

The volatility scalar tells you how many blocks to hold at a standard forecast of +10: your daily cash volatility target divided by the instrument value volatility. With £62,500 daily target and £668 per contract, that's 93.52 contracts. This is your "full position" — what you'd hold with an average-strength buy signal.

The subsystem position adjusts for your actual forecast: volatility scalar times forecast divided by 10. At forecast +10 (average buy), you hold 93.52. At forecast -6 (moderately bearish), you short (-6 × 93.52) / 10 = 56.11 contracts. At forecast +20 (maximum bullish), you hold 187.04. At forecast +5 (weak buy), only 46.76. The forecast linearly scales your position from zero to double the baseline.

There's a danger in calm markets. When measured volatility drops very low (as it did for Swiss francs before the peg broke, or Eurodollar futures when interest rates were near zero), the volatility scalar gets very large because you're dividing by a tiny number. This means enormous positions right before volatility explodes. The forecast cap at ±20 limits the damage, but the real protection is avoiding instruments with dangerously low natural volatility in the first place.

For semi-automatic traders who eyeball the chart, an approximate price volatility is fine. For staunch systems traders, Carver recommends an exponentially weighted moving average with a 25-day equivalent lookback. Research shows almost no performance difference between 6-month and 1-year lookbacks, so the exact choice doesn't matter much.

Chapter 11: Portfolios

Diversification is the only free lunch in investing. By spreading money across uncorrelated assets, you can roughly double your Sharpe ratio without taking on any additional risk. Yet most individual investors hold fewer than five stocks and nothing else. This chapter explains how to combine multiple instruments into a properly diversified portfolio.

The math is straightforward. Each instrument has a subsystem position (from Chapter 10). Multiply by the instrument weight (how much of your portfolio goes to this instrument) and the instrument diversification multiplier (a correction factor) to get the final portfolio position. Round to the nearest whole number, and that's your trade.

Instrument weights come from handcrafting (Chapter 4). For three assets — bonds, S&P 500, and NASDAQ — the weights might be 50%, 25%, 25%. The diversification multiplier accounts for the fact that a diversified portfolio has lower volatility than its components. Without it, your actual risk would fall below your target. For this three-asset example, the multiplier is 1.41.

An important subtlety: if you're running trading rules (not just buy-and-hold), you need correlations between your strategy returns, not between the underlying prices. These are lower because your rules modify the exposure. A good rule of thumb: multiply the raw instrument correlation by 0.70 to get the strategy correlation. For passive investors using constant forecasts, use raw correlations directly.

A worked example ties it together. With a €100,000 annual volatility target, the subsystem positions are 9.11 bond contracts (forecast +10), -7.43 S&P contracts (forecast -10), and -13.9 NASDAQ contracts (forecast -15). Multiply each by weight and multiplier: bonds = 9.11 × 50% × 1.41 = 6.42, rounded to 6. S&P = -7.43 × 25% × 1.41 = -2.62, rounded to -3. NASDAQ = -13.9 × 25% × 1.41 = -4.91, rounded to -5. If you currently hold 4 bond contracts, buy 2 more. If you're at -2 S&P, sell 1 more. If NASDAQ is already at -5, do nothing.

Carver caps the diversification multiplier at 2.5 because correlations spike during crises. In 2008, everything fell together — stocks, commodities, corporate bonds, emerging markets. The "free lunch" of diversification temporarily disappeared. A multiplier above 2.5 would leave you dangerously overleveraged exactly when the diversification benefit you're counting on vanishes.

Position inertia prevents wasteful trading. Don't trade unless your target position differs from your current position by more than 10%. If you hold 133 Italian bond futures and the target drifts to 134, the one-contract difference doesn't justify paying spreads twice. With 10% inertia, you only trade when the target reaches 147 or drops to 120. This cuts trading costs dramatically with essentially zero impact on returns for any strategy that holds positions longer than a few days.

For semi-automatic traders who don't have a fixed portfolio, the diversification multiplier is simpler: maximum number of bets divided by average number. If you can hold up to 4 positions but usually have 3, the multiplier is 4/3 = 1.33.

Chapter 12: Speed and Size

Trading costs are the one thing you can measure precisely. Unlike returns (which are uncertain), you know exactly how much each trade costs. This chapter builds a framework for making sure costs never eat too much of your profits.

The key metric is standardized cost: the round-trip cost of trading one block, expressed as a fraction of your annualized volatility in Sharpe ratio units. The formula: (2 × cost per block) / (16 × daily instrument currency volatility). This tells you how much of your raw Sharpe ratio each round trip consumes. For Euro Stoxx futures: trading one contract costs about €8 total (half the spread plus brokerage), and daily volatility is €506. Standardized cost: (2 × 8) / (16 × 506) = 0.002 SR. Very cheap. For a FTSE 100 spread bet: cost is £4, daily vol is £49.50. Standardized cost: 0.01 SR. Ten times more expensive.

Carver's speed limit rule: never spend more than one-third of your expected Sharpe ratio on trading costs. If you expect SR 0.40 per instrument, your maximum annual cost is 0.13 SR. If each trade costs 0.01 SR, you can afford at most 13 round trips per year — roughly one trade every four weeks. For the cheapest futures at 0.001 SR per trip, you could do 130 round trips, which allows holding periods as short as a few days.

More expensive instruments force slower trading. Spread bets can only be held for weeks, not days.

Day trading requires about 500 round trips per year. At the speed limit, your standardized cost must be about 0.00025 SR. Even the cheapest futures cost ten times that. To day trade profitably, you'd need to consistently execute at mid-price or better, which requires professional infrastructure. This is why the vast majority of amateur day traders lose money: even tiny per-trade costs become devastating at high frequency.

Small accounts have the opposite problem: not enough capital to hold properly sized positions. If your S&P 500 futures position should be 2.7 contracts but the minimum is 1, your actual risk is either zero or roughly double what you intended. Your positions become "lumpy" — binary switches rather than smoothly scaled. Carver recommends a maximum possible position of at least 4 blocks. If you can't reach this, either trade fewer instruments or drop the problematic one entirely.

Larger accounts face the mirror problem: their orders are big enough to move the market. If your typical trade exceeds the liquidity at the best available price, you'll push the price against yourself as you fill. Solutions include breaking orders into smaller chunks, using limit orders (risking the price moving away), or employing algorithmic execution strategies. None guarantees execution at the half-spread cost the framework assumes.

The bottom line: know your costs precisely, set your trading speed to stay within the limit, and accept that more expensive instruments must be held for longer periods. A cheap future can be traded every few days. An expensive ETF should be rebalanced every few months. Fighting the speed limit is fighting math.

"Never spend more than one-third of your expected Sharpe ratio on trading costs. Everything above that is just making your broker rich." — Robert Carver

Chapter 13: Semi-automatic Trader

The semi-automatic trader is someone who wants to make their own trading decisions but needs a system to handle the dangerous parts: how much to bet, when to cut losses, how to manage overall risk. Think of it as "human brain for strategy, computer brain for execution." The example uses £100,000 capital, spread bets on stock indices and currencies, and a few minutes of daily attention.

You translate your opinion into a number from -20 to +20 using a simple table. "I think oil will go up" becomes a forecast of +10 (a standard buy). "I'm really convinced the euro is going to crash" becomes -20 (maximum sell). "I have a slight feeling bonds might rally" becomes +5 (weak buy). The system then calculates exactly how large your position should be given current volatility, your risk target, and how many other bets you have open.

The most important rule: once you enter a trade, you never change your forecast. You don't "add to winners" or "reduce losers." That's your emotions talking, and your emotions are terrible at trading. Instead, exits are handled by a trailing stop loss that closes the position if the price falls a set number of daily standard deviations from its best level since entry. With the recommended setting of X=4, the average trade lasts about 6.5 weeks.

Why no profit targets? Because most markets trend. Setting a profit target means you exit winning trades too early, missing the long trends that generate most of your returns. The trailing stop lets winners run while mechanically cutting losers. This gives your strategy positive skew — frequent small losses, occasional big wins — which is far easier to survive psychologically than the alternative.

The risk math: with a post-cost Sharpe ratio of about 0.30 (conservative for a semi-automatic approach), the recommended volatility target is 15%. On £100,000, that's an annual cash volatility of £15,000, or a daily target of about £938. Maximum four positions, average three. Each individual bet puts about 1.25% of capital at risk. Annual cost drag: about 1.2% of capital — more than a passive ETF but far less than a hedge fund.

You can add new bets on top of existing positions (pyramiding), but each new bet counts toward your maximum of four positions. Total forecast on any single instrument must never exceed 40 units across all concurrent bets. So if you have a +10 forecast on oil and open a second bet at +25, you can't add a third bet above +5 (because 10 + 25 + 5 = 40).

The daily routine takes minutes. Check stop losses on existing positions — trade immediately if any triggered. Adjust stop levels for new highs (longs) or new lows (shorts). Look for potential new entries and assign a numeric forecast. Calculate position sizes. If you have a day job, update stop-loss limits with your broker and leave. If trading full-time, continue monitoring for new entries throughout the day.

Block values for spread bets work slightly differently from futures because the bet size is in pounds per point. For crude oil at $65 with £10 per point, a 1% move is 0.65 points = £6.50 per block. You don't need to worry about exchange rates because the spread bet is already denominated in your currency. This simplicity is one reason spread bets appeal to UK-based retail traders despite costing more per trade.

Chapter 14: Asset Allocating Investor

The asset allocating investor is the simplest application of the framework. You don't predict prices. You don't use trading rules. You set every forecast to a constant +10 ("I believe all markets will earn their historical risk premium over time") and let the framework handle diversification, volatility adjustment, and rebalancing. The example uses €10 million across ten ETFs, but the principles work just as well with €10,000.

Without leverage, your volatility target is limited by the calmest instrument in the portfolio. If inflation-linked bonds have 6.88% annualized volatility and you don't want total leverage above 90% of capital, the math gives a 7.5% volatility target. Expected return: about 3% per year above the risk-free rate (assuming SR of 0.40). Not spectacular, but steady, diversified, and practically free — annual costs under 0.25%.

The portfolio uses ten ETFs spanning developed equities, emerging equities, developed bonds, and inflation-linked bonds. Handcrafted weights, with sensible constraints: no more than 40% in bonds or equities, no more than 30% in emerging markets. The instrument diversification multiplier is 1.61, reflecting the meaningful decorrelation between asset classes.

Rebalancing happens weekly in about 15 minutes. Get your account value. Calculate the daily volatility target. Get current prices. Update your volatility estimate (using a slow 20-week lookback to minimize trading). Calculate target positions. Trade only if the current position is more than 10% away from target. Most weeks, you trade nothing. The occasional rebalance costs about 0.4 round trips per year across the whole portfolio.

If you want to incorporate views ("I think European stocks will outperform"), translate your prediction into a Sharpe ratio and adjust instrument weights using conservative multipliers. If you expect Euro Stoxx to return 8% with 16% volatility (SR 0.50 vs portfolio average of 0.20), you increase its weight by about 17%. But be cautious: every adjustment generates additional trading costs, and overconfident predictions are the number one source of performance destruction.

The 2008 crash diary demonstrates the framework beautifully. On July 1, the portfolio was 57% in bonds by cash value despite only 40% bond weight — because the volatility-based sizing naturally buys more of the calmer asset. By October 1, after Lehman's bankruptcy, the system tilted modestly toward equities (strategists were bullish). By October 10, equity volatility had exploded. The system mechanically sold equities and bought bonds, limiting losses while most human investors were frozen with fear.

The key lesson from 2008: the system's modest equity tilt cost some money, but far less than a human would have lost by panicking and selling everything at the bottom, or by going all-in on equities because "they're so cheap now!" The framework imposes discipline precisely when discipline matters most.

The biggest risk for the asset allocating investor isn't a market crash — it's boredom. The system is designed to be boring. You rebalance weekly, mostly do nothing, earn modest returns, and never have an exciting trading story to tell at dinner parties. That's the point. Excitement in investing usually means you're doing something wrong.

"The biggest risk for the asset allocating investor isn't a market crash — it's boredom." — Robert Carver

Chapter 15: Staunch Systems Trader

The staunch systems trader is the full-fat version: multiple automated trading rules, multiple instruments, fully systematic. No human judgment at any point in the process. Carver's example uses $250,000, six futures contracts, and four trading rule variations. Even a part-time trader can run this with free daily data and about 30 minutes per evening.

Instrument selection is a balancing act between diversification and practical constraints. The six futures: Eurodollar (interest rates), US 5-year Treasury note (bonds), Euro Stoxx (equities), V2TX (European volatility), Mexican peso (FX), and corn (commodities). One from each major asset class, each cheap enough to trade at reasonable frequency, each large enough to support multiple contracts at this capital level. V2TX is limited to 10% of the portfolio because it has nasty negative skew (selling volatility futures is dangerous).

Two types of trading rules drive the system. EWMAC is trend following: it compares a fast moving average to a slow one. When the fast is above the slow, prices are trending up — buy. When below, trending down — sell. Three speed settings (fast, medium, slow) provide diversification across holding periods. Carry measures whether an instrument pays you to hold it. If the expected yield exceeds the cost of funding, buy. If not, sell.

Forecast weights give carry 50% and the three EWMAC variants 50% (split 21%/8%/21%). The medium EWMAC gets only 8% because it's highly correlated with both neighbors — adding it barely improves diversification. The forecast diversification multiplier is 1.31, which inflates the combined forecast to restore the expected absolute value of 10.

Back-tested Sharpe ratio after costs: 0.53. After applying a 25% pessimism discount: 0.40 expected in real trading. With slightly positive skew (the EWMAC trend-following component offsets V2TX's negative skew), the recommended volatility target is 20%. That means $250,000 × 20% = $50,000 annual cash volatility target.

The daily routine: get your account value, calculate the current daily volatility target ($50,000 / 16 = $3,125). Update prices. Calculate price volatility for each instrument using the 25-day EWMA lookback. Generate forecasts from each trading rule on each instrument. Combine forecasts using the weights and diversification multiplier. Calculate subsystem positions. Multiply by instrument weights (Eurodollar 11.7%, T-note 11.7%, Euro Stoxx 20%, V2TX 9.8%, MXP/USD 23.3%, Corn 23.3%) and the instrument diversification multiplier (1.89). Round. Trade only if position inertia (10%) is exceeded. The whole process fits in a spreadsheet.

The October-December 2014 trading diary shows the system in action. On October 15, the system held a large long in Treasury notes (strong momentum plus high carry forecast). V2TX was short on carry (-20 forecast) despite a recent volatility spike. By December 1, cumulative profits of $10,000 had grown capital to $260,000, which mechanically increased all position sizes through the Kelly-inspired roll-up mechanism. Trading occurred almost daily across the six instruments — not because any individual rule changes fast, but because four rules on six instruments produce frequent small adjustments.

The system won't make you rich quickly. At a 20% volatility target and 0.40 expected Sharpe ratio, you're looking at about 8% expected annual returns before fees and taxes. On $250,000, that's $20,000 per year. Not life-changing money. But it compounds, it's systematic, and it doesn't require you to be glued to a screen. Most importantly, it won't blow up — which is more than most active traders can say.

Epilogue: What Makes a Good Systematic Trader?

Carver closes with seven qualities that separate successful systematic traders from the wreckage. Be humble: assume you're not as smart as you think. Be simple: use rules a child could understand. Be sceptical: don't trust back-tests, brokers, or books (including this one). Be thoughtful: understand why your system makes money. Be thrifty: know your costs to the penny. Be nervous: only bet what you can afford to lose. Be diligent in design but lazy in execution — build the system carefully, then don't touch it.

The hardest quality on the list is the last one: diligent design followed by lazy operation. Every instinct screams at you to tinker, improve, or actively manage a system that's having a bad month. But the evidence is overwhelming: the more you meddle, the worse you do. The best systematic traders do their hard work upfront and then spend their trading hours doing something else entirely.

Even with everything done right, you might still lose money. Markets are uncertain, and no framework eliminates that uncertainty. What the framework does is size your risk so losses are survivable, measure your risk so surprises are smaller, and remove the emotional override that turns manageable losses into catastrophic ones. In a field where most participants lose money, that's an extraordinary advantage.

Carver's final words capture the spirit of the entire book: "So it only remains for me to say: Good Luck!" He means it literally. Luck matters in trading, and acknowledging that is the most humble — and therefore the most systematic — thing a trader can do.

"So it only remains for me to say: Good Luck!" — Robert Carver
