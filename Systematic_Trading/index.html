Introduction

On 23 January 2009, Robert Carver was managing a portfolio of systematic trading strategies for a large London hedge fund when GDP data revealed the UK economy was crumbling faster than anyone expected. His personal research told him that moments of maximum fear were historically the best time to buy, and he had carefully identified Barclays as the bank most likely to rebound. The stock had just crashed 15% to its lowest level ever. Everything was set up perfectly.

Then panic took over. Instead of committing 10% of his portfolio to banking stocks as planned, he cut his position to just 1%. He bought Barclays at 53p per share and sold months later at an average of £2.50. The trade was profitable, but the emotional override cost him a fortune. It was, he says, one of the biggest mistakes of his investing career.

"I had planned carefully and meticulously, done everything right, and then at the last moment let my emotions get the better of me." — Robert Carver

Just months earlier, in September 2008, Carver had watched from the same desk as Lehman Brothers collapsed. While terrified humans debated shutting everything down, the firm's computer system stuck to its preprogrammed rules and mechanically exploited the market chaos. That day the fund made over a billion dollars — the first ten-digit day in its history. The juxtaposition was devastating: machines that obeyed rules thrived while humans who knew better choked.

These two episodes frame the entire book. Humans are brilliant analysts but terrible executors of financial decisions. The solution is to systematize as much of the process as possible — not because computers are smarter, but because they are immune to the emotional sabotage that makes smart people do stupid things with money.

Carver organizes his framework around three trader types. The asset allocating investor builds diversified portfolios of ETFs without trying to predict prices. The semi-automatic trader makes discretionary forecasts but uses a systematic framework for position sizing and risk management. The staunch systems trader goes all in on rules-based trading with no human override. Carver himself runs all three: ETFs for asset allocation, UK equities as a semi-automatic trader, and over 40 futures contracts with eight fully systematic rules.

Chapter 1: The Flawed Human Brain

Classical finance assumed perfectly rational actors making optimal decisions in efficient markets. From the 1980s onward, psychologists dismantled this fantasy. Our brains carry evolutionary baggage from environments where snap judgments about predators mattered more than careful probabilistic reasoning. This mismatch created behavioural finance, unified by prospect theory.

The most destructive bias is what Hersh Shefrin called "get-evenitis" — the inability to take losses. We hold losing positions hoping they recover, and sell winners too quickly for the warm confirmation that we were right. Carver back-tested an "early profit taker" against an "early loss taker" across 31 futures contracts. The early loss taker won in 27 of them. Trading against your instincts is almost always the right move.

"Unfortunately taking small profits and letting losses run is almost always a bad idea." — Robert Carver

Trading also shares characteristics with gambling addiction: the illusion of control, near misses, rapid feedback loops. Day trading is particularly dangerous because it combines all the addictive elements. Only a tiny percentage of day traders cover their costs, yet people keep trying — their frantic churning indistinguishable from an addict at a slot machine.

Knowing the right answer is not enough. You need a commitment mechanism to prevent meddling. Carver invokes Odysseus tying himself to the mast to hear the Sirens, and Victor Niederhoffer locking himself in a racquetball court while his assistant Susan had orders to close positions no matter how much he begged. Automation helps, but even automated systems have override buttons. The real commitment comes from designing a system you understand and trust deeply enough to leave alone.

Three pitfalls destroy trading systems before they start: over-fitting (tuning rules to historical noise), overtrading (trading too frequently relative to costs), and over-betting (sizing positions too large for your capital). A system with a one-in-ten chance of losing 5% daily becomes terrifying at ten times leverage — you face a 10% chance of losing half your capital on any given day. Most sane people would start meddling immediately.

Standard deviation measures the dispersion of returns and serves as the primary risk metric throughout the book. Equities have an annualized standard deviation of about 20%. You convert between daily and annual by multiplying or dividing by 16 (the square root of 256 business days). A Gaussian normal distribution puts about two-thirds of returns within one sigma of the mean and 95% within two sigma — though real markets produce fat tails that make extreme events far more common than the bell curve predicts.

Chapter 2: Systematic Trading Rules

Good trading rules can come from two directions. Ideas first starts with a hypothesis about market behavior and tests it against data. Data first mines historical patterns and hopes they persist. Leda Braga of Systematica hedge fund advocates ideas first: "We think about that first before mining the data." Carver agrees. Ideas-first rules are simpler, more transparent, less prone to over-fitting, and their profits are easier to explain.

An explainable rule gives you confidence it will keep working. Trend following profits because prospect theory predicts people will run losses and cut winners — as long as humans have these biases, momentum strategies should earn returns. A rule you understand is also a rule you can trust during drawdowns, which matters enormously for sticking to the plan.

Where do trading profits come from? Several sources. Risk premia reward you for bearing specific risks: equities earn a premium over bonds, longer bonds earn more than shorter ones, high-yield currencies earn the carry premium. Skew preferences create opportunities because people overpay for positive-skew instruments (lottery tickets, deep out-of-the-money options) and demand premiums for negative-skew positions. Leverage constraints leave high-Sharpe but low-volatility assets unloved by institutions that cannot borrow. And behavioural effects like prospect theory create persistent mispricings.

The Sharpe ratio is the central performance metric: annualized return divided by annualized standard deviation. Realistic Sharpe ratios for a single equity are about 0.15. A diversified equity portfolio might reach 0.25. Spanning multiple asset classes gets you to 0.40. Carver's own highly diversified futures system back-tests at 1.0 — and he considers that an upper bound for anyone.

"Is leverage dangerous? Not always. Leverage alone is a poor measure of risk, as varying amounts make sense for different assets. What makes it deadly is the potential for large unexpected losses." — Robert Carver

Two paths to artificially high Sharpe ratios are deadly. Hidden negative skew produces steady gains for years before catastrophic losses reveal the true risk — Long Term Capital Management had an apparent Sharpe ratio of 4.6 before blowing up in 1998. Trading faster exploits the law of active management but runs headlong into costs. At spread-bet prices, it is impossible even in theory to day-trade profitably.

Skew is the most overlooked strategy characteristic. Positive-skew strategies (trend following, buying options) produce frequent small losses with occasional large gains. Negative-skew strategies (carry, relative value, selling options) produce steady income punctuated by blowups. Nick Leeson lost a billion dollars selling straddles on Japanese equity options. John Paulson made billions buying cheap credit default swaps before 2007. Both were playing skew — on opposite sides.

Chapter 3: Fitting

Shortly after leaving his hedge fund, Carver visited Aqueduct Capital, a prop trading firm. The boss proudly introduced a young quant who had discovered 50 profitable trading rules in a single month using off-the-shelf back-testing software. Carver managed to keep a straight face. The firm was liquidated a few months later.

"This is Joe. He's only been here a month and he's already come up with 50 new trading rules that are profitable in back-tests!" — Aqueduct Capital boss

The fundamental problem with fitting is the multiple testing problem. If you test 100 random rules with zero true profitability, Table 3 shows you will still find 16 that pass a Sharpe ratio threshold of 0.5, and 2 that pass a threshold of 2.0. With only one year of data, you need an SR above 3.4 to be confident a rule from a pool of 50 is genuinely profitable. Even with 30 years of data, you can only safely test a handful of rules at an SR cutoff of 0.6.

How long does it take to determine if a rule is genuinely profitable? Table 5 is sobering. A rule with a true Sharpe of 0.5 takes 20 years on average to pass the T-test. At SR 0.3 — typical for a single instrument — you need nearly 40 years. Even distinguishing between a 0.3 SR rule and a 0.8 SR rule takes about 30 years if they are uncorrelated. The practical lesson: you almost never have enough data to definitively pick one rule over another.

Always use out-of-sample testing. In-sample back-tests (fitting and testing on the same data) produce fantasy results. Carver prefers an expanding window: fit on all past data, test on the next period, extend the window, repeat. A rolling window discards old data to adapt to regime changes but needs decades to be statistically meaningful. Never use a time machine.

Carver's fitting process avoids fitting almost entirely. Come up with a small number of ideas-first rules. For each rule, select a few variations based on behavior (trading speed, correlation with others) — not performance. Reserve actual return data for determining forecast weights later. If starting from scratch, begin with just two rules: trend following and carry. Diversification across instruments matters far more than having many rules. Adding new instruments is more boring than inventing new rules, but far more beneficial.

Chapter 4: Portfolio Allocation

Classic Markowitz optimization is elegant mathematics that produces ugly portfolios. Carver demonstrates with three assets: NASDAQ, S&P 500, and 20-year US bonds. The single-period optimizer puts 68% in bonds and 0% in NASDAQ — extreme, unstable weights driven by noisy estimates of Sharpe ratios that cannot be statistically distinguished.

"When I asked one of these rocket scientists what they thought about the extreme portfolio weights they'd found I often got a shrug. 'These are what the optimizer came up with.'" — Robert Carver

Bootstrapping fixes the problem by running the optimization hundreds of times on different random subsets of the data and averaging the resulting weights. Extreme allocations cancel out, and the averaged weights naturally reflect the uncertainty in the data. The three-asset bootstrap gives 53% bonds, 27% S&P, 20% NASDAQ — much more reasonable than the single-period result.

Even simpler is handcrafting: write down sensible weights using a pencil and the back of an envelope. Three assets with identical correlations get equal weights. For three assets with mixed correlations, the table gives specific weights (e.g., correlations of 0.0, 0.9, 0.0 yield weights of 27%, 46%, 27%). The more diversifying asset gets more weight.

Handcrafted weights for the three-asset example: 50% bonds, 25% S&P, 25% NASDAQ. Close to bootstrap results despite taking only seconds. You can adjust for Sharpe ratios using Table 12 multipliers, but only if you have more than ten years of data and use conservative adjustments. With less data, don't adjust at all — you cannot distinguish performance reliably.

Chapter 5: Framework Overview

Trading systems are like cars. The trading rules are the engine. The framework is everything else: chassis, drivetrain, gearbox. Most traders obsess over the engine and neglect the boring but essential position management that translates forecasts into appropriately sized trades.

A typical flawed system might say "buy when the 20-day MA crosses above the 40-day" with a position limit of "10 Eurodollar contracts" and a stop loss at "3% of capital." Every one of these parameters is arbitrary. Why 3%? Does it make sense for a conservative investor and an aggressive one? For 2006 calm and 2008 chaos? The framework separates concerns: trading rules produce forecasts, volatility targeting sets overall risk, and position sizing converts the two into actual positions.

The framework is modular, transparent, and uses well-defined interfaces. A forecast of +10 means the same thing regardless of which rule produced it or which instrument it applies to. You can swap out any component — a new trading rule, a different position-sizing formula — without breaking the rest. The three trader types plug different engines into the same chassis.

Seven components snap together in sequence. Instruments define what you trade. Forecasts produce scaled predictions with an expected absolute value of 10, capped at ±20. Combined forecasts merge multiple rule outputs via weighted average. Volatility targeting sets overall portfolio risk. Position sizing converts forecasts and risk targets into actual trade sizes. Portfolios combine multiple trading subsystems using instrument weights. Speed and size constraints ensure you never pay too much in costs or run into minimum position problems.

The beauty of this architecture is that each component can be tested and understood independently. If your returns disappoint, you can diagnose whether the problem lies in the trading rules, the forecast combination, the volatility estimate, or the portfolio construction — rather than staring at a black box wondering what went wrong. Transparency breeds trust, and trust is what keeps you from meddling during drawdowns.

Chapter 6: Instruments

Before you can trade an instrument, it must meet minimum requirements: available price data, manageable minimum sizes, and your understanding of what moves its price. The Japanese government bond future at 150 million yen per contract is simply untradeable for most individuals. Even Berkshire Hathaway A-shares at over $100,000 each pose granularity problems.

Instruments with extremely low volatility should be excluded entirely. When a pegged currency like the Swiss franc finally breaks loose, the explosion can wipe out years of gains in minutes. In January 2015, the Swiss National Bank removed its EUR/CHF peg and the franc appreciated over 16% in a single session. OTC brokers stopped accepting orders, re-marked fills after the fact, and some went into liquidation. Exchange-traded futures operated normally — always prefer exchange over OTC.

"Minimum sizes reduce the granularity of what you can trade. Your positions become binary – all or nothing (in the case of JGBs, always nothing)." — Robert Carver

Choose instruments with low correlation to each other, low trading costs, and adequate liquidity. Exchange traded funds are useful when leverage is unavailable but cost more to trade than futures. Spread bets offer tax-free returns in the UK but are OTC and more expensive. Futures are cheapest and exchange-traded but have large minimum sizes.

The choice between exchange-traded and OTC products matters more than most traders realize. When the Swiss franc peg broke, OTC brokers stopped accepting orders, re-marked fills retroactively, and several went into administration. Futures exchanges operated normally throughout the chaos. Derivatives provide straightforward leverage with clearly defined margin requirements. Cash instruments like stocks require actual capital but avoid counterparty risk. Active funds charge higher fees and rarely demonstrate persistent alpha after costs — passive index trackers or ETFs are almost always the better choice for the asset allocating investor.

Chapter 7: Forecasts

Early in his career, Carver asked a more experienced trader named Sergei how to size a position on German bond futures. Sergei pointed to his heart: "How much do you like this trade?" To his gut: "How much can you afford to lose?" To his forehead: "How risky is it?" Unhelpful but totally correct. You need all three elements, and the forecast captures the first.

"You need to know three things. First, how much do you like this trade? That comes from here," Sergei replied, pointing to his heart. — Sergei

A forecast is a scaled prediction proportional to expected risk-adjusted returns. The recommended average absolute value is 10, capped at ±20. A forecast of +10 is an average bullish view, +20 is maximum conviction, -5 is mildly bearish. The scaling uses volatility standardization: divide expected return by expected standard deviation, which is effectively a Sharpe ratio. This lets the same rule work across all instruments without modification.

Capping at 20 serves multiple purposes. It prevents any single position from dominating portfolio risk. Very large forecasts appear rarely in back-tests, so there is limited evidence they translate into proportionally larger returns. Extreme forecasts often coincide with abnormally low volatility that is about to spike. The reduction in returns from capping is tiny — only about 5% of forecasts exceed the cap — but the risk control is significant.

Semi-automatic traders translate qualitative opinions into the same scale: "Very strong sell" = -20, "Sell" = -10, "Weak buy" = +5, and so on. Asset allocating investors use a constant forecast of +10 for all instruments — the simplest possible "rule" that says the market will continue delivering its historical risk premium.

A critical rule for semi-automatic traders: never change your forecast once a bet is open. The temptation to downgrade a winning position or upgrade a losing one is precisely the emotional sabotage the framework exists to prevent. Instead, a systematic trailing stop loss handles exits. The stop triggers when the price moves a set number of daily standard deviations from its best level since entry. This approach inherits the favorable positive skew of trend following — profits run while losses are mechanically cut.

Chapter 8: Combined Forecasts

When multiple trading rules disagree, you combine their forecasts using a weighted average. The weights are forecast weights, found through handcrafting or bootstrapping. Group variations of the same rule together, allocate within groups, then across rules. Carver's example gives carry 50% and three EWMAC trend-following variations a combined 50% (with the middle-speed variation getting only 8% because it is highly correlated with its neighbors).

Consider crude oil with an EWMAC forecast of +15 and a carry forecast of -10. At 50/50 weights, the raw combined forecast is +2.5 — a weak bullish view reflecting the disagreement between momentum and carry signals. The combined forecast captures the net conviction across all your rules for a single instrument.

Combining forecasts reduces the scale of the output. Two imperfectly correlated forecasts, each with average absolute value 10, produce a combined forecast below 10. Multiply by the forecast diversification multiplier to restore the scale. With two perfectly correlated forecasts, the multiplier is 1.0 — combining adds nothing. At zero correlation the multiplier jumps to 1.41. With ten uncorrelated forecasts it reaches 3.2. Carver caps the multiplier at 2.5 because higher values effectively turn a continuous forecast into a binary signal — always near +20 or -20 — which eliminates the cost advantage of scaled positioning.

The complete combination process runs in five steps: generate individual forecasts (expected absolute value ~10, capped at ±20), apply forecast weights (positive, summing to 100%), compute the weighted average, multiply by the forecast diversification multiplier, and cap the rescaled result at ±20. Floor any negative correlations at zero when calculating the multiplier, since negative correlations between your own rules likely reflect over-fitting rather than genuine diversification.

Chapter 9: Volatility Targeting

This is the most important decision in trading system design. Nearly all amateurs lose money because positions are too large. The volatility target is the annualized expected standard deviation of your portfolio returns, expressed as a percentage of trading capital.

With $100,000 capital and a 200% volatility target, you face a $20,000 worst daily loss every month. A 50% target reduces that to $5,000. Negative-skew strategies make the worst days even nastier — with skew of -2, the worst monthly loss every ten years at 50% volatility is $36,000 instead of $32,000 for zero skew.

The Kelly criterion, invented by physicist John Kelly in 1956, says set your volatility target equal to your expected Sharpe ratio. But full Kelly is dangerously aggressive — even with a correct estimate, there is a 10% chance of losing half your capital over ten years. Carver advocates Half-Kelly: set risk at half the optimal level.

"My experience has been that most cautious investors who use Kelly find the frequency of substantial bankroll reductions to be uncomfortably large." — Ed Thorpe

Carver's own system (back-tested SR 1.0, adjusted by 0.75 pessimism factor to 0.75, then halved for Half-Kelly) runs at 37% volatility target. For staunch systems traders with SR 0.50, he recommends 25%. For semi-automatic traders who cannot back-test: maximum 25%. For asset allocating investors with static portfolios: the target is usually constrained by lack of leverage to single-digit percentages.

Never change your percentage volatility target upward — the temptation to increase risk during good times is pure meddling. Start with less capital and gradually increase until fully invested. Roll up profits into capital (Kelly instructs this) and reduce after losses. Check daily if using leverage.

Chapter 10: Position Sizing

Position sizing connects forecasts and risk targets to actual trade sizes. The instrument block is the minimum tradeable unit (one share, one futures contract, one pound per point on a spread bet). The block value is how much a 1% price move translates into cash. For WTI crude at $75 with 1,000 barrels per contract, that is $750.

Price volatility — the daily standard deviation of percentage returns — is measured with a 25-day exponentially weighted moving average. Multiply price volatility by block value to get instrument currency volatility, then convert to your account currency to get instrument value volatility.

The volatility scalar tells you how many blocks to hold at a constant forecast of +10: daily cash volatility target divided by instrument value volatility. The subsystem position adjusts for the actual forecast: volatility scalar times forecast divided by 10. A crude oil example with £62,500 daily target, £668 instrument value volatility, and forecast of -6 gives a short position of 56.11 contracts. Do not round yet — that comes later at the portfolio level.

How do you measure price volatility? Carver offers three methods. You can eyeball the chart — fine for semi-automatic traders who watch markets daily. You can calculate a precise standard deviation over a moving window, though short windows (10 days) are noisy and long windows (100 days) are sluggish. Or you can use an exponentially weighted moving average with a 25-day equivalent look-back — the default in the RiskMetrics system and Carver's preferred approach. Research shows almost no performance difference for look-back periods between six months and a year, but shorter periods help the system adapt to regime changes in volatility.

A danger lurks in calm markets. When measured volatility drops to abnormally low levels, position sizes balloon. Credit default swaps in early 2007, zero-interest-rate Eurodollar futures, and the Swiss franc before the peg broke all exhibited deceptively low volatility that invited enormous positions — right before volatility exploded. The capping of forecasts at ±20 provides some protection, but the real safeguard is excluding instruments whose natural volatility is so low that you need extreme leverage to reach your target.

Chapter 11: Portfolios

Diversification is the only free lunch in investing. Spanning multiple asset classes can double your expected Sharpe ratio. Yet individual investors typically hold fewer than five stocks. The portfolio combines multiple trading subsystems using instrument weights determined by handcrafting or bootstrapping.

A critical subtlety: you need correlations between trading subsystem returns, not raw instrument returns. For dynamic strategies, subsystem return correlations are roughly 0.70 times instrument return correlations. For static asset allocators, use instrument correlations directly.

The instrument diversification multiplier scales positions up to compensate for the diversification benefit that makes portfolio volatility lower than its components. In crises like 2008, correlations spike toward 1.0 and the diversification benefit vanishes — which is why the multiplier is capped at 2.5. For semi-automatic traders, the multiplier is simply the maximum number of concurrent bets divided by the average expected number. The maximum itself should never exceed 2.5 times the average.

The final portfolio instrument position is the subsystem position times the instrument weight times the diversification multiplier, rounded to the nearest integer. A three-asset example with bonds, S&P 500, and NASDAQ (50%, 25%, 25% weights and a 1.41 multiplier) converts subsystem positions of 9.11, -7.43, and -13.9 into portfolio positions of 6, -3, and -5 contracts respectively.

Use position inertia to avoid unnecessary trading. If you hold 133 Italian government bond futures and the target moves to 133.52, that rounds to 134 — but the 0.04 contract change does not justify paying the spread twice. With 10% inertia, you only trade when the target reaches 147 or drops below 120. This dramatically reduces trading costs with negligible impact on pre-cost performance, and it works for any trading rule with a holding period longer than a few days.

Chapter 12: Speed and Size

Trading costs are the one thing you can measure with certainty, unlike returns. Standardized cost measures the round-trip expense as a fraction of annualized volatility, in Sharpe ratio units. Execution cost is typically half the bid-offer spread for smaller traders. The cheapest futures (FTSE 100) cost about 0.001 SR per round trip. Index spread bets cost about 0.01 SR. Individual equity spread bets and ETFs cost more.

"I wouldn't trade a strategy which paid two-thirds of its profits out as costs, amounting to 1.0 SR annually. I personally think it's foolhardy to pay more than a third of your expected profits in trading costs." — Robert Carver

The speed limit rule: never pay more than one-third of your expected Sharpe ratio in costs. For staunch systems traders expecting 0.40 SR per instrument, that is a maximum of 0.13 SR in annual costs. For semi-automatic and asset allocating investors expecting 0.25 SR, the limit is 0.08 SR. With Euro Stoxx futures at 0.002 SR per round trip and a 0.13 limit, the maximum turnover is 65 round trips per year — a holding period of about one week.

Day trading requires execution costs near zero (consistently hitting mid-price or better). Even the cheapest futures cost about 0.001 SR per round trip, meaning 500 annual round trips eats 0.5 SR. This is why the vast majority of amateur day traders are unprofitable.

Small accounts face the opposite problem: minimum position sizes create lumpiness. Carver recommends a maximum possible position of at least four instrument blocks. If you cannot reach this threshold, either reduce the number of instruments in your portfolio or drop the problematic instrument entirely. Build the most diversified portfolio possible without running into minimum size problems.

Chapter 13: Semi-automatic Trader

The semi-automatic trader makes discretionary forecasts but wraps them in a systematic framework for stop losses, position sizing, and risk management. The example uses £100,000 trading capital, spread bets on equity indices and FX, with a 15% volatility target (based on a conservative post-cost SR expectation of 0.30).

Forecasts are never changed once a bet is open. Instead, a systematic trailing stop loss closes positions when the price moves X daily standard deviations from its best level since entry. With X=4, the average holding period is 6.5 weeks and turnover is 8 round trips per year — exactly at the 0.08 SR speed limit for spread bets costing 0.01 SR each.

Position sizing uses the same framework: daily volatility target of £937.50 (annual £15,000 divided by 16), instrument weights of 25% (maximum four bets), diversification multiplier of 1.33 (max 4 bets, average 3). Each bet puts about 1.25% of capital at risk on average. You can pyramid by adding new bets on top of existing positions, but each counts toward the maximum. Total forecast on any single instrument must never exceed 40 units.

No profit targets. Carver's research shows systematic profit targets hurt because most markets trend, and taking profits early means exiting trends prematurely. By using only stop losses, the strategy inherits the favorable positive skew of trend following.

Chapter 14: Asset Allocating Investor

The asset allocating investor does not forecast prices. Using a constant forecast of +10 for all instruments, the entire system reduces to portfolio construction with volatility-adjusted position sizing. The example uses €10,000,000 across ten ETFs spanning developed and emerging equities, developed and inflation-linked bonds.

Without leverage, the volatility target is constrained by the lowest-volatility instrument. The achievable target turns out to be 7.5%, implying modest expected returns of 3.0% per year (assuming SR of 0.40). Cost drag is just 0.24% annually, cheaper than most active funds. The 20-week look-back for estimating price volatility keeps turnover at 0.4 round trips per year.

The 2008 crash trading diary illustrates the framework in action. Starting July 2008 with €10,000,000, the portfolio was 57% in bonds by cash value despite only 40% bond weight — reflecting the risk parity nature of volatility-adjusted sizing. By October 10, after Lehman's collapse, equity volatility had exploded. The system mechanically sold equities and bought bonds, limiting losses while most human investors were paralyzed with fear.

Performance opinions can be incorporated by adjusting instrument weights using the Sharpe ratio multipliers from Table 12, but changes should be small and infrequent. Tilting the portfolio slightly toward equities in October 2008 (when strategists were bullish) had modest effect because the uncertainty-adjusted multipliers are conservative by design.

Chapter 15: Staunch Systems Trader

The staunch systems trader uses both the framework and multiple trading rules. Carver's example uses $250,000 capital, six futures contracts (Eurodollar, US 5-year note, Euro Stoxx, V2TX equity volatility, Mexican peso, corn) spanning all major asset classes, and four trading rule variations: carry plus three speeds of EWMAC trend following.

Forecast weights are handcrafted: carry gets 50%, with the three EWMAC variations splitting the other 50% (21%, 8%, 21% for fast, medium, and slow). The forecast diversification multiplier is 1.31. All instruments use the same rule set, with costs calculated using the most expensive instrument (V2TX at 0.009 SR) to stay safely within the 0.13 SR speed limit.

The back-tested Sharpe ratio after costs is 0.53. Applying a 75% pessimism factor gives a realistic 0.40, which Table 25 maps to a 20% volatility target assuming roughly zero skew (90% of the portfolio is in benign assets). The instrument diversification multiplier is 1.89 after adjusting Euro Stoxx's weight upward to 20% to meet the four-contract minimum position requirement.

The October 2014 trading diary shows the system in action: large long US treasury notes (strong momentum and carry), short V2TX (negative carry forecast of -20 offset by trend-following signals), and modest positions elsewhere. By December, cumulative profits of $10,000 increased trading capital and widened all positions slightly through the Kelly-inspired roll-up mechanism.

Epilogue: What Makes a Good Systematic Trader?

Carver closes with seven qualities. Be humble: underestimate your intelligence, skill, and luck. Be simple: use rules that haven't been over-fitted, handcrafted portfolio weights, and constant forecasts where possible. Be sceptical: don't trust back-tests, brokers, or authors (including Carver). Be thoughtful: understand why you might be making money. Be thrifty: know your trading costs and never break the speed limit. Be nervous: only commit capital you can afford to lose, use Half-Kelly, and diversify ruthlessly. Be diligent when creating systems but lazy when running them — the hard work goes into design, then you leave it alone.

"So it only remains for me to say: Good Luck!" — Robert Carver

Key Takeaways

Emotions are the enemy, not ignorance

Humans are better analysts than any computer but worse executors. Systematize decisions to remove emotional interference. The Barclays trade and the Lehman billion-dollar day prove the point.

Over-fitting is the cardinal sin

You need 20-40 years of data to determine whether a single rule is profitable. Test few rules, pool data across instruments, never use in-sample results, and always apply pessimism factors to back-tests.

Diversification beats optimization

Adding instruments matters more than adding rules. Handcrafted portfolio weights beat unstable Markowitz optimization. Equal weights are hard to beat unless correlations meaningfully differ.

The forecast framework scales everything

Forecasts with average absolute value 10, capped at ±20, standardized by volatility. The same framework serves discretionary traders, passive investors, and full system traders.

Half-Kelly for risk sizing

Set your volatility target at half the pessimistic estimate of your Sharpe ratio. Full Kelly works mathematically but produces unbearable drawdowns in practice.

Never break the speed limit

Pay no more than one-third of expected Sharpe ratio in trading costs. This severely constrains how fast you can trade, especially with expensive instruments.

Position inertia saves money for free

Do not trade unless the target position is more than 10% away from current. This dramatically cuts costs with zero impact on pre-cost performance.

Avoid low-volatility instruments

They require extreme leverage, are expensive to trade relative to risk, and blow up spectacularly when volatility returns. The Swiss franc and LTCM are cautionary tales.

Contrarian Ideas

Don't fit your trading rules at all

Most practitioners obsess over calibrating rules to maximize back-test performance. Carver argues you should select rules based on behavior (speed, correlation) and reserve performance data exclusively for setting forecast weights.

50 rules is worse than 2

The marginal value of additional rules is low. Instrument diversification dominates rule diversification. Carver runs eight rules from five themes and considers this generous.

Back-tests are dangerous, not helpful

Every back-test is a form of in-sample fitting. Even the best out-of-sample methods require decades of data to produce statistically meaningful results. Treat all back-test returns with deep skepticism.

Sharpe ratios above 1.0 are fantasy

No systematic hedge fund has sustained SR above 1.0 for more than a few years. If your back-test shows 2.0+, you have over-fitted. The highest realistic SR for a highly diversified system is 1.0.

Optimization is more art than science

Experienced portfolio optimizers routinely override their own models to get "sensible" results. Handcrafting formalizes this intuition without the pretense of mathematical precision.

Frameworks & Models

The Three Trader Types

  • Asset allocating investor: constant +10 forecast, ETFs, no leverage, 7-20% volatility target
  • Semi-automatic trader: discretionary forecasts (-20 to +20), systematic stop losses, spread bets, 15-25% target
  • Staunch systems trader: multiple rule variations, futures, 20-50% target

The Modular Framework

  1. Trading rules produce scaled forecasts (avg abs value 10, capped ±20)
  2. Combined forecast = weighted average × forecast diversification multiplier
  3. Volatility target set via Half-Kelly
  4. Subsystem position = volatility scalar × forecast / 10
  5. Portfolio position = subsystem × instrument weight × instrument diversification multiplier
  6. Trade only if position inertia threshold exceeded (10%)

Handcrafting Method

  1. Group correlated assets
  2. Allocate within groups using Table 8
  3. Allocate across groups
  4. Multiply weights through all levels
  5. Optionally adjust for Sharpe ratios (Table 12 multipliers)

Speed Limit Rule

  • Max cost = 1/3 of expected Sharpe ratio
  • Staunch traders: 0.13 SR/year max
  • Semi-automatic/asset allocators: 0.08 SR/year
  • Standardized cost = 2C / (16 × ICV)

Key Trading Rules

Systematic Trading: A unique new method for designing trading and investing systems by Robert Carver. Published by Harriman House, 2015. 326 pages.

A former hedge fund manager's complete framework for removing emotion from trading decisions. Covers the full stack from trading rules and portfolio allocation through position sizing and cost management, with worked examples for three distinct trader types.

About the Author

Robert Carver spent nearly a decade as a portfolio manager for AHL, one of the world's largest systematic hedge funds, which is part of the Man Group. At AHL he was responsible for the design of trading systems and the management of multi-billion dollar portfolios of systematic strategies across global futures markets.

After leaving the hedge fund industry, Carver continued to manage his own money using the same systematic principles described in the book. He trades over 40 futures contracts with eight trading rules, manages a portfolio of UK equities as a semi-automatic trader, and holds ETFs as an asset allocating investor. He holds degrees in economics and finance from Manchester and Birkbeck, University of London.

Carver has since written several follow-up books including Smart Portfolios (2017) on portfolio construction and Leveraged Trading (2019) for smaller account traders. His website systematictrading.org provides spreadsheets, code, and additional details for implementing the framework described in this book.

Why This Book Matters

Most trading books fall into one of two traps: theoretical elegance divorced from practice, or specific rules without a coherent framework. Carver bridges the gap with a modular system that works for a retiree holding ten ETFs and a professional running forty futures contracts. The emphasis on realistic expectations — Sharpe ratios capped at 1.0, back-tests discounted by pessimism factors, Half-Kelly for position sizing — is a powerful antidote to the overconfidence that dominates trading literature.

The handcrafting method for portfolio weights is a genuine contribution. It produces results close to sophisticated bootstrap optimization without requiring any computing power, and it exposes the dirty secret of quantitative portfolio management: experts routinely fiddle with optimizer output until it looks right. Carver formalized the fiddling.

Perhaps most importantly, the book is honest about the limits of systematic trading. You will still need luck. You will still face drawdowns. The framework does not eliminate risk — it sizes it appropriately, measures it consistently, and prevents the emotional sabotage that turns smart analysis into poor execution. In a field drowning in false promises, that is a remarkably valuable offering.
