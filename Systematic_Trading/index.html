Introduction

On 23 January 2009, Robert Carver was managing a portfolio of systematic trading strategies for a large London hedge fund when GDP data revealed the UK economy was crumbling faster than anyone expected. His personal research told him that moments of maximum fear were historically the best time to buy, and he had carefully identified Barclays as the bank most likely to rebound — its balance sheet was in relatively good condition compared to peers. The stock had just crashed 15% to its lowest level ever. Everything was set up perfectly for a massive position.

Then panic took over. Instead of committing 10% of his portfolio to banking stocks as planned, he slashed his position to just 1%. He bought Barclays at 53p per share and sold months later at an average of £2.50 — nearly a five-fold return. The trade was profitable, but the emotional override cost him a fortune. Had he stuck to the original plan, the profit would have been ten times larger. It was, he says, one of the biggest mistakes of his investing career: doing everything right in the analysis and then letting fear override reason at the moment of execution.

"I had planned carefully and meticulously, done everything right, and then at the last moment let my emotions get the better of me." — Robert Carver

Just months earlier, in September 2008, Carver had watched from the same trading desk as Lehman Brothers collapsed and the US government scrambled to rescue insurer AIG and mortgage agencies Freddie Mac and Fannie Mae. While terrified humans debated shutting the fund's trading system down, the computer stuck to its preprogrammed rules and mechanically exploited the market chaos. A colleague showed Carver the live estimate of the day's profitability. For the first time in the firm's history, the number had ten digits — over a billion dollars in a single day. The juxtaposition was devastating: machines that obeyed rules thrived while humans who knew better choked.

"Our computer system had stuck to its preprogrammed set of trading rules and mechanically exploited the market moves almost to perfection, whilst terrified humans had discussed closing it down." — Robert Carver

These two episodes frame the entire book. Humans are brilliant analysts but terrible executors of financial decisions. The solution is to systematize as much of the process as possible — not because computers are smarter, but because they are immune to the emotional sabotage that makes smart people do stupid things with money. The gap between knowing what to do and actually doing it is enormous, and twenty years of managing his own investments convinced Carver that the only reliable bridge is a set of explicit, objective rules that you commit to following regardless of how you feel.

The book is organized into four parts. Part One covers the theoretical basis for systematic strategies — why human brains fail at trading and what kinds of trading rules actually work. Part Two is a toolbox covering back-testing and portfolio optimization, powerful techniques that become dangerous when misused. Part Three builds a complete, modular framework for creating systematic strategies. Part Four applies that framework to three distinct trader types, with detailed worked examples for each.

Carver organizes the framework around those three types. The asset allocating investor builds diversified portfolios of ETFs without trying to predict prices at all — a constant forecast of +10 for everything. The semi-automatic trader makes discretionary forecasts but uses the systematic framework for position sizing, stop losses, and risk management. The staunch systems trader goes all in on rules-based trading with no human override. Carver himself runs all three: ETFs for asset allocation, UK equities as a semi-automatic trader, and over 40 futures contracts with eight fully systematic trading rules across five themes.

The broader context matters too. Institutional investors have been migrating from expensive active management (fees of 1%+) to cheaper passive management. Passive indexing is itself a form of systematic trading, just an extremely simple one. Hedge fund returns decompose into beta (market tracking), alpha (genuine skill), and alternative beta (returns from well-known strategies that require active trading but not special skill). Most of what hedge funds charge alpha fees for turns out to be alternative beta — and you can capture it yourself with the right systematic framework.

Chapter 1: The Flawed Human Brain

Classical finance, built in the 1950s on the assumption of purely rational actors, produced elegant theories of perfectly efficient markets. From the 1980s onward, psychologists systematically dismantled this fantasy. Our brains carry evolutionary baggage from environments where snap judgments about predators mattered far more than careful probabilistic reasoning. The mismatch between Stone Age instincts and modern financial markets created the field of behavioural finance, unified by Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky's prospect theory.

The most destructive trading bias is what Hersh Shefrin named "get-evenitis" — the visceral inability to accept losses. Prospect theory predicts this precisely: losses feel roughly twice as painful as equivalent gains feel good. So we hold losing positions hoping they will recover (avoiding the pain of crystallizing a loss), and we sell winners too quickly for the warm confirmation that we were right. Carver back-tested an "early profit taker" against an "early loss taker" across 31 futures contracts over decades of data. The early loss taker — the strategy that cuts losses and lets profits run — won in 27 of the 31 markets. Trading against your instincts is almost always the right move.

"Unfortunately taking small profits and letting losses run is almost always a bad idea." — Robert Carver

Trading shares characteristics with gambling addiction. Psychologists identify four factors that make gambling addictive: illusion of control, near misses, frequent small prizes, and rapid continuous flow. Systematic investing eliminates the illusion of control almost entirely — the computer decides, not you. But day trading maximizes every addictive factor. The rapid feedback loop, the near misses, the small wins that keep you coming back. Only a tiny percentage of day traders actually cover their costs and earn a reasonable income, yet people keep pouring money in.

"Their frantic churning of trades is not so very different from the addict spending hours in front of a slot machine." — Robert Carver

Knowing the right answer is not enough — you need a commitment mechanism to prevent what Carver calls meddling, driven by the biggest cognitive bias of all: overconfidence. We think that being cleverer and knowing more should translate into better decisions, but usually it does not. The earliest recorded commitment mechanism comes from Odysseus, who ordered his crew to stuff their ears with beeswax and tie him to the mast so he could hear the Sirens without steering the ship onto the rocks. In trading, the analogy is building a system you trust and then physically preventing yourself from overriding it.

Victor Niederhoffer provides a vivid modern example. He locked himself inside a racquetball court and issued instructions to his assistant and future wife Susan: "Do not listen to my entreaties if I wish to double further. If the losses reach 50% of the winnings, reduce my positions by one-half. If I beg to be released, sell everything out." Victor immediately called to override. Susan closed the entire position, and Victor lived to fight another day. The lesson is clear: even the most disciplined trader needs external constraints when emotions flare.

"'Do not listen to my entreaties if I wish to double further. If the losses reach 50% of the winnings, reduce my positions by one-half. If I beg to be released, sell everything out.'" — Victor Niederhoffer to Susan

Three pitfalls destroy trading systems before they start. Over-fitting is the narrative fallacy applied to data — our brains see patterns in random noise and construct convincing stories about why those patterns should persist. Overtrading results from illusory superiority (nearly all drivers consider themselves above average, and nearly all traders believe their extra activity adds value). Over-betting is endemic among amateurs with easy access to leveraged derivatives — at ten times leverage, there is a 10% chance of losing half your capital on any given day. Most sane people would start meddling with their system immediately after a loss of that magnitude.

Standard deviation is the primary risk metric throughout the book. Volatility is used as shorthand. Equities have an annualized standard deviation of about 20%. Bonds range from 1.5% (two-year) to 8% (ten-year). To convert between daily and annual, multiply or divide by 16 — the square root of 256 business days. Average daily returns annualize by multiplying by 256. One standard deviation (one sigma) captures about 68% of observations under the normal distribution; two sigma captures 95%.

But the Gaussian normal distribution dangerously underestimates extreme events. Between 1914 and 2014, the Dow Jones fell by more than four standard deviations roughly 30 times — an event the normal distribution predicts should happen once per century. At a 200% annualized volatility target, your daily standard deviation is 12.5%, and there is roughly a 2.5% chance of losing more than 24.2% in a single day. The fat tails of real market returns mean that "once in a lifetime" events happen several times per decade, and your risk management must account for this or you will eventually be destroyed by an event your model said was impossible.

Chapter 2: Systematic Trading Rules

Good trading rules can come from two directions. Ideas first starts with a hypothesis about market behavior — perhaps that interest rates attract currency flows — and then tests it against data. Data first mines historical patterns computationally and hopes they persist. Leda Braga, head of the Systematica hedge fund, captures the ideas-first approach perfectly: "There's a creative moment when you think of a hypothesis, maybe it's that interest rate attracts currency rates. So we think about that first before mining the data." Carver agrees. Ideas-first rules are simpler, more transparent, less prone to over-fitting, and their profits are easier to explain — which matters because explainability gives you confidence the rule will keep working.

A good rule must also be systematizable. Not all trading styles qualify. Esoteric technical analysis patterns where two chartists disagree on what they see in the same chart cannot be programmed. Merger arbitrage requires event-by-event judgment. Strategies based on Google or Twitter sentiment lack sufficient historical data. The simplest rules — fewer parameters, fewer moving parts — carry the least over-fitting risk and are the easiest to trust during drawdowns.

Where do trading profits actually come from? The most reliable source is risk premia. Markets function as insurance: those who buy protection accept lower returns; those who sell insurance earn a premium for bearing risk. Equities earn a premium over bonds. Longer-duration bonds earn more than shorter ones. Equity value strategies harvest a premium by buying cheap stocks that others avoid. John Paulson made billions in 2006-2007 by buying cheap credit default swap insurance on risky mortgages — a classic positive-skew bet where the insurance was massively underpriced because everyone assumed housing prices could never fall nationally.

The Sharpe ratio is the central performance metric: annualized mean return divided by annualized standard deviation. With roughly 256 business days per year, annualized SR equals about 16 times daily SR. The metric assumes symmetric gains and losses, which makes it unreliable for comparing assets with meaningful skew. Realistic Sharpe ratios are far lower than most people expect: about 0.15 for a single equity, 0.25 for a global equity index, 0.40 for a multi-asset portfolio, and 0.50 to 1.0 for a highly diversified futures system. Even the most sophisticated institutional investors rarely sustain a Sharpe ratio above 1.0.

Realistic Sharpe ratios by strategy type. SR above 1.0 is rarely sustained even by sophisticated institutional investors.

Skew is the most overlooked characteristic when evaluating strategies. Negative skew means a higher probability of large downward moves — equities are mildly negative, while selling volatility futures or running carry trades is severely negative. Positive skew means large upward moves are more likely — safe havens like gold and Swiss francs, or trend-following strategies that cut losses and let winners run. In October 1987, the stock market fell 20% in a single day — roughly a 20-sigma event that the normal distribution considers essentially impossible.

Two paths to artificially high Sharpe ratios are deadly. The first is hidden negative skew. Consider an imaginary strategy producing alternating returns of 100% and 65% for twenty years — a spectacular Sharpe ratio of 4.6. Then in year 21 it loses 100% and you lose everything. Even after the blowup, the 21-year Sharpe ratio is still an "excellent" 1.7. Long Term Capital Management had an apparent SR of 4.6 before its catastrophic 1998 collapse. If you see a Sharpe ratio above 2.0, the most likely explanation is hidden negative skew waiting to reveal itself.

"Is leverage dangerous? Not always. Leverage alone is a poor measure of risk, as varying amounts make sense for different assets. What makes it deadly is the potential for large unexpected losses." — Robert Carver

The second deadly path is trading too quickly. The law of active management says Sharpe ratio scales with the square root of trading frequency: a rule with monthly SR of 0.40 becomes 1.8 daily and 5.2 hourly — in theory, before costs. But costs are real and devastating. Trading every couple of days on futures could theoretically produce SR above 1.0, but for expensive spread bets it is impossible to trade quickly and profitably at any frequency. Only futures are cheap enough for faster strategies, and even there, the cost drag is severe.

The Greek bonds example illustrates how negative skew hides in plain sight. From 2007 to early 2010, relative value trades buying and selling five-year Greek government bonds earned a steady return below 1% with almost zero measured volatility. The trade looked like free money. Then in early 2010 the Greek debt crisis erupted and the relationship collapsed completely. The steady gains had tricked traders into thinking the position was low risk, when in reality they were earning a tiny premium for bearing catastrophic sovereign default risk. The same pattern killed Nick Leeson, who lost a billion dollars selling straddles on Japanese equity options in January 1995, and devastated FX carry traders when the Swiss National Bank abandoned its currency peg in January 2015.

Crowded trades amplify all these dangers. When the majority of the market holds the same leveraged bet, a small adverse move triggers cascading liquidation. After a long stable period, profits shrink, apparent risk drops, leverage increases — and then the music stops. Long Term Capital Management in 1998 and the Quant Quake of August 2007 both followed this pattern: relative value strategies that worked beautifully until everyone was doing them simultaneously.

Chapter 3: Fitting

Shortly after leaving his hedge fund, Carver visited Aqueduct Capital, a proprietary trading firm. The boss proudly introduced a young quantitative analyst who had discovered 50 profitable trading rules in a single month using off-the-shelf back-testing software. Carver managed to keep a straight face. The firm was liquidated a few months later. The story illustrates the most dangerous process in systematic trading: fitting, the act of selecting trading rules from a pool of candidates based on historical performance.

"This is Joe. He's only been here a month and he's already come up with 50 new trading rules that are profitable in back-tests!" — Aqueduct Capital boss

The fundamental problem is the multiple testing problem. If you test 100 completely random rules with zero true profitability, pure chance guarantees that some will look profitable. Carver's Table 3 quantifies this: testing 100 random rules, you will find 30 that pass a Sharpe ratio threshold of 0.5, 16 that pass 1.0, and roughly 2 that pass 2.0. The more rules you test, the higher the bar must be before you can trust a result. With only one year of data, you need an SR above 3.4 to be confident that a rule from a pool of 50 is genuinely profitable rather than a statistical fluke.

How long does it take to determine if a rule is genuinely profitable? Table 5 applies a T-test with a two-sigma threshold (2.5% chance of false positive) and the results are sobering. A rule with a true Sharpe of 0.5 needs 20 years of data to pass. At SR 0.3 — typical for a single instrument — you need nearly 40 years. Even distinguishing between a 0.3 SR rule and a 0.8 SR rule takes about 30 years if they are uncorrelated. With highly correlated rules (0.95+), you can distinguish them faster, but only if the SR difference is at least 1.0 — a massive gap that almost never exists between similar rules in practice.

At SR 0.3 (typical for one instrument), you need nearly 40 years of data to statistically confirm profitability.

Carver tested the impact of fitting on CME gold futures using an early loss taker rule with 90 variations and a one-year rolling window. Three approaches: pick the best variation each year (SR 0.07), pick a random variation (SR 0.20), or keep all variations equally weighted (SR 0.33). The supposedly optimal approach — picking the best — performed worst because one year of data is nowhere near enough to identify the genuinely superior rule. The "smart" selection process actually destroyed value by chasing noise.

Always use out-of-sample testing. In-sample back-tests — fitting and testing on the same data — produce fantasy results that systematically favor complex, over-fitted rules. Half out-of-sample testing splits data into fit and test halves, but wastes half your precious history. Carver prefers the expanding window: fit on all data up to a point, test on the next unseen period, extend the window, repeat. Nothing is wasted, and the system never sees future data. A rolling window discards old data beyond a fixed lookback, which helps adapt to regime changes but needs a very long window to be statistically meaningful.

At his former hedge fund, Carver watched teams reorganize by asset class and become convinced they needed market-specific trading rules — one set for bonds, another for equities, another for commodities. This is the narrative fallacy at work: our minds crave stories, and market-specific rules tell a better story than one-size-fits-all. But pooling data across instruments gives you more history, which is exactly what you need when it takes 40 years to confirm a single rule. Diversification across instruments is preferable to diversification across rules.

"This is a classic example of the narrative fallacy, a cognitive bias we have seen before. To our human minds it makes sense to have different stories, and so different trading rules, for different instruments." — Robert Carver

Seven rules for effective fitting: keep it simple (complex methods hide over-fitting), test fewer alternatives (the smaller the pool, the lower the multiple-testing penalty), ban time machines (always use rolling or expanding out-of-sample), don't drop rules casually (you need 30+ years to tell them apart), pool data across instruments, compare apples with apples (negative-skew rules have deceptively high Sharpe ratios), and remember the future won't look like the past (many assets did extraordinarily well over the last 40 years due to falling inflation that cannot repeat from current levels).

Carver's own process avoids fitting almost entirely. Come up with a small number of ideas-first rules. For each rule, select a few variations based on behavior — trading speed, correlation with other rules — not performance. Reserve actual return data for determining forecast weights later. If starting from scratch, begin with just two rules: trend following (EWMAC) and carry. In his own portfolio he runs eight rules from five themes, but considers even this generous. Adding new instruments delivers far more benefit than adding rules, because the average correlation between different rules on the same instrument is higher than between the same rule on different instruments.

Chapter 4: Portfolio Allocation

Classic Markowitz optimization is elegant mathematics that produces ugly portfolios. Harry Markowitz's single-period optimization finds the maximum risk-adjusted return using estimates of mean returns and correlations. In practice the results are extreme and unstable. Carver demonstrates with three assets: NASDAQ, S&P 500, and 20-year US bonds. The optimizer puts 68% in bonds and 0% in NASDAQ — an allocation that would have looked brilliant during the tech bust but absurd during the preceding bubble. Change the estimation period slightly and the weights swing wildly.

"When I asked one of these rocket scientists what they thought about the extreme portfolio weights they'd found I often got a shrug. 'These are what the optimizer came up with.'" — Robert Carver

The root problem is estimation error. With 15 years of data, you cannot reliably distinguish Sharpe ratios between assets — but you can distinguish their correlations. The classic optimizer treats unreliable SR estimates as gospel truth, producing extreme bets on small differences that are statistically meaningless. Equal weights make sense when assets have the same volatility (always true after volatility standardization), similar Sharpe ratios, and similar correlations. When correlations differ meaningfully, you can tilt weights — but only with great caution.

Bootstrapping fixes the instability by repeating the optimization hundreds of times on different random subsets of the historical data, then averaging the resulting weights. Extreme allocations that depend on one lucky data sample cancel out, while genuine patterns survive across many samples. The three-asset bootstrap produces 53% bonds, 27% S&P, and 20% NASDAQ — far more reasonable than the single-period result of 68%/32%/0%. The averaging naturally reflects uncertainty: when data is noisy, bootstrapped weights drift toward equal; when differences are genuine, they show up in the average.

Even simpler is handcrafting, Carver's signature contribution: construct portfolio weights with a pencil and the back of an envelope. Group correlated assets together, allocate within groups using Table 8 (a lookup table built from bootstrap experiments on artificial data), then allocate across groups. Two assets always get 50/50. Three assets with identical correlations get equal weights. For three assets with mixed correlations (say 0.0, 0.9, 0.0 between them), the table gives specific weights: 27%, 46%, 27%. The more diversifying asset — the one least correlated with the others — gets relatively more weight.

The three-asset handcrafted result: 50% bonds, 25% S&P, 25% NASDAQ. Close to bootstrap results despite taking seconds instead of hours. Carver demonstrates the method on a 16-asset portfolio spanning 11 equities (3 UK banks, 2 UK retailers, 3 US banks, 3 US retailers) and 5 bonds (2 UK, 3 US) with four levels of grouping: sector within country, country within asset class, asset class, then overall. The US bonds are particularly interesting: the 2-year has correlation of 0.50 with the 20-year and 0.50 with the 30-year, while the 20-year and 30-year are correlated at 0.90 — producing handcrafted weights of 42% (2-year), 29% (20-year), and 29% (30-year), materially different from the naive 33% each that equal weighting would give.

You can adjust handcrafted weights for Sharpe ratios using Table 12 multipliers, but only with appropriate caution. The table has three columns: Column A for costs (known with certainty), Column B for historical SR estimates (uncertain), and Column C for less than 10 years of data (don't adjust at all). With the three-asset example, historic SRs of 0.0 (NASDAQ), 0.5 (S&P), and 0.75 (bonds) produce final adjusted weights of 18% NASDAQ, 24% S&P, and 58% bonds — tilting toward better performers without the extreme 0% allocation that single-period optimization would produce.

"When I bring in Sharpe ratio estimates, handcrafting up-weights better performing assets and produces similar results to bootstrapping, but does not result in extreme portfolios like single period optimisation." — Robert Carver

Table 14 provides pessimism factors for every back-testing methodology. In-sample single-period optimization: expect only 25% of back-tested returns to survive in live trading. Out-of-sample single-period: 75%. In-sample bootstrapping: 60%. Out-of-sample bootstrapping: 75%. Handcrafting without Sharpe ratios: 70%. Handcrafting with Sharpe ratios: 65% (slightly lower because the SR adjustment introduces another source of estimation error). Always discount your back-test before using it to set your volatility target.

Is handcrafting cheating because it cannot easily be repeated for a rolling out-of-sample back-test? Carver tests this directly. Using in-sample handcrafting rather than rolling out-of-sample bootstrapping gave an insignificant advantage: Sharpe ratio of 0.54 versus 0.52. In comparison, in-sample single-period optimization produced an unrealistically high SR of 0.84 — and when forced into a rolling out-of-sample test, collapsed to just 0.30. Handcrafted weights are stable enough that the distinction between in-sample and out-of-sample barely matters, which is precisely why they work.

Chapter 5: Framework Overview

Trading systems are like cars. The trading rules are the engine — the source of power. The framework is everything else: chassis, drivetrain, gearbox, suspension, brakes. Most traders obsess over the engine, constantly tinkering with new rules and indicators, while completely neglecting the boring but essential position management that translates forecasts into appropriately sized trades. A Ferrari engine bolted to a shopping cart chassis is not a race car.

Carver opens with a deliberately bad example to show what happens without a proper framework. The system says: buy when the 20-day moving average crosses above the 40-day, take a position of 10 Eurodollar contracts, and set a stop loss at 3% of capital. Every one of these parameters is arbitrary and dangerous. Why 10 contracts? Does that make sense for a $50,000 account and a $5 million account? Why 3% of capital as a stop loss? That threshold is based on the trader's pain tolerance, not on market volatility. In calm markets it will rarely trigger; in volatile markets it triggers constantly. The position size ignores how risky the instrument is — 10 Eurodollar contracts carry completely different risk than 10 crude oil contracts.

"The solution is to separate out the components of your system: trading rules (including explicit or implicit stop losses), position sizing, and the calculation of your volatility target." — Robert Carver

The framework solves all these problems by separating concerns into independent modules. Each module has a clear job and a well-defined interface with the others. You can swap out any component — a new trading rule, a different portfolio construction method — without breaking the rest. A forecast of +10 means the same thing regardless of which rule produced it, which instrument it applies to, or which trader type is using the framework.

Seven components snap together in sequence. Instruments define what you trade. Forecasts produce scaled predictions with an expected absolute value of 10, capped at ±20. Combined forecasts merge multiple rule outputs via weighted average and apply a diversification multiplier. Volatility targeting sets overall portfolio risk as a percentage of trading capital. Position sizing converts forecasts and risk targets into actual trade sizes using volatility scalars. Portfolios combine multiple trading subsystems with instrument weights and a second diversification multiplier. Speed and size constraints ensure costs never consume too much of expected returns and minimum position sizes remain practical.

The seven-component modular framework. Forecasts and volatility targets flow into position sizing, then portfolio construction adds instrument weights.

The beauty of modularity is diagnosability. If your returns disappoint, you can isolate whether the problem lies in the trading rules, the forecast combination, the volatility estimate, the portfolio weights, or the cost management — rather than staring at a black box wondering what went wrong. Transparency breeds trust, and trust is what keeps you from meddling during the inevitable drawdowns that every system experiences.

The framework adapts to all three trader types. The asset allocating investor uses a constant forecast of +10 and lets the framework handle everything else. The semi-automatic trader plugs in discretionary forecasts. The staunch systems trader feeds in outputs from multiple automated rules. The chassis stays the same; only the engine changes. This universality means you can validate the framework components independently of any particular trading strategy.

The interface between rules and framework is the forecast: a single number scaled so that its expected absolute value is 10, capped at ±20. Positive means buy, negative means sell. The magnitude indicates conviction. A +10 is an average bullish signal; +20 is maximum conviction; -5 is mildly bearish. Because forecasts are volatility-standardized, a +10 on German bonds carries the same expected risk as a +10 on crude oil, which is what allows the position-sizing module to treat all instruments identically.

Chapter 6: Instruments

Before you can trade an instrument, it must meet three minimum requirements: available price data (at minimum daily), manageable minimum trade sizes, and your genuine understanding of what drives its price. "More buyers than sellers" is not an acceptable answer, Carver warns. You need to know the fundamental forces — interest rate differentials for FX, earnings growth for equities, inflation expectations for bonds — well enough to evaluate whether your trading rules make sense in context.

Minimum trade sizes create hard constraints that many traders underestimate. The Japanese government bond future is worth over 150 million yen (roughly $1 million) per contract. Berkshire Hathaway A-shares trade above $100,000 each. These instruments reduce your portfolio to binary positions — all or nothing — which defeats the purpose of a framework built around continuously scaled forecasts. In the case of JGBs, for most individual traders the answer is "always nothing."

"More buyers than sellers is not an answer!" — Robert Carver, on understanding what drives an instrument's price

Instruments with extremely low volatility should be excluded entirely, for four distinct reasons. First, when suppressed risk returns to normal it does so violently, creating outsized losses. Second, you need excessive leverage to reach meaningful position sizes, amplifying tail risk. Third, low-vol instruments consume portfolio risk budget that could be better spent on properly volatile assets. Fourth, they tend to be expensive to trade relative to their risk, violating the speed limit rule.

The Swiss franc peg removal illustrates all four dangers simultaneously. Before January 2015, the Swiss National Bank held EUR/CHF at a floor of 1.20. Carver explicitly avoided this instrument because the price was controlled by a central bank rather than normal market forces, making historical data irrelevant. When the peg broke, the franc appreciated over 16% in a single session. OTC brokers stopped accepting orders, re-marked fills retroactively, and several went into administration. The futures exchange operated normally throughout. This episode alone justifies Carver's strong preference for exchange-traded instruments over OTC products.

Choose instruments to maximize diversification at acceptable cost. The ideal portfolio contains one instrument from each major asset class (bonds, equities, FX, commodities, volatility) with low correlations between them. Cheaper instruments can be traded faster and still stay within the speed limit. More liquid instruments have tighter spreads. But even expensive instruments can work if traded slowly enough — individual equities via spread bets at holding periods of months rather than days.

Exchange traded funds provide access to asset classes that would otherwise require leverage. They cost more to trade than futures but offer tax advantages in some jurisdictions and require no margin account. Spread bets offer tax-free returns under UK law (classified as gambling) but are OTC products with wider spreads. Futures are the cheapest and most transparent, but their large minimum sizes require significant capital. Active managed funds should be avoided — higher fees, no demonstrated persistent alpha, and you lose control over the systematic framework.

Carver's own instrument selection process prioritizes breadth over depth. Rather than trading ten correlated equity index futures, he trades one from each sector: a bond future, a short-term interest rate future, an equity index, a volatility future, an FX cross, and a commodity. With $250,000 in capital, this is about the maximum number of futures that can be traded without running into minimum position size problems. Larger accounts can add more instruments from each category, but the first one in each asset class provides the biggest diversification benefit.

Chapter 7: Forecasts

Early in his career, Carver asked a more experienced trader named Sergei how to size a position on German bond futures. Sergei pointed to his heart: "How much do you like this trade?" Then to his gut: "How much can you afford to lose?" Then tapped his forehead: "How risky is it?" It was cryptic advice, but entirely correct. Position sizing requires three inputs: your conviction about the trade (the forecast), your risk tolerance (the volatility target), and the instrument's risk profile (price volatility). The forecast captures the first element.

"You need to know three things. First, how much do you like this trade? That comes from here," Sergei replied, pointing to his heart. — Sergei

A good forecast must be scaled, not binary. A binary system that flips between fully long and fully short forces you to execute a complete reversal trade every time the signal changes — enormously expensive. A scaled forecast of +5 (weak buy) produces a smaller position than +15 (strong buy), which means less turnover and lower costs. The forecast must also be proportional to risk-adjusted expected returns using volatility standardization. If German Bund futures have an expected return of 2% with 8% volatility (ratio 0.25) and the German Schatz has 1% expected return with 2% volatility (ratio 0.50), the Schatz forecast should be twice the Bund's, because the risk-adjusted opportunity is twice as attractive.

The scaling convention throughout the framework is an expected average absolute value of 10, with forecasts capped at ±20. A forecast of +10 means an average-strength buy signal. A forecast of -20 is the maximum bearish conviction the system can express. Zero means neutral. This standardization is what allows a single position-sizing formula to work identically across all instruments and all trading rules. The forecast scalar (specific to each rule) is calculated by dividing 10 by the average absolute forecast value observed in back-testing.

All forecasts are scaled to an expected absolute value of 10 and capped at ±20. This allows one formula to size positions across all instruments.

Capping at ±20 serves five distinct purposes. First, risk control: without a cap, one rule variation could produce enormous forecasts that dominate the entire portfolio. Second, limited data: absolute forecasts above 20 appear only about 5% of the time in back-tests, so there is insufficient evidence that they translate into proportionally larger returns. Third, extremes behave differently: after a very sharp market fall, trend-following rules produce massive negative forecasts, but "dead cat bounces" are common and dividend yields above 50% usually signal imminent bankruptcy rather than a bargain.

Fourth, higher realized volatility: very large forecasts often coincide with periods of abnormally low measured volatility that is about to spike. Japanese government bonds in January 2013 had extremely strong positive EWMAC forecasts because volatility was near zero. When Prime Minister Shinzo Abe unveiled his economic reforms, volatility spiked dramatically and prices dropped. Capping the forecast at 20 contained the resulting position size. Fifth, the cost is tiny: only about 5% of forecasts are affected, so the reduction in expected returns from capping is minimal while the risk control is significant.

For semi-automatic traders, qualitative opinions translate directly into the same scale. "Very strong sell" maps to -20, "Sell" to -10, "Weak buy" to +5, "Very strong buy" to +20. A critical discipline: never change your forecast once a bet is open. The temptation to upgrade a losing position or take profits on a winner is exactly the emotional sabotage the framework exists to prevent. Instead, use a systematic trailing stop loss that triggers when the price moves a set number of volatility units from its best level since entry.

The asset allocating investor uses the simplest possible forecast: a constant +10 for all instruments, all the time. This "no-rule rule" says markets will continue delivering their historical risk premia. No prediction of price direction, no signal processing, no data analysis. The entire intelligence of the system lives in the portfolio construction and volatility management modules. Yet this approach captures the bulk of available returns for a patient, diversified investor who cannot or will not use leverage.

Chapter 8: Combined Forecasts

When you run multiple trading rules on the same instrument, their forecasts will often disagree. EWMAC might signal a strong uptrend while carry says the instrument is expensive relative to its yield. The combined forecast is a weighted average that nets out these conflicting views. The forecast weights are positive numbers that sum to 100%, determined through handcrafting or bootstrapping using the same methods as portfolio allocation weights.

Carver's example makes this concrete. Consider crude oil with three EWMAC variations (fast, medium, slow) plus a carry rule. The handcrafted weights follow the same grouping logic as portfolio weights: the three EWMAC variations form one group (50% total), carry forms another (50% total). Within the EWMAC group, the middle-speed variation gets only 8% because it is highly correlated with both neighbors (adjacent EWMAC lookbacks have correlation of 0.90), while the fast and slow variations each get 21%. The final weights: carry 50%, EWMAC-fast 21%, EWMAC-medium 8%, EWMAC-slow 21%.

If the EWMAC composite signals +15 and carry signals -10, the 50/50 raw combined forecast is +2.5 — a very weak bullish view reflecting genuine disagreement between momentum and fundamental signals. This net conviction then drives position sizing. The beauty is that conflicting rules naturally reduce position sizes, which is exactly what you want when the evidence is mixed.

Combining imperfectly correlated forecasts reduces the scale of the output. Two forecasts each with average absolute value 10 will produce a combined forecast with average absolute value below 10, because they partially cancel each other. The forecast diversification multiplier restores the combined forecast to the expected scale of 10. With perfectly correlated forecasts the multiplier is 1.0 — combining adds nothing. At zero correlation, the multiplier is 1.41. With ten uncorrelated forecasts it reaches 3.2. At fifty uncorrelated forecasts: 7.1. But Carver caps the multiplier at 2.5 to prevent combined forecasts from becoming effectively binary — permanently near +20 or -20 — which would eliminate the cost advantage of scaled positioning.

Floor all negative correlations at zero when computing the multiplier. Negative correlations between your own trading rules most likely indicate over-fitting rather than genuine diversification benefit. If rule A and rule B on the same instrument are negatively correlated, it usually means one of them is fitting to noise in a way that happens to oppose the other.

The complete five-step process: generate individual forecasts (each with expected absolute value ~10, capped at ±20), apply forecast weights (positive, summing to 100%), compute the weighted average, multiply by the forecast diversification multiplier (at least 1.0, capped at 2.5), and cap the rescaled result at ±20. This same sequence runs for every instrument in the portfolio, producing one combined forecast per instrument that feeds into the position-sizing module.

The interaction between forecast weights and trading costs matters. Faster rules produce more turnover and therefore higher costs. Unless faster rules deliver enough additional pre-cost Sharpe ratio to compensate — and Carver has never found consistent evidence that they do — you should tilt forecast weights toward slower rules. In practice, the speed-limit adjustments are small, never larger than a factor of 0.95 or 1.05, because the cost differences between adjacent-speed variations are modest.

For the asset allocating investor running a single constant forecast of +10, this entire chapter is irrelevant — there is nothing to combine. For the semi-automatic trader who makes one discretionary forecast per instrument, there is similarly nothing to combine. Only the staunch systems trader running multiple automated rules needs forecast combination, and even then Carver recommends keeping the number of rules small enough that the handcrafting method handles the weights easily.

Chapter 9: Volatility Targeting

This is the most important decision in the design of any trading system. Nearly all amateur traders lose money not because their rules are bad, but because their positions are too large. Painful losses are the primary reason traders start meddling with their systems, and once meddling begins, the systematic advantage vanishes. The percentage volatility target is the annualized expected standard deviation of your total portfolio returns, expressed as a fraction of trading capital. Multiply by capital to get the cash volatility target.

The numbers make the consequences vivid. With $100,000 capital and a 200% volatility target, the worst daily loss you would expect in a typical month is $20,000. Your worst weekly loss in a typical year would be about $55,000. Over a decade, you could lose $80,000 in a single month. Even at 50% volatility, the worst monthly day costs $5,000 and the worst yearly week costs $14,000. After ten years, there is still a 10% chance your cumulative losses will exceed $62,000 at the higher target.

Negative skew makes losses worse in a subtle way. With skew of -2 at a 50% volatility target, individual bad days are nastier ($6,100 versus $5,000 for the worst monthly loss) but cumulative losses are actually smaller because the frequent small gains of negative-skew strategies partially compensate. The danger is that the frequent gains lull you into complacency until the truly catastrophic event arrives.

The Kelly criterion, invented by physicist John Kelly in 1956, provides the theoretical framework for optimal bet sizing. Kelly says set your volatility target equal to your expected Sharpe ratio — it maximizes the geometric mean of compound returns over time. But a simple example shows why full Kelly is dangerous in practice. Imagine you expect an average return of 100% per trade. If you bet everything and lose 90% on the first trade, then gain 190% on the second, you end up with only 29% of your starting capital despite averaging 100% per trade. Compound returns punish volatility. The optimal bet is only a quarter of your capital.

Carver's derivation of his 37% volatility target: back-tested SR of 1.0, discounted by 25% for over-fitting, then halved for safety.

"My experience has been that most cautious investors who use Kelly find the frequency of substantial bankroll reductions to be uncomfortably large." — Ed Thorpe

Carver's own system (back-tested SR roughly 1.0 across 45 instruments and 8 rules) runs at a 37% volatility target. The derivation: start with back-tested SR 1.0, apply a 0.75 pessimism factor (Table 14, out-of-sample bootstrapping), then halve for Half-Kelly: 1.0 × 0.75 × 0.50 = 0.375, or 37%. William Poundstone's Fortune's Formula provides an excellent readable history of the Kelly criterion for those wanting to go deeper.

Recommended targets vary by trader type and realistic Sharpe ratio. Staunch systems traders with SR 0.50 should target 25% volatility; at SR 0.75, 37%. Semi-automatic traders should never exceed 25% because they cannot back-test their discretionary forecasts. Asset allocating investors are usually constrained by lack of leverage to single-digit percentages — a diversified ETF portfolio with SR 0.40 would ideally target 20%, but without borrowing you might only achieve 7-8%. For any strategy with significant negative skew, halve the targets: SR 0.50 becomes 12% rather than 25%.

Never change your percentage volatility target upward. The temptation to increase risk during good times is pure meddling — you are most likely to raise your target just before a drawdown, because good times breed overconfidence. The one permitted exception is a gross miscalculation of risk tolerance after your first major loss, and even then the change should only be downward and one-time. Better to start with less trading capital and gradually add more, so the percentage target stays fixed while your absolute risk exposure grows naturally. Roll up profits into capital (Kelly instructs this for compound growth) and mechanically reduce after losses. Check daily if using leverage, at least weekly otherwise.

Chapter 10: Position Sizing

Position sizing connects the abstract world of forecasts and risk targets to the concrete reality of how many contracts, shares, or pounds-per-point to actually trade. The chain starts with the instrument block, the minimum tradeable unit: one share of Apple, one futures contract of crude oil, one pound per point on a spread bet. The block value is how much cash a 1% price move produces per block. For Apple at $400 per share, the block value is $4. For WTI crude oil futures at $75 per barrel with 1,000 barrels per contract, it is $750. For a FTSE 100 spread bet at £10 per point with the index at 6,500, a 1% move of 65 points produces a block value of £650.

Price volatility is the daily standard deviation of percentage returns, measured using an exponentially weighted moving average with a default 36-day lookback (equivalent to a 25-day simple moving average). The smoothing parameter A equals 2 / (1 + L) where L is the lookback — for L=36, A is roughly 0.054. Each day's squared return is blended with the previous variance estimate to produce a new variance, and you take the square root. This is the same approach used by the RiskMetrics system that became the industry standard for risk management.

Multiply price volatility by block value to get instrument currency volatility — the daily cash risk of holding one block. For crude oil with block value $750 and price volatility 1.333%, that is $997.50 per contract per day. If you trade in a different currency from the instrument, convert using the exchange rate to get instrument value volatility. A UK investor trading crude oil converts: $997.50 × 0.67 USD/GBP = £668.

The volatility scalar is the key number: how many blocks you would hold at a constant average forecast of +10. It equals the daily cash volatility target divided by instrument value volatility. With a £1,000,000 annual target, the daily target is £62,500 (dividing by 16). The volatility scalar for crude oil: £62,500 / £668 = 93.52 contracts. This is your full position size when the forecast is at its average strength.

The subsystem position adjusts for the actual forecast: volatility scalar times forecast divided by 10. At forecast +10 (average buy signal), you hold the full 93.52 contracts. At forecast -6, you short 56.11 contracts. At forecast -20 (maximum sell), you sell 187.04 blocks. At forecast +5 (weak buy), only 46.76. Asset allocating investors with their constant +10 forecast always hold exactly the volatility scalar — their position changes only when price volatility or exchange rates move.

Complete position sizing chain for WTI crude oil futures with a forecast of -6 (moderately bearish).

Three methods exist for measuring recent price volatility. You can eyeball the chart — perfectly adequate for semi-automatic traders who watch markets daily and only need an approximate number. You can calculate a precise standard deviation over a simple moving window, though short windows (10 days) are noisy and long windows (100 days) are sluggish. Or you can use the EWMA approach with its 25-day equivalent lookback — Carver's preferred default. Research shows almost no performance difference for lookback periods between six months and a year, so the choice is not critical.

A danger lurks in calm markets. When measured volatility drops to abnormally low levels, position sizes balloon because the volatility scalar grows inversely. Credit default swaps in early 2007 had near-zero measured volatility, inviting enormous positions. Zero-interest-rate Eurodollar futures showed minimal price movement. The Swiss franc before the peg broke was stable to the point of appearing risk-free. In every case, the calm was a prelude to explosive volatility that destroyed overleveraged positions. The forecast cap at ±20 provides some protection, but the real safeguard is excluding instruments whose natural volatility is so low that reaching your risk target requires extreme leverage.

The Eurodollar future illustrates a practical complication. Each contract represents $1 million of 3-month deposits, but the block value depends on the relationship between price changes and interest rate changes. A 1% price rise corresponds to roughly $2,450 in profit per contract. Because short-term interest rate futures have very low price volatility (often under 0.1% daily), you need dozens or hundreds of contracts to reach meaningful position sizes — which is fine with $250,000 capital and futures, but impossible with $20,000 and individual shares.

Chapter 11: Portfolios

Diversification is the only free lunch in investing. Allocating across uncorrelated asset classes can double your expected Sharpe ratio without any increase in risk. Yet individual investors typically hold fewer than five stocks and nothing else. The portfolio module combines multiple trading subsystems — one per instrument — using instrument weights determined by handcrafting or bootstrapping.

A critical subtlety: you need correlations between trading subsystem returns, not raw instrument price returns. When you run dynamic trading rules on two instruments, the correlation of your strategy returns is lower than the correlation of the underlying prices because the rules modify the exposure. A good approximation is that subsystem return correlations are roughly 0.70 times instrument return correlations. For static asset allocators using constant forecasts, use instrument return correlations directly without the 0.70 adjustment.

The instrument diversification multiplier scales all positions upward to compensate for the fact that a diversified portfolio's volatility is lower than the sum of its parts. Without this adjustment, your actual portfolio volatility would consistently fall below your target. The formula is 1 divided by the square root of the weighted correlation matrix (W × H × W'). For three assets with weights 50%/25%/25% and average correlation of 0.25, the multiplier is 1.41.

In crises like 2008, correlations spike toward 1.0 as all risky assets fall together. The diversification benefit that the multiplier assumes vanishes precisely when you need it most, leaving you over-leveraged at the worst possible moment. This is why Carver caps the multiplier at 2.5 regardless of what the correlation math suggests. For semi-automatic traders, the multiplier is simply the maximum number of concurrent bets divided by the average expected number, capped at 2.5.

A complete three-asset example ties everything together. With US 20-year bonds, S&P 500, and NASDAQ, instrument weights of 50%/25%/25%, and a diversification multiplier of 1.41, the subsystem positions of 9.11 (bonds, forecast +10), -7.43 (S&P, forecast -10), and -13.9 (NASDAQ, forecast -15) become portfolio positions of 6.42, -2.62, and -4.91 respectively. After rounding: buy 6 bond contracts, sell 3 S&P, sell 5 NASDAQ.

Generating trades from target positions requires comparing the rounded target to the current position. If you hold 4 bond contracts and the target is 6, buy 2. If S&P is currently -2 and the target rounds to -3, sell 1 more. If NASDAQ is already at -5 and the target rounds to -5, do nothing. Each trade incurs costs, so unnecessary trading must be avoided.

Position inertia provides the solution. Do not trade unless the target position differs from the current position by more than 10%. Consider Italian government bond futures where the desired unrounded position is 133.48. That rounds to 133. If the target drifts to 133.52, it rounds to 134 — but the 0.04 contract difference does not justify paying the spread twice. With 10% inertia (a buffer of 13.3 contracts), you only trade when the target reaches 147 or drops below 120. This dramatically reduces trading costs with negligible impact on pre-cost performance for any rule with a holding period longer than a few days.

Don't adjust instrument weights for different Sharpe ratios between subsystems. The diversification benefit of equal-risk allocation is well-established, and the SR differences between subsystems are rarely statistically significant. The correlation-based weighting already captures the most important structural differences between instruments.

Chapter 12: Speed and Size

Trading costs are the one thing you can measure with near-certainty, unlike returns. The standardized cost measures the round-trip cost of trading one block as a fraction of annualized volatility, expressed in Sharpe ratio units: (2 × C) / (16 × ICV), where C is the cost per block and ICV is the daily instrument currency volatility. This formula reveals how much of your raw Sharpe ratio gets eaten by each round trip.

Consider Euro Stoxx futures. Price volatility is roughly 1.5% per day. With a block value of €337 per 1% move, daily instrument currency volatility is €506. The annualized figure: €506 × 16 = €8,096. The cost to trade one block: €5 execution (half the 1-point spread) plus €3 brokerage = €8 total. Standardized cost: (2 × €8) / €8,096 = 0.002 SR units per round trip. Extremely cheap.

Now compare a FTSE 100 spread bet at £1 per point. Price volatility is 0.75% per day, FTSE at 6,600, so a 1% move is 66 points = £66 block value. Daily ICV: 0.75% × £66 = £49.50. Cost: half the typical 4-point spread = £4 (no fees or taxes on spread bets). Standardized cost: (2 × £4) / (16 × £49.50) = 0.01 SR. That is roughly ten times more expensive than the equivalent FTSE 100 future — unsurprising for a retail product, but critical for determining how fast you can trade.

Trading costs vary by an order of magnitude. Spread bets cost ~10x more than equivalent futures per round trip.

"I wouldn't trade a strategy which paid two-thirds of its profits out as costs. I personally think it's foolhardy to pay more than a third of your expected profits in trading costs." — Robert Carver

The speed limit rule: never pay more than one-third of your expected Sharpe ratio in annual trading costs. For staunch systems traders expecting 0.40 SR per instrument, the maximum annual cost is 0.13 SR. For semi-automatic and asset allocating investors expecting 0.25 SR, the limit is 0.08 SR. Turnover — the number of round trips per year — multiplied by standardized cost gives annual cost. The speed limit tells you exactly how fast you can trade each instrument: maximum turnover = speed limit / standardized cost per instrument.

Day trading requires about 500 round trips per year, meaning standardized cost must be roughly 0.00025 SR — essentially zero. You would need to consistently hit mid-price or better on execution, a capability that requires co-located servers and professional market-making infrastructure. This is why the vast majority of amateur day traders are unprofitable: even tiny per-trade costs multiply into devastating annual drags at high frequency.

Sources of turnover, in order of importance: forecast changes (the main driver), instrument currency volatility changes, trading capital changes, exchange rate movements, and system parameter changes (minimal). For the asset allocating investor running a constant forecast, the only turnover comes from volatility estimation. Using the default 25-day lookback produces a turnover of 1.6 round trips per year, which at 0.08 SR per trip yields 0.13 SR in annual costs — already violating the 0.08 limit. Switching to a 20-week lookback drops turnover to 0.40, bringing costs to a comfortable 0.032 SR.

Small accounts face the opposite problem from speed: minimum position sizes create lumpiness. With €20,000 capital, your S&P 500 futures position is always either 0 or 1 contract regardless of what the forecast says. Carver recommends a maximum possible position of at least four instrument blocks. If your capital cannot support this, either reduce the number of instruments (concentrating risk but improving granularity) or drop the problematic instrument entirely. Berkshire Hathaway A-shares at $220,000 per share and Japanese 10-year bond futures at $1.3 million per contract are simply untradeable for most individual investors.

Build the most diversified portfolio possible without running into minimum-size problems. At least one instrument from each major asset class. Carver himself has about six of his 40+ futures instruments where the maximum position is below four contracts — he accepts the lumpiness rather than sacrifice the diversification benefit of including them.

Chapter 13: Semi-automatic Trader

The semi-automatic trader makes discretionary forecasts but wraps them in the systematic framework for stop losses, position sizing, and risk management. The example uses £100,000 trading capital, spread bets on equity indices and FX, with sporadic trades as opportunities arise. This is the part-time trader's approach — someone with a day job who spends a few minutes each morning checking positions and occasionally entering new bets.

Forecasts translate from qualitative opinions to the standard numeric scale: "Very strong sell" = -20, "Strong sell" = -15, "Sell" = -10, "Weak sell" = -5, "Neutral" = 0, "Weak buy" = +5, "Buy" = +10, "Strong buy" = +15, "Very strong buy" = +20. This translation is the bridge between human judgment and systematic position sizing. A "Buy" opinion produces a position exactly as large as the framework says an average positive signal deserves, given current volatility and risk targets.

The cardinal rule: never change your forecast once a bet is open. The temptation to downgrade a losing position (cutting your view to cut your loss) or upgrade a winner (increasing conviction because you feel validated) is precisely the emotional sabotage the framework prevents. Instead, a systematic trailing stop loss handles exits. The stop triggers when the price moves X daily standard deviations from its best level since entry (highest for longs, lowest for shorts).

With X=4, the average holding period is about 6.5 weeks and turnover is 8 round trips per year. This happens to sit exactly at the 0.01 SR speed limit for spread bets costing 0.01 SR each. The match is not coincidental — Carver designed the recommended X value to maximize holding period while staying within the cost constraint. Faster stops (X=1, holding period 4 days) require instruments five times cheaper. Slower stops (X=10, holding period 26 weeks) work with expensive ETFs but miss shorter trends. No profit targets — Carver's research shows they consistently underperform because most markets trend, and taking profits early means exiting trends prematurely.

"This is because most markets exhibit trending behaviour, and a profit target will get you out of trends too early." — Robert Carver

Volatility targeting: with a conservative post-cost SR estimate of 0.30, the recommended target is 15%. Annual cash volatility target: £100,000 × 15% = £15,000. Daily: £15,000 / 16 = £937.50. Expected annual cost drag: 0.08 × 15% = 1.2% of capital per year — cheaper than a hedge fund, more than passive ETFs, and a fair price for the expected alpha from discretionary forecasting.

Portfolio management: maximum four positions, average three expected. Instrument weight = 100% / 4 = 25%. Diversification multiplier = 4 / 3 = 1.33. Capital at risk per individual bet works out to about 1.25% of total capital, assuming X=4, forecast of 10, 15% volatility target, and three concurrent bets. You can pyramid — add a new bet on top of an existing position — but each counts toward the maximum of four. Total forecast on any single instrument must never exceed 40 units across all concurrent bets.

The daily process takes minutes. Check stop losses on all existing positions and trade immediately if any have triggered. Adjust stop levels: move up for new highs (longs), down for new lows (shorts), and recalculate if price volatility has changed. Look at potential new entries and assign a numeric forecast. Calculate position sizes for any new trades. Part-time traders can then update stop-loss limits with their broker and leave for the day; full-time traders continue monitoring for additional entries and stop triggers.

Block values for spread bets work differently from futures because the bet size is in pounds per point rather than contracts. For crude oil at $65 with a minimum bet of £10 per point, a 1% move is 0.65 points = £6.50 block value. You won't use exchange rates even on non-UK markets because the spread bet is already denominated in pounds. This simplification is one reason spread bets appeal to UK-based retail traders despite their higher costs.

Chapter 14: Asset Allocating Investor

The asset allocating investor does not forecast prices at all. Using a constant forecast of +10 for every instrument, every day, the entire intelligence of the system lives in portfolio construction and volatility-adjusted position sizing. The example uses €10,000,000 across ten ETFs spanning developed-market equities, emerging-market equities, developed-market bonds, and inflation-linked bonds. This approach is suitable for pension funds, trusts, or individuals with significant capital who want systematic risk management without active trading.

Without leverage, the volatility target is constrained by the lowest-volatility instrument in the portfolio. Start with an initial guess equal to that instrument's annualized price volatility (6.88% for inflation-linked bonds in the example). Calculate all positions, sum their total market value, and compute the actual leverage implied. If the desired leverage ceiling is 90% (leaving a 10% cash buffer), adjust: achievable target = initial guess × (desired leverage / actual leverage). The result: 6.88% × (90% / 82.6%) = 7.5% annualized volatility target. Expected return: 0.4 × 7.5% = 3.0% per year above the risk-free rate.

Costs are remarkably low. The 20-week lookback for price volatility estimation produces just 0.4 round trips per year. With the most expensive ETF costing 0.08 SR per round trip, annual cost is 0.4 × 0.08 = 0.032 SR = 0.24% of capital. ETF holding costs range from 0.05% (cheap S&P 500 tracker) to 0.55% (emerging-market equities). Total annual drag is well under 1% — far cheaper than any active fund.

Portfolio construction uses handcrafting with ten instruments across four asset classes. Constraints prevent over-concentration: maximum 40% in bonds or equities, maximum 30% in emerging-market equities, maximum 25% in inflation-linked bonds. Within regions, equal weight per country. Within asset classes, the split is roughly 25% EM bonds, 25% inflation-linked, 50% developed for bonds, and 30% EM, 70% developed for equities. Top level: 40% bonds, 60% equities. The instrument diversification multiplier works out to 1.61.

Performance predictions can be incorporated by translating views into annualized Sharpe ratios and adjusting instrument weights. If a strategist predicts Euro Stoxx will hit 3,000 in 12 months (an 8% rise) and annualized volatility is 16%, the implied SR is 8% / 16% = 0.50. Compare this to the portfolio average SR of 0.20, compute the outperformance of 0.30, and look up the adjustment multiplier in Table 12 Column B ("without certainty"): increase Euro Stoxx weight by roughly 17%. Renormalize all weights to 100%. These adjustments should be small and infrequent because each one generates additional trading costs.

The 2008 crash trading diary brings the framework to life across three snapshot dates. On 1 July 2008, with €10,000,000 in capital and a daily volatility target of €46,875, the portfolio is 57% bonds by cash value despite only 40% bond weight — a natural consequence of risk-parity sizing. Bonds have lower volatility, so the framework buys more of them to equalize risk contribution.

On 1 October 2008, two weeks after Lehman's bankruptcy, capital is nearly unchanged at €9,705,000. Economic strategists are bullish: they assign equities an expected SR of 0.6 and bonds SR of 0.0. The weight adjustments are modest — equities increase from 60% to about 70%, bonds decrease. But the framework prevents the kind of massive all-in equity bet that human conviction would demand, because the uncertainty-adjusted multipliers from Table 12 are deliberately conservative.

On 10 October 2008, equity markets have fallen off a cliff. Carver comes into the office on a Saturday to assess the damage. Capital is down to €9,126,500. Equity volatility has exploded. The strategists have belatedly reversed course — now underweight equities, overweight bonds. The system generates major sell orders across all equity positions (74 to 268 blocks depending on the instrument). The framework mechanically sells equities and buys bonds, limiting losses while most human investors are paralyzed with fear. The October tilt toward equities cost some money, but far less than a purely emotional response would have.

Chapter 15: Staunch Systems Trader

The staunch systems trader uses both the modular framework and multiple automated trading rules. This is the full system: rules produce forecasts, forecasts combine, positions size automatically, portfolios construct themselves. Carver's example uses $250,000 initial capital, six futures contracts spanning all major asset classes, and four trading rule variations. Even part-time traders can run this system using free daily price data and a spreadsheet.

Instrument selection balances diversification against minimum block-size constraints. The recommended set: Eurodollar (short- term interest rates), US 5-year Treasury note (bonds), Euro Stoxx (equity index), V2TX (European equity volatility), MXP/USD (FX, Mexican peso), and Corn (agricultural commodity). One future from each major asset class. All have standardized costs below 0.01 SR. V2TX has markedly negative skew (selling volatility is dangerous) so it is limited to about 10% of the portfolio. Eurodollar should be traded roughly three years out because nearest-month contracts have near-zero volatility when interest rates are low.

Two trading rules drive the system: EWMAC (exponentially weighted moving average crossover, a trend-following rule) and carry (profiting from yield differentials when prices don't move). EWMAC uses pairs of fast and slow moving averages at a fixed 4:1 ratio: 16/64, 32/128, and 64/256 lookback periods. Faster variations (2/8, 4/16) require cheaper instruments and generate more turnover. Only the cheapest futures can profitably use the fastest EWMAC speeds. The carry rule divides the net expected return by annualized standard deviation and multiplies by a forecast scalar of 30.

Forecast weights are handcrafted: carry gets 50%, the three EWMAC variations split the other 50% (21% fast, 8% medium, 21% slow). The medium variation gets only 8% because it is highly correlated with both neighbors at 0.90, so it adds minimal diversification. All instruments use the same weight set. The forecast diversification multiplier is 1.31, reflecting the partial correlation between the four rule variations.

The back-tested Sharpe ratio after costs is 0.53. Applying a 75% pessimism factor gives a realistic expectation of 0.40. With roughly zero aggregate skew (V2TX's negative skew is offset by the positive skew of EWMAC trend following on benign assets), Table 25 maps SR 0.40 to a 20% volatility target. Annualized cash volatility target: $250,000 × 20% = $50,000.

Price volatility uses the default 25-day EWMA lookback. The weighted average turnover across all rules is about 9.57 round trips per year. With the forecast diversification multiplier of 1.31, the combined forecast turnover rises to 12.5. The most expensive instrument (V2TX at 0.009 SR per round trip) incurs total annual costs of 12.5 × 0.009 = 0.113 SR — safely within the 0.13 SR speed limit for staunch systems traders. Slowing down the volatility lookback would save only 0.008 SR annually, not worth the loss of responsiveness.

Instrument weights are handcrafted with practical constraints. Within interest rates: 50% T-note, 50% Eurodollar. Within equities: 66.6% Euro Stoxx, 33.3% V2TX (constrained by the need for Euro Stoxx to have a minimum weight of 20% to support four- contract positions). FX: 100% MXP/USD. Commodities: 100% Corn. Across groups: at least 30% equities (for Euro Stoxx sizing), remaining 70% split equally among rates, FX, and commodities at 23.3% each. Final weights: Eurodollar 11.7%, T-note 11.7%, Euro Stoxx 20%, V2TX 9.8%, MXP/USD 23.3%, Corn 23.3%. The instrument diversification multiplier is 1.89.

The October-December 2014 trading diary shows the system in live operation. On 15 October 2014, the daily volatility target is $3,125 per instrument. The system holds a large long position in US Treasury notes (strong momentum rally plus a carry forecast above 19). V2TX has a short carry forecast of -20, though implied volatility has spiked over the previous two weeks. The slowest EWMAC rules are still short V2TX after a steady decline in implied vol through most of 2014.

By 1 December 2014, cumulative profits of roughly $10,000 have grown trading capital to $260,000. The annualized cash volatility target increases to $52,000. A pronounced equity rally has the system modestly long Euro Stoxx. V2TX is falling in price but the carry forecast has weakened. Trading occurs almost daily between snapshots — a natural consequence of four rule variations generating different signals across six instruments. The Kelly-inspired roll-up mechanism ensures winning periods translate into larger positions, compounding returns over time.

Epilogue: What Makes a Good Systematic Trader?

Carver closes with seven qualities that define success in systematic trading. Be humble: underestimate your intelligence, skill, and luck. Don't try anything too clever. The traders who blow up are almost always the ones who believe they are smarter than the market.

Keep your trading rules simple. Don't over-fit. Don't even fit if you can avoid it. Handcrafting is both simple and effective for portfolio weights. A system you can explain on the back of a napkin is a system you can trust during a drawdown.

Be sceptical. Don't trust your broker, your trainer, your favorite author — including Carver himself. Don't trust back-tests, especially in-sample ones. The future will almost certainly be worse than the past. Maximum expected Sharpe ratios: 1.0 for a highly diversified staunch system, 0.5 for semi-automatic, 0.4 for asset allocators. If you are making steady gains with few losses, there is a good chance you are running a negative-skew strategy that will eventually blow up.

Be thoughtful: understand why you might be making money and why you might not. Understanding the sources of your returns — risk premia, behavioral effects, alternative beta — is what lets you stay invested when those returns temporarily disappear.

Be thrifty: know your trading costs precisely and stick to the speed limit. Never set turnover so high that you spend more than one-third of your pessimistic expected Sharpe ratio on trading costs per subsystem. Don't make your broker or market makers wealthy at your expense.

Be nervous: only commit trading capital you can afford to lose. Use Half-Kelly. Trade the most diversified set of instruments possible. Stay away from low-volatility instruments. At some point, some instruments or trading rules will go badly wrong — diversification limits the damage.

Be diligent when creating systems but lazy when running them. The hard work goes into designing a safe system you are comfortable with. Then you leave it alone. The temptation to tinker, improve, or actively risk- manage a systematic strategy is the most time- consuming activity in trading, and it almost always destroys rather than creates value.

"So it only remains for me to say: Good Luck!" — Robert Carver

Key Takeaways

Emotions are the enemy, not ignorance

Humans are better analysts than any computer but worse executors. Systematize decisions to remove emotional interference. The Barclays trade (right analysis, wrong execution) and the Lehman billion-dollar day (computer beats terrified humans) prove that knowing what to do and doing it are entirely different skills.

Over-fitting is the cardinal sin

You need 20-40 years of data to determine whether a single rule is profitable. Test few rules, pool data across instruments, never use in-sample results, and always apply pessimism factors to back-tests. Joe's 50 profitable rules at Aqueduct Capital led straight to liquidation.

Diversification beats optimization

Adding instruments matters far more than adding rules. Handcrafted portfolio weights beat unstable Markowitz optimization. Equal weights are remarkably hard to beat unless correlations meaningfully differ between assets.

The forecast framework scales everything

Forecasts with expected absolute value 10, capped at ±20, standardized by volatility. The same framework serves discretionary traders (opinions become numbers), passive investors (constant +10), and full system traders (EWMAC and carry generate signals).

Half-Kelly for risk sizing

Set your volatility target at half the pessimistic estimate of your Sharpe ratio. Full Kelly maximizes compound growth mathematically but produces unbearable drawdowns in practice. Even Ed Thorpe — who invented card counting — warns that Kelly reductions are "uncomfortably large."

Never break the speed limit

Pay no more than one-third of expected Sharpe ratio in trading costs. Euro Stoxx futures at 0.002 SR per trip allow fast trading; spread bets at 0.01 SR per trip force holding periods of six weeks or more. Day trading is mathematically impossible for retail traders.

Position inertia saves money for free

Do not trade unless the target position differs from current by more than 10%. The Italian bond example shows how a 0.04-contract change does not justify two lots of commission. Inertia cuts costs dramatically with zero impact on pre-cost performance.

Understand your skew

Positive skew (trend following, buying options) means frequent small losses and occasional large gains. Negative skew (carry, relative value, selling options) means steady income punctuated by blowups. If your Sharpe ratio looks too good, you probably have hidden negative skew.

Contrarian Ideas

Don't fit your trading rules at all

Most practitioners obsess over calibrating rules to maximize back-test performance. Carver argues you should select rules based on behavior (speed, correlation) and reserve performance data exclusively for setting forecast weights. Pre-selecting profitable rules contaminates the entire back-test.

50 rules is worse than 2

The marginal value of additional rules is surprisingly low. Instrument diversification dominates rule diversification by a wide margin. Carver runs eight rules from five themes and considers this generous. If starting from scratch, just trend following and carry.

Back-tests are dangerous, not helpful

Every back-test is a form of fitting. Even the best out-of-sample methods require decades of data to produce statistically meaningful results. Treat all back-test returns with deep skepticism and apply pessimism factors ranging from 25% to 75% depending on methodology.

Sharpe ratios above 1.0 are fantasy

No systematic strategy has sustainably produced SR above 1.0 after costs. If your back-test shows 2.0+, you have over-fitted. LTCM's apparent 4.6 SR was hidden negative skew, not genuine alpha.

Optimization is more art than science

Experienced portfolio optimizers routinely override their models to get "sensible" results. Handcrafting formalizes this common-sense fiddling without the pretense of mathematical precision, and produces results statistically indistinguishable from sophisticated bootstrapping.

Never change your forecast once a bet is open

This contradicts every instinct. When a position is losing, you want to reduce it. When it is winning, you want to add. Both impulses are the emotional sabotage that the framework exists to prevent. Let the trailing stop handle exits.

Frameworks & Models

The Three Trader Types

  • Asset allocating investor: constant +10 forecast, ETFs, no leverage, 7-20% volatility target, weekly rebalancing
  • Semi-automatic trader: discretionary forecasts (-20 to +20), systematic trailing stop losses, spread bets, 15-25% target, maximum 4 positions
  • Staunch systems trader: EWMAC + carry rules, futures, 20-50% target, daily process, forecast and instrument diversification multipliers

The Modular Framework

  1. Trading rules produce scaled forecasts (avg abs value 10, capped ±20)
  2. Combined forecast = weighted average × forecast diversification multiplier (max 2.5)
  3. Volatility target set via Half-Kelly
  4. Subsystem position = volatility scalar × forecast / 10
  5. Portfolio position = subsystem × instrument weight × instrument div multiplier (max 2.5)
  6. Trade only if position inertia threshold exceeded (10%)

Handcrafting Method

  1. Group correlated assets
  2. Allocate within groups using Table 8 (correlation-based lookup)
  3. Allocate across groups
  4. Multiply weights through all levels
  5. Optionally adjust for Sharpe ratios using Table 12 (conservative multipliers)

Speed Limit Rule

  • Max annual cost = 1/3 of expected Sharpe ratio
  • Staunch traders: 0.13 SR/year max
  • Semi-automatic/asset allocators: 0.08 SR/year
  • Standardized cost = 2C / (16 × ICV)
  • Max turnover = speed limit / standardized cost per instrument

Key Trading Rules

  • EWMAC: fast minus slow exponentially weighted moving average, divided by price standard deviation, times forecast scalar. Lookback pairs at 4:1 ratio: 2/8, 4/16, 8/32, 16/64, 32/128, 64/256. Adjacent pairs correlated at 0.90.
  • Carry: net expected return (dividend yield minus funding cost, or near vs far contract price difference) divided by annualized standard deviation. Forecast scalar = 30. About 10 round trips per year with weekly updates.

Robert Carver spent nearly a decade as a portfolio manager for AHL, one of the world's largest systematic hedge funds, part of the Man Group. At AHL he was responsible for the design of trading systems and the management of multi-billion dollar portfolios of systematic strategies across global futures markets. It was there that he witnessed the billion-dollar Lehman day and developed many of the ideas in this book.

After leaving the hedge fund industry, Carver continued to manage his own money using the same systematic principles. He trades over 40 futures contracts with eight trading rules, manages UK equities as a semi-automatic trader, and holds ETFs as an asset allocating investor. He holds degrees in economics and finance from Manchester and Birkbeck, University of London, and brings a rare combination of institutional experience and retail pragmatism to his writing.

Carver has since written several follow-up books: Smart Portfolios (2017) on portfolio construction for individual investors, Leveraged Trading (2019) for smaller-account traders, and Advanced Futures Trading Strategies (2023) extending the framework to more complex rule sets. His website systematictrading.org provides spreadsheets, Python code, and additional implementation details.

Why This Book Matters

Most trading books fall into one of two traps: theoretical elegance divorced from practice, or specific rules without a coherent framework. Carver bridges the gap with a modular system that works for a retiree holding ten ETFs and a professional running forty futures contracts. The emphasis on realistic expectations — Sharpe ratios capped at 1.0, back-tests discounted by pessimism factors, Half-Kelly for position sizing — is a powerful antidote to the overconfidence that dominates trading literature.

The handcrafting method for portfolio weights is a genuine contribution to quantitative finance. It produces results statistically indistinguishable from sophisticated bootstrap optimization without requiring any computing power, and it exposes the dirty secret of institutional portfolio management: experienced quants routinely fiddle with optimizer output until it "looks right." Carver formalized the fiddling into a transparent, repeatable process that anyone can follow.

Perhaps most importantly, the book is unflinchingly honest about the limits of systematic trading. You will still need luck. You will still face drawdowns that test your resolve. The framework does not eliminate risk — it sizes it appropriately, measures it consistently, and prevents the emotional sabotage that turns smart analysis into poor execution. In a field drowning in false promises of easy riches, that honesty is a remarkably valuable offering.
