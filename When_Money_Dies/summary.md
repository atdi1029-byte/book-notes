# When Money Dies — Summary

**Author:** Adam Fergusson
**First Published:** 1975 (2010 PublicAffairs edition with new foreword)
**Pages:** 273

## Overview

*When Money Dies* is the definitive account of the German hyperinflation of 1914–1923, told not as an economics textbook but as a human story. Adam Fergusson traces the mark's decade-long death from a currency on par with the British shilling to one where the number of marks needed to buy a pound equalled the number of yards to the sun. Drawing primarily on British Foreign Office records, contemporary diaries, and diplomatic dispatches, Fergusson shows how inflation corroded not just savings but morality, social bonds, and democratic institutions — creating the conditions that made the next catastrophe inevitable.

## Core Thesis

Inflation is not primarily an economic phenomenon but a moral and political one. The German hyperinflation was not caused by ignorance of how to stop it — at no point between 1914 and 1923 was stabilization technically impossible. It was caused by the repeated choice of the soft political option: printing money to avoid unemployment, social unrest, and the politically unacceptable consequences of fiscal discipline. Each postponement made the eventual reckoning more savage, until the mark had to die completely before a new currency could be born.

## Key Arguments

1. **War financing planted the seeds.** Karl Helfferich's decision to finance WWI entirely through borrowing rather than taxation (as Britain did) created a debt mountain that could only be serviced through further inflation. The Reichsbank's trick of counting Treasury bills as note-coverage gave unlimited license to print.

2. **The reparations trap was real but also an alibi.** While the London Ultimatum's demand for 132 billion gold marks was genuinely crushing, German leaders from Wirth to Rathenau used reparations as cover for domestic profligacy. The budget was already unbalanceable before a single reparation payment.

3. **Inflation was deliberately used as industrial policy.** A cheap mark made German exports irresistibly competitive, satisfied workers with rising nominal wages, and postponed political reckoning. Hugo Stinnes and the industrialists who profited most had every incentive to maintain the policy.

4. **The middle classes were destroyed by design.** Fixed-income earners — pensioners, civil servants, professionals, small savers — bore the full cost. War loans worth £45,000 at purchase shrank to £1,000. Insurance policies became worthless. The social contract between citizen and state was shattered.

5. **Hyperinflation bred extremism from both ends.** Communist Red Centuries and Hitler's nascent Nazi movement both fed on the same desperation. The Beer Hall Putsch of November 1923 was timed to exploit peak misery. Hitler "set his hopes on the revolt of starving billionaires."

6. **The Rentenmark miracle was psychological, not monetary.** The new currency was unconvertible paper backed by nothing tangible, yet it worked because it had a different name and the public chose to believe in it. The real stabilization came from fiscal discipline — balancing the budget and refusing to finance government through the printing press.

7. **Stabilization was nearly as devastating as inflation.** The cure brought massive unemployment, a food glut no one could afford, waves of bankruptcies, and the Stinnes empire collapse. The middle classes who survived inflation found their remaining assets further diminished by deflation.

8. **The moral damage outlasted the economic damage.** Corruption became endemic at every level. Thrift, honesty, and hard work lost their appeal when speculation yielded greater rewards. Anti-Semitism intensified as scapegoating replaced analysis. The false prosperity of 1926–1928, built on American loans, was the worst possible prelude to the Great Depression.

## Parallel Stories

Fergusson weaves three parallel hyperinflations together:
- **Austria** collapsed first, was rescued by the Geneva Protocols and League of Nations oversight, but the cure (mass unemployment, housing crisis) was nearly as painful as the disease.
- **Hungary** followed a similar arc with an additional layer of anti-Semitic violence, eventually stabilized under a League commissioner.
- **Germany** was the last and worst, requiring military dictatorship and constitutional suspension before stabilization could occur.

## The Moral Lesson

The book's final pages argue that hyperinflation reduces human values to animal values — a kilo of potatoes becomes worth more than family silver, a side of pork more than a grand piano, food more needed than freedom. The take-off point into hyperinflation was not financial but moral: the moment when a society decides it would rather debase its currency than face hard truths. Fergusson's warning is that this choice is always available to democratic governments, and always tempting.

> "This is, I believe, a moral tale. It goes far to prove the revolutionary axiom that if you wish to destroy a nation you must first corrupt its currency."
