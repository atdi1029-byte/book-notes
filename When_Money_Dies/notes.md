# When Money Dies — Notes (First Half)

## Note to the 2010 Edition

- Book first published 1975; 2010 note warns no advanced economy is immune from severe inflation
- **Numerical designations preserved**: milliard = 1,000 million, billion = million × million
- **"Quantitative easing"** = modern euphemism for surreptitious deficit financing — assault on monetary discipline postpones but doesn't prevent reckoning
- Budget can be balanced with printing press, but costs citizens' savings, pensions, confidence, morals, and morale
- 1975 context: US inflation 8%, Britain 10-27%, Japan 30% — scars of recovery slow to heal
- **Quantity theory of money's finest lesson**: Germany after WWI
- Germany experienced inflation twice; explains country's determination **never to return**

## Prologue

### Literature Gap on Inflation's Human Impact

- **Shortage in existing literature**: economic analyses ignore human element; historical accounts underestimate inflation as powerful engine of upheavals
- **Agony of inflation like acute pain**: totally absorbing while it lasts, forgotten when gone
- Cannot give fair account outside context of: political subversion, Army turmoil, quarrel with France, reparations problem, parallel hyperinflations in Austria/Hungary

### The Four Villains of 1923

- **Ludendorff**: soulless ex-Quartermaster-General, rallying point of reactionary forces
- **Stinnes**: plutocratic profiteer owing allegiance only to Mammon
- **Havenstein**: mad banker whose object was to swamp country with banknotes
- **Hitler**: power-hungry demagogue calling forth all evil in human nature
- Re Havenstein: description unjust, but **his sound mind made no difference to wreckage he wrought**
- **German people were the victims**

### Book's Approach

- Presents **many forgotten facts and hitherto unpublished opinions**
- **British Foreign Office records**: most bountiful store, supplied by embassy under **Lord D'Abernon**
- Special thread wound through Austria, Hungary, Russia, Poland, France: **effect of inflation on people as individuals and nations**
- **Not an economic study** — but impossible to tell tale without introducing vast figures
- October 1923: **number of marks to pound equalled number of yards to the sun**
- **Dr Schacht**: at war's end theoretically could buy 500,000,000,000 eggs; five years later, only single egg
- When stability returned, paper marks needed to buy gold mark = **quantity of square millimeters in square kilometer**

### Moral Tale

> "This is, I believe, a moral tale. It goes far to prove the revolutionary axiom that if you wish to destroy a nation you must first corrupt its currency. Thus must sound money be the first bastion of a society's defence."

## Chapter 1: Gold for Iron

### Pre-War Currency Stability

- Just before WWI in 1913: German mark, British shilling, French franc, Italian lira all worth about the same (4-5 to a dollar)
- End of 1923: possible to exchange shilling/franc/lira for **up to 1,000,000,000,000 marks**
- In practice by then **no one willing to take marks** in return for anything
- **Mark was dead, one millionth of former self** — took almost ten years to die

### The Gradual Fall and Sudden Collapse

- Mark's fall began gradually during war: foreign exchange value halved, then halved again by August 1919
- Early 1920: cost of living risen 9× since 1914, but mark had only **one-fortieth of overseas purchasing power** left
- Then mark **sped downwards with gathering momentum**, dragging social misery and political disruption
- Not until 1923 did currency finally **go over cliff-edge of sanity**

### 1923: The Year of Madness

- Year of galloping inflation when **kind of madness gripped Germany's financial authorities**
- Year of astronomical figures, 'wheelbarrow inflation', financial phenomena never observed before
- Death of old mark = **merciful release**, but events ensured Germany would undergo **appalling rigors of financial reconstruction**
- **Re-establishment of monetary sanity**: bankrupted thousands, robbed millions of livelihoods
- Indirectly **exacted more terrible price which whole world had to pay**

### Inflation's Destructive Power

- Process of collapse of trusted medium of exchange **unleashes such greed, violence, unhappiness, hatred** (bred from fear) as **no society can survive uncrippled and unchanged**
- 1922-1923 brought **catastrophe** to German, Austrian, Hungarian bourgeoisie, plus hunger, disease, destitution, sometimes death to wider public
- What most severely damaged morale: **climax of unreality** to years of unimagined strain
- **Financially, for nearly four years, ultimate cataclysm always just round corner** — arrived again and again
- Speeches, records, letters, diaries all report: **things could not go on like that any longer** — yet they always did, from bad to worse

### Inflation's Universal Corrosion

- Inflation **aggravated every evil**, ruined every chance of national revival
- Eventually produced conditions in which **extremists of Right and Left** could raise mob against State, set class against class, race against race
- **Undermined national resolution** when simple want might have bolstered it
- **Unfairly discriminatory nature** brought out worst in everybody
- Caused fear among those who already knew too much of it
- Fostered **xenophobia**, promoted **contempt for government**, corrupted even where corruption had been unknown
- **Worst possible prelude** to great depression, and thus to what followed

### Public Misperception: "The Dollar is Going Up"

- Natural reaction: not to assume **money was falling in value** as goods became more expensive
- Not that currency depreciating, but (especially in beginning) that **other currencies unfairly rising**
- **Erna von Pustau** (daughter of small Hamburg businessman):

> "We used to say 'The dollar is going up again', while in reality the dollar remained stable but our mark was falling. But, you see, we could hardly say our mark was falling since in figures it was constantly going up — and so were the prices — and this was much more visible than the realisation that the value of our money was going down … It all seemed just madness, and it made the people mad."

### The German Revolution's Economic Origins

- German revolution originated as **military mutiny** against bungling of Army's leaders
- Support for **Soldiers' Councils** rested heavily on **personal financial calamities** so many soldiers and families already experiencing
- Frustrations aggravated by arrival at front (spring 1918) of **group of seasoned anti-war agitators**
- When defeat came, Army torn in two, **essentially between professional soldiers and conscripts**

### The Dolchstoss Myth

- Supreme Command **exculpating itself** for losing war
- **Odium of signing Armistice** placed firmly on civilian authority
- **President Ebert** at Brandenburger Tor: "I salute you, who return unvanquished from the field of battle"
- Myth of **Dolchstoss** (stab in back) thereafter cultivated to point when **democratic political evolution poisoned at heart**
- New Republic saddled with **hatred of Officer Corps**, permanently generating **Right-wing discontent**

### The Ebert-Gröner Bargain

- Critical point came **few hours before Armistice signed**
- **Spartakists** (German Bolsheviks) already massing in Berlin streets
- Civilian power (President Ebert), fearing democratic hours numbered, struck **bargain** with **German High Command** (General Gröner) to co-operate in suppression of Bolshevism
- **Saved the Army**: Republican government instructed Soldiers' Councils to support officers without reserve
- Within three months: **Weimar Assembly meeting under military protection**
- By May 1919 Army proper entirely reorganised, with 400,000 men trained in arms

### Scapegoating and the Myth of Military Invincibility

- Nation which learnt to **make virtue of war**, to **exalt warriors above all professions**, was bound to seek scapegoats
- **Myth that German Army undefeated in field**: believed because Germany wanted to believe it
- If what Hindenburg termed '**lamentable outcome of war**' not fault of Supreme Command, had logically to be fault of someone else
- Yet **genuinely did not seem to occur** to Supreme Command that breakdown of military machine stemmed **as much from economic mismanagement as anything else**

## Germany's War Financing: The Helfferich System

### The Origins of Inflation (1914-1918)

- Germany expected short, victorious war, planned finances accordingly
- **Karl Helfferich** (State Secretary for Finance, 1915-1917) presided over first stage of inflation
- August 1914: gold redemption suspended, protecting reserves while enabling monetary expansion

### Loan Banks and the Printing Press

- War financed through **loan banks** (Darlehenskassen) whose funds came from printing money
- Loan banks advanced money for war bond subscriptions
- **Critical mistake**: Reichsbank permitted to include three-month Treasury bills in note-coverage, allowing unlimited amounts to be rediscounted against banknotes

### Borrowing Over Taxation

- Government financed war "wilfully and simply" by borrowing, not taxation
- Taxation played "not the smallest part" in meeting war costs before 1916
- Allied blockade threw Germany back on her own resources, making resource depletion inevitable

### The Scale of Wartime Inflation

- Total war expenditure: **164,000 million marks**
- Due to currency depreciation, equivalent to only ~110,000 million pre-war marks
- **"Mark gleich Mark"** already a fiction during the war
- War loans provided three-fifths of financing

### Minimal Tax Effort

- **Dr. Hjalmar Schacht** criticized Helfferich's approach
- Helfferich announced in Reichstag (March 1915): war to be financed exclusively by borrowing
- Tax revenues raised: less than 8,000 million marks/year even in 1917
- Money in circulation increased **5× by 1917** vs. 1913

> "Germany tried to meet the colossal costs of the war by an appeal to the self-sacrificing spirit of the people. 'I gave gold for iron' was the slogan for the surrender of ornaments and jewellery. 'Invest in War Loan' ran the appeal to the patriotic sense of duty of all classes." — **Dr. Hjalmar Schacht**

### Germany vs. Britain: Contrasting Approaches

- **Britain** taxed industries and groups to whom war spelled prosperity
- **Germany** relied on War Loans, which lost value after the war
- Schacht: Britain's taxation policy "socially more equitable"

### Financial Opacity During Wartime

- All German stock exchanges closed for duration of war
- Foreign exchange rates unpublished; only neutral markets (Amsterdam, Zurich) knew reality
- Impossible to distinguish domestic price rises from war shortages vs. inflation
- Only after war, with censorship lifted but Allied blockade continuing, did economic disaster become clear

## Social Consequences of Wartime Inflation

### Disruption of Pay Differentials

- *Vossische Zeitung* (August 1921): soldiers in trenches had double the deserters at home
- Deserters motivated by economic reasons: price rises caused poverty for enlisted men
- Soldiers didn't share in wage increases, trading profits, or war industries

> "Our military defeat was due to the fact that for every 1,000 men we had in the trenches, double that number of deserters and embusqués remained at home... The first to suffer had to be those who did not share in the general increase in paper revenue..." — *Vossische Zeitung*

### The Currency Explanation

- *Vossische Zeitung* correctly identified cause: "exploitation of our currency for the purpose of obtaining money from the Treasury"
- In issuing War Loans, Treasury bills without withdrawing corresponding amounts in taxes, government "created new paper income and wealth incessantly, while the real national wealth was steadily being diminished by the war"

## The Armistice and Its Economic Impact

### Immediate Collapse

- War damaged German economy; Armistice and peace terms "shook it to the foundations"
- **Compiègne Armistice** (November 11, 1918): surrender of fleet, withdrawal from Alsace-Lorraine, continuation of Allied blockade
- German standard of living estimated to have fallen to **half** pre-war levels
- First street demonstration in Munich (November 7): sparked by **6 pfennig increase in beer price**

### January 1919 Elections and Weimar Constitution

- Over 30 million voters produced coalition to face peace terms
- New **Democratic Party** main author of **Weimar constitution** (completed August)
- **Sir John Wheeler-Bennett**: German people in 1919 "deprived of their physical and moral capacity for further resistance"

## Treaty of Versailles: The Economic Straitjacket

### Germany's Illusions Shattered

- Germany hoped peace based on **Wilson's Fourteen Points**
- French determination, founded on fear and desire for vengeance, ensured prevention of German resurgence

### Territorial Losses and Reparations

- Treaty separated Germany from one-seventh of pre-war territory and one-tenth of population
- Alsace-Lorraine returned to France; France to occupy Rhineland and exploit Saar coal for 15 years
- Clauses making Germany responsible for war demanded "colossal reparations - in money and in kind"
- War guilt had not previously impinged upon German consciousness

### Currency Collapse Accelerates

- Before war: mark stood at 20 to pound sterling
- End of war (December 1918): stood at 43
- Before Treaty acceptance (June 1919): pound bought **60 marks**
- December 1919: pound bought **185 marks**
- Average annual wartime depreciation (~20%) came to resemble stability

## Chapter 2: Joyless Streets (Austria Post-War)

### Austria's Dismemberment

- **Treaties of St Germain and Trianon** dismantled Hapsburg Empire entirely
- Austria and Hungary reduced to fractions of former size
- **Vienna**: once imperial capital, now "vast city without adequate hinterland" forbidden from *Anschluss* with Germany
- Revolution deposed **Emperor Karl**; Budapest: **Bela Kun's** Bolshevik revolution succeeded by counter-revolution, then **regency of Horthy** (beginning 1920)

### Political Dysfunction

- **G.M. Young** (British Treasury) reported to **Lord Curzon**:

> "Out of the capital and dynastic patrimony of a great empire, the Treaty of St Germain created a Republic. Nominally at least, it made this oddly mixed community of bankers and peasants responsible for the cost of a great war."

- Austrian constitution: "super-democracy, contrived to ensure that as little authority as possible is exercised by anyone"
- President: "purely ceremonial figure; he opens flower shows and legitimates bastards"
- Austria "quite unripe for the advanced parliamentary system which the revolution gave her"

### Price Collapse and Black Markets

- Pre-war (1914): Austrian krone nearly at par with mark
- 1914 Vienna: kilogram best wheat flour cost **44 heller** (equivalent ~6d sterling)
- December 1918: wheat flour unobtainable; indefinable flour mixture from illicit dealer **22 kronen/kilo** — exactly **fifty times** as much
- By May 1922: pound sterling worth 1,200 marks, would have bought **35,000 kronen**

### Cultural Evidence: *The Joyless Street*

- Early **Greta Garbo** film (1925) made in Vienna by **Pabst**
- Odious butcher insulting/taunting queues at his shop, refusing meat to women he found unattractive or unwilling
- Unlicensed, gluttonous revelry of nightlife speculators and profiteers
- Ultimate attack by starving, angry crowd on café full of merrymakers
- "Faithful reflection of the times"

### Anna Eisenmenger's Diary

- Detailed contemporary account of inflation's scourge in post-war Vienna
- Middle-class widow with war-blinded son, tubercular daughter, son-in-law with amputated legs, hungry grandson, Communist son
- Progressively turned to illegal practices to keep family going
- Resorted to **Schleichhändler** (smugglers) for basic foods
- **"Hamstering"** (hoarding) became "no more than commonsense" though indictable offense
- Pre-war investments in "gilt-edged securities" still provided income: ~5,000 kronen/year = ~£200

### The Bank Official's Warning

- October 1918: resolved to cash 20,000 kronen for immediate use
- Bank manager advised converting to Swiss francs
- Banking hall full of animated discussions about stamping, new notes, purchasing foreign money
- Bank official:

> "Well, wasn't I right? If you had bought Swiss francs when I advised you, you would not now have lost three-fourths of your fortune."

> "Surely there can't be anything safer than government securities... 'My dear lady, where is the State which guaranteed these securities to you? It is dead.'"

### Food Crisis and Barter Economy

- Son's War Loan "already become unsaleable"
- Grandson developed scurvy
- New food prices (strictly rationed) **four times** previous official prices
- Other food hardly available for money; obtained principally by barter
- Doctor's wife exchanged "beautiful piano for a sack of wheat flour"
- Frau Eisenmenger exchanged "husband's gold watch for four sacks of potatoes"
- Farmer eyed grand piano: "My wife has been wanting one of those things for a long time. If you'll give it to me, you shall have all you want for three months"

### Spring 1919: No Relief

- Spring brought no alleviation for those without political leverage
- Furniture, fittings, pianos, carpets bought up wholesale by "gold-currency people" (occupying Italians, British, Americans)
- "Last valuables of countless houses flowed on to the market, no one warning their owners not to part with goods whose intrinsic value remained unimpaired"
- Viennese with bundle of kronen "thinks he has grown richer, without taking into account the enormous rise in prices"

### November 1919: Beyond Understanding

- Year after Armistice: Frau Eisenmenger's position "alarmingly worse"
- "Financial situation beyond her understanding"
- Krone at 25 Swiss centimes the previous Christmas, now quoted at **one-twelfth of a centime**
- "Gambling on the stock exchange has become the fashion - the only way to avoid losing all one's money"
- Mob attempted to set Parliament building on fire; mounted policemen torn from horses, which were slaughtered in Ringstrasse
- Warm bleeding flesh dragged away by crowd
- "Side by side with unprecedented want among the bulk of the population, there is a striking display of luxury among those who are benefitting from the inflation"

### December 1919: Industrial Shares Rising

- December 15, 1919: "Value of my industrial investments is rising to an extent which seems to be incomprehensible and almost makes me uneasy"
- Daughter could make **two dollars a day** at American Mission; could be exchanged for **400 krone**, only **100 krone less** than monthly pension of retired privy councillor
- "Former civil servants and officers are undoubtedly the poorest of the poor in Austria today"
- "Speculation on the stock exchange has spread to all ranks of the population and shares rise like air balloons to limitless heights"
- "What was happening to Austria then was simply a foretaste of what was to come to Germany"

## Industrial Recovery and Living Standards (1920)

### Class Disparities in Post-War Germany

- German living standards fell steeply during war, while France/Britain's actually rose slightly
- **British consul in Leipzig** reported wages/cost of living reached equilibrium, purchasing power nearly equal to pre-war levels
- Middle class (fixed incomes, pensions, government officials) hardest hit - 10,000 marks/year now inadequate for bare necessities
- Saxon industry in "very satisfactory condition" despite eight-hour workday

### The Paradox of German Industry (1920)

- **Apparent industrial health confused the inflation issue**
- Currency depreciation made German industry highly competitive in foreign markets
- Industrial lot materially improved over previous twelve months
- Most workers had realized work (not political dogma) was the answer to difficulties

## Political Polarization and Reparations (June 1920)

### June 1920 Elections

- **Two storm-cones for the future**:
  1. June elections returned central coalition under **Herr Fehrenbach** (Chancellor) and **Dr Wirth** (Finance Ministry)
  2. Political polarization: DNVP (far Right) trebled its vote, Independent Socialists (far Left) doubled
- Moderate coalition (Democrats and Majority Socialists) lost much voter confidence

### Money Supply Explosion

- At war outbreak: 2,700 million marks in circulation
- November 1918: 27,000 million marks
- November 1920: 77,000 million marks
- **Pound sterling**: 240 marks (November 1920)
- **Dollar**: 60 marks
- As mark improved, unemployment rapidly rose to 6% (summer 1920) - full employment became primary objective at mark's expense

## Lord D'Abernon's Observations (June 1920)

### Britain's Most Versed Money Observer

- **Lord D'Abernon** arrived June 1920 as British Ambassador to Berlin
- "More practically versed in money matters than most in any office in that city"
- Enjoyed full confidence of German ministers wrestling with inflation/reparations twin problems
- Watched helplessly as "chickens of unlimited deficit financing swarmed in to roost"

### The Impossible Budget Math

- German budget barely balanced, leaving nothing for war indemnities
- Raising enough for June reparations would require **twice the revenue** from latest tax proposals
- "Altogether impossible to conceive that twice the new rate of taxation could be imposed without producing a revolution" - D'Abernon

### Germany's Debt Reality (October 1920)

- National debt: 287,800 million marks
- At old 1914 parities: £14,400 million
- At new 1920 exchange rate: only £1,200 million
- **Germany's national debt had been wiped out** a year before the great inflation began

## German Elite Pessimism

### President Loebe's Testimony

> "I know from the case of my own children how badly people are fed. You cannot get milk, and an egg costs 2¾ marks. The financial condition of the middle classes, the small employers and so on is even worse than that of the working class, to which I belong. Only today we had a memo from the lower officials in South Germany saying that if their pay was not increased they would wreck the till. Working-class wages have roughly been increased eight to ten times compared with pre-war; smaller salaries have only been increased two to four times. This makes an impossible situation when the cost of living has gone up tenfold."

## The London Ultimatum (April 27, 1921)

### Reparations Commission Fixed Total Liability

- **132,000 million gold marks** (£6,600 million) total liability
- Payment terms: 2,000 million gold marks (£100 million) annually **plus 26% of exports**
- Threat of further sanctions: occupation of Ruhr by French if compliance not within the week
- **"London Ultimatum"** drove mark to 268 to the pound
- Caused **Fehrenbach government to fall**

### Dr Wirth Accepts the Terms

- **Dr Wirth** became new Chancellor
- Accepted terms knowing heavy additional taxation required
- Mark advanced to 232 to pound as financial uncertainty briefly retreated
- Communist-inspired armed rising by **Mansfeld miners** collapsed ignominiously

## Chapter 4: Delirium of Milliards

### Rathenau's Diagnosis

- **"Delirium of milliards"** - Rathenau's phrase
- "The majority of statesmen and financiers think in terms of paper"

> "They sit in their offices and look at papers which are lying in front of them, and on those papers are written figures which again represent papers … They write down noughts, and nine noughts mean a milliard. A milliard comes easily and trippingly to the tongue, but no one can imagine a milliard."

> "What is a milliard? Does a wood contain a milliard leaves? Are there a milliard blades of grass in a meadow? Who knows? If the Tiergarten were to be cleared and wheat sown upon its surface, how many stalks would grow? Two milliards!"

- Rathenau diagnosed delirium not of people in general but of **those controlling the country's finances**
- Note circulation raised from 75 milliard to 80 milliard marks
- Mark slid from 261 to pound (June) to 310 (mid-August)
- Blame laid squarely by **Rathenau (as by Wirth)** on reparation payments

## The Breaking Point (August 1921)

### Mannheimer's Signal

- Close confederate of **Herr Hugo Stinnes** said breaking point came when German government repaid loan
- **Mannheimer**: "confidential man" of **Dr Rudolf Havenstein** (Reichsbank President since 1908)
- Instructed by chief, went to England (August 1921) and bought foreign currency at any price
- "For Germany had any amount of paper marks but no foreign currency"
- **First signal of absolute breakdown in mark's value**

### Four Reasons for Depreciation Policy

1. Enable heavy industry to compete with foreign countries
2. Satisfy workmen's demands by increasing wages with worthless paper marks
3. Avoid political troubles and disturbances
4. Prove to the world Germany could not fulfil Versailles Treaty

### Bavaria's Foreboding (Munich, August 1921)

- Gloomiest forebodings about cost of living
- Farmers' scandalous profiteering in milk and grain
- Ill-feeling in towns against peasants thoroughly aroused
- **Munich municipality** voted against dearer bread in vote-catching move
- Action encouraged advance demands for higher wages

## Frontkämpfertag - War Army Day (August 24, 1921)

### The March

- Nakedly political, anti-Republic display in Berlin
- March-past of 2,000 war veterans headed by **39-year-old Prince Eitel Friedrich** (Kaiser's second son)
- Marched under archway: *"In Kriege Unbesiegt"* (unbeaten in war)

### Army Chaplain's Sermon

- Before 20,000 spectators
- Suggested Germany's greatness could only be recovered by military power, through monarchy and Hohenzollerns
- Speeches by three generals: **Ludendorff, Graf Waldersee, von der Goltz**
- Caused *"rauschender Jubel"* (roaring cheers) and long cheers for Hohenzollerns
- Arena lined by 2,000-3,000 youths/girls, two-deep, precision drill — "most striking"

### Academic Honors for Ludendorff

- **University of Königsberg** conferred honorary doctorate of medicine same day
- Diploma tribute: "to the hero who with the sharp edge of his unbroken sword defended the German nation... till that nation, trustful of lying promises, abandoned its unconquered Army and its strong leader"

### Erzberger's Murder (August 26, 1921)

- Two days after *Frontkämpfertag*: **Mathias Erzberger** murdered by Nationalist gunmen in Black Forest
- Likely inspired by speeches of DNVP leader **Karl Helfferich** (wartime Finance Minister)
- Erzberger: civilian, Republican, **Jew**
- Belgian Socialist deputy: "Assassination seems now to have become the rule in Germany"
- **Herr Harden** explained to D'Abernon: Right hunting for culprits responsible for downfall
- **Over 300 assassinations among Left leaders** since Armistice, "and no one is punished"

### Wirth's Confidence vs. Economic Crisis

- **Chancellor Dr Wirth** expressed confidence public order could be maintained
- Government's worries more for **economic crisis** than political one
- Recent fall in exchange forced him to grant new salary/wage increases of about 10 milliards

### Bavaria's Economic-Political Crisis

- **British Consul-General in Munich, Mr William Seeds** reported Socialist trade unions holding mass meeting
- Mass meeting of ~50,000 went off without serious disturbance
- Only one demonstrator killed, another wounded in scuffle with police

## International Financial Community's Verdict

### Berne Conference (September 1921)

- Bankers from Switzerland, Italy, Germany soberly concluded:
- **Impossible for Germany to continue payments to Entente**
- Sooner or later she would have to declare herself bankrupt
- Mark at 310 to pound (mid-August) sped downwards to over 400 (mid-September), still going down

### Joseph Addison's Analysis (September 20, 1921)

- **Mr Joseph Addison** (Councillor, British Embassy Berlin) reported to Foreign Office:

> "The daily creation of fresh paper money which the government requires in order to meet its obligations both at home and abroad inevitably decreases the purchasing value of the mark and leads to fresh demands, which in turn bring about a further decline, and so on *ad infinitum*."

### Speculation Against the Mark

> "Millions of persons in this country are, I think accurately, reported to be buying foreign currencies in anticipation of fresh tax burdens, and to be hoarding foreign bank notes … I hardly know a single German of either sex who is not speculating in foreign currencies, such as Austrian crowns, Polish marks and even Kerensky roubles. In as much also as a fall in the value of the mark is inevitably accompanied by a rise in the quotation of industrial shares, speculators are supposed to be operating systematically to depreciate the mark with a view to reaping the benefit of higher quotations in the share market."

### Tax Structure and Incentives

- Four principal taxes:
  1. Once-and-for-all increment tax on increase in property value 1913-1919
  2. Capital tax on value of possessions after increment tax paid
  3. Unearned income tax 10%
  4. Ordinary income tax affected wider public as money wages rose
- Income tax started at 10% on first 24,000 marks, rose to maximum 60% on all income over 395,000 marks/year
- As mark fell and wages rose, taxation of real income became proportionately greater

### German Industry's Advantages

- Government subventions cheapening production
- Coal available below cost price in Britain
- Bread sold considerably under open market price
- Railway transport offered at less than working costs
- German budget suffered from immense/continuous gain of German commerce and industry

### Capital Flight Consequences

- Where possible, created credit balances abroad via direct arrangement with customers
- Under-invoiced exported products
- Over-invoiced imports in return
- Such manoeuvres had extra advantage of avoiding taxation in Germany
- On large scale, this would tend toward mark's inflation

### Labor Unrest and Government Crisis

- Present labor unrest caused by mark's fall and impending new taxation
- German manufacturer/trader making lots of money in Germany and abroad
- State getting ever nearer bankruptcy

### Budget Reality (October 1921)

- State of budget somber
- Government's ordinary expenditure + estimated Allied payments = 113 milliard marks
- Revenue from previous budget + new July taxation proposals would amount to less than 90 milliards
- Calculations based on conversion ratio: one gold mark to 13 paper marks
- Already true rate was 22 to one
- **Capital levy** failed to produce substantial real revenue because of time lapse between assessment and payment

## Wirth Government Crisis (October 1921)

### Upper Silesia Crisis

- Wirth's government on point of resignation
- Straw that broke it: October 17 announcement of **League of Nations' decision** on Upper Silesia future
- Former imperial province to be partitioned between Germany and Poland
- Indignation universal and acute
- Chancellor obliged to resign; **October 26**: back in power again
- Meanwhile mark hit new low of 600 to pound

### Public Anger and Speculation

- Public angry but not yet depressed/pessimistic as politicians
- Berlin shops selling huge quantities of goods
- Share prices moved upwards as dollar improved against mark
- Banks so full of stock exchange orders some were days behind opening letters

### D'Abernon's San Francisco Comparison

> "Of course the rise is unhealthy and feverish. We cannot be very far from a panic. As soon as inflation ceases to increase and deflation sets in, there will be the devil to pay. Behrenstrasse here, which corresponds to Lombard Street, reminds me of San Francisco after the Earthquake. Almost every bank is being increased or rebuilt, and one can hardly get along the roadway. Something like the South Sea Bubble."

### Profiteering and Speculation

- *Berliner Tageblatt* (November 9, 1921):

> "We still import more than we export. And yet we must surrender 26 per cent of the gross value of our exports. Foreign countries, frightened by our rivalry, are constructing barbed-wire fences. Obligations are forced on us for decades to come, and nothing is done to stabilise the mark by any international action. When the [London] Ultimatum was accepted the dollar was at 60 marks. Today it is 180 or more."

- Demanded profits on exchange and dollar speculations be taxed intensively
- "The assessment of all our taxes is so behindhand that hundreds of thousands of the wealthy classes have not yet paid their taxes for the year 1920"

## Capital Flight

### Estimates of German Banknotes Abroad

- **Arthur Eichhorn** newspaper article commented authoritatively on capital flight:

> "Evasion of taxation, fear of socialisation, and inflation have combined to drive capital out of countries with a depreciated currency into countries where the currency is sound or at a premium … This has nothing to do with international trade or with normal credit operations, but merely increases the chaos of exchange rates."

### The Inevitable Outcome (Mid-November 1921)

- With capital escaping, budget unbalanceable, no one wanting to hold marks: inevitable happened
- By mid-November 1921: dollar would buy 250 paper marks, pound sterling 1,040
- Germany needed to find 500 million gold marks before end of February to pay Allies

## Financial Crisis and International Pressure (Late 1921)

### The London Ultimatum and Default Fears

- Germany faced threat of Ruhr occupation if default occurred
- London bankers refused credits unless Germany restored financial order and France became "more reasonable"
- Three urgent measures needed but politically impossible:
  - Balance budget to stop printing presses
  - End industrial subventions
  - Revise customs duties with efficient tax collection

### Industrial Unrest and Price Inflation

- Rising cost of living caused industrial unrest in the Ruhr
- **British Vice-Consul in Essen** reported inadequate punishment for profiteering
- Wholesale prices often 100% above retail prices
- Prices of existing stock increased within 24 hours

### Sir Basil Blackett's Assessment (November 22, 1921)

**Blackett**, Controller of Finance at British Treasury, presented sobering memorandum:

> "In spite of his robust common sense, the man in the [German] street is beginning to believe what some interested industrialists are telling him, so that he seems almost readily to subscribe to the false doctrine that it is good for trade that a government, by inflationary finance, should habitually spend more than its income..."

- German industrialists knew their activity was "destroying the export trade of its neighbours"
- Each class wanted taxation to fall on other classes
- Big industrialists converting paper marks into foreign currencies or real assets
- **Incentive to saving was gone** when saving became vital necessity for the State

### Impact on the Middle Classes

- Fall in mark "gradually-wiping out the middle classes"
- 1 million marks in German War Loan, worth £45,000 at purchase, now worth only £1,000 sterling
- German pensioner with 10,000 marks pre-war income now had £10 sterling value and less than £30 purchasing power
- Insurance policies worth less than annual premiums paid
- Rent restriction Acts forced middle classes "to starvation in order to subsidise the German workman's wages and the employer's profits"
- Foreigners buying up real property, factory interests, furniture, pianos - "A gradual process of buying up and carrying off Germany's movable capital"

## Evidence from Occupied Zones

### British Consul-General in Cologne (November 23, 1921)

**Mr Paget Thurstan** observed:

> "It is no uncommon spectacle to see queues of intending purchasers lined outside the shops waiting for the doors to reopen... wholesale prices being now often considerably above retail prices, the difference in certain articles being nearly 100 per cent. Clearly, as long as such conditions prevail, there can be no limit to the rise in the cost of living as measured in marks, and settlements of wages' disputes are merely ephemeral."

### Chancellor Wirth's Concerns

**Addison's interview with Chancellor**: fast-rising prices giving immediate concern for public order

> "The Chancellor is doing his best to keep things quiet, but the impossibility for the working classes to obtain even obvious necessities except at exorbitant prices, coupled with severe winter setting in, might lead to serious trouble."

- About to preside over Cabinet meeting in semi-darkness from partial electrical workers' strike
- Elements for disturbances and revolutions present in Germany by late November 1921
- Food riots taking place in Berlin
- Mark dropped to just over 1,300 to the pound

## The Vienna Riots (December 2, 1921)

### The December 2 Riot

- Triggered by severe fall in krone
- 30,000-person unarmed crowd wrecked and looted food shops, restaurants, cafés
- Attacked hotels in inner quarter
- Crowd penetrated to second floor, invaded and ransacked **Sir William Goode's** apartment
- Goode "kept the mob for an hour from entering a room where several English and American ladies had taken refuge"

### Demonstrators' Demands

- Seizure of all foreign currency
- State control of securities market
- All gold to State hands, including church and monastery holdings
- Progressive property tax, immediate child insurance system
- **Chancellor gave ground on all matters except food subsidies**

### Pattern of Suffering

- Austrians' capacity for long-suffering proved remarkable
- Four-year concatenation of financial crises relatively tamely received
- Middle class hardest hit, had not taken effective public action
- With krone above 2,000 to pound, Austria settled back into usual lethargy
- Rich nightlife grew more phrenetic while retired barristers and ex-generals found "stone-breaking beside the Danube"
## Chapter 8: The Slide (Cont'd) — Stock Market and Banking

**October 1922: Stock Market Peak in Paper Terms**
- Share price index peaked Oct 24, 1922 — thereafter declined in paper marks though soared in gold
- Example rises (Jan → Oct 1922): Daimler 331 → 1,605; Kammgarnspinnerei Düsseldorf 131 → 2,550
- Real terms disaster: end of war share index 1/3 of 1913; 1921 at 1/5th; Oct 1922: 3/100ths
- Daimler Motor Company could have been swapped for 327 of its cars
- Foreign buying began post-October as speculators realized advantages of buying German assets with cheap credit

**End of 1922: Hyperinflation Accelerates**
- **Erna von Pustau**: "By end of year, my allowance worth less than one cup of coffee. Baker sold rolls for 20 marks in morning, 25 marks in afternoon."
- Milk: 78 → 202 marks/lb (Nov); Butter: 800 → 2,000; Sugar: 90 → 220; Eggs: 22 → 30 marks each
- **Notgeld** (emergency currency) proliferated — 40 issuing bodies in occupied territories alone
- Towns paying employees with own currency: Krefeld, Coblenz, Düsseldorf, Bonn, Cologne (signed by **Konrad Adenauer**)

## Fourth London Conference and Ruhr Invasion

**December 1922: Poincaré's Hardline**
- **Poincaré** to **Bonar Law**: "Whatever happens, I shall advance into the Ruhr on January 15."
- **Lloyd George** believed Poincaré's proposals showed "sinister resolve to engineer German default to justify invasion"
- Germany's Note proposing mark stabilisation with two-year moratorium rejected
- Jan 9, 1923: Reparation Commission (France, Belgium, Italy; Britain dissenting) decided Germany in voluntary default
- Jan 11: **General Degoutte** despatched control commission to Ruhr with troops

**The Ruhr Invasion: Economic Catastrophe**
- Ruhr provided 85% of Germany's coal, 80% of steel/pig-iron, 70% of goods/minerals traffic, 10% of population
- Mark fell from 35,000/pound (Christmas 1922) → 48,000 (day after invasion) → 227,500 (end Jan 1923)
- 2 million workers, 6 million souls had to be supported by rest of country
- Germany denied coal, coke, iron, steel — had to import at rising world prices
- Exchequer deprived of tax revenue from huge proportion of industry

## Chapter 9: Ruhrkampf

**National Unity Against Occupation**
- **Tirpitz**: France assigned "historic role of welding divergent forces of our people into unity again"
- Class hatred suspended — industrialists imprisoned alongside employees became national heroes
- **Erna von Pustau**: "All the old patriotic songs were sung. Even Father gave money to help the Ruhr. We were willing to suffer cold for our fatherland."

**Mass Expulsions and Casualties**
- 147,000 Germans expelled by **General Degoutte**; 5,764 railway workers + 17,237 dependants
- 2,000+ wounded, 376 killed during occupation
- Sabotage: bridges, points, junctions dynamited; signals jammed; military trains derailed, ships sunk

**Reactionary Movements Emerge**
- **Hitler** appeared in Ruhr on recruiting drive, arranged disruption
- Well-paid workers spent annual holidays shooting French officers or blowing up supply trains
- **Rote Hundertschaften** (Red Centuries): French stimulated local Communists to undermine German authorities

**Government's Monetary Response**
- Mid-February: Reichsbank threw large amounts of foreign bills onto market, mark recovered 50,000 → 20,000 to dollar
- February note circulation increased 450 milliards/week
- Early March single day: floating debt increased 800 milliards via Treasury bills
- Mark held ~100,000/pound through March and first two weeks April despite French expansion to Mannheim, Karlsruhe, Darmstadt

**Leipzig Spring Fair Disappointment**
- Export prices too high to get orders due to mark stability on foreign exchanges
- German production shifted to low-class goods to maximize employment and meet reduced purchasing power
- Average citizen preferred buying three pairs of indifferent boots to one good pair
- **"His was becoming a cheapjack country"**

**Mark Breaks (April 18)**
- Government gold loan under-subscribed by 75%
- **Hugo Stinnes** presented Reichsbank demand for £93,000 in foreign bills — intervention policy collapsed
- Within 24 hours mark at 140,000/pound

**Gold Reserves Dwindle**
- **Lord D'Abernon**: "Inflation is like a drug... fatal in the end, but gets its votaries over many difficult moments"
- March: gold reserves 1,004 million gold marks; mid-May: one-fifth gone
- Intervention attempts made it easier for speculators to pick up foreign currencies at artificially low prices

**Labour Unrest and May Day**
- May Day: **Hitler's** attempt to break up Munich trade union demonstration thwarted by **von Lossow**
- Mark dropped past 200,000/pound
- **Ernest Hemingway** returned to Kehl late April: Champagne 38,000 marks/bottle, luncheon 3,500, sandwich 900, beer 350

**Currency Speculation Becomes Mass Phenomenon**
- Over 1 million Germans engaged in exchange speculation early 1923
- **Winkelbankiers** (back-street operators) played significant part determining daily exchange rate
- May: working classes paid more tax than assessed tax payers (former tapped at source, latter's returns out of date)
- May 1 → 31: mark fell 220,000 → 320,000/pound
- June 1: first **five-million-mark note** issued

**Massive Unemployment Arrives**
- Until Ruhr invasion: inflationist policy governed by fear of unemployment
- Now massive unemployment came, inflation pursued more vigorously than ever
- June: of 511 milliard marks income tax, only 90 milliards from assessed payers — wage-earners prepayments the rest
- **Stinnes** seen as maleficent influence on Germany's finances

**Barter Economy**
- Cinema seat cost lump of coal; bottle of paraffin bought shirt
- **Herr von der Osten** kept girlfriend for half pound butter/month (1922) → whole pound (summer 1923)
- **Erna von Pustau**: "Communities printed own money based on goods. Shoe factories paid workers in bonds for shoes."
- **5,000-mark cup of coffee would cost 8,000 marks by time it was drunk**

**The Grim Reality**
- Agony displayed by shrunken-necked gentlemen in mended white collars and shiny suits
- Exhausted workmen queueing with big shopping bags to rush wages to shops
- **Lady Listowel** (Budapest): "One remembered where there used to be a picture, carpet, secretaire. Eventually rooms almost empty. People didn't just die. They were terribly hungry."

## June 1923: The Mark Collapses

**Price Explosions After May 20**
- Egg: 800 → 2,400 marks; Milk: 1,800 → 3,800; Flour: 2,400 → 6,600; Pork: 10,400 → 32,000
- Ruhr: salaries doubled while commodity costs trebled
- Tradesmen couldn't establish prices, many shut shops

**Reichsbank's Failed Interventions**
- June: 6 million gold marks lost in single day in futile intervention
- June 21: daily circulation increase 157 milliards
- June 26-27: circulation increased **1,523,534,460,000 marks in one day**
- June 28: mark at 170,000/dollar, total circulation 11,000 milliards

**Taxation System Collapses**
- Nation of 60 million couldn't raise £30 million/year to meet £80 million expenditure
- **Multiplier** (Minister's tool to adjust taxation): income tax multiplied 25x in May, 40x announced for August
- Tax returns never kept pace with depreciation
- **Dr. Hermes**: raising duties offered "no financial guarantee for the future" — despair was keynote

**Crackdown on Speculation Backfires**
- Third week June: ordinance fixed legal exchange rates, aimed at **Winkelbankiers**
- **Rhineland High Commission refused to adopt** — huge transactions continued in Cologne
- **General Degoutte** accused Germans of "wilfully debasing the mark" by forbidding strengthening move
- Arbitrage opportunity: buy dollars at 160,000, use for Treasury bills quoted much higher

**Havenstein's Defiance**
- **Dr. Havenstein** believed Berlin must keep mark low "whatever it might do elsewhere"
- Last days June: mark sank 600,000 → 800,000/pound
- Nearly **80 million gold marks** thrown away to satisfy speculators
- July 10: free gold reserve fallen to £35 million
- If intervention continued: **bankruptcy within 60 days**

**Banking Crisis (July 19)**
- Reichsbank suspended discounting trade paper, refused to sacrifice more gold
- Demands on Reichsbank = **entire paper circulation in country**
- End July: only **£25 million in gold left**

## July 1923: Political and Social Disintegration

**Government Loses Confidence**
- **Germania** (July 27): confidence "completely shattered" — anger "universal"
- "Beyond a few ordinances controlling meat consumption and occasional speeches, they remain passive"
- Credits issued to industrial circles for hoarding foreign bills
- **"The real collapse of our currency began when certain industrial circles became more powerful than government"**

**Strikes and Civil Unrest**
- Workers demanded higher wages and **daily payment**
- July 20-21 **Breslau**: thousand arrests after riots and plundering
- July 24 **Frankfort**: one man kicked to death
- Last week July: demonstrators killed in **Rosenheim** and **Potsdam**

**Communist Influence**
- **Rote Hundertschaften** (Red Centuries): probably **30,000 strong in Saxony** vs. 11,500 police
- Soviet agents among them, purpose to supplement police
- Dangerous from national viewpoint

**Wage Indexation Agreement**
- Beginning July: wages related by indexation to cost of living
- Weekly adjustment plus daily payments to maintain value
- In practice: wage rises always **15 days in arrears** of cost of living
- Great saving to Exchequer as mark mounted: 800,000 (July 7) → 5,000,000 or 21 million/dollar (July 31)

**German Ambassador's Plea to Curzon**
> "The mark following its latest tremendous collapse is beginning to lose its purchasing power in internal trade also. The Reichsbank no longer has sufficient foreign currency for industry and State to procure necessities abroad, especially coal and food. The desperation of the masses is being exploited by radical agitators from both Left and Right."

**Wheat Crisis**
- Wheat crop rather good — scarcity caused by **farmers' refusal to accept paper**
- From Dresden to Coblenz: new high denomination notes awaited anxiously

**Fiscal Breakdown**
- July 11-20: floating debt increased 28,000 → 40,000 milliard marks — daily issue 2,000 milliards
- Of 12,000 milliard expenditure: only **4% from taxation** — not even enough to cover interest
- **High official to British Ambassador**: "Wir schiessen dieselben Böcke wie die Österreicher" — "We shoot the same goats as the Austrians"

## Austria's Contrasting Recovery

**Geneva Protocols Success**
- **Dr. Zimmermann** took financial control; **Geneva Protocols** approved
- Austria undergoing "full throes of exorcism of devil of inflation — plainly the most unpleasant process"

**Austrian Unemployment Crisis**
- Aug 1922: 21,000 → Feb 1923: 161,000
- Over half in **Vienna** — more than 100,000 idle receiving maximum dole of 87,360 kronen (~5s/week)
- About **1/5 of population** dependent on government money
- Railways had **two ex-employees pensioners for every three workers** still on staff

**Economic Recovery Signs (March 1923 onwards)**
- Unemployment began falling substantially from March peak of 170,000
- August: down to 84,000 (53,000 in Vienna)
- **Krone rock-steady** — more stable against dollar than any other European currency
- June 1, 1923: long-term international loan was **huge, immediate success**

**Social Misery Despite Financial Soundness**
- Cost of living began creeping upwards from January, adding **3% in August alone**
- State officials in 1923: received **1/4 of 1914 spending power**
- State pensioners: **1/3 in real terms**

**Housing Crisis in Vienna**
- Rents kept so low, flats in short supply
- **42,000+ families** waiting for **6,000 or so dwellings** built yearly
- No new buildings put up by private developers — most virtually expropriated

**The Failed Experiment Metaphor**
> "Austria's reconstruction compared to experiment gipsy made to teach horse to do without food. When he got it down to a straw a day, the horse died."

## Chapter 11: Havenstein

**Cuno Cabinet Falls**
- **Dr. Gustav Stresemann** (Volkspartei) replaces Cuno
- **Right** suspects him of Socialist line; **Communists/patriotic organisations** less ruly than ever
- Whispers of military coup with **Ludendorff** as leader, **Hitler** as Bavarian deputy

**Havenstein's Catastrophic Policy**
- "No situation was so bad that summer that **Dr. Havenstein** could not make it worse"
- Held firmly **money supply unconnected with either price levels or exchange rates**
- Saw duty as supplying to limit of ability

**August 17 Speech Disaster**
- Announced Reichsbank would increase daily issues from 20,000 → 46,000 milliard marks daily
- Before speech: mark at 3 million/dollar, 12.5 million/pound
- Within 48 hours: fell to 5.2 million/dollar, 22 million/pound
- Circulation in August had exchange value of £9 million — less than 1/30 what it had been 10 years earlier
- **Lord D'Abernon**: "No one could anticipate such an ingenuous revelation of extreme folly"

**Tax System Breakdown**
- August receipts nominally 98x greater than April, in real terms worth half as much
- Aug 20-30 receipts: **0.7% of expenditure**

**Wage Chaos**
- Aug 13: firms gave wage rises of 5-15 million marks/week; bulk paid by cheque
- Average cash available: only 5 million (6 shillings)
- Aug 14: Wupperman employees surrounded directors' houses demanding 50 million marks (£3)/head
- Within week: tremendous unemployment setting in

**Berlin Paralysis**
- Printers' strike; large crowds with baskets/wheelbarrows surrounding Reichsbank demanding banknotes
- Currency "still current, nothing having replaced it; but hardly a serious measure of value"
- Berlin like besieged city — country districts stopped supplies of meat, eggs, vegetables

**Mathematical Impossibility of Commerce**
- Each morning: press published daily prices with **different multiplier for every trade**
- Purchase demanded 3-4 minutes calculation; several more to count notes
- Example multipliers (ordinary fare as base): Tramway ×50,000; Taxi-autos ×600,000; Bookshops ×300,000
- **Lord D'Abernon**: "Foreigner charged 1 million marks green fee at golf course — about a shilling. Housewife charged similar rates can make no such reflection."

**Interest Rate Farce**
- Aug 3: Reichsbank raised discount rate 18 → 30% per annum
- Market rate: 1% per day (365% annual)
- Current accounts showing 3-4%/week = 200%/year
- Should have set Bank rate at 360% to address reality

**Havenstein's Mission**
- Day and night: 30 paper mills, 150 printing firms, 2,000 presses toiling
- Private state banks permitted to issue up to 15,000 milliard marks as once-and-for-all limit
- Sept 1: 500 million mark note issued; new word **"billiard"** coined (three more noughts to milliard)

**Coalition Fractures**
- Without supplementary estimate for 6,000 milliard marks (£400,000) for Ruhr subvention, coalition began to founder
- **Rosenberg**: "Alternative is chaos with honour or chaos with dishonour"
- **August 8 Cuno speech**: warned imports curtailed, taxation "unrelenting" — but it was Cuno's last effort

**Plunder in Austria**
- **Anna Eisenmenger's diary from Linz**: 80-100 men/women plundering farm
- Cart of 3 slaughtered pigs, dead hens, cows slaughtered, udders slit
- Uncle estimates damage 100,000 peace kronen; no insurance would pay
- Peaceful Linz "looked as if visited by earthquake"

**Rye Loan and Bodenmark**
- Sept 18: plans for **Boden Credit Bank** (later Rentenbank) backed by mortgages on agricultural land and industry
- Fundamentally expedient to induce farmers to cooperate in feeding nation
- **Bodenmark** via **Kontomark** — units worth ten cents each
- Old fiction of **"Mark gleich Mark"** formally abandoned

**Final Dissolution**
- Sept 26: **Stresemann** suspended seven articles of Weimar constitution, declared State of Emergency
- **General von Seeckt** became supreme executive/administrative power
- Germany divided into seven military districts with local dictator over each
- Simultaneously: **President Ebert announced end of passive resistance in Ruhr**

## Chapter 12: The Bottom of the Abyss

**Price of Dictatorship**
- Restrictions on liberty, freedom of expression, press, association
- Death penalty for treason, arson, railway sabotage, leading armed mobs
- Bavaria appointed **Gustav von Kahr** as Commissioner-General with dictatorial powers
- Germany had **two dictators**: von Seeckt in Berlin, von Kahr in Bavaria

**Hyperinflation Accelerates**
- Last week Sept: Reichsbank issued 3,267 million million marks (£5.2 million exchange value per day)
- Mark's collapse: 315 million/pound (Sept 11) → 1,500 million (Oct 2) → 5,700 million (Oct 9)
- October 1923: purchasing power of average wage fell to **less than 20% of normal**
- **Lord D'Abernon**: "A beggar would hardly accept any smaller note"

**Psychological Collapse After Passive Resistance**
- Eight months propaganda suddenly abandoned — government admitted making fools of themselves
- Contempt for Republic became nearly universal
- **Joseph Addison**: "Population ripe to accept any system of firmness or any man who appears to know what he wants"
- Workers realized striking useless — employers welcomed it

**Return to Work in Occupied Zone**
- Oct 6: German government abandoned *Rhein-Ruhr-Hilfe* (aid to occupied area)
- Oct 7: industrialists (Stinnes, Prussian State Mines — not **Krupp** in jail) approached French about resuming work

**Currency Reform Schemes — Rentenmark**
- Oct 10: pound at 7 milliard officially, 18 milliard on free market
- Oct 15: official 18.5 milliard, free market into 40 milliards
- **Rentenmark Ordinance** published Oct 15 — *Bodenmark* rechristened
- Released Reichsbank from obligation to finance government

**Bavaria's Explosive Situation**
- **Von Kahr** and **von Lossow** in full control, calculatingly ignoring Berlin's instructions
- **Robert Clive**: "For practical purposes Bavaria is no longer part of the Reich"

**Economic Desperation Critical**
- Few families afford meat more than once weekly
- Eggs unprocurable, milk terribly scarce, bread 16x price of days earlier
- **Herr von Haniel**: "Desperate economic conditions mainly responsible. Things could not be worse. Demoralisation and irresponsibility rampant."

**Bremen's Gold Note Experiment**
- Oct 24: Bremen issued gold notes totaling 1 million dollars in denominations of quarter, half, one dollar
- Backed by all receipts and resources of Bremen
- Contrary to Gresham's Law: **better medium drove inferior out of circulation**

**Separatist Threats**
- Sept 30 Düsseldorf: 20,000 separatists killed 11 policemen
- Oct 21: **Rhenish Republic** proclaimed at Aachen — immediately quashed by Curzon's note to Belgium
- Separatist administrations declared at Bonn, Trier, Wiesbaden, Mainz
- French turned attention to **Palatinate**

**Hamburg Communist Uprising**
- Oct 15: authorities withdrew bread cards
- Week later: dockers/shipbuilders struck; police stations rushed, open shops plundered
- German cruiser *Hamburg* arrived, put ashore marines, arrested 800 Left-wing activists

**Currency Collapse Metrics — October**
- Oct 21: 24 milliards → 80 milliards in three days
- At 60 milliards: approximately **one mark for every second elapsed since birth of Christ**
- End month: banknote circulation = **2,496,822,909,038,000,000 marks**
- First billiard, 5-billiard, 10-billiard notes ready Nov 1

**Demand for Wages of Fixed Value**
- Hamburg: workers turned down offer of wage in gold mark notes
- Needed paper marks for living expenses — people hardly knew what they wanted
- Nov 1: Hamburg assembly voted 500,000 milliard marks (£2,000) relief

**National Unemployment Crisis**
- Same story all over Germany — worst in Ruhr
- **Krupp, Mannesmann, Gelsenkirchen, Phoenix, Prussian State Mines** dismissing thousands
- Trade unions: 18.7% members unemployed; another 40% on short time 4-40 hours/week
- Government paying relief to 877,262 in unoccupied Germany, short-time relief to 1,700,000

**Stresemann's November Prognosis**
- Prospect: 5 million workers without work, without support, largely without food
- More riots inevitable
- Rhineland: domain of lawlessness, anarchy, unrestrained terrorism
- Population wouldn't accept currency losing half value every successive day

**Saxony's Red Dictatorship**
- **Dr. Zeigner's** Communist ministers issued manifesto for red dictatorship
- Declined Berlin's ultimatum to restore order
- **"Most surely, the pips had squeaked"**

**Five Simultaneous Crises (Oct 30)**
- Oct 30: mark at 310 milliards/pound
- Berlin faced: **Bavaria, Saxony, Rhineland, Ruhr resumption, financial chaos**
- Ruhr outcome depended on NY negotiations for credits

**Hitler's Putsch and Context**
- Nov 1: Hitler's Bavarian levies mustering on Thuringia border
- Nov 2: violent Bourse panic — realization of **eight official kinds of paper** in circulation
- **Lord D'Abernon**: "Chaos the like of which had not been seen in economic history"

**Berlin Distress (Early November)**
- Nov 6: looting of foodshops with anti-Semitic overtones in poorer Berlin quarters
- **Mr. J.C. Vaughan** protest to Foreign Office:

- Every Zurich provision shop: placard "Send food parcels to friends in Germany"

**Beer Hall Putsch (Nov 8)**

- Persuaded many thousands fault lay with Armistice signers, French, Jews, Bolsheviks

**Martial Law and Currency Stabilization**
- Bloodletting in Bavaria hardly cooled passions
- First 100-billiard note already out
- **Havenstein's presses**: 74 million million million marks/week
- Cost-of-living index (1914 = 1): Sept average 15 million → Oct 3,657 million → Nov 12: 218,000 million

**Appeals from Occupied Territories**
- Women of Cologne appeal "To Women of British Empire":
> "During passive resistance we existed through paper doles. Now these ceased and we face starvation. Industry cannot recover, millions out of work... armed hordes of adventurers let loose on disarmed population in name of separatism... Winter is before us, and we have no coal."

**Abandoning the Occupied Zone**
- Government preparing to leave occupied zone to own fate
- Danger: end of aid would throw area into separatists' arms
- Even old age pensions to be abandoned

**Local Currency Initiatives**
- Solingen: gold-based marks with cover from foreign exchange balances
- Höchst dye works: second issue covered by 400,000+ Swiss francs in Zurich
- Could only afford 70 gold pfennigs (8½d or 20 cents) per hour
- Margin between skilled/unskilled reduced to ~10%

## The Rentenmark's Paradoxical Success

**The Anomalous Currency**
- **Rentenmark** not legal tender but "a legal means of payment"
- Not convertible to gold or assets backing it
- Paper mark remained legal tender, "mummified" but circulating
- Legal reduction not sanctioned until Aug 30, 1924: "Eine Billion Mark" → one Reichsmark

**Psychology of Stabilization**
- Success not from stopping presses, but from **fiscal discipline**
- **Bresciani-Turroni**: Public thought it different from paper mark despite being unconvertible paper
> "On basis of simple fact new paper money had different name, public thought it was something different. New money was accepted. It was held and not spent as rapidly."

**Dr. Schacht's Two Immediate Problems**
- **Black market**: dollar at 12,000 milliard marks (3x official) late November
- **Notgeld** (emergency money): threat to stability
- Nov 25 Cologne meeting: resisted industrialists wanting Notgeld acceptance restored

**Crushing the Black Market**
- Speculators closing books lacked money to meet commitments
- Credits formerly gladly given no longer available
- Those who bought dollars at 12,000 milliard forced to sell at 4,200 milliard — lost 2/3 of outlay
- Before Dec 1: £10 million in foreign currency returned to Bank
- Speculators fled to Paris — first signal stabilization was real

**Dr. Schacht's Operation**
- Worked from former charwoman's cupboard in Ministry of Finance
- **Fräulein Steffeck**: "He sat and smoked in little dark room. Did he read letters? No. Write letters? No. He telephoned a great deal. And he smoked. We did not eat much. We usually went home late, third class. Apart from that he did nothing."

**Food Shortages Persist**
- Three days after stabilization, no more food than before
- Berlin's daily milk: 1.8M litres → 130,000 litres
- Butter unobtainable, too expensive for most

**Stresemann's Fall**
- Nov 23: Reichstag voted Ministry down on no-confidence motion
- **Stresemann** came to depose **Havenstein** — Havenstein died Nov 20 (stabilization day)
- **Hilferding** achieved nothing; little sign **Dr. Luther** would achieve more

**Signs of Sanity**
- Agreement between Ruhr mine-owners and **MICUM**
- Postal charges on gold basis
- 10% railway personnel dismissal
- Miners: 8-hour day underground; State officials back to 9-hour day
- Official salaries reduced below pre-war levels

**Unemployment Explosion**
- Christmas 1923: registered unemployed 1.5M+ in unoccupied Germany
- Real total probably 1M more
- Majority on short-time or reduced pay
- Workers accepted employment on any terms; employers took advantage

**Government's Limited Options**
- Made **Schacht** President of Reichsbank for life
- Only half officials' salaries paid week ending Dec 17
- Budgetary deficit: 99% pre-stabilization → 92% after Nov 20 → 44% last ten days Dec
- February 1924: budget finally balanced

**Rentenmark's Greatest Triumph — Food Returns**
- Beginning to appear in towns mid-December
- Before November: only increase in animals slaughtered was dogs
- After stabilization: consumption of every daily need increased regularly except dogmeat
- **Lord D'Abernon** (Christmas Day 1923):
> "Not even most fanatical advocate of stabilisation could have anticipated more remarkable results. Food abundant in great towns; potatoes and cereals in large quantities; butter now offered at stable if high prices. Animals crowd abattoirs and queues disappeared. Economic détente brought political pacification — dictatorship and Putsches no longer discussed."

**Euphoria Fades**
- February 1924 **Dr. Kuczynski**: "Difference in public feeling compared with two months ago absolutely astounding. Then everyone depressed. Today full of confidence. Change mainly psychological."
- 1924: period of extreme monetary stringency consolidated financial recovery
- Too much to hope years of profligacy could be easily paid for
- Destitution of middle classes only part of price
- Economic and political reckonings still to come

## Chapter 14: Unemployment Breaks Out

**Schacht's Clear Assessment**
- Rentenmark could hold tide only so long
- New credits from abroad essential
- No departure from strictest discipline despite pleading
- Jan 24, 1924:
> "After long devaluation, stability can only be regained at cost of severe crisis. We are in midst of this crisis. External commerce at standstill. Balance of trade active only because imports ceased. Industry living on old stocks."

**Seeking Foreign Support**
- Saw **Lord Norman** about **Golddiskontbank** project to head off French-backed Rhineland bank
- End January: Paris for **Dawes Committee** hearings
- Icy meeting with **Poincaré** — France still insisting on pound of flesh

**Industrialists Turn to France**
- **Stinnes** more dangerously ready than most to agree to Paris proposals
- Guiding thought: patriot's first duty to make money, not become bankrupt

**Stabilization's Devastating Effects**
- Food importers especially hard hit
- Biggest importers ordered during depreciation hoping to repay in depreciated marks
- Found themselves threatened with bankruptcy, panicked, threw stocks on market

**January 1924: Glut in Cologne**
- "Abundant supplies" vegetables/fruit, German potatoes, eggs, butter
- Italian cauliflower, Canary tomatoes, Spanish grapes, American apples, French nuts
- Fortnight later: abundance become glut
- Mountains of Hawaiian pineapple, Colombian salmon, American corned beef
- Mass of population simply unable to buy

**Price Paradoxes**
- Food cheaper but few earning enough to buy
- Christmas: 1 billion marks bought 2-3 oranges
- February: same sum bought 20-30 from unemployed hawking streets
- British Embassy: "Poor not really getting proper amount of food, haven't enough money to profit from cheaper market"

**Hitler's Trial and Bavaria**
- Bavaria settled politically after **Hitler's 24-day trial** (Feb 2, 1924)
- Trial gave national platform, ended in minimum sentences
- **Mr. Clive**: "National Socialism thrived in depression of 1923. Hitler's greatest enemy is the Rentenmark. Six months stabilisation had calming effect on Nationalist hotheads."

**Middle Classes Destroyed**
- Story throughout urban Germany
- Whether hit harder end of 1923 vs. 1922 hard to say
- People on savings, pensions, disability grants, fixed incomes sold chattels or did small work
- Numbers amounted to millions
- Had wealth shot away by war without knowing it
- Charitable institutions had income reduced to trickle
- Industrial debenture holders lost capital — industries redeemed debts with worthless paper
- Share holders: capital reduced 3/4 by 1913

**Wealth Redistribution's Visible Evidence**
- Destitution not general
- Great wealth ostentatiously flaunted
- Misled French into supposing reparations refusal was Teutonic knavery
- Full employment, booming home market, competitive foreign position — all made possible by vast borrowing
- But inflation shed deadly rain discriminately

**Professional Classes Suffer**
- Rentier classes: judges, army officers, parliamentary deputies whose positions supplemented by private means
- Civil litigation became luxury — who would buy books? Who in hurry for architectural advice?
- Private patients slow to come forward
- Health-insurance companies couldn't pay full fees

**Partial Truth of Destitution**
- Not only middle classes going through mill
- Unorganised workers gone down with middle classes: tailors, cab-drivers, chimney-sweeps, domestic servants
- Painters, gardeners, chauffeurs, carpenters, shop assistants, printers, laundrywomen, cabinet-makers, porters
- Third group: people receiving small subsidies/pensions from governments
- Numbers (excluding unemployed) reckoned at end of year at **5,600,000+**

**Austrian Parallel**
- **Frau Eisenmenger** (December 1923):
> "Foodstuffs which three years ago entirely unobtainable can now be bought everywhere. But who can buy them? My holding in shares worth more than 10 million kronen, I am at wits end to know where to find money to buy food."

- Jan 2, 1924: full implications of reforms dawned:
> "For 15,000 kronen we get — one schilling! Thousands reduced to beggary. Old married couple had stock worth 2 million pre-war kronen bringing 80,000/year [£3,200]. Today brings 8 new schillings/year. Panic seized Stock Exchange. My millions dwindled to about thousand new schillings. We belong to new poor. Middle class reduced to proletariate. I cannot go on."

**Hungary's Descent**
- 1923 dismal year — began with anti-Semitism outbreak January
- **Count Bethlen's** government under Right-wing nationalist influence
- Korona fell: 14,500 (March) → 92,000 (August) → 120,000/pound (October)
- Farmers hoarded corn and cattle
- October 1923: Reparations Commission did for Hungary what done for Austria year before
- December: Protocols in Geneva, **Mr. Jeremiah Smith** to Budapest as League's Commissioner-General
- 1925 currency reform: 12,500 korona → one new pengo

**Germany's Minimal Restitution**
- Feb 14, 1924 decree: industrial debentures/mortgages revalued at 15% of original gold price
- Further law July 1925 raised mortgage revaluation to 25%
- 1924 decree imposed modest tax on inflation profits: under 2% of original gold value
- Move to revalue government loans became law 1925: stockholders received 2½% of original investment

**Brief Economic Recovery**
- Economic recovery, interest rates fell 100% (Jan) → 30% (May)
- Unemployed reduced to half by April 1924
- **Schacht** intervened with drastic credit restrictions
- Stability restored, but not without higher interest rates, more bankruptcies, more unemployment

**Second False Dawn**
- Dawes Committee presented plan, adopted August
- Reparations paid only to extent currency could stand strain
- Large foreign loan, evacuation of French/Belgians from Ruhr
- Once more: immediate confidence return
- December 1924: registered unemployed as low as 436,000 — would never be so few again until Hitler's arrival

**Post-Stabilization Depression**
- Germany's trouble: inflation boom never liquidated
- Vast enterprises established/expanded during plenty became unproductive when capital grew short
- More realistic transport, fuel, food prices; wages raised substantially
- Firms found real interest paid on loans for first time positive rather than negative
- Obliged to pay real taxes for first time

**The Stinnes Débâcle**
- Huge stocks of unsold iron/coal in Ruhr
- Pit after pit forced to close
- **Hugo Stinnes** deceived by artificial prosperity into fanatical confidence in coal's future
- June 1925: Stinnes empire collapse
- Event pricked the abscess
- Defects of "vertical" industrial concentrations revealed
- Speculators found they had to pay for folly, improvidence, greed

**Final Collapse**
- Germany crashed downwards again just as people supposed with international help she was rising
- Confidence shattered
- Flow of foreign money slackened
- Reichsbank credit restriction maintained
- Alarming rise in cost of living
- Unemployed passed 1,300,000 by December 1925, gathering pace daily

**The Frozen Capital Problem**
- Inflationary period: new factories built, establishments reorganised
- Too late found this undermined capital structure
- Capital frozen in factories for which there was no economic demand
- Once demand shut off and cash flow dammed: productive apparatus fate sealed
- 1913: 7,700 bankruptcies; 1924: 5,700; 1925: 10,800

## Post-Stabilization Crisis (1925-1926)

**Wave of Bankruptcies**
- Q3 1925 - Q2 1927: **31,000 bankruptcies** — rate of 15,000/year
- Protested bills/week doubled 2,691 → 5,406 between May-November 1925
- Banks immobilized by bad loans, unwilling to liquidate securities
- Stock market moribund — endless sellers, no buyers

**Mass Unemployment Returns**
- December 1925: **genuine mass unemployment** — nightmare 1922's policies sought to avoid
- **Krupp** dismissed 12,000; similar scale at **Mannesmann, Gelsenkirchen, Phoenix**
- **Opel** dismissed 5,000 of 7,000 workers
- Locomotive industry: 2% of pre-war output
- 145 bankruptcies in textile industry (first half November)
- Shipbuilding at half pre-war employment
- **February 1926**: registered unemployed exceeded **2 million**
- Average stayed above 2 million throughout 1926

**Professional Class Recovery**
- By May 1926: professional classes recovered radically
- By 1927: living standards very high during prosperity boom
- **Exception**: legions of unemployables and hundreds of thousands without work bore "outward scars" of inflation

## Chapter 15: The Wounds are Bared

**Psychological Trauma**
- Inflation's scourge followed defeat's scourge
- Those who lived through attributed **fear, greed, immorality, demoralization, dishonor** primarily to inflation

**Judith Listowel on Hungary**
- Anti-Semitism negligible before inflation
- When Jews made success of inflation, they were hated; ostentatious about it, hated more
- Wiser Jews greatly upset, foreseeing antagonism
> "My relations and friends too stupid. They didn't understand what inflation meant. Didn't rush to get rid of money (that's what Jews and Germans did). All my relations thought it would stop next week."

**Rise of Anti-Semitism**
- Jews badly treated in Hungary since 1860s
- Nine out of ten bore grudges
- When immigrant gentiles arrived, who to blame for taking opportunities?
- **"The fault did not lie with Jews but somehow much higher up"**

**Corruption in Hungarian Civil Service**
- Elite branches maintained integrity and suffered for it
- **Real corruption at county level** — entirely new phenomenon
- When father protested irregularities, reply: *"Your Excellency, will you feed my children?"*
- **Communal hatred was new. Social resentment was new. Bribery and corruption: that was new.**

**German Corruption**
- **Lord D'Abernon** (1924): corruption "appalling" — before war, bribery almost unknown
- From tax-evasion, food-hoarding, speculation — all crimes against State became survival matters
- Where lower classes turned to theft and prostitution (up 50% in 1923 over 1913/1925), middle/upper resorted to graft and fraud
- **Corruption bred corruption**

**Moral Decay**
- Old virtues of thrift, honesty, hard work lost appeal
- Speculation could yield far greater rewards than labor
- **Patriotism, social obligations, morals slid away**
- **The ethic cracked**
- Not being able to hold what one had was real basis of human despair

**Personal Testimonies**
- **Erna von Pustau**: "It is ordinary conditions of our life that make a woman evil"
- **Frau von Eisenmenger**:
> "Growing lack of consideration for one's fellow men impresses me painfully. I can understand instinct of self-preservation in people whose existence threatened should overcome all moral laws."

**1924 Scandals**
- March: **Dr. Zeigner** (ex-Premier Saxony) sentenced for corrupt practices
- End of year: formidable array of financial scandals confirmed old integrity had sunk

**The Barmat Affair**
- **Barmat and Kutisker affairs** rocked country
- **Iwan Kutisker**: Lithuanian Jew who recognized inflation principles immediately
- Acquired bank, became financially influential
- **Julius Barmat**: notorious swindler, Russian Jew of Lithuanian extraction

**Profiteering Schemes**
- **Inflation profiteering**: borrowing paper, converting to goods/factories, repaying with depreciated paper
- **Deflation profiteering**: selling everything for stable marks, lending at extravagant interest in period of tightest credit

**The Arbitration Scandal**
- Bank president **von Dombois** and directors (**Rühe, Hellwig**) arbitrated Barmat-Michael dispute
- Received nearly **40,000 gold marks**
- Large credits from *Seehandlung* (Prussian State Bank) began flowing to Barmat and Kutisker
- Two families borrowed **50 million gold marks** at 10-18% and re-lent at 100-200%

**Unraveling**
- Bank president and two directors apprehended after Christmas 1924
- *Seehandlung* loss: **15 million marks**
- **Reich Post Office** advanced Julius Barmat **15 million gold marks**
- **Dr. Höfle** (Minister of Posts) resigned Jan 9
- **300 police** in 30-40 arrests — 13 State officials

**High-Level Involvement**
- Trail to higher corridors: confidential report disappeared from Foreign Office
- Minute from **Reichspräsident Ebert** asking help for Barmat
- Though Ebert only implicated by rumor, his son deeply involved
- **Addison**: "Good friend 'der brave Joseph' [**Dr. Wirth**, ex-Chancellor] is in it up to the hilt... To name the others would be complete list of all Social Democrat leaders and most highly placed officials."

**Nationalist Involvement**
- Sums: upwards of **£2 million in bribes** over and above loans
- Miserable story echoed four years later in **Sklarek Brothers scandal** (bribery of Berlin municipality)
- Episode indicated advanced corruption doubtless due to general demoralization from prolonged chaos and inadequate salaries

## Post-Stabilization Germany (1925-1927)

**Two Major Events (1925)**
- **Hindenburg** succeeded as President (April)
- **Treaty of Locarno** signed (December)
- Neither viewed with more than minority approval

**American Loans and Extravagance**
- **£200 million (1924-1926)**, £1,000 million by 1929
- Induced spirit of state/municipal/personal extravagance
- **Parker Gilbert**: How will foreign debts ever be repaid?
- **Dr. Stresemann** to Mayor of Duisburg:
> "Fact Prussian State granted 14 million marks rebuilding Berlin Opera House creates impression in world we are rolling in money. Please tell me what I am to say to foreign representatives when they tell me these things awaken impression Germany won rather than lost the war."

**Recovery and Confidence**
- Traders asserted business unprofitable owing to heavy State charges
- But remained deep satisfaction at improvement, firm confidence life would be better
- **Savings habit returned** to people who learned to rid themselves of cash at earliest opportunities
- **Habit of borrowing** set in more fiercely than ever

**Public Spending Spree**
- States and municipalities making up for what missed during inflation
- Much expenditure on public building to mop up unemployment
- Soon easy long-term loans replaced by stiffer short-term ones
- Before long, even peasants as badly in debt as municipalities
- **Public/private light-headedness about money matters one of legacies of inflation** — deadly one

**1926 Prosperity Observations**
- German middle/lower classes had money to spend freely
- Able to travel in multitudes
- All well and newly (though hideously) clad
- Cafés and amusements full
- Museums, galleries, castles crowded with trippers paying entrance
- Festivals organized by governments, expensive new exhibition buildings
- **Stresemann's fears of jaundiced foreign eyes entirely justified**

**False Prosperity as Prelude to Depression**
- Second onset of massive economic self-deception within decade
- In this case flowering over great unemployment below
- Poor psychological prelude to slump to come
- Inflation experience ensured emergencies of depression couldn't be met with any monetary flexibility
- **"Easy enough to understand why record of sad years 1919-1923 always weighs like nightmare on German people"**

**Survival of Democracy and Rise of Authoritarianism**
- Democracy may have survived inflation, but little evidence of universal gratitude
- **Monarchism more popular creed**
- **Lord D'Abernon**:
> "If one drives Sunday afternoon through any German province there is always same sight — men of every age similarly dressed, marching rigidly in rank, accompanied by bands and banners. They represent every school of political thought. Not astonishing there are great numbers who can think of foreign politics only in terms of war."

**Preparing for Future War**
- Aggressive posturing of extreme, reactionary, militarist groups cooled somewhat
- But party discipline strong; political strength of Nationalist parties grew steadily
- **Hindenburg's election** gave fillip to Nationalist and monarchist movements

**General Wauchope's Warning**
- Memorandum beginning 1927: Germany morally mobilized for war in future to right wrongs of Versailles
> "Greatest loss which Germany suffered was ruin of her middle classes. If 'natural leaders' now in present party of Right, Germany may again become danger. Great numbers of factories could be rapidly reorganised for war material production."

**German Productivity and Militarization**
- Output per man: 40% below US, only 90% of pre-war, but **40% above Britain**
- Deduced fair chance of fighting new war "within life of this generation"
- German laborer worked considerably longer for less than British, unremittingly
- For French: existence of **60 million industrious and martially-minded people** overflowing with self-justification was serious alarm
- Ill-suppressed revanchism and expert nuclear **Reichswehr** which **von Seeckt** salvaged were additional reasons for fears

## Epilogue: Lessons from Hyperinflation

**The Pre-1914 Financial Mindset**
- **Havenstein** and Reichsbank only understood financial conditions before 1914
- Mild inflation has therapeutic advantages for democracies
- Most electorates accept politicians' statements about controlling prices
- Modern currencies lose significant value anyway
> "Once inflation well under way, 'it develops powerful lobby that has no interest in rational arguments.'"

**The Political Impossibility of Stopping**
- No moment between 1914 and summer 1923 when currency stability could not theoretically have been secured
- Until late date, German gold/foreign currency reserves constituted substantial proportion of paper's exchange value

**Mid-1920 Turning Point**
- After brief post-Kapp stability, export competitiveness declined, unemployment began rising
- Recovery required immediate repercussions: bankruptcies, redundancies, unemployment, strikes, hunger, demonstrations, violence, fear of insurrection
- Point not lost on inflators — stabilization postponed
- Day by day through 1920-1922, reckoning postponed, more readily as consequences became more frightening
- **"The longer the delay, the more savage the cure"**

**Pattern Across Austria, Hungary, Germany**
- Austria by end 1922: in hands of receivers, regained stable currency only under absolute direction of foreigner
- Hungary passed chance of self-redemption
- Germany returned under military dictatorship with much of constitution suspended
- **Common pattern**: All required bailout by others; each accepted greater disruption than feared when excessive printing might have been stopped; in all three, after inflation reached advanced stage, disaster became prerequisite of recovery

**The Take-Off Point into Hyperinflation**
- Take-off not found on graphs of depreciation, circulation velocity, or balance of payments
- Nor did it coincide with ultimate crisis of confidence
- **What really broke Germany: constant taking of soft political option on money**
- **Take-off point was moral, not financial**
- No imaginable circumstances more unsuited to imposing new financial order than November 1923
> "Inflation was no longer an option. Rentenmark itself hardly more than expedient then, could scarcely have been introduced successfully had not mark lost its entire meaning."

**The Nature of Money and Value**
- Money only useful when acknowledged by more than one person
- Once no one acknowledged it, Germans learned their paper money had no value
- **The discovery that shattered society**: traditional repository of purchasing power had disappeared
- No means left of measuring worth of anything
- Life became obsessional search for *Sachwerte* (things of real, constant value)
- **Stinnes** bought factories; railway workers bought gewgaws
- Degree of necessity became sole criterion of value
- **Man's values became animal values**
> "Contrary to any philosophic assumption, it was not a salutary experience."

**Hierarchy of Human Needs**
- What is precious sustains life
- When life secure, society acknowledges value of luxuries
- When life insecure or conditions harsh, values change
- For destitute in Germany and Austria: existence came near metaphysical conceptions

**In Hyperinflation**
- In war, boots or seat in boat more vital than untold millions
- In hyperinflation:
  - Kilo of potatoes worth more than family silver
  - Side of pork more than grand piano
  - Prostitute in family better than infant corpse
  - Theft preferable to starvation
  - Warmth finer than honour
  - Clothing more essential than democracy
  - **Food more needed than freedom**
