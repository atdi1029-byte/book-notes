Prologue

The Gap in the Literature

Economic analyses of inflation ignore the human element; historical accounts underestimate inflation as a powerful engine of social upheaval. The agony of inflation is like acute pain— totally absorbing while it lasts, entirely forgotten when it ends. This book attempts to recover that lived experience before it disappears from memory.

The Four Villains

The book introduces what it calls the four villains of 1923: Ludendorff, the soulless ex-Quartermaster-General who became a rallying point for reactionary forces; Hugo Stinnes, the plutocratic profiteer owing allegiance only to Mammon; Rudolf Havenstein, the Reichsbank president whose sound mind made no difference to the wreckage he wrought; and Adolf Hitler, the power-hungry demagogue calling forth all evil in human nature. But the German people were the victims.

The scale of the catastrophe defies comprehension. By October 1923, the number of marks to the pound equalled the number of yards to the sun. Dr. Schacht noted that at war’s end, a man could theoretically buy 500 billion eggs; five years later, the same amount of money could not buy a single one. When stability finally returned, the paper marks needed to buy one gold mark equalled the number of square millimeters in a square kilometer.

The book draws primarily on British Foreign Office records—dispatches from the embassy under Lord D’Abernon, the book’s most important witness. A special thread runs through the parallel experiences of Austria, Hungary, Russia, and Poland, always focused on inflation’s effect on individuals and nations. This is “a moral tale”—proof of the revolutionary axiom that to destroy a nation you must first corrupt its currency.

1. Gold for Iron

The Mark’s Slow Death

Just before the First World War in 1913, the German mark, British shilling, French franc, and Italian lira all traded at roughly equivalent values—four or five to the dollar. By the end of 1923, it was possible to exchange a single shilling for up to one trillion marks. In practice, by then no one was willing to accept marks in exchange for anything. The mark was dead, reduced to one millionth of its former self, and it had taken almost ten years to die.

The mark’s fall began gradually during the war. Its foreign exchange value halved, then halved again by August 1919. By early 1920, the cost of living had risen ninefold since 1914, yet the mark retained only one-fortieth of its overseas purchasing power. Then it sped downwards with gathering momentum, dragging social misery and political disruption in its wake. Not until 1923 did the currency finally go over “the cliff-edge of sanity.”

That year of galloping inflation brought financial phenomena never before observed. The death of the old mark was a merciful release, but the events surrounding it ensured Germany would undergo appalling rigors of financial reconstruction. Re-establishing monetary sanity bankrupted thousands and robbed millions of their livelihoods. Inflation aggravated every evil and ruined every chance of national revival. It produced conditions in which extremists of both Right and Left could raise the mob against the state, set class against class, race against race.

The Dollar Goes Up

One of inflation’s most insidious features emerges through Erna von Pustau, daughter of a small Hamburg businessman: people said “the dollar is going up” rather than “the mark is falling.” Since the mark was constantly going up in nominal figures—and so were prices—this was much more visible than the realization that money’s value was going down.

“It all seemed just madness, and it made the people mad.” — Erna von Pustau

The German revolution of 1918 originated as a military mutiny, but its deeper support rested on the financial calamities soldiers and their families were already experiencing. The Supreme Command exculpated itself by cultivating the myth of the Dolchstoss—the stab in the back by civilians. President Ebert greeted returning troops at the Brandenburger Tor with “I salute you, who return unvanquished from the field of battle.” This myth poisoned the democratic political evolution at its heart and permanently generated right-wing discontent. What genuinely did not occur to the Supreme Command was that the military breakdown stemmed as much from economic mismanagement as anything else.

Borrowing Instead of Taxing

Germany had financed its war almost entirely through borrowing rather than taxation. Karl Helfferich, State Secretary for Finance, announced in the Reichstag in March 1915 that the war would be financed exclusively by loans. The critical mechanism was the Reichsbank’s permission to count Treasury bills as note-coverage, allowing unlimited money printing. Total war expenditure reached 164 billion marks. Britain, by contrast, had taxed industries and groups who prospered from war— Schacht called it “socially more equitable.” By war’s end, money in circulation had increased fivefold. All stock exchanges were closed, foreign exchange rates unpublished. Only when censorship lifted did the economic disaster become clear.

2. Joyless Streets

Vienna’s Collapse

While Germany’s inflation was still in its early stages, Austria’s was already devastating. The Treaties of St. Germain and Trianon had dismantled the Habsburg Empire entirely, reducing Austria to a fraction of its former size. Vienna, once an imperial capital of grandeur, became a vast city without adequate hinterland, forbidden from Anschluss with Germany. The British Treasury’s G.M. Young reported to Lord Curzon that the Treaty had created a republic from “an oddly mixed community of bankers and peasants” and made it nominally responsible for the cost of a great war.

The Austrian constitution was described as a “super-democracy, contrived to ensure that as little authority as possible is exercised by anyone.” The president was a purely ceremonial figure who “opens flower shows and legitimates bastards.” Price collapse was swift and brutal: a kilogram of wheat flour that cost 44 heller in 1914 Vienna was unobtainable by December 1918; an indefinable flour mixture from black-market dealers cost 22 kronen—exactly fifty times as much.

The early Greta Garbo film The Joyless Street (1925), made in Vienna by director Pabst, as cultural evidence. The film depicts an odious butcher taunting queues at his shop, refusing meat to women he found unattractive or unwilling, while unlicensed speculators gorged at gluttonous nightlife revels—until a starving crowd finally attacks a café full of merrymakers. The film is “a faithful reflection of the times.”

Anna Eisenmenger’s Diary

The most powerful witness is Anna Eisenmenger, a middle-class Viennese widow whose diary provides a running chronicle of inflation’s scourge. Her family was a microcosm of Austria’s agony: a war-blinded son, a tubercular daughter, a son-in-law with amputated legs, a hungry grandson, a Communist son. She progressively turned to illegal practices to survive. “Hamstering”—hoarding food—became “no more than common sense” though it was an indictable offense.

A bank official warned her to convert her savings to Swiss francs. She refused, trusting government securities. He later told her bluntly:

“My dear lady, where is the State which guaranteed these securities to you? It is dead.” — Viennese bank official to Anna Eisenmenger

The barter economy took hold: a doctor’s wife exchanged a beautiful piano for a sack of wheat flour; Frau Eisenmenger traded her husband’s gold watch for four sacks of potatoes. A farmer eyed her grand piano: “If you’ll give it to me, you shall have all you want for three months.” By November 1919, mobs attempted to set the Parliament building on fire, and horses were slaughtered in the Ringstrasse, the warm bleeding flesh dragged away by the crowd.

3. The Bill Presented

The Paradox of Prosperity

In Germany by 1920, the paradox of inflation was in full view. A British consul in Leipzig reported that wages and the cost of living had reached equilibrium, with purchasing power nearly equal to pre-war levels. Saxon industry was in “very satisfactory condition.” But this apparent industrial health confused the inflation issue: currency depreciation made German industry highly competitive abroad while devastating the middle class on fixed incomes. A salary of 10,000 marks per year—comfortable before the war—was now inadequate for bare necessities.

The June 1920 elections raised two storm-cones for the future. The far-right DNVP trebled its vote and the Independent Socialists on the far left doubled theirs, while the moderate coalition lost much voter confidence. Money in circulation had exploded from 2,700 million marks at the war’s outbreak to 27,000 million at the Armistice and 77,000 million by November 1920. As the mark briefly improved, unemployment rapidly rose to six percent— making full employment the government’s primary objective at the mark’s expense.

Lord D’Abernon arrived as British Ambassador in June 1920, “more practically versed in money matters than most in any office in that city.” He watched helplessly as “the chickens of unlimited deficit financing swarmed in to roost.” His arithmetic was devastating: the German budget barely balanced, leaving nothing for war indemnities. Raising enough for reparations would require twice the revenue from the latest tax proposals— “altogether impossible without producing a revolution.” By October 1920, Germany’s national debt at old 1914 parities would have been £14,400 million; at new exchange rates it was only £1,200 million. Germany’s national debt had effectively been wiped out a year before the great inflation even began.

The London Ultimatum

The Reparations Commission fixed Germany’s total liability at 132 billion gold marks in the London Ultimatum of April 1921, with annual payments of 2 billion gold marks plus 26% of exports. The threat of occupying the Ruhr drove the mark to 268 to the pound and brought down the Fehrenbach government. Dr. Wirth became Chancellor and accepted the terms, knowing heavy additional taxation was required. Meanwhile, over 300 left-wing leaders had been assassinated since the Armistice and no one was punished.

4. Delirium of Milliards

Rathenau’s Warning

Walther Rathenau coined the phrase “delirium of milliards” to describe how Germany’s financial authorities had lost contact with reality. “The majority of statesmen and financiers think in terms of paper,” he warned. “They sit in their offices and look at papers which are lying in front of them, and on those papers are written figures which again represent papers. They write down noughts, and nine noughts mean a milliard.”

“Does a wood contain a milliard leaves? If the Tiergarten were cleared and wheat sown, two milliards of stalks might grow.” — Walther Rathenau

The delirium was not of the people but of those controlling the country’s finances.

The breaking point came in August 1921 when a close confederate of Hugo Stinnes named Mannheimer— described as the “confidential man” of Reichsbank President Dr. Rudolf Havenstein—was sent to England to buy foreign currency at any price. Germany had any amount of paper marks but no foreign currency. This was the first signal of absolute breakdown. Four reasons were identified for the deliberate depreciation policy: enabling heavy industry to compete abroad, satisfying workers with worthless wage increases, avoiding political troubles, and proving to the world that Germany could not fulfil the Versailles Treaty.

Two days after the nakedly political Frontkämpfertag—a march-past of war veterans under Prince Eitel Friedrich with generals Ludendorff, Waldersee, and von der Goltz delivering revanchist speeches to roaring cheers—Mathias Erzberger was murdered by Nationalist gunmen in the Black Forest. Erzberger was a civilian, a Republican, and a Jew. A Belgian deputy observed that “assassination seems now to have become the rule in Germany.” The right was hunting for culprits responsible for the national downfall, and over 300 left-wing assassinations had gone unpunished.

The Vicious Cycle

Joseph Addison of the British Embassy filed a dispatch that crystallized the vicious cycle: daily creation of fresh paper money decreases the mark’s purchasing value, leading to fresh demands which bring further decline, ad infinitum. He reported that hardly a single German of either sex was not speculating in foreign currencies—Austrian crowns, Polish marks, even Kerensky roubles. Capital was fleeing the country through under-invoiced exports and over-invoiced imports, arrangements that had the additional advantage of avoiding German taxation. By mid-November 1921, a pound sterling bought 1,040 paper marks.

5. The Slide to Disaster

Blackett’s Memorandum

Sir Basil Blackett of the British Treasury presented a sobering memorandum in late November 1921. The German man in the street, despite robust common sense, was beginning to believe what industrialists told him: that it was good for trade when a government habitually spent more than its income through inflationary finance. Each class wanted taxation to fall on other classes. Big industrialists were converting paper marks into foreign currencies or real assets while the incentive to save—when saving had become a vital necessity for the State—had been destroyed.

The Middle Class Destroyed

The fall in the mark was gradually wiping out the middle classes. A million marks in German War Loan, worth £45,000 at purchase, was now worth only £1,000 sterling. A German pensioner with 10,000 marks of pre-war income now had £10 in exchange value and less than £30 in purchasing power. Insurance policies were worth less than the annual premiums paid on them. Rent restriction acts forced the middle classes “to starvation in order to subsidise the German workman’s wages and the employer’s profits.” Foreigners were buying up real property, factory interests, furniture, and pianos—a gradual process of carrying off Germany’s movable capital.

In the occupied zones, British Consul-General Paget Thurstan observed queues outside shops, wholesale prices exceeding retail by nearly 100 percent, and wage settlements that were merely ephemeral. Chancellor Wirth presided over a Cabinet meeting in semi-darkness due to a partial electrical workers’ strike, warning that elements for disturbances and revolution were present. Food riots were taking place in Berlin. The mark dropped to 1,300 to the pound.

Vienna Explodes

Vienna exploded on December 2, 1921, when 30,000 unarmed demonstrators wrecked and looted food shops, restaurants, cafés, and hotels. The crowd penetrated Sir William Goode’s apartment, and he held them off for an hour from a room where English and American ladies had taken refuge. Demonstrators demanded seizure of all foreign currency, state control of the securities market, and all gold transferred to state hands including church holdings. The Chancellor gave ground on everything except food subsidies. Then Austria settled back into its characteristic lethargy— rich nightlife growing more frenzied while retired barristers and ex-generals found “stone-breaking beside the Danube.”

6. Summer of ’22

Moral Rot

By the summer of 1922, the moral rot was setting in alongside the financial collapse. Rathenau was assassinated on June 24, and the mark—which had briefly stabilized—plunged anew. Ernest Hemingway visited the German border town of Kehl and left vivid dispatches about the surreal disconnect between German prices and real purchasing power. The rise of anti-Semitism was becoming impossible to ignore, as desperate populations sought scapegoats for their misery.

The Geneva Protocols

Austria’s crisis reached its own crescendo. Chancellor Seipel made a desperate tour of European capitals seeking help, warning that Austria was on the brink of total collapse. The result was the Geneva Protocols of October 1922—an international rescue package that placed Austria under League of Nations financial oversight. Dr. Zimmermann took control of Austrian finances. The cure worked, but it was brutal: unemployment surged from 21,000 in August 1922 to 161,000 by February 1923, with over half concentrated in Vienna. About one-fifth of the population became dependent on government money. The railways had two pensioners for every three workers still on staff.

The krone stabilized and held—more stable against the dollar than any other European currency. But state officials received only one-quarter of their 1914 spending power, and pensioners only one-third. The housing crisis in Vienna was acute: 42,000 families waited for roughly 6,000 dwellings built yearly, and no private developers would build since landlords had been virtually expropriated. The metaphor circulating was of the gypsy who taught his horse to do without food: when he got it down to a straw a day, the horse died.

7. The Paper Chase

Hungary’s Descent

Hungary experienced its own parallel descent. Dr. Hegedüs and later Dr. Kallay struggled to manage a currency that was collapsing along the same trajectory as Austria’s krone and Germany’s mark. Anti-Semitism was more violent in Hungary than in either of the other countries, and the political situation under Count Bethlen’s government was further complicated by right-wing nationalist pressure.

Mark Gleich Mark

In Germany, the autumn of 1922 brought the “paper chase.” The Mark gleich Mark principle—the legal fiction that a mark was a mark regardless of when it was issued—benefited farmers and debtors enormously. A farmer could borrow to buy land and repay the loan weeks later with a fraction of its real value. The academic and professional classes, by contrast, were devastated. For the first time, wages began falling behind prices rather than roughly keeping pace.

Notgeld and the Printing Presses

The printing presses accelerated. Notgeld —emergency currency—proliferated wildly, with over forty issuing bodies in the occupied territories alone. Towns paid employees with their own locally printed currency: Krefeld, Coblenz, Düsseldorf, Bonn, and Cologne, where the emergency notes bore the signature of the young Konrad Adenauer. The stock market peaked in paper terms in October 1922, but in real terms it was a disaster—the share price index had fallen to three one-hundredths of its 1913 level. The Daimler Motor Company could have been bought for the price of 327 of its own cars.

8. Autumn of ’22

Prices by the Hour

Erna von Pustau captured the daily absurdity: by the end of the year, her allowance was worth less than one cup of coffee. A baker sold rolls for 20 marks in the morning and 25 marks in the afternoon. Milk rose from 78 to 202 marks per pound in November; butter from 800 to 2,000; eggs from 22 to 30 marks each. Foreign buying of German assets began in earnest as speculators realized they could acquire factories and real estate with cheap credit.

The Road to the Ruhr

The Fourth London Conference in December saw Poincaré tell Bonar Law flatly: “Whatever happens, I shall advance into the Ruhr on January 15.” Lloyd George believed the French proposals showed a “sinister resolve to engineer German default to justify invasion.” Germany’s last-minute proposal for mark stabilization with a two-year moratorium was rejected. On January 9, 1923, the Reparation Commission declared Germany in voluntary default, and two days later General Degoutte dispatched troops to the Ruhr.

The economic impact was catastrophic. The Ruhr provided 85% of Germany’s coal, 80% of its steel and pig iron, 70% of its goods traffic, and 10% of its population. The mark fell from 35,000 to the pound at Christmas to 48,000 the day after the invasion, and to 227,500 by month’s end. Two million workers and six million people had to be supported by the rest of the country. Germany was denied coal, coke, iron, and steel and had to import them at rising world prices, while the exchequer lost tax revenue from a huge proportion of its industry.

9. Ruhrkampf

Unexpected Unity

The invasion of the Ruhr had an unexpected effect: it briefly unified Germany. Admiral Tirpitz declared that France had assumed the “historic role of welding divergent forces of our people into unity again.” Class hatred was suspended as industrialists imprisoned alongside their employees became national heroes. Erna von Pustau remembered the old patriotic songs being sung: “Even Father gave money to help the Ruhr. We were willing to suffer cold for our fatherland.”

Passive resistance (Ruhrkampf) was sustained at enormous cost. General Degoutte expelled 147,000 Germans; 5,764 railway workers and 17,237 dependants were removed. Over 2,000 were wounded and 376 killed during the occupation. Sabotage was widespread—bridges, junctions, and signals were dynamited; military trains derailed; ships sunk. Well-paid workers spent their annual holidays shooting French officers or blowing up supply trains. But reactionary movements also fed on the crisis. Hitler appeared in the Ruhr on a recruiting drive, and the French stimulated local Communists through Rote Hundertschaften (Red Centuries) to undermine German authorities from the other flank.

The Reichsbank threw large amounts of foreign bills onto the market in February, briefly recovering the mark from 50,000 to 20,000 per dollar, but note circulation was increasing 450 billion marks per week. When the government’s gold loan was under-subscribed by 75% in April, and Hugo Stinnes presented the Reichsbank with a demand for £93,000 in foreign bills, the intervention policy collapsed. Within 24 hours the mark was at 140,000 to the pound. Lord D’Abernon observed:

“Inflation is like a drug—fatal in the end, but gets its votaries over many difficult moments.” — Lord D’Abernon

A Nation of Speculators

Over one million Germans were engaged in exchange speculation by early 1923. Winkelbankiers —back-street currency operators—played a significant role in determining the daily exchange rate. The barter economy expanded: a cinema seat cost a lump of coal; a bottle of paraffin bought a shirt. Herr von der Osten kept his girlfriend for half a pound of butter per month in 1922, rising to a whole pound by summer 1923. A 5,000-mark cup of coffee would cost 8,000 marks by the time it was drunk. The human toll was visible in the shrunken-necked gentlemen in mended white collars and shiny suits, and the exhausted workmen queueing with big shopping bags to rush their wages to shops before prices rose again.

10. The Crumbling Bastion

Prices Beyond Adjustment

After May 20, 1923, prices exploded beyond any remaining capacity for adjustment. Eggs rose from 800 to 2,400 marks; milk from 1,800 to 3,800; flour from 2,400 to 6,600; pork from 10,400 to 32,000. In the Ruhr, salaries doubled while commodity costs trebled. Tradesmen could no longer establish prices and many simply shut their shops.

The Reichsbank lost 6 million gold marks in a single day of futile intervention in June. On June 26-27, the note circulation increased by 1,523,534,460,000 marks in one day. The nation of 60 million people could not raise £30 million per year to meet £80 million in expenditure. The Minister’s tool for adjusting taxation —a multiplier applied to income tax—was raised from 25 times in May to 40 times for August, but returns never kept pace with depreciation.

Havenstein believed the mark must be kept low “whatever it might do elsewhere,” and in the last days of June the mark sank from 600,000 to 800,000 per pound. Nearly 80 million gold marks were thrown away to satisfy speculators. By July 10, the free gold reserve had fallen to £35 million. If intervention continued at this pace, the Reichsbank faced bankruptcy within 60 days. On July 19 it suspended discounting of trade paper entirely, and by month’s end only £25 million in gold remained.

Farmers Refuse Paper

Workers demanded daily payment and strikes erupted across the country. Riots and plundering broke out in Breslau, Frankfurt, Rosenheim, and Potsdam. Wages were theoretically indexed to the cost of living with weekly adjustment, but in practice rises always lagged 15 days behind reality. The German Ambassador pleaded to Curzon that the mark was losing its purchasing power even in domestic trade, and that radical agitators from both Left and Right were exploiting mass desperation. Farmers refused to accept paper money, leaving wheat rotting in barns while cities faced famine. Of 12,000 billion marks in government expenditure, only 4% came from taxation—not even enough to cover interest on the debt.

11. Havenstein

The Man Who Made It Worse

Dr. Gustav Stresemann replaced the hapless Cuno as Chancellor in August 1923, but the situation was already beyond any normal political remedy. No situation that summer was so bad that Reichsbank President Havenstein could not make worse. Havenstein held firmly to the belief that the money supply was unconnected with either price levels or exchange rates, and saw his duty as supplying currency to the limit of his ability.

On August 17, Havenstein made a speech announcing the Reichsbank would increase daily note issues from 20,000 to 46,000 billion marks. Before the speech, the mark stood at 3 million per dollar; within 48 hours it had fallen to 5.2 million. Lord D’Abernon remarked:

“No one could anticipate such an ingenuous revelation of extreme folly.” — Lord D’Abernon on Havenstein’s speech

August tax receipts were nominally 98 times greater than April’s but in real terms worth half as much. By the end of August, receipts covered only 0.7% of expenditure.

Berlin was paralyzed. Printers went on strike while large crowds with baskets and wheelbarrows surrounded the Reichsbank demanding banknotes. Currency was still current—nothing having replaced it—but it had ceased to be a serious measure of value. Each morning the press published daily prices with a different multiplier for every trade: tramway fares multiplied by 50,000; taxis by 600,000; bookshops by 300,000. A foreigner was charged one million marks for a round of golf— about a shilling. A housewife, D’Abernon noted, could make no such comforting reflection.

State of Emergency

Day and night, 30 paper mills, 150 printing firms, and 2,000 presses worked to produce banknotes. The Reichsbank discount rate was raised to 30% per annum, but the market rate was 1% per day— 365% annually. On September 1, a 500-million-mark note was issued and a new word, billiard, was coined to add three more noughts to the milliard. Stresemann suspended seven articles of the Weimar constitution on September 26, declaring a State of Emergency. General von Seeckt became supreme executive power, and Germany was divided into seven military districts, each under a local dictator. Simultaneously, President Ebert announced the end of passive resistance in the Ruhr.

12. The Bottom of the Abyss

Two Dictators

Germany now had two dictators: von Seeckt in Berlin and Gustav von Kahr in Bavaria, appointed Commissioner- General with dictatorial powers. The death penalty was imposed for treason, arson, railway sabotage, and leading armed mobs. The mark’s collapse accelerated from 315 million per pound on September 11 to 1,500 million on October 2 to 5,700 million on October 9. The purchasing power of the average wage fell to less than 20% of normal.

The psychological collapse following the abandonment of passive resistance was devastating. Eight months of propaganda were suddenly reversed—the government admitted it had been making fools of itself. Contempt for the Republic became nearly universal. Joseph Addison reported:

“The population is ripe to accept any system of firmness or any man who appears to know what he wants.” — Joseph Addison, British Embassy Berlin

Workers realized that striking was useless; employers welcomed it.

On October 15, the Rentenmark Ordinance was published—a currency reform devised by Dr. Luther and Dr. Schacht that released the Reichsbank from its obligation to finance the government. But events outpaced the reform. In the last week of October, the mark stood at 310 billion to the pound. At 60 billion, it had represented approximately one mark for every second elapsed since the birth of Christ. Banknote circulation reached 2,496,822,909,038,000,000 marks. First billiard and ten-billiard notes were ready for November 1.

Five Crises at Once

Berlin faced five simultaneous crises: Bavaria, Saxony, the Rhineland, Ruhr resumption, and financial chaos. Separatist movements proclaimed a Rhenish Republic at Aachen. Hamburg’s Communists staged an uprising, rushing police stations and plundering shops until a German cruiser arrived and arrested 800 activists. On November 6, looting with anti-Semitic overtones broke out in poorer Berlin quarters. A British consular official protested to the Foreign Office after seeing three nearly moribund women: “Either last stages of decline or starvation—no doubt the latter. That we should tolerate such a state five years after the Armistice is appalling.”

Farmers’ barns were bursting with unsold food while Germany suffered widespread famine— the farmers simply refused to accept paper. Every provision shop in Zurich displayed placards reading “Send food parcels to friends in Germany.” Havenstein’s presses were now producing 74 million million million marks per week. The cost-of-living index, with 1914 as 1, rose from 15 million in September to 3,657 million in October to 218,000 million by November 12. Hitler exploited these miseries to drum up opposition to all authority. He “set his hopes in 1923 on the revolt of starving billionaires.” The Beer Hall Putsch on November 8 was the inevitable culmination.

13. Schacht

The Rentenmark Illusion

The Rentenmark was an anomalous currency— not legal tender but “a legal means of payment,” not convertible to gold or to the assets backing it. The old paper mark remained legal tender, “mummified” but still circulating. Its success was not monetary but psychological. As Bresciani-Turroni observed, on the basis of the simple fact that the new paper money had a different name, the public thought it was something different. It was accepted. It was held and not spent as rapidly. The legal reduction was not sanctioned until August 1924: one trillion old marks equalled one new Reichsmark.

Crushing the Speculators

Dr. Schacht faced two immediate problems: the black market, where the dollar traded at 12,000 billion marks (three times the official rate), and the proliferation of Notgeld that threatened stability. Working from a former charwoman’s cupboard in the Ministry of Finance, Schacht crushed the speculators by cutting off credit. Those who had bought dollars at 12,000 billion were forced to sell at 4,200 billion, losing two-thirds of their outlay. Before December 1, £10 million in foreign currency flowed back to the Reichsbank. Speculators fleeing to Paris was the first signal that stabilization was real.

His secretary Fraulein Steffeck recalled the operation with comic understatement: “He sat and smoked in his little dark room. Did he read letters? No. Write letters? No. He telephoned a great deal. And he smoked. We did not eat much.” But three days after stabilization there was still no more food than before. Berlin’s daily milk supply had collapsed from 1.8 million litres to 130,000. Butter was unobtainable, too expensive for most.

Stresemann fell on a no-confidence vote on November 23. In a grim coincidence, Havenstein died on November 20—the day of stabilization —just as Stresemann was coming to depose him. Schacht was made President of the Reichsbank for life. The budgetary deficit fell from 99% before stabilization to 92% after November 20, to 44% in the last ten days of December. By February 1924, the budget was finally balanced. The Rentenmark’s greatest triumph came in mid-December when food began appearing in towns. Before November, the only increase in animals slaughtered had been dogs. After stabilization, consumption of every daily need increased regularly—except dogmeat. Lord D’Abernon wrote on Christmas Day 1923 that not even the most fanatical advocate of stabilization could have anticipated such results.

14. Unemployment Breaks Out

The Price of Stability

Schacht knew the Rentenmark could hold the tide only so long. New credits from abroad were essential, and he permitted no departure from the strictest discipline despite pleading from every direction. In January 1924 he assessed the situation bluntly: after long devaluation, stability can only be regained at the cost of severe crisis. External commerce was at a standstill. The balance of trade was active only because imports had ceased. Industry was living on old stocks.

The stabilization brought a paradox visible on every street. Cologne in January was flooded with abundant supplies—Italian cauliflower, Canary tomatoes, Spanish grapes, American apples, French nuts. Within a fortnight, abundance became glut: mountains of Hawaiian pineapple, Colombian salmon, American corned beef. But the mass of the population simply could not buy. At Christmas, one billion marks bought two or three oranges; by February, the same sum bought twenty or thirty from unemployed men hawking on the streets. The British Embassy reported that the poor were “not really getting proper food—they haven’t enough money to profit from a cheaper market.”

The New Poor

The middle classes were destroyed. People on savings, pensions, disability grants, and fixed incomes sold their chattels or did small work. Their numbers amounted to millions. They had their wealth shot away by war without knowing it. Charitable institutions had income reduced to a trickle. Industrial debenture holders lost their capital as industries redeemed debts with worthless paper. The broader devastation included unorganized workers who went down with the middle classes: tailors, cab-drivers, chimney-sweeps, domestic servants, painters, gardeners, chauffeurs, carpenters, shop assistants, printers, laundrywomen, porters. A third group receiving small government pensions numbered over 5.6 million, excluding the unemployed.

Frau Eisenmenger in Vienna recorded her despair: foods unobtainable three years earlier could now be bought everywhere, but who could buy them? For 15,000 kronen they received one schilling. Her millions dwindled to about a thousand new schillings. “We belong to the new poor. The middle class is reduced to the proletariat. I cannot go on.” Hitler’s 24-day trial in February 1924 gave him a national platform but ended in minimum sentences. A British observer noted that “Hitler’s greatest enemy is the Rentenmark. Six months of stabilisation has had a calming effect on Nationalist hotheads.”

Germany’s minimal restitution efforts added insult to injury. Industrial debentures and mortgages were revalued at only 15% of their original gold price, later raised to 25%. A modest tax on inflation profits yielded under 2% of original gold value. Government loan holders received 2.5% of their original investment. Economic recovery came fitfully—interest rates fell from 100% in January to 30% by May 1924, and the Dawes Plan brought a large foreign loan and French evacuation of the Ruhr. By December 1924, registered unemployed fell to 436,000—a level that would never be reached again until Hitler’s arrival.

15. The Wounds Are Bared

Scapegoats and Anti-Semitism

Inflation’s scourge followed defeat’s scourge, and those who lived through it attributed their fear, greed, immorality, and demoralization primarily to inflation rather than to the war itself. Judith Listowel testified that in Hungary, anti-Semitism had been negligible before inflation; when Jews made a success of it and were ostentatious about their gains, they were hated. Wiser Jews were greatly upset, foreseeing the antagonism. But the fault “did not lie with the Jews but somehow much higher up.”

The Corruption Epidemic

Corruption became endemic. In Hungary, the elite branches of civil service maintained integrity and suffered for it, but real corruption at the county level was entirely new. When one official’s father protested irregularities, the reply was:

“Your Excellency, will you feed my children?” — Hungarian county official

Lord D’Abernon found German corruption “appalling” by 1924— before the war, bribery had been almost unknown. Tax evasion, food hoarding, speculation—all crimes against the state—had become matters of simple survival. The lower classes turned to theft and prostitution (up 50% in 1923 over 1913), while the middle and upper classes resorted to graft and fraud. The old virtues of thrift, honesty, and hard work had lost their appeal when speculation yielded far greater rewards.

The Barmat and Kutisker affairs rocked Germany. Julius Barmat, a Russian Jew of Lithuanian extraction, and Iwan Kutisker, a Lithuanian who had immediately grasped inflation’s principles, had between them borrowed 50 million gold marks from Prussian state institutions at 10-18% and re-lent at 100-200%. The trail led to the highest corridors: a confidential report disappeared from the Foreign Office, a minute from President Ebert requesting help for Barmat surfaced, and the ex-Chancellor Dr. Wirth was implicated “up to the hilt.” Three hundred police made arrests involving thirteen state officials. Upwards of £2 million in bribes had changed hands above and beyond the loans themselves.

False Prosperity

The post-stabilization years brought an influx of American loans—£200 million between 1924 and 1926, rising to £1,000 million by 1929 —that induced a spirit of extravagant spending at every level. Stresemann protested that 14 million marks spent rebuilding the Berlin Opera House created the impression abroad that Germany was “rolling in money.” But a deeper problem festered: the inflation boom had never been properly liquidated. Vast enterprises expanded during inflationary plenty became unproductive when capital grew short. Hugo Stinnes, deceived by artificial prosperity into fanatical confidence in coal’s future, saw his empire collapse in June 1925. Between the third quarter of 1925 and second quarter of 1927, Germany suffered 31,000 bankruptcies. By February 1926, registered unemployed exceeded 2 million—the nightmare that inflation had been designed to prevent.

General Wauchope warned at the beginning of 1927 that Germany was “morally mobilized for war in the future to right the wrongs of Versailles.” The greatest loss, he wrote, was the ruin of the middle classes. German output per man was 40% below the United States but 40% above Britain. He deduced a fair chance of Germany fighting a new war “within the life of this generation.” For France, the existence of 60 million industrious and martially-minded people overflowing with self-justification was alarm enough; the ill-suppressed revanchism and the expert Reichswehr that von Seeckt had salvaged made the alarm existential. The false prosperity of 1926—cafés full, museums crowded, new exhibition buildings— was the worst possible prelude to the depression to come.

Epilogue

The Moral

At no moment between 1914 and the summer of 1923 could currency stability not theoretically have been secured. Until a late date, German gold and foreign currency reserves constituted a substantial proportion of the paper’s exchange value. But the political will was never there. After the brief post-Kapp stability of mid-1920, any attempt at stabilization would have required immediate repercussions: bankruptcies, redundancies, unemployment, strikes, hunger, demonstrations, violence, the fear of insurrection. This was the point not lost on the inflators. Stabilization was postponed. Day by day through 1920-1922, the reckoning was deferred, more readily as the consequences became more frightening. The longer the delay, the more savage the cure.

The pattern repeated across all three countries. Austria was in the hands of receivers by end of 1922, regaining stable currency only under the absolute direction of a foreigner. Hungary passed its chance of self-redemption. Germany returned under military dictatorship with much of its constitution suspended. All required bailout by others. Each accepted greater disruption than they had feared when excessive printing might have been stopped. In all three, after inflation reached an advanced stage, disaster became a prerequisite of recovery.

The Moral Take-Off Point

The take-off point into hyperinflation was not found on graphs of depreciation, circulation velocity, or balance of payments. Nor did it coincide with the ultimate crisis of confidence. What really broke Germany was the constant taking of the soft political option on money. The take-off point was moral, not financial. No imaginable circumstances were more unsuited to imposing a new financial order than November 1923; yet it was precisely then that the Rentenmark was introduced, succeeding because the old mark had lost its entire meaning.

Animal Values

The book’s haunting final pages meditate on what hyperinflation does to human values. Money is only useful when more than one person acknowledges it. Once no one did, Germans learned their paper had no value, and the traditional repository of purchasing power vanished. There was no means left of measuring the worth of anything. Life became an obsessional search for Sachwerte—things of real, constant value. Stinnes bought factories; railway workers bought gewgaws. The degree of necessity became the sole criterion of value, and man’s values became animal values. A kilo of potatoes was worth more than the family silver. A side of pork more than a grand piano. A prostitute in the family better than an infant corpse. Theft preferable to starvation. Warmth finer than honour. Clothing more essential than democracy. Food more needed than freedom.

“Contrary to any philosophic assumption, it was not a salutary experience.” — Fergusson, final line

Key Takeaways

Inflation is always a political choice

At every stage from 1914 to 1923, stabilization was technically possible. The obstacle was never economic ignorance but political cowardice— the unwillingness to accept unemployment, social unrest, and loss of industrial competitiveness that honest money would demand. Each postponement made the eventual reckoning more savage.

War financing through borrowing plants the seeds of destruction

Helfferich’s decision to finance WWI entirely by loans rather than taxation created a debt mountain that could only be serviced through further inflation. Britain’s contrasting approach of taxing war-enriched industries preserved its currency and social contract. The Reichsbank’s trick of counting Treasury bills as note-coverage gave unlimited license to print.

Inflation is the most unfairly discriminatory of all economic phenomena

It punishes the virtuous—savers, pensioners, professionals on fixed incomes—while rewarding debtors, speculators, and those with access to real assets or foreign currency. This systematic injustice corrodes social trust far more than any recession.

The middle class is the primary casualty

The professional and rentier classes—judges, officers, civil servants, teachers, doctors, small savers—were systematically destroyed. War loans worth £45,000 at purchase shrank to £1,000. Their ruin had consequences far beyond economics: it destroyed the social stratum most invested in democratic institutions and moderation.

Reparations were real but also served as alibi

The London Ultimatum’s demand for 132 billion gold marks was genuinely crushing, but German leaders used reparations as cover for domestic fiscal irresponsibility. The budget was already unbalanceable before any reparation payments began.

Industrial policy and currency manipulation are inseparable

A cheap mark made German exports irresistibly competitive, satisfied workers with nominal wage increases, and enriched industrialists like Stinnes. The gains were real but temporary, and they came at the cost of destroying the currency’s function as a store of value.

Stabilization is as painful as the disease

The Rentenmark brought food back to the cities within weeks, but also mass unemployment, waves of bankruptcies, frozen capital in useless factories, and the collapse of the Stinnes empire. Those who survived inflation often had their remaining assets further diminished by deflation.

The moral damage outlasts the economic damage

Corruption became endemic, thrift lost its appeal, anti-Semitism intensified as populations sought scapegoats. The ethic cracked: speculation yielded more than labor, crime became survival, and public servants could not live on their salaries. This moral deterioration prepared the ground for what followed.

Parallel collapses illuminate the pattern

Austria, Hungary, and Germany each went through the same cycle: printing to avoid pain, accelerating collapse, foreign bailout or military dictatorship, brutal stabilization. The pattern suggests this is not a German phenomenon but a universal political temptation.

The take-off point is moral, not financial

Hyperinflation does not begin when some technical threshold is crossed on a graph. It begins when a society decides it would rather debase its currency than face hard truths. Once inflation becomes a political tool, it develops a powerful lobby with no interest in rational arguments, and stopping it requires more pain than any democratic government is willing to impose—until the currency dies completely.

Contrarian Ideas

Germany’s national debt was wiped out before hyperinflation even began

By October 1920, the national debt at old parities would have been £14,400 million; at current exchange rates it was only £1,200 million. Inflation had already erased the war debt a full year before the great inflation started— undermining the narrative that reparations alone drove the collapse.

The Rentenmark was a collective hallucination that worked

The new currency was unconvertible paper backed by nothing tangible, yet it succeeded because it had a different name and the public chose to believe in it. This suggests that money is ultimately a shared fiction, and the critical resource is not gold reserves but public trust.

Inflation was deliberately maintained as industrial and foreign policy

It was not mere incompetence. Industrialists, workers, and politicians all had rational reasons to prefer a falling mark: competitive exports, rising nominal wages, debt elimination, and proof that reparations were impossible. The losers— the middle class—were politically diffuse and slow to organize.

Stabilization required dictatorship

None of the three countries stabilized under normal democratic governance. Austria needed a foreign financial controller, Hungary a League commissioner, Germany a military dictator with the constitution suspended. This suggests that democratic politics may be structurally incapable of imposing the pain required to end advanced inflation.

Full employment through inflation leads to worse unemployment

Germany’s entire inflationary policy was governed by fear of unemployment. But when stabilization finally came, unemployment exceeded 2 million—far worse than what honest money would have caused earlier. The policy created the precise outcome it was designed to prevent.

Gresham’s Law reversed in extremis

Bremen’s gold-backed notes drove inferior paper out of circulation rather than the reverse. When the bad money becomes bad enough, the traditional law inverts: people hoard the good money of course, but they also refuse the bad entirely, creating demand for sound alternatives.

Allegories & Cultural References

The Joyless Street

Pabst’s 1925 film starring early Greta Garbo, made in Vienna, serves as cultural testimony to the inflation era: the butcher taunting hungry queues, speculators gorging at nightclubs, the final explosion of the starving crowd against the revelers. It is “a faithful reflection of the times.”

The Gypsy and the Horse

The metaphor circulating about Austria’s stabilization: a gypsy trained his horse to eat less and less. When he got it down to a straw a day, the horse died. Austria’s financial soundness was achieved through mass suffering—the cure was real but the patient barely survived.

San Francisco After the Earthquake

Lord D’Abernon compared Berlin’s financial district to San Francisco after the 1906 earthquake and the South Sea Bubble simultaneously: every bank being rebuilt, the roadway impassable, and a speculative mania gripping the entire population.

Gold for Iron

The wartime slogan “I gave gold for iron”—surrendering jewelry and ornaments for the war effort—becomes bitterly ironic as the paper marks received in return for real gold become worthless. It serves as the opening chapter title, framing the entire arc of betrayed patriotic trust.

Yards to the Sun

A chosen metaphor for the incomprehensibility of the numbers: by October 1923, the number of marks to the pound equalled the number of yards to the sun. Similarly, the paper marks needed to buy one gold mark equalled the square millimeters in a square kilometer. These analogies attempt to make the astronomical figures viscerally real.

Seconds Since Christ

At 60 billion marks to the pound, this was approximately one mark for every second elapsed since the birth of Christ— another attempt to give human scale to numbers that had passed beyond comprehension.

When Money Dies: The Nightmare of Deficit Spending, Devaluation, and Hyperinflation in Weimar Germany
Adam Fergusson · 1975 (2010 PublicAffairs edition) · 273 pages

The definitive account of the German hyperinflation of 1914–1923, told not as economics but as human tragedy. Through diplomatic dispatches, personal diaries, and contemporary journalism, the book traces how a great nation chose to destroy its own currency rather than face hard truths—and how that choice corroded not just savings but morality, social bonds, and the democratic institutions that might have prevented what came next.

About the Author

Adam Fergusson (born 1932) is a British journalist, author, and former politician who served as a Member of the European Parliament for the Conservative Party from 1979 to 1984. Educated at Eton and Trinity College, Cambridge, he worked as a journalist for the Glasgow Herald and The Times before turning to political and historical writing. His background in both journalism and European politics gave him an unusual vantage point from which to investigate the Weimar hyperinflation.

Fergusson wrote When Money Dies in 1975, drawing primarily on the British Foreign Office archives—the dispatches from Lord D’Abernon’s embassy in Berlin and the consular reports scattered across Germany and Austria. He supplemented these with personal diaries, memoirs, and contemporary journalism to reconstruct the human experience of currency collapse. The book went out of print for decades before being reissued by PublicAffairs in 2010 with a foreword by Niall Ferguson, who noted its renewed relevance in the age of quantitative easing.

Fergusson also served as Special Adviser to Geoffrey Howe when Howe was Foreign Secretary. His other works include analyses of European integration and political commentary, but When Money Dies remains his defining achievement—the book that established hyperinflation as a subject of narrative history rather than pure economics.

Why This Book Matters

When Money Dies is the definitive account of what happens when a modern industrial nation destroys its own currency. No other book on the Weimar hyperinflation combines economic analysis with such vivid human testimony—the diarists, diplomats, and ordinary citizens whose voices make abstract numbers feel like lived catastrophe. The book’s central argument—that inflation is always a political choice, never an economic inevitability—carries permanent relevance for any society that finances spending through money creation rather than taxation.

The book found a second life after the 2008 financial crisis, when central banks launched unprecedented programs of quantitative easing. Warren Buffett recommended it; hedge fund managers passed copies around trading desks. The 2010 reissue with Niall Ferguson’s foreword became an unexpected bestseller. Its lesson—that the take-off point into hyperinflation is moral, not financial, rooted in the constant choice of the soft political option—speaks to any era in which governments defer hard choices by printing money.

Perhaps most importantly, Fergusson demonstrates that inflation’s greatest damage is not economic but moral. When a kilo of potatoes is worth more than the family silver, when theft becomes preferable to starvation, when a nation’s values become animal values—that is the true cost of debased currency. The book is a warning that civilized societies are more fragile than they appear, and that the distance between bourgeois comfort and barter economies is shorter than anyone wants to believe.
