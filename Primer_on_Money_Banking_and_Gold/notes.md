## Front Matter

### Foreword by Paul Volcker (2008)
- Written in early 2008, just before the financial crisis
- Volcker praises Bernstein's clarity but notes the world has grown far more complex since the 1960s: CDOs, SIVs, hedge funds, conduits, sub-prime mortgages
- In the 1960s, the Fed focused on volume of bank reserves; today it focuses on short-term interest rate targets (Fed funds rate) — fully transparent and publicly stated after each FOMC meeting
- Volcker wonders if transparency has truly reduced uncertainty — "those of us of a certain vintage have some doubts"
- Key change since 1968: policy is now about shaping **expectations of the future**, not just current reserves
- On inflation: Bernstein was too calm. His 1968 view was that productivity would prevent runaway inflation. Then Vietnam + Nixon's weak-kneed Fed response + 1970s stagflation proved him wrong
- Volcker's own herculean effort (Aug 1979 – Aug 1987): controlled money supply, let short-term rates go wherever they would, did not let go until inflation was licked
- "The alternative would have been a United States transformed into a banana republic"
- Bernstein predicted in 1968 that abandoning gold was "practically and politically impossible." Nixon did it in 1971
- Gold bull market followed: topped over $800/oz in 1981 after hovering near $40

### New Introduction (Bernstein, 2008)
- Book originated accidentally: Bernstein and Robert Heilbroner wrote A Primer on Government Spending in 1963 (Kennedy tax cut advocacy). Sold over 100,000 copies. Heilbroner suggested a money/banking/gold sequel
- Bernstein's background: worked at two small NY commercial banks 1947–1951, was at Federal Reserve Bank of New York research department 1940–1942
- First edition 1965; revised 1968 with updated empirical material and three new chapters on postwar monetary history
- Bernstein admits he was "much too calm" about inflation in the 1960s
- He was a child of the Depression — worried more about unemployment than inflation
- In retrospect, inflation was already embedded by 1969. As late as October 1978, Humphrey-Hawkins Full Employment Law specified fighting inflation should NOT take priority over reducing unemployment
- James Tobin (1980): "demand management cannot stabilize the inflationary price trend without chronic sacrifice of output and employment unless assisted by direct incomes policies"
- Friedman's 1963 dictum: "inflation is always and everywhere a monetary phenomenon" — ignored in this primer, though Bernstein acknowledges the huge impact of Friedman's work
- The big change in monetary policy since 1968: elevated **expectations** as the core element in executing policy. The word "expectations" is conspicuous by its absence in this book
- Bernstein's defense: "the best laid plans of man and beast gang oft agley" — passing judgment on past positions ignores that the future was unknown

### Original Introduction
- Opens with Baron Rothschild: knew only two men who really understood gold — "Unfortunately, they disagree"
- The book's purpose: answer basic questions (Are we on the gold standard? Why are we losing gold? Where did $7 trillion in annual check-writing come from? Did the Government print it?)
- Main thesis: these questions are less difficult than they appear — but the informed citizen must understand the difference between personal finances and the great national accounts

## PART ONE: THE MONEY PROBLEM

### Chapter 1: Why Worry About Money and Gold?
- Opens with the Midas legend: gold brings wealth only in terms of what you can buy with it
- The core problem: ensure enough money to buy everything produced, but not so much that we try to buy more than produced
- Money invented to relieve the "almost insurmountable difficulties" of barter
- As societies grow more complex and specialized, money becomes more abstract — now primarily numbers on bank ledgers
- Most money has no tangible existence: we can't see, feel, or smell it — which makes quantity hard to regulate
- Gold's persistence despite its abstraction: Freud suggested it's related to erotic fantasies of early childhood; its physical properties (density, resistance to oxidation, never tarnishes) make it useful
- Will Rogers on the Klondike: "There is a lot of difference between pioneering for gold and pioneering for spinach"
- Total world accumulation of monetary gold: ~40,000 tons — American industry pours 40,000 tons of steel in under ONE HOUR
- The regulatory failure: history shows repeated alternations between inflation and deflation — men have done a poor job regulating money supply
- Deflation: prices fall, profits vanish, production shrinks, unemployment spreads — until prices fall so low the inadequate supply of money seems sufficient
- Inflation: money outruns goods supply, prices rise — losers are the frugal, conservative, prudent, and the poor who can't battle for higher incomes
- **Conclusion**: regulation of money supply is "intimately involved with our prosperity and with our social tranquility" — broadest political and economic implications

### Chapter 2: Spending and Financing
- Basic truth: each of us works not from kindness but because we expect to sell our production and be paid
- Core principle: businessmen must have money to pay workers and suppliers; customers must have money to buy goods
- **Key conclusion**: an increase in production or higher prices "simply cannot be sustained unless people are willing and able to lay out the extra money"
- Example of the family: spending $6,000/year on a basket of goods. Prices rise 10%. Family needs to find $600 more. Options: demand wage increase (employer must find $600), draw down savings, borrow, or cut purchases
- If family can't find $600: retailers find sales falling, cut orders; wholesalers tell manufacturers to ship less; manufacturers cut production; employees laid off
- The pencil manufacturer example: wants to expand production 10% (100M → 110M pencils). Must finance extra raw materials, labor. Options: use cash reserves, ask suppliers to wait, draw down savings, borrow, take on investors
- **Key insight**: money that is spent must come from somewhere — either one's own cash balance or someone else's money holding
- But: "money makes the world go round" — the supply of money does set limits to how far business expansion can go
- Important qualifier: the number of dollars in accounts is only an indirect guide to the rate of spending. Expenditure is what counts
- Money's peculiar characteristic: **we need not spend it the instant we receive it**. We can let it sit, or lend it to others for interest. This is what makes monetary control complex
- One man's payment is another man's receipt — money doesn't disappear when spent, it moves to the payee
- Recapitulation: main problem is keeping rate of spending in line with production of goods and services — not too low (deflation) nor too high (inflation)

### Chapter 3: The Price of Money
- Most obvious reason for holding money: to pay for things we buy. Most of us earn irregularly but spend continuously, so we need cash on hand
- The "precautionary motive": keeping a little more than absolutely required — for emergencies, opportunities, irresistible hats or neckties in shop windows
- Some carry more than needed: why let money sit earning nothing? Because putting it to work involves risk and inconvenience — the rate must be "adequate"
- "Adequate" is subjective: but risk matters — credit risk (borrower may not repay), and liquidity risk (can we get it back before maturity?)
- **Bond price/yield example**: 1953, US borrowed for 30 years at 3¼%. By 1959, new bonds yielded 4%+. The 1953 bonds fell to $820 in market. Original buyer's 6-year return: $195 interest minus $180 capital loss = $15 total, or $2.50/year on $1,000. "A poor piece of business"
- Walter Bagehot's old saying: "John Bull can stand many things, but he cannot stand two percent"
- Historically: periods of **high and rising interest rates** associated with vigorous and sustained prosperity, not low rates
- **The paradox of collective saving/spending**: one man's payment is another's receipt. If we all try to hold more cash, no one can actually succeed — because to get cash someone must pay it out. If no one pays out, no one can accumulate. This is why liquidity crunches develop
- The 1929–1933 panic: everyone a seller, no one a buyer — panic when people realize "he who parts with cash today will have a hard time getting it back tomorrow"
- Interest rates as signals: rising rates = demand for money exceeds supply; falling rates = excess supply being offered at lower prices
- **Conclusion**: supply of money is crucial in determining how much spending occurs — so we must turn to how money is created

## PART TWO: THE CREATION OF MONEY

### Chapter 4: Money in Hand and Money in the Bank
- Money takes two forms: (1) coin and currency (visible, tangible), (2) checking accounts (invisible, bookkeeping entries). They come into existence differently and have different impacts
- Historical data: $20B in checking accounts + currency at end of WWI; nearly $30B in 1929; fell below $20B in Great Depression; rose to $100B+ by end of WWII; $180B+ by book's writing
- **Q: Who determines the amount of currency in circulation?** Not the Government — it is THE PUBLIC. The millions of individuals, businesses, and financial institutions continuously choosing between currency and checking accounts
- At Christmas, currency flows out; after Christmas, shopkeepers deposit excess back
- Government prints and mints currency but has no lasting influence on how much circulates vs. is deposited
- **Critical point**: virtually all currency that gets into circulation is withdrawn FROM bank accounts. All excess currency IS redeposited in bank accounts
- Currency vs. checking accounts: a shift from one to the other doesn't change total money supply
- **Key deduction**: since currency withdrawals come from checking accounts, and checking accounts can be replenished from currency, **the factors that determine the amount of money we have IN THE BANK are the key to where our money comes from**
- Summary of argument so far:
  1. An increase in money supply can only come from an increase in currency, coin, or checking accounts
  2. An increase in currency comes through withdrawals from checking accounts
  3. Therefore, **an increase in the money supply must originally come through a rise in checking accounts**
  4. For someone to get more without someone else getting less, the source must be **outside the system** — i.e., the banks themselves
- Could it be the Government? Government is legally and morally prohibited from financing expenditures by printing money (with one exception: buying gold)
- Government must finance by taxation or borrowing — both transfer money, not create it
- **The answer**: it is the banks themselves. When a bank lends or buys a security, the depositor's account goes up without any other depositor's account going down

### Chapter 5: The Business of Banking
- Central revelation: "it isn't the Government that creates the money we use at all. Rather, the very largest part of the money in our bank accounts today originally came into being when some commercial bank officer approved a loan to a customer or decided to invest in a marketable security"
- Commercial banks (as distinct from savings banks): the only institutions that accept and handle DEMAND DEPOSITS (checking accounts)
- The history of banking "is littered with the failures of banks that forgot or simply didn't bother to have enough cash on hand"
- The banker's core challenge: gauge how fast depositors are putting money in vs. drawing it out
- **Reserve**: cash held to cover the possibility of net withdrawals. Not the only meaning of "reserve" (banks also have loss reserves) but the relevant one here
- **Secondary reserve**: short-term Treasury obligations that can be liquidated quickly — earns some interest but highly liquid
- The banker's decision: allocate among (1) cash reserve, (2) secondary reserve (short-term T-bills), (3) loans and investments
- Three factors in loan vs. security decision: **liquidity, cost, and demand**
  - Securities have a market; loans must be held until paid off
  - Securities require little investigation; loans need extensive credit analysis, follow-up, servicing
  - Sometimes loan demand is strong; sometimes weak (especially in recessions)
- The relationship between banker and public: "The public and the banker sit on opposite sides of the desk in a figurative as well as a literal sense"
- Banking is "a constant struggle between the necessity to be liquid... and the desire to have money lent out and invested so that it will earn interest to show the bank a profit"

### Chapter 6: Bank Credit and Money
- **Most crucial point**: one man's payment is another man's receipt. Total deposits can be increased ONLY when one man's deposit is NOT correspondingly offset by withdrawal from another man's account
- This only happens when funds deposited come from ONE OF THE COMMERCIAL BANKS THEMSELVES
- Pencil manufacturer goes to bank, gets $50,000 loan: bank bookkeeper increases pencil company account by $50,000. "The company's account is increased just as though a check for $50,000 from some other lender had been deposited in it"
- From economy's viewpoint, THIS IS RADICALLY DIFFERENT from borrowing from a friend: pencil maker has more money, but no one has any less. Total purchasing power increased
- Second version: pencil maker sells $50,000 in gov securities to bond dealer; dealer sells to local bank. When the BANK buys the securities, bank sends check to dealer, dealer deposits in his account. Dealer has more money, no one has less — total deposits increased
- The banker's objection: "I can't even lend all the cash I have because I must keep enough on hand for withdrawals. How am I creating money?"
- **The resolution**: Every withdrawal from one checking account is offset by a deposit in another. Money withdrawn from bank A ends up deposited in bank B. The banking SYSTEM doesn't lose cash — only individual banks do
- The banker is both right and wrong:
  - Right: he cannot lend more than he has available, and must maintain reserves
  - Wrong: he IS creating money, because "all banks together are not limited by cash on hand. One bank's loss is another bank's gain"
- Why can't savings banks do the same? Because savings banks must pay out of their commercial bank checking accounts. When savings bank makes a loan, borrower deposits proceeds in checking account — savings bank's commercial bank account is reduced, borrower's checking account goes up. Net: no new money. Savings bank is "just like any other depositor in the system"
- Why are commercial banks unique? Because money paid out by one commercial bank is REDEPOSITED in another commercial bank — it retains spendable form. No other institution shares this property
- **The paradox**: in the individual bank, loans and investments FOLLOW from deposits. In the SYSTEM as a whole, deposits are CREATED by loans and investments
- 1967 data: commercial banks lent/invested $37B; deposits went up $42.7B (extra came from additional cash entering system)
- But all this requires CONTROL — which is what the Federal Reserve provides

## PART THREE: THE CONTROL OF MONEY

### Chapter 7: The Federal Reserve System
- Established 1913, following banking crisis of 1907
- Structure: 12 Federal Reserve Districts, each with a Federal Reserve Bank. Plus Board of Governors in Washington
- Each FRB has 9-member Board of Directors:
  - Class A (3): bankers elected by district's commercial banks
  - Class B (3): businessmen (not bankers) elected by commercial banks
  - Class C (3): appointed by Board of Governors
- Board of Governors: 7 members, appointed by President, confirmed by Senate, for 14-year terms — designed for independence from political pressure
- ~6,000 member banks (less than half of all commercial banks) but hold more than two-thirds of total commercial bank deposits
- Membership requirements: lending/investing restrictions, regular examinations, minimum cash reserves relative to deposits
- Membership incentives: (1) prestige signals safety, attracts deposits; (2) privilege of borrowing from Fed when short on cash
- **Focal point**: supply of member bank cash reserves relative to outstanding deposits
- The Fed is a "bankers' bank" — same relationship to member banks as commercial banks have to the public
- Public keeps cash in commercial bank accounts; commercial banks keep cash in Fed accounts
- Commercial banks transfer funds via checking accounts; commercial banks transfer funds via Fed accounts
- Public draws currency from commercial banks; commercial banks draw currency from Fed Banks
- **The Accord between the two**: when Treasury pays Social Security to Mr. Jones, he deposits check in NY State Bank, NY State Bank deposits at Fed, Fed increases NY State Bank's account and reduces Treasury's — but no other bank lost. This is a NET GAIN for banking system as a whole
- When Treasury takes in taxes, reverse happens — reserves lost to system
- This shows one major way system can gain or lose reserves: Treasury transactions

### Chapter 8: Federal Reserve Tools of Control
- Fed has three tools: (1) Open-market operations, (2) Discount rate, (3) Reserve requirements
- **Without the Fed**: if bank is short on cash, it can (a) sell securities, (b) slow new loans, (c) borrow from other banks. But if ALL banks are short, they can't all borrow from each other. Result: cascade of selling, panic, runs
- **Open-market operations**: when Fed BUYS securities, it pays with a check drawn on itself. Selling bank gets cash, no other bank loses — net addition to system reserves. When Fed SELLS securities, buyers pay with checks from their banks, those banks lose reserves — net drain from system
- Effect on interest rates: Fed buying → drives bond prices up, yields down → lower cost of financing expansion. Fed selling → drives bond prices down, yields up → more expensive, harder to raise cash
- **Federal Open Market Committee**: 7 Governors + NY Fed President + 4 rotating Presidents of other Feds. Meets every 3 weeks. Famous for "masterpieces of on-the-one-hand-on-the-other-hand language"
- Example of ambiguous 1955 FOMC quote: Chairman Martin asked if market was "tight but not too tight" — response was "it would also be correct to say it had been easy but not too easy"
- Example operating instruction: "permit the supply of bank credit and money to increase further, but at the same time avoid redundant bank reserves that would encourage capital outflows internationally"
- **Discount rate**: rate at which member banks borrow from Fed. If low relative to T-bill yields, banks borrow from Fed instead of selling securities (prevents reserve shortage spreading). If high, banks prefer to sell securities (shortage spreads unless Fed buys)
- Fed can never FORCE banks to borrow; can rarely deny a legitimate request
- Member bank borrowings as indicator: trend upward = banks feel short on reserves (trying to maintain status quo); Fed open-market operations = active effort to change things

### Chapter 9: Reserve Requirements
- The third tool, and the broadest
- 1913: discount rate was the only genuine tool. Open-market operations discovered "more or less by accident"
- Banking Act of 1935: Board permanently empowered to vary reserve requirements
- 1967 requirements:
  - Reserve city banks: 16.5% on demand deposits; 3% small time deposits; 6% large time deposits
  - Country banks: 12% on demand deposits (same time deposit requirements)
- Reserve requirements = "blunt instrument" — hits ALL banks at once, not targeted. Open-market operations and discount rate are more fine-tuned
- **With reserve requirement, the math of credit expansion**:
  - If giant nationwide bank has $25B in reserves and 20% requirement: max deposits = $125B (5x reserves)
  - With 16.5% requirement: expansion could be 6-fold
  - With 25%: expansion only 4-fold
- Individual bank can lend only its EXCESS reserves; but banking SYSTEM can lend MANY TIMES the total excess reserves (because reserves lost by one end up in another)
- **The multiplier example**: Bank A has $50,000 excess reserves → lends it → goes to Bank B → B has $40,000 excess → buys securities → goes to Bank C → C has $32,000 excess → etc. Eventual total = $250,000 (5x starting excess with 20% requirement)
- **Critical caveat**: this is purely theoretical. Reality depends on "willingness of all business firms and individuals to increase their holdings of demand deposits and currency." If public uses additional liquidity to repay loans or shift to time deposits, money supply may not reach theoretical limit
- **Combining the tools**:
  - Lower reserve requirement → more of any reserve can be lent
  - Discount rate and open-market operations determine TOTAL volume of reserves
  - At 16.5% requirement: every extra dollar of excess reserves could theoretically lead to $6 expansion in demand deposits
- Fed monitors "free reserves" (excess reserves minus borrowed reserves from Fed):
  - Positive free reserves: banks feel flush, can expand
  - Negative free reserves: banks pressed, likely to contract

### Chapter 10: Currency: A Complication
- We've assumed withdrawals from one bank are always redeposited in another — now we drop that assumption
- **The complication**: when people hold currency (not depositing it), that withdrawal from banking system has no offsetting deposit. Banking system LOSES reserves
- WWI example: Americans drew out $18B in currency that was never redeposited during WWII
- Christmas: currency flows out of banks for weeks, then returns. This is a temporary, reversible drain
- Sustained currency outflows can force banks to curtail lending, which shrinks money supply
- **How system handles it**: Fed can replenish reserves lost to currency drain via (a) making discount window available, (b) buying Government securities in open market
- WWII example: currency in circulation rose from $8B (1940) to $30B (1945) — but member bank reserves actually INCREASED by $3B+ because Fed purchased $20B+ in gov securities
- **Where does currency come from?** Member banks draw currency from Fed accounts, reducing their reserve balances. Fed provides it in exchange — **Federal Reserve Notes**
- As of 1967: $40B of $46B in circulation = Federal Reserve Notes (nearly 9 of every 10 dollars)
- **Federal Reserve Notes**: NOT redeemable in anything except "legal tender" — and Federal Reserve Notes themselves ARE legal tender. "They are really redeemable only in themselves!" An irredeemable obligation
- "Does it seem surprising that nearly 90% of the currency we use is really worth no more than the paper on which it is printed? It should be no more surprising than the fact that highly prized gold really has little intrinsic value"
- The backing for checking accounts? Assets of commercial banks: 3/4 loans and securities (sound only insofar as borrowers are sound, which depends on prosperity of economy). The rest is "cash" = this same irredeemable currency plus Fed accounts. Those are redeemable only in more Federal Reserve Notes
- "In short, the money we use every day... is intrinsically worthless: it has no tangible backing, in the strict sense of the word. Yet... we value it highly because of what we have to give up to get it and what we can buy with it"
- **Gold's only real attraction**: its supply is limited, whereas only men's judgments can limit credit-based money. But gold also may set the limit too low, preventing adequate money supply for economic growth
- **Conclusion**: "When we look back over the ground and ask what the American dollar is really based upon, we would have to say that it consists essentially of promises and bookkeeping machines"

## PART FOUR: GOLD

### Chapter 11: Gold at Home
- Until 1968: Federal Reserve Notes outstanding could not exceed FOUR TIMES Fed holdings of gold certificates
- September 30, 1967: $39.6B in Fed Notes; required $9.9B in gold certificates; actual $12.5B — margin shrinking rapidly
- 1964: Congress removed gold cert requirement against FRB deposit liabilities (member bank reserve balances)
- 1968: gold certificate reserve requirement eliminated completely
- Why eliminated? (1) Our monetary understanding is so advanced that arbitrary barriers are obsolete; (2) the original framers couldn't imagine the money supply needed to finance a modern economy
- **How gold is "monetized"**: Treasury buys gold from Homestake Mining. Pays with check drawn on Fed. Homestake deposits check → bank deposits at Fed → Fed reduces Treasury balance. But Treasury replenishes by printing up gold certificates and depositing them at Fed. Result: member bank reserves and deposits increased — WITHOUT taxation or borrowing
- This is the ONE exception to the rule that Government can't create money: "You don't have to have any money in the bank to buy gold — you just pay for it by printing up those certificates"
- Bernstein's whimsical point: if we monetized missiles or paper clips or Justices of the Supreme Court the same way, those could also be financed by printing certificates
- **When US loses gold** (foreign government cashes in dollar balances for gold):
  - Gold moves from Fort Knox to Fed of NY vaults (or to "earmark" — just notated as belonging to foreign govt)
  - Foreign govt issues check to US Treasury
  - Treasury deposits at Fed → reserves and deposits of member banks are reduced
  - Treasury then cancels the gold certificates → Treasury's Fed balance drops back
  - Net: banking system reserves reduced; gold certificate reserve of Fed Banks smaller
- **Net effect of gold flows**: gold inflows → member bank reserves increase. Gold outflows → reserves decrease
- Gold movements affect business activity even apart from gold certificate requirements, through their effect on member bank reserves

### Chapter 12: Gold Abroad
- No nation can indefinitely pay out to foreigners more than it receives — same as individuals or corporations
- Gold = "the only unquestioned and generally acceptable means of payment among nations"
- Historical note: Britain adopted gold as SOLE standard in 1821. Most others followed in the latter 19th century after gold discoveries. But by 1937 — barely 50 years later — not one country in the world maintained a fixed tie between currency and gold
- The disappearance of gold from sight: under old gold standard, all money freely convertible. In monetary collapse after 1929, everyone rushed to convert. "The demand for gold was so much greater than its limited supply that the one thing everybody wanted was denied them" — convertibility suspended
- Keynes (1930): called gold "part of the apparatus of conservatism" because its fixed supply "so rigorously set the upper limit to any expansion in the quantity of money"
- Keynes's Auri Sacra Fames: "Almost throughout the world, gold has been withdrawn from circulation... Gold is out of sight — gone back into the soil. But when gods are no longer seen in a yellow panoply walking the earth, we begin to rationalise them; and it is not long before there is nothing left"
- **How the US got into balance of payments trouble**: richest country, held 3/4 of world's monetary gold at end of WWII, yet paying out more to foreigners than receiving
  - Trade balance: FAVORABLE — exports $36B, exceeded imports by $2B in 1966
  - Foreign assets: $60B+ (up from $19B in 1950), earning $6B/year
  - Aid: 75-80% of every dollar given away comes right back as countries buy American goods
  - Military spending abroad: $4B+/year — unavoidably abroad, often deposited in France/Europe
- **1967 Balance of Payments table**: receipts $49.8B, payments $53.3B, overall deficit $3.5B
- From 1950 to 1967: total cumulative deficit ~$40B; $28B in the last 9 years
- **Why do foreigners continue to hold dollars?**
  1. Dollar = good substitute for gold (US pledged convertibility at $35/oz → dollar "good as gold"); holding gold earns no interest and incurs safekeeping costs
  2. Foreigners actually need dollars for day-to-day dealings with Americans; Eurodollar markets; used to repay postwar US loans
  3. US kept short-term interest rates high enough to make dollar assets attractive (T-bill yield rose from 4.45% to 5.80%, 1965–1967)
- Gold loss over 10 years to Dec 1967: $10B+. But foreigners accumulated dollar balances even faster
- **What if run on gold begins?** Two options: (1) suspend convertibility of dollar into gold — dollar would be "devalued," chaos in FX markets; or (2) raise price of gold (devaluation in gold terms). Both routes penalize those who held dollars faithfully
- Note: "in neither case is the number of dollars in American bank accounts changed nor has anything happened to the domestic buying power of the dollar" — it's a purely international issue
- **Two-tiered price system (spring 1968)**: governments and central banks trade gold among themselves at $35/oz; private market at floating prices. Since industrial demand = only about half annual production, and existing stocks = ~100 years supply, refusal of major nations to buy at $35 could cause sharp price decline
- Hoarding gold for the first time became a "highly risky form of investment"
- If free price rises well above $35, governments would cash dollars for gold and resell at profit — two-tiered system would collapse

## PART FIVE: THEORY IN PRACTICE

### Chapter 13: The Experience of 1938 to 1945
- **Four major segments in 1938–1963**: (1) gold rush pre-WWII, (2) war finance, (3) postwar inflation to Korean War, (4) long period of rising rates, tightening money, gold outflows
- **Segment 1 (1937–1941)**: Frightened Europeans converted capital into dollars → gold poured in → member bank reserves doubled from $7B to $14B. But with few credit-worthy borrowers and low interest rates, banks not eager to deploy reserves. Excess reserves swelled from $1B to $7B
- Some increase in bank investments (up ~$6B); deposits increased because gold inflow monetization deposited in banks
- Interest rates fell: corporate bonds above 4% in 1933, fell to 3% by 1938, 2¾% at Pearl Harbor. Short-term paper near zero
- Despite massive reserve accumulation, banks and depositors willing to hold dollars idle — money NOT burning a hole in pockets
- Monetary policy "fell into wide disrepute" — "pushing on a string." Attention shifted to Government deficit spending
- **Segment 2 (WWII)**: War's economic impact: workers taken from factories to battlefield; government absorbs substantial production; civilian living standards must fall unless massive productive capacity exists
- Total war spending: $308B (government budget expenditures $340B, taxes covered $130B, borrowed $210B)
- Problem: $210B to be borrowed was five times the entire quantity of money in US in 1941! How to borrow without sending interest rates astronomical?
- Answer: **one-third was borrowed from commercial banks**. Banks issued checks for government bonds → Treasury's account increased, no other depositor's account reduced → NEW money created
- "If we think about it objectively, the ultimate impact would have been essentially the same if the Government had printed that $70 billion instead of borrowing it from commercial banks"
- Result: money supply from $40B → $100B+ in five years. So vast that interest rates STAYED at Depression-era lows even as government debt more than quintupled
- Currency in circulation rose from $8B (1940) → $30B (1945). Fed bought $22B in gov securities to replace lost reserves
- Price controls and rationing prevented the inflation that would otherwise have resulted from massive money supply growth with no civilian goods increase
- **When war ended**: "all the money built up as a result of the gold inflow of the 1930s and the subsequent impact of war finance rushed like a torrent into the postwar economy"

### Chapter 14: The Experience of 1945 to 1963
- Post-war: consumers stampeded for automobiles, homes, clothes, appliances; businessmen re-equipped run-down facilities; Western Europe reconstructed; even bystander countries joined queue
- Treasury returned $20B+ of war loan proceeds to economy by repaying debt in 1946–47 (war ended early, surplus cash)
- **1945–1948 inflation**: consumer prices up 33%, wholesale prices up 51%. (WWII had held wholesale prices to only +7% from 1942–45)
- Money supply rose 2.5x from 1940–1945 while GNP just doubled — excess liquidity drove the postwar inflation
- Commercial bank holdings of gov securities shrank $30B from 1945–1950; insurance companies and corporations also sold $7B; individuals sold $4B in marketable gov securities
- Money that had sat idle began moving: GNP expanded one-third from 1945–1950 on only 10% money supply increase. Debt outside Federal Government grew from $202.9B (1945) to $323.0B (1950)
- Concern grew that rising interest rates would cause massive dumping of gov securities; also fear that $40B+ in Savings Bonds redeemable on demand could cause run on Treasury
- **Fed intervention**: Fed let shorter-term bonds run off but was heavy buyer of issues maturing in 1+ year, holding prices stable. But situation becoming critical: if Fed had to keep buying at pegged prices, they'd lose control of money supply
- **Treasury-Federal Reserve Accord of April 1951**: freed Fed from obligation to be residual buyer at fixed prices. Security prices could seek their own level
- **Post-Accord era**: Fed biased toward preventing inflation. Dollar volume of business activity expanded 75% from 1951–1963; money supply increased only 20%
- Real climax: 13% GNP expansion from 1958–1960 with less than 3% money supply increase → interest rate on long-term bonds rose nearly 50%
- Strategy: Fed refused to provide permanent reserves. Banks had to BORROW from discount window to meet demand → member bank borrowings oscillated between near-zero in recessions and $1B+ in expansions
- Once borrowings hit $1B (1952, 1955–57, 1959): banks became strict, sold securities, interest rates rose steeply
- **Mystery**: commercial bank gov security holdings flat 1951–1963, but loans to businesses/consumers rose from $50B to ~$150B — how?
  - Fed reduced reserve requirements in multiple steps (from 24% to 16.5% for central reserve city banks on demand deposits)
  - Public shifted from demand deposits to time/savings deposits (lower reserve requirement)
  - Over 60% of deposit expansion since 1951 was in time deposits — required only 1/3 to 1/4 the reserves of demand deposits

### Chapter 15: The Experience of 1966
- 1966 = unique chapter in American monetary history. "Nothing like it had occurred since the debacle of the early 1930s"
- Background: 1963–1965 saw extraordinary balance — 5%/year real growth, falling labor costs, improving balance of payments, stable long-term rates despite rapid growth
- **What upset the balance (1965–1966)**:
  1. No slack: unemployment fell from 4.5% to 3.7%; manufacturing plants at 90%+ capacity; prices of manufactured goods up nearly 5%/year after 8 years of stability; member banks' free reserves exhausted
  2. Vietnam escalation: federal goods/services purchases rose $10B in 1966 (all defense), running $12B annual rate above end-1965 levels
  3. Interest rate ceiling: law prevented paying more than 4.25% on bonds over 5 years. When yields went above 4.25%, Treasury couldn't issue long bonds → flooded short-term market → T-bill yields rose 1/3 in one year
  4. Federal agencies (FNMA, Export-Import Bank) sold assets as "negative expenditures" to make deficit look smaller → competed with other short-term paper
  5. Corporate borrowing exploded: plant/equipment expenditures up $8.7B in 1966 alone; inventory accumulation $12B (vs. $5B average 1962–64); commercial paper outstanding up $4B in 1966 alone
- **Fed's response**: despite shift from positive to negative free reserves in early 1965, overall policy through spring 1966 was still expansionary. Total reserves grew 4.9%/year spring 1965 to spring 1966
- **April 1966**: Fed pressed on the brakes — "gradual reduction in reserve availability"; next month "greater reduction if growth in required reserves does not moderate substantially"
- **Result**: member bank borrowings surged from $450M (Q1) to $750M (summer) to $900M (October). Total member bank reserves shrank slightly (seasonally adjusted). Money supply followed
- **Mighty collision**: businesses and individuals rushed to borrow in excess of current needs just to assure liquidity. Long-term bond market "became so disorganized and panic-ridden that many corporations sought accommodation from banks on a temporary basis"
- **Interest rate explosion**: T-bill yield zoomed from 4.5% (April) to 5.5% (October). Prime rate climbed from 4.5% → 6% in 7 months. Top corporations borrowed long-term at highest rates since 1920. Common stocks fell to cheapest vs. earnings since 1958
- **Disintermediation**: as T-bills, agency paper, and corporate bonds rose above 5%, individuals withdrew from savings accounts and time deposits. Policy loans against insurance policies expanded as much in 1966 as in the three prior years combined
- **Housing collapse**: savings institutions lost deposits. Mutual savings bank deposit gains fell from $4B/year to barely $1B above year-end 1965. Savings & loan deposits showed virtually no gain. Non-farm housing starts collapsed from 1.3M annualized (start of 1966) to 826,000 in October — lowest since WWII
- **Autumn reversal**: Administration took two steps: (1) suspended federal agency asset sales, returning to conventional Treasury financing; (2) proposed suspension of investment tax credit to curb capital spending and reduce deficit. In January 1967, proposed general tax increase
- Fed shifted to "somewhat easier conditions in the money market" in November
- Member bank borrowings fell from $900M (October 1966) to $200M (March 1967). Reserves expanded. Interest rates fell. Stock market made new highs within 6 months of October 1966 lows
- **Three lessons of 1966**:
  1. **Expectations** matter as much as current needs. Tight money feeds on itself, creating speculative demand almost impossible to satisfy. Consequences may be more catastrophic than the inflation it was trying to avert
  2. **Excessive reliance on monetary policy is dangerous and unfair**: a tax increase takes something from everyone; tight money completely cuts off SOME sectors (housing, smaller borrowers) while leaving others relatively unscathed
  3. **Financial sophistication and interconnectedness** were underestimated. The "disintermediation" that occurred raised dangers of failures that might have touched off a 1931–33 scale crisis

## CONCLUSION: Money and Gold in the Future

- Opens with Lenin's prediction that Soviets would someday coat public lavatories with gold — expressing scorn for the tyranny of money
- William Jennings Bryan: "You shall not crucify mankind upon a cross of gold"
- The traditional debate: conservatives (protect savings, maintain price stability, prefer gold discipline) vs. reformers (impatient with arbitrary barriers to expansion, chronic unemployment)
- **The long-run history of US prices**: not a story of permanent inflation. Peacetime price record = horizontal oscillation, with as many years of declining prices as rising
- **Why no permanent inflation**: American economy is "fabulously productive and intensely profit-oriented." When demand exceeds supply and profits expand, fresh capital attracted, production expands, supply catches up, prices recede. Post-WWII inflation was brief — within 3 years most shortages overcome
- Cost of living rose average 1.5%/year from 1953–1967; under 1%/year in wholesale prices — too little to influence spending/saving decisions
- **Could acceptance of mild inflation lead to anticipatory buying and destabilization?** Evidence suggests not until prices double in six months or less. Many countries had 10–15%/year inflation without financial system breakdown
- "When all is said and done, the productivity of the American economy is the ultimate barrier to runaway inflation in our country"
- Catastrophic inflations of history (Confederacy, defeated post-WWI/WWII Europe, underdeveloped nations): result of real shortage of goods and breakdown of production — not just excess money
- Note on stocks as inflation hedge: from rational viewpoint, stocks have done MUCH BETTER in years of stable prices than rising prices. Yet P/E multiple has risen 5x faster than cost of living since WWII
- **Could a little inflation be bad?** Yes: businessmen accumulate excess inventory and overexpand, leading to eventual contraction and unemployment when capacity exceeds demand
- But the cure may be worse than disease:
  - Choosing exactly right policies at exactly right moment is "extraordinarily difficult" in such a complex economy
  - Tight money hits hardest those who least deserve it (housing, small businesses); large corporations continue to get credit
  - Risks of too LITTLE demand may be greater: lost economies of scale, rigidities, job discrimination for minorities, squeezed profit margins, 6%+ unemployment required to contain wages
  - No data proves stable-price countries grow faster than moderate-inflation countries (example: Germany and Austria both had low and high inflation respectively, with opposite growth outcomes)
- **Conclusion**: "We have no basis for believing that stable prices are essential for economic growth and full employment, nor can we necessarily argue that... we would do better to err on the side of too little demand than on the side of too much"
- **On gold and the international system**: practical case for defending gold is stronger than theoretical. Suspending convertibility or raising gold price would cause extreme chaos in international trade in short run and complete loss of confidence in long run
- The two options: (1) suspend convertibility — dollar devalued, chaos; (2) raise gold price — same effect on foreigners. But both affect only foreigners and Americans doing business abroad — domestic dollar's buying power unchanged
- Long run: if dollar devalued, German could buy dollars for 3 marks instead of 4, so American goods become very cheap, demand for dollars revives. Economic forces eventually reassert
- **Chip away at the golden foundations**: "we should continue to chip away at the golden foundations on which our domestic and international financial relationships have been built"
- Special Drawing Rights: the effort to create new internationally acceptable reserve currency to substitute for gold
- **Final conclusion**: "our wealth lies neither in the vaults at Fort Knox nor on the ledgers of our banks. Rather, it lies all around us, in what we have so prodigiously produced in the past and what we are capable of producing in the future"

## APPENDIX: Reading the Weekly Federal Reserve Statement

- Central bankers taciturn by temperament — decisions affect security prices and business activity, so they minimize hints that could encourage speculation
- Weekly Federal Reserve statement = one of the "great detective games in the financial world"
- Key items in the weekly statement:
  1. **Federal Reserve open-market operations**: purchases/sales of gov securities + repurchase agreements (short-term loans to gov security dealers)
  2. **Discounts and advances to member banks**: how much banks are borrowing or repaying
  3. **Float**: delays in check clearing. When weather/strikes slow mail, NY gets credited before SF is debited → two parties use same money temporarily. Runs $1–2B
  4. **Gold stock**: gold inflows/outflows affect reserves
  5. **Treasury currency outstanding**: silver certificates and coin issued by Treasury — same impact as gold certificates
  6. **Currency in circulation**: more currency out = lower reserve balances at Fed
  7. **Other deposits at Fed** (Treasury, foreign govts, non-member banks): withdrawals → member bank reserves increase; deposits → reserves decrease
  8. **Treasury cash**: gold held by Treasury without gold certificates issued against it
- **Worked example (week ending Sept. 25, 1963)**:
  - Float running off (mid-month checks clearing)
  - Currency returning after Labor Day
  - Treasury paying out more than taking in
  - Required reserves jumping (autumn seasonal business expansion)
  - Fed was SELLING gov securities → draining reserves at time of seasonal need → banks had to borrow more from discount window → free reserves dropped sharply
  - Interpretation: "The Federal Reserve made clear its intention to supply only grudgingly the reserves required by the banks to finance the rising level of business activity"
  - One-year trend: Fed was heavy buyer of gov securities, but primarily to offset currency expansion and gold losses — not providing extra stimulus
