Key Takeaways

  1. Live below your means and save 5–10% of income — preferably through tax-advantaged retirement accounts. At a 30% tax bracket, saving 10% only requires cutting spending by 7%.
  2. Eliminate all consumer debt. Paying off 12% credit card debt is equivalent to earning a guaranteed, tax-free 12% return. The healthy amount of consumer debt is zero.
  3. Invest long-term money in ownership assets (stocks, real estate, small business) — they return ~9% historically vs. ~5% for bonds and ~4% for savings. Over 40 years, $10K at 9% becomes $314K vs. $48K at 4%.
  4. Use index funds. They outperform ~75% of actively managed funds over 10+ years, primarily because of lower fees (~0.1% vs. 1.1% average).
  5. Buy term life insurance, never cash value. Cash value costs 8x more for the same coverage. Agents push it because they earn 8–10x higher commissions.
  6. Insure only against catastrophes. Raise deductibles, skip small-potato policies (extended warranties, cellphone insurance, etc.). Insurance companies pay out only 60 cents per dollar collected on average.
  7. You are your own best financial advisor. Commission-based advisors have structural conflicts of interest. If you hire help, use hourly fee-only advisors.
  8. Max out retirement accounts before all other investing. Prioritize: (1) employer plans with matching, (2) other employer/self-employed plans, (3) IRA/Roth, (4) taxable accounts.
  9. Don't try to time the market or pick stocks. Nobody can reliably predict markets. Day trading is gambling. Buy-and-hold outperforms active trading for the vast majority of investors.
  10. Own your home when you can stay 3–5+ years. Fixed mortgage payments don't rise with inflation; rent does. Over 20–30 years, owning becomes significantly cheaper.

Budgeting & Spending

  • Track spending only if you're not saving enough. If already saving well, don't obsess over every dollar.
  • The biggest lever is cutting expenses — a $1,000/year reduction invested in a retirement account compounds dramatically over decades.
  • Avoid rent-to-own (100–200% effective interest), car leases, and buying consumer items on credit.
  • Wholesale superstores save ~30% on groceries. Water filters beat bottled water with 100%+ ROI.

Debt Management

  • Bad debt (consumer) vs. good debt (real estate, education, business). Even good debt can be excessive.
  • Consumer-debt-to-income ratio >25% = danger zone; consider bankruptcy.
  • Credit counseling agencies are largely biased — funded by creditors, they function as collection agencies.
  • Strategies: transfer balances to lower-rate cards, use savings to pay off high-interest debt, convert consumer debt to mortgage debt (lower rate + tax deduction).

Investing

  • Two types: Lending (bonds, CDs) and ownership (stocks, real estate, business). Ownership assets produce higher long-term returns.
  • Asset allocation rule: Subtract your age from 110 for stock percentage (moderate risk). A 40-year-old: 70% stocks, 30% bonds.
  • International diversification: 20–35% of stock allocation should be international.
  • Rebalance every 2–3 years, not more frequently.
  • Dollar-cost averaging eases into volatile investments; best when a lump sum is a large portion of assets.
  • Avoid: day trading, options/futures, cryptocurrency speculation, limited partnerships, most hedge funds.

Taxes

  • Know your marginal tax bracket — it determines the value of every deduction and retirement contribution.
  • No-income-tax states: Alaska, Florida, Nevada, South Dakota, Tennessee, Texas, Wyoming.
  • Donating appreciated stock gives a double tax benefit: full market value deduction + avoids capital gains tax.
  • A fund averaging 14%/year pre-tax can net less than a 12%/year fund after taxes.
  • SALT deduction capped at $10,000. Mortgage interest deduction capped at $750K of debt.

Retirement Accounts

  • 401(k): $22,500 limit ($30K if 50+). Don't leave employer match money on the table.
  • Roth IRA: No upfront deduction but tax-free withdrawals. AGI limits: $138K single, $218K married.
  • SEP-IRA (self-employed): Up to 20% of net income, max $66,000.
  • Don't put in retirement accounts: Municipal bonds, annuities, limited partnerships.
  • RMDs: Start at age 73 (moving to 75 in 2033).
  • Inherited accounts: Must be emptied within 10 years.

Real Estate

  • Buy when you can stay 3–5+ years. Transaction costs require ~15% appreciation to break even.
  • 20% down avoids PMI (0.5–1% of mortgage/year).
  • 30-year mortgage offers flexibility over 15-year — you can voluntarily pay extra.
  • Better to contribute extra money to retirement accounts than pay down mortgage faster (tax deduction + financial aid protection).
  • Refinance makes sense when you can recover costs within a few years. Factor in lost tax deductions.

Insurance

  • Three laws: (1) Insure catastrophes, skip small stuff. (2) Buy broad coverage, not narrow. (3) Shop around, buy direct.
  • Life: Term only. Buy enough to replace 10–20 years of income if dependents rely on you.
  • Disability: Replace full take-home pay. Waiting period of 3–6 months reduces premiums.
  • Health: Take highest deductible you can afford. Use HSA ($3,850/$7,750 limits) for triple tax benefit.
  • Home: Get guaranteed replacement cost. Standard policies exclude flood and earthquake — buy separately.
  • Auto: $500–$1,000 deductible. Drop comprehensive/collision as car ages.
  • Umbrella: Cheap protection ($200/year for $1M). Cover at least 2x your assets.

Education Planning

  • Save for retirement first, education second. Retirement accounts aren't counted in financial aid; education savings are.
  • 529 plans: Tax-free growth/withdrawal for education. Best for affluent families unlikely to get aid.
  • Money in child's name: Colleges expect 20% annually vs. 6% in parent's name.
  • Alternatives to traditional college: Coding boot camps (~$13K), apprenticeships, trade schools.
  • Average aid discount at private colleges: ~50% of sticker price. Always apply for aid regardless.

Financial Advisors

  • Commission-based = salespeople. Percentage-of-AUM = better but still conflicted. Hourly fee-only = best.
  • Key question: “What percentage of your income comes from fees vs. commissions?” Right answer: 100% fees.
  • Verify via SEC (adviserinfo.sec.gov) or state regulators.
  • NAPFA (napfa.org) for fee-only planner referrals.

Estate Planning

  • Everyone with minor children needs a will. You don't need an attorney.
  • Living trusts avoid probate (5%+ of estate in legal fees) but don't reduce estate taxes.
  • Estate tax exemption: $12.92M individual, $25.84M married.
  • Annual gift exclusion: $17,000 per beneficiary.

Identity Theft Protection

  • Rotate free credit reports every 4 months (one agency each time) for year-round monitoring.
  • Freeze credit reports (free by law) — most effective protection.
  • One credit card is enough. Close unnecessary accounts.
  • Never give personal info on inbound calls (numbers can be spoofed).

Contrarian Ideas Worth Remembering

  1. You don't need to track every dollar if you're already saving enough.
  2. Money-market funds are preferable to bank savings despite lacking FDIC insurance.
  3. Don't count your home as a financial asset unless you plan to sell.
  4. Consumer items (cars) are not assets — many financial tools wrongly list them.
  5. “Safe” investments (savings accounts) can be the riskiest for long-term goals due to inflation.
  6. Paying down your mortgage faster is often the wrong move vs. funding retirement accounts.
  7. Most home renovations never recoup their cost.
  8. Free online financial content is often the most conflicted (advertiser influence, affiliate kickbacks).
  9. Having too much in retirement accounts can backfire (forced RMDs push you into higher brackets).
  10. Short-term stock trading is gambling, not investing.

Chapter 1: Embracing Financial Literacy

The State of Financial Literacy

  • Most Americans, especially younger adults, score below 60% on financial literacy surveys.
  • Root cause: Most were never taught personal finance. In some families, kids hear about money only during disagreements and crises.

Three Pillars of Financial Literacy

  1. Managing everyday transactions
  2. Investing for the long term
  3. Protecting your money

Unreliable Sources of Financial Information

  • Advertising conflicts: Publications may avoid articles that upset advertisers.
  • Advertorials: Content written by advertisers, sometimes not clearly labeled.
  • Affiliate relationships: Websites earn referral fees for recommendations.
  • Celebrity endorsers are dangerous: FTX spent hundreds of millions on celebrity endorsers (Shaquille O'Neil, Stephen Curry, Tom Brady, Larry David, etc.). Celebrities have no special financial knowledge.

The Wade Cook Case Study

  • Stock market average annual returns: ~9%.
  • Wade Cook promoted seminars promising 300% annualized returns ($6,000+ per seminar).
  • If you could actually earn 300% annually, starting with $10,000, you'd top the world's wealthiest list in 11 years.
  • Cook's own firm lost 89% of its money trading.
  • He eventually served a 7-year prison term for tax evasion.

Chapter 2: Establishing a Financial Foundation

Cash Flow

  • Cash flow = Income − Expenditures.
  • If you're already a good saver, you may NOT need to track spending.

Transaction Accounts

  • Consider small banks, credit unions, and online banks.
  • FDIC insurance: $250,000 single, $500,000 joint.
  • Keep only enough in checking for monthly needs.
  • Money-market funds generally pay higher rates than bank savings accounts despite lacking FDIC insurance.

Budgeting Framework

At 30% marginal tax rate, you only need to cut spending by 7% to save 10% (via tax-sheltered retirement account).

Credit Scores and Reports

  • FICO range: 300–850. Median: ~720. Best rates: mid-700s+.
  • Free credit report every 12 months from each bureau via annualcreditreport.com.
  • FICO score: $19.95 per bureau request.

Chapter 3: Measuring Your Financial Health

Common Money Mistakes

  1. Not planning
  2. Overspending
  3. Buying with consumer credit
  4. Delaying retirement saving
  5. Falling prey to financial sales pitches
  6. Not doing homework
  7. Making decisions based on emotion
  8. Not separating wheat from chaff
  9. Exposing yourself to catastrophic risk
  10. Focusing too much on money

Net Worth Calculation

  • Financial Assets − Financial Liabilities = Net Worth.
  • Exclude personal residence unless you plan to sell.
  • Consumer items (cars, clothing) are NOT assets.

Bad Debt vs. Good Debt

  • Bad debt: Consumer items that decrease in value. High interest, not tax-deductible.
  • Good debt: Real estate, small business, education. Lower interest, usually tax-deductible.
  • Healthy amount of bad debt = zero.
  • Bad debt danger ratio >25% of annual income = real trouble.

Savings Rate Target

  • At least 5–10% of annual income for longer-term goals.
  • Emergency fund: 3–6 months of living expenses.

Chapter 6: Dealing with Debt

Using Savings to Reduce Consumer Debt

  • Paying off 12% credit card = guaranteed, tax-free 12% return.
  • To justify NOT paying off 12% debt, you'd need ~18% pre-tax return.

Five Sources to Pay Down Debt

  1. Borrow against cash value life insurance
  2. Sell non-retirement investments
  3. Tap home equity
  4. Borrow against employer retirement account
  5. Borrow from family

Credit Card Traps

  • Teaser rates (1.9%) jump to ~15% after 6 months.
  • One late payment can trigger penalty rates of 19.8–29%+.
  • No grace period on new purchases if carrying a balance.

Credit Counseling Warning

  • Most agencies are funded by creditor fees = essentially collection agencies.
  • Red flag: If they offer Debt Management Programs (DMPs), do NOT work with them.
  • Recommended: Institute for Financial Literacy (207-873-0068).

Bankruptcy

  • Consider when consumer-debt-to-income exceeds 25%.
  • Chapter 7: Discharges eligible debts entirely.
  • Chapter 13: Court-supervised repayment schedule.
  • Stays on credit report up to 10 years; most lenders ignore after 5–7 years.

Chapter 7: Reducing Your Spending

Four Keys to Successful Spending

  1. Live within your means
  2. Find the best values (total long-term cost)
  3. Cut excess spending
  4. Shun consumer credit

Complaint Resolution

Document, Escalate, Share, Litigate. Always buy with credit card (if paying in full) — 60 days to dispute charges.

Rent-to-Own: The Worst Way to Buy

A $489 TV ends up costing $1,800+. Effective interest rate exceeds 100–200%.

Food Strategies

  • Install a water filtration system instead of buying bottled water — 100%+ ROI after first year.
  • Wholesale superstores (Costco, Sam's Club) charge ~30% less than retail.

Transportation

  • Cars are NOT investments. Don't finance car purchases.
  • Each 5 mph over 60 reduces fuel economy by ~7%.
  • Don't lease, don't borrow — buy with cash or buy quality used.

Insurance Premium Traps

  1. Keeping low deductibles (higher deductibles save money long-term)
  2. Covering small potential losses
  3. Failing to shop around

Chapter 10: Understanding Your Investment Choices

Bonds

  • Values move inversely to interest rates.
  • AAA = highest safety; BB or B = “junk” (1–2% default probability).
  • Longer maturity = higher yield but more price fluctuation.

Stocks

  • Historical return: ~9% annual average.
  • Average P/E ratio: ~15.
  • 20th century: Dow fell >20% during 16 different periods; average decline lasted <2 years.

Mutual Funds vs. Individual Stocks

  • Need 15–20 different securities to diversify adequately.
  • Limit individual stock holdings to no more than 20% of total investments.

Real Estate

  • Comparable to stock returns over generations.
  • Leverage: Can borrow 80–90%+ of property value.
  • Best investments: single-family homes, small multi-unit buildings.
  • Worst: Limited partnerships (10%+ commission), time-shares, second homes.

Cryptocurrencies — Critique

No recourse, no inherent value, not truly scarce (25,000+ exist), not widely accepted, problematic exchanges (FTX collapse).

Annuities

Only consider AFTER fully funding all tax-deductible retirement accounts.

Chapter 11: Investing in Funds

Index Funds

  • Over 10+ years, index funds outperform ~75% of peers.
  • Average U.S. stock fund expense ratio: 1.1%. Index fund: ~0.1%.
  • Vanguard is the largest and lowest-cost provider.

Fund Selection Criteria

  1. Read the prospectus
  2. Keep costs low — invest in no-load funds
  3. Evaluate 5–10 year historic performance
  4. Assess fund manager and family reputation
  5. Rate tax friendliness (for non-retirement accounts)

Loads (Sales Commissions)

Range from 3% to 8.5% of investment — avoid entirely.

Chapter 13: Investing in Taxable Accounts

Money-Market Fund Selection

  • Not high tax bracket: Fidelity Government Cash Reserves, Vanguard Cash Reserves
  • High state bracket: U.S. Treasury money-market funds
  • High federal AND state: State-focused tax-free money-market funds
  • High federal, low state: Federal-tax-free-only money-market funds

CDs vs. Bonds

  • Expect 1–2% more per year in bond funds than CDs.
  • Don't buy CDs just for FDIC insurance.

Chapter 14: Paying for College

College Costs

  • Private: ~$59,000/year. Public (in-state): ~$29,000/year.
  • Alternatives: coding boot camps (~$13,000), apprenticeships, trade schools.
  • Average private college financial aid discount: ~50% of sticker price.

Retirement Accounts and Financial Aid

  • Retirement plan balances are NOT counted as assets in financial aid analysis.
  • Colleges expect 20% of money in child's name annually vs. 6% in parent's name.
  • Save in YOUR name, not the child's.

529 Plans

  • Can put $300,000+ per beneficiary.
  • Front-loading: up to $85,000 per donor ($150,000 married) at once.
  • Tax-free growth and withdrawal for education expenses.
  • Can fund up to $10,000/year for K–12 private school.

Loan Programs

  • Federal student loans have variable rates tied to Treasury bills, with caps.
  • PLUS loans: parents can borrow full remaining amount after other aid.
  • IRA withdrawals for college: penalty-free but taxed as income — absolute last resort.

Chapter 15: Investing in Real Estate

Buy vs. Rent

  • Don't buy unless you can stay 3+ years (preferably 5+).
  • Transaction costs require ~15% appreciation to break even.
  • Lenders limit housing costs to 35–40% of gross income.

Mortgage Multiplier (by interest rate)

3%: 5.0x income | 5%: 4.2x | 7%: 3.5x | 9%: 2.9x

Fixed vs. Adjustable Rate

  • ARM typical caps: 2%/year, 5–6% lifetime.
  • Loans to avoid: balloon, negative amortization, interest-only, prepayment penalties.

Down Payment

  • 20% down avoids PMI (0.5–1.0% of mortgage/year).
  • 15-year vs. 30-year: 30-year offers flexibility; can make extra payments voluntarily.

Refinancing Payback

Factor in lost tax deductions: $100/month savings at 27% rate = $73 real savings.

Reverse Mortgages (age 62+)

  • Tax-free payments from lender against equity.
  • Alternative: sell and use capital gains exclusion ($250K single, $500K married).

Selling

Agent commissions: 5–6% (negotiable). Above $500K, push for 4%.

Chapter 16: Insurance Fundamentals

Three Laws of Insurance

  1. Insure for big stuff, not small stuff. Raise deductibles ($250 to $1,000 saves 15–20%).
  2. Buy broad coverage. Life insurance > flight insurance. Major medical > cancer insurance.
  3. Shop around and buy direct. Commissions are never disclosed in documents.

Policies to Avoid

Extended warranties, home warranty plans, dental (individual), credit life/disability, daily hospitalization, mail package insurance, cellphone insurance. On average, insurance companies pay out only 60 cents per dollar collected. Many small-potato policies pay back ~20 cents.

Commission Rates

  • Life and disability: 50% of first year's premium.
  • Cash value life insurance: 80–100% of first year's premium.

Chapter 17: Insurance on You

Life Insurance

Who doesn't need it: Single without children, working couples on one income, independently wealthy, retired, minor children.

How much: Multiply after-tax income by factor based on years to replace (5 years = 4.5x, 10 = 8.5x, 20 = 15x, 30 = 20x).

Term vs. Cash Value: Buy Term

  • Cash value costs ~8x (800%) more for the same coverage.
  • Agent commissions: 8–10x more for cash value than term.
  • Cash value = “the most oversold insurance and financial product in history.”

Disability Insurance

  • More than 1/3 of all disabilities strike people under 45.
  • Replace full monthly take-home pay.
  • If YOU pay premiums, benefits are tax-free.
  • Recommended waiting period: 3–6 months.

Health Insurance

  • ACA: Must cover adult children to age 26. No preexisting condition exclusions.
  • Open enrollment: November 1 – January 15.
  • HSA contribution limits: $3,850 single / $7,750 family (requires high-deductible plan).
  • Medical expense deduction threshold: 7.5% of AGI.

Chapter 18: Estate Planning

Wills

  • Essential when you have minor children.
  • You do NOT need an attorney — three witnesses make it valid.

Living Trusts

  • Avoid probate (5%+ of estate in legal fees).
  • Most valuable for: age 60+, single, assets >$100,000, property in multiple states.
  • Do NOT minimize estate taxes — only avoid probate.

Estate Tax Thresholds

  • Individual: $12.92 million exempt. Married: $25.84 million.
  • Annual gift exclusion: $17,000 per beneficiary.

Chapter 19: Working with Financial Planners

Three Compensation Models

  1. Commission-based (worst): Not really planners — they're salespeople.
  2. Percentage-of-AUM (better but flawed): ~1% annually. Won't recommend strategies that reduce their asset pool.
  3. Hourly-based (Tyson's preferred): $100–several hundred/hour. No product sales = objectivity.

Major Conflicts of Interest

  • Not recommending employer retirement plans (no commission in it).
  • Ignoring consumer debts (paying off reduces investable assets).
  • Not recommending real estate or small business (takes money away from securities).
  • Creating dependency by making finances seem impossibly complicated.

Media Warnings

  • Stock market panic: Dow fell below 6,500 in early 2009; has since risen more than 5x. Those who sold missed the recovery.

Chapter 22: Survival Guide for Life Changes

First Job

Start saving from first paycheck: 5%, then increase to 10%.

Getting Married

  • Discuss finances before marriage. Never hide money or debts.
  • Coordinate employer benefits — save more in the better plan.

Having Children

  • Households with school-age children spend ~20% more than childless households.
  • Child-care tax credit: up to $2,000 per child under 17.
  • Secure health, disability, and life insurance BEFORE pregnancy/birth.

Starting a Small Business

  • Live as Spartan as possible while employed.
  • COBRA: Continue employer health coverage for 18 months after leaving.
  • SEP-IRA: Shelter up to 20% of business income from taxes.

Divorcing

  • Separate emotions from finances. Don't rely on divorce attorney for financial/tax advice.
  • You do NOT need an attorney to get divorced.

Receiving a Windfall

  • Do NOT rush to invest. Park in money market fund while educating yourself.
  • Beware brokers/advisors who aggressively pursue you.
  • Priority: pay down debts, then diversify.

Retiring

  • Plan activities, not just finances.
  • Pension option: 75% joint and survivor is popular middle ground.
  • You may no longer need disability insurance but may need more liability insurance.

Chapter 23: Ten Tactics to Thwart Identity Theft

  1. Never give personal info over phone on inbound calls (numbers can be spoofed).
  2. Never respond to phishing emails.
  3. Review monthly financial statements.
  4. Secure and shred receipts.
  5. Close unnecessary credit accounts. You only need 1 card.
  6. Review credit reports (rotate 3 agencies every 4 months = year-round monitoring for free).
  7. Freeze credit reports (free by law) or place fraud alerts (1 year initial, 7 years extended).
  8. Keep personal info off checks.
  9. Protect computer with firewall and virus protection.
  10. Protect mail: use locked mailbox, use fraud-prevention pens, sign up for automatic bill payment.

Tyson's Keys to Personal Financial Success

  1. Don't buy depreciating consumer items on credit. Use debt only for things that gain value: real estate, a business, or an education.
  2. Credit cards are for convenience, not for carrying debt. If you tend to run up balances, switch to cash, checks, and debit cards only.
  3. Live within your means. Many people engaging in conspicuous consumption are borrowing against their future; some end up bankrupt.
  4. Save and invest at least 5 to 10 percent of your income. Preferably through a retirement savings account for the tax reduction.
  5. Avoid financial products with high commissions and expenses.
  6. Never purchase a financial product you don't understand.
  7. Invest the majority of long-term money in ownership vehicles (stocks, real estate, your own business).
  8. Avoid emotionally based financial decisions.
  9. Ignore financial prognosticators. Nobody has a working crystal ball.
  10. Own your home. In the long run, owning is more cost-effective than renting.
  11. Purchase broad insurance for catastrophes; eliminate insurance for small potential losses.
  12. If married, discuss joint goals.
  13. Hire yourself first. You are the best financial person you can hire.
  14. Invest in yourself and others. Education, health, relationships.
