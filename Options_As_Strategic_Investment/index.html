Glossary

Core Option Terminology

Call option: The right — not obligation — to buy 100 shares of stock at a fixed price before a specified date. Example: a July 50 call gives you the right to buy XYZ at $50 per share any time until July expiration.

Put option: The right — not obligation — to sell 100 shares at a fixed price before expiration. A July 50 put lets you sell XYZ at $50 even if the stock falls to $30.

Strike price (striking price): The fixed price at which the option holder may buy (call) or sell (put). Intervals are 2½ pts for stocks under $35, 5 pts for most stocks, 10 pts for stocks over $200.

Expiration date: The Saturday after the third Friday of the expiration month. All equity options expire on one of three standard cycles: Jan/Apr/Jul/Oct, Feb/May/Aug/Nov, or Mar/Jun/Sep/Dec.

Premium: The price of an option contract. A premium of $3 on a standard 100-share contract = $300 cost.

Intrinsic value: How far the option is in-the-money. A call with strike 50 on a stock at 57 has 7 pts of intrinsic value. Options cannot have negative intrinsic value.

Time value premium: Option price minus intrinsic value. A call worth $9 with $7 intrinsic has $2 of time premium. Time value decays to zero at expiration.

In-the-money (ITM): A call is ITM when stock price exceeds strike; a put is ITM when stock price is below strike. Deep ITM options trade close to intrinsic value.

Out-of-the-money (OTM): A call is OTM when stock is below strike; a put is OTM when stock is above strike. OTM options consist entirely of time premium.

At-the-money (ATM): Option whose strike is closest to the current stock price. ATM options carry the most time value premium and have delta near 0.50.

Greeks

Delta: Rate of change of option price per $1 move in the underlying stock. Deep ITM calls approach delta 1.0; ATM calls are near 0.50; OTM calls approach 0. Put delta equals call delta minus 1, so puts always have negative delta.

Gamma: Rate of change of delta per $1 stock move. Highest for ATM short-term options. A short straddle has large negative gamma — the position becomes aggressively directional on any significant price move.

Theta: Daily time decay — how much value an option loses per calendar day. Largest for ATM near-expiration options. Positive theta means time works in your favor (short options); negative theta means time works against you (long options).

Vega: Change in option price per 1% change in implied volatility. Always positive for long options; long-term options have the highest vega. Selling options means negative vega — any volatility spike is your enemy.

Rho: Change in option price per 1% change in the risk-free interest rate. Matters most for LEAPS and ITM options; relatively unimportant for short-term equity options.

Equivalent Stock Position (ESP): The delta-equivalent number of shares held by an option position. ESP = quantity × delta × 100. A delta-neutral position has ESP = 0.

Volatility Concepts

Historical volatility (HV): Annualized standard deviation of daily log price changes. Calculated as: daily std dev × √260. Common lookback periods: 10, 20, 50, or 100 trading days.

Implied volatility (IV): The volatility value that, plugged into the Black-Scholes formula, produces the observed market price. Each option has its own IV; a composite IV is computed by weighting individual options by volume and proximity to the ATM strike.

Volatility skew: Different options on the same stock trade at different implied volatilities across strikes. Index markets show a "reverse skew" — OTM puts carry higher IV than OTM calls. Commodity markets often show the opposite ("forward skew").

IV percentile: Where current IV falls within the historical range of the past 600 trading days. McMillan's rule: only buy volatility below the 20th percentile; only sell above the 80th percentile.

Implied deviation: Standard deviation of individual option IVs across all strikes and months, normalized by average IV. A reading above 20–25% signals an exploitable skew.

Strategy Mechanics

Covered call write: Long stock plus short call against it. The short call caps upside but generates premium income and partial downside protection.

Naked (uncovered) option: An option sold without an offsetting stock or option position. Carries theoretically unlimited risk for naked calls; large risk for naked puts.

Delta-neutral: A position whose total position delta (ESP) equals zero — no immediate directional bias. Achieved by setting ratio of options to stock such that their deltas cancel. The position only stays neutral momentarily as the underlying moves.

Ratio write: Long 100 shares plus short 2 or more calls. Creates a position with max profit at the written strike, declining profit outside the break-even range.

Calendar spread (time spread): Same strike, different expiration months. The near-term option is sold; the longer-term is bought. Profits from faster near-term time decay.

Butterfly spread: Long 1 lower-strike option, short 2 middle-strike options, long 1 higher-strike option (equal spacing). Max profit at the middle strike; limited risk.

Backspread (reverse ratio spread): Sell fewer lower-strike options, buy more higher-strike options. Net credit; profits from large moves in one direction.

Conversion / Reversal: Arbitrage positions locking in riskless profit when put-call parity is violated. Conversion: long stock + long put + short call. Reversal: the opposite. Kept in balance by arbitrageurs continuously.

Box spread: A bull call spread plus a bear put spread on the same strikes. The position is always worth exactly the difference between strikes at expiration — riskless if bought below that value.

LEAPS: Long-term Equity Anticipation Securities. Listed options with 2+ years to expiration. Same mechanics as short-term options but with much slower time decay and higher sensitivity to interest rates and dividends.

SPAN margin: Standardized Portfolio Analysis of Risk. A 16-scenario risk array that tests positions across price moves and volatility changes; the largest loss across scenarios becomes the margin requirement.

60/40 rule: For index and futures options, gains and losses are treated as 60% long-term and 40% short-term regardless of holding period. Mark-to-market at year-end.

Chapter 1: Definitions — Option Basics

Every options strategy traces back to a handful of foundational concepts, and McMillan spends the opening chapter building those concepts with precision. An option is a contract giving its holder the right — but never the obligation — to buy or sell 100 shares of stock at a stated price before a specific date. The price is called the striking price; the date is expiration; and the market price paid for that right is the premium. The Options Clearing Corporation (OCC) stands as guarantor of every listed option, meaning the creditworthiness of the original seller is irrelevant — the OCC ensures performance. Assignment, when it comes, is distributed randomly among all short holders of that contract.

The price of any option divides cleanly into two components. Intrinsic value is how far the option is in-the-money — a call with a $50 strike on a $57 stock holds $7 of intrinsic value. Everything above intrinsic is time value premium, which reflects the probability that the option will gain additional value before expiration. At expiration, time value is zero without exception; the option is worth intrinsic value or nothing. Understanding this decomposition is the starting point for every strategy in the book.

Six variables drive option prices, and McMillan lists them in order of importance. Stock price is primary — calls rise as stock rises, puts fall. Strike price determines intrinsic value. Time to expiration gives the option its opportunity to reach profitability; more time always means more premium. Volatility is the most actionable factor for traders — higher volatility expands time premium on all options regardless of direction. Interest rates affect the carrying cost relationship between puts and calls, generally boosting call prices slightly. Dividends reduce call value because they flow to stock holders, not option holders.

Time decay is not linear. McMillan presents the square-root-of-time relationship: a three-month option decays roughly twice as fast as a nine-month option. More practically, an option loses approximately two-thirds of its time value in the second half of its life. The final weeks before expiration see the steepest decay, which creates the asymmetry that every premium seller tries to exploit and every premium buyer must overcome.

Delta is the hedge ratio — the rate at which an option price moves relative to the underlying stock. Deep in-the-money calls have delta near 1.0 and move essentially point-for-point with stock. At-the-money calls carry delta around 0.50 for short-term options and closer to 0.625 for longer-term. OTM calls approach zero. Put delta equals call delta minus 1, producing negative values: an ATM put moves about $0.50 opposite to each $1 stock move. LEAPS at-the-money calls carry delta near 0.70, materially higher than equivalent short-term options, reflecting the greater certainty of large-cap stocks over long horizons.

The auto-exercise rule removes a common source of confusion: any option that is at least 37.5 cents in-the-money at expiration is automatically exercised by the OCC. Holders don't need to submit instructions; the system acts on their behalf. This matters most for covered call writers who forget they're short a call and wake up Monday morning having been assigned — or for spread traders whose short leg is exercised while their long leg is not.

Strike price intervals follow a standardized schedule: stocks under $35 use 2½-point intervals, most stocks use 5-point intervals, and high-priced stocks above $200 use 10-point intervals. Expiration cycles assign each stock to one of three quarterly series — the Jan/Apr/Jul/Oct cycle, the Feb/May/Aug/Nov cycle, or the Mar/Jun/Sep/Dec cycle. However, the two nearest calendar months are always available regardless of cycle, so a stock on the January cycle will always have February and March listed even though those months aren't part of its natural cycle.

McMillan closes the chapter by introducing the OCC's role as the silent infrastructure of the listed options market. Before the OCC was established in 1973, options were bilateral contracts between buyer and seller, subject to counterparty default. The OCC's creation — coinciding with the founding of the CBOE — transformed options from bespoke private contracts into standardized fungible instruments that could be bought, sold, and closed on an exchange. This standardization is what makes rolling, spreading, and complex multi-leg strategies possible.

Chapter 2: Covered Call Writing

Covered call writing is the most conservative options strategy and the first one most investors encounter. The writer owns 100 shares of stock and sells a call against that position. If the stock remains below the strike at expiration, the call expires worthless and the writer keeps the premium. If the stock rises above the strike, the shares are called away at the strike price. The premium received in either case partially offsets any downside and represents the ceiling on potential return.

McMillan distinguishes sharply between in-the-money and out-of-the-money writes, and the difference shapes everything. An ITM covered write — selling a call whose strike is below the current stock price — delivers more premium immediately and provides more downside protection, but it also caps the upside aggressively. The call is almost certain to be exercised if the stock stays flat or rises, making the position appropriate for neutral-to-mildly-bearish outlooks. An OTM write collects less premium but allows more stock appreciation before the shares are called away, suitable for investors who remain moderately bullish but want some income cushion.

Return calculations require care. McMillan defines three distinct return measures: return if exercised (the annualized return assuming the stock is called away at expiration), return if unchanged (the annualized premium income if the stock stays flat), and downside break-even (how far the stock can fall before the writer starts losing money). Each must include commissions — the transaction costs on 100-share lots were significant when this book was written, and ignoring them inflates paper returns by a full percentage point or more.

The net investment calculation uses 50% margin on the stock position, then subtracts the premium received. For a stock at $48 with a call sold at $4, the net investment per contract is roughly $2,000 ($4,800 × 50% margin = $2,400, minus $400 premium = $2,000 net). Annualizing a 2-month write at this return rate gives the writer a benchmark for comparing opportunities across strikes and expirations. The selection criterion McMillan prefers is a monthly return of 2–4% combined with a strike no more than 3–4% OTM — positions that produce reliable income without sacrificing most of the stock's upside.

Follow-up actions separate skilled covered writers from passive ones. If the stock rises dramatically, the writer can roll up — buy back the written call and sell a higher-strike call — to capture more appreciation, usually at a net debit. If the stock falls, the writer can roll down — buy back and write a lower strike — which reduces the break-even but locks in a partial loss if the stock subsequently recovers. Rolling forward means buying back the expiring call and selling the next month at the same strike, typically for a small credit that extends the income stream. McMillan warns against rolling down mechanically: it commits the writer to a lower strike just as a recovery might be beginning.

The incremental return concept captures the mindset of the most systematic covered writers. Rather than agonizing over whether to be called away, they select a target price for each stock and simply sell the highest available strike each month — accumulating premium so long as the stock remains below the target. Assignment is not a failure; it's the highest-return outcome. This disciplined approach treats covered writing as a repeating income system rather than a stock-picking exercise.

McMillan also introduces an important equivalence that recurs throughout the book: a covered call write has exactly the same profit-and-loss profile at expiration as a naked put write at the same strike. Both strategies profit when the stock stays flat or rises, lose when it falls, and collect the same net premium. The covered write requires owning stock and selling a call; the naked put requires selling a put secured by cash or margin. Which is better depends on margin efficiency, tax treatment, and the dividend on the underlying stock — but the risk profiles are identical.

Chapter 3: Call Buying

Call buying is the purest bullish speculation in the options toolkit — you pay a premium for the right to profit if the stock rises above the strike, with maximum loss capped at the premium paid. The leverage is the appeal: a stock moving from $50 to $58 produces a 16% gain, but an ATM call might double or triple in that same move. The danger is the same source of leverage — time is always working against the buyer.

Choosing which call to buy requires balancing three competing factors: cost, delta, and time remaining. A slightly in-the-money call has a high delta (0.60–0.75), meaning it captures most of the stock's move, with relatively lower percentage risk of total loss since it already has intrinsic value. An at-the-money call offers maximum time value and maximum sensitivity to volatility changes. A deep out-of-the-money call is cheap in absolute terms but carries high probability of expiring worthless — the stock must move significantly just to reach break-even. McMillan consistently steers readers toward slightly ITM or ATM options as the best risk-reward tradeoff for directional buyers.

Follow-up discipline matters as much as strike selection. If a call rises 20–30% quickly, taking profits is often wiser than hoping for more — time decay starts eroding gains immediately. If the stock has moved far and the call is now deep ITM with little remaining time premium, rolling up to a higher strike captures the profit while maintaining upside exposure for less capital. The most profitable call buying combines a genuine directional catalyst with proper exit planning rather than holding passively and hoping.

McMillan's most important caution is about time: buyers must anticipate not just that the stock will move, but that it will move soon enough. A call that is correct directionally but expires before the catalyst fires produces a loss. This is why he recommends never holding options through the final few weeks of heavy decay unless the position is already strongly profitable and the underlying is moving in the right direction.

Chapter 4: The Reverse Hedge

The reverse hedge — also called a simulated straddle — predates the existence of listed puts and represents the original way traders bet on large moves without directional commitment. The setup is short 100 shares of stock combined with long 2 (or more) calls at the same strike. If the stock rallies, the calls more than offset the short stock loss. If the stock collapses, the short stock profits while the calls expire worthless. Maximum loss occurs when the stock pins exactly at the strike at expiration — neither the calls nor the short produces much profit.

A simpler variant, the protected short sale, combines a long call with a short stock position. This limits the upside risk on the short to the call premium paid, producing a profit profile nearly identical to a long put. McMillan notes that once listed puts became available in 1977, these simulated positions largely became obsolete — buying a put directly is cheaper (one commission versus two), requires less margin, and avoids the dividend risk of being short stock. The chapter survives in the book primarily for historical context and for markets where puts may not yet trade.

Chapter 5: Naked Call Writing

A naked call writer sells calls without owning the underlying stock — collecting premium immediately in exchange for unlimited upside risk if the stock rallies sharply. It is the most dangerous single-option strategy in the book and McMillan treats it with corresponding seriousness. The margin requirement is calculated as the greater of 20% of stock price plus the premium minus the out-of-the-money amount, or 10% of stock price plus the premium — and is marked to market daily as the stock moves. A writer who does not post additional margin during an adverse move faces forced liquidation.

Two distinct philosophies drive naked call writing. The first is selling OTM calls for income — writing calls with strikes well above the current stock price, collecting smaller premiums but maintaining a high probability of expiring worthless. This works well when implied volatility is high and the stock has limited upside catalyst. The second approach — selling deep ITM calls with delta near 1.0 as a stock substitute — is a synthetic short position that McMillan explicitly deprecates now that listed puts exist. A purchased put achieves the same bearish exposure with limited risk and no margin requirement.

The critical risk management rule for naked calls is simple: close the position if the stock approaches the strike. Naked call writers who hope the stock will reverse and allow a cheap repurchase are making a speculative decision on top of an already-losing position. McMillan is direct: the theoretical edge in naked call writing comes from selling overpriced premium, not from correctly predicting that losing positions will recover.

Chapter 6: Ratio Call Writing

Ratio call writing is covered writing taken one step further: the investor owns 100 shares of stock but sells 2 (or more) calls against that position instead of 1. The extra calls are naked — unhedged by stock — which creates unlimited risk if the stock rallies far above the strike. In exchange, the writer collects twice the premium and creates a profit zone that is wider than a covered write. The maximum profit occurs exactly at the written strike at expiration.

McMillan provides specific formulas for the break-even levels. With stock at 49, selling 2 October 50 calls at $6 each, the maximum profit is 13 points (strike minus stock plus two call premiums). The downside break-even is 37; the upside break-even is 63. The profit zone is 26 points wide, centered at the strike. Beyond those levels, the writer faces losses — accelerating rapidly on the upside because the extra naked call has unlimited risk above the break-even.

The variable ratio write — sometimes called a trapezoidal hedge — is a refinement that widens the maximum profit zone. Instead of selling two calls at the same strike, the writer sells one ITM call and one OTM call against 100 shares. The stock sits between the two strikes. Maximum profit is the entire time value received from both calls, and it persists across the zone between the two strikes rather than pinning to a single point. The downside break-even is the lower strike minus total time value; the upside break-even is the higher strike plus total time value.

The neutral ratio concept gives the ratio write a quantitative foundation. The number of calls to sell equals 1 divided by the delta of the call being sold. If the call has a delta of 0.50, selling 2 calls per 100 shares creates a delta-neutral position at inception. McMillan is explicit that this neutrality is momentary — every stock move shifts deltas and the position begins accumulating directional bias almost immediately. Continuous adjustment is impractical for retail traders due to commissions.

The Equivalent Stock Position (ESP) provides a unified way to measure the true directional exposure of any combination of stock and options. ESP = quantity × delta × 100. A position long 300 shares and short 5 calls with delta 0.80 has ESP = 300 − (5 × 0.80 × 100) = 300 − 400 = −100 shares. The position is effectively short 100 shares of equivalent stock regardless of its physical construction. ESP allows comparison across wildly different positions and structures.

Follow-up actions for ratio writes require judgment about which risk is materializing. If the stock rallies strongly, the writer should roll up: buy back the ITM calls and sell calls at the next higher strike, maintaining the same 2:1 ratio. This action moves the profit zone higher but may require a debit. If the stock falls, the writer rolls down similarly. McMillan's preferred defensive tool is stop orders placed in advance — a buy stop above the position converts it to a covered write if triggered; a sell stop below converts it to naked calls. Pre-committing to these levels removes the emotional pressure of deciding under duress.

Chapter 7: Bull Spreads

A bull call spread — buying a lower-strike call and selling a higher-strike call in the same expiration — is the simplest way to take a moderately bullish position with defined risk on both sides. The buyer pays a net debit equal to the difference in premiums. Maximum profit equals the difference between strikes minus the debit, realized when the stock closes above the higher strike. Maximum loss is the debit, realized when the stock closes below the lower strike. Break-even is the lower strike plus the net debit.

The spread is always entered for a debit because the lower strike call costs more than the higher strike. An example: stock at $32, buy October 30 call at $3, sell October 35 call at $1. Net debit: $2. Maximum profit: $3 (if stock above $35). Break-even: $32. The spread costs two commissions to enter and two to close — four legs total — which McMillan notes must be accounted for in any return calculation.

Bull spreads make most sense when implied volatility is high and outright call buying is expensive. The short call partially funds the long call while capping the upside. The trade-off is giving up any profit above the higher strike, which is acceptable if the bullish thesis is moderate rather than aggressive. McMillan also presents the bull spread as a substitute for covered writing: buy the spread with a small capital outlay and invest the rest in T-bills, producing a similar return profile with far less capital committed and no stock ownership.

Chapter 8: Bear Spreads

A bear call spread — selling a lower-strike call and buying a higher-strike call — produces a net credit and profits when the stock stays below the lower strike at expiration. Maximum profit is the credit received; maximum loss is the difference between strikes minus the credit. Margin equals the spread width minus the credit. McMillan identifies a significant problem with bear call spreads: the written call is in-the-money (or close to it), creating early exercise risk before the position becomes profitable.

This early exercise vulnerability leads McMillan to prefer bear put spreads as the superior vehicle for bearish speculation: buy a higher-strike put, sell a lower-strike put. The written put is OTM, so there's no early exercise risk at inception. ITM puts lose time value faster than ITM calls, so the spread widens quickly when the stock falls — producing faster profits. The entire bear spread chapter ultimately serves as a setup for the put spread chapter later in the book, where McMillan makes the case for puts as the superior bearish tool.

Chapter 9: Calendar Spreads

A calendar spread sells a near-term call and buys a longer-term call at the same strike. The near-term option decays faster, creating a spread that widens as time passes — so long as the stock remains near the strike. This is the pure neutral strategy: you profit from time passing without major stock movement.

McMillan distinguishes two philosophies. The neutral calendar uses an at-the-money strike. If the stock pins at the strike through near-term expiration, the short call expires worthless while the long call retains significant time premium — the spread earns its maximum profit. The risk is any large stock move: if the stock travels far from the strike, both options approach parity simultaneously, the spread collapses toward zero, and the debit is lost.

The bullish calendar uses an OTM call. Near-term, the small debit is at risk while waiting for the near-term call to expire worthless. Then, with the longer-term call owned at minimal net cost, a subsequent stock rally produces large profits. This combines a short-term neutral bet with a longer-term bullish position for a low total debit.

The ideal time to enter a calendar spread is when near-term implied volatility is elevated relative to longer-term — perhaps before an earnings announcement that creates near-term uncertainty. Selling expensive near-term premium against cheaper long-term optionality captures the term structure discount. McMillan's roll decision rule: close the near-term position when it trades at half the original spread cost, then evaluate whether the remaining long call justifies continued holding.

Chapter 10: Butterfly Spreads

A butterfly spread is built from three strikes in equal intervals: buy one lower-strike call, sell two middle-strike calls, buy one higher-strike call. It is the natural extension of combining a bull spread and a bear spread — the two purchased options form wings; the two sold options form the body. Maximum profit occurs when the stock closes exactly at the middle strike at expiration. Maximum loss is the net debit, incurred if the stock closes outside either wing.

McMillan's numerical example: buy the 60 call, sell two 70 calls, buy the 80 call. Net debit: $2. Maximum profit: $8 (at stock = 70). Break-evens: $62 and $78. The profit-to-risk ratio of 4:1 at the optimal point is attractive. The problem is that four commissions plus the bid-ask spread on each leg make butterflies expensive to execute, and the actual range of maximum profit is narrow. McMillan's criterion: only enter a butterfly if maximum profit is at least 3–4 times the maximum risk. This screens out the many butterfly setups that look good on paper but fail after transaction costs.

Chapter 23 later reveals that butterflies can be constructed four different ways: all calls, all puts, call bull spread plus put bear spread, or call bear spread plus put bull spread. All four produce identical payoffs at expiration, but McMillan recommends the call bull spread plus put bear spread combination because it uses OTM options exclusively — eliminating early exercise risk on any written leg.

Chapter 11: Ratio Call Spreads

A ratio call spread buys one (or more) lower-strike calls and sells two (or more) higher-strike calls. Unlike a ratio write, both legs are options — so the downside risk is limited to the net debit (or zero if entered for a credit), but upside risk remains unlimited above the break-even. This trades the large downside risk of a ratio write for a smaller initial investment.

Two approaches dominate practice. The simulated ratio write buys a deep ITM call at parity (no time premium) and sells two OTM calls — closely replicating a ratio write's P&L but with capped downside. The credit ratio spread establishes the position for a net credit when both strikes are OTM: if the stock stays below the lower strike, both options expire worthless and the writer keeps the credit with zero risk on the downside.

Delta-neutral ratio spreads set the ratio by dividing the delta of the long call by the delta of the short call. With a 40-strike call at delta 0.80 and a 45-strike call at delta 0.50, the neutral ratio is 8:5 — buy 5 lower calls, sell 8 upper calls. This is the starting point; follow-up action adjusts the ratio as the underlying moves. If the stock rallies strongly, buying additional lower-strike calls reduces the ratio toward 1:1, converting a risky position into a simple bull spread.

Chapter 12: Calendar and Ratio Combinations

The ratio calendar spread sells two near-term OTM calls and buys one longer-term OTM call at the same strike — ideally for a credit. If the stock sits still and the near-term calls expire worthless, the trader owns the longer-term call for free. This "free ride" makes the strategy seductive. The risk is obvious: if the stock rallies before the near-term calls expire, the two short calls create unlimited exposure with only one long call as partial hedge. McMillan insists on planning defensive action before entering — knowing exactly at what stock price to buy back the short calls and convert to a simple long call.

The delta-neutral variant uses the delta ratio to determine the neutral number of near-term calls to sell: if the short call has delta 0.30 and the long call has delta 0.20, the neutral ratio is 1.5:1. The OTM version involves naked calls; the ITM version buys more long calls than it sells (creating no naked exposure but a larger debit). The chapter establishes the principle that combining a calendar component (different expirations) with a ratio component (different quantities) creates four-dimensional positions that are both powerful and hazardous.

Chapter 13: Reverse Spreads

A reverse calendar spread sells the longer-term call and buys the near-term call — the opposite of a standard calendar. It profits from large stock moves or from implied volatility decreasing. The problem for retail traders is margin: brokers treat the long near-term call as naked for margin purposes since it expires before the short long-term call. The position requires substantial collateral relative to its reward.

The backspread (reverse ratio spread) is more practical: sell one lower-strike call, buy two higher-strike calls. Done for a credit, the position has no downside risk — if the stock falls below the written strike, all options expire worthless and the trader keeps the credit. Maximum loss occurs when the stock closes exactly at the long strike at expiration (where the two OTM calls are worthless but the short call is fully ITM). If the stock rallies far above the long strikes, the two long calls produce unlimited profit. Example: sell one July 40 call at $4, buy two July 45 calls at $1 each. Credit: $2. Maximum loss: $3 (at stock = 45). Upside break-even: $48.

Backspreads work best when implied volatility is low — buying cheap OTM calls in size, positioned for a volatility explosion or a large directional move. The neutral delta ratio sets the initial quantities: divide the lower call's delta by the higher call's delta, then buy that many higher calls per lower call sold.

Chapter 14: Diagonalizing a Spread

Diagonalizing simply means using different strikes and different expirations simultaneously rather than matching them across one dimension only. A diagonal bull spread buys a longer-term lower-strike call and sells a near-term higher-strike call. Compared to a standard bull spread, the diagonal is slightly more bearish at the near-term expiration (because the near-term written call may expire worthless while the long call's time premium decays) but can be cheaper to establish.

The deeper insight is the "free call" construction. A diagonal bear spread — selling a near-term lower-strike call and buying a longer-term higher-strike call for a net credit — lets the near-term call expire worthless, leaving the trader owning the longer-term call at zero net cost. The long call is paid for by the near-term write. This strategy requires the stock to stay below the written strike in the near term; if it does, the trader owns the longer-dated call free and then needs only a subsequent rally to profit.

A diagonal backspread — selling one near-term lower-strike and buying two longer-term higher-strike calls for even money — extends the concept: if the near-term expires worthless, the trader owns two longer-term calls for free, with unlimited upside exposure and zero remaining risk. McMillan presents this as one of the more elegant low-risk/high-reward structures available to patient traders.

Chapter 15: Put Option Basics

Puts gain value as stock prices fall, making them the natural vehicle for bearish speculation and portfolio protection. A put is in-the-money when the stock trades below the strike, out-of-the-money when above. The dynamics of put pricing differ from calls in subtle but important ways that McMillan covers systematically.

Put-call parity defines the relationship between puts and calls at the same strike: Put = Call − Stock + Strike + PV(Strike) − Dividends. The practical consequence is that at-the-money puts always trade for less than at-the-money calls — the difference is the carrying cost of the stock (strike × interest rate × time). This relationship is maintained by arbitrageurs through conversions and reversals.

ITM puts lose time value faster than ITM calls. When a put is deep in-the-money, the holder has the right to sell stock at the strike and can earn interest on the proceeds — so the rational holder exercises early and collects cash. This creates an incentive to exercise deeply ITM puts early, particularly when interest rates are high. McMillan identifies three conditions triggering early put exercise: the put is trading at a discount to parity; a large dividend is about to make the call side more valuable; or the interest earned on exercise proceeds exceeds the remaining time premium.

Dividends affect puts in the opposite direction from calls. Stocks fall on ex-dividend dates by approximately the dividend amount, which benefits put holders. Large dividends increase put values, and traders sometimes buy puts specifically to profit from dividend-related stock price declines.

Chapter 16: Put Buying

Put buying is the straightforward bearish speculation — buy a put, profit if the stock falls. The choice of strike follows the same logic as call buying but inverted. A slightly in-the-money put (stock below strike) carries delta of −0.55 to −0.65 and provides the best balance of leverage and probability. A deep ITM put approaches delta −1.0 and moves nearly point-for-point with the stock, but it's expensive and the percentage gain is limited. An OTM put requires a larger move to profit and carries high probability of total loss.

McMillan contrasts put buying with short stock selling: the put limits risk to the premium paid with no possibility of infinite loss; there's no margin requirement for simple long puts; no dividends are owed to stock lenders; and the uptick rule that restricts short selling doesn't apply. The short sale has advantages too — unlimited profit potential on a collapse and interest earned on the short proceeds — but the risk profile favors put buying for most investors unwilling to accept unlimited downside risk.

Chapter 17: Protective Puts and Collars

A protective put — buying a put against a long stock position — creates a floor below which losses cannot exceed the premium paid. It is economically equivalent to a long call: both limit downside to a fixed amount while leaving unlimited upside. McMillan recommends slightly OTM puts as the best balance between protection cost and retained upside. The put costs money that offsets the stock's gain, so the break-even rises by the put premium.

Tax treatment complicates protective puts. If the stock was purchased less than a year ago, buying a put against it resets the holding period to zero — a rule that prevents investors from locking in a short-term gain while waiting for long-term treatment. The married put exception applies only when stock and put are purchased simultaneously with the intent to exercise; selling the put rather than exercising it triggers the short-sale rule.

The collar adds a covered write to the protective put: buy stock, sell OTM call, buy OTM put. The call premium partially or fully finances the put. In a no-cost collar, the call premium exactly equals the put premium, and the investor locks in a range of outcomes between the two strikes with zero net premium outlay. McMillan's LEAPS collar table illustrates the striking efficiency of this structure: at 50% volatility, buying a 2.5-year ATM put can be financed by selling a call only 40% out-of-the-money — allowing enormous upside before the call caps gains. The higher the volatility, the farther OTM the financing call can be written.

Chapter 18: Straddle and Strangle Buying

A straddle buy — long call plus long put at the same strike — profits from large moves in either direction. It is the quintessential volatility purchase. Maximum loss is the total premium paid, occurring when the stock closes exactly at the strike at expiration. Break-even prices are the strike plus the total premium and the strike minus the total premium. McMillan's example: stock at 50, buy July 50 call at $3 and July 50 put at $2. Total cost: $5. Profit above $55 or below $45.

Straddles should be purchased when implied volatility is low — when options are cheap relative to what the stock is likely to move. The catalyst model is common: a pending earnings announcement, FDA drug decision, or other binary event may produce a move larger than the options market is pricing. If the stock moves enough to cover the premium cost, the straddle buyer profits regardless of direction.

McMillan's follow-up rule for long straddles is critical and counterintuitive: never take small profits on the winning side. If the stock rallies and the call gains value, don't sell the call for a modest profit — that caps the unlimited upside that makes straddle buying worthwhile. Instead, roll the losing put upward: sell the put and buy a new put at a higher strike, reducing the cost basis without capping upside. This asymmetric roll captures the convexity of the long straddle.

The strangle buys an OTM call and OTM put at different strikes. It's cheaper than the straddle but requires a larger move to profit — the stock must travel outside the outer strikes rather than the single strike. The zone of maximum loss is wider (between the two strikes). McMillan notes that an in-the-money strangle — buying an ITM call and ITM put — guarantees a floor value equal to the difference between strikes while offering additional profit on large moves. The premium cost equals the guaranteed floor value, so the risk on the strangle is small but real.

Chapter 19–20: Straddle Writing

Straddle writing — selling a call and put at the same strike — is the mirror image of straddle buying. The writer collects the total premium immediately and profits if the stock stays near the strike through expiration. Maximum profit is the total premium received; losses accumulate in either direction beyond the break-even prices. The risk is unlimited on the upside and large on the downside.

The covered straddle write (owning the stock) provides partial protection: if the stock falls, the stock itself offsets the put loss. But McMillan shows that the covered straddle is actually inferior to simply owning the stock and selling the call — the covered write is better because the put adds risk without additional premium in proportion to the risk.

Naked straddle selling — McMillan's preferred form — works best when implied volatility is high. The premium collected is large; the probability that the stock will stay within the break-even range depends on the volatility priced into the options versus actual realized volatility. If IV is 40% and historical vol is 20%, the straddle writer has a substantial edge — the options are priced for twice the likely movement.

Follow-up actions for naked straddle sellers require quick responses. If the stock rallies to one break-even, the writer buys stock to create a covered call — the long stock offsets the short call risk. If the stock falls to the lower break-even, the writer sells stock short to create a covered put. The danger is a whipsaw: the stock hits the upside break-even, triggering a stock purchase, then reverses and falls to the downside break-even, now triggering a stock sale. Both protective actions lose money in a volatile environment. McMillan recommends entering straddle writes with smaller size — enough margin to absorb the follow-up cost if the worst case develops.

Chapter 21: Synthetic Positions

Synthetic positions replicate the economic exposure of one instrument using a combination of other instruments. A synthetic long stock position — long call plus short put at the same strike — produces a profit-and-loss profile identical to owning 100 shares of stock. A synthetic short stock position — short call plus long put at the same strike — replicates short stock. These equivalences arise directly from put-call parity and are maintained by arbitrageurs.

The split-strike synthetic (risk reversal) modifies the structure by using different strikes. A bullish split-strike buys an OTM call and sells an OTM put — creating a position that profits if the stock rises above the call strike, suffers if it falls below the put strike, and is flat in the zone between. This is structurally identical to a collar without the stock, or to a naked put combined with a purchased call. When options are expensive, the put premium helps finance the call, making the structure efficient for bullish investors who want leveraged upside without paying full call prices.

The bearish split-strike — buy an OTM put, sell an OTM call — replicates a protective collar on a short stock position. McMillan notes that these synthetic positions have the same margin implications as their component options; the synthetics don't create margin efficiency, only structural equivalence.

Chapter 22: Basic Put Spreads

The put bear spread — buying a higher-strike put and selling a lower-strike put — produces a net debit and profits when the stock falls. Maximum profit equals the strike difference minus the debit, realized when the stock closes below the lower strike. Maximum loss is the debit, realized when the stock closes above the higher strike. Break-even: higher strike minus net debit.

McMillan presents two reasons put bear spreads are superior to call bear spreads. First, the written put is OTM, eliminating early exercise risk before the position is profitable. Second, ITM puts lose time value faster than equivalent ITM calls — meaning the spread widens more quickly on a downward stock move, producing faster profits.

The put bull spread — buying a lower-strike put and selling a higher-strike put — is a credit spread that profits when the stock remains above the higher strike. Maximum profit is the credit received; maximum loss is the strike difference minus the credit. This is the put-side equivalent of the covered write: collecting premium against limited risk, profitable in neutral to bullish conditions.

The put calendar spread sells a near-term put and buys a longer-term put at the same strike — neutral when the stock pins at the strike through near-term expiration, bearish when using OTM puts and expecting a delayed decline. McMillan notes that put calendars are generally inferior to call calendars because puts carry less time premium (due to the carrying cost relationship), so the spread captures less differential decay.

Chapter 23: Combined Spreads

This chapter synthesizes all the spread structures and reveals how they interrelate. The butterfly can be constructed four ways — using all calls, all puts, a call bull spread plus a put bear spread, or a call bear spread plus a put bull spread — all producing identical payoffs at expiration. The recommended construction uses a call bull spread for the lower wing and a put bear spread for the upper wing, combining OTM options in both legs to eliminate early exercise risk on the written options.

The calendar combination (two-pronged calendar) sells a near-term OTM call AND a near-term OTM put while buying a longer-term OTM call AND a longer-term OTM put. The stock sits between the two strikes. If both near-term options expire worthless, the trader owns a longer-term strangle at minimal net cost and then needs a significant stock move to profit. Selection criterion: the near-term combination should cost at least half the longer-term combination to justify the structure.

The diagonal butterfly — selling a near-term straddle and buying a longer-term OTM strangle — creates a credit position. Maximum risk is the difference between adjacent strikes minus the net credit. The goal is to buy back the near-term straddle for less than the credit received, leaving the longer-term strangle owned for free. McMillan's selection rule: the near-term straddle should cost at least 1.5 times the longer-term strangle to provide enough credit cushion to make the risk/reward worthwhile.

Chapter 24: Ratio Put Spreads

A ratio put spread buys a higher-strike put and sells two (or more) lower-strike puts. There is no risk to the upside — if the stock rallies, all puts expire worthless and the trader loses only the net debit (or keeps a credit). Maximum profit occurs when the stock closes exactly at the lower strike at expiration. Below that point, the extra naked puts create unlimited downside exposure.

McMillan's example: stock at 50, buy one January 50 put at $4, sell two January 45 puts at $2 each. Cost: zero (even money). Maximum profit: $5 at stock = 45. Profit zone: $40 to $50. Downside break-even: $40. The structure works when the trader believes the stock will decline moderately but not collapse — the "soft landing" bet.

The ratio put calendar spread — buying a longer-term put and selling two or more near-term puts at the same strike — extends the concept across time. If the near-term puts expire worthless (stock above the strike), the trader owns the longer-term put at reduced cost, then positions for a subsequent decline. The critical risk: if the stock drops sharply before the near-term expiration, the naked near-term puts create large losses. McMillan's rule: close the position if the stock falls 8–10% below the written strike before the near-term puts expire.

Chapter 25: LEAPS

LEAPS (Long-term Equity Anticipation Securities) are listed options with two or more years to expiration. They follow the same mechanics as short-term options — calls give the right to buy, puts to sell — but their pricing differs in ways that create strategic opportunities unavailable in shorter-dated contracts. LEAPS eventually become standard equity options when their remaining life falls below nine months.

The most important difference is time decay. A LEAPS option with 24 months remaining loses only about 0.12% of its value per day at-the-money; the same option with 3 months remaining loses 0.60% per day; with 2 weeks remaining, over 3% per day. This slower decay allows LEAPS buyers to be right directionally over a long period without constantly fighting erosion. The trade-off is that LEAPS still lose roughly 25% of their value over a six-month period even if the stock moves favorably.

LEAPS at-the-money calls carry delta near 0.70 — materially higher than the 0.50–0.60 of short-term ATM calls. LEAPS puts show the opposite relationship: an ATM LEAPS put has delta around −0.30, much smaller in magnitude than an ATM short-term put. This asymmetry reflects the long horizon over which stocks are more likely to rise than fall, embedded in option pricing through interest rate effects.

The LEAPS stock substitute strategy captures the economic exposure of stock ownership while freeing capital and limiting risk. An investor sells stock and buys a deep ITM LEAPS call, investing the freed capital in T-bills. The analysis compares costs: time value premium paid plus dividends foregone versus interest earned on the savings. McMillan's example with stock at $50 and a 40-strike LEAPS call at $12 shows a net cost of about $1 per 100 shares — worthwhile for the downside protection to approximately $39.50.

Covered LEAPS writing — buying a LEAPS call and selling near-term calls against it — creates a diagonal spread. The LEAPS call provides ongoing "stock coverage" while the near-term calls generate monthly premium income. But McMillan warns about a counterintuitive risk: if the stock rallies rapidly and both the near-term call and the LEAPS call go deeply in-the-money simultaneously, the short call's delta can exceed the LEAPS call's delta — creating a net short position that loses on a continued rally. The safety valve is selling only OTM near-term calls against the LEAPS position.

The naked LEAPS put — McMillan's preferred alternative to covered call writing — produces identical P&L at expiration while generating superior returns. The LEAPS put writer collects a large premium and backs the position with existing stocks or bonds rather than committing cash to stock ownership. The collateral earns T-bill interest. McMillan compares a specific example where the naked LEAPS put returned 24.4% while the covered call write on the same stock returned only 21.5% — the collateral flexibility makes the difference.

Chapter 26: Straddle Selling

Selling a straddle — short call plus short put at the same strike — is one of the highest-expected-return strategies in the book but requires both capital and discipline to execute properly. The premium collected is the maximum profit; losses begin when the stock moves beyond either break-even. Short-term straddles benefit from the fastest time decay, making them more efficient than LEAPS straddle sells. McMillan explicitly warns against LEAPS straddle selling: the extremely slow decay means the position must survive two years of potential whipsaw with little theta working in its favor.

The best conditions for straddle selling: implied volatility is high relative to historical volatility; no upcoming catalysts (earnings, mergers, FDA) that would justify the elevated IV; and the stock has a recent history of trading in a range. If all three conditions align, the seller has a quantifiable edge — collecting premiums priced for 40% annual movement when the stock historically moves 20%.

Chapter 27: Arbitrage

Arbitrage in options markets refers to riskless or near-riskless profits from mispricing between related securities. McMillan covers four main structures that keep options prices aligned.

Call discount arbitrage occurs when a call trades below parity — when the stock's intrinsic value exceeds the call price. The arbitrageur buys the call, sells short the stock, and exercises immediately, locking in a riskless profit equal to the discount. This situation arises only for deep ITM calls near expiration or before large ex-dividend dates when the call rightfully should trade at zero time premium.

A conversion locks in profit when the call is overpriced relative to the put after accounting for carrying costs. The arbitrageur buys stock, buys a put, and sells a call at the same strike and expiration. The position is fully hedged; the locked-in profit equals call price minus stock minus put price plus strike plus dividends minus carrying cost. A reversal is the opposite — short stock, short put, long call — profitable when the put is overpriced. Both structures are executed continuously by market makers and institutional arbitrageurs, keeping put-call parity in line at all times.

The box spread combines a bull call spread and a bear put spread on the same strikes. The combined position is worth exactly the difference between strikes at expiration regardless of where the stock closes — it is a riskless bond. If a trader can buy the box for less than the discounted value of the strike difference (adjusted for carrying cost), the spread is a riskless profit. Box spread arbitrage is the ultimate enforcer of put-call parity.

The interest play — short stock plus a long call just above parity — earns interest on the short proceeds while the small call time premium is the cost. It demonstrates elegantly why rising interest rates increase call prices: the interest earned on the short stock position is the economic basis for call pricing above intrinsic value.

Chapter 28: Black-Scholes and Volatility

The Black-Scholes model (1973) transformed options from intuition-based instruments to mathematically priced securities. The formula C = S·N(d₁) − X·e−rT·N(d₂) prices a European call as the difference between the stock price weighted by delta and the present value of the strike weighted by the probability of exercise. Delta is not an input to the formula but an output: delta = N(d₁), where d₁ incorporates volatility, time, and moneyness. The key insight is that all option pricing reduces to one unknown — volatility — since the stock price, strike, interest rate, and time are all observable.

Historical volatility is calculated as the annualized standard deviation of daily log price changes. The procedure: take the natural log of each day's closing price divided by the prior day's; calculate the standard deviation of those log relatives; multiply by the square root of 260 (trading days per year). McMillan uses 10-day, 20-day, 50-day, and 100-day lookback periods to capture different volatility regimes — short windows are reactive; long windows are stable but slow.

Implied volatility is the volatility value that makes Black-Scholes produce the observed market price. Each option has its own implied volatility; the composite IV for a stock is computed by weighting individual option IVs by volume and by proximity to the at-the-money strike. ATM options receive the highest weight because they have the most time premium and are least distorted by other factors. The composite IV is smoothed with a 20-day moving average to filter daily noise.

The trading signal from volatility is the book's most actionable insight: when implied volatility is significantly higher than historical volatility, options are overpriced and premium selling (straddles, ratio writes) has a positive expected value. When implied is significantly below historical, options are cheap and premium buying (straddles, backspreads) has an edge. The threshold ratio of roughly 1.20 (expensive) and 0.80 (cheap) provides a starting screen, but McMillan emphasizes using the percentile approach — tracking where current IV falls in a 600-day historical range — for more reliable signals.

The probability calculator based on the lognormal distribution gives quantitative rigor to strategy evaluation. P(stock ends below K) = N[ln(K/S) / (v × √T)] where K is the target price, S is current stock price, v is annual volatility, and T is time in years. This probability approximately equals the option's delta for OTM strikes. McMillan uses it to evaluate break-even probabilities before entering any volatility trade: buying straddles requires >80% probability of reaching break-even; selling naked options requires <25% probability of reaching the written strike.

Chapter 29: Stock Index Options and Futures

Index options differ from equity options in several ways that change how strategies behave. They settle in cash — there is no delivery of stock baskets. Most major index options are European-style: they cannot be exercised early, eliminating the early exercise premium that drives dividend arbitrage in equity options. Position limits are far larger than for individual equity options, making index options the preferred vehicle for institutional hedging.

The two most important US index options are OEX (S&P 100) and SPX (S&P 500). OEX is American-style — it can be exercised any time — and has historically been the most liquid single options market in the world. SPX is European-style and uses a unique settlement mechanism: settlement value is calculated using the opening prices of all 500 components on expiration Friday morning, after trading closes Thursday. This creates a window of risk: a trader hedged with SPX positions through Thursday's close may wake up Friday to a settlement price significantly different from Thursday's close.

Portfolio hedging with index puts requires calculating the correct number of contracts. The formula: contracts needed = (portfolio value × beta) / (index level × $100). A $1 million portfolio with beta 1.2 against an index at 450 requires approximately 27 put contracts to be fully hedged. Buying ATM puts provides full protection; 5–10% OTM puts provide catastrophic protection while allowing the portfolio to absorb moderate declines independently. LEAPS puts are preferred for long-horizon hedges because they avoid the quarterly reset cost of rolling short-term protective puts.

Chapter 30: Stock Index Hedging

Portfolio managers use index options and futures to adjust exposure without touching individual stock positions — preserving the manager's specific stock selection while controlling overall market risk. The beta-adjusted hedge ratio is the core calculation: multiply portfolio value by portfolio beta, then divide by the index contract value (index level × $100 for options, or futures price × multiplier for futures).

Tracking error is the unavoidable imprecision: a diversified stock portfolio does not move perfectly in line with the index, so the hedge is always approximate. Mini-index contracts allow finer-grained hedging for smaller portfolios by reducing the minimum contract size. Overwriting — selling index calls against a long portfolio — generates premium income but reduces upside participation. McMillan frames overwriting as a decision to sacrifice potential stock appreciation for immediate income, appropriate when market returns are expected to be modest.

Chapter 31: Index Spreading

All the spreading techniques developed for individual equity options — bull spreads, bear spreads, butterflies, calendars, ratio spreads — apply directly to index options. Cash settlement simplifies follow-up since there's no risk of inadvertent stock assignment. The primary difference is that bid-ask spreads in index options are wider than in liquid equity options, making limit orders essential to avoid poor fills.

One feature specific to index options is the volatility skew. OTM index puts trade at significantly higher implied volatility than OTM calls — the reverse skew that emerged after the 1987 crash. Ratio put spreads exploit this directly: buy a less-OTM put and sell more deeply OTM puts that are overpriced by the skew. The spread collects elevated premium from the expensive OTM puts while limiting risk through the long put. This is the index market's most systematic premium-selling opportunity.

Chapter 32: Structured Products and PERCS

Structured products package options into retail-friendly formats, typically combining a zero-coupon bond for principal protection with an imbedded call option for upside participation. The zero-coupon bond ensures return of principal at maturity; the call provides market exposure up to a capped level. The investor pays above par for this protection: the yield sacrificed over the bond's life is the effective cost of the imbedded option. These products work best when implied volatility is low — cheap calls make the imbedded option efficient.

PERCS (Preferred Equity Redemption Cumulative Stock) are economically identical to a covered call write on common stock. They pay a higher dividend than common shares but convert at a fixed cap price — the equivalent of selling an OTM call. ELKS, LYONS, and DECS are similar vehicles with minor variations. McMillan's core lesson: always identify and analyze the imbedded option before buying any structured product. The retail premium paid for packaging is often substantial.

Chapter 33: Mathematical Considerations for Index Options

The lognormal distribution underlying Black-Scholes has a well-documented deficiency: actual stock price distributions have fat tails. Moves beyond ±4 standard deviations occur 12–20 times more frequently in practice than the lognormal predicts. At ±3 standard deviations, empirical frequency is roughly five times the model prediction. Within ±2 standard deviations, actual and modeled frequencies align reasonably well.

The strategy implications are significant. Option buyers have better expected returns than Black-Scholes implies because fat tails produce more large moves. Covered writers have roughly the same expected return as outright stock holders — not the superior return that the model predicts — because fat tail moves destroy premium collections. Ratio spreads are the most dangerous in fat-tail environments: the unlimited risk zones are entered far more frequently than the lognormal would suggest.

The "ever touches" probability — the probability that the stock reaches a target price at any point during the option's life — is always higher than the endpoint probability used in the standard break-even analysis. McMillan recommends using Monte Carlo simulation with fat-tailed distributions for complex multi-leg strategies; the standard lognormal calculator underestimates how often stop-loss levels or break-even prices will be reached before expiration.

Chapter 34: Futures and Futures Options

Futures contracts obligate both buyer and seller to transact at a fixed price at a future date; futures options give the right (not obligation) to enter a futures contract at a fixed price. The fair value of an index futures contract is: FV = Index × (1 + Rate × Time) − PV(Dividends), where each dividend is discounted to present value individually. When futures trade above fair value, program traders buy stocks and sell futures; when below fair value, they do the reverse. This arbitrage keeps futures prices tightly aligned with theoretical fair value.

The Black model prices futures options by setting the risk-free rate to zero in the standard Black-Scholes formula and discounting the result: Call = e−rt × BSM[r=0]. Put-call parity holds exactly for European futures options — a call and put at the same strike on the same futures contract must have identical implied volatility. This makes futures options a cleaner laboratory for volatility trading than equity options, where dividends and American exercise complicate the relationships.

SPAN margin (Standardized Portfolio Analysis of Risk) evaluates options portfolios through a 16-scenario risk array: seven price move scenarios in each direction combined with two volatility scenarios, plus two extreme "catastrophe" scenarios. The largest loss across all 16 scenarios becomes the margin requirement. SPAN allows offsets between correlated positions — a long call position partially offsets the margin on a short put in the same underlying — making it far more capital efficient than the simple percentage rules applied to equity options.

Chapter 35: Futures Option Strategies

The volatility skew in futures markets determines which spread structures have theoretical edges. Stock index futures show the same reverse skew as equity index options — OTM puts are more expensive than OTM calls — making ratio put spreads and call backspreads the preferred neutral strategies. Commodity futures often show a forward skew: OTM calls are overpriced because supply shocks can send prices dramatically higher (gold, silver, sugar, soybeans, coffee). In these markets, ratio call spreads (selling the expensive OTM calls) and put backspreads (buying cheap OTM puts) have structural edges.

Intermarket spreads — trading the relationship between two related futures — use options to create directional exposure on the spread rather than on either leg alone. The neutral delta ratio for an intermarket spread equals (v₁ × P₁ × u₁ × Δ₁) / (v₂ × P₂ × u₂ × Δ₂), where v is contract value per point, P is price, u is units, and Δ is delta. Using in-the-money options for intermarket spreads is superior to OTM options because ITM options have higher deltas, reducing the quantity of contracts needed and keeping commission costs manageable.

Chapter 36: The Basics of Volatility Trading

Volatility is the options market's central tradeable variable. Unlike stock price, which can move in either direction indefinitely, volatility is mean-reverting — periods of extreme high or low volatility tend to revert toward long-term averages. This mean reversion is what makes volatility trading viable: if implied volatility is at an extreme high, it is more likely to fall than to continue rising; if at an extreme low, more likely to rise. The trader who buys or sells options based on volatility is taking a position on this mean reversion.

The percentile approach is McMillan's preferred framework. For each underlying, track composite implied volatility over a rolling 600-day window. Calculate what percentile today's reading occupies within that history. The rules: only buy volatility (straddles, backspreads) when IV is below the 20th percentile — cheaper than 80% of all past readings. Only sell volatility (straddles, ratio spreads, naked options) when IV is above the 80th percentile. And critically: do not enter on the first day of an extreme reading. Wait for IV to reverse direction — to peak and begin falling before selling, to bottom and begin rising before buying. Entering against a strong IV trend is the most common and costly mistake in volatility trading.

The IV/HV comparison provides a parallel screen. When implied volatility exceeds historical volatility by more than 20% (ratio above 1.20), options are overpriced and premium selling has an edge. When implied is below historical by more than 20% (ratio below 0.80), options are cheap and premium buying is favorable. Neither criterion is sufficient alone; McMillan uses all three — percentile extreme, IV/HV ratio, and trend reversal — as a three-confirmation system.

Chapter 37: Volatility Measurement Tools

Reading a volatility chart requires patience and a specific kind of restraint. The natural instinct is to buy when implied volatility is "low" and sell when it is "high" — but this static threshold approach fails consistently. Volatility can trend: a stock with historically 30% average IV may enter a regime where 50% is the new normal after a merger or regulatory change. Comparing current IV to past IV of the same stock says nothing about whether options are fairly priced.

McMillan illustrates with RMBS (Rambus Inc.), a tech company whose historical volatility ranged from 50% to 110% before February 2000, then exploded past 120% after legal developments. A trader who saw 110% IV in January 2000 and sold straddles based on historical comparisons would have been devastated. The correct lesson: only trade volatility when there is no fundamental reason for the extreme reading. High IV before a binary FDA decision is fully justified — that's not a selling opportunity. High IV with no apparent catalyst, after it has peaked and started falling, is the genuine opportunity.

Implied deviation — the dispersion of individual option IVs across all strikes and expirations for the same underlying — is a skew measurement tool. When individual options are priced very differently from one another, the implied deviation is high, signaling an exploitable skew. The threshold of 20–25% relative implied deviation (normalized by average IV) marks the level where neutral spread strategies designed to capitalize on the skew distortion become attractive.

Chapter 38: The Distribution of Stock Prices

The lognormal distribution assumption in Black-Scholes says that stock price moves follow a smooth bell curve — large moves are exponentially rare. The empirical data say otherwise. At ±2 standard deviations from the mean, the model is roughly accurate. At ±3 standard deviations, actual moves are about five times more frequent than predicted. At ±4 standard deviations, actual moves are 12–20 times more frequent. These "fat tails" are not anomalies; they are a persistent structural feature of equity markets.

The implications cascade through every strategy. Option buyers benefit from fat tails because large moves occur more often than the pricing model assumes — the options are systematically underpriced for extreme scenarios. Naked option sellers face the reverse: they collect premiums priced for a lognormal world but face losses priced by the actual fat-tailed world. Ratio spreads are the most exposed: the unlimited risk zones are breached far more often than model probabilities suggest. Even covered call writers suffer: the premium collected compensates for moderate declines, but a fat-tail crash destroys the position in ways that standard expected-return analysis underestimates.

The "ever touches" probability — the chance that the stock reaches a target price at any point during the option's life, not just at expiration — amplifies these fat-tail concerns. This probability is always higher than the endpoint probability, and for stop-loss planning it is the relevant figure. A position with a 15% probability of ending beyond a certain price might have a 25% probability of touching that price intraday at some point before expiration — enough to trigger a stop-loss without ultimately generating a loss if the stock recovers.

Chapter 39: Volatility Trading Techniques

McMillan's three-step volatility trading process builds discipline into strategy selection. Step one: identify the signal — either IV in an extreme percentile, a large IV/HV discrepancy, or a clear reversal in IV trend. Step two: run the probability calculator on the chosen strategy's break-even prices. Buying volatility requires >80% probability of reaching break-even on an "ever touches" basis; selling requires <25% probability of reaching the written strikes. Step three: verify that the historical price distribution supports the trade by examining a histogram of past moves — rejecting setups where the stock has never moved the required distance in the available time.

For buying volatility with IV at extreme lows, the primary vehicle is the straddle or strangle. McMillan recommends the delta-neutral ratio for straddle construction: buy calls and puts in proportion to their respective deltas so that the position starts truly direction-neutral. With a call delta of 0.60 and a put delta of 0.40, buying 2 calls and 3 puts creates a balanced position. Mid-range expirations (3–6 months) typically show the best probability of success — close enough that decay hasn't destroyed the position, far enough that the stock has time to make the required move.

For selling volatility with IV at extreme highs, naked writing of index options is the safest vehicle because index options have the lowest gap risk. Individual stock options face binary event risk — FDA decisions, unexpected earnings, merger announcements — that can produce overnight gaps far outside any reasonable break-even range. The naked strangle (selling OTM put and OTM call) with strikes chosen at <25% probability of being reached produces consistent returns. McMillan's specific OEX example: at index level 650, selling September 550 puts and 750 calls gives approximately 11% and 17% probability of being reached — well inside the 25% threshold on both sides.

The volatility backspread — selling a long-term ATM option and buying more shorter-term OTM options — creates a position with negative vega (profits from IV decline) but limited risk (the long options hedge the short). This is McMillan's preferred "sophisticated sell-vol" strategy for traders who want to exploit high IV without the unlimited risk of naked options. The delta-neutral construction sets the ratio between legs. The position must be closed before the near-term options expire; at their expiration, all the remaining risk is concentrated in the short long-term option.

The volatility skew creates persistent structural trading opportunities. In index markets, OTM puts are systematically overpriced (reverse skew) because portfolio managers continuously buy protective puts, inflating their implied volatility. Ratio put spreads exploit this: the sold OTM puts are expensive relative to the purchased less-OTM puts. When the stock stays flat or rises, the spread earns its maximum profit from the elevated premium received on the short leg. The skew will exist until expiration, and all positions based on it have positive expected return if held to expiry.

Chapter 40: The Greeks

Delta is simultaneously a measure of directional exposure, a probability estimate, and a hedge ratio. For individual options, delta ranges from 0 to 1 for calls (negative for puts); for positions, position delta equals the sum of individual deltas × quantity × 100. Position delta is the Equivalent Stock Position — the number of shares of stock the entire position behaves like at the margin. A delta-neutral position has zero ESP and no immediate directional bias, but this neutrality is momentary — every stock move shifts deltas throughout the position.

Gamma is the rate of change of delta per dollar of stock movement. It is highest for ATM short-term options and approaches zero for options far from the money or far from expiration. Long options have positive gamma; short options have negative gamma. The gamma risk of a short straddle is severe near expiration: with a stock at $88 and 100 straddles sold, the position gamma might be −600, meaning a single two-point adverse move converts the "delta-neutral" position into one with directional exposure equivalent to ±1,300 shares. The loss formula for a quick two-point move is: 2 × |position delta| + |position gamma|.

Vega is the change in option price per 1% change in implied volatility. It is always positive for long options: calls and puts both gain value when IV rises. Short options have negative vega — their enemy is any volatility spike. For a short straddle, the position vega determines the dollar loss for each percentage point rise in IV. McMillan's example: selling 100 straddles with combined vega of $0.036 per contract produces a position vega of −$3,600 per 1% IV change. A sudden 5% spike in implied volatility costs the straddle seller $18,000 — dwarfing the theta income of $600 per day that makes the position attractive.

Theta is the daily time decay — positive for sellers, negative for buyers. The short straddle's most appealing characteristic is its positive theta: $600 per day flowing to the seller simply from the passage of time. But theta must be weighed against the gamma and vega risks that can overwhelm it in adverse conditions. McMillan's framework for evaluating any position is to calculate all four major Greeks (delta, gamma, theta, vega) simultaneously — never accepting a positive theta without accounting for the gamma cost that comes with it.

Rho measures sensitivity to interest rate changes. For most short-term equity options, rho is relatively minor. For LEAPS, rho is significant: a 1% rise in rates might add $0.18 to a one-year ITM call price. Calls have positive rho (rising rates help); puts have negative rho. This asymmetry is why the put-call parity relationship includes a carrying cost term that favors calls over puts.

Constructing gamma-neutral positions requires solving simultaneous equations. Given two options with different gammas, the gamma-neutral ratio is the ratio of their gammas. If an ATM option has gamma 0.050 and an OTM option has gamma 0.025, buying 100 ATM options and selling 200 OTM options creates zero net gamma. The resulting position delta is then neutralized with stock. McMillan demonstrates that traders can simultaneously target specific levels of delta, gamma, theta, and vega by setting up a system of equations — one equation per Greek target — and solving for the required option quantities. This is the foundation of professional options market-making.

Long gamma trading — buying near-term options and selling longer-term options (reverse calendar) — creates a position that benefits from large stock moves at the cost of daily theta. A vega-neutral, long-gamma construction uses simultaneous equations to find quantities where gamma is maximized while vega cancels across terms. The risk is severe theta drag: if the stock doesn't move quickly, daily decay destroys the position before any large move occurs. McMillan's example shows a long-gamma position losing $625 per day in theta — a position that requires a significant stock move within a week to justify its cost.

Chapter 41: Taxes

Options taxation intersects with stock taxation in complex ways that can dramatically affect after-tax returns. The basic rule for equity options: gains and losses are short-term regardless of how long the option is held, unless the option is exercised (in which case the premium is folded into the stock's cost basis and the stock's holding period governs).

Index and futures options follow the 60/40 rule: 60% of gains and losses are treated as long-term, 40% as short-term, regardless of holding period. These contracts are also marked to market at year-end — unrealized gains and losses are taxed as if realized on December 31. The advantage is that even intraday trades receive favorable 60/40 treatment; the disadvantage is that year-end positions cannot be managed for timing purposes.

Covered call writing tax rules are intricate. An OTM covered call does not affect the stock's holding period — the premium simply adds to the proceeds if the stock is called away. A "qualified" ITM covered call suspends but does not eliminate the stock's holding period; an "unqualified" ITM call eliminates the holding period entirely. Qualification depends on whether the call's strike is above the "benchmark" price — stock under $25: benchmark is 85% of stock price; stock $25–$60: benchmark is the next lower strike; stock above $60 with more than 90 days life: benchmark is two strikes below current price.

The wash sale rule prevents harvesting option losses while maintaining economic exposure. Selling stock at a loss and buying a call on the same stock within 30 days triggers the wash sale, disallowing the loss. McMillan's workaround: sell the stock at a loss and simultaneously sell an ITM put. Put assignment is not a "purchase" under the wash sale rules, so the combination avoids triggering the limitation while maintaining exposure through the short put obligation.

Chapter 42: The Best Strategy?

There is no single best strategy. The question McMillan poses in the final chapter is not mathematical — it is about suitability. What strategy fits this investor's temperament, capital base, tax situation, and willingness to monitor positions? Mathematical ranking from highest to lowest expected return: ratio writing and straddle selling, T-bill/option strategies, covered writing and neutral spreads, and at the bottom, speculative OTM option buying. But mathematical ranking is irrelevant if the investor cannot psychologically sustain the strategy during its inevitable losing stretches.

The suitability framework maps investor type to strategy. A conservative investor who understands stock ownership risk should write covered calls — the strategy that most resembles the stock experience they already know. A moderate investor comfortable with limited-risk positions benefits from spreads. An aggressive investor who accepts the possibility of 100% premium loss should limit option buying to 15–20% of total assets. The wealthy aggressive investor who can sustain margin calls and maintain positions through drawdowns is the only one suited to ratio writing and straddle selling.

McMillan's test of suitability is simple: "How will I react if the worst case occurs?" If the answer involves sleepless nights, forced liquidation, or abandoning a trading system, the strategy is wrong regardless of its theoretical edge. The best strategy is the highest-expected-return approach that a trader can actually maintain through adversity — not the strategy with the best backtest.

Trading & Investing Lessons

Volatility Is the Real Asset Class

Most investors think of stocks, bonds, and commodities as the tradeable asset classes. McMillan argues that volatility itself is a tradeable asset — one with strong mean reversion properties and predictable cyclical extremes. When implied volatility reaches multi-year lows, premium buying strategies have positive expected value regardless of stock direction. When it reaches multi-year highs, premium selling strategies have edge. Investors who track only price direction miss the volatility dimension entirely.

Delta-Neutral Trading Removes Directional Bias

The delta-neutral framework — sizing positions so their total ESP equals zero — transforms options from directional bets into volatility plays. A trader who is delta-neutral at inception profits from volatility realization (if long options) or from volatility decay (if short options) rather than from directional price moves. This is a fundamentally different investment hypothesis than stock picking, and it requires monitoring a different set of signals.

Fat Tails Punish Ratio Sellers and Reward Option Buyers

The empirical fat-tail distribution means that risk models based on lognormal assumptions underestimate the frequency of extreme moves by 12–20x. For investors who sell naked options or write ratios, this statistical reality is the primary long-term risk. Position sizing must account for events that standard models classify as "impossible" — because empirically they occur multiple times per decade in every market.

Skew Is a Persistent Structural Opportunity

The reverse skew in index options — OTM puts more expensive than OTM calls — has been a persistent feature of equity markets since 1987. Ratio put spreads that sell the overpriced OTM puts while buying less expensive less-OTM puts exploit this structural mispricing. Strategies aligned with the skew (not fighting it) have positive expected return simply from the put/call IV differential resolving at expiration.

Follow-Up Actions Determine Outcomes More Than Entry

McMillan devotes more page space to follow-up actions than to initial setups. The covered writer who rolls intelligently earns 30–50% annualized; the one who panics and closes positions at the first sign of trouble earns far less. The ratio writer who has pre-set stop levels and adjusts mechanically avoids the emotional paralysis that destroys accounts. Entry creates the opportunity; follow-up determines the result.

Covered Write = Naked Put — Use the Better Structure

The equivalence between covered call writing and naked put selling — identical P&L at expiration — is one of the most actionable insights in the book. Naked puts use collateral more efficiently (existing stocks or bonds can serve as margin), avoid dividend tracking requirements, and allow easier roll management. Investors who automatically default to covered writing should evaluate whether a cash-secured put achieves the same income with lower execution complexity.

The Collar Is Underused Portfolio Insurance

No-cost collars — selling an OTM call to finance an OTM put — provide genuine downside protection with zero net premium outlay. In high-volatility environments, the financing call can be placed very far OTM, allowing substantial upside before capping. Long-term investors holding concentrated stock positions use LEAPS collars to protect multi-year gains while maintaining significant upside. The instrument is widely available, requires no prediction of direction, and is systematically underused by retail investors who are unfamiliar with it.

Key Takeaways

The Core Equivalences

Covered call write = naked put write (same P&L at expiration). Long stock + long put = long call (protective put profile). Short stock + long call = long put (synthetic equivalence). These relationships are maintained by arbitrage and have no exceptions in theory; understanding them is the foundation of advanced options thinking.

Delta-Neutral Is Momentary, Not Permanent

No options position stays delta-neutral for more than an instant. Every stock move shifts deltas; the position accumulates directional bias continuously. Ratio writers, straddle sellers, and market makers who start delta-neutral must monitor and adjust throughout the life of the position — or accept that they are actually taking directional risk.

Implied Volatility Reverts to the Mean

No stock or index has high implied volatility indefinitely. The 600-day percentile framework works because IV clusters around a long-term average, occasionally spiking to extremes and then reverting. Buying at the 10th percentile and selling at the 90th percentile captures most of the mean-reversion cycle.

Never Sell Volatility Before Binary Events

High IV before an earnings announcement, FDA decision, merger vote, or regulatory ruling is justified by the binary uncertainty. Selling options in these conditions means collecting fair-value premium while accepting a much higher real probability of loss. Only sell volatility when high IV has no fundamental explanation.

Time Value Decay Is Non-Linear and Exploitable

An option loses two-thirds of its time value in the second half of its life. Strategies that are short near-term options collect premium at an accelerating rate in the final weeks. Strategies that are long near-term options fight accelerating decay. This asymmetry is why short-term straddle selling and calendar spreading (short the near-term) consistently generate income over neutral market periods.

Suitability Over Optimality

The highest mathematical expected-return strategy (ratio writing, straddle selling) requires capital, margin flexibility, emotional stability, and willingness to absorb periodic large losses. Deploying a strategy one cannot sustain in adverse conditions produces far worse outcomes than a lower-expected-return strategy executed consistently. Match strategy to psychology first; optimize within that constraint.

LEAPS Enable Long-Term Strategies Without Stock Capital

LEAPS options allow investors to substitute long-dated calls for stock positions, freeing capital for other uses. They also allow put strategies (naked puts, covered puts) with two-year horizons, giving time for slowly-developing bearish or bullish theses to play out. The slow time decay of LEAPS means the investor pays relatively little for the extra time.

Contrarian Ideas

Option Buying Has Better Expected Return Than Models Suggest

The conventional wisdom is that options are a loser's game — premium sellers consistently outperform because they collect time value from undisciplined buyers. McMillan's fat-tail analysis challenges this. In a world where 4-sigma moves occur 12–20× more often than lognormal pricing assumes, option buyers systematically pay less than fair value for extreme scenarios. The expected return from buying options — when done at low-IV conditions with sound break-even analysis — is positive.

Covered Writing Doesn't Outperform Stock Ownership

The popular belief is that covered call writing is a "free lunch" — extra income on top of stock returns. McMillan's fat-tail analysis shows that, adjusted for tail risk, covered writing has essentially the same expected return as owning stock. The premium collected in good times is offset by the catastrophic losses when stocks crash far below the covered write's protection. The strategy improves risk-adjusted returns in range-bound markets but is not superior to stock ownership in a fat-tail world.

Butterflies Are Often Uneconomical

Butterfly spreads look attractive on paper — limited risk, defined maximum profit, neutral outlook. In practice, four commissions plus the bid-ask spread on each leg often consume most of the theoretical profit. McMillan's 3:1 minimum ratio (max profit to max risk) is a strict filter that most butterfly setups fail to pass after realistic transaction cost accounting.

The Skew Will Not Correct Before Expiration

Traders who see overpriced OTM puts in the index market and try to short them outright — expecting the skew to correct itself before expiration — consistently lose. The skew persists until expiration, when it must mathematically collapse. Strategies that monetize the skew by holding to expiration have positive expected return; strategies that try to trade the skew's fluctuations before expiration are fighting structural institutional demand for portfolio protection.

OTM Option Buying Is Almost Always Negative Expected Value

OTM calls and puts are cheap in absolute terms but almost always expensive in probability-adjusted terms. The stock must move significantly just to break even, and the probability of that move occurring in the available time is almost always below the implied probability priced into the option. Except when IV is at multi-year lows, OTM speculative buying destroys capital systematically over time.

Frameworks & Proposals

The Three-Confirmation Volatility Trading System

McMillan's most complete trading framework requires all three confirmations before entering a volatility trade: (1) IV at an extreme percentile on the 600-day scale; (2) significant IV/HV discrepancy exceeding the 1.20 or 0.80 ratio thresholds; (3) IV chart trend has reversed direction. Selling on a first confirmation while IV is still rising is the most common failure mode. All three signals align only a few times per year for any given underlying — but when they do, the probability-adjusted edge is strong.

The Strategy Suitability Matrix

Conservative investors (covered call, cash-secured put). Moderate investors (bull/bear spreads, calendars, butterflies). Aggressive buyers (call and put buying, limited to 15–20% of assets). Aggressive sellers (ratio writing, straddle writing, requires "staying power" — capital to absorb the inevitable large losses without being forced out). Deploying any strategy above one's suitability tier produces losses from forced liquidation at the worst possible time.

The Delta-Neutral Follow-Up Framework for Ratio Writes

When a ratio write moves against the writer, the follow-up sequence is: (1) calculate new delta from current stock price; (2) determine whether ESP has moved materially positive or negative; (3) roll calls up or down to reset ESP toward zero; (4) alternatively, use pre-placed stop orders (buy stop above position = converts to covered write; sell stop below = converts to naked write). The stop order framework removes emotional decision-making by committing to actions before the stress occurs.

ESP Position Accounting

The Equivalent Stock Position framework provides a unified language for expressing the directional risk of any options position regardless of complexity. ESP = (Σ option deltas × 100) + shares held. Portfolio managers who adopt ESP accounting can aggregate risk across all option positions plus stock holdings into a single number — the net directional exposure in share equivalents — making risk management quantitative rather than intuitive.

The LEAPS Daily Decay Table

Traders who routinely buy short-term OTM options would benefit enormously from memorizing this table.

The Collar Volatility Financing Table

For no-cost LEAPS collars (2.5-year options), McMillan provides specific data on how far OTM the financing call can be placed at different volatility levels. At 30% volatility, buying an ATM put can be financed by selling a call only 30% OTM. At 40% vol, the financing call can be 35% OTM. At 50% vol, 40% OTM. At 70% vol, 50% OTM. The counterintuitive result: high-volatility stocks allow the most upside participation before the collar's cap is reached — because high IV inflates the put's cost and the call's premium equally, but the call can be placed very far away while still covering the put premium. Investors in volatile growth stocks who want downside protection should study this table carefully.

The Ratio Put Spread "Soft Landing" Structure

The ratio put spread (buy 1 higher-strike put, sell 2 lower-strike puts, typically for even money) is a precise bet on a moderate stock decline — not a collapse. The structure is elegant because it costs nothing (zero debit), has no upside risk if the stock rallies, and earns maximum profit at exactly the sold strike. The danger is that the stock continues falling through the sold strike and into the naked put zone below the lower break-even. McMillan's rule — close when the stock falls 8–10% below the written strike — provides specific guidance that prevents the "soft landing" trade from becoming a catastrophic short position in a stock that keeps falling. This discipline is rarely discussed in options literature, which tends to focus on entry setups and ignore the specific exit triggers that prevent winners from turning into disasters.

Program Trading and Futures Fair Value

The mechanics of index arbitrage are rarely explained in accessible terms, but McMillan provides a clear account. When futures trade above fair value by more than the round-trip transaction cost (approximately $0.27 in the book's examples), program traders simultaneously buy every stock in the basket and sell the futures contract — locking in the difference as riskless profit. When futures trade below fair value, they do the reverse: short the basket, buy futures. This simultaneous computer-driven execution is "program trading" — often blamed for market volatility but actually the mechanism that prevents persistent large pricing anomalies. The "limit-locked" situation occurs when futures can't trade (circuit breakers), making the implied futures price recoverable from the options market: Implied futures = Strike + Call price − Put price. This formula gives traders real-time price discovery even when the futures market is frozen.

The Tax Advantage of LEAPS Spreads

A LEAPS bull call spread held more than one year creates an unusual tax situation. The long leg — held over a year — generates a long-term capital gain. The short leg — closed within a year of purchase — generates a short-term capital loss. The two are offset against each other at different tax rates: the long-term gain is taxed at preferential rates while the short-term loss provides a full deduction at ordinary income rates. For high-income investors in high tax brackets, this asymmetry — long-term gains combined with short-term loss deductions — significantly improves the after-tax return compared to equivalent positions using short-term options only. McMillan identifies this as one of the few genuine tax advantages available in options trading without triggering wash sale or straddle tax rules.
