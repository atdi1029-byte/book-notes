Glossary

Core Option Terminology

Call option: The right — not the obligation — to buy 100 shares of the underlying stock at a fixed price before a specified date. A July 50 call lets you buy XYZ stock for $50 per share any time until July expiration, regardless of where the stock is actually trading.

Put option: The right — not the obligation — to sell 100 shares at a fixed strike price before expiration. A July 50 put lets you sell XYZ at $50 even if the stock has fallen to $30. Puts gain value as stock prices decline.

Strike price (striking price): The fixed price at which the option contract permits purchase (call) or sale (put). Strikes are set at 2½-point intervals for stocks under $35, 5-point intervals for stocks between $35 and $200, and 10-point intervals for stocks above $200. Listed exchanges always provide strikes above and below the current stock price.

Expiration date: The Saturday after the third Friday of the expiration month. All trading effectively ends at the Friday close. Equity options are assigned to one of three standard expiration cycles: Jan/Apr/Jul/Oct, Feb/May/Aug/Nov, or Mar/Jun/Sep/Dec. The two nearest months are always available, plus the next cycle month.

Premium: The price of an option contract quoted on a per-share basis. A premium of $3 on a standard 100-share contract costs the buyer $300 plus commissions. Premium consists of two components: intrinsic value (the in-the-money amount) and time value premium (everything above intrinsic).

Intrinsic value: The amount by which an option is in-the-money. A call with a $50 strike on a stock trading at $57 has $7 of intrinsic value. A put with a $60 strike on a stock at $54 has $6 of intrinsic value. Options cannot have negative intrinsic value — the floor is zero.

Time value premium: Option price minus intrinsic value. An option priced at $9 with $7 of intrinsic value has $2 of time premium. Time value decays to zero at expiration; this decay is non-linear and accelerates sharply in the final weeks of an option's life. An option loses roughly two-thirds of its time value in the last half of its lifespan.

In-the-money (ITM): A call is in-the-money when the stock price exceeds the strike; a put is in-the-money when the stock is below the strike. Deep ITM options trade close to intrinsic value with very little time premium remaining and delta approaching 1.0 for calls or −1.0 for puts.

Out-of-the-money (OTM): A call is out-of-the-money when stock is below strike; a put is OTM when stock is above strike. OTM options consist entirely of time premium — no intrinsic value — and will expire worthless if the stock fails to reach the strike before expiration.

At-the-money (ATM): The option whose strike is closest to the current stock price. ATM options carry the highest time value premium in absolute dollars and have delta near 0.50. They are the most sensitive to changes in implied volatility and to time decay.

The Greeks

Delta: The rate of change of an option's price per $1 move in the underlying stock. Deep ITM calls approach delta 1.0 (moving point-for-point with stock); ATM calls run near 0.50; OTM calls approach zero. Put delta equals call delta minus 1, so puts always carry negative delta. Delta is also approximately equal to the probability that the option will finish in-the-money at expiration.

Gamma: The rate of change of delta per $1 move in the underlying. Gamma is highest for ATM short-term options — a one-week ATM option can see its delta jump 0.28 points on a single $1 stock move. Long options have positive gamma (delta increases in your favor on moves); short options have negative gamma (delta shifts against you).

Theta: Daily time decay — how much value an option loses per calendar day. Theta is largest for ATM near-expiration options and smallest for deep OTM or long-dated options. Short options benefit from positive theta (time working for you); long options suffer negative theta (time working against you).

Vega: Change in option price per 1% change in implied volatility. Vega is always positive for long options (calls and puts alike gain value when volatility rises). Longer-term options have higher vega than short-term ones. Selling options means negative vega — any volatility spike is your enemy. Selling 100 straddles on a stock can produce a position vega of −$3,600 per 1% IV change.

Rho: Change in option price per 1% change in the risk-free interest rate. Call rho is positive (rising rates increase call values); put rho is negative. Rho matters most for LEAPS and deep ITM options; for short-term equity options it is largely irrelevant.

Equivalent Stock Position (ESP): The delta-equivalent share exposure of an options position. ESP = option quantity × delta × 100. A position with ESP of zero is delta-neutral — neither long nor short the market at inception. ESP is the unified language for expressing directional risk across complex multi-leg positions.

Volatility Concepts

Historical volatility (HV): The annualized standard deviation of daily logarithmic price changes. Calculated by taking the standard deviation of daily log-price relatives (ln(P_t / P_{t-1})) and multiplying by √260. Common lookback windows are 10, 20, 50, and 100 trading days. Each window tells a slightly different story about recent price behavior.

Implied volatility (IV): The volatility value that, plugged into the Black-Scholes formula, produces the current observed market price. Each option has its own IV; a composite is computed by weighting individual options by volume and proximity to the ATM strike, then smoothed with a 20-day moving average.

Volatility skew: The phenomenon where options at different strikes carry different implied volatilities. Index markets show a persistent "reverse skew" where OTM puts trade at significantly higher IV than OTM calls. Commodity futures often show the opposite ("forward skew") where OTM calls are richest.

IV percentile: Where current IV falls relative to its own history over a rolling 600-trading-day window. McMillan's rule: buy volatility only when IV is below the 20th percentile of its own history; sell volatility only when IV is above the 80th percentile. Equally important: wait for IV to reverse direction before entering, not just to reach the extreme.

Implied deviation: The standard deviation of individual option IVs across all strikes and months, normalized by average IV. A reading above 20–25% signals an exploitable skew — some options are significantly more expensive than others, creating spread opportunities.

Strategy Mechanics

Roll forward: Buy back an expiring option and sell the next month at the same strike, typically for a small net credit. Extends the income stream without changing the directional stance.

Roll up / roll down: Close the existing option and reopen at a higher (up) or lower (down) strike. Rolling up captures more appreciation potential; rolling down increases downside protection but typically at a net debit.

Delta-neutral: A position where the sum of all option deltas weighted by share exposure equals zero — the position has no directional bias at inception. Neutrality is only momentary; every stock move shifts deltas and re-exposes the position to directional risk.

Auto-exercise rule: Options that are $0.375 or more in-the-money at expiration are automatically exercised by the Options Clearing Corporation (OCC) on behalf of the holder. The OCC is the issuer and guarantor of all listed options, eliminating counterparty risk and making the secondary market possible.

Put-call parity: The mathematical relationship between put and call prices at the same strike and expiration. Put = Call − Stock + Strike + PV(Strike) − Dividends. Any deviation from parity creates an arbitrage opportunity that market makers will quickly eliminate. This relationship underlies all synthetic position equivalences.

Chapter 1: Definitions

An option is a contract, not a stock. That distinction matters more than it might seem. A stock represents ownership in a company; an option is merely a right that expires. The buyer pays a premium for the right to buy (call) or sell (put) 100 shares of a specific stock at a fixed price — the striking price — on or before a fixed date. At expiration, that right is either exercised, sold, or it vanishes. Unlike stocks, options have a built-in clock working against the buyer every single day.

McMillan opens by establishing the four specifications that uniquely identify any option contract: the type (put or call), the underlying stock, the expiration date, and the striking price. An "XYZ July 50 call" is an option to buy 100 shares of XYZ at $50 per share anytime through July expiration. If that option is quoted at $5, buying it costs $500 — not $5 — because each contract covers 100 shares. This scaling trips up beginners who confuse the per-share quote with the per-contract cost.

Strike prices are not arbitrary — exchanges set them at standard intervals around the current stock price. Stocks under $35 get 2½-point spacing; most stocks between $35 and $200 get 5-point spacing; stocks above $200 get 10-point spacing. When a stock moves far enough, new strikes are added. This means an active trader always has multiple strikes to choose from above and below the market, each representing a different risk-reward tradeoff.

The value of an option has two distinct components. Intrinsic value is the amount the option is in-the-money — a $50 call on a $57 stock has $7 of intrinsic value. Time value premium is everything above that. An option priced at $9 with $7 intrinsic has $2 of time premium. At expiration, all time premium vanishes and the option is worth exactly its intrinsic value or zero. This "wasting" nature of options is what makes them fundamentally different from stocks.

Time decay is not linear — it accelerates. An option loses roughly two-thirds of its time value in the last half of its life. The classic square-root-of-time relationship says a three-month option decays roughly twice as fast per day as a nine-month option at the same strike. This non-linearity means buyers must be right not just about direction but about timing. A call that is correct directionally but expires six weeks before the stock moves is a total loss.

Delta — the hedge ratio — measures how much the option moves per $1 change in the stock. Deep in-the-money calls approach delta 1.0, moving nearly point-for-point with the stock. At-the-money calls run near 0.50. Deep out-of-the-money calls approach zero, barely responding to stock moves. Delta also approximates the probability that the option will be in-the-money at expiration: a 0.25 delta call has roughly a 25% chance of finishing ITM. Put delta equals call delta minus one, so puts always carry negative delta.

The Options Clearing Corporation stands behind every listed option. The OCC is the actual issuer and guarantor — when you buy a call, your counterparty is not the specific person who sold it; it is the OCC. This eliminates counterparty default risk and makes the liquid secondary market possible. Assignment — when a short option holder is required to fulfill the contract — is random among all short holders of that series, administered by the OCC.

Six factors drive call option prices upward: a higher stock price, a lower strike price, more time to expiration, higher volatility, higher interest rates, and lower dividends. Puts respond in the opposite direction to most of these — higher dividends increase put values, for instance, because dividends reduce the stock price on the ex-date. McMillan returns to these six factors throughout the book because every strategy is ultimately an attempt to profit from some combination of them changing favorably.

Chapter 2: Covered Call Writing

Covered call writing is the most popular options strategy among individual investors and the natural starting point for anyone learning to use options systematically. The setup is simple: own 100 shares of stock, sell one call option against it. The premium received immediately reduces the effective cost basis of the stock and creates a buffer against modest declines. In exchange, the writer gives up any appreciation above the strike price.

McMillan insists that return calculations for covered writes must be done rigorously and must include commissions. Three measures matter: return if exercised (the annualized return assuming the stock is called away at the strike), return if unchanged (the annualized premium income if the stock stays flat), and downside break-even (how far the stock can fall before the writer starts losing money). The net investment formula uses 50% margin on the stock, then subtracts the premium received — so a stock at $48 with a $4 call sold produces a net investment of roughly $2,000 per contract.

The choice between in-the-money and out-of-the-money writes involves a real tradeoff. ITM writes collect more premium and offer more downside protection but cap the upside immediately. OTM writes collect less but let the stock appreciate to the strike before capping gains. McMillan's preferred criterion is a monthly return of 2–4% combined with a strike no more than 3–4% out of the money. This balances income generation with enough upside to avoid penalizing bullish stocks.

Dividends complicate the picture. The covered writer collects dividends while holding the stock, which improves the overall return. But large upcoming dividends can trigger early assignment — a call buyer may exercise the day before an ex-dividend to capture the dividend instead. McMillan warns writers to check dividend calendars carefully and to avoid writing calls with imminent large dividends unless the premium more than compensates.

Follow-up actions separate skilled covered writers from passive ones. If the stock rallies dramatically, the writer can roll up — buy back the written call and sell a higher-strike call to capture more appreciation, usually at a net debit. If the stock falls, the writer can roll down to a lower strike, reducing the break-even but locking in a partial loss if the stock subsequently recovers. Rolling forward means buying back the expiring call and selling next month at the same strike for a small credit, extending the income stream without changing the directional bet.

McMillan is pointed in his warning about rolling down mechanically. A writer who rolls down after a stock decline commits to a lower strike just as a potential recovery might be beginning. If the stock then rebounds, the writer has capped the gain at the new (lower) strike and realized both a stock loss and an option loss in the process. Rolling down is sometimes the right move, but it requires genuine conviction that the stock will not recover — not just a reflexive response to a red position.

The incremental return concept captures the mindset of the most disciplined covered writers. They identify a target price for each stock — the highest level at which they are comfortable selling — and write the highest available strike each month, accumulating premium systematically. Assignment is not a failure in this framework; it is the highest-return outcome. The writer simply reinvests in the next opportunity. This treats covered writing as a repeating income machine rather than a stock-picking exercise.

A critical equivalence runs throughout the book: a covered call write has exactly the same profit-and-loss profile at expiration as a naked put write at the same strike. Both profit when the stock stays flat or rises; both lose when it falls; both collect the same net premium. Which is more efficient depends on margin requirements, dividends, and tax treatment — but the risk profiles are mathematically identical. This equivalence will reappear dozens of times as McMillan shows how seemingly different strategies collapse to the same economic reality.

Chapter 3: Call Buying

Call buying is the purest bullish speculation in the options toolkit. You pay a premium for the right to profit if the stock rises above the strike, with maximum loss capped at the premium paid. The leverage is the appeal: a stock moving from $50 to $58 produces a 16% gain on stock; an at-the-money call might double in that same move. The danger is the same source of leverage — time is always working against the buyer, and the stock must move not just in the right direction but fast enough.

Choosing which call to buy requires balancing three competing factors: cost, delta, and time remaining. A slightly in-the-money call carries a delta of 0.60–0.75, capturing most of the stock's move with relatively lower percentage risk of total loss since intrinsic value provides a floor. An at-the-money call offers maximum sensitivity to the stock and to volatility changes. A deep out-of-the-money call is cheap in absolute terms but requires a large move just to reach break-even. McMillan consistently steers readers toward slightly ITM or ATM options as the best starting point for directional buyers.

Timing matters as much as direction. A call that is right about the stock's eventual destination but wrong about speed produces a loss. McMillan recommends never holding options through the final few weeks of heavy time decay unless the position is already strongly profitable and the stock is moving. The last thirty days of an option's life accelerate decay enormously — an option losing $0.10 per day at sixty days out may lose $0.35 per day in the final two weeks at the same stock price.

Follow-up discipline matters as much as entry. If a call rises 20–30% quickly, taking partial profits is often wiser than hoping for more — time decay starts eroding gains immediately. If the stock has moved far and the call is now deep in-the-money with little remaining time premium, rolling up to a higher strike locks in the gain while maintaining upside exposure for less capital at risk. The most damaging call-buying behavior is holding a profitable position past its peak out of greed, then watching it decay back to a small gain or a loss.

McMillan introduces the idea of converting a winning call into a bull spread as a follow-up move: sell an out-of-the-money call against the long call, collecting enough premium to reduce or eliminate the remaining cost basis. The position now has a defined maximum profit but also a lower probability of total loss. This transformation from speculative call buyer to hedged spread holder can be the right move when the stock has made a good initial move but still has further to run.

The mathematics of call buying are unforgiving. Most calls expire worthless or are closed at a loss. The strategy can still be profitable in aggregate if the winning trades are large enough — a 200% gain on a third of positions can overcome 100% losses on the other two-thirds. But this requires discipline to let winners run and to cut losses on calls that are clearly not working, not simply to average down by buying more of an option that is decaying toward zero.

McMillan's summary for call buyers is spare: buy slightly in-the-money or at-the-money options with at least 60 days of time remaining, buy only when you have a genuine directional thesis and a catalyst, never let a call decay to zero when you could close it for something, and never hold past the point where time decay is eating profits faster than the stock is generating them.

Delta is the most practical tool for call selection. An at-the-money call has a delta near 0.50, meaning it moves roughly half a point for every point the stock moves. A slightly in-the-money call with delta 0.65 gives more bang per dollar invested while costing more. An out-of-the-money call with delta 0.25 requires the stock to move significantly just to overcome time decay. McMillan's practical rule: if the delta is below 0.30, the call is likely a lottery ticket — possible but statistically unlikely to pay off.

Chapter 4: The Reverse Hedge

The reverse hedge — also called a simulated straddle — predates the existence of listed puts and represents the original way traders positioned for large moves without directional conviction. The setup combines short stock with long calls: sell 100 shares short and simultaneously buy two (or more) calls at the same strike. The position profits from large moves in either direction, with a maximum loss at the strike at expiration equal to twice the call premium paid.

The mechanics work as follows: if the stock collapses, the short stock profits while the long calls expire worthless — the net gain is the stock's decline minus twice the premium paid. If the stock rallies, the long calls gain more than the short stock loses, because two calls at a 0.50 delta each behave like one 100-share position but with explosive upside as the stock moves far above the strike. The break-even points sit at the strike price plus and minus the total premium paid.

A simpler cousin of the reverse hedge is the protected short sale: sell 100 shares short and buy just one call (not two) at the same strike. This structure limits the upside risk on the short position to the premium paid for the call — the worst case is losing the call premium as the stock rises and the short is covered at the strike. Below the strike, the short profits normally. McMillan notes this is economically identical to owning a put: profit if the stock falls, lose the premium if it rises.

The reverse hedge was important historically because listed put options did not exist in the early years of the Chicago Board Options Exchange. Traders who wanted to bet on volatility without knowing the direction had to construct long-volatility positions synthetically using calls and short stock. Once listed puts arrived, the straddle — long call plus long put — became the cleaner, simpler, lower-commission alternative that made the reverse hedge mostly obsolete.

McMillan uses this chapter to introduce the concept that the same economic exposure can be constructed multiple ways. Understanding these structural equivalences is not academic — it allows the trader to choose the most capital-efficient or tax-advantageous route to the desired risk profile. The reverse hedge, the protected short sale, and the straddle all express variations of the same underlying view: large move expected, direction unknown.

The mathematics of the reverse hedge make the risk profile concrete. With 100 shares short at $50 and two calls purchased for $3 each, the maximum loss occurs at exactly $50 at expiration — the short stock position breaks even, but the $600 in call premium is gone. Above $56 (the strike plus both premiums), the long calls earn more than the short stock loses, and profit accelerates. Below $44 (the short entry minus the call cost), the short stock generates enough profit to more than offset the full premium paid. The position is profitable on large moves in either direction and loses on moderate outcomes near the strike.

McMillan emphasizes that the reverse hedge requires a stock capable of large moves — high volatility is essential. A low-volatility utility stock is an almost certain loser in this structure because it will sit near the strike at expiration, eating the maximum loss. The ideal candidate is a stock with pending news — earnings, FDA decisions, regulatory outcomes — where the direction is uncertain but a large move in either direction is probable. This is the same logic that underpins the modern straddle buy, and the reverse hedge was simply the mechanism available before listed puts made straddle buying straightforward.

The protected short sale — the simpler cousin — uses just one call against the short position. This converts unlimited short-side risk into defined risk equal to the call premium paid. If the stock collapses, the short profits generously; if it rallies, the call is exercised and the loss is capped at the premium paid plus any gap between the short entry and the strike. McMillan notes that this is economically equivalent to buying a put — same profit graph, same maximum loss, same mechanism — but implemented through the short stock and call combination.

Chapter 5: Naked Call Writing

Naked call writing — selling calls without owning the underlying stock — is theoretically one of the riskiest strategies in the options toolbox. The risk is unlimited on the upside: if XYZ is at $40 and a trader sells a $45 call for $1, then XYZ gets acquired at $80, the loss is $34 per share, or $3,400 per contract. There is no natural cap. This unlimited risk must be weighed against the limited reward — the $1 premium collected.

The margin requirement for naked calls reflects this risk: the greater of 20% of the stock price plus the call premium minus the out-of-the-money amount, or 10% of the stock price plus the call premium as a minimum. This margin is marked to market daily as the stock moves, meaning a naked call writer who is wrong must continuously post more collateral as the position deteriorates.

McMillan describes two philosophies for naked call writing. The first is selling out-of-the-money calls for income, profiting from the probability that the stock stays below the strike through expiration. This is a high-probability strategy with limited premium but manageable ongoing exposure. The second — selling deep in-the-money calls at near-parity as a stock substitute — is largely a historical curiosity; once listed puts existed, synthetic short positions via puts were more efficient.

Follow-up discipline is everything for naked call writers. McMillan is explicit: never let naked calls run deeply in-the-money without action. If the stock approaches the strike, the position should be closed or rolled to a higher strike. Naked call writers who freeze when a position moves against them typically suffer the full loss of a takeover, an earnings surprise, or a sustained rally. The strategy demands pre-planned exit rules, not hope-based holding.

The psychological challenge of naked writing is that it feels comfortable for long stretches — premium collected, options expiring, account growing slowly — and then violently punishes a single lapse of attention. McMillan treats naked call writing as a professional activity requiring margin capacity to sustain adverse moves, active monitoring, and pre-committed responses to specific price triggers.

"The potential loss from naked call writing is theoretically unlimited. That is the main drawback of the strategy." — Lawrence G. McMillan

The two-tier margin structure matters in practice because it determines how much capital a naked call writer ties up. On a $50 stock with a call trading at $2, the 20% calculation gives $10 + $2 = $12 per share required as margin. But if the call is $3 out-of-the-money, subtract that from the requirement: $10 + $2 − $3 = $9 per share. The 10% floor catches very short-term, deep out-of-the-money calls where the 20% formula falls too low. Crucially, this margin is marked to market daily — if the stock rallies $5, the margin requirement jumps immediately, not at month end.

The deep in-the-money naked call as a stock substitute was more relevant before listed puts existed. An investor with a bearish view could sell a call with a $1.00 delta — moving point-for-point with the stock — to simulate short stock without the borrowing and uptick rule constraints. McMillan notes this approach is mostly obsolete now that puts provide cleaner, lower-margin bearish exposure. The OTM income strategy remains active: sell calls with high probability of expiring worthless, collect the premium, repeat. The probability of profit equals the probability the stock stays below the strike, which can be estimated from implied volatility using the Black-Scholes distribution.

McMillan dedicates special attention to selection among naked call candidates. High implied volatility is desirable — it means more premium per unit of risk assumed. But the writer must distinguish between volatility that is high because of pending news (do NOT write) versus high because the stock has historically been volatile (potentially attractive). Writing naked calls ahead of earnings or FDA announcements is a documented route to catastrophic losses, and McMillan's selection framework explicitly screens for this. The naked call writer profits from the slow passage of time; any catalyst that causes a sudden, sharp move destroys the position regardless of how well it was originally structured.

Chapter 6: Ratio Call Writing

Ratio call writing combines the income generation of covered writing with the premium of selling extra calls. The basic setup: buy 100 shares and sell two calls at the same strike (typically at-the-money or slightly out-of-the-money). The extra call is "naked" — not covered by the stock. Maximum profit occurs when the stock is exactly at the strike at expiration, at which point the written calls expire worthless, the stock is worth the strike price, and the writer keeps the entire double premium.

The profit profile has a characteristic shape. With a stock at $49 and two October 50 calls sold at $6 each, the maximum profit is $13 per share at the $50 strike: one point of appreciation on the stock plus $12 in premium collected. The downside break-even is $37 (the $50 strike minus $13). The upside break-even is $63 ($50 plus $13). Between those points the position profits; outside them it loses — modestly on the downside (stock can only fall to zero) and without limit on the upside.

The neutral ratio is the key discipline. For any given call, the mathematically neutral number of contracts to write is one divided by the delta of the call. If the call has a delta of 0.50, the neutral ratio is 2:1 (two calls per 100 shares owned). If the call has a delta of 0.40, the neutral ratio is 2.5:1. Writing at the neutral ratio makes the position delta-zero at inception — neither long nor short the market. The Equivalent Stock Position (ESP) gives a single number that expresses this: long 100 shares plus short two 0.50-delta calls equals an ESP of zero.

The variable ratio write (trapezoidal hedge) offers a wider profit zone. Instead of selling two calls at the same strike, the writer sells one in-the-money call and one out-of-the-money call against the stock position. With the stock sitting between the two strikes, the maximum profit is the total time value collected from both calls, and this maximum applies across the entire range between the two strikes rather than just at one point. The break-evens are wider, the maximum profit zone is broader, and the strategy can be more forgiving of moderate stock movement.

Follow-up action is critical for ratio writers. If the stock rallies toward and through the upside break-even, the writer must act — the naked calls are becoming increasingly dangerous. Rolling up (buying back the ITM calls and selling calls at the next higher strike) resets the position but typically requires a debit. Alternatively, buying back just the naked call portion converts the position to a covered write, limiting further upside risk. McMillan recommends pre-placing buy stop orders above the upside break-even — these automatically trigger stock purchases that convert the naked call to a covered position without requiring the writer to monitor the position continuously.

On the downside, if the stock falls below the lower break-even, the writer can roll down by buying back the calls and selling lower-strike calls. This extends the time premium collected but commits to a lower strike just as the stock may be bottoming. McMillan's broader point is that follow-up for ratio writes is not a passive exercise — it requires regular recalculation of the new neutral delta and willingness to adjust position size as the stock moves and deltas shift.

The risk that most surprises ratio write beginners is the whipsaw: the stock approaches the upside break-even, triggering a roll-up at a debit, then reverses and falls below the downside break-even, requiring a roll-down. Both adjustments cost money, and the writer has paid twice for moves that ultimately went nowhere. McMillan's antidote is smaller initial positions (fewer naked calls per 100 shares) combined with wider strikes, which gives the position more room before defensive action is required.

Telescoping stops — a technique McMillan introduces here — involve progressively tightening the protective stops as the position accumulates profit. A writer who has earned $8 of the $13 maximum profit might narrow the stop to protect $5 of that gain, even though the original stop was set much further away. This captures much of the intended profit while preventing the most painful outcome: watching a near-perfect ratio write deteriorate into a loss because the writer refused to exit near the peak.

Chapter 7: Bull Spreads

A bull spread is the option trader's way of expressing a moderately bullish view while limiting both risk and cost. The construction is simple: buy a lower-strike call and sell a higher-strike call, both with the same expiration. The net cost is the debit — always a debit, because the lower-strike call always costs more than the higher-strike call. That debit is the maximum loss. The maximum gain is the difference in strikes minus the debit, realized when the stock is above the higher strike at expiration.

A concrete example: stock at $32, buy an October 30 call for $3, sell an October 35 call for $1. Net debit: $2. Maximum loss: $2 (if stock stays below $30). Maximum profit: $3 (if stock rises above $35 — the $5 strike difference minus the $2 debit). Break-even: $32 (lower strike plus debit). The risk-reward ratio here is $3 gain for $2 risk — reasonable for a moderately bullish view where dramatic upside is not expected.

Bull spreads are distinctly better than outright call buying when implied volatility is high. When options are expensive, the at-the-money call costs more, but the premium collected on the sold call offsets a meaningful portion of that cost. The spread effectively converts some of the overpriced volatility into premium income. The tradeoff is capped upside — the position cannot profit above the higher strike no matter how far the stock runs.

McMillan presents the bull spread as a substitute for covered call writing that uses far less capital. A covered write on a $50 stock requires $25 per share of margin plus the stock purchase; a bull spread on the same stock using the 50 and 55 strikes might cost $2 per share in debit while capturing much of the same income profile. The excess capital can sit in T-bills, earning interest. Over many repetitions, the T-bill-plus-spread approach can approximate the returns of covered writing with a fraction of the capital commitment.

The margin requirement for a bull spread is simply the net debit — no additional posting required, because the long call fully hedges the short call. This defined-risk nature makes bull spreads suitable for investors who cannot or will not write naked options. The maximum loss is known and limited from the moment of entry.

Follow-up for bull spreads is simpler than for naked strategies. If the stock rallies to the upper strike quickly, the position has made most of its maximum profit and the writer can close it. If the stock falls and the spread is losing value, the loss is limited to the original debit — there is no expanding risk requiring emergency action. This contained loss profile is precisely why spread strategies appeal to investors who want leverage without the open-ended downside of pure option selling.

McMillan's most useful observation about bull spreads is their role in a rising implied volatility environment. When options are expensive — implied volatility elevated above historical — buying an outright call overpays for premium. A bull spread sells some of that expensive volatility back through the short call leg, effectively reducing the net premium paid and making the trade viable even when solo calls are overpriced. The short call gives up the upside beyond the upper strike, but in a moderately bullish scenario that upside would rarely have been achieved anyway.

Bull spreads in practice require matching the expected move to the strike width. A trader expecting a stock to move from $40 to $45 should use a 40–45 spread, not a 40–50 spread — the wider spread costs more debit for the same maximum move and reduces the return on risk. Equally, a trader using a $2 spread on a $100 stock is making a bet that barely moves the needle — the strike width should be calibrated to the realistic expected price range, not chosen arbitrarily. McMillan's rule: select the upper strike just below where the stock is unlikely to go, not the highest available strike.

Chapter 8: Bear Spreads

Bear spreads express a moderately bearish view while defining the maximum risk. The call bear spread — sometimes called a credit spread — works by selling a lower-strike call and buying a higher-strike call for protection. The net credit received is the maximum profit; the maximum loss is the strike difference minus the credit. Example: sell an October 35 call for $2, buy an October 40 call for $0.50. Net credit: $1.50. Maximum gain: $1.50 (stock stays below $35). Maximum loss: $3.50 (stock above $40).

McMillan notes an important practical difference between call bear spreads and put bear spreads: the put version is usually superior. In a put bear spread, the investor buys a higher-strike put and sells a lower-strike put. The written put is out-of-the-money, reducing early exercise risk. In-the-money puts lose time value more quickly than in-the-money calls, which means the spread widens faster when the stock declines — exactly the desired behavior. The same underlying bearish view, expressed through puts instead of calls, tends to produce better execution.

The margin requirement for a call bear spread is the difference in strikes minus the net credit received. This is locked up for the life of the spread, making the capital efficiency calculation important: a $5 spread with a $1.50 credit requires $3.50 in margin to earn $1.50 — a 43% return if the stock cooperates. The time frame matters for annualizing this comparison.

Bear spreads work best when the trader expects a flat to declining market over a specific horizon. Unlike outright put buying, which requires a substantial move to cover the full premium paid, a bear spread profits even if the stock merely stays flat below the sold strike. The credit received provides a small built-in cushion against the stock drifting slightly upward.

McMillan works through a concrete bear spread example that clarifies the mechanics. With the stock at $35, sell the October 35 call at $2, buy the October 40 call at $0.50: net credit $1.50. Maximum profit: $1.50 (if stock is below $35 at expiration). Maximum loss: $3.50 (the $5 strike difference minus the $1.50 credit) if stock is above $40. Break-even: $36.50 (lower strike plus the credit received). The trade requires the stock to stay flat or decline — the premium collected is the entire profit.

Put bear spreads are structurally superior for two reasons McMillan explains carefully. First, the written put in a put bear spread is out-of-the-money at initiation — there is no early assignment risk until the stock actually falls below the lower strike. In a call bear spread, the sold call is in-the-money if the stock rallies, creating immediate early exercise risk. Second, in-the-money puts lose time value faster than in-the-money calls, so put bear spreads widen more rapidly on a stock decline, which benefits the position sooner.

Follow-up actions for bear spreads are simpler than for ratio positions but still require discipline. If the stock rallies to the upper strike and appears likely to continue higher, McMillan's guidance is to close the spread entirely — accept the maximum loss rather than watch the short call move deeper into the money with the long call barely helping. If the stock falls quickly to the lower strike before expiration, the spread is already near full value, and closing early at 80–90% of maximum profit avoids the final weeks of theta grinding and the risk of a reversal.

Bear spreads belong in a trader's toolkit specifically for situations where the analyst has a defined price target on the downside but not a conviction about a collapse. A put buyer who expects a stock to fall from $35 to $28 doesn't need unlimited downside — the profit from $35 to $28 is the goal. A put bear spread with strikes at 35 and 28 (or 35 and 30) captures exactly that move, costs less than an outright put, and has a cleaner break-even that only requires the thesis to work moderately well.

Chapter 9: Calendar Spreads

Calendar spreads — also called time spreads or horizontal spreads — exploit the difference in time decay rates between near-term and longer-term options at the same strike. The setup: sell a near-term call and buy a longer-term call at the same strike. Because near-term options decay faster than longer-term ones, the spread widens as time passes and the stock stays near the strike. That widening differential is the profit.

Two philosophies drive calendar spread selection. The neutral calendar uses an at-the-money strike: the goal is for the stock to stay near the strike through near-term expiration, at which point the short call is bought back cheaply while the long call retains significant time value. The trade is then closed for a profit equal to the spread differential. The bullish calendar uses an out-of-the-money strike: the near-term call expires worthless because the stock stays below the strike, and then the stock rallies toward or through the strike while the long call appreciates.

The maximum profit on a neutral calendar occurs when the stock is exactly at the strike at near-term expiration. This is because both options are at-the-money: the near-term is worth very little (nearly expired ATM), while the long call still has substantial time premium (more time remaining). The spread between them is widest at this point. If the stock moves far from the strike in either direction, both calls approach their respective intrinsic or zero values and the spread collapses — the trade loses the original debit.

The risk in a calendar spread is always limited to the debit paid, which makes this a defined-risk strategy. But the risk profile is not symmetric: a big move in either direction loses the debit, while a stock sitting quietly near the strike profits. This means calendar spreads perform best in low-volatility environments where the stock is expected to drift sideways rather than make a big move before near-term expiration.

McMillan's selection guideline is to prefer strikes that are at-the-money or slightly out-of-the-money, with near-term expiration two to three months away. The closer the near-term expiration, the faster the decay differential — but also the less time for the bullish scenario to develop if using an OTM calendar. The roll decision — when to move from one month to the next — is typically triggered when the near-term option has declined to about half of the original spread cost. Rolling at that point captures the remaining time premium without waiting for total decay.

The profit and loss at near-term expiration form an arc, not a line. If the stock sits exactly at the strike when the near-term option expires, the position has its maximum profit: the near-term sold call has decayed to near zero and is bought back cheaply, while the longer-term call still holds substantial time value. If the stock has moved far away from the strike — sharply higher or lower — both options trade near intrinsic or near zero, the spread collapses, and the trader approaches maximum loss (the original debit).

The bullish variant of the calendar spread works on a different logic. The trader uses out-of-the-money calls at a strike above the current stock price. If the stock stays below that strike through near-term expiration, the near-term call expires worthless and the trader now owns the longer-term call for the initial debit minus the expired premium. Then, if the stock rallies above the strike, the surviving long-term call profits handsomely. The risk is that the stock immediately rallies past the strike before near-term expiration — the near-term short call costs money to close, negating the idea.

Rolling the calendar forward is the classic follow-up when the near-term option expires and the stock has remained near the strike. Buy back the expired near-term call for minimal cost, sell the next month's call at the same strike for credit, and repeat the process. Each roll collects another month's worth of at-the-money time premium while the long-term call slowly decays. McMillan's guideline is to roll to the next month when the near-term option has about half the spread cost left in it — waiting longer risks a whipsaw that costs more to unwind than the remaining premium is worth.

Chapter 10: Butterfly Spreads

The butterfly spread is the option market's version of a bet on extreme range-bound behavior. It combines a bull spread and a bear spread at the same middle strike: buy one lower-strike call, sell two middle-strike calls, and buy one upper-strike call, all at equal spacing and the same expiration. The maximum profit occurs when the stock is pinned exactly at the middle strike at expiration. Both sold calls expire worthless, the lower call is worth the full strike difference, and the upper call is worthless.

The mechanics are precise. With a butterfly using 60, 70, and 80 strikes and a $2 net debit: maximum profit is $8 (the $10 strike difference minus the $2 debit) at the $70 strike at expiration. Maximum loss is $2 (the debit) if the stock ends below $60 or above $80. Break-even points are $62 and $78. The net investment is just the debit — the four legs offset each other's margin requirements completely.

McMillan's criterion for butterfly attractiveness is strict: only enter if the maximum profit is at least 3–4 times the maximum risk. In the example above, $8 maximum profit versus $2 maximum risk is a 4:1 ratio — barely acceptable. A butterfly with a $4 debit for an $8 maximum profit (2:1) is not attractive: commissions eat significantly into a $400 per contract maximum gain, and the probability of the stock ending precisely at the middle strike is quite low.

The practical drawback of butterfly spreads is transaction cost. Four legs mean four bid-ask spreads paid on entry and potentially four more on exit. For retail investors paying meaningful commissions, the all-in cost of establishing and unwinding a butterfly can reduce the theoretical maximum profit by 20–30%. This is why McMillan treats butterflies as primarily appropriate when options are very fairly priced and the trader has genuine conviction about narrow range-bound behavior — not as a routine income strategy.

The butterfly can also be constructed with puts (buy higher put, sell two middle puts, buy lower put) or with a combination of calls and puts. McMillan explains that all four construction methods produce identical profit-and-loss graphs at expiration. The practical preference is to use the all-calls or all-puts version that avoids selling in-the-money options, which carry early exercise risk that can disrupt the structure before expiration.

A butterfly can also be constructed entirely with puts instead of calls, or with a combination of both, and McMillan notes that all four variations produce identical profit-and-loss outcomes at expiration. The preference for all-call or all-put constructions comes down to practical considerations: using all calls when the middle strike is slightly above the current stock price; all puts when slightly below. The mixed construction — buying a bull call spread and a bear put spread at the same strikes — is theoretically equivalent but creates early exercise risk on in-the-money written options, which McMillan recommends avoiding.

Adjusting a butterfly when the stock moves away from the middle strike is difficult. Unlike a ratio spread where selling additional calls at a higher strike creates a new break-even, the butterfly's profit is already narrowly defined. McMillan's practical guidance is that butterflies, once entered, are best left alone and closed at expiration or when the maximum profit is approached. Dynamic adjustment typically involves turning the butterfly into something else — a vertical spread or a single long option — which destroys the original logic of the trade.

The butterfly's most legitimate use case is in a market where all options are extremely fairly priced — no skew, implied volatility at historical averages — and the trader has high conviction that the stock will stay in a narrow range. In practice, that combination rarely presents itself. The skew in index options typically makes butterfly construction slightly lopsided, and individual stock butterflies suffer from wide bid-ask spreads on each of the four legs. McMillan's summary is honest: butterflies are intellectually elegant but practically expensive, and most traders use them far too frequently for situations that would be better served by a simpler calendar spread or covered write.

Chapter 11: Ratio Call Spreads

Ratio call spreads combine the limited downside risk of buying calls with the premium income of selling more calls at a higher strike. The setup: buy one (or more) lower-strike calls and sell two (or more) higher-strike calls. Unlike a simple ratio write (which involves actual stock ownership), a ratio call spread uses only options — the downside is limited to the net debit paid, if any. The upside risk is unlimited above the upper break-even, as the naked calls on the top have no offsetting position.

This creates an interesting asymmetric profile. Below the lower strike at expiration, the loss is limited to the debit (or a credit if established for a credit). Above the upper break-even, losses grow without limit. Between the two break-even points, the position profits. McMillan contrasts this directly with a simple ratio write: the ratio write has significant downside risk (the stock can fall to zero) but limited upside risk at the position break-even. The ratio spread has limited downside but unlimited upside risk — a fundamentally different risk structure from the same ratio.

The neutral ratio for a spread is calculated by dividing the delta of the long call by the delta of the short call. If the October 40 call has a delta of 0.80 and the October 45 call has a delta of 0.50, the neutral ratio is 0.80/0.50 = 1.6:1, meaning selling 8 calls for every 5 long calls (or approximately 5:8) creates a position with zero initial delta. This precision matters because a poorly chosen ratio introduces unintended directional bias from the outset.

One popular application is the delta spread: a ratio designed to be delta-neutral at inception, not for directional speculation but for volatility decay. If implied volatility is high and the trader expects it to fall, a delta-neutral ratio spread collects premium while remaining insensitive to small directional moves. As time passes and volatility contracts, the position profits from both time decay and IV compression.

Follow-up when the stock rallies in a ratio call spread requires buying additional long calls to reduce the effective ratio. If the ratio was 2:1 and the stock has rallied substantially, buying one more long call at the short strike converts the naked portion to a covered position. McMillan provides the break-even cost formula for these additional purchases: (short calls × strike difference minus total debit) / number of naked calls remaining.

Two philosophies for ratio call spread construction produce dramatically different positions. The first simulates a ratio write: buy a deep in-the-money call trading near parity (no time premium) plus sell two out-of-the-money calls. The deep ITM call provides near-identical price exposure to long stock at a fraction of the capital, making the position economically close to the covered ratio write but with strictly limited downside. The second philosophy uses strikes where the position can be entered for a credit: if the stock is well below both strikes, the worst case is a small gain or zero loss if the stock stays put and all options expire worthless.

The delta calculation for a neutral ratio spread is the same formula as for ratio writes: divide the delta of the long call by the delta of the short call to find the number of short options needed per long option. With an April 40 call delta of 0.80 and an April 45 call delta of 0.50, the neutral ratio is 0.80 / 0.50 = 1.60 to 1, or roughly 8 long for every 5 short in even lots. This makes the position delta-neutral at inception but not thereafter — gamma ensures the delta changes as the stock moves.

The break-even formula for adding long calls to a ratio spread that has moved against the trader is one of the more useful calculations in this chapter. If 5 calls were sold, 3 were bought, the strike difference is 5 points, and the position was established for even money, buying 2 more long calls to close the ratio costs money at whatever the market price is. McMillan's formula determines the maximum acceptable price for those additional calls — the price at which the cost of reducing the ratio to 1:1 exactly equals the remaining profit potential in the spread. Paying more than that break-even means the follow-up makes the position worse than simply closing the whole spread.

Chapter 12: Calendar and Ratio Combinations

This chapter explores combinations that blend the time decay advantage of calendar spreads with the premium collection of ratio strategies. The ratio calendar spread is the primary vehicle: sell two near-term out-of-the-money calls and buy one longer-term out-of-the-money call at the same strike. The position is established for a credit — the two near-term calls collected more than the one longer-term call costs. If the near-term calls expire worthless (stock stays below strike), the trader owns the longer-term call essentially free.

The risk in a ratio calendar spread is concentrated in the near-term expiration period. If the stock rallies hard before the near-term options expire, the two short calls are moving against the position while the one long call provides only partial offset. The unhedged portion behaves like a naked call. McMillan's remedy: plan the defensive action point before entering. If the stock moves to within a certain distance of the strike before the near-term expiration, close the entire spread regardless of whether the analysis still seems compelling.

The delta-neutral calendar spread uses a ratio calculated to make the position neutral at inception. Selling more near-term calls than long-term calls (when the near-term has a lower delta than the long-term) can produce delta neutrality, but the resulting naked portion creates significant risk if the stock moves. Alternatively, the neutral version can be constructed by buying more long-term calls than near-term calls sold — this avoids naked positions and creates a pure long-vega, long-theta-negative exposure.

McMillan's practical conclusion is that ratio calendar spreads are among the most complex strategies in the book, suitable only for traders who understand the multi-dimensional risk profile and can react quickly to adverse moves. The potential reward — owning a long-dated call for free after near-term expiration — is real, but the path to that reward requires active management and willingness to exit before the worst outcome materializes.

The ratio calendar spread is established for a credit: the near-term premium collected from two calls exceeds the cost of the single longer-term call purchased. If the near-term options expire worthless — stock stays below the strike — the trader owns the longer-term call for free. At that point the position shifts from a defined-risk spread to a free speculative bet on a future rally. The risk is entirely concentrated in the period before near-term expiration: if the stock rallies hard before those calls expire, the naked short calls create large, accelerating losses.

McMillan's critical guidance for ratio calendar spreads is to plan the defensive exit based on the upside break-even of the near-term spread, not merely the margin requirement. The margin system calculates a minimum posting requirement, which is always far below the actual loss exposure if the stock blasts through the strike. A prudent trader calculates where the loss equals the initial credit received, treats that as the stop-loss point, and places a contingent order or mental stop before entering. Failing to plan this in advance leads to paralysis when the stock moves — the natural impulse to "wait for a reversal" is exactly what turns a manageable loss into a large one.

The ITM ratio calendar spread flips the structure: buy more near-term calls than the single longer-term call purchased. Because the near-term calls are in-the-money with high delta, the position delta is initially positive — it behaves more like a long stock substitute. There are no naked short calls, eliminating the explosive upside risk. The trade is profitable if the stock rallies before near-term expiration and the long calls are sold for more than cost, with the longer-term call surviving as a kicker.

The delta-neutral calendar spread uses a ratio based on the delta comparison of the two strikes. If the near-term option has a delta of 0.70 and the longer-term option a delta of 0.50, the delta-neutral ratio is 0.70 / 0.50 = 1.4 near-term options sold per longer-term option purchased. This ensures the position starts with no directional bias, profiting from time decay if the stock stays near the strike and losing if the stock makes a large move in either direction. Adjusting for delta drift as the stock moves is the ongoing management task.

Chapter 13: Reverse Spreads

Reverse spreads are the mirror images of ratio spreads: instead of selling more options than you buy, you buy more than you sell. This creates a long-volatility position — profits come from large moves in either direction, while the loss is limited to the net debit (or is reduced by the net credit) plus the difference in strike values at expiration.

The backspread (also called a reverse ratio spread) is the primary example: sell one lower-strike call and buy two higher-strike calls. Example: sell one July 40 call at $4, buy two July 45 calls at $1 each. Net credit: $2. Maximum loss: $3 (at the $45 strike at expiration — both long calls expire worthless, short call costs $5 to close). Upside break-even: $48 (upper strike plus max loss). Below $40: profit $2 (the credit). Above $48: unlimited profit as the two long calls outperform the one short.

Backspreads work best when implied volatility is low — options are cheap to buy — and a large move is expected. The position profits from a volatility expansion as well as from the stock move itself. When established for a credit, the trader has a "free" bet: collect money if the stock stays flat or falls below the lower strike, and win unlimited if it rallies hard.

The reverse calendar spread is even simpler in concept but harder to execute: sell the longer-term call and buy the near-term call at the same strike. This position profits if the stock moves far from the strike (collapses the spread value) or if implied volatility falls quickly (near-term options decline less than longer-term in absolute dollar terms). The practical challenge is margin: exchanges treat this like a naked call on the short (longer-term) side, requiring substantial collateral.

The neutral backspread uses the same delta-ratio approach as ratio spreads but in reverse: buy more options than sold, with the ratio equal to the delta of the sold option divided by the delta of the bought options. This creates a position that is delta-zero initially but has large positive gamma — it accelerates in the trader's favor on big moves in either direction.

The reverse calendar spread works opposite to a standard calendar: sell the longer-term call and buy the near-term call at the same strike. This position profits from two specific outcomes: a large stock move away from the strike (both options go to parity or zero, collapsing the spread that was originally established at a credit), or a significant decline in implied volatility (the longer-term call, which has more vega, loses more value than the near-term). The practical problem for retail traders is that the long near-term option creates a "naked long" in regulatory terms that is classified as a naked write of the longer-term option, requiring full naked margin.

The backspread's break-even calculations are essential for evaluating attractiveness. Using McMillan's example — sell one July 40 call at $4, buy two July 45 calls at $1 each — the net credit is $2. Maximum loss occurs at $45 at expiration: the short call is $5 in-the-money (loss of $1 after credit), the long calls expire exactly at-the-money (zero value), total loss is $3. Below $40 at expiration, all three options expire worthless and the $2 credit is kept. Above the upside break-even of $48 (the upper strike plus the maximum loss), the two long calls are more than $3 in-the-money and profit accelerates without limit.

Backspreads are the rare options strategy that performs best when implied volatility is already low at entry. Since the trader is buying more options than selling, low IV means cheap options to buy — the maximum loss is limited to a small amount, while the profit potential on a large move is theoretically unlimited. If implied volatility rises after entry, the long-heavy position gains from the vega expansion. McMillan positions backspreads as the opposite of ratio spreads in the volatility cycle: enter backspreads when vol is low and rising is expected, enter ratio spreads when vol is high and declining is expected.

Chapter 14: Diagonalizing a Spread

Any spread that uses different strikes AND different expirations is a diagonal spread. Diagonalizing changes the character of the position in subtle but important ways. A bull spread where the long option expires later than the short option becomes slightly more bearish at the near-term expiration (the long option still retains value even if the stock falls below the lower strike). A calendar spread where the two strikes differ becomes directionally biased.

The diagonal bull spread — buying a longer-term lower-strike call and selling a near-term higher-strike call — is McMillan's primary example of diagonalizing done well. If the near-term option expires worthless (stock below the higher strike), the trader can sell the next month's option against the long call, continuing to collect premium. Repeat this enough times and the long call's cost basis drops to zero — the trader effectively owns the long-term option for free.

The diagonal approach to "owning a call for free" uses a bear spread constructed diagonally: sell a near-term lower-strike call (collecting premium) and buy a longer-term higher-strike call (paying less than collected). If the near-term call expires worthless, the profit from the trade covers the cost of the long-term call — the trader now holds a free option. This is an elegant structure when the trader wants long-term upside exposure but is willing to give up near-term upside to fund it.

A diagonal backspread extends this logic: sell one near-term lower-strike call and buy two longer-term higher-strike calls for net even or a credit. If the near-term call expires worthless, the trader owns two long-term calls for free — a pure speculative bet on a large rally at no cost. The risk is a near-term rally that forces closure of the position at a loss before the near-term expires.

McMillan's broader point about diagonal spreads is that the time dimension adds a degree of freedom that allows more creative positioning. A trader who understands how to diagonalize can fine-tune the break-even points, reduce net debit, and create structures that have limited downside with theoretically unlimited upside — all by mixing different expiration months rather than using a single expiration throughout.

The diagonal element — different expirations — creates a position that morphs over time in ways a vertical spread cannot. A standard bull spread fixes both legs at the same expiration; at expiration, profit or loss is fully determined. A diagonal bull spread — long a six-month lower-strike call, short a one-month higher-strike call — after the near-term call expires leaves the trader holding a longer-term call at a reduced net cost. If the near-term call expired worthless, the effective cost of the remaining long-term call is the original debit minus any premium already received, potentially turning an expensive speculative call into a cheap one.

The "free call" construction uses a diagonal bear spread: sell the near-term lower-strike call and buy the longer-term higher-strike call, structured to collect more on the near-term sale than paid for the longer-term purchase. If the near-term call expires worthless, the trader has effectively been paid to own a long-term call. The risk: the near-term short call is in-the-money if the stock rallies before expiration, creating potential early assignment. McMillan prefers structuring these with enough strike separation that the near-term call is out-of-the-money at initiation — this reduces early assignment risk and gives the position more probability of the near-term call expiring worthless.

McMillan's broader framework for diagonal spreads is that the time dimension is a tool for reducing cost, not for dramatically changing the directional character of the trade. A diagonal bull spread is still a moderately bullish trade — it does not become a volatility trade or a neutral trade just because the expirations differ. Traders who expect a large move should use backspreads or outright option purchases; those who want the precise mechanics of a calendar with some directional tilt can use diagonal constructions to blend the two.

Chapter 15: Put Option Basics

Puts are the mirror image of calls, and understanding their specific pricing nuances is essential before any put strategy makes sense. A put gives the holder the right to sell 100 shares at the strike price before expiration. It gains value as the stock declines. An at-the-money put on a $50 stock with a $50 strike is worth something because there is time — the stock could fall below $50 before expiration.

The relationship between put prices and call prices is governed by put-call parity: Put = Call − Stock + Strike + PV(Strike) − Dividends. At the same strike and expiration, an at-the-money put always sells for less than an at-the-money call. The difference is the carrying cost — the interest that would be earned on the strike price if held in cash for the remaining life of the option, minus any dividends the stock pays. Rising interest rates increase the gap; rising dividends close it.

Put time value behaves differently from call time value for in-the-money options. Deep in-the-money puts lose time value more rapidly than their call equivalents. A put deep in-the-money trades close to its intrinsic value almost immediately — the holder would rather exercise early to get the cash than wait. This creates an important asymmetry: in-the-money calls retain time value longer than in-the-money puts, because the call holder delays exercise to keep the interest on the unspent purchase price.

Large dividends specifically increase put values. When a stock goes ex-dividend, its price drops by approximately the dividend amount. An in-the-money put therefore becomes more valuable before an ex-dividend date, because the stock price drop makes the put even more in-the-money. Conversely, call holders may exercise early to capture the dividend — converting from the option to actual stock ownership before the ex-date. McMillan dedicates considerable attention to dividend timing because it drives a significant portion of early exercise behavior.

Early exercise of puts is rational when the put is deeply in-the-money and the interest that could be earned on the exercise proceeds (at the strike price) exceeds the remaining time value in the put. If a trader holds a $60 put and the stock is at $40, the $20 intrinsic value sitting in the put could instead be earning interest as cash. When the interest on $6,000 for the remaining weeks exceeds the remaining time premium, early exercise is the correct financial decision.

The auto-exercise rule applies to puts as well: any put that is $0.375 or more in-the-money at expiration is automatically exercised. Traders who are short puts — who have sold puts — must be prepared for assignment at any time on American-style puts, especially as the put moves deeper in-the-money or a large dividend approaches.

The put-call parity formula has direct practical implications. At the money, a put should trade for less than the corresponding call by the carrying cost amount: Strike × interest rate × time minus expected dividends. On a $50 stock with a 5% interest rate and three months to expiration with no dividend, the at-the-money call should be approximately $0.625 more expensive than the at-the-money put (50 × 5% × 0.25). This differential reflects the cost of carrying long stock versus the interest earned in a cash-secured put position. When this relationship breaks down — either option trades outside the no-arbitrage band — professional arbitrageurs step in immediately through conversions or reversals.

Put delta behavior deserves specific attention because it differs from call delta in one key way for deep in-the-money options. A deep in-the-money put approaches a delta of −1.0 asymptotically, but it gets there faster than a deep in-the-money call approaches +1.0. This happens because a deeply ITM put is near parity — it has almost no time value and trades like a short stock position. McMillan's table demonstrates that a put's time value decays faster when in-the-money than the corresponding call's does, because the interest rate effect works against the put holder: the longer the put remains unexercised, the more interest income on the proceeds of a hypothetical stock sale is being foregone.

Chapter 16: Put Buying

Put buying is the cleanest bearish speculation: pay a premium for the right to profit from a stock's decline, with maximum risk limited to the premium paid. A trader who buys a July 50 put for $3 risks $300 per contract, period. If the stock falls to $40 by July expiration, the put is worth $10 and the trader has made $700. No margin, no borrowed shares to recall, no uptick rule — puts are the most accessible short-side instrument for individual investors.

Choosing between strikes for put buying follows the same logic as call buying but in reverse. A slightly in-the-money put has a delta of 0.55–0.65 in absolute terms, capturing most of the stock's decline with less percentage risk of total loss than an out-of-the-money put. A deep in-the-money put behaves nearly like short stock — effective but expensive. An out-of-the-money put is cheap but requires a large decline to reach break-even. McMillan consistently recommends slightly ITM or ATM puts as the best starting point for directional put buyers.

Put buying versus short selling involves concrete tradeoffs. The put buyer's maximum loss is known and limited from the start. The short seller earns interest on the short proceeds but owes any dividends declared during the holding period, faces margin calls if the stock rallies, and can have shares recalled by the lender at an inconvenient time. For most individual investors, puts offer a cleaner, lower-friction expression of the bearish view.

Delta interpretation is crucial for put buyers. The at-the-money put has a delta of approximately −0.45 (slightly less than −0.50 because of the asymmetry introduced by interest rates). A put with a delta of −0.60 moves $0.60 for each $1 decline in the stock — effective leverage without the borrowing complications of a short position. As the stock declines and the put moves deeper in-the-money, the delta approaches −1.0 and the put behaves like 100 shares of short stock.

Follow-up for put buyers mirrors call buyer discipline. Take profits when the stock has moved substantially and the put is deeply in-the-money with little remaining time premium — the effective leverage has been exhausted. Roll down to a lower strike if the stock continues declining after the initial move has been captured. Never let a profitable put decay back to zero simply because the trader is waiting for a slightly better price.

The delta of a put purchase is the most immediate practical consideration for position sizing. An at-the-money put with a delta of −0.45 will gain approximately $0.45 for every dollar the stock falls. A 10-point stock decline on 10 put contracts (1,000 shares) generates approximately $4,500 in profit — a significant return on the typical $2–4 in premium paid per option. But if the stock barely moves or rises, 100% of the premium is at risk regardless of how right the bearish thesis eventually proves. Sizing put positions conservatively is essential precisely because the all-or-nothing nature of out-of-the-money puts makes over-concentration devastating to a portfolio.

Put buyers have three distinct follow-up decisions when the trade moves in their favor. First: take profit by selling the put when it has doubled or tripled in value. Second: roll down — sell the appreciated in-the-money put and buy a lower-strike put, capturing most of the gain while maintaining bearish exposure on a further decline. Third: sell a call against the profitable put position, creating a synthetic short stock position for free, which is an aggressive way to extend the trade without additional cost. McMillan's general recommendation leans toward rolling down rather than holding puts deeply in-the-money, where time value has disappeared and only intrinsic value remains.

A practical consideration McMillan emphasizes for put buyers is the bid-ask spread problem on illiquid puts. Out-of-the-money puts on small or mid-cap stocks often have wide bid-ask spreads — buying at $1.50 and finding the bid is only $0.80 means a 47% loss before the stock moves a single tick. Liquidity considerations should filter out many candidates that look attractive on paper. McMillan's preference is for options where the spread is no more than $0.25 (or 10% of option value, whichever is smaller) and where open interest is sufficient that exit is reliably available.

Chapter 17: Protective Puts and Collars

Buying a put against a stock position — the protective put — is the simplest form of portfolio insurance. The setup: own 100 shares, buy one put at a strike below the current stock price. The put limits the downside to the strike price minus the put premium, regardless of how far the stock falls. The profit-and-loss graph is identical to buying a call at the same strike — a key structural equivalence that McMillan uses to connect disparate strategies.

The choice of which put to buy involves the usual tradeoff: further out-of-the-money puts cost less but provide protection only against catastrophic declines. Slightly out-of-the-money puts (5–10% below the current price) strike a balance between cost and meaningful downside protection. At-the-money puts provide full protection from any decline but cost the most. The decision depends on how much downside the investor is willing to absorb as a normal fluctuation versus how much requires insurance.

A critical tax warning applies to protective puts. If an investor has held stock for less than a year and buys a put, the holding period for long-term capital gain treatment is reset to zero. The IRS treats the put purchase as effectively ending the at-risk period. To preserve a long-term holding period, the investor must either already have held the stock for more than a year before buying the put, or identify the put as a "married put" at purchase — intending to exercise it, not sell it.

The collar extends the protective put concept by layering an out-of-the-money call sale on top. Buy the stock, buy an OTM put for protection, sell an OTM call to offset the put's cost. The resulting position has a bounded range: the downside is limited by the put strike, and the upside is capped by the call strike. Net cost can approach zero if the call premium equals the put premium — a no-cost collar or zero-cost collar.

McMillan presents a useful table for LEAPS collars. With two-and-a-half year options at various volatility levels, the at-the-money put can be financed by an out-of-the-money call at these distances from the current stock price: 30% volatility → call 30% OTM; 40% vol → call 35% OTM; 50% vol → call 40% OTM; 70% vol → call 50% OTM. Higher volatility means both options are more expensive, but the OTM call can be placed much further away because the premium it generates is larger in absolute terms.

The collar is one of the most practically valuable strategies in the book for long-term equity investors. It allows a holder of concentrated stock positions to create insurance without selling shares — preserving the tax basis and dividend stream while eliminating the risk of catastrophic loss. McMillan describes it as equivalent to owning a bull spread — which it is, structurally — but with the psychological advantage of understanding it as "insured stock ownership" rather than as an options position.

McMillan's LEAPS collar table is one of the most actionable pieces of data in the book for long-term equity investors. At a portfolio volatility of 30%, a two-and-a-half year at-the-money put can be financed by selling a call that is 30% above the current stock price. At 40% volatility, the at-the-money put is financed by a call only 35% out-of-the-money. At 50% volatility, the at-the-money put is financed by a call 40% above. This means that in high-volatility environments — precisely when investors most want downside protection — collars offer the best terms: more upside participation before the cap kicks in, because the expensive implied volatility inflates both legs.

The tax warning about protective puts deserves more than a footnote in any practical application. An investor who has held stock for 11 months, built a significant gain, and buys a put to lock in that gain will discover that the purchase resets the holding period to zero under the IRS's short-sale rule. The put must be held and the stock sold via put exercise to qualify for long-term treatment — simply selling the put for a profit and then selling the stock does not preserve the holding period. McMillan's recommended workaround for investors who want the protection without the holding period risk is to use the collar: the call sale partially offsets the put cost without triggering the same tax treatment.

Chapter 18: Straddle and Strangle Buying

A straddle buy combines a long call and a long put at the same strike and expiration — both at-the-money. The position profits from large moves in either direction. Maximum loss is the total premium paid, occurring when the stock is exactly at the strike at expiration. Break-even points are the strike plus the total premium (upside) and the strike minus the total premium (downside). With a stock at $50, a July 50 call at $3, and a July 50 put at $2, the total cost is $5 and break-evens are $55 and $45.

Straddle buying is fundamentally a bet on realized volatility exceeding implied volatility. The straddle buyer pays for a certain amount of movement — the break-even range represents the market's expectation of likely price swing. If the stock actually moves farther than this range in either direction, the buyer profits. If it stays within the range, the buyer loses time premium at the rate of theta per day. The edge, if any, comes from buying when implied volatility is low relative to expected future movement.

McMillan's selection criteria for straddle buying are clear. First, implied volatility should be low — below the 20th percentile of its own history, or meaningfully below recent historical volatility. Second, a catalyst should exist: an earnings report, an FDA decision, a merger vote, a product launch — some event that might generate movement beyond the normal range. Third, the mathematical projection should work: estimate the ±1 standard deviation move expected over the straddle's life, and check that a move of that size would produce a profit larger than the straddle's cost.

The strangle is a less expensive alternative: buy an out-of-the-money call and an out-of-the-money put (different strikes). The strangle costs less than the straddle because both options are OTM, but the stock must move even further to reach break-even — the distance from the lower put strike to the upper call strike plus the total premium paid. A strangle is attractive when the trader expects a very large move — larger than the ATM straddle's break-even — and wants to reduce the cost of the position.

An in-the-money strangle deserves mention: buying an in-the-money call and an in-the-money put (with strikes that straddle the stock from the ITM side on each leg). The combined position has guaranteed intrinsic value equal to the difference in strikes, and the time value risk is smaller in percentage terms. McMillan presents this as a lower-risk straddle variant — the cost is higher but the percentage risk of total loss is smaller because so much of the cost is in intrinsic value.

Follow-up discipline for straddle buyers is unconventional: McMillan warns against taking small profits in either direction. If the stock rallies $6 and the call has doubled, the instinct is to take the gain — but doing so eliminates the entire reason for holding the straddle. If the stock reverses and falls $12 from the high, the put would have been the big winner; closing the call early sacrificed that outcome. The better approach when the stock has moved significantly in one direction is to roll the losing leg — sell the now-worthless put and buy a put at a lower strike. This reduces risk without capping the ongoing upside if the stock continues to rally.

The hardest part of straddle buying is discipline around timing and size. Straddles need large moves to be profitable, and large moves are rare. Most straddles expire as losses. The winning trades, when they come, need to be large enough to overcome the accumulated losses — which requires letting winners run far past the comfortable exit point and sizing positions conservatively enough that a string of time-decay losses does not wipe out the account before the big move materializes.

McMillan's probability analysis provides the most rigorous framework for straddle selection. The straddle's upside break-even is the strike plus the total premium; the downside break-even is the strike minus total premium. Using the Black-Scholes probability formula — specifically the "ever-touches" variant rather than the endpoint probability — the trader can calculate the likelihood that the stock will reach either break-even at some point before expiration, not just at the final day. A straddle that has only a 40% probability of reaching either break-even is a losing proposition over time despite the unlimited theoretical profit. McMillan's threshold is 80% probability of reaching either break-even, confirmed by historical price movement analysis.

Chapter 19: Covered Straddle Writing

A covered straddle write combines owning 100 shares of stock with selling both an at-the-money call and an at-the-money put at the same strike and expiration. The stock covers the call (as in any covered write), and cash or margin covers the put. Maximum profit occurs when the stock is exactly at the strike at expiration — both options expire worthless and the writer keeps the combined premium from both the call and the put.

The superficial appeal is the double premium. If a covered call on a $50 stock generates $3 in premium, adding the put sale generates another $2, for a total of $5 — a much wider break-even and a higher income stream. But McMillan's analysis is largely cautionary about this construction. The covered straddle is actually inferior to a simple covered write in most circumstances, because the short put adds substantial downside risk without proportional compensation.

The problem becomes clear when the stock declines. In a simple covered write, the writer is losing on the stock and cushioned only by the call premium. In a covered straddle, the writer is losing on the stock AND simultaneously losing on the short put (the put buyer is profiting from the same decline). The covered straddle writer gets hit twice on the downside — double exposure to the same adverse move.

The break-even calculation illustrates the risk. Buy XYZ at $50, sell the $50 call for $3, sell the $50 put for $2. Total premium: $5. Break-even: $45 ($50 stock cost minus $5 collected). Below $45, the position loses. Above $50, the stock is called away but the writer keeps the $5 premium — a maximum profit of $5. The maximum profit range is a single point (at expiration exactly at $50), and the position has significant downside exposure below the break-even.

McMillan argues that most investors would be better served by choosing between a covered call and a naked put rather than combining them. A covered call and a naked put at the same strike have identical profit-and-loss profiles at expiration — both are economically identical. Adding both together doubles the downside risk while producing the same limited upside. The only genuine reason to prefer the covered straddle is if the combined premium is so large that the wider break-even justifies the doubled downside exposure.

A protected variant — the covered straddle butterfly — adds OTM put and call purchases to cap losses on both sides. Buying a put one or two strikes below and a call one or two strikes above converts the open-ended straddle write into a defined-risk position, at the cost of reducing net premium. McMillan notes that this construction is mathematically equivalent to a butterfly spread with long stock, and its mechanics should be evaluated accordingly.

The covered straddle is a strategy that looks conservative but creates a concentrated loss scenario that many traders fail to fully model. When the stock declines below the put strike, the trader is losing on the stock (as in any covered write) AND has an additional obligation from the put assignment — forced to buy more stock at the strike price when the stock is already below it. The effect is that the covered straddle writer ends up owning twice the shares at an elevated cost basis, which is a significantly worse outcome than simply writing covered calls against the original position.

McMillan's selection guidance for covered straddles, when used, emphasizes the importance of high implied volatility to justify the double premium exposure. The position should only be considered when the combined premium from both options is substantially above normal — at least 10–15% of the stock price — so that the downside buffer meaningfully exceeds the risk of forced double-purchase on a decline. Even then, McMillan's preference is the naked put combined with a T-bill position as a cleaner, more capital-efficient alternative with the identical P&L profile.

Chapter 20: Naked Straddle Writing

Naked straddle selling — selling both an at-the-money call and an at-the-money put without owning the underlying stock — is among the most powerful premium collection strategies and among the riskiest. The writer collects the combined call and put premium and profits when the stock stays near the strike through expiration. Losses are theoretically unlimited on the upside and very large on the downside. This strategy requires experienced, well-capitalized traders with pre-planned defensive procedures.

The mathematical edge in naked straddle writing comes from implied volatility exceeding realized volatility. When the options are pricing in 40% annual volatility and the stock subsequently moves at 20% annual volatility, the straddle writer has collected twice the premium that the actual risk justified. McMillan's entry condition is strict: implied volatility must be above the 80th percentile of its own history before selling a straddle. Entering when IV is low simply accepts unfavorable economics.

The break-even calculation is the first step. With a stock at $50, a call sold at $3, and a put sold at $2, the total premium is $5. The upside break-even is $55; the downside is $45. The stock must stay within this 10-point range for the position to be profitable. This range can seem comfortable in a quiet market but narrows quickly in a trending environment. A persistent directional trend is the straddle seller's worst enemy — not a single large gap, but a sustained drift in one direction that continuously widens the loss.

Follow-up actions are mandatory — not optional. McMillan's preferred procedure when the stock rallies to the upside break-even is to buy 100 shares of stock, converting the short call to a covered write and leaving a combined position of long stock plus short put. If the stock then continues higher, the call assignment is hedged. If the stock falls to the downside break-even, the writer instead sells stock short, converting the short put to a covered put.

The great danger is the whipsaw. The stock rallies to $55, the writer buys stock at $55. Then the stock reverses to $45, now triggering a short sale at $45. The writer has bought high and shorted low, realizing losses on each protective action in addition to potential losses on the straddle itself. McMillan recommends sizing naked straddle positions conservatively — no more than 10–15% of available capital on any single straddle — specifically to ensure the account can absorb follow-up costs without being forced into catastrophic positions by a margin call.

Short-term straddles are strongly preferred over LEAPS straddles. The time decay advantage — the reason straddle selling makes sense at all — is most powerful in the final weeks of a near-term option's life. LEAPS straddle selling gives up this advantage: decay is agonizingly slow for years and the whipsaw risk accumulates over a much longer horizon with much more time for adverse moves to compound. McMillan is emphatic on this point — straddle selling is a near-term strategy, and using it on long-dated options destroys most of the theoretical edge.

The ideal market condition for naked straddle selling is a stock that has just concluded a high-volatility event — earnings release, acquisition announcement, or regulatory decision — and implied volatility remains elevated even as the fundamental uncertainty has resolved. After earnings, for example, a stock often continues trading with elevated implied volatility for several days while the options market catches up to the new lower-risk environment. Writing straddles immediately post-event exploits this lag: premium is high, the catalyst is resolved, and the stock is likely to consolidate rather than move dramatically again in the near term.

Delta neutrality in naked straddle management is more important than many traders realize. When the stock drifts toward a break-even, the position's delta changes — it is no longer neutral. A straddle on a $50 stock that rallies to $54 now has a positive delta because the call is gaining value faster than the put. Selling stock to rebalance the delta — creating a covered call while retaining the written put — is the professional follow-up. This dynamic delta hedging is what separates professional straddle writers from amateurs who simply collect premium and hope for the best.

Chapter 21: Synthetic Positions

Synthetic positions use options to replicate the economic exposure of another instrument. The foundation is put-call parity: because call = stock + put (adjusted for carrying cost), any one leg can be synthesized from the other two. These equivalences are maintained by arbitrageurs who trade whenever synthetic and real prices diverge, ensuring the relationships hold in practice and not just in theory.

A synthetic long stock position combines a long call and a short put at the same strike and expiration. Below the strike, the short put loses (just like owning stock). Above the strike, the long call gains (just like owning stock). At expiration the combined profit-and-loss graph is identical to owning 100 shares — but without the capital required to own the actual stock, and without the dividend collection. This structure is used by arbitrageurs, market makers, and sophisticated traders who want stock-like exposure more capital-efficiently.

A synthetic short stock combines a long put and a short call at the same strike — the mirror image. A synthetic put combines short stock and a long call: if the stock falls, the short stock profits; if it rallies, the call caps the loss. McMillan demonstrates that every position — stock, calls, and puts — can be replicated by some combination of the other two, with carrying cost as the adjustment factor.

The split-strike synthetic (risk reversal) uses different strikes for the two legs. A bullish split-strike buys an out-of-the-money call and sells an out-of-the-money put at a lower strike. The position profits if the stock rallies above the call strike, profits also if the stock stays between the two strikes (both options expire worthless), and loses only if the stock falls below the put strike. This is attractive when options are expensive — the premium collected on the put sale offsets much of the call's cost.

A bearish split-strike (the put side) buys an OTM put and sells an OTM call — equivalent to a protective collar without actually owning the stock. This position benefits from a decline without requiring the capital to own shares. The loss is limited if the stock rallies above the call strike, and the profit grows as the stock falls below the put strike.

McMillan uses synthetic positions to demonstrate a fundamental point: the options market is deeply interconnected. Different strategies that appear unrelated often share the same economic profile when analyzed through the lens of put-call parity. Recognizing these equivalences allows the trader to choose the most capital-efficient or tax-advantageous path to any desired risk exposure, rather than blindly applying a named strategy.

The practical utility of synthetic positions shows up most clearly in tax management and margin efficiency. An investor who wants to be long a stock but has large paper gains in current shares they cannot sell without a tax event can buy a synthetic long at a different tax lot. Alternatively, a trader who cannot short a stock (no locate, uptick rule on equity) can replicate the short stock exposure through a synthetic short — long put, short call — at the same strike with no borrowing required. These equivalences are not theoretical curiosities; they are working tools in the options professional's kit.

McMillan's framework for split-strike synthetics (risk reversals) ties directly to volatility skew. When implied volatility is elevated — options in general are expensive — a bullish split-strike position (buy OTM call, sell OTM put) benefits from selling the expensive put while still getting upside exposure through the call. The net cost is lower than a straight call purchase, and if the put is sold at a higher implied volatility than the call is purchased, the position has a structural edge in the options pricing. This is how professional risk reversal trading exploits the persistent skew structure that post-1987 options markets exhibit.

Chapter 22: Basic Put Spreads

Put spreads are among the most useful and underused tools in the bearish trader's toolkit. A put bear spread — buying a higher-strike put and selling a lower-strike put — captures a modest decline while dramatically reducing the cost and risk of outright put buying. Example: stock at $50, buy a July 50 put for $4, sell a July 45 put for $2. Net debit: $2. Maximum profit: $3 (strike difference of $5 minus the $2 debit) if the stock is below $45 at expiration. Maximum loss: $2 (the debit) if the stock is above $50.

McMillan argues that put bear spreads are superior to call bear spreads for two specific reasons. First, the written put in a put bear spread is out-of-the-money — below the current stock price — which means early exercise risk is low. In a call bear spread, the written call is at- or in-the-money, and early exercise can force an assignment at an inconvenient time. Second, deeply in-the-money puts lose time value more rapidly than deeply in-the-money calls, meaning that when the stock declines sharply into the profitable zone, the put bear spread widens faster — the short put's time value evaporates quickly, maximizing the spread's value sooner.

A put bull spread reverses the logic: buy a lower-strike put and sell a higher-strike put, collecting a net credit. Maximum profit is the credit received (stock above the higher strike). Maximum loss is the strike difference minus the credit (stock below the lower strike). This is the put equivalent of a call bull spread and similarly appropriate for moderately bullish views where the premium collected offsets the cost of the insurance-like long put.

Put calendar spreads follow the same structure as call calendar spreads but with a different behavioral nuance. A neutral put calendar — sell near-term put, buy longer-term put at the same strike — profits when the stock stays near the strike through near-term expiration. However, McMillan notes that neutral call calendars are generally superior to put calendars because calls carry more time value than puts at the same strike. The call calendar generates more premium from the short near-term leg relative to the cost of the long leg, producing a better initial credit and a more attractive risk-reward ratio.

The bearish put calendar uses out-of-the-money puts: sell a near-term OTM put (below the current stock price) and buy a longer-term OTM put at the same strike. The near-term put expires worthless if the stock stays above the strike — the trader owns the longer-term put at reduced cost. Then, if the stock subsequently declines below the strike, the longer-term put becomes profitable. This two-stage structure — first collect the near-term premium, then profit from the subsequent decline — is the bearish calendar's core logic.

Put bear spreads have a specific follow-up structure. When the stock falls as anticipated and the spread approaches maximum value, McMillan recommends closing at 80% of maximum profit rather than holding to expiration for the final 20%. In the last weeks, time decay on the now-in-the-money written lower-strike put slows down while the risk of a stock reversal remains. The position that has made 80% of its theoretical maximum is effectively done — squeezing out the remaining premium requires holding through unnecessary gamma risk.

The put bull spread provides a lower-risk alternative to naked put writing for the investor who is bullish on a stock. Sell the higher-strike put and buy a lower-strike put at the same expiration, collecting a net credit. If the stock stays above the higher strike, both puts expire worthless and the full credit is kept. If the stock declines below the higher strike but stays above the lower strike, the loss grows linearly. Below the lower strike, the maximum loss is capped at the strike difference minus the credit — the bought put limits the exposure. The put bull spread is the margin-efficient, defined-risk version of the naked put write.

McMillan's comparison of neutral call calendars and neutral put calendars identifies a structural reason to prefer calls for this strategy. Call options typically have more time value than puts at the same strike and expiration because of the interest rate component in their pricing. More time value in the sold short-term call means more premium collected; more time value in the long longer-term call means more value retained. The calendar spread's profit is the difference in time decay rates, and this difference is maximized with calls. McMillan's practical conclusion: default to call calendars for neutral time-decay plays; use put calendars only when specific structural reasons apply, such as anticipated dividend impact.

Chapter 23: Combined Spreads

Combined spreads build on the simple spread structures to create more complex positions with specific risk-reward characteristics. The all-calls butterfly, all-puts butterfly, and mixed butterfly are all variations of the same structure — the interesting question is which construction is most efficient and which avoids the most dangerous risks like early assignment on in-the-money shorts.

McMillan presents four distinct ways to construct a butterfly at the 50, 60, and 70 strike levels. All four produce identical profit-and-loss graphs at expiration, but they differ in execution quality. The best construction combines a call bull spread (long 50 call, short 60 call) with a put bear spread (long 70 put, short 60 put). This approach sells the at-the-money options but avoids selling any in-the-money options — the short positions are all at-the-money or out-of-the-money at inception, minimizing early exercise risk throughout the position's life.

The calendar combination is one of McMillan's more creative constructions: sell a near-term out-of-the-money call AND a near-term out-of-the-money put, while simultaneously buying a longer-term out-of-the-money call and a longer-term out-of-the-money put at the same strikes but different months. The net risk is the debit paid (if both near-term options expire worthless), and the potential is a large profit if the stock makes a significant move after the near-term options have expired.

Selection criteria for the calendar combination are specific: the stock should be roughly midway between the two strikes; the strikes should be at least 10 points apart; near-term expiration should be two to three months away; and the near-term combination premium should be more than half the cost of the longer-term combination. When these conditions align, the trade offers a reasonable probability of either breaking even (through near-term premium collection) or winning large if a subsequent move occurs.

The diagonal butterfly — sell a near-term straddle and buy longer-term out-of-the-money puts and calls — is an advanced structure that establishes a credit position (the near-term straddle premium exceeds the cost of the long-dated wings). The objective is to buy back the near-term straddle for less than the credit received, keeping the longer-term wing options essentially free. McMillan's selection rule: the near-term straddle premium should be at least 1.5 times the cost of the longer-term combination to justify the trade.

The calendar combination — selling both a near-term call and a near-term put while buying longer-term versions of each — is McMillan's preferred structure for traders who want volatility exposure with defined risk. The maximum loss is the net debit paid, which occurs if both near-term options expire worthless AND the longer-term options are then immediately sold for nothing (unlikely but theoretically possible). In practice, the longer-term options retain significant time value even after the near-term pair expires, so the actual maximum loss is smaller than the theoretical maximum. The position's profit potential is two-pronged: it gains from time decay if the stock stays between the strikes during near-term expiration, AND from a large move in either direction thereafter.

Selection for the calendar combination requires specific criteria. The stock price should be roughly midway between the two strikes at initiation. The strikes should be at least 10 points apart for equity options. Time to near-term expiration should be 2–3 months, giving enough time for near-term decay but not so long that the premium differential is minimal. Crucially, the near-term combination price should be more than half the longer-term combination price — this ensures the trader is not overpaying for the long legs relative to the short-term premium collected.

The diagonal butterfly — selling a near-term straddle and buying longer-term out-of-the-money calls and puts — is the credit version of the calendar combination. If the near-term straddle can be sold for more than the longer-term combination costs, the position is established for a credit. The maximum risk is the difference between the nearest strike pair and the credit received — a specifically defined amount. The objective is to close the near-term straddle cheaply after it decays, leaving the trader with the longer-term combination at net zero or better cost. McMillan's selection rule: only enter when the near-term straddle is at least 1.5 times the price of the longer-term OTM combination, ensuring adequate credit cushion.

Chapter 24: Ratio Put Spreads

A ratio put spread buys one higher-strike put and sells two or more lower-strike puts. Unlike a simple put bear spread, the ratio spread has no upside risk — if the stock rallies, both puts expire worthless and the position breaks even (or earns a small credit if established for a credit). The downside risk is real: below the lower strike, the naked portion of the sold puts begins to accumulate losses. Maximum profit occurs when the stock is exactly at the lower strike at expiration.

A concrete example clarifies the structure. Stock at $50, buy one January 50 put for $4, sell two January 45 puts for $2 each. Net cost: even money. Profit range: $40–$50 (between break-even and lower strike). Maximum profit: $5 at the $45 strike. Downside break-even: $40 (the lower strike minus the maximum profit of $5). Above $50: even money. The position has no risk on the upside and significant risk below $40.

McMillan's preferred application of ratio put spreads is in index options, where the reverse volatility skew makes the strategy particularly attractive. Index put options at out-of-the-money strikes trade at higher implied volatility than at-the-money puts — a persistent feature of the post-1987 market. The ratio put spread sells these expensive out-of-the-money puts while buying the less expensive at-the-money puts. The theoretical edge from the skew adds to the normal premium-collection advantage of the short legs.

The ratio put calendar spread adds a time dimension: buy a longer-term put and sell two or more near-term puts at the same strike. If the near-term puts expire worthless (stock stays above the strike), the trader owns the longer-term put at zero cost. Then if the stock declines after near-term expiration, the longer-term put generates profit. McMillan's key defensive rule: close the entire position if the stock falls 8–10% below the written strike before near-term expiration. At that point, the naked short puts are becoming dangerous and the theoretical logic of the trade has broken down.

The ratio put spread's maximum profit formula mirrors the ratio call spread: the profit is maximized when the stock is at the lower strike at expiration. Using McMillan's example — buy one January 50 put for $4, sell two January 45 puts at $2 each, net zero debit — the maximum profit is $5 (the $5 difference between strikes, received when both puts are in-the-money by exactly their respective amounts). The upside break-even is anywhere above $50 — all options expire worthless, the net cost was zero, so no loss. The downside break-even is $40 — below that, the two short puts create a loss faster than the long put compensates.

McMillan identifies the primary use case for ratio put spreads in index options markets, where the persistent reverse volatility skew makes the sold out-of-the-money puts structurally expensive. In equity index markets — SPX, OEX — out-of-the-money puts consistently trade at higher implied volatility than at-the-money or out-of-the-money calls. This means the trader buying the slightly OTM put and selling two further OTM puts is selling the expensive ones and buying the fairly-priced one — an inherent structural edge that persists regardless of the market's direction.

The ratio put calendar spread is the temporal extension: buy a longer-term put and sell two near-term puts at the same strike. If the near-term puts expire worthless (stock stays above the strike), the longer-term put is now owned for free (or at a credit if the short premium exceeded the long cost). At that point, if the stock falls below the strike, the longer- term put generates substantial profit with no offsetting short put burden. The risk is a sharp decline before the near-term puts expire: the two naked short puts accelerate losses quickly. McMillan's defensive rule: close the position if the stock falls 8–10% below the strike before near-term expiration.

The ratio calendar combination wraps both sides — puts and calls — into a single structure. Sell two near-term straddles (or OTM combinations) and buy one longer-term straddle (or OTM combination) at the same strikes. The near-term premium collected is twice the longer-term combination cost, establishing the position for a credit. If both near-term options expire worthless — the stock stays between the put and call strikes — the trader owns the longer-term combination for free. The risk is a directional break in either direction before near-term expiration, at which point the two short positions (one naked call, one naked put) accumulate losses quickly. McMillan's emphasis: enter only small positions and close immediately at the first sign of a breakout.

Chapter 25: LEAPS

LEAPS — Long-term Equity Anticipation Securities — are listed options with expirations extending two or more years into the future. They trade exactly like standard equity options but with a very different time profile. A January LEAPS option expiring 24 months away decays at roughly 0.12% of its value per day — compared to a standard 3-month option at the same strike losing 0.60% per day. This difference in decay rate is the central fact that shapes every LEAPS strategy.

McMillan's decay table captures the progression precisely. At 24 months remaining, an at-the-money LEAPS call loses just 0.12% of its value daily. At 18 months: 0.14%. At 12 months: 0.19%. At 6 months: 0.27%. At 3 months: 0.60%. At 1 month: 1.27%. For a 20% out-of-the-money option the accelerations are even more dramatic — 0.18% at 24 months, 3.57% at 3 months. The practical implication: a LEAPS buyer has months to be right about direction with negligible decay cost; a short-term OTM option buyer is fighting exponential decay from day one.

The delta of a LEAPS call is substantially higher than a short-term call at the same strike. An at-the-money 2-year LEAPS call has a delta of approximately 0.70 — compared to 0.50 for a 3-month ATM call. This is because the longer time allows more scenarios where the stock finishes in-the-money. The higher delta means LEAPS buyers get more of the stock's move per dollar invested. Conversely, LEAPS puts have lower delta in absolute terms (approximately −0.30 at-the-money) versus −0.50 for short-term ATM puts.

The LEAPS stock substitute strategy exploits these characteristics. Rather than buying 100 shares of a $50 stock for $5,000, the trader buys a deep in-the-money LEAPS call with a $40 strike for $12 (intrinsic value $10 plus $2 time premium). The remaining $3,810 goes into T-bills. Net opportunity cost: $200 time premium plus $50 in dividends foregone minus $190 in T-bill interest = $60. For $60 of opportunity cost, the trader has the same upside as owning the stock, with maximum loss limited to the $1,200 call premium. When the analysis works, the LEAPS substitution is the most capital-efficient way to hold a large-cap equity long.

Covered LEAPS writing — selling near-term calls against a long LEAPS position — converts the long-term speculative bet into an income-generating machine. Each near-term call sold brings in premium that reduces the net cost of the LEAPS. If the near-term calls consistently expire worthless, the cost basis of the LEAPS approaches zero over time. When the LEAPS itself eventually expires or is sold, whatever profit remains is pure gain. This strategy requires patience but very little ongoing capital.

Naked LEAPS puts are McMillan's preferred alternative to covered call writing. The naked put at the same strike has an identical profit-and-loss profile to the covered write, but instead of tying up stock capital, the put writer posts collateral (stocks or bonds already owned). That collateral continues earning returns — dividends, interest — while the put premium is collected. McMillan's example shows naked LEAPS put writing returning 24.4% versus 21.5% for covered call writing on an equivalent position, purely from the more efficient capital usage.

One LEAPS strategy that does NOT work as advertised is the diagonal LEAPS spread: buying a LEAPS lower-strike call and selling short-term higher-strike calls against it. The problem emerges when the stock rallies strongly: the short calls (with higher delta because they are now in-the-money and near expiration) can lose more than the long LEAPS call gains (whose delta, though high, is capped by the longer time remaining). The spread can lose money precisely when the direction bet was right. McMillan warns: use LEAPS diagonals only when comfortable with a neutral posture, not as a leveraged bullish bet.

McMillan's worked example for the LEAPS stock substitute makes the economics explicit. Suppose the stock trades at $50, and a two-year LEAPS call at the $40 strike costs $12. Instead of buying 100 shares for $5,000, buy one LEAPS for $1,200 and invest the remaining $3,800 in 5% Treasury bills. The T-bill earns $190 in interest over the period. The costs of using LEAPS instead of stock: $200 in time value paid (the amount the LEAPS is above intrinsic value) plus $50 in foregone dividends equals $250 — offset by the $190 interest, leaving a net cost of about $60 for two years of controlled upside exposure with a defined $1,200 maximum loss instead of a potential $5,000 loss. For volatile stocks, this risk reduction justifies the net cost.

Chapter 26: Straddle Selling

This chapter revisits naked straddle selling with more emphasis on the selection criteria and follow-up mechanics that separate successful straddle sellers from those who suffer occasional catastrophic losses. The core premise remains unchanged: sell at-the-money calls and puts simultaneously, collect the combined premium, and profit from the stock staying near the strike as time decay erodes both options toward zero.

McMillan identifies the best conditions for straddle selling: implied volatility must be high on a historical basis (options genuinely expensive, not just nominally high), the stock must be expected to remain in a range over the selling horizon, and the trader must have specific, pre-committed follow-up procedures for if the stock moves to either break-even point. Absent these conditions — and especially without the follow-up discipline — straddle selling is speculation on the stock staying flat, dressed in the language of premium collection.

The order of preference for underlying assets in naked straddle selling matters. McMillan recommends index options first (broad-based indices cannot gap open 30% on a takeover bid the way an individual stock can), futures options second, and individual equity options last. The 1987 stock market crash — when major indices opened down more than 20 points — represents the worst-case tail risk for index straddle sellers. But for individual stocks, the equivalent risk (a takeover announcement, a massive earnings miss) is far more common. Straddle sellers on individual equities must accept a realistic possibility of waking up to a 40% gap against them.

Short-term options are emphatically preferred for straddle selling over LEAPS. The entire edge in straddle selling comes from time decay, and LEAPS decay too slowly to make the strategy worthwhile. A straddle seller needs the theta working hard immediately — the final 30 days of a near-term option's life produce the most decay per day. LEAPS straddles also give the stock years to wander into the loss zone, with no mechanism to recover the lost premium from the long passage of time.

The mechanics of naked straddle selling are identical to the covered straddle, but without stock ownership. Sell an at-the-money call and an at-the-money put simultaneously. Premium collected is the maximum profit, achieved when the stock closes exactly at the strike at expiration. The break-even range extends from (strike minus total premium) on the downside to (strike plus total premium) on the upside. Outside those levels, losses grow without limit in either direction — the structure is a short volatility bet with unlimited risk.

McMillan's worked example for index straddle selling on OEX provides specific probability guidance. With OEX at 650, a September straddle might sell the 550 put and 750 call, collecting a combined premium. The 550 put has roughly 11% probability of being reached; the 750 call has roughly 17% probability. Using 15% of the higher strike as required margin buffer ($11,250) creates a safety margin that allows for defensive action before margin calls occur. Expected return of approximately 2.8% for a five-week period should never be annualized — the conditions that made the trade attractive may not repeat, and annualizing creates a false sense of certainty.

Follow-up when the stock drifts toward one break-even requires immediate action rather than hope. McMillan's framework: when the stock reaches the break-even price, the position has reached its maximum acceptable loss level. If the stock rallies to the upside break-even, buy stock to delta-hedge the short call (creates a covered call); if the stock falls to the downside break-even, sell stock short to delta-hedge the short put. Each hedge eliminates one side's unlimited risk while retaining the opposing side's premium profit potential. The danger McMillan specifically warns against is the whipsaw — the stock hits the upside, the writer buys stock, then the stock reverses to the downside and the writer sells stock at a lower price than the hedge purchase, creating double losses on each reversal cycle.

LEAPS straddle selling is explicitly recommended against by McMillan, even though the premium collected is much larger. The problem is mathematics: time decay on a two-year straddle is glacially slow, providing almost no theta income in the early months. Meanwhile, the position is continuously exposed to vega risk — a spike in implied volatility inflates the straddle value, creating large losses. The slow theta and high vega of LEAPS options invert the favorable tradeoff that makes short-term straddle selling attractive. Short-term straddle writers profit from rapid time decay; LEAPS straddle writers have almost nothing working in their favor for months.

Chapter 27: Arbitrage

Arbitrage in options markets exploits pricing discrepancies between related instruments to create risk-free profits. In practice, most arbitrage opportunities are captured instantly by professional market makers with low transaction costs — individual traders rarely see clean arbitrage. But understanding the mechanics is essential because arbitrage forces maintain the pricing relationships that underlie all options theory.

Basic call discount arbitrage occurs when a deeply in-the-money call trades at a discount — below its intrinsic value. The solution: buy the call, sell the stock short, and immediately exercise the call to cover the short. The profit equals the discount, risk-free. This arises near expiration or just before ex-dividend dates when the mechanics of option pricing temporarily break down. Market makers correct it within seconds.

Conversions and reversals are the workhorse arbitrage trades of options market makers. A conversion combines long stock, long put, and short call at the same strike and expiration. If the net cost is less than the strike price (adjusted for carrying cost), the trade is profitable regardless of where the stock ends up. A reversal is the mirror image: short stock, long call, short put. These positions extract the pricing error between the three instruments and lock in a defined profit.

Box spreads combine a bull call spread and a bear put spread at the same two strikes. At expiration, a box spread is always worth exactly the strike difference — regardless of where the stock trades. If the box can be established for less than the strike difference (adjusted for carrying cost), the trade is a pure arbitrage. Box spreads are essentially interest rate trades: the "locked-in" return is the difference between the strike spread and the cost, annualized. Market makers use them to borrow or lend at specific implied rates.

Dividend arbitrage uses options to capture dividends without stock price risk. Buy the stock, buy a put at the same strike as the expected ex-dividend price decline, and collect the dividend. The put protects against any decline beyond the dividend. This only works when the dividend exceeds the cost of the put plus carrying costs — a condition that rarely holds in efficient markets but occasionally appears with unusually large special dividends.

The risks that afflict supposedly risk-free arbitrage are more numerous than they appear. Early assignment on the short option unwinds one leg of a conversion at an unpredictable time. A declared extra dividend destroys the economics of a reversal (the short stock holder must pay the dividend, which was not in the original calculation). Interest rate changes during the life of the position shift the carrying cost and alter the profitability of box spreads. McMillan presents arbitrage as a discipline with its own embedded risks — invisible in theory, costly in practice.

McMillan's explanation of the interest play illuminates why interest rates affect option prices. An investor shorts stock and simultaneously buys an in-the-money call slightly above parity. The short stock generates a large credit balance in the brokerage account, and that credit earns interest. The cost of the position is the time premium in the call — the small amount the call costs above intrinsic value. If interest earned on the credit balance exceeds the time premium paid for the call, the position generates a riskless profit. This arbitrage is what forces call prices higher when interest rates rise: as the credit balance earns more, the call's time premium can be bid up without the position becoming unprofitable for the arbitrageur.

Box spreads deserve special attention because they appear more complex than they are. Buy a call bull spread (buy lower strike call, sell upper strike call) and simultaneously buy a put bear spread (buy upper strike put, sell lower strike put) at the same strikes. At expiration, exactly one of the four options will be in-the-money by the full strike difference, and the net value is always the strike difference — no matter where the stock closes. If this combination costs less than the present value of the strike difference (discounting at the risk-free rate), the box is an arbitrage. Professional arbitrageurs actively scan for mispricings in box spreads, which is why they trade very close to their theoretical value in liquid markets.

Chapter 28: Black-Scholes and Volatility

The Black-Scholes option pricing formula, published in 1973 by Fischer Black and Myron Scholes (with key contributions from Robert Merton), transformed options from speculative instruments into scientifically priceable securities. The formula calculates a call price as: C = S × N(d₁) − X × e^(−rT) × N(d₂), where S is the stock price, X is the strike, r is the risk-free rate, T is time in years, and N() is the cumulative normal distribution function. Delta equals N(d₁) — a direct output of the formula, not an approximation.

Of the five inputs to Black-Scholes — stock price, strike price, time to expiration, interest rate, and volatility — only volatility is not directly observable. Every other input is known. This makes volatility the central uncertainty in option pricing and the driver of all sophisticated options analysis. McMillan devotes considerable attention to measuring it accurately.

Historical volatility is calculated from the stock's actual price history. The process: take the daily logarithmic price relatives (ln(P_t / P_{t-1})), compute their standard deviation, and annualize by multiplying by √260 (trading days in a year). Common lookback windows are 10 days (recent regime), 20 days (standard), 50 days (medium-term), and 100 days (long-term). Each window captures a different aspect of the stock's behavior, and sophisticated traders compare all of them.

Implied volatility runs Black-Scholes in reverse: given the current market price of an option, what volatility makes the model price equal to the market price? This iterative process yields the implied volatility for each option. Because different options on the same stock can have different implied volatilities, McMillan recommends computing a composite IV by weighting individual options by their daily volume and proximity to the at-the-money strike, then smoothing the result with a 20-day moving average.

The comparison of implied volatility to historical volatility is McMillan's primary trading signal. When IV significantly exceeds HV, options are expensive — the market is paying for more volatility than the stock has recently exhibited. Selling strategies (straddles, ratio writes, covered writes) have a mathematical edge. When IV is significantly below HV, options are cheap — buying strategies (straddles, backspreads, long calls or puts) have the edge. This IV-versus-HV comparison is the most practical tool for identifying when volatility strategies have genuine expected value.

The probability calculation derived from Black-Scholes allows precise risk estimation for any strategy. The probability that a stock ends below a price K at time T is N[ln(K/S) / (v × √T)]. For a stock at $50 with 30% volatility and 3 months remaining, the probability of ending below $45 is approximately 23%. This calculation lets the trader assess whether a particular strategy's break-even points are realistic given the stock's actual volatility profile.

"Volatility is the single most important concept in options pricing. If you cannot assess volatility accurately, you cannot evaluate whether options are overpriced or underpriced." — Lawrence G. McMillan

Gamma, the second derivative of the option price with respect to stock price, measures how rapidly delta changes. An at-the-money short-term option has the highest gamma — its delta can swing from 0.25 to 0.75 on a two-point stock move near expiration. For a delta-neutral ratio spread short 10,000 options with a position gamma of −600, a two-point adverse stock move doesn't just create a delta mismatch — it creates an instantaneous loss of approximately $1,200 from gamma effect alone, even before the delta loss is counted. Understanding position gamma is what separates traders who know their total risk from those who only measure the directional exposure.

Vega measures the option's sensitivity to a one-percentage-point change in implied volatility. A calendar spread has positive vega — rising implied volatility benefits it, because the longer-term option's value increases more than the shorter-term option's. A ratio write has negative vega — rising implied volatility increases the value of the written options more than the owned stock, creating losses. Position vega is the most asymmetric risk for short-volatility strategies: a 5% spike in implied volatility on a position with vega of −$3,600 per 1% creates an instant $18,000 loss regardless of where the stock is. McMillan emphasizes that traders must calculate position vega before entering any multi-leg strategy.

Chapter 29: Stock Index Options and Futures

Stock index options differ from equity options in three fundamental ways: they settle in cash (no stock delivery), many are European-style (exercisable only at expiration), and position limits are substantially larger. These differences create unique opportunities and unique risks that experienced equity options traders must relearn when moving to index products.

The two most important index products McMillan discusses are OEX (options on the S&P 100 index) and SPX (options on the S&P 500). OEX options are American-style — exercisable at any time — and highly liquid, making them the preferred vehicle for short-term hedging. SPX options are European-style, with settlement at Friday's opening prices (not Thursday's close). This settlement quirk creates settlement risk: a trader holding SPX options overnight into settlement can be surprised by a large gap between Thursday's close and Friday's opening index value.

Portfolio hedging with index puts is the most common institutional use of index options. The formula for calculating the number of puts needed: Puts = (Portfolio Value × Portfolio Beta) / (Index Level × $100). A $1 million portfolio with a beta of 1.2 and the index at 450 requires approximately 27 puts. The hedge is imperfect — individual stocks in the portfolio may not move in exact proportion to the index — but it protects against broad market declines without requiring the sale of individual positions.

Index options eliminate the early exercise risk that plagues equity option writers. Because index options settle in cash, there is no benefit to exercising a call early to capture a dividend (indices don't pay dividends in the same way individual stocks do). This makes covered call writing on an index more predictable than covered writing on individual stocks, where dividend timing can trigger unexpected early assignment.

The volatility skew in index options is systematic and persistent. Out-of-the-money index puts consistently trade at higher implied volatility than at-the-money index puts, which trade higher than out-of-the-money calls. This reverse skew began after the 1987 stock market crash, when institutional demand for downside protection drove put premiums permanently higher. McMillan identifies this as both a risk (put buyers overpay for insurance) and an opportunity (creative spread structures can exploit the pricing discrepancy).

The portfolio hedging formula requires three inputs that must be calculated carefully. Portfolio value divided by (index level × $100 per point) gives the base number of index options without regard to beta. Multiplying by the portfolio's beta adjusts for the fact that a high-beta growth portfolio moves more than the index per percentage point. A $1 million portfolio with beta 1.2 against an S&P 500 index at 450 requires (1,000,000 × 1.2) / (450 × 100) = 26.7, rounded to 27 put options, to fully hedge market-related risk. The hedge does not protect against individual stock risk that is not correlated with the index — tracking error remains.

The early exercise elimination in cash-settled index options creates a specific advantage for call writers. On equity options, the call writer faces early assignment risk any time the option is deep in-the-money near an ex-dividend date — the holder may exercise to capture the dividend. On cash-settled index options, there is no stock to own and no dividend to capture, so early exercise is never economically rational. This makes index covered writes and naked index call writes structurally more predictable than their equity counterparts. McMillan notes this as one of the primary reasons professional traders prefer index options for systematic premium collection strategies.

The SPX settlement risk is a specific hazard unique to S&P 500 options. SPX options expire based on Friday's opening prices — the prices at which stocks actually open for trading Friday morning, not Thursday's close. A trader holding SPX options through expiration stops trading Thursday at 4:15 pm but doesn't know the final settlement value until Friday morning's opening prices are published. The gap between Thursday's close and Friday's open can be several points in either direction. McMillan's recommendation: avoid holding SPX positions through expiration unless the position is so deep in- or out-of-the-money that no reasonable overnight gap can affect the outcome.

Chapter 30: Stock Index Hedging

Hedging a stock portfolio with index options requires careful calculation and constant monitoring. The basic framework: determine the portfolio's beta, multiply by the portfolio's dollar value, and divide by the dollar value of one index option contract (index level × $100 multiplier). The result is the number of puts needed for a complete hedge.

A complete hedge eliminates all market-related movement from the portfolio — both up and down. For most investors, a complete hedge is not the goal. An investor who wants to reduce portfolio risk by 50% buys half the calculated number of puts. One who wants catastrophic protection only — covering losses beyond 10% — buys out-of-the-money puts at the 10%-below-current-price strike rather than at-the-money puts.

LEAPS puts offer particularly attractive hedging economics. A LEAPS put with two years to expiration on the S&P 500 provides insurance over a long horizon at a cost that is much lower per day than rolling short-term puts. The annualized cost of long-term portfolio protection is typically lower using LEAPS than using rolling 3-month puts, because the time value of LEAPS is large in absolute terms but small relative to the protection provided.

Overwriting — selling index calls against a long portfolio — converts equity exposure to a capped-upside, cushioned-downside position. The call premium received provides a buffer against moderate declines. The upside is capped at the call strike. This strategy is particularly popular among institutional portfolio managers who want to generate income on a portfolio they cannot easily reduce in size.

Tracking error between the portfolio and the hedging index is the primary practical challenge. A portfolio of 30 technology stocks will not move in perfect lockstep with the S&P 500. If the market falls 10% but the portfolio falls 15% because of sector-specific risk, the index hedge covers only $10 of the $15 loss. McMillan recommends using beta-adjusted hedges and accepting that some residual risk will always remain with any index-based hedge on a non-index portfolio.

The four-step process for calculating futures-based hedges parallels the index option calculation but uses futures pricing. Step one: determine the portfolio's beta against the relevant index. Step two: multiply beta by portfolio value to get beta-adjusted portfolio value. Step three: divide by the futures price multiplied by the contract multiplier ($250 for S&P 500 futures, $100 for many index options contracts). Step four: round to the nearest whole contract. A $5 million portfolio with beta 1.15 against S&P 500 futures at 460 requires (5,000,000 × 1.15) / (460 × 250) = 50 contracts.

LEAPS puts for portfolio protection have a specific cost-benefit profile that McMillan calculates explicitly. A two-year LEAPS put on the S&P 500 loses time value slowly — approximately 0.12-0.14% per day — meaning a $5 put on the index costs roughly $0.006 per day in theta. Over two years, the total time value paid is the put's premium minus any intrinsic value at purchase. In exchange, the portfolio is protected from market crashes for two years with no need to roll monthly. McMillan compares this to monthly OTM put purchases rolled repeatedly — the rolling approach often costs more in transaction costs and is subject to the timing risk that a crash occurs in the first week of an expensive new position.

Overwriting — selling index calls against a long portfolio — reduces the portfolio's cost basis by the premium collected but creates a ceiling on participation if the market rallies. McMillan's nuanced point is that overwriting works best on diversified index-like portfolios where the correlation to the index is high. On concentrated portfolios with idiosyncratic individual stock exposure, selling index calls against a portfolio that doesn't perfectly track the index creates a mismatch — the portfolio might rally 20% on a single stock while the index gains only 5%, leaving the writer stuck under the index call cap while the stock surges.

Chapter 31: Index Spreading

Index spreading applies all the equity spread strategies — bull spreads, bear spreads, calendar spreads, ratio spreads — to index options. Most of the mechanics transfer directly. The key differences are execution-related: index options often have wider bid-ask spreads than equity options, making limit orders essential and slippage a meaningful cost factor for multi-leg positions.

Calendar spreads on index options face the specific challenge of different settlement mechanisms between months. Near-term index options may settle at a Friday opening value based on Thursday's close, while the longer-term options continue trading. A calendar spread that appears delta-neutral based on closing prices can suddenly have significant gap exposure as settlement approaches. Experienced index traders close calendar positions before settlement rather than holding through the settlement calculation.

Ratio put spreads are particularly attractive in index options because of the persistent reverse volatility skew. In a ratio put spread — buy one at-the-money put, sell two further out-of-the-money puts — the sold options (OTM puts) are overpriced relative to the bought option (ATM put) because of the skew. This creates a structural edge: the spread collects more premium than a fair-value analysis would justify, improving the expected return for the strategy even before considering any directional view on the market.

Bull spreads on index options follow the same structure as equity bull spreads but with cash settlement mechanics. Buy the lower strike index call, sell the higher strike call, pay a net debit. Maximum profit if the index closes above the higher strike at expiration equals the strike difference minus the debit paid. Maximum loss if the index closes below the lower strike equals the debit paid. The cash settlement means no delivery of the underlying index portfolio — profit or loss is simply credited or debited to the account based on the settlement price. McMillan notes that the wide bid-ask spreads in index options make limit orders essential — market orders often give away $0.50–$1.00 per contract unnecessarily.

Bear spreads on indices are particularly useful for hedging a specific short-term risk, such as an expected market reaction to a Federal Reserve announcement or economic data release. A bear call spread or bear put spread defines both the maximum profit and maximum cost of the hedge. Unlike buying outright index puts — where the full premium is at risk if the market doesn't move — the spread caps the cost while retaining the directional exposure needed. The shorter the time to expiration and the smaller the expected move, the more efficiently a spread captures the hedging intent.

Calendar spreads on index options face the specific challenge that different expiration months may settle differently. Near-term SPX options settle on opening prices; longer-dated options settle on closing prices. This creates a subtle mismatch when using a calendar spread: the short near-term option's final value may be determined by an entirely different settlement mechanism than the long longer-term option. McMillan recommends using options from the same settlement class whenever possible and avoiding holding index calendar spreads through the near-term expiration without accounting for this settlement divergence.

Ratio put spreads in index options are McMillan's preferred exploitation of the reverse volatility skew. Buy one at-the-money put, sell two out-of-the-money puts for a credit (or small debit). The sold OTM puts carry higher implied volatility than the ATM put — the skew works in the ratio spread's favor. At expiration, if the index closes between the two strikes, both puts are in-the-money but the net profit at the lower strike is maximized. If the index stays above the ATM strike, all puts expire worthless and the credit is kept. Only a significant market crash — index falling below the downside break-even — creates losses.

McMillan's practical summary for index spreading emphasizes position sizing above all else. Index options represent large notional values — a single SPX option at index level 4,000 controls a $400,000 notional exposure. Even a conservative ratio spread of modest size can represent several hundred thousand dollars in underlying exposure. Traders comfortable with 10 equity option contracts regularly often underestimate the scale of index positions. McMillan's guidance: start with far smaller index positions than feel comfortable, understand the full notional exposure, and scale up only after experiencing the volatility of index moves firsthand.

Chapter 32: Structured Products and PERCS

Structured products are securities manufactured by financial institutions that embed option positions within a bond-like wrapper. The archetype is the principal-protected note: a zero-coupon bond combined with a call option. The zero-coupon bond guarantees the return of principal at maturity; the call option provides upside participation in an equity index up to a cap. The investor gives up interest income to fund the option premium.

Understanding structured products requires reversing the manufacturing process. The institution buys a zero-coupon bond (perhaps at 65 cents on the dollar for a five-year note) and uses the remaining 35 cents to buy a call option on the equity index. The option is capped — the investor participates only up to some maximum return — because the institution must also cover its manufacturing costs and profit margin. The adjustment factor is the percentage of upside the investor retains.

Structured products are most efficient when implied volatility is low — the call option component is cheap, so the institution can provide better participation rates for the same cost to the investor. When IV is high, the call is expensive and the participation rate or cap must be reduced. Investors buying structured products in high-volatility environments are paying the most for the least upside participation.

PERCS (Preferred Equity Redemption Cumulative Stock) are a transparent form of structured product: they pay a higher dividend than common stock in exchange for a cap on price appreciation. McMillan demonstrates that a PERCS is mathematically identical to a covered call write — owning the stock and selling an out-of-the-money call. The higher dividend compensates for the capped upside, and the risk profile (profit when flat or modestly up, lose when stock declines) is exactly the same as a covered write. Analyzing a PERCS as a covered write allows the investor to determine whether the premium is fair.

Understanding structured products starts with the manufacturing process working in reverse. The institution assembles a zero-coupon bond priced to return $1,000 at maturity — bought today for approximately $600–$700 at prevailing interest rates — and uses the remaining $300–$400 to buy a call option on the underlying index. The call is the upside participation element; the bond is the principal protection. The investor sees a product that says "100% principal protection plus 80% of the index's gain up to 25%," but the economic reality is a zero-coupon bond combined with an at-the-money call option with a cap. The cap is necessary because the remaining premium budget only buys a call with a limited upside feature.

The break-even formula for structured products is specific. If the product participates in 80% of the index's gains up to 25%, the investor reaches break-even when the index is up 6.25% (0.80 × 6.25% = 5%). Actually, the break-even equals the adjustment factor — the percentage of upside given up — divided by the participation rate: (strike / (1 − adjustment factor)). McMillan's emphasis: structured products are most attractive when implied volatility is low, because the call option component is cheap relative to normal pricing, so the institution can offer a higher participation rate or higher cap for the same premium budget. In high-volatility environments, calls are expensive and the participation rate or cap must be cut, making the product less attractive.

PERCS — Preferred Equity Redemption Cumulative Stock — are the most transparent structured vehicle because their construction maps directly to options terminology. The PERCS investor owns the equivalent of stock plus a short OTM call: they collect a higher dividend than common stockholders (the call premium, in effect) but are capped in their upside at the call-away price set at issuance. If the stock closes above the PERCS cap price at redemption, the holder receives the cap price, not the higher stock price — they have been "called away" just like a covered writer. McMillan's evaluation framework: strip out the imbedded option, price it using Black-Scholes, and compare the implied dividend yield to the actual enhanced dividend received.

McMillan's broader lesson from structured products is about investor behavior under uncertainty. Principal-protected notes flourish because investors systematically undervalue the opportunity cost of the interest income they sacrifice for protection. A 5% risk-free rate means $1,000 invested in T-bills for five years grows to approximately $1,276. The structured product that "protects" that $1,000 is actually guaranteeing a negative real return compared to the T-bill alternative, while delivering the illusion of safety. The investor who would accept a 0% nominal return to avoid any paper loss is paying for protection that costs far more than they realize.

Chapter 33: Mathematical Considerations

The standard Black-Scholes model assumes that stock returns follow a lognormal distribution — stock prices make smooth, continuous moves, and extreme moves are exponentially rare. Reality is dramatically different. McMillan's analysis of actual stock price data finds that moves beyond ±4 standard deviations occur 12 to 20 times more frequently than the lognormal model predicts. At ±3 standard deviations, actual frequency is about 5 times the model's expectation. This fat tail phenomenon fundamentally alters the expected return of every options strategy.

The implications by strategy are specific and important. Option buyers — straddle buyers, call buyers, put buyers — have better expected returns than Black-Scholes suggests because the fat tails produce big payoffs that the model underestimates. Covered writers have expected returns roughly equal to stock ownership (not superior, as naive analysis suggests) because fat tail events destroy premiums at exactly the wrong moment. Bull spreads have slightly worse expected returns than stock ownership because the cap prevents capturing the full upside of fat tail rallies. Ratio spreads and naked straddle sales are the most dangerous — their unlimited-loss positions in the fat tail region generate catastrophic losses precisely when the model says such losses are nearly impossible.

Probability calculations from Black-Scholes can be adapted for practical use. The probability that a stock ends below price K at time T is approximately N[ln(K/S) / (v × √T)]. But traders often want to know the probability that the stock ever touches a target price during the life of the option — not just where it ends up. The "ever touches" probability is meaningfully higher than the endpoint probability, because the stock can touch a price and then recover. For stop-loss planning and barrier analysis, the ever-touches probability is the relevant number.

Monte Carlo simulation with fat-tailed distributions is the most accurate tool for complex positions. Rather than using the analytical Black-Scholes formula, the trader simulates 10,000 or more random price paths drawn from a fat-tailed distribution (not the normal distribution Black-Scholes assumes), counts profitable outcomes, and computes empirical probability of profit. This is computationally intensive but captures the real-world tail risk that analytical models systematically underestimate.

McMillan's examination of the fat tail problem in options pricing starts with empirical observation, not theory. When researchers analyzed decades of stock price data, they found that moves beyond two standard deviations occurred approximately as often as the normal distribution predicts. But at three and four standard deviations, actual moves were five to twenty times more frequent than the lognormal model expects. This divergence is not a rounding error or a statistical artifact — it persists across different markets, time periods, and measurement methods. Black-Scholes-based risk models consistently underestimate the probability of tail events.

The strategy implications follow directly. Covered call writing appears superior to stock ownership in a Black-Scholes world because the premium collected offsets small declines — but under a fat-tailed distribution, occasional catastrophic drops destroy the accumulated premium and then some. McMillan's calculation shows that covered writing and stock ownership have nearly identical expected returns under fat tails, not the clear edge for covered writing that the normal model suggests. Option buyers, conversely, actually benefit from fat tails because the large moves that the model underprices are precisely what option buyers profit from.

The "probability of ever touching" formula is the most practically useful output of this chapter. Unlike the endpoint probability — the chance the stock closes below a specific price at expiration — the ever-touching probability captures the chance the stock reaches that level at any point during the option's life. For stop-loss placement, break-even analysis, and knock-out option pricing, the ever-touching probability is more relevant than the endpoint probability. A put purchased to hedge against a 10% stock decline should be evaluated on the probability that the stock touches the put's break-even at some point before expiration, not just at the final day — and that probability is substantially higher than the endpoint figure.

Monte Carlo simulation with fat-tailed distributions is the gold standard for evaluating complex multi-leg positions. Instead of assuming a smooth lognormal path, the simulation draws random returns from a fat-tailed distribution, runs thousands of price paths, and counts how often the position is profitable. The output is a probability distribution of outcomes rather than a single expected value — it shows not just the average result but the range of results and the frequency of extreme outcomes. McMillan recommends Monte Carlo as the primary tool for any position with non-linear payoffs — ratio spreads, butterflies, calendar combinations — where the analytical formulas produce misleading expected values under lognormal assumptions.

Chapter 34: Futures and Futures Options

Futures contracts are standardized agreements to buy or sell an asset at a future date at a price agreed upon today. The futures price for a stock index is not the market's prediction of where the index will be — it is calculated mechanically by the cost-of-carry formula: Futures Fair Value = Index × [1 + Time × (Risk-free Rate − Dividend Yield)]. When futures trade above fair value, arbitrageurs buy the index basket and sell futures. When below, they sell the basket and buy futures. This program trading keeps futures perpetually near fair value.

The implied futures price can be extracted from options when futures are limit-locked (trading halted at a circuit-breaker limit). Using put-call parity: Implied Futures = Strike + Call − Put. This formula was extensively used during the 1987 crash, when futures markets were locked at limit down but options continued trading. Sophisticated traders used the implied futures price to assess the true state of the equity market when price discovery in the futures market had broken down.

The Black model for pricing options on futures adjusts Black-Scholes for the fact that futures require no upfront investment. The result: Call = e^(−rT) × BSM[r=0%]. This discounted version of the formula acknowledges that futures positions use only margin rather than full capital. An important consequence: put-call parity holds exactly for futures options — calls and puts at the same strike on the same futures contract have exactly the same implied volatility. Skew in futures options is measured differently from equity option skew.

SPAN margin (Standardized Portfolio Analysis of Risk) is the CME's portfolio-based margin system. Rather than margining each position independently, SPAN runs 16 test scenarios — seven price moves combined with two volatility changes, plus two extreme scenarios — and sets the margin requirement equal to the largest projected loss across all tests. This allows hedged positions (a long call against a short call, for example) to carry lower margin than each position alone, recognizing that the positions offset each other's risk.

Currency options add a further dimension: the underlying asset is a foreign currency, and its pricing involves interest rate differentials between two countries rather than just one risk-free rate. Options on currency futures (trading on the CME) and options on actual spot currency (trading on the Philadelphia Exchange) share the same strategies but require adjusting for the two-country interest rate framework. The volatility skew in currency markets often differs from equity skew, creating different spread opportunities.

The futures fair value formula is the foundation for program trading and index arbitrage. In simple form: futures fair value = index level × (1 + rate × time) minus the present value of dividends expected before expiration. When futures trade above fair value — say, fair value is 450.00 but futures trade at 451.50 — program traders simultaneously buy the stocks in the index and sell futures, locking in the difference as profit. When futures trade below fair value, the trade is reversed. The difference between the actual futures price and fair value — called the "basis" — is published in real time by financial data services and watched closely by arbitrageurs.

The implied futures price calculation from options is essential during limit-locked markets. When S&P 500 futures hit a circuit breaker and stop trading, the options market continues. Using the put-call parity relationship: implied futures price = strike + call price − put price. If the October 450 call is trading at $8.00 and the October 450 put is trading at $12.00, the implied futures price is 450 + 8 − 12 = $446. This tells traders the options market's consensus on where futures would trade if the halt ended — a critical signal during fast-moving markets.

Currency options require one additional pricing input: the interest rate differential between the two countries. A dollar-denominated investor holding British pound calls is effectively receiving interest on the pounds (the foreign rate) while paying interest on the dollars used to buy them (the domestic rate). The Black-Scholes model for currency options substitutes the interest rate differential (domestic minus foreign rate) for the simple risk-free rate used in equity options. When the foreign rate is higher than the domestic rate, currency calls are cheaper than the domestic interest-rate formula suggests; when the foreign rate is lower, calls are more expensive. This adds complexity to currency option strategy selection that doesn't exist in equity or index options.

Chapter 35: Futures Option Strategies

Volatility skew in futures markets creates strategy opportunities that are structurally different from equity options. Two forms of skew appear: reverse skew (like equity index options — out-of-the-money puts expensive, calls cheap) and forward skew (commodity markets — out-of-the-money calls expensive, puts cheap). Gold, silver, soybeans, coffee, and sugar typically show forward skew, driven by fear of supply shortages pushing prices to extreme upside levels. Each skew type calls for a different strategy.

In reverse-skewed markets, ratio put spreads exploit the overpriced OTM puts: buy one ATM put, sell two further OTM puts. The sold options carry inflated implied volatility, generating more premium than their fair share of risk warrants. In forward-skewed commodity markets, ratio call spreads are the analogous play: buy one ATM call, sell two further OTM calls. The structural premium in the OTM calls provides edge to the ratio seller.

Backspreads exploit the opposite condition. When skew is present but the trader expects it to disappear (or expects a large move), the backspread positions long the cheaper options and short the expensive ones in more-to-less ratios. In reverse-skewed markets, a call backspread (sell 1 ATM call, buy 2 OTM calls) is long the cheap OTM calls and short the expensive ATM call. If a large rally occurs, the two OTM calls gain enormously; if the market stays flat, only a modest premium is lost.

Intermarket spreads using options on related futures — T-bonds versus T-notes, crude oil versus heating oil — allow simultaneous positions in two markets. The ratio for intermarket spreading is calculated using the volatilities, prices, and units of both markets: Ratio = (v₁ × P₁ × u₁ × Δ₁) / (v₂ × P₂ × u₂ × Δ₂). In-the-money options are preferred for intermarket spreads because their higher delta reduces slippage and makes the ratio calculation more stable.

McMillan's summary of futures option strategy selection: identify the skew structure first, then match the strategy to the skew. If OTM options are expensive relative to ATM (whether calls or puts), sell the expensive OTM options. If they are cheap, buy them. The skew tells you which direction the IV structure favors, and the strategy should run with that structure rather than against it.

The forward skew in commodity futures markets — where OTM calls are more expensive than OTM puts — creates the mirror-image of the index options opportunity. In gold, coffee, sugar, and soybean markets, buyers have historically paid up for OTM calls because the fear is a supply shock driving prices sharply higher, not a demand collapse driving them lower. The excess implied volatility in OTM commodity calls is the structural mispricing that ratio call spreads and put backspreads are designed to exploit. McMillan's rule: identify the skew direction first, then select the strategy that sells the expensive options and buys the cheap ones, not the reverse.

Intermarket options spreads — using options on two related futures contracts to express a relative value view — require the intermarket spread ratio formula. The ratio accounts for different contract sizes, different prices, different deltas, and the correlation between the two contracts. The formula: ratio = (value per point × price × units × delta) of the first contract divided by the same product for the second contract. A spread between crude oil and heating oil contracts, for example, requires balancing the different contract sizes (1,000 barrels for crude, 42,000 gallons for heating oil converted to barrels) and the different current prices. Ignoring this calculation creates a position that looks spread but is actually directionally biased in ways the trader didn't intend.

The follow-up for skew-based strategies in futures options is driven by one signal: when the skew disappears. If a trader entered a ratio put spread in index futures because OTM puts were at a 10-percentage-point IV premium to ATM options, and that premium narrows to 3 points during the trade's life, the theoretical edge has been captured — take the profit even if time remains. Holding a skew trade past the point where the skew has normalized means the remaining profit potential is just directional exposure, which the trader wasn't trying to capture. McMillan's guideline: when the basis for entering the trade (the skew) no longer exists, exit the trade.

Chapter 36: The Basics of Volatility Trading

Volatility trading — taking positions based on whether options are expensive or cheap rather than based on direction — requires two separate analytical frameworks working together. The first is measuring volatility accurately enough to identify genuine mispricings. The second is selecting the right structural trade to express the view. Both are necessary; either alone is insufficient.

The percentile approach is McMillan's preferred measurement method. Plot the composite implied volatility of a stock over a rolling 600-day window. Rank all the daily readings from lowest to highest. When current IV is at the 85th percentile — higher than 85% of all readings over the past 2.5 years — options are expensive on a historical basis. When IV is at the 15th percentile, options are cheap. This avoids the mistake of comparing today's IV to a fixed threshold (like "above 30% means expensive") that ignores the fact that different stocks have very different normal volatility ranges.

Equally important is waiting for a trend reversal before entering. If IV is at the 90th percentile and still rising, do not sell volatility yet — buying additional premium is showing the market's resolve. Wait for IV to peak and begin declining before selling. Similarly, if IV is at the 10th percentile and still falling, wait for the bottom and a reversal before buying. Entering against a strong IV trend produces losses even when the eventual target is correct.

The implied deviation measures the skew magnitude — how spread out the individual option IVs are across strikes. Calculate the standard deviation of IVs across all strikes and months, divide by the average IV to normalize it. When this relative implied deviation exceeds 20–25%, the skew is large enough to warrant a spread strategy that exploits the discrepancy. Below that level, spread strategies based on skew may not generate enough premium to justify the transaction costs.

Once an IV extreme or skew is identified, the strategy selection follows the structure of the mispricing. High IV broadly → sell premium across strikes (straddles, strangles, ratio spreads). Low IV broadly → buy premium (straddles, backspreads, long calls or puts). Skew present → use spreads that are long the cheap options and short the expensive ones, regardless of the overall IV level. McMillan always applies all three criteria before entering a volatility trade.

The 600-day rolling window for the percentile calculation is specific and deliberate. Using too short a window — 60 or 90 days — means the percentile is dominated by recent readings and loses its comparative power. A stock that has traded volatile for three months looks "expensive" by a short-window percentile even if it has been dramatically more volatile in the past. The 600-day window, roughly 2.5 years of trading, captures enough of the stock's volatility history to give a meaningful sense of where current IV stands in the full range of behavior. McMillan's 20th and 80th percentile thresholds are not arbitrary — they represent the extremes where the mispricing is large enough to compensate for the transaction costs and timing risk of entering a volatility trade.

The implied deviation statistic — the spread of individual option IVs across all strikes — adds a skew dimension to the simple average IV level. If all options on a stock have implied volatility of exactly 30%, the implied deviation is zero: no skew exists. If OTM puts are at 40% IV and ATM calls are at 20% IV, the implied deviation is large: substantial skew exists that may be exploitable. McMillan's alert threshold of 20–25% relative implied deviation (divided by average IV) triggers a search for the appropriate skew-exploiting strategy — ratio spreads, backspreads, or calendar spreads depending on the direction of the skew.

The practical discipline of waiting for a trend reversal before entering a volatility trade is the hardest behavioral challenge McMillan describes. IV rarely moves from high to low in a straight line — it spikes, partially retreats, spikes again, and eventually trends down. Entering a short-volatility position when IV first hits the 80th percentile and is still rising means fighting momentum; the IV could easily reach 90th or 95th percentile before reversing, creating large losses on a theoretically correct trade. McMillan's discipline: confirm that IV has peaked and is now falling before selling volatility; confirm that IV has bottomed and is now rising before buying volatility. The entry is never at the extreme — it's after the reversal.

Chapter 37: Volatility Measurement Tools

The most common error in volatility measurement is comparing today's implied volatility to a static number — "IV above 30% means sell, below 20% means buy" — without reference to the stock's own history. A stock that normally trades at 50% implied volatility might have options that are cheap when IV is at 35%. A stock that normally trades at 15% IV might be expensive at 25%. Absolute thresholds are meaningless; relative historical positioning is everything.

McMillan's specific example of RAMBUS Inc. (RMBS) makes this vivid. RMBS's historical volatility ranged between 50% and 110% for years. A trader comparing current HV to past HV might see 60% as "moderate." But in February 2000, RMBS's implied volatility suddenly jumped above 120% and never returned to the old range. The company had entered a new volatility regime — patent litigation, massive growth, and then collapse — and the old 50–110% range was no longer relevant. Selling volatility based on the old historical range would have been catastrophic.

The fundamental check before any volatility trade is identifying whether the extreme IV has a rational explanation. High IV before an FDA drug approval decision is not mispricing — it reflects genuine uncertainty about a binary outcome. Low IV just before a company is acquired is not cheap options — it reflects the fact that the stock is about to go to a known price. McMillan insists: only trade volatility when there is no clear fundamental reason for the extreme reading. IV high because of an earnings report = do not sell. IV high for no discernible reason = potential sell.

Comparing IV to HV over multiple lookback windows provides additional confirmation. If 20-day HV is 25%, 50-day HV is 22%, and 100-day HV is 20%, and IV is at 38%, the discrepancy is large and consistent across all windows — a genuine signal. If 20-day HV is 36% (driven by recent unusual movement), 50-day is 22%, and IV is at 30%, the signal is murkier — the recent HV partially explains the elevated IV.

The RAMBUS Inc. example is McMillan's most vivid demonstration of the failure of static volatility thresholds. RAMBUS traded with historical volatility between 50% and 110% for years — a wide range but internally consistent. A system that said "sell IV above 80%" would have sold repeatedly and profitably throughout this period. Then in early 2000, RAMBUS became embroiled in litigation involving DRAM chip licensing fees — fundamental business risk, not just market risk. Historical volatility exploded above 120% and stayed there. A trader using the static "sell at 80%" rule continued to sell volatility into a genuinely new risk environment, suffering repeated losses as IV remained elevated for fundamental reasons not captured by any historical comparison.

The forward-looking fundamental check is McMillan's insurance against systematic volatility traps. Before any volatility sale, ask: Is there an ongoing litigation? A pending FDA decision? An acquisition offer that could be revised or withdrawn? A management scandal? These factors create durable elevated IV that will not revert to historical levels simply because the percentile reading is high. McMillan's approach is to require that no fundamental explanation exists for the elevated IV before entering — if you cannot explain why IV is high using only supply and demand for options (without invoking company-specific news), then the opportunity may be legitimate.

Comparing IV to HV across multiple lookback windows builds confidence in the signal. If 20-day HV is 25%, 50-day HV is 22%, and 100-day HV is 23%, but current IV is 45%, the signal is robust: options are expensive by any historical comparison. If 20-day HV is 45%, 50-day HV is 35%, and 100-day HV is 22%, and current IV is 45%, the picture is murkier — IV matches recent history even if it exceeds long-run history. McMillan emphasizes the 20-day HV as the most relevant short-term comparison because it captures what the stock has actually been doing recently, not what it did two years ago.

Reading a volatility chart requires understanding the concept of momentum in implied volatility. IV trends just like price: once it starts rising, it tends to continue rising for a period before topping. A volatility chart that shows IV at the 70th percentile and rising is a worse entry for a short-volatility trade than one showing IV at the 80th percentile but flat or declining. McMillan's rule — wait for a reversal before entering — is essentially a momentum rule applied to the volatility time series. The highest-probability entries for volatility selling are when IV has clearly peaked and started falling; for volatility buying, when IV has bottomed and started rising. Ignoring this timing and entering on the percentile reading alone is the single most common mistake McMillan observes among volatility traders.

Chapter 38: The Distribution of Stock Prices

The lognormal distribution that Black-Scholes assumes implies that stock price moves follow a bell curve shape on a logarithmic scale. Three-sigma events should occur about 0.3% of the time — roughly once every 320 trading days, or once every 1.5 years per stock. McMillan's empirical data shows actual three-sigma events occurring roughly 5 times more often than this. Four-sigma events — which the model says should appear about once in 30,000 years — appear in real markets every few hundred trading days. The model's tails are catastrophically thin.

This finding directly challenges the risk management systems that major financial institutions built on Black-Scholes-derived models. If the model says a five-sigma loss has a 1-in-3.5-million-year probability, but it actually occurs every decade or so, any firm sizing its risk exposure based on model probabilities is wildly over-leveraged. McMillan wrote this observation years before the 2008 financial crisis demonstrated it catastrophically, but the mathematical warning was present in every edition of this book.

For individual traders, the fat tail finding has specific strategy implications. Option sellers — especially sellers of far out-of-the-money options — face a permanent, hidden subsidy the model does not capture: the options they sell should be worth much more than Black-Scholes says, because extreme moves are far more likely. Conversely, buyers of far OTM options are systematically getting better value than the model acknowledges. McMillan translates this into a specific rule: do not sell naked options at strikes more than 3 standard deviations from the current price, even if they appear essentially worthless based on the model — they are not as worthless as they look.

The comparison between the "ever-touches" probability and the "ends-at" probability matters specifically for stop-loss planning. A stock might have a 15% probability of ending below $40 at a specific future date, but a 35% probability of touching $40 at some point during the next 90 days. A trader who places a stop-loss at $40 will be stopped out 35% of the time, even if only 15% of those stops represent situations where the stock truly moved against the thesis. Understanding both probabilities helps calibrate stop placement.

The empirical fat tail statistics are specific and striking. Using 20 years of stock market data, researchers found that daily moves beyond ±2σ occurred at roughly the frequency the normal distribution predicts. But beyond ±3σ, actual moves were approximately 5 times more frequent than lognormal. Beyond ±4σ, actual moves were 12 to 20 times more frequent than lognormal. This is not a small adjustment — a "once per thousand years" event in the normal model actually occurs several times per decade in real markets. The 1987 crash, the 1998 LTCM crisis, the 2008 financial collapse, and the 2020 pandemic selloff all represent multi-sigma events that the normal model would categorize as essentially impossible.

For ratio spread sellers — the strategy McMillan shows is most at risk from fat tails — the practical implication is severe. A ratio call spread that is theoretically profitable 85% of the time based on Black-Scholes models may actually lose on its short leg in tail events far more frequently than 15%. The unlimited loss scenarios that ratio spreads create are precisely the outcomes that fat tails amplify. McMillan's conclusion: use ratio spreads only with delta-neutral construction, position sizing that accounts for the larger-than- model tail risk, and pre-planned defensive actions at defined price levels.

The "ever-touches" formula has specific applications in risk management beyond pure probability analysis. For a stop-loss placed 10% below the current price, the relevant question is not "what is the probability the stock closes below that level at expiration?" but "what is the probability the stock hits that level at any point before expiration?" The ever-touches probability is always significantly higher than the endpoint probability — for a 10% decline in a 30-day window with 20% annualized volatility, the ever-touches probability might be 35% while the endpoint probability is only 12%. This gap matters enormously for stop-loss placement: trades that look like they have an 88% probability of success on an endpoint basis are actually stopped out 35% of the time if the stop is placed at the break-even.

McMillan's conclusion about the fat tail chapter is a measured one that neither dismisses Black-Scholes nor treats it as infallible. For most strategies in most conditions, the lognormal model provides useful approximations. The systematic exception is the rare but catastrophic tail event — the 4σ+ move that happens far more often than the model suggests. Traders who size positions as if the Black-Scholes model perfectly describes tail risk will be periodically wiped out by events that were, by the model's own logic, essentially impossible. The correct response is not to abandon the model but to add a fat-tail premium to any sizing calculation — assume the catastrophic scenario happens roughly 10× more often than the model predicts, and size accordingly.

Chapter 39: Volatility Trading Techniques

McMillan's three-step selection process for any volatility trade is the most actionable framework in the book. Step one: use ONE primary criterion — IV percentile at an extreme, or a large IV-versus-HV discrepancy, or an IV chart trend reversal. Any of these is sufficient to identify a candidate. Step two: calculate the probability of reaching the break-even prices of the proposed strategy. For buying volatility (straddles), the probability of the break-even being reached at least once should exceed 80%. For selling volatility (naked strangles), the probability of the break-even being reached should be below 25%. Step three: check the historical price histogram — has the stock actually made moves of the required size in this time frame before? If the distribution is clustered around small moves with no history of reaching the break-even distance, the trade is not well-supported regardless of what the IV analysis says.

For buying volatility when IV is low, the straddle is the primary vehicle. If the stock is close to the strike, buy an ATM straddle. If the stock sits between two strikes, buy the strangle (OTM call and OTM put). The ratio between calls and puts should be set delta-neutral: if the call has delta 0.60 and the put has delta 0.40, buy three puts for every two calls to create an initially neutral position. This prevents the straddle from having an unintended directional bias from the outset.

McMillan's worked example at 40% implied volatility with a stock at $39.60 illustrates the progression across expiration months. A January straddle costs $2.75 (break-evens $42.75 and $37.25); an April straddle costs $6.85; a July straddle costs $23.05 (using a 2:3 neutral ratio); and an October straddle $27 (2:3 ratio, break-evens $53.50 and $31.00). The mid-range expirations — July and October — typically offer the best probability of success, giving the stock enough time to reach the break-even while not suffering from the extremely slow time decay of LEAPS straddles.

For selling volatility when IV is high, index options are the preferred vehicle — broad-based indices cannot gap open on a takeover announcement. Sell an out-of-the-money strangle: a naked put below the market and a naked call above. McMillan's specific example: OEX at 650, sell September 550 puts (11% probability of reaching the strike) and September 750 calls (17% probability). Both legs satisfy the below-25% probability criterion. Expected return approximately 2.8% over the five-week holding period. Key: do not annualize this return mentally and then over-leverage — conditions in five weeks may be completely different.

The volatility backspread is the most complex sell-volatility strategy: sell one longer-term ATM option and buy two or more shorter-term OTM options. The position has negative vega (profits if IV falls) and limited risk (the short-term long options hedge the long-term short). Example: sell one October 120 call at $13, buy two July 130 calls at $2.50 each. Net credit: $8. Position vega: −0.07 per 1% IV change. If IV falls 10 points, the position gains $700 from vega alone. The critical rule: close before the near-term options (the hedges) expire. After July expiration, the position becomes a naked short October call with no protection.

Trading the volatility skew requires understanding which options are expensive and which are cheap within the same expiration. In reverse-skewed index markets, OTM puts are expensive and OTM calls are cheap. The correct trade is either a ratio put spread (sell the expensive OTM puts, buy fewer ATM puts) or a call backspread (sell the ATM call, buy more of the cheap OTM calls). The expected return is positive because the trade systematically shorts the overpriced options and longs the underpriced ones. McMillan's OEX example: buy 20 June 590 calls, sell 10 June 580 calls. As the OEX rises toward 600, the 590 calls gain more in IV than the 580 calls (the skew shifts upward with the market), creating a profit from IV differential in addition to delta.

McMillan's probability calculator serves as the final gate before any volatility trade. After identifying an IV extreme (step one), the trader calculates the strategy's break-even prices and runs those prices through the probability formula. For buying volatility, the calculation asks: what is the probability the stock reaches the break-even price at any point before expiration? McMillan's threshold is 80% — if the ever-touches probability is below 80%, the straddle or strangle is not attractive regardless of how cheap options appear. For selling volatility, the threshold runs the other direction: the probability that the stock reaches either short strike must be below 25%.

The historical price movement check — the third step in McMillan's three-step process — is often skipped and often critical. Using the break-even prices calculated from the strategy, the trader examines whether the stock has historically moved the required distance in the required time. If the straddle needs a 15% move in 60 days to break even, how often has this stock moved 15% in any 60-day period over the past five years? If the answer is "only twice, during the 2008 crash and the 2020 pandemic," the trade depends on a once-a-decade event recurring promptly — a poor probability assessment regardless of how low IV appears. If the answer is "eight times in the past five years," the historical distribution supports the trade.

Chapter 40: The Greeks

The Greeks are the unified language for describing how any options position — regardless of complexity — responds to changes in the underlying stock, the passage of time, and shifts in implied volatility. A single-leg covered call and a ten-leg volatility backspread can both be fully described using the same five numbers: delta, gamma, theta, vega, and rho. McMillan argues that no sophisticated trader should ever enter a position without knowing all five Greek values for the entire portfolio, not just for individual legs.

Delta at the position level is the Equivalent Stock Position — the total number of share equivalents the portfolio is long or short. A position with an ESP of +200 is long the equivalent of 200 shares; −200 is short 200 shares. A delta-neutral position has ESP of zero. But delta-neutrality is momentary — every move in the stock changes all the deltas in the portfolio, and yesterday's neutral position may be strongly directional today. McMillan's example: selling 100 straddles at-the-money produces a delta-neutral position (ESP ≈ 0), but with a position gamma of −600. A two-point adverse move creates a position delta of ±1,200 shares — the position has gone from flat to massively directional in a single move.

Gamma is the risk that deltas will shift. Negative gamma positions (short options of any kind) become increasingly directional on adverse moves — exactly when the trader least wants to be exposed. The loss formula for a short-gamma position on a two-point adverse move is: Total loss = 2 × |position delta| + |position gamma|. A position with delta 0 and gamma −600 loses $600 on a one-point adverse move and $1,800 on a two-point adverse move — the loss accelerates with the size of the move, not linearly. This non-linear loss profile is the defining characteristic of short-gamma positions and the source of their danger in fast markets.

Vega is the single most dangerous Greek for short premium strategies. McMillan's example: selling 100 straddles produces a position vega of −$3,600 per percentage point change in implied volatility. If IV rises 5 percentage points (not unusual in a market disruption), the position loses $18,000 from vega alone — independent of any stock price movement. The 1987 crash didn't just cause large stock moves; it caused massive spikes in implied volatility that destroyed the value of short premium positions even for traders who had managed their delta exposure. Vega risk is invisible in quiet markets and catastrophic in disruptions.

Theta is the only Greek that consistently works in the favor of short premium positions. Selling 100 straddles generates positive theta of approximately +$600 per day. This daily gain is what makes short premium strategies attractive in theory. The challenge: negative gamma means that any significant move creates losses that overwhelm the theta collected. The straddle seller is making $600 per day in theta while potentially losing $3,000 in a single day from delta and gamma combined. The strategy is profitable only when the stock sits quietly near the strike throughout the entire holding period.

Gamma-neutral construction allows a trader to build positions that are neutral to both delta and gamma at inception. The process: divide the gamma of one option by the gamma of another to find the gamma-neutral ratio, execute that ratio, then use stock or futures to neutralize the resulting delta. Example: the Oct 60 call has gamma 0.050 and the Oct 70 call has gamma 0.025. Buying 100 Oct 60 calls and selling 200 Oct 70 calls (2:1 ratio) creates a gamma-neutral position — but results in a long position of 1,000 equivalent shares. Shorting 1,000 actual shares neutralizes the delta. The result: a position that is neither gamma-short nor long-short at the current stock price.

Simultaneous equations allow targeting multiple Greek values at once. If a trader wants a position that is gamma-neutral AND has a vega of −$250 (to profit $250 per 1% IV decline), set up two equations: 0.045x + 0.026y = 0 (gamma neutral) and 0.08x + 0.06y = −2.5 (vega target of −$250 per contract × 100). Solve the system for x and y to find the exact quantities of each option that produce both targets simultaneously. Then neutralize the resulting position delta with stock or futures.

Long-gamma trading — the opposite approach — benefits from large moves and suffers from time decay. Long straddles, backspreads, and reverse calendar spreads are all long-gamma. A vega-neutral, long-gamma position (a reverse calendar spread constructed with precise ratios) benefits from large moves without being exposed to volatility changes. McMillan's example: buy 308 March 60 calls and sell 186 June 60 calls, simultaneously shorting 6,000 shares to neutralize delta. The result: gamma = +1,001 shares, vega ≈ 0, theta = −$625 per day. This position makes money on large moves in either direction but loses $625 per day if the stock sits still — pure exposure to realized volatility.

Chapter 41: Taxes

Tax treatment of options is more complex than most traders realize, with rules that can turn a profitable strategy into a tax-inefficient one or convert an apparent tax loss into a taxable gain. McMillan covers the major rules in sufficient depth that traders can identify when a tax question has been triggered — though he emphasizes consulting a tax professional before implementing specific tax-motivated strategies.

The basic rule: options bought and then sold produce short-term capital gains or losses, regardless of holding period. An option held for two years and then sold is still short-term. If an option expires worthless, the loss is short-term in the period it expires. If an option is exercised, the premium paid joins the stock's cost basis, and the holding period of the resulting stock position begins at exercise.

The 60/40 rule is the most favorable tax treatment available in the options world, and it applies only to cash-settled index options (SPX, OEX, NDX) and options on futures. Under this rule, 60% of gains are treated as long-term capital gains and 40% as short-term, regardless of how briefly the position was held. Day-trading index options produces the same 60/40 split as holding for a year. Additionally, open positions in these products are marked to market at year-end — unrealized gains are taxed as if realized, but losses are also immediately deductible. For active short-term traders, this combination of partial long-term treatment and mark-to-market accounting is substantially more favorable than the treatment of equity options.

Covered call writing has a labyrinth of tax rules that McMillan navigates carefully. An out-of-the-money covered call does not affect the stock's holding period — the stock continues accumulating toward long-term treatment while the call premium is collected. An ITM "qualified" covered call suspends (but does not eliminate) the stock's holding period while the call is open — the holding period pauses and resumes when the call is closed. An ITM "unqualified" covered call eliminates the holding period entirely, potentially converting a near-long-term gain to short-term.

The qualified call criteria are specific. The call must have more than 30 days to expiration. The strike must not be lower than a "benchmark" price that depends on the stock's price: for stocks between $25 and $60, the benchmark is the next lower strike; for stocks above $60 with more than 90 days remaining, two strikes below the current price. Selling a deeply in-the-money call on a stock held for 11 months triggers the unqualified rule, resetting the clock — a potentially costly mistake for someone trying to achieve long-term capital gain treatment.

The wash sale rule prohibits claiming a stock loss if substantially identical securities were purchased within 30 days before or after the sale. For options, buying a call on a stock within 30 days of selling that stock at a loss triggers the wash sale — the call is considered an acquisition of the right to buy substantially identical property. McMillan's workaround: sell the stock for a loss and simultaneously sell an in-the-money put. A put assignment is not a "purchase" of the stock — it is an obligation to buy — so the IRS does not treat it as a wash sale trigger.

Tax deferral for option holders with profitable positions approaching year-end involves careful construction. The three primary deferral methods are: buy an in-the-money put against a profitable long call (locks in the gain while deferring the tax event to next year, but the short-sale rules for puts may complicate the holding period); sell an in-the-money call at a lower strike against the profitable call (creates a spread that locks in most of the gain); or short the underlying stock against the long call. Each has specific mechanics and risks that require understanding the complete tax picture before execution.

LEAPS spread tax treatment creates one of the most favorable combinations in the options tax code for patient investors. If a bull call spread using LEAPS options is held for more than a year, the long side of the spread — the option purchased — qualifies for long-term capital gain treatment on any profit. The short side — the option sold — generates a short-term capital loss when closed. For investors in high tax brackets, offsetting short-term losses against ordinary income while receiving long-term capital gain treatment on the profit creates a meaningful tax advantage over holding the equivalent position in stocks alone. McMillan flags this as one of the few places where options tax rules genuinely favor the investor over the equivalent equity position.

Chapter 42: The Best Strategy?

McMillan's answer to the question of the "best" options strategy is deliberate and precise: there is no single best strategy. There is only the best strategy for a particular investor given their risk tolerance, financial resources, time available for monitoring, and realistic assessment of their emotional response to adverse outcomes. Mathematical optimality and psychological suitability are two different things, and a strategy that is mathematically superior but psychologically unsuitable for the investor will produce worse outcomes than a less optimal strategy that the investor can actually execute with discipline.

Mathematical ranking by theoretical expected return, from highest to lowest, places ratio writing, ratio spreading, straddle selling, and naked writing at the top. These strategies collect the most premium relative to their capital requirements and benefit most from time decay in quiet markets. But their expected return is high because their downside is severe in adverse conditions. T-bill plus option strategies and calendar combinations rank second — these offer occasional large payoffs with limited downside. Covered call writing and large-debit spreads rank third — reliable but modest returns with high commission costs relative to profit potential. Speculative option buying — especially out-of-the-money calls and puts — ranks last because time value premium erosion is a permanent headwind that only large directional moves can overcome.

The suitability framework organizes investors into four categories. Conservative investors understand stock ownership risk and should limit themselves to covered call writing and protective puts. Moderate investors can handle spreads — the defined-risk structures limit the damage from being wrong while still providing meaningful leverage. Aggressive buyers can speculate with calls and puts but should limit this activity to 15–20% of total investment assets, accepting that many positions will expire worthless. Wealthy aggressive investors who have the capital to absorb multiple large adverse outcomes — McMillan calls this "staying power" — can engage in straddle selling and ratio writing, but only if they have genuinely internalized the follow-up procedures and can execute them without freezing.

The most important test before entering any strategy is McMillan's "sleepless nights" check: if the worst case materializes, can you execute the follow-up action calmly, or will you be paralyzed? A sophisticated investor who cannot sleep while holding a naked straddle should not hold naked straddles, regardless of their account size or the strategy's theoretical merits. The market has repeatedly destroyed technically capable traders who failed to manage the emotional dimension of risk.

"The best strategy is the one that you can execute properly — including the follow-up actions — without being dominated by fear or greed. Suitability is more important than mathematical optimality." — Lawrence G. McMillan

McMillan's mathematical ranking of strategies by theoretical expected return is worth taking seriously precisely because it contradicts intuition. Most retail investors favor call and put buying — speculative option purchases — because the stories of large percentage gains are compelling. McMillan places these strategies at the bottom of the expected return ranking because time value premium erosion is relentless: the options that generate 200% returns when the stock moves big must be offset against the far more frequent cases where the option expires worthless or is closed at a loss. Ratio writing and straddle selling rank highest because they collect premium rather than paying it — they are on the structurally advantaged side of the time decay equation.

Equivalent positions — different constructions that produce identical P&L profiles — are one of McMillan's recurring analytical tools throughout the book. A covered call write is equivalent to a naked put: same break-even, same maximum profit, same loss profile. A synthetic long stock (long call plus short put at the same strike) is equivalent to owning stock. A protective put (long stock plus long put) is equivalent to a long call. These equivalences matter because different constructions have different margin requirements, different tax treatments, and different mechanical risks — knowing that two trades are economically identical allows the trader to choose the administratively superior version.

McMillan's closing framework is a suitability checklist that operates before any mathematical analysis. First question: can the investor tolerate losing 100% of the capital allocated to options strategies? If not, buying options is inappropriate regardless of how compelling the analysis appears. Second question: can the investor monitor positions daily during market hours? If not, strategies requiring active management — ratio spreads, naked straddles — are inappropriate. Third question: does the investor have sufficient capital to absorb margin calls and defend positions through adverse moves? If not, naked strategies are inappropriate. The answers to these three questions determine the universe of suitable strategies before a single chart is examined.

The book's final message is about discipline over cleverness. An options trader who masters one or two strategies well — executing with correct position sizing, using delta-neutral ratios, following pre-planned defensive rules — will outperform a trader who dabbles in every strategy from Chapter 1 to Chapter 42 without fully internalizing any of them. McMillan spent his career watching traders damage their accounts by overcomplicating their approaches, using strategies they didn't fully understand, and abandoning their plans at the first sign of adversity. The best strategy is the one that fits the investor's psychology, risk tolerance, and monitoring capability — not the one with the highest theoretical expected return in a textbook.

Trading & Investing Lessons

Implied Volatility as a Valuation Metric

Every experienced stock investor knows the concept of valuation — buying cheap relative to earnings, book value, or cash flow, and avoiding expensive. McMillan introduces the same concept applied to options: implied volatility is the options market's "valuation metric." When IV is at the 90th percentile of its own history, options are expensive — just as a stock at 30× earnings when its historical average is 15× is expensive. Selling overpriced volatility and buying underpriced volatility is the options equivalent of value investing.

Fat Tails and Black Swan Positioning

McMillan's finding that extreme moves occur 12–20 times more often than standard models predict is the mathematical foundation of tail-risk positioning. For stock investors, this means that catastrophic declines in individual stocks or indices are not "once-in-a-century" events — they are recurring features of markets. Owning out-of-the-money puts as portfolio insurance, even when they consistently expire worthless, is statistically rational when fat-tail probabilities are properly understood. The premium paid for insurance is lower than Black-Scholes suggests it should be.

The Equivalence of Covered Calls and Naked Puts

Every stock investor who sells covered calls should understand that this is economically identical to selling a naked put at the same strike. If you're comfortable holding the stock and collecting call premium, you should be equally comfortable selling a put secured by cash — you'd collect more income and earn interest on the cash simultaneously. McMillan's point is that understanding synthetic equivalences allows investors to choose the most efficient path to any risk-reward profile rather than being limited by the names of strategies they've heard about.

Skew as a Persistent Market Structural Feature

The persistent reverse skew in index options — OTM puts more expensive than OTM calls — is not a temporary anomaly that will revert to fair value. It reflects the permanent, post-1987 institutional demand for portfolio protection. Investors who understand this can build careers around it: ratio put spreads, call backspreads, and other structures that systematically collect premium from the overpriced put side of the skew have positive expected return over long periods. This is a genuine, durable edge arising from the structure of institutional demand.

Follow-Up Actions Are the Real Skill

Strategy selection matters, but follow-up action execution is where long-term profitability is actually determined. Any trader can set up a ratio write, a calendar spread, or a straddle sale. The difference between profitable and unprofitable practitioners lies entirely in what they do when the position moves against them. McMillan's follow-up frameworks — rolling, delta-adjusting, using pre-placed stops — are not optional enhancements to strategies; they are the strategies. For stock investors, this parallels the discipline of cutting losses in individual stocks rather than averaging down into deteriorating fundamentals.

Position Sizing and the Kelly Criterion

McMillan recommends limiting any single straddle or ratio write to 10–15% of available capital — not because the strategy is bad, but because follow-up actions require reserve capital to execute. A trader who is fully invested when a position needs follow-up cannot take the necessary defensive action and is forced into passive loss-taking. This is the options equivalent of the position sizing discipline that governs all serious investing: size positions so that adverse outcomes are survivable, and so that capital remains available to exploit the opportunities that adverse moves create.

The Greeks as a Unified Risk Dashboard

Stock portfolio managers track exposure in terms of sector concentration, market cap distribution, and beta to the market. Options traders track the same underlying risks but with more precision — delta is the market exposure, gamma is the rate at which that exposure changes, vega is the volatility exposure, and theta is the time income or cost. A stock manager who learns to read a position's Greek summary has access to a risk dashboard more precise than any tool available in pure equity management.

Key Takeaways

Time Decay Is Structural, Not Incidental

The wasting nature of options is not a quirk — it is the defining characteristic. Every option strategy is, at its core, a position on whether the stock will move enough before expiration to overcome the continuously shrinking time premium. Sellers benefit from decay; buyers fight it. Understanding this shapes every decision about entry timing, strike selection, and holding period.

Delta-Neutral Is a Starting Point, Not an End State

Every delta-neutral position created at inception has a delta within minutes. Gamma constantly shifts deltas as the stock moves. True neutrality requires continuous rebalancing — possible in theory, prohibitive in practice for retail traders due to commissions. The practical implication: design positions with break-even zones wide enough that the drift in delta from normal stock movement stays within acceptable limits without constant adjustment.

Implied Volatility Reverts, But Not on Schedule

High implied volatility does not revert to normal immediately. IV can stay elevated for months after an event that drove it up, and it can stay depressed through long periods of calm. The percentile approach combined with waiting for an actual reversal before entering prevents the common mistake of selling volatility into a rising volatility regime or buying it into a declining one.

The Skew Is Not an Anomaly — It Is the Market

Post-1987, the persistent reverse skew in index options represents a permanent structural feature of the market, not a pricing error waiting to be corrected. Strategies built on the assumption that the skew will disappear will be repeatedly disappointed. Strategies that accept the skew as permanent and position accordingly — collecting premium from the overpriced puts while hedging with the underpriced calls — have genuine long-term positive expected return.

Tax Rules Can Override Strategy Selection

In-the-money covered call writing — the most conservative form of options income generation — can accidentally trigger unqualified covered call status, resetting a near-long-term holding period to zero. The tax cost of this mistake can exceed the premium collected. Options traders must understand the tax rules as part of strategy selection, not as an afterthought when filing returns.

Follow-Up Procedures Must Be Pre-Committed

McMillan's most repeated practical lesson is that follow-up actions must be decided before entering a position, not improvised when the position moves against the trader. Pre-placing stop orders, setting delta-rebalancing triggers, and committing to rolling rules before entry removes the emotional decision from the adverse-move scenario. Traders who wait to decide what to do until the position is losing will almost always wait too long and take a larger loss than necessary.

The Best Strategy Matches the Investor

The mathematically superior strategy executed inconsistently produces worse results than a mathematically inferior strategy executed consistently. The trader who can sleep through a naked straddle loss and mechanically follow the pre-planned follow-up procedure will outperform the trader who intellectually understands ratio writes but panics at the first adverse move. Self-knowledge is a genuine edge.

Contrarian Ideas

Covered Call Writing Is Not Conservative

Covered call writing is universally marketed as the conservative options strategy — appropriate for retirement accounts and income-seeking investors. McMillan's analysis challenges this. The covered call writer has unlimited downside exposure to the stock (cushioned only by the small premium collected) and capped upside. The expected return is not higher than owning stock outright — and under fat-tail distributions, it may actually be lower, because premium collected is destroyed by large adverse moves. True conservatism requires protective puts, not covered calls.

Out-of-the-Money Options Are Systematically Cheap

Black-Scholes says OTM options are fairly priced given the normal distribution assumption. McMillan's fat-tail finding says the model is wrong — extreme moves are far more likely than the model admits. This makes OTM options systematically underpriced relative to the actual probability of reaching their strikes. Buying OTM straddles before events with genuine binary outcomes (FDA decisions, merger votes) may be a systematically positive expected value trade that the model incorrectly labels as speculation.

Adding a Put to a Covered Call Makes Things Worse

The covered straddle — buying stock, selling a call, selling a put — seems like more premium for the same position. McMillan demonstrates it is actually worse: the short put adds downside exposure without proportional additional premium, and the position's risk profile is strictly inferior to a simple covered write. More premium is not always better; the risk structure matters more than the absolute premium amount.

LEAPS Decay More Slowly Than Expected

Most investors assume that options are options — a 2-year LEAPS call loses value steadily over two years. McMillan's decay table shows that this intuition is dramatically wrong: an ATM LEAPS loses only 0.12% of its value per day at 24 months out, accelerating to 1.27% per day at one month out. This non-linearity makes LEAPS dramatically superior to short-term options for investors who want long-term directional exposure without the punishing daily decay that short-term ATM options suffer.

Delta-Neutral Does Not Mean Risk-Free

Institutional risk managers sometimes treat delta-neutral positions as essentially safe — the position has no directional exposure. McMillan's Greek analysis shows the opposite. A delta-neutral short straddle has position gamma of −600, meaning a two-point adverse move creates 1,200 shares of directional exposure instantly. It also has massive negative vega, meaning any volatility spike creates large losses independent of the stock's direction. Delta-neutral is the starting point, not a measure of total risk.

Frameworks & Proposals

The Three-Step Volatility Trade Selection Framework

Before entering any volatility trade, McMillan requires three independent confirmations. Step one: identify one primary criterion — IV percentile at an extreme (above 80th or below 20th), a large IV-versus-HV discrepancy (ratio above 1.20 or below 0.80), or a clear trend reversal on the IV chart. Step two: calculate the break-even prices of the proposed strategy and determine the probability of reaching them using the fat-tail-adjusted probability formula. Buying volatility requires above-80% probability; selling requires below-25%. Step three: check the stock's historical price histogram to confirm that moves of the required size have actually occurred before in similar time frames. All three must confirm before entering.

The Suitability Ladder

McMillan organizes options strategies into four rungs of a suitability ladder, each requiring greater capitalization, monitoring capability, and emotional resilience than the one below it. Rung one: covered call writing and protective puts (understand stock ownership risk; standard margin). Rung two: spreads — defined-risk strategies requiring understanding of multi-leg positions. Rung three: option buying for speculation — limit to 15–20% of assets; accept high loss frequency. Rung four: naked writing, ratio writing, straddle selling — requires "staying power" capital to absorb multiple large adverse outcomes without being forced into liquidation. Investing above one's rung consistently produces forced liquidation at the worst possible moment.

The Equivalent Stock Position (ESP) Framework

ESP provides a single number that expresses the total directional exposure of any portfolio, however many legs it contains. ESP = Σ (option delta × quantity × shares per contract) + shares held. A portfolio with ESP of +500 is long the equivalent of 500 shares; ESP of −300 is short 300 shares; ESP of 0 is delta-neutral. Every position adjustment — roll, rebalance, hedge — should be evaluated in terms of its effect on total portfolio ESP. This transforms position management from strategy-by-strategy monitoring into unified portfolio risk management.

The Follow-Up Decision Tree for Ratio Writes

When a ratio write moves adverse, the decision sequence is: (1) Calculate current ESP from new stock price and updated deltas. (2) Determine whether ESP has moved meaningfully positive or negative. (3) If positive (stock rallied), roll calls up to a higher strike or buy back the naked portion. (4) If negative (stock fell), roll calls down or sell additional stock. (5) Alternatively, use pre-placed stop orders — a buy stop above the upside break-even converts the position to a covered write automatically; a sell stop below the downside break-even converts to a naked write. The key is having the decision tree built before entering the position, not improvising under pressure.

The Delta-Neutral Construction Protocol

To construct any delta-neutral position: (1) Select the two (or more) options to use. (2) Divide their deltas to determine the neutral ratio. (3) Execute the trade in that ratio. (4) Calculate the resulting position ESP (typically near zero but not exactly, due to rounding). (5) Use stock or futures to neutralize the remaining delta. (6) Record position gamma, theta, and vega to understand how the position will behave on adverse moves and volatility changes. (7) Set delta-rebalancing triggers — specific ESP thresholds at which the position will be rebalanced toward neutrality.

What Didn't Fit

The LEAPS Decay Table in Full

McMillan's decay table is one of the most practically useful tables in the book, worth memorizing. At 24 months remaining, an at-the-money LEAPS call loses 0.12% of its value per day; a 20% out-of-the-money LEAPS loses 0.18% per day. At 18 months: 0.14% and 0.27%. At 12 months: 0.19% and 0.55%. At 9 months: 0.22% and 0.76%. At 6 months: 0.27% and 1.18%. At 3 months: 0.60% and 3.57%. At 1 month: 1.27%. The OTM decay rates at short expirations are staggering — a 20% OTM option three months from expiration is losing 3.57% of its remaining value every single day. Traders who buy short-term OTM calls expecting them to eventually reach the money are fighting an exponential decay curve that very few directional moves can outrun.

The LEAPS Stock Substitute Arithmetic

McMillan's explicit calculation of the LEAPS stock substitute demonstrates the strategy's economics precisely. Stock at $50, 2-year LEAPS call with $40 strike priced at $12 (intrinsic $10 + $2 time premium). Capital required for the LEAPS: $1,200 versus $5,000 for the stock. The $3,810 difference invested in T-bills at 5% earns $190 in interest over six months. The cost of the LEAPS strategy: $200 time premium plus $50 in foregone dividends minus $190 in T-bill interest = $60 net opportunity cost. For $60 in additional cost, the LEAPS holder reduces maximum loss from $5,000 (stock goes to zero) to $1,200. This is genuine economic value, not just leverage for leverage's sake.

The Settlement Risk of SPX Options

SPX options settle at Friday's opening prices based on each component stock's opening print on that Friday — not Thursday's closing index value. A trader holding an SPX position through settlement is exposed to the opening gaps in every constituent stock simultaneously. In normal markets this gap is small. But on the most volatile Fridays — especially expiration Fridays during market disruptions — the settlement value can differ meaningfully from Thursday's close. McMillan uses this detail to explain why experienced SPX traders close positions on Thursday afternoon before settlement day rather than holding through the Friday open, eliminating this gap risk at the cost of one day of remaining time premium.

The No-Cost Collar Volatility Table

McMillan's table for constructing zero-cost collars with LEAPS options reveals how powerful volatility is in shaping collar economics. At 30% volatility, an at-the-money LEAPS put can be financed by selling a call 30% out of the money — the call is far away but still generates enough premium to pay for full downside protection. At 40% vol: the financing call is 35% OTM. At 50% vol: 40% OTM. At 70% vol: 50% OTM. Higher volatility makes both the put and call more expensive in absolute terms, but the OTM call can be placed dramatically further away from the current stock price because its absolute premium is so much larger. In high-volatility environments, the no-cost collar offers exceptional protection coverage — the stock can rally 50% before the upside is capped.

Straddle vs. Strangle Comparison in Practice

McMillan's worked comparison between the straddle and strangle on the same stock at the same time reveals the practical tradeoff. An ATM straddle costs $5 (break-evens $55 and $45 on a $50 stock); a strangle using the $45 put and $55 call costs $2 (break-evens $43 and $57). The strangle is cheaper but requires a much larger move — $7 in either direction versus $5 for the straddle. The strangle's advantage is that the zone of maximum loss is now $10 wide (between $45 and $55) rather than just the single point at $50. If the trader expects a move but is uncertain of its exact timing, the strangle's wider maximum-loss zone provides more time for the catalyst to develop without the position immediately losing maximum value.
