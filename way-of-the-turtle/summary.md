# Way of the Turtle — Summary

**Author:** Curtis M. Faith
**Published:** 2007, McGraw-Hill
**Pages:** 259

## Overview

Way of the Turtle is the definitive insider account of the legendary
Turtle trading experiment — a bet between Richard Dennis and William
Eckhardt over whether great traders are born or made. In 1983, Dennis
recruited 13 ordinary people, taught them his trend-following system
in two weeks, and gave each a million-dollar trading account. Curtis
Faith, selected at age 19, became the most successful Turtle, earning
over $31 million for Dennis during the initial period.

The book reveals the complete Turtle trading system — Donchian channel
breakouts, ATR-based position sizing, N-factor volatility normalization,
and strict risk management rules — but its deeper argument is that the
system itself was never the secret. The real edge came from psychology:
the ability to execute simple rules consistently despite cognitive biases
that cause most traders to deviate. Faith demonstrates that identical
rules, taught to identically trained traders, produced wildly different
results because of psychological differences in execution.

## Key Themes

### 1. Psychology Over System
The Turtle experiment proved that trading success is 80% psychology and
20% method. Every Turtle learned the same rules, yet only about half
made significant money. The difference was entirely psychological —
willingness to take every signal, tolerance for drawdowns, and the
discipline to avoid modifying rules during losing streaks.

### 2. Behavioral Finance as Edge
Cognitive biases (loss aversion, anchoring, disposition effect, recency
bias, outcome bias) create systematic market inefficiencies. Trend
following works because it exploits these biases — buying when anchored
traders refuse to believe prices will go higher, holding when loss-averse
traders exit winners too early. The biases are permanent because human
psychology doesn't change.

### 3. Position Sizing and Risk Management
The N-factor (ATR-based) position sizing system was genuinely innovative
for 1983. By normalizing dollar volatility across markets, every position
carried roughly equal risk. Unit limits (4 per market, 6 correlated, 12
total direction) prevented catastrophic concentration. These limits saved
Richard Dennis over $100 million during the October 1987 crash.

### 4. The Illusion of Backtesting
Backtests systematically overstate future performance due to trader
effects (crowding), random effects (luck), optimization paradox, and
overfitting. Faith introduces robust measures (RAR%, R-cubed) that are
less sensitive to start/end dates than traditional metrics (CAGR%, MAR,
Sharpe). Monte Carlo simulation reveals the distribution of possible
outcomes rather than a single misleading number.

### 5. Simplicity and Robustness
Simple systems survive market regime changes; complex systems break.
The six tested systems show that basic moving-average crossovers and
channel breakouts all produce similar long-term results — and that
adding stops to trend-following systems often hurts performance rather
than helping it. Diversity across markets and systems is more valuable
than optimizing any single system.

### 6. Trading as a Way of Life
Faith extends Turtle principles beyond markets: embrace risk, accept
failure as prerequisite to learning, don't stay on wrong paths due to
sunk costs, and don't let ego drive decisions. Money is a tool, not
an objective — traders who care more about trading well than about
money tend to make more of it.

## The Complete Turtle System (Bonus Chapter)

- **Markets:** Liquid U.S. futures (bonds, currencies, metals, energies)
- **Position sizing:** 1 unit = 1N (ATR) = 1% of account equity
- **Entries:** System 1 (20-day breakout, filtered); System 2 (55-day breakout, unfiltered)
- **Adding units:** Every 1/2 N above entry, max 4 units per market
- **Stops:** 2N from entry (2% account risk); raised as units added
- **Exits:** System 1 = 10-day breakout against; System 2 = 20-day breakout against
- **Tactics:** Limit orders, buy strength/sell weakness, wait for bounces in fast markets
