## Chapter 1: Risk Junkies

> *High risk, high reward: It takes balls of steel to play this game.*
> — Told to a friend before starting the Turtle program

### Investors vs. Traders

- **Investors** buy actual things (companies, real estate) for long-term appreciation
  - **Warren Buffett** example: buys companies, not stocks; profits from market mispricing vs. intrinsic value
- **Traders** buy stocks, futures, options — they buy and sell *risk*, not underlying assets
  - Don't care about management quality, oil consumption outlook, or coffee production
- Referenced: **Peter Bernstein**, *Against the Gods: The Remarkable Story of Risk* — markets evolved specifically to enable risk transfer between parties

### Hedging

- **Hedging**: buying/selling futures to offset business risk from price fluctuations
- **Southwest Airlines** example: hedged oil at ~$26/barrel while prices rose above $60
  - Still getting 85% of its oil at $26 years later — major competitive advantage
  - Southwest is one of the most profitable airlines *because* it focused on its core business and used markets to neutralize oil risk
- Who sells those hedging contracts? Traders.

### Types of Risk: Liquidity vs. Price

Two major risk categories traders deal in:

- **Liquidity risk**: the risk that you cannot buy or sell when you want to
  - Traders who specialize here: **scalpers** and **market makers** — profit from the **bid-ask spread**
  - Example: stock XYZ bid $28.50 / ask $28.55 → scalper earns the $0.05 spread
  - Variant: **arbitrage** — trading liquidity of one market vs. another (e.g., crude oil London vs. NY)
- **Price risk**: the possibility that prices move significantly up or down
  - Traders who take on price risk: **speculators** or **position traders**
  - Make money by buying low/selling high, or **going short** (sell first, buy back later at lower price)

### The Three-Party Trade: ACME, Sam, and Mr. Ice

Illustrative example of all three trader types interacting:

- **ACME Corporation** (hedger): buys 10 British pound futures contracts on **CME** to hedge rising costs at its UK lab
  - Executes through broker **MAN Financial**; floor trader **Sam** (scalper) sells them at $1.8452 ask
- **Sam** (scalper): immediately tries to buy back at $1.8450 bid to capture 2-tick spread = ~$100 profit on 10 contracts
  - British pound contract = 62,500 GBP; minimum tick = $0.0001 = $6.25/tick
  - Sam buys from **Mr. Ice** (speculator), who is building a short position betting price will fall
- **Mr. Ice** (speculator): holds the short position for days or months depending on price movement

Three roles summarized:
- **The hedger**: eliminates price risk
- **The scalper**: trades liquidity risk, earns the spread
- **The speculator**: assumes the original price risk, bets on direction

### Panic in the Pits

- When a large buyer (e.g., **Calyon Financial**, representing hedge funds) starts buying aggressively, scalpers with short positions panic
- Floor traders already short 10–100 contracts face large losses if price spikes
- Collective psychology drives price: when enough sellers refuse to sell at current price and buyers will pay more → price moves up
- Experienced trader response: exit short early, flip long to profit from the panic buying
- Inexperienced trader: holds too long, faces 50-tick loss on 50 contracts = **$15,625 loss** in minutes

> "The hedgers, scalpers, and speculators are still there, hiding behind the screen — waiting to eat you alive if you let them."

### Death of the Pits (sidebar)

- Electronic exchanges replacing trading pits: lower costs, faster execution, millisecond fill confirmations
- Volume has migrated to electronic markets wherever both coexist
- Pits offered unique information: you could *see* the psychology of the market in traders' faces
- New electronic markets create new opportunities: lower execution costs, higher volume, ability to trade millions without moving price

## Chapter 2: Taming the Turtle Mind

> *Human emotion is both the source of opportunity in trading and the greatest challenge. Master it and you will succeed. Ignore it at your peril.*

### Behavioral Finance Foundation

- Markets are comprised of individuals with hopes, fears, and cognitive limitations
- **Behavioral finance** explains market phenomena through psychological factors — brought to popular attention by:
  - **Robert Shiller**, *Irrational Exuberance*
  - **Hersh Shefrin**, *Beyond Greed and Fear*
- Core finding: people make **systematic errors** in judgment under uncertainty — not random errors, but predictable ones
- **The Turtle Way works because it exploits systematic, repeated irrationality embedded in every person**

The four emotions that undermine traders:
- **Hope**: "I hope this goes up right after I buy it"
- **Fear**: "I can't take another loss; I'll sit this one out"
- **Greed**: "I'm making so much money, I'm going to double my position"
- **Despair**: "This system doesn't work; I keep losing money"

### Cognitive Biases Affecting Trading

**Loss aversion**: strong preference for avoiding losses over acquiring gains
- Losses have ~2x the psychological power of equivalent gains
- In trading: a $10,000 rule-following loss is felt as strongly as a $20,000 missed opportunity
- Makes it extremely painful to take a stop loss — the trader holds hoping for recovery

**Sunk cost effect**: treating already-spent money as more valuable than future money
- Classic example: ACME spends $100M on tech, then a better alternative emerges — rational actor ignores sunk cost, irrational one keeps spending to avoid "waste"
- In trading: trader planned to exit at -$1,000, now at -$2,000; holds because exiting feels like "wasting" the $1,000 already lost
- Result: many new traders hold losing positions until account is decimated (30–50% losses)
- Real-world example: Silicon Valley friends in 1999–2000 — tech stock options, watched them drop to 1/10th or 1/100th of value, always said "I'll sell when it gets back to $X"

**Disposition effect**: tendency to sell winners too early and hold losers too long
- Related to sunk cost and loss aversion — both stem from unwillingness to face reality of prior bad decisions
- Destroys ability to recover large losses when winning trades are cut short

**Outcome bias**: judging a decision by its result rather than the quality of the process
- A correct approach can still produce losing trades; outcome bias causes traders to abandon valid systems after short losing streaks
- Worsened by **recency bias**: recent trades weigh more heavily than older data
  - Two months of losses can outweigh six months of prior wins in trader's mind

**Anchoring**: relying too heavily on a salient reference price
- Tech stock friends anchored on recent highs — current price always looked "too low" to sell
- Prevents rational reassessment of the current market

**Bandwagon effect** (herd effect): believing things because many others believe them
- Responsible for the "unstoppable" price surge at the end of bubbles

**Belief in the law of small numbers**: drawing conclusions from too little data
- Misapplies the statistical law of large numbers (valid for large samples) to tiny samples
- A strategy working 4 of 6 times is NOT statistically significant
- Three years of fund outperformance says almost nothing about long-term expectations
- Combined with recency bias + outcome bias → traders abandon valid approaches *just before they start working again*

> Key insight: **If a trader is NOT influenced by cognitive biases, almost every bias creates profit opportunities for them.** Biased traders are the prey; unbiased traders are the predators.

### Trading Styles

#### Trend Following

- Enter when markets hit historical highs/lows; exit when trend reverses and sustains reversal for weeks
- Has generated excellent returns consistently for as long as futures have been traded
- **Hard to follow** for three reasons:
  1. Large trends are infrequent → 65–70% of trades are *losers*
  2. **Draw downs**: losing periods after trends end, can last months in choppy markets; expected drawdown ≈ expected annual return (e.g., 30% return system → expect 30% drawdown)
  3. Requires substantial capital due to wide entry-to-stop-loss distance

> "The trend is your friend until the end when it bends."

#### Countertrend Trading

- Opposite of trend following: sell short near new highs, buy near new lows
- Premise: *most* breakouts to new highs do NOT result in sustained trends
- Profits from **support and resistance** (covered in Chapter 6)

## Chapter 2 (continued): Trading Styles & Market States

### Swing Trading & Day Trading

- **Swing trading** = trend following on shorter timeframes; trades last
  3-4 days vs. months; uses 5-min, 15-min, or hourly charts
- **Day trading** = style defined by time frame, not strategy; trader
  exits before close to avoid overnight news risk
  - Three day-trading sub-styles: position trading, scalping, arbitrage
- **Scalping**: capturing the **bid-ask spread**; creates liquidity;
  e.g., buy gold at $550 bid, sell at $551 ask
- **Arbitrage**: exploiting price differences across exchanges;
  e.g., buy gold on Comex at $550, sell e-mini on CBOT Globex at $555

### Watching the Market State

- Each strategy works best in specific **market states** — four exist:
  1. **Stable and quiet**: prices range-bound, little movement
  2. **Stable and volatile**: large daily/weekly swings, no monthly trend
  3. **Trending and quiet**: slow drift over months, minimal retracement
  4. **Trending and volatile**: large moves with significant reversals
- Trend followers love **trending and quiet** — profits don't get
  given back; volatile markets are punishing (hard to hold through dips)
- Countertrend traders love **stable and volatile** — large swings,
  narrow range
- Swing traders like any **volatile** market (trending or not)
- States shift over time; a market can move from trending+quiet to
  trending+volatile as a trend matures

> "A Turtle never tries to predict market direction but instead *looks
> for indications that a market is in a particular state*. Good traders
> don't try to predict what the market *will do*; instead they look at
> the indications of what the market *is doing*."

## Chapter 3: The First $2 Million Is the Toughest

> "Trade with an edge, manage risk, be consistent, and keep it simple.
> The entire Turtle training, and indeed the basis for all successful
> trading, can be summed up in these four core principles."

### Setting & Class Composition

- Training held at the **Union League Club** in Chicago (1983), two
  blocks from the CBOT — a staid gentleman's club (members: Philip
  Danforth Armour, George Pullman, Marshall Field, John Deere)
- First Turtle class: **13 trainees** — 11 men, 2 women; mix of
  experienced traders and complete novices; author (**Curtis Faith**)
  was 19, the youngest
- **Rich Dennis** and **Bill Eckhardt** both taught; approached markets
  scientifically — computerized analysis, not gut feelings; their
  research gave them the confidence to stake money on teaching novices

### Risk of Ruin

- **Risk of ruin**: probability of losing all capital through a string
  of losses — a gambling concept applied to trading
- Even a positive-expectation game can ruin you with wrong bet sizing
  - Example: 50/50 game paying 2:1 on rolls of 4,5,6; with $1,000,
    betting $500 gives 25% chance of ruin on first two rolls
- **Key insight**: risk of ruin increases *disproportionately* as bet
  size rises — doubling the amount risked per trade can triple,
  quadruple, or quintuple the risk of ruin

### The Science of Controlled Risk (Money Management)

- **Money management** = controlling position size to survive bad
  periods while maximizing profit potential
- Two Turtle approaches:
  1. Positions broken into small **units** — losses only hit a portion
  2. Position sizing based on **N** (daily market volatility in dollar
     terms), so all positions move up/down by roughly the same dollar
     amount
- **N** = what **Rich Dennis** and **Bill Eckhardt** called their
  volatility measure; now known as **Average True Range (ATR)**,
  named by **J. Welles Wilder** in *New Concepts in Technical
  Trading Systems*
- In 1983 this was genuinely innovative — most traders sized positions
  by broker margin requirements or subjective criteria, not volatility
- Later popularized by **Van Tharp** in *Trade Your Way to Financial
  Freedom* (1998)

### The Turtle Edge

- Large accounts (like Rich gave the Turtles) create their own
  problem: order size moves the market, raising execution costs
- Turtles taught to use **limit orders** ("or better" orders) rather
  than market orders — over time small price improvements add up
  significantly
- The pivotal difference between winning and losing traders:
  *Turtles were taught how to think in terms of the long run* and
  were given a system with an **edge**

### Expectation: Quantifying the Edge

- **Edge** = systematic advantage; casinos have it over players;
  card counters temporarily gain it over casinos
- Blackjack card counters: bet small when house has edge, bet big when
  deck is high-card-rich (house must hit to 16, goes bust more often)
  — teams operate to avoid detection
- **Expectation** answers "What happens if I keep doing this?" —
  positive expectation games can be won over time; negative
  expectation games (roulette, craps) will eventually bankrupt the player
- Expectation formula: average dollar won per trade ÷ average dollar
  risked per trade
  - Example: expectation of 0.2 = make 20¢ for every $1 risked
- Risk measured as: (entry price − stop loss) × contracts × contract
  size
  - Gold example: long at $350, stop at $320, 10 contracts × 100 oz
    = **$3,000 risk**
- **Outcome bias** = judging a decision by its outcome rather than
  its quality at the time made — Turtles explicitly trained to ignore it

> **The Turtle Mind:**
> - Think in terms of the long run when trading
> - Avoid outcome bias
> - Believe in the effects of trading with positive expectation

- Losses are *cost of doing business*, not trading errors — identical
  to how casino owners view losing hands
- Periods of losses typically **precede** periods of good trading

### Trend Following (Breakout Systems)

- **Trends** = sustained price changes over weeks or months
- Turtles bought as market moved from sideways to trending up; sold
  short as it began trending down; exited when trend went sideways again
- **Key contrarian claim**: the specific rules matter far less than
  executing any consistent trend-following system — "many other widely
  known trend-following methods work equally well"
- Two systems taught:
  - **System 1**: 20-day (4-week) breakout to determine highs/lows
  - **System 2**: 60-day (12-week) breakout
  - Each system's stop loss = **2N** (two average true ranges from
    entry) = approximately 2% of account
- Named after **Richard Donchian**, who popularized the breakout method
  (**Donchian channels**)

### Four Core Turtle Principles (Summary)

1. **Trade with an Edge**: use a positive-expectation system
2. **Manage Risk**: control risk to survive long enough to see benefits
3. **Be Consistent**: execute the plan every time
4. **Keep It Simple**: "catch every trend — two or three trades might
   account for all your profits, so don't miss a trend"

### Things Heat Up: First Trade (January 1984)

- After training, Turtles given desks in Insurance Exchange building
  next to CBOT; practice accounts sized at 3 contracts per market,
  max 4 units (12 contracts) per commodity (~$50,000–$100,000 equivalent)
- Required to log reasons for every trade; Faith's entries: "Entered
  long at $400.00 because it was a 60-day breakout according to System 2"
- **February heating oil** rose from $0.80 → $0.84; Faith bought 3
  contracts, scaled to full 12 contracts; oil hit $0.98 in under a week
- **Critical observation**: Faith was the *only* Turtle fully loaded —
  every other Turtle had declined to follow the system despite identical
  training (feared expiring contract, thought move too fast, etc.)
- When oil dipped from $0.98 → $0.94 (~$1,200/contract loss), other
  Turtles with positions liquidated; Faith held per training
- Oil recovered, peaked at $1.05; Faith exited at $1.03 (near contract
  high of $1.053); **account up $78,000** — roughly 3× what any other
  Turtle made
- On a normal $1M account (18 contracts vs. 3), trade would have
  yielded ~**$500,000 (50% return)** in one month

> "The difference in return had nothing to do with knowledge and
> everything to do with emotional and psychological factors... Over the
> years I kept finding evidence that *emotional and psychological
> strength are the most important ingredients in successful trading*."

### The First Report Card

- Rich's Q&A after month 1: asked Turtles why they hadn't bought
  heating oil — answers revealed fear, doubt about contract expiry,
  recency bias
- Faith's reframe: *not* taking the trade was the riskiest move; Rich
  confirmed this publicly
- Rich had promised $1M accounts to those who proved themselves;
  several received them; Faith received a **$2 million account** for
  how he handled the heating oil trade

## Chapter 4: Think Like a Turtle

> "Good trading is not about being right, it is about trading right.
> If you want to be successful, you need to think of the long run
> and ignore the outcomes of individual trades."

### The Bet Was a Draw

- Conventional wisdom: Dennis won the bet (trading can be taught)
- Faith's view: **the bet was a draw** — 1/3 to 1/2 of Turtles were
  less profitable than the best or outright unsuccessful; dropped from
  program with losing records
- Difference between best and worst Turtles = **psychological makeup**,
  not knowledge

### It's Not About Who's Right

- Beginners want to predict; wins feel like being right, losses feel
  like being wrong — wrong framework
- *Turtles do not care about being right. They care about making money.*
- The future is **unknowable in specifics but foreseeable in character**
  — you can't know if gold goes up tomorrow, but you know trends will
  occur because human emotion doesn't change
- Counterintuitive: "easier to make money when you are wrong most of
  the time" — expecting to lose on most trades removes emotional
  attachment to individual outcomes
- 10 losing trades in a row while sticking to the plan = trading well
  with bad luck, not bad trading

### Forget the Past

- Most traders dwell on past mistakes; Turtles learn from the past but
  don't dwell on it; losses are part of the game, not failures
- **Recency bias**: overweighting recent events — recent past feels more
  important than it is; most traders exhibit this, so markets show it too
- Cocoa market example (1998–1999): **17 consecutive losing breakout
  trades** before a massive winning trend in November 1998; Faith's
  friend skipped the winning trade because he'd lost so much recently
- Turtles cut for poor performance were often victims of recency bias —
  and ironically, trends tend to emerge precisely when everyone gives up
- **Avoid the Future Tense**: recency bias + need to be right +
  predicting the future = the three cognitive traps that destroy traders

## Chapter 4 (cont.): Thinking Like a Turtle

### The Third Affliction: Prediction vs. Probability

- The third cognitive affliction is thinking in terms of prediction rather than **possibilities and probabilities**
- Author's standard response to "which way will the market go?": "I have no idea" — and he meant it
- People default to "likely/unlikely" thinking, not probabilistic thinking; actuaries are the exception
- Insurance analogy: insurers don't predict hurricanes — they price policies against probability distributions
  - If you *knew* your house would be destroyed, you'd move, not buy insurance
  - Insurance companies profit by selling policies for less than the probable cost of payout
- Trading is the same: "The best you can do is be confident that the rewards will outweigh the risks over the long run"

### Thinking in Probabilities

- **Normal distribution** (Gaussian): bell-shaped curve showing decreasing likelihood as you move from the mean
  - Example: women's height averages 5'4"; only ~1% are 5'10" or taller (99.18th percentile at 70 inches)
- A **histogram** achieves the same insight empirically — count and categorize without complex math
- Applied to trading: histogram of monthly returns from 20-year test of the **Donchian Trend system** shows:
  - Distribution resembles a normal bell curve but with **skew** and **fat tails** to the right
  - The elongation toward the right = the good months; this is what trend-following captures
- **R-multiple** (invented by **Chuck Branscomb**, popularized by **Van Tharp** in *Trade Your Way to Financial Freedom*):
  - Profit expressed as a multiple of the initial risk (R)
  - Example: buy gold at $450, stop at $440 = $1,000 risk per contract; if trade earns $5,000 = 5R trade
  - Donchian Trend 22-year test: 3,746 losing trades vs. 1,854 winning trades
  - Losing trades cluster near -1R (stops work); winning trades extend to 10R, 12R, even 20R+
  - 43 trades returned 10R or more

> "We thought in terms of probabilities, and that gave us the confidence to make decisions in the face of large degrees of risk and uncertainty."

- Turtles knew most individual trades would lose but that winners would be large enough to cover all losses with profit left over
- Key insight: **don't measure personal worth by outcome of a single trade** — most trades probably lose

### Dos and Don'ts for Thinking Like a Turtle

1. **Trade in the present** — don't dwell on the past or try to predict the future
2. **Think in probabilities, not prediction** — focus on methods where probabilities favor success over the long run
3. **Take responsibility for your own trades** — don't blame others, markets, or brokers

### Playing Favorites

- Some Turtles couldn't accept probabilistic thinking; they needed to "be right" and predict markets
- One Turtle was convinced **Rich Dennis** had withheld secret rules from most of the group — "completely ludicrous"
- Why would Dennis give traders his own money to lose while holding back key information?
- Author's actual edge: used a simpler approach — 100% allocated to the longer-term **10-week breakout system**, fewer trades, less monitoring

### Excuses, Excuses

- Conspiracy theories (hidden rules, market manipulation) are a defense mechanism against taking responsibility
- **Inability to take responsibility is the single most significant factor leading to failure**
- Trading is one of the few activities where you cannot hide — results are direct and unavoidable
- No evidence of mass-scale collusion of the kind imagined by blame-shifters

> "You make the trades and you are responsible for the outcome. Blaming others for your mistakes is a sure way to lose."

## Chapter 5: Trading with an Edge

> *"Trading with an edge is what separates the professionals from the amateurs. Ignore this and you will be eaten by those who don't."*

### What Is an Edge?

- **Edge**: borrowed from gambling theory — the statistical advantage held by the casino
  - Card counting in blackjack creates a player edge; without an edge, you lose to friction costs
- Without an edge in trading, commissions + slippage + computer costs + exchange fees will erode capital
- Formal definition: *An edge in trading is an exploitable statistical advantage based on market behavior that is likely to recur in the future*
- The best edges come from **cognitive biases** that distort market behavior

### Elements of an Edge

- Finding an edge requires: entry points with greater-than-normal probability of move in desired direction, **paired** with compatible exit strategies
- System edges come from three components:
  - **Portfolio selection**: algorithms determining which markets are valid to trade on a given day
  - **Entry signals**: algorithms determining when to buy or sell to enter
  - **Exit signals**: algorithms determining when to buy or sell to exit
- Important: an entry may have edge only for short-term or only for long-term — not necessarily both
- Entry strategies must be matched to exit strategies of the same style (trend-following entries with trend-following exits, etc.)

### The Edge Ratio (E-Ratio)

- **Maximum Adverse Excursion (MAE)**: maximum price move in the *bad* direction after entry
- **Maximum Favorable Excursion (MFE)**: maximum price move in the *good* direction after entry
- A random entry would produce MFE ≈ MAE (coin-flip test confirmed: E5=1.01, E10=1.005, E50=0.997)
- **E-ratio formula**:
  1. Compute MFE and MAE for the specified time frame
  2. Divide each by the ATR at entry (normalizes for volatility across markets)
  3. Average the volatility-adjusted MFE and MAE over all signals
  4. E-ratio = average volatility-adjusted MFE ÷ average volatility-adjusted MAE
- Notation: **E10-ratio** = MFE/MAE measured over 10 days from entry; **E70** = 70 days, etc.
- An E-ratio > 1.0 = positive edge; < 1.0 = negative edge

### E-Ratio Applied to the Donchian Trend System

- Test: 28 high-volume U.S. futures markets, Jan 1, 1996 – Jun 30, 2006
- **Donchian channel breakout**: buy when price exceeds highest high of previous 20 days; sell short when price breaks below lowest low of previous 20 days
- **Trend portfolio filter**: only take long trades when 50-day MA > 300-day MA; only short when 50-day MA < 300-day MA
- Results without filter: E5-ratio = 0.99, E10-ratio = 1.0 (no short-term edge — expected for trend system)
- **E70-ratio = 1.20**: 20-day breakout trades move 20% farther in the direction of the break than against it over 70 days
- Surprising finding: E-ratio starts *below* 1.0 for very short periods after breakout
  - Explains why breakout trading is psychologically difficult — initial move is often against you
  - Also explains why **countertrend trading** (betting against the breakout) has a short-term edge

### The Trend Portfolio Filter Edge

- Running 70,000 random entries *with* the trend portfolio filter: **E70-ratio = 1.27**
  - Remarkable: the filter alone (even on random entries) produces higher edge than the breakout signal itself
- Adding filter to the breakout: E70-ratio rises from 1.20 to **1.33**; E120-ratio reaches ~1.6
- The filter works by eliminating counter-trend breakouts — those trades were generating the largest adverse moves
- The combination also smooths the edge ratio curve (less erratic climb), indicating more consistent edge

### The Exit Edge

- Exits should also have an edge, but exit edge is harder to isolate — exits depend on both entry and exit conditions
- Cannot measure exit edge the same way as entry edge (you can't isolate an exit from the entry context)
- Key distinction: **for entries, what matters is what happens after you enter** (money is at risk)
- **For exits, what happens after you exit is irrelevant** — only what happened before the exit counts
- Evaluate exits by their effect on overall system performance metrics, not by post-exit price behavior

## Chapter 6: Falling Off the Edge (begins)

> *"Edges are found in the places that are the battlegrounds between buyers and sellers. Your task as a trader is to find those places and wait to see who wins and who loses."*

### Why Edges Exist

- Trading edges exist because market players are **not rational** — economists are wrong about this
- Cognitive biases create systematic divergences between market perceptions and reality
- These divergences recur because human psychology doesn't change

### Support and Resistance

- **Support and resistance**: the tendency for prices not to exceed previous price levels
- Results from three cognitive biases operating simultaneously:
  1. **Anchoring**: tendency to base price perceptions on readily available reference points
     - Recent highs and lows become anchors because they're visible on charts and psychologically salient
     - Example (coffee futures, 2000): price low of ~$1.13 becomes anchor; when price later drops to $1.15, traders view it as "low but not *too* low" relative to the anchor
  2. **Recency bias**: tendency to weight recent data more heavily than older data
     - The most recent high or low counts *more* in traders' mental models than earlier price history
  3. **Disposition effect** (referenced as third cause)
- The self-reinforcing mechanism: if many traders believe price will bounce at a support level, they reduce selling near that level — which itself creates the bounce, validating the belief
- Support and resistance is a **self-fulfilling phenomenon**: the belief in it causes the behavior that creates it

<!-- pp. 81-100 -->

## Chapter 6 (continued): Support and Resistance

### The Disposition Effect and Self-Fulfilling Prophecy
- **Disposition effect**: traders' tendency to lock in profits rather than let
  winners run; fear of losing profits grows stronger as profits rise
- This creates resistance: traders who bought earlier set a mental anchor at
  the recent high and resolve to sell if price returns there — creating a
  natural cluster of sell orders at that level
- Previous highs become the **anchor** against which subsequent prices are
  measured; more traders sell as price approaches those highs
- Support and resistance is a **self-fulfilling prophecy** — the belief in it
  causes it to happen

### Finding the Edge in Support and Resistance
- Support/resistance is a loose construct, not a hard rule — prices tend to
  bounce at those levels, but not guaranteed, and not at the exact price
- **Countertrend traders**: edge comes directly from support/resistance
  holding; they profit when price bounces off previous highs/lows
- **Trend-following traders**: edge comes from support/resistance *breaking
  down*; they buy resistance breakouts (long entries) and sell support
  breakdowns (short entries)
- Turtles used short-term resistance breakdowns to exit long trades and
  bought short-term resistance breakouts to exit short trades

### Heating Oil Case Study (December 2006)
- Support at $2.10 held mid-June; price bounced to $2.31 (new resistance)
- Second test of support hesitated but failed; price broke below $2.05
  (prior low set Aug 30) on September 5
- Once $2.05 broke, no obvious support remained → price fell to $1.85,
  then $1.73 — a drop of ~37¢ from break point
- Scenario: trader who bought 5 contracts at $2.10, added 5 more at $2.05
  "because it's cheaper" → 10-contract losing trade → **$115,500 loss**
  (10 contracts × $0.275/gallon avg loss × 42,000 gallons/contract)
- New traders panic and end up on the wrong side when price moves swiftly;
  trend followers were *selling* on the way down and making money

> *"The source of the edge for trend followers is the gap in human perception
> at the time when support and resistance breaks down."*

- When support breaks, there are no new buyers because rational actors
  wait for price to stop dropping; panic sellers keep driving it lower
  until selling exhausts itself

### Shaky Ground — Points of Price Instability
- Prices near support/resistance edges = **points of price instability**:
  unlikely to stay there, likely to move significantly in one direction
- When a price level breaks that the market hasn't seen in a long time,
  there are no remaining obvious anchors for turning trader psychology
- Battle analogy: the general waits for the best moment — when price is
  between levels, neither side is committed; as price approaches
  support/resistance, both sides engage, and one must lose
- Practical implication: small price difference between a trade working and
  not working → cost of being wrong is lower → good entry points
- Example: countertrend buyer at $2.10 places stop 6¢ below;
  trend-following seller at $2.10 places stop $0.05–$0.06 above entry
- **Edges come from systematic misperceptions caused by cognitive biases**;
  good traders bet on the winning side and exit quickly when wrong

## Chapter 7: By What Measure?

> *"Mature understanding of and respect for risk is the hallmark of the
> best traders. They know that if you don't keep an eye on risk, it will
> set its eye on you."*

### Introduction: The Risk Measurement Problem
- The key question for any trading system or fund: "How can you know
  whether a system or manager is a good one?"
- Industry answer: highest **risk/reward ratio** — but there are many
  competing definitions of both risk and reward
- Danger: industry sometimes defines risk so narrowly that entire categories
  of risk are ignored — and those ignored risks are just as likely to hurt
- **Long-Term Capital Management** cited as the prime example of risks
  outside traditional measures that caused catastrophic losses

### Richard Dennis and Position Size Risk
- **Richard Dennis** and **Bill Eckhardt** were deeply concerned with
  position size because oversized positions + adverse price move = total ruin
- Prior to the Turtle program, Dennis had traded during a period when the
  silver market was **locked limit down for days** — no exit possible
  because no buyers within COMEX daily price change limits
- Dennis trimmed his position before the silver lockdown became fatal,
  likely saving tens of millions of dollars
- During the Turtle program, Dennis constantly monitored Turtles' positions
  and reduced his own when aggregate risk felt too high — contrary to his
  "gunslinger" reputation, he was very careful with risk

### The Four Primary Risks

- **Drawdowns**: strings of losses that reduce capital
- **Low returns**: periods of small gains insufficient to live on
- **Price shocks**: sudden movements causing large unrecoverable losses
- **System death**: market dynamics change, causing a previously profitable
  system to start losing money

### Drawdowns
- **Drawdown** is the biggest reason most traders quit and end up net losers
- Example (Donchian Trend system, $100K, Jan 1996–May 2006):
  - CAGR of **43.7%** over 10+ years
  - But included a **38% drawdown** period
- Logarithmic scale on equity curves makes drawdowns *look smaller* than
  they are — new traders systematically underestimate the psychological pain
- "John Newbie" starts trading June 1, hits a **42% drawdown** (slightly
  worse than historical max) almost immediately → fear spiral:
  - "What if the system has stopped working?"
  - "What if this is just the beginning of an even larger drawdown?"
  - Result: stops trading or selectively skips trades → misses winners →
    quits after losing half the account
- **Root cause of new trader failure**: overestimating ability to withstand
  large fluctuations at aggressive risk levels
- Dennis distinguished drawdowns from profit giveback: giving back profits
  was part of the game for trend followers, not the same as a loss streak
- Author's personal worst drawdown as a Turtle: **~70%** — very few people
  can psychologically sustain that
- Successful former Turtles who raise institutional money trade at greatly
  reduced risk levels — almost a requirement for raising outside capital

### Low Returns
- Two systems with same 30% CAGR but different smoothness: one returns 30%
  every year; other returns 5%, 5%, 100% in consecutive years
- The smoother system is preferable and *also* more likely to deliver good
  returns in future periods — consistent delivery is itself a positive signal
- Risk of subpar returns is lower for systems with historically smooth returns

### Price Shocks
- **Price shock**: sudden/rapid move caused by natural catastrophe,
  political event, or economic disaster
- Two notable examples since author began trading:
  1. **U.S. stock market crash of 1987** (Black Monday)
  2. **September 11, 2001**
- Author's experience on **Black Monday, October 19, 1987**:
  - Was short ~1,200 contracts of December eurodollars + 600 T-bills
  - Had significant long positions in gold, silver, and currencies
  - Made money on crash day itself
  - Next morning: eurodollar opened at **92.85** (vs. close at 90.64) —
    more than 2 points higher, ~$5,500/contract, with no exit opportunity
  - Gold opened down $25, silver down over $1
  - Total loss: **~$11 million on a $20 million account** — entire year's
    profits vanished *overnight*
  - Cause: government lowered interest rates overnight with no warning
- Donchian Trend backtest showed **65% drawdown** spike from the 1987 shock
  — that drawdown was *twice* anything historical testing would have predicted
- **Historical testing systematically understates price shock drawdowns**
  by approximately 2× because there was no opportunity to exit

### System Death
- **System death**: a system that worked (or appeared to work in backtests)
  suddenly stops functioning and starts losing money
- More common with short-term systems optimized for recent price action
- Difficult to distinguish from a normal drawdown — the greatest source of
  anxiety for new traders
- Markets are dynamic; changes can be permanent — but great traders
  distinguish themselves by adhering to methods others have abandoned
- Counterintuitive cycle: when trend following appears dead, experts declare
  its end; money flees trend-following funds → strategies become less crowded
  → trend following becomes profitable again, often spectacularly so
- "At least three or four times since the Turtle program began someone has
  made the claim that trend following has ceased to work. I always laugh at
  this, knowing that profitable markets are most likely quite near."

### Measuring What You Cannot See — Risk Metrics
1. **Maximum drawdown**: highest % loss from peak to equity low during test
   (e.g., 65% in the 1987 crash scenario)
2. **Longest drawdown**: largest period from equity peak to subsequent new
   peak — measures how long to recover from a losing streak
3. **Standard deviation of returns**: dispersion of returns; high SD means
   returns vary significantly month to month
4. **R-squared**: smoothness of fit to the CAGR% trend line; fixed-income
   account = 1.0; erratic returns < 1.0

### The Flip Side of Risk: Reward Metrics
- **CAGR%** (compound annual growth rate / geometric average return):
  reflects the compounding growth rate that would produce the actual ending
  equity; can be skewed by a single exceptional period
- **Average one-year trailing return**: rolling one-year average; less
  sensitive to a single outlier than CAGR; better proxy for "typical" year
- **Average monthly return**: simple average of each month's returns
- Recommendation: examine the equity curve *visually* alongside a graph
  of monthly return distribution — more revealing than any single number

### Measuring Risk versus Reward
- Most common unified measures in the futures fund industry:
  the **Sharpe ratio** and the **MAR ratio**

### The Sharpe Ratio
- Invented by Nobel laureate **William F. Sharpe** in 1966 as a
  reward-to-variability measure for comparing **mutual funds**
- Formula: (CAGR% − risk-free rate) ÷ standard deviation of returns
- Excellent for comparing stock portfolio managers; insufficient for futures
  and commodities hedge funds because futures differ from mutual funds in
  four key ways:
  - **Management style risk**: frequent trading can lose money far faster
    than buy-and-hold
  - **Diversification strategy risk**: futures funds concentrate assets in
    fewer instruments at any time
  - **Exposure**: futures use higher leverage, amplifying market fluctuations
  - **Confidence risk**: limited track records increase uncertainty about
    future returns

> *"Smoothness does not equal risk!"*

- Author's core contrarian claim: there is an **inverse relationship**
  between smoothness of returns and actual risk in many instances
- Smooth returns can hide catastrophic tail risk — LTCM had an excellent
  Sharpe ratio right up until it lost nearly the entire $4.7 billion fund

### When Genius Failed — LTCM as Case Study
- **Long-Term Capital Management (LTCM)** used extreme leverage on
  fixed-income convergence trades; positions became so large that LTCM
  could not exit when losses began
- Russian bond default in 1998 triggered adverse price movement; rest of
  market knew LTCM would eventually be forced to unwind → kept moving
  prices against LTCM's positions → nearly wiped out the entire fund
- Before collapse: LTCM averaged **~40% annual returns** distributed
  very smoothly → excellent Sharpe ratio → perceived as "safe"
- Reference: *When Genius Failed* by **Roger Lowenstein**

## Chapter 7 (continued): Measuring System Performance

### Hidden Risk in Smooth Returns
- **Amaranth Advisors** lost ~65% of its $9 billion fund in two months
  on natural gas trades — positions were massive relative to market size;
  had previously shown an excellent Sharpe ratio
- Hedge funds selling **out-of-the-money options** generate smooth,
  consistent returns — but carry catastrophic tail risk invisible in
  standard metrics
- Writing eurodollar options in 1987 could have produced a single-day
  loss exceeding the entire fund value
- Investors are "seduced by steady returns and multiyear track records
  of funds that have not yet experienced a truly bad day"

### The MAR Ratio
- **MAR ratio** = annual return ÷ maximum drawdown (using month-end figures)
  — devised by **Managed Accounts Reports, LLC**
- Quick, dirty, useful filter for poorly performing strategies
- Donchian Trend system MAR = 1.22 (Jan 1996–Jun 2006):
  CAGR 27.38%, max drawdown 22.35% (month-end)
- Author prefers peak-to-trough daily drawdown: actual max was 27.58%,
  giving a truer MAR of 0.99 — month-end figures systematically
  **understate** true drawdown

### System Death Risk
- Strategies with the best historical risk/reward attract the most
  capital → billions chase the same trades → liquidity runs out →
  **early system death**
- Arbitrage is the clearest example: pure arb is near risk-free, but
  competition compresses spreads until the strategy dies
- Trend following survives long-term *because* most investors dislike it:
  large drawdowns and equity swings keep imitators away
- Trend following returns are **cyclical**: influx of capital after good
  periods → tough years → withdrawals → returns recover

> "Be careful what you look for: if you get too greedy when examining a
> strategy, you are going to increase the chances that you will not get
> the results you seek."

### Everybody's Different
- No single metric universally applies; author combines MAR ratio,
  drawdowns, overall return, Sharpe ratio, and *R*-squared
- Doesn't fixate on specific figures — a MAR of 1.5 today doesn't
  guarantee 1.5 in the future; use metrics as rough guidance only

## Chapter 8: Risk and Money Management

> *Ruin is the risk you should be concerned with the most. It can come
> like a thief in the night and steal everything if you aren't watching
> carefully.*

### What Money Management Is
- **Money management** (from gambling theory): keeping **risk of ruin**
  at acceptable levels while maximizing profit by choosing position
  *size* and limiting aggregate exposure to price shocks
- Not a science — "more art than religion than art"; no universal right
  answers, only individually appropriate ones
- Core trade-off: too much risk → lose everything or quit; too little
  risk → leave too much money on the table
- Two ways excessive risk forces you out:
  1. Extended drawdowns exceeding psychological limits
  2. Sudden price shock wiping out the account

### The Reality of Risk
- Trading system vendors lie: "The risk is real, and trading is not easy"
- Power of compounding: $50,000 at 30%/year → ~$10 million in 20 years
  — but only if you never blow up and restart
- 100–200% return targets dramatically increase blowup probability;
  conservative approach strongly recommended for the first several years
- Donchian Trend at 3% risk-per-trade in 1987: would have gone bust
  overnight on the single day interest-rate markets reversed
- **Prudent rule**: target drawdown level in simulations should be at
  most *half* the drawdown you believe you can tolerate — leaves buffer
  for out-of-sample shocks

### Don't Believe Everything You Hear
- Money management is simple, not complicated
- Natural gas (NG) ATR example: $7,500/contract daily move; 2-ATR stop
  = $15,000 loss; for a $50,000 account = 30% at risk on one trade →
  prudent NG position size is **zero** contracts for accounts under $1M
- Trading with too much risk is "the single most common reason for
  failure among new traders"

### Risk of Ruin Revisited
- Classical ruin formula assumes random adverse outcomes — author
  disagrees with this framing
- Real causes of trader failure:
  - **No plan**: trading on hunches, rumors, belief in price-prediction
  - **Too much risk**: not 50% more than prudent — some traders run
    5–10× what the author considers aggressive
  - **Unrealistic expectations**: doctors and dentists disproportionately
    drawn to futures trading; intelligence + success elsewhere breeds
    overconfidence; trading is *simple* but not *easy*
- Turtles taught the same rules in two weeks — some still failed because
  they did not follow the methods

### Turtle Money Management: Staying in the Game
- Primary goal: **stay in the game** — positive expectancy systems make
  you rich only if you continue trading
- Two deaths: slow painful death (anguish/frustration → quit) and
  **blowup** (rapid account wipeout)
- New traders routinely overestimate drawdown tolerance; stopping after
  a drawdown — worst possible time — is the typical result
- Turtle Way: risk the same dollar amount in each market by
  **normalizing for volatility** — prevents one high-volatility market
  from dominating results
- Trading one contract per market without volatility normalization is
  dangerously misleading: a large gain in a small contract may not
  offset a small loss in a large one

### The N Factor (Volatility-Based Position Sizing)
- **Rich Dennis** and **Bill Eckhardt** sized positions by daily ATR
  (called *N*), so each market moved ~the same dollar amount per day
- Unit size formula: number of contracts where 1-ATR = 1% of account
  - $1M account → 1-ATR = $10,000; divide market's 1-ATR dollar value
    into $10,000 to get contracts per unit
- Key insight from October 1987: stops don't protect against overnight
  gaps — the market gapped *through* all stops
  - Author used 1.5-ATR stops vs. typical Turtle 2-ATR stops; if sized
    purely on stop distance, would have held 4× the position and lost
    4× as much
  - Volatility-based sizing meant he held same relative position as other
    Turtles — **survived intact**

### Unit Limits and Correlation Risk
- **Rich and Bill imposed overall risk limits**:
  - Max 4 units per single market
  - Max 6 units in highly correlated markets
  - Max 10 units in any one direction (long or short); 12 if in
    uncorrelated markets
- October 1987: almost every market moved against them simultaneously —
  these limits "probably saved Rich more than $100 million that day"
- Interest-rate markets (eurodollars, T-bonds, T-bills, 2-yr notes):
  all signal together during a move; unit limits meant holding only the
  first two that signaled — later entrants more likely to lose
- **Unit limits also filtered lagging markets** — a critical function
  often missed by people claiming to replicate the Turtle system

### Rules for Estimating Risk
- Best method: stress-test against major historical price shocks of the
  last 30–50 years using simulation software
- Also plan for unprecedented disasters (nuclear attack on a financial
  center, etc.) — aggressive traders will almost certainly be wiped out
  by such an event

## Chapter 9: Turtle-Style Building Blocks

> *Don't spend all your time admiring the fancy tools in the magazines.
> First learn how to use the basic ones well. It's not the size of your
> tools that counts but how well you use them.*

### What Building Blocks Are
- **Building blocks**: tools that indicate market state (stable/quiet,
  stable/volatile, trending/quiet, trending/volatile)
- Subcategories: indicators, oscillators, ratios — all grouped under
  one umbrella
- No building block works all the time; goal is to find tools that
  improve the odds that a trend has started or ended
- "It is possible to make good returns even when the odds are only
  slightly in your favor (ask your favorite casino owner)"

### The Five Core Building Blocks
1. **Breakouts** — price exceeds highest high or lowest low for N days;
   primary tool of the original Turtle system; shorter N = shorter-term
   trend signal; longer N = longer-term; works best combined with a
   trend filter (as in the Donchian Trend system)
2. **Moving averages** — continuously calculated price averages;
   *simple moving average* = plain mean over N days; *exponential
   moving average* (EMA) = weighted blend of prior average and current
   price; MA crossover (faster crosses slower) is a common entry signal;
   extra complexity beyond basic MAs adds curve-fitting risk without
   meaningful edge improvement
3. **Volatility channels** — moving average ± a volatility measure
   (ATR or standard deviation); price breaking above the upper band
   signals possible trend start; two systems built on this discussed in
   Chapter 10
4. **Time-based exits** — exit after a fixed number of days (e.g., 10
   or 80); can smooth drawdowns by triggering before moving-average or
   breakout signals catch the reversal
5. **Simple lookbacks** — compare current price to price N days ago;
   can be enhanced with ATR filter (e.g., buy if price exceeds price
   100 days ago by 2 ATR)

### Don't Chase Indicators
- All trend-following building blocks are fundamentally interchangeable
  — tuned appropriately, they all respond to the same market moves
- Better to master simple, robust building blocks than search for the
  "perfect nuclear-powered indicator that works in past years' markets"

## Chapter 10 (continued): Turtle-Style Systems Tested

### The Case for Historical Testing

- Anti-backtesting argument ("the past won't repeat") misses the point: even
  discretionary traders rely on their personal experience of past price action
- All trading knowledge is ultimately derived from the past; the only question
  is whether you process it systematically or informally
- Computer simulation allows more rigorous pre-trade analysis; better to find
  that a promising idea fails in simulation than in a live account
- Main risk of backtesting is **overoptimization** — computers make it easy to
  find methods that look good historically but won't hold up (covered Ch. 11)

> "A historical simulation does not predict what you will encounter in future
> trading, but it will give you a way to determine whether an approach is
> likely to be profitable in the future."

### The Myth of the Expert

- In most fields, **pseudo-experts** vastly outnumber true experts; they are
  indistinguishable to outsiders but don't actually *understand* the field
- True experts have no need for rigid rules — they understand deeply
- Pseudo-experts copy what experts *do* without knowing *why*, then codify
  behavior into rigid rules where none were intended
- Tell-tale sign: unclear writing = unclear thinking; true experts can explain
  complex ideas simply
- Trading pseudo-expert example: someone who runs 1,000-trade simulations from
  two weeks of short-term data and draws confident conclusions — the math is
  done correctly but the sample is meaningless

> "Don't confuse experience with expertise or knowledge with wisdom."

### Common Building Blocks for System Tests

**Markets tested:** AUD, GBP, corn, cocoa, CAD, crude oil, cotton, EUR,
eurodollar, feeder cattle, gold, copper, heating oil, unleaded gas, JPY,
coffee, cattle, hogs, MXN, natural gas, soybeans, sugar, CHF, silver,
Treasury notes, Treasury bonds, wheat
- Selected from liquid U.S. markets; highly correlated markets excluded
- U.S.-only to allow easy replication (foreign data sold separately)

**Money management:** Half as aggressive as original Turtle sizing
- 1 ATR = 0.5% of equity (vs. 1% for Turtles)
- Contracts = (0.5% × equity) ÷ (ATR in dollars)

**Test period:** January 1996 – June 2006

### The Six Systems Tested

#### ATR Channel Breakout
- **Volatility channel** using ATR as the width measure
- Channel top = 350-day MA + 7 ATR; channel bottom = 350-day MA − 3 ATR
- Enter long on open if prior close > channel top; short if prior close <
  channel bottom
- Exit when close crosses back through the 350-day moving average
- Variant popularized as the **PGO (Pretty Good Oscillator)** by trader
  **Mark Johnson** on **Chuck LeBeau's** System Trader's Club forum

#### Bollinger Breakout
- Described by **Chuck LeBeau** and **David Lucas** in *Technical Traders
  Guide to Computer Analysis of the Futures Markets* (1992)
- **Bollinger band** (invented by **John Bollinger**): ±2.5 standard
  deviations around a 350-day moving average
- Same entry/exit logic as ATR Channel Breakout (close vs. band edges;
  exit at 350-day MA)

#### Donchian Trend
- Simplified version of original Turtle system (described in Ch. 5)
- Entry: 20-day breakout; Exit: 10-day breakout
- **Trend filter:** 350-day / 25-day exponential MA — trades only allowed in
  direction of faster MA relative to slower MA
- Uses 2-ATR stop (same as original Turtle)

#### Donchian Trend with Time Exit
- Same entry as Donchian Trend but **exits after 80 days flat** — no stops
- Author's direct test of the "entries don't matter, only exits do" claim
- Deliberately simple: if it performs well vs. sophisticated exits, entry
  edge is doing the work

#### Dual Moving Average
- Buys/sells when **100-day MA crosses 350-day MA**
- Always in the market (long or short) — exits only occur when the cross
  reverses and a new trade opens in the opposite direction
- Very long-term, low-frequency system

#### Triple Moving Average
- Three MAs: **150-day, 250-day, 350-day**
- Trades when 150-day crosses 250-day; 350-day acts as trend filter
- Both 150 and 250 must be on same side of 350-day for a trade to trigger
- Not always in the market (unlike Dual MA); exits when 150 crosses back
  through 250

  only exits do"; a good entry with *any* reasonable exit can be profitable
- **Dual MA beat Triple MA** despite being simpler — complexity does not
  equal better performance
- **Donchian Trend** lagged badly vs. the others — breakout edge has eroded
  since the Turtle era (author attributes this to **trader effects**, Ch. 11)
- Three systems (Dual MA, Triple MA, Donchian Time) have **no stops at all**
  yet match or beat systems with stops on every metric

### Adding Stops: A Counterintuitive Finding

- Testing Dual MA with stops at various ATR widths: **zero-stop case had
  the best MAR, CAGR, Sharpe, drawdown, and drawdown length** — every metric
- Same result for Triple MA: every single measure worse with any stop added
- Donchian Time: stops hurt except at very large widths (≥10 ATR), where
  performance approaches the no-stop case
- **Why?** For trend followers, drawdowns come from **giving back profits at
  trend reversals** — not from individual losing trades. Adding stops doesn't
  reduce this profit-giveback drawdown; it only removes good trades early
- The Turtles understood this: surrendering profits accumulated in a trend
  is an unavoidable structural feature of trend-following, not a flaw to be
  stopped out of

> "It is not entry risk that causes Draw downs for trend followers; it is
> giving back profits."

## Chapter 11: Lies, Damn Lies, and Backtests

> *"Charlatans and scoundrels lurk in dark corners, awaiting the
> unsuspecting. Don't be their prey."*

### The Backtest Fraud Problem

- Trading system ads promise extraordinary returns from historical simulation;
  many vendors knowingly sell systems with inflated backtest results
- Even well-intentioned vendors often don't understand the limits of
  historical testing or why live results diverge from backtests
- Experienced traders know their systems underperform backtests but often
  don't know *why* — they compensate by discounting results without
  understanding the cause

### Four Sources of Backtest-to-Live Divergence

1. **Trader effects** — a profitable method noticed by many traders gets
   crowded, reducing or eliminating the edge
2. **Random effects** — historical results may be better than the true
   underlying edge due to pure luck
3. **Optimization paradox** — the act of selecting a specific parameter
   (e.g., 25-day vs. 30-day MA) reduces the predictive value of the backtest
4. **Overfitting / curve fitting** — a system tuned too closely to historical
   data has no generalizability; slight shifts in market behavior produce
   dramatically worse results

### Trader Effects (Deep Dive)

- Analogous to physics **observer effect**: the act of trading changes the
  market conditions the trade depends on
- Any strategy that works well and becomes widely known will attract
  imitators; enough imitators destroy the edge
- **Breakout front-running example:** If you know large traders will buy at a
  breakout level, you can buy ahead of them, drive the price to their trigger,
  sell back to them for a guaranteed profit
  - Gold example: large buyer (ACME) has 1,000 contracts at $410.50; front-
    runner buys at $409, causes price to reach $411, sells 1,000 to ACME at
    $411, dumps extra 100 at $407 — net profit ~$160,000 in seconds
  - The breakout that appeared in the backtest was manufactured by the front-
    runner; ACME now holds a position entered for the wrong reason
- A real system that became enormously popular (hundreds of millions traded)
  suffered a prolonged drawdown "much longer and higher than anything in 20
  years of backtesting" — traders had learned to front-run its predictable
  entry signals; illiquid markets in its portfolio made it even more
  vulnerable to anticipatory buying

- **Turtles' own countermeasure:** Deliberately varied order timing and size;
  some used small stops, some large; some bought exactly at breakout, some
  just before or after — created enough obfuscation to protect **Richard
  Dennis**'s fills
- Author's personal tactic: occasionally placing **fake orders** in the
  opposite direction to move the market before canceling and entering the
  real order at a better price — "like bluffing in poker"

> "Other traders are not that dense. They will exploit any repeated patterns
> that they notice. This is one of the reasons why it is better to develop
> your own system."

- Trader effects can arise without conscious front-running: if enough
  traders independently adopt the same strategy, the collective action
  changes the market dynamics that made the strategy work in the first place

## Chapter 11 (continued): Why Backtests Lie

### Random Effects

- Most traders — and even pension/hedge fund managers — drastically underestimate how much **purely random chance** can affect trading results
- Simulation: 100 tests using a coin-flip entry (random long/short at open) with a time-based exit of 20–120 days
  - Best result: **16.9% CAGR**, turned $1M → $5.5M over 10.5 years
  - Worst result: **–20% per year**
  - Conclusion: enormous variance exists even with zero edge
- Adding a **trend filter** (Donchian-style) to the random system raised average return to **32.46%**, cut avg drawdown to **43.74%**, but huge variance remained
  - Best of 100 random+trend tests: **53.3% return**, MAR 1.58, max DD 33.6%
  - Worst: **17.5% return**, DD 62.7%

> "Luck or random effects play a large role in the performance of actual traders and actual funds even though the best traders do not like to admit that to their investors." — Curtis Faith

- **The core problem**: it's impossible to distinguish a great trader with average luck from an average trader with excellent luck by looking only at track records
- Random effects are too large for track records alone to be definitive

### Lucky Genes

- Analogy: genes explain why children of tall parents tend to be tall but rarely as tall — **reversion to the mean** (the *regression effect*)
- A lucky gene combination produces a tall child, but luck in gene arrangement is not heritable — offspring regress toward average
- Same dynamic applies to trading performance: lucky traders will revert, not continue to outperform

### Bad News for Investors

- In a universe of 1,000 traders where only 5–6 are truly excellent, ~800 are average (not unlucky, just average)
- If **2% of average traders get lucky** enough to post a 10-year track record, that creates ~16 lucky average traders — outnumbering the 5–6 excellent ones
- Result: out of ~21 traders with excellent 10-year records, only ~¼ are actually excellent
- Investors using track records to select managers will mostly pick lucky average traders

### Luck and Time

- Time is the corrective: lucky traders revert; truly excellent traders persist
- 5-year track records are near-useless — the ratio of lucky-to-excellent traders is far worse
- Short-term data (3.5 years, Jan 2003–Jun 2006):
  - Random entry system averaged **35% return**, MAR 1.06
  - Real systems: Triple MA = 48.5% (MAR 1.50), Bollinger Breakout = 52.2% (MAR 1.54), Dual MA = 49.7% (MAR 1.25)
  - Of 100 random simulations, **17 beat MAR 1.54**; **7 beat 52.2% return**
  - Single best random trader: **71.4% return**, DD 34.5%, MAR 2.07
- Implication: a 3-year record with great numbers is close to meaningless
- **Good investors invest in people, not historical performance** — identify traits that predict future excellence

### Optimization

- **Optimization**: the process of finding the best numeric values (**parameters**) for a trading system — e.g., number of days in a moving average
- Common view: "optimization is bad because it leads to curve fitting" — Faith calls this "bunk"
- **Optimization is good when done correctly**: it reveals how system performance changes as parameters vary, exposing overfitting and random effects

### The Optimization Paradox

- The **optimization paradox**: optimizing parameters makes a system *more likely* to perform well in the future but *less likely* to perform as well as historical tests indicate
- Optimization improves true expected performance while degrading the predictive accuracy of the simulation metrics
- Bollinger Breakout example — entry threshold (channel width in std devs) varied from 1.0 to 4.0:
  - Peak MAR ratio at **2.4 std deviations**
  - Using arbitrary 3.0 std dev (from stats class intuition) would have meant **8× less money** over 10.5 years: 54.5% vs 28.2% return
- Moving average days parameter: peak at **350-day** moving average; any value above or below lowers MAR
- Exit threshold parameter: peak at **–0.8 std deviations** from the moving average

### The Basis of Predictive Value

- Historical tests have predictive value only to the extent the future resembles the past
- A test with all optimized parameters represents the **best-case view of the past** — trades that would have resulted from the single best parameter set
- **Points A vs B on the performance mountain**:
  - Point A (sub-optimal): future shifts of the optimal left or right give roughly symmetric error — equally likely to over- or under-predict
  - Point B (optimal peak): any future shift in either direction produces worse performance → test result **always over-predicts future**
- Even so: don't use value A. Values near B are still higher than values near A even after a drift. Use the optimized value but expect reality to fall short of the backtest

### Overfitting or Curve Fitting

- **Overfitting**: adding rules to a system that improve historical performance but apply to only a tiny number of trades
- System vendors often sell "Plus" or "II" versions of systems after poor periods — these frequently contain overfit rules targeting specific past drawdowns

**Overfitting demonstration with Dual MA system**:
- System had a bad drawdown in the last 6 months of test data
- Added rule: reduce positions by 90% when drawdown reaches 38% threshold
- Result: returns improved from 41.4% → 45.7%, drawdown from 56.0% → 39.2%, MAR 0.74 → 1.17
- **The catch**: the rule only fires *once* in the entire test — at the very end, where the drawdown happened to sit
- **Cliff phenomenon**: varying the drawdown threshold from 37% to 38% swings performance from **–0.4% to +45.7% per year** — a 1% parameter change creates a catastrophic cliff
- **Cliffs are diagnostic**: large performance swings from tiny parameter changes = definitive sign of data overfitting

### The Importance of Sample Size

- Primary cause of overfitting: rules that apply to very few instances — **small sample size** for that specific rule
- Example: seasonality rule tested on 10 years of data has at most 10 instances of any seasonal pattern — statistically worthless
- Demonstration: searched 4,000 tests for seasonal patterns; found that reducing positions 96% for the first 2 days of September and first 25 days of July improved MAR from 1.17 → 1.48 and returns from 45.7% → 58.2%
- **Reality**: these seasonal patterns only worked because those months happened to coincide with past drawdowns — no reason to expect recurrence
- True system metrics: 41.4% return, 56.0% drawdown, MAR 0.74 — not 58.2%, 39.4%, 1.48

## Chapter 12: On Solid Ground

> *"Trading with poor methods is like learning to juggle while standing in a rowboat during a storm. Sure, it can be done, but it is much easier to juggle when one is standing on solid ground."*

- Chapter 11 covered why backtests lie; Chapter 12 covers how to test correctly
- Historical simulation gives only a **rough sense** of the future — but a rough edge is enough for a good trader to make money
- Key concept: understanding the **degree of roughness** (margin of error) in your estimates

### Statistical Basis for Testing

- Historical testing works via **inferential statistics**: using a sample to draw conclusions about a larger population
- Analogy: pollsters poll 500 people to infer behavior of millions — works if sample is representative
- Two critical factors for statistical validity:
  1. **Sample size** — total number of trades (but also how many instances of each specific rule)
  2. **Representativeness** — does the historical sample reflect what future markets will look like?
- Common trader mistake: understanding sample size only as "number of trades" while ignoring whether specific rules have enough instances
- Testing only recent history = polling at the Democratic National Convention — you get an answer, but not the right one
- Tests covering only a short period may capture only one or two **market states** (stable, volatile, trending, choppy) — the system may fail badly when market state shifts

### Existing Measures Are Not Robust

- Standard performance measures — **MAR ratio, CAGR%, Sharpe ratio** — are not robust: they are too sensitive to start/end dates and small changes in data
- A **robust statistic** is one where changing a small part of the data set does not significantly change the statistic
- Non-robust measures: small changes in a few trades can produce large swings in MAR, making it easy to fool yourself
- **Bill Eckhardt** asked Faith at his Turtle interview: "Do you know what a robust statistical estimator is?" — Faith admitted he had no idea
  - This question revealed how far ahead of the industry **Richard Dennis** and **Bill Eckhardt** were in 1983
  - The branch of mathematics addressing imperfect information: **robust statistics**
- Illustration: shifting test dates by 1–2 months (start Feb 1 instead of Jan 1, end Apr 30 instead of Jun 30) can dramatically change MAR and CAGR — because these measures are sensitive to which trades fall inside the window

## Chapter 12 (continued): Measuring Your System's Performance

### Why CAGR% and MAR Are Fragile

- **Triple Moving Average** system: changing start/stop dates boosted return
  from 43.2% → 46.2%, MAR 1.39 → 1.61, Sharpe 1.25 → 1.37
- **ATR Channel Breakout**: original dates 51.7% / MAR 1.31; revised 54.9% / MAR 1.49
- Root cause: **CAGR%** is the slope of a log-graph line from start to end —
  shifting dates eliminates drawdowns at either end, steepening that slope
- **MAR ratio** is doubly sensitive: both numerator (return) and denominator
  (max drawdown) react to start/end date changes — errors compound
- Maximum drawdown is a single point on the equity curve; if that point sits
  near a test boundary, shifting dates can eliminate it entirely

### Regressed Annual Return (RAR%)

- **RAR%** = the slope of a **linear regression** (best-fit line) through all
  points on the log equity curve, not just start-to-end
- Because it uses all data points, it is far less sensitive to endpoint changes
- Example: same ATR CBO test — CAGR% changed 3.0 pct points (43.2→46.2),
  RAR% changed only 0.11 pct points (54.67→54.78)
- CAGR% was ~30× more sensitive to end-date changes than RAR% in this case
- RAR% should replace CAGR% in the numerator of risk/reward ratios

### R-Cubed: A New Risk/Reward Measure

- **R-cubed** (also called **RRRR — robust risk/reward ratio**) = RAR% ÷
  **length-adjusted average maximum drawdown**
- Denominator has two components:
  1. **Average maximum drawdown**: average of the five largest drawdowns
  2. **Length adjustment**: average of the five longest drawdown durations
     (in days ÷ 365), multiplied by the average max drawdown
- Example: RAR% = 50%, avg max DD = 25%, avg DD length = 1 year (365 days)
  → R-cubed = 50 / (25 × 365/365) = **2.0**
- Advantages over MAR ratio:
  - Uses 5 drawdowns instead of 1 — dilutes the effect of a single lucky/unlucky event
  - Captures duration, not just severity — a 30% DD lasting 2 months ≠ one lasting 2 years
  - Less sensitive to start/end date changes

### Robust Sharpe Ratio

- **Robust Sharpe** = RAR% ÷ annualized standard deviation of monthly returns
- Same logic: RAR% in numerator makes it less endpoint-sensitive than classic Sharpe

### Robust Measures and Overfitting

- Non-robust measures are more susceptible to luck: a trader who happened to
  miss the largest drawdown (e.g., was on vacation) shows inflated MAR
- Adding a drawdown-cutting rule to Dual Moving Average: CAGR% rose 41.4→45.7%
  (+10.3%), MAR jumped 0.74→1.17 (+60%) — but RAR% barely moved 53.5→53.75
  (+0.4%) and R-cubed changed 3.29→3.86 (+17.3%)
- Robust measures resist showing large improvements from changes affecting only
  a small number of trades — the hallmark of curve fitting

### Representative Samples

Two factors determine whether backtest results will represent future performance:
- **Number of markets**: more markets → more market states covered
- **Duration of test**: longer periods → more regime changes included

> "Be young, but don't be foolish: Study history."

- Young traders mistake recent conditions for universal market behavior;
  internet-boom day traders failed because their methods only worked in
  that bubble — testing across history would have revealed the dependency
- Recommendation: test all available data; data is cheap, losses from
  untested edge cases are expensive

### Sample Size

- No magic number; larger is always better, smaller always worse
- <20 trades: large degree of error; >100: much more predictive; several hundred: probably sufficient
- Trading data doesn't follow nice normal distributions — academic sample-size
  formulas don't apply directly
- Key challenge: **infrequent rules** (e.g., behavior at end of large price
  bubbles) cannot accumulate enough samples — tests have low statistical meaning
- Generalize infrequent rules so they trigger more often to increase sample size
- Two practices that compound small-sample problems:
  - **Single-market optimization**: each market optimized separately → far fewer trades per rule
  - **Complex systems**: many rules make it impossible to assess how often each rule fired

### Back to the Future

- The only honest answer to "what will this system earn?" is: **you cannot know**
- Best you can do: use tools that show a *range* of potential values and the
  factors that influence those values

#### Lucky Systems
- Systems that recently performed well may have benefited from ideal market
  conditions — do not expect that luck to repeat
- After good periods, expect difficult periods; mean-reversion of luck is real

#### Parameter Scrambling
- Before trading any system, change its parameters by **20–25%** of their values
  (move to a point well down the optimization curve)
- Bollinger Breakout example: moving from optimal (350-day MA, –0.8 exit) to
  scrambled (250-day, 0.0 exit) dropped RAR% from 59→58% but R-cubed from 3.67→2.18
- A dramatic drop from modest parameter changes signals fragility — expect
  similar degradation in live trading

#### Rolling Optimization Windows
- **Rolling optimization**: pick a date 8–10 years in the past, optimize on all
  prior data, then test on the 2 years immediately following
- Repeat for multiple starting dates; compare how out-of-sample performance
  compares with in-sample test
- **Table 12-4** (Bollinger Breakout, five 10-year windows):
  - RAR% actual varied from test value by –8.3% to +6.3%
  - R-cubed actual varied from test value by –29.5% to –5.0%
  - Optimal parameters differed for every period — illustrates imprecision of testing

### Monte Carlo Simulation

- **Monte Carlo simulation**: uses random numbers to generate many "alternative
  universes" from actual historical price/trade data
- Named for Monaco's casinos; method was pioneered during the **Manhattan Project**
  by **Richard Feynman** to model uranium fission reactions without being able
  to solve equations analytically — random sampling revealed distribution of outcomes
- Application to trading: can't predict the future exactly, but can map the
  distribution of plausible outcomes

#### Two Monte Carlo Methods

1. **Trade scrambling**: randomly reorder and re-date actual trades; rebuild
   equity curve from scrambled sequence
2. **Equity curve scrambling**: randomly assemble chunks of the original equity
   curve into new curves

- **Equity curve scrambling is superior** — trade scrambling understates drawdowns
  because it breaks the correlation between simultaneous losing trades
- At the tail end of large trends, correlated markets all reverse together
  (gold, silver, sugar all fell together in spring 2006); trade scrambling
  separates those losses, making DD appear smaller than it really is
- Same dynamic in the **1987 crash**: eurodollar gap caused normally uncorrelated
  markets to gap against positions simultaneously — trade scrambling would dilute this
- Author's method at **Trading Blox**: scramble 20-day chunks of the equity curve
  (not single days) to preserve **serial correlation** (bad days cluster) →
  more realistic drawdown simulation

#### Monte Carlo Reports

- Generate 2,000 alternative equity curves; plot distribution of RAR% values
- Example: 90% of 2,000 simulated curves showed RAR% > 42% — gives lower-bound confidence
- **Critical warning**: Monte Carlo does not fix a bad backtest — if your base
  simulation is overfit and RAR% is overstated by 20%, the Monte Carlo
  distribution will also be overstated by that same amount

### Some Like It Rough

- Backtest = "at best a rough approximation" of future performance
- Robust measures are better predictors but still imprecise

> "Anyone who tells you that you can expect to see a particular level of
> performance is lying or does not know what he is talking about. If he is
> trying to sell you something, I strongly suspect the former."

## Chapter 13: Bulletproof Systems

> *Trading is not a sprint; it's boxing. The market will beat you up, screw
> with your head, and do anything it can to defeat you. But when the bell
> sounds at the end of the twelfth round, you must be standing in the ring
> in order to win.*

### The New Trader Fallacy

- New traders pick the system with the best historical metrics (e.g., "Omega"
  with +10% CAGR and +0.2 MAR over "Alpha") and assume future superiority
- Flaw: Omega may have outperformed only because past conditions happened to
  favor it; if future market regimes differ, the gap disappears
- Example: Omega better in quiet trends, Alpha better in volatile trends;
  if historical test had 13 quiet years and 7 volatile, Omega looks superior —
  but if future skews volatile, Alpha wins

### The Unknowable Future

- Even a long sequence like VQQVQVVQQQQVVQ doesn't provide enough data to
  reliably predict future market-type distribution
- The honest conclusion: we do not know what future market conditions will be
  and cannot predict relative system performance accurately
- **Mature response**: accept the unknowability; build a program that doesn't
  depend on any particular market regime

### Robust Trading

- Two attributes of a robust trading program: **diversity** and **simplicity**
  (analogized to ecosystem resilience in nature)

#### Diversity
- Ecosystems survive because they don't rely on a single species for each role;
  diversity insulates against radical change in any one component
- Trading analog: diversify across markets, systems, timeframes

#### Simplicity
- In stable environments, complex species have advantages; during radical change,
  simple organisms (viruses, bacteria) survive because they depend less on
  specific conditions
- Complex trading systems are more brittle; simple systems are more adaptable
  when market regimes shift

## Chapter 13 (continued): Building Robust Trading Systems

### Robust Organisms
- Species that survived constant environmental change developed adaptability —
  a model for robust trading systems
- Two building blocks of robustness in nature: **diversity** and **simplicity**
- Apply to trading: simplicity = minimize rules that create dependencies on
  specific conditions; diversity = trade uncorrelated markets + multiple system types

### Robust Systems
- Primary methods: (1) rules that adapt to changing conditions, (2) keep systems
  simple and less tied to specific market behaviors
- **Portfolio filter**: makes systems more robust by filtering out unfavorable
  conditions — e.g., **Donchian Trend system** does not allow trades when market
  breaks out against the trend
- Complex systems accumulate rules tied to specific past behaviors — the more
  rules, the more dependent on conditions that may not recur
- Simple rules built on durable concepts outperform complex rules tailored to
  specific market behavior

### Market Diversification
- Trading more markets increases the probability of encountering favorable
  conditions for your system at any given time
- Correlated markets (e.g., several short-term U.S. interest-rate products that
  move in lockstep) add no real diversification — don't double-count
- Foreign markets valuable for non-monitoring systems (open/close only);
  time-zone differences matter less when you're only tracking open and close

### Deciding Which Markets to Trade
- **TradeStation** limitation: can only test one market at a time, leading traders
  to think in terms of individual markets rather than portfolios
- **Critical error**: excluding markets because they underperform in backtests
  - Trends may occur only every several years — a 5-10 year test is too short
  - Diversification benefit can outweigh individual market underperformance
- **The missed coffee trade (1985-86)**: Rich Dennis pulled Turtles from coffee
  citing insufficient volume. The March 1986 coffee contract produced what would
  have been the single biggest Turtle trade ever:
  - Entry: 141.91, Exit: 234.37 (65 bars later)
  - *N* = 1.29 cents → unit size of 103 contracts → 4 units = 412 contracts
  - Profit: ~$34,000/contract × 412 = ~$14 million = **280% return on $5M account**
  - No other Turtle-era trade came close
- **Primary legitimate reason to exclude a market: liquidity**
  - Illiquid markets: can't get in/out, susceptible to large price shocks
  - As account size grows, liquidity becomes more of a constraint (not less)
  - Even small accounts face illiquid market risk: orders pile up with no
    counterparty; one contract sits in a queue of 200-500 buy orders

### Three Classes of Markets
1. **Fundamentals-driven markets** — currencies, interest rates; macroeconomic
   forces (central banks, monetary policy) dominate over speculators;
   greatest liquidity, cleanest trends — easiest for trend followers
2. **Speculator-driven markets** — stocks, coffee, gold, silver, crude oil;
   perception-driven pricing; harder for trend followers
3. **Aggregated derivative markets** — e.g., e-mini S&P futures; speculation
   diluted because the instrument aggregates many component stocks, averaging
   out momentum; **hardest for trend followers**
   - Author never traded the S&P as a Turtle for this reason

### Trader Memory
- Markets change character as participants forget past extreme moves
- Gold/silver 1978 trend ($900/oz gold, $50/oz silver) created "trader memory"
  that made the market choppy and nearly untradeble for trend followers for ~20 years
- By 2006, the 1978 memory faded → gold's spring 2006 move was much easier to
  trade — the market appeared to have "changed its character"
- **Don't exclude a market because it hasn't trended in 20 years** — you can't
  predict when the next trend will occur

### Capital Constraints on Diversification
- Minimum ~$100,000 to trade a long-term trend-following futures system with
  reasonable diversification; many individual traders can't afford adequate
  diversification
- Reason hedge funds and large operators have more consistent performance:
  they can trade 50-60 markets simultaneously

### System Diversification
- Trading multiple non-correlated systems dramatically improves robustness
- Example: **Bollinger Breakout system** split into two parts:
  - Better system (long volatility breakouts only): RAR% = 38.2%, R-cubed = 1.19
  - Worse system (short volatility breakouts only): RAR% = 14.5%, R-cubed = 0.41
  - **Combined**: RAR% = 61.2%, R-cubed = 5.20 — dramatic improvement
- Negatively correlated systems are most powerful: one profits while the other
  draws down
- Capital requirement to diversify across systems: $200,000+; trading 4-5
  systems may require $1 million+

### Facing Reality
- A robust program accepts that future market conditions are unpredictable;
  builds systems that work across conditions rather than being optimized for
  specific ones
- Many systems + many markets = much more likely to perform consistently than
  few highly tailored systems

> *The market does not care how you feel. It will not prop up your ego or console

## Bonus Chapter: Original Turtle Trading Rules

> *"I always say that you could publish my trading rules in the newspaper and no one would follow them. The key is consistency and discipline. Almost anybody can make up a list of rules that are 80% as good as what we taught our people. What they couldn't do is give them the confidence to stick to those rules even when things are going bad."*
> — **Richard Dennis**, quoted in *Market Wizards* by Jack D. Schwager

### A Complete Trading System

- Most successful traders use a **mechanical trading system** — not a coincidence
- A complete system answers every decision a trader must make; nothing left to subjective judgment
- Key benefit: during losing periods, you *know* the system works long-term, so you can follow signals instead of freezing or overriding
- Relying on judgment alone: you'll be fearful when you should be bold, and bold when you should be cautious
- The **Turtle Trading System** was *complete* — covered every aspect, left no discretionary gaps

**Six components of a complete trading system:**
- **Markets** — what to buy or sell
- **Position Sizing** — how much to buy or sell
- **Entries** — when to buy or sell
- **Stops** — when to exit a losing position
- **Exits** — when to exit a winning position
- **Tactics** — how to execute the buy or sell

### Markets: What the Turtles Traded

- Turtles were **futures traders** (then called commodities traders) on major U.S. exchanges
- Primary criterion for market selection: **liquidity** — with millions at stake, low-volume markets cause too much market impact
- Excluded grains: **Richard Dennis** already held full legal position limits in his own account
- Excluded meats: corruption in the floor pits (years later the FBI conducted a major sting in the Chicago meat pits, indicting many traders for price manipulation)

**Markets traded:**
- *Chicago Board of Trade*: 30-year T-bond, 10-year T-note
- *NY Coffee Cocoa and Sugar Exchange*: Coffee, Cocoa, Sugar, Cotton
- *Chicago Mercantile Exchange*: Swiss franc, Deutschmark, British pound, French franc, Japanese yen, Canadian dollar, S&P 500, Eurodollar, 90-day T-bill
- *Comex*: Gold, Silver, Copper
- *NY Mercantile Exchange*: Crude oil, Heating oil, Unleaded gas
- Turtles could opt out of any market, but if they did, they had to stay out consistently — no inconsistent trading

### Position Sizing

- The Turtle system normalized **dollar volatility** across markets — positions were sized so that a 1N move in any market equaled approximately the same dollar amount
- This made diversification genuinely effective: each market's risk contribution was equalized regardless of underlying volatility

### Volatility: The Meaning of N

- **N** = 20-day exponential moving average of the **True Range** (now called **ATR** — Average True Range)
- N represents the average daily price range including opening gaps
- **True Range formula:** `TR = maximum(H – L, H – PDC, PDC – L)`
  - H = current high, L = current low, PDC = previous day's close
- **N formula:** `N = (19 × PDN + TR) / 20`
  - Requires a 20-day simple average of TR to initialize

### Dollar Volatility Adjustment

- `Dollar volatility = N × dollars per point`
- This converts abstract N into a concrete dollar risk per day for that market

### Volatility-Adjusted Position Units

- Positions built in **units** — 1 unit sized so that **1N = 1% of account equity**
- `Unit size = (1% of account) / (N × dollars per point)`
- **Heating oil example** (Dec 2002): N = 0.0141, account = $1M, dollars per point = 42,000
  - Unit size = (0.01 × $1,000,000) / (0.0141 × 42,000) = **16.88 → truncated to 16 contracts**
- Turtles received a **unit size sheet every Monday** listing N and unit sizes for all traded futures
- Smaller accounts (e.g. $100,000) suffer from granularity: same calculation yields ~1 contract, making volatility-adjusted diversification nearly impossible

### Units as a Measure of Risk

- The unit was simultaneously the position size unit *and* the portfolio risk unit
- **Maximum unit limits (4 levels):**

| Level | Type | Max Units |
|-------|------|-----------|
| 1 | Single market | 4 |
| 2 | Closely correlated markets | 6 |
| 3 | Loosely correlated markets | 10 |
| 4 | Single direction (long or short) | 12 |

- *Closely correlated*: heating oil & crude oil; gold & silver; currencies as a group; T-bills & eurodollars
- *Loosely correlated*: gold & copper; silver & copper
- **"Loaded"** = at the maximum permitted units for a given level (e.g., "loaded in yen" = 4 units of yen)
- After the **October 1987 crash**, the Fed cut rates sharply overnight; Turtles were short interest-rate futures and lost 40–60% of equity in a single day — losses would have been even larger without the unit caps

### Adjusting Trading Size

- After large drawdowns, reduce notional account size to trade smaller
- Rule: **decrease notional account by 20% for every 10% drawdown** from the year's starting equity
  - Example: $1M account, down 10% ($100K) → trade as if $800K account
  - Down another 10% of $800K ($80K, total $180K loss) → trade as if $640K account
- Turtles used **notional accounts** (not actual running balances) — started at $0 P&L with a set account size (e.g., $1M in Feb 1983), adjusted annually by Dennis based on trader performance

### Entries

- Turtles used **channel breakout** systems taught by **Richard Donchian** — considered surprisingly simple by most traders who fixate on entry complexity
- Two systems, with discretion to allocate any proportion between them:
  - **System 1**: shorter-term, **20-day breakout**
  - **System 2**: longer-term, **55-day breakout**
- **Breakout** = price exceeds the high or low of the preceding N days by one tick
- Always entered at the breakout intraday, not at close or next open; entered on the open if market gapped through breakout price

**System 1 Entry rules:**
- Enter long if price exceeds 20-day high by one tick; enter short if price drops below 20-day low by one tick
- **Skip signal if the last breakout was a winner** — defined as: price moved 2N against the position before a profitable 10-day exit occurred
- Failsafe: if a System 1 signal is skipped, **always enter at the 55-day breakout** to avoid missing major moves
- Direction of last breakout irrelevant to the skip rule — a losing long or losing short both reset the filter

**System 2 Entry:**
- Enter on 55-day high/low breakout by one tick
- **No filter** — all signals taken regardless of whether previous breakout was a winner

### Adding Units

- After initial entry, add units at **½N intervals** above (long) or below (short) the entry price
- Add intervals based on *actual fill price*, not the breakout price (accounts for slippage)
- Can add all 4 units in a single day if market moves fast enough

**Gold example** (N = 2.50, 55-day breakout = 310):
- Unit 1: 310.00
- Unit 2: 311.25 (310.00 + ½ × 2.50)
- Unit 3: 312.50
- Unit 4: 313.75

**Crude Oil example** (N = 1.20, 55-day breakout = 28.30):
- Unit 1: 28.30 → Unit 2: 28.90 → Unit 3: 29.50 → Unit 4: 30.10

### Consistency in Taking Entry Signals

- Most annual profits often come from **only 2–3 large winning trades** — missing even one signal can devastate yearly returns
- Turtles with the best records consistently took every entry signal
- Turtles dropped from the program **failed to enter positions consistently** when rules indicated

### Stops

> *"There are old traders and there are bold traders, but there are no old bold traders."*

- Turtles **always** used stops — the most important habit for long-term survival
- Predefined exit point must be set *before* entering the position; once market hits the price, exit every time without exception
- Wavering from this "will eventually result in disaster"
- Famous failures where stops were not used: **Barings Bank** and **Long-Term Capital Management** — both allowed small losses to become catastrophic ones
- **Turtle stops were mental/internal** (not placed with brokers) — large positions would reveal strategies to floor traders; instead, Turtles held a private price and exited via limit or market orders when hit
- Non-negotiable: if price traded at the stop, the position was exited, every single time

### Stop Placement

- No trade could incur more than **2% risk** of account equity
- Since 1N = 1% of account, stops were placed **2N away** from entry price

## Stop Mechanics (continued): Building a Full Position

### Raising Stops as Units Are Added
- 1 N = 1% of account equity → max stop = **2 N** (2% risk) for longs below
  entry, shorts above entry
- As each new unit is added, stops for all earlier units are **raised by 1½ N**
- Result: all stops for the entire position converge to **2 N from the most
  recently added unit**
- Exception: fast markets or opening gaps cause wider spacing between units,
  so stops may differ slightly across units

### Crude Oil Example (N = 1.20, 55-day breakout = 28.30)
- **Unit 1 only:** entry 28.30, stop 25.90 (2 N below)
- **Units 1–2:** both stopped at 26.50 (2 N below unit 2 entry of 28.90)
- **Units 1–3:** all stopped at 27.10 (2 N below unit 3 entry of 29.50)
- **Units 1–4:** all stopped at 27.70 (2 N below unit 4 entry of 30.10)
- **Gap scenario** (unit 4 fills at 30.80 due to gap open): unit 4 stop is
  28.40; earlier units remain at 27.70 — stops differ because of gap skid

## The Whipsaw: Alternative Stop Strategy

### Mechanics
- Instead of 2 N risk per trade, stops placed at **½ N** for **½% account risk**
- If a unit is stopped out, it is **reentered** if price returns to the original
  entry price
- Benefits: stops for earlier units do NOT need to be raised as new units are
  added — total risk stays ≤ 2% across all 4 units automatically
- Drawback: **more frequent losses**, lower win/loss ratio — psychologically
  harder despite better long-run profitability
- A few Turtles used the Whipsaw with good success

### Whipsaw Crude Oil Example (same N = 1.20)
- Each unit's stop is ½ N below its own entry price (stops trail the unit, not
  the position)
- Unit 1: stop 27.70; Unit 2: stop 28.30; Unit 3: stop 28.90; Unit 4: stop 29.50

---

## Benefits of N-Based Stops

- Because stops scale with **N (volatility)**, risk is automatically equalized
  across all markets
- More volatile market → wider stops but fewer contracts per unit → same dollar
  risk
- Result: **better diversification and more robust risk management** without
  manual adjustment per market

## Exits

### The Core Problem with Early Exits
- "You can never go broke taking a profit" — the Turtles explicitly rejected
  this
- **Exiting winning positions too early is one of the most common mistakes**
  in trend-following
- Prices never go straight up — riding a trend requires letting prices move
  against you temporarily:
  - Early in trend: 10–30% profits may fade to small losses
  - Mid-trend: an 80–100% profit may drop 30–40% before continuing
- The temptation to "lock in profits" is very strong — and very damaging

### Why Exit Timing Determines Success or Failure
- Most breakouts do NOT become trends — most trades are losses
- Winning trades must earn enough on average to offset all the losses
- If you exit winners at 1 N profit but stop out losers at 2 N, you need twice
  as many winners to break even
- **Exit point cannot be considered in isolation** — it interacts with entry,
  money management, and position sizing
- Proper exit for winning positions is "the most important aspect of trading
  and the least appreciated"

### Turtle Exits
- **System 1 exit:** 10-day low for longs, 10-day high for shorts — ALL units
  in the position exit on a 10-day breakout against the trade
- **System 2 exit:** 20-day low for longs, 20-day high for shorts — ALL units
  exit on a 20-day breakout against the trade
- Turtles typically did NOT place stop orders; they watched price and phoned
  in exit orders when the breakout level was touched

### These Are Difficult Exits
- Waiting for a 10- or 20-day new low can mean watching **20%, 40%, or even
  100% of significant profits evaporate**
- Very strong tendency to exit earlier — requires enormous discipline to hold
- Maintaining discipline during large winning trades is the **hallmark of an
  experienced successful trader**

## Tactics

> "God is in the details." — **Mies van der Rohe** (quoted to illustrate that
> execution details matter as much as the rules themselves)

### Entering Orders
- **Richard Dennis** and **William Eckhardt** advised against placing stop
  orders — watch the market and enter when price hits the trigger level
- Prefer **limit orders** over market orders: better fills, less slippage
- The "**bounce**" — random short-term price oscillation — means placing a
  limit order at the lower end of the bounce gets better fills
- Limit orders do not move the market (or move it less) vs. market orders
- Takes practice to identify optimal limit price, but consistently worth it

### Fast Markets
- In a fast market, price can move **thousands of dollars per contract in
  minutes** — a limit order simply won't fill
- Beginner instinct: panic and place market orders → invariably fills at the
  high/low of the run at the worst possible price
- Fast markets: sellers stop selling and hold out for higher asks → spread
  widens → price spikes → then new sellers enter and price collapses back
- **Market orders placed into fast markets fill at the top of the spike**,
  right before stabilization
- Turtle approach: **wait for at least a temporary price reversal** before
  placing orders — results in far better fills
- If price stabilizes past the stop level, exit without panicking — but exit
  calmly, not reactively

### Simultaneous Entry Signals
- Quiet days: no orders at all, just monitoring existing positions
- Frantic days: multiple markets gap open through entry levels simultaneously —
  e.g., crude oil, heating oil, and unleaded gas all triggering at once
- Also common: multiple contract months of the same futures market signal
  simultaneously
- Key: act **efficiently and quickly** without panicking into market orders

### Buy Strength, Sell Weakness
- When simultaneous signals arrive from correlated markets: **buy the strongest,
  short the weakest**
- Within a correlated group, the strongest market almost always outperforms
  weaker ones on the long side; the weakest produces the best short trades
- Enter only **one unit in a single contract month** at a time (e.g., don't
  buy Feb, Mar, and Apr heating oil simultaneously — pick the strongest,
  most liquid month)
- Methods for measuring strength:
  - Visual chart inspection (simplest)
  - Count how many N the price has moved since breakout — buy the highest
  - Subtract price 3 months ago from current price, divide by N to normalize
    → highest value = strongest market

### Rolling Over Expiring Contracts
- Two factors before rolling into a new contract month:
  1. **Near-month vs. far-month divergence:** near months often trend well
     while distant contracts don't — do NOT roll unless the new contract's
     price action would have generated an existing position signal
  2. **Volume and open interest:** roll before liquidity declines too much
     in the expiring contract
- General rule: roll **a few weeks before expiration** unless the near month
  is significantly outperforming farther-out months

## Finally: The Complete Turtle System — Why Rules Alone Aren't Enough

### The Rules Are Simple; Following Them Is Not
- The entire Turtle System is "not very complicated" — knowing the rules is
  insufficient to make money
- **Consistency and discipline** are what matter

> "I always say that you could publish my trading rules in the newspaper and
> no one would follow them. The key is consistency and discipline. Almost
> anybody can make up a list of rules that are 80% as good as what we taught
> our people. What they couldn't do is give them the confidence to stick to
> those rules even when things are going bad." — **Richard Dennis**

### Why Many Turtles Didn't Make Money
- Some Turtles failed — not because the rules didn't work, but because **they
  could not follow the rules**
- The system depends on capturing **infrequent large trends** — many months
  can pass between winning periods, sometimes a year or two
- During losing stretches, self-doubt compounds: "What if the rules don't work
  anymore? What if the markets have changed? What if there's a secret I'm
  missing?"
- One fired Turtle (first class, dismissed before year-end) convinced himself
  **hidden secrets were being withheld** — unable to accept that his poor
  performance was due to his own failure to follow the rules

### The Temptation to Modify Rules
- Many Turtles changed the rules "to reduce risk" — often producing the
  **opposite of the intended effect**
- Example: entering positions more slowly than specified (1 unit every ½ N)
  seems conservative, but in practice it increases the chance that
  retracements will hit stops — faster entry may actually be safer because
  the position survives retracements better
- Subtle rule changes can have **major impacts on profitability** under certain
  market conditions

### Building Confidence Through Personal Research
- Cannot rely on others' summaries or reported results — **must personally
  backtest** using historical data
- Dig into actual trades, daily equity logs, frequency and extent of losses
- Knowing that 8-month losing periods have occurred many times in 20 years
  makes them survivable
- Knowing that adding quickly is key to profitability makes it possible to
  actually do it
