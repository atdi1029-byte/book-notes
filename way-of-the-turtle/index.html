Glossary

Core System Concepts

N (ATR): The 20-day exponential moving average of the True Range, measuring daily volatility in dollar terms. Called "N" by Dennis and Eckhardt, later standardized as Average True Range by J. Welles Wilder. The foundation of all Turtle position sizing.

Unit: The fundamental position size in the Turtle system, calculated so that a 1N price move equals 1% of account equity. All positions, risk limits, and adding rules are expressed in units.

Breakout: Price exceeding the highest high or lowest low of the previous N days by at least one tick. The primary entry signal for Donchian channel systems.

Donchian Channel: A price channel defined by the highest high and lowest low over N days, named after Richard Donchian. Breakouts above or below the channel trigger entries.

Trend Filter: A moving-average condition that allows trades only in the direction of the major trend. Dramatically improves edge by filtering counter-trend breakouts.

Risk and Performance Metrics

Risk of Ruin: The probability of losing all trading capital through a sequence of losses. Increases disproportionately as position size grows.

Drawdown: The peak-to-trough decline in account equity during a losing period. The primary reason most traders quit.

E-Ratio: Edge Ratio, computed as volatility-adjusted MFE divided by MAE over a specified time period. Values above 1.0 indicate positive edge.

MAE / MFE: Maximum Adverse Excursion and Maximum Favorable Excursion — the worst and best price moves after entry, measuring how far a trade goes against you and for you.

R-Multiple: Trade profit expressed as a multiple of initial risk (R). A 5R trade earned five times its stop-loss distance. Invented by Chuck Branscomb, popularized by Van Tharp.

RAR%: Regressed Annual Return — the slope of a linear regression through all points on the log equity curve. Far more robust than CAGR% because it uses every data point, not just start and end.

R-Cubed: Robust Risk/Reward Ratio (RRRR) — RAR% divided by length-adjusted average maximum drawdown. Uses five largest drawdowns instead of one, capturing both severity and duration.

MAR Ratio: Annual return divided by maximum drawdown. Quick filter for system quality, but fragile because it depends on a single worst drawdown event.

Behavioral Finance

Disposition Effect: The tendency to sell winners too early and hold losers too long. Creates support and resistance levels as traders anchor on previous prices.

Loss Aversion: Losses carry roughly twice the psychological weight of equivalent gains. Makes taking stop losses extremely painful for most traders.

Anchoring: Over-relying on a salient reference price (usually a recent high or low) when making decisions. Previous price levels become psychological magnets.

Outcome Bias: Judging a decision by its result rather than the quality of the process at the time. Causes traders to abandon valid systems after short losing streaks.

Recency Bias: Overweighting recent events relative to older data. Two months of losses can outweigh six months of prior wins in a trader's mind.

Trading Styles

Trend Following: Entering when markets hit new highs or lows and holding until the trend reverses. Wins only 30-35% of trades but profits are much larger than losses.

Countertrend Trading: Betting against breakouts, selling near highs and buying near lows. Profits from support and resistance holding firm.

Scalping: Capturing the bid-ask spread by providing liquidity. Profit per trade is tiny but accumulated over thousands of trades.

Introduction: The Turtle Experiment

In 1983, a nineteen-year-old programmer named Curtis Faith walked into the Chicago Board of Trade for a job interview that would change his life. The man across the table was Richard Dennis, known as the "Prince of the Pits," a trader who had turned a $400 loan into over $200 million trading commodities. Dennis had placed full-page ads in the Wall Street Journal, Barron's, and the New York Times announcing an audacious experiment: he would select a handful of ordinary people, teach them his methods in two weeks, and give each a million-dollar account to trade. Over a thousand people applied. Thirteen were chosen.

The CBOT building at 141 West Jackson Boulevard was itself a monument to the scale of the opportunity. A forty-five-story landmark crowned by a statue of Ceres, the Roman goddess of agriculture, it housed trading pits where millions of dollars' worth of grains, meats, and currencies changed hands every few seconds. Inside, the noise was deafening, the energy manic, and the stakes immediate. This was where Dennis had built his fortune, and it was where his experiment would unfold.

The experiment was born from a long-running debate between Dennis and his friend William Eckhardt, a brilliant mathematician and trader. Dennis believed great traders could be made; Eckhardt believed they were born. To settle it, Dennis was willing to stake millions of his own money. He named the trainees "Turtles" after turtle-farming operations he had seen in Singapore, telling Eckhardt they would "grow traders just like they grow turtles." The screening process tested not trading knowledge but analytical thinking and comfort with risk. Applicants answered sixty-three true/false questions and eleven short-answer prompts designed to reveal how they thought about probability, uncertainty, and decision-making under pressure.

Faith's background made him uniquely prepared. At seventeen, he had begun programming trading systems on an Apple II computer, writing thirty to forty programs that tested strategies against historical data. By the time of his interview, he was working for Harvard Investment Service in Massachusetts, analyzing systems professionally. This hands-on experience with backtesting was extraordinarily rare in 1983, and it gave Faith an immediate advantage in grasping what Dennis and Eckhardt taught. The first Turtle class was a deliberate mix: a D&D manual editor, a linguistics PhD, a state chess champion who traded grains for Cargill, a professional blackjack player, an accountant. The common thread was comfort with probability and game theory, not market experience.

"The difficulty in trading lies not in the concepts but in the application." — Curtis Faith

The interview itself was a window into how Dennis and Eckhardt thought about trader selection. They were testing for what candidates did not believe as much as what they did. Faith recounted his skepticism about a "philosopher's stone" — a glass disk with lines that traders supposedly laid over charts to predict prices with uncanny accuracy. His dismissal of this magical thinking signaled exactly the right mindset: healthy skepticism combined with openness to systematic evidence. At that point, Faith sensed he would get the offer. Dennis was described as a mountain of a man with a friendly face and quiet manner; Eckhardt was thin, average height, and looked like a professor. Together they probed each candidate's theory of markets and relationship to uncertainty.

The stakes of the bet were extraordinary. Dennis was willing to risk millions of his own money to prove his conviction that trading ability could be systematized and transmitted. This was not an academic exercise or a small pilot study. Each Turtle would trade real money in real markets, with real consequences for Dennis's personal fortune. The bet represented his belief that he thoroughly understood the reasons for his own success and could distill those reasons into a teachable methodology. A second class was trained the following year, in just one week rather than two, suggesting Dennis had refined his teaching approach. In total, about twenty-three traders went through the program across both classes.

Training lasted two weeks at the Union League Club in Chicago, two blocks from the trading floor — a staid gentleman's club whose past members included Philip Danforth Armour, George Pullman, Marshall Field, and John Deere. Dennis and Eckhardt taught the complete system: entries, exits, position sizing, and risk management. Then each Turtle was given a desk, a phone, and a practice account. After a month of observed trading, Dennis evaluated performance and allocated real capital. Faith received the largest account of any Turtle: $2 million.

The results were remarkable. Over the program's life, the Turtles collectively earned an average annual return exceeding 80 percent. Faith himself made over $31 million for Dennis during the initial trading period, more than any other participant. But beneath those headline numbers lay a more complicated story. Between a third and half of the Turtles were either unprofitable or only marginally profitable, and several were dropped from the program with losing records. Everyone had learned the same rules in the same room from the same teachers. The experiment proved that trading methods could be taught — but it also revealed something more troubling about why identical training produces wildly different results.

Chapter 1: Risk Junkies

Markets exist because different parties have different relationships with risk. Faith opens by drawing a sharp line between investors and traders. Warren Buffett is an investor: he buys companies, not stocks, profiting from the gap between market price and intrinsic value. Traders are fundamentally different. They buy and sell risk itself, indifferent to the underlying asset. A trader doesn't care whether oil consumption will rise or whether a coffee harvest will fail. What matters is whether the price will move, and by how much. As Peter Bernstein documented in Against the Gods, financial markets evolved specifically to enable risk transfer between parties who want to shed it and parties willing to carry it for a price.

The mechanics of risk transfer play out through three distinct roles in every market. Hedgers use futures to neutralize business risk — Southwest Airlines locked in oil at $26 per barrel while competitors paid over $60, gaining an enormous competitive advantage simply by using the futures market to do what it was designed for. Scalpers and market makers provide liquidity, earning the bid-ask spread on thousands of small trades. And speculators — the category the Turtles fell into — assume the directional price risk that hedgers want to offload. Faith illustrates this with a three-party transaction involving ACME Corporation hedging British pound exposure, a floor scalper named Sam capturing a two-tick spread, and a speculator called Mr. Ice building a short position. Each party gets what it wants: ACME gets certainty, Sam gets his spread, and Mr. Ice gets his directional bet.

Hedging deserves particular attention because it reveals why speculators are essential to the economy, not parasitic. Southwest Airlines hedged its fuel costs not because it wanted to trade oil but because it wanted to run an airline. By locking in oil at $26 per barrel while competitors paid market prices above $60, Southwest could plan routes, set ticket prices, and invest in operations without worrying about energy cost volatility. The futures contracts that made this possible required someone on the other side — someone willing to take the risk that oil prices would rise. That someone was a speculator. Without speculators, hedgers would have no one to trade with, and businesses would bear the full brunt of commodity price swings.

Two distinct types of risk drive all trading activity. Liquidity risk is the danger that you cannot buy or sell when you need to — scalpers and market makers specialize here, profiting from the bid-ask spread. A stock with a $28.50 bid and $28.55 ask offers the scalper a five-cent profit per share on every round trip. Arbitrage is a variant: trading the liquidity difference between two markets, buying gold on COMEX at $550 and simultaneously selling the electronic mini-gold on Globex at $555. Price risk is the possibility that prices move significantly in either direction. Speculators and position traders assume price risk, making money by buying low and selling high, or going short and buying back later at lower prices. The Turtles were price-risk speculators, carrying directional bets for days, weeks, or months.

The three-party trade example illustrates these roles concretely. ACME Corporation buys ten British pound futures contracts on the CME to hedge costs at its UK laboratory, executing through broker MAN Financial. Floor trader Sam sells them at $1.8452, immediately trying to buy back at $1.8450 to capture the two-tick spread — roughly $100 profit on ten contracts. A British pound contract represents 62,500 GBP, with a minimum tick of $0.0001 equaling $6.25. Sam buys from Mr. Ice, a speculator building a short position betting the pound will fall. Each party is satisfied: ACME has neutralized its currency exposure, Sam has earned his spread, and Mr. Ice holds the directional bet he wanted. This is how markets transfer risk from those who don't want it to those willing to carry it for profit.

The trading pit itself was a crucible of human emotion. When a large buyer like Calyon Financial began aggressively accumulating contracts on behalf of hedge fund clients, floor traders already short ten to a hundred contracts faced cascading losses. Panic spread visibly — you could read it on faces, hear it in voices. The collective psychology drove price: when enough sellers refused to sell at the current level and buyers kept bidding higher, the imbalance became self-reinforcing. Experienced traders recognized the shift early and flipped from short to long, riding the panic wave for profit. Inexperienced traders froze, watching a fifty-tick loss on fifty contracts pile up to $15,625 in minutes.

The experienced trader's response to panic in the pit was revealing. Rather than freezing or capitulating, a veteran would exit a losing short position early, flip long, and ride the panic buying for profit. The key was recognizing the collective psychology shift before it was fully reflected in price. This is a microcosm of what the Turtle system does on longer timeframes: identify the moment when crowd behavior creates a directional imbalance, position accordingly, and hold until the imbalance resolves. The pit compressed this dynamic into seconds; the Turtle system stretched it across weeks and months.

By 2007, electronic exchanges were rapidly replacing the open-outcry pits. Volume migrated to screens wherever both formats coexisted. The pits had offered a unique informational edge: you could literally see the psychology of the market in traders' faces and body language. Electronic markets eliminated that human-readable data but created new opportunities — dramatically lower execution costs, millisecond fill confirmations, and the ability to trade millions without visibly moving the market. The hedgers, scalpers, and speculators didn't disappear; they simply hid behind screens, making markets feel deceptively abstract for newcomers who might forget that every trade has a human being on the other side.

"The hedgers, scalpers, and speculators are still there, hiding behind the screen — waiting to eat you alive if you let them." — Curtis Faith

Chapter 2: Taming the Turtle Mind

If Chapter 1 explains what markets are, Chapter 2 explains why most people lose money in them. The answer is behavioral finance — the systematic study of how cognitive biases distort decision-making under uncertainty. Faith draws on Robert Shiller's Irrational Exuberance and Hersh Shefrin's Beyond Greed and Fear to argue that markets are not populated by rational actors but by deeply flawed human beings who make predictable errors. The Turtle system works precisely because it exploits these errors. If everyone traded rationally, there would be no trends to follow and no edge to capture.

Four emotions drive trading failure: hope, fear, greed, and despair. Hope whispers that a losing position will recover. Fear says to skip the next signal after a string of losses. Greed demands doubling down when profits are running. Despair concludes the system is broken after an inevitable drawdown. Behind these emotions lie specific cognitive biases that Faith catalogs with clinical precision. Loss aversion gives losses roughly twice the psychological weight of equivalent gains, making stop losses feel unbearable. The sunk cost effect tricks traders into holding losers because exiting feels like "wasting" money already lost — Faith watched Silicon Valley friends in 1999-2000 refuse to sell tech stocks that dropped to one-tenth their peak value, always waiting for a recovery that never came.

The disposition effect combines these biases into perhaps the most destructive pattern: selling winners too early while holding losers too long. It systematically inverts the fundamental rule of trend following, which is to let winners run and cut losers short. Add outcome bias (judging decisions by results rather than process quality) and recency bias (overweighting recent events), and you get traders who abandon valid systems after short losing streaks, right before the next winning period begins. The bandwagon effect creates the "unstoppable" surge at bubble peaks. The belief in the law of small numbers leads traders to draw confident conclusions from statistically meaningless samples. Faith's central insight is elegant: if a trader is free from these biases, every biased trader in the market becomes a source of profit. The unbiased are predators; the biased are prey.

Beyond individual biases, Faith catalogs four distinct trading styles, each suited to different temperaments and market conditions. Trend following — the Turtle approach — enters when markets hit new highs or lows and holds until the trend reverses. It produces excellent long-term returns but is psychologically punishing: 65-70% of trades are losers, drawdowns can match annual returns in magnitude, and months of losses precede the handful of big winners that make the entire year. Countertrend trading does the opposite, selling short near new highs and buying near new lows, profiting when support and resistance levels hold. Swing trading follows trends on shorter timeframes, with trades lasting three to four days using five-minute to hourly charts. Day trading is defined by timeframe rather than strategy: positions close before the session ends to avoid overnight risk, with sub-styles including scalping, position trading, and arbitrage.

Faith identifies four market states that determine which strategies work: stable and quiet, stable and volatile, trending and quiet, trending and volatile. Trend followers thrive in trending-quiet markets where profits accumulate steadily without being shaken out by sharp retracements. Countertrend traders prefer stable-volatile markets with large swings inside a range. Swing traders like any volatile market, trending or not. The states shift over time as market dynamics evolve, and no single strategy works in all of them.

The critical insight is that a Turtle doesn't try to predict what the market will do. Instead, a Turtle reads the current state of the market and responds to what is actually happening. This distinction between prediction and observation runs throughout the book and represents the fundamental philosophical shift that separates consistently profitable traders from everyone else. Most people want a forecast; Turtles want a reading of current conditions.

The anchoring bias deserves special attention because it directly creates the support and resistance levels that the Turtle system exploits, as explored in depth in Chapter 6. When tech stocks reached their 2000 peaks, every holder anchored on those prices as their reference point. As prices fell, each decline looked "temporary" relative to the anchor. Faith watched friends hold positions that dropped to one-tenth and then one-hundredth of their peak values, always saying they would sell when the price returned to some arbitrary level derived from the anchor. The anchor had no predictive power whatsoever — it was purely a psychological artifact — yet it governed investment decisions involving millions of dollars.

Understanding these biases and market states gives the unbiased trader a structural advantage. Every cognitive error committed by the majority creates a corresponding opportunity for the minority who recognize it. Loss aversion creates support levels that eventually break. The disposition effect creates resistance levels that trend followers exploit when they fail. Recency bias causes traders to abandon strategies at exactly the wrong time. The entire Turtle framework is built on the premise that these psychological patterns are permanent features of markets, as durable as the human emotions that produce them.

"A Turtle never tries to predict market direction but instead looks for indications that a market is in a particular state." — Curtis Faith

Chapter 3: The First $2 Million Is the Toughest

The entire Turtle training can be distilled into four principles: trade with an edge, manage risk, be consistent, and keep it simple. Faith begins with the concept of risk of ruin, borrowed from gambling theory. Even a game with positive expectation can bankrupt you if your bet size is wrong. Consider a coin flip paying 2:1 on heads: with a $1,000 bankroll, betting $500 per flip gives you a 25 percent chance of ruin on just the first two flips. The insight that risk of ruin increases disproportionately with bet size — doubling the amount risked can triple or quadruple the probability of going bust — was the foundation of everything the Turtles learned about money management.

Position sizing in the Turtle system revolved around N, a measure of daily market volatility equivalent to what J. Welles Wilder later standardized as the Average True Range. Dennis and Eckhardt sized positions so that a one-N price move equaled approximately one percent of account equity. This meant a volatile market like crude oil would command fewer contracts than a quieter market like corn, ensuring that no single market could dominate the portfolio's profit-and-loss. In 1983, this was genuinely innovative. Most traders sized positions by broker margin requirements or gut feeling, not by systematic volatility measurement. Van Tharp later popularized the concept in Trade Your Way to Financial Freedom, but Dennis and Eckhardt were doing it a decade earlier.

Expectation — the average dollar won per dollar risked — is how the Turtles quantified their edge. An expectation of 0.2 means earning twenty cents for every dollar of risk. The concept mirrors the casino's house edge: positive expectation games can be won over time, but negative expectation games will always bankrupt the player eventually. Turtles were explicitly trained to avoid outcome bias, treating individual losses as the cost of doing business rather than evidence that the system was broken. Losing streaks, counterintuitively, often preceded the most profitable periods.

The Turtles traded two breakout systems named after Richard Donchian: System 1 used a 20-day breakout with a 2N stop, and System 2 used a longer 60-day breakout. Both were pure trend-following approaches — buy when price makes a new high, sell short when it makes a new low. Faith's first real trade proved the psychological thesis of the entire program. In February 1984, heating oil broke above its 60-day high and Faith bought three practice contracts, scaling to the full twelve as price rose from $0.80 to $0.98 in under a week. He was the only Turtle who followed the system fully. Every other trainee found a reason to deviate — the contract was expiring, the move seemed too fast, the market felt overextended. When oil dipped from $0.98 to $0.94, several Turtles with positions liquidated in fear. Faith held, oil recovered to $1.05, and he exited near the high. His practice account was up $78,000, roughly three times what any other Turtle made. On a real million-dollar account, the trade would have returned approximately fifty percent in a single month.

"The difference in return had nothing to do with knowledge and everything to do with emotional and psychological factors." — Curtis Faith

The Turtles traded two systems named after Richard Donchian, who had popularized breakout methods decades earlier. System 1 used 20-day breakouts with a 2N stop; System 2 used longer 60-day breakouts. Both were pure trend-following: buy when price makes a new high, sell short when it makes a new low, and hold until the trend reverses. Faith's contrarian claim is that the specific rules mattered far less than consistent execution — many other widely known trend-following methods work equally well. The Turtles' four core principles distill the entire training: trade with an edge, manage risk, be consistent, and keep it simple.

The heating oil trade exposed a pattern that would repeat throughout the program. Large accounts create their own challenge: order size moves the market, raising execution costs. The Turtles were taught to use limit orders rather than market orders, because over time even small price improvements compound into significant savings. But the more fundamental lesson was about consistency. Every Turtle had identical information, identical training, and identical access to the same markets. The divergence in results came entirely from who was willing to pull the trigger when the system said to trade.

After the first month, Dennis conducted a debrief. He asked each Turtle why they hadn't fully loaded the heating oil trade. The answers were textbook cognitive bias: fear of an expiring contract, the sense that the move was happening too fast, recency bias from recent sideways action, anchoring on prices that now seemed "too high." Faith's reframe was that not taking the trade was actually the riskiest move, because it guaranteed missing the program's first major trend. Dennis agreed publicly, confirming Faith's judgment in front of the entire class.

The allocation that followed reflected Dennis's assessment of psychological readiness. Faith received a $2 million account, the largest of any Turtle. Others received smaller amounts based on how they had handled the trial month. On a normal million-dollar account, the heating oil trade alone would have returned approximately fifty percent in a single month. The lesson was clear: the system worked, but only for those willing to follow it completely. Over the years, Faith kept finding evidence that emotional and psychological strength were the most important ingredients in successful trading — far more important than the specific rules being followed.

Chapter 4: Think Like a Turtle

The conventional narrative says Dennis won the bet — trading can be taught. Faith disagrees. He calls the bet a draw. Between a third and half of the Turtles were unprofitable or only marginally profitable, and several were dropped from the program with losing records. Everyone learned the same rules in the same two weeks. The difference between the best and worst Turtles was entirely psychological makeup, not knowledge. Dennis proved that trading methods could be transmitted; Eckhardt proved that psychological readiness could not.

Turtles don't care about being right. They care about making money. This distinction sounds trivial but is operationally profound. Beginners treat wins as validation and losses as personal failure, which hooks them into prediction mode — trying to guess what the market will do next. The Turtle approach is orthogonal: you can't know if gold will rise tomorrow, but you know that trends will occur because human emotion doesn't change. The future is unknowable in specifics but foreseeable in character. When you expect to lose on most trades, individual outcomes lose their emotional charge, and you become free to execute the system mechanically. Ten losing trades in a row while sticking to the plan is trading well with bad luck, not bad trading.

Faith illustrates the danger of recency bias with a cocoa market story from 1998-1999. A trader friend endured seventeen consecutive losing breakout trades before cocoa finally launched a massive trend in November 1998. Worn down by months of small losses, the friend skipped the winning trade — the one that would have paid for all seventeen losers many times over. The Turtles who were cut from the program were often victims of exactly this pattern: they stopped trading right before conditions turned favorable. Trends tend to emerge precisely when everyone gives up on them.

Instead of prediction, Turtles think in probabilities. Faith uses insurance as the analogy: insurers don't predict hurricanes, they price policies against probability distributions. If you knew your house would be destroyed, you would move, not buy insurance. Insurance companies profit by pricing policies against the probability of payout, not by predicting specific disasters. Trading works the same way: the best you can do is structure your approach so that the rewards outweigh the risks over the long run.

A histogram of monthly returns from a twenty-year test of the Donchian Trend system shows a bell-curve shape with a pronounced rightward skew — that elongated tail of outsized winning months is what trend following captures. Of 3,746 losing trades and 1,854 winning trades in the test, forty-three returned ten times initial risk or more. The math works not because winning trades are frequent, but because they are large enough to dwarf the accumulated losses. The R-multiple framework, invented by Chuck Branscomb and popularized by Van Tharp, expresses each trade's profit as a multiple of initial risk. A trade risking $1,000 that earns $5,000 is a 5R trade. In the Donchian test, losing trades clustered near -1R while winners extended to 10R, 12R, even 20R and beyond.

Faith distills the Turtle mindset into three rules: trade in the present without dwelling on the past or trying to predict the future; think in probabilities rather than predictions, focusing on methods where the odds favor success over the long run; and take responsibility for your own trades without blaming markets, brokers, or other people. Some Turtles could never accept this probabilistic worldview. One was convinced that Dennis had withheld secret rules from most of the group, sharing them only with Faith — a conspiracy theory that Faith calls "completely ludicrous." Why would Dennis give traders his own money to lose while holding back key information?

Conspiracy theories about hidden rules were a defense mechanism. The Turtle who accused Dennis of withholding secrets was unable to accept that his own failure to follow simple rules explained his poor performance. Trading is one of the few domains where excuses are immediately falsifiable: the rules were written down, the trades were logged, and the results spoke for themselves. No amount of rationalization could change the fact that he had been given the same system as Faith and produced inferior results. Blaming market manipulation or hidden information was emotionally easier than confronting the truth about his own psychological limitations.

The inability to take personal responsibility was, in Faith's view, the single most significant factor leading to failure. Trading is one of the few activities where results are direct and unavoidable. There is no middle management to absorb blame, no team to share credit. You make the trades and you are responsible for the outcome. Faith's actual edge over other Turtles was not secret knowledge but a simpler approach: he allocated one hundred percent to the longer-term ten-week breakout system, which required fewer trades and less constant monitoring, reducing the opportunities for psychological interference.

"We thought in terms of probabilities, and that gave us the confidence to make decisions in the face of large degrees of risk and uncertainty." — Curtis Faith

Chapter 5: Trading with an Edge

An edge in trading is an exploitable statistical advantage based on market behavior likely to recur. Without one, commissions, slippage, and fees will grind your capital to zero. Faith borrows the concept from gambling: card counters gain an edge over the casino by betting small when the deck favors the house and large when it favors the player. The best trading edges come from cognitive biases that distort market behavior, because those biases are permanent features of human psychology. Finding an edge requires matching entry points with greater-than-normal probability of a favorable move to exit strategies compatible with the same timeframe.

To measure edge objectively, Faith introduces the E-Ratio (Edge Ratio). For each trade, calculate the Maximum Favorable Excursion (MFE, how far price moved in your favor) and Maximum Adverse Excursion (MAE, how far it moved against you), divide each by the ATR at entry to normalize for volatility, then average across all trades. The ratio of average MFE to average MAE gives the E-Ratio. A value above 1.0 means the system has positive edge; below 1.0 means negative edge. A random entry system produces an E-Ratio almost exactly at 1.0 across all timeframes, confirming the measure works.

Applied to the Donchian Trend system across 28 liquid U.S. futures markets from 1996 to 2006, the 20-day breakout showed no short-term edge (E5-ratio = 0.99, E10 = 1.0) but a meaningful long-term edge (E70 = 1.20). This confirms what trend followers know intuitively: breakouts often go against you initially before the trend develops. The E-Ratio starts below 1.0 in the first few days after entry, explaining why breakout trading is psychologically brutal — and why countertrend trading has a legitimate short-term edge.

The most remarkable finding involved the trend portfolio filter. Running 70,000 random entries with only the filter active (long when 50-day MA exceeds 300-day MA, short when below) produced an E70 of 1.27 — meaning the filter alone, without any entry signal at all, generated more edge than the breakout signal itself. Adding the filter to the breakout raised E70 from 1.20 to 1.33 and E120 to approximately 1.6. The filter works by eliminating counter-trend breakouts, which were generating the largest adverse moves. This finding has profound practical implications: even a mediocre entry signal combined with a good trend filter can produce strong long-term results.

The combination also smoothed the edge-ratio curve, meaning the climb in E-Ratio over increasing time horizons was less erratic and more consistent. This is significant because erratic edge curves suggest the edge depends on specific market conditions, while smooth curves indicate the edge is structural and likely to persist. The filter's power comes from a simple insight: breakouts that occur against the major trend are far more likely to fail than breakouts in the direction of the trend. Removing those counter-trend entries eliminates the trades most likely to produce large adverse excursions.

Faith also addresses the question of exit edge. Entries can be measured independently by examining what happens after entry, because that is when money is at risk. Exits are harder to evaluate in isolation because they depend on both the entry context and the exit timing. What happens after you exit is irrelevant — only what happened before the exit counts. The practical implication is that exits should be evaluated by their effect on overall system performance rather than by post-exit price behavior. A Turtle who exits a winning trade and then watches it go higher has not necessarily made a mistake — the exit served its function within the complete system.

A coin-flip test confirmed the E-Ratio's validity. Faith ran random entries — literally flipping a coin to go long or short — and measured the resulting E-Ratios across multiple timeframes. At E5, the ratio was 1.01; at E10, 1.005; at E50, 0.997. All clustered around 1.0 as expected, confirming that a truly edgeless system produces equal favorable and adverse excursions. Any system producing E-Ratios meaningfully above 1.0 therefore has genuine statistical edge, and the timeframe at which the ratio rises above 1.0 tells you whether the edge is short-term or long-term.

System edges come from three components working together: portfolio selection (which markets are valid to trade on a given day), entry signals (when to buy or sell), and exit signals (when to close the position). An entry may have edge only for the short term or only for the long term, not necessarily both. This means entry strategies must be matched to exit strategies of compatible timeframes. A breakout entry designed to capture multi-month trends paired with an exit that closes after three days would destroy the edge entirely. The Turtle system was powerful precisely because its long-term breakout entries were matched to long-term breakout exits.

Chapter 6: Falling Off the Edge

Trading edges exist because markets are not populated by rational actors. Economists are wrong about this, and the evidence is overwhelming. Cognitive biases create systematic divergences between perception and reality, and those divergences recur because human psychology is fundamentally stable over centuries. Support and resistance — the tendency for prices to bounce at previous highs and lows — is the clearest example. Faith traces it to three biases operating simultaneously: anchoring makes recent highs and lows psychologically salient reference points; recency bias gives the most recent prices extra weight; and the disposition effect causes traders to set mental sell targets at previous highs, creating clusters of sell orders that actually create the resistance they believe in. Support and resistance is a self-fulfilling prophecy — the belief in it generates the behavior that produces it.

The edge for countertrend traders comes from support and resistance holding. The edge for trend followers comes from it breaking down. When a price level that has held multiple tests finally breaks, there are suddenly no buyers (in a support break) or no sellers (in a resistance break). Panic sets in. Traders who bought at support are now underwater and scrambling to exit, while rational new buyers are waiting for price to stabilize before stepping in. The result is a rapid, one-directional move — exactly the kind of trend that breakout systems capture. Faith uses a detailed heating oil case study from December 2006: support at $2.10 held through mid-June, price bounced to $2.31, then broke below the prior low of $2.05 in September. A trader who averaged down from $2.10 to $2.05 on ten contracts would have lost $115,500 as price plummeted to $1.73.

Prices near support or resistance are what Faith calls points of price instability — they are unlikely to stay there and likely to move significantly in one direction. This makes them excellent entry points for both countertrend and trend-following traders, because the cost of being wrong is relatively low. A countertrend buyer at $2.10 places a stop six cents below; a trend-following seller at the same level places a stop five or six cents above. Both risk a small amount for the chance of a large move.

Faith uses a military analogy to explain the dynamics at these critical price levels. A general waits for the optimal moment to commit forces. When price is between support and resistance levels, neither side is fully committed — it is a neutral zone. As price approaches a known level, both buyers and sellers engage with increasing conviction. One side must lose, and when it does, the retreat can be rapid and disorderly. This is why breakouts and bounces at major levels tend to produce large moves: the loser's forced exit fuels the winner's advance.

The self-reinforcing nature of support and resistance creates a fascinating paradox. The very fact that many traders believe a price level will hold causes them to act in ways that make it hold. Buy orders cluster near support because traders expect a bounce; that buying pressure itself creates the bounce. But this reinforcement has limits. When a level breaks — when enough traders are forced to liquidate positions rather than defend the level — the cascade is amplified by the same psychology that built the support. All those buy orders that were "banking" on the level now become forced sell orders as stops trigger.

The Turtles used short-term resistance breakdowns to exit long trades and bought short-term resistance breakouts to exit short trades. This exit methodology exploited the same behavioral dynamics from the other direction. When a countertrend bounce formed after a strong trend, the Turtles didn't try to predict whether the bounce would hold. They simply waited for the bounce to develop far enough to trigger their exit rule, then closed the position. If the trend resumed, they would re-enter on the next breakout signal.

The Turtles used both sides of the support and resistance dynamic. On entry, they bought resistance breakouts for long positions and sold support breakdowns for short positions, profiting from the cascade that follows when a level fails. On exit, they used short-term support and resistance in the opposite direction: exiting longs when a 10- or 20-day low was broken, and covering shorts when a 10- or 20-day high was hit. This meant the same behavioral dynamics that created their entry edge also defined their exit criteria, producing a self-consistent system built entirely on the psychology of price levels.

Edges come from systematic misperceptions caused by cognitive biases. Good traders identify the instability points where one side must lose, bet on the winning side with controlled risk, and exit quickly when wrong. The trader who averaged down on heating oil from $2.10 to $2.05 was doing the opposite: increasing exposure at a point of instability without a plan for being wrong. That is how $115,500 losses happen.

"The source of the edge for trend followers is the gap in human perception at the time when support and resistance breaks down." — Curtis Faith

Chapter 7: By What Measure?

How do you know whether a trading system or fund manager is actually good? The industry answer is the highest risk-adjusted return, but Faith argues that most standard metrics either miss critical categories of risk or actively mislead. Smooth returns can mask catastrophic tail risk. Long-Term Capital Management averaged approximately 40 percent annual returns distributed so smoothly that its Sharpe ratio was excellent — right up until the 1998 Russian bond default triggered losses that nearly wiped out the entire $4.7 billion fund. Amaranth Advisors lost 65 percent of its $9 billion fund in two months on natural gas trades, also after years of smooth performance. Hedge funds that write out-of-the-money options generate consistent, steady returns until the day a tail event destroys everything.

Faith identifies four primary risks that any system faces: drawdowns (strings of losses reducing capital), low returns (periods of insufficient gains), price shocks (sudden catastrophic moves), and system death (the edge permanently disappearing). His personal experience with price shocks is visceral. On Black Monday, October 19, 1987, Faith was short approximately 1,200 eurodollar contracts and 600 T-bills while holding large long positions in gold, silver, and currencies. He made money on crash day itself. But the next morning, the government had cut interest rates overnight with no warning — eurodollars gapped open more than two full points higher, roughly $5,500 per contract, with no exit possible. Gold opened down $25, silver down over a dollar. Total damage: approximately $11 million on a $20 million account, an entire year's profits vaporized overnight.

The Sharpe ratio, invented by Nobel laureate William F. Sharpe in 1966, works well for comparing stock mutual funds but fails for futures traders. Futures differ from stocks in management style risk (active trading loses money faster than buy-and-hold), diversification strategy (concentrated positions), leverage exposure, and confidence risk from limited track records. Faith's central claim about the Sharpe ratio is counterintuitive: smoothness of returns does not equal low risk. In many cases, smooth returns indicate hidden catastrophic risk that standard metrics cannot detect. The MAR ratio (annual return divided by maximum drawdown) is simpler and more useful as a quick filter, but it depends entirely on a single worst drawdown, making it fragile and easy to game by choosing favorable test dates.

Richard Dennis and Bill Eckhardt were obsessed with position-size risk because they had lived through markets that locked limit-down for days, making exit impossible. Dennis had once been trapped in silver futures during a multi-day lockdown and survived only by trimming his position before the worst of it. During the Turtle program, he constantly monitored aggregate positions and reduced his own exposure when total risk felt excessive — behavior that contradicted his public "gunslinger" reputation.

The distinction Dennis drew between drawdowns and profit giveback was important for maintaining the right psychological framework. Giving back profits accumulated during a trend was simply part of the game for trend followers, not evidence of failure. A system that captured large trends necessarily surrendered some portion of those profits when the trend reversed, because exit signals always lag. This structural feature could not be eliminated without also eliminating the system's ability to capture the trends in the first place. Faith's personal worst drawdown as a Turtle reached approximately 70 percent — a level that very few human beings can psychologically sustain.

Faith's personal drawdown experience provides a concrete baseline. During his time as a Turtle, his account drew down approximately 70% at its worst. Very few people can psychologically sustain that level of equity decline. Successful former Turtles who went on to raise institutional money traded at greatly reduced risk levels — almost a mandatory requirement for attracting outside capital, because institutional investors simply will not tolerate the volatility that produces Turtle-level returns. Two systems with identical 30% average annual returns but different smoothness profiles tell very different stories: the smoother system is not just more pleasant to live with, it is also more likely to deliver good results in future periods, because consistent delivery is itself a positive signal about the system's robustness.

Measuring risk and reward requires a combination of metrics, not reliance on any single number. Faith recommends examining maximum drawdown (the deepest peak-to-trough decline), longest drawdown (the time from peak to new peak), standard deviation of returns (how much results vary month to month), and R-squared (smoothness of fit to the trend line). On the reward side, CAGR% measures compound growth but can be skewed by a single exceptional period. Average one-year trailing return is less sensitive to outliers. The most revealing approach is visual: examine the equity curve alongside a histogram of monthly returns to see both the trajectory and the distribution simultaneously.

System death, the most insidious of the four risks, is hardest to distinguish from a normal drawdown. Markets are dynamic, and changes can be permanent — but great traders distinguish themselves by adhering to methods that others have abandoned during difficult periods. A counterintuitive cycle operates here: when trend following appears dead, experts declare its demise, money flees trend-following funds, strategies become less crowded, and then trend following becomes profitable again, often spectacularly so. Faith has watched this cycle repeat at least three or four times since the Turtle program began, and he always laughed at the obituaries, knowing that profitable trends were likely just around the corner.

Chapter 8: Risk and Money Management

Money management is the art of keeping risk of ruin at acceptable levels while maximizing profit. It is not a science — there are no universally correct answers, only individually appropriate ones. The core trade-off is stark: too much risk and you blow up or quit after an unbearable drawdown; too little risk and you leave enormous potential on the table. Faith calculates that $50,000 compounding at 30 percent annually becomes roughly $10 million in twenty years, but only if you never blow up and have to restart. Targeting 100-200 percent returns dramatically increases the probability of that restart, which is why Faith recommends conservative risk levels for the first several years.

The reality check on risk is brutal. A natural gas contract might have an ATR of $7,500 per day; a 2-ATR stop means $15,000 of risk per contract. For a $50,000 account, that is thirty percent of capital on a single trade. The prudent natural gas position size for accounts under $1 million is zero contracts. Trading with too much risk is the single most common reason for failure among new traders, and Faith has no patience for the delusion that boldness compensates for undersized accounts.

The Turtle approach to money management centered on volatility normalization. By sizing every position so that a 1N move equaled one percent of account equity, each market contributed roughly equal dollar risk regardless of its underlying volatility. This was critical during October 1987, when almost every market moved against the Turtles simultaneously. Faith had used 1.5-ATR stops rather than the standard 2-ATR stops; if he had sized positions based on stop distance alone, he would have held four times as many contracts and lost four times as much. Volatility-based sizing meant his relative position was the same as other Turtles, and he survived the crash intact.

Dennis and Eckhardt imposed strict unit limits across four levels: maximum 4 units in any single market, 6 in highly correlated markets, 10 in any one direction, and 12 in uncorrelated markets. These limits probably saved Dennis more than $100 million on Black Monday. When interest-rate markets — eurodollars, T-bonds, T-bills, two-year notes — all signaled simultaneously during the crash, the unit limits meant holding only the first two that triggered. Later entrants into correlated markets were more likely to lose because they entered further from the optimal price. The limits also served as an implicit market filter, screening out lagging markets, a critical function that people who later tried to replicate the Turtle system often missed entirely.

The real-world causes of trader failure, in Faith's experience, came down to three patterns. First, having no plan at all: trading on hunches, rumors, or a belief in price prediction. Second, taking too much risk — not fifty percent more than prudent, but five to ten times what Faith would consider aggressive. Third, unrealistic expectations, a problem disproportionately common among successful professionals from other fields. Doctors and dentists were particularly drawn to futures trading, and their intelligence and success elsewhere bred overconfidence. Trading is simple but not easy, and expertise in medicine translates into nothing useful on the trading floor.

Faith's prudent rule for drawdown planning is characteristically blunt: target drawdowns in simulation at most half the level you believe you can psychologically tolerate. If you think you can handle a thirty percent drawdown, design your system for fifteen percent. The buffer exists because out-of-sample market shocks will inevitably exceed what historical testing predicts. The Donchian Trend system running at three percent risk per trade would have gone bust overnight during the 1987 crash on a single day when interest-rate markets reversed. Historical testing at that risk level showed acceptable drawdowns; reality delivered an extinction event.

Trading system vendors are part of the problem. They routinely understate risk and overstate potential returns. Faith is blunt: "The risk is real, and trading is not easy." The power of compounding makes conservative approaches enormously attractive when measured over decades rather than quarters. Fifty thousand dollars compounding at 30% annually becomes roughly ten million in twenty years. But that trajectory requires never blowing up and restarting from zero. Each blowup resets the compounding clock, turning what should be a geometric curve into a series of aborted attempts. Conservative risk levels in the early years protect the compounding engine that eventually produces extraordinary wealth.

New traders routinely overestimate their drawdown tolerance. Stopping after a drawdown — at the worst possible time — is the typical outcome. There are two ways excessive risk forces you out of the game: the slow painful death of anguish and frustration leading to quitting, and the sudden blowup that wipes out the account in days or hours. Both are preventable through proper position sizing, but prevention requires accepting lower returns in exchange for survivability. The Turtle Way resolved this trade-off through volatility normalization: risk the same dollar amount in each market so that no single position dominates the portfolio, and cap aggregate exposure through unit limits so that correlated disasters cannot destroy the account.

"Ruin is the risk you should be concerned with the most. It can come like a thief in the night and steal everything if you aren't watching carefully." — Curtis Faith

Chapter 9: Turtle-Style Building Blocks

Building blocks are the technical tools that indicate which market state prevails — stable or trending, quiet or volatile. No single tool works all the time, and Faith warns against chasing exotic indicators when simple ones perform just as well. He identifies five core building blocks that underpin virtually all trend-following systems. Breakouts are the simplest: price exceeding the highest high or lowest low of the previous N days. The Turtle system used 20-day and 55-day breakouts, but any reasonable N produces similar long-term results when combined with a trend filter.

Moving averages smooth price data to reveal underlying trends. Simple moving averages weight every day equally; exponential moving averages (EMAs) give more weight to recent prices. Crossover systems — buying when a faster average crosses above a slower one — are perhaps the most widely used entry signals in trend following. Faith emphasizes that extra complexity beyond basic moving averages adds curve-fitting risk without meaningful edge improvement. Volatility channels surround a moving average with a band based on ATR or standard deviation; price breaking through the band suggests a potential trend change. Time-based exits close positions after a fixed number of days regardless of price action, which can smooth drawdowns. Simple lookbacks compare current price to price N days ago, optionally filtered by an ATR threshold.

Breakouts are the simplest building block: when price exceeds the highest high or lowest low of the previous N days, it signals a potential trend. Shorter lookback periods (10-20 days) generate more signals and shorter trades; longer periods (50-60 days) produce fewer but more significant signals. The original Turtle system used both 20-day and 55-day breakouts. Faith emphasizes that the specific number of days matters far less than having some form of breakout signal combined with a trend filter. Any reasonable N, from 15 to 60, produces similar long-term results when the overall framework is sound.

Moving averages reveal underlying trends by smoothing daily noise. Simple moving averages (SMA) weight every day equally, while exponential moving averages (EMA) give more weight to recent prices and respond faster to changes. Crossover systems generate buy signals when a faster average crosses above a slower one and sell signals on the reverse. Faith warns that adding complexity beyond basic moving averages — triple exponential smoothing, adaptive algorithms, weighted schemes — adds curve-fitting risk without meaningful edge improvement. The marginal gain from a more sophisticated average is almost always smaller than the marginal fragility it introduces.

Volatility channels wrap a band around a moving average using ATR or standard deviation as the width measure. Price breaking through the upper band suggests the start of an uptrend; breaking through the lower band signals a potential downtrend. Time-based exits close positions after a fixed number of days regardless of price, which can smooth drawdowns by triggering exit before traditional signals catch a reversal. Simple lookbacks compare current price to price N days ago, optionally filtered by an ATR threshold to distinguish genuine moves from noise.

The essential insight of this chapter is that all trend-following building blocks are fundamentally interchangeable. Tuned appropriately, breakouts, moving-average crossovers, and volatility-channel breaks all respond to the same market movements. They are different lenses for observing the same underlying phenomenon: sustained directional price movement. The search for a "nuclear-powered indicator" that magically outperforms these simple tools is a fool's errand driven by the same ego that makes traders want to predict markets rather than follow them.

Each building block has its own characteristics. Breakouts are the most straightforward: a clear, binary signal that price has exceeded a defined threshold. Moving-average crossovers are smoother but slower, often entering trends after breakout systems. Volatility channels adapt to market conditions because the band width changes with volatility. Time-based exits add a calendar dimension, particularly useful for managing trades that neither hit stops nor develop into trends. Simple lookbacks offer the purest measure of momentum: has the market moved enough from where it was N days ago to suggest a trend? Combined with an ATR filter to screen out noise, lookbacks can be surprisingly effective despite their simplicity.

Faith's advice is to master the basic tools before chasing exotic alternatives. A trader who deeply understands how a 20-day breakout interacts with a 350-day moving average trend filter will outperform someone who has a library of proprietary indicators but no understanding of why any of them work. The building blocks are not the edge; the edge comes from how they are combined with position sizing, risk management, and psychological discipline. No indicator can compensate for the absence of those fundamentals.

Chapter 10: Turtle-Style Trading: Step by Step

Faith puts six trend-following systems under rigorous testing across 28 liquid U.S. futures markets from January 1996 to June 2006. The systems span a range of complexity: the ATR Channel Breakout (volatility channel using ATR around a 350-day moving average), the Bollinger Breakout (same concept using standard deviation, based on John Bollinger's bands), the Donchian Trend (20-day breakout with trend filter), the Donchian Trend with Time Exit (breakout entry but exit after 80 days flat with no stops), the Dual Moving Average (100-day/350-day crossover, always in the market), and the Triple Moving Average (150/250/350-day, filtered by the slowest MA).

The results are revealing. The Dual Moving Average — one of the simplest possible systems — produced the highest CAGR (57.8%) and best MAR ratio (1.82). The Donchian Trend with Time Exit, which has no stops at all and simply exits after 80 days, delivered 57.2% with a win rate of 58.3%. Meanwhile, the Donchian Trend, the system closest to what the original Turtles traded, lagged with 29.4% returns and the worst MAR of 0.80. Faith attributes this degradation to trader effects — as breakout trading became widely known after the Turtle secrets leaked, other traders learned to front-run the predictable signals.

Faith introduces the concept of the pseudo-expert to explain why so much trading education is misleading. In most fields, pseudo-experts vastly outnumber true experts, and they are indistinguishable to outsiders. True experts understand deeply and can explain complex ideas simply. Pseudo-experts copy what experts do without knowing why, then codify behavior into rigid rules where none were intended. The tell-tale sign is unclear writing: unclear writing signals unclear thinking. A trading pseudo-expert might run a thousand-trade simulation from two weeks of short-term data and draw confident conclusions — the math is executed correctly, but the sample is utterly meaningless. Don't confuse experience with expertise or knowledge with wisdom.

The backtesting debate is addressed directly. Many successful discretionary traders distrust historical simulation, arguing that the past won't repeat. Faith counters that this critique misses the point: even discretionary traders rely on their personal experience of past price action. All trading knowledge is ultimately derived from the past; the only question is whether you process it systematically or informally. Computer simulation allows more rigorous pre-trade analysis. The main risk is overoptimization — computers make it easy to find methods that look spectacular historically but have no edge going forward. Chapters 11 and 12 address this problem in detail.

The performance table for the six systems reveals important patterns. The Bollinger Breakout had the highest win rate (54.6%) with a strong MAR of 1.52. Donchian Time Exit — the system with no stops that exits after 80 days flat — achieved the highest CAGR (57.2%) with a 58.3% win rate. The Dual MA and Triple MA systems, which are always or nearly always in the market, both performed well despite extreme simplicity. Three of the six systems have no stops at all yet match or beat those with stops on every metric. This challenges the received wisdom that stops are always beneficial.

The most counterintuitive finding involves stops. Testing the Dual Moving Average with stops at various ATR widths showed that the no-stop case had the best performance on every single metric: MAR, CAGR, Sharpe, drawdown severity, and drawdown length. The same result held for the Triple Moving Average and the Donchian Time Exit. Stops only approached the no-stop case at very large widths of ten ATR or more, at which point they rarely trigger and effectively replicate the no-stop condition.

The reason is structural: for trend followers, drawdowns come from giving back profits when trends reverse, not from individual losing trades. Adding stops doesn't reduce this profit-giveback drawdown; it only removes good trades prematurely by triggering during normal retracements within a larger trend. The Turtles understood this intuitively: surrendering accumulated profits at trend reversals was an unavoidable structural feature of trend following, not a flaw to be fixed with tighter stops.

Faith's five-month out-of-sample test through November 2006 provided a reality check. The last five months of 2006 were broadly bad for trend-following strategies. The Donchian Time Exit barely budged — CAGR dropped by just 0.2%, drawdown was unchanged. The Dual MA, which had been the best system in the original test, saw its maximum drawdown increase by 48% and its CAGR drop by 15%. This pattern — the "best" in-sample system degrading most out of sample — is a warning about selection bias and reinforces the argument for simplicity and robustness over peak performance.

"It is not entry risk that causes drawdowns for trend followers; it is giving back profits." — Curtis Faith

Chapter 11: Lies, Damn Lies, and Backtests

Every backtest lies, and Faith identifies four reasons why. Trader effects are the most insidious: when a profitable method becomes widely known, imitators crowd in and destroy the edge. It works exactly like the observer effect in physics. Faith describes how breakout front-running operates: if you know a large trader will buy at a breakout level, you buy ahead of them, drive the price to their trigger, sell your position back to them at a profit, and dump any excess. A gold front-running example nets $160,000 in seconds. One real system that became enormously popular suffered a prolonged drawdown far worse than anything in twenty years of backtesting because competitors had learned to anticipate its signals.

Trader effects can arise without conscious front-running. If enough traders independently adopt the same strategy, the collective action changes the market dynamics that made the strategy work in the first place. A breakout that would have triggered genuine trend-following demand in 1983 might trigger a manufactured front-running cascade by 2006, with the breakout itself becoming an artifact of anticipatory buying rather than genuine market conviction. This is why Faith repeatedly emphasizes developing your own system rather than copying a published one: any strategy that others can predict will be systematically exploited.

The Turtles countered this with deliberate obfuscation. They varied order timing and size, some using large stops and some small, some buying exactly at breakouts and some just before or after. Faith personally placed fake orders in the opposite direction to move the market before canceling and entering his real trade at a better price — a bluffing technique borrowed from poker. These tactics protected Dennis's fills but could not protect the broader breakout edge from erosion as the strategy became public knowledge.

Random effects are equally devastating. Faith ran 100 simulations using pure coin-flip entries with time-based exits. The best random trader achieved 16.9% annual returns over ten and a half years, turning $1 million into $5.5 million with zero edge. Adding a trend filter to the random entries raised the average return to 32.5% with the best random trader hitting 53.3%. Of 100 random simulations using just 3.5 years of data, seventeen beat the MAR ratio of Faith's best real system. The implication is staggering: a three-year track record with excellent numbers is close to meaningless. In a universe of 1,000 traders where only five or six are truly excellent, roughly sixteen average traders will get lucky enough to post ten-year records indistinguishable from the excellent ones.

The implications for investors are sobering. Faith imagines a universe of 1,000 traders where only five or six are truly excellent. If roughly two percent of the average traders get lucky enough to post a respectable ten-year track record, that creates about sixteen lucky average traders — outnumbering the genuinely excellent ones by roughly three to one. Investors selecting managers based on track records will mostly end up with lucky average performers rather than truly skilled ones. Time is the only corrective: lucky traders revert to the mean while truly excellent traders persist, but five-year records are near-useless for distinguishing the two.

Faith goes further with a shorter test window. Using just 3.5 years of data from January 2003 to June 2006, the random entry system averaged 35% returns with a MAR of 1.06. Of 100 random simulations, seventeen beat the MAR of Faith's best real system. The single best random trader achieved a 71.4% return with a MAR of 2.07 — performance that would attract institutional capital and media attention despite having absolutely zero edge. Good investors, Faith concludes, invest in people and process, not in historical performance numbers that may be nothing more than luck.

The optimization paradox adds another layer. Optimizing parameters genuinely improves expected future performance — using the best Bollinger entry threshold of 2.4 standard deviations versus an arbitrary 3.0 would have meant eight times more money over ten years. But the optimized test also overstates what you'll actually achieve, because any future shift from the peak parameter will produce worse results. The test represents the best-case view of the past, and any drift in optimal parameters will cause reality to fall short of the simulation.

Faith demonstrates overfitting with a devastatingly simple example. He adds a single rule to the Dual Moving Average that reduces positions by 90% when drawdown hits 38%. Returns improve from 41.4% to 45.7% and drawdown drops from 56% to 39%. The catch: the rule fires exactly once in the entire test, at the very end, where the drawdown happened to sit. Varying the threshold by just one percentage point — from 37% to 38% — swings annual performance from negative 0.4% to positive 45.7%. This cliff is the unmistakable signature of curve fitting. Large performance swings from tiny parameter changes are diagnostic of overfitting. Faith then pushes the demonstration further by searching 4,000 seasonal tests and finding that reducing positions by 96% for the first two days of September and the first 25 days of July improves the already-overfit system further. The "improved" numbers are complete fiction — the seasonal patterns coincided with past drawdowns by pure chance.

Chapter 12: On Solid Ground

If Chapter 11 explains why backtests lie, Chapter 12 explains how to test honestly. The foundation is inferential statistics: using a sample to draw conclusions about a larger population. Two factors determine validity — sample size and representativeness. A test covering only recent history is like polling exclusively at the Democratic National Convention: you get an answer, but it tells you nothing about the general population. Short test periods capture only one or two market states, and the system may fail catastrophically when conditions shift.

Standard metrics are dangerously fragile. Faith demonstrates that shifting test start and end dates by just one or two months can swing CAGR% by several percentage points and MAR ratio by over ten percent. Maximum drawdown is a single point on the equity curve; if it happens to sit near a test boundary, a tiny date shift can eliminate it entirely. His solution is RAR% (Regressed Annual Return), which fits a linear regression through all points on the log equity curve rather than drawing a line from start to end. In his tests, CAGR% was thirty times more sensitive to end-date changes than RAR%, making RAR% a far more stable measure of true system performance.

Building on RAR%, Faith proposes R-Cubed (Robust Risk/Reward Ratio): RAR% divided by the product of the average of the five largest drawdowns and the average of their durations. Unlike MAR, which depends on a single worst event, R-Cubed uses five drawdowns, capturing both severity and duration. Adding the drawdown-cutting overfit rule to the Dual MA system moved CAGR% by 10.3% and MAR by 60%, but RAR% barely budged (0.4%) and R-Cubed moved only 17.3%. Robust measures resist showing large improvements from changes affecting only a few trades — the hallmark of curve fitting.

Sample size is critical but commonly misunderstood. There is no magic number, but fewer than twenty trades means a very large degree of error, while several hundred provides much more statistical significance. The key challenge is that infrequent rules — those that trigger only during rare market conditions like the end of large price bubbles — can never accumulate enough samples for statistical validity. Faith's recommendation is to generalize such rules so they fire more often, increasing the sample. Two practices that compound small-sample problems are single-market optimization (each market optimized separately, dramatically reducing trades per rule) and overly complex systems where many interacting rules make it impossible to assess how often each one actually fired.

Parameter scrambling is one of Faith's most practical recommendations. Before trading any system live, change its parameters by 20-25% from their optimal values and observe how much performance degrades. The Bollinger Breakout, moving from optimal parameters (350-day MA, -0.8 exit threshold) to scrambled values (250-day, 0.0 exit), dropped RAR% only slightly from 59% to 58%, but R-Cubed fell dramatically from 3.67 to 2.18. A dramatic drop from modest parameter changes signals fragility — expect similar or worse degradation in live trading.

Rolling optimization provides another reality check. Pick a date eight to ten years in the past, optimize on all prior data, then test on the two years immediately following. Repeat for multiple starting dates and compare how out-of-sample performance compares with in-sample results. Faith's rolling tests of the Bollinger Breakout showed RAR% varying from 8.3% worse to 6.3% better than the test prediction, while R-Cubed consistently underperformed by 5-30%. Optimal parameters differed for every period, illustrating the fundamental imprecision of historical testing.

Monte Carlo simulation maps the distribution of plausible outcomes using random resampling. Named for Monaco's casinos, the method was pioneered during the Manhattan Project by Richard Feynman to model uranium fission when equations couldn't be solved analytically. Faith distinguishes two methods: trade scrambling (randomly reordering individual trades) and equity-curve scrambling (randomly assembling chunks of the original equity curve). Equity-curve scrambling is superior because trade scrambling understates drawdowns by breaking the correlation between simultaneous losses. During the 1987 crash, normally uncorrelated markets all gapped against positions simultaneously; trade scrambling would artificially separate those losses, producing unrealistically mild drawdown estimates. Faith's implementation at Trading Blox scrambles 20-day chunks to preserve serial correlation.

The honest answer to "what will this system earn?" is that you cannot know. The best you can do is map the distribution of plausible outcomes and ensure your testing methods don't give you false confidence. A Monte Carlo report showing that 90% of simulated equity curves achieved a RAR% above 42% provides useful lower-bound confidence, but only if the underlying backtest is honestly constructed. Monte Carlo does not fix a bad backtest — it merely quantifies the uncertainty around whatever the base simulation shows.

Chapter 13: Bulletproof Systems

New traders invariably pick the system with the best historical metrics, assuming past superiority predicts future superiority. Faith calls this the New Trader Fallacy. If the historical test happened to contain thirteen quiet-trending years and seven volatile years, a system optimized for quiet trends will look better — but if the future skews volatile, it will underperform. Since we genuinely cannot predict future market-state distributions, the mature response is to build a program that doesn't depend on any particular regime.

Robustness requires two things: diversity and simplicity. Faith draws the analogy from ecology. Diverse ecosystems survive radical environmental change because they don't rely on a single species for any critical function. Simple organisms — bacteria, viruses — survive catastrophic shifts because they depend on fewer specific conditions. Complex, specialized organisms are the first casualties. Trading systems follow the same pattern: complex systems with many rules create dependencies on specific historical conditions that may never recur, while simple systems built on durable concepts (prices trend, markets have momentum, volatility clusters) remain adaptive across regime changes.

Market diversification increases the probability of encountering favorable conditions for your system at any given time. But Faith warns against excluding markets that underperform in backtests. Trends in some markets occur only every several years; a five-to-ten-year test is too short to capture them. He tells the story of the missed coffee trade of 1985-86: Dennis pulled the Turtles from coffee citing insufficient volume. The March 1986 contract then produced what would have been the single biggest Turtle trade ever — entry at 141.91, exit at 234.37, sixty-five bars, approximately $14 million in profit on a $5 million account, a 280% return. No other Turtle-era trade came close. The only legitimate reason to exclude a market is genuine illiquidity.

Faith categorizes markets into three classes. Fundamentals-driven markets (currencies, interest rates) are dominated by macroeconomic forces like central bank policy and produce the cleanest, most tradeable trends with the greatest liquidity. They are the easiest for trend followers. Speculator-driven markets (stocks, coffee, gold, crude oil) are perception-driven and harder because crowd psychology creates choppier price action. Aggregated derivative markets (index futures like the S&P) average out individual stock momentum, making them the hardest for trend-following strategies. Faith never traded the S&P as a Turtle for exactly this reason.

Trader memory also affects market character. The gold and silver trend of 1978, which drove gold to $900 per ounce and silver to $50, created a collective memory that made those markets choppy and nearly untradeable for trend followers for approximately twenty years. By 2006, that memory had faded sufficiently for gold to produce clean, tradeable moves again. The implication is that excluding markets based on recent behavior is shortsighted — you cannot predict when collective memory will fade enough for the next major trend to emerge.

System diversification — trading multiple non-correlated systems simultaneously — can dramatically improve robustness. Faith demonstrates this by splitting the Bollinger Breakout into long-only and short-only components. The long-only system produced an R-Cubed of 1.19; the short-only system just 0.41. Combined, they achieved an R-Cubed of 5.20 — a dramatic improvement driven by negative correlation. When one component draws down, the other tends to profit, smoothing the overall equity curve. The capital required for meaningful diversification across both markets and systems is substantial — minimum $100,000 for market diversification alone, and potentially $1 million or more for four or five independent systems. This capital barrier is one reason institutional trend followers deliver more consistent returns than individual traders.

Chapter 14: Mastering Your Demons

The Turtle principles were not new. The basic ideas had been espoused by famous traders long before Richard Dennis was born. Jesse Livermore, whose fictionalized biography Reminiscences of a Stock Operator first inspired Faith to trade, advocated many of the same principles a century earlier. The paradox is that simplicity itself is a barrier. People fight the methods because they cannot accept that such straightforward rules could actually work. The ego demands complexity, secrecy, and the feeling of possessing special knowledge.

Discretionary trading feeds the ego; systematic trading starves it. When the system specifies exactly when, how much, and in which direction to trade, there is nothing left for the ego to claim credit for. This is intolerable for many people. Faith observed online traders boasting of 200% returns from excessive leverage, then blowing up their accounts months later. The ego wants to be right, to predict, to know secrets — all of which work against profitable execution of a mechanical system.

Faith tells the story of the Great Ping-Pong Battle among the Turtles. One trainee was far superior at the game, beating everyone routinely 21-10. For a tournament, Faith changed strategy: conservative play, traditional grip, a sandpaper paddle to neutralize spin. The superior player's ego made him play too aggressively under pressure, and Faith won. The twist is revealing: that same Turtle failed in the trading program. His ego prevented him from following rules consistently, and he blamed his underperformance on Faith receiving "secret knowledge" from Dennis — a conspiracy theory born from the inability to accept that simple discipline outperformed natural talent.

Humility is the antidote. It accepts the future as unknowable, prevents over-prediction, stops traders from taking losses personally, and allows them to embrace simple systems without needing those systems to feel special. Faith admits his own ego failure: during a strong move when he held maximum units, he asked other Turtles how many they held — effectively bragging when they were underperforming. He overheard them saying, "Did you hear him today? What an ass." It was a formative moment. Consistency, not brilliance, is the single most critical factor in trading success. The Turtles' biggest advantage was being trained by a legendary trader, which gave them faith in the methods. Without that external authority, traders must build their own conviction through personal research and backtesting.

Beyond the trading floor, Faith extends the Turtle philosophy to life decisions. Failure is a prerequisite to learning, not an obstacle to be avoided. Children learn languages faster not because of superior brain plasticity but because they aren't afraid of sounding foolish. Adults are paralyzed by embarrassment. Faith observed this in Buenos Aires: students with months of immersion spoke better than those with years of classroom study, because immersion forces mistakes and mistakes drive learning. He has failed more often and in more variety than anyone he knows, and his biggest successes came only because he was willing to risk failing first.

The sunk cost principle applies to life paths as directly as it applies to trading positions. Don't stay on a wrong path because of time already invested. When the market signals a trade isn't working, don't hope — exit. Apply the same logic to careers, relationships, and projects. Faith's personal experience validated this: his company's stock collapsed, a divorce left him nearly broke, and he found himself working a conventional job for the first time since his Turtle days. The feared outcome proved far less devastating than the fear itself. He discovered that what he actually enjoyed — interesting conversations over lunch, challenging projects — required very little money, and losing his wealth forced him to develop management skills and empathy that proved invaluable later.

Money, Faith argues, is easier to make when you don't want it badly. One Turtle was so desperate for money that he destroyed his telephone in rage when the market moved against his position. That desperation made consistent execution impossible. Faith was successful at least partly because he cared about trading well rather than about the money. When money becomes the objective rather than a byproduct of excellent execution, it distorts every decision. The ego wants the money as proof of worth; the disciplined trader wants the process as proof of consistency.

Faith recommends several resources for traders working on the psychological dimension: Van Tharp, Brett Steenbarger, Ari Kiev, and Mark Douglas. He adds an important caveat: trend following is not for most people. Each trading style requires a specific psychological makeup, and the critical task is to match your personality to the style that suits you. Someone who needs frequent validation will be miserable as a trend follower, where most trades lose. Someone who can't tolerate being wrong frequently should not be a countertrend trader. The Turtle principles work, but only for those whose temperament is compatible with the patience, discipline, and emotional detachment they require.

Bonus Chapter: Original Turtle Trading Rules

The bonus chapter lays out the complete, unabridged Turtle system in mechanical detail. A complete trading system answers every decision a trader faces: what to buy, how much, when to enter, when to exit a loser, when to exit a winner, and how to execute. Nothing is left to judgment. Richard Dennis himself acknowledged that the rules could be published in a newspaper and no one would follow them — the key was consistency and discipline, not secrecy.

The Turtles traded liquid U.S. futures across four exchanges: Treasury bonds and notes on the CBOT, currencies and eurodollars on the CME, metals on COMEX, and energies on NYMEX. They excluded grains (Dennis already held maximum legal positions) and meats (corruption in the floor pits, later confirmed by an FBI sting operation). All positions were sized in units calibrated to volatility: 1 unit = the number of contracts where a 1-ATR move equals 1% of account equity. A heating oil example with N of 0.0141 and a $1 million account yields 16 contracts per unit. Turtles received a unit-size sheet every Monday.

Risk was managed through a four-tier unit limit system. Maximum four units in any single market, six in closely correlated markets (heating oil and crude oil, gold and silver, currencies as a group), ten in loosely correlated markets, and twelve in any single direction. A Turtle who was "loaded" in yen held four units of Japanese yen futures. After the October 1987 crash, when the Fed cut rates overnight and the Turtles lost 40-60% of equity in a single day, these unit caps were revealed as the program's most critical safety mechanism. Without them, the losses from correlated interest-rate positions would have been catastrophic. The drawdown reduction rule added further protection: for every 10% drawdown from the year's starting equity, reduce the notional account by 20%. Down 10% on a $1 million account meant trading as if you had $800,000.

Entries used Donchian channel breakouts. System 1 entered on a 20-day high or low, with a filter that skipped signals if the previous breakout had been a winner (to avoid choppy markets), plus a failsafe that always entered at the 55-day breakout to prevent missing major trends. System 2 entered on a 55-day breakout with no filter — all signals taken. After initial entry, units were added at half-N intervals, up to a maximum of four units per market. Stops were set at 2N from entry (2% account risk), raised as each new unit was added so that all stops converged to 2N from the most recently added unit. The alternative Whipsaw strategy used tighter half-N stops with re-entry on return to the original price, keeping total risk at 2% across all four units but accepting more frequent small losses.

Stops were placed at 2N from entry, limiting each trade to 2% of account equity. As new units were added, stops for all earlier units were raised by half N, so that all stops converged to 2N from the most recently added unit. In a crude oil example with N of 1.20 and a 55-day breakout at 28.30: Unit 1 entered at 28.30 with a stop at 25.90; after Unit 2 entered at 28.90, both stopped at 26.50; after all four units were filled, all stops sat at 27.70. The alternative Whipsaw strategy used much tighter half-N stops with half-percent account risk per trade, re-entering at the original price if stopped out. Total risk stayed at 2% across all four units, but the Whipsaw produced more frequent small losses in exchange for better average entries when trends resumed.

Exits used the opposite breakout: System 1 exited on a 10-day breakout against the trade, System 2 on a 20-day breakout against. All units in a position exited simultaneously when the breakout level was touched. These exits are psychologically brutal — waiting for a 10- or 20-day new low in a winning long position can mean watching 20%, 40%, or even 100% of accumulated profits evaporate before the exit triggers. Maintaining discipline during these profit givebacks separates experienced, successful traders from everyone else.

Most annual profits came from only two or three large winning trades. Exiting those trades too early — locking in a comfortable 10-30% gain rather than holding for a possible 200% move — could devastate entire-year performance. The common wisdom that "you can never go broke taking a profit" was explicitly rejected by the Turtles. If you exit winners at 1N profit but stop out losers at 2N, you need twice as many winners as losers just to break even. The exit point cannot be considered in isolation; it interacts with every other component of the system.

Tactics completed the system. Dennis and Eckhardt advised against placing stop orders with brokers, because large positions would reveal strategy details to floor traders. Instead, Turtles held private trigger prices and executed via limit or market orders when those levels were hit. Limit orders were preferred over market orders for better fills, exploiting the natural "bounce" in price oscillation to enter at the lower end of a range. In fast markets — where prices could move thousands of dollars per contract in minutes — the rule was to wait for at least a temporary reversal before placing orders, avoiding the spike highs where panicked market orders invariably fill. When correlated markets signaled simultaneously, the rule was to buy the strongest and short the weakest, measuring strength by how many N units price had moved since breakout.

Trading & Investing Lessons

Edge Erodes When Crowded

The Donchian Trend system's decline from star performer to laggard is a textbook case of what happens when a strategy becomes popular. As breakout trading became widely known, front-runners learned to anticipate the signals, effectively taxing every breakout trader. This applies to any trading strategy, indicator, or signal service: the more people following the same approach, the smaller the edge for each participant.

Simplicity Is a Feature, Not a Bug

The Dual Moving Average — about as simple as a system can be — outperformed every more complex system in Faith's tests. Adding rules, filters, and conditions feels like progress but often just adds fragility. When in doubt, choose the simpler approach with fewer parameters to optimize.

Stops Can Hurt Trend Followers

The finding that adding stops to trend-following systems degraded every performance metric challenges the universal "always use stops" advice. For trend followers, the real drawdown risk is profit giveback at trend reversals, not entry-risk losses. This doesn't mean stops are unnecessary for all strategies, but it means their effect must be tested, not assumed.

Volatility Normalization Is Non-Negotiable

Trading one contract per market without adjusting for volatility is dangerously misleading. A large gain in a quiet market may not offset a small loss in a volatile one. N-based sizing ensures every position carries roughly equal dollar risk, which is what makes diversification genuinely effective rather than cosmetic.

Drawdown Tolerance Is Always Overestimated

Faith's personal drawdown as a Turtle reached approximately 70%. Almost no one can psychologically sustain that. His rule of thumb: target drawdowns in simulation at most half what you believe you can tolerate, because out-of-sample shocks will inevitably exceed historical testing.

Track Records Are Mostly Luck

In a universe of 1,000 traders, roughly sixteen average traders will post excellent ten-year records through pure luck, outnumbering the five or six genuinely excellent ones. Three-year records are even more meaningless. Good investors invest in people and process, not past performance.

Key Takeaways

Psychology Determines Results

Identical rules taught to identically trained traders produced wildly different outcomes. The difference was entirely psychological: willingness to take every signal, tolerance for drawdowns, and discipline to avoid modifying rules during losing streaks.

The Edge Comes from Human Nature

Cognitive biases create systematic market inefficiencies that trend following exploits. These biases are permanent because human psychology doesn't change across centuries.

Simple Systems Beat Complex Ones

Across six tested systems, simpler approaches matched or exceeded more complex ones on every metric. Complexity adds curve-fitting risk and fragility without meaningful edge improvement.

Position Sizing Matters More Than Entries

The N-factor volatility normalization was genuinely innovative and saved millions during the 1987 crash. How much you trade is more important than when you trade.

Most Backtests Are Fiction

Trader effects, random luck, optimization paradox, and overfitting systematically inflate backtest results. Robust measures like RAR% and R-Cubed resist this inflation far better than CAGR% and MAR.

Consistency Is the Only Strategy

A positive-expectation system works only if you execute it consistently. Skipping signals, reducing size during drawdowns, or modifying rules destroys the statistical edge the system depends on.

Diversify Across Markets and Systems

Trading multiple non-correlated systems across many markets dramatically improves robustness. Excluding markets because they underperformed in backtests can cost you the biggest trade of your career.

Risk Management Is Not Optional

Unit limits, volatility-based sizing, and maximum directional exposure rules prevented catastrophe during the 1987 crash. These constraints saved more money than any entry signal ever made.

Contrarian Ideas

The Dennis-Eckhardt Bet Was a Draw

Despite the popular narrative that Dennis won (trading can be taught), Faith argues it was a draw. Half the Turtles failed, proving that psychological readiness cannot be taught even when methods can.

Stops Hurt Trend-Following Performance

Adding stops to moving-average crossover and time-exit systems degraded every performance metric. The no-stop case was best across MAR, CAGR, Sharpe, and drawdown measures.

Smooth Returns Signal Hidden Risk

High Sharpe ratios and smooth equity curves can mask catastrophic tail risk. LTCM and Amaranth both had excellent risk-adjusted metrics right up until their collapses.

Three-Year Track Records Are Meaningless

Random coin-flip systems routinely beat real systems over three-year periods. In a pool of 1,000 traders, lucky average performers outnumber genuinely excellent ones by roughly 3-to-1 even over ten years.

You Should Expect to Lose Most Trades

The Donchian system produced 3,746 losers versus 1,854 winners. Expecting to be wrong on most trades removes emotional attachment to individual outcomes and paradoxically makes consistent execution easier.

Being Right Is a Hindrance

The need to predict and be validated by outcomes is a cognitive trap. Traders who don't care about being right are free to execute systems that win through mathematics rather than prophecy.

Proposals & Frameworks

The Four Core Turtle Principles

  1. Trade with an edge: Use a positive-expectation system backed by statistical evidence
  2. Manage risk: Size positions to survive long enough to capture the edge
  3. Be consistent: Execute the plan every time, especially during losing streaks
  4. Keep it simple: Simple systems hold up better across changing market conditions

The E-Ratio Framework

A quantitative method for measuring entry edge: normalize MFE and MAE by ATR, average across all trades, divide. Values above 1.0 confirm positive edge; below 1.0 confirm negative edge. E-Ratios measured at different time horizons (E5, E10, E70) reveal whether the edge is short-term or long-term.

Robust Performance Metrics

RAR% (regression-based return) replaces CAGR%; R-Cubed (RAR% / length-adjusted average drawdown) replaces MAR ratio; Robust Sharpe (RAR% / standard deviation) replaces classic Sharpe. All use every data point rather than depending on start/end dates or single extreme events.

Four Sources of Backtest Divergence

  1. Trader effects: Strategy crowding erodes the edge
  2. Random effects: Luck inflates historical results
  3. Optimization paradox: Selecting best parameters guarantees overprediction
  4. Overfitting: Rules tuned to too few instances have no generalizability

The Unit-Based Risk Management System

All positions expressed in volatility-normalized units. Four-tier limits (single market: 4, correlated: 6, directional: 10, uncorrelated: 12) cap aggregate exposure. Drawdown triggers reduce notional account by 20% for every 10% decline. Stops at 2N, raised as units are added. The entire framework ensures no single position, market, or direction can cause catastrophic loss.

Three Market Classes

  1. Fundamentals-driven: Currencies, interest rates — cleanest trends, highest liquidity
  2. Speculator-driven: Stocks, commodities — perception-based, harder to trend-follow
  3. Aggregated derivatives: Index futures — momentum averaged out, hardest for trend following

What Didn't Fit

The Turtle Selection Process

The screening test consisted of sixty-three true/false questions and eleven short-answer prompts, including questions like "The majority of traders are always wrong — true or false?" (the word "always" made it deliberately tricky) and "Name a risky thing you have done and why." Dennis and Eckhardt screened for what candidates did not believe as much as what they did. Faith's skepticism about a "philosopher's stone" — a glass disk with lines that supposedly predicted prices — signaled the right mindset. The first class included remarkably diverse backgrounds: a D&D manual editor, a linguistics PhD from the University of Chicago, the Massachusetts state chess champion, a professional blackjack and backgammon player, and an accountant. The common thread was comfort with probability and game theory, not market experience. Surprising finding: raw intelligence did not reliably correlate with eventual trading success.

The Death of the Trading Pits

Electronic exchanges were already replacing open-outcry trading floors by 2007. The pits offered a unique information advantage: you could literally see fear and greed on traders' faces, read the body language of a scalper getting squeezed, and hear the pitch of panic in voices. Electronic markets eliminated this human-readable data but created their own opportunities: dramatically lower execution costs, the ability to trade millions of dollars without visibly moving the market, millisecond fill confirmations, and access to global markets around the clock. The hedgers, scalpers, and speculators didn't disappear; they just hid behind screens.

The FBI Meat Pit Sting

One reason the Turtles were told to exclude meat futures wasn't just low volume — it was corruption. Years after the Turtle program, the FBI conducted a major undercover sting operation in the Chicago meat pits, resulting in indictments of numerous floor traders for price manipulation. Dennis's instinct to avoid these markets was vindicated by federal law enforcement.

Faith's 1987 Crash Survival

Faith's account of the 1987 crash is one of the most specific in trading literature. He was short approximately 1,200 eurodollar contracts and 600 T-bills while long gold, silver, and currencies. He made money on crash day itself. But overnight, the government slashed interest rates without warning. Eurodollars gapped open more than two full points higher the next morning — roughly $5,500 per contract loss with no exit possible. Gold opened down $25, silver down over a dollar. Total damage: approximately $11 million on a $20 million account, wiping out an entire year of profits in a single gap. His survival was entirely due to volatility-based position sizing: because he used 1.5-ATR stops rather than 2-ATR stops, he held the same relative position size as other Turtles despite the tighter stops, and the position wasn't catastrophically oversized.

Trader Memory and Market Character

Markets change personality as participants forget past extreme moves. The gold and silver trend of 1978, which drove gold to $900 per ounce and silver to $50, created a "trader memory" that made those markets choppy and nearly untradeable for trend followers for approximately twenty years. By 2006, the 1978 memory had faded sufficiently that gold's spring move was dramatically easier to trade. Faith uses this to argue against excluding markets that haven't trended recently: you simply cannot predict when collective memory will fade enough for the next major trend to emerge.
