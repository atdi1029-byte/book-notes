Glossary

Cycle Hierarchy

Long-Term Dominant Cycle: Cycles longer than 1 year, ranging from 2 to 11 years by market. The 4-Year Cycle in stocks, 5.5-year in gold, 3-year in corn. Sets the longest-term trend direction.

Seasonal Cycle: Yearly cycle with highs and lows at approximately the same time each year. Soybeans low Jun-Oct, high Apr-Jul. Gold (bull) tops Q1, bottoms Q2. Currencies low May-Sep, high Nov-Apr.

Primary Cycle (PC): The dominant weekly cycle. S&P averages 22 weeks, T-Bonds 21 weeks, gold 18 weeks. Sets the trend for the daily Trading Cycle.

Trading Cycle (TC): The daily cycle you trade. Measured bottom to bottom, averaging 14–25 bars. Most cluster in the 18–22 bar range.

Alpha Cycle: First half of the Trading Cycle. Always begins when the Trading Cycle begins.

Beta Cycle: Second half of the Trading Cycle. Begins when Alpha bottoms, ends when the Trading Cycle bottoms.

Cycle Concepts

Right Translation: In a bull market, cycle crests shift to the right — the upswing is longer than the downswing. Higher crests and higher troughs. Average move from bottom to top = 3 weeks, top to bottom = 1 week.

Left Translation: In a bear market, cycle crests shift to the left — the downswing is longer. Lower crests and troughs. Average move from bottom to top = 1 week, top to bottom = 3 weeks.

70% Timing Band: The middle 70% of historical cycle lengths. 7 out of 10 turns occur within this range. By the last day of the band, 90%+ of cycles have turned. ~20% occur before the band, ~10% after.

Centered Detrend: Moving average of the same length as the suspected cycle, plotted at the midpoint (centered back half the cycle). Eliminates effects of longer cycles. Cannot be used in real time (lags by half the cycle).

Half-Span Detrend: Moving average of half the cycle length, plotted at current time. Approximates centered detrend behavior in real time.

Oscillators & Patterns

RSI3M3: RSI(3) smoothed with a 3-bar moving average. Buy line = 30, sell line = 70–75. Primary daily oscillator for Trading Cycle identification.

RSI3M3 Detrend: RSI3M3 minus its 5-bar moving average crossover. Catches signals in strong trends where RSI3M3 stays above its buy line.

CCI (Commodity Channel Index): Standard buy/sell at ±100. Bressert uses smoothed versions: 5-day CCI smoothed with 8-term MA for T-Bond Trading Cycles (buy -35, sell 60); 30-month CCI for S&P 4-Year Cycles.

MACD Detrend: The spread between the MACD line and its signal/crossover. Turns before the MACD itself. Generates entries well before the traditional MACD crossover.

3-10 Oscillator: 3-day MA minus 10-day MA, with 16-term crossover. Sensitive daily oscillator. Two key patterns: TSS and SBS.

TSS (Two Step Sell): Oscillator high A above crossover, low B below crossover, then higher “second step” high C above A. Two-day downturn = setup. Trigger = drop below previous week’s low.

SBS (Small Bump Sell): Following a low below crossover, high B above crossover and zero, deeper low C below crossover, then “small bump” high D above crossover but below B. Two-day downturn = setup.

Kiss Pattern: MACD bouncing off (or “kissing”) its crossover line. Bull Kiss = detrend rises above zero, dips back to test crossover, bounces. Bear Kiss = mirror image. More reliable when the price extreme before the Kiss is above (buy) or below (sell) the previous extreme.

Hook Pattern: Oscillator drops below -1, rises (usually staying below 0), drops below crossover, then recovers above it. Shaped like a fishing hook. Primarily in bear markets but still produces 10%+ moves.

Trading Mechanics

Setup: An oscillator formation that signals a potential turn — drop below buy line, divergence, crossover penetration, or specific pattern completion.

Trigger Entry: Price-based mechanical entry that removes emotion. Buy = price exceeds high of setup bar. Sell = price drops below low of setup bar. Stop placed at the cycle extreme.

Mid-Cycle Pause (MCP): Price projection technique. Measure diagonal from cycle peak to trough, find midpoint, measure vertical distance to previous extreme, mirror it. ±10% range = target zone.

Speed Resistance Lines: From cycle low to high, divide by 3 (.33 and .66). Draw lines from the low through those points. Upper speedline break = prices drop to lower. Lower speedline break = test cycle low.

Money Contract: First of three contracts. Take profit equal to dollar risk, usually within 5 days. Reduces total exposure by two-thirds.

Chapter 1: Focus on Cycles

Everything in nature moves in cycles — planets orbit the sun, tides rise and fall, salmon spawn on schedule, even your heartbeat follows a rhythm. Walter Bressert spent decades documenting this principle in financial markets, building on the Foundation for the Study of Cycles, a non-profit that has cataloged over 3,000 cycles in weather, war, sunspots, drought, marriage rates, inflation, bank failures, real estate, manufacturing, and stock prices since the 1930s. His core premise: fundamentals move markets, but the fundamental picture is always most bullish at tops and most bearish at bottoms. Cycle analysis assumes fundamental events will occur to move prices in the direction of the cycle — the news follows the cycle, not the other way around.

Example 1-1 — Composite of three dominant cycles forming market price pattern [view]

Each market has its own cycle profile of several dominant cycles. The basic principle: identify the longest dominant cycle affecting price, then work down cycle by cycle to the smallest dominant cycle you wish to trade. Bressert identifies four dominant cycles in every market: (1) Long-term dominant cycles longer than one year, ranging from 2 to 11 years — the 4-year cycle in stocks, 5.5-year cycle in gold, 3-year in corn, 6-year in crude oil. (2) The Seasonal Cycle, a yearly rhythm especially powerful in agricultural markets but also present in metals, financials, and currencies. Soybeans low June–October, high April–July. Gold (bull) tops Q1, bottoms Q2. Currencies low May–September, high November–April. (3) The Primary Cycle, the dominant weekly cycle averaging 9 to 22 weeks by market. (4) The Trading Cycle, measured in days, typically 14 to 25 bars from bottom to bottom, containing two sub-cycles: the Alpha Cycle (first half) and Beta Cycle (second half).

Example 1-2 — Full cycle hierarchy: Seasonal → Primary → 1/2 Primary → Trading → Alpha/Beta [view]

The nesting is the key to the entire system. Each longer cycle determines the trend for the next shorter cycle. If the Primary Cycle is moving up, the Trading Cycle shows right translation — higher crests, higher troughs, tops occurring late in the cycle (bullish). On average the move from bottom to top takes three weeks, top to bottom one week. If the Primary Cycle is moving down, left translation appears — lower crests, lower troughs, tops occurring early. Average bottom to top is one week, top to bottom is three weeks. This single observation tells you whether to hold through a pullback or take profits quickly. Every PC low was above the previous PC low in a bull phase — every one. When about two-thirds of PC lows started falling below the previous low, the market had shifted to bear.

Example 1-3 — Right translation (bull) vs. left translation (bear) showing how crests shift [view]

From this nesting, Bressert distills the fundamental cyclic trading rule: if the longer-term cycle is moving up, buy the troughs of the shorter cycle. If the longer-term cycle is moving down, sell the crests. The challenge is knowing when the cycle is about to turn — which is where oscillators and timing bands enter.

The 70% Timing Band is Bressert’s central timing tool. By eliminating the most extreme 15% on each side of historical cycle lengths, he narrows the window where 7 out of 10 cycles will turn. The critical probability insight: by the last day of the 70% band, 90% or more of cycles have already turned. About 20% turn before the band, 10% after. The probability of a successful trade is much greater later in the timing band than earlier. Patience is mathematically rewarded.

Bressert publishes comprehensive timing band tables for over 20 markets. Key values: S&P Index Primary Cycle averages 22 weeks, band 16–25. T-Bonds have two interactive Primary Cycles — 21-week and 14-week — with Trading Cycle averaging 21 market days, band 15–30. Gold Primary Cycle averages 18 weeks, band 11–23, with Trading Cycle averaging 15 market days, band 13–20. Japanese Yen Primary Cycle averages 31 weeks, band 26–44. Soybeans Primary Cycle averages 14 weeks, band 11–22. All Trading Cycles are provided in both calendar days (include weekends, because the world doesn’t stop on weekends) and market days (for oscillator calculations).

An important adjustment: timing bands should be shifted for bull versus bear markets. In a bull market, the trough-to-crest phase stretches from the average to the end of the band (longer upswings), while the crest-to-trough phase compresses from the beginning to the average (shorter pullbacks). In bear markets, the reverse. For the S&P 1/2 Primary Cycle: trough-to-crest averages 5 weeks with a 2–10 week band. In a bull market, expect 5–10 weeks up. In a bear, 2–5 weeks. Crest-to-trough averages 5 weeks, band 1–9. Bull = 1 –5 weeks down. Bear = 5–9 weeks. This single adjustment can dramatically narrow your trading window.

Bressert details a 7-step process for plotting timing bands on a chart, using a color code: Red for Trading Cycle trough-to-trough, Brown for Alpha/Beta trough-to-trough, Green for trough-to-crest, Orange for crest-to-trough. At Step 7, three overlapping bands converge to give the most probable time for the Trading Cycle trough: (1) the red T-T of the Trading Cycle, (2) the brown T-T of the Beta Cycle, and (3) the orange C-T of the Beta Cycle. The Trading Cycle will frequently bottom within the overlap area of all three. Once the trough is confirmed, it becomes the new Day Zero for the next cycle.

Chapter 2: Technical Tools with Cycles

Cycles tell you when a turn is likely. Technical tools tell you where. Bressert combines the two through three specific techniques, each anchored to individual cycles. The Mid-Cycle Pause Price Objective (MCP) projects how far prices will travel to the next cycle top or bottom. The construction: measure the diagonal distance from a cycle peak B to the following trough C, find the midpoint X, measure the vertical distance from X down to the price low of the preceding cycle A (call it X–X1), then mirror that same distance above X to get X2. A 10% range around X2 gives you the zone where the next cycle of the same length should top. The MCP works identically in bear markets (mirror image).

MCP construction — bull market 5-step process [view]
MCP construction — bear market [view]

The critical MCP rule: it will only be met if the next longer cycle continues in the same direction. An MCP for a Trading Cycle requires the Primary Cycle to keep trending. An MCP for a Primary Cycle requires the Seasonal Cycle to cooperate. If the longer cycle has turned, the MCP is invalidated. There are four basic MCP/retracement situations: bull market before a top (MCP ahead, 60-40% range as resistance), bear market before a top (MCP met, retracement expected), bull market before a bottom (MCP met, retracement expected), and bear market before a bottom (MCP ahead, 60-40% as support).

60-40% retracements derive from Fibonacci .618 and .382. Most Primary Cycles, Trading Cycles, and Alpha Cycles retrace at least 40%. In practice, waiting for prices to enter the 38–62% retracement zone before taking a buy signal is “like stretching a rubber band — the further it is stretched, the more powerful the snap back.” In T-Bonds, five of six Trading Cycle bottoms retraced at least 38%, and only one exceeded 62%. After an extended uptrend, a close below a 62% retracement will frequently signify a trend reversal — a dual-use indicator.

60-40% retracement construction and four MCP/retracement situations [view]

Speed Resistance Lines, developed by Edson Gould and popularized by Gerald Appel, divide the vertical distance from a cycle low to high by three (.33 and .66). Lines drawn from the low through those points create two speedlines. When the upper speedline is decisively penetrated (entire day’s range below the line, or penetration exceeding 1.5% of price), prices often drop to the lower speedline. When the lower breaks, expect a test of the cycle low. These are most powerful when prices retrace 60–40% to meet a speedline within the overlap area of timing bands as oscillators overextend.

Speed Resistance Lines — bull and bear construction [view]

Cyclic Support/Resistance Lines are drawn horizontally from the midpoint of the MCP diagonal. These levels can offer support in declining markets AND resistance in rising ones, sometimes months or years after being established. The longer the cycle that created the level, the greater its power. On weekly and monthly range charts, old highs and lows that have stopped prices more than twice become the most reliable S/R levels.

The key insight of this chapter: no single technical tool works in all markets — each must be isolated through historical research. But cycles provide the “rule” that brings the technical picture into focus through the use of time. A Mid-Cycle Pause price objective is only valid for the cycle in which it occurs. A 60–40% retracement is only valid if the next longer cycle continues in the same direction. Support and resistance lines are always valid, but cycles tend to top and bottom at or near these levels. Technical tools plus cycles plus oscillators equals the ability to anticipate trends and trend reversals.

Combined with timing bands, these tools provide a complete framework: the timing band says when to expect a turn, the 60–40% zone and S/R levels say where, the MCP says how far, and oscillators will say now. Each tool alone is useful but incomplete. Together they produce what Bressert calls high probability Oscillator/Cycle Combinations.

Chapter 3: Focus on Oscillators

An oscillator is any mathematically calculated indicator that moves up and down with price to show overbought and oversold conditions. The problem: not every overbought reading is a significant top, not every oversold reading is a meaningful bottom. That is where the combination with cycles becomes essential. Bressert’s core insight: cycle analysis provides the TIME dimension, oscillators provide the PRICE dimension. Together they confirm tops and bottoms as they form and project the next turning point into the future. Without the cycle’s timing context, oscillator signals are mostly noise.

The mechanical system has two components. The Setup is an oscillator formation: the oscillator drops below a buy line, shows divergence, penetrates a crossover, or completes a specific pattern. The Trigger is a price-based entry: usually exceeding the high of the setup bar for buys, or dropping below the low for sells. Once the trigger fires, you enter with a predetermined stop at the cycle extreme. This removes emotion completely — Bressert calls these “no thinkums.”

Seven techniques enhance oscillator performance: (1) Price/oscillator turns — simplest approach, prices and oscillator turn at approximately the same time. Problem: most oscillators have “wiggles” that aren’t real turns. (2) Smoothing — eliminates false wiggles, normally with a simple moving average. (3) Levels (buy/sell lines) — eliminate false turns and indicate where tops/bottoms can be expected (70/30 for Stochastic, ±100 for CCI). Review buy/sell lines every 6–12 months. (4) Crossover lines — excellent filters; entries not as close to the turn but probability greatly increased. (5) The zero line — often acts as support/resistance for oscillators; frequently an important component in patterns. (6) Price/oscillator patterns — combine multiple techniques to identify recurring formations. (7) Setup/trigger entries — turn patterns into mechanical trades.

The three-step development process for any Oscillator/Cycle Combination: (1) the TIME is right for a cycle turn, as determined by timing bands, (2) the Price/Oscillator Pattern sets up, (3) price activity triggers entry by exceeding the buy/sell stop. The result is a pre-determined probability of success based on historical research. No single oscillator picks every top and bottom — use several. Learn one well first, then expand your toolkit.

The trigger entry normally puts you in on strength when buying and weakness when selling. For buys: a buy stop above the high of the day that turned the oscillator up, or the day of the crossover, or the day before/after either of those. For any of these, the stop could also be placed above the next larger time period (daily oscillator upturn → buy stop above high of that week). In some situations a close exceeding the high, or a pivot point, may be preferable. The trigger entry eliminates emotional attachment — the entry is pre-determined by historical performance.

Research requirements are non-negotiable: minimum 5 years on any oscillator pattern, preferably 10 or 20. You need a large number of samples to establish the confidence to take positions against prevailing sentiment. Without enough research, you will encounter unanticipated patterns that almost always cost money. Keep a notebook — over time, reviewing notes reveals recurring gems “like money in the bank.” Do not add notes to original research or you will lose direction.

Bressert acknowledges the limits honestly: cycles contract, extend, and skip beats. Oscillators do NOT always overextend at cycle highs/lows. Even when they do, they often reach their most extreme before or after the exact price turn. Awareness of these limitations demands development of high probability parameters through three pillars: (1) don’t expect one oscillator to catch everything — use several, (2) develop historically researched patterns with mechanical setups and triggers, (3) use market-proven money management to control risk and optimize profits.

An important caveat on buy/sell lines: Bressert uses two approaches to determine them. The first looks for the level of greatest overextension reached by most cycle turns. The second looks for a less extreme level that still identifies cycle turns when followed by an oscillator turn and trigger entry. For example, the S&P MACD Detrend has a buy line at -90 (greatest overextension) but a -70 level also includes all 4-year cycle lows and was met or exceeded by a rise above the crossover. Both approaches should be researched and followed.

Chapter 4: Centered Detrending

Centered detrending is the foundation for identifying cycles visually. Step 1: have an idea of the approximate cycle length. Step 2: calculate a moving average of that length on the close. Step 3: center it — plot it at the midpoint, not the current bar. A 20-week moving average gets plotted back 10.5 weeks (conveniently rounded to 11 weeks back). The centered moving average becomes the zero line, and the bars plotted relative to it form the centered detrend. This eliminates the effects of all cycles longer than the one you’re searching for, letting individual cycles stand out clearly.

Chart D-2 — Weekly S&P 1983-1990 with 20-week centered detrend, 16 cycles identified, median length 21.5 weeks [view]

The basic premise: cycle lows occur below the centered moving average, highs occur above it. Only in extremely rare situations does the reverse happen. In the S&P from 1983 to 1990, the median Primary Cycle was 21.5 weeks. 75% of lows occurred 15–26 weeks from the previous low. Since 1950, the median has been 22 weeks.

One of Bressert’s most important insights: cycle highs and lows are not always price highs and lows. In the S&P (Chart D-3), a detrended cycle low at point B was at a higher price than the actual price low four weeks earlier at point C. The uptrend from rising 4-year and 8-year cycles prevented the weaker 20-week cycle from making a substantial price decline. The detrend reveals where the actual cycle low occurred even when the price chart obscures it. This phenomenon appears across gold, Swiss Franc, T-Bonds, and soybeans.

Chart D-3 — S&P showing detrended cycle low at higher price than actual price low (strong uptrend effect) [view]

In gold, the dominant weekly cycle is 18 weeks. Chart D-5 shows weekly gold from 1981 to 1987 detrended with an 18-week centered moving average, with cycle lows clearly visible in the detrend panel. Chart D-6 zooms in on a prolonged trading range where the price chart suggested a cycle bottom at point D, but the detrend reveals the actual cycle low occurred weeks earlier at point C — a tug-of-war between longer-term cycles moving up and shorter-term cycles moving down. Once the Primary Cycle bottomed, gold was free to move rapidly higher. The centered detrend makes you aware this can happen, helping determine direction when markets break out of wedge or triangle patterns.

The Swiss Franc has a monthly 4-year cycle and a weekly 22-week Primary Cycle. Chart D-10 shows the Swiss Franc with both 50-week and 22-week centered detrends. The 50-week detrend illustrates the seasonal tendency: lows May–September, highs November–April. The 22-week detrend reveals Primary Cycle fluctuations fitting the 80% timing band of 16–28 weeks. The Swiss Franc also has an 8-week Trading Cycle (timing band 6–11 weeks) that provides some of the lowest-risk trading opportunities in currencies.

T-Bonds present a special case: two interactive Primary Cycles of 21 weeks and 14 weeks. Most 21-week cycles are composed of two smaller 14-week cycles. Only four cycles rose to a top in 3–5 weeks — all in bear moves. The Federal Reserve effect can extend cycle lengths and keep oscillators at extremes for extended periods, making T-Bonds particularly tricky but rich with opportunities for those who understand the interplay.

For T-Bond daily Trading Cycles: 65% bottom 17–23 market days from the previous low. 35% bottom 28–30 days from the low. Guideline: expect the low at 17–23 days; if prices keep declining after day 23, no bottom is likely until day 28–30. All cycles with a 14–22 day count to the high then made the Trading Cycle low at 28–30 days — “money in the bank” information.

The critical limitation: centered detrending cannot be used for real-time trading because the centered moving average lags by half the cycle period. It should be used to confirm tops and bottoms after they occur. For real- time decisions, you need oscillators and real-time detrends — the subject of the next two chapters. But always remember: almost all cycle highs occur above the centered moving average, and almost all cycle lows occur below it. This basic principle validates (or invalidates) your real-time analysis.

Chapter 5: The Detrend as an Oscillator

A current-time detrend uses a non-centered moving average, plotted at the current bar. Unlike the centered version, it can be used in real time, but it looks very different. With a 20-week real-time detrend in the S&P, Primary Cycle highs and lows don’t match the centered version, and many lows appear above the moving average. What stands out instead are the Seasonal lows. The 20-week moving average is close to half the Seasonal Cycle length, so it is Seasonal turns that dominate this detrend.

Chart D-17 — S&P 20-week real-time detrend showing Seasonal lows instead of Primary Cycle turns [view]

The half-span detrend offers a better compromise: a moving average of half the cycle length highlights that cycle’s turns somewhat like a centered detrend. A 10-week moving average (half of the 20-week Primary Cycle) shows Primary Cycle turns in the S&P reasonably well, though in half the cycles the detrend tops before prices top. The crucial feature: both real-time and half-span detrends establish levels — consistent overbought/oversold thresholds where cycles tend to turn.

Chart D-19 — S&P 10-week detrend with four levels (A, B, C, D) [view]

Level A: met or exceeded following every Primary Cycle low but one. Expectation: no cycle high will be made until Level A is reached. Level B: met or exceeded at 5 of 8 Primary Cycle highs. When this overbought level is reached late in the timing for a high, start looking for a top. Level C: met or exceeded on the downside following every PC top — approximate level for 6 of 9 PC lows in a bull market. No bottom until the detrend drops to C. Level D: a drop below this means a bottom is imminent. These levels persist for years and appear in most markets.

Monthly detrends reveal the biggest moves. Running 40, 20, and 10-month detrends on the S&P from 1960 to 1990 shows: Level A in the 40-month detrend at -7 was exceeded by every 4-year and 8-year cycle bottom. Level C in the 20-month detrend at -20 captured four of five 4-year lows. Level E in the 10-month detrend, combined with Level C in the 20-month and Level A in the 40-month, signals a 4-year or 8-year cycle bottom. When all three converge, you are looking at a generational buying opportunity. The 1982 S&P bottom showed shorter-term detrends bottoming before longer-term ones — a pattern to watch for at all major cycle lows.

Chart D-24 — Monthly S&P 1960-1990 with 40, 20, and 10-month detrends [view]

To discover levels and patterns, run 40, 20, and 10-term detrends for monthly, weekly, and daily charts in a condensed scale with as much history as possible. The levels from major turning points near major cycle lows often become the parameters governing the market for years to come.

The combination of multiple detrend timeframes provides a powerful framework. In Chart D-22, the S&P 40-week detrend showed every Seasonal low below the moving average (7 of 8), and every Seasonal high above it. At point 1, the 40-week was slightly below the moving average, the 10-week was at Level B, and the timing for a Primary Cycle low was right — making this bottom relatively low risk. At the same time, the detrend high at 3 was much lower than at 4, and barely reached Level A, indicating potential for a short at the 22-week Primary Cycle top. Three detrend timeframes, three confirming signals, one high-probability trade.

Weekly charts and oscillators are the most important and accurate for indicating Seasonal and Primary Cycle tops and bottoms. But monthly charts show the longer-term cycles and the potential for major tops and bottoms followed by the really big moves. The professional trader watches all three timeframes simultaneously and acts when they converge.

Chapter 6: Detrending Oscillators

The detrend is both an oscillator and a tool to improve other oscillators. An oscillator detrend is the spread between two components — most commonly the MACD minus its signal line. This spread turns before the MACD itself, often exactly at tops and bottoms. It has buy/sell lines that help identify major turns, and combined with a crossover generates trigger entries well before the traditional MACD crossover, with much smaller dollar risk.

Chart DO-1 — Weekly Yen 1981-1990 with MACD (middle) and MACD Detrend (bottom) showing six persistent levels [view]

In the Japanese Yen, the MACD detrend establishes levels that persist across years. Level LA (.18) started as a valid sell line, then lost effectiveness in the powerful bull until February 1987 when it slowed prices to form a Kiss Pattern. Level LB (.42) was set by the major high preceding the 1982 low and was exceeded at four of five Primary Cycle tops, all producing sizable declines. Level LC (.78) was reached only twice (both only by .01), both times the same week as the price high. Level LD (-.23) confirmed five of seven significant bottoms. Level LE (-.40) and LF (-.79) mark progressively deeper levels — the first reaction following a big decline often dwarfs subsequent turning points but eventually acts as a resistance level.

Bressert’s detailed comparison of the MACD versus its detrend at specific turns is revelatory. At Yen highs 3, 4, and 5: the detrend high was the same week as the price high in all three cases. The MACD high lagged by 2 weeks at highs 3 and 5. The downside MACD crossover occurred well after the price turn — too late for a useful entry. At four lows (B, C, D, E): three had the detrend bottom the same week as the price low, while the MACD bottomed weeks later. Using the MACD crossover for entry at these lows would have actually bought tops following three of the four lows. The detrend clearly outperforms.

Three possible entry patterns emerge: (1) sell the low of the week the detrend turns down, or buy the high of the upturn week; (2) use the 5-term crossover of the detrend as the setup, with the price extreme of the crossover week as trigger; (3) following an overextended detrend, use the MACD turn itself as the setup. Each has different timing and risk characteristics.

The Kiss Pattern is one of Bressert’s signature formations. A Bull Kiss: following a price low, the detrend rises above zero, dips back to test the crossover (dropping to .50 to -.99), then bounces. The upside crossover triggers a buy. A Bear Kiss is the mirror image. The pattern is more reliable when the price extreme before the Kiss is above (for buys) or below (for sells) the previous extreme. Chapter 9: MACD

The MACD, invented by Gerald Appel, is constructed from three exponential moving averages: a 12-day (fast) minus a 26-day (slow) produces the MACD line, and a 9-day EMA of the MACD is the signal/crossover. The MACD crossing its signal confirms tops/bottoms, but usually after the market has turned. The detrend between the MACD and signal turns earlier and more accurately. Bressert applies this to the Japanese Yen Primary Cycle, which has an 80% timing band of 26–44 weeks, average 31.

Eight lows (indicated by circles) occurred following sizable MACD declines — all followed by sizable price rises. In bull markets, expect Primary Cycle bottoms at MACD lows following sizable declines, to be followed by sizable rises.

The setup for bottoms: price must be 26+ weeks from the previous low AND the detrend must be below zero. Using the crossover as setup with trigger at the crossover week’s high gave 10 confirmations — 8 highly profitable, 1 serious loss, 3 did not enter. Confirmed bottoms: cycles 1, 2, 3, 4, 7, 9, 10, 11, 12, and 13.

For Primary Cycle highs: detrend must be above zero and drop below the crossover. Trigger when prices drop below the crossover week’s low. Critical guideline: if the crossover doesn’t happen by weeks 3–4, the uptrend should continue. 75% of entries occurred the week following crossover. If the Primary Cycle high fails to exceed the previous one, expect a sizable decline in both price and time (4 of 5 such cases produced big downmoves). About 25% of cycles will have a setup and trigger before the actual cycle high — these are likely to lose money, indicating the uptrend will continue.

Cycle highs made 17 weeks or less from the Primary Cycle low have potential for big downmoves. Highs made 20+ weeks tend to have small downmoves followed by continuation. Lower prices more than 4 weeks after trigger entry are likely to be followed by a downtrend into at least week 12 from entry.

Chapter 11: CCI

The Commodity Channel Index, published by Donald Lambert in Futures magazine in 1980, moves between sell (+100) and buy (-100) lines. It is calculated from the typical price (H+L+C)/3, a moving average of typical prices, and the mean deviation, scaled by the constant .015 so 70–80% of random fluctuations fall within ±100%. Bressert applies it to two very different cycles with spectacular results.

Chart CCI-1 — Monthly S&P 1950-1990 with 30-month CCI and detrend identifying every 4-year cycle bottom [view]

The 4-Year Cycle in the S&P uses a 30-month CCI with 8-month crossover on monthly data. From 1950 to 1990 (40 years): every 4-year high exceeded the sell line at +100. Every 4-year bottom dropped below zero. 6 of 9 bottoms dropped below the buy line at -100. Every 4-year high was followed by a decline in the detrend below the buy line at -90. The complete confirmation requires five conditions: (1) price low 32+ months from previous bottom, (2) detrend below -90 and turning up, (3) CCI below zero (preferably below -100), (4) CCI rising above crossover, (5) prices exceeding the crossover month’s high. This confirmed every single 4-year cycle bottom from 1953 to 1987 — a “stellar indicator.”

Bressert walks through each historical confirmation in detail: the 9/53 low (51 months), 10/57 (49 months), 6/62 (56 months), 10/66 (52 months), 5/70 (43 months), 10/74 (53 months), 3/78 (41 months — shortest ever but still above the 32-month minimum since 1917), 8/82 (53 months), and 10/87 (50 months — the hardest one, with confirmation taking 12 months because the CCI crossover high was not exceeded for a full year). Each case shows how the five conditions interacted and when the trigger entry fired.

Following a 4-year bottom, there is a period of “free time” until the CCI exceeds the sell line — during this time, market declines and Primary Cycle lows can be aggressively bought with high probability that prices will continue rising over the longer term. Following the high, there is free time until the CCI drops below zero. These free-time windows are among the most valuable insights in the book.

The 4-Week Trading Cycle in T-Bonds uses a smoothed 5-day CCI with 8-term MA crossover on daily data. Sell line at 60, buy line at -35 (not the standard ±100). The buy pattern: (1) prices 15–30 days from previous Trading Cycle low, (2) 10-day detrend below -.80, (3) CCI drops below -35 and turns up (ideally following a high above 60), (4) trigger above the upturn day’s high, (5) protective stop below the cycle’s lowest price. If the oscillator rises above the crossover then drops back below before rising above the sell line, raise the stop to below the crossover day’s low.

Chapter 13: 3-10 Oscillator

The 3-10 Oscillator is the 3-day moving average minus the 10-day moving average, with a 16-term moving average as crossover. It is more sensitive than the MACD, turning at overextensions to indicate highs and lows, but its real power comes from two specific patterns: the Two Step Sell (TSS) and the Small Bump Sell (SBS). These patterns identify Primary Cycle highs with remarkable consistency.

Validity rules are strict: A high is valid only when above the zero line AND the crossover, following a low below the crossover. A low is valid only when below the crossover, following a high above the crossover. This filtering eliminates most false signals. In Example 1, of 15 oscillator turns, only 6 qualify as valid setups.

TSS (Two Step Sell): (1) oscillator high A above the crossover; (2) oscillator low B below the crossover; (3) higher “second step” high C above both the crossover and the previous high A; (4) two-day downturn completes setup; (5) trigger = drop below previous week’s low. The higher second step signals exhaustion — the market climbed the stairs but couldn’t reach the next floor.

SBS (Small Bump Sell): (1) following a low below crossover at A, high B above crossover and zero line; (2) deeper low C below crossover and below A; (3) “small bump” high D above crossover and zero but below B; (4) two-day downturn completes setup. The small bump after a deeper low signals a failed rally attempt — the market couldn’t get back to where it was.

In gold from February 1988 to December 1989, every Primary Cycle high was followed by a completed sell pattern. 4 of 5 entered the market within 7 days of the top. 4 of 5 PC highs were identified by the TSS specifically. Of 9 SBS setups, only 5 triggered entry — all 5 occurred 9+ weeks from the PC low. Of 8 TSS entries, 4 occurred at PC highs, 3 in bear markets (4–6 weeks from the low).

Three rules eliminate most losing trades: (1) 8-Week Rule: don’t enter in an outside down week where the high is the highest of the past 8 weeks — in gold, prices often continue higher. (2) 6-Week Rule: if the downturn occurs less than 6 weeks from the PC low and the high is exceeded, use a modified trigger; cancel if not filled in 3 weeks. (3) 14-Week Rule: after the 14th week from the PC low, don’t enter short — you should be preparing to go long.

Key research findings from Table 3 (sorted by PC high): of 9 entries occurring 9–17 weeks from the PC low, 8 made the next PC low 3–6 weeks from entry. This is a “real jewel” — it keeps you from taking profits too soon and from trying to buy until at least week 3 after entry. From Table 4: entries less than 9 weeks from the PC low tend to be PC highs, indicating a bear market where the previous PC low will likely be broken. Table 5: median decline from entry to PC low is about 6%, no decline exceeded 10.1%.

The same TSS/SBS process can be applied to intraday data for earlier entries in anticipation of the daily trigger. Different markets may have different characteristics — some may favor TSS, others SBS. The only way to know is thorough research over 10+ years, cataloging bull and bear behaviors separately.

Chapter 14: Trading & Money Management

Bressert’s 12 Cardinal Mistakes of futures trading are drawn from decades of personal experience. (1) Lack of a game plan — 90%+ of traders he encountered had none. Must include: where to enter, how much to risk, where to exit if wrong, where to take profits if right, how much to make if right, a safety stop, and an approximate timeline. (2) Lack of money management — controlling risk through stops while balancing loss potential against profit potential. The Controlled Risk Money Management system trades in multiples of three contracts, each with a different profit objective. The Manual distills Bressert’s entire system into a streamlined reference, built around T-Bonds and the RSI3M3. The core message: standalone buy/sell signals don’t consider the whole picture. The centered detrend is built first: a 20-bar MA of the close, centered back 10 bars. Most cycle tops above the sell line at +0.80, most bottoms below the buy line at -0.80. A rise above +2.0 means a top is imminent; a drop below -2.0 means a bottom is ready. But the centered detrend lags by 10 bars — excellent for historical cycles, useless for real-time trading. Enter the RSI3M3.

The RSI3M3 overlaid on the centered detrend (Chart 5) shows cycle tops and bottoms almost as well as the centered version, but is current to the most recent bar. Four steps construct a mechanical buy signal: (1) RSI3M3 drops below buy line at 30, (2) oscillator turns up — this bar is the setup bar, (3) buy stop placed one tick above the setup bar’s high, (4) once entered, protective sell stop one tick below the cycle bottom. By waiting for the setup bar’s high to be exceeded (instead of trading on the close), accuracy improves 10–25%.

Trading & Investing Lessons

Cycles as a Market Timing Edge

Markets are not random. Documented cycles exist in every asset class. Knowing where you are in the cycle is the single most powerful edge a trader can have. When fundamentals look worst, the cycle is bottoming. When fundamentals look best, the cycle is topping. The 4-Year Cycle in the S&P has averaged 107% gains from bottom to top since 1917 — more than a doubling each time.

Confluence Is Everything

No single oscillator picks every turn. Stack confirmations: timing band says a bottom is due, RSI3M3 below 30 and turning up, CCI crosses above its crossover, 38–62% Fibonacci zone reached, EMA trend up, Keltner overextended. Three or more confluent signals produce dramatically higher win rates than any single signal.

The 3-Contract System

Money Contract locks in quick profit within 5 days, reducing risk by two-thirds. Short-Term captures the Trading Cycle. Long-Term stays on for the rare massive move. Without the Money Contract, 80% of T-Bond short trades would have lost money. With it, the system was consistently profitable.

Patience Pays More Than Precision

Probability is much higher later in the timing band. Waiting for three timing bands to overlap before taking a signal dramatically increases odds. Rush in early and you fight the flow. Wait for convergence and the market does the heavy lifting.

Translation Tells the Trend

Right translation = longer cycle is up. Bull: bottom to top 3 weeks, top to bottom 1 week. Bear: reversed. Knowing this tells you whether to hold or take profits.

Your Equity Has Cycles Too

Biggest losses come after biggest wins. Success breeds overconfidence. Remove profits at predetermined levels, reduce position sizes as equity grows, never change strategy during market hours.

Key Takeaways

Every Market Has a Cycle Profile

Four dominant cycles: long-term (2–11 years), Seasonal (annual), Primary (weekly), Trading (daily). Each longer cycle determines the trend for the next shorter one.

The 70% Timing Band

Seven of ten turns occur within the band. By the end, 90% are done. Late-band setups have dramatically higher success rates.

Oscillators + Cycles = Mechanical

Neither works well alone. Combined through Setup/Trigger entries, they produce signals with researched probabilities.

RSI3M3 Is the Workhorse

RSI(3) smoothed with 3-bar MA, buy 30, sell 70–75. Plus its detrend (minus 5-bar crossover) for strong-trend signals. Together they catch virtually all Trading Cycle turns.

Trade in Multiples of Three

Money, Short-Term, Long-Term. Different exit objectives. Be wrong on expectations and still make money.

Research Before Trading

Minimum 5 years (preferably 10–20) of backtesting. Keep a notebook. Catalog bull and bear separately. Review buy/sell lines every 6–12 months.

Contrarian Ideas

Fundamentals Are Lagging

Most bullish at tops, most bearish at bottoms. The news follows the cycle.

Cycle Lows Aren’t Price Lows

In powerful uptrends, the actual cycle low occurs at a higher price than the preceding price low. Traders fixated on price lows miss these turns.

Success Is Dangerous

Biggest losses follow biggest wins. Overconfidence breeds reckless sizing.

Oscillator Wiggles Are Meaningless

Without cycle timing context, oscillator signals are mostly noise.

Never Meet a Margin Call

It means you’re wrong. Meeting it only increases the loss.

What Didn’t Fit

Pivot Point Entry System

Bressert spent two years in the 1970s hand-charting intraday movement and discovered seven pivot points that professionals watch: yesterday’s high/low/close and today’s open/high/low/close. Combined with four time windows (the open, 30 minutes after, mid-day 11:20–11:45 Chicago time, 35 minutes before close), these create a mechanical entry system. A breakout above yesterday’s high in the last 35 minutes is far more significant than the same breakout 30 minutes after the open. Different markets behave differently — currencies can knock out both pivots in the last 30 minutes, while gold almost never does. Stops are placed at the most distant opposing pivot point, giving you a predetermined dollar risk before the order is placed. The system forces you to wait for the market to tell you it’s ready to move.

Calendar Days vs. Market Days

Bressert insists on counting Trading Cycle timing bands in calendar days including weekends because the world doesn’t stop. Currency transactions, metals trading, government decisions continue. Many cycle highs and lows show up as Friday or Monday extremes. But oscillator calculations must use market days only. This requires maintaining two separate timing band tables for each market — a detail most traders skip but Bressert considers essential.

The Outside Up Month at Major Bottoms

At the 1974 and 1982 S&P bottoms, the CCI and detrend turned up in the same month as an outside up month (higher high, higher low than previous month, closing near the high). This offered an aggressive buy signal before the full Setup/Trigger sequence completed. When a confirmed cycle bottom is followed by a test of the lows that holds, buy against those lows. One of the lowest-risk entries possible, though it doesn’t occur often.

Soybean Oil Seasonal Patterns

The CCI identified 8 of 9 Seasonal lows in soybean oil. All 8 confirmed lows had the CCI slightly above or below the -100 buy line. The crossover occurred within 0–2 weeks of the price low. In 8 of 8 years, the high of crossover week was exceeded the following week. Following entry, the nearest overhead trendline was exceeded in all 8 years. In 5 of 7 years, the move exceeded 20% from entry. Seasonal oscillators should be researched over 20–30 years minimum, but even then, in the heat of the market, accepting a single pattern as confirmation is hard — which is why multiple oscillator confirmations matter even more at Seasonal turns.

The Federal Reserve Effect on T-Bonds

The Fed can move interest rates for prolonged periods, extending Primary Cycle lengths and keeping oscillators at extremes far longer than in other markets. T-Bonds have two interactive Primary Cycles (21-week and 14-week), with most 21-week cycles actually composed of two 14-week cycles. This dual interaction makes T-Bonds tricky for pure cycle analysis but rich for those who understand the interplay and use multiple oscillators to sort the signals.
