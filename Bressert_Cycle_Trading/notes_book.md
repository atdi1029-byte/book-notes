## CHAPTER 1: FOCUS ON CYCLES (pp. 6-20)

### The Nature of Cycles
- Everything in nature moves in cycles: solar system around Milky Way, planets around sun, seasons, tides, full moon, animal migration, salmon spawning
- Human cycles: sleep cycle every 16 hours, monthly emotional highs/lows, heartbeat
- Foundation for the Study of Cycles has documented 3,000+ cycles since the 1930s
- Cycles exist in gold, silver, copper, S&P index, interest rates, currencies, soybeans, corn, cattle, sugar, and other agricultural/financial markets
- Key premise: fundamentals move markets, but fundamental picture is most bullish at tops and most bearish at bottoms. Cycle analysis assumes current fundamental info is relative only to current price structure and that fundamental events will occur to move prices in the direction of the cycle

### Basic Cyclic Concepts

#### Cycles in Prices
- "Trade with the trend" — if trend is up, buy dips; if down, sell rallies
- Cycles identify trends and trend reversals: once bottomed, trend is up until cycle tops; once topped, trend is down until cycle bottoms
- Each market has an individual CYCLE PROFILE of several dominant cycles
- Basic principle: identify the longest dominant cycle affecting price, then work down cycle-by-cycle to the smallest dominant cycle you wish to trade

#### The Four Dominant Cycles (Example 1-1, p.8 — composite of 3 cycles forming Composite Cycle 1A)
1. **LONG-TERM DOMINANT CYCLES** — longer than 1 year, establish longer-term trends. Range 2 to 11 years for most markets.
2. **SEASONAL CYCLE** — yearly cycle, highs/lows at approximately same time periods each year. Very distinct in agricultural markets, also present in metals, financials. Follow reliable patterns with unusually high accuracy.
3. **PRIMARY CYCLE** — dominant weekly cycle shorter than 1 year. Average length ranges 9 to 22 weeks per market. Some markets have a 1/2 Primary Cycle sub-cycle (averages half the PC length). Primary Cycle normally tops/bottoms with a concurrent Trading Cycle.
4. **TRADING CYCLE** — measured in days, used to enter/exit. Most Trading Cycle lengths are 3 to 5 weeks in agricultures and metals; financials tend to have ~8-week Trading Cycles. Contains two sub-cycles:
   - **ALPHA CYCLE** — first smaller cycle within Trading Cycle. Trading Cycle always begins with a new Alpha Cycle.
   - **BETA CYCLE** — second smaller cycle, begins when Alpha Cycle bottoms. Beta Cycle always bottoms when Trading Cycle bottoms.

#### Example 1-2 (p.10) — Full cycle hierarchy diagram
Shows Seasonal → Primary → 1/2 Primary → Trading → Alpha/Beta → Price Activity
- PC crest, 1/2 PC crest, TC crests, BC crests, AC crests all labeled
- Measured in WEEKS along bottom axis

#### Trading Cycle Cresting Behavior
- If the next longer cycle (Primary) is moving UP, the Trading Cycle tends to crest with the BETA Cycle Crest (Right Translation)
- If the Primary Cycle is moving DOWN, the Trading Cycle tends to crest with the ALPHA Cycle Crest (Left Translation)

### Right and Left Translation (Example 1-3, p.11)
- **Right Translation**: Higher crests and higher troughs in a rising market as the next longer dominant cycle moves up (BULL MARKET). Crests shift to the right. Troughs above previous troughs, crests above previous crests.
- **Left Translation**: Lower troughs and lower crests in a falling market as the next longer dominant cycle moves down (BEAR MARKET). Crests shift to the left. Each trough below previous trough, each crest below previous crest.

#### Four Key Cyclic Concepts:
1. Right Translation — higher crests/troughs occur in rising market (trend is up)
2. Left Translation — lower troughs/crests occur in falling market (trend is down)
3. Each longer cycle determines the trend for the next shorter cycle
4. If direction of longer cycle is known, trend is known for shorter cycle

#### Cyclic Trading Rule:
- If the longer-term cycle is moving up → buy the troughs of the shorter cycle
- If the longer-term cycle is moving down → sell the crests of the shorter cycle

### The Primary Cycle (pp.15-16)
- Most consistent aspect of markets is TIME
- Timing Bands for PC tops/bottoms indicate when to look for specific oscillator patterns
- Two types of Primary Cycles:
  - **Simple** (Example 1-4): Just up/down. 3 measurable components: (1) Trough-to-Trough, (2) Trough-to-Crest, (3) Crest-to-Trough
  - **Complex** (Example 1-5): Has 1/2 Primary Cycle within it, causing 2 tops and an intervening low. 7 measurable components: (1) PC T-T, (2) First 1/2 PC T-T, (3) First 1/2 PC T-C, (4) First 1/2 PC C-T, (5) Second 1/2 PC T-T, (6) Second 1/2 PC T-C, (7) Second 1/2 PC C-T

### 70% Timing Bands — Measuring Cycles (p.16)
- To eliminate unpredictable extremes and narrow time periods for future projections, only the middle 70% of total samples are used for each cyclic component
- Called **70% Cycle Timing Bands**
- 7 out of 10 times, a cyclic component will top or bottom within the Timing Band
- ~20% will occur before the Band, ~10% will occur after the Band

### Timing Band Adjustment for Bull/Bear Markets (p.17)
- Problem: measurements don't differentiate bull vs bear → time periods can be very broad
- **Bull market (uptrend)**: Trough-to-Crest Band = from average to END of Timing Band; Crest-to-Trough Band = from BEGINNING to average
- **Bear market (downtrend)**: T-C Band = from BEGINNING to average; C-T Band = from average to END of Timing Band
- Example: S&P Index 1/2 PC T-C Band avg=5, range 2-10 weeks. Bull = 5-10 weeks. Bear = 2-5 weeks. C-T Band avg=5, range 1-9. Bull = 1-5 weeks. Bear = 5-9 weeks.
- Timing Bands plotted on chart to visually anticipate highs and lows
- Cycle highs/lows occur within Timing Bands ~7 out of 10 times
- When oscillators complete Setup/Trigger patterns within Timing Bands, a cycle high or low is usually in place

### Timing Band Counting Rules (p.17)
- T-T and T-C Timing Bands counted from the LOW that begins the cycle
- C-T Timing Band counted from the HIGH of the cycle

### The Trading Cycle (pp.17-19)
- Also has 7 similar components to the Primary Cycle
- Bressert prefers CALENDAR DAY count for Trading Cycle and its components
- Bar chart examples plotted on 7-day chart paper (calendar days include weekends)
- Reason: world doesn't stop on weekends — currency transactions, metals trading, government decisions, business events all continue. Many cycle highs/lows show up as Friday or Monday price highs/lows
- However, for OSCILLATOR calculations, only MARKET DAYS are used
- Therefore 2 sets of Timing Band tables: calendar days (include weekends) and market days (for detrending/oscillator calculation, plotted on 5-day chart paper)

### 7 Steps to Plotting the Timing Bands (pp.24-29)
Color code system for charting:
- **Red** — Trading Cycle Trough to Trough
- **Brown** — Alpha/Beta Cycle Trough to Trough
- **Green** — Alpha/Beta Cycle Trough to Crest
- **Orange** — Alpha/Beta Cycle Crest to Trough

Example Timing Bands used (likely Soybeans based on values):
| Component | Avg | TBand |
|-----------|-----|-------|
| Trough-to-Trough | 28 | 21-36 |
| Alpha Trough-to-Trough | 14 | 10-18 |
| Alpha Trough-to-Crest | 9 | 4-13 |
| Alpha Crest-to-Trough | 4 | 1-8 |
| Beta Trough-to-Trough | 15 | 12-19 |
| Beta Trough-to-Crest | 6 | 3-10 |
| Beta Crest-to-Trough | 8 | 3-12 |

**STEP 1**: Plot Trading Cycle T-T Band. Draw red vertical line on day of last TC Trough (= "Day Zero"). Count out horizontally to draw red horizontal line from Day 22 to Day 36. Mark average (Day 28) with a small dash.

**STEP 2**: Plot Alpha Cycle T-T Band. TC and Alpha Cycle always begin together, so count from same Day Zero. Draw brown band from Day 10 to Day 18. Mark average Day 14.

**STEP 3**: Plot Alpha Cycle T-C Band. Count from Day Zero (red line). Draw green band from Day 4 to Day 13. Mark average Day 9.

**STEP 4**: Plot Alpha Cycle C-T Band. Must wait until Alpha Crest occurs. Draw orange vertical line through the green T-C band to mark Alpha Crest. This becomes new Day Zero for C-T band. Draw orange band from Day 1 to Day 8. Mark average Day 4.
- NOTE: The brown AT-T band (2) and orange AC-T band (4) will OVERLAP — Alpha Cycle Trough frequently occurs during this overlap area.

**STEP 5**: Plot Beta Cycle T-T Band. Once Alpha Cycle Trough occurs, draw brown vertical line (extends up to same height as red TC line). This becomes new Day Zero for Beta bands. Draw brown band from Day 12 to Day 19. Mark average Day 15.

**STEP 6**: Plot Beta Cycle T-C Band. From the Alpha Cycle Trough (brown vertical line), draw green band from Day 3 to Day 10. Mark average Day 6.

**STEP 7**: Plot Beta Cycle C-T Band. Draw orange vertical line through Beta T-C band to mark Beta Crest. This becomes new Day Zero for Beta C-T. Draw orange band from Day 3 to Day 12. Mark average Day 8.

**KEY INSIGHT**: At Step 7, there are now 3 overlapping lines giving the most probable time for the Trading Cycle Trough to occur:
1. The red T-T of the Trading Cycle (1)
2. The brown T-T of the Beta Cycle (5)
3. The orange C-T of the Beta Cycle (7)

The Trading Cycle will frequently bottom within the overlap area of all three.

Once the TC Trough is confirmed, this becomes the new Day Zero for Bands 1, 2, and 3 of the next Trading Cycle.

### Same 7 steps apply to Primary Cycle and 1/2 Primary Cycle Timing Bands
Can be plotted on weekly charts and daily charts.

## CHAPTER 2: TECHNICAL TOOLS WITH CYCLES (pp.32-39)

### Technical Analysis Overview (p.32)
- No single technical tool works in all markets — each must be isolated through historical research
- Cycles are "simply another technical tool, but one that serves as the 'rule' that brings the technical picture into focus through the use of time"
- Tools that provide time and price objectives are greatly improved when combined with cycles
- Mid-Cycle Pause Price Objective depends on the cycle — once cycle ends, price objective no longer valid
- 60-40% Retracements are only valid for the cycle in which they occur
- Support/Resistance Lines are always valid; cycles tend to top/bottom at or near S/R levels
- Technical tools + cycles + oscillators = price levels for support/resistance, allowing anticipation of Trends and Trend Reversals

### Mid-Cycle Pause Price Objectives (MCP) (pp.32-34)
- Like taking a bus without knowing your destination — MCP tells you where to "get off"
- Determines the distance prices will move to a cyclic top (bull) or bottom (bear)
- Used with Beta Cycles, Trading Cycles, 1/2 Primary Cycles, and Primary Cycles
- **CRITICAL RULE**: MCP will ONLY be met or exceeded if the next longer cycle continues to move in the same direction. A MCP for a Trading Cycle requires the 1/2 PC or PC to continue in the same direction. A MCP for a PC requires the Seasonal Cycle to continue in the same direction. A MCP will NOT be met in a trading range.

#### Constructing MCP in a BULL Market (p.33, 5-step diagram):
1. Measure the diagonal distance from the cyclic top B to the cyclic low C
2. Mark the midpoint of diagonal line BC as X
3. Measure the VERTICAL distance from midpoint X down to the price low of the preceding Cycle A. This distance = X-X1
4. Measure the same distance X-X1 ABOVE X to point X2. The Mid-Cycle Pause Price Objective is X2
5. Measure 10% of the total distance X1-X2 and plot this above AND below X2. This is the RANGE within which the next cycle of the same length should top

#### Constructing MCP in a BEAR Market (p.34, 5-step diagram):
1. Measure the diagonal distance from the cycle low E to the cyclic top F
2. Mark the midpoint of diagonal line EF as X
3. Measure the VERTICAL distance from midpoint X UP to the price high of the preceding Cycle D. This distance = X-X1
4. Measure the same distance X-X1 BELOW X to X2. The MCP Objective is X2
5. Measure 10% of total distance X1-X2, plot above and below X2 = RANGE where next cycle should bottom

#### Four Basic MCP/Retracement Situations (p.36, diagram):
1. **Bull Market Before a Top**: Market moving up, MCP ahead, 60-40% range = resistance that may need to be exceeded for MCP to be reached
2. **Bear Market Before a Top**: MCP has been met, 60-40% retracement expected to be reached; prices then reverse, new MCP generated in direction of longer-term cycle
3. **Bull Market Before a Bottom**: MCP has been met, 60-40% retracement expected; prices reverse and new MCP generated
4. **Bear Market Before a Bottom**: Market moving down, MCP ahead, 60-40% range = support that may need to be exceeded for MCP to be reached

### 60-40% Retracements (pp.35-36)
- Derived from Fibonacci .618-.382 relationship (general area: .618 rounds to 60%, .382 rounds to 40%)
- Used to determine price range for cyclic HIGHS in bear markets and cyclic LOWS in bull markets
- **Only valid if the next longer cycle continues to move in the same direction** (same rule as MCP)
- Should be used in conjunction with 70% Timing Bands
- Most Primary Cycles, Trading Cycles, and Alpha Cycles will retrace AT LEAST 40%
- Often used to enter a market when a cyclic top/bottom is due — you can enter on the retracement and still catch a substantial part of the move

#### Construction in BULL Market (p.35):
1. Measure vertical distance (in grids) from cyclic trough A to crest B
2. Multiply by .40 and .60 (e.g., 60 grids × .40 = 24; 60 × .60 = 36)
3. Directly below price high B, plot the .40 and .60 numbers, measuring DOWN from the high
4. Connect the two points with an arrowhead pointing down
5. This is the price range within which the cycle should bottom (combined with Timing Bands for time period)

#### Construction in BEAR Market (p.36):
1. Measure vertical distance from cycle crest B to trough C
2. Multiply by .40 and .60
3. Directly ABOVE the price low C, plot .40 and .60, measuring UP from the low
4. Connect with arrowhead pointing up
5. This is the price range within which the cycle should top

### Speed Resistance Lines (pp.37-38)
- Developed by Edson Gould, popularized by Gerald Appel in *Winning Market Systems*
- Used with individual cycles, they become powerful for price objectives, cyclic tops/bottoms, and trend reversals
- Most powerful buy/sell signals generated when prices retrace 60-40% to meet a Speed Resistance Line in the overlap area of Cyclic Timing Bands as one or more oscillators overextend
- Can be constructed for Trading Cycles, Alpha/Beta Cycles, Primary Cycles, 1/2 Primary Cycles, Seasonal Cycles, and Long-Term Cycles
- Based on distance from low-to-high or high-to-low of individual cycle

#### Construction in BULL Market (p.37):
1. Measure vertical distance (grids) from cyclic trough A to crest B
2. Divide by 3 (multiply by .33 and .66): e.g., 60 × .33 = 20 grids; 60 × .66 = 40 grids
3. Below crest B, place dots at .33 and .66 points, measuring DOWN
4. From low A, draw line through .33 point = UPPER Speedline
5. From low A, draw line through .66 point = LOWER Speedline
- When upper Speedline is decisively penetrated, prices often drop to lower Speedline
- Prices dropping decisively below lower Speedline will often test and/or drop below the low at A

#### Construction in BEAR Market (p.38):
1. Measure vertical distance from crest B to trough C
2. Multiply by .33 and .66
3. Above trough C, place dots at .33 and .66 points, measuring UP
4. From crest B, draw line through .33 point = LOWER Speedline
5. From crest B, draw line through .66 point = UPPER Speedline
- When lower Speedline is decisively penetrated, prices often rise to upper Speedline
- Prices rising decisively above upper Speedline will often test and/or exceed the high at B

#### How to Determine Decisive Penetration (p.38):
- Entire day's range below Speedline (bull) or above Speedline (bear)
- Penetration > 1.5% of the price level at which the Speedline is penetrated
- Example: at 50 cents, 50 × .015 = .0075. Subtract from 50: 50 - .0075 = .4925. If close is below .4925, Speedline has been decisively penetrated

### Cyclic Support/Resistance Lines (pp.39)
- Price levels providing support in declining markets and resistance in rising markets
- Cycles will often top/bottom around these lines, sometimes months or years after the S/R line was established
- A S/R level can offer SUPPORT in declining market AND RESISTANCE in rising market (dual role)
- Strength determined by how often prices have rebounded/congested at these levels — the longer the cycle, the greater the effect
- Most effective when constructed for Trading Cycles, Primary Cycles, Seasonal Cycles, and Long-Term Cycles
- **Construction on daily chart**: Draw line horizontally from midpoint of diagonal distance measured to construct MCP. Give newly constructed S/R lines time to test its congesting and rebounding power before anticipating its effect on future price movement
- **Rule 1 (market up)**: Draw from midpoint of diagonal from crest A to trough B
- **Rule 2 (market down)**: Draw from midpoint of diagonal from trough B to crest C
- In Weekly and Monthly Range Charts, the "old" highs and lows = frequent S/R Levels, especially those that have stopped prices more than twice

## CHAPTER 3: FOCUS ON OSCILLATORS (pp.40-...)

### What is an Oscillator? (p.40)
- Indicators of tops and bottoms in price
- Move up with prices to indicate a top; move down with prices to indicate a bottom
- **Bressert's definition**: Any mathematically calculated line or lines that move up and down with price activity in such a way that overbought and oversold situations can be identified
- Mathematically calculated oscillators are often at their EXTREMES at tops and bottoms
- They visually illustrate the swings of the market from overbought to oversold and back
- **Problem**: Not all overbought/oversold levels or patterns are significant price highs or lows — that's where Oscillator/Cycle Combinations are essential

### The Powerful Oscillator/Cycle Combination (p.40)
- Power lies in the combination of TIME and PRICE
- Cycle analysis provides clear-cut means of projecting cycle lengths
- Oscillators (factor of price and time) show overbought/oversold levels at cycle highs/lows
- **Together**: Time periods from cycle analysis + oscillator patterns confirm tops/bottoms as they form and make projections into the future

### The Oscillator Defined (p.40)
- Can be as simple as the difference between two moving averages (e.g., 3-Day minus 10-Day Moving Average)
- Can be price fluctuation around a single moving average (as in a Detrend)
- Can be as complex as the Commodity Channel Index (CCI) which cannot realistically be calculated by hand
- Basic quality: moves with prices to indicate overbought and oversold levels

### The Setup and Trigger Entry (pp.41)
- Bressert's approach: take emotion out of decisions by making them mechanical in a two-step process
- **Setup**: An oscillator formation that can incorporate anyone of a number of factors — oscillator turns down, oscillator goes above Sell Line or below Buy Line, divergence, penetration of Crossover line. Whatever it is, once a Setup has formed, price activity makes the entry decision (the Trigger)
- **Trigger entry**: Usually exceeding the high of a previous day (week/month) for a buy; dropping below the low of the price range for a sell
- Once an Oscillator/Cycle Combination (Setup + Trigger entry) is identified, it should be researched over 5 to 40 years of historical data to evaluate performance

### How to Research Oscillators (pp.41-42)
1. **Minimum 5 years of research** on any oscillator pattern (preferably 10 or 20 years). Need a large number of samples to establish confidence to take positions against prevailing sentiment. Without enough research, you'll encounter unanticipated patterns that almost always cost money.
2. **Keep a notebook/cassette tape** to record findings. Don't add notes to original research — you'll lose direction. Over time, reviewing notes reveals recurring gems "like money in the bank."
3. **Learn one oscillator well** — focus research on one oscillator in several markets rather than jumping between many oscillators. What you learn about one can often be applied to others.
4. **Do not rely on a single oscillator** — no oscillator will pick every top and bottom. Combinations of oscillators increase performance. Some sizable moves will be missed by relying on just one.

### Seven Techniques to Enhance Oscillator Performance (pp.42-46)
Purpose: confirm highs/lows through identification and evaluation of overbought/oversold levels. Cycle highs/lows most often occur at overbought/oversold levels. Combination of oscillators and cycles allows:
1. Identification of most significant patterns at OB/OS levels (often the cycle highs/lows)
2. Establishment of time expectations for the next cycle high/low and significant OB/OS level

**Important caveat**: Cycles contract, extend, and skip beats, while oscillators do NOT always overextend at cycle highs/lows. Even when they do overextend, they often reach their most overextended before or after the exact price high. Awareness of these limitations demands development of high probability parameters.

**Three pillars of approach**:
- *First*: Don't expect oscillators to identify every cycle high/low — use SEVERAL oscillators
- *Second*: Develop historically researched patterns with high probability, using mechanical Setup/Trigger entry combinations
- *Third*: Use market-proven money management to control risk and optimize profits

#### THE SEVEN TECHNIQUES:

**1) PRICE/OSCILLATOR TURNS** — Simplest approach. Prices and oscillator turn at approximately the same time (within several days for daily, weeks for weekly, months for monthly). Problem: most oscillators have "wiggles" of a day or more that aren't the highs/lows we seek. Several other techniques serve to qualify the turns to separate false from genuine oscillator turns.

**2) SMOOTHING THE OSCILLATOR** — Eliminates many false wiggles. Normally accomplished with a simple moving average, but can also use exponential average or another oscillator. Once smoothed, upturns and downturns become tradable or become part of a pattern.

**3) LEVELS (BUY/SELL LINES)** — Eliminate some false turns and indicate levels at which tops/bottoms can be expected. A Level is an oscillator level frequently exceeded as oscillators make highs/lows (e.g., 70 and 30 for Stochastic, 100 and -100 for CCI). Some are built into the oscillator; others determined by observation through historical research.
- **Author's Note**: Two approaches to determine Buy/Sell Lines:
  - (a) Look for the Level of greatest overextension reached by most cycle tops/bottoms — used for most Buy/Sell Lines in the book
  - (b) Review cycle bottoms and look for a Level that is not as extreme but still identifies cycle bottoms (and is followed by an oscillator turn + Trigger entry). Example: S&P MACD Detrend has Buy Line at -90 (greatest overextension) but -70 Level also included all 4-Year cycle lows and was met or exceeded by a rise above the MACD Crossover Line (= Zero Line of Detrend)
- Review Buy/Sell Lines every 6-12 months, searching for extremes AND less extreme levels that perform as well when followed by oscillator turn + Trigger entry

**4) CROSSOVER LINES** — Excellent filters for oscillators that tend to turn before prices. With a crossover, market entry is not as close to price tops/bottoms as a simple downturn, but probability of accurate confirmation greatly increased. The Signal line in MACD functions as a Crossover. A Crossover combined with a smoothed oscillator AND Buy/Sell Lines is used in the CCI chapter to identify highs/lows of the 4-Week Trading Cycle in T-Bonds.

**5) THE ZERO LINE** — Often a level of support/resistance for oscillators. Can be criteria for constructing a pattern to identify cycle highs/lows, or to generate an entry signal. In every price Detrend, the Zero Line is the moving average around which prices are detrended; in oscillator Detrends, the Zero Line is usually the Crossover. Historical review shows that in most markets the 40, 20, and 10-unit time periods act as Support and Resistance Levels. The Zero Line is frequently used as an important component in Price/Oscillator Patterns.

**6) PRICE/OSCILLATOR PATTERNS** — Combine one or more of the above techniques with price to identify recurring oscillator patterns. Price/Oscillator Patterns combine with cycle timing to produce Oscillator/Cycle Combinations, providing mechanical identification of cycles AND mechanical entry/exit signals. Specific examples: oscillator patterns for gold in Chapter 13 ("3-10 Oscillator"), MACD with Japanese Yen in Chapter 6 ("Detrending Oscillators").

**7) SETUP/TRIGGER ENTRY PATTERNS** — Turn Price/Oscillator Patterns into "no thinkums" (mechanical trades). These patterns take most of the judgment out of analysis and trading. The Setup is the Price/Oscillator Pattern developed through historical research. Using the Setup alone means you decide when to enter/exit. The Trigger entry eliminates emotional attachment — the entry is pre-determined by the historical performance of the oscillator pattern combined with simple mechanical entry/exit techniques that let the price action complete the pattern and trigger a market entry.

#### Trigger Entry Details (p.46):
- Normally puts you in on STRENGTH when buying and WEAKNESS when selling
- **Buy Trigger**: A buy stop above the high of:
  - The day (week, month, hourly range) that turned the oscillator up; or
  - The time period that resulted in the Crossover; or
  - The time period that preceded the oscillator upturn or Crossover; or
  - The time period that followed the oscillator upturn or Crossover
- For any of the above, the Trigger entry stop could also be placed above the next LARGER time period (e.g., daily oscillator upturn → buy stop above high of that WEEK)
- In some situations, the close of a time period exceeding the high, or a pivot Point, may be preferable

#### The Development of the Oscillator/Cycle Combination — 3-Step Process (p.46):
At a bottom:
1. The TIME is right for a cycle high or low, as determined by cycle Timing Bands
2. The Price/Oscillator Pattern sets up
3. Price activity puts you in the market by exceeding the Trigger entry buy stop

The pattern is then complete and you have a pre-determined probability of a cycle top/bottom being in place and of making money, based on historical time period and price moves to the Primary Cycle high. The mirror image = sell signals.

## CHAPTER 4: FINDING CYCLES WITH CENTERED DETRENDING (pp.48-60)

### Detrending Defined (p.48)
- A process that eliminates the trend of a market relative to a moving average or oscillator
- On a chart, allows individual cycles and overextension levels to be identified
- The detrending process for CYCLE IDENTIFICATION is somewhat different from that used with oscillators (examined separately in Chapter 5)

### Detrending and Cycles (p.48)
- Futures markets composed of many cycles
- Combination of longer-term cycles and Seasonal Cycle determines the trend
- To trade, must analyze the shorter-term cycles that frequently enable top/bottom confirmation within days or weeks
- Smaller cycles are often hard to identify because more powerful longer cycles obscure their pattern
- **Detrending eliminates the effects of cycles LONGER than the cycle you're searching for** — each individual cycle can then be more accurately measured

### The Detrending Process (p.48)
- **Step 1**: Have an idea of the approximate length of the cycle you wish to identify
- **Step 2**: Run a moving average that is the same length as the suspected cycle, calculated on the close
- **Step 3**: The moving average is then CENTERED — plotted at the midpoint of the time period of the suspected cycle length
  - Example: A 20-Week Centered Moving Average would be plotted 10.5 weeks before the current week, which is more conveniently plotted 11 weeks before the current week
- The centered moving average then becomes a Zero Line as the bars of the chart are plotted relative to it
- This results in the actual Centered Detrend in the bottom panel of the chart

### Centered Detrend — General Principles (p.65)
- Works for identifying dominant cycles of ALL lengths in daily, weekly, monthly, quarterly, and yearly charts
- Also works with intra-day data of various time periods
- Detrending eliminates most effects of longer-term cycles, but cycles shorter than the one detrended will still show up and can cause some distortion
- For a valid Centered Detrend, the moving average must be reasonably close to the cycle length
- Average cycle lengths for daily Trading Cycles and weekly Primary Cycles of most futures markets are given in Chapter One
- Seasonal Cycle can be detrended against a 40-Week or 50-Week Moving Average
- **Starting place for intra-day cycles**: 55-minute and 110-minute time periods

### Centered Detrend Limitations (p.65)
- **Cannot be used in real-time trading** because the centered moving average will be well past the high or low before you can confirm it
- This is where oscillator analysis and real-time detrending come into play
- While the Centered Detrend cannot be used in real-time, it should be used to CONFIRM the tops and bottoms after they have occurred
- **Remember**: Almost all cycle highs occur ABOVE the centered moving average, and almost all cycle lows occur BELOW the centered moving average

## CHAPTER 5: THE DETREND AS AN OSCILLATOR (pp.66-78)

### Centered vs. Real-Time Detrend (p.66)
- Centered Detrend = most accurate way to identify cycle highs and lows; can see Right/Left Translation and flow of cycles; can see why price highs/lows are not always cycle highs/lows
- BUT centered MA is not useful for real-time analysis (lagging by half the cycle period)
- **A current-time Detrend** (prices detrended around a moving average that is NOT centered) can be used as a market oscillator to indicate overextended price levels and also to help identify cycle highs and lows

### Half-Span Detrend (p.69)

#### Chart D-18 (p.69): Weekly S&P Index, 12/83-5/87 — 10-Week Detrend

- The 10-Week Moving Average (half of 20-week cycle) shows the highs and lows of the 20-Week Primary Cycle in the S&P Index

- In half of the cycles, the Detrend tops BEFORE prices top

#### Key Characteristic of Current-Time Detrends (p.69):
- The Detrend highs and lows tend to move to "LEVELS" above and below the moving average that are often indicators that a cycle high or low is forming, or about to form

## CHAPTER 6: DETRENDING OSCILLATORS (pp.79-80)

### Overview (p.79)
- The Detrend is both an oscillator AND a tool to improve the performance of individual oscillators
- Long time periods needed initially; detail is lost in condensed weekly charts (1981-1989), but this can be overcome by evaluating each top/bottom using expanded charts with more detail
- Once Buy and Sell Levels have been established, the more detailed charts can be used for regular oscillator runs
- **As a tool to improve oscillator performance**: The Detrend should be plotted directly below the oscillator and evaluated using Buy/Sell Lines, the Zero Line, and divergence with the oscillator

### Oscillator Detrend Defined (p.79)
- An Oscillator Detrend = the difference, or spread, between two values or two components of an oscillator
- In COMPUTRAC, the Spread Tool allows the detrending of oscillators
- **MACD** is based on exponential moving averages, detrended by subtracting one EMA from the other

### Chart DO-1 (p.80): Weekly Japanese Yen, 1/2/81-2/9/90 — MACD and Detrend
- Top panel: weekly Japanese Yen price from 1982 to 1990 (1982 low around 39, rising to highs of 84)
- Price labels: LOW→HIGH marked; points B, C, D, E; numbers 1-5 at highs
- Middle panel: COMPUTRAC MACD (solid line = MACD, dashed line = Signal/Crossover)
- Bottom panel: Detrend of the MACD
- Labels in bottom panel: numbers 1-5 at highs; DV (divergence) markers; levels LC, LB, LA, LO, LE, LF marked on right axis
- Level values: LC = 1.0, LB = 0.5, LA = 0.0, LO = -0.5, LE = (unlabeled), LF = -1.0

### MACD Characteristics (p.79-80):
- A crossing by the solid MACD line of the dashed slower Signal line (the Crossover) often confirms tops and bottoms, usually well AFTER the market has turned
- A downturn in the MACD line often occurs at, or shortly after, a top; an upturn at, or shortly after, a bottom
- **The Detrend tends to turn slightly BEFORE the MACD**, often exactly at tops and bottoms
- The Detrend has the benefit of Buy/Sell Lines that help identify major tops and bottoms
- A rise above a Sell Line followed by a downturn of the Detrend often indicates a top
- A drop below a Buy Line followed by an upturn of the Detrend often indicates a sizable bottom
- More Detrend analysis of the MACD is covered in Chapter 9

### Chapter 6 continued: MACD Detrend Levels for Japanese Yen (pp.81-82)

#### Chart DO-1 Analysis (detailed):
- Major tops and bottoms occur at or before turns of the MACD, but there are also many wiggles/false turns between
- MACD turns close to major highs/lows, but does NOT give any indication of the Level at which a high or low might occur
- Zero Line acts as Support/Resistance level — unless the Crossover is used to setup a Trigger entry, the MACD itself does not indicate whether a top or bottom is likely to occur near the Zero Line
- **Using the Detrend will turn the MACD into a much more powerful oscillator** that frequently generates Trigger entries well BEFORE the MACD Crossover, with much smaller dollar risk

#### Determining Oscillator Detrend Levels (p.81):
- First thing to do with an Oscillator Detrend is determine Levels that will function as Buy and Sell Lines
- **General guideline**: When a Buy Line is exceeded, the first downturn and Trigger entry will often confirm the high. Mirror image for Sell Lines.
- Turning points of the Detrend near major tops/bottoms often develop into valid Levels for years to come
- 1982 was a major low in the Yen — several Levels from this time period were important Buy/Sell Lines years later

#### Key 3-10 Characteristics (p.93):
- In a strong rising market, the 3-10 has a tendency NOT to drop below the Zero Line (indicated by up-pointing arrows)

## CHAPTER 7: 3-DIMENSIONAL ANALYSIS (pp.98-100)

### Overview (p.98)
- 3-D oscillator analysis evaluates markets on 3 different levels: long-term, intermediate-term, and short-term
- Oscillators run on 3 different time periods each give a different picture of the market
- **Longer and intermediate-term time periods** generally indicate tops and bottoms of the longer-term cycles
- **Shorter-term time periods** indicate highs and lows of shorter-term cycles and often generate entry and exit patterns with lower dollar risk than longer time periods

### Time Period Hierarchy (p.98):
- **Monthly data** = long-term, showing highs/lows of cycles longer than 1 year; also useful for Seasonal Cycle identification
- **Weekly data** = best for identification of highs/lows of the Seasonal Cycle AND also the weekly Primary Cycles
- **Daily data** = identifies the Primary Cycle and the daily Trading Cycles
- **Intra-day data** = identifies and trades the Trading Cycle, as well as shorter-term daily and intra-day cycles using same concepts

### How to Get the Most from 3-D Analysis (p.98):
1. Use the monthly oscillator to establish parameters that indicate overbought and oversold levels of longer-term cycles
2. Use weekly oscillators to establish parameters that indicate overbought and oversold levels of the Seasonal Cycles and/or the weekly Primary Cycle
3. Use daily oscillators to establish parameters that indicate overbought and oversold levels of the Primary Cycle and/or the Trading Cycle

## CHAPTER 8: THE PRIMARY CYCLE IN GOLD (pp.106-120)

### Overview (p.106)
- Dominant weekly cycle in gold = **18-Week Primary Cycle** measured low-to-low
- PC is actually a summation of many shorter-term cycles that often contracts, extends, and seems to skip a beat
- **18 weeks is an average**; 90% of all Gold PCs since 1972 have bottomed within a Timing Band of **11 to 23 weeks** from the previous low
- While this is a broad period, there are ways to greatly improve the probability of identifying PC lows and highs shortly after they occur

### Right and Left Translation Review (p.107)
- **Right Translation** (bull market): Prices up for a LONGER period than they go down; the up-phase lasts longer than the down-phase
- **Left Translation** (bear market): Prices up for a SHORTER period than they go down; the down-phase lasts longer than the up-phase
- Understanding R/L Translation helps determine trading strategies and money management

### Key Bull vs. Bear Cycle Characteristics (p.111):
- **Bull markets**: Average weeks from low to high is MORE THAN TWICE the bear markets; percentage rise is almost DOUBLE
- **Bear markets**: Number of weeks from high to low is about HALF the number of weeks in a bear market; percentage decline is 20% less than in a bear market

Bull market long positions should have relatively wide stop until Week 9, then moved closer each week. Average rise of 21.5% indicates sizable moneymaking moves about half the time.
- **80% of bear market highs occur 1-6 weeks from PC low** → relatively quick top expected with average rise of only 11.3% (not a big moneymaker). Long positions are HIGH RISK; close stop should be moved closer starting Week 4. Most effort should go into selling short at PC tops.
- **PC low activity in bull markets**: PC low tends to be HIGHER than the previous PC low — every PC low was above previous PC low until the market shifted to bear phase (when about two-thirds of PC lows were below previous PC low). This is helpful for establishing expectations on move size, duration, and money management changes.

#### MACD Detrend Construction for Gold (p.113):
The COMPUTRAC MACD is also the basis for an oscillator that has identified 80% of PC lows:

### The Three Oscillator Patterns: Oversold, Kiss, and Hook (pp.114-115)

#### 1. THE OVERSOLD Pattern (p.114):
1. A drop in the oscillator below -1. In the time period studied, all oscillator lows for this signal occurred at -1 to -11.
2. An upturn in the oscillator, which exceeds the Crossover and is followed by...
3. Prices exceeding the high of the Crossover Week.

#### 2. THE KISS Pattern (p.114):
1. Rising from below the Zero Line, the oscillator rises to a high above the Zero Line, then drops below the Crossover to .50 to -.99, and turns back up.
2. Exceeding the Crossover, and being followed by...
3. Prices exceeding the high of Crossover Week.

#### 3. THE HOOK Pattern (p.115):
1. An oscillator low below -1 that rises to a level that is generally below 0, and then turns down.
2. Dropping below the Crossover line and rising back above it, followed by...
3. Prices exceeding the high of Crossover week.
(Diagram shows the Hook shape: oscillator drops below -1 at K2, rises to K3, drops to cross below Crossover, then rises back above it → Crossover Week → Entry Week)

## CHAPTER 9: MOVING AVERAGE CONVERGENCE/DIVERGENCE (MACD) (pp.124-133)

### Overview (p.124)
- MACD invented by Gerald Appel, in most analytical packages
- Powerful oscillator for identifying cycle highs/lows and developing mechanical trades
- Constructed with three exponential moving averages — flexible, adjustable for short, intermediate, or long-term analysis

### MACD Characteristics (p.124):
- Crossing of slower line (Crossover) often confirms tops/bottoms, usually AFTER market has turned
- Downturn in the faster line often occurs at or shortly after a top; upturn at or shortly after a bottom
- **The Detrend between the fast line and the Crossover often indicates tops and bottoms earlier and more accurately than the MACD alone**, and works exceptionally well with Crossovers and Buy/Sell Lines

## CHAPTER 10: RELATIVE STRENGTH INDEX (RSI) (pp.134-140)

### Overview (p.134)
- RSI can be an excellent indicator of cycle highs and lows when properly "tuned" to an individual market
- Consistently establishes parameters that keep you out of a market until a high probability top or bottom is ready to form
- Like other oscillators, takes "trial and error" research to adapt to individual markets

### RSI Adaptation Methods (p.134):
1. Use different time periods to calculate the RSI
2. Adjust the Buy/Sell Lines
3. Smooth the oscillator

### RSI Disadvantage (p.134):
- Tends to have sizable wiggles within it as prices move from cycle low to cycle high and vice versa
- Wiggles also tend to occur at the actual cycle tops and bottoms
- Unless smoothed, the wiggles eliminate the use of Trigger entries following turns in the oscillator or Crossovers
- **Fibonacci time periods of 3, 5, 8, 13, and 21** will show potential for identifying specific cycle highs and lows
- Shorter periods (3, 5) will generally help indicate highs/lows of the Trading Cycle
- Longer periods (13, 21) will help identify highs/lows of the Primary Cycle

## CHAPTER 11: COMMODITY CHANNEL INDEX (CCI) (pp.144-160)

### Overview (p.144)
- CCI first published in FUTURES magazine in 1980, available in most analytical packages
- Moves between a Sell Line of 100 and Buy Line of -100
- Powerful tool for identifying cycle highs/lows by itself, but performance greatly improved with combinations of smoothing, crossovers, Buy/Sell Lines, and Setup/Trigger entries

### CCI and the 4-Year Cycle in the S&P Index (pp.144-154)

#### Chart CCI-1 (p.145): Monthly Cash S&P Index showing 4-Year Cycles with 30-Month CCI and 8-Month Crossover (Panel A) with Detrend (Panel B)
- Cash S&P from January 1950 through January 1990 (40 years)
- 4-Year Cycle highs/lows indicated by dots above/below prices
- Panel A = 30-Month CCI with 8-Month Crossover (darker line)
- Panel B = Detrend of the CCI and the Crossover

#### Key CCI Observations for S&P 4-Year Cycle (pp.144-146):
- Every 4-Year Cycle high exceeded the Sell Line at 100
- Every 4-Year Cycle bottom dropped below zero
- 6 of the 9 cycle bottoms occurred below the Buy Line at -100

**General expectations**:
- Following a 4-Year Cycle bottom, expect the 4-Year Cycle top to occur ONLY after the CCI has exceeded the Sell Line. Unfortunately, the oscillator can spend time above the Sell Line without clearly indicating when the high occurs. However, following a cycle bottom there is a period of "free time" until the oscillator exceeds the Buy Line (during this time, market declines and Primary Cycle lows can be aggressively bought with high probability that prices will continue to rise over the longer term)
- Following the high, expect the CCI to drop below zero, with 67% probability of a decline below the Buy Line at -100. This gives a time period of "free time" until the CCI drops below zero.
- Every 4-Year Cycle high was followed by a decline in the Detrend below the Buy Line at -90

**The complete Oscillator/Cycle Combination for 4-Year Cycle bottoms** (combining timing of the low + level of the Detrend low + level of the CCI low + completion of the CCI Crossover + Trigger entry of exceeding the high of the month of the Crossover) = "stellar indicator" of 4-Year Cycle bottoms.

#### Detrend Analysis for 4-Year Cycle:
- Every 4-Year Cycle low saw the Detrend drop below the Buy Line at -90
- Of the 9 cycle lows: 6 made the downside penetration of the -90 level as, or after, the cycle bottomed; 3 penetrated the -90 level and turned up BEFORE the cycle bottomed
- **The important factor**: EVERY 4-Year cycle bottom saw the Detrend drop below the Buy Line, either before, at, or after the price low
- A second look at the CCI shows that EVERY cycle bottom was followed by a rise above the Crossover

#### 5 Requirements for Confirming S&P 4-Year Cycle Bottom (p.146):
1. The price low must be 32 or more months from the previous 4-Year Cycle bottom
2. The Detrend must drop below the Buy Line at -90 and turn up
3. The CCI must drop below zero. A drop below the Buy Line at -100 (which occurred at 6 of the 9 bottoms) would be comforting but is not necessary
4. The CCI must then rise above the Crossover
5. Following requirements 1-4 being met, the Trigger entry of prices exceeding the high of the Crossover month confirmed EVERY 4-Year cycle bottom. It is the safest entry, but not always the one with the lowest dollar risk.

**Time Guideline**: Minimum length of the 4-Year Cycle since 1917 has been 32 months, with 15 of 17 cycles (nearly 90%) bottoming 43 months or more from the previous bottom. So the oscillator pattern should coincide with a price low 32 or more months from the previous cycle bottom, and will most likely be 43 or more months.

This Oscillator/Cycle Combination can be expected to confirm the bottom of the cycle due in 1991. In every cycle but 1987, the bottom was confirmed within months of the low, and barring a 1987-type crash, the 1991 bottom is also likely to be confirmed within months of the low.

These Oscillator/Cycle Combinations for the CCI give high
probability confirmations of the Seasonal highs and lows as
well as mechanical entries. Seasonal oscillators should
generally be researched over a minimum 20-30 year time period
to verify their performance. Even then, in the heat of the
market it is often hard to accept a single pattern as solid
confirmation of a top or bottom no matter how well it has
performed in the past. But when other Oscillator/Cycle
combinations also give confirmations of a Seasonal high or
low, you better pay attention, especially if the fundamental
picture says exactly the opposite.

## THE CCI AND THE 4-WEEK TRADING CYCLE IN T-BONDS (p.164)

With all of the economic factors and reports affecting the
T-Bond market it may be surprising to see that it has a
rather consistent 4-week Trading Cycle.

### CHART CCI-8 (p.166)
- Shows daily T-Bonds nearby contract from 880923 through

- Trading Cycle lows are numbered with vertical lines
- Cycle highs indicated by dots above prices; larger dots

### The Centered Detrend (Panel B)
- Cycle lows determined by the 21-Day Centered Detrend
- Cycle lows should be below the moving average (Zero Line
  in the Detrend chart) and highs above it
- Each 4-Week Trading Cycle low made with the Detrend well
  below the moving average
- Detrend low not always exactly the price low, but usually
  within 4 days of it
- All Detrend highs are above the moving average

### The Timing of Cycle Lows (p.164)
Of the 14 complete cycles in this time period:
- ~60% bottomed 18 to 23 market days from the previous
  Trading Cycle low
- ~40% occurred 28 to 30 days from the low
- **Trading Cycle Trough-to-Trough Timing Band**: 15-30 days,
  average of 21 days
- **Guideline**: expect the 4-Week Trading Cycle to bottom 15
  to 23 days from the previous low; if prices continue lower
  after the 23rd day, a bottom is not likely until the 28th
  to the 30th day

### The Half-Span Detrend (Panel C, p.164)
- Current-Time Detrend around a 10-Day Moving Average (one-
  half the span of the 4-Week Trading Cycle)
- Pinpoints cycle highs and lows when the Detrend drops
  below a Buy Line
- Most cycle lows made as, or after, the Detrend dropped
  below the Buy Line at -80
- **Guideline**: following a cycle high, the Detrend should
  drop below the Buy Line at -.80 to make a 4-Week Trading
  Cycle bottom. Look for 70% of bottoms within the 15-30
  day Timing Band.

### The Commodity Channel Index (Panel D, p.165)
- Smoothed CCI with a Crossover
- Lighter line = 5-Day CCI smoothed with 8-Term MA (called
  "the CCI" from here on)
- Darker line = Crossover = 8-Term MA of the CCI
- **Sell Line at 60**, **Buy Line at -35**
- 15 Trading cycle bottoms, 14 declines in CCI below Buy Line
- Following a drop from above Sell Line to below Buy Line, an
  upturn in the CCI seems to occur near most Trading Cycle
  bottoms; rises above Sell Line often near Trading Cycle tops

### Oscillator/Cycle Combination for 4-Week TC Bottoms (p.165)

**Buy pattern:**
1. Prices should normally be 15-30 days from the previous
   4-Week Cycle low. No less than 15, could be more than 30
   in an extended cycle.
2. The 10-Day Detrend must have dropped below Buy Line at -.80
3. The CCI must drop below Buy Line at -35 and turn up.
   Ideally would have followed a CCI high above 60 (occurred
   in most Trading Cycles). In other time periods it may be
   necessary to lower the Sell Line to 25.
4. Buy with a Trigger entry as prices exceed the high of the
   CCI upturn day.
5. Protective sell stop below the lowest price of the cycle
   before entry. If oscillator rises above Crossover then
   drops below Crossover before rising above Sell Line, raise
   stop to under price low of the Crossover day.

### Controlled Risk Money Management (p.167)

Research Table CI-8 incorporates the Oscillator/Cycle
Combination with multiple contract money management from
Chapter 14 ("Controlled Risk Money Management").

**Number 1 Position**: Take profits when prices rise $1000
(1 full point) above the entry price. Reasonable for a bull
market, too much for a bear market.

**Number 2 Position**: After No.1 profits taken, raise sell
stop to 1/2 point (.50) below entry price. With $1000
profit in hand, total exposure = $500 for 2 positions.
Take profits following CCI rise above Sell Line, then:
- a downturn in the CCI, followed by
- a drop below the price low of the downturn day

## CHAPTER TWELVE: STOCHASTIC (p.176)

The Stochastic is one of the more widely watched oscillators
because it usually tops and bottoms near the price highs and
lows of the markets. Conventional wisdom: overbought above 70,
oversold below 30. In reality, the Stochastic tends to stay
above 70 for long periods in a bull market and below 30 in a
bear market.

However, it is an excellent indicator of cycle tops and
bottoms of various lengths. Calculations are in the Appendix.
It can be calculated to turn quickly (fast Stochastic) or
slowly (slow Stochastic, the one used here). The Crossover
(%D) is kept only as a confirmation for questionable situations.

## CHAPTER THIRTEEN: 3-10 OSCILLATOR (p.184)

Oscillators based on daily data are more oscillators based
on weekly data, and will give many of overbought and oversold
levels. More entry signals daily oscillators, and one of these
signals is almost Cycle high... but which one?

The only way to determine whether or not an oscillator can
determine a Primary Cycle high is through research. Simple
turns in the oscillator seldom work, and each oscillator
should be evaluated based on the individual patterns that
occur in different markets.

### The 3-10 Oscillator Definition (p.184)
- A good daily oscillator = **3-Day Moving Average minus a
  10-Day Moving Average**
- The oscillator turns at overextensions often indicate highs
  and lows, but what really makes this oscillator a
  powerhouse are the patterns based on the fluctuations of
  the 3-10 Oscillator around the Crossover
- **Crossover** = a 16-Term Moving Average of the oscillator

### Construction (p.184)
To construct this oscillator with the Crossover, plot a
3-10 Moving Average, and on the same chart plot a 16-Term
Moving Average of the 3-10 Oscillator.

### Two Key Patterns: TSS and SBS

**Two Step Sell (TSS)** and **Small Bump Sell (SBS)** — two
specific patterns for identifying highs.

### Validity Rules for Highs and Lows (p.184)
- A high is valid only when it is above the Zero Line *and*
  the Crossover, and follows a low that was formed below
  the Crossover
- A low is valid only when below the Crossover and following
  a high that was above the Crossover

### Example 1 — Formation of Oscillator Patterns (p.185)
Shows the oscillator as a solid line and the Crossover as a
dashed line. Each turn is numbered. Of the 15 oscillator
turns only the 6 circled Setups would qualify to form the
two patterns:
- Circled highs: 1, 5, 11 (above Zero Line and Crossover,
  following low below Crossover)
- Circled lows: 2, 8, 14 (below Crossover, following high
  above Crossover)

### Example 2 — Two Step Sell (TSS) Pattern (p.186)
1. First, an oscillator high is made at A above the Crossover.
   This high can be above or below the previous oscillator
   high and the Zero Line.
2. Then an oscillator low is formed below the Crossover at B,
   which can be above, or below, the previous low.
3. The high at C then rises above the Crossover, and the high
   at A to form a higher "second step."
4. A 2-day downturn in the oscillator completes the Setup
   stage, and the Trigger for market entry is a drop below
   the previous week's low, or the low of the current week.

In Example 1, the oscillator high at 5 is a TSS setup. The
high at 3 might also have been a TSS Setup, but would not
normally have triggered a market entry, which usually occurs
only at tops. If it had, you would have been stopped out and
gone short at the Trigger entry following 5.

### Example 3 — Small Bump Sell (SBS) Pattern (p.187)
1. Following an oscillator low below the Crossover at A, a
   high is formed at B above the Crossover and the Zero Line.
2. The oscillator then forms a second oscillator low at C
   below the Crossover and the previous oscillator low at A.
3. The Setup is formed at D by a 2-day oscillator downturn
   that must occur above the Crossover and the Zero Line, but
   below the oscillator high at B.
4. The Trigger entry is a drop below the previous week's low,
   or the low of the current week.

In Example 1, the oscillator high at 11 is a Small Bump Sell.
The oscillator high at 15 is NOT a SBS because it was not
above the Zero Line.

## CHAPTER FOURTEEN: TRADING AND MONEY MANAGEMENT (p.196)

Successful futures trading involves four inter-related
aspects of trading:

1. **MARKET ANALYSIS** — determining market trend, topping/
   bottoming levels, and timing for tops and bottoms.
2. **MONEY MANAGEMENT** — calculating risk/reward, size of
   positions and preservation of trading capital.
3. **MARKET ENTRY AND EXIT** — actual time and price levels for
   buying and selling.
4. **A GAME PLAN** — combining all of the above into a Trading
   Strategy that includes:
   - How and when you will enter the market
   - How and when you will take profits
   - Placement and adjustment of trailing stops
   - Other strategies you wish to include in your Game Plan

Most people learn to trade the futures markets on their own
through actual trading in the futures markets. No one can
tell you how to trade, but you can learn from the mistakes
of others.

**The single most important aspect of trading futures, or any
highly leveraged market, is money management.** You will make
mistakes, and you will lose money, but with the proper
approach, you can turn futures into a profitable venture.

### 12 CARDINAL MISTAKES IN TRADING FUTURES (p.197)

1. Lack of a Game Plan
2. Lack of Money Management
3. Failure to Use Protective Stop/Loss Orders
4. Taking Small Profits and Letting Your Losses Run
5. Overstaying Your Position
6. Averaging a Loss
7. Meeting Margin Calls
8. Increasing Your Commitment with Success
9. Overtrading Your Account
10. Failure to Remove Profits from Your Account
11. Changing Your Strategy During Market Hours
12. Lack of Patience, or Trading for Excitement, Not Profit

### Introduction: Trading Psychology (pp.197-198)

**Self-Discipline**: The greatest cause of loss is lack of self-
discipline — lack of self-discipline to follow your game plan;
lack of self-discipline to be patient; lack of self-discipline
to take a loss or profit; and lack of self-discipline to
follow proven money management concepts.

**Fear and Greed**: With the tremendous leverage of futures
markets, you are intensely exposed to the basic emotions of
fear and greed. At certain times, these emotions can make you
completely and absolutely irrational, oblivious to what is
really happening. It can make you rely on hope, hope that the
market will do what you want it to do because it must!
Otherwise you will lose all of your risk capital.

**Danger of Success**: Each time Bressert made one of the 12
CARDINAL MISTAKES, he promised not to repeat it. But after
becoming successful again and making large amounts of money,
he invariably became overconfident, sloppy and 'dangerous.'
You are most likely to make these same mistakes when you are
*making money* not losing it. After several losses, you
naturally tighten up your discipline and become more
conservative.

**Overconfidence**: Following a string of profitable trades you
are most likely to lose *large* amounts of money. If you began
with $30,000 and limited yourself to 10% risk ($3000/trade),
with profits increasing to $100,000, you can now lose $10,000
per trade. This is the time you should reduce your risk to 5%;
but flushed with success, you are more prone to break your
rules, double up your positions and let your losses run.
Reviewing his records, Bressert found his largest losses came
from his *smallest* positions.

**Hope**: One of the early signs that you have made a serious
mistake is when you change your routine and begin to call your
broker frequently for quotes and 'reasons' for the market to
go your way. *Hope* is the most devastating of all emotions in
trading futures because it can lull you into complacency. You
*know* when you find yourself *hoping* that you are wrong, and
should immediately get out of the market.

**Balance**: Trading futures is a game of psychology. It is a
game of balance. Emotional extremes create an imbalance. In
your elation at being successful, you make mistakes of greed.
In your reluctance to take a loss you make mistakes of fear.

**Take Profits**: Tremendous amounts of money can be made in the
futures markets. Profits are there for the making, but the
real key to trading futures is not making money; it is keeping
it. It is not basking in the elation of success; it is taking
your profits and looking over your shoulder.

**Profit/Loss Cycle**: Every experienced futures trader has a
profit/loss cycle. Without exception, every trader has
experienced a cycle of success, of over commitment, of over-
confidence, followed by losses and the feeling of failure.
Bressert has made and lost many millions of dollars and knows
that these 12 CARDINAL MISTAKES can be overcome through
strict, unbending self-discipline, and mechanical rules that
cannot be broken.

### THE 12 CARDINAL MISTAKES (Detail)

**(1) LACK OF A GAME PLAN (p.199)**
Better than 90% of the futures traders I have come in contact
with had no game plan. They did not know what to do if they
were wrong, and they did not know what to do if they were
right. The large paper profits they made often turned into a
large loss because they did not know where to get out.

**A game plan must include:**
- Know how and where you are going to enter a market
- Know how much money you are going to risk on each trade
- Know how and where you are going to get out if wrong
- Know how and where you are going to take profits if right
- Know how much money you are going to make if right
- Have a safety stop in case the market does the unexpected
- Have an approximate idea of when a market should meet your
  objectives; of when it should begin to make a move, and if
  it has not done so, get out

**(2) LACK OF MONEY MANAGEMENT (p.200)**
Few traders have a workable concept of money management.
Money management is controlling your risk through the use of
stops, while balancing your potential for loss against your
potential for profit.

**Key insight**: A trade that might lose $500 if wrong and make
$1500 if right as a 3-to-1 reward/risk ratio is misleading
because the most important aspect is not how much you make if
right, but **what the odds are of making money, of being
right.** What are your odds of losing money, of being wrong?

**Better approach**: You are better off with a trade in which
you might lose $1000 if wrong, or make $1000 if right, that
would work eight times out of ten, than to take a trade where
you would make $1500 and lose only $500, but which is
profitable only one time out of three.

## CHAPTER FOURTEEN (continued): 12 CARDINAL MISTAKES

**(3) FAILURE TO USE PROTECTIVE STOP/LOSS ORDERS (p.201)**
It is the failure to use *stop/loss orders* once you enter the
market -- not mental stops, but real stops that cannot be
moved. All too often traders use mental stops because, in the
past, they have been stopped out and then watched the market
move in their direction. This does not invalidate the use of
stops. It simply means that their stop was in the wrong place
-- they did not have a good technical stop.

When a stop/loss order that was determined before you entered
the market is hit, it means your analysis was wrong, your game
plan was wrong. With a mental stop, as soon as the market has
gone through your stop price, you no longer act like a rational
human being. You are now operating on *fear and hope*. How many
times have *you* had a mental stop and instructed your broker
to call you when prices went through it. By the time he could
call you, the market had run an extra $1000 against you. You
probably decided to hold onto the trade in the *hope* that it
would let you out on a retracement to your entry price.
Unfortunately, it never touched that price again, and you took
a larger loss.

Or you held the trade overnight because you *hoped* it was
going to go higher the next day. But the next day it was lower,
and by then your loss was so large you couldn't *'afford'* to
get out -- and what should have been a small loss turned into
a disaster.

**"The first loss is the smallest. It is also the easiest to
take even though it may seem hard at the time."**

**Solution**: Have an unbreakable rule (and the discipline to
follow it!) that stop/loss orders must be placed each and every
time the market is entered. Have the stop order waiting before
the open or immediately after entering the market. Do your
homework when the market is closed, and place your order before
the open. Another rule: the initial protective stop/loss order
cannot be changed to increase the risk, only to reduce it.

**(4) TAKING SMALL PROFITS AND LETTING YOUR LOSSES RUN (p.201)**
A very common mistake among inexperienced traders is taking
small profits and letting losses run. This is often the result
of no game plan. After one or two losing trades, you're very
likely to take a small profit on the next trade, even though
that trade could have turned into a large profit-maker that
would have offset all your losses.

Letting your losses run happens to new futures traders, but is
not uncommon among professional futures traders either. After
entering a market, you don't know where to get out. Once you
start losing money your tendency is to let your loss get larger
and larger as you *hope* that the market will retrace to let
you break even -- which, of course, it seldom does.

**Solution**: Use pre-determined stop/loss orders to prevent your
losses from running, and follow your game plan to take profits
at your profit objective, or with a trailing stop.

**(5) OVERSTAYING YOUR POSITION (p.202)**
[Not detailed in the visible text beyond the heading]

**(6) AVERAGING A LOSS (p.202)**
This is usually a holdover from trading stocks. In futures,
with five or ten percent margin, averaging a loss can be
disastrous.

A typical approach: after you have bought a contract and it
drops lower, you figure that since it was a good buy then, it
is a better buy now. You can also justify averaging down by
figuring you will have a lower average entry price and require
a smaller move to break even. Unfortunately, you will lose
twice as much if the market continues against you, as it
almost always does.

There are approaches that will allow you to buy a market at
one price level, add on again at even a lower level, as long
as this was your *pre-determined* game plan *before* you bought
the first contract. And you must also have an unmovable
stop/loss order that takes you out of all contracts.

**Rule**: *Never average a loss unless your pre-determined game
plan called for buying the market at lower levels with an
unmovable stop/loss order to take you out of all contracts
if it is hit.*

**(7) MEETING MARGIN CALLS (p.202)**
Most often, meeting a margin call will only increase your loss.
A margin call means you are wrong in the market, and your
position should be closed out. Margin calls are met because
people do not want to admit being wrong and take a loss;
because they *hope* the market will eventually go in their
direction.

Margin calls are the result of making one or more of the other
12 CARDINAL MISTAKES such as not having a game plan, not using
stop/loss orders, overtrading and poor money management.

**Rule**: *You should never have a margin call, much less meet
one using the rules to overcome the 12 CARDINAL MISTAKES.*

**(8) INCREASING YOUR COMMITMENT WITH SUCCESS (p.203)**
One of the most dangerous mistakes you can make in trading is
to increase your exposure as you become more successful. Just
by being successful you will risk more dollars per trade
because you have more money. But, because you have more money
(and confidence) when successful, you are also more likely to
take larger percentage risks. Not surprisingly, this ruins
more futures traders than a series of small losses.

**Solution**: *Do not allow your percentage commitment to increase
as you realize profits (it should actually decrease), and
maintain your stop/loss discipline.*

**(9) OVERTRADING YOUR ACCOUNT (p.203)**
...or risking too large a percentage of equity on any single
trade. Either with too large a dollar risk per contract or by
trading too many contracts for any single trade, or by trading
too many markets.

This often happens after a period of success when you *'know'*
the market is going to do something. You are so certain that
this is going to be a really big move, that you risk much more
than the maximum 5-10% of your equity. Already emotionally out
of balance, all it takes is a couple of limit moves against
you and you are bust.

**Solution**: *You must have a hard and fast rule that you can
risk no more than a certain percentage of your equity on any
trade regardless of how good the trade looks, and should have
a program for lowering that percentage as your equity
increases.*

**(10) FAILURE TO REMOVE PROFITS FROM YOUR ACCOUNT (p.203)**
It seems to be a natural law that the futures markets, over a
given period of time, will allow you to make only so much money
and then you are going to have to start giving some back. Yet,
no more than a handful of the futures traders I know have a
rule to take profits *out* of their account. They leave profits
in their account and go for the 'big trade,' the one trade
that will give them a real 'killing,' and that usually kills
their profits.

**Solution**: *Pre-determine an equity level at which you remove
profits from your account. When you make profits in the
futures markets, take some money out and put it somewhere else.
The markets are not a cornucopia. If you chart your equity, you
will see that it will also move in cycles. You will make some,
lose some, make some, lose some. By taking money out of your
account when you are profitable, you will not make the mistake
of losing larger amounts of money when your down cycle begins.*

**(11) CHANGING YOUR STRATEGY DURING MARKET HOURS (p.204)**
During market hours you are subject to emotional reactions of
fear and greed much more than you are when the market is closed.
Have you ever sat down in the quiet of the night before the
trading day, and very calmly figured out what you wanted to do
the next day; yet, shortly after the market opened you did
exactly the opposite of what you had planned?

With rare exception, the best approach is to *not* change your
trading strategy during market hours.

**Solution**: *Develop your trading strategy before the market
opens and have the discipline to not change your game plan
during the day, unless it is to stand aside.*

**(12) LACK OF PATIENCE (OR TRADING FOR EXCITEMENT, NOT PROFIT)
(p.204)**
The average life of a futures trader is somewhere between five
minutes and nine months. Not all traders trade because they
want to make money. Many trade because they want the action.
Think about it... must you have a trade a day, or can you
patiently wait for the high probability trades, even if it
means standing aside for a week or two?

**Solution**: *You must evaluate your own trading and determine
whether you really trade to make money, or for the action and
excitement. To overcome this mistake, you must develop
patience, do your homework, and research the markets for high
probability trades.*

**Final words on the 12 Cardinal Mistakes (p.204):**
For those of you that do wish to learn how to make money in
futures, rest assured you can. Do not expect to make money in
each and every trade, but if you avoid the 12 CARDINAL
MISTAKES, you must make money over an extended period of time.
Certainly the market will do the unexpected, and at times you
will lose more than you expected; but if you consciously try
not to make these mistakes, you must make money.

By studying the past history of a market you can isolate high
probability trades and situations that offer exceptionally
large profits relative to the dollar risk... if you have the
patience to wait for them.

## PART II: CONTROLLED RISK MONEY MANAGEMENT (p.205)

Most futures traders spend 99% of their time on analysis and
the buying and selling of markets. Many of these traders
ultimately join the legions of ex-futures traders because they
ignored the most important aspect of speculation -- money
management.

You can be a good analyst and lose money due to poor money
management. But, if you have sound, market-proven money
management concepts, and the discipline to follow them, you
will never lose all of your money.

Since developing the following money management concepts in
the mid-1970s, Bressert has had large profits as well as large
losses -- but never a single margin call. There is no guarantee
that you will make money using these money management rules,
but you will never lose the farm.

### Key Money Management Rules (p.205)

**Before entering the market, determine a stop/loss as well as
a profit objective.**

Many traders often enter the market with a price objective, but
without a clearly defined protective stop. When the market
moves against them they are often forced out by the size of
their margin call. They lose control, and the results are often
disastrous. What should have been a relatively small loss
becomes an extremely large loss.

**The stop/loss must be in the market, not in your mind.**

If you have been stopped out only to have the market make the
move without you, the problem was how you determined where to
place your stop, not whether to use stops.

**Risk Rules:**
- Never risk more than 10% of equity on any single trade. If
  possible, risk 5% or less.
- Never risk more than 20% in anyone complex.
- The most important question in trading leveraged markets is:
  *How much of your equity is at risk?* On any given day, for
  any given trade, you must know how much you will lose if the
  market goes against you.
- You can maintain control by never risking more than 10% in
  any one trade and by adjusting stops so you are never risking
  more than a maximum of 20% of open equity at any time.
- In reality, the 20% risk factor should exist for only a few
  days at most, as explained in the following multiple contract
  approach which will greatly reduce your exposure within
  several days of entering the market.

### TRADE IN MULTIPLE CONTRACTS (p.206)

One of the most important concepts is to trade multiples of
three. Whether two, three, ten, or a hundred contracts are
traded, most traders make the mistake of entering and exiting
all contracts at the same price level. They are going to be
all right or all wrong. In using multiple contracts, no fewer
than three contracts should be traded per position, and one-
third of each position should have a different profit
objective. If trading 3 contracts, each contract would have a
different price objective; if trading 90 contracts, each
grouping of 30 contracts would have a different price
objective... with each one-third of a position having a
different price objective you can be wrong on your expectations
and still make money!

**Example**: A $30,000 account risking 10% of equity can afford
to risk $3,000 on the overall position. If the dollar risk per
contract from point of entry to stop/loss is $900, commissions
and 'skiddage' might equal another $100; the dollar risk per
contract is $1,000 with a total $3,000 for the three contracts
in the position.

### Contract No.1: The Money Contract (p.206)
The first contract, called the Money contract, is the most
important. When possible, the profit figure for the Money
Contract should equal the dollar risk, but should seldom be
more than $1,000 under normal market conditions. In the
example, the pre-determined dollar risk per contract, including
'skiddage' is $1,000, so the pre-determined profit objective
for the Money Contract is also $1,000.

It is important to have all three contracts on before the
market moves. All three contracts can be entered at once, or
can be put on at different price levels. Once the three
contracts are positioned, place an exit order for the money
position at the pre-determined profit objective. This order
should be placed every day before the open.

**Profits on the money contract should be taken as quickly as
possible. Normally, the money contract should be liquidated
within five.** If not, you may be expecting too much from the
market, or the market may be telling you that it is not going
to move in your direction.

**When the money contract is liquidated, the whole tone of the
market changes** because, now, your risk is lowered by two-thirds
of your initial risk exposure, and best of all, you have
$1,000 in closed profits in your account.

In the $30,000 account example, risking 10%, a three-contract
position was entered at $1,000 risking 10%, or 3-1/3% risk per
contract. Once profits have been taken on the Money Contract,
not only has its 3-1/3% risk of $1000 been eliminated, but the
$1,000 profit in pocket effectively negates the exposure of
the 3-1/3% risk of $1000 for the No. 2 Short-Term Contract,
dropping the total dollar risk to about 3-1/3%, or $1,000, for
the two remaining positions -- your emotional commitment is
similarly reduced.

### Contract No.2: The Short-Term Profit Objective Contract (p.207)
The Short-Term Contract is also designed to take profits at a
pre-determined profit objective. Normally, this can be the
crest of a Trading Cycle in a Bull market, or the Trading Cycle
trough in a Bear market. Either get out at this price objective,
or, as prices approach your price objective, move stops closer
and have the market take you out.

In the $30,000 account, if you make $2,000 on the Short-term
Contract, you now have $3,000 in closed profits and a third
position that has a $2,000 open profit.

### Contract No.3: The Long-Term Profit Objective Contract (p.207)
The purpose of the Long-Term Contract is to keep you in the
market for the BIG moves. Assuming you liquidated the Short-
Term Contract near the Trading Cycle top, the Long-Term
Contract will give up some profit as the Trading Cycle bottoms.
But, the purpose of the Long-Term Contract is to comfortably
ride with the market until your long-term price objective is
reached, which is often the price objective for the Primary
Cycle or the Seasonal Cycle.

These money management concepts can be modified depending upon
the position of the Trading Cycle and when the buy/sell signal
is generated.

### Multiple Contract Pyramid (pp.207-208)
Shown in the example on the following page, is the first three
contract position, which is entered at the Trading Cycle bottom
with a dollar risk of about $1,000 per contract (point of entry
to the Trading Cycle low, which is the stop; plus commissions
and expected 'skiddage').

Within several days of entry, the No.1 Money Contract should
be liquidated with $1,000 profit. As the Trading Cycle moves
up a $1,000 profit is taken as the No.2 Short-Term Profit
Objective is met. The No.3 Contract is held through the
Trading Cycle bottom in anticipation of reaching the higher
primary Cycle or Seasonal Cycle price objective.

As the Trading Cycle bottoms, three more contracts are bought
for a total of four contracts -- two Long-Term Contracts, the
Money Contract and the Short-Term Profit Objective Contract.
As the market moves up, the money contract is liquidated at
the pre-determined price objective and a short-term profit is
taken as the Trading Cycle tops. Both Long-Term Contracts are
held, expecting higher prices as the long-term objective is
met.

As the next Trading Cycle bottoms in this example, the market
does not retrace as expected; so new contracts are not added.
The market takes off without the additional three contracts,
but leaves two Long-Term Contracts that can be liquidated at
two *different* price levels as the Primary Cycle tops. Should
the market fail to reach the long-term price objective,
technically determined fail-safe stops must be maintained for
the two remaining Long-Term Contract. But, assuming all goes
well, each of the two Long-Term Contracts can be liquidated at
different price levels as the long-term objective is met.
(Bressert's own approach is to take one-half of the profits on
strength, before the market tops.)

## PART III: ENTERING AND EXITING THE MARKETS LIKE A
## PROFESSIONAL (p.209)

Many trading systems and methods base entry and exit signals
on the closing price. Yet, more often than not, actually
entering on the close gives a poor entry price because a
market on such a day frequently closes near the extreme of the
day's range.

**Two alternatives if you do not wish to trade on the close:**
1. Wait to enter until the day after the close signals a
   bottom (or top) to enter the market, taking a chance on
   incurring an even larger dollar risk, or missing the move
   should the market gap open the next day
2. Attempt to anticipate the close and enter before the close.
   While the second approach usually lowers the dollar risk per
   trade, the probability of incurring a loss is increased,
   especially when buying a bottom or selling a top.

In an effort to solve this dilemma, Bressert spent two years in
the early '70s hand-charting intra-day price movement. He
noticed price levels and time factors that were common to all
markets. The following brief summary explains the basics on how
to enter and exit the market before the close with a pre-
determined dollar risk. This approach forces you to wait for
the market to tell you that it is ready to go up (or down).
Use of this approach assumes a reasonably reliable means of
analysis to determine approximately when a bottom (or top) is
due... plus the discipline to follow your rules.

A consistent means of entering and exiting the market is one
of the most important elements of success in the highly
leveraged futures markets. Professionals let the market tell
them when to get in and when to get out. Proper use of market
entry and exit rules will increase profits and provide you with
meaningful stops that take much of the guesswork out of getting
in and out of the market. They give you a pre-determined dollar
risk for the trade before the order is placed.

### PIVOT POINTS (p.209)
Seven key price levels called 'Pivot Points' are watched
closely by all market professionals, and can be used in
combination with four time periods during the day to determine
when to enter or exit the market. The key price levels, or
Pivot Points, are the previous day's high, low, and close and
today's (day of entry) open, high, low, and close.

Price action on the day of entry often gives highly reliable
clues about future direction by the way prices act relative to
these Pivot Points. These points become more effective as the
day progresses -- the later in the day a Pivot Point is taken
out, the greater the probability of continued movement in that
direction the following day.

For example, today's price penetrating yesterday's high one-
half hour after the open is not nearly as significant as
today's price penetrating yesterday's high during the last 35
minutes of the trading session.

### Time is Important (p.210)
The four time periods that have special signs in analyzing
Pivot Points are:
1. The open
2. Thirty minutes after the open of each market range from
   7:20 a.m. until 9:00 a.m. (Chicago time)
3. Mid-day -- about 11:20 to 11:45 a.m., Chicago time -- same
   for all markets
4. Thirty-five minutes before the close

### KEY TO MARKET ENTRY SHEET (p.212)
- **H** = High
- **L** = Low
- **C** = Close
- **R** = Range (H minus L) for Entry Day
- **Y** = Yesterday
- **E** = Day of Entry/Exit 'Today'
- **B** = Buy
- **S** = Sell
- **HE<HY** = High of Entry Day is *lower* than Yesterday's High
- **HE>HY** = High of Entry Day is *higher* than Yesterday's High

- **R<C** = Range of Entry Day extends *below* Yesterday's Close
- **LE<LY** = Low of Entry Day is *lower* than Yesterday's Low
- **LE>LY** = Low of Entry Day is *higher* than Yesterday's Low

The Market Entry Sheet illustrates the placing of buy and sell

day. 'B' indicates the suggested place to have a buy stop
resting; 'S' indicates the suggested spot for a resting sell
stop. *These stops could be as close as one tick from a Pivot
Point or as far away as 1.5% of the price at the Pivot Point,
depending upon volatility and market history.*

The Market Entry Sheet includes a section for going long and a
section for going short. Each section is further divided for an
open above, or an open below, the previous day's close.

**Example walkthrough (Situation 1A — Buy Long):**
Your analysis suggests it is time to go long or to close out
a short position. Opening price appears above the previous
day's close. Price has not exceeded the previous day's high
(HE < HY). Price is above the previous day's low (LE > LY).
The range of the day extends below the previous day's close
(R < C). Stand aside on the open and for the first 30 minutes
after the open. If prices stay in the same range after 30
minutes of trading (1B), you can place a buy stop above the
most distant Pivot Point -- the previous day's high, in this
case. If prices move up and fill the buy order, place a
protective sell stop below the most distant Pivot Point -- the
previous day's low. This allows you to calculate your dollar
risk for the trade before you enter the market.

### Move Stop Orders (p.211)
- If the range of the day stays the same, the buy stop could
  be lowered to the next closest Pivot Point after mid-day
  (1C). Our illustration maintains the buy stop at the
  previous day's high because the range is inside of the
  previous day.
- Thirty-five minutes before the close (10), the buy stop has
  been lowered to today's high, and the protective stop would
  be raised to today's open if the buy order is filled.
- Other variations depend on price action: the buy order could
  have been kept at the previous day's high, moved down to the
  current day's open or lowered to the previous day's close;
  the sell stop also could have been placed at another Pivot
  Point.

### Pivot Point Rules (p.212)
1. Market entry/exit Pivot Points work best at cycle tops and
   bottoms, and oscillator overextensions. Prices move in
   cyclical patterns, and you should be in tune with these
   cyclical phases to use Pivot Point rules to catch market
   turns. However, price patterns on the Market Entry Sheet
   and Pivot Points can be used whenever oscillators indicate
   that a bottom or top is forming, whether you follow cycles
   or not.
2. The Pivot Points are excellent filters for intra-day
   trading systems or oscillators. If the trading signal is
   good, it should be followed by penetration of the nearest
   pivot Point.
3. When using Pivot Points to establish a position, always
   enter a protective stop/loss order immediately after your
   initial order is filled.
4. If the market does not appear to be closing as expected,
   get out of the market. A good rule of thumb is the trading
   maxim: *When in doubt, stay out; if in doubt, get out!*
5. As with cycles, the use of Pivot Points is an art, not a
   science. Different markets have different characteristics,
   and Pivot Points work better in some markets than in
   others. Some markets work better at different times of the
   day than other markets. As you gain experience in using
   Pivot Points, you will be able to modify these guidelines
   to fit your own trading personality.

**Caution on different markets**: Currencies, for example, have
been known to knock out both high and low Pivot Points during
the last 30 minutes of trading while such action is almost
never seen in gold. Before using Pivot Points in the markets,
learn the characteristics of your favorite market by historical
research or paper trading until you feel comfortable with both
the concept and the application.

## APPENDIX: OSCILLATOR CALCULATIONS (p.214)

### COMMODITY CHANNEL INDEX (CCI)
1. Calculate each day's **typical price** by adding each day's
   high, low, close, and dividing by 3:
   **TP = (H + L + C) / 3**
2. Compute a **moving average** of these typical prices. The
   length of the CCI is the length of the moving average.
3. Calculate the **mean deviation** for the number of days
   desired in the moving average.
4. The CCI is then calculated by subtracting the moving
   average from today's typical price and dividing by the
   mean deviation times the constant .015:
   **CCI = (TP - MA) / (0.015 x Mean Deviation)**
5. The purpose of the .015 constant is to scale the CCI
   values so 70-80% of the random fluctuations fall within a
   +100% to -100% channel. +100% is the Sell Line; -100% is
   the Buy Line.

**Reference**: "Commodity Channel Index", by Lambert, Donald R.,
COMMODITIES magazine, Oct. 1980, pp. 40-41.

### MOVING AVERAGE CONVERGENCE-DIVERGENCE (MACD)
The MACD Lines for the COMPUTRAC MACD are calculated as
follows:
1. The MACD (Moving Average Convergence-Divergence) line is
   the difference between: a 12 day or .15 EMA (Exponential
   Moving Average) of the Close, and a 26-day or .075 EMA
   of the close.
2. The "Crossover" line is a 9-day or .20 EMA of the MACD
   line.
3. Due to the nature of exponential moving averages, MACDs
   of the same calculation that were begun at different time
   periods will be slightly different.

**Reference**: *The Moving Average Convergence-Divergence Method*,
by Gerald Appel, 1979. SIGNALERT Corporation, 40 Middle Neck
Road, Great Neck NY 11021.

### MOVING AVERAGE
A Moving Average is calculated by averaging "X" time periods,
including the most current:

**MA = [N + (N-1) + (N-2) + ... + (N-X+1)] / X**

where N is the most recent price and X is the number of time
periods used for the moving average.

### MOVING AVERAGE, EXPONENTIAL (EMA)
The Exponential Moving Average is calculated as follows:

**EMA = (N - Y) x K + Y**

where:
- N = the most current price
- Y = yesterday's EMA value
- K = 2 / (X + 1), where X = the # of days in the time period

### RELATIVE STRENGTH INDICATOR (RSI)
The RSI is calculated as follows:

**RSI = 100 - [100 / (1 + RS)]**

where:
- **RS = Avg of X Days' Closes UP / Avg of X Days' Closes DOWN**
- Avg UP = SUM of X Days' Closes UP / X
- Avg DOWN = SUM of X days' Closes DOWN / X
- X = the term, or time period, for the calculations

The UP sum is determined by first noting the UP days within the
time period and adding together each one's respective gain over
the previous close. The DOWN sum is obtained by noting the DOWN
days within the same period and adding together each one's
respective loss from the previous day's close. (After the
initial period, each subsequent UP or DOWN sum is determined by
multiplying each by (X - 1)/X and adding (1/X) x Today's
Figure, where X is the number of days in the Period.)

**Reference**: *New Concepts in Technical Trading Systems*, by
Wilder J. Welles, Jr., Greensboro, NC, PP. 63-70.

### THE STOCHASTIC
A 10-Stochastic is calculated as follows:

**The "K" line:**
**K = 100 x [(C - L10) / (H10 - L10)]**

where C is the current CLOSE, L10 is the LOWEST LOW of the
previous 10 days, and H10 is the HIGHEST HIGH of the same time
period.

**The Crossover line, "%D":**
**%D = 100 x (H3 / L3)**

where H3 is the 3-day SUM of (C - L10), and L3 is the 3-day
SUM of (H10 - L10).

**Slow Stochastic**: To calculate the slow Stochastic, %D becomes
the figure for %K, and the new %D, or Crossover, is calculated
from the original %D above as follows:

**%D slow = [%D + (%D - 1) + (%D - 2)] / 3**
