## Prologue: On the Plumage of Birds

### The Black Swan Defined

- **Black Swan**: an event with three attributes:
  1. **Outlier** — lies outside the realm of regular expectations
  2. **Extreme impact** — carries massive consequences
  3. **Retrospective predictability** — humans concoct explanations *after* the fact, making it seem predictable in hindsight
- Term originates from Old World belief that all swans were white, shattered by discovery of black swans in Australia
- One observation can invalidate millennia of confirmatory evidence

### Historical Examples

- **WWI**, **Hitler's rise**, **Soviet bloc collapse**, **Islamic fundamentalism**, **the Internet**, **1987 market crash** — all Black Swan dynamics
- Life is the cumulative effect of a handful of significant shocks
- Most significant personal events (profession, meeting a mate, exile, enrichment) occur **according to no plan**

> "Black Swan logic makes what you don't know far more relevant than what you do know."

- **September 11, 2001**: if the risk had been conceivable before September 10, fighter planes would have circled, bulletproof doors installed — event happened precisely *because* it was not supposed to happen
- **Expert Problem ("Tragedy of the Empty Suit")**: certain professionals believe they are experts when they are not, know no more than general population, but better at **narrating** or **smoking you with complicated mathematical models**

### Adapting to Black Swans

- Cannot naively predict them, must **adjust to their existence**
- Set yourself up to collect **serendipitous Black Swans** by maximizing exposure
- Almost no discovery or technology came from design and planning — they were Black Swans
- **Tinker as much as possible** and collect as many Black Swan opportunities as you can
- Free markets work because they allow people to be lucky through aggressive trial and error, not by giving rewards for skill

### We Learn the Precise, Not the General

- After 9/11, people learned precise rules (avoid Islamic prototerrorists, tall buildings), not general lessons about unpredictability
- **Maginot Line**: French built wall along previous German invasion route — Hitler simply went around it
- French were "excellent students of history; they just learned with too much precision"

> "We do not spontaneously learn that we don't learn that we don't learn."

### A New Kind of Ingratitude

- **Silent heroes** who saved lives, helped avoid disasters, left no traces — we never remember them
- **Thought experiment**: legislator enacts law requiring locked cockpit doors on Sept 10, 2001 — prevents 9/11, gets no statues, likely booted from office
- Prevention is more valuable than treatment, but we glorify those who left names in history

### Platonicity

- **Platonicity** (after **Plato**): tendency to mistake the map for territory
- **Platonic fold**: explosive boundary where Platonic mind-set contacts messy reality — here Black Swan is produced
- **Edna Ullmann-Margalit** caught inconsistency: using precise metaphor to describe the imprecise
- **You need a story to displace a story** — metaphors and stories more potent than ideas

### Summary of Claims

- World dominated by extreme, unknown, very improbable
- **Bolder claim**: despite progress, future will be **increasingly less predictable**
- Both human nature and social "science" conspire to hide this

## Chapter 1: The Apprenticeship of an Empirical Skeptic

### The Lebanese Civil War

- 1975 war lasted 17 years; nobody saw it coming, nobody expected it to last
- Taleb's family believed it would end "in a matter of days"

- **Robert Fisk** calls this "hotel journalism" — mental contagion from shared categorization
- Political clusters reverse throughout history — categorization becomes pathological when seen as permanent

> "Categorizing always produces reduction in true complexity... it drives us to a misunderstanding of the fabric of the world."

### Efficient Markets and Information Toxicity

- At **Wharton School** age 22: realized public information already "included" in prices
- Stopped reading newspapers and watching TV — freed up time, read 100+ additional books per year

- Colleague **Demetrius** on Park Avenue; people dazed, adults sobbing, cousin's neighbor committed suicide
- Developed specialty in rare events (**Platonic fold**), became "quant" studying **flaws and limits** of mathematical models
- Convinced was incompetent at predicting — but others also incompetent and didn't know it

> "I feared a Pyrrhic victory: I had been vindicated intellectually, but I was afraid of being too right and seeing the system crumble under my feet."

### The Four-Letter Word of Independence

## Chapter 3: The Speculator and the Prostitute

### Scalable vs. Nonscalable Professions

- **Nonscalable** (subject to gravity): dentist, consultant, prostitute — cap on clients, paid by hour, work predictable
- **Scalable** (add zeroes with no greater labor): writer, trader, recording artist — same work whether 100 or 100 million units
- **J.K. Rowling** doesn't rewrite each Harry Potter for each reader; baker bakes every piece of bread

> "The distinction between writer and baker, speculator and doctor, fraudster and prostitute, is a helpful way to look at the world of activities."

### The Advent of Scalability

- **Giaccomo**, opera singer before sound recording: shielded from big egos at **La Scala**, inequalities mild
- Recording technology brought injustice: **Vladimir Horowitz** (dead) putting poor living émigré pianist out of business
- Evolution is scalable: DNA that wins reproduces like bestselling book
- Big transition: **alphabet** invention, then **printing press** — promoted winner-take-all ecology
- **Art De Vany**: "The movie makes the actor" — large dose of nonlinear luck makes the movie
- America more creative than nations of museumgoers — tolerant of bottom-up tinkering, leveraged on idea generation

### Mediocristan vs. Extremistan

**Mediocristan** (thought experiment):
- Round up 1,000 people in stadium, add heaviest person on planet — represents <0.6% of total weight
- **Supreme Law**: when sample is large, no single instance will significantly change the aggregate
- Examples: height, weight, calorie consumption, IQ

**Extremistan** (thought experiment):
- Add **Bill Gates** ($80 billion) to 1,000 people — he represents **99.9%** of total wealth
- Add **J.K. Rowling** to 1,000 authors — dwarfs remaining thousand combined

> "In Extremistan, inequalities are such that one single observation can disproportionately impact the aggregate, or the total."

| **Mediocristan** | **Extremistan** |
|------------------|-----------------|
| Nonscalable | Scalable |
| Mild/type 1 randomness | Wild/type 2 randomness |
| Typical member is mediocre | Giant or dwarf, no typical member |
| Winners get small segment of pie | Winner-take-almost-all |
| Ancestral environment | Modern environment |
| Impervious to Black Swan | Vulnerable to Black Swan |
| Physical quantities (height) | Numbers (wealth) |

- **Gray swans**: **Mandelbrotian randomness** — rare but somewhat tractable (power laws, Pareto-Zipf, fractal laws)
- Most Black Swans result from **"tunneling"** — missing sources of uncertainty due to lack of imagination

## Chapter 4: One Thousand and One Days, or How Not to Be a Sucker

### The Turkey Problem

- **Bertrand Russell's turkey**: fed every day for 1,000 days, each feeding confirms safety — on day 1,001 (Wednesday before Thanksgiving), the **unexpected** happens
- The **Problem of Induction**: how can we logically go from specific instances to general conclusions?
- Turkey's experience has **negative value** — increases confidence as risk grows, feeling of safety at maximum when risk highest

### Historical Examples

- **Captain E.J. Smith, RMS Titanic** (1907): "I have never been in any accident... never saw a wreck"
- **Summer 1982**: large American banks lost all past earnings — lent to South/Central American countries that all defaulted simultaneously
- **Long-Term Capital Management (LTCM), 1998**: hedge fund using methods of two "Nobel economists," phony bell curve mathematics — one of largest trading losses in history, happened "in the blink of an eye"
- **Black Swan is a sucker's problem**: for the turkey it's a Black Swan; for the butcher, it is not

### Brief History of the Black Swan Problem

- **David Hume** (1711-1776): jovial bon vivant, wrote bestselling history of England; philosophical works "fell deadborn off the presses" — famous at the time for work now obscure
- **Sextus Empiricus** (2nd century AD): formulated turkey problem with great precision 1.5 millennia before Hume
- Belonged to school of **empirical medicine** — practitioners doubted theories, relied on experience without trusting it too much
- **Algazel** (Al-Ghazali, 11th century): Arabic skeptic who wrote *Tahafut al falasifah* ("The Incompetence of Philosophers")
- Debate between Algazel and **Averroës** — aftermath: Arabs abandoned scientific method, West embraced Averroës's rationalism
- **Pierre Bayle** (1647-1706): introduced Hume to ancient skepticism; **Pierre-Daniel Huet** (1630-1721): bishop who wrote *Philosophical Treatise on the Weaknesses of the Human Mind*
- Both erudites who spent lives reading — **erudition is important**: shield against Platonicity

### I Don't Want to Be a Turkey

- Taleb not promoting philosophical skepticism — **skeptical in matters that have implications for daily life**
- Not advocating total risk phobia — showing how to avoid crossing street **blindfolded**

### Five Themes of Blindness to Black Swan

1. **Error of confirmation**: focus on preselected segments, generalize to unseen
2. **Narrative fallacy**: fool ourselves with stories
3. **Human nature not programmed** for Black Swans
4. **Distortion of silent evidence**: what we see is not all there is
5. **"Tunneling"**: focus on few well-defined sources of uncertainty

## Chapter 5: Confirmation Shmonfirmation!

### The Round-Trip Fallacy

- Confusing "no evidence of Black Swans" with "evidence of no Black Swans"
- **O.J. Simpson breakfast example**: "I had breakfast with him and he didn't kill anybody" doesn't confirm innocence
- "Almost all terrorists are Moslems" ≠ "Almost all Moslems are terrorists" — the logical mistake overestimates odds by close to 50,000 times

> "I never meant to say that the Conservatives are generally stupid. I meant to say that stupid people are generally Conservative" — **John Stuart Mill**

### Domain Specificity

- **Danny Kahneman and Amos Tversky** (1971): plied statistics professors with statistical questions not phrased as statistical — many made elementary mistakes; would have flunked their own exams
- **Domain specificity**: inability to automatically transfer knowledge from one situation to another
- **Reebok Sports Club**: people ride escalator then head directly to StairMasters

### Medical Mistakes

- **NED vs. END**: medical literature uses "No Evidence of Disease" — no such thing as "Evidence of No Disease"
- 1960s: doctors dismissed mothers' milk as primitive — confused absence of evidence of benefits with evidence of absence of benefits
- Removal of tonsils, dietary fiber dismissed as useless — medicine caused plenty of damage from this simple inferential confusion
- **Bruno Dupire** (mathematician) during London walk: "Look, Nassim, look! No Black Swan!" — pointing at a red Mini Cooper

### Back to Mediocristan

- Our inherited instincts suited for East African survival, not modern post-alphabet, statistically complex world
- **Sources of Black Swans have multiplied beyond measurability** — instinct to make inferences quickly and "tunnel" remains ingrained

## Chapter 6: The Narrative Fallacy

### Definition

- **Narrative fallacy**: our vulnerability to overinterpretation and predilection for compact stories over raw truths
- Italian professor at Rome conference: explained Taleb's skepticism as product of "Eastern Orthodox Mediterranean heritage" — using *because* while arguing against overcausation
- Narrativity comes from ingrained biological need to reduce dimensionality

### Splitting Brains and Pattern Detection

- **Split-brain patients**: left hemisphere invents explanations for actions induced in isolated right hemisphere — invariably offers interpretation
- **Post hoc rationalization**: women asked to select from twelve identical nylon stockings — supplied backfit explanations for why they chose each
- **Alan Snyder**: inhibiting left hemisphere with magnetic pulses makes people more realistic, better at seeing objects without theories
- **Dopamine**: higher concentration increases pattern perception, lowers skepticism
- **L-dopa** (Parkinson's treatment) side effect: small minority become compulsive gamblers — illustrates relation between knowledge and randomness

### Andrey Nikolayevich's Rule

- **Andrey Nikolayevich Kolmogorov** defined degree of randomness ("Kolmogorov complexity")
- More you summarize, more order you impose, less randomness — *the same condition that makes us simplify pushes us to think the world is less random than it actually is*
- **The Black Swan is what we leave out of simplification**
- **E. M. Forster**: "The king died and the queen died" vs. "The king died, and then the queen died of grief" — second sentence lighter to carry, easier to remember

### Narrative and Probability

- **Saddam Hussein capture** (December 2003): Bloomberg at 13:01 — "TREASURIES RISE; HUSSEIN CAPTURE MAY NOT CURB TERROR"; at 13:31 — "TREASURIES FALL; HUSSEIN CAPTURE BOOSTS ALLURE OF RISKY ASSETS" — same cause explaining opposite events
- **Kahneman and Tversky**: "earthquake in California causing massive flooding" rated more likely than "massive flood somewhere in America" — adding a cause increases perceived probability
- **Paul Slovic**: people more likely to pay for terrorism insurance than plain insurance (which covers terrorism)

### Black Swan Blindness

- Two varieties of rare events:
  a) **Narrated Black Swans**: those on television — overestimated
  b) **Unnarrated Black Swans**: those nobody talks about — severely underestimated
- **Greg Barron and Ido Erev**: agents underweigh small probabilities when deriving them from experience, overestimate when supplied with frequency
- **Hyman Minsky**: stability encourages risk taking, complacency, lowered awareness — then crisis occurs
- **Stalin**: "One death is tragedy; a million is statistic"

### System 1 and System 2

- **System 1** (experiential): effortless, automatic, fast, emotional — produces **heuristics** that can lead to severe mistakes
- **System 2** (cogitative): reasoned, slow, logical, self-aware

> "Most of our mistakes in reasoning come from using System 1 when we are in fact thinking that we are using System 2."

### How to Avert the Narrative Fallacy

- Favor experimentation over storytelling, experience over history, clinical knowledge over theories
- Use narrative for good purpose — only diamond can cut diamond

## Chapter 7: Living in the Antechamber of Hope

### Extremistan's Unfairness

- Intellectual/scientific/artistic activities belong to Extremistan — severe concentration of success
- We are punished twice: reward mechanism based on illusion of regularity; hormonal system needs tangible steady results
- **Brother-in-law the salesman**: gets steady commissions — holidays terrible with comparisons, wife briefly fears she married loser

### Lumpy Outcomes

- Positive lumpy outcomes prevail in occupations with sense of mission — researcher, writer, artist
- Need capacity for continuously adjourned gratification to survive peer cruelty without becoming demoralized
- No confirmation, no validation — then bang, lumpy event brings grand vindication, or may never come

> "Believe me, it is tough to deal with the social consequences of the appearance of continuous failure. We are social animals; hell is other people."

### Nonlinearities

- Our emotional apparatus designed for linear causality — if you study every day, expect proportional learning
- Modern reality rarely gives linear positive progression — may think about problem for year, learn nothing, then something comes in a flash
- **Happiness research**: making $1 million in one year then nothing in nine doesn't bring same pleasure as $100,000 every year for ten years
- Happiness depends on **number of instances** of positive feelings, not their intensity
- **Reverse property**: better to lump all pain into brief period

### The Antechamber of Hope

- **Yevgenia's favorite book**: *Il deserto dei tartari* by **Dino Buzzati**
- **Giovanni Drogo**: promising officer assigned to remote fortress, watches desert for Tartars who never come — spends 35 years waiting, dies at roadside inn as the great event finally occurs without him

> "She did not mind the sweet trap of anticipation: to her it was a life worth living."

- **Need for peer validation**: if engaged in Black Swan-dependent activity, better to be part of a group
- Schools of thought allow someone with unusual ideas to find company — "members of group can be ostracized together — which is better than being ostracized alone"

### Bleed or Blowup

- Two categories: those exposed to blowup without knowing it (turkey), and those playing reverse turkey
- **Nero Tulip's "bleed" strategy**: lose steadily, daily, for long time — except when some event pays disproportionately well
- Nero least genetically designed for such strategy — continuous small losses caused hippocampus damage from chronic stress
- In close to twenty years of trading, had only four good years — "all he needed was one good year per century"
- **Nero tore up his employee evaluation form** in slow motion before his boss — "either be fired or left alone. He was left alone."

> "Humans will believe anything you say provided you do not exhibit the smallest shadow of diffidence."

## Chapter 8: Giacomo Casanova's Unfailing Luck — The Problem of Silent Evidence

### Literary Success and Silent Evidence

- **Balzac** is less uniquely talented than we think — countless writers vanished from history
- *The New Yorker* rejects ~100 manuscripts/day — huge cemetery of unpublished "geniuses"
- **Balzac's *Lost Illusions***: Lucien discovers immense cemetery of "**nightingales**" (unsold works)
- What if there are dozens of comparable literary masterpieces that happened to perish?

### How to Become a Millionaire in Ten Steps

- Studies identify traits of millionaires: courage, risk-taking, optimism — cemetery of failed persons has identical traits
- Main difference: **luck**
- **Computational epistemology**: simulate alternative worlds via computer — manufacture "geniuses" purely by luck, then post-hoc explanations emerge

### A Health Club for Rats

- **Russian Mafia** toughened by **Gulag**? — Assemble rats, subject to increasing radiation — stronger ones survive; every single rat including strong ones **weaker** after radiation than before
- More lethal the risks, less visible they are — severely victimized eliminated from evidence

### What You See and What You Don't See

- **Hurricane Katrina** (2005): funds for "rebuilding" taken from somewhere else — cancer research, diabetes — those patients dying lonely don't vote
- **Frédéric Bastiat** (19th century French humanist): "What We See and What We Don't See" — governments show what they did, not the alternative
- **September 11**: ~2,500 killed in Twin Towers; close to **1,000 additional people died** as silent victims — switched from flying to driving (road considerably more lethal)
- **Ralph Nader**: forgot to trumpet tens of thousands saved by seat belt laws — easier to sell "Look what I did for you" than "Look what I avoided for you"

> "A life saved is a statistic; a person hurt is an anecdote. Statistics are invisible; anecdotes are salient."

### Giacomo Casanova

- Adventurer, intellectual, legendary seducer — felt lucky star would always pull him out
- Of all colorful adventurers, many occasionally crushed, few bounce back repeatedly — those who survive write books
- **NYC resilience**: seemingly bounces back from every crisis — but think of Carthage, Tyre, Jericho, all with their own "we always come back" narrative, now destroyed
- **Bias causes survivor to be unqualified witness of process**

### The Anthropic Bias

- **Self-sampling assumption**: some reason the odds of our existence so low cannot be attributed to accident of fate
- **Casanova makes it simple**: start with many adventurers, one bound to survive — but *we are the surviving Casanovas*
- Cannot compute odds without considering the condition that we are in existence
- **Do not compute odds from vantage point of winning gambler** — compute from all those who started in the cohort
- **Educational system** forces students to squeeze explanations, shames them for saying "I don't know"

## Chapter 9: The Ludic Fallacy

### The Uncertainty of the Nerd

- **Ludic fallacy**: from *ludus* (Latin for games) — mistaking games for reality
- Casino has known, **Gaussian**, computable probabilities — real life: you don't know the odds

> "The casino is the only human venture I know where the probabilities are known, Gaussian, and almost computable."

- **Frank Knight**: **Knightian risks** (computable) vs. **Knightian uncertainty** (cannot compute)
- **Computable risks are largely absent from real life** — laboratory contraptions
- **Simon Foucher** (1673): "We are dogma-prone from our mother's wombs"

### Gambling with the Wrong Dice

- Casino discovered four largest losses fell **completely outside** their models:
  1. **$100 million loss**: *Siegfried and Roy* performer mauled by tiger he'd raised
  2. Disgruntled contractor tried to dynamite casino
  3. Employee hid IRS tax filing forms in desk for years — near loss of gambling license
  4. Owner's daughter kidnapped — forced to violate gambling laws for ransom
- Dollar value swamped on-model risks by factor of **close to 1,000 to 1**

- **Yogi Berra**: "It is tough to make predictions, especially about the future"
- **Berra-Hadamard-Poincar-Hayek-Popper conjecture**: structural, built-in limits to prediction

## Chapter 10: The Scandal of Prediction

### Epistemic Arrogance

- **Calibration exercise**: ask people for 98% confidence range — actual error rate turns out **close to 45%** (Harvard Business School students)
- Replicated across populations: intended 2% error rate usually **15-30%**
- **Umberto Eco's library test**: asked sixty attendees to estimate range for 30,000 books — **not a single one** wide enough
- **The longer the odds, the larger the epistemic arrogance**

### Information Is Bad for Knowledge

- **Fire hydrant experiment**: more intermediate steps (blurry to clear) = *worse* at recognizing object — more information means more wrong hypotheses
- **Stuart Oskamp** (1965): clinical psychologists given more patient information — diagnostic abilities didn't improve, just got more confident
- **Paul Slovic**: bookmakers given more variables — accuracy didn't increase, confidence went up markedly
- **Information proved to be toxic**

### The Expert Problem

- **Paul Meehl and Robyn Dawes**: "expert" performing no better than computer with single metric
- **James Shanteau's findings**:
  - **Experts who are experts**: livestock judges, astronomers, chess masters, physicists, accountants
  - **Experts who are not**: stockbrokers, clinical psychologists, intelligence analysts, economists, financial forecasters, political scientists
- **Things that move** don't have experts; **things that don't move** do
- **Robert Trivers**: we're excellent at predicting raid outcomes (ancestral), terrible at predicting large-scale wars (novel)

### Philip Tetlock's Expert Study

- Studied ~300 specialists, ~27,000 predictions over ~5 years
- **Expert error rates clearly many times what they estimated**
- No difference: PhD vs. undergraduate, published professors vs. journalists
- **Only regularity: big reputation = worse predictors**
- Self-justification patterns: "playing a different game," "it was an outlier," "I was *almost* right"
- **Asymmetry**: successes attributed to skills, failures to external randomness
- 94% of Swedes believe they're in the top 50% of drivers; 84% of Frenchmen believe top 50% of lovers

### The Hedgehog and the Fox

- **Isaiah Berlin**: hedgehog knows one big thing, fox knows many things
- Hedgehogs overrepresented among famous people — **famous people worse at forecasting**
- Taleb not playing oracle — "I am not recommending anyone become hedgehog — rather, be fox with open mind"

### Forecasting Record

- **Spyros Makridakis** M-Competitions: "statistically sophisticated methods do not necessarily provide more accurate forecasts than simpler ones"
- **Robert Engel** invented GARCH, got Nobel — no one tested if it works in real life; simpler methods fare better
- **Game theory practitioners**: no better at predicting than university students
- **Makridakis and Hibon** encountered shocking hostility from theoretical statisticians toward empirical verification

### Tunneling and Planning Errors

- Projects fail from **tunneling** — neglect sources of uncertainty outside plan
- Student project estimation: optimistic promised 26 days, pessimistic 47 — **average actual: 56 days**
- Unexpected almost always pushes one direction: **higher costs, longer completion time**
- Excel spreadsheets made projections feel concrete — become **reified** (invested with concreteness)
- **Anchoring** (**Kahneman and Tversky**): spin wheel of fortune, then estimate African countries in UN — estimates correlate with random number

### The Character of Prediction Errors

**Mediocristan (life expectancy)**:
- Older you get, less additional life expected — conditional expectation *drops*

**Extremistan (human projects)**:
- Project expected 79 days; on day 79 not finished → expect another 25 days; day 600 not done → expect extra 1,590 days
- **The longer you wait, the longer you will be expected to wait**
- Arab-Israeli conflict: 60 years old — considered "simple problem" 60 years ago

### Three Fallacies of Forecasting Without Error Rate

1. **Variability matters**: error rate far more significant than projection itself — "don't cross river if four feet deep on average"
2. **Forecast degradation**: accuracy degrades as projected period lengthens — read **Orwell's *1984*** or any 1975 forecasts for 2000
3. **Misunderstanding random character**: variables accommodate far more extreme scenarios than expected

> "Perhaps the wise one is the one who knows that he cannot see things far away."

## Chapter 11: How to Look for Bird Poop

### Popper and the Unpredictability of Knowledge

- **Karl Popper**: predicting historical events requires predicting technological innovation, which is fundamentally unpredictable
- **Law of Iterated Expectations**: if you expect to expect something at a future date, you already expect it now
- Stone Age planner predicting the future must predict the wheel — but to predict the wheel means already knowing how to build it
- **Auguste Comte** declared mankind forever ignorant of chemical composition of stars — spectroscope discovered while ink was scarcely dry (**Charles Sanders Peirce**)

> "Prediction requires knowing about technologies that will be discovered in the future. But that very knowledge would almost automatically allow us to start developing those technologies right away. Ergo, we do not know what we will know."

### Henri Poincar and the Three Body Problem

- Perhaps last great thinking mathematician — **Einstein displaced Poincaré in consciousness** (winner-take-all)
- In *La science et l'hypothèse*, angrily disparaged the bell curve
- Two planets predictable; add third body (even tiny comet) — effects become explosive over time
- Won competition for King Oscar of Sweden — found calculation error led to opposite conclusion: **unpredictability**

### Michael Berry's Billiard Ball

- First hit: relatively easy to predict
- **Ninth impact**: need to account for gravitational pull of someone standing next to table
- **Fifty-sixth impact**: every single elementary particle of universe must be present in assumptions
- Must also predict where these variables will be in the future

### Edward Lorenz and the Butterfly Effect

- MIT meteorologist (1960s): repeated same simulation, got wildly different results — small rounding in input parameters caused consequential divergences
- **Butterfly effect**: butterfly in India could cause hurricane in New York two years later

### Friedrich Hayek

- **1974 Nobel acceptance speech "The Pretense of Knowledge"**: railed against use of hard science tools in social sciences
- True forecast done organically by system, not by fiat — single institution cannot aggregate knowledge
- Attacked socialism as product of **nerd knowledge, or Platonicity** — aptly called this "scientism"
- Bankruptcy as subsidy: corporations go bust, transferring wealth to consumers — New Yorkers benefit from quixotic overconfidence of restaurant entrepreneurs

### Free Will and Prediction

- If you believe in free will, you can't truly believe in social science prediction
- **Rationality** became a straitjacket in orthodox economics — **Paul Samuelson** built "optimization" on it
- Reduced social science to second-rate engineering — intellectual fraud
- Before these idiot savants: interesting work by true thinkers — **Keynes, Hayek, Mandelbrot**
- **G.L.S. Shackle**: introduced "unknowledge" (unread books in Eco's library) — now almost completely obscure

> "You have to learn to live without a general theory, for Pluto's sake!"

## Chapter 12: Epistemocracy, a Dream

### Black Swan Asymmetry

- **Popper**: you can be certain about disconfirmation, not about what is right — be confident that we don't know what we're talking about

### The Past's Past, and the Past's Future

- **Fundamental asymmetry**: we fail to learn about difference between past predictions and subsequent outcomes
- We don't frame tomorrow in terms of what we thought about yesterday
- **Future blindness**: inability to think dynamically, position oneself with respect to future observer — like autism ("mind blindness") but for time

### Prediction, Misprediction, and Happiness

- **Danny Kahneman** ("anticipated utility") and **Dan Gilbert** ("affective forecasting"): we overestimate effects of both pleasant and unpleasant future events
- New car brings temporary elevated contentment — forget you made same prediction error last time
- **Trivers's theory of self-deception**: misprediction motivates us to perform important acts

### The Melting Ice Cube

- **Aaron Brown and Paul Wilmott** thought experiment:
  - Forward: ice cube → puddle (specific, easier)
  - Backward: puddle → ice cube (infinite possibilities, much harder)
- Limitations preventing us from unfrying egg also prevent reverse engineering history
- Forward process (butterfly → hurricane) greatly simpler than reverse (hurricane → which butterfly?)

### Randomness as Incomplete Information

- In practice, randomness is **incomplete information** (what Taleb called **opacity**)
- Pregnant woman: sex of child purely random to her (50%), not to doctor who did ultrasound

> "Randomness, in the end, is just unknowledge. The world is opaque and appearances fool us."

### History's Value and Limits

- History useful for thrill of knowing past, provided remains harmless narrative
- Can get **negative confirmation** from history (invaluable), but plenty of illusions of knowledge too
- Back to **Menodotus**: know history without theorizing from it — the empirical skeptics called this **epilogism**
- Most historians pursue causation as central — **Herodotus**, **Ibn Khaldoun**, **Marx**, **Hegel**
- Need generation of skeptical-empiricist historians who understand difference between forward and reverse processes
## Chapter Thirteen: Appelles the Painter, or What Do You Do If You Cannot Predict?

### Disagreement with Russell
- **Bertrand Russell** called demand for certainty an "intellectual vice" — Taleb agrees but disagrees philosophy can teach people to withhold judgment
- Judgments embedded in how we view objects — we don't see "tree," we see pleasant or ugly tree
- Not possible to strip small values we attach to matters without great, paralyzing effort
- Philosophers since **Aristotle** taught we are deep-thinking animals, but we more readily **narrate backward** to give ourselves illusion of understanding

### Being a Fool in the Right Places
- Accept being human involves epistemic arrogance — don't be ashamed of it
- What to avoid: unnecessary dependence on large-scale harmful predictions
- Be fooled in small matters, not in large

## Chapter Fourteen: From Mediocristan to Extremistan, and Back (Tournament Effects & Long Tail)

> "Academic success is partly (but significantly) a lottery."

- Getting published in *The New Yorker* because letterhead attracted editor's attention — reward follows for life
- **Jack Green's** *Fire the Bastards!* analyzed how *The Recognitions* reviews anchored on each other

### Lingua Franca and Power Laws

- **Vilfredo Pareto** found power laws in income distribution
- **George Zipf** discovered **Zipf's law**: more you use a word, less effort to use it again
- Out of 60,000 English words, only a few hundred constitute bulk of conversation

### Ideas and Contagions
- **Dan Sperber** proposed **epidemiology of representations**: ideas spread using people as carriers
- But humans not photocopiers — "You do not make a cake for the sake of merely replicating a recipe — you try to make *your* own cake"

### Nobody Is Safe in Extremistan

### The Long Tail
- **Chris Anderson** revived idea — fractal concentration has another layer of randomness
- Web causes concentration *and* creates reservoir of proto-Googles waiting in background
- Web bookstore can carry near-infinite books (print-on-demand); author can "sit there, bide your time"
- Long tail could free us from dominant political parties, academic system, press clusters

> "The long tail is a by-product of Extremistan that makes it somewhat less unfair for the little guy, but it now becomes extremely unfair for the big man."

### Globalization and Black Swan Risk
- Globalization creates interlocking fragility while reducing volatility and giving appearance of stability
- **We have never lived before under threat of global collapse**
- Financial institutions merging into smaller number of very large banks — all interrelated
- Fewer failures, but when they occur: "I shiver at the thought"

> "We will have fewer but more severe crises. The rarer the event, the less we know about its odds."

### Network Theory
- **Duncan Watts**, **Steven Strogatz**, **Albert-Laszlo Barabasi** — networks have concentration among few central nodes
- Networks more robust to random insults but more vulnerable to Black Swans
- **2003 Northeast Blackout**: perfect example of what could happen if one big bank went under
- **Banks have no long tail** — we'd be better off if they went bust occasionally and were rapidly replaced

### Social Rank, Inequality, and Longevity
- **Michael Marmot** (Whitehall Studies): top of pecking order live longer, even adjusting for disease
- Oscar-winning actors live ~5 years longer than peers who don't win
- Absence of role for the *average* in intellectual production more unsettling than unequal wealth

## Chapter Fifteen: The Bell Curve, That Great Intellectual Fraud

### The Increase in the Decrease
- Main point of Gaussian: odds of deviation decline faster and faster (exponentially) as you move from average
- Height example (average 1.67m): +10cm = 1 in 6.3; +50cm = 1 in 3.5 million; +70cm = 1 in 780 billion; +110cm = 1 in 36 googol
- Only one curve delivers this decline: the **bell curve** and its nonscalable siblings

### The Mandelbrotian (Scalable)
- Wealth distribution: when you double amount, incidence cut by constant factor — **speed of decrease remains constant**
- If wealth were Gaussian: >€2 million = 1 in 127,000; >€4 million = 1 in 886 quadrillion
- Another term for scalable: **power laws**

> "Gaussian–bell curve variations face a headwind that makes probabilities drop at a faster and faster rate, while 'scalables,' or Mandelbrotian variations, do not have such a restriction."

### Inequality and the 80/20 Rule
- Gaussian inequality decreases with larger deviations; scalable inequality stays the same
- Two authors sold 1 million copies → most likely breakdown: 993,000 and 7,000
- **Vilfredo Pareto** observed 80% of Italian land owned by 20% of people
- U.S. books: proportions more like 97/20; literary nonfiction even worse
- **Malcolm Gladwell** article: most prisoner abuse attributable to very small number of vicious guards

### Quételet's Average Monster
- **Adolphe Quételet** (1796-1874) most destructive figure in probability history — invented **l'homme moyen** (average man)
- Created "standards" of means — anyone at extremes became **abnormal**
- Inspired **Marx**, who cited Quételet on minimizing wealth distribution deviations
- **Augustin Cournot** objected: true average person = half male, half female
- **Poincaré** suspicious of Gaussian — noted **circular reasoning**: physicists used it because mathematicians said so; mathematicians because physicists found it empirical

### Galton and the Bell Curve Worship
- **Sir Francis Galton** (Darwin's first cousin) fell in love with normal distribution, claimed Greeks would have deified it
- Built the **quincunx** (pinball machine showing bell curve)
- Applied to genetics (justified) but enthusiasm thrust it into social issues

### Where Gaussian Works and Doesn't
- **Qualitative inference** (yes/no): cancer/not, pregnant/not — Mediocristan, no serious problems
- **Aggregates** (income, wealth, returns, book sales): one number can disrupt averages — wrong distribution creates catastrophe
- Standard deviation meaningless outside Mediocristan; correlation and regression exhibit severe instability in Extremistan

### The Coin Flip Thought Experiment
- 40 coin flips: only 1 in ~1 trillion for all heads; middle outcomes dominate
- **Two key assumptions** for proto-Gaussian:
  1. **Independence**: coin has no memory — but in real life, winning leads to more winning
  2. **No wild jumps**: step size always known — but real life has unknown step sizes
- If either assumption fails → **Mandelbrotian-style scale-invariant randomness**

### Taleb's Emotional Investment
- Spent decade and a half searching for right thinker — found **Benoît Mandelbrot**
- Couldn't find anyone who both accepted Black Swan idea AND rejected Gaussian tools

- "I had to invent my predecessors, so people take me seriously"

### Mandelbrotian Geometry and Fractality
- Mountains not triangles; trees not circles; straight lines almost never seen — **Mother Nature didn't attend Euclid courses**
- **Fractal**: word Mandelbrot coined from *fractured* — repetition of geometric patterns at different scales
- Veins in leaves look like branches; branches look like trees; rocks look like small mountains
- **Mandelbrot set**: most famous object in math history — generates pictures of ever-increasing complexity using deceptively minuscule recursive rule

### Applications

### Power Law Mechanics
- **Power exponent** determines scalability — small differences in exponent = massive outcome changes
- Between exponent 1.1 and 1.3, top contribution drops from 66% to 34%
- **We cannot measure exponents directly** — only estimate from past data
- **The masquerade problem**: true exponent of 1.7 will appear as ~2.4 in your data

- Word frequency: 1.2 | Website hits: 1.4 | Book sales: 1.5 | Earthquakes: 2.8
- Net worth of Americans: 1.1 | Market moves: 3 (or lower) | Terrorist deaths: 2 (possibly much lower)

### My Mistakes

### Lessons from Mother Nature
- After completing book, Taleb convinced world system was "mother of all accidents waiting to happen"
- Oldest system around: **Mother Nature** — billions of years vastly more proof than thousands of days
- **Medical empiricists** of post-classical Levant (like **Menodotus of Nicomedia**) proposed **historia**: maximal recording of facts with minimal interpretation
- **"Medicina soror philosophiae"** — medicine was where wisdom practiced

> "The central things the old teachers communicate to you are dogmas (rules you need to execute without necessarily understanding them) not kerygmas (rules you can understand and that have a purpose clear to you)."

### Redundancy as Insurance
- **Mother Nature likes redundancies**: two eyes, two lungs, two kidneys — each with more capacity than needed ordinarily
- Opposite of redundancy: **naive optimization** — economics classes promote it, **Paul Samuelson** mathematized it poorly
- If Mother Nature given to economists, would dispense with individual kidneys — use central kidney on time-share basis

### Comparative Advantage and Overspecialization
- **Ricardo's** comparative advantage: countries focus on "what they do best"
- Simple perturbation (wine price random, Extremistan variations) reaches opposite conclusion
- **Mother Nature does not like overspecialization**

### Debt and Fragility
- Debt makes you very fragile under perturbations
- **"Felix qui nihil debet"** — "Happy is he who owes nothing"
- Grandmothers who survived Great Depression advised: several years of income in cash before taking personal risk
- Taleb's barbell: high cash reserves + aggressive risks with small portion — had banks done that, no bank crises in history
- Documents since **Babylonians** showing ills of debt — Near Eastern religions banned it
- Forecasting harmful because people *borrow* in response to forecasts

### Big Is Ugly — and Fragile
- **Lehman Brothers** failure (September 2008) brought down entire edifice
- Mother Nature doesn't limit interactions — just limits **size of its units**
- "Economies of scale" prevalent in consciousness without evidence — evidence suggests opposite
- **Charles Tapiero** and Taleb showed mathematically: unforeseen random shocks hurt large organisms vastly more than smaller ones

### Climate Change Under Uncertainty
- Don't trust models — facing **nonlinearities** and **magnifications of errors** (Lorenz butterfly effects)
- Need to be **hyper-conservationists ecologically** since we don't know what we're harming
- Burden of proof on someone disrupting old system, not on conservationist

### Species Density and Globalization
- **Nathan Myhrvold**: larger environments more scalable — allowing biggest to get bigger at expense of smallest
- Small islands have many more species per square meter than larger ones
- Not anti-globalization — just need awareness of side effects and trade-offs

### The Spandrel Effect and Functional Redundancy
- **Functional redundancy** (degeneracy): same function performed by two different structures
- **Spandrel effect** (after **Steven Jay Gould**): auxiliary offshoot of adaptation leads to new function
- **Paul Feyerabend** example: permanently impotent from war injury, yet married four times — other parts of body came to satisfy
- **Aspirin**: originally antipyretic, then analgesic, anti-inflammatory, now blood thinner
- **Convexity to uncertainty**: when you have functional redundancies, randomness helps on balance

## The Fourth Quadrant: Solution Framework

> "It is much more sound to take risks you can measure than to measure the risks you are taking."

### Two Decision Types × Two Event Generators

| | Simple Payoffs | Complex Payoffs |
|---|---|---|
| **Mediocristan** | **I. Extremely Safe** | **II. Sort of Safe** |
| **Extremistan** | **III. Safe** (payoff limited) | **IV. Black Swan Domain** |

- All Black Swan skepticism should focus on Fourth Quadrant
- Late Berkeley statistician **David Freedman** advised: tell modelers "This is where your tools work" rather than "This is what you don't know"

### Rare Events and Consequences
- **Contribution = probability × effect** — rarer events have more severe consequences
- **Kurtosis**: with 10,000 data points, one observation = 90% of kurtosis — single number misses whole thing
- **There is no reliable way to compute small probabilities**
- Drug sales: successful drugs underestimated **22× their sales**
- War: can predict occurrence, NOT magnitude

### Phrenic Rules: Exiting the Fourth Quadrant
1. **Respect time & nondemonstrative knowledge** — things that worked long are preferable
2. **Avoid optimization; love redundancy** — opposite of debt
3. **Avoid predicting small-probability payoffs**
4. **Beware "atypicality" of remote events** — stress testing/scenario analysis flawed
5. **Beware moral hazard with bonuses** — optimal strategy: earn bonuses on hidden risks, blow up, write thank-you letter
6. **Avoid risk metrics from Mediocristan** — standard deviation, Sharpe ratio, Markowitz, ANOVA all fail
7. **Positive or negative Black Swan?** — biotech faces positive uncertainty; banks face exclusively negative
8. **Do not confuse low volatility with low risk** — fooled Bernanke and entire banking system
9. **Beware presentations of risk numbers** — framing effects change perception
