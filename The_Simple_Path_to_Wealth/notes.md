## Chapter I: Introduction

### Core Premise

- Book grew from letters to his teenage daughter → blog → book
- **Money is the single most powerful tool** for navigating the modern world
- Master money or it will master you — no middle ground
- Most people reasonably don't want to think about money — they have
  other meaningful things to do with their time
- This gap is exploited by **financial industry charlatans** who profit by
  making investing unnecessarily complex

> "Complex investments exist only to profit those who create and sell them.
> Further, not only are they more costly to the investor, they are less
> effective." — *JL Collins*

### The Core Framework (Key Guidelines)

- **Spend less than you earn — invest the surplus — avoid debt**
- Do just this and "you'll wind up rich. Not just in money."
- **Debt** = being covered with leeches; same draining effect
- If lifestyle matches or exceeds income → "you are no more than a gilded slave"
- Avoid **fiscally irresponsible people** — never marry one or give access
  to your money
- Avoid **investment advisors**: by the time you know enough to pick a good
  one, you know enough to handle it yourself
- "You own the things you own and they in turn own you"
- **Money can buy many things, but nothing more valuable than your freedom**

### Saving Rate

- Save a portion of every dollar that comes your way
- The greater the % saved and invested, the sooner you reach **F-You Money**
- Target: **50% savings rate** — with no debt, this is "perfectly doable"
- Dual benefit of high savings rate: you learn to live on less *and*
  have more to invest simultaneously

### Market Mindset

- The stock market is a powerful wealth-building tool — **invest in it**
- Market drops are "absolutely normal and to be expected" — ignore them,
  buy more shares
- Nobody can predict when drops happen — media pundits who claim otherwise
  are "delusional, trying to sell you something, or both"
- **Financial independence threshold**: when you can live on 4% of your
  investments per year

### Origin Story

- Collins learned these lessons "the hard way" over decades
- Goal: share what works, where the minefields are, and how simple it all
  can be — so his daughter's path is smoother and her financial freedom
  comes sooner

## Chapter III: My Story: It Has Never Been About Retirement

### F-You Money as the Goal

- FI pursuit was never about retirement — it was about **options**, the ability to say "no," and freedom
- Term **"F-You Money"** originates from **James Clavell's** novel *Noble House* — enough money to be free of others' demands on your life and time
- First F-You Money moment: age 25, with $5,000 saved after two years earning $10,000/year
- Used it to negotiate unpaid leave for Europe travel; boss initially refused, then offered 6 weeks; author later negotiated a full month of annual vacation going forward

> "F-You Money not only paid for the trip, it bought me room to negotiate. I'd never be a slave again."

- Key insight: F-You Money doesn't have to be enough to live on forever — sometimes it's just enough to "step to the side for a while"

### Living the Philosophy

- Quit jobs five more times over career; was laid off once; sideline gaps ranged from 3 months to 5 years
- During one gap (business-buying phase), wife quit her job to be a stay-at-home mom — with no working income for three years, **net worth still grew** — first proof they had achieved true **financial independence**
- Wife later volunteered at daughter's school library, was offered a paid part-time position; stress-free work she loved
- Deliberately rejected **"multiple income stream"** investing — no cattle, gold, annuities, royalties; simplicity is better

### Mistakes and What Saved Them

- Spent years believing he could pick stocks that would outperform the index — classic investing mistake
- Lost $50,000 in Mariah International (gold mining penny stock)
- **Three things that saved them:**
  - Unwavering **50% savings rate**
  - Avoiding debt — never had a car payment
  - Finally embracing **indexing** as taught by **Jack Bogle**, founder of **The Vanguard Group** and inventor of index funds

> "I knew no one else following such a path... I had no one to tell me stock picking was a sucker's game."

- Regret: spent too much time worrying about outcomes — calls it "a huge waste"
- Final lesson: money is a small part of a big world; F-You Money buys freedom to explore it on your own terms

## Chapter 1: Debt: The Unacceptable Burden

### Debt as the Single Biggest Obstacle to Wealth

- **Debt** is marketed as normal, even desirable — the "ticket to the good life"
- U.S. total debt burden at time of writing: **~$12 trillion**
  - ~$8 trillion in home mortgages
  - ~$1 trillion in student loans
  - ~$3 trillion in other consumer debt (credit cards, auto loans)
- Debt enables sellers to charge far more: average new car ~$32,000; college over $100,000 — neither would be possible without easy financing
- Core argument: debt is not normal — it is "the vicious, pernicious destroyer of wealth-building potential"

### Psychological Harms of Debt

- Lifestyle diminished: income partially pre-spent on interest before you even receive it
- **Enslaved to income source** — unable to make choices aligned with values or long-term goals
- Stress builds to the point of feeling "buried alive"
- Emotional experience mirrors **addiction**: shame, guilt, loneliness, helplessness — "a prison of your own making"
- Risk of self-destructive coping: drinking, smoking, or ironically more spending — a dangerous, self-perpetuating cycle
- Brain tends to shut down on the subject, hoping it resolves magically; debt habits become hardwired into financial attitudes

### Paying Off Existing Debt: The Framework

**Interest rate guideline:**
- **Less than 3%**: pay slowly, route excess to investments
- **3–5%**: flexible — either debt payoff or investing works
- **More than 5%**: pay it off ASAP

**The action plan:**
1. List all debts
2. Eliminate all non-essential spending — "those routine $5 coffees, $20 dinners and $12 cocktails add up"
3. Rank debts by interest rate (highest first)
4. Pay minimums on all; pour everything extra at highest-rate debt first
5. Cascade down the list until zero

**What NOT to do:**
- Don't pay a debt counseling service — they add cost, have no magic formula
- Don't bother consolidating loans — goal is zero interest, not just lower interest (18% → 12% is irrelevant if you're paying it off fast)
- Don't pay smallest loans first for psychological boost — author explicitly rejects the **debt snowball** method: "adapt yourself and your attitudes to the numbers" not the other way around

> "This is not going to be easy. Simple, yes. Easy, no."

- Key insight: the discipline built paying off debt **is exactly the platform** needed to build wealth — once debt is gone, just redirect those same payments to investments

### "Good Debt" — Cautionary Notes

**Business loans:**
- Can move a business forward, but "history of commerce is littered with failed companies ruined by the debt they took on"
- Dangerous tool requiring great care

**Mortgage loans:**
- Classic "good debt" framing — but don't be so sure
- Easy mortgage availability tempts people into houses far larger/pricier than needed
- Goal if pursuing FI: **the least house to meet your needs**, not the most house you can technically afford
- Full cost of a bigger house: higher mortgage, taxes, insurance, utilities, maintenance, repairs, landscaping, furnishings, opportunity cost of equity
- "Houses are an expensive indulgence, not an investment"

**Student loans:**
- Most dangerous form of "good debt" — uniquely inescapable
- **Survive bankruptcy. Follow you to your grave. Wages and Social Security can be garnished.**
- 1968–72: University of Illinois total annual cost = **$1,200** (author worked summers at $20/day to cover it)
- 2010–14: University of Rhode Island (state school) = **$40,000/year**; NYU = ~$60,000/year
- CPI inflation 1970–2014: 6x. College costs same period: **33x** (from $4,800 to $160,000 for 4-year state school)
- Driver: **easy student loans flooded universities with money** → building booms, administrative bloat
  - University president salary 1970: ~$25–30,000 → today: ~$500,000+, into the millions
- Perverse cultural shift: frugal student living became **embarrassing** (everyone's going out for sushi on loan money)
- Deeper harm: college debt has **warped higher education** from pursuit of learning into job training to justify the debt — shackles young people to jobs long after the appeal has faded
- "We are creating a generation of indentured servants"

## Chapter 3: Can Everyone Really Retire a Millionaire?

### The Math of Compounding

- Short answer: mathematically yes; practically, almost no one will do it
- S&P 500 averaged **~11.9% annually** (1975–2015) with dividends reinvested (~8.7% spending dividends)
- **$12,000 invested in 1975 → $1,077,485 by 2015**
- No lump sum? **$130/month ($1,560/yr) → $985,102** over same period
- Bump to **$150/month → $1,136,656** (million + new Tesla and Corvette)
- Compounding takes time — the key variable is starting young

### Financial Independence vs. the Million Dollar Target

- $1M is arbitrary; better question: can everyone achieve **financial independence**?
- **Early Retirement Extreme** (earlyretirementextreme.com): living on $7,000/yr → needs only $175,000 at 4% withdrawal
- Counterexample: friend in 1995 received **$800,000 bonus** and still couldn't make ends meet — burning >$175K every 3 months
- Key insight: **wealth is about limiting needs as much as accumulating assets**
- High-income earners often go broke; low-income earners often get there — it's what you value, not what you earn

### The Core Formula

> ***Spend less than you earn — invest the surplus — avoid debt***

- Example: earn $25,000/yr, live on $12,500 → invest the rest in **VTSAX** at 11.9% → financially independent in **~11.5 years** ($317,175)
- Then stop contributing, double spending back to $25K, leave nest egg alone → in 10 more years it grows to **$961,946** → yields $38,478/yr at 4% withdrawal rate
- Conclusion: even on $25K income, a million-dollar net worth is mathematically achievable

### Why Almost Nobody Does It

- **Marketing forces** relentlessly blur the line between need and want
- Credit cards and payday loans marketed as solutions to "not having the money"
- The "can't raise a child properly without a video camera" mindset — manufactured necessity
- "It is simply business pursuing its own needs. But it is deadly to your wealth."
- Referenced blogs: **earlyretirementextreme.com**, **mrmoneymustache.com**

## Chapter 4: How to Think About Money

### Level I: It's Not Just About Spending

- Four mental frameworks for a $100 bill — determines your financial trajectory:
  1. **Spend it now** → poor
  2. **Invest it, spend earnings (8-12%)** → middle class
  3. **Invest it, spend only 4%, reinvest rest** → upper middle class (inflation-hedged)
  4. **Invest it, compound everything, spend later** → rich

### The Mike Tyson Problem

- **Mike Tyson** earned ~$300M, went bankrupt; lifestyle cost ~$400,000/month
- Root cause: understood money only in terms of buying stuff
- Same pattern across athletes, performers, doctors, executives — money flows immediately out and into pockets of others
- The fix: **stop thinking about what money can buy; start thinking about what money can earn**
- When you spend money, you lose both the principal AND all future earnings on that principal — compounding in reverse

### Level II: Opportunity Cost

- **Opportunity cost** = what you give up when committing money to one use over another
- Proxy: **VTSAX** at 8% (conservative end of 8-12% range)
- A $20,000 car paid in cash costs you $21,600 in year one ($20K + $1,600 in lost earnings)
- Over 10 years: $16,000 in lost annual earnings (not counting compounding on those losses)
- Real cost of the car far exceeds sticker price — and the $20K principal is gone forever too
- **Opportunity cost is the evil twin of compounding**
- Leasing or borrowing to buy: "I don't want to pay $20,000 for this car. I want to pay much, much more."
- Once financially independent: compounding exceeds opportunity cost of spending — that's the definition of FI

### Level III: How to Think About Investments

- **Warren Buffett's rules**: Rule #1: Never lose money. Rule #2: Never forget Rule #1.
- Misunderstood — Buffett doesn't time the market; he's on record against it:

> "The Dow started the last century at 66 and ended at 11,400. How could you lose money during a period like that? A lot of people did because they tried to dance in and out." —Warren Buffett

- During 2008-9 crash, Buffett "lost" ~$25B (fortune dropped from $62B to $37B) — yet didn't panic, didn't sell, continued buying
- Buffett thinks in terms of **owning businesses**, not price fluctuations — short-term price drops are irrelevant if the company is sound
- Apply same thinking to **VTSAX**: $10,000 buys 186.32 shares; whether price is $52 or $56, you own the same shares of ~3,700 US companies
- VTSAX is **"self-cleansing"**: failing companies drop off the index, replaced by newer/stronger firms; winners can grow 200%, 1,000%, 10,000%+ with no upside cap
- For maximum security: hold 100% VTSAX, spend only the ~2% dividend — principal never depleted

## Chapter 5: Investing in a Raging Bull (or Bear) Market

### Core Investing Principles

- **Market timing is impossible** — no credentialed guru can reliably do it
- The market is the most powerful wealth-building tool ever
- The market always goes up long-term, but the ride is always wild
- Can't predict swings → must psychologically toughen up and ride them out
- Goal: money working as hard as possible, as soon as possible

### The Fear Problem

- Common reader fears: "Is NOW a good time to invest before a crash?" / "Should I wait for the bottom?"
- **Fear is the curse**: prevents investing; causes panic-selling at bottoms; makes you miss recovery
- Crashes, corrections, pullbacks are **expected parts of the process** — not exceptions
- Successful investing is by definition **long-term** — short-term investing is speculation

### Why You Can't Wait for the Crash

- To time the market successfully even once: must call the high AND call the low — and repeat
- "The world is filled with sad investors who got the first right and then sat on the sidelines while the market recovered and marched right past its old high"
- Market timing is an unwinnable game:

> *The person who could reliably do this would be far richer than Warren Buffett, and twice as lionized.*

- TV/media predictions (Dow to 25,000 or crash to 5,000) are entertainment, not intelligence — extreme predictions = ratings
- Equally credentialed experts always disagree; nobody reliably knows

### The Long View

- Dow: ~250 sixty years ago → 17,823 by January 2015 — through wars, crashes, crises
- S&P 500: ~500 in January 1995 → 2,059 by January 2015 (includes 2000-2009, worst decade since Great Depression)
- With 60-year horizon: market will almost certainly be higher in 20 years; high confidence in 10 years
- **The real question isn't "should I invest now?" — it's "should I invest in stocks at all?"**
- You will watch your wealth get cut in half more than once — if you can't stay the course through that, stocks aren't for you
- Smoother ride possible via **asset allocation** (covered in Chapter 14)

### Note on Indexes

- Collins uses Dow and S&P 500 interchangeably for long-term history — they track together with remarkable consistency
- Prefers S&P 500 (broader, more precise); uses Dow for pre-1950s data where S&P data is limited

## Chapter 6 (continued): Why Simple Beats Complex

### The "Adaptive Markets" Professor Problem
- MIT professor argues markets are more volatile, buy-and-hold is dead
- Interviewer points out: even during the "lost decade" of the 2000s,
  buy-and-hold returned 4% annualized
- Professor's response: correct premise, **wrong conclusion** — investors
  didn't earn that 4% because they panic-sold after early losses
- His solution: hold "the entire spectrum of investment opportunities"
  (stocks, bonds, currencies, commodities, domestic, international)
- JL Collins's critique: this is just buy-and-hold everything — contradicts
  his own claim that buy-and-hold doesn't work

> "So, markets are efficient except when they're not. And buy and hold
> doesn't work because most people don't stick to it at the wrong time.
> OK wisdom, but is this news?" — reader comment (Patrick)

### The Real Solution: Cure the Behavior, Not the Portfolio
- Two approaches to market volatility:
  - **Treat the symptoms**: broad asset allocation (the professor's way) —
    guarantees sub-par performance while offering only slim hope of security
  - **Cure the disease**: recognize and fix your own panic-driven psychology
- Collins's formulation: *"Toughen up cupcake and cure your bad behavior"*

### Eight Things to Know About the Market
1. **Market crashes are to be expected** — in 40 years of investing:
   great recession of 1974-75, inflation of late 1970s/early 80s
   (mortgage rates ~20%, 10-year Treasuries at 15%+), the 1979
   *Business Week* "Death of Equities" cover (which marked the start
   of the greatest bull market ever), **Black Monday 1987** (>22% drop
   in one day), early 1990s recession, **Tech Crash**, 9/11, 2008
2. **The market always recovers. Always.** Dow closed at 616 in 1974;
   17,823 at end of 2014. $1,000 left alone = $89,790 by 2015.
   Annualized return over that 40 years: **11.9%** with dividends reinvested
3. **The market always goes up. Always.** Not every year/month/day —
   but the long-term trend is relentlessly upward through disaster after disaster
4. **The market is the single best performing investment class over time**
5. **Future collapses are guaranteed** — every crash will feel like the end;
   only those with nerve will stay the course and prosper
6. **You must learn to ignore the noise and ride out the storm** —
   keep adding money during downturns
7. **You must know this emotionally, not just intellectually** — crashes
   should be like blizzards in winter: expected, not surprising
8. **Major crashes = wonderful buying opportunities** — expect 2008-level
   events every ~25 years; a 24-year-old today will see 2-3 of them

> "Everybody makes money when the market is rising. But what determines
> whether it will make you wealthy or leave you bleeding on the side of
> the road is what you do during the times it is collapsing."

## Chapter 7: The Market Always Goes Up

### Collins's Black Monday Story (1987)
- Called his broker at end of day not knowing what had happened
- Broker: "We've just had the biggest meltdown in history. Market is down
  over 500 points. Over 22%."
- Time Magazine cover: *"The Crash — After a Wild Week on Wall Street,
  the World is Different"* — completely wrong, as always
- Collins held 3-4 months, then lost his nerve and sold — near the bottom
- Took a year to get back in, by which time the market had already
  surpassed its pre-crash high; locked in losses AND paid a premium to re-enter
- *"My mistake of '87 taught me exactly how to weather all the future
  storms that came rolling in, including the Class 5 financial hurricane
  of 2008."*

> "We've stayed the course, with a side-dish of panic." — blog reader

### Three Lessons from the Long-Term Chart (DJIA 1900-2012)
- Through disaster after disaster, market always makes its way higher
- It's a wild ride along the way
- There is a **Big Ugly Event** (the 1929 crash — addressed in Ch. 9)

### What the Stock Market Actually Is
- **Publicly traded companies**: buying stock = owning a piece of that business
- **DJIA**: created 1896 by **Charles Dow** using 12 stocks; today = 30 large
  companies. Used here only for historical perspective (goes back furthest)
- **CRSP U.S. Total Market Index**: more useful — virtually every publicly
  traded U.S. company. This is what **VTSAX** tracks (~3,700 companies)
- **VTSAX** (Vanguard Total Stock Market Index Fund, Admiral Shares):
  Collins's preferred proxy for "the market" going forward
- **John Bogle** launched the world's first index fund in 1976 (tracked S&P 500);
  in 1992 Vanguard created the Total Stock Market Index Fund
- VTSAX/VTSMX/VTI all hold the same portfolio — just different share classes

### Why the Market Always Goes Up: Two Reasons

**1. The market is self-cleansing**
- Of the original 12 DJIA stocks, only one remains today: **General Electric**
- Bad companies fail and disappear (max loss: -100%); new companies
  launch and can return 100%, 1,000%, 10,000% — **no upside limit**
- This asymmetry creates a **powerful upward bias** across the index
- VTSAX's ~3,700 companies on a bell curve: worst performers go to zero
  and vanish; best performers have unlimited upside
- *Critically: this only works with broad-based index funds* — active
  management disrupts the self-cleansing mechanism and adds fees

**2. Owning stock = owning living, breathing, dynamic companies**
- Stocks aren't slips of paper; they represent companies filled with people
  competing intensely to expand and serve customers
- This competitive dynamism makes stocks "the most powerful and
  successful investment class in history"

## Chapter 8: Why Most People Lose Money in the Market

> "Most people lose money in the stock market." — Collins

### Reason 1: We Think We Can Time the Market
- **Market timing** sounds appealing; in practice we buy high and sell low
- Humans are psychologically hard-wired to panic sell in downturns and
  buy in bull runs — confirmed by academic research over 20+ years
- Sobering fact: **the vast majority of mutual fund investors get worse
  returns than the funds themselves report** — because they trade in/out
  at the wrong times

### Reason 2: We Believe We Can Pick Individual Stocks
- Stock picking is nearly impossible to do consistently — even for professionals
- The very few who can do it (e.g., **Warren Buffett**, **Michael Price**,
  **Peter Lynch**) are household names precisely because it is so rare
- Collins's 2011 example: picked 5 stocks, made 19% in 4 months (~60%
  annualized) while the market was flat — exciting, but not repeatable
- "It is also impossible to do year after year. It's a great rush, but a
  very poor foundation upon which to try and build wealth."

### Reason 3: We Believe We Can Pick Winning Fund Managers
- **Actively Managed Stock Mutual Funds**: profitable for the companies,
  not for investors
- Striking fact: as of 2013, ~4,600 equity mutual funds in U.S. —
  more funds than publicly traded stocks (~3,700)
- ~7% of funds fail each year; >half of all funds will fold in next decade
- **Vanguard research (2013)**: studied all 1,540 actively managed equity
  funds from 1998. Over 15 years:
  - Only 55% survived
  - Only **18%** managed to both survive AND outperform the index
  - **82% failed to beat the unmanaged index** — yet 100% charged high fees
- **Journal of Finance study** (Professors **Laurant Barras**, **Olivier Scaillet**,
  **Russ Wermers**, Feb 2010): 2,076 actively managed U.S. funds over
  30 years (1976-2006) — only **0.6%** showed genuine skill; result was
  "statistically indistinguishable from zero"
- **Brad Barber** (UC Davis) and **Terrance Odean** (UC Berkeley):
  only ~1% of active traders outperform; the more frequently they trade,
  the worse they do
- Fund companies' trick: launch many funds, quietly close losers, fold
  assets into survivors — then advertise only the winners

### Reason 4: We Focus on the Foam
- **The beer/foam analogy**: pour beer carefully = mostly beer, little foam.
  Pour fast = mostly foam. The stock market is served in a dark mug —
  you can't see the ratio
- **The beer** = actual operating businesses; real, underlying value; what
  drives the market ever higher over time
- **The foam** = daily price fluctuations; CNBC; the market that resembles
  Las Vegas; daily/weekly/monthly volatility that drives investors to panic
- CNBC experts confidently contradict each other because they're all

## Chapter 9: The Big Ugly Event

### The 1929 Crash
- The one true **Big Ugly Event** in 115 years of market history
- Stocks fell from 391 to 41 — **90% loss** over two years
- Invested at the peak? Portfolio wouldn't fully recover until the mid-1950s
  — **26 years** to break even
- Buying on **margin** (borrowed money) = complete wipeout for many speculators
- **Lesson: never buy stocks on margin**

### Putting the Big Ugly in Context
1. **Entry point matters enormously** — the "90% loss" requires buying
   precisely at the 1929 peak. Bought in 1926-27 (halfway up)? Back
   in positive territory within 10 years. Bought in 1920? Back even by 1936
   (7 years after the '29 collapse)
2. **Early-career investors benefit from crashes** — if you were 22 in 1929,
   you had decades of buying stocks at bargain prices. Any pullback is
   a gift when you're still accumulating
3. **Retirees in 1929**: portfolio dropped 90% to $100K on $1M — but the
   Depression was *deflationary*, so that $100K had far more buying power
   than pre-crash. And it was poised for sharp recovery
4. **The Big Ugly has happened only once in 115 years** — hasn't recurred
   in 86 years; post-1929 controls may make it less likely (though not impossible)
5. **2008 came close** but didn't tip over — encouraging

### The Other Catastrophic Risk: Hyperinflation
- Two economic disasters that can destroy wealth on a major scale:
  **deflation** (1929-style) and **hyperinflation**
- U.S. hasn't faced hyperinflation since 1776; Zimbabwe experienced it
  in 2008; Hungary's worst case: July 1946, inflation reached
  **41.9 quadrillion percent**; German hyperinflation of the 1920s
  helped usher in the Nazis
- **Deflation dynamic**: delay is rewarded (prices fall), action is punished;
  creates a self-reinforcing spiral of crashing prices
- **Inflation dynamic**: delay is punished (prices rise), action is rewarded;
  spiral leads to worthless currency
- Governments prefer mild inflation: stimulates economy, lets debtors
  repay with cheaper dollars — hence "the hidden tax"
- **VTSAX as inflation hedge**: stocks represent businesses with real assets
  and products whose value rises with inflation — especially effective at
  low-to-moderate inflation levels

### The Bottom Line on Risk
- Big Ugly Events are real, scary, and destructive — but extremely rare
- In the context of the core strategy (spend less than you earn, invest
  the surplus, avoid debt), they are **survivable**
- Stocks are the answer to both risks: they provide inflation protection
  and, with enough time, recovery from deflationary crashes

## Chapter 12: Bonds

- Role in portfolio: **deflation hedge** + income + smoother ride
- Stocks hedge inflation; bonds hedge deflation — "yin and yang"
- Bonds pay interest, sometimes tax-advantaged:
  - **Municipal Bonds**: exempt from federal income tax + state tax where issued
  - **U.S. Treasury Bonds**: exempt from state and local taxes

### Stage 2: What Bonds Are
- Stock = buying ownership in a company
- Bond = **loaning money** to a company or government agency
- Deflation scenario: money lent is paid back with *more* purchasing power — offsets deflation losses elsewhere
- Inflation scenario: money paid back buys *less* — bonds lose value in real terms; stocks rise with inflation

### Stage 3: Why VBTLX Mitigates Individual Bond Risks
- **VBTLX** holds **7,843 bonds** (at time of writing)
- All **investment grade** (minimum Baa rating) — reduces default risk
- Widely differing maturity dates — mitigates interest rate risk
- Broad range of terms — reduces inflation risk

### Stage 4: The Two Key Bond Elements

**Interest rate** = what borrower pays lender (you or the fund)
**Term** = length of the loan

- Example: $1,000 bond at 10% for 10 years → $100/year interest → $1,000 returned at maturity
- Hold to maturity = get exactly what you paid (barring default)

**Default risk**: bond issuers rated AAA → D by rating agencies; lower rating = higher risk = higher interest demanded

### Stage 5: Interest Rate Risk
- Only matters if you sell before maturity
- **When rates rise → bond prices fall** (your old bond paying less is worth less)
- **When rates fall → bond prices rise** (your old bond paying more is worth more)
- Secondary bond market prices bonds precisely based on current rates
- If held to maturity: no interest rate risk — you get principal back regardless

### Stage 6: Term Risk and the Yield Curve
- Longer term = more exposure to interest rate changes = greater risk = typically higher interest paid
- U.S. Treasury term categories:
  - **Bills**: 1-5 year (short-term)
  - **Notes**: 6-12 year (mid-term)
  - **Bonds**: 12+ year (long-term)
- **Normal yield curve**: long-term rates higher than short-term (upward slope)
- **Inverted yield curve**: short-term rates exceed long-term — signals investors anticipate low inflation or deflation; "sets the hearts of bond analysts all aflutter"

### Stage 7: Inflation Risk
- Biggest risk to bonds — inflation erodes the purchasing power of fixed payments
- Anticipated inflation is baked into interest rates; this is why long-term bonds typically pay more
- Inverted yield curve = market anticipating deflation or very low inflation

### Stage 8: Additional Bond Risks
- **Credit downgrades**: company you lent to gets in trouble; rating drops; bond value drops
- **Callable bonds**: issuer can repay early (when rates drop and they can refinance cheaply) — eliminates your gain just when rates fell in your favor
- **Liquidity risk**: unpopular issuer → few buyers → forced to sell at lower price
- All of these risks are **mitigated by owning VBTLX** (broad-based bond index fund)

### Stage 9: Municipal Bonds
- Issued by state/local governments; fund public works (schools, airports, sewer systems)
- Lower interest rates than corporate bonds BUT tax-exempt from federal income tax
- Also typically exempt from state taxes in the issuing state
- Appeal: high income tax bracket investors, especially in high-tax states
- Vanguard offers several municipal bond funds including state-specific options

### Stage 10: Bond Universe
- Bonds come from national governments, state/local governments, agencies, and companies
- Term lengths, rates, and payment terms are essentially unlimited in variety
- For simple path purposes: VBTLX covers what you need

## Chapter 19: The 401(k), 403(b), TSP, IRA and Roth Buckets

### The Bucket Framework
- Key distinction: **401(k)s and IRAs are not investments** — they are *buckets* that hold investments
- Two types of buckets:
  - **Ordinary Bucket** — taxable account, no special advantages; holds tax-efficient investments
  - **Tax-Advantaged Bucket** — defers or eliminates taxes on growth
- Tax-advantaged buckets don't eliminate tax obligations — they **defer** them (except Roth)
- Penalties for early withdrawal (before 59½); **RMDs** (Required Minimum Distributions) begin at 70½

### Tax Efficiency and Bucket Placement
- **Tax-efficient** investments (go in Ordinary Bucket): stocks/funds paying qualified dividends, rare capital gains distributions — **VTSAX** is the classic example; dividends modest and mostly qualified
- **Tax-inefficient** investments (go in Tax-Advantaged Bucket): bonds, CDs, REITs — pay interest, non-qualified dividends, taxable gains
- Placement by asset:
  - **VTSAX (stocks)** → Ordinary Bucket (or tax-advantaged; fine in both since it's a large portion)
  - **VBTLX (bonds)** → Tax-Advantaged Bucket (interest payments; exclude municipal bonds)
  - **Cash** → Ordinary Bucket (need immediate access)
- Rule: proper **allocation** trumps bucket choice; tax bracket and time horizon matter

### Withdrawal Strategies to Minimize Taxes
- Structuring income to fall under IRS tax-free limits can result in **zero actual tax owed** despite money being technically taxable
- **Roth Conversion Ladder**: shift money tax-free from Traditional IRA to Roth IRA while in low-income years
- Referenced: **Go Curry Cracker** ("Never Pay Taxes Again"), **The Mad Fientist** (early retirement + Roth conversion strategies)

### Employer-Based Tax-Advantaged Buckets

#### 401(k) and 403(b)
- Contributions **deductible** from income

- Many plans laden with excessive fees — hold your nose and max out anyway; the tax deferral is too good to miss

#### Roth 401(k)
- Contributions NOT deductible; all earnings and withdrawals after 59½ are **tax-free**

### Individually-Based Tax-Advantaged Buckets: IRAs
- Held separately from employer plans; investor controls company and investment choices
- Can only fund with **earned income** or rollovers from employer plans
- 2016 cap: **$5,500/year** ($6,500 if 50+) — in addition to 401(k) contributions
- Can contribute to both IRA and Roth IRA but total must stay within cap

#### Deductible IRA
- Contributions deductible; earnings tax-deferred; taxes due on withdrawal
- Deductibility **phased out** at higher income levels; penalty before 59½; RMDs at 70½

#### Non-Deductible IRA
- Contributions NOT deductible; no income limits; earnings tax-deferred
- Taxes due only on earnings (not original contributions) at withdrawal — requires extra record-keeping
- Penalty before 59½; RMDs at 70½

#### Roth IRA
- Contributions NOT deductible (after-tax money)
- All earnings grow **tax-free**; withdrawals after 59½ tax-free
- **No RMD** — a major structural advantage
- Contributions (not earnings) can be withdrawn **anytime**, tax and penalty free
- Roth conversion contributions accessible after **5 years**, tax and penalty free
- Can withdraw any amount for first-home purchase or college expenses
- Eligibility **phased out** at higher income levels

### Roth vs. Traditional: The Real Math
- Roth requires **after-tax dollars** — to contribute $5,000 in the 25% bracket you need $6,250 ($1,250 goes to taxes)
- That $1,250 is permanently removed from compounding; if invested in taxable account it grows to **$221,909** over 30 years at 11.9%
- If you fail to invest the tax savings from a traditional IRA, the Roth wins — behavior matters
- Collins' concern: government could change Roth rules; a **national sales tax or VAT** would effectively tax Roth withdrawals at point of spending

### Collins' Investment Hierarchy
1. Fund 401(k)-type plan to the **full employer match**
2. Fully fund Roth if income is low (paying little/no income tax)
3. As income tax rate rises, fully fund **deductible IRA** instead
4. Keep existing Roth and let it grow
5. Finish funding 401(k)-type plan to max
6. Consider non-deductible IRA if ineligible for others
7. Fund taxable (ordinary) account with remaining money

> "Hold your nose and max out your contributions. I always did." — Collins (on fee-laden 401(k) plans)

- Final rule: roll 401(k)/403(b) into IRA when leaving employer (but **not TSP**, given its ultra-low fees)

## Chapter 20: RMDs — The Ugly Surprise at the End of the Tax-Deferred Rainbow

### The RMD Problem
- At age **70½**, all tax-advantaged accounts except Roth IRA require **Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs)**
- Fail to take full RMD → **50% penalty** on the shortfall amount
- RMD amounts grow over time and can push retirees into higher tax brackets
- Example (Fidelity calculator, $1M starting balance, 8% return, born 1945):
  | Age | RMD | Balance |
  |-----|-----|---------|
  | 70 | $39,416 | $1,127,000 |
  | 75 | $57,611 | $1,367,000 |
  | 80 | $82,836 | $1,590,000 |
  | 85 | $116,271 | $1,742,000 |
  | 90 | $154,719 | $1,750,000 |
- Account balance continues growing even under RMDs (good news), but tax exposure escalates

### Tax Bracket Impact
- $154,719 RMD alone (no other income) puts retiree in **28% bracket** (2016 married filing jointly)
- But marginal brackets are graduated — only dollars *above* each threshold taxed at that rate
- With $75,300 in other income, any RMD amount gets taxed at 25%+
- Standard deduction ($12,600) + personal exemptions ($8,100) effectively push 25% threshold to **$96,000 AGI** for married couple

### The RMD Mitigation Window Strategy
- Between retirement and age 70½, a lower-income window exists — **use it**
- Example: retire at 60, 10-year window; income below $96,000 threshold
- Withdraw from 401(k)/IRA at **15% bracket** and roll into Roth — locks in low rate before RMDs force higher-bracket withdrawals
- Worked example: $50,000 other income → can withdraw $46,000 from IRA at 15%, total $96,000 (just below 25% threshold)
- Collins personally doing this — rolling into Roth during low-income retirement years
- Even partial withdrawal before 70½ at 25% bracket can be worth it if holdings are very large
- Key principle: **be aware of the looming RMD hit** so you can take it on your own terms, not the government's schedule

## Chapter 21 (continued): HSA — The Ultimate Retirement Account

### HSA Rules Summary
- Must be covered by a **high-deductible health insurance plan** to qualify
- Contributions are **tax deductible**; payroll deductions also avoid Social Security/Medicare taxes
- Withdrawals for qualified medical expenses (including dental and vision) are tax and penalty free
- Unused money **carries forward indefinitely** — no "use it or lose it" (unlike FSAs)
- Can cover spouse and dependents even if not on your insurance plan
- Non-medical withdrawals: taxed + 20% penalty — UNLESS age 65+ or permanently disabled (then taxed only)
- Spouse inherits HSA with all benefits intact; non-spouse heirs pay ordinary income tax
- **HSA vs. FSA**: FSA forfeits unused annual funds; HSA money is yours forever

### The Power Move: HSA as Stealth Investment Account
- You are **not required** to pay medical bills from your HSA
- Strategy: pay medical expenses out-of-pocket, save receipts, let HSA grow invested — then
  reimburse yourself tax-free years later using saved receipts
- Invest HSA in low-cost index funds like **VTSAX** rather than leaving in savings account
- Result: effectively combines **Roth IRA** (tax-free withdrawals) + **traditional IRA**
  (tax-deductible contributions) — "the best of both worlds"
- At 65, withdraw for any purpose penalty-free (just pay taxes if non-medical)
- RMD status for HSAs unclear under current law — potentially advantageous
- Bottom line: anyone with a high-deductible plan should fund an HSA fully

## Chapter 23: Why I Don't Like Investment Advisors

### The Structural Problem
- Most advisors **don't** get better results — investing only *seems* complex because the financial
  industry profits from that perception
- Advisors include: money managers, investment managers, brokers, insurance salespeople,
  financial planners — anyone who makes money managing yours
- Core conflict: **advisor's interests are structurally opposed to client's interests**
  - More money in complex, fee-laden products than in simple low-cost ones
  - Doing what's best for the client requires doing what's worst for the advisor

> "Advisors who put their clients' interests ahead of their own are, to steal a phrase from
> **Joe Landsdale** in *Edge of Dark Water*, 'rarer than baptized rattlesnakes.'"

- Advisors are drawn to highest-commission products, not best investments
- Field attracts con men (example: **Bernie Madoff** — impeccable credentials, clients begged
  to get in, advisors paid handsomely to funnel clients to him)

### The Three Ways Advisors Are Paid (All Work Against You)

**1. Commissions ("loads")**
- Paid each time you buy/sell; Vanguard charges zero load
- **American Funds** and others charge ~5.75% load — on $10,000, only $9,425 works for you
- Some funds pay 1% recurring annual management fee to the selling advisor
- Add it up: 5.75% load + 1% management fee + 1.5% expense ratio = **8.25% gone immediately**
- Compare to VTSAX expense ratio: **0.05%**
- **Annuities** and whole/universal life insurance: commissions up to 10%, buried in the product
- **Churning** (frequent buying/selling to generate commissions) is illegal but easily disguised
  as "adjusting your asset allocation"

**2. AUM (Assets Under Management) Model**
- Flat fee of 1-2% of total assets annually; presented as more "objective"
- But 1-2% annually is a **massive drag**: on $100,000 at 11.9% for 20 years:
  - Without fee: $947,549
  - With 2% fee (net 9.9%): $660,623 — **$286,926 less**
- Conflict of interest persists: advisor will advise against paying off mortgage or funding
  college savings because that money leaves their management
- Advisors using AUM model predominantly pick actively managed funds that underperform the index

**3. Hourly Fees**
- Most honest model but advisors dislike it (limits billable time)
- Rates: $200–$300+/hour
- Clients dislike writing a check (vs. invisible fees skimmed off top) — making it "uncomfortable"
  is actually protective
- Still carries risk of bad advice regardless of how you pay

**4. Combinations of the above** — if advisor uses mixed model, it's likely not for your benefit

### Collins's Conclusion
- Picking a good advisor is harder than picking winning stocks or active funds
- Active funds: ~20% outperform in any given year; over 30 years, less than 1%
- **Two choices for novice investor**: learn to pick an advisor, OR learn to pick investments
- Learning investments = easier, cheaper, and better results
- "The great irony of successful investing is that simple is cheaper and more profitable.
  Complicated investments only benefit the people and companies that sell them."

## Chapter 26: Why I Don't Like Dollar Cost Averaging

### What DCA Is

- **Dollar cost averaging (DCA):** take a lump sum, divide into equal parts, invest
  at regular intervals over an extended period (e.g., $120,000 ÷ 12 = $10,000/month)
- Appeal: if market drops right after you start, later installments buy at lower prices

### Why DCA Is Flawed

- DCA only works if the market drops during the investment period — you're making a bet
- **Key data point:** Between 1970–2013, the market was up 33 out of 43 years = **77% of the time**
- By DCA-ing you protect against a ~23% probability scenario while giving up gains
  in the ~77% scenario — each installment costs more as the market rises
- DCA is implicitly **market timing** — you're saying the market is too high to invest all at once
- Additional problems:
  - Distorts asset allocation: you're holding excess cash on the sidelines
  - The time horizon dilemma: longer period = more upside missed; shorter = DCA less useful
  - At the end of your DCA period you face the same plunge risk you were trying to avoid

### What to Do Instead

- **Wealth accumulation phase:** regular investing from income is unavoidable DCA anyway —
  put every dollar to work immediately as it comes in
- **Wealth preservation phase:** you already hold bonds as a buffer; invest lump sums
  according to your full allocation and let that allocation absorb the volatility
- Concession: if the psychological terror of investing all at once will keep you up at night,
  go ahead and DCA — but acknowledge you've adjusted your investing to your psychology
  rather than the other way around

## Chapter 29: Withdrawal Rates: How Much Can I Spend Anyway?

### The Answer: 4% (Maybe More)

- Having followed the three pillars (avoid debt, spend less than you earn, invest the surplus),
  the question becomes: what safe annual withdrawal rate avoids running out of money?
- The **"4% rule"** comes from the **Trinity Study** (1998) — three professors from
  **Trinity University** ran historical simulations across various portfolio allocations
  (stocks/bonds mixes) and withdrawal rates over 30-year periods, updated in 2009

### What the Trinity Study Actually Found

- Most-cited result: **4% withdrawal, 50/50 stock/bond portfolio, inflation-adjusted**
  succeeded 96% of the time over 30 years (failed in only 2 of 55 starting years: 1965 and 1966)
- In failure years portfolios were depleted; in most success years portfolios grew to
  spectacular levels — meaning 4% was often leaving enormous money on the table
- At 7% withdrawal (no inflation adjustment), success rate was still 85%
- Media latched onto 4% because it was the safest, most defensible number to report
- 3% withdrawal: essentially a sure bet in any historical scenario

### Summary Table (Trinity Study, Table 1 — Success Rates)

| Portfolio | Rate | 30 Years |
|---|---|---|
| 100% Stocks | 3% | 100% |
| 100% Stocks | 4% | 98% |
| 100% Stocks | 5% | 96% |
| 75% Stocks/25% Bonds | 4% | 100% |
| 75% Stocks/25% Bonds | 5% | 98% |
| 50% Stocks/50% Bonds | 4% | 100% |
| 50% Stocks/50% Bonds | 6% | 85% |
| 25% Stocks/75% Bonds | 6% | 38% |
| 100% Bonds | 5% | 64% |

- **Critical insight:** More stocks = higher survival rate at higher withdrawal rates;
  bonds alone fail badly at extended horizons — stocks are critical to portfolio survival

### Practical Guidelines

- **Want inflation raises + certainty:** withdraw under 4%, hold 75% stocks/25% bonds
- **Skip inflation raises:** can push to ~6% with 50/50 mix
- **Study authors' own suggestion:** up to 7% is viable if you stay alert and flexible —
  cut withdrawals when the market dives, resume when it recovers
- Caveat: 2007-early 2008 retirees likely hit two more failure years not yet in the data;
  starting withdrawals at March 2009 bottom = very likely fine
- Collins' own withdrawal rate from his assets: implied to be in the comfortable range
  given the book's overall framework

## Chapter 30: How Do I Pull My 4%?

### The Setup
- At financial independence, assets = 25x annual spending; ~4% covers all needs
- Roll 401(k) into IRA at Vanguard; split **VTSAX** (stocks) and **VBTLX** (bonds) per risk profile

### Account Placement Strategy
- **VBTLX in regular IRA** — tax-inefficient, benefits most from tax deferral
- **VTSAX in Roth IRA** — last money spent, most likely inherited; Roth is best long-term vehicle
- **VTSAX in taxable account** — more tax-efficient of the two funds
- **VTSAX also in regular IRA** — even stocks benefit from tax deferral
- Collins's household allocation (75/25 VTSAX/VBTLX) across: 2 Roths, 2 IRAs, 1 taxable account

### Mechanics of Withdrawal
- Vanguard (or similar) allows easy transfers:
  - Scheduled automatic transfers (weekly, monthly, quarterly, annually)
  - Direct dividend/interest/capital gains distributions to checking
  - Ad hoc transfers online anytime
- No complexity required — one phone call sets it up

### Guiding Principles
1. Look at **all funds combined** to determine 75/25 allocation — not account by account
2. **Tax-advantaged accounts**: reinvest all dividends, interest, capital gains
3. **Taxable account**: send distributions directly to checking (paying tax then reinvesting is pointless churn)
4. Let tax-advantaged investments grow tax-deferred as long as possible
5. Before age 70½: shift IRA → Roth each year, staying within **15% tax bracket** (RMD prep)
6. At age 70½: RMDs replace taxable account withdrawals; taxable account resumes growing

### Pulling the 4% in Action (Collins's 11-Step Sequence)
1. Spend non-investment income first (freelance, consulting, etc.) — reduces draws on portfolio
2. Enter retirement spending down "cats & dogs" (legacy individual stocks, etc.) — worst first; use capital losses to offset gains; can carry forward up to $3,000/year in losses
3. After cats & dogs exhausted: draw from **taxable VTSAX account**
4. Judge withdrawals vs. total portfolio, not just the taxable account's balance
5. Transfer ad hoc when checking runs low — no rigid schedule needed
6. Track spending by category in a simple spreadsheet — know where cuts could happen
7. Annually: shift IRA → Roth up to the 15% bracket limit
8. At 70½: stop taxable draws; live on RMDs from IRAs (50% penalty for missing them)
9. Excess RMDs (above spending needs): reinvest in taxable VTSAX
10. If taxable runs out before 70½: draw from IRAs early, still staying in 15% bracket

### "Living Only Off Income" Alternative (Collins doesn't prefer it)
- Transfer all dividends, interest, cap gains to checking automatically
- $1M / 75-25: VTSAX yields ~2% ($15,000) + VBTLX ~3% ($7,500) = $22,500/year
- Need $40,000? Sell ~$17,500 in shares (~$1,500/month from taxable)
- Collins finds this overly cumbersome; prefers drawing on total return

### What Collins Would NOT Do
- **Do NOT set a fixed 4% schedule and ignore what happens**
- The Trinity Study was about survival, not a mandate to be rigid
- If markets drop 50%, cut spending — same as you would with a salary cut
- In good times, spending a bit more than 4% is fine
- Reassess once a year (Collins does it on wife's birthday or after 20%+ market moves)

> "True financial security—and enjoying the full potential of your wealth—can only be found in this flexibility. As the winds change, so will my withdrawals."

## Chapter 31: Social Security — How Secure and When to Take It

### Historical Context
- **Social Security** created in **1935** during the Great Depression — elderly were the poorest group, literally living on cat food
- 1935 life expectancy (age 20+ survivors): men ~65, women ~68
- System designed so relatively few would collect and those who did would only collect briefly
- As of 2013: men ~77, women ~82 — system's math has been severely disrupted
- SS ran surpluses 1935-2011; total surplus accumulated: **$2.7 trillion**

### The Funding Timeline
- **1935–2011**: Annual surpluses build to $2.7 trillion (held in U.S. Treasury Bonds)
- **2012–2021**: Payroll taxes < annual payouts, but ~4.4% interest on $2.7T covers the gap
- **2021–2033**: Interest no longer covers shortfall; begin drawing down the $2.7T
- **2033**: Trust Fund depleted
- **After 2033**: Payroll taxes alone cover only **75% of scheduled benefits**

### Does the $2.7 Trillion Actually Exist?
- The **Trust Fund** = U.S. Treasury Bonds (not cash in a vault)
- "The government spent the money" — yes and no
- Treasury bonds are real obligations backed by "the full faith and credit of the United States Government"
- Same bonds held by China, Japan, pension funds — no less real
- Holding cash would be worse: inflation erodes it and it earns nothing; at least bonds pay interest
- ~29% of U.S. debt ($4.63T) is owed to ourselves (SS, Medicare, Military/Civil Service pensions); China holds ~$1.1T (8.2%)

### When to Claim Social Security
- Eligible at **age 62**, but checks are reduced the earlier you claim
- Every month of delay (up to age 70) increases check size
- Government actuarial tables are accurate — payments are priced to the odds

**Key questions for timing:**
- **Do you need the money now?** If yes, take it immediately
- **Do you think SS will collapse?** (Collins doesn't — explains below)
- **How long will you live?** Break-even between claiming at 62 vs. 66 is around **age 84**
- **Married, higher earner?** Spouse can switch to your (larger) benefit upon your death — delay to maximize their survivor benefit

**Collins's personal plan**: He expects to live to 80-85, wife to 95-100. He'll delay until 70 (maximizes her survivor check); she starts at 66.

- Bonus consideration: at advanced age, mental acuity declines and managing investments gets harder; a reliable government check becomes more valuable than the dollars alone

### Will Social Security Collapse?
- Collins is not worried for those **55 and older** — they will collect every dime
- **AARP** is the most powerful lobby in history; elderly are a growing, voting bloc
- Politicians don't cut benefits for large voting populations
- All proposed fixes target those **under 55**

### For Those Under 55
- System will still exist, but the deal will be worse:
  - Smaller promised benefits
  - Higher payroll tax caps (2003: $87K; 2013: $113,700 — trend continues)
  - Higher "full retirement age" (now 67 for anyone born 1960+; will keep rising)
  - Benefits may become **means-tested** (based on need, not contributions)
  - Congress will tinker; SS will survive in some form

### Is SS a Good Deal?
- For fiscally disciplined investors: **probably not** — 7.65% employee + 7.65% employer contribution invested independently via VTSAX would likely far outperform
- For most people: **yes** — without it, most would be destitute in old age
- Good for society as a whole; suboptimal for those who follow *The Simple Path*

### Collins's Recommendation
> Plan as if Social Security will NOT be there. Live below your means, invest the surplus, avoid debt, accumulate F-You Money. If/when Social Security comes through, enjoy.

## Chapter 32: How to Give Like a Billionaire

### The JJ Collins Charitable Foundation

- Collins and wife set up a **Donor-Advised Fund (DAF)** through the
  **Vanguard Charitable Endowment Program** — their version of a
  "billionaire's charitable foundation"
- Trigger: two life changes (selling house + retiring) would eliminate
  itemized deductions and drop them into a lower tax bracket — better to
  front-load charitable tax benefits while they still mattered
- Personal aside: the $1,200 spent gifting a gourmet dinner for 10 to
  teachers at daughter's school yielded the most personal satisfaction
  of any spending — illustrates giving as joy, not obligation

### Why a Donor-Advised Fund (Vanguard Charitable)

- Open with as little as **$25,000** — no billionaire required
- **Tax deduction taken in the year you fund it** — so you can time it
  to high-income/high-deduction years even if you distribute to
  charities over many years
- **Appreciated assets (stocks, mutual funds)**: transfer directly into
  the DAF, get deduction for full market value, pay **zero capital
  gains** — double tax win
- **RMD strategy**: roll tax-advantaged account funds directly into DAF
  tax-free (avoids RMD income hit)
- Money inside the DAF grows **tax-free** while waiting to be allocated
- You control which charities receive funds, how much, and when —
  can be automated
- Balance below $15,000 triggers a $250/year maintenance fee
- **Vanguard runs it** — rock-bottom expenses
- Bonus: deflects solicitors ("we only give through our foundation,
  send a written proposal" — gets zero proposals); keeps names off
  mailing lists charities sell

### Collins' Rules for Charitable Giving

- **Concentrate your giving** — Collins chose just two charities;
  spreading small amounts dilutes impact and a larger percentage is
  eaten by processing costs
- Many small donations = many mailing lists
- **Never give to phone solicitors**
- Heavy advertising by a charity is a red flag — signals spending
  priorities not focused on delivery
- Do your homework: many charities are simply inefficient at getting
  dollars to those in need
- Recommended vetting resource: **Charity Navigator** (charitynavigator.org)

### Direct Giving

- Giving directly to friends/neighbors isn't tax-deductible but has
  immediate, tangible impact — Collins plans to do more of this in
  retirement when he's no longer itemizing anyway
- Contrarian point: **no one has an obligation to give** — anyone
  claiming otherwise is selling something
- Only societal obligation: ensure you and your children are not a
  burden to others — the rest is personal choice

## Chapter 35: Some Final Thoughts on Risk

### The Core Premise and Its Honest Limits

- The whole book rests on one premise: **the stock market always goes up**
- Evidence: **Dow Jones** started the 20th century at **68**, ended at
  **11,497** — through two world wars, a deflationary depression, high
  inflation, and countless smaller crises
- But Collins is honest: the 4% rule fails about **4% of the time**
  and requires adjustment; the U.S. could enter a Japan-style 25-year
  down cycle — no guarantees
- Civilizational extinction risks (asteroids, pandemics, AI, zombies):
  extinction events happen ~once per 100 million years — the odds of
  it being on our watch are vanishingly small, and if it happens,
  how you're invested won't matter anyway

### Risk vs. Volatility — A Better Frame

- You don't get to choose **whether** to have risk — only **what kind**
- **Stocks**: very volatile short-term; over 5-10 years odds strongly
  favor good returns; over 20 years **virtually guaranteed** to make
  you wealthier (based on last 120 years)
- **Cash**: feels safe, but inflation erodes it daily; fine for
  short-term spending, but over 10-20+ years it's "an almost
  guaranteed loser"
- Reframe: think **volatility**, not risk — stocks have high volatility
  and high wealth-building potential; cash has low volatility and slow
  purchasing-power erosion
- Best choice depends on your **needs, psychology, and goals** —
  no universal answer

### Fear Is Its Own Risk

- Fear of volatility causes panic selling — itself a destructive risk
- Getting past fear allowed Collins to ride out 2008 without panic and
  achieve F-You Money
- Armed with understanding that drops are normal, market events lose
  their power to destabilize behavior

> "Everything you want is on the other side of fear."
> — *Jack Canfield* (Afterword epigraph)

- Closing message: the Path is before you — take the first step and
  begin
