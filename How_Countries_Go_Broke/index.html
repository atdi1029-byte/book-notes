Key Takeaways

The Big Debt Cycle Is Inevitable and Predictable

Debt crises follow a roughly 80-year pattern (±25 years) driven by human nature’s bias toward excessive credit creation. Short-term debt cycles (~6 years each) accumulate into a Big Debt Cycle that ends when debt assets and liabilities grow so large relative to money, goods, and incomes that the system breaks.

Only about 20% of the 750 debt/currency markets that have existed since 1700 survive today—all others were severely devalued or destroyed. The cycle is poorly understood because it spans roughly one human lifetime, meaning few people experience both the beginning and the end.

Central Banks Always Print and Devalue

When faced with the choice between deflationary collapse and monetary devaluation, central banks have always chosen to print money. Austerity causes more pain than benefit. Restructurings wipe out too much wealth too fast. Wealth transfers don’t happen at sufficient scale without revolutions. Printing is the path of least resistance.

The process—whether sudden (breaking a gold peg overnight) or gradual (fiat systems eroding purchasing power over decades)—destroys the wealth of debt holders and resets the cycle.

Debt-to-Revenue Matters More Than Debt-to-GDP

Dalio insists on measuring government debt against actual tax revenue rather than GDP, since GDP does not pay the government’s bills. By this metric, the US stands at 583% debt-to-revenue with a primary deficit of 12% of income.

The trajectory points toward 700%+ within a decade. Comparing countries by debt-to-GDP is misleading: Japan’s 215% debt-to-GDP sounds bad, but its 1,376% debt-to-revenue tells the true story.

The Fed Is More Powerful Than Congress on Deficits

A 1% reduction in interest rates is four times more effective than a 1% increase in taxes for reducing the deficit over 20 years. Interest costs compound across the entire debt stock, while tax changes only affect marginal revenue.

This makes the Fed’s cooperation essential to any realistic deficit reduction plan—transforming the seemingly technical question of monetary independence into the most consequential political issue of the era.

Nine Stages of Going Broke

Synthesizing 35 historical debt crises, Dalio identifies a nine-stage sequence from deep indebtedness through private sector crisis, government debt squeeze, selling cascade, central bank printing, central bank losses, debt restructuring, extraordinary taxes and capital controls, to return to equilibrium.

The sequence is mechanical rather than primarily psychological. In 31 of 35 cases, large persistent government deficits preceded the crisis. The US is currently between Stages 5 and 6.

Five Forces Drive the Overall Big Cycle

The Big Debt Cycle interacts with internal political order/disorder, external geopolitical order/disorder, acts of nature (which historically killed more people than wars), and technological innovation. Together they produce seismic shifts roughly once per lifetime.

All five forces are currently aligned in a pattern Dalio considers analogous to 1905–14 and 1933–38—late Stage 5, just before depression/war.

Japan Shows What NOT to Do

Japan refused to restructure debts for 9 years, failed to drive rates below growth for 23 years, and created “zombie institutions.” Since 2013, JGB holders lost 45% vs. US bonds and 60% vs. gold.

Japanese workers went from earning more than Americans to approximately 60% less in dollar terms. A convenience store lunch went from 10 to 16 minutes of labor. If real rates rose 3%, the BoJ would suffer mark-to-market losses of ~30% of GDP.

A Workable Solution Exists

Dalio proposes cutting the US deficit from ~6% to 3% of GDP through balanced spending cuts (~4%), tax increases (~4%), and real interest rate reduction (~1%). This would reduce debt by $9 trillion over 10 years.

The 1993–98 Clinton era achieved a 5% of GDP improvement. A single change—applying Social Security taxes to incomes over $250K—provides 38% of the target.

65% Chance of Crisis Within 5 Years

Dalio assigns a 65% probability of major debt restructuring/monetization within 5 years, rising to 80% within 10 years. Long-term risk is at 2.4 standard deviations above the mean (very high), but short-term crisis risk remains near zero thanks to the dollar’s reserve status.

Two warning signs: another QE round to force down real rates, and government gaining control over the central bank.

Gold Outperforms During Devaluations

Across the 35 cases, gold outperforms local currency cash by ~60% from devaluation start to bottom. In specific cases: Japan WWII (+282%), Weimar Germany (+245%), US 1971 (+185%). The average gold vs. bond excess return was +94%.

The principle: don’t own government bonds when there is extreme debt monetization.

Extreme Factionalism Kills

Global polarization is at its highest since 1900. Democracy fails when large wealth/values gaps combine with worsening conditions and weak leadership. The bottom 60% of Americans have less than a sixth-grade reading level on average and receive more in public assistance than they pay in taxes.

How people deal with each other matters more than any financial variable.

Contrarian Ideas

Devaluation Risk Matters More Than Default Risk

Credit rating agencies only rate default risk, giving a misleading impression that higher-rated sovereign debt is safe. Devaluation risk—losing purchasing power through currency depreciation—is as probable as outright default for countries with debts in their own currency.

Japan’s bonds carry top ratings yet have lost 60% vs. gold since 2013. A better system would rate debt’s risk of losing value through both default and devaluation.

Austerity Makes Debt Worse, Not Better

Because one person’s spending is another’s income, cutting spending typically increases debt-to-income ratios rather than reducing them. The intuitive response to too much debt is precisely the wrong one.

Dalio considers austerity the least effective of the four levers, yet it is almost always the first thing attempted because it feels responsible.

All Debt Systems Are Structured Like Ponzi Schemes

Investors hold increasing amounts of debt assets believing they can convert them into money with real buying power. As debt grows relative to real things, conversion becomes impossible for everyone simultaneously. The system works like musical chairs.

The US ratio of government debt and money supply to gold was 7x and 1.3x in 1945; today it is 37x and 6x.

Debt Busts Are Mechanical, Not Psychological

Dalio demonstrated to policymakers before both the 2008 US crisis and the 2010–12 European crisis that debt buying would slow because financial institutions were hitting regulatory leverage limits. The crises were calculable from balance sheets, not animal spirits.

Policy makers who say “if we control budget deficits in out-years, investors will have confidence” fail to calculate who will actually buy what amounts of bonds.

Political Headlines Don’t Move Markets

During the 1962 Cuban Missile Crisis, the 13-year-old Dalio expected a massive market impact. The stock market barely reacted. What matters to markets is money from investments, not political headlines.

Both sides pulled missiles and claimed victory without revealing concessions—the resolution was face-saving, not market-moving.

Intellectual Property Protections Don’t Work

You should assume most good ideas openly shown will be replicated in about six months. This applies across technology, trading strategies, and industrial processes—making technology races a matter of speed rather than legal protection.

Technology Won’t Save Us This Time

Despite being the most powerful productive force in human history (estimated at 150% of the last 30 years’ tech impact), AI gains are insufficient to overcome the combined headwinds from debt, conflict, climate, and demographics.

During other periods of high inventiveness—the Industrial Revolutions, the Roaring ’20s—productivity technologies were squelched when the other Big Cycle forces turned negative.

Civility Taken Too Far Creates Weakness

Winners who got rich and civilized invariably became decadent and weak, only to be taken down by stronger challengers. This barbarism cycle operates in the modern world through creative destruction and geopolitical competition.

Europe is identified as weak and potentially easy prey—Russia likely its enemy, Trump’s “America First” likely meaning the US won’t defend it.

Debt Isn’t Always Bad, Even When Uneconomic

Credit can create great improvements that would have been forgone without it. Losses can be spread out if the government controls the restructuring and debt is in a printable currency. Spreading repayment at 3–4% per year is far less traumatic than 50% in one year.

Key Frameworks

The Six-Stage Big Debt Cycle

  1. Sound Money Stage — Low debt, hard money (gold, silver, more recently Bitcoin), healthy credit growth. Country competitive. Stock earnings yields roughly 4x bond yields due to lingering cautiousness.
  2. Bubble Stage — Debt-financed speculation exceeds income growth. Investors invest without ability or care to assess present value. “Wealth created out of nothing.” Identifiable when debt growth significantly exceeds income growth for 3+ years.
  3. Top Stage — Bubble pops due to tightening. Self-reinforcing contraction spreads “like an aggressive cancer.” Usually temporarily reversed by more credit until that is no longer possible.
  4. Deleveraging Stage — Debt problems spread from private sector to government to central bank. Selling takes form of “runs on banks.” Death spiral: sell debt → rates up → harder to service → more risky → rates higher.
  5. Beautiful Deleveraging — Balanced deflationary restructurings and inflationary monetization. Positive growth with falling debt burdens and acceptable inflation. Most crises in own currency resolve in 1–3 years.
  6. New Equilibrium — Credibility restored through surpluses, high real yields, raised reserves. Pays to be lender, costly to be borrower. Very attractive for lender-creditors entering new cycle.

The Nine-Stage Crisis Sequence

  1. Private sector and government get deep in debt
  2. Private sector crisis; government gets deeper in debt to help
  3. Government “debt squeeze”—free-market demand falls short of supply
  4. Selling causes tightening, economic weakening, currency pressure, reserve depletion
  5. Central bank prints money and buys bonds (QE)
  6. Central bank loses money—short-term funding costs exceed bond returns
  7. Debts restructured and devalued
  8. Extraordinary taxes and capital controls imposed
  9. Return to equilibrium; new monetary system established

Part I: The Big Debt Cycle

Chapter 1: The Big Debt Cycle in a Tiny Nutshell

How the Machine Works

Credit is the primary vehicle for funding spending and can easily be created. One person’s spending equals another’s earnings. When lots of credit is created, people spend and earn more and most asset prices rise. This creates a natural bias toward excessive credit creation.

But credit creates debt that must be paid back, producing the opposite effect. This makes the credit/spending/debt-paying-back dynamic inherently cyclical.

Short-Term Cycles Add Up

Short-term debt cycles (~6 years ±3) see expansions when money is cheap and contractions when rates rise. These accumulate because peaks and troughs tend to be higher each time. Credit is a stimulant creating a high people want more of.

Early in the Big Debt Cycle, debt burdens are low, lending is productive, and lingering cautiousness from prior tight money creates discipline. Late in the cycle, debts grow so large that balancing creditors and debtors becomes impossible.

“Because it is more enjoyable to borrow and spend, if one isn’t careful, debt and debt service can grow like a cancer, eating up one’s buying power and squeezing out other consumption.”

The Core Problem

The creation of unsustainably large amounts of debt assets and liabilities relative to money, goods, and investment assets. A debt is a promise to deliver money. When more promises exist than money to honor them, crisis follows.

The central bank faces a binary choice: print (devaluing money) or don’t print (deflationary collapse). They always eventually print. Either way, debt assets lose value.

Currency Denomination Is Everything

Whether debt is in a currency the central bank can print—and whether that currency is a reserve currency—is the most important factor. Credit agencies only rate default risk, missing devaluation risk entirely. A better approach would rate debt’s total risk of losing value.

The Six Stages

The cycle progresses like a disease: Sound Money, Bubble, Top, Deleveraging, Beautiful Deleveraging (if managed well), New Equilibrium. The system works like a Ponzi scheme—investors hold debt believing they can convert it to real buying power, but as debt grows relative to real things, conversion becomes impossible for everyone.

The US ratio of debt and money supply to gold was 7x and 1.3x in 1945. Today: 37x and 6x. In a typical deleveraging, debt-to-income must fall roughly 50%. Countries that spread this at 3–4% per year experience far less trauma than those where it happens at once.

Why the Cycle Isn’t Understood

Five reasons the Big Debt Cycle remains poorly understood despite millennia of repetition:

  1. Long duration — ~80 years (±25), roughly one lifetime, so we don’t learn through experience
  2. Focus on present — people overlook the big picture while focused on current events
  3. Bias toward credit — most people like the spending ability credit provides
  4. False alarms — many warnings never materialized, creating complacency
  5. Faded memories — 2008 and European PIIGS crisis memories faded; people assume policy makers learned
“These Big Debt Cycles have always worked in timeless and universally consistent ways that are not well-understood but should be.”

Chapter 2: The Mechanics in Words and Concepts

Price Determination

Price = Total Spending / Total Quantity Sold. Dalio tracks dollar flows rather than quantity bought—an approach he developed in the 1970s analyzing livestock, grain, and oilseed prices. Conventional economics assumes constant price elasticity; Dalio’s framework shows it varies with participant motivations.

“By identifying rates of unsustainable buying and/or rates of unsustainable selling you can identify unsustainable prices and unsustainable economic conditions.”

The Five Major Players

Borrower-debtors, lender-creditors, banks (profiting on the spread), central governments (wanting to give people what they want without paying for it), and central banks (creating money/credit). Debt expansions only occur when both sides are willing—what’s good for one is often bad for the other.

Banks create bubbles by lending more than they have. This works when loans are productive and depositors don’t demand too much back. Until recently (1913 in the US), most countries didn’t even have central banks.

Debt = Currency = Same Thing

A debt asset is a promise to receive currency. If you don’t like the currency, you don’t like the bonds. This creates powerful arbitrage dynamics between countries.

If Country A’s 10-year rate is 3% lower than Country B’s and the currency expected to fall only 2%/year (not appreciating to compensate): a virtually risk-free profit exists. Investors sell the lower-yielding debt/currency, forcing either a ~40% spot currency drop or a 5% rate rise. If neither happens, loss compounds 5%/year totaling 40% over a decade.

The Crises Are Mechanical, Not Psychological

Dalio showed policymakers before 2008 and 2010–12 that debt buying would slow because of balance sheet constraints, not psychology. Policy makers who promise fiscal discipline in “out-years” fail to calculate who will actually buy bonds at what amounts.

Money Creation and Growth

More money/credit plus excess capacity equals non-inflationary growth. More money/credit plus no capacity equals inflation with little real growth. High productivity growth allows the non-inflationary period to continue longer.

Early cycle (excess capacity): strong growth, little inflation. Late cycle: weak growth, big price rises.

The Four Levers

Austerity (doesn’t work). Restructuring (deflationary). Printing (inflationary). Taxes (insufficient without revolution). Central banks always eventually choose printing because the alternatives are worse.

“The easiest path, though not the best path for the long-term health of the system, is for governments to resolve their debt problems and spend as they would like to spend by having the central bank print money and purchase the bonds.”

Chapter 3: The Mechanics in Numbers and Equations

The Blood Flow Analogy

Dalio compares the economy to a body’s circulatory system. Money flows like blood through arteries. Debt service accumulating is like plaque building up—it squeezes out the desired flow of nutrients to the economy. Too much plaque equals default (economic heart attack). The metaphor extends: debt growth isn’t necessarily bad, just as eating isn’t bad—it only becomes dangerous when borrowed money fails to produce income that rises faster than debt service.

Key Formulas

Future Debt/Revenue = [(Expenses Excl. Interest − Revenue) + Current Debt × (1 + Interest Rate)] / [Revenue × (1 + Growth Rate)]. If primary deficit = 0 and interest rate = revenue growth rate, debts stay flat. 2% higher rates than growth for 20 years increases debt-to-income by ~50%.

US Debt Trajectory

Primary deficit ~12% of income. Borrowing ~20% of income yearly for interest alone. Current debt-to-revenue: ~583%. Projected: ~700% within a decade.

The Death Spiral

With 580% debt-to-income, if interest rates rise 0.5%/year: by Year 10, debts reach 865% of income, interest consumes 67% of revenue, total debt service hits 342%. Central bank intervention alternative: money supply balloons from $5.7T to $11.8T as it buys $6T in bonds, but printing 1% of GDP causes ~10% currency depreciation at 5.8x debt-to-income.

“A spiral of rising interest rates leading to worsening credit risk, leading to less demand for the debt, leading to higher interest rates is a classic debt ‘death spiral.’”

Single-Lever Scenarios to Stabilize US Debt

  • Interest rates drop to 1.0% (fall of 3.5%)
  • Inflation rises to 4.5% (increase of 2.5%)
  • Spending cuts of 12% (47% of discretionary)
  • Revenue increase of 11%

Each alone is “intolerably too large.” The right answer is a combination.

Why Central Bankers Don’t Prevent Dangerous Debt Levels

  1. Everyone (including central bankers) wants markets and economy up—push limits until debts become unsustainable
  2. Not clear exactly what risky levels are because future incomes are uncertain
  3. Opportunity costs of not providing credit can be as bad as too much credit
  4. Debt crises, even big ones, can usually be managed to reduce pain to acceptable levels

Healthy vs. Unhealthy Debt

Healthy: (1) private lenders give credit for productive uses that pay back loans, (2) government borrows for productivity gains (infrastructure, education) paid via tax revenue.

Unhealthy: (1) central bank chronically printing/buying debt to make up for shortage in demand, (2) government chronically running large deficits with debt service rising faster than revenue.

“The best way to anticipate a debt crisis happening is not by focusing on a single influence or number like debt as a percent of GDP; it is by understanding and focusing on a number of interrelated dynamics.”

Part II: The Archetypal Sequence

Chapter 4: The Archetypal Sequence

Drawing on 35 major debt crises over the last 100 years, Dalio builds an archetypal template for how countries go broke. The template distinguishes between two fundamentally different types of crises:

Hard-currency cases: governments promise money they can’t print (gold, silver, foreign currencies). When paying becomes tough, they almost always renege, and the value tumbles at the moment the promise breaks. August 15, 1971 (Nixon closing the gold window) and April 1933 (Roosevelt’s gold confiscation) are the two defining American examples.

Fiat-currency cases: currency value is based on faith and incentives the central bank provides. The same debt/credit dynamics play out, but devaluations happen gradually rather than all at once. Japan’s bond holders losing 60% vs. gold over a decade is the quintessential modern example.

Big Debt Cycles typically include regimes going back and forth between hard and fiat money, with each system producing its own extreme consequences. The causes of going broke include chronic overspending, debt accumulation, costly wars, costly shocks (pandemics, droughts), or some combination.

Risk depends on three key factors: whether debt is denominated in the country’s own currency (less painful if so), whether debtors and creditors are primarily domestic (giving the government more control), and whether the country has reserve currency status (creating greater marginal inclination to buy its debt). However, reserve currency governments have a strong tendency to abuse the privilege, making the eventual decline more abrupt and painful.

Chapter 5: Stages 1–4 (Private Crisis to Debt Selling)

Stage 1: Deep in Debt

Large and growing stock of debt from chronic deficit spending. A rising share goes to consumption and social safety nets; a declining share goes to productivity-enhancing investment. Countries become reliant on large social safety nets, making cuts a political third rail. In 31 of 35 cases, large persistent government deficits preceded the crisis.

Public balance sheets often look better than reality. Heavy private borrowing that the public sector must implicitly back up—banks the government can’t afford to let fail—creates hidden liabilities that only become visible in a crisis.

Stage 2: Private Crisis, Government Steps In

Government can borrow more easily than the private sector (the central bank can print its currency, the government has power to tax, and reserve currency status gives additional borrowing capacity). In 15 of 21 cases, government debt soared while private debt plunged roughly one year before the crisis—a reliable short leading indicator of trouble ahead.

Stage 3: Government Debt Squeeze

In 25 of 35 cases, debt service as percentage of revenues accelerated going into crisis. When debt service hits ~100% of income: spending squeezed AND rollovers may not happen.

Stage 4: Selling Cascade

Investors flee the country’s assets, bringing a run on currency and debt. Central bank defends with tightening and reserve sales. Ultimately forced to change course.

Central banks typically spend over half their reserves before giving up after ~2 years. During defense: gold rallies 42% median. After defense abandoned: gold rallies another 51%.

Central banks typically spend over half their reserves defending the currency before giving up after roughly two years. Pre-intervention reserves average ~5% of GDP, covering ~10% of the money supply and ~11% of government debt. The defense fails when having no reserves becomes scarier than letting the currency fall.

19 capital-flight behaviors: corporations keep revenue offshore and increase hedging, domestic banks become forced sellers as liquidity dries up, international banks close lines of business and sell subsidiaries, reserve allocators shift new reserves to different currencies, domestic savers convert deposits to hard currency and buy real goods ahead of inflation, high-net-worth individuals move money abroad and open foreign accounts, and speculators engage in self-reinforcing bond vigilante action.

Chapter 6: Stages 5–6 (Crisis at the Central Bank)

Stage 5: Central Bank Prints

The central bank doesn’t literally print money—it borrows reserves from commercial banks at very short-term rates, then uses those reserves to buy government bonds. The government’s financing problem doesn’t disappear; the balance sheet hit is simply transferred from the government to the central bank and, ultimately, to currency holders through depreciation.

Central banks take the same risks as commercial banks—they just have the unique ability to print money to monetize their debts and account for losses in less apparent ways. The trend toward greater central bank bond holdings and shortening of debt maturities typically starts nearly a decade before the actual crisis.

Stage 6: Central Bank Loses Money

The interest rate the central bank pays on its liabilities (deposits from commercial banks) exceeds the return on its assets (bonds it holds). This is a notable red flag, but not critical until significant negative net worth emerges. When the central bank is forced to print more money to cover its own negative cash flow, it enters the death spiral—printing to cover losses devalues the currency further, which increases losses, which requires more printing.

The central bank is caught in an impossible position: it must maintain easy enough policy to support the weak economy, but tight enough policy to discourage capital flight. This impossible dual mandate is the hallmark of an unsustainable situation.

Historical cases with large central bank losses include Argentina (cash flow losses of 3.3–3.5% of GDP), Peru (2.6% of GDP), and Turkey (2.6% of GDP with negative net reserves). The US Fed’s mark-to-market losses as of writing: ~$700 billion (2.5% of GDP)—relatively minor compared to historical crises, but a red flag nonetheless.

Chapter 7: Stages 7–9 (Resolution)

Stage 7: Devaluation and Restructuring

Currency devalues and debts are restructured. Remaining holders take large real losses until a new monetary system with credibility is established. A “beautiful deleveraging” occurs when deflationary restructurings and inflationary monetization are balanced properly—producing positive growth with falling debt burdens and acceptable inflation. Most big debt crises with debts in the country’s own currency are restructured in 1–3 years.

Gold outperforms local currency cash by ~60% from devaluation start to bottom. The data across all 35 cases is remarkably consistent:

Stage 8: Extraordinary Policies

Marginal income and inheritance tax rates rise ~10% in the years leading to devaluation. Capital controls have been imposed across all major countries historically: the UK (4 periods since 1900), US (2 periods), China (3), Germany (4), Russia (6), Japan (2). Other extraordinary measures include freezing payments, seizing assets of adversary nations, confiscatory taxes, defaulting on or extending debt maturities, and changing the very definition of what money is.

These measures were not limited to authoritarian regimes. Roosevelt confiscated private gold in 1933 and devalued the dollar by 40%. Nixon unilaterally closed the gold window. Similar radical changes are well within the range of what democracies do under pressure.

Stage 9: Return to Equilibrium

Five classic steps: restructure debts, deep fiscal adjustment, obtain reserves, impose high real rates, limit central bank actions. Inflation (−38%) and growth (−26%) do the heavy lifting of debt reduction; interest payments (+76%) work massively against it.

Chapter 8: The Overall Big Cycle

“If I had to pick the most important chapter in the book, this would be it.”

The Big Debt Cycle does not operate in isolation. It interacts with four other major forces—internal political order, external geopolitical order, acts of nature, and technology—to produce seismic shifts roughly once per lifetime. “Orders” (monetary, political, geopolitical) are ways of operating that change when systems break down, usually in a big crisis. These changes happen in highly mechanistic ways that can be measured and monitored.

Where We Are

We are 80 years into the Overall Big Cycle that began at the end of WWII—roughly 90–95% through. Like a human life cycle, the Overall Big Cycle progresses through knowable stages. We are in late Stage 5: high debt ratios, high internal conflict, high external conflict. This stage precedes radical changes that “haven’t happened in one’s lifetime but have happened many times throughout history.” The current pattern is comparable to 1905–14 and 1933–38.

Internal Order/Disorder

Democracy fails when: large wealth/values gaps, bad/worsening conditions, weak leadership. Plato described the demagogue pattern 2,400 years ago. Historical transitions: Caesar, Napoleon, Mussolini, Hitler, Franco, Erdogan. Takes 3–5 years within democracy’s rules, then becomes increasingly extreme.

“In times of disorder, financial, political, and military power matters more than laws, and authoritarianism works better than weak, disorganized collectivism.”

International Order/Disorder

Shift from multilateralism (which only worked after wars when a dominant power enforced rules) to unilateralism. The post-WWII American order is fading. Alliances change fast when winning matters more than loyalties.

Acts of Nature

Throughout history, killed more people than wars. Droughts, floods, pandemics increasing and increasingly costly. Nearly all nations too indebted to prepare.

Technology

AI is the most powerful productive force in human history. But new technologies are double-edged swords—advancing harm as well as good.

“The biggest, most important force is how people deal with each other.”

Part III: Looking Back

Chapter 9: 1865 to 1945

Post-Civil War

The Civil War produced the first US example of the Big Debt Cycle in action. Government debt rose from 2% to 40% of GDP. Interest consumed over half the federal budget. The government effectively defaulted on gold conversion, printed “greenbacks” (unbacked paper currency), gold soared to ~$250/oz, and inflation hit 80%.

The devaluation and restructuring reduced debt burdens, enabling the Second Industrial Revolution (1870–1914): railroads, steel, electricity, telephone, oil, automobile. This was the classic Gilded Age—robber barons accumulated vast wealth while workers labored in brutal conditions. The wealth gap produced populist movements including William Jennings Bryan’s “cross of gold” campaign.

No Central Bank

With the dollar fixed to gold and no central bank, busts were devastating and long-lasting. The Panic of 1873 triggered the Long Depression lasting until 1896. The Panics of 1893 and 1907 further demonstrated the system’s fragility. The Federal Reserve was created in 1913 specifically to lend money during big debt crises—but this solved one problem while creating another, as central banks inevitably overprint.

WWI and Consequences

The US entered WWI late, financed the allied war effort, and manufactured goods for both sides—emerging as the world’s richest creditor nation. Germany was left terribly indebted under the Treaty of Versailles. Russia’s war costs brought revolution. The 1918 pandemic killed millions, adding nature’s force to the cycle.

Roaring ’20s to WWII

The 1920s saw a classic debt-financed technology bubble: mass production of cars, planes, and radios, combined with easy credit and speculation. The 1929 crash burst the bubble and led to the Great Depression—the defining Big Debt Cycle crisis of the 20th century.

Roosevelt’s response followed the template exactly: outlawed private gold ownership, changed the official rate from $20.67 to $35/oz (devaluing the dollar by ~40%), and imposed strict foreign exchange controls. The political consequences were equally predictable—hard right vs. hard left produced autocracies in Spain, Italy, Japan, and Germany. Classic pre-war maneuvers of economic warfare, alliance building, and territorial expansion led to WWII.

The pattern is clear: debt crisis → political extremism → conflict. Every element of today’s situation has a direct parallel in the 1930s.

Chapters 10–11: Bretton Woods (1945–1971)

MP0: Dollar Pegged to Gold

After WWII, the US held approximately two-thirds of the world’s gold reserves. The dollar became the world’s reserve currency under the Bretton Woods agreement, with other currencies pegged to it at fixed exchange rates. Citizens couldn’t own gold—governments deliberately channeled savings into debt assets.

The system had an inherent, fatal flaw: it has never worked long-term because governments invariably create more paper-money claims than their gold reserves can honor. The temptation to spend beyond gold-backed limits is irresistible.

27 Years of Overspending

From 1945 to 1971, the US went through five short-term debt cycles within the Bretton Woods framework. The Vietnam War combined with Johnson’s “war on poverty” created far more paper-money claims than gold reserves could support. The early 1960s expansion continued until inflation hit 3.8% (1965–66). The Fed tightened in response, inverting the yield curve for the first time since 1929.

Charles de Gaulle called for monetary reform in 1965 and began converting France’s dollar reserves into gold, accelerating the run. By 1968, the inflation-adjusted S&P hit a price that would remain its peak for the next 25 years—a measure of just how much the subsequent devaluation destroyed.

Dalio’s Cuban Missile Crisis Lesson

The 1962 crisis brought the US and Soviet Union to the brink of nuclear war. Dalio (age 13) expected a massive market crash. The stock market barely reacted. Both sides pulled missiles (US from Turkey, USSR from Cuba) and claimed victory without revealing concessions.

Key investment lesson: what matters to markets is money from investments, not political headlines. Big political events don’t matter until they affect cash flows.

China-US Rapprochement

The China-Soviet split in the 1960s created the “enemy of my enemy is my friend” dynamic. Kissinger’s secret visit (1971) and Nixon’s 1972 visit brought China and the US together against the common Soviet threat.

Nixon Closes the Gold Window

August 15, 1971. Sunday night television announcement. Dalio, a young clerk on the NYSE floor, expected a market crash. Instead, the following Monday was the best market day that year—stocks rose more than 3%. The devaluation made debt easier to service and assets more valuable in nominal terms.

Puzzled, Dalio studied history and discovered that Roosevelt had done the exact same thing in 1933, for the exact same reasons, with the exact same market effect. This was his pivotal lesson: debt crises follow a template, and understanding that template is worth more than any prediction. It set the course for his entire career as a global macro investor.

Chapters 12–13: Fiat Money and QE (1971–2020)

1970s Stagflation

Freed from the gold restraint, fiat money (MP1) produced the classic stagflation of the 1970s. The Fed increased the monetary base by 100%, M2 rose 180%, and CPI surged ~140%. Stocks rose only ~30% nominally—meaning they fell 45% in real (inflation-adjusted) terms. Gold, by contrast, rose 10x. Real interest rates fell as low as −4%.

Labor’s share of revenue rose from 68% (1965) to the US historical high of 74% (1980)—a direct consequence of easy money policies that benefited borrower-debtors (including workers) at the expense of lender-creditors. The 1973 oil shock added geopolitical disruption, demonstrating how acts of nature and geopolitical conflict amplify the debt cycle.

Volcker Tightening and Reagan Revolution

Paul Volcker applied the tightest monetary policy “since the birth of Jesus Christ,” raising rates high enough to break inflation’s back. Reagan, Thatcher, and Kohl swung hard right politically—cutting labor’s share of revenue, reducing inflation, raising corporate profits, and cutting taxes. Markets loved the new environment: falling rates, falling inflation, and big stock and bond gains.

The entire environment shifted from benefiting borrower-debtors (the 1970s) to benefiting lender-creditors (the 1980s onward). This shift created the foundation for the 40-year decline in interest rates from 1981 to 2020—the longest sustained leveraging-up phase in US history.

Emerging markets with dollar-denominated debt suffered classic inflationary depressions as the strong dollar crushed their ability to service foreign debts—the “lost decade” for Latin America and other developing economies.

Dalio’s 1982 Mistake

In 1982, Dalio publicly predicted a US debt crisis. He was wrong—he failed to anticipate how forcefully capital would flow from emerging markets into American markets as the dollar strengthened. Emerging markets with dollar-denominated debt suffered classic inflationary depressions (the “lost decade”), but their pain was America’s gain.

The experience taught painful lessons about capital flows, diversification, and humility. It also led to fundamental improvements in Bridgewater’s investment process, including the development of risk parity and systematic diversification approaches that would define the firm’s later success.

1990s to 2008

The Soviet collapse and Deng’s economic reforms unleashed massive new productive capacity. The dot-com bubble (1995–2000) saw the Nasdaq fall ~80% when it burst. Clinton achieved a budget surplus—the most successful US fiscal adjustment, cutting the deficit by 5% of GDP. The 2006–07 real estate and mortgage bubble led to the 2008 crisis.

In 27 years from peak rates (1981) to zero (2008), the US went through four short-term cycles in one long leveraging-up phase. When rates finally hit zero, MP1 was exhausted. The Fed turned to MP2 (QE)—the first time since Roosevelt in 1933 that the central bank had to resort to outright money printing and bond buying.

Dalio had anticipated the 2008 crisis by calculating that financial institutions were hitting regulatory leverage limits—the bust was mechanical, not psychological. He demonstrated this to policymakers before both the US and European crises.

The Real Bond Yield

Dalio identifies the real bond yield (~2% over 100 years) as the single most important number in the financial world. It governs all capital markets. Periods far from 2% indicate excessively cheap or expensive credit.

He worked with Larry Summers at US Treasury on TIPS design. Invested in non-US inflation-indexed bonds when no US market existed. Bridgewater became the world’s largest global inflation-indexed bond manager. Believes these markets remain underappreciated as indicators and storeholds of wealth.

The Yield Curve

The relationship between short-term and long-term rates is critical. Higher rates are normally required for longer-term debt (reward for lending longer). When the central bank wants to slow growth, it raises short rates relative to long rates—inverted yield curve. When it wants to stimulate, the opposite—positive yield curve.

High real yields + flat/inverted curve = tight money, good for lender-creditors, bad for borrower-debtors. Low real yields + positive curve = easy money, good for borrower-debtors, bad for lender-creditors. Extreme shifts cause big, volatile swings.

Who Holds US Government Debt

Lot from the central bank, lot from commercial banks, roughly a third from foreign investors. Some worried the US won’t pay back; becoming sellers. Hard to imagine big buyers will keep buying if: (a) US handles debt irresponsibly, (b) threatens sanctions, (c) returns are bad, (d) loses prominence.

US debt service is currently ~100% of government revenue, projected ~150% in 15 years. Government debt at highest level since 1946, projected much higher. The most important dynamic: government tends to acquire more debt when private sector is acquiring less.

Chapter 14: Pandemic and MP3 (2020–present)

COVID and Monetary Policy 3

COVID triggered MP3: coordinated fiscal-monetary stimulus. Tests the “sacrosanct rule” of central bank independence—but nearly every sacrosanct rule breaks down late in the Big Cycle.

MP3 arose because MP1 (interest rate cuts) and MP2 (QE/bond buying) couldn’t get money to those who needed it most. QE benefited asset owners, widening inequality. The capitalist system provides capital to the well-off, not to those who have least. MP3 bypasses this by putting money directly in people’s hands—stimulus checks, enhanced unemployment, PPP loans.

The Bubble

The pandemic response produced the fourth and largest debt monetization since 2008. Money and credit became essentially free. US household “paper wealth” tripled from $32 trillion to $99 trillion. Dalio’s bubble indicator surged from 18% (2010) to 75% (late 2020)—analogous to the Nifty Fifty, the Japan bubble, and the dot-com bubble.

A key development during this period: the wealth transfer from the public sector to the private sector. Government and central bank balance sheets deteriorated so that household and corporate balance sheets could improve. This made the private sector safer even as sovereign risk increased—an important nuance for understanding why the economy didn’t collapse despite enormous debt accumulation.

Tightening and Aftermath

Inflation surged from debt/credit surge + supply chains + conflicts. Fed tightened: nominal yields 0.5% to >4%, real yields −1.1% to ~2.5%. Bubble stocks fell average 75%. Nominal wealth down ~12%, real wealth down ~18%—largest since 2009.

Didn’t weaken economy as much as typical because central government carried the debt rather than private sector. AI and AI companies became the new hot things.

Political Consequences

Inflation + Biden’s impaired acuity = Trump’s decisive 2024 victory with mandate for radical renovation. A close Trump loss would have triggered major domestic conflict. Five forces converging: government debt, civil conflict, US-China confrontation, climate, AI.

The 2008 crisis had produced populism of the right (Tea Party) and left (Occupy Wall Street). Rise of populism especially among non-college-educated, non-urban white population led to Trump’s 2016 election. His first term shifted domestic, international, economic, political, and geopolitical orders to be “more aggressive, top-down/autocratic, rightist, nationalistic, protectionist, and militaristic.”

Greece as Case Study

Greece illustrates how the template plays out for a country that cannot print its own currency. After joining the euro, capital flowed in because Greek assets seemed safer, total debt rose ~90% of GDP. The 2008 crisis hit, government stimulus worsened deficits, and late 2009 revealed substantial underreporting of debt.

Massive sell-off followed. Short-term rates spiked to over 70% by 2012. Austerity deepened the depression (“ugly deleveraging”). Debt rose another ~70% of GDP during the austerity period. The ECB stepped in with huge money printing, politically toxic as stable countries decried the bailout.

The 2012 Troika (ECB, IMF, European Commission) restructured debts by ~50% of GDP. Capital controls imposed in 2015; bank runs as citizens pulled money; new taxes. Greece stayed in the euro—more painful and drawn out than if it could have printed its own currency.

Chapter 15: China’s Big Cycle

Century of Humiliation to Deng

The 110-year Century of Humiliation (1839–1949) is profoundly embedded in Chinese leaders’ psyche. The lesson burned into collective memory: China must have economic and military power to protect its people, or it will be exploited by foreign powers. This isn’t abstract ideology—it shapes every policy decision.

The Mao era imposed rigid Marxist-Leninist dogmas that caused enormous suffering through the Great Leap Forward and Cultural Revolution. But the China-Soviet split in the 1960s created the “enemy of my enemy is my friend” dynamic that brought the US and China together against the common Soviet threat (Kissinger’s secret visit 1971, Nixon’s visit 1972).

Deng’s Transformation (1978)

Deng Xiaoping introduced freer, market-based economics with characteristic pragmatism: “To get rich is glorious.” “It doesn’t matter whether a cat is black or white as long as it catches mice.” He abandoned Mao’s ideological rigidity in favor of whatever actually worked.

The results since 1984 are staggering: per capita income increased 20-fold, life expectancy rose 12 years, and poverty fell from 81% to under 1%. By many measures, these are the greatest economic accomplishments in human history—lifting hundreds of millions out of poverty in a single generation.

Xi Jinping Era (2012–present)

Xi’s first term emphasized openness, reform, and anti-corruption. But by October 2022, leadership had shifted from reform-minded globalists to loyal patriotic communists. The approach became more Mao-like as internal and external conflicts intensified.

China’s total debt exceeds 300% of GDP—with non-financial businesses and local government financing vehicles alone exceeding 150% of GDP. However, the debt is in local currency with predominantly domestic creditors, giving the government significant control. Nominal bond rates are approaching zero. The yield curve inverted in late February 2025—a classic sign of “pushing on a string,” where monetary stimulus struggles to generate real economic activity.

China went through six classic stages: unproductive communist economy → market economy → capital markets development → debt bubble → debt bust (2021–present) → great power conflict. The Big Cycle pattern is consistent across all of China’s political systems.

China’s Cultural Roots

Understanding Chinese policy requires understanding deep cultural roots:

  • Da tong (big unity/harmony) — ancient Confucian principle: good things shared by all, leadership for public good
  • Confucianism (~500 BCE) — harmony through clear hierarchy, moral leadership, education, meritocracy
  • Legalism (~250 BCE) — strict rule of law and pragmatism over morality; running China as hierarchical empire
  • Chinese word “country” = two characters: “state” + “family”

These roots explain the leadership’s natural inclination toward Marxism, common prosperity, and autocratic leadership. The Big Cycle boom-bust pattern is consistent across all of China’s political systems.

Taiwan and Great Power Conflict

Taiwan critical for advanced chips—more important than oil was in previous cycle. China views America’s Taiwan handling as more intrusive than Americans saw Russia’s Cuba influence in the 1960s.

A very senior Chinese leader explained that Western countries follow the “Mediterranean approach” (head-on confrontation) while the Chinese use the much subtler, deceptive approach of Sun Tzu’s Art of War (~2,500 years ago).

Under-the-surface attacks “have been vicious.” Emerging countries (85% of world population) are China’s big new target market. The squaring-off of two great powers with allies lining up looks like the 1930s. Trump described Xi as a “great leader” who “controls 1.4 billion people with an iron fist.”

Chapter 16: The Japanese Case

What NOT to Do

After the 1989–90 bubble burst, Japan committed three critical errors:

  1. Did not restructure debts for 9 years—“zombie institutions” lingered on balance sheets
  2. Held rigid employment/cost policies preventing adaptation
  3. Did not drive rates below growth/inflation for 23 years

Result: continuous deflation and stagnation until mid-2013. Demographics made it worse: 1990 had 12% over 65 and 69% working-age; now 29% over 65 and 59% working-age.

The Three Arrows (2013)

After 23 years of stagnation, Kuroda and Abe finally began appropriate policy with “Abenomics”—three arrows: dramatically increase the money supply, boost government spending, and enact structural reforms. The BoJ bought massive amounts of government bonds, now holding bonds worth more than 90% of GDP. Rates were pushed 0.9% below growth and 1% below inflation. The yen depreciated substantially.

The results demonstrate both the power and the cost of monetary policy: the debt service burden actually fell even as total debt rose, because the BoJ absorbed the interest costs. But the yen’s decline devastated the purchasing power of Japanese savers and workers in international terms.

The Devastating Price Data

In yen: Non-tradable goods (wages, services, housing) essentially flat. Tradable commodities surged: soybeans +234%, wheat +210%, oil +331%, coal +830%.

In dollars: Aggregate CPI −49%, housing −53%, services −52%. The yen collapsed faster than commodities rose.

The 3% Rate Rise Scenario

If real rates rose 3%: BoJ mark-to-market losses ~30% of GDP. Government deficit widens from ~4% to ~8%. Debt rises from 220% to 300% over 20 years. Combined cash flow need: 5–6% of GDP per year.

The Five Dynamics of Japan’s Resolution

  1. Government deficit spending floods private sector with cash, aids private deleveraging—left Japan with highest government debt of any major country
  2. Central bank monetizes debt—keeps long rates low; government debt minus CB holdings falls as % GDP
  3. Currency depreciation acts as implicit tax on bondholders—yen bond holders lost over 50% in dollar and real terms, comparable to a default
  4. Domestic savers taxed to lesser degree—weak economy kept currency declines from translating into much domestic inflation
  5. Country becomes more competitive—everything became much cheaper in dollars, attracting FDI
“The principle should resonate: don’t own government bonds when there are extreme amounts of debt monetization.”

Lessons from Japan for the World

Japan’s experience carries universal lessons. The debt reduction happened not through fiscal responsibility or restructuring, but through currency depreciation—an implicit tax on bondholders equivalent to a default. The country became much cheaper in dollar terms, attracting FDI. Japan went from overvalued to inexpensive relative to the US by P/E measures.

Remarkably, Japan experienced no major internal or external conflicts during this entire 35-year ordeal. But that era of peace is ending: Japan is now preparing for potential conflict with China as the US’s most important regional ally, adding military spending pressures to an already unsustainable fiscal position.

Chapter 18: The 3% 3-Part Solution

The Fed is more powerful than Congress for deficit trajectory. Fiscal tightening (negative for growth) combined with monetary easing (positive for growth) can offset each other while both reducing debt.

Phase changes over three years. $9T less debt in 10 years, $26T in 20 years. Clinton-era precedent proves feasibility. Current conditions match the painless adjustment profile.

If the 2017 Trump tax cuts are extended as promised, the deficit grows by an additional 1.5% of GDP, raising the required adjustment from ~3% to over 4% of GDP.

Timing is critical: it is better to reduce deficits in good times than wait for a crisis in bad times. The US is currently near full employment, growth is moderately strong, inflation slightly elevated, and private sector balance sheets are strong. If not now, the debt problem grows through self-reinforcing compound dynamics.

Dalio also favors complementary approaches: marketing government assets, creating a US sovereign wealth fund, and exploring a US-backed stablecoin. These could provide additional fiscal flexibility beyond the core spending/tax/rate adjustments.

Chapter 19: What the Future Looks Like

Investment Philosophy

“He who lives by the crystal ball is destined to eat ground glass.”

Success comes from managing uncertainty, not predicting with certainty. The approach: look for big, unsustainable conditions and bet they won’t be sustained. Build understanding into templates and models, then use computerized expert systems to apply them systematically. Everything except the quantum world is predestined—AI will bring us closer to modeling these dynamics accurately.

The Holy Grail of Investing: assemble 15+ uncorrelated great bets. You’ll be wrong ~33% of the time but never exactly right. Because it takes only one really bad bet or a series of moderately bad bets to knock you out of the game, extreme risk aversion is essential. The best way to navigate the years ahead: play probabilities, diversify well, and stick with sound fundamentals.

Probabilities

Dalio assigns a 65% probability of major debt restructuring or monetization within the next 5 years, rising to 80% within 10 years. He writes at what he calls a “maximum point of uncertainty” (early March 2025, with Trump only 40 days in office).

Trump is “more inclined to do previously unimaginable things” than any president in the last 80 years—perhaps ever. His policies of shifting to a confrontational, unilateral approach are remarkably similar to hard-right countries in the 1930s. Countries at this stage of the Big Cycle have a strong tendency toward populist, nationalistic, protectionist, militaristic, and autocratic leadership.

The Short-Term Cycle Position

The US and major economies are approximately five years into the 13th short-term debt/economic cycle since 1945—about two-thirds through (typical cycle: 6 years total). Monetary tightening began March 2022. The Fed and G7 central banks are now in relatively neutral monetary policy, with nominal and real rates appearing roughly right—though not high enough given the fiscal supply-and-demand dynamic.

There is significant risk that both a debt squeeze and an economic downturn happen simultaneously 2–3 years from now. At that point, the policy options will be severely constrained.

The Current Meme

American exceptionalism + AI + Trump fixing inefficiencies. Has merit, but “more than reflected in prices.” US’s exceptional characteristics (innovation, capital markets, legal system) are real, but producing enormous gaps impossible to rectify.

Bottom 60%: less than sixth-grade reading level on average, receive more assistance than they pay in taxes. Top 1% (~3 million people): tremendously rewarded. An abnormally large number of unicorn companies are producing billionaires quickly. Trump’s policies aim to shift money, power, and freedom to the most productive.

Domestic Politics

Polarization highest since 1900. Party-line voting at historical peak. Global phenomenon: France 65%→74%, Germany 56%→68%, Canada 44%→66%, UK 52%→65%.

“When democracies fail, autocracies come in.”

Fights expected between executive vs. judicial branch, federal vs. state/local. In limits-of-power testing, judicial branch will lose—executive controls DOJ, army, National Guard; judiciary controls no enforcement powers. Dalio expects president will win most power struggles.

CEO or Demagogue?

Strong CEO and demagogue approaches can be indistinguishable—both take control and make radical changes. The difference: a CEO has a board; a US president has none. Policies remarkably like hard-right countries in the 1930s. Power-maximizing analogous to Andrew Jackson (right) and FDR (left), though even more aggressive.

The Deep US Problem

Deep and pervasive rot in education, family, and social systems—multigenerational, nearly impossible to fix. The top 1% and machines are making revolutionary changes. The next 9% help. The top 10% is doing great. The next 30% is so-so. The bottom 60% is doing terribly.

Negative consequences of radical cost-cutting: many hurt by cuts will fight back and valuable support systems will be eliminated. Dalio’s wife works with the poorest students—school lunch programs being eliminated will have terrible second-order consequences.

International Order

The shift from cooperative, multilateral world order to confrontational, unilateral self-interest through bold use of financial, political, and military power is the defining geopolitical transition of this era. Classic Stage 5: regional wars going on locally (Russia-Ukraine, Israel-Iran proxies) but not yet violent between leading global powers.

At this stage, the strong increasingly prey on the weak. Europe is identified as weak and potentially easy prey—Trump, Putin, and European leaders all know it. Russia is Europe’s likely adversary, while Trump’s “America First” likely means the US won’t defend it.

“At this stage in the Big Cycle, alliances often change fast as circumstances change quickly and winning is more important than loyalties.”

Alliances expected to change in “previously unimaginable ways.” Trump-Putin alignment possible. Europe-China alignment possible. Germany and Russia switched from allies to enemies during WWII—precedent for rapid alliance shifts.

US-China

The US is in relative decline while conflicts are increasing—the classic Thucydides Trap dynamic where a rising power threatens an established one. However, Dalio does not expect military war—both sides understand mutually assured destruction. Only a real threat to Chinese sovereignty (including Taiwan) would trigger actual fighting. Dalio could imagine Trump willing to negotiate Taiwan away under the right terms for the right price. Both strongmen running great powers will have regular direct conversations.

The under-the-surface attacks, however, “have been vicious.” The squaring-off of two great powers with allies lining up looks like the 1930s.

The only way to win a great power conflict: secretly build technology of overwhelming power that can be deployed without triggering intolerable retaliation. Historical precedent: the atomic bomb leading to Hiroshima and Nagasaki. Dalio is sure both countries are working on “mind-blowingly powerful secret technologies.”

US-China Technology Competition

The US leads in advanced chips but is weak in production. China is close behind in development, ahead in inexpensive chips, and deploying AI aggressively. China is home to 20 of the 40 best computer science programs in the world. Other critical domains: quantum computing, gene editing/biotech, robotics, and space.

Intellectual property protections don’t work. You should assume most good ideas openly shown will be replicated in about six months. This makes the technology race a matter of speed rather than legal protection.

“Because these technologies will impact almost everything, there will be exceptionally big differences between the performance levels of countries, investors, and companies who use them well. Those who know how to use these tools effectively will be rewarded, and those who fail to do so will be penalized.”

Technology Won’t Overcome the Headwinds

AI is estimated at 150% of the last 30 years’ tech impact—the most powerful productivity revolution in human history. But back-of-the-envelope calculations show this is insufficient to negate the combined headwinds of debt, internal/external conflict, climate change, and demographics.

This has happened before. During the Industrial Revolutions and the Roaring ’20s, productivity-improving technologies were squelched when the other Big Cycle forces turned negative. The technology force, however powerful, cannot overcome all four other forces moving against it simultaneously.

Acts of Nature

Throughout history, acts of nature have killed more people than wars and toppled more orders than all the other four forces combined. Droughts, floods, pandemics, rising sea levels, and health problems are increasing in frequency and becoming increasingly costly.

Nearly all nations are too heavily indebted to proactively prevent or prepare for these shocks. Global South countries without resources face domestic conflict, driving emigration that strains receiving countries and makes politics more unstable everywhere.

Where to Be

Rising neutral powers at take-off points: India, ASEAN (Singapore, Indonesia, Vietnam), UAE, Saudi Arabia. The US-China conflict is driving capital, businesses, and talent to geopolitically neutral locations with strong fundamentals.

Best places: countries that educate well, earn more than they spend, have internal order, low war risk, and benefit from technology. Having great human capital will matter most. Globalization continues as an unstoppable force despite the rise of nationalism and protectionism—the volume of globalized deal-making is vastly greater than ever.

The next 5–10 years will bring enormous changes in all major orders. Going from now until then will feel like going through a time warp into a very different reality. Many countries, companies, and people now up will be down; those down will be up.

“If you’re not worried, you need to worry—and if you’re worried, you don’t need to worry.”

Worrying about things that can go wrong protects you; not worrying leaves you exposed. The book ends with a note of hope: it is still possible to cut deficits to 3% of GDP, greatly reducing crisis risks. A workable solution exists—the question is whether the political will exists to implement it before the window closes. Unfortunately, extreme factionalism dominates, with each side firmly believing the other is doing harm.

“The biggest, most important force is how people deal with each other. If people deal with their problems and opportunities together rather than fight each other, they can get the best possible results.”

How Countries Go Broke by Ray Dalio · 2025 · 396 pages

Dalio’s definitive study of the Big Debt Cycle—the roughly 80-year pattern by which nations accumulate unsustainable debt, exhaust conventional monetary tools, and ultimately restructure or devalue their obligations. Drawing on 50+ years as a global macro investor and 35 major debt crises, he argues America is in the late stages of its Big Debt Cycle and proposes a concrete “3% 3-Part Solution” to avert disaster, while examining China and Japan as cautionary cases.
