## Chapter 1: The Big Debt Cycle in a Tiny Nutshell

### How the Machine Works

**Credit is the primary vehicle for funding spending and can easily be created.** One person's spending = another's earnings. When lots of credit creation, people spend/earn more, most asset prices go up. Result: **central governments and central banks have bias toward creating lots of credit.** But credit creates debt that must be paid back — opposite effect.

The credit/spending/debt-paying-back dynamic is inherently cyclical.

### The Short-Term Debt Cycle (~6 years ±3 years)
- **Expansion**: money/credit readily available, interest rates low relative to inflation and return rates, borrowing/spending encouraged, asset prices/activity/inflation pick up
- **Contraction**: money/credit restrained, interest rates relatively high, less borrowing, lower asset prices, slowing economy, interest rates come down, cycle begins again

### Short-Term Cycles Add Up to Big, Long-Term Cycles
- Credit is a stimulant creating a high people want more of — **bias toward creating it**
- Short-term cyclical highs and lows in debt typically higher than the ones before
- **Early in Big Debt Cycle**: debt burdens lower, more potential for profitable lending, lingering cautiousness from memories of previous tight money
- **Late in Big Debt Cycle**: debts/debt service high relative to incomes, risks of default higher, lots of debt assets/liabilities relative to income, balancing act between creditors and debtors becomes challenging
- **Short-term cycles end** due to economic considerations; **long-term cycles end** because debt burdens too great to be sustained

> "Because it is more enjoyable to borrow and spend, if one isn't careful, debt and debt service can grow like a cancer, eating up one's buying power and squeezing out other consumption. This is what makes the long-term Big Debt Cycle."

### The Core Problem

**Creation of unsustainably large amounts of debt assets and debt liabilities relative to amounts of money, goods, services, and investment assets in existence.**

A debt is a promise to deliver money. A debt crisis occurs when there have been more promises made than there is money to deliver on them. Central bank forced to choose: (a) printing lot of money (devalues it) or (b) not printing (big default crisis). **In the end, the central bank always prints and devalues. But either way — default or devaluation — too much debt causes debt assets (e.g., bonds) to be worth less.**

### Currency Denomination Matters
- **Most important factor**: whether debt denominated in currency the central bank can "print" and whether it's a reserve currency
- Credit rating agencies only rate **default** risk — miss **devaluation** risk, giving misimpression all higher-rated debt is safe
- Better approach: rate riskiness of debt **losing value** through both default and devaluation
- For countries with debts in own currencies: rate central banks' debts by considering risk of money devaluation (as probable as, if not more than, default on government debt)

**Default or devaluation, I don't care. What I care about is losing my storehold of wealth, which inevitably will happen one way or another.**

### The Five Stages of the Big Debt Cycle

The cycle progresses like a disease through identifiable stages:
1. **Private sector** borrows healthily, then overborrows and has payment problems
2. **Government sector** tries to help, then overborrows and has payment problems
3. **Central bank** tries to help by "printing money" and buying government debt, then has problems paying it back
4. Central bank monetizes more debt if denominated in currency it can print

### Stage 1: The Sound Money Stage
- Net debt levels low, money is sound, country competitive, debt growth fuels productivity growth
- Money is "hard" — gold, silver, **more recently Bitcoin** — medium of exchange that is storehold of wealth, limited in supply
- Cycle = building up "paper money" and debt relative to "hard money" and real assets — works like a **Ponzi scheme or musical chairs**
- US 1945: ratios of government debt and money supply to gold were 7x and 1.3x; now 37x and 6x
- Late 1940s-early 1950s: stock earnings yields roughly **4x bond yields** (risky assets cheap due to memories of prior damage)

### Stage 2: The Debt Bubble Stage
- Money readily available and cheap, debt-financed boom, sentiment very bullish
- Investors invest **without ability or care to assess present value of future cash flows**
- "Wealth created out of nothing" — greater imagined wealth versus actual existing wealth

> "There is always a current most popular meme that just about everyone believes. It is reflected in the price and is bound to be wrong in some way. These memes typically are due to a mix of extrapolating what happened before and emotional considerations."

- **Bubble identifiable by**: 3+ years debt growth significantly faster than income growth, high asset prices relative to traditional present value measures, and many other factors measured in Dalio's bubble indicator
- Eventually reaches **point of no return** — self-reinforcing debt "death spiral" when borrowing to service debt while interest rates rising

### Stage 3: The Top Stage
- Bubble pops due to **tightening of money** and unsustainable prior debt growth rate
- Self-reinforcing contraction begins — debt problems **spread very quickly, like an aggressive cancer**
- Very important for policy makers to deal with it quickly
- In most cases, temporarily reversed by more credit/debt — continues until can't anymore

### Stage 4: The Deleveraging Stage
- Debt problems spread from **private sector to central government to central bank**

> "Net selling of debt assets, especially net selling of government debt assets, is a big red flag."

- Selling takes form of "runs on banks" — turning-in of debt assets to get real money, which banks don't have enough of
- Death spiral: holders sell debt → interest rates up → debt harder to service → more risky → rates higher → central bank provides money → reduces value of money/credit
- **Spending cuts paradox**: because one person's spending = another's income, cutting spending typically only increases debt-to-income ratios
- Typically shifted to mix of **debt restructurings and debt monetizations**, with mix dependent on how much debt denominated in country's currency

### Stage 5: The Deleveraging Process
- When interest rates approach 0%, central bank switches to new "tools" — printing money and buying government/private debt
- **"Pushing on a string"** (phrase coined 1930s): central bankers struggle to convert stimulative policies into spending because people/investors hoard low-risk cash even at 0% interest
- Central banks must choose between **"hard" money** (deflationary depressions) or **"soft" money** (printing/devaluation) — **always eventually choose to print and devalue**
- If currency linked to gold/silver, that link typically broken → **move to fiat monetary system**
- Important to **watch real and nominal interest rates and supply/demand for debt**
- **Extraordinary policies** common: extraordinary taxes, capital controls, freezing/seizing assets, creating new forms of money

### Beautiful Deleveraging
- In typical deleveraging, debt-to-income ratio must fall by roughly **50%, give or take about 20%**
- **"Beautiful deleveraging"**: simultaneous debt restructurings (deflationary/depressing) and monetary stimulations (inflationary/stimulating) in balanced way
- If balance right: positive growth with falling debt burdens and acceptable inflation
- In countries studied, most big debt crises with debts in own currency restructured quickly, typically in **1-3 years**

### Stage 6: New Equilibrium
- Requirements for viable system: (a) debt/money sound enough as storehold of wealth, (b) debt service in line with incomes, (c) creditors and debtors believe conditions will persist, (d) real interest rates align with needs of both parties
- Requires **credibility-restoring actions**: government earning more than spending, central bank offering high real yields, raising reserves, possibly linking currency to something hard
- **Interest rates need to be relatively high** in relation to inflation — pays to be lender, costly to be borrower
- Can be **very attractive for lender-creditors**

### Concluding Observations

> "Big debt crises are inevitable."

> "It pays to build up savings in the good times so there are savings to draw on in the bad times."

> "The best way to anticipate a debt crisis happening is not by focusing on a single influence or number like debt as a percent of GDP; it is by understanding and focusing on a number of interrelated dynamics."

> "If debts are denominated in a country's own currency, its central bank can and will 'print' the money to alleviate the debt crisis."

> "All debt crises, even big ones, can be managed well by economic policy makers restructuring and monetizing the debt so that the deflationary ways of reducing the debt burdens and the inflationary ways of reducing debt burdens balance each other. The key is to spread the paying back over time."

- Example: if debt-to-income needs to fall ~50%, spreading at 3-4% per year much less traumatic than 50% in one year
- Japanese case: large debt/monetization led to currency/debt depreciation — bond holders lost **45% vs. US dollar debt since 2013** and **60% vs. gold since 2013**

## Chapter 2: The Mechanics in Words and Concepts

### Five Most Important Drivers of Change
1. The debt/credit/money/economic cycle
2. The internal political order/disorder cycle
3. The external geopolitical order/disorder cycle
4. Acts of nature
5. Human inventiveness/new technologies

**Money and credit are the lifeblood of the economy.** Central government is like the brain (taking 15-30% for functions); central bank is like the heart producing/pumping money and credit. If exchanges go well and capital used productively, all prosper; if not, system becomes ill.

### Price Determination Framework
- **Price = Total Spending ($) / Total Quantity Sold (Q)**
- Dalio tracks **dollar flows** rather than quantity bought (differs from conventional economics)
- Conventional view assumes constant price elasticity; Dalio's framework shows elasticity varies with market participant motivations
- Dalio discovered this approach in 1970s analyzing livestock, grain, oilseed prices; extended to financial assets and entire economies

> "By identifying rates of unsustainable buying and/or rates of unsustainable selling you can identify unsustainable prices and unsustainable economic conditions."

### Debt Dynamics vs. Conventional Psychology
- Most people mistakenly view debt busts/depressions as primarily psychological
- Dalio showed policymakers before 2008 US crisis and 2010-12 Europe crisis that debt buying would slow because financial institutions were hitting regulatory leverage limits
- Policy makers often say: "If we control budget deficits in out-years, investors will have confidence" — this is naive, fails to look at bond buyer motivations and calculate who will buy what amounts

### Money Creation and Economic Growth
- More money/credit + excess capacity = **non-inflationary growth** (both $ and Q increase)
- More money/credit + no capacity = **little real growth, much inflation**
- **High productivity growth allows non-inflationary growth to continue longer** — can show up as quality improvements or marginal costs falling to zero
- Early cycle (excess capacity): strong growth, little inflation; late cycle: weak growth, big price rises

### Why People Buy and Sell
- People buy goods/services to use and buy investments to make money (as storeholds of wealth)
- **All investment markets compete on basis of total returns** (yield + price change)
- **Arbitrages and relatively sure bets are most powerful determinants of relative pricing**
- Expected **real** returns (relative to inflation) influence how much money goes into each investment
- Real returns matter more than nominal because investments are storeholds of wealth (buying power matters)

### Debt Is Currency and Currency Is Debt
- Since debt asset = promise to receive currency at future date, **debt and currency are essentially the same thing**
- If you don't like the currency, you must not like the debt, and vice versa (taking yields into account)
- Gold vs. bonds example: gold has no yield; if Treasury yields 5%, illogical to buy gold unless price expected to rise >5%/year — market prices gold to rise 5% relative to Treasuries
- Government interest rate differences between countries drive currency expectations
- **Currency arbitrage**: if Country A rate is X% lower than B's, expected currency appreciation must match, otherwise virtually risk-free profits exist
- **Example**: 10-year rate in Country A is 3% lower than Country B's. If Country A's currency expected to fall 2%/year instead of rising: virtually risk-free profit opportunity. Investors flock to trade, selling lower-yielding debt/currency, producing one of two adjustments:
  1. Spot currency must fall by ~40%, or
  2. 10-year interest rate must rise by 5%, sending bond prices down ~40%
- If neither adjustment happens (capital controls, etc.): loss compounds to 5%/year, totaling 40% over 10 years
- **Country A's bond return will be very bad** — annual interest rate and currency weakness won't compensate for inflation

### The Five Major Players
1. **Borrower-debtors** (private/government)
2. **Lender-creditors** (private/government)
3. **Banks** (intermediaries — profit on spread between borrowing and lending rates)
4. **Central governments** (political organizations wanting to give people what they want, typically without paying for it)
5. **Central banks** (create money/credit, influence cost of money)

- **Debt/credit expansions only occur when both borrower-debtors and lender-creditors are willing** — deal must be good for both
- **What's good for one is often bad for the other**
- Banks create bubbles/crises by lending out more money than they have — works well when loans used productively and depositors don't demand more than banks have
- **Debt crises happen when**: loans aren't adequately paid back, OR banks' creditors want more money back than banks can give them

### Origins of Central Banks
- Until recently (e.g., 1913 in US), most countries didn't have central banks
- Money was typically physical gold/silver or paper certificates; boom/bust cycles occurred when too many debt assets/liabilities led to "runs"
- These runs produced debt/market/economic collapses → eventually led governments to create **central banks to lend money when big debt crises happened**
- Where do central banks get their money? They "print" it (physically and digitally) — alleviates debt problems but reduces buying power of money/debt assets and raises inflation
- **Central banks want to keep debt/economic growth and inflation at acceptable levels** — influence by raising/lowering interest rates and tightening/easing money availability

> "Over the long run, debts can't rise faster than the incomes needed to service them, and interest rates can't be too high for borrower-debtors or too low for lender-creditors for very long."

> "The greater the size of the debt assets and debt liabilities relative to the real incomes being produced, the more difficult is the balancing act... so the greater the likelihood of a debt-caused downturn in the markets and economy."

### The Predictable Cycle
- **Debt growth slow, inflation low** → central banks lower rates, create money/credit → good to be borrower, bad to be lender
- **Debt growth unsustainably fast, inflation high** → central banks raise rates, limit credit → good to be lender, bad to be borrower
- Short-term cycle: ~6 years ±3; long-term cycle: ~80 years ±25; both evolve around upward productivity trend
- As of March 2025: **12 complete cycles in US since 1945, about two-thirds through the 13th**

### What Separates Sustainable from Unsustainable
- Whether **debt creates sufficient income to pay for debt service**
- If incomes fail to grow as quickly as debt and debt service, ratio mechanically grows, requiring more borrowing
- **Sure sign of debt crisis**: large and rising borrowing used to pay for debt service
- Four reasons central bankers don't prevent dangerous debt levels:
  1. Everyone (including central bankers) wants markets/economy up — push limits until debts become so burdensome they must be restructured
  2. Not clear exactly what risky debt levels are because unclear what will happen to future incomes
  3. Opportunity costs and risks of not providing credit — too little credit can create problems as bad as too much
  4. Debt crises, even big ones, can usually be managed to reduce pain to acceptable levels
- **Debt isn't always bad, even when it's not economic** — credit can create great improvements that would have been forgone without it, and losses can be spread out if government controls restructuring and debt is in printable currency

> "Over time, from one cycle to the next, debt liabilities and debt assets have virtually always increased to produce the long-term debt cycle expansion. In virtually all cases, that has continued until the debt burdens have become unsustainably large or the debt assets have become intolerably low-returning."

### Central Banks' Difficult Choice
When lots of debt assets/liabilities relative to incomes, central bankers must choose:
1. **Not monetizing debt**: letting interest rates rise, making cash valuable, devaluing stocks/hard assets, causing deflation/defaults/depression — typically happens first, proves intolerable
2. **Monetizing debt**: printing money and buying debt, making money readily available, reducing its value, raising inflation, minimizing defaults, stimulating economy — typically happens eventually

### Four Levers to Reduce Debt Burdens
1. **Austerity** — doesn't work (one person's spending = another's income, cuts worsen debt-to-income ratios)
2. **Debt defaults/restructurings** — deflationary (one person's debts = another's assets)
3. **Central bank printing money and buying debt (monetizing)** — inflationary
4. **Transfers via taxes from those with more to those with less**

> "The easiest path, though not the best path for the long-term health of the system, is for governments to resolve their debt problems and spend as they would like to spend by having the central bank print money and purchase the bonds."

### The Big Debt Cycle Needs to Be Better Understood

> "Because really big debt crises that take form of debt restructurings and devaluations come at ends of Big Debt Cycles happen roughly once in a lifetime, they are not well-understood relative to short-term cycles."

- Most people don't know about or worry about long-term debt cycles ending even though they're much bigger deals
- It's dangerous — like eating fatty foods and having cholesterol accumulate in arteries, saying it doesn't seem to cause trouble while increasing probability of heart attack

### Healthy vs. Unhealthy Debt
- **Healthy**: (1) private lenders give credit for productive uses that pay back loans, (2) government borrows for productivity gains (infrastructure, education) paid via tax revenue, sometimes borrows more in weak times and pays back in strong
- **Unhealthy**: (1) central bank chronically printing/buying debt to make up for shortage in demand, (2) government chronically running large deficits with debt service rising faster than revenue (tax revenue)

### Important Takeaways (Chapter 2 Summary)
- **Debt crises are inevitable** — lending is never done perfectly, cycle affects psychology
- **Most debt crises can be managed well** if policy makers spread out negative impacts
- **All debt crises provide investment opportunities** if investors understand how they work
- Inevitably at end of Big Debt Cycle: difficult to keep real interest rates high enough for creditors without being too high for debtors — both tight-money contraction and loose-money inflation occur
- Central banks always **eventually choose to print and devalue** — each can only print its own currency

## Chapter 3: The Mechanics in Numbers and Equations

### Defining Unsustainable Debt
- Think of money flow like **blood flow**; income statements and balance sheets are the reports that show it
- Debt growth isn't necessarily bad if borrowed money → income rising faster than debt service
- **When debts grow faster than incomes**, it's like **plaque in arteries** — reduces income flow due to increased debt service
- If constrained too much = **default (economic equivalent of heart attack)**

### The Four Most Important Debt Risk Indicators
1. **Debts relative to income** — as ratio rises, higher interest/rollover payments, greater risk existing debt won't be rolled over
2. **Debt service relative to income** — total amount debtor must pay to avoid default; as it rises, investors expect problems, stop lending/sell
3. **Nominal interest rates relative to: (a) inflation rates, (b) nominal income growth rates** — shows how attractive credit conditions are for lenders vs. borrowers. Example: 100% debt-to-income, 5% interest, 3% growth → next year owes ~102% of income
4. **Debt and debt service relative to savings (reserves)** — large savings cushion reduces default risk even if other metrics unhealthy

> "If you watch these ratios over time, you will see them go to extreme levels and return to more normal levels one way or another. If you understand the cause/effect relationships that drive these changes, you can understand how to navigate them."

- Countries with **very high debt levels, large deficits, low savings, very high and fast-rising interest rates** = very high risk

### Mathematical Formulas

**1. Future Debt/Future Revenue** = [(Expenses Excl. Interest - Revenue) + Current Debt x (1 + Interest Rate)] / [Current Revenue x (1 + Growth Rate)]

**2. Future Debt Service/Future Revenue** = [(Interest Costs + Principal Payments)] / [Current Revenue x (1 + Growth Rate)]
- Where: Future Interest Costs = Future Debt Level x Average Effective Interest Rate; Future Principal Payments = Future Debt Level x Share of Debts Coming Due
- Interest rates rising gradually affects debt service as bonds "roll" (mature and reissue at new rates)
- When debtors stressed, creditors shorten maturity, increasing principal payments

**3. Interest Rate to Keep Debt Flat** = Revenue Growth Rate - [(Expenses Excl. Interest - Revenue) / Starting Debt Level]
- If primary deficit = 0, debts stay flat when interest rate = revenue growth rate
- If primary deficit = 5% of debt, interest rates need to be 5% below revenue growth

**4. Debts/debt service relative to savings**: Same formulas using savings as denominator. Large debts + large savings = less concerning (buffer exists).

**Focuses on debt-to-revenue, not debt-to-GDP** — GDP doesn't matter for government's debt service; actual cash flows matter.

### Rules of Thumb
- Interest rates = income growth and no primary deficit → debts stay flat
- 2% higher rates than growth for 20 years → debt-to-income increases ~50%
- Starting at 50% debt → goes to 75%; starting at 400% → goes to 600%

> "Debt service expenses accumulating is like plaque in the arteries accumulating in that it squeezes out the desired flow of nutrients to the economy."

> "The main effect of high debt levels is making the debtor vulnerable to not being able to roll it forward."

### US Debt Trajectory
- US expenditures excl. interest projected ~112% of income over next decade; **primary deficit ~12% of income**
- US borrowing ~20% of income yearly for interest on existing debt
- Current US debt-to-income = ~580%; projected to rise ~120% over 10 years to ~700%

**Comparative debt levels:**

### Interest Rate Differentials
- Using CBO projections: US will average 3.45% effective interest rates, 3.9% nominal growth over next decade
- Difference = ~-0.4%, leaving US in manageable range
- But even small interest rate disadvantages compound dramatically at high debt levels

### Interest Rate Death Spiral Example
- Government with 580% debt-to-income, 3.9% nominal growth, 3.5% interest rates, 32% spending above income, 35% of debt maturing yearly
- Year 1: Must sell $12.2 trillion debt ($10.5T rollover + $1T interest + new borrowing)
- If public unwilling to buy at current rates → rates rise → larger deficits → more upward pressure
- Model: 0.5%/year interest rate increase → Year 10 debts at 865% of income, interest at 67%, total debt service 342%

> "A spiral of rising interest rates leading to worsening credit risk, leading to less demand for the debt, leading to higher interest rates is a classic debt 'death spiral.'"

### Central Bank Intervention Example
- Instead of allowing spiral, central bank buys excess issuance, keeping private holdings at 600% of government income, rates flat at 3.5%
- Money supply balloons from $5.7T to $11.8T as central bank buys $6T bonds
- **Mechanically**: central bank prints money (creates reserves), gives private players cash for bonds → increases M0
- Lowering interest rates/increasing money supply reduces currency attractiveness
- Printing 1% of GDP → ~10% currency depreciation at 5.8x debt-to-income
- Greater currency sensitivity to money supply + less private willingness to lend → more weakness

### How the US Can Stabilize Debt-to-Income
**Four single-lever scenarios** (each alone "intolerably too large"):
- Interest rates drop to **1.0%** (fall of 3.5%)
- Inflation rises to **4.5%** (increase of 2.5%)
- **Spending cuts of 12%** (47% of discretionary)
- **Revenue increase of 11%**

Requires right combination — Dalio promises "3% 3-Part Solution" in Chapter 18.

## PART II: THE ARCHETYPAL SEQUENCE

## Chapter 4: The Archetypal Sequence

Author studied **35 major debt crises** over last 100 years. Template for how Big Debt Cycles transpire.

### Hard Money vs. Fiat Money
- **Hard currency cases**: governments promise money they can't print (gold, silver, dollars); when paying becomes tough, they almost always renege; value tumbles at moment promise broken
- **August 15, 1971**: most currencies shifted hard to fiat (Nixon closing gold window). Author witnessed personally. Same thing happened **April 1933**.
- **Fiat monetary systems**: currency value based on faith and incentives central banks provide; same debt/credit dynamics; key difference: devaluations don't happen all at once — **more gradually**
- **Bank of Japan** example: since 2013, bond holders lost 60% vs. gold, 45% vs. US dollar debt, 6% in domestic purchasing power (average inflation 1%)
- **Historical pattern**: Big Debt Cycles typically include regimes going back and forth between hard and fiat — each leads to extreme consequences

### Nine Stages of the Final Crisis

**Stage 1**: Private sector and government get deep in debt
**Stage 2**: Private sector suffers debt crisis, government gets deeper in debt to help
**Stage 3**: Government experiences debt squeeze — free-market demand falls short of supply. **Net selling = big red flag.**
**Stage 4**: Selling leads to: (a) free-market-driven tightening, (b) weakening economy, (c) declining reserves, (d) currency pressure
**Stage 5**: Central bank prints money and buys bonds (when rates hit 0% or long rates limit short rate decline)
**Stage 6**: Central bank loses money (interest on liabilities > return on assets). **Big red flag** — signals central bank's death spiral
**Stage 7**: Debts restructured and devalued ("beautiful deleveraging" when managed best)
**Stage 8**: Extraordinary taxes and capital controls imposed
**Stage 9**: Deleveraging creates return to equilibrium — new stable monetary system established

**Causes of going broke**: chronic overspending, debt accumulations, costly wars, costly shocks (droughts, floods, pandemics), or some mix.

**Risk depends on**: whether debt in own currency (less painful), debtors/creditors domestic (more control), reserve currency status (greater marginal inclinations to buy). **Strong tendency**: reserve currency governments abuse privilege, making decline more abrupt and painful.

## Chapter 5: Stages 1-4 in Detail

### Stage 1: Deep in Debt
- Large/growing stock of debt from chronic deficit spending
- **Rising share of spending to consumption/social safety net; declining share to productivity-enhancing investment**
- Countries become reliant on large social safety net — cutting it becomes political third rail (e.g., Brazil, US today)
- **31 of 35 cases**: large, persistent government deficits going into crisis
- Public balance sheet sometimes looks better than reality — heavy private borrowing that public sector must back up, implicit guarantees to banks government can't afford to let fail

### Stage 2: Private Crisis, Government Steps In
- Government can borrow more easily (central banks can print, power to tax, especially true for reserve currencies)
- **15 of 21 cases**: government debt soars while private debt plunges ~1 year before crisis — **short leading indicator of trouble**

### Stage 3: Government Debt Squeeze
- **25 of 35 cases**: debt service as % of revenues accelerated going into crisis
- When debt service ~100% of income: squeezing out spending AND/OR requiring rollovers that might not happen
- At time of devaluation: government typically on path toward indefinitely increasing debts absent devaluation

### Stage 4: Selling of Government Debt
- Investors' and savers' flight from country's assets, bringing run on currency/debt to breaking point
- Central bank attempts to defend currency with tightening + reserve sales
- **Ultimately forced to change course** due to: painful economic effects of tightening, inadequacy of reserves
- **Large red flag**: when smart investors recognize losses inevitable (either default or lots of printing/currency weakness/inflation)
- Foreign inflows plunge; governments shorten debt maturity to make bonds more palatable
- Credit spreads rise for hard currency debt; risky assets sell off

### Central Bank Reserve Depletion
- **Pre-intervention median**: reserves ~5% GDP, covers ~10% money supply, ~11% government debt
- **Central bank typically spends over half reserves** to defend currency (worst 6-month period: median 49% decline)
- Central bank stops when defense will fail and having no reserves is scarier than currency falling
- **Currency falls during defense** (gold rallies 42% median)
- **After ~2 years of defense, gives up**: reserves at ~6% of money stock, 3% of government debt
- **After intervention stops**: gold rallies another 51% median

**Key examples**: Argentina 1990s (1.3% GDP reserves), Argentina 2001 (8.7% GDP, 43% money stock), Brazil 1999 (8.5% GDP), Turkey 2001 (6.1% GDP), Mexico Tequila Crisis (3.9% GDP), USA 1971 (1.8% GDP)

### Capital Flight Behaviors (19 specific patterns)
**Corporate**: keep international revenue offshore in foreign FX, increase hedging, sweep cash out of subsidiaries, halt new FDI
**Domestic banks**: forced sellers when liquidity dries up; central bank tactics make offshore more attractive
**International banks**: close lines of business, literally sell/give away subsidiaries
**International investors**: liquidity dries up pushing out large foreign investors; reserve allocators accumulate new reserves in different currency; can't sell but don't roll exposures
**Domestic savers**: convert deposits to hard currency, buy real goods ahead of inflation, high-net-worth move money abroad, open foreign bank accounts
**Speculators**: bond vigilante action becomes self-reinforcing; equity investors pull out

## Chapter 6: Stages 5-6 (Crisis at the Central Bank)

### Stage 5: Central Bank Prints Money and Buys Bonds
- Central bank doesn't literally "print money" — borrows reserves from commercial banks at very short-term rates
- Government can't escape financing needs; turns to central bank
- Central bank buys bonds directly or provides guarantees/backstops
- **Balance sheet hit transferred from government to central bank and currency holders**
- Often takes losses on assets bought at uneconomical prices (defaults, inflation, rising rates)
- Trends toward greater central bank bond holdings and shortening of maturities start nearly a decade before crisis

**Recent QE examples:**
| Country | QE (% GDP) | QE (% Total Debt) | QE (% Govt Debt) |
|---------|------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| USA (2008+, four waves) | 18% | 5% | 16% |
| Europe (four waves) | 30% | 10% | 36% |
| Japan (three waves) | 95% | 22% | 46% |

### Risk: Central Banks Take Same Risks as Commercial Banks
- Only difference: can print money to monetize debts and account for losses in less apparent ways
- Central bank buys bond → pays by creating new deposit → pays interest on deposit
- If deposit rates rise above bond returns → lose money, negative cash flow
- Under mark-to-market accounting → losses; if losses > capital → negative net worth

### Stage 6: Central Bank Loses Money
- Interest rate on liabilities > interest rate on assets → negative cash flow
- Notable but not big red flag until **significant negative net worth**
- Forced to print more money to cover negative cash flow → **big red flag: death spiral**
- Central bank caught between maintaining easy policy (support weak economy) AND tight enough policy (discourage currency flight) — **hallmark of unsustainable situation**

**Historical cases with large CB cash flow losses:**
| Case | CB Balance Sheet (% GDP) | CB Cash Flow Losses (% GDP) | CB Net Reserves (% GDP) |
|------|--------------------------|----------------------------|------------------------|
| Argentina Late 80s | 31.5% | -3.3% | 4.7% |
| Argentina Recent | 34.0% | -3.5% | 1.4% |
| Peru Late 80s | 6.9% | -2.6% | 2.5% |
| Dutch Guilder 1780s | 5.8% | -3.3% | 1.8% |
| Turkey Today | 17.2% | -2.6% | -2.5% |

- Central bank losses matter less than private sector losses BUT **big losses = step toward more advanced stage near end of Big Debt Cycle**
- As losses move from small to very large: create cash flow needs only met with lot of money printing, significant downward currency pressure

## Chapter 7: Stages 7-9 (Resolution)

### Stage 7: Debts Restructured and Devalued
- Currency devalues and remaining holders take big real losses in purchasing power — continues until new monetary system with credibility established
- **Gold outperforms local currency by ~60%** from devaluation start to currency bottom
- Digital currencies like Bitcoin may benefit in future crises

**Historical returns during devaluations:**
- Japan WWII: gold +282%, commodities +203%, equities +100%, bonds -53%
- Weimar Germany: gold +245%, commodities +241%, equities +754%, bonds -99%
- USA 1971: gold +185%, commodities +162%, equities -44%
- Mexico 1982: gold +53%, commodities +73%, equities -27%, bonds -81%
- Average gold vs. bonds: **+94% excess return**; equities/gold/commodities vs. bonds: **+71%**

### Reserves/Debt Dynamics
- Ratio of reserves to debt typically falls first, then rises
- Initial fall driven by: (1) rapidly increasing debt levels, (2) reserve sales attempting currency defense
- After policymakers let currency go, ratio improves as devaluation mechanically reduces local currency debt value relative to hard currency reserves
- This improves country's competitiveness, helping earn more in hard currency terms
- Government debt typically rises first; money growth lags at point central bank defends currency
- Controls (FX controls, official rates different from market rates) create distortions and harm more than help
- After giving up defense, money printing accelerates, producing inflation that improves nominal income relative to debts

### Stage 8: Extraordinary Policies
- **Marginal income/inheritance tax rates rise ~10%** in years leading to devaluation
- **Capital controls** common across all major countries historically:
  - UK: 4 periods (1900, 1920, 1940, 1960)
  - US: 2 periods (1900, 1920)
  - China: 3 periods; Germany: 4 periods; Russia: 6 periods; Japan: 2 periods

### Stage 9: Return to Equilibrium

> "When there are inflationary depressions so the debt is devalued, at the end of the cycle, government reserves are raised through asset sales, and a strictly enforced transition from a rapidly declining currency to a relatively stable currency is achieved by the central bank linking the currency to a hard currency or a hard asset (e.g., gold) while having very tight money and a very high real interest rate."

**Five Classic Steps:**
1. **Restructuring debts** to manageable levels (defaulting/restructuring foreign and often local currency debts)
2. **Deep, painful fiscal adjustment** (sustainable finances without money printing)
3. **Obtaining sufficient reserves** (devaluation helps competitiveness, asset sales, IMF borrowing)
4. **High real interest rates** (more than compensating for currency risks)
5. **Limits on central bank actions** undermining sustainable finances

**Debt burden reduction attribution (average case: 89% → 70% GDP):**
- Central bank purchases: -7%
- **Inflation: -38%**
- **Real GDP growth: -26%**
- Primary surpluses: -16%
- Defaults/restructuring: -8%
- Interest payments (countervailing): +76%

> "When debts are restructured and/or devalued, it is typically a terrible time in markets and economies, but this terrible time reduces the debt burdens and establishes the foundation for the improvement."

### Author's Proposed Monetary Policy
> "If I were running monetary policy, my goal would be to keep the long-term real interest rate relatively stable at a rate that balances the needs of both borrower-debtors and lender-creditors... That would mean seeking to have the real Treasury bond yield around 2%, varying that target by something like 1%, and targeting the yield curve slope so that a) the short-term rate is about 1% below the long-term rate and b) the short-term rate divided by the long-term rate is about 70%, give or take about 2% and about 50%, respectively."

### The Yield Curve

> "The relationship between short-term rates and long-term rates (i.e., the yield curve) is very important because when short-term interest rates are high relative to long-term rates that indicates money is tight and encourages the holding and lending of cash."

- Higher interest rate normally required by creditors to hold longer-term debt (reward for lending longer)
- When central bank wants to slow growth: raises short-term rates relative to long-term → **inverted yield curve**
- When wants to stimulate: does opposite → positive yield curve
- **High real yields + flat/inverted curve** = tight money, good for lender-creditors, bad for borrower-debtors
- **Low real yields + positive curve** = easy money, good for borrower-debtors, bad for lender-creditors
- Extreme shifts cause big/volatile swings — disruptive to economies, causes pain and inefficiencies

### Debt Service Components
- Interest owed = amount borrowed x interest rate, plus principal = total debt service
- Debt service roughly flat 1950-2000 (government debt roughly flat/falling relative to revenue)
- Interest payments rose slightly 1950-1990 as average rate slowly rose; fell 1990-2022 as rate slowly fell
- **Future projections (CBO)**: central government debt high and projected to rise fast, interest rates on high debts projected to rise → **big increase in government debt service relative to revenue** → significant squeeze on spending unless more borrowing (likely financed by central bank)

### Who Holds US Government Debt

- Some worried US won't pay back; becoming sellers
- Dollar = leading reserve currency of leading world power
- Hard to imagine big buyers will keep buying if: (a) US handles debt irresponsibly, (b) threatens sanctions, (c) returns are bad, (d) loses prominence

## Chapter 14: Since 2020 — Pandemic and Big Fiscal Deficits Monetized

### COVID-19 and Monetary Policy 3 (MP3)

## Chapter 16: The Japanese Case and Lessons It Provides

> "This chapter shows how a heavily indebted reserve currency country, Japan, handled its debts... it is especially interesting because for more than two decades Japanese policy makers did the exact opposite of what should be done to execute a beautiful deleveraging—i.e., they did not restructure the debts for nine years and they didn't drive interest rates below inflation rates and nominal growth rates for 23 years."

### Before 1945

- Japan had elevated civilization, happily isolated until foreign powers demanded "trade"
- **US Commodore Matthew Perry** arrived 1853 → fall of 250-year-old **Tokugawa family shogunate**
- Foreign military superiority collapsed existing domestic and monetary orders

> "As the Japanese realized that the foreign, more modern approaches were better, the Japanese government was replaced by a new government in 1868, which largely copied the Western powers' approaches."

- **Meiji Era (1868)**: constitutional monarchy, modernization by following Western styles → became great power
- Similar to China's later transformation under Deng Xiaoping (~100 years later)
- Defeated China (1894-95), Russia (1904-05); annexed Korea (1910)
- 1912-1926: parliamentary democracy → **classic combination**: debt/economic crisis + democracy dysfunction = collapse of public trust + hard-right takeover
- 1921: PM assassinated by nationalist; 1929 crash led nationalist military to seize control
- **1925 "Peace Preservation Law"** silenced leftists and democratic activists

> "The Great Depression made the economic situation worse and from 1937 to 1940 all political parties were dissolved, and there was increasing autocratic control that left the power exclusively in the hands of the military."

- Japan invaded **Manchuria** (1931), more of China (1937)
- **US, UK, Netherlands imposed export restrictions** — froze assets, cut off oil → **Pearl Harbor attack**
- **Japan lost WWII** due to nuclear weapons; **all money and debt destroyed**; occupied/reconstructed by US (1945-52)

### Debt-to-GDP Since 1870

- Two Big Debt Cycles visible: 1930-45 run-up (war), then post-1990 bubble
- Short-term debt/economic cycles are imperceptible when viewing Big Debt Cycles

### Since 1945: Rebuilding and Bubble

> "From 1945 through 1990, Japan rebuilt itself to become the second-greatest economic power in the world and in the process built up a huge debt burden that funded a bubble that burst in 1989-90, which has had a huge weakening effect on Japan ever since."

### What NOT to Do (1990-2013)

> "The Japanese government's handling of its debt problem from 1990 until 2013 exemplified exactly what not to do... even though Japan had the capacity to execute a beautiful deleveraging because almost all of its debt was denominated in its local currency and almost all of the difficult debtor-creditor relationships were between Japanese parties, plus it was a net creditor to the rest of the world."

- **Did not restructure debts** — lingered on bank/company balance sheets, making them "zombie institutions"
- **Held rigid employment/cost policies** — couldn't effectively cut costs and adapt
- **Did not make interest rates low relative to nominal growth/inflation** until deflation and near-zero rates (1995)
- **Did not meaningfully monetize debts** until after deflation set in
- Nearly **two decades of insufficient adjustment**
- Result: **continuous deflation and economic stagnation** until mid-2013
- **Government didn't deal with non-performing loans until 1999** (9 years after bubble popped)
- Didn't monetize debt and bring rates significantly below growth/inflation until 2013 (23 years)

**Demographics headwind**: 1990 had 12% over 65 and 69% working-age; now **29% over 65 and only 59% working-age**

### "Three Arrows" Policy (Late 2012/Early 2013)

> "Fiscal and monetary policies changed greatly and appropriately when Bank of Japan Governor Kuroda and Prime Minister Abe came to power in late 2012/early 2013 and initiated their 'three arrows' policy to 1) increase the money supply, 2) boost central government spending, and 3) enact economic and regulatory reforms to make the Japanese economy more competitive."

- 2013-2019: no deflation, low positive growth (0.9%/year), beginning of healing
- **Deflationary/depressing psychological conditions lingered** after 23 years of debt depression
- **Extremely large debt monetization** + fiscal deficits (5% GDP on average)
- **BoJ bought massive amounts** — now holds government bonds worth >90% of GDP
- Interest rates pushed **0.9% below nominal growth rate and 1% below inflation rate**
- Yen depreciated — all very stimulative

**Japanese government bonds became terrible store of wealth**:
- **Lost 45% relative to US bonds, 60% relative to gold**
- Average interest rate ~2.2% below US rate
- Currency depreciated 5.5%/year in real terms vs. dollar
- -45% cumulative return almost entirely due to currency depreciation

> "The principle should resonate: don't own government bonds when there are extreme amounts of debt monetization."

**Wage competitiveness**:
- Modest inflation of 0.8%/year in worker compensation (yen terms) since 2013
- Big yen depreciations + wage gains in other nations made Japan more competitive
- **Total decline of 58% in cost of Japanese worker** relative to American worker since 2013

- **Remarkably, no really big internal or external conflicts** during this period
- Though Japan now preparing for war with China as US's most important ally in region

### Five Dynamics of How Japan Got Here

1. **Government deficit spending** floods private sector with cash, aids private deleveraging — left Japan with highest government debt levels of any major country
2. **Central bank monetizes debt** — keeps long rates low, lowers debt service; govt debt minus CB holdings falls as % GDP
   - Japan's debt service (interest + principal) in yen fell ~7% during period debts rose nearly 30%
   - About half due to lower interest rates, half due to BoJ purchases
   - Massive debt increase concurrent with improvement in Japan's central government balance sheet
   - **Net assets** (govt assets minus liabilities) now 20% better in dollar terms vs. 2013

3. **Currency depreciation** as implicit tax on foreign/domestic bondholders
   - Yen-denominated bond holders lost over 50% of value in dollar and real terms — comparable to default
   - Japanese government debt measured in dollars: down since 2001 despite rapid yen borrowing
   - Debt measured in gold: down ~80% from peaks — effective debt reduction without formal default

4. **Domestic savers taxed** (lesser degree)
   - Japanese debt holders did OK in nominal terms, even while assets performed poorly in dollar terms
   - Weak economy kept currency declines from translating into much domestic inflation
   - Japan prices: import prices rose as yen devalued, offset by low domestic price growth (services CPI)

5. **Country becomes more competitive** — everything became much cheaper in dollar terms
   - Attracted substantial FDI inflows starting ~2013 (rose from near 0% to nearly 1% of GDP)
   - Japan went from overvalued market (by P/E measures) to inexpensive relative to US
   - Home prices and productivity-adjusted wages in USD both fell considerably

**Three levers**:
- ~12% spending cut (if used alone)
- ~11% tax increase (if used alone)
- ~3% interest rate cut (if used alone)

**Interest rate changes most powerful**:
- 1% rate drop = **4x more effective** than 1% tax increase over 20 years
- 1% tax increase = **1.2x more effective** than 1% spending reduction
- Rate cuts also boost asset prices, raise capital gains tax receipts, stimulate economy, raise inflation (which raises tax revenues)

> "The biggest influence on the government's deficit is ironically not Congress, which determines spending and taxes—it is the Federal Reserve, which determines interest rates."

### Fiscal-Monetary Coordination

- **Fiscal tightening** (deficit reduction) = negative for growth/inflation
- **Monetary easing** (in conjunction) = positive for growth/inflation
- If balanced well, **budget deficit can be reduced significantly without unacceptable economic effects**
- Biggest imbalance now: central government has too much debt vs. private sector in relatively good shape
- Fed helped fund large deficits that created the debt problem originally; cooperation makes sense

> "So, what's the problem with cutting spending and raising taxes other than the political problem of anger from those who are getting less money from spending or who are paying more in taxes? I don't see it."

### Proposed Implementation

- **~4% spending cuts + ~4% tax increases + ~1% real interest rate cuts** → 3% of GDP goal
- Spreads burden so no single group bears too much; politically agnostic approach
- Phase changes over **three years** to avoid abrupt shocks
- Impact: **$9 trillion less debt in 10 years, $26 trillion in 20 years** (31% lower than current trajectory)

**Without Fed cooperation**: Would need ~6% spending cuts + ~6% tax increases
- Large by historical standards but manageable if balanced well
- If too depressing, Fed would respond by lowering rates anyway

> "For these reasons, I know that if this 3% 3-part plan is followed it would be worlds better than if it is not followed."

### Who Benefits from Lower Interest Rates?

- **Bondholders**: Lower real yield BUT benefit from rising bond prices + safer bonds (reduced default risk)
- **World would celebrate** reduced US debt risk and demonstration that American political system can solve big problems
- **Equities and major markets** would benefit

### Historical Precedent

**Most successful US case**: 1993-98 — cut deficit from **4% GDP to +1% surplus** (5% of GDP improvement)
- Would be equivalent to cutting deficit by **$1.5 trillion today**

**Median fiscal adjustment** (all cases): 5.7% change in primary structural deficit (59% revenue increases, 41% spending cuts)

**Painless cases** (Belgium 82-87, Italy 90-97, Sweden 93-00, Denmark 83-86, Ireland 87-89):
- NO significant hard currency debts
- Strong domestic/global economy during adjustment
- Easier financial conditions; bond yields **fell 3.4%** on average
- Growth averaged **0.9% vs potential**

**Painful cases** (Greece 10-14, Ireland 11-14, Greece 90-94, Spain 10-14, Hungary 07-09):
- All had **significant hard currency debts**
- All during **weak economic conditions**
- Growth averaged **-2.3% vs potential**; unemployment **+2.6% above 10-year average**

### Specific Policy Options

**Tax increases** (from CBO):
- Apply Social Security taxes to incomes >$250K: $143B/yr (0.38% GDP) = **38% of target**
- 2% increase in income tax rates for four highest brackets: $57B/yr (0.15% GDP) = 15% of target
- Impose net investment income taxes on business income: $42B/yr (0.11% GDP) = 11% of target
- Tariffs (10% on all imports): ~0.6% GDP
- **Elon Musk's DOGE claim**: if can cut budget deficit by $2 trillion (half claim of $1 trillion cut) = 3% GDP

**Budget breakdown**: ~70% non-interest spending is "mandatory"
- Social Security: $1.6T (22%), Medicare: $1.1T (16%), Medicaid: $666B (9%), Other mandatory: $898B (13%)
- Discretionary: Defense $862B (12%), Non-defense $878B (12%), Education $83B (1%)

**Two modest Social Security changes** could produce ~10% of required cuts:
- Phase in retirement age increase from 67 to 70
- Use more realistic inflation measure for benefit calculations
- To achieve 4% cuts from discretionary alone would require **15% cuts** on average

> "The important thing is getting to a reasonable mix that adds up to a deficit reduction of 3% of GDP to get the deficit down to 3% of GDP."

### Timing Is Critical

> "When there are large government debts that are growing quickly... the most important things to do are to 1) cut the deficit by enough to rectify the problem, 2) cut the deficit when economic conditions are good so the cuts are counter-cyclical, and 3) have monetary policy be stimulative enough to keep the economy strong in the face of such cuts."

**Now is exceptionally good time** — three reasons:
1. Better to reduce deficits in good times than wait for crisis in bad times
2. US near full employment, growth moderately strong, inflation slightly elevated, private sector balance sheets strong
3. If not now, debt problem grows — self-reinforcing compound dynamic

**Caveat**: If 2017 Trump tax cuts extended as promised, deficit larger by **1.5% of GDP** — would require >4% of GDP cuts

> "If our representatives in Washington don't get a debt limit deal done, it will be because of their lack of reasonableness and their inability to compromise—not because a good and workable plan is beyond their reach."
