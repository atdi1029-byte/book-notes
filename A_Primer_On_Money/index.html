Glossary

Money & Its Forms

Demand deposits: Bank account balances withdrawable on demand, used by writing a check. By 1964 they constituted ~80% of the U.S. money supply. Example: your checking account balance.

Legal tender: Any form of payment the U.S. Government declares acceptable for taxes and all public or private debts. Courts will enforce payment in it — that enforcement is what gives it value, not any physical backing. Example: Federal Reserve notes printed "legal tender for all debts, public and private."

Federal Reserve notes: The paper currency in your wallet. By 1964 they represented 94% of all currency in circulation ($31.1 billion of $33 billion total). Backed by "the credit of the U.S. Government" — meaning the full taxing and productive power of the economy, not gold.

Money supply: Demand deposits at commercial banks plus currency and coin held outside banks. Savings accounts, money market funds, and other instruments are explicitly excluded — they are not immediately spendable and the underlying currency is already counted once inside the banking system.

Pen-and-ink money (checkbook money): Patman's term for bank-created deposits. When a bank makes a loan or buys a security, it simply credits the borrower's account — no printing required. This is how ~80% of the money supply is created.

Banking Mechanics

Fractional reserve banking: The system in which banks hold only a fraction of their deposit liabilities as reserves. A bank with $100 in reserves and a 10% requirement can support $1,000 in deposits — creating $900 "out of thin air." Originated with 17th-century goldsmith bankers who discovered most depositors never claimed their gold simultaneously.

Reserve requirement: The percentage of outstanding deposits a commercial bank must hold as reserves at the Federal Reserve. In 1964, roughly 10%. Raising this ratio shrinks the money supply; lowering it expands it. The Fed Board sets this rate independently.

Bank reserves: Credits held by commercial banks at the Federal Reserve — not their depositors' money, not their own cash, but bookkeeping entries created by the Fed itself. Total reserves in the system can only be changed by the Federal Reserve, not by private banks. The popular textbook image of banks "depositing" cash at the Fed is false.

Money multiplier: The mathematical relationship between reserves and total deposits. With a 10% reserve requirement, each $1 of new reserves can support $10 of total deposits. Formula: A (reserves) × B (deposits permitted per reserve dollar) = C (total deposits). The Fed controls both A and B.

Discount window: The Fed's original primary tool — making short-term loans to commercial banks against "eligible paper" (short-term commercial IOUs). Congress designed the 1913 system around the discount window, intending that money supply would expand and contract automatically with business credit demand. In practice, largely displaced by open market operations after the 1930s.

Eligible paper: Short-term commercial and industrial IOUs that banks could present to their regional Federal Reserve bank for discount (i.e., sell at a small markdown). The original 1913 design intended this as the main reserve-creation mechanism — tying money supply directly to local business credit demand. Now almost never used.

Federal Reserve Operations

Open market operations: The Fed's purchase or sale of U.S. government securities in the secondary market. Buying adds reserves to the banking system (expanding deposits); selling removes them. Since the 1950s this has been the dominant monetary policy tool, executed entirely through 21 private securities dealers in New York.

Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC): The 12-member body that sets open market policy. Composed of 7 public Board of Governors members plus 5 of the 12 regional Federal Reserve bank presidents. The bank presidents are elected by privately owned commercial banks — giving private banking interests votes on national monetary policy.

Primary dealers: The 21 private firms (as of 1964) authorized to transact directly with the Federal Reserve in open market operations. They function as gatekeepers between the Fed and the bond market, earning spreads on every transaction. Marriner Eccles called them a "tollgate" the government is forced to pay.

Discount rate: The interest rate the Fed charges commercial banks for loans through the discount window. Was the primary policy tool until the 1930s; now secondary to open market operations. Changes in the discount rate are more symbolic than operational.

Treasury-Fed Accord (1951): Agreement giving the Federal Reserve independence to set interest rates without being obligated to peg bond prices at wartime levels. Patman argues this was later mythologized — the actual inflation data showed prices had already peaked before the Accord, and the policy shift added billions annually to debt servicing costs.

Policy Concepts

Tight money: Restricting the money supply — raising interest rates and reducing credit availability. The Fed's dominant postwar posture. Patman argues it consistently hurt small business and ordinary consumers while enriching creditors and financial institutions.

Easy money: Expanding the money supply — lowering rates and increasing credit availability. The 1913 Federal Reserve Act was designed around easy money as the default. Keynes and the Depression experience showed easy money cannot stimulate a truly depressed economy ("pushing on a string").

Bills-only policy: The Fed's 1952–1961 restriction of open market operations exclusively to very short-term Treasury bills. Intended to let the market freely set long-term rates. In practice, perverse — the investment, housing, and business lending targets the Fed wanted to hit respond to long-term rates, which the bills-only policy couldn't directly reach.

Balance of payments: The net flow of dollars between the U.S. and foreign countries. By the early 1960s, the U.S. was running a deficit — dollars flowing abroad. The Fed used high short-term rates to attract foreign capital, but this choked domestic investment. Kennedy eventually solved this with the Interest Equalization Tax instead — a fiscal tool that achieved the same goal at far lower economic cost.

Introduction: Money Is Manufactured

Wright Patman opens with a declaration that sounds radical only because it has been so successfully obscured: money is a manufactured item. It doesn't grow in the ground, it isn't found in rivers, and it doesn't fall from the sky. Its supply is determined by its manufacturers — the banking system — and like any manufactured good, it can be produced in the wrong quantities, at the wrong price, and for the benefit of the wrong people. Patman's forty years in Congress had taught him that the public's ignorance of this basic fact was not accidental. The mystery surrounding monetary policy was carefully tended by those who profited from it.

The constitutional premise is straightforward. Article I, Section 8 gives Congress alone the power to "coin money, regulate the value thereof." The Supreme Court interpreted this broadly — "whatever power there is over the currency is vested in Congress." But Congress has largely farmed this power out. The Federal Reserve creates reserves for the banking system, and private commercial banks create the checkbook money that constitutes most of what Americans actually use. The question Patman wants Congress — and the public — to ask is: on whose terms, at whose cost, and for whose benefit?

The money supply is one of the two or three most important influences on economic life — on employment, prices, investment, and growth — yet most citizens have no more understanding of how it works than they do of how a nuclear reactor works. Patman's primer is an attempt to close that gap. It is educational in the best sense: clear, specific, and blunt about who wins and who loses from decisions that most people never know are being made.

Harvard's Seymour Harris, who wrote the preface, summarizes Patman's forty-year crusade. The core constitutional claim — that the power to create money belongs to Congress alone and was unlawfully farmed out — runs through every page. Harris notes that Patman's opponents were "powerful, numerous, and well organized, and often do not distinguish the financial from the general interest." That sentence, written in 1964, describes every subsequent debate about the Fed's independence with equal precision.

Only two institutions in the United States can manufacture money: the Federal Reserve and the commercial banks. No other financial institution has this power. Savings and loan associations, mutual savings banks, credit unions, insurance companies, pension funds — all of these move money around, but none of them can create it. The Fed creates reserves, the raw material. Commercial banks create the checkbook money that most Americans actually use, by making loans. The primer is organized around understanding these two institutions — how they create money, who controls them, who benefits from their decisions, and what reforms would make them serve the public rather than themselves.

One structural drift Patman documents with particular concern is the commercial banking system's departure from its original purpose. Banks were chartered to serve local communities — providing credit for small businesses, farmers, and ordinary borrowers who lacked alternatives. The original Federal Reserve Act included residency requirements for bank directors and other provisions designed to anchor banks in their communities. Over time, those provisions eroded. Holding companies spread. Directors in other states were permitted. The absentee-owned bank replaced the locally controlled institution. And the loans that local communities needed were increasingly displaced by purchases of long-term U.S. Government bonds and tax-free municipal bonds — profitable, requiring no judgment about local creditworthiness, generating no paperwork, and providing no direct benefit to the community that granted the charter.

The primer is organized to build understanding from first principles. Chapters I and II establish what money is and why it matters. Chapters III through V explain how money is created and who controls the creation process. Chapters VI through VIII examine who owns and subsidizes the banking system. Chapter IX reviews the postwar record of active monetary policy. Chapter X lays out the reform agenda. Throughout, Patman's method is the same: start with the mechanism, identify who benefits, quantify the transfer, and propose the structural change that would redirect the benefit to the public.

Chapter I: Money and Society

The 1963 data that opens this chapter delivers an immediate shock. Total gross farm receipts for the entire United States — every bushel of wheat, every head of cattle, every bale of cotton produced on American soil — came to roughly $36 billion. Personal income from interest alone came to roughly $33 billion. The nation's farmers produced almost exactly as much total income as the nation's creditors collected simply for holding claims. This was not a curiosity. It was a statement about who the monetary system worked for.

Interest is not a fee paid at the bank. It is embedded in the cost of everything. Mining companies borrow to extract ore. Smelters borrow to process it. Manufacturers borrow for inventory. Distributors borrow for warehousing. Retailers borrow to stock shelves. Utilities borrow to build power lines. Each stage of production passes its interest cost forward to the next. By the time a consumer buys a product, she has paid interest to every creditor in the chain — invisibly, because the cost is folded into the price rather than listed separately on a receipt.

Raise interest rates and you raise the cost of business investment in new plants and equipment — the third largest component of U.S. annual output. The mechanism is not mysterious: higher rates mean firms only borrow if expected profit exceeds total costs including interest. Raise rates enough and investment becomes unprofitable. Investment falls, capital goods industries contract, workers are laid off, demand for raw materials collapses. Small businesses are hit hardest — they lack the bargaining power of large corporations and are rationed out of credit entirely even at high rates, while Fortune 500 companies still get their share.

The long-run cost of sustained high interest is subtler and more devastating. Every generation's standard of living depends on the previous generation's investment in new machinery and technology. High interest today means less investment today, which means tomorrow's workers have less efficient equipment, which means lower output per worker, which means a smaller economy and a reduced standard of living — even if no recession ever occurs. "What may appear to be a simple decision to rein in the money supply and raise interest rates is, in fact, a simultaneous decision about the whole range of economic life."

Rising interest rates also redistribute income in a specific direction. Higher rates boost the stock prices of banks and life insurance companies, whose earnings depend on interest spreads. The major owners of those companies are a tiny minority of families. So raising rates constitutes a transfer from interest payers — essentially everyone, as consumers — to interest receivers — a handful of the already wealthy. Patman was making what would later be called a distributional critique of monetary policy, decades before it became fashionable.

The inflation-fighting argument for tight money deserves scrutiny. Tight money fights inflation by cutting demand for goods and labor — but modern prices, Patman argues, are not very responsive to demand declines. The steel industry's price behavior made this plain: even during recessions, administered prices barely budged. Tight money can only suppress inflation by keeping significant numbers of people unemployed and the economy permanently below capacity. That is not a side effect of the policy. It is the mechanism. And as Patman notes, the conditions under which tight money could work painlessly — a full boom with genuine capital-goods backlogs — existed at most twice in the preceding decade, briefly, and the high rates stayed long after those conditions vanished.

The fiscal cost is concrete. By fiscal year 1963, the federal government was paying roughly $10 billion annually in interest on the national debt — approximately twice what it would have paid at 1952 interest rates. The excess cost came to about $5 billion per year, or $131 annually for an average family of five. Those who denounced government deficits the loudest were typically the same people who supported the high interest rates that generated much of the deficit in the first place. This was not inconsistency. It was a consistent defense of creditor interests dressed in the language of fiscal responsibility.

Patman frames this in Cold War terms that would have resonated in 1964. U.S. intelligence estimated the Soviet economy growing at 7–10% annually. The U.S. was growing at under 3.5%. Part of that gap was attributable to sustained tight money dampening investment. An unelected body making monetary decisions in the interest of the financial sector was, in Patman's framing, a competitive handicap that the country was voluntarily imposing on itself.

Chapter II: What Is Money?

Money's history is the history of social agreement. Seashells, whale's teeth, boar's tusks, stones, feathers, bricks, coconuts, cocoa beans, iron rings, salt, beaver pelts, blankets, bronze axes, stone wheels on Pacific islands — all of these have functioned as money wherever people agreed they would. Ancient Rome paid its legionnaires partly in salt, which is where the word "salary" comes from. The material is irrelevant. What matters is the shared belief that the thing can be exchanged for other goods and services. Paper with pictures of dead presidents is money because courts will enforce debts denominated in it. That legal enforceability — not gold, not government promises, not intrinsic value — is what gives the dollar its value.

American colonial history illustrates this with unusual clarity. The earliest settlers used wampum — clamshell beads — as money. Massachusetts made it legal tender in 1637. New Amsterdam fixed an official wampum exchange rate in 1641. New Yorkers were still paying the Brooklyn ferry fare in wampum as late as 1693. The colonies also used English, Spanish, and French coins as trade expanded. And then George III forbade the colonies from minting coins or issuing any other money — part of a mercantilist policy designed to keep colonial commerce dependent on London. Combined with coin drains to finance European wars, this created a severe money shortage in the colonies. Historians note that colonial resentment of this money suppression was one of the major reasons for the Declaration of Independence. Money, from the beginning, was a political instrument.

The continental currency episode became the cautionary tale. The Continental Congress issued "Continental dollars" to finance the Revolution, but without the power to tax or declare them legal tender, and with British counterfeiting adding supply, the Continentals became worthless. "Not worth a Continental" entered the language as a phrase for something with no intrinsic value and nothing behind it. Virginia had tried tobacco as legal tender earlier, issuing warehouse receipts that circulated as currency into the 1900s — but tobacco prices were set in European markets, making the monetary unit unstable and long-term contracts unreliable.

By 1964, money in the United States took three forms: coins minted by the Treasury, paper currency (overwhelmingly Federal Reserve notes, which represented 94% of all currency), and commercial bank demand deposits — checkbook money. In February 1964 the totals were: $32 billion in currency and coin, $119.7 billion in demand deposits, for a total money supply of $151.9 billion. That means roughly 80 cents of every dollar Americans used existed only as a number in a bank's ledger. This is pen-and-ink money, created not by printing presses but by bookkeeping entries when banks make loans.

The question of what backs the dollar deserves a direct answer. Federal Reserve notes are backed by "the credit of the U.S. Government" — that phrase is printed on every bill. American citizens can exchange them for other Federal Reserve notes or use them to pay taxes and debts. That's it. Foreign central banks could, at the time, exchange dollar holdings for Treasury gold — so gold "backed" the dollar only for international transactions. Domestically, the dollar was backed by the productive capacity of the U.S. economy — the inventories, machinery, and farmland against which the underlying bank loans were made.

The gold standard question is clarified by Patman with unusual precision. Even during the century when the dollar was officially convertible to gold, it was practically impossible to maintain that convertibility. In 1914, money in existence totaled roughly $20 billion while monetary gold reserves totaled only $1.6 billion — a 12-to-1 ratio. In 1963, the money supply was $157 billion while gold reserves were $15.6 billion — the same 12-to-1 ratio. The gold standard had always been a fiction maintained by the convention that not everyone would demand conversion simultaneously. The 1934 law that recalled all gold coins and made conversion impossible for U.S. citizens merely formalized what had always been true in practice.

Lincoln's view on this was unambiguous:

"Money is the creature of law, and the creation of the original issue of money should be maintained as an exclusive monopoly of the National Government. The privilege of creating and issuing money is not only the supreme prerogative of the Government, it is the Government's greatest opportunity." — President Abraham Lincoln

The Supreme Court reinforced this repeatedly — in McCulloch v. Maryland, in the Legal Tender Cases, in the Gold Clause Cases of 1935. The constitutional power to create money belongs to Congress. Congress delegated it. And the delegation, Patman argues, was made on terms that consistently favored private banking interests over the public.

The money supply must be managed — this much was acknowledged even by the Fed officials Patman most opposed. "Money does not manage itself" was the favorite saying of Chairman Martin. Too little money means high interest rates, unemployment, idle capacity, and stunted investment. Too much money overheats the economy past its productive capacity and generates inflation. The question was never whether the money supply should be managed. The question was who should manage it, for whose benefit, under what oversight. Every other argument in the primer is an attempt to answer that question with the specificity it deserves.

Chapter III: How Is Money Created?

Most people have no idea how money is created, and the financial system has worked hard to keep it that way. Patman's charge is blunt: bankers and officials deliberately cloak the system in language that implies the money supply is subject to powerful natural laws beyond human control. This is propaganda in service of private profit. Money is created by people, through deliberate decisions, using mechanisms that can be altered whenever the political will exists to alter them.

The origin story begins with goldsmith bankers in 17th-century Western Europe. Goldsmiths held gold for safekeeping and issued receipts — "claim checks" — that began circulating as money. People traded the receipts without ever withdrawing the gold. The key discovery followed: since depositors rarely all claimed their gold at once, a goldsmith could issue receipts to borrowers for gold he didn't actually hold. He was lending gold he didn't own, earning interest on it, and as long as no one panicked, no one would notice. This is fractional reserve banking. It provided capital for enterprises when governments failed to supply adequate currency, and it fueled rapid economic expansion during the New World settlement era.

The dangers were equally real. If depositors lost confidence and simultaneously demanded their gold, the bank would collapse. Its money would become worthless overnight. Savings would be wiped out, businesses would be forced into bankruptcy, communities would be economically paralyzed. There is a story from Amsterdam of a banker who announced it would be a hanging offense to start a run on a goldsmith — the announcement caused an immediate run, the goldsmith couldn't pay, and his customers hanged him. Patman also notes something more sinister: suspicion that some large goldsmith bankers deliberately precipitated contractions, forcing widespread bankruptcies so they could acquire assets at bargain prices. The structure of fractional reserve banking creates both the risk of accidental collapse and the opportunity for deliberate manipulation.

Modern U.S. banking is a two-layer system. Commercial banks hold the public's checking accounts. The Federal Reserve holds the commercial banks' reserves. When the Fed wants to increase the money supply, it buys government securities in the open market. Here is the exact mechanic: the Fed buys a $1 million bond from a securities dealer. The dealer deposits the Fed's check at his bank, say Chase Manhattan. Chase credits the dealer's account $1 million and sends the check to the New York Fed. The New York Fed credits Chase's reserve account $1 million. Two new deposits now exist — one at Chase (the dealer's checking account), one at the Fed (Chase's reserve balance). The Federal Reserve created money by writing a check.

The multiplier effect is where the power becomes staggering. If the reserve requirement is 10%, a $1,000 injection of new reserves allows the banking system to support $10,000 in total deposits. The receiving bank lends against its new reserves, the borrower deposits the proceeds elsewhere, that bank lends against those deposits, and so on through the system until the full multiplied amount is created. The formula is simple: reserves × (1/reserve requirement) = total deposits. The Fed controls both variables — the amount of reserves via open market operations, and the multiplier via the reserve requirement ratio.

There is a critical political difference between the two methods of expanding the money supply that Patman insists on. If the Fed lowers reserve requirements, all the new money-creating power goes to private commercial banks. They earn interest on every asset those new reserves support. If the Fed instead buys securities in the open market, it creates some of the money directly — and the interest on those Fed-held securities eventually returns to the U.S. Treasury. The distinction determines who profits from money creation. Potentially billions of dollars in annual interest flow differently depending on which method is chosen. This is not a technical footnote. It is the central question of monetary politics.

The textbook explanation of bank reserves is misleading in a way that has political consequences. Standard economics textbooks describe customers depositing cash, banks keeping a fraction as reserves, and lending the rest. This implies banks hold their depositors' money at the Fed — that the reserves represent actual cash entrusted to the central bank. The truth is the opposite. Private banks collectively have never deposited a penny of their own or their depositors' funds at the Federal Reserve. Reserves are created by the Fed, not deposited by banks. The total level of reserves in the system can only change when the Fed acts. Patman visited the New York Fed personally and asked to see the banks' "cash reserves." The officials explained: there are no cash reserves. They are bookkeeping credits. "The cash is just a myth."

The comparison between government money and government bonds is worth dwelling on.

"If our Nation can issue a dollar bond it can issue a dollar bill. The element that makes the bond good makes the bill good also. The difference between the bond and the bill is that the bond lets money brokers collect twice the amount of the bond and an additional 20 percent, whereas the currency pays nobody but those who contribute directly in some useful way." — Thomas A. Edison

Lincoln proved the point during the Civil War. He issued $450 million in U.S. Notes (greenbacks) rather than bonds. Had those been bonds at 5%, the United States would have paid $2.3 billion in interest by 1964 — roughly five times the original amount. The greenbacks were no more inflationary than the equivalent bank deposits that would have been created if bonds had been issued instead. But issuing greenbacks triggered a "political furor" precisely because it threatened the profits of those who earn their living as intermediaries between the government's borrowing and the money supply.

Chapter IV: Why Was the Federal Reserve Act Passed?

The United States had been through this before. The First Bank of the United States lasted until 1811, federally chartered but privately controlled. The Second Bank followed, with the same structure. Andrew Jackson killed the Second Bank in 1836, refusing to renew its charter, and unleashed a long period of near-banking anarchy. The number of state-chartered banks tripled between 1834 and 1861. The National Bank Act of 1863 created federally chartered banks with uniform currency — but tied that currency to the amount of government bonds outstanding rather than to the economy's actual credit needs. The fundamental problem was never solved: the money supply couldn't expand and contract with business activity. When government ran surpluses and retired bonds, the currency supply contracted regardless of economic need. The monetary system was mechanically linked to fiscal decisions rather than to the real economy it was supposed to serve.

Three structural flaws made the pre-1913 system chronically unstable. First, individual banks stood alone — there were no pooled reserves, no lender of last resort. A run on one bank could trigger chain-reaction runs on perfectly solvent neighbors. Second, the money supply was inelastic — tied to gold and bond issuance, not to seasonal credit demand. Every harvest season, Southern and Western country banks withdrew deposits from New York correspondent banks to finance crop movement. New York banks called their overnight "call loans" to the stock market. Stocks crashed. Panic spread. Third, there were no minimum standards — the system was only as strong as its weakest member, with no meaningful supervision against insolvency or fraud.

The Panic of 1907 brought matters to a head — and it was not an accident. It was triggered by a deliberate act of financial warfare between two private groups. The Standard Oil group quietly bought into the copper mining empire of F. Augustus Heinze in Montana, then dumped those holdings at a critical moment, collapsing his Mercantile National Bank of New York. The chain reaction of runs spread from New York across the country. Hundreds of millions in currency were hoarded; legal tender sold at a premium. J. P. Morgan organized the New York Clearing House to stem the panic — and simultaneously extracted a side deal from President Theodore Roosevelt: antitrust immunity to acquire the southern steel industry through U.S. Steel. A financial crisis, deliberately triggered, yielding enormous private gain on both sides.

The seasonal harvest cycle illustrates how the pre-Fed system's rigidity damaged the economy year after year. When cotton and grain harvests arrived each autumn, Southern and Western country banks needed large amounts of currency to pay farmers. Those banks withdrew their deposits from New York correspondent banks. New York banks, suddenly short of funds, called their overnight "call loans" — loans made to stock market speculators, collateralized by stocks, repayable on demand. Stock prices crashed as margin calls swept the market. The panic spread outward from Wall Street to Main Street. And the banks that might have prevented the crisis by using their reserves to pay depositors were actually penalized for doing so — under the existing law, using reserves to pay depositors risked losing their charter. So they closed their doors instead, making panics worse. The system was structured to amplify instability, not absorb it.

The aftermath produced competing visions of reform. Senator Nelson Aldrich headed a National Monetary Commission that published 20 volumes and proposed a privately owned, privately controlled central bank — the bankers' preferred solution. Simultaneously, the House Pujo Committee investigation exposed what it called the "money trust": a network of holding companies and interlocking directorates giving a small Wall Street group control of most major banks nationally. The public demanded a public central bank, not another Wall Street instrument. President Woodrow Wilson and Senator Robert L. Owen championed public control.

"We must have a currency, not rigid as now, but readily elastically responsive to sound credit… Our banking laws must mobilize reserves; must not permit the concentration anywhere in a few hands of the monetary resources of the country… the control of the system of banking and of issue must be public, not private, must be vested in the Government itself." — President Woodrow Wilson

The resulting Federal Reserve Act of 1913 was a compromise. Western and Southern public opinion, stirred by the Pujo findings, demanded decentralization. The result was twelve separate regional Federal Reserve banks joined in a monetary pipeline, rather than a single Wall Street-controlled institution. Private banks got the right to elect directors of the regional banks. The public got a Board of Governors in Washington with ultimate authority. The original design was built around three goals: mobilize scattered reserves into a common pool, provide an elastic currency that expanded with economic need, and enforce minimum standards through a central supervisory body.

The Federal Reserve Act represented a genuine improvement over what preceded it in five ways: centralized reserves at district banks rather than in private hands, a decentralized structure that reduced New York's dominance, dramatically improved check-clearing operations, the possibility of an elastic currency through the discount window, and a common supervisory body. But from the beginning, there was a tension built into the compromise. Private commercial banks elected six of the nine directors of each regional bank. The "public" supervisory authority sat in Washington while the money-creating apparatus was spread across twelve institutions whose leadership the banking industry effectively controlled. The compromise contained the seeds of the "power revolution" Patman would spend the next half-century fighting.

Chapter V: Who Determines the Money Supply?

The Federal Open Market Committee is arguably among the most powerful groups in America. In 1964, it was almost entirely unknown to the public. Its power, Patman writes, rivals the President's in deciding how "the world's greatest economic machine will operate" — yet it answers to no voters, holds no press conferences, and makes no annual accounting to Congress. It is known mainly to major bankers, large financial houses, and trained economists whose professional interests grant them access to this knowledge. The secrecy is not accidental. It is, in part, the point.

The FOMC has twelve voting members in any given year: the seven members of the Board of Governors plus five of the twelve regional Federal Reserve bank presidents. The New York Fed president always votes — always. The other eleven rotate through four slots. Board members serve 14-year terms and cannot be removed by the President except for cause. A president who served two full constitutional terms could, if lucky, appoint a majority of the seven. The five bank presidents who vote are elected, indirectly, by private commercial bankers — not appointed by any public authority. They take a public oath when they join the FOMC, but they take no such oath as bank presidents.

Credit, Patman explains, has two dimensions: availability and price. Interest rates — the price of credit — are famously "sticky"; unlike most prices they don't move weekly with supply and demand. More consequentially, the FOMC often changes availability first, without changing the rate. Banks can be fully "loaned up" — at maximum lending capacity — for extended periods regardless of demand, or they can sit on excess reserves during recoveries. When the Fed eases, banks may continue charging old rates for weeks or months before passing the reduction through. The prime rate, keyed to decisions by a handful of large New York banks, propagates across the country not through market competition but through price leadership.

The original Federal Reserve Act was designed around an entirely different theory of monetary control. Its authors envisioned a passive system: banks would present short-term commercial paper to their regional Fed bank, which would discount it at a set rate, automatically supplying whatever money the economy needed for legitimate business. The discount rate was the only control mechanism the framers anticipated needing. No specific limits were placed on total money creation — the automatic nature of the system was supposed to prevent both excess and shortage. That design was quietly revolutionized over the following two decades.

By the 1920s, individual regional banks were conducting open market operations independently and often at cross-purposes. An informal coordinating committee emerged, dominated by the New York bank. The Banking Act of 1933 recognized the FOMC officially; the Banking Act of 1935 reorganized it to give the Board of Governors majority representation. But here is the "power revolution" Patman identifies: when Congress authorized joint open market operations, it assumed discounting would remain primary. It didn't grasp that the center of gravity had shifted. Open market operations displaced the discount window so completely that by 1963, the Fed earned $1.133 billion from government securities and only $8.9 million from discounts — a ratio of 127 to 1. Control of the money supply had passed from a purely public body to a mixed body with private banking dominance, without anyone explicitly deciding it should.

"Here, then, is the 'power revolution' at the Federal Reserve which destroyed the ingenious compromise of the original legislation. Control of the money supply, with its enormous economic consequences, has passed from a purely public group, composed only of public servants, to a mixed body with dubious qualifications to represent the public interest." — Wright Patman

The Fed influences interest rates through three channels simultaneously. First, it controls the total supply of money available for lending — more reserves means more credit chasing borrowers, pushing rates down; fewer reserves means the opposite. Second, it directly moves government bond prices: buying bonds raises prices and lowers yields; selling bonds lowers prices and raises yields. Third, and most subtly, it shapes expectations — if the Fed signals tight money, lenders demand higher rates now in anticipation of future scarcity, and borrowers accept those higher rates expecting them to rise further. The rate the market sets today reflects not just current supply and demand but the market's reading of where the Fed intends to take things. This expectations channel is why credible Fed communication matters as much as actual policy action.

The Treasury's interest rate-setting process is less "market-determined" than it appears. The Treasury is always the largest single borrower, continuously rolling over maturing debt. It nominally sets its own borrowing rates — but in practice negotiates with a small group of large banks and financial houses who study FOMC attitudes closely and can usually predict future policy. The government weighs heavily on both sides of the bargaining: as the borrower through the Treasury and as the credit supply determinant through the FOMC. The rate that emerges from this process is more the product of negotiation between the government and its bankers than of genuine competitive bidding.

The "open market" where all this happens is not, despite its name, open to anyone. It is the financial district of lower Manhattan. And the Fed is legally required to conduct its operations through a designated set of primary dealers — 21 in 1964, all of them major New York banks or Wall Street securities firms. Every Monday the Treasury notified these 21 dealers of the coming week's borrowing needs. Bids on Tuesday. The 21 dealers always knew who got the prior bid. Marriner Eccles, who chaired the Fed during the New Deal, called this arrangement plainly: "The practical effect of requiring all purchases to be made through the open market is to take money from the taxpayer and give it to these dealers."

Chapter VI: Who Owns the Federal Reserve Banks?

The American Bankers Association's official textbook, Money and Banking, stated flatly: "The member banks own the 12 Federal Reserve banks." Fed officials consistently rejected this characterization. The dispute is not semantic. It concerns whether the Federal Reserve is a public institution managing a public function, or a private institution managing a semi-public function for private benefit. The answer determines how its actions should be judged and whether its independence from democratic oversight is legitimate.

The facts about the "stock" are unambiguous. Under the original Federal Reserve Act, member banks were required to invest 6% of their capital in Federal Reserve stock. That stock cannot be sold, transferred, or pledged as collateral. It cannot be voted in proportion to shares held. It pays a fixed 6% annual dividend — no more, regardless of the Fed's profits. On dissolution, any assets remaining after paying off the stock revert to the U.S. Government, not the member banks. Marriner Eccles, in a 1941 letter to Patman, was direct: "This so-called stock ownership, however, is more in the nature of an enforced subscription to the capital of the Federal Reserve banks than an ownership in the usual sense… Member banks have no right to participate in earnings above the statutory dividend, and upon liquidation any funds remaining after retirement of the stock revert to the Government."

Two subsequent Fed chairmen said the same thing. William McChesney Martin testified in 1956 that the stock conveyed no proprietary interest and that "the banks do not own the Federal Reserve System." Board members Robertson and Shepardson both confirmed that member banks have no ownership claim. Yet the "private ownership" mythology persisted — propagated by banking industry textbooks and used to justify behavior inconsistent with a public institution. Patman found that the Federal Reserve Bank of Chicago had justified certain expenditures inappropriate for a public agency by citing what private businesses do. The confusion about ownership had real operational consequences.

The original rationale for requiring member banks to hold Fed stock has completely evaporated. In 1913, the framers expected the Fed to issue money primarily by discounting eligible commercial paper that member banks presented. Banks were exchanging their private loan obligations for Federal Reserve notes — a potentially risky exchange that required some demonstration of commitment. The stock requirement was a form of collateral. But when open market operations replaced discounting as the primary mechanism, the economic logic for the stock disappeared. The legal confusion remains, and with it a $24 million annual dividend flowing from the public's money-creating machinery to private bank shareholders — for no service rendered in return.

The private ownership mythology creates direct operational harm. The Federal Reserve Bank of Chicago, Patman found, justified expenditures that would be inappropriate for a public agency by citing what private businesses do. Christmas gratuities, boating trips for non-employees, scholarships, and other expenditures were defended on the grounds that the Fed's funds were private, not public. If the Fed's own officials believed the institution was private, they would naturally manage it as if private interests were the reference point — rather than asking, at every decision, what a public agency accountable to taxpayers would do.

A minority in Congress, when the Federal Reserve Act was being written, wanted the Fed's stock sold to the public directly — not given exclusively to member banks. Had that happened, the "ownership" question would be unambiguous: the Fed would function as a publicly traded institution with transparent accountability to its shareholders. Instead, the compromise that gave stock only to member banks created permanent ambiguity — an institution that is public in law, private in the mythology promoted by its own member banks, and genuinely confused about its own nature in practice.

The annual $24 million dividend flowing to member banks from Fed stock is not compensation for any service. It is a subsidy — a payment from the public money-creating machinery to the private institutions that benefit from it. The Fed earned $1.151 billion in 1963; it returned $879 million to the Treasury and paid $28.9 million in dividends to member banks. No law specifies how much the Fed must return to the Treasury or when — the Fed decides how much to spend on "expenses" first, then remits the rest. The dividend is guaranteed by statute; the Treasury remittance is whatever is left over. The structure was designed to protect private bank income at the expense of public revenue.

Chapter VII: The Federal Deposit Insurance Act

The catastrophe of 1929–1933 cannot be overstated in numerical terms. Commercial banks fell from 26,401 in 1928 to 14,771 in 1933 — nearly half the banking system was destroyed. The specific mechanism was a credit machine sustaining a wildly inflating stock market: banks had poured money into brokers' loans, collateralized by the very stocks being speculated on. Those loans grew from $2 billion to over $8 billion during the boom. When the crash came, $4 billion in brokers' loans were called within four months, another $4 billion within three years. The nation's money supply fell by roughly one-third between 1929 and 1933. The Federal Reserve, which had been created precisely to prevent this kind of collapse, allowed it to happen.

Roosevelt's response was swift and sweeping. The emergency banking legislation passed in a single day. The Banking Act of 1933 then made structural changes that have defined American banking since: temporary deposit insurance that became the permanent FDIC; prohibition on paying interest on demand deposits (eliminating cutthroat competition for deposits that had made banks desperate for yield); Federal Reserve power to change reserve requirements; and most consequentially, the Glass-Steagall separation — commercial banks barred from stock market loans, investment banks barred from accepting public deposits. The Securities Exchange Act of 1934 established the SEC and gave the Fed authority to set margin requirements on stock purchases.

The FDIC insured deposits up to $10,000 per account against bank failure. By the end of 1962, 13,455 banks had joined. Since 1933, 445 insured banks had failed, with total deposits of roughly $600 million — and only a few thousand depositors with accounts above $10,000 had lost any money. The insurance worked. But its funding structure contains problems Patman was concerned about. The FDIC reserves stood at $1.40 per $100 of insured deposits. Life insurance companies maintain $20 or more per $100 of potential liabilities — more than thirteen times more conservative. The deposit insurance system that underpins the entire banking system is dramatically undercapitalized relative to other comparable financial guarantees.

The FDIC's role as a banking regulator introduced a different problem. FDIC examiners evaluated loans on the probability of repayment, not on public welfare or economic utility. A loan for patent medicine backed by good collateral was "sound." A loan for a college education without collateral was "unsound." This created a systematic bias toward secured lending to established businesses and against risk-bearing lending to individuals and small enterprises — precisely the kind of lending that generates the most social value. Banking regulation designed primarily to protect the FDIC's balance sheet was not the same as banking regulation designed to serve the broader economy.

The FDIC's funding history contains an embarrassing episode. Its startup capital came from two sources: the Treasury purchased $150 million in FDIC stock, and the Federal Reserve — on Congress's instruction — purchased $139 million. Both investments were repaid in 1947 and 1948, but only at 2% simple interest. Simple interest rather than compound interest represented a significant gift to the banking industry at the expense of taxpayers, since both the Treasury and Fed investments had been outstanding for more than a decade. Patman noted that the 80th Congress's decision to return more than half of the FDIC's gross assessment income back to member banks — steered by Congressman Jesse P. Wolcott — was rewarded by the Eisenhower administration with Wolcott's appointment to the FDIC's board of directors. The regulator was managed, in part, by someone who had legislated in the banking industry's favor.

The FDIC also functions as a market entry barrier that protects existing banks from competition — a role that is rarely discussed as a subsidy but operates as one. It is practically impossible to attract depositors without FDIC insurance. And the FDIC has discretion to refuse insurance to new bank entrants if their entry would "weaken" existing banks. This means the federal deposit insurance system acts as a gatekeeper that preserves existing banking franchises from competitive challenge — not by formal charter restriction, but by controlling access to the insurance that depositors require before they will trust a new institution. The cartel-protection effect is real even if that is not the statute's stated purpose.

FDIC insurance itself functions as a subsidy to the banking system in a less obvious way. Before deposit insurance, Americans held substantial cash at home or in places other than banks — not trusting that deposits were safe. Insurance brought that money into the banking system, increasing the deposit base and thus the money-creating power of commercial banks. Every dollar in additional deposits that FDIC insurance attracted represented additional lending capacity for the banking system, additional interest income, additional profits — none of which flowed back to the public that funded the insurance in the first place. The insurance was funded by taxpayers; the profits from the expanded deposit base went to bank shareholders.

Chapter VIII: How the Fed Gives Away Public Funds

Private commercial banks occupy a unique position in the American economy. They create money at negligible cost — a bookkeeping entry costs essentially nothing — and earn interest on that money. The power to create money was delegated by Congress at no charge. On top of this foundational privilege, the federal government provides banks with additional subsidies that Patman catalogs with increasing indignation through this chapter.

The Federal Reserve's $207 million in gross expenses in 1963 were mostly the cost of providing free services to member banks. Check clearing alone cost scores of millions. The government protects banks from competition through three specific mechanisms: charter gatekeeping (the Comptroller won't issue a new national bank charter if it would "weaken" existing banks), FDIC insurance as a condition of attracting depositors (making new entry practically impossible), and the federal prohibition on paying interest on demand deposits (eliminating competition for deposits and lowering bank costs). Each of these protections translates directly into higher bank profits at public expense.

The interest prohibition on demand deposits deserves special attention. The Banking Act of 1933 made it unlawful for commercial banks to pay interest on checking account balances. The stated rationale was that competition for deposits had pushed banks into excessively risky investments — they needed high yields to cover the interest they were paying depositors. By eliminating deposit interest, Congress removed the competitive pressure for yield. But the effect was to hand banks their raw material — the deposits that are the reserve base for money creation — at zero cost. Banks pay nothing for the deposits they use to create money; they charge interest on every loan made against those deposits. The spread between zero cost of funds and the lending rate is the entirety of banking profit, and it was guaranteed by federal statute at public expense.

The cumulative effect of these overlapping subsidies is a privately chartered system that in practice functions on public resources. The money-creating power is a public franchise delegated at no charge. The reserve infrastructure is publicly created and maintained. The deposit insurance that brings in the raw material is publicly funded. The competition that might have forced banks to pay for deposits is publicly prohibited. The charter oversight that could have produced a more competitive market is used instead to protect incumbents. Patman's description of commercial banks as "highly socialistic institutions" is deliberately provocative — but the underlying point is precise: the industry depends on public resources and public protection while distributing its profits privately.

The 1958 episode is the clearest illustration of how these subsidies work in practice. Early in 1958, the Fed lowered reserve requirements to combat a recession, enabling private banks to create approximately $10 billion in new money-creating power. The stated purpose was to stimulate business lending. Business loans actually fell. The banks used the new power to buy $10 billion of Government securities instead — earning risk-free interest income on money created at public expense, rather than making the productive loans the policy was intended to encourage. The Fed could have created this $10 billion itself; the interest would have returned to the Treasury. Instead, it went to bank profits. Almost one-quarter of the $10 billion went to just 18 big New York City banks. Two percent of American banks received three-quarters of the giveaway.

The vault cash bill of 1959 was even more brazen. The American Bankers Association proposed transferring $16.8 billion in Government securities from the Federal Reserve to private banks. The mechanism: lower reserve requirements, releasing $9.8 billion in reserves that banks would use to buy bonds from the Fed; plus an additional $7 billion in new Fed security acquisitions that would also flow to private banks. The ABA's own plan would have let private banks create $12 of new money for every $1 created by the Federal Reserve. The Fed's Board of Governors endorsed the bankers' plan. So did the Comptroller. The bill passed in modified form, allowing $2.6 billion of vault cash to count as reserves — equivalent to a reserve requirement reduction. Banks created roughly $15 billion in new deposits and bought Government securities. Patman's proposed remedy: transfer $15 billion of Government securities from Fed to Treasury, cancel the debt, cut annual interest payments by over half a billion dollars.

The asymmetry is stark. Under Policy "A" (low reserves, low reserve requirement, as actually practiced), private banks hold $180 billion in interest-bearing government assets for every $20 billion the Fed holds. Under Policy "B" (higher reserves, higher requirement), the Fed holds $40 billion and banks hold $160 billion — same total money supply, very different distribution of who earns interest. The choice between these policies transfers tens of billions of dollars of government interest payments between the public and private sectors. The Fed has consistently chosen the policy that maximizes private bank income. Patman has asked high experts for one plausible reason this arrangement should exist. He received none.

Chapter IX: What Is Monetary Policy?

Monetary policy is either passive or active. A passive policy means rules — money supply grows at 3% annually, tied to some economic metric — and no day-to-day discretion. An active policy means the central bank deliberately adjusts the money supply to influence economic conditions. The United States has used active monetary policy since the 1920s. This chapter is Patman's account of what that actually means in practice — and his verdict is damning.

The first and most important insight is asymmetry. Tight money can always choke an expansion. Easy money cannot reliably revive a depression. During a genuine economic collapse, business prospects are so bleak that firms won't borrow even at near-zero rates; banks won't lend to risky borrowers regardless of reserve availability. The Fed can pull the credit string tight — it cannot push a depressed economy into borrowing and spending. The 1930s demonstrated this conclusively: enormous excess reserves sat unused throughout the decade because neither the supply nor demand sides of credit were functional. Keynes called it a "liquidity trap." Patman calls it "pushing on a string."

The 1936–37 blunder is the canonical example of the asymmetry's other implication: easy money can't fix a depression, but tight money can make one worse. Veterans' soldiers' bonus certificates were paid out in August 1936 — several billion dollars of purchasing power injected into an economy still carrying 17% unemployment. Patman had advocated paying the bonus immediately during the Depression itself. The Fed, anticipating "inflationary" pressure from the bonus payments, raised reserve requirements in June 1936, reducing the money supply by almost the exact amount veterans received. The economy plunged into the 1937–38 relapse. Excess reserves had been large throughout 1935–39, yet the economy hadn't recovered. The lesson was precise: the failure to recover was not caused by insufficient reserves; the 1937 collapse was caused by the Fed's deliberate tightening. The bank claimed responsibility for the recovery and evaded responsibility for the relapse.

The postwar record under active policy is a sequence of self-inflicted wounds. Tight money in 1955 cooled consumer durable buying. Tight money in spring–summer 1957 restrained business investment and produced the recession of July 1957 through August 1958. Tight money in late 1959 and early 1960 produced the recession of May 1960 through February 1961. In both cases the Fed tightened based on inflation concerns that the data did not support — the industrial wholesale price index showed essentially no inflation since 1958 through 1964, while unemployment ranged from 4.3% to 6.7%.

The bills-only policy, formally adopted in 1952 and kept secret from the public until 1954, deserves special attention. For eight years, the FOMC confined its open market operations to very short-term Treasury bills. The stated rationale: letting the market freely determine long-term interest rates. The contradiction was immediate: the Fed's targets — business investment, housing construction, mortgage lending — respond to long-term rates. Bills-only could only directly affect short-term rates. The policy was maximally ineffective for its own stated goals by design.

The 1957–58 cycle showed the absurdity in practice. In 1957, the Fed tried to dampen an investment boom using bills-only — rates rose broadly, thousands of small firms were bankrupted, unable to carry inventories. Yet the long-term investment boom it targeted had already staggered by early 1957 on its own, and collapsed. In 1958, the Fed reversed course and tried to stimulate. It injected reserves; short-term rates fell. Long-term rates barely moved. A speculative binge developed: elevator operators, used car dealers, and professionals borrowed short-term to buy long-term government bonds. When the Fed reversed again in mid-1958, billions of high-riding dollars were lost overnight. Bills-only was finally abandoned in February 1961 after Kennedy took office — replaced by a policy of deliberately managing the entire yield curve rather than just its short end.

The 13-year postaccord verdict is quantified. Since 1951, interest rates had climbed steadily with only slight interruptions. By fiscal 1963, the government was paying roughly $10 billion annually in interest. If the 1940s interest rate average of 1.8% had prevailed through 1963, the government would have saved $40.1 billion total — about 13% of the entire national debt at that time. Those who denounced government deficits the loudest, Patman notes, also supported the high interest rates that generated most of the deficit. The contradiction is perfect: raising interest rates to fight "inflationary" deficits actually enlarges the deficit by increasing debt service costs. The policy defeats its own stated purpose while serving an unstated one — the income of the financial sector.

Two broad conclusions follow. First, active independent monetary policy is a costly luxury: an agency reading its own economic data and acting unilaterally uses a monetary sledgehammer to crack economic policy walnuts. Second, self-sufficient central banks structurally drift toward high-interest policy — not from malice but from institutional logic. Inflation is the only economic ailment directly susceptible to monetary cure. Unemployment, stagnation, and recession cannot be laid uniquely at the central bank's door. So the bank concentrates on the one problem it can claim to solve — inflation — using the one tool it has — keeping rates high and the economy somewhat sluggish. As Patman puts it: "If honorable men look for inflation hard enough, they can convince themselves they have found it. Most of us then pay the price of being preserved from a monster which the evidence suggests is a mere phantom."

Chapter X: What Improvements Are Needed?

The monetary system is man-made. It is "the creature of law," as Lincoln said — and that means it can be changed. The specific changes Patman proposes are not radical departures from the system's founding principles; they are, in his reading, a restoration of those principles after decades of quiet erosion. Monetary reform has historically happened only during crises, when public attention is finally focused enough to overwhelm bankers' resistance. Between crises, the system becomes mysterious and uninteresting to most legislators, allowing partial reversals of earlier reforms. The goal of the primer is to build public understanding sturdy enough to survive the intervals between crises.

The independence argument that the Fed and financial press defend so vigorously collapses under examination. The Fed's version of independence means producing its own economic diagnosis, setting its own priority list, and acting on its own conclusions — with no obligation to support executive or congressional policy. The inevitable result is policy collision. If the Fed's priority schedule differs from the President's, it is sheer luck if they aren't simultaneously pressing the accelerator and the brake. President Kennedy cut taxes by $11 billion to sustain economic expansion — and then had to ask the Federal Reserve not to nullify the tax cut's effects with tight money. Should an elected president have to beg a congressionally created body to support his economic program?

The inflation alibi that justifies this independence does not survive scrutiny of the historical record. The only genuine inflations of the past 25 years in Patman's 1964 accounting — during World War II and the early Korean War period — were caused by wartime demand that could only have been controlled by higher taxes, price controls, or rationing. Not by tight money. The financial community had never pushed for higher taxes to absorb purchasing power. It had never defended the wartime price and rationing controls that actually held inflation in check. It had not suggested raising reserve requirements during the war. Yet it claimed constant vigilance against inflation as the justification for an independence that allowed it to keep interest rates high in peacetime — enriching creditors and calling it prudence.

The accountability gap is real and specific. The Fed does not present an annual report to Congress explaining or justifying its policy. It does not seek congressional approval. It provides its own revenues from interest on government bond holdings, so it escapes the appropriations process entirely. It is not subject to executive review. It resists GAO audit, arguing that monetary policy is above ordinary law. It is not elected. Some officials have described their role using "quasi-judicial" language — they are deliberating, not governing. Patman calls this the "fourth branch of government" argument and rejects it entirely. Congress, the President, the courts — all exercise wide economic power and all are accountable. The Fed exercises equivalent power and is not.

The independence argument decoded: Fed Chairman William McChesney Martin argued the Fed must be insulated from "shortsighted pressures for abuse of that money." New York Fed president Alfred Hayes argued for freedom from "partisan influences." Patman's translation is careful and fair: "unpopular action" means tight money that hurts many people. "Prevent abuse" means prevent inflation. "Shortsighted pressures" means the preferences of elected representatives and the voters who elected them. The Fed is asking the public to cede monetary control to unelected trustees who will do for the people what the people would not do for themselves. This is, as Patman says, "papa knows best" thinking — utterly alien to American democratic principles.

The argument is not merely Patman's. Milton Friedman of the University of Chicago, who was advising Senator Goldwater at the time and could not be dismissed as a left-wing critic, testified in 1964 that a truly "independent" monetary authority — "a fourth branch of the constitutional structure" — was "most undesirable politically" because it gave enormous power to individuals not subject to close electoral control. Paul Samuelson of MIT, economic adviser to President Kennedy, said that a central bank not responsible "is irresponsible rather than independent." Left and right agreed: unaccountable monetary authority is constitutionally indefensible.

The legal argument is the most explosive. The Federal Reserve Act of 1913 was designed on the full convertibility theory — automatically supply whatever currency the economy needed, restrict nothing. The Board could review and set discount rates. That was it. After the Banking Act of 1935, Fed officials began proclaiming "responsibility" for national monetary policies — but the 1935 act contained no such provision and referenced no change to the convertibility concept. The Fed's "monetary policies as practiced today were never authorized by law." The monetary powers are reserved to Congress by the Constitution. Congress can delegate them, but the Supreme Court has held unconstitutional grants of power without adequate legal definition of purposes — just as it struck down the NRA in the 1930s. If legally challenged, Patman argues, the Fed's discretionary monetary policies could be held unconstitutional. The Fed is governing without authorization.

The Reform Agenda

The subcommittee's formal reform proposals, signed by all eight Democratic members, cover every dimension of the system Patman has indicted. On the Fed's public character: retire the Federal Reserve stock, vest all open market operations power in the Board of Governors (removing bank president votes), make the Chairman's term coterminous with the President's, reduce Governors from seven to five with five-year renewable terms, and require only that appointees be "men of integrity devoted to the public interest" rather than representatives of specific sectoral interests.

On public control of expenditures: require a public audit by the Comptroller General of all Fed expenditures, require all capital gains and interest from government securities to flow to the Treasury, and fund Fed expenses through congressional appropriations rather than the current arrangement where the Fed funds itself from interest income. This last reform would bring the Fed under the normal budget process for the first time.

On monetary-fiscal coordination: require the President to set forth monetary policy guidelines in his Economic Reports, and establish a mechanism by which the Fed must file a formal statement of reasons if it diverges from presidential monetary policy — symmetrical to the President's Economic Report. This stops well short of making the Fed subordinate; it requires only transparency and explanation.

On the structural bias in money creation: the current ratio of private bank money-creating power to Federal Reserve money-creating power is roughly 7 to 1. Patman proposes gradually shifting this toward 4 to 1 by raising reserve requirements back to 1953 levels. This would reduce private bank income from government securities and return more of it to the Treasury, without eliminating the private banking system or its role in credit allocation. The discount window should be revitalized as a right rather than a privilege, funneling reserves through regional banks according to actual local credit demand rather than through 21 New York dealers regardless of where credit is needed.

The guiding principle underlying all proposals: commercial banks are quasi-public institutions. They "live off the Government," using its money-creating power at no cost and receiving subsidies from the public purse. The only justification for extending that power to private banks is that they provide a necessary public service — making credit judgments and bearing risk that government cannot do on a non-arbitrary basis. When banks use their money-creating power primarily to lend to the Government rather than to businesses and consumers, that justification disappears. A commercial bank that is, in effect, just a government bond fund with a charter has no claim to its privileges.

Trading & Investing Lessons

The Fed Put Runs on Private Sector Logic

Patman's exposure of how reserve requirement changes create windfall gains for private banks has a direct parallel in markets. When the Fed signals accommodation — lower rates, QE, reserve requirement cuts — the primary beneficiaries are financial sector firms. Banks, insurance companies, and asset managers see earnings power expand before the "stimulus" reaches the real economy. Trading the financial sector long on early Fed accommodation signals, and understanding why they outperform, requires understanding the mechanism Patman describes: private banks capture the first-mover advantage on every new reserve dollar created.

Interest Rate Policy Is an Income Transfer — Trade the Directionality

Rising interest rates transfer income from borrowers (households, small businesses, capital-intensive industries) to creditors (banks, insurance companies, holders of fixed-income assets). Falling rates do the opposite. This is not a side effect — it is the mechanism. An investor who understands this trades rate environments directionally: short regional banks and homebuilders when the Fed is in sustained tightening mode, long financials when rates are rising and the credit transmission is working. Patman's income transfer framing is a clean mental model for sector rotation around rate cycles.

The Multiplier Creates Leverage Risk in the System

The money multiplier means that the banking system is permanently leveraged — $1 in reserves supports $10 or more in deposits. This leverage is not a bug; it is the system's design. But it means that reserve contractions cascade: a 10% reduction in reserves forces a much larger contraction in deposits and credit. The 1929–1933 period showed this with catastrophic clarity — a one-third collapse in the money supply destroyed half the banking system. For investors, the lesson is that credit-driven asset booms are inherently fragile at the reserve margin. Watching reserve levels and FOMC posture for signs of reversal is early warning before loan books show stress.

Central Banks Are Not Neutral — Know Their Structural Bias

Patman's insight that self-sufficient central banks structurally drift toward high-interest policy is actionable. The Fed's institutional incentives favor the one problem it can unambiguously claim to solve: inflation. Employment, growth, and distribution are politically contested; inflation is not. So when the Fed has discretion, it tends to tighten more than the data warrant, and to hold tight longer than necessary. Investors who understand this structural bias buy duration earlier than the market consensus — because they know the Fed will eventually relent, and that relenting is usually delayed.

Who Benefits From a Policy Change Is the First Question

The 1958 $10 billion reserve release went almost entirely to 18 New York banks, which used it to buy government securities — not to make business loans. The policy's stated purpose was economic stimulus; its actual effect was bank income enhancement. Before trading any Fed policy action, ask: who actually captures the benefit, through what mechanism, and on what timeline? Stated policy goals and actual distributional outcomes diverge regularly. Patman showed that the institutional structure of the banking system determines where new reserves flow — and that flow rarely matches the public rationale.

The Yield Curve Is a Policy Instrument, Not Just a Market Signal

The bills-only policy episode is a case study in what happens when a central bank confuses the tool with the target. The Fed wanted to reduce long-term investment; it could only directly affect short-term rates. For eight years it used the wrong lever. When Kennedy forced the abandonment of bills-only, the Fed regained control of the entire curve — and immediately solved the balance-of-payments problem without the domestic economic damage that raising short rates would have caused. Investors who watch where the Fed is operating on the curve, not just whether rates are up or down, have more information about policy transmission than those focused only on the fed funds rate.

Key Takeaways

Money Is a Legal Construct, Not a Natural Resource

The foundational insight of the primer. Money exists because courts enforce debts denominated in it. Its value is the value of everything that productive capacity can produce — not gold, not government promises, not paper quality. Understanding this unlocks every subsequent argument about who should create money, how much should exist, and at what price.

Banks Create Money by Making Loans

When a bank makes a loan, it credits the borrower's account — creating a new deposit, new money. It does not lend out existing depositors' money. The constraint on this money creation is the reserve requirement set by the Fed. About 80% of the money supply in 1964 was created this way, at negligible cost to the banks doing the creating.

The Fed Creates Reserves by Writing Checks

Federal Reserve reserves are not deposits from private banks — they are created by the Fed itself when it buys government securities. The total level of reserves in the banking system can only change when the Fed acts. This is not a technicality; it determines who profits from the money-creation process.

The Choice of How to Create Money Determines Who Profits

Lowering reserve requirements gives all new money-creating power to private banks. Having the Fed buy securities creates money at the Fed level, with interest flowing back to the Treasury. Same money supply outcome, vastly different income distribution. The Fed systematically chooses the method that maximizes private bank income.

The FOMC Is Not a Public Body

Five of its twelve voting members are elected, indirectly, by private commercial banks. They take no oath as bank presidents. The banking industry's views on monetary policy are structurally represented in the body that makes monetary policy. This is not democratic governance.

Tight Money Systematically Transfers Income to Creditors

High interest rates increase income for holders of financial assets (banks, insurance companies, wealthy families) and increase costs for everyone who borrows (households, small businesses, the government). The Fed's sustained postwar preference for high rates was not neutral. It was a sustained income transfer from borrowers to creditors.

Central Banks Structurally Bias Toward Fighting Inflation

Because inflation is the only economic ailment for which the central bank can claim unique responsibility, unaccountable central banks will find inflation even when the data don't show it, and will maintain restrictive policies long after inflation threats have passed.

The $10B Giveaway and Vault Cash Bill Show Intent

The 1958 reserve release and the 1959 ABA vault cash bill were not accidents of policy. They were deliberate transfers of public assets — money-creating power — to private banking institutions, with the endorsement of the banking regulators whose job was to serve the public.

Fed Independence Was Never Legally Authorized

The 1913 Federal Reserve Act did not authorize discretionary monetary policy. It authorized automatic money supply tied to commercial credit demand. The Fed claimed responsibility for monetary policy after 1935 without explicit legislative authority. That power claim was never properly tested in court.

Democratic Accountability Is Not Optional

Both Milton Friedman and Paul Samuelson agreed in 1964: a monetary authority not subject to electoral accountability is not independent — it is irresponsible. The question of who controls money creation is the question of who controls economic life. It must be answerable to the people through their elected representatives.

Contrarian Ideas

Banks Don't Hold Your Deposits at the Fed

Conventional belief: Banks accept deposits and keep a fraction at the Fed as required reserves, lending the rest.
Patman's counter: Private banks have never deposited a penny of their own or their customers' money at the Federal Reserve. Reserves are created by the Fed, not deposited by banks. When a Fed official confirmed this to Patman at the New York Fed — "the cash is just a myth" — it contradicted what every standard economics textbook was teaching. The political consequence: bankers who believed the textbook myth were lobbying to be paid interest on reserves they thought they had "deposited" — a demand based on a false premise.

The Gold Standard Was Always a Fiction

Conventional belief: The U.S. was on the gold standard until FDR took it off in 1934.
Patman's counter: Money in existence was twelve times the monetary gold supply in 1914 — and twelve times again in 1963. Full gold convertibility was always technically impossible; it was maintained by the convention that not everyone would demand gold at once. Going off the gold standard formalized what was already true. The dollar was always backed by productive capacity, not by gold.

The 1951 Treasury-Fed Accord Solved a Problem That Didn't Exist

Conventional belief: The Fed's pre-1951 policy of pegging bond rates was inflationary, and the Accord was a necessary liberation.
Patman's counter (documented by Alvin Hansen's data): Fed holdings of government securities were actually $5.1 billion lower in June 1950 than in December 1946. The money supply was essentially flat. Wholesale prices in June 1950 were the same as September 1947. The price surge following Korea peaked a month before the Accord — and stopped before the policy change could have caused it. The Accord liberated the Fed from a non-existent inflation problem and handed it powers that added $40 billion in excess interest costs to the national debt over the following 17 years.

Easy Money Cannot Stimulate a True Depression

Conventional belief: Monetary easing is the cure for recession and depression.
Patman's counter: Monetary policy is asymmetrical. Tight money can always stop a boom. Easy money cannot start an economy when business prospects are too bleak to borrow and banks are too frightened to lend. The 1930s excess reserves sat unused throughout the decade. Stimulus requires fiscal intervention — spending and tax cuts — not just money supply expansion.

Raising Interest Rates Enlarges the Deficit It Claims to Fight

Conventional belief: Tight money controls inflation and reduces government deficits by restraining spending.
Patman's counter: Higher interest rates increase the cost of carrying the national debt — by 1963, approximately $5 billion annually in excess cost relative to 1940s rates. Those who simultaneously denounced deficits and supported high interest rates were making the deficit larger while claiming to shrink it. The policy was internally incoherent and served creditor interests regardless of that incoherence.

Proposals & Frameworks

The Money Creation Accounting Framework

Patman's formula: A × B = C, where A is total reserves, B is deposits permitted per dollar of reserves (1/reserve requirement ratio), and C is total bank deposits. The Fed controls A through open market operations and B through reserve requirement changes. This is the complete analytical framework for understanding money supply dynamics — deceptively simple, consistently ignored in public discourse.

Policy A vs. Policy B: The Distribution Framework

Two policies that produce identical money supplies have radically different distributional effects. Policy A (low reserves, low requirements): private banks hold more government securities, earn more interest income. Policy B (high reserves, high requirements): the Fed holds more securities, interest flows to Treasury. The choice between them transfers billions of dollars per year between public and private sectors. Patman uses this framework to show that monetary policy is always redistributive — it never has a "neutral" distributional option.

The Structural Bias Diagnosis

Patman proposes a systematic framework for understanding why independent central banks consistently produce high-interest outcomes: (1) central banks can only unambiguously claim responsibility for fighting inflation; (2) unemployment, stagnation, and growth shortfalls are politically contested and cannot be laid uniquely at the central bank's door; (3) therefore, unaccountable central banks concentrate on the one problem they can clearly own — inflation — and use the one tool they have — restricting credit; (4) the result is a permanent high-interest bias that serves creditor interests regardless of economic conditions. This framework predicts behavior without requiring conspiracy — it emerges from institutional incentives alone.

The Seven-Point Reform Framework

Patman's reform agenda addresses every layer of the system: (1) eliminate private ownership fiction by retiring Fed stock; (2) return FOMC to purely public membership; (3) synchronize Chairman's term with President's; (4) require GAO audit and congressional appropriations; (5) establish monetary policy guidelines in Presidential Economic Reports; (6) shift money-creation ratio from 7:1 to 4:1 (private:public); (7) revitalize the discount window to fund reserves through regional banks rather than 21 New York dealers. Each reform addresses a specific mechanism of private capture, not just the general principle.

What Didn't Fit

The 1920 Credit Contraction

One of the most revealing episodes in early Fed history came in 1920, when the Federal Reserve informally coerced banks to call in loans — not through formal policy but through a "voluntary conspiratorial credit contraction" that helped trigger the 1920–21 depression. The largest credit volume before World War II had been reached in 1920; the Fed then deliberately contracted it. This episode predates the formal development of "monetary policy" as a concept — the Fed was making monetary policy before it had legal authority to do so, and it did so by causing a depression. Patman uses this to establish that the Fed's power grab was neither accidental nor new.

The Bills-Only Bond Speculation Debacle

The 1958 attempt to stimulate through easy money under the bills-only framework produced one of the more tragicomic episodes in Fed history. As the Fed injected reserves and short-term rates fell, long-term bond prices rose gently enough to attract speculators. Elevator operators, used car dealers, and professionals across the country borrowed short-term at low rates to buy long-term government bonds at rising prices. When the Fed reversed course in mid-1958, those bonds dropped sharply — "billions of high-riding dollars were lost." The people who lost money were the same ordinary citizens the policy was supposedly designed to help. The Fed's operational restriction (bills-only) had created a speculative dynamic in long-term bonds, reversed it without warning, and the losses fell on retail participants rather than the institutional sector that understood the policy mechanics.

Morgan's Antitrust Deal During the Panic of 1907

The political economy of the Panic of 1907 is richer than the standard "J.P. Morgan saves the day" narrative. After organizing the New York Clearing House to stop the bank runs, Morgan separately approached President Roosevelt with a deal: U.S. Steel would be allowed to acquire the Tennessee Coal, Iron and Railroad Company — a Birmingham-centered steel empire — in exchange for Morgan's continued rescue effort. Roosevelt granted the antitrust exemption. Morgan was simultaneously performing a public service and extracting private gain from the public's desperate position. The parallel to later financial crisis politics — too-big-to-fail institutions extracting regulatory concessions as the price of stability — is direct.

Treasury Leaving $4 Billion in Free Deposits at Private Banks

Among the quieter subsidies Patman documents is the Treasury's practice of leaving an average of $4 billion in deposits with private banks interest-free — ostensibly as compensation for banking services rendered. This arrangement forces the federal debt to be higher than necessary, because the government must borrow more than it would need if it held its own funds. Taxpayers then pay interest on that excess debt, while the banks lend those same Treasury deposits to others at interest. The arrangement amounts to a double payment: services rendered are compensated once through free deposits and a second time through the interest income those deposits generate. Patman introduced legislation to replace free deposits with direct fee payments, but the reform never passed.

Patman's Personal Visit to the New York Fed

When Patman heard testimony from Robert V. Roosa of the New York Fed that bank reserves were bookkeeping entries rather than cash deposits, he didn't take it on faith — he went to see for himself. He visited the Federal Reserve Bank of New York and asked to see the $28 billion in government securities held there. They exist, he confirmed, as physical bonds in a vault. Then he asked to see the cash reserves that member banks had "deposited" at the Fed. The officials explained that there are no cash reserves; they are bookkeeping credits created by the Fed. He visited the Federal Reserve Bank of Richmond and received the same answer. The personal verification tour had a rhetorical purpose as much as an informational one: Patman wanted to be able to say that he had seen with his own eyes what officials claimed, and to report to Congress what they told him directly. The cash reserve is a myth.

A congressional document rather than a commercial book, written to explain the American monetary system to non-specialists and to build the public understanding necessary for meaningful democratic oversight of monetary policy. Simultaneously a textbook on money mechanics and a political indictment of the Federal Reserve, the private banking system, and the erosion of congressional control over money creation. Remains one of the clearest plain-language accounts of how the banking system actually works — and who benefits from it.

About Wright Patman

Wright Patman (1893–1976) represented the First Congressional District of Texas for 47 years, from 1929 until his death in office. A product of East Texas poverty, he never lost the populist conviction that the monetary system was rigged against ordinary Americans by a financial elite that had captured both the Federal Reserve and the regulatory apparatus surrounding it. He was the House author of the Employment Act of 1946, which established the federal government's responsibility for maximum employment and production. He chaired the Joint Economic Committee, the House Small Business Committee, and eventually the House Banking and Currency Committee — positions he used to conduct relentless investigations of Federal Reserve operations, bank holding companies, and tax-exempt foundations.

Patman was not a credentialed economist — a fact his opponents used against him regularly. But his understanding of banking mechanics was detailed and practical in ways that formal economists sometimes missed. He knew, from personal investigation, that bank reserves were bookkeeping entries rather than cash deposits — something that standard textbooks of his era were still getting wrong. He knew the precise numbers: how much the 1958 reserve release had transferred to 18 New York banks, how much excess interest the nation had paid since 1951, how the 21 primary dealers extracted profit from every government security transaction. He was tireless, sometimes excessive, always specific. Even Harvard's Seymour Harris, who endorsed the primer, noted that Patman sometimes overstated — but that his opponents were powerful and disciplined, and that someone had to keep them honest.

Patman died before the deregulation wave of the 1980s transformed the banking system he spent his career scrutinizing. But his analysis — that the Federal Reserve systematically favors financial interests, that money creation is a public function being performed for private profit, and that democratic accountability of monetary policy is not optional — runs through every subsequent debate about the Fed's role, from Volcker's 1980 rate shock to the QE programs of the 2010s. The primer he wrote in 1964 to educate Congress remains one of the most honest accounts of how the system works that has ever been written by someone in a position to investigate it.

Why This Book Matters

Most discussions of Federal Reserve independence treat it as a settled question — a technical necessity that sophisticated democracies simply accept. Patman's primer is a sustained challenge to that settlement, written at the precise moment it was being consolidated. The Treasury-Fed Accord of 1951 had just given the Fed its independence. The bills-only fiasco had just ended. The 1958 and 1959 giveaways to private banks had just happened. Patman wrote from inside the congressional oversight process, with access to testimony, data, and the officials themselves — and his conclusion was that the Fed had acquired power it was never legally given, was exercising it in ways that consistently served private banking interests, and was using the language of technical independence to immunize itself from democratic accountability.

The debates Patman was conducting in 1964 are the same debates being conducted today. Who benefits from quantitative easing? Does Fed independence serve price stability or private banking interests? Should the Fed's mandate explicitly include employment and distributional outcomes, or only inflation? Is the FOMC's composition appropriately public? These questions were not resolved when Patman wrote; they were not resolved when he died; they remain unresolved now. Reading the primer is a reminder that the institutional arrangements we treat as natural and permanent were designed by people, contested by people, and can be redesigned by people — if they understand them well enough to demand it.

The primer also stands as evidence of what congressional oversight can produce when it is serious. Patman didn't just complain; he documented. He visited the New York Fed. He read the balance sheets. He compared the data from 1946 to 1963. He asked the officials to explain their own systems and reported what they said. The result is a document that has outlasted most formal academic treatments of the same period precisely because it is grounded in specifics rather than theory. It pairs naturally with The Creature from Jekyll Island for the historical narrative, with Seven Deadly Innocent Frauds for the contemporary monetary theory, and with A History of Money and Banking for the longer arc of American financial politics.
