# A Primer on Money — Summary

**Author:** Wright Patman (Chairman, Subcommittee on Domestic Finance)
**Published:** August 5, 1964
**Publisher:** U.S. Government Printing Office
**Pages:** ~141 (plus index)

## What It Is

A congressional document — not an academic treatise — written by
Rep. Wright Patman of Texas to explain the American monetary system
to a general audience. Patman was Chairman of the House Subcommittee
on Domestic Finance and spent forty years fighting for public
understanding of banking. The primer is simultaneously a textbook
on money mechanics and a political indictment of the Federal Reserve
and the private banking system.

## Core Argument

Money is a manufactured item, not a natural resource. Its creation is
a power granted by the Constitution exclusively to Congress. Congress
has delegated that power — first to the Federal Reserve, then to
private commercial banks — and the delegation has been progressively
captured by private banking interests. The result is a system in
which private banks create money at negligible cost, earn interest
on it, receive massive government subsidies, and face no meaningful
democratic accountability. The Federal Reserve, meanwhile, has
transformed from a passive money supplier into an active economic
policymaker — without legal authorization and without democratic
control — and systematically pursues high-interest policies that
transfer income from ordinary citizens to the financial sector.

## Key Concepts

- **Fractional reserve banking**: Banks create deposits by making
  loans, holding only a fraction in reserve. A $1,000 reserve
  injection can produce $10,000 in new deposits (10% reserve ratio).
- **Open market operations**: The Fed creates/destroys reserves by
  buying/selling government securities through 21 private dealers.
  This replaced the discount window as the primary policy tool and
  shifted power from public Board members to bank-selected regional
  presidents.
- **The money multiplier**: Fed sets the ceiling on total deposits
  via reserve requirements; banks decide how much money is actually
  created within that ceiling.
- **Pen-and-ink money**: Bank-created checkbook deposits, now ~80%
  of the money supply — created by bookkeeping entry, not printing.

## The Political Indictment

1. **Private capture**: The FOMC includes five regional bank
   presidents chosen by private bankers, giving private interests
   votes on national monetary policy.
2. **The 1958 giveaway**: The Fed lowered reserve requirements,
   enabling banks to create $10 billion — but they used it to buy
   government bonds, not make business loans. Nearly a quarter went
   to 18 big NYC banks.
3. **The vault cash bill (1959)**: The ABA lobbied to transfer
   $16.8 billion in government securities from the Fed (public) to
   private banks. The Fed's own Board endorsed it.
4. **Interest subsidy**: High postwar interest rates added ~$5B/year
   to debt servicing costs — $131/year for an average family of five.
5. **The "bills only" disaster**: The Fed restricted itself to
   short-term securities for 8 years, rendering monetary policy
   unable to affect the long-term rates it was targeting.

## Historic Episodes

- Goldsmith bankers invented fractional reserve in 17th-century
  Europe; an Amsterdam banker was hanged after a customer caused a run.
- Colonial money suppression by George III was, historians argue,
  one of the major causes of the Revolution.
- The Panic of 1907 was deliberately triggered by the Standard Oil
  group destroying a copper rival — J.P. Morgan then extorted an
  antitrust exemption from Roosevelt as the price of his rescue.
- The 1936-37 blunder: the Fed raised reserve requirements to offset
  the soldiers' bonus, shrinking the money supply at 17% unemployment
  and causing the 1937-38 relapse.
- The 1951 Treasury-Fed Accord was later mythologized as a
  liberation from inflationary policy — the actual data showed prices
  had peaked a month before the accord.

## Reform Proposals

Patman proposed abolishing the FOMC (returning powers to the public
Board of Governors), making the Fed Chairman's term coterminous with
the President's, requiring GAO audits, shifting money-creation ratios
from 7:1 (private:public) to 4:1, revitalizing the discount window,
and opening the "open market" to genuine public participation.

## Who Should Read This

Essential reading for anyone interested in monetary theory, the
history of the Federal Reserve, or the politics of money. The
analysis remains startlingly current — the debates about Fed
independence, quantitative easing, and who benefits from monetary
policy are exactly the debates Patman was conducting in 1964.
Pairs with *When Money Dies*, *A History of Money and Banking*,
and *The Creature from Jekyll Island*.
