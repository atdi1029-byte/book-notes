# A Primer on Money — Wright Patman (1964)
*Subcommittee on Domestic Finance, Committee on Banking
and Currency, House of Representatives, 88th Congress, 2d Session*

---

## Prefatory Material

### "The Patman Crusade" — Seymour E. Harris (Harvard, Littauer Professor)

- **Wright Patman** (Texas, Chair) spent 40 years fighting for public
  understanding of the monetary system; this primer emerged from
  his personal notes
- Core constitutional claim: power to create money belongs to
  **Congress alone**, not the Fed or commercial banks — Congress
  has farmed this power out
- 1959 legislation put billions in additional reserves at banks'
  disposal; impulse came from the **American Bankers Association**
  — each reserve dollar yields $5 of additional business for banks
  at no cost to them; profits flow to banks, not Treasury
- Patman's central critique of the **Federal Reserve Board**: no
  inherent right to independence; "independence from popular
  pressure" as justification applies equally to tax policy — not a
  valid special claim
- **FOMC structure problem**: 12 regional bank presidents all
  participate in discussion; only 5 can vote — but 12 presidents
  (elected by privately-owned commercial banks) dominate the
  "discussion" committee alongside 7 public Board members
- Fed often favors higher interest rates than the economy needs,
  even when Patman wants reflationary policies
- Harris's verdict: Patman sometimes exaggerates, but he keeps
  finance men on their toes; opponents are "powerful, numerous,
  and well organized, and often do not distinguish the financial
  from the general interest"

## Introduction (pp. 1–5)

### Core Thesis: Money Is Manufactured

- **Money is a manufactured item** — its supply is determined by its
  manufacturers (the banking system), not by natural forces
- The **money supply** is one of the 2–3 most important influences
  on business activity, incomes, prices, and economic growth
- Under the Constitution, creating money is the **right and duty
  of Congress** — but Congress has entirely farmed this out
- Only **two institutions** can manufacture money (currency +
  demand deposits): the **Federal Reserve** and the **commercial
  banks**; no other financial institution has this power

### The Banking System's Power and Drift

- Bank profits depend on lending and investing **bank-created
  money** — the privilege of creating the very money they profit
  from is their franchise
- Banks have drifted from their primary purpose: serving local
  communities with credit for small businesses and farmers
- Instead, banks increasingly buy **long-term U.S. Government
  bonds** and **tax-free municipal bonds** — profitable, zero
  paperwork, no local benefit
- Originally, bank directors had residency requirements (same area
  as the bank); laws relaxed → **holding companies** now allowed,
  directors in other states → **absentee ownership** of local banks
- "The independent bank, locally owned and operated, is a bulwark
  of strength in our country"

### Federal Reserve: The Democratic Problem

- **Federal Reserve System**: 12 regional banks + Board in
  Washington; created by Congress, yet operates independently
- Fed is widely acknowledged as the dominant influence on business
  cycles; "an important group of economists believes the money
  supply is the main factor causing the ups and downs of the
  economy"
- Problem 1 — **democratic accountability**: In a democracy,
  responsibility for economic policy rests with elected
  representatives (President + Congress); the Fed answers to no
  one; if it pursues harmful policy, "there is nothing Congress
  can do to reverse the policy"
- Problem 2 — **dual control centers**: President/Congress control
  fiscal policy; Fed controls money supply; if they pull in
  opposite directions, the economy lurches; no rational mechanism
  to reconcile
- Concrete example: President + Congress enacted **$11 billion
  tax cut** to keep the economic upturn alive through 1964–65; yet
  President had to ask the Fed not to nullify his unemployment/
  income efforts — should an elected President have to beg a
  congressionally created body?
- Problem 3 — **private capture**: Each of 12 regional banks has
  9 directors, **6 selected by privately owned commercial banks**;
  FOMC has 12 members — 7 public Board members + 5 regional bank
  presidents (chosen by bank-elected boards)

### Patman's Reform Proposals

- **Abolish the FOMC**; transfer its powers to the Federal Reserve
  Board (the public members); short terms of office
- Congress must set **explicit goals for monetary policy**; annual
  review of Fed actions by Senate and House Banking/Currency
  Committees
- The **Full Employment Act** declared intent for "maximum
  employment, production, and purchasing power" but Congress never
  directly counseled the Fed to pursue this — the Fed filled the
  vacuum with its own ends
- No room for "unsound money" arguments — neither inflation nor
  deflation is the goal; **prosperity and high employment** under
  the Full Employment Act is the standard

## Chapter I: Money and Society (pp. 7–12)

### Interest Rates as a Cost of Living

- Money supply and interest rate decisions are **public decisions**
  made by the Government — as deliberate as deciding how many
  automobiles to produce — yet the public barely participates
- 1963 data: total gross farm receipts ~$36 billion; personal
  income from interest alone ~$33 billion — comparable magnitudes
- Interest is embedded in **every stage of production**: mining,
  smelting, manufacturing, distribution, retail, utilities,
  transport — all borrow to operate; costs pyramid up the chain
  to the consumer

### Interest Rates and Investment

- Interest rates are a **determining factor in business
  investment** — new plants, equipment, inventory
- Investment is the **third largest component** of U.S. annual
  output (after consumer spending and government purchases)
- Mechanism: raise rates → firms only borrow if expected profit
  exceeds total costs including interest → high rates make
  investment unprofitable → investment falls → capital goods
  industries (machinery, construction) contract → workers laid off
  → demand for raw materials (copper, steel, aluminum) drops
- **Small business disproportionately harmed**: lacks bargaining
  power with lenders; rationed out of credit even at high rates
  while large firms get their share

### The Hidden Long-Run Cost of High Interest

- Beyond recession risk: high interest **taxes future growth**
  by slowing adoption of new technology and capacity expansion
- Modern standard of living built on 250 years of rising
  "output per man-hour" — requires continuous investment in new
  machinery incorporating innovations
- High interest → less investment today → tomorrow's labor has
  less and less efficient equipment → **lower full-employment
  output tomorrow** even if no recession today
- "What may appear, then, to be a simple decision to rein in the
  money supply and raise interest rates is, in fact, a simultaneous
  decision about the whole range of economic life"

### Rising Rates Transfer Income to the Wealthy

- Rising interest rates boost stock prices of **banks and life
  insurance companies**; major owners are a tiny minority of
  families → gratuitous additions to personal wealth
- This is a **shift of income from interest payers** (all of us
  as consumers) **to substantial interest receivers** (a handful)

### Tight Money as Inflation Fighter: The Verdict

- Tight money fights inflation by cutting demand for goods and
  labor — but **modern prices are not very responsive to demand
  declines**; steel industry made this clear
- Tight money can only stop inflation by **keeping significant
  numbers of people unemployed** and the economy stagnating
- Economy stagnated mid-1950s through early 1960s; investment
  limped; unemployment re-emerged as major problem for the first
  time since the 1930s
- "Fighting inflation with tight money is like using a cannon to
  kill a fly — if it doesn't kill the insect it will at least do
  a great deal of damage"
- The only "painless" tight money scenario: economy at full
  boom with capital-goods backlogs — conditions existing at most
  **two brief periods** in the past decade (Korean War era,
  possibly some of 1956); both passed, but high rates remained

### Tight Money and the Federal Debt

- By fiscal year 1963, Federal Government paying **~$10 billion/year**
  in interest on the debt — roughly **twice** what it would have
  been at 1952 interest rates
- Excess cost = ~$5 billion/year = **$26.20 per person** in the
  country; for an average family of five, **$131/year** in
  additional tax burden from higher interest alone
- Cold War framing: Soviet economy grew 7–10%/year (U.S.
  Intelligence Service estimates); U.S. grew at less than
  **3½%** — sustained tight money contributing to the gap

## Chapter II: What Is Money? (pp. 13–16+)

### Definition

- Historical money forms: seashells, whale's teeth, boar's tusks,
  stones, feathers, bricks, coconuts, cocoa beans, iron rings,
  salt, beaver pelts, blankets, bronze axes, stone wheels (South
  Pacific)
- Ancient Greece: oxen as money; gold/silver coins as fractions
  of an ox; Rome: gold/silver coins + bronze/copper pieces +
  salt payments to legionnaires (origin of "salary" / "worth his
  salt")
- **Core definition**: *Money is anything that people will accept
  in exchange for goods or services, in the belief that they may
  in turn exchange it, now or later, for other goods or services*
- The **material is irrelevant** — what matters is the social
  agreement about its meaning; paper IOUs can serve as money

### American Colonial Money History

- Earliest settlers used **wampum** (clamshell beads); 1637
  Massachusetts made wampum legal tender; 1641 New Amsterdam fixed
  wampum/Dutch exchange rate; New Yorkers used wampum as late
  as 1693 (could pay the ferry to Brooklyn in wampum)
- Colonies also used English, Spanish, and French coins; as trade
  grew these became dominant
- Colonies began minting their own coins (gold/silver) and issuing
  paper notes "good for" gold or silver — often wishful thinking
- **George III forbade** the Colonies to mint coins or issue other
  money; combined with foreign coin drain to finance Napoleonic
  Wars → **severe money shortage** in Colonies
- Historians: colonial resentment of this money suppression was
  **one of the major reasons** for the Declaration of Independence

### Commodity Money and Its Problems (Tobacco)

- Virginia, Maryland, North Carolina adopted **tobacco as legal
  tender**; warehouse operators issued receipts; receipts
  circulated as currency as late as 1900
- Problem: tobacco prices set in **European markets**, fluctuating
  season to season → value of the monetary unit unstable →
  creditors and debtors couldn't price long-term contracts
  reliably → eventually forced price-fixing efforts by state
  legislatures

### Continental Currency and Revolutionary Failure

- Continental Congress issued paper **"Continental dollars"** to
  finance the Revolution; trade disruption + British counterfeiting
  + Congress lacking power to tax or declare legal tender →
  Continentals became worthless
- Origin of phrase: **"not worth a Continental"** = something with
  no intrinsic value and nothing behind it

### State Bank Notes and the National Bank Act

- Pre-Lincoln: private state-chartered banks could print paper
  money; "**free banking**" era — almost anyone could open a bank
  and issue notes; many **"wildcat" banks** issued worthless paper
- **National Bank Act of 1863** (at Lincoln's request): federal
  charters, federal regulation, national bank notes under federal
  rules; 1865: **10% tax on State bank notes** → killed state
  note issuance entirely

> "Money is the creature of law, and the creation of the original
> issue of money should be maintained as an exclusive monopoly of
> the National Government. The privilege of creating and issuing
> money is not only the supreme prerogative of the Government, it
> is the Government's greatest opportunity."
> — *President Abraham Lincoln*

- Reason for federal intervention: U.S. rapidly industrializing;
  trade going national; no reliable uniform currency → commerce
  impeded

## Chapter II (continued): What Is Money?

### Forms of Money in Use (1964)
- Three forms of money: **coins**, **currency** (paper money), and **commercial bank demand deposits** (checkbook money)
- Demand deposits ~80% of all money by 1964; coins/currency only ~20%
- February 1964 totals: Currency & coin = $32B; Demand deposits = $119.7B; Total = $151.9B
- **Savings deposits** explicitly excluded — not immediately spendable by check

### Legal Tender and Why Money Has Value
- **Legal tender**: any form the U.S. Government declares good for payment of taxes and all public/private debts
- Dollar's value rests on its legal status: courts will order debtors to pay in dollars, making dollars essential to creditors
- The dollar is valuable *because* debtors need it to discharge legal obligations — not because of gold behind it

### Coins and Currency
- U.S. Treasury is the only institution that may mint coins; issues them through Federal Reserve banks
- Gold coins prohibited since **1934 law** — called in all previously issued gold coins
- Currency breakdown (Feb. 1964): Federal Reserve notes = $31.1B (94%); Silver certificates = $1.7B; U.S. notes = $312M; National bank notes = $37M
- **Federal Reserve notes** are now effectively the only paper money issued; Treasury stopped issuing silver certificates

### What Backs the Dollar
- **Federal Reserve notes** backed by "the credit of the U.S. Government" — printed on every bill
- American citizens can only: (a) exchange for other Federal Reserve notes, or (b) use to pay taxes and all debts
- Foreign **central banks** may exchange dollar holdings for U.S. Treasury gold — so gold "backs" the dollar only in international trade
- Domestically, the dollar is backed by the credit and assets of all U.S. citizens — a claim on the real productive capacity of the economy

### The Gold Standard Question
- Dollar was convertible to gold for ~100 years prior to 1934 (except 18 years around Civil War)
- In theory convertible; in practice impossible — in 1914, money in existence was ~12x the monetary gold supply ($20B money vs. $1.6B gold); same ratio held in 1963 ($157.4B money vs. $15.6B gold)
- **1934 law**: called in all monetary gold to U.S. Treasury; made conversion impossible for U.S. citizens
- U.S. still "on gold standard" only for international transactions — foreigners can collect in gold or goods
- Patman's argument: going off the gold standard merely formalized what was already true; checkbook money was always backed by productive assets (inventories, machinery, farm commodities), not gold

### Managed Paper Currency
- We have a **managed paper currency**: every dollar = a dollar of debt owed by an individual, business, or government
- "Managed" because the **Federal Reserve System** consciously controls the maximum amount of money that may be created at all times
- **Money is not real wealth** — it is only a claim to real wealth

### Congressional Power Over Money
- Money creation is an **inherent power of Government** (Lincoln quoted approvingly)
- **Reginald McKenna** (Chancellor of the Exchequer, WWI Britain):
> "They who control the credit of the nation direct the policy of governments, and hold in their hands the destiny of the people."
- Constitution Art. I §8 ¶5: Congress has power to "coin money, regulate the value thereof"
- Supreme Court extended this broadly — "whatever power there is over the currency is vested in the Congress"
- Key cases: *McCulloch v. Maryland* (1819) — Congress can charter banks, states cannot tax federal monetary instrumentalities; Legal Tender Cases (1870s) — Congress can make paper money legal tender even retroactively; Gold Clause Cases (1935, *Nortz v. U.S.*, *Norman v. Baltimore & Ohio R.R.*) — Congress can change the gold value of money and prohibit gold circulation
- Congress **delegated** money-creating power to the Federal Reserve System and to private commercial banks

### Why Money Must Be Managed
- "Money does not manage itself" — favorite saying of **Chairman Martin** of the Federal Reserve Board
- Too little money → high interest rates, unemployment, idle capacity, stunted investment
- Too much money → overheats economy past capacity → **inflation**
- The right amount of money = like the right number of theater tickets: too many causes chaos, too few leaves empty seats

### The Money Supply Defined
- **Money supply** = demand deposits in commercial banks + currency and coin outside banks
- Savings deposits at S&Ls, insurance company funds, pension funds *not* included — they are not immediately spendable, and the underlying currency is already counted once inside the commercial banking system

## Chapter III: How Is Money Created?

### The Secrecy Problem
- Most people don't understand money creation; bankers and officials deliberately cloak the system in secrecy
- Patman's charge: private managers of money "unravel the mystery" language is cover for protecting private control over a public function
- Officials use language implying money supply is subject to "powerful economic laws over which men have no control" — Patman calls this propaganda

### Historical Growth of Money Supply
- 1914 (Fed organized): $12 billion total money supply
- 1929: $26 billion — money nearly doubled in 15 years to accommodate growth
- Money supply has *always* grown — it has been created by men, not found in nature

### The Goldsmith Bankers
- **Goldsmith bankers** did virtually all banking in 17th-century Western Europe
- Goldsmiths issued **"claim checks"** (receipts) for gold deposited for safekeeping
- Receipts began circulating as money — people traded receipts without ever withdrawing gold
- Key discovery: few depositors ever claimed their gold simultaneously → goldsmiths could issue receipts for *more* gold than they held → **fractional reserve banking** is born

### Fractional Reserve System
- Goldsmiths issued receipts to *borrowers* who were not depositing gold — effectively lending gold they didn't own but held in trust
- As long as demands for gold were infrequent, they could earn interest on deposited gold they had no right to lend
- This is **the fractional reserve system**: reserves = only a fraction of total claims outstanding

### Advantages of Fractional Reserve
- Provided money for enterprises when governments neglected to supply adequate currency
- Allowed rapid economic expansion during New World settlement era

### Dangers of Fractional Reserve
- System was "playing the odds" — any bank run (mass simultaneous withdrawal demands) would collapse it
- If run started: bank could not honor claims → money became worthless → savings wiped out → businesses forced into bankruptcy → community economic paralysis
- Amsterdam example: a banker proposed making it a **hanging offense** to start a run on a goldsmith — immediately caused a run; goldsmith couldn't pay; customers hanged him
- Goldsmith money was also purely local — couldn't function in foreign or inter-regional commerce
- Suspicion that some large goldsmith bankers **deliberately precipitated depressions** — forcing bankruptcies so they could buy assets at bargain prices

### From Goldsmiths to Modern Banking
- Early 18th century: governments of Britain and Western Europe created central banks (e.g., **Bank of England**) to hold reserves and regulate money issuance
- Modern U.S. system is a **two-layer system**:
  - Lower layer: commercial banks (hold public's checking accounts)
  - Upper layer: **Federal Reserve System** (holds commercial banks' reserves)
- **Reserves** = demand deposits owed by the Federal Reserve to commercial banks — not the banks' "own" money or depositors' money, but new money created by the Fed

### How Reserves Are Created
- The Fed creates reserves by making loans to commercial banks or by **purchasing U.S. Government securities**
- When Fed buys $1M of bonds from a bond dealer: dealer deposits check at his bank → bank credits dealer's checking account → bank sends check to Fed → Fed credits bank's reserve account by $1M
- Net result: two new deposits — one at the commercial bank (dealer's checking account), one at the Fed (bank's reserve account)
- **The Federal Reserve creates money by writing a check** — it never has a problem making checks "good" because it can print the bills to cover them

> *"The Federal Reserve System is an agency of Congress authorized to create money."*

### The Money Multiplier Mechanism
- Banks must keep a required % of outstanding deposits as reserves at the Fed (e.g., 10%)
- With $1,000 in new reserves and 10% requirement, a local bank lends ~$1,100 (keeping ~$100, expecting 90% to leave)
- That $900 flows to other banks as new reserves → they lend against it → cascading deposits
- End result: $1,000 in new reserves → ~$10,000 in new total deposits throughout the system
- Formula: **A × B = C** (Amount of reserves × deposits permitted per dollar of reserves = Total bank deposits)
- Fed can change money supply by: (1) changing the amount of reserves (open market operations), or (2) changing the reserve requirement ratio (B in the formula)

### Two Routes to Expanding Money Supply — Key Political Difference
- **Route 1 — Lower reserve requirements**: ALL new money created by private commercial banks; interest flows to private banks
- **Route 2 — Fed buys securities (open market operations)**: Fed creates some money directly; Fed's income reverts to U.S. Treasury after expenses
- This is not a technicality — it determines **who profits from money creation**, potentially millions/billions in tax burden difference

### The Myth That Banks "Deposit" Reserves
- Standard college textbook explanation is **misleading**: describes customer depositing cash → bank holds fraction as reserve → lends rest
- This implies banks *own* or *hold* their depositors' money at the Fed, which is false
- Truth: private banks *collectively* have never deposited a penny of their own or depositors' funds with the Federal Reserve
- Reserves are *created* by the Fed — they can be transferred between banks but the **total level of reserves in the system can only be changed by the Federal Reserve**
- Bankers who believe the textbook myth agitate to be paid interest on their "deposited" reserves — lobbying based on a false premise

### Destroying Money
- Money creation runs in reverse: when bank repays loan to Fed, reserves shrink → bank must call loans/sell investments → deposit contraction cascade
- Fed selling securities to public: buyer's bank loses reserves → multiple contraction of deposits

## Chapter III (continued): How the Federal Reserve Creates Money

### The "Cash Reserves" Myth

- **Robert V. Roosa**, former VP of NY Fed, testified in 1960: banks do NOT give the Fed
  cash deposits to accumulate reserves — the Fed *creates* those reserves itself
- Patman personally visited the NY Fed to see the $28B in gov't securities — they exist
- He then asked to see "cash reserves" member banks held on deposit — bank officials
  explained there are *no* cash reserves; they are bookkeeping credits created by the Fed
- Same answer at the **Federal Reserve Bank of Richmond**: "the cash is just a myth"

### Reserve Creation Since 1917

- End of 1917: banks had $1.5B in reserve credits
- By end of 1959: Fed had created $47B gross in bank reserves; banks drew down $28B
  as currency → net reserve balance: **$18.5B**
- Key implication: if original $1.5B came from actual bank deposits, banks got $28B
  back in cash plus still have $18.5B in reserves — entitling them to create 7× that
  amount (~$129B) in bank-created money

### Four Methods the Fed Uses to Create/Extinguish Reserves

1. **Open market operations** — buying/selling gov't securities (primary method, NY Fed
   acts as agent for entire System)
2. **Purchasing gold** as agent for the U.S. Treasury
3. **Making loans** to commercial banks (secured usually by gov't securities)
4. **Purchasing "eligible paper"** (IOUs signed by bank customers) — almost never used

### Open Market Operations — Mechanics

- The **"open market"** consists of just **21 private dealers** in U.S. gov't securities,
  mostly large NY and Chicago banks plus Wall Street securities firms
- **Creating reserves:** Fed buys $1,000 bond from dealer → dealer deposits check at
  Chase Manhattan → Chase credits dealer's account $1,000 → Chase sends check to
  NY Fed → NY Fed credits Chase's *reserve account* $1,000. Net: $1,000 new reserves
- **Extinguishing reserves:** reverse process — Fed sells bond, dealer pays with personal
  bank check → Fed reduces that bank's reserve account by $1,000

### Gold and Reserve Creation

- Treasury (not Fed) buys/sells gold; Fed acts as agent
- Sources: newly mined domestic gold (small), and **foreign central banks** (most transactions)
- Foreign central banks prefer dollar credits (earn interest) over physical gold (storage cost)
  — this is why they don't always redeem dollars for gold even though they legally can
- At time of writing, dollars held abroad **exceeded** the Treasury's gold supply
- When Treasury buys $1M gold → mining company's bank gains $1M in reserves →
  banks can create $7M in new deposits
- **Gold outflows do NOT automatically reduce total bank reserves** — the Fed can offset
  by buying gov't securities in the open market

### Fed Loans to Banks

- When Fed lends to a bank, it simply credits that bank's reserve account — creates money
- Such loans are intentionally **temporary** (usually ≤15 days); banks are not entitled to
  borrow continuously — only when temporarily short of reserves
- Collateral: almost always U.S. gov't securities in practice
- **Eligible paper** (customer IOUs): Congress originally intended this as the *main* reserve
  creation method — would have tied money supply to actual local business demand.
  In practice, Fed now creates almost no reserves this way; funnels credit via NY open market

### Currency, Coin, and Checkbook Money

- Currency and coin = ~20% of money supply; bank deposits = ~80%
- When public wants more cash → bank draws down its Fed reserve account → reserves fall
- The **Open Market Committee** (Board of Governors + 5 regional bank presidents) sets
  only the *maximum* amount of checkbook money — cannot force banks to lend
- Banks will only create deposits by making loans/investments they judge will be repaid

### Do Banks "Pay" for Their Reserves?

- No — reserves cannot be paid for by private banks; only the Fed creates them
- **1935 law** (sponsored by bank associations) made it illegal for banks to pay interest
  on demand deposits — so banks pay nothing for reserves, yet earn interest on all
  assets those reserves support
- When Fed provides more reserves, this *automatically* enables banks to make more profits
- Banks can convert reserves to cash (FR notes) at any time — but doing so eliminates
  the reserve base and prevents deposit expansion

### Policy A vs. Policy B: Who Profits from Government Securities?

| | Bank Reserves | Reserve Req. | Bank Deposits | Bank-held Govt Securities |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Policy "A" | $20B | 10% | $200B | $180B |
| Policy "B" | $40B | 20% | $200B | $160B |

- Both policies produce the same $200B in total deposits and money supply
- **Critical difference**: under Policy A, private banks own $180B in interest-bearing assets;
  under Policy B, the Fed (= public) owns $20B more gov't securities and banks own $20B less
- The Fed has *arbitrarily chosen* a low-reserve/low-requirement policy that gifts private
  banks $20B more in interest-earning gov't securities than they'd hold otherwise
- As of Jan. 31, 1964: Fed owned $32,753M in U.S. gov't securities
- Private commercial banks owned **$62.7B** in U.S. gov't securities, acquired with
  bank-created money — the gov't's own money-creation power used to lend to itself at interest

### Government Bonds vs. Government Money

- When the Fed buys gov't securities, it pays with FR notes (non-interest-bearing obligations)
  — effectively trading interest-bearing gov't bonds for non-interest-bearing currency
- **Jan. 31, 1964:** Fed had $33.9B FR notes outstanding against $32.8B in gov't securities
- The government, in effect, has exchanged interest-bearing debt for non-interest-bearing money
  — taxpayers are off the hook for both interest and principal on Fed-held securities
  (since the money supply never shrinks, the Fed never needs to sell those securities back)

> "If our Nation can issue a dollar bond it can issue a dollar bill. The element that makes
> the bond good makes the bill good also. The difference between the bond and the bill is that
> the bond lets money brokers collect twice the amount of the bond and an additional 20 percent,
> whereas the currency pays nobody but those who contribute directly in some useful way."
> — **Thomas A. Edison**

- **Lincoln's greenbacks:** issued $450M in U.S. Notes (1862 act) instead of bonds.
  Had those been bonds at 5% interest, the U.S. would have paid **$2.3 billion** by 1964 —
  ~5× the original sum. The greenbacks were *no more inflationary* than the equivalent
  bank-deposit money that would have been created to buy those bonds
- Political reality: bankers are powerful enough to suppress government money creation;
  Lincoln triggered a "political furor" just by issuing $346M in greenbacks

### "Printing Press Money" vs. "Pen-and-Ink Money"

- **Printing press money**: engraved on special paper/inks; costs ~$0.00008 per bill regardless
  of denomination; issued only by the government
- **Pen-and-ink money** (now "office machine money"): created by private banks via bookkeeping
  entries when making loans, buying securities, or acquiring any asset — the dominant form
- The Fed determines the *ceiling* on how much pen-and-ink money exists; private bankers
  decide how much is actually created within that ceiling

## Chapter IV: Why Was the Federal Reserve Act Passed?

### The Federal Reserve as Central Bank

- The **Federal Reserve Act (1913)** was the most notable step in a long process toward
  a stable money system
- A **central bank's** core functions: manage money supply, set general interest rate levels,
  handle the nation's international debt settlements
- Most central banks are government-owned; the Fed is distinctive in being decentralized
  (12 regional banks) and in allowing private commercial banks to elect directors
- Non-national bank membership is voluntary — yet member banks hold ~85% of all
  commercial bank assets

### Pre-Fed U.S. Banking History

- **Bank of England** (est. 1694) was the global model for centuries
- U.S. experimented briefly with the **First and Second Banks of the United States**
  — both federally chartered, combining central and private banking functions,
  predominantly privately owned and controlled
- **Andrew Jackson** refused to renew the Second Bank's charter in 1836 → long period
  of near-banking anarchy until the Civil War; State banks tripled in number 1834–1861
- **National Bank Act (1863–1864)**: created system of federally chartered private banks
  issuing currency of uniform value — but tied to the amount of gov't bonds outstanding,
  not to the economy's actual needs

### The Structural Flaws of Pre-1913 Banking

1. **Individual banks stood alone** — no pooled reserves; a run on one could trigger
   chain-reaction runs on solvent banks
2. **Inelastic money supply** — tied to gold and outstanding gov't bonds, not to
   seasonal or cyclical demand; "elastic currency" was impossible
3. **No minimum standards** — system only as strong as its weakest banks; no supervision
   against insolvency and fraud

- **Harvest season problem**: Southern/Western country banks withdrew deposits from
  NY correspondent banks to finance crop movements → NY banks called **"call loans"**
  (overnight stock market loans) → stocks crashed → panic spread
- Banks were penalized for using reserves to pay depositors (could lose charter) →
  they closed doors instead → made panics worse

### Major Pre-Fed Panics

- **Panics of 1873, 1884, 1893, and 1907** — each followed speculative booms in
  railroads, land, mines, cotton, stocks; corrupt practices played a role
- **Panic of 1873**: **Jay Gould and James Fisk** tried to corner the gold market
  with help from President **Grant's** brother-in-law; multiple congressional members
  were corrupted
- **Panic of 1907** — deliberately triggered financial warfare between two private groups:
  - **Standard Oil group** vs. **F. Augustus Heinze** (copper mining rival in Montana)
  - Standard Oil quietly bought into Heinze's copper stocks, then dumped them at a
    critical moment, breaking his **Mercantile National Bank of New York**
  - Chain reaction: runs across NY, then nationwide; hundreds of millions hoarded;
    legal tender sold at a premium
  - **J. P. Morgan** organized the NY Clearing House Association to stem the panic;
    President **Theodore Roosevelt** approved a $100M "certificate" issue to function as money
  - As a side deal, Morgan won Roosevelt's antitrust exemption to acquire the southern
    steel industry (centered in Birmingham) through U.S. Steel Corp.

### Road to the Federal Reserve Act

- 1908: Congress authorized a **National Monetary Commission** headed by Senator
  **Nelson Aldrich** — published 20 volumes; Aldrich proposed a privately owned,
  privately controlled central bank (bankers' preferred solution)
- Simultaneously, the House **Pujo Committee** investigation exposed the **"money trust"**:
  a network of holding companies and interlocking directorates giving a small Wall Street
  group control of most major banks nationally
- Public demanded a *public* central bank — not another Wall Street instrument
- **President Woodrow Wilson** and **Senator Robert L. Owen** (Senate Banking Committee
  chair) championed public control

> "We must have a currency, not rigid as now, but readily elastically responsive to sound
> credit, the expanding and contracting credits of everyday transactions… Our banking laws
> must mobilize reserves; must not permit the concentration anywhere in a few hands of
> the monetary resources of the country… the control of the system of banking and of issue
> must be public, not private, must be vested in the Government itself."
> — **President Woodrow Wilson**

### Compromise Structure of the Fed

- Western and Southern public opinion (stirred by Pujo findings) demanded decentralization
  — no single Wall Street-controlled bank
- Result: **12 separate regional Federal Reserve banks**, each with more-or-less independent
  powers, yet joined in a monetary pipeline to shift reserves where needed
- **Private banks got**: the right to elect directors of the 12 regional banks
- **Public got**: a Board of Governors in Washington with ultimate authority

### Three Structural Goals the Fed Was Built to Fix

1. **Mobilize scattered reserves** into a common pool lendable to any bank in any region
2. **Provide an elastic currency** — money supply that expands/contracts with economic need,
   not with gold discoveries or gov't bond issuance
3. **Enforce minimum standards** via a central supervisory body, preventing insolvency
   and protecting depositors

## Chapter IV (continued): The Federal Reserve System — Structure & Operations

### Fed as Improvement Over Predecessor
- Five key improvements over the pre-1913 system:
  1. **Centralized reserves** at district Federal Reserve banks (not pyramided at private banks) — made conversion to currency in crises possible
  2. **Decentralized central bank** — 12 regional banks to reduce NYC concentration of money power
  3. Hoped all banks would join and face uniform regulation — this failed; today over half of U.S. banks are non-members (though member banks hold ~85% of deposits)
  4. **Check-clearing operations** dramatically improved — reduced time and cost of clearing checks drawn on out-of-city banks
  5. **Elastic currency** — money supply could expand/contract with business needs via the **discount window**; but Patman notes this is not clear-cut and can produce perverse supply changes

### Federal Reserve Structure
- Three basic parts: **Board of Governors**, **12 Federal Reserve Banks**, ~6,100 private commercial member banks
- Most important policy body: **Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC)**

**(1) Board of Governors**
- 7 members appointed by President, confirmed by Senate
- 14-year terms, staggered (one new member every 2 years)
- President can appoint majority only if serving two full constitutional terms
- Salary: $20,000/year (Chairman: $20,500)

**(2) Federal Reserve Banks**
- 12 banks: Boston, New York, Philadelphia, Richmond, Atlanta, Cleveland, Chicago, St. Louis, Dallas, Kansas City, Minneapolis, San Francisco

**(3) Private Member Banks**
- As of June 1963: 6,058 commercial banks in system
- ~4,500 national banks (required members); ~1,500 state-chartered (voluntary)

**(4) Federal Open Market Committee**
- 12 members: 7 Board of Governors + 5 of the 12 Federal Reserve bank presidents

### Fed Operations: Three Types
1. **Routine** — check clearing (~40% of total system cost); distributing currency; fiscal agent for U.S. Government (issuing notes/bonds); holding member bank reserves; foreign central bank liaison
2. **Regulatory** — sets membership requirements; examines state member banks' books (national banks examined by **Comptroller of the Currency**)
3. **Policy** — most consequential: determines money supply and thus interest rates and economic activity; chief tool is open market operations (FOMC); secondary tools: discount rate, reserve requirements, stock market margin requirements

### Fed Revenue (1963 data)
- Total earnings: $1.151 billion
  - U.S. Government securities: **$1.133 billion (98.9% of income)**
  - Discounts and advances: $8.9 million (tiny)
- Expenses: $178 million total
- Dividends to private member banks: $28.9 million
- **Returned to U.S. Treasury: $879,685,219**
- No law specifies how much must be returned or when — the Fed decides how much to spend on "expenses" first

## Chapter V: Who Determines the Money Supply?

### The Hidden Power Center
- **Federal Open Market Committee** is arguably among the most powerful groups in America — but virtually unknown to the public
- Its power rivals the President's in deciding how the "world's greatest economic machine will operate"
- Known mainly to major bankers, giant financial houses, and trained economists whose professional interests give them access to this knowledge

### FOMC Composition & Democratic Deficit
- 19 participants total; **12 voting members** in any given year
- 7 Board of Governors (public, appointed by President) + 5 of 12 Federal Reserve bank presidents
- **Federal Reserve Bank of New York president always votes** — 8 permanent voting slots, 4 rotating
- The 12 bank presidents are elected *indirectly* by private commercial bankers — not appointed by any public authority
- Board members serve 14-year terms; **cannot be removed** by President except for cause
- Because terms are staggered, a President can "just barely hope to appoint a majority" of the seven if he serves two full terms

> "In many ways their power is equal to that of the President in deciding how the world's greatest economic machine will operate." — Patman

### Credit Has Two Dimensions: Price and Availability
- **"Sticky" interest rates**: unlike most prices, interest rates don't move up and down weekly with supply and demand shifts
- Credit has two dimensions: **availability** (is money freely lendable?) and **price** (the interest rate)
- The FOMC often changes *availability* first, without changing rates — analogous to a car manufacturer setting a model-year price and holding it
- Commercial banks may be "loaned up" — at maximum lending capacity — for extended periods regardless of demand
- When Fed eases, banks may continue charging old rates for weeks or months before lowering them
- **Price leadership**: commercial banks nationwide key lending rates to the **prime rate** set by big New York banks
- The prime rate is itself set by large NYC banks using: their feel for credit supply/demand, knowledge of Treasury needs, expectations about Fed policy — "liberally laced with a large dose of discretion"

### Treasury Rate-Setting: Negotiated, Not Market-Determined
- Treasury is always the largest single borrower — constantly rolling over maturing bonds
- Treasury *nominally* sets rates but in practice negotiates with a **small group of big banks and financial houses**
- Large banks study FOMC attitudes closely and can usually predict future policy — this shapes their side of the bargaining
- Outcome: "The Government weighing heavily on both sides of the bargaining process" — Treasury as borrower, FOMC as credit supply determinant

### Historical Shift: How Money Supply Control Evolved
- Pre-National Bank Act: Fed government confined to coin/currency; money supply mainly from commercial banking system
- Pre-1914: legal reserve requirements applied to bank *notes*, not deposits — deposit creation not understood as alternative; 10% Federal tax made state bank note issue prohibitive
- Federal Reserve Act (1914): designed as an automatic system — monetizing "eligible" short-term commercial paper to expand money with business needs; elastic currency via discount window
- Key insight: **"no specific limits were placed on the amount of money the system could create"** — the discount rate was the only limiting mechanism

### Open Market Operations: The "Power Revolution"
- At founding, **discount rate** was considered *the* key policy tool; open market operations were insignificant
- By the 1920s, individual banks doing open-market operations independently and often at cross-purposes
- By 1930: informal "policy conference" of 12 bank presidents, coordinated by New York bank
- 1933 emergency banking legislation: FOMC recognized as official part of System
- **Banking Act of 1935**: reorganized FOMC to give Board of Governors majority (7 of 12 voting members) — but bank presidents retained five seats
- Since the 1951 "accord," open market operations have become **the fundamental technique of credit policy**, far more important than discount rate or reserve requirements; used continuously vs. infrequent changes in other tools
- By 1963: Fed earned $1.138 billion from government securities vs. $8.9 million from discounts — open market dominance quantified

### The "Power Revolution" Explained
- Original design: public control secured through Board of Governors controlling the discount rate; reserves supplied only through discount window (12 independent regional banks)
- When Congress authorized FOMC for joint open-market operations (1933, 1935), it assumed discounts/advances would remain primary — didn't grasp the power shift
- Result: **control of money supply passed from a purely public group to a mixed body with private banking dominance**
- Five regional bank presidents who rotate onto FOMC take a public oath when joining — but take *no such oath* as bank presidents
- Regional banks now function mainly as **economic intelligence units** for FOMC; discount window nearly obsolete
- Open market operations are effectively the preserve of the **New York Federal Reserve Bank**, which executes all system transactions

> "Here, then, is the 'power revolution' at the Federal Reserve which destroyed the ingenious compromise of the original legislation. Control of the money supply, with its enormous economic consequences, has passed from a purely public group, composed only of public servants, to a mixed body with dubious qualifications to represent the public interest." — Patman

### Patman's Reform Proposal
- All FOMC members should be public appointees of the President
- FOMC should be abolished and powers transferred to an enlarged **Federal Reserve Board**

### How the Fed Fixes Interest Rates (Three Channels)
1. **Money supply** — open market ops set total reserves → determines money available for lending → demand for borrowing relative to supply sets rate
2. **Government bond prices** — buying bonds raises prices (lowers yields); selling bonds lowers prices (raises yields)
3. **Expectations** — if Fed signals tight money, lenders demand higher rates now; borrowers accept higher rates expecting them to rise further

### The "Open Market": Actually a Closed Club
- The "open market" is Manhattan's financial district — where corporate bonds, government bonds, stocks, commodity futures are traded
- Government securities "may be bought and sold only in the open market" (1935 Federal Reserve Act amendment)
- In practice: only **21 authorized primary dealers** (as of 1963; was 12 before 1952)
- 6 are "bank" dealers (departments of major commercial banks); 15 are "nonbank" dealers whose *entire income* comes from operating this tollgate
- Treasury notifies 21 dealers each Monday of coming week's needs; bids on Tuesday; sale to highest bidders
- 21 dealers always know before Tuesday who got the prior bid — it's a tightly closed, exclusive club

> "The practical effect of requiring all purchases to be made through the open market is to take money from the taxpayer and give it to these dealers. It forces the Government to pay a toll for borrowing money." — **Marriner Eccles**, former Chairman, Board of Governors

## Chapter VI: Who Owns the Federal Reserve Banks?

### The Ownership Controversy
- Private bankers actively advertise that they *own* the Fed; **American Bankers Association** textbook (*Money and Banking*, p. 234): "The member banks own the 12 Federal Reserve banks"
- Federal Reserve officials consistently reject this view

### The "Stock" Is Not Real Stock
- Original Federal Reserve Act required member banks to invest 6% of their capital in Federal Reserve "stock"
- **Marriner S. Eccles** (Chairman, Board of Governors), letter to Rep. Wright Patman, April 18, 1941:

> "This so-called stock ownership, however, is more in the nature of an enforced subscription to the capital of the Federal Reserve banks than an ownership in the usual sense. The stock cannot be sold, transferred, or hypothecated, nor can it be voted in accordance with the par value of the shares held... Member banks have no right to participate in earnings above the statutory dividend, and upon liquidation any funds remaining after retirement of the stock revert to the Government."

- **Eccles** to House Banking and Currency Committee (1942): "Well, the Government, in effect, for all practical purposes, owns the Federal Reserve banks."
- **William McChesney Martin** (current Chairman), to Joint Economic Committee (1956), confirmed "stock" is a misnomer — it conveys no proprietary interest; "The banks do not own the Federal Reserve System"
- Board members **Robertson** and **Shepardson** both testified member banks have no ownership claim

### Why Stock Was Required (Historical Context)
- Framers expected Fed to issue money against commercial/industrial "eligible paper," not government securities
- Member banks were exchanging their private loan obligations for Fed notes — risky exchange requiring skin in the game
- The 12 regional banks each were to judge quality of loans from their member banks — stock requirement as collateral/commitment mechanism
- This original design was overtaken by events (open market operations replaced discounting) — the "stock" rationale became moot but the confusion persists

### Ongoing Confusion
- Academic economists divided — some agree with private ownership view, some with public
- Some regional Fed bank officials behave as if they run private institutions (e.g., **Federal Reserve Bank of Chicago** justified expenditures inappropriate for public funds by citing what private businesses do)
- Root cause: bank-dominated boards of directors elect bank presidents who are often ex-bankers themselves

## Chapter VI (cont.): Federal Reserve Stock — Problems and Elimination

### Nature of the "Stock"
- Fed member bank **"stock"** is not stock in any ordinary sense:
  - No proprietary interest (unlike private corporate stock)
  - Cannot be sold or pledged for loans — no ownership claim
  - On dissolution, net assets go to the **U.S. Treasury**, not member banks
  - No voting rights tied to share amount — each bank gets one vote per class
- Original rationale: safeguard against misuse of Government credit, attract
  private bank participation via a 6% tax-free dividend (higher than any
  bank could earn on deposits); a minority in Congress wanted stock sold
  to the public instead

### Problems Created by Stock Ownership
- Creates **misconceptions**: private bankers led to believe they own the Fed
  and have the right to control and profit from it
- Fed officials led to believe Fed funds are not public funds — used to
  justify scholarships, Christmas gratuities, boating trips for non-employees
- **Direct subsidy to private banks**: dividends run ~$24 million/year;
  net loss to taxpayers since that money would otherwise go to Treasury as
  interest income
- Patman's position: stock serves no purpose today; bill before Congress
  would have banks return it, Fed pays it off
- **The Fed does not need the money** — it is a money-creating system;
  it can write a check whenever it needs money

> "No sound reason has been given for keeping this stock." — *Chapter VI*

## Chapter VII: Why Was the Federal Deposit Insurance Act Passed?

### The 1929 Crash — Cause and Consequence
- For 18 years after the Federal Reserve Act, no basic changes to banking law
- **Fatal flaw**: in the late 1920s, banks transformed into a credit machine
  sustaining a wildly inflating stock market — money created via **brokers'
  loans** collateralized by the very stocks being speculated on
- Brokers' loans grew from **$2 billion to over $8 billion** during the boom
- When crash came, $4 billion in brokers' loans called in 4 months;
  another $4 billion within 3 years → Nation's money supply fell by ~$8
  billion (**one-third**) between 1929 and 1933
- Commercial banks fell from **26,401 (1928) to 14,771 (1933)**

### Roosevelt's Response — Banking Act of 1933
- **Bank holiday** declared; Emergency Banking Act of 1933 passed in a
  single day — checked all banks, reopened sound ones, closed unsound
- Key provisions of Banking Act of 1933:
  1. Temporary (later permanent) **deposit insurance** — became FDIC
  2. Banks prohibited from paying interest on **demand deposits**
     (reduced cutthroat competition for deposits)
  3. Fed given power to change **reserve requirements** (subject to
     Presidential approval; 1935 act gave Board sole authority)
  4. **Glass-Steagall separation**: commercial banks barred from stock
     market loans; investment banks barred from accepting public deposits
- **Securities and Exchange Act of 1934**: established the **SEC**;
  gave the Fed power to set **margin requirements** (cash downpayment
  on stock purchases)

### Banking Act of 1935 — Creating a Real Central Bank
- Made FDIC permanent; Board of Governors given independent power to
  change reserve requirements
- **Board restructured**: Secretary of Treasury and Comptroller removed;
  9 members cut to 7; renamed from "Federal Reserve Board" to
  **Board of Governors of the Federal Reserve System**
- Established **Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC)** — for the first
  time gave a true central bank authority over Government securities
  purchases/sales for the entire System
- 1935 act also allowed Fed banks to extend credit on any collateral a
  commercial bank possessed (not just "eligible paper")

### The FDIC
- **FDIC**: Government corporation insuring depositors up to **$10,000**
  per account against bank failure
- Membership: by end of 1962, **13,455 banks** were members; only a few
  hundred small banks had not joined
- Since 1933: **445 insured banks failed** through December 31, 1962;
  total deposits ~$600 million; slightly more than 5,000 depositors with
  accounts over $10,000 lost any money
- Funded by: assessments of **1/12 of 1%** of total deposits, plus
  interest on U.S. Government securities held (~half each)
- Startup capital: Treasury bought $150M of stock, Fed (on Congress's
  instruction) bought $139M — repaid 1947–48 at only **2% simple
  interest** (should have been compound)
- Since 1950, FDIC has returned ~57% of gross assessments back to banks
  (steered by the "do nothing" 80th Congress via **Congressman Jesse P.
  Wolcott**; **Eisenhower** rewarded him with FDIC Board chairmanship)
- Reserve ratio: **$1.40 per $100 of insured deposits** — vs. life
  insurance companies that keep $20+ per $100 of potential liabilities
  (13x more); Patman argues FDIC is underfunded

### FDIC's Regulatory Power — and Its Problems
- FDIC can block "unsafe and unsound practices"; controls bank entry by
  refusing insurance → effectively controls how many banks exist
- **Critique**: FDIC examiners judge loans on probability of repayment,
  not public welfare — a loan for patent medicine with good collateral
  is "good"; a loan for college tuition without collateral is "bad"

> "The regulatory authorities and examiners…are not especially interested
> in the justification given loans from the standpoint of public welfare
> and economic stability, but rather in the probabilities of their being
> repaid at maturity." — Prof. **Raymond P. Kent**, *Money and Banking*

## Chapter VIII: How the Federal Reserve Gives Away Public Funds to Private Banks

### The Core Argument
- Private banks are unique: they **create money at negligible cost** and
  earn interest on that money — the power to create money was delegated
  by Congress at no charge
- Fed's $207 million gross expenses in 1963 were mostly costs of providing
  **free services** to member banks; check clearing alone costs scores of
  millions
- Government protects banks from competition in three ways:
  1. **Charter gatekeeping**: Comptroller won't issue new national bank
     charter if it would "weaken" existing banks; state boards are the same
  2. **FDIC insurance requirement**: impossible to attract depositors without
     FDIC — entry to banking is effectively blocked by a Federal agency
  3. **Interest prohibition on demand deposits**: federal law makes it
     unlawful to pay interest on demand deposits — eliminates competition
     for deposits, lowers bank costs
- FDIC insurance itself is a subsidy: it keeps money in banks that would
  otherwise be hoarded in lockboxes — increases the deposit base,
  increasing bank profit-making power

### The 1958 $10 Billion Giveaway
- Early 1958: Fed lowered reserve requirements, allowing private banks to
  create ~**$10 billion** in new money — stated purpose: combat recession
- Banks used the money-creating power not to make business loans (which
  **actually fell**) but to buy **$10 billion of Government securities**
- The Fed could have created this $10 billion itself — interest would
  have returned to Treasury, not gone to bank profits
- Almost one-fourth of the $10 billion went to just **18 big banks in
  New York City**; 2% of banks received three-fourths of the giveaway

### The Bond Giveaway Bill (1959)
- **American Bankers Association (ABA)** proposed transferring **$16.8
  billion** of Government securities from the Federal Reserve to private
  banks — called the "vault cash bill" (misleadingly)
- Mechanism: Fed lowers reserve requirements → releases $9.8B in "excess"
  reserves → banks use reserves to buy $9.8B in Gov't bonds from the Fed;
  plus the Fed would acquire an additional $7B in new bonds by mid-1962,
  which would also go to private banks
- ABA plan would let private banks create **$12 of new money for every $1**
  created by the Federal Reserve
- Fed's Board of Governors **endorsed** the bankers' plan, as did the
  Comptroller and other top banking officials — despite knowing it would
  shift earning assets from the Fed to private banks
- Bill passed, but House managers disclaimed any intent to reduce Fed
  holdings; as passed, allowed **$2.6 billion of vault cash** as reserves
  — equivalent to lowering reserve requirements by that amount
- Result: banks created ~**$15 billion** of new deposits and bought
  Government securities with the new money-creating power (mid-1960 to
  mid-1964)
- Patman's remedy: transfer $15 billion of Gov't securities from Fed to
  Treasury → cancel the debt → reduce public debt by $15B and cut annual
  interest by **over half a billion dollars**

> "There seems to be considerable agreement that the Federal Reserve banks
> should work toward a reduction of their enormous holdings of Government
> obligations. At the present time the Reserve banks hold about $24 billion
> of Government obligations, an amount far in excess of their needs either
> for earnings or for credit control." — ABA Economic Policy Commission

## Chapter IX: What Is Monetary Policy?

### Active vs. Passive Monetary Policy
- **Passive monetary policy**: no day-to-day discretion; money supply
  governed by automatic rules (e.g., grow at 3%/year tied to economy)
  — like a thermostat
- **Active monetary policy**: central bank deliberately adjusts money
  supply valve to influence economic conditions — the U.S. approach
- **Tight money**: restricts money supply → decreases availability,
  raises price (interest rates)
- **Easy money**: opposite — expands supply, lowers rates

### Asymmetry of Monetary Policy — "Pushing on a String"
- **Key insight**: monetary policy is not symmetrical — tight money can
  always choke a boom; easy money cannot always revive a depression
- During depression: business prospects so bleak that firms won't borrow
  even at rock-bottom rates; banks won't lend to risky borrowers; both
  sides paralyze the credit channel
- **"Push on a string"** analogy: Fed can pull (restrict) but cannot push
  (stimulate) when the economy is prostrate
- 1930s confirmed: enormous excess reserves sat unused — banks had the
  capacity but not the will to lend

### History of Active Monetary Policy in the U.S.
- **1913 Federal Reserve Act**: intended to be passive — automatic money
  supply tied to "eligible paper" (commercial loan demand); no active
  discretion envisioned
- **1920**: Fed informally coerced banks to call in loans — "voluntary"
  conspiratorial credit contraction that worsened the **1920–21
  depression**; largest credit volume before WWII was reached in 1920
- **1921–1926**: Fed permitted money supply expansion during recovery
- **1927–1929**: policy of restraint; virtually no change in money supply
  Aug 1927–Aug 1929 — but credit fed into broker/speculative channels,
  not the broader economy; this was excess credit in a **sectoral** sense
- **1929–1933**: extraordinary tightening — **one-third decrease** in
  money supply; catastrophic and largely self-inflicted by Fed
- **Banking Act of 1935**: gave formal shape to FOMC; Fed officials
  simply *claimed* responsibility for "monetary policy" — the Act itself
  makes no mention of "monetary policy" or "monetary controls"

### The 1936–37 Blunder
- Veterans' **soldiers' bonus** certificates paid out August 1936 —
  several billion dollars of purchasing power injected into economy
- Patman had advocated paying the bonus immediately during the Depression
  (soldiers in France earned $1/day while civilian wages soared)
- Fed raised reserve requirements in June 1936, anticipating
  "inflationary" bonus payments — reduced money supply by almost the
  **exact amount veterans received**
- Result: economy with **17% unemployment** was further hobbled;
  plunged into the **1937–38 relapse** — classic proof that easy money
  cannot stimulate a depression but tight money can deepen one
- The 1937 blunder became the **canonical example of "pushing on a
  string"** — excess reserves had been large throughout 1935–39, yet
  the economy did not recover; it was the Fed's tight policy (not
  excess reserves) that caused the 1937 collapse

### Eisenhower Era — Active Policy as Anti-Inflation Tool
- Post-1953: U.S. used active monetary policy almost exclusively for
  **inflation control** and regulation of business activity
- Tight money 1955: cooled consumer durable buying surge
- Tight money spring–summer 1957: restrained business investment →
  recession **July 1957–August 1958**
- Tight money late 1959–early 1960 → recession **May 1960–February 1961**
- Late 1961–early 1962: rates rose again despite low consumer demand and
  low business investment — new reason: **dollar outflow** to foreign
  deposits and securities
- Late 1962: money supply increased → interest rate rise stabilized —
  possibly a "happy accident"
- Key principle stated: **money stock must grow with the economy** — this
  is the first commandment of monetary policy, active or passive

## Chapter IX (continued): The Fed's Postwar Policy Record

### WWII Policy Context (pp. 101-102)
- During WWII, Fed used **selective credit controls** under wartime authority
  (prescribed min. down payments, max. terms on consumer durables)
- Core WWII policy: provide whatever money/credit the economy needed
  to maximize weapons production and meet civilian needs
- Fed maintained market yield on long-term Govt bonds at max **2½%**
  through March 1951; 91-day Treasury bill rate held below ½ of 1%
- Key lesson from WWII/Depression: the Depression was caused not by
  private sector failure but by **Government's failure to recognize its
  role** in coordinating resources — if coordinated properly, private
  enterprise can deliver full employment and max production
- **Employment Act of 1946**: declared it national policy to "coordinate
  and utilize all its plans, functions, and resources… to foster and
  promote free competitive enterprise… maximum employment, production,
  and purchasing power"
- Patman was the House author of the Employment Act of 1946 —
  firsthand witness that monetary coordination was assumed to continue

### The Treasury-Fed Accord of March 4, 1951

- **Fall 1950**: Korean War begins; Fed officials staged a revolt against
  Treasury/President policy — Open Market Committee met Aug. 18, 1950,
  decided unilaterally to raise discount rate and go "their own way"
  on long-term bond rates, defying the President and Treasury Secretary
- **January 1951**: President called Fed Chairman (**Marriner Eccles**)
  and entire FOMC to White House; assurances of rate stability given
- Eccles then publicly contradicted those assurances — FOMC reduced
  long-term bond buying anyway, raising rates
- President formed a four-man committee (Director of Defense
  Mobilization, Treasury Secretary, Fed Chairman, CEA Chairman) to
  find compromise; hoped to maintain existing rates until report
- Before the committee could report (~10 days), Treasury and Fed
  officials reached a so-called "accord," signed March 3, released
  March 4, 1951
- **Key personnel drama**: Eccles' term as Chairman expired Jan. 31;
  Truman refused to reappoint him specifically due to his role in
  raising short-term rates the prior 6 months; replaced by
  **Thomas B. McCabe** (a Republican Board member)
- **John Snyder** (Treasury Secretary) was hospitalized during the
  negotiations; **William McChesney** (Under Secretary) signed for Treasury
- McCabe then resigned; Truman appointed **William McChesney Martin**
  as Chairman

### What the Accord Actually Said vs. What It Became
- At signing, the President and Treasury Secretary believed it was
  a settlement close to the Treasury's position — NOT a grant of
  permanent Fed independence or license to raise rates dramatically
- In the first month after the accord, long-term rate barely moved;
  by December 1952 (end of Truman administration), long-term rate
  still only 2¾% (vs. 2.47% in March 1951)
- **Revisionist myth**: Fed officials later created the impression that
  pre-accord policy was inflationary disaster; **Alvin Hansen**
  (Harvard, one of the most influential American economists of 35 years)
  refuted this in 1957, citing the actual data:
  - Fed holdings of Govt securities were $5.1B *less* in June 1950
    than December 1946 — money supply did *not* increase
  - Currency plus demand deposits: $110.2B in June 1950, $110B in
    December 1946 — essentially flat
  - Wholesale prices in June 1950 = same level as September 1947
    (3 years of price stability)
  - The price surge following Korea **stopped a month before the accord**
    — wholesale index peaked February 13, 1951

> "It would be difficult to find statements more misleading than those cited above… The reader is lead to believe that there was a continued spiral of rising costs and prices all through this period." — Alvin Hansen, *The American Economy*, 1957

### The "Bills Only" Policy (pp. 107-110)
- Post-accord, Fed adopted **"bills only" policy** (formally recommended
  Nov. 1952, kept secret until 1954, practiced for 8 years)
- **Definition**: FOMC would confine open market operations exclusively
  to very short-term Govt securities, preferably 91-day Treasury bills
- Rationale: long-term rates would be affected only indirectly, with a
  time lag — the market would be "free" to set long-term rates
- **Contradiction**: Fed's specific economic targets (business investment,
  housing, construction) could only be hit via *long-term* rates; yet
  bills-only could only directly affect *short-term* rates — the policy
  was maximally ineffective for its own stated goals

- **1957 case study**: Fed wanted to dampen "investment boom" — used
  bills-only to tighten credit broadly; consumer rates rose, thousands
  of small firms bankrupted, unable to carry inventories; yet long-term
  investment boom was already staggering by early 1957 and collapsed
  — economy slid into stagnation the Fed helped cause
- **1958 case study**: Fed reversed, tried to stimulate via easy money;
  injected reserves into banks → short-term rates fell but long-term
  rates stayed up; bond prices rose so gently that a speculative binge
  developed — elevator boys, used car dealers, professionals all
  borrowing short-term to buy long-term Govt bonds; then mid-1958
  reversal → **"billions of high-riding dollars were lost"** in the
  resulting bond market debacle
- Bills-only abandoned **February 1961** under Congressional/Kennedy pressure

### Why Bills-Only Was Finally Killed (p. 110)
- Early 1961: U.S. in recession + **balance-of-payments deficit** →
  dollars flowing abroad seeking higher foreign short-term rates
- Conflict: recession demanded lower short-term rates; but lowering
  short-term rates would intensify dollar outflows abroad
- Solution: abandon bills-only, drive down *long-term* rates directly
  (what businesses and mortgages actually borrow at) while keeping
  short-term rates stable to stem dollar outflow — this worked
- Kennedy/Johnson administrations then shifted from pure monetary policy
  to fiscal tools: **$11 billion tax cut of 1964**, accelerated
  depreciation schedules, investment tax credit (1962)
- **Interest Equalization Tax**: a tax on long-term foreign investment
  in U.S. securities — fiscal substitute for raising long-term rates;
  cut foreign borrowing sharply without crushing domestic investment

### The 13-Year Postaccord Verdict (pp. 111-117)
- Since the accord: interest rates have **climbed steadily** with only
  slight interruptions — a continual income transfer from the rest of
  the country to banks, financial institutions, and individuals with
  significant interest income
- **Fed's inflation justification scrutinized**: industrial wholesale
  price index shows zero hint of inflation since 1958 through 1964
  — unemployment ranged 4.3%–6.7% over 1957–63 (Table 4)
- Patman's argument: if no-inflation claim is true, the Fed is
  implicitly admitting it can only prevent inflation by **crippling
  economic growth** and maintaining widespread unemployment and idle
  capacity — that is the price it asks the country to pay
- **Balance-of-payments cost**: high short-term rates to stem $2B/yr
  capital outflow cost the economy many billions in lost domestic
  investment; the "interest equalization tax" shows a fiscal tool
  could have done this at near-zero economic cost
- **Low-investment economy result**: with perpetually high interest,
  the U.S. became a low-investment economy — business investment as %
  of GNP declined (Table 5); this means slower growth, lower
  efficiency gains, smaller additions to capacity — a critical choice
  made by an unelected body

### The Hidden Deficit Enlargement (pp. 115-116)
- Fiscal year 1963: Govt paid ~$10B in interest on the national debt;
  deficit was $8.8B — those who decried the deficit also supported
  higher interest rates that *caused* much of it
- **Table 6**: if 1940s interest rates (avg. 1.8%) had prevailed
  through 1963, the Govt would have saved $40.1 billion total in
  interest payments over 17 postwar years — about **13% of the
  total national debt**
- Excess interest charges now ~$5B/year, almost half going to banks,
  financial institutions, and corporations
- High interest *defeats* anti-deficit goals: it raises the cost of
  carrying the debt faster than any plausible spending cut

> "Raising interest rates is not a simple solution to straightforward economic problems, because interest will not work in a simple fashion. In the deficit case, high interest actually defeats the very purpose of those who say they fear the presumed inflationary potential of Government deficits and want tight money."

### Two Broad Conclusions on Monetary Policy (pp. 116-117)
1. **Active independent monetary policy is a costly luxury**: an agency
   that takes its own economic readings and acts unilaterally without
   consultation uses the "monetary sledge hammer to crack economic
   policy walnuts"
2. **Self-sufficient central banks structurally drift toward
   high-interest policy**: not from malice but from the logic of their
   control system — inflation is the *only* economic ailment directly
   susceptible to monetary cure; unemployment, stagnation, and recession
   cannot be laid uniquely at the central bank's door; therefore the
   bank concentrates on the one problem it can claim to solve (inflation)
   via the one tool it has (keeping rates high and the economy
   somewhat sluggish)

> "If honorable men look for inflation hard enough, they can convince themselves they have found it. Most of us then pay the price of being preserved from a monster which the evidence suggests is a mere phantom."

## Chapter X: What Improvements Are Needed in the Money System?

### Framing the Reform Agenda (pp. 118-119)
- Money system is man-made ("the creature of law" — Lincoln) → no
  reason to treat it as perfect or fixed
- Monetary reform has historically happened **only in crises** — panics
  or breakdowns focus public attention enough to overwhelm bankers'
  resistance; between crises, money becomes mysterious and uninteresting
  to the public and legislators, allowing partial reversals of reforms
- Most proposed reforms do NOT require new legislation — they are
  implied responsibilities of the Federal agencies under existing law
- Central goal: bring monetary management under **genuine public control**
  to coordinate it with other public policies — this was the original
  intent of the Federal Reserve Act

### The Federal Reserve and "Independence" (pp. 119-122)
- **What Fed independence means in practice**: Fed policymakers produce
  their own separate economic diagnosis, set their own priority list,
  and act on their own conclusions — with no obligation to support
  Executive or Congressional policies
- The inevitable result: **policy clashes** — different groups
  distilling different trends from the same economic data; if the
  Fed's priority schedule differs from the President's, it's "sheer
  luck" if the accelerator and brake pedals aren't both pressed at once
- **Congress analogy**: Congress is also "independent" — but every
  2 years the House and 1/3 of Senate face election; policies
  approved or rejected by the people. Congress is accountable.
- **Fed's accountability gap**: does not present annual report to
  Congress explaining/justifying policy; does not ask Congressional
  approval; provides its own revenues from interest on Govt bond
  holdings (no appropriations needed → escapes budget control);
  not subject to executive review; not elected; resists GAO audit
  (argues money/credit management is above ordinary law)
- **"Fourth branch of government"**: Fed exercises wide economic power
  in both formulation and execution; democratic control would ordinarily
  be assumed — but this is not the case; some Fed officials use
  "quasi-judicial" language to describe their role

### The Fed's Independence Argument — Decoded (p. 122)
- **William McChesney Martin** (Fed Chairman): the Fed is insulated
  from "shortsighted pressures for abuse of that money"
- **Alfred Hayes** (NY Fed president): unpopular long-term measures
  require freedom from "partisan influences" and "shortsighted pressures"
- **Patman's translation**: "unpopular action" = tight money policy
  that hurts many people; "prevent abuse" = prevent inflation;
  "shortsighted pressures" = the preferences of elected representatives
  and the voters who elected them → the public must cede monetary
  control to unelected trustees who will do for the people what the
  people would not do for themselves
- **Banking community** strongly champions Fed independence — Patman
  argues this is because Fed policy consistently favors the financial
  sector's view of the economy; bankers deeply mistrust democratic
  governments in money matters and are convinced economic wisdom is
  the possession of a special few

## Chapter (cont.): The Case Against Fed Independence

### The Inflation Alibi

- The Fed and financial press warn constantly that independence must be
  "preserved" to prevent inflation — but Patman calls this alibi dubious
- **Contrarian claim**: The only true inflations of the past 25 years
  (WWII through 1946, early Korean War) were caused by wartime demand,
  not by monetary looseness — and could only have been stopped by
  higher taxes or price/rationing controls, *not* tight money
- The financial community never pushed for higher taxes to sop up
  purchasing power, never defended price/rationing controls at WWII's
  end, and never suggested raising reserve requirements — yet claims
  vigilance against inflation
- The real victims of inflation are low- and middle-income families on
  fixed incomes; the wealthy merely see some reduction in purchasing
  power of family fortunes
- In 13 years there has been no public outcry against tight money
  because press and trusted political figures assured the public it was
  necessary — a manufactured consent argument

### Monetary Policy Cannot Stand Alone

- A rising price level cannot normally be contained by monetary policy
  alone without severe damage to full employment and growth
- Even mild recessions won't achieve price stability; what works is
  **enlightened restraint** by business and labor in wage-price policies
- Presidents Kennedy and Johnson worked hard to maintain price stability
  through persuasion — the very "political" mechanism the Fed claims
  is inferior to its own judgment

### The Absurdity of the Independence Argument

- Fed claims monetary policymakers must be independent because their
  actions have "widespread effects and are frequently unpopular"
- But fiscal policy (taxation) is equally widespread and unpopular —
  yet Congress still raises taxes when necessary
  (e.g., Kennedy's $11 billion tax cut debated publicly, not farmed
  out to an independent "fiscal policy board")
- Foreign policy involves war and peace — far more consequential —
  yet no one proposes an unaccountable "foreign policy board"
- **Core democratic principle**: fiscal and foreign policymakers are
  held responsible to the people; monetary policy must be no different

> "We insist in our democracy — it is almost the essence of the system —
> that fiscal and foreign policymakers be held responsible, however
> indirectly, to the people for their policies. Why should monetary
> policy be treated differently?"

### Monetary-Fiscal Coordination

- Having the Fed formulate monetary policy uncoordinated with the
  President's economic program is "totally misguided"
- The President is elected on economic platforms (Kennedy: "getting the
  country moving again") — he should be able to fashion a total
  economic package including monetary policy
- The economy is like a **dual-control car with two drivers**: one
  (fiscal/President) insists on his independent right to use his own
  brake and accelerator — pure luck if the motor doesn't stall
- The Fed has deliberately pushed its brake when the President and
  Congress were pressing their accelerator — e.g., tight money in early
  Kennedy administration during a recession; and pressed the brake too
  hard in late 1959, contributing to the **1960 recession**

> *"This is no way to run economic policymaking."* — Both speed and
> direction signals must come from one, and only one, source.

### The Trustee Problem and Democratic Theory

- The Fed's implicit argument is the **trustee notion**: people don't
  know what's good for them, so an elite group should decide and act
  without accountability
- This is "papa knows best" thinking — utterly alien to American
  democratic principles
- Monetary economics is not nuclear physics; the fundamentals can be
  understood by anyone
- The Fed is asking for enormous economic power with no responsibility:
  "Trust us. We need this power unfettered by any responsibility
  to anyone."
- All power in a democracy derives from the people; holders of power
  are either directly or indirectly accountable through elected officials

> **Prof. Milton Friedman** (University of Chicago, adviser to Senator
> Goldwater), at 1964 House Banking and Currency subcommittee hearings:
> "Should there be a truly 'independent' monetary authority? A fourth
> branch of the constitutional structure? … it is most undesirable
> politically to give so much power in individuals not subject to close
> control by the electorate." (*"The Federal Reserve After 50 Years,"*
> pp. 1133–1134)

> **Prof. Paul Samuelson** (MIT, economic adviser to President Kennedy,
> 1960 campaign):
> "A central bank that is not responsible is irresponsible rather than
> independent… But it does mean being responsive to the changing values,
> views, moods, and even fads of the American citizenry."
> (*"The Federal Reserve After 50 Years,"* pp. 1107–1110)

- Both left and right economists agreed: an unaccountable monetary
  authority is constitutionally and democratically indefensible

### The Fed's Unauthorized Power — Legal Analysis

- Congress never gave the Fed authority to determine monetary policy
  — the **Federal Reserve Act of 1913** was designed on the
  **full convertibility theory**: automatically supply whatever currency
  the economy needed; restrict nothing
- The 1913 Act gave Fed banks the task of **discounting eligible paper**
  to supply member banks with credit needed for business and trade;
  the Board could only review and set **discount rates**
- After the **Banking Act of 1935**, Fed officials began proclaiming
  "responsibility" for national monetary policies — but the 1935 act
  contained no such provision; it referenced no change to the
  convertibility concept
- **The Fed's "monetary policies" as practiced today were never
  authorized by law**
- The monetary powers are reserved to Congress by the Constitution;
  Congress can delegate them, but the Supreme Court has held
  unconstitutional grants of power without adequate law defining and
  limiting the purposes — just as the Court struck down the **NRA**
- Patman's view: if legally challenged, the Fed's monetary policies
  could be held unconstitutional

### The "Power Revolution" — Two Unauthorized Permutations

1. **Active regulator**: Fed transformed from passive money supplier to
   active regulator of economic activity — without authorization
2. **Open Market Committee**: shift to open market operations gave
   voting rights on monetary policy to **five regional bank presidents**
   (chosen by private bankers) plus persuasion rights to all 12
   presidents — directly contrary to the expressed intent of the Act

- **The regional bank presidents have become policymakers** — and
  private bankers select them; they are unlikely to choose presidents
  whose monetary views don't conform to banker preferences
- By the 1930s, monetary policy was decided by men some of whom were
  selected by private interests — then the **1951 Accord** made it worse:
  the Fed gained full independence *and* lost the minimum virtue of
  being composed solely of public servants

### Summary Indictment of Fed Independence

- Independence serves no useful purpose
- Based on erroneous views of public maturity
- Flies in the face of democratic institutions
- Creates irrational divisions of responsibility in economic policymaking
- Violates the spirit of the Constitution
- Represents a presumptuous power grab
- Is **unauthorized by law**

> "Central bank independence should be tolerated no longer. The central
> bank must be brought back into the Government."

## Needed Federal Reserve Reforms

*[Subcommittee on Domestic Finance press release, June 28, 1964 —
signed by all 8 Democratic members including Chairman Wright Patman,
Henry S. Reuss, Claude Pepper, Joseph G. Minish, et al.]*

### A. Public Character Reforms
- **Retire Federal Reserve stock** (eliminate private ownership)
- **Vest all open market operations power in the Federal Reserve Board**
  (removing regional bank president votes)
- Make Chairman's term coterminous with President's
- Reduce Governors to 5; reduce terms to 5 years with reappointment
  allowed; remove requirement of geographic/sectoral representation —
  require only that Governors be "men of integrity devoted to the
  public interest"

### B. Effectiveness / Economic Knowledge
- Remove present Governor selection criteria favoring financial,
  agricultural, industrial, commercial interests

### C. Public Control of Expenditures
- **Public audit** by Comptroller General of all Fed expenditures
- Fed to pay into Treasury all capital gains and interest received from
  U.S. Government securities
- Congressional appropriations for Fed expenses

### D. Statutory Guidelines — Employment Act of 1946
- Require President to set forth monetary policy guidelines in his
  Economic Reports
- Express sense of Congress that the Fed operate in the open market to
  facilitate Presidential monetary policy; if Fed diverges, it must
  file a **statement of reasons** (like the President's Economic Report)
- Transfer bank supervisory functions to Comptroller of the Currency,
  FDIC, or a new Federal banking authority — let the Fed concentrate
  solely on monetary policy

### E. Money Supply Studies
- Fed should study whether its present methods of controlling money
  supply are defective (economists had observed divergences between
  what Fed claims and what actually happened)

### F. Reappraise Tight Money
- Fed should consider alternate ways to achieve economic goals besides
  raising interest rates — raising rates transfers billions of dollars
  from low/middle-income family budgets to interest-income families
- Raising rates also adds ~$11 billion/year to cost of carrying the
  Federal debt

### G–H. Debt Management and Securities
- Fed should **buy a larger portion of new U.S. securities directly
  from the Treasury**, then sell them in the open market when propitious
  — eliminating the private securities dealer intermediary and its
  profit/cost markup
- Most industrial nations' central banks already do this; isolating the
  central bank from debt management is "an absurdity indeed"
- During WWII: government-supporting Fed held interest on 91-day bills
  at 3/8% and long-term bonds below 2½% — demonstrating this is feasible

### I. Rebalance Money-Creating Power
- Currently the Fed has divided money-creating power ~1-to-7 between
  Government and private banks (private banks hold ~7x more Government
  securities than the Fed)
- Goal: gradually shift to a **1-to-4 ratio** — achieved by raising
  reserve requirements back to 1953 levels
- Patman has asked high experts for one plausible reason the Government
  should extend money-creating power to private banks at no cost
  so banks can lend back to the Government at interest — and received
  none

### J. Revitalize Discount Window
- Fed should extend more credit through the **12 regional Federal
  Reserve banks via discounting eligible paper** — making it a right
  rather than a privilege
- Current open market operations funnel reserves through **21 New York
  securities dealers** — the new reserves go to New York banks first,
  regardless of where credit is actually needed
- Example: an insurance company in Omaha selling Government securities
  to a dealer sends credit to Omaha banks whether Nebraska needs it or
  not; banks in Peoria may lack credit while Omaha has excess
- A bank presenting eligible paper at its regional Fed bank provides
  the best evidence of where credit is needed

### K. Create a Truly Open Market
- The Fed's "open market" is actually a **very closed market** — only
  21 professional dealers, with minimum public knowledge of its
  existence
- High Government officials, bankers, and Wall Street operators were
  unaware the so-called open market existed

## Needed FDIC Reforms

- FDIC's function should be **restricted to deposit insurance** — it
  should not substitute examiners' judgment for private management's
  lending decisions
- Banks serving small business must assume prudent risks; they cannot
  confine lending to gold-plated, doubly secured loans as FDIC
  examiners insist
- Analogy: life insurance companies have a stake in policyholders'
  health but cannot dictate diet and lifestyle — same principle applies
- FDIC should promulgate **objective standards of eligibility** for
  deposit insurance and issue it to any comers who meet those standards
  — not maintain a closed shop for existing banks

---

## Needed Treasury Reforms

- With Fed managing debt properly, Treasury's practice of
  **seeking advice from buyers** (underwriters/profiteers) on new
  obligation pricing will be eliminated
- Key remaining problem: Treasury leaves an average **$4 billion in
  deposits with private banks interest-free** — ostensibly to
  compensate banks for services
- This arrangement forces the Federal debt to be higher than necessary;
  taxpayers pay interest on excess debt to compensate banks who then
  lend those funds to others at interest
- If banks render substantial services, appropriate fees should be
  negotiated and paid directly — Patman introduced legislation to
  this effect

## Object of Proposed Reforms — Commercial Banks as Quasi-Public Institutions

- The guiding principle: encourage commercial bank lending to business
  and consumers; discourage commercial bank lending to the Government
- Commercial banks are **"highly socialistic institutions"** — they
  "live off the Government," using its money-creating power free of
  cost and receiving subsidies from the public purse
- **Marriner Eccles** (then Fed Chairman), opposing Patman's 1941/1943
  proposal to have the Fed purchase war obligations interest-free:
  argued bank profits were low and costs rising, so banks would need
  to increase service charges — Patman called this point "well taken"
- The essential justification for extending money-creating power to
  private banks: they provide a necessary public service by making
  loans involving judgment and risk — something Government cannot
  do on a non-arbitrary basis
- The **only** reason Government should extend money-creating powers
  to private banks is to guarantee enough bank profits to assure
  adequate banking services for the general public benefit
- When banks use that power primarily to lend to the Government rather
  than to business and consumers, the justification disappears
