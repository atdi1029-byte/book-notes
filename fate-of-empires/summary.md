# The Fate of Empires and Search for Survival

**Author:** Sir John Bagot Glubb (Glubb Pasha)
**Published:** 1977
**Pages:** 79
**Category:** History / Political Philosophy

## Summary

Sir John Glubb, a British officer who spent 36 years commanding Arab forces in Iraq and Jordan, draws on his intimate knowledge of Middle Eastern history to identify a striking pattern: every great empire in recorded history has lasted approximately 250 years -- about ten human generations -- regardless of its technology, political system, or geographic reach. From Assyria to Britain, the lifecycle follows the same six stages: the Age of Pioneers, Conquests, Commerce, Affluence, Intellect, and Decadence.

The essay's power lies in its specific historical parallels. Tenth-century Baghdad mirrors modern London with eerie precision: pop singers, feminist movements, a five-day work week, materialist students, and corrupt officials all appeared in the Arab Empire at the same stage of decline as they appear in the West today. Glubb argues that decadence is not physical but moral -- a disease of selfishness bred by too long a period of wealth and power. The markers are always the same: defensiveness, pessimism, frivolity, an influx of foreigners, the welfare state, and a weakening of religion.

The companion essay, "Search for Survival," shifts from diagnosis to prescription, arguing that Britain (and the West) can reverse course through a revival of duty, self-sacrifice, personal initiative, and spiritual devotion. Glubb is skeptical that intellectual solutions alone can save a declining nation -- only a change of heart, not a change of policy, offers genuine hope.
