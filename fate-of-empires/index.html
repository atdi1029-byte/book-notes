Glossary

Empire Lifecycle Stages

Age of Pioneers (The Outburst): The explosive initial phase when a poor, hardy, obscure people suddenly erupts onto the world stage with extraordinary energy and courage. Example: the Arab tribes bursting from the desert in 633 AD to simultaneously attack both superpowers of their era.

Age of Conquests: The period of military expansion following the outburst, when the new power subdues vast territories and establishes dominion. Marked by courage, initiative, and willingness to improvise.

Age of Commerce: The phase when unified territories generate commercial prosperity. Military traditions still hold, but the desire to make money gradually overtakes the pursuit of glory and honour.

Age of Affluence: The turning point where accumulated wealth begins to corrode duty and public service. Education shifts from producing brave patriots to producing graduates who command the highest salaries.

Age of Intellect: Surplus wealth funds universities and academic pursuits. Discussion and debate proliferate, but the power of decisive action declines. Every declining empire sees an explosion of intellectual activity.

Age of Decadence: The final stage, marked by defensiveness, pessimism, materialism, frivolity, influx of foreigners, the welfare state, and weakening of religion. Not a physical decline but a moral and spiritual one.

Key Concepts

250-Year Rule: Glubb's central finding that the average lifespan of a great empire is approximately 250 years, or ten human generations of 25 years each. This figure has remained consistent across 3,000 years of recorded history regardless of technology or political system.

The Human Yardstick: The generation (~25 years), the only unit of measurement unchanged since the Assyrians. Ten generations equals one empire's lifespan.

High Noon: The transitional peak where the Age of Conquests blends into the Age of Affluence. Immense wealth dazzles observers, but beneath the surface, greed is replacing duty. Examples: Augustus in Rome, Harun al-Rashid in Baghdad, Queen Victoria in Britain.

Providential Turnover: Glubb's theory that the rotation of dominant civilisations serves a divine purpose: each sovereign race spreads its unique cultural contributions worldwide before being replaced, ensuring no single culture permanently monopolises humanity's character.

Decadence of a System: The crucial distinction that decline is systemic, not biological. Citizens of a decadent nation who emigrate to new surroundings become productive members of their adopted countries. The disease is environmental, not genetic.

Learning from History

The central premise of Sir John Glubb's essay is deceptively simple: if four thousand years of human history were studied thoroughly and impartially, the lessons available would be priceless. The problem is that nobody does this. History as it is taught in schools and universities is fractured into narrow periods and single countries, governed by national vanity rather than objective inquiry. Students learn endlessly about the Tudors and the Stewarts, the Battle of Crecy and Guy Fawkes, but never study the continuous flow of human development that would reveal the deeper patterns beneath these isolated episodes.

Three failures cripple historical study. First, it is limited to short periods or single nations. Second, national histories invariably cast ancestors as patriotic heroes and enemies as imperialists or rebels. Third, world history courses leap from Greece to Rome with barely a glance at the civilisations that filled the centuries between them. The result is that each generation believes its circumstances are unprecedented, when in fact the same patterns have repeated with remarkable consistency across three millennia. Physical science advanced through meticulous experiments and carefully recorded results, each generation building on its predecessors. Historical work, by contrast, remains dominated by emotion and prejudice.

"The only thing we learn from history is that men never learn from history." -- Common saying, quoted by Glubb

Having established the failure of historical education, Glubb turns to his central discovery. He uses the word "empire" not in the British Empire sense of a home-country plus overseas colonies, but simply to mean a great power or superpower -- a nation that dominates the international scene of its era. Most empires in history were large landblocks without significant overseas possessions. What matters is not the shape of the territory but the fact of dominance.

The data is striking. Glubb assembles a table of eleven empires spanning from Assyria (859-612 BC, 247 years) through Persia (208 years), Greece (231 years), the Roman Republic (233 years), the Roman Empire (207 years), the Arab Empire (246 years), the Mameluke Empire (267 years), the Ottoman Empire (250 years), Spain (250 years), Romanov Russia (234 years), to Britain (1700-1950, 250 years). Despite wildly different conditions -- different continents, different centuries, different technologies, different political systems -- the durations cluster around 250 years with remarkable consistency.

The most provocative element of this finding is what it rules out. Technology does not affect empire duration. The Assyrians fought on foot with spears and bows and lasted 247 years. The British fought with artillery, railways, and ocean-going ships and lasted 250 years. Technology shaped the shape of empires -- Assyrians conquered land-accessible neighbours while the British conquered water-accessible continents -- but it had no measurable effect on how long any empire lasted. This 250-year span equates to roughly ten human generations of 25 years each, making the generation the only unit of measurement unchanged since antiquity.

Political systems are equally irrelevant to lifespan. Past empires display nearly every possible variation of government: absolute monarchy, military dictatorship, republic, slave-soldier oligarchy, constitutional monarchy. All pass through the same sequence of stages in approximately the same time. The Mameluke Empire was ruled entirely by former slaves imported as boys from the Russian Steppes, trained as soldiers, who seized power by murdering their master. Even this bizarre system produced 267 years of greatness before collapsing through the identical stages of affluence, internal rivalry, and decay that brought down every other empire.

Glubb acknowledges that the dates are somewhat arbitrary -- empires do not start and end on certain days, but normally expand gradually before declining. But the clustering around 250 years is tight enough to justify treating it as a real phenomenon requiring explanation, not a statistical coincidence. The rest of his essay is that explanation: a theory of the six stages through which every empire passes on its journey from outburst to decadence.

The Outburst

The birth of an empire is sudden, violent, and almost always unforeseen. Glubb calls it the outburst -- the moment when an obscure, impoverished people erupts onto the world stage with extraordinary energy. In every case, the new conquerors are poor, hardy, and enterprising. The empires they overthrow are wealthy but defensive-minded. The pattern repeats with eerie precision across millennia.

Macedonia was an insignificant state north of Greece until Philip II came to power in 359 BC. Within a generation, his son Alexander the Great had destroyed the Persian Empire and established Macedonian dominion from the Danube to India. Around 600 AD, the world was divided between two superpowers -- the Eastern Roman Empire and the Persian Empire. The Arabs were despised, backward desert tribes with no government, no constitution, and no army. Then Prophet Muhammad preached from 613 to 632, and in 633 the Arabs burst from the desert, simultaneously attacking both superpowers. Within twenty years the Persian Empire had ceased to exist and the Arabs ruled from the Atlantic to the borders of India.

In 1211, the Mongols were savage tribes in the Mongolian steppes. Genghis Khan invaded China, and by 1253 the Mongols had established the largest empire the world had ever known, stretching from Asia Minor to the China Sea. In 1492, the agreement between Ferdinand and Isabella was signed after the fall of Granada. Within fifty years, Cortez had conquered Mexico and Spain had become the world's greatest empire.

"Fearless initiative characterises the conquering pioneers; readiness to improvise and experiment, untramelled by tradition." -- Sir John Glubb

What characterises these outbursts is not merely military victory but unresting enterprise in every field. Men hack through jungles, climb mountains, brave oceans in cockleshells. The Arabs crossed the Strait of Gibraltar in 711 AD with just 12,000 men, defeated a Gothic army more than twice their strength, marched 250 miles through unknown territory, and seized Toledo. At the equivalent stage in British history, Captain Cook was discovering Australia. The profile is always the same: poor, hardy, often half- starved, ill-clad, abounding in courage, energy, and initiative, overcoming every obstacle, always seeming to be in control of events.

What causes these outbursts? The easiest explanation is that poor races are tempted by the wealth of older civilisations. But Glubb identifies two distinct types of invasion. The first is mere plunder -- Attila and the Huns ravaged Europe from 450 to 453 AD, and when Attila died the empire fell apart immediately. The second is far more interesting: invasion driven by admiration. Many barbarians entering the Roman Empire did so not from greed but from cultural aspiration. They wanted to become Romans. This distinction between destruction and assimilation shapes the aftermath of every imperial collapse.

Glubb then offers a theological reading of the pattern. Whatever causes the overthrow of civilisations, certain benefits result from the rotation. Every race has distinctive characteristics in philosophy, art, religion, and law. During its period of pre-eminence, each culture's unique qualities are carried far and wide across the world. If the same nation retained dominance indefinitely, its peculiar qualities would permanently stamp the entire human race. Under the system of rotating empires, each great civilisation has time to spread its contributions before yielding to another people with entirely different gifts. To believers in divine Providence, this rotation appears as a manifestation of wisdom tending toward the slow, ultimate perfection of humanity.

The early stages of empire -- the Ages of Pioneers and Conquests -- shade gradually into the Age of Commerce. Conquest unifies vast territories under one government, which automatically generates commercial prosperity. Military traditions still hold and armies still guard the frontiers, but the desire to make money gradually overtakes the pursuit of glory. To the merchant, honour and glory are empty words that add nothing to the bank balance. The course of empire, Glubb tells us, can be summarised in nine words: the USA in the age of the pioneers -- a nation of frontiersmen, explorers, and doers whose energy would inevitably channel itself into commercial expansion and eventually into the comforts that commerce buys.

Commerce, Art, and Affluence

Wealth pours into the conquering nation almost effortlessly, and the problem becomes how to spend it. Art, architecture, and luxury find rich patrons. Splendid municipal buildings rise, palaces multiply, highways and railways and hotels transform the landscape. The first half of the Age of Commerce is what Glubb calls "peculiarly splendid" -- the ancient virtues of courage, patriotism, and duty still survive alongside the new wealth. Boys' schools are intentionally rough: frugal eating, hard living, breaking ice for cold baths, producing strong and fearless men for whom duty is the watchword. Great enterprise continues in exploration for new wealth, carrying forward some of the adventurous spirit of the conquest era.

But money is the agent of decline, and the process is gradual and almost imperceptible. The first injury is moral. Money replaces honour and adventure as the objective of the best young men. Ambition shifts from fame and service to cash. Education transforms accordingly: schools no longer aim to produce brave patriots but qualifications commanding the highest salaries. Ghazali, the great Arab moralist writing in the declining Arab Empire around 1100 AD, complained that students attended college "not to acquire learning and virtue, but to obtain those qualifications which will enable them to grow rich." Glubb notes drily that the same observation is everywhere evident in the modern West.

"The Age of Affluence silences the voice of duty. The ambition of the best young men shifts from fame and service to cash." -- Sir John Glubb

High Noon is Glubb's term for the transitional peak where the Age of Conquests blends into the Age of Affluence. The accumulated wealth dazzles observers. He names the exemplars: Augustus in Rome, Harun al-Rashid in Baghdad, Sulaiman the Magnificent in the Ottoman Empire, Queen Victoria in Britain, and perhaps Woodrow Wilson in the United States. Enough ancient virtues survive to defend the frontiers, but beneath the glittering surface, greed is replacing duty and public service. The change can be summarised in a single phrase: from service to selfishness.

The transition from affluence to decline is marked by the spread of defensiveness . The nation is no longer interested in glory but anxious to retain its wealth and luxury. The Great Wall of China, Hadrian's Wall, and the Maginot Line are all expressions of the same defensive psychology separated by centuries. Money being more available than courage, empires begin to subsidise their enemies rather than fight them. An intellectual device accompanies the military shift: aggression is rebranded as primitive and immoral, civilised peoples declare themselves "too proud to fight," and empires are proclaimed wicked. This enables a posture of moral superiority that masks the reality of weakness. But nations that proclaim themselves unwilling to fight are liable to be conquered by peoples still in the militarist stage.

Glubb is careful to note that each successive passion -- military glory, commercial wealth, intellectual achievement -- is legitimate and beneficial in itself. The puzzle is why they cannot all be pursued simultaneously in moderation. They never are. Each passion eclipses the one before it, and the trajectory is always the same: from courage to commerce to cleverness to collapse.

The Slide into Decline

With the Age of Intellect, surplus wealth funds knowledge on an unprecedented scale. Merchant princes endow colleges and universities -- a pattern repeated in empire after empire, divided by many centuries. Under Malik Shah, the Seljuk sultan of the eleventh century, the declining Arab Empire saw a passion for university-building: a university sprang up in every town where a handful had sufficed during the glory years. The modern parallel is unmistakable: Harvard, Yale, Oxford, and Cambridge once met Britain's and America's needs; now almost every city has a university. The trajectory of ambition shifts once more: from military glory to the accumulation of wealth to the acquisition of academic honours.

The paradox at the heart of Glubb's argument is that every period of decline is characterised by an expansion of intellectual activity. The Athens described in the Acts of the Apostles, where citizens "spent their time in nothing else, but either to tell or to hear some new thing," was a declining city-state. Under Caliph Mamun in the ninth century, Arab scientists measured the circumference of the earth with remarkable accuracy -- seven centuries before Western Europe knew it was not flat. Yet less than fifty years after these brilliant discoveries, the Arab Empire collapsed. Science did not save the empire. The intellectuals survived the collapse and became servants of their illiterate conquerors. Rashid al-Din, the great Persian historian, served as Prime Minister under the Mongols but was obliged to wait on his knees before the Khan, receiving food scraps tossed over the Khan's shoulder at banquets.

"Amid a Babel of talk, the ship drifts on to the rocks." -- Sir John Glubb

The most dangerous by-product of the Age of Intellect is the belief that the human brain alone can solve the world's problems. Any organisation -- even a local bowls club -- requires self-sacrifice and service to survive. National survival depends on the loyalty and self-sacrifice of citizens, not mere cleverness. When intellect is cultivated at the cost of unselfishness, collapse follows. The head and the heart are natural rivals: the brilliant but cynical intellectual stands at the opposite end of the spectrum from the emotional self- dedication of the hero or martyr. There are times, Glubb observes, when the perhaps unsophisticated self-dedication of the hero is more essential than the sarcasms of the clever.

A counterintuitive symptom accompanies national decline: the intensification of internal political hatreds. One would expect external threats to unite a nation's factions; the reverse occurs. Byzantium, threatened and dominated by the Ottoman Turks in the fourteenth century, spent its last fifty years fighting repeated civil wars among its own factions while the Ottomans simply administered the final blow. The British Parliament, which formerly saw rival parties observing unwritten rules and addressing each other as "honourable gentlemen," descended into booing, shouting, and angry exchanges that spilled into the streets as strikes, boycotts, and demonstrations. As the nation weakens, internal rivalries become more acute, not less. The divisions are never reconciled to save the nation.

Symptoms of Decadence

A recurring phenomenon of great empires at their peak is the influx of foreigners to the capital city. Roman historians complained of Asians and Africans in Rome. Baghdad at its ninth-century peak was spectacularly cosmopolitan -- Persians, Turks, Arabs, Armenians, Egyptians, Africans, Greeks. London and New York followed the same pattern. During the Outburst and Age of Conquests, the nation is ethnically homogeneous, producing solidarity and comradeship. In the Ages of Commerce and Affluence, immigrants flood in, drawn by wealth. Glubb stresses that immigrants are not inferior -- merely different -- and that the differences introduce divisions. In acute emergencies, immigrants are often less willing to sacrifice their lives than descendants of the founding stock. Former conquered peoples, proud during High Noon, revive ancient grievances when decline sets in.

Frivolity is the companion of pessimism. Decline brings universal pessimism, which itself hastens decline: success once bred the self-confidence that sustained empires through crises. Republican Rome survived the Gauls sacking the city in 390 BC and the catastrophe of Cannae in 216 BC through sheer resolution. Later imperial Rome, deeply pessimistic, could not summon the same will. "Let us eat, drink and be merry, for tomorrow we die" becomes the prevailing mood, and mass spectacle fills the void: Roman bread and games, Byzantine chariot races where the Greens versus Blues rivalry became a matter of national crisis, and the modern equivalents of football and baseball. The heroes of declining nations are always the same: the athlete, the singer, the actor. The word "celebrity" now designates a comedian or footballer, not a statesman or literary genius.

"The heroes of declining nations are always the same -- the athlete, the singer, or the actor." -- Sir John Glubb

Glubb's most vivid case study is the decline of Baghdad. The city's High Noon came in the first half of the ninth century, when it was the greatest and richest city in the world. In 861, the Caliph Mutawakkil was murdered by his own Turkish mercenaries. A military dictatorship followed for thirty years. The empire fragmented, and Baghdad's authority shrank to Iraq alone. Contemporary tenth-century historians recorded the symptoms: indifference to religion, increasing materialism, lax sexual morals, corruption of officials, and politicians amassing fortunes while in office.

The parallels with the modern West are what make Glubb's essay unforgettable. Pop singers in Baghdad accompanied erotic songs on the lute -- an instrument resembling a modern guitar. Obscene sexual language spread through the culture. Caliphs issued bans on pop singers, who always returned within a few years. Women demanded admission to professions monopolised by men. The historian Ibn Bessam complained: "What have the professions of clerk, tax-collector or preacher to do with women?" Many women practised law, became university professors, and agitated for female judges. At the moment of economic crisis following the 861 military takeover, the people of Baghdad introduced a five-day working week. Glubb's reaction on first reading the sources: "I could scarcely believe my eyes. The descriptions might have been taken out of The Times today."

The citizen's of dominant empires invariably believe their pre-eminence will last forever. Rome was the "mistress of the world for all time." The Abbasid Caliphs were "appointed by God to rule until the day of judgment." Victorian Britons assumed the same. Hitler promised a thousand-year Reich. The belief in permanent superiority encourages the attribution of success to hereditary virtue -- a "race of supermen" illusion that leads to the employment of cheap foreign labour for menial work and foreign mercenaries to fight battles, while the imperial people relax into leisure and amusement. The modern Western version is the idea that progress will be automatic without effort and that every year will naturally show a rise in the standard of living.

The age of decline often shows philanthropy and sympathy for other races -- the imperial people enjoy playing what Glubb calls "Lady Bountiful." The Roman Empire threw open equal citizenship to all peoples; provincials became senators and emperors. Under Harun al-Rashid, the Arab government provided grants to university students and opened the first free public hospital in Baghdad -- the world's first welfare-state institution. Under his son Mamun, free hospitals spread across the Arab world from Spain to what is now Pakistan. The welfare state appears as the high-water mark of human attainment, but Glubb suggests it may be merely one more regular milestone in the life of an ageing empire. The assumption of permanent wealth leads to lavish benevolence until the economy collapses and the universities close and the hospitals fall to ruin.

Systems, Ideology, and Religion

Western nations attach immense importance to the ideology of political institutions and pour scorn on countries whose systems differ from their own democracy. But the life-expectancy of a great nation does not appear to be affected in any way by the nature of its institutions. Past empires show nearly every possible variation of political system, and all pass through the same sequence of stages in approximately the same time. The Mameluke Empire provides the most precisely datable example in history: exactly 267 years, beginning and ending on fixed days. Its ruling class was recruited entirely from young boys born in southern Russia, imported as slaves, and trained as professional soldiers under the Ayoubid sultans . On 1 May 1250 they mutinied, murdered Turan Shah, and seized power. They defeated the previously invincible Mongols, drove them from Syria, captured Acre in 1291 to end the Crusades, and built the finest army in the world. Then came a century of fabulous wealth, luxury, bitter internal rivalries, and collapse by Ottoman military defeat in 1517. Even this "illogical and fantastic" political system followed the same stages.

"The life-expectancy of a great nation does not appear to be in any way affected by the nature of its institutions." -- Sir John Glubb

Religion, broadly defined as the human feeling that some invisible Power controls life and the natural world, follows a predictable arc within the imperial lifecycle. The Age of Conquests is typically accompanied by religious fervour -- even Genghis Khan claimed God had delegated him to exterminate decadent civilisations, framing conquest as holy sacrifice. Money gradually dissolves the spirit of service and duty that religious conviction sustained. Decline produces pessimism, which produces frivolity and sensual indulgence as despair's by-products, which in turn produces a nostalgic yearning for the "days of religion." But seeds of religious revival are quietly sown at the height of vice and frivolity. After generations of suffering, selfishness is purged and religion regains its sway, beginning a new era.

Glubb addresses the question of what happens when all races have exhausted their turn at dominance. The answer is that history creates new peoples. Invaders and conquered populations intermarry and produce new races. The barbarian invasions of Rome, the Arab conquests of Spain and Persia, the Turkish conquests, and the Norman Conquest of England all resulted in new ethnic combinations. The modern equivalent is migration rather than armed invasion. The United States is the obvious example of a new nation formed by the transformation of ethnic content through what Glubb calls "modern nomadism."

One of the essay's most important distinctions is that decadence is the disintegration of a system, not of its individual members . Citizens corrupted by too much money and power for too long become selfish and idle within their national framework. But when those same individuals emigrate to entirely new surroundings, they do not remain decadent -- they become normal citizens of their adopted countries, and their children and grandchildren may attain pre-eminence. The decline of a nation does not undermine the basic character of its members. Decadence is environmental and systemic, not biological. Nor is it physical: citizens of nations in decline are often physically larger and stronger than their barbarian invaders. British soldiers in World War I, raised in luxury, adapted readily to trench life. Decadence is a moral and spiritual disease resulting from too long a period of wealth and power, producing cynicism, decline of religion, pessimism, and frivolity. The decadent will not make efforts to save themselves because they are not convinced that anything in life is worth saving.

The Pattern Emerges

Glubb presents a striking example of the insularity of Western historical thinking. A television director vetoed a proposed series on Arab history, asking: "What earthly interest could the history of medieval Arabs have for the general public today?" Yet Arab imperial history -- conquest, commercialism, affluence, intellectualism, science, decadence -- is an exact precursor of British imperial history and lasted almost exactly the same time. If British historians a century ago had seriously studied the Arab Empire, they could have foreseen almost everything that happened in Britain down to 1976. The rise of empires is internally driven and therefore uniform; the nature of the fall depends on external circumstances and is therefore diverse. Rome split into Western and Eastern halves. The Arab Empire fragmented. The Mamelukes were conquered in a single campaign. Spain lost its colonies but survived as a nation. Romanov Russia was succeeded by the Soviet Union.

"If British historians a century ago had seriously studied the Arab Empire, they could have foreseen almost everything that has happened in Britain down to 1976." -- Sir John Glubb

The summary Glubb provides is a masterpiece of compression. The average empire lasts 250 years, roughly ten generations. This has not varied for 3,000 years. The six stages are the Age of Pioneers, Conquests, Commerce, Affluence, Intellect, and Decadence. Decadence is marked by defensiveness, pessimism, materialism, frivolity, an influx of foreigners, the welfare state, and a weakening of religion. It is caused by too long a period of wealth and power, selfishness, love of money, and the loss of a sense of duty. The life histories of great states are amazingly similar due to internal factors. Their falls are diverse, largely the result of external causes. History should be taught as the history of the human race, not of one small country or short period.

Could understanding the pattern forestall decline? Glubb is skeptical. Men bursting with energy cannot easily be restrained from subduing their neighbours. Men who see wealth open to them will not readily be prevented from pursuing it. Periods of wealth produce art, architecture, music, science, and literature that enrich humanity. Great empires, despite their wars and tragedies, often bring peace, security, and prosperity to vast territories. The rise and fall of nations may be inevitable and perhaps even divinely ordained. But knowing this would at least reduce the pointless hatred between factions fighting over developments that are beyond human control.

Search for Survival: Diagnosis

The companion essay, written in response to overwhelming reader mail after the first piece, opens with clarification. Glubb's intent was not pessimistic. Britain will continue to exist; the question is whether it will remain strong, united, and free, or become a nation of underlings and mendicants. The diagnosis must come first, then the remedy. He returns to his core complaint: we learn nothing from history because our studies are too narrow. His own daughter was studying "the history of the trade union movement in Britain" -- contemporary politics masquerading as history.

The chief cause of outbursts by backward races, Glubb now clarifies, is jealousy: the desire to share the advantages of older, wealthier civilisations. But why do these barbarian conquests so often succeed against overwhelming odds? Because old states solidify into rigid forms, weakening individual initiative and psychological drive. The Vietnam War demonstrated this: the Vietcong showed more initiative and enterprise than the Americans, despite the latter's vastly superior resources. Highly organised armies of old nations develop crippling overhead. Glubb offers a personal anecdote: a British military column declared well water unfit, and the RAF flew in water at enormous expense. Meanwhile, Arab irregulars marching ahead with a handful of dates each had already engaged the enemy.

"Any form of organisation is liable to weaken initiative. Therefore the more the State is a welfare state, the more it destroys the initiative and self-reliance of its citizens." -- Sir John Glubb

The welfare state's intentions are benevolent, but it was fear and want that produced individual initiative and drive. Remove those, and initiative dies. The pioneer felled trees, built his own cabin, defended it himself -- a real man relying entirely on his own efforts. In modern industrial society the individual is helpless: job encased in a closed shop, unable to leave without penalties, all needs provided by the state. The dilemma Glubb identifies is profound: how can an industrial nation avoid organising? How can it organise without destroying human freedom and initiative? Decline, he now amends, is due not only to too long a period of wealth and power but also to too much organisation and regimentation.

The decadent mentality no longer embarks on new enterprises. Instead it turns inward, quarrelling over the division of inherited wealth. While Britain's car workers struck, the French, Germans, Italians, and Japanese invaded and conquered the British car market. Intensified political in-fighting produces leaders who promise their supporters they will injure the other half of the nation. Glubb argues that the ruler must stand for the whole nation -- differences of opinion on policy are acceptable, but feuds that divide the country are fatal. He identifies the Crown as Britain's last impartial institution, noting that in 1649 the House of Commons executed the King and abolished the Lords, and the result was not democratic rule but ten years of military dictatorship under Cromwell.

Search for Survival: Strength and Leadership

Sea power, Glubb argues, is one of the most benevolent ways a superpower can promote peace and commerce -- less provocative than land garrisons in dependent territories. The objection that nuclear war makes naval bases irrelevant is dismissed: by the same logic, one should give up all planning. Maintaining British and American naval power worldwide would have been a major factor in preventing future conflicts. Europe's dependence on Middle Eastern oil transported around the Cape of Good Hope makes the withdrawal of the Royal Navy from the Persian Gulf and Indian Ocean particularly dangerous. Framing the question as "we can't afford it" is like a housewife saying she cannot buy food because of hire-purchase payments on a new car -- a question of priorities, not resources.

The modern tendency toward coercion is one of the most disastrous trends Glubb identifies. If you disagree with someone, the instinct is to force compliance through strikes, blockades, or boycotts. Everyone proclaims freedom of the individual yet immediately declares blockades when anyone differs -- every citizen aspires to be a little dictator while loudly denouncing dictatorship. Violence is self-defeating: it produces resentment, which produces counter-violence, which produces counter- counter-violence lasting centuries. Napoleon's wars led to the Franco-Prussian War of 1870, which led to both World Wars. Strongbow's invasion of Ireland in 1170 produced effects still visible eight centuries later. That violence solves nothing and only breeds more violence is, Glubb declares, a universal human law.

Yet the paradox remains: knowing violence breeds more violence, nations must still maintain strength, because many nations deliberately use violence for selfish ends. Reducing armed forces to fund social programmes is dangerously short-sighted -- it might lead to a war that destroys all social services and national independence. Strength wins respect in this world. The weak who preach pacifism are not merely ignored but exposed to resentment and contempt.

"Only readiness for self-sacrifice can enable a community to survive." -- Sir John Glubb

Early nineteenth-century European visitors to London were amazed by the intense pace and activity. By mid-century, that energy had transferred to New York. By 1956, Americans were walking as leisurely as Romans, executives taking two-hour lunches. Glubb identifies leadership as the antidote to declining energy. True leadership brings duties, not privileges. Leaders must not ask subordinates to do more than they do themselves. The key to morale is personal relationships: senior men must know their subordinates personally and foster comradeship and mutual pride. Ever-increasing paperwork keeps leaders at their desks, destroying the human relationships that are the actual substance of leadership. Glubb's personal rule commanding the Arab Legion: three days per week in the office, three days visiting men in the field, regardless of the size of the in-tray.

The outstanding characteristic of declining national life is selfishness. Money-making has become the chief object of existence. Selfishness extends beyond individuals to confederations, trade unions, and businesses, all pursuing their own interests at the expense of the community. Winston Churchill 's 1940 offer of "nothing but blood, toil, tears and sweat" stands as the model of constructive leadership -- rousing people to enthusiasm through a call to sacrifice, not buying their loyalty with promises of money. Bribery and corruption in public life represent another deeply alarming symptom. British civil servants were historically admired across the Middle East for never accepting bribes. Their recent decline signals the broader decay. No amount of intellectual cleverness can restore greatness to a nation that has lost its energy, initiative, honesty, and dedication to service.

Search for Survival: Women, Population, and Hope

Glubb notes that female prominence in public life increases during periods of national decline -- a pattern that surprised him and which he admits no historian has studied systematically. The Arab feminist movement ended at the close of the ninth century when the breakdown of public security made it unsafe for women to go out unescorted. Whether the men of a nation can become decadent while the women do not remains an open question. He observes that increasing female prominence coincides with some men desiring to imitate women -- in Baghdad during the Arab decline, young men wore female clothes and strings of beads. Glubb calls this the "reversal of the sexes" and records it as a symptom without claiming to explain it.

The first ten years of a child's life stamp its future character, and Glubb claims the mother's influence during those years is decisive. Most great men in history, he asserts, owed their characters to their mothers. Working mothers of small children therefore represent a danger to the quality of the next generation. He rejects the idea that private morality is a private matter: the morals of every citizen are of vital importance to the future of the country. Women render noble and selfless service through domestic duties and child-rearing without public recognition. The historical veneration of women through chivalry and deference reflects a deeper social truth: the nation is literally founded upon its women. These views are obviously of their time, but Glubb states them with the conviction of a man who spent his career watching civilisations from the inside.

On population and the future, Glubb counsels humility. Throughout history, the wisest men have consistently failed to foresee events -- Providence always has an unexpected card. Nobody foresaw the sudden wealth of Iran and desert Arabia from oil. Modern transport will not produce a single blended human race, he predicts; more likely it will create pockets of various races isolated amid different ethnic majorities. He strongly opposes forced integration by governments while supporting equal access to public services. Middle Eastern governments historically accommodated diversity by allowing separate quarters for different communities, and there was no trouble when all were fairly treated by an impartial government. As mixed populations become inevitable, mutual toleration is wiser than compulsory homogeneity.

"The fault, dear Brutus, is not in our stars, but in ourselves, that we are underlings." -- Shakespeare, quoted by Glubb

The essay closes with a message of hope. Decline is not caused by external forces beyond our control but by our own greed, selfishness, immorality, and loss of courage. These are failings each person can help to rectify. Glubb prescribes individual duties: carry out duties to family scrupulously, work as hard as possible, treat subordinates with comradeship and personal attention, speak and write in favour of self-sacrifice and service. Only a revival of spiritual devotion -- not fashionable political "-isms" -- can inspire selfless service. If leaders fail to inspire, individuals must set the example themselves. The spirit of the prophet who cried "Here am I! Send me!" is what every citizen should cultivate.

Trading & Investing Lessons

The 250-Year Cycle as Macro Framework

Glubb's lifecycle stages map directly onto long-duration macro investing. The transition from the Age of Commerce to the Age of Affluence corresponds to the point where an economy shifts from productive investment to asset inflation. Identifying where a nation sits on this curve -- particularly the shift from service to selfishness, from production to consumption -- is the single most important macro call an investor can make. Nations in the Age of Commerce (early-stage growth economies) offer better risk-adjusted returns than nations deep in the Age of Affluence.

Defensiveness as a Sell Signal

When a nation shifts from offence to defence -- building walls literal or metaphorical, subsidising enemies rather than competing with them -- the best days are behind it. In markets, the equivalent is companies that stop innovating and start buying back stock, lobbying for tariffs, or litigating against competitors. Defensiveness is a lagging indicator of decline, not a sign of strength.

The Frivolity Indicator

When the heroes of a society become athletes, singers, and actors rather than builders, scientists, and explorers, capital is flowing into entertainment rather than productive enterprise. The dominance of celebrity culture in an economy's attention allocation is a contrarian indicator: the more a nation celebrates its entertainers, the less future productive growth it will generate.

Decadence of Systems, Not Individuals

Glubb's insight that decline is systemic, not biological, has direct investment implications. Talented individuals emigrating from declining systems to growth-stage systems create disproportionate value. Immigration flows from decadent to pioneer-stage economies are a leading indicator of where future growth will concentrate. The brain drain is an investable signal.

The Intellectual Trap

The Age of Intellect produces endless debate while the ship drifts onto the rocks. In markets, this manifests as over-analysis, committee-driven decision-making, and the proliferation of financial models that substitute complexity for conviction. The best traders share the pioneer's bias toward action: try something, adapt quickly, move forward. When an investing culture becomes dominated by academic theory and backtesting at the expense of real- time judgment, it is in its own Age of Intellect.

Subsidies Replace Courage

Empires that pay off enemies rather than confront them are spending future strength for present comfort. In portfolio management, the equivalent is averaging down into losing positions or rolling losing options forward. The short-term pain of a stop-loss is the market equivalent of courage; the comfortable evasion of subsidies is the equivalent of denial. Money being more available than courage is a description of bad risk management.

Key Takeaways

The 250-year clock is remarkably consistent

Across eleven empires spanning 3,000 years, from Assyria to Britain, the average lifespan of a great power is approximately 250 years -- roughly ten human generations. This figure has not varied regardless of technology, geography, or political system. Assyrian foot soldiers and British artillery produced empires of identical duration.

Six stages define every empire's lifecycle

The sequence is always the same: Pioneers, Conquests, Commerce, Affluence, Intellect, Decadence. Each stage grows organically from the one before it, and no empire has been known to skip a stage or reverse the sequence. The trajectory can be summarised as: from courage to commerce to cleverness to collapse.

Decline is moral, not physical or intellectual

Citizens of declining empires are not weaker or stupider than their ancestors. They are often physically larger and intellectually more productive. The disease is moral: cynicism, selfishness, loss of duty, and the conviction that nothing is worth saving. When individuals emigrate to new systems, they thrive -- proving the disease is environmental, not genetic.

Money is the universal solvent of empires

Too long a period of wealth and power corrodes every virtue that built the empire in the first place. Honour yields to profit, duty yields to comfort, adventure yields to security. The Age of Affluence silences the voice of duty so gradually that no generation notices the change happening in its own time.

Intellectual activity increases during decline

Every declining empire sees an explosion of universities, debate, and scientific achievement -- and none of it saves the empire. Arab scientists measured the earth's circumference seven centuries before Europe, yet the Arab Empire collapsed within fifty years of these discoveries. Discussion destroys the power of action.

Internal division intensifies as external threats grow

The most counterintuitive symptom of decline is that factions fight each other more bitterly as the nation weakens, not less. Byzantium spent its last fifty years in civil wars while the Ottomans waited to deliver the final blow. Unity comes from shared purpose, and decadent nations have none.

Political systems do not determine national lifespan

Monarchies, republics, military dictatorships, and slave-soldier oligarchies all follow the same lifecycle in the same time. The Mamelukes -- a system of imported slave-boys who murdered their way to power -- lasted 267 years and passed through identical stages as the British constitutional monarchy.

Contrarian Ideas

Technology does not extend empire duration

Conventional wisdom holds that technological superiority sustains great powers. Glubb's data shows the opposite: technology changes the shape of empires (land vs sea) but has zero measurable effect on how long they last. Spears and aircraft carriers produce identical lifespans.

The welfare state is a symptom of decline, not progress

Free hospitals, university grants, and social programmes are celebrated as civilisational achievements. Glubb reframes them as regular milestones in the life of an ageing empire, born of the assumption that wealth is permanent -- an assumption that collapses when the economy does.

Intellectualism accelerates collapse rather than preventing it

The instinct that education and reasoned debate can solve national decline is precisely wrong. Every empire's intellectual golden age coincides with its political collapse. The belief that the human brain alone can solve the world's problems is the most dangerous by-product of the Age of Intellect.

Democracy is irrelevant to national survival

Western nations pour scorn on countries with different political systems, assuming democracy confers survival advantages. Past empires show every possible variation of government, and all follow the same trajectory. The Mameluke slave- state lasted exactly as long as British parliamentary democracy.

Pacifism masks weakness, not moral progress

Nations do not disarm from conscience but from weakening duty and increasing selfishness. The declaration that empires are wicked and civilised peoples are "too proud to fight" enables moral superiority that masks the reality of decline. Nations proclaiming themselves unwilling to fight are conquered by peoples still in the militarist stage.

Proposals & Frameworks

The Six-Stage Imperial Lifecycle

Glubb's central framework: every great empire passes through the Ages of Pioneers, Conquests, Commerce, Affluence, Intellect, and Decadence in that order. No empire skips a stage. No empire reverses the sequence. Each stage grows organically from the conditions created by its predecessor. The entire cycle takes approximately 250 years.

The Seven Markers of Decadence

Decadence is identifiable by seven consistent symptoms: (1) defensiveness, (2) pessimism, (3) materialism, (4) frivolity, (5) influx of foreigners, (6) the welfare state, and (7) weakening of religion. These appear in every declining empire regardless of its political system or geographic location.

The Four Causes of Decline

All seven symptoms trace back to four root causes: (1) too long a period of wealth and power, (2) selfishness, (3) love of money, and (4) loss of a sense of duty. The trajectory from service to selfishness is the master narrative of every imperial collapse.

Proposed University Department

Glubb calls for the creation of a university department dedicated solely to studying the rhythm of rise and fall of powerful nations, covering all the world's great powers over 3,000 years. His essay examined only eleven empires focused on the Middle East and modern West; such a department would likely find at least twenty-four great powers available for comparative analysis, including India, China, and South America.

Individual Prescription for Reversal

Rather than waiting for political solutions, each citizen should: carry out family duties scrupulously, work as hard as possible, treat subordinates with comradeship and personal attention, and speak and write in favour of self-sacrifice, service, and unselfishness. The motto Labor Omnia Vincit -- work conquers everything -- is the constructive counter to the decadent spirit of "Why should I?"

What Didn't Fit

The Mamelukes: History's Most Bizarre Empire

The Mameluke Empire deserves more attention than the chapter framework allowed. These were boys purchased as slaves from the Russian Steppes, imported to Egypt, and trained as professional soldiers under the Ayoubid sultans descended from Saladin. On 1 May 1250 they mutinied and murdered Sultan Turan Shah. They then defeated the Mongols -- the first power in history to do so -- drove them from Syria, and saved the entire Mediterranean world from the fate that befell Persia. They captured Acre in 1291, ending the Crusades. Every sultan rose from the ranks of private soldiers; the ruling class was perpetually replenished by fresh imports of slave-boys. This system, which no political theorist would design or endorse, produced 267 years of greatness and followed the identical lifecycle of every other empire.

Spain's 780 Years Under Arab Rule

Glubb uses a striking temporal device to convey how long the Arabs ruled Spain. 780 years back from 1976 takes you to 1196, the reign of King Richard Coeur de Lion. When the Arabs arrived in 712, no Spanish nation existed -- only petty kings clinging to mountain fortresses in Aragon and Castile. The reconquest took nearly eight centuries. When Ferdinand and Isabella finally agreed terms after the fall of Granada in 1492, it took only fifty years for Spain to become the world's greatest empire under Cortez. The outburst was instantaneous once the preconditions aligned -- confirming the pattern of sudden, explosive emergence.

Arab Scientists and the Earth's Circumference

Under Caliph Mamun in the ninth century, Arab scientists measured the circumference of the earth with remarkable accuracy -- achieving this seven centuries before Western Europe even accepted that the earth was not flat. Yet less than fifty years after these extraordinary scientific achievements, the Arab Empire collapsed. The intellectual flowering continued under foreign conquerors, but the scientists became servants of illiterate masters. Glubb uses this as his most devastating evidence that intellectual achievement cannot prevent imperial decline: the smartest people in the world could not save their own civilisation.

The European Community as a New Augustus

Most press debate about European Community membership focused on the price of butter. Glubb saw something far larger. The Roman Republic in its final days was corrupt, materialistic, and bitterly divided. Augustus transformed it into a single trading area with common laws and a common language, rejuvenating wealth, culture, and power -- he found Rome built of brick and left it made of marble. Glubb frames the EC in this context: not a grocery- pricing arrangement but a potential vehicle for civilisational renewal through political union. Whether this vision has been vindicated or disappointed remains one of the great open questions of European history.

Cyprus, Ireland, and the Failure of Propinquity

Against predictions that modern transport will blend humanity into a single culture, Glubb offers two devastating counterexamples. Greeks and Turks lived together on Cyprus for centuries before descending into violent hostility. Two races have shared the island of Ireland without ever amalgamating. Physical proximity does not produce cooperation or fraternisation. If anything, close contact between different peoples sometimes intensifies rather than resolves conflict -- making the dream of a seamlessly blended global culture more fantasy than forecast.

About the Author

Sir John Bagot Glubb (1897-1986), known as "Glubb Pasha," was a British soldier who spent thirty-six years in the Middle East commanding Arab forces. He served in World War I in France and Belgium, was wounded three times, and received the Military Cross. In 1920 he transferred to Iraq, and in 1926 he left the regular British Army to serve the Iraqi government directly. In 1930 he signed a contract with the Transjordan government (modern Jordan), and from 1939 to 1956 he commanded the famous Jordan Arab Legion, transforming it into the best-trained Arab military force of its era.

After his retirement, Glubb published seventeen books, mostly on Middle Eastern history and the Arab world. His unique qualification for writing about the rise and fall of empires was that he had lived inside both Western and Eastern civilisations, spending decades among peoples whose own imperial history -- from the Arab conquests through the Ottoman period -- was far richer and longer than Britain's. He lectured widely in Britain, the United States, and Europe.

Glubb wrote The Fate of Empires at the age of 79, drawing on a lifetime of observation. His perspective is that of a man who commanded desert warriors, watched the British Empire recede, and spent decades reading Arabic historical sources that most Western historians ignored. The essay's power derives not from academic theory but from this first-hand experience of civilisational transition.

Why This Book Matters

In just 79 pages, Glubb accomplishes something that most historians never attempt: a unified theory of imperial decline drawn from three thousand years of evidence. The essay has circulated quietly since 1977, passed from reader to reader, never achieving mainstream academic recognition but never going out of print either. Its appeal lies in its simplicity and its uncomfortable precision. The markers of decadence Glubb identifies -- celebrity worship, the welfare state, loss of duty, internal division, frivolity, the influx of foreigners -- read less like historical analysis and more like a description of the front page of any Western newspaper today.

The essay matters for investors and students of macro trends because it provides a framework that transcends the usual two-to-four-year political cycle. Most economic analysis operates on quarterly or annual horizons. Glubb operates on a 250-year horizon, which reframes questions about national competitiveness, innovation, and institutional decay. Whether one agrees with his conclusions or not, the questions he raises -- Can intellectualism save a declining civilisation? Does the welfare state weaken the initiative that built the wealth funding it? Is moral renewal possible without religious revival? -- are questions that refuse to become irrelevant.

Some of Glubb's views, particularly on women and race, are unmistakably products of their era. But the core framework -- the six stages, the 250-year clock, the systemic nature of decline -- remains as provocative and testable as it was in 1977. The essay asks its readers to do something genuinely difficult: look at their own civilisation from the outside, as one more data point in a pattern that has repeated for three millennia. Few books of any length deliver that shift in perspective. This one does it in 79 pages.

The Fate of Empires and Search for Survival by Sir John Bagot Glubb. Published by William Blackwood & Sons, Edinburgh, 1977. 79 pages.
