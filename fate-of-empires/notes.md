## About John Bagot Glubb

- **Sir John Bagot Glubb** (1897-1986), nickname "Glubb Pasha," British soldier and Middle East expert
- Served in WWI in France and Belgium, wounded three times, awarded Military Cross
- 1920: transferred to Iraq as regular officer; 1926: left regular army for Iraq Government
- 1930: signed contract with Transjordan Government (modern Jordan)
- 1939-1956: commanded the famous **Jordan Arab Legion**, transformed it into best-trained Arab force
- Since retirement: published seventeen books, chiefly on Middle East; lectured widely in Britain, US, Europe

## Introduction: The Case for Historical Study

- **Central thesis**: Priceless lessons could be learned if 4,000 years of human history were studied thoroughly and impartially
- Current historical teaching is limited to narrow periods; endless focus on Tudors, Stewarts, Battle of Crecy, Guy Fawkes
- **Problem**: History is taught by country/period, not as continuous human development
- School examination systems force narrow curricula; prevents teaching genuinely useful content
- These two articles originally appeared in *Blackwood's Magazine*
- **Argument**: History should be the history of the human race, not one small country or period

## Chapter I: Learning from History

### Why History Is Not Learned

> "The only thing we learn from history is that men never learn from history"

- This generalization partly true, but society which claims to probe every problem still ignores historical bases
- **Three main failures of historical study**:
  1. Limited to short periods or single country; slant governed by national vanity rather than objectivity
  2. National histories write ancestors as patriotic heroes, enemies as imperialists/rebels
  3. World history studies disconnected periods (Greece, Rome, rarely the intervals between them)
- Recently Greece and Rome become discredited; history becomes parochial history of our own countries

### The Solution: Study Continuous Human Flow

- **Essential principle**: history must be meaningful study of whole flow of human development
- Is a continuous process, gradually developing, changing, turning back, moving forward in single mighty stream
- Lessons must be learned by studying whole flow, not selecting short periods here and there
- Every age and culture derived from predecessors, adds contribution, passes to successors
- If boycott various periods of history, origins of new cultures cannot be explained
- Physical science expanded through building on predecessors, meticulous experiments, carefully recorded results
- **Key criticism**: Historical work still dominated by emotion and prejudice; not yet employed scientific methods like physical science

## Chapter II: The Lives of Empires

### Definition and Data

- "Empire" visualized as home-country plus colonies (British Empire model), but essay uses term for great power/super-power
- Most empires in history were large landblocks without significant overseas possessions

### Table of Imperial Durations

| Persia | 538-330 B.C. | 208 years |
| Greece | 331-100 B.C. | 231 years |
| Roman Republic | 260-27 B.C. | 233 years |
| Roman Empire | 27 B.C.-A.D. 180 | 207 years |
| Arab Empire | A.D. 634-880 | 246 years |

| Romanov Russia | 1682-1916 | 234 years |
| Britain | 1700-1950 | 250 years |

### Critical Observations

- Dates largely arbitrary; empires don't start/end on certain dates—normally gradual expansion then decline
- **Remarkable fact**: despite different conditions, durations show striking similarity (~200-250 years typical)
- **Contrarian observation**: Technology does NOT affect empire duration. Assyrians (foot soldiers, spears, bows) lasted ~247 years; British (artillery, railways, ocean ships) lasted ~250 years
- **Geographic factor**: Technology shaped empire shapes, not duration. Assyrians conquered land-accessible neighbors; British conquered water-accessible continents
- **Assumption justified**: ~250-year duration is fair estimate for average empire's life-expectancy
- **Conclusion**: There is sufficient resemblance between life-periods of different empires to justify further study

## Chapter III: The Human Yardstick

### The Generational Cycle

- Only unit of measurement unchanged since Assyrians is human "generation" (~25 years)
- 250-year empire ≈ 10 generations of people
- Closer examination of rise/fall characteristics may emphasize significance of sequence of generations

## Chapter IV: Stage One, The Outburst

### Examples of Sudden Empire Emergence

- **Macedonia**: insignificant state north of Greece until Philip (359-336 B.C.). By 323 B.C., Persian Empire ceased; Macedonian Empire extended from Danube to India
- After **Alexander the Great's** death, Macedonian generals fought each other, but pre-eminence survived 231 years
- **A.D. 600**: World divided between two super-powers (Eastern Roman Empire, Persian Empire). Arabs were despised, backward tribes with no government, no constitution, no army
- **Prophet Muhammad** preached A.D. 613-632. In 633, Arabs burst from desert, simultaneously attacked two super-powers. Within twenty years, Persian Empire ceased; Arabs established empire from Atlantic to Northern India borders
- **A.D. 1211**: Mongols were savage tribes in Mongolian steppes. Genghis Khan invaded China; by 1253, Mongols established empire from Asia Minor to China Sea—largest empire world has known
- **A.D. 712-1492**: Arabs ruled Spain for 780 years (780 years back in British history = 1196, King Richard Coeur de Lion). No Spanish nation existed; petty kings of Aragon and Castile held mountains
- **1492**: Agreement between Ferdinand and Isabella signed after Granada fell. Within fifty years, Cortez conquered Mexico; Spain became world's greatest empire

## Chapter V: Characteristics of the Outburst

### Energy and Courage

- Sudden outbursts characterized by extraordinary display of energy and courage
- New conquerors normally poor, hardy, enterprising, aggressive
- Decaying empires wealthy but defensive-minded
- Roman legions in greatness dug ditch around camps at night for surprise; ditches became high walls over time
- Early: empty spaces between wide defensive works allowed counter-attacks
- Later: access only by narrow gates; counter-attacks impossible; legions became passive defenders

### Initiative and Enterprise

- Not distinguished by victory alone, but unresting enterprise in every field
- Men hack through jungles, climb mountains, brave Atlantic and Pacific in cockleshells
- **Arabs crossed Strait of Gibraltar** A.D. 711 with 12,000 men, defeated Gothic army >2x their strength, marched 250 miles through unknown territory, seized Toledo
- Same stage in British history: **Captain Cook** discovered Australia
- **Fearless initiative** characterizes conquering pioneers; readiness to improvise and experiment; untramelled by tradition
- Will turn anything available to their purpose; if one method fails, try something else
- Uninhibited by textbooks or book learning; action is solution to every problem
- **Characteristic profile**: Poor, hardy, often half-starved, ill-clad, abound in courage, energy, initiative; overcome every obstacle, always seem in control

## Chapter VI: The Causes of Race Outbursts

### The Temptation Theory

- Modern instinct: seek reason for everything; doubt veracity if reason cannot be found
- Many examples of sudden eruption of obscure race into conquerors; truth of phenomenon cannot be doubted
- Easiest explanation: poor, obscure race tempted by wealth of ancient civilization

### Two Types of Invasions

- **Type 1**: Mere loot, plunder, rape (e.g., **Attila and Huns** ravaged Europe A.D. 450-453). When Attila died, empire fell apart; tribes returned to Eastern Europe
- **Type 2**: **Admiration model**—Many barbarians invading Roman Empire did so from admiration for Roman civilization; aspired to become Romans
- Distinction important: some motivated by greed alone; others by cultural aspiration

## Chapter VII: A Providential Turnover?

### The Theory of Rotating Greatness

- Whatever causes overthrow of civilizations by barbarians, certain resulting benefits can be sensed
- Every race has distinctive characteristics in philosophy, administration, romance, poetry, religion, legal systems
- During pre-eminence of each culture, distinctive characteristics carried far and wide across world
- If same nation retained dominance indefinitely, peculiar qualities would permanently characterize whole human race
- Under system of ~250-year empires, **sovereign race has time to spread its particular virtues far and wide**
- Then another people with entirely different peculiarities takes place; their accomplishments likewise disseminated
- **By this system, each immeasurable races of world enjoys period of greatness**, during which peculiar qualities placed at service of mankind
- To believers in God as Ruler and Director of human affairs, such system appears as manifestation of divine wisdom—tending toward slow, ultimate perfection of humanity

## XIII: The Age of Commerce

- The **Age of Pioneers** (outburst) → **Age of Conquests** → **Age of Commerce**:
  conquest unifies vast territories under one government, automatically
  generating commercial prosperity
- Overlap: military traditions still hold, armies still guard frontiers,
  but the desire to make money gradually overtakes glory and honour
- To the merchant, honour and glory are "empty words which add nothing
  to the bank balance" — a decisive cultural shift

## XIV: Art and Luxury

- Wealth pours into the country almost effortlessly; the problem becomes
  how to spend it
- **Art, architecture, luxury** find rich patrons; splendid municipal
  buildings, palaces, investment in infrastructure (highways, railways,
  hotels) mark the era
- First half of the Age of Commerce is "peculiarly splendid": ancient
  virtues of courage, patriotism, duty still survive alongside new wealth
- Boys' schools intentionally rough — frugal eating, hard living, breaking
  ice for baths — producing "strong, hardy and fearless" men; **duty**
  is the watchword
- Great enterprise in exploration for new wealth: daring initiative in far
  corners of the earth, carrying forward some of the adventurous spirit
  of the conquest era

## XV: The Age of Affluence

- **Money is the agent of decline** — but the process is gradual and
  almost imperceptible
- First injury is moral: money replaces honour and adventure as the
  objective of the best young men; men now seek wealth for themselves,
  not for country or community
- **The Age of Affluence silences the voice of duty**; ambition shifts
  from fame and service to cash
- Education transforms: schools no longer aim to produce brave patriots
  but qualifications commanding the highest salaries
- **Ghazali** (Arab moralist, 1058–1111) complained of this exact
  phenomenon in the declining Arab world: students attend college "not
  to acquire learning and virtue, but to obtain those qualifications
  which will enable them to grow rich" — Glubb notes the same is
  "everywhere evident among us in the West today"

## XVI: High Noon

- **High Noon** = the transitional peak: Age of Conquests blending into
  Age of Affluence; immense accumulated wealth dazzles observers
- Historical examples: age of **Augustus** in Rome, **Harun al-Rashid**
  in Baghdad, **Sulaiman the Magnificent** in the Ottoman Empire,
  **Queen Victoria** in Britain, possibly **Woodrow Wilson** in the USA
- Enough ancient virtues survive to defend frontiers; but beneath the
  surface, **greed is replacing duty and public service**
- The change can be summarised as: **from service to selfishness**

## XVII: Defensiveness

- Transition from Age of Conquests to Age of Affluence marked by
  **spread of defensiveness**: the nation is no longer interested in
  glory but anxious to retain wealth and luxury
- Historical pattern: **Great Wall of China**, **Hadrian's Wall**,
  **Maginot Line** (1939) — all expressions of the same defensive
  psychology
- Money being more available than courage, **subsidies replace weapons**
  to buy off enemies
- The intellectual device: military aggression is rebranded as
  "primitive and immoral"; civilised peoples are "too proud to fight";
  empires are declared wicked — this enables a posture of **moral
  superiority** to mask the reality of weakness
- Critical insight: nations that proclaim themselves unwilling to fight
  are liable to be conquered by peoples still in the militarist stage
- History suggests nations do not disarm from conscience but from
  **weakening duty and increasing selfishness**

## XVIII: The Age of Intellect

- Four ages now identified: **Pioneers → Conquests → Commerce →
  Affluence** → now a fifth emerges: the **Age of Intellect**
- Surplus wealth funds knowledge; merchant princes endow colleges and
  universities — a pattern repeated "in empire after empire, divided by
  many centuries"
- Arab example: under **Malik Shah** (Seljuk sultan, 11th c.), the Arab
  Empire in political decline saw a passion for university-building —
  a university sprang up in every town where a handful had sufficed the
  glory years
- Modern parallel: Harvard, Yale, Oxford, Cambridge once met Britain's
  and America's needs; now almost every city has a university
- Ambition's trajectory: military glory → accumulation of wealth →
  **acquisition of academic honours**
- Striking observation: each successive passion (military, commercial,
  intellectual) is legitimate and beneficial in itself — the puzzle is
  why they cannot all be pursued simultaneously in moderation; they
  never are

## XIX: The Effects of Intellectualism

- Paradox: **every period of decline is characterised by expansion of
  intellectual activity**
- Biblical parallel: Acts of the Apostles on declining Greek
  intellectualism — "All the Athenians and strangers which were there
  spent their time in nothing else, but either to tell or to hear some
  new thing"
- **Arab science under Mamun** (9th century): Arabs measured the
  circumference of the earth with remarkable accuracy — seven centuries
  before Western Europe knew it wasn't flat. Yet less than fifty years
  after these discoveries, the Arab Empire collapsed. Science did not
  save the empire
- Arab/Persian intellectual flowering occurred *after* political
  collapse; the intellectuals became servants of illiterate conquerors —
  e.g. **Rashid al-Din** (historian), Prime Minister under the Mongols,
  obliged to wait on his knees before the Khan, passed food scraps over
  his shoulder at banquets
- Intellectualism breeds endless debate: discussion, argument,
  talk in assemblies, press, television — but "this constant dedication
  to discussion seems to destroy the power of action"

> "Amid a Babel of talk, the ship drifts on to the rocks."

## XX: The Inadequacy of Intellect

- Most dangerous by-product of the Age of Intellect: the belief that
  **the human brain alone can solve the world's problems**
- Any organisation — even a local bowls club — requires self-sacrifice
  and service to survive; national survival depends on **loyalty and
  self-sacrifice of citizens**, not mere cleverness
- Intellect cultivated at the cost of unselfishness leads to collapse
- The head and the heart are natural rivals: the brilliant but cynical
  intellectual stands at the opposite end from the emotional self-sacrifice
  of the hero or martyr
- "There are times when the perhaps unsophisticated self-dedication of
  the hero is more essential than the sarcasms of the clever"

## XXI: Civil Dissensions

- Counterintuitive symptom of national decline: **intensification of
  internal political hatreds** — one would expect external threat to
  unite factions; the reverse occurs
- **Byzantine example**: threatened and dominated by Ottoman Turks in the
  14th century, the Byzantines spent their last fifty years fighting one
  another in repeated civil wars; the Ottomans simply administered the
  *coup de grâce*
- British Parliament example: formerly rival parties observed unwritten
  rules, called each other "honourable gentlemen"; now booing, shouting,
  angry exchanges; rivalries spill into streets as strikes, boycotts,
  demonstrations
- Key pattern: **as the nation weakens, internal rivalries become more
  acute, not less** — divisions are not reconciled to save the nation

## XXII: The Influx of Foreigners

- A recurring phenomenon of great empires: **influx of foreigners to the
  capital city**
- Roman historians complained of Asians and Africans in Rome; Baghdad at
  its 9th-century peak was cosmopolitan — Persians, Turks, Arabs,
  Armenians, Egyptians, Africans, Greeks
- Modern parallels: London, New York
- **During the Outburst and Age of Conquests, the nation is ethnically
  homogeneous** — solidarity and comradeship result; in the Ages of
  Commerce and Affluence, immigrants flood in drawn by wealth
- Four ways foreign influx weakens empire:
  1. Different basic temperaments introduce "cracks and schisms" into
     national policy even among loyal citizens
  2. In acute emergency, immigrants often less willing to sacrifice lives
     than descendants of the founding stock
  3. Immigrants form protective communities prioritising their own
     interests first
  4. Former conquered peoples, proud during High Noon, revive ancient
     grievances when decline sets in — demanding secession
- Original conquering stock often found in relative purity in rural
  areas and far frontiers; cities are the locus of cosmopolitanism
- Glubb stresses: immigrants are not inferior — merely different —
  and the differences introduce divisions

## XXIII: Frivolity

- Decline brings **universal pessimism** which itself hastens decline;
  success bred self-confidence, which sustained the empire through crises
- Republican Rome survived the Gauls sacking the city (390 BC) and
  Cannae (216 BC) through sheer resolution; later imperial Rome was
  deeply pessimistic and could not
- **Frivolity is the companion of pessimism** — "let us eat, drink and
  be merry, for tomorrow we die"
- Historical pattern of mass spectacle:
  - Roman mob: bread and games, gladiatorial shows, chariot races
  - Byzantine Empire: the Greens vs Blues hippodrome rivalries became
    a matter of major national crisis
  - Modern Britain: football; USA: baseball
- **The heroes of declining nations are always the same: the athlete,
  the singer, the actor** — the word "celebrity" now designates a
  comedian or footballer, not a statesman or literary genius

## XXIV: The Arab Decline — A Case Study

- Baghdad's High Noon: first half of 9th century, greatest and richest
  city in the world
- **861**: Caliph **Mutawakkil** murdered by Turkish mercenaries; military
  dictatorship for thirty years; empire fragments; Baghdad's authority
  shrinks to Iraq alone
- Contemporary 10th-century Baghdad historians (still extant) lamented:
  - Indifference to religion
  - Increasing materialism
  - Laxity of sexual morals
  - Corruption of officials
  - Politicians amassing fortunes while in office
- **Pop singers** of Baghdad accompanied erotic songs on the lute (resembling
  a modern guitar); obscene sexual language spread; khalifs issued bans
  on pop singers — they always returned within a few years
- Women demanded admission to professions monopolised by men; historian
  **Ibn Bessam** complained: "What have the professions of clerk, tax-
  collector or preacher to do with women?" Many women practised law,
  became university professors; agitation for female judges
- At the moment of economic crisis following the 861 military takeover,
  the people of Baghdad introduced a **five-day working week**
- Glubb's reaction on first reading: "I could scarcely believe my eyes...
  The descriptions might have been taken out of *The Times* today."

## XXV: Political Ideology

- Western nations attach immense importance to the ideology of political
  institutions and pour scorn on countries whose systems differ from
  their own democracy
- But **the life-expectancy of a great nation does not appear to be in
  any way affected by the nature of its institutions**
- Past empires show nearly every possible variation of political system;
  all pass through the same sequence from Pioneers through Conquest,
  Commerce, Affluence to decline and collapse

## XXVI: The Mameluke Empire

- The **Mamelukes** provide the most precisely datable empire in history:
  exactly **267 years** (began and ended on fixed days)
- Origin: slaves imported as boys from the Russian Steppes, trained as
  professional soldiers under the **Ayoubid sultans** (descendants of
  Saladin); on 1 May 1250 they mutinied, murdered **Turan Shah**, and
  seized power
- Achievements: defeated the previously invincible Mongols, drove them
  from Syria (saving the Mediterranean world from Persia's fate);
  captured Acre in 1291, ending the Crusades
- Peak: 1309–1341, finest army in the world; then century of fabulous
  wealth → luxury → decline → bitter internal rivalries → collapse
  1517 by Ottoman military defeat
- Ruling class entirely recruited from young boys born in southern Russia,
  all beginning as private soldiers; even sultans rose from the ranks
- Confirms the pattern: even the most "illogical and fantastic" political
  system passes through the same stages in approximately the same time

## XXVII: The Master Race Illusion

- Citizens of dominant empires invariably believe their pre-eminence will
  last forever: Rome as "mistress of the world for all time"; Abbasid
  Khalifs "appointed by God to rule until the day of judgment"; Victorian
  British; Hitler's thousand-year Reich
- The belief in permanent superiority encourages attribution of success
  to **hereditary virtue** — a "race of supermen" illusion — leading to:
  - Employment of cheap foreign labour for menial work
  - Foreign mercenaries to fight their battles
  - Relaxation of their own energies into leisure and amusement
- Modern Western version: the idea that "progress will be automatic without
  effort" and "every year will show a rise in the standard of living"
- **Self-assurance of superiority goes hand in hand with the luxury
  that undermines character**

## XXVIII: The Welfare State

- The age of decline often shows philanthropy and sympathy for other
  races — the imperial people enjoy playing "Lady Bountiful"
- Roman Empire: equal citizenship thrown open to all peoples; provincials
  became senators and emperors
- **Arab Empire under Harun al-Rashid** (786–809): government grants to
  university students; **first free public hospital opened in Baghdad**;
  under son Mamun, free hospitals spread across the Arab world from
  Spain to Pakistan
- The welfare state appears as the high-water mark of human attainment,
  but may be merely "one more regular milestone in the life-story of an
  aging and decrepit empire"
- The assumption of permanent wealth leads to lavish benevolence until
  the economy collapses and universities close and hospitals fall to ruin

## XXIX: Religion

- Glubb defines religion broadly: "the human feeling that there is
  something, some invisible Power, apart from material objects, which
  controls human life and the natural world"
- Ancient idol worship was not primitive foolishness — the idol was a
  **symbol** of an unknown spiritual reality demanding moral obedience
- The **Age of Conquests** is typically accompanied by religious fervour
  — even Genghis Khan claimed God had delegated him to exterminate
  decadent civilisations, framing conquest as holy sacrifice
- Money (Age of Commerce/Affluence) gradually dissolves the spirit of
  service and duty that religious conviction sustained
- Decline → pessimism → frivolity and sensual indulgence as despair's
  by-products → nostalgic yearning for the "days of religion"
- Seeds of religious revival are quietly sown at the height of vice and
  frivolity; after generations of suffering, selfishness is purged and
  religion regains its sway — a new era begins

## XXX: New Combinations

- When all races have exhausted their turn at dominance, where will new
  energetic conquerors come from?
- The answer: history shows invaders and conquered populations
  **intermarry and produce new races** — the barbarian invasions of Rome,
  Arab conquests of Spain and Persia, Turkish conquests, Norman Conquest
  of England all resulted in new ethnic combinations
- Modern equivalent: migration rather than armed invasion — political
  refugees, economic migrants — constantly reshapes national populations
- The United States is the obvious example of a new nation formed by
  transformation of ethnic content through "modern nomadism"

## XXXI: Decadence of a System

- **Decadence is the disintegration of a system, not of its individual
  members**
- Citizens corrupted by too much money and power for too long become
  selfish and idle within their national framework
- But when individuals emigrate into entirely new surroundings, they do
  not remain decadent — they become normal citizens of adopted countries;
  second and third generations may attain pre-eminence
- Proves: **the decline of a nation does not undermine the basic character
  of its members** — decadence is environmental and systemic, not
  biological

## XXXII: Decadence is Not Physical

- Citizens of nations in decline are not physically inferior —
  they are often **physically larger and stronger than their barbarian
  invaders**
- Evidence: British WWI soldiers raised in luxury adapted readily to
  frontline trench life; Victorian explorers showed equal endurance to
  desert nomads
- **Decadence is a moral and spiritual disease**, resulting from too long
  a period of wealth and power, producing cynicism, decline of religion,
  pessimism, and frivolity
- The decadent will not make efforts to save themselves because they are
  "not convinced that anything in life is worth saving"

## XXXIII: Human Diversity

- Generalisations are always dangerous; every nation and culture is
  unique; critics can always find exceptions
- But the same holds for individuals — no two people identical, yet we
  generalise meaningfully about childhood, youth, middle age, old age
- Nations follow a similar pattern despite superficial differences
- Glubb's striking example: a TV director vetoed a proposed series on
  Arab history asking "What *earthly* interest could the history of
  medieval Arabs have for the general public today?" — yet Arab imperial
  history (conquest → commercialism → affluence → intellectualism →
  science → decadence) is "an exact precursor of British imperial history
  and lasted almost exactly the same time"
- If British historians a century ago had seriously studied the Arab
  Empire, they could have "foreseen almost everything that has happened
  in Britain down to 1976"

## XXXIV: A Variety of Falls

- **Rise is internally driven; the nature of the fall depends on external
  circumstances** — hence rises are uniform, falls are diverse
- The internal decay runs its ten-generation course; what happens next
  depends on who is present to fill the vacuum
- Examples of diverse outcomes:
  - Rome → split into Western (overrun by barbarians) and Eastern
    (Byzantine Empire, surviving centuries more)
  - Arab Empire → fragmented into many pieces; Moslem Spain ran 250
    years as its own empire; Syria and Iraq subject to Turks for 1,000 years
  - Mameluke Empire → conquered in one campaign by Ottomans
  - Spanish Empire (1500–1750) → lost colonies but Spain survived as
    independent nation
  - Romanov Russia (1682–1916) → succeeded by Soviet Union
- The *lives* of great powers are surprisingly uniform;
  the *results* of their falls are completely diverse

## XXXV: Inadequacy of Our Historical Studies

- Western nations derive limited value from historical study because they
  study only their own nation's history — the time frame is too short to
  perceive the longer rhythms of rise and fall
- National histories are intentionally parochial: "our ancestors' actions
  are glorious, while those of other peoples are mean, tyrannical or
  cowardly"
- True historical understanding requires the history of the **human race**
  across civilisations — without this breadth, the cycle of rise and fall
  passes unperceived, generation after generation

## Section XXXVI: Small Nations

- "**Empires**" in this essay = nations that achieved great-power or super-power status,
  dominating the international scene for two or three centuries
- Question: do smaller, self-contained nations follow the same lifecycle?
- Glubb's answer: **decadence is the outcome of too long a period of wealth and power**
  — small nations that never shared in that wealth won't share in the decadence
- No firm generalization possible across all small states

## Section XXXVII: The Emerging Pattern

- Despite infinite variety in human affairs, a general pattern emerges across ~3,000 years:
  successive empires followed similar stages, lived lives of **similar length**
- Life-expectation of a great nation: begins with violent, usually unforeseen **outburst of energy**;
  ends in lowering of moral standards, cynicism, pessimism, and frivolity
- Glubb proposes a university department dedicated solely to studying the rhythm of
  rise and fall of powerful nations — covering all the world's great powers over 3,000 years
  - His essay covered only 11 empires (Middle East + modern West); excluded India,
    China, South America due to lack of knowledge
  - Such a school would likely find **at least 24 great powers** available for analysis

---

## Section XXXVIII: Would It Help?

- If the life-pattern of nations were fully understood, could measures be taken to forestall decline?
- Possible intervention: prevent the **Age of Conquests and Commerce** from
  deteriorating into the **Age of Intellect** (endless talk, no action)
- If taught in schools, the public might support policies maintaining duty and
  self-sacrifice, and forestalling the accumulation of excessive wealth
- But Glubb is skeptical: **human nature's weaknesses are too obvious**
  - Men bursting with energy cannot easily be restrained from subduing neighbors
  - Men who see wealth open to them will not readily be prevented from pursuing it
- Counterpoint: periods of wealth produce art, architecture, music, science, literature
- Great empires, despite wars and tragedies, often brought **peace, security, and
  prosperity** to vast territories during their height
- Conclusion: the rise and fall of nations may be **inevitable and divinely ordained**
  — but knowing this would reduce pointless hatred between factions fighting over
  developments that are beyond human control

> "Perhaps, in fact, we may reach the conclusion that the successive rise and fall of
> great nations is inevitable and, indeed, a system divinely ordained."
> — Glubb

## Section XXXIX: Summary (Master List)

- **(a)** We do not learn from history because our studies are brief and prejudiced
- **(b)** **250 years** emerges as the average length of national greatness
- **(c)** This average has not varied for 3,000 years — possibly represents **ten generations**
- **(d)** The six stages of rise and fall:
  1. **The Age of Pioneers** (outburst)
  2. **The Age of Conquests**
  3. **The Age of Commerce**
  4. **The Age of Affluence**
  5. **The Age of Intellect**
  6. **The Age of Decadence**
- **(e)** **Decadence** is marked by:
  - Defensiveness
  - Pessimism
  - Materialism
  - Frivolity
  - An influx of foreigners
  - The Welfare State
  - A weakening of religion
- **(f)** Decadence is **caused by**:
  - Too long a period of wealth and power
  - Selfishness
  - Love of money
  - The loss of a sense of duty
- **(g)** Life histories of great states are **amazingly similar** — due to **internal** factors
- **(h)** Their **falls** are diverse — largely the result of **external** causes
- **(i)** History should be taught as the history of the **human race**, not just one country

## Introduction

- Written in response to overwhelming reader mail after the first essay
- Glubb clarifies: his intent was **not pessimistic** — "the moral I would like to suggest
  is in the opposite sense — that we can save ourselves"
- Britain will continue to exist; the question is whether it will remain "strong, united
  and free, or become a nation of underlings and mendicants"
- Goal: **diagnosis first**, then proposed remedy

---

## Chapter 1: Learning from History

### A History of Past Events

- We learn nothing from history because our studies are **too narrow** — normally limited
  to our own country
- Glubb's daughter studying "the history of the trade union movement in Britain" —
  **contemporary politics masquerading as history**
- Physical science advances through experiments → theories; history of great nations
  should be studied the same way to derive **governing laws**
- History must mean the **history of the human race** from the beginning of recorded time

### Race Outbursts

- Chief cause of outbursts by "backward races": **jealousy** — desire to share the
  advantages of older, wealthier civilizations
- Why do barbarian conquests so often succeed despite overwhelming odds?
  - Old states **solidify into rigid forms**, weakening individual initiative and
    psychological drive
  - Example: **Vietnam War** — the Vietcong showed more initiative and enterprise
    than the Americans, despite the latter's vastly superior resources

### Military Luxury

- Highly organized armies of "old" nations develop **crippling overhead**
- Glubb's personal anecdote: British military column declared well water unfit;
  R.A.F. flew in water at enormous expense — meanwhile Arab irregulars marching
  ahead with a handful of dates each had already engaged the enemy
- British troops required daily meat rations; medical officers counting calories —
  while leaner, more mobile fighters moved faster and fought more effectively
- The **complicated machinery of regular armies** belonging to old nations is itself a
  military liability

## Chapter 2: Organisation and the Loss of Initiative

### Initiative and Self-Reliance

- In war, **morale is to the physical as three is to one** — energy, enthusiasm,
  and initiative determine ultimate outcome
- Key claim: **any form of organisation is liable to weaken initiative**
- Therefore: **the more the State is a welfare state, the more it destroys the
  initiative and self-reliance of its citizens**
- The welfare state's intentions are benevolent — but it was **fear and want** that
  produced individual initiative and drive; remove those, and initiative dies

### All Organisation Weakens Initiative

- The pioneer felled trees, built his own cabin, defended it himself — a real man
  relying entirely on his own efforts
- In modern industrial society, the individual is helpless:
  - Job encased in a closed shop; can only comply with union
  - If sick or unemployed, welfare state provides
  - Cannot leave to become self-employed without penalties
- The dilemma: **how can an industrial nation avoid organising? How can it organise
  without destroying human freedom and initiative?**
- Updated diagnosis: decline is due to too long a period of wealth and power,
  **plus too much organisation and regimentation**

## Chapter 3: Dividing the Nation

### Enterprise and the Exacerbation of Politics

- Decadent mentality no longer embarks on new enterprises — instead turns inward,
  quarreling over division of **inherited wealth**
- "A remarkable and unexpected symptom of national decline is the **intensification
  of internal political hatreds**"
- Two causes:
  1. **Substitution of money-making for honour, service, or adventure** as the object
     of life — money-making is selfish, destroys spirit of service to community
  2. **Loss of personal initiative** — without it, men cannot embark on fresh ventures,
     only fight over what exists
- Result: while Britain's car workers strike, French, Germans, Italians, and Japanese
  **invade and conquer** the domestic car market

### The Ruler Should Stand for All

- Intensified political in-fighting leads to leaders promising their supporters they
  will **injure the other half of the nation**
- The ruler must stand for the **whole nation** — differences of opinion on policy
  are acceptable, but not feuds that divide the nation

### The Influence of the Crown

- With bitter party politics, the Crown is the only institution covering the whole
  nation impartially
- British liberties depend more on the Crown than people realize:
  - Armed services swear allegiance to the Crown → kept out of party politics
  - Judiciary retains independence from political pressure
- Warning: in 1649, the **House of Commons executed the King** and abolished
  the Lords — result was not democratic rule but **ten years of military dictatorship**
  under Cromwell

### Secession

- Secession movements fragment nations into small states with separate passports,
  customs, and currencies — **narrowing free-trading areas** and making defence impossible
- Small splinter armies with different languages, training, weapons, and equipment
  are impossible to command in coalition

### Small is Beautiful — Or Is It?

- Small countries: personal, accessible leaders; everyone knows everyone
- But: **commerce and defence require larger areas** without customs barriers
  and with integrated armed forces
- Possible compromise: local autonomies combined with full union for commerce
  and defence

### The European Community

- Most press debate about EC membership focuses on "today's price of butter" —
  **short-sighted**
- Glubb frames the EC as a reaction against the ever-increasing proliferation of
  splinter states
- Historical parallel: **Roman Republic** was corrupt, materialistic, bitterly divided
  — transformed into the **Roman Empire** under **Augustus**, becoming a single
  trading area with common laws and language, rejuvenating wealth, culture, and power
  - "Augustus found Rome built of brick but left it made of marble"
- EC should be viewed in this broader statesman-like context of potential revival

### One Super-Power

- A single world super-power is unlikely to hold its position: **too many nationalisms**
  would reassert themselves through local guerrilla resistance
- A world conqueror would face innumerable local rebellions escalating to local wars

## Chapter 4: Sea Power

### Command of the Sea

- Sea power is one of the most **benevolent ways** a super-power can promote peace
  and commerce — less provocative than land garrisons in dependent territories
- Objection that nuclear war makes naval bases irrelevant is dismissed: by the same
  logic, one should give up all planning now
- Maintaining **British and American naval power** worldwide would have been a major
  factor in preventing future world wars

### The Oil Route

- Europe depends on Middle Eastern oil transported around the **Cape of Good Hope**
- The **Royal Navy** has been withdrawn from the Persian Gulf and Indian Ocean
- Naval bases and cooperation with South Africa abandoned for **ideological reasons**
  — Europe's oil lifeline left to chance or the Russians
- Framing the naval funding question as "we can't afford it" is like a housewife
  saying she can't buy food because of hire-purchase payments on a new car —
  **a question of priorities, not resources**

## Chapter 5: The Lust to Coerce

### Liberty or Compulsion

- Modern tendency: **reliance on coercion** — if you disagree with someone, force
  them to comply via strikes, blockades, or boycotts
- The irony: proclaim freedom of the individual loudly, yet immediately declare
  blockades when anyone differs — "every one of us seems to aspire to be a
  little dictator, though we all loudly denounce dictatorship"
- Coercing South Africa via boycott accomplishes less than would partnership
  and persuasion

### The Use of Violence

- Violence is **self-defeating**: produces resentment, which produces
  counter-violence, which produces counter-counter-violence, lasting centuries
- Chain of causation: **Napoleon's wars** → Franco-Prussian War of 1870 →
  both World Wars
- Ireland: **Strongbow's** 1170 invasion at request of the King of Leinster →
  Henry II's 1172 crossing → effects still visible today
- "That violence solves nothing and only breeds more violence is a **universal human law**"

### The Need to Maintain Strength

- Paradox: knowing violence breeds more violence, we must still maintain strength —
  many nations deliberately use violence for selfish ends
- **Reducing armed forces to fund social programs is dangerously short-sighted** —
  might lead to a war losing all social services and national independence
- "**Strength wins respect in this world.**" The weak who preach pacifism are ignored
  — their admonitions expose them to resentment and contempt

## Chapter 6: Energy and Leadership

### A Decline in Physical Energy

- Early 19th century: European visitors to London amazed by **intense pace and activity**
- By mid-century, pace transferred to **New York**
- By 1956: **Jean Gimpel** (*Blackwood's*, Feb 1977) reports Americans walking as
  leisurely as Romans, executives taking two-hour lunches — the energy had dissipated
- Question: is the decline in energy physical (comfort, overeating) or mental/moral
  (decline in idealism and spirit)?

### The Lost Art of Leadership

- Leadership principle: **"He that is greatest among you, let him be as the younger;
  and he that is chief, as he that doth serve."**
- Leadership should bring **duties, not privileges**
- Leaders must not ask subordinates to do more than they do themselves
- Key to morale: **personal relationships** — senior men must know subordinates
  personally, foster comradeship and mutual pride

### The Plague of Paperwork

- Ever-increasing paperwork keeps senior people at their desks, **militating against
  human relationships** — the actual substance of leadership
- Glubb's personal rule commanding the **Arab Legion**: three days per week in the
  office; three days visiting men in the field regardless of in-tray load
- Directors calling for more reports and statistics make it impossible for
  subordinates to carry out **essential tasks of leadership**

### Selfishness

- Report from American correspondent: outstanding characteristic of American life
  is **selfishness** — money-making has become the chief object of life
- Selfishness extends beyond individuals to **confederations, trade unions,
  and businesses** — all pursuing their own interests at the expense of the community

## Chapter 6 (cont.): The Necessity for Survival — Work, Corruption, Intellectualism

### The Necessity for Work

- Motto *Labor Omnia Vincit* ("Work conquers everything") as the constructive ideal
- Example: Asian immigrants arrive penniless in the US, wealthy within 5-6 years — because
  they work day and night; native Americans restrict hours
- **Trade unions** — achieved great things in Britain but also contributed to decline by
  urging members to restrict output
- Both business executives (long lunches) and shop-floor workers (output limits) are
  symptoms of the same decline: loss of energy and enthusiasm
- Positive leadership requires a **clarion call to service and sacrifice**, not vote-buying
  with money promises
- **Winston Churchill's** 1940 "nothing to offer but blood, toil, tears and sweat" cited as
  the model of constructive leadership — rousing people to enthusiasm, not bribery

### Corruption

- Bribery and corruption in public life described as "another deeply alarming symptom"
- **British civil servants** historically admired across the Middle East for never accepting
  bribes — Glubb personally heard this from Middle Eastern people throughout his career
- Recent (at time of writing) rise in British public servant corruption signals decline
- **Corruption is a cancer which destroys a nation from within**

### Intellectualism Cannot Provide a Solution

- Modern illusion: human cleverness, new ideology, new laws, intellectual planning,
  or a change of party can save a declining nation
- Politicians unwilling to admit decline is due to **loss of moral fibre**
- Core argument: no amount of intellectual cleverness can restore greatness to a nation
  that has lost its energy, initiative, honesty, and dedication to service

> "Only readiness for self-sacrifice can enable a community to survive."
> — Glubb

- The real problem: how to reform moral standards and revive devotion to service
- Glubb's personal belief: Britain could reverse course if it shed negative attitudes,
  stopped internal quarrels, and set to work energetically — "hit all our detractors for six"

## Chapter 7: The Position of Women

### Feminism

- Glubb received little comment on his observation that **female public prominence
  increases during periods of national decline** — the pattern surprised him
- Historical example: the Arab feminist movement ended at the close of the 9th century
  when breakdown of public security made it unsafe for women to go out unescorted
- The reason for women's emergence into public life during decadence is unknown — no
  historians have studied the rise and fall of nations systematically
- Provocative open question: can the men of a nation become decadent while the women
  do not?

### Reversal of the Sexes

- Increasing female prominence in public life coincides with some men desiring to
  imitate women — historical parallel: in **Baghdad** during Arab decline, young men
  wore female clothes and strings of beads
- This tendency Glubb associates with increased **homosexuality** — calls it the
  **"reversal of the sexes"**: men wanting to be women, women wanting to be men
- Acknowledges the majority continue normal relations; nonetheless flags this as a
  **sign of decadence**
- Offers no explanation or cure — records the pattern without being able to explain it

### Women and the Next Generation

- The first ten years of a child's life stamp its future character; **the mother's
  influence during those years is decisive**
- Glubb's claim: most great men in history owed their characters to their mothers,
  not their fathers
- **Working mothers of small children** therefore represent a grave danger to the
  quality of the next generation — the future human capital of the nation

### Broken Homes

- Rising desertion of spouses and children is an "alarming portent for the future"
- Children of broken homes grow up mentally unstable, refractory, cynical, delinquent
- Rejects the idea that private morality is a private matter:

> "The morals of every one of us are of vital importance to the future of our country."
> — Glubb

### Noble and Selfless Service

- Glubb's view: women render more **noble and selfless service** than men — the vital
  foundation of the nation — through domestic duties and child-rearing without public
  recognition
- Historical veneration of women (chivalry, deference, opening doors) reflects this
  deeper social truth: the nation is literally founded upon its women
- Calls the demand for women's "liberation" the most absurd of modern internal quarrels
- Personal testimony: his grandmothers (died pre-1910) presided "like empresses" over
  their households — no sense of inferiority; any good Glubb did he attributes to his mother

### Women and Culture

- Women are "the inspiration and the guardians of romance, poetry and culture"
- Men without female influence become coarse and brutal (army/frontier observation)

> "Men are the common clay of the human race, the hewers of wood and the drawers of
> water. How tragic it is that women, our guardian angels, the inspirers of all our best
> actions, should desire to descend from their high estate and rake in the mud of the
> sordid rat race."
> — Glubb

## Chapter 8: Population and the Future

### The Unexpected Happens

- Humility about prediction: throughout history, the wisest men of their age have
  consistently failed to foresee events — Providence always has an unexpected card
- Example: nobody ten or twenty years earlier foresaw the sudden wealth of **Iran and
  desert Arabia** from oil

### Means of Transport and Communication

- Modern transport/communication will profoundly affect the future — some predict
  a single human race with a common mixed culture
- Glubb is skeptical: a single global culture would represent **tragic monotony and
  impoverishment** of human life
- Key counter-argument: **propinquity does not mean cooperation or fraternisation**
  - Greeks and Turks lived together in **Cyprus** for centuries; only recently descended
    into violent hostility
  - **Ireland**: two races sharing an island that have never amalgamated
- More likely outcome: ease of transport creates **pockets of various races isolated
  amid different ethnic majorities**, not a blended monoculture

### Is Compulsory Integration Wise?

- **Middle Eastern governments** historically accommodated diversity by allowing
  separate quarters for Arabs, Turks, Armenians, Persians, Circassians, Jews,
  Christians, etc. — no trouble when all were fairly treated by an impartial government
- Glubb strongly opposes **forced integration** by governments — considers it officious
  and counterproductive
- Distinguishes: equal access to public benefits (schools, roads, services) — yes.
  Forced social amalgamation — no.

### National Homogeneity

- The idea of **national homogeneity** is of Western origin and has produced violence:
  - US: troops used to force school integration
  - **Israel**: hundreds of thousands of Palestine Arabs evicted by military force to
    achieve an entirely Jewish state
- "The intellectual conception of homogeneity, in an age of rapid transportation, seems
  destined to lead only to violence and hatred"
- Glubb acknowledges he earlier cited **influx of foreigners as a source of national
  weakness** — he still believes homogeneous nations would be better, but the solution
  is not mass forced transfer of populations
- As mixed populations become inevitable, **mutual toleration** is wiser than forced
  integration

### World Population

- Glubb admits uncertainty about the effect of global population growth
- Observation: sophisticated nations do not increase much in population; immense
  increases in **Asian populations** may constitute a threat to other nations

## Chapter 9: The Spirit Makes Alive

### The Effect of Spirit

- Core thesis of the final chapter: **it is not the cleverness of planning but the spirit
  which inspires the persons involved that ensures success**
- "Love will always find a way" — patient, benevolent, not jealous or arrogant
- The ideals of the **British Commonwealth** are based on this spirit: above all, not
  using compulsion except as a last resort to protect the weak from oppression

### A Revival of Religion

- Glubb's conviction: **moral standards can only be raised by a revival of religion**
- The proliferation of political and intellectual "-isms" proves no intellectual panacea
  can command general support — clever theories produce earthly paradise blueprints
  but cannot be built with violent, selfish people
- **Religion** defined broadly — not sectarian doctrine but:
  - The conviction that this life is not the end
  - That there is a spiritual world, invisible but penetrating all creation
  - That it can "strike a sympathetic note in every human heart"
- Only religion can persuade people to abandon short-term selfishness and dedicate
  themselves to the common good in **complete self-oblivion**
- Warning: **pride in our own cleverness** closes our minds to the spiritual world

### A Message of Hope

- The essay's purpose is not doom — decline is not due to external forces beyond
  our control, but to **our own greed, selfishness, immorality, and loss of courage**

> "The fault, dear Brutus, is not in our stars, but in ourselves, that we are underlings."
> — Shakespeare, quoted by Glubb

- These are failings each person can help to rectify — therefore hope exists
- Glubb's prescribed individual duties:
  - Carry out duties to family scrupulously
  - Work as hard as possible
  - Treat subordinates with comradeship and personal relations
  - Speak and write in favour of self-sacrifice, service, and unselfishness
- "Our country is obsessed by the grudging spirit of 'Why should I?'"
- Need leaders who inspire selfless service — but if leaders fail, individuals must
  set the example themselves

> "We need the spirit of the prophet who, when he heard that hard service was needed,
> cried joyfully, 'Here am I! Send me!'"
> — Glubb

## Summary (Glubb's 22-Point Conclusions)

- History teaching should encompass the **history of the human race**, not just
  national histories
- Too long a period of wealth leads inevitably to decadence — it must be studied and
  countered
- Old nations suffer from **atrophy through bureaucracy**, destroying individual
  initiative and psychological drive
- Armed forces from welfare states lose initiative — too luxurious a standard of
  living produces "all tail and no teeth"
- **Over-organisation** is a chief danger in industrial nations — destroys initiative
- Welfare state intentions are humane but a **spoon-fed population loses all initiative**
- Decadent nations stop exploring new wealth; energies are wasted in "sordid squabbles
  over inherited wealth"
- The **Crown's impartiality** is a national asset
- Security and prosperity require **large unified free-trade areas** — fragmentation
  increases poverty
- The **European Community** exists to create a wider trading area and integrated
  defence, not to reduce grocery prices
- Anglo-American command of the sea would be the surest guarantee of world peace
  and free trade — not grasped by modern politicians
- Modern **coercion** (weapons, strikes, blockades, boycotts) is one of the most
  disastrous trends — reason and persuasion produce better lasting results
- Loss of **physical energy** is a notable feature of declining nations; cause unknown
- Every individual can contribute by working harder and fostering comradeship and teamwork
- **Bribery and corruption** cannot be stopped by laws or penalties — only by
  diffusion of higher moral standards
- Increasing female prominence in public life is a symptom of national decline —
  reversal of the sexes unexplained, no research done
- Women are **guardians of the national future** through child-rearing; mothers of small
  children entering the workforce endangers the next generation
- Men should venerate women; women should not abandon their "high estate"
- Ease of travel will produce mixed populations — better to allow separate communities
  than force integration, which breeds hatred
- **Love — patient and benevolent — will always find a way**; the British Commonwealth
  ideals are based on this spirit
- Only a **revival of spiritual devotion** — not fashionable '-isms' — can inspire
  selfless service
- Each person can contribute through moral, dedicated living; if leaders fail to inspire,
  individuals must "go it alone"
