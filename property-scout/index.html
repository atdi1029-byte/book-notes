The complete guide to buying government property -- Maryland focus

Why Government Properties Sell Cheap

The government is not in the real estate business. When they end up owning property -- through unpaid taxes, criminal seizures, or loan defaults -- they want to get rid of it as fast as possible and recover whatever money they're owed. They don't care about getting market value. They care about getting the tax debt, the loan balance, or just clearing their books.

This creates a massive gap between what the government sells a property for and what it's actually worth on the open market. A house assessed at $150,000 might have $8,000 in unpaid taxes. The county doesn't need $150,000 -- they need $8,000. So they auction it for $8,000. That gap is your opportunity.

There are five main pipelines that funnel cheap property from the government to everyday buyers. Each works differently, has different risks, and requires different amounts of money and knowledge. Understanding all five -- and knowing which one fits your situation -- is the entire game.

Pipeline 1: Tax Lien Certificates

Medium difficulty

When a property owner doesn't pay their property taxes for about two years, the county government steps in. They can't just let people not pay -- the county needs that revenue for schools, roads, and services. So they put a "lien" on the property. A lien is a legal claim that says "this property owes us money."

Then the county holds an auction. But here's the key: in a tax lien state like Maryland, they're not selling the property itself. They're selling that lien -- the debt. When you buy a tax lien certificate, you're essentially paying the county what the owner owed, and now the owner owes YOU instead. You become the new creditor.

The property owner now has to pay you back the full amount plus interest. In Maryland, interest rates range from 6% (owner-occupied properties in some counties) up to 20% per year (non-owner-occupied in Montgomery County). That interest is guaranteed by law -- it's not negotiable.

Most of the time, the owner pays. Maybe it takes 3 months, maybe 18 months, but they pay. You get your money back plus the interest. That's the typical outcome -- and it's still a great return. Where else are you getting 12-20% guaranteed by a real property?

But sometimes the owner doesn't pay. Maybe they abandoned the house. Maybe they died and the heirs don't know about it. Maybe they just can't afford it. When that happens, you can file for foreclosure. After a waiting period (6 months in most of Maryland, 9 months for owner-occupied properties in Baltimore City), you hire a Maryland attorney, file a complaint in Circuit Court, and if the court approves, you get a deed. You now own the property. That $8,000 tax lien just turned into a house.

Real example: You buy a tax lien certificate at a Washington County auction. The delinquent taxes are $4,200. You win the bid at $4,200 (no overbid). The interest rate is 18%. After 8 months, the owner redeems. You get back $4,200 + $504 interest = $4,704. You earned $504 on a $4,200 investment in 8 months. Annualized, that's 18%.

Alternative outcome: The owner doesn't pay. After 6 months you file foreclosure. Attorney and legal fees cost you $3,500. Court process takes 10 more months. Total time: 16 months. Total invested: $7,700. But the property is worth $95,000. You just bought a $95K house for $7,700.

Pipeline 2: Tax Deed Sales

Medium difficulty

In tax deed states, the county skips the lien step entirely and sells the actual property at auction. You bid, you win, you get the deed -- you own the house immediately. No waiting for redemption, no foreclosure process, no attorney needed for the acquisition itself.

Maryland is NOT a tax deed state -- it's a tax lien state. But this matters to you because neighboring states ARE tax deed states. Pennsylvania does tax deed sales. So does Georgia, Florida, and Texas. If you're willing to look beyond Maryland, you can buy properties outright at auction in a single day.

The tradeoff: tax deed states tend to have more competition because the prize is more obvious. In a tax lien state, you might be the only bidder on a $3,000 lien because most people don't understand the system. In a tax deed state, everyone understands "I'm buying a house for cheap" -- so more people show up.

The Pennsylvania option: PA is right next door and runs judicial tax sales where you can buy properties outright. The counties of Adams, Franklin, and Cumberland are all within driving distance of the Harpers Ferry area. Pennsylvania tax sales happen year-round -- different counties, different schedules.

Pipeline 3: HUD / USDA / VA Foreclosures

Easiest

The federal government backs a huge portion of home mortgages in America. When you get an FHA loan, USDA loan, or VA loan, the federal agency is guaranteeing that loan. If the borrower stops paying, the bank forecloses, and the agency takes ownership of the property. Now the government owns a house they don't want.

HUD homes come from defaulted FHA loans. The Department of Housing and Urban Development lists them on HUDHomeStore.gov. This is the most beginner-friendly pipeline because it works like normal home buying -- you use a real estate agent, you can get financing, and if you're going to live in the property, you get first dibs before investors can even bid.

USDA properties come from defaulted rural development loans. Listed on resales.usda.gov. These are specifically in rural areas -- which is great if you're looking near Harpers Ferry, Western Maryland, or the Eastern Shore. Rural doesn't mean middle-of-nowhere -- many suburban areas qualify as "rural" under USDA's definition.

VA properties come from defaulted VA loans. Listed on listings.vrmco.com. You do NOT need to be a veteran to buy a VA-foreclosed property -- anyone can buy them. They just originated from veteran loans.

The incredible thing about HUD homes is the $100 down program. When you buy a HUD-owned home with an FHA loan, your down payment is literally $100. Not 3.5%, not $10,000 -- one hundred dollars. HUD also offers a 3% closing cost credit on eligible properties. With $30,000 in savings, you could buy a HUD home, keep almost all your cash, and use it for repairs and living expenses while you fix the place up.

Why this is probably your best first move: You want to live near Harpers Ferry. You have $30K. HUD homes let you buy with $100 down, keep your cash, get into a house you can live in, and build equity while you learn the rest of this system. No auctions, no foreclosure lawsuits, no attorneys beyond the standard closing. Just find a house, make an offer, close.

Pipeline 4: Federal Seized Assets

Advanced

When someone commits a federal crime -- drug trafficking, money laundering, fraud, tax evasion -- the government can seize their assets. Houses, cars, boats, jewelry, bank accounts, businesses. The US Marshals Service and the IRS are the two main agencies that handle these seizures.

US Marshals manages seized assets from criminal cases. These get auctioned through Bid4Assets (the same platform used for Maryland tax sales). The Marshals typically provide clear title via a Government Deed, which is better than most government sales. No buyer's premium. But inventory is tiny and sporadic -- a property near you might pop up once or twice a year.

IRS auctions sell property seized for unpaid taxes. About 300 auctions per year nationwide (not just real estate -- also cars, equipment, etc.). These are riskier because the IRS provides NO title guarantee. You might need a quiet title action afterward ($1,500-$3,000 legal cost) to clean up the ownership. Auctions can be in-person, online, or sealed bid.

Treasury Department handles forfeitures from customs, Secret Service, and ATF cases. Listed at treasury.gov/auctions. Mostly vehicles and personal property, but occasionally real estate.

These are exciting to read about but shouldn't be your primary strategy. The inventory is too small and unpredictable. Think of it as a bonus -- check the sites weekly, and if something shows up near you at a good price, jump on it. But don't wait around for it.

Pipeline 5: Surplus Government Property

Advanced

The federal government owns about 640 million acres of land -- nearly 28% of the entire United States. They also own hundreds of thousands of buildings. When they no longer need a piece of real estate (closed military bases, decommissioned post offices, excess federal land, abandoned buildings), it gets classified as "surplus" and sold.

RealEstateSales.gov (run by GSA) is the main portal. You need a Login.gov account to register. Properties range from vacant lots to massive commercial buildings. Most are not residential, but occasionally a small property shows up that could work.

GSA Auctions (gsaauctions.gov) handles more general government surplus -- vehicles, furniture, equipment, plus some real estate. GovDeals.com is where state and local governments sell surplus (Maryland State Police sells seized vehicles here). GovPlanet.com sells heavy equipment and military vehicles if that interests you.

For your situation, this pipeline is mostly interesting for land. If you find a cheap federal surplus lot in Western Maryland, you could buy land for almost nothing and build later, or hold it as an investment. But residential opportunities are rare.

Which Pipeline Should You Start With?

Scenario A: "I want to live in a house near Harpers Ferry"

Go with HUD homes. Search HUDHomeStore.gov for Washington County MD, Frederick County MD, and Jefferson County WV. Use the FHA $100 down program. Keep your $30K for repairs and a financial cushion. You could be in a house within 60-90 days.

Scenario B: "I want to invest my $30K and earn passive returns"

Buy tax lien certificates. Spread $20K across 5-8 liens at Maryland's annual tax sales (May-June). Keep $10K as reserve for foreclosure costs on any that don't redeem. Target 12-18% returns. This is essentially lending money at high interest, secured by real property.

Scenario C: "I want to buy the cheapest property possible"

Target tax liens on cheap properties in rural counties. Allegany County (Cumberland, MD) has the lowest property values in the state. Liens can be as low as $500-$2,000. If the owner doesn't redeem, you foreclose and own a house for under $10K total. Combine this with checking IRS auctions and US Marshals weekly for windfalls.

Scenario D: "I want to flip houses"

Combine HUD homes + tax liens. Buy a HUD home with FHA $100 down, fix it up with your $30K, live in it for 1 year (FHA requirement), then rent or sell it. Meanwhile, buy tax liens on the side with leftover cash. When one doesn't redeem, you get a second property for almost nothing.

Maryland Tax Lien Sales -- The Complete Guide

This section covers absolutely everything about how tax lien sales work in Maryland. Read this front to back and you'll know more than 90% of people who show up at auction.

The Timeline: How a Property Ends Up at Tax Sale

Maryland property taxes are due July 1st of each year and become delinquent on October 1st. When a property owner doesn't pay, the county starts adding interest and penalties. But they don't immediately auction it off -- they give the owner about two years to catch up.

After roughly two years of non-payment, the county treasurer places the property on the "tax sale list." This list is published in local newspapers and posted on the county's website 4-8 weeks before the auction. This is your research window -- the time to investigate every property on the list before you bid.

The actual auctions happen once per year in most counties. The bulk of Maryland tax sales fall between May and June. Baltimore County is the exception -- they run theirs in August through October. St. Mary's County goes early in March. Each county runs independently with its own rules, deposits, and platforms.

How the Bidding Actually Works

Maryland uses a highest-bidder auction format. This is different from some states (like New Jersey) where you bid DOWN the interest rate. In Maryland, you bid UP from a minimum.

The minimum bid (called the "sale amount") is the total of all unpaid taxes, interest, penalties, and the county's costs to run the auction. If a property has $4,000 in delinquent taxes and $800 in fees, the minimum bid is $4,800.

Bidders compete by offering more than the minimum. The highest bidder wins. But here's the critical thing most beginners don't understand: you only earn interest on the original lien amount (the unpaid taxes), NOT on whatever extra you bid above that.

The overbid trap explained: Say the lien amount is $4,000. You bid $12,000 to win the auction (because someone else bid $11,000). You paid $12,000 total. The interest rate is 18%. The owner redeems after 12 months. You earn interest only on the $4,000 lien: $4,000 x 18% = $720. Your total return: $720 on a $12,000 investment = 6%. The $8,000 overbid earned you nothing. If you'd won at $4,000, you'd have made 18%. Overbidding is the #1 way beginners destroy their returns.

So why do people overbid? Because they're betting the owner won't redeem. If you overbid $8,000 and the owner doesn't pay, you get to foreclose on a property that might be worth $100,000+. The overbid is the "price of admission" to potentially getting the property. Experienced investors calculate: "Is this property worth enough that even with my overbid + foreclosure costs, it's still a great deal?" If yes, they bid aggressively.

The High Bid Premium (Maryland-Specific)

Maryland has a unique rule: if your winning bid exceeds 40% of the property's full cash value (the assessed value from SDAT), you must pay an additional "high bid premium" equal to 20% of the amount exceeding that threshold.

Example:
Property assessed at $100,000
40% threshold: $40,000
Your winning bid: $55,000
Amount over threshold: $15,000
High bid premium: $15,000 x 20% = $3,000

You pay $55,000 + $3,000 = $58,000 total.
The premium is refundable if the owner redeems or you foreclose, but it earns ZERO interest. It's dead capital.

Interest Rates by County

Maryland law allows each county to set its own interest rate, with different rates for owner-occupied vs. non-owner-occupied properties.

Category Typical Rate
Non-owner-occupied (most counties) 12-18% per year
Montgomery County non-owner-occupied 20% per year
Owner-occupied (reduced rate) 6-10% per year
Montgomery County owner-occupied 6% per year

Interest is calculated daily from the date of the tax sale. The rate applies to the lien amount only (not overbid, not high bid premium). When the owner redeems, they pay you the full principal + accrued interest + documented costs (title search up to $250, attorney fees up to $500 pre-foreclosure).

Redemption -- When the Owner Pays You Back

In Maryland, the property owner (or anyone with a legal interest -- a mortgage holder, an heir, even a tenant in some cases) can "redeem" the property by paying you back at any time. There is no fixed redemption period that expires automatically. Instead, the right to redeem exists until a court finalizes the foreclosure.

What the owner must pay you to redeem:

  • The full amount you paid at auction (your winning bid)
  • Interest at the applicable rate (on the lien amount, not the overbid)
  • Title search fees (up to $250)
  • Attorney fees (up to $500 before foreclosure is filed; "reasonable" amount after filing)
  • Recording fees, certified mail costs, and other documented expenses
  • The high bid premium (refunded to you without interest)

Most owners who redeem do so within 6-18 months. The most frustrating scenario: you've already spent $3,000+ on foreclosure legal fees, you're 11 months into the process, and the owner redeems at the last minute. You get your money back plus interest, plus reasonable attorney fees -- but your net return after all the hassle might be modest. This is a real risk you need to accept.

Foreclosure -- Getting the Property

If the owner doesn't redeem, here's the legal process to actually get the property. This is Maryland-specific and governed by the Tax-Property Article, Title 14, Subtitle 8, Part III.

1
Wait the required period. 6 months from the sale date for most properties. 9 months for owner-occupied residential properties in Baltimore City. You cannot file before this -- the court will dismiss it.
2
Send two separate written notices. Maryland law requires you to send formal notices to the property owner and ALL parties with a legal interest (mortgage holders, other lien holders, heirs if known). The notices must include specific information mandated by statute -- your name, the sale date, the property description, the amount owed, and the owner's right to redeem. These must be sent by certified mail AND regular mail. Keep proof of mailing -- you'll need it in court.
3
Hire a Maryland attorney. You CANNOT file a tax sale foreclosure pro se (by yourself) in Maryland. You must use a licensed attorney. This is non-negotiable. Attorneys who specialize in tax sale foreclosures typically charge $1,500-$4,000 for the full process. Some work on a flat fee, others bill hourly. Get quotes from at least 3 attorneys. Search "Maryland tax sale attorney" -- there are firms that do nothing but this.
4
File the Complaint to Foreclose the Right of Redemption. Your attorney files this in the Circuit Court for the county where the property is located. The complaint must include proof of purchase, proof of notice, a title search, and other documentation. Court filing fees run $200-$400.
5
Serve all parties. Every person and entity with a legal interest must be formally served with the lawsuit. This includes the owner, all mortgage holders, all lien holders, and anyone else who appears in the title search. Service costs $100-$300.
6
Court review and judgment. If all parties are properly served and no one contests (or the court rules in your favor after a hearing), the court enters a judgment foreclosing the right of redemption. This order directs the county to issue you a deed. The court can also order publication in a newspaper if parties can't be located ($200-$500).
7
Receive your deed. The county or municipality issues a tax sale deed to you. You record it at the county clerk's office ($100-$300 in recording fees). You now legally own the property. The previous owner and all subordinate lien holders lose their rights.

Total timeline: From buying the lien to holding the deed: 12-24 months. The foreclosure process alone (steps 2-7) typically takes 6-18 months depending on how complicated the title is and how busy the court is.

The 2-Year Deadline -- Don't Miss This

Your tax lien certificate expires after 2 years. If you do not file a foreclosure complaint within 2 years of the sale date, your certificate becomes VOID. You lose your entire investment -- the lien amount, the overbid, everything. The property goes back to the owner free and clear. There is no extension, no appeal, no second chance. This is the single biggest mistake passive investors make.

Set a calendar reminder for 5 months after purchase. At that point, you're 1 month away from being eligible to file (6-month wait) and you have 19 months of runway. If the owner hasn't redeemed by month 5, start talking to an attorney and preparing to file.

What Liens Survive a Tax Sale Foreclosure

One of the most powerful things about tax lien foreclosure is that most other debts attached to the property get wiped out. But not all of them.

Wiped Out (you get clean title):

  • Private mortgages (first mortgage, second mortgage, HELOC)
  • Judgment liens (court-ordered debts)
  • HOA and condo association liens
  • Mechanic's liens (contractor debts)
  • Most other private encumbrances

This is huge. Imagine a house with a $200,000 mortgage that you acquire through tax sale foreclosure for $10,000. That $200,000 mortgage is gone. The bank loses their claim. This is why tax sales can produce such insane returns.

Survives (these DON'T go away):

  • IRS federal tax liens: The IRS gets a 120-day right of redemption AFTER your foreclosure is finalized. During those 120 days, the IRS can pay what you paid and take the property from you. If they don't act in 120 days, their lien is extinguished. But the uncertainty is stressful -- and the IRS does occasionally exercise this right on valuable properties.
  • Other federal liens: SBA loans, HUD liens, USDA liens may have similar federal protections.
  • EPA / environmental liens: Superfund liens survive tax sales. If the property has environmental contamination, YOU become the responsible party for cleanup. This can cost more than the property is worth -- sometimes millions.
  • Some municipal liens: Water/sewer bills may carry over depending on the jurisdiction.

Registering for a Maryland Tax Sale

Each county has its own registration process, but here's what's generally required:

  • Register 1-3 weeks before the sale date (deadlines vary -- check your county)
  • Government-issued photo ID
  • Deposit required (varies by county):
    • Cecil County: $100 (non-refundable)
    • Howard County: $1,000 via ACH
    • Prince George's: $150 registration + $1,000 deposit via ACH
    • Baltimore City: varies by year
  • Payment for won properties due within 24 hours to 3 business days
  • Accepted payment: certified check, wire transfer, or ACH (varies)
  • Some counties require proof you haven't defaulted on prior tax sales

Platform: Most Maryland counties now use online auctions, primarily through Bid4Assets.com. Baltimore City, Baltimore County, and several others run their sales there. Some counties (Montgomery, Prince George's) use their own sealed-bid systems. A few rural counties (Allegany, Garrett) still do in-person auctions at the courthouse.

Create your Bid4Assets account now -- it's free. Set up alerts for Maryland tax sales. When a county posts their sale, you'll get notified. One deposit covers all properties in that county's sale.

HUD Homes -- Complete Guide

If tax liens are the advanced play, HUD homes are the entry point. This is the closest thing to "normal" home buying in the government property world, and it's probably where you should start.

What Creates a HUD Home

About 15% of all US mortgages are FHA loans -- loans insured by the Federal Housing Administration (part of HUD). When an FHA borrower stops paying their mortgage, the bank forecloses. Because the loan was FHA-insured, HUD pays the bank's loss and takes ownership of the property. Now HUD owns a house they need to sell.

HUD doesn't want to be a landlord. They don't want to maintain these houses. They want to sell them fast, recover as much as possible, and move on. This urgency is why HUD homes often sell 10-30% below market value. They'll sometimes accept even lower offers on properties that have been sitting unsold for months.

How to Search and Bid

1
Search HUDHomeStore.gov. Filter by state (Maryland), county, price range, bedrooms, property type. Each listing shows the address, asking price, listing date, bid deadline, and property condition rating. New properties appear regularly -- check at least twice a week.
2
Understand the condition codes. HUD rates properties in three categories:

"Insured" (IE): The property meets FHA minimum standards. You can use an FHA loan with just $100 down. The house is livable as-is, though it may need cosmetic work.

"Insured with Escrow" (IN): The property needs some repairs but is mostly OK. You can use FHA financing, and HUD sets aside money in escrow for the specific repairs needed. Repairs must be completed within a set timeframe after closing.

"Uninsured" (UI): The property does NOT meet FHA standards -- too much damage. You'll need cash or conventional financing (no FHA). These are the cheapest but roughest properties. Often missing major systems (HVAC, plumbing), have structural issues, or have been stripped/vandalized.
3
Get a HUD-registered real estate agent. You MUST submit bids through a real estate agent who is registered in HUD's online system (SAMS). This is free -- any licensed agent can register. The agent's commission (typically 3%) is paid by HUD, not you. If your current agent isn't registered, ask them to sign up -- it takes about a day.
4
Bid during the owner-occupant exclusive period. For the first 7-15 days after listing (varies by property), ONLY people who will live in the house can bid. No investors. This is your advantage -- you're competing against fewer people. After the exclusive period, investors can bid too.

Your agent submits the bid electronically. You can bid at, above, or below the asking price. HUD evaluates all bids received during each bidding period and picks the highest "net" offer (your bid minus any closing cost credits HUD is providing).
5
Get pre-approved for financing. Before bidding, get pre-approved with an FHA-approved lender. This shows HUD you're serious and can close. The pre-approval letter goes with your bid. For the $100 down program, tell your lender specifically that you're buying a HUD home and want the FHA $100 down payment option.
6
Close the deal. Standard closing process -- title search, appraisal, inspection (highly recommended even though the property is as-is), and settlement with a Maryland attorney. Typical closing timeline: 30-60 days from accepted offer.

The $100 Down Program -- How It Actually Works

This is the single best deal in government real estate for someone in your situation. Here are the specifics:

  • Only applies to HUD-owned properties listed on HUDHomeStore.gov -- not random houses
  • Property must be coded "Insured" (IE) or "Insured with Escrow" (IN)
  • You must use an FHA loan from an FHA-approved lender
  • You must be an owner-occupant (live in the house for at least 1 year)
  • Your down payment is exactly $100. That's it. Not a percentage -- a flat $100.
  • HUD offers a 3% closing cost credit on eligible properties (this comes off the sale price)
  • Standard FHA qualification: credit score 580+ for 3.5% down (but you're paying $100, so the credit threshold may effectively be higher -- talk to your lender)
  • You still pay closing costs (appraisal, title insurance, attorney, recording fees) -- typically $5,000-$10,000 depending on price. But the 3% credit helps offset this.
  • The 2026 FHA loan limit for most Maryland counties is $541,287 (higher in some DC-adjacent counties)
Real math example:
HUD home in Washington County, MD: $85,000
Down payment: $100
3% HUD closing cost credit: -$2,550
Estimated closing costs: $6,000
Net closing costs: $3,450
Total cash needed: $3,550

You'd have $26,450 left from your $30K for repairs, furniture, and a financial cushion. Your monthly mortgage payment (30-year FHA at ~7% including PMI, taxes, insurance): roughly $750-$850/month.

USDA Properties -- Rural Deals

USDA Rural Development loans are designed for properties in "rural" areas -- but USDA's definition of rural is surprisingly broad. Many suburbs qualify. Pretty much everything around Harpers Ferry qualifies.

When USDA borrowers default, USDA takes the properties and lists them at resales.usda.gov. You can also use the USDA eligibility map at eligibility.sc.egov.usda.gov to check which areas qualify for USDA financing.

The USDA advantage: USDA loans offer 0% down payment. Zero. For eligible buyers in eligible areas, you can buy a home with literally no money down (you still pay closing costs). Income limits apply -- you must be at or below 115% of the area median income. For a single person in Washington County, MD, that's roughly $90,000-$100,000/year.

USDA REO properties can sometimes be purchased with USDA financing, giving you both the below- market price of a government foreclosure AND the 0% down payment benefit. Check resales.usda.gov weekly.

VA Foreclosures -- Open to Everyone

VA (Department of Veterans Affairs) properties come from defaulted VA loans. They're listed at listings.vrmco.com. Important: you do NOT need to be a veteran to buy a VA-foreclosed property. Anyone can buy them.

VA properties are typically in better condition than HUD properties because VA borrowers tend to have higher financial stability. Prices may be slightly higher, but the properties need less work. Worth checking, especially in the Harpers Ferry area where there are military installations nearby (Fort Detrick in Frederick, various DC-area installations).

Federal Seized Property -- Complete Guide

When the federal government seizes assets from criminals and tax evaders, those assets eventually get sold to the public. Here's exactly how each agency works.

US Marshals Service

The Marshals manage the largest federal asset forfeiture program. When FBI, DEA, ATF, or other federal agencies seize property during criminal investigations, the Marshals take custody and eventually sell it.

How to buy from the US Marshals:

  1. Go to bid4assets.com and search for "US Marshals" or browse their storefront at bid4assets.com/storefront/usms
  2. Create a free Bid4Assets account
  3. Browse current auctions -- they list real estate, vehicles, jewelry, electronics, and more
  4. Register for the specific auction you want to bid on (deposit required, varies per auction)
  5. Bid online during the auction window
  6. Highest bidder wins
  7. Payment due within the timeframe specified (usually a few business days)

Key details:

  • No buyer's premium -- what you bid is what you pay. Many private auction houses add 10-15% on top; USMS doesn't.
  • Clear title via Government Deed -- the Marshals typically provide good title, which is better than IRS or tax sale purchases.
  • Open houses sometimes available -- for real estate, they may allow in-person viewing before the auction.
  • Everything is as-is -- no warranties on condition, no returns.
  • Inventory is unpredictable and sparse. Maryland-specific properties might appear a few times per year at most.

IRS Seized Property

When someone owes the IRS a lot of money and refuses to pay, the IRS can seize their assets -- houses, cars, businesses, land. About 300 auctions happen per year nationwide.

How to buy from the IRS:

  1. Check irsauctions.gov for current listings
  2. Also check treasury.gov/auctions/treasury/rp/ for Treasury Department seizures
  3. Auctions can be:
    • In-person: Show up with government ID, cashier's checks in various denominations ($1,000, $2,500, $5,000), and cash for the deposit
    • Online: Register on irsauctions.gov, submit deposit, bid electronically
    • Sealed bid: Submit your bid in a sealed envelope by the deadline
  4. Minimum deposit is typically 20% of the minimum bid price, paid by cashier's check
  5. Balance due within a specified period (usually 30 days)
The big IRS risk: no title guarantee. Unlike the US Marshals, the IRS does NOT guarantee clean title. Other liens may still be attached to the property. You might need a "quiet title action" -- a lawsuit to establish clean ownership -- costing $1,500-$3,000 in attorney fees. Always do a thorough title search before bidding on IRS property.

Other Federal Sources

RealEstateSales.gov (GSA)

The General Services Administration sells surplus federal real property -- decommissioned buildings, excess land, former military installations. You need a Login.gov account to register. Most properties are commercial/institutional, but occasionally smaller residential-scale properties or raw land appears. Check monthly.

GSA Auctions (gsaauctions.gov)

Broader government surplus including vehicles, office furniture, electronics, and occasionally real estate. Good for non-property finds -- you can buy a fleet vehicle for $3,000 that's worth $15,000.

FDIC.gov -- Failed Bank Assets

When a bank fails, the FDIC (Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation) takes over and sells off the bank's assets -- including any real estate the bank owned or had loans on. This includes foreclosed homes the bank hadn't sold yet, bank branches, commercial properties, and vacant land. Search at fdic.gov/buying/owned for current listings. Properties are sold through contracted brokers -- you can often use a real estate agent and sometimes get financing. FDIC properties can be in surprisingly good condition because banks typically maintain them better than individual owners who abandoned the property. Inventory spikes after bank failures -- when a regional bank goes under, suddenly dozens of properties flood the market at once.

GovDeals.com

Where state and local governments sell surplus. Maryland State Police lists seized vehicles here. Counties sell surplus equipment, vehicles, and occasionally property. Good browsing for non-real-estate deals.

GovPlanet.com

Military surplus vehicles and heavy equipment. Want a decommissioned Humvee or a backhoe? This is the place. Not real estate related but interesting if you're buying property to develop and need equipment.

Maryland DHCD REO

The Maryland Department of Housing and Community Development sells foreclosed properties from state-backed mortgage programs. This is a hidden gem most people don't know about. Homebuying 101 -- The Complete Guide for People Without Much Money

Forget the government auction stuff for a moment. This section covers everything you need to know about buying a home in general -- especially when you don't have a lot of money. The basics of mortgages, credit, down payments, closing costs, and all the programs designed to help people in your exact situation.

How Mortgages Work -- From Zero

A mortgage is a loan to buy a house. The house itself is the collateral -- if you stop paying, the bank takes the house (foreclosure). You borrow money, buy the house, and pay it back over 15 or 30 years with interest.

Your monthly payment has four components, called PITI:

  • Principal: The actual loan amount being paid down. Early in the loan, most of your payment goes to interest; later, more goes to principal.
  • Interest: The cost of borrowing. If your rate is 7% on a $100,000 loan, you're paying about $7,000/year in interest (more in year 1, less in year 30 as the balance shrinks).
  • Taxes: Property taxes, collected monthly and held in "escrow" by the bank, then paid to the county for you.
  • Insurance: Homeowner's insurance, also collected monthly in escrow. Protects the house (and the bank's investment) from fire, storms, theft, etc.

On top of PITI, FHA and USDA loans add mortgage insurance (PMI/MIP). This protects the bank (not you) in case you default. FHA charges an upfront MIP of 1.75% of the loan amount (rolled into the loan) plus an annual MIP of about 0.55% (added to monthly payment). On a $100K loan, that's roughly $46/month extra.

Types of Loans and Which to Get

FHA Loan Best for you

Backed by the Federal Housing Administration. Designed for first-time and lower-income buyers. Down payment: 3.5% with 580+ credit score (or 10% with 500-579). On HUD homes: $100 down. More lenient credit requirements than conventional loans. Downside: mortgage insurance for the life of the loan (unless you refinance later).

USDA Loan

Zero down payment. Seriously -- 0%. But only for properties in USDA-eligible rural areas (check their map -- much of Maryland outside DC/Baltimore qualifies). Income limits apply (115% of area median). Lower mortgage insurance than FHA. Great option for the Harpers Ferry area.

VA Loan

Zero down payment, no mortgage insurance. Best loan product in America. Only for veterans, active military, and some surviving spouses. If you qualify, use this.

Conventional Loan

Not government-backed. Requires 5-20% down (PMI if under 20%). Stricter credit requirements (usually 620-680 minimum). Better terms if you have great credit and 20% down, but harder to qualify for with limited funds.

Hard Money Loan

From private lenders, not banks. Short-term (6-18 months), high interest (10-15%), based on the property value -- not your income or credit. Used by investors for flips, not for living in. Quick approval (days vs weeks). You'd use this if you find an incredible deal that needs cash fast.

Credit Scores -- What You Need and How to Fix Yours

Your credit score determines what loans you qualify for and what interest rate you get. Here's the breakdown:

ScoreWhat You Get
740+ Best rates on everything. Conventional loans easy to get.
680-739 Good rates. Conventional and FHA both available.
620-679 FHA is your best bet. Conventional possible but at higher rates.
580-619 FHA with 3.5% down (or $100 on HUD homes). Few other options.
500-579 FHA with 10% down. Very limited options.
Below 500 No standard mortgages. Cash only, hard money, or seller financing.

How to improve your score fast:

  • Pay every bill on time. Payment history is 35% of your score. One late payment can drop you 50-100 points. Set up autopay for everything.
  • Lower your credit utilization. This is 30% of your score. If you have a $1,000 credit limit and a $900 balance, that's 90% utilization = terrible. Get it below 30% ($300 on a $1,000 limit). Below 10% is ideal.
  • Don't close old accounts. Length of credit history matters (15% of score). That old credit card you never use? Keep it open.
  • Don't apply for new credit. Each application is a "hard inquiry" that drops your score 5-10 points. Exception: mortgage shopping within a 14-45 day window counts as one inquiry.
  • Dispute errors. Pull your free report at annualcreditreport.com. If anything is wrong (old debts you already paid, accounts that aren't yours), dispute them. This alone can boost your score significantly.
  • Become an authorized user. If someone you trust has a credit card with a long history and low balance, ask them to add you as an authorized user. Their history appears on your report. You don't even need the physical card.

Timeline: Paying down credit card balances can improve your score within 30-60 days (next billing cycle). Dispute resolutions take 30-45 days. Building from a thin file takes 6-12 months.

Down Payment Assistance Programs -- Maryland Edition

Maryland has some of the best down payment assistance programs in the country. These are not handouts -- they're low-interest or deferred loans and grants specifically designed to help people buy homes.

Maryland Mortgage Program (MMP)

  • Below-market interest rates on 30-year fixed mortgages
  • Down payment assistance up to $10,000 or 3-6% of the mortgage amount
  • Available for first-time buyers AND repeat buyers in targeted areas
  • Works with FHA, VA, USDA, and conventional loans
  • Apply through any MMP-approved lender
  • Income and purchase price limits apply (varies by county)

1st Time Advantage

  • 30-year fixed-rate mortgages with competitive rates
  • Includes DPA (down payment assistance) as a 0% interest deferred second mortgage
  • You don't pay the DPA back until you sell, refinance, or pay off the first mortgage
  • Must be a first-time buyer (haven't owned a home in 3 years)

Flex Loans

  • Available to repeat buyers too (not just first-time)
  • Includes a 0% interest second mortgage for down payment and closing costs
  • Same deferred repayment as 1st Time Advantage

HomeAbility

  • For buyers with disabilities
  • Up to 25% of the purchase price as a 0% interest deferred second loan
  • Enormous assistance -- on a $100K house, that's $25,000

SmartBuy 3.0

  • Up to $20,000 toward student loan debt payoff
  • Must use the payoff to eliminate ALL outstanding student debt
  • Can combine with other MMP programs

Local Programs

  • Prince George's County: up to $10,000 in assistance
  • Gaithersburg: up to $40,000 as a 0% deferred loan
  • Montgomery County: various programs through HOC (Housing Opportunities Commission)
  • Baltimore City: several programs through the city's housing department
  • Check your specific county -- most have something
Stack them together. You can often combine multiple assistance programs. FHA $100 down on a HUD home + Maryland MMP down payment assistance + county-level assistance. People buy homes with essentially zero out of pocket using these combinations. Talk to an MMP-approved lender about what you qualify for.

The Home Buying Process -- Every Step

1
Check your credit score. Free at annualcreditreport.com or through your bank/credit card app. Know where you stand before talking to lenders.
2
Get pre-approved. Talk to 2-3 lenders (including at least one MMP-approved lender). They'll tell you how much you can borrow, what rate you'll get, and what programs you qualify for. Pre-approval takes 1-3 days. You'll need: pay stubs, tax returns, bank statements, ID.
3
Find a real estate agent. As a buyer, you don't pay your agent -- the seller does (typically 2.5-3% commission). Find someone who knows the area you're looking in and is registered for HUD bidding.
4
Search and tour homes. HUDHomeStore.gov, Zillow, Redfin, Realtor.com, resales.usda.gov. Drive through neighborhoods. See places in person. Look beyond the cosmetic -- a ugly house with good bones is better than a pretty house with foundation problems.
5
Make an offer. Your agent writes the offer. Include your pre-approval letter, earnest money deposit (typically $500-$2,000 held in escrow -- you get it back if the deal falls through for covered reasons), and any contingencies (inspection, financing, appraisal).
6
Home inspection. ALWAYS get an inspection, even on as-is properties. Cost: $350-$500. The inspector checks foundation, roof, HVAC, plumbing, electrical, structure. This tells you what you're really buying and what repairs will cost.
7
Appraisal. The lender sends an appraiser to confirm the house is worth what you're paying. If the appraisal comes in low, you can renegotiate the price, pay the difference in cash, or walk away. Cost: $400-$600 (you pay this).
8
Final approval and clear to close. The lender reviews everything one last time. They may ask for additional documents. Don't change jobs, make large purchases, or open new credit during this period -- it can kill your loan.
9
Closing / settlement. You sit down with a Maryland attorney, sign a mountain of paperwork, hand over your closing costs (certified check or wire), and get the keys. The deed is recorded. You own a house. Total time from offer to close: 30-60 days.

Hidden Costs of Homeownership Nobody Tells You About

  • Maintenance: 1-2% of home value per year. A $100K house costs $1,000-$2,000/yr in maintenance. Things break constantly -- water heater, appliances, pipes, roof patches.
  • Utilities: $200-$400/month. Electric, gas/oil, water, sewer, trash, internet. Older houses (which is what you'll be buying) cost more to heat and cool.
  • HOA fees: If there's an HOA, $50-$300+/month. Avoid HOAs if possible -- especially on a tight budget.
  • Yard/exterior maintenance: Mower, snow removal, gutter cleaning, tree trimming. Budget $500-$1,500/year or do it yourself.
  • Property tax increases: Taxes can go up. Maryland reassesses every 3 years. If you buy cheap and the area improves, your taxes could jump significantly.
  • Emergency fund: You need $3,000-$5,000 accessible at all times for emergencies. Furnace dies in January? That's $5,000 you need immediately.
The real rule: Your total housing cost (PITI + insurance + maintenance + utilities) should not exceed 35% of your gross monthly income. Push it to 40% and you're house-poor -- owning property but unable to enjoy your life. With $30K saved up, make sure you still have a financial cushion after buying.

Maryland Tax Sale Law

The governing statute is the Tax-Property Article, Title 14, Subtitle 8, Part III of the Maryland Code. Key provisions:

  • Section 14-808: County authority to conduct tax sales
  • Section 14-817: High bid premium calculation
  • Section 14-820: Interest rates on redemption
  • Section 14-827: Right of redemption (until court forecloses)
  • Section 14-833: Foreclosure complaint requirements, notice rules, 6-month and 9-month waiting periods

Maryland is an attorney state for real estate. An attorney must handle settlement/closing on any property purchase, and you must use an attorney for tax sale foreclosures. Budget $500-$1,500 for standard closings, $1,500-$4,000+ for foreclosures.

Baltimore City vs Suburbs vs Rural

Baltimore City

Thousands of vacant and abandoned properties. Tax sale inventory is massive -- hundreds of properties per sale. Less competition per individual property because there are so many. Liens can be very cheap ($1,000-$5,000). But the condition risk is extreme -- many properties are gutted shells, have structural collapse, or need $50,000+ in rehab. Water bills are included in Baltimore City tax liens (combined with property taxes). Owner-occupied properties get a 9-month wait before foreclosure (vs 6 months elsewhere). High crime in some areas makes renovation and rental challenging.

Suburban Counties (Montgomery, Howard, Anne Arundel, Prince George's)

Higher property values, better neighborhoods, stronger rental demand. But tax sales are fiercely competitive -- institutional investors and experienced bidders drive overbids sky-high. A $3,000 lien might get bid to $40,000+ in Montgomery County. Returns on the lien investment get crushed. These counties make sense only if you're specifically targeting the foreclosure play (betting the owner won't redeem) and you've done deep due diligence on the property's value.

Rural Counties (Allegany, Garrett, Dorchester, Somerset, Caroline)

Lowest competition, lowest prices, easiest to win auctions at or near the minimum bid. Properties may be cheaper but they're also in slower markets -- harder to flip, lower rental demand. Best for building a portfolio of passive lien income (earn interest) or finding a personal home on the cheap. Allegany County (Cumberland) in particular has homes under $100K everywhere and tax liens sometimes go for the minimum bid because nobody else shows up.

How to Evaluate Any Property

Before you spend a single dollar, you need to know how to research a property and figure out if it's worth buying. This is the skill that separates people who make money from people who lose money.

Step 1: Look It Up on SDAT

SDAT (State Department of Assessments and Taxation) is Maryland's official property database. Go to sdat.dat.maryland.gov/RealProperty and search by address.

You'll find:

  • Assessed value: The government's estimate of the property's worth (for tax purposes). Usually lower than market value, but gives you a floor.
  • Owner name: Who currently owns the property.
  • Land use code: Residential, commercial, agricultural, vacant, etc.
  • Zoning: What you're allowed to do with the property.
  • Transfer history: When it last sold and for how much.
  • Tax account status: Is it current, delinquent, or in tax sale?
  • Building details: Square footage, number of rooms, year built, construction type.
Red flag: If the assessed value is lower than the tax lien amount, that's a warning. You might end up owning something worth less than what you paid.

Step 2: Google Earth / Street View

Type the address into Google Maps and drop into Street View. Look for:

  • Condition of the house (roof, siding, windows, yard)
  • Is it boarded up? Vacant? Occupied?
  • What do the neighboring houses look like?
  • Is the neighborhood maintained or declining?
  • Proximity to amenities, main roads, schools
  • Any obvious problems (leaning walls, caved-in roof, overgrown lot)

Note: Street View images can be years old. Check the date stamp in the bottom of the image. A house that looked fine in 2020 might be a wreck in 2026. Nothing replaces an in-person drive-by if the property is within driving distance.

Step 3: Check Comparable Sales (Comps)

Comps tell you what a property is actually worth in today's market. Search for recently sold homes near your target that are similar in size, condition, and type.

How to find comps:

  • Go to Zillow or Redfin
  • Search the address, then filter for "Recently Sold" within 0.5-1 mile
  • Look for homes sold in the last 6 months
  • Match: similar square footage (within 20%), similar bedrooms/bathrooms, similar lot size, similar age
  • Find 3-5 good comps and average their sale prices

For the ARV (After Repair Value): Find comps that are renovated/updated. That's what your property could sell for after you fix it up. For the as-is value: Find comps in similar condition to how the property looks now.

Example: You're looking at a 3BR 1200sf house in Hagerstown. You find 3 similar renovated homes that sold recently: $175K, $182K, $168K. Average: $175K. That's your ARV estimate. Now find 3 similar unrenovated homes: $85K, $92K, $78K. Average: $85K. That's the as-is value.

Step 4: Check for Liens and Title Issues

A property might look great and be priced right, but hidden liens can destroy the deal. Here's what to check:

Maryland Land Records (mdlandrec.net): Search by owner name or property address. Look for recorded mortgages, judgment liens, mechanic's liens, and releases. Free registration required. If you're buying through tax sale foreclosure, most private liens get wiped out -- but you need to know they exist for proper notice requirements.

MD Judiciary Case Search (casesearch.courts.state.md.us): Search by the owner's name. Look for pending lawsuits, judgments, and foreclosure actions. Multiple lawsuits against the owner = trouble.

IRS lien search: Check Maryland Land Records for any "Notice of Federal Tax Lien" recorded against the property. If there's an IRS lien, the IRS has 120 days post-foreclosure to take the property from you. This is a serious risk.

Environmental check: Search EPA's site (epa.gov/superfund) and Maryland Department of the Environment records. Was this property ever a gas station, dry cleaner, auto shop, or industrial site? Environmental contamination makes a property radioactive (sometimes literally).

For serious purchases: Hire a title company or attorney to do a full title search. Cost: $250-$500. Worth every penny. They'll find things you'll miss. If you're buying through normal channels (HUD, agent), your lender will require title insurance anyway -- the title company does the search as part of that process.

Scenario 1: Tax Lien Passive Income

You buy 6 tax liens at the Washington County sale for a total of $18,000 (average $3,000 each). Keep $12,000 in reserve.

5 owners redeem within 12 months at 18% interest. You earn $2,700 in interest on $15,000 invested. 1 owner doesn't redeem -- you decide whether to foreclose ($3,500 legal cost) or let it go.

Scenario 2: HUD Home Near Harpers Ferry

HUD lists a 3BR/1BA in Hagerstown for $75,000 (condition: Insured with Escrow). You bid $72,000, HUD accepts. FHA $100 down program.

Traps, Risks, and How to Avoid Them

Every one of these has cost real people real money. Read them all before you spend a dollar.

1. Buying a Lien on Worthless Property

You see a tax lien for $2,000 and think "cheap!" But the property is a condemned building on a postage-stamp lot worth $1,500. Even if you foreclose, you own something worth less than you paid. Add in $3,000-$5,000 in foreclosure costs and you're deep in the hole.

How to avoid: ALWAYS check the assessed value on SDAT before bidding. Google Earth the address. If the lien amount is more than 30% of the assessed value, scrutinize hard. Drive by if you can. Never bid on a property you haven't at least virtually visited.

2. Overbidding and Killing Your Returns

Auction fever is real. You planned to bid $5,000 but someone else bids $4,800 and your competitive instinct kicks in. You end up at $15,000 on a $3,000 lien. If the owner redeems, you earned 18% on the $3,000 lien ($540) but tied up $15,000 for a year. Your actual return: 3.6%. You'd have done better in a savings account.

How to avoid: Write down your maximum bid for every property BEFORE the auction. Tape it to your monitor. When the bidding hits your max, stop. No exceptions. The next property might go for the minimum.

3. IRS Liens Surviving Your Foreclosure

You spend 18 months and $4,000 in legal fees to foreclose on a property. You get the deed. Then the IRS exercises their 120-day right of redemption and takes the property. They pay what you paid -- no profit, no premium for your time and legal costs.

How to avoid: Search Maryland Land Records (mdlandrec.net) for "Notice of Federal Tax Lien" on every property before bidding. If you find an IRS lien, walk away unless the numbers are so good that even losing to the IRS doesn't hurt.

4. Environmental Contamination

You foreclose on a former gas station lot. Soil is contaminated with petroleum. EPA Superfund liens survive tax sales. You're now the "responsible party" for cleanup. Cost: $50,000 to $500,000+. The property is worth $20,000. You're financially destroyed.

How to avoid: Check EPA's Superfund search. Google the property's history. What was it before? Commercial or industrial history = red flag. Stick to residential properties when starting out. If it was ever a gas station, dry cleaner, auto repair shop, factory, or chemical business, walk away.

5. Letting Your Certificate Expire

In Maryland, your tax lien certificate becomes void after 2 years if you haven't filed foreclosure. Zero refund, zero recourse, total loss. This happens to passive investors who buy liens and forget about them.

How to avoid: Set a calendar reminder for 5 months after purchase. At month 5, evaluate: has the owner redeemed? If not, start the foreclosure process. Don't wait until month 22 hoping they'll pay.

6. Occupied Properties and Eviction

You foreclose and discover someone is living in the house -- either the former owner or a tenant. Maryland is a tenant-friendly state. Eviction takes 2-4 months and costs $1,000-$3,000 in legal fees. During that time, the property may be damaged, especially if the occupant is hostile.

How to avoid: Drive by the property before bidding. Check for signs of occupancy (cars, mail, lights, maintained yard). Budget for eviction costs on any property that might be occupied. Consider "cash for keys" -- offering the occupant $1,000-$3,000 to leave voluntarily. It's faster, cheaper, and less adversarial than court.

7. The High Bid Premium Surprise

You bid $60,000 on a property assessed at $100,000. The 40% threshold is $40,000. You're $20,000 over. The premium: $20,000 x 20% = $4,000 extra. This is refundable but earns zero interest. It's dead capital that inflates your investment without improving your return.

How to avoid: Know the assessed value (from SDAT) before you bid. Calculate your 40% threshold. If you'll go over, factor the premium into your return calculations.

8. Confusing Tax Lien with Tax Deed

The single most common beginner mistake: thinking you bought a house when you bought a piece of paper. A tax lien certificate is NOT ownership. You own the debt. The property owner still owns the house. You can't move in, you can't renovate, you can't rent it out. You have to go through the entire foreclosure process (6-24 months, $2,500- $5,500+ in legal costs) before you own anything.

How to avoid: Just knowing this puts you ahead. Tax LIEN = you own the debt. Tax DEED = you own the property. Maryland is a lien state. Always.

9. Not Budgeting for Property Taxes During Foreclosure

While you're waiting 12-24 months for the foreclosure process, the property is still accruing new taxes. If you don't pay them, someone else can buy a NEW tax lien on top of yours. Now you're in a mess -- competing lien holders, legal complications, potential loss of your investment.

How to avoid: Budget for ongoing property taxes from the day you buy the lien. In Maryland, rates range from about $0.60 to $1.10 per $100 of assessed value per year. On a $100K property, that's $600-$1,100/year you need to set aside.

10. Falling for "Tax Lien Guru" Scams

Instagram, YouTube, and TikTok are full of people selling $997 courses on "how to buy houses for pennies on the dollar through tax liens." They show screenshots of properties, flash cash, and promise passive income. Most of them have never actually completed a tax lien foreclosure.

Reality: The information is all public and free. County websites, state law, auction platforms -- it's all accessible. You're reading a guide right now that covers everything those courses teach. The real knowledge comes from doing your first deal, not watching someone else talk about it. Save your $997 for an actual lien.
