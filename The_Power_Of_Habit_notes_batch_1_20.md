## Introduction: The Central Premise

Most daily choices feel deliberate but are actually **habits**—behaviors we decide on
once, then stop thinking about while continuing to do them. Though individual habits
seem small, they accumulate over time with enormous impacts on health, productivity,
financial security, and happiness.

Understanding habits is a recent scientific development. For centuries, philosophers
and thinkers puzzled over habits (Aristotle, William James), but only in the past two
decades have **neurologists, psychologists, sociologists, and marketers** begun to
understand how habits work and, critically, how they can change.

> "All our life, so far as it has definite form, is but a mass of habits."
> — William James (1892)

A Duke University study found that **more than 40 percent** of daily actions are habits,
not conscious decisions. This book draws on hundreds of academic studies, interviews
with over 300 scientists and executives, and research from dozens of companies to
explore how habits function at individual, organizational, and societal levels.

## The Prologue: Lisa Allen's Transformation (The Habit Cure)

### The Study Subject

**Lisa Allen** was the scientists' favorite participant. Age 34, she had started smoking
and drinking at 16, struggled with obesity, and accumulated $10,000 in debt. At one
point, collection agencies were pursuing her and her longest job lasted less than a
year. Yet when researchers met her, she was lean, vibrant, with the toned legs of a
runner—a decade younger than in her medical file. She had no outstanding debts, didn't
drink, was in her thirty-ninth month at a graphic design firm, and had completed a
master's degree while buying a home.

The researchers—neurologists, psychologists, geneticists, and a sociologist at the
**National Institutes of Health**—wanted to understand how she had remade her life in
a relatively short period.

### The Research Method

For three years, the team studied more than two dozen former smokers, chronic
overeaters, problem drinkers, obsessive shoppers, and people with other destructive
habits. They measured vital signs, installed video cameras in homes to observe daily
routines, sequenced DNA sections, and used technologies to peer inside people's skulls
in real time, watching brain electrical impulses while subjects faced temptations like
cigarette smoke and lavish meals. The goal: to understand how habits work on a
neurological level and what it takes to change them.

### The Cairo Turning Point

Lisa's transformation began with a trip to Cairo. Her husband had come home from work
announcing he was leaving her for another woman. After processing the betrayal through
mourning, obsessive spying, and a drunken confrontation at the girlfriend's house,
Lisa reached a breaking point. In her hotel room in Cairo at dawn, woken by the call
to prayer from a mosque, she experienced complete despair—unable to smoke properly
(she tried to light a pen instead of a cigarette), unable to sleep, feeling "like
everything I had ever wanted had crumbled."

> "It was like this wave of sadness... I felt like I couldn't even smoke right. And
> then I started thinking about my ex-husband, and how hard it would be to find another
> job when I got back, and how much I was going to hate it and how unhealthy I felt
> all the time."

She broke down emotionally. But in that moment, looking at the pyramids and desert from
the taxi, she made a decision: **she would trek through the Egyptian desert**. She had
one year to prepare, and she was certain she would have to make sacrifices. In
particular, she would have to quit smoking.

### The Cascade of Change

When Lisa finally crossed the desert eleven months later (in a motorized tour with
others, carrying water, food, tents, GPS, and two-way radios—cigarettes wouldn't have
mattered), the researchers later learned the critical detail: **Lisa didn't know that.**
What mattered was that one small shift in her perception—the conviction that she *had
to* give up smoking—had triggered a series of changes radiating through every part of
her life.

Over the next six months, she replaced smoking with jogging. This change cascaded: it
altered how she ate, worked, slept, saved money, scheduled her workdays, and planned
for the future. She ran half-marathons, then a full marathon, went back to school, bought
a house, and got engaged. The researchers tracked her brain scans and discovered something
remarkable: her old habit neural patterns had been **overridden by new patterns**. Her old
behaviors were still neurologically detectable, but new urges had crowded them out.

### The Keystone Habit Concept

The researchers concluded that it wasn't the divorce or desert trek itself that caused the
shift. **Lisa had focused on changing one habit—smoking**—at first. Everyone in the study
had gone through a similar process. By focusing on one pattern (known as a **"keystone
habit"**), Lisa had taught herself how to reprogram the other routines in her life as well.

It's not just individuals who are capable of such shifts. When companies focus on changing
habits, whole organizations can transform. Companies like **Procter & Gamble, Starbucks,
Alcoa, and Target** have seized on this insight to influence how work gets done, how
employees communicate, and how customers realize they're shopping.

> "You're helping us understand how a decision becomes an automatic behavior," the doctor
> told Lisa.

## The Book's Structure and Scope

The book is divided into three parts:

**Part One** focuses on how habits emerge within individual lives. It explores the
neurology of habit formation, how to build new habits and change old ones, and methods
for changing habitual patterns. Examples include how one man made toothbrushing a
national obsession, how Procter & Gamble turned Febreze spray into a billion-dollar
business by leveraging consumers' habitual urges, how **Alcoholics Anonymous** reforms
lives by attacking habits at the core of addiction, and how coach **Tony Dungy** reversed
the fortunes of the worst team in the National Football League by focusing on players'
automatic reactions to subtle on-field cues.

**Part Two** examines the habits of successful companies and organizations. It details
how an executive named **Paul O'Neill**—before he became treasury secretary—remade a
struggling aluminum manufacturer into the top performer in the Dow Jones Industrial
Average by focusing on one keystone habit. It covers how **Starbucks** turned a high
school dropout into a top manager by instilling habits designed to strengthen willpower,
and why even the most talented surgeons can make catastrophic mistakes when a hospital's
organizational habits go awry.

**Part Three** looks at the habits of societies. It recounts how **Martin Luther King, Jr.**
and the civil rights movement succeeded, in part, by changing the ingrained social habits
of Montgomery, Alabama, and why a similar focus helped a young pastor named **Rick Warren**
build the nation's largest church in Saddleback Valley, California. It explores thorny
ethical questions, such as whether a murderer in Britain should go free if he can
convincingly argue that his habits led him to kill.

**Central Argument**: Habits can be changed, if we understand how they work.

## Author's Personal Journey into Habit Science

**Charles Duhigg** first became interested in the science of habits eight years ago as a
newspaper reporter in Baghdad. The U.S. military, he observed, is one of the biggest
habit-formation experiments in history. Basic training teaches soldiers carefully designed
habits for shooting, thinking, and communicating under fire. On the battlefield, every
command issued draws on behaviors practiced to the point of automation. The entire
organization relies on endlessly rehearsed routines for building bases, setting strategic
priorities, and deciding how to respond to attacks.

In Iraq for about two months, Duhigg heard about an impromptu habit modification program
in Kufa, a small city ninety miles south of Baghdad. An **army major** had analyzed
videotapes of recent riots and identified a pattern: violence was usually preceded by a
crowd gathering in a plaza or open space, growing in size over several hours. Food
vendors would show up, spectators gathered, and eventually someone threw a rock or bottle,
triggering violence.

### The Kufa Intervention

When the major met with Kufa's mayor, he made an unusual request: Could they keep food
vendors out of the plazas? The mayor agreed. A few weeks later, a small crowd gathered
near the **Great Mosque of Kufa**. Throughout the afternoon, it grew in size. Some people
started chanting angry slogans. Iraqi police sensed trouble and radioed the U.S. base,
asking troops to stand by. At dusk, the crowd got restless and hungry. People looked for
kebab sellers normally filling the plaza—but there were none. Spectators left. Chanters
became dispirited. By 8 P.M., everyone was gone. **There hadn't been a riot since he
arrived.**

When Duhigg visited the base near Kufa and spoke with the major, he was told: "You
wouldn't necessarily think about a crowd's dynamics in terms of habits. But he had spent
his entire career getting drilled in the psychology of habit formation." At boot camp, the
major had absorbed habits for loading weapons, falling asleep in war zones, maintaining
focus amid chaos, and making decisions while exhausted. As he moved up ranks, he learned
the importance of **organizational habits**—how the right routines made it easier to work
alongside people he normally couldn't stand. The community, he said, was a giant collection
of habits occurring among thousands of people that, depending on how they're influenced,
could result in violence or peace.

> "Understanding habits is the most important thing I've learned in the army. It's
> changed everything about how I see the world. You want to fall asleep fast and wake up
> feeling good? Pay attention to your nighttime patterns and what you automatically do
> when you get up. You want to make running easy? Create triggers to make it a routine. I
> drill my kids on this stuff. My wife and I write out habit plans for our marriage. This
> is all we talk about in command meetings. Not one person in Kufa would have told me that
> we could influence crowds by taking away the kebab stands, but once you see everything
> as a bunch of habits, it's like someone gave you a flashlight and a crowbar and you can
> get to work."

The major was a small man from Georgia who perpetually spit either sunflower seeds or
chewing tobacco. Prior to the military, his best career option had been repairing
telephone lines or, possibly, becoming a methamphetamine entrepreneur (a path some high
school peers had chosen). Now he oversaw eight hundred troops in one of the most
sophisticated fighting organizations on earth.

> "I'm telling you, if a hick like me can learn this stuff, anyone can. I tell my
> soldiers all the time, there's nothing you can't do if you get the habits right."

### The Broader Understanding

In the past decade, understanding of the neurology and psychology of habits and the way
patterns work within our lives, societies, and organizations has expanded dramatically.
We now know why habits emerge, how they change, and the science behind their mechanics.
We can break habits into parts and rebuild them to our specifications. We understand how
to make people eat less, exercise more, work more efficiently, and live healthier lives.
Transforming a habit isn't necessarily easy or quick, and it isn't always simple.

> "But it is possible. And now we understand how."
