## Chapter 1: Second-Level Thinking

### The Challenge of Investment Success

> "The art of investment has one characteristic that is not generally appreciated. A creditable, if unspectacular, result can be achieved by the lay investor with a minimum of effort and capability; but to improve this easily attainable standard requires much application and more than a trace of wisdom." —**Ben Graham**

> "It's not supposed to be easy. Anyone who finds it easy is stupid." —**Charlie Munger**

### Why Few Succeed

- Few people have what it takes to be **great investors**
- **Valid approaches work some of the time but not all**
- Investing can't be reduced to an algorithm

#### Fundamental Reasons
- **No rule always works**—the environment isn't controllable
- **Psychology plays a major role** in markets, and it's highly variable
- If others emulate an approach, that will blunt its effectiveness

### Investing as Art, Not Science

- Investing, like economics, is **more art than science**
- Investment approach must be **intuitive and adaptive** rather than fixed and mechanistic

### The Goal: Beating the Market

- Anyone can achieve **average investment performance**—just invest in an index fund
- Successful investors want to **beat the market**

> "Beating the market matters, but limiting risk matters just as much." —**Seth Klarman**

### Superior Insight: The Key Requirement

- To beat the market, you need either **good luck or superior insight**
- As with any art form, **some people just understand investing better than others**
- They have—or manage to acquire—that necessary **"trace of wisdom"**

### The Universality of the Profit Motive

- Everyone wants to make money
- All of economics is based on belief in the **universality of the profit motive**

#### The Competitive Challenge
- Universality makes **beating the market a difficult task**
- Millions of people are competing for each available dollar of investment gain
- Who'll get it? **The person who's a step ahead**
- In investing, it calls for **more perceptive thinking … at what Marks calls the second level**

### Second-Level Thinking Defined

- Only a few will achieve the **superior insight, intuition, sense of value, and awareness of psychology** required for consistently above-average results
- Doing so requires **second-level thinking**

#### The Necessity of Being Different
- Goal in investing isn't to earn average returns—you want to do **better than average**
- Thus, thinking has to be **better than that of others**—both more powerful and at a higher level
- You must **think of something they haven't thought of, see things they miss, or bring insight they don't possess**
- In short: **being right may be necessary for investment success, but it won't be sufficient**
- You must be **more right than others**, which by definition means your thinking has to be different

> "Marks's comments in this paragraph are excellent. He successfully articulates the critical importance of second-level thinking to investment success." —**Paul Johnson**

### Examples of First-Level vs. Second-Level Thinking

#### Example 1: Quality Company
- **First-level thinking**: "It's a good company; let's buy the stock."
- **Second-level thinking**: "It's a good company, but everyone thinks it's a great company, and it's not. So the stock's overrated and overpriced; let's sell."

#### Example 2: Economic Outlook
- **First-level thinking**: "The outlook calls for low growth and rising inflation. Let's dump our stocks."
- **Second-level thinking**: "The outlook stinks, but everyone else is selling in panic. Buy!"

#### Example 3: Earnings Expectations
- **First-level thinking**: "I think the company's earnings will fall; sell."
- **Second-level thinking**: "I think the company's earnings will fall less than people expect, and the pleasant surprise will lift the stock; buy."

### Characteristics of Each Level

- **First-level thinking** is:
  - Simplistic and superficial
  - All the first-level thinker needs is an opinion about the future

- **Second-level thinking** is:
  - Deep, complex, and convoluted
  - Takes a great many things into account:
    - What is the range of likely future outcomes?
    - Which outcome do I think will occur?
    - What's the probability I'm right?
    - What does the consensus think?
    - How does my expectation differ from the consensus?
    - How does the current price comport with the consensus view and with mine?
    - Is the consensus psychology too bullish or bearish?
    - What will happen if the consensus turns out to be right, and what if I'm right?

## Chapter 2: Understanding Market Efficiency (and Its Limitations)

### First-Level vs. Second-Level Thinking

- **First-level thinking** is simplistic: look for easy formulas and obvious answers
- **Second-level thinking** is complex, demanding, and rare
- To beat the market in a **zero-sum competition**, you must outthink the consensus
- Most investors can't beat the market since, collectively, they **are** the market

### The Chicago School and Efficient Market Hypothesis

- 1960s emergence of new financial theory at **University of Chicago** (1967–1969)
- Theory included: **risk aversion**, **volatility** as risk, **risk-adjusted returns**, **alpha**, **beta**, **random walk hypothesis**, **efficient market hypothesis** (EMH)
- EMH states that:
  - Many market participants share roughly equal information access
  - Information reflects fully and immediately in market prices
  - No investor can consistently identify and profit from mispricings
  - Markets provide "no free lunch"

### Misconceptions About Market Efficiency

> "In theory there's no difference between theory and practice, but in practice there is." — Yogi Berra

- **Efficient ≠ right**: EMH means "speedy, quick to incorporate information," not "correct"
- Yahoo at $237 (Jan 2000) vs. $11 (April 2001) shows prices aren't always right
- If efficient markets reflect consensus, sharing consensus views yields average returns
- **Beat the market by holding idiosyncratic, nonconsensus views**

Assumptions underlying EMH are flawed:
- **Human psychology**: Investors driven by greed, fear, envy—not clinical objectivity
- **Specialization bias**: Most professionals assigned to particular niches
- **Short-selling constraints**: Tiny percentage sell short

### Market Efficiency Spectrum

- Markets fall on an **efficiency spectrum**, not binary states
- **Efficient markets are possible** when:
  - Asset class widely known with broad following
  - Socially acceptable, not controversial
  - Merits clear and comprehensible
  - Information distributed widely and evenly

- **Foreign exchange** example: millions analyzing currencies—consistent outperformance difficult
- **Stock markets** (NYSE): millions prospecting, driven by profit motive—mispricing detection rare

### Inefficiency as Opportunity

- **Inefficiency** = markets prone to mistakes that can be taken advantage of
- **Three characteristics** of inefficient markets:
  - Market prices often far above or below intrinsic values
  - Risk-adjusted return on one asset class can significantly diverge from others
  - Some investors outperform consistently

- Inefficient markets don't guarantee generous returns—they provide **mispricings** allowing winners and losers
- **"In every poker game there's a fish. If you've played 45 minutes and haven't figured out who the fish is, then it's you."**
- **Necessary condition for outperformance**: Inefficiencies must exist. Sufficient condition: Must be more insightful than others

### Market Efficiency as Presumption

- Efficiency is **rebuttable presumption**: assume efficiency true until proven otherwise
- **Five key questions**:
  - Why should a bargain exist despite thousands of willing buyers?
  - If return generous relative to risk, might hidden risk be overlooked?
  - Why would seller part with asset at price delivering excessive return?
  - Do you truly know more about the asset than the seller?
  - If such a great proposition, why hasn't someone else snapped it up?

- Efficiency alone doesn't prevent outperformance. Inefficiency is necessary condition; guarantees nothing.
- **Practical conclusion**: Let others believe markets unbeatable. Abstention creates opportunities for those who will.

## Chapter 3: The Most Important Thing Is... Value

### Buy Low, Sell High: The Essence of Value Investing

- Oldest rule in investing: "Buy low; sell high"—**low and high are relative to intrinsic value**
- Goal: Buy below intrinsic value; sell above
- Must estimate asset's **intrinsic value**—the indispensable starting point

> "Warren Buffett says that the best investment course would teach just two things well: How to value an investment and how to think about market price movements."

### Fundamental vs. Technical Analysis

Two basic approaches:
1. **Fundamental analysis**: gauge security's intrinsic value based on company attributes
2. **Technical analysis**: study of price behavior of securities

- **Random walk hypothesis** undermined technical analysis: stock's past price movements absolutely unhelpful for predicting future movements
- **Momentum investing** assumes things rising continue rising. Drawback: How will momentum investors sell before decline?

### Value vs. Growth Investing

Two fundamental approaches:
- **Value investors**: estimate current intrinsic value; buy when price is lower
- **Growth investors**: find companies whose value will grow rapidly

Key distinction: **value today vs. value tomorrow**

### What Makes a Business Valuable?

- Sources: **financial resources, management, factories, patents, brand names, growth potential**
- Above all: **ability to generate earnings and cash flow**
- Value investing **emphasis on tangible factors** (hard assets, cash flows); less weight on intangibles

### The Nifty Fifty and Growth Investing Failure

- 1968: **First National City Bank** followed "**Nifty Fifty**" approach—buy companies with brightest growth outlooks at any price
- Portfolio included IBM, Xerox, Kodak, Polaroid, Merck, Coca-Cola, Philip Morris
- Decades later: Kodak and Polaroid undone by tech changes; others deteriorated
- Lesson: Cannot accurately predict company growth over 40+ years. **Long-term growth persistence unpredictable**

### Value Investing as Analytical Discipline

- **Value investors buy stocks** when current value is high relative to current price
- **Growth investors buy stocks** because they believe future value growth will produce substantial appreciation
- Distinction: **value emphasizes current-day considerations; growth emphasizes future**

## Chapter 3: Understanding Value (continued)

### Growth vs. Value Investing Comparison

- **Growth investing** centers on finding big winners—companies with breakthrough products
- Harder to predict the future than assess the present
- **Value investing** offers more consistent results with less drama
- Marks's preference: "consistency trumps drama"

### The Challenge of Value Investing

- Depends on accurate value estimates—without them, you're as likely to overpay as underpay
- Must hold your valuation view firmly—being right about value doesn't mean being proved right immediately
- "It's hard to consistently do the right thing as an investor. But it's impossible to consistently do the right thing at the right time."

> "Ideally, considerably less. The bigger the discount, the bigger your margin of safety." —Seth Klarman

> "If you do a good job valuing a stock, I guarantee that the market will agree with you. I just don't tell them when." —Joel Greenblatt

### The Patience Required

- After buying at 60 when intrinsic value is 80, price may drop to 50
- "Being too far ahead of your time is indistinguishable from being wrong"

> "Fear of looking wrong. Superior investors' batting averages will be well below 1.000 and marked by errors and slumps. Judgment that prove correct don't necessarily do so promptly. If you're not okay with that, try another field." —Howard Marks

### Investor Psychology and Price Behavior

**The demand curve paradox:** In investing, opposite of normal demand:
- People fall more in love with assets as prices rise (feeling validated)
- Like them less as prices fall (begin to doubt their decision)
- If you liked it at 60, you should like it more at 50 and much more at 40 and 30

### The Danger of Losing Conviction

- Eventually investors wonder if they're wrong: "It's down so much, I'd better get out before it goes to zero"
- This panic-driven thinking creates bottoms and forces selling
- **Investors without knowledge of fundamentals** cannot possess the resolve needed to act correctly

> "An accurate opinion on valuation, loosely held, will be of limited help. An incorrect opinion on valuation, strongly held, is far worse."

### Most Investors' Approach

- Give most investors truth serum: "I look for things that will go up"
- Serious profit pursuit must be based on **fundamentally derived intrinsic value**
- An accurate estimate of intrinsic value is the essential foundation for steady, unemotional, potentially profitable investing

### Three Requirements for Value Investing Success

1. **Have a view on intrinsic value**
2. **Hold that view strongly** enough to buy even as declining prices suggest you're wrong
3. **Be right**

## Chapter 4: The Relationship Between Price and Value

> Investment success doesn't come from "buying good things," but rather from "buying things well."

### Price as the Starting Point

- Even with accurate value estimates, you must compare asset's price relative to value
- **No asset is so good it can't become bad if bought at too high a price**
- **Few assets are so bad they can't be good when bought cheap enough**

### The Fallacy of "Good" Asset Classes

- No asset class or investment has birthright of high returns
- Only attractive if priced right
- **Bottom line: there's no such thing as a good or bad idea regardless of price**

> "No matter how good an investment sounds, if price has not yet been considered, you can't know if it is a good investment." —Joel Greenblatt

### Efficient Market Hypothesis and Fair Value

- If you buy something for fair value, expect a fair return—no more than the risk justifies
- Buying at intrinsic value is no great shakes
- Paying *more* than intrinsic value is clearly a mistake

### The Nifty Fifty Example

**Late 1960s-early 1970s:** The **Nifty Fifty** stalwart companies had P/E ratios of 80-90:
- Postwar average P/E for stocks: midteens
- Within a few years: **Nifty Fifty** P/Es fell to 8 or 9
- Investors lost 90% even in America's best companies
- Great companies bought at the wrong price

**Oaktree philosophy:** "Well bought is half sold"
- If bought cheap, exit questions answer themselves

### Price Convergence with Value

- If intrinsic value estimate is correct, over time price should converge with value

> "Keeping this statement in mind, that the market eventually gets it right, is one of the most important things to remember when the market acts emotionally." —Joel Greenblatt

### Psychology and Technicals

Beyond fundamental value, price is affected by **psychology and technicals**—nonfundamental factors

**Two technical examples:**
1. **Forced selling** during market crashes when leveraged investors receive margin calls
2. **Cash inflows to mutual funds** requiring portfolio managers to buy

- "There's nothing better than buying from someone who has to sell regardless of price during a crash"
- Many of Oaktree's best buys came from forced sellers
- **Being a forced seller is the worst**—essential to arrange affairs so you can hold on

### The Primacy of Psychology

**Psychology** exerts powerful influence on price:
- Key to understanding price/value relationship lies in insight into other investors' minds
- **Investor psychology can cause a security to be priced anywhere in the short run, regardless of fundamentals**
- Key is who likes the investment now and who doesn't
- **Investing is a popularity contest**—most dangerous thing is buying at the peak of popularity
- At that point, all favorable facts already factored into price; no new buyers left

> "The biggest losers had something in common: no one could find a flaw. Nothing's perfect, however. When you pay for perfection, you don't get what you expected, and the high price exposes you to risk of loss." —Howard Marks

**The safest and most potentially profitable thing:** Buy something when no one likes it. Given time, its popularity and price can only go one way: up.

### Mastering Psychology

"Clearly, this is yet another area that is (a) of critical importance and (b) extremely hard to master."

**Two challenges:**
1. Psychology is elusive
2. Psychological factors that weigh on other investors will weigh on you too

> "The human side of investing is the critical side. Control over emotion and ego is quite difficult, since everything in the investing environment conspires to make investors do the wrong thing at the wrong time." —Howard Marks

**Essential understanding:** Fundamental value is only one factor determining security price. Try to have psychology and technicals on your side as well.

### Bubbles: The Opposite of Value Investing

**Mindlessly chasing bubbles** ignores the relationship between price and value entirely.

All bubbles start with some nugget of truth:
- Tulips are beautiful and rare (17th-century Holland)
- The Internet is going to change the world
- Real estate can keep up with inflation

**Bubble mechanics:**
- A few clever investors figure out these truths and invest
- Others catch on or notice people making money—they buy as well
- As price rises, investors think less about whether price is fair
- Extreme demand curve paradox: people like something more when price rises

### The 2004-2006 Housing Bubble

People conjured only good things about houses:
- Desirability of American dream
- Ability to benefit from inflation
- Accepted wisdom: "home prices only go up"

### The Tech Bubble and Greater Fool Theory

- Buyers didn't worry whether stocks were priced too high—sure someone else would pay more
- **Greater fool theory** works only until it doesn't
- Valuation eventually comes into play

**Key points about bubbles:**
- The positives behind stocks can be genuine and still produce losses if you overpay
- "Prices are too high" is far from synonymous with "the next move will be downward"
- **Eventually, though, valuation has to matter**

> "Read that final bullet again!" —Joel Greenblatt

### The Problem with "Attractive at Any Price"

In bubbles, "attractive" morphs into "attractive at any price":
- "It's not cheap, but I think it'll keep going up"
- Buying on that basis is extremely chancy—**that's what makes bubbles**
- Counting on others to give you profit regardless of value is probably the least dependable

### Routes to Investment Profit

**1. Benefiting from a rise in intrinsic value:**
- Problem: increases in value are hard to predict accurately
- Conventional view usually baked into price

**2. Applying leverage:**
- Doesn't make anything a better investment
- Merely magnifies whatever gains or losses may materialize
- Introduces risk of ruin

> "This is one of the most important comments in chapter 4. This is a lesson that all young investors fail to learn at their own peril." —Paul Johnson

**3. Selling for more than your asset's worth:**
- Hoped-for arrival of this sucker can't be counted on

**4. Buying something for less than its value (Marks's preferred route):**
- "In my opinion, this is what it's all about—the most dependable way to make money"
- Doesn't require serendipity—just requires that market participants wake up to reality
- **When the market's functioning properly, value exerts a magnetic pull on price**

> "Eventually the market does operate and get it 'right.' Buying at the right price is the hard part. Once done correctly, time and other market participants take care of the rest." —Paul Johnson

### Even Buying Cheap Isn't Infallible

Of all routes to profit, buying cheap is most reliable, but isn't sure:
- Can be wrong about current value
- Events can reduce value
- Deterioration in attitudes can make something sell even further below value
- Convergence can take more time than you have

> "The market can remain irrational longer than you can remain solvent." —John Maynard Keynes

**Trying to buy below value isn't infallible, but it's the best chance we have.**

## Chapter 5: Understanding Risk

> "Risk means more things can happen than will happen." —Elroy Dimson

### Risk: The Essential Element

**Investing consists of exactly one thing:** dealing with the future:
- Because none can know the future with certainty, risk is inescapable
- Risk is an essential—**the** essential—element in investing
- Unlikely to succeed long without dealing explicitly with risk

**Three-step process:**
1. Understanding risk
2. Recognizing when it's high
3. Controlling it

> "Marks's discussion of risk in chapters 5, 6, and 7 is the most comprehensive and complete I have ever seen. These three chapters are the highlight of the book for me." —Paul Johnson

### Three Reasons Risk Assessment Is Essential

**First:** Risk is a bad thing; most people want to avoid or minimize it:
- Underlying assumption in financial theory: people are naturally **risk-averse**

**Second:** Investment decisions should be a function of both risk and potential return:
- Investors must be bribed with higher prospective returns to take incremental risks
- This process—**equilibration**—renders prospective returns proportional to risk
- Investor's second job: determine whether the return justifies taking the risk

**Third:** When considering investment results, return means little by itself:
- Was return achieved in safe instruments or risky ones?
- Must have feeling for "risk-adjusted return"

### The Risk-Return Relationship

**The traditional graph:** Shows capital market line sloping upward to the right—indicates positive relationship between risk and return

**The correct formulation:** Riskier investments have to offer higher **expected**, **promised**, or **prospective** returns

**But there's absolutely nothing to say those higher prospective returns have to materialize.**

**Common misconception:** "Riskier investments provide higher returns"
- **Wrong:** If riskier investments reliably produced higher returns, they wouldn't be riskier

### Marks's Visualization of Risk

The capital market line with probability distributions:
- Shows not just expected return but range of possible outcomes
- Illustrates that uncertainty (width of distribution) increases with risk

> "Investing requires us to deal with the future. The graphic illustrates the essence of 'riskier' investments—greater uncertainty regarding the outcome, as well as the increased likelihood of some painful ones." —Howard Marks

**Riskier investments** are those for which the outcome is less certain—the probability distribution of returns is wider.

**The traditional risk/return graph is deceptive:**
- Communicates the positive connection between risk and return
- **Fails to suggest the uncertainty involved**
- Has brought many people misery through unwavering intimation that taking more risk leads to making more money

### Defining Risk

**Synonyms for risk:** danger, hazard, jeopardy, peril—all sound undesirable.

**Yet finance theory** defines risk precisely as **volatility**—none of these conveys "peril."

**Marks's objection to volatility as risk:**
- Academicians settled on volatility as matter of convenience
- Needed a number that was objective and could be ascertained historically
- **Volatility fits the bill; most other types of risk do not**
- "I just don't think volatility is the risk most investors care about"

**The real risk investors care about:**
- Many kinds of risk exist; **volatility may be the least relevant**
- Hard to believe volatility is the risk investors factor in when setting prices
- Marks has never heard anyone say "I won't buy it because its price might show big fluctuations"

**Rather than volatility, people decline to make investments primarily because they're worried about:**
- **Loss of capital**
- **Unacceptably low return**

**Risk** is—first and foremost—**the likelihood of losing money.**

## Chapter 5: Understanding Risk (continued)

### Many Forms of Investment Risk

- **Permanent capital loss** is the only risk **Paul Johnson** says investors should truly worry about—but many other types demand attention
- Different risks matter to different investors based on circumstances

Key risk types:
- **Falling short of goals** — each investor has unique needs; failure to achieve required returns carries personal, subjective risk
- **Underperformance** — investors avoiding risk often get beaten by indices
  - In 1999, underperformance by **Warren Buffett** and **Julian Robertson** was a badge of courage as they refused tech bubble participation
- **Career risk** — when money managers fear losses threatening employment more than gains benefiting clients
- **Unconventionality** — stewards of others' money prefer mediocre performance over being different and fired
  - Creates opportunities for unorthodox investors brave enough to be unconventional
- **Illiquidity** — when investor needs cash but can't convert investment at reasonable price

### What Creates Risk of Loss

- Risk of loss doesn't require weak fundamentals—sound company bought at too-high price carries substantial risk
- **Arrogance** + failure to understand risk can wreak havoc
- **Psychology that's too positive** drives the core mechanism: investors pay too much for sexy stocks
  - High prices imply both high risk and low future returns
- **Distinction from theory**: theorists say high return compensates for high risk; **pragmatic value investors** feel opposite—high returns AND low risk come from buying undervalued assets at low prices
- Cheap, overlooked securities often have strong fundamentals yet low volatility—exactly what value investors seek

### Measuring Risk: The Central Problem

> "The riskiest things: The greatest risk doesn't come from low quality or high volatility. It comes from paying prices that are too high." —Howard Marks

- Risk measurement is fundamentally a **matter of opinion**, not objective calculation

**Four reasons why risk can't be quantified precisely:**

1. **Subjectivity of measurement** — investors define risk differently
2. **Nonexistent standard** — no universal way to compare risk across investments
3. **Risk is deceptive** — rare, extraordinary events are hard to quantify
   - The **improbable disaster** that occurs infrequently seems safer than it really is
4. **Subjectivity persists** — risk is largely invisible before the fact, hidden and unquantifiable

- **Ben Graham** and **David Dodd**: "the relation between different kinds of investments and the risk of loss is entirely too indefinite to permit of sound mathematical formulation"
- **Skillful investors** judge risk primarily on: (a) **stability and dependability of value**, (b) **relationship between price and value**

### Attempts to Quantify: Sharpe Ratio and Beyond

- Financial institutions employ quantitative models (**value at risk**) to measure risk
- Results are no better than inputs—subjective estimates will never rival experienced investors' judgments
- **Sharpe ratio** = portfolio excess return / standard deviation
  - Used because it's the best tool available
  - Problem: doesn't measure future loss risk itself
- For **private assets** lacking market prices, subjective risk adjustment is the only tool available

### Risk as Revealed After the Fact

- Risk can't be measured in retrospect more reliably than a priori
- The fact an investment worked doesn't mean it wasn't risky; a loss doesn't mean it was
- **Nassim Nicholas Taleb's** *Fooled by Randomness* explores "alternative histories"—central to understanding risk

> "There's a big difference between probability and outcome. Probable things fail to happen—and improbable things happen—all the time."

- Even after investment closes, impossible to tell whether risk was reasonable or merely lucky
- Only over very large numbers of trials can probability estimates be validated

### The Central Role of Uncertainty

> "Risk means more things can happen than will happen." —Elroy Dimson

- Many things are possible but only one future occurs
- **Investing is fundamentally about the future** — impossible to "know" future
- Most important: **improbable things happen regularly** in investing; must think about full range of possibilities
- Greatest losses often arise when investors ignore improbable possibilities

### Bell-Shaped Distributions and Normal Distribution Myth

- To cope with future, need sense of possible outcomes and likelihoods
- Most phenomena form **bell-shaped curve**: peak at center, trailing off at extremes
- Normal distribution is specific bell-shaped distribution with definite statistical properties
- Failure to distinguish between **bell-shaped** (general) and **normal** (specific) contributed to recent credit crisis
- **Financial engineers** assumed normally distributed events
  - Normal distribution assumes distant-tail events infrequent
  - **Financial behavior driven by emotion should have "fatter" tails**
  - When mortgage defaults began, events thought unlikely proved fallible

### Risk as Opinion

> "Fully understanding this insight will be a major step toward understanding investment performance." —Paul Johnson

- **Here's the key**: risk is largely a matter of opinion, hard to be definitive even after the fact
- **For most part**: investment performance is what happens when **geopolitical, macro-economic, company-level, technical and psychological** developments collide with extant portfolio
- Many futures possible; only one occurs

### Recognizing Risk: Recency and Expectations

- **Investment risk comes primarily from too-high prices; too-high prices from excessive optimism and inadequate risk aversion**
- **In boom times** — people forget risk, embrace risk-taking to excess; bull markets feature prices so high they imply losses
- Truth: **risk tolerance is antithetical to successful investing** — without fear, risky investments won't carry compensation

### The Myth of "Risk Is Gone"

- **Belief that risk has been banished** is one of most dangerous risk sources; major bubbles are fueled by this belief

**2005–2007 beliefs about risk reduction:**
- Adroit central bank management eased economic cycles
- Globalization spread risk geographically
- Securitization/syndication distributed risk
- Risk "tranched out" to those best able to bear it
- Lower rates made leverage less risky
- Derivatives hedged risk
- Computers, math, modeling made markets better understood

- **Result**: Tales like risk control prove illusory; world becomes more risky despite appearing safer
- **"Too much trust, too little worry"** — carefree, unworried investors are their own worst enemy

### The Root Cause: Excess Optimism and Inadequate Risk Aversion

- **Crisis root cause**: Investors believed too much, worried too little, took too much risk
- **Worry and its relatives** — distrust, skepticism, risk aversion — are **essential** to safe financial systems
  - Worry prevents risky loans, excessive debt, concentrated portfolios
  - When worry is in short supply, financial system becomes precarious
  - Only when investors are sufficiently **risk-averse** do markets offer adequate premiums
- **The bottom line**: Risk premium appears only when investors are suitably risk-averse

### Summary on Risk Understanding

- **Investment risk is largely invisible before the fact**—except to those with unusual insight
- Great financial disasters represent **risk forecasting and management failures**

**Six core reasons for this invisibility:**

1. Risk exists only in future; impossible to know future; only one scenario occurs
2. Risk-bearing decisions contemplated in normal patterns; occasionally something very different happens
3. Future expectations cluster around historic norms; people underestimate potential for change
4. "Worst-case" projections often turn out not negative enough
5. Risk shows up lumpily; unusual spate of defaults can occur at point in time
6. People overestimate ability to gauge risk and understand new mechanisms

**Finally**: risk-taking viewed primarily as money-making avenue; but **it can't always work that way**—when risk-bearing doesn't work, it **really** doesn't

## Chapter 7: Controlling Risk

> "When you boil it all down, it's the investor's job to intelligently bear risk for profit. Doing it well is what separates the best from the rest."

### Risk Control vs. Absolute Returns

- Outstanding investors distinguished by **risk control ability** as much as return generation
- High absolute return is more recognizable than superior risk-adjusted performance

**What makes great investors:**
- Take risks **less than commensurate** with returns earned
- High returns with high risk means little — unless sustained many years

**Examples of great risk controllers:**
- **Warren Buffett, Peter Lynch, Bill Miller, Julian Robertson**
- Remarkable records due to decades of consistency and absence of disasters

### Risk Is Invisible Until Loss Occurs

- Risk awards never given in good times because risk is covert, invisible
- **Risk = possibility of loss** — not observable until negative events occur
- **Loss is what happens when risk meets adversity**

**Key insight**: Risk may have been present even though loss didn't occur
- Absence of loss ≠ portfolio was safely constructed
- **Only skilled observer can distinguish low-risk vs. high-risk portfolio in good times**

**Bottom line**: Risk control invisible in good times but essential — good times easily turn bad

### Two Views of Value Added

**Traditional view:**
- Manager takes same risk as benchmark, earns superior return
- Value added = "alpha" through skill

**Alternative (better) view:**
- Manager achieves same return as benchmark while taking less risk
- Value added through reduced risk at given return

**Builder analogy:**
- Good builder avoids construction flaws
- Poor builder incorporates flaws
- When no earthquakes occur, can't tell the difference
- **Warren Buffett's swimmers**: Only when tide goes out do we see who's clothed vs. naked

**Cost of risk control:**
- Usually more good years than bad years
- Bad years needed for risk control value to become evident
- Cost of risk control = return forgone (seems excessive in good years)
- Like prudent homeowners carrying insurance even when there's no fire

> "Controlling the risk in your portfolio is a very important and worthwhile pursuit. The fruits, however, come only in the form of losses that don't happen." —Marks

> "This is the ultimate paradox of risk management." —Paul Johnson

### Bearing Risk Intelligently

- Bearing risk unknowingly = huge mistake
- Intelligent acceptance of recognized risk for profit = wise and profitable

**Life insurance analogy:**
- It's **risk they're aware of** — factor mortality into approach
- It's **risk they can analyze** — have doctors assess applicants' health
- It's **risk they can diversify** — mix policyholders by age, gender, occupation, location
- It's **risk they're well paid to bear** — set premiums to profit if policyholders die per actuarial tables

**Oaktree's approach:**
- Try to be aware of risks
- Employ skilled professionals for analysis
- Diversify portfolios appropriately
- Invest only when convinced likely return far exceeds risk compensation

> "Risky assets can make for good investments if they're cheap enough. The essential element is knowing when that's the case. That's it: the intelligent bearing of risk for profit."

### Risk Bearing vs. Risk Control

- **Risk control** is essential
- **Risk bearing** is neither wise nor unwise per se
- Can be done well or poorly, at right/wrong time

**Careful risk controllers know:**
- They don't know the future
- Future can include negative outcomes
- Principal pitfalls: inability to know "how bad is bad"

### The Danger of Complacency

- Extreme volatility and loss surface infrequently
- As time passes without occurrence, seems more likely it'll never happen
- Becomes tempting to relax rules and increase leverage
- Often done just before risk rears its head

**Nassim Nicholas Taleb** in *Fooled by Randomness*:

> "Reality is far more vicious than Russian roulette. First, it delivers the fatal bullet rather infrequently, like a revolver that would have hundreds, even thousands of chambers instead of six. After a few dozen tries, one forgets about the existence of a bullet, under a numbing false sense of security."

**2004-2007 financial crisis:**
- Institutions played high-risk game thinking it was low-risk
- Assumptions on losses and volatility too low
- But how conservative is conservative enough?

### Worst-Case Thinking and Black Swans

- Can't run business on worst-case assumptions
- A "worst-case assumption" is really a misnomer
- **Quants shouldn't have assumed nationwide home price decline couldn't happen**

**Decision-making under uncertainty:**
- Base decisions on what we think probably will happen
- Base that on what usually happened in past
- Expect results close to norm most of time
- Tend to forget potential for outliers beyond usual range
- Rarely consider outcomes that happened only once a century or never

**Black swans:**
- Once in a while, a "black swan" will materialize
- Always saying "We can't do such-and-such because outcome could be worse" = frozen in inaction
- Should suffice to be prepared for once-in-a-generation events
- But a generation isn't forever
- What to do about that? No easy answer

### Risk Control vs. Risk Avoidance

**Critical distinction:**
- **Risk control** = best route to loss avoidance
- **Risk avoidance** = likely to lead to return avoidance

**Oaktree's stance:**
- Doesn't run from risk
- Welcomes it at right time, in right instances, at right price

**Risk bearing for profit:**
- Could easily avoid all risk
- But assured of avoiding returns above risk-free rate
- **Will Rogers**: "You've got to go out on a limb sometimes because that's where the fruit is."

**Oaktree's first tenet:**
- Stresses "importance of risk control"
- Has nothing to do with risk avoidance
- By bearing risk when well paid to do so, strive to add value for clients

**Long-term success:**
- Road to success runs through risk control more than aggressiveness
- Over full career, most investors' results determined more by how many losers they have and how bad they are
- **Skillful risk control = mark of superior investor**

> "The math behind the compounding of negative returns helps ensure this outcome (e.g., a 40 percent loss in one year requires a return of 67 percent to fully recover)." —Joel Greenblatt

## Chapter 8: Being Attentive to Cycles

> "I think it's essential to remember that just about everything is cyclical. Cycles always prevail eventually. Nothing goes in one direction forever. Trees don't grow to the sky. Few things go to zero."

### Cyclicality as Fundamental Truth

**November 2001 memo** — "You Can't Predict. You Can Prepare":
- Never know what lies ahead, but can prepare for possibilities
- Very few sure things in investing

**Two concepts to hold with confidence:**
- **Rule number one**: most things will prove to be cyclical
- **Rule number two**: some of greatest opportunities for gain/loss come when others forget rule number one

**Nature of cycles:**
- Very few things move in straight line
- Progress and deterioration alternate
- **Underlying principle**: things will wax and wane, grow and decline

### Why Cycles Exist: Human Involvement

- **Mechanical things** can go in straight line if adequately powered
- **Human-involved processes** produce variable, cyclical results
- **People are emotional and inconsistent**, not steady and clinical

**Investor psychology's impact:**
- When people feel good about future, behavior strongly impacted
- Spend more, save less, borrow to increase profit potential
- All capable of reversing in a second

**Cycles are self-correcting:**
- Reversal not necessarily dependent on exogenous events
- Reverse because trends create reasons for own reversal
- **"Success carries within itself the seeds of failure, and failure the seeds of success"**

### The Credit Cycle

> "The credit cycle deserves a very special mention for its inevitability, extreme volatility and ability to create opportunities for investors attuned to it. Of all the cycles, it's my favorite."

**Power of credit cycle:**
- Small fluctuation in economy produces large fluctuation in credit availability
- Great impact on asset prices

**Credit cycle process:**

**Up-leg (prosperity phase):**
- Economy moves into period of prosperity
- Providers of capital thrive
- Risk averseness disappears
- Financial institutions compete by lowering returns, lowering credit standards, easing covenants

**At extreme:**
- Providers finance borrowers and projects not worthy of financing
- **The Economist**: "The worst loans are made at the best of times"

**Capital destruction:**
- Investment where cost of capital exceeds return *on* capital
- Eventually cases where no return *of* capital

**Down-leg (contraction phase):**
- Losses cause lenders to become discouraged
- Risk averseness rises, along with interest rates, credit restrictions
- Less capital made available
- Companies starved for capital → defaults and bankruptcies

**Ready to reverse again:**
- Because competition to make loans is low, high returns can be demanded

> "Again, you get higher return and lower risk." —Christopher Davis

**Contrarians' opportunity:**
- Those who commit capital at this point get high returns
- Tempting returns draw in capital → recovery fueled

**Historical examples of loose credit bubbles:**
- Real estate 1989-1992
- Emerging markets 1994-1998
- Long-Term Capital Management 1998
- Venture capital funds and telecommunications 2000-2001
- **Field of Dreams**: "If you build it, they will come"
- Financial world: if you offer cheap money, they will borrow, buy and build — often without discipline

> "Understanding that cycles are eventually self-correcting is one way to maintain some optimism when bargain hunting after large market drops." —Joel Greenblatt

### Cycles Never Stop

- Cycles will never stop occurring
- If completely efficient market existed and people made decisions unemotionally, perhaps cycles would be banished
- But that'll never be the case

**Economic fluctuations:**
- Economies wax and wane as consumers spend more/less
- Companies overexpand facilities during up cycle → burdensome when economy turns down
- Providers of capital too generous when economy doing well, pull reins too tight when things cease to look good

**Investor behavior:**
- Investors overvalue companies when doing well, undervalue when things get difficult

## Chapter 9: The Most Important Thing Is... Awareness of the Pendulum

### The Investor Pendulum

- Second investor memo (1991) devoted to **pendulum-like oscillation of investor attitudes and behavior**
- Securities markets mood swings resemble pendulum movement
- Midpoint describes average location, but **pendulum spends very little time there**
- Instead, it's almost always **swinging toward or away from the extremes**
- When pendulum is near either extreme, it will inevitably **move back toward midpoint**

> "Chapter 9 is essentially an extension of the three earlier chapters on risk." —Paul Johnson

### Pendulum Swings Between:

- **Euphoria and depression**
- **Celebrating positive developments and obsessing over negatives**
- **Overpriced and underpriced**

Also swings regarding: **greed vs fear**; **optimistic vs pessimistic lens**; **credulousness vs skepticism**; **risk tolerance vs risk aversion**

### Risk Aversion and the Pendulum

- **Risk aversion is essential ingredient in rational market**—position of pendulum regarding risk is particularly important
- Improper amounts of risk aversion are **key contributors to market excesses**
- Inevitable hallmark of bubbles: **dearth of risk aversion**; in crashes, investors **fear too much**

### The Greed/Fear Cycle

- Greed/fear cycle is **caused by changing attitudes toward risk**
- When **greed** is prevalent: investors feel high comfort with risk
- When **fear** is widespread: indicates high aversion to risk
- Academics consider investors' attitude toward risk a **constant**; certainly **it fluctuates greatly**

### Finance Theory vs Reality

- Finance theory dependent on assumption that investors are **risk-averse**
- Must be **induced (bribed)** to bear risk with higher expected returns
- **Reaping dependably high returns from risky investments is an oxymoron**
- But there are times when this caveat is **ignored**—when people get **too comfortable with risk**

> "Marks links risk and security prices/valuation directly, something that is not done in general finance theory but is exceedingly important for investors to understand." —Paul Johnson

### Two Main Risks in Investing

- **Risk of losing money** and **risk of missing opportunity**
- Possible to largely eliminate either one, **but not both**
- From time to time, at extremes of pendulum's swing, **one or the other predominates**

### 2005-2007 vs 2007-2008 Example

**2005-2007 (Risk of Missing Opportunity)**:
- Things going swimmingly
- Many believed risk had been **banished**
- Only worry: might **miss an opportunity**
- They weren't concerned about losing money
- They **behaved too aggressively**

**Late 2007-2008 (Risk of Losing Money)**:
- Credit crisis in full flower
- No one worried about missing opportunity
- People worried **only about losing money**
- Ran to **safety of government securities** with yields near zero
- Investors feared too much, positioned portfolios **too defensively**

> "These two bullets provide an excellent recap of the many mistakes investors made in the years leading up to the 2008 financial crisis." —Paul Johnson

### The Pattern Repeats

> When things are going well and prices are high, investors rush to buy, forgetting all prudence. Then, when there's chaos all around and assets are on the bargain counter, they lose all willingness to bear risk and rush to sell. And it will ever be so.

### Three Stages of a Bull Market

1. **First stage**: when a few forward-looking people begin to believe things will get better
2. **Second stage**: when most investors realize improvement is actually taking place
3. **Third stage**: when everyone concludes things will get better forever

> "Why would anyone waste time trying for a better description? This one says it all."

- Changes in **valuation parameters** caused primarily by **changes in investor psychology** account for most **short-term changes in security prices**

> "Stocks are cheapest when everything looks grim."

> "It is hard for the average investor to commit capital when the outlook is gloomy. Yet it is precisely in these moments that potential returns are at their highest." —Paul Johnson

### Market Psychology Progression

**Early Stage (Depression/Pessimism)**:
- Depressing outlook keeps investors away
- Only a few **astute and daring bargain hunters** willing to take new positions
- Market starts moving up

**Middle Stage (Improvement)**:
- Outlook seems less poor
- People begin to appreciate **improvement is taking place**
- Requires **less imagination to be a buyer**
- They pay prices **more reflective of stocks' fair values**

**Late Stage (Euphoria)**:
- **Dizziness sets in**
- People become willing to **extrapolate improvement**
- Masses become **excited (and envious)** about profits made by early investors
- They **ignore the cyclical nature** of things

> "What the wise man does in the beginning, the fool does in the end."

- People become willing to **pay prices that assume the good times will go on ad infinitum**

> "The ultimate danger zone is reached when investors are in agreement that things can only get better forever. That makes no sense, but most people fall for it. It's what creates bubbles—just as the opposite produces crashes." —Howard Marks

### Three Stages of a Bear Market

1. **First stage**: when just a few thoughtful investors recognize that things won't always be rosy
2. **Second stage**: when most investors recognize things are deteriorating
3. **Third stage**: when everyone's convinced things can only get worse

**Prediction (March 2008)**:
- One of these days, we'll reach **third stage**, and herd will give up on there being a solution
- Likely to encounter **investment opportunities of a lifetime**
- **Major bottoms occur when everyone forgets that tide also comes in**
- Those are the times we live for

### The 2008 Crisis Validated This

- Six months later, progression had gone **all the way to third stage**
- Full meltdown of world financial system considered possible
- First steps: bankruptcy of **Lehman Brothers**, absorption/rescue of **Bear Stearns**, **Merrill Lynch**, **AIG**, **Fannie Mae**, **Freddie Mac**
- Since this was **biggest crisis ever**, investors bought into third stage **more than ever before**
- In many asset classes, **price declines in 2008**, **resultant investment opportunities**, **gains in 2009**—were the **greatest I've ever seen**

### Opportunity at Extremes

- At one extreme—**darkest of times**—takes **analytical ability**, **objectivity**, **resolve**, **imagination** to think things will ever get better
- Few people who possess those qualities can make **unusual profits with low risk**
- At other extreme: stage is set for **painful losses**

### Pendulum vs Cycles

- Oscillation of investor pendulum very similar to **up-and-down fluctuation of economic and market cycles**
- Marks makes distinction between two, but **they're both highly important**, and **key lessons are the same**

### Key Observations About the Pendulum

**1. Pendulum Should Reside at Midpoint, But Doesn't**
- In theory, pendulum should reside mostly at **midpoint between extremes**
- **But it doesn't for long**

> "Markets will always create opportunities. In markets with few opportunities, it's important to be patient. Value opportunities will eventually present themselves." —Joel Greenblatt

**2. Usually Swinging Toward/From One Extreme**
- Primarily because of workings of **investor psychology**

**3. Cannot Stay at Extreme Forever**
- Pendulum cannot continue to swing toward an extreme forever
- Although when positioned at **greatest extreme**, people increasingly describe that as **permanent condition**

**4. Pent-Up Energy Causes Swing Back**
- Like a pendulum, swing toward extreme causes **energy to build up**
- Sometimes, **pent-up energy is itself the cause** of the swing back

**5. Swing Back is Faster**
- Swing back from extreme is usually **more rapid**—takes much less time than swing to the extreme
- **Sheldon Stone**: "The air goes out of the balloon much faster than it went in"
The occurrence of this pendulum-like pattern in most market phenomena is **extremely dependable**. But we never know:
- **How far** the pendulum will swing in its arc
- **What might cause** the swing to stop and turn back
- **When** this reversal will occur
- **How far** it will then swing in opposite direction

**Requirements for Bullish Phase to Hold**:
- Environment characterized by: **greed**, **optimism**, **exuberance**, **confidence**, **credulity**, **daring**, **risk tolerance**, **aggressiveness**
- But these traits **will not govern a market forever**
- Eventually they give way to: **fear**, **pessimism**, **prudence**, **uncertainty**, **skepticism**, **caution**, **risk aversion**, **reticence**

> Busts are the product of booms, and I'm convinced it's usually more correct to attribute a bust to the excesses of the preceding boom than to the specific event that sets off the correction.

### The Certainty of Reversal

> There are a few things of which we can be sure, and this is one: Extreme market behavior will reverse. Those who believe that the pendulum will move in one direction forever—or reside at an extreme forever—eventually will lose huge sums. Those who understand the pendulum's behavior can benefit enormously.

## Chapter 10: Combating Negative Influences (continued)

### The Tech Bubble as Case Study

- Marks wrote "Bubble.com" memo January 3, 2000, warning that tech/telecom stocks had revolutionary potential but faced impossible-to-predict outcomes
- Standing in front of the tech boom was "comparable to standing in front of a freight train"
- Soon after, tech stocks collapsed under their own weight without any specific trigger event
- When the bubble burst, losses exceeded 99% for many stocks (vs. typical 90% losses in other busts)
- Broad indices experienced first three-year decline since the Great Depression

> To say technology, Internet and telecommunications are too high and about to decline is comparable today to standing in front of a freight train. To say they have benefited from a boom of colossal proportions and should be examined skeptically is something I feel I owe you.
> — "Bubble.com," January 3, 2000

### What Investors Ignored

- In retrospect, tech developments did change the world, but investors suspended common sense
- Ignored reality that not all companies could win
- Ignored that there would be a lengthy shake-out period
- Ignored that profitability wouldn't come easily from free services
- Ignored that money-losing companies valued at high multiples carried great danger
- **Joel Greenblatt** quote: **Buffett** distinguished between aviation's value as innovation vs. as business investment — would have "shot down Wilbur in Kitty Hawk" given industry's capital-destroying history

### Psychological Forces in Bubbles

- **Greed, excitement, illogicality, suspension of disbelief** cost people fortunes in tech bubble
- Disciplined value investors looked foolish months and years before bubble burst
- **Howard Marks**: "Positive feelings—sometimes called animal spirits—are major contributors to asset overpricing"
- Disciplined investors initially look like "pessimists, grumps, or old fogeys" until proven right

### Keys to Avoiding Bubbles

- Refuse to join when greed drives positives to be wildly overrated and negatives ignored
- In 2008, failing to buy in bull market risked opportunity cost, but failing to sell risked unlimited loss and "Armageddon"
- **Howard Marks**: "Emotion and ego: Refusing to join in the errors of the herd—like so much else in investing—requires strength of mind and ego"
- Mastery over psychology isn't sufficient alone, but combining it with analytical proficiency produces great results

### The Difficulty of Resisting

- Hard to resist buying at top when everyone's buying, pundits positive, rationale widely accepted, prices soaring, risk-takers reporting huge returns
- Equally hard to resist selling (and tough to buy) when opposite is true and holding appears to risk total loss
- **Howard Marks**: "Fear of looking wrong: Remember, you're not going to be wrong in a vacuum. Assets go too far because of the actions of others"
- Comparing your results to others' is a "very corrosive process—albeit natural"

### No Simple Solution

- No formula tells you when market has reached irrational extreme
- No foolproof tool keeps you on right side of decisions
- No magic pill protects against destructive emotions
- **Charlie Munger**: "It's not supposed to be easy"

### Oaktree's Defensive Weapons

- **Strongly held sense of intrinsic value**
  - **Joel Greenblatt**: Without this, investor has no home base; cannot withstand psychological influences; those who can't value have no business investing beyond luck
- **Insistence on acting when price diverges from value**
- **Conversance with past cycles**—gained through reading/talking to veterans first, then through experience
- **Understanding that market excesses are ultimately punished, not rewarded**
- **Thorough understanding of psychology's insidious effect at market extremes**
- **Promise to remember that when things seem "too good to be true," they usually are**
- **Willingness to look wrong** while market goes from misvalued to more misvalued
- **Like-minded friends and colleagues** for mutual support

These don't guarantee success but give a fighting chance.

## Chapter 11: The Most Important Thing Is... Contrarianism

> To buy when others are despondently selling and to sell when others are euphorically buying takes the greatest courage, but provides the greatest profit.
> — **Sir John Templeton**

### The Core Principle

- Most investors are **trend followers**
- Superior investors are the opposite—require **second-level thinking**
- By definition, most of the crowd can't share superior judgments
- The trend and consensus view are things to game against, not follow
- Consensus portfolio is one to diverge from
- As pendulum swings through cycles, key to success lies in doing the opposite
- **Warren Buffett**: "The less prudence with which others conduct their affairs, the greater the prudence with which we should conduct our own affairs"
- **Paul Johnson**: "Contrarianism is an important skill for successful value investors. However, the most important ingredient to developing this skill is experience."

### The Logic of Crowd Error

- Markets swing dramatically between bullish/bearish, overpriced/underpriced
- Movements driven by "the crowd," "the herd," or "most people"
- Bull markets occur because more people want to buy than sell, or buyers more motivated than sellers
- Market rises as people switch from sellers to buyers, and buyers become more motivated while sellers less so

### Market Extremes as Inflection Points

- Extremes represent inflection points when bullishness/bearishness reaches maximum
- At top, last person who will become a buyer does so
- Since every buyer has joined by the time top is reached, bullishness can go no further
- Since there's no one left to turn bullish, market stops rising
- If one person switches from buyer to seller next day, market starts declining
- **At extremes, most people are wrong**
- Therefore, key to success: diverging from the crowd and profiting through contrarianism

### The Difficulty of Contrarianism

- From time to time: rabid buyers or terrified sellers; urgency to get in or out; overheated or ice-cold markets; unsustainably high or ridiculously low prices
- Markets and investor attitudes spend only small portion of time at "the happy medium"
- Joining herd in these cycles is dangerous
- Extreme highs created when avid buyers control, pushing prices to unsustainable levels
- Lows created when panicky sellers predominate, parting with assets at grossly inadequate prices
- "Buy low; sell high" is time-honored dictum, but investors swept up in cycles do the opposite
- Proper contrarian response: buy when they hate 'em, sell when they love 'em
- **"Once-in-a-lifetime" market extremes** occur once a decade or so—not often enough to build career around, but should be important component of approach
- **Joel Greenblatt**: "I love this thought. Extreme circumstances occur more often than seems reasonable. You never catch the bottom or the top, and that's where the pain and degree of difficulty come in!"

### Requirements for Successful Contrarianism

- Ability to detect instances where prices diverged significantly from intrinsic value
- Strong stomach to defy conventional wisdom
- Resist myth that market is always efficient and thus right
- Need experience on which to base resolute behavior
- Must have support of understanding, patient constituencies
- Without time to ride out extremes while waiting for reason to prevail, you become "six-foot-tall man who drowned crossing stream that was five feet deep on average"
- **Joel Greenblatt**: "One of Buffett's and Marks's greatest concepts. In the long run, the market gets it right. But you have to survive over the short run, to get to the long run."

### Recognizing Pendulum Swings

- Alert investors can recognize opportunities from pendulum-like market swings
- Accepting broad concept is one thing; putting into practice is another
- We never know how far pendulum will swing, when it will reverse, and how far it will go in opposite direction
- But once it reaches extreme position, market eventually will swing back toward midpoint (or beyond)
- Investors believing pendulum would move in one direction forever—or stay at extreme—are inevitably disappointed
- Because of market variability, no tool (not even contrarianism) can be relied on completely

### Limitations of Contrarianism

- **Not an approach that will make you money all the time**—much of the time there aren't great excesses to bet against
- **Joel Greenblatt**: "I've put it this way: just because no one else will jump in front of a Mack truck barreling down the highway doesn't mean that you should!"
- Even when excess develops, "overpriced" is different from "going down tomorrow"
- Markets can be over/underpriced and stay that way—or become more so—for years
- **Can be extremely painful when trend goes against you**
- **Seth Klarman**: Important to remember **Graham and Dodd** teachings—if you look to markets for report card, owning declining stock will make you feel like failure. But if you remember you own fractional interest in business and can buy at greater discount to value daily, you might maintain cheerful disposition (example: **Warren Buffett** bargain hunting in 1973-74 bear market)
- **Contrarianism itself can appear too popular**, making it seem like herd behavior
- **Not enough to bet against the crowd**—given difficulties mentioned, potentially profitable recognition of divergences must be based on reason and analysis
- Must do things because you know why crowd is wrong
- Only then can you hold firmly to views and buy more as positions appear mistaken and losses accrue rather than gains

### David Swensen and Yale Model

- **David Swensen** heads **Yale University endowment**
- Yale's performance outstanding; Swensen had greater impact on endowment investing than anyone over last two decades
- His thinking highly unusual when Yale began implementing in 1980s, came to represent endowment canon

> Investment success requires sticking with positions made uncomfortable by their variance with popular opinion. Casual commitments invite casual reversal, exposing portfolio managers to the damaging whipsaw of buying high and selling low. Only with the confidence created by a strong decision-making process can investors sell speculative excess and buy despair-driven value.

> Active management strategies demand uninstitutional behavior from institutions, creating a paradox that few can unravel. Establishing and maintaining an unconventional investment profile requires acceptance of uncomfortably idiosyncratic portfolios, which frequently appear downright imprudent in the eyes of conventional wisdom.
> — **Pioneering Portfolio Management**, 2000

### The Ultimate Contrarian Principle

- Most profitable actions by definition are contrarian: buying when everyone else selling (price low) or selling when everyone else buying (price high)
- These actions lonely and uncomfortable
- How can we know it's the opposite (consensus action) that's the comfortable one? Because most people are doing it
- **Howard Marks**: "Fear of looking wrong: The very words used here—uninstitutional, idiosyncratic, imprudent, lonely, and uncomfortable—provide an idea of how challenging it is to maintain nonconsensus positions. But doing so is an absolute must if superior performance is to be achieved."

### What's Obvious Is Usually Wrong

- Most interesting aspect of investing: how paradoxical it is
- Things that seem most obvious—on which everyone agrees—turn out not to be true
- Reality simpler and more systematic: most people don't understand the moneymaking process
- **What's clear to broad consensus of investors is almost always wrong**
- Coalescing of popular opinion behind investment tends to eliminate profit potential
- Investment that "everyone" believes to be great idea cannot be so by definition

### Why "Everyone Likes It" Means Trouble

- If everyone likes it, probably because it has been doing well
- Most think outstanding past performance presages outstanding future performance
- Actually, more likely that outstanding past performance has borrowed from future, presaging **subpar performance** going forward
- **Joel Greenblatt**: "This is extremely simple and extremely insightful"
- If everyone likes it, price likely risen to reflect adulation—little further appreciation likely
- If everyone likes it, area likely mined too thoroughly, too much capital flow in, few bargains remain
- If everyone likes it, significant risk prices will fall if crowd changes collective mind and exits

### Superior Investors Buy What Others Don't See

- Superior investors know—and buy—when price of something is lower than it should be
- Price can be lower than should be only when most people don't see its merit
- **Yogi Berra**: "Nobody goes to that restaurant anymore; it's too crowded"
- Just as nonsensical: "Everyone realizes that investment's a bargain"
- If everyone realizes it, they'll buy, price no longer low
- **Large amounts of money aren't made by buying what everybody likes. They're made by buying what everybody underestimates**

### Two Elements of Superior Investing

- Seeing some quality others don't see or appreciate (not reflected in price), and
- Having it turn out to be true (or at least accepted by market)

- Process begins with investors who are unusually perceptive, unconventional, iconoclastic or early
- Successful investors spend a lot of time being lonely

## Chapter 12: Finding Bargains (continued)

### The Bond Market Evolution

- In the 1990s-2000s, little money flowed to **high grade bonds** due to the **Greenspan Fed's** low interest rate policy
- After prolonged neglect and reduced holdings, bonds became positioned as superior performers when environment shifted toward valuing safety over upside
- Pattern repeats regularly: investors recognize attractions after appreciation, realize they don't own enough, creating profits for early movers

### Characteristics of Underpriced Assets

**Shopping list for bargains:**
- Little known and not fully understood
- Fundamentally questionable on the surface
- Controversial, unseemly, or scary
- Deemed inappropriate for "respectable" portfolios
- Unappreciated, unpopular, and unloved
- Trailing a record of poor returns
- Recently the subject of disinvestment, not accumulation

> "This is an excellent 'shopping list.'" — **Paul Johnson**

### The Core Principle of Bargains

**Necessary condition for bargains:** perception must be considerably worse than reality
- Best opportunities found among things most others won't do
- If everyone feels good about something and is glad to join in, it won't be bargain-priced

### Marks's Early Career Examples

**Convertible securities (1978):**
- Small, underappreciated market backwater
- Issued as last resort by weak companies lacking alternatives
- Mainstream investors felt they introduced unnecessary complexity
- Seen as "defensive hybrid vehicle" with no merit
- 1984 *BusinessWeek* article quoted: "Real men don't buy converts, so chickens like me can buy cheap"

**High yield bonds (late 1978):**
- Low-rated securities burdened with "junk bonds" sobriquet
- Fell short of institutions' "investment grade or better" requirements
- Rating agency described B-rated bonds as "generally lacking the characteristics of a desirable investment"
- The dismissal ignored price entirely

> "More broadly, this is the problem with all agency ratings. The supposed safety attracts investors who fail to do their own homework, making them the ultimate buyers of conventional wisdom." — **Seth Klarman**

### Historical Performance of Unloved Assets

- If nobody owns something, demand (and price) can only go up
- By going from taboo to tolerated, assets can perform quite well
- In 1987, **Bruce Karsh** and **Sheldon Stone** proposed forming fund to invest in **distressed debt**
- The idea: investing in bonds of bankrupt or near-bankrupt companies—could anything be more unseemly and less respectable?
- Whatever asset is considered worst at a given point in time has good likelihood of being cheapest

### Quality vs. Price

- Investment bargains needn't have anything to do with high quality
- Things tend to be cheaper if low quality has scared people away
- Each of these asset classes (converts, high yield, distressed) satisfied most criteria: little known, not understood, not respected, uncomfortably idiosyncratic, imprudent-appearing
- Each turned into great places to be for next twenty or thirty years

## Chapter 13: Patient Opportunism

> "The market's not a very accommodating machine; it won't provide high returns just because you need them." — **Peter Bernstein**

### The Nature of Opportunity

- 2005-2007 boom-bust cycle gave chances to sell at elevated levels and buy at panic prices in late 2007-2008—a "chance of a lifetime"
- There aren't always great things to do
- Sometimes we maximize contribution by being discerning and relatively inactive
- **Patient opportunism**—waiting for bargains—is often your best strategy

> "Marks offers great advice in this chapter. The challenge is that many investors confuse action for adding value when, in fact, all of the studies suggest that most investors overtrade their portfolio." — **Paul Johnson**

### The Oaktree Approach

**Core principle:** "We don't look for our investments; they find us"
- Try to sit on hands, don't go out with a "buy list"
- Wait for the phone to ring
- If we call the owner and say "you own X and we want to buy it," price goes up
- If owner calls and says "We're stuck with X and looking for an exit," price goes down
- Rather than initiating transactions, prefer to react opportunistically

### Market Conditions and Action

- Investment environment is a given; we must accept it and invest within it
- Not always a pendulum or cycle extreme to bet against
- Sometimes greed/fear, optimism/pessimism, credulousness/skepticism are balanced—no clear mistakes being made
- Most things may seem roughly fairly priced
- In that case, may not be great bargains to buy or compelling sales to make

> "This is one of the hardest things to master for professional investors: coming in each day for work and doing nothing." — **Joel Greenblatt**

### Philosophical Foundation: Mujo

**Japanese cultural concept learned at Wharton:**
- **Mujo** defined as recognition of "the turning of the wheel of the law"
- Implies acceptance of inevitability of change, of rise and fall
- Cycles will rise and fall, things will come and go
- Environment will change in ways beyond our control
- We must recognize, accept, cope, and respond

> "What's past is past and can't be undone. It has led to the circumstances we now face. All we can do is recognize our circumstances for what they are and make the best decisions we can, given the givens." — *"It Is What It Is,"* March 27, 2006

### Warren Buffett's Baseball Analogy

- **Warren Buffett's** philosophy is less spiritually based than mujo—his reference is to **baseball**
- **Berkshire Hathaway 1997 Annual Report:** discussed **Ted Williams** (the "Splendid Splinter"), one of the greatest hitters in history
- Williams conducted intensive study of his own game
- Broke strike zone into 77 baseball-sized "cells" and charted results at the plate
- Learned batting average was much better when he went after only pitches in his "sweet spot"
- Of course, he couldn't wait all day for perfect pitch—if he let three strikes go by without swinging, he'd be called out

**November 1, 1974 *Forbes* article:**
- Buffett pointed out investors have advantage in that regard
- Because they can't strike out looking, investors needn't feel pressured to act
- Can pass up lots of opportunities until they see one that's terrific

> "Investing is the greatest business in the world because you never have to swing. You stand at the plate; the pitcher throws you General Motors at 47! U.S. Steel at 39! And nobody calls a strike on you. There's no penalty except opportunity. All day you wait for the pitch you like; then, when the fielders are asleep, you step up and hit it." — *"What's Your Game Plan?"* September 5, 2003

### The Penalty Structure

- Only real penalty is for making losing investments
- No penalty for omitting losing investments
- Of course, just rewards
- Even for missing a few winners, penalty is bearable

> "Still, calibration is important. Set the bar too high and you might remain out of the market for a very long time." — **Seth Klarman**

### Professional Money Manager Pressures

- For professional investors paid to manage others' money, stakes are higher
- If they miss too many opportunities and returns are too low in good times, money managers can come under pressure from clients and eventually lose accounts
- A lot depends on how clients have been conditioned

**Oaktree's belief:** missing a profitable opportunity is of less significance than investing in a loser
- Clients are prepared for results that put risk control ahead of full participation in gains

### Buffett's Version of Patient Opportunism

- Standing at the plate with bat on shoulders
- Bat should come off shoulders when there are opportunities for profit with controlled risk, but only then
- One way to be selective: making every effort to ascertain whether we're in low-return environment or high-return environment

### The Cat, the Tree, the Carrot, and the Stick

**Allegory for low-return environments:**
- Cat = investor whose job is to cope with investment environment (the tree)
- Carrot = incentive to accept increased risk (higher returns seemingly available from riskier investments)
- Stick = motivation to forsake safety (modest level of prospective returns on safer investments)
- Carrot lures cat to higher branches—riskier strategies in pursuit of dinner (targeted return)
- Stick prods cat up tree because it can't get dinner while staying close to ground
- Together, stick and carrot can cause cat to climb until it arrives high up in tree, in treacherous position
- Critical observation: cat pursues high returns even in low-return environment and bears consequences (increased risk) although often unknowingly

### "Reaching for Yield" Pattern

**Bond investors' behavior:**
- Classical pattern: investing in riskier credits as yields on safer ones decline
- Taking new and bigger risks to access returns investors were accustomed to before market rose
- Same pattern repeats cyclically
- Motto of those who reach for return: "If you can't get the return you need from safe investments, pursue it via risky investments"

**Pre-credit crisis example:**
- Investors succumbed to **siren song of leverage**
- Borrowed cheap short-term funds, used money to buy assets that offered higher returns due to illiquidity and/or fundamental risk
- Institutional investors worldwide took Wall Street up on newest promises of "silver bullets": **securitization** and **structure**
- On surface, investments made sense: promised satisfactory absolute returns
- Results would be great... as long as nothing untoward happened

> "But, as usual, the pursuit of profit led to mistakes. The expected returns looked good, but the range of possible outcomes included some very nasty ones." — *"No Different This Time,"* December 17, 2007

### The Fate of Aggressive Competitors

- Many leading competitors from Oaktree's early years are no longer leading (or competitors at all)
- While some faltered due to flaws in organization or business model, others disappeared because they insisted on pursuing high returns in low-return environments
- You cannot create investment opportunities when they're not there
- Dumbest thing: insist on perpetuating high returns and give back profits in the process
- If it's not there, hoping won't make it so
- When prices are high, inescapable that prospective returns are low (and risks are high)

### Coping with Low-Return Environments

**In 2004, Marks wrote memo "Risk and Return Today":**
- Expressed view that capital market line then was "low and flat"
- Prospective returns in almost all markets among lowest ever seen
- Risk premiums were narrowest
- If prospective returns should rise, likely through price declines

**Several weeks later, suggested possibilities for coping:**

- **Invest as if it's not true** — wishing won't make it so. **Peter Bernstein** response: "The market's not a very accommodating machine; it won't provide high returns just because you need them."

- **Invest anyway** — trying for acceptable relative returns under the circumstances, even if not attractive in absolute

- **Invest anyway** — ignoring short-run risk and focusing on long run; suggest getting commitment from investment committee that they'll ignore short-term losses

- **Hold cash** — tough for people who need to meet actuarial assumption or spending rate

> "In recent years, holding cash is so completely out of favor that it has become the ultimate contrarian investment." — **Seth Klarman**

- **Concentrate your investments in "special niches and special people"** — gets harder as portfolio size grows; identifying managers with truly superior talent isn't easy

### The Classic Mistake: Reaching for Return

**The truth:** no easy answer for investors faced with skimpy prospective returns and risk premiums

**But one classic mistake Marks most strongly feels is wrong:** reaching for return

**Given today's paucity of prospective return at low-risk end:**
- Many investors moving capital to riskier investments
- But: (a) they're making those riskier investments just when prospective returns on those investments are lowest they've ever been
- (b) accepting return increments for stepping up in risk that are as slim as they've ever been
- (c) signing up today for things they turned down in past when prospective returns were much higher
- May be exactly wrong time to add to risk in pursuit of more return
- Want to take risk when others fleeing from it, not when competing to do so

> "There They Go Again," May 6, 2005

> "This highlights one of the pitfalls of investing with a return requirement, as corporate pension funds are forced to do." — **Seth Klarman**

### Timing Considerations

- Written too early: May 2005 wasn't perfect time to get off merry-go-round; May 2007 was
- Being early provided good reminder about pain involved in being too far ahead of your time
- Having said that, much better to get off too soon in May 2005 than to stay on past May 2007

### Requirements for High Returns in Low-Return Environment

To wring high returns from low-return environment requires:
- Ability to swim against tide
- Find relatively few winners
- Must be based on some combination of exceptional skill, high risk bearing, and good luck

**High-return environments offer different opportunities:**
- Offer opportunities for generous returns through purchases at low prices
- Typically these can be earned with low risk
- In crises of 1990, 2002, 2008, Oaktree funds earned unusually high returns through investments where loss was unlikely

### The Nature of Forced Sellers

- Absolute best buying opportunities come when asset holders are forced to sell
- In those crises, forced sellers present in large numbers
- Holders become forced sellers for reasons like:
  - Funds they manage experience withdrawals
  - Portfolio holdings violate investment guidelines
  - Receive margin calls

> "Interesting thought: investors should work to never put themselves in a position to be a forced seller." — **Paul Johnson**

### The Goal of Active Investment Management

- Real goal: to buy things for less than they're worth
- What **efficient market hypothesis** says we can't do
- Theory's objection seems reasonable: why should someone part with something at bargain price, especially if potential seller is informed and rational?

**Beauty of forced sellers:**
- They have no choice
- Have gun at their heads
- Have to sell regardless of price
- Those last three words—**regardless of price**—are most beautiful in the world if you're on the other side of the transaction

### The 2008 Crisis Example: Senior Bank Loans

- If single holder forced to sell, dozens of buyers will accommodate—trade may take place at only slightly reduced price
- But if chaos is widespread, many forced to sell at same time, few in position to provide required liquidity

**Fourth quarter 2008 example:**
- Focus on **leveraged investment entities'** holdings of **senior bank loans**
- These loans highly rated; credit freely available during years leading up to crisis
- Easy to borrow large sums to lever debt portfolios, magnifying potential returns
- Typical investor on "margin" might have posted additional capital if price of collateral fell below 85 cents on dollar
- Secure in knowledge that in past, loans like these had never traded much below "par" (100 cents on dollar)

**When credit crisis hit:**
- Everything went wrong for leveraged investors in bank loans
- Loan prices fell
- Liquidity dried up
- Since much buying had been done with borrowed funds, credit market contraction affected large numbers of holders
- As number of would-be sellers exploded, buyers for cash disappeared

**The cascade:**
- Prices fell to 95, then 90, then 85
- As each portfolio reached "trigger," bank issued margin call or demand for capital infusion
- Few investors had resources and nerve required to add capital in that environment
- Banks took over portfolios and liquidated them
- **BWIC** ("bee-wick") came into common use—acronym for "bid wanted in competition"
- Investors informed of BWIC in afternoon; told bids wanted for auction next morning
- Few possible buyers bid low, hoping to get real bargains
- Banks weren't concerned with getting fair prices; all they needed was enough proceeds to cover loans (perhaps 75-80 cents on dollar)
- BWICs took place at incredibly low prices

**Ultimate results:**
- Loan prices eventually fell into 60s
- Every holder on short-term credit who couldn't access additional capital likely wiped out
- Selling prices were ridiculous
- Declines on senior loan indices in 2008 exceeded those on subordinated high yield bond indices
- Could buy first lien debt at prices from which you'd break even if issuing company turned out to be worth 20-40% of what buyout fund had paid just a year or two earlier
- Promised yields were very large
- In fact, much of this paper appreciated dramatically in 2009

### Keys to Success in Crisis

- Time for patient opportunist to step forward
- Primarily those who had been cognizant of risks in 2006-2007 and kept powder dry—waiting for opportunity—were able to do so
- Key during crisis: be (a) insulated from forces that require selling and (b) positioned to be buyer instead

> "Although extremely challenging to follow, this is excellent advice." — **Paul Johnson**

### Requirements for Crisis Investing

To satisfy criteria, investor needs:
- Staunch reliance on value
- Little or no use of leverage
- Long-term capital
- Strong stomach
- **Patient opportunism**
- Buttressed by contrarian attitude and strong balance sheet
- Can yield amazing profits during meltdowns

## Chapter 14: Knowing What You Don't Know

> "We have two classes of forecasters: Those who don't know—and those who don't know they don't know." — **John Kenneth Galbraith**

> "It's frightening to think that you might not know something, but more frightening to think that, by and large, the world is run by people who have faith that they know exactly what's going on." — **Amos Tversky**

> "There are two kinds of people who lose money: those who know nothing and those who know everything." — **Henry Kaufman**

### Awareness of Limited Knowledge

- Three quotes chosen to lead off chapter
- Awareness of limited extent of foreknowledge is essential component of Marks's approach to investing
- Firmly convinced: (a) it's hard to know what macro future holds and (b) few possess superior knowledge of these matters that can regularly be turned into investing advantage
- Two caveats:
  - More we concentrate on smaller-picture things, more it's possible to gain knowledge advantage
  - Exception comes in suggestion that investors should try to "know the knowable"

### The Impossibility of Proving Unknowability

- Not going to try to prove contention that future is unknowable
- Can't prove a negative, and that certainly includes this one
- Haven't met anyone who consistently knows what lies ahead macro-wise
- Of all economists and strategists you follow, are any correct most of the time?

> "I can sum up in the chapter title following: Be very careful with your own forecasts and even more careful with those of others!" — **Paul Johnson**

### Research on Forecasting Accuracy

**"Research" on forecasting (limited and anecdotal):**
- Consisted primarily of reading forecasts and observing their lack of utility
- Wrote two memos: "The Value of Predictions, or Where'd All This Rain Come From?" (1993) and "The Value of Predictions II, or Give That Man a Cigar" (1996)
- In second memo, used data from three semiannual *Wall Street Journal* economic polls to examine usefulness of forecasts

**First question: Were forecasts generally accurate?**
- Answer: clearly no
- On average, predictions for ninety-day T-bill rate, thirty-year bond rate, and yen/dollar exchange rate six and twelve months out were off by 15%
- Average forecaster missed interest rate on long bond six months later by 96 basis points

**Second question: Were forecasts valuable?**
- Predictions most useful when they correctly anticipate change
- In *Journal* polls, forecasters completely missed several major changes: interest rate increases of 1994 and 1996, rate decline of 1995, massive gyrations of dollar/yen relationship
- In summary, simply wasn't much correlation between predicted changes and actual changes

**Third question: What was source of forecasts?**
- Answer: most forecasts consisted of extrapolations
- On average, predictions within 5% of levels that prevailed at time they were made
- Like many forecasters, these economists driving with eyes firmly fixed on rearview mirror
- Bears out old adage: "it's difficult to make accurate predictions, especially with regard to the future"

**Fourth question: Were forecasters ever right?**
- Answer: firm yes
- For example, in each semiannual forecast, someone nailed yield on thirty-year bond within 10-20 basis points, even as interest rates changed radically
- Winning forecast much more accurate than consensus forecast (off by 70-130 basis points)

**Fifth question: If forecasters were sometimes right—and right so dramatically—then why remain so negative on forecasts?**
- Because important thing in forecasting isn't getting it right once
- Important thing is getting it right consistently

**Sixth question: "two things that might make you think twice about heeding winners' forecasts"**
- First, they generally failed to make accurate predictions in surveys other than the one they won
- Second, half the time in surveys they didn't win, their forecasts much more wrong than even inaccurate consensus

**Pattern for occasional correctness:**
- One way to be right sometimes is to always be bullish or always be bearish
- If hold fixed view long enough, may be right sooner or later
- If always an outlier, likely eventually be applauded for extremely unconventional forecast that correctly foresaw what no one else did
- But that doesn't mean forecasts are regularly of any value

**The problem with being right occasionally:**
- Possible to be right about macro-future once in a while, but not on regular basis
- Doesn't do any good to possess survey of sixty-four forecasts that includes a few that are accurate; you have to know which ones they are

> "The Value of Predictions II, or Give That Man a Cigar," August 22, 1996

### The Great Investing Dilemma

**Discussion suggests we have dilemma:**
- Investment results will be determined entirely by what happens in future
- While we may know what will happen much of time when things are "normal," can't know much about what will happen at those moments when knowing would make biggest difference

> "With this, Marks presents the great investing dilemma." — **Paul Johnson**

**The forecasting paradox:**
- Most of time, people predict future that is a lot like recent past
- They're not necessarily wrong: most of time future largely *is* rerun of recent past
- However, many forecasts that correctly extrapolate past experience are of little value
- Just as forecasters usually assume future that's a lot like past, so do markets (which usually price in continuation of recent history)
- Thus if future turns out to be like past, unlikely big money will be made, even by those who foresaw correctly that it would
- Once in a while, however, future turns out to be very different from past
- It's at these times that accurate forecasts would be of great value
- It's also at these times that forecasts are least likely to be correct
- Some forecasters may turn out to be correct at these pivotal moments, suggesting that it's possible to correctly forecast key events
- But it's unlikely to be same people consistently
- Sum of this discussion suggests that, on balance, forecasts are of very little value

> "These nine bullets offer the best discussion of the shortcomings of forecasting I have ever read." — **Paul Johnson**

### The 2007-2008 Test Case

- If you need proof, ask how many forecasters correctly predicted subprime problem, global credit crisis, and massive meltdown of 2007-2008
- Might be able to think of a few, and might conclude their forecasts were valuable
- But then ask how many of those few went on to correctly foresee economic recovery that started slowly in 2009 and massive market rebound that year
- Answer: "very few"

**Why those who got 2007-2008 right probably missed 2009:**
- Those who got 2007-2008 right probably did so at least in part because of tendency toward negative views
- As such, they probably stayed negative for 2009
- Overall usefulness of those forecasts wasn't great... even though they were partially right about some of most momentous financial events in last eighty years

**The key question:**
- Not "are forecasters sometimes right?" but rather "are forecasts as a whole—or any one person's forecasts—consistently actionable and valuable?"
- No one should bet much on answer being affirmative

### The Value Problem with Forecasts

- A prediction of global crisis in 2007-2008 would have had great potential value
- But if you saw that it came from someone who wasn't right consistently—and someone with visible negative bias—would you have acted?
- That's trouble with inconsistent forecasters: not that they're never right, but that record isn't positive enough to inspire action on their occasional brainstorms

## Chapter 14: The Limits of Foreknowledge (continued)

### The "I Know" vs. "I Don't Know" Schools

- Most investors belong to the **"I know" school** — easily identifiable by their confidence
- They believe knowledge of future economies, interest rates, markets, and mainstream stocks is essential for success
- They're confident it can be achieved and that they can do it
- Comfortable investing based on their opinions about the future
- Eager to share their views with others
- Rarely look back to assess their forecasting track record

### The "I Don't Know" Mindset

- The key word for the "I don't know" school is **guarded**
- Adherents believe you can't know the future, don't have to know it, and the proper goal is to invest well without that knowledge
- As a member of the "I know" school, you get to opine on the future and be sought out for opinions
- As a member of the "I don't know" school, social consequences are less favorable
- You'll never enjoy that moment when your forecast comes true and the *Wall Street Journal* runs your picture
- You'll be spared losses from forecasts that miss the mark and from investing based on overrated knowledge of the future

### The Probability vs. Outcome Distinction

- The biggest problems arise when investors forget the difference between **probability and outcome**
- When they forget about the limits on foreknowledge
- Specific errors:
  - Believing the probability distribution is knowable with certainty (and that they know it)
  - Assuming the most likely outcome is the one that will happen
  - Assuming the expected result accurately represents the actual result
  - Ignoring the possibility of **improbable outcomes**

> "Understanding uncertainty: Risk and uncertainty aren't the same as loss, but they create the potential for loss when things go wrong." — **Howard Marks**

- Imprudent investors overlook these limitations and make mistakes
- **2004–2007**: people overestimated the extent to which outcomes were knowable and controllable
- They underestimated the risk in what they were doing

### Behavioral Implications

- Whether you think the future is knowable or unknowable has significant ramifications for behavior

**Investors who feel they know the future will:**
- Make directional bets
- Concentrate positions
- Lever holdings
- Count on future growth
- Do things that would increase risk in the absence of foreknowledge

**Investors who feel they don't know the future will:**
- Diversify
- Hedge
- Lever less (or not at all)
- Emphasize value today over growth tomorrow
- Stay high in the capital structure
- Gird for a variety of possible outcomes

- The first group did better in years leading up to the crash
- The second group was better prepared when the crash unfolded
- They had more capital available and more-intact psyches to profit from purchases at the nadir

> "If you know the future, it's silly to play defense." — **Howard Marks**

- Investing in an unknowable future as an agnostic is daunting
- If foreknowledge is elusive, investing as if you know what's coming is close to nuts

> "It ain't what you don't know that gets you into trouble. It's what you know for sure that just ain't so." — **Mark Twain**

- Overestimating what you're capable of knowing can be extremely dangerous
- Acknowledging the boundaries of what you can know—and working within those limits—can give you a great advantage

## Chapter 15: Having a Sense of Where We Stand

> "We may never know where we're going, but we'd better have a good idea where we are."

### The Challenge of Market Cycles

Market cycles present a daunting challenge:
- Their ups and downs are inevitable
- They will profoundly influence our performance
- They are unpredictable as to extent and timing

- We must cope with a force that will have great impact but is largely unknowable
- The obvious answers are often not the right ones

### Three Approaches to Cycles

**First possibility**: Accept cycles are unpredictable, redouble efforts to predict the future
- Data and experience show the only thing we can predict about cycles is their **inevitability**

**Second possibility**: Accept the future isn't knowable, throw up hands, ignore cycles
- Try to make good investments and hold them throughout
- This is the **buy-and-hold approach**

**Third possibility (the right one)**: Why not try to figure out where we stand in each cycle and what that implies for our actions?

> "In the world of investing, ... nothing is as dependable as cycles. We cannot know how far a trend will go, when it will turn, what will make it turn or how far things will then go in the opposite direction. But I'm confident that every trend will stop sooner or later. So what can we do about cycles? We may never know where we're going, but we'd better have a good idea where we are." — "It Is What It Is," March 27, 2006

### Taking the Market's Temperature

- Understanding where we stand requires work, but it can be done
- Must be alert to what's going on
- **Santayana**: "Those who cannot remember the past are condemned to repeat it"
- Those unaware of what's going on around them are destined to be buffeted by it

**Key concept**: **Inference**
- Everyone sees what happens each day as reported in the media
- But how many make an effort to understand what those events say about the psyches of market participants and the investment climate?

**Questions to ask yourself:**
- Are investors optimistic or pessimistic?
- Do media talking heads say markets should be piled into or avoided?
- Are novel investment schemes readily accepted or dismissed?
- Are securities offerings and fund openings treated as opportunities to get rich or possible pitfalls?
- Has the credit cycle rendered capital readily available or impossible to obtain?
- Are price/earnings ratios high or low in the context of history?
- Are yield spreads tight or generous?

> "These insightful questions can easily act as a checklist that investors could use periodically to take the market's temperature." — **Paul Johnson**

- All these things are important
- None entails forecasting
- We can make excellent investment decisions on the basis of present observations, with no need to make guesses about the future
- The key is to take note of these things and let them tell you what to do
- Markets don't cry out for action every day
- They do at the extremes, when their pronouncements are highly important

### The 2007–2008 Credit Crisis as a Case Study

- Can be viewed as painful time or as the greatest learning experience in our lifetimes
- Understanding the latter can make anyone a better investor
- No better example to illustrate the importance of accurate observations and the folly of forecasting

**Leading up to mid-2007:**
- Period of unbridled and unconscious risk taking
- Attitudes cool toward stocks and bonds
- Money flowed to "alternative investments" like private equity buyouts
- Unquestioning acceptance that homes and real estate would provide profits
- Too-free access to capital with low interest rates and loose terms
- Leverage used in amounts that proved excessive

**Indicators of heatedness:**
- High yield bond and below-investment-grade leveraged loan issuance at record levels
- Unusually high percentage rated **triple-C**
- Issuance of debt to raise money for dividends to owners was routine
- Debt increasingly issued with coupons paid with more debt, few or no covenants to protect creditors
- Formerly rare triple-A debt ratings assigned by thousands to tranches of untested structured vehicles
- Buyouts done at increasing multiples of cash flow and leverage ratios
- Buyouts of firms in highly cyclical industries like semiconductor manufacturing

**Clear inference possible:**
- Providers of capital were competing to do so
- Easing terms and interest rates rather than demanding adequate protection and rewards
- **"Too much money chasing too few deals"** — seven scariest words for the thoughtful investor

> "The riskiest thing: When buyers compete to put large amounts of capital to work in a market, prices are bid up relative to value, prospective returns shrink, and risk rises." — **Howard Marks**

### Money as a Commodity

- Think of money as a commodity like others
- Everyone's money is pretty much the same
- To place more money (get people to choose you for financing), you have to make your money cheaper

**Two ways to lower the price:**
1. Reduce the interest rate you charge on loans
2. Agree to a higher price for the thing you're buying
- Either way, you're settling for a lower prospective return

### Warning Signs in 2004–2007

**The movement from skepticism to credulousness** regarding the **silver bullet or can't-lose investment**:
- Thoughtful investors noticed appetite for silver bullets running high
- Greed had won over fear
- Signified a nonskeptical—and thus risky—market

**Hedge funds as sure things:**
- Came to be viewed as such during the last decade
- Especially "**absolute return**" funds
- Long/short or arbitrage funds that wouldn't pursue high returns by making directional bets
- Managers' skill or technology would enable consistent returns of 8–11% regardless of market direction

**Too few people recognized:**
- Achieving rock-steady returns in that range would be phenomenal—perhaps too good to be true
- Too few wondered how many managers have enough talent for this miracle
- In 2008, the term *absolute return* was shown to have been overused and misused (average fund lost about 18%)

**Risk elimination through financial engineering:**
- **Tranching** deserves particular attention
- Consists of allocating portfolio's value and cash flow to stakeholders in tiers of seniority
- In 2004–2007, the notion arose that if you cut risk into small pieces and sell off to investors best suited to hold them, **the risk disappears**
- Sounds like magic
- No coincidence that tranched securitizations became site of many worst meltdowns
- **There's simply no magic in investing**

**The error became clear beginning August 2007:**
- Risk hadn't been banished
- In fact, had been elevated by investors' excessive trust and insufficient skepticism

**The period from 2004 through mid-2007:**
- Presented one of the greatest opportunities to outperform by reducing risk
- All you had to do was take the market's temperature during an overheated period and deplane as it continued upward
- Those able to do so exemplify the principles of **contrarianism**
- Contrarian investors who cut risk and otherwise prepared during lead-up lost less in 2008 meltdown
- Were best positioned to take advantage of vast bargains it created

### Responding to Current Realities vs. Expecting the Future

- Few fields where decisions on strategies and tactics aren't influenced by what we see in the environment
- Shouldn't our investment actions be equally affected by the investing climate?

- Most people strive to adjust portfolios based on what they think lies ahead
- Most would admit forward visibility just isn't that great
- Make the case for responding to current realities and their implications, as opposed to expecting the future to be made clear

- Markets move cyclically, rising and falling
- The pendulum oscillates, rarely pausing at the "happy medium," the midpoint of its arc
- What are investors to do about it?
- **Response is simple: try to figure out what's going on around us, and use that to guide our actions**

## Chapter 16 (continued): The Role of Luck

### "I Know" vs "I Don't Know" Schools of Thought

- **"I know" school** investors predict a single future scenario, build portfolios to maximize returns under that outcome, and largely ignore other possibilities
- They divide into winners and losers based on early results, attribute success to skill and failures to bad luck
- Question to ask: "Could they really have seen the future?" Their probabilistic approach means outcomes are largely "up to the gods"
- **"I don't know" school** thinks of future events as a probability distribution, with many possible outcomes
- They accept undistinguished results for a while, confident superior skill will show over many rolls of the dice

> "Short-term gains and short-term losses are potential impostors, as neither is necessarily indicative of real investment ability." — Paul Johnson

- Investment performance depends on what unfolds, but key questions: Were events within the portfolio manager's ken? What would performance have been if other events occurred?
- **Taleb's "alternative histories"** concept central to understanding randomness

### Embracing Uncertainty

- **Taleb's ideas novel and provocative**: once you realize the extent randomness affects outcomes, you view everything differently
- "I know" school views single future as knowable and conquerable
- "I don't know" school thinks in probability distributions—one outcome most likely, but many other possibilities may have collective likelihood higher than the single most likely one

### Investment Principles from Uncertainty

Marks's recommendations from the "I don't know" school:
- Spend time finding value among knowable things (industries, companies, securities) rather than base decisions on macro forecasts
- Get value on your side through strongly held, analytically derived opinions and buying for less when opportunities arise
- Practice **defensive investing**—survival under negative outcomes more important than maximum returns under favorable ones
- Act contrary to the herd at extremes: aggressive when market is low, cautious when high
- View strategies and results with suspicion until proven over many trials

- Healthy respect for risk, awareness we don't know the future, understanding best we can do is view future as probability distribution and invest accordingly
- Insistence on defensive investing and emphasis on avoiding pitfalls

## Chapter 17: The Most Important Thing Is… Investing Defensively

> "There are old investors, and there are bold investors, but there are no old bold investors." — Old saying

### The Fundamental Choice: Offense vs Defense

- First step in giving investment advice: understand attitude toward risk and return
- People care about both making money and avoiding losses
- Can't simultaneously maximize profit and loss avoidance—requires striking a balance
- Decision should be made consciously and rationally
- Best framework: **offense versus defense**

### The Tennis Analogy

- Marks references **Charles Ellis's "The Loser's Game"** (*The Financial Analysts Journal*, 1975)—seminal article on defensive investing
- Ellis described analysis from **Dr. Simon Ramo's** *Extraordinary Tennis for the Ordinary Tennis Player*
- **Professional tennis = "winner's game"**: match goes to player who hits the most winners
- Pros can make almost any shot they want, get to most balls
- Pros so consistent that statisticians track rare "unforced errors"
- **Amateur tennis = "loser's game"**: winner just keeps ball in play until loser hits it into net or off court
- Points aren't won; they're lost—Marks recognized his own loss-avoidance strategy

### Ellis's Application to Investing

- **Charles Ellis** applied Ramo's idea to investments
- His views on market efficiency and high trading costs led him to conclude pursuing winners in mainstream stock markets unlikely to pay off
- Instead: **avoid hitting losers**

### How Much Control Do You Have?

- Choice between offense and defense based on how much investor believes is within control
- Professional tennis players have so much control they should go for winners
- Investment results only partly within investors' control—can make good money and outlast opponents without trying tough shots
- Even highly skilled investors can be guilty of mis-hits; overaggressive shot can lose the match
- **Defense—keeping things from going wrong—important part of every great investor's game**

### Football vs Soccer as Investment Metaphors

- **American football**: offensive and defensive squads alternate, clear switching points
- Marks finds this analogy poor for investing: no whistle to signal switches, rarely know when to switch, no time-outs
- **Soccer**: same eleven players on field whole game, must deal with all eventualities
- Collectively must have potential to score goals and stop opposition
- Coach must decide: offensive team (score many, hold others to fewer) or defensive team (shut out opposition) or balanced
- Few can switch tactics to match market conditions on timely basis
- Should commit to approach that serves through variety of scenarios

### Oaktree's Defensive Preference

- **Oaktree's preference for defense clear**: in good times, okay to keep up with indices (may lag a bit)
- Even average investors make money in good times; few managers get fired for being average in up markets
- Oaktree portfolios set up to outperform in bad times—that's when outperformance is essential
- Keep up in good times and outperform in bad times = above-average results over full cycles with below-average volatility
- Clients enjoy outperformance when others suffering

### No Right Answer Between Offense and Defense

- Decision should be function of personality, leanings, belief in ability, peculiarities of markets and clients
- No inherently right choice—lots of routes to success

### Defining Offense and Defense

- **Offense**: adoption of aggressive tactics and elevated risk pursuing above-average gains
- **Defense**: Rather than doing the right thing, main emphasis on not doing the wrong thing
- Difference between doing right thing and avoiding wrong thing: sounds similar on surface, but big difference in mind-set and tactics
- Defense can be seen as attempt at higher returns through avoidance of minuses rather than inclusion of pluses
- More through consistent moderate progress than occasional flashes of brilliance

### Two Elements of Investment Defense

1. **Exclusion of losers from portfolios**:
   - Extensive due diligence, high standards, demanding low price and generous margin for error
   - Less willing to bet on continued prosperity, rosy forecasts, uncertain developments

2. **Avoidance of poor years** and especially exposure to crashes:
   - Requires thoughtful portfolio diversification, limits on overall riskiness, general tilt toward safety
   - **Concentration** and **leverage**: examples of offense that add to returns when they work but harmful when they don't
   - Use enough and they can jeopardize survival if things go awry
   - Defense increases likelihood of getting through tough times and surviving long enough to enjoy eventual payoff

> "Here is part of the tradeoff with diversification. You must be diversified enough to survive bad times or bad luck so that skill and good process can have the chance to pay off over the long term." — Joel Greenblatt

### Preparing for the Worst

- Investors must brace for untoward developments
- Easy to say prepare for bad days, but how bad? What's worst case?
- Amount of risk you'll bear = function of extent to which you choose to pursue return
- Amount of safety = based on how much potential return you're willing to forgo
- No right answer, just trade-offs

> "Because ensuring the ability to survive under adverse circumstances is incompatible with maximizing returns in the good times, investors must choose between the two."

### Margin of Safety/Margin for Error

- Critical element in defensive investing: what **Warren Buffett** calls **"margin of safety"** or **"margin for error"**

> "Margin of safety" and "Mr. Market" are the two ideas that Buffett refers to as Graham's greatest contributions to the investing world." — Joel Greenblatt

- Not hard to make successful investments if future unfolds as expected
- Tightly targeted investments can be highly successful if future turns out as you hope
- But consider: how will you fare if future doesn't oblige? What makes outcomes tolerable even when future doesn't meet expectations? Answer: **margin for error**

> "Understanding uncertainty: Despite the presence of uncertainty, many investors try to select the ideal strategy through which to maximize return. But if instead we acknowledge the existence of uncertainty, we should insist on building in a generous margin of safety." — Howard Marks

### Margin for Error Analogy: Lending

- Lender example: not hard to make loans that will be repaid if conditions remain stable
- But what enables loan repayment even if conditions deteriorate?
- **Margin for error**: if borrower becomes jobless, probability of repayment greater if there are savings, salable assets, alternative income sources
- Contrast: lender who insists on margin for error lends only to strong borrowers—few credit losses, but forgoes opportunities that go to less insistent lenders
- Aggressive lender looks smarter (makes more money) as long as environment remains salutary
- Prudent lender's reward comes only in bad times: reduced credit losses
- Won't enjoy highest highs but will avoid lowest lows—what happens to those who emphasize defense

### Another Margin for Error Example

- Find something worth $100. Buy for $90 = good chance of gain, moderate chance of loss if too optimistic
- Buy for $70 instead = $20 reduction provides additional room to be wrong and still come out okay
- **Low price = ultimate source of margin for error**

- Choice: maximize returns through aggressive tactics, or build in protection through margin for error
- Can't have both in full measure

### Offense vs Defense: A Conscious Balance

- Of two ways to perform as investor—racking up exceptional gains and avoiding losses—Marks believes **avoiding losses more dependable**
- Achieving gains usually involves being right about events on the come
- Losses minimized by ascertaining tangible value present, herd's expectations moderate, prices low
- Latter can be done with greater consistency

### Fixed Income vs Equities

- Conscious balance must be struck between striving for return and limiting risk
- **In fixed income** (where Marks started): returns limited, manager's greatest contribution through avoidance of loss
- Upside truly "fixed," only variability on downside—avoiding it holds the key
- Distinguishing yourself as bond investor: not which paying bonds you hold, but whether you exclude bonds that don't pay
- According to **Graham and Dodd**: emphasis on exclusion makes fixed income investing **a negative art**

- **In equities and other upside-oriented areas**: avoiding losses isn't enough; potential for return must be present
- Fixed income investor can practice defense exclusively
- Investor beyond fixed income (seeking higher return) must balance offense and defense

- Key word: **balance**
- Need for offense in addition to defense doesn't mean indifference to mix
- To strive for more return, generally take on more uncertainty—more risk
- **Decision to go that way should be made consciously and intelligently**

### Oaktree's Activities Based on Defense

- Oaktree's activities based on defense (but not to exclusion of offense)
- Can't invest successfully in convertibles, distressed debt, or real estate without thinking about both upside and downside

### The Testosterone-Laden World of Investing

- Investing = testosterone-laden world where too many think about how good they are and how much they'll make if they swing for the fences and connect
- "I know" school investors: talk about home runs hit in past and home-runs-in-the-making in current portfolio
- How many talk about consistency, or that their worst year wasn't too bad?

- **Brief outstanding investment careers**: not as short as professional athletes, but shorter than they should be
- Where are leading competitors from when Marks first managed high yield bonds 25-30 years ago? Almost none around
- Not one of Oaktree's prominent distressed debt competitors from early days remains a leader today
- Where'd they go? Many disappeared due to organizational flaws; rest gone because they swung for fences but struck out

### The Great Paradox

- Brings up what Marks considers a great paradox:
- **Many investment managers' careers end not because they fail to hit home runs, but because they strike out too often—not because they don't have enough winners, but because they have too many losers**

- And yet, lots of managers keep swinging for the fences

### Why Managers Keep Swinging

- They bet too much when they think they have a winning idea or correct view of future, concentrating portfolios rather than diversifying
- Incur excessive transaction costs changing holdings too often or attempting to time the market
- Position portfolios for favorable scenarios and hoped-for outcomes rather than ensuring survival under inevitable miscalculation or stroke of bad luck

- **At Oaktree**: "if we avoid the losers, the winners will take care of themselves"
- Our motto since the beginning, always will be
- Go for batting average, not home runs
- Know others will get headlines for big victories and spectacular seasons
- But expect to be around at the finish because of consistent good performance that produces satisfied clients

### Playing Offense: High-Octane Activity

- **Playing offense—trying for winners through risk bearing—high-octane activity**
- Might bring gains you seek… or pronounced disappointment
- More challenging and potentially lucrative the waters, more likely to have attracted skilled fishermen
- Unless skills fully competitive, more likely to be prey than victor

### Requirements for Aggressive Investing

- Technical skills
- Intestinal fortitude
- Patient clients (if managing money for others)
- Dependable capital

- When developments become adverse, need these things to get through
- Investment decisions may work out in long run or on average, but without these, aggressive investor may not get to see the long run

### Operating a High-Risk Portfolio

- Like performing on high wire without a net
- Payoff may be high and bring oohs and aahs, but slipups will kill you

### Bottom Line on Superior Performance

- Has a lot to do with daring to be great
- One of investor's first and most fundamental decisions: how far out the portfolio will venture
- How much emphasis on diversifying, avoiding loss, ensuring against below-pack performance, how much on sacrificing these in hope of doing better?

- Learned a lot from favorite fortune cookie: **"The cautious seldom err or write great poetry"**
- Cuts two ways: caution can help avoid mistakes, but can also keep from great accomplishments

- Personally, like caution in money managers
- Believes in many cases, avoidance of losses and terrible years more easily achieved than repeated greatness
- Thus **risk control more likely to create solid foundation for superior long-term track record**
- Investing scared, requiring good value and substantial margin for error, being conscious of what you don't know and can't control = hallmarks of best investors

> "All investors should heed this advice." — Paul Johnson

### The Choice Defies Easy Answer

- Many unwilling to do enough of anything (buy a stock, commit to asset class, invest with manager) that could significantly harm results if it doesn't work
- But to be able to materially help return if it succeeds, you have to do enough so it could materially hurt if it fails

> "Fear of looking wrong: This dilemma shows how the fear of looking wrong interferes with implementing judgments and how hard it is to be a successful investor if you're worried about appearances." — Howard Marks

### Investment as Two-Edged Sword

- Almost everything in investing = two-edged sword
- Taking bigger risks, substituting concentration for diversification, using leverage to magnify gains
- Only exception: genuine personal skill
- As for all the rest, if it'll help if it works, that means it'll hurt if it doesn't
- Makes choice between offense and defense important and challenging

### Many See as Aspiring for More vs Settling for Less

- For thoughtful investor: answer is that defense can provide good returns achieved consistently
- Offense may consist of dreams that often go unmet
- **For me, defense is the way to go**

### The Cost of Defensive Investing

- Can cause you to miss out on things that are hot and get hotter
- Can leave you with bat on shoulder trip after trip to the plate
- May hit fewer home runs than another investor… but also likely to have fewer strikeouts and fewer inning-ending double plays
- **Defensive investing** sounds very erudite, but can simplify it: **Invest scared!**

> "I love this comment. I have met few investors who invest scared, except during a general market panic." — Paul Johnson

### What "Invest Scared" Means

- Worry about possibility of loss
- Worry that there's something you don't know
- Worry that you can make high-quality decisions but be hit by bad luck or surprise events
- Investing scared will:
  - Prevent hubris
  - Keep guard up and mental adrenaline flowing
  - Make you insist on adequate margin of safety
  - Increase chances portfolio prepared for things going wrong
- And if nothing goes wrong, surely the winners will take care of themselves

## Chapter 18: Avoiding Pitfalls — Crisis Lessons

### Recognizing Pitfalls

- First step: **be on the lookout** for them
- Greed and optimism lead people to:
  - Pursue high-return/low-risk strategies
  - Pay elevated prices for securities in vogue
  - Hold things after they become highly priced, hoping for more appreciation
- Hindsight reveals unrealistic expectations and ignored risks
- "Learning about pitfalls through painful experience is of only limited help. The key is to try to anticipate them."

### What We Learn from a Crisis—or Ought To

- **Too much capital availability** makes money flow to the wrong places
  - Scarce capital = patient decisions; abundant capital = undeserving investments

- **When capital goes where it shouldn't, bad things happen**
  - Money everywhere: unqualified borrowers get "money on a silver platter"—delinquencies, bankruptcies, losses

- **When capital is in oversupply, investors compete by accepting low returns and slender margin for error**
  - Bidding more = saying you'll take less return

- **Widespread disregard for risk creates great risk**
  - "Nothing can go wrong," "No price is too high," "Someone will always pay me more"
  - People thought better companies meant buyout transactions could support larger leverage
  - **Ignored risk of untoward developments** and danger in highly leveraged capital structures

- **Inadequate due diligence leads to investment losses**
  - Best defense: thorough, insightful analysis and **Warren Buffett's "margin for error"**
  - Hot markets: people worry about missing out, not losing money
  - Skeptical analysis becomes "the province of old fogeys"

- **In heady times, capital is devoted to innovative investments, many of which fail**
  - Bullish investors focus on what might work, not what might go wrong
  - Eagerness replaces prudence
  - People accept new products they don't understand

- **Hidden fault lines in portfolios can make seemingly unrelated assets move in tandem**
  - Easier to assess return/risk of individual investment than to understand **correlation**
  - Correlation often underestimated, especially its increase in crisis

- **Psychological and technical factors can swamp fundamentals**
  - Long run: value creation/destruction driven by fundamentals
  - Short run: markets highly responsive to investor psychology and technical factors
  - **"I think confidence matters more than anything else in the short run."**

> "The market eventually gets it right. In the short term, psychology and technical factors can make the wait for the long term exceptionally painful, but often this is the source of great opportunity." — Joel Greenblatt

- **Markets change, invalidating models**
  - "Quant" fund failures center on computer model failures
  - Computers profit from patterns in past markets
  - Can't predict pattern changes or anticipate aberrant periods

- **Leverage magnifies outcomes but doesn't add value**
  - Makes sense to use leverage for assets at bargain prices with high promised returns
  - Dangerous to leverage assets offering low returns or narrow risk spreads—fully priced or overpriced
  - "Makes little sense to use leverage to try to turn inadequate returns into adequate returns"

- **Excesses correct**
  - When psychology is extremely rosy and markets "priced for perfection"—scene set for capital destruction
  - May happen because assumptions too optimistic, negative events occur, or "simply because too-high prices collapse of their own weight"

### The Meta-Lesson: Supply/Demand Balance and Capital Eagerness

- Eleven lessons reduce to one: **be alert to supply/demand balance for investable funds and eagerness to spend**
- **Credit crunch** (too little capital): worthwhile investments go begging, business slows
- Opposite deserves equal attention: "**too much money chasing too few ideas**"
- Oversupply of capital and "dearth of prudence" (2004-2007) can be dangerous
- Must be recognized and dealt with

> "NO DIFFERENT THIS TIME," DECEMBER 17, 2007

### The Crisis: Pitfalls Everywhere

- Global crisis = great learning opportunity with "grave errors"
- Pitfalls everywhere: investors unworried, even ebullient in years leading up
- People believed risk had been banished—worry only about missing opportunity, not losing money
- Risky, untested innovations adopted on shaky assumptions
- Undue weight given to opaque models, "black boxes," financial engineers, "quants"
- **Leverage piled on top of leverage**

### Recognizing the Pre-Crisis Environment

- Almost no one knew exact consequences, but "it was possible to have a sense that we were riding for a fall"
- Even if specific pitfalls not susceptible to identification/avoidance, "this was a perfect time to recognize that many were lurking"
- Adopting a more defensive posture would have helped
- **Failure to do so = great error of the crisis**

### What Investors Could Have Done

- Taking note of carefree, incautious behavior of others
- Preparing psychologically for downturn
- Selling assets, or at least more risk-prone ones
- Reducing leverage
- Raising cash
- Generally tilting portfolios toward increased defensiveness

- "Any of these would have helped"
- While avoiding declines completely was "nigh onto impossible," elevated caution could lead to "smaller losses" and "relative outperformance"

### The Two Opportunities: Succumb or Go Defensive

- Crisis "rife with potential pitfalls": opportunities to succumb/lose, then opportunities to go into shell and miss out
- Relatively loss-free periods make people think of risk as **volatility**, convinced they can live with it
- But ability to live with volatility and maintain composure "has been overestimated—and usually it has"
- Error comes to light at market nadir
- **Loss of confidence and resolve** causes investors to sell at bottom, converting downward fluctuations into permanent losses and preventing participation in recovery
- "This is the greatest error in investing—the most unfortunate aspect of pro-cyclical behavior—because of its permanence and because it tends to affect large portions of portfolios"

### Pro-Cyclical Behavior: The Essential Pitfall

- **Countercyclical behavior** = essential element in avoiding full crisis effect
- **Pro-cyclical behavior** = greatest potential pitfall
- Investors who maintained/added to bullish positions as market rose were least prepared for bust and recovery

Effects of pro-cyclical behavior:
- Declines had maximum psychological impact
- Margin calls and collateral confiscations decimated leveraged vehicles
- Troubled holdings required remedial management action
- Loss of confidence prevented doing the right thing at the right time

### The Defensive Investor Advantage

- Can't spend relative outperformance, but human nature makes defensive investors derive comfort when they lose less than others
- Two important effects:
  1. Enables them to maintain equanimity and resist psychological pressures to sell at lows
  2. Being in better frame of mind and financial condition allows them to profit from carnage by buying at lows
- "Thus, they generally do better in recoveries"

### Credit Markets 2007-2009: The Formula for Success

- Credit markets particularly hard-hit (focus of innovation, risk taking, leverage)
- Their 2009 gains = "best in their history"
- Surviving declines and buying at resultant lows = great formula for success
- "But first it required the avoidance of pitfalls"

## Chapter 19: Adding Value

### The Core Challenge

- Not hard to perform in line with market in risk/return
- The trick: **do better than the market—add value**
- Requires superior investment skill, superior insight
- Returns to **second-level thinking** and exceptional skill

### Introducing Beta and Alpha

- **Beta**: measure of portfolio's relative sensitivity to market movements
- **Alpha**: personal investment skill—"the ability to generate performance that is unrelated to movement of the market"

### Achieving Market Return: The Index Fund

- Easy to achieve market return via **passive index fund**
- Holds every security in a given market index in proportion to equity capitalization
- Mirrors index characteristics
- Delivers index return
- "Epitomizes investing without value added"

### Active Investing: Two Paths to Outperformance

**First path: Aggressiveness or defensiveness** (market timing)
- Increase portfolio market sensitivity by overweighting volatile stocks or utilizing leverage
- Increases "systematic riskiness" (**beta**)
- Theory: may increase return, but differential fully explained by increased systematic risk
- "Thus doing these things won't improve the portfolio's risk-adjusted return"

**Second path: Deviation from the index** (stock-picking)
- Buy more of some index stocks, underweight/exclude others, add non-index stocks
- Alters exposure to specific company events
- Portfolios diverge from index for "nonsystematic" (idiosyncratic) reasons—return deviates too
- Long run: unless investors have superior insight, deviations cancel out

### Active Investors Without Superior Insight

- "No better than passive investors"
- Portfolios shouldn't perform better than passive portfolio
- Could be worse due to nonsystematic risks and transaction costs

### Understanding Performance Variability

- If market index up 15%, not every non-value-added active investor achieves 15%
- All hold different portfolios—some perform better than others, "just not consistently or dependably"

### Pro-Risk Aggressive Investors

- Should make more than index in good times, lose more in bad times
- Where **beta** comes in: theory means relative volatility
- **Portfolio beta above 1**: more volatile than reference market
- **Portfolio beta below 1**: less volatile
- Example: market up 15%, portfolio beta 1.2 = should return 18% (plus or minus alpha)

### Understanding Beta and Systematic Risk

- Increased return explained by increased beta (systematic risk)
- Returns don't increase to compensate for non-systematic risk
- Risk intrinsic and inescapable in investing = **systematic risk** ("nondiversifiable")
- Rest of risk from holding individual stocks = **nonsystematic risk**
- Can be eliminated by diversifying—"why should investors be compensated with additional return for bearing it?"

### The Performance Formula

y = α + βx

- **α** (alpha): symbol for alpha
- **β** (beta): symbol for beta
- **x**: return of the market
- Market-related return of portfolio = beta times market return
- Alpha (skill-related return) added to arrive at total return

### Risk-Adjusted Return: The Key Metric

- Marks dismisses identity between risk and volatility but insists on considering portfolio return in light of overall riskiness
- Manager earning 18% with risky portfolio not necessarily superior to one earning 15% with lower-risk portfolio
- **Risk-adjusted return holds the key**—best assessed judgmentally, not scientifically

> "Such an important concept for business students!" — Joel Greenblatt

### Alpha: Investment Skill Exists

- Marks also dismisses idea that alpha term must be zero
- Investment skill exists, though not everyone has it
- "Only through thinking about risk-adjusted return might we determine whether an investor possesses superior insight, investment skill or alpha…that is, whether the investor adds value"

## Chapter 20: The Most Important Thing Is ... Reasonable Expectations

### Return Expectations Must Be Realistic

- Return goals must be **explicit** and **reasonable** relative to the **risk entailed**
- Higher returns are "unnatural" and require extraordinary circumstances: depressed buying environment, exceptional skill, extensive risk-bearing, heavy leverage, or good luck
- Higher returns achieved in normal markets should be questioned skeptically—apply the **"too good to be true" test**

### The Requirements for Extraordinary Returns

- Normal risk-bearing produces returns in **single digits or low double digits**
- Expecting returns beyond market norms requires at least one of these conditions:
  - **Extremely depressed environment** in which to buy
  - **Extraordinary investment skill** (rare by definition)
  - **Extensive risk-bearing** (works against you when things go wrong)
  - **Heavy leverage** (magnifies losses as well as gains)
  - **Good luck** (cannot be counted on)

### The Madoff Case Study

- **Bernard Madoff** perpetrated the greatest **Ponzi scheme** to deceive investors
- Claimed ~10% annual returns consistently for almost twenty years, regardless of market environment
- Returns were not extraordinarily high—but the **consistency was impossible**
- Investors failed to ask **"why me?"**—if such returns were feasible, why would he offer them to clients?
- **No rational explanation** for the returns existed
- Few investors applied **skepticism** or questioned whether steady 10% returns were **achievable with T-bill dependability**
- Madoff scandal demonstrates the critical importance of saying **"too good to be true"** to unreasonable expectations

### Oaktree's Approach to Market Timing

- Philosophy: **Disavowal of market timing**—don't try to ascertain when the bottom has been reached
- Instead: Buy opportunistically when something is cheap; if it gets cheaper, buy more
- **Goal for good years**: Match market returns
- **Goal for bad years**: Perform better than the market
- **Dual objective**: Match upside participation + downside protection
- Aggressive investors participate fully in bull markets but give back gains in bear markets
- **Defensive investors** protect on downside but sacrifice upside
- **Superior skill** is the only source of **asymmetrical performance**

### Unrealistic Return Expectations and the 2005-2007 Investor Mindset

> I need 8 percent. I'd be glad to earn 10 percent instead. Twelve percent would be even better. Fifteen percent would be great. Twenty percent would be terrific. And 30 percent would be out of this world.

- This monologue captures typical investor sentiment pre-crisis: no acknowledgment of risk constraints
- **Critical flaw**: The investor hasn't asked whether each return goal is reasonable or what would have to be true to achieve it
- **Corrected approach**: "I need 8 percent. I'd be glad to earn 10 percent instead. Twelve percent would be even better. But I won't try for more than that, because doing so would entail risks I'm just not willing to bear."

### Skepticism as an Investment Essential

- Ask **"Is it too good to be true?"** before accepting return promises
- Ask **"Why me?"**—if an opportunity is so lucrative, why is it available to you cheaply?
- If an economist or strategist offers a sure-to-be-right forecast, ask why they're still working
- **Perfection is the enemy of the good**: Demanding perfect conditions (buying at bottom) causes investors to miss good investments
- Voltaire: **"The perfect is the enemy of the good"**

### Key Adages for Investment Discipline

- **"What the wise man does in the beginning, the fool does in the end"**—ability to resist excesses and avoid late-stage participation is rare but essential
- **"Being too far ahead of your time is indistinguishable from being wrong"**—underpriced assets may take years to be appreciated; requires patience and fortitude
- **"Never forget the six-foot-tall man who drowned crossing the stream that was five feet deep on average"**—margin for error prevents catastrophe

### Risk, Uncertainty, and Investment Philosophy

- **Risk** is not just volatility—it includes possibility of permanent loss
- Riskier investments have wider range of possible outcomes and higher probability of loss
- **Correlation** is critical: diversification only works if portfolio holdings respond differently to given developments
- Most investors confuse holding many different things with true diversification
- **Defensive investing** emphasizes **risk control** and avoiding losses rather than maximizing gains
- During good years, defensive positions may underperform; their value becomes evident only in bad years

### The Foundation of Superior Investing

- **Value** is the foundation: know what you're buying and what it's worth
- Price-to-value relationship holds ultimate key to investment success
- **Psychology and technicals** dominate short-term price movements and provide opportunity for big profits or big mistakes
- **Contrarian approach**: When others are unwilling/cautious, be aggressive; when others are panicked, turn aggressive
- **Behavioral cycles**: Investor psychology swings from optimism→pessimism; credulous→skeptical
- Markets and economies cycle; most people extrapolate current direction as permanent—creates dangerous overvaluation and undervaluation extremes

### Final Requirements for Investment Success

- **Superior insight into value**—must see things others don't or do better analysis
- **Solid factual and analytical foundation**—must hold firmly to value estimate
- **Understanding that higher returns require higher risk** or exceptional skill—most investors don't possess either
- **Reasonable expectations**—anything else usually leads to trouble through accepting greater risk than perceived
- **Awareness of macro unknowability**—better to gain knowledge advantage at micro level than try to forecast macro future
- **Margin for error**—tangible lasting value, buying well below value, avoiding leverage, diversifying
- **Psychological discipline**: Reason must overcome emotion; recognize dangerous impulses without succumbing
