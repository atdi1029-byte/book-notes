# The Prize: The Epic Quest for Oil, Money & Power — Part 1 (Compact)
## Daniel Yergin (1991) — Prologue through Chapter 19

---

## Prologue (p6-13)

Winston Churchill, until summer 1911 an "economist" opposing
increased military spending, was transformed by the Agadir crisis
when Kaiser Wilhelm sent the gunboat *Panther* to Morocco.
Appointed First Lord of the Admiralty, Churchill faced whether
to convert the Royal Navy from coal to oil. The strategic
benefits — speed, efficiency, manpower — were compelling.
"To commit the Navy irrevocably to oil was indeed 'to take
arms against a sea of troubles.'" But: "Mastery itself was
the prize of the venture."

**1990 bookend**: Saddam Hussein's invasion of Kuwait stripped
away the illusion that oil had lost strategic significance.

**Three great themes**: (1) Rise of capitalism and modern
business — Standard Oil among the world's first multinationals,
seven of the top twenty Fortune 500 being oil companies. (2) Oil
intertwined with global politics — WWI's internal combustion
engine, Japan's Pearl Harbor to seize East Indies petroleum,
"oil power" of the 1970s. The Shah was destroyed by the very
oil wealth he craved. (3) "Hydrocarbon Man" — kerosene pushed
back the night, gasoline was initially dumped into rivers, then
the automobile transformed civilization. Oil provides fertilizer,
transports food, supplies plastics. The environmental movement
now challenges this — yet Hydrocarbon Man shows little
inclination to give up his cars.

## Chapter 1: Oil on the Brain (p14-40)

### *The Missing $526.08*
Professor Benjamin Silliman Jr. of Yale was retained in 1854 to
analyze "rock oil" for George Bissell and James Townsend's
investor group. Silliman withheld his report over an unpaid
$526.08 fee, hiding it with a friend and disappearing on a
Southern tour. When finally released (April 16, 1855), it was
"a turning point" — rock oil could yield high-quality
illuminating oil. Armed with the report, the group formed the
Pennsylvania Rock Oil Company.

### *Bissell's Vision*
George Bissell conceived that rock oil could serve as
illuminant, not medicine. Self-supporting from age twelve,
fluent in French, Portuguese, Hebrew, Sanskrit, and others,
Bissell spotted a rock oil sample at Dartmouth. In 1856,
sheltering under a druggist's awning on Broadway, he saw an
advertisement showing drilling derricks for salt — the last
piece fell into place: adapt salt-boring to oil recovery.

### *Price and Innovation*
Whale oil cost $2.50/gallon; camphene was flammable with
"an even more unattractive tendency to explode." Abraham
Gesner developed "kerosene" from asphalt (Greek *keros* +
*elaion*). By 1859, an estimated thirty-four companies
produced $5 million/year of "coal-oils." Oil was ancient —
bitumen caulked Babylon's walls and probably Noah's ark.
"Greek fire" (petroleum + lime) was a state secret more
terrible than gunpowder.

### *The "Colonel"*
Edwin Drake — a jack-of-all-trades, former railroad conductor
living in the same New Haven hotel as Townsend — was recruited
because he had a railroad pass and nothing else to do.
Townsend invented "Colonel" Drake via letters. Drake arrived
in Titusville (population 125) in December 1857. His driller
Uncle Billy Smith finally joined in spring 1859. On **August
27, 1859**, at sixty-nine feet, the drill dropped into a
crevice. Sunday, Uncle Billy peered down and saw dark fluid.
Drake attached a hand pump — doing exactly what scoffers had
ridiculed. That same day he received Townsend's letter ordering
him to close up. A week earlier, he would have obeyed. Farmers
shouted "The Yankee has struck oil."

### *"The Light of the Age"*
"As an illuminator the oil is without a figure... the brightest
and yet the cheapest in the world." Bissell spent hundreds of
thousands leasing farms. He became wealthy and donated a
gymnasium to Dartmouth with bowling alleys "in remembrance of
disciplinary troubles" from that "sinful sport." Townsend felt
denied credit. Drake lost everything through improvident
speculation, became a semi-invalid in poverty. "If you have
any of the milk of human kindness left in your bosom for me
or my family, send me some money." Pennsylvania granted him
a small pension in 1873.

### *The First Boom*
By November 1860, seventy-five wells were producing.
Production rose from 450,000 barrels (1860) to 3 million
(1862). Prices swung wildly — $10/barrel in January 1861,
50 cents by June, 10 cents by year's end. The Civil War
stimulated demand by cutting off turpentine from the South
and replacing lost cotton export revenue with kerosene exports.

Pithole exploded from dense forest to 15,000 people with
fifty hotels, two banks, and a post office handling 5,000
letters daily. Then production gave out — a parcel sold for
$2 million was auctioned for $4.37 a decade later.

### *Rule of Capture*
The **"rule of capture"** — based on English game law about
migratory animals — meant surface owners could drain common
pools, driving chaotic competition and waste. Pipelines
overcame the teamsters' stranglehold despite armed attacks,
arson, and sabotage. Oil exchanges emerged; futures became
"the favorite speculative commodity of the time."

## Chapter 2: "Our Plan" — Rockefeller (p40-75)

### *The Auction*
February 1865: Rockefeller bought out partner Maurice Clark
at auction for $72,500. "I ever point to that day as the
beginning of the success I have made in my life." At
twenty-six, he was tall, thin, taciturn, with cold blue
eyes that made people uneasy.

### *"Methodical to an Extreme"*
Born 1839 in rural New York. His father sold herbal remedies
and boasted: "I trade with the boys and skin 'em." The son
at age seven launched a turkey-selling venture. Mathematics
was his best subject. He held "intimate conversations" with
himself, counseling caution. He stuck to building cash
reserves, insulating against busts and enabling him to
exploit downturns.

### *Building Standard Oil*
Rockefeller pursued **integration** — timber for barrels,
tank cars, warehouses, boats on the Hudson. Henry Flagler,
who had gone broke in salt and learned the value of
"cooperation," became his closest colleague. Their desks
sat back to back. Flagler devised transportation
arrangements extracting **rebates** and even **drawbacks**
— competitors unknowingly subsidized Standard Oil. "Few
business practices did as much to rouse public antipathy."

On **January 10, 1870**, Standard Oil was established —
the name chosen to indicate "a standard quality of product."
Rockefeller held a quarter of the stock, already controlling
a tenth of American refining.

### *"Now Try Our Plan"*
The **South Improvement Company** (1872) enraged the Oil
Regions. Three thousand men marched to the Titusville Opera
House to denounce "the Monster" and "the Forty Thieves."
By spring 1872, Rockefeller already controlled most of
Cleveland's refining. "Now try our plan." Standard operated
through secretly controlled firms, communicated in code
(Standard was "Morose"). Standard would "make him feel sick"
or give competitors "a good sweating." By **1879**, Standard
controlled **90 percent of refining capacity**.

### *The Trust*
The **Standard Oil Trust Agreement** (January 2, 1882)
formalized control — Rockefeller held 191,700 of 700,000
shares. Management by committee: Domestic Trade, Export Trade,
Manufacturing, Pipe Line, Lubricating, and the all-powerful
Executive Committee. Headquarters at **26 Broadway** (1885).
Senior executives lunched daily in a private dining room.
William Vanderbilt: "I never came into contact with any
class of men so smart and able."

Focus on being the **low-cost producer** never wavered.
A corporate intelligence system tracked virtually every
buyer in the country. Rockefeller's faith in oil: "We must
try and not lose our nerve when the market gets to the
bottom. We will surely make a great mistake if we do
not buy."

### *"The Wise Old Owl"*
Rockefeller recited: "A wise old owl lived in an oak, /
The more he saw the less he spoke." He maintained curious
frugality — once billing a guest $600 for a summer stay.
He loved fast horses. He played "Buzz" with his children
and could never get past 71 despite his gift for numbers.
He gave away $550 million, founded the University of
Chicago, and insisted buildings not bear his name.

### *Lima Oil Gamble*
The **Lima-Indiana fields** opened in the mid-1880s —
sulfurous "skunk juice." Rockefeller overruled "conservative
brethren" and stored 40 million barrels. **Herman Frasch**
solved the deodorizing problem; the price doubled immediately.
Rockefeller ordered **"Buy all we can get."** By 1891,
Standard produced a quarter of America's output. By 1895,
the Joseph Seep Agency effectively determined crude prices.

Standard was both reviled as "the Octopus" and admired as
an "upbuilder." Henry Demarest Lloyd's 1881 *Atlantic
Monthly* article "The Story of a Great Monopoly" went
through seven printings. Mothers warned: "Rockefeller will
get you if you don't mind."

## Chapter 3: Competitive Commerce (p75-100)

### *Russian Oil*
Robert Nobel took Ludwig's "walnut money" — 25,000 rubles
meant for rifle stocks — and bought a Baku refinery in
1873. Ludwig became "the Oil King of Baku," commissioning
the first bulk tanker (*Zoroaster*, 1878), hiring the first
professional petroleum geologist, and building a vertically
integrated company whose workers proudly called themselves
"Nobelites." Russian production reached **10.8 million
barrels** by 1884 — a third of American output. **Black
Town** had over two hundred refineries producing oil smoke
"like confinement in a chimney-pot."

The **Rothschilds** — Baron Alphonse (best moustaches on
the Continent) and Baron Edmond (sponsor of Jewish
settlement in Palestine) — financed the Baku-Batum
railroad, forming **Bnito** in 1886. America's share of
world exports fell from 78% (1888) to 71% (1891).

Ludwig Nobel died in 1888. Newspapers confused the brothers
and reported Alfred's death. Alfred, distressed by
obituaries condemning him as a "merchant of death,"
rewrote his will — the **Nobel Prizes**.

### *Marcus Samuel's Coup*
**Marcus Samuel**, son of a "shell merchant" who sold
knickknack boxes at Brighton, conceived a scheme to break
Standard's Asian kerosene grip. He needed: new tankers
capable of steam cleaning for return cargo; Suez Canal
access (Standard had been refused); storage tanks across
Asia; guaranteed Rothschild supply. Standard's solicitors
lobbied against his Suez passage, arguing Russian naval
officers would "undertake all kinds of mischief."

On **August 23, 1892**, the *Murex* (named for a seashell,
memorializing his father) passed through the Suez Canal.
The one oversight: customers wouldn't relinquish Standard's
blue tins — used for roofing, birdcages, opium cups. Samuel
sent tinplate and ordered manufacture of **red** receptacles.
"What color do you suggest?" cabled Shanghai. Mark answered:
"Red!" Red roofs replaced blue across Asia.

Samuel lacked organizational talent — "he ran everything
out of his hat." The brothers' decisions involved violent
quarrels: "calling each other fool, idiot, imbecile, until
suddenly they were in agreement again."

### *The Oil Wars of the 1890s*
Standard, the Rothschilds, and the Nobels battled while
simultaneously exploring mergers. A grand alliance signed
**March 14, 1895** — 75/25 American/Russian — never came
into force. Standard's attention turned to **Sumatra**
and **Royal Dutch**.

## Chapters 4-6: Royal Dutch, Shell, Deterding

### *Royal Dutch*
**Aeilko Jans Zijlker** discovered oil potential in Sumatra
in 1880 when a native overseer's bright torch caught his
attention. Royal Dutch launched 1890; Zijlker died in
Singapore before seeing success. **J.B.A. Kessler** found
chaos — men labored waist-deep in water, out of rice.
On February 28, 1892, oil arrived with "a roar as of a
mighty storm." **"To stagnate means to liquidate."**
Three hundred marauding pirates had to be overcome.

### *Henri Deterding and the Merger*
**Henri Deterding** rose from bank clerk to managing
director. Fred Lane resigned from Shell's board with a
devastating critique of Samuel: "Business like this cannot
be conducted by an occasional glance in one's spare time."
Deterding insisted on a 60-40 split. Samuel protested;
Deterding refused to compromise: "I am at present in a
generous mood. If you leave this room without accepting it,
the offer is off." Samuel accepted.

In **1907**, the **Royal Dutch/Shell Group** formed — 60%
Royal Dutch, 40% Shell. Three men ran it: Deterding
(business), Hugo Loudon (technical), Robert Waley Cohen
(commercial). Deterding sent **Mark Abrahams** to Tulsa
in 1912 — arriving with his own typewriter and $2,500 in
a money belt — to create **Roxana Petroleum**. "At last
we *are* in America!"

### *Russia in Turmoil*
Baku became a revolutionary hotbed. A secret printing
operation called **"Nina"** produced Lenin's *Iskra*. A
young Georgian seminarian named **Joseph Djugashvili** —
later **Stalin** — operated underground as "Koba." Baku
oil workers struck in 1903. The 1905 Revolution destroyed
**two-thirds of all Russian oil wells**. Stalin later:
"There in Baku I received my second baptism in
revolutionary combat."

### *Rothschilds Sell Out*
By 1912, the Rothschilds — weary of anti-Semitism, strikes,
and arson — sold to Royal Dutch/Shell, transforming uncertain
Russian assets into diversified international holdings. The
Group's portfolio: 53% East Indies, 17% Rumania, 29% Russia.

## Chapter 7: "Beer and Skittles" in Persia

### *The D'Arcy Concession*
**Antoine Kitabgi** sought a "capitalist of the highest
order" for a Persian oil concession. **William Knox D'Arcy**
— Australian gold millionaire with the only private box at
Epsom beside the royal box — was "an investor and speculator,
not a manager." On **May 28, 1901**, Shah Muzaffar al-Din
signed: £20,000 cash, £20,000 in shares, 16% of profits,
sixty years, three-quarters of the country.

### *George Reynolds*
**George Reynolds** directed exploration at inaccessible
Chiah Surkh — equipment shipped to Basra, up the Tigris,
then by mule. The "infernal heat" reached 120 degrees.
Workers' introductions: "even the wheelbarrow was a
startling innovation." D'Arcy telegraphed: "Delay serious.
Pray expedite." He spent over £200,000. "Every purse has
its limit."

### *Rescue by Patriots*
D'Arcy sought Admiralty help; the Chancellor refused.
**Lord Strathcona**, eighty-four, agreed to head a
"syndicate of patriots" — not for commerce "but really
from an imperial point of view." Partner **Burmah Oil**'s
vice-chairman visited D'Arcy's Epsom box; the rich food
made him sick for weeks. He never accepted another
invitation.

### *Discovery at Masjid-i-Suleiman*
Operations were "not all beer and skittles" — drinking
water was "water with dung in suspension," nearest dentist
1,500 miles away in Karachi. On **May 26, 1908**, at 4 AM,
a gusher rose fifty feet. Wilson sent coded news: "See
Psalm 104 verse 15 third sentence." Reynolds received
Burmah's shutdown letter — echoing Drake half a century
earlier. **Anglo-Persian Oil Company** went public April
19, 1909; investors stood ten deep at the Bank of Scotland
counter. D'Arcy received shares worth £895,000 but
lamented: "I feel like signing away a child."

## Chapter 8: The Fateful Plunge

### *Fisher and Churchill*
**Admiral "Jacky" Fisher** — born in Ceylon, went to sea
at thirteen, "a mixture of Machiavelli and a child" —
dominated the Royal Navy. He told Churchill: "The first of
all necessities is SPEED." Oil offered four extra knots.
Fisher: "Your old women will have a nice time of it when
German Motor Battleships are cocking a snook at our
'Tortoises'!"

The three naval programs of 1912-14 — the greatest addition
in Royal Navy history — were all oil-based. The **Queen
Elizabeth class** was the "fateful plunge." Churchill:
"Mastery itself was the prize of the venture."

### *The Government Buys In*
**Charles Greenway** ("Champagne Charlie," "Old Spats and
Monocle") campaigned against Shell's "unwelcome embrace,"
stressing Samuel's "Jewishness" and Deterding's "Dutchness."
Churchill told Parliament on **June 17, 1914**: "If we
cannot get oil, we cannot get corn, cotton and a thousand
commodities necessary for the preservation of the economic
energies of Great Britain." The government invested **£2.2
million** for 51% of Anglo-Persian. The bill passed **254
to 18**. Samuel Samuel popped up three times to object;
Churchill: "He had better hear the case for the prosecution
before he offers an argument for the defense."

Fisher privately admired Deterding: "He is Napoleon and
Cromwell rolled into one. *He is the greatest man I ever
met.*" Deterding wrote patriotically to Churchill on July
31, 1914, promising oil and tankers. Eleven days after the
vote, Archduke Franz Ferdinand was assassinated.

## Chapter 9: Blood of Victory — WWI

### *Taxi Armada*
General Gallieni commandeered 3,000 Paris taxicabs for the
First Battle of the Marne (September 1914). "By the meter
or on a flat rate?" asked a driver. "By the meter." "All
right, let's go," putting down his flag. Troops rushed to
the critical point saved Paris.

### *Tanks and Transport*
Colonel Swinton conceived armored vehicles impervious to
machine guns and barbed wire. The high command ignored it;
Churchill funded development from Navy coffers. Various
code names were considered — "cistern," "reservoir" — it
became the **"tank."** First used prematurely at the Somme
(1916), decisive at **Amiens** (August 8, 1918): 456 tanks
broke through, Ludendorff's "black day of the German Army."
When the High Command declared victory impossible, the first
reason was the tank.

British vehicles grew from 827 motorcars (August 1914) to
**56,000 trucks, 23,000 cars, 34,000 motorcycles**. The U.S.
brought another 50,000 gasoline-driven vehicles. "The victory
of the Allies over Germany was in some ways the victory of
the truck over the locomotive."

### *War in the Air*
At war's outbreak, the British aviation industry numbered
a thousand people and had produced 250 planes. General Foch
dismissed aviation: "good sport, but for the Army worthless."
Yet by July 1915, every machine at the outbreak was obsolete.
Air combat evolved from handguns to synchronized machine guns.
Wartime production: Britain 55,000; France 68,000; Germany
48,000. Aircraft speeds doubled to over 120 mph.

### *Supply Crisis*
By 1917, unrestricted submarine warfare threatened Allied
oil. The *Murex* was sunk. Clemenceau appealed that gasoline
was "as vital as blood in the coming battles." The U.S.
supplied **80% of the Allies' wartime petroleum**. "Gasolineless
Sundays" were instituted; Wilson: "I suppose I must walk
to church."

### *Shell's TNT*
Marcus Samuel masterminded extracting **toluol** from Borneo
crude. Shell's Rotterdam plant was secretly dismantled at
night, each piece numbered and camouflaged, then re-erected
in Somerset. It provided **80% of British military TNT**.

### *Empire Jack*
**Norton-Griffiths** ("Empire Jack") destroyed seventy
Rumanian refineries and 800,000 tons of crude ahead of
the Germans, kicking challengers, pulling his revolver:
"I don't speak your blasted language." The destruction
"did materially reduce the oil supplies," Ludendorff
admitted.

### *"Floated to Victory"*
Germany's Air Force and mechanized warfare would have
ceased within two months by October 1918. Lord Curzon:
**"The Allied cause had floated to victory upon a wave
of oil."** Berenger: **"the blood of victory."**

## Chapter 10: The Turkish Petroleum Company

### *Mr. Five Percent*
**Calouste Gulbenkian** — Armenian, suspicious enough to
employ two sets of doctors — built the TPC. At age seven,
his first transaction: exchanging a silver piece for an
antique coin. He was drenched by a Baku gusher but never
visited oil country again. His motto: **"Never give up an
oil concession."** At a famous luncheon, Teagle called him
an "oil merchant." Gulbenkian reddened: **"I classify
myself as a business architect. I designed this Turkish
Petroleum Company... Now the three of you are trying to
throw me out on my ass."**

### *Baba Gurgur and the Red Line*
On **October 15, 1927**, a gusher at Baba Gurgur flowed
at **95,000 barrels/day** — near holes that had burned for
millennia (Nebuchadnezzar's "fiery furnace"). Gulbenkian
drew a thick red line on a map along the Ottoman Empire's
boundaries. The **Red Line Agreement** bound partners not
to operate independently within that territory. He had
never seen an oil tanker.

### *Postwar Oil Diplomacy*
Clemenceau traded Mosul for British support in Syria — a
casual exchange neither Prime Minister bothered to share
with his foreign minister. The **San Remo Agreement** (1920)
gave France 25% of Mesopotamian oil. The news stunned
Washington — Standard Oil of New Jersey protested, the
press denounced it as imperialism violating the Open Door.
Congress passed the **Mineral Leasing Act of 1920**,
denying drilling rights to foreigners whose governments
denied reciprocal access. The Wilson Administration, once
the embodiment of progressivism, now gave full support to
the oil companies.

Colonel Mercier created the **Compagnie Francaise des
Petroles** (1924), though "none of the initial investors
begged for the favor." Ninety reluctant banks and
companies were assembled.

### *Walter Teagle and the Open Door*
Commerce Secretary Hoover suggested a consortium; **Walter
Teagle** organized the Near East Development Company.
Teagle was second-generation oil — his maternal grandfather
was Maurice Clark, the partner Rockefeller bought out. At
Cornell, he scored a perfect hundred on a de-sulfurization
thesis; his father's telegram said only: "Come home at
once." He fired a still at nineteen cents an hour, then
became a formidable salesman: "He'd trade and trade.
If it was company money, he'd think he was paying too
much for a five cent cigar." In 1917, at thirty-nine,
he became president of Jersey Standard.

### *Faisal of Iraq*
The British put **Faisal** on Iraq's throne — Lawrence
of Arabia described him as "an absolute ripper." He was
crowned in August 1921, having been deposed from Syria
and found sitting on his luggage at a Palestine railway
station. His brother Abdullah got "the vacant lot" of
Transjordan. Iraq was a collection of Shia Arabs, Sunni
Arabs, Jews, Kurds, and Yazidis — Jews were the largest
single group in Baghdad. U.S. motor vehicles had jumped
from **1.8 to 9.2 million** between 1914 and 1920.

## Chapter 11: The Age of Gasoline

### *Motorization*
Captain Eisenhower's 1919 convoy averaged five and two-thirds
mph — "Through Darkest America with Truck and Tank." Roads
varied "from average to nonexistent." Some days, counting on
sixty miles, they did three. As President, he championed the
interstate highway system. By 1929, **23.1 million** autos
registered — **78% of the world's total** (vs. 702 per auto
in Japan, 6,130 in the Soviet Union). Total U.S. oil demand
rose from 1.03 million bbl/day (1919) to 2.58 million (1929).
Oil's share of energy rose from 10% to 25%. Gasoline stations
grew from 12,000 (1921) to **143,000** (1929).

Los Angeles was the incubator of the modern service station.
Shell pioneered the standardized "cracker box." Stations
sold "TBA" — tires, batteries, accessories. Shell required
free uniforms, no magazine reading on duty, and instructed
"Salesmen should be careful in their attendance upon Oriental
and Latin classes." Oil companies created "uniquely American
contributions to cartography" — the road map (Gulf started
in 1914).

Bruce Barton: "It is the juice of the fountain of eternal
youth that you are selling. It is health. It is comfort.
And you have sold merely a bad smelling liquid at so many
cents per gallon." "The 'new light' had given way to the
'new fuel.'"

### *Teapot Dome*
Secretary of the Interior **Albert Fall** — "the frontiersman"
with black Stetson — leased naval oil reserves to Sinclair
and Doheny. Teagle warned: "all through the industry it
smells." Harding: "if Albert Fall isn't an honest man, I'm
not fit to be President." Doheny delivered $100,000 in "a
little black bag." Fall became **the first Cabinet officer
convicted** for a felony in office. Sinclair stopped at a
board meeting on the way to jail. Doheny was acquitted: "You
can't convict a million dollars."

**Rockefeller Jr.** led a proxy fight to oust Standard of
Indiana's Colonel Stewart over Liberty Bond kickbacks,
winning with **60% of votes**. "Not for mere profits but
in the name of decency."

### *Geophysics*
The torsion balance, seismograph, magnetometer, aerial
surveillance, and micropaleontology transformed oil finding.
**Signal Hill** (1921) made California number one, producing
one quarter of world oil. Deepest wells went from 6,000
feet (1918) to **10,000** (1930).

## Chapter 12: The Fight for New Production

### *Harry Doherty and Conservation*
Doherty — "the professor" with oversized glasses — argued
the **rule of capture** must be eliminated. Flush production
exhausted underground pressure, leaving oil unrecoverable.
Fields should be **unitized**. The industry called him
"that crazy man" and barred him three times from API
meetings. Coolidge's Federal Oil Conservation Board
confirmed: dissipating gas left vast petroleum
unrecoverable. Doherty prophesied trouble, but J. Howard
Pew countered that rivers would change course before
petroleum was exhausted.

### *The Emerging Industry*
Standard successor companies invaded each other's
territories. Phillips opened its first station in 1927;
by 1930 it had **6,750 outlets**. Texaco and Shell marketed
in all forty-eight states. Ida Tarbell wrote with
astonishment: "It certainly looks as if the Standard Oil
Company might be crumbling — crumbling within."

### *Mexico's Golden Lane*
Lord Cowdray's **Potrero del Llano 4** flowed at **110,000
barrels/day**. Mexico became the world's second-largest
producer by 1921. The Revolution's **Article 27** restored
national subsoil ownership. Cardenas ordered preparations
to set oil fields on fire if America invaded. Production
fell 80% in a decade as Mexico lost competitiveness.

### *Venezuela*
**General Gomez**, cruel dictator who fathered **97
illegitimate children**, turned Venezuela into a personal
hacienda but offered political predictability. Shell's
**Barroso well** (December 1922) blew out at **100,000
barrels/day** — discovered by George Reynolds, the same
man who found oil in Persia. Jersey's 4,200 underwater
acres in Lake Maracaibo were considered a joke — "the
company could go into the fishing business."

### *The Russian Question*
The **Front Uni** (1922) pledged to fight the Soviets
together. Deterding launched a price war when Socony
dealt with Moscow. Teagle: "The idea of trying to be on
friendly terms with the man who burglarizes your house
has never appealed to me." Deterding's anti-communism
intensified after marrying White Russian **Lydia Pavlova**.

## Chapter 13: The Flood — East Texas

### *Dad Joiner*
**Columbus "Dad" Joiner**, seventy, drilled with rusted
equipment on Daisy Bradford's farm, guided by "Doc Lloyd"
— a 300-pound fake geologist whose only real contribution
was telling Joiner where to drill. Workers were paid in
"royalty rights." A Texaco geologist: "I'll drink every
barrel of oil you get out of that hole." On **October 3,
1930**, oil shot above the derrick. The crowd went mad;
a crewman fired a pistol into the spray — tackled before
a spark could cause an explosion.

The **East Texas field** — forty-five miles long, 140,000
acres — was the largest ever in the lower forty-eight.
Prices collapsed to **six cents, even two cents** a barrel.
"Teakettle" refineries offered free chicken dinners with
each fill-up.

### *H. L. Hunt*
**H. L. Hunt** — stout, maintaining two wives with growing
families — acquired Joiner's rights over a 36-hour
negotiation at the Baker Hotel, armed with secret intelligence
on a nearby well. He paid $1.33 million. Joiner died at 87,
still wildcatting, net worth barely more than his car.

### *Anarchy and Martial Law*
Oklahoma's "Alfalfa Bill" Murray declared martial law.
Texas Governor Sterling sent **National Guard and Rangers**
to "Proration Hill." Even chickens, which had feasted on
gas-flare insects, "returned to scratching for worms."
The Railroad Commission issued **nineteen proration orders
in 1932**, each declared invalid.

### *Hot Oil and Federal Intervention*
**"Hot oil"** — production above quotas — reached half a
million barrels daily, smuggled through secret pipelines
at night, hidden in camouflaged tanks covered with weeds,
smoothed by graft. Origin of the term: a militiaman
shivering on a chilly night was told by a suspect to lean
against a tank — "It's hot enough to keep you warm."

**Harold Ickes** became Oil Administrator — "a plump,
blond, bespectacled gentleman" with "powerful liberal
convictions, stinging polemics, and immense self-
righteousness." Raised by a stern Calvinist mother,
not permitted to whistle on Sunday. He kept an internal
investigating unit using wiretaps. After Harry Sinclair
visited in 1933, Ickes wrote: "I kept wondering whether
the ghost of Albert B. Fall, carrying a little black
satchel, might not emerge from one of the gloomy corners."

On **May 5, 1933**, entering a Cabinet meeting, Ickes
received a telegram: East Texas at **four cents a barrel**.
On **September 2, 1933**, he set monthly state quotas —
**the rule of capture was overturned**. "What had made
sense for deer and game birds on estates in medieval
England would no longer do." The **Connally Hot Oil Act**
and Interstate Oil Compact stabilized the system. An oil
tariff of twenty-one cents/barrel provided protection.
From 1934-1940, oil averaged **$1.00-$1.18/barrel**.
The magical "dollar a barrel" had been realized.

## Chapter 14: "Friends" and Enemies

### *Achnacarry*
In August 1928, Deterding rented a Scottish castle with
50,000 acres of shooting. Teagle, Cadman, and others
joined for "grouse shooting" — "it was lousy," Teagle
said, but they were not after grouse. The **"As-Is"
Agreement**: each company got a quota based on its 1928
market share. The British government had given Anglo-Persian
a mandate to seek such arrangements — "Such a policy will
in the long run be more in the interest of the consumer
than cutthroat competition."

The agreement was undermined by "fringe" players and
uncontrollable production. Companies called each other
**"friends"** while deeply distrusting one another.
"Friends in London say..." The domestic U.S. market was
explicitly excluded to avoid antitrust. The "As-Is"
effectively ended by September 1939.

### *European Nationalism*
Throughout 1930s Europe, governments imposed import quotas,
set prices, restricted foreign exchange, forced blending of
alcohol from surplus crops into motor fuel, blocked profit
remittances, and compelled foreign companies to participate
in national cartels. In France, government allocated specific
market shares to each company. In Nazi Germany, regulations
mounted as the regime geared for war. A Jersey executive
warned: "failure to cooperate with a national commercial
endeavor invariably provoked retaliatory legislation which
was more costly." An observer: "Operations in the oil
business in Europe are **90 percent political and 10
percent oil**."

### *The Shah's Cancellation*
**Reza Shah** canceled Anglo-Persian's concession on
**November 16, 1932**. He had personally beaten an
ayatollah. Cadman flew to Tehran and had his pilot taxi
visibly from palace windows. The Shah retreated: concession
area reduced by three-quarters, extended to **1993**,
minimum £750,000/year guaranteed.

### *Mexican Expropriation*
**General Lazaro Cardenas** — "the long, mask-like face and
inscrutable, obsidian eyes of the Indian," son of an
herbalist, a general by twenty-five — viewed foreign oil
as a painful presence. Deterding "was incapable of
conceiving of Mexico as anything but a Colonial Government
to which you simply dictated orders." In 1937, Bolivia
confiscated Standard Oil's subsidiary to great applause.
Mexico's oil workers struck; a review commission described
company officials as "men without respect unaccustomed to
speaking the truth."

On **March 18, 1938**, at 9:45 PM, Cardenas signed the
expropriation order from the Yellow Room and broadcast it.
A **six-hour parade** followed through Mexico City.
**Petroleos Mexicanos (Pemex)** was established. Valuations
ranged wildly: Mexico said $7 million, companies $408
million. A creative commission found 90% of reserves had
already been produced — settlement: **$30 million**. Shell's
British stockholders eventually got **$130 million**.

Nazi Germany became Mexico's number-one petroleum customer.
Japanese companies explored for Mexican oil. Companies called
it the biggest trauma since the Bolshevik Revolution. Mexico
established a model for the future — Pemex became one of
the first important state-owned oil companies in the world.

## Chapter 15: Arabian Concessions

### *Major Frank Holmes — "Abu al-Naft"*
On a beef-buying trip to Ethiopia in 1918, Holmes first
heard of Arabian oil seepages. Anglo-Persian's directors
declared Arabia "devoid of all prospects" (Albania was
the play). Holmes won Bahrain and Saudi concessions;
people "ran when they saw him coming" in London.

### *Bahrain Discovery*
Gulf joined the TPC and became bound by the Red Line,
so it brought the Bahrain concession to **Standard of
California (Socal)**, which was not bound. Both Socal
(in Bahrain) and Gulf (in Kuwait) then faced British
opposition — the "nationality clause" excluded Americans.
After prolonged diplomatic pressure, Britain backed off:
American capital would encourage rapid development and
reduce need for British subsidies to local rulers.

On **May 31, 1932**, oil was struck in Bahrain — the
first petroleum discovery in the Arab world. Holmes's
instincts were vindicated. After all, Bahrain was only
twenty miles from the Arabian mainland, where the
geology was exactly the same.

### *Ibn Saud and the Saudi Concession*
**Jack Philby** — eccentric father of double agent Kim —
told Ibn Saud he was "like folk asleep on buried treasure."
The King: "Oh, Philby, if anyone would offer me a million
pounds, I would give him all the concessions he wanted."
Philby served simultaneously as Socal's paid agent, Saudi
adviser, and IPC confidant — his $1,000/month funded Kim's
Cambridge fees, "where Kim was taking the first steps on
the way to becoming a Soviet spy."

Socal won on **May 29, 1933** — £35,000 in gold sovereigns,
all bearing **male monarchs** (not Queen Victoria). Five
years of failed wells followed. In **March 1938**, **Well
Number 7** struck oil at 4,727 feet. In April 1939, **Ibn
Saud himself turned the valve** for the first cargo.

### *Kuwait*
Anglo-Persian and Gulf formed the fifty-fifty **Kuwait Oil
Company**. On **February 23, 1938**, petroleum struck at the
**Burgan field** — crude ignited in sand, transforming the
walls into **sheets of glass**. The Sheikh's economy had
been devastated by Mikimoto's Japanese cultured pearls.

Holmes tapped his nose when asked what made him certain
of Arabian oil: **"This was my geologist."**

## Chapter 16: Japan's Road to War

### *The Manchurian Affair*
On September 18, 1931, the Japanese Army staged a bomb
attack on the South Manchurian Railway — damage was only
thirty-one inches of track, so negligible a train passed
minutes later. But it gave the pretext to seize Manchuria
— "Japan's life line." Prime Minister Hamaguchi, who
favored naval cooperation, was shot at a railway station
by an ultranationalist youth. "Government by assassination"
took hold.

### *Oil Dependency*
Japan produced only **7% of consumed oil**; **80% from the
U.S.**, 10% from Dutch East Indies. The Army and Navy split
over strategy: Army focused north (Manchuria, Soviet threat);
Navy on ***hokushu nanshin*** — "defend in the north, advance
to the south" toward the East Indies for oil. **60%** of the
internal market was held by Shell's Rising Sun and
Standard-Vacuum. The 1934 Petroleum Industry Law required
six months' inventories and aimed to reduce foreign
companies' role.

### *Hull and Nomura*
**Cordell Hull**, Tennessee backwoodsman turned statesman,
held perhaps **forty or fifty** secret nighttime meetings
with Ambassador **Nomura** at the Wardman Park Hotel. Hull
had a startling advantage: **"Magic"** had cracked the
Japanese diplomatic code "Purple." In May 1941, the
Germans informed Tokyo the Americans had broken their
codes — **the Japanese simply did not believe it**,
convinced Americans were not capable of such a feat.

### *Yamamoto's Gamble*
**Admiral Yamamoto** — who grew up in oil-producing
Niigata, spent four years in the U.S., read Lincoln
biographies, regularly received *Life* magazine —
restricted Navy training to coastal waters to conserve
petroleum. He even sponsored a "scientist" who claimed
to change water into oil. He scoffed at ultranationalists'
"armchair arguments about war." Yet: "It's out of the
question! To fight the United States is like fighting the
whole world. But it has been decided. So I will do my
best. Doubtless I will die." His plan: "decide the fate
of the war on the very first day."

### *The Embargo and Countdown*
Between June 25 and July 2, 1941, Tokyo made the fateful
decision: put off doing anything about the Soviet Union,
concentrate on the southern strategy — Indochina first,
then the East Indies. They knew this risked an American
embargo, "a matter of life or death to the empire."
Through **"Magic" intercepts**, Washington read Tokyo's
debate. "After the occupation of French Indochina, next
on our schedule is the Netherlands East Indies."

Roosevelt did not want a full embargo — he wanted it as
a flexible diplomatic tool. But **Dean Acheson** turned
the July 25 asset freeze into a de facto total embargo
by blocking release of frozen funds. "Whether or not we
had a policy, we had a state of affairs." Two Japanese
tankers sat empty in San Pedro harbor.

Ickes had pushed for full embargo; Roosevelt: "Please
let me know if this would continue to be your judgment
if this were to tip the delicate scales and cause Japan
to attack the Dutch East Indies." He told Ickes: "I
simply have not got enough Navy to go around."

Oil inventories would last **no more than two years**.
On September 5-6, the most senior Japanese officials met
with the Emperor. "Vital military supplies, including oil,
are dwindling day by day." The Emperor asked how long
hostilities would last. The Army Chief said three months.
The Emperor retorted sharply — the China Incident had been
promised in one month and lasted four years. **"The Pacific
was boundless."** The Emperor read a poem by Emperor Meiji:
"Since all are brothers in this world, Why is there such
constant turmoil?" The hall fell silent.

Prince Konoye proposed a summit with Roosevelt — even
willing to jettison the Axis alliance. He suggested
Honolulu. Roosevelt counter-proposed Juneau, Alaska, but
Hull opposed the meeting. Konoye fell; **Tojo** replaced
him: **"Two years from now we will have no petroleum for
military use. Ships will stop moving. I fear that we
would become a third-class nation."**

On November 22, a "Magic" intercept: American agreement
must be received by November 29 — "after that, things are
automatically going to happen." On November 26, a Japanese
naval task force sailed from the Kurile Islands under radio
silence. Hull told Stimson: **"I have washed my hands of
it."** Washington sent a final alert: **"This dispatch is
to be considered a war warning."**

### *Pearl Harbor*
On December 6, Roosevelt sent a personal message to the
Emperor: "This son of man has just sent his final message
to the Son of God." Aloft above the Japanese flagship flew
the flag from the 1905 Tsushima victory. Crews were told
they would **destroy America's ability to cheat Japan of
its deserved place on earth**.

On **December 7, 1941**, bombs fell at 7:55 AM. Hull
received a call from Roosevelt: "There's a report that
the Japanese have attacked Pearl Harbor." He could not
control his rage at Nomura: "infamous falsehoods and
distortions on a scale so huge that I never imagined any
Government on this planet was capable of uttering them."
No one opened the door for the diplomats; they were now
enemies.

Eight battleships sunk, capsized, or damaged. **2,335
servicemen killed.** The carriers survived only because
they were at sea. But Admiral Nagumo declined a third wave
targeting oil tanks and repair facilities. **Nimitz**: **"We
had about 4.5 million barrels of oil out there and all of
it was vulnerable to .50 caliber bullets. Had the Japanese
destroyed the oil, it would have prolonged the war another
two years."** Oil was central to Japan's decision for war —
**yet they forgot about oil when planning Operation
Hawaii**. Yamamoto himself had observed that Japan's 1904
surprise at Port Arthur wasn't "thoroughgoing" enough.
The same mistake was made again.

## Chapter 17: Germany's Formula for War

### *Synthetic Fuels*
In June 1932, I.G. Farben officials met Hitler, who spent
two and a half hours lecturing on motorization. "German
motor fuel must become a reality, even if this entails
sacrifices." I.G. Farben promised campaign contributions.
Standard Oil's Frank Howard visited Leuna in 1926: "the
most important which has ever faced the company since the
dissolution." Standard received patent rights; I.G. got
546,000 Standard shares ($35 million).

By 1940, synthetic output reached **72,000 barrels/day —
46% of total supply** and **95% of aviation gasoline**.
The Four-Year Plan ordered sixfold expansion; costs were
"of no importance." Mussolini confided to Hitler that an
oil embargo would have forced him from Abyssinia "within
a week."

### *Slave Labor*
I.G. Farben built plants adjacent to **Auschwitz**, paying
3-4 marks/day per slave to the SS. Half price for children.
Workers subsisted on 1,000 calories, worked months, then
died or were killed. **Primo Levi** (Prisoner 174,517):
"the only thing alive are machines and slaves — and the
former are more alive than the latter." Plants used more
electricity than all of Berlin. By 1944, **a third of
the synthetic fuel workforce** was slave labor.

### *Operation Barbarossa*
Many factors drove the Russian invasion: hatred of
Bolshevism, contempt for "little worm" Slavs, the drive
for *lebensraum*. But oil was central. "In the economic
field, Hitler's obsession was oil." Speer confirmed at
interrogation: "the need for oil certainly was a prime
motive." Ploesti provided **58% of Germany's imports in
1940** — and Soviet troops were dangerously close after
seizing northeastern Rumania.

**Directive Number 21 — Operation Barbarossa**: three
million men, 600,000 motor vehicles, 625,000 horses
struck on June 22, 1941. Stalin had refused all warnings
— ordering one defector shot as a trick. Vehicles burned
twice anticipated fuel on bad roads. In August, Hitler
overruled generals wanting Moscow: **"My generals know
nothing about the economic aspects of war."** The
Wehrmacht had to reach Baku. "The Volga will be our
Mississippi." They reached Moscow's outskirts but bogged
down in winter. Japan's Pearl Harbor decision allowed
Stalin to transfer crack Siberian divisions westward.

### *Operation Blau and Stalingrad*
In 1942, **Operation Blau** targeted Caucasus oil. A
Technical Oil Brigade of **15,000 men** was assembled
to rehabilitate captured fields. At Maikop, Russians
destroyed everything so thoroughly the Germans managed
only **seventy barrels/day**. Mountain troops planted
the swastika on Mount Elbrus but couldn't reach Baku.
The irony: **Germans ran short of oil in their quest for
oil**. Russian tanks ran on diesel, useless to German
panzers needing gasoline. In desperation, they
transported oil on the backs of **camels**.

Stalingrad was meant as a sideshow — its name made it
pregnant with symbolism. Guderian: "the shortage of fuel
was unbearable." Hitler told Manstein in a midnight call:
**"Unless we get the Baku oil, the war is lost."** He
then described armies meeting in the Middle East and
marching to India. The Sixth Army's tanks had fuel for
**twenty miles** — needing thirty to escape. They
surrendered February 1943 — Germany's first major defeat.

### *Rommel in North Africa*
Rommel was a brilliant master of mobile warfare,
scathing about quartermasters who urged caution. At one
point **85% of his transport** was captured British and
American vehicles. His forces raced 300 miles in one
week, reaching El Alamein — sixty miles from Alexandria.
Mussolini flew over with a white charger for his
triumphant Cairo entry. Rommel dreamed of Baku via
Palestine and Iraq.

But the very distance created vulnerability. Malta-based
Allies attacked Axis shipping. Supply trucks from Tripoli
used more gasoline getting to the front and back than
they carried. "My moves would be decided more by the
petrol gauge than by tactical requirements."

**Montgomery** — austere, ascetic, "I assure you I am
quite sane" — hung Rommel's drawing in his trailer.
At the Battle of Alam Haifa (September 1942), the
Afrika Korps was stopped dead by fuel shortage —
promised petrol never arrived from Italy. Montgomery's
counterattack at El Alamein (October 1942) caught the
Germans stunned. When four fuel ships were sunk at
Tobruk, Rommel lay awake all night. His Christmas
present: captured coffee in a **miniature oil barrel**.

Forced to take poison by the SS in October 1944.
His epitaph: **"The battle is fought and decided by
the Quartermasters before the shooting begins."**
**"Shortage of petrol! It's enough to make one weep."**

### *The Bombing Campaign*
On **May 12, 1944**, 935 bombers hit synthetic plants.
Speer: **"On that day the technological war was decided."**
By September 1944, aviation gasoline was **6% of earlier
levels**. Jet fighters sat without fuel. Pilot training
cut to **one hour weekly**. Galland: "This was actually
the fatal blow for the Luftwaffe!"

In the **Battle of the Bulge**, Peiper came within a
thousand feet of 2.5 million gallons at Stavelot but
didn't know it was there. He turned away. German trucks
were dragged by **oxen**. Hitler's body was doused in
the last available gasoline.

## Chapter 18: Japan's Achilles' Heel

### *Destruction of Balikpapan*
Shell men destroyed four decades of industrial creation
in less than a day — the tank farm became a hellish
conflagration, flames leaping 150 feet. Of seventy-five
stranded, only thirty-five survived.

### *"Victory Drunk" but Vulnerable*
By mid-March 1942, Japan controlled the East Indies.
Tojo bragged Hong Kong fell in eighteen days, Singapore
in seventy. A "victory fever" gripped Tokyo. But the
Japanese rehabilitated Indies oil fields with astonishing
speed — by 1943, output reached **49.6 million barrels,
75% of the 1940 level**. Using Caltex's abandoned rig,
they drilled a super giant field at Minas, Sumatra.

### *The Shipping War*
The fatal weakness was shipping. American submarines —
crewed by men the Japanese thought "too soft and
luxury-loving" — proved the best in the war. **86% of
merchant shipping** was sunk. Less than 2% of U.S. naval
personnel — the submariners — caused **55% of total
losses**. "Towards the end we were fairly certain a
tanker would be sunk shortly after departing from port."
Oil imports peaked in early 1943; by early 1945 they
ceased entirely.

Civilian gasoline in 1944: **4% of 1940 levels**.
Vehicles ran on charcoal or wood burners. Potatoes,
sugar, rice wine, and sake were requisitioned for
conversion to alcohol fuel.

### *Constraints on Combat*
In the **Marianas campaign** (June 1944), the carrier
force approached directly rather than circuitously to
conserve oil — the result was the **"Great Marianas
Turkey Shoot"** (273 Japanese planes lost vs. 29
American). The oil shortage forced the fleet to split
between Japan and Singapore, dividing strength at the
worst time.

Pilot training was cut to **thirty hours**, then
navigation eliminated — "pilots were simply to follow
their leaders." Aviation gasoline made from wood
turpentine mixed with alcohol. Japan lost **40% of
aircraft on ferrying operations alone**.

Japan's synthetic fuel effort failed miserably — **8%
of the 14-million-barrel target**. At the **Battle of
Leyte Gulf** (October 1944), Admiral Kurita was forty
miles from MacArthur's invasion beach but **pulled away
because of shortage of fuel** — the greatest naval
battle in history, a devastating Japanese defeat that
introduced *kamikaze* suicide pilots. The word meant
"divine wind"; one to three suicide planes could
achieve what eight bombers and sixteen fighters
otherwise required — and **fuel was cut in half**.

### *Kamikaze and Pine Roots*
The **pine root campaign**: "two hundred pine roots will
keep a plane in the air for an hour." Each gallon
required **2.5 man-days**. Only 3,000 barrels of
pine root gasoline were produced; **none was ever tried
in a plane**.

### *The Yamato*
The world's largest battleship — enough fuel for a
**one-way trip** — was sunk by 300 planes on April 7,
1945. Navy fuel oil: **0.8 million barrels**. Below a
million, the Navy could not operate.

### *The Yamato's End*
The Navy sacrificed the ***Yamato***, world's largest
battleship, on a suicide mission to Okinawa — enough
fuel for a **one-way trip**. "Any large-scale operation
requiring heavy supplies of fuel became almost out of
the question." Sunk by 300 planes on April 7, 1945.
Navy fuel oil: **0.8 million barrels** — below a million,
the Navy could not operate.

### *Surrender*
Japan's fuel oil inventories: **29.6 million barrels** in
April 1937; by July 1945, just **0.8 million**. The
government still pushed "100 million people united and
ready to die." The Americans expected **a million
military casualties** in a Home Islands invasion —
contributing to the decision to use the atomic bomb.

After Hiroshima (August 6) and Nagasaki (August 9),
the Army Chief still insisted suicide was the only
acceptable way out. Admiral Onishi advocated **20 million
Japanese sacrificing themselves** in suicide attacks.
Insurgent soldiers tried to capture the Emperor's
surrender recording the night of August 14. They failed.

Finding an **ambulance with gasoline** to save the
wounded Tojo proved harder than finding a doctor.
MacArthur's motorcade to Yokohama — powered by charcoal
— repeatedly broke down over twenty miles. Americans
found **316,000 barrels** hidden in caves for suicide
flights. Pine root gasoline tested in jeeps: **gummed up
engines beyond use**.

## Chapter 19: The Allies' War

### *Britain's Oil Regime*
The oil industry merged into the **Petroleum Board**,
pumps painted dark green, product sold as **"Pool."**
Rationing imposed immediately. Deterding had died in
Germany in 1939, having married his German secretary
and embraced Nazism — his preference shares were swiftly
distributed to prevent German control. Plans were made
to destroy British oil stocks if invaded.

### *America's Oil Czar*
**Harold Ickes** became Petroleum Coordinator. The *Oil
Weekly* warned against "a man who has no qualities nor
specific traits of ability." But Ickes chose oil man
**Ralph Davies** as deputy and proved pragmatic.

The U.S. had **one million barrels/day surplus capacity**
— a result of 1930s prorationing that turned out to be
an invaluable strategic margin. **Without it, the course
of WWII might well have been different.** "Old Dr. New
Deal had to call in his partner 'Dr. Win-the-War.'"
The Justice Department's 1940 antitrust case against
twenty-two major companies was quietly set aside.

Production rose from 3.7 million barrels/day (1940)
to **4.7 million** (1945) — a 30% increase calling on
the full reserve. The U.S. supplied **6 of the 7
billion barrels** consumed by the Allies — equivalent
to more than a quarter of all oil produced since
Drake's well.

### *Battle of the Atlantic*
U-boats devastated shipping — tankers were recognizable
by their distinctive profile. Admiral Doenitz: "Rescue
no one and take no one with you." In the first three
months of 1942, tanker sinkings were **four times the
number built**. Miami's neon beachfront provided perfect
silhouettes — hotel owners insisted the lights stay on
for tourist season. Crowds at Atlantic City watched
tankers explode on the dark horizon.

Churchill: "the blackest cloud which we had to face."
Ralph Davies reported England had only two months of
Royal Navy fuel vs. seven months' minimum. Ickes
launched conservation with windshield stickers — "I'm
using one-third less gas" — but couldn't explain the
real reason (U-boat intelligence), so Congress called
it a "phony shortage." In March 1943, 108 ships sunk.
"The Germans never came so near to disrupting
communication between the New World and the Old."

But in late March, the Allies broke U-boat codes, closed
their own ciphers, added support groups, improved radar,
and deployed long-range aircraft. In **May 1943, 30% of
U-boats at sea were lost**. Doenitz reported "the greatest
crisis in submarine warfare." After **forty-five months**,
the Battle of the Atlantic was over.

### *Big Inch*
**Big Inch** (1,254 miles) and **Little Inch** (1,475
miles) — extraordinary engineering feats — carried crude
and products from Texas to the East Coast. Pipelines went
from moving 4% to **42% of all oil** to the East Coast.

### *100-Octane*
Developed primarily by Shell researchers, 100-octane fuel
gave **15-30% more power**. The advantage was proved in the
**Battle of Britain** — Spitfires outperformed Messerschmitts
on 87-octane. **Catalytic cracking** — pioneered by
Frenchman Eugene Houdry and Sun Oil — made mass production
possible. Units were up to fifteen stories tall. Production
grew from under 40,000 barrels/day (1940) to **514,000**
(1945). "Squeezed it out of a hat."

### *Innovation and Supply*
Half the tonnage shipped from the U.S. was oil. When a
soldier went overseas, sixty-seven pounds of his supplies
were petroleum products. The "jerrycan" — copied from a
German five-gallon can with a built-in American pouring
spout — became ubiquitous. **PLUTO** (Pipeline Under the
Ocean) was designed to supply half of Allied fuel needs
from England to France — but averaged only **150
barrels/day**, one-sixth of one percent of consumption.

### *Rationing*
Nationwide gasoline rationing came through a side door —
**rubber**. Japan had cut off 90% of natural rubber. The
Baruch Committee recommended gasoline rationing to conserve
tires. The "basic" **A** sticker provided one and a half to
four gallons a week. A black market in coupons flourished.
Civilian use dropped **30%**. A thirty-five-mph speed limit
was imposed. "There's a war on."

### *Patton's Gasoline Crisis*
Patton's Third Army covered 500 miles in a month. His
troops hijacked trains and commandeered fuel convoys.
**"My chief difficulty is not the Germans but gasoline."**
On August 30, supply was cut to a tenth of normal.
Eisenhower directed fuel to Montgomery instead. August
31: tanks reached the Meuse — **gas tanks empty**. "My
men can eat their belts, but my tanks have gotta have gas."

The delay let Germans regroup. **Nine months of bitter
warfare followed.** Three-quarters of the million Allied
casualties occurred after this check. Liddell Hart:
**"The best chance of a quick finish was probably lost
when the 'gas' was turned off from Patton's tanks."**

Stalin toasted: **"This is a war of engines and octanes.
I drink to the American auto industry and the American
oil industry."** American forces used **one hundred times
more gasoline** in WWII than in WWI. A typical division:
4,000 horsepower in WWI, **187,000** in WWII.

The Army-Navy Petroleum Board pronounced: **"At no time
did the Services lack for oil in the proper quantities,
in the proper kinds and at the proper places."** Though
mostly true, there was one exception — the unforgiving
minute.
