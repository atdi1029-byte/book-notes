## Chapter 20: The New Center of Gravity

### DeGolyer's Mission to the Middle East

Everette DeGolyer — discoverer of Mexico's Golden Lane field,
founder of DeGolyer and McNaughton (world's leading petroleum
engineering consulting firm), millionaire several times over.
His interests ranged to Texas Instruments, chili history, and
the Saturday Review of Literature.

In 1943, given a wartime mission to appraise Middle Eastern
oil potential. He had prophesied in 1940: "No such galaxy of
fields of the first magnitude over such a wide area has been
developed previously in the history of the oil industry."
Getting there was arduous — military planes via Miami,
Caribbean, Brazil, Africa. He wrote his wife: "a pretty
barren land... Texas is a garden in comparison."

Three Saudi structures had 750 million barrels, but similar
structures suggested far more. For DeGolyer, the Peninsula
was "an El Dorado." He had discovered a well flowing 110,000
bpd — nothing on so vast a scale in half a century.

He reported proven reserves at about 25 billion barrels.
He suspected much larger — up to 300 billion for the region,
100 billion for Saudi Arabia. One mission member: "The oil in
this region is the greatest single prize in all history."

His key judgment: "The center of gravity of world oil
production is shifting from the Gulf-Caribbean area to the
Middle East." The US had produced 90% of Allied oil in
WWII — the high-water mark.

### "The Allies Have the Money"

In 1940, the Middle East produced less than 5% of world oil
vs. 63% for the US. The "hundred men" in Saudi Arabia planned
to fill wells with cement to deny them to Germany. Ibn Saud
faced financial crisis — pilgrim flow halted, drought. "The
Arabs have the religion, but the Allies have the money."

Roosevelt initially refused: "a little far afield for us."
The British provided about $2 million. American oil men tried
to convince Ibn Saud British aid was really American — "It
just came indirectly."

US discovery rates fell sharply. Ickes published "We're
Running Out of Oil!" (December 1943): "America's crown is
sliding down over one eye." Feis: "the pencil came to an awed
pause at one point — the Middle East."

### Suspicions, Solidification, and Government Plans

Americans feared the British would "diddle" them out of the
Saudi concession. In February 1943, Ickes lobbied Roosevelt:
Saudi Arabia was "probably the greatest and richest oil field
in the world." On February 18, Roosevelt authorized Lend
Lease to Ibn Saud.

Ickes aimed for 100% government ownership of Casoc. When the
company presidents came, Ickes proposed buying all of Casoc.
"They had gone fishing for a cod and had caught a whale." He
whittled to a one-third share for $40 million.

The rest of the industry rose up in "righteous and fiery
indignation." A $120 million government pipeline plan also
died — called "a move towards fascism." Lord Beaverbrook:
"Oil is the single greatest post-war asset remaining to us."

Roosevelt showed Ambassador Halifax a map: "Persian oil is
yours. We share Iraq and Kuwait. As for Saudi Arabian oil,
it's ours." Churchill: "A wrangle about oil would be a poor
prelude for the tremendous joint enterprise." The Anglo-
American Petroleum Agreement (August 1944) created production
quotas linked to Achnacarry. Both independents and majors
opposed it; it was withdrawn.

### The "Twins" — Roosevelt Meets Ibn Saud

After Yalta (February 1945), Roosevelt met Ibn Saud on the
USS Quincy. The King traveled with 48 retainers (originally
100 live sheep, reduced to 7), slept in a tent with Oriental
carpets. Roosevelt pushed the elevator's emergency button to
smoke two cigarettes between meetings.

The two bonded — Ibn Saud declared himself Roosevelt's
"twin." "You are luckier because you can still walk." "No,
your chair will take you wherever you want to go." Roosevelt
gave the King one of his wheelchairs.

Churchill's meeting clashed on customs — "my religion
prescribed smoking cigars and drinking alcohol as an absolute
sacred rite." He gave perfumes worth 100 pounds; the King
gave diamond-hilted swords and 3,000 pounds of jewels.
Churchill promised a Rolls-Royce.

### "What Do We Do Now?"

Roosevelt died April 12, 1945 before resolving oil issues.
The gloom about reserves was receding. J. Edgar Pew assured
Senators: "Of that I am as sure as I am that the sun shall
rise and set tomorrow."

Ickes resigned in a bitter scrap with Truman over the
appointment of Edwin Pauley — "the kind of letter sent by
a man who is sure he can have his way." But Truman was not
Roosevelt: he accepted "tersely and with alacrity and
delight." Ickes requested six weeks to wind up; Truman gave
him two days. The Oil Czar left office, denouncing Truman
for "lack of adherence to the strict truth."

Forrestal championed Middle East oil: "The prestige of the
United States is in part related to the wealth of the
Government in terms of oil resources." But he was a minority.
Claire Wilcox: "The Agreement is either dangerous or
useless." Even schoolteachers in Texas mobilized against it.
By 1947, the Anglo-American Petroleum Agreement was dead.
The special US-Saudi relationship was "an unlikely union —
Bedouin Arabs and Texas oil men, a traditional Islamic
autocracy allied with modern American capitalism."

## Chapter 21: The Postwar Petroleum Order

### The Postwar Oil Boom

Gasoline rationing lifted within 24 hours of Japan's
capitulation — "Fill 'er up!" By 1950: 40 million cars (up
from 26 million). Gasoline sales 42% higher. Oil meeting
more energy needs than coal. The pessimistic predictions
were eviscerated: proven US reserves 21% higher in 1950
than 1946.

But there was a shortage of available oil in 1947-48 —
crude prices more than doubled. More than twenty
Congressional investigations launched. Refineries needed
redesign for civilian products; steel was in short supply
worldwide; tankers broke in half at sea.

Oil companies became advocates of conservation — Standard
of Indiana urged motorists to avoid "jackrabbit" starts.
In 1948, imports exceeded exports for the first time.
The US was now dependent on other countries for the
marginal barrel.

### Aramco and the Great Oil Deals

Aramco needed capital and markets. Socal's investment: $80
million. Tapline: $100 million more. Saudi Arabia only two
decades old; Ibn Saud in his mid-sixties with 45 sons.
Socal's MacPherson called the concession "a gold mine."
Chairman Collier overruled: "much more gold" through Jersey's
system. Socal and Texaco received about $470 million for
selling 40%.

Jersey and Socony invoked "supervening illegality" to break
the Red Line Agreement. Gulbenkian: "We do not acquiesce."
At the Hotel Aviz in Lisbon, he became a tourist attraction,
carefully checking odometer readings. His tactic: "make
demands step by step." He called the IPC "as architecturally
designed as Raphael's School of Athens."

At the signing in November 1948, he found "one more point."
Only one bottle of champagne was drunk among twelve. He
signed at 1:30 AM. "Mr. Five Percent was no more."

Aramco finalized December 1948: Jersey 30%, Socony 10%.
Socony took only 10% — "it would not be very long before
Socony came to regret its parsimony."

### Kuwait, Iran, Marshall Plan, Tapline

Gulf found Shell for "shadow integration" of Kuwaiti oil.
Stalin on Iran: "We're not going to risk our oil supply."
Europe in 1946-47: the Thames froze, industry halted three
weeks. 20% of Marshall Plan aid went to oil. "The Englishman
began to realize there was no value in being cold."

Tapline finished September 1950 — 1,040 miles, replacing
7,200 via Suez. "Imported petroleum killed synthetic fuels.
And they would remain dead for three decades."

## Chapter 22: Fifty-Fifty: The New Deal in Oil

### The Fight Over Rents

Ricardo's concept: rents = market price minus production
cost. Middle East: 25 cents/barrel cost vs. $2.50 selling.
"A rich discovery means a dissatisfied landlord." For
producing countries, oil meant "power, influence, status —
all previously lacking."

### Venezuela's Fifty-Fifty

After Gomez died (1935), the "Generation of '28" reformed
oil regulation. Perez Alfonzo found the actual split was
60-40 in companies' favor and fixed it. He insisted on
royalties in kind: "The veil of mystery over oil marketing
was removed forever."

### The Neutral Zone and J. Paul Getty

The Neutral Zone — where "the erosion of the postwar
petroleum order began." Aminoil won Kuwait's share: $7.5
million cash and a million-dollar yacht.

Getty was "obsessed with value — bought it and never sold
it." Married five times. During the Depression, fired all
employees and rehired at lower salaries. Bought the Pierre
Hotel at a quarter of cost. After the Nazi seizure of
Austria, he entered Rothschild's home to look at furniture.

He won the Saudi Neutral Zone: $9.5 million up front, 55
cents/barrel. Geologist Paul Walton spotted a mound from a
DC-3 resembling Kuwait's Burgan field. Five dry holes.
Getty wandered Europe researching a Rembrandt, totting up
"newspaper — 10 centimes."

The sixth hole struck oil — "somewhere between colossal and
history-making." Fortune: richest man. "If you can count
your money, you don't have a billion dollars." Pay phone for
guests at his manor. Walton got $1,200.

Gulbenkian: "I fear the wind of nationalization may spread."

### The Saudi Fifty-Fifty Deal

A Venezuelan delegation translated documents into Arabic.
Betancourt's motive: "low-cost Middle East was a grave
threat to Venezuela." The Saudis found US tax take ($43
million) exceeded their royalties ($39 million).

The foreign tax credit: Saudi taxes deductible from Aramco's
US tax bill. December 30, 1950: fifty-fifty signed. By
1951: Saudi Arabia $110 million; US Treasury $6 million.
Jersey: "'50/50' is a good position which needs no defense."

## Chapter 23: "Old Mossy" and the Struggle for Iran

### The Young Shah and Anglo-Iranian

Mohammed Reza Pahlavi faced fundamentalists, communists,
poverty. Anglo-Iranian: L250 million profit vs. L90 million
to Iran (1945-50). Fraser — "obstinate, narrow old
skinflint" — resisted reform until the Saudi fifty-fifty
deal. Too late.

### Mossadegh and Nationalization

Mossadegh: "The source of all misfortunes is only the oil
company." PM Razmara assassinated entering a mosque. The
Majlis nationalized. Scores of sheep sacrificed.

Mossadegh — seventy, frail, completely bald — received
dignitaries in pajamas from bed. He collapsed; a doctor
checked his pulse. "Mossadegh opened one eye and winked."

### Harriman's Mission

Harriman found Mossadegh on his bed: "You do not know how
crafty they are." His grandson was at school. "Where?" "In
England, of course. Where else?" Walters ordered Alice in
Wonderland "as a guidebook."

"Nothing can be larger than the sum of its parts."
Mossadegh: "That is false. Consider the fox. His tail is
often much longer than he is." He laughed uproariously.
Kashani: "Do you understand?" Harriman: "I do not frighten
easily." Kashani: "There was no harm in trying."

### "Stand Firm, You Cads!" / Operation Ajax

British oil men staged a farewell revue. The guest house
lady interdicted a tank commander with her umbrella.
Passengers sang obscene versions of "Colonel Bogey."

Operation Ajax: first attempt failed; Shah fled to Rome.
Then "tumblers doing handsprings and wrestlers showing off
biceps." Mossadegh fled over his back wall. Eisenhower:
"more like a dime novel."

The consortium: Anglo-Iranian 40%, Shell 14%, five American
majors 8% each. Anglo-Iranian got ~$590 million. "A
wonderful deal for Fraser — Anglo-Iranian had nothing to
sell. It had already been nationalized."

## Chapter 24: The Suez Crisis

The Suez Canal — de Lesseps's handiwork (no engineer but a
diplomat; at sixty-four married a woman of twenty, fathered
twelve). Disraeli acquired Egypt's 44% in 1875: "You have
it, Madam." By 1955, petroleum was two-thirds of canal
traffic and two-thirds of Europe's oil passed through it.

Nasser — son of a post office clerk, a born plotter — was
"the model for the military officer turned fiery nationalist
leader." On July 26, 1956, he repeatedly heaped calumny on
"de Lesseps" — the code word. The army seized the canal.
Nasser then saw Cyd Charisse in Meet Me in Las Vegas. When
British and French pilots were withdrawn, the Egyptians
managed the canal with Soviet bloc assistance.

Eden told Soviet leaders: "I must be absolutely blunt about
the oil... we would fight for it. We had no intention of
being strangled to death." Macmillan read thousands of pages
of novels in the first two weeks: "I'd have gone barmy!"
Eden, Macmillan, Mollet were haunted by the 1930s — Nasser
was a resurrected Mussolini. Eden had resigned in 1938:
"Always a lap behind, the fatal lap."

Eisenhower believed force was neither warranted nor
justified. At Sevres (October 24), Britain, France, and
Israel agreed on the secret plan. October 29: Israel
attacked. Eisenhower was absolutely furious — he dumped
a diatribe on a hapless aide, mistaking him for Eden.

Nasser scuttled ships in the canal, choking off oil — the
very reason for the attack. Eisenhower refused emergency
supplies: "left to boil in their own oil." Macmillan: "Oil
sanctions! That finishes it." An immense run on the pound
started. The IMF refused emergency aid.

The Texas Railroad Commission barely increased production —
"England apparently still looks on us as a province." The
Oil Lift eventually swung into operation — a cooperative
venture redeploying tankers so the Western Hemisphere
again supplied Europe. Belgium banned private driving on
Sundays. Britain rationed gasoline — the "Suez sixpence."

Eden resigned January 1957 — "the last prime minister to
believe Britain was a great power and the first to confront
a crisis which proved she was not." Macmillan succeeded him,
reading Pride and Prejudice to soothe his soul. Supertankers
became a key consequence. In 1970, Eden prayed for "a lake
of oil" under the North Sea. "That was exactly what they
were beginning to do."

## Chapter 25: The Elephants

### The Era of Giant Discoveries

Free world crude: 8.7 million bpd (1948) to 42 million
(1972). US share slipped from 64% to 22%. Middle East grew
from 1.1 to 18.2 million — 1,500% increase. Proven reserves:
62 billion to 534 billion barrels — almost ninefold. Reserve
life: nineteen years (1950) to thirty-five years (1972) —
reserves found even faster than consumed.

### Enrico Mattei and ENI

Mattei — "a born condottiere," unruly son of a policeman,
left school at fourteen. He transformed AGIP into ENI,
a sprawling conglomerate of thirty-six subsidiaries. Credited
with coining "Sette Sorrelle" (Seven Sisters). He owned a
newspaper, subsidized politicians, financed the Christian
Democrats. "To deal with a government is like sucking
needles."

He came to Shell; Loudon asked how he got into oil. Mattei
talked nonstop through lunch. "When we went to dessert,
he asked for something. We couldn't do it, and that was the
end." He broke fifty-fifty with Iran — effectively 75-25.
"The fifty-fifty principle had been pierced."

Mattei died in a 1962 plane crash at fifty-six — "the most
important individual in Italy, more important than the
premier or the Pope." Without him, "ENI's buccaneer days
were over." But he had instigated a revolution that would
eventually overthrow the majors' dominance.

### Japan / Iraqi Revolution / Road to OPEC

Japan's Arabian Oil Company took only 44% of profits —
providing 15% of Japan's total supply by the mid-1960s.
The Shah to Standard of Indiana: "You know — we are not
Arabs. We are Aryans, and we have a great history."

July 1958: Iraq's monarchy overthrown in an explosion of
violence. King Faisal II beheaded. PM Nuri es-Said was
recognized fleeing disguised as a woman, lynched, his body
flattened by a car. Nasserism seemed destined to reign.

Perez Alfonzo — "iron determination, yet soft-spoken and
almost monastic." His family bought a 1950 Singer car; it
was shipped to Venezuela but sat rusting at the docks. The
engine burned out because nobody checked the oil. He
installed it in his garden as "a corroded, overgrown shrine
to the dangers of oil wealth."

Tariki — the "Red Sheikh," first American-educated Saudi
technocrat — was excluded from Texas bars because taken for
a Mexican. Journalist Wanda Jablonski — who had wrangled
an evening in King Saud's harem — introduced them at the
1959 Cairo congress. They met secretly at a yacht club in
Maadi: "a James Bond atmosphere." "She had just been the
matchmaker for OPEC."

## Chapter 26: OPEC and the Surge Pot

### The Second Price Cut — Jersey's Debate

In July 1960, Jersey's board debated the posted price.
Chairman Rathbone — "an engineer with a T Square" whose
entire career was domestic — was determined to cut. Howard
Page vigorously objected: "You can't just be guided by
market forces in an industry so essential to governments."
Wanda Jablonski briefed the board on Nasser-worship and
anti-Western hostility. Rathbone dismissed Page as "a
know-it-all." On August 9, Jersey cut prices up to 14
cents. Loudon: "the fatal move."

### The Founding of OPEC

Within hours, Tariki telegraphed Perez Alfonzo. Iraq
recognized an opportunity — by using the price cut to
establish an organization including non-Arab countries,
they could isolate oil policy from Nasser. On September 14,
1960: OPEC founded in Baghdad by Saudi Arabia, Venezuela,
Kuwait, Iraq, and Iran. Perez Alfonzo held the telegram
aloft: "We've done it!"

Companies dismissed OPEC. "We attached little importance
to it," said Page. A CIA report gave four lines. The Shah
called prorationing "nice in theory but unrealistic." Tariki
was fired 1962. Perez Alfonzo resigned 1963, calling
petroleum "the excrement of the devil."

### Libya and the New Competitors

Libya's 1955 law gave many small concessions to ensure it
wasn't "in the hands of one oil company." Jersey struck
Zelten 1959: "Libya has hit the jack-pot." Libyan crude
was "sweet" (low-sulfur), perfectly located across the
Mediterranean. By 1969, output exceeded Saudi Arabia's.
Corruption pervaded — "nickeled and dimed to death."
Prices fell 22% (1960-69).

Eisenhower imposed import quotas 1959 — the "Brownsville
U-Turn": oil trucked to Mexico around a traffic circle,
back to Texas as "overland." Companies in the Middle East
grew from 9 to 81 (1948-72). Returns fell from 20%+ to
11-13%.

### The Surge Pot

The Iran-Saudi rivalry dominated the 1960s — the Gulf
became the "swing area" or "surge pot." Page refused
Oman: "I am absolutely sure we don't want oil because we
are liable to lose the Aramco concession." Between 1957
and 1970, Iranian production grew 387% vs. Saudi 258%.

## Chapter 27: Hydrocarbon Man

### The Explosion of Oil Consumption

"If it can be said that the sun energized the planet, it was
oil that now powered its human population." Total world energy
consumption tripled (1949-72). Oil demand increased five and
a half times. US consumption tripled (5.8 to 16.4 million
bpd). Western Europe: fifteen times. Japan: 137 times over.
Motor vehicles outside the US: 18.9 to 161 million.

The petrochemical industry — The Graduate (1967): "Plastics."
American automobiles grew longer and wider, getting all of
eight miles per gallon. Gasoline stations sprouted everywhere.
Credit cards tied customers to companies. Texaco sponsored
Milton Berle: "Trust Your Car to the Man Who Wears the Star."

### Old King Coal Deposed

Coal could not withstand the tidal wave of petroleum. John L.
Lewis's combative strikes were "a powerful invitation to find
a substitute." London's "Killer Fogs" from coal prompted
smokeless zones. In Western Europe: coal from 75% (1955)
to 22% (1972); oil from 23% to 60%.

Japan in 1956: "We are no longer living in postwar
reconstruction." Oil went from 7% to 70% of Japan's energy.
In 1955, Japan produced 69,000 cars; by 1968, 4.1 million.
Alfred Chandler's recipe for the German and Japanese
miracles: "improved institutional arrangements and cheap oil."

## Chapter 28: The Hinge Years

### The Persepolis Celebration

October 1971: the Shah's $100-200 million bash at
Persepolis. Maxim's flew in 165 chefs and 25,000 bottles
of wine. Pompidou: "If I did go, they would probably make
me the head-waiter." The Shah: "We can hardly offer them
bread and radishes, can we?" He rode Princess Anne's
stallion named Cossack — the name kept from him.

### Anglo-American Retreat / End of Surplus

Britain withdrew from the Gulf by 1971 — "the end of a
century-old security system." Dubai: "Who asked them to
leave?" Bahrain: "Britain could do with another Winston
Churchill." The British position: only six thousand troops
at 12 million pounds/year. Some sheikhs offered to pay.

US oil production peaked at 11.3 million bpd in 1970.
Texas Railroad Commission: "Damned historic, and a sad
one. That old warrior can't rise anymore." Net imports
rose from 2.2 million (1967) to 6 million bpd (1973).
Placke warned from Tripoli: "Control of the flow of
resources has been of strategic concern throughout
history." No one in Washington paid attention.

### The Libyan Squeeze / Leapfrogging

Qaddafi's September 1969 coup: "People who have lived
without oil for 5,000 years can live without it again."
Jalloud — more fun-loving than the puritanical Qaddafi
but relentless — once threw a proposal back as a paper
ball, once entered with a submachine gun. He set his .45
in front of Hammer, who "had never before negotiated
across a gun barrel."

Occidental cut from 800,000 to 500,000 bpd. Hammer
couldn't get replacement oil — even Exxon's chairman
wouldn't help. "Here the President of Exxon was
confronted with an art dealer, a non-fraternity type."
Hammer commuted daily Tripoli-Paris (back 2 AM, off
6 AM), phoning his board from the Ritz.

Libya won a 30-cent posted price increase and 55%
profit share. The Shah then broke fifty-fifty. Companies
formed a "Front Uni" with an antitrust waiver. The
Tehran Agreement (February 1971): "the real turning
point for OPEC." Barran: "the buyer's market for oil
is over." Libya followed with 90 cents more.

### Participation

"Participation" — exporting countries acquiring partial
ownership — was consciously devised as an alternative to
outright nationalization. Yamani: "a bond that would be
indissoluble, like a Catholic marriage." Ed Guinn of
Bunker Hunt: "Two skeletons in a closet. One said, 'How
did we get here?' The other: 'If we had any guts we'd
get out.'" Qaddafi nationalized 51% of all Libya companies
— standing with Idi Amin to announce "a big hard blow on
America's cold insolent face."

The Shah: "Seventy-two years of foreign control was
ended." By 1973, surplus "available" production capacity
was just 500,000 bpd — 1% of consumption. A 99% 
utilization rate was extraordinarily precarious.

## Chapter 29: The Oil Weapon

### The US Joins the World Market

Nixon abolished quotas April 1973 — domestic production
could no longer keep up. He immediately imposed "voluntary"
allocations: quotas managed surplus; allocations distributed
shortage. James Akins published "The Oil Crisis: This Time
the Wolf Is Here." Ehrlichman: "Conservation is not the
Republican ethic." By summer 1973, US imports: 6.2 million
bpd (double 1970). "Near-panic buying by U.S. and European
independents as well as the Japanese."

### Sadat's Gamble / Faisal Changes His Mind

Sadat concluded war was the only way to break the stalemate.
Kissinger: "What literally no one understood was the mind
of the man." His purpose was "psychological and diplomatic,
much more than military." He shared his plan with Faisal —
meaning oil would be central.

Faisal had rejected the oil weapon in 1972: "Politics and
oil should not be mixed." Saudi Arabia's 1967 oil embargo
availed nothing except loss of revenues. But by 1973 he
changed — Saudi Arabia was the swing producer, at 8.4
million bpd (62% above 1972). He warned Aramco: "You will
lose everything." Washington: "a large degree of disbelief."
Three Aramco companies publicly called for a policy change.

In June, Brezhnev demanded an unscheduled late-night meeting
with Nixon at San Clemente — the Middle East was explosive.
Nixon and Kissinger dismissed it.

### The October War

October 6 — Yom Kippur: 222 Egyptian jets, 3,000 field
guns, simultaneous Syrian attack. CIA on October 5:
"military preparations do not indicate that any party
intends to initiate hostilities." Israel's "conception"
precluded an Egyptian attack. The surprise was for Israel
what Pearl Harbor had been for America.

Israel was in deep trouble — Dayan: "the Third Temple is
going under." Meir prepared a secret letter to Nixon.
On October 12, two private letters to Nixon: one from the
four Aramco chairmen warning about "a snowballing effect";
one from Golda Meir — survival hung in the balance.

Crosswinds at Lajes delayed the C-5As half a day — they
arrived in daylight, immense white stars visible. Instead
of keeping a low profile, the US was now an active ally.

### The Oil Weapon Unsheathed

October 16: Gulf states raised the posted price by 70% to
$5.11 — unilaterally. Yamani: "This is a moment for which
I have been waiting a long time. We are masters of our own
commodity." The pretense of negotiating was over.

On October 17, the Arab oil ministers agreed to cut
production 5% monthly, with total halt to the US.
On October 20: Saudi Arabia cut off all shipments. Same
day: Saturday Night Massacre. Brezhnev threatened
unilateral Soviet intervention; US forces went to DefCon 3
— worldwide nuclear alert. "The three-decade-old postwar
petroleum order had died its final death."

## Chapter 30: "Bidding for Our Life"

### Panic at the Pump

Net December loss: 4.4 million bpd — 14% of traded oil.
Ingredients for panic: war, cutbacks, embargoes, the specter
of permanent shortage. Nigerian oil sold for over $16;
Iranian auction bids reached $17. A Japanese trading company
bid $22.60. "We weren't bidding just for oil. We were
bidding for our life."

In Japan: toilet paper hoarding. In the US: stations with
"Sorry, No Gas Today" signs. The allocation system
perversely prevented shifting gasoline to where it was
needed. Americans' confidence in the future was severely
undermined.

### "Equal Misery" / Britain's Demand

Companies adopted "equal suffering" — diverting a third of
their oil. BP's Drake refused PM Heath's demand for special
treatment: "Are you asking me as a shareholder or as a
government? If government, put it in writing." "You know
perfectly well I can't." "Then I won't do it."

Having lived through one nationalization in Iran, Drake
was not about to be party to any more. Whitehall's civil
servants were more deft — they reminded companies that
Britain decided who got North Sea licenses.

### A New World of Prices

In December 1973, the Shah pushed through $11.65 — a
fourfold increase since the war. He called it "kindness
and generosity." Saudi Arabia wanted $8 — afraid a
skyrocketing price could cause depression. The Shah
lectured: "The era of their terrific progress based on
cheap oil is finished."

Nixon on November 7: "Project Independence — by the end
of this decade we will meet our own energy needs." His
staff said impossible. He fired energy czar Love, replaced
him with Simon — likening the post to Albert Speer.
Simon at hearings: "blood oozing from the gash."

### Sheathing the Oil Weapon

Kissinger's shuttle diplomacy: an Egyptian-Israeli
disengagement in January 1974, Syrian-Israeli in May.
Sadat: "The United States holds 99 percent of the cards"
(though privately: only 60%). Japan endorsed the Arab
position — its first foreign policy split with the US.
The IEA was created. France's Jobert: "Bonjour les
traitres" and called the IEA "a machine de guerre."

The embargo ended March 18, 1974. Kissinger walked through
a tremendous hall where men in black robes sat against the
walls, reflecting "what strange twists of fate had caused a
refugee from Nazi persecution to wind up in Arabia."

## Chapter 31: OPEC's Imperium

### Oil and the World Economy

Petroleum earnings rose from $23 billion (1972) to $140
billion (1977). The exporters embarked on a dizzying spending
program — ports clogged, ships waited weeks, vendors scrambled
for hotel rooms. Arms transactions became huge. A Datsun
cheaper to fuel than a camel.

US GNP plunged 6% (1973-75); unemployment doubled to 9%.
Japan's GNP declined for the first time since WWII. The
"OPEC tax" — a massive withdrawal of purchasing power. Non-oil
developing countries were the hardest hit — a new category,
the "fourth world," was invented. By 1978, OPEC's $67 billion
surplus had become a $2 billion deficit.

### The Saudis Versus the Shah

The Shah saw December 1973 as his moment — revenues to
fulfill "Iran's Great Civilization." "Everything you can dream
of can be achieved here." The Empress: "One of the only things
my husband likes in life is speed!" He applied his passion
to hurtle Iran into the twenty-first century, ignoring
disorientation and resentment.

The Saudis never approved the December 1973 increase — too
large, too dangerous. Higher prices could trigger conservation
and alternatives, diminishing the value of their vast
reserves. Yamani warned: "If the Shah departs, we could have
a violent, anti-American regime in Teheran."

From 1974 to 1978: two small increases ($10.84 to $12.70).
Real price eroded ~10%. By 1977, the Shah shifted — "We
acquired money we could not spend." Iran joined Saudi
moderation.

### Yamani — The Symbol of the New Age

Yamani — born Mecca 1930, grandfather a grand mufti. NYU and
Harvard Law. Oil minister at 32. "Give me Tariki's rantings.
Yamani drives you to the wall with sweet reasonableness."
His voice was soft, forcing adversaries to strain. The
angrier, the quieter. "Once you start thinking short term,
you are in trouble."

Kissinger: "watchful eyes and little Van Dyke beard made him
look like a priggish young don... He became the technician
par excellence." Yamani never gambled: "I hate it. It rots
the soul." In oil politics: "It's always a calculated risk."

### Faisal's Assassination / End of Concessions

March 1975: Faisal assassinated by his nephew. December 1975:
"Carlos" attacked OPEC Vienna. Yamani spent 44 hours with
worry beads, convinced he would die.

Kuwait took 100%: "thank you very much and good bye." Gulf
and BP asked $2 billion; got $50 million. Goodman of Gulf:
"It was the ebbing of American power — the Romans retreating
from Hadrian's Wall." When he reminded Kuwait of all Gulf
had done, the Kuwaiti became angry: "Whatever you did, you
got paid for. You never did us any favors."

Venezuela nationalized January 1, 1976, establishing PDVSA.
"We couldn't win," said Exxon. Companies negotiated service
contracts and supply deals — Exxon signed for 900,000 bpd.
Saudi Arabia took 100% of Aramco — agreed 1976 in Yamani's
suite at the Al-Yamama Hotel, not signed until 1990.
43 years after Standard of California's $175,000 payment,
proven reserves were 149 billion barrels. "It was truly
the end of the era."

## Chapter 32: The Adjustment

### Japan / France

Japan: "Instead of using the resources in the ground, we
would use the resources in our head." MITI curtailed
elevator service. The "energy conservation look" — short-
sleeve jackets — never caught on. "The Japanese people
are accustomed to crises like earthquakes."

France: Pompidou, dying, confirmed nuclear, coal, and
conservation. Inspectors measured temperatures — fines
for exceeding twenty degrees. Exxon's tiger was tamed —
no longer in the tank, advising motorists to check tires.
Gasoline stations couldn't give away trinkets. Total tried
a billboard: "This is France." Banned. The explanation:
"Consumers say, 'companies must be rich, therefore there
must not be any energy problem.'"

### "Obscene Profits"

Senator Jackson's hearings: oil executives lined up under
television lights. "They came across as inept, insulated,
self-satisfied." When an Exxon VP couldn't recall the
dividend, Jackson called him "childish." Exxon 1973
earnings up 59%. "I am not embarrassed," said chairman
Jamieson. Many thought otherwise. Companies became among
the most unpopular institutions in America. Depletion
allowance and foreign tax credit curtailed. 45 of 100
Senators voted for "divestiture" — the industry called
it "dismemberment."

### Ford and Carter

Ford's two key achievements: the Alaska pipeline ($10
billion) and fuel efficiency standards (13 to 27.5 mpg).
The strategic petroleum reserve was also established.

Carter: "the moral equivalent of war." Detractors: "Meow."
Schlesinger's priority: let domestic oil rise to world
prices. Carter: "I can't understand it." Schlesinger on
natural gas: "I understand now what Hell is." Carter:
"I said 90 days. I made the pledge and I intend to keep
it." "Three kinds of lies — lies, damn lies, and energy
lies."

### Alaska / Mexico / North Sea

Prudhoe Bay: 10 billion barrels — largest in North America.
Gulf had refused: "oil will never get to $5 a barrel in
our lifetime." The 800-mile pipeline completed 1977. In
Mexico, Lopez Portillo cautioned: "You can't eat more than
you can digest." Production surged from 500,000 to 1.9
million bpd. "Borrower of the year."

Phillips struck Ekofisk after "Don't drill any more wells."
Drilling superintendent: "What the astronauts have done is
great, but how about this?" Benn turned the first North
Sea valve sourly, noting "the international capitalist
establishment." Wilson hoped to be "OPEC chairman by 1980."

### Carter's New Year's Eve Toast

Carter toasted Shah December 31, 1977: "Iran is an island
of stability... a tribute to the respect and love which
your people give you." Shortly after: "The Shah is in
big trouble."

## Chapter 33: The Second Shock: The Great Panic

### Revolution in Iran

A January 7, 1978 newspaper article savaging Khomeini
triggered revolution. The "40-40" cycle: demonstrations,
deaths, 40-day mourning, more demonstrations. Oil workers
struck. In The Fields, workers occupied Osco's headquarters,
sleeping in corridors while Westerners stepped over them.

Production: 5.5 million bpd down to zero by Christmas.
Paul Grimm of Osco was shot dead driving to work. Oil men
evacuated — strictly limited to two suitcases. They shot
their dogs, as their predecessors had done leaving Abadan
in 1951.

Shah: "We are melting away daily like snow in water." A
telephone prank: told Senator Kennedy was calling, the Shah
picked up to hear: "Mohammed, abdicate." He left January 16
carrying a casket of Iranian soil. Khomeini returned on an
Air France 747 — seats sold to reporters to finance the
flight. The defense attache: "Army surrenders; Khomeini
wins. Destroying all classified."

### The Great Panic

The actual shortage was only 4-5% of world demand. But panic
drove prices from $13 to $34. Five factors: (1) apparent
demand growth, (2) disruption of contractual arrangements —
the real end of the classic integrated industry, (3)
contradictory government policies, (4) exporter opportunism,
(5) sheer emotion and fear.

BP lost 40% of its supply (Iran). It invoked force majeure,
cutting buyers. Exxon announced phasing out third-party
contracts: "The handwriting had been on the wall." Japan
was hit hard. The Japanese tried everything — one trading
house gave gloves to secretaries; another provided a
world-class acupuncturist for the Iraqi oil minister.

Motorists keeping tanks three-quarters full (instead of
one-quarter) sucked a billion gallons from stations. Panic
buying added 3 million bpd phantom "demand" — more than
doubling the actual shortage. Shell received a telex:
force majeure cutting supply. That afternoon: the same
volume offered at 50% higher on spot.

Three Mile Island raised doubts about nuclear power. Gas
lines returned in America — 150,000 barrels wasted daily
by cars idling. Carter went to Tokyo where Schmidt "got
personally abusive." On Thatcher: "a tough lady, highly
opinionated." Eizenstat: "the worst of times." Approval
plummeted to 25%.

At the Caracas OPEC meeting, Yamani warned for 11 hours:
"There will be a glut. It is coming." The Iranian: "In
the name of almighty God, there will be no surplus and
prices will not fall."

### The Embassy Seizure

November 4, 1979: militants seized the US embassy.
Elizabeth Ann Swift: "We're going down." 50 Americans
held for 444 days. In late November, fundamentalists
seized the Great Mosque in Mecca. In December, Soviets
invaded Afghanistan. Carter issued the Carter Doctrine.

In April 1980, Desert One rescue failed — a helicopter
collided with a C-130. The CIA director: "how vicious
the struggle for energy supplies will become." A Foreign
Affairs article: "Oil and the Decline of the West."

## Chapter 34: "We're Going Down"

### The Iraq-Iran War

September 22, 1980: Iraqi warplanes attacked a dozen Iranian
targets. Saddam Hussein — shaped by Tikrit's values of
"suspicion, stealth, surprise, and the unalloyed use of
force" — billed it as the "Second Battle of Qadisiyah."
His uncle published Three Whom God Should Not Have Invented:
Persians, Jews, and Flies.

Iran's "human wave" assaults: hundreds of thousands of young
people with plastic keys to heaven around their necks.
Children cleared minefields for the tanks. The Iraqis were
unprepared. The Arabs of Khuzistan showed no desire to be
"liberated."

The war removed 4 million daily barrels — 15% of OPEC
output. But inventories were brimming. The IEA urged
drawdowns. D'Avignon to the Japanese: "If you don't get
your trading companies under control, forget about Toyotas
and Sonys coming into Europe."

OPEC output in 1981 was 27% below 1979 — lowest since 1970.
"We engineered the glut," Yamani said. Saudi Arabia pushed
to its limit — an increase equivalent to a quarter of lost
production. October 1981: prices reunified at $34. It was
the last OPEC increase for a decade.

## Chapter 35: Just Another Commodity?

### The Greatest Boom

The greatest boom of them all. Young geology graduates
wined, dined, hired at $50,000/year. Dallas introduced
J.R. Ewing. Doctors and dentists poured money into drilling
funds. Exxon spent a billion on Colony Shale Oil in
Colorado. But by 1982 the outlook changed — Colony was
terminated. Colorado boomtowns became ghost towns, following
the tradition of Pithole, Pennsylvania. "My business just
died," said a shop owner.

### The Fundamentals

The Federal Reserve's prime rate reached 21.5%. The deepest
recession since the Depression. Oil demand fell 6 million
bpd (1979-83); non-OPEC production increased 4 million.
Conservation was massive: the US became 25% more energy
efficient by 1985; Japan 31%. If the US had stayed at 1973
efficiency, it would have used 13 million more barrels daily
in 1985. Oil's energy share dropped from 53% (1978) to 43%
(1985).

### OPEC Becomes a Cartel / The Commodity Market

In March 1982, OPEC set an output limit of 18 million bpd
with individual quotas — for the first time, a true cartel.
Saudi Arabia would adjust production to support the system.
In March 1983, OPEC cut prices from $34 to $29 — its first
price cut in history.

Nymex introduced crude oil futures March 30, 1983 — two
weeks after OPEC's London meeting. Founded 1872 as the
Butter and Cheese Exchange, its previous mainstay was Maine
potatoes. A senior executive dismissed futures "as a way
for dentists to lose money." WTI joined the gold price
and the Dow as vital measures. "Maine potatoes became a
distant, quaint, and embarrassing memory."

### Corporate Restructuring

T. Boone Pickens — Amarillo independent, former Phillips
trainee who quit because "you've got to learn to keep your
mouth shut" — exploited the "value gap." "It was cheaper
to explore for oil on the floor of the New York Stock
Exchange." Mukluk, Alaska: the most expensive dry hole
ever ($2 billion) — struck salt water: "simply 30 million
years too late."

Pickens targeted Gulf Oil — one of the Seven Sisters.
Chevron won for $13.2 billion. Jimmy Lee: "I never had any
intention but that Gulf would be here for ever and ever. It
had been my whole life." Pickens's group made $760 million.
Exxon spent $16 billion on share buy-backs and cut employees
by 40%. Restructuring propelled over $100 billion into
investor pockets.

### The Mexican Weekend / Continental Illinois

Mexico ran up $84 billion in debt based on oil wealth.
Silva Herzog told Treasury Secretary Regan: "No, Mr.
Secretary, we have a problem." At 3 AM, negotiations nearly
broke down over a hidden $100 million fee. "I thought of
my father and the oil expropriation of 1938."

Penn Square — a go-go energy bank whose lending officer
drank Amaretto from Gucci loafers — generated $2 billion in
bad loans sold to Continental Illinois. Continental suffered
the largest bank run in history. Energy lending
"instantaneously went out of fashion."

## Chapter 36: The Good Sweating

### Saudi Arabia's Impossible Position

Saudi earnings fell from $119 billion (1981) to $26 billion
(1985). Production fell to 2.2 million bpd — half its quota.
Exports to the US: 26,000 bpd — "essentially nothing." At
times Saudi output fell below the British North Sea sector.
Yamani on television: "At present we are suffering because
of the weakness of Arab political power based on oil."

In June 1985, King Fahd sharply criticized cheating: "If
Member countries feel they have a free hand to act, then
all should enjoy this situation and Saudi Arabia would
certainly secure its own interests."

### Netback Deals / The "Good Sweating"

The Saudis moved from defense of price to defense of
volume — using "netback deals" guaranteeing refiners a
predetermined profit. The refiner had no compulsion to
strive for higher prices. "Increasing volume and reduced
concern about selling price — a perfect recipe for falling
prices."

In November 1985, WTI peaked at $31.75. OPEC declared war
for market share. Then prices collapsed 70% — to $10. Some
Gulf cargoes: $6. The variation in OPEC output was marginal
— about 3% increase in total supply was enough to drive
prices to unimaginable levels.

Shell's planners had prepared an "Oil Collapse Scenario."
When it struck, "there was an eerie calm at Shell Centre."
In general, the industry responded with massive cuts in
expenditure. At Billy Jack Mason's Exxon station in Austin:
a promotion of zero cents/gallon — a six-mile line.

### George Bush's Saudi Trip

Bush: "I think it is essential that we talk about stability
and not just have a continued free fall like a parachutist
without a parachute." Disavowed by the Reagan White House:
"The way to address price stability is to let the free
market work."

At a late-night session with King Fahd (past 2 AM), the
subject was Gulf security. Bush stuck: "I know I'm correct.
Some things you're sure of." The Washington Post agreed:
"A steadily increasing dependence on imported oil is not a
happy prospect."

### Yamani's Fall

Yamani spoke at Harvard's 350th anniversary — asked how
Saudi oil policy was made: "We play it by ear." The audience
roared. The "play it by ear" remark was interpreted in
Riyadh as criticism. A month later, he learned he'd been
fired from a television broadcast. Twenty-four years —
"a good, long run in any position anywhere."

### $18 and the OPEC Tax Cut

In December 1986, OPEC agreed to a "reference price" of
$18/barrel. Parra recalled a 1926 book: producers were
committing "hara-kiri" by overproduction. The new prices
wiped out the Second Oil Shock increases. The "OPEC tax
cut" — $50 billion transferred to consumers in 1986 alone.

### Iran-Iraq War Ends

After eight years, Iran accepted a cease-fire (July 1988).
Khomeini: "Taking this decision was more deadly than taking
poison." The USS Vincennes had shot down an Iranian Airbus
(290 passengers). Iraq thought it had won — "the dominant
political power in the Gulf."

### Reintegration / Epilogue

State oil companies went downstream: Venezuela built US
refineries; Kuwait bought gas stations ("Q-8") and 22% of
BP; Saudi Arabia acquired half of Texaco's US stations.
Kuwait turned itself into an integrated oil company.
Thatcher had sold the government's BP stake — reversing
Churchill's 1914 decision.

At the 1985 Bonn summit — not a single word about oil in
the communique. The crisis seemed over. At a 1989
discussion, an oil minister was asked how long the lessons
of the 1970s-80s would be remembered. "About three years,
without reminding," he said. Within a year, he was no
longer minister. A month later, his country was invaded.
