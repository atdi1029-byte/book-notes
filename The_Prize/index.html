Glossary

Industry Terms

Posted price: The publicly announced price at which oil companies offered to buy crude — the basis for calculating royalties and taxes. An administered price, not a market one.

Rule of capture: Legal doctrine from English game law — whoever pumps oil from a common underground reservoir owns it. Drove chaotic overproduction until prorationing.

Prorationing: Government-imposed production limits to prevent waste and stabilize prices. The Texas Railroad Commission became the model OPEC later copied.

Concession: Government grant giving a company exclusive exploration/production rights for a set period. Every major concession in this book was eventually nationalized.

Fifty-fifty: Profit-sharing formula replacing flat royalties after 1950. Began in Venezuela, spread to the Middle East via the foreign tax credit.

Netback deal: Pricing mechanism guaranteeing refiners a predetermined profit margin. Used by Saudi Arabia in 1985 to recapture market share; crashed oil to $10.

Swing producer: Country that adjusts output to balance world supply and demand. Saudi Arabia nearly bankrupted itself playing this role.

Organizations

Standard Oil Trust: Rockefeller’s monopoly, 90% of American refining by 1879. Dissolved 1911 into thirty-four successor companies.

Royal Dutch/Shell: The 60/40 merger (1907), engineered by Henri Deterding to challenge Standard Oil.

Seven Sisters: The seven Western oil majors dominating the postwar industry. Term coined by Enrico Mattei as an insult.

OPEC: Founded September 14, 1960 in Baghdad. Dismissed by the CIA for its first decade.

IEA: Western consumer nations’ counterweight to OPEC, created 1974. France called it “a machine de guerre.”

Aramco: Arabian-American Oil Company. Nationalized in stages, completed 1990.

Key Concepts

Red Line Agreement (1928): Partners bound not to operate independently within the old Ottoman Empire boundaries, drawn by Gulbenkian on a map.

As-Is Agreement (1928): Secret pact at Achnacarry Castle to freeze market shares. Companies called each other “friends.”

Hot oil: Production exceeding quotas, smuggled through secret pipelines at night.

Oil weapon: Embargoes and production cuts as political leverage, most dramatically in October 1973.

Prologue

Winston Churchill changed his mind almost overnight. Until the summer of 1911, the young Home Secretary was one of the “economists” opposing increased military spending in the Anglo-German naval race. Then on July 1, 1911, Kaiser Wilhelm sent the gunboat Panther to Agadir on the Atlantic coast of Morocco. The crisis transformed Churchill. Appointed First Lord of the Admiralty, he faced a seemingly technical question with vast implications: should the Royal Navy convert from coal to oil?

The strategic benefits were compelling — greater speed, more efficient use of manpower, the ability to refuel at sea. But it meant trading the security of Welsh coal for dependence on distant Persian supplies. Churchill committed himself with all his driving energy and enthusiasm. “To commit the Navy irrevocably to oil was indeed to take arms against a sea of troubles.” But he had no choice: “Mastery itself was the prize of the venture.”

At the beginning of the 1990s — almost eighty years after Churchill made the commitment to petroleum — oil once again became the focus of global conflict. On August 2, 1990, Saddam Hussein invaded Kuwait. His goal was not only conquest but capture of its riches. In short, mastery itself was once more the prize.

Three great themes animate the story. The first is the rise of capitalism and modern business — Standard Oil was among the world’s first multinationals, and seven of the top twenty Fortune 500 companies were oil companies. The second is oil as a commodity intimately intertwined with national strategies and global politics — from WWI through the Cold War to the 1990s. The third theme illuminates how ours has become a “Hydrocarbon Society” — kerosene pushed back the night, the automobile transformed civilization, and oil provides the fertilizer, plastics, and transportation on which modern life depends.

Yet Hydrocarbon Man shows little inclination to give up his cars, his suburban home, and what he takes to be the essentials of his way of life. The peoples of the developing world give no indication that they want to deny themselves the benefits of an oil-powered economy. The global environmental agendas of the industrial world will be measured against the magnitude of population growth ahead.

The twentieth century rightly deserves the title “the century of oil.” The canvas is global. The story is a chronicle of epic events that have touched all our lives. Populating its pages are the tycoons and entrepreneurs — Rockefeller, Henri Deterding, Calouste Gulbenkian, J. Paul Getty, Armand Hammer, T. Boone Pickens. Yet no less important are the likes of Churchill, Adolf Hitler, Joseph Stalin, Ibn Saud, Mohammed Mossadegh, Dwight Eisenhower, Henry Kissinger, George Bush, and Saddam Hussein.

At the beginning of the 1990s, oil was fueling the heated debate over energy needs versus environmental protection. The global environmental agendas would be measured against extraordinary population growth. In the meantime, the stage was set for one of the great clashes between environmental protection and economic growth.

It is the author’s hope that this exploration of the economic, social, political, and strategic consequences of our world’s reliance on oil will illuminate the past, enable us better to understand the present, and help to anticipate the future.

Chapter 1: Oil on the Brain — The Beginning

The oil industry was born from a missing payment of $526.08. In 1854, a group of New Haven investors hired Benjamin Silliman Jr. of Yale to analyze “rock oil.” Silliman withheld his report over the unpaid fee, hid it with a friend, and vanished on a Southern tour. When finally released, it was “a turning point in the establishment of the petroleum business” — rock oil could yield high-quality illuminating fluid.

The visionary was George Bissell, a self-made polymath fluent in nine languages. In 1856, sheltering under a druggist’s awning on Broadway, he spotted an ad showing drilling derricks for salt — the last piece fell into place. The investors recruited Edwin Drake, a jovial jack-of-all-trades chosen because he had a railroad pass. Banker Townsend invented “Colonel” Drake via letters to impress the backwoodsmen.

Drake arrived in Titusville (population 125) in December 1857. His drillers quit — they thought he was insane. On August 27, 1859, at sixty-nine feet, the drill dropped into a crevice. Uncle Billy Smith peered down Sunday morning and saw dark fluid. That same day Drake received Townsend’s letter ordering him to shut down. A week earlier, he would have obeyed.

Production surged from 450,000 barrels (1860) to three million (1862). Prices swung wildly — $10 in January 1861, ten cents by year’s end. Pithole exploded from forest to 15,000 people with fifty hotels, then collapsed — a parcel sold for $2 million auctioned for $4.37.

The rule of capture — based on English game law about migratory animals — meant surface owners could drain common pools, driving chaotic competition. Pipelines overcame the teamsters’ stranglehold despite armed attacks and arson.

Bissell grew wealthy and donated a Dartmouth gymnasium with bowling alleys “in remembrance of disciplinary troubles.” Drake lost everything. “If you have any of the milk of human kindness left in your bosom for me or my family, send me some money.” Pennsylvania granted him a small pension in 1873.

The Civil War stimulated demand by cutting off turpentine from the South and replacing lost cotton export revenue with kerosene exports. Oil exchanges emerged; futures became “the favorite speculative commodity of the time.” The industry’s volatility was established from the very first year — a pattern that would never change.

The context was desperate need. Whale oil cost $2.50 a gallon. Camphene had “an even more unattractive tendency to explode in people’s houses.” Abraham Gesner developed “kerosene” from the Greek keros and elaion. Oil was ancient — bitumen caulked Babylon’s walls, and Greek fire was a state secret more terrible than gunpowder.

“As an illuminator the oil is without a figure … the brightest and yet the cheapest in the world.” —Scientific American, 1860

Chapter 2: “Our Plan” — John D. Rockefeller

In February 1865, John D. Rockefeller bought out partner Maurice Clark at auction for $72,500. “I ever point to that day as the beginning of the success I have made in my life.” Tall, thin, with cold blue eyes that made people uneasy, he was methodical to an extreme. His father boasted: “I trade with the boys and skin ’em.”

Rockefeller’s genius was integration — timber for barrels, tank cars, warehouses, boats on the Hudson. Partner Henry Flagler devised rebates and drawbacks — competitors unknowingly subsidized Standard Oil. On January 10, 1870, Standard Oil was established. By 1879, it controlled 90% of refining capacity.

The South Improvement Company (1872) provoked 3,000 men to march on the Titusville Opera House. Standard operated through secret firms, communicated in code (“Morose”), and gave competitors “a good sweating.” The Trust Agreement (1882) formalized control. Headquarters at 26 Broadway became the nerve center of the world’s largest enterprise.

Rockefeller’s faith in oil never wavered: “We must try and not lose our nerve when the market gets to the bottom.” He maintained curious frugality — once billing a guest $600. He played “Buzz” and could never get past 71 despite his gift for numbers. He gave away $550 million.

The Lima-Indiana fields produced sulfurous “skunk juice.” Rockefeller stored 40 million barrels while others refused it. Herman Frasch solved the deodorizing problem. “Buy all we can get.”

Ida Tarbell’s exposé fueled the antitrust movement. On May 15, 1911, the Supreme Court dissolved the trust into thirty-four companies. The irony: Rockefeller became wealthier after the breakup as freed companies competed and grew.

Standard was both reviled as “the Octopus” and admired as an “upbuilder.” Mothers warned: “Rockefeller will get you if you don’t mind.” William Vanderbilt: “I never came into contact with any class of men so smart and able.”

Rockefeller’s favorite verse: “A wise old owl lived in an oak, the more he saw the less he spoke.” He founded the University of Chicago and insisted buildings not bear his name. The Standard Oil story established patterns that repeat for the next century: whoever controls transportation controls the industry.

Chapter 3: Competitive Commerce

Robert Nobel took Ludwig’s “walnut money” — 25,000 rubles meant for rifle stocks — and bought a Baku refinery in 1873. Ludwig became “the Oil King of Baku,” commissioning the first bulk tanker (Zoroaster, 1878). Russian production reached 10.8 million barrels by 1884 — a third of American output.

The Rothschilds — Baron Alphonse (best moustaches on the Continent) — financed the Baku-Batum railroad. When Ludwig died in 1888, newspapers confused the brothers and reported Alfred’s death. Distressed by obituaries condemning him as a “merchant of death,” Alfred rewrote his will — the Nobel Prizes.

Marcus Samuel, son of a shell merchant, conceived a scheme to break Standard’s Asian grip. On August 23, 1892, the Murex passed through the Suez Canal. Asian customers refused Standard’s blue tins used for roofing and birdcages. Samuel ordered red replacements — red roofs replaced blue across Asia.

Samuel lacked organizational talent — “he ran everything out of his hat.” The brothers’ decisions involved “calling each other fool, idiot, imbecile, until suddenly they were in agreement again.”

Standard, the Rothschilds, and the Nobels battled while simultaneously exploring mergers. A grand alliance signed March 14, 1895 — 75/25 American/Russian — never came into force. Standard’s attention turned to Sumatra and Royal Dutch.

The global oil trade was becoming truly worldwide. America’s share of world exports fell from 78% to 71%. The Russian fields were enormous but plagued by revolutionary violence and ethnic tensions. Baku’s Black Town had over two hundred refineries producing smoke “like confinement in a chimney-pot.”

The Rothschilds brought financial sophistication but grew weary of the chaos. The Nobels brought engineering talent but faced political instability. Only Standard had the organizational capacity to compete globally — but it was about to face its most formidable challenger.

The three-way contest — American monopoly, European rivals, Russian chaos — set the pattern for global oil competition that endures to this day: commercial logic constantly interrupted by geopolitical reality.

Chapter 4: The New Century

Aeilko Jans Zijlker discovered oil potential in Sumatra in 1880 when a native overseer’s bright torch caught his attention. Royal Dutch launched in 1890; Zijlker died in Singapore before seeing success. J.B.A. Kessler found chaos — men labored waist-deep in water, out of rice. On February 28, 1892, oil arrived with “a roar as of a mighty storm.” “To stagnate means to liquidate.”

Standard Oil eyed Royal Dutch with predatory interest. W. H. Libby proposed quadrupling Royal Dutch’s capital with Standard taking all additional shares. Kessler recommended rejection. Directors created a special class of preference stock — admission by invitation only — to prevent hostile takeover.

Standard’s agents reported that Royal Dutch would never merge. It was not merely a “sentimental barrier” — the managers greatly enjoyed receiving 15% of profits. “Dutch obstacles are about the most difficult in the World for Americans to remove, for Americans are always in a hurry and Dutchmen never.”

The dawn of the automobile age was transforming demand. The internal combustion engine had turned gasoline from a useless byproduct — sometimes dumped into rivers at night — into the most valuable fraction of crude oil. The entire industry was pivoting from illumination to transportation.

New competitors emerged everywhere. Gulf Oil was founded on the Spindletop gusher of 1901 in Texas — Captain Lucas’s well blew mud, gas, and then oil over a hundred feet in the air. The Texas fields broke Standard’s grip on domestic supply.

Standard’s legal troubles mounted. State after state filed antitrust suits. The federal case worked its way toward the Supreme Court. Rockefeller’s empire, which had seemed invincible, was under siege from every direction — competitors in the field, regulators in the courts, and rivals overseas.

The twentieth century was arriving with an insatiable appetite for petroleum. The question was no longer whether oil would power the modern world, but who would control it.

The new century brought technological revolution — the torsion balance, the seismograph, and aerial surveillance began transforming oil finding from guesswork into science. But the human element remained paramount: the wildcatter’s instinct, the promoter’s nerve, and the financier’s capital still determined who won and who lost.

Chapter 5: The Dragon Slain

The dissolution of Standard Oil in 1911 was supposed to slay the dragon. The Supreme Court ordered the trust broken into thirty-four companies, each theoretically independent. The reality was more complex. The successor companies immediately began invading each other’s territories, creating a far more competitive industry than Rockefeller’s unified trust had been.

Henri Deterding of Royal Dutch was the man who filled the vacuum. Rising from bank clerk to managing director, he combined Napoleon’s ambition with a banker’s precision. Fred Lane resigned from Shell’s board with a devastating critique of Marcus Samuel: “Business like this cannot be conducted by an occasional glance in one’s spare time.”

Deterding forced the merger. He insisted on a 60–40 split in Royal Dutch’s favor. Samuel protested. “I am at present in a generous mood. If you leave this room without accepting it, the offer is off.” Samuel accepted. In 1907, the Royal Dutch/Shell Group was born — three men ran it: Deterding (business), Hugo Loudon (technical), and Robert Waley Cohen (commercial).

Deterding sent Mark Abrahams to Tulsa in 1912 with a typewriter and $2,500 in a money belt to create Roxana Petroleum. “At last we are in America!” Shell was now a truly global competitor.

In Baku, revolution was brewing. A young Georgian seminarian named Joseph Djugashvili — later Stalin — organized underground cells. The secret printing operation “Nina” produced Lenin’s Iskra. The 1905 Revolution destroyed two-thirds of all Russian oil wells. Stalin: “There in Baku I received my second baptism in revolutionary combat.”

By 1912, the Rothschilds — weary of anti-Semitism, strikes, and arson — sold to Royal Dutch/Shell. The Group’s portfolio: 53% East Indies, 17% Rumania, 29% Russia. Deterding’s anti-communism later intensified after marrying White Russian Lydia Pavlova.

Fisher privately admired Deterding: “He is Napoleon and Cromwell rolled into one. He is the greatest man I ever met.” The former bank clerk from Amsterdam had built the only enterprise that could challenge the fragments of Standard Oil globally.

The pre-war oil industry was taking its modern shape: American companies competing domestically and reaching overseas, Royal Dutch/Shell dominant in Asia and expanding everywhere, and the Russian fields in chaos. The missing piece was the Middle East — but that was about to change.

Chapter 6: The Oil Wars — Rise of Royal Dutch

The “oil wars” of the early twentieth century were fought not with weapons but with price cuts, market invasions, and corporate espionage. Royal Dutch/Shell, Standard’s successors, and a host of independents battled across every continent for market share in the rapidly growing petroleum trade.

Shell’s tanker fleet gave it a decisive advantage in Asian markets. The company pioneered bulk distribution systems that Standard had neglected, establishing storage depots from Calcutta to Yokohama. Standard’s tin-can distribution system — elegant in its simplicity — could not compete with Shell’s economies of scale.

The competition extended to every corner of the globe. In Borneo, the Dutch East Indies, China, India, and Japan, the two groups fought for every marginal barrel of kerosene sales. The prize was enormous — Asia’s billions represented the largest untapped market for illumination in the world.

Deterding’s management style was autocratic but effective. He made decisions quickly, held grudges permanently, and pursued vendettas against competitors with relentless energy. His motto: “To stagnate means to liquidate.” Under his leadership, Royal Dutch/Shell grew from an Anglo-Dutch curiosity into a truly global enterprise.

The oil wars established that the petroleum industry would be globally competitive, not nationally monopolistic. Standard’s successors could not simply divide the world among themselves; they faced a foreign rival with equal ambition and superior flexibility. This competitive dynamic would shape the industry for the rest of the century.

The wars also demonstrated that governments were not neutral observers. The Dutch government protected Royal Dutch. The British government favored Shell. Washington backed Standard’s successors through the Open Door policy. Oil was already too important to be left to businessmen alone.

The personal rivalry between Deterding and the Standard men became legendary. Deterding was simultaneously feared and admired — his ruthlessness in business was matched only by his charm in social settings. He entertained lavishly, collected art, and cultivated relationships with every government that mattered.

By 1914, the global oil industry had taken its recognizable shape: a handful of giant companies competing intensely across multiple continents, each backed by its home government, each pursuing vertical integration as the key to survival. The structure that Rockefeller pioneered and Deterding refined would endure for decades — until the producing countries demanded their share.

Chapter 7: “Beer and Skittles” in Persia

William Knox D’Arcy, Australian gold millionaire with the only private box at Epsom beside the royal box, signed a sixty-year concession covering three-quarters of Persia on May 28, 1901. He spent over £200,000 in brutal conditions — heat reaching 120 degrees, workers for whom “even the wheelbarrow was a startling innovation.”

George Reynolds directed exploration at inaccessible Chiah Surkh — equipment shipped to Basra, up the Tigris, then by mule. D’Arcy telegraphed: “Delay serious. Pray expedite. Every purse has its limit.” Lord Strathcona, eighty-four, agreed to head a “syndicate of patriots” — “not for commerce but really from an imperial point of view.”

Operations were “not all beer and skittles” — drinking water was “water with dung in suspension,” the nearest dentist 1,500 miles away in Karachi. Three hundred pirates had to be overcome. The desert was merciless.

On May 26, 1908, at 4 AM, a gusher rose fifty feet at Masjid-i-Suleiman. Wilson sent coded news: “See Psalm 104 verse 15 third sentence.” Reynolds received Burmah’s shutdown letter the same day — echoing Drake half a century earlier.

The Anglo-Persian Oil Company went public April 19, 1909. Investors stood ten deep at the Bank of Scotland counter. D’Arcy received shares worth £895,000 but lamented: “I feel like signing away a child.”

The discovery was the first major strike in the Middle East. It would tie Britain to Persia for decades, create the company that became BP, and establish the pattern of Western oil development in the region that would eventually provoke the nationalist backlash.

Burmah Oil’s vice-chairman had visited D’Arcy’s Epsom box; the rich food made him sick for weeks. He never accepted another invitation. The social gulf between D’Arcy’s London luxury and Reynolds’s Persian hardship captured the essential tension of the oil business: the money men and the field men inhabited different worlds.

Reynolds himself received little recognition for his years of perseverance in impossible conditions. He was let go with a minuscule bonus — but his instincts about where to drill would later be vindicated again when he discovered Venezuela’s Barroso field for Shell.

Chapter 8: The Fateful Plunge

Admiral “Jacky” Fisher — born in Ceylon, went to sea at thirteen, “a mixture of Machiavelli and a child” — preached oil for years. He told Churchill: “The first of all necessities is SPEED.” Oil offered four extra knots. “Your old women will have a nice time of it when German Motor Battleships are cocking a snook at our Tortoises!”

The three naval programs of 1912–14 — the greatest addition in Royal Navy history — were all oil-based. The Queen Elizabeth class was the “fateful plunge.” Many thought the conversion pure folly — it meant abandoning safe Welsh coal for distant, insecure Persian supplies.

Charles Greenway (“Champagne Charlie,” “Old Spats and Monocle”) of Anglo-Persian campaigned against Shell’s “unwelcome embrace,” stressing Samuel’s “Jewishness” and Deterding’s “Dutchness.” Churchill told Parliament on June 17, 1914: “If we cannot get oil, we cannot get corn, cotton and a thousand commodities necessary for the preservation of the economic energies of Great Britain.”

The government invested £2.2 million for 51% of Anglo-Persian. The bill passed 254 to 18. Samuel Samuel objected three times. Churchill swatted him down. On July 31, Deterding wrote to Churchill promising oil and tankers. Eleven days after the vote, Archduke Franz Ferdinand was assassinated.

The decision tied the British Empire to the Middle East permanently. What seemed a technical naval matter became a strategic commitment that shaped British foreign policy for generations. The pattern would repeat: military advantage creates dependency, and dependency creates vulnerability.

Fisher’s vision was vindicated by the war that followed. The speed advantage of oil-powered vessels proved decisive in engagement after engagement. Coal- powered ships simply could not keep up. The age of coal in naval warfare was over.

But the dependency Fisher and Churchill created also established a template. Britain now had to protect its Persian oil interests — requiring a military presence, political arrangements with local rulers, and continuous diplomatic attention. Oil had become a permanent item on the imperial agenda.

The Parliamentary debate was perhaps the last moment when Britain could have chosen a different path. After June 1914, there was no turning back. Oil was no longer just a commodity — it was a national security imperative.

Chapter 9: Blood of Victory — World War I

General Gallieni commandeered 3,000 Paris taxicabs for the First Battle of the Marne. “By the meter or on a flat rate?” “By the meter.” “All right, let’s go.” The troops saved Paris. It was the combustion engine’s first decisive military act.

Churchill funded tank development from Navy coffers after the high command ignored the concept. The code name stuck: the “tank.” At Amiens (August 1918), 456 tanks broke through — Ludendorff’s “black day of the German Army.” British motor vehicles grew from 827 to 56,000 trucks, 23,000 cars, 34,000 motorcycles.

Aviation evolved from Foch’s “good sport, but for the Army worthless” to 55,000 British, 68,000 French, and 48,000 German aircraft produced during the war. Speeds doubled to over 120 mph. Marcus Samuel extracted toluol from Borneo crude for TNT — Shell provided 80% of British military TNT.

“Empire Jack” Norton-Griffiths destroyed seventy Romanian refineries ahead of the Germans, pulling his revolver on challengers. By 1917, unrestricted submarine warfare threatened Allied oil. Clemenceau appealed that gasoline was “as vital as blood in the coming battles.” The U.S. supplied 80% of Allied petroleum.

Lord Curzon pronounced: “The Allied cause had floated to victory upon a wave of oil.” The war proved oil’s military supremacy beyond doubt. Every major power understood that petroleum access was a survival question.

Wilson instituted “Gasolineless Sundays” and remarked: “I suppose I must walk to church.” The Allied effort to coordinate petroleum supply became a model for future wartime cooperation — and a preview of the far more complex logistics that World War II would demand.

The logistics of fuel supply had been grossly underestimated. Pre-war planners assumed modest petroleum requirements; the reality was that mechanized warfare consumed oil at rates nobody had imagined. The side with more oil won.

The submarine campaign against tankers was a preview of the far more devastating Battle of the Atlantic in the next war. Oil’s Achilles’ heel was always transportation — the long, vulnerable supply lines from wellhead to battlefield.

“The Allied cause had floated to victory upon a wave of oil.” —Lord Curzon, 1918

Chapter 10: Opening the Door on the Middle East

Calouste Gulbenkian — Armenian, suspicious enough to employ two sets of doctors — architected the Turkish Petroleum Company. His first transaction at age seven: exchanging a silver piece for an antique coin. Motto: “Never give up an oil concession.” When Teagle called him an “oil merchant,” Gulbenkian reddened: “I classify myself as a business architect … Now the three of you are trying to throw me out on my ass.”

On October 15, 1927, a gusher at Baba Gurgur flowed at 95,000 barrels per day near holes burned since Nebuchadnezzar’s time. Gulbenkian drew the thick red line on a map. The Red Line Agreement locked up the richest petroleum province on earth.

Clemenceau traded Mosul for British support in Syria — neither PM shared the deal with his foreign minister. The San Remo Agreement (1920) gave France 25%. Washington erupted. Walter Teagle — second-generation oil man, scorer of a perfect hundred at Cornell — fought through the Open Door.

The British installed Faisal on Iraq’s throne — Lawrence of Arabia called him “an absolute ripper.” Found sitting on his luggage at a Palestine railway station after being deposed from Syria. Iraq was a collection of Shia, Sunni, Jews, Kurds, and Yazidis.

Colonel Mercier created the Compagnie Francaise des Petroles (1924) — “none of the initial investors begged for the favor.” Ninety reluctant banks were assembled. U.S. motor vehicles jumped from 1.8 to 9.2 million between 1914 and 1920.

The postwar scramble established that access to Middle Eastern oil required navigating imperial rivalries, corporate ambitions, and local politics simultaneously. Oil was already “90 percent political and 10 percent oil.”

Gulbenkian would fight for decades to preserve his 5% stake. At the Hotel Aviz in Lisbon, he became a tourist attraction. At the final signing in 1948, he found “one more point.” Only one bottle of champagne was drunk among twelve. “Mr. Five Percent was no more.”

The Middle Eastern oil architecture — shared concessions, negotiated quotas, interlocking partnerships — imposed order for a generation. But the producing countries were not at the table, and they would eventually demand their seat.

Chapter 11: From Shortage to Surplus — The Age of Gasoline

Captain Eisenhower’s 1919 convoy averaged five and two-thirds mph — “Through Darkest America with Truck and Tank.” As President, he would build the interstates. By 1929, 23.1 million autos — 78% of the world’s total. Gas stations: 12,000 (1921) to 143,000 (1929).

Los Angeles pioneered the standardized station. Shell required free uniforms and no magazine reading. Bruce Barton: “It is the juice of the fountain of eternal youth that you are selling … and you have sold merely a bad smelling liquid.” Oil companies created road maps — “uniquely American contributions to cartography.”

The Teapot Dome scandal: Interior Secretary Albert Fall leased naval reserves. Doheny delivered $100,000 in “a little black bag.” Fall became the first Cabinet officer convicted of a felony. Doheny was acquitted: “You can’t convict a million dollars.”

Rockefeller Jr. led a proxy fight to oust Standard of Indiana’s Colonel Stewart: “Not for mere profits but in the name of decency.” The torsion balance and seismograph transformed oil finding. Signal Hill (1921) made California number one.

The fear of shortage dominated the early 1920s. But new discoveries kept outpacing demand. The industry oscillated between panic about running out and panic about drowning in surplus — a rhythm that would repeat throughout the century.

Geophysics was revolutionizing exploration. The magnetometer, aerial surveillance, and micropaleontology turned oil finding from art into science. Deepest wells went from 6,000 feet (1918) to 10,000 (1930). But the human element remained — the wildcatter’s instinct still mattered.

America was becoming an automobile civilization. The car was not just transportation but identity, freedom, and social status. Gas stations were not just fuel stops but social landmarks. The entire landscape was being reshaped around petroleum.

The “new light” of kerosene had given way to the “new fuel” of gasoline. Oil was no longer merely an illuminant — it was the engine of modern life.

Chapter 12: “The Fight for New Production”

Harry Doherty — “the professor” with oversized glasses — argued the rule of capture must be eliminated. The industry called him “that crazy man” and barred him three times from API meetings. Fields should be unitized to prevent waste. J. Howard Pew countered that rivers would change course before petroleum was exhausted.

Standard successor companies invaded each other’s territories. Phillips opened its first station in 1927; by 1930 it had 6,750 outlets. Texaco and Shell marketed in all forty-eight states. Ida Tarbell: “It certainly looks as if the Standard Oil Company might be crumbling — crumbling within.”

Mexico’s Golden Lane had briefly made it the world’s second-largest producer. Lord Cowdray’s Potrero del Llano 4 flowed at 110,000 barrels per day. But the Revolution’s Article 27 restored national subsoil ownership. Production fell 80% in a decade.

Venezuela offered friendlier terms under General Gomez, who fathered 97 illegitimate children but offered political predictability. Shell’s Barroso well (December 1922) blew out at 100,000 barrels per day — discovered by George Reynolds, the same man who found oil in Persia.

Jersey’s 4,200 underwater acres in Lake Maracaibo were considered a joke — “the company could go into the fishing business.” Exploration was hazardous: geologists traveled by canoe or mule, mosquitoes were the worst anywhere, one driller was killed by an arrow from hostile Indians.

The Front Uni of 1922 pledged to fight the Soviets together. Deterding launched a price war when Socony dealt with Moscow. Teagle: “The idea of trying to be on friendly terms with the man who burglarizes your house has never appealed to me.”

Royal Dutch/Shell called it “the fight for new production” in its 1920 annual report: “We must not be outstripped in this struggle to obtain new territory.” The scramble was global — from Venezuela to Russia to Sumatra to Persia.

The fight for new production established that oil companies had to grow or die. Standing still meant losing reserves to depletion and competitors. The imperative of perpetual exploration and expansion became the industry’s defining characteristic.

Chapter 13: The Flood

Columbus “Dad” Joiner, seventy, drilled with rusted equipment on Daisy Bradford’s farm. A Texaco geologist: “I’ll drink every barrel.” On October 3, 1930, oil shot above the derrick. A crewman fired a pistol into the spray — tackled before a spark could cause an explosion.

The East Texas field — forty-five miles long, 140,000 acres — was the largest ever in the lower forty-eight. Prices collapsed to six cents, even two cents. “Teakettle” refineries offered free chicken dinners.

H. L. Hunt acquired Joiner’s rights over a 36-hour negotiation, armed with secret intelligence. He paid $1.33 million. Joiner died at 87, still wildcatting, net worth barely more than his car.

Oklahoma’s “Alfalfa Bill” Murray declared martial law. Texas sent the National Guard. “Hot oil” reached half a million barrels daily, smuggled through secret pipelines at night.

Harold Ickes became Oil Administrator. On September 2, 1933, he set monthly quotas — the rule of capture was overturned. “What had made sense for deer and game birds in medieval England would no longer do.”

The Connally Hot Oil Act and Interstate Oil Compact stabilized the system. Oil averaged $1.00–$1.18 per barrel from 1934 to 1940. The magical “dollar a barrel” was realized.

Even the chickens at Proration Hill had feasted on gas-flare insects and now “returned to scratching for worms.” The origin of the term “hot oil”: a militiaman shivering on a cold night was told to lean against a tank — “It’s hot enough to keep you warm.”

The East Texas flood and its aftermath established the prorationing system that would govern American oil production for four decades. The Texas Railroad Commission became the de facto regulator of world oil prices — the model OPEC would eventually copy.

Chapter 14: “Friends” — and Enemies

In August 1928, Deterding rented a Scottish castle with 50,000 acres of shooting. Teagle, Cadman, and others joined for “grouse shooting.” “It was lousy,” Teagle said, but they were not after grouse. The “As-Is” Agreement froze market shares at 1928 levels.

Companies called each other “friends” while deeply distrusting one another. “Friends in London say …” The domestic U.S. market was explicitly excluded to avoid antitrust.

Throughout 1930s Europe, governments imposed quotas, blocked remittances, and compelled participation in national cartels. In Europe, oil operations were “90 percent political and 10 percent oil.”

Reza Shah canceled Anglo-Persian’s concession on November 16, 1932. He had personally beaten an ayatollah. Cadman flew to Tehran and had his pilot taxi visibly from palace windows. The Shah retreated: area reduced, concession extended to 1993.

Cardenas signed Mexico’s expropriation order at 9:45 PM on March 18, 1938. A six-hour parade followed. Pemex was established. Nazi Germany became Mexico’s number-one petroleum customer.

Deterding “was incapable of conceiving of Mexico as anything but a Colonial Government to which you simply dictated orders.” Mexico established the model — Pemex became one of the first important state-owned oil companies.

Bolivia confiscated Standard Oil’s subsidiary in 1937 to great applause. Companies called it the biggest trauma since the Bolshevik Revolution. The pattern was clear: nationalism was the permanent enemy of foreign oil concessions.

The Achnacarry agreement effectively ended by September 1939. War was coming, and with it a scramble for oil that would make the commercial competitions of the 1920s and 1930s seem quaint.

Chapter 15: The Arabian Concessions

Major Frank Holmes — “Abu al-Naft” (Father of Oil) — first heard of Arabian oil seepages on a beef-buying trip to Ethiopia in 1918. Anglo-Persian declared Arabia “devoid of all prospects.” People “ran when they saw him coming” in London.

Gulf joined the TPC and became bound by the Red Line, so it brought the Bahrain concession to Standard of California (Socal). On May 31, 1932, oil struck in Bahrain — the first petroleum discovery in the Arab world. Bahrain was only twenty miles from the Arabian mainland.

Jack Philby, eccentric father of double agent Kim, told Ibn Saud he was “like folk asleep on buried treasure.” His $1,000-per-month retainer funded Kim’s Cambridge fees, “where Kim was taking the first steps on the way to becoming a Soviet spy.”

Socal won the Saudi concession on May 29, 1933 — payment in gold sovereigns bearing male monarchs only. Five years of dry wells followed. In March 1938, Well Number 7 struck oil. In 1939, Ibn Saud himself turned the valve for the first cargo.

In Kuwait, Anglo-Persian and Gulf formed the Kuwait Oil Company. The Burgan field discovery (February 23, 1938) was so powerful that crude ignited in sand, transforming walls into sheets of glass. The Sheikh’s economy had been devastated by Japanese cultured pearls.

Holmes tapped his nose when asked what made him certain of Arabian oil: “This was my geologist.” His instincts proved more reliable than the professional assessments of every major company.

The Arabian concessions were obtained for trivial sums — the Saudi deal cost $175,000. Proven reserves would eventually be valued in the trillions. No transaction in history has had a greater disproportion between price paid and value received.

The war intervened before the Arabian discoveries could be fully developed. But the geological surveys and early wells had established beyond doubt that the Arabian Peninsula contained oil on a scale unprecedented in human history. The center of gravity was shifting.

Chapter 16: Japan’s Road to War

Japan produced only 7% of consumed oil ; 80% from the U.S., 10% from the Dutch East Indies. The Army focused north (Manchuria); the Navy on hokushu nanshin — “defend in the north, advance to the south” toward Indonesian oil.

Cordell Hull held fifty secret nighttime meetings with Ambassador Nomura. Hull had “Magic” — the cracked Japanese diplomatic code. The Germans warned Tokyo the Americans had broken their codes; the Japanese simply did not believe it.

Yamamoto spent four years in America, read Lincoln biographies, received Life magazine. He scoffed at “armchair arguments about war” yet designed Pearl Harbor: “Decide the fate of the war on the very first day.”

Dean Acheson turned the July 1941 asset freeze into a total embargo. “Whether or not we had a policy, we had a state of affairs.” Oil inventories would last no more than two years. Tojo: “Two years from now we will have no petroleum. Ships will stop moving.”

The Emperor read Meiji’s poem: “Since all are brothers in this world, why is there such constant turmoil?” The hall fell silent. A “Magic” intercept: agreement must come by November 29 — “after that, things are automatically going to happen.”

On December 7, 1941, bombs fell. Hull raged at Nomura: “Infamous falsehoods on a scale so huge …” Eight battleships sunk, 2,335 killed. But Nagumo declined a third wave at oil tanks.

Nimitz: “Had the Japanese destroyed the oil, it would have prolonged the war another two years.” Oil drove Japan to war — yet they forgot about oil when planning the attack.

Roosevelt had sent a personal message to the Emperor the night before: “This son of man has just sent his final message to the Son of God.” Aloft above the Japanese flagship flew the flag from the 1905 Tsushima victory. Pearl Harbor was the supreme irony: a war fought entirely for oil, launched with an attack that forgot about oil.

Chapter 17: Germany’s Formula for War

In June 1932, I.G. Farben officials met Hitler, who lectured for two and a half hours: “German motor fuel must become a reality.” By 1940, synthetic output reached 72,000 barrels per day — 46% of supply and 95% of aviation gasoline. Plants used more electricity than all of Berlin.

I.G. Farben built plants at Auschwitz, paying 3–4 marks per day per slave. Half price for children. Primo Levi: “The only thing alive are machines and slaves — and the former are more alive than the latter.” By 1944, a third of the workforce was slave labor.

Operation Barbarossa: three million men, 600,000 vehicles, 625,000 horses. Hitler overruled generals: “My generals know nothing about the economic aspects of war.” The Wehrmacht had to reach Baku.

Operation Blau targeted Caucasus oil. The irony: Germans ran short of oil in their quest for oil. They transported fuel on camels. The Sixth Army’s tanks had fuel for twenty miles; they needed thirty to escape.

Rommel’s moves were “decided more by the petrol gauge than by tactical requirements.” His epitaph: “The battle is fought and decided by the Quartermasters before the shooting begins.”

On May 12, 1944, 935 bombers hit synthetic plants. Speer: “On that day the technological war was decided.” By September, aviation gasoline was 6% of earlier levels.

In the Battle of the Bulge, Peiper came within a thousand feet of 2.5 million gallons at Stavelot but turned away, not knowing it was there. German trucks were dragged by oxen. Hitler’s body was doused in the last available gasoline.

Standard Oil’s partnership with I.G. Farben became a postwar scandal. Frank Howard’s 1926 visit to Leuna had produced patent-sharing agreements worth $35 million in Standard shares. The relationship continued into the war years, raising questions about corporate complicity that haunted the industry for decades.

“Unless we get the Baku oil, the war is lost.” —Adolf Hitler to Manstein

Chapter 18: Japan’s Achilles’ Heel

Shell men at Balikpapan destroyed four decades of industrial creation in a day. Japan rehabilitated the Indies fields with astonishing speed — by 1943, output reached 75% of prewar levels. But the fatal weakness was shipping.

American submarines sank 86% of merchant shipping. Less than 2% of U.S. naval personnel caused 55% of Japanese losses. Oil imports peaked early 1943; by early 1945 they ceased entirely.

Civilian gasoline fell to 4% of prewar levels. Pilot training cut to thirty hours, then navigation eliminated. Japan lost 40% of aircraft on ferrying operations alone. At Leyte Gulf, Admiral Kurita pulled away forty miles from MacArthur’s beach because of fuel.

The kamikaze was fuel-efficient: one to three planes achieved what eight bombers required, fuel cut in half. The pine root campaign: “two hundred pine roots will keep a plane in the air for an hour.” Only 3,000 barrels produced; none ever tried in a plane.

The Yamato — world’s largest battleship — was given fuel for a one-way trip to Okinawa. Sunk by 300 aircraft. Navy fuel: 0.8 million barrels; below a million, the Navy could not operate.

Finding an ambulance with gasoline to save the wounded Tojo proved harder than finding a doctor. MacArthur’s charcoal-powered motorcade broke down repeatedly over twenty miles. Americans found 316,000 barrels hidden in caves for suicide flights.

Japan’s fuel inventories: 29.6 million barrels (April 1937) to 0.8 million (July 1945). Admiral Onishi advocated twenty million Japanese sacrificing themselves. The Americans expected a million casualties in a Home Islands invasion — contributing to the atomic bomb decision.

Japan’s war was, start to finish, about oil. It went to war to secure oil, lost because its oil was sunk at sea, and surrendered when its oil was gone.

Chapter 19: The Allies’ War

Britain’s oil industry merged into the Petroleum Board. All product sold as “Pool.” Deterding had died in Germany in 1939, having embraced Nazism. His preference shares were swiftly distributed to prevent German control.

America had one million barrels per day of surplus capacity — a legacy of 1930s prorationing. Production rose from 3.7 to 4.7 million barrels per day. The U.S. supplied six of seven billion barrels consumed by the Allies.

U-boats devastated Atlantic shipping. Miami’s neon provided perfect silhouettes — hotel owners insisted lights stay on for tourist season. In May 1943, 30% of U-boats at sea were lost. The Battle of the Atlantic was over.

Big Inch and Little Inch pipelines carried oil from Texas to the East Coast. 100-octane fuel gave 15–30% more power — proved in the Battle of Britain. Catalytic cracking production: under 40,000 to 514,000 barrels per day.

Patton: “My chief difficulty is not the Germans but gasoline.” Tanks reached the Meuse with empty gas tanks. Nine months of bitter warfare followed. Liddell Hart: “The best chance of a quick finish was probably lost when the gas was turned off.”

PLUTO (Pipeline Under the Ocean) was designed to supply half of Allied fuel needs from England to France — but averaged only 150 barrels per day, one-sixth of one percent of consumption. The “jerrycan” — copied from a German five-gallon design with an American pouring spout — became ubiquitous.

Rationing came through rubber — Japan cut off 90% of natural rubber. The “A” sticker provided one and a half to four gallons a week. A black market flourished. “There’s a war on.”

American forces used 100 times more gasoline in WWII than WWI. A typical division: 4,000 horsepower in WWI, 187,000 in WWII. Half the tonnage shipped was oil. When a soldier went overseas, sixty-seven pounds of his supplies were petroleum.

“This is a war of engines and octanes. I drink to the American auto industry and the American oil industry.” —Joseph Stalin

Chapter 20: The New Center of Gravity

DeGolyer, dispatched to the Middle East in 1943, reported proven reserves at 25 billion barrels, with suspected totals of 300 billion. One member: “The greatest single prize in all history.” His key judgment: the center of gravity was shifting from the Gulf-Caribbean to the Middle East.

Ibn Saud faced financial crisis. “The Arabs have the religion, but the Allies have the money.” Ickes published “We’re Running Out of Oil!” Roosevelt authorized Lend Lease to Saudi Arabia.

Ickes proposed buying all of Casoc. “They had gone fishing for a cod and had caught a whale.” A government pipeline plan died — called “a move towards fascism.” Lord Beaverbrook: “Oil is the single greatest post-war asset remaining to us.”

Roosevelt met Ibn Saud on the USS Quincy. The King traveled with 48 retainers and 7 live sheep. The two bonded as “twins.” Roosevelt gave a wheelchair. Churchill: “My religion prescribed smoking cigars and drinking alcohol as an absolute sacred rite.”

Roosevelt showed Halifax a map: “Persian oil is yours. We share Iraq and Kuwait. As for Saudi Arabian oil, it’s ours.” The special relationship became “an unlikely union — Bedouin Arabs and Texas oil men.”

Roosevelt died April 12, 1945. Ickes resigned in a bitter scrap with Truman, who gave him two days instead of six weeks. Forrestal: “The prestige of the United States is in part related to the wealth of the Government in terms of oil resources.”

The Anglo-American Petroleum Agreement was killed by universal opposition. Even schoolteachers in Texas mobilized against it. By 1947 it was dead. The special U.S.-Saudi relationship would have to operate through private companies, not government treaties.

The postwar landscape was set: the Middle East had the oil, America had the companies, Britain had the legacy interests, and everyone had conflicting ambitions. The collision was a matter of time.

Chapter 21: The Postwar Petroleum Order

Gasoline rationing lifted within 24 hours of Japan’s capitulation — “Fill ’er up!” By 1950: 40 million cars. Gasoline sales 42% higher. Pessimistic predictions eviscerated: proven U.S. reserves 21% higher in 1950 than 1946.

A shortage struck in 1947–48. Crude prices more than doubled. More than twenty Congressional investigations launched. Refineries needed redesign; steel was in short supply; tankers broke in half at sea.

Aramco needed capital. Socal and Texaco sold 40% to Jersey and Socony for about $470 million. Jersey and Socony broke the Red Line Agreement by invoking “supervening illegality.” Gulbenkian: “We do not acquiesce.”

Tapline finished September 1950 — 1,040 miles, replacing 7,200 via Suez. “Imported petroleum killed synthetic fuels. And they would remain dead for three decades.”

Gulf found Shell for “shadow integration” of Kuwaiti oil. Europe in 1946–47: the Thames froze, industry halted. Twenty percent of Marshall Plan aid went to oil. “The Englishman began to realize there was no value in being cold.”

In 1948, U.S. imports exceeded exports for the first time. America was now dependent on other countries for the marginal barrel. The implications would take decades to fully manifest, but the structural shift was irreversible.

Stalin on Iran: “We’re not going to risk our oil supply.” The Cold War added another dimension to oil politics. Every producing country became a chess piece in the superpower rivalry.

The postwar petroleum order was built on cheap Middle Eastern oil flowing through Western companies under fifty-fifty deals. It would last barely two decades before the producing countries dismantled it.

Chapter 22: Fifty-Fifty — The New Deal in Oil

Ricardo’s concept: rents equal market price minus production cost. Middle East: 25 cents per barrel cost versus $2.50 selling. “A rich discovery means a dissatisfied landlord.”

Perez Alfonzo in Venezuela found the actual split was 60–40 in companies’ favor and fixed it. He insisted on royalties in kind: “The veil of mystery over oil marketing was removed forever.”

J. Paul Getty won the Saudi Neutral Zone with $9.5 million. Five dry holes. The sixth: “somewhere between colossal and history-making.” “If you can count your money, you don’t have a billion dollars.” The geologist got $1,200. Getty installed a pay phone for guests.

The foreign tax credit transformed economics: Saudi taxes deductible from Aramco’s U.S. tax bill. By 1951: Saudi Arabia $110 million; U.S. Treasury $6 million. Venezuela’s motive for spreading the formula: low-cost Middle East was “a grave threat.”

Gulbenkian warned: “I fear the wind of nationalization may spread.” Jersey: “50/50 is a good position which needs no defense.” The companies thought they had found a stable equilibrium. They were wrong.

The fifty-fifty formula was revolutionary because it acknowledged that producing countries had rights beyond mere royalties. But it still left pricing, production, and marketing decisions entirely in company hands — a contradiction that could not endure.

Anglo-Iranian in Iran stubbornly resisted the new formula. Chairman Fraser was an “obstinate, narrow old skinflint.” Iran received £90 million while Anglo-Iranian booked £250 million in profit between 1945 and 1950. The stage was set for the most dramatic confrontation yet.

The fifty-fifty deal was a holding action, not a permanent settlement. The producing countries would want sixty-forty, then seventy-thirty, then nationalization. Each concession bred the demand for the next. The ratchet only turned one way.

Chapter 23: “Old Mossy” and the Struggle for Iran

Mohammed Mossadegh — seventy, frail, completely bald, receiving dignitaries in pajamas from bed. He collapsed during meetings; a doctor checked his pulse. “Mossadegh opened one eye and winked.” “The source of all misfortunes is only the oil company.”

PM Razmara was assassinated entering a mosque. The Majlis nationalized. Scores of sheep sacrificed. Harriman found Mossadegh insisting the British were impossibly crafty — then learned his grandson was at school “In England, of course. Where else?” Walters ordered Alice in Wonderland “as a guidebook.”

British oil men staged a farewell revue: “Stand Firm, You Cads!” The guest house lady interdicted a tank commander with her umbrella. Passengers sang obscene versions of “Colonel Bogey.”

The British embargo removed a substantial amount of petroleum from world commerce. But other countries increased production. Iranian output plummeted from 666,000 to 20,000 barrels per day while total world production rose.

Operation Ajax: first attempt failed; Shah fled to Rome. Then “tumblers doing handsprings.” Mossadegh fled over his back wall. Eisenhower: “more like a dime novel.”

The consortium: Anglo-Iranian 40%, Shell 14%, five American majors 8% each. “A wonderful deal for Fraser — Anglo-Iranian had nothing to sell. It had already been nationalized.”

The Iran crisis established two lessons: nationalism was unstoppable, and covert intervention could restore order temporarily but created resentment that eventually produced something far worse. The 1979 revolution was Ajax’s delayed consequence.

Churchill, who had purchased the government’s stake thirty-seven years earlier, had lived long enough to head the government at the company’s greatest crisis. The irony was perfect. The decision he championed in 1914 led directly to the confrontation he faced in 1951.

Chapter 24: The Suez Crisis

On July 26, 1956, Nasser invoked “de Lesseps” repeatedly — the code word. The army seized the canal. Then Nasser saw Cyd Charisse in Meet Me in Las Vegas. Two-thirds of Europe’s oil passed through Suez.

Eden: “I must be absolutely blunt about the oil … we would fight for it.” Macmillan read novels for two weeks to keep from going “barmy.” Eden, Macmillan, and Mollet were haunted by the 1930s — Nasser was a resurrected Mussolini.

At Sèvres (October 24), Britain, France, and Israel agreed on the secret plan. Eisenhower was furious. He refused emergency oil supplies: “left to boil in their own oil.” An immense run on the pound started.

The Texas Railroad Commission barely increased production: “England apparently still looks on us as a province.” Belgium banned Sunday driving. Britain rationed gasoline — the “Suez sixpence.”

Eden resigned — “the last prime minister to believe Britain was a great power and the first to confront a crisis which proved she was not.” Macmillan read Pride and Prejudice to soothe his soul.

Supertankers became a consequence — vessels too large for the canal. In 1970, Eden prayed for “a lake of oil” under the North Sea. “That was exactly what they were beginning to find.”

Suez proved that oil could bring down a government and end an imperial delusion. The crisis demonstrated that America, not Britain, now held the power to allocate oil in an emergency. The torch had passed.

The canal remained vital to oil transport for decades. But the lesson was psychological as much as logistical: no single choke point should be allowed to determine the fate of industrial civilization.

Chapter 25: The Elephants

Free world crude: 8.7 million barrels per day (1948) to 42 million (1972). U.S. share slipped from 64% to 22%. Middle East grew from 1.1 to 18.2 million — a 1,500% increase. Proven reserves grew ninefold to 534 billion barrels.

Enrico Mattei — “a born condottiere,” left school at fourteen — transformed AGIP into ENI. He coined “Sette Sorelle” (Seven Sisters). He broke fifty-fifty with an effectively 75–25 deal with Iran.

Mattei came to Shell; Loudon asked how he got into oil. Mattei talked nonstop through lunch. “When we went to dessert, he asked for something. We couldn’t do it, and that was the end.” He died in a 1962 plane crash — “more important than the premier or the Pope.”

July 1958: Iraq’s monarchy overthrown. King Faisal II beheaded. PM Nuri es-Said recognized fleeing disguised as a woman, lynched, his body flattened by a car. The Shah to Standard of Indiana: “We are not Arabs. We are Aryans.”

Japan’s Arabian Oil Company took only 44% of profits. The era of giant discoveries was transforming the power balance. With every new elephant found, the producing countries grew more confident that they were giving away too much.

Perez Alfonzo installed a rusted Singer car in his garden as “a corroded shrine to the dangers of oil wealth.” Tariki was excluded from Texas bars because taken for a Mexican. Wanda Jablonski introduced them: “She had just been the matchmaker for OPEC.”

The elephants changed everything. So much oil had been found that the producing countries could afford to be aggressive — if one country shut down, others would fill the gap. The surplus itself became the weapon that the producing countries would use to extract better terms.

Reserve life extended from nineteen years (1950) to thirty-five (1972) — reserves were being found even faster than consumed. The industry was drowning in its own success, and the political consequences would be catastrophic.

Chapter 26: OPEC and the Surge Pot

In July 1960, Jersey’s chairman Rathbone cut posted prices over Howard Page’s objection: “You can’t just be guided by market forces in an industry so essential to governments.” Loudon: “the fatal move.”

On September 14, 1960, OPEC was founded in Baghdad. Companies dismissed it; the CIA gave four lines. Perez Alfonzo: “We’ve done it!” He resigned in 1963, calling petroleum “the excrement of the devil.”

Libya’s 1955 law created many small concessions. Jersey struck Zelten 1959: “Libya has hit the jack-pot.” Sweet, perfectly located crude. By 1969, Libya exceeded Saudi output. Corruption pervaded.

The Iran-Saudi rivalry dominated the 1960s. Page refused Oman: “I am absolutely sure we don’t want oil because we are liable to lose the Aramco concession.” Between 1957 and 1970, Iran grew 387% versus Saudi 258%.

The Gulf became the “surge pot” — where incremental demand was met. Companies in the Middle East grew from 9 to 81 (1948–72). Returns fell from 20%+ to 11–13%.

Eisenhower’s import quotas created the “Brownsville U-Turn” absurdity. The quotas protected domestic producers but accelerated American dependence on imports by depleting domestic reserves faster.

OPEC accomplished nothing in its first decade. The real power shift was structural: surplus capacity was vanishing, and the companies were losing control of the market without realizing it.

Nobody noticed that the surge pot was running dry. The cushion of spare capacity that had protected consumers for decades was being consumed by relentlessly growing demand. When the cushion finally disappeared, the consequences would be explosive.

Chapter 27: Hydrocarbon Man

Total world energy consumption tripled (1949–72). Oil demand increased five and a half times. U.S. consumption tripled. Japan’s: 137 times. Motor vehicles outside America: 18.9 to 161 million.

The petrochemical industry exploded — The Graduate: “Plastics.” American cars got eight miles per gallon. Gasoline stations on every corner. Credit cards tied customers. Texaco sponsored Milton Berle: “Trust Your Car to the Man Who Wears the Star.”

Coal could not withstand the tidal wave. London’s “Killer Fogs” from coal prompted smokeless zones. In Western Europe: coal from 75% (1955) to 22% (1972); oil from 23% to 60%. Japan: 7% to 70% oil.

Japan in 1955 produced 69,000 cars; by 1968, 4.1 million. Alfred Chandler’s recipe: “improved institutional arrangements and cheap oil.” “If it can be said that the sun energized the planet, it was oil that now powered its human population.”

Shell claimed its TCP additive was an Air Force secret. Socony countered it was harmful. The FTC investigated. The “additives war” reflected intensity of competition in surplus.

The American motorist was riding high. Stations washed windows, checked tires, handed out drinking glasses — all for free. “Price wars” erupted with hand-lettered signs proclaiming prices half a cent less than across the street.

Oil transformed not just transportation but the entire landscape of modern life. Suburbs, shopping malls, highway systems, fast food restaurants, and the daily commute — all were products of cheap, abundant petroleum. Hydrocarbon Man had built his civilization around a resource he took completely for granted.

For most of the century, growing reliance on petroleum was celebrated as progress. But with the rise of the environmental movement, oil found itself on the defensive — charged with being a threat to present and future generations. Yet Hydrocarbon Man showed little inclination to change.

Chapter 28: The Hinge Years — Countries Versus Companies

The Shah’s $200 million Persepolis celebration (1971): Maxim’s flew in 165 chefs and 25,000 bottles of wine. “We can hardly offer them bread and radishes.” He rode Princess Anne’s stallion Cossack — the name was kept from him.

Britain withdrew from the Gulf by 1971. Dubai: “Who asked them to leave?” U.S. production peaked at 11.3 million barrels per day in 1970. The Railroad Commission: “That old warrior can’t rise anymore.”

In Libya, Qaddafi squeezed Hammer. Jalloud set his .45 in front of Hammer, who “had never before negotiated across a gun barrel.” Libya won 30 cents and 55%. The Tehran Agreement (1971): “the real turning point for OPEC.”

Yamani’s “participation” was an alternative to nationalization: “a Catholic marriage.” Qaddafi stood with Idi Amin to announce “a big hard blow on America’s cold insolent face.”

By 1973, surplus capacity was 500,000 barrels per day — 1% of consumption. Placke warned: “Control of the flow of resources has been of strategic concern throughout history.” Nobody listened.

The Santa Barbara spill galvanized environmental opposition. The Alaska pipeline was blocked for years. Limits to Growth warned of depletion. The tension between energy supply and environmental protection was becoming irreconcilable.

Nixon abolished import quotas in April 1973. Domestic production could no longer keep up. Net imports rose from 2.2 million (1967) to 6 million barrels per day (1973). “Near-panic buying by U.S. and European independents as well as the Japanese.”

Every structural support was removed simultaneously: British withdrawal, American peak, surplus gone, environmental constraints, resource nationalism. Any one was manageable. Together, they made catastrophe inevitable.

Chapter 29: The Oil Weapon

Sadat concluded war was the only way. Kissinger: “What literally no one understood was the mind of the man.” His purpose was psychological, not military. He shared the plan with King Faisal.

Faisal had rejected the oil weapon in 1972: “Politics and oil should not be mixed.” By 1973 he changed. He warned Aramco: “You will lose everything.” Washington: “a large degree of disbelief.”

On October 6 — Yom Kippur — 222 Egyptian jets and 3,000 guns struck. Israel’s intelligence failed. Dayan: “The Third Temple is going under.”

October 16: price raised 70% to $5.11 — unilaterally. Yamani: “We are masters of our own commodity.” October 17: 5% monthly cuts plus total ban on the U.S. October 20: Saudi complete cutoff. Same day: Saturday Night Massacre. Brezhnev threatened; DefCon 3.

“The three-decade-old postwar petroleum order had died its final death.”

The CIA had failed to predict the attack. The State Department had failed to take Faisal’s warnings seriously. The companies had warned but were ignored. The intelligence failure was compounded by a policy failure.

James Akins had published “The Oil Crisis: This Time the Wolf Is Here” months earlier. Ehrlichman dismissed conservation: “Conservation is not the Republican ethic.” By summer 1973, U.S. imports had doubled from 1970 to 6.2 million barrels per day. “Near-panic buying by U.S. and European independents as well as the Japanese.”

The oil weapon was powerful not because of the actual barrels removed but because of the panic it induced. Fear multiplied the economic impact far beyond the physical disruption. Psychology, not geology, determined the price.

“We are masters of our own commodity.” —Sheikh Yamani, October 1973

Chapter 30: “Bidding for Our Life”

Net loss: 4.4 million barrels per day — 14% of traded oil. Nigerian oil sold for $16; Iranian auction bids reached $22.60. “We were bidding for our life.”

Japan: toilet paper hoarding. America: “Sorry, No Gas Today.” The allocation system perversely prevented shifting gasoline to where it was needed. Nixon: “Project Independence.”

BP’s Drake refused Heath’s demand: “Are you asking me as a shareholder or as a government? If government, put it in writing.” “You know perfectly well I can’t.” “Then I won’t do it.”

December 1973: the Shah pushed through $11.65 — fourfold increase. Saudi Arabia wanted $8. The Shah lectured: “The era of their terrific progress based on cheap oil is finished.”

Kissinger’s shuttle diplomacy ended the embargo March 18, 1974. He walked through a tremendous hall of men in black robes, reflecting “what strange twists of fate had caused a refugee from Nazi persecution to wind up in Arabia.”

Japan endorsed the Arab position — its first foreign policy split with the U.S. The IEA was created. France’s Jobert: “Bonjour les traitres.”

Companies adopted “equal suffering” — diverting a third of their oil to maintain balance. Whitehall’s civil servants were more subtle than Heath: they reminded companies that Britain decided who got North Sea licenses.

The fourfold price increase transformed the global economy more profoundly than any event since the end of World War II. It redistributed wealth on a staggering scale, ended the postwar boom, and launched a decade of inflation and stagnation that reshaped politics across the Western world.

Chapter 31: OPEC’s Imperium

Petroleum earnings: $23 billion (1972) to $140 billion (1977). U.S. GNP plunged 6%; unemployment doubled. Japan’s GNP declined for the first time. The “fourth world” was invented for non-oil developing countries.

Yamani — Mecca 1930, grandson of a grand mufti, NYU and Harvard Law, oil minister at 32. “Give me Tariki’s rantings. Yamani drives you to the wall with sweet reasonableness.” He never gambled: “It rots the soul.”

The Shah saw December 1973 as his moment. “Everything you can dream of can be achieved here.” The Empress: “One of the only things my husband likes in life is speed!” Yamani warned: “If the Shah departs, we could have a violent, anti-American regime in Teheran.”

Faisal was assassinated March 1975. “Carlos” attacked OPEC Vienna — Yamani spent 44 hours with worry beads. A “blank check” arms policy developed.

Kuwait told Gulf “thank you and good-bye.” Gulf asked $2 billion; got $50 million. Goodman: “The Romans retreating from Hadrian’s Wall.” Venezuela nationalized. Saudi Arabia took 100% of Aramco. “It was truly the end of the era.”

By 1978, OPEC’s $67 billion surplus had become a $2 billion deficit. The spending binge consumed the windfall faster than anyone imagined. Ports clogged, ships waited weeks, arms purchases skyrocketed. A Datsun was now cheaper to fuel than a camel.

From 1974 to 1978, two small increases ($10.84 to $12.70). Real price eroded about 10%. By 1977, the Shah shifted to moderation: “We acquired money we could not spend.” The OPEC imperium was already hollowing from within.

The nationalization of the concessions completed a cycle that had begun with D’Arcy in 1901. Every concession granted in the first half of the century was now in government hands. The oil companies that had built the industry were reduced to contractors and traders.

Chapter 32: The Adjustment

Japan: “Instead of using the resources in the ground, we would use the resources in our head.” MITI curtailed elevator service. France: Pompidou confirmed nuclear and conservation. Inspectors fined buildings exceeding twenty degrees.

Senator Jackson’s hearings: oil executives “came across as inept, insulated, self-satisfied.” Exxon 1973 earnings up 59%. “I am not embarrassed,” said chairman Jamieson. Many thought otherwise. 45 of 100 Senators voted for divestiture.

Ford’s achievements: Alaska pipeline ($10 billion) and fuel efficiency (13 to 27.5 mpg). Carter: “the moral equivalent of war.” Detractors: “Meow.” Schlesinger: “I understand now what Hell is.”

Phillips struck Ekofisk in the North Sea after “Don’t drill any more wells.” Mexico surged to 1.9 million barrels per day. Alaska came online — Gulf had refused: “Oil will never get to $5 in our lifetime.”

The U.S. became 25% more energy efficient by 1985; Japan 31%. If the U.S. had stayed at 1973 efficiency, it would have used 13 million more barrels daily. Conservation was the hidden fuel.

The strategic petroleum reserve was established. Carter: “I said 90 days. I made the pledge and I intend to keep it.” “Three kinds of lies — lies, damn lies, and energy lies.”

The adjustment proved that high prices are self-correcting. Conservation, substitution, and new supply combined to erode OPEC’s power from within. The weapon that had seemed invincible in 1973 was being neutralized by market forces.

On New Year’s Eve 1977, Carter toasted the Shah: “Iran is an island of stability.” It was the most catastrophically wrong assessment in modern diplomatic history.

Chapter 33: The Second Shock — The Great Panic

A January 1978 newspaper article savaging Khomeini triggered revolution. The “40-40” cycle spiraled. Production fell from 5.5 million to zero by Christmas. Paul Grimm was shot dead driving to work. Oil men shot their dogs before evacuating.

The Shah: “We are melting away daily like snow in water.” A telephone prank: “Mohammed, abdicate.” He left carrying a casket of Iranian soil. Khomeini returned on an Air France 747.

The actual shortage was 4–5%. Panic drove prices from $13 to $34. Motorists keeping tanks full added three million barrels per day of phantom demand — more than doubling the shortage.

Three Mile Island raised nuclear doubts. Gas lines returned. Carter went to Tokyo where Schmidt “got personally abusive.” Yamani warned for 11 hours: “There will be a glut.” The Iranian: “In the name of almighty God, there will be no surplus.”

Shell received a force majeure telex cutting supply; that afternoon the same volume was offered at 50% higher on spot. Japan: one trading house gave gloves to secretaries; another provided an acupuncturist for the Iraqi oil minister.

November 4, 1979: embassy seized. Elizabeth Ann Swift: “We’re going down.” Fifty Americans held 444 days. Desert One rescue failed. Soviets invaded Afghanistan.

Five factors drove the panic: apparent demand growth, contractual disruption, contradictory policies, exporter opportunism, and sheer emotion. BP invoked force majeure, cutting 40% of supply. Exxon phased out third-party contracts.

The second shock proved that panic buying is more destructive than actual supply disruption. Psychology determines the price in crisis. The lesson would be forgotten — and relearned — in every subsequent disruption.

Chapter 34: “We’re Going Down”

Saddam Hussein — shaped by Tikrit’s values of “suspicion, stealth, surprise, and the unalloyed use of force” — attacked Iran on September 22, 1980. His uncle published Three Whom God Should Not Have Invented: Persians, Jews, and Flies.

Iran’s “human wave” assaults: hundreds of thousands with plastic keys to heaven. Children cleared minefields. The war removed four million daily barrels — but inventories were brimming.

The IEA urged drawdowns. D’Avignon to the Japanese: “If you don’t get your trading companies under control, forget about Toyotas coming into Europe.”

OPEC output in 1981 was 27% below 1979. Saudi Arabia pushed to its limit. October 1981: prices unified at $34. It was the last OPEC increase for a decade.

The Shah died in exile. The hostage crisis consumed Carter’s presidency. The CIA director warned “how vicious the struggle for energy supplies will become.” A Foreign Affairs article: “Oil and the Decline of the West.”

The Iraq-Iran war became the longest conventional conflict of the twentieth century. Eight years, a million casualties, and the use of chemical weapons. Neither side could win — but Iraq accumulated the military machine and the debts that would drive it to invade Kuwait.

“We engineered the glut,” Yamani said. Saudi Arabia had increased production to offset the war’s disruption. The surplus that Yamani had predicted was arriving. The forces unleashed by high prices — conservation, new supply, recession — were overwhelming OPEC’s ability to control the market.

The “third shock that wasn’t” demonstrated that the world had changed. The oil market was no longer as vulnerable to disruption as it had been in 1973 or 1979. Inventories, strategic reserves, and diversified supply provided resilience. But complacency was the inevitable consequence.

Chapter 35: Just Another Commodity?

The greatest boom: young geologists hired at $50,000. Dallas introduced J.R. Ewing. Exxon spent a billion on Colony Shale Oil. By 1982, Colony was terminated. Colorado boomtowns became ghost towns. “My business just died.”

The Federal Reserve’s prime rate: 21.5%. Demand fell six million barrels per day. Non-OPEC production increased four million. In March 1982, OPEC set output limits — for the first time a true cartel.

Nymex introduced crude futures March 1983. Founded 1872 as the Butter and Cheese Exchange, previous mainstay: Maine potatoes. WTI joined the Dow and gold as vital measures. “Maine potatoes became a distant, quaint, and embarrassing memory.”

T. Boone Pickens exploited the “value gap.” “It was cheaper to explore for oil on the floor of the New York Stock Exchange.” Chevron swallowed Gulf for $13.2 billion. Mukluk: most expensive dry hole ever ($2 billion) — “simply 30 million years too late.”

Mexico ran up $84 billion in debt. Silva Herzog: “No, Mr. Secretary, we have a problem.” Penn Square’s lending officer drank Amaretto from Gucci loafers. Continental Illinois: largest bank run in history.

At the 1985 Bonn summit — not a single word about oil in the communiqué. The question: was oil just another commodity? Futures markets said yes. Geopolitics said no. The answer would arrive within months.

The corporate restructuring wave was devastating. Exxon spent $16 billion on share buy-backs and cut employees by 40%. Over $100 billion flowed to investors. The industry shrank dramatically. Jimmy Lee of Gulf: “I never had any intention but that Gulf would be here for ever and ever. It had been my whole life.”

The transformation of oil from a strategic commodity managed by governments and corporations into a financial instrument traded on exchanges was perhaps the most important structural change since the industry’s founding. Price was now set by traders, not ministers.

Chapter 36: The Good Sweating — How Low Can It Go?

Saudi earnings: $119 billion (1981) to $26 billion (1985). Production: 2.2 million barrels per day — at times below British North Sea output. U.S. exports: 26,000 barrels per day — “essentially nothing.” King Fahd warned: if cheating continued, “Saudi Arabia would certainly secure its own interests.”

Netback deals guaranteed refiners a profit regardless of selling price. “A perfect recipe for falling prices.” Oil crashed from $31.75 to $10. Some Gulf cargoes: $6. Shell’s “Oil Collapse Scenario”: “an eerie calm at Shell Centre.”

Vice President Bush: “a continued free fall like a parachutist without a parachute.” Reagan’s White House: “Let the free market work.”

Yamani at Harvard’s 350th: “We play it by ear.” The remark was interpreted in Riyadh as criticism. A month later, he learned from television he’d been fired. Twenty-four years — “a good, long run.”

December 1986: OPEC agreed to $18. The “OPEC tax cut” transferred $50 billion to consumers. After eight years, Iran accepted a cease-fire. Khomeini: “Taking this decision was more deadly than taking poison.” Iraq thought it had won — “the dominant political power in the Gulf.”

State companies went downstream: Venezuela built U.S. refineries; Kuwait bought 22% of BP; Saudi Arabia acquired half of Texaco’s stations. Thatcher sold BP — reversing Churchill’s 1914 decision.

Billy Jack Mason’s Exxon station in Austin ran zero cents per gallon — a six-mile line. The 1986 price collapse was as dramatic as the 1973 increase had been. What OPEC had taken in two shocks, the market took back in one.

The price collapse proved that cartels are inherently unstable. Saudi Arabia’s attempt to be the swing producer nearly destroyed its finances. The lesson: whoever defends the price alone while others cheat will be punished. OPEC never fully recovered its 1970s power.

Epilogue

At a 1989 discussion, an oil minister was asked how long the lessons of the 1970s and 1980s would be remembered. “About three years, without reminding,” he said. Within a year, he was no longer minister. A month later, his country was invaded.

On August 2, 1990, Saddam Hussein invaded Kuwait. If successful, Iraq would dominate the Arab world and the Persian Gulf. The stakes were so enormous the invasion was not accepted as a fait accompli. The United Nations imposed an embargo, and military force was mustered to defend Saudi Arabia.

Churchill had captured the fundamental truth before World War I: oil had meant mastery throughout the twentieth century. At the century’s end, mastery itself was once more the prize.

The story of oil is a story of individual people, of powerful economic forces, of technological change, of political struggles, of international conflict, and of epic change. It is the story of the twentieth century itself — and it is far from over.

Three great themes wove through the entire narrative: oil and capitalism, oil and war, oil and civilization. None existed independently of the others. Every economic decision had political consequences; every political decision had economic consequences; and every consequence shaped the civilization that Hydrocarbon Man had built.

The minister’s prophecy — three years without reminding — was the book’s most haunting line. Complacency is the permanent condition; crisis is the periodic reminder. And the reminder always comes from a direction nobody was watching.

“About three years, without reminding.” —unnamed oil minister, 1989

Trading & Investing Lessons

Commodity Cycles Are Driven by Psychology

Every boom follows the same arc: scarcity drives prices up, high prices attract capital, oversupply crashes prices. The lag between investment and production creates violent swings.

Geopolitical Risk Is Permanent and Underpriced

Markets discount threats until they materialize. Washington greeted Faisal’s warning with disbelief. Carter toasted the Shah as stable. Energy positions must always price in sudden disruption.

Spare Capacity Is the Master Variable

The most dangerous moment is the erosion of spare capacity. In 1973, surplus was 1% of consumption. Any disruption became catastrophic. Monitor spare capacity as the leading indicator.

The Swing Producer Always Suffers

Saudi Arabia nearly bankrupted itself defending prices while others cheated. The entity that adjusts bears disproportionate cost.

Control of Resources Is Temporary

Every concession was eventually nationalized. Standard’s monopoly was broken. The Seven Sisters lost their empire. Invest in adaptability, not permanence.

Panic Creates More Shortage Than the Event

The second shock: actual disruption 4–5%, panic made it feel like 10%. Behavioral cascades are the real price driver. Always fade the panic.

Key Takeaways

Oil Is Never Just a Commodity

Simultaneously a commercial product, strategic asset, and political weapon. Every attempt to treat it as purely one fails.

Technology Creates and Destroys Oil Empires

Drilling replaced skimming. Tankers killed geography. Synthetic fuels sustained Nazi Germany. Conservation broke OPEC. Futures transformed pricing.

The Oil Curse Is Real

Venezuela, Iran, Mexico, Nigeria, Iraq — oil wealth corroded institutions. Perez Alfonzo’s “excrement of the devil” proved prophetic.

Surplus Masks Fragility

Abundant supply breeds complacency. The 1985 Bonn summit ignored oil; Kuwait was invaded five years later.

Integration Is the Winning Strategy

From Rockefeller through state downstream expansion, controlling the chain from wellhead to pump is the most durable advantage.

Contrarian Ideas

The 1911 Dissolution Helped Standard Oil

The freed successor companies collectively became far more valuable. Rockefeller’s wealth increased after the breakup.

Conservation, Not Alternatives, Broke OPEC

The response was efficiency, not solar or nuclear. The U.S. became 25% more efficient; Japan 31%. Conservation was the “hidden fuel.”

OPEC Was Irrelevant for Its First Decade

The real shift was the disappearance of surplus capacity and British military withdrawal — structural changes unrelated to OPEC.

Pearl Harbor Failed on Its Own Terms

Japan fought for oil, forgot about oil at Pearl Harbor. Nimitz: two years prolonged. The war of oil was lost at the moment of oil.

High Prices Are Self-Correcting

Every spike triggered conservation, new supply, substitution. The oil weapon turned on those who wielded it.
