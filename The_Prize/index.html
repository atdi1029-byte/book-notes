Glossary

Industry Terms

Posted price: The publicly announced price at which oil companies offered to buy crude from producing countries — the basis for calculating royalties and taxes. Not a market price but an administered one.

Rule of capture: Legal doctrine from English game law: whoever pumps oil from a common underground reservoir owns it, regardless of property lines. Drove chaotic overproduction until prorationing.

Prorationing: Government-imposed limits on oil production to prevent waste and stabilize prices. The Texas Railroad Commission became the model for OPEC decades later.

Concession: A government grant giving a company exclusive rights to explore and produce oil in a defined area for a set period, typically 60–99 years in the early deals.

Fifty-fifty: The profit-sharing formula that replaced flat royalties after 1950 — producing countries received half of net profits. Began in Venezuela, spread to the Middle East via the foreign tax credit.

Netback deal: A pricing mechanism guaranteeing refiners a predetermined profit margin, used by Saudi Arabia in 1985 to recapture market share. Destroyed price discipline.

Participation: Producing countries acquiring partial ownership of oil operations as an alternative to outright nationalization. Yamani called it “a Catholic marriage.”

Swing producer: The country that adjusts its output up or down to balance world supply and demand. Saudi Arabia played this role from the 1970s onward — and was nearly destroyed by it.

Organizations

Standard Oil Trust: Rockefeller’s monopoly, controlling 90% of American refining by 1879. Dissolved by the Supreme Court in 1911 into successor companies that became today’s ExxonMobil, Chevron, and others.

Royal Dutch/Shell: The 60/40 merger (1907) of Royal Dutch and Shell Transport, created by Henri Deterding to challenge Standard Oil globally.

Seven Sisters: The seven Western oil majors that dominated the postwar industry: Jersey Standard, Socony, Socal, Texaco, Gulf, BP, and Shell. Term coined by Enrico Mattei.

OPEC: Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries, founded September 14, 1960 in Baghdad by Saudi Arabia, Venezuela, Kuwait, Iraq, and Iran.

IEA: International Energy Agency, created in 1974 as the Western consumer nations’ counterweight to OPEC. France called it “a machine de guerre.”

Aramco: Arabian-American Oil Company, the consortium of four American companies that developed Saudi oil. Nationalized in stages, completed 1990.

Key Concepts

Red Line Agreement: The 1928 pact binding TPC partners not to operate independently within the old Ottoman Empire boundaries, drawn by Gulbenkian on a map.

As-Is Agreement: The secret 1928 pact at Achnacarry Castle where the three major groups agreed to freeze market shares. Companies referred to each other as “friends.”

Hot oil: Production exceeding state quotas, smuggled through secret pipelines at night. Origin: a militiaman told to lean against a warm pipeline tank.

Oil weapon: The use of oil embargoes and production cuts as political leverage, most dramatically in October 1973 during the Yom Kippur War.

Chapter 1: Oil on the Brain

The oil industry was born from a missing payment of $526.08. In 1854, a group of New Haven investors hired Benjamin Silliman Jr., Yale’s most distinguished chemist, to analyze whether “rock oil” — a dark substance skimmed from creeks in northwestern Pennsylvania — could serve as an illuminant. Silliman withheld his report over the unpaid fee, hid it with a friend, and disappeared on a tour of the South. When finally released, his findings were electrifying: rock oil could be distilled into a high-quality illuminating fluid. The report became “a turning point in the establishment of the petroleum business.”

The visionary behind the venture was George Bissell, a self-made polymath fluent in nine languages who had spotted a rock oil sample at Dartmouth College. In 1856, sheltering from the heat under a druggist’s awning on Broadway, he saw an advertisement showing drilling derricks for salt — and the last piece fell into place. If you could drill for salt, why not drill for oil? The investors recruited Edwin Drake, a jovial jack-of-all-trades chosen mainly because he had a railroad pass and nothing better to do. The banker Townsend sent letters ahead addressed to “Colonel” Drake — a title invented to impress the frontier backwoodsmen.

Drake arrived in Titusville, population 125, in December 1857. Drillers quit because they thought he was insane. On August 27, 1859, at sixty-nine feet, the drill dropped into a crevice. Uncle Billy Smith peered down on Sunday morning and saw dark fluid rising. Drake attached a hand pump, and that same day received a letter from Townsend ordering him to shut down. A week earlier, he would have obeyed. Farmers shouted “The Yankee has struck oil.” The age of petroleum had begun.

“As an illuminator the oil is without a figure … the brightest and yet the cheapest in the world.”—Scientific American, 1860

What followed was America’s first oil boom. Production surged from 450,000 barrels in 1860 to three million by 1862. Prices swung wildly — ten dollars a barrel in January 1861, fifty cents by June, ten cents by year’s end. Pithole exploded from dense forest to a town of 15,000 with fifty hotels, then collapsed when production gave out — a parcel sold for two million dollars was auctioned for $4.37 a decade later. The rule of capture, based on English game law about migratory animals, meant anyone could drain a common pool, driving frenzied competition and spectacular waste. Drake himself lost everything through improvident speculation. He wrote from poverty: “If you have any of the milk of human kindness left in your bosom for me or my family, send me some money.” Pennsylvania granted him a small pension in 1873.

Chapter 2: Rockefeller’s Standard Oil

In February 1865, a twenty-six-year-old Cleveland refiner named John D. Rockefeller bought out his partner at auction for $72,500. “I ever point to that day as the beginning of the success I have made in my life.” Tall, thin, and taciturn, with cold blue eyes that made people uneasy, Rockefeller was methodical to an extreme. His father sold herbal remedies and boasted of cheating his own children to prepare them for the world. The son held “intimate conversations” with himself, counseling caution, building cash reserves so he could exploit every downturn while competitors went bankrupt.

Rockefeller’s genius was integration — he controlled not just refining but timber for barrels, tank cars, warehouses, and boats on the Hudson. His partner Henry Flagler devised transportation arrangements that extracted not only rebates from railroads but drawbacks — competitors unknowingly subsidized Standard Oil with every barrel they shipped. On January 10, 1870, Standard Oil was established, already controlling a tenth of American refining. The South Improvement Company scheme in 1872 provoked three thousand men to march on the Titusville Opera House, denouncing “the Monster.” By 1879, Standard controlled ninety percent of refining capacity.

The Standard Oil Trust (January 2, 1882) formalized the empire. Headquarters at 26 Broadway became the nerve center of the world’s largest enterprise. A corporate intelligence system tracked virtually every buyer in the country. Rockefeller’s personal frugality was legendary — he once billed a guest $600 for a summer stay. He loved fast horses, played “Buzz” with his children, and could never get past 71 despite his gift for numbers. He gave away $550 million, founded the University of Chicago, and recited his favorite verse: “A wise old owl lived in an oak, the more he saw the less he spoke.” Mothers warned children: “Rockefeller will get you if you don’t mind.”

“We must try and not lose our nerve when the market gets to the bottom. We will surely make a great mistake if we do not buy.” —John D. Rockefeller

Ida Tarbell’s devastating 1904 exposé — her father had been ruined by Standard — fueled the political movement that culminated in the Supreme Court’s dissolution order of May 15, 1911. The thirty-four successor companies would become the backbone of the modern oil industry: Jersey Standard became Exxon, Standard of New York became Mobil, Standard of California became Chevron, Standard of Indiana became Amoco.

Chapter 3: The Global Competitors

Robert Nobel took his brother Ludwig’s money — 25,000 rubles meant for walnut rifle stocks — and bought a Baku refinery in 1873. Ludwig became “the Oil King of Baku,” commissioning the first bulk tanker (the Zoroaster, 1878) and hiring the first professional petroleum geologist. Russian production reached 10.8 million barrels by 1884, a third of American output. When Ludwig died in 1888, newspapers mistakenly reported Alfred’s death. Distressed by obituaries condemning him as a “merchant of death,” Alfred rewrote his will — creating the Nobel Prizes.

Marcus Samuel, son of a shell merchant who sold knickknack boxes at Brighton, conceived a scheme to break Standard’s Asian kerosene grip. On August 23, 1892, the Murex — named for a seashell, memorializing his father — passed through the Suez Canal. The one oversight: Asian customers refused to relinquish Standard’s blue tins, used for roofing, birdcages, and opium cups. Samuel ordered red replacements, and red roofs replaced blue across Asia. But Samuel lacked organizational talent — “he ran everything out of his hat.”

Henri Deterding, who rose from bank clerk to managing director of Royal Dutch, forced the merger. He gave Samuel an ultimatum: 60–40 in Royal Dutch’s favor. “If you leave this room without accepting it, the offer is off.” Samuel accepted. In 1907, the Royal Dutch/Shell Group was born — the only serious global rival to Standard Oil. Meanwhile in Baku, a young Georgian seminarian named Joseph Djugashvili — later Stalin — organized revolutionary cells among oil workers. “There in Baku I received my second baptism in revolutionary combat.”

Chapter 4: Persia and the Fateful Plunge

In 1901, William Knox D’Arcy, an Australian gold millionaire who kept the only private box at Epsom beside the royal box, signed a sixty-year concession covering three-quarters of Persia. He spent over £200,000 in brutal conditions — heat reaching 120 degrees, workers for whom “even the wheelbarrow was a startling innovation.” His geologist George Reynolds persevered for years in conditions that were “not all beer and skittles” — drinking water was “water with dung in suspension,” the nearest dentist 1,500 miles away in Karachi.

On May 26, 1908, at four in the morning, a gusher rose fifty feet at Masjid-i-Suleiman. Wilson sent coded news: “See Psalm 104 verse 15 third sentence.” Reynolds received the company’s shutdown letter the same day — echoing Drake half a century earlier. The Anglo-Persian Oil Company went public; investors stood ten deep at the Bank of Scotland counter.

The discovery set the stage for Winston Churchill’s fateful plunge. Admiral “Jacky” Fisher had been preaching oil for years: “Your old women will have a nice time of it when German Motor Battleships are cocking a snook at our Tortoises!” Churchill, as First Lord of the Admiralty, committed the Royal Navy to oil — faster, more efficient, but dependent on distant Persian supplies instead of safe Welsh coal. On June 17, 1914, he told Parliament: “If we cannot get oil, we cannot get corn, cotton and a thousand commodities necessary for the preservation of the economic energies of Great Britain.” The government invested £2.2 million for 51% of Anglo-Persian. The bill passed 254 to 18. Eleven days after the vote, Archduke Franz Ferdinand was assassinated.

“Mastery itself was the prize of the venture.”—Winston Churchill

Chapter 5: Blood of Victory — WWI

General Gallieni commandeered 3,000 Paris taxicabs for the First Battle of the Marne in September 1914. “By the meter or on a flat rate?” asked a driver. “By the meter.” “All right, let’s go,” he said, putting down his flag. The troops rushed to the critical point and saved Paris. It was the internal combustion engine’s first decisive military act.

Churchill funded development of armored vehicles from Navy coffers after the high command ignored the concept. Various code names were considered — “cistern,” “reservoir” — and it became the “tank.” First used prematurely at the Somme, tanks proved decisive at Amiens in August 1918: 456 tanks broke through what Ludendorff called the “black day of the German Army.” British motor vehicles grew from 827 at the war’s start to 56,000 trucks, 23,000 cars, and 34,000 motorcycles. The victory of the Allies was “in some ways the victory of the truck over the locomotive.”

Marcus Samuel masterminded extracting toluol from Borneo crude to make TNT — Shell’s plant provided 80% of British military TNT. “Empire Jack” Norton-Griffiths destroyed seventy Romanian refineries ahead of the Germans, pulling his revolver on anyone who objected. By 1917, unrestricted submarine warfare threatened Allied oil. Clemenceau appealed that gasoline was “as vital as blood in the coming battles.” The United States supplied 80% of Allied wartime petroleum. Lord Curzon pronounced the epitaph: “The Allied cause had floated to victory upon a wave of oil.”

Chapter 6: The Middle East Opens

Calouste Gulbenkian, an Armenian who employed two sets of doctors because he trusted neither, architected the Turkish Petroleum Company. His first transaction at age seven: exchanging a silver piece for an antique coin. He was drenched by a Baku gusher but never visited oil country again. When Walter Teagle of Jersey Standard called him an “oil merchant,” Gulbenkian reddened: “I classify myself as a business architect. Now the three of you are trying to throw me out on my ass.” His motto: “Never give up an oil concession.”

On October 15, 1927, a gusher at Baba Gurgur in Iraq flowed at 95,000 barrels per day near holes that had burned since Nebuchadnezzar’s time. Gulbenkian drew a thick red line on a map along the old Ottoman Empire’s boundaries. The Red Line Agreement bound partners not to operate independently within that territory. “Mr. Five Percent” had never seen an oil tanker, yet he had locked up the richest petroleum province on earth.

The postwar diplomatic scramble was ferocious. Clemenceau casually traded Mosul for British support in Syria — an exchange neither prime minister shared with his foreign minister. The San Remo Agreement of 1920 gave France 25% of Mesopotamian oil. Washington erupted in protest. Commerce Secretary Hoover organized a consortium, and Teagle of Jersey Standard fought his way in through the Open Door. Meanwhile the British installed Faisal on Iraq’s throne — Lawrence of Arabia called him “an absolute ripper.” He had been found sitting on his luggage at a Palestine railway station after being deposed from Syria.

Chapter 7: The Age of Gasoline

In 1919, Captain Eisenhower joined a military convoy crossing the United States — averaging five and two-thirds miles per hour through roads varying “from average to nonexistent.” Some days they managed three miles when sixty were planned. As President, he would champion the interstate highway system. By 1929, America had 23.1 million automobiles — 78% of the world’s total. Oil’s share of energy rose from 10% to 25%. Gas stations multiplied from 12,000 in 1921 to 143,000 by 1929. Los Angeles pioneered the standardized station. Bruce Barton told oil men: “It is the juice of the fountain of eternal youth that you are selling … and you have sold merely a bad smelling liquid at so many cents per gallon.”

The Teapot Dome scandal showed oil’s corrupting power. Interior Secretary Albert Fall leased naval reserves to Sinclair and Doheny. Doheny delivered $100,000 in “a little black bag.” Fall became the first Cabinet officer convicted of a felony. Doheny was acquitted: “You can’t convict a million dollars.”

Chapter 8: The Flood and the Concessions

Columbus “Dad” Joiner, seventy years old, drilled with rusted equipment guided by a 300-pound fake geologist named “Doc Lloyd.” A Texaco geologist sneered: “I’ll drink every barrel of oil you get out of that hole.” On October 3, 1930, oil shot above the derrick; a crewman fired a pistol into the spray and was tackled before a spark could cause an explosion. The East Texas field — forty-five miles long, 140,000 acres — was the largest ever found in the lower forty-eight. Prices collapsed to six cents, even two cents a barrel.

H. L. Hunt, maintaining two wives with growing families, acquired Joiner’s rights over a 36-hour negotiation at the Baker Hotel, armed with secret intelligence on a nearby well. He paid $1.33 million and became one of the world’s richest men. Joiner died at 87, still wildcatting, his net worth barely more than his car. Texas Governor Sterling sent the National Guard. Harold Ickes — a “plump, bespectacled gentleman” with stinging polemics and immense self-righteousness — set monthly quotas and finally overturned the rule of capture: “What had made sense for deer and game birds on estates in medieval England would no longer do.”

In the Arabian desert, Major Frank Holmes — called “Abu al-Naft” (Father of Oil) — won concessions that the majors dismissed. When asked what told him Arabia had oil, Holmes tapped his nose: “This was my geologist.” Jack Philby, eccentric father of double agent Kim, told Ibn Saud he was “like folk asleep on buried treasure.” His $1,000-per-month retainer from Standard of California funded Kim’s Cambridge fees, “where Kim was taking the first steps on the way to becoming a Soviet spy.” On May 29, 1933, Socal won the Saudi concession — payment in gold sovereigns, all bearing male monarchs (not Queen Victoria). Five years of dry wells followed before Well Number 7 struck oil at 4,727 feet. In 1939, Ibn Saud himself turned the valve for the first cargo.

Chapter 9: Japan’s Road to War

Japan produced only 7% of its consumed oil; 80% came from the United States, 10% from the Dutch East Indies. The tension between north (the Army’s Manchurian focus) and south (the Navy’s drive toward Indonesian oil) defined Japanese strategy for a decade. Cordell Hull, the Tennessee backwoodsman turned Secretary of State, held perhaps fifty secret nighttime meetings with Ambassador Nomura at the Wardman Park Hotel. Hull had a devastating advantage: “Magic” had cracked the Japanese diplomatic code. In May 1941, the Germans warned Tokyo that the Americans had broken their codes — the Japanese simply did not believe it, convinced Americans were not capable of such a feat.

Admiral Yamamoto, who had spent four years in America, read Lincoln biographies, and regularly received Life magazine, scoffed at ultranationalists’ “armchair arguments about war.” Yet he designed the attack: “Decide the fate of the war on the very first day.” When the asset freeze of July 1941 became a total embargo through Dean Acheson’s bureaucratic maneuvering — “Whether or not we had a policy, we had a state of affairs” — Japan’s clock started ticking. Oil inventories would last no more than two years.

At the September Imperial Conference, the Emperor read a poem by Emperor Meiji: “Since all are brothers in this world, why is there such constant turmoil?” The hall fell silent. Tojo replaced the fallen Konoye: “Two years from now we will have no petroleum for military use. Ships will stop moving.” On December 7, 1941, bombs fell at Pearl Harbor. Hull’s rage at Nomura was uncontrollable. Eight battleships sunk, 2,335 servicemen killed. But Nagumo declined a third wave targeting oil tanks. Nimitz later calculated: “Had the Japanese destroyed the oil, it would have prolonged the war another two years.” Oil drove Japan to war — yet they forgot about oil when planning the attack.

Chapter 10: Germany’s Formula

In June 1932, I.G. Farben officials met Hitler, who lectured for two and a half hours: “German motor fuel must become a reality, even if this entails sacrifices.” By 1940, synthetic fuel output reached 72,000 barrels per day — 46% of total supply and 95% of aviation gasoline. Plants used more electricity than all of Berlin. A third of the workforce by 1944 was slave labor, drawn from adjacent concentration camps including Auschwitz. Primo Levi (Prisoner 174,517): “The only thing alive are machines and slaves — and the former are more alive than the latter.”

Operation Barbarossa hurled three million men, 600,000 motor vehicles, and 625,000 horses into Russia on June 22, 1941. Hitler overruled generals wanting Moscow: “My generals know nothing about the economic aspects of war.” The Wehrmacht had to reach Baku. Operation Blau in 1942 targeted the Caucasus, but the irony was devastating: Germans ran short of oil in their quest for oil. They transported fuel on the backs of camels. The Sixth Army’s tanks at Stalingrad had fuel for twenty miles — they needed thirty to escape. Hitler told Manstein: “Unless we get the Baku oil, the war is lost.”

Rommel’s Afrika Korps fought brilliantly but was strangled by supply lines. “My moves would be decided more by the petrol gauge than by tactical requirements.” His epitaph: “The battle is fought and decided by the Quartermasters before the shooting begins.” On May 12, 1944, 935 bombers hit Germany’s synthetic fuel plants. Speer declared: “On that day the technological war was decided.” By September, aviation gasoline was 6% of earlier levels. In the Battle of the Bulge, Peiper came within a thousand feet of 2.5 million gallons at Stavelot — but turned away, not knowing it was there. Hitler’s body was doused in the last available gasoline.

Chapter 11: Japan’s Achilles’ Heel

Japan rehabilitated the conquered East Indies oil fields with astonishing speed — by 1943, output reached 75% of prewar levels. But the shipping lanes were fatal. American submarines, crewed by men the Japanese thought “too soft and luxury-loving,” sank 86% of merchant shipping. Less than 2% of U.S. naval personnel — the submariners — caused 55% of total Japanese losses. Oil imports peaked in early 1943; by early 1945 they ceased entirely. Civilian gasoline fell to 4% of prewar levels. Pilot training was cut to thirty hours, then navigation eliminated.

Desperation produced the kamikaze — one to three suicide planes achieved what eight bombers and sixteen fighters otherwise required, and fuel was cut in half. The pine root campaign promised that “two hundred pine roots will keep a plane in the air for an hour.” Only 3,000 barrels were produced; none was ever tried in a plane. The great battleship Yamato was given enough fuel for a one-way trip to Okinawa and sunk by 300 aircraft. Finding an ambulance with gasoline to save the wounded Tojo after his suicide attempt proved harder than finding a doctor.

Chapter 12: The Allies’ War

America’s secret weapon was a million barrels per day of surplus production capacity — a legacy of 1930s prorationing that became an invaluable strategic margin. Production rose from 3.7 million barrels per day in 1940 to 4.7 million in 1945. The U.S. supplied six of the seven billion barrels consumed by the Allies. The Big Inch and Little Inch pipelines — 1,254 and 1,475 miles respectively — carried oil from Texas to the East Coast, increasing pipeline share from 4% to 42%.

The Battle of the Atlantic nearly severed the supply chain. U-boat commanders sank tankers that were “easily recognizable by their distinctive profile.” Miami’s neon beachfront provided perfect silhouettes — hotel owners insisted the lights stay on for tourist season. In May 1943, 30% of U-boats at sea were lost, and the tide turned.

Patton’s Third Army covered 500 miles in a month, hijacking trains and commandeering fuel convoys. “My chief difficulty is not the Germans but gasoline.” On August 30, 1944, supply was cut to a tenth of normal. Tanks reached the Meuse with empty gas tanks. Nine months of bitter warfare followed — three-quarters of the million Allied casualties occurred after this check. Liddell Hart: “The best chance of a quick finish was probably lost when the ‘gas’ was turned off from Patton’s tanks.”

“This is a war of engines and octanes. I drink to the American auto industry and the American oil industry.”—Joseph Stalin

Chapter 13: The New Center of Gravity

Everette DeGolyer, dispatched to the Middle East in 1943, reported back that proven reserves were about 25 billion barrels — with suspected totals up to 300 billion. One mission member called it “the greatest single prize in all history.” DeGolyer’s key judgment: “The center of gravity of world oil production is shifting from the Gulf-Caribbean area to the Middle East.” The U.S. had produced 90% of Allied oil in WWII — the high-water mark it would never reach again.

Roosevelt met Ibn Saud on the USS Quincy after Yalta. The King traveled with 48 retainers and seven live sheep. The two bonded — Ibn Saud declared himself Roosevelt’s “twin.” Roosevelt gave one of his wheelchairs; the King gave diamond-hilted swords. Churchill’s meeting was less harmonious — “my religion prescribed smoking cigars and drinking alcohol as an absolute sacred rite.” The special U.S.-Saudi relationship became “an unlikely union — Bedouin Arabs and Texas oil men.”

Chapter 14: Fifty-Fifty and Mossadegh

The fifty-fifty formula began in Venezuela, where Perez Alfonzo insisted on royalties in kind: “The veil of mystery over oil marketing was removed forever.” J. Paul Getty won the Saudi Neutral Zone with an audacious $9.5 million bid. Five dry holes followed while Getty wandered Europe researching a Rembrandt, noting “newspaper — 10 centimes.” The sixth hole struck “somewhere between colossal and history-making.” He installed a pay phone for guests at his manor. The geologist who found the oil received $1,200.

In Iran, Mohammed Mossadegh — seventy, frail, receiving dignitaries in pajamas from bed — nationalized Anglo-Iranian. When he collapsed during a meeting and a doctor checked his pulse, “Mossadegh opened one eye and winked.” Harriman found him insisting the British were impossibly crafty, then learned Mossadegh’s grandson attended school in England. “Where else?” British oil men staged a farewell revue called “Stand Firm, You Cads!” The guest house lady interdicted a tank commander with her umbrella. Operation Ajax — the CIA coup — toppled Mossadegh after a first attempt failed and the Shah fled to Rome. Eisenhower thought it read “more like a dime novel.”

Chapter 15: Suez and the Elephants

On July 26, 1956, Nasser repeatedly invoked “de Lesseps” during a speech — the code word for seizing the canal. Then he went to see Cyd Charisse in Meet Me in Las Vegas. Eden told Soviet leaders: “I must be absolutely blunt about the oil … we would fight for it.” Eisenhower was furious at the Anglo-French-Israeli invasion, refusing emergency oil supplies: “left to boil in their own oil.” Eden resigned — “the last prime minister to believe Britain was a great power and the first to confront a crisis which proved she was not.”

Free world crude production grew from 8.7 million barrels per day in 1948 to 42 million by 1972. Enrico Mattei, the Italian “born condottiere” who coined the term “Seven Sisters,” broke the fifty-fifty formula with a 75–25 deal. He died in a 1962 plane crash at fifty-six — “the most important individual in Italy, more important than the premier or the Pope.”

Chapter 16: OPEC and Hydrocarbon Man

In July 1960, Jersey Standard cut posted prices over the vigorous objections of Howard Page: “You can’t just be guided by market forces in an industry so essential to governments.” Within hours, Tariki telegraphed Perez Alfonzo. On September 14, 1960, OPEC was founded in Baghdad. Companies dismissed it; a CIA report gave four lines. Perez Alfonzo resigned in 1963, calling petroleum “the excrement of the devil.”

Oil had created Hydrocarbon Man. Total world energy consumption tripled between 1949 and 1972; oil demand increased five and a half times. U.S. consumption tripled. Japan’s increased 137 times. Motor vehicles outside America grew from 18.9 million to 161 million. Oil toppled King Coal everywhere — in Western Europe, coal fell from 75% to 22% of energy while oil rose from 23% to 60%. Alfred Chandler’s recipe for the German and Japanese economic miracles: “improved institutional arrangements and cheap oil.”

Chapter 17: The Hinge Years

In October 1971, the Shah hosted a $200 million bash at Persepolis. Maxim’s flew in 165 chefs and 25,000 bottles of wine. By then, the structural shift was irreversible. U.S. oil production peaked at 11.3 million barrels per day in 1970. The Texas Railroad Commission opened to full production for the first time: “That old warrior can’t rise anymore.” Surplus capacity was gone.

In Libya, Qaddafi squeezed Armand Hammer’s Occidental Petroleum. Jalloud, the negotiator, set his .45 in front of Hammer, who “had never before negotiated across a gun barrel.” Libya won a 30-cent price increase and 55% profit share. The Tehran Agreement of February 1971 was “the real turning point for OPEC.” The buyer’s market for oil was over. By 1973, available surplus capacity was 500,000 barrels per day — just 1% of consumption. A 99% utilization rate was extraordinarily precarious.

Chapter 18: The Oil Weapon

Sadat concluded war was the only way to break the stalemate: “What literally no one understood was the mind of the man,” Kissinger later admitted. King Faisal had long refused to use the oil weapon — the 1967 embargo had availed nothing. But by 1973 he warned Aramco directly: “You will lose everything.” Washington responded with “a large degree of disbelief.”

On October 6, 1973 — Yom Kippur — 222 Egyptian jets and 3,000 field guns struck simultaneously with Syrian forces. Israel faced its gravest crisis since independence. Dayan: “The Third Temple is going under.” On October 16, Gulf states raised the posted price 70% to $5.11 — unilaterally. Yamani: “This is a moment for which I have been waiting a long time. We are masters of our own commodity.” The next day, Arab ministers agreed to cut production 5% monthly with a total ban on the United States. Saudi Arabia cut off all shipments. The same day: the Saturday Night Massacre. Brezhnev threatened unilateral Soviet intervention; U.S. forces went to DefCon 3 — worldwide nuclear alert. “The three-decade-old postwar petroleum order had died its final death.”

The net loss was 4.4 million barrels per day — 14% of traded oil. Panic buying drove auction prices above $17. A Japanese trading company bid $22.60: “We were bidding for our life.” In December, the Shah pushed through $11.65 per barrel — a fourfold increase in three months. Nixon announced “Project Independence” and fired his energy czar, comparing the replacement to Albert Speer.

Chapter 19: OPEC’s Imperium

Petroleum earnings rose from $23 billion in 1972 to $140 billion by 1977 — the greatest transfer of wealth in history. U.S. GNP plunged 6%; unemployment doubled to 9%. Japan’s GNP declined for the first time since the war. Yamani — born in Mecca, grandson of a grand mufti, NYU and Harvard Law — became oil minister at thirty-two. “Give me Tariki’s rantings. Yamani drives you to the wall with sweet reasonableness.” He never gambled: “It rots the soul.”

Kuwait told Gulf Oil “thank you very much and good-bye.” Gulf asked $2 billion; it got $50 million. Goodman of Gulf: “It was the ebbing of American power — the Romans retreating from Hadrian’s Wall.” Venezuela nationalized. Saudi Arabia took 100% of Aramco — agreed in 1976, not formally signed until 1990. Forty-three years after Standard of California’s $175,000 payment, proven reserves were 149 billion barrels. “It was truly the end of the era.”

Chapter 20: The Second Shock

On New Year’s Eve 1977, Carter toasted the Shah: “Iran is an island of stability … a tribute to the respect and love which your people give you.” Within weeks, a newspaper article savaging Khomeini triggered revolution. The “40-40” cycle — demonstrations, deaths, forty-day mourning, more demonstrations — spiraled out of control. The Shah’s melancholy: “We are melting away daily like snow in water.” A telephone prank: told Senator Kennedy was calling, the Shah picked up to hear “Mohammed, abdicate.” He left carrying a casket of Iranian soil.

The actual shortage was only 4–5% of world demand, but panic drove prices from $13 to $34. Motorists keeping tanks three-quarters full instead of one-quarter sucked a billion gallons from gas stations, adding three million barrels per day of phantom demand — more than doubling the actual shortage. Japan tried everything — one trading house provided a world-class acupuncturist for the Iraqi oil minister. Carter went to Tokyo where Schmidt “got personally abusive.” Yamani warned OPEC for eleven hours: “There will be a glut. It is coming.” The Iranian delegate: “In the name of almighty God, there will be no surplus and prices will not fall.”

Chapter 21: The Good Sweating

The world adjusted. Japan shifted to “knowledge- intensive industry.” France built nuclear plants and banned gas station advertising. The U.S. became 25% more energy efficient by 1985; Japan 31%. Non-OPEC production surged — Alaska, Mexico, the North Sea. Oil demand fell six million barrels per day between 1979 and 1983. OPEC became a true cartel in 1982, setting production quotas for the first time. T. Boone Pickens exploited the “value gap”: “It was cheaper to explore for oil on the floor of the New York Stock Exchange.” Chevron swallowed Gulf Oil for $13.2 billion.

Saudi earnings fell from $119 billion to $26 billion. Production collapsed to 2.2 million barrels per day — at times below British North Sea output. In 1985, the Saudis abandoned defense of price for defense of volume, using netback deals that guaranteed refiners a profit regardless of selling price. Oil collapsed from $31.75 to $10 a barrel. Vice President Bush traveled to Saudi Arabia — “I think it is essential that we talk about stability and not just have a continued free fall like a parachutist without a parachute.”

Yamani spoke at Harvard’s 350th anniversary. Asked how Saudi oil policy was made, he said: “We play it by ear.” The remark was interpreted in Riyadh as criticism. A month later, he learned from a television broadcast that he had been fired. Twenty-four years — “a good, long run in any position anywhere.” OPEC agreed to $18 a barrel. The price war was over.

Chapter 22: Epilogue — Kuwait

State oil companies went downstream — Venezuela built U.S. refineries; Kuwait bought gas stations and 22% of BP; Saudi Arabia acquired half of Texaco’s American stations. Thatcher sold the government’s BP stake, reversing Churchill’s 1914 decision. At the 1985 Bonn summit, not a single word about oil appeared in the communiqué. The crisis seemed over.

At a 1989 discussion, an oil minister was asked how long the lessons of the 1970s and 1980s would be remembered. “About three years, without reminding,” he said. Within a year, he was no longer minister. A month later, his country was invaded. On August 2, 1990, Saddam Hussein invaded Kuwait. Mastery itself was once more the prize.

Trading & Investing Lessons

Commodity Cycles Are Driven by Psychology

Every oil boom in this book — Pithole, East Texas, Libya, the 1970s — follows the same pattern: scarcity drives prices up, high prices attract capital and innovation, oversupply crashes prices. The lag between investment and production creates violent swings. Traders who understand the cycle’s rhythm profit; those caught at either extreme are destroyed.

Geopolitical Risk Is Permanent and Underpriced

Markets consistently discount geopolitical threats until they materialize. Washington greeted Faisal’s 1973 oil weapon warning with “a large degree of disbelief.” Carter toasted the Shah as an “island of stability” months before revolution. The minister who predicted three years of memory lost his country. Energy positions should always price in the possibility of sudden disruption.

Spare Capacity Is the Master Variable

The most dangerous moment in oil markets is not a specific event but the erosion of spare capacity. In 1973, surplus was 1% of consumption. Any disruption became catastrophic. Monitor spare capacity as the single most important leading indicator for energy markets.

The Swing Producer Always Suffers

Saudi Arabia nearly bankrupted itself defending OPEC prices in the 1980s while others cheated on quotas. The swing producer absorbs all the pain. This dynamic applies to any cartel or dominant market maker — the entity that adjusts bears disproportionate cost.

Control of Resources Is Temporary

Every concession in this book was eventually nationalized. Standard Oil’s monopoly was broken. The Seven Sisters lost their empire. Companies that assume permanent control of strategic resources are building on sand. Invest in adaptability, not in the permanence of current arrangements.

Panic Creates More Shortage Than the Event Itself

The second oil shock proved that panic buying — motorists topping off tanks, companies building inventories — can double the effective shortage. The actual Iranian disruption was 4–5%; panic made it feel like 10%. Behavioral cascades are the real price driver in crisis.

Key Takeaways

Oil Is Never Just a Commodity

It is simultaneously a commercial product, a strategic asset, and a political weapon. Every attempt to treat it as purely one of these fails.

Technology Creates and Destroys Oil Empires

Drilling replaced surface skimming. Tankers killed geography. Synthetic fuels sustained Nazi Germany. Conservation after 1973 broke OPEC’s grip. Futures markets transformed pricing. Each innovation reshuffled power.

The “Oil Curse” Is Real

Venezuela, Iran, Mexico, Nigeria, Iraq — oil wealth corroded institutions, fueled authoritarianism, and ultimately impoverished the countries that depended on it. Perez Alfonzo’s “excrement of the devil” proved prophetic.

Surplus Masks Fragility

Periods of abundant supply breed complacency. The Bonn summit that ignored oil in 1985 was followed by Kuwait’s invasion five years later. Markets and governments consistently forget how precarious energy security is.

Integration Is the Winning Strategy

From Rockefeller through Aramco through state company downstream expansion, the most durable competitive advantage in oil is vertical integration — controlling the chain from wellhead to gas pump.

Contrarian Ideas

The 1911 Dissolution Helped Standard Oil

The thirty-four successor companies, freed to compete against each other, collectively became far more valuable than the unified trust. Rockefeller’s wealth actually increased after the breakup.

Conservation, Not Alternative Energy, Broke OPEC

The response to the 1970s shocks was not solar or nuclear but efficiency — the U.S. became 25% more efficient, Japan 31%. Demand fell more than supply rose. Conservation was the “hidden fuel.”

OPEC Was Irrelevant for Its First Decade

From 1960 to 1970, OPEC accomplished nothing. The real shift was the disappearance of surplus capacity and the withdrawal of British military power from the Gulf — structural changes that had nothing to do with OPEC meetings.

Japan’s Pearl Harbor Attack Failed on Its Own Terms

The entire war was fought for oil. Yet the attack planners forgot to destroy Pearl Harbor’s oil tanks. Nimitz said this would have prolonged the war two years. Japan fought the war of oil without thinking about oil at the decisive moment.

High Oil Prices Are Self-Correcting

Every price spike in this book triggered the forces that destroyed it — conservation, new production, substitution. The Shah who pushed $11.65 in 1973 was overthrown partly because oil wealth destabilized Iran. The oil weapon turned on those who wielded it.

Proposals & Frameworks

Three Themes of Oil History

Yergin identifies three interwoven strands: (1) oil as the foundation of capitalism and modern business, (2) oil as a strategic commodity intertwined with geopolitics and war, (3) oil as the substance that created “Hydrocarbon Society.”

The Commodity-Strategic Duality

Oil oscillates between periods when it behaves like an ordinary commodity (subject to supply and demand) and periods when it becomes a strategic weapon (subject to political will). The transition between these modes is always sudden and always underestimated.

The Concession Lifecycle

Every major oil concession follows the same arc: discovery, development, rising nationalism, renegotiation, and eventual nationalization. D’Arcy’s 1901 Persian concession was the first granted and the first canceled.

The Price Spiral

Price increases trigger conservation and new supply, which trigger price collapse, which triggers complacency, which triggers the next crisis. The cycle length is roughly a decade.

What Didn’t Fit

The Pine Root Campaign

In the final months of the Pacific War, Japan launched a desperate campaign to extract aviation fuel from pine tree roots. Two hundred roots would keep a plane airborne for an hour, they claimed. Each gallon required 2.5 man-days of labor. The total output was 3,000 barrels. None was ever tested in an actual aircraft. When Americans tried it in captured jeeps, the fuel gummed up engines beyond use. The campaign epitomized the gap between desperation and physics — ideology cannot substitute for hydrocarbons.

Penn Square and the Gucci Loafers

The 1980s oil bust destroyed more than producers. Penn Square Bank in Oklahoma, whose lending officer drank Amaretto from Gucci loafers at closing parties, generated $2 billion in bad energy loans sold to Continental Illinois. Continental suffered the largest bank run in history. The episode proved that oil booms create not just industrial excess but financial contagion — energy lending “instantaneously went out of fashion.”

Getty’s Pay Phone

J. Paul Getty, identified by Fortune as the richest man in the world, installed a pay telephone for guests at his English manor. Married five times, he once fired all employees during the Depression and rehired them at lower salaries. After the Nazi seizure of Austria, he entered the Rothschild home to look at the furniture. When asked about his wealth, he quipped: “If you can count your money, you don’t have a billion dollars.” The geologist who found his colossal oil field received $1,200.

The Suez Sixpence and Literary Consolation

During the Suez crisis, Macmillan read thousands of pages of novels in two weeks to keep from going “barmy.” After succeeding Eden as Prime Minister, he soothed his soul with Pride and Prejudice. Belgium banned private driving on Sundays. Britain rationed gasoline — the ration was called the “Suez sixpence.” Eden, retired and broken, prayed for “a lake of oil” under the North Sea. That was exactly what they were beginning to find.

Carlos at OPEC Vienna

In December 1975, the Venezuelan terrorist “Carlos” led an armed assault on OPEC headquarters in Vienna. Yamani spent 44 hours with worry beads, convinced he would die. The attackers demanded a plane, flew to Algeria, and eventually surrendered. The episode demonstrated that oil’s new wealth had made OPEC ministers targets of revolutionary violence — the prize was now so vast that even terrorists came calling.

The Prize: The Epic Quest for Oil, Money & Power
Daniel Yergin · Simon & Schuster, 1991 · ~900 pages (main text)
Winner of the Pulitzer Prize for General Nonfiction. A panoramic narrative history of the oil industry from the drilling of the first well in Pennsylvania through Iraq’s invasion of Kuwait, tracing oil’s role in shaping capitalism, warfare, and modern civilization.

About the Author

Daniel Yergin is one of the world’s leading authorities on energy, international politics, and economics. He earned his Ph.D. from Cambridge University and co-founded Cambridge Energy Research Associates (CERA), which became the premier energy consulting firm globally. His research draws on seven years of interviews, archival work, and access to corporate and government records spanning a dozen countries.

Yergin’s later works include The Commanding Heights (1998), on the battle between government and the marketplace, and The Quest (2011), which extends The Prize’s narrative through the age of climate change and renewable energy. The New Map (2020) examines how energy revolutions are redrawing geopolitics. He has served on numerous government advisory boards and received the United States Energy Award for “lifelong achievements in energy.”

Newsweek described him as “one of those rare historians who can bring the past to life on the page” — combining a novelist’s instinct for character and drama with an expert’s command of the economic and strategic forces that shaped the twentieth century.

Why This Book Matters

The Prize is the definitive history of the commodity that shaped the modern world. No other book weaves together the personalities, politics, technology, and economics of oil with such narrative power. It explains why Churchill converted the Royal Navy to oil, why Japan attacked Pearl Harbor, why the Middle East became the center of global conflict, and why an embargo by desert kingdoms could bring industrial civilization to its knees. These are not separate stories — they are one story, and Yergin tells it as no one else has.

The book’s insight — that oil is never just a commodity but always simultaneously a strategic weapon, a political instrument, and the lifeblood of civilization — has only become more relevant in the decades since its publication. Every energy crisis since 1991 has validated Yergin’s framework: the assumption that oil has been tamed is always the prelude to the next disruption. For anyone who trades commodities, follows geopolitics, or wants to understand why the modern world works the way it does, The Prize is essential reading.

The minister’s warning — “about three years, without reminding” — remains the book’s most haunting line. It is a reminder that markets and nations have short memories, that the lessons of crisis fade faster than the crises themselves, and that mastery itself is always the prize of the venture.
