Glossary

Industry Terms

Posted price: The publicly announced price at which oil companies offered to buy crude from producing countries — the basis for calculating royalties and taxes. Not a true market price but an administered one that became the fulcrum of the entire concession system.

Rule of capture: Legal doctrine from English game law: whoever pumps oil from a common underground reservoir owns it, regardless of property lines. Drove chaotic overproduction from Pennsylvania through East Texas until prorationing killed it.

Prorationing: Government-imposed limits on oil production to prevent waste and stabilize prices. The Texas Railroad Commission became the de facto regulator of American oil output and the model OPEC later copied.

Concession: A government grant giving a company exclusive rights to explore and produce oil in a defined area for a set period, typically 60–99 years in early deals. Every major concession in this book was eventually nationalized.

Fifty-fifty: The profit-sharing formula that replaced flat royalties after 1950 — producing countries received half of net profits. Began in Venezuela, spread to the Middle East via the foreign tax credit mechanism.

Netback deal: A pricing mechanism guaranteeing refiners a predetermined profit margin, used by Saudi Arabia in 1985 to recapture market share. Destroyed price discipline and crashed oil to $10.

Participation: Producing countries acquiring partial ownership of oil operations as an alternative to outright nationalization. Yamani called it “a bond that would be indissoluble, like a Catholic marriage.”

Swing producer: The country that adjusts its output up or down to balance world supply and demand. Saudi Arabia played this role from the 1970s onward — and was nearly destroyed by it in the 1980s when its production fell below British North Sea levels.

Organizations

Standard Oil Trust: Rockefeller’s monopoly, controlling 90% of American refining by 1879. Dissolved by the Supreme Court in 1911 into thirty-four successor companies that became ExxonMobil, Chevron, and others.

Royal Dutch/Shell: The 60/40 merger (1907) of Royal Dutch and Shell Transport, engineered by Henri Deterding to challenge Standard Oil globally. The only serious rival.

Seven Sisters: The seven Western oil majors that dominated the postwar industry: Jersey Standard, Socony, Socal, Texaco, Gulf, BP, and Shell. Term coined by Enrico Mattei of Italy as an insult.

OPEC: Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries, founded September 14, 1960 in Baghdad by Saudi Arabia, Venezuela, Kuwait, Iraq, and Iran. Dismissed by companies and the CIA for its first decade.

IEA: International Energy Agency, created in 1974 as the Western consumer nations’ counterweight to OPEC. France called it “a machine de guerre” and refused to join.

Aramco: Arabian-American Oil Company, the consortium of four American companies that developed Saudi oil. Nationalized in stages from the 1970s, formally completed 1990.

Key Concepts

Red Line Agreement: The 1928 pact binding Turkish Petroleum Company partners not to operate independently within the old Ottoman Empire boundaries, drawn by Gulbenkian on a map with a thick red pencil.

As-Is Agreement: The secret 1928 pact at Achnacarry Castle where the three major oil groups agreed to freeze market shares. Companies referred to each other as “friends.”

Hot oil: Production exceeding state quotas, smuggled through secret pipelines at night. Origin of the name: a militiaman was told to lean against a warm pipeline tank on a cold night.

Oil weapon: The use of oil embargoes and production cuts as political leverage, most dramatically deployed in October 1973 during the Yom Kippur War.

Chapter 1: Oil on the Brain

The oil industry was born from a missing payment of $526.08. In 1854, a group of New Haven investors hired Benjamin Silliman Jr., Yale’s most distinguished chemist, to analyze whether “rock oil” — a dark substance skimmed from creeks in northwestern Pennsylvania — could serve as an illuminant. Silliman withheld his report over the unpaid fee, hid it with a friend, and disappeared on a tour of the South. When finally released on April 16, 1855, his findings were electrifying: rock oil could be distilled into a high-quality illuminating fluid. The report became “a turning point in the establishment of the petroleum business.”

The visionary behind the venture was George Bissell, a self-made polymath fluent in nine languages who had spotted a rock oil sample at Dartmouth College. Self-supporting from age twelve, Bissell had worked his way through Dartmouth by teaching and writing articles, then became a professor of Latin and Greek before reinventing himself as a lawyer. In 1853, passing through western Pennsylvania, he saw the primitive oil-gathering industry with its skimmings and oil-soaked rags. In 1856, sheltering from the heat under a druggist’s awning on Broadway, he saw an advertisement showing drilling derricks for salt — and the last piece fell into place. If you could drill for salt, why not drill for oil?

The context was desperate need. Whale oil cost $2.50 a gallon and whale schools were being decimated. The most popular cheaper alternative, camphene derived from turpentine, produced good light but had “the unfortunate drawback of being highly flammable, compounded by an even more unattractive tendency to explode in people’s houses.” Town gas was expensive. The industrial revolution demanded lubrication for its machines — power looms and steam presses created too much friction for lard. Abraham Gesner, a Canadian doctor turned geologist, had developed “kerosene” from asphalt — the name from the Greek keros (wax) and elaion (oil). By 1859, thirty-four companies produced $5 million a year of coal-oils.

Oil was hardly unfamiliar to mankind. Bitumen had caulked the walls of Babylon and probably Noah’s ark. The Roman naturalist Pliny cataloged its pharmaceutical uses — it checked bleeding, healed wounds, treated cataracts, and was “useful for straightening out eyelashes which inconvenience the eyes.” Greek fire — a mixture of petroleum and lime that ignited on contact with water — was a state secret more terrible than gunpowder, hurled from Byzantine ships for centuries.

The investors recruited Edwin Drake, a jovial jack-of-all-trades chosen mainly because he had a railroad pass and nothing better to do. The banker James Townsend sent letters ahead addressed to “Colonel” E. L. Drake — a title invented to impress the frontier backwoodsmen. The stratagem worked. Drake arrived in Titusville, population 125, in December 1857. The first drillers he hired simply disappeared or begged off — they thought he was insane. He tied compensation to one dollar per foot drilled. His blacksmith driller Uncle Billy Smith finally joined in spring 1859.

On August 27, 1859, at sixty-nine feet, the drill dropped into a crevice. On Sunday morning, Uncle Billy peered down and saw dark fluid rising. Drake attached a hand pump — doing exactly what scoffers had ridiculed. That same day he received Townsend’s letter ordering him to shut down and come home. A week earlier, he would have obeyed. Farmers shouted “The Yankee has struck oil.” The age of petroleum had begun.

“As an illuminator the oil is without a figure … the brightest and yet the cheapest in the world.” —Scientific American, 1860

What followed was America’s first oil boom. Production surged from 450,000 barrels in 1860 to three million by 1862. Prices swung wildly — ten dollars a barrel in January 1861, fifty cents by June, ten cents by year’s end. The Civil War stimulated demand by cutting off turpentine from the South and replacing lost cotton export revenue with kerosene exports. Pithole exploded from dense forest to a town of 15,000 with fifty hotels, two banks, and a post office handling 5,000 letters daily. Then production gave out — a parcel sold for two million dollars was auctioned for $4.37 a decade later. Bissell grew wealthy and donated a gymnasium to Dartmouth with bowling alleys “in remembrance of disciplinary troubles” from that “sinful sport.” Townsend felt denied credit.

Drake himself lost everything through improvident speculation and became a semi-invalid in poverty. He wrote from destitution: “If you have any of the milk of human kindness left in your bosom for me or my family, send me some money.” Pennsylvania granted him a small pension in 1873. The rule of capture — based on English game law about migratory animals — meant surface owners could drain common underground pools, driving frenzied competition and spectacular waste. Pipelines overcame the teamsters’ stranglehold despite armed attacks, arson, and sabotage. Oil exchanges emerged; futures became “the favorite speculative commodity of the time.”

Chapter 2: Rockefeller’s Standard Oil

In February 1865, a twenty-six-year-old Cleveland refiner named John D. Rockefeller bought out his partner Maurice Clark at auction for $72,500. “I ever point to that day as the beginning of the success I have made in my life.” Tall, thin, and taciturn, with cold blue eyes that made people uneasy, Rockefeller was methodical to an extreme. His father, a traveling salesman who sold herbal remedies, boasted: “I trade with the boys and skin ’em.” The son at age seven launched a turkey-selling venture. Mathematics was his best subject. He held “intimate conversations” with himself, counseling caution, building cash reserves so he could exploit every downturn while competitors went bankrupt.

Rockefeller’s genius was integration — he controlled not just refining but timber for barrels, tank cars, warehouses, and boats on the Hudson. His partner Henry Flagler, who had gone broke in salt and learned the value of “cooperation,” became his closest colleague — their desks sat back to back. Flagler devised transportation arrangements that extracted not only rebates from railroads but drawbacks — competitors unknowingly subsidized Standard Oil with every barrel they shipped. “Few business practices did as much to rouse public antipathy.”

On January 10, 1870, Standard Oil was established — the name chosen to indicate “a standard quality of product.” Rockefeller held a quarter of the stock, already controlling a tenth of American refining. The South Improvement Company scheme in 1872 provoked fury in the Oil Regions — three thousand men marched on the Titusville Opera House to denounce “the Monster” and “the Forty Thieves.” By spring 1872, Rockefeller already controlled most of Cleveland’s refining. “Now try our plan.” Standard operated through secretly controlled firms, communicated in code (Standard was “Morose”), and would “make him feel sick” or give competitors “a good sweating.” By 1879, Standard controlled ninety percent of refining capacity.

The Standard Oil Trust Agreement (January 2, 1882) formalized the empire. Rockefeller held 191,700 of 700,000 shares. Management ran by committee: Domestic Trade, Export Trade, Manufacturing, Pipe Line, Lubricating, and the all-powerful Executive Committee. Headquarters at 26 Broadway became the nerve center of the world’s largest enterprise. Senior executives lunched daily in a private dining room. William Vanderbilt: “I never came into contact with any class of men so smart and able.” A corporate intelligence system tracked virtually every buyer in the country.

“We must try and not lose our nerve when the market gets to the bottom. We will surely make a great mistake if we do not buy.” —John D. Rockefeller

Rockefeller’s personal frugality was legendary — he once billed a guest $600 for a summer stay. He loved fast horses, played “Buzz” with his children, and could never get past 71 despite his gift for numbers. He recited his favorite verse: “A wise old owl lived in an oak, the more he saw the less he spoke.” He gave away $550 million, founded the University of Chicago, and insisted buildings not bear his name. Mothers warned children: “Rockefeller will get you if you don’t mind.”

The Lima-Indiana fields opened in the mid-1880s — sulfurous “skunk juice” that nobody else wanted. Rockefeller overruled his “conservative brethren” and stored 40 million barrels. Herman Frasch solved the deodorizing problem; the price doubled immediately. Rockefeller ordered “Buy all we can get.” By 1891, Standard produced a quarter of America’s output. Standard was both reviled as “the Octopus” and admired as an “upbuilder.” Henry Demarest Lloyd’s 1881 Atlantic Monthly article “The Story of a Great Monopoly” went through seven printings.

Ida Tarbell’s devastating 1904 exposé — her father had been ruined by Standard — fueled the political movement that culminated in the Supreme Court’s dissolution order of May 15, 1911. Standard was broken into thirty-four successor companies: Jersey Standard became Exxon, Standard of New York became Mobil, Standard of California became Chevron, Standard of Indiana became Amoco. The dissolution irony: Rockefeller’s wealth actually increased as the freed companies competed and grew. Tarbell wrote with astonishment: “It certainly looks as if the Standard Oil Company might be crumbling — crumbling within.”

The Standard Oil story established patterns that would repeat for the next century: the tension between monopoly and competition, the imperative of integration, the rule that whoever controls transportation controls the industry, and the inevitability that political power will eventually confront economic power. Rockefeller built the prototype of the modern multinational corporation. His successors would fight over the world.

Chapter 3: The Global Competitors

Robert Nobel took his brother Ludwig’s money — 25,000 rubles meant for walnut rifle stocks — and bought a Baku refinery in 1873. Ludwig became “the Oil King of Baku,” commissioning the first bulk tanker (the Zoroaster, 1878), hiring the first professional petroleum geologist, and building a vertically integrated company whose workers proudly called themselves “Nobelites.” Russian production reached 10.8 million barrels by 1884 — a third of American output. Black Town had over two hundred refineries producing oil smoke “like confinement in a chimney-pot.”

The Rothschilds — Baron Alphonse (reputedly the best moustaches on the Continent) and Baron Edmond (sponsor of Jewish settlement in Palestine) — financed the Baku-Batum railroad, forming Bnito in 1886. America’s share of world exports fell from 78% in 1888 to 71% by 1891. When Ludwig Nobel died in 1888, newspapers confused the brothers and reported Alfred’s death. Distressed by obituaries condemning him as a “merchant of death,” Alfred rewrote his will — creating the Nobel Prizes. An oil fortune and a mistaken obituary changed the course of science.

Marcus Samuel, son of a shell merchant who sold knickknack boxes at Brighton, conceived a scheme to break Standard’s Asian kerosene grip. He needed new tankers capable of steam cleaning for return cargo, Suez Canal access that Standard had been refused, storage tanks across Asia, and guaranteed Rothschild supply. Standard’s solicitors lobbied against his Suez passage, arguing Russian naval officers would “undertake all kinds of mischief.”

On August 23, 1892, the Murex — named for a seashell, memorializing his father — passed through the Suez Canal. The one oversight: Asian customers refused to relinquish Standard’s blue tins, used for roofing, birdcages, and opium cups. Samuel sent tinplate and ordered red replacements. “What color do you suggest?” cabled Shanghai. Mark answered: “Red!” Red roofs replaced blue across Asia. But Samuel lacked organizational talent — “he ran everything out of his hat.” The brothers’ decisions involved violent quarrels: “calling each other fool, idiot, imbecile, until suddenly they were in agreement again.”

Henri Deterding, who rose from bank clerk to managing director of Royal Dutch, forced the decisive merger. Fred Lane resigned from Shell’s board with a devastating critique of Samuel: “Business like this cannot be conducted by an occasional glance in one’s spare time.” Deterding insisted on a 60–40 split in Royal Dutch’s favor. Samuel protested; Deterding refused to budge: “I am at present in a generous mood. If you leave this room without accepting it, the offer is off.” Samuel accepted. In 1907, the Royal Dutch/Shell Group was born — the only serious global rival to Standard Oil.

Deterding sent Mark Abrahams to Tulsa in 1912 — arriving with his own typewriter and $2,500 in a money belt — to create Roxana Petroleum. “At last we are in America!” Standard had tried and failed to acquire Royal Dutch — Dutch officials and the company itself intervened. A special class of preference stock was created, admission by invitation only, to prevent hostile takeover. Standard’s agents reported unhappily that Royal Dutch would never merge; it was not merely a “sentimental barrier” — the managers greatly enjoyed receiving 15% of profits.

Meanwhile in Baku, a young Georgian seminarian named Joseph Djugashvili — later Stalin — organized revolutionary cells among oil workers. A secret printing operation called “Nina” produced Lenin’s Iskra. Baku oil workers struck in 1903. The 1905 Revolution destroyed two- thirds of all Russian oil wells. Stalin later: “There in Baku I received my second baptism in revolutionary combat.” By 1912, the Rothschilds — weary of anti-Semitism, strikes, and arson — sold their Russian holdings to Royal Dutch/Shell, transforming uncertain assets into diversified international holdings.

The global oil industry was now a three-way contest: Standard’s American empire fragmenting under antitrust pressure, Royal Dutch/Shell expanding aggressively across four continents, and the Russian fields descending into revolutionary chaos. The stage was set for the next chapter: the discovery that would tie oil permanently to the fate of empires.

Chapter 4: Persia and the Fateful Plunge

In 1901, William Knox D’Arcy, an Australian gold millionaire who kept the only private box at Epsom beside the royal box, signed a sixty-year concession covering three-quarters of Persia. Antoine Kitabgi had sought “a capitalist of the highest order”; what he found was “an investor and speculator, not a manager.” On May 28, 1901, Shah Muzaffar al-Din signed: £20,000 cash, £20,000 in shares, 16% of profits, sixty years, three-quarters of the country. D’Arcy spent over £200,000 in brutal conditions — heat reaching 120 degrees, workers for whom “even the wheelbarrow was a startling innovation.”

His geologist George Reynolds directed exploration at inaccessible Chiah Surkh — equipment shipped to Basra, up the Tigris, then by mule across mountain passes. D’Arcy telegraphed: “Delay serious. Pray expedite. Every purse has its limit.” He sought Admiralty help; the Chancellor refused. Lord Strathcona, eighty-four years old, agreed to head a “syndicate of patriots” — not for commerce “but really from an imperial point of view.” Partner Burmah Oil’s vice-chairman visited D’Arcy’s Epsom box; the rich food made him sick for weeks. He never accepted another invitation.

Operations were “not all beer and skittles” — drinking water was “water with dung in suspension,” the nearest dentist 1,500 miles away in Karachi. On May 26, 1908, at four in the morning, a gusher rose fifty feet at Masjid-i-Suleiman. Wilson sent coded news: “See Psalm 104 verse 15 third sentence.” Reynolds received Burmah’s shutdown letter the same day — echoing Drake half a century earlier. The Anglo-Persian Oil Company went public; investors stood ten deep at the Bank of Scotland counter. D’Arcy received shares worth £895,000 but lamented: “I feel like signing away a child.”

The discovery set the stage for Churchill’s fateful plunge. Admiral “Jacky” Fisher — born in Ceylon, went to sea at thirteen, “a mixture of Machiavelli and a child” — had been preaching oil for years. He told Churchill: “The first of all necessities is SPEED.” Oil offered four extra knots over coal. Fisher: “Your old women will have a nice time of it when German Motor Battleships are cocking a snook at our Tortoises!” The three naval programs of 1912–14 — the greatest addition in Royal Navy history — were all oil-based. The Queen Elizabeth class was the “fateful plunge.”

Charles Greenway (“Champagne Charlie,” “Old Spats and Monocle”) campaigned against Shell’s “unwelcome embrace,” stressing Samuel’s “Jewishness” and Deterding’s “Dutchness.” On June 17, 1914, Churchill told Parliament: “If we cannot get oil, we cannot get corn, cotton and a thousand commodities necessary for the preservation of the economic energies of Great Britain.” The government invested £2.2 million for 51% of Anglo-Persian. The bill passed 254 to 18. Samuel Samuel popped up three times to object; Churchill: “He had better hear the case for the prosecution before he offers an argument for the defense.”

“Mastery itself was the prize of the venture.” —Winston Churchill

Fisher privately admired Deterding: “He is Napoleon and Cromwell rolled into one. He is the greatest man I ever met.” Deterding wrote patriotically to Churchill on July 31, 1914, promising oil and tankers. Eleven days after the Parliamentary vote, Archduke Franz Ferdinand was assassinated. Churchill’s decision to stake the Royal Navy on oil — trading the security of Welsh coal for the speed of Persian petroleum — was the single most consequential energy decision of the twentieth century. It tied the British Empire to the Middle East, made oil a matter of national survival, and established the pattern every great power would follow: whoever controls the oil controls the future.

The debate in Parliament was fierce but brief. Samuel Samuel popped up three times to object on behalf of Shell’s interests. Churchill swatted him down: “He had better hear the case for the prosecution before he offers an argument for the defense.” Fisher privately admired Deterding despite the rivalry: “He is Napoleon and Cromwell rolled into one. He is the greatest man I ever met.” Deterding, for his part, wrote patriotically to Churchill on July 31, 1914, promising oil and tankers for the coming conflict.

The coal-to-oil conversion also established a template that would recur throughout this book: a strategic decision taken for military advantage creates a permanent dependency that shapes politics, economics, and foreign policy for generations. Britain in 1914, Japan in 1941, America in 1973 — the pattern is always the same. The advantages of oil are irresistible; the vulnerabilities are discovered later.

Chapter 5: Blood of Victory — WWI

General Gallieni commandeered 3,000 Paris taxicabs for the First Battle of the Marne in September 1914. “By the meter or on a flat rate?” asked a driver. “By the meter.” “All right, let’s go,” he said, putting down his flag. The troops rushed to the critical point and saved Paris. It was the internal combustion engine’s first decisive military act.

Colonel Swinton conceived armored vehicles impervious to machine guns and barbed wire. The high command ignored it; Churchill funded development from Navy coffers. Various code names were considered — “cistern,” “reservoir” — and it became the “tank.” First used prematurely at the Somme in 1916, tanks proved decisive at Amiens in August 1918: 456 tanks broke through what Ludendorff called the “black day of the German Army.” When the High Command declared victory impossible, the first reason given was the tank.

British motor vehicles grew from 827 motorcars at the war’s outbreak to 56,000 trucks, 23,000 cars, and 34,000 motorcycles. The United States brought another 50,000 gasoline-driven vehicles. “The victory of the Allies over Germany was in some ways the victory of the truck over the locomotive.” General Foch had initially dismissed aviation: “Good sport, but for the Army worthless.” Yet by 1918, wartime aircraft production reached 55,000 for Britain, 68,000 for France, and 48,000 for Germany. Aircraft speeds doubled to over 120 mph.

Marcus Samuel masterminded extracting toluol from Borneo crude to make TNT. Shell’s Rotterdam plant was secretly dismantled at night, each piece numbered and camouflaged, then re-erected in Somerset. It provided 80% of British military TNT. The shell merchant’s son had become an arsenal.

“Empire Jack” Norton-Griffiths destroyed seventy Romanian refineries and 800,000 tons of crude ahead of the advancing Germans, kicking challengers and pulling his revolver on anyone who objected: “I don’t speak your blasted language.” The destruction “did materially reduce the oil supplies,” Ludendorff later admitted.

By 1917, unrestricted submarine warfare threatened Allied oil supplies. The Murex — Samuel’s pioneering tanker — was sunk. Clemenceau appealed to President Wilson that gasoline was “as vital as blood in the coming battles.” The United States supplied 80% of Allied wartime petroleum. “Gasolineless Sundays” were instituted; Wilson: “I suppose I must walk to church.”

The “jerrycan” had not yet been invented, and fuel logistics were primitive by later standards. But the lesson was already clear: mechanized warfare consumed petroleum at a rate that dwarfed all pre-war estimates. The side with more oil won; the side without it lost. This would be proved again, far more devastatingly, in the next war.

Germany’s Air Force and mechanized warfare would have ceased within two months by October 1918. Lord Curzon pronounced the epitaph: “The Allied cause had floated to victory upon a wave of oil.” Senator Berenger called it “the blood of victory.” The war had proved oil’s military supremacy over coal beyond any doubt. Every major power now understood that access to petroleum was a matter of national survival. The scramble for the world’s oil was about to begin.

Chapter 6: The Middle East Opens

Calouste Gulbenkian, an Armenian suspicious enough to employ two sets of doctors because he trusted neither, architected the Turkish Petroleum Company. At age seven, his first transaction was exchanging a silver piece for an antique coin. He was drenched by a Baku gusher but never visited oil country again. His motto: “Never give up an oil concession.” When Walter Teagle of Jersey Standard called him an “oil merchant” at a famous luncheon, Gulbenkian reddened: “I classify myself as a business architect. I designed this Turkish Petroleum Company … Now the three of you are trying to throw me out on my ass.”

On October 15, 1927, a gusher at Baba Gurgur in Iraq flowed at 95,000 barrels per day near holes that had burned since Nebuchadnezzar’s time — the biblical “fiery furnace.” Gulbenkian drew a thick red line on a map along the old Ottoman Empire’s boundaries. The Red Line Agreement bound partners not to operate independently within that territory. “Mr. Five Percent” had never seen an oil tanker, yet he had locked up the richest petroleum province on earth.

The postwar diplomatic scramble was ferocious. Clemenceau casually traded Mosul for British support in Syria — an exchange neither prime minister shared with his foreign minister. The San Remo Agreement of 1920 gave France 25% of Mesopotamian oil. Washington erupted in protest. Congress passed the Mineral Leasing Act of 1920, denying drilling rights to foreigners whose governments denied reciprocal access. Commerce Secretary Hoover organized an American consortium; Teagle fought his way in through the Open Door policy.

Teagle was second-generation oil — his maternal grandfather was Maurice Clark, the partner Rockefeller bought out at auction. At Cornell, he scored a perfect hundred on a de-sulfurization thesis; his father’s telegram said only: “Come home at once.” He fired a still at nineteen cents an hour, then became a formidable negotiator: “He’d trade and trade. If it was company money, he’d think he was paying too much for a five cent cigar.” At thirty-nine he became president of Jersey Standard.

The British installed Faisal on Iraq’s throne — Lawrence of Arabia called him “an absolute ripper.” He was crowned in August 1921, having been deposed from Syria and found sitting on his luggage at a Palestine railway station. His brother Abdullah got “the vacant lot” of Transjordan. Iraq was a collection of Shia Arabs, Sunni Arabs, Jews, Kurds, and Yazidis — Jews were the largest single group in Baghdad. Colonel Mercier created the Compagnie Francaise des Petroles in 1924, though “none of the initial investors begged for the favor” — ninety reluctant banks and companies had to be assembled.

The Red Line Agreement and the broader postwar oil diplomacy established a pattern that would define the century: access to Middle Eastern oil required navigating a tangle of imperial rivalries, corporate ambitions, local politics, and personality clashes that made purely commercial logic impossible. Oil was already, as one observer put it, “90 percent political and 10 percent oil.” U.S. motor vehicles had jumped from 1.8 to 9.2 million between 1914 and 1920, and the demand machine was only accelerating.

Gulbenkian would later fight the dissolution of his carefully constructed architecture. At the Hotel Aviz in Lisbon, he became a tourist attraction, carefully checking odometer readings on his car. His tactic was “make demands step by step.” At the final signing in November 1948, he found “one more point.” Only one bottle of champagne was drunk among twelve negotiators. He signed at 1:30 AM. “Mr. Five Percent was no more” — but his legacy, the framework that governed Middle Eastern oil for decades, endured long after.

The architecture Gulbenkian built — shared concessions, negotiated quotas, interlocking partnerships — was an attempt to impose order on a chaotic industry. It worked for a generation. But like every arrangement in this book, it contained the seeds of its own destruction: the producing countries were not at the table, and they would eventually demand their seat.

Chapter 7: The Age of Gasoline

In 1919, Captain Eisenhower joined a military convoy crossing the United States, averaging five and two-thirds miles per hour through roads varying “from average to nonexistent.” Some days, counting on sixty miles, they managed three. The journey was called “Through Darkest America with Truck and Tank.” As President, Eisenhower would champion the interstate highway system, transforming America into a nation built around the automobile.

By 1929, America had 23.1 million automobiles — 78% of the world’s total, compared to one car for every 702 people in Japan and one for every 6,130 in the Soviet Union. Total U.S. oil demand rose from 1.03 million barrels per day in 1919 to 2.58 million by 1929. Oil’s share of energy rose from 10% to 25%. Gas stations multiplied from 12,000 in 1921 to 143,000 by 1929. Los Angeles pioneered the standardized service station. Shell required free uniforms and no magazine reading on duty.

Bruce Barton told oil men: “It is the juice of the fountain of eternal youth that you are selling. It is health. It is comfort. And you have sold merely a bad smelling liquid at so many cents per gallon.” Oil companies created “uniquely American contributions to cartography” — the road map, started by Gulf in 1914. The “new light” had given way to the “new fuel.”

The Teapot Dome scandal demonstrated oil’s corrupting power. Interior Secretary Albert Fall — “the frontiersman” with a black Stetson — leased naval oil reserves to Sinclair and Doheny. Teagle warned: “All through the industry it smells.” Harding: “If Albert Fall isn’t an honest man, I’m not fit to be President.” Doheny delivered $100,000 in “a little black bag.” Fall became the first Cabinet officer convicted of a felony. Sinclair stopped at a board meeting on the way to jail. Doheny was acquitted: “You can’t convict a million dollars.”

Rockefeller Jr. led a proxy fight to oust Standard of Indiana’s Colonel Stewart over Liberty Bond kickbacks, winning with 60% of votes: “Not for mere profits but in the name of decency.” The torsion balance, seismograph, magnetometer, and micropaleontology transformed oil finding. Signal Hill in 1921 made California the number-one producer. Deepest wells went from 6,000 feet in 1918 to 10,000 by 1930.

Harry Doherty — “the professor” with oversized glasses — argued the rule of capture must be eliminated. Flush production exhausted underground pressure, leaving oil permanently unrecoverable. Fields should be unitized. The industry called him “that crazy man” and barred him three times from API meetings. J. Howard Pew countered that rivers would change course before petroleum was exhausted. The Standard successor companies invaded each other’s territories. Phillips opened its first station in 1927; by 1930 it had 6,750 outlets.

Mexico’s Golden Lane had briefly made it the world’s second-largest producer. Lord Cowdray’s Potrero del Llano 4 flowed at 110,000 barrels per day. But the Revolution’s Article 27 restored national subsoil ownership, and Cardenas ordered preparations to set oil fields on fire if America invaded. Production fell 80% in a decade. Venezuela offered a friendlier climate under General Gomez, a cruel dictator who fathered 97 illegitimate children but offered political predictability. Shell’s Barroso well in December 1922 blew out at 100,000 barrels per day — discovered by George Reynolds, the same man who found oil in Persia. Jersey’s 4,200 underwater acres in Lake Maracaibo were considered a joke — “the company could go into the fishing business.”

The Front Uni of 1922 pledged to fight the Soviets together, but Deterding launched a price war when Socony dealt with Moscow. Teagle: “The idea of trying to be on friendly terms with the man who burglarizes your house has never appealed to me.” Deterding’s anti-communism intensified after marrying White Russian Lydia Pavlova. The fight for new production was global — from Venezuela to the Soviet Union to Sumatra, the major companies scrambled for reserves to feed the insatiable gasoline machine.

Chapter 8: The Flood and the Concessions

Columbus “Dad” Joiner, seventy years old, drilled with rusted equipment on Daisy Bradford’s farm, guided by “Doc Lloyd” — a 300-pound fake geologist whose only real contribution was telling Joiner where to drill. Workers were paid in “royalty rights.” A Texaco geologist sneered: “I’ll drink every barrel of oil you get out of that hole.” On October 3, 1930, oil shot above the derrick. A crewman fired a pistol into the spray and was tackled before a spark could cause an explosion. The crowd went mad.

The East Texas field — forty-five miles long, 140,000 acres — was the largest ever found in the lower forty-eight. Prices collapsed to six cents, even two cents a barrel. “Teakettle” refineries offered free chicken dinners with each fill-up. H. L. Hunt — stout, maintaining two wives with growing families — acquired Joiner’s rights over a 36-hour negotiation at the Baker Hotel, armed with secret intelligence on a nearby well. He paid $1.33 million and became one of the world’s richest men. Joiner died at 87, still wildcatting, his net worth barely more than his car.

Oklahoma’s “Alfalfa Bill” Murray declared martial law. Texas Governor Sterling sent the National Guard to “Proration Hill.” “Hot oil” — production above quotas — reached half a million barrels daily, smuggled through secret pipelines at night, hidden in camouflaged tanks covered with weeds. Harold Ickes became Oil Administrator — “a plump, bespectacled gentleman” with stinging polemics and immense self-righteousness. On September 2, 1933, he set monthly state quotas — the rule of capture was overturned: “What had made sense for deer and game birds on estates in medieval England would no longer do.”

In August 1928, Deterding rented a Scottish castle with 50,000 acres of shooting. Teagle, Cadman, and others joined for “grouse shooting” — “it was lousy,” Teagle said, but they were not after grouse. The “As-Is” Agreement: each company got a quota based on its 1928 market share. Companies called each other “friends” while deeply distrusting one another. The domestic U.S. market was explicitly excluded to avoid antitrust. The agreement was undermined by “fringe” players and uncontrollable production.

Reza Shah canceled Anglo-Persian’s concession on November 16, 1932. He had personally beaten an ayatollah. Cadman flew to Tehran and had his pilot taxi visibly from palace windows. The Shah retreated: concession area reduced by three-quarters, extended to 1993. Operations in the oil business in 1930s Europe were “90 percent political and 10 percent oil.”

General Lazaro Cardenas of Mexico signed the expropriation order at 9:45 PM on March 18, 1938, from the Yellow Room and broadcast it live. A six-hour parade followed through Mexico City. Petroleos Mexicanos (Pemex) was established — one of the first important state-owned oil companies. Nazi Germany became Mexico’s number-one petroleum customer. Companies called it the biggest trauma since the Bolshevik Revolution.

In Arabia, Major Frank Holmes — called “Abu al-Naft” (Father of Oil) — won concessions that the majors dismissed. Anglo-Persian’s directors declared Arabia “devoid of all prospects.” When asked what told him Arabia had oil, Holmes tapped his nose: “This was my geologist.” Jack Philby, eccentric father of double agent Kim, told Ibn Saud he was “like folk asleep on buried treasure.” His $1,000-per-month retainer from Standard of California funded Kim’s Cambridge fees, “where Kim was taking the first steps on the way to becoming a Soviet spy.”

Socal won the Saudi concession on May 29, 1933 — payment in gold sovereigns, all bearing male monarchs (not Queen Victoria). Five years of dry wells followed before Well Number 7 struck oil at 4,727 feet. In April 1939, Ibn Saud himself turned the valve for the first cargo. In Kuwait, the Burgan field discovery on February 23, 1938 was so powerful that crude ignited in the sand, transforming the walls into sheets of glass. The Sheikh’s economy had been devastated by Mikimoto’s Japanese cultured pearls, which destroyed the Gulf pearl trade.

Chapter 9: Japan’s Road to War

Japan produced only 7% of its consumed oil; 80% came from the United States, 10% from the Dutch East Indies. The tension between north — the Army’s Manchurian focus against the Soviet threat — and south — the Navy’s drive toward Indonesian oil under the doctrine of hokushu nanshin (“defend in the north, advance to the south”) — defined Japanese strategy for a decade. Sixty percent of the internal market was held by Shell’s Rising Sun and Standard-Vacuum. The 1934 Petroleum Industry Law required six months’ inventories and aimed to squeeze out foreign companies.

On September 18, 1931, the Japanese Army staged a bomb attack on the South Manchurian Railway — damage was only thirty-one inches of track, so negligible that a train passed minutes later. But it gave the pretext to seize Manchuria — “Japan’s life line.” Prime Minister Hamaguchi, who favored cooperation, was shot at a railway station by an ultranationalist youth. “Government by assassination” took hold.

Cordell Hull, the Tennessee backwoodsman turned Secretary of State, held perhaps fifty secret nighttime meetings with Ambassador Nomura at the Wardman Park Hotel. Hull had a devastating advantage: “Magic” had cracked the Japanese diplomatic code “Purple.” In May 1941, the Germans warned Tokyo that the Americans had broken their codes — the Japanese simply did not believe it, convinced Americans were not capable of such a feat.

Admiral Yamamoto — who grew up in oil-producing Niigata, spent four years in America, read Lincoln biographies, and regularly received Life magazine — restricted Navy training to coastal waters to conserve petroleum. He even sponsored a “scientist” who claimed to change water into oil. He scoffed at ultranationalists’ “armchair arguments about war” yet designed the Pearl Harbor attack: “Decide the fate of the war on the very first day.” He told friends: “It’s out of the question! To fight the United States is like fighting the whole world. But it has been decided. So I will do my best. Doubtless I will die.”

Between June 25 and July 2, 1941, Tokyo made the fateful decision: put off doing anything about the Soviet Union, concentrate on the southern strategy. They knew this risked an American embargo, “a matter of life or death to the empire.” Roosevelt did not want a full embargo — he wanted it as a flexible diplomatic tool. But Dean Acheson turned the July 25 asset freeze into a de facto total embargo by blocking release of frozen funds. “Whether or not we had a policy, we had a state of affairs.” Two Japanese tankers sat empty in San Pedro harbor. Ickes had pushed for full embargo; Roosevelt replied: “I simply have not got enough Navy to go around.”

Oil inventories would last no more than two years. At the September Imperial Conference, the Emperor asked how long hostilities would last. The Army Chief said three months. The Emperor retorted sharply — the China Incident had been promised in one month and lasted four years. “The Pacific was boundless.” He read a poem by Emperor Meiji: “Since all are brothers in this world, why is there such constant turmoil?” The hall fell silent. Tojo replaced the fallen Konoye: “Two years from now we will have no petroleum for military use. Ships will stop moving. I fear that we would become a third-class nation.”

On November 22, a “Magic” intercept revealed that American agreement must be received by November 29 — “after that, things are automatically going to happen.” A Japanese naval task force sailed from the Kurile Islands under radio silence. Hull told Stimson: “I have washed my hands of it.” Washington sent a final alert: “This dispatch is to be considered a war warning.”

On December 7, 1941, bombs fell at 7:55 AM. Hull could not control his rage at Nomura: “Infamous falsehoods and distortions on a scale so huge that I never imagined any Government on this planet was capable of uttering them.” Eight battleships sunk, 2,335 servicemen killed. But Admiral Nagumo declined a third wave targeting oil tanks and repair facilities. Nimitz later calculated: “We had about 4.5 million barrels of oil out there and all of it was vulnerable to .50 caliber bullets. Had the Japanese destroyed the oil, it would have prolonged the war another two years.” Oil drove Japan to war — yet they forgot about oil when planning the attack. Yamamoto himself had observed that Japan’s 1904 surprise at Port Arthur wasn’t “thoroughgoing enough.” The same mistake was made again.

Chapter 10: Germany’s Formula

In June 1932, I.G. Farben officials met Hitler, who spent two and a half hours lecturing on motorization: “German motor fuel must become a reality, even if this entails sacrifices.” I.G. Farben promised campaign contributions. Standard Oil’s Frank Howard had visited the Leuna plant in 1926: “The most important development which has ever faced the company since the dissolution.” Standard received patent rights for synthetic fuel; I.G. got 546,000 Standard shares worth $35 million.

By 1940, synthetic fuel output reached 72,000 barrels per day — 46% of total supply and 95% of aviation gasoline. The Four-Year Plan ordered sixfold expansion; costs were “of no importance.” Mussolini confided to Hitler that an oil embargo would have forced him from Abyssinia “within a week.” The plants used more electricity than all of Berlin.

I.G. Farben built plants adjacent to Auschwitz, paying the SS 3–4 marks per day per slave laborer. Half price for children. Workers subsisted on 1,000 calories, worked for months, then died or were killed. Primo Levi (Prisoner 174,517): “The only thing alive are machines and slaves — and the former are more alive than the latter.” By 1944, a third of the synthetic fuel workforce was slave labor. Synthetic fuel was produced at the intersection of industrial genius and industrial evil.

Operation Barbarossa hurled three million men, 600,000 motor vehicles, and 625,000 horses into Russia on June 22, 1941. Stalin had refused all warnings — ordering one defector shot as a trick. Vehicles burned twice the anticipated fuel on bad roads. In August, Hitler overruled generals wanting Moscow: “My generals know nothing about the economic aspects of war.” The Wehrmacht had to reach Baku. “The Volga will be our Mississippi.”

Operation Blau in 1942 targeted the Caucasus oil fields. A Technical Oil Brigade of 15,000 men was assembled to rehabilitate captured fields. At Maikop, Russians destroyed everything so thoroughly that the Germans managed only seventy barrels per day. Mountain troops planted the swastika on Mount Elbrus but couldn’t reach Baku. The devastating irony: Germans ran short of oil in their quest for oil. They transported fuel on the backs of camels. At Stalingrad, the Sixth Army’s tanks had fuel for twenty miles — they needed thirty to escape. Hitler told Manstein in a midnight call: “Unless we get the Baku oil, the war is lost.”

“Unless we get the Baku oil, the war is lost.” —Adolf Hitler to Manstein

Rommel’s Afrika Korps fought brilliantly across North Africa but was strangled by supply lines. At one point 85% of his transport was captured British and American vehicles. He raced 300 miles in one week to El Alamein, sixty miles from Alexandria. Mussolini flew over with a white charger for his triumphant Cairo entry. But supply trucks from Tripoli used more gasoline getting to the front and back than they carried. “My moves would be decided more by the petrol gauge than by tactical requirements.” Montgomery hung Rommel’s drawing in his trailer and waited for the fuel shortage to do its work. His Christmas present from captured stores: coffee in a miniature oil barrel. Rommel’s epitaph: “The battle is fought and decided by the Quartermasters before the shooting begins.”

Rommel was forced to take poison by the SS in October 1944. His final dispatches were consumed with the fuel question. “Shortage of petrol! It’s enough to make one weep.” He remains the supreme example of a commander defeated not by the enemy but by the supply line.

On May 12, 1944, 935 bombers hit Germany’s synthetic fuel plants. Speer declared: “On that day the technological war was decided.” By September, aviation gasoline was 6% of earlier levels. Jet fighters sat without fuel. Pilot training was cut to one hour weekly. In the Battle of the Bulge, Peiper came within a thousand feet of 2.5 million gallons at Stavelot — but didn’t know it was there. He turned away. German trucks were dragged by oxen. Hitler’s body was doused in the last available gasoline.

Chapter 11: Japan’s Achilles’ Heel

Shell men at Balikpapan destroyed four decades of industrial creation in less than a day — the tank farm became a hellish conflagration, flames leaping 150 feet. Of seventy-five stranded, only thirty-five survived. The scorched-earth policy was meant to deny Japan the oil it had gone to war to seize.

It only partly worked. By mid-March 1942, Japan controlled the East Indies. Tojo bragged Hong Kong fell in eighteen days, Singapore in seventy. A “victory fever” gripped Tokyo. Japanese engineers rehabilitated the Indies oil fields with astonishing speed — by 1943, output reached 49.6 million barrels, 75% of the 1940 level. Using Caltex’s abandoned rig, they drilled a super giant field at Minas, Sumatra — a discovery that would have been celebrated in peacetime.

But the fatal weakness was shipping. American submarines — crewed by men the Japanese thought “too soft and luxury-loving” — proved the best in the war. 86% of merchant shipping was sunk. Less than 2% of U.S. naval personnel — the submariners — caused 55% of total Japanese losses. “Towards the end we were fairly certain a tanker would be sunk shortly after departing from port.” Oil imports peaked in early 1943; by early 1945 they ceased entirely.

Civilian gasoline fell to 4% of prewar levels. Vehicles ran on charcoal or wood burners. Potatoes, sugar, rice wine, and sake were requisitioned for conversion to alcohol fuel. In the Marianas campaign of June 1944, the carrier force approached directly rather than circuitously to conserve oil — the result was the “Great Marianas Turkey Shoot” with 273 Japanese planes lost versus 29 American. The oil shortage forced the fleet to split between Japan and Singapore, dividing strength at the worst possible time.

Pilot training was cut to thirty hours, then navigation eliminated — “pilots were simply to follow their leaders.” Japan lost 40% of aircraft on ferrying operations alone. Japan’s synthetic fuel effort failed miserably — only 8% of its 14-million-barrel target. At the Battle of Leyte Gulf in October 1944, Admiral Kurita was forty miles from MacArthur’s invasion beach but pulled away because of fuel shortage. The battle introduced kamikaze suicide pilots — one to three planes achieved what eight bombers and sixteen fighters otherwise required, and fuel was cut in half.

The pine root campaign promised that “two hundred pine roots will keep a plane in the air for an hour.” Each gallon required 2.5 man-days of labor. Only 3,000 barrels were produced; none was ever tried in a plane. The great battleship Yamato — the largest warship ever built — was given enough fuel for a one-way trip to Okinawa and sunk by 300 aircraft on April 7, 1945. Navy fuel oil stood at 0.8 million barrels — below a million, the Navy could not operate.

Japan’s fuel oil inventories had been 29.6 million barrels in April 1937; by July 1945, just 0.8 million. The Americans expected a million military casualties in a Home Islands invasion — contributing to the decision to use the atomic bomb. After Hiroshima and Nagasaki, Admiral Onishi advocated twenty million Japanese sacrificing themselves in suicide attacks. Finding an ambulance with gasoline to save the wounded Tojo after his suicide attempt proved harder than finding a doctor. MacArthur’s motorcade to Yokohama — powered by charcoal — repeatedly broke down over twenty miles. Americans found 316,000 barrels hidden in caves for suicide flights. Pine root gasoline tested in jeeps gummed up engines beyond use.

Japan’s war was, from start to finish, a war about oil. It went to war to secure oil, lost the war because its oil was sunk at sea, and surrendered when its oil was gone. Yet at the decisive moment — Pearl Harbor — it forgot about oil entirely. The irony haunted Nimitz for the rest of his life.

Chapter 12: The Allies’ War

The oil industry in Britain merged into the Petroleum Board. Pumps were painted dark green, all product sold as “Pool. ” Rationing was imposed immediately. Deterding had died in Germany in 1939, having married his German secretary and embraced Nazism — his preference shares were swiftly distributed to prevent German control. Plans were made to destroy British oil stocks if the country was invaded.

America’s secret weapon was a million barrels per day of surplus production capacity — a legacy of 1930s prorationing that became an invaluable strategic margin. Without it, the course of WWII might well have been different. “Old Dr. New Deal had to call in his partner Dr. Win-the-War.” The Justice Department’s 1940 antitrust case against twenty-two major companies was quietly set aside. Production rose from 3.7 million barrels per day in 1940 to 4.7 million in 1945 — a 30% increase calling on the full reserve. The U.S. supplied six of the seven billion barrels consumed by the Allies.

U-boats devastated Atlantic shipping — tankers were “easily recognizable by their distinctive profile.” Admiral Doenitz: “Rescue no one and take no one with you.” In the first three months of 1942, tanker sinkings were four times the number built. Miami’s neon beachfront provided perfect silhouettes for U-boat commanders — hotel owners insisted the lights stay on for tourist season. Crowds at Atlantic City watched tankers explode on the dark horizon. Churchill called it “the blackest cloud which we had to face.” Ralph Davies reported England had only two months of Royal Navy fuel versus a seven-month minimum.

Ickes launched conservation with windshield stickers — “I’m using one-third less gas” — but couldn’t explain the real reason without revealing U-boat intelligence. Congress called it a “phony shortage.” In March 1943, 108 ships were sunk. But in late March, the Allies broke U-boat codes, added support groups, improved radar, and deployed long-range aircraft. In May 1943, 30% of U-boats at sea were lost. Doenitz reported “the greatest crisis in submarine warfare.” After forty-five months, the Battle of the Atlantic was over.

Big Inch (1,254 miles) and Little Inch (1,475 miles) — extraordinary engineering feats — carried crude and products from Texas to the East Coast. Pipelines went from moving 4% to 42% of all oil to the eastern seaboard. One hundred-octane aviation fuel, developed primarily by Shell researchers, gave 15–30% more power. The advantage was proved in the Battle of Britain — Spitfires outperformed Messerschmitts on 87-octane. Catalytic cracking made mass production possible; output grew from under 40,000 barrels per day in 1940 to 514,000 by 1945.

Nationwide gasoline rationing came through a side door — rubber. Japan had cut off 90% of natural rubber supplies. The Baruch Committee recommended gasoline rationing to conserve tires. The basic “A” sticker provided one and a half to four gallons a week. A black market in coupons flourished. Civilian use dropped 30%. A thirty-five-mph speed limit was imposed. “There’s a war on.”

Patton’s Third Army covered 500 miles in a month, hijacking trains and commandeering fuel convoys. “My chief difficulty is not the Germans but gasoline.” On August 30, 1944, supply was cut to a tenth of normal. Eisenhower directed fuel to Montgomery instead. Tanks reached the Meuse with empty gas tanks. “My men can eat their belts, but my tanks have gotta have gas.” The delay let Germans regroup. Nine months of bitter warfare followed — three-quarters of the million Allied casualties occurred after this check. Liddell Hart: “The best chance of a quick finish was probably lost when the ‘gas’ was turned off from Patton’s tanks.”

“This is a war of engines and octanes. I drink to the American auto industry and the American oil industry.” —Joseph Stalin

American forces used one hundred times more gasoline in WWII than in WWI. A typical division: 4,000 horsepower in WWI, 187,000 in WWII. The Army-Navy Petroleum Board pronounced: “At no time did the Services lack for oil in the proper quantities, in the proper kinds and at the proper places” — though mostly true, there was one exception: the unforgiving minute when Patton’s tanks ran dry at the Meuse. Half the tonnage shipped from the U.S. was oil. When a soldier went overseas, sixty-seven pounds of his supplies were petroleum products.

Chapter 13: The New Center of Gravity

Everette DeGolyer, dispatched to the Middle East in 1943, reported proven reserves at about 25 billion barrels — with suspected totals up to 300 billion for the region, 100 billion for Saudi Arabia alone. One mission member called it “the greatest single prize in all history.” DeGolyer’s key judgment: “The center of gravity of world oil production is shifting from the Gulf-Caribbean area to the Middle East.” The U.S. had produced 90% of Allied oil in WWII — the high-water mark it would never reach again.

In 1940, the Middle East produced less than 5% of world oil versus 63% for the United States. The “hundred men” in Saudi Arabia planned to fill wells with cement to deny them to Germany. Ibn Saud faced financial crisis — pilgrim flow halted, drought devastated the economy. “The Arabs have the religion, but the Allies have the money.” Roosevelt initially refused Lend Lease — “a little far afield for us” — but Ickes published “We’re Running Out of Oil!” and convinced Roosevelt that Saudi Arabia was “probably the greatest and richest oil field in the world.”

Ickes aimed for 100% government ownership of Casoc. When the company presidents came, he proposed buying all of it. “They had gone fishing for a cod and had caught a whale.” A $120 million government pipeline plan also died — called “a move towards fascism” by the rest of the industry. Lord Beaverbrook warned: “Oil is the single greatest post-war asset remaining to us.”

After Yalta in February 1945, Roosevelt met Ibn Saud on the USS Quincy. The King traveled with 48 retainers and seven live sheep (reduced from the original hundred). He slept in a tent with Oriental carpets on deck. Roosevelt pushed the elevator’s emergency button to smoke two cigarettes between meetings. The two bonded — Ibn Saud declared himself Roosevelt’s “twin.” “You are luckier because you can still walk.” “No, your chair will take you wherever you want to go.” Roosevelt gave one of his wheelchairs.

Churchill’s meeting with Ibn Saud clashed on customs — “my religion prescribed smoking cigars and drinking alcohol as an absolute sacred rite.” He gave perfumes worth a hundred pounds; the King gave diamond-hilted swords and three thousand pounds of jewels. Churchill promised a Rolls-Royce. Roosevelt showed Ambassador Halifax a map: “Persian oil is yours. We share Iraq and Kuwait. As for Saudi Arabian oil, it’s ours.” The special U.S.-Saudi relationship became “an unlikely union — Bedouin Arabs and Texas oil men.”

James Forrestal championed Middle East oil: “The prestige of the United States is in part related to the wealth of the Government in terms of oil resources.” But he was a minority voice. The Anglo-American Petroleum Agreement, which would have created joint production quotas, was killed by opposition from both independents and majors. Even schoolteachers in Texas mobilized against it.

Roosevelt died April 12, 1945, before resolving oil issues. Ickes resigned in a bitter scrap with Truman. The Oil Czar left office denouncing Truman for “lack of adherence to the strict truth.” Gasoline rationing lifted within 24 hours of Japan’s capitulation — “Fill ’er up!” By 1950: 40 million cars, gasoline sales 42% higher, oil meeting more energy needs than coal. In 1948, imports exceeded exports for the first time. The U.S. was now dependent on other countries for the marginal barrel.

Aramco needed capital. Socal and Texaco received about $470 million for selling 40% to Jersey Standard and Socony. Jersey and Socony invoked “supervening illegality” to break the Red Line Agreement. Gulbenkian refused to acquiesce. Tapline — 1,040 miles, replacing 7,200 via Suez — finished September 1950. “Imported petroleum killed synthetic fuels. And they would remain dead for three decades.”

Chapter 14: Fifty-Fifty and Mossadegh

The fifty-fifty formula began in Venezuela, where Perez Alfonzo found the actual split was 60–40 in companies’ favor and fixed it. He insisted on royalties in kind: “The veil of mystery over oil marketing was removed forever.” His motive for spreading the formula to the Middle East was defensive — low-cost Middle Eastern production was “a grave threat to Venezuela.”

In the Saudi Neutral Zone, J. Paul Getty won with an audacious $9.5 million bid. Getty was “obsessed with value — bought it and never sold it.” Married five times, he fired all employees during the Depression and rehired at lower salaries. After the Nazi seizure of Austria, he entered the Rothschild home to look at the furniture. Five dry holes followed while Getty wandered Europe researching a Rembrandt, noting “newspaper — 10 centimes.” The sixth hole struck “somewhere between colossal and history-making.” Fortune named him the richest man in the world. “If you can count your money, you don’t have a billion dollars.” He installed a pay phone for guests at his manor. The geologist who found the oil received $1,200.

The foreign tax credit transformed the economics. Saudi taxes became deductible from Aramco’s U.S. tax bill. By 1951: Saudi Arabia received $110 million; the U.S. Treasury got $6 million. Jersey Standard: “‘50/50’ is a good position which needs no defense.” Gulbenkian warned: “I fear the wind of nationalization may spread.”

In Iran, Mohammed Mossadegh — seventy, frail, completely bald — received dignitaries in pajamas from bed. He collapsed during a meeting; a doctor checked his pulse. “Mossadegh opened one eye and winked.” Anglo-Iranian had booked £250 million in profit versus £90 million to Iran between 1945 and 1950. Chairman Fraser — “obstinate, narrow old skinflint” — resisted reform until the Saudi fifty-fifty deal. Too late. PM Razmara was assassinated entering a mosque. The Majlis nationalized. Scores of sheep were sacrificed.

Harriman found Mossadegh on his bed: “You do not know how crafty they are.” His grandson was at school. “Where?” “In England, of course. Where else?” Walters ordered Alice in Wonderland “as a guidebook.” British oil men staged a farewell revue called “Stand Firm, You Cads!” The guest house lady interdicted a tank commander with her umbrella. Passengers sang obscene versions of “Colonel Bogey.”

Operation Ajax — the CIA coup — toppled Mossadegh after a first attempt failed and the Shah fled to Rome. Then “tumblers doing handsprings and wrestlers showing off biceps.” Mossadegh fled over his back wall. Eisenhower thought it read “more like a dime novel.” The consortium that replaced Anglo-Iranian gave it 40%, Shell 14%, and five American majors 8% each. Anglo-Iranian got about $590 million. “A wonderful deal for Fraser — Anglo-Iranian had nothing to sell. It had already been nationalized.”

The consortium that replaced Anglo-Iranian split ownership: 40% to BP (renamed from Anglo-Iranian), 14% to Shell, and 8% each to five American majors. Anglo-Iranian received about $590 million in compensation. “A wonderful deal for Fraser — Anglo-Iranian had nothing to sell. It had already been nationalized.” The Iranian oil industry resumed production, but the terms were fundamentally altered — the age of unilateral Western control was ending.

The Iranian episode established two lessons that would echo through the rest of the century. First: nationalism in oil-producing countries was unstoppable, and companies that refused to share fairly would lose everything. Second: covert intervention could restore the old order temporarily, but the resentment it created would eventually produce something far worse — the Islamic Revolution of 1979 was, in many ways, the delayed consequence of Ajax.

Chapter 15: Suez and the Elephants

On July 26, 1956, Nasser repeatedly invoked “de Lesseps” during a speech — the code word for the army to seize the Suez Canal. Then he went to see Cyd Charisse in Meet Me in Las Vegas. By 1955, petroleum was two-thirds of canal traffic and two-thirds of Europe’s oil passed through it. Eden told Soviet leaders: “I must be absolutely blunt about the oil … we would fight for it.” Macmillan read thousands of pages of novels in two weeks to keep from going “barmy.”

Eden, Macmillan, and French PM Mollet were haunted by the 1930s — Nasser was a resurrected Mussolini. At Sèvres on October 24, Britain, France, and Israel agreed on the secret plan. Eisenhower was absolutely furious at the invasion, refusing emergency oil supplies: “left to boil in their own oil.” Macmillan: “Oil sanctions! That finishes it.” An immense run on the pound started. The IMF refused emergency aid.

The Texas Railroad Commission barely increased production — “England apparently still looks on us as a province.” Belgium banned private driving on Sundays. Britain rationed gasoline — the “Suez sixpence.” Eden resigned in January 1957 — “the last prime minister to believe Britain was a great power and the first to confront a crisis which proved she was not.” Macmillan succeeded him, reading Pride and Prejudice to soothe his soul. Supertankers became a key consequence — vessels too large for the canal, making it strategically less important.

Free world crude production grew from 8.7 million barrels per day in 1948 to 42 million by 1972. The era of giant discoveries — “elephants” — transformed the industry. U.S. share slipped from 64% to 22%. Middle East production grew from 1.1 to 18.2 million barrels per day — a 1,500% increase. Proven reserves grew from 62 billion to 534 billion barrels — almost ninefold.

Enrico Mattei, the Italian “born condottiere” who coined the term “Seven Sisters,” transformed AGIP into ENI — a sprawling conglomerate of thirty-six subsidiaries. He came to Shell; Loudon asked how he got into oil. Mattei talked nonstop through lunch. “When we went to dessert, he asked for something. We couldn’t do it, and that was the end.” He broke fifty-fifty with an effectively 75–25 deal with Iran. He died in a 1962 plane crash at fifty-six — “the most important individual in Italy, more important than the premier or the Pope.”

In Iraq, the monarchy was overthrown in July 1958 in an explosion of violence. King Faisal II was beheaded. PM Nuri es-Said was recognized fleeing disguised as a woman, lynched, his body flattened by a car. Japan’s Arabian Oil Company took only 44% of profits. The Shah told Standard of Indiana: “You know — we are not Arabs. We are Aryans, and we have a great history.”

Perez Alfonzo — “iron determination, yet soft-spoken and almost monastic” — had installed a rusted 1950 Singer car in his garden as “a corroded, overgrown shrine to the dangers of oil wealth.” Tariki, the “Red Sheikh” and first American- educated Saudi technocrat, was excluded from Texas bars because taken for a Mexican. Journalist Wanda Jablonski — who had wrangled an evening in King Saud’s harem — introduced them at the 1959 Cairo congress. They met secretly at a yacht club in Maadi: “a James Bond atmosphere.” “She had just been the matchmaker for OPEC.”

In 1970, Eden, retired and broken, prayed for “a lake of oil” under the North Sea. “That was exactly what they were beginning to find.”

Chapter 16: OPEC and Hydrocarbon Man

In July 1960, Jersey Standard’s board debated the posted price. Chairman Rathbone — “an engineer with a T Square” whose entire career had been domestic — was determined to cut. Howard Page vigorously objected: “You can’t just be guided by market forces in an industry so essential to governments.” Wanda Jablonski briefed the board on anti-Western hostility. Rathbone dismissed Page as “a know-it-all.” On August 9, Jersey cut prices up to 14 cents. Loudon of Shell called it “the fatal move.”

Within hours, Tariki telegraphed Perez Alfonzo. On September 14, 1960, OPEC was founded in Baghdad by Saudi Arabia, Venezuela, Kuwait, Iraq, and Iran. Companies dismissed it entirely. “We attached little importance to it,” said Page. A CIA report gave four lines. Perez Alfonzo held the telegram aloft: “We’ve done it!” Tariki was fired in 1962. Perez Alfonzo resigned in 1963, calling petroleum “the excrement of the devil.”

Libya’s 1955 petroleum law gave many small concessions to ensure the country was not “in the hands of one oil company.” Jersey struck Zelten in 1959: “Libya has hit the jack-pot.” Libyan crude was sweet (low-sulfur) and perfectly located across the Mediterranean. By 1969, output exceeded Saudi Arabia’s. Eisenhower imposed import quotas in 1959 — the “Brownsville U-Turn”: oil trucked to Mexico around a traffic circle, back to Texas as “overland imports.” Companies in the Middle East grew from 9 to 81 between 1948 and 1972. Returns fell from over 20% to 11–13%.

Oil had created Hydrocarbon Man. Total world energy consumption tripled between 1949 and 1972; oil demand increased five and a half times. U.S. consumption tripled. Japan’s increased 137 times. Motor vehicles outside America grew from 18.9 million to 161 million. The petrochemical industry exploded — The Graduate (1967): “Plastics.” American automobiles grew longer and wider, getting all of eight miles per gallon.

Coal could not withstand the tidal wave. John L. Lewis’s combative strikes were “a powerful invitation to find a substitute.” London’s “Killer Fogs” from coal prompted smokeless zones. In Western Europe, coal fell from 75% of energy in 1955 to 22% by 1972; oil rose from 23% to 60%. Japan went from 7% to 70% oil. In 1955, Japan produced 69,000 cars; by 1968, 4.1 million. Alfred Chandler’s recipe for the German and Japanese economic miracles: “Improved institutional arrangements and cheap oil.”

Gasoline stations sprouted on every corner. Credit cards tied customers to particular companies. Texaco sponsored Milton Berle: “Trust Your Car to the Man Who Wears the Star.” Shell claimed its TCP additive was such a wonder fuel that the Air Force had classified it a military secret. Socony countered that TCP was actually harmful. An FTC investigation was launched. The “additives war” reflected the intensity of competition in an industry drowning in surplus.

Eisenhower imposed import quotas in 1959, creating absurdities like the “Brownsville U-Turn” — oil trucked to Mexico, driven around a traffic circle, and brought back to Texas as “overland imports.” Companies in the Middle East grew from 9 to 81 between 1948 and 1972, and returns fell from over 20% to 11–13% as competition intensified.

The Iran-Saudi rivalry dominated OPEC’s first decade. Howard Page refused Oman: “I am absolutely sure we don’t want oil because we are liable to lose the Aramco concession.” Between 1957 and 1970, Iranian production grew 387% versus Saudi 258%. The Gulf became the “swing area” or “surge pot” — where incremental demand was met. Nobody noticed that the surge pot was running dry.

Chapter 17: The Hinge Years

In October 1971, the Shah hosted a $200 million celebration at Persepolis. Maxim’s flew in 165 chefs and 25,000 bottles of wine. Pompidou: “If I did go, they would probably make me the head-waiter.” The Shah: “We can hardly offer them bread and radishes, can we?” He rode Princess Anne’s stallion named Cossack — the name was kept from him.

Britain withdrew from the Persian Gulf by 1971 — “the end of a century-old security system.” Dubai: “Who asked them to leave?” Bahrain: “Britain could do with another Winston Churchill.” The British position had required only 6,000 troops at 12 million pounds per year. Some sheikhs offered to pay. U.S. oil production peaked at 11.3 million barrels per day in 1970. The Texas Railroad Commission opened to full production for the first time: “That old warrior can’t rise anymore.”

Environmental concerns emerged. The Santa Barbara oil spill of 1969 galvanized public opinion. Limits to Growth warned of resource depletion. The Alaskan elephant — Prudhoe Bay at 10 billion barrels, the largest field in North America — was discovered but blocked by environmentalists for years.

In Libya, Qaddafi’s September 1969 coup produced a new kind of adversary: “People who have lived without oil for 5,000 years can live without it again.” His deputy Jalloud once threw a proposal back as a paper ball, once entered negotiations with a submachine gun. He set his .45 in front of Armand Hammer, who “had never before negotiated across a gun barrel.” Occidental was cut from 800,000 to 500,000 barrels per day. Hammer commuted daily Tripoli-Paris, phoning his board from the Ritz.

Libya won a 30-cent price increase and 55% profit share. The Shah then broke fifty-fifty. Companies formed a “Front Uni” with an antitrust waiver. The Tehran Agreement of February 1971 was “the real turning point for OPEC.” Barran of Shell: “The buyer’s market for oil is over.” Libya followed with 90 cents more.

Participation — producing countries acquiring partial ownership — was consciously devised as an alternative to outright nationalization. Yamani called it “a Catholic marriage.” Qaddafi nationalized 51% of all Libyan companies, standing with Idi Amin to announce “a big hard blow on America’s cold insolent face.” The Shah: “Seventy-two years of foreign control was ended.”

By 1973, surplus “available” production capacity was just 500,000 barrels per day — 1% of consumption. A 99% utilization rate was extraordinarily precarious. James Placke warned from Tripoli: “Control of the flow of resources has been of strategic concern throughout history.” No one in Washington paid attention. Net imports rose from 2.2 million in 1967 to 6 million barrels per day in 1973. The stage was set.

The hinge years were the period when every structural support of the postwar petroleum order was removed simultaneously: British military withdrawal, American production peak, the end of surplus capacity, environmental constraints on new development, and the rise of resource nationalism. Any one of these would have been manageable. Together, they made catastrophe inevitable.

Chapter 18: The Oil Weapon

Sadat concluded war was the only way to break the stalemate. Kissinger later admitted: “What literally no one understood was the mind of the man.” Sadat’s purpose was “psychological and diplomatic, much more than military” — a Clausewitzian calculation that limited war would force negotiations. He shared his plan with King Faisal, meaning oil would be central.

Faisal had long rejected the oil weapon — the 1967 embargo availed nothing except lost revenues. “Politics and oil should not be mixed.” But by 1973 he changed. Saudi Arabia was producing 8.4 million barrels per day, 62% above 1972. He warned Aramco directly: “You will lose everything.” Washington responded with “a large degree of disbelief.” James Akins published “The Oil Crisis: This Time the Wolf Is Here.” Ehrlichman: “Conservation is not the Republican ethic.”

On October 6, 1973 — Yom Kippur — 222 Egyptian jets and 3,000 field guns struck simultaneously with Syrian forces. The CIA on October 5 had reported: “Military preparations do not indicate that any party intends to initiate hostilities.” Israel’s intelligence “conception” precluded an Egyptian attack. Dayan: “The Third Temple is going under.” Meir prepared a secret letter to Nixon.

On October 16, Gulf states raised the posted price 70% to $5.11 — unilaterally. Yamani: “This is a moment for which I have been waiting a long time. We are masters of our own commodity.” The pretense of negotiating was over. The next day, Arab ministers agreed to cut production 5% monthly, with a total ban on the United States. On October 20, Saudi Arabia cut off all shipments. The same day: the Saturday Night Massacre. Brezhnev threatened unilateral Soviet intervention; U.S. forces went to DefCon 3 — worldwide nuclear alert.

“We are masters of our own commodity.” —Sheikh Yamani, October 1973

The net loss was 4.4 million barrels per day — 14% of traded oil. Panic buying drove auction prices above $17. A Japanese trading company bid $22.60: “We weren’t bidding just for oil. We were bidding for our life.” In Japan, toilet paper hoarding. In America, stations with “Sorry, No Gas Today” signs. The allocation system perversely prevented shifting gasoline to where it was needed.

BP’s Drake refused PM Heath’s demand for special treatment: “Are you asking me as a shareholder or as a government? If government, put it in writing.” “You know perfectly well I can’t.” “Then I won’t do it.” Having lived through one nationalization in Iran, Drake was not about to be party to any more.

In December 1973, the Shah pushed through $11.65 per barrel — a fourfold increase in three months. Saudi Arabia wanted $8, afraid the skyrocketing price could cause depression. The Shah lectured: “The era of their terrific progress based on cheap oil is finished.” Nixon announced “Project Independence” — his staff said impossible. Kissinger’s shuttle diplomacy eventually sheathed the weapon: the embargo ended March 18, 1974. Kissinger walked through a tremendous hall where men in black robes sat against the walls, reflecting “what strange twists of fate had caused a refugee from Nazi persecution to wind up in Arabia.”

“The three-decade-old postwar petroleum order had died its final death.” Japan endorsed the Arab position — its first foreign policy split with the United States. The IEA was created. France’s Jobert: “Bonjour les traitres.”

Chapter 19: OPEC’s Imperium

Petroleum earnings rose from $23 billion in 1972 to $140 billion by 1977 — the greatest transfer of wealth in history. U.S. GNP plunged 6%; unemployment doubled to 9%. Japan’s GNP declined for the first time since the war. The “OPEC tax” was a massive withdrawal of purchasing power. Non-oil developing countries were hit hardest — a new category, the “fourth world,” was invented.

Yamani — born in Mecca 1930, grandson of a grand mufti, NYU and Harvard Law — became oil minister at thirty-two. “Give me Tariki’s rantings. Yamani drives you to the wall with sweet reasonableness.” His voice was so soft that adversaries had to strain to hear. The angrier he got, the quieter he became. He never gambled: “I hate it. It rots the soul.” Kissinger: “watchful eyes and little Van Dyke beard made him look like a priggish young don.”

The Shah saw December 1973 as his moment — revenues to fulfill “Iran’s Great Civilization.” “Everything you can dream of can be achieved here.” The Empress: “One of the only things my husband likes in life is speed!” He hurtled Iran toward the twenty-first century, ignoring the disorientation and resentment building beneath the surface. Yamani warned: “If the Shah departs, we could have a violent, anti-American regime in Teheran.”

King Faisal was assassinated in March 1975 by his nephew. In December 1975, “Carlos” attacked OPEC headquarters in Vienna. Yamani spent 44 hours with worry beads, convinced he would die. Kuwait told Gulf Oil “thank you very much and good-bye.” Gulf asked $2 billion; it got $50 million. Goodman of Gulf: “It was the ebbing of American power — the Romans retreating from Hadrian’s Wall.”

Venezuela nationalized on January 1, 1976, establishing PDVSA. Saudi Arabia took 100% of Aramco — agreed in 1976, not formally signed until 1990. Forty-three years after Standard of California’s $175,000 payment, proven reserves were 149 billion barrels. “It was truly the end of the era.”

Japan responded with shoene rukku — the energy conservation revolution. MITI curtailed elevator service. France built nuclear plants and imposed temperature inspections. The U.S. became 25% more energy efficient by 1985; Japan 31%. Ford achieved two key milestones: the Alaska pipeline ($10 billion) and fuel efficiency standards (13 to 27.5 mpg). Carter called energy “the moral equivalent of war.” Detractors: “Meow.”

Phillips struck Ekofisk in the North Sea after being told “Don’t drill any more wells.” The drilling superintendent: “What the astronauts have done is great, but how about this?” Mexico surged from 500,000 to 1.9 million barrels per day. Alaska’s Prudhoe Bay — 10 billion barrels, largest in North America — came online. Gulf had refused the acreage: “Oil will never get to $5 a barrel in our lifetime.” Non-OPEC supply was rising everywhere.

On New Year’s Eve 1977, Carter toasted the Shah: “Iran is an island of stability … a tribute to the respect and love which your people give you.” It was the most catastrophically wrong assessment in modern diplomatic history.

Chapter 20: The Second Shock

A January 7, 1978 newspaper article savaging Khomeini triggered revolution. The “40-40” cycle — demonstrations, deaths, forty-day mourning, more demonstrations — spiraled out of control. Oil workers struck. In The Fields, workers occupied Osco’s headquarters, sleeping in corridors while Westerners stepped over them. Production fell from 5.5 million barrels per day to zero by Christmas.

Paul Grimm of Osco was shot dead driving to work. Oil men evacuated, strictly limited to two suitcases. They shot their dogs, as their predecessors had done leaving Abadan in 1951. The Shah: “We are melting away daily like snow in water.” A telephone prank: told Senator Kennedy was calling, the Shah picked up to hear “Mohammed, abdicate.” He left January 16 carrying a casket of Iranian soil. Khomeini returned on an Air France 747 — seats sold to reporters to finance the flight. The defense attaché: “Army surrenders; Khomeini wins. Destroying all classified.”

The actual shortage was only 4–5% of world demand. But panic drove prices from $13 to $34. Five factors: apparent demand growth, disruption of contractual arrangements, contradictory government policies, exporter opportunism, and sheer emotion. BP lost 40% of its supply and invoked force majeure. Exxon announced phasing out third-party contracts.

Motorists keeping tanks three-quarters full instead of one-quarter sucked a billion gallons from gas stations, adding three million barrels per day of phantom demand — more than doubling the actual shortage. Shell received a telex invoking force majeure to cut supply; that afternoon, the same volume was offered at 50% higher on the spot market. Japan tried everything — one trading house gave gloves to secretaries; another provided a world-class acupuncturist for the Iraqi oil minister.

Three Mile Island raised doubts about nuclear power. Gas lines returned in America — 150,000 barrels wasted daily by cars idling in queues. Carter went to Tokyo where Schmidt “got personally abusive.” On Thatcher: “a tough lady, highly opinionated.” Approval plummeted to 25%. At the Caracas OPEC meeting, Yamani warned for eleven hours: “There will be a glut. It is coming.” The Iranian delegate: “In the name of almighty God, there will be no surplus and prices will not fall.”

On November 4, 1979, militants seized the U.S. embassy. Elizabeth Ann Swift: “We’re going down.” Fifty Americans held for 444 days. In late November, fundamentalists seized the Great Mosque in Mecca. In December, Soviets invaded Afghanistan. Carter issued the Carter Doctrine. The Desert One rescue attempt failed when a helicopter collided with a C-130.

The Iraq-Iran war erupted in September 1980. Saddam Hussein — shaped by Tikrit’s values of “suspicion, stealth, surprise, and the unalloyed use of force” — billed it as the “Second Battle of Qadisiyah.” Iran’s “human wave” assaults sent hundreds of thousands of young people with plastic keys to heaven around their necks. Children cleared minefields for the tanks. The war removed four million daily barrels — but inventories were brimming. OPEC output in 1981 was 27% below 1979 — the lowest since 1970. Prices unified at $34. It was the last OPEC increase for a decade.

Yamani had been right. The glut was coming. The Iranian delegate who invoked God to deny the surplus would be proven spectacularly wrong. The forces that high prices unleashed — conservation, new production, substitution, recession — were already gathering strength. They would crash the oil market more violently than anyone imagined.

Chapter 21: The Good Sweating

The greatest boom of them all swept the oil industry. Young geology graduates were wined, dined, and hired at $50,000 a year. Dallas introduced J.R. Ewing. Doctors and dentists poured money into drilling funds. Exxon spent a billion on Colony Shale Oil in Colorado. But by 1982 the outlook changed — Colony was terminated. Colorado boomtowns became ghost towns, following the tradition of Pithole, Pennsylvania. “My business just died,” said a shop owner.

The Federal Reserve’s prime rate reached 21.5%. The deepest recession since the Depression cut oil demand by six million barrels per day between 1979 and 1983. Non-OPEC production increased by four million. Conservation was massive: if the U.S. had stayed at 1973 efficiency, it would have used 13 million more barrels daily in 1985. Oil’s energy share dropped from 53% to 43%.

In March 1982, OPEC set an output limit of 18 million barrels per day with individual quotas — for the first time, a true cartel. Saudi Arabia would adjust production to support the system. In March 1983, OPEC cut prices from $34 to $29 — its first price cut in history. Nymex introduced crude oil futures the same month. Founded in 1872 as the Butter and Cheese Exchange, its previous mainstay was Maine potatoes. “Maine potatoes became a distant, quaint, and embarrassing memory.”

T. Boone Pickens — an Amarillo independent, former Phillips trainee who quit because “you’ve got to learn to keep your mouth shut” — exploited the “value gap.” “It was cheaper to explore for oil on the floor of the New York Stock Exchange.” Mukluk in Alaska was the most expensive dry hole ever ($2 billion) — it struck salt water: “simply 30 million years too late.” Pickens targeted Gulf Oil. Chevron won it for $13.2 billion. Jimmy Lee: “I never had any intention but that Gulf would be here for ever and ever.”

Saudi earnings fell from $119 billion in 1981 to $26 billion by 1985. Production collapsed to 2.2 million barrels per day — at times below British North Sea output. Exports to the U.S.: 26,000 barrels per day — “essentially nothing.” In June 1985, King Fahd warned OPEC: if cheating continued, “Saudi Arabia would certainly secure its own interests.”

The Saudis moved from defense of price to defense of volume, using netback deals that guaranteed refiners a predetermined profit regardless of selling price. “Increasing volume and reduced concern about selling price — a perfect recipe for falling prices.” Oil collapsed from $31.75 to $10 a barrel. Some Gulf cargoes went for $6. Shell’s planners had prepared an “Oil Collapse Scenario” — when it struck, “there was an eerie calm at Shell Centre.” Billy Jack Mason’s Exxon station in Austin ran a promotion at zero cents per gallon — a six-mile line.

Vice President Bush traveled to Saudi Arabia: “I think it is essential that we talk about stability and not just have a continued free fall like a parachutist without a parachute.” The Reagan White House disavowed him: “The way to address price stability is to let the free market work.” Yamani spoke at Harvard’s 350th anniversary. Asked how Saudi oil policy was made, he said: “We play it by ear.” The remark was interpreted in Riyadh as criticism. A month later, he learned from a television broadcast that he had been fired. Twenty-four years — “a good, long run in any position anywhere.”

Mexico ran up $84 billion in debt based on oil wealth. Silva Herzog told Treasury Secretary Regan: “No, Mr. Secretary, we have a problem.” Penn Square Bank in Oklahoma — whose lending officer drank Amaretto from Gucci loafers — generated $2 billion in bad energy loans sold to Continental Illinois, which suffered the largest bank run in history. Energy lending “instantaneously went out of fashion.”

In December 1986, OPEC agreed to a reference price of $18 per barrel. The OPEC tax cut transferred $50 billion to consumers in 1986 alone. After eight years, Iran accepted a cease-fire in the Iraq war. Khomeini: “Taking this decision was more deadly than taking poison.” Iraq thought it had won — “the dominant political power in the Gulf.”

Chapter 22: Epilogue — Kuwait

State oil companies went downstream — Venezuela built U.S. refineries; Kuwait bought gas stations (“Q-8”) and 22% of BP; Saudi Arabia acquired half of Texaco’s American stations. Thatcher sold the government’s BP stake — reversing Churchill’s 1914 decision. The reintegration of the industry was proceeding, but now with producing countries as the owners.

At the 1985 Bonn summit, not a single word about oil appeared in the communiqué. The crisis seemed over. At a 1989 discussion, an oil minister was asked how long the lessons of the 1970s and 1980s would be remembered. “About three years, without reminding,” he said. Within a year, he was no longer minister. A month later, his country was invaded.

On August 2, 1990, Saddam Hussein invaded Kuwait. His goal was not only conquest of a sovereign state but the capture of its riches. If successful, Iraq would dominate the Arab world and the Persian Gulf, where the bulk of the planet’s oil reserves were concentrated. The stakes were so enormous that the invasion was not accepted as a fait accompli. The United Nations imposed an embargo, and many nations mustered military force to defend Saudi Arabia.

Churchill had captured the fundamental truth on the eve of World War I: oil had meant mastery throughout the twentieth century. And now, at the century’s end, mastery itself was once more the prize. The themes Yergin traced from Drake’s well to Rockefeller’s monopoly to Churchill’s conversion to the OPEC revolution had come full circle. Oil was still central to security, prosperity, and the very nature of civilization.

The minister’s prophecy proved devastatingly accurate. Three years was all the memory lasted. The lesson of this book is that it never lasts longer. Complacency is the permanent condition; crisis is the periodic reminder. And the reminder always comes from a direction nobody was watching.

“About three years, without reminding.” —unnamed oil minister, 1989

The story of oil, Yergin concludes, is a story of individual people, of powerful economic forces, of technological change, of political struggles, of international conflict, and of epic change. It is the story of the twentieth century itself — and it is far from over.

Trading & Investing Lessons

Commodity Cycles Are Driven by Psychology

Every oil boom in this book — Pithole, East Texas, Libya, the 1970s — follows the same arc: scarcity drives prices up, high prices attract capital and innovation, oversupply crashes prices. The lag between investment and production creates violent swings. Traders who understand the cycle’s rhythm profit; those caught at either extreme are destroyed.

Geopolitical Risk Is Permanent and Underpriced

Markets consistently discount geopolitical threats until they materialize. Washington greeted Faisal’s warning with “a large degree of disbelief.” Carter toasted the Shah as an “island of stability.” Energy positions should always price in the possibility of sudden disruption.

Spare Capacity Is the Master Variable

The most dangerous moment in oil markets is not a specific event but the erosion of spare capacity. In 1973, surplus was 1% of consumption — any disruption became catastrophic. Monitor spare capacity as the single most important leading indicator for energy markets.

The Swing Producer Always Suffers

Saudi Arabia nearly bankrupted itself defending OPEC prices in the 1980s while others cheated on quotas. The entity that adjusts bears disproportionate cost. This applies to any cartel or dominant market maker.

Control of Resources Is Temporary

Every concession in this book was eventually nationalized. Standard Oil’s monopoly was broken. The Seven Sisters lost their empire. Companies that assume permanent control of strategic resources are building on sand.

Panic Creates More Shortage Than the Event

The second oil shock proved that panic buying can double the effective shortage. The actual Iranian disruption was 4–5%; panic made it feel like 10%. Behavioral cascades are the real price driver in crisis. Always fade the panic, never join it.

Key Takeaways

Oil Is Never Just a Commodity

It is simultaneously a commercial product, a strategic asset, and a political weapon. Every attempt to treat it as purely one of these fails.

Technology Creates and Destroys Oil Empires

Drilling replaced skimming. Tankers killed geography. Synthetic fuels sustained Nazi Germany. Conservation broke OPEC’s grip. Futures transformed pricing. Each innovation reshuffled power.

The Oil Curse Is Real

Venezuela, Iran, Mexico, Nigeria, Iraq — oil wealth corroded institutions, fueled authoritarianism, and ultimately impoverished the countries that depended on it. Perez Alfonzo’s “excrement of the devil” proved prophetic.

Surplus Masks Fragility

Periods of abundant supply breed complacency. The Bonn summit that ignored oil in 1985 was followed by Kuwait’s invasion five years later. Markets and governments consistently forget how precarious energy security is.

Integration Is the Winning Strategy

From Rockefeller through Aramco through state company downstream expansion, the most durable competitive advantage in oil is controlling the chain from wellhead to gas pump.

Contrarian Ideas

The 1911 Dissolution Helped Standard Oil

The thirty-four successor companies, freed to compete, collectively became far more valuable than the unified trust. Rockefeller’s wealth increased after the breakup.

Conservation, Not Alternatives, Broke OPEC

The response to the 1970s shocks was not solar or nuclear but efficiency. Demand fell more than supply rose. Conservation was the “hidden fuel.”

OPEC Was Irrelevant for Its First Decade

From 1960 to 1970, OPEC accomplished nothing. The real shift was the disappearance of surplus capacity and the withdrawal of British military power — structural changes unrelated to OPEC meetings.

Pearl Harbor Failed on Its Own Terms

Japan fought the entire war for oil yet forgot to destroy Pearl Harbor’s oil tanks. Nimitz said this would have prolonged the war two years. The war of oil was lost at the moment of oil.

High Prices Are Self-Correcting

Every price spike triggered the forces that destroyed it — conservation, new production, substitution. The oil weapon turned on those who wielded it.

Proposals & Frameworks

Three Themes of Oil History

Yergin identifies three strands: (1) oil as the foundation of capitalism and modern business, (2) oil as a strategic commodity intertwined with geopolitics and war, (3) oil as the substance that created Hydrocarbon Society.

The Commodity-Strategic Duality

Oil oscillates between periods when it behaves like an ordinary commodity and periods when it becomes a strategic weapon. The transition is always sudden and always underestimated.

The Concession Lifecycle

Every concession follows the same arc: discovery, development, rising nationalism, renegotiation, nationalization. D’Arcy’s 1901 Persian concession was the first granted and the first canceled.

The Price Spiral

Price increases trigger conservation and new supply, which trigger collapse, which triggers complacency, which triggers the next crisis. The cycle length is roughly a decade.

What Didn’t Fit

The Pine Root Campaign

In the final months of the Pacific War, Japan launched a desperate campaign to extract aviation fuel from pine tree roots. Two hundred roots would keep a plane airborne for an hour, they claimed. Each gallon required 2.5 man-days of labor. The total output was 3,000 barrels — and none was ever tested in an actual aircraft. When Americans tried the fuel in captured jeeps, it gummed up engines beyond use. The campaign epitomized the gap between desperation and physics. Ideology cannot substitute for hydrocarbons, no matter how fervently a government believes otherwise.

Penn Square and the Gucci Loafers

The 1980s oil bust destroyed more than producers. Penn Square Bank in Oklahoma, whose lending officer drank Amaretto from Gucci loafers at closing parties, generated $2 billion in bad energy loans sold to Continental Illinois — which suffered the largest bank run in American history. Energy lending “instantaneously went out of fashion.” The episode proved that oil booms create not just industrial excess but financial contagion, and that the most dangerous moment is when bankers start believing the commodity price can only go up.

Getty’s Pay Phone

J. Paul Getty, identified by Fortune as the richest man in the world, installed a pay telephone for guests at his English manor. Married five times, he fired all employees during the Depression and rehired at lower salaries. After the Nazi seizure of Austria, he entered the Rothschild home to examine the furniture. His geologist Paul Walton spotted the Saudi Neutral Zone discovery from a DC-3, recognizing a mound resembling Kuwait’s Burgan field. Five dry holes later, the sixth struck “somewhere between colossal and history-making.” Walton received $1,200. Getty: “If you can count your money, you don’t have a billion dollars.”

The Suez Sixpence and Literary Consolation

During the Suez crisis, Macmillan read thousands of pages of novels in two weeks to keep from going “barmy.” After succeeding Eden as Prime Minister, he soothed his soul with Pride and Prejudice. Belgium banned private driving on Sundays. Britain rationed gasoline — the ration was called the “Suez sixpence.” Eden, retired and broken, prayed for “a lake of oil” under the North Sea. That was exactly what Phillips Petroleum was beginning to find with the Ekofisk discovery — vindicating Eden’s prayer a decade too late to save his career.

Carlos at OPEC Vienna

In December 1975, the Venezuelan terrorist “Carlos” led an armed assault on OPEC headquarters in Vienna during a ministers’ meeting. Yamani spent 44 hours with his worry beads, convinced he would die. The attackers demanded a plane, flew to Algeria, and eventually surrendered. The episode demonstrated that oil’s new wealth had made OPEC ministers targets of revolutionary violence — the prize was now so vast that even terrorists came calling. Yamani never fully recovered from the experience; it reinforced his conviction that oil wealth was as dangerous as it was valuable.

The New Map (2020) examines how energy revolutions are redrawing geopolitics. He has received the United States Energy Award for “lifelong achievements in energy.”

Newsweek described him as “one of those rare historians who can bring the past to life on the page” — combining a novelist’s instinct for character and drama with an expert’s command of the economic and strategic forces that shaped the twentieth century.

Why This Book Matters

The Prize is the definitive history of the commodity that shaped the modern world. No other book weaves together the personalities, politics, technology, and economics of oil with such narrative power. It explains why Churchill converted the Royal Navy, why Japan attacked Pearl Harbor, why the Middle East became the center of global conflict, and why desert kingdoms could bring industrial civilization to its knees. These are not separate stories — they are one story, and Yergin tells it as no one else has.

The book’s central insight — that oil is never just a commodity but always simultaneously a strategic weapon, a political instrument, and the lifeblood of civilization — has only become more relevant since its publication. Every energy crisis since 1991 has validated Yergin’s framework: the assumption that oil has been tamed is always the prelude to the next disruption.

The minister’s warning — “about three years, without reminding” — is the book’s most haunting line. Markets and nations have short memories. The lessons of crisis fade faster than the crises themselves. And mastery itself is always the prize of the venture.
