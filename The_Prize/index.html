Glossary

Industry Terms

Posted price: The publicly announced price at which oil companies offered to buy crude — the basis for calculating royalties and taxes. An administered price, not a market one.

Rule of capture: Legal doctrine from English game law — whoever pumps oil from a common underground reservoir owns it. Drove chaotic overproduction until prorationing.

Prorationing: Government-imposed production limits to prevent waste and stabilize prices. The Texas Railroad Commission became the model OPEC later copied.

Concession: Government grant giving a company exclusive exploration/production rights for a set period. Every major concession in this book was eventually nationalized.

Fifty-fifty: Profit-sharing formula replacing flat royalties after 1950. Began in Venezuela, spread to the Middle East via the foreign tax credit.

Netback deal: Pricing mechanism guaranteeing refiners a predetermined profit margin. Used by Saudi Arabia in 1985 to recapture market share; crashed oil to $10.

Swing producer: Country that adjusts output to balance world supply and demand. Saudi Arabia nearly bankrupted itself playing this role.

Organizations

Standard Oil Trust: Rockefeller’s monopoly, 90% of American refining by 1879. Dissolved 1911 into thirty-four successor companies.

Royal Dutch/Shell: The 60/40 merger (1907), engineered by Henri Deterding to challenge Standard Oil.

Seven Sisters: The seven Western oil majors dominating the postwar industry. Term coined by Enrico Mattei as an insult.

OPEC: Founded September 14, 1960 in Baghdad. Dismissed by the CIA for its first decade.

IEA: Western consumer nations’ counterweight to OPEC, created 1974. France called it “a machine de guerre.”

Aramco: Arabian-American Oil Company. Nationalized in stages, completed 1990.

Key Concepts

Red Line Agreement (1928): Partners bound not to operate independently within the old Ottoman Empire boundaries, drawn by Gulbenkian on a map.

As-Is Agreement (1928): Secret pact at Achnacarry Castle to freeze market shares. Companies called each other “friends.”

Hot oil: Production exceeding quotas, smuggled through secret pipelines at night.

Oil weapon: Embargoes and production cuts as political leverage, most dramatically in October 1973.

Prologue: The Mastery Itself

Until the summer of 1911, Winston Churchill was an “economist” in the old British political sense—a man who resisted military spending on principle and questioned the bloated naval budgets that the Admiralty demanded year after year. He had built his early reputation on fiscal restraint, aligning himself with the Liberal tradition of small government and balanced books. Then Kaiser Wilhelm sent the gunboat Panther to the Moroccan port of Agadir, and the world tilted on its axis. The Agadir crisis of 1911 revealed that Germany’s ambitions extended far beyond European borders, and that the next great war would not be fought with diplomacy alone. Churchill underwent a transformation so complete that his colleagues barely recognized the man who emerged from those anxious summer months.

Appointed First Lord of the Admiralty in October 1911, Churchill confronted the most consequential technological decision in modern naval history: whether to convert the Royal Navy from Welsh coal to Persian oil. The strategic calculus was compelling but terrifying. Oil-fired ships were faster by several knots, required fewer stokers in their boiler rooms, could refuel at sea rather than in port, and generated far greater horsepower per ton of fuel. A fleet running on oil could outmaneuver any coal-burning adversary, arriving at the decisive point before the enemy could react. But coal was British—mined from Welsh valleys by British hands on British soil. Oil came from Persia, a distant and unstable land where Britain had no sovereign control. To stake the Empire’s survival on a fuel source six thousand miles away was, as Churchill himself acknowledged, “to take arms against a sea of troubles.”

Churchill chose oil. He did so with the conviction that speed and efficiency would prove decisive in the coming conflict, and that the risks of dependence were outweighed by the catastrophe of falling behind. The new Queen Elizabeth-class battleships—the most powerful warships ever built at that time—were designed from keel to smokestack as oil-burning vessels. There would be no going back. The decision committed not just the Royal Navy but the entire British Empire to a fuel that it did not possess, could not produce on home soil, and would need to secure by diplomacy, commerce, or force for generations to come. In Churchill’s own memorable phrase, “Mastery itself was the prize of the venture,” a line that gave Daniel Yergin the title for his monumental history.

Nearly eight decades later, on August 2, 1990, Saddam Hussein’s tanks rolled across the Kuwaiti border and shattered whatever illusion remained that oil had lost its strategic significance. The Cold War was ending, the Berlin Wall had fallen, and optimists spoke of a peace dividend and the triumph of liberal democracy. Then came the invasion, and suddenly the world was reminded that the commodity which powered its cars, heated its homes, fertilized its crops, and manufactured its plastics was concentrated beneath some of the most volatile real estate on earth. The crisis demonstrated that oil was not just an economic input but a geopolitical weapon, capable of reshaping alliances, toppling governments, and provoking wars within weeks of a single dictator’s ambition.

Yergin frames his epic around three interlocking themes that have defined the modern world since the 1850s. The first is the rise of capitalism and modern business—the story of how Standard Oil became one of the world’s first true multinational corporations, how seven of the top twenty companies on the Fortune 500 list were oil companies, and how the industry pioneered the techniques of vertical integration, global logistics, and corporate intelligence that every modern conglomerate would later adopt. Oil did not merely fuel the industrial revolution; it created the organizational templates through which capitalism scaled from regional enterprise to planetary dominance.

The second theme is oil’s entanglement with global politics and national strategy. World War I was the first conflict decided by the internal combustion engine—by trucks, tanks, and aircraft that ran on petroleum. Japan attacked Pearl Harbor primarily to secure the oil fields of the Dutch East Indies. The Arab oil embargo of 1973 humbled the most powerful economies on earth. The Shah of Iran was ultimately destroyed by the very oil wealth he had pursued so obsessively, as petrodollars distorted his society beyond repair. From Baku to Baghdad, from Venezuela to Vietnam, oil has been the hidden variable in nearly every major geopolitical equation of the twentieth century.

The third theme is what Yergin calls “Hydrocarbon Man”—the civilization that petroleum built. Kerosene pushed back the night, extending productive hours for millions who had lived by candlelight and whale oil. Gasoline, initially considered a worthless byproduct dumped into rivers, became the lifeblood of the automobile age. Oil-derived fertilizers feed billions; oil-based plastics shape every object we touch; oil-powered transport connects every corner of the globe. The environmental movement now challenges this dependence, questioning whether Hydrocarbon Man can survive his own success. Yet as Yergin observes, Hydrocarbon Man shows remarkably little inclination to surrender the mobility, comfort, and material abundance that oil provides.

What makes oil unique among commodities is the sheer breadth of its influence. Coal powered the Industrial Revolution, but coal is heavy, dirty, and difficult to transport—it tethers industry to railheads and ports. Oil is liquid, energy-dense, and infinitely versatile: it can be refined into gasoline for cars, diesel for trucks, kerosene for jets, naphtha for petrochemicals, asphalt for roads, and lubricants for every moving part in the global economy. A single barrel of crude oil contains the energy equivalent of roughly 25,000 hours of human labor. No other substance combines this energy density with this range of applications. This is why control of oil has mattered more than control of any other resource in modern history, and why the story of oil is inseparable from the story of power itself.

The Prize spans nearly a century and a half, from the first crude wells of western Pennsylvania to the burning oil fields of Kuwait. It is a story populated by wildcatters and robber barons, admirals and dictators, geologists and diplomats, each driven by the same fundamental recognition: that control of oil means control of power. The book earned the Pulitzer Prize for its scope and narrative ambition, weaving together business history, military strategy, diplomatic maneuvering, and technological innovation into a single coherent epic. What follows is that story—from the moment a retired railroad conductor lowered a drill into the Pennsylvania earth, to the moment the world’s most advanced military machine assembled in the Arabian desert to protect the resource that made everything else possible.

Chapter 1: Oil on the Brain

The modern oil industry began not with a gusher but with an unpaid bill. In 1854, a group of investors led by George Bissell and James Townsend retained Professor Benjamin Silliman Jr. of Yale University to analyze samples of “rock oil” collected from seeps in western Pennsylvania. Silliman was one of the most respected chemists in America, and his endorsement would make or break the venture. But when the investors fell behind on his $526.08 fee, Silliman refused to release his findings. He hid the completed report with a friend, vanished on a tour of the American South, and left his clients in agonized suspense. Not until April 16, 1855, did the report finally surface, and its conclusions were transformative: rock oil could be distilled into a high-quality illuminating oil superior to most alternatives on the market.

George Bissell was the visionary behind the enterprise, a man of extraordinary intellect and restless ambition. Self-supporting from the age of twelve, fluent in French, Portuguese, Hebrew, Sanskrit, and several other languages, Bissell had first encountered rock oil as a sample displayed at Dartmouth College and immediately grasped its commercial potential—not as the patent medicine it was sold as, but as an illuminant that could replace the increasingly expensive whale oil and the dangerously explosive camphene that lit American homes. The crucial missing piece fell into place in 1856, when Bissell sheltered from a rainstorm under a druggist’s awning on Broadway in New York. There he spotted an advertisement for salt-drilling operations that depicted derricks boring deep into the earth. If salt could be extracted by drilling, why not oil?

The economics of illumination in the 1850s made oil a prize worth pursuing. Whale oil cost $2.50 per gallon and was growing scarcer as the great whale populations of the Atlantic and Pacific were hunted toward extinction. Camphene, derived from turpentine, was cheaper but had what one contemporary drily noted was “an even more unattractive tendency to explode.” Abraham Gesner, a Canadian geologist, had developed a process for distilling “kerosene” from asphalt—the name derived from the Greek words keros (wax) and elaion (oil)—and by 1859 an estimated thirty-four companies were producing five million dollars a year of various coal-oils. But these were expensive and supply-constrained. Rock oil promised to undercut them all.

“As an illuminator the oil is without a figure in comparison with any other known substance. It is the brightest and yet the cheapest in the world.” —Early advocate of rock oil

Petroleum itself was ancient. Bitumen had caulked the walls of Babylon and probably waterproofed Noah’s ark, if the biblical account reflected any historical memory. The Byzantines had weaponized petroleum as “Greek fire”—a mixture of crude oil and quicklime that burned on water and constituted a state secret “more terrible than gunpowder.” Native Americans had skimmed oil from Pennsylvania’s creek surfaces for medicinal and ceremonial purposes for centuries. What Bissell and Townsend proposed was something entirely new: the deliberate extraction of oil from the earth through industrial drilling, treated not as a curiosity or a folk remedy but as a commodity to be produced, refined, and sold at scale.

The man they chose to execute this vision was Edwin Drake, a selection that owed more to convenience than competence. Drake was a jack-of-all-trades and former railroad conductor who happened to live in the same New Haven hotel as Townsend. His chief qualifications were a railroad pass that allowed free travel to Pennsylvania and nothing else to occupy his time. Townsend, understanding that a man of no particular distinction would inspire no particular confidence, invented the title “Colonel” Drake by addressing letters to him with the honorary rank. When Drake arrived in Titusville—a hamlet of perhaps 125 souls in December 1857—locals regarded him with the bemused condescension that frontier communities reserved for city men with grand ambitions and empty pockets.

Drake endured nearly two years of frustration, failed equipment, and dwindling funds before his driller, “Uncle Billy” Smith, finally arrived in the spring of 1859. Together they improvised their way past every obstacle, adapting salt-boring techniques to the unfamiliar task of oil extraction. On August 27, 1859, at a depth of sixty-nine feet, the drill dropped into a crevice and could go no further. The next day—a Sunday—Uncle Billy peered down the pipe and saw dark fluid rising from the earth. Drake attached a common hand pump and began drawing oil from the well, doing exactly what the scoffers and skeptics had ridiculed as madness. On that same day, he received a letter from Townsend ordering him to shut down operations. Had the letter arrived a week earlier, Drake would have obeyed, and the age of petroleum might have been delayed by years.

The news spread through the Pennsylvania hills with the speed of a brush fire. “The Yankee has struck oil!” farmers shouted, and within weeks the region was convulsed by the first great oil boom. Bissell spent hundreds of thousands of dollars leasing farmland and became a wealthy man. He eventually donated a gymnasium to Dartmouth College, complete with bowling alleys “in remembrance of disciplinary troubles” arising from that “sinful sport” during his student days. Townsend felt cheated of the credit he deserved. And Drake himself—the man who actually struck oil—lost everything through improvident speculation, became a semi-invalid living in poverty, and was reduced to writing plaintive letters to former associates begging for help.

“If you have any of the milk of human kindness left in your bosom for me or my family, send me some money.” —Edwin Drake, letter from poverty

Pennsylvania finally granted Drake a small pension in 1873, a belated acknowledgment of the man who had launched an industry. By that time, the oil regions had already experienced the full cycle of boom and bust that would define the petroleum business for over a century. Production rose from 450,000 barrels in 1860 to three million in 1862. Prices swung from ten dollars a barrel in January 1861 to fifty cents by June and ten cents by year’s end. The town of Pithole exploded from dense forest to a city of 15,000 people with fifty hotels, two banks, and a post office that handled 5,000 letters daily— then collapsed entirely when production gave out. A parcel of land sold for two million dollars was auctioned a decade later for $4.37.

The Civil War played an unexpected role in the young oil industry’s survival. The conflict cut off the South’s turpentine supply, which had been essential for manufacturing camphene—kerosene’s chief competitor as an illuminant. Simultaneously, the collapse of cotton exports deprived the North of a major source of foreign exchange, and kerosene exports to Europe stepped into the gap with remarkable speed. By 1862, petroleum was already becoming a significant American export commodity, lighting homes across the Atlantic while the nation that produced it was tearing itself apart. The war also accelerated the demand for lubricants to keep the Union’s machinery running and created the conditions for rapid capital formation in the northern states that would fuel the postwar industrial expansion. Oil and war were entangled from the beginning.

The legal framework governing this frenzy was the rule of capture, borrowed from English common law regarding migratory game animals. Just as a hunter owned whatever deer crossed his property, a surface landowner could drain oil from a common underground pool regardless of whose land the oil had originally lain beneath. The result was chaos—a competitive race to pump as fast as possible, since any oil left in the ground might be siphoned by a neighbor. Pipelines eventually broke the stranglehold of teamsters who charged extortionate rates to haul barrels over muddy roads, though not without armed resistance, arson, and sabotage from the displaced haulers. Oil exchanges emerged, futures contracts developed, and petroleum became what one observer called “the favorite speculative commodity of the time.” The infrastructure of a modern commodity market was being invented in real time, by men who were making up the rules as they went along.

Chapter 2: “Our Plan” — Rockefeller

On a cold day in February 1865, a twenty-five-year-old Cleveland merchant named John D. Rockefeller stood in an auction room and bid against his own business partner, Maurice Clark, for control of their shared oil refinery. The bidding climbed past expectations—$50,000, $60,000, $70,000—until Clark finally dropped out at $72,500. Rockefeller had wagered everything on this moment, and he never forgot it. “I ever point to that day as the beginning of the success I have made in my life,” he said decades later. The man who walked out of that auction room was already recognizable as the figure who would dominate American industry for half a century: tall, thin, taciturn, with cold blue eyes that made people profoundly uneasy in his presence.

Rockefeller was born in 1839 in rural New York, the son of a traveling salesman who peddled herbal remedies and boasted openly about his talent for deception. “I trade with the boys and skin ’em,” the elder Rockefeller declared, and he applied the same principle to his own children, charging them interest on small loans to teach the value of money. The son absorbed the lesson about financial discipline while rejecting the moral framework. Young John was devout, methodical, and intensely self-controlled. At age seven he launched a turkey-selling venture. Mathematics was his best subject. He developed the habit of holding “intimate conversations” with himself, counseling patience and caution. He stuck relentlessly to building cash reserves, a strategy that insulated him against the busts that destroyed his competitors and enabled him to exploit downturns by buying distressed assets at pennies on the dollar.

The refining business that Rockefeller entered was brutally competitive, with dozens of small operators in Cleveland alone, all squeezing thin margins from volatile crude prices. Rockefeller understood from the beginning that survival required integration—controlling not just the refinery but the timber that built the barrels, the tank cars that moved the oil, the warehouses that stored it, and the boats that carried it down the Hudson to market. His closest partner in this strategy was Henry Flagler, a man who had gone broke in the salt business and learned from that failure the supreme value of “cooperation” among competitors. Their desks sat back to back in the same office, and together they devised the transportation arrangements that would become Standard Oil’s most powerful and most hated weapon.

Flagler negotiated rebates from the railroads—secret discounts on Standard Oil’s shipping costs that were unavailable to competitors. More insidiously, he extracted drawbacks: payments from the railroads on every barrel that Standard Oil’s competitors shipped. In effect, rival refiners were unknowingly subsidizing the very company that was driving them out of business. “Few business practices did as much to rouse public antipathy,” Yergin observes. On January 10, 1870, Standard Oil was formally established, the name chosen to indicate “a standard quality of product.” Rockefeller held a quarter of the stock and already controlled approximately one-tenth of all American refining capacity. He was thirty years old.

The instrument of Rockefeller’s consolidation was the South Improvement Company, a secret alliance with the railroads formed in 1872 that provoked the first great popular uprising against Standard Oil. When the scheme leaked, three thousand men marched to the Titusville Opera House to denounce “the Monster” and “the Forty Thieves.” The South Improvement Company was dissolved under public pressure, but Rockefeller had already used the threat of it to consolidate most of Cleveland’s refining capacity. “Now try our plan,” he told resistant competitors, and those who refused found their credit cut, their railroad rates doubled, and their customers poached. Standard operated through secretly controlled firms, communicated in code—Standard itself was “Morose”—and would threaten to “make him feel sick” or give a rival “a good sweating.”

By 1879, Standard Oil controlled ninety percent of American refining capacity, a dominance unprecedented in any industry. The Standard Oil Trust Agreement of January 2, 1882, formalized this empire under a single legal structure. Rockefeller held 191,700 of 700,000 shares. Management was organized by committee— Domestic Trade, Export Trade, Manufacturing, Pipe Line, Lubricating, and the all-powerful Executive Committee that coordinated everything from headquarters at 26 Broadway in Manhattan, occupied from 1885. Senior executives lunched together daily in a private dining room, sharing intelligence and coordinating strategy with an efficiency that awed even their enemies.

“I never came into contact with any class of men so smart and able as they are in their business.” —William Vanderbilt, on the Standard Oil executives

The trust was a machine of remarkable sophistication, its tentacles extending into transportation, refining, distribution, and even the retail sale of kerosene in remote frontier towns. Standard maintained agents in every significant market, reporting on competitors’ prices, shipments, and customer relationships with a thoroughness that anticipated modern corporate intelligence. The system was self-reinforcing: the information gave Standard a competitive advantage that generated profits, which funded further expansion, which produced more information. Competitors faced a choice between joining the trust on Rockefeller’s terms or being gradually strangled by an organization that knew more about their businesses than they did themselves.

The obsession with being the low-cost producer never wavered throughout Rockefeller’s career. He drove costs down at every stage of the operation—manufacturing his own barrels, running his own cooperages, buying in bulk, eliminating waste with an intensity that bordered on the pathological. A fraction of a cent saved on each barrel of kerosene translated into millions over the scale of Standard’s operations, and Rockefeller understood this arithmetic with the clarity of a man for whom numbers were a native language. When oil prices crashed, Rockefeller saw opportunity where others saw catastrophe. “We must try and not lose our nerve when the market gets to the bottom,” he counseled. “We will surely make a great mistake if we do not buy.” His personal demeanor matched this philosophy of patient accumulation. He recited a favorite verse: “A wise old owl lived in an oak, / The more he saw the less he spoke.” He maintained a curious frugality that coexisted with enormous wealth—once billing a houseguest six hundred dollars for a summer stay.

The Lima-Indiana oil fields that opened in the mid-1880s tested Rockefeller’s conviction against the judgment of his own partners. The oil was sulfurous—dismissed as “skunk juice” by refiners who could not remove its nauseating stench. Rockefeller overruled his “conservative brethren” and ordered Standard to stockpile forty million barrels, gambling that chemistry would eventually solve the problem. Herman Frasch, a German-born chemist, did exactly that, developing a process for removing sulfur that doubled the value of Lima crude overnight. Rockefeller’s order was characteristically blunt: “Buy all we can get.” By 1891, Standard Oil produced a quarter of America’s crude output, and by 1895 the Joseph Seep Agency effectively determined crude prices for the entire nation.

Standard Oil was simultaneously the most reviled and the most admired corporation in America. Henry Demarest Lloyd’s 1881 article in the Atlantic Monthly, “The Story of a Great Monopoly,” went through seven printings and established the template for muckraking journalism. Mothers warned their children: “Rockefeller will get you if you don’t mind.” The company was denounced as “the Octopus,” its tentacles reaching into every corner of American commerce. Yet Rockefeller also gave away $550 million over his lifetime, founded the University of Chicago, and insisted that no building bear his name. He loved fast horses, played the counting game “Buzz” with his children, and despite his extraordinary gift for numbers could never get past seventy-one without making a mistake. The contradiction between public monster and private philanthropist was never resolved—because in Rockefeller’s mind, no contradiction existed.

Chapter 3: Competitive Commerce

Standard Oil’s global dominance was first challenged not from within America but from the oil fields surrounding the ancient city of Baku, on the western shore of the Caspian Sea. In 1873, Robert Nobel arrived in the Caucasus on a mission to purchase walnut wood for rifle stocks on behalf of his brother Ludwig’s armaments business. Robert took the 25,000 rubles designated for walnut purchases and bought a small oil refinery instead—a unilateral act of insubordination that launched one of the great industrial dynasties of the nineteenth century. Ludwig, initially furious, quickly recognized the opportunity and poured his engineering genius into transforming Baku’s primitive oil operations into a modern, vertically integrated enterprise whose workers proudly called themselves “Nobelites.”

Ludwig Nobel’s innovations were remarkable. He commissioned the first purpose-built oil tanker, the Zoroaster, launched in 1878, which replaced the dangerous practice of shipping oil in barrels aboard conventional vessels. He hired the first professional petroleum geologist, bringing scientific method to an industry that still relied largely on luck and intuition. He built pipelines, refineries, storage facilities, and a distribution network that stretched across the Russian Empire. Russian oil production reached 10.8 million barrels by 1884—fully a third of American output. But Baku itself was a hellscape. The district known as Black Town housed over two hundred refineries, and the air was so thick with petroleum smoke that one visitor compared it to “confinement in a chimney-pot.”

The Rothschilds entered the Russian oil business through the back door of railroad finance. Baron Alphonse—reputed to have the best moustaches on the Continent—and Baron Edmond, who would later become the principal financial sponsor of Jewish settlement in Palestine, had bankrolled the construction of the Baku-to-Batum railroad. Recognizing that the railroad’s profitability depended on oil traffic, they formed Bnito in 1886 and began competing directly with the Nobels for Baku’s output. The combined Russian challenge was substantial: America’s share of world petroleum exports fell from seventy-eight percent in 1888 to seventy-one percent by 1891, the first significant erosion of Standard Oil’s global supremacy.

Death produced an unexpected legacy from the Nobel family. When Ludwig Nobel died in 1888, several newspapers confused the brothers and published obituaries for Alfred Nobel, the inventor of dynamite. Alfred was horrified to read himself condemned as a “merchant of death” whose fortune was built on the instruments of destruction. The experience shattered his complacency about his own legacy and drove him to rewrite his will, directing his vast fortune to the establishment of annual prizes for achievements in physics, chemistry, medicine, literature, and peace. The Nobel Prizes—among the most prestigious honors in the world—thus owe their existence, indirectly, to a journalist’s error and the Baku oil trade that entangled the Nobel family in the first place.

The man who broke Standard Oil’s stranglehold on the Asian kerosene market was Marcus Samuel, the son of a London merchant who sold decorative boxes made from seashells to tourists at Brighton. Samuel conceived a daring scheme to ship Russian kerosene through the Suez Canal to the markets of the Far East, undercutting Standard’s prices by eliminating the long voyage around the Cape of Good Hope. The plan required solving four problems simultaneously: designing tankers that could be steam-cleaned for return cargo to satisfy Suez Canal safety regulations; securing Canal passage that Standard’s own solicitors had lobbied to deny him; building storage tanks across Asia; and guaranteeing a reliable supply of Rothschild kerosene.

On August 23, 1892, Samuel’s tanker the Murex—named for a genus of seashell in tribute to his late father—passed through the Suez Canal loaded with Russian kerosene bound for Singapore and Bangkok. It was a commercial coup that Standard Oil had not anticipated and could not easily counter. But Samuel discovered that conquering a market required more than clever logistics. Asian customers refused to relinquish Standard’s familiar blue kerosene tins, which they prized for secondary uses—roofing material, birdcages, even opium cups. Samuel responded by shipping tinplate to Asia and manufacturing his own containers. When his Shanghai agent cabled asking what color the tins should be, Samuel’s answer was emphatic: “Red!” Within a few years, red roofs replaced blue across the villages of Southeast Asia.

The Murex’s voyage through the Suez Canal was a watershed in the history of global commerce. It demonstrated that bulk oil could be shipped economically through the world’s most important maritime chokepoint, opening the vast Asian market to European and Russian producers for the first time. Standard Oil had relied on its American production base and its network of sailing vessels carrying tinned kerosene around the Cape of Good Hope—a route that added weeks and significant cost to every shipment. Samuel’s innovation cut that distance in half and introduced the specialized oil tanker as a permanent feature of global shipping. Within a decade, tankers would account for a significant share of world tonnage, and the chokepoints through which they passed—Suez, the Straits of Malacca, the Strait of Hormuz—would become strategic flashpoints that naval planners in every major capital monitored obsessively.

Samuel’s great weakness was organizational. “He ran everything out of his hat,” one associate observed, making decisions on instinct rather than system. The Samuel brothers’ business discussions were legendary for their volatility— “calling each other fool, idiot, imbecile, until suddenly they were in agreement again.” Marcus craved social respectability more than commercial empire, aspiring to become Lord Mayor of London, a goal he eventually achieved. His company, which would become Shell Transport and Trading, lacked the managerial depth and strategic discipline that its rapidly expanding operations demanded. This weakness would prove decisive when a more systematic competitor arrived on the scene.

Through the 1890s, Standard Oil, the Rothschilds, and the Nobels fought a three-cornered war for global markets while simultaneously exploring the possibility of a grand merger that would divide the world among them. A sweeping alliance was actually signed on March 14, 1895, allocating seventy-five percent of the global market to Standard and twenty-five percent to the Russian producers. But the agreement never came into force—undermined by mutual suspicion, shifting market conditions, and the emergence of new production sources that no cartel could control. Standard’s attention was already turning eastward, toward the oil fields of Sumatra and a small Dutch company called Royal Dutch that would soon become its most formidable rival.

The failure of the 1895 grand alliance was a harbinger of a pattern that would repeat throughout petroleum history: every attempt to stabilize the market through cartel arrangements would eventually be undone by the entry of new producers, the discovery of new fields, and the inability of competing commercial interests to maintain discipline over time. The oil business was simply too dynamic, too geographically dispersed, and too lucrative to be contained within any fixed allocation scheme. Standard Oil’s dominance had been sustained not by agreement but by ruthless competitive advantage—by controlling refining, transportation, and distribution so completely that rivals could not survive. When that control eroded, nothing could replace it. The dream of a managed global oil market would persist for decades, animating everything from the Achnacarry Agreement to OPEC, but the forces of competition would always reassert themselves in the end.

Chapter 4: The New Century

The oil fields of the Dutch East Indies entered the global petroleum contest through the keen eye of a tobacco planter. In 1880, Aeilko Jans Zijlker was traveling through the jungles of Sumatra when he noticed a native overseer carrying a torch that burned with unusual brightness and steadiness. Investigation revealed that the torch was fueled by oil seeping naturally from the ground—a geological gift that the local population had exploited for generations without recognizing its commercial potential. Zijlker spent a decade turning this observation into an enterprise, founding the Royal Dutch Company in 1890 with a charter from the Dutch crown. He died in Singapore before seeing his company produce a single barrel, a fate that echoed the misfortunes of so many petroleum pioneers.

J.B.A. Kessler inherited the chaos that Zijlker left behind. When he arrived in Sumatra, he found men laboring waist-deep in swamp water, supplies exhausted, and rice running out. The jungle itself was an enemy—malaria, tropical heat, impenetrable vegetation, and three hundred marauding pirates who viewed the drilling operation as an irresistible target for plunder. Kessler imposed order through sheer force of will, transforming a failing expedition into a functioning enterprise. On February 28, 1892, oil arrived at the surface “with a roar as of a mighty storm,” and Royal Dutch was in business. Kessler’s motto captured the survival mentality that would define the company: “To stagnate means to liquidate.”

The man who transformed Royal Dutch from a colonial operation into a global competitor was Henri Deterding, a former bank clerk from the Netherlands who rose to become managing director through a combination of mathematical brilliance, commercial instinct, and ruthless negotiating skill. Deterding could calculate complex shipping costs and profit margins in his head faster than his colleagues could work them on paper. He understood that the oil business was ultimately a logistics business—that the company which moved petroleum most efficiently from wellhead to consumer would dominate the market regardless of how much crude it produced. Under his leadership, Royal Dutch developed a level of operational discipline that contrasted sharply with Shell’s improvisational style.

The merger that created one of the world’s great oil companies came about because Marcus Samuel could not manage the empire he had built. Fred Lane, a respected City of London financier who sat on Shell’s board, resigned with a devastating critique: “Business like this cannot be conducted by an occasional glance in one’s spare time.” Lane’s departure signaled to the market that Shell was badly led, and Samuel’s position weakened rapidly. Deterding sensed the opportunity and pressed his advantage with characteristic directness. When negotiations opened for a merger, he insisted on a sixty-forty split in Royal Dutch’s favor. Samuel protested, but Deterding was immovable: “I am at present in a generous mood. If you leave this room without accepting it, the offer is off.” Samuel accepted.

In 1907, the Royal Dutch/Shell Group was formally established—sixty percent Royal Dutch, forty percent Shell Transport and Trading. Three men ran the combined operation: Deterding handled business strategy, Hugo Loudon oversaw technical operations, and Robert Waley Cohen managed commercial affairs. The Group immediately began challenging Standard Oil on its home turf. Deterding dispatched Mark Abrahams to Tulsa, Oklahoma, in 1912, where Abrahams arrived carrying his own typewriter and $2,500 in a money belt. He established Roxana Petroleum as Shell’s American subsidiary, and Deterding could finally declare: “At last we are in America!” The American venture was a calculated provocation, signaling to the Standard Oil fragments that Shell intended to fight on their home soil as aggressively as it fought in Asia, Europe, and the Middle East.

The Rothschilds’ departure from Russian oil in 1912 was a landmark event in the industry’s evolution. Weary of the strikes, arson, anti-Semitic violence, and revolutionary chaos that had plagued their Baku operations for a decade, the banking dynasty sold its entire Russian petroleum portfolio to Royal Dutch/Shell. The transaction transformed uncertain Russian assets into diversified international holdings, a trade the Rothschilds never regretted. For Shell, the acquisition dramatically expanded its global reach and reshaped its portfolio: fifty-three percent East Indies, seventeen percent Romania, twenty-nine percent Russia. The Group was now the only entity that could challenge Standard Oil on every continent simultaneously, with production, refining, and distribution capabilities spanning from the tropics to the Arctic.

Meanwhile, the Russian oil industry that had fueled both the Nobels and the Rothschilds was being consumed by revolutionary violence. Baku had become a hotbed of radical politics, its oil workers radicalized by brutal working conditions and inspired by the revolutionary movements sweeping the Russian Empire. A secret printing operation codenamed “Nina” produced copies of Lenin’s newspaper Iskra in the oil district. Among the underground organizers was a young Georgian seminarian named Joseph Djugashvili—who would later take the name Stalin—operating under the alias “Koba.” The 1905 Revolution devastated the industry, destroying two-thirds of all Russian oil wells in an orgy of arson and sabotage.

Stalin later credited Baku with his political formation: “There in Baku I received my second baptism in revolutionary combat.” The violence drove the Rothschilds to a momentous decision. By 1912, weary of anti-Semitic persecution, crippling strikes, and the constant threat of arson, they sold their entire Russian oil operation to the Royal Dutch/Shell Group. The deal transformed uncertain Russian assets into diversified international holdings, a trade the Rothschilds never regretted. For Shell, the acquisition reshaped its global portfolio: fifty-three percent East Indies, seventeen percent Romania, and twenty-nine percent Russia. The Group was now the only entity that could challenge Standard Oil on every continent simultaneously.

The opening years of the twentieth century thus saw the oil industry consolidate from dozens of competing firms into a handful of dominant players whose rivalries would shape global politics for generations. Standard Oil remained the colossus, but its monopoly was cracking under pressure from Royal Dutch/Shell in the East, political upheaval in Russia, and a growing antitrust movement at home that would soon strike at its very foundations. The age of kerosene illumination was giving way to something larger—the age of the internal combustion engine—and the companies that controlled the world’s oil supply were positioning themselves for a contest whose stakes were about to multiply beyond anything their founders had imagined.

Chapter 5: The Dragon Slain

The campaign to destroy Standard Oil’s monopoly was led by a woman whose father had been ruined by it. Ida Tarbell grew up in the oil regions of Pennsylvania, the daughter of a small producer who watched helplessly as Rockefeller’s machine crushed independent operators through rebates, drawbacks, and predatory pricing. She brought a journalist’s tenacity and a historian’s rigor to her investigation, spending years combing through court records, corporate filings, and the testimony of scores of witnesses. Her nineteen-part series in McClure’s Magazine, published between 1902 and 1904, was the most devastating piece of investigative journalism in American history to that point. It portrayed Standard Oil not merely as an aggressive competitor but as a predatory conspiracy that corrupted railroads, crushed rivals, and subverted democratic governance for private gain.

Tarbell’s work ignited a political firestorm that even Rockefeller’s army of lawyers and lobbyists could not contain. President Theodore Roosevelt, already committed to trust-busting as a defining cause of his presidency, seized on the public outrage to launch a federal prosecution. The case wound its way through the courts for years, generating mountains of testimony and legal argument. Standard Oil’s attorneys deployed every delay and procedural maneuver available, but the evidence of monopolistic behavior was overwhelming. Rockefeller himself maintained his characteristic silence, retreating to his estates and his golf courses while his subordinates fought the legal battle. When reporters cornered him, he offered only bland pleasantries and his wise old owl poem.

On May 15, 1911, the Supreme Court of the United States ordered Standard Oil dissolved into thirty-four independent companies. It was the most dramatic antitrust action in American history and seemed, at the time, to represent a definitive victory for the forces of competition over monopoly. The successor companies were forbidden from coordinating their activities, and each was expected to compete independently in its designated territory. Among the largest fragments were Standard Oil of New Jersey (later Exxon), Standard Oil of New York (later Mobil), Standard Oil of California (later Chevron), and Standard Oil of Indiana (later Amoco). Together, these companies and their siblings would dominate the global oil industry for the rest of the century.

The supreme irony of the dissolution was that it made Rockefeller richer than the monopoly ever had. As the successor companies competed and expanded into new markets, their stock prices soared. Rockefeller, who held substantial shares in all thirty-four entities, watched his net worth multiply several times over in the years following the breakup. The trust had been worth hundreds of millions; the independent companies were collectively worth billions. Rockefeller had been the wealthiest man in America before the dissolution; he became the wealthiest man in the world after it, his fortune estimated at nearly a billion dollars at its peak—an almost inconceivable sum in an era when the entire federal budget was barely three times that amount.

At the same time that Standard Oil was being dismembered by the courts, a discovery in southeastern Texas was rewriting the geography of American petroleum. Spindletop, the great salt dome gusher near Beaumont, had blown in on January 10, 1901, sending a column of oil two hundred feet into the air and producing more petroleum in a single day than all the wells in Pennsylvania combined. Spindletop demonstrated that the oil business was not merely a northeastern phenomenon but a continental one, with reserves of staggering size waiting beneath the Gulf Coast, the Great Plains, and the Rocky Mountain West. It also spawned a new generation of oil companies— Gulf Oil, Texaco, and Sun Oil—that owed nothing to Rockefeller and competed aggressively with the Standard Oil fragments from their inception.

The dissolution transformed the American oil industry from a monopoly into an oligopoly, a shift that paradoxically increased both competition and consolidation. The successor companies invaded each other’s territories with a ferocity that Standard’s internal committees had suppressed for decades. Phillips Petroleum opened its first gas station in 1927 and had 6,750 outlets within three years. Texaco and Shell marketed in all forty-eight states. Ida Tarbell herself watched the result with astonishment: “It certainly looks as if the Standard Oil Company might be crumbling—crumbling within.” But what was actually happening was not destruction but metamorphosis. The single octopus was becoming a school of sharks, each one leaner and more aggressive than the monopoly had ever been.

The new competitive landscape demanded new methods of finding oil. The wildcatter’s intuition was gradually supplemented by the instruments of modern geophysics: the torsion balance, the seismograph, the magnetometer, aerial surveillance, and the emerging science of micropaleontology. The Signal Hill discovery in 1921 made California the nation’s top-producing state, generating a quarter of all world oil output. The deepest wells pushed from 6,000 feet in 1918 to 10,000 feet by 1930, opening geological horizons that earlier technology could not have reached. The oil industry was becoming a capital-intensive, technology-driven enterprise that bore little resemblance to the days of Uncle Billy Smith peering down a sixty-nine-foot hole with a candle.

Throughout this transformation, Rockefeller himself endured, living on to the age of ninety-seven, increasingly removed from the industry he had created. He spent his final decades playing golf, distributing dimes to children and strangers, and overseeing the philanthropic empire that would become his most enduring legacy. The University of Chicago, the Rockefeller Foundation, the Rockefeller Institute for Medical Research—these institutions reshaped American education, public health, and scientific research in ways that outlasted every barrel of oil Standard ever refined. Whether Rockefeller was a villain or a visionary depended entirely on which part of his legacy one chose to examine, and the debate has never been resolved.

Chapter 6: The Oil Wars

The decade before World War I saw oil transform from a commodity into a strategic asset, and nowhere was this transformation more visible than in the bitter three-cornered contest between Standard Oil, Royal Dutch/Shell, and the various state-backed enterprises that sought to wrest control of petroleum from private hands. The battlefield was global—stretching from the jungles of Borneo to the deserts of Mesopotamia, from the refineries of Romania to the wellheads of California—and the combatants employed every weapon available: price wars, espionage, diplomatic pressure, bribery, and occasionally outright sabotage. Oil was becoming too important to be left to oilmen, and governments from London to Berlin to Washington were drawn inexorably into the struggle.

Henri Deterding waged his campaign against Standard Oil with the methodical intensity of a military commander. He understood that the American colossus, even after its dissolution into thirty-four fragments, retained enormous advantages in capital, distribution networks, and sheer scale. To compete, Shell needed to be everywhere at once—producing in the East Indies, refining in Europe, marketing in Asia, and establishing a foothold in the United States itself. Deterding drove his managers relentlessly, demanding efficiency, punishing complacency, and making decisions with a speed that left competitors scrambling to respond. His colleagues compared him to Napoleon, and the comparison was not entirely flattering—Deterding shared both the Corsican’s strategic brilliance and his autocratic temperament.

The contest for the Romanian oil fields illustrated the emerging pattern of oil as a pawn in great-power diplomacy. Romania possessed some of Europe’s richest petroleum deposits, and control of those fields was contested by German, British, Dutch, and American interests, each backed to varying degrees by their home governments. Standard Oil and Deutsche Bank jockeyed for concessions; Royal Dutch/Shell maneuvered to consolidate its position; and the Romanian government played the rivals against each other, extracting maximum advantage from its geological good fortune. The competition prefigured the pattern that would dominate Middle Eastern oil politics for the rest of the century—producing nations leveraging their resources against consuming nations desperate for supply.

In the Far East, Shell and Standard fought for the vast and growing market in kerosene that was replacing traditional illuminants across China, India, and Southeast Asia. Shell’s advantage was geographic proximity to its East Indies production; Standard’s was its global marketing infrastructure and the sheer volume of American crude. The competition drove prices down, benefiting millions of Asian consumers who gained access to cheap, reliable light for the first time. But it also drove both companies toward greater integration, forcing them to control every stage of the supply chain from wellhead to lamp wick in order to squeeze out the margins that competition eroded at every turn.

The emergence of the automobile added a new dimension to the oil wars. Before 1900, petroleum’s primary product was kerosene; gasoline was a dangerous waste byproduct that refiners often dumped into rivers. Henry Ford’s Model T, introduced in 1908, changed everything. By making the automobile affordable for middle-class Americans, Ford created a market for gasoline that would soon dwarf the kerosene trade. The oil companies recognized the implications almost immediately: the future of petroleum was not illumination but transportation, and the company that controlled the gasoline supply chain would dominate the industry for the next century. Refineries were redesigned to maximize gasoline output; distribution networks were reoriented around filling stations rather than lamp-oil dealers; and the entire economics of the oil business shifted beneath the industry’s feet.

The scale of the gasoline revolution can be measured in a single statistic: in 1899, American refineries produced roughly 15 million barrels of kerosene and virtually no gasoline. By 1919, gasoline production exceeded kerosene for the first time, and by 1929 it dwarfed kerosene by a ratio of more than five to one. The reversal was so complete that oil companies which had built their empires on illumination found themselves reorganizing around a product their founders had considered worthless. The entire value chain had to be reinvented—new refining processes to maximize gasoline yield, new distribution networks centered on roadside filling stations rather than general stores, new marketing strategies pitched at automobile owners rather than housewives buying lamp oil. The transition was remarkably swift, completed in barely two decades.

Governments entered the oil business not merely as regulators but as owners and operators. The British government’s decision to acquire a controlling stake in Anglo-Persian Oil Company in 1914 was the most dramatic example, but it was not unique. France, Germany, and Russia all pursued various forms of state involvement in petroleum, driven by the recognition that a commodity so vital to economic life and military power could not be left entirely to the vagaries of the market. The tension between private enterprise and state control would define oil politics for the rest of the century, producing everything from nationalization in Mexico and Iran to the elaborate regulatory frameworks of Texas and Louisiana.

The oil wars of the pre-World War I era established patterns that persist to this day. The consuming nations’ anxiety about security of supply, the producing nations’ determination to maximize the value of their geological inheritance, the oil companies’ drive to control every link in the supply chain, and the constant tension between competition and cartelization—all of these dynamics were visible in the decade before 1914, and all of them remain operative more than a century later. The specific players have changed, but the fundamental game has not. Oil remains the prize, and the contest to control it remains the central drama of the global economy.

By the eve of World War I, petroleum had completed its transformation from a Pennsylvania curiosity into a global strategic commodity. The industry was dominated by a handful of giant companies—the Standard Oil fragments, Royal Dutch/Shell, and the emerging Anglo-Persian—that competed and cooperated in roughly equal measure. Governments had been drawn into the contest, some as regulators, others as owners, and the line between private commerce and state policy had blurred beyond recognition. The automobile was multiplying demand at a rate that outpaced even the most optimistic production forecasts. And in the admiralties and war ministries of the great powers, military planners were recognizing that the next war would be fought not with cavalry and sailing ships but with tanks, trucks, aircraft, and battleships that consumed petroleum by the millions of barrels.

Chapter 7: “Beer and Skittles” in Persia

The story of oil in Persia began with a middleman, a millionaire, and a shah in need of cash. Antoine Kitabgi, a well-connected Persian official, set out in the late 1890s to find “a capitalist of the highest order” willing to bankroll the exploration of Persia’s rumored petroleum deposits. He found his man in William Knox D’Arcy, an Australian who had made a fortune in gold mining and now lived the life of an English gentleman on a scale so lavish that he maintained the only private box at Epsom racecourse besides the royal family’s. D’Arcy was, by temperament, “an investor and speculator, not a manager”—the kind of man who would risk a fortune on a hunch and then delegate the details to others. On May 28, 1901, Shah Muzaffar al-Din signed a concession granting D’Arcy exclusive rights to petroleum across three-quarters of Persia for sixty years, in exchange for £20,000 in cash, £20,000 in shares, and sixteen percent of profits.

The man charged with actually finding the oil was George Reynolds, an engineer whose stubbornness matched the terrain he was sent to explore. Operations centered on Chiah Surkh, an inaccessible site where equipment had to be shipped to Basra, hauled up the Tigris by barge, and then carried overland by mule through mountains where the temperature reached 120 degrees and where, as one observer noted, “even the wheelbarrow was a startling innovation.” Workers dropped from heat exhaustion; supply lines stretched across hundreds of miles of roadless desert; and D’Arcy, watching his fortune drain away from the comfort of his London club, sent increasingly desperate telegrams: “Delay serious. Pray expedite.” He spent over £200,000—a staggering sum—without seeing a drop of commercial oil.

D’Arcy’s money was running out, and he sought help from the British government. The Chancellor of the Exchequer refused, but the Admiralty— increasingly aware that the Navy’s future depended on securing oil supplies—arranged a rescue. Lord Strathcona, eighty-four years old and motivated by imperial patriotism rather than profit, agreed to lead a “syndicate of patriots” that would provide additional capital. “Not for commerce,” Strathcona insisted, “but really from an imperial point of view.” Burmah Oil became the operational partner, though the relationship was not without its comedy. Burmah’s vice-chairman visited D’Arcy at Epsom, where the rich food made him sick for weeks. He never accepted another invitation from D’Arcy.

Life in the Persian oil fields was, as one veteran observed, “not all beer and skittles.” The drinking water was described as “water with dung in suspension”; the nearest dentist was 1,500 miles away in Karachi; malaria was endemic; and the local tribesmen alternated between indifference and hostility. George Reynolds endured these conditions year after year, driving his workers with the same single-minded determination that had carried Drake through the Pennsylvania wilderness half a century earlier. He clashed constantly with his London overseers, who demanded results without understanding the conditions, and with Burmah Oil’s accountants, who scrutinized every expenditure while offering no useful technical guidance.

The breakthrough came at four o’clock in the morning on May 26, 1908, at a site called Masjid-i-Suleiman—the Mosque of Solomon. A gusher rose fifty feet into the night sky, and the drilling crew knew immediately that they had struck oil in commercial quantities. The geologist Wilson sent the news to London in code, referencing a biblical passage: “See Psalm 104 verse 15 third sentence”— “and oil to make his face to shine.” In an echo of Drake’s story that bordered on the uncanny, Reynolds had received Burmah’s letter ordering him to shut down operations shortly before the discovery. Had the shutdown come a week earlier, the history of the Middle East might have unfolded very differently.

The Anglo-Persian Oil Company went public on April 19, 1909, and the offering was a sensation. Investors stood ten deep at the Bank of Scotland counter, scrambling to buy shares in what promised to be one of the great industrial enterprises of the new century. D’Arcy received shares valued at £895,000—a handsome return on his investment, though far less than the concession would ultimately prove to be worth. He signed the final papers with mixed emotions.

“I feel like signing away a child.” —William Knox D’Arcy, on transferring his Persian oil concession

D’Arcy’s lament was prescient. The concession he had nursed from a Persian court document into one of the great industrial prizes of the twentieth century would generate billions in revenue, reshape the geopolitics of the Middle East, and fuel the British Empire through two world wars. D’Arcy himself would die in 1917 as a wealthy man but not an extraordinarily wealthy one, having traded the upside of his gamble for the security of Burmah Oil’s partnership. Reynolds, the engineer who had actually found the oil through years of grinding labor in conditions that would have broken most men, received far less recognition and compensation than his achievement warranted—a pattern depressingly familiar in the history of petroleum exploration, where the men who risk their capital are celebrated while the men who risk their lives are forgotten.

The discovery at Masjid-i-Suleiman transformed the geopolitics of the Middle East by establishing Persia as a significant oil-producing nation and by giving the British government a direct strategic interest in the region’s stability. Anglo-Persian was not merely a commercial enterprise; it was an instrument of imperial policy, a reality that would shape the company’s relationship with its host country for decades and ultimately contribute to the political upheavals that consumed Iran in the twentieth century. The concession that D’Arcy negotiated in 1901 would remain a source of resentment and conflict until Mohammed Mossadegh nationalized it in 1951, setting off a chain of events that continues to reverberate today.

The Persian discovery also marked the moment when the center of gravity of the global oil industry began its long migration from the Western Hemisphere to the Middle East. Pennsylvania, Ohio, Texas, California, Venezuela—these had been the great producing regions of the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries. But the geological formations beneath Persia, Iraq, and the Arabian Peninsula contained reserves of a magnitude that dwarfed anything in the Americas. The full extent of these reserves would not be understood for decades, but the Masjid-i-Suleiman discovery was the first clear signal that the future of petroleum lay beneath the sands of the ancient world.

Chapter 8: The Fateful Plunge

The man who convinced the Royal Navy to bet its future on oil was Admiral John “Jacky” Fisher, one of the most colorful and controversial figures in British military history. Born in Ceylon, sent to sea at the age of thirteen, Fisher was described by contemporaries as “a mixture of Machiavelli and a child”—capable of brilliant strategic insight one moment and petulant tantrums the next. He had championed every major naval innovation of his era, from torpedoes to turbine engines, and by the time Churchill arrived at the Admiralty, Fisher had already been preaching the gospel of oil for more than a decade. “The first of all necessities is SPEED,” Fisher told Churchill, and oil offered an advantage of four extra knots over coal-fired vessels—enough to determine the outcome of any naval engagement.

Fisher’s advocacy was blunt, profane, and laced with the apocalyptic urgency of a man who believed war with Germany was both inevitable and imminent. “Your old women will have a nice time of it when German Motor Battleships are cocking a snook at our ‘Tortoises’!” he wrote to Churchill, dismissing the admirals who clung to coal as timid relics of a vanishing age. Churchill needed little convincing. The three naval construction programs of 1912 through 1914—the greatest additions to the Royal Navy in its history—were designed entirely around oil propulsion. The centerpiece was the Queen Elizabeth class of super-dreadnoughts, the most powerful warships afloat, capable of speeds and firepower that no coal-burning vessel could match. Churchill called this decision “the fateful plunge”—an irreversible commitment that bound the Empire’s survival to a fuel source it did not control.

The strategic implications of oil dependence demanded a political solution, and the man who provided it was Charles Greenway, chairman of Anglo-Persian Oil Company, known in the industry as “Champagne Charlie” and “Old Spats and Monocle” for his dandyish appearance and expensive tastes. Greenway campaigned relentlessly for government investment in Anglo-Persian, arguing that only state ownership could guarantee the Navy a secure and reliable oil supply independent of foreign commercial interests. He was not above playing the card of prejudice, stressing Marcus Samuel’s “Jewishness” and Deterding’s “Dutchness” to emphasize that Shell could never be trusted with so vital a national interest.

Churchill made the case to Parliament on June 17, 1914, in a speech that framed oil as a matter of national survival rather than commercial policy. “If we cannot get oil, we cannot get corn, cotton and a thousand commodities necessary for the preservation of the economic energies of Great Britain,” he declared. The government proposed investing £2.2 million for a fifty-one percent stake in Anglo-Persian, effectively nationalizing the company without calling it nationalization. The debate was spirited but one-sided. Marcus Samuel rose three times to object, only to be swatted down by Churchill: “He had better hear the case for the prosecution before he offers an argument for the defense.” The bill passed 254 to 18.

“If we cannot get oil, we cannot get corn, cotton and a thousand commodities necessary for the preservation of the economic energies of Great Britain.” —Winston Churchill, address to Parliament, June 17, 1914

Fisher, despite his public support for Anglo-Persian, privately harbored an enormous admiration for the man he was supposed to distrust. Of Henri Deterding, Fisher wrote: “He is Napoleon and Cromwell rolled into one. He is the greatest man I ever met.” The compliment was all the more striking given that Fisher spent most of his career surrounded by some of the most powerful figures in the British Empire. For his part, Deterding wrote patriotically to Churchill on July 31, 1914—the eve of war—promising to supply oil and tankers to the Royal Navy regardless of commercial considerations. It was a pledge that Shell would honor throughout the conflict, even as it fought Anglo-Persian for postwar advantage.

The timing of Churchill’s parliamentary triumph was extraordinary in its historical irony. The vote to acquire Anglo-Persian took place on June 17, 1914. Eleven days later, on June 28, Archduke Franz Ferdinand of Austria was assassinated in Sarajevo, setting in motion the chain of events that would plunge Europe into the most destructive war in human history. Within weeks, the strategic decision that Churchill had fought for would be tested under conditions that even the most pessimistic planners had not imagined. The Royal Navy’s oil-fired fleet would prove its superiority in speed and efficiency, but the broader question of wartime oil supply would generate crises that brought the Allied cause to the brink of collapse.

The coal-to-oil transition also transformed naval logistics in ways that extended far beyond raw speed. Coal-fired warships required enormous crews of stokers to feed the furnaces—men who worked in conditions of almost unbearable heat and physical exhaustion, shoveling tons of coal per hour into roaring boilers. Oil-fired vessels eliminated most of this labor, freeing manpower for combat roles and reducing the physical punishment that coaling inflicted on crews. Refueling at sea became practical with oil, whereas coaling stations had required ships to anchor in port for hours or even days, exposing them to attack. The operational flexibility that oil provided was as significant as the speed advantage—oil-fired fleets could range farther, stay at sea longer, and respond to threats more rapidly than their coal-burning predecessors. Every major navy in the world would eventually follow Britain’s lead, and the global network of coaling stations that had defined nineteenth-century naval strategy became as obsolete as the sailing ships they had replaced.

The government’s purchase of Anglo-Persian established a precedent that would echo through the twentieth century. For the first time, a major industrial nation had acknowledged that oil was too important to be left to private enterprise alone, that the security of supply was a function of state power as much as market forces, and that the line between commercial competition and national strategy had been permanently erased. Every subsequent government intervention in the oil industry—from nationalization in Mexico and Iran to the creation of strategic petroleum reserves in the United States—can trace its intellectual lineage to Churchill’s fateful plunge in the summer of 1914.

The decision also bound Britain’s strategic fortunes to the Persian Gulf for the next eight decades. Anglo-Persian’s concession covered three-quarters of the Persian landmass, and the British government’s fifty-one percent stake gave it both the incentive and the obligation to protect that concession by whatever means necessary. This meant maintaining political influence in Tehran, projecting naval power into the Gulf, and treating any threat to Persian oil as a threat to the Empire itself. The consequences of this entanglement would include the overthrow of Mossadegh in 1953, the support of the Shah’s autocracy for a quarter century, and ultimately the Islamic Revolution of 1979—a chain of causation that began with Churchill’s speech to Parliament on a summer afternoon in 1914.

Chapter 9: Blood of Victory — World War I

The first hint that petroleum would decide the outcome of the Great War came in September 1914, when General Joseph Gallieni, the military governor of Paris, commandeered approximately 3,000 taxicabs to rush reinforcements to the First Battle of the Marne. The German advance had reached the outskirts of the capital, and conventional transport could not move troops fast enough to exploit a gap in the enemy line. The taxi drivers, being Parisian, demanded to know the terms of their service. “By the meter or on a flat rate?” asked one. “By the meter,” came the answer. “All right, let’s go,” the driver replied, putting down his flag as though picking up a fare on the Champs-Élysées. The troops arrived in time, the German advance was halted, and the war settled into the stalemate of the trenches.

The internal combustion engine transformed every dimension of the war. On the ground, the critical innovation was the tank, conceived by Colonel Swinton as an armored vehicle impervious to machine-gun fire and capable of crossing the barbed wire and trenches that had turned the Western Front into a killing ground. The high command ignored the idea; Churchill, then at the Admiralty, funded its development from naval coffers. Various code names were considered—“cistern,” “reservoir”—before the deliberately bland term “tank” was adopted. First deployed prematurely at the Somme in 1916, tanks proved decisive at the Battle of Amiens on August 8, 1918, when 456 of them broke through the German line in what Ludendorff called the “black day of the German Army.”

The mechanization of the British Army was staggering in its scale. At the outbreak of war in August 1914, Britain possessed 827 motorcars for military use. By the armistice, that number had grown to 56,000 trucks, 23,000 automobiles, and 34,000 motorcycles. The United States brought another 50,000 gasoline-driven vehicles to France. The horse, which had carried armies since antiquity, was being replaced by the internal combustion engine, and the logistical consequences were revolutionary. Trucks could move supplies faster, farther, and more reliably than horse-drawn wagons, reducing the dependency on railroads that had governed military strategy since the American Civil War. “The victory of the Allies over Germany was in some ways the victory of the truck over the locomotive,” one historian observed.

In the air, the transformation was even more dramatic. At the war’s outbreak, the entire British aviation industry numbered about a thousand people and had produced approximately 250 aircraft. General Foch dismissed the airplane as “good sport, but for the Army worthless.” Yet by July 1915, every aircraft in service at the war’s beginning was obsolete. Air combat evolved from pilots firing handguns at each other to sophisticated dogfights with synchronized machine guns. Wartime production reached astonishing levels: Britain built 55,000 aircraft, France 68,000, and Germany 48,000. Aircraft speeds doubled to over 120 miles per hour. The airplane had gone from a novelty to a weapon system in less than four years, and every one of those aircraft ran on petroleum.

By 1917, the oil supply that sustained this vast mechanized war machine was under mortal threat. Germany’s unrestricted submarine campaign was sinking Allied tankers at a rate that threatened to strangle the war effort. Samuel’s pioneering tanker, the Murex, was among the casualties. French Premier Clemenceau sent an urgent appeal to President Wilson, declaring that gasoline was “as vital as blood in the coming battles.” The United States, which supplied eighty percent of the Allies’ wartime petroleum, responded with rationing at home. “Gasolineless Sundays” were instituted; when told he could not drive on the Sabbath, President Wilson remarked drily: “I suppose I must walk to church.”

Marcus Samuel, the aging founder of Shell who had been mocked for running his empire “out of his hat,” made a contribution to the war effort that his critics never expected. He masterminded the extraction of toluol from Borneo crude oil, a chemical essential for manufacturing the TNT explosive that filled Allied shells. Shell’s toluol plant in Rotterdam was secretly dismantled at night, each piece numbered and camouflaged, then shipped to England and re-erected in Somerset. The operation provided eighty percent of British military TNT, a statistic that transformed Samuel’s reputation from that of a disorganized merchant prince to a wartime hero. It was, perhaps, his finest hour.

One of the war’s most colorful episodes involved Colonel Norton-Griffiths, known as “Empire Jack,” who was dispatched to Romania in 1916 to prevent the country’s oil from falling into German hands. Norton-Griffiths accomplished his mission with characteristic flamboyance, destroying seventy refineries and 800,000 tons of crude in a campaign of industrial sabotage that featured him kicking challengers, pulling his revolver on reluctant officials, and bellowing “I don’t speak your blasted language” at anyone who stood in his way. Ludendorff later acknowledged that the destruction “did materially reduce the oil supplies” available to the Central Powers. It was scorched-earth warfare applied to an industrial commodity, a tactic that would be repeated in Borneo and Baku during the next world war.

The war also revealed the vulnerability of petroleum supply lines in ways that would shape military strategy for generations. Oil had to travel thousands of miles from American wells or Persian fields to the battlefields of France and Belgium, crossing oceans patrolled by submarines and passing through chokepoints controlled by potentially hostile powers. The logistical chain was staggeringly complex: crude had to be extracted, refined into dozens of specialized products, loaded onto tankers, shipped across submarine-infested waters, offloaded at ports under bombardment, transported by rail or truck to forward depots, and distributed to thousands of individual vehicles, aircraft, and generators. A failure at any point in this chain could halt an army as effectively as enemy fire. The lesson was absorbed by every major military power and would drive strategic planning through the Second World War.

“The Allied cause had floated to victory upon a wave of oil.” —Lord Curzon, November 1918

By October 1918, Germany’s air force and mechanized units would have ceased to function within two months for lack of fuel. When the High Command informed the Kaiser that victory was impossible, the first reason cited was the tank. The war had consumed petroleum on a scale that no one had anticipated, and the nations that controlled the supply had won. Lord Curzon spoke for the victors when he declared that “the Allied cause had floated to victory upon a wave of oil.” The French senator Berenger put it more vividly: oil was “the blood of victory.” The lesson was not lost on the vanquished. Germany and Japan would spend the next two decades pursuing synthetic fuels, foreign conquests, and desperate gambles to secure the petroleum they lacked—efforts that would lead directly to the even greater catastrophe of World War II.

The Great War established oil as the supreme strategic commodity of the twentieth century, displacing coal, iron, and even gold from that distinction. Every major military lesson of the conflict pointed to the same conclusion: mobility won battles, mobility required petroleum, and the nation that controlled petroleum controlled the battlefield. The British, who had entered the war with the advantage of Churchill’s foresight and Fisher’s advocacy, emerged from it with a determination to secure oil supplies that shaped their diplomacy for the next three decades—driving them into Persia, Iraq, and the Arabian Gulf with a sense of strategic necessity that no economic calculation could have generated alone. The Americans, who had supplied eighty percent of the Allies’ oil, emerged with the comforting knowledge that they sat atop the world’s largest proven reserves—a comfort that would prove increasingly illusory as domestic production approached its geological limits and the center of gravity of world oil shifted inexorably toward the Middle East.

Chapter 10: Opening the Door on the Middle East

The architect of Middle Eastern oil diplomacy was a man who had never seen an oil tanker and never visited an oil field after his first encounter left him drenched in crude. Calouste Gulbenkian was an Armenian financier of extraordinary shrewdness, so suspicious by nature that he employed two separate sets of doctors on the theory that neither could be trusted alone. His first business transaction, at the age of seven, involved exchanging a silver coin for an antique of greater value—a bargain that set the pattern for a career built on extracting maximum value from every deal. Gulbenkian’s singular achievement was assembling the Turkish Petroleum Company, a consortium that brought together British, Dutch, German, and Turkish interests to develop the oil potential of Mesopotamia. His motto was simple and inflexible: “Never give up an oil concession.”

Gulbenkian’s role was that of a “business architect”—a term he insisted upon with ferocious pride. At a famous luncheon with the heads of the major oil companies, Standard Oil’s Walter Teagle made the mistake of calling him “an oil merchant.” Gulbenkian reddened and delivered a rebuke that became legendary in industry circles: “I classify myself as a business architect. I designed this Turkish Petroleum Company. I put in its every brick. I conceived the finance. Now the three of you are trying to throw me out on my ass.”

“I classify myself as a business architect. I designed this Turkish Petroleum Company. I put in its every brick. I conceived the finance.” —Calouste Gulbenkian

They did not succeed in dislodging him. Gulbenkian retained his five percent interest in the company through every subsequent reorganization, earning the nickname “Mr. Five Percent” that followed him to his grave.

The geological prize that Gulbenkian’s consortium was pursuing proved even richer than the most optimistic estimates suggested. On October 15, 1927, a drilling crew at Baba Gurgur in northern Iraq struck oil that flowed at an extraordinary 95,000 barrels per day. The well was located near natural gas seepages that had burned continuously for millennia—ancient fires that some scholars identified with Nebuchadnezzar’s “fiery furnace” from the Book of Daniel. The discovery confirmed that Mesopotamia sat atop petroleum reserves of staggering magnitude, and the scramble to control those reserves would dominate Middle Eastern politics for the rest of the century. The Baba Gurgur gusher was so powerful that it took the drilling crew eight days to bring it under control, and the roar of escaping gas and oil could be heard miles away. The well produced more oil in a single day than most American fields produced in a month, and it was only the beginning. Subsequent exploration would reveal that Iraq’s reserves were among the largest on earth—a geological inheritance that would make the country simultaneously blessed and cursed, the object of foreign ambition and domestic conflict for the rest of the twentieth century and beyond.

Gulbenkian’s most consequential act was drawing a thick red line on a map of the Middle East, tracing the boundaries of the former Ottoman Empire. The Red Line Agreement that resulted bound all partners in the Turkish Petroleum Company (renamed the Iraq Petroleum Company) not to pursue oil concessions independently within that territory. It was a cartel arrangement of breathtaking scope, covering virtually the entire Arabian Peninsula, Iraq, Syria, and Turkey. The agreement would constrain and frustrate American oil companies for decades, and its eventual dissolution would be one of the pivotal moments in the postwar reorganization of the global petroleum order. Gulbenkian himself had drawn the line from memory, having never visited the region it enclosed.

The postwar diplomatic maneuvering over Middle Eastern oil was as cynical as any chapter in the history of great-power politics. French Premier Clemenceau traded Mosul to the British in exchange for support in Syria—a casual exchange that neither prime minister bothered to share with his foreign minister. The San Remo Agreement of 1920 gave France twenty-five percent of Mesopotamian oil, a deal negotiated behind closed doors that stunned Washington when it became public. Standard Oil of New Jersey protested; the American press denounced the agreement as European imperialism violating the Open Door principle. Congress responded with the Mineral Leasing Act of 1920, denying drilling rights to citizens of nations whose governments refused American companies reciprocal access to their territories.

Walter Teagle, the president of Standard Oil of New Jersey, led the American campaign to break into Middle Eastern oil. Teagle was second-generation petroleum—his maternal grandfather was Maurice Clark, the very partner Rockefeller had bought out in that decisive 1865 auction. At Cornell, Teagle had scored a perfect hundred on a thesis about petroleum desulfurization; his father’s response was a telegram reading simply: “Come home at once.” He started at the bottom, firing a still at nineteen cents an hour, then became a formidable negotiator who colleagues said “would think he was paying too much for a five-cent cigar” if spending company money. At thirty-nine, he became president of Jersey Standard, the largest of the Standard Oil fragments and the most aggressive in pursuing international expansion.

The political architecture of the postwar Middle East was constructed by the British with a mixture of imperial ambition and improvisational pragmatism that would have lasting consequences. The British installed Faisal on the newly created throne of Iraq—Lawrence of Arabia described him as “an absolute ripper”—after he had been deposed from Syria and found sitting dejectedly on his luggage at a Palestinian railway station. His brother Abdullah received what one diplomat dismissively called “the vacant lot” of Transjordan. Iraq itself was a patchwork of Shia Arabs, Sunni Arabs, Jews, Kurds, and Yazidis—the Jewish community was actually the largest single group in Baghdad—held together by little more than British authority and the promise of oil revenue.

The postwar settlements created nations and drew borders with little regard for ethnic, tribal, or religious realities on the ground. The mandates that Britain and France imposed on the former Ottoman territories were exercises in cartographic convenience, not political wisdom. Iraq’s three Ottoman provinces—Mosul in the Kurdish north, Baghdad in the Sunni center, and Basra in the Shia south—had no history of common governance, no shared identity, and no natural inclination toward unity. What held them together was oil revenue and foreign authority, and when both proved insufficient, the consequences would be catastrophic. The borders drawn in the 1920s remained the borders of the twenty-first century, and the conflicts they generated—Kurdish separatism, Sunni-Shia sectarian violence, struggles over oil-revenue distribution— persisted with undiminished ferocity.

Commerce Secretary Herbert Hoover organized the American response to European exclusion by suggesting a consortium approach. Teagle assembled the Near East Development Company, bringing together Jersey Standard, Socony, Gulf, Atlantic, and Pan American to present a united American front. The negotiations dragged on for years, complicated by the Red Line Agreement’s restrictions, by French insistence on their share, and by Gulbenkian’s unyielding defense of his five percent. The Americans eventually won their seat at the table, but the process revealed the fundamental tension at the heart of American oil policy: the government wanted American companies to control foreign reserves for strategic reasons, while the companies wanted government support without government control. This tension—between free enterprise and strategic necessity—would define American oil diplomacy for the rest of the century.

Colonel Mercier created the Compagnie Française des Pétroles in 1924 to represent France’s share in the Iraq Petroleum Company, though the enterprise was assembled with notable reluctance. “None of the initial investors begged for the favor,” one participant recalled; ninety banks and companies had to be corralled into participating, none of them enthusiastic about sinking capital into a distant and uncertain venture. Meanwhile, American motor vehicle registrations had jumped from 1.8 million to 9.2 million between 1914 and 1920, creating an insatiable domestic demand for petroleum that made access to Middle Eastern reserves not merely desirable but strategically essential. The stage was set for a confrontation between producing nations and consuming nations, between private companies and sovereign governments, that would define the oil industry for the rest of the century.

Chapter 11: From Shortage to Surplus

The automobile conquered America with a speed that astonished even its most enthusiastic promoters. In 1919, a young Army captain named Dwight Eisenhower joined a military convoy attempting to drive across the continental United States, an expedition that averaged a humiliating five and two-thirds miles per hour. Eisenhower titled his report “Through Darkest America with Truck and Tank.” Roads varied “from average to nonexistent”; some days, counting on sixty miles, the convoy managed three. The experience left such an impression on Eisenhower that decades later, as president, he championed the Interstate Highway System as both a civilian infrastructure project and a military necessity. By 1929, 23.1 million automobiles were registered in the United States— seventy-eight percent of the world’s total, compared to one car for every 702 people in Japan and one for every 6,130 in the Soviet Union.

The growth in automobile ownership drove a parallel explosion in petroleum demand. Total American oil consumption rose from 1.03 million barrels per day in 1919 to 2.58 million by 1929. Oil’s share of total energy consumption grew from ten percent to twenty-five percent in a single decade. Gasoline stations multiplied from 12,000 in 1921 to 143,000 by 1929, transforming the American landscape. Los Angeles became the incubator of the modern service station, and Shell pioneered the standardized “cracker box” design that made its outlets instantly recognizable. Stations sold not just fuel but “TBA”—tires, batteries, and accessories. Oil companies created “uniquely American contributions to cartography” in the form of the free road map, first distributed by Gulf Oil in 1914.

The advertising industry seized on the automobile’s emotional appeal with characteristic exuberance. Bruce Barton, one of the era’s most influential admen, told oil executives that they were selling something far more profound than a commodity.

“It is the juice of the fountain of eternal youth that you are selling. It is health. It is comfort. And you have sold merely a bad smelling liquid at so many cents per gallon.” —Bruce Barton, to oil industry executives

The phrase captured a fundamental truth about the petroleum business that would endure for a century. Oil companies were not merely extracting and refining a hydrocarbon; they were enabling a way of life. The “new light” of kerosene illumination had given way to the “new fuel” of gasoline mobility, and the transformation was irreversible.

The dark side of the petroleum boom was epitomized by the Teapot Dome scandal, the most notorious corruption case in American political history until Watergate. Secretary of the Interior Albert Fall—“the frontiersman” with his signature black Stetson—secretly leased naval oil reserves at Teapot Dome, Wyoming, and Elk Hills, California, to Harry Sinclair and Edward Doheny in exchange for bribes. Walter Teagle of Standard Oil smelled trouble from the start: “All through the industry it smells,” he warned. President Harding, with the tragic loyalty of a man who could not imagine betrayal, declared: “If Albert Fall isn’t an honest man, I’m not fit to be President.” Doheny delivered $100,000 in cash in “a little black bag.”

Fall became the first Cabinet officer in American history convicted of a felony committed while in office. Harry Sinclair attended a board meeting on his way to prison. Doheny, inexplicably, was acquitted, prompting the famous observation: “You can’t convict a million dollars.” The scandal reached even into the Standard Oil family. John D. Rockefeller Jr. led a dramatic proxy fight to oust Colonel Robert Stewart, the chairman of Standard Oil of Indiana, over Stewart’s involvement in Liberty Bond kickbacks connected to the affair. Rockefeller won with sixty percent of shareholder votes, declaring he acted “not for mere profits but in the name of decency.” It was perhaps the most principled act of corporate governance in American business history to that date.

While the Teapot Dome scandal dominated headlines, the technology of oil exploration was undergoing a quiet revolution. The torsion balance, seismograph, magnetometer, aerial surveillance, and the emerging science of micropaleontology were transforming the search for petroleum from a gambler’s art into an applied science. These instruments could detect geological structures thousands of feet below the surface, dramatically improving the odds of drilling in productive formations. The Signal Hill discovery of 1921 made California the nation’s leading oil-producing state, responsible for one quarter of total world output. The deepest wells pushed from 6,000 feet in 1918 to 10,000 by 1930, opening new geological horizons and extending the industry’s reach into formations that had been physically inaccessible just a decade earlier.

The gasoline age also created new cultural rituals and social patterns that would define American life for generations. The Sunday drive became a national pastime; the road trip became a rite of passage; the gas station became a gathering place and a symbol of local commerce. Shell required its station attendants to wear free uniforms, banned magazine reading on duty, and issued instructions of startling racial specificity about service to different customer groups. The oil companies understood that they were not merely selling fuel but cultivating a relationship with the American consumer that was intimate, habitual, and enormously profitable. The filling station was the point of contact, and its design, cleanliness, and service standards became objects of obsessive corporate attention.

Oil companies also recognized the power of cartography as a marketing tool. Gulf Oil began distributing free road maps in 1914, and by the 1920s every major oil company produced its own maps—“uniquely American contributions to cartography,” as one historian noted. These maps did more than guide motorists; they shaped how Americans understood their own country, presenting the nation as a network of highways connecting gas stations, hotels, and attractions. The maps were advertisements disguised as public services, training a generation of drivers to associate long-distance travel with the brand that had shown them the way. The road map era, which lasted until digital navigation made paper maps obsolete, was one of the petroleum industry’s most successful exercises in consumer psychology—the creation of a mental landscape in which oil companies were not merely fuel vendors but enablers of the American dream of mobility and freedom.

By the end of the 1920s, the American petroleum industry had undergone a transformation as dramatic as any in its history. The Standard Oil monopoly had given way to vigorous competition among dozens of major and independent companies. The automobile had replaced the lamp as the primary driver of demand. The science of exploration had matured from intuition to instrumentation. And the scale of the enterprise had grown to encompass millions of workers, hundreds of thousands of service stations, and a transportation infrastructure that stretched from coast to coast. But the very success of this expansion was creating a problem that the industry had not anticipated and could not easily solve: overproduction on a scale that threatened to destroy the economics of the entire business.

Chapter 12: “The Fight for New Production”

The man who saw the catastrophe coming and tried to prevent it was Henry Doherty, a utilities magnate known as “the professor” for his oversized glasses and academic manner. Doherty argued with prophetic urgency that the rule of capture—the legal doctrine that allowed surface landowners to drain oil from common underground pools—was leading the industry toward self-destruction. When wells pumped at maximum rate, they exhausted the natural underground pressure that drove oil to the surface, leaving vast quantities of petroleum permanently trapped in the rock. Doherty’s solution was unitization: treating each oil field as a single reservoir, managed collectively by all surface owners to maximize total recovery. The industry called him “that crazy man” and barred him from speaking at American Petroleum Institute meetings on three separate occasions.

President Coolidge’s Federal Oil Conservation Board eventually confirmed what Doherty had been arguing: the dissipation of natural gas during flush production was leaving enormous quantities of oil permanently unrecoverable, a waste of national resources on a staggering scale. But the political will to act did not exist. Independent producers viewed unitization as a veiled attempt by the major companies to control production, and they fought it with the tenacity of men defending their livelihoods. J. Howard Pew of Sun Oil spoke for the optimists when he declared that rivers would change their course before petroleum was exhausted. The confidence was misplaced, but it reflected a deep American faith in abundance that no amount of geological evidence could shake.

While the debate over conservation played out in Washington and the trade press, the Standard Oil successor companies were busy invading each other’s territories with a competitive ferocity that the old trust had suppressed for decades. Phillips Petroleum opened its first gasoline station in 1927 and had 6,750 outlets within three years. Texaco and Shell marketed in all forty-eight states. The old geographic divisions that had allocated territory among the Standard fragments were dissolving under the pressure of competition, as each company sought growth by expanding into markets that had previously been the exclusive preserve of its siblings. Ida Tarbell watched with astonishment: “It certainly looks as if the Standard Oil Company might be crumbling—crumbling within.”

The global search for new production drove oil companies into some of the most unstable political environments on earth. Mexico’s Golden Lane had produced spectacular results—Lord Cowdray’s Potrero del Llano 4 well flowed at an extraordinary 110,000 barrels per day, making Mexico the world’s second-largest producer by 1921. But the Mexican Revolution’s Article 27 restored national ownership of subsoil resources, threatening to strip foreign companies of their concessions. General Cárdenas ordered preparations to set the oil fields on fire if the United States invaded. Mexican production fell eighty percent in a decade as political uncertainty and declining geological productivity combined to drive the foreign companies elsewhere. The decline of Mexican oil production was one of the great cautionary tales of the interwar period, demonstrating that geological abundance alone was not sufficient to sustain an oil industry—political stability, legal certainty, and access to international capital were equally essential. Mexico’s loss was Venezuela’s gain.

Venezuela offered what Mexico could not: political predictability under a dictator who understood the value of foreign investment. General Juan Vicente Gómez was a cruel and venal strongman who fathered ninety-seven illegitimate children and ran the country as his personal hacienda. But he provided the stability that oil companies craved, and they rewarded him with the revenue that kept his regime afloat. Shell’s Barroso well blew out in December 1922 at 100,000 barrels per day—discovered, remarkably, by the same George Reynolds who had found oil in Persia fourteen years earlier. Jersey Standard’s 4,200 underwater acres in Lake Maracaibo were initially derided by competitors who suggested “the company could go into the fishing business.” The wells proved spectacularly productive, and Lake Maracaibo became one of the most important oil-producing areas in the world. Reynolds’s career thus spanned the entire arc of the early petroleum age—from the deserts of Persia to the jungles of Venezuela, from the dawn of Middle Eastern oil to the rise of Latin American production. He was, in many ways, the quintessential petroleum explorer: technically brilliant, personally difficult, and perpetually undervalued by the corporate hierarchies that profited from his discoveries.

The Russian question haunted the international oil industry throughout the 1920s. The Bolshevik Revolution had confiscated the Nobel and Rothschild properties that Shell had purchased, and the major companies debated fiercely over whether to deal with the Soviet government or maintain a united boycott. The Front Uni of 1922 pledged the companies to fight the Soviets together, but the alliance crumbled when Standard Oil of New York negotiated a separate deal with Moscow. Teagle was indignant: “The idea of trying to be on friendly terms with the man who burglarizes your house has never appealed to me.” Henri Deterding’s anti-communism intensified into something approaching obsession after he married Lydia Pavlova, a White Russian emigree whose family had lost everything in the Revolution. Deterding would eventually cross the line from anti-communism into fascist sympathies, ending his days in Germany in the late 1930s in a relationship with the Nazi regime that horrified his former colleagues and tarnished a brilliant career.

The interplay between oil and politics in the 1920s established a pattern that would prove enduring: the oil companies needed political stability to operate, but their very presence destabilized the political environments they entered. In Mexico, foreign oil wealth fueled revolutionary resentment. In Venezuela, petroleum revenue propped up a dictator whose cruelty was tolerated because he kept the oil flowing. In Russia, the confiscation of oil properties became a symbol of revolutionary legitimacy. And in the Middle East, the scramble for concessions was already creating the tangled web of commitments, resentments, and expectations that would explode into crisis after crisis for the rest of the century. Oil did not merely respond to political conditions; it created them.

The abundance of new production from Venezuela, the East Indies, California, Oklahoma, and Texas created a global surplus that depressed prices and threatened the profitability of the entire industry. Oil companies that had spent decades searching desperately for new reserves now found themselves drowning in supply. The fundamental problem was that petroleum extraction operated under economic incentives that rewarded speed over efficiency: the rule of capture compelled producers to pump as fast as possible, flush production dissipated the pressure needed for long-term recovery, and the resulting glut destroyed prices faster than demand could absorb the surplus. The stage was set for the greatest oil crisis of the interwar period— not a shortage but a flood.

By the late 1920s, the oil industry was caught in a paradox of its own making. The technology of exploration had improved dramatically, the geography of production had expanded to every continent, and the demand from the automobile age was growing at double-digit rates. Yet the industry was less profitable and more chaotic than at any point since the wildcat days of the 1860s. The rule of capture remained the law of the land in every major producing state. Unitization was blocked by political opposition and legal uncertainty. And the international cartel arrangements that the major companies periodically attempted were undermined by the entry of new producers who refused to accept quotas. Something would have to give, and when it did, the consequences would reshape not just the oil industry but the relationship between private enterprise and government authority in the United States. The trigger, when it came, would be a seventy-year-old wildcatter drilling with rusted equipment on a farm in East Texas.

The world that Rockefeller had built—a world of monopoly control, managed production, and administered prices—had been replaced by a world of savage competition, uncontrolled output, and price volatility that threatened the survival of producers and consumers alike. The Standard Oil trust had been destroyed, but nothing had replaced the function it served. The major companies tried cartels; the cartels collapsed. The government tried conservation boards; the industry resisted. Individual producers tried restraint; the rule of capture made restraint suicidal, since any oil left unpumped would simply be drained by a neighbor. The petroleum industry at the end of the 1920s was a system in search of a governing principle, and it would find one only through the most devastating economic crisis in American history—the combination of the East Texas gusher and the Great Depression that together brought oil prices to levels that could not sustain production, much less investment, and finally forced the creation of the regulatory framework that would govern the industry for the next forty years.

Chapter 13: The Flood

By 1930, the American oil industry had spent a decade wrestling with overproduction, conservation debates, and the stubborn persistence of the rule of capture. Into this volatile landscape stumbled Columbus “Dad” Joiner, a seventy-year-old wildcatter drilling with rusted equipment on Daisy Bradford’s farm in East Texas. His geological adviser was a three-hundred-pound charlatan called “Doc Lloyd,” whose only real contribution was telling Joiner where to drill. Workers accepted payment in royalty rights because there was no cash. A Texaco geologist scoffed that he would drink every barrel of oil that hole produced. On October 3, 1930, oil shot above the derrick and the crowd went mad. A crewman fired a pistol into the spray and was tackled before a spark could ignite an inferno.

What Joiner had tapped was the East Texas field — forty-five miles long, spanning 140,000 acres, the largest petroleum deposit ever found in the lower forty-eight states. The discovery came at the worst possible moment. The Great Depression had already crushed demand, and now a biblical flood of crude overwhelmed every market mechanism the industry possessed. Prices collapsed to six cents a barrel, then to two cents — less than a cup of water. Ramshackle “teakettle” refineries sprang up overnight, offering free chicken dinners with each gasoline fill-up to lure customers. The glut was so severe that legitimate producers faced ruin while speculators and fly-by-night operators thrived on chaos. East Texas became a lawless frontier where the rule of capture reigned supreme.

H. L. Hunt — a stout gambler who maintained two wives with growing families in separate households — recognized the opportunity that Joiner could not exploit. Over a thirty-six-hour negotiation at the Baker Hotel in Dallas, Hunt acquired Joiner’s rights for $1.33 million. Hunt possessed secret intelligence about a nearby well that confirmed the field’s enormous extent, knowledge Joiner lacked. The deal made Hunt one of the wealthiest men in America. Joiner, by contrast, died at eighty-seven still wildcatting, his net worth barely exceeding the value of his automobile. The two men embodied the eternal divide in the oil business: the wildcatter who finds the prize and the dealmaker who captures its value.

The flood of East Texas crude created anarchy that demanded political intervention. In Oklahoma, Governor “Alfalfa Bill” Murray declared martial law over the oil fields. In Texas, Governor Sterling dispatched the National Guard and Texas Rangers to enforce order at what became known as “Proration Hill.” The disruption reached even the chickens, which had grown fat feasting on insects attracted to gas flares — when production was curtailed, the birds returned to scratching for worms. The Texas Railroad Commission issued nineteen proration orders in 1932, each one declared invalid by the courts. The legal framework simply could not keep pace with the scale of overproduction.

“Hot oil” — production in excess of legal quotas — reached half a million barrels daily and became a criminal enterprise of impressive ingenuity. Smugglers built secret pipelines that operated under cover of darkness, concealed storage tanks beneath weeds and camouflage, and smoothed their operations with systematic graft. The term itself had an unlikely origin: a militiaman shivering on a chilly night was advised by a suspect to lean against a tank — “It’s hot enough to keep you warm.” The scale of illegal production made a mockery of state regulation and demonstrated that only federal authority could impose order on an industry that refused to discipline itself.

The man who would impose that discipline was Harold Ickes, Franklin Roosevelt’s Secretary of the Interior and newly appointed Oil Administrator. Ickes was “a plump, blond, bespectacled gentleman” armed with powerful liberal convictions, stinging polemics, and immense self-righteousness. Raised by a stern Calvinist mother who forbade whistling on Sundays, he maintained an internal investigating unit that employed wiretaps. When Harry Sinclair visited in 1933, Ickes wrote that he kept wondering whether “the ghost of Albert B. Fall, carrying a little black satchel, might not emerge from one of the gloomy corners.” He was suspicious, combative, and exactly the kind of zealot the crisis required.

The turning point came on May 5, 1933. Entering a Cabinet meeting, Ickes received a telegram reporting that East Texas crude had fallen to four cents a barrel. Roosevelt gave him emergency authority. On September 2, 1933, Ickes set monthly production quotas for each state — and with that stroke, the ancient rule of capture was effectively overturned. What had made sense for deer and game birds on estates in medieval England would no longer govern the extraction of petroleum in the twentieth century. The legal doctrine that surface owners could drain common pools without limit had finally been replaced by managed production.

The regulatory architecture that emerged would govern American oil for decades. The Connally Hot Oil Act made interstate transport of illegally produced oil a federal crime. The Interstate Oil Compact coordinated state-level proration. An oil tariff of twenty-one cents per barrel provided protection against cheap imports. The system worked: from 1934 to 1940, crude oil averaged between one dollar and $1.18 per barrel. The magical “dollar a barrel” that producers had dreamed of was finally realized. The wild boom-and-bust cycle that had characterized American oil since Drake’s well was tamed — not by the market, but by government intervention that the industry had spent decades resisting.

Chapter 14: “Friends” — and Enemies

In August 1928, Henri Deterding rented Achnacarry Castle in the Scottish Highlands — a sprawling estate with fifty thousand acres of shooting grounds. Walter Teagle of Jersey Standard, Sir John Cadman of Anglo-Persian, and other industry chieftains arrived for what was officially a grouse-hunting holiday. “It was lousy,” Teagle said of the shooting, but they were not there for grouse. Behind closed doors, the most powerful men in the global oil business hammered out the “As-Is” Agreement, which assigned each company a market quota based on its 1928 share. The British government had quietly encouraged Anglo-Persian to seek such arrangements, reasoning that managed competition would serve consumers better than cutthroat price wars in the long run.

The agreement was elegant in theory but brittle in practice. “Fringe” producers — independents, newcomers, and state-backed companies — refused to respect quotas they had never agreed to. Uncontrollable production from new discoveries constantly upset the carefully calibrated shares. The companies referred to each other as “friends” in their correspondence, using the word with heavy irony that barely concealed deep mutual distrust. “Friends in London say...” became a standard opening for messages that often carried threats. The domestic American market was explicitly excluded from the arrangement to avoid antitrust prosecution. By September 1939, the “As-Is” had effectively collapsed under its own contradictions.

Throughout the 1930s, European governments imposed an increasingly suffocating web of nationalist regulations on the international oil companies. Import quotas, price controls, restrictions on foreign exchange, forced blending of domestically produced alcohol into motor fuel, blocked profit remittances, and compulsory participation in national cartels became the norm across the continent. In France, the government allocated specific market shares to each company by decree. In Nazi Germany, regulations multiplied as the regime geared for war. A Jersey Standard executive warned his colleagues that “failure to cooperate with a national commercial endeavor invariably provoked retaliatory legislation which was more costly.” An industry observer captured the reality with precision: operations in the European oil business were “ninety percent political and ten percent oil.”

In Persia, Reza Shah — a military strongman who had once personally beaten an ayatollah — abruptly canceled Anglo-Persian’s concession on November 16, 1932. The Shah was furious at declining royalty payments during the Depression and contemptuous of what he saw as colonial exploitation. Sir John Cadman flew to Tehran and instructed his pilot to taxi the aircraft conspicuously past the palace windows, a theatrical display of British reach and technological superiority. The gambit worked. The Shah retreated from outright cancellation and accepted a renegotiated deal: the concession area was reduced by three-quarters, but the term was extended to 1993, with a guaranteed minimum payment of 750,000 pounds per year.

The most dramatic confrontation between nationalism and the international oil order occurred in Mexico. General Lazaro Cardenas — a man with “the long, mask-like face and inscrutable, obsidian eyes of the Indian,” the son of an herbalist who became a general by twenty-five — viewed the foreign oil presence as a painful wound on Mexican sovereignty. Deterding proved incapable of reading the political moment, treating Mexico as “a Colonial Government to which you simply dictated orders.” Bolivia’s confiscation of Standard Oil’s subsidiary in 1937 to great popular applause served as both precedent and inspiration. When Mexico’s oil workers struck, a government review commission described company officials as “men without respect unaccustomed to speaking the truth.”

On March 18, 1938, at 9:45 PM, Cardenas signed the expropriation order from the Yellow Room of the presidential palace and broadcast the news to the nation. A six-hour parade surged through Mexico City in celebration. Petroleos Mexicanos (Pemex) was established as the national oil company. The valuations told the story of the gulf between the two sides: Mexico assessed the properties at seven million dollars; the companies claimed four hundred and eight million. A creative commission ultimately determined that ninety percent of reserves had already been produced, arriving at a settlement of thirty million dollars. Shell’s British stockholders eventually received 130 million dollars.

The aftermath of Mexican expropriation sent tremors through the global industry. Nazi Germany eagerly became Mexico’s number-one petroleum customer, and Japanese companies explored for Mexican crude. The international majors called the episode the biggest trauma since the Bolshevik Revolution had stripped the Nobels and Rothschilds of their Russian properties. But the deeper significance was structural: Mexico had established a model that dozens of countries would eventually follow. Pemex became one of the first important state-owned oil companies in the world, proving that a developing nation could seize control of its petroleum resources and survive the international backlash.

The decade between the Achnacarry Agreement and the outbreak of World War II thus witnessed the simultaneous failure of private cooperation and the rise of state power over oil. The “friends” could not maintain their cartel against competitive pressures and nationalist ambitions. European governments treated oil as an instrument of state policy. Reza Shah and Cardenas demonstrated that concession agreements written in an earlier era of colonial confidence could be torn up when the balance of power shifted. The international petroleum order that had been built by Rockefeller, Deterding, and their contemporaries was cracking at every joint, and the approaching war would shatter it entirely.

Chapter 15: The Arabian Concessions

The man who first grasped the petroleum potential of the Arabian Peninsula was not a geologist but a New Zealand-born mining engineer and compulsive promoter named Major Frank Holmes. On a beef-buying trip to Ethiopia in 1918, Holmes first heard stories of oil seepages along the Arabian coast. He pursued the leads with bulldog tenacity, winning concessions in Bahrain and Saudi Arabia while the established companies dismissed the entire region. Anglo-Persian’s directors declared Arabia “devoid of all prospects” — they were focused on Albania. Holmes earned the Arabic nickname “Abu al-Naft” — Father of Oil — but in London, people “ran when they saw him coming,” weary of his relentless salesmanship.

Holmes brought the Bahrain concession to Gulf Oil, but Gulf had joined the Turkish Petroleum Company and was therefore bound by the Red Line Agreement, which prohibited independent operations within the old Ottoman boundaries. Gulf passed the concession to Standard of California (Socal), which stood outside the Red Line. Both Socal in Bahrain and Gulf in Kuwait then confronted British opposition in the form of a “nationality clause” that excluded American companies. Prolonged diplomatic pressure from Washington eventually persuaded Britain to relent, on the pragmatic calculation that American capital would encourage rapid development and reduce the need for British subsidies to impoverished local rulers.

On May 31, 1932, oil was struck in Bahrain — the first petroleum discovery in the Arab world. Holmes’s instincts were spectacularly vindicated. The geological implications were enormous: Bahrain sat only twenty miles from the Arabian mainland, where the underlying rock formations were identical. If oil existed in commercial quantities on a tiny island, the vast peninsula beyond almost certainly held far greater reserves. The discovery transformed the competitive dynamics of the Middle East and triggered a scramble for Arabian concessions that would reshape the global balance of power for the rest of the twentieth century.

The key intermediary in Saudi Arabia was Jack Philby — an eccentric British Arabist, convert to Islam, and father of the future double agent Kim Philby. He told Ibn Saud that the King was “like folk asleep on buried treasure.” Ibn Saud replied with characteristic directness: “Oh, Philby, if anyone would offer me a million pounds, I would give him all the concessions he wanted.” Philby served simultaneously as Socal’s paid agent, the King’s adviser, and a confidant of the Iraq Petroleum Company — a triple role that would be scandalous by modern standards. His thousand-dollar monthly retainer from Socal funded young Kim’s fees at Cambridge, “where Kim was taking the first steps on the way to becoming a Soviet spy.”

Socal won the Saudi concession on May 29, 1933, paying thirty-five thousand pounds in gold sovereigns. Ibn Saud specified that all coins must bear male monarchs — no Queen Victoria. Five agonizing years of dry holes followed, draining confidence and budgets. Then, in March 1938, Well Number 7 struck oil at 4,727 feet. In April 1939, Ibn Saud himself turned the valve to load the first tanker cargo of Saudi crude. The ceremony marked the beginning of a relationship between American oil companies and the Saudi monarchy that would become one of the most consequential partnerships of the modern era. Neither side yet understood the scale of what lay beneath the desert.

In Kuwait, Anglo-Persian and Gulf formed a fifty-fifty joint venture called the Kuwait Oil Company. On February 23, 1938, they struck petroleum at the Burgan field, where the crude ignited sand on contact, transforming the walls of the wellbore into sheets of glass. Kuwait’s economy had been devastated by the collapse of its traditional pearl-diving industry after Mikimoto perfected the Japanese cultured pearl. Oil would replace pearls as the foundation of Kuwaiti wealth, and Burgan would prove to be one of the largest fields on earth. The Sheikh who signed the concession could not have imagined the transformation his tiny emirate would undergo within a single generation.

When people asked Holmes what had given him such certainty about Arabian oil, he tapped his nose and declared: “This was my geologist.” The line captured something essential about the early days of petroleum exploration, when instinct and persistence mattered as much as science. The professional geologists at Anglo-Persian had dismissed Arabia; the amateur with a good nose had been right. Holmes never received the fortune his discoveries warranted — he was a promoter, not an operator — but his legacy was the opening of the most prolific oil province in human history.

The Arabian concessions of the 1930s redrew the map of global petroleum. Within a decade, Bahrain, Saudi Arabia, and Kuwait had all revealed commercial deposits that dwarfed many established producing regions. The center of gravity in the oil world, which had rested in the United States since Drake’s well, began its slow migration toward the Persian Gulf. The concessions also established the template for relationships between Western oil companies and Middle Eastern rulers — a template built on gold payments, royalty percentages, and exclusive territorial rights that would endure until the nationalist revolutions of the 1950s and 1960s tore it apart.

Chapter 16: Japan’s Road to War

On September 18, 1931, officers of the Japanese Army staged a bomb attack on the South Manchurian Railway. The damage was laughably slight — thirty-one inches of track, so negligible that a train passed over the spot minutes later. But the manufactured incident gave the military its pretext to seize all of Manchuria, which propagandists called “Japan’s life line.” Prime Minister Hamaguchi, who had favored naval cooperation with the West, was shot at a railway station by an ultranationalist youth. “Government by assassination” took hold in Tokyo, as military factions eliminated civilian politicians who stood in the way of imperial expansion. The Manchurian Affair signaled that Japan’s trajectory toward confrontation with the Western powers had begun.

Japan’s fundamental vulnerability was oil. The home islands produced only seven percent of consumed petroleum, with eighty percent imported from the United States and ten percent from the Dutch East Indies. The Army and Navy split over strategy: the Army focused northward toward Manchuria and the Soviet threat, while the Navy championed hokushu nanshin — “defend in the north, advance to the south” — toward the oil-rich East Indies. Foreign companies, principally Shell’s Rising Sun subsidiary and Standard-Vacuum, controlled sixty percent of the domestic market. The 1934 Petroleum Industry Law required six months’ inventories and aimed to reduce foreign companies’ role. Every strategic calculation in Tokyo ultimately circled back to the same inescapable fact: without imported oil, Japan could not fight.

Cordell Hull, the Tennessee backwoodsman turned Secretary of State, held perhaps forty or fifty secret nighttime meetings with Japanese Ambassador Nomura at the Wardman Park Hotel in Washington. Hull possessed a startling advantage: American codebreakers had cracked the Japanese diplomatic cipher, codenamed “Purple,” and the intelligence derived from it was called “Magic.” Hull could read Tokyo’s instructions to Nomura before the ambassador himself received them. In May 1941, the Germans informed Tokyo that the Americans had broken their codes. The Japanese simply did not believe it, convinced that Americans were not capable of such a feat. This astonishing arrogance would cost them dearly throughout the war.

Admiral Isoroku Yamamoto — who had grown up in oil-producing Niigata prefecture, spent four years studying in the United States, read Lincoln biographies, and regularly received Life magazine — understood American industrial power better than any officer in Japan. He restricted Navy training to coastal waters to conserve precious petroleum. He even sponsored a “scientist” who claimed to be able to convert water into oil, so desperate was the fuel situation. Yamamoto scoffed at the ultranationalists’ “armchair arguments about war.” Yet when the decision was made, he accepted it with fatalistic resolve: “It’s out of the question! To fight the United States is like fighting the whole world. But it has been decided. So I will do my best. Doubtless I will die.”

Between June 25 and July 2, 1941, Tokyo made the fateful choice: postpone action against the Soviet Union and concentrate on the southern strategy — Indochina first, then the East Indies. Japanese leaders knew this risked an American embargo, which they described as “a matter of life or death to the empire.” Through Magic intercepts, Washington read Tokyo’s internal debate in nearly real time. “After the occupation of French Indochina, next on our schedule is the Netherlands East Indies.” Roosevelt wanted to use the threat of embargo as a flexible diplomatic tool, not a blunt instrument. But Dean Acheson at the State Department turned the July 25 asset freeze into a de facto total oil embargo by blocking the release of frozen funds. “Whether or not we had a policy, we had a state of affairs.”

Roosevelt had understood the danger of a total embargo with remarkable clarity. He told Ickes, who had pushed for cutting off Japan entirely: “Please let me know if this would continue to be your judgment if this were to tip the delicate scales and cause Japan to attack the Dutch East Indies.” He added with characteristic bluntness: “I simply have not got enough Navy to go around.” But the bureaucratic machinery had overtaken presidential intent. Two Japanese tankers sat empty in San Pedro harbor, unable to load. Japan’s oil inventories would last no more than two years. The clock was now ticking toward a decision that could not be reversed.

On September 5, Japan’s most senior officials met with Emperor Hirohito. “Vital military supplies, including oil, are dwindling day by day,” they reported. The Emperor asked how long hostilities would last. The Army Chief estimated three months. Hirohito retorted sharply — the China Incident had been promised in one month and lasted four years. “The Pacific was boundless.” Then the Emperor read a poem by his grandfather, Emperor Meiji: “Since all are brothers in this world, why is there such constant turmoil?” The hall fell silent. Prince Konoye proposed a summit with Roosevelt, willing to jettison the Axis alliance entirely. Roosevelt suggested Juneau, Alaska, but Hull opposed the meeting. Konoye fell from power, replaced by General Tojo.

“Two years from now we will have no petroleum for military use. Ships will stop moving. I fear that we would become a third-class nation.” — General Hideki Tojo, October 1941

On November 22, a Magic intercept revealed that American agreement must be received by November 29 — “after that, things are automatically going to happen.” Four days later, a Japanese naval task force sailed from the Kurile Islands under strict radio silence. Hull told Secretary of War Stimson: “I have washed my hands of it.” Washington dispatched a final alert to Pacific commanders: “This dispatch is to be considered a war warning.” On December 7, 1941, bombs fell on Pearl Harbor at 7:55 AM. Eight battleships were sunk, capsized, or damaged. 2,335 servicemen were killed. But Admiral Nagumo declined a third attack wave that would have targeted the oil storage tanks. Admiral Nimitz later observed that had the Japanese destroyed the 4.5 million barrels of vulnerable fuel, “it would have prolonged the war another two years.” Oil drove Japan to war — yet they forgot about oil when planning the attack itself.

Chapter 17: Germany’s Formula for War

In June 1932, officials from I.G. Farben — the German chemical colossus — met with Adolf Hitler, who spent two and a half hours lecturing them on the centrality of motorization to Germany’s future. “German motor fuel must become a reality, even if this entails sacrifices,” he declared. I.G. Farben had developed the hydrogenation process for converting coal into liquid fuel, and Hitler saw in it the key to freeing Germany from dependence on imported petroleum. The company promised campaign contributions in exchange for guaranteed markets. Standard Oil’s Frank Howard had visited the Leuna synthetic fuel plant in 1926 and called it “the most important development which has ever faced the company since the dissolution.” Standard received patent rights while I.G. Farben got 546,000 Standard shares worth thirty-five million dollars.

By 1940, Germany’s synthetic fuel output reached 72,000 barrels per day — forty-six percent of total supply and an astonishing ninety-five percent of aviation gasoline. Hitler’s Four-Year Plan ordered a sixfold expansion of synthetic capacity, declaring that costs were “of no importance.” The strategic calculus was straightforward: without domestic fuel production, Germany could not wage modern mechanized war. Mussolini privately confided to Hitler that an effective oil embargo would have forced him to withdraw from Abyssinia “within a week.” Both dictators understood that petroleum was the essential ingredient of military power, and both built their war machines on the assumption that they could secure sufficient supplies through conquest, synthesis, or both.

The moral dimension of Germany’s synthetic fuel program reached its nadir at Auschwitz, where I.G. Farben built production facilities adjacent to the death camp. The company paid the SS three to four marks per day for each slave laborer, with children available at half price. Workers subsisted on a thousand calories per day, labored for months until their bodies gave out, and then were killed or left to die. Primo Levi, Prisoner 174,517, described the Buna synthetic rubber plant in terms that captured the horror: “The only thing alive are machines and slaves — and the former are more alive than the latter.” The plants consumed more electricity than all of Berlin. By 1944, a third of the synthetic fuel workforce consisted of slave labor.

Many factors drove Hitler’s decision to invade the Soviet Union: ideological hatred of Bolshevism, racial contempt for Slavic peoples, the drive for lebensraum. But oil was central to the calculation. “In the economic field, Hitler’s obsession was oil,” observed one analyst. Albert Speer confirmed at his postwar interrogation that “the need for oil certainly was a prime motive.” Romania’s Ploesti fields provided fifty-eight percent of Germany’s oil imports in 1940, and Soviet troops had moved dangerously close after seizing northeastern Romania. Directive Number 21 — Operation Barbarossa — launched three million men, 600,000 motor vehicles, and 625,000 horses eastward on June 22, 1941. Stalin had refused all warnings, ordering one defector shot as a provocation.

The invasion consumed fuel at twice the anticipated rate as vehicles churned through roads that turned to mud. In August, Hitler overruled his generals, who wanted to drive on Moscow, and redirected forces southward toward the Caucasus oil fields. “My generals know nothing about the economic aspects of war,” he snapped. The Wehrmacht had to reach Baku. “The Volga will be our Mississippi,” Hitler declared, imagining a continental empire sustained by captured petroleum. German forces reached the outskirts of Moscow but bogged down in the savage winter. Japan’s decision to strike south rather than north freed Stalin to transfer crack Siberian divisions westward — troops accustomed to fighting in precisely the conditions that paralyzed the Wehrmacht.

In 1942, Operation Blau targeted the Caucasus oil fields directly. A Technical Oil Brigade of 15,000 men was assembled to rehabilitate captured production facilities. At Maikop, the retreating Russians destroyed everything so thoroughly that the Germans managed to extract only seventy barrels per day from fields that had produced thousands. Mountain troops planted the swastika on Mount Elbrus but could not reach Baku. The supreme irony of the campaign was inescapable: the Germans ran short of oil in their quest for oil. Russian tanks operated on diesel fuel, which was useless to German panzers requiring gasoline. In desperation, Wehrmacht units resorted to transporting fuel on the backs of camels.

Stalingrad was originally meant as a sideshow to protect the flank of the Caucasus drive, but its name made it pregnant with symbolism for both dictators. General Guderian wrote that “the shortage of fuel was unbearable.” Hitler told Field Marshal Manstein in a midnight telephone call: “Unless we get the Baku oil, the war is lost.” He then descended into fantasy, describing German and Japanese armies meeting in the Middle East and marching together to India. The reality was more prosaic: the Sixth Army’s tanks had fuel for twenty miles but needed thirty to break out of the Soviet encirclement. They surrendered in February 1943 — Germany’s first catastrophic defeat and the turning point of the European war.

In North Africa, Field Marshal Erwin Rommel waged a brilliant campaign of mobile warfare while perpetually starving for fuel. At one point, eighty-five percent of his transport consisted of captured British and American vehicles. His forces raced three hundred miles in a single week, reaching El Alamein just sixty miles from Alexandria. Mussolini flew over with a white charger for his triumphant entry into Cairo. Rommel dreamed of driving through Palestine and Iraq to link up with German forces at Baku. But the very distances that made his advances spectacular made his supply lines vulnerable. Malta-based Allied aircraft attacked Axis shipping relentlessly. Supply trucks from Tripoli consumed more gasoline traveling to the front and back than they carried as cargo.

“My moves would be decided more by the petrol gauge than by tactical requirements.” — Field Marshal Erwin Rommel

Bernard Montgomery — austere, ascetic, given to announcing “I assure you I am quite sane” — hung a drawing of Rommel in his command trailer to study his adversary. At the Battle of Alam Haifa in September 1942, the Afrika Korps was stopped dead by fuel shortage when promised petroleum shipments from Italy never arrived. Montgomery’s counterattack at El Alamein in October caught the Germans when four fuel ships were sunk at Tobruk. Rommel lay awake all night. His Christmas present that year was a tin of captured coffee packed in a miniature oil barrel — a mordant joke about the commodity that had defined and doomed his campaign. Forced to take poison by the SS in October 1944, Rommel left behind an epitaph that every quartermaster would endorse: “The battle is fought and decided by the Quartermasters before the shooting begins.”

The final chapter of Germany’s oil war was written from the air. On May 12, 1944, 935 Allied bombers struck synthetic fuel plants across the Reich. Speer recognized the catastrophe immediately: “On that day the technological war was decided.” By September 1944, aviation gasoline production had fallen to six percent of earlier levels. Jet fighters — Germany’s last technological advantage — sat on runways without fuel. Pilot training was cut to one hour per week. In the Battle of the Bulge, Colonel Peiper’s spearhead came within a thousand feet of 2.5 million gallons of American fuel at Stavelot but did not know it was there and turned away. German trucks were dragged by oxen. When Hitler’s body was burned in the garden of the Reich Chancellery, it was doused in the last available gasoline — a final, grotesque symmetry.

Chapter 18: Japan’s Achilles’ Heel

As Japanese forces swept across Southeast Asia in early 1942, Shell’s men at Balikpapan in Borneo carried out a devastating act of industrial sabotage. They destroyed four decades of petroleum infrastructure in less than a single day — the tank farm became a hellish conflagration with flames leaping 150 feet into the tropical sky. Of seventy-five Shell employees stranded by the Japanese advance, only thirty-five survived. The destruction was meant to deny the conquerors the prize they had come for, but the Japanese proved far more resourceful at rehabilitation than anyone expected. By 1943, output from the captured East Indies fields reached 49.6 million barrels, seventy-five percent of the 1940 level. Using Caltex’s abandoned drilling rig, they even discovered a super giant field at Minas, Sumatra.

By mid-March 1942, Japan controlled the oil-rich East Indies that had been the strategic objective of the entire southern advance. General Tojo bragged that Hong Kong had fallen in eighteen days and Singapore in seventy. A “victory fever” gripped Tokyo. Japan now had access to the petroleum reserves it needed to sustain its war machine. But possession of oil fields meant nothing without the ability to transport crude to refineries and finished products to combat forces. The distance between the East Indies and the Japanese home islands was vast, and every barrel had to travel by sea through waters that were about to become the most dangerous shipping lanes on earth.

The fatal weakness was shipping, and the instrument of destruction was the American submarine fleet. Japanese planners had dismissed American submariners as “too soft and luxury-loving” to wage effective undersea warfare. They could not have been more wrong. American submarines proved the most effective in the war, sinking eighty-six percent of Japan’s merchant shipping. Less than two percent of United States naval personnel — the submariners — inflicted fifty-five percent of total Japanese maritime losses. Japanese sailors came to expect death: “Towards the end we were fairly certain a tanker would be sunk shortly after departing from port.” Oil imports peaked in early 1943 and by early 1945 had ceased entirely.

The collapse of oil supply cascaded through every aspect of Japanese life. Civilian gasoline consumption in 1944 fell to four percent of 1940 levels. Vehicles were converted to run on charcoal burners or wood gas. Potatoes, sugar, rice wine, and sake were requisitioned for conversion to alcohol fuel. The home front experienced petroleum deprivation on a scale unimaginable in any Western combatant nation. Every drop of remaining fuel was diverted to the military, and even that was insufficient. The Japanese war machine was slowly strangling as its petroleum lifeline was severed tanker by tanker in the dark waters of the Pacific.

The oil shortage warped Japanese military tactics in ways that proved catastrophic. In the Marianas campaign of June 1944, the carrier force approached directly rather than taking a circuitous route because fuel conservation was deemed more important than tactical surprise. The result was the “Great Marianas Turkey Shoot” — 273 Japanese aircraft destroyed against just 29 American losses. The fuel crisis also forced the fleet to split its basing between Japan and Singapore, dividing strength at the worst possible moment. Pilot training was slashed to thirty hours, then navigation was eliminated entirely — “pilots were simply to follow their leaders.” Aviation gasoline was manufactured from wood turpentine mixed with alcohol. Japan lost forty percent of its aircraft on ferrying operations alone, before they ever reached combat.

Japan’s attempt to produce synthetic fuel domestically failed miserably, achieving only eight percent of its fourteen-million-barrel target. At the Battle of Leyte Gulf in October 1944 — the largest naval engagement in history — Admiral Kurita’s force came within forty miles of MacArthur’s invasion beaches but pulled away because of fuel shortage, missing what may have been the last chance to inflict a devastating blow on American naval power. The battle also introduced the kamikaze — “divine wind” — as a deliberate tactical innovation. One to three suicide planes could achieve what eight bombers and sixteen fighters otherwise required, and fuel consumption was cut in half. The calculus was grimly rational: when petroleum was scarcer than human lives, the exchange made military sense.

The desperation reached its absurd extreme in the pine root campaign, which ordered civilians to harvest tree roots for fuel distillation. “Two hundred pine roots will keep a plane in the air for an hour,” proclaimed the propaganda. Each gallon required 2.5 man-days of backbreaking labor. Only three thousand barrels of pine root gasoline were ever produced — and none of it was ever actually tested in an aircraft. The campaign diverted labor from food production at a time when the population was already malnourished. Meanwhile, the world’s largest battleship, the Yamato, was dispatched on a suicide mission to Okinawa with fuel for a one-way trip. Three hundred American planes sank her on April 7, 1945. Navy fuel oil stood at 0.8 million barrels — below a million, the Navy could not operate.

Japan’s fuel oil inventories told the entire story of the war in a single statistic: 29.6 million barrels in April 1937; 0.8 million by July 1945. Even after Hiroshima on August 6 and Nagasaki on August 9, the Army Chief insisted that suicide was the only acceptable way out. Admiral Onishi advocated twenty million Japanese sacrificing themselves in suicide attacks. Insurgent soldiers tried to capture the Emperor’s surrender recording the night of August 14 but failed. When Tojo attempted suicide and needed medical attention, finding an ambulance with gasoline proved harder than finding a doctor. MacArthur’s motorcade from the airfield to Yokohama — powered by charcoal — repeatedly broke down over twenty miles. Americans discovered 316,000 barrels of aviation fuel hidden in caves for kamikaze missions. Pine root gasoline was tested in American jeeps: it gummed up engines beyond repair.

Chapter 19: The Allies’ War

In Britain, the oil industry was effectively nationalized for the duration of the war. Companies merged their operations into the Petroleum Board. Pumps were painted dark green, and all products were sold under the generic label “Pool.” Rationing was imposed immediately. Henri Deterding had died in Germany in 1939, having married his German secretary and embraced Nazism in his final years. Shell’s board moved swiftly to distribute his preference shares before German authorities could seize them. Plans were drawn up to destroy all British oil stocks in the event of a German invasion. The oil industry that had been built on competitive commerce was reorganized as an arm of the state, subordinated entirely to the single imperative of survival.

In the United States, Harold Ickes became Petroleum Coordinator, a role that made him the de facto oil czar of the democratic world. The Oil Weekly warned against entrusting such power to “a man who has no qualities nor specific traits of ability” for the role. But Ickes proved pragmatic, choosing oilman Ralph Davies as his deputy and working constructively with the industry he had spent a decade antagonizing. The crucial advantage America possessed was one million barrels per day of surplus production capacity — a direct result of the prorationing system established in the 1930s. This margin proved to be an invaluable strategic asset. “Without it, the course of World War II might well have been different.”

American production rose from 3.7 million barrels per day in 1940 to 4.7 million in 1945 — a thirty percent increase that drew on every barrel of the strategic reserve. The United States supplied six of the seven billion barrels consumed by the Allies during the war, equivalent to more than a quarter of all petroleum produced since Drake’s well in 1859. “Old Dr. New Deal had to call in his partner Dr. Win-the-War,” Roosevelt quipped. The Justice Department’s 1940 antitrust case against twenty-two major oil companies was quietly set aside — the government needed the industry’s cooperation far more than it needed legal victories. The wartime alliance between Washington and the oil companies would reshape their relationship for a generation.

The Battle of the Atlantic nearly severed the petroleum lifeline between America and Britain. German U-boats devastated tanker shipping — the vessels were easily identified by their distinctive low profiles. Admiral Doenitz issued his infamous order: “Rescue no one and take no one with you.” In the first three months of 1942, tanker sinkings ran at four times the rate of new construction. Miami’s neon beachfront provided perfect silhouettes for U-boat commanders — hotel owners insisted the lights stay on for tourist season. Crowds at Atlantic City watched tankers explode on the dark horizon. Churchill called it “the blackest cloud which we had to face.” Ralph Davies reported that England had only two months of Royal Navy fuel versus the seven months considered the minimum safe level.

Ickes launched a conservation campaign with windshield stickers reading “I’m using one-third less gas,” but he could not explain the real reason for the shortage — revealing U-boat intelligence would have been suicidal. Congress, kept in the dark, called it a “phony shortage.” In March 1943, 108 Allied ships were sunk, and Germany came closer to severing transatlantic communications than at any other point in the war. Then in late March, a combination of breakthroughs — cracking U-boat codes, closing Allied cipher vulnerabilities, deploying support groups with improved radar, and fielding long-range aircraft — turned the tide. In May 1943, thirty percent of U-boats at sea were destroyed. Doenitz reported “the greatest crisis in submarine warfare.” After forty-five months, the Battle of the Atlantic was over.

Two extraordinary engineering feats helped secure the petroleum supply chain. The Big Inch pipeline stretched 1,254 miles and the Little Inch 1,475 miles, carrying crude oil and refined products from Texas to the East Coast. Before the war, pipelines moved just four percent of oil to the eastern seaboard; by war’s end, they carried forty-two percent. The pipelines bypassed the U-boat menace entirely, replacing vulnerable coastal tanker routes with an overland artery that could not be torpedoed. Meanwhile, Shell researchers developed 100-octane aviation fuel that gave Allied fighters fifteen to thirty percent more power — the advantage proved decisive in the Battle of Britain, where Spitfires on 100-octane outperformed Messerschmitts running on 87-octane.

Half of all tonnage shipped from the United States to overseas theaters was petroleum. When a soldier went overseas, sixty-seven pounds of his supplies were oil products. The ubiquitous “jerrycan” — reverse-engineered from a captured German five-gallon container with an American pouring spout added — became the war’s most recognizable logistics item. PLUTO (Pipeline Under the Ocean) was designed to supply half of Allied fuel needs from England to France after D-Day, but it averaged only 150 barrels per day, one-sixth of one percent of actual consumption. Nationwide gasoline rationing in the United States came through a side door — Japan had cut off ninety percent of the natural rubber supply, and the Baruch Committee recommended rationing gasoline to conserve tires. The basic “A” sticker provided just one and a half to four gallons per week.

General George Patton’s Third Army covered five hundred miles in a single month, outrunning its supply lines in one of the most spectacular advances of the war. Patton’s troops hijacked fuel trains and commandeered supply convoys. “My chief difficulty is not the Germans but gasoline,” he declared. On August 30, his fuel supply was cut to a tenth of normal as Eisenhower directed petroleum to Montgomery’s forces instead. Patton’s tanks reached the Meuse River with their gas tanks empty. “My men can eat their belts, but my tanks have gotta have gas.” The delay allowed the Germans to regroup. Nine months of bitter warfare followed, and three-quarters of the million Allied casualties occurred after this check. Military historian Liddell Hart concluded: “The best chance of a quick finish was probably lost when the gas was turned off from Patton’s tanks.”

“This is a war of engines and octanes. I drink to the American auto industry and the American oil industry.” — Joseph Stalin, wartime toast

Stalin’s toast captured a fundamental truth. American forces consumed one hundred times more gasoline in World War II than in World War I. A typical division deployed 4,000 horsepower in the first war and 187,000 in the second. The Army-Navy Petroleum Board pronounced with justifiable pride that “at no time did the Services lack for oil in the proper quantities, in the proper kinds and at the proper places” — though Patton’s experience at the Meuse qualified that claim. World crude oil production in 1945 told the story of American dominance: the United States produced 4.7 million barrels per day out of a global total of 7.1 million. The war had been fought and won on American petroleum. That dominance, however, had reached its high-water mark.

Chapter 20: The New Center of Gravity

In 1943, the United States government dispatched Everette DeGolyer on a wartime mission to appraise the petroleum potential of the Middle East. DeGolyer was the most respected figure in petroleum engineering — discoverer of Mexico’s Golden Lane field, founder of DeGolyer and McNaughton (the world’s leading petroleum consulting firm), a millionaire several times over whose interests ranged from Texas Instruments to chili history to the Saturday Review of Literature. Getting to the Middle East required an arduous journey by military aircraft via Miami, the Caribbean, Brazil, and Africa. He wrote his wife that the Arabian peninsula was “a pretty barren land... Texas is a garden in comparison.” But what lay beneath that barren surface staggered even DeGolyer’s experienced imagination.

Three Saudi structures alone contained an estimated 750 million barrels, but similar geological formations suggested far greater reserves yet to be discovered. For DeGolyer, the Peninsula was “an El Dorado.” He reported proven reserves of approximately twenty-five billion barrels and suspected the true figure might reach 300 billion for the entire region, with 100 billion for Saudi Arabia alone. One member of his mission delivered the judgment that would echo through the rest of the century: “The oil in this region is the greatest single prize in all history.” DeGolyer’s key conclusion was stark and transformative: “The center of gravity of world oil production is shifting from the Gulf-Caribbean area to the Middle East.” The United States had produced ninety percent of Allied petroleum in the war. That was the high-water mark.

In 1940, the Middle East produced less than five percent of world oil versus sixty-three percent for the United States. Ibn Saud faced financial crisis as the war halted pilgrim traffic to Mecca and drought devastated his kingdom. The “hundred men” staffing Aramco’s Saudi operations made plans to fill wells with cement rather than let them fall to the Germans. An Arab proverb captured the relationship: “The Arabs have the religion, but the Allies have the money.” Roosevelt initially declined to extend aid to Saudi Arabia, calling it “a little far afield for us.” The British stepped in with approximately two million dollars. American oil men tried to convince Ibn Saud that British assistance was really American money — “it just came indirectly.”

The wartime anxiety over oil reserves intensified when American discovery rates fell sharply. Harold Ickes published a provocative article titled “We’re Running Out of Oil!” in December 1943, warning that “America’s crown is sliding down over one eye.” Herbert Feis at the State Department reported that “the pencil came to an awed pause at one point — the Middle East.” Americans feared that the British would “diddle” them out of the Saudi concession. In February 1943, Ickes lobbied Roosevelt directly, calling Saudi Arabia “probably the greatest and richest oil field in the world.” On February 18, Roosevelt authorized Lend-Lease aid to Ibn Saud — a presidential declaration that the defense of Saudi Arabia was vital to the defense of the United States.

Ickes’s ambitions went far beyond aid. He aimed for one hundred percent government ownership of the California Arabian Standard Oil Company (Casoc). When the company presidents visited Washington, Ickes proposed buying all of Casoc outright. “They had gone fishing for a cod and had caught a whale,” observed one participant. Ickes eventually whittled his proposal down to a one-third government share for forty million dollars. But the rest of the American oil industry erupted in what was described as “righteous and fiery indignation.” A separate plan for a 120-million-dollar government pipeline from Saudi Arabia to the Mediterranean also died, denounced as “a move towards fascism.” The industry that had fought government regulation for decades would not accept government ownership, even when national security was the justification.

Roosevelt attempted to divide the Middle Eastern petroleum spoils with Churchill through personal diplomacy. He showed British Ambassador Halifax a map with characteristic informality: “Persian oil is yours. We share Iraq and Kuwait. As for Saudi Arabian oil, it’s ours.” Churchill, alarmed by American interest in traditionally British spheres, cautioned that “a wrangle about oil would be a poor prelude for the tremendous joint enterprise” the Allies were undertaking. The Anglo-American Petroleum Agreement of August 1944 attempted to create international production quotas modeled on the Achnacarry arrangement. Both independents and majors opposed it, and it was eventually withdrawn — killed by the same domestic political forces that had defeated government ownership of Casoc.

After the Yalta Conference in February 1945, Roosevelt arranged a meeting with Ibn Saud aboard the USS Quincy in the Great Bitter Lake. The King traveled with forty-eight retainers and insisted on sleeping in a tent pitched on deck, covered with Oriental carpets. Roosevelt, chain-smoking despite his declining health, pushed the elevator’s emergency stop button to steal two cigarettes between meetings with the King, who forbade tobacco in his presence. The two men bonded instantly. Ibn Saud declared Roosevelt his “twin.” Roosevelt: “You are luckier because you can still walk.” Ibn Saud: “No, your chair will take you wherever you want to go.” Roosevelt gave the King one of his own wheelchairs — a gift that delighted Ibn Saud more than any diplomatic concession.

Churchill’s subsequent meeting with Ibn Saud was less harmonious. The Prime Minister announced that “my religion prescribed smoking cigars and drinking alcohol as an absolute sacred rite,” a declaration that did not amuse his abstemious host. Churchill gave perfumes worth one hundred pounds; the King reciprocated with diamond-hilted swords and three thousand pounds of jewels. Churchill promised a Rolls-Royce. Roosevelt died on April 12, 1945, before resolving the great questions of postwar oil policy. Ickes resigned in a bitter clash with Truman, who accepted his departure “tersely and with alacrity and delight.” By 1947, the Anglo-American Petroleum Agreement was dead. What survived was the special relationship between the United States and Saudi Arabia — “an unlikely union: Bedouin Arabs and Texas oil men, a traditional Islamic autocracy allied with modern American capitalism.”

Chapter 21: The Postwar Petroleum Order

Gasoline rationing was lifted within twenty-four hours of Japan’s capitulation. “Fill ’er up!” became the national cry. By 1950, forty million automobiles were registered in the United States, up from twenty-six million before the war. Gasoline sales surged forty-two percent. Oil was meeting more of the nation’s energy needs than coal for the first time in history. The pessimistic wartime predictions about exhaustion were spectacularly wrong: proven American reserves stood twenty-one percent higher in 1950 than they had in 1946. The postwar boom unleashed consumer demand on a scale that made even the roaring twenties look modest, and petroleum was both the fuel and the symbol of the new American prosperity.

The transition from wartime to peacetime was not entirely smooth. A genuine shortage of available oil developed in 1947-48 as crude prices more than doubled. More than twenty Congressional investigations were launched. Refineries needed redesigning for civilian products after years of producing aviation fuel and military-grade lubricants. Steel was in short supply worldwide, constraining the construction of new production and refining capacity. Tankers broke in half at sea due to wartime construction shortcuts. Oil companies, remarkably, became advocates of conservation — Standard of Indiana urged motorists to avoid “jackrabbit” starts. In 1948, American imports exceeded exports for the first time. The United States was now dependent on other countries for the marginal barrel of oil.

Aramco needed both capital and markets on a scale that its original owners, Socal and Texaco, could not provide alone. Socal had invested eighty million dollars; the Tapline pipeline from Saudi Arabia to the Mediterranean would require another hundred million. Saudi Arabia was barely two decades old as a unified kingdom, and Ibn Saud, now in his mid-sixties with forty-five sons, was spending lavishly. Socal’s MacPherson called the concession “a gold mine,” but Chairman Collier corrected him: there was “much more gold” to be extracted through Jersey Standard’s global marketing system. Socal and Texaco sold forty percent of Aramco to Jersey Standard and Socony-Vacuum for approximately 470 million dollars — one of the largest corporate transactions of the era.

The Aramco deal required Jersey Standard and Socony to break free of the Red Line Agreement that had bound Turkish Petroleum Company partners since 1928. They invoked “supervening illegality” — arguing that the wartime occupation of France had dissolved the agreement. Calouste Gulbenkian, now in his late seventies and holding court at the Hotel Aviz in Lisbon, refused to acquiesce. He had become a tourist attraction himself, carefully checking his car’s odometer readings against known distances. His negotiating tactic was simple: “make demands step by step.” He called the Iraq Petroleum Company “as architecturally designed as Raphael’s School of Athens.” At the final signing in November 1948, he found “one more point” to raise. Only one bottle of champagne was consumed among twelve negotiators. Gulbenkian signed at 1:30 AM. “Mr. Five Percent was no more.”

The Aramco ownership was finalized in December 1948: Jersey Standard took thirty percent and Socony ten. Socony’s decision to limit itself to a ten percent share was a strategic miscalculation of historic proportions — “it would not be very long before Socony came to regret its parsimony.” Meanwhile, Gulf Oil found a partner in Shell for the “shadow integration” of its massive Kuwaiti production. Stalin made clear that Soviet interests in Iranian oil would not be surrendered: “We’re not going to risk our oil supply.” The great postwar oil deals were reshaping the structure of the industry, concentrating control of Middle Eastern production in the hands of a small number of American and British companies.

Europe’s desperate need for petroleum gave Middle Eastern oil its first great market. The winter of 1946-47 was catastrophic: the Thames froze, British industry shut down for three weeks, and the continent faced economic collapse. Twenty percent of Marshall Plan aid went to petroleum purchases. “The Englishman began to realize there was no value in being cold,” observed one official. The Marshall Plan effectively subsidized Europe’s conversion from coal to oil, creating enormous demand for the cheap crude flowing from the Persian Gulf. The Tapline was completed in September 1950 — 1,040 miles of pipeline that cut the shipping distance from 7,200 miles via the Suez Canal. Imported petroleum killed the synthetic fuel programs that had sustained Germany and Japan during the war. “And they would remain dead for three decades.”

The postwar petroleum order that crystallized by the late 1940s rested on several pillars: American surplus capacity providing a strategic cushion, the major international companies controlling production and pricing through interlocking ownership of Middle Eastern concessions, cheap crude from the Persian Gulf displacing coal across Europe and Asia, and the United States government providing diplomatic and military security for the producing regions. It was an order built by a handful of oilmen and diplomats, largely without public debate or legislative authorization. It delivered cheap energy that fueled the greatest economic boom in human history. And it contained within itself the seeds of its own destruction.

The transformation in global energy markets was breathtaking in its speed and scale. In barely a decade, the world moved from wartime scarcity and rationing to abundance that seemed limitless. The United States, which had provided the petroleum that won the war, was now importing oil for the first time. The Middle East, which had been a marginal producer before the war, was emerging as the dominant source of global supply. The companies that controlled this supply — Jersey Standard, Socony, Socal, Texaco, Gulf, Shell, and Anglo-Iranian — wielded power that rivaled sovereign governments. They would soon discover that the governments of the countries where the oil lay buried intended to claim a larger share of the prize.

Chapter 22: Fifty-Fifty

The economist David Ricardo had long ago explained the concept of rent: the difference between the market price and the cost of production. In the Middle East, that gap was staggering — production costs of approximately twenty-five cents per barrel against a selling price of $2.50. The rents flowing to the oil companies were enormous, and the producing countries received only a fraction. As one observer noted, “a rich discovery means a dissatisfied landlord.” For the governments of Venezuela, Saudi Arabia, Iran, Iraq, and Kuwait, oil represented far more than revenue — it meant “power, influence, status — all previously lacking.” The fight over how to divide the rents from this extraordinary resource would dominate the next quarter century of international politics.

Venezuela led the way. After the death of the dictator Gomez in 1935, a new generation of reformers — the “Generation of ’28” — modernized the country’s oil regulation. Juan Pablo Perez Alfonzo, the most important of these reformers, discovered that the actual split of petroleum profits was sixty-forty in the companies’ favor, not the fifty-fifty that had been advertised. He corrected the imbalance and insisted on receiving royalties in kind — actual barrels of oil rather than cash payments calculated by the companies. “The veil of mystery over oil marketing was removed forever,” he declared. Venezuela’s fifty-fifty principle would become the template for producing countries around the world.

The Neutral Zone between Saudi Arabia and Kuwait became the unlikely venue where the postwar petroleum order first began to erode. Aminoil won Kuwait’s share of the zone with a package that included $7.5 million in cash and a million-dollar yacht for the Sheikh. The Saudi share attracted J. Paul Getty, a man “obsessed with value — bought it and never sold it.” Getty had married five times, fired all his employees during the Depression and rehired them at lower salaries, bought the Pierre Hotel in New York at a quarter of its construction cost, and entered a Rothschild home after the Nazi seizure of Austria to inspect the furniture. He installed a pay telephone for guests at his English manor. He was, in short, the most parsimonious billionaire in history.

Getty won the Saudi Neutral Zone concession with an offer of $9.5 million up front and fifty-five cents per barrel — terms that the established majors considered suicidal. His geologist, Paul Walton, spotted a promising mound from a DC-3 window that resembled Kuwait’s Burgan field. Five dry holes followed while Getty wandered Europe researching a Rembrandt painting and recording expenses in his diary down to “newspaper — 10 centimes.” The sixth well struck oil of a magnitude described as “somewhere between colossal and history-making.” Fortune magazine declared Getty the richest man in America. His response was characteristic: “If you can count your money, you don’t have a billion dollars.” Walton, who had found the oil, received $1,200.

A Venezuelan delegation traveled to the Middle East to spread the fifty-fifty gospel, translating key documents into Arabic. Romulo Betancourt had a strategic motive beyond solidarity: “Low-cost Middle East oil was a grave threat to Venezuela.” If Middle Eastern governments claimed a larger share of profits through taxation, the cost of their oil would rise, protecting Venezuela’s competitive position. The Saudis were receptive. They had discovered that the United States Treasury collected more in taxes from Aramco — forty-three million dollars — than Saudi Arabia received in royalties, which totaled only thirty-nine million. The King’s government was earning less from Saudi oil than a foreign government five thousand miles away.

The solution was elegant and revolutionary: the foreign tax credit. Saudi taxes paid by Aramco would be deductible dollar-for-dollar from the company’s American tax bill. On December 30, 1950, the fifty-fifty agreement was signed. The results were immediate and dramatic: by 1951, Saudi Arabia received 110 million dollars while the United States Treasury collected just six million. The American taxpayer was effectively subsidizing the Saudi monarchy, though few Americans understood the mechanism. Jersey Standard’s leadership defended the arrangement: “Fifty-fifty is a good position which needs no defense.” Gulbenkian, watching from Lisbon with the shrewdness of seven decades in the oil business, offered a darker prophecy: “I fear the wind of nationalization may spread.”

The fifty-fifty principle spread with remarkable speed across the Middle East. Kuwait, Iraq, and Iran all demanded equivalent terms. The logic was irresistible: if Saudi Arabia received half the profits, no neighboring government could accept less without facing domestic upheaval. The companies acquiesced because the foreign tax credit meant the cost fell primarily on the United States Treasury rather than on their own profits. The arrangement was a masterpiece of indirect subsidy — Washington gained political stability in the Middle East, the companies maintained their concessions, and the producing countries received dramatically increased revenues. The only losers were American taxpayers, who were never consulted.

Yet fifty-fifty contained a fatal contradiction. By establishing the principle that producing countries were entitled to half the profits, the oil companies had conceded that the resource belonged to the nation, not to the concessionaire. It was only a matter of time before governments asked why they should settle for half when they could have all. The Neutral Zone concessions had already shown that newcomers would offer better terms. Getty’s fifty-five cents per barrel made Aramco’s royalty structure look exploitative. Every new deal ratcheted the baseline upward. The postwar petroleum order, built on the assumption that a small group of international companies could manage global oil in stable partnership with producing governments, had begun its slow unraveling.

Chapter 23: “Old Mossy” and the Struggle for Iran

Mohammed Reza Pahlavi, the young Shah of Iran, inherited a country beset by fundamentalists, communists, and grinding poverty. His position was precarious, his authority fragile. The source of national resentment was Anglo-Iranian Oil Company, which between 1945 and 1950 had earned 250 million pounds in profit while paying Iran just ninety million. The company’s chairman, Sir William Fraser — dismissed by critics as an “obstinate, narrow old skinflint” — resisted every proposal for reform until the Saudi fifty-fifty deal made Anglo-Iranian’s terms look colonial by comparison. By the time Fraser was prepared to offer better conditions, it was far too late. The grievance had metastasized from a financial dispute into a movement for national liberation.

Mohammed Mossadegh rode the wave of anti-British fury to power. “The source of all misfortunes is only the oil company,” he declared, reducing Iran’s complex problems to a single enemy. Prime Minister Razmara, who had cautioned against nationalization, was assassinated entering a mosque. The Majlis voted to nationalize Anglo-Iranian’s assets. Scores of sheep were sacrificed in celebration. Mossadegh himself was an extraordinary figure — seventy years old, frail, completely bald, he received foreign dignitaries in pajamas from his bed. During meetings, he would collapse dramatically; a doctor would rush to check his pulse. “Mossadegh opened one eye and winked,” reported one observer. His theatricality masked a shrewd political intelligence.

President Truman sent Averell Harriman to Tehran to mediate. Harriman found Mossadegh in bed as usual, railing against British perfidy: “You do not know how crafty they are.” Harriman asked about his grandson. “He is at school.” “Where?” “In England, of course. Where else?” The contradiction seemed lost on Mossadegh. Harriman’s aide Vernon Walters ordered a copy of Alice in Wonderland “as a guidebook” for navigating Iranian politics. In one memorable exchange, Mossadegh claimed that “nothing can be larger than the sum of its parts.” Then he paused: “That is false. Consider the fox. His tail is often much longer than he is.” He laughed uproariously at his own joke.

When Harriman met the militant cleric Kashani, the Ayatollah’s opening gambit was a threat. Harriman responded: “I do not frighten easily.” Kashani smiled: “There was no harm in trying.” The negotiations went nowhere. Anglo-Iranian’s British staff staged a farewell revue before departing. At the refinery gate, the guest house lady interdicted a tank commander with her umbrella. Passengers on the departing ship sang obscene versions of “Colonel Bogey.” The British left Abadan with the stiff-upper-lip theatricality that had characterized their presence for four decades. Behind them, they left an oil industry that Iran lacked the expertise to operate and a nation that could not sell its crude because the international majors closed ranks against nationalized oil.

Iran’s economy deteriorated rapidly without oil revenue. Mossadegh’s coalition began to fracture as the economic pain mounted. In Washington and London, the decision was made to remove him. Operation Ajax, a joint CIA-MI6 covert operation, was launched in August 1953. The first attempt failed, and the Shah fled to Rome in panic. Then, through a combination of paid street mobs, cooperative military officers, and the kind of operatic confusion that characterized Iranian politics — “tumblers doing handsprings and wrestlers showing off biceps” — Mossadegh was overthrown. He fled over his back wall in pajamas. Eisenhower dismissed the entire episode as “more like a dime novel” than a genuine intelligence operation.

The settlement imposed after the coup created a new consortium to operate Iran’s oil: Anglo-Iranian received forty percent, Shell fourteen percent, and five American majors eight percent each. Anglo-Iranian was compensated with approximately 590 million dollars for the loss of its exclusive concession. The irony was exquisite: “A wonderful deal for Fraser — Anglo-Iranian had nothing to sell. It had already been nationalized.” Fraser received payment for assets his company no longer possessed. The Americans were brought in to provide political balance and to ensure that the United States had a stake in Iranian stability. The consortium was a pragmatic solution, but it planted seeds of future resentment.

The Iranian crisis established several precedents that would echo through the decades ahead. First, it demonstrated that nationalization was not a death sentence for a producing country — Iran had survived two years without oil revenue and eventually got better terms. Second, it showed that the international oil companies could not maintain their position without the active support of Western governments, including covert action. Third, it proved that the fifty-fifty principle alone could not satisfy nationalist ambitions when the deeper grievance was about sovereignty rather than money. Mossadegh became a martyr figure across the Middle East, and the coup that removed him would poison Iranian-American relations for generations.

The Shah returned to Tehran emboldened but also dependent on American support in ways that would prove corrosive over time. The consortium arrangement gave Iran significantly more revenue than the old Anglo-Iranian concession, but the principle of foreign control remained intact. Anglo-Iranian itself was renamed British Petroleum — a rebranding that acknowledged the toxicity of the old name. The struggle for Iranian oil had lasted three years, consumed one government, installed another, and redistributed billions of dollars in future revenue. It was, in retrospect, a dress rehearsal for the far larger confrontations that would reshape the global oil industry in the 1970s. The genie of resource nationalism, once released, could never be put back in the bottle.

Chapter 24: The Suez Crisis

The Suez Canal was the creation of Ferdinand de Lesseps, a man who was not an engineer but a diplomat — and who at sixty-four married a woman of twenty and fathered twelve more children. Benjamin Disraeli acquired Egypt’s forty-four percent stake in the canal company for Britain in 1875, reporting to Queen Victoria with characteristic flair: “You have it, Madam.” By 1955, petroleum constituted two-thirds of canal traffic, and two-thirds of Europe’s oil supply passed through the narrow waterway. The canal was the jugular vein of European energy, and any threat to it was a threat to the economic survival of Britain, France, and the entire continent.

Gamal Abdel Nasser — son of a post office clerk, a born plotter who had participated in multiple coup attempts before succeeding — embodied the new Arab nationalism that was sweeping the Middle East. He became “the model for the military officer turned fiery nationalist leader,” inspiring imitators from Baghdad to Algiers. On July 26, 1956, Nasser delivered a speech in which he repeatedly heaped calumny on “de Lesseps” — the name was the prearranged code word. As he spoke, the Egyptian army seized the canal. Nasser then went to see Cyd Charisse in Meet Me in Las Vegas. When British and French pilots were withdrawn in an attempt to prove Egypt could not operate the canal, the Egyptians managed it competently with Soviet bloc assistance.

British Prime Minister Anthony Eden was haunted by the ghost of 1930s appeasement. He had resigned from Chamberlain’s cabinet in 1938, always “a lap behind, the fatal lap.” Now he saw Nasser as a resurrected Mussolini and was determined not to repeat the mistake of accommodation. Eden told Soviet leaders with unusual bluntness: “I must be absolutely blunt about the oil... we would fight for it. We had no intention of being strangled to death.” Harold Macmillan, Chancellor of the Exchequer, read thousands of pages of novels in the first two weeks of the crisis: “I’d have gone barmy!” Both Eden and French Prime Minister Mollet were trapped by their generational trauma, unable to distinguish between a genuine existential threat and a manageable diplomatic challenge.

President Eisenhower believed that military force was neither warranted nor justified. He urged negotiation and warned against the use of force. But Eden, Mollet, and Israeli Prime Minister Ben-Gurion met secretly at Sevres on October 24 and agreed on a coordinated plan: Israel would attack Egypt, and Britain and France would intervene as supposed peacekeepers to “separate the combatants” and seize the canal. On October 29, Israel attacked. Eisenhower was absolutely furious when he learned of the conspiracy — he dumped a diatribe on a hapless aide, mistaking him for Eden on the telephone. The collision between America’s Cold War strategy and European imperial reflexes was total.

Nasser’s response to the invasion was to scuttle ships in the canal, choking off the very oil flow that had been the reason for the attack in the first place. The strategic logic collapsed immediately: Britain and France had gone to war to secure their oil supply and had instead destroyed it. Eisenhower refused to authorize emergency petroleum supplies to the allies, leaving them “to boil in their own oil.” Macmillan grasped the implications with devastating clarity: “Oil sanctions! That finishes it.” An immense speculative run on the pound sterling began. The International Monetary Fund, under American pressure, refused emergency financial assistance. Britain’s imperial pretensions were being dismantled not by military defeat but by economic asphyxiation.

The Texas Railroad Commission barely increased American production to help Europe, and the commissioners made their displeasure at British behavior clear: “England apparently still looks on us as a province.” Eventually, the Oil Lift swung into operation — a cooperative venture that redeployed tankers so the Western Hemisphere could once again supply Europe, as it had during both world wars. Belgium banned private driving on Sundays. Britain imposed gasoline rationing — the “Suez sixpence.” The crisis demonstrated with painful clarity how dependent Europe had become on Middle Eastern oil and how vulnerable that dependency made European governments to disruptions in supply.

Eden resigned in January 1957 — “the last prime minister to believe Britain was a great power and the first to confront a crisis which proved she was not.” Macmillan succeeded him, reading Pride and Prejudice to soothe his soul. The crisis accelerated several transformations that would reshape the oil industry. Supertankers became a priority, capable of carrying crude around the Cape of Good Hope and bypassing the canal entirely. Pipeline capacity was expanded. The strategic vulnerability of the Suez chokepoint was permanently embedded in Western military planning. In 1970, the dying Eden expressed a single wish: for “a lake of oil” under the North Sea. “That was exactly what they were beginning to do.”

The Suez Crisis was a watershed in the history of oil and empire. It demonstrated that the era of European dominance in the Middle East was finished, that the United States was now the decisive Western power in the region, and that oil could be used as a weapon by producing countries as well as consuming nations. Nasser emerged as the hero of the Arab world despite his military defeat, because he had faced down two European empires and survived. The canal was eventually reopened under Egyptian control, validating his nationalization. For the oil industry, Suez was a warning that the infrastructure connecting production to consumption was as strategically important as the oil itself, and that any power that controlled a chokepoint held a veto over the world’s energy supply.

Chapter 25: The Elephants

The postwar decades unleashed a transformation in the global oil industry that dwarfed anything the pioneers could have imagined. Free world crude production surged from 8.7 million barrels per day in 1948 to an astonishing 42 million by 1972. The United States, once the undisputed colossus of petroleum, watched its share slip from sixty-four percent to just twenty-two. The Middle East filled the void with staggering force, climbing from 1.1 million to 18.2 million barrels daily — a fifteen-hundred-percent increase in barely a generation. Proven reserves expanded almost ninefold, from 62 billion to 534 billion barrels, and the reserve life of global oil actually lengthened from nineteen years to thirty-five. New oil was being found even faster than it could be consumed. The age of the elephant fields had arrived.

No figure embodied the challenge to the established order more flamboyantly than Enrico Mattei, the Italian wildcatter who transformed a moribund state agency into a sprawling industrial empire. Born the unruly son of a policeman, Mattei left school at fourteen and clawed his way upward with the ferocity of what one observer called “a born condottiere.” He took the languishing AGIP and rebuilt it as ENI, a conglomerate of thirty-six subsidiaries that reached into chemicals, textiles, and newspapers. He is widely credited with coining the derisive phrase “Sette Sorrelle” — the Seven Sisters — to describe the major oil companies whose dominance he was determined to break. Of dealing with governments, Mattei was blunt:

“To deal with a government is like sucking needles.” — Enrico Mattei

He owned newspapers, subsidized politicians, and financed the Christian Democrats with the casual deftness of a Renaissance prince.

Mattei’s methods were as unorthodox as his ambitions. When he visited Shell, chairman John Loudon politely asked how he had gotten into the oil business. Mattei talked without pause through the entire lunch. “When we went to dessert, he asked for something,” Loudon recalled. “We couldn’t do it, and that was the end.” What Mattei wanted was access to crude on terms that would shatter the prevailing fifty-fifty arrangement between companies and host governments. He found a willing partner in Iran, offering an effective seventy-five-twenty-five split in the host country’s favor. The fifty-fifty principle, the bedrock of the postwar petroleum order, had been pierced. The majors watched in dismay, knowing that every producing country would now demand better terms.

Mattei’s life ended abruptly in October 1962, when his plane crashed in bad weather near Milan. He was fifty-six years old. Suspicions of sabotage lingered for decades, though no proof ever surfaced. At his death he was widely considered “the most important individual in Italy, more important than the premier or the Pope.” Without his manic energy and political cunning, ENI’s buccaneer days were over. But the revolution he had instigated — the idea that newcomers could challenge the majors and that producing nations deserved a larger share — would eventually overthrow the old order entirely. He had lit a fuse that no amount of corporate diplomacy could extinguish.

Japan entered the Middle Eastern oil game with its own national champion, the Arabian Oil Company, which accepted an unprecedented split of just forty-four percent of profits. The company provided fifteen percent of Japan’s total oil supply by the mid-1960s, a lifeline for a nation that imported virtually every barrel it consumed. The Shah of Iran, meanwhile, courted American independents with characteristic grandiosity. To executives from Standard of Indiana, he declared: “You know — we are not Arabs. We are Aryans, and we have a great history.” The Shah saw every oil deal as an affirmation of Persian civilization and a down payment on the modern industrial state he intended to build.

The Middle East’s political landscape shifted violently in July 1958, when Iraq’s monarchy was overthrown in an explosion of nationalist fury. King Faisal II was dragged from his palace and beheaded. Prime Minister Nuri es-Said, attempting to flee disguised as a woman, was recognized by a mob, lynched, and his body run over by a car and left flattened in the street. The new military regime aligned itself with Nasser’s pan-Arab vision, and for a time it seemed Nasserism was destined to sweep the entire region. Western oil executives, watching from behind compound walls, understood that the political ground beneath their concessions was shifting in ways that contractual language could not contain.

Two men from opposite ends of the petroleum world would forge the most consequential partnership in the industry’s history. Juan Pablo Perez Alfonzo of Venezuela was a man of iron determination yet almost monastic discipline. His family had once purchased a 1950 Singer automobile and shipped it to Caracas, where it sat rusting at the docks because nobody bothered to collect it. When someone finally drove it home, the engine burned out because no one had checked the oil. Perez Alfonzo installed the wreck in his garden as “a corroded, overgrown shrine to the dangers of oil wealth” — a daily reminder that petroleum could destroy as easily as it enriched.

His counterpart was Abdullah Tariki, the “Red Sheikh,” Saudi Arabia’s first American-educated petroleum technocrat. While studying at the University of Texas, Tariki had been refused entry to bars because he was mistaken for a Mexican. The experience left him with a smoldering resentment of Western condescension that would fuel his career. The matchmaker who brought these two together was Wanda Jablonski, a journalist of legendary access who had once wrangled an evening inside King Saud’s harem. At the 1959 Arab Oil Congress in Cairo, she introduced Tariki and Perez Alfonzo. They met secretly at a yacht club in the Cairo suburb of Maadi, surrounded by what Jablonski described as “a James Bond atmosphere.” She had just been the matchmaker for what would become OPEC.

Chapter 26: OPEC and the Surge Pot

The event that catalyzed the creation of OPEC was not a revolution or a war but a corporate decision made in a boardroom on the Avenue of the Americas in Manhattan. In July 1960, the board of Standard Oil of New Jersey — the mightiest of the Seven Sisters — debated whether to cut the posted price of crude oil. Chairman Monroe Rathbone, an engineer whose entire career had been spent in domestic operations, was determined to do it. Howard Page, Jersey’s most experienced Middle East hand, vigorously objected. “You can’t just be guided by market forces in an industry so essential to governments,” Page warned. Wanda Jablonski briefed the board on the intensity of anti-Western hostility sweeping the Arab world. Rathbone dismissed Page as “a know-it-all.”

On August 9, 1960, Jersey cut posted prices by up to fourteen cents a barrel. Shell’s John Loudon would later call it “the fatal move.” The cut slashed the revenues of every producing government in the Middle East and Venezuela at a stroke. It was the second such reduction in barely a year, and it confirmed what nationalists like Tariki had been arguing all along: the companies set prices unilaterally, treating sovereign nations as passive recipients of whatever crumbs the market afforded. Within hours of the announcement, Tariki fired off a telegram to Perez Alfonzo. The moment they had been preparing for had arrived.

Iraq recognized the opportunity with particular clarity. By using the price cut to establish an organization that included non-Arab countries — specifically Iran and Venezuela — Iraq could isolate oil policy from Nasser’s pan-Arab orbit and create an independent power base. On September 14, 1960, in a Baghdad conference room, five nations — Saudi Arabia, Venezuela, Kuwait, Iraq, and Iran — founded the Organization of the Petroleum Exporting Countries. Perez Alfonzo held the founding telegram aloft and declared: “We’ve done it!” The mood was electric, though none of the five founders could have imagined how profoundly their creation would reshape the world economy within a single decade.

The Western oil establishment reacted with studied indifference. Howard Page, who had tried so hard to prevent the price cut that provoked OPEC’s founding, admitted:

“We attached little importance to it.” — Howard Page, Standard Oil of New Jersey

A CIA report devoted exactly four lines to the Baghdad conference. The Shah of Iran called the idea of prorationing “nice in theory but unrealistic.” Even OPEC’s own founders stumbled. Tariki was fired from his Saudi post in 1962, replaced by the more diplomatic Ahmed Zaki Yamani. Perez Alfonzo resigned in 1963, retreating to his garden shrine and calling petroleum “the excrement of the devil.” For the rest of the decade, OPEC appeared to be little more than a talking shop.

While OPEC struggled for relevance, a new source of supply burst onto the scene. Libya’s 1955 petroleum law had deliberately parceled concessions among many small companies to ensure the country was never “in the hands of one oil company.” When Jersey struck the massive Zelten field in 1959, the verdict was immediate: “Libya has hit the jack-pot.” Libyan crude was “sweet” — low in sulfur — and perfectly located just across the Mediterranean from Europe’s refineries. Production soared so rapidly that by 1969 Libya exceeded Saudi Arabia in daily output. But corruption pervaded the kingdom under King Idris, and operators complained of being “nickeled and dimed to death.”

Across the Atlantic, the United States was erecting its own peculiar barriers. President Eisenhower imposed import quotas in 1959 to protect domestic producers, spawning ingenious evasions. The most famous was the “Brownsville U-Turn”: oil was trucked from Texas into Mexico, driven around a traffic circle, and brought back across the border as “overland imports” exempt from the quota. The quotas sheltered American producers but ensured that cheap Middle Eastern oil flooded every other market. The number of companies operating in the Middle East exploded from nine in 1948 to eighty-one by 1972. Returns on investment fell from twenty percent or more to eleven or thirteen percent as competition intensified.

The most consequential dynamic of the 1960s was the relentless rivalry between Iran and Saudi Arabia for market share. The Persian Gulf became what industry insiders called the “swing area” or the “surge pot” — the reservoir of surplus capacity that balanced global supply. Howard Page understood the danger better than anyone. When offered a concession in Oman, he refused: “I am absolutely sure we don’t want oil because we are liable to lose the Aramco concession.” Every barrel Jersey lifted outside Saudi Arabia was a barrel the Saudis would notice. Between 1957 and 1970, Iranian production grew by 387 percent compared to Saudi Arabia’s 258. The Shah was winning the production race, and Riyadh was taking note.

The decade of the 1960s ended with posted prices having fallen twenty-two percent, producing governments increasingly resentful, and a new generation of nationalist leaders waiting in the wings. OPEC had accomplished nothing concrete in its first ten years — no output limits, no price restoration, no collective bargaining power. Yet its mere existence had planted an idea that proved impossible to uproot: the producing nations, not the consuming companies, were the rightful masters of the commodity buried beneath their soil. That idea, dormant through a decade of surplus, would explode into reality the moment the market tightened. The surge pot was full. The question was how long it would last.

Chapter 27: Hydrocarbon Man

If the sun energized the planet, it was oil that now powered its human population. Between 1949 and 1972, total world energy consumption tripled while oil demand specifically increased five and a half times. American consumption alone surged from 5.8 million to 16.4 million barrels per day. Western Europe’s thirst for petroleum grew fifteenfold. But the most extraordinary transformation occurred in Japan, where oil consumption multiplied 137 times over the same period. Motor vehicle registrations outside the United States leapt from 18.9 million to 161 million. Humanity had become, in the fullest sense, Hydrocarbon Man — a species whose civilization, prosperity, and daily comfort depended almost entirely on liquid petroleum.

The automobile was the engine of this transformation, and nowhere more so than in the United States. American cars grew longer, wider, and heavier through the 1950s and 1960s, achieving a magnificent eight miles per gallon in some cases. Gasoline stations sprouted on every corner, competing with giveaways and promotions. Credit cards tied customers to specific brands with the loyalty of medieval vassals. Texaco sponsored comedian Milton Berle on television, urging Americans to “Trust Your Car to the Man Who Wears the Star.” The petrochemical industry gave the world plastics, synthetic fibers, and fertilizers. When a well-meaning neighbor whispered the word “Plastics” to Dustin Hoffman in The Graduate (1967), the audience laughed because everyone recognized the gospel truth of it.

Coal, the fuel that had powered the Industrial Revolution and built the British Empire, could not withstand the tidal wave of cheap petroleum. In the United States, the combative strikes led by John L. Lewis of the United Mine Workers proved to be “a powerful invitation to find a substitute.” Every time Lewis shut down the coal mines, another factory switched to oil or natural gas and never switched back. In London, the “Killer Fogs” of the early 1950s — dense clouds of coal smoke that choked the city and killed thousands — prompted the Clean Air Act and the establishment of smokeless zones. In Western Europe as a whole, coal’s share of energy plummeted from seventy-five percent in 1955 to just twenty-two percent by 1972, while oil surged from twenty-three to sixty percent.

Japan’s metamorphosis was the most dramatic of all. In 1956, the government’s economic white paper declared: “We are no longer living in postwar reconstruction.” The statement marked the beginning of the Japanese economic miracle, and oil was its essential fuel. Petroleum went from seven percent to seventy percent of Japan’s total energy supply. In 1955, Japanese factories produced 69,000 automobiles. By 1968, they were turning out 4.1 million — and within a few more years, Japanese cars would be conquering export markets worldwide. Business historian Alfred Chandler distilled the recipe for both the German and Japanese postwar miracles into a simple formula: “improved institutional arrangements and cheap oil.”

The abundance of cheap petroleum reshaped not just industry but the texture of daily life. Suburbs spread outward from city centers, connected by highways that assumed limitless gasoline. Central heating replaced coal grates. Air travel, powered by kerosene, shrank the globe. Farmers switched from draft animals to diesel tractors, and the resulting agricultural revolution fed billions. Synthetic fertilizers derived from petroleum and natural gas multiplied crop yields. The entire postwar consumer economy — from polyester clothing to vinyl records, from nylon stockings to plastic packaging — rested on the products of the refinery. No previous energy source had ever penetrated so deeply into every corner of human existence.

This extraordinary dependence was built on an assumption that few bothered to examine: that the oil would always be there, that it would always be cheap, and that the nations sitting atop the reserves would always be willing to sell. The consuming world had constructed an entire civilization on a resource controlled by a handful of countries in the most politically volatile region on earth. So long as the surge pot remained full and prices remained low, nobody noticed the vulnerability. But the architecture of Hydrocarbon Man contained a structural flaw that would become catastrophically apparent in the decade ahead. The higher the dependence climbed, the greater the leverage of those who controlled the supply.

By 1972, oil had achieved a dominance over the global energy system that no single fuel had ever matched. It provided more than half the world’s commercial energy, fueled virtually all transportation, and served as the feedstock for an ever-expanding petrochemical industry. The major oil companies had grown into the largest private enterprises in history, their revenues exceeding the GNP of most nations. Tankers the size of aircraft carriers crisscrossed the oceans in a ceaseless ballet of supply. Pipelines threaded through deserts and under seas. The infrastructure of Hydrocarbon Man was vast, intricate, and breathtakingly fragile. Everything depended on the uninterrupted flow of oil from a few giant fields in the Middle East to the factories and filling stations of the industrialized world.

The world that Hydrocarbon Man had built was, in retrospect, an astonishing gamble. The consuming nations had traded energy independence for convenience and low cost, and for two decades the bargain had seemed extraordinarily favorable. Economic growth rates were the highest in history. Living standards rose across the Western world at a pace without precedent. Japan climbed from ruin to the second-largest economy on earth. But all of it rested on the presumption that the political arrangements governing oil would remain stable, that the companies would continue to mediate between producers and consumers, and that the producing nations would remain content with their share. By the early 1970s, every one of those presumptions was crumbling.

Chapter 28: The Hinge Years

In October 1971, the Shah of Iran staged the most extravagant celebration the modern world had ever seen. At the ruins of Persepolis, the ancient capital of the Achaemenid Empire, he hosted a party costing between $100 million and $200 million to celebrate 2,500 years of Persian monarchy. Maxim’s of Paris flew in 165 chefs and 25,000 bottles of wine. French president Georges Pompidou declined the invitation, quipping: “If I did go, they would probably make me the head-waiter.” The Shah dismissed his critics with aristocratic disdain: “We can hardly offer them bread and radishes, can we?” He rode a stallion named Cossack, owned by Princess Anne — the horse’s name was carefully kept from him.

Behind the pageantry, the geopolitical foundations of the petroleum order were crumbling. Britain withdrew its military forces from the Persian Gulf by 1971, ending what had been a century-old security system. The withdrawal cost only six thousand troops and twelve million pounds a year, and some of the Gulf sheikhs had even offered to pay. In Dubai, the reaction was bewildered: “Who asked them to leave?” In Bahrain, leaders lamented that “Britain could do with another Winston Churchill.” The American position in the region was scarcely more reassuring. Nixon was consumed by Vietnam and Watergate, and his administration relied on the Shah as a regional policeman — a strategy that would prove disastrously shortsighted.

The most ominous development of all occurred not in the Middle East but in Texas. In 1970, American oil production peaked at 11.3 million barrels per day and began an irreversible decline. The Texas Railroad Commission, which had regulated domestic production since the 1930s, set allowable output at one hundred percent for the first time — there was no more spare capacity to release. One commissioner called it “damned historic, and a sad one. That old warrior can’t rise anymore.” Net US imports surged from 2.2 million barrels in 1967 to 6 million by 1973. An American diplomat named James Placke warned from Tripoli that “control of the flow of resources has been of strategic concern throughout history.” No one in Washington paid attention.

In September 1969, a group of young Libyan military officers overthrew King Idris while he was taking a cure in Turkey. Their leader was a twenty-seven-year-old captain named Muammar Qaddafi, an austere, intense figure who declared: “People who have lived without oil for 5,000 years can live without it again.” His deputy, Major Abdessalam Jalloud, was more fun-loving than the puritanical Qaddafi but equally relentless at the negotiating table. He once threw a company proposal back as a crumpled paper ball. On another occasion, he entered the room carrying a submachine gun. During one particularly tense session with Armand Hammer of Occidental Petroleum, Jalloud set his .45-caliber pistol on the table between them. Hammer, a veteran dealmaker, admitted he “had never before negotiated across a gun barrel.”

Occidental was uniquely vulnerable. Libya cut the company’s production from 800,000 to 500,000 barrels per day and demanded higher prices. Hammer desperately sought replacement crude from other companies, but even the chairman of Exxon refused to help. “Here the President of Exxon was confronted with an art dealer, a non-fraternity type,” one observer noted acidly. Hammer commuted daily between Tripoli and Paris — back at two in the morning, departing again at six — phoning his board from a suite at the Ritz. In the end, Occidental capitulated. Libya won a thirty-cent increase in the posted price and a fifty-five percent profit share. The precedent was set, and every producing country in the world took notice.

The Shah, predictably, was not about to let Libya outdo Iran. He immediately broke through the fifty-fifty barrier. The major companies, alarmed by the cascade of demands, formed a united front — the “Front Uni” — with a special antitrust waiver from the US government. The companies proposed to negotiate a single comprehensive agreement with all producing nations. But the nations refused to be lumped together. In February 1971, the Tehran Agreement was signed, granting substantial increases. Shell’s Sir David Barran declared:

“The buyer’s market for oil is over.” — Sir David Barran, Shell

Libya then demanded and received an additional ninety cents. Each country used the other’s gains as a floor for its next demand — a leapfrog that the companies could not stop.

The concept of “participation” emerged as the next front in the struggle. Rather than outright nationalization, which risked international legal complications and loss of technical expertise, producing countries would acquire partial ownership of the operating concessions. Yamani described participation as “a bond that would be indissoluble, like a Catholic marriage.” Not everyone was convinced. Ed Guinn of Bunker Hunt told a bleak joke about two skeletons discovered in a closet. One asked the other: “How did we get here?” The reply: “If we had any guts we’d get out.” Meanwhile, Qaddafi skipped the incremental approach entirely, nationalizing fifty-one percent of all Libyan concessions.

The Shah brought the era of concessions to a formal close. “Seventy-two years of foreign control was ended,” he proclaimed. By 1973, the world’s available surplus production capacity had shrunk to a mere 500,000 barrels per day — barely one percent of total consumption. The system was running at a ninety-nine percent utilization rate, a state of affairs that any engineer would recognize as extraordinarily precarious. A single disruption — a war, a revolution, a deliberate cutback — could send the entire edifice crashing. The hinge years had swung the door open. What came through it would be an earthquake.

Chapter 29: The Oil Weapon

In April 1973, President Richard Nixon abolished oil import quotas, acknowledging what everyone in the industry already knew: American domestic production could no longer keep up with demand. He immediately replaced the quotas with “voluntary” allocations — a system designed to distribute shortage rather than manage surplus. The distinction was fundamental and terrifying. James Akins, a State Department analyst, published an article titled “The Oil Crisis: This Time the Wolf Is Here,” warning that the era of cheap energy was over. Nixon’s domestic adviser John Ehrlichman dismissed the concern with a quip that revealed the administration’s willful blindness: “Conservation is not the Republican ethic.” By summer 1973, US imports had reached 6.2 million barrels per day — double the level of 1970.

In Egypt, President Anwar Sadat had concluded that war was the only way to break the diplomatic stalemate that had frozen the region since 1967. Henry Kissinger would later admit: “What literally no one understood was the mind of the man.” Sadat’s purpose was “psychological and diplomatic, much more than military” — he did not need to conquer the Sinai, only to shatter the myth of Israeli invincibility and force the superpowers to engage. Crucially, he shared his war plans with King Faisal of Saudi Arabia. By linking military strategy to oil strategy, Sadat ensured that petroleum would stand at the center of the coming conflict. The oil weapon, long discussed and never used effectively, was about to be unsheathed.

Faisal’s conversion was the pivotal event. As recently as 1972, the Saudi king had rejected the oil weapon flatly: “Politics and oil should not be mixed.” The disastrous Arab oil embargo of 1967 had achieved nothing except lost revenues. But by 1973, the calculus had changed. Saudi Arabia was now the world’s swing producer, pumping 8.4 million barrels daily — sixty-two percent above the previous year. The kingdom had leverage it had never possessed before. Faisal sent a blunt warning to the Aramco partners: “You will lose everything.” In Washington, the reaction was “a large degree of disbelief.” Three of the four Aramco parent companies took the extraordinary step of publicly calling for a change in American Middle East policy — an action without precedent in the industry’s history.

In June 1973, Leonid Brezhnev demanded an unscheduled late-night meeting with Nixon at the president’s San Clemente compound. The Soviet leader warned that the Middle East was on the verge of explosion. Nixon and Kissinger dismissed the warning. Three months later, on October 6 — Yom Kippur, the holiest day in the Jewish calendar — 222 Egyptian jets screamed across the Suez Canal, 3,000 field guns opened fire, and Syrian tanks poured across the Golan Heights. On October 5, the CIA had assessed that “military preparations do not indicate that any party intends to initiate hostilities.” Israel’s own intelligence suffered from what analysts called the “conception” — the fixed belief that Egypt would never attack without air superiority. The surprise was for Israel what Pearl Harbor had been for America.

Israel was in deep trouble. Moshe Dayan, the legendary defense minister, told Prime Minister Golda Meir: “The Third Temple is going under.” Meir prepared a secret letter to Nixon. On October 12, two private communications arrived at the White House within hours of each other: one from the four Aramco chairmen warning about “a snowballing effect” if the United States supported Israel; the other from Golda Meir, making clear that Israel’s survival hung in the balance. Nixon authorized an emergency airlift. The massive C-5A transport planes arrived in daylight, their immense white stars visible to everyone on the ground. Any pretense of American neutrality was over — the United States was now an open combatant in the eyes of the Arab world.

On October 16, the Gulf oil states raised the posted price by seventy percent — to $5.11 per barrel — in a unilateral announcement that shattered every remaining fiction of company-government negotiation. Yamani savored the moment:

“This is a moment for which I have been waiting a long time. We are masters of our own commodity.” — Ahmed Zaki Yamani

The pretense of negotiating was over forever. The following day, Arab oil ministers agreed to cut production by five percent each month, with a total embargo on shipments to the United States. On October 20, Saudi Arabia cut off all exports to America. That same day, Nixon fired special prosecutor Archibald Cox in the Saturday Night Massacre.

The convergence of crises was staggering. Brezhnev threatened unilateral Soviet military intervention in the Middle East. American forces went to DefCon 3 — a worldwide nuclear alert, the highest since the Cuban Missile Crisis. For a few terrifying hours, the world teetered on the edge of superpower confrontation over oil-soaked desert. The three- decade-old postwar petroleum order — the system of concessions, posted prices, and company mediation that had governed the flow of oil since Aramco’s creation — had died its final death. What replaced it was a world in which producing nations held the whip hand, consuming nations scrambled, and the price of oil would be set not by accountants in Manhattan but by ministers in Riyadh and Tehran.

The oil weapon had been unsheathed with devastating effect. In barely two weeks, the entire structure of global energy had been overturned. The comfortable assumptions of Hydrocarbon Man — cheap oil, reliable supply, Western control — lay in ruins. The embargo would last five months and transform everything: the global economy, the balance of geopolitical power, the relationship between consuming and producing nations, and the daily lives of hundreds of millions of people who had never given a moment’s thought to where their gasoline came from. The age of energy innocence was over.

Chapter 30: “Bidding for Our Life”

The net loss of oil in December 1973 was 4.4 million barrels per day — fourteen percent of all internationally traded petroleum. It was enough to trigger a panic that fed on itself with self-reinforcing fury. The ingredients were explosive: an ongoing war, production cutbacks, targeted embargoes, and the specter of permanent shortage. Nigerian crude sold for over sixteen dollars a barrel. At an Iranian government auction, bids reached seventeen dollars. A Japanese trading company, desperate for supply, bid $22.60. When asked to justify the price, one Japanese executive replied: “We weren’t bidding just for oil. We were bidding for our life.” The phrase captured the existential terror that had gripped the importing world.

The panic manifested differently in each country but was universal in its intensity. In Japan, housewives hoarded toilet paper until stores ran out. In the United States, gasoline stations displayed “Sorry, No Gas Today” signs and the ones that remained open attracted lines stretching around city blocks. The federal allocation system, designed to distribute supply equitably, perversely prevented gasoline from being shifted to the regions where it was most needed. Suburbs with long commutes ran dry while downtown stations had surplus. The resulting frustration boiled over into fistfights at pumps and a deep erosion of Americans’ confidence in their institutions. The world’s most powerful nation had been brought to its knees by countries most of its citizens could not find on a map.

The major oil companies found themselves in an impossible position. They adopted a policy of “equal suffering” — diverting roughly a third of their oil from embargoed to non-embargoed countries, spreading the shortage as evenly as possible. BP’s chairman, Sir Eric Drake, found himself confronted by British Prime Minister Edward Heath, who demanded preferential treatment for Britain. Drake asked a pointed question: “Are you asking me as a shareholder or as a government? If government, put it in writing.” Heath replied: “You know perfectly well I can’t.” Drake: “Then I won’t do it.” Having lived through BP’s nationalization in Iran two decades earlier, Drake understood that favoring one government over another was a recipe for losing everything.

Whitehall’s civil servants proved more deft than the prime minister. They quietly reminded the oil companies that Britain decided who received North Sea exploration licenses — a hint that required no written instruction to be understood. The crisis exposed the fundamental tension at the heart of the major oil companies’ existence: they were private enterprises with obligations to shareholders, yet their product was so essential to national survival that governments treated them as quasi-sovereign entities. Every barrel diverted to Britain was a barrel denied to Japan or Germany. The companies walked a tightrope over an abyss, knowing that one misstep could bring nationalization from any direction.

In December 1973, at an OPEC meeting in Tehran, the Shah pushed through a posted price of $11.65 per barrel — a fourfold increase since the outbreak of war barely ten weeks earlier. He called it “kindness and generosity,” noting that Western industrial goods had risen in price for decades while oil had not. Saudi Arabia had wanted a lower price of eight dollars, fearing that too dramatic an increase would trigger a global depression and accelerate conservation efforts that would ultimately diminish the value of their vast reserves. The Shah lectured them: “The era of their terrific progress based on cheap oil is finished.” It was a statement of triumph delivered with imperial certainty.

President Nixon, besieged by Watergate, went on television on November 7, 1973, to announce Project Independence: by the end of the decade, the United States would meet its own energy needs without relying on any foreign source. His own staff told him it was impossible. Nixon fired his energy coordinator, John Love, and replaced him with William Simon, a tough former Wall Street bond trader whom Nixon compared to Albert Speer — an analogy that thrilled no one. Simon appeared at Congressional hearings looking as if he had been in combat, with “blood oozing from the gash.” The energy crisis had become a domestic political emergency rivaling Watergate itself.

Kissinger’s shuttle diplomacy eventually provided the diplomatic framework for ending the embargo. He brokered an Egyptian-Israeli disengagement agreement in January 1974 and a Syrian-Israeli accord in May. Sadat declared that “the United States holds 99 percent of the cards,” though he privately estimated the figure at sixty percent. Japan, desperate for oil, endorsed the Arab position — its first significant foreign policy break with Washington since the war. France went further, with foreign minister Michel Jobert greeting his allies at a conference with the words “Bonjour les traitres” and denouncing the proposed International Energy Agency as “a machine de guerre.”

The embargo was formally lifted on March 18, 1974. Kissinger traveled to Riyadh to seal the diplomatic arrangements. He walked through a tremendous hall where men in black robes sat silently against the walls, and found himself reflecting on “what strange twists of fate had caused a refugee from Nazi persecution to wind up in Arabia.” The embargo was over, but the world it had created was permanent. Oil would never again be just another commodity. It had become the most powerful geopolitical weapon on earth, and the nations that controlled it had demonstrated, beyond any remaining doubt, that they were willing to use it.

Chapter 31: OPEC’s Imperium

The money was staggering. OPEC petroleum earnings surged from $23 billion in 1972 to $140 billion by 1977. The exporting nations embarked on a dizzying spending program that strained the physical capacity of their economies. Ports clogged with unloaded cargo. Ships waited weeks for berths. Western vendors scrambled for hotel rooms in Jeddah, Kuwait City, and Tehran, paying astronomical rates for accommodations that would have been condemned elsewhere. Arms transactions became enormous — the Shah alone placed billions in military orders. In Saudi Arabia, a Datsun proved cheaper to fuel and maintain than a camel. The traditional world had collided with the modern at the speed of a gushing wellhead.

The consuming world paid a devastating price. American GNP plunged six percent between 1973 and 1975 while unemployment doubled to nine percent. Japan’s GNP declined for the first time since the end of World War II. The phenomenon was unprecedented: simultaneous inflation and recession, which economists gave the ungainly name “stagflation.” The fourfold oil price increase functioned as what analysts called the “OPEC tax” — a massive withdrawal of purchasing power from consuming economies and a transfer to producing nations that mostly could not spend it fast enough. The hardest hit were the non-oil developing countries, for whom a new category was invented: the “fourth world,” nations too poor to afford oil and too weak to demand concessions.

The Shah saw December 1973 as his moment of destiny. The quadrupled revenues would fund what he called “Iran’s Great Civilization” — a crash modernization program that would vault the country into the twenty-first century. “Everything you can dream of can be achieved here,” he told visitors. Empress Farah offered a revealing insight into her husband’s psychology: “One of the only things my husband likes in life is speed!” The Shah applied that passion to every dimension of Iranian life, ordering billions in infrastructure, weapons, and industrial equipment while ignoring the social disorientation and religious resentment that modernization provoked. He was building a skyscraper on sand.

The Saudis watched the Shah’s hubris with deep unease. They had never approved the December 1973 price increase, considering it too large and too dangerous. Higher prices, the Saudis argued, would inevitably trigger conservation efforts and the development of alternative energy sources, diminishing the long-term value of their vast reserves. Yamani issued a prophetic warning: “If the Shah departs, we could have a violent, anti-American regime in Teheran.” Between 1974 and 1978, OPEC managed only two small increases, moving from $10.84 to $12.70 per barrel. Adjusted for inflation, the real price eroded by roughly ten percent. By 1977, even the Shah had begun to acknowledge the limits of his spending binge: “We acquired money we could not spend.” Iran quietly joined Saudi moderation.

Ahmed Zaki Yamani became the most recognized figure of the new age. Born in Mecca in 1930, the grandson of a grand mufti, he attended NYU and Harvard Law School before being appointed Saudi oil minister at the astonishing age of thirty-two. Where Tariki had been abrasive, Yamani was devastating in his softness. “Give me Tariki’s rantings,” one Western negotiator said. “Yamani drives you to the wall with sweet reasonableness.” His voice was so quiet that adversaries had to strain to hear him, which gave him an odd power in any room. The angrier he became, the quieter he spoke. His guiding principle was long-term thinking:

“Once you start thinking short term, you are in trouble.” — Ahmed Zaki Yamani

Kissinger offered a diplomat’s assessment of Yamani: “watchful eyes and little Van Dyke beard made him look like a priggish young don... He became the technician par excellence.” Unlike many of his OPEC counterparts, Yamani understood that oil was ultimately a depletable resource and that the producing nations’ power was temporary. He never gambled: “I hate it. It rots the soul.” In oil politics, by contrast, everything was “a calculated risk.” This distinction — between gambling and calculated risk — defined Yamani’s approach and set him apart from the Shah, who gambled on a civilizational scale with the recklessness of a man who believed history owed him vindication.

Violence punctuated the era. In March 1975, King Faisal was assassinated by his own nephew during a royal audience. In December of the same year, the Venezuelan terrorist “Carlos” attacked OPEC headquarters in Vienna, taking the assembled oil ministers hostage. Yamani spent forty-four hours clutching his worry beads, convinced he was going to die. Carlos singled him out as a target, and only a combination of diplomatic pressure and ransom money secured his release. The attacks demonstrated that oil had become so central to global affairs that its ministers were targets for every revolutionary and fanatic with a cause.

The concession era ended not with a bang but with a series of polite corporate surrenders. Kuwait took one hundred percent ownership of its oil operations with the brisk announcement: “Thank you very much and good bye.” Gulf Oil and BP asked for two billion dollars in compensation; they received fifty million. A Gulf executive reflected:

“It was the ebbing of American power — the Romans retreating from Hadrian’s Wall.” — Goodman, Gulf Oil

Venezuela nationalized on January 1, 1976, establishing PDVSA. Saudi Arabia took one hundred percent of Aramco — agreed in principle in 1976 in Yamani’s suite at the Al-Yamama Hotel, though the paperwork was not signed until 1990. Forty-three years after Standard of California’s original payment of $175,000, proven Saudi reserves stood at 149 billion barrels. It was truly the end of the era.

Chapter 32: The Adjustment

The consuming nations responded to the oil shock with a mixture of ingenuity, austerity, and occasional absurdity. Japan, the most oil-dependent of the major economies, reacted with characteristic discipline. “Instead of using the resources in the ground,” officials declared, “we would use the resources in our head.” MITI curtailed elevator service in government buildings. An “energy conservation look” featuring short-sleeve jackets was promoted for summer business wear, though it never caught on with tradition-bound salarymen. Japanese industry threw itself into energy efficiency with the same fervor it brought to manufacturing automobiles. The national spirit was stoic: “The Japanese people are accustomed to crises like earthquakes.”

France responded with Gallic pragmatism. President Pompidou, gravely ill, confirmed the nation’s commitment to nuclear power, coal revival, and aggressive conservation before he died in office. Government inspectors patrolled buildings with thermometers, levying fines on anyone who exceeded a room temperature of twenty degrees Celsius. The oil companies, once the swaggering barons of European commerce, were humbled overnight. Exxon’s famous advertising tiger was tamed — no longer leaping into the tank with predatory energy, but quietly advising motorists to check their tire pressure. Gasoline stations could no longer give away promotional trinkets. When Total attempted a patriotic billboard reading “This is France,” it was immediately banned — authorities feared the public would conclude that the companies were rich and therefore no energy problem existed.

In the United States, the oil companies became pariahs. Senator Henry “Scoop” Jackson hauled their executives before his committee under the glare of television lights. “They came across as inept, insulated, self-satisfied,” one observer noted. When an Exxon vice president could not recall his own company’s dividend, Jackson called him “childish” on national television. Exxon’s 1973 earnings were up fifty-nine percent; chairman Ken Jamieson told Congress: “I am not embarrassed.” Many thought otherwise. The oil companies became among the most unpopular institutions in America. The cherished depletion allowance was curtailed, and forty-five of one hundred Senators voted for “divestiture” — which the industry called “dismember- ment” and fought with every lobbying dollar at its disposal.

President Gerald Ford’s administration achieved two lasting energy accomplishments. The first was the Alaska pipeline, a ten-billion-dollar engineering marvel stretching eight hundred miles from the Arctic to the port of Valdez. The second was fuel efficiency standards that would raise the average from thirteen to 27.5 miles per gallon — a change that would ultimately save more oil than any pipeline could deliver. Ford also established the Strategic Petroleum Reserve, a system of salt caverns along the Gulf Coast that could store hundreds of millions of barrels as insurance against future disruption. These were practical, unglamorous measures that received little public credit but would prove their worth in the decades ahead.

President Jimmy Carter made energy the centerpiece of his domestic agenda, calling it “the moral equivalent of war.” Critics seized on the acronym: MEOW. Carter’s energy secretary, James Schlesinger, fought to deregulate domestic oil prices and bring them up to world levels. Carter initially resisted: “I can’t understand it.” Schlesinger, grappling with the separate nightmare of natural gas regulation, confided: “I understand now what Hell is.” Carter’s relationship with Congress was tortured. He set a ninety-day deadline for an energy plan and refused to budge: “I said 90 days. I made the pledge and I intend to keep it.” The resulting legislation was a mess of compromises that satisfied no one.

New supply began to arrive from unexpected quarters. Prudhoe Bay on Alaska’s North Slope held ten billion barrels — the largest field ever found in North America. Gulf Oil had been offered the acreage years earlier and refused, reasoning that “oil will never get to five dollars a barrel in our lifetime.” The eight-hundred-mile pipeline was completed in 1977. In Mexico, President Lopez Portillo cautioned that “you can’t eat more than you can digest,” then watched as production surged from 500,000 to 1.9 million barrels per day. International bankers showered Mexico with loans, naming it “borrower of the year.” The compliment would prove poisonous within a few short years.

The North Sea added another major province. Phillips Petroleum struck the giant Ekofisk field after company management had ordered its team to stop drilling. The drilling superintendent, flushed with excitement, declared: “What the astronauts have done is great, but how about this?” When British energy secretary Tony Benn turned the valve to start the flow of North Sea oil, he did so sourly, his mind on “the international capitalist establishment” that would profit. Prime Minister Harold Wilson was more cheerful, predicting that Britain would become “OPEC chairman by 1980.” The North Sea would not make Britain an oil exporter on the Saudi scale, but it would transform the nation’s balance of payments and provide a crucial buffer against future supply disruptions.

On New Year’s Eve 1977, President Carter raised his glass at a state dinner in Tehran and offered a toast to the Shah that would haunt his presidency: “Iran is an island of stability in one of the more troubled areas of the world. This is a great tribute to the respect, the admiration, and the love which your people give to you.” Shortly afterward, intelligence reports began arriving in Washington with a very different assessment: “The Shah is in big trouble.” The island of stability was built on a volcano. Within a year, it would erupt, triggering a second oil shock that would dwarf the first and destroy Carter’s presidency along with the Shah’s throne.

Chapter 33: The Second Shock

The Iranian revolution began with a newspaper article. On January 7, 1978, a government-planted story in a Tehran daily savagely attacked Ayatollah Ruhollah Khomeini, the exiled cleric who had become the focal point of religious opposition to the Shah. Theology students in Qom rioted. Police fired. Demonstrators died. Under Shia tradition, a forty-day mourning period followed, after which new demonstrations erupted, producing new deaths and new mourning cycles. This “40-40” rhythm became a ratchet that drove the revolution forward with mechanical inevitability. Each cycle was larger and angrier than the last. Oil workers joined the strikes. The lifeblood of the Iranian economy began to slow, then stop.

In the oil fields of Khuzestan, workers occupied the headquarters of Osco, the operating company, sleeping in corridors while departing Western technicians stepped over them. Production plummeted from 5.5 million barrels per day to zero by Christmas 1978. Paul Grimm , an American Osco executive, was shot dead while driving to work. The remaining foreign oil workers were evacuated under strict limits of two suitcases each. In a haunting echo of 1951, when the British had left Abadan during Mossadegh’s nationalization, the departing workers shot their dogs rather than leave them behind. The parallels with the first Iranian crisis were unmistakable, but the scale was incomparably larger.

The Shah, his power dissolving daily, described his predicament with an image of pathetic precision: “We are melting away daily like snow in water.” A telephone prankster called the palace, claiming to be Senator Kennedy. The Shah picked up the phone and heard a single command: “Mohammed, abdicate.” He left Iran on January 16, 1979, carrying a casket of Iranian soil. Khomeini returned on an Air France 747 — seats had been sold to reporters to finance the flight. The American defense attaché in Tehran sent a final cable that captured the totality of the collapse: “Army surrenders; Khomeini wins. Destroying all classified.”

The actual oil shortage caused by the Iranian revolution was modest — perhaps four to five percent of world demand. But the panic it unleashed was wildly disproportionate. Prices surged from $13 to $34 per barrel. Five factors combined to create the hysteria: apparent demand growth, the disruption of long-term contractual arrangements that marked the true end of the integrated oil industry, contradictory government policies, opportunistic behavior by exporting nations, and sheer emotion and fear. BP lost forty percent of its supply when Iran shut down and invoked force majeure, cutting deliveries to buyers around the world. Exxon announced it was phasing out third-party contracts entirely: “The handwriting had been on the wall.”

Japan was hit hardest. Japanese trading companies tried everything to secure supply. One firm gave gloves to the secretaries of Iraqi oil officials. Another provided a world-class acupuncturist for the Iraqi oil minister’s personal use. The desperation extended to ordinary consumers as well. American motorists, gripped by anxiety, began keeping their tanks three-quarters full instead of the usual quarter. This seemingly minor behavioral change sucked a billion gallons of gasoline out of the retail system, creating phantom “demand” of three million barrels per day — more than doubling the actual physical shortage. Shell received a telex invoking force majeure on a contract; that same afternoon, the identical volume was offered back at fifty percent above the previous price.

The nuclear accident at Three Mile Island in March 1979 delivered another blow, raising profound doubts about the one technology that could theoretically replace oil for electricity generation. Gas lines returned to America. An estimated 150,000 barrels of gasoline per day were wasted by cars idling in queues. President Carter attended the G-7 summit in Tokyo, where West German chancellor Helmut Schmidt “got personally abusive.” Carter found Margaret Thatcher “a tough lady, highly opinionated.” His domestic adviser Stuart Eizenstat described the period as “the worst of times.” Carter’s approval rating plummeted to twenty-five percent.

At the OPEC meeting in Caracas, Yamani spent eleven hours warning his fellow ministers that the price surge would boomerang. “There will be a glut. It is coming,” he insisted. The Iranian delegate dismissed him with theological certainty: “In the name of almighty God, there will be no surplus and prices will not fall.” Yamani was right and the Iranian was wrong, but it would take several years for the correction to arrive. In the meantime, the second oil shock would inflict even greater damage on the world economy than the first, triggering the deepest global recession since the Great Depression and reshaping the political landscape of every consuming nation.

On November 4, 1979, Iranian militants overran the American embassy in Tehran. Elizabeth Ann Swift, a political officer, reported on an open phone line: “We’re going down.” Fifty Americans were taken hostage and held for 444 humiliating days. In late November, Sunni fundamentalists seized the Great Mosque in Mecca. In December, Soviet troops invaded Afghanistan. Carter responded with the Carter Doctrine, declaring that any attempt to control the Persian Gulf would be repelled by military force. In April 1980, the Desert One rescue attempt ended in catastrophe when a helicopter collided with a C-130 transport in the Iranian desert. The CIA director warned “how vicious the struggle for energy supplies will become.” A Foreign Affairs article captured the zeitgeist with its title: “Oil and the Decline of the West.”

Chapter 34: “We’re Going Down”

On September 22, 1980, Iraqi warplanes attacked a dozen Iranian airfields, oil installations, and military bases. Saddam Hussein, shaped by the values of his hometown Tikrit — “suspicion, stealth, surprise, and the unalloyed use of force” — billed the invasion as the “Second Battle of Qadisiyah,” invoking the seventh-century Arab victory over Persia. His uncle had published a book titled Three Whom God Should Not Have Invented: Persians, Jews, and Flies — a work that captured the particular brand of hatred driving Iraqi policy. Saddam expected a quick victory over a revolutionary regime that had purged its own military. He was catastrophically wrong.

The Iran-Iraq War became the longest conventional conflict of the twentieth century. Iran’s response featured “human wave” assaults in which hundreds of thousands of young volunteers, many of them teenagers, charged Iraqi positions wearing plastic keys to heaven around their necks. Children were sent ahead of tanks to clear minefields with their bodies. The Iraqis, who had expected the Arab population of Iran’s oil-rich Khuzestan province to welcome them as liberators, found instead that the Arabs of Khuzestan showed no desire whatsoever to be “liberated.” The war ground into a savage stalemate that would consume eight years and a million lives.

The immediate impact on oil markets was dramatic. The war removed four million barrels per day from production — fifteen percent of total OPEC output. Under normal circumstances, this would have triggered a third price shock. But circumstances were no longer normal. The inventories that panicked buyers had accumulated during the second shock were now brimming. The International Energy Agency urged its members to draw down stocks rather than bid up prices. The most pointed warning came from Viscount Étienne Davignon, who told the Japanese bluntly: “If you don’t get your trading companies under control, forget about Toyotas and Sonys coming into Europe.”

Saudi Arabia responded to the crisis by pushing production to its physical limit, increasing output by an amount equivalent to a quarter of the lost Iranian and Iraqi production. Yamani described the strategy with characteristic precision: “We engineered the glut.” The Saudis understood that the second shock had pushed prices to a level that was already triggering massive conservation efforts and the development of new supply from the North Sea, Alaska, and Mexico. Every additional dollar on the price accelerated these trends. In October 1981, OPEC reunified its fragmented prices at thirty-four dollars per barrel. It would be the last OPEC price increase for an entire decade.

OPEC output in 1981 was twenty-seven percent below its 1979 level — the lowest since 1970. The cartel was losing market share at an alarming rate. Non-OPEC production was surging. Conservation was biting. The recession triggered by the second shock was crushing demand. The producing nations found themselves in a trap of their own making: the very price increases that had seemed like triumphs were now destroying the market on which their revenues depended. The oil weapon, so devastating when first employed in 1973, had proven to be double-edged. It could punish consumers in the short term, but in the long term it punished producers even more by making alternatives economically viable.

The war between Iran and Iraq dragged on with hideous intensity, swallowing resources that both nations desperately needed. Iraq used chemical weapons. Iran sent its children to die. The international community watched with a mixture of horror and cynical calculation — neither superpower was displeased to see two hostile regimes bleed each other white. The conflict destabilized insurance markets, tanker routes, and diplomatic relationships throughout the Gulf. Yet the oil continued to flow from the rest of OPEC, and the market, gorged on the inventories of panic, refused to tighten. The age of the producer had reached its zenith and was beginning an agonizing descent.

For the consuming nations, the adjustment was painful but transformative. Industries that had grown fat on cheap energy were forced to innovate or die. Japanese manufacturers led the way, turning energy efficiency into a competitive advantage that would dominate global markets for decades. American automakers, slower to adapt, would pay a steep price in lost market share. European governments poured resources into nuclear power, particularly France, which would eventually generate eighty percent of its electricity from the atom. The second shock had administered a lesson that the first had only hinted at: dependence on a single energy source controlled by unstable regimes was an existential vulnerability, not merely an economic inconvenience.

By the early 1980s, the outlines of a new energy landscape were becoming visible. Oil was losing its monopoly on the world’s energy supply. Conservation, efficiency, alternative fuels, and new production provinces were combining to erode OPEC’s dominance. The cartel’s share of world oil production was falling steadily. Prices, adjusted for inflation, were already declining. The consuming nations had learned, at enormous cost, that markets work — that high prices call forth both supply and substitution, and that the most powerful weapon against a cartel is patience combined with technology. The question was whether this lesson would be remembered when prices fell and the crisis faded from memory.

Chapter 35: Just Another Commodity?

The early 1980s witnessed the greatest oil boom in history — and the most spectacular bust. Young geology graduates were wined, dined, and hired at starting salaries of $50,000 a year. The television show Dallas introduced J.R. Ewing to a global audience as the archetype of swaggering petroleum wealth. Doctors and dentists poured their savings into drilling fund partnerships, seduced by tax shelters and the conviction that oil could only go higher. Exxon spent a billion dollars on the Colony Shale Oil project in Colorado, betting that $34 oil made synthetic fuels economically viable. By 1982, the outlook had changed so drastically that Colony was terminated overnight. Colorado boomtowns became ghost towns, following the venerable tradition of Pithole, Pennsylvania, a century earlier.

The fundamentals had turned with brutal swiftness. The Federal Reserve under Paul Volcker had pushed the prime rate to 21.5 percent, deliberately inducing the deepest recession since the Great Depression in order to break inflation. Global oil demand fell by six million barrels per day between 1979 and 1983. Non-OPEC production increased by four million. Conservation was massive and structural: the United States became twenty-five percent more energy efficient by 1985, and Japan improved by thirty-one percent. If the US had maintained 1973 levels of energy efficiency, it would have consumed thirteen million additional barrels per day in 1985. Oil’s share of total energy dropped from fifty-three percent in 1978 to forty-three percent by 1985. The tide had turned.

In March 1982, OPEC took a step it had resisted for its entire existence: it set a total output limit of eighteen million barrels per day and assigned individual quotas to each member. For the first time, OPEC had become a true cartel in the economic sense — an organization that restricted output to maintain price. Saudi Arabia accepted the role of “swing producer,” agreeing to adjust its own production up or down to keep the system in balance. In March 1983, OPEC cut its benchmark price from thirty-four to twenty-nine dollars — the first price reduction in the organization’s history. The psychological impact was enormous. For a decade, oil had seemed to move in only one direction. Now the arrow pointed down.

Just two weeks after OPEC’s historic London meeting, the New York Mercantile Exchange introduced crude oil futures trading on March 30, 1983. Nymex had been founded in 1872 as the Butter and Cheese Exchange of New York, and its previous claim to fame had been Maine potato futures. A senior exchange executive dismissed the new crude oil contract as “a way for dentists to lose money.” He was spectacularly wrong. Within months, the West Texas Intermediate benchmark had joined the gold price and the Dow Jones Industrial Average as one of the vital measures watched by markets worldwide. Maine potatoes became “a distant, quaint, and embarrassing memory.”

The bust unleashed a wave of corporate restructuring that convulsed the oil industry. T. Boone Pickens, an Amarillo independent who had once been a Phillips Petroleum trainee, exploited what he called the “value gap” — the difference between what oil companies were worth on the stock market and what their reserves were worth in the ground. His logic was pitiless: “It was cheaper to explore for oil on the floor of the New York Stock Exchange than in the ground.” The most expensive dry hole in history reinforced the point. Mukluk, a $2 billion well in Alaska, struck nothing but salt water. The geologists’ verdict was elegantly grim: they had arrived “simply 30 million years too late.”

Pickens targeted Gulf Oil itself — one of the original Seven Sisters, a pillar of the industry since Spindletop. Chevron won the takeover battle for $13.2 billion, the largest corporate acquisition in history at the time. Jimmy Lee, Gulf’s chairman, spoke with the grief of a man watching his life’s work vanish:

“I never had any intention but that Gulf would be here for ever and ever. It had been my whole life.” — Jimmy Lee, Gulf Oil

Pickens and his investor group made $760 million on the deal. Exxon responded to the restructuring wave not by buying another company but by spending $16 billion on share buybacks and cutting its workforce by forty percent. In total, the restructuring wave propelled over $100 billion into investor pockets.

The debt crisis that had been building in the developing world exploded in August 1982. Mexico had borrowed $84 billion against its oil wealth, and when prices declined, the house of cards collapsed. Finance minister Jesus Silva Herzog flew to Washington and delivered the grim news to Treasury Secretary Donald Regan: “No, Mr. Secretary, we have a problem.” At three in the morning, negotiations nearly broke down over a hidden $100 million fee. Silva Herzog, exhausted and desperate, “thought of my father and the oil expropriation of 1938.” Mexico’s crisis threatened to bring down the entire international banking system.

The epicenter of domestic financial carnage was Penn Square Bank in Oklahoma City — a go-go energy lender whose star lending officer was famous for drinking Amaretto from Gucci loafers at parties. Penn Square had generated $2 billion in energy loans and sold participations to Continental Illinois, one of the nation’s largest banks. When the energy loans went bad, Continental suffered the largest bank run in American history and had to be rescued by the federal government. Energy lending “instantan- eously went out of fashion.” The boom-bust cycle that had begun with Spindletop in 1901 and repeated itself through Pithole, Ranger, Signal Hill, and East Texas had now played out on a global scale, leaving wreckage from Oklahoma City to Mexico City to the trading floors of Manhattan.

Chapter 36: The Good Sweating

Saudi Arabia bore the crushing weight of OPEC’s attempt to defend prices. The kingdom’s oil revenues plummeted from $119 billion in 1981 to just $26 billion in 1985. Production fell to 2.2 million barrels per day — half of its assigned OPEC quota. Saudi exports to the United States dwindled to 26,000 barrels per day, which Yamani described as “essentially nothing.” At times, Saudi output actually fell below that of the British North Sea sector — an absurdity for a nation sitting on the world’s largest reserves. Yamani appeared on television: “At present we are suffering because of the weakness of Arab political power based on oil.” The kingdom that had once wielded the oil weapon was now its victim.

The problem was cheating. OPEC members systematically exceeded their quotas, selling oil at discounts through secret deals while Saudi Arabia shouldered the entire burden of production restraint. In June 1985, King Fahd lost patience and issued a sharp warning: “If Member countries feel they have a free hand to act, then all should enjoy this situation and Saudi Arabia would certainly secure its own interests.” The statement was a declaration of war — not against consuming nations but against Saudi Arabia’s own OPEC partners. After years of sacrificing revenue to hold the price line, the kingdom had decided that if no one else would play by the rules, neither would it.

The Saudi counterattack took the form of “netback deals” — contracts that guaranteed refiners a predetermined profit margin regardless of what happened to crude prices. The refiner had no incentive to seek higher prices for the finished products because the margin was locked in. The genius of the netback was that it guaranteed volume at the expense of price, flooding the market with cheap crude. It was, as one analyst noted, “increasing volume and reduced concern about selling price — a perfect recipe for falling prices.” Saudi Arabia had shifted from defense of price to defense of market share, and the consequences were immediate and devastating.

In November 1985, West Texas Intermediate peaked at $31.75 per barrel. OPEC formally declared its intention to fight for market share. Then prices collapsed. The fall was breathtaking — a seventy-percent decline that took crude to ten dollars a barrel, with some Gulf cargoes changing hands at six. The total change in OPEC output that triggered this catastrophe was marginal — roughly a three-percent increase in total global supply. It was a vivid demonstration of how fragile price structures become when they are held above their natural level by artificial restraint. A tiny release of pressure had produced an avalanche.

Shell’s scenario planners had prepared for exactly this possibility. They had developed an “Oil Collapse Scenario” and distributed it to management years in advance. When the crash came, “there was an eerie calm at Shell Centre” while competitors scrambled in panic. The broader industry responded with massive cuts in capital expenditure, workforce reductions, and project cancellations. The human cost fell heaviest on the oil patch communities of Texas, Oklahoma, Louisiana, and Alaska. At Billy Jack Mason’s Exxon station in Austin, the owner ran a promotion of zero cents per gallon — free gasoline — and attracted a line six miles long. It was a publicity stunt, but it captured the surreal new reality of an industry drowning in the commodity it produced.

Vice President George H.W. Bush, a former Texas oil man, traveled to Saudi Arabia in April 1986 to plead for price stability. “I think it is essential that we talk about stability and not just have a continued free fall like a parachutist without a parachute,” he said publicly. The Reagan White House immediately disavowed him: “The way to address price stability is to let the free market work.” Bush met privately with King Fahd in a session that stretched past two in the morning, discussing Gulf security and oil markets with the conviction of a man who understood both. “I know I’m correct,” Bush said afterward. “Some things you’re sure of.”

Yamani’s quarter-century reign as the world’s most famous oil minister ended with the abruptness of a desert sunset. Speaking at Harvard’s 350th anniversary celebration, he was asked how Saudi oil policy was formulated. “We play it by ear,” he replied, and the audience roared with laughter. In Riyadh, the remark was interpreted not as self- deprecating wit but as criticism of the royal family’s decision-making. A month later, Yamani learned he had been fired from a television broadcast — not a phone call, not a royal audience, but a news bulletin. Twenty-four years as oil minister, the most consequential tenure in petroleum history — “a good, long run in any position anywhere.”

In December 1986, OPEC agreed to a “reference price” of eighteen dollars per barrel. An OPEC historian recalled a 1926 book about the early American oil industry in which producers were said to be committing “hara-kiri” through overproduction. The parallels were exact. The new price effectively wiped out the entire second oil shock increase. Consumers received what amounted to an “OPEC tax cut” of fifty billion dollars in 1986 alone. Meanwhile, the Iran-Iraq War finally ground to a halt in July 1988. Khomeini accepted the cease-fire with bitter anguish:

“Taking this decision was more deadly than taking poison.” — Ayatollah Khomeini

Eight years, a million dead, and neither side had gained an inch. Iraq emerged believing it was “the dominant political power in the Gulf.” That delusion would lead to catastrophe within two years.

Epilogue

In the aftermath of the price collapse, the major oil companies and the state-owned enterprises of the producing nations began a quiet process of reintegration that would have seemed impossible a decade earlier. The exporting countries, having seized control of production, now moved downstream into refining and marketing. Venezuela built refineries in the United States. Kuwait purchased gas stations across Europe under the brand name “Q-8” and acquired twenty-two percent of BP. Saudi Arabia bought half of Texaco’s American retail network. Kuwait had effectively transformed itself from a raw-material exporter into an integrated oil company. The wheel had come full circle: the nations that had expelled the majors were now becoming majors themselves.

Britain, where the modern oil industry had been shaped by government intervention more than anywhere else, reversed course under Margaret Thatcher. She sold the government’s stake in BP, undoing what Winston Churchill had done in 1914 when he acquired a controlling interest to secure fuel for the Royal Navy. Churchill had believed that oil was too important to be left to private enterprise; Thatcher believed the opposite. The privatization symbolized a broader ideological shift that was sweeping the Western world — the conviction that markets, not governments, should allocate resources, even resources as strategically vital as petroleum. The debate that had raged since the industry’s birth — who should control oil? — remained as unresolved as ever.

At the 1985 G-7 summit in Bonn, the leaders of the industrialized world gathered and issued their communiqué. Not a single word was devoted to oil. A decade earlier, petroleum had consumed every agenda, dominated every bilateral meeting, and haunted every economic projection. Now it merited no mention at all. The crisis appeared to be over. Prices were stable. Supply was abundant. The consuming nations had diversified their energy sources, improved their efficiency, and built strategic reserves. The terror of 1973 and 1979 had receded into the comfortable past. It seemed that oil had become just another commodity — important, certainly, but no longer the geopolitical weapon that had held the world hostage.

But the deeper patterns of the oil industry — the boom-bust cycles, the political volatility of producing regions, the consuming world’s chronic inability to sustain conservation efforts when prices fell — had not changed at all. OPEC’s share of world production had declined, but its reserves remained dominant. The Middle East still held the majority of the world’s proven oil. The infrastructure of Hydrocarbon Man was, if anything, more sprawling and more dependent on uninterrupted supply than ever before. The vulnerabilities that had produced the shocks of the 1970s had been mitigated but not eliminated. The question was not whether another crisis would come, but when.

At a 1989 discussion, an oil minister from a Gulf state was asked how long the lessons of the 1970s and 1980s would be remembered by consuming nations. His answer was precise and prophetic: “About three years, without reminding.” Within a year, that minister was no longer in office. A month after that, his country was invaded. The minister was Kuwaiti. The invader was Saddam Hussein. The cycle that Daniel Yergin had traced across a century — from Colonel Drake’s well at Titusville to Spindletop, from the Red Line to Aramco, from Mossadegh to the Shah, from the oil weapon to the oil glut — was about to begin again.

The story of oil is ultimately a story about the collision between geology and politics, between the underground distribution of a finite resource and the aboveground ambitions of the nations, companies, and individuals who fight to control it. The great fields of the Middle East were created by the same tectonic forces that shaped the continents, but their exploitation was shaped by human decisions — by concession-hunters and wildcatters, by kings and revolutionaries, by engineers and financiers. Every chapter of the oil industry’s history has been written in the tension between the resource’s physical reality and the political structures imposed upon it. That tension has never been resolved. It can only be managed, and then only temporarily.

Hydrocarbon Man emerged from the twentieth century more dependent on oil than ever, despite two devastating price shocks, a global recession, and the rise of alternative energy sources. The automobile, the airplane, the petrochemical plant, and the fertilizer factory remained powered overwhelmingly by petroleum. The developing world was only beginning its own love affair with the internal combustion engine. China and India, with their billions of consumers, were poised to demand quantities of oil that would dwarf the consumption of the Western economies. The era of cheap oil might be over, as countless analysts had proclaimed, but the era of oil itself showed no sign of ending. The prize remained what it had always been: not merely a commodity but the strategic resource on which modern civilization depended.

The genius of Yergin’s chronicle lies in its recognition that oil has never been just an economic commodity. It has been an instrument of empire, a weapon of war, a catalyst for revolution, and the foundation of the most prosperous civilization in human history. The men who shaped its story — Rockefeller and Deterding , Churchill and Ibn Saud, Mattei and Yamani, Kissinger and the Shah — were as diverse as humanity itself, united only by their understanding that whoever controlled oil controlled the future. That understanding has not changed. The prize endures.

Trading & Investing Lessons

Commodity Cycles Are Driven by Psychology

Every oil boom in this book — Pithole in the 1860s, East Texas in 1930, Libya in the 1960s, the shale boom of the 1980s — follows an identical arc. Scarcity drives prices up, high prices attract a flood of capital and innovation, oversupply crashes prices, and the crash drives out investment until scarcity returns. The lag between investment decisions and actual production creates violent swings that reward traders who understand the cycle’s rhythm and destroy those caught at either extreme. Pithole went from forest to 15,000 people and back to nothing. Colorado shale boomtowns became ghost towns when Exxon killed Colony. The cycle never changes — only the names and the scale.

Geopolitical Risk Is Permanent and Underpriced

Markets consistently discount geopolitical threats to oil supply until those threats materialize in catastrophic fashion. Washington greeted King Faisal’s 1973 warning with “a large degree of disbelief.” Carter toasted the Shah as an “island of stability” months before revolution. The CIA on October 5, 1973 reported that “military preparations do not indicate that any party intends to initiate hostilities.” The minister who predicted three years of memory lost his country to invasion. Energy positions should always carry a geopolitical risk premium — the cost of being wrong about stability is far greater than the cost of hedging against disruption.

Spare Capacity Is the Master Variable

The single most dangerous condition in oil markets is not any specific event but the erosion of spare production capacity. In 1973, surplus available capacity had fallen to 500,000 barrels per day — just 1% of world consumption. At that utilization rate, any disruption became catastrophic because there was no buffer to absorb the shock. The Texas Railroad Commission had opened to full production for the first time, and there was nowhere left to turn. Conversely, Saudi Arabia’s massive spare capacity in the 1980s eventually gave it the power to crash prices at will. Monitor spare capacity as the single most important leading indicator for energy markets.

The Swing Producer Always Suffers

Saudi Arabia nearly bankrupted itself in the 1980s defending OPEC prices while every other member cheated on their quotas. Saudi production fell from over 10 million barrels per day to 2.2 million — at times below British North Sea output — while revenues collapsed from $119 billion to $26 billion. The swing producer absorbs all the pain of market adjustment while free riders enjoy the benefits. This dynamic applies to any cartel or dominant market maker: the entity that adjusts production to stabilize prices bears disproportionate cost while those who maintain output at its expense profit. The Texas Railroad Commission played this role domestically before OPEC inherited it internationally.

Control of Resources Is Temporary

Every major oil concession in this book was eventually nationalized. D’Arcy’s 1901 Persian concession was the first granted and the first canceled. The Seven Sisters lost their entire Middle Eastern empire between 1951 and 1976. Standard Oil’s monopoly was broken by antitrust. Rockefeller, Deterding, Gulbenkian, the Aramco partners — none achieved permanent control. Companies that build strategy around the assumption of perpetual access to strategic resources are building on sand. The only durable advantage is adaptability — the ability to pivot when the political ground shifts, as it always does.

Panic Creates More Shortage Than the Event

The second oil shock of 1979 proved that panic buying can double or triple the effective shortage far beyond the actual physical disruption. Iranian production fell by about 4–5% of world demand, but motorists keeping their tanks three-quarters full instead of one-quarter sucked a billion gallons from gas stations, adding three million barrels per day of phantom demand. Shell received a force majeure telex cutting supply in the morning; that afternoon, the exact same volume was offered at 50% higher on the spot market. Behavioral cascades — not geology — are the real price driver in crisis. The lesson for traders: always fade the panic, never join it. The physical shortage is always smaller than it appears.

Key Takeaways

Oil Is Never Just a Commodity

Oil functions simultaneously as a commercial product subject to supply and demand, a strategic asset essential to military power, and a political weapon that can be deployed against nations. Churchill’s conversion of the Royal Navy showed oil as a strategic asset. The 1973 embargo showed it as a weapon. The futures markets of the 1980s treated it as a commodity. Every attempt to treat oil as purely one of these three things fails — the other dimensions always reassert themselves at the worst possible moment.

Technology Creates and Destroys Oil Empires

Drake’s drilling replaced surface skimming. Samuel’s tankers killed geographic advantage. The internal combustion engine made gasoline the dominant product. Synthetic fuels sustained Nazi Germany for years. Catalytic cracking produced 100- octane fuel that won the Battle of Britain. Conservation after 1973 broke OPEC’s grip more effectively than any military action could have. NYMEX futures transformed pricing from a negotiated process to a market-determined one. Each technological innovation reshuffled the power hierarchy completely. The next disruption will come from a technology that today’s incumbents are dismissing.

The Oil Curse Is Real

Venezuela, Iran, Mexico, Nigeria, Iraq — oil wealth corroded institutions, fueled authoritarianism, and ultimately impoverished the very countries that depended on it. The Shah hurtled Iran toward his “Great Civilization” and created the disorientation that fueled revolution. Gomez turned Venezuela into a personal hacienda. Saddam Hussein’s oil-funded military machine led him to invade two neighbors. Perez Alfonzo’s “excrement of the devil” proved devastatingly prophetic. Only countries that treated oil wealth as a temporary windfall to be invested wisely — rather than a permanent entitlement to be spent lavishly — escaped the curse.

Surplus Masks Fragility

Periods of abundant supply breed complacency that makes the next crisis inevitable. At the 1985 Bonn summit, not a single word about oil appeared in the communiqué. Five years later, Iraq invaded Kuwait. The minister who was asked how long the lessons would last said “about three years, without reminding.” Within a year his country was invaded. Markets and governments consistently forget how precarious energy security is. The surplus itself creates the illusion of permanence, and the illusion persists until reality intrudes.

Integration Is the Winning Strategy

From Rockefeller’s control of refining, transportation, and marketing through Aramco’s wellhead-to-tanker operations through the state companies’ downstream expansion into Western refineries and gas stations, the most durable competitive advantage in oil has always been vertical integration — controlling the chain from wellhead to gas pump. Venezuela built U.S. refineries. Kuwait bought 22% of BP and gas stations. Saudi Arabia acquired half of Texaco’s American stations. The producing countries learned what Rockefeller knew a century earlier: whoever controls the full chain controls the margin.

Contrarian Ideas

The 1911 Dissolution Helped Standard Oil

The thirty-four successor companies, freed to compete against each other and invade each other’s territories, collectively became far more valuable than the unified trust had been. Rockefeller’s personal wealth actually increased after the breakup, as each successor company grew independently. Phillips opened 6,750 stations by 1930; Texaco and Shell marketed in all forty-eight states. The competitive energy that Rockefeller had suppressed within the trust was unleashed by dissolution. Ida Tarbell herself was astonished: “It certainly looks as if the Standard Oil Company might be crumbling — crumbling within.”

Conservation, Not Alternative Energy, Broke OPEC

The popular narrative credits solar, nuclear, and other alternative energy sources with reducing Western dependence on OPEC oil. The reality is different. The response to the 1970s price shocks was overwhelmingly efficiency — the United States became 25% more energy efficient by 1985, Japan 31%. If the U.S. had stayed at 1973 efficiency levels, it would have consumed 13 million more barrels daily in 1985. Oil demand fell six million barrels per day between 1979 and 1983, while non-OPEC production increased by four million. Conservation was the “hidden fuel” that broke OPEC’s pricing power far more decisively than any supply-side alternative.

OPEC Was Irrelevant for Its First Decade

From its founding in 1960 to 1970, OPEC accomplished essentially nothing. Companies dismissed it; a CIA report gave it four lines. The real power shift had nothing to do with OPEC meetings in Baghdad or Vienna. It was structural: U.S. production peaked in 1970, eliminating the world’s spare capacity cushion. Britain withdrew from the Persian Gulf, removing the security guarantee. Environmental regulation blocked Alaskan development. These structural changes — entirely unrelated to OPEC’s existence — created the conditions that allowed the 1973 embargo to succeed. OPEC was the beneficiary of circumstances, not the architect of them.

Pearl Harbor Failed on Its Own Terms

Japan fought the entire Pacific War for oil. Every strategic decision — the southern advance, the attack on the Dutch East Indies, the confrontation with America — was driven by petroleum dependence. Yet when planning the Pearl Harbor attack itself, the planners forgot to target the oil storage facilities. Admiral Nimitz calculated that destroying the 4.5 million barrels of oil stored at Pearl Harbor would have prolonged the war by two years. Yamamoto himself had noted that Japan’s 1904 surprise attack on Port Arthur had not been “thoroughgoing enough.” The same mistake was made again. The war of oil was lost at the very moment of oil.

High Oil Prices Are Self-Correcting

Every major price spike in this book triggered the forces that eventually destroyed it — conservation, new production from previously uneconomic sources, substitution of alternative fuels, and economic recession that reduced demand. The Shah who pushed prices to $11.65 in 1973 was overthrown partly because the oil wealth destabilized Iran. The $34 oil of 1980 triggered conservation that crashed demand by six million barrels per day. The oil weapon invariably turned on those who wielded it, because the market response to high prices — though slow — is overwhelming and irreversible.

Proposals & Frameworks

Three Themes of Oil History

Yergin identifies three interwoven strands that animate the entire story. The first is oil as the foundation of capitalism and modern business — Standard Oil was among the world’s first multinationals, and the industry embodied the twentieth-century evolution from wildcatters to corporate bureaucracies to state-owned companies. The second is oil as a commodity intimately intertwined with national strategies, global politics, and military power — from Churchill’s Navy through both world wars to the 1973 embargo. The third illuminates how ours has become a “Hydrocarbon Society” — oil makes possible where we live, how we work, how we travel, and even where we conduct our courtships.

The Commodity-Strategic Duality

Oil oscillates between periods when it behaves like an ordinary commodity subject to supply and demand, and periods when it becomes a strategic weapon subject to political will. The transition between these modes is always sudden and always underestimated. At the 1985 Bonn summit, oil was a non-issue; five years later, half a million troops were deployed to the Persian Gulf. The duality means that purely economic analysis of oil markets is always incomplete — the political dimension can override market fundamentals at any moment, as it did in 1973, 1979, and 1990.

The Concession Lifecycle

Every major oil concession follows an identical arc: discovery by foreign companies, development that creates enormous wealth, rising nationalism as the producing country demands a larger share, renegotiation under duress, and eventual nationalization. D’Arcy’s 1901 Persian concession was the first granted and the first canceled. The Saudi concession that cost $175,000 in 1933 was nationalized forty-three years later when proven reserves were 149 billion barrels. The lifecycle is driven by the fundamental political reality that no sovereign nation will permanently tolerate foreign control of its most valuable resource.

The Price Spiral

Price increases trigger conservation and new supply, which trigger price collapse, which triggers complacency and underinvestment, which triggers the next crisis when demand eventually catches up. The cycle length is roughly a decade — from the 1973 shock to the 1986 collapse was thirteen years; from the 1986 collapse to the 1990 Kuwait invasion was four years. The spiral is driven by the long lead times in oil investment: decisions made during a price spike produce supply five to ten years later, long after the conditions that justified them have disappeared.

What Didn’t Fit

The Pine Root Campaign

In the final desperate months of the Pacific War, Japan launched a nationwide campaign to extract aviation fuel from pine tree roots. The government promised that two hundred pine roots would keep a plane in the air for one hour. Schoolchildren and civilians were mobilized across the country. Each gallon of pine root fuel required 2.5 man-days of backbreaking labor. The total output of the entire campaign was a mere 3,000 barrels — and not a single drop was ever actually tested in an aircraft. When American occupation forces tried the fuel in captured jeeps, it gummed up the engines beyond use. The pine root campaign remains the supreme example of the gap between ideological determination and physical reality: you cannot will hydrocarbons into existence.

Penn Square and the Gucci Loafers

The 1980s oil bust destroyed far more than drilling companies and wildcatters. Penn Square Bank in Oklahoma City, a small shopping-mall bank whose star lending officer Bill Patterson was famous for drinking Amaretto from Gucci loafers at closing parties, generated over $2 billion in energy loans that it sold to larger institutions, primarily Continental Illinois of Chicago. When oil prices collapsed, the loans went bad. Continental Illinois suffered the largest bank run in American history, requiring a federal bailout that essentially nationalized the bank. Energy lending “instantaneously went out of fashion.” The episode proved that oil booms create not just industrial excess but financial contagion — and that the most dangerous moment is when bankers start believing the commodity price can only go up.

Getty’s Pay Phone and the $1,200 Geologist

J. Paul Getty, identified by Fortune magazine as the richest man in the world, installed a pay telephone for guests at his English manor — Sutton Place, a Tudor estate he had purchased for a fraction of its value. Married five times, he fired all employees during the Depression and rehired them at lower salaries. After the Nazi seizure of Austria, he entered the Rothschild home in Vienna to examine the furniture. His geologist Paul Walton spotted the Saudi Neutral Zone discovery from a DC-3, recognizing a surface mound that resembled Kuwait’s Burgan field. Five dry holes followed before the sixth struck “somewhere between colossal and history-making.” Walton received $1,200 for one of the greatest oil discoveries of the century. Getty: “If you can count your money, you don’t have a billion dollars.”

The Suez Sixpence and Literary Consolation

During the Suez crisis, Harold Macmillan read thousands of pages of novels in two weeks to keep from going “barmy” as the political situation deteriorated around him. After succeeding the broken Eden as Prime Minister, he soothed his soul with Jane Austen’s Pride and Prejudice. Belgium banned all private driving on Sundays. Britain imposed gasoline rationing — the ration was popularly known as the “Suez sixpence.” Eden, retired and destroyed, prayed for “a lake of oil” under the North Sea. That was exactly what Phillips Petroleum was beginning to find with its Ekofisk discovery — vindicating Eden’s prayer a decade too late to save his career or his reputation.

Carlos at OPEC Vienna

In December 1975, the Venezuelan terrorist Ilich Ramírez Sánchez — known as “Carlos the Jackal” — led an armed assault on OPEC headquarters in Vienna during a ministers’ meeting. The attackers burst in during a session, killing three people. Sheikh Yamani spent forty-four hours with his worry beads, convinced he was going to die. The attackers demanded a plane, flew to Algeria with their hostages, and eventually surrendered in exchange for safe passage. The episode demonstrated that oil’s new wealth had made OPEC ministers targets of revolutionary violence — the prize was now so vast that even international terrorists came calling. Yamani never fully recovered from the experience, and it reinforced his lifelong conviction that oil wealth was as dangerous as it was valuable.
