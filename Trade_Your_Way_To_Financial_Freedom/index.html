Glossary

Core System Concepts

Expectancy: The average amount you can expect to make per dollar risked over many trades. Calculated as (probability of win x average win) minus (probability of loss x average loss). A system with 36% wins but large winners can have higher expectancy than one with 60% wins. This is the single most important metric for evaluating any trading system.

R-Multiple: Every trade's profit or loss expressed as a multiple of initial risk (R). If you risk $500 and make $1,500, that is a 3R trade. If you lose $1,000, that is a -2R trade. Normalizing trades this way lets you compare systems regardless of market or instrument.

Position Sizing: The algorithm that determines how large a position you take at any given time. Tharp considers this the most important element of system design after psychology, yet virtually every trading book neglects it.

Setup: Conditions that must occur before other action is taken. Tharp argues setups represent only 10% of a system's importance, yet traders devote 90% of their energy to finding the "right" setup.

Positive Expectancy: A system that will make money over the long term if played at a low enough risk level. Even random entry can produce positive expectancy when combined with proper exits and position sizing.

Entry & Exit Techniques

Channel Breakout: Enter when price makes a new high (long) or low (short) for the last N days. The Turtles famously used 20-day breakouts, later shifting to 40+ days as markets adapted.

Volatility Breakout: Enter when price moves a specific multiple of the average true range from the previous close. A 0.8x ATR move signals potential trend continuation.

ADX (Average Directional Index): Developed by Wilder; measures trend strength (not direction). ADX above 15 indicates a trending market. Rising ADX prevents oscillator-based entries from working properly.

Maximum Adverse Excursion (MAE): The maximum loss a position experiences during its lifetime. Winning trades rarely have MAE exceeding 1.5x ATR, while losing trades frequently do. This insight allows tighter stops.

Trailing Stop: A stop-loss order that moves with the prevailing trend, typically used to protect profits. Wilder's 3x ATR trailing stop is the most common baseline.

Position Sizing Models

Percent Risk Model: Size positions so that risk per trade equals a fixed percentage of equity. At 1% risk on a $1M account, maximum loss per trade is $10,000. Produced the highest returns in Tharp's comparative testing.

Percent Volatility Model: Size positions based on each instrument's volatility as a percentage of equity. High-volatility instruments get smaller positions, low-volatility get larger. Equalizes portfolio fluctuations.

Anti-Martingale Strategy: Position size increases when winning (equity grows, so dollar risk grows proportionally). All equity-based sizing models are anti-martingale. Contrast with martingale (doubling after losses), which leads to ruin.

Psychology & Biases

Conservative-with-Profits / Risky-with-Losses Bias: 80% of people prefer a sure $9,000 over a 95% chance of $10,000 (cutting profits), yet also prefer a 95% chance of losing $10,000 over a sure $9,000 loss (letting losses run). This is the exact opposite of what works.

Gambler's Fallacy: The belief that after a losing streak, a win is "due." Leads traders to increase position size after losses and decrease after wins, which is backwards.

Degrees of Freedom: Each parameter or rule added to a system gives it another degree of freedom to fit past data. Ten rules with three values each produce 59,049 combinations, virtually guaranteeing a curve-fitted result.

Chapter 1: The Legend of the Holy Grail

The book opens with a seductive promise: take yesterday's true range, bracket today's price by 40% of that range, and you have an 80% chance of making money tomorrow. It sounds like the Holy Grail of trading. But Tharp immediately dismantles the fantasy by listing everything this "system" fails to address. How do you protect capital when the trade goes wrong? When do you take profits? How much do you buy or sell? Which markets should you trade? A single entry signal, no matter how accurate, is not a trading system. It is a fragment of one, and treating fragments as complete systems is the foundational error that destroys most traders.

Tharp frames the trader's journey through Joseph Campbell's hero mythology. The wasteland in Campbell's telling is the inauthentic life of following the crowd, chasing the same indicators everyone else uses, buying the same newsletter picks, attending the same seminars on high-probability entry signals. The Grail is not a magic system handed down from above. It is self-knowledge, the discovery of a methodology that fits your temperament, your time horizon, your psychological makeup. Jack Schwager reached the same conclusion after interviewing dozens of market wizards: the single most important characteristic of successful traders was that each had found a system right for them personally.

"People make money in the markets by finding themselves, achieving their potential, and getting in tune with the market." Van Tharp

The allocation of importance Tharp assigns is deliberately provocative: psychology accounts for 60% of trading success, position sizing for 30%, and system development for just 10%. He illustrates this with Ed Seykota's famous college trading course, where Seykota spent one week teaching a simple moving-average crossover system and then eight weeks trying to convince the students to accept the losses the system would inevitably generate. The technique was trivial. The psychological work of trusting it through drawdowns was not.

Tharp developed his coaching methodology through Neuro-Linguistic Programming (NLP), created by Richard Bandler and John Grinder. Rather than modeling a single genius trader, he studied the common elements across many successful traders and investors. Three patterns emerged: all great traders had the ability to find low-risk ideas, all had disciplined research processes, and all understood position sizing at a deep level. Institutional traders he met on an Asian tour mostly failed the last test; they could not answer how much they could lose before losing their jobs.

The chapter closes with a compound-interest lesson that doubles as a psychological mirror. Saving just one dollar per day at 15% tax-free from age twenty makes you a millionaire at retirement. Yet 65 million American families carry revolving credit card debt averaging $7,000 at 18% interest, compounding in the wrong direction. The arithmetic of wealth is simple. The psychology of executing it is where the real work begins, and that is what the rest of the book is about.

Chapter 2: Judgmental Biases

Every trader believes they are trading the market. They are not. They are trading their beliefs about the market, and those beliefs are riddled with systematic errors. Information doubles every year. The conscious mind can process five to nine chunks at a time. To cope with this mismatch, we generalize, delete, and distort information in predictable ways. Karl Popper observed that progress in knowledge comes more from efforts to find fault with our theories than from efforts to prove them, but traders do the opposite. They seek confirmation of what they already believe and filter out everything else.

Tharp catalogs fourteen biases across three domains: system development, system testing, and live trading. The representation bias is the most foundational. A daily bar chart is just a line on paper, not the market itself. We accept it as meaningful because everyone uses it, because we were taught it, because data comes pre-formatted that way. But the map is not the territory. The reliability bias compounds this: we assume our data is accurate when every database Tharp has examined contains errors, from stock splits not accounted for to bad prints and missing data points.

The lotto bias drives the desire to pick tops and bottoms, to buy low and sell high, when the real money is in riding big moves and cutting losses quickly. The law of small numbers leads traders to perceive patterns after just three occurrences; seminar speakers sell entire systems based on five or ten cherry-picked examples. Conservatism bias means that once a belief forms, it can take months to accept contradicting evidence. The need-to-understand bias makes us find explanations where none exist. Financial news tells you why the market moved after the fact, but those explanations are fabricated stories layered onto random noise.

In system testing, degrees of freedom become the silent killer. Each parameter added to a system gives it another way to fit past data. Ten rules with three values each create 59,049 possible combinations, virtually guaranteeing that at least one will look spectacular on historical data and fail in real time. Postdictive error uses information that would not have been available at the time of the trade, such as today's close in a calculation that must happen before the close. Failing to include transaction costs rounds out the testing biases, creating an illusion of profitability that evaporates when commissions and slippage hit real capital.

The trading biases are the most psychologically devastating. The conservative-with-profits, risky-with-losses bias explains why 80% of people will take a sure $9,000 over a 95% chance of $10,000 (risk aversion with gains) while simultaneously preferring a 95% chance of losing $10,000 over a sure $9,000 loss (risk seeking with losses). This is the behavioral foundation for cutting profits short and letting losses run. Ralph Vince demonstrated the power of this bias in an experiment where forty Ph.D.s played a game with 60% probability of winning, each starting with $1,000. Only two of the forty ended with more than $1,000. Optimal betting at 20% of equity would have averaged $7,490. Even brilliant people cannot execute a positive-expectancy game without discipline.

"The golden rule of trading — 'Cut your losses short and let your profits run' — has nothing to do with entry and everything to do with exits." Van Tharp

Chapter 3: Setting Your Objectives

At an O'Hare Airport consultation, a trader named Sam laid out his goals: he wanted to buy his wife a $15,000 car from trading profits within three months, using a $10,000 account. That requires a 150% return, annualized to roughly 1,000%. His maximum acceptable loss? Ten percent, or $1,000. Sam wanted a 15-to-1 reward-to-risk ratio. Tharp told him he had never seen anything close; three-to-one is usually very good. Most beginning traders with small accounts carry similarly unrealistic expectations, and the gap between ambition and reality is where accounts go to die.

Tharp's most counterintuitive claim in the entire book lands here: designing objectives should occupy at least 50% of your system-development time. A CTA expert he consulted agreed. Yet at seminars, when Tharp assigned objectives exercises, participants grumbled. They wanted indicators, entry signals, the dopamine of discovering the next setup. Nobody wanted to sit with a blank page and answer hard questions about themselves: What is your edge? What return do you realistically need? What drawdown would make you stop trading? How much time can you devote? What are your computer skills? Your emotional vulnerabilities?

Tom Basso, president of Trendstat, Inc. in Scottsdale, Arizona, serves as the model practitioner. Schwager nicknamed him "Mr. Serenity" in New Market Wizards. Basso managed $95 million (later growing to half a billion) and needed $80,000 a year to live, none of which came from trading profits. His self-assessment was surgically honest: strategic thinking and patience were his edges, not prediction or speed. He expected 40% returns, would tolerate a 25% worst-case drawdown, and risked 0.8-1.0% per trade with clients, noting he would push to 1-1.5% with his own money but that 2-3% would "push the envelope." The entry, he said, was the least important component.

The lesson from Basso is that objectives are not wishes. They are engineering constraints. A system that targets 40% returns with 25% maximum drawdown is a fundamentally different machine than one targeting 150% with 10% drawdown. The components you select, the markets you trade, the position sizing you use, and the exits you design all flow from these constraints. If you skip the objectives step, you are building a machine without knowing what it is supposed to do. Tharp warns that emotional responses to the objectives questions reveal psychological issues that must be addressed before real capital is at risk.

Chapter 4: Steps to Developing a System

Tharp distills his modeling of hundreds of successful traders into a twelve-step framework. The NLP foundation means this is not a theoretical model but an empirical one, built by interviewing people who actually do it well and extracting the common elements. Step one is a self-inventory: skills, temperament, time, capital, computer ability, math ability, tolerance for loss. If you have a full-time job, a long-term system with end-of-day data is your only realistic option, requiring just thirty minutes per night. If you are managing other people's money, their psychology gets layered on top of yours. One client's withdrawal pattern told the whole story: withdraw after two losing months, wait for five winning months to reinvest, then hit two more losing months. The client always lost even though the manager made money.

Step two demands opening your mind and gathering market information with fresh eyes. Charles LeBeau discovered that when he began computerized testing, hundreds of his long-held market beliefs failed. Everything you have been taught is a belief, not a fact, and the useful question is not whether a belief is true but whether a more useful belief exists. Steps three through six cover objectives (again, 20-50% of total effort), time-frame selection, studying the best historical moves, and converting those moves into a measurable concept. A random entry gives 45-55% reliability; your concept should push that above 55%, especially over one-to-five-day windows.

Steps seven and eight add stops and profit-taking exits, which will typically drag your initial 60% reliability down to 50-55%. Step nine offers an alternative path: instead of optimizing reliability, hunt for huge R-multiple trades. Accept a 25% hit rate if one 12.5R winner covers three 0.75R losses. A live cattle breakout example drove the point home: three entries at $0.75 loss each, then a $10 profit for a 12.5R multiple. One trade like that can absorb seven consecutive 1R losses and still be profitable.

Step ten is where everything changes: position sizing. Tharp calls it the most important part of any system. Three graduates of his program illustrate: Webster Management became the top-ranked fund by reward-to-risk ratio, Maricopa Asset Management compounded at 40% per year with hardly a losing month, and Ray Kelly compounded at 40%+ for eight years with only two losing months. Steps eleven and twelve cover system improvement (adding independent markets, combining noncorrelated systems) and worst-case mental planning. Basso's exercise for the last step: imagine taking the other side of each trade you put on, and ask yourself how that person feels.

Chapter 5: Selecting a Concept That Works

Fewer than 20% of market participants have a system, and most of those who do simply use predefined indicators out of the box. A chaos theory expert told Tharp that systems are impossible, that trading is all luck and psychology. Tharp agreed with one caveat: if you define a system as just an entry technique, then yes, it is hopeless. But a complete methodology with positive expectancy through stops, exits, and position sizing is not only possible but demonstrably profitable. Any concept can work if you build the right framework around it.

Trend following, explained by Tom Basso, is the most intuitive concept: wait for prices to begin moving in a direction, then follow. The advantage is that you never miss a major move. The disadvantage is that markets only trend 15-25% of the time, so you must endure many false starts. The S&P futures market, opened in 1982, produced abnormally large price changes within five years that a random distribution would predict only once per century, confirming that trends are real phenomena driven by supply and demand fundamentals, not statistical artifacts.

Fundamental analysis, covered by Charles LeBeau, offers something technical analysis cannot: price objectives. Fundamentals can tell you not just direction but approximate magnitude. The limitation is timing; results are "painfully imprecise." LeBeau's practical wisdom: news follows price direction while fundamental analysis predicts it. Media contacts after the close asking why the market moved simply gather bullish or bearish commentary to match the day's direction. "Buy the rumor, sell the fact" works because anticipation moves prices more than confirmation. Of eight to ten fundamental forecasts per year, six or seven will materialize; position yourself in half and you will have an extremely profitable year.

Seasonal tendencies, explained by Jerry Toepke of Moore Research Center, exploit recurring annual cycles. Heating oil hits its seasonal low in July and rises into winter demand. Unleaded gasoline dips in winter and rallies into the summer driving season. Treasury bonds tend to rise October through May as fiscal-year liquidity shifts. The critical distinction is that patterns driven by identifiable fundamentals are tradable, while patterns based on pure statistical correlation, like the Super Bowl indicator, are not.

Spreading, by LIFFE floor trader Kevin Thomas, involves simultaneous long and short positions in related markets for lower risk and margin. Arbitrage, by Ray Kelly, is the art of correcting inefficiencies. Kelly averaged 40-60% returns annually from 1987 to 1994 with only one losing month. His snow-blower story captures the mindset: during a blizzard, a teenager bought a $265 snow blower on his father's credit card, cleared eleven driveways for $550, sold the blower back for $200, and netted $485 by 8 PM. Arbitrage is not about predicting anything; it is about seeing price discrepancies that others do not and moving faster.

Neural networks, covered by Louis Mendelsohn, use intermarket data from ten related markets as inputs to predict tomorrow's high, low, and trend direction. The chapter closes with Tharp's own section on market order: Elliott Wave theory, sunspot cycles, Fibonacci numbers, and Gann analysis. His verdict is pragmatic rather than mystical. Even a 48-to-52% edge is card-counter territory. If any of these methods can deliver 55% reliability when combined with sound exits and position sizing, they are worth using.

Chapter 6: Understanding Expectancy

A client's reaction to the expectancy chapter summed up the industry's blind spot: "Oh, no... I don't think expectancy is one of our edges." Yet expectancy is one of the most important trade secrets in existence. Tharp identifies six variables that determine investment success: reliability (hit rate), relative size of profits versus losses, cost of trading, opportunity (frequency), size of trading capital, and position-sizing model. Most people obsess over the first variable. A system with a 1% win rate can still be profitable if the one winner is enormous.

The snow fight metaphor makes the abstraction physical. You are hiding behind a wall of snow (your capital). White snowballs are winning trades that build the wall. Black snowballs are losing trades that dissolve it. The size of each snowball represents the relative size of wins and losses. Transaction costs slightly damage the wall on every throw regardless of color. Frequency determines how many snowballs arrive per minute. And position sizing determines whether 10,000 snowballs hit simultaneously or one at a time. If white snow volume exceeds black, the wall grows. That is positive expectancy.

Two marble games demonstrate why win rate is misleading. Game one has 60 blue (win) and 40 black (lose) marbles, each paying 1:1. Expectancy is $0.20 per dollar risked. Game two has 100 marbles across seven colors: 64% of draws lose, but the winning marbles pay 5:1, 10:1, and 20:1. Despite winning only 36% of the time, game two has an expectancy of $0.78 per dollar risked, nearly four times better than game one. The win rate is half the story. The payout structure is the other half, and it is the half most traders ignore entirely.

A 90%-accurate system sounds like a dream until you look at the numbers: it wins $275 on average but loses $2,700 on average. Expectancy = (0.9 x 275) - (0.1 x 2,700) = negative $22.50 per trade. You get to be right 90% of the time and you still go broke. This is the prediction trap, and it catches traders who define success as being right rather than making money.

R-multiples provide the normalization framework. Every trade's outcome is expressed as a multiple of initial risk. A natural gas trade entered at 2,511 with a stop at 2,407 (risk = 104 points) and exited at 3,069 produced a profit of 558 points, or a 5.37R multiple. By converting all trades to R-multiples, you can compare systems, track expectancy, and size positions coherently. Even with positive expectancy, oversizing positions can produce ruin. Risk 100% on the first draw of a 60% game and there is a 40% chance of immediate bankruptcy. Risk 50% and three losses in a row (possible roughly one in five attempts) reduces $100 to $12.50, requiring a 700% gain just to break even.

"The key factor in determining the value of your system is its expectancy per dollar risked." Van Tharp

The opportunity factor adds another dimension. A game with $0.78 expectancy but one draw every five minutes generates $9.36 per hour. A game with $0.20 expectancy but one draw per minute generates $12.00 per hour. The lower-expectancy, higher-frequency game actually wins. Expectancy times opportunity equals practical value, and this is why market makers with tiny per-trade edges can generate enormous daily income from hundreds of transactions.

Chapter 7: Using Setups

An Elliott Wave expert was right 70% of the time on his market calls but made money on only 30% of his actual trades. The disconnect was profound. His tight stops kept getting hit, and after being stopped out, he was too nervous to re-enter. He had confused a setup with a complete trading system. Tharp delivers his most quotable line: if you learn one critical thing from this book, it is that a setup is about 10% or less of your trading system. Most people place 90% of their emphasis on finding the right setup, but setups are actually one of the least important parts.

Entry divides into four phases: market selection, market direction, setup conditions, and market timing. For market selection, you need liquidity, sufficient volatility for profit without destruction, and independence across your portfolio. Ed Seykota's test for market direction is elegant: hang the chart on a wall, walk to the other side of the room, and if the trend is obvious, trade it. Markets trend only 15-20% of the time, so you need a filter for sideways conditions. Perry Kaufman's efficiency ratio serves this purpose, measuring movement speed divided by volatility.

Short-term setups include failed-test patterns (a market tests a previous high or low and fails, as in the Turtle Soup strategy by Connors and Raschke), climax reversals (gap to new extreme, failure to follow through, reversal), and retracement setups (find the longer-term trend, wait for a pullback, enter on resumption). Richard Wyckoff's advice: do not buy on breakouts, wait for the retracement test. Retracements offer tight stops, high reward-to-risk, and a way to re-enter after being stopped out.

The critical distinction between filters and setups determines whether adding complexity helps or hurts. Filters use the same data as your system (optimized indicators on price) and typically add degrees of freedom without real predictive power. Setups use different data: time-based patterns, volume metrics, fundamental statistics, or composite breadth data. The Arms Index (TRIN) illustrates: a five-day moving average above 1.2 signals a potential bottom, below 0.8 a potential top, creating one-to-three-day windows of opportunity using data independent of price.

The chapter reviews setups from five well-known systems. William O'Neil's CANSLIM consists entirely of setup criteria: current earnings up 70%+, five-year annual earnings compounding at 24%+, something new (product, management, or price high), small share count, relative strength in the top 80th percentile, moderate institutional sponsorship, and positive overall market direction. Warren Buffett's twelve criteria are almost all setups too: simple businesses, consistent operating histories, high returns on equity with little debt, honest management, rational capital allocation. Virtually all of Buffett's thought process goes into the decision to enter, and he rarely sells, so his track record validates the approach. The Motley Fool's Foolish Four is the simplest: buy the second through fifth highest-yielding Dow stocks, allocate 40/20/20/20, and rebalance annually.

Chapter 8: Entry or Market Timing

The most subversive experiment in the book belongs to Tom Basso. In 1991, he tested whether exits and position sizing matter more than entries by using completely random entries on four futures markets, exiting based on predetermined rules. The result: consistent profitability on 80% of runs, even with coin-flip entries. The system's reliability was 38%, average for a trend-following approach, yet it made money. If random entry can be profitable, the obsession with finding the perfect entry signal is not just misguided but actively harmful, because it diverts attention from the components that actually determine outcomes.

Chuck LeBeau and David Lucas confirmed this through their own testing in the Technical Traders' Bulletin. They combined various entry signals with fixed exits at 5, 10, 15, and 20 days. Entries performed no better than random when the exit was a fixed time period. Adding protective stops actually reduced reliability because stops designed to limit risk can turn trades into losses prematurely. Trend-following systems make money from a few large trades per year; if those entries get whipsawed, the entire year's performance suffers.

Channel breakouts remain one of the most robust entry techniques. Enter when price makes a new high for the last N days (long) or new low (short). The famous Turtles made billions on 20-day breakouts before shifting to 40+ days as markets adapted. The weakness is capital intensity; most channel-breakout systems need $1 million or more to trade properly across a diversified portfolio. Volatility breakouts enter when price moves 0.8 times the average true range in a single day. J. Wells Wilder's system multiplies average true range by 3.0 as a trailing stop, and his exit rule turns out to be nearly identical to the random-entry system, reinforcing Tharp's thesis that exits, not entries, drive results.

Wilder's Directional Movement Indicator quantifies trend strength. DI+ measures upward movement, DI- measures downward movement, and the ADX averages the difference. ADX above 15 signals a trending market. The key insight is that a jump from 15 to 20 is more significant than a jump from 25 to 27, because the rate of change matters more than the absolute level. When ADX is rising, overbought/oversold oscillators stop working because the market is not oscillating; it is trending. Entry signals include DI crossovers, ADX spikes of 4+ points in two days, and confirmation with visual chart analysis.

Moving averages work in trending markets but generate endless whipsaws in consolidations, which is the market condition 85% of the time. Perry Kaufman's adaptive moving average addresses this by adjusting speed based on market efficiency. In a clean trend, the smoothing constant approaches 2.666 (fast); in noise, it approaches 0.06452 (slow). The formula squares the final constant before applying it, creating a nonlinear response that virtually eliminates whipsaws in sideways markets while remaining responsive when real trends develop.

The chapter's most original contribution is the velocity and acceleration framework. Price movement follows the same physics as any moving object: it has speed (price change per unit time) and acceleration (change in speed). A market moving fast and still accelerating is likely to continue. A market decelerating and then accelerating in the opposite direction offers a potential reversal entry. Tharp notes this approach is largely untested and worth exploring, which makes it one of the few genuinely open research questions he presents rather than a proven technique.

Chapter 9: Knowing When to Fold 'Em

Maximum Adverse Excursion (MAE) is the most powerful concept in the exits toolkit. Using data from a channel-breakout system with a 3x ATR stop on British Pound trades from 1985 to 1992, Tharp shows that no winning trade had an MAE greater than 1.5x ATR. Only three of twenty-four winners (12.5%) even exceeded 1.0x ATR. Meanwhile, two-thirds of losing trades had MAE above 1.0x ATR, and nearly half exceeded 1.5x ATR. The implication is surgical: if good trades rarely go far against you, then you can use much tighter stops than you thought possible. Tighter stops mean smaller losses when stopped out, which means you can make multiple attempts to catch a big move, and when you do catch it, the R-multiple is larger because initial risk was smaller.

The three-moving-averages system popularized by R.C. Allen uses the crossing pattern of short, medium, and long averages for both entry and exit. When the shortest average crosses the medium, you exit; no reversal signal fires until both shorter averages cross the longest. Channel breakout stops set the exit at the lowest low (for longs) of the last ten days, giving price substantial room to breathe. The advantage is noise filtration; the disadvantage is giving back large open profits because the stop doubles as both protective stop and profit-taking exit.

Time stops exit a position after a fixed period if no profit materializes. The premise is that if a trade does not move in your favor quickly, it probably will not. The best practitioners treat each day as an entirely new day: if you cannot justify being in the position today, close it. Discretionary stops work for professionals with deep market intuition but are dangerous for amateurs. Tharp identifies six life circumstances where psychological stops are essential: going through a divorce, death of someone close, birth of a child, moving house, burnout, and being so excited about the market that judgment is impaired. In each case, you are not at 100% and the market will exploit it.

Ken Roberts' 1-2-3 methodology places stops just beyond point 3 on the reversal pattern. Tharp critiques this as too subjective and suggests four statistical alternatives: 3x ATR, ATR plus 3 standard deviations, Cynthia Kase's dev-stop, or an MAE-based stop just beyond the historical excursion threshold. The underlying message across all stop types is the same: your stop defines your R, your R determines your position size, and your position size determines whether you survive long enough to realize your system's expectancy.

Chapter 10: How to Take Profits

Exits control whether you profit or lose and by how much, yet they receive a fraction of the attention lavished on entries. Tharp reviews four exit categories: exits that minimize initial loss, exits that maximize profits, exits that minimize profit giveback, and psychological exits. His recommendation is to use simple multiple exits rather than complex optimized ones, because simpler exits are easier to conceptualize, require minimal parameter tuning, and best meet system objectives without overfitting.

William O'Neil's CANSLIM has a basic profit-taking rule of 20% on any position, with an 8% stop loss, creating a fundamental 2.5R exit. But O'Neil tempers this with thirty-six additional selling rules: exceptions to the basic rule, reasons to sell early, and eight rules for when to hold. Warren Buffett generally does not sell, citing capital gains tax and the logic that if a company delivers excellent returns on capital, there is no reason to exit. Tharp pushes back on the "Buffett never sells" mythology. When Buffett disclosed owning roughly 20% of the world's silver supply in early 1998, silver paid no dividends and carried storage and protection costs. Without a planned exit strategy for that silver, Tharp argues, Buffett would be making one of the biggest mistakes of his investing career. If he does have an exit plan, then he likely has them for his stock positions too; they are simply undisclosed.

"If Warren Buffett does not have a planned exit strategy for that silver, then, in my opinion, he is making one of the biggest mistakes of his investing career." Van Tharp

Chapter 11: The Opportunity and Cost Factors

Expectancy times opportunity equals dollar volume, and dollar volume is what pays your bills. Four traders illustrate the arithmetic. Trader one has $3.32 expectancy but only one trade every twenty days, generating $0.145 per day. Trader four, a market maker, has just $0.018 expectancy but makes 500 trades daily, generating $6.00 per day. The market maker, with a per-trade edge smaller than a rounding error, produces forty-one times the daily income of the most skilled position trader. A floor trader in Tharp's supertrader program started each year with $100,000 and made $1.7 million in just over three months in 1997 by knowing his edge and exploiting it through sheer frequency.

Cost is the mirror image of opportunity. Long-term trend followers making $5,000 per winning trade barely notice a $100 commission. Short-term traders averaging $50 per trade are devastated by it. The rule is simple: the shorter your time horizon, the more trade cost becomes a central variable. Beyond commissions, execution costs (slippage), tax costs, and psychological costs all subtract from expectancy. The psychological cost is the most significant: Tharp admits to nearly losing a year's profits in a single day due to psychological mistakes. If brilliant Ph.D.s cannot execute a simple positive-expectancy game, execution under real financial pressure is the hardest problem in trading.

Chapter 12: Position Sizing

Position sizing answers the question "how much?" throughout a trade. It is not risk control, it is not diversification, and it is not the part of the system that maximizes performance. It determines equal opportunity and equal exposure across portfolio elements, allowing you to scale profits as your account grows. The snow-fight metaphor returns: equity is the wall, position size determines how many simultaneous positions you can maintain, and if ten thousand snowballs hit at once instead of one at a time, the result changes dramatically even if the total number is the same.

Model one: one unit per fixed amount of money. Trade one contract per $50,000 of equity. Simple, always allows a position even with limited funds, but treats all instruments as equal when they are not and cannot accelerate exposure during winning streaks. Testing on ten commodities from 1981 to 1991 with a 55/21-day breakout system produced $237,457 profit and a 5.75% compounded annual return with 7.12% maximum drawdown.

Model two: equal units for stock traders. Divide capital into equal chunks and allocate one chunk per position. A $50,000 account split five ways puts $10,000 into each stock regardless of price. Better for small accounts, gives equal weighting, but slow to grow exposure.

Model three: percent risk. Control position size so risk per trade equals a fixed percentage of equity. At 1% risk on $1 million, if gold is at $380 with a stop at $370, you risk $1,000 per contract and can take ten contracts. Testing at 1% risk produced $1,844,493 profit, 20.52% annual return, and 14.14% maximum drawdown, obliterating the fixed-amount and equal-units models. The best reward-to-risk ratio occurs at 25% risk per position, but that requires tolerating 84% drawdown, and drawdown recovery is asymmetric: a 50% loss requires a 100% gain to recover.

Model four: percent volatility. Size positions so each instrument's daily volatility equals a fixed percentage of equity. High-volatility instruments get smaller positions; low-volatility instruments get larger. This equalizes expected portfolio fluctuations across all holdings. At 2.5% volatility allocation, returns reached 67-86% annually but with 69-86% drawdown. William O'Neil says even a multimillion-dollar portfolio should own only six to seven stocks; someone with $20,000 to $160,000 should limit themselves to four or five. Buffett concentrates heavily on the best businesses. Ken Roberts says trade only one contract and avoid expensive markets like the S&P, deutschemark, or yen.

"Money management is the most important aspect of system development, other than psychology, and yet virtually every book on the market that talks about trading or system development totally neglects the topic." Van Tharp

Chapter 13: Conclusion

To master trading, you must first master yourself. That is the book's thesis distilled to eight words. The six elements of a high-expectancy system (reliability, reward-to-risk ratio, trading cost, opportunity, equity size, and position-sizing algorithm) are all quantifiable, all testable, and all subordinate to the psychological capacity to execute them under pressure. Position sizing accounts for most of the variability in professional trader performance, yet Tharp interviewed fifty traders and found fifty different methodologies. The method is not the key variable. The key variables are positive expectancy, sufficient opportunity, deep understanding of position sizing, and the discipline to execute all three consistently.

Order execution is fundamentally about communication with your broker. If you are a trend follower trading breakouts, your broker needs to know you want to trade real breakouts, not false ones where traders are merely testing new prices without follow-through. A broker who understands your intent can exercise discretion. A day trader needs minimum cost and minimum slippage and must communicate that explicitly. If you do not tell your broker what you want, you will not get it.

Portfolio testing expands the opportunity factor. Trading a portfolio of markets opens up many more chances at big trades, and with sufficient opportunity, there need be no losing quarter, perhaps no losing month. Multiple noncorrelated systems add another layer: when one system is in drawdown, another is likely producing. When a giant winner arrives, you have a much larger capital base for position sizing. Tharp believes traders who understand these principles can make 50% per year; some supertraders in his program already surpass that.

The seven steps to disciplined trading are the final framework. Have a plan and test it. Assume total responsibility for everything that happens. Find your weaknesses and work on them. Do global planning for every catastrophe. Conduct daily self-analysis. Mentally rehearse what could go wrong today. Debrief every evening. The marble-game demonstration drives home the responsibility lesson: when Tharp has an audience play a positive-expectancy game, he has one person draw all the losing marbles in sequence. Half the audience goes broke, and when asked if the person who drew the streak is responsible, many raise their hands. They went broke through poor position sizing but prefer to blame the marble drawer.

"In life and in trading, there will always be someone who picks the losing marbles. Only when you finally realize that the result somehow depends upon you will you start learning to correct your mistakes." Van Tharp

The final message is about beliefs. You cannot trade the markets; you can only trade your beliefs about the markets. But beliefs about yourself matter just as much: what do you think you are capable of? How worthy of success do you believe yourself to be? Weak beliefs about yourself can undermine even a great system. Tharp recommends reading the book four or five times, because people filter through their belief systems and miss material on the first pass. Subsequent readings surface new insights and make the principles second nature.

Trading & Investing Lessons

Expectancy Over Win Rate

A system with 36% accuracy and $0.78 expectancy is nearly four times more profitable than one with 60% accuracy and $0.20 expectancy. Stop asking "how often am I right?" and start asking "how much do I make per dollar risked?" This reframes every trading decision from ego satisfaction to mathematical edge.

Position Sizing as the Primary Lever

In Tharp's comparative testing, percent-risk position sizing at 1% generated $1.8 million in profit versus $237,000 for fixed-amount sizing on the same system, same markets, same time period. The only variable that changed was how much capital was allocated per trade. If you are spending your time optimizing entry signals instead of position sizing algorithms, you are optimizing the wrong variable by an order of magnitude.

The Random Entry Lesson

Tom Basso's 1991 experiment proved that coin-flip entries with proper exits and position sizing produce consistent profitability. This should fundamentally change how you evaluate any trading system. If the entry does not matter, then the pitch "our system has 85% accuracy" is a red flag, not a selling point. What matters is the full R-multiple distribution and how position sizing interacts with it.

MAE for Stop Placement

Winning trades rarely move far against you. If no winner in your system's history exceeded 1.5x ATR in adverse excursion, then a stop beyond 3x ATR is wasting capital on every loss. Track MAE for your own trades and tighten stops to just beyond the historical excursion threshold of winners. Smaller R means larger R-multiples on wins and the ability to take multiple shots at the same setup.

Opportunity Times Expectancy

A high-frequency strategy with tiny edge can outperform a low-frequency strategy with large edge. This is why market makers prosper and why traders should think about daily dollar volume of expectancy, not just per-trade expectancy. It also explains why trading more markets (expanding opportunity) with a proven system can dramatically improve results even without changing the system itself.

Psychology as Infrastructure

When forty Ph.D.s cannot profitably play a game with 60% odds, the problem is not intelligence or education. It is the psychological infrastructure required to size bets properly, tolerate losing streaks, and resist the urge to override the system. Every successful trader Tharp modeled had this infrastructure. Most failing traders he coached had good systems and bad psychology.

Key Takeaways

The Holy Grail Is Internal

The search for a perfect system is a search for self-knowledge. Every successful trader Schwager interviewed had found a methodology that fit their own psychology, not a universally optimal system.

60/30/10 Allocation of Importance

Psychology accounts for 60% of trading success, position sizing for 30%, and system development for just 10%. Yet the industry's entire infrastructure of books, seminars, and software is built around the 10%.

Objectives Before Everything

Designing objectives should be 50% of system development time. Return target, drawdown tolerance, time horizon, and risk budget define the engineering constraints. Without them, you are building a machine without knowing what it should do.

Fourteen Biases, Three Domains

Biases in system development (representation, reliability, lotto, small numbers, conservatism, randomness, need-to-understand), testing (degrees of freedom, postdictive error, ignoring costs), and live trading (gambler's fallacy, profit-cutting, current-trade-must-win) form a taxonomy of failure.

Setups Are 10% of the System

A setup that is 70% accurate produces only 30% winning trades when combined with stops, exits, and real execution. Traders who spend 90% of their time on setups are optimizing the wrong component.

Four Position-Sizing Models

Fixed amount, equal units, percent risk, and percent volatility. Percent risk dominated in testing, producing 20.52% annual returns versus 5.75% for fixed amount on identical trades.

Seven Steps to Discipline

Plan and test, assume total responsibility, find weaknesses, global planning, daily self-analysis, mental rehearsal, daily debriefing. The framework transforms trading from reactive gambling into a structured professional practice.

Contrarian Ideas

Random Entry Can Be Profitable

The industry sells entries. Tharp proves that coin-flip entries with proper exits and position sizing generate consistent profits. This inverts the entire value proposition of most trading education.

High Win Rates Are Dangerous

A 90%-accurate system can lose money steadily. A 36%-accurate system can be four times more profitable. Win rate and profitability are independent variables, and optimizing for accuracy often produces negative expectancy.

Buffett Probably Has Exit Plans

The "Buffett never sells" narrative is projection by authors with entry bias. His silver position (20% of world supply, no dividends, storage costs) would be irrational without an exit strategy.

Most Trading Software Is Harmful

Software loaded with indicators appeals to psychological weaknesses. Optimization tools produce curve-fitted results that look spectacular on history and fail in real time. The more parameters you add, the more certain you are fitting noise.

Short-Term Trading Is a Losing Game

Transaction costs that are irrelevant to a $5,000-per-trade trend follower are devastating to a $50-per-trade day trader. Most short-term traders lose not because their systems are bad but because costs consume their edge entirely.

Frameworks

The Twelve-Step System Development Model

(1) Self-inventory, (2) open mind and gather information, (3) determine objectives (20-50% of effort), (4) select time frame, (5) study best historical moves, (6) develop measurable concept, (7) add stops and transaction costs, (8) add exits and calculate expectancy, (9) hunt huge R-multiple trades, (10) optimize with position sizing, (11) improve system, (12) worst-case mental planning.

The Expectancy Formula

Expectancy = (Probability of Win x Average Win) - (Probability of Loss x Average Loss). A system with 36% wins at 5:1 and 10:1 payouts can have $0.78 expectancy versus $0.20 for a 60% system at 1:1. Multiply by opportunity (trade frequency) to get daily dollar volume.

CANSLIM as Pure Setup

Current earnings up 70%+, Annual earnings compounding 24%+, New product/management/price high, Small share count, Leader in relative strength, Institutional sponsorship (moderate), Market direction positive. Seven setup criteria that most people mistake for a complete system.

Four Entry Phases

(1) Market selection (liquidity, volatility, independence), (2) Market direction (Seykota's wall test, efficiency ratio), (3) Setup conditions (fundamental, seasonal, volume-based), (4) Market timing (confirmation that the expected move has started).

Four Position-Sizing Models

Fixed amount (simplest, weakest), equal units (better for stocks), percent risk (strongest returns), percent volatility (equalizes fluctuations). Percent risk at 1% produced 20.52% annual returns versus 5.75% for fixed amount in identical testing.

What Didn't Fit

David Mobley's Rise and Fall

The foreword is written by David Mobley Sr., who grew his assets from $3 million to $50 million in five years, compounding at over 40% annually. Mobley started investing at age thirteen in 1969, opening a brokerage account at Merrill Lynch in Toledo, Ohio, under the Unified Gift to Minors Act. He made his first million through real estate by age twenty-five in 1981, lost it all, and rebuilt through investing. He found Tharp through Schwager's Market Wizards and credits Tharp's "Holy Grail secrets" for his success. The key message: the Holy Grail is personal, different for each person, a hidden secret you discover for yourself.

The Investment Conference Paradox

At one conference, a speaker on high-probability entry signals packed the room and sold $10,000 worth of books in thirty minutes. Another speaker at the same conference, presenting on money management (which Tharp considers the actual key to success), drew thirty attendees, and only four bought the book. People systematically gravitate toward things that do not work and ignore the things that do. The psychology of what traders buy reveals the same biases that make them lose money.

The Short-Term FX Trader Who Could Not Scale

A short-term foreign exchange trader made roughly six trades per day, with gains equaling losses on average but winning 75% of the time. He had $500,000 in capital plus a $10 million credit line. With proper position sizing, Tharp calculated he could have made $100 million per year. But the trader could not scale because of psychological issues that manifested at larger position sizes. The system was sound; the operator was the bottleneck. This is the purest illustration of Tharp's 60/30/10 allocation: the psychology prevented a hundredfold improvement in results.

Ray Kelly's SEC Fee Arbitrage

Kelly discovered that the SEC charged a fixed $150 ticket on NYSE trades plus a 0.003% fee. On a $100 million trade, the SEC fee was $3,333.33. He negotiated to execute identical trades in Toronto instead, avoiding the fee entirely. He also spent two years negotiating a 67% return strategy with a brokerage firm that required three divisions to cooperate. As negotiations dragged, the return dropped to 35% and the trade was never executed. Tens of millions in potential profit evaporated because institutional bureaucracy killed the opportunity. Kelly's philosophy: "Economics does not have a moral code. If there is money on the table, it belongs to the person who picks it up."

The Sunspot-Market Connection

John Nelson predicted radio propagation quality at 88% accuracy using planetary alignments. Sunspot cycles correlate with historical highs and lows of civilization. A solar flare on March 5, 1989, overloaded power grids in Canada, leaving over a million people without electricity. The market effect? None discernible. But Tharp's pragmatic verdict is that even a 48-to-52% edge is card-counter territory, and if any of these unconventional methods can deliver 55% reliability when combined with sound exits and position sizing, they are worth using.

About the Author

Van K. Tharp, Ph.D. was a trading coach and psychologist who spent over fifteen years modeling the common traits of successful traders through the framework of Neuro-Linguistic Programming. He founded the Van Tharp Institute (originally the International Institute of Trading Mastery) and developed the Peak Performance Course for Traders and Investors, through which he trained thousands of traders and conducted psychological evaluations to identify the behavioral patterns that separate consistent winners from chronic losers.

Tharp's background was unusual for the trading world. He was not a trader himself but a modeler of traders, applying Bandler and Grinder's NLP methodology to extract the common elements of peak performance. His client list included some of the most successful money managers of the 1980s and 1990s, including Ray Kelly, Tom Basso, and the graduates of his supertrader program who went on to manage top-performing funds. His contribution was not a trading system but a meta-framework for building any trading system, centered on the insight that psychology and position sizing matter far more than the entry signals that consume most traders' attention.

Tharp passed away in 2022, but his institute continues to operate, and his books remain foundational texts in trading psychology and system design. Trade Your Way to Financial Freedom was his most widely read work, and its influence can be seen in the emphasis on expectancy, R-multiples, and position sizing that now pervades serious trading education.

Why This Book Matters

Most trading books sell you a system. This one tells you why that entire approach is wrong. By demonstrating that random entries can be profitable with proper exits and position sizing, Tharp dismantled the industry's core value proposition and redirected attention to the components that actually determine outcomes. The book's influence is visible everywhere in modern trading education: the vocabulary of R-multiples, the emphasis on expectancy over win rate, the insistence that psychology and position sizing account for 90% of results. Before Tharp, these ideas existed in scattered form. He synthesized them into a coherent framework accessible to non-mathematicians.

The book also matters as a corrective to the self-help branch of trading literature. Tharp does not offer affirmations or visualization exercises. He offers a twelve-step engineering process with testable outputs, psychological inventories with honest self-assessment, and mathematical frameworks (expectancy, R-multiples, position-sizing models) that can be validated on historical data. The combination of rigorous quantitative thinking with deep psychological insight is rare in any field. In trading education, it was revolutionary.

For any trader who has ever wondered why their system works in backtesting but fails in real time, or why they cut winners short and hold losers too long, or why they cannot follow their own rules under pressure, this book provides both the diagnosis and the treatment plan. It is not a trading system. It is a book about how to build one that fits who you actually are.
