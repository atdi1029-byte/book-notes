### Chapter 2: Judgmental Biases (pp. 17-42)
- We trade our BELIEFS about the market, not the market itself
- Information overload: information doubles every year (was every 5 years at start of computer age)
- Conscious mind processes 5-9 chunks at a time
- We cope by generalizing, deleting, distorting information
- Karl Popper: "progress in knowledge results more from efforts to find fault with our theories, rather than prove them"
- Three categories of biases: (1) system development, (2) testing systems, (3) trading your system

**Biases Affecting System Development:**

1. **Representation bias**: daily bar chart is just a line on paper, not the market itself. We accept it as meaningful because everyone uses it, we were taught it, data comes in that format
2. **Reliability bias**: assuming data is accurate. Data vendors have many errors — stock splits not accounted for, bad prints, missing data. "I've never seen a database that I would consider totally reliable"
3. **Lotto bias**: wanting to control the market by picking tops/bottoms. People want to buy low and sell high, but the real money is in riding big moves and cutting losses
4. **Law of small numbers**: drawing conclusions from too few examples. "After three occurrences, the human mind tends to perceive a pattern." Seminars selling systems based on 5-10 examples
5. **Conservatism bias**: once we form beliefs, we resist new information. Take months to accept new data
6. **Randomness bias**: assuming the market is random when it may have patterns, or assuming patterns when there's randomness
7. **Need-to-understand bias**: finding order/meaning where none exists. Most market "explanations" are made up after the fact — financial news tells you WHY the market moved, but the explanations are fabricated

**Biases Affecting System Testing:**

8. **Degrees of freedom**: each parameter/rule added to a system gives it a degree of freedom to fit past data. Too many = curve fitting. Example: 10 rules with 3 values each = 59,049 combinations to test
9. **Postdictive error**: using information not available at the time of the trade. Example: using today's close in a calculation that needs to happen before the close
10. **Not considering the cost of trading**: $100 round-turn on futures × many trades = significant drag
11. **Failing to account for randomness in data**: need statistical tests, not just equity curves

**Biases Affecting How You Trade Your System:**

12. **Gambler's fallacy**: belief that after a losing streak, a win is "due." People bet MORE after losses and LESS after wins — exact opposite of what works. Larry Williams quote: "After three or four losing trades in a row, the probability of the next trade being a winner is way in your favor" — Tharp says this is wrong
13. **Conservative-with-profits-and-risky-with-losses bias**: 80% of people prefer a sure $9,000 loss over a 95% chance of $10,000 loss (risky option has higher expected loss of $9,500). 80% prefer sure $9,000 gain over 95% chance of $10,000 gain (risky option has higher expected value of $9,500). This means people cut profits short and let losses run — the OPPOSITE of what works
- Ralph Vince experiment: 40 Ph.D.s played 100 trials of a 60% win game with $1,000. Only 2 (5%) ended with more than $1,000. If they'd bet constant $10, they'd have $1,200. Optimal betting (20% of equity) = $7,490 average
14. **"My current trade must be a winner" bias**: overwhelming desire to make current positions work out. Nurse losing trades, take profits too early. Once ego is involved in a prediction, very difficult to trade against it
- Client story: daily commodity fax, known worldwide for accuracy. Despite accuracy, his ability to TRADE the commodity was poor — ego tied to predictions

**Chapter 2 Summary of all biases:**
- System Development: representation, reliability, lotto, law of small numbers, conservatism, randomness, need-to-understand
- System Testing: degrees of freedom, postdictive error, not enough protection
- Trading Your System: gambler's fallacy, conservative-with-profits/risky-with-losses, current-trade-must-be-winner
- "The golden rule of trading — 'Cut your losses short and let your profits run' — has nothing to do with entry and everything to do with exits"

### Chapter 4: Steps to Developing a System (pp. 61-80)
- 12-step model for system development, derived from modeling hundreds of great traders
- NLP (Neuro-Linguistic Programming) basis: find several people who do something well, interview them, find common elements

**The 12 Steps:**

1. **Take an Inventory**: self-assessment of skills, temperament, time, resources, strengths, weaknesses. Computer skills? Math skills? Capital? Loss tolerance? Time constraints?
   - If you have a full-time job, use long-term system with end-of-day data — only 30 min/night
   - Managing other people's money adds THEIR psychology to yours
   - Client withdrawal pattern story: client withdraws after 2 losing months, waits for 5 winning months to reinvest, then hits 2 more losing months — client always loses even though manager makes money

2. **Develop an Open Mind and Gather Market Information**:
   - "You don't trade or invest in markets — you trade or invest according to your beliefs about the markets" (Einstein sidebar)
   - Everything you've been taught is a belief, not a fact
   - Charles LeBeau: when he started computerized testing, hundreds of his market beliefs failed
   - Evaluate each belief by its "utility" — is there a more useful belief?
   - Recommended reading: Schwager's Market Wizards, LeBeau's Computer Analysis of the Futures Market, Kaufman's Smarter Trading, O'Neil's How to Make Money in Stocks, Chande's Beyond Technical Analysis
   - Psychological reading: Sloman's Nothing and When You're Troubled, Barach's Mindtraps

3. **Determine Your Objectives**: should occupy 20-50% of system development time. Most people spend just a few minutes. Refer back to Chapter 3

4. **Determine Your Time Frame for Trading**:
   - Table 4-1: Long-term trading advantages (low psychological pressure, low transaction costs, can use stops/options, infinite profit opportunity, low data costs) vs disadvantages (whipsawed by intraday moves, large equity swings, must be patient, reliability usually <50%, infrequent trades, one missed opportunity can ruin the year)
   - Table 4-2: Short-term trading advantages (many opportunities, exciting, high-probability entries possible, no overnight risk) vs disadvantages (high transaction costs, excitement is psychological need not advantage, profits limited by time, data costs very high, short-ten systems subject to random noise, intense psychological pressure)
   - Short-term FX trader story: made ~6 trades/day, gains = losses but made money on 75% of trades. Had $500K + $10M credit line. With position sizing, could have made $100M/year — but couldn't scale due to psychological issues

5. **Determine the Best Historical Moves and Notice What They Have in Common**: collect 50-100 sample moves in your time frame. Notice setup conditions, volume patterns, fundamental conditions, timing relationships. O'Neil's CANSLIM system and Daily Graphs as resources

6. **What's the Concept Behind Those Moves and How Can You Objectively Measure It?**: your concept gives you setup conditions + entry signal. Test entry reliability independently — a random entry gives 45-55% reliability; your concept should give 55%+ especially in 1-5 day periods. Consider the false-positive rate. "The more you understand your concept, the less historical testing you will have to do"

7. **Add Your Stops and Transaction Costs**: protective stop = when to get out to protect capital. Add slippage + commissions. Initial 60% reliability will probably drop to 50-55% after stops and costs

8. **Add Your Profit-Taking Exits and Determine Your Expectancy**: expectancy = average amount you'll make per dollar risked over many trades. Best systems have 3-4 different exits. Long-term traders: wide stops, exits designed to capture 20x average risk. Short-term: tight stops, 1:1 reward-to-risk but 50-60% hit rate with a few big winners

9. **Look for Huge Reward Trades**: alternative to steps 6-8. Focus on huge R-multiple trades (10R or bigger). Accept 25% hit rate if one 12.5R winner covers three 0.75R losses. Live cattle breakout example (Figure 4-1): 3 entries at $0.75 loss each, then $10 profit = 12.5R multiple. One 10R trade can be profitable with up to 7 1R losses

10. **Optimize with Position Sizing**: most important part of any system. Position sizing can make you rich or bankrupt regardless of system quality. Three graduates of Tharp's program: Webster Management (top-ranked fund by reward-to-risk), Maricopa Asset Management (40%/year, hardly a losing month), Ray Kelly (8 years compounded 40%+, only 2 losing months — mostly arbitrage)

11. **Determine How You Can Improve Your System**: ongoing process. Add independent markets for more opportunity. Add noncorrelated systems. Combine long-term trending system with short-term consolidation system

12. **Worst-Case Scenario — Mental Planning**: brainstorm every possible catastrophe. 500-point Dow drop, Gulf War, meteor strike, European common currency, computers stolen. Develop plans for each. Tom Basso exercise: "Imagine what it's like to take the other side of each trade. Pretend you just bought it instead of sold it. How would you feel?"

### Chapter 5: Selecting a Concept That Works (pp. 81-128)
- Fewer than 20% of market participants have a system; of those, most just use predefined indicators
- Chaos theory expert called Tharp: "systems are impossible, it's all luck and psychology." Tharp agreed IF you define a system as just an entry technique, but disagreed because a methodology with positive expectancy through stops, exits, and position sizing IS possible
- "You can trade ANY concept as long as you have a positive expectancy"

**1. Trend Following** (by Tom Basso):
- Break down "trend following": need a TREND (prices must move up after you buy or down after you sell), then you FOLLOW it (wait for trend to shift, then enter)
- "Let your profits run. Cut your losses short" = perfect description
- Advantage: never miss a major move. Longer-term indicators = lower transaction costs
- Disadvantage: can't distinguish between major profitable move and short-lived whipsaw. Markets only trend 15-25% of the time — must endure many unfavorable markets
- Does trend following still work? Yes — trends exist because of supply/demand fundamentals. S&P futures market: opened in 1982, had abnormally large price changes within 5 years that you'd only expect once per century in a random distribution
- Not for everyone: not for scalpers, hedgers, day traders (day ends, forcing liquidation). Good for patient strategic thinkers
- Editor's note: biggest problem = markets don't always trend, but stock market has thousands of stocks so there are always trending ones. Short selling difficulty: (1) few stocks trending down in bull markets, (2) people don't understand shorting, (3) regulators make it difficult (uptick rule)

**2. Fundamental Analysis** (by Charles LeBeau):
- Definition: use of actual/anticipated supply and demand to forecast direction and magnitude of future price changes
- Advantage over technical analysis: can determine price OBJECTIVES (magnitude), not just direction/timing
- Limitations: tells direction + general magnitude but NOT timing or exact price levels. Results are "painfully imprecise"
- Practical tips:
  - Don't do your own fundamental analysis unless highly specialized — use experts' free research
  - News ≠ fundamentals: "News follows price direction, while fundamental analysis predicts price direction." Media calls after close asking why market moved, gets bullish/bearish news to match
  - "Buy the rumor, sell the fact" — anticipation of bullish news moves market more than the actual news
  - Judge reports in context of expectations, not face value. "Bullish" report that's less bullish than expected = actually bearish
  - Look for demand-driven markets (long sustained uptrends, high profitability)
  - Be patient with fundamental forecast timing — may have to wait weeks or months
  - Many forecasts fail to materialize for a major price change. 8-10 forecasts/year, 6-7 will occur, position in half = extremely profitable year
  - "Be decisive and willing to take your share of losses"
- Editor: LeBeau's comments primarily for futures, but also applicable to stocks (O'Neil's CANSLIM, Buffett's business model)

**3. Seasonal Tendencies** (by Jerry Toepke, Moore Research Center):
- Seasonal approach: anticipate future price movement based on recurring annual cycles rather than reacting to news
- Supply/demand cycles: grain harvest, heating oil demand, driving season gasoline, income tax liquidity
- Four components: seasonal low, seasonal rise, seasonal high, seasonal decline
- Heating oil (Figure 5-1): low in July (hottest month), rises into winter as demand increases
- Unleaded gasoline (Figure 5-2): low in winter, rises into summer driving season (Memorial Day)
- T-bonds (Figure 5-3): fiscal year starts Oct 1, bond prices tend to rise Oct-May, decline May-Sep (tighter liquidity as taxes paid)
- Soybeans (Figure 5-4): influenced by Brazil's counter-seasonal harvest since 1980
- Seasonal patterns rarely appear as perfect cycles — punctuated by brief rallies/dips due to fundamentals
- Sample size matters — but "modern" history may be more practical. Brazil's 1980 entry changed soybean patterns permanently
- Patterns driven by fundamentals > pure statistical correlations. Super Bowl indicator = no cause-and-effect = don't trade it

**4. Spreading** (by Kevin Thomas, LIFFE floor trader):
- Spreads: simultaneous long and short positions in related markets. Lower risk profile, lower margin
- Types: intercontract spreads (same market, different months), intermarket spreads (related markets, e.g., wheat/corn), currency cross rates as synthetic spreads, basis trading (cash vs futures), butterfly spreads
- Euromarks example (Figure 5-5, 5-6): Sep 96 vs Sep 97 spread with RSI trendlines. Divergence at point A, breakout at point B. Spread moved 76 ticks, margin 600 deutschemarks vs 1,500 for straight position

### Chapter 6: Understanding Expectancy and Other Keys to Trading Success (pp. 130-158)
- Client reaction to expectancy chapter: "Oh, no... I don't think expectancy is one of our edges" — yet expectancy is one of the most important trade secrets
- Six key variables to investment/trading success:
  1. **Reliability** (hit rate): % of winning trades
  2. **Relative size of profits vs losses**: when traded at smallest level (1 share/1 contract)
  3. **Cost of trading**: execution costs + commissions per trade
  4. **Opportunity** (frequency): how often you can trade
  5. **Size of trading capital**: your "wall" in the snowball fight
  6. **Position-sizing model**: how many units you trade — multiplier on all gains AND losses

- Most people obsess over #1 (being right). Yet a system with 1% win rate can be profitable if the one winner is huge

- **Snow Fight Metaphor**: you're hiding behind a wall of snow (your capital). White snowballs = winning trades (build the wall). Black snowballs = losing trades (dissolve/destroy the wall). Size of snowballs = relative size of wins/losses. Cost = each snowball slightly damages wall regardless of color. Frequency = rate snowballs arrive. Position sizing = if 10,000 snowballs hit simultaneously vs one at a time, the effect changes dramatically
- Wall growing = positive expectancy (white snow volume > black). Wall shrinking = negative expectancy

- **Expectancy Under a Magnifying Glass**:
  - Simple marble game: 60 blue (win), 40 black (lose), risk $1 per draw
  - Formula 6-1: Expectancy = (PW × AW) - (PL × AL) = (0.6 × 1) - (0.4 × 1) = 0.20 (20 cents per dollar risked)
  - Over 1,000 trials, could have 10 losses in a row despite 60% win rate
  - Complex marble game (Table 6-1): 100 marbles, multiple colors with different payoffs
    - Losing: 50 black (1:1), 10 blue (2:1), 4 red (3:1) = 64% chance of losing
    - Winning: 20 green (1:1), 10 white (5:1), 3 yellow (10:1), 3 clear (20:1) = 36% chance of winning
    - Total positive expectancy: 0.2 + 0.5 + 0.3 + 0.6 = 1.6
    - Total negative expectancy: 0.5 + 0.2 + 0.12 = 0.82
    - NET expectancy = 1.6 - 0.82 = 0.78 (78 cents per dollar risked)
  - Game 2 has 36% win rate but 78 cents expectancy vs Game 1's 60% win rate but only 20 cents expectancy — Game 2 is nearly 4× more profitable!
  - "The key factor in determining the value of your system is its expectancy per dollar risked"

- **Position Sizing and Expectancy**:
  - You can only realize expectancy if you size positions wisely relative to equity
  - Risk 100% on first draw in 60% game: 40% chance of immediate ruin
  - Risk 50%: three losses in a row (possible ~1 in 5) reduces $100 to $12.50, needing 700% gain just to break even
  - "Your position size on a given trade must be low enough so that you can realize the long-term expectancy of your system over many trades"

- **Opportunity Factor**: expectancy × frequency = practical value. Game with 78¢ expectancy but 1 draw/5 min vs game with 20¢ expectancy but 1 draw/min. Game 1: $9.36/hr. Game 2: $12/hr. Higher-frequency lower-expectancy game wins!

### Chapter 6 (continued): Expectancy and R Multiples

- **Prediction trap**: 90% accurate system that wins $275 avg but loses $2,700 avg → Expectancy = (0.9 × 275) - (0.1 × 2700) = -$22.50. "You get to be right 90% of the time and you eventually lose all your money"

- **R Multiples**: reward-to-risk ratio for each trade. R = initial risk (distance to stop). If you risk $500 and make $1,500, that's a 3R trade. If you lose $1,000, that's a -2R trade
  - Figure 6-1: Natural gas trade example — long 8/4/97 at 2,511, initial exit (3× ATR stop) at 2,407. Exit 9/29/97 at 3,069. Profit = 558 points, R = 104 points, so 5.37R multiple

- **R Multiple Distribution**: Figure 6-2 shows marble game R-multiples over 60 trades. Long losing streak between trades 46-55. People react by either (1) betting it's time for a winner or (2) betting against expectancy because of recent streak — both irrational

- **Figure 6-3**: Equity curves at 1%, 1.5%, 2% risk per trade. At 1%: 40.1% return, 12.3% max drawdown. Significant losing streaks of 5, 6, and 10 trades
- **Figure 6-4**: Betting 1% AGAINST the expectancy — still "right" 64% of time but lose 37% of starting equity

- **Expectancy Applied to the Market** (real trading example):
  - Table 6-2: 103 trades over 2 years, 60 winners (58.3%), 43 losers (41.7%). Total profit $54,147, total loss $43,304, net $10,843
  - Expectancy per dollar risked = $1.127 - $0.91 = $0.217 (21.7 cents per dollar risked)
  - Table 6-3: grouped by $500 ranges. Entire net profit came from ONE trade ($14,256). Eliminating one $3,221 loss would increase profits by 40%

- **Fred's System** (option trader): 21 trades May-Aug, made $1,890.43 ($90/trade avg). Tables 6-5 through 6-7. Expectancy = $3.627 - $2.848 = $0.779 (78 cents per dollar risked). But has one giant 25-to-1 loss that offsets the one giant 25-to-1 gain

- **Ethel's System** (stock trader): 18 trades over 1 year, $7,175 profit ($398.61/trade avg). 55.6% winners. Tables 6-8 through 6-10. Expectancy = $2.229 - $1.393 = $0.836 (84 cents per dollar risked). One trade ($7,358) = entire year's profit. Ethel's expectancy slightly better than Fred's

- **Expectancy × Opportunity comparison**:
  - Fred: 78¢ × 54 opportunities/year = $42.12
  - Ethel: 64¢ × 18 opportunities/year = $15.12
  - Fred's system is actually much better when opportunity is factored in

- **7-step review of how to use expectancy**:
  1. Calculate overall expectancy of your system
  2. Eliminate position sizing effects — look at single units only
  3. Divide trades into profit/loss groups based on typical smallest loss (your 1R level)
  4. Convert groupings into probability matrix
  5. Calculate expectancy per dollar risked using Formula 6-2
  6. If 100+ trades and expectancy > 50¢ per dollar risked, it's a good system
  7. Determine opportunity (how often you can trade)

- Key reminders: (1) Expectancy and probability of winning are NOT the same — high win rate can mean negative expectancy; (2) Even with positive expectancy, oversizing positions can lose all your money

### Chapter 7: Using Setups (pp. 165-196)
- "Speculation, in its truest sense, calls for anticipation" — Richard D. Wyckoff
- Setups = conditions that must occur before action. Foundation for entry and exit
- Elliott Wave expert story: right 70% on ideas but only 30% of trades made money. Tight stops got him stopped out, then too nervous to re-enter. Confusing a SETUP (Elliott Wave analysis) with a complete TRADING SYSTEM

- **"If you learn one critical thing from this book: a setup is about 10% (or less) of your trading system. Most people will place 90% of their emphasis on finding the right setups, but setups are actually one of the least important parts"**

- **Four Phases of Entry**:
  1. **Market Selection**: liquidity, newness (avoid new issues), market makers/trading rules, volatility (need enough for profit but not so much you're destroyed), capitalization (small-cap for aggressive, large-cap for conservative), how well market fits your trading criteria, portfolio of independent markets
  2. **Market Direction**: assess predominant direction over last 6 months. Ed Seykota: hang chart on wall, walk to other side of room — if trend is obvious, it's a market worth trading. Markets trend 15-20% of time, sideways rest. Need filter for sideways (Perry Kaufman's efficiency ratio)
  3. **Setup Conditions**: windows of opportunity, fundamental conditions, seasonal situations, etc. Many publicly offered "systems" consist of nothing but setups
  4. **Market Timing**: the move you're expecting should START before you enter. Wait for confirmation

- **Setups for Stalking the Market** (short-term):
  a. **Failed-test setups**: market tests previous high/low and fails. Turtle Soup (Connors & Raschke): 20-day breakout highs that fail. Turtles made money on breakouts, Turtle Soup bets against them. Figure 7-1 shows multiple failed breakouts
  b. **Climax reversals / exhaustion patterns**: gap to new extreme, failure to follow through, reversal. Based on: (1) gaps to extremes tend to be filled, (2) days reversing from extremes tend to follow through next morning. Need: signal market reached extreme + high volatility + move in your direction as entry. Figure 7-2: trending day + follow-through as "test" setup
  c. **Retracement setups**: (1) find longer-term trend, (2) get pullback, (3) enter on resumption of trend. Wyckoff: "Don't buy on breakouts. Wait for the retracement test." Figure 7-3 shows bonds with clear breakout (B) followed by retracement (R). Advantages: tight stops, high R:R, good for swing/position trading, way to re-enter after being stopped out

- **Filters vs Setups**: Filters use same data as your system (e.g., optimized indicators on price data) — avoid these. Setups use DIFFERENT data (time, volume, fundamentals, composite) — these add value. Critical difference
  - **Time filters**: seasonal patterns, cycles, astrology — time-based data differs from price
  - **Sequential price data**: retracement pattern is price data but in a specific HIGH-PROBABILITY SEQUENCE
  - **Fundamental data**: supply/demand statistics, earnings, crop reports
  - **Volume data**: Arms Index (TRIN), advance/decline ratios. MA(5) of Arms Index: >1.2 = potential bottom, <0.8 = potential top (1-3 day opportunities)
  - **Composite data**: tick (upticks vs downticks on NYSE), breadth of market. "People who try to trade a market index without looking at anything but the price data of the index could be at a severe disadvantage"

### Chapter 7 (continued): Setups Used by Well-Known Systems

**Volatility setups**: range of prices = activity level, different from price data alone
- Narrow-range setup: market in trend + range contracts to narrow band → potential breakout
  - Idea 1: range of last 5 days ≤ 60% of range of last 50 days
  - Idea 2: inside day (high/low within previous day's range) + narrowest range of X days
- Computer seminar story: asked participants to develop systems returning 100%+/year without optimizing. Most did it via great exits + money management. One exception found narrow-range setup = potential predictor of breakout. "This sort of setup can easily add 10 to 15 cents to your expectancy per dollar risked"

**Business Fundamentals**: earnings, yield, sales, profit margins, stock owner's earnings, book value, EPS, shares outstanding — all different from price data

**Management Information**: who runs the company? Track record? Buffett has strong tenets on management quality

**Stock Market Setups — Three Well-Known Systems:**

1. **William O'Neil's CANSLIM Model**:
   - C = Current earnings per share (up 70%+ over same quarter last year)
   - A = Annual earnings (compounded 5-year rate of 24%+)
   - N = New factor (new product, management, industry change, OR new high price — actually two setups)
   - S = Shares outstanding (small number, under 25 million — median of best performers was 4.8 million)
   - L = Leader (relative strength ranking in top 75-80 percentile, O'Neil says 80%+)
   - I = Institutional sponsorship (some, but not too much — too many institutions = too late + selling pressure if trouble)
   - M = Market direction (75% of stocks follow the overall market, so need positive market as setup)
   - "What most people think of as O'Neil's trading system just consists of his setups"

2. **Warren Buffett's "Value" Model**: buys businesses, not stocks. Never intends to sell. 12 criteria (per Hagstrom):
   - Business setups (3): (1) simple/understandable, (2) consistent operating history, (3) favorable long-term prospects (can raise prices without losing business, no close substitute, no regulation problems)
   - Management setups (3): (4) honest with public (no hiding behind GAAP), (5) rational capital allocation (not reinvesting at below-average returns), (6) not conformists (resist peer imitation, resist using up available funds on copycat projects)
   - Financial setups (4): (7) high return on equity with little debt, (8) owner earnings (net income + depreciation - capital expenditures - working capital needs), (9) high profit margins + systematic cost cutting, (10-12 are entry criteria for Ch 8)
   - "Most of his thought process goes into the decision to enter the market" — virtually all setups. Rarely sells, so his track record validates the approach

3. **Motley Fool "Foolish Four" Approach** (O'Higgins/Downes, Beating the Dow):
   - Simple: from Dow Jones 30 Industrials, buy the 2nd through 5th highest-yielding stocks
   - Allocation: 40% to 2nd highest yield, 20% each to 3rd-5th
   - Highest yielder often a "dog" (yielding up to 30%/year), 2nd best is better
   - Buy Nov 1, sell May 1 (best months for stock market) or buy anytime
   - No stop-loss procedure — purely setups-based with allocation rules
   - "The technique is obviously very simple — as are most techniques that work"

**Futures Market Setups:**

4. **Perry Kaufman's Market Efficiency Model** (from Smarter Trading):
   - Problem: long-term MAs are dependable but respond slowly; short-term = too much noise/whipsaw
   - Solution: adaptive method that speeds up in trends, slows down in sideways
   - Efficiency Ratio = Movement Speed / Volatility
     - Movement speed = close today minus close 10 days ago
     - Volatility = sum of |daily close changes| over 10 days
   - Ranges from 0 (no trend, pure noise) to 1 (perfect trend, no noise)
   - Use as filter: only enter when efficiency ratio is high enough (market trending cleanly)

## Chapter 8: Entry or Market Timing

### Two Entry Approaches

- **Reliability-focused approach**: assumes good stock-picking matters; look for signals better than random
- **High R-multiple approach**: design entries that generate large risk-reward trades regardless of win rate
- Most people chase entry signals and ignore the deeper issue: **positive expectancy depends on position sizing**, not entry perfection
- Systems with 60%+ win rates often fail because they lack edge with proper position sizing

> "Avoiding mistakes makes people stupid and having to be right makes you obsolete." — Robert Kiyosaki

### Trying to Beat Random Entry

- **Tom Basso experiment (1991)**: tested whether exits and position sizing matter more than entries
  - Used random entries on 4 futures markets, exited based on predetermined rules
  - Made money consistently on 80% of runs, even with coin-flip entries
  - Reliability was 38%—average for a trend-following system—yet still profitable
  - Conclusion: **exits and position sizing drive profits**, not entry reliability

### The LeBeau and Lucas Studies

- **Chuck LeBeau and David Lucas** (Technical Traders' Bulletin): tested entry timing with various rule combinations
- Exit rules: fixed stops at 5, 10, 15, 20 days or close-of-business
- Findings: entries performed no better than random when exiting after fixed periods
- Adding protective stops *reduces* entry signal reliability—stops designed to limit risk can turn trades into losses too early
- Trend-following systems make money from few large trades; if entries get whipsawed, reliability suffers

### Common Entry Techniques

#### Channel Breakouts
- Enter on highest high (long) or lowest low (short) of last X days
- Concept: **capture trend from the breakout moment**
- Issues: requires knowing ideal channel length; large drawdowns when whipsawed; capital-intensive (needs $1M+ for most systems)
- Research shows **20-day and 40+ day breakouts still work** per modern testing
- **Donchian channel** (1960s origin): enter when market breaks new N-day extreme
- Famous "Turtles" made billions on 20-day breakouts; later shifted to 40+ days as markets adapted
[CHART: p.203 "Figure 8-1 — 40-day channel breakout pattern in uptrending corn market, marked with 'B' at entry"]

#### Visual Entry Based on Charts
- **Ed Seykota approach**: put long-term charts on wall, visually inspect for obvious trends
  - Price information alone is purer than summary indicators
  - If price clearly trends, odds are 60%+ that trend continues
- **Gut-feeling traders** (e.g., a client making $1M/year on visual patterns alone): intuitive read of trend strength
  - Challenge: difficult to systematize or teach; subject to bias; works if you've internalized price patterns deeply

#### Patterns and Trend Confirmation
- **Short-term patterns**: gaps, spikes, reversals, thrust days, inside days, wide-ranging days
- **Continuation patterns**: triangles, flags, pennants (require directional entry after breakout)
- **Top/bottom patterns**: double bottoms/tops, head-and-shoulders, rounded tops, island reversals
- **Candlestick patterns**: open-close gap ("clear" or "filled"), various named patterns (doji, hammer, hanging man)
- Most patterns difficult to test and computerize; subjective interpretation limits reliability
- **Jack Schwager's *Schwager on Futures***: excellent reference for pattern names and descriptions

> "Most people are much better off just entering in the direction of a major trend than they are looking for a particular pattern in the trend."

### Pure Prediction Approaches
- **Elliott Wave, Gann, countertrend trading**: promise to predict tops and bottoms
- Problem: no evidence these methods outperform reliability above 50%
- Even Michael Jordan of markets analogy: claimed markets perfectly orderly, but couldn't make money; most such predictors fail
- **Prediction is dangerous unless accompanied by a sound system**; volatility breakouts can compensate for prediction gaps

### Volatility Breakouts
- **Sudden dramatic price moves** in one direction, moving 0.8× average true range in a single day
- If true range ~3 points, volatility breakout = 2.4-point move from close
- **J. Wells Wilder Jr.'s system**: multiply average true range by constant (3.0) as trailing stop; called **Average Range Channel (ARC)**
  - Exit is almost exact same as random-entry system (3× average true range)
  - Volatility breakout signals **potential trend end or new trend start**
- Advantages: simple to apply; different from channel breakouts (doesn't require clear trend); good for traders using various models
- **Caution**: don't enter against strong volatility breakout without confirmation

[CHART: p.212 "Figure 8-3 — Volatility breakouts marked with 'B' on bonds chart showing sudden price movement"]

### Directional Movement and the Average Directional Index (ADX)

- **Directional Movement Indicator (DI)**: developed by Wilder; quantifies trend strength
  - **DI+ (up days)**: sum of up days' range movement, divided by average true range over period
  - **DI- (down days)**: sum of down days' range movement, divided by average true range
  - **Directional Movement Index**: |DI+ − DI-| ÷ (DI+ + DI-) × 100 (ranges 0–100)
  - Wilder suggests 14 days; LeBeau/Lucas recommend 14–20 days, with 18 optimal
  
- **ADX (Average Directional Index)**: moving average of the DI; indicates trend *strength* not direction
  - ADX > 15 = trending market
  - Greater increases = stronger trend (jump from 15 to 20 > jump from 25 to 27)
  - Declines indicate weakening trend or market at no longer trending
  - Rising ADX prevents overbought/oversold oscillators from working
  - **Long-term ADX lag**: market may appear to enter downtrend before ADX shows it; disadvantage for early entries

- Entry signals using ADX:
  1. Enter after **DI+ crosses above DI-** (long) or DI- above DI+ (short)
  2. Enter when **ADX rises >4 points in 2 days** (strong trend signal)
  3. Enter at **highest value of last 10 days ADX** after first signal
  4. Use setups (e.g., visual chart inspection) to confirm ADX signals

### Moving Averages and Adaptive Systems

- **Simple moving averages**: sum of last N days ÷ N; updated daily
  - Works in trending markets but whipsawed in consolidations (85% of time markets consolidate)
  - **Displaced moving average**: shift forward N days to avoid lag
  
- **Weighted moving average**: gives more weight to recent data (day 1 = 1×, day 2 = 2×, etc.)
  - **Exponential moving average**: weights most recent data most heavily; equivalent to ~20-day simple MA
  
- **Adaptive moving averages** (**Perry Kaufman**, **Chande/Kroll**): adjust speed per market conditions
  - Kaufman's approach: use **efficiency ratio** based on speed and noise
    - Fast market = smoothing constant closer to 2.666; slow = closer to 0.06452
    - Formula: SC = [ER × (fastest − slowest)] + slowest
    - Must square final SC before applying; high ER = fast adaptation
  - AMA formula: AMA = AMA(yesterday) + SC² × [price − AMA(yesterday)]
  - Overcomes whipsaws better than fixed-period averages in mixed markets

- **Donchian moving average cross** (1960s): buy when 5-day > 20-day, sell when reversed
  - Works in pure trending markets; fails in consolidation (15% of time only)
  - Requires neutral zone: enter long only when 4-day/9-day cross above 18-day average *and* both rising

### Oscillators and Stochastics
- **RSI, Williams %R, stochastics**: designed to find tops and bottoms
- **Most unreliable for entry signals** when used alone; often yield false extremes
- **Better use**: confirm overbought/oversold with RSI after price setup already indicated extreme reversal
- Wilder's approach for RSI: wait for clear trend signal first, then use RSI-to-entry strategy with **narrow stops** and high R-multiple reward

### Designing Your Own Entry Signal: Speed and Acceleration

- **Core insight**: motion in markets parallels physics (velocity, acceleration, momentum)
  - A fast-moving market is more likely to continue fast in same direction
  - Rapid directional change indicates potential momentum shift
  - Slowing velocity before breakout = strong signal market is reconsidering
  
- **Speed (velocity)**: change in price per unit time; measured by distance / days
  - Zero speed = no movement; positive = distance traveled per period
  - Indicators like **momentum** implicitly measure velocity
  
- **Acceleration/Deceleration**: change in velocity itself
  - If market moving fast and still accelerating = likely continues in same direction
  - If decelerating then begins to reverse = potential entry signal
  
- Example: price up 10 points over 10 days (speed = 1 pt/day); if then up 2 pts in one day = deceleration; if then up 4 pts = acceleration
- **Velocity change as entry signal**: when speed changes direction *and* begins to accelerate in opposite direction, potential reversal entry

### Systems Reviewed

#### William O'Neil's System
- Uses **CANSLIM** setups; entry on price strength/volume
- Historically: best trades on first trading day of month (when psychology favors buying)
- Often combines with 20-day breakout on high volume
- Improved by adding 20-day high check: buy only when stock moves to new 20-day high

#### Warren Buffett's Business Evaluation Model
- Not a timing entry technique; instead **fundamental filters**: market is reference point to find foolish offers
- Entry when company meets criteria *and* market undervalues it (Buffett's quote: "When we invest in stocks, we invest in a business")
- Requires discipline and large capital; opposite of short-term timing

#### Motley Fool Foolish-Four Approach
- Finds Dow Jones highest-yield stocks
- Entry: first business day of month; pick highest-yielding 5, skip one, trade 4 (Foolish-Four)
- Entry signal: when stock price breaks above 20-day high *with volume* ≥50% above daily average
- Variations: timing entry on N-day breakout from consolidation

#### Perry Kaufman's Adaptive Trading
- Efficiency ratio blends speed and noise; controls moving-average adaptation
- Filters reduce whipsaws; can map efficiency ratio to moving-average speed range
- More sophisticated than fixed MA but requires parameter tuning per market

#### William Gallagher's Fundamentals Approach
- Evaluate fundamentals *as setup*; when fundamentals strong, enter in direction fundamentals suggest
- Requires expertise to determine market-specific fundamentals
- Fundamental data varies per market; defer to experts for each market's key data
- **Timing trap**: can be right about fundamentals but terribly wrong about timing; needs good timing system

#### Ken Roberts' I-2-3 Reversal
- Two setups before entering: (1) market makes 9-month high/low, (2) market makes I-2-3 reversal
- When market completes I-2-3 and makes new extreme, *then* market may turn around
- Combines with support/resistance: can be traded successfully with right stops/exits
- Creates many whipsaws; requires protective stop and position sizing discipline

### Summary

- Good timing can increase entry reliability but **cannot overcome poor exits or position sizing**
- **Best entry indicators**: channel breakout (40+ days), volatility breakout (0.8× ATR), ADX movement, adaptive moving average
- **Avoid**: pure prediction, oscillators alone, fixed short-period moving averages
- Entry design should reflect your edge: if you understand trend strength (ADX), use it; if visual patterns work for you, test them systematically
- **Velocity and acceleration** offer promising untested approach based on physics analogy—worth exploring with your data
- Most important: once you have an entry, focus on **exits, position sizing, and risk management**

## Chapter 12: What Do You Mean Position Sizing?

### Definition and Core Concept

- **Position sizing** answers the question "how much?" throughout a trade—determining position size at any given time
- It is NOT: how much you will lose on a trade, how to exit profitable trades, diversification, risk control, risk avoidance, or part of the system that maximizes performance
- Position sizing determines **equal opportunity and equal exposure across portfolio elements**, allowing you to scale profits as account grows
- The **snow fight metaphor** (Chapter 6) applies: equity size is the wall; position size determines how many simultaneous positions (snowballs) you can maintain

### The Four Position-Sizing Models

#### Model 1: One Unit Per Fixed Amount of Money

- Trade one unit (100 shares, one contract) per fixed dollar amount (e.g., $50,000 of equity)
- **Advantage**: Simple; allows you to always take a position even with limited funds
- **Disadvantage**: Does not allow rapid exposure increase during winning streaks; treats all investments as equal when they are not; small accounts can be easily wiped out

**Testing Example**: $1 million initial equity, 10 commodities (1981–1991)
- 55-/21-day breakout system: $237,457 profit, 5.75% compounded annual return, max drawdown 7.12%
[CHART: p.302 "Figure 12-3 — Dow Jones stocks traded with fixed-amount model, 100 shares/$100,000"]

#### Model 2: Equal Units for Stock Traders

- Divide capital into equal units; trade one unit per investment opportunity
- Example: $50,000 account split into 5 units of $10,000 each; buy 100 shares of multiple stocks weighted equally
- **Advantage**: Gives equal weighting to all investments; allows traders to see exact leverage and product control; better for small accounts
- **Disadvantage**: Slow exposure growth; small accounts struggle to increase beyond one unit; inefficient leverage across diversified instruments

**Example: Stocks A–E**
- Stock A: $100/share × 100 shares = $10,000
- Stock E: $7/share × 1,428 shares = $9,996
- Each position = ~$10,000 with full profit/loss responsibility
[CHART: p.291 "Table 12-3 — Distribution of Funds as Shares in Equal Units Model"]

#### Model 3: Percent Risk Model

- Control position size as a **percentage of equity at risk**, determining worst-case loss per trade
- Example: $50,000 account, gold at $380/oz with $370 stop = $1,000 risk per contract
- 2.5% risk limit = $1,250 allowable loss, allowing 1.25 contracts
- **Best reward-to-risk ratio**: Occurs around 25% risk per position, but requires tolerating 84% drawdown
- **Critical insight**: Drawdown recovery requirements are asymmetrical—50% loss requires 100% gain to recover

[CHART: p.283 "Table 12-1 — Recovery after Drawdowns (5%, 10%, 15% ... 90% drawdown levels)"]

**Testing Results** (55-/21-day breakout with $1M equity):
- 1% risk: $1,844,493 profit, 20.52% annual return, 14.14% max drawdown
- 2.5% risk: Best reward-to-risk at 25%, but 84% drawdown tolerance required
- Model rejects tight stops; 1 R-risk stop not achievable with tight stops over many instruments
[CHART: p.293 "Table 12-4 — 55/21-Day Breakout System with Risk Position Sizing"]

For example, most long-term trend followers use trailing stops...in addition, most trend followers are usually using a model that makes money 40 to 50 percent of the time and has a reward-to-risk ratio of 20 to 2.5."

#### Model 4: Percent Volatility Model

- Equate position size to **volatility as a percentage of equity**
- Example: Gold 10-day average true range = $3 = $300 per contract; allow 2% equity exposure = $1,000 limit = 3.3 contracts
- Volatility-adjusted: High volatility instruments get smaller positions; low volatility get larger positions
- **Advantage**: Equalizes expected portfolio fluctuations; sophisticated; suits tight-stop traders
- **Disadvantage**: Must reject some trades when they're too risky; daily volatility is not the actual risk; Gallacher points out exposure remains unequal

**Key Insight**: Best reward-to-risk occurs at **2.5% volatility allocation**, producing 67–86% annual gain with 69–86% drawdown

- **Doing that requires a lot of discipline**

> "However, doing that requires a lot of discipline." — Tharp

#### Seven Steps to Disciplined Trading

1. **Have a trading plan and test it** — develop confidence and a strong
   understanding of the concept you are trading
2. **Assume total responsibility** for everything that happens to you — even
   if a broker rips you off, ask how you were involved; when you stop
   repeating the same mistakes, you can be successful
3. **Find your weaknesses and work on them** — keep a diary, notice common
   emotional patterns, assume they are yours to fix
4. **Do global planning** — list everything that could go wrong, determine
   response plans, rehearse until second nature; knowing how to respond to
   the unexpected is the key to success
5. **Daily self-analysis** — you are the most important factor in your
   trading; ask how you are feeling, what is going on in your life;
   awareness reduces the control these issues have over you
6. **Determine what could go wrong today** — mentally rehearse each option
   until you have it down pat; athletes do extensive mental rehearsal and
   traders must too
7. **Daily debriefing** — ask: "Did I follow my rules?" If yes, pat yourself
   on the back; if you followed rules and lost money, still pat yourself on
   the back twice; if no, determine why and mentally rehearse the correct
   response

> "Those seven steps should have a gigantic influence on anyone's trading."

### The Most Important Thing Traders Can Do

- **Take total responsibility** for everything that happens — in the market
  and in your life
- The marble game illustration: audience given $10,000 play equity, 40% of
  marbles are losers, one marble loses 5-to-1 (a -5R multiple), game runs
  100 draws — large losing streaks will occur
- Tharp is "sneaky" — when someone draws a losing marble, he asks them to
  keep drawing until they hit a winner, meaning one person draws the entire
  losing streak
- Result: half the audience goes broke — but when asked "Is the person who
  drew the losing streak responsible for your losses?" many raise their hands
- This reveals the failure: they went broke through poor **money management**
  but prefer to blame external factors
- **The most astute traders always look to themselves to correct mistakes**;
  they clear out psychological issues that prevent profitability and continue
  to profit from their mistakes

> "In life and in trading, there will always be someone who picks the losing
> marbles. Only when you finally realize that the result somehow depends upon
> you will you start learning to correct your mistakes. In life and in
> trading... correct your mistakes."

### Beliefs and Final Words of Wisdom

- **You cannot trade the markets — you can only trade your beliefs about
  the market**; therefore, knowing exactly what those beliefs are is critical
- Key beliefs about yourself (not the market) also determine success: what
  do you think you are capable of doing? Is trading important to you? How
  worthy of success do you believe yourself to be?
- **Weak beliefs about yourself can undermine trading with a great system**
- First advice: look to yourself as the source of everything that happens;
  identify common patterns and fix them — your chances of success rise
  dramatically
- Games reflect behavior — if you cannot succeed in a positive-expectancy
  game (like the IITM online game), you will face the same psychological
  issues in real markets
- Final recommendation: **read this book four or five times** — people
  filter through their belief systems and miss material on first reading;
  subsequent readings surface new gems and make the principles second nature

## Appendix II: Key Terms Defined (selected critical terms)

- **Adaptive Moving Average** — moving average that is either quick or slow
  depending on market efficiency; signals entry
- **Anti-Martingale Strategy** — position size increases when winning; any
  equity-based position sizing is anti-martingale
- **Average True Range (ATR)** — average over last X days of the true range:
  largest of (today's high − today's low), (today's high − yesterday's
  close), (today's low − yesterday's close)
- **Bias** — tendency to move in a particular direction; most biases in this
  book are psychological biases
- **Chaos Theory** — theory that one moves from stability to chaos; used to
  explain explosive market moves and nonrandomness of markets
- **Degree of Freedom** — number of independent observations minus parameters
  estimated; more degrees of freedom helps describe past price movement but
  hurts prediction of future movement
- **Dev-stop** — stop loss developed by **Cynthia Kase** based on standard
  deviation of price movement
- **Discretionary Trading** — depends on trader instincts vs. systematic
  approach; best discretionary traders develop a systematic approach then use
  discretion in exits and position sizing
- **Drawdown** — decrease in account value from losing trades or paper losses
- **Expectancy** — how much you can expect to make per dollar risked over
  many trades; best stated in R-multiple terms
- **Gambler's Fallacy** — belief that a loss is due after winners or a gain
  after losers
- **Holy Grail System** — mythical system that perfectly follows the market,
  always right, large gains and zero drawdowns; doesn't exist; the real
  meaning is that the "secret" is inside you
- **Judgmental Heuristics** — shortcuts the human mind uses to make decisions;
  lead to biases that often cause people to lose money
- **Largest Expected Equity Drop (LEED)** — term used by **Gallacher** to
  assist in limiting risk; refers to the largest drop in equity you will
  tolerate
- **Low-risk Idea** — an idea with positive expectancy traded at a risk level
  that allows for the worst possible short-term situation so that the
  long-term expectancy can be realized
- **Martingale Strategy** — position size increases after losses; classic
  form doubles bet size after each loss
- **Maximum Adverse Excursion (MAE)** — maximum loss attributable to price
  movement against the position during the life of a trade
- **Mental Rehearsal** — psychological process of preplanning an event or
  strategy in one's mind before actually doing it
- **Modeling** — process of determining how some form of peak performance is
  accomplished and then passing on that training to others
- **Negative Expectancy System** — system in which you will never make money
  long-term; all casino games are designed this way; includes some highly
  reliable systems (high hit rate) that tend to have occasional large losses
- **NeuroLinguistic Programming (NLP)** — psychological training developed by
  **Richard Bandler** and **John Grinder**; forms the foundation for the
  science of modeling excellence in human behavior; what is usually taught in
  NLP seminars are techniques developed from modeling, not the modeling
  process itself
- **Optimization** — finding parameters and indicators that best predict price
  changes in historical data; a highly optimized system usually does a poor
  job of predicting future prices
- **Peak-to-Trough Drawdown** — maximum drawdown from highest equity peak to
  lowest equity trough prior to reaching a new equity high
- **Position Sizing** — the most important of the six key elements of
  successful trading; determines how large a position you take throughout a
  trade; in most cases, algorithms are based upon current equity
- **Positive Expectancy** — describes a system or game that will make money
  over the long term if played at a low-enough risk level
- **Post-dictive Error** — error made when you take into account future data
  you should not know; e.g., buying on the open each day if the closing price
  is up gives a great system — but only because you are making a post-dictive
  error
- **R-multiple** — all profits expressed as a multiple of the initial risk
  (R); a 10R multiple is a profit 10 times the initial risk
- **Relative Strength Indicator (RSI)** — futures market indicator described
  by **J. Wells Wilder Jr.**, ascertains overbought/oversold conditions;
  based on close-to-close price change
- **Reward-to-Risk Ratio** — average return on account (yearly basis) divided
  by maximum peak-to-trough drawdown; any ratio over 3 is excellent
- **System** — a set of rules for trading; complete system has: (1) setup
  conditions, (2) entry signal, (3) worst-case disaster stop, (4) profit
  taking exit, (5) position sizing algorithm; many commercial systems lack
  all criteria
- **Trailing Stop** — stop loss order that moves with the prevailing trend;
  typically used to exit profitable trades
- **Trend-following** — systematic process of capturing extreme market moves
  while staying in the market as long as it continues to move
- **Validity** — indicates how "real" something is; does it measure what it
  is supposed to measure?
- **Volatility** — range of prices in a given time period; highly volatile
  markets have a large daily price range; one of the most useful concepts in
  trading
- **Volatility Breakout** — entry technique triggered when the market moves
  a specific amount from the open based on previous daily ranges; e.g., a 1.5
  ATR volatility breakout enters if the market moves more than 1.5 times the
  average true range of the last X days from today's open

## Chapter (Part 3): Understanding the Key Parts of Your System

### Maximum Adverse Excursion (MAE) — Winning vs. Losing Trades

- Data from a **channel breakout system** with a **three-times-ATR stop**, BP trades 1985–1992
- **MAE** recorded for each trade in both dollar amount and as a ratio to **average true range (ATR)**
- **Table 9-1** separates winning and losing trades, showing MAE in $ and in ATR units

### Key Findings from the Data

- No winning trades had MAE greater than **1.5× ATR**
- Only **3 of 24 winning trades** (12.5%) had MAE above **1× ATR**
- **66.7% of losing trades** had MAE above 1× ATR
- Almost half of all losing trades had MAE above **1.5× ATR**
- Mean MAE for all losing trades: **1.44 average true ranges**

> "Good trades seldom go too far against us."

- When you compile MAE data with a big enough stop, the MAE of winning trades will **seldom go below a certain value** — good trades don't move far against you

### Practical Application

- Constantly check what the MAE threshold value is (it shifts as markets change)
- Knowing it lets you use **much tighter stops** than originally anticipated
- Benefits of tighter stops:
  - Smaller losses when stopped out
  - Ability to make **multiple attempts** to capture a big move
  - **Bigger R-multiples** on winning trades (same profit, smaller initial risk)

### Tight Stops

- **Tight stops** are appropriate under specific conditions:
  1. Predicting a **major market change** and the market is confirming that prediction
  2. Trading **shorter time-frame data**
- Use is also a function of **personal risk tolerance**
- Advantages of tight stops:
  - Lose much less money when the trade is wrong
  - Small loss allows **multiple re-entry attempts** to catch a big move

## Chapter 9: Knowing When to Fold 'Em

### Three-Moving-Averages System
- **R.C. Allen** popularized: entry signal when both shorter averages cross the longer
- Shortest average on top (or bottom) = entry; stop triggered when short crosses medium
- No reversal signal until *both* short and medium averages cross the longer average

### Channel Breakouts (recap)
- Enter when price makes new high for last 40 days; stop = new low for last 10 days
- Advantage: gives price a lot of room, filters noise
- Disadvantage: gives back large profits — stop doubles as protective stop *and* profit-taking exit

### Support and Resistance Stops
- Trader identifies support/resistance areas on chart; stops placed at extremes of those levels
- Some traders add a **constant** to the stop (entry price + support area + buffer) to avoid
  being stopped out at obvious levels where many stops cluster

### Time Stops
- Premise: if a position doesn't move in your favor quickly, it probably won't
- **Time stop**: exits position after a fixed period if no profit (or profit above arbitrary level)
- Practical method: treat each day in a trade as an entirely new day — if you can't justify
  being in the trade today, close it out
- Best suited for **short-term traders**; poor fit for long-term traders who can't easily re-enter
- Before using: test how often a position does nothing for N days, then takes off — avoid if
  you'd miss major moves; use if it mainly just cuts losses faster

### Discretionary and Psychological Stops

- **Discretionary stops**: used by many top professionals with strong market intuition —
  *not recommended for amateur or beginning traders*
- **Psychological stop**: ideal for most market participants
  - If you're not in the market for the long haul, you should consider psychological stops
  - Long-term trend followers may have trouble: one good trade can make a whole year —
    a poorly timed psychological exit can cost the entire year's gains
  - Risk: when psychologically off-balance, you'll likely exit right before the big trade

### Psychological Stop Triggers (times to get out of the market)
Six life circumstances where the human being is the most important — and most vulnerable — factor:
1. Going through a **divorce or separation**
2. **Death of a significant person** in your life / hospitalization
3. **Birth of a child**
4. **Moving home or office**
5. **Psychologically exhausted or burnt out**
6. *So excited about the market that you see your...* (continues next page)

> "There are certain time periods when the most important factor in your trading-you, the human being-is nowhere near 100 percent. These are the times you should just consider getting out of the market."

## Part 3 / Chapter 9: Knowing When to Fold 'Em (pp. 252–253)

### Ken Roberts' 1-2-3 Methodology

- Market makes a **9-month high or low**, then forms a **1-2-3 pattern**
- Entry signal: market hits a new price extreme in the opposite direction — i.e., market passes point 2 on the 1-2-3 pattern again
- Stop loss: placed just beyond point 3 on the chart

- Author's critique: users would be better off with a stop based on a **statistical extreme**
- Suggested alternatives:
  - (1) **3× the ATR**
  - (2) ATR plus **3× the standard deviation of the ATR**
  - (3) a **dev-stop** (copyrighted indicator by **Cynthia A. Kase**)
  - (4) an estimate of the **MAE** (Maximum Adverse Excursion) with stop just beyond that

### Chapter Notes

- Note 1: Short-term trading is difficult due to **very large commissions** from stockbrokers; discount Internet trading has improved this
- Note 2: Stock exchanges encourage margin at 50% and teach people they could lose it all — justified because most people lack a plan and are **psychologically wired to lose money**
- Note 3: 1-2-3 pattern suggested by **J. Wells Wilder** in *New Concepts in Technical Trading Systems*
- Note 4: **MAE** concept from Sweeney, *Campaign Trading*
- Note 6: If slippage + commissions = $100/trade, subtract $600 from a $1,500 profit → effectively a 9-R profit, not 20-R; **transaction costs are the major factor in short-term trading success**
- Note 8: **LeBeau and Lucas** have strong discussion of stops — see Recommended Readings

## Chapter 10: How to Take Profits

### Summary of Exit Categories

- Exits don't give control over the market, but they control
  whether you profit or lose — and how much
- Four general exit categories reviewed:
  - Exits that minimize initial loss
  - Exits that maximize profits
  - Exits that minimize how much profit you give back
  - Psychological exits
- Each category has multiple strategies with significant overlap
- Recommendation: use **simple multiple exits** — easy to
  conceptualize, minimal optimization required, best meet
  system objectives

### Exits Used by Common Systems

#### William O'Neil's CANSLIM System

- Basic profit-taking rule: take **20% profit** whenever achieved
- Stop loss ~8% → fundamental exit = **2.5-R profit**
- O'Neil tempers this with **36 other selling rules**:
  - Some are exceptions to the basic rule
  - Some are reasons to sell early
  - Plus 8 additional rules on when to hold a stock
- Tharp defers to O'Neil's book for full detail; focus here is
  how the system fits the chapter's framework

#### Warren Buffett's Business Approach

- Buffett generally does not sell — two stated reasons:
  1. Selling triggers **capital gains tax** (turns over profits
     to U.S. government)
  2. If a company delivers excellent returns on capital, why
     exit?
  3. Selling also incurs **transaction costs**
- Tharp's critique: the "Buffett never sells" idea is
  **more myth than fact**
  - Buffett himself has not written about his own exit strategy
  - Others have projected their own entry-biased thinking
    onto him
- Key example: Buffett owned ~20% of world silver supply
  (announced early 1998)
  - Silver pays no dividends and has **storage/protection costs**
  - Without a planned exit strategy for silver, Tharp argues
    Buffett would be making one of his biggest investing mistakes
  - If he *does* have an exit plan, then he likely has exit
    plans for his stock positions too — just undisclosed

> "If Warren Buffett does not have a planned exit strategy for
> that silver, then, in my opinion, he is making one of the
> biggest mistakes of his investing career."
> — Van Tharp

- Conclusion: most writing about Buffett reflects authors'
  **entry/setup bias**, ignoring exit strategy entirely

#### Motley Fool Foolish-Four Method

- **Once-a-year rebalancing** into the 2nd, 3rd, 4th, and 5th
  best dividend-paying stocks in the **Dow Jones Industrials**
- If the same stocks appear year-over-year → no exit taken

### The Cost of Trading Opportunity

- There is a definite cost to trading: market maker takes an edge, broker takes a cut — **your profit is what remains**
- **Cost per trade** is part of the expectancy equation
- The fewer trades you make, the less cost matters per trade

### Cost Matters More for High-Frequency Traders
- **Long-term trend followers** (low trade frequency): trade cost is nearly irrelevant vs. potential $5,000/trade profit — a $100 commission is noise
- **Short-term traders** (high frequency): if average profit per trade is $50, a $100 trading cost is devastating — more than wipes out the edge
- Rule: the shorter your time horizon, the more **trade cost** becomes a central variable

### Look for Low Commissions
- Unless a broker provides specific essential services, always seek **best execution at lowest possible commission**
