## INTRODUCTION (pp. 10-18)

### Risk as Physical Experience
- Risk-taking reminds you that you have a body — risk threatens to hurt you
- Fight-or-flight response activates even in non-lethal risk (sports, finance)
- Churchill on polo match in India: "Rarely have I seen such strained faces on both sides... Far graver crises cause less keen emotion"
- Financial risk triggers same biological reactions as physical danger
- Money is a "powerful token distilling many of the threats and opportunities we faced over eons of evolutionary time"

### Financial Risk Carries Longer Consequences
- Physical risk: defense reactions switch on/off in minutes or hours
- Financial risk: change in income/social rank lingers for months/years
- We're not built for long-term biochemical disturbances
- Winning streak → euphoric, manic, foolhardy, puffed up with self-importance
- Losing streak → fear, stress hormones linger, pathological risk-aversion, depression
- Medical consequences: viral infections, high blood pressure, abdominal fat, gastric ulcers
- "Financial risk-taking is as much a biological activity, with as many medical consequences, as facing down a grizzly bear"

### The Body-Brain Connection
- Economics treats risk assessment as purely intellectual (calculating probabilities, returns)
- Coates wants to "add some guts" — neuroscience shows we do more than think about risk
- Bodies prepare physically: emergency network of physiological circuitry switches on
- Body and brain become "a single entity, united in the face of challenge"
- Normally this fusion gives fast reactions and gut feelings for successful risk-taking
- But chemical surges can overwhelm → irrational exuberance OR pessimism

### INCOMING! — The Trading Floor Scenario
- Trading floor as "parabolic reflector" — gathers signals from faraway places
- Two traders: Logan (mortgage-backed bonds) and Scott (arbitrage desk)
- Fed meeting day — expected to leave rates unchanged
- Pre-noon: subtle ripple in prices, most don't consciously notice
- But bodies register: breathing quickens, muscles tense, blood pressure rises
- Locus ceruleus (brain stem) — responds to novelty, promotes arousal state
- When stable correlations between asset prices broke down, locus ceruleus tripped alarm
- Rumor spreads: Fed may raise rates unexpectedly

### The Biological Preparation
- Trading is physical task requiring fast reactions, stamina, fuel (glucose)
- Bodies prepare: metabolism speeds up, energy stores readied, breathing accelerates
- Heart rates speed up, immune cells position at vulnerable points (skin)
- Blood redistributed: away from gut/reproductive organs → arms, thighs, lungs, heart, brain
- Steroid hormones surge — turbo-charging the body

### Key Hormones
- **Testosterone**: produced by testes, primes for challenge/competition; increases haemoglobin, oxygen capacity, confidence, appetite for risk. "The hour between dog and wolf" = moment of transformation
- **Adrenalin**: from adrenal glands atop kidneys; quickens reactions, taps glucose deposits in liver
- **Cortisol**: stress hormone from rim of adrenal glands; stimulates dopamine release along pleasure pathways. At low levels it thrills. Cortisol + dopamine = "one of the most addictive drugs known to the human brain"

## CHAPTER 1: The Biology of a Market Bubble (pp. 19-20, continues...)

### The Feeling of a Bubble
- Coates's interest in biology of markets dates to 1990s Wall Street
- Traded derivatives at Goldman Sachs, then Merrill Lynch, then ran desk at Deutsche Bank
- Dot.com bubble: Nasdaq from below 600 (1991) → 2,000 (1998) → 5,000+ (2000)
- Asian Financial Crisis 1998 reined Nasdaq back ~500 points, then it shot up again
- Return exceeded 300% in year and a half
- Rally unprecedented in speed and magnitude; paucity of hard financial data supporting it
- Legendary investors who bet against trend retired in disgust (Julian Robertson/Tiger Capital)
- Keynes: markets can remain irrational longer than you can remain solvent

### Bubble Culture & Dot.com Folly
- Kozmo.com: NYC startup promising free delivery within an hour — bicycle messengers running red lights, went bankrupt
- Investors not engaged in rational choice OR game theory — little evidence of either thought process
- When pressed, investors shot off starbursts of trendy concepts like "convergence"
- History was irrelevant, statistics counted for little
- "Fear and greed" account misses something: investors naively and fervently believe they're buying the future
- Bull market validates beliefs → profits bring feelings of euphoria and omnipotence
- "Assessment of risk is replaced by judgements of certainty — they just know what is going to happen"
- Tom Wolfe: traders as "Masters of the Universe"
- Physical transformation: walk more erect, more purposeful, bearing says "Don't mess with me"
- Traders' behavior showed symptoms of clinical mania: racing thoughts, less sleep, clubbing till 4 am, increased sexual behavior, overconfident risk-taking with worsening risk-reward trade-offs

### Hubris Syndrome
- David Owen (Lord Owen) — former British Foreign Secretary, neurologist by training
- Identified "Hubris Syndrome" in political/business leaders
- Characterized by: recklessness, inattention to detail, overwhelming self-confidence, contempt for others
- "A disorder of the possession of power... associated with overwhelming success held for a period of years and with minimal constraint on the leader"
- Manic behavior on Wall Street comes not just from wealth but from feeling of consummate power

### Is There an Irrational Exuberance Molecule?
- Women were relatively immune to dot.com frenzy (only 10-20% of men's testosterone levels)
- Overconfidence and risk-taking during bubble feels chemical, not rational — "it feels as if it is driven by a chemical"
- "Traders on a roll are traders under the influence of a drug that has the power to transform them into different people"
- After dot.com burst: traders like "revellers with a hangover, heads cradled in hands"
- NYT day after 1929 crash: "Wall St. was a street of vanished hopes, of curiously silent apprehension and a sort of paralyzed hypnosis"
- Randolph Nesse (Michigan psychiatrist, 1999): dot.com bubble differed because traders' brains changed by Prozac and antidepressants — "if investor caution is being inhibited by psychotropic drugs, bubbles could grow larger"
- Others blamed increasing cocaine use among bankers (mostly exaggerated for traders/managers)
- Coates's insight: traders' bodies were producing an endogenous narcotic — what was this bull-market molecule?

### Rockefeller University & Hormones
- Linda Wilbrecht doing PhD on neurogenesis at Rockefeller
- Bruce McEwen (Rockefeller) — landmark research mapping steroid receptors in the brain
- With colleagues Donald Pfaff and Jay Weiss
- Before McEwen: hormones understood via simple thermostat model (hypothalamus → gland → hormone → feedback)
- McEwen's breakthrough: steroid receptors exist in brain regions OTHER than hypothalamus
- Hormones don't just affect body — they return to the brain, changing how we think and behave
- A single steroid hormone can alter almost every function of body (growth, metabolism, immune function) AND brain (mood, memory, behavior)
- Steroids evolved to coordinate body, brain, and behavior during archetypal situations (fighting, fleeing, feeding, hunting, mating, status struggles)
- "Steroids, like a drill sergeant, ensure that body and brain fall into line as a single functioning unit"
- Ancient Greeks: archetypal moments feel like visitations by the gods — but it's actually hormones

### The Winner Effect — Key Model
- At Rockefeller, Coates found the "winner effect" from animal behavior research
- Two males fight for turf/mate → anticipatory testosterone surge (chemical bracer)
- Testosterone increases blood oxygen capacity, lean-muscle mass, confidence, appetite for risk
- Winner emerges with even higher testosterone, loser with lower
- Winner carries elevated testosterone into next round → androgenic priming gives competitive edge
- Creates positive feedback loop explaining winning and losing streaks in sports
- BUT: at some point elevated steroids begin to have OPPOSITE effect
- Animals on winning streaks start more fights, spend more time in the open → increased mortality
- "As testosterone levels rise, confidence and risk-taking segue into overconfidence and reckless behaviour"
- This model perfectly describes trader behavior as bull market morphs into bubble
- Could also explain why women were relatively unaffected (10-20% testosterone of men)

### Andrew Sullivan's Testosterone Account
- Sullivan (NYT Magazine, April 2000): described injecting testosterone every two weeks
- "Within hours, I feel a deep surge of energy... less edgy than a double espresso, but just as powerful"
- "My wit is quicker, my mind faster, but my judgment is more impulsive"
- "I feel braced" — could have been describing what it feels like to be a trader on a roll

### Irrational Pessimism — Cortisol
- If testosterone = molecule of irrational exuberance, cortisol = molecule of irrational pessimism
- Cortisol: main hormone of stress response, works with adrenalin
- Adrenalin: fast-acting (seconds), half-life 2-3 minutes — designed for quick escape
- Cortisol: kicks in for long siege — shuts down expensive functions (digestion, reproduction, growth, storage, immune function), breaks down energy stores, flushes glucose into blood
- Short-term cortisol: beneficial — increases arousal, sharpens attention, slight thrill
- Long-term elevated cortisol: opposite effects — anxiety, selective recall of disturbing memories, tendency to find danger where none exists
- Chronic stress → irrational risk-aversion among traders and managers
- **Core hypothesis**: testosterone rises in bull markets → increases risk-taking → morphs rally into bubble. Cortisol rises in bear markets → makes traders irrationally risk-averse → exaggerates sell-off into crash
- Steroid hormones in traders' bodies may systematically shift risk preferences across the business cycle, destabilizing it

### Coates's Career Pivot
- Concluded hypothesis needed testing → retired from Wall Street
- Returned to Cambridge (where he'd done PhD in economics)
- Spent 4 years retraining in neuroscience and endocrinology
- Designed experimental protocol to test winner effect in financial markets
- Ran studies on London trading floors — results supported hypothesis

### Mind and Body in Financial Markets — The Philosophical Problem
- Body-brain feedback is new even within physiology/neuroscience, barely touched economics
- Root cause: Western philosophical tradition of mind-body dualism
- Pythagoras → Plato (eternal rational soul in decaying flesh) → St. Paul/Christian dogma → Descartes ("ghost in the machine")
- Platonic dualism: widely disputed in philosophy, ignored in neuroscience — but LINGERS in economics
- Neo-classical economics assumes volitional, rational behavior — "walking computers"
- "As if" defense: people behave on average as if they calculated rationally
- Behavioral economics (Thaler, Kahneman, Tversky) challenged rationality assumption but still focused on psychology, not physiology
- Kahneman: "we think with our body" — research on physiology of attention and arousal

### Aristotle vs. Plato
- For Aristotle: no mind-body split. First and greatest biologist.
- "Think mortal thoughts" — based politics/ethics on actual human behavior, not idealized
- Raphael's School of Athens: Plato points to heavens, Aristotle gestures to the world
- Economics needs to "put the body back into the economy"
- Current situation: "worst of both worlds" — unstable biology + risk-management practices that increase limits during bubble and decrease during crash + bonus schemes rewarding high-variance trading
- Nature and nurture conspire to create recurrent disasters
- Policy suggestion: more even balance in banks between men and women, young and old (different biology)

### What Unites Us — The Brain's Central Operation
- Brain's basic operation is organization of MOVEMENT, not pure thought
- Reasoning skills inextricably tangled with motor circuits
- Body and brain evolved together to physically pursue opportunities or flee threats
- Brain sparks electrical activity in muscles and organs → hormones flood body → somatic/visceral signals feed back to brain, biasing attention, mood, memory
- More accurate to speak of "whole-person response" rather than separating brain and body
- During powerful moments (flow, love, risk-taking, fear) — mind-body split dissolves, they merge as one

## CHAPTER 2: Thinking with Your Body (pp. 41-60)

### Why Animals Can't Play Sports
- Evolutionary advances occurred in BODY not just brain: vocal cords, opposable thumb, upright posture, lack of fur
- Lack of fur + upright posture = minimized sun exposure + better cooling → could chase fur-covered prey to heat exhaustion
- On African savannah: didn't need to outrun or outfight prey, merely "outcool" it
- Sci-fi view (big brain, withered body) is wrong — brain became MORE tightly bound to body over time
- True miracle of evolution: development of advanced control systems synchronizing body and brain
- Body sends info to brain "freighted with suggestions, sometimes merely whispered, at others forcefully shouted"
- These informational prods = desires, emotions, gut feelings

### The Tunicate — Why We Have Brains
- Daniel Wolpert (Cambridge): tunicate (sea squirt) born with brain, eyespot, otolith
- Swims freely searching for feeding grounds; once it cements to seafloor, it EATS its own brain
- "If you do not need to move, you do not need a brain"
- Brain evolved primarily to plan and execute physical movement
- Brain consumes ~20% of daily energy — metabolically expensive
- "Motor chauvinists" (Wolpert's term): thought itself proceeds by hijacking algorithms originally developed for planning movements
- Andy Clark (Edinburgh): "we have inherited a mind on the hoof"

### The Complexity of Movement
- Steven Pinker: understanding quantum physics, decoding genome, sending rocket to moon are simple compared to reverse-engineering human movement
- Walking: we "repeatedly tip over and break our fall in the nick of time" — "no one has yet figured out how we do it"
- True genius of nervous system seen in child building Lego or jogger on uneven terrain
- We can beat chess grandmaster with computer but can't build robot with 8-year-old's dexterity
- Humans' true physical achievement: ability to LEARN movements that don't come naturally (ballet, guitar, gymnastics, skiing)
- Downhill skier: 90+ mph, carving turns on sheer ice, milliseconds from accident — no animal can do this
- Master pianist: all ten fingers working simultaneously at speeds eye can't follow, yet modulated for artistic meaning
- Shakespeare: "In form and moving how express and admirable! In action how like an Angel!"

### Neo-cortex and Cerebellum
- Neo-cortex: home to choice, planning, higher thought — BUT evolved together with expanded cortico-spinal tract (nerve bundle controlling musculature)
- Neo-cortex first gave us ability to learn new movements (making tools, throwing spear, riding horse)
- Cerebellum: actually OUTGREW the neo-cortex; stores memories of how to do things (ride bike, play flute)
- Acts like operating system for nervous system — makes neural operations faster, more efficient (like extra RAM)
- Coordinates physical actions, gives precision and split-second timing
- When impaired (drunk) → slow, uncoordinated
- Also streamlines neo-cortex performance
- Modern humans may have had SMALLER neo-cortex than Neanderthals but LARGER cerebellum → more efficient OS → more brainpower

### Revving the Brain
- Movement needs energy → brain must organize support operations (fuel, oxygen, blood flow, cooling, waste venting)
- Thoughts are decisions to DO something → come with physical implications
- Thinking about options triggers rapid series of somatic shifts: eyes widen/squint, pupils dilate, skin flushes/blanches
- "All thoughts involving choice of action involve a kaleidoscopic shift from one bodily state to another. Choice is a whole-body experience."
- Stress: unpleasant aspects (nervous stomach, high BP, elevated glucose) are gastro-intestinal, cardiovascular, metabolic, attentional PREPARATION for impending physical effort
- Gut feelings = changes taking place in bodies of traders/investors as they prepare appropriate physical response
- Generated faster than consciousness can keep up → transmitted to parts of brain with only dim awareness

### Controlling Our Internal Weather — Homeostasis
- Brain stem (reptile brain): controls automatic reflexes (breathing, heart rate, sweating, blinking, startle, pattern generators for chewing/walking)
- Acts as life-support system; other brain parts can be damaged and you survive in a coma — as long as brain stem operates
- Evolutionary progression: amphibians (can't control water evaporation) → reptiles (retain water, but cold-blooded, dependent on sun) → mammals (thermoregulation, internal temperature control)
- Thermoregulation metabolically expensive: idling mammal burns 5-10x energy of idling reptile
- But mammals = "like tanks" — could move farther, faster than primitive foes; required better supply-line management (homeostatic circuitry)
- Humans took on even more bodily control → more advanced nervous system + dialogue between body and brain

### Key Brain Regions for Body Regulation
- **Hypothalamus**: found by projecting lines from bridge of nose and front of ears; regulates hormones, eating, sleeping, sodium levels, water retention, reproduction, aggression; main integration site for emotional behavior; orchestrates coherent emotional responses (angry cat: hissing + arched back + fluffed fur + adrenalin = assembled by hypothalamus)
- **Amygdala**: assigns emotional significance to events; without it, grizzly bear = large moving object, nothing more; registers danger externally AND internally (signs of danger in body like rapid breathing → can trigger runaway anxiety/panic attacks)
- **Insula**: collects interoceptive information (perception of internal organs — heart, lungs, liver); assembled into image of overall body state; pathways to insula present only in primates; awareness of overall body state may be uniquely human

### Interoception — Bud Craig's Research
- Bud Craig (University of Arizona): mapped nervous circuitry for interoception
- We have "sense organs that point inwards" — receptors sensing pain, temperature, chemical gradients, tissue stretching, immune activation throughout the body
- Vagus nerve: superhighway from abdomen to brain, collecting info from gut, pancreas, heart, lungs
- Information channeled through integration sites → assembled in the INSULA into unified experience
- Craig + Damasio + Bechara: gut feelings, emotions, rationality, even self-consciousness may be advanced tools evolved to regulate the body
- Body = "éminence grise" standing behind the brain, applying pressure at the right point and time

## CHAPTER 3: The Speed of Thought (pp. 59-60, continues...)

### A Wake-Up Call on the Treasury Desk
- Setting: large Wall Street investment bank, near Stock Exchange and Fed, crisp March morning
- 7 am: bankers trickle from subway (Broadway, Broad Street, Bowling Green) and taxis
- 31st floor: ~1,000 desks, each with half a dozen screens, grid-work of aisles
- Art Deco building visible from side window — ghosts of Jazz Age
- Three departments upstairs: bonds (fixed income), currencies, commodities; equities downstairs
- Split between traders and salespeople
- Salespeople convince clients (pension funds, insurance companies, mutual funds) to invest money or execute trades with bank's traders

### The DuPont Trade — How Trading Works
- DuPont Pension Fund calls: needs to invest $750 million in 10-year US Treasury bonds
- Fund manager gives trade to Esmee (salesperson), not to bigger banks — personal relationship matters
- Esmee yells to Martin (Treasury desk trader): "offer 750 tens, DuPont!"
- "Is this in competition?" — No, trade is out of comp (exclusive)
- Martin sees 10-year Treasuries quoted at 100.24-100.25 (bid-offer)
- Broker screen offer good for ~$100 million only; for $750 million he must rely on "feel for the market"
- Must judge: how deep is the market, how much can he buy without moving prices, is market going up or down
- Martin has been unconsciously mapping trading patterns all morning — highs, lows, sizes, speed of movement
- Each scenario triggers minute body shift: muscle tightening, shiver of dread, shot of excitement
- Offers $750M at 100.25; shorts bonds he doesn't own
- Liquidity danger: thin market, big trade can disproportionately move prices
- Broadcasts over "squawk box" (global intercom) looking to buy at 100.24
- Bank of China sells $150M at 100.24; other sales add $175M
- Market weakens: 100.23-24, then 100.22-23, then 100.21-22
- Posts high bid at 100.215, buys $50M first, builds to $225M; other banks see large buyer, reverse course
- Prices climb; Martin scrambles to lift offers at 100.23, then 100.24, buys last at 100.26
- Average buy price just under 100.23; sold at 100.25 → $500,000 profit in 45 minutes
- Esmee gets $250,000 sales credit; sales manager thanks Martin; client happy buying below 100.26
- Martin strolls to coffee room "feeling invincible" — "That guy's got balls, selling $750 million tens right on the offer side"

### Treasury Desk Dynamics
- Treasury desk is privileged: bonds less risky than stocks, corporate bonds, or MBS
- During crises clients sell risky assets and buy Treasuries → volume balloons, bid-offer spreads widen
- Martin can make half a point ($5M) at a crack during crises
- Treasury desk profits buffer losses from riskier desks — helps cover credit risk
- Treasury market is most liquid of all bond markets: bid-offer spread 1 cent vs 10-25 cents for corporates
- "Treasury bonds being the thoroughbreds of trading instruments"
- Trading resembles not rational economic man but "a tennis player at net" — split-second timing

### The Enigma of Fast Reactions
- Lion at 50mph from 100 feet: 1 second. Spear at 65mph from 30 feet: 300 milliseconds
- "Evolution, like qualifying heats at the Olympics, took place against the sustained ticking of a stopwatch"
- Baseball (90mph, 60 feet): 450ms. Tennis serve (140mph): <400ms. Penalty kick (36 feet): 290ms. Hockey puck (half from blue line): <200ms
- Table tennis (70mph, 14-16 feet): 160ms — difference between winning and losing = few thousandths of a second
- Sprinters react to starting gun in ~120ms, approaching 100ms mark → races now use electronic speakers behind each runner (30ms delay otherwise)
- Cricket: short distance infielder ("silly point") at 14-17 feet, ball at 100mph off bat → 90ms to react. Raman Lamba killed by ball to temple
- Muhammad Ali: Norman Mailer at Rumble in the Jungle — "a kaleidoscope-dozen of punches at the air in two seconds." Ali's left jab measured at ~40ms
- Driver at 70mph: only 370ms to avoid car 75 feet ahead

### Visual System Is Surprisingly Slow
- Image hits retina → ~100ms (one-tenth of a second) before consciously registered
- Photons → chemical signal → electrical signal → visual cortex (back of brain) → "what" stream (identity) + "where" stream (location/motion) → unified image → consciousness
- "By the time we see something, the world has already moved on" — we're watching news footage, not live
- Eyes don't operate like movie cameras; they SAMPLE visual scenes
- Saccades: eyes fix, snapshot, jump, snapshot — like hummingbird flitting flower to flower
- Visual system stops sending images during jumps; brain weaves snapshots seamlessly
- Up to 5 saccades per second; minimum shift time = one-fifth of a second
- Cricket infielder: how catch ball in <100ms if not even aware of it? Can't direct attention (saccade takes 2x longer)
- Additional 300-400ms for cognitive decision + 50ms for motor command to reach muscles

### I, Camera? — Visual Pre-Processing
- All sensory info comes "filtered, warped and pre-interpreted" — like news on TV
- Brain compensates for 100ms visual delay by ANTICIPATING object location → fast-forwards what you see
- "Flash-lag effect" proves this: moving blue circle with blinking yellow dot inside; yellow appears to trail behind because it can't be anticipated
- Eye/brain tricks: focus on front edge of moving objects, process more info in lower visual field, group objects into 3-4 units (subitising), assume moving objects that change direction are alive
- Hearing processes FASTER than vision (25% faster): responding to auditory cue saves up to 50ms
- Sound receptors more sensitive than eye receptors
- Athletes (tennis, table tennis) rely on SOUND of ball on racket as much as sight for trajectory
- "Horizon of simultaneity": ~15 meters — at this distance sights and sounds perceived as simultaneous (closer = hear first, farther = see first)
- Old trading floor intercoms broadcasting futures prices ("One, two... three, four...") may have sharpened trader attention through multi-sensory engagement
- Multi-sensory input: perceptual acuity increases when more senses involved (info from 2+ senses = more likely real event → brain takes it more seriously)

### Knowing Before Knowing — Pre-Attentive Processing
- When fast reactions needed, brain cuts out consciousness entirely → reflexes, automatic behavior, "pre-attentive processing"
- Pre-attentive processing: perception, decision-making, movement initiation WITHOUT conscious involvement
- Remarque, *All Quiet on the Western Front*: soldiers learn to distinguish deadly "daisy cutter" shells from general din
- "At the first droning of the shells, we rush back, in one part of our being, a thousand years. By the animal instinct that is awakened in us we are led and protected. It is not conscious; it is far quicker, much more sure, less fallible, than consciousness."
- Total sensory bandwidth: retina = 10 million bits/sec + other senses = 1 million → total 11 million bits/sec
- Only ~40 bits/sec reach consciousness — "a trivial slice of all the information coming into the brain"
- Blindsight: WWI soldiers blinded by damage to visual cortex (eyes intact) could still duck objects tossed at them
- Superior colliculus: ancient nucleus in midbrain, formerly used by reptiles to track insects; tracks motion, captures attention, orients head toward moving objects; operates without conscious awareness
- Pre-conscious attention captures: moving objects (especially animate), primitive threats (snakes, spiders), human voices, faces expressing fear/anger — all registered in as little as 15ms
- Subliminal advertising exploited this; more usefully, pre-conscious processing triggers reflex motor commands
- Startle response: involuntary muscle contraction to withdraw from threat; triggered by expanding symmetrical shadow in visual field (incoming object heading straight at us); if expanding asymmetrically (>5 degrees off), brain calculates it will miss → no startle
- Head reacts in 70ms, torso in 100ms — roughly time for infielder to catch ball at silly point; may rely on startle response

### Learning and Automaticity
- Higher in nervous system (spine → brainstem → cortex) = more neurons, longer distances, slower response
- Brain passes control of learned movements DOWN to lower regions for speed → "unconscious competence"
- Tetris brain-scanning study: beginners' brains lit up broadly (cortex active); experts' cortex died down, used less glucose/oxygen, reacted much faster
- "Thinking, one could say, is something we do only when we are no good at an activity"
- Skilled anticipation: batter studies pitcher's likely pitches; cricket infielder registers batsman's stance, glance, grip BEFORE ball is bowled; boxer scans opponent's footwork and stabilizer muscles before knockout blow
- Pre-rehearsed motor programs brought online; subtle adjustments made based on flightpath
- Ken Dryden (legendary hockey goalie): "When a game gets close, my conscious mind goes blank. I feel nothing. I hear nothing... my body moves — like a goalie moves; I don't tell it to move or how to move or where... I trust it and the unconscious mind that moves it."

### What Lies Beneath — Consciousness as Bystander
- George Loewenstein (Yale economist): "There is little evidence beyond fallible introspection supporting the standard assumption of complete volitional control of behavior"
- "We are for the most part on autopilot"
- Benjamin Libet (1970s, UC): wired subjects with EEG; found brains prepared action 300ms BEFORE subjects made conscious decision to move finger
- Consciousness = "bystander observing a decision already taken, almost like watching ourselves on video"
- Role of consciousness: may not choose and initiate actions, but rather observe and VETO decisions already made — like stifling inappropriate emotional urges
- V.S. Ramachandran: "we do not in fact have free will; what we have is free won't"
- Kant: consciousness constructs unified experience from raw sensations using organizing constructs (space, time, cause/effect)
- Behind the veil of sensations lie "things in themselves" (Ding an sich) — inaccessible to rational analysis
- Freud (inspired by Kant): below rational selves lies "a devil's cauldron of envy and sexual perversion"
- Neuroscience reveals something more like "the inner workings of a BMW" — a meticulously engineered control mechanism

### Fast Times on the Trading Floor
- Financial decision-making is physical activity demanding physical traits — not just IQ
- Traders with high IQ but no appetite for risk = Cassandra (predict future but can't affect it)
- Good call + appetite for risk + slow reactions = still behind the market, won't survive
- Flow traders need: IQ, economics knowledge, risk appetite, driving ambition, AND physical build for extended visuo-motor scanning
- Most flow-trading desks employ former athletes (World Cup skier, college tennis star)
- Chicago Board of Trade pits = "wrestling ring" — height, strength, speed, willingness to elbow competitors matter; few women in financial mosh pits
- High-frequency trading: buy/sell billions, hold seconds to minutes; predict small moves not long-term direction; shorter holding period = greater need for speed

### Black Boxes vs. Human Traders
- Algorithmic trading ("black boxes"): backed by quants (mathematicians, engineers, physicists)
- Black box: analyze price data, find anomalies/patterns, select/execute trade in <10ms; some down to 2-3ms; next generation: microseconds
- London hedge fund trading Chicago: 40ms lag from speed-of-light signal travel + router delays → co-locate servers at exchange
- "Execution-only" boxes: don't find trades, just execute them better (pinging for hidden bids, slicing large blocks without moving prices)
- Boxes do what Martin did with DuPont trade, only better; managers ask why pay traders when boxes can do it
- "Thinking" boxes: use genetic algorithms to learn; parse news, download economic releases, trade before human reads first line
- Already majority of volume on major stock exchanges; spreading to currency and bond markets
- "I, like many others, find the markets increasingly inhuman"

### Human vs. Machine Scorecard (2007-08 Credit Crisis)
- High-frequency trading: draw (both made historic amounts)
- Medium-term (seconds to minutes): humans pulled slightly ahead as flow traders made record amounts
- Medium-to-long-term (minutes to hours/days): humans outperformed boxes in stat arb and quant equity — only humans understood political decisions of central bankers and Treasury officials
- "In what may have been the first major test of human versus machine trading, humans won, but only just"
- During crises, boxes fail to comprehend new data → managers unplug them → humans step into breach

### Division of Labor — The Future
- Machines can handle execution; risk appetite can be managed by floor managers
- What remains for human: good JUDGEMENT — the call on the market
- People with good judgment but risk-averse or physically slow could be fitted with "prosthetic risk-taker" (execution boxes) → lift Cassandra's curse
- Could level playing field: not just young men, but men and women, young and old, selected for judgment quality
- BUT bodies still crucial: gut feelings are "perhaps the most important data informing our call on the market"
- "Gut feelings emerge from a massive information-gathering exercise conducted by the body"
- "The body remains the most advanced black box ever created"

### Soros's Backache and Trader Hunches

### Online vs. Offline Thinking
- Martin pricing DuPont trade had 1-2 minutes — no time for decision trees

- Daniel Kahneman: fast and slow thinking; Arie Kruglanski: locomotion and assessment
- Colin Camerer, George Loewenstein, Drazen Prelec: automatic vs. controlled thought (neuro-economics founders)
- Automatic: involuntary, effortless, parallel, opaque to introspection
- Controlled: voluntary, effortful, serial, open to inspection

### Can We Trust Hunches? — Kahneman-Klein Collaboration
- Intuition is not occult gift — it's a SKILL (pattern recognition)
- Kahneman doubted intuition; Gary Klein (naturalistic decision-making) believed in it
- Klein studied firefighters, paramedics, fighter pilots — experts with unquestionably reliable intuitions
- Kahneman studied social scientists, political forecasters, stock pickers — predictions no better than chance
- Resolution: intuition reliable when TWO conditions met:
  1. Environment regular enough to produce repeating patterns
  2. Person encounters patterns frequently with quick feedback to learn
- Chess, firefighting, paramedicine = meet conditions → trustworthy intuition
- Political forecasting = too fluid/complex, feedback too delayed → unreliable intuition
- Kahneman: "Intuition cannot be trusted in the absence of stable regularities in the environment"

### Do Financial Markets Have Stable Regularities?
- Efficient Markets Hypothesis says no: market moves with news, news unpredictable, therefore market unpredictable → gut feelings = mythology
- Robert Shiller disagrees: investing is like any other occupation — training and hard work improve performance
- Coates + Lionel Page study: tested via Sharpe Ratios — how consistently traders made money
- S&P 500 long-term Sharpe Ratio ≈ 0.4; experienced traders in study had Sharpes >1.0 (hedge fund gold standard)
- Credit crisis 2007-09: star traders turned out to be like crazy cab drivers (high variance); lost more in 2 years than made in previous 5
- But consistently profitable traders had RISING Sharpe Ratios plotted against years of experience — learning curve
- Upward-sloping Sharpe curve = genuine skill, not luck → could be used by banks to identify skilled traders
- Financial markets DO meet Kahneman-Klein criteria for trustworthy intuition
- Traders compare current market to past experiences: "This credit crisis feels just like the Russian default in '98"

### Hunches and Gut Feelings — The Somatic Marker Hypothesis
- Dylan Evans: "the Hamlet Problem" — without visceral coloring of options, we'd be lost in sea of possibilities, unable to choose
- Damasio and Bechara: patients with damage to body-signal-integration areas had normal/exceptional cognitive abilities but made TERRIBLE decisions
- IQs counted for nothing without homeostatic/emotional feedback from body
- "Feeling was an integral component of the machinery of reason"
- **Somatic Marker Hypothesis**: each memory stored with bodily sensations felt at the time ("somatic markers")
- When facing new situation, scroll through options → each triggers subtle body shift: muscle tensing, quickened breath, shiver of dread, frisson of excitement → until one "just feels right"
- "Rationality by itself gains no purchase on the world, it merely spins its wheels, without the grit of somatic markers"
- Damasio: "Descartes' Error" — thinking decisions are matter of mind alone, pure reason
- Good judgment may owe as much to body's ability to produce and detect signals as to IQ
- Ivy League stars with brilliant analyses can't make money; across the aisle, trader from unknown university with no analytics consistently prints money — may have better body-brain connective circuitry

### Feedback — Body-Brain Loops
- Body-brain feedback: thought affects body, body's changes feed back to brain, changing the way it thinks
- Depression example: force smile, straighten posture, walk briskly → may actually feel happier
- Runaway feedback: fear → heart beats faster, sweat, hyperventilate → aware of symptoms → worry more → panic attack
- Also works positively: arguments escalate, sexual arousal builds — brain is "participant, not spectator"
- William James: "Everyone knows how panic is increased by flight, and how the giving way to the symptoms of grief or anger increases those passions themselves. Each fit of sobbing makes the sorrow more acute"
- Why route signals through body instead of keeping signaling within brain? Body's influence transforms the brain's very thoughts and feelings. Extra processing time in brain might actually slow eventual action.
- James on emotions: we commonly think feelings come first then behavior. Actually reversed — "We are sad because we are crying, scared because we are running away"
- We perceive bear → brain triggers automatic escape (running) → physiological change reported back to brain → shows up in consciousness as feeling of fear
- Joe LeDoux: feeling itself plays very small role — mere "icing on the cake," observer tagging along
- Glucose and self-control: brain = 2% of body mass but 20% of energy use
- Miami radiologists: verbal fluency task drew 23% more glucose than at rest
- Florida State psychologists: taxing mental/physical activities deplete glucose → reduces self-control
- "Last in, first out" rule: self-control (evolutionarily recent) is first to be rationed when fuel is low
- May explain why fights break out easily in sports (ice hockey), why we snap after long office hours, why diets fail when stressed

### James vs. Cannon — The Emotions Debate (pp. 101-104)
- Charles Sherrington (Nobel 1932): "emotions move us" — literally. Functional role of emotion is to promote fast behavioral reactions
- William James: emotions emerge when we perceive changes our body goes through during an emotional episode (tense stomach, pounding heart, raised body temperature)
- "Without the bodily states following on the perception, the latter would be purely cognitive in form, pale, colourless, destitute of emotional warmth"
- Emotional language draws on bodily metaphors: sinking stomach, broken heart, blood-boiling anger, chilling moment, heart-pounding experience
- James's account (physical reaction first, conscious feeling second) seems counter-intuitive but makes sense for fast reactions: we act first, feel later
- Walter Cannon (Harvard physiologist, 1920s) challenged James:
  - Bodily feedback too SLOW — adrenalin release takes 1-2 seconds, visceral organs left behind by high-speed emotional events
  - Bodily feedback not UNIQUE enough — heart pounds and breathing accelerates whether scared, angry, joyous, or in love; same suite of physical reactions for all strong emotions
  - Example: young man hearing he inherited a fortune — became pale, then exhilarated, then vomited. Same physical agitation for joy and disgust
- Cannon's arguments won for decades; James's theory retreated to "interesting but disproved"
- 1970s-80s revival: scientists re-examined body-brain feedback, reconsidered James

### Muscles and Our First Response (pp. 104-107)
- Cannon focused only on visceral nervous system (heart, lungs, gut, glands) — but that's just one communication line
- Visceral nerve fibres: slow, 5-30 meters/second (some only 1 m/s) — like a 56k modem
- Muscular nervous system: different fibre class, signals at ~120 meters/second — like broadband/instant messaging
- Speed of muscular system explains how body keeps up with fast emotional events
- Muscles play intimate role in emotional expressions: anger/sadness/elation change posture, muscles tense/relax
- William Condon (U. Pittsburgh, 1960s): discovered micro-expressions studying slow-motion film of psychotherapy patients
- Facial displays of anger, disgust, fear flicker and vanish within 40 milliseconds
- Paul Ekman (UC): trained police and security services to spot micro-expressions for lie detection
- Faces are uniquely significant: we learn others' intentions through faces, broadcast our own emotions
- Micro-expressions betray true feelings: salesman smiles but micro-expression reveals contempt
- Observable in as little as 30 milliseconds — faster than conscious awareness
- Could have entire conversation of micro-expression exchanges within a single second, all unconscious

### Facial Feedback Theory of Emotions (pp. 107-108)
- Speed of facial expressions inspired "facial feedback theory of emotions" — echoing James: we act first, feel later
- Do people with very expressive faces ("facial athletes") experience richer emotional lives? Possibly
- Botox users: by anaesthetising facial muscles, may be dampening emotional AND cognitive reactions
- Irony for Method actors: conjuring real emotion means using real facial expressions, but botox kills this
- Robert Levenson and Paul Ekman experiments: instructed participants to flex/relax specific facial muscles or hold pencil at back of teeth
- Without knowing it, participants composed expressions of happiness or sadness
- After exercise, tested for mood: simply moving facial muscles alone, without any emotive input, caused subjects to FEEL the mood portrayed on their faces
- James confirmed: muscles can communicate emotional feelings to the brain
- "In depression the flexors tend to prevail; in elation or belligerent excitement the extensors take the lead" — James

### Your Gut Is Telling You Something (pp. 108-110)
- Body transmits info to brain via wide palette of signals: electrical (muscles), electrical (visceral organs), hormonal (blood-borne)
- Contrary to Cannon: body HAS enough different signals to compose messages with "subtlety of a piano keyboard, and some with the speed of a radio transmission"
- Sequential order of body's response systems during challenging event:
  1. **Muscles** (milliseconds): facial muscles, body posture — first responders
  2. **Visceral nervous system** (milliseconds to seconds): lungs, liver, adrenal glands — support muscles
  3. **Fast-acting hormones** like adrenalin (seconds to minutes): flood blood, unpack energy stores
  4. **Steroid hormones** (hours to days): retool body for sustained change of life
- Each layer reports back to the brain, altering emotions, moods, memories, thoughts

### Gwen's Feedback Loop — The Trading Floor Narrative (pp. 110-113)
- Gwen: former college tennis star (last 16 at Australian Open), now trades 5-year Treasuries
- In a month-long slump; catches Ash (floor manager) glaring at her with pity/disgust

- Visceral nervous system activates next: tissues and organs support muscles (fuel, oxygen, cooling), adrenalin floods arteries
- Fight-or-flight prepares body: increased breathing, heart rate, sweating, dilated pupils, suppressed digestion
- Reports state of arousal to brain via spinal cord → slants perception of world
- Rest-and-digest system (other branch of visceral nervous system): brings gut feelings
- Vagus nerve: main nerve of rest-and-digest system; from Greek for "wanderer"
- Vagus emerges from brain stem → voicebox → heart → lungs → liver → pancreas → gut
- Can modulate tone of voice, slow breathing and heart rate, control early digestion
- Vagus originates from same brain stem region as facial muscle control → synchronizes facial expressions with heart rate and gut state
- 80% of vagus nerve fibres carry information FROM body TO brain (not the other way)
- Most returning info comes from gut → gut feelings literally come from the gut (at least partly)

### The Enteric Nervous System — The Second Brain (pp. 112-114)
- Gut controlled by enteric nervous system: acts independently of brain, contains ~100 million neurons (more than spinal cord)
- Produces same neurotransmitters as the brain
- Michael Gershon coined "the Second Brain"; vagus nerve = hotline between two brains
- Digestive tract technically OUTSIDE the body — Gershon: "a tunnel that permits the exterior to run right through us"
- Ulrich Trendelenburg (German physiologist, 1917): removed intestinal section from guinea pig, severed all brain connections
- When he blew air into it, after a moment's delay, the intestine contracted and blew air back — "like some gentle creature playing a simple game"
- This proved the gut has an independent nervous system
- Brain and gut connected via vagus: conditions in one show up as symptoms in the other
- Stress → brain tells gut to stop digesting (waste of energy); Alzheimer's patients → constipation; opiate addicts → constipation; antidepressants → diarrhoea
- Crohn's disease patients more easily aroused by emotional stimuli
- Gut hormones during feeding enhance memory formation (evolutionary: remember where you found food)
- Good meal can calm brain and suffuse with well-being

### Emotions vs. Moods (pp. 114-116)
- Emotions: short-lived, designed to be fleeting to provide timely info and not interfere with new data
- Moods: slower, longer-term attitude, slow-burning emotion that slants worldview
- Both alter attitude to events, tinker with memories, change thinking
- Purpose of feedback loops: synchronize body and brain for coherent behavior
- "During important moments in our lives we do not want our tissues multi-tasking"
- At moments of euphoria, flow, love, fear, fight — body and brain merge

### The Iowa Gambling Task — Somatic Markers in Action (pp. 116-118)
- Damasio and Bechara tested somatic markers with Iowa Gambling Task
- Four decks of cards: two display low amounts ($50-$100) but lead to profit over time; two display high amounts ($500-$1,000) but lead to losses
- Players don't know deck properties initially; must figure out through play
- Key finding: players began choosing profitable decks BEFORE they consciously knew why
- Skin conductance (sweat) monitored: spiked when players contemplated playing from losing decks
- This somatic prod steered them away from dangerous choices pre-consciously
- Normal players guided toward money-making decks long before conscious rationality figured out why

### Confabulation — We Don't Know Why We Act (pp. 120-121)

### The Market's Morse Code — Information Theory (pp. 129-132)
- Claude Shannon (Bell Labs, 1950s): information is proportional to novelty/uncertainty
- Counter-intuitive: uncertainty = information. Real information should tell us something NEW, therefore be unpredictable
- Text message example: "I am late. The car has a flat tire. I will be home in one hour" (63 chars) compressed to "Flat tire. Home 1hr." (20 chars) — eliminating redundancy leaves only unpredictable content
- Shannon's fundamental discovery: **information is synonymous with unpredictability, with novelty**
- "When receiving pure information we are in a state of maximum uncertainty about what comes next"
- Sensory apparatus designed to attend almost exclusively to information — ignores predictable events, orients rapidly to novel ones

### Cerebellum and the Expected vs. Unexpected (pp. 131-133)
- Cerebellum: when we plan an action, neo-cortex sends copy of plan to cerebellum, which DAMPENS or CANCELS the expected sensation
- This is why we can't tickle ourselves — we produced the motion, cerebellum dampens expected sensation
- If feedback differs from expectation → it carries information → something went wrong → teaches us to calibrate
- Frog's eye: blind unless something MOVES — "a pure example of a sensory system doing just what it was built to do — attending exclusively to information"
- Troxler fading: 19th-century German physiologist noted we gradually lose awareness of unchanging visual stimuli
- We are "built to ignore the world unless something of importance happens"
- Stimulus hunger: without novelty, we don't function well — "highway hypnosis" or "moth effect" where drivers are drawn to rare lights on featureless roads

### Noradrenalin and the Locus Ceruleus (pp. 133-135)
- When alarm centre (locus ceruleus) is tripped, neurons boost firing rate and spray **noradrenalin** throughout brain
- Noradrenalin = neuromodulator (not involved in specific brain activity like French or math — instead alters sensitivity of ALL neurons, making them fire more easily/rapidly)
- Effect comparable to "turning up the lights in a room and the volume on a microphone"
- Noradrenalin brings Martin to high alert: enhanced arousal, lowered sensory thresholds, senses on edge
- In relaxed state: scans environment randomly like a 360-degree radar (low signal-to-noise ratio)
- When surprised: senses drawn to focal point, filter out background, concentrate on relevant info → improved signal-to-noise ratio
- This = Cocktail Party Effect: picking out a voice across a crowded room
- Erich Maria Remarque (soldiers in WWI): "there is suddenly in our veins, in our hands, in our eyes, a tense waiting, a heightening alertness, a strange sharpening of the senses"
- Locus ceruleus also projects DOWN into fight-or-flight nervous system → sprays noradrenalin onto heart, arteries, bronchial tubes, adrenal glands
- Body placed in state of preparedness so once brain decides on action, body is ready to execute

### Daniel Berlyne's Arousal Curve (pp. 135-137)
- Daniel Berlyne (U. Toronto, 1960s): plotted arousal against information — found an elegant **n-shaped** (inverted-U) curve
- Low information → bored and sleepy
- High information → confused, anxious
- Just the right amount → curiosity piqued, thirst for novelty quenched, diffuse satisfaction throughout body and brain
- Brain must distinguish significant from trivial information — locus ceruleus alone can't do this
- Higher-brain regions (hippocampus, posterior neo-cortex) scroll rapidly through stored patterns below conscious awareness
- Amygdala tags matches with emotional urgency — gives "quick and dirty assessment" of threat or opportunity
- This tag team (pattern recognition + emotional assessment + alarm centre) = our gut feelings about what's important
- "Good art critics rely on gut feelings just as much as profitable traders"
- Art that merely shocks (Shock of the New, Robert Hughes) = locus ceruleus tricked; art that engages amygdala and deeper brain = genuine masterwork
- Financial markets = most information-dense environment; VIX ("Fear Index") tracks uncertainty
- During credit crisis 2007-08: VIX spiked from sleepy 12% to over 80%
- "Good traders like Martin do not just process information, they feel it"
- Market broadcasts info "on the tom tom of volatility" and trader's body "like a tuning fork, vibrates in sympathy"

### The Pleasure of Information — Dopamine (pp. 143-148)
- We are addicted to information, driven by the neuromodulator **dopamine**
- Produced by cells at top of brain stem; targets brain regions controlling reward and movement
- When we receive valuable info or perform survival-promoting act (eating, sex, making money) → dopamine released along pleasure pathways → rewarding, euphoric experience
- Brain seems to value dopamine MORE than the food or sex itself — given choice between eating and self-stimulating dopamine, animals self-stimulate until they starve
- Noradrenalin modulates arousal (how awake/attentive); dopamine modulates motivation (how eagerly we want things)
- Drugs of abuse exploit dopamine: alcohol, cocaine, amphetamine all increase dopamine in **nucleus accumbens** (in basal ganglia, midway between brain stem and cortex)
- Dopamine increase by substance: food = 50%, sex = 100%, nicotine = 200%, cocaine = 400%, amphetamine = 1,000%
- **Key discovery**: dopamine is NOT the molecule of pleasure but of ANTICIPATION/WANTING
- Animal given juice → dopamine spike. After repetition, dopamine spike drifts FORWARD in time to coincide with CUE predicting juice, not the juice itself
- Two types of reward: pleasure of consumption vs. pleasure of anticipation — dopamine is the latter
- Kent Berridge and Terry Robinson: dopamine stimulates the *wanting* of juice rather than the *liking* of it
- Desire is "closer to a craving... powerfully motivating and in some sense enjoyable, although at times it can be more like a maddening itch"

### Dopamine and Unexpected Rewards (pp. 146-147)
- Give monkey expected one squirt → dopamine settles. Give unexpected TWO squirts → dopamine perks up again
- Amount of dopamine released depends NOT on absolute reward but on how UNEXPECTED it is
- "We enjoy and crave environments in which we receive unexpected rewards; in other words, we enjoy risk"
- Dopamine spikes with information; acts as learning signal — Jon Horvitz (Columbia), Peter Redgrave (Sheffield)
- Controversial extension: any experience predicting future pleasure/pain, even unpleasant ones, can deliver dopamine

### Dopamine and Drug Addiction (pp. 147-148)
- Drug-taking brain chemistry evolves: first delivers pleasurable hit + potent dopamine, but with repeated use dopamine drifts forward to attach to CUES (music, nightclub, people) rather than consumption itself
- Addicts come to lose the pleasure of consumption, may find the act itself distasteful, but cannot resist the craving
- To kick addiction: must separate from cues — change neighborhoods, avoid old friends
- Anti-drug ads featuring syringes and dark alleys BACKFIRED — images were cues predicting drugs, delivered large dopamine hit to reformed addicts, driving them back
- Curiosity itself may be a form of dopamine-driven addiction — racing to finish mystery novels, scientists working day and night to discover insulin or DNA structure

### Dopamine, Exploration, and Animal Spirits (pp. 148-150)
- Gambling = dopamine-driven addiction (unexpected slot machine payoff → massive dopamine)
- Trading provides some of highest rewards available, highly uncertain, attained through huge risks → powerful dopamine
- Traders on a roll may be in grip of addiction; like addicts who habituate to drug dose, traders habituate to certain levels of risk and profit → irresistibly compelled to increase position size
- Greg Berns (Emory): "In the real world, action and reward go together. Goodies don't just fall in your lap; you have to go out and find them"
- German research group depleted dopamine in rats: animals would eat food placed in mouths but would NOT walk even a short distance to obtain it

- *Jurassic Park* analogy: T. rex "doesn't want to be fed. He wants to hunt"
- Keynes's "animal spirits": "a spontaneous urge to action rather than inaction... a large proportion of our positive activities depend on spontaneous optimism rather than on a mathematical expectation"
- If spontaneous optimism falters, "enterprise will fade and die"
- Business enterprise driven by pure love of risk-taking, not just calculation of odds
- Fred Previc (Texas A&M): rapid growth of dopamine-producing cells (from dietary shift to meat-eating) changed history — encouraged risk-taking just for the hell of it, fuelled "a robust lust for life"

### Rat Park — Enriched Environments and Addiction (pp. 150-152)
- Bruce Alexander (Vancouver, 1970s): "Rat Park" experiment
- Bare cage: rats given two bottles (water and morphine-laced water) → preferred morphine, became addicted
- Rat Park (enriched environment: running wheel, foliage, other rats): rats did NOT prefer morphine, did NOT develop addiction
- In enriched environment, rats got daily dopamine from normal forms of search, work, and play
- Animals addicted to cocaine, once returned to enriched environment, actually kicked their habit
- Implication for obesity: have we denied people "Human Park" — access to sports, art, science, green spaces? Placed them in empty cages? Obesity epidemic may be dimming gut feelings and entrepreneurial drive

### Testosterone's Mechanism of Action (pp. 161-162)
- Testosterone and dopamine work synergistically: testosterone increases dopamine in nucleus accumbens

### Testosterone — What It Is and How Men Are Made (pp. 161-164)

### The Winner Effect — Full Scientific Account (pp. 174-180)
- Biologists observed: winning a fight → more likely to win NEXT fight, even controlling for size ("resource holding potential") and motivation
- Explanations: (1) victory teaches animal about its abilities, (2) pheromones from winner deter rivals, (3) **testosterone** — most persuasive account
- Pre-competition: males experience pronounced testosterone RISE → anabolic effects on muscle mass, hemoglobin; also quickens reactions, sharpens visuo-motor scanning, enhances camouflage-breaking ability
- Testosterone increases persistence and fearlessness — "no point equipping an animal with augmented fighting capacity if it were not willing to use it"
- Post-competition: winner emerges with EVEN HIGHER testosterone, loser with LOWER
- Rhesus monkeys: winner experienced 10x increase, loser dropped to 1/10th of baseline → these new levels persisted for SEVERAL WEEKS
- Dominant males intervene to protect weaker males specifically to deprive likely winner of winner-effect benefits
- In defeat: animals' colours dim, testicles and brains shrink, they lapse into depression
- Winner enters next round with elevated testosterone → androgenic priming → positive feedback loop: victory → raised testosterone → further victory

### Winner Effect in Humans (pp. 178-180)
- Many social scientists deny "hot hands" in sports — claim it's statistical illusion

- Controlled for rank, form, motivation; looked at matches one seed apart, going to tie break in first set, decided by narrowest margin (2 points)
- Result: winner of first set had 60% chance of winning second set and the match → in sport, winning contributes to further wins
- Pre-competition testosterone surge documented in tennis, wrestling, ice hockey, chess, even medical exams
- Winning athletes experience post-game testosterone spike → positive-feedback loop is physiological substrate of winning and losing streaks
- Home advantage: testosterone-driven victories more common on home turf
- Team effects: Wayne Gretzky or Michael Jordan can inspire teammates — rising/falling testosterone can be COMMUNICATED to teammates
- Fan effects: 1994 World Cup final (Brazil vs. Italy) — Brazilian fans' testosterone rose, Italian fans' crashed
- Testosterone cycles can be experienced vicariously by observers → prospect of large groups spiraling upward in confidence

### Fight-or-Flight on the Trading Floor (pp. 199-200)
- Stress response = rapid switch from everyday to emergency mode; evolved for imminent physical threats (mountain lion)
- Body marshals all glucose and oxygen, shuts down expensive functions (digestion, reproduction, growth, immune)
- Useful for physical danger but "largely counterproductive when seated in the workplace"
- Stress response unfolds in stages: two fast (electrical), two slow (hormonal)
  1. Amygdala registers danger → electrical signals to rest of brain (milliseconds)
  2. Electrical signals from amygdala via brain stem to visceral organs → heart rate, blood pressure, breathing increase (less than a second)
  3. Adrenalin released → works over seconds and minutes (fuel)
  4. If crisis persists → adrenal cortex secretes cortisol (hours, even days)
- Martin and Scott experience same fight-or-flight (like wolf and elk — one in control, one not) but diverge at cortisol stage
- Martin benefits from moderate cortisol; Scott suffers from ever-higher levels that impair judgment

### LeDoux's High Road and Low Road (pp. 200)

- Scott's amygdala processes the shocking analyst reports → passes alarm to locus ceruleus and brain stem → accelerates fight-or-flight already running at low levels

### Scott's Physical Stress Response — The Details (pp. 201-202)

### The Stress Response to a Bear Market — Cortisol (pp. 205-207)
- Amygdala switches on the "big engines" → cortisol floods Scott's body
- Cortisol had already been released in small amounts (gave traders a buzz); now released "pulse after pulse" in quantities that alter the character of the stress response → hunker down for long siege
- **HPA axis**: amygdala → hypothalamus → pituitary (releases chemical messenger into blood) → adrenal cortex → cortisol
- Cortisol's message to the body: fight-or-flight is taking longer than expected; shut down long-term functions; marshal all resources for immediate use
- Cortisol slows digestion (inhibits digestive enzymes, shunts blood from stomach walls), inhibits growth hormone (stunts growth in stressed young adults)
- Crucially: cortisol REVERSES the body's anabolic processes — it is a CATABOLIC steroid
  - Blocks testosterone and insulin
  - Breaks glycogen into glucose
  - Breaks fat cells into free fatty acids
  - Breaks muscles into amino acids → shunted to liver → converted to glucose
- Suppresses reproductive system: inhibits testosterone synthesis (men), oestrogen and ovulation (women)
- Prepares for injury: acts as powerful anti-inflammatory; aided by endorphins (natural opiates — "runner's high") which dull pain; soldiers fight on unaware of wounds
- Martin advises Scott: "This thing's a freight train; don't get in the way"
- Scott sells out remains of his position over the afternoon; cortisol levels continue to rise

- Gwen: thriving on the flows, sustaining mild fight-or-flight with moderate/invigorating cortisol and adrenalin, "just as she used to in a mid-tennis match"
- Martin: toughened physiology from years of experience, hasn't needed much cortisol or even much fight-or-flight → his vagus nerve merely released its brake, letting the "naturally powerful idle of his body carry him through the afternoon without even breaking a sweat"

### The Vagal Brake — Physiological Elegance (pp. 208-209)
- Heart's default setting is FASTER than resting rate — vagus applies a brake, slowing heart and breathing
- For minor stressors: vagus merely eases its brake → heart accelerates on its own — far more efficient and gentle than launching full fight-or-flight
- "Minute vagal adjustments to our heart throughout the day" — reserve fight-or-flight for real emergencies
- Physiological elite: people with excellent vagal tone barely need cortisol or adrenalin even for vigorous challenges; they just release the vagal brake → "a marvellous trick"
- Martin belongs to this physiological elite

### What Triggers the Stress Response — Novelty, Uncertainty, Uncontrollability (pp. 209-211)
- Hans Selye (McGill, 1950s): believed stress response triggered by actual bodily harm (hunger, thirst, injury, hypothermia)
- John Mason (Yale): discovered hypothalamus and adrenals react more powerfully to the EXPECTATION of harm than to harm itself
- Three key triggers: **novelty**, **uncertainty**, **uncontrollability**
- **Novelty**: rats in new cage → elevated corticosterone even with no threat; stress response is largely PREPARATORY
- **Uncertainty**: John Hennessey and Seymour Levine (1970s) — shocks delivered at predictable intervals/with audible tone → normal cortisol; unpredictable timing → cortisol rises with unpredictability, maxing at complete randomness
  - "We are more scared when uncertain about where the monster lurks than when it finally pops out and snarls at us"
  - London Blitz: central London (daily, predictable bombing) had FEWER gastric ulcers than outer suburbs (intermittent, unpredictable raids)
- **Uncontrollability**: "yoked" experiments — two animals receive identical shocks but only one has a bar lever to stop them; the one without control shows far more stress
  - Even the ILLUSION of control (bar unplugged) reduces stress
  - "Loss of control in a threatening situation provokes the most terrifying stress response"

### Coates's Cortisol Experiment on Traders (pp. 211-212)
- Same London trading floor study that measured testosterone
- Cortisol rose substantially with market VOLATILITY (not just losses)
- So sensitive were traders' cortisol levels to volatility that they displayed "a remarkably tight relationship with the prices of derivatives" — raises possibility that stress hormones form the physiological foundation of the derivatives market
- P&L variability (proxy for uncontrollability) also predicted cortisol levels
- Hormone fluctuations were extraordinarily large: normally cortisol drops ~50% from morning to afternoon, but on volatile days some traders' cortisol INCREASED 500% — "levels normally seen only in clinical patients"

### Scott's Descent — Novelty, Uncertainty, and Loss of Control (pp. 212-213)
- Scott trapped in a situation that is novel (never seen anything like this crisis — have to go back to 1929 for comparison), uncertain (VIX from 11% to 25%, eventually 80%), and uncontrollable (lost record amounts of money, by definition has lost control)
- "The cumulative effect of the novel, uncertain and uncontrollable conditions of the market is a massive upwelling of cortisol in Scott and other traders along Wall Street"
- By 4 pm Ash orders Scott to close positions; Scott can't concentrate — locus ceruleus firing pattern has shifted from focused bursts to sustained firing → scanning becomes hurried, indiscriminate, panicky
- Scott sits out the rest of the day, "powerless to trade profitably"

### Scott's Weekend and the Unraveling (pp. 213-215)
- Weekend in Hamptons with girlfriend: can't sleep, can't eat; dreams of beach house vanish
- Spends weekend on phone with colleagues collecting horror stories (reassuring himself that others lost too)
- By Sunday spirits rally: arb desk lost $125M last week but still up $180M on the year; bank had good year; maybe $1.5M bonus piggybacking off desk pool
- Starts wearing best suits, dining with salespeople he used to disdain — "if you are not making money, you'd better at least have the sales force on your side"
- Coming weeks: markets plunge into financial crisis of historic proportions; markets "inflict maximum pain, searching out and dashing every hope"
- Fed lowers rates repeatedly but fails to ignite a rally in risky assets
- Arb desk hemorrhages money: not only giving back the year's profits but losing an ADDITIONAL $375M
- Logan on the mortgage desk sucked into "the vortex now being called the Credit Crisis" — despite best efforts, client flows keep him long mortgages; he's lost more this year than he made in the past five
- Layoff rumors spread; uncertainty and uncontrollability reach "new, debilitating levels"
- Robert Sapolsky (primatologist): dominant monkeys under uncontrollable stress take to biting subordinates → lowers THEIR cortisol while raising subordinates'
- Bank managers do the same: "appearing to understand this ugly piece of physiology, offload their cortisol onto juniors"
- December: wintry atmosphere; 15% layoffs just before Christmas; few bonuses; Martin and Gwen get small ones; Scott gets nothing; Stefan's $25M bonus last year helped blow up the bank
- Black boxes being unplugged (algorithms failing); hedge funds seeing capital flight; firms closing doors

### Chronic Stress and Risk-Aversion (pp. 215-220)
- Critical distinction: **acute** cortisol exposure (short-term) vs. **chronic** (long-term) → very different, often OPPOSITE effects
- Cortisol follows same n-shaped dose-response curve: moderate levels = beneficial; high levels = toxic
- Moderate cortisol: enhanced vigilance, signal detection, metabolic preparedness, improved mood "almost to the point of euphoria" → welcome edge
- Chronic cortisol: devastates cardiovascular and immune systems, impairs ability to assess risk
- Stress response evolved for rapid, short-lived physical threats → designed to switch on quickly and switch off quickly
- When it fails to switch off: "widespread medical problems ensue, largely because the stress response is metabolically expensive. The state of heightened readiness it promotes can be maintained over the long term only at the cost of breaking down many tissues in the body, much like burning furniture to keep a house warm"
- Brain regions can't distinguish brief physical threats from prolonged psychological/work-related ones → stress can endure for months, even years

### Cortisol's Effects on Memory and the Brain (pp. 217-220)
- Cortisol acts on dense receptor fields in amygdala and hippocampus (the two memory centers)
- Amygdala: stores emotional significance of events
- Hippocampus: stores factual details (autobiographical memories)
- Bicycle example: motor control → cerebellum; conceptual learning → neo-cortex; facts about the ride → hippocampus; fear of near-accident → amygdala
- Damaged hippocampus: no factual recall of intersection but amygdala fills you with dread → "I'm scared, I don't like it here"
- Damaged amygdala: remembers every fact but feels nothing about it → "Just the facts, ma'am"
- Stress hormones instruct BOTH amygdala and hippocampus to remember stressful events → **"flashbulb memories"** (mugging, 9/11, car accident) — captured for life with vivid detail
- Adrenalin (via vagus nerve) assists cortisol in laying down these memories; beta-blockers administered after trauma may help prevent flashbulb memory formation → lower risk of panic attacks and PTSD
- Under chronic cortisol: retrieval becomes biased toward memories stored under stress → Scott now dwells on nasty events (failed calculus, locker-room fight, dot.com losses) rather than pleasant ones (girlfriend, vacations, winning trades)
- Selective negative recall → irrational risk-aversion
- Chronic cortisol physically changes brain regions:
  - Hippocampus neurons can be KILLED → volume reduced up to 15% (seen in Cushing's syndrome patients)
  - But hippocampus is one of few brain regions that can REGROW neurons → when stress ends, it regenerates; Bruce McEwen: hippocampus "effectively hibernates through hard times"
  - Amygdala neurons experience opposite: cortisol causes rich arborisation (growth of branches) → thinking becomes MORE emotional, LESS factual → impaired rational analysis
  - Extreme stress can take pre-frontal cortex effectively OFFLINE → brain runs on stored, impulsive reactions
- Shell-shocked traders with overactive amygdala: "become prey to rumour and imaginary patterns"
- Study: healthy participants shown random patterns → found nothing; stressed participants → found patterns in the noise
- Paul Fussell (*The Great War and Modern Memory*): WWI troops under unimaginable stress and information deprivation fell prey to medieval-style rumors — spies, angels in the sky, factories rendering corpses, feral deserter tribes
- Financial crisis parallel: every bank going under, hedge funds conspiring, Chinese dumping Treasuries, UK defaulting, broker suicides → "each rumoured catastrophe is now given as much credence, and has as much effect on markets, as hard economic data"

### CRH and Anticipatory Angst (pp. 220)
- Cortisol's brain effects compounded by **CRH** (corticotropin-releasing hormone), produced in the amygdala
- CRH instils anxiety and "anticipatory angst" — general fear of the world, timid behavior
- Together cortisol and CRH suppress testosterone → the confidence/exploratory/risk-taking hormone is crushed
- Scott develops selective attention to depressing facts; finds danger everywhere; "even his beloved New York City, once sparkling with opportunity and excitement, has lately taken on a menacing silhouette"
- Under chronic stress, Scott and most colleagues become "irrationally risk-averse"

### The End Game — "Gun Shy" Traders (pp. 220-221)
- Mid-December: month and a half of endless volatility and losses; Christmas normally playful but now crushed by layoffs (15% of sales and trading staff)
- Martin and Gwen: small bonuses, deep resentment (their record profits kept bank afloat while Stefan's $25M bonus helped blow it up)
- Scott: no bonus, doesn't know how long he'll be kept on
- Traders "desperately need to make money, but find themselves oddly unable to initiate a trade, even one that looks attractive, being held back from the phones as if by a force field"
- They have become **"gun shy"**
- Economic theory assumes agents respond rationally to price signals (lower rates → buy risky assets) → but central banks have met "very limited success in arresting the downward momentum"
- Possible explanation: chronically high cortisol has made traders irrationally risk-averse and even PRICE INSENSITIVE
- "Compared to the Gothic fears now vexing Scott, a price change... seems trivial"

### Stress-Related Disease in the Financial Industry (pp. 222-226)
- Prolonged stress endangers personal health of people in finance and all affected industries
- **Chronic effects cascade**: blood shunted from digestive tract → gastric ulcers; immune system suppressed → constant colds, flu, herpes; growth hormone suppressed; reproductive tract and testosterone production suppressed
- Cortisol + tensed muscles prevent blood flow to corpora cavernosa → bull-market-insatiable bankers now can't maintain erection; testosterone (the chemical inducement for erotic thoughts) is crushed
- Cortisol's interaction with dopamine → increased susceptibility to drug addiction
- Elevated cortisol reduces sleep, especially **REM sleep** → deprives people of mental/physical recovery
- "Steroids may orchestrate a symphony of physiological effects, but as time passes the music turns into a cacophony"
- **Hypertension**: chronically raised heart rate and blood pressure → small tears in arterial walls → macrophages/white blood cells form plaques → atherosclerosis → myocardial ischemia/angina → thrombus → heart attack or stroke
- Insulin inhibited for months → high glucose + LDL cholesterol course through arteries; muscles broken down for nutrients; unused glucose deposited as abdominal fat (greatest heart disease risk)
- Cushing's syndrome: extreme example — atrophied limbs, fat on torso/neck/face, "apple on toothpicks"
- "A year into the financial crisis, the testosterone-ripped iron men of the bull market start to look decidedly puffy"
- **Whitehall Studies** (UK): civil servants in departments undergoing privatisation — highest job insecurity = higher cholesterol, weight gain, stroke incidence
- **Swedish study**: 40,000 people over 16 years — health strongly correlated with business cycle; mortality from cardiovascular disease, cancer, suicide all increase during recessions
- **Credit Crisis health data**: UK private health insurers saw surge in peptic ulcer, stress, depression claims after autumn 2007; British United Provident Association reported 47% rise in financial workers seeking stress/depression treatment (July 2008)
- **London heart attack study**: 2007-09 spike in heart attacks in London against decreasing incidence in rest of UK; estimated ~2,000 additional deaths from credit crisis impact on financial district
- Steroid feedback loop: market losses → risk-aversion → further sell-off → bear market becomes crash; body-brain interactions systematically shift risk preferences across business cycle
- "Cortisol is the molecule of irrational pessimism" — just as economists refer to irrational pessimism/dimming of animal spirits (Greenspan, Keynes)

## PART IV: RESILIENCE

## CHAPTER 8: Toughness (pp. 227-241+)

### Can We Control Our Stress Response? (pp. 227-229)
- Evolution equipped us with stress response that can be "fatally dysfunctional in modern society"
- Fire brigade analogy: stress response may save our house from emergency "only to destroy it with water damage"
- Chronic stress responsible for many of modern medicine's most deadly/intractable problems: hypertension, heart disease, type 2 diabetes, immune disorders, depression
- Can we turn off cortisol? Only with great difficulty
- **Joe LeDoux**: many axons run UP from brain stem/limbic system to neocortex (influencing rational thought), but far fewer run DOWN — we have less conscious control over primitive brain regions
- "Anyone who has experienced ungrounded panic attacks, or been in love with the wrong person, knows that efforts to consciously change our feelings are doomed to an endless cycle of repetition and failure"

### Open Field Experiment — Conscious vs Unconscious Stress (pp. 229-230)
- Rodents in open field (dangerous, exposed to predators) → classic stress response: freezing, defecation, elevated corticosterone
- After several days of repetition: rodent habituated behaviorally (stopped freezing/defecating) BUT corticosterone remained stubbornly elevated
- The rodent's conscious brain stopped registering stress, but its adrenal glands knew it was still in danger
- Same finding with traders: questionnaire answers about stress had little correlation with P&L or volatility, but cortisol levels faithfully tracked these stressors
- "People often invent stories to accompany their behaviour... we may even persuade ourselves we are not stressed"
- Hypothalamus and adrenals respond to objective cues more than encouraging self-talk; "their pathologies may not be amenable to a talking cure"

### Two Paths to Mitigating Chronic Stress (pp. 230-231)
- Despite bleakness, physiology offers more promise than discouragement
- Stress is largely physiological preparation for physical action → raises possibility of **training our physiology** (like sports science does)
- Two approaches: (1) physiological toughening, (2) objective changes in workplace

### The Toughened Individual (pp. 231-234)
- Mental toughness less well understood than physical toughness; work increasingly mental → more anxiety, fatigue, depression
- **Challenge vs threat**: toughened individual sees novelty as challenge (opportunity for gain); untoughened dreads it (potential harm) — each attitude corresponds to distinctive physiological state
- What neuro-chemical profile characterises coping vs being scared? Why can some maintain low anxiety under uncontrollable stressors?
- **Neal Miller** (Rockefeller): father of behavioral medicine; his lab (students Bruce McEwen and Jay Weiss) discovered cortisol receptors in brain and hormone feedback loops
- Miller & Weiss: chronic/unrelenting stress → physical illness + learned helplessness + depleted noradrenalin; BUT acute/short-lived stress repeated over and over → hardier physiology + increased immunity to further stressors
- Key finding: mental toughening process bears similarities to physical toughening

### Physical-Mental Toughening Parallel (pp. 234-235)
- Athletic training: stress → mild tissue damage → rest/recovery → expanded productive capacity; coaches time this for peak competition performance (optimal glucose, haemoglobin, adrenalin, cortisol, testosterone)
- Mental toughening: similar process of challenge + psychological loading + recovery → resilience + optimal hormones/neuromodulators/nervous-system activation

### Physiological Cocktail of Toughness (pp. 235-238)
- **Catabolic hormones** (cortisol): must be kept on short leash; cortisol strip-mines body for nutrients; after a few days/weeks without turning off, body disintegrates; "a continual flow kills"
- **Anabolic hormones** (testosterone, growth hormone, insulin, IGF): step in during downtime to rebuild; convert amino acids → muscle/bone; remove excess glucose; rejuvenate cells
- **Growth index**: ratio of anabolic to catabolic hormones — high = robust rebuilding capacity ("thriving," per McEwen/Epel/Ickovics); low = "failure to thrive" (high cortisol drains muscle/vitality)
- **McEwen's allostatic load index**: blood pressure, BMI, hip-to-waist ratio, cholesterol, blood glucose, noradrenalin, cortisol from urine — better predictor of future health than any single component
- **Amines** (dopamine, noradrenalin, adrenalin, serotonin): switch on quickly, focus attention, release glucose, promote fight-or-flight; half-life only minutes so they switch off fast
- **Richard Dienstbier**: toughened individual enjoys powerful + immediate amine reaction → doesn't need to draw on slower/more damaging cortisol response
- Toughened: amines lower at rest, rise more strongly when stressed, shut off quickly → homeostasis maintained → handle stress without emotional distress
- Brain silently compares demands vs resources (training + skill) → sufficient = challenge (relish it); insufficient = threat (shrink from it)
- Amine-producing cells can be TRAINED to increase productive capacity (like muscles) → less likely to deplete during stress → more likely to view events as challenges → less cortisol needed
- Depleted amines → psychiatric disorders: depleted dopamine → anhedonia (can't experience pleasure); depleted noradrenalin → lacking arousal/enthusiasm → learned helplessness
- Depressed people: depleted noradrenalin + dopamine, chronically high cortisol

### Toughened Profile Summary (pp. 238-239)
- High ratio anabolic:catabolic hormones
- Rapid, large amine increases when challenged, followed by moderate cortisol
- Counter-intuitive: toughened person has STRONGER initial stress response but masters the situation, permitting cortisol to abate; untoughened mounts weak arousal but cortisol lingers → catabolic damage
- Toughened people endure sustained challenge without depleting amines → no learned helplessness
- **Elite traders study**: most experienced/profitable traders had extremely high AND volatile steroid hormone levels (both testosterone and cortisol) — initially puzzling, but behind their poker faces "there roared an endocrine system on fire"; like Olympic athletes, they could call on hormones when needed and return to baseline quickly
- Amateur traders: opposite profile — chronically raised cortisol, stressors viewed as threats

### The Vagus Nerve (pp. 239-241)
- Fight-or-flight prepares for vigorous action; rest-and-digest takes over afterward
- Shakespeare: "great nature's second course" — together with anabolic hormones, rest-and-digest "knits up the body's ravell'd sleeve of care"
- **Stephen Porges**: vagus nerve as evolved tool for conserving energy; traced its evolutionary history through three stages
- **Stage 1 (reptilian)**: vagus orchestrates freeze response — motionless, conserve energy, avoid detection; passed to mammals as feigning death; also activated in diving mammals (seals — slow heart rate to conserve oxygen)
- Vagal freezing in humans: triggered by dipping face in cold water (dive reflex — slows heart rate, breathing, releases painkillers); may explain sudden death from shocking news; voodoo death cases
- Trapped rodents in water: realizing futility of escape → freeze, cardiac arrest, dive into depths to drown — triggering dive-induced freezing for rapid/painless death
- **Stage 2**: vagus cooperates with fight-or-flight — removes its "vagal brake" from visceral organs so sympathetic nervous system can take over (what Martin and Gwen experienced with Fed announcement)
- **Stage 3 (most advanced, human)**: vagus as tool of social engagement — diplomacy instead of confrontation; calm voice, eye contact, cooperative facial expressions = metabolically efficient alternative to fight-or-flight; calms visceral arousal; "the diplomatic core of the body"
- All three vagal reactions present in humans: challenge → first try social engagement → if fails, fight-or-flight → if fails, freeze (ancient reptilian response); "in the course of this confrontation we have regressed millions of years in evolutionary time"

### Vagal Tone and Heart-Rate Variability (pp. 241)
- Advanced vagus nerve permits subtle stress response adjustment, conserving energy
- Challenge response: efficient cardiac output + low blood pressure in peripheral arteries; threat response: inefficient cardiac output + high blood pressure
- Poor vagal tone → overreact to mild stressors with full fight-or-flight; "uproar is their only music"; drains energy/health; children with low vagal tone → more behavioral problems later
- Truly toughened (world-class athletes): may perform near max just by releasing vagal brake — cruising on body's idle speed
- **Heart-rate variability (HRV)**: vagal tone measured through HRV; breathe in → heart speeds up, breathe out → slows down; high variability = good vagal tone = mini-rest every breath
- High HRV correlated with health markers; low/no HRV = risk factor for hypertension and heart disease; can be monitored commercially (chest/finger devices)
- Toughened profile includes good vagal tone + high HRV; soldiers study: high HRV co-existed with high anabolic:catabolic ratio

### Challenge vs Threat — Offense vs Defence (pp. 241)
- Toughened individual views novelty as challenge → draws on different physiological systems → "offence vs defence"
- Challenge = energizing, enjoyable, leads to flow; threat = draining, unpleasant, leads to fear of world
- Implications for social confrontations: villagers fighting developer vs takeover sharks who "revel in such battles"; defenders are soaked in stress hormones, left with nasty memories; "the best defence is a good offence" has physiological support

### The Science of Grace Under Pressure (pp. 242-244)
- Much toughness is genetic — certain genes make people more immune to stress hormones
- **Developmental influences**: acute, short-lived, moderate stressors early in life can toughen an animal for adulthood
- Young rats handled by humans → develop larger adrenal glands but more muted stress response as adults; tend to live ~18% longer than non-stressed rats
- BUT major stressors early in life (maternal separation) → anxious adult ill-prepared for normal life events
- Adult toughening also possible: handling, exercise wheel, mild shock, even drug-depleted amines — any brief/repeated stressor works because stress response is general/bodywide
- **Exercise**: most important toughening stressor for humans; expands amine-producing cell capacity; inoculates against anxiety, stress, depression, learned helplessness; floods brain with growth factors ("brain fertiliser") keeping neurons young
- **Cold water exposure**: rats swimming in cold water develop capacity for quick/powerful arousal via adrenalin (not cortisol), and switch it off quickly; subsequently less prone to learned helplessness; tentative human evidence too
- **Walter Cannon** (1920s): prescient worry about central heating/air conditioning/hot water eliminating thermal stress — "The man who daily takes a cold bath and works until he sweats may be keeping 'fit'" by not letting thermoregulation atrophy; climate control may contribute to obesity epidemic AND eliminated a valuable toughening process
- **Dienstbier**: cold tolerance may increase emotional stability through shared nervous system for thermoregulation and emotional arousal
- Nordic practice of sauna + cold plunge may provide ideal stress/recovery toughening pattern
- Too early to recommend specific toughening regime for finance, but financial institutions should recognize that handling risk involves physiology, not just probability/macroeconomics knowledge
- Training programs should access primitive brain (not just rational cortex) → may involve more physical exercise; banks could learn from top-class athletes' programs for controlling hormones/emotions

### Learning to Listen (pp. 244-247)
- Body signals like fatigue and stress are messages telling us what actions to take
- **Fatigue reconsidered**: common sense says exhaustion = ran out of fuel (like car running out of gas); but often mental fatigue disappears if we merely change activities
- **Neuroscience model of fatigue**: fatigue is a signal that expected return from current activity has dropped below metabolic cost; brain searches for optimal allocation of attentional/metabolic resources; cure for fatigue is not rest but a fresh task
- Overtime work doesn't inherently cause hypertension/heart disease — these occur mainly when workers have no control over attention allocation
- Flexibility in choosing work tasks could reduce fatigue while management finds workers refreshed by new assignments as much as by vacation
- **Novelty paradox**: novelty rejuvenates when we're fatigued, but turns toxic under chronic stress (Berlyne's n-shaped hill — novelty/complexity beyond moderate levels promotes anxiety)
- **Holmes and Rahe Social Readjustment Rating Scale**: even POSITIVE life changes (marriage, birth of child, outstanding achievement) add novelty that exacerbates chronic stress; heart disease and hypertension are "silent killers" partly because we don't realize positive novelty is stressing us
- When chronically stressed: minimize novelty, seek familiarity — but we often do the opposite (exotic vacation); under chronic stress, foreign travel adds to physiological load; better to stay home with family, familiar music, old films
- Exercise under chronic stress: mostly analgesic (natural opioids), per Porges; what we really need is familiarity
- **Vagus nerve and familiarity**: familiar faces/voices of friends and family signal brain stem that fight-or-flight is not needed; vagus stands down from high alert; "if you are blessed with a calm family and friends whose fortunes are uncorrelated with your own, it can help enormously... just to look into their faces"

### Engaging the Vagus (pp. 247-248)
- **Vagal nerve stimulation (VNS)**: implanted electronic device in chest has treated intractable depression and chronic pain
- **Dive reflex**: splashing cold water on face engages vagus → slows heart, breathing, may release painkillers
- **Breathing exercises**: slow, deep diaphragmatic breaths (not shallow chest breathing) bring vagus online; playing wind instruments, singing, expanding phrase duration when talking — all have vagal influence on heart
- Controlled breathing is central to yoga, meditation, Buddhist mindfulness
- **Read Montague**: Buddhist meditation practitioners engaged gut feelings more than others → more rational financial decision-making
- Research into stress, vagus, and gut feelings is "building a bridge between East and West"
- Increasing vagal tone = "Holy Grail for stress research"

### Stress in the Workplace (pp. 248-253)
- Can banks make objective workplace changes to reduce chronic stress? Novelty, uncertainty, uncontrollability endemic to markets
- At moderate levels: uncertainty sparks risk-taking (that's the job); concern is when it becomes chronic/debilitating
- **Whitehall Studies** revisited: uncertainty → hypertension, cholesterol, heart disease among civil servants
- Coates's own research: market uncertainty (volatility, P&L uncontrollability) had very powerful effect on traders' cortisol
- **Patient-controlled analgesia**: doctors who let patients administer their own painkillers found patients used LESS medication; removing uncertainty/uncontrollability reduced pain signal → now standard practice
- Can't eliminate workplace uncertainty, but can establish control outside work: what we eat, exercise, who we spend time with → "toehold amidst the chaos" that deludes us into feeling in charge → reduces bodily damage
- **Social support**: Swedish study of 752 men — chronically stressed men had 3x death rate over 7 years; BUT those with supportive circle of friends/family showed NO correlation between stress and mortality
- **Devolving control** (Theorell & Karasek, *Healthy Work*): specialised workers mechanically executing upper management plans = high workload + low control = high stress illness; concluded healthy worker = productive worker = lower medical costs
- **Volvo car plant** (Sweden): devolved production control to small worker groups → stress-related illness dropped noticeably
- In banks: uncertainty from market AND chain of command; some hedge funds separate trade conception from execution (different desks) → reduces stress, improves decision-making
- Upper management often removes losing positions from individual trader accounts during crises → restores sense of control
- **Managerial instability**: biggest stress source in banks; rumors of layoffs, management shake-ups, responsibility changes; middle managers under stress "often act like dominant monkeys" → bully juniors; senior management should restrain this
- Not arguing for cosy atmosphere — concerned with stabilizing risk preferences as crisis wears on, preventing trading community from becoming "clinical population"

### Bonus Structure and Pro-Cyclical Incentives (pp. 253-256)
- Most effective stress reduction: stabilize bankers'/traders' careers — make employment more like building a career than "elbowing for room at the roulette wheel"
- **Tortoise vs Hare fable**: Tortoise makes bank $10M/yr for 5 years, gets $1M/yr bonus = $50M profit, $5M paid; Hare makes $100M/yr for 4 years, $20M/yr bonus, loses $500M in year 5 with no bonus = bank lost $100M but paid Hare $80M
- Which trader would you rather be? Hare — even out of a job, losing big is taken as sign you're a "hitter," rewarded with offers from other banks
- **Strategic calculation**: traders maximize volatility of results + frequency of bonus payments → "high-water marks" strategy; managers and CEOs conclude same; if all Hares blow up together, no single person/bank looks bad
- Risk-management + bonus logic AMPLIFY destabilizing biology of risk-taking — traders given freedom to increase risk in bull markets when system least needs it
- **Solution**: bonus scheme paying over full business cycle (~4-5 years); if profitable → draw from pool; if blow up like Hare → lose all previous bonuses; banks could increase deferred bonus percentage the longer a trader remains profitable (5% on 1-yr, 7% on 2-yr, 10% on 3-yr returns)
- **Risk managers**: should pull trading stars off the floor for days at a time for physiological cooling-off (like rain break in tennis); **Sarbanes-Oxley Act** (2002) mandates no-contact vacations → inadvertent benefit of breaking physiological feedback loops
- **Hiring**: banks hire as if growth continues forever → fire indiscriminately in downturn; trading floors called "rotating door" and "self-cleaning ovens" → exaggerates stress in crisis
- If hired in smaller numbers with long-term commitment + bonuses over longer periods → less volatile earnings, smaller losses, fewer layoffs; prudent risk-taking → secure employment → less irrational pessimism when economy needs risk-takers
- Nature AND nurture (biology AND management) both contribute to financial crises; evidence that management has "more than enough clout to tame the beasts lurking within risk-takers"
- Study finding: high testosterone AND high Sharpe Ratios (risk-adjusted returns) in same traders; management used draconian risk-management (pulling plug on losers, sit-on-hands orders) + profit-sharing → harnessed risk appetite; "biology needs the guiding hand of experience and well-structured incentives"

### Wellness Programmes (pp. 256-257)
- Banks should participate in workplace "wellness programmes" — preventive medicine clinics located in gym or office
- Combined venue: personal trainer + physiologist + doctor at one visit
- Survey entire employee life: workplace, home, recreation, eating habits → coordinate stress reduction
- Can spot trends in company health (musculoskeletal disorders, stress-related illness) and investigate work-floor causes
- "Wise and far-seeing managers who put health and stabilised risk preferences at the top of their company's agenda"

## CHAPTER 9: From Molecule to Market (pp. 258-261+)

### Managing the Biology of the Market (pp. 258-260)
- Financial crises more frequent and severe than any time since Crash of 1929
- Causes: historically low real interest rates, financial deregulation, low margin requirements, high leverage, opening of Asian/emerging markets, decline of partnerships → shift from long-term to short-term profits
- Bull and bear markets grossly exaggerated by irrational exuberance and pessimism of risk-takers — these are BIOLOGICAL reactions to conditions of above-average opportunity/threat
- Hormones + cascade of molecular signals build up in traders/investors during bull and bear markets → shift risk preferences → amplify the cycle
- Under pathologically elevated hormones, trading community at peak of bubble or pit of crash "may effectively become a clinical population" → price and interest-rate insensitive → contributes to "Black Swan" events (Nassim Taleb)
- Explains why central banks have had such limited success arresting bull markets or cushioning crashes
- Risk managers and policy-makers should bear in mind the likely clinical state of trading community under extreme scenarios (e.g., 50% stock market drop → traumatised community won't respond to lower interest rates)

### Keynes and Animal Spirits (pp. 260-261)
- **Keynes** fully understood challenges of non-rational decision-making; described "animal spirits" driving investment/market sentiment
- Never attempted to specify what animal spirits actually are biologically; as they bulked larger in his thinking, he lost faith in interest rates as policy tool
- Came to believe in fiscal policy — state taking over role of stabilizing economy that individuals can no longer do on their own
- Keynes & Bertrand Russell anecdote: Russell said problem with politics is irrationality, solution is to conduct it rationally; Keynes found such conversations "really quite boring" — hinting at his doubts about rational-choice ideals
- Rational-choice theory → cure for irrational behavior = more information, or correct conclusions from existing info (talking cure); or change prices (interest rates) and let rational agents reallocate → neither has worked well in practice
- **David Owen** (senior British politician/neurologist): studied "hubris syndrome" — political leaders acquiring mental illness (irrational exuberance) while exercising power; presents conundrum for political theory: how to protect from leaders who develop mental illness in office?
- Owen's concerns echo central banking challenges — managing/containing damage of unbalanced biology in markets
- Not much in canon of economic/political theory to help; but neuroscience/physiology suggest there is a great deal we CAN do

### Women in the Financial World (pp. 261+)
- If bull market amplified by testosterone feedback loop among traders (mostly young men) → could biological diversity stabilize markets?
- Women produce 10-20% of male testosterone levels; not exposed to same prenatal androgens → less prone to winner effect
- **Shelley Taylor**: women's stress response is NOT fight-or-flight (that's a male response); women's default is **"tend-and-befriend"** — urge to affiliate, care for others; if you have children, this makes more evolutionary sense than fighting/fleeing
- Women: same average cortisol levels as men but triggered by different events — less stressed by competitive failures, more stressed by social/relationship problems
- Net result: women may be less hormonally reactive to making/losing money → greater numbers among risk-takers could dampen market volatility
- **Older men**: testosterone peaks mid-twenties then slowly declines (accelerating after 50); cortisol drifts upward → less susceptible to testosterone feedback loops; bring lifetime of experience (Crash of '87, S&L crisis) → less likely to jump into risks without considering range of outcomes
- Trading floors hostile to older traders — slower reactions mistaken for fear; but legendary investors (Warren Buffett, Benjamin Graham) achieved status later in life
- Women: ~5% of traders; even that low number includes diversity-push hires; common explanations (macho environment, too risk-averse) not fully convincing
- Women already on trading floors (50% of sales force, seated next to trading desks); many formerly male fields (law, medicine) now gender-balanced
- **"Boys Will Be Boys" study** (Brad Barber & Terrance Odean, UC): analyzed 35,000 brokerage accounts 1991-1997; single women outperformed single men by 1.44%; Chicago Hedge Fund Research (2009): women-run hedge funds significantly outperformed men-run over 9 years
- Men's underperformance traced to overtrading (overconfidence → excessive buying/selling → bid-offer spread + commissions erode returns)
- Women's superior returns: lower transaction costs? Higher actual risk-taking? Better judgement?
- **Key insight**: women ~60% of risk-takers at big UK asset-management companies; asset management IS risk-taking but with longer time horizons (days/weeks/years vs seconds/minutes); difference between men and women may not be level of risk-aversion but TIME HORIZON over which they make decisions
- Men dominated trading floors because trading was traditionally high-frequency; but with execution-only boxes and computers, what's increasingly needed is the call on the market + understanding of risk → long-term strategic thinking → women excel
- **Solution**: judge all risk-takers (especially women) over full business cycle; bonuses calculated over longer periods → market would value stability + long-term performance → banks would naturally hire more women without affirmative action

### The Elephant Musth Analogy (pp. 262-263)
- Counter-argument: bubbles are a small price for channelling men's testosterone into non-violent activities (Andrew Sullivan); Keynes thought capitalism was preferred antidote to violent urges — "better to terrorise your chequebook than your neighbour"
- Coates disagrees: extreme testosterone behavior occurs when young males are ISOLATED from elders
- **Elephant musth**: young male elephants without elder bulls go into musth prematurely — testosterone surges 40-50x above baseline → run amok, kill other animals, trample villages; South African park rangers solution: bring in an elder male elephant → his presence calmed the rogues
- Lesson for finance: "when it comes to allocating the capital of society... we probably do not want volatile behaviour. We want balanced judgement and stable asset prices, and we are more likely to get these if we have the whole village present — men and women, young and old"

### Biological Diversity as Market Stabiliser (pp. 263)
- Need not claim women/older men are BETTER risk-takers — just DIFFERENT
- Difference = greater stability; a balanced financial community "could not possibly do any worse than the system we have now"
- Good science behind this policy: biology can help understand and regulate markets "in a manner that is not in the least bit threatening"

### A Return to What We Once Knew (pp. 264-267)
- Science often merely reveals tacit knowledge shared between body and brain that we've always possessed but couldn't articulate
- Most homeostatic regulation inaccessible to consciousness; gut feelings on the fringes of awareness; fatigue/stress can reach full consciousness but are often misunderstood
- "We are in the strange position of creatures who on the one hand generate bodily messages intended to maintain health and happiness... but on the other sometimes do not know... what they mean"
- Body and pre-conscious brain meeting conscious brain = "two peoples meeting on their mutual border and each trying to communicate through a language the other only dimly comprehends"
- Through biology we are learning to decipher these signals
- **Aristotle**: created template of Western mind; uniquely appreciated role of body in thinking; believed mind is "of necessity embodied" — without a body we wouldn't have much to think about
- **Plato vs Aristotle**: Plato's vision of pure rationality (spark of pure reason in decaying body) dominated Western thought; clean, ordered, crystalline ideas = "bleached white columns of a Greek temple"
- Aristotle's realism: "a marketplace seething with activity and sweat and emotion"; studied messy details of embodied existence — desire, greed, ambition, anger, hate, alongside bravery, altruism, love
- "We may thrill to the ethereal beauty of Plato's vision, but we feel at home with Aristotle"
- **Idealism vs realism** in political thought: Plato's *Republic* → unearthly ideals forcing people into ill-suited roles → social/political disasters; equally, otherworldly ideals of economic rationality → marketplace fatally prone to financial crises
- Aristotle recommended political institutions that accommodate actual human beings; looked at biological details and judged policies by how well they suited our nature
- Neuroscience/physiology today rediscovering unity of brain and body that Aristotle understood; we should follow his template for conceiving social science grounded in human biology — "from molecule to market"
- Economics beginning to merge with medicine and epidemiology; Rudolf Virchow: "politics is medicine writ large" — today extends to economics
- C.P. Snow's "two cultures" (science and humanities) could be bridged through human biology
- **Temple of Delphi**: "Know Thyself!" — today that increasingly means know your biochemistry; "Doing so turns out not to be a dehumanising experience at all. It is a liberating one."
