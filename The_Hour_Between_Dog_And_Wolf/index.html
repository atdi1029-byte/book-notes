Introduction

Risk is a physical experience. When you take risk, your body reminds you in the most insistent manner that you have flesh and bone. A driver speeding along a winding road, a surfer riding a monster wave, a soldier sprinting across no-man’s land — each faces the possibility of injury or death, and that possibility sharpens the mind by calling forth an overwhelming biological reaction known as the fight-or-flight response. Winston Churchill, a hardened campaigner from deadly wars, marvelled at how a regimental polo match in India could produce faces more strained than those he saw during graver crises. The same biological reactions fire when the threat is financial rather than physical, because money acts as a powerful token distilling the threats and opportunities we faced over eons of evolutionary time.

“Financial risk-taking is as much a biological activity, with as many medical consequences, as facing down a grizzly bear.” — John Coates

Financial risk carries consequences that physical risk does not. A change in income or social rank lingers for months, even years. Our defence reactions were designed to switch on in an emergency and then switch off after minutes or hours. When the biochemical disturbance persists — through an ongoing series of wins or losses — it can transform us beyond recognition. On a winning streak we become euphoric, manic, foolhardy, puffed up with self-importance. On a losing streak, stress hormones linger, promoting pathological risk-aversion, depression, and a cascade of medical problems from high blood pressure to gastric ulcers.

Economics has traditionally treated risk assessment as a purely intellectual affair — the calculation of probabilities, expected returns, optimal allocations. John Coates wants to add some guts to this bloodless account. Neuroscience and physiology have shown that when we take risk, we do far more than think about it. Our bodies prepare physically, switching on an emergency network of physiological circuitry. The resulting surge in electrical and chemical activity feeds back on the brain, affecting the way it thinks. Body and brain twine as a single entity, united in the face of challenge. Normally this fusion sharpens reactions and produces the gut feelings essential for successful risk-taking. But when the chemical surges become too powerful, they can overwhelm us, producing irrational exuberance in a bubble or paralysing pessimism in a crash.

The book follows a cast of fictional traders — Scott, Martin, Gwen, and Logan — through a composite trading day during the dot-com bubble and subsequent credit crisis. Their stories interweave with deep dives into neuroscience, endocrinology, and evolutionary biology, revealing how the same hormones that once helped our ancestors hunt on the African savannah now drive the boom-and-bust cycle on Wall Street. Two threads run through the narrative: the overt trading behaviour visible on the floor, and the hidden physiology behind it.

Chapter 1: The Biology of a Market Bubble

Coates worked on Wall Street through the 1990s — at Goldman Sachs, then Merrill Lynch, then running a trading desk at Deutsche Bank — and had a front-row seat to the dot-com bubble. The Nasdaq rose from below 600 in 1991 to above 5,000 in 2000, a rally unprecedented in speed and magnitude, supported by a paucity of hard financial data. Legendary investors who bet against the trend retired in disgust; Julian Robertson of Tiger Capital shut down his fund, echoing Keynes’s observation that markets can remain irrational longer than you can remain solvent. Investors were not engaged in rational calculation or game theory. When pressed, they shot off starbursts of trendy concepts like “convergence.” History was irrelevant, statistics counted for little. Fear and greed is too simple an account — what Coates observed was something more clinical. Traders walked more erect, slept less, clubbed until 4 a.m., and took on ever riskier bets with worsening risk-reward trade-offs. They showed symptoms of clinical mania.

“Assessment of risk is replaced by judgements of certainty — they just know what is going to happen.” — John Coates

David Owen, a former British Foreign Secretary and trained neurologist, identified a condition he called hubris syndrome in political and business leaders — characterised by recklessness, inattention to detail, overwhelming self-confidence, and contempt for others. Women on the trading floor were relatively immune to the frenzy. They produce only 10–20% of men’s testosterone levels. The overconfidence and risk-taking during the bubble felt chemical, not rational. Coates began to suspect that an endogenous narcotic was transforming traders into different people. He found his answer at Rockefeller University, where Bruce McEwen had mapped steroid receptors throughout the brain, showing that hormones do not merely affect the body — they return to the brain and change how we think and behave.

The key model is the winner effect, documented extensively in animal behaviour research. When two males fight for territory or a mate, both experience an anticipatory testosterone surge. The winner emerges with even higher testosterone; the loser with lower. The winner carries elevated testosterone into the next round, gaining a competitive edge through increased muscle mass, hemoglobin, confidence, and appetite for risk. This creates a positive feedback loop that explains winning and losing streaks across species. But at some point, elevated steroids begin to have the opposite effect. Animals on winning streaks start more fights, spend more time in the open, and suffer increased mortality. Confidence segues into overconfidence, and risk-taking becomes reckless. This model perfectly describes how a bull market morphs into a bubble.

If testosterone is the molecule of irrational exuberance, then cortisol is the molecule of irrational pessimism. Cortisol is the main hormone of the stress response, designed for the long siege. It shuts down expensive bodily functions — digestion, reproduction, growth, immune defence — and breaks down energy stores to flush glucose into the blood. Short-term cortisol sharpens attention and delivers a slight thrill. But when cortisol stays elevated for weeks or months, it produces anxiety, selective recall of disturbing memories, and a tendency to find danger where none exists. The core hypothesis emerges: testosterone rises in bull markets, increasing risk-taking until a rally morphs into a bubble; cortisol rises in bear markets, making traders irrationally risk-averse until a sell-off becomes a crash. Steroid hormones in traders’ bodies may systematically shift risk preferences across the business cycle, destabilising it.

Coates retired from Wall Street, returned to Cambridge, retrained in neuroscience and endocrinology for four years, and designed experiments to test the winner effect on London trading floors. The results supported his hypothesis. He then turns to the philosophical roots of the problem: the Western tradition of mind-body dualism, running from Pythagoras through Plato, St. Paul, and Descartes, treats the mind as a ghost in the machine — separate from and superior to the body. Neo-classical economics inherited this assumption, modelling economic agents as walking computers. Behavioural economics challenged rationality but still focused on psychology, not physiology. Aristotle, by contrast, saw no mind-body split. For him, the soul could not exist without the body. Economics, Coates argues, needs to put the body back into the economy.

Chapter 2: Thinking with Your Body

The brain did not evolve for pure thought. It evolved to plan and execute physical movement. The tunicate, or sea squirt, is born with a brain, an eyespot, and an otolith. It swims freely searching for feeding grounds, but once it cements itself to the seafloor, it eats its own brain — because if you do not need to move, you do not need a brain. Daniel Wolpert of Cambridge calls those who believe in this primacy of movement “motor chauvinists,” arguing that thought itself proceeds by hijacking algorithms originally developed for planning movements. Andy Clark puts it memorably: we have inherited “a mind on the hoof.” The true genius of the human nervous system is not our capacity for abstract reasoning but our ability to learn movements that do not come naturally — ballet, guitar, downhill skiing at 90 miles per hour. No computer can match the dexterity of an eight-year-old building Lego.

The neo-cortex, home to higher thought and planning, did not evolve in isolation. It expanded together with the corticospinal tract, the nerve bundle controlling fine musculature. Meanwhile the cerebellum — which stores procedural memories of how to ride a bicycle or play a flute — actually outgrew the neo-cortex. Modern humans may have had a smaller neo-cortex than Neanderthals but a larger cerebellum, giving us a more efficient operating system and, paradoxically, more effective brainpower. Every thought involving a choice of action triggers a kaleidoscopic shift from one bodily state to another: eyes widen or squint, pupils dilate, skin flushes or blanches. Choice is a whole-body experience.

“All thoughts involving choice of action involve a kaleidoscopic shift from one bodily state to another. Choice is a whole-body experience.” — John Coates

The brain stem controls automatic reflexes — breathing, heart rate, sweating, the startle response. It acts as a life-support system so fundamental that other brain regions can be destroyed while a person survives in a coma, as long as the brain stem operates. The great evolutionary advance of mammals was thermoregulation — the ability to maintain internal temperature regardless of external conditions. This is metabolically expensive (an idling mammal burns five to ten times the energy of an idling reptile), but it made mammals like tanks, able to move farther and faster while requiring more sophisticated supply-line management. Humans took on even more bodily control, developing an ever more advanced nervous system and a richer dialogue between body and brain.

Three brain regions are critical for this dialogue. The hypothalamus regulates hormones, eating, sleeping, reproduction, and aggression, serving as the main integration site for emotional behaviour. The amygdala assigns emotional significance to events — without it, a grizzly bear is merely a large moving object. The insula collects interoceptive information from internal organs and assembles it into a unified image of overall body state. Bud Craig at the University of Arizona mapped the nervous circuitry for interoception, revealing sense organs that point inward, feeding information up through the vagus nerve to the brain. Craig, Antonio Damasio, and Antoine Bechara have argued that gut feelings, emotions, rationality, and even self-consciousness may be advanced tools evolved to regulate the body. The body stands behind the brain like an éminence grise, applying pressure at exactly the right point and time.

Chapter 3: The Speed of Thought

A 31st-floor trading room on Wall Street: roughly a thousand desks, each with half a dozen screens, arranged in a gridwork of aisles. The DuPont Pension Fund needs to invest $750 million in ten-year US Treasury bonds. The trade comes to Martin, who must price and execute it in under two minutes. He cannot simply read a screen — broker quotes are good for perhaps $100 million, and for the remaining $650 million he must rely on his feel for the market. He has been unconsciously mapping trading patterns all morning — highs, lows, sizes, speeds of movement — and each scenario triggers a minute body shift: a muscle tightening, a shiver of dread, a shot of excitement. He offers the bonds at 100.25, then spends 45 minutes buying them back at an average price just below 100.23. Half a million dollars in profit. The Treasury desk is the privileged perch of the trading floor: during crises, clients sell risky assets and buy Treasuries, volumes balloon, spreads widen, and Martin can make five million dollars at a crack.

How can anyone make decisions this fast? The answer lies in the architecture of the visual system and the pre-conscious brain. An image hitting the retina takes roughly 100 milliseconds to reach consciousness — by the time we see something, the world has already moved on. The eyes do not operate like cameras; they sample visual scenes through rapid fixation-and-jump movements called saccades, up to five per second, and the brain weaves these snapshots into a seamless experience. A cricket infielder standing 14 feet from the batsman has roughly 90 milliseconds to react to a ball travelling at 100 miles per hour. That is less time than it takes for the image to reach consciousness, let alone for the brain to formulate a decision and send commands to muscles. Yet fielders catch these balls routinely.

The brain compensates for its slowness by anticipating. It fast-forwards what you see, placing moving objects slightly ahead of their actual position. The flash-lag effect proves this: a moving blue circle contains a blinking yellow dot, and the dot appears to trail behind because it cannot be anticipated. Hearing processes roughly 25% faster than vision, which is why top athletes rely as much on the sound of ball on racket as on sight. At what Coates calls the horizon of simultaneity — about 15 metres — sights and sounds are perceived as simultaneous. Old trading-floor intercoms broadcasting futures prices may have sharpened trader attention through multi-sensory engagement.

“Thinking, one could say, is something we do only when we are no good at an activity.” — John Coates

When speed truly matters, the brain cuts out consciousness entirely. Pre-attentive processing handles perception, decision-making, and movement initiation without conscious involvement. Erich Maria Remarque described how WWI soldiers learned to distinguish deadly “daisy-cutter” shells from the general din: “By the animal instinct that is awakened in us we are led and protected. It is far quicker, much more sure, less fallible, than consciousness.” Of the roughly 11 million bits per second entering our brains through our senses, only about 40 bits reach consciousness. The superior colliculus, an ancient nucleus in the midbrain that reptiles once used to track insects, still operates in humans, tracking motion and capturing attention without conscious awareness. The startle response — an involuntary muscle contraction triggered by an expanding symmetrical shadow — fires in about 70 milliseconds for the head, 100 for the torso. Skilled anticipation fills the gap: batters study pitchers, infielders register batsmen’s stances, boxers scan opponents’ footwork. Learned movements are passed down to lower brain regions for speed, achieving what psychologists call unconscious competence.

Benjamin Libet’s famous experiments in the 1970s found that the brain begins preparing an action roughly 300 milliseconds before the conscious decision to move. Consciousness may not choose and initiate actions but rather observe and veto decisions already made. V.S. Ramachandran put it sharply: “We do not in fact have free will; what we have is free won’t.” Algorithmic trading (“black boxes”) now executes trades in under 10 milliseconds, and during the 2007–08 credit crisis, the first major test of human versus machine trading, humans won — but only just. Machines excel at execution, but during crises they fail to comprehend novel data and managers unplug them, leaving humans to step into the breach. What remains uniquely human is judgement — the call on the market — and gut feelings are perhaps the most important data informing that call.

Chapter 4: Gut Feelings

George Soros once said that when he was actively running his fund, he suffered from backache, and he used the onset of acute pain as a signal that something was wrong in his portfolio. Gut feelings are not mystical; they are the body receiving information through interoceptive pathways, passing it to the brain via signals that act silently, barely breaking consciousness, yet powerfully influencing every decision. Daniel Kahneman distinguished between fast and slow thinking; others have called it automatic versus controlled thought. Automatic processing is involuntary, effortless, parallel, and opaque to introspection — exactly the kind of processing a trader needs when pricing a $750 million trade in under two minutes.

Can intuition be trusted? Kahneman and Gary Klein, who came from opposite sides of the question, collaborated and found a resolution: intuition is reliable when the environment is regular enough to produce repeating patterns and the person encounters those patterns frequently with quick feedback. Chess, firefighting, and paramedicine meet these criteria. Political forecasting does not. Do financial markets qualify? Coates and Lionel Page tested traders’ Sharpe Ratios and found that experienced traders had ratios above 1.0, well above the S&P 500’s long-term 0.4, and that Sharpe Ratios rose with years of experience — a genuine learning curve. Financial markets do meet the Kahneman-Klein criteria for trustworthy intuition.

“Rationality by itself gains no purchase on the world, it merely spins its wheels, without the grit of somatic markers.” — Antonio Damasio, paraphrased by Coates

Damasio and Bechara’s research on patients with damage to body-signal-integration areas revealed something startling: these patients had normal or exceptional cognitive abilities but made terrible decisions. Their IQs counted for nothing without homeostatic and emotional feedback from the body. Feeling, it turned out, was an integral component of the machinery of reason. The somatic marker hypothesis proposes that each memory is stored with the bodily sensations felt at the time. When we face a new situation, we scroll through options, each triggering a subtle body shift — a muscle tensing, quickened breath, a frisson of excitement — until one option just feels right. The Iowa Gambling Task confirmed this: players began choosing profitable decks before they consciously knew why, guided by skin conductance spikes when they contemplated the losing decks. Good judgement may owe as much to the body’s ability to produce and detect signals as it does to IQ.

William James proposed a counter-intuitive idea about emotions: we do not cry because we are sad; we are sad because we are crying. We perceive a threat, the body reacts automatically, and the physiological changes are then reported to the brain as the feeling of fear. Walter Cannon challenged James, arguing that bodily feedback is too slow and not unique enough to differentiate emotions. But Cannon focused only on the visceral nervous system. The muscular nervous system transmits signals at 120 metres per second — broadband compared to the visceral system’s dial-up speeds of 5–30 metres per second. Paul Ekman’s work on micro-expressions showed that facial displays of anger, disgust, and fear flicker and vanish within 40 milliseconds, and experiments by Robert Levenson and Ekman demonstrated that simply composing facial expressions of happiness or sadness caused subjects to feel those moods. James was right: the body has enough different signals to compose messages with the subtlety of a piano keyboard and the speed of a radio transmission.

The enteric nervous system — containing roughly 100 million neurons, more than the spinal cord — acts as a “second brain” controlling the gut independently. Michael Gershon coined the term, noting that the digestive tract is technically outside the body, a tunnel permitting the exterior to run right through us. The vagus nerve serves as a hotline between the two brains. Traders’ stress hormones, Coates found, accurately tracked market volatility and P&L swings, while their self-reported questionnaires about stress had “little if anything to do with reality” — as random and irrelevant as split-brain confabulations. If you want to know what traders think of the market, do not ask their opinions; look at what trades they put on.

Chapter 5: The Thrill of the Search

After Martin’s DuPont trade, his body gears down. Heart rate and metabolism drop to a slow idle, adrenalin dissipates, and the vagus nerve takes control “like a mother’s hand on a troubled brow.” The half-million-dollar profit trickles through his veins like a potent muscle relaxer. This recovery period is what sports physiologists consider the key to continued growth: challenge followed by recovery, stress followed by rest. Fight-or-flight must switch off quickly after a challenge so that rest-and-digest systems can switch on. Denied recovery even when things go well, biology becomes unbalanced and pathological states emerge.

Then a subsonic tremor shakes the market. Prices flicker at a different frequency, dancing into a new pattern like a single turn of a kaleidoscope. Volatility has barely budged, but the minuscule changes are unexpected, and nothing snaps us to attention faster than the unexpected. Claude Shannon at Bell Labs discovered that information is synonymous with unpredictability — pure information puts us in a state of maximum uncertainty about what comes next. Our sensory apparatus is designed to attend almost exclusively to information: it ignores predictable events and orients rapidly to novel ones. The cerebellum dampens expected sensations (which is why we cannot tickle ourselves) and flags the unexpected as important. Without novelty we do not function well — “highway hypnosis” on featureless roads is a symptom of stimulus hunger.

“Good traders like Martin do not just process information, they feel it.” — John Coates

When the alarm centre — the locus ceruleus — is tripped, it sprays noradrenalin throughout the brain, acting as a neuromodulator that alters the sensitivity of all neurons, like turning up the lights in a room and the volume on a microphone. Daniel Berlyne of the University of Toronto plotted arousal against information and found an elegant inverted-U curve: too little information produces boredom, too much produces anxiety, and just the right amount produces curiosity, diffuse satisfaction, and the coveted state of flow. The amygdala tags pattern-recognition matches with emotional urgency, providing a quick-and-dirty assessment of threat or opportunity. This tag team of pattern recognition, emotional assessment, and alarm centre constitutes our gut feelings about what matters. The financial markets are the most information-dense environment most people ever encounter; the VIX “Fear Index” spiked from a sleepy 12% to over 80% during the credit crisis.

Around 11 a.m. a rumour reaches the floor: a Fed governor gave a speech to senior bankers expressing concern about an unjustified stock rally and signalling a possible rate hike. The Fed rate is the benchmark for all asset values — Wall Street lore says “never fight the Fed.” The bond market is hit hardest because interest-rate news has the most immediate and undiluted effect there. Martin calls a hasty meeting with a dozen traders. As the sell-off deepens, the Treasury desk loses more than four million dollars. But Martin stays cool, immersed in the flow, scrutinising every price change across multiple layers of analysis until he senses a capitulation trade — nervous managers dumping bonds at any price. At 2:15 p.m. the announcement comes: a quarter point. The market breathes a collective sigh of relief and rockets upward. By the close, the Treasury desk has hauled in almost $12 million.

Dopamine, the neuromodulator of anticipation and wanting, underpins our addiction to information and risk. When we receive valuable information or perform a survival-promoting act, dopamine floods the pleasure pathways. The brain values dopamine so highly that animals given a choice between eating and self-stimulating their dopamine systems will self-stimulate until they starve. The key discovery is that dopamine is not the molecule of pleasure but of anticipation — it spikes not at the reward itself but at the cue predicting the reward. The amount of dopamine released depends not on the absolute reward but on how unexpected it is. We enjoy and crave environments in which we receive unexpected rewards; in other words, we enjoy risk. Keynes called this the “animal spirits” driving investment — “a spontaneous urge to action rather than inaction.” Bruce Alexander’s famous Rat Park experiment showed that animals in enriched environments with normal opportunities for search, work, and play did not become addicted to morphine — they got their daily dopamine from living a full life.

Chapter 6: The Fuel of Exuberance

Spring arrives. With the Fed silenced, the markets become an unsupervised playground. Stocks are up 40%, bonds about 20% over the past two years. News, no matter its content, arrives with the breathless promise of unparalleled opportunity. Money rains on Wall Street: every department reports record profits. The irresistible urge to increase risk takes hold. Traders who traded $100 million now trade $200 million, even $1 billion. During the housing bubble of 2002–2006, position sizes, P&Ls, and bonuses all added a decimal point. The golden boy of the 1990s traded $500 million risk-weighted with bonuses of $1–3 million; by the mid-2000s, individual bonuses could exceed $50 million.

Testosterone research has a colourful history. In 1889, the 72-year-old French-American neurologist Charles Edward Brown-Séquard drank a brew from dog and guinea pig testicles and told a Paris audience it was a rejuvenating elixir — largely a placebo effect that stamped the field with crazed expectations. Adolf Butenandt isolated the first androgen in 1931, extracting 50 milligrams of androsterone from 25,000 litres of urine from Berlin police barracks. Leopold Ružička synthesised testosterone from cholesterol in 1935. They shared the 1939 Nobel Prize, with Butenandt reportedly telling the committee: “Dynamite, gentlemen, it is pure dynamite!” Testosterone and dopamine work synergistically — testosterone increases dopamine in the nucleus accumbens, constituting the bass line of euphoria while dopamine provides the treble.

“Trying to hoard steroid molecules in a vesicle would be like trying to lock ghosts in a room.” — John Coates

Unlike adrenalin, steroids cannot be stored — they cross cell membranes freely. They must be produced on demand, taking 15 minutes or more just to get started and hours to take full effect. But their mechanism is uniquely powerful: they enter the cell nucleus and cause gene transcription, making proteins, the building blocks of the body. A single steroid hormone can orchestrate a bewildering suite of changes across nearly every tissue: bone density, lean-muscle mass, hemoglobin, clotting agents, mood, sexual fantasy, and risk-taking behaviour. The SRY gene on the Y chromosome almost single-handedly creates a male from the default female template through testosterone. Coates measured 2D:4D finger ratios (the ratio of index to ring finger length, a marker of prenatal testosterone exposure) on his London trading floor and found that lower ratios predicted profitability over the previous two years and survival in the business. A hormone these traders had been exposed to before birth was predicting their lifetime performance.

On the trading floor, the winner effect accelerates. Scott doubles his core S&P position to 4,000 contracts, then builds to 5,000, then 6,000. Risk managers challenge him but lack clout; management’s incentives align with risk-taking because bonuses are a percentage of P&L. Testosterone follows the same inverted-U dose-response curve as Berlyne’s arousal hill: below optimal, motivation is low; at optimal, performance peaks; beyond optimal, overconfidence becomes recklessness. Pope and Katz at Harvard found that many steroid users succumb to mania — one university athlete became so convinced of his invincibility that he asked a friend to film him driving a car into a tree. In finance, the feedback loop causes traders to pass through thrill and excitement until they are convinced of their own infallibility. The traders responsible for bank-crippling losses frequently turn out to be the stars of yesterday. Testosterone does not start a bull market — that is usually a technological breakthrough or the opening of new markets — but it may be the catalyst that turns a rally into a bubble.

Chapter 7: Stress Response on Wall Street

September arrives. Martin, Gwen, Scott, and Logan return from summer tanned and ready for the final run to bonus time. But the mortgage market has been on a roller-coaster ride through August. Mortgage-backed bonds — bundles of individual home mortgages — normally carry yield spreads of 1.10% over Treasuries, but spreads have widened to 1.60% with rumours of rising defaults and foreclosures. Scott and Logan decide to reload on credit risk. Logan buys $100 million in sub-prime mortgages yielding a tantalising 10–15%. Scott buys $300 million of the ABX index, a basket of sub-prime mortgages trading at 41, down 59% from par. Housing news arrives: existing home sales down 8%, Case-Shiller shows the largest price drop ever. The ABX falls through Scott’s worst-case scenario and keeps falling. Within weeks, the arb desk is hemorrhaging money — not only giving back the year’s profits but losing an additional $375 million.

“Deep in his brain, ancient circuits register the anomaly and the fact that it is a nasty one.” — John Coates

The stress response unfolds in four stages. First, the amygdala registers danger and sends electrical signals to the rest of the brain in milliseconds. Second, electrical signals race from the amygdala via the brain stem to visceral organs, accelerating heart rate, blood pressure, and breathing in less than a second. Third, adrenalin is released, working over seconds and minutes to mobilise fuel. Fourth, if the crisis persists, the adrenal cortex secretes cortisol, which takes hours or even days to achieve full effect. Joe LeDoux’s model distinguishes between the “high road” (slow, accurate processing through the sensory cortex) and the “low road” (fast, crude processing through the amygdala). We react first and calm down later, feeling slightly foolish — unless the threat is real.

The three key triggers of the stress response are novelty, uncertainty, and uncontrollability. John Mason at Yale discovered that the hypothalamus and adrenals react more powerfully to the expectation of harm than to harm itself. Rats in new cages show elevated corticosterone even with no threat. Hennessey and Levine found that shocks delivered at predictable intervals produced normal cortisol, but unpredictable shocks sent cortisol soaring — we are more scared when uncertain about where the monster lurks than when it finally pops out. During the London Blitz, central London residents (daily, predictable bombing) developed fewer gastric ulcers than those in the outer suburbs (intermittent, unpredictable raids). In “yoked” experiments, two animals receive identical shocks but only one has a lever to stop them — the one without control shows far greater stress. Even the illusion of control reduces cortisol.

Coates’s cortisol experiment on the London trading floor found that cortisol rose substantially with market volatility — so sensitively that traders’ hormone levels displayed a remarkably tight relationship with derivatives prices. On volatile days, some traders’ cortisol increased 500% from morning levels, reaching concentrations normally seen only in clinical patients. Under chronic elevation, cortisol acts on dense receptor fields in the amygdala and hippocampus. Amygdala neurons grow richly branched, making thinking more emotional and less factual. Hippocampus neurons can be killed, shrinking the region by up to 15%. Retrieval becomes biased toward memories stored under stress, and the shell-shocked trader becomes prey to rumour and imaginary patterns. As Paul Fussell documented, WWI troops under extreme stress fell prey to medieval-style rumours — spies in the trenches, angels in the sky. On Wall Street during the crisis, every rumoured catastrophe received as much credence as hard economic data.

The health consequences of prolonged stress are devastating. Blood is shunted from the digestive tract, causing ulcers. The immune system is suppressed, leaving people battling constant colds and recurrent viruses. Growth hormone and testosterone production are crushed. Hypertension develops as chronically raised blood pressure tears arterial walls, attracting plaques that can calcify into atherosclerosis or break off as thrombi, causing heart attacks and strokes. Insulin is inhibited, and unused glucose deposits as abdominal fat. A study found that during 2007–09, there was a spike in heart attacks in London against a decreasing incidence in the rest of the UK, with an estimated 2,000 additional deaths attributable to the credit crisis. Cortisol is the molecule of irrational pessimism, just as testosterone is the molecule of irrational exuberance.

Chapter 8: Toughness

The stress response evolved to save our lives in emergencies, but it can be fatally dysfunctional in modern society. Like a fire brigade that saves our house from flames only to destroy it with water damage, chronic stress is responsible for many of modern medicine’s most intractable problems: hypertension, heart disease, type 2 diabetes, immune disorders, and depression. Can we control the cortisol feedback loop? Only with great difficulty. Joe LeDoux explains that many axons run up from the brain stem and limbic system to the neo-cortex, influencing rational thought, but far fewer run down. We have proportionally less conscious control over primitive brain regions. A rodent placed repeatedly in an open field stops freezing and defecating (its conscious behaviour habituates) but its corticosterone remains stubbornly elevated — the adrenal glands know the danger even when the conscious brain denies it. Traders’ stress questionnaires similarly bore no relation to their actual cortisol levels.

“Anyone who has experienced ungrounded panic attacks, or been in love with the wrong person, knows that efforts to consciously change our feelings are doomed to an endless cycle of repetition and failure.” — John Coates

Despite this, research into toughening holds real promise. Neal Miller at Rockefeller, whose students Bruce McEwen and Jay Weiss discovered cortisol receptors in the brain, found that chronic, unrelenting stress produces illness and learned helplessness, but acute, short-lived stress repeated over and over produces a hardier physiology with increased immunity to further stressors. Mental toughening, it turns out, bears deep similarities to physical toughening. Athletic training works through stress, mild tissue damage, and recovery — a cycle that expands the productive capacity of cells. A similar process of challenge, psychological loading, and recovery can tune our brain and nervous system for resilience. The physiological cocktail of a toughened individual includes a high ratio of anabolic to catabolic hormones (the growth index), strong amine responses that switch on and off quickly, and good vagal tone measured through high heart-rate variability.

Counter-intuitively, toughened people have a stronger initial stress response, not a weaker one. They mount a rapid, powerful amine reaction that masters the situation, permitting cortisol to abate. Untoughened people mount a weak initial arousal but their cortisol lingers, causing catabolic damage. Richard Dienstbier found that toughened individuals enjoy powerful, immediate amine reactions and do not need to draw on the slower, more damaging cortisol response. Coates found the same profile in elite traders: the most experienced and profitable displayed extremely high and volatile steroid hormone levels, both testosterone and cortisol. Behind their poker faces “roared an endocrine system on fire.” Like Olympic athletes, they could call on their hormones when needed and return to baseline quickly. Amateur traders showed the opposite profile — chronically raised cortisol, every stressor viewed as a threat.

Stephen Porges traced the evolution of the vagus nerve through three stages. In reptiles, it orchestrates the freeze response — motionlessness to conserve energy and avoid detection. In mammals, it cooperates with fight-or-flight by releasing its “vagal brake.” In humans, it reaches its most sophisticated form as a tool of social engagement — calm voice, eye contact, cooperative facial expressions that promote diplomacy instead of confrontation. When chronically stressed, the best medicine is not exotic novelty but familiarity — home turf, familiar faces, old films. The vagus nerve needs familiar surroundings to stand down from high alert. Slow, deep diaphragmatic breathing, playing wind instruments, singing, and meditation all engage the vagus. Read Montague found that Buddhist meditation practitioners engaged gut feelings more readily than others, making more rational financial decisions. Increasing vagal tone, Coates concludes, is something of a Holy Grail for stress research.

Beyond individual toughening, objective workplace changes can help. Theorell and Karasek’s pioneering research in Healthy Work found that the combination of high workload and low control produced the highest incidence of stress-related illness, and that a healthy worker is a productive worker with lower medical costs. A Volvo plant in Sweden that devolved control to small worker groups saw stress-related illness drop noticeably. Patient-controlled analgesia — giving patients the ability to administer their own painkillers — reduced the amount of medication used, because removing uncertainty and uncontrollability reduced the pain signal itself. In banks, separating trade conception from execution across different desks reduces stress and improves decision-making. But the biggest stress source is managerial instability — rumours of layoffs, shake-ups, and shifting responsibilities, compounded by middle managers who, like dominant monkeys under stress, take to bullying juniors.

Chapter 9: From Molecule to Market

Financial crises now occur with alarming frequency and severity. The fundamental causes — low real interest rates, financial deregulation, high leverage, the opening of emerging markets, the decline of partnerships and the shift to short-term profits — have created the conditions. But the resulting bull and bear markets have been grossly exaggerated by the biology of risk-takers. Hormones and their cascading molecular signals build up in the bodies of traders and investors during extended bull and bear markets, shifting risk preferences and amplifying the cycle. Under pathologically elevated hormones, the trading community at the peak of a bubble or the pit of a crash may effectively become a clinical population — price and interest-rate insensitive, contributing to what Nassim Taleb calls Black Swan events. This may explain why central banks have met with such limited success in arresting runaway markets in either direction.

The most concrete reform Coates proposes is a restructuring of bonus incentives. Consider two traders: Tortoise makes the bank $10 million a year for five years and receives $1 million annually — the bank nets $45 million. Hare makes $100 million a year for four years, receiving $20 million in bonuses, then loses $500 million in the fifth year — the bank has lost $100 million but paid Hare $80 million. And Hare is not finished: losing big is taken as a sign that you are a “hitter,” rewarded with offers from other banks. The strategic calculation corrodes the entire system: traders, managers, and CEOs all conclude that short-term volatility maximises personal wealth. Risk-management practices and bonus calculations work to amplify the destabilising biology. The solution is a bonus scheme paying over a full business cycle of four to five years, with clawback provisions, combined with mandatory cooling-off periods enforced by risk managers.

“When it comes to allocating the capital of society… we probably do not want volatile behaviour. We want balanced judgement and stable asset prices, and we are more likely to get these if we have the whole village present — men and women, young and old.” — John Coates

Biological diversity on trading floors could serve as a market stabiliser. Women produce 10–20% of male testosterone levels and are less prone to the winner effect. Shelley Taylor found that women’s default stress response is not fight-or-flight but tend-and-befriend. Women are less hormonally reactive to making and losing money. Barber and Odean’s “Boys Will Be Boys” study found that single women outperformed single men by 1.44% over six years across 35,000 brokerage accounts, largely because men overtrade out of overconfidence. Women constitute 60% of risk-takers at big UK asset-management companies, demonstrating that the difference between men and women may not be the level of risk-aversion but the time horizon over which they make decisions. Older men, whose testosterone has declined and cortisol has risen, bring lifetime experience and are less susceptible to hormonal feedback loops. The young male elephants of South Africa provide a vivid analogy: without elder bulls, they enter musth prematurely, their testosterone surging 40 to 50 times above baseline, and they run amok. Park rangers solved the problem by introducing an elder male — his presence calmed the rogues.

Coates closes with Aristotle, who uniquely appreciated the role of the body in thinking and believed that mind is of necessity embodied. Plato’s vision of pure rationality — clean, ordered, crystalline, like the bleached white columns of a Greek temple — has dominated Western thought and, through it, economics. But Aristotle’s realism, grounded in the messy details of embodied existence, is closer to both everyday experience and cutting-edge neuroscience. Aristotle looked at the ways we are actually built, our biological details, and judged policies by how well they suited human nature. Neuroscience and physiology are today rediscovering the unity of brain and body that Aristotle already understood. The inscription on the Temple of Delphi — Know Thyself! — increasingly means know your biochemistry. Doing so, Coates concludes, turns out not to be a dehumanising experience at all. It is a liberating one.

Trading & Investing Lessons

The Winner Effect as Position-Sizing Trap

Winning trades elevate testosterone, which increases confidence and risk appetite, leading to larger positions. This biological feedback loop explains why traders who “let winners run” often end up overexposed. A mechanical position-sizing system that caps exposure regardless of recent performance counteracts the biology.

Cortisol and Missing the Bottom

Chronic cortisol from a bear market makes traders irrationally risk-averse and even price-insensitive. This explains why retail investors consistently sell at the bottom and why central bank rate cuts fail to spark buying during crashes — the trading community is in a clinical state. Systematic rebalancing rules that force buying when fear is highest exploit this biological blind spot.

The Vagal Brake as Risk-Management Tool

Elite traders manage stress not through willpower but through physiological conditioning — high vagal tone, rapid hormone recovery, exercise routines that build amine reserves. Cold exposure, breathing exercises, and adequate sleep are not lifestyle luxuries but performance infrastructure.

Gut Feelings as Genuine Market Intelligence

Somatic markers encode years of pattern recognition in the body. The experienced trader whose stomach tightens at a price pattern is processing real information. Gut feelings should not override analysis but should be integrated with it, particularly in fast-moving markets where conscious processing cannot keep up.

Biological Diversity as Portfolio Defence

A trading desk dominated by young men is biologically correlated — their testosterone cycles amplify each other through the winner effect and social contagion. Diverse teams with different hormonal profiles produce more stable aggregate risk preferences, much as a diversified portfolio reduces idiosyncratic risk.

Bonus Structure Determines Biology

Annual bonuses based on short-term P&L create incentives to maximise volatility, which in turn amplifies the hormonal feedback loops. Longer evaluation periods (full business cycles) align biological incentives with prudent risk-taking, naturally selecting for Tortoises over Hares.

Key Takeaways

Risk-taking is a biological activity

The body does not merely observe financial risk from the sidelines. It activates the same fight-or-flight circuitry evolved for physical danger, producing hormonal cascades that feed back on the brain and alter the very way we think about risk.

Testosterone drives irrational exuberance

The winner effect creates a positive feedback loop: winning trades raise testosterone, which increases confidence and risk appetite, leading to more winning and more testosterone, until overconfidence becomes recklessness and a rally becomes a bubble.

Cortisol drives irrational pessimism

Chronic cortisol from sustained losses biases memory retrieval toward negative events, grows the amygdala while shrinking the hippocampus, and produces anxiety, learned helplessness, and price-insensitive risk-aversion that deepens sell-offs into crashes.

Gut feelings encode real intelligence

Somatic markers store bodily sensations alongside memories, creating a rapid-retrieval system that guides decisions pre-consciously. Expert intuition is reliable in environments with stable regularities and quick feedback — conditions financial markets meet.

Toughness has a measurable physiological profile

High anabolic-to-catabolic hormone ratio, strong amine responses, good vagal tone, and high heart-rate variability characterise the toughened individual. This profile can be trained through intermittent acute stress followed by recovery, just like athletic conditioning.

Management amplifies biological instability

Annual bonuses, expanding risk limits during booms, mass hiring and firing, and managerial bullying all work pro-cyclically, reinforcing the hormonal feedback loops that destabilise markets.

Biological diversity could stabilise markets

Women and older men have different hormonal profiles that are less susceptible to the testosterone feedback loop. A balanced financial community would produce more stable aggregate risk preferences.

Contrarian Ideas

Rational-choice theory is not just incomplete but counterproductive

Conventional view: irrational behaviour can be cured by providing more information or changing prices. Coates’s counter: when traders are in a clinical hormonal state, they are literally insensitive to price signals and additional information. Rate cuts during crashes and rate hikes during bubbles fail because they are treating a biological condition with an informational cure.

Talking therapy cannot fix stress

Conventional view: coaching, counselling, and self-awareness can help people manage stress. Counter: the hypothalamus and adrenal glands respond to objective cues, not encouraging self-talk. Rodents and traders alike show that conscious habituation does not reduce cortisol. Physical training and environmental changes work; talking cures largely do not.

Toughened people have stronger, not weaker, stress responses

The intuition that tough people are calm and unemotional is wrong. They experience rapid, powerful hormonal surges that master the situation and return to baseline quickly. It is the untoughened who mount weak responses that linger and cause damage.

Fatigue is a signal to change tasks, not to rest

Common sense says exhaustion means depleted energy. Neuroscience suggests fatigue is a signal that expected return from the current activity has dropped below metabolic cost. The cure is often a new task, not a vacation — which may explain why overtime does not inherently cause illness, but lack of autonomy over work allocation does.

Women are not more risk-averse, just longer-term

Lab experiments suggest women are more risk-averse, but real-world investment data show they outperform men. The difference may not be risk tolerance but time horizon: women prefer to analyse and hold, while men overtrade out of overconfidence, eroding returns through transaction costs.

Proposals & Frameworks

The Winner Effect Model

Victory raises testosterone, which primes for further victory through increased muscle mass, hemoglobin, confidence, and risk appetite. This positive feedback loop explains winning and losing streaks, but beyond optimal levels it produces overconfidence and recklessness. The model maps directly onto market cycles: testosterone in bull markets inflates bubbles, cortisol in bear markets deepens crashes.

The Toughened Physiology Profile

A toughened individual exhibits: (1) high ratio of anabolic to catabolic hormones (growth index), (2) rapid, powerful amine responses that switch off quickly, (3) moderate cortisol that abates after the challenge, (4) good vagal tone and high heart-rate variability. Achievable through intermittent acute stress (exercise, cold exposure) followed by recovery.

Business-Cycle Bonus Scheme

Pay bonuses over a full business cycle (4–5 years) rather than annually. Profitable traders draw from an accumulating pool; those who blow up forfeit all previous deferred bonuses. Increase the deferred percentage with tenure (5% on one-year returns, 7% on two-year, 10% on three-year). This aligns biological incentives with prudent risk-taking.

Physiological Risk Management

Risk managers should monitor trader physiology (cortisol, testosterone, heart-rate variability) and mandate cooling-off periods for traders on hot streaks, just as sports coaches manage athlete recovery cycles. Mandatory no-contact vacations (Sarbanes-Oxley style) break physiological feedback loops.

Biological Diversity Policy

Hire more women and older men for risk-taking roles. Judge performance over full business cycles rather than quarterly. Different hormonal profiles produce more stable aggregate risk preferences, reducing systemic instability without requiring anyone to be a “better” trader — just different.

What Didn’t Fit

Brown-Séquard’s Testicle Cocktail

The history of testosterone research begins with a 72-year-old neurologist drinking a brew from dog and guinea pig testicles in 1889 and telling a Paris audience it had revitalised him, including a visit to his wife. The effect was largely placebo, but it stamped the field with extravagant expectations that persist to this day. Butenandt’s subsequent isolation of androsterone required 25,000 litres of urine from Berlin police barracks — a detail that captures both the determination and the absurdity of early endocrinology. These stories matter because they explain why testosterone research was long dismissed as pseudoscience, delaying serious investigation of its role in financial risk-taking by decades.

The Enteric Nervous System as Second Brain

The gut contains roughly 100 million neurons and produces the same neurotransmitters as the brain. Ulrich Trendelenburg in 1917 removed an intestinal section from a guinea pig, severed all brain connections, and blew air into it. After a moment’s delay, the intestine contracted and blew the air back — “like some gentle creature playing a simple game.” The gut’s independent intelligence explains why “gut feelings” are not merely metaphorical. Conditions in the brain show up as gut symptoms (stress causes nausea, Alzheimer’s causes constipation) and vice versa (a good meal suffuses the brain with well-being, gut hormones during feeding enhance memory formation).

Seasonal Testosterone and October Crashes

October has produced a disproportionate share of market crashes: 1907, 1929, 1987, 1997, 2007, 2008. In many animals, testosterone rises through summer and drops in autumn, producing what researchers call “irritable male syndrome” — moody, withdrawn, depressive behaviour. Stocks also tend to outperform on sunny days and underperform between the autumn equinox and winter solstice, a pattern linked to Seasonal Affective Disorder. The possibility that seasonal hormonal cycles contribute to market seasonality remains intriguing but unproven.

The Orienting Response and the Drag-Racing Car

Just before a major announcement, the body enters what Porges calls the orienting response — heart and lungs slow almost to a stop. The body is like a drag-racing car at the starting line: fight-or-flight is building (engine revving) while the vagus holds it back (front brakes locked). When the news drops, the brake releases and the body rockets forward far faster than it could accelerate from rest. This mechanism explains why experienced traders appear almost unnaturally calm before major events — their stillness is not passivity but coiled readiness.

Confabulation and the Unreliability of Self-Report

Split-brain patients whose right hemisphere was given instructions to giggle or wave invented plausible but false explanations when their left hemisphere was asked why. Timothy Wilson documented how people constantly trick themselves into thinking they understand their own actions. On the trading floor, Coates found the same pattern: traders’ hormones accurately tracked risk, but their questionnaire responses were as random as split-brain confabulations. This has profound implications for any research or management practice that relies on self-reported stress levels, risk appetite, or market outlook.

About the Author

John Coates spent over a decade on Wall Street, trading derivatives at Goldman Sachs and Merrill Lynch before running a trading desk at Deutsche Bank. He had a front-row seat to the dot-com bubble and its aftermath, an experience that convinced him the biology of risk-taking was poorly understood and dangerously ignored by economics. He retired from finance, returned to the University of Cambridge where he had completed his PhD in economics, and spent four years retraining in neuroscience and endocrinology under researchers including Bruce McEwen at Rockefeller University.

At Cambridge, Coates designed and ran pioneering experiments on London trading floors, sampling traders’ testosterone and cortisol levels and correlating them with trading performance. His research provided the first empirical evidence for the winner effect in financial markets and demonstrated that market volatility has a direct physiological impact on the people who make pricing decisions. He is a Senior Research Fellow in Neuroscience and Finance at the University of Cambridge and has published in journals including the Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences.

Coates brings a rare combination of practical trading experience and rigorous scientific training. He has lived the hormonal cycles he describes — the testosterone-fuelled confidence of a winning streak, the cortisol-soaked anxiety of a losing one — and then left the industry to study them with the tools of modern biology.

Why This Book Matters

Most accounts of financial crises focus on institutional failures, regulatory gaps, or psychological biases. Coates adds a dimension that none of these capture: the physiology of the people making the decisions. By showing that hormones systematically shift risk preferences across the business cycle, he explains not just why bubbles and crashes happen but why they are so resistant to conventional policy interventions. Rate cuts fail during crashes because the trading community is in a clinical hormonal state, literally insensitive to price changes. Rate hikes fail during bubbles because testosterone-elevated traders are convinced of their own infallibility. This is not a metaphor — it is measurable biology, documented in peer-reviewed research on actual trading floors.

The book matters because its policy implications are concrete and actionable. Business-cycle bonus schemes, physiological monitoring, mandatory recovery periods, biological diversity in hiring — these are practical reforms that work with human biology rather than against it. In a world where financial crises have become more frequent and severe, understanding the biology of boom and bust is not an academic luxury. It is a prerequisite for building a more stable financial system.

More broadly, the book challenges the deepest assumption of Western economics: that human beings are rational agents whose decisions can be improved simply by providing better information. Coates demonstrates that we are embodied creatures whose thinking is inextricable from our physiology. The ancient inscription at Delphi — Know Thyself — has always been good advice. Today it means knowing your biochemistry, and that knowledge, far from being dehumanising, is the foundation for wiser decisions in markets and in life.
