The Man Who Built the Dashboard

J. Welles Wilder Jr. — mechanical engineer, real estate developer, commodities trader, and the man who gave us the tools that still run every trading screen on earth.

Before there was a Relative Strength Index, before there was an ATR or a Parabolic SAR or an ADX, there was a mechanical engineer from North Carolina who could not find a single technical indicator that satisfied him. J. Welles Wilder Jr. was born on June 11, 1935, graduated from North Carolina State University with an engineering degree in 1962, and had served in the U.S. Navy during the Korean War before that. He was methodical, quantitative, and frustrated. The existing tools for analyzing commodity futures in the 1970s were primitive. Chart patterns were subjective. Moving averages lagged badly. There was no standardized way to measure momentum, volatility, or trend strength. Wilder decided to build his own.

His path to trading was not direct. After engineering he went into real estate development, made money, got interested in commodity futures, lost money, and then applied his engineering brain to the problem of why. He founded Trend Research Ltd. and its subsidiary The Delta Society as vehicles for his research. He worked alone, with a calculator and reams of price data, in an era before personal computers. Every formula he developed had to be hand-calculated, which meant every formula had to be elegant enough to compute by hand. This constraint shaped everything — the smoothing method he chose, the 14-period default, the simplicity of the RSI formula itself. Complexity was not an option when you were doing the math with pencil and paper.

In 1978 he self-published a slim book called "New Concepts in Technical Trading Systems." It was not flashy. It had no marketing campaign. It was a technical manual written by an engineer for other systematic thinkers. And it quietly became one of the most influential books in the history of financial markets. Forbes magazine called Wilder "the premier technical trader publishing his work today" in October 1980. The book has never been updated. It has never gone out of print.

What made the book extraordinary was not any single indicator but the sheer density of innovation it contained. In one volume, Wilder introduced six new technical indicators, any one of which would have been a career-defining contribution: the Relative Strength Index (RSI), the Average True Range (ATR), the Parabolic SAR, the Directional Movement Index (DMI), the Average Directional Index (ADX), and the Commodity Selection Index (CSI). Nearly fifty years later, RSI, ATR, and ADX remain among the five most widely used indicators in the world. Every trading platform on earth includes them as defaults.

Why "Relative Strength"?
The name is a source of endless confusion. In traditional securities analysis, "relative strength" means comparing one stock's performance against another stock or an index — an external comparison. Wilder's RSI measures something completely different: a security's price strength relative to its own recent history. It is internally referenced. Wilder acknowledged the naming could confuse people but kept it anyway. When you see "RS" in the RSI formula, it means "Relative Strength" as Wilder defined it: the ratio of average gains to average losses over the lookback period.

Wilder eventually moved to New Zealand and later Christchurch, continuing to trade and refine his ideas but largely stepping away from the public eye. He became interested in the Delta Phenomenon, a theory about hidden order in market cycles based on lunar and solar rhythms, which he published through The Delta Society. It was more esoteric than his early engineering-driven work, and it never achieved the same mainstream adoption. But his 1978 contribution was already locked in. By the time personal computers arrived and charting software became standard, Wilder's indicators were the first ones programmed into every platform. They became the default not because of marketing or institutional backing, but because they worked, and because they were simple enough to compute efficiently even on early hardware.

The man died in 2021 at age 85, having outlived most of the traders who first used his tools. His obituary was small. Most retail traders today who use RSI daily have never heard his name. But every time someone opens a chart and toggles on that oscillator panel at the bottom — the one that bounces between 0 and 100 with lines at 30 and 70 — they are using something a mechanical engineer from North Carolina built by hand, with a calculator, in the 1970s.

How RSI Actually Works

The formula, the smoothing, why he chose 14, and the mathematical details most tutorials skip.

The RSI formula looks simple on the surface, but there are subtleties in how the averaging works that most tutorials either skip or get wrong. Understanding these details matters because different software implementations can produce slightly different RSI values for the same data — and once you understand why, you will also understand why several RSI variants exist.

Step 1: Calculate Price Changes

For each bar (day, hour, whatever your timeframe), calculate the change from the prior close. If the change is positive, it is a gain. If negative, take the absolute value and call it a loss. If flat, both gain and loss are zero for that bar. You now have two parallel series: a gains series and a losses series, each the same length as your price data minus one bar.

Step 2: Seed the Averages

For the very first RSI value, Wilder uses a plain arithmetic average to create the initial seed:

First Average (seed)
First Avg Gain = Sum of Gains over 14 bars / 14
First Avg Loss = Sum of Losses over 14 bars / 14

This simple average is calculated exactly once. Every subsequent bar uses a different method.

Step 3: Wilder's Smoothed Moving Average

This is where most people get confused. After the seed, Wilder does not use a simple moving average. He does not use a standard EMA either. He uses what is now called Wilder's Smoothed Moving Average (also called Modified Moving Average, SMMA, or RMA on different platforms):

Wilder's Smoothed Average
Avg Gain = ((Prior Avg Gain x 13) + Current Gain) / 14
Avg Loss = ((Prior Avg Loss x 13) + Current Loss) / 14

Generalized for any period n:

General form
Avg = ((Prior Avg x (n-1)) + Current Value) / n
Why this matters
Wilder's SMMA is mathematically equivalent to an Exponential Moving Average (EMA) with a smoothing factor of alpha = 1/n. A standard EMA uses alpha = 2/(n+1). For n=14, Wilder's alpha is 0.0714 versus a standard EMA's 0.1333. This means Wilder's smoothing decays more slowly — it gives more weight to older data, producing a smoother, less reactive oscillator. A "14-period Wilder RSI" behaves more like a 27-period standard EMA in terms of responsiveness.

Step 4: Relative Strength

RS
RS = Average Gain / Average Loss

RS is just a ratio. When gains dominate, RS is large. When losses dominate, RS is small. When gains and losses are equal, RS = 1.

Step 5: The RSI Formula

RSI
RSI = 100 - (100 / (1 + RS))

This formula maps the RS ratio onto a bounded 0 to 100 scale. When all bars are gains (RS approaches infinity), RSI approaches 100. When all bars are losses (RS = 0), RSI = 0. When gains equal losses (RS = 1), RSI = 50. The formula is an elegant normalization that makes RSI values comparable across any security, timeframe, or market.

Why 14 Periods?

Wilder chose 14 based on the half lunar cycle — a 28-day lunar month divided by two gives 14 trading days. He believed commodity markets exhibited natural rhythms tied to lunar cycles, and a half-cycle lookback would capture one complete swing. Whether you believe in lunar market cycles or not, the choice stuck. Every charting platform in the world still defaults RSI to 14. It has become a self-fulfilling standard — because millions of traders watch RSI(14), the levels it identifies become significant through collective attention even if the original astronomical reasoning was arbitrary.

The Data Length Dependency Problem

Here is something most RSI tutorials never mention: because Wilder's SMMA never truly forgets old data (it decays exponentially but never reaches zero), the starting point in a dataset affects every subsequent RSI value. If two charting platforms start calculating from different historical dates, they will produce slightly different RSI readings for the same security on the same day. This is called data length dependency.

In practice, the differences are usually small after a few hundred bars because the exponential decay makes very old data negligible. But the issue is real, and it is one of the reasons Peter Cutler developed his variant (covered in Chapter 6) that uses a simple moving average instead, eliminating the dependency entirely.

RSI maps the gain/loss ratio to a bounded 0-100 scale. The relationship is not linear — extreme readings require increasingly lopsided ratios.

Classic RSI Signals

The four core techniques Wilder published, plus RSI trendlines — arguably the most underused trick in technical analysis.

Overbought and Oversold

The most basic RSI signal: when RSI rises above 70, the security is considered overbought. When it drops below 30, oversold. These thresholds have been the default on every charting platform since 1978 and they are the first thing every new trader learns about RSI. They are also the most frequently misused.

The trap is simple: in a strong uptrend, RSI can remain above 70 for weeks or months. Selling just because RSI hit 70 in a bull market will have you shorting into a freight train. Similarly, in a bear market, RSI can camp below 30 for extended periods. Buying every touch of 30 in a downtrend is a recipe for catching falling knives. Wilder himself acknowledged this limitation — he suggested using longer periods (like 21 or 25) to reduce false signals in strong trends, and he considered overbought/oversold to be his least reliable RSI technique. His favorite was the failure swing, covered below.

The #1 RSI Mistake
Treating overbought as "sell" and oversold as "buy" without any trend context. Overbought in an uptrend often means "strongly trending up" not "about to reverse." Andrew Cardwell's range shift rules (Chapter 4) solve this problem entirely.

Divergence

Divergence occurs when price and RSI disagree about the direction of momentum. It comes in two forms:

Regular (Classic) Divergence

Bullish divergence: price makes a lower low, but RSI makes a higher low. The new price low is not confirmed by new momentum low — the selling pressure is weakening even as price drops further. This can signal that the downtrend is running out of steam.

Bearish divergence: price makes a higher high, but RSI makes a lower high. The new price high is not confirmed by new momentum high — buying pressure is weakening even as price pushes higher. This can signal the uptrend is exhausting itself.

Wilder included divergence in his original book but considered it secondary to failure swings. The problem with divergence in practice is that it can persist for many bars without producing a reversal. In strong trends, you can see three, four, five bearish divergences in a row, each followed by higher prices. Divergence tells you momentum is decelerating; it does not tell you momentum has reversed. There is a critical difference.

Hidden Divergence

Hidden divergence signals trend continuation, not reversal. It is the mirror image of regular divergence:

Bullish hidden divergence: price makes a higher low (uptrend pullback), but RSI makes a lower low. The price structure says uptrend intact (higher low). RSI is temporarily weaker, but the trend structure is dominant. Signal: expect continuation higher.

Bearish hidden divergence: price makes a lower high (downtrend rally), but RSI makes a higher high. Price structure says downtrend intact (lower high). RSI is temporarily stronger, but the trend structure is dominant. Signal: expect continuation lower.

Memory Rule
Regular divergence = Reversal signal. Price and RSI disagree, and you expect price to follow RSI's direction.

Hidden divergence = Continuation signal. Price and RSI disagree, and you expect the existing trend to continue.

Failure Swings

This was Wilder's own favorite RSI signal — the one he considered most reliable. A failure swing is formed entirely within the RSI panel, independent of what price is doing. It signals that momentum has attempted to return to an extreme and failed, which Wilder viewed as a stronger sign of exhaustion than divergence.

Bullish Failure Swing (Bottom)

  1. RSI drops below 30 (enters oversold territory)
  2. RSI bounces back above 30 (exits oversold)
  3. RSI pulls back but holds above 30 — it cannot re-enter oversold territory
  4. RSI breaks above the prior bounce high — buy signal

The logic: momentum dropped into oversold, tried to get back there, and failed. It could not muster enough selling pressure to return to the extreme. The failure to re-enter oversold is the signal that sellers are exhausted.

Bearish Failure Swing (Top)

  1. RSI rises above 70 (enters overbought territory)
  2. RSI pulls back below 70 (exits overbought)
  3. RSI bounces but stays below 70 — it cannot re-enter overbought territory
  4. RSI breaks below the prior pullback low — sell signal
The failure swing forms entirely in the RSI panel. Momentum tries to revisit oversold and fails — sellers are exhausted.

RSI Trendlines

This is arguably the most underused technique in all of RSI analysis. The idea is simple: draw trendlines directly on the RSI panel, just like you would on a price chart. Connect swing lows for support, swing highs for resistance.

The reason this works so well is that RSI trendline breaks frequently precede price trendline breaks. Momentum shifts before price does. If you are watching only price trendlines, you are getting the signal late. The RSI trendline gives you advance warning that the trend is about to break, often by several bars. Many traders who have backtested this technique report that RSI trendline breaks are among their most reliable leading signals.

The technique is particularly powerful on higher timeframes (daily and weekly charts). On intraday charts the noise level can produce too many false trendline breaks. But on daily charts, a clean RSI trendline that has held for multiple touches breaking cleanly is a strong signal that deserves attention regardless of whether the price trendline has broken yet.

Andrew Cardwell's Revolution

The man known as "Dr. RSI" who trained under Wilder, spent decades extending the theory, and fixed the biggest problem with overbought/oversold.

Andrew Cardwell is the most important figure in RSI analysis after Wilder himself. He trained directly under Wilder, then spent the next several decades studying RSI behavior across thousands of charts in every market and timeframe. His two major contributions — range shift rules and positive/negative reversals — fundamentally changed how sophisticated traders use RSI, and they solved the single biggest problem with Wilder's original framework.

The Range Shift Discovery

Wilder's original framework treats 30 and 70 as fixed thresholds that apply in all conditions. Cardwell discovered that RSI does not oscillate symmetrically between 0 and 100. Instead, its operating range shifts depending on the prevailing trend:

Bull Market RSI Range
RSI oscillates between 40 and 80.

Pullbacks to 40-50 are buying opportunities. RSI touching 40 in an uptrend is the equivalent of "oversold" — not 30.

RSI rarely drops below 40 unless the trend is reversing.
Bear Market RSI Range
RSI oscillates between 20 and 60.

Rallies to 50-60 are shorting opportunities. RSI touching 60 in a downtrend is the equivalent of "overbought" — not 70.

RSI rarely rises above 60 unless the trend is reversing.

This is the range shift. When RSI breaks out of one range and establishes itself in another, it signals a trend change. A market that was oscillating in the 20–60 bear range that now pushes above 60 and holds there has shifted into the bull range. The trend has changed. Conversely, a market oscillating in the 40–80 bull range that breaks below 40 and stays there has shifted into the bear range.

This single insight eliminates the biggest problem with traditional RSI usage. You no longer sell because RSI hit 70 in a bull market — you recognize that 70 is within the normal bull range and wait for the range to actually shift before turning bearish. You no longer buy because RSI hit 30 in a bear market — you recognize that 30 is within the normal bear range and wait for the range to shift before turning bullish.

Positive and Negative Reversals

This is where Cardwell really challenged conventional wisdom. He discovered a class of RSI signals that look like divergence but are actually trend continuation signals, not reversal signals. He called them reversals because they reverse the traditional interpretation of divergence.

Positive Reversal

Price makes a lower low, but RSI makes a higher low. This looks identical to classic bullish divergence. But Cardwell's key insight: if this pattern occurs within an uptrend (RSI operating in the 40–80 bull range), it is not a reversal signal. It is a continuation signal that predicts price will go higher. The trend is intact; the dip is a buying opportunity.

Negative Reversal

Price makes a higher high, but RSI makes a lower high. This looks identical to classic bearish divergence. But if it occurs within a downtrend (RSI operating in the 20–60 bear range), it is a continuation signal that predicts price will go lower. The trend is intact; the rally is a shorting opportunity.

The Cardwell Rule
Whether a divergence pattern is a reversal or a continuation signal depends entirely on the prevailing trend context. Same pattern, opposite interpretation, depending on which range RSI is operating in. This is why Cardwell considers most "bearish divergence" signals in uptrends to be false alarms — they are actually positive reversals predicting continuation.

Cardwell's framework reframes RSI from a simple overbought/oversold toggle into a trend identification and confirmation tool. The range tells you the trend. The reversals tell you the trend is continuing. The actual range shift tells you the trend is changing. It is a complete system, and it makes the classic "sell at 70, buy at 30" approach look crude by comparison.

The Stochastic Oscillator

George Lane, the Chicago trading floors, and a fundamentally different question about price.

The Stochastic Oscillator predates RSI by over twenty years. Its origin is not a book or an academic paper but a back room behind the Chicago Board of Trade, where a group of commodities traders spent their evenings after the market close trying to build better analytical tools. The man who led that effort was George C. Lane.

The Origin Story

Lane joined Investment Educators in Chicago in 1954 as an assistant. Investment Educators was a commodities trading education firm founded by Ralph Dystant and Roy Larson. Lane started by handling logistics for their seminars — setting up chairs, distributing handouts. He was not a trader yet. But he was sharp, and he absorbed everything.

After Larson retired, Lane moved up to teaching courses himself. Meanwhile, a group of active traders from the Chicago Board of Trade, the Chicago Mercantile Exchange, and the MidAmerica Commodity Exchange would come to Investment Educators after trading hours to collaborate on indicator development. It was a workshop atmosphere — floor traders contributing market intuition, Lane contributing the mathematical frameworks to test their ideas.

They worked through a series of oscillator concepts, expressing each as a percentage of 100. As they tested each candidate, they named them sequentially: %A, %B, %C, %D, %K, %R, and so on. The letters were not abbreviations for anything — they were just sequence labels, the way an engineer might name prototypes. The ones that survived the testing process kept their letters. That is why the stochastic oscillator has %K and %D as its two lines — those were the designations from the original development sequence. %K was the raw oscillator that worked. %D was the smoothed signal line that confirmed it.

Lane first taught the stochastic in Investment Educators seminars beginning in 1957. He never published it in a single definitive academic paper. It spread through seminars, word of mouth, and the trading floor community. By the time RSI appeared in 1978, the stochastic had already been in active use for two decades. Lane continued teaching and refining it until his death in 2004.

The Core Philosophy: Where Does Price Close?

The stochastic asks a fundamentally different question than RSI. RSI asks: how much is price gaining versus losing on average? The stochastic asks: where did today's price close within the recent range?

Lane's observation was based on studying closing prices in relation to daily ranges. In uptrends, closes tend to accumulate near the top of the range. In downtrends, closes accumulate near the bottom of the range. As a trend ages and momentum fades, closes begin to drift away from the extreme of the range. The stochastic captures this drift. When prices have been closing near the top of their range and then start closing in the middle or bottom, the stochastic turns down — signaling that buyers are losing their grip before the price trend itself reverses.

RSI vs. Stochastic: Two Questions
RSI: "Are recent gains bigger than recent losses?" (momentum)

Stochastic: "Where is price closing within its recent range?" (position)

Both are momentum oscillators, but they measure different aspects of momentum. This is why they sometimes diverge from each other — and why using both can give you more information than either alone.

The Formula

%K (Fast Stochastic)

%K
%K = ((Close - Lowest Low over n periods) / (Highest High over n - Lowest Low over n)) x 100

Default n = 14 periods. %K = 100 means today's close is at the very top of the 14-period range. %K = 0 means today's close is at the very bottom. %K = 50 means the close is exactly in the middle of the range.

%D (Signal Line)

%D
%D = 3-period SMA of %K

%D is simply a smoothed version of %K. It creates a slower, smoother line that acts as a trigger. Crossovers of %K through %D generate buy and sell signals, similar to MACD signal line crossovers.

Fast, Slow, and Full Stochastic

This is one of the most confusing aspects of the stochastic, and most tutorials make it worse. Here is the clean version:

Version %K %D Character
Fast Raw %K (no smoothing) 3-period SMA of raw %K Very reactive, noisy, original form
Slow Fast %D (the 3-period SMA) 3-period SMA of Slow %K One extra smoothing step, most commonly used
Full Fully parameterized (period, slowing factor) Parameterized SMA of %K Complete control; slowing=1 equals Fast, slowing=3 equals Slow

When most traders say "stochastics" they mean the Slow Stochastic. The Fast Stochastic is rarely used in practice because it whipsaws too aggressively. The Full Stochastic is what you get on most charting platforms when you add the indicator — it defaults to settings that reproduce the Slow Stochastic but lets you customize all three parameters.

Stochastic Signals

Overbought/Oversold: Above 80 is overbought, below 20 is oversold. Same caveats as RSI — in strong trends, stochastics can camp in overbought or oversold for extended periods.

%K/%D Crossovers: When %K crosses above %D in oversold territory (below 20), that is a buy signal. When %K crosses below %D in overbought territory (above 80), that is a sell signal. Crossovers in the middle zone (20–80) are generally ignored as noise.

Divergence: Lane himself stressed that divergence was the highest-quality stochastic signal — more reliable than crossovers alone. The same regular and hidden divergence concepts from RSI apply here.

Lane's personal preference: he emphasized watching %D, not %K. He said the signal was in the turns of %D, and that %K was useful primarily as a timing trigger for entries after %D had confirmed the direction.

Every RSI Variant Worth Knowing

From Cutler's simple fix to Ehlers' signal processing wizardry — the complete family tree of RSI descendants.

Cutler's RSI

Peter Cutler developed this variant to solve one specific problem: the data length dependency in Wilder's RSI (discussed in Chapter 2). The fix is straightforward — replace Wilder's SMMA with a simple moving average (SMA) for both the average gain and average loss calculations.

Cutler's RSI
Avg Gain = Sum of Gains over n periods / n (fresh SMA each bar)
Avg Loss = Sum of Losses over n periods / n (fresh SMA each bar)

The consequences are subtle but real. Cutler's RSI produces identical values regardless of where in the historical data you start calculating, because the SMA has a fixed window — old data drops off cleanly. The tradeoff: by giving equal weight to all bars in the window rather than emphasizing recent bars, Cutler's version is slightly more reactive and crosses overbought/oversold levels more frequently than Wilder's. In practice the two track closely, but they will diverge in fast-moving markets where the weighting difference matters.

RSI(2) — Connors Mean Reversion

This is not a new formula — it is Wilder's exact RSI with the period set to 2 instead of 14. Larry Connors and Cesar Alvarez popularized it in their 2008 book "Short-Term Trading Strategies That Work" as the core of a mean-reversion system for equities.

With only 2 periods, RSI becomes hyper-reactive. It swings violently between extremes. This is the point. The strategy is specifically designed for short-term bounce trades in stocks that are trending up on a longer timeframe but have pulled back sharply:

  1. Confirm the stock is above its 200-day SMA (long-term uptrend)
  2. Buy when RSI(2) drops below 10 (deeply oversold short-term)
  3. Exit when price crosses above the 5-day SMA (dynamic exit, not a fixed target)
  4. No hard stop-loss in Connors' original research (controversial)

Connors' backtesting showed that the more extreme the RSI(2) reading, the stronger the subsequent bounce. Buying below 5 outperformed buying below 10. Buying below 2 outperformed buying below 5. The strategy backtested well on the S&P 500 through the 1990s and 2000s, but it is a pure mean-reversion play — it fails catastrophically in crash environments where "oversold" can stay oversold for months.

Connors RSI (CRSI)

Larry Connors later developed a composite oscillator that averages three separate momentum components into a single value. It was formalized in his research reports and the book "Short-Term Trading Strategies That Work."

The Three Components

Component 1 — RSI(3): Standard Wilder RSI with a 3-period lookback. Extremely fast and reactive to short-term price swings.

Component 2 — Streak RSI: First, count the consecutive up-closes or down-closes (the "streak"). Three up days in a row = +3. Four down days = −4. Then apply RSI(2) to the time series of these streak counts. This captures the momentum of the streak itself.

Component 3 — ROC Percent Rank: Calculate today's rate of change (one-day percentage price change) and rank it as a percentile against the last 100 periods. If today's move is larger than 85 of the last 100 moves, the rank is 85. This tells you whether today's move is historically large or small.

Connors RSI
CRSI(3,2,100) = [RSI(3) + RSI(Streak,2) + ROC Percentile(100)] / 3

The composite moves faster than standard RSI, which is why the thresholds are wider: below 10 = oversold, above 90 = overbought (versus 30/70 for classic RSI). CRSI is specifically designed for short-term mean reversion in equities, not trend following.

Stochastic RSI (StochRSI)

Developed by Tushar S. Chande and Stanley Kroll in their 1994 book "The New Technical Trader." The motivation was a real problem: in strong trends, standard RSI can spend weeks between 40 and 70 without touching the overbought or oversold zones, making those zones useless as signals.

Their solution was elegant: apply the stochastic formula to RSI values instead of price values. Literally a stochastic of the RSI.

Stochastic RSI
StochRSI = (RSI - Lowest RSI over m periods) / (Highest RSI over m - Lowest RSI over m)

The result ranges from 0 to 1 (or 0 to 100). Common defaults: 14-period RSI, 14-period lookback for the stochastic calculation, with a 3-period SMA for smoothing. Overbought above 0.80, oversold below 0.20.

StochRSI is significantly more sensitive than RSI. It reaches extreme readings more often, turns faster, and generates more signals. The cost is more false signals and more noise. It has become particularly popular in crypto trading precisely because crypto markets move faster and more violently than equities, and the extra sensitivity matches the asset class's character.

Laguerre RSI

John F. Ehlers is a former aerospace engineer who spent decades applying digital signal processing (DSP) theory to market indicators. He published the Laguerre RSI in his 2004 book "Cybernetic Analysis for Stocks and Futures." It represents the most mathematically sophisticated RSI variant in common use.

The core idea: the Laguerre filter is a mathematical construct from DSP that creates a multi-stage smoothing cascade. Ehlers applies it to price data to extract four progressively smoothed layers, then applies RSI logic across those layers.

Laguerre Filter (4 stages)
L0 = (1 - g) x Price + g x L0[prior]
L1 = -g x L0 + L0[prior] + g x L1[prior]
L2 = -g x L1 + L1[prior] + g x L2[prior]
L3 = -g x L2 + L2[prior] + g x L3[prior]

Where g (gamma) = damping factor (0.2 to 0.8)

Lower gamma (0.2) means more reactive, faster response, more noise. Higher gamma (0.8) means smoother, slower response, less noise. After computing the four filter stages, RSI logic is applied by summing the positive differences between consecutive layers (CU = "count up") and the negative differences (CD = "count down"):

Laguerre RSI output
CU = sum of (Ln - Ln+1) where Ln > Ln+1
CD = sum of (Ln+1 - Ln) where Ln < Ln+1
LaRSI = CU / (CU + CD)

The key property that makes Laguerre RSI special: its value distribution is approximately U-shaped, spending more time at the extremes (near 0 and 1) rather than clustering near 0.5 like standard RSI tends to. This makes it naturally better at identifying overbought and oversold conditions because those zones are where it spends the most time. The four-stage cascade also gives it unusually low lag relative to its smoothness — the holy grail tradeoff in signal processing.

RSI3M3 (Bressert)

Walter Bressert was a cycle trader who published extensively on market cycle timing from the 1970s through the 1990s. He ran the HAL Commodity Cycles newsletter from 1974 to 1985 (profitable 10 of 12 years, rated #1 in bull markets by Futures magazine) and later published CycleWatch. His primary oscillator was deceptively simple:

RSI3M3
RSI3M3 = SMA(3) of RSI(3)

A 3-period RSI smoothed by a 3-period simple moving average. That is it. Despite its simplicity, Bressert reported an average 85% accuracy for identifying 10-day cycle bottoms on daily charts. The double-3 configuration is fast enough to catch short-cycle turns (5–10 day cycles) while smooth enough to reduce the extreme whipsaws of a raw RSI(3).

Bressert used it with a mechanical setup/trigger entry system: RSI3M3 drops below 30 (setup), turns up (setup bar identified), then a buy stop is placed one tick above the high of the setup bar (trigger). The protective stop goes below the cycle low. He combined this with cycle timing bands — historical windows where 70% of cycle tops/bottoms had occurred. When the RSI3M3 signal fires within a timing band, the probability of a successful trade increases significantly.

Ehlers Smoothed RSI

A separate contribution from John Ehlers, published in Technical Analysis of Stocks & Commodities magazine. He applies a SuperSmoother filter — a two-pole Butterworth filter adapted for market data — to the RSI output. The SuperSmoother is a low-pass filter: it passes trends and medium-frequency cycles while attenuating high-frequency noise. Applied to RSI, the result is noticeably smoother with fewer false crossings of the overbought/oversold levels, at the cost of slightly more lag than raw RSI.

Ehlers Adaptive RSI

Ehlers' most ambitious RSI variant. It uses his Autocorrelation Periodogram algorithm to dynamically measure the dominant cycle length in the current price data, then feeds that cycle length as the RSI period. When the market's dominant cycle is long, the RSI period lengthens automatically. When cycles shorten, the period contracts.

The result: the RSI is always tuned to the current market rhythm rather than locked to an arbitrary fixed period. This is the DSP philosophy taken to its logical conclusion — let the market tell you its own periodicity rather than imposing a static number. The tradeoff is computational complexity and the challenge of the periodogram producing unstable cycle estimates in choppy, non-cyclical markets.

RSX (Jurik RSI)

Developed by Mark Jurik of Jurik Research. RSX replaces Wilder's SMMA with Jurik's proprietary JMA (Jurik Moving Average) smoothing, which is a multi-stage adaptive filter with near-zero phase lag. The result looks like a smoother version of RSI with significantly less lag. The catch: the JMA algorithm is closed-source and proprietary. You cannot see the math. You have to trust Jurik's implementation or license it. RSX is available in some commercial indicator packages and has a following among traders willing to pay for reduced lag, but the black-box nature keeps many systematic traders away.

DSS Bressert

The Double Smoothed Stochastic — William Blau's mathematical innovation, Walter Bressert's practical refinement, and why double smoothing solves a real problem with regular stochastics.

The Problem With Standard Stochastics

The standard stochastic oscillator, whether fast, slow, or full, uses simple moving averages for smoothing. SMA-based smoothing has a specific mathematical weakness: it gives equal weight to every bar in the window, and when the oldest bar drops off the window, it creates an abrupt step-change in the average. If the bar dropping off happened to be a large gain or large loss, the SMA jumps discretely even if recent price action has been smooth. These discontinuities show up as small jags and false signals in the stochastic output — noise that has nothing to do with actual price momentum and everything to do with old data exiting the calculation window.

William Blau's Innovation

William Blau published the foundational concept of double smoothing in Technical Analysis of Stocks & Commodities magazine in January 1991, and expanded it in his 1995 book "Momentum, Direction, and Divergence." Blau was a mathematician and electrical engineer whose insight was both simple and powerful: apply EMA smoothing twice to the stochastic formula — once to the raw components, then again to the result.

The EMA does not have the "oldest bar drops off" problem because it decays exponentially rather than using a fixed window. Every past data point contributes some weight, but recent data dominates. Applying this twice creates a double cascade of smoothing that eliminates most of the artificial jags while preserving the sensitivity to genuine momentum shifts.

The DSS Formula

The standard stochastic ratio is:

Raw stochastic ratio
Raw = (Close - Lowest Low(n)) / (Highest High(n) - Lowest Low(n)) x 100

The DSS applies EMA smoothing in two passes:

First Pass: EMA of the Raw Ratio

First smoothing (MIT)
MIT = EMA(Raw Stochastic Ratio, period)

This produces a smoothed intermediate value called MIT (Moving Intermediate Term). It has already eliminated most of the SMA step-change artifacts.

Second Pass: Stochastic of the Smoothed Value

Second smoothing (DSS)
DSS = EMA( (MIT - Lowest MIT(n)) / (Highest MIT(n) - Lowest MIT(n)) x 100, period )

The second pass applies the stochastic formula again — but this time to the already-smoothed MIT values, and then smooths that with another EMA. The result is an oscillator that is doubly smoothed, doubly normalized, and significantly cleaner than a standard stochastic with equivalent parameter settings.

Bressert's Calibration

Walter Bressert took Blau's mathematical framework and calibrated it for his own cycle trading methodology. The Bressert version uses shorter default periods than Blau — typically period = 8 or 13 with EMA smoothing period = 5 — producing a more sensitive oscillator tuned for short trading cycles of 5 to 13 bars. Common thresholds: overbought above 80, oversold below 20.

Bressert integrated DSS into his Cycle Trading Pattern Manual as a primary oscillator for timing entries at cycle tops and bottoms. Combined with his timing bands and trend indicators (EMA Trend, MACD Trend), the DSS provided the price confirmation component while the cycles provided the time component.

Why Double Smoothing Matters

Standard Stochastic
SMA smoothing creates step-changes when old data drops off the window. Equal weighting means recent and old data contribute equally. More false crossings of overbought/oversold. Slower to reach extremes with heavy smoothing.
DSS Bressert
EMA smoothing decays exponentially — no step-changes. Recent data dominates. Fewer false crossings. Faster to reach overbought/oversold extremes despite being smoother. Better suited for short cycle identification.

The DSS is one of those rare indicators where the mathematical improvement translates directly into practical trading improvement. The smoother curve produces cleaner crossover signals, the faster response to extremes gives earlier entries, and the elimination of SMA artifacts reduces the number of times you get whipsawed on noise. If you use standard stochastics and find yourself getting faked out by small jags that reverse immediately, the DSS addresses exactly that problem.

Wilder's Complete Toolkit

RSI was not alone in that 1978 book. The other five indicators Wilder published alongside it are still among the most widely used tools in technical analysis.

To fully appreciate what Wilder accomplished, you need to understand that he did not just invent one indicator. He published six in one book, and three of them — RSI, ATR, and ADX — became permanent fixtures on every trading platform on earth. Here are the other five, briefly:

Average True Range (ATR)

The Average True Range measures market volatility. The "true range" of a bar is the greatest of three values: the high minus the low, the absolute value of the high minus the prior close, or the absolute value of the low minus the prior close. The last two account for overnight gaps, which the simple high-minus-low range would miss. ATR is the smoothed average of true range values over n periods (default 14, using Wilder's SMMA).

ATR does not tell you direction. It tells you how much a security is moving. High ATR means high volatility. Low ATR means low volatility. It is foundational for stop-loss placement (many traders set stops at 1.5x or 2x ATR from entry), position sizing (adjusting share count inversely to ATR so that each position carries roughly equal dollar risk), and it is the building block for dozens of later indicators including Keltner Channels, SuperTrend, and Chandelier Exits.

Parabolic SAR (Stop and Reverse)

A trailing stop system that gets tighter as a trend extends. The SAR (Stop And Reverse) is a dot that appears below price in uptrends and above price in downtrends. It uses an Acceleration Factor (AF) that starts at 0.02 and increases by 0.02 each time price makes a new extreme in the trend direction, capping at 0.20.

When price hits the SAR dot, the system reverses — if you were long, you go short; if short, you go long. It is an always-in-the-market system, which is both its strength (you never miss a major trend) and its weakness (you get whipsawed badly in ranging markets). Modern traders often use Parabolic SAR as a trailing stop mechanism rather than a reversal system, keeping the exit logic but not automatically reversing.

Directional Movement Index (DMI) and Average Directional Index (ADX)

The DMI consists of two lines: +DI (positive directional indicator, measuring upward price movement) and −DI (measuring downward price movement). When +DI is above −DI, the trend is up. When −DI is above +DI, the trend is down. Crossovers of the two lines generate directional signals.

The ADX is derived from the difference between +DI and −DI, smoothed and normalized to a 0–100 scale. It measures trend strength regardless of direction. ADX above 25 typically indicates a trending market. Below 20 suggests a ranging, trendless market. ADX does not tell you whether the trend is up or down — only how strong the trend is.

This is one of Wilder's most elegant contributions. The insight that trend strength is a separate dimension from trend direction was not obvious in 1978. ADX lets you answer the question "should I be using trend-following tools or mean-reversion tools right now?" before choosing which tool to apply. In a high-ADX environment, use RSI for trend confirmation. In a low-ADX environment, use RSI for overbought/oversold mean reversion.

Commodity Selection Index (CSI)

A ranking tool that combines ATR and ADX to score commodities by their suitability for trading. High ATR (big moves) plus high ADX (trending) equals a high CSI score. The idea was portfolio selection — focus your capital on the markets that are both moving and trending, not the ones that are flat or choppy. It is rarely used today, but the concept of ranking markets by "tradability" has been adopted by many quantitative strategies.

Swing Index

The least remembered of Wilder's six indicators. It attempts to assign a numerical value to price swings using open, high, low, and close data. Wilder used it as a confirmation filter for other signals. It never achieved the adoption of his other tools and is not available on most modern platforms.

The 1978 Score Card
Out of six indicators published in one book:
• RSI — default on every platform, used by millions daily
• ATR — foundation of modern volatility measurement, basis for dozens of derived indicators
• ADX/DMI — standard trend strength tool, on every platform
• Parabolic SAR — widely available, commonly used as trailing stop
• CSI — concept adopted, indicator itself less used
• Swing Index — mostly forgotten

Four out of six in permanent, widespread use nearly fifty years later. There is no comparable single-book contribution in the history of technical analysis.

The Oscillator Cousins

The indicators that share DNA with RSI and stochastic — each solving a specific problem the originals left open.

Williams %R

Larry Williams is one of the most decorated traders in history. Born October 6, 1942, in Miles City, Montana, he graduated from the University of Oregon School of Journalism in 1964 and started trading in 1962 — before he even finished college. His most famous achievement: in the 1987 Robbins World Cup Championship of Futures Trading, he turned $10,000 into $1,137,600 — a return of 11,376% in twelve months trading commodity futures. This remains one of the most documented real-money trading performances in history. His daughter, actress Michelle Williams, won the same competition in 1997.

Williams developed %R around 1973, formally introducing it in his 1979 book "How I Made One Million Dollars Trading Commodities Last Year." He also created the Ultimate Oscillator, COT indices, and various accumulation/distribution indicators. But %R is his most widely used contribution.

The Formula

Williams %R
%R = ((Highest High - Close) / (Highest High - Lowest Low)) x -100

The negative sign is critical — it inverts the scale so that 0 to −20 is overbought (price near the top of its range) and −80 to −100 is oversold (price near the bottom). Default period is 14.

The Stochastic Connection

Here is something most traders do not realize: Williams %R is arithmetically identical to Fast %K (the raw stochastic), just mirrored at the zero line. Where %K uses (Close - Lowest Low) / (Highest High - Lowest Low), %R uses (Highest High - Close) / (Highest High - Lowest Low) with a negative multiplier. Same data, same range, same information — flipped upside down. When %K reads 85, %R reads −15. When %K reads 10, %R reads −90. They are mathematically equivalent expressions of the same relationship: where does the close sit within the recent range?

The practical difference is purely cosmetic — Williams preferred a scale where the overbought zone was near zero (the "top") and the oversold zone was at −100 (the "bottom"). Lane preferred a scale where overbought was at the top (100) and oversold at the bottom (0). Same oscillator, different orientation. Some traders find %R more intuitive because the "reading near zero means near the high" mapping feels natural.

Money Flow Index (MFI)

Developed by Gene Quong and Avrum Soudack, both technical analysts at Shearson Lehman Brothers in the late 1980s. The MFI is often called "volume-weighted RSI" because it uses Wilder's exact RSI formula but replaces price changes with dollar-weighted price changes — incorporating volume into the momentum calculation.

Why Volume Matters

Standard RSI treats every bar equally regardless of how many shares traded. A day where the stock rises 1% on 10 million shares gets the same RSI weight as a day where it rises 1% on 100,000 shares. But those are fundamentally different events. The high-volume day represents conviction — millions of dollars of real capital flowing in. The low-volume day might be noise. MFI captures this distinction by multiplying price movement by volume before averaging, so high-conviction moves carry more weight.

The Formula

Money Flow Index (step by step)
1. Typical Price = (High + Low + Close) / 3
2. Raw Money Flow = Typical Price x Volume
3. If TP > yesterday's TP: Positive Money Flow
If TP < yesterday's TP: Negative Money Flow
4. Money Flow Ratio = Sum(+MF, 14) / Sum(-MF, 14)
5. MFI = 100 - (100 / (1 + Money Flow Ratio))

Step 5 is identical to the RSI formula. The only difference is the input: RSI feeds in price changes, MFI feeds in dollar-volume-weighted price changes. Key levels: above 80 = overbought, below 20 = oversold.

MFI divergences from price are considered more reliable than pure RSI divergences because they require both price movement and volume confirmation. When price makes a new high but MFI does not, it means the new high came on weaker volume — distribution, with smart money selling into retail buying. That is a more meaningful signal than price diverging from a pure momentum calculation.

Chande Momentum Oscillator (CMO)

Tushar S. Chande, PhD — the same man who co-created Stochastic RSI — also developed the CMO. Chande holds a PhD in engineering from the University of Illinois, an MBA from Pittsburgh, and nine U.S. patents in laser manufacturing. He published CMO in "The New Technical Trader" (1994), co-authored with Stanley Kroll.

The Formula and Why It Differs from RSI

Chande Momentum Oscillator
CMO = ((Sum of Up Closes - Sum of Down Closes) / (Sum of Up Closes + Sum of Down Closes)) x 100

Compare this to RSI (simplified): RSI = (Avg Up / (Avg Up + Avg Down)) x 100. The difference is subtle but important. RSI divides the up movement by the total movement — it tells you what fraction of the movement was upward. CMO takes the difference between up and down movement divided by the sum — it tells you the net momentum as a proportion of total movement. And CMO uses raw sums, not Wilder's smoothed averages.

The consequences: CMO oscillates from −100 to +100 (versus RSI's 0–100), making the zero line meaningful. Above zero means net upward momentum; below zero means net downward. RSI has no equivalent zero-line interpretation. Overbought above +50, oversold below −50.

Chande's criticism of RSI was specific: Wilder's exponential smoothing means RSI values carry historical contamination from outside the lookback window (old data never fully drops off). CMO uses raw sums over exactly n periods — no historical leakage, no data length dependency. The tradeoff: CMO is more volatile and reactive than RSI, which makes it noisier in choppy markets but more honest about the actual momentum over the exact window.

True Strength Index (TSI)

William Blau — the same mathematician who invented the double smoothing behind DSS Bressert — published the TSI in Technical Analysis of Stocks & Commodities magazine in November 1991. He expanded it in his 1995 book "Momentum, Direction, and Divergence."

TSI applies Blau's signature technique — double EMA smoothing — directly to raw price momentum (one-bar price change). The double smoothing eliminates noise while preserving trend direction more cleanly than a single EMA could.

True Strength Index
1. Momentum = Close - Close[prior]
2. First smooth: EMA(25) of Momentum
3. Double smooth: EMA(13) of result
4. Same process for |Momentum|
5. TSI = (Double-smoothed Momentum / Double-smoothed |Momentum|) x 100

TSI ranges from −100 to +100. The zero line is meaningful: crossing above zero is bullish, below is bearish. A signal line (7 or 13-period EMA of TSI) provides crossover signals. Overbought above +25, oversold below −25.

The relationship to DSS is direct: both use double EMA smoothing, but TSI applies it to raw momentum while DSS applies it to stochastic ratios. They are two expressions of the same underlying philosophy — that single smoothing is insufficient for simultaneously reducing noise and preserving responsiveness.

Constance Brown's Composite Index

Constance M. Brown, CMT, MFTA is a former institutional trader (12 years), former hedge fund manager (6 years), and founder of Aerodynamic Investments Inc. She was inducted into the Canadian Society of Technical Analysts Hall of Fame in 2018. George Lane himself — the inventor of the stochastic — recommended Brown's Composite Index in the foreword to her book.

Her 1999 book "Technical Analysis for the Trading Professional" (McGraw-Hill, second edition 2012) significantly advanced RSI theory. Her most important contribution was developing the RSI bull/bear range concept — the same idea Cardwell popularized — where RSI operates in context-dependent ranges: 40–80 in bull markets, 20–60 in bear markets. Brown and Cardwell arrived at similar conclusions through independent research.

The CMB Composite Index

Brown's signature indicator is designed to identify RSI divergence failures before they occur. The construction:

  1. Take the standard 14-period RSI
  2. Calculate a 9-period momentum of RSI (current RSI minus RSI from 9 bars ago)
  3. Add the momentum term to RSI, producing an unbounded composite that can go above 100 or below 0

Because the Composite Index is unbounded while RSI is capped at 0–100, they will sometimes diverge from each other. When the Composite makes a new high but RSI does not, that is a bullish signal. When the Composite makes a new low but RSI holds, that is bearish. Two moving averages are plotted on the Composite: 13-period (fast) and 33-period (slow) for additional crossover signals.

The brilliance of Brown's approach is that it solves the RSI ceiling/floor problem. Standard RSI is compressed between 0 and 100, which means at extremes it literally cannot go any higher or lower. Momentum can still be accelerating even when RSI is pinned at 85. The Composite Index captures that continuing acceleration because it is not bounded — giving you information that RSI alone physically cannot provide.

Bollinger Bands on RSI

Instead of using fixed 30/70 levels to define overbought and oversold, you can apply Bollinger Bands directly to the RSI line. The RSI becomes the data source; a moving average of RSI and standard deviation bands around it create dynamic overbought/oversold levels that adapt to current conditions.

John Bollinger himself noted in "Bollinger on Bollinger Bands" (2001) that Bollinger Bands can be applied to any indicator series, not just price. When applied to RSI:

  • RSI above the upper band = overbought relative to its own recent behavior
  • RSI below the lower band = oversold relative to its own recent behavior
  • The bands expand when RSI is swinging widely, compress when RSI is range-bound

This solves the same problem as Cardwell's range shifts but from a different angle. Fixed 70/30 levels were calibrated for 1978 commodity futures. In a trending crypto bull market, RSI might never touch 70 on pullbacks — the upper Bollinger Band on RSI would sit at 85 and flag "overbought" only when RSI is truly extreme relative to its current regime. In a flat, range-bound stock, RSI might oscillate between 45 and 55 and never touch traditional levels — the Bollinger Bands would narrow and flag extremes within that compressed range.

The technique is closely related to StochRSI (which normalizes RSI relative to its own range) and solves a similar problem. StochRSI uses a stochastic approach; Bollinger Bands on RSI uses a volatility-band approach. Both make "extreme" a relative concept rather than an absolute one.

Period Optimization

The period you choose changes everything about how RSI behaves. Here is what each range does and when to use it.

Wilder chose 14 for a specific reason (half lunar cycle), but there is nothing sacred about it. The RSI period controls the fundamental character of the oscillator — how fast it moves, how often it reaches extremes, and what kind of trading it is suited for. Changing the period does not just make RSI "faster" or "slower" — it changes what questions the oscillator can answer.

Ultra-Short: RSI(2) to RSI(5)

At 2 to 5 periods, RSI becomes a mean-reversion detector. It swings violently between extremes, frequently reaching 0 and 100 (or very close). Every move of any significance pushes RSI to an extreme. This makes it useless for trend identification but powerful for spotting short-term overextension.

Larry Connors' backtesting on S&P 500 stocks (1995–2008) showed RSI(2) produced statistically significant edge for mean-reversion strategies. His finding: the more extreme the RSI(2) reading, the stronger the subsequent bounce. Buying below RSI(2) = 5 outperformed buying below 10. Buying below 2 outperformed buying below 5.

At these short periods, the standard 30/70 levels are meaningless — RSI blows through them constantly. Use 5/95 or 10/90 instead. And always filter with a longer-term trend indicator (200-day SMA or longer-period RSI) to ensure you are only taking mean-reversion trades in the direction of the larger trend.

Short: RSI(7) to RSI(9)

Popular with short-term swing traders and day traders. More responsive than 14, less chaotic than 2–5. This range gives you enough smoothing to identify genuine momentum shifts while still reacting quickly enough for trades lasting 1–5 days.

RSI(7) to RSI(9) is particularly favored in forex markets, where 24-hour trading and high liquidity create smoother price action that rewards faster oscillator settings. On intraday charts (M15, H1), shorter RSI periods pick up the mini-cycles that a 14-period RSI would smooth over entirely.

Adjust overbought/oversold levels to 20/80 rather than 30/70 at these periods. The faster oscillator needs wider thresholds to filter noise.

Standard: RSI(14)

Wilder's original, calibrated for daily commodity futures charts. It remains the default on every platform for a reason — it is long enough to smooth out daily noise while short enough to capture meaningful momentum shifts within a multi-week swing. Best suited for daily charts of stocks, commodities, and indices.

At 14 periods, the standard 30/70 levels work as designed (in ranging markets) or shift to Cardwell's 40/80 and 20/60 ranges (in trending markets). This is the period where all of Wilder's original techniques — failure swings, divergence, overbought/oversold — were calibrated and tested.

One important note: because Wilder's SMMA has an equivalent EMA alpha of 1/14 = 0.0714, a 14-period Wilder RSI actually behaves like a 27-period standard EMA in terms of responsiveness. If you switch platforms and the RSI looks different, check whether it is using Wilder's SMMA or a standard EMA — the difference in effective period is almost double.

Long: RSI(21) to RSI(25)

Slower, fewer signals, less noise. Longer-period RSI stays closer to the 50 centerline and rarely reaches the 30/70 extremes. It is best suited for weekly charts and longer-term position trading.

Wilder himself suggested using longer periods (21 or 25) to reduce false signals in strong trends. When RSI(21) finally reaches an extreme, it is a more significant event than RSI(14) reaching the same level, because it required a more sustained move to get there. Some commodity traders use RSI(21) on daily charts specifically to filter out intraweek noise and focus on moves that last multiple weeks.

The Period Tradeoff
Shorter period = more signals, more noise, better for mean reversion.
Longer period = fewer signals, less noise, better for trend confirmation.

There is no "best" period. There is only the right period for your timeframe, asset class, and strategy type. Pick the period that matches what you are trying to do, not the one that generates the most signals.

RSI Across Asset Classes

The same indicator behaves differently on stocks, crypto, forex, and commodities. Understanding why changes how you use it.

RSI was born in the commodity futures markets of the 1970s. It was calibrated for daily commodity charts — markets with discrete trading sessions, moderate volatility, and strong seasonal cycles. Today it is applied to everything from 1-minute crypto charts to monthly stock index charts. But it does not behave the same way everywhere, and the differences matter.

Stocks

RSI performs best on equities, particularly for mean-reversion strategies. Stocks have discrete trading sessions with overnight gaps, which create cleaner RSI extremes on daily charts than 24-hour markets. The gap dynamics (gap-and-fade, gap-and-go) produce sharp RSI moves that resolve predictably.

Connors' RSI(2) research was conducted almost exclusively on equities, and the edge he found was strong and consistent. The Cardwell range shift rules also work cleanly on stocks because equity markets tend to have well-defined trend regimes with identifiable transitions.

Recommended settings: RSI(14) for trend analysis, RSI(2) to RSI(5) for short-term mean reversion. Standard 30/70 in ranging markets, Cardwell ranges in trending markets. ADX works well as a trend/range filter for equities.

Crypto

Crypto markets have extremely high volatility, 24/7 trading (no overnight gaps), and a tendency toward parabolic moves followed by sharp crashes. This combination changes RSI behavior significantly:

  • RSI reaches overbought/oversold levels far more frequently than in stocks
  • Standard 70/30 generates too many signals and premature reversals
  • During parabolic runs, RSI can stay above 80 for weeks — Cardwell's bull range shifts to something like 40–90
  • The 24/7 nature means no gap dynamics to create clean RSI extremes

Recommended settings: RSI(14) on daily/weekly for trend confirmation. RSI(7) on 4h for swing entries. Widen thresholds to 20/80 or even 15/85. StochRSI is particularly popular in crypto because its extra sensitivity matches the asset class's volatility. DSS Bressert also works well for crypto cycle timing.

Forex

RSI has a weaker documented track record in forex. The 24-hour market (no overnight gaps), extreme liquidity, and macro-driven price action create challenges. Standard RSI(14) generates many false signals in ranging forex pairs, which is problematic because most forex pairs range most of the time.

Shorter periods (7–9) are preferred in forex because they capture the faster oscillations in these highly liquid markets. RSI signals in forex are typically filtered with trend direction from a higher timeframe — a 4h RSI pullback in the direction of the daily trend, for example.

Recommended settings: RSI(7) to RSI(9) on 15m–4h charts. Levels at 20/80. Always filter with a higher-timeframe trend bias. RSI trendline breaks work well on daily forex charts where the price action is smoother than equities.

Commodities

This is RSI's home territory — the asset class Wilder calibrated it for. Commodity markets have strong seasonal cycles and supply-driven fundamentals that can sustain RSI extremes for long periods.

Recommended settings: RSI(14) on daily charts — Wilder's original calibration works well. In strong trending commodity markets (oil in a supply squeeze, gold in a macro flight-to-safety), adjust levels to 20/80. Bressert's cycle-based approach (RSI3M3 + timing bands) was specifically developed for commodity futures and remains well-suited to them.

The Universal Rule
Regardless of asset class, the single most important thing you can do is identify the trend regime first, then choose your RSI technique accordingly. Trending market? Use RSI for confirmation (Cardwell ranges, hidden divergence, failure swings). Ranging market? Use RSI for mean reversion (classic 30/70, regular divergence, overbought/oversold). ADX or a longer-period RSI on a higher timeframe tells you which mode to operate in. This rule applies to stocks, crypto, forex, and commodities alike.

Multi-Timeframe RSI Analysis

One of the most powerful RSI techniques is checking it across multiple timeframes before making a trade. The principle is simple: align the trend on the higher timeframe with the entry on the lower timeframe.

The Three-Screen Framework

Originally from Alexander Elder's Triple Screen Trading System, adapted for RSI:

  1. Weekly RSI(14) — establishes the trend. Is RSI in the 40–80 bull range or the 20–60 bear range (Cardwell)? This sets your directional bias.
  2. Daily RSI(14) — identifies pullback opportunities within the trend. In a weekly uptrend, you want daily RSI to pull back to the lower end of its range.
  3. 4-hour or intraday RSI or StochRSI — timing the entry. Look for the failure swing or divergence that confirms the pullback is ending.

The key: never take an entry on the lower timeframe that contradicts the higher timeframe. If weekly RSI says bear range (20–60), do not go long just because the 4-hour RSI shows a bullish failure swing. The failure swing might produce a bounce, but you are fighting the larger trend.

RSI + Stochastic Confluence

Because RSI and stochastic measure different aspects of momentum (average gains/losses vs. position within range), they sometimes give conflicting signals. When they agree, the signal is significantly stronger. Here is how to combine them:

Strong buy signal: RSI in oversold territory (or pulling back to 40 in a bull trend using Cardwell ranges) and stochastic %K crossing above %D in oversold territory. Both indicators independently confirm that the pullback is ending.

Strong sell signal: RSI in overbought territory (or pushing up to 60 in a bear trend) and stochastic %K crossing below %D in overbought territory.

Conflicting signals: when RSI says one thing and stochastic says another, the signal is weak. This usually happens during transitions or in choppy markets. The proper response to conflicting signals is no trade, not trying to pick which indicator is "right."

RSI + ADX: The Wilder Combination

Wilder published both RSI and ADX in the same book, and combining them is one of the most powerful yet underused approaches. ADX tells you the character of the market right now:

ADX above 25 (trending): use RSI for trend confirmation. Look for bullish failure swings and Cardwell positive reversals in uptrends. Use overbought/oversold readings as signs of trend strength, not reversal. Apply RSI trendlines for early exit signals.

ADX below 20 (ranging): use RSI for mean reversion. Now the classic 30/70 overbought/oversold approach works well because the market genuinely is oscillating within a range. Divergences are more reliable here. Failure swings are more reliable here.

The mistake most traders make is using the same RSI interpretation in both environments. Buying oversold in a trending down market is catching knives. Buying oversold in a ranging market is a legitimate mean reversion trade. ADX is the switch that tells you which mode to operate in.

RSI Trendline Breakout Strategy

A specific trading strategy built around the RSI trendline technique from Chapter 3:

  1. On a daily chart, identify a clear RSI trendline with at least 2–3 touches (support in uptrend, resistance in downtrend)
  2. Wait for the RSI trendline to break cleanly
  3. Confirm with either a failure swing forming or a Cardwell range shift in progress
  4. Enter on the next candle after the RSI trendline break, with a stop below the most recent swing low (for longs) or above swing high (for shorts)
  5. Use ATR for stop distance calibration — if your stop is less than 1x ATR from entry, it is probably too tight

This strategy works because RSI trendlines frequently break before price trendlines. You are getting into the trade before the crowd that is watching price trendlines.

Bressert's Cycle Timing Framework

For traders who want to combine oscillators with cycle analysis (Bressert's core methodology):

  1. Identify the cycle: determine which cycle you are trading (Trading Cycle, Primary Cycle, etc.) and where you are within it
  2. Check the timing band: are you within the window where 70% of cycle tops/bottoms historically occur?
  3. Watch RSI3M3 or DSS: wait for the oscillator to reach an extreme and form a setup
  4. Trigger entry: price action confirms — buy stop above the setup bar high for longs
  5. Protective stop: below the cycle low for longs, above the cycle high for shorts

The key insight from Bressert: the probability of a successful trade is much greater later in the timing band than earlier. If the timing band is days 15–25 from the last cycle low, a signal on day 22 is far more reliable than a signal on day 16. Be patient. Let the cycle mature before acting on the oscillator.

Divergence Trading Rules

Divergence is one of the most misused RSI signals. Here are rules to filter for higher-quality divergence trades:

  1. Trend context first: use Cardwell range shifts to determine the trend. Only trade regular bearish divergence in ranging or bear markets. In bull markets, what looks like bearish divergence is often a negative reversal (continuation).
  2. Require extreme readings: divergence where RSI stays between 40 and 60 is meaningless noise. Require at least one leg of the divergence to touch or penetrate the 30 or 70 level.
  3. Fewer bars = better: divergence that forms over 5–15 bars is stronger than divergence stretched over 30–50 bars. Extended divergence tends to resolve by RSI catching up to price rather than price reversing.
  4. Wait for confirmation: divergence alone is not an entry signal. Wait for a failure swing, trendline break, or candle pattern to confirm before entering.
  5. Hidden divergence for continuation: in clear trends, hidden divergence (higher low in price / lower low in RSI for uptrend) is often a better signal than regular divergence because you are trading with the trend.
