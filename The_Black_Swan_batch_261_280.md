### Mandelbrot's Rejection and Legacy

- **George Shultz** (later Reagan's Secretary of State) called Mandelbrot to rescind the Harvard offer in 1960s
- 44 years later (as of writing), nothing has changed in economics/social science statistics—still treating world as Gaussian despite evidence
- Nobel medals awarded before understanding **Mandelbrotian fractals**, which were rejected as "too good for them" (*margaritas ante porcos*)
- **Asymmetry of induction**: easier to reject bell curve than accept it; single extreme event can destroy Gaussian argument, but quiet periods can't prove it

> "You can always produce data 'corroborating' that the underlying process is Gaussian by finding periods that do not have rare events, just like you can find an afternoon during which no one killed anyone and use it as 'evidence' of peaceful behavior."

### The Logic of Fractal Randomness (With Warning)

- **Power law exponents** determine scalability: if wealth doubles from $1M to $2M, incidence of people with that wealth is cut in four (exponent of two)
- **Scalability property**: ratio of two exceedances equals ratio of two numbers to negative power of exponent
- Example: 96 books sell >250K copies yearly; exponent ~1.5 means ~34 books sell >500K copies (96 × (500K/250K)^-1.5)
- Table 2 shows exponents for phenomena: books sold (1.5), earthquakes (2.8), company size (1.5), wars (0.8), terrorist deaths (2 but possibly lower)

### The Problem of Precision

**Table 3: Meaning of the Exponent** shows extreme sensitivity
- Between exponents 1.1 and 1.3, top 1% share drops from 66% to 34%—just 0.2 difference changes result dramatically
- We cannot observe exponents directly—only estimate from past data or theories with potential hidden weaknesses
- Exponent of 1.5 is approximation, hard to compute, has monstrous sampling error
- Number of books selling >1M could be 8, 20, or 2—high uncertainty

### The Crossover Problem

- Exponents apply above certain "crossover" point—may start at 200K books or 400K
- Wealth behaves differently below vs. above $600M
- **Scalability is sufficient for decision-making** even without precise exponent—knowing distribution is scalable/fractal matters more than exact number

### The Problem of the Upper Bound

- Some accept fractals "up to a point," proposing truncation at upper limit
- **"I don't know how high" vs. "there is no limit"** carry same consequences in practice
- Proposing arbitrary upper limit ($150B vs. $151B vs. $152B?) is highly unsafe—better to consider variable unlimited

### Beware the Precision (Masquerade Problem)

- **Masquerade problem**: exponents likely to be overestimated (higher exponent = smaller role for large deviations)
- What you see is less Black Swannish than reality
- Example: generate process with exponent 1.7, you'll likely compute 2.4—takes long time for fractal processes to reveal properties
- Fractals can appear Gaussian when cutpoint starts high—extreme deviations rare enough to fool observers

### The Water Puddle Revisited

- Problem of induction returns with **Extremistan**: mechanism can deliver large values, but how often/how large remains uncertain
- Like water puddle—plenty of ice cubes could have generated it
- Three "popular science" books reviewed: **Mark Buchanan's *Ubiquity***, **Philip Ball's *Critical Mass***, **Paul Ormerod's *Why Most Things Fail***
- Authors present world as full of power laws with **universality**—wonderful similarity between natural/social processes
- They show correspondence between critical phenomena in physics and social organization (avalanches, contagions, informational cascades)
- **Critical points** in physics have universal properties independent of underlying system details

### The Epistemological Trap

- Problem: all three authors fall into trap of **not differentiating forward vs. backward processes** (problem vs. inverse problem)
- Those who work with data but don't make decisions tend toward narrative fallacy
- Without feedback, models confirm reality instead of reality testing models
- Complexity theory should make us **more suspicious** of precise models, not less
- **Fractals make Black Swans gray, not predictable**—they account for some surprises but not all

> "Complexity theory should make us more suspicious of scientific claims of precise models of reality. It does not make all the swans white; that is predictable: it makes them gray, and only gray."

### From Representation to Reality

- Epistemologically different place for bottom-up empiricist vs. top-down theorist
- Cannot read equations of universe; must observe data and calibrate
- History runs forward, not backward—humbling process for those aware of narrative fallacy
- **Opacity problem**: incompleteness of information, invisibility of world's generator

> "What I am talking about is opacity, incompleteness of information, the invisibility of the generator of the world. History does not reveal its mind to us—we need to guess what's inside of it."

### The Circularity of Statistics Problem

- Need past data to determine if distribution is Gaussian, fractal, or else
- Need to know if you have enough data to "build confidence"
- **Circular regression**: distribution tells you how much data needed; data tells you distribution type
- **Severe regress argument**: causes problems for historical data analysis

### Why Statisticians Ignore the Problem

### Models of Extremistan Genesis

2. **Percolation models**: address terrain/structure, not individual behavior—how liquid flows on porous surface, how sand avalanches form

### Where Is the Gray Swan?

- Taleb hates Black Swan as humanist—causes unfairness and damage
- **Fractal randomness reduces surprises, makes swans appear possible, makes them gray**
- But **fractal randomness does not yield precise answers**

> "But fractal randomness does not yield precise answers."

- If you know stock market *can* crash (1987), it's not Black Swan
- If you know biotech can deliver megablockbuster drug, won't be Black Swan
- Fractals allow accounting for **some** Black Swans, not all
- **Gray swan**: modelable extreme events; **Black swan**: unknown unknowns
