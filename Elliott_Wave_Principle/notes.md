# Elliott Wave Principle — Notes
## by A.J. Frost and Robert R. Prechter, Jr. (1978, 10th Edition 2005)

### Foreword (by Charles J. Collins)
- Ecclesiastes quote: "The thing that hath been, it is that which shall be" — human nature doesn't change
- Four men built reputations on this truth: Arthur Pigou, Charles H. Dow, Bernard Baruch, Ralph Nelson Elliott
- Pigou: business swings caused by excesses of human optimism followed by pessimism — pendulum analogy
- Dow: market moves in orderly sequence, not random; three upward swings in primary uptrend; reverse movements cancel 3/8 or more of each swing
- Baruch: "What actually registers in the stock market's fluctuations are not the events themselves, but the human reactions to these events"
- Elliott: working in Mexico, fell ill (anemia), retired to rocking chair in California, studied DJIA history and movement
- Collins received telegram from Elliott on last day of rail list decline 1935, stating it was only the first setback in a bull market — proved right
- Collins published Elliott's theory as "The Wave Principle" booklet, introduced him to Financial World magazine
- Elliott later expanded into "Nature's Law" incorporating Fibonacci

### Authors' Note
- Goal: explain Wave Principle in simple, concise terms
- Early works on Elliott now out of print; scattered writings since then
- "Once you grasp the Wave Principle, you will have at your command a new and fascinating approach to market analysis, and even beyond that, a mathematical philosophy that can be applied to other spheres of life"

### Page 14: Elliott's Original Letter (Nov 28, 1934)
- Letter from R.N. Elliott to C.J. Collins (Investment Counsel, Detroit)
- "About six months ago I discovered 3 features in market action, and insofar as I know they are novel"
- Claims he could have forecasted the DJIA top of 107 last April and the 85 bottom
- "Incidentally it permits me to forecast that the present major bull swing will be followed by a major bear collapse"
- Classifies waves of all movements into six categories with 83% time element accuracy
- His discoveries are "much less mechanical than the Dow theory but add great forecasting value which it lacks"

## PART I: ELLIOTT THEORY

## Chapter 1: The Broad Concept (pp. 19-62)

### Basic Tenets (pp. 21-30)
- Wave Principle governed by man's social nature — forms are repetitive, therefore predictive
- Market has a law of its own — not propelled by external causality
- Path of prices is NOT a product of news; market is NOT a cyclically rhythmic machine
- Market progression unfolds in WAVES — patterns of directional movement

**The Five-Wave Pattern:**
- Progress takes form of five waves (labeled 1-5)
- Waves 1, 3, 5 = directional (actionary); Waves 2, 4 = countertrend interruptions
- Three rules of five-wave form: (1) Wave 2 never moves beyond start of wave 1; (2) Wave 3 is never the shortest wave; (3) Wave 4 never enters price territory of wave 1
- [CHART: p.22 "Figure 1-1 — The Basic Pattern: five-wave upward impulse"]

**Wave Mode — Motive vs. Corrective:**
- Two modes: motive (five-wave structure) and corrective (three-wave structure)
- Motive waves impel the market; corrective waves are partial retracements
- [CHART: p.23 "Figure 1-2 — Complete eight-wave cycle: five motive + three corrective (A-B-C)"]

**Compound Construction:**
- Each eight-wave cycle becomes two subdivisions of the wave of next higher degree
- The process of building to greater degrees continues indefinitely
- Essential principle: ACTION in same direction as larger trend = five waves; REACTION against larger trend = three waves
- [CHART: p.24 "Figure 1-3 — Compound construction showing 2, 8, and 34 waves"]

**Number of Waves at Each Degree:**
- Motive + Corrective = Cycle: 1+1=2, 5+3=8, 21+13=34, 89+55=144
- These are all Fibonacci numbers
- [CHART: p.25 "Figure 1-4 — Complete Market Cycle showing all subdivisions"]

**Why 5-3?**
- One wave doesn't allow fluctuation; three waves (unqualified) don't allow progress
- Five waves in the trend direction + three waves countertrend = minimum requirement for both fluctuation AND progress
- Nature follows the most efficient path: 5-3

**Wave Degree and Nomenclature:**
- Nine degrees named (largest to smallest): Grand Supercycle, Supercycle, Cycle, Primary, Intermediate, Minor, Minute, Minuette, Subminuette
- Extended to 15 degrees with Supermillennium, Millennium, Submillennium at top and Micro, Submicro, Miniscule at bottom
- Labeling system: alternating Roman/Arabic numerals for motive waves, upper/lower case letters for corrective waves
- Degree determined by relative size (form), not absolute price or time

**Wave Function:**
- Every wave serves one of two functions: action or reaction
- Actionary/trend wave: trends in same direction as wave of one larger degree (odd numbers: 1, 3, 5, a, c)
- Reactionary/countertrend wave: trends opposite to wave of one larger degree (even numbers: 2, 4, b)
- All reactionary waves develop in corrective mode
- Critical insight: motive waves don't always point upward, corrective waves don't always point downward — it's about RELATIVE direction

### Detailed Analytics — Motive Waves (pp. 31-40)

**Impulse:**
- Most common motive wave, per basic five-wave pattern
- Rules: Wave 4 does NOT overlap wave 1 price territory ("no overlap" rule); Wave 3 is never the shortest actionary wave; Subwave 3 is always an impulse
- Futures markets can show brief intraday overlapping due to leverage — rare
- Rules vs. Guidelines: rules govern ALL waves and are never broken; guidelines are typical tendencies (not requirements)

**Extension:**
- Elongated impulse with exaggerated subdivisions
- Only ONE of the three actionary subwaves (1, 3, or 5) typically extends
- Extended wave subdivisions nearly same amplitude/duration as other four waves → nine-wave count
- In stock market, WAVE 3 is the most commonly extended wave
- If waves 1 and 3 are equal length → fifth wave likely to be protracted (extended)
- If wave 3 extends → fifth should be simply constructed, resembling wave 1
- [CHART: p.33 "Figure 1-5 — Extensions: first, third, fifth wave, and unidentified"]
- [CHART: p.33 "Figures 1-6, 1-7, 1-8 — Incorrect vs. correct counting with extensions"]
- Extensions within extensions: third wave of extended third wave can itself extend (Figure 1-9)
- Fifth wave extension of fifth wave extension also occurs — common in commodity bull markets (Figure 1-10)
- [CHART: p.34 "Figure 1-9 — Third wave extension of third wave extension"]
- [CHART: p.34 "Figure 1-10 — Fifth wave extension of fifth wave extension"]

**Truncation (Failure):**
- Fifth wave does not move beyond the end of wave 3
- Elliott called it "failure"; authors prefer "truncation"
- Can be verified by checking that presumed fifth wave contains five subwaves
- Occurs following a particularly strong third wave
- U.S. stock market examples: October 1962 (Cuban missile crisis) and year-end 1976
- [CHART: p.35 "Figures 1-11, 1-12 — Bull and bear market truncation patterns"]
- [CHART: p.36 "Figure 1-13 — DJIA Weekly: 1962 truncation at Cuban crisis"]
- [CHART: p.36 "Figure 1-14 — DJIA Daily: 1976 truncation"]

**Diagonal:**
- Motive pattern but NOT an impulse — has two corrective characteristics
- Like impulse: no reactionary subwave fully retraces preceding actionary wave; wave 3 not shortest
- Unlike impulse: wave 4 ALMOST ALWAYS overlaps wave 1; all waves are "threes" (3-3-3-3-3)
- Wedge-shaped pattern within converging (or diverging) boundary lines

**Ending Diagonal:**
- Occurs primarily in wave 5 position ("too far too fast")
- Small percentage appear in C-wave of A-B-C formations
- Found at TERMINATION POINTS of larger patterns → signals exhaustion
- Contracting diagonal (most common): wedge within two converging lines
- Fifth wave of ending diagonal often ends with "throw-over" — brief break of trendline
- Rising ending diagonal → sharp decline retracing at least to where it began
- Falling ending diagonal → upward thrust
- [CHART: p.37 "Figures 1-15, 1-16 — Ending diagonal in bull and bear markets"]
- [CHART: p.38 "Figure 1-17 — DJIA Daily: ending diagonal in early 1978"]
- [CHART: p.39 "Figure 1-18 — DJIA Daily: ending diagonal with throw-over"]
- [CHART: p.39 "Figure 1-19 — DJIA Hourly: possible expanding diagonal, Jan-Feb 1980"]
- Fifth wave extensions, truncated fifths, and ending diagonals ALL imply "dramatic reversal ahead"

**Leading Diagonal:**
- Diagonal in wave 1 position of impulses and wave A of zigzags
- Subdivisions appear to be 3-3-3-3-3 (some cases 5-3-5-3-5)
- Must be careful not to mistake for a series of first and second waves (Figure 1-8 pattern)
- Leading diagonal in wave 1 → typically followed by deep retracement
- Can also take expanding shape — appears primarily at start of declines
- [CHART: p.40 "Figure 1-20 — Leading diagonal DJIA Hourly, Nov-Dec 1979"]

### Detailed Analytics — Corrective Waves (pp. 41-54)

- Markets move against the trend of one larger degree only with seeming struggle
- Corrective waves are more varied, less clearly identifiable than motive waves
- They can increase or decrease in complexity as they unfold
- **Key rule: corrections are NEVER fives** — only motive waves are fives
- Two corrective styles: SHARP (angle steeply against larger trend) and SIDEWAYS (net retracement but overall sideways appearance)
- Three main categories: Zigzag, Flat, Triangle
- Plus combinations: double three and triple three

**Zigzag (5-3-5):**
- Simple three-wave declining pattern (bull market): A-B-C with 5-3-5 subwave sequence
- Top of wave B is noticeably lower than start of wave A
- Bear market zigzag = inverted (upward correction)
- Can occur in double or triple zigzag (two or three zigzags connected by intervening "three" wave X)
- W-X-Y notation for double zigzag; W-X-Y-X-Z for triple
- These formations analogous to extensions of impulse waves
- [CHART: p.43 "Figures 1-22 through 1-26 — Zigzag patterns: bull, bear, double zigzag"]
- [CHART: p.44 "Figure 1-27 — DJIA Hourly: double zigzag July-October 1975"]
- [CHART: p.45 "Figure 1-28 — S&P 500: double zigzag Jan 1977 - Mar 1978"]

**Flat (3-3-5):**
- Differs from zigzag: subwave sequence is 3-3-5
- Wave A lacks sufficient force to unfold into full five waves
- Wave B terminates near start of wave A; wave C terminates just slightly beyond end of wave A
- Flat retraces less of preceding impulse than zigzag
- Occurs when larger trend is strong; virtually always precedes or follows an extension
- Fourth wave frequently sports a flat; second wave rarely does
- Three types of 3-3-5 corrections:
  - **Regular flat**: B ends near start of A, C ends slightly past end of A
  - **Expanded flat** (most common): B goes beyond start of A, C ends substantially beyond end of A — Elliott called this "irregular"
  - **Running flat** (rare): B goes well beyond start of A, but C fails to reach level where A ended — forces of larger trend are so powerful pattern skews in trend direction
- [CHART: p.45 "Figures 1-29, 1-30 — Flat in bull market"]
- [CHART: p.46 "Figures 1-31, 1-32 — Flat in bear market"]
- [CHART: p.47 "Figures 1-33 through 1-37 — Expanded flat patterns + DJIA 1973 example"]
- [CHART: p.48 "Figures 1-38 through 1-41 — Running flat patterns, bull and bear"]
- Warning: running flats are very rare — never label a correction prematurely as running flat (wrong 9 times out of 10)

**Triangle (3-3-3-3-3):**
- Reflects balance of forces, sideways movement with decreasing volume and volatility
- Five overlapping waves subdividing 3-3-3-3-3, labeled A-B-C-D-E
- Delineated by connecting termination points of waves A-C and B-D
- Wave E can undershoot or overshoot the A-C line (happens more often than not)
- Three varieties: contracting, barrier, and expanding
- Barrier triangle: horizontal line on one side (flat on side that next wave will exceed)
- Running triangle: wave B exceeds start of wave A but still has net retracement at wave E
- [CHART: p.50 "Figure 1-42 — Variations of Elliott Wave Triangles: contracting, barrier, expanding"]
- [CHART: p.50 "Figure 1-43 — Running triangle in bull and bear markets"]
- Subwaves are mostly zigzags; wave C sometimes more complex (multiple zigzag)
- In rare cases, wave E is itself a triangle → nine-wave protraction (analogous to extension)
- Silver 1973-1977 example of nine-wave triangle (Figure 1-44)
- [CHART: p.51 "Figure 1-44 — Silver: nine-wave triangle, 1972-1978"]

**Triangle Position and Thrust:**
- Triangle always occurs PRIOR TO the final actionary wave: wave 4 in impulse, wave B in A-B-C, final wave X in combinations
- Post-triangle wave ("thrust") is swift and short, traveling approximately the widest part of the triangle
- Thrust is usually an impulse but can be ending diagonal
- In powerful markets, no thrust but instead a prolonged fifth wave
- Many analysts fooled into labeling completed triangle too early — boundary lines almost never collapse quickly; give triangles time
- When boundary lines of contracting triangle reach apex, it often coincides with a turning point

**Combination (Double Three and Triple Three):**
- Sideways combination of simpler corrective patterns labeled W-X-Y (-X-Z)
- Each reactionary wave X can take shape of any corrective pattern but is most commonly a zigzag
- Combinations appear to be the flat correction's way of extending sideways action
- In combinations, never more than one zigzag, never more than one triangle, and triangle only as final wave
- Key difference from double/triple zigzags: zigzags aim for adequate PRICE correction; combinations aim for adequate TIME/duration correction
- [CHART: p.53 "Figures 1-45, 1-46 — Double three and triple three schematics"]
- [CHART: p.53 "Figure 1-47 — Flat + zigzag + triangle combination"]
- [CHART: p.53 "Figure 1-48 — Flat + zigzag + zigzag combination"]
- Wave counting tip: 9, 13, or 17 with few overlaps = likely motive; 7, 11, or 15 with numerous overlaps = likely corrective

### Orthodox Tops and Bottoms (p. 55)
- Pattern's end can differ from the associated price extreme
- "Orthodox" top or bottom = the end of the pattern, even if not the price extreme
- Example: in expanded flat, wave B makes new high but the orthodox top is end of wave (5)
- Important for forecasting: length and duration measured from orthodox ending points

### Reconciling Function and Mode (pp. 55-56)
- Actionary waves labeled: 1, 3, 5, A, C, E, W, Y, Z
- Reactionary waves labeled: 2, 4, B, D, X
- Five specific exceptions where actionary waves develop in corrective mode ("actionary corrective" waves):
  - Waves 1, 3, 5 in ending diagonal
  - Wave A in flat correction
  - Waves A, C, E in triangle
  - Waves W and Y in double/triple zigzags and combinations
  - Wave Z in triple zigzag and triple three

### Additional Terminology (pp. 56-57)
- **Progressive wave**: motive wave upward not within a correction — labeled 1, 3, or 5
- **Regressive wave**: any declining wave regardless of mode
- **Proregressive wave**: upward wave within a corrective wave of any larger degree
- Both regressive and proregressive waves are part of corrections; only progressive waves achieve progress
- Elliott wave terms are qualitative (based on form) vs. conventional terms that are quantitative (20% = bear market) and therefore arbitrary
- **Cardinal waves**: waves denoted by numbers composing the essential five-wave impulse
- **Consonant/subcardinal waves**: waves denoted by letters serving as components of cardinal waves 2 and 4

### Erroneous Concepts and Patterns (pp. 58-60)
- Elliott's "irregular top" concept was flawed — it was actually a misidentification of a third wave extension as a fifth wave extension
- The "irregular type 2" A-B-C correction was invented to handle the leftover waves — these two erroneous concepts were born of the same tendency and one requires the other
- Correct count (Figure 1-50) eliminates both problems
- Elliott's "double retracement" after fifth wave extension is simply natural behavior (corrections bottom in fourth wave area)
- Elliott's "half moon" shape is not a separate pattern — just how some declines look on semilog scale
- Elliott's "A-B base" was invented to force his Principle into a 13-year triangle — no interpreter today accepts it; the authors have never seen an A-B base and such a pattern would invalidate the Wave Principle
- No other formations than those listed in this chapter will occur in price movement of broad stock market averages
- Hourly readings are nearly perfect for detailing Subminuette waves; minute-by-minute transactions reveal even smaller degrees
- Wave Principle requires free market pricing — government price fixing (gold, silver) restricts wave expression

## Chapter 2: Guidelines of Wave Formation (pp. 63-98)

### Alternation (pp. 63-65)
- Broad guideline: expect a DIFFERENCE in the next expression of a similar wave
- Bolton: "not convinced alternation is inevitable, but frequent enough to look for it"
- Tells you what NOT to expect — the day most investors "catch on" to an apparent market habit is the day it will change
- Elliott went further: alternation was virtually a law of markets

**Within an Impulse:**
- If wave 2 is sharp correction → expect wave 4 to be sideways, and vice versa
- Sharp corrections: zigzags (single, double, triple); sometimes double threes beginning with zigzag
- Sideways corrections: flats, triangles, double/triple combinations
- One of the two corrective processes will contain a move beyond the orthodox end of the preceding impulse, and the other will not
- [CHART: p.64 "Figure 2-1 — Alternation patterns: sharp/sideways in bull and bear"]
- Diagonals do NOT display alternation between subwaves 2 and 4

**Within Corrective Waves:**
- If wave A is flat → expect wave B to be zigzag, and vice versa (Figures 2-2, 2-3)
- Alternation in complexity also occurs: simple A → complex B → more complex C (Figures 2-4, 2-5)
- [CHART: p.65 "Figures 2-2, 2-3 — Alternation within corrective waves"]
- [CHART: p.66 "Figures 2-4, 2-5 — Alternation in complexity"]

### Depth of Corrective Waves (pp. 66-68)
- Corrections (especially fourth waves) tend to register maximum retracement within span of travel of previous fourth wave of one lesser degree, most commonly near its terminus
- Example #1: 1929-1932 bear — Supercycle low bottomed in area of previous Cycle degree fourth wave (expanding triangle 1890-1921)
- Example #2: 1937-1942 bear — zigzag terminated in area of fourth Primary wave of 1932-1937 bull
- Example #3: 1962 bear — wave (4) plunge brought averages to just above 1956 high (fourth wave area of wave (3))
- Example #4: 1974 bear — Cycle wave IV ended in area of previous fourth wave of lesser degree (Primary wave (4) from 1942)
- Example #5: London gold bear market 1974-1976 terminated in fourth wave area of one lesser degree
- If FIRST wave in sequence extends → correction following fifth wave has typical limit of bottom of SECOND wave of lesser degree
- Flat/triangle following extension may fail by slim margin to reach fourth wave area; zigzag may cut deeper into second wave area
- "Double bottoms" sometimes formed this way

### Behavior Following Fifth Wave Extensions (pp. 68-69)
- When fifth wave of advance is an extension → ensuing correction will be sharp and find support at level of low of wave 2 of the extension
- Sometimes correction ends there (only wave A); sometimes entire correction ends there
- This level provides advance warning of dramatic reversal to a specific level
- [CHART: p.69 "Figures 2-6, 2-7 — Behavior following fifth wave extensions"]

### Wave Equality (pp. 69-70)
- Two of the three motive waves in a five-wave sequence tend toward equality in time and magnitude
- Especially true when one wave is extended — the other two non-extended waves tend toward equality
- If perfect equality lacking → .618 multiple is next likely relationship
- For waves larger than Intermediate: price relationships in percentage terms; for Intermediate and below: arithmetic terms work fine
- Example: 1942-1966 Cycle wave — Primary wave (1) traveled 120 points (129%, 49 months); Primary wave (5) traveled 438 points (80% = .618 × 129%, 40 months)

### Charting the Waves (pp. 70-71)
- Hamilton Bolton kept "hourly close" chart — end-of-hour prices
- Authors recommend the same practice — plot hourly DJIA fluctuations from WSJ and Barron's
- Bar charts fine for general use but can mislead near time boundaries
- Use actual print figures, not "opening" or "theoretical intraday" figures
- Short term charts needed for fast markets; longer term charts for lethargic/choppy markets
- Forecasting sideways trend can be as valuable as forecasting directional trends

### Channeling (pp. 71-73)
- Elliott noted that parallel trend channel typically marks impulse wave boundaries with dramatic precision
- Initial technique: after wave 3 ends, connect points 1 and 3, draw parallel through point 2 → estimated boundary for wave 4
- After wave 4 ends: connect points 2 and 4, draw parallel through point 3 → estimated boundary for wave 5 (final channel)
- If wave 3 abnormally strong/vertical → parallel from its top may be too high; parallel touching wave 1 top may be more useful
- Zigzag corrections also form channels with four touch points (start of A, end of B on one line; end of A, end of C on other)
- [CHART: p.72 "Figure 2-8 — Temporary channel after wave 3"]
- [CHART: p.72 "Figure 2-9 — Final channel after wave 4"]

### Throw-over (pp. 73-74)
- Brief penetration of upper channel line (in bull) or lower line (in bear) during fifth wave
- Signaled by declining volume as fifth wave approaches upper trendline
- Heavy volume at upper trendline → indicates possible penetration (throw-over)
- Near throw-over point, small wave 4 may trend sideways just below parallel, then wave 5 breaks through
- Confirmed by immediate reversal back below the line
- Can be telegraphed by preceding "throw-under" in wave 4 or wave 2 of 5
- [CHART: p.74 "Figure 2-10 — Elliott's throw-over drawing from The Wave Principle"]

### Scale (pp. 74-76)
- Semilog scale for long-term plots; arithmetic scale for short-term tracking
- Elliott's assumption that semilog channeling indicates inflation was demonstrably incorrect
- 1920s bull market (mild deflation) channels perfectly on semilog; 1930s bull (mild inflation) channels on arithmetic
- The two waves are surprisingly similar: same price multiples (6x and 5x), both contain extended fifths, same percentage gain above bottom for third wave peak
- Use both scales — if channel doesn't fit on one, try the other
- [CHART: p.75 "Figures 2-11, 2-12 — DJIA Monthly: semilog scale (1920s) vs arithmetic (1930s)"]

### Volume (p. 76)
- Volume expands and contracts with speed of price change in bull markets
- Low volume often coincides with turning points in corrective phases
- Normal fifth wave (below Primary): volume less than in third wave
- If fifth wave volume equals or exceeds third wave → signals extension of the fifth
- At Primary degree and above, volume in advancing fifth wave tends to be higher (more participants in long-term bull)
- Volume at terminal point of bull market above Primary degree tends to run at all-time high
- Volume spikes at throw-over points and diagonal resistance lines
- Elliott claimed volume independently follows Wave Principle patterns — authors find no convincing evidence for this

### The "Right Look" (pp. 77-78)
- Overall appearance of a wave must conform to the appropriate illustration
- Any five-wave sequence can be forced into a three-wave count by labeling 1-2-3 as single wave A — but this is INCORRECT if wave 4 terminates well above wave 1 top
- Internal count is a guide to overall shape, and overall shape is a guide to internal count
- Danger: emotional involvement with market can lead to accepting misshapen patterns
- If hourly chart confusing → step back to daily/weekly; if weekly has too many possibilities → focus on shorter term until bigger picture clarifies

### Wave Personality (pp. 78-85)
- Each wave type reflects the mass psychology it embodies
- Market history repeats but not exactly — related waves (siblings, cousins) have similar characteristics
- Wave personality is manifest whether the wave is Grand Supercycle or Subminuette

**Individual Wave Characters:**
1. **First waves** — About half are part of "basing" process, heavily corrected by wave 2; other half rise from large bases (1949), downside failures (1962), or extreme compression (1962, 1974) — these are more dynamic
2. **Second waves** — Retrace so much of wave 1 that most profits are eroded; investors convinced bear market is back; end on very low volume and volatility (drying up of selling pressure)
3. **Third waves** — "Wonders to behold" — strong, broad, unmistakable trend; favorable fundamentals; greatest volume and price movement; most often the extended wave; produce breakouts, continuation gaps, volume expansions, exceptional breadth; virtually all stocks participate
4. **Fourth waves** — Predictable in depth (by alternation) and form; build base for final fifth wave; lagging stocks build tops and begin declining; initial deterioration sets stage for non-confirmations and subtle weakness during fifth wave
5. **Fifth waves** — Always less dynamic than third waves in breadth; slower maximum speed of price change (except in extensions); volume usually lesser; optimism runs extremely high despite narrowing breadth; advisory services most bullish; year-end 1976 rally turned lowest percentage of bears (4.5%) in recorded history despite that fifth wave's failure
6. **A waves** — Investment world generally convinced this is just a pullback before next leg up; public surges to buy side; A wave sets tone for B wave (five-wave A → zigzag B; three-wave A → flat or triangle B)
7. **B waves** — "Phonies" — sucker plays, bull traps, speculators' paradise; narrow leadership; often "unconfirmed" by other averages; rarely technically strong; virtually always doomed to complete retracement by wave C. Examples: 1930 (nearly everyone thought depression was over), 1961-1962 (unheard-of P/E multiples), 1966-1968 ("cheapies" mania, Dow unconfirmed), 1977 (Transports new high, unconfirmed by Industrials)
8. **C waves** — Usually devastating; third waves in character; no place to hide except cash; illusions from A and B waves evaporate; fear takes over. Examples: 1930-1932, 1962, 1969-1970, 1973-1974
9. **D waves** — In all but expanding triangles, accompanied by increased volume (hybrids: part corrective, part first-wave-like)
10. **E waves** — Appear to be dramatic kickoff of new downtrend but are actually ending waves; accompanied by strongly supportive news; tend to stage false breakdowns through triangle boundary; emotional as fifth waves

### Summary of Rules and Guidelines for Waves (pp. 86-91)
**IMPULSE Rules:**
- Always five waves; wave 1 = impulse or (rarely) diagonal; wave 3 = always impulse; wave 5 = impulse or diagonal
- Wave 2 = zigzag, flat, or combination; wave 4 = zigzag, flat, triangle, or combination
- Wave 2 never beyond start of 1; wave 3 always beyond end of 1; wave 3 never shortest; wave 4 never beyond end of 1
- Never all three (1, 3, 5) extended

**IMPULSE Guidelines:**
- Wave 4 almost always different corrective pattern from wave 2 (alternation)
- Wave 2 usually zigzag/zigzag combo; wave 4 usually flat/triangle/flat combo
- Wave 1, 3, or 5 usually extended; extension appears "stretched" with large subdivisions
- Often extended subwave is same number as parent wave
- Wave 1 least commonly extended
- When wave 3 extended → waves 1 and 5 tend toward equality or .618 ratio
- Wave 4 typically ends within price range of subwave 4 of wave 3
- Wave 4 often divides impulse into Fibonacci proportion in time and/or price

**DIAGONAL Rules and Guidelines** (pp. 87-88)
- Ending diagonal: wave 5 of impulse or wave C of flat; all subwaves are zigzags
- Leading diagonal: wave 1 of impulse or wave A of zigzag
- Wave 4 always enters wave 1 territory; converging or diverging boundary lines
- Waves 2 and 4 retrace .66 to .81 of preceding wave

**CORRECTIVE WAVE Rules and Guidelines** (pp. 89-91)
- Zigzag: A = impulse/leading diagonal, C = impulse/diagonal, B = any corrective. B never beyond start of A. Wave B retraces 38-79% of A. Wave C usually same length as A.
- Flat: A never triangle, C = impulse/diagonal, B retraces at least 90% of A. B usually 100-138% of A, C usually 100-165% of A. Expanded flat: B > 105% of A and C beyond end of A. Running flat: B > 100% of A and C doesn't reach end of A.
- Contracting Triangle: five waves, at least 4 of A-E are zigzag/combo. Wave C never beyond A, D never beyond B, E never beyond C (converging lines). Never more than one complex subwave. About 60% of time, B does not end beyond start of A (= running triangle). Wave C typically most complex.
- Barrier Triangle: same as contracting except B and D end at essentially same level.
- Expanding Triangle: C, D, E each beyond preceding same-directional subwave. B, C, D retrace 100-150% of preceding. No subwave observed to subdivide into triangle.
- Combinations: W-X-Y(-X-Z); double/triple zigzags replace zigzags; double/triple threes replace flats/triangles. No expanding triangle as component.

### Learning the Basics (pp. 92-93)
- Best learning procedure: keep hourly chart and fit all wiggles into Elliott wave patterns
- Go with the MOST valid wave count; knowledge of alternative interpretations helpful for adjusting to unexpected events
- "When you have eliminated the impossible, whatever remains, however improbable, must be the truth" — Sherlock Holmes quote applied to Elliott
- Deductive reasoning: know what rules will NOT allow → whatever remains is proper perspective
- Example: Prechter's Nov 1976 Merrill Lynch report analyzed in real time — correctly identified third Primary wave not yet complete, fifth Intermediate underway, expanding triangle in wave 4, reverse symmetrical triangle should produce rally to 1020-1030 (falling short of trendline at 1090-1100)

### Practical Application (pp. 94-97)
- Goal: identify market lows for buying and highs for selling
- Wave Principle is not a trading system but provides built-in objective method for placing stops
- "Preferred count" = interpretation satisfying most guidelines; "alternate count" = next best
- Always invest with preferred count; maintain two or three best counts
- Even when preferred count is wrong, you may recognize it at a higher level and adjust
- Three-wave rally after a turning point = sign of upward correction, warning of vulnerability at higher levels
- Wave Principle provides a built-in objective method for stop placement (pattern is either over or it isn't)
- Setting targets in advance: prepares you psychologically to buy in despair and sell in euphoria

## Chapter 3: Historical and Mathematical Background of the Wave Principle (pp. 99-130)

### Leonardo Fibonacci da Pisa (pp. 99-101)
- Born c. 1170-1180, Pisa, Italy; son of prominent merchant and city official

- Three major works: *Liber Abacci* (1202, revised 1228), *Practica Geometriae* (1220), *Liber Quadratorum*
- Greatest mathematician of the Middle Ages — Joseph Gies (Encyclopedia Britannica editor) called him one of world's great intellectual pioneers
- Only monuments: statue across Arno River from Leaning Tower, and two streets (Pisa and Florence)

### The Golden Ratio (pp. 104-108)
- After first several numbers, ratio of any number to next higher ≈ .618; to next lower ≈ 1.618
- The ratio approaches phi (φ) = .618034... (irrational number)
- Between alternate numbers: ratio ≈ .382 (inverse = 2.618)
- Phi is the ONLY number whose reciprocal equals itself minus 1: 1/.618 = 1.618; 1 + .618 = 1.618
- Key mathematical identities: .618² = 1 - .618 = .382; 1.618² = 1 + 1.618 = 2.618; 1.618 × .618 = 1
- Formula: (√5 + 1)/2 = 1.618 and (√5 - 1)/2 = .618; so √5 = 2.236 is the key
- Any Fibonacci number × 4, plus a selected Fibonacci number = another Fibonacci number (e.g., 3×4=12; +1=13)
- 10 additional properties listed, including: no two consecutive Fibonacci numbers share common factors; sum of any 10 is divisible by 11; prime Fibonacci numbers have prime sequence positions

### The Golden Section (pp. 109)

## Chapter 4: Ratio Analysis and Fibonacci Time Sequences (pp. 133-154)

### Ratio Analysis (pp. 133-135)
- Assessment of proportionate relationship (time and amplitude) of one wave to another
- Robert Rhea (1934, *The Story of the Averages*) — first data on time/amplitude ratios
- Rhea's key discovery: nine bull and nine bear markets from 1896-1932 → bear markets run 61.1% of the time required for bull periods
- Sum of all primary movements in each bull market = 1.621 × net advance → secondary reactions retrace 62.1% of the net advance — Fibonacci ratio discovered without knowing it
- Amplitude of virtually every wave is related to an adjacent, alternate, or component wave by a ratio between Fibonacci numbers

### Retracements (p. 135)
- Sharp corrections: tend to retrace 61.8% or 50% of previous wave (especially wave 2, B of zigzag, wave X)
- Leading diagonal in wave 1 → typically followed by 78.6% (√φ) zigzag retracement
- Sideways corrections: tend to retrace 38.2% of previous impulse (especially wave 4)

### Motive Wave Multiples (pp. 136-137)

- All three motive waves related by equality, 1.618, or 2.618 (and inverses .618, .382)
- Wave I (1932-1937) gained 371.6%; wave III (1942-1966) gained 971.7% = 2.618 × wave I
- Wave 5's length sometimes related to net travel of waves 1-3 by Fibonacci ratio
- When wave 1 extends → wave 2 often subdivides entire impulse into Golden Section
- Unless wave 1 extends, wave 4 often divides impulse into Golden Section: .382 and .618

## Chapter 7: Other Approaches to the Stock Market (pp. 183-198)

### Dow Theory (pp. 183-185)
- Charles Dow: primary trend = broad "tide"; secondary reactions and rallies = "waves"; minor movements = "ripples"
- Key tenet: both averages (Industrial + Transportation) must move in unison to confirm — a new extreme by one alone lacks "confirmation"
- Elliott Wave Principle has points in common: impulse waves = healthy breadth and confirmation; corrective/ending waves = divergences and non-confirmations
- Dow Theorists' three "phases" of market advance correspond to Elliott's waves 1, 3, 5
- Wave counts can forewarn the Dow Theorist of upcoming non-confirmations (e.g., May 1977 Transportation new highs while Industrials had five-wave decline → non-confirmation inevitable)
- Dow Theory = "grandfather" of Wave Principle — deserves respect for historical significance

### The Kondratieff Wave Economic Cycle (pp. 185-189)
- 50-60 year cycle of economic expansion and contraction — observed by Nikolai Kondratieff (Russian economist, 1920s)
- Corresponds to Supercycle (occasionally Cycle) degree under Wave Principle
- Wave (I) to deep low of (II) in 1842 ≈ one Kondratieff; extended (III) ≈ two Kondratieffs; (IV) ≈ most of one; (V) ≈ most of one

### Cycles (pp. 188-189)
- Fixed cycle periodicities consistently fail in practice because market reflects spiral, not circle properties
- Edward R. Dewey compiled list of cycles, and many attempted to verify fixed periodicities with negligible results
- Collins (1954): "Elliott alone among cycle theorists provided a cycle theory compatible with what actually happened in the postwar period"
- Market reflects properties of a spiral more than a circle — properties of nature more than a machine

### The Decennial Pattern (pp. 189-190)
- Edgar Lawrence Smith: average of 9 decades (1881-1970) of stock prices shows consistent pattern
- Years ending in "5" always up; years ending in "7" always down; years ending in "0" usually down
- Authors note: this average reflects a single five-wave impulse → years 1-2 (wave 1), years 2-4 (wave 2), years 4-6/7 (wave 3), years 7-8 (wave 4), years 8-10 (wave 5)

### News (pp. 190-192)
- Market appears to sense changes in the social condition before they occur — "discounts the future"
- But authors argue: it's not that investors are clairvoyant — rather, people's emotional states and trends (reflected in market prices) CAUSE them to behave in ways that ultimately affect economic statistics and politics
- "The market, for forecasting purposes, IS the news"

### Random Walk Theory (pp. 192-193)
- Academic theory: stock prices move randomly with no predictable patterns
- Existence of very successful professional traders who make hundreds of profitable decisions per year "flatly disproves" the Random Walk idea
- David Bergamini (*Mathematics*): "In reality the stock market is not a mass of random fluctuations" — the Wave Principle demonstrates the market has a nature

### Technical Analysis (pp. 193-195)
- Classical TA (support/resistance, gaps, volume, momentum) is compatible with Elliott — aids in correctly counting waves
- Head and shoulders patterns: relate to normal Elliott wave progression; neckline of H&S formation is often the lower boundary of Elliott trend channel
- Breakout gaps and continuation gaps generally accompany third waves
- After years of working with Wave Principle: applying classical TA to stock market averages feels like "use of stone tools in an age of modern technology"
- Indicators (short selling, option transactions, market opinion polls) reach extremes at end of C waves, second waves, and fifth waves
- Momentum indicators reveal ebbing of market power in fifth waves and B waves → "momentum divergences"

### The "Economic Analysis" Approach (pp. 195-197)
- Forecasting the market by forecasting the economy is doomed to fail
- "If anything, the market is a far more reliable predictor of the economy than vice versa"
- Monetary phenomena relate to stock prices in complex ways but always create Elliott pattern

### Exogenous Forces (pp. 197-198)
- Authors do not reject the possibility of exogenous forces (sunspot cycles, planetary alignments) triggering patterns
