# Your Money or Your Life — Notes
## By Vicki Robin and Joe Dominguez (2018 revised edition)

### Foreword (by Mr. Money Mustache)

- Only one reason to read a money book: to give yourself a better life. Money is not really the thing you're after — you wouldn't lock yourself in a dark, silent box forever in exchange for becoming a billionaire.
- A healthy relationship with money is a by-product of living a happy, healthy life. What you actually want is to become a lighthearted, productive, free person who never has to worry about money.
- Most money books, magazines, websites, podcasts fail because they chase more money and more things without questioning the big picture of what we're buying and why. Despite record income levels, most people remain broke with record debt.
- Standard money advice is "too meek" — claims we all have different values and should follow our hearts. Reality: we're all much more similar than different. Centuries of philosophy and decades of research reveal universal "happiness buttons": friendship, health, community, overcoming challenges, ingenuity, feeling in control.
- We're tempted by convenience, status, luxury — and we justify expensive "true passions" that can lock up a lifetime's income. Even well-known finance gurus repeat the "spending money equals happiness" myth, creating a polished conveyor belt of desire.
- YMOYL is a personal development guide disguised as a money book — helps you figure out what you really want from life while training away money-wasting habits. "Training" is the right metaphor: small daily steps build into bigger accomplishments until you look back and barely recognize your past self.
- Bigger picture: almost everything we buy comes with unseen destruction somewhere else. Streamlining your lifestyle for happiness also dramatically cuts environmental damage.
- "Make no mistake — it's almost impossible to read this book thoroughly without also drastically improving your financial situation for the rest of your life."
- The method works on the root of the problem — personal beliefs and habits — not just the symptoms of bank/credit card statements.

**Who Is This Book For?**

- Hares: like Joe, set FI goal of age 30. Sprint mentality — the more they save, the sooner they're free. Fixated on increasing savings percentage, beg forbearance from friends and spouses

- Relationships with partners and children improve

- Free time increases by reducing expenses and time on the job

**The Four FIs:**
1. Financial Intelligence (FI1) — step back from assumptions and emotions about money, observe objectively. Does money really buy happiness? Begins with knowing how much you've earned, what you have to show for it, how much is coming in and going out. Also knowing what money really is and what you're trading for it. Tangible outcome: getting out of debt and having 6 months of basic living expenses in the bank.
2. Financial Integrity (FI2) — learning the true impact of your earning and spending on family and planet. Knowing what is enough and what is excess/clutter. Having all aspects of financial life aligned with values.
3. Financial Independence (FI3) — more than secure income. Independence from crippling financial beliefs, crippling debt, crippling inability to manage modern conveniences. Anything that frees you from dependence on money to handle your life.
4. Financial Interdependence (FI4) — the independence we crave is separation from dead-end routines, not from one another. Happiest moments come from love and contribution. After achieving FI, most people actually want to spend their time making the world better.

### Chapter 1: The Money Trap: The Old Road Map for Money

**"MONEY: THE TENDER TRAP?"**
- "Your money or your life." If someone thrust a gun in your ribs, most of us would hand over our wallets — we value our lives more than money. Or do we?
- Opens with personal stories illustrating how people feel trapped by the money-work cycle:

**Rachel Z.** — worked 70 hours/week as successful saleswoman. After reading The Poverty of Affluence by Paul Wachtel, realized her feeling of "something was missing" wasn't unique. Despite comfortable home with all the trimmings, felt: "Is this all? Do I have to work and work and then retire — worn out — to spend money I saved and waste my time till my life is over?"

**Don M.** — loved music but worked in data processing. Assumed all the trappings of adulthood (wife, skill, job, car, house, mortgage, lawn) would make him feel like a man. Instead felt increasingly trapped.

**Elaine H.** — hated her job as computer programmer, did bare minimum but so well she couldn't be fired. Accumulated symbols of success (sports car, house in the country) but none balanced the boredom. Tried travel, workshops — nothing countered the workweek doldrums. Resigned herself to her job biting the center out of her life.

**Kristy** — classic DINKs (double income, no kids) in high-paying tech. Young, rich, good-looking. Then watched coworker collapse from stress, nearly die at his desk. In Japan they call it karoshi (death by overwork). That person came back a week later acting like nothing happened. Then her boss was hospitalized with a blood clot, her best friend was fired. Kristy started popping anxiety meds and bolting awake at 3 a.m. Thought: "I can't do this anymore. This isn't worth it."

**Nicole** — followed her father's script: lawyer who encouraged professional training regardless of cost. Got an advanced family nurse practitioner degree (8+ years, $100,000+ in debt). But it was 2011, not 1969 — her net earnings after paying staff, office rent, overhead, insurance, and continuing education weren't enough to pay debt faster than interest built up. "I don't think I will ever actually pay it off."

**Brian** — old friend Kevin stopped by, making six figures from online educational product business. Brian wanted to replicate it: support family, be stay-at-home dad, teach effectiveness tools. But online education has a low bar to entry and success isn't guaranteed. Brian spent money learning, buying tools, investing hours — the expensive goose "has, to date, failed to lay any golden eggs."

**The perfect work life would offer:** enough challenge to be interesting, ease to be enjoyable, camaraderie to be nourishing, solitude to be productive, enough hours to get the job done, enough leisure to feel refreshed, contribution to feel needed, silliness to have fun, and enough money to pay the bills... and then some.

- Even the best jobs have trade-offs. Midlife often reveals we've been living our parents' agenda, or that a 17-year-old's career choice (dentist? lawyer?) locked us into decades of compromise.
- "For all the hype about 'going for the gold,' we're so weary at the end of the day that going for the sofa is as good as it gets."
- There IS a way to have both: "Your money or your life?" — "I'll take both, thank you."

**"We Aren't Making a Living, We're Making a Dying"**
- For most working people, what they do for money dominates their waking hours; life is what can be fit into the scant remaining time.
- Vivid description of the average worker's day: alarm at 6:45, shower, dress in professional uniform, commute, deal with boss/coworkers/clients, hide mistakes, smile at impossible deadlines, sigh of relief when the ax of "restructuring" falls on other heads, commute home, eat, watch TV, bed. "Eight hours of blessed oblivion — if we're lucky."
- "And they call this making a living? Think about it." For many of us, isn't it closer to "making a dying"? We're killing ourselves — our health, relationships, sense of joy and wonder — for our jobs, so slowly we barely notice.
- Even those who like their jobs sense a larger arena beyond 9-to-5: doing work we love with no limitations or fear of getting fired. "How much have we had to compromise our dreams in order to keep our funding or our jobs?"

**"We Think We Are Our Jobs"**
- Even if financially able to leave jobs that limit joy and insult values, we're psychologically unable to free ourselves. We take our identity and self-worth from our jobs.
- Jobs have replaced family, neighborhood, civic affairs, church as our primary allegiance, principal source of love and self-expression.
- "What do you do?" — the getting-to-know-you question. Do you feel pride? Shame? Do you give an exotic title to a mundane occupation to increase status?
- "Jobism" — a hidden hierarchy based on what you do for money, pervading interactions on the job, in social settings, at home. Stay-at-home moms = second-class citizens. Teachers considered lower-status than doctors.
- At high school reunions we assess peers not by fulfillment but by position, residence, car, and where they send their kids to college.

**"The High Cost of Making a Dying"**
- Psychotherapist Douglas LaBier's book Modern Madness: the steady stream of "successful" professionals showing up with exhausted bodies and empty souls. Focusing on money/position/success at the expense of personal fulfillment had led 60% of his sample of several hundred to suffer from depression, anxiety, and other job-related disorders.
- 2015 OECD study: nearly 12% of Americans work 50+ hours per week.
- Conference Board study (same year): less than half of Americans are "satisfied" with their jobs.
- "We are working more, but enjoying life less. We have developed a national disease based on how we earn money."

**"What Do We Have to Show for It?"**
- Even if we aren't happier, you'd think we'd at least have savings. Not so — savings rate has gone down.
- US personal savings rate: hovered around 5% recently, up from a low of less than 2% in 2007, but down from well over 10% pre-1980.
- Stagnating wages for vast majority: Economic Policy Institute (2016) — wages for lowest 70% of workers grew only 5.3% since 2000; poorest 10% saw only 2.2% improvement. Meanwhile 90th and 95th percentile earners experienced 15.7% and 19.8% growth respectively.
- Consumer debt by late 2017: topped $3.7 trillion, more than double the total at end of 2000. That's more than $11,000 for every man, woman, and child in the country.
- Between mortgages, car financing, student loans, and credit card debt, we can't afford to quit. More Americans ending up living in cars or on the streets.

**"We Make a Dying at Work so We Can Live It Up on the Weekend"**
- Vivid weekend spending montage: groceries (4 sacks used to cost $75 instead of $125), mall trip, nursery run, home improvement store, dinner out, Sunday pancakes... the bottom line: we think we work to pay the bills, but we spend more than we need, which sends us back to work to get the money to spend — an endless cycle.

**"What About Happiness?"**
- Beyond a certain minimum of comfort, money is not buying happiness.
- In seminars, over 1,000 people from US and Canada rated happiness on a 1-5 scale (1=miserable, 5=joyous). Average happiness score was consistently between 2.6 and 2.8 (not even a 3!) regardless of whether income was under $1,500/month or over $6,000/month.
- Life Rating Scale data by monthly income: $0-1500 avg 2.81, $1501-3000 avg 2.77, $3001-4500 avg 2.84, $4501-6000 avg 2.86, over $6000 avg 2.63.
- When asked "How much money would it take to make you happy?" the answer was always "more than I have now" (by 50-100%).
- National Opinion Research Center surveys: percentage of Americans describing themselves as "very happy" has been steadily declining since the late 1950s.

**"PROSPERITY AND THE PLANET"**
- Our affluent lifestyles are having an increasingly devastating effect on the planet.
- 1987: UN World Commission on Environment and Development warned consumption patterns in the developed world were a primary engine of global environmental damage.
- The Ecological Footprint (Global Footprint Network): measures everything we buy in terms of earth's resources. Even careful, conscious North Americans would learn that if everyone consumed the way they do, we'd need four earths.
- Earth Overshoot Day (with World Wildlife Fund): the annual date when we've used up the resources the planet can renew in a single year. In 1971 it fell on December 21. Ten years later: November 12. October 11 in 1991. September 23 in 2001. August 3 in 2016. Getting earlier and earlier.
- With ecological debt, we can't just start filling the coffers again — we have only one planet.

**"The Biggest Loser in the Money Game"**
- Annie Leonard's short film The Story of Stuff: our appetite for things drives a one-way trip from earth to factory to store to house to dump.
- We've ignored that our affluence rests on the free graces of nature — soil, water, air — now being taxed to the limit.
- Corporations have little incentive to encourage less consumption. Politically it's suicidal to propose consumption-limiting legislation.
- The only way green corporations/governments/nonprofits have changed us is by making comfort, control, and convenience better served by products that demand less from the earth (e.g., solar energy became competitive, EV charging stations).
- Robert Ornstein and Paul Ehrlich, New World New Mind: our minds were designed for short-term threats (tigers, fires). Piling debts and environmental threats build up too slowly for our threat-detection systems to register.

**"Is More Better?"**
- The pervasive consumer myth: more is better. We build our working lives on this. Our expectation is to make more money as years go on, have more possessions, more prestige, more respect.
- The "more is better" mentality rooted in the materially expansive frontiersman mentality. Even Star Trek called space "the final frontier." Manifest Destiny as a political sales pitch.
- "More is better" leads us to trade in cars every three years, buy new clothes for every event and season, upgrade everything from TV to smartphone just because a new version exists.
- "More is better" turns out to be a formula for dissatisfaction. In an environment of more, "enough" is always receding. You lose the ability to identify the point of sufficiency. This is a psychological cul-de-sac, an invisible catch-22.

**"The Limits to Growth"**
- Economy's version: "growth is good." Modern economics worships growth. Growth will solve poverty, reduce unemployment, keep us apace with inflation.
- What we overlook: all feedstock for economic growth comes from nature, which is not infinitely abundant. Nothing in nature grows forever — every plant, animal, population reaches a maximum and then stabilizes or declines.
- By ignoring this fundamental reality, we as individuals and our economy are exceeding the earth's capacity.

**"The Cleopatra Explanation"**
- Pam Tillis: "Just call me Cleopatra, everybody, 'cause I'm the Queen of Denial."
- Catalog of excuses for not changing: technology will save us, the government will fix it, it's a Third World problem, scientists have lied before, I'm in debt so what can I do...
- "As people and as a planet we suffer from upward mobility and downward nobility."

**"The Creation of Consumers"**
- We cling to affluence because of the very nature of our relationship with money. Money is the movie screen on which our lives play out — we project onto it the capacity to fulfill fantasies, allay fears, soothe pain.
- People in industrialized nations used to be called "citizens." Now we are "consumers" — which means (by dictionary definition) people who "use up," "waste," "destroy," and "squander."
- Consumerism is a 20th-century invention, created when encouraging people to buy more goods was seen as necessary for continued economic growth.
- By the early 1920s, the Industrial Revolution's machinery had so successfully filled human needs that economic activity slowed. Workers instinctively knowing they had enough were asking for shorter workweeks and more leisure. Two sectors alarmed: moralists (Protestant work ethic — "idle hands do the devil's work") and industrialists (reduced demand = halted growth).
- The alternative to growth was seen not as maturity but as stagnation and death of productivity. Stroke of genius: the same populace would be the new market — educated to want not only what they needed but also new things they didn't need. Enter the concept of "standard of living."
- 1929: Herbert Hoover's Committee on Recent Economic Changes: "wants are almost insatiable; one want satisfied makes way for another... economically we have a boundless field before us."
- Leisure was transformed from "relaxed activity" into an opportunity for increased consumption. Henry Ford: "Where people work less they buy more... business is the exchange of goods."
- The Hoover Commission agreed: leisure was a hole to fill up with more wants (requiring more work to pay for them).
- Consumerism promoted all the deadly sins (lust, covetousness, gluttony, pride, envy) except perhaps anger and sloth.
- Only temporarily subdued by the Depression, consumerism returned post-WWII. 1955 US retailing analyst Victor Lebow: "Our enormously productive economy demands that we make consumption our way of life, that we convert the buying and use of goods into rituals, that we seek our spiritual satisfaction, our ego satisfaction, in consumption.... We need things consumed, burned up, worn out, replaced, and discarded at an ever-increasing rate."
- With Internet and smartphones, we can now be consumers wherever and whenever. From desire to delivery: just a few swipes, taps, or clicks away.

**"The Right to Buy"**
- Americans deeply believe it is our right to consume. "Born to shop. Whoever dies with the most toys wins. Life, liberty, and the pursuit of material possessions."
- We believe consuming keeps America strong — if we don't consume, factories shut down, unemployment rises. Consumer Confidence Index as benchmark for America's dominance.
- Saving has become un-American. The language itself promotes consumption: "disposable" income — what else would we do with it besides dispose of it?
- Average American child aged 2-11 sees more than 25,000 advertisements per year. Globally, ad spending is well over $500 billion.
- Marketing theory: people are driven by fear, exclusivity, guilt, greed, and need for approval. Advertising uses market research and sophisticated psychology to throw us off balance emotionally, then promises to resolve our discomfort with a product.

**"THE BEGINNING OF A NEW ROAD MAP FOR MONEY"**
- What makes consuming so all-consuming? The advertising industry conspired to sell us materialism, and we bought it. Why?
- Money is the "last taboo" — easier to tell our therapist about our sex life than to tell our accountant about our finances. Money governs our lives as much as or more than any other factor, yet it rarely comes up as a legitimate topic between friends or partners.

**"Patterns of Belief"**
- The mind is a pattern-making and pattern-repeating device. Patterns come from personal experience (first 5 years of life), genetics, culture. Once recorded and deemed useful for survival, they become very hard to change.
- Not all patterns correspond to objective reality, yet they persist and govern our behavior. Financial beliefs may be no more reality-based than the flat-Earth theory.

**"What Do Our Actions Say?"**
- While we might say "money can't buy happiness" and "the best things in life are free," our behavior tells a different story.
- When depressed, lonely, unloved → we buy something. When celebrating → we buy something. When bored → we buy something. When we think there must be more to life → we buy something (a self-help book, a workshop, a house in the country).
- "None of this is wrong. It's just what we do." We've learned to seek external solutions to signals from the mind, heart, or soul that something is out of balance. We try to satisfy psychological and spiritual needs with consumption at a physical level.

**The Fulfillment Curve (FIGURE 1-2) — KEY CONCEPT:**
- X-axis: Money Spent. Y-axis: Fulfillment.
- The curve rises steeply through Survival (basic needs met — food, warmth, shelter). Then through Comforts (niceties beyond bare necessities — toys, wardrobe, bicycle). Then through Luxuries. At the peak: ENOUGH. After that, the curve declines — more spending actually decreases fulfillment, heading toward an R.I.P. tombstone.
- In the beginning, more possessions did mean more fulfillment. As babies, our minds recorded: Need → Look outside yourself → Get stuff → Get fulfillment.
- Then we learned: "Those things cost money, dear. Money that we go out and earn for you — because we love you." New rule: Need → Look outside yourself → Get money → Get stuff → Get fulfillment.
- Eventually we slipped beyond niceties to outright luxuries and hardly noticed. A car is a luxury that the vast majority of the world never enjoys — but for us, it's the beginning of a lifelong love affair. Each new luxury was still a thrill, but it cost more per thrill and the "high" wore off more quickly.
- Then into adult life: house, job, family. More money brought more worry, more time/energy commitments, more taxes, more bills. Until one day we found ourselves unfulfilled in our big home with three-car garage and expensive exercise equipment, yearning for the life we had as poor college students who could find joy in a walk in the park.
- We hit a fulfillment ceiling and never recognized that the formula of money = fulfillment had not only stopped working but started to work against us. No matter how much we bought, the fulfillment curve kept heading down.

**"Enough: The Peak of the Curve"**
- The peak is called "enough." Part of the secret to life comes from identifying for yourself that point of maximum fulfillment.
- "Enough" is a fearless, trusting, honest, self-observant place. It's appreciating and fully enjoying what money brings into your life and yet never purchasing anything that isn't needed and wanted.
- Once you discover what is enough for you, your fulfillment curve can reverse direction and head straight up.
- "Enough" is not deprivation. It is not "less is more." It's the Goldilocks feeling of just-rightness — having enough of everything you want and need, but nothing in excess. Everyone's enough looks different.

**"Clutter: A Fate Worse Than Dearth"**
- Everything beyond the peak of enough is clutter — anything you have that doesn't serve you, whatever is excess for you, yet takes up space in your world.
- Letting go of clutter is not dearth (lack); it's lightening up and opening space for something new.
- Downscaling, frugality, and thrift sound like deprivation only because we've been conditioned. Enough is actually a place of alertness, creativity, and freedom.

**"Stations of the Crass" — the "gazingus pin" concept:**
- A "gazingus pin" is any item you just can't pass by without buying. Everyone has them — from earbuds to tiny screwdrivers to shoes to chocolate kisses.
- Vivid description of the unconscious buying cycle: you come to the gazingus pin section, your mind starts cranking out justifications, "an alien arm" reaches out and picks it up, you go to checkout like a "windup zombie," arrive home, put it in the gazingus pin drawer (where there are already five or ten others), and forget about it until the next shopping trip.

**"Faces and Functions of Clutter"**
- Physical clutter: attics, basements, garages, closets, storage sheds — havens of projects we'll never finish and products we'll never use.
- Activity clutter: meaningless power lunches, cocktail parties, social events, long evenings glued to screens. Disorganized days full of busyness with no accomplishment.
- Mental clutter: cluttered motives, unplanned errands, hobby equipment with low use-to-stuff ratio (photography buffs with suitcases of lenses who get best shots with smartphones).
- Simple living tradition in American psyche: Puritans, Thoreau, Quakers, Duane Elgin, Cecile Andrews. Now called minimalism or "tidying up."

**STEP 1: Making Peace with the Past**
- Purpose: increase awareness (not arrogance or shame). Locate yourself in time and space. Review — without blame — your earning and spending activity in the past.

**Part A: How Much Have You Earned in Your Life?**
- Find out the sum total of your gross income, from the first penny to the most recent paycheck.
- Sources to check: old income-tax returns, tips, under-the-table jobs, informal consulting, gambling winnings, gifts from relatives, money stolen, cash prizes, rent collected, summer jobs in high school and college.
- Social Security Administration has kept a record of lifetime earnings for salaried employees.
- Checklist of income sources: Social Security statement, income tax returns, checkbook records, old statements, gifts, winnings, loans, capital gains, illegal sources, contract labor not reported to the IRS.

**The Value of Step 1:**
1. Clears the fog about your past relationship with money — most people have no idea how much has entered their lives
2. Eradicates myths and false self-concepts like "I can't earn very much money" or "I don't have to worry; I can always earn lots of money"
3. Gets you to ground zero with a clear head and confidence in your wage-earning ability
4. Allows you to see and let go of skeletons — secrets or lies distorting your current relationship with money

**Anecdote:** A divorced woman in her mid-thirties who attended a seminar. Suburban housewife most of her adult life, she felt dependent, unworldly, unworthy. She "knew" step 1 didn't apply — she'd made no financial contribution to her marriage. Was ashamed of accepting the divorce settlement. But after digging in, she discovered she'd earned more than $50,000 from assorted odd jobs during those years. For the first time she saw herself as a competent wage earner. This gave her the confidence to apply for and land a job at twice the salary she'd assumed she could get.

**Useful Attitudes:**
- "No shame, no blame" — the mantra for the whole program. Difference between recrimination (shame/blame) and discrimination (sorting true from false). Recrimination immobilizes; discrimination illuminates.
- "Your lifetime earnings represent just a number, not your value."
- Impeccability — accuracy and accountability in every step. Go for full-frontal integrity, not just "close enough."

**Part B: What Have You Got to Show for It?**
- Calculate net worth: total assets minus total liabilities.
- This is what you currently have to show for your total lifetime income; the rest is memories and illusions.

**Liquid Assets:** cash on hand, savings accounts, checking accounts, CDs, US savings bonds, stocks, bonds, mutual funds, money market funds, brokerage account balance, life insurance cash value.

**Fixed Assets:** market value of major possessions (house, car). Go through attic, basement, garage, storage shed — itemize everything worth more than a dollar. Assign current cash value (what you could get at a garage sale or online auction, not what you paid).
- Don't ignore anything. Don't let sentimentality, grief, embarrassment, or guilt deter you from putting a price on everything.
- One woman took three months going through every box, photo, drawer, and cupboard. The process led her into a deep experience of gratitude for what she already had. "Once we're above the survival level, the difference between prosperity and poverty lies simply in our degree of gratitude."

**Liabilities:** all debts — loans, bills outstanding, mortgage balance, car payments, bank loans, loans from friends, credit card debts, student loans, unpaid medical and dental bills.

**Net Worth:** liquid assets + fixed assets − liabilities = current net worth. Does not include nonmaterial assets (education, skills, goodwill, relationships). "Net worth does not equal self-worth."

**Why Do a Balance Sheet?**
- Until now, your financial life has had little direction or consciousness — "like someone driving around without any destination—burning gas, spinning your wheels, and getting nowhere."
- One person realized she could liquidate excess possessions, invest the proceeds, and have enough interest income to be immediately FI.
- Another sold possessions he'd been keeping "just might need them someday," set aside the proceeds to replace any he actually needed. His money earned interest, life became simpler, and someone who really needed the items got use of them.

**Summary of Step 1:**
A. Find out how much money you have earned in your lifetime.
B. Create a balance sheet of your assets and liabilities. What do you have to show for the money you've earned?

### Chapter 2: Money Ain't What It Used to Be—and Never Was

**Opening story — Ondrea and Kyle W.:**
- Kyle, 22, idealist, "allergic" to money. Long hair, small room in rural area, considered deep conversation the best entertainment. Despite avoiding money, accumulated $15,000 debt planned to pay off "someday."
- Ondrea, over $40,000 in debt. Had equated being on her own with accumulating possessions and going into debt. "Play now, pay later" — worked part-time as administrative assistant to cover immediate expenses and pursue personal growth.
- After getting married, Kyle's "I do" nearly quadrupled his debt. Doing Step 1 forced them to face a net worth of minus $55,000 — and a new way of life was born for both of them.

**What IS money?**
- We can't have an effective working relationship with money if we don't know what it is — or worse, when we identify it as something it is not. Without a universally true definition, our handling of money is "anywhere from inept to insane."

**Joe Dominguez's seminar performance — "What is money?":**
- Joe would come onstage in his best Wall Street costuming, pace the front of the stage, focus on the biggest, toughest-looking guy (Joe was barely five feet seven) and bark: "How big is yours?"
- After the shocked silence: "What are you all thinking? I'm just asking him how big his paycheck is. Isn't that really the most personal question you can ask a man?"
- Then he'd take out a hundred-dollar bill, pull on it ("Pretty strong paper"), take out a lighter and threaten to burn it. The room would gasp and lurch forward. He'd snap the lighter shut.
- "What was that reaction? Why would burning this piece of paper make you react? Clearly money is more than pieces of paper or metal."

**Audience guesses about what money is — Joe shoots each one down:**
1. "A means of exchange" — only when both parties agree on its value. It's fiat currency, not even worth the paper it's printed on. (Piranha-infested island story: cannibals in a canoe don't recognize your money — they recognize you as dinner.)
2. "A store of value" — truthy but not true. Bubbles burst, governments fail, hyperinflation sets in. Enron pensions tanked in 2001, Great Recession of 2008, Madoff Ponzi scheme 2009. Stephen Colbert's term "truthiness" — it's not the stuff, it's what the stuff means.
3. "Money is status" — sometimes, but other qualities (appearance, intellect, family reputation) can confer more status than ostentatious wealth.
4. "It's power" — you can buy favors, influence, lobbyists. But Gandhi, MLK, and "the little guy with integrity" stories show money is no guarantee of power.
5. "Money is evil / root of all evil" — No, the LOVE of money is the root of all evil, not money itself.
6. Other guesses: "It's a tool of repression." "It's not fair." "It's a mystery." "It's not important." "It's abundant." "It's how we keep score."

**Joe's conclusion — Money = Life Energy:**
- "The only thing you can say that is always true for you, one hundred percent of the time: Money is something you trade your life energy for."
- You sell your time for money. Ned over there sells his time for a hundred dollars and you sell yours for twenty an hour — Ned's money is irrelevant to you. The only real asset you have is your time. The hours of your life.
- "You're born. You have about eighty-eight hundred hours in a year. Maybe six hundred and fifty thousand hours before you die. You'll easily spend half of them sleeping and keeping your body fed, clothed, and reasonably comfortable. Maybe you're already halfway through your life, meaning you're down to a hundred and fifty thousand hours left to spend."
- Joe and Vicki lived on less than $800 a month each. They'd formed the New Road Map Foundation, a charitable foundation giving away all seminar proceeds.

**"A Simple Summary of What Money Is — and Is Not" — four levels:**
1. Material — pieces of paper, metal, plastic. Daily transactions of earning, spending, banking, investing. What most people think of as money most of the time.
2. Psychological — your fears and longings. Your personality (big spender or tightwad?). Who influenced your feelings about money?
3. Cultural — beliefs embedded in laws and customs. More is better, growth is good, winners and losers, private property, rewards and punishments.
4. Life energy — something you trade the hours of your life for.

- The first three layers are real and important — but not always. The last (life energy) is what transforms your relationship with money, shining a bright light on all false notions.
- "Knowing that money is life energy puts you in the driver's seat of your money life. How much of my life am I willing to sell to have money in my pocket?"
- This program does not promise more or less money or stuff. It only promises to transform your relationship with money.
- Joe's performance: getting on his knees begging money to love him, shrinking from the "evil" hundred-dollar bill, holding it out like a carrot and running after it but never grasping it. "This is what your relationship with money looks like! If you were money, would you hang out with you?"

**Money is also like water** — it flows, and as it flows, it nourishes life. We feel happy when it flows our way, distressed when it doesn't. Knowing money is life energy doesn't flatten our emotional responses; examining its role can actually make our experience richer.

**Money as a game we all play:**
- We are all in the money game. Few of us could live a day without touching something money has touched. The game feeds on players being involved — if players lose interest, the game collapses, which horrifies us. So: buy something, feed the economy, everyone wins if we all play.
- The game comes with bogeymen: inflation, cost of living, recession, depression. If the economist-gods announce a recession, we might skip vacation even with plenty of money. If cost of living goes up, we automatically feel poorer even when what's now included (cell phones) was a luxury a few decades ago.
- The Matrix analogy: money as a simulated reality pulled over our eyes to blind us from the truth. Morpheus: "You take the blue pill, the story ends... You take the red pill, you stay in Wonderland." Knowing money is life energy is like taking the red pill.
- Winning isn't having the most toys. It's having precisely what you need and nothing in excess, and being able to stop playing the game at will.

**"Your Life Energy"**
- What does "money = life energy" mean to you? Money is something you consider valuable enough to devote easily a quarter of your allotted time on earth getting, spending, worrying about, fantasizing about. Ultimately YOU are the one who determines what money is worth to you. You "pay" for money with your time. You choose how to spend it.
- Life energy is more real than money in our actual experience. Money has no intrinsic reality; life energy does — it's tangible, finite, limited, irretrievable, and precious.

**"A FIRST LOOK AT FINANCIAL INDEPENDENCE"**
- FI at the material level conjures images of making a killing, inheriting a fortune, winning the lottery, cruises, jewels, Porsches. This is FI as an unreachable fantasy of inexhaustible riches.
- "Rich" exists only in comparison. John Stuart Mill: "Men do not desire to be rich, only to be richer than other men." As soon as rich becomes available to the likes of us, it will no longer be rich.
- The book's definition of FI: the experience of having enough — and then some. Enough is found at the peak of the fulfillment curve. It is quantifiable. The old notion of FI as "being rich forever" is not achievable. Enough is. Enough may be different for you than for your neighbor, but it's a real, reachable figure.

**"Financial and Psychological Freedom"**
- First step toward having enough is liberating your mind from preexisting attitudes toward money. Until you do this, no amount of money will free you.
- Once free from unconsciously held assumptions, guilt, resentment, envy, frustration, and despair about money: you have those feelings the way you have an article of clothing — you can try it on but are free to take it off.
- No longer compelled by parental/social messages about how you "should" relate to money. No longer intimidated by financial professionals. Never buy things you don't want or need. Immune to the seductiveness of malls, markets, and media.
- "The broken record in your mind stops, the one that calculates hours till quitting time, days till payday, paydays till you have a down payment for a motorcycle... The silence, at first, is thundering."
- When FI, money isn't something that happens TO you but something you include in your life in a purposeful way. The normal drama of nine-to-five, making a dying, getting ahead — all these brass rings we automatically reach for — can be seen as just one series of choices among many.
- "Financial Independence is freedom from the fog, fear, and fanaticism so many of us feel about money."

**STEP 2: Being in the Present — Tracking Your Life Energy**
- Now that you know money = life energy, Step 2 brings this into your daily life.
- Two parts:
  - A. Establish the actual costs in time and money required to maintain your job(s), and compute your real hourly wage.
  - B. Keep track of every cent that comes into or goes out of your life.

**Part A: How Much Are You Trading Your Life Energy For?**
- Most people's calculation is unrealistic: "I earn $1,000/week, work 40 hours, so I trade one hour for $25." It's not that simple.
- Key question: If you didn't need that money-earning job, what time expenditures and monetary expenses would disappear from your life?
- Categories of hidden job costs (with hypothetical weekly figures):
  1. **Commuting**: 7.5 hours/week, $100/week (gas, maintenance, parking, tolls)
  2. **Costuming**: 1.5 hours/week, $25/week (work wardrobe, grooming, cosmetics — not what you'd wear on days off)
  3. **Meals**: 5 hours/week, $50/week (coffee breaks, lunches out, meals ordered because too tired to cook, latte "rewards")
  4. **Daily decompression**: 5 hours/week, $30/week (time to decompress after work — TV, beer, ranting about the job to whoever will listen)
  5. **Escape entertainment**: 5 hours/week, $40/week ("It's been such a heavy week, let's blow it off!" — reward spending for surviving a boring job. Distinguished from genuine art/culture absorption which is about elevation, not escape)
  6. **Vacations**: 5 hours/week, $30/week (if vacationing = time-out to recover so you can get back into the fight, that's a job expense. Prorated over 52 weeks)
  7. **Job-related illness**: 1 hour/week, $25/week (stress-induced sickness; Greater Good Science Center research: happiness strengthens immune system, reduces chronic illness. Volunteers have less illness than paid employees)
  8. **Other job-related expenses**: hired help (housekeeper, gardener, nanny you wouldn't need without a job), day care, educational programs, professional magazines, conferences, life coach

**Figure 2-2: Life Energy vs. Earnings — What Is Your Real Hourly Wage?**
- Basic job: 40 hours/week, $1,000/week = $25/hour apparent wage
- Total adjustments: +30 hours/week, −$300/week in job-related expenses
- Actual: 70 hours/week, $700/week = **$10/hour real wage** (before taxes!)
- Corollary: every dollar you spend represents 6 minutes of your life energy. "Is this item worth 120 minutes of my life energy? Is it worth the two hours sitting in traffic, driving to work, or hunting down future clients?"
- Intangibles not even counted: time spent planning career strategies, deteriorating family life due to job demands, maintaining a lifestyle in line with the job.

**Anecdote — Mark H.:** Project manager in construction, unhappy for ten years but thought "income equaled expenses, that's life in the big city." After doing Step 2, discovered nearly half of what he made was spent ON the job (gas, oil, repairs, lunches). "In short, I could stay home, work part-time where I live, and actually save money by making half of what I formerly made." Realized he could quit and train for the career he'd always wanted (counselor/therapist). His wife could survive on her paycheck from teaching kids with special needs. "We're actually feeling less stress because we're focused on healing our crazy relationship with money, not just the bucks."

**Why Do This Step?**
1. Puts paid employment into real perspective — shows what you're actually getting paid
2. Allows you to assess current and future jobs realistically. A job with longer commute or more costuming might be LESS remunerative than one with a lower salary.
3. Clarifies motives for working and selecting jobs. Mark H.'s story is not anomalous — many people spend all their income maintaining their job.
4. Another FIer found this step increased his consciousness of unnecessary job expenses so much that his net earnings per hour DOUBLED — he began bringing lunch, switched to mass transit, reevaluated need for stylish clothes, started walking with wife for exercise.
5. Someone else used it as a criterion for accepting/rejecting jobs — could immediately see whether a job was worth the life energy.

**Part B: Keep Track of Every Cent That Comes Into or Goes Out of Your Life**
- Now become conscious of the movement of life energy (money) in every moment. Track income and expenses on a daily basis.
- Many people separate "money" and "love-truth-beauty-spirituality" into two boxes. Families fall deeper into debt because calling each other out on spending seems unloving. Friends never get back loaned money because insisting feels nasty. Root thought: "Money is money and love is love and never the twain shall meet."

**"A Spiritual Discipline"**
- Tracking every cent is presented as a spiritual discipline, like watching the breath in meditation. "Instead of watching your breath, you watch your money."
- No official notebook or app required. Methods: pocket memo book, appointment book section, phone/computer with bank-linked apps, debit cards paid off monthly, whatever works best for you.

**Anecdotes about tracking approaches:**
- **Carolyn H.** — natural tracker, but her husband is not. Compromise: they use round numbers, track cash as best they can, allow for an "unaccounted-for cash" category. Seeing a large number in that category motivated her husband to be more aware far more effectively than any argument or FI reading ever did.
- **Mike L.** — "every penny" stickler who led voluntary simplicity groups. When he became his own contractor for a fixer-upper house, daily transactions shot up. He decided to use a debit card and let the bank send data to his financial program until spending settled.

Cut food expense category by nearly 50%, saving hundreds of dollars a month. Those savings quickly added up to cover a three-week trip to Europe. "Being more efficient with my money meant I was happier overall."

**"Every Cent?... But Why?"**
- Best way to become conscious of how money actually comes and goes vs. how you think it does.
- We reverse the old adage to "penny wise, pound foolish" — agonizing over a $75 purchase while small daily purchases leak far more.
- Why every cent? To establish important lifelong habits. The "fudge factor" is a slippery slope — round to the dollar, then to major expenses only, then to the nearest thousand. Like dieting: break it with a buttered muffin and by evening you're gobbling a carton of ice cream and pound cake.
- Credit cards make it easier to spend carelessly. If having trouble tracking, try going "plastic-free" for a month — withdraw cash from bank and track spending that way.
- Reference to The Millionaire Next Door (Stanley and Danko): people with high net worth relative to income KNOW how much they spend on clothes, travel, housing, transportation. Those who don't achieve high net worth have NO IDEA how much they spend.

**Useful Attitudes:**
- "No leeway" — a telescope with one lens just a wee bit out of alignment still doesn't allow you to see the stars. A shift in attitude from laxity to accuracy, precision, and impeccability. People have lost weight, kept desks cleared, and patched up broken relationships through this step. "Integrity is integrity."
- "No judgment — lots of discernment." Judgment = blaming in terms of good and bad. Discernment = sorting true from false, wheat from chaff. Through writing down every cent, you begin to discern which expenses are fitting/fulfilling and which are unnecessary/extravagant/embarrassing. Discernment connects to the higher faculty that knows the truth and recognizes what we really want before we die. "Aligning your spending with this faculty is the key to Financial Integrity."

**Summary of Step 2:**
1. Establish accurately and honestly how much money you are trading your life energy for, and discover your real hourly wage.
2. Learn about your money behavior by keeping track of every cent that comes into or goes out of your life.

### Chapter 3: Where Is It All Going?

**Not a Budget — an Awareness Tool:**
- This program is the opposite of a budget. A budget is a planning tool based on standardized categories (Housing 25-35%, Food 20%, Health Care 5-10%, save 5%). This program is an "awareness of your enough point" tool — about your uniqueness, not fitting into standardized categories.
- Example: Justin, trading 10 hours of household work for a room in an older woman's home. Saving for a camper. Plans to travel the US, couch-surfing and blogging. Where does foraging go — Food? Entertainment? Not everyone's squiggly life fits into the straight lines of a spreadsheet.
- Analogy to mindful eating vs. dieting. Diets impose change from outside in. Mindful eating: slow down, pay attention to actual hunger, taste your food, pause to see if you've had enough. Inside-out, guided by the body's well-being.
- Bob Schwartz's Diets Don't Work! — four rules: (1) Eat when hungry, (2) Eat what your body wants, (3) Eat each bite consciously, (4) Stop when your body has had enough.
- This financial program parallels mindful eating: (1) Follow internal signals, not external admonishments. (2) Observe and adjust patterns over the long term, not short term. Based on consciousness, fulfillment, and choice — not budgeting or deprivation.

**"NO SHAME, NO BLAME"**
- Reminder of the mantra when confronting the truth about your choices. "How fortunate to be able to do this yourself, instead of being audited by the IRS."

**Anecdote — Anita C.:** shopaholic addicted to clothes and costume jewelry. Compulsive mall visits just to see what was on sale. The ritual of shopping made her feel OK about herself. After starting the program, she continued shopping until it no longer felt good to have so much and not wear it. As interim measure, she gave excess away as presents. "Slowly the desire to shop weakened." One day at a department store surveying sweaters: "Is this going to be what I do with my life? Is this what it's all going to be about? What am I doing? I have enough already!" She left empty-handed. "Sometime after that experience, Anita discovered that she had lost her desire to shop." If she'd been working with a standard budget, she might never have recognized her shopping as a compulsion — just remained a "social shopper" in denial.

**Step 3: Monthly Tabulation**
- After a month of tracking (Step 2), establish spending categories that reflect the uniqueness of YOUR life (not oversimplified budget-book categories).
- Within each major category, find and separate important subcategories that give a vastly more accurate picture of spending. These subcategories become an encyclopedia of your unique spending habits — perhaps the most accurate description of your lifestyle to date.
- "This detailed portrait of your life is your true bottom line. Forget the mythology of your life. Forget the story you tell yourself and others."

**Establishing Categories — detailed guidance:**

**Food:** Don't just have one category. Possible subcategories: "Just Us" (home cooking), "Guests" (entertaining), Restaurants (which can further parse into "Too Tired to Cook" vs. "Special Occasions"), Snacking, Coffee breaks, TV food, healthy food vs. junk food. "When you throw your hands up in disgust, crying, 'Where does it all go? I hardly buy anything!' you can answer yourself in a firm and steady tone, 'It goes to the candy machine on the third floor of my office building.'"

**Housing:** Mortgage/rent, utilities, mortgage deduction. Rule of thumb used to be 25% of income — but people often discover housing eats 40% or more. Creative solutions cited: tech couple spending summers in a costly city and wintering on cheap out-of-the-way beaches; a single woman paying off her mortgage by living in her camper on a friend's land and renting out her house; a retired woman becoming an in-demand house sitter. "See why the standard budget book categories don't work anymore?"

**Clothing:** Distinguish between utility (everyday home life), fashion (work wardrobe, desire never to appear in same outfit twice), and specialized recreational apparel. One doctor found 20% of his income was consistently unaccounted for — discovered a shoe fetish (golf shoes, running shoes, boating shoes, hiking shoes, climbing shoes, ski shoes, etc. — rarely wore anything but around-the-house slip-ons). As of 2015, US footwear industry exceeded $64 billion in sales. Consumer Reports survey: average American woman owns 19 pairs of shoes — only 4 worn regularly.

**Transportation:** Subcategories can save hundreds. Reflect on WHY you own a car — convenience, status, necessity? Consider ridesharing, car-sharing, walkability. What percentage of costs come from wrong location? Review auto insurance — how much is habit, convention, and scare tactics?

**Technology:** Cell phones, Internet, cable, tablets, smart watches — rapidly becoming a substantial spending category. With subcategories (Landline, Cell Phone, Equipment) you can see patterns more clearly. Distinguish work tech from leisure tech.

**Entertainment:** Break out streaming services, music streaming, home entertainment center (site of consumption — upgrading tech → upgrading furniture → repainting). Do you go clubbing? Rent entertainers for parties? What portion of services/gadgets for kids are Entertainment vs. Babysitting? Should you have a category called "Children"?

**Refining Your Categories:**
- Distinguish job-related expenses from other expenses within each category (e.g., Transportation: commuting vs. pleasure mileage)
- Medical subcategories: sickness, wellness, health insurance, prescription drugs, nonprescription drugs
- Handle "unusual" expenses: after a year of hearing "this was an unusual month," they realized every month IS unusual. Extraordinary expenses are a continuing part of life.
- Also establish subcategories for income: wages/salaries/tips vs. interest/dividend income vs. "side hustle" vs. "Selling Off My Clutter" (eBay, Yard Sales, Antiques) vs. Investment Income

**"TOTALING IT ALL UP"**
- At end of month, transfer each entry from tracking system into Monthly Tabulation. Add up income columns for total monthly income. Add up expenditure subcategories. Sum all expenditure categories for total monthly expenses.
- Find a system that works for you — personalized spreadsheet, note card in purse, bank statement charts, online platforms linking bank and credit card accounts. The key is finding YOUR system.

**THE BALANCING ACT**
- End-of-month balancing: count cash in wallet/piggy bank, note checking and savings balances. If records are accurate: $ at START + $ IN − $ OUT = $ at END.
- The difference between what you should have and what you actually have is your monthly error. When it's consistently zero, you've mastered Step 2.
- Figure 3-2: Sample end-of-month balance. $ at START: $1,931.34 (cash $103.13 + checking $383.60 + savings $1,444.61). + $ IN: $2,622.23 = $4,553.57. − $ OUT: $1,996.86. = $ you should have: $2,556.71. Actual $ at end: $2,555.29. Monthly error: $1.42. Savings this month: $623.95.

**"MAKING MONEY REAL"**
- The magic key: translate dollars spent into hours of life energy using your real hourly wage.
- Formula: Money spent in category ÷ Real hourly wage = Hours of life energy
- Example: $80 on magazines ÷ $10/hour real wage = 8 hours of life energy. That pile of unread magazines = a whole day of work! Those magazines drain energy three times: earning the money, staying up to read them, feeling guilty when unfinished by the time the next issue arrives.
- Housing example: $1,500/month ÷ $10/hour = 150 hours/month. If you work a standard 40-hour week, your housing costs eat up almost every hour you put in on your job. Every working hour goes to paying for a house you get to enjoy perhaps 2-3 hours a day.
- Convert every row of your Monthly Tabulation from dollars to hours of life energy.

5. Add up total monthly income and total monthly expenses. Balance ($ at start + income − expenses ± error = $ at end)
6. Convert dollars spent in each subcategory into hours of life energy

- Evaluate spending by asking three questions about each subcategory:

**Question 1 in depth:**
- Reveals where spending is automatic, addictive — life energy flows one direction while fulfillment lies in the opposite.

**"Cheap Thrills and Deep Thrills"**
- Grandson tells grandfather: "Grandpa, I know what happiness is. It's that feeling you get right after you buy something."
- Cheap thrills = that ka-ching feeling from buying. Happiness rarely lasts — half-life of the distance from cash register to car.
- Deep thrills = achieving a dream bigger than your ego's aspirations. George Bernard Shaw in preface to Man and Superman: "This is the true joy in life, the being used for a purpose recognized by yourself as a mighty one; the being thoroughly worn out before you are thrown on the scrap heap; the being a force of Nature instead of a feverish selfish little clod."
- Cheap thrills come from external rewards. Deep thrills come from "being used for a purpose recognized by yourself as a mighty one." Cheap thrills are passing. Deep thrills are lasting. Your internal yardstick for fulfillment measures deep thrills.

**"Developing an Internal Yardstick for Fulfillment"**
- Most of us use external yardsticks: pleasing others, getting an A, beating the competition, making a top-ten list, winning, scoring big in romance or sales.
- External yardstick = looking elsewhere to see how you're doing (gleam in mother's/partner's eye, tally of votes, best-seller lists).
- True fulfillment is the long-term satisfaction from achieving a dream — not the jolt from a temporary win. If your dream is getting an A, you'll chase external rewards your whole life.

**"Enough — the Deepest Thrill"**
- Asking yourself monthly whether you got fulfillment in proportion to life energy spent awakens the natural sense of knowing when enough is enough.
- Differentiate passing fancy from real fulfillment. Recall the fulfillment curve: the point of enoughness where desires disappear because they've been completely met. Any less would be not enough; any more would be too much.
- A fulfilling car meets transportation needs perfectly, is pleasing to the eye, runs many thousands of miles, doesn't insult your wallet or values, and is reliable and pleasurable to drive. Unless a classic 1957 big-tail-fin convertible has been a lifelong dream — then it's worth every hour you'll work for it.
- Internal yardstick dismisses superficial desires — to impress others or relieve boredom — as good reasons to upgrade and tack three more years onto your working life.
- Test: the purchase leaves you satisfied, content, at peace. You stop craving because you've actually scratched the right itch.

**"Financial Integrity"**
- Having an internal yardstick = one part of Financial Integrity. You make financial choices independent of what advertising and industry have decided would be good for their business.
- Nina N.: "Before doing this evaluation she felt powerless over the money in her wallet. 'I'd walk into a store and my money would fly out of my wallet. Not literally, but that's how it felt. I couldn't stop it.'" The +/−/0 system is a form of FI — being able to "just say no" to unconscious spending.

**Question 2 in depth — Values and Life Purpose:**

Our values reflect our beliefs, but since actions reflect real motivations, our values are revealed by our behaviors, not our professed beliefs. "Do as I say, not as I do!"
- How we spend our time and money speaks volumes about who we are and what we stand for. Looking at Monthly Tabulations: what does $250 (25 hours of life energy) on eating out reveal about values? Perhaps convenience, love of good food, or social time. What about 12 hours donated to charity? 15 hours for cell phone bill?
- You may find that the values expressed in your expenditures are not the values you really want to be living. Habit, peer pressure, or boredom may have gotten the best of you.

- Joanna Macy's three directions for finding purpose: (1) Work with your passion — what you'd give your life to, (2) Work with your pain — where suffering in the world calls you to action, (3) Work with what's at hand — daily opportunities to respond to simple needs of others.

**"Measuring Our Movement Toward Purpose"**
- Viktor Frankl (Man's Search for Meaning, Holocaust survivor): beyond intellect or psychology, the factor that allowed some people to retain humanity in Nazi death camps was meaning. "The will to have meaning and purpose in life is superior to the will to have power or the will to find pleasure."

**Question 3 in depth — "How might this expenditure change if I didn't have to work for money?"**

### Chapter 5: Getting It Out in the Open

**Step 5: Making Life Energy Visible**
- Plot the results of previous steps on a graph — a clear, simple picture of your financial situation over time and the transformation of your relationship with money (life energy).

**Three keys to sustaining behavior change:**
1. Make it a habit rather than a choice — like brushing teeth
2. Be accountable to someone else — commit to an accountability partner, share monthly accounting, even give them your tracking app password
3. Chart — checking off a daily to-do list or weighing yourself daily does wonders for commitment. Charting does the same for money.

**"MAKING YOUR WALL CHART"**

- Each month: plot total monthly income and total monthly expenses in two different colors. Connect each point with a line to the previous month.
- First month = snapshot. Real learning comes month by month, year by year. The Wall Chart adds the dynamic dimension of time to the static Monthly Tabulation.

By month 2 expenses drop steeply. But this austerity isn't sustainable. By month 3, expenses rebound with a vengeance, making up for the deprivation.
- The temptation: either take up the burden of budgeting or quit.

### Chapter 6: The American Dream—on a Shoestring

**Step 6: Valuing Your Life Energy—Minimizing Spending**

**The concept of "enoughness":** No English word captures living at the peak of the fulfillment curve—always having plenty but never being burdened with excess. The word would need to evoke careful stewarding of tangible resources (time, money, possessions) coupled with joyful expansion of spiritual resources (creativity, intelligence, love).

**Simplicity vs. minimalism vs. frugality:** Simplicity and minimalism can seem austere or monastic. "Tidying up" (Marie Kondo) sparks joy but focuses on stuff, not deeper issues of time, money, and fulfillment. Frugality used to serve that function, but by mid-20th century it seemed quaint—a Depression-era relic.

**Historical roots of frugality:** A perennial ideal across times and cultures. Ancient: Socrates, Plato praised "the golden mean." Old Testament: "Give me neither poverty nor wealth, but only enough." Jesus: "Ye cannot serve both God and money." American history: Benjamin Franklin, Thoreau, Emerson, Robert Frost. Groups: Amish, Quakers, Hutterites, Mennonites. The "more is better" consumer culture is actually a Johnny-come-lately on the American scene.

**THE PLEASURES OF FRUGALITY**
- Latin root: *frugal* shares roots with *frug* (virtue), *frux* (fruit/value), and *frui* (to enjoy or have use of)
- "Frugality is *enjoying* the *virtue* of getting good *value* for every minute of your life energy and from everything you *have the use of*."
- Key distinction: frugality = high joy-to-stuff ratio. If you get one unit of joy per possession, that's frugal. If you need ten possessions to register on the joy meter, you're missing the point.
- Materialist vs. frugal person: Materialist consumes juice of five oranges as prelude to pancake breakfast. Frugal person relishes a single orange—color, texture, smell, translucence, flavor.
- Spanish word *aprovechar* captures it perfectly: to use something wisely, getting full value from life.
- "North Americans have been called materialists, but that's a misnomer. All too often it's not material things we enjoy as much as what these things symbolize: conquest, status, success, achievement."
- You don't need to possess something to enjoy it—merely use it. Frugality embraces the sharing economy.

**Frugality as sharing and community:**
- "A man's home is his castle" has devolved into McMansion feudalism. What's outside the walls of "mine" belongs to the rest of us.
- Being frugal and having enough means more is available for others—both practical and ethical.
- Sharing resources: tool libraries, lawn mower loans, Freecycle Network, Buy Nothing Project.
- Financial Interdependence (not just independence) is key to transitioning from "more is better" to "enough is enough."

**Creative frugality as self-esteem:** Saving life energy through careful consuming = self-respect. If you're 40, actuarial tables give you 356,532 hours of life energy left. Spend them well.

**ONE SURE WAY TO SAVE MONEY: Stop Trying to Impress Other People**
- Thorstein Veblen coined "conspicuous consumption" in *The Theory of the Leisure Class* (1899). Stuart Chase summarized: "People above the line of base subsistence... do not use the surplus... to live more wisely, intelligently, understandingly, but to impress other people."
- Social media makes this harder—humble-brags about exotic vacations, upscale restaurants. "If you stop trying to impress other people you will save thousands, perhaps millions of dollars."

**TEN SURE WAYS TO SAVE MONEY:**
1. **Don't Go Shopping** — If you don't go, you won't spend. Online shopping makes this harder: one-click purchases, targeted ads. 2014 survey: 75% of Americans made impulse buys. Shopping fills needs for reward, antidepressant, esteem, socializing. "Consumption seems to be our favorite high, our nationally sanctioned addiction."
2. **Live Within Your Means** — Buy only what you can pay for promptly. Buying on credit = paying 3x the price. Two sides: shiny (have everything now) vs. tarnished (pay with your life + interest).
3. **Take Care of What You Have** — Bodies, teeth, possessions. Preventive care saves thousands. Think repair rather than replace.
4. **Wear It Out** — Use things 20% longer. Extend replacement cycles.
5. **Do It Yourself** — DIY saves money and builds skills. Tina U. story: lived in Fiji where natives assumed she made everything she owned. Makerspaces putting DIY back in fashion. Heating system story: FIer researched when 3 companies gave 3 different diagnoses.
6. **Anticipate Your Needs** — Plan ahead for cheaper prices. Avoid convenience store markups and impulse buying. Parkinson's corollary: "Needs expand to encompass whatever you want to buy on impulse."
7. **Research Value, Quality, Durability, Multiple Use, and Price** — Don't just buy cheapest. $40 tool lasting 10 years beats $30 one lasting 5. One $10 item serving 4 purposes = $10 savings. Become an "expert materialist."
8. **Buy It for Less** — Comparison-shop, bargain/haggle, buy used. Cars lose 20% in first year. Thrift stores, garage sales, Craigslist, Freecycle. Running shoes story: $95 shoes for $28 by asking for discount.
9. **Meet Your Needs Differently** — Principle of substitution: hundreds of ways to meet any need. Separate needs from strategies. Donella Meadows quote: "People don't need enormous cars; they need respect... People need identity, community, challenge, acknowledgement, love, joy."
10. **Follow the Nine Steps of This Program** — The transformation, not the tips, saves money. "Self-denial and self-indulgence both yield to self-awareness."

**SAVING ON THE BASICS**
- Previous editions had "101 Sure Ways to Save Money" but times changed.
- Bloggers, YouTubers, podcasters now offer endless frugal tips.
- Harry N. story: looking at Wall Chart, reconsidered hiring house cleaner and gardener. Instead of firing them outright, reduced frequency—cleaner every other week, gardener once a month. Used savings for his kids' education fund.

**Housing:** Largest expense. Average new home grew from 1,660 sq ft (1973) to 2,687 sq ft (2015). Consider: smaller home, housemate, cohousing, renting out unused space, buying a duplex, digital nomad lifestyle. Rachel Z. story: moved to lower level of her house, rented out bedroom, rental income covered mortgage. Cara and Richard M.: artists who became apartment managers—free housing + income = quit day jobs for art.

- Environmental externalities not included in price.
- "Pogonomics Principle" — from Pogo cartoon: "We have met the enemy and he is us."
- "No shame, no blame" — we were born into consumer culture.

### Chapter 7: For Love or Money: Valuing Your Life Energy—Work and Income

**Step 7: Valuing Your Life Energy—Maximizing Income**

**Central question:** "What is work?" Common wisdom says work = what we do to make a living. But that definition robs us of life. When "work = what you do for money," free time becomes lesser.

**WHAT IS WORK? — Competing definitions:**
- E.F. Schumacher: Three purposes of work — (1) provide necessary goods/services, (2) perfect our gifts as good stewards, (3) liberate ourselves from egocentricity through service and cooperation.
- Robert Theobald: "Work is defined as something that people do not want to do and money as the reward that compensates for the unpleasantness of work."
- Studs Terkel (*Working*): Work is "about violence—to the spirit as well as to the body... about daily humiliations. To survive the day is triumph enough."
- Kahlil Gibran: "Work is love made visible."

**WORK THROUGH THE AGES:**
- **Minimum daily requirement of work:** ~3 hours/day for basic survival across human history. Marshall Sahlins (*Stone Age Economics*): Kung men in Kalahari hunted 2-2.5 days/week, ~15 hour workweek. Women gathered similar amounts. "The workweek in the old days beats today's banker's hours by quite a bit."
- Pre-industrial life was more whole—work blended into family, celebrations, play. Industrial Revolution compartmentalized life into "work" and "nonwork."
- 19th century workers fought for shorter workweek. Fell from 60 hours (turn of century) to 35 hours (Depression). Then locked at 40 hours and has crept up to 50-60.

**The Right to Life, Liberty, and the Pursuit of a Paycheck:**
- During Depression, free time = unemployment. New Deal established 40-hour week and government as employer of last resort.
- Benjamin Kline Hunnicutt (*Work Without End*): "Since the Depression, few Americans have thought of work reduction as a natural, continuous, and positive result of economic growth."
- "Growth is good" + "full employment" + "full consumption" became linked. Leisure became a commodity to consume rather than time to enjoy.
- Take Back Your Time campaign (John de Graaf). Slow Food movement.

**Work Takes On New Meaning:**
- We've lost the fabric of family, culture, community that gave meaning outside the workplace. Work has become an end in itself.
- Hunnicutt: "Meaning, justification, purpose, and even salvation were now sought in work."
- Arlie Hochschild (*The Time Bind*): Families have three jobs—work, home, and repair of relationships. Some offices are more comfortable than homes, creating guilty desire to stay at work.
- Protestant ethic: work as arena for salvation. Successful financial life = successful religious life.
- Jobs now serve function of religion ("Who am I?"), family ("Who are my people?"), and romance ("Job Charming" illusion). "We're like the princess who keeps kissing toads. Our jobs are our toads."

**DID WE WIN THE INDUSTRIAL REVOLUTION?**
- Modern workplace problems: underemployment, overwork, downsizing, ethical conflicts, retirement insecurity
- Only 7% of corporations offer defined benefit pensions; 25% offer hybrids like 401(k)s
- Conference Board (2014): majority of Americans unhappy at work. 1987: 61.1% liked jobs. 2010 low: 42.6%. Job security and health plans declined most (11+ points since 1987).

**WHAT IS THE PURPOSE OF WORK?**
- 8 categories of purpose: Earning money, Security, Tradition, Service, Learning, Power, Socializing, Time structuring
- Key insight: "In reality, there is only one purpose served by paid employment: getting paid. The other 'purposes' are other types of rewards... all equally available in unpaid activities."
- "Growth potential, communication channels, interest in work, and recognition make a job satisfying—not pay."
- US is only advanced economy without guaranteed paid vacation. Less than half of low-wage workers get any paid vacation.

**BREAKING THE LINK BETWEEN WORK AND WAGES:**
- "The real problem with work is not that our expectations are too high. It's that we have confused work with paid employment."
- Redefine "work" as any productive or purposeful activity. Paid employment = just one form.
- "Our fulfillment as human beings lies not in our jobs but in the whole picture of our lives."
- "By breaking the link, we get our life back."

**THE STUNNING IMPLICATIONS OF BREAKING THE LINK:**
1. **Redefining Work Increases Choices** — Concert pianist who became programmer and got sick. Breaking the link: "I am a teacher, but currently I'm writing computer programs to make money." Donna O. story: doctor who opened women's clinic, struggled with economics of medicine vs. values, found FI as recovery process.
2. **Redefining Work Allows You to Work from the Inside Out** — Stop selecting identity from a menu. Margaret P. story: single mom who became financial planner, got stomach pains from commission conflict. FI support group of 20 people helped each follow their hearts.
3. **Redefining Work Makes Us Life Designers, Not Just Wage Earners** — Pre-industrial = Jacks and Jills of all trades. "When we stop working for money, we may be out of a job but we are not out of work."
4. **Redefining Work Adds Life to Your Retirement** — Retirement ≠ stop working. It = stop working for money. You remain valuable.
5. **Redefining Work Honors Unpaid Activity** — Nancy's time-tracking story: tracked every 15 minutes for a week, discovered she spent most time on "unimportant" tasks (cleaning, cooking, family). Realized if paid to clean a house, it would be on her accomplishments list.

**Chapter 7 continued:**

**Aikido "unbendable arm" analogy:** Focusing on getting paid diverts energy from your calling. Like the aikido exercise—resisting (focusing on pay) loses; extending energy toward infinity (your calling) becomes unbreakable.

**Step 7 mechanics — Maximizing Income:**

**NEW OPTIONS FOR PAID EMPLOYMENT:**

**SUMMARY OF STEP 7:** Increase your income by valuing the life energy you invest in your job, exchanging it for the highest pay consistent with your health and integrity.

### Chapter 8: Catching Fire: The Crossover Point

**Step 8: Capital and the Crossover Point**

**The shift:** Steps 1-7 transformed your relationship with money. Steps 8-9 transform your relationship with your future.

**Capital defined:** Money that makes money, as opposed to money sitting in the bank. The gap between income and expenses = savings = potential capital. "Whether $50 or $500, your capital can start producing an income."

**A New Line on Your Chart: Monthly Investment Income**
- Investment income (dividends, interest, rental income, business profits) flows whether or not you go to work.
- Formula: **(capital × current long-term interest rate) / 12 = monthly investment income**
- Use 30-year US Treasury bond rate as proxy (conservative estimate of long-term returns).
- Uses 4% for examples (also the "safe withdrawal rate" in traditional financial planning).

**The 4% Rule / 25x Rule:**
- 4% withdrawal rate: withdraw 4% of capital annually = preserves capital, protects against inflation.
- Mr. Money Mustache's rule of thumb: Crossover Point = 25× annual expenses.
- Example: $36,000/year expenses → need $900,000 in assets ($36,000 × 25).
- 3% withdrawal may not cover expenses. 5% may deplete capital too quickly. 4% = Goldilocks.

**Compound interest examples:**
- $100 at 4% → $104 → $108.16 → $112.49 → $116.99 → ...
- Rule of thumb: money doubles in ~10 years at 7% return.
- At conservative 4%, $100 becomes $200 in 18 years.

**Nina's Wall Chart progression:**
- Year 1: debt, no investment income line
- Year 4 January: $215/month investment income vs $845 expenses
- Year 5 January: $350/month investment income vs <$1,000 expenses
- Year 5 February: $545/month investment income vs ~$950 expenses
- Investment income line steadily rising toward expense line = approaching Crossover Point


**Chapter 8 continued:**

**WHERE DO YOU ACTUALLY PUT YOUR SAVINGS?**
- First: build liquid cash cushion (3-6 months of expenses) in bank account. FDIC insured up to $250,000.
- Consider: credit unions (member-owned, lower fees, better rates), online banks, ethical banking choices.
- Beyond cushion: laddered CDs or index funds. Joe Dominguez originally recommended US Treasury bonds, but "the golden opportunity of Treasury bonds is long gone."
- 401(k): Take advantage of employer match — "essentially giving you free money" at average 6% of paycheck. Grows tax-free.
- SEP for self-employed. IRA for anyone.

**THE CROSSOVER POINT:**
- Project monthly investment income line and expense line into the future.
- When investment income line crosses expense line = **Crossover Point = Financial Independence**.
- "At the Crossover Point, where monthly investment income exceeds your monthly expenses, you will be financially independent. You will have passive income from a source other than a job."

**THE POWER OF A VISIBLE TIME HORIZON:**
- Knowing you'll work for money only a finite period = becoming more motivated, high-integrity worker.
- Larry D. story: HR employee who calculated his Crossover Point. "Finally I saw Financial Independence was going to happen. We were right on schedule. I stopped worrying about whether anybody was going to fire me." Wife said: "I'm bulletproof! I'm bulletproof!" Negotiated toughest settlements ever, prevailed big-time.
- FIRE community: Not all "set for life" from investments alone. Many do sequential sabbaticals, seasonal work, side hustles + FI baseline. "You know you can do it again. You're savvy and empowered."

**Sam and Donna's homesteading story:** Returned to Kansas hometown, bought land from friend, built passive solar house from old barn lumber. Sam hauled trash 5 hrs/day, Donna nursed 2 days/week. Aimed to eliminate rent/utilities, live below taxable levels. Then: two kids, health insurance, car that quit, house improvements. Discovered YMOYL program. Four years later reached Crossover Point — "could stop working for money and start working for his dreams."

**CACHE:**
- Cache (pronounced "cash") = savings beyond core capital or cushion. Extra safety net.
- Rosemary I. story: scared as Crossover Point approached. "Maybe this is what trapeze artists feel when they are assessing when to let go of their bar." Built up cache to face down "inner worry wart."
- Post-FI expenses drop: no commuting, no dress-for-success, no restaurant lunches. Cache grows naturally.
- **Three pillars of Financial Independence: Capital, Cushion, Cache.**

**NATURAL WEALTH AND FINANCIAL INTERDEPENDENCE:**
- **ABCs of Natural Wealth:**
  - **A = Abilities** — DIY skills, knowledge. Save money and can be monetized later. Damon S. story: enrolled in wilderness college, learned to forage and grow food.
  - **B = Belonging** — Who walks with you in life. Social capital. Riane Eisler's "caring economy." Loneliness is epidemic and expensive.
  - **C = Community** — Where you plant your roots. Mutual-aid networks. "Isolation is expensive; sharing is wealth."
- Two currencies: national currency (money) and natural currency (giving/receiving among kin).
- Financial *Inter*dependence includes all four corners: abilities, belonging, community, and stuff.

**The ABCs of Inflation:**
- Joe Dominguez: "Consciousness grows faster than inflation." You can "inflate" skills, competencies, knowledge, and community connections faster than money inflates.

**Crossover Point Jitters:**
- "I'm there. I'm FI." / "You don't get it! I'm terrified. What am I going to do with all this freedom?"
- Joe Dominguez quelled jitters by spending wealth-accumulation years designing his camper and going to state parks on weekends. By crossover, he was ready.
- Post-FI adjustments: jumpy/bored at first, then settle into routine based on inner rhythms. Personal and physical issues long deferred will surface. "They don't mean you made a mistake; they mean you are getting in shape—body and soul."
- Buckminster Fuller: "We are called to be architects of the future, not its victims."

**SUMMARY OF STEP 8:** Each month apply (capital × long-term interest rate / 12) to get monthly investment income. Post as third line on Wall Chart. Project to Crossover Point.

### Chapter 9: Where to Stash Your Cash for Long-Term Financial Freedom

**Step 9: Investing for FI**

"This step will help you become knowledgeable and sophisticated about income-producing investments that can provide a consistent income sufficient for your needs over the long term."

**EMPOWER YOURSELF:**
- Take back the power you've given over to money. Capital produces monthly investment income, freeing you from mandatory employment.


**Chapter 9 continued:**

**Investment glossary for FIers:**
- **Risk Tolerance:** How much loss can you tolerate before losing sleep? FIers inherently conservative — want to stay above Crossover Point during market declines.
- **Asset Classes:** Stocks, fixed income (bonds), real estate, commodities, foreign currencies. Diversify across classes.
- **Income types:** (1) Interest (bonds, CDs), (2) Dividends (stocks, funds), (3) Capital gains, (4) Rents, (5) Royalties.
- **Time Horizon:** Young = more risk tolerance. Near/post-FI = more conservative. CW: young invest 90% stocks/10% bonds. FIers tend more conservative than CW.
- **Fees:** Actively managed funds charge more but rarely beat index funds. Index funds = low fees, good performance.
- **Financial Advisers:** Fee-only advisers work only for you (not commissions). Shop around.
- **Socially Responsible Investing (SRI):** $23 trillion globally, 22% of US professionally managed money. ESG factors increasingly mainstream. SRI returns have matched or exceeded traditional. Three goals: avoid negative impacts, seek positive impacts, influence corporate policies.

**TREASURY BONDS: JOE'S PLAN**
- Joe retired at 31 in 1969 with $75,000 (~$225,000 today). At 8% average return = ~$20,000/year.
- Interest rates were 6.5%+ in 1969, spiked to 15% in 1981, back to 6.5% by 1997 (when Joe died).
- Joe's criteria for FI investing: greatest safety of capital, safety of interest ("full faith and credit"), tax-exempt from state/local, noncallable, liquid, cheapest availability (TreasuryDirect, no middlemen), stable income.
- Treasury bonds = IOUs from US government. Maturity 10-30 years. FDIC not relevant — backed by US government directly.
- Bond prices fluctuate with interest rates, but hold to maturity = face value back.
- Russian czarist bond story: Joe gave yellowing pre-1917 bonds as gifts to newly FI friends — a reminder that no investment is guaranteed forever.

**LOW-COST INDEX FUNDS: THE FIRE PLAN**
- John Bogle founded Vanguard (1975) — revolutionary: returning net profits to shareholders via lower costs.
- Warren Buffett: "A low-cost fund is the most sensible equity investment for the great majority of investors."
- Index funds track market indices (S&P 500, NASDAQ, Dow Jones, bond indices). Passive, low fees, broad diversification.
- Stock market big hits: Great Depression (-86%, 27 years to recover), Mid-1970s (-46%, ~decade), 1987 (-32%, 4 years), 2007-09 (-50%, 6 years).
- **Bogleheads philosophy:** Low-fee index funds diversified across asset classes, hold for years.
- SRI index fund fees: 0.22-0.50% (higher than non-SRI but lower than active funds).
- **Boglehead rules:** Pay off debt, live below means, invest rest in low-cost index funds, hold for many years.

**Do People Really Do This?**

### Quick Reference for the 9-Step Program

**Step 1: Making Peace with the Past** — A. Calculate total lifetime earnings. B. Calculate net worth (assets minus liabilities).

**Step 2: Being in the Present—Tracking Your Life Energy** — A. Calculate real hourly wage (include all job-related costs). B. Track every cent.

**Step 3: Where Is It All Going? (Monthly Tabulation)** — Total expenses by category. Convert dollars to "hours of life energy."

**Step 4: Three Questions That Will Transform Your Life** — For each spending category: (1) Did I receive fulfillment in proportion to life energy spent? (2) Is this expenditure in alignment with my values and life purpose? (3) How might this change if I didn't have to work for money?

**Step 5: Making Life Energy Visible** — Create Wall Chart plotting monthly income and expenses. Look at it daily.

**Step 6: Valuing Your Life Energy—Minimizing Spending** — Lower expenses through conscious spending. Quality of life over standard of living.

**Step 7: Valuing Your Life Energy—Maximizing Income** — Trade life energy for highest pay consistent with health and integrity.

**Step 8: Capital and the Crossover Point** — (capital × interest rate) / 12 = monthly investment income. Plot third line on Wall Chart. Project to Crossover Point.

**Step 9: Investing for FI** — Three pillars: Capital, Cushion, Cache.
