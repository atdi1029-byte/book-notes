## Bernoulli and the Geometric Mean

### Arithmetic vs. Geometric Mean in Risky Wagers

- **Double-or-nothing paradox**: With $100,000, a fair coin toss wagering everything yields arithmetic mean of $100,000 (($200,000 + $0)/2)
- But **geometric mean** is zero: multiply outcomes ($200,000 x $0), take square root = zero
- Geometric mean reflects true value when risk of total loss exists — people intuitively reject such wagers
- **Bernoulli's insight**: geometric mean always less than arithmetic mean (unless all values identical)
- Geometric mean is "more conservative" way of valuing risky propositions, reflecting distaste for risk

> "Fair" wagers are in fact unfavorable. This, says Bernoulli,
> is "Nature's admonition to avoid the dice altogether."

### The Petersburg Merchant and Insurance

- Bernoulli discussed a merchant shipping goods overseas who faces risk of ship sinking
- Insurance is always **unfavorable wager** by arithmetic mean (insurance company profits from premiums)
- But a poor merchant improves his geometric mean by buying insurance (even when "overpriced")
- Wealthier insurance company also improves its geometric mean by selling that insurance
- Insurance makes sense when parties differ in wealth — redistribution of risk improves geometric means for both

### Connection to Kelly Criterion

- **Bernoulli's dictum**: reasonable people always maximize geometric mean of outcomes, even unknowingly
- Kelly's prescription restated as simple rule: *choose the wager/investment with the highest geometric mean of outcomes*
- This broader rule is the **Kelly criterion** — broader than the edge/odds formula for bet sizing
- When outcomes aren't equally likely, weight them by probability (maximize expected logarithm of wealth)
- Anyone following this rule acts as if they had **logarithmic utility**

### Historical Uncertainty About Kelly-Bernoulli Link

- No evidence **Kelly** knew of Bernoulli's 1738 article
- Kelly almost certainly would have cited it if he knew, being a communications scientist
- **Bernoulli's article directly influenced Henry Latané**, not Kelly — Latané introduced these ideas to economists

## Henry Latané

### Background and Career

- **Henry Latané** entered job market with Harvard M.B.A. in grim year of 1930
- Claimed to be last man hired on Wall Street before the Depression
- Worked as financial analyst in 1930s and 1940s
- Mid-career, quit Wall Street, returned to school for Ph.D., became educator and theorist
- 1951: began doctoral work on portfolio theory at University of North Carolina

### Discovery and Application of Bernoulli

- Read translated Bernoulli article, realized ideas could apply to stock portfolios
- Met **Leonard Savage**, convinced him geometric mean policy made sense for long-term investors
- February 17, 1956: presented at prestigious **Cowles Foundation Seminar at Yale**
- **Harry Markowitz** was in attendance

### Latané's 1957 Dissertation

- Addressed problem of choosing a stock portfolio (what Bernoulli didn't do, Kelly only alluded to vaguely)
- Published 1959 as "Criteria for Choice Among Risky Ventures" in *Journal of Political Economy*
- Called his approach the **geometric mean criterion**
- Demonstrated it's a **myopic** strategy: don't need crystal ball on future market behavior
- Make decisions based on current deck composition — market will change, but that doesn't matter
- Adjust portfolio when returns/volatility change, always maintaining highest geometric mean

## Harry Markowitz and Mean-Variance Analysis

### Markowitz's Portfolio Theory

- **Harry Markowitz**: founder of dominant school of portfolio theory — **mean-variance analysis**
- Used statistics to show how **diversification** (buying different stocks, not too much in any one) cuts risk
- **Keynes** (1942) called small gambles in many companies a "travesty of investment policy" — post-Samuelson, that notion went into "dustbin of medieval superstition"
- Markowitz showed you can minimize risk even if you can't beat the market

### Mean-Variance Framework

- Two statistics from historical data: **mean** (average annual return) and **variance** (volatility measure)
- Higher variance = higher risk (loose measure)
- **First concise statement** of trade-off between risk and return
- Theory refuses to take sides — whether higher return matters more than lower risk is personal taste
- Surviving portfolios called **"efficient"** — form arc called the **"efficient frontier"**
- Financial advisers quickly adopted as orthodoxy

### Static vs. Dynamic Limitation

- Mean-variance is static, single-period theory — assumes buy now, sell at end of given time frame
- Most people buy and hold until strong reason to sell
- **Geometric mean criterion resolves this** — gambles favorable as one-shot are ruinous when repeated
- Geometric mean approximately equals arithmetic mean minus one-half the variance

## Leo Breiman and the Capital Growth Criterion

- **Leo Breiman** (1960, statistician): "Investment Policies for Expanding Businesses Optimal in a Long-Run Sense"
- Appeared in *Naval Research Logistics Quarterly*
- **First to show**: maximizing geometric mean minimizes time to achieve any particular wealth goal
- Complex lineage led to **welter of names** for Kelly criterion:
  - **Latané**: "geometric mean principle" or "G policy"
  - **Breiman**: "capital growth criterion"
  - **Markowitz**: "MEL" (maximize expected logarithm)
  - **Thorp**: "Kelly[-Breiman-Bernoulli-Latané]"
- **Bernoulli's priority**: 218 years before Kelly — but Kelly's unique contribution was connecting **inside information and capital growth**, which couldn't exist before **Shannon rendered information measurable**

## The Trouble with Markowitz

- Geometric mean of return = **"compound return on investment"** (Wall Street's usual scorecard)
- **Richard W. McEnally** (Latané's colleague at UNC): advice sounds laudable but "difficult or impossible to implement in practice"

### Three Penny Stocks Example

- Three wheels with different return profiles:
  - Wheel 1: arithmetic mean $1.41, geometric mean $1.41
  - Wheel 2: arithmetic mean $0.00, geometric mean $1.67
  - Wheel 3: arithmetic mean $1.22, geometric mean $1.75
- Third wheel has highest geometric mean — Kelly chooses it
- Second wheel worst by Kelly: has zero as outcome, geometric mean is *zero* — any long-term investor must go bust
- **Mean-variance analysis refuses to decide** among three — yet wheel with total loss risk has *lower* variance than third wheel
- Example of how variance is not perfect measure of risk
- Both Markowitz and Kelly agree on **value of diversification**

## Two Ways to Use Law of Large Numbers

### Non-Compounding (Henpecked Gambler)

- Kelly mentioned both in his article
- Gambler whose wife permits $1 bet each week — not allowed to reinvest past winnings
- Should forget Kelly criterion — maximize arithmetic mean instead (winnings don't compound, simply accumulate)

### Compounding (Kelly Gambler)

- **Kelly criterion** meaningful only when profits are reinvested
- Gambler who starts with $1 and reinvests: betting on first wheel ($1.41 geometric mean), $1.41^52 = $67,108,864
- Second wheel (compounding): $0^52 = $0 — almost certain to get zero in a year's worth of betting
- Third wheel: $1.22^52 = $37,877
- **Standard mean-variance analysis does not treat compounding**
- Wealth from compounding fantastically greater than otherwise
- **Practical theory of investment must largely be theory of *reinvestment***

### Thorp's 1969 Contribution

- Demonstrated partial incompatibility of mean-variance analysis and maximizing geometric mean
- Concluded: **"the Kelly criterion should replace the Markowitz criterion as the guide to portfolio selection"**
- No major economics journal would have published such heresy
- Appeared in *Review of the International Statistical Institute* — probably few economists saw it

## Shannon's Demon

### Shannon's MIT Talks (Mid-1960s to 1971)

- **Claude Shannon**: efficient market mob's worst nightmare
- Smart guy making money hand over fist in the market
- Mid-1960s: began holding regular meetings at MIT on scientific investing
- Attended by eclectic assortment including **Paul Samuelson**
- So many wanted to attend, talks moved to MIT's biggest halls

### The Rebalancing Strategy

- Incredible scheme for making money off *fluctuations*
- Stock price jitters up and down randomly, no overall trend
- Put half capital in stock, half in "cash" account
- Each day at noon, **"rebalance"** the portfolio to restore 50-50 proportions

### Example: Starting with $1,000

- Start: $500 stock, $500 cash
- Stock halves (volatile day): $750 portfolio ($250 stock, $500 cash)
- Rebalance: withdraw $125 cash, buy stock → $375/$375
- Next day stock *doubles*: $375 stock → $750
- With $375 cash: $1,125 total
- After dramatic plunge then recovery to starting price, buy-and-hold investor has no profit
- **Shannon's investor made $125**
- Defies instincts: sell when up, buy when down ("throwing good money after bad")

### Geometric Random Walk Model

- Chart: price of imaginary stock starting at $1, either doubles or halves with equal probability each time unit
- **Geometric random walk** (popular model of stock prices)
- Upper line: 50-50 rebalanced portfolio — exponentially growing wealth
- Shannon's rebalancer achieving superior return *and* superior risk-adjusted return

### Maxwell's Demon Connection

- **James Clerk Maxwell** (1871, *Theory of Heat*): described perpetual motion machine with tiny trapdoor in partition
- **"Maxwell's demon"**: uses superpower vision to sort air molecules by speed — creates hot/cold chambers
- **Leo Szilard** (1929): described something close to the bit, anticipating Shannon
- Full solution supplied 1982 by IBM scientist **Charles Bennett**
- **Rolf Landauer** (1961, IBM): erasing computer memory always increases entropy
- Bennett used Landauer's result: entropy increase in demon's brain at least as great as entropy decrease in air chamber
- **Demon can't make net energy profit** — only redistributing entropy and energy
- **Samuelson** (1974): high-IQ trader "is in effect possessed of a 'Maxwell's Demon'"
- Like Maxwell's demon, **Shannon's stock system turns randomness into profit**

> "Naw," said Shannon. "The commissions would kill you."

- Shannon's system = early example of **constant-proportion rebalanced portfolio**, independently studied by **Mark Rubinstein** and **Eugene Fama**
- **Thomas Cover** (Stanford) built on Shannon's idea — believed new algorithms could make concept profitable
- Shannon's main point: refute belief that random walk = absolute barrier to beating market

### Arithmetic/Geometric Mean Paradox

- Volatile stock with zero geometric mean return must have *positive* arithmetic mean return
- **Mr. Dollar-a-Week**: bets $1/week on penny stock, averages 25% weekly profit while stock went nowhere
- Doesn't think *big* — same amount each week, no compounding
- Shannon's 50-50 split achieves highest geometric mean
- Shannon's scheme is special case of Kelly gambling
- Harvesting volatility: if you could find stock that doubles/halves daily, $1 → $1 million in ~240 trades
- Reality: realistic volatility means gains < commissions

## The Feud

### Origins: Latané's Footnote

- Feud between pro-Kelly and anti-Kelly camps started with a *footnote*
- 1959: **Latané** name-dropped **Professor L. J. Savage** in footnote
- Savage claimed geometric mean "approximately valid for all utility functions"
- Famous names sell theories — Savage's opinion carried weight

### Paul Samuelson Enters the Ring (1969)

> "Associated with independent discoveries by J. B. Williams,
> John Kelly, and H. A. Latané is the notion that if one is
> investing for many periods, the proper behavior is to
> maximize the *geometric* mean of return rather than the
> arithmetic mean. I believe this to be incorrect…"

- Samuelson challenged Latané's statement attributed to Savage
- Savage (1969) said his position "differs from the view attributed to him in 1959"
- Samuelson's article: "Lifetime Portfolio Selection by Dynamic Stochastic Programming" — widely cited
- **Shannon** unaware of Samuelson's comments until 1985 when **Thomas Cover** mentioned them — Shannon shocked, said he and Samuelson were friends

### The Two Sides

- Samuelson's side: unparalleled stature, fierce debater
- Joined by **Robert C. Merton** (MIT circle)
- Pro-Kelly: **Latané**, **Hakansson**, mathematicians, statisticians, information theorists
- Except for **Harry Markowitz**, pro-Kelly people lacked Samuelson's influence

### Samuelson's Critique: "Fallacy"

- Called Kelly criterion a **"fallacy"** — fundamental flaw
- 1971: conceded as valid this **Theorem**:

> "Acting to maximize the geometric mean at every step will,
> if the period is 'sufficiently long,' 'almost certainly'
> result in higher terminal wealth and terminal utility than
> from any other decision rule…"

- But identified **False Corollary**: ending richer via Kelly doesn't mean Kelly is rational choice
- **Bumper sticker analogy**: WHOEVER DIES WITH THE MOST TOYS WINS — error assumes ending richer = rational
- Kelly is *greedy* — perpetually takes risks for higher peaks
- **Performance car analogy**: if 0-to-60 were only criterion, everyone would drive Lamborghinis — other things matter
- Kelly shuns tiniest risk of losing everything — **Nils Hakansson**: "automatically built in…air-tight survival motive"
- Short term: Kelly settles for *lower* return than possible
- **Automotive analogy** (**Jarrod Wilcox**): driving is favorable "wager" — one fatal accident per 6,000 years of driving — Kelly-like philosophy would forgo driving
- **Keynes**: "in the long run we are all dead"
- **Kelly may risk money you need for gains you find superfluous**

> "The promises of the Kelly criterion recall those tales
> of mischievous genies granting wishes that never turn out
> as planned."

### Pinball Machine Argument

- Samuelson and Merton's visual: Kelly applied to coin toss paying 6x on heads, losing on tails
- Bettor's edge = 200%; payoff odds 5:1; Kelly wager = 40%
- Start $100 — after four tosses: 16 possible outcomes
- Luckiest (4 heads): $8,100
- Average luck (2 heads/2 tails): $324
- Worst (4 tails): $12.96
- **5 of 16 outcomes** end with *less* than starting wealth
- Kelly maximizes **geometric mean** and **median** ($324)
- **"Epsilon ain't zero"** (Samuelson quoting Gertrude Stein who never actually said this) — small risks are not zero
- Fraidy-cat Alice may rationally refuse even tiny risk
- Less aggressive schemes handle bad luck better (lower average compound return, though)

### The Utility Theory Battle

- **Hakansson** (1971): recast Kelly within utility theory — Merton and Samuelson found math mistake
- **Savage** died 1971 — death didn't end squabbling
- Samuelson: Latané should "spare the dead"
- **Tsvi Ophir** (Hebrew University, 1978): "surprising to note" how erroneous propositions persist after debunking

> "Neither Samuelson nor Merton nor indeed Ophir has
> challenged the basic principle…If they follow a
> significantly different policy and I follow the G policy,
> in the long run I become almost certain to have more
> wealth than they." — Latané (1978)

### Pro-Kelly Arguments

- No one claimed the false corollary was true — **Thorp** (1971): "We heartily agree that the corollary is false"
- Pro-Kelly position: **utility can be irrelevant**
- **Kelly**: system "has nothing to do with the value function which he attached to his money"
- **Latané**: "My position as to the usefulness of G in no sense depends on utility"
- Two prongs of argument:
  1. **Positivist**: utility = unnecessary concept to discard — dollars under Kelly survive/grow faster than any system
  2. **Pragmatic** (Latané's Wall Street years): compound return is the portfolio manager's scorecard
- **Markowitz** (1972, 1976): utility of long-term investor should be denominated in **compound return**, not terminal wealth — "only reasonable criterion for preferring one long-term investment to another"

### Thorp's Real-World Results

- **Convertible Hedge Associates**: Nov 1969 – Dec 1973, cumulative gain **102.9%** vs. loss (-0.5%) for Dow Jones
- "Proponents of efficient market theory, please explain"

### Merton and Samuelson Concede (Sort Of)

> "It's a free country. Anybody can set up whatever criteria
> he wishes…In our experience, once understanding of the
> issues is realized, few decision makers retain their
> interest in average compound return."

- *It's a duty* to talk people out of caring about compound return? Comments mystified pro-Kelly people
- Thorp: "most people I talk to say 'Yeah, sounds great to me, I want that.'"

## The Kelly Criterion Debate Continues

### Why Economists Resist

- Economists insist people have precise mathematical utility functions — portfolio optimization should match preferences
- Reality: people's feelings about wealth are fluid, inconsistent — preferences often generated on demand
- **The only stable preference**: people want as much money as possible, as fast as possible
- **Behavioral finance**: people motivated by envy — compare returns to neighbors and indexes
- Of all strategies, **only Kelly's is unbeatable in long run**

### Time Horizons and Leverage

- Stock market slow compared to blackjack (double or nothing every 40 seconds vs. years to double in stocks)
- No buy-and-hold investor lives long enough for high confidence Kelly pulls ahead
- Kelly more relevant to **in-and-out traders**
- **Kelly answer** to asset allocation: put all money in stocks with modest leverage
- Few investors comfortable with all-equity portfolio — a market crash could cut life savings drastically

### Samuelson's One-Syllable Attack (1979)

- "Why We Should Not Make Mean Log of Wealth Big Though Years to Act Are Long" — published in *Journal of Banking and Finance*
- **Thorp**: Samuelson was saying "You people are so dumb, I'm going to have to explain this in words of one syllable"
- Gimmick prevented using "geometric," "logarithmic," "maximize," or mentioning Bernoulli, Kelly, Shannon, Latané

> "When you lose—and you *sure can* lose—with N large, you
> can lose real big. Q. E. D."

> "No need to say more. I've made my point. And, save for
> the last word, have done so in prose of but one syllable."

### Tribal Psychology

- Samuelson: people *most* impressed with Kelly are *least* schooled in economics — true, they're information theorists, gamblers, mathematicians, portfolio managers
- At least partly due to Samuelson and Merton's influence, Kelly's reputation among economists scarcely better than painter **Thomas Kinkade** among art critics
- **Shannon's stock market lecture** put Kelly on Samuelson's agenda — "Not invented here" bias from economists

### Samuelson-Cover Correspondence (1988)

- **Paul Samuelson** wrote to Stanford's **Thomas Cover**
- Called Kelly system a **"complete swindle"**
- "Mathematicians who ignore remainders in approximation should be halved, then quartered, then…"
- Last letter in words of one syllable: "If I like your ways to guess at chance, I need not (and will not) use your 'growth' stuff with them."

## All Gambles Are Alike

- Kelly betting makes all gambles interchangeable — converts *any* opportunity into capital-growth-optimal investment
- When wager too risky: Kelly bettor stakes fraction of bankroll; when no total loss possible: may use **leverage**

### The 50% Rule (Bankroll Fluctuations)

- In infinite series of Kelly bets, chance of bankroll ever dipping to half = 1/2
- Chance of ever dipping to 1/3 = 1/3; to 1% = 1%
- Chance of ever being reduced to zero is zero — **you never go broke, always recover**
- **Bad news**: no matter how rich, risk of serious dips persists — if bankroll at $1 million, 50% chance of losing half that million at some point
- Kelly bettor **spends lot of time being less wealthy than he *was***
- Kelly gambler's bankroll **more volatile** than historical Dow or S&P 500

## Taming Kelly Volatility

### Method 1: Fixed Fraction (Half Kelly)

- Stake fixed fraction of Kelly bet — popular: **"half Kelly"**
- **Appealing trade-off**: cuts volatility drastically, decreases return by only a quarter
- Full Kelly: 10% compound per time unit → 7.5% with half
- Full Kelly: 1/3 chance of halving before doubling
- Half Kelly: only 1/9 chance of losing half before doubling
- **"Bright clear line"** (**Ray Dillinger**) between "aggressive investing" and "insane investing"
- At twice Kelly bet, compound return = zero
- Serious overbetting → negative returns
- **Bill Benter** (millions on racetrack): easy for best models to overestimate edge by factor of 2 — someone attempting Kelly might unknowingly place twice-Kelly bet

> "Those individuals or institutions who are long term
> compounders should consider the possibility of using the
> Kelly criterion…Investors with less tolerance may prefer
> to use a lesser fraction." — Ed Thorp (1997 Montreal)

### Method 2: Diversification

- Blackjack players pool bankrolls — team wins more consistently, setbacks fewer
- 100 simultaneous biased coins (55% heads): Kelly bettor commits nearly entire bankroll, split equally
- Doesn't stake everything because barely possible for all 100 to come up tails
- Creates **smooth curve of exponential growth**
- **Princeton-Newport** almost always highly diversified — trades designed to be **"volatility neutral"**
- Average stock investor can diversify only so far — global economy means virtually all stocks correlated

## Black Monday 1987

- **October 19, 1987**: Dow lost 23% of value in single day — biggest single-day drop ever
- **Princeton-Newport's** $600 million portfolio shed only ~$2 million in crash
- Thorp's computer found rich opportunities in panicked valuations — made ~**$2 million profit** in new trades
- Princeton-Newport closed October 1987 about even
- **Year return: astonishing 34%**
- **Mark Rubinstein** estimated chance of S&P falling 29% in single day: **1 in 10^160**

> "So improbable is such an event that it would not be
> anticipated to occur even if the stock market were to last
> for 20 billion years, the upper end of the currently
> estimated duration of the universe." — Rubinstein

- **Robert C. Merton** in 1970s: market could act like flea (jumps) as well as ant — implied **Black-Scholes** not exactly right
- Kelly system not married to any specific model — works with flealike jumps; **mean-variance analysis** ill-suited

## Long-Term Capital Management

### John Meriwether

- **Born Chicago 1947**, mathematically inclined, educated by priests — Northwestern on **golf caddie scholarship**
- Taught high school math, got MBA at University of Chicago
- At **Salomon Brothers**: when NYC near default, bond market panicked — Meriwether reasoned NYC problems irrelevant to other municipalities' credit
- **Bought government bonds at bargain prices**, made killing
- Started Salomon's Arbitrage Group — became firm's biggest profit maker
- Featured in **Michael Lewis's *Liar's Poker***
- Kept 68-acre estate in North Salem, NY — true passion: **horse racing** — **hedged daily bets with rosary beads in briefcase**

### LTCM Launch

- Recruited **Robert C. Merton** and **Myron Scholes** — Lowenstein: "like putting Michael Jordan and Muhammad Ali on same team"
- **Thorp declined to invest** — worried Merton/Scholes lacked experience investing other people's money
- Heard Meriwether was **"martingale man"** — "if things went against him, he'd bet more"
- **First fund to raise $1 billion**
- Fees: **25% of profits** plus **1% of assets** (vs. usual 20% and less)
- 25% was deal-breaker for Rockefeller Foundation

### Investor Base and Performance

- Harvard, **Merrill Lynch**, **Kuwait pension fund**, **Bank of China**, **Mike Ovitz**
- Launched **March 1994** — returns after fees: **20%** (1994), **43%** (1995), **41%** (1996)
- Capital mushroomed to **$7.1 billion** by October 1997
- Gray-market LTCM shares trading at ~10% premium
- 1997 return: 17% (S&P up 31%) — Meriwether returned money to investors; one "protested so angrily that LTCM allowed him to stay"
- Capital down to **$4.7 billion** by end of December

### Strategy and Leverage

- **Myron Scholes**: "vacuuming up nickels no one else could see" — would snatch imaginary nickel during presentations
- Core: **convergence trades** in government bonds
- **"On the run"** (brand-new 30-year Treasury) vs. **"off the run"** (older bond) — people pay irrational premium for new
- Buy older bonds, short new ones, wait for convergence
- Actual return per dollar: **0.67%** — with **~30x leverage**, achieved 20% returns
- At **Conseco**: **Andrew Chow** challenged Scholes — Scholes snapped: **"You're the reason—because of fools like you we can."** Conseco did not invest
- On many positions, leverage **effectively infinite** — zero haircuts on many deals
- LTCM paid zero haircuts: testimony to fund management's ability to romance creditors

### Value at Risk (VaR)

- After **Black Monday 1987**, J.P. Morgan concerned with risk management
- **Til Guldimann** and **Jacques Longerstaey** devised VaR
- Concept: compute how much portfolio stands to lose within time frame, with what probability
- Banks hired **"risk managers"** to prepare daily reports
- **Basel Committee** endorsed VaR for capital requirements
- LTCM October 1994 risk document: **1% chance of losing 20%+ in a year** — 20% loss = worst case considered

### VaR Criticisms

- **Paul Wilmott**: "I've got a bad feeling about this..."
- **Problem 1**: mystique of numbers — only as good as assumptions
- **Problem 2**: sidesteps two Kelly questions — what level of risk leads to highest long-run return? What is chance of losing *everything*?
- Weekly risk meetings at Greenwich — top secret **"Risk Aggregator"** — worst-case: loss of **$2.5 billion**
- **David Modest**: "We certainly spent hours and hours thinking about it." Then people shrugged and went back to trading

### Russian Default and Collapse

- After fall of communism, billions in Western money flowed into Russia — **Boesky** offered expertise, **Caesar Kimmel** ran Moscow casino
- **IMF** made **$17 billion loan** — ~$4.5 billion quickly wired to mobsters' offshore accounts
- Russian treasury bonds (**GKOs**) = junkiest of junk bonds, paying **40% interest and up**
- **Greg Hawkins** devised trade collecting rich GKO interest through Western intermediaries — LTCM had **no direct contact** with dubious Russian parties
- August 17, 1998: Russia **devalued ruble and defaulted**
- **"Flight to quality"** — LTCM's philosophy (people overpay for safe investments) temporarily reversed
- LTCM lost **$551 million** in one week

> "When you're down by half, people figure you can go down
> all the way. They're going to push the market against you.
> You're *finished*." — Vinny Mattone

> "A banker is a fellow who lends you his umbrella when the
> sun is shining but wants it back the moment it rains."
> — Mark Twain

- **Jarrod Wilcox**: LTCM should have reduced leverage — "instead, they allowed the leverage to drift up to like sixty times. No Las Vegas gambler would ever make that mistake—no surviving one."
- **Peter Fisher** (NY Fed) received Risk Aggregator printout: ~375 positions making nearly identical bets worldwide
- USD Swap Spread bet: 15bp annual volatility, potential loss $240 million — but spread had already moved **40 basis points** in 8 months
- "This was supposed to be diversification, but it wasn't."
- Fisher estimated real loss at **$3 to $5 billion** if LTCM failed: "I'm not worried about markets trading down. I'm worried that they won't trade at all."
- September 23, 1998: consortium invested **$3.625 billion**
- LTCM lost **$4.4 billion** (90% of peak) — partners' stake now worth **$28 million**
- **Merton** reportedly lost ~$100 million — mortified at having talked Harvard into investing
- **Larry Hilibrand** in tears — had borrowed **$24 million** from **Crédit Lyonnais** — net worth: ~$100 million to **$20 million in debt** — requested bailout cover personal debts, consortium said no

### Fat Tails and the Real Problem: Overbetting

- Press postmortem: three explanations (none satisfying) — leverage, fat tails, hubris
- LTCM's official leverage (25.6) was *less* than Morgan Stanley (26.5), Lehman (33.2), Salomon (42.5) — none imploded
- **"Fat tails"**: big movements much more common than normal distribution predicts — Merton's "flealike jumps"
- **Thorp**: LTCM based some models on **four years of data** — spread hovered 3-4 points; as recently as 1990 it topped 9%
- Best single-word explanation: **overbetting** — unlike leverage or fat tails, overbetting is *always* bad
- **Thorp** linked collapse to Merton/Scholes's critique of Kelly: "I could see that they didn't understand how it controlled the danger of extreme risk."
- LTCM problem was **group-think** — systematically rosy projections, too little skeptical probing
- "LTCM had a whole lot of bets on Southeast Asian debt, a whole lot of bets on the spread between government and junk…it's not really millions of small bets. It's a few big bets." — Thorp
- **William Ziemba** estimated leverage ~**twice the Kelly level** — true compound growth rate near zero
- **Kelly mapping** vs. **mean-variance mapping**: return curve boomerangs back to zero with excessive leverage, while mean-variance shows straight line

### Market Efficiency Debate

- **Richard Roll** (1992): "I have personally tried to invest money…in every single anomaly academics have dreamed up. And I have yet to make a nickel."

### Statistical Arbitrage: Indicators Project

- **Thorp** began pursuing automated trading as early as 1979
- **Jerome Baesel** ran the **"Indicators Project"** at Princeton-Newport
- Software picked stocks up/down most in previous two weeks (adjusted for dividends and splits)
- Found *up* stocks had strong tendency to fall back; *down* stocks tended to rise — market over-reacts
- Exactly opposite of "momentum investors" — emotion fades, pendulum swings back
- Concluded: ~20% annual return — but Princeton-Newport already making more (28-30% in 1980-82)
- **Brilliant concept quietly tabled**

### Jerry Bamberger and Morgan Stanley

- **Jerry Bamberger** independently got same idea at **Morgan Stanley** (1982-83)
- Bamberger's improvement: divided stocks into **industry groups**, counterbalanced long/short in each
- Feeling unappreciated at Morgan Stanley, quit — answered Princeton-Newport ad for promising low-risk strategies
- Chain-smoker (Thorp had policy of not hiring smokers) — compromised with outdoor cigarettes
- Forbidden from computer room (gigabyte drives, each size of washing machine, "vulnerable to tiniest airborne mote")
- Thorp noticed same lunch daily: "How often do you have a tuna salad sandwich?" — "Every day for the last six years"
- **BOSS Partners** began managing $30-60 million — earned **25-30% annualized in 1985**
- Return eroded as Morgan Stanley's **Nunzio Tartaglia** expanded competing operation to $900 million in trades
- Competition killed the edge — both operations eventually closed

### Ridgeline Partners (1994-2002)

- After Princeton-Newport, Thorp took time off — discovered irresistible opportunities in **Japanese warrants**
- Former investor suggested new statistical arbitrage fund
- Launched **Ridgeline Partners** August 1994 — "I just made phone calls and before the day was done, we were 'full'"

- Highly automated — by time Thorp logged on in California (three hours behind New York), million shares already traded

### Thorp's Basketball System

### Hong Kong Racing

- Horse racing is legal gambling in Hong Kong — "by far the most popular form of recreation"

- Up to 100 people needed to hustle to betting windows

- Syndicate split; within few years all three were **multimillionaires**
