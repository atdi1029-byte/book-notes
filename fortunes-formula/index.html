Prologue: The Wire Service

In 1905, the most valuable commodity in American gambling was not a tip on a horse. It was the speed at which you learned the result. Before the internet, before television, before radio, race results traveled by telegraph wire — and whoever controlled that wire controlled the entire illegal gambling industry. The story of that wire is, in miniature, the story of this book: information is money, and the people who understood that principle first got extraordinarily rich.

John Payne, a corrupt telegraph operator at Western Union's Cincinnati office, quit when the company banned gambling-related traffic and founded his own service. He stationed a man at the racetrack with a hand mirror to flash the winner in code to a nearby building, which telegraphed results to pool halls across Cincinnati. The critical advantage was timing: bookmakers knew the results before their customers, preventing post-race bets on horses that had already won.

In Chicago, the violent gangster Mont Tennes acquired the Illinois franchise for Payne's wire in 1907, naming it the General News Bureau. The franchise cost Tennes $300 a day; he recouped many times over from more than 700 bookie joints, each paying half their daily receipts. Tennes survived six bomb attacks in a single summer from rival gangsters envious of the wire's profits. He eventually squeezed Payne out entirely, expanding from New Orleans to San Francisco.

The largest stockholder of Tennes's operation was Moses "Moe" Annenberg, publisher of the Racing Form. Annenberg hired James Ragan to run the wire and expanded it coast to coast, into Canada, Mexico, and Cuba. By the mid-1930s, Annenberg was AT&T's fifth largest customer. AT&T's in-house legal opinion acknowledged that its customers were "bound to be owners of poolrooms and bookmakers," but the company officially claimed shock that gambling occurred on its network.

"It is my intention to witness the sport of kings without the vice of kings." — Chicago mayor Carter Harrison II

When Al Capone's enforcer Frank Nitti demanded a piece, Annenberg fled to Miami and eventually agreed to pay a million dollars a year in protection money. In 1939, facing tax evasion charges, he walked away from the wire entirely. Continental Press Service filled the vacuum, and the most powerful Italian and Jewish mobsters in America — Lucky Luciano, Meyer Lansky, Frank Costello, Joe Adonis, Ben Siegel, and Longy Zwillman — allied in a national organization called "the Combination."

Ben Siegel ran the Combination's rival wire service, Trans-American, from the West Coast. He charged casino bookmakers not just a subscription fee but a cut of their income — sometimes 100 percent. When Siegel decided Trans-American was his own business and refused to give it back, the Combination broke its own rule against executing board members. On June 20, 1947, an unknown gunman fired a full clip of steel-jacketed bullets through a trellis of rose arbor in Beverly Hills. Four hit Siegel; one passed through his right eyeball and came to rest fifteen feet away on the dining room floor. Half an hour before the murder, four toughs assembled in the Flamingo lobby and announced they were taking over.

"Senator, if you want to cut out gambling there's just two things you need to do. Burn the stables and shoot the horses." — Frank Costello, testifying before the Kefauver Committee

Senator Carey Estes Kefauver — a folksy man in a coonskin cap — branded Continental Press "Public Enemy Number One" and organized televised hearings that ran fifteen months. The hearings were riveting early reality TV: mobsters invoked the Fifth Amendment, a Chicago police captain admitted betting a fortune on sports while allowing bookie joints to operate, and Louisiana police explained they didn't have the heart to close casinos that employed the underprivileged. Willie Moretti, a short, loud mobster with an omnipresent diamond stickpin, freely admitted knowing every big-name crime figure, calling them "well-charactered men" he met at racetracks. He invited senators to visit him at home on the Jersey shore. It was too much for Vito Genovese: on October 4, 1951, Moretti was shot twice in the forehead at Joe's Elbow Room in Cliffside, New Jersey.

Congress banned the interstate transmission of gambling results, and the wire service died — partly because, at the dawn of television, it was already technologically obsolete. But twelve miles from Zwillman's mansion, AT&T had built a scientific think tank with its monopolistic riches. In 1956, a young scientist there — pondering his employer's ambivalent relationship with bookmaking — devised the most successful gambling system of all time.

Part One: Entropy

Claude Elwood Shannon was born in 1916 in Gaylord, Michigan, where he built model planes and a telegraph system to a friend's house as a boy. He studied electrical engineering at the University of Michigan, then went to MIT for graduate work, where he produced what is considered one of the most important master's theses of the twentieth century. Shannon realized that Boolean algebra — the abstract symbolic logic created by George Boole in the 1850s — could be physically embodied in electrical circuits. Every logical statement could be represented by circuits using AND, OR, and NOT gates. This single insight became the foundation of all digital computing.

During World War II, Shannon worked on cryptography at Bell Labs, proving that the one-time pad achieves perfect secrecy if the key is truly random, used once, and as long as the message. He worked alongside Alan Turing, who had cracked the German Enigma cipher; the two spoke daily in the cafeteria. Shannon was developing a unit of information he called the bit (a name credited to John Tukey). Turing mentioned he had his own unit called the ban — named for Banbury, the town where his cryptographic team's scratch paper was manufactured. The bit, not the ban, changed the world.

Shannon's landmark 1948 paper, "A Mathematical Theory of Communication," founded information theory. Its core insight: information is measurable, quantified in bits. Shannon introduced entropy as a measure of uncertainty and proved that every communication channel has a maximum rate for reliable transmission — channel capacity. The revolutionary finding was that you can get arbitrarily close to error-free communication by clever encoding, even through noisy channels. John von Neumann advised Shannon to use the word "entropy" because "no one really knows what entropy means, so in a debate you will always have the advantage."

"To make the chance of error as small as you wish? Nobody had ever thought of that. How he got that insight, I don't know. But almost all modern communication engineering is based on that work." — Robert Fano

Shannon was a polymath and a prankster. He juggled while riding a unicycle down Bell Labs hallways. His home on the shore of Mystic Lakes in Winchester, Massachusetts, featured a "Toy Room" containing five pianos, thirty-some instruments from piccolos to sousaphones, a flamethrowing trumpet, a rocket-powered Frisbee, and an estimated $100,000 worth of surplus electronic equipment. He built "Theseus," a robotic mouse that threaded a maze; a chess-playing machine with a robot arm that made sarcastic comments; and the "Ultimate Machine" — a cigar box with a toggle switch that, when flipped on, opened its lid, extended a robot hand, and flipped the switch off again. He christened his home "Entropy House" — a name whose appropriateness was apparent to all who saw the interior.

Shannon met Edward O. Thorp at MIT in November 1960. Thorp, a mathematics PhD, had spent months developing a card-counting system for blackjack using probability theory and IBM computer simulations. He needed a National Academy member to submit his paper; Shannon was the only mathematician member at MIT. The secretary warned Shannon had only minutes, but Thorp's main points captivated him. Shannon's only objection was the title: he suggested changing "A Winning Strategy for Blackjack" to "A Favorable Strategy for Twenty-one." Then Shannon asked: "Are you working on anything else in the gambling area?" Thorp mentioned roulette, and Shannon was riveted — possibly more interested in roulette than blackjack, because it had a gadget to build.

They collaborated on the first wearable computer designed to beat a casino. The cigarette-pack-sized device used Newtonian mechanics to predict which octant of the roulette wheel the ball would land in. A user clicked toe-operated switches hidden in shoes when the rotor and ball passed reference points; the device communicated predictions via musical tones through a concealed earphone. In lab conditions, they achieved a 44 percent edge on the house. Shannon worried about secrecy, citing research showing any two people in the United States are connected by a chain of about three mutual friends — a precursor to Stanley Milgram's "six degrees of separation" work in 1967.

Meanwhile, John Kelly Jr. — a strikingly handsome, chain-smoking physicist from Corsicana, Texas, who was rated the smartest person at Bell Labs next to Shannon — was working on a problem inspired by the television quiz show scandal. Kelly imagined a gambler with a "private wire" delivering tips that were reliable but not perfectly so. The greedy strategy of betting everything loses all the first time a tip is wrong. The timid strategy of minimum bets squanders the advantage. Kelly found the sweet spot: a proportional betting system where each wager is scaled to your current bankroll, so you can never go broke.

The Kelly formula is deceptively simple: bet a fraction of your bankroll equal to your edge divided by the odds. When your edge is zero, don't bet. When the race is fixed, bet everything. Kelly's most beautiful equation, G_max = R, states that the maximum growth rate of a gambler's money equals the information transmission rate. A single bit of certain inside information at even odds lets you double your money. AT&T worried about the paper's gambling implications; Kelly changed the title to the anodyne "A New Interpretation of Information Rate," and Shannon refereed it into the July 1956 Bell System Technical Journal.

"You've heard the one about the horse player? He said, 'I hope I break even today. I need the money.'" — Kelly's humor about gambling

Kelly died tragically young. On March 18, 1965, walking along a Manhattan street after a meeting, he held his hand to his head and said "Wait a minute!" Moments later he slumped to the sidewalk, dead of a brain hemorrhage at forty-one. But his legacy endured in unexpected ways: Arthur C. Clarke had visited Bell Labs and heard Kelly's voice-synthesis recording of a computer singing "Bicycle Built for Two." Clarke remembered it when writing the screenplay for Stanley Kubrick's 2001: A Space Odyssey — the homicidal computer HAL sings the same song while being unplugged.

Part Two: Blackjack

In January 1961, Thorp presented "Fortune's Formula: A Winning Strategy for Blackjack" at the American Mathematical Society. The talk made the front page of the Boston Globe, and gamblers from all over the country began calling. His wife Vivian filled every page of a legal pad with messages, then refused to take more; their baby daughter burst into tears whenever the phone rang. Shannon suggested Thorp use Kelly's formula for bet sizing. The best financial offer came from a syndicate led by Emmanuel "Manny" Kimmel — a gnomelike, five-foot-five associate of the mob who offered $100,000.

Kimmel was a legendary figure. He had grown up on Prince Street in Jewish Newark alongside Abner "Longy" Zwillman — Der Langer, "the Tall One" — who was regarded almost as a god for beating up the Irish kids who harassed merchants and upset pushcarts. Zwillman concluded there were two ways to make real money in America: politics and gambling. He ran the numbers racket, skimming $400 out of every $1,000 wagered. When a minor thug named Leo Kaplus began roughing up Zwillman's numbers runners and boasted he'd kick Zwillman in the balls, Zwillman tracked him to a Newark bar and shot him once in the testicles. Zwillman made at least $20 million during Prohibition, was the sugar daddy of actress Jean Harlow, and invested in everything from steel companies to the Muzak Corporation.

Kimmel, who owned garages and parking lots as fronts for gambling operations, was famous as a proposition man who would bet on anything. He taught himself calculus and probability theory, memorized sucker bets, and could doctor sugar cubes with DDT to control which one a fly would land on. A midnight blue Cadillac pulled up to the Thorps' Cambridge apartment in February 1961. The driver was a dazzling blonde in mink; next to her, another blonde in mink. Not until the women got out was the elderly, gnomelike Kimmel visible. He introduced the blondes as his nieces and did not seem to be joking.

Kimmel's cut would be 90 percent. Thorp agreed — he was more interested in proving the concept than in the money. The deal was sealed when Kimmel pulled a pearl necklace from his cashmere pocket and presented it to Vivian. Thorp talked the bankroll down from $100,000 to $10,000 because Kelly-optimal bets with a larger bankroll would have exceeded the table limits.

The Reno experiment produced indelible scenes. At Harolds Club, Thorp won $500 in fifteen minutes before the owner's son ordered progressively deeper shuffles to foil his counting. At another casino, Thorp watched a dealer hold the deck edge-up, briefly bend back the top card's corner — a queen of hearts that would have busted her — and with imperceptible sleight of hand deal the second card instead. Eddie Hand bellowed out what she had done; the pit boss said there was nothing he could do. At Harvey's Wagon Wheel, Thorp and Kimmel cleaned out the table's money tray twice in two hours. Beautiful women passed and smiled at Thorp, who was puzzled by his sudden popularity. Kimmel, unable to leave, lost $11,000 in forty-five minutes.

"As Eddie Hand had said, Kimmel was 'more trouble than an $18 whore.'" — Fortune's Formula

The trip total: $10,000 into $21,000 in thirty person-hours — would have been $32,000 without Kimmel's binge. The Las Vegas roulette trip in June 1961 was more cautious: Claude posed as a system player recording numbers, Betty looked the most innocent (her hair hid the wires), and Vivian took lookout. They bet ten-cent chips in deference to jitters. When a number hit, they won $3.60. Thin wires kept breaking, and the team brought soldering irons for repairs. Practical problems prevented serious gambling, but the concept was proven.

By 1966, Thorp was ahead about $25,000 from blackjack after a dozen trips — a trifle by Vegas standards. The casinos fought back with countermeasures and worse. At one casino, Thorp's coffee was drugged; he staggered from the table with dilated eyes and took eight hours to recover. Three known beatings of card-counters occurred; one had his face "caved in." The beatings took place in the counting room — soundproof, ideal for torture. The Las Vegas Resort Hotel Association briefly changed blackjack rules in 1964, but blackjack play dropped so precipitously that casinos reverted within weeks, settling instead on the "professor stopper" or "shoe" — multiple decks shuffled together.

Part Three: Arbitrage

Paul Samuelson loved Harvard, but the love was not entirely requited. A colleague had been passed over for tenure due to a "disability" — the disability was coming from Kansas. Samuelson was Jewish, and in 1940 he accepted an offer from MIT, a science and engineering school willing to hire a Jew just because he was smart. He made economics mathematical and channeled his encyclopedic knowledge into the perennial bestselling textbook Economics.

"Let those who will, write the nation's laws, if I can write its textbooks." — Paul Samuelson

The efficient market hypothesis emerged from the discovery that stock prices follow a random walk. British statistician Maurice Kendall analyzed weekly wheat prices from 1883 to 1934 and found they wandered aimlessly, "almost as if the Demon of Chance drew a random number." Louis Bachelier's forgotten 1900 thesis had anticipated the idea: when a stock's price reflects everything known, future changes must be random. Samuelson's skepticism was formalized as the efficient market hypothesis — the market is so good at pricing securities that no one can beat it except by luck. Eugene Fama developed it in three forms: weak (you can't predict from past prices), semistrong (you can't beat using public information), and strong (you can't beat even with private information).

Shannon, meanwhile, had turned to the stock market. His first wife Norma Levor had left him during a breakdown at Princeton; she moved to Hollywood, joined the Communist Party, was blacklisted, and fled to France. Shannon married Betty Moore, a Rosie-the- Riveter type from Bell Labs' machine shop who could work a drill press and lathe. Before the second marriage, Shannon kept his life savings in a checking account earning no interest. Betty suggested bonds or stocks; Shannon filled three library shelves with about a hundred books on economics, including Adam Smith, von Neumann, and his favorite, Fred Schwed's Where Are the Customers' Yachts?

Thorp, forced out of blackjack by casino heat, had discovered warrants — stock options that were priced like carnival games, costing too much given their probability of paying off. His innovation was delta hedging: calculating exactly how much stock to buy to offset the risk of short-selling warrants. Unlike irrational stock prices, warrants have expiration dates — mispricing can't persist forever. Thorp and Sheen Kassouf wrote Beat the Market in 1967, the first book to discuss delta hedging in print, and parlayed $40,000 into $100,000.

In 1969, a Dartmouth philosophy major turned stockbroker named James "Jay" Regan contacted Thorp. He was one of the few who had read Beat the Market and appreciated its importance. They agreed to a bicoastal partnership: Thorp did the math in California, Regan handled the business in Princeton. Their fund, Convertible Hedge Associates (later Princeton-Newport Partners), launched in November 1969.

The fund's early investors included Ralph Gerard, a UC Irvine dean who was a relative of Benjamin Graham and introduced Thorp to a money manager named Warren Buffett. Thorp met Buffett for bridge at the Buffetts' Emerald Bay home; they hit it off over nontransitive dice. Ed and Vivian believed Buffett would one day be the richest man in America. Bob Evans, head of Paramount Studios, also invested — Evans's first coup was buying the rights to Mario Puzo's The Godfather for $12,500, because Puzo owed the mob $10,000 in gambling debts and they were about to break his arms.

Princeton-Newport's performance was extraordinary. Unknown to academia, Thorp had devised his own version of what became the Black-Scholes pricing formulas by 1967 — years before Fischer Black, Myron Scholes, and Robert C. Merton published theirs. In 1972, Thorp's computer identified Resorts International warrants as incredibly underpriced at 27 cents when his model said they were worth about $4. He bought roughly 10,800 warrants for $2,900. By the mid-1980s, he sold them for $100 each — a 370-fold return, roughly 80 percent annually over a decade.

The fund executed the largest trade ever on the NYSE during the 1981 AT&T breakup: buying 5 million shares of old AT&T and shorting equivalent amounts of the new companies, a $333 million position at six-to-one leverage. It cleared $1.6 million in profit on the former employer of both Shannon and Kelly. In 1982, the fund committed $25 million to S&P 500 futures, executing up to 700 trades per day — more than one percent of total NYSE volume — and earned $6 million in four months before the market caught on.

Princeton-Newport never had a down year, quarter, or even a down month for thirteen consecutive years. Regan lived on a baronial 225-acre New Jersey ranch and raised horses. Thorp, the introvert, described the fund's approach as "getting rich slow" and didn't quit his UC Irvine teaching job until 1982. He eventually bought the largest home in Newport Beach, complete with a fallout shelter — sixteen-inch concrete walls and steel doors, calculated to withstand a one-megaton hydrogen bomb blast within one mile.

Part Four: St. Petersburg Wager

Daniel Bernoulli came from a family of competitive geniuses. His uncle Jakob discovered the law of large numbers. His father Johann threw Daniel out of the house after they jointly won the French Academy of Sciences prize in 1734, then published a nearly identical book under his own name with a false date, claiming Daniel had plagiarized him. Daniel fled to St. Petersburg to work for the Westernizing Russian court, where he wrote the article that would eventually connect to Shannon and Kelly's ideas two centuries later.

The St. Petersburg paradox concerns a coin-toss game with escalating payoffs: 1 ducat if heads appears on the first toss, 2 on the second, 4 on the third, doubling each time. The expected value of the game is infinite — each possibility contributes half a ducat, and the sum of infinitely many halves is infinite. The implication is absurd: no price should be too high to pay for the privilege of playing.

"Although the standard calculation shows that the value of Paul's expectation is infinitely great, it has…to be admitted that any fairly reasonable man would sell his chance, with great pleasure, for twenty ducats."— Daniel Bernoulli

Bernoulli's solution distinguished between money and utility — the subjective value people place on money. Your second ducat, like your second million, is never quite as sweet. A friend twice as rich values a $100 bet only half as much as you would. This logarithmic utility function adjusts the infinite series downward, making it converge to a finite, modest sum. But Bernoulli's real contribution, overlooked for centuries, was broader: risky ventures should be evaluated by the geometric mean of outcomes, not the arithmetic mean.

The geometric mean always falls below the arithmetic mean unless all values are identical. A double-or-nothing bet on your entire net worth has an arithmetic mean equal to your starting wealth, but a geometric mean of zero — reflecting the catastrophic risk of total loss. Bernoulli called this "Nature's admonition to avoid the dice altogether." Insurance is always an unfavorable wager by arithmetic mean, since the insurance company must profit from premiums. But a poor merchant shipping goods overseas improves his geometric mean by buying insurance, and the wealthier insurance company also improves its geometric mean by selling it. Both parties benefit from the redistribution of risk.

The connection to Kelly is direct: Kelly's prescription, restated broadly, is to choose the wager or investment with the highest geometric mean of outcomes. Henry Latané, who entered the job market with a Harvard MBA in the grim year of 1930 and claimed to be the last man hired on Wall Street before the Depression, read the first English translation of Bernoulli's article in 1954 and realized the ideas could apply to stock portfolios. He presented at the Cowles Foundation Seminar at Yale in 1956, with Harry Markowitz in attendance, and developed what he called the geometric mean criterion.

The rivalry between Latané's geometric mean approach and Markowitz's mean-variance analysis became one of the most contentious debates in financial economics. Markowitz used statistics to show how diversification cuts risk, plotting portfolios on an "efficient frontier." But mean-variance is a static, single-period theory — it assumes you buy now and sell at the end of a given time frame. Most investors buy and hold, reinvesting profits. The geometric mean criterion handles this compounding naturally: gambles that seem favorable as one-shot bets can be ruinous when repeated.

Part Five: RICO

Ivan Boesky had a mysterious past. He claimed his father ran Detroit delicatessens, though he actually owned topless bars called the Brass Rail. After graduating from law school five years late (having dropped out twice), Boesky married into wealth and specialized in risk arbitrage — buying targets and shorting acquirers in announced mergers. Wall Street called him "Piggy" for his appetite for leverage; he claimed to achieve nine-to-one leverage through "rolling" — buying and selling the same stock simultaneously to exploit five-day settlement windows.

Boesky's most important tipster was Martin Siegel, an investment banker at Kidder Peabody. Their arrangement produced some of the most cinematic scenes in the book. In January 1983, Siegel went to the Plaza Hotel lobby and was approached by a muscular Iranian who said "Red light." Siegel replied "Green light." The courier handed over a briefcase with stacks of hundreds tied with ribbons reading "Caesar's Palace." Boesky justified the cloak-and-dagger routine by claiming he had once been a CIA agent in Iran. The following year, the same courier delivered $250,000 — except it was $40,000 short, and some bills were singles. Siegel confronted Boesky, who insisted the courier was a man of "impeccable character who would never steal money."

"You never want to talk to me. You never call anymore. Don't you love me anymore?" — Ivan Boesky to Martin Siegel, as their relationship deteriorated

Rudolph Giuliani arrived to prosecute Wall Street with a complex inheritance. His uncle was a bookie and loan shark for the mob; his father Harold had spent a year in Sing Sing for holding up a milkman at gunpoint. Harold later became bartender and muscle for a loan-sharking operation, beating delinquent borrowers with a baseball bat. The young Giuliani idolized the Kennedys and kept a dartboard with Nixon's picture in college. He changed his registration to Republican only a month before being named the third highest official in Reagan's Justice Department.

Giuliani wielded the RICO (Racketeer- Influenced and Corrupt Organizations) statute like a weapon, using it to freeze assets from indictment through trial. The law had been authored by Notre Dame professor G. Robert Blakey, a former aide to Robert Kennedy, and the acronym was allegedly chosen to recall Edward G. Robinson's Rico Bandello in Little Caesar. Giuliani used it to give Fat Tony Salerno, rated by Fortune as America's wealthiest gangster, a hundred-year sentence. On an FBI tape, Salerno said of the Kimmel family's parking lot empire: "We own Kinney."

The investigation reached Princeton-Newport through Michael Milken. The fund had engaged in "stock parking" — selling securities at a loss to Milken's Drexel operation with a verbal understanding to buy them back at a similar price, converting long-term capital losses to short-term for tax benefits. Wiretapped conversations were damning. On December 17, 1987, federal agents raided Princeton-Newport's offices. Thorp thought it was a publicity stunt. Regan showed up to a meeting with prosecutors wearing a cap reading "shit happens."

RICO charges were filed in the summer of 1988 — the first time the statute had been used against a securities firm. The fund dissolved in December 1988 as investors fled. Its nineteen-year record remained extraordinary: $1 invested at the outset had grown to roughly $14.78, a compound return of 15.1 percent annually after fees, with a standard deviation of only about 4 percent. The fund had never had a down year, quarter, or month. The jury convicted on 63 of 64 counts, but all six convictions were overturned on appeal. Regan paid about $5 million in legal fees. Thorp put up a dartboard in Newport Beach with Giuliani's photo.

"There was an explosion in the hedge fund world shortly after Princeton-Newport's dissolution. We could have been running a five- or ten-billion-dollar hedge fund." — Ed Thorp

Part Six: Blowing Up

John Meriwether, born in Chicago in 1947, was mathematically inclined and educated by priests. He attended Northwestern on a golf caddie scholarship, taught high school math, and got his MBA at the University of Chicago. At Salomon Brothers, when New York City nearly defaulted and the bond market panicked, Meriwether reasoned that the city's problems were irrelevant to other municipalities' credit, bought government bonds at bargain prices, and made a killing. He started Salomon's Arbitrage Group, which became the firm's biggest profit maker. His true passion was horse racing — he hedged his daily bets with rosary beads in his briefcase.

Long-Term Capital Management recruited Robert C. Merton and Myron Scholes — "like putting Michael Jordan and Muhammad Ali on the same team." Thorp declined to invest: he worried Merton and Scholes lacked experience investing other people's money, and he'd heard Meriwether was a "martingale man" who doubled down when things went against him. LTCM became the first fund to raise a billion dollars, charging 25 percent of profits plus 1 percent of assets. The investor list read like a who's who: Harvard, Merrill Lynch, the Kuwait pension fund, the Bank of China, Mike Ovitz.

Returns were spectacular: 20 percent in 1994, 43 percent in 1995, 41 percent in 1996. Myron Scholes described the strategy as "vacuuming up nickels no one else could see," and would snatch an imaginary nickel off the floor during presentations. The core trades were convergence bets in government bonds — buying older "off the run" bonds and shorting newer "on the run" issues, waiting for the irrational premium on new bonds to disappear. The actual return per dollar invested was 0.67 percent; LTCM achieved its spectacular returns through roughly 30x leverage.

When Andrew Chow of Conseco challenged Scholes on risk, Scholes snapped: "You're the reason — because of fools like you we can." Conseco did not invest.

Russia's default on August 17, 1998, triggered a global flight to quality that reversed LTCM's positions. The fund lost $551 million in a single week. The problem was not simply leverage or "fat tails" in market distributions. William Ziemba estimated LTCM's leverage at roughly twice the Kelly level — the point at which true compound growth rate falls to near zero. Thorp linked the collapse directly to Merton and Scholes's philosophical opposition to the Kelly criterion: "I could see that they didn't understand how it controlled the danger of extreme risk."

"Ten or 15 guys with an average IQ of maybe 170 could get themselves into a position where they can lose all their money."— Warren Buffett

Peter Fisher of the New York Fed received LTCM's "Risk Aggregator" printout and discovered roughly 375 positions making nearly identical bets worldwide. "This was supposed to be diversification, but it wasn't." A consortium invested $3.625 billion to prevent a collapse. LTCM lost $4.4 billion — 90 percent of its peak. Larry Hilibrand, who had borrowed $24 million from Crédit Lyonnais, went from a net worth of about $100 million to $20 million in debt. He asked the bailout consortium to cover his personal debts; they said no.

"A man who risks his entire fortune acts like a simpleton, however great may be the possible gain." — Daniel Bernoulli (1738)

The pattern repeated with John Koonmen's Eifuku Master Trust ("eternal luck"). Koonmen, who had lost so much at Lehman Brothers Tokyo that they scaled back bonuses for the entire department, acquired LTCM's pool table and started his own fund. Investors included Soros and UBS. After losing 24 percent in 2001 and gaining 76 percent in 2002, Eifuku lost 98 percent in the first seven trading days of 2003 — $1.4 billion in positions backed by just $155 million across three trades. Koonmen was "eerily emotionless." He closed the fund and went to Africa to photograph wildlife.

Part Seven: Signal and Noise

Shannon's stock-picking approach was related to signal extraction from noise — separating a stock's true earnings growth trajectory from the random fluctuations that obscure it. He plotted earnings on logarithmic graph paper, drew trend lines into the future, and visited start-up companies to talk with management. He bought Kentucky Fried Chicken stock and served the food to friends: "If we try it and don't like it, we simply won't consider an investment." According to Barron's in 1986, Shannon outperformed all but 3 of 77 money managers — and 1,025 of 1,026 mutual funds. His portfolio achieved roughly 28 percent annually from the late 1950s through 1986, working with his wife and a decrepit Apple II computer.

Shannon's three greatest picks told the story: Teledyne, founded by his friend Henry Singleton, was bought at 88 cents a share and reached $300 twenty-five years later. Codex Corporation, an MIT spinoff, cost 50 cents a share and was eventually absorbed by Motorola at $40. Harrison Labs, started by a Bell Labs scientist, was Shannon's best — a 29 percent annual return over thirty-two years, a 3,500-fold increase. The blackboard equation Ed Thorp had once spotted in Shannon's office — 211 = 2,048 — had come true.

But Shannon's gain came from three smart picks, and three data points lack statistical significance. Richard Roll tried investing in every single anomaly that academics dreamed up and had "yet to make a nickel." The efficient market debate remained unresolved. Buffett's famous coin-toss analogy drove the point home: if 215 million Americans paired off and bet a dollar on coin tosses, after twenty rounds there would be 215 survivors with over a million dollars each. Some would write books called How I Turned a Dollar into a Million. "Then some business school professor will probably be rude enough to bring up the fact that if 215 million orangutans had engaged in a similar exercise, the result would be the same — 215 egotistical orangutans with twenty straight winning flips."

After Princeton-Newport's dissolution, Thorp pioneered statistical arbitrage. The Indicators Project, run by Jerome Baesel, had found that stocks that went up the most in two weeks tended to fall back, and those that went down the most tended to rise — markets overreact. Jerry Bamberger independently developed the same idea at Morgan Stanley, then quit for Thorp. Bamberger was a chain-smoker who ate the same tuna salad sandwich every day for six years. Their BOSS Partners earned 25 to 30 percent annualized in 1985 before competition from Morgan Stanley's Nunzio Tartaglia killed the edge.

Thorp launched Ridgeline Partners in August 1994, capped at about $300 million, trading roughly 4 million shares a day. Each trade had about a 0.5 percent edge, half going to transaction costs, with the remaining quarter-percent adding up to handsome returns. Ridgeline averaged 18 percent annually after fees, including a stunning 47 percent in 1998 — the year of the Russian default that destroyed LTCM. Over 28.5 years, Thorp claimed a 20 percent annual return with a 2 percent standard deviation, comprising roughly 1.25 million individual bets.

The Kelly criterion found its greatest pure application not on Wall Street but at the Hong Kong racetracks. Alan Woods, a professional blackjack player, partnered with Bill Benter and Walter Simmons to build a computer system using 130 variables and video analysis. They used fractional Kelly accounting for the effect of their own wagers on pari-mutuel odds and needed up to 100 people to hustle bets to the windows. After splitting acrimoniously, all three became multimillionaires. Woods, who claimed $150 million in lifetime winnings, lived as a white-haired recluse in a Manila high-rise, receiving race results as instant messages with emoticons, having not watched a horse race in person in eighteen years.

"When you look at how much money I have consistently made from the horses, compared to what I've done in the market, horses would seem to be a far safer investment than stocks." — Alan Woods

Claude Shannon died on February 24, 2001 — the same year as HAL. Memory lapses had worsened into Alzheimer's disease; he would be driving and realize he didn't know where he was going, and eventually failed to recognize his own children. Betty put him in a Medford, Massachusetts nursing home and visited daily. Shannon was a tinkerer to the end — he customized other patients' walkers and took apart the home's fax machine. Few obituaries mentioned his influence on gambling and investment.

Thorp closed Ridgeline Partners in October 2002 and endowed a chair at UC Irvine mathematics with a million dollars invested entirely in stocks, limiting the university to a 2 percent yearly withdrawal — expected to compound exponentially. He had arranged to have his body cryonically frozen, following the Kelly criterion into infinity.

Key Takeaways

Information Is Money

From the race wire to Shannon's entropy to Thorp's blackjack system, the book's central thesis is that information asymmetry creates exploitable edges. The formula never changes: whoever has better information and knows how to size their bets wins.

The Kelly Criterion Is About Survival

Kelly betting is not just a formula for maximizing returns — it's a survival mechanism. By scaling bets proportionally to your bankroll, you can never go broke. You might experience severe drawdowns (50 percent chance of ever losing half your wealth), but you always recover. LTCM's failure was fundamentally a failure to understand this: at twice the Kelly level, true compound growth rate falls to near zero.

Geometric Mean Trumps Arithmetic Mean

Bernoulli saw it in 1738, Kelly formalized it in 1956, and Thorp proved it with real money: when you reinvest profits, the geometric mean of outcomes is the only measure that matters. A bet that looks attractive by arithmetic mean can be catastrophic when compounded. The double-or- nothing wager on your entire net worth has an arithmetic mean equal to your starting wealth but a geometric mean of zero.

Overbetting Is Always Bad

Unlike leverage or fat tails, which can sometimes work in your favor, overbetting is always destructive. At twice the Kelly bet, compound returns fall to zero. Serious overbetting produces negative returns — you literally get poorer by betting. Half-Kelly is a popular compromise: it cuts volatility drastically while decreasing return by only a quarter.

The Feud Obscured the Truth

Samuelson and Merton called the Kelly criterion a "fallacy" and a "complete swindle," while Thorp and Latané produced real-world results that validated it. The debate was partly tribal — economists resented ideas from information theorists and gamblers. As Thomas Cover observed, the Kelly criterion keeps turning up in unexpected contexts, "like pi," which usually means "it's fundamental."

Markets Are Not Perfectly Efficient

Shannon's 28 percent returns, Thorp's decades of outperformance, and the Hong Kong racing syndicates all challenge the strong form of market efficiency. But Shannon's gains came from three picks — statistically insignificant — and Thorp's edge required extraordinary mathematical sophistication. For most people, index funds remain the rational choice.

Contrarian Ideas

Utility Theory May Be Unnecessary

The pro-Kelly camp argues that utility functions are an unnecessary concept. Kelly's system "has nothing to do with the value function which he attached to his money" — it simply maximizes compound growth. Dollars under Kelly survive and grow faster than under any alternative system. The only stable human preference is wanting as much money as possible, as fast as possible.

Mean-Variance Analysis Is Incomplete

Markowitz's framework treats risk as variance, but variance is not a perfect measure of risk. A penny stock with a zero outcome (total loss) can have lower variance than a volatile stock with no possibility of total loss. Mean-variance refuses to distinguish between the two, while Kelly's geometric mean immediately flags the possibility of ruin.

The Academic Establishment Resisted Real Evidence

Samuelson invested in Berkshire Hathaway while publicly dismissing the possibility of beating the market. Critics called his circle the "Random Walk Cosa Nostra," and Nobel prizes flowed to associates who shared their views. Thorp's paper arguing Kelly should replace Markowitz as the guide to portfolio selection was published in an obscure statistics journal because no major economics journal would have accepted such heresy.

Fund Managers Have Perverse Incentives

Shannon recognized that a fund manager has a call option on investors' wealth — sharing the upside but not the downside. This creates intense temptation to boost returns through overbetting. Investors choose funds on a few basis points of return difference, so the manager who takes excessive risk gets the most capital.

Frameworks & Formulas

The Kelly Formula

f* = edge / odds, where f* is the fraction of bankroll to wager, edge is the expected profit per dollar bet, and odds is the payoff ratio. When extended to multiple outcomes, the rule becomes: maximize the expected logarithm of wealth, or equivalently, maximize the geometric mean of outcomes.

Shannon's Rebalancing Demon

Put half your capital in a volatile stock and half in cash. Each day, rebalance to restore the 50-50 split. In a geometric random walk (stock either doubles or halves with equal probability), this produces exponentially growing wealth from a stock that goes nowhere — like Maxwell's demon creating a temperature gradient from random molecular motion. Shannon himself dismissed the practical application: the commissions would kill you.

Half-Kelly

Stake half the Kelly-optimal amount. This cuts volatility drastically while decreasing return by only about a quarter. Full Kelly gives a one-in-three chance of halving your bankroll before doubling it; half-Kelly reduces this to one in nine. Bill Benter, who made millions at Hong Kong racetracks, noted that the best models can easily overestimate edge by a factor of two — someone attempting Kelly might unknowingly be placing a twice-Kelly bet.

The 50% Rule

In an infinite series of Kelly bets, the chance of your bankroll ever dipping to half its current level is exactly one-half. The chance of ever dipping to one-third is one-third; to one percent, one percent. The chance of ever reaching zero is zero. You always recover, but you spend a lot of time being less wealthy than you were.

Fortune's Formula: The Untold Story of the Scientific Betting System That Beat the Casinos and Wall Street

By William Poundstone · 2005 · Hill and Wang / Farrar, Straus and Giroux · 400 pages

A narrative history linking information theory, gambling, organized crime, and Wall Street through the stories of Claude Shannon, John Kelly, Ed Thorp, and the Kelly criterion — the formula that connects the speed of information transmission to the maximum rate of wealth growth.

About the Author

William Poundstone is an American author and journalist who has written more than a dozen books on science, mathematics, and human behavior. His previous works include Prisoner's Dilemma (on John von Neumann and game theory), How Would You Move Mount Fuji? (on hiring puzzles at Microsoft), and Carl Sagan: A Life in the Cosmos. Poundstone has a talent for making abstract mathematical concepts come alive through narrative, weaving together real characters, specific numbers, and vivid scenes into stories that read like thrillers.

His interests span the boundary between theoretical knowledge and practical application — the same territory occupied by Shannon, Kelly, and Thorp. Fortune's Formula reflects this perfectly: it's a book about abstract mathematics that reads like a mob history, and a mob history that illuminates some of the deepest questions in finance and probability.

Why This Book Matters

Fortune's Formula connects three worlds that rarely appear in the same story: organized crime, pure mathematics, and Wall Street. The thread linking them is information — from the race wire that made gangsters rich, to Shannon's entropy equations, to the Kelly criterion that tells you exactly how much to bet when you have an edge. The book makes a powerful case that the most important financial ideas of the twentieth century came not from economists but from physicists, mathematicians, and information theorists working at the margins of academic respectability.

The LTCM collapse gives the argument its dramatic force. The same Nobel laureates who dismissed the Kelly criterion as a "fallacy" proceeded to overbet by a factor of two and nearly brought down the global financial system. Thorp, who understood Kelly, closed October 1987 about even and earned 47 percent during the 1998 Russian crisis that destroyed LTCM. The lesson is not just theoretical: how you size your bets matters more than which bets you choose. That insight — first glimpsed by Bernoulli in 1738, formalized by Kelly in 1956, and proven with billions of dollars by Thorp over decades — remains the book's most enduring contribution.

What Didn't Fit

Poundstone's book reads like a crime novel crossed with a mathematics textbook. Here are the most vivid stories that did not make the chapter coverage.

The Murder of James Ragan

James Ragan, who ran the Continental Press wire service, was shot at a Chicago intersection when a banana truck pulled up alongside his car, the tarpaulin lifted, and two shots rang out. Ragan survived the initial shooting and spent six weeks under police guard — until someone poisoned him with mercury, either in his Coca-Colas or through his catheter. The mob seized Continental Press after his death.

Longy Zwillman: Gangster, Art Connoisseur, Muzak Owner

Longy Zwillman was the real mastermind behind the New Jersey gambling Combination. When a minor thug named Leo Kaplus threatened to kick Zwillman in the balls, Zwillman tracked him to a Newark bar and shot him once in the testicles. He was a connoisseur of art, books, and opera who dressed soberly and drove American-built cars. He bankrolled Jean Harlow's early career, lending or bribing Columbia Pictures' Harry Cohn a reported $500,000 to secure her a two-picture deal. His most peculiar investment: the Muzak Corporation, which the Chicago Crime Commission concluded he controlled. He was found dead in 1959, hanged with a plastic electrical cord.

SIGSALY: The 55-Ton Telephone

Shannon's wartime work included SIGSALY, the first digitally scrambled wireless phone system. Each terminal was room-sized and weighed 55 tons, with isolation booths and air conditioning. The encryption key was a vinyl LP record of pure white noise — a one-time pad. “Adding” the noise to Roosevelt's voice produced indecipherable hiss; to recover words, you “subtracted” the same key noise at the other end. Master recordings were destroyed after pressing exactly the number of copies needed.

Hoover's Fixed Races

J. Edgar Hoover regularly left the office to bet on horses, with agents placing his real bets at the hundred-dollar window. He got inside tips from Frank Costello via Walter Winchell — when the mob fixed a race, Costello passed along the winning horse. After Hoover's death in 1972, Costello told the Justice Department: “You'll never know how many races I had to fix for those lousy bets of his.”

Shannon and Kelly Scale the Dome

One evening at MIT circa 1956, Shannon and Kelly kicked off their shoes and began scaling the Kresge Auditorium dome, an Eero Saarinen building. Campus police showed up. Robert Fano barely talked them out of arresting the “distinguished visitors from Bell Telephone Laboratories.”

Black Monday: 1 in 10160

Mark Rubinstein estimated the probability of the S&P falling 29% in a single day at 1 in 10160. Princeton-Newport, using Kelly-sized positions, lost only about $2 million on Black Monday, made roughly $2 million in new trades immediately after, and finished 1987 up 34%.

Mulheren, Guns, and Fat Tony Salerno

After Boesky implicated John Mulheren, Mulheren (off his lithium for manic-depressive illness) packed guns and went after Boesky. Police stopped him. Giuliani offered a deal: overlook the threat if Mulheren testified on stock parking. Mulheren indignantly refused. In prison he met Fat Tony Salerno, who told him: “You're the only guy on Wall Street who's not a rat.”

Shannon's Portfolio: Sixty-Fold

A snapshot of Shannon's portfolio on January 22, 1981 showed a total value of $582,717.50 with an average appreciation of sixty-fold. Eighty-one percent was in Teledyne, with Motorola and Hewlett-Packard making up most of the rest. Shannon was not a passive investor — he served as an active Teledyne board member, scouted acquisitions, and wrote detailed reports on potential investments.

Thorp's Basketball System

Thorp developed a basketball betting system that identified wagers with a typical 6% edge, sized using the Kelly criterion. Over 101 days in early 1994, it produced $123,000 in profit on $50,000 — and almost literally broke Little Caesar's sports book, which went out of business during the experiment. Thorp quit because the system required someone in Las Vegas to transport lots of cash, which made everyone nervous.

God Knew the Stock Market Was Efficient

At a 1998 UCLA conference, Eugene Fama pointed at Robert Haugen, called him a criminal, and declared: “God knew that the stock market was efficient.”
