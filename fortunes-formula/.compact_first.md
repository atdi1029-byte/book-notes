## Prologue: The Wire Service

### Chicago, 1905: The Information Edge

- In 1905, **race results** were worth fortunes — gamblers paid premium prices for advance knowledge of winners before odds could adjust
- The **wire service** transmitted race results from tracks to bookmakers via telegraph, creating information asymmetry
- Whoever controlled the wire controlled the gambling industry
- **Mont Tennes**, a Chicago gambling kingpin, monopolized the wire service and crushed competitors by delaying results to disfavored bookies
- Tennes understood **exclusive information access** was more valuable than running bets themselves
- The wire service became a classic **monopoly rent** — Tennes charged exorbitant fees because bookies had no alternative
- This created the first modern example of **monetizing an information advantage** in gambling

> "The wire service was worth a fortune to anyone who controlled it. It was worth even more to anyone who could use it without paying."

- The book's central figure, **Claude Shannon**, would later revolutionize information theory at Bell Labs
- The prologue establishes the core theme: **the relationship between information, uncertainty, and profit**
- From race wires to Shannon's mathematical theories to Wall Street, the formula stays the same
- **Information asymmetry** creates exploitable edges, whether in 1905 Chicago or modern financial markets

## Part One: Entropy

### Claude Shannon: The Juggling Mathematician

- **Claude Elwood Shannon** (1916–2001), born in Gaylord, Michigan — built model planes and a telegraph system to a friend's house as a boy
- Studied electrical engineering at University of Michigan, then MIT for graduate work
- Shannon was a **polymath**: juggled, rode unicycles, built mechanical mice, invented chess-playing machines
- His playful approach to problems masked profound mathematical insight
- At MIT, he connected **Boolean algebra** to electrical circuits — his 1937 master's thesis is considered one of the most important of the 20th century
- Shannon realized **binary circuits** (on/off switches) could perform logical operations
- Any logical statement could be represented by circuits using **AND, OR, NOT gates**
- This insight became the foundation of **digital computing** — all modern computers descend from this idea
- **George Boole** (1815–1864) had created symbolic logic in the 1850s
- Shannon saw that relays and switches in telephone circuits were physical embodiments of Boole's abstract logic

### World War II and Cryptography

- During WWII, Shannon worked on **cryptography** and fire-control systems at Bell Labs
- Analyzed the security of encryption systems, asking: what is unbreakable secrecy?
- Developed the concept of **perfect secrecy**: proved the **one-time pad** achieves it if the key is truly random, used once, and as long as the message
- This work led directly to his information theory — encryption is about **reducing an eavesdropper's information**

### Information Theory: The 1948 Paper

- Shannon's landmark paper, **"A Mathematical Theory of Communication"** (Bell System Technical Journal, 1948), founded information theory
- Core insight: **information is measurable** — quantified in **bits**
- A **bit** is the answer to a yes/no question, the fundamental unit of information
- Introduced **entropy** (H) as a measure of uncertainty: H = -Σ p(i) log₂ p(i)
- High entropy = high uncertainty = more information needed to resolve it
- A fair coin flip has 1 bit of entropy; a weighted coin (90% heads) has less
- Shannon showed that **compressing data** is only possible because messages contain redundancy
- Proved every communication channel has a maximum rate for reliable transmission: **channel capacity**
- Revolutionary finding: you can get arbitrarily close to error-free communication by clever encoding, even in noisy environments
- This enabled everything from **deep-space probes** to **digital cell phones**

### Shannon's Gambling Connection

- Shannon became interested in **roulette** and **blackjack** as applications of information theory
- He saw gambling as a **decision-making problem** under uncertainty — exactly what information theory addresses
- In the 1950s–60s, Shannon and wife **Betty** built a wearable computer to predict roulette outcomes
- The device used physics (measuring wheel speed and ball trajectory) to gain an edge
- This was the first **wearable computer** designed to beat a casino
- Device had **toe-operated switches** hidden in shoes, transmitted predictions via radio to an earpiece
- The machine estimated the wheel's deceleration and the ball's trajectory using **Newtonian mechanics**
- Shannon and **Ed Thorp** tested it, achieving a **44% edge** in lab conditions, though practical casino use proved difficult

### Ed Thorp: The Math Professor Who Beat Blackjack

- **Edward O. Thorp**, MIT mathematics PhD, applied information theory to casino games
- In 1960–61, developed **card counting** for blackjack using probability theory
- Card counting tracks the ratio of high cards to low cards remaining in the deck
- When the deck is rich in tens and aces, the player has an advantage — bet more
- Thorp's system, refined with **IBM computer simulations**, provably beat the house
- Published **"Beat the Dealer"** (1962), the first book to prove blackjack was beatable — became a bestseller
- Casinos responded with countermeasures: multiple decks, frequent shuffling, banning suspected counters
- Shannon and Thorp met at MIT in 1960 — both saw gambling as applied information theory
- They collaborated on the roulette computer, blending physics, electronics, and probability

### John Payne and the Wire Service Birth

- **John Payne**, corrupt telegraph operator at **Western Union's Cincinnati office** (early 1900s), quit after anti-gambling policy
- Founded **Payne Telegraph Service of Cincinnati** to report race results to bookies
- Stationed employee at racetrack with hand mirror to flash winner in code to nearby building, which telegraphed results to pool halls across Cincinnati via leased wires
- Critical advantage: bookies knew results before bettors, preventing post-race bets on horses already won
- Chicago mayor **Carter Harrison II** banned racetrack betting — caused track attendance to plummet and illegal bookmaking to flourish

> "It is my intention to witness the sport of kings without the vice of kings." — Chicago mayor Carter Harrison II

### Mont Tennes and the Chicago Mob

- 1907: violent Chicago gangster **Mont Tennes** acquired Illinois franchise for Payne's wire service, named it **General News Bureau**
- Franchise cost Tennes $300/day; recouped many times over from 700+ Chicago bookie joints paying half their daily receipts
- Survived six bomb attacks (July–September 1907) from rival gangsters envious of wire service profits
- Eventually squeezed Payne out; General News Bureau expanded to New Orleans and San Francisco
- 1916: **Judge Kenesaw Mountain Landis** launched federal probe; **Clarence Darrow** represented Tennes, arguing Fifth Amendment
- 1927: Tennes retired, issued 100 shares of stock, sold them all, died peacefully 1941
- Bequeathed part of fortune to **Camp Honors**, character-building summer camp for wayward boys

### Moses Annenberg and the Trust

- Largest stockholder (48 shares): **Moses "Moe" Annenberg**, publisher of the *Racing Form*
- Hired **James Ragan** to run wire service; expanded by buying up smaller services or driving them out
- **Irving Wexler** (bootlegger, owner of **Greater New York News Service**) challenged Annenberg
- Annenberg retaliated brilliantly: knew Wexler was tapping into General News lines
- Delayed race results on the tapped line, phoned timely results to his own men, who placed big bets on winning horses with Wexler's subscribers (who received delayed results)
- Annenberg's men refunded losses to Wexler's bookies, advising switch to General News Bureau
- Service (also called **"the Trust"** or **"the Wire"**) expanded coast-to-coast, to Canada, Mexico, Cuba
- AT&T in-house legal opinion (1924): acknowledged customers were "bound to be owners of poolrooms and bookmakers"
- AT&T officially claimed shock that gambling occurred on its network; by mid-1930s, Annenberg was AT&T's fifth largest customer

### The Mob Seizes the Wire

- Chicago mobster **John Lynch** took Annenberg to court — attorney **Weymouth Kirkland** argued wire service was patently illegal, court had no jurisdiction (cited 1725 precedent: English judge refused to divide loot of two disputing highwaymen)
- Lynch appealed to **Al Capone's** mob (Capone in prison for tax evasion)
- Capone enforcer **Frank Nitti** told Ragan: ally with Capone or Annenberg dead in twenty-four hours
- Ragan refused; Annenberg fled to Miami; negotiations dragged for years
- Eventually agreed to pay Capone's people $1 million/year in protection money while retaining ownership
- 1939: Annenberg faced tax evasion charges; to prove reform, walked away from wire service
- **Continental Press Service** filled vacuum; **James Ragan** remained at helm
- Chicago mob approached Ragan again; he still refused, prepared affidavits implicating Nitti in attempted murder
- Most powerful Italian and Jewish mobsters allied in national organization called **"the Combination"**
- Combination founded rival: **Trans-American Publishing and News Service**

### Ben Siegel and Trans-American

- Trans-American run by **Ben Siegel** ("Bugsy" — a name he hated), New Yorker who moved to West Coast
- Siegel reasoned casino bookmaking draws people in to play other games — charged usual subscription *plus a cut of income*, in some cases 100% of bookmaking income
- June 24, 1946: **James Ragan** shot at Chicago intersection — banana truck pulled up, tarpaulin lifted, two shots rang out (one mangled arm/shoulder)
- Ragan spent six weeks under police guard; someone poisoned him (mercury in Coca-Colas or catheter)
- Mob seized Continental Press after Ragan's death

- Siegel decided Trans-American was his own business, not the Combination's
- Building **Flamingo hotel and casino** in Las Vegas; cost far exceeded projections, owed $2 million
- Told Combination's "board of directors" they could have Trans-American back for $2 million; board's response was cool
- Tracked down exiled **Lucky Luciano** in Havana; Luciano advised Siegel to give wire service back immediately

> "Go to hell and take the rest of those bastards along with you. I'll keep the goddamn wire as long as I want." — Ben Siegel to Lucky Luciano

- Rule: no board member got death sentence; Combination broke rule for first time with Siegel
- June 20, 1947: unknown gunman shot Siegel through trellis of rose arbor in Beverly Hills
- Fired full clip of steel-jacketed bullets from .30 caliber army carbine; four bullets hit — one passed through right eyeball, came to rest fifteen feet away on dining room floor tile
- Half hour before murder, four toughs assembled in Flamingo lobby; at appointed time, walked to manager, announced takeover

- Real mastermind behind Combination: New Jersey gangster **Longy Zwillman**
- Presented himself as legitimate businessman baffled why brought before committee

> "In the old days, I met everybody. Every place you went, you met somebody." — Longy Zwillman

## Shannon's Crisis and Project X

### Personal Breakdown at Princeton

- Shannon's postdoctoral work at Princeton with **Hermann Weyl** on topology went nowhere
- Marriage to **Norma** deteriorating; Shannon became pathologically reclusive
- During violent argument, Norma fled to Princeton Junction, took train to Manhattan, never returning
- Shannon told **Vannevar Bush** he feared complete nervous breakdown

> "For a time it looked as though he might completely crack up nervously and emotionally." — Weaver to Bush

### Project X and SIGSALY

- **Thornton Fry** renewed Bell Labs job offer; Shannon accepted and pivoted to war work
- **Project X** (declassified 1976): joint Bell Labs and Britain's **Bletchley Park** effort
- Team included Shannon and **Alan Turing**
- Built **SIGSALY**: first digitally scrambled wireless phone system — room-sized, 55-ton terminals with isolation booths and air conditioning
- Installed at Pentagon (Roosevelt), Selfridges basement (Churchill), and for **Field Marshal Montgomery** / **General MacArthur** in field
- Used the **onetime pad** — key was vinyl LP record of random **white noise**
- "Adding" noise to Roosevelt's voice produced indecipherable hiss; to recover words, "subtract" same key noise
- Master destroyed after pressing exact number needed; LPs distributed by trusted couriers

> "A secrecy system is almost identical with a noisy communications system." — Shannon

### Shannon and Turing

- **Alan Turing** cracked German **Enigma** cipher for Allies
- 1943: Turing visited Bell Labs; Shannon and Turing spoke daily in cafeteria
- Shannon working on measuring information using the **bit** (name credited to **John Tukey**)
- Turing told Shannon he had a unit called the **ban** — evidence making guess ten times more likely
- "Ban" from Banbury, town where cryptographic team's scratch paper was manufactured
- **The bit, not the ban, changed the world**

### The Transistor and 1948 Paper

- Shannon spotted strange object on **William Shockley's** desk: first **transistor**
- Shockley: solid-state amplifier could do anything vacuum tube could — small, could be miniaturized
- Hardware enabling Shannon's theory to become reality
- Shannon's information theory paper published 1948; Shannon then thirty-two, most work done earlier (1939-1943)
- Shannon habitually worked with office door closed, told few people what he was doing
- Bell Labs colleagues astonished he'd devised important result and sat on it

### Marriage to Betty Moore

- Shannon met **Mary Elizabeth Moore** ("Betty") through visits to **John Pierce's** office
- Pierce worked on radar, known as science fiction fan
- Betty was in math group's computing pool — Rosie-the-Riveter type who could work drill press and lathe
- One of only three female employees in machine shop
- First date December 1948; married March 27, 1949

### Move to MIT (1956) and Fading

- Shannon began teaching MIT spring 1956; initially expected 8-10 students, but forty showed up (MIT and Harvard faculty)
- After few months resigned from Bell Labs for MIT professorship; initial salary $17,000/year

> "Foreign visitors often spend a day at Bell Laboratories but spend six months at M.I.T." — Shannon

- Shannon enjoyed MIT stimulation in limited doses; did best work alone
- **Robert Fano**: "He kind of faded away, Claude."
- Shannon took few PhD students; often had to meet him at home
- **William Sutherland** remembers walking in on Shannon's oboe practice
- Betty: "He slept when he felt like sleeping," would spend hours at kitchen table thinking
- Shannon's publishing career nearly over; never completed the long-anticipated book on information theory
- **Marvin Minsky** speculated Shannon stopped because he felt he'd proven almost everything worth proving

### Artificial Intelligence Pioneer

- Shannon organized first major academic conference on AI at **Dartmouth** 1956
- Shannon's stature made field respectable
- Built early chess-playing computer and **outguessing machine** (prominent in early machine learning history)
- Talent for parrying inevitable ham-handed questions:

> Q. Will robots be complex enough to be friends of people?
> A. I think so. But it's quite a distance away.
> Q. Can you imagine a robot President of the United States?
> A. Could be. I think by then you wouldn't speak of the United States any more.

### Shannon's Correspondence

- Letters from world-renowned scientists poured in; Shannon turned down increasing share
- Received letters from schoolchildren and crackpots alike
- CIA approached in 1983: "We need an excellent original thinker, and find that you are still the best"
- Shannon's procrastination system: folders labeled "Letters I've procrastinated on answering for too long"
- Letters now boxed at Library of Congress, many still awaiting answers

## Baldwin's Blackjack Breakthrough

- **Roger Baldwin** and three associates at U.S. Army's Aberdeen Proving Ground spent nearly three years calculating optimum blackjack strategy
- Discovered house edge was only **0.62 percent** with optimal play — radically better than roulette (5.26%) or slots (10-20%)
- **Edward Thorp** tested the strategy in Vegas with $10 — pile shrank to $1.50 in half an hour
- But experience sparked key insight: **card removal matters** — seeing three aces played means at most one remains
- Summer 1959 at MIT: Thorp gained access to **IBM 704 mainframe**, taught himself FORTRAN
- Discovered **fives** make the biggest difference to house advantage

### Meeting Claude Shannon

- Thorp needed National Academy member to submit paper — only mathematician member at MIT was Shannon
- Secretary warned Shannon had only minutes; Thorp outlined main points
- Shannon asked astute questions, was satisfied — told Thorp he appeared to have made the "big theoretical breakthrough"
- Shannon's main objection: the title — suggested changing "A Winning Strategy for Blackjack" to **"A Favorable Strategy for Twenty-one"**
- Shannon asked: "Are you working on anything else in the gambling area?"
- Thorp mentioned roulette; Shannon was riveted — **possibly more interested in roulette than blackjack** because it had a gadget to build
- They spoke for several hours; agreed to collaborate on a **roulette prediction machine**

## The Roulette Project (1960-1961)

- Worked from early 1960 to June 1961
- Ordered reconditioned professional roulette wheel from Reno with ivory balls: **$1,500**
- Set wheel on dusty slate billiard table, filmed with strobe light and special high-speed clock

### Roulette Physics

- **Rotor** rotates within stationary **stator**; croupier spins rotor one direction, tosses ball opposite
- Ball initially pressed against rim by centrifugal force; as momentum decreases, drops inward in spiral
- **Vanes (deflectors)**: eight diamond-shaped metal pieces; spiraling ball hits deflector about half the time
- Ball hits frets in head-on collision — relative speed high, hardest part to predict

### The Discovery: Wheel Tilt

- Thorp discovered he could guess approximately where ball would land — "like ESP"
- Reason: **the roulette wheel was slightly tilted**, making ball favor the downhill side
- Even half a chip's thickness of tilt gave substantial advantage
- Joked about slipping chip under wheel in casinos; Shannon proposed ice sliver that would melt

### The Prediction Device

- Built **cigarette-pack-sized device** with twelve transistors that slipped into pocket
- User clicked toe-operated switch in shoe when rotor passed reference point (twice for one revolution), then same for ball
- Device predicted which **octant** (1/8 of wheel) ball would land in
- Communicated via **musical tones** through concealed earphone
- Thorp mentally ticked off notes as *do re mi fa so la ti do* — last note indicated octant to bet
- Each octant consisted of five numbers close together on rotor
- Estimated **44 percent edge** on the house
- Shannon worried about secrecy — cited research showing any two people in U.S. connected by chain of about **three mutual friends** (precursor to "six degrees of separation" by **Stanley Milgram** in 1967)

## Gambler's Ruin

- Shannon had another worry: **easy to lose money even with a mathematical advantage**
- Chart gambler's wealth: with "fair" wagers, actual wealth varies wildly in a **"random walk"**
- Wealth hits zero relatively early — gambler tapped out permanently
- House edge accelerates ruin; gambler likely to go bust long before breaking the bank

### The Martingale System

- Best-known betting system: **"martingale" or "doubling up"**
- Bettor keeps doubling bet until win — guaranteed $1 ahead when streak ends
- Unlucky streak demands bets of $128...$256...$512 — either runs out of money or casino refuses
- **Casanova** used martingale in Venetian casinos, playing **Faro** with money of mistress **M—M—** (wealthy nun)

> "I still played on the martingale, but with such bad luck that I was soon left without a sequin."

- Dashed M—M—'s hope of escaping convent to marry Casanova
- **Faro** history: anonymous California woman so addicted she burned through $50,000 divorce settlement, turned prostitute on Douglas Alley, sold herself for 50 cents — minimum bet on the Faro table

> "She wasn't any bargain beauty...She sold herself for 50 cents an act. Fifty cents — the minimum bet on the Faro table."

## Randomness, Disorder, Uncertainty

### Shannon's Radical Insight: Meaning Is Irrelevant

- Shannon's most radical insight: **meaning is irrelevant** to information
- Information tied to **chance** — true information is unpredictable, essentially a series of random events
- The essence of a message is its **improbability** — more improbable, less compressible
- Predecessors: **Harry Nyquist** and **Ralph Hartley** (both Bell Labs, 1920s)
- Shannon read Hartley's paper in college, credited it as "an important influence on my life"

### Von Neumann's Suggestion: "Entropy"

- **John von Neumann** of Princeton's Institute for Advanced Study advised Shannon to use **entropy**

> "Use 'entropy' and you can never lose a debate — because no one really knows what 'entropy' means." — von Neumann

- Not entirely flippant: equation for entropy in physics takes same form as Shannon's information equation
- Both are logarithms of a probability measure
- Shannon christened Massachusetts home **"Entropy House"** — name whose appropriateness was apparent to all who saw the interior

## The Bandwagon

- Shannon achieved results that seemed "almost magical"
- Key finding: through message encoding, you can use virtually entire capacity of a communication channel
- Overturned conventional wisdom: you can encode messages so chance of error is arbitrarily small — without ANY additional bandwidth

> "To make the chance of error as small as you wish? Nobody had ever thought of that. How he got that insight, I don't know. But almost all modern communication engineering is based on that work." — Robert Fano

- **Sputnik changed everything**: satellite communications required maximum efficiency

## Private Wire: Kelly's Gambling Theory

### The Gambler's Problem

- Kelly's scenario: gambler with **"private wire"** gets advance tips — not 100% reliable but giving an edge
- **Greedy strategy** (bet everything): loses all first time a tip is wrong
- **Timid strategy** (minimum bets): squanders advantage of inside information
- Right strategy: **parlaying** — compound return like stock/bond investor
- Same math Shannon used for noisy channels applies to the greedy-but-prudent bettor

### Pari-Mutuel Betting and "Bet Your Beliefs"

- At U.S. racetracks, bettors set the odds (pari-mutuel system)
- Kelly's strategy: **bet entire bankroll each race, apportioning among horses according to informed estimate of each horse's chance of winning**
- Bet on every horse — one *has* to win — so you can never end up completely broke
- Most people find this hard to believe — you don't get rich in roulette by betting on every number
- But at a take-free track with inside tips, posted odds reflect uninformed beliefs
- Kelly's gambler ignores tote board, bets according to superior probability estimates
- In the long run, "bet your beliefs" earns maximum possible compound return
- **Less poetic reality**: U.S. racetracks skim 14-19% of amount wagered — would take phenomenal tips to overcome

### The Kelly Formula

- Formula: **edge/odds** = fraction of bankroll to wager on favorable bet
- **Edge**: expected average profit per dollar wagered
- **Odds**: public/tote-board odds (profit if you win)
- When edge is zero (no inside information), Kelly bet = zero — don't bet
- When race is fixed (sure thing), edge = odds → Kelly says bet 100%

### G_max = R

- One of Kelly's equations "as beautifully daring as E = mc²"
- **G_max = R**: maximum growth rate of gambler's money equals information transmission rate
- **G**: growth rate (compound return); **R**: Shannon's information rate in bits per time unit
- To Einstein's contemporaries, *E = mc²* made no sense; Kelly's equation provokes similar mystification: money = information?
- Currency units don't matter — *G_max* describes percentage return; 7% is 7% in any currency
- A single bit of certain inside information at even odds allows bettor to double money (100% return)
- **A bit is worth 10,000 basis points**
- Maximum occurs only when winning horse signaled in fewest bits possible
- Most concise way to identify one winning horse from eight equally likely: **three-bit code**
- Kelly's bettor can increase wealth by factor of 8 every time he receives 3 bits of sure information (8 = 2³)

### Kelly System Properties

- **Proportional betting**: each wager scaled to current bankroll — can never go broke
- Wealth grows **geometrically** vs. arithmetic growth for fixed wagers
- Simulation with biased coin (55% heads):
  - **Bet-it-all**: goes bust on third toss
  - **Martingale**: goes bust on bet 19
  - **Kelly**: increased bankroll **74-fold in 500 bets**
- **Underbetting** (half-Kelly): steadier but slower ($16.07 final)
- **Overbetting** (twice-Kelly): treacherous — briefly outperforms, then wealth falls to near zero, would return to $1 or less infinite times
- **Ashley Revell** (2004): London man sold all possessions, staked **$135,300** on red at Plaza Hotel roulette — won; decided against double or nothing

### Bernoulli's Law of Large Numbers

- **Jakob Bernoulli** (1713): law misunderstood by gamblers ever since
- American roulette: bet on red has **18/38 chance of winning**
- Does *not* mean red guaranteed to come up 18 times out of 38, nor that red is "due" after a run of black
- The *difference* between actual and expected number of reds tends to **grow** with more spins
- Bernoulli's law says only that the *percentage* of reds tends to approach expected percentage
- Innumerate gamblers found this "less practical value than they'd like" — no use profiting from negative-expectation bet
- **Shannon** invoked law of large numbers throughout information theory
- **Kelly system manages money** so bettor stays in game long enough for law of large numbers to work

### AT&T's Concerns and Publication

- AT&T worried about title "Information Theory and Gambling" — Kelly changed to **"A New Interpretation of Information Rate"**
- Shannon refereed the paper; appeared July 1956 in *Bell System Technical Journal*
- Gambling and investment differ only by a minus sign: favorable bets = "investments"; unfavorable = "gambling"

### Hoover and Fixed Races

## Part Three: Arbitrage

### Paul Samuelson and Market Efficiency

- **Paul Samuelson** loved Harvard; love not entirely requited — boxed into low-paying post
- Colleague passed over for tenure due to disability — disability was coming from Kansas (Samuelson was Jewish)
- 1940: accepted offer from **MIT** — science and engineering school, willing to hire a Jew just because he was smart
- Made economics mathematical; comfortable with differential equations as a physicist

> "Let those who will, write the nation's laws, if I can write its textbooks."

- 1948: channeled encyclopedic knowledge into *Economics* textbook — perennial bestseller
- Democrat who tutored **Adlai Stevenson** and **President Kennedy**
- Paid $125 for yearly warrant subscription; learned if tips were good, service would cost more

### Maurice Kendall and Random Walk

- 1953: British statistician **Maurice Kendall** analyzed weekly wheat prices (1883-1934)
- Unexpected conclusion: couldn't predict prices — "wandered aimlessly, almost as if the Demon of Chance drew a random number"
- People who *thought* they could predict stock market were deluding themselves

### Louis Bachelier's Forgotten Theory

- **Jimmie Savage** sent postcards: "Ever hear of this guy?"
- **Louis Bachelier's** 1900 thesis **"A Theory of Speculation"**: stock price changes fundamentally unpredictable
- When stock's price reflects everything known, future changes must be random

> "The mathematical expectation of the speculator is zero."

- Thesis got middling grade; Bachelier (born 1870, died 1946) spent career in obscurity
- Treatment more advanced than **Einstein's** 1905 Brownian motion paper — Einstein never heard of Bachelier
- Samuelson found mistake: model allowed negative prices
- Fixed with **log-normal random walk** — each day's price multiplied by random factor

### The Efficient Market Hypothesis

- Samuelson's skepticism formalized as **efficient market hypothesis** — market so good at pricing that no one beats it save by luck
- Favored analogy: choose stocks by throwing darts at financial pages
- **Eugene Fama** developed idea theoretically and empirically
- **Warren Buffett**: "I'd be a bum in the street with a tin cup if the markets were efficient"
- Samuelson even invested money in Berkshire Hathaway — Buffett's success fit within "unexplained cases"

### Fama's Three Forms

- **Weak**: Can't predict from past prices (technical analysis worthless)
- **Semistrong**: Can't beat using any public information (fundamental analysis worthless)
- **Strong**: Can't beat even with private information

> "A respect for evidence compels me to incline toward the hypothesis that most portfolio decision makers should go out of business — take up plumbing, teach Greek..."

- Critics called Samuelson's clique the **"Random Walk Cosa Nostra"**
- Nobel prizes flowed to Samuelson's students and associates who shared market efficiency views
- **Robert Fano** wrote paper arguing stock prices follow predictable cycles — told seeking publication "would be pointless"

## Thorp's Transition to Wall Street

### Silver Debacle

- By 1964, blackjack unprofitable due to casino heat — Thorp had $25,000 from blackjack plus $15,000 in savings
- Bought silver at $1.30/ounce; price rose to $2, bought more on margin; price fell, margin calls — lost ~$6,000
- Key lesson: **"You are unlikely to get an edge out of what you see in the news"**
- Read **Paul Cootner's** *The Random Character of Stock Market Prices* (1964, MIT Press) — collection of key articles on random walk model

### Discovery of Warrants

- Texas investors (life insurance stock experts) contacted Thorp — picks "promptly went down the tubes"; Thorp received defective steak knives as consolation
- Found **RHM Warrant Service** run by **Sidney Fried** — claimed warrants bought for pennies, sold for dollars
- After an hour of thinking, Thorp realized: **there was a simple way to price warrants** using random walk mathematics
- Most warrants **priced like carnival games** — cost too much given win probability
- **Thorp's innovation**: calculate *exactly how much stock* to buy to offset warrant short-selling risk
- Technique now called **"delta hedging"** (after Greek letter symbolizing change)
- Delta hedging: paper profit/loss from small stock price change is offset by warrant price change
- Profit comes when "irrational" warrant price moves into line with stock price
- **Keynes**: "The market can remain irrational longer than you can remain solvent"
- Must know when other "irrational" people will see sense — beauty of warrants: **expiration dates** force convergence
- On expiration date, warrant goes *poof* — any irrational notion of value disappears

### Beat the Market (1967)

- Thorp and **Sheen Kassouf** (Columbia PhD, fair warrant pricing) met at UC Irvine; weekly research seminars (no students, just figuring out how to get rich)
- Thorp parlayed $40,000 into $100,000 by 1967
- Wrote *Beat the Market* — first discussion in print of delta hedging; **$50,000 advance** (5x Kassouf's salary)
- Samuelson reviewed for *Journal of the American Statistical Association*:

> "Just as astronomers loathe astrology, scientists rightly resent vulgarization of their craft."

- Few weeks of 1969: **3.20%** gain; 1970: **13.04%** (S&P returned 3.22%); 1971: **26.66%**

- Essentially bond with "bonus" stock option attached — easy to price the bond part, but the option threw people
- Unknown to academia, Thorp had devised version of what are now called **Black-Scholes pricing formulas** by 1967
- Option value depends on: strike price, stock's current price, time to expiration, and stock **volatility**
- More volatile stock = more likely to rise enough to make option valuable (can't lose more than option cost)
- Computer-savvy Thorp had real edge over most options traders
- Hired **Steve Mizusawa** (physics/CS major) who slept only 5 hours/day — came in handy trading on NY, London, and Asian exchanges
- As fund grew, salaries increased exponentially; Thorp told hire **David Gelbaum** he could expect fivefold increase in five years — it came to pass

### Resorts International Warrants

- 1972: computer model determined **Resorts International** warrants incredibly underpriced at **27 cents** (model said ~$4)
- Company building casino in Atlantic City; stock dropped to ~$8, warrants had $40 strike price
- Thorp bought ~10,800 warrants for ~$2,900, shorted 800 shares as hedge
- Stock plunged to $1.50 — Thorp covered shorts for $5,200 profit, still held warrants
- Six years later: people offered $4 per warrant; Thorp checked model (said ~$8) — bought more
- By mid-1980s: sold warrants for **$100 each** — **370 times** purchase price, ~**80% annual return** over decade
- Most extreme Kelly position: invested **150% of fund's assets** in single "sure thing" deal
- Real test: **"whether you can sleep at night"**
- Broker: **Michael Milken** — Thorp and Milken never actually met (saw him once across trading floor behind glass)

## Robert C. Merton and Black-Scholes

- **Robert C. Merton**: son of famous sociologist who invented **focus group** and popularized "role model" and "self-fulfilling prophecy"
- Father taught son about stock market and poker; younger Merton stared at lightbulbs during poker to mask reactions
- At 19: bought Friden stock, shorted Singer during merger announcement — nice profit
- Switched from math at **Caltech** to economics at **MIT** (only school that accepted him — full fellowship)
- First course taught by **Samuelson**, who immediately hired him as research assistant
- Samuelson encouraged tackling **option pricing** — the still-unsolved problem
- **Myron Scholes** and **Fischer Black** (at consulting firm **Arthur D. Little**) also working on it
- Merton reasoned "correct" option price is where no one can profit — assumption of **no arbitrage**
- Group discovered **National General** warrants most underpriced — bought a block
- 1972: **American Financial** announced takeover, changed warrant terms — MIT group lost everything

> "Although our trading didn't turn out very well, this event helped validate our formula."

- Black sent preprint to Thorp, explaining he'd taken delta hedging reasoning further
- Thorp verified on **Hewlett-Packard 9830A** ($6,000, 7,616 bytes) — formulas equivalent except for interest rate factor
- Published 1973 — name deprived both Merton and Thorp of credit
- Merton delayed publishing his derivation until Black-Scholes article appeared (matter of courtesy)
- Published in new **AT&T** journal: *Bell Journal of Economics and Management Science*
- Journal was acknowledgment of how profoundly quantitative methods from information theory were transforming finance

> "I never thought about credit...What I saw was a way to make a lot of money." — Thorp

### Sharpe's Zero-Sum Argument

- **William Sharpe** (developer of **Capital Asset Pricing Model**): one of brightest, most militant of Random Walk Cosa Nostra
- Would ask money managers if they *really* beat the market, then grill them over details
- Taught two years at UC Irvine — came to know Thorp, had friendly parries over market efficiency
- Proved active investing is a **zero-sum game**:
- World's investors collectively own 100% of stock (nothing owned by extraterrestrials!)
- Passive investors match market by definition; subtract their returns from total
- Active investors as a group also match market — but pay ~2% in fees
- Majority of active investors **do worse than market**

> "It follows from the laws of arithmetic."

- Thorp's arbitrage experience differed from efficient market assumptions
- Mispricings took weeks or months to diminish, even with Thorp trying to profit at mathematical maximum rate

### The AMC Convertible Bond Trade

- Thorp tried to win Sharpe over during 1975 UC Irvine lecture
- **American Motors Corporation** convertible bond maturing 1988: issued at $1,000, sunk to $600
- Bond exchangeable for 100 shares at $6/share — same price as stock
- Bond paid **5% interest**, stock paid no dividends — owning bond gave all upside with downside protection
- Even in bankruptcy, bondholders recover more than stockholders
- True worst-case (stock flat): still made net **6.33% return** from interest differential and short-sale proceeds
- Because trade was sure thing, Kelly permitted leverage

> "Situations that simple and clear are few and far between, but we made a large part of our living off scenarios just like that."

## Part Four: St. Petersburg Wager

### Daniel Bernoulli

- **Daniel Bernoulli (1700-1782)**: mathematician from competitive genius family
- Uncle **Jakob Bernoulli** discovered law of large numbers
- Father **Johann** threw Daniel out of house after they jointly won French Academy prize (1734)
- Johann published nearly identical book under own name with false date — claimed Daniel plagiarized
- Daniel left for **St. Petersburg**, worked for Westernizing Russian court

### The St. Petersburg Paradox

- Peter tosses coin repeatedly until heads; pays Paul escalating amounts: 1, 2, 4, 8... ducats
- Expected value calculation: each possibility contributes ½ ducat — sum of infinite series = **infinite**
- Implies no price too high to pay — obviously absurd

> "Although the standard calculation shows that the value of Paul's expectation is infinitely great, it has...to be admitted that any fairly reasonable man would sell his chance, with great pleasure, for twenty ducats."

### Bernoulli's Solution: Utility

- Distinguished between **money** and **utility** (subjective value people place on money)
- **"The value of an item must not be based on its price, but rather on the utility it yields"**
- Price is objective, same for everyone; **utility is subjective**, depends on circumstances
- **Logarithmic utility**: "utility resulting from any small increase in wealth will be inversely proportionate to the quantity of goods previously possessed"
- Friend twice as rich values $100 bet only half as much as you would
- "Your second ducat, like your second million, is never quite as sweet"
- Adjusted for diminishing returns, infinite series **converges to finite, modest sum**

### Challenges to Logarithmic Utility

- **Karl Menger** (1930s Vienna): created revised St. Petersburg wagers where Bernoulli's solution fails
- Sweeten payoffs so expected utility again infinite — absurd
- Not good model of poverty (losing 90% of last million same as losing 90% of last dime?)
- Not good model at extreme wealth (advantage to $10B vs. $1B if just concerned with living well?)
- Better resolution: **"bliss level"** = ceiling on utility — truncates infinite series at reasonable value
- **Leonard Savage** (1954): called logarithmic curve "prototype for Everyman's utility function"
- **William Stanley Jevons** (1835-1882): applied to consumer goods — explains all-you-can-eat restaurants staying in business

### Geometric Mean

- Bernoulli's real contribution: risky ventures should be evaluated by **geometric mean** of outcomes
- **Arithmetic mean**: add values, divide by count — what batting averages are
- **Geometric mean**: multiply *n* values, take *nth* root
- **John Burr Williams** (1936): "If a speculator is in the habit of risking his capital plus profits in each successive trade, he will choose the geometric rather than the arithmetic mean"
- January 1954 *Econometrica*: first English translation of Bernoulli's 1738 article revealed his achievement long distorted and underrated
- Article not really about St. Petersburg paradox or utility — both mentioned only as asides
- **Real contribution**: risky ventures evaluated by geometric mean
