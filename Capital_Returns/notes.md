## Foreword (Neil Ostrer & William Arah, Founding Members)

### Marathon's Two Core Ideas
1. **High returns attract capital; low returns repel it** — the ebb/flow
   of capital predictably alters the competitive environment of industries
   (the **capital cycle**)
2. **Management skill in capital allocation** is vital over the long term —
   the best managers understand the capital cycle and don't lose their
   heads in good times

### Portfolio Approach
- Capital cycle opportunities have **long gestation periods** with
  uncertain timing of payoff
- Marathon's approach: invest in **relatively large number of stocks**,
  hold for **long periods** — goes against industry preference for
  concentrated portfolios held for ever-shorter periods
- Clients (pension funds, state funds, endowments — mostly US) often
  staffed by non-financial backgrounds; describe the approach as
  "just common sense"
- Capital cycle thinking **not widely followed** by competitors — creates
  investment opportunities

## Introduction (Edward Chancellor)

### The Capital Cycle — Core Mechanism
- Capital flows into **high-return businesses**, leaves when returns fall
  below **cost of capital** — constant flux, not static
- Inflow → new investment → increased capacity → pushes returns down
- Low returns → capital exits → capacity reduced → profitability recovers
- Resembles **Schumpeter's "creative destruction"**: the bust clears away
  capital misallocated during the boom

> "The key to the 'capital cycle' approach… is to understand how changes
> in the amount of capital employed within an industry are likely to impact
> upon future returns." — Chancellor

- Rooted in **Michael Porter's** *Competitive Advantage* framework:
  capital cycle analysis = how competitive advantage changes over time,
  viewed from the **supply side**
- Porter's five forces: supplier power, buyer power, substitution threat,
  rivalry, new entrants — capital cycle is specifically about the
  **new entrants / supply** dimension evolving dynamically

### A Stylized Capital Cycle (The "Macro Industries" Parable)

**The Boom Phase:**
- Fictional firm **Macro Industries** (CEO: William Blewist-Hard) earns
  returns above cost of capital; CEO on cover of *Fortune*
- 7 of 9 sell-side analysts have buy recommendations; stock at 14x P/E
  (below market average — still "value"-looking)
- Strategy dept. projects strong demand in emerging markets; announces
  50% capacity expansion over 3 years
- **Greedspin** investment bank arranges secondary share offering;
  lead banker **Stanley Churn** is a close friend of CEO
- Share price rises on announcement; growth and momentum investors pile in

**The Bust Phase (5 years later):**
- CEO resigned after activist pressure (hedge fund **Factastic Investment**)
- Profits collapsed; stock down 46% in 12 months
- $2.5bn new plant in Durham, NC — delayed and over budget
- Competitors also expanded; new low-cost entrant **Dynamic Widget** IPO'd
  and disappointed
- China became net exporter of widgets; market in excess supply
- Merger talks; stock below book; only 3 brokers still cover it,
  2 with sell recommendations

### Key Cycle Dynamics
- **Demand forecasts** have wide error margins and systematic bias:
  optimistic in booms, pessimistic in busts
- **CEO pay** tied to earnings or market cap → incentivizes asset growth
- **Investment bankers** lubricate the cycle: earn fees from IPOs,
  secondary offerings, M&A — incentivized to grow capacity in booms
  and consolidate in busts; naturally **myopic time horizons**
- Analysts and investors **extrapolate linearly in a cyclical world**
- **Lag between investment and new production** makes supply changes
  "lumpy" — prone to overshooting; economists call this the
  **"cobweb effect"**
- Turn of the cycle (beaten-down stocks becoming interesting) is
  invisible to short-horizon analysts obsessing over near-term uncertainty

### Some Recent Capital Cycles

#### Telecom / TMT Bubble
- Telecoms companies operated on assumption that internet traffic was
  **doubling every 100 days** — used to justify massive capex at
  **WorldCom, Global Crossing**, and "alternative carriers"
- Post-bust: networks plagued with **"dark fibre"** — expensively laid
  cables carrying no traffic for years

#### Global Shipping (2001–2009)
- Between 2001 and 2007, daily rates for **"Panamax" class ships** rose
  **tenfold** as China's trade share surged
- New ship orders correlated with spot rates; supply response takes up to
  **3 years** to deliver
- 2004–2009: global dry bulk fleet **doubled from 75 to 150m deadweight
  tonnes**
- Combined with global slowdown → **90% fall** in Panamax daily rates,
  wiping out all gains from the decade
- Investor buying shipping in summer 2007 lost **two-thirds** of their
  money; **Maersk Group** shares similarly devastated
- Ships ordered in boom continued being delivered long after downturn;
  industry still suffering poor capacity utilization at time of writing

#### US Housing / Spain / Ireland
- US housing bubble peaked 2006; **excess new home stock = ~5x annual
  production** required to satisfy new household formation demand
- Spain and Ireland worse: excess stock ~**15x pre-boom annual supply**
- Housing booms invariably justified by **demographic projections** later
  shown to be circular (Spain: immigration was *caused by* the property
  boom, not an independent driver)
- "Value" investors who bought US homebuilders at book value (near
  historical lows) suffered **very heavy losses** — ignored capital cycle
- Contrarian: UK and Australia had similar price bubbles but **strict
  building regulations** prevented supply response → markets recovered
  rapidly post-GFC

#### The Commodity "Supercycle"
- Took off post-dotcom bust in low interest rate environment (2002+)
- Propelled by **China's** investment-heavy economy: consistent double-
  digit GDP growth; post-GFC investment share of GDP rose to **~50%**,
  higher than any other economy in history
- By 2010, China accounted for **>40% of global demand** for iron ore,
  coal, zinc, aluminium; incremental demand share even higher
- Commodity prices arguably at **bubble levels** vs. historic trends
- Global mining company ROCE rose from ~**7.5% at turn of century** to
  peak **~35% in 2005**; rebounded post-GFC to ~20%
- Post-Lehman: analysts still extrapolated China demand into distant
  future, assuming China would converge with and eventually overtake
  the US economy

## Introduction (continued): The Capital Cycle Anomaly & Tenets

### Commodity Supercycle as Classic Capital Cycle
- Annual global mine production rose 20% annually 2000–2011; iron ore production doubled in volume
- Mining capex climbed >5x: ~$30bn/year at turn of century → peak >$160bn
- Supply lags are long — up to **nine years** to develop a greenfield mine site
- Supply also lumpy: **Vale's Serra Sul** (Brazil) had ~$20bn capex budget, expected to add ~5% to global iron ore production alone
- Non-traditional producers (Iran, parts of Africa) entered during high-price years; **Fortescue Metals Group** expanded to become 4th largest iron ore producer by 2011
- Supercycle turned in 2011, coinciding with China slowdown; seaborne iron ore down ~70% from peak by April 2015
- New capacity commissioned at peak prices still coming online → contributes further to overcapacity
- Conclusion: the commodity supercycle bears all hallmarks of a **classic capital cycle** — high prices → rising investment → new entrants → supply glut → bust

### The Capital Cycle Anomaly (Academic Evidence)
- Inverse relationship between **capital expenditure and investment returns** now documented academically
- **Chart I.2** (SocGen, Andrew Lapthorne): lowest asset growth decile returned ~11.5% annually (1990–2015); highest asset growth decile returned ~5.5% — a massive spread
- **Eugene Fama** and **Kenneth French** proposed adding "profits" and "investment" to their factor model; observe companies that invest *less* deliver higher returns
- This finding termed the **"asset-growth anomaly"**
- *Journal of Finance* paper: corporate events associated with asset expansion (M&A, equity issuance, new loans) → followed by low returns; asset contraction events (spin-offs, buybacks, debt prepayment, dividend initiations) → followed by positive excess returns
- Negative impact of asset expansion on shareholder returns persists for **up to five years**
- Asset growth is a **stronger** determinant of returns than value (P/B), size (market cap), or momentum
- Companies often accelerate investment after stock has done well → same companies later underperform → asset growth may explain **momentum reversal**
- Key insight: *when analyzing value and growth stocks alike, asset growth at both company and sector level must be accounted for*
- One researcher claims the **value effect disappears entirely** after controlling for capital investment

### Mean Reversion
- **"Asset-growth anomaly"** can be understood through mean reversion, but mean reversion is not driven by animal spirits alone — it works through **differential rates of investment**
- Companies earning above cost of capital tend to invest more → drives down future returns
- Companies earning below cost of capital invest less → natural forces restore profitability

> "A business which sells at a premium does so because it earns a large return upon its capital; this large return attracts competition; and generally speaking, it is not likely to continue indefinitely."
> — **Benjamin Graham** and **David Dodd**, *Security Analysis* (1934)

- University of Arizona researcher: corporate investment in developed economies (US + EAFE) is a **significant negative predictor** of aggregate profitability, stock market returns, and even GDP growth
- During late-1990s US bubble: investment share of GDP rose above average; after bust, both investment and profitability declined → recession
- **US post-GFC anomaly**: since 2010, US stocks look expensive by CAPE, yet US corporate investment has been lacklustre → mean reversion mechanism missing → profits stayed elevated longer than expected → market delivered robust returns
- **China counter-example**: stocks appear cheap by valuation, but investment and asset growth elevated → poor corporate profitability

### Explanations for the Capital Cycle Anomaly

#### Overconfidence
- Investors and managers are infatuated with **asset growth**; corporate expansion fires the imagination of both managers and shareholders
- Behavioural finance: investors and managers are prone to **overconfidence** in demand forecasts
- Poor historical performance of high-growth-expectation stocks reflects this mistaken fetishism

> "It's tough to make predictions, especially about the future."
> — **Yogi Berra**

#### Competition Neglect
- Overinvestment is not solitary — it comes about because several players simultaneously expand capacity
- **"Competition neglect"** — **Robin Greenwood** and **Samuel Hanson** (Harvard Business School): particularly strong when firms receive **delayed feedback** about consequences of their own decisions
- *American Economic Review* paper (Camerer & Lavallo): managers so overestimate their own skills they neglect competitive threats
- Linked to **"base-rate neglect"**: investors focus on projected future profitability but ignore changes in the industry's asset base — sometimes morphs into **cognitive dissonance** (wilful refusal to consider disconfirming evidence)

#### Inside View vs. Outside View
- **"Inside view"** — term coined by **Daniel Kahneman**: individuals in a group focus on specific circumstances and search evidence in their own experience
- Analysts with specialized industry knowledge especially prone to inside view — assume their case is unique

> "An inside view considers a problem by focusing on the specific task and the information at hand... an outside view considers the problem as an instance in a broader reference class."
> — **Michael Mauboussin** (formerly of Legg Mason)

- Outside view: looking for historical parallels (e.g., US real estate 2000s vs. Japanese real estate 1980s)
- Kahneman: outside view is unnatural because it forces analysts to set aside cherished proprietary information
- *AER* authors: "In the inside view, there is no special role for anticipation of the number of competitors or their abilities. In the outside view, the fact that most entries fail cannot be ignored."

#### Extrapolation
- Inside view linked to tendency to **extrapolate** — behavioural finance (**Kahneman** and **Amos Tversky**): anchoring on information at hand, recency bias, drawing strong inferences from small samples
- These weaknesses drive investors to make **linear forecasts** despite most economic activity being cyclical
- Economic cycles: trade, credit, liquidity, real estate, profit, commodity, venture capital, industry capital cycles — "our inclination to extrapolate must be hard-wired"
- Value investors buying cheap/depressed stocks are protected against extrapolation tendency
- Capital cycle analyst adds: mean reversion is driven by **supply-side changes** that quantitative value investors overlook

#### Skewed Incentives
- CEO compensation linked to short-term EPS or shareholder returns → stock prices often react positively to major capex announcements
- Companies that invest more attract **premium valuations**; high asset growth stocks exhibit **positive momentum** → reinforces overinvestment
- Executive pay frequently linked to revenue or market cap size → incentives skewed toward growth
- Managers with large ownership stakes more likely to shrink capital (buybacks) when few profitable alternatives exist
- **Investment bankers** drive the capital cycle — compensated by fees from debt/equity issuance and IPOs, not by long-term outcomes for clients
- Brokerage analysts: pay linked to commissions (stock turnover), serve as cheerleaders; have little interest in long-term outcomes

> "Rarely does one find a brokerage house study that points out... that a popular industry is heading for a fall or that an unpopular one is due to prosper. Wall Street's view of the future is notoriously fallible."
> — **Benjamin Graham**

#### Prisoner's Dilemma
- **Game theory** explains overinvestment: if future demand can profitably accommodate expansion by *one* player but not several, it's collectively rational not to expand — but individually rational to break ranks
- In competitive industries (low barriers to entry), first mover expands → others feel obliged to follow to protect market share → aggregate profits collapse
- Excessive asset growth results from lack of cooperative behaviour within an industry

#### Limits to Arbitrage
- Why don't smart investors just short high-asset-growth stocks? Fast-growing companies have **volatile share prices** → shorting is expensive (as dot-com short-sellers learned in the late 1990s)
- High-asset-growth companies often have **large market caps** (telecoms in 1990s, mining stocks 2000s) → avoiding them means large bets against the benchmark → **career risk**
- Capital cycles vary in length; nobody knows in advance when they will turn → uncertainty is another limit to arbitrage
- **Marathon's** private ownership and long-standing client relationships enable tolerance of benchmark deviation — a structural advantage for capital cycle investing

### Fundamentals of Capital Cycle Analysis

#### Focus on Supply Rather Than Demand
- Most investors spend most time forecasting **demand** — the harder and more uncertain variable
- Capital cycle analysis focuses on **supply**, which is far less uncertain and easier to forecast
- Increases in aggregate supply are often **well-flagged** in advance via capital spending data; in some industries (aircraft, shipbuilding) supply pipelines are known years ahead
- Because investors focus on demand, stock prices often **fail to anticipate negative supply shocks**

#### Analyze Competitive Conditions
- Returns are driven by changes on the **supply side**; profitability threatened when competitive conditions deteriorate
- Negative capital cycle phase: industry fragmentation, increasing supply
- Red flags: rash of **IPOs** in hot sectors, secondary share issuances, rising debt levels
- Understanding supply-side dynamics helps avoid **value traps** (e.g., US housing stocks in 2005–06)

#### Caveat Investment Banker
- Investment banks supply finance to capital-hungry businesses — earning generous fees; bankers are **paid to drive capital cycles**, not to worry about long-term consequences for clients
- Brokers focus on next quarter's earnings; they go "over the wall" to assist bankers marketing new share issuances
- Good capital cycle analyst is a **contrarian by nature**, always sceptical of Wall Street's siren call

#### Selecting the Right Corporate Managers
- Marathon cites **Warren Buffett**: most CEOs rose to the top via marketing, production, engineering — not necessarily **capital allocation skills**
- Buffett: after ten years on the job, a CEO whose company retains earnings equal to 10% of net worth will have deployed over **60% of all capital** in the business — capital allocation is the dominant job
- Marathon spends extensive time meeting and questioning managers to assess capital allocation ability
- Best managers (e.g., **Björn Wahlroos** at Finland's **Sampo**) understand their industry's capital cycle and invest contrarily

#### Generalists Make Better Capital Cycle Analysts
- Industry specialists prone to the inside view — lost in detail, comparing companies *within* a sector, missing risks to the industry as a whole (**"reference group neglect"**)
- Marathon prefers **generalists**: less likely to suffer reference group neglect, better able to apply capital cycle dynamics across industries

#### Adopt a Long-Term Approach
- Capital cycles take a **long time** to play out: Nasdaq bubbling started 1995, didn't burst until spring 2000
- Marathon warned of rising mining investment danger in **May 2006** — but the supercycle didn't turn for another five years
- Marathon's very low portfolio turnover is structurally suited to capital cycle investing

### Capital Cycle Breakdowns
- Capital cycle analysis can fail when:
  - **Disruptive technology** destroys established business models (Internet destroyed Yellow Pages, newspapers, bookshops, music/video rental)
  - **Policymakers protect industries** — interfere with creative destruction (see "Broken banks," "Twilight zone")
  - **State capitalism** (modern China) — cheap capital, debt forgiveness, never-ending asset growth distort normal cycle dynamics

### The Tenets of Capital Cycle Analysis (Summary)
- Most investors focus on demand; demand is harder to forecast than supply
- Changes in supply **drive industry profitability**; stock prices often fail to anticipate supply shifts
- The **value/growth dichotomy is false** — companies in industries with supportive supply side can justify high valuations
- Management's **capital allocation skills** are paramount; meeting management is essential
- **Investment bankers drive the capital cycle** largely to the detriment of investors
- Policymaker interference and new technology can disrupt normal capital cycle operation
- **Generalists** better able to adopt the outside view required for capital cycle analysis
- **Long-term investors** better suited to applying the capital cycle approach

### 1.1 Evolution of Cooperation (February 2004)

**Core thesis:** Instability within an industry can create the conditions for
improved future returns. A history of poor returns may signal a contrarian
opportunity if cooperative behavior is about to emerge.

- **Robert Axelrod** (*The Evolution of Cooperation*) ran repeated prisoner's
  dilemma tournaments; found **"tit for tat"** (reciprocity) is the most
  successful long-run strategy
- WWI trenches: unspoken truces emerged between British and German troops
  stationed opposite each other for extended periods — a real-world "tit for
  tat." Generals broke it by rotating troops more frequently, removing the
  **"shadow of the future"**
- **Hyman Minsky** (*Stabilizing an Unstable Economy*): financial stability
  is destabilizing (leads to excess); inversely, **instability can create
  conditions of stability** from a capital cycle standpoint
- Ideal capital cycle opportunity: a small number of large players evolve
  from excess competition to **"pricing discipline"** — retaliation (price
  cuts) is most powerful for dominant price setters with barriers to entry
- Industries where "tit for tat" fails:
  - **Autos** — too many competitive dimensions (price, spec, financing,
    warranties); product differentiation paradoxically impedes supernormal
    returns
  - **Airlines** — pricing devolved to front-line managers, creating
    death-by-a-thousand-cuts competition
  - **VW** — State of Lower Saxony (18.2% shareholder) has stakeholder/
    employment agenda, not shareholder maximization; politics blocks the cycle
- Industries where it works: **steel, paper** (undifferentiated products),
  **European automotive glass** (three players, long-term supply agreements,
  infrequent capacity decisions signaled in advance)
- **"Shadow of the future"** concept: participants cooperate if they believe
  a competitor will retaliate next round; industries where managers signal
  future responses to competitor behavior are favorable
- Exit barriers and Chapter 11 protection blunt natural evolutionary forces;
  Fed's low interest rate policy (replacing tech bubble with housing bubble)
  stymied evolutionary forces in the broader economy

> "The really juicy investment returns are to be found in industries which are
> evolving to this state [cooperation]. The joy from a capital cycle
> perspective is that most investors are, for a variety of behavioural reasons,
> taken by surprise." — Marathon AM

**Ideal setup:** few players, rational management, barriers to entry, NO exit
barriers, non-complex rules of engagement. Look for industries currently
*evolving toward* this state, not those already there.

### 1.2 Cod Philosophy (August 2004)

**Core thesis:** The cod industry is a 500-year capital cycle case study.
Profits migrated to whoever controlled the **pinch-point** in the processing
chain; technology kept destroying those pinch-points; government intervention
then prevented the normal capital exit that would have restored returns.

- Book referenced: *Cod* by **Mark Kurlansky** — read from an investor/capital
  cycle perspective
- Cod's appeal: high protein, easy to dry/salt, nearly 100% usable, markets
  from N. America to Europe to Caribbean
- By mid-16th century, **over 60% of all fish eaten in Europe** were cod —
  remained so for ~200 years

**Pinch-point migration:**
1. **Processing ports** (Newfoundland, New England, Iceland) — natural rock
   slabs for drying; "men of no particular skill, and with very little
   capital, made fortunes"
2. **Boston** — harbors too small for transatlantic cargo; bottleneck migrated
   to the nearest sizeable market; New England cod trade powered a three-way
   Atlantic economy (cod → Europe, slaves → Caribbean, molasses → New England
   rum distilleries) creating a "**cod aristocracy**"
3. **Technology disruption** — three simultaneous innovations destroyed the
   old pinch-points:
   - **Clarence Birdseye** (1920s): food-freezing (developed via home
     experiments including keeping live pickerel in his bathtub)
   - **Steam-powered trawlers**: larger, more efficient, could strip the ocean
   - **Sonar** (standard by 1930s): could accurately locate cod shoals
   - Combined: Spanish vessels fished Newfoundland and docked frozen fish
     directly in La Rochelle; old harbors and Boston market went into decline

**The crisis:** New equipment was expensive → heavy borrowing → incentive to
catch *more* fish to repay debts → overfishing → classic prisoner's dilemma
→ "Cod wars" erupted over fishing rights

**Government intervention wrecked the capital cycle:**
- Iceland asserted sovereignty: 1 mile → 4 miles → 50 miles → **200 miles**
  (1973); Canada, US, EU followed; North Atlantic carved into four exclusive
  zones with quotas
- Ordinarily capital would leave, capacity shrink, prices rise to economic
  returns. Instead, **government subsidies kept capacity high and prices low**
- Canada: invested **$3 for every $1 the fisheries earned** — gold standard
  of bureaucratic inefficiency

**Lesson:** Over 150 years, profits migrated from ports → markets → food
processors → ultimately to *consumers* as the chief beneficiaries. The
driver: declining cost of technology eliminating pinch-point excess profits.

> "Marathon focuses not just on the magnitude of a company's profitability
> (the size of the pinch-point) but also its sustainability (why dock at
> Boston at all?)"

- Commoditization timelines vary: integrated steel mills (Bessemer Process →
  mini mills) = ~70 years; department stores vs. big-box retail = ~30 years;
  semiconductors = less than 2 years
- Question for investors: how long for media distribution, telecom, online
  auctions?

### 1.3 This Time's No Different (May 2006)

**Core thesis:** The commodity "supercycle" narrative in 2006 was the same
"this time is different" story that always marks cycle peaks. Supply response
was already underway; demand extrapolation was speculative.

- **Copper stolen from French railway lines** and UK pennies worth more melted
  than as currency — symptom of a commodity mania
- Copper up **6x since end of 2001**; iron ore, zinc, aluminium, gold all
  surging
- Bull narrative: Chinese/Indian demand, supply constrained by 1990s
  underinvestment, better investment discipline this cycle, mining equipment
  shortages, rising extraction costs (~30% over two years) mean prices must
  stay high — **"circular logic of bull markets"**
- Wall Street response: hedge funds becoming "commodity experts," banks
  doubling commodities trading staff, **seven-figure sign-on bonuses** for
  traders who "were probably unemployable a few years ago," specialist
  commodity indices developed (to sell derivatives), retail investors piling in

**The bear case (Marathon, 2006):**
- Copper cost of production: **$0.80–0.90/lb** (marginal ~$1.20/lb); current
  price **$3.60/lb** — three times cost of production (was 60 cents five
  years prior)
- Mining exploration costs **doubled between 2003 and 2005** — supply coming
- Countries become more efficient in raw material use as economies develop;
  China has stated desire to shift toward services
- **Germany**: copper pipe demand halved from 90,000 to 45,000 tonnes as
  construction industry switched to cheaper PVC
- **Container shipping mini-bubble** as direct analogy: "once-in-a-generation"
  supercycle narrative → M&A boom mid-2005 → shipyards fully booked → peak
  marked by the frenzy → **shipping rates and share prices fallen sharply**
  while supply continues to increase

### 1.4 Supercycle Woes (May 2011)

**Core thesis:** By 2011 the commodity capital cycle was showing all the
classic signs of a late-cycle peak — surging capex, M&A frenzy, IPO boom,
China demand concerns.

- Mining capex from 124 MSCI ACWI companies: predicted to reach **$180bn in
  2011**, up from less than **$30bn ten years prior** — a **6x increase**
- Supply surge coming (Merrill Lynch estimates, 2000–2014):
  - Nickel: 1,000 → 2,000 metric tonnes (+100%)
  - Copper: 15,000 → 20,000 (+33%)
  - Aluminium: 25,000 → 50,000 (+100%)
  - **Iron ore: 1bn → 2.25bn metric tonnes (+125%)** — most dramatic
- Metals & mining IPO flotations rose **50% between 2005 and 2010**; M&A
  deals growing larger — classic late-cycle capital markets activity
- **China red flags:**
  - Fixed asset investment at ~**50% of GDP** — Japan with its roads-to-
    nowhere peaked at only 30%
  - Chinese industrial sector return on net operating assets trending down
  - Power industry capex running at **over 100% of operating cash flow**
  - Sectors (cement, steel) over-indebted; capex/EBITDA running at near-100%
  - Even if Beijing consolidates industry, reduced investment growth = lower
    commodity demand
- Metals & mining sector in FTSE World Index near **all-time high as share of
  index** — up 3x from 1999 low; benchmark-hugging investors at risk
- "China new paradigm" narrative: Middle Kingdom consumes ~half of global
  iron ore, nickel, copper, zinc production
- Conclusion: current chapter in commodity story is "**much closer to its
  conclusion than the beginning**"

### 1.5 No Small Beer (February 2010)

**Core thesis:** Global brewing industry consolidation from 2002–2010 created
a favorable capital cycle — capacity reduced, pricing discipline improved,
balance sheets under repair. Market was underestimating the long-run benefit.

> "Most people hate the taste of beer to begin with. It is, however, a
> prejudice that many have been able to overcome."
> — Sir Winston Churchill

- **Heineken** held 2002–2008: underperformed European market by ~30% due to
  overpriced acquisitions ($9.5bn over the decade); ROIC dropped from 20% to
  below 10%; culminated in acquiring **Scottish & Newcastle** UK assets in
  2008 — just as UK entered recession and currency turned
- Overall brewing sector ROIC: declined from **13% (2000) → 9% (2008)**

**Consolidation wave:**
- 2002: **SAB** (South Africa) acquires **Miller** (US) → SABMiller
- 2004: **AmBev** (Brazil) + **Interbrew** (Belgium) → InBev
- 2007: SABMiller + **Molson Coors** JV in US
- 2008: Heineken + Carlsberg buy **S&N**; **InBev acquires Anheuser-Busch**
  for **$60bn** — largest deal
- 2010: Heineken bids **$7.6bn** for Mexican **FEMSA** (#2 home market, #4
  Brazil)
- Result: **top 4 brewers control ~50% of global beer volume** (up from 13.3%
  in 1998)
- US: **AB InBev + Miller Coors = 80%** of world's largest beer market by
  value
- UK: Heineken, Molson Coors, AB InBev = **67%**
- Russia: Carlsberg, AB InBev, Heineken = **70%**

**Why returns should improve:**
- Consolidation → **capacity reduction** (UK: 10% of brewing capacity removed
  after Heineken/S&N; similar in Ireland, Finland, France)
- Post-M&A debt (avg net debt/EBITDA ~3x vs. long-run 1.5x) → **balance
  sheet discipline** → less need to chase volume; capex cut from ~2x
  depreciation to below 1.5x (from ~10% to below 8% of sales)
- **Pricing power**: flat Western volumes but meaningful price increases; US
  and W. Europe took "decent" price rises 2005–2010 despite flat volumes
- **Emerging market premiumization**: China (+9% p.a.), Brazil (+5%) volume
  growth; premium lager <5% of market in EM vs. 25% in Europe — long runway
- Each of four major players has **different geographic profit focus** → less

**Valuation:** Free cash flow yields of **6–7%** imply only GDP-level growth
— market underestimating long-run benefit of consolidation and improved
discipline. Marathon increasing exposure to Heineken, Carlsberg, AB InBev
(European portfolios) and SABMiller (UK portfolios).

**Core thesis:** "There is no cure for high prices like high prices." The very
persistence of oil above $100/barrel was stimulating supply responses and
demand destruction that would eventually undermine oil company earnings and
share prices.

- Bull narrative: **peak oil** + EM demand + declining reserves + rising costs

- **US shale gas revolution**: new drilling techniques dramatically boosted

- Gas import plants being reengineered to *export*; industries shifting

- Transport becoming more fuel efficient (new aircraft, fuel-efficient ships

**Oil major capex problem:**
- Companies anchoring future oil price assumptions to current elevated levels:

  - **BP**: hurdle price for new projects raised from $16 (2002) to **above

  - **Petrobras**: planning to spend **$225bn over five years**; assumes

### 1.7 Major Concerns (March 2014)

**Core thesis:** Oil majors' cheap valuations were a **value trap**. Despite
rising oil prices (2003–2012), capex growth ate all the earnings benefit;
returns were falling even before any oil price decline.

- Top 5 oil majors = **>40% of MSCI World Oil & Gas Index**; trading at
  discounts to market P/E with ~2x dividend yield — looks attractive
- **But the numbers:**
  - 2003–2012: Brent crude +16%/year; oil major net income only +8%/year
  - EPS growth (including buybacks): +10% — lagging S&P's +12%
  - Capex surged: from **1.2x D&A (2003–07) to 1.7x D&A (2007–12)**
  - Return on equity: **27% (2007) → 17% (2012)** — while oil price rose
    nearly 20%
- Rising capex failed to grow earnings because majors struggled just to
  **maintain production volumes** — replacement costs escalating

**Key dynamic:** Capital cycle theory predicted this. High prices → high
returns → surge in capex → but production barely maintained → returns fall
even as prices stay elevated. The capex trap.

## Introduction (Edward Chancellor)

### The Capital Cycle — Core Mechanism

- Capital attracted to high-return businesses, exits when returns fall below cost of capital — process is **cyclical, not static**
- Inflow → new investment → increased capacity → returns fall; outflow → capacity shrinks → profitability recovers
- Resembles **Schumpeter's "creative destruction"**: the bust clears misallocation from the boom
- **Capital cycle analysis** = how changes in capital employed in an industry impact future returns; focused on the **supply side** of competitive dynamics
- Connects to **Michael Porter** (*Competitive Advantage*, Harvard Business School) — "essence of formulating competitive strategy is relating a company to its environment"; Porter's **five forces** (supplier power, buyer power, substitution threat, rivalry, new entrants) — capital cycle is how competitive advantage *changes over time* from an investor's perspective

### A Stylized Capital Cycle — The "Macro Industries" Parable

- Fictional widget maker **Macro Industries** (CEO: William Blewist-Hard) illustrates the full cycle
- **Boom phase signals**: CEO on *Fortune* cover; 7 buy / 2 hold analyst recommendations; P/E at 14x (below market, held by value investors); plans 50% capacity increase; secondary share offering arranged by investment bank **Greedspin** (lead banker Stanley Churn, personal friend of CEO); expansion cheered by *FT* Lex column; growth and momentum investors pile in
- **Bust phase signals** (5 years later): CEO ousted by activist hedge fund **Factastic Investment**; stock down 46%; $2.5bn new Durham, NC plant delayed and over budget; competitors also expanded; new low-cost entrant **Dynamic Widget** IPO'd and disappointed; China (largest consumer) became net exporter; stock trading below book; only 3 brokers still cover, 2 with sell ratings

### Why the Cycle Persists — Structural Biases

- **Managerial overconfidence**: high profitability makes managers confuse benign conditions with personal skill — media amplifies by seeking corporate heroes
- **Demand forecast bias**: systematically too optimistic in booms, too pessimistic in busts; wide margin of error
- **Capital discipline loosens** when returns are high: competitors follow (fear of losing share or equally hubristic); CEO pay tied to earnings/market cap → incentivizes asset growth; capacity announcement → share price rises → growth and momentum investors reinforce
- **Investment bankers lubricate the cycle**: earn fees from secondary issues, IPOs, M&A; analysts prefer fast-growing "sexy" sectors (higher turnover = more commissions); neither bankers nor analysts have long-term incentive alignment; both extrapolate linearly in a cyclical world
- **Cobweb effect**: lag between investment decision and new supply coming online makes supply changes **lumpy and prone to overshooting** — recognized by economists; not smooth as textbook supply curves imply
- **Cycle turn**: excess capacity → profits collapse → management changed → capex slashed → industry consolidates → supply contracts → recovery sets up. Short-horizon investors miss the turn, obsessing over near-term uncertainty

> "It's not just a question of incentives. Both analysts and investors are given to extrapolating current trends. In a cyclical world, they think linearly."

### Capital Cycle Diagram (Chart I.1)

Four-stage loop:
1. New entrants attracted by high returns → investors optimistic
2. Rising competition → returns fall below cost of capital → share price underperforms
3. Business investment declines, industry consolidation, firms exit → investors pessimistic
4. Improving supply side → returns rise above cost of capital → share price outperforms

### Some Recent Capital Cycles

**Semiconductors & Airlines**: chronically prone to violent capital cycles — frequent excess capacity, persistently disappointing returns

**TMT Bubble (late 1990s)**:
- Telecoms operated on mistaken assumption internet traffic doubled every 100 days
- Justified massive capex by **WorldCom**, **Global Crossing**, and "alternative carriers"
- Result: "**dark fibre**" — enormous excess network capacity, expensively laid fibre optic cable sitting unlit for years

**Global Shipping (2001–2009)**:
- Between 2001 and 2007, daily "**Panamax**" ship rates rose **tenfold** on China-driven demand
- New ship orders strongly correlated with spot rates; 3-year delivery lag made overshooting inevitable
- 2004–2009: global dry bulk fleet **doubled** from 75 to 150 million deadweight tonnes
- Result: 90% fall in Panamax daily rates; investor buying at 2007 peak lost **two-thirds of money**; **Maersk Group** shares fell similarly; excess ships kept delivering long after the bust

**US Homebuilding (2002–2006)**:
- At peak, excess new homes ≈ **5× annual production** needed to satisfy new household formation
- Spain and Ireland worse: excess stock ≈ **15× average annual pre-boom supply**
- Booms justified by "rosy demographic projections" — in Spain, immigration itself was a function of the construction boom; foreigners left by hundreds of thousands after the bust
- Value investors who bought homebuilders near book value (historical lows) suffered heavy losses — ignored supply-side dynamics
- **UK and Australia**: similar price bubbles but strict building regulations **prevented supply response** → both markets recovered rapidly post-GFC (contrarian insight: regulation constrained the capital cycle)

**Commodity Supercycle (post-2002)**:
- China's investment-heavy economy drove consistent double-digit GDP growth; post-GFC investment share reached ~**50% of GDP** — higher than any other economy in history
- By 2010, China accounted for **>40% of global demand** for iron ore, coal, zinc, aluminium
- Mining company **return on capital employed** rose from ~7.5% at turn of century to peak ~**35% in 2005**; rebounded post-GFC to ~20%
- Even after Lehman bust, analysts extrapolated China demand growth to infinity — China destined to converge with and surpass US economy narrative

## Introduction (continued): The Capital Cycle Anomaly & Behavioural Explanations

### Commodity Supercycle as Capital Cycle Archetype
- Annual global mine production rose ~20% p.a. in USD terms between 2000–2011;
  iron ore volume doubled over the same period
- Mining capex climbed >5× — from ~$30bn/yr at century turn to $160bn+ at peak
- Supply lags are extreme: up to **nine years** to develop a greenfield mine;
  **Vale's Serra Sul** (Brazil) had a ~$20bn capex budget and alone added ~5%
  to global iron ore production
- Non-traditional producers (Iran, Africa) and newcomers like
  **Fortescue Metals Group** (became 4th largest iron ore producer by 2011)
  flooded in; dubious foreign outfits floated on the London Stock Exchange
- Supercycle turned in 2011, roughly coinciding with China growth slowdown;
  by April 2015, seaborne iron ore was down ~70% (USD) from peak
- New capacity commissioned at peak prices continued coming online,
  deepening the oversupply — textbook capital cycle resolution

### THE CAPITAL CYCLE ANOMALY

- Academic finance now supports the capital cycle view: multiple papers document
  an **inverse relationship between capital expenditure and investment returns**
- **Andrew Lapthorne** (Société Générale strategist) chart: lowest asset-growth
  decile ~12% avg annual returns (1990–2015) vs. ~5% for highest asset-growth
  decile — a dramatic spread across global stocks (Chart I.2)
- **Eugene Fama** and **Ken French** added profits and investment to their
  factor model; their finding: companies that invest *less* deliver higher returns
  — termed the **"asset-growth anomaly"**
- *Journal of Finance* paper: corporate events tied to asset expansion
  (M&A, equity issuance, new loans) → low subsequent returns; conversely,
  asset contraction events (spin-offs, buybacks, debt repayment, dividend
  initiations) → positive excess returns; negative impact persists **up to five years**
- Asset growth is a **stronger** determinant of returns than value (P/B), size
  (market cap), or momentum (both horizons)
- Companies often accelerate investment after strong stock performance →
  later underperform; may explain **momentum reversal**
- Key insight: *when analyzing value and growth stocks, take into account
  asset growth at both the company and sectoral level*
- One researcher claims the value effect disappears entirely after
  controlling for capital investment

### MEAN REVERSION

- **Mean reversion** works not just through "animal spirits" but through
  differential rates of investment:
  - High-ROIC firms invest more → drive down future returns
  - Below-cost-of-capital firms invest less → returns eventually recover
- **Benjamin Graham** and **David Dodd**, *Security Analysis* (1934):

> "A business which sells at a premium does so because it earns a large return
> upon its capital; this large return attracts competition; and generally speaking,
> it is not likely to continue indefinitely. Conversely in the case of a business
> selling at a large discount because of abnormally low earnings. The absence of
> new competition, the withdrawal of old competition from the field, and other
> natural economic forces, should tend eventually to improve the situation and
> restore a normal rate of profit on the investment."
> — Graham & Dodd, *Security Analysis* (1934)

- University of Arizona researcher: corporate investment in most developed
  economies (US + EAFE) is a **significant negative predictor** of aggregate
  profitability, stock market returns, and GDP growth
- Late-1990s US bubble: investment share of GDP rose above average →
  bubble burst revealed capital misallocation → investment and profitability fell,
  recession followed
- **The US post-GFC conundrum**: stocks looked expensive on CAPE since 2010,
  yet profits stayed elevated *because* corporate investment was lacklustre —
  the mean-reversion mechanism was absent; profits held up, markets delivered
  robust returns despite seemingly stretched valuations
- **China counterexample**: stocks looked cheap on valuation but elevated
  investment and asset growth produced poor corporate profitability

### EXPLANATIONS FOR THE CAPITAL CYCLE ANOMALY

Behavioural + agency factors combine to explain why the anomaly persists:
- **Overconfidence**, **base-rate neglect**, **cognitive dissonance**,
  **narrow-framing**, **extrapolation**
- Agency/incentive problems reinforce behavioural biases
- Rational investors unable to arbitrage it away due to structural "limits to
  arbitrage"

### OVERCONFIDENCE
- Managers and investors are "infatuated with asset growth" — corporate
  expansion fires imagination of both groups
- Poor historic performance of high-growth-expectation (high-valuation) stocks
  reflects this fetish
- Particularly acute in demand forecasting — overconfidence about future
  demand is the proximate cause of most overinvestment cycles

### COMPETITION NEGLECT
- Overinvestment is collective, not solitary — multiple players expand
  simultaneously, each ignoring the supply impact of others' decisions
- **Robin Greenwood** and **Samuel Hanson** (Harvard Business School):
  **"competition neglect"** is "particularly strong when firms receive delayed
  feedback about the consequences of their own decisions"
- *American Economic Review* paper: managers so overestimate their own skills
  that they neglect competitive threats — explains high failure rate of new entrants
- Related bias: **"base-rate neglect"** — investors focus on current/projected
  profitability but ignore changes in the industry's asset base from which
  returns are generated
- Extreme form: **"cognitive dissonance"** — wilful refusal to consider
  disconfirming evidence once a course of action has been decided upon

### INSIDE VIEW
- **Daniel Kahneman** coined the **"inside view"**: decision-makers focus on
  specific circumstances and search for evidence in their own experiences
- **Michael Mauboussin** (formerly Legg Mason):

> "An inside view considers a problem by focusing on the specific task and
> the information at hand, and predicts based on that unique set of inputs…
> In contrast, an outside view considers the problem as an instance in a broader
> reference class… Kahneman notes this is a very unnatural way to think precisely
> because it forces analysts to set aside all of the cherished information they
> have unearthed about a company. This is why people use the outside view
> so rarely."
> — Michael Mauboussin

- Specialists (sell-side analysts with deep industry knowledge) are most
  prone to the inside view — they assume their case is unique
- **Outside view** example: comparing US real estate boom of the 2000s to
  Japan's real estate market in the 1980s
- AER authors: "In the inside view, there is no special role for anticipation
  of the number of competitors or their abilities. In the outside view,
  the fact that most entries fail cannot be ignored."

### EXTRAPOLATION
- Behavioural finance (**Kahneman** and **Amos Tversky**): we "anchor" on
  information in front of us and are overly influenced by immediate experiences
  (**"recency bias"**)
- Also: tendency to draw strong inferences from small samples
- These combine to make investors project linear trends in an inherently
  cyclical world — trade cycles, credit cycles, liquidity cycles, real estate
  cycles, profit cycles, commodity cycles, venture capital cycles,
  industry capital cycles all exist
- Value investors buying cheap, depressed-earnings stocks are naturally
  protected from extrapolation error
- Key distinction: capital cycle analysis adds supply-side mean reversion
  to the quantitative value investor's toolkit — pure valuation metrics miss it

> "The main behavioral explanation for value stocks' long-run outperformance
> is excessive extrapolation by investors of multiyear growth rates. In reality,
> growth mean reverts faster than the market expects, making growth stocks
> more likely to disappoint."
> — recent investment textbook (cited by Chancellor)

### SKEWED INCENTIVES
- CEO compensation tied to **short-term EPS** or annual shareholder returns →
  managers favour growth
- Stock prices often *react positively* to major capex announcements;
  high-asset-growth stocks often exhibit positive momentum → further incentive
- Executive pay also linked to firm size (revenue or market cap) →
  structural bias toward expansion
- Managers with large ownership stakes more likely to shrink capital via
  buybacks when they see few profitable alternatives (alignment works
  in the right direction)
- **Investment bankers** drive the capital cycle (debt/equity issuance, IPOs)
  but are compensated by fee generation, not client outcomes → cheerleaders
  for investment booms
- Sell-side analysts: pay linked to brokerage commissions from stock turnover,
  not long-term accuracy → little interest in long-term outcomes

### PRISONER'S DILEMMA
- Game theory explains collective overinvestment: if future demand can
  profitably support only *one* player expanding, but all expand simultaneously,
  aggregate profits decline
- Collectively rational for incumbents to prevent any expansion (only one
  gains); but competitive industries with low barriers → one player breaks
  ranks → others follow to protect market share
- Excessive asset growth is a product of **lack of cooperative behaviour**
  within an industry

### LIMITS TO ARBITRAGE
- Why don't investors simply short high-asset-growth stocks?
  - Fast-growing companies have **volatile share prices** — shorting is expensive
  - Late 1990s: short-sellers of internet/tech stocks were wiped out before
    being proven right
  - High asset-growth companies often have **large market caps**
    (telecoms in 1990s, global miners more recently) → avoiding them
    means large active bets against the benchmark
  - Short-term underperformance of capital cycle strategy creates
    **"career risk"** — the only risk that keeps professional investors awake
  - Capital cycles vary in length; nobody knows in advance when they will
    turn → timing uncertainty is an additional limit to arbitrage
- **Marathon's structural advantage**: private ownership + long-standing client
  relationships → can adopt a long-term approach tolerant of benchmark
  deviation — a prerequisite for applying capital cycle analysis

### 1.8 A Capital Cycle Revolution (March 2014)

**Fade rate framework and Vestas case study**

- Marathon invests in two phases of the capital cycle:
  - **"Growth" universe**: companies whose high returns are more durable than the
    market expects (good company resists becoming mediocre)
  - **"Value" universe**: companies whose improvement is underestimated (bad company
    improves faster than expected)
- Key concept: **fade rate** — the rate at which a company's returns revert to
  mediocrity; mispriced systematically for behavioral reasons
- Framework from **Holt Value Associates** (now Credit Suisse): stock-market-implied
  fade rate reveals mispricings relative to competitive conditions
  - **Purchase Candidate A**: sustains high returns longer than market expects
  - **Purchase Candidate B**: improves faster than market expects

### Why Markets Misprice Fade Rates

- **Candidate A mispricing** stems from:
  - Underestimating durability of barriers to entry
  - Underappreciating addressable market scale
  - Ignoring management capital allocation skills (e.g., **Bunzl** — analysts modeled
    core returns accurately but never credited 20 years of value-adding bolt-on M&A)
  - Bias against "boring" high-return companies that lack exciting near-term catalysts
- **Candidate B opportunity** arises when:
  - Market misjudges beneficial effects of reduced competition as weak firms exit
  - "Unruly oligopoly" tires of excess competition → peaceful coexistence
  - Capital cycle turn coincides with maximum pessimism and depressed share prices
  - New management recruited externally (e.g., **Sergio Marchionne** at Fiat;
    **Rupert Soames** joining Serco)

### Vestas Wind Systems: Capital Cycle in Practice

- Marathon initially bought **Vestas** in 2003 — weak US market due to tax incentive changes
- Demand recovered → share price rose ~40x trough to 2008 peak
- Crisis hit: wind farm projects shelved just as new capacity came online
- Vestas' capex-to-depreciation rose from ~1x (2005) to ~5x (2008) → excess capacity
- Residual holding fell 96% — a "Return to Go"
- Should have sold entirely post-crisis; instead held → four more years of underperformance
- Recovery: new Swedish chairman (2013) → Marathon bought more shares (+90%), became
  largest shareholder; capex slashed to 0.4x depreciation → cash flow and balance sheet repaired
- Share price rose 360% subsequently
- Lesson: a stock can cycle through value → growth → value again within years;
  **selling discipline** as important as buying discipline

## 1.9 Growth Paradox (September 2014)

**Why corporate profitability lags GDP growth**

- Since 1960: US earnings compounded at 2% real per year; US economy grew 3.1% real
- Average dividend payout 45% → companies reinvested majority of earnings and still
  lagged the economy
- Paradox: corporate profits as % of GDP *rose* from 6% (1960) to 10%+ (2013)
  — yet per-share earnings growth was poor

### Reasons Earnings Per Share Lag GDP

1. **Net share issuance**: new issuance exceeded buybacks by ~2% per year
   (**William Bernstein** and **Robert Arnott**, "Earnings Growth: The Two Percent
   Dilution," *Financial Analysts Journal*, 2003)
   - Management procyclically buys back at peaks, issues at troughs (banking sector
     is the extreme example)
   - M&A also procyclical — deal volume peaks in late bull markets, destroying value
   - Stock option burn rates of ~1% annually further drag per-share returns

2. **Unlisted companies capture disproportionate profit growth**: private firms less
   encumbered by agency problems and short-term EPS pressure, invest more aggressively
   - New technology/business models developed privately; IPO occurs when growth matures
   - IPO activity is strongly procyclical: listings at peak valuations dilute
     aggregate EPS; post-IPO capital inflow drives further supply-side competition
     (e.g., late-1990s telecom IPOs funding fiber-optic overcapacity)

3. **Capital cycle destroys returns in high-growth industries**:
   - Primary driver of healthy corporate profitability = *favorable supply side*,
     not high demand growth
   - Strong demand growth attracts capital floods → erodes returns
   - Semiconductors: revolutionary technology, depressing investor experience —
     fragmented supply, high capex, low differentiation → value destruction
   - Airlines: revolutionized travel; atrocious investor returns until consolidation
     improved supply side
   - Mobile phones: Nokia, Motorola, RIM shareholders suffered despite smartphone boom

> "No correlation between long-term GDP growth and equity market return."

- China: stellar GDP growth since 1993; real equity return **negative** at -3% per year
- Conclusion: look for companies with prudent capital allocation; analyze supply side
  not demand outlook; find depressed industries at positive capital cycle inflection points

## Chapter 2: Value in Growth

- Original Marathon capital cycle approach: invest where capital withdraws, avoid where
  assets accumulate rapidly — focused on **mean reversion**
- Same analysis also identifies companies capable of *repelling* mean reversion —
  those with **moats** (**Warren Buffett**'s term)
- From capital cycle perspective: lack of competition prevents supply side from
  responding to high profitability → returns persist
- The "value/growth dichotomy" is false for a true value investor: value = price
  below intrinsic value, not merely low P/E or price-to-book

### 2.1 Warning Labels (September 2002)

**Style labelling distorts investment process**

- Traditional **value manager** definition: low P/B, P/E, P/S — associated with
  **Benjamin Graham**, "cigar-butt" investing (one last puff from discarded assets)
- Marathon portfolio has below-average multiples but strong earnings growth —
  *not* because of cigar-butt hunting; smaller companies with above-average growth
  often trade cheap
- **Large-cap bias problem**: MSCI Europe has 540 stocks; only 88 have market cap
  above $10bn. Liquidity constraints force large fund managers into large-caps.
  Three-quarters of industrial stocks are under $10bn → screened out by institutions.
  85% of healthcare stocks are above $10bn → pharma gets disproportionate attention
- Style labelling encourages use of inappropriate quantitative tools
- **Bill Miller** (Legg Mason): owned Amazon and AOL — labeled "value"
- **Warren Buffett**: owns Coke and Disney — labeled "growth"
- Same stocks (Citigroup, AIG, IBM) appear in top 15 holdings of both "value" and
  "growth" mutual funds (Lipper data)
- Capital cycle lens: a growth stock *becomes* a value stock when excess capital erodes
  returns — the technology bust turned growth stocks into value stocks overnight
- **Energis** (UK telecoms): bid to 10x invested capital in "New Economy" boom;
  excess investment → shares fell to tiny fraction of capital invested; never recovered
  — classic **value trap**
- **Assa Abloy** (locks: Yale, VingCard, Vachette): 25% sales growth p.a., 38% EPS
  CAGR over 10 years; shares fell 56% in growth derate → now below 1.5x sales vs.
  prior 4x → Marathon buyer at discount to intrinsic value
- **Stora Enso** (Finnish paper): cheaper than Assa Abloy on every quantitative metric
  *except* intrinsic value — being sold

### 2.2 Long Game (March 2003)

**Long-term investing: competitive advantage from asking more valuable questions**

- Marathon average holding period: ~5 years (expected to rise as portfolio quality rises)
- Short-term investor asks about next quarter's margins, EPS, revenue trends — information
  with brief shelf life; must be correct, incremental, and overwhelming to matter
- Competition for short-term information is ferocious — fed by investment banks
  (Salomon Smith Barney: "three month sales momentum model"; Deutsche Bank: "Weekly Autos")
- Long-term investors seek **information with shelf life**: capital allocation patterns
  visible in advertising, R&D, capex, debt, buybacks/issuance, M&A

> "Long-term investing works not because it is more difficult, but because there is
> less competition out there for the really valuable bits of information."

- **Colgate-Palmolive** case: introduced first line extension (blue minty gel) early 1980s
  with heavy advertising — analysts fixated on margin drag and depreciation drag,
  rated underperform; the advertising built subconscious brand loyalty across a generation
  - Colgate's share price rose 25x in the two decades after that launch
  - Annual turnover in Colgate shares ~100% → very few investors captured the full gain
  - Colgate's investor relations: "Most people don't think [advertising spend] is important"
  - Marathon was the only fund manager to meet Colgate's director of advertising
  - Colgate didn't outperform S&P 500 for *10 full years* after introduction —
    short-horizon investors would never have cared

> J.K. Galbraith: "nothing is so admirable in politics as a short-term memory."

### 2.3 Double Agents (June 2004)

**Agency conflicts create durable competitive moats**

- **Charles T. Munger** (Berkshire Hathaway vice-chairman) test at US business schools:
  "When should raising price *increase* volume sold?"
  - Students answer: luxury goods signaling quality
  - Munger's real answer: when the customer is *not* the purchaser — the agent
    (intermediary) can be bribed with higher prices → both higher margins and higher volume

- **Agency model**: consumer relies on supposedly independent intermediary who forms
  tacit alliance with producer to exploit consumer ignorance → supernormal profits

- **Geberit** (Swiss sanitary systems): sells to plumbers via wholesalers
  - Plumbers paid on percentage commission → *welcome* price increases
  - "Push-pull" strategy: educate plumbers to pull product; reps push to wholesalers
  - ~1/3 of sales from products introduced in last 3 years; 15%+ operating margins;
    ~50% market share in 7 core European markets; 8% annual top-line growth for 2 decades

- **Nobel Biocare** and **Straumann** (Swiss dental implants): dentists are the agents;
  Nobel Biocare revenues +17% p.a. since 1995; 24% operating margins
  - Dentists who adopt implant technique earn higher revenues → keen to sell high-end

- **William Demant** (hearing aids): 20%+ margins; 7% of sales on R&D
  - High-end hearing aids require customized fitting → $1,000 device + $2,000 fitting fee
  - Fitter-agent not price-sensitive; hearing aid market highly fragmented
  - Other leaders: Siemens, GN Store Nord, Phonak

- **Labtest** (Intertek subsidiary, Hong Kong): gatekeeper between Chinese manufacturers
  and US retailers; charges the *Chinese producer* (not the US retailer who selected the
  tester) → payer distanced from service selector → 33% margins

- Agency moats are durable even as consumers become better informed (Internet age);
  parallel to agency problems in fund management — persistent despite transparency

### 2.4 Digital Moats (August 2007)

**Internet companies investing in competitive position can ignore short-term profitability**

- Marathon avoided all internet companies during dotcom bubble (could not justify valuations)
- By 2007, two former dotcom-era companies are among best portfolio performers:
  **Amazon.com** and **Priceline.com**

- **Amazon**: strategy = use low cost and scalability of internet to deliver savings;
  sacrifice near-term margins to dominate long-term
  - Amazon Web Services and "Fulfilment by Amazon" require large upfront investment
    expensed through P&L → margin volatility
  - Wall Street panicked as stock fell from $60 to $40 on margin compression
  - By 2007: sales growing 35% YoY; stock nearly doubled from start of year
  - Net margins: 0.7% (2003) → 8.5% (2004) → 4.2% (2005) → 1.8% (2006)
  - Hints at high-single-digit margins at maturity; at 2.3x sales, not overvalued

- **Priceline.com**: fell from $974 to $7 post-bubble; "name your own price" model
  undifferentiated; acquisition of **Booking.com** (2005) pivoted strategy to European
  hotel agency model — 32,000 hotels signed, 100,000 to target; minimal platform costs;
  generating cash used for buybacks; 3-5 year path to making current valuation look cheap

- Low margin → dominant position → high long-term absolute profit is well-trodden:
  **Wal-Mart** cited as the classic precedent

## 2.5 Quality Time (August 2011)

### Portfolio Shift Toward High-Quality, Durable Moats

- Marathon's European portfolio gradually shifted toward higher-quality companies with superior **barriers to entry**
- Three factors driving improved returns on equity:
  1. Emergence of **oligopolies** in formerly low-return industries
  2. Evolution of business models with high/rising barriers to entry
  3. Management behavior that encourages these trends
- Even if aggregate European profits decline, portfolio companies should resist the trend

### Agency Business Models (~10% of European Portfolio)

- **Agency model**: company relies on intermediary (doctor, plumber, locksmith) to sell high-margin products to uninformed end consumers
- Consumer doesn't perceive common interest between producer and agent to sell premium products
- Average estimated **RoE of 27%** (2011) — ~11 percentage points above European non-financial average
- Examples: medical devices, locks, electrical and plumbing fittings

### Branded Consumer Goods (~9% of Portfolio)

- Added beer stocks, **Unilever**, **Swedish Match**
- Average RoE of **48%**

### Subscription-Based Services (~12% of Portfolio)

- Annuity-like revenue streams; forecast **RoE of 42%**
- Common theme: long-term customer commitment + **inertia on renewals** + scale economies = durable barriers
- Especially powerful when cost of service is small fraction of customer's total spend

#### Key Examples:
- **Rightmove** (UK property listings): winner-takes-all network effect; charges estate agents below cost of print ads; 65% of annual subscriptions already received by March; 16% price increases
- **Fresenius Medical Care (FMC)**: 34% US dialysis market share; dialysis = only 2% of private insurer total costs; shifting to multiyear contracts with price escalators
- **Capita**: UK BPO with multiyear government contracts; improving margins by delivering cost savings to clients
- **Experian, Reed Elsevier, Wolters Kluwer, Informa**: unique information datasets — value depends on completeness, making cancellation irrational even in downturns

### Portfolio Quality Summary

- Agency + subscriptions + branded goods = ~31% of European portfolio; ~40% including pharma/telecom
- Average **RoE of 39%** for this group = 2.4x the average for European non-financials
- Thesis: quality portfolio is less dependent on whether aggregate European profits are cyclically or structurally squeezed

## 2.6 Escaping the Semis' Cycle (February 2013)

### The Semiconductor Paradox

- **Moore's Law** drove 30 years of dramatic performance improvements benefiting the economy — but investors did poorly
- Since 1994, **Philadelphia Semiconductor Index** underperformed the Nasdaq by ~200 percentage points with greater volatility
- Classic capital cycle: good times → capacity expansion → new entrants (Japan 1970s, Korea 1980s, Taiwan mid-1990s, China more recently) → oversupply → poor returns

### Analog Semiconductors: The Exception

- **Analog semis** = ~15% of total semiconductor market; bridge real world (heat, sound, pressure) to digital world
- End markets: mobile phones, automobiles (airbag sensors), industrial automation
- Much higher product differentiation than digital; more complex, heterogeneous design required

#### Analog Devices
- Gross margin: **60%** avg (2000–2012); operating margin: **25%**
- Capex/sales: avg 6% since 2000, falling to 4% in recent years
- Free cash flow conversion: consistently **>100% of net income**
- Revenue CAGR since 1990: **8% per year**

#### Linear Technology
- Gross margin: **76%** avg; operating margin: **~50%**
- Capex/sales: ~5%; cash conversion >100%
- Revenue CAGR since 1990: **14% per year**
- Both companies offered **5% free cash flow yields** at time of writing

### Why Analog Escapes the Capital Cycle

- **Human capital moat**: analog design is trial-and-error, not simulation-driven; average engineer tenure at Analog Devices = **20 years**; hard to poach because process knowledge is company-specific
- New engineering graduates prefer digital (less steep learning curve, experience valued less in digital)
- **Fragmented but sub-segment concentrated**: top-5 concentration ~50%, but Analog Devices has **40%+ share in data converters**
- **Low price relative to importance**: avg Linear Technology chip price < $2, yet critical to end product (airbag sensor); price competition replaced by quality competition
- **High switching costs**: once designed into OEM application (often 10-year lifecycles), replacing requires revising entire production process
- **Product longevity**: >1/3 of Analog Devices sales come from products >10 years old — shelters from Moore's Law obsolescence
- Collectively these factors **short-circuit the capital cycle** that devastates digital semis

> "The analog production process is less standardized than most tech components, and thus far less vulnerable to obsolescence from the endless march of Moore's law." — Marathon

## 2.7 Value in Growth (August 2013)

### The Value vs. Growth False Dichotomy

- **Nassim Taleb's "epistemic arrogance"**: investors think they're better at forecasting than they are; DCF models encourage anchoring (calibrated to produce values near current price)
- Simple value proxies (P/B, P/E, FCF yield) are useful but say nothing about business model, industry structure, or management capital allocation
- **S&P Style Indices** define "value" by P/B, P/E, P/S; "growth" by EPS growth and price momentum — too crude
- Marathon's capital cycle approach doesn't fit either conventional label

### Baidu Case Study: Expensive on Metrics, Cheap on Capital Cycle Logic

- **Baidu**: dominant Chinese internet search engine; added to Marathon portfolios
- At purchase: 7.2x book, 18x earnings — unappealing to traditional value investors
- **Why it's compelling**:
  - **70% market share** in a winner-takes-all market
  - Asset-light model; profit-to-cash conversion **>100%**
  - Asset-light balance sheet limits overinvestment and working capital creep
  - Monetization per search < 1/10th of developed market peers — large upside runway
  - Founder/CEO/Chairman **Robin Li** holds 20.7% stake — aligned with shareholders
- Key risk: supply-side disruption

> "It should never be forgotten that, in its most basic form, investing is always and everywhere about price and value. Price is what you pay, says the Sage of Omaha, and value is what you get." — Marathon

## 2.8 Quality Control (May 2014)

### Capital Cycle Applied to High-Return Businesses

- Capital cycle is usually associated with "value" recovery situations; less understood that it also identifies **sustainably high-return businesses**
- Marathon's best performers over 10 years: **Coloplast, Intertek, Geberit, Gartner, Kao, Priceline**

### Two Sources of Pricing Power

1. **Concentrated market structure** + rational capacity management through the cycle
2. **Intrinsic pricing power** from intangible assets (price not the primary purchase decision)

### Types of Intangible Assets Creating Moats

- **Consumer brands**: Colgate — private label penetration only 2% in toothpaste
- **Agency relationships**: Legrand, Assa Abloy, Geberit — electricians/architects/plumbers specify products; price is pass-through to end customer
- **Workflow embedding**: Oracle, ADP, Paychex — switching risk outweighs cost savings
- **Network effects**: Secom (security), Praxair/Air Liquide (industrial gases), USS (car auctions), Intertek (testing)
- **Technological leadership**: Intel, Linear Technology — less durable unless combined with other factors

### The Virtuous Cycle of Intangibles

- High returns → abundant FCF → reinvested to reinforce moat (P&G: $40bn advertising over 5 years; Intel: ~$40bn R&D)
- Repels new entrants, **short-circuiting the destructive side of the capital cycle**
- Creates compounding at above-average rates over sustained periods
- Also **lower risk**: high-margin businesses more resilient to adverse shocks (a 1% margin fall hits a 5% margin business far harder than a 20% margin business)

### Why Quality Is Underappreciated

- **Hyperbolic discounting** and **recency bias** make human brains poor at multi-year planning
- Institutional short-termism: annual performance-pay for fund managers discourages long-term thinking
- **P/E fixation** vs. P/FCF: investors treat all earnings growth as equal, missing that high-ROCE businesses are far more value-creative
- Marathon: prepared to pay more (in P/E terms) for same earnings growth if ROCE and FCF conversion are superior
- At time of writing: margins near peak across all sectors; quality businesses at no valuation premium → compelling entry point

## 2.9 Under the Radar (February 2015)

### The "Mission Critical, Low Cost" Archetype

- Preferred growth stocks: companies doing **unglamorous but essential** things where customers pay little attention to price
- Common management refrain: "our product is a small fraction of total cost but vital to their operation"
- If a critical component fails and shuts down a production line, the cost of failure dwarfs the component price → **reliability > price**

### Examples by Industry

**Technology / Semiconductors**
- **Linear Technologies**: ROCE **141%**; **Analog Devices**: ROCE 25%
- Chips link real-world phenomena to digital systems; tiny fraction of equipment cost

**Payroll Processing**
- **Paychex**: ROCE 35%; raises prices >3%/year without losing clients
- **ADP**: ROCE 25%; costs employer ~$3/paycheck — detail-intensive work with high error risk if done in-house

**CAD-CAM Software**
- **Aveva**, **Dassault Systèmes**: mission-critical to design engineers; essential supply chain link

**Flavors, Fragrances, Enzymes**
- **Novozymes** (Denmark): 50%+ global market share in enzymes; enzymes in detergents < 5% of total cost; huge scale economies
- **Croda** (UK, ROCE 23%): 90% margin on a specific anti-aging cosmetic active ingredient (**Matrizyl**); product < 1% of total sale price — regretted not charging a royalty

**Specialty Chemicals**
- **Victrex** (UK): world leader in polyetheretherketone (PEEK polymer); works with OEMs like Apple in design phase; operating margins >35%, ROCE ~25%

**Laboratory Supplies**
- **Waters** (liquid chromatography), **Pall Corporation** (filtration), **Mettler-Toledo** (measurement)
- Scientists reluctant to switch suppliers — Waters claims it can't even displace its own old technologies
- FDA approval requirements create massive switching costs: switching one small supplier may require FDA recertification of entire production process

**Engineering / Industrial**
- **Rotork** (ROCE 24%): actuators controlling flows in oil/gas refineries; Royal Dutch Shell specifies Rotork by name for subcontractors; organic sales CAGR 12%/year over past decade
- **Spirax-Sarco** (ROCE ~17%): steam-based industrial process kits; engineers embedded at customer plants; 20% margins
- **IMI** (ROCE >20%): controls liquids and gases in critical applications

### The Lesson: Don't Pass on Quality at "Full" Prices

- Spirax-Sarco encountered at **17.5x earnings, ~£8/share** in 2005 → demurred as "fully valued"
- Five years later: **£18/share** → again concluded "fully valued"
- Subsequently nearly doubled again
- **Lesson**: for true "under-the-radar" businesses, a full price is often justified

## 3.1 Food for Thought (September 2003)

### Ahold Collapse: Why Specialist Analysts Failed

- **Ahold** (Dutch international food retailer): one of Europe's most significant destructions of shareholder value
- Marathon's generalist team spotted dangers from capital misallocation, mismanagement, murky accounting in advance; specialist sell-side analysts did not
- Five systematic flaws in the specialist analyst model:

**1. Too Close to Management ("Capture")**
- Specialist analysts covering a handful of companies risk becoming management mouthpieces
- Ahold evidence: analyst report titles — "Live From Zaandam," "Visit with Stop & Shop," "A night with the CFO"
- **Reciprocation tendency**: privileged information access creates psychological debt to management
- When things go wrong: **Stockholm syndrome** — analyst defends the captors

**2. Too Much Information**
- More data → more confidence, but not more accuracy (horse racing handicapper studies confirm this)
- Obsessing over Ahold's quarterly like-for-like sales per sq ft missed the forest for the trees
- Simple 5-year cash flow analysis revealed the key insight: Ahold was **not generating cash from its core business**
- **Cognitive dissonance**: once a conclusion is formed, conflicting information is blocked out
  - One broker concluded Ahold was "unfairly derated" → subsequently blind to negative information → foodservice profitability was later found to have been fraudulently misstated

**3. Living in a Cocoon**
- Specialists overexposed to company management + peer analysts; underexposed to broader world
- Herding reinforces the **"insider view"** (**Daniel Kahneman**)
- Ahold retail analysts compared it only to US peers (Albertson's, Kroger) — couldn't assess it vs. a Scandinavian paper company or Thai cement plant

**4. Poor Incentives**
- Ahold CEO rewarded primarily on **EPS growth** — boostable via acquisitions and leverage
- CEO owned fewer than **1,700 shares** (~$70,000 peak value) — minimal alignment
- Sell-side analysts never commented on incentives (avoidance: don't upset corporate finance colleagues)

**5. An Even Worse Performance Metric**
- Ahold delivered **23 consecutive quarters of double-digit EPS growth** — then had to restate 2000, 2001, and first 3 quarters of 2002
- **Goodhart's Law**: once a metric becomes a measuring stick, it ceases to be reliable
- Quarterly EPS targets are gameable via accrual accounting; analysts and management play a predictable beat-the-number game

> Marathon uses specialist analysts to cut through jargon and stay abreast of trends — but refuses to import the specialist model in-house because expertise doesn't necessarily produce superior investment results.

### 3.3 — A Capital Allocator: Sampo (September 2010)

**Theme: The best managers understand their industry's capital cycle
and invest countercyclically**

- When investors buy shares they **outsource capital allocation** to
  the CEO; "fund management" skills as important as operational skills
- **Warren Buffett** observation: most CEOs are unskilled at capital
  allocation because they rose through marketing, engineering, or
  institutional politics — not finance
- Financial companies hardest to manage: most capital allocation
  decisions; worst example = **Fred "the Shred" Goodwin** blowing
  RBS's balance sheet on ABN Amro assets just before the GFC

#### Björn Wahlroos / Sampo — Case Study in Great Capital Allocation

- **Björn Wahlroos** joined Sampo in 2001 via reverse takeover:
  sold boutique bank Mandatum to Sampo for €400m, became CEO
- First act: sold Nokia stake from 35m to 6.7m shares at avg €35/share
  (Nokia now trades at €7.2 — the sale was prescient)
- Injected Finnish P&C business into pan-Nordic "If" venture; received
  38% stake + €170m cash; combined ratio fell from 105% → 90% by 2005
  (oligopolistic pricing discipline introduced)
- 2003: bought out 100% of "If" equity at implied value €2.4bn;
  today lowest broker sum-of-parts valuation is €4bn, Wahlroos
  invites buyers at €8–9bn
- 2007 (pre-GFC): sold Finnish retail banking to **Danske Bank** at
  top-of-market price of €4.1bn cash; reinvested in **Nordea**
  (largest Nordic bank), building >20% stake at avg €6.39/share
  vs. current €7.70; half acquired at ~0.6x book vs. 3.6x achieved
  on the sale — remarkable arbitrage
- Pre-Lehman masterstroke: reduced equity weighting to 8% of investment
  portfolio, held large liquid fixed income position → deployed
  €8–9bn into commercial credit in autumn 2008 at distressed prices
- Bought **UPM-Kymmene** bonds yielding >8% (easier since Wahlroos
  was also UPM chairman); investment yielded €1.5bn gain
- Result: Sampo outperformed European financial peer group by ~2.5x
  since January 2001
- **Ideal manager checklist** (Marathon's): understands industry capital
  cycle, allocates countercyclically, properly incentivized (large
  personal equity stake), dispassionate about selling when someone
  overpays

### 3.4 — Northern Stars: Nordic Stocks (March 2011)

**Theme: Superior Nordic long-term performance reflects management quality**

- **Sweden** = world's top equity market over 20th century: 7.6% real
  annual returns vs. 6.7% US; compounded over 100 years, 2x better

- Nordic champions: **H&M, Ikea** (retail); **Maersk** (shipping);

#### Why Nordic Companies Excel

- **Wallenberg family** controls Electrolux (since 1956), Atlas Copco

### 3.5 — Say on Pay (February 2012)

**Theme: Long-term insider ownership is the best solution to the
principal-agent problem**

- Since 1998: FTSE 100 CEO pay up 4x; employee earnings up 50%;
  FTSE 100 index price *unchanged* — pay disconnected from returns
- **EPS as incentive metric** — Marathon's "bête noire":
  - Manipulable by executives
  - Takes no account of risk
  - Encourages value-destroying acquisitions and buybacks
    (especially with low rates)
  - Drives quarterly EPS guessing game; meeting EPS target becomes
    the main strategic purpose rather than capital allocation
  - **Peter Drucker** observed: searching for the right performance
    measure is "certain to do harm and to misdirect"
- **TSR (Total Shareholder Return)** better than EPS but suffers from:
  - Point-to-point distortion (start/end prices may be inflated
    by takeover speculation or general overvaluation)
  - Benchmark choice (peer group vs. market index) distorts incentives
  - **Sir Martin Sorrell** (WPP) became wealthy outperforming a small
    peer group while shareholders suffered sector underperformance
- Marathon preference: incentives benchmarked vs. **stock market index**
  (not peer group); longer measurement periods (3–5+ years);
  multiyear phasing of benefits; average European holding period
  is only 12 months — management should give low weight to
  short-term investors' preferences
- "Balanced" composite measures (EPS + ROCE + TSR) confuse management
  and invite **gaming strategies**
- **Insider ownership** = best imperfect solution to principal-agent problem:
  - Marathon skews portfolios to companies where entrepreneurs
    retain sizeable holdings
  - **Reckitt Benckiser** model: executives required to build
    significant shareholdings
  - **HSBC**: deferred shares vest after 5 years, must be held
    until retirement
  - Wealth protection instinct guards against excessive risk-taking

### 3.6 — Happy Families (March 2012)

### 4.1 — Anglo Irish Bank: The Meetings (2002-2006)

**Theme: The capital cycle of a bank blow-up, documented in
real time through eight meeting reports 2002-2006**

Meeting dates and key observations:

**May 2002** — Anglo Irish Bank first encountered; market cap £1.9bn;

**Mar 2003** — Market cap £2.5bn; loan growth accelerating; management

### 4.2 — Insecuritization (June 2006)

**Theme: Securitization removes incentives for careful lending
by allowing originators to offload risk**

- EETC (Enhanced Equipment Trust Certificates): airline industry
  securitized aircraft loans; when airlines went bankrupt post-9/11,
  holders found their "senior secured" paper worthless (aircraft had
  no value in oversupplied market); securitization created illusion
  of safety via structure, not underlying asset quality
- GreenPoint Financial: Alt-A mortgage originator; originated and
  immediately sold mortgages to Wall Street; loan quality deteriorated
  as securitization removed repayment pressure; Marathon shorted;
  GreenPoint collapsed 2007
- Moral hazard of originate-then-distribute: originator has zero
  incentive to assess borrower quality if loan is immediately sold
- Rating agencies: paid by issuers (conflict of interest);
  models used historical default data from low-rate era;
  AAA ratings on subprime CDO tranches
- CDO/CLO structure: pooled loans → tranched → senior tranche
  rated AAA by design (if underlying defaults stay below threshold);
  correlation assumptions wrong; when housing fell, all tranches fell
- Capital cycle connection: securitization is a capital supply
  mechanism — it allowed the mortgage market to attract vastly
  more capital than it could if originators held loans on balance sheet;
  this excess capital → overbuilding → bust

### 4.3 — Carry on Private Equity (September 2006)

**Theme: Private equity boom of 2005-2007 is a capital cycle
bubble, not a sustainable business model innovation**

Ten concerns Marathon identified at peak PE cycle:
1. Deals done at 6-7× EBITDA (historical avg ~5×)
2. Debt-to-EBITDA ratios at 6-7× (2× historical norm)
3. Covenant-lite loans: banks competing to lend with no protections
4. PIK (payment in kind) notes: interest deferred → compounds
5. "Club deals": PE firms teaming up rather than competing
   = lower prices for seller → higher returns?? No — herd behavior
6. Secondary buyouts: PE selling to PE → no strategic buyer
   willing to pay the price
7. Management incentives misaligned: carried interest focuses
   PE on exits, not operations
8. Pension fund inflows chasing past returns = capital at peak
9. Vintage-year risk: all 2006-2007 funds will face same
   exit market simultaneously
10. LBO borrowing doubled in Europe in 2005 alone

PE industry raised $250bn in 2005; Stanley Churn (fictional) memo
captures the logic: "We just need to bring [management] on board
and reload their incentives ... the principal-agent conflict isn't
our problem — it's our solution."

Contrast with Marathon's actual approach: avoid capital-cycle
industries; hold for long periods; look for businesses that don't
need PE financial engineering to create value

### 4.5 — Pass the Parcel (November 2006)

**Theme: CDO/CLO structure replaced bank relationship lending
with a hot-potato system designed to transfer risk, not manage it**

- Banks shifted from "originate and hold" (1960s-1990s) to
  "originate and distribute" (2000s)
- CDO structure: buy corporate loans/mortgages, pool them,
  slice into tranches; senior tranche = AAA; sell all tranches
- CLO (Collateralized Loan Obligation): same structure for
  leveraged loans (PE buyout debt)
- Key problem: when bank holds loan, it cares about repayment;
  when bank sells loan in a CDO, it only cares about the fee
- Moral hazard chain: borrower → originating bank → CDO arranger
  → rating agency → CDO buyer (pension fund/SWF); nobody at any
  step has exposure to final outcome
- IKB Deutsche Industriebank (Germany): set up Rhineland Funding
  conduit with €14bn in CDOs funded by short-term commercial paper;
  when CP market froze August 2007, IKB collapsed; German government
  bailed out
- Sachsen LB: set up Ormond Quay conduit with €17bn = 11× its equity;
  regional savings bank from Saxony with almost no capacity to
  understand what it owned; collapsed 2007

### 5.4 — Broken Banks (May 2013)

**Theme: European banking system structurally impaired —
political block prevents the consolidation necessary for
the capital cycle to clear**

- EU banking total assets: 3.5× EU GDP
  (vs 1.0× for US banking system)
- Too many banks: Germany alone has 1,500+ banks;
  most are savings/cooperative banks with implicit state backing
- Political barrier to consolidation: regional politicians
  protect local savings banks; mergers require political approval;
  Germany's banking system cannot consolidate without the
  Länder governments allowing it
- Result: zombie banks that cannot shrink fast enough to
  raise ROE; overstaffed, over-branched, under-earning
- Contrast: US bank crisis resolved faster (FDIC seized and
  resolved 500+ banks 2008-2012); EU dragged process out
  over 10 years
- Key metric: European bank return on equity averaged 4-6%
  post-crisis vs 10-12% cost of equity → destroying value
  every year of operation

### 5.5 — Twilight Zone (November 2013)

**Theme: QE and ZIRP have created zombie corporations
that should fail but cannot — this prevents the capital
cycle from clearing and kills long-term productivity**

- **Zombie firms**: defined as companies that cannot cover
  interest expense from operating income; kept alive by
  cheap credit; prevent healthy competitors from gaining
  market share (zombies keep prices low, absorb workers)
- EU auto industry: massive excess capacity; BMW/Mercedes
  profitable but competitors (Fiat, PSA, Renault) burning
  cash; political pressure prevents plant closures
- **Volkswagen**: announced €50bn CapEx program over 5 years
  while return on invested capital was barely above cost of
  capital → classic late-cycle capital deployment
- EU steel industry: ArcelorMittal and peers running mills
  at 60-70% utilization; no plant closures; EU politicians
  block consolidation citing employment
- Ultra-low interest rates = zombie life support: at 5%
  rates, weak firms fail; at 0.5% rates, they survive
  indefinitely; the cycle cannot clear; healthy firms
  lose the pricing power they'd gain from competitor exits

### 5.6 — Capital Punishment (December 2013)

**Theme: Three forces frustrate the capital cycle —
politics, China's state capitalism, and disruptive technology**

Marathon identifies three cases where normal capital cycle
dynamics break down:

**1. Political interference** (steel, autos, airlines):
Politicians block consolidation to protect jobs; subsidize
losers; prevent exits; airlines in particular: constantly
in capital cycle bust but politically too important to die

**2. China's state capitalism**: SOEs don't respond to
returns signals; investment/GDP decisions made by
government planners; capital floods into excess capacity
regardless of returns (900 breweries, cement everywhere);
loss-making SOEs recapitalized by state banks rather than
being allowed to fail

**3. Disruptive technology**: A new technology can eliminate
the value of existing capital stock even before the cycle
clears; digital photography destroyed film; streaming
destroyed video rental; old capital exits not via bankruptcy
but via obsolescence — which is even harder to model

Marathon's response: shift toward businesses with durable
competitive advantages that can resist these distortions;
look for pricing power, network effects, switching costs

### 5.7 — Living Dead (January 2014)

**Theme: QE creates zombie capitalism — Marathon's critique

- Federal Reserve QE program: $85bn/month at peak (2013);

- Japan ZIRP: Bank of Japan at 0% rates since 1999; result
  not growth but deflation and zombie corporations; "The Lost
  Decade" became "The Lost Two Decades"
- High yield bond spreads at all-time lows by 2014 despite

- CLO issuance: $82bn in 2013, approaching pre-crisis levels;

- BIS warning (2013): "The return of risk appetite is not

- Marathon's critique: central bank policy saved the

### 5.8 — Relax, Mr. Piketty (May 2014)

**Theme: Piketty's Capital in the Twenty-First Century
misunderstands the capital cycle; returns to capital
are not inevitably rising**

- Piketty thesis: r > g (return on capital > GDP growth)
  → wealth concentration increases forever
- Marathon's counter: Piketty conflates capital stock (total
  value of wealth) with return on capital (profit rate)
- Capital cycle analysis shows: high returns attract capital
  → excess capital → returns fall; the mechanism that Piketty
  ignores is competition and new investment
- Piketty data: 19th-century wealth concentration was high
  partly because entry barriers were high (land, regulatory
  monopolies); post-WWII competition and creative destruction
  reduced returns
- Credit markets in 2014 did support Piketty's concern:
  CLOs at $82bn, covenant-lite above 50%, BIS warning —
  but this is QE-driven distortion, not inevitable capitalism
- Long-run forecast: when interest rates normalize, many
  QE-era returns will prove illusory; the capital cycle
  will reassert itself

### 6.1 — Oriental Tricks (November 2003)

### 6.3 — Game of Loans (March 2005)

**Theme: Despite 10%+ annual GDP growth for 25 years,
Chinese stocks have delivered appalling investment returns —
why?**

The paradox: China is the greatest economic growth story
of the modern era. Yet a dollar invested in the Hang Seng
China Enterprises Index in 1993 with dividends reinvested
was worth just 35 cents by 2005. How?

Root causes:
- **State capitalism subverts allocation**: 600 central SOEs
  + thousands of provincial/municipal equivalents; they pursue
  national development agenda, not shareholder returns
- **Debt forgiveness**: SOEs with ultra-low returns stay alive
  because banks don't demand repayment; zombie firm problem
  at national scale; the listed version of an SOE has to pay
  market interest but unlisted subsidiaries don't
- **Provincial competition creates copycat projects**: every
  province wants a brewery, a steel mill, an auto plant;
  result = 900 breweries in China; beer cheaper than water
  in some provinces
- **Investment-driven growth**: 40%+ of China's economy is
  investment spending (vs ~18% in US); even exceeded Korea's
  investment-heavy 1960s model; diminishing returns inevitable
- **Input growth, not productivity growth**: China meets 10%
  GDP target by deploying ever-more inputs (workers, capital,
  land); not improving productivity; Tsing Tao Brewery: shortening
  production cycle could raise output 20% without new capital
- **Real estate**: 2004 decision to auction all land drove up
  developer land costs → higher housing prices → oversupply

Marathon's prediction (2005): wait for the hangover from
the binge on cheap capital, then look for strong franchises
at reasonable prices. The 2008 Olympics would delay the
cycle's clearing for political reasons.

### 6.5 — Value Traps (September 2014)

**Theme: Chinese Big Four banks look cheap — Marathon
explains why they are not, using capital cycle analysis**

The surface case: China's Big Four banks (ICBC, CCB, BofC, ABC)
- Average ROE 20% in 2013; 21% average over previous 5 years
- Compound profit growth 18% since 2008
- Aggregate market cap > $650bn; liquid
- Trading at approximately book value → apparently cheap

**Deconstructing ICBC's 20.8% ROE**:
- ROA = 1.4% × leverage of 15× = ROE 20.8%
- If credit costs rise to unremarkable 1% of loans:
  ROE falls from 20.6% to 16.8%
- At normalized 10× leverage AND 1% credit costs:
  sustainable ROE = ~11.3%
- ICBC's credit costs eerily low despite rapid loan growth
  — same pattern as Bank of America pre-Lehman
  (Chart 6.2: ICBC vs BofA loan growth and credit costs 2003-2013)

**Capital cycle for banks**:
- Credit is a commodity → capital cycle fully applicable
- Banks with the best returns attract new capital;
  eventually capacity (credit) overshoots; returns fall
- Chinese banks: NOT at the right point of cycle
- Credit costs still near zero; loan growth elevated;
  capital being raised to sustain growth not deleverage
- The denouement: either rapid (Asian crisis-style)
  or drawn out (Japan-style) but inevitable

**Marathon's conclusion**: Not buying Chinese banks.
The "value" is a mirage created by understated credit
costs and unsustainable leverage.

## KEY THEMES & SYNTHESIS

### The Capital Cycle — Core Framework

The **capital cycle** is Marathon's central organizing framework:

**Phase 1 — Normal** (trough to mid-cycle):
- Supply is rational; returns are adequate; no new entrants;
  existing players maintain pricing discipline

**Phase 2 — Expansion** (mid-cycle to peak):
- High returns attract new capital; IPOs, debt issuance,
  expansion plans; management bullish; analysts upgrade

**Phase 3 — Excess** (peak):
- Capital overshoots demand; pricing power erodes;
- Debt levels peak; management makes empire-building
  acquisitions; investment bankers at maximum activity
- Minsky's Ponzi finance phase: companies can only service
  debt by taking on more debt

**Phase 4 — Bust** (peak to trough):
- Returns collapse; weaker players fail or shrink;
  capital exits; M&A collapses; IPO window closes

**Phase 5 — Trough** (where buying begins):
- Shiller P/E below 15; dividend yield > bond yield;
  management in defensive mode; no new entrants

**Supply-side signals Marathon watches**:
- IPO activity (rising = bearish)
- CapEx as % of depreciation (>2× = bearish)
- Industry consolidation vs. fragmentation
- New entrant announcements
- Management confidence in investor meetings
- Insider buying vs. selling

### Behavioral Finance Failures the Cycle Exploits

- **Competition neglect**: investors model demand; ignore supply
- **Extrapolation**: recent good returns will continue
- **Inside view**: management (and analysts close to them)
  mistake company narrative for industry reality
- **Career risk**: fund managers won't short hot sectors
  even when overvalued (benchmark risk)
- **Recency bias**: trough looks permanent; peak looks permanent

### What Good Looks Like

Quality businesses that escape the worst of the cycle:
1. **Durable competitive advantages**: pricing power, switching costs,
   network effects, regulatory moats
2. **Capital-light models**: agency businesses (Geberit, Experian),
   consumer brands (Colgate, Unilever), software
3. **Frugal, aligned management**: founders still involved;
   employee ownership; decentralized decisions
4. **Nordic management model**: Sampo, Handelsbanken, Atlas Copco,
   Ikea — frugal, long-term, anti-promotional
5. **Family control** (with caveats): aligns long-term interests;
   avoids PE pressure; risk = succession and self-dealing

- **Hyman Minsky**: Ponzi finance stages (hedging → speculative
