# Capital Returns: Investing Through the Capital Cycle

**Author:** Edward Chancellor (ed.), Marathon Asset Management
**Published:** 2015
**Pages:** 249

## What It Is

Capital Returns is a curated collection of Marathon Asset Management's
investment letters from 2002 to 2015, edited and introduced by Edward
Chancellor. The letters are not case studies or reconstructed arguments
— they were written in real time, sent to clients, and often proved
prescient years before the mainstream caught on. The collection is
organized thematically into seven chapters covering the capital cycle
framework, supply-side investing, management quality, bubble anatomy,
post-crisis distortions, China, and Wall Street behavior.

## The Core Framework: The Capital Cycle

The capital cycle is the mechanism by which high returns in any
industry attract new capital, which funds new supply, which eventually
overshoots demand, collapses returns, and forces capital to exit.
Marathon's insight is that investors overwhelmingly focus on demand
(earnings forecasts, GDP growth, consumer trends) while almost entirely
ignoring supply (new entrants, CapEx expansion, IPO activity, M&A).
Supply signals are publicly available and often dramatic, but they're
slow-moving and unglamorous — precisely why they're overlooked.

The key behavioral failure is "competition neglect": analysts build
detailed demand models while treating industry supply as fixed.
When supply catches up and margins compress, the analysts are always
"surprised." Marathon is never surprised, because they watch the supply
side obsessively.

## Seven Chapters

1. **Capital Cycle Investing** — framework, tenets, behavioral
   failures, how Marathon operationalizes it
2. **Industry Capital Cycles** — brewing, commodities, oil, semis,
   homebuilders, wind turbines: the framework applied
3. **Management Quality** — Nordic model, family control, culture,
   management meetings as research tool
4. **Accidents in Waiting** — Anglo Irish Bank (documented across
   8 meetings 2002-2006), securitization, PE bubble, Spanish
   construction, German conduits, Northern Rock, Handelsbanken
5. **The Living Dead** — post-crisis: QE creates zombies,
   European banks can't clear, Piketty debate
6. **China Syndrome** — why Marathon avoided China throughout;
   state capitalism subverts the cycle; IPO manipulation;
   Cinda; ICBC value trap; 2015 bubble
7. **Inside the Mind of Wall Street** — satirical Stanley Churn
   letters 2003-2013; Greedspin Partners; RearView Capital;
   the most cutting critique of investment banking ever written
   by an active fund manager

## What Marathon Got Right (and Wrong)

Right: Anglo Irish Bank (documented collapse in real time),
Spanish construction bubble, German conduit banks (IKB, Sachsen LB),
Northern Rock, PE bubble 2005-07, China 2015 bubble, PIIGS
recovery opportunities (Bank of Ireland +200%+, ICG +200%+)

Wrong: PICC IPO (bearish but stock returned 637% by 2014);
China avoidance cost them participation in a decade of returns
in certain sectors; timing of some post-2008 recovery calls

## Why It Matters

Capital Returns is the closest thing to a practitioner's manual for
supply-side investing. Every sector blow-up since Lehman — shale oil,
Chinese solar, US homebuilders, European banks — follows the same
template Marathon documented. The framework is generalizable, the
evidence is specific, and the satirical Chapter 7 is one of the
sharpest indictments of investment banking culture in print.
