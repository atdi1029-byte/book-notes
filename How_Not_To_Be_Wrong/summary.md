# How Not to Be Wrong: The Power of Mathematical Thinking
## Jordan Ellenberg (2014) — 468 pages

### One-Line Summary
Mathematics is not computation but a powerful extension of common sense that reveals hidden structures in the world and helps us reason correctly about everything from politics to probability to public policy.

### Key Themes

**1. Linearity and Its Limits**
Linear thinking — extrapolating straight lines into the future — is one of the most common and dangerous errors in reasoning. The Laffer Curve shows that tax policy has a nonlinear relationship with revenue (both 0% and 100% tax rates yield zero revenue). Obesity projections, missile trajectories, and economic forecasts all suffer from false linearity: things that are straight locally are curved globally.

**2. The Misuse of Significance Testing**
The null hypothesis significance test (p < 0.05) has become the "gatekeeper of scientific publication" but is deeply misunderstood. It does NOT tell you the probability that your hypothesis is true. The reductio ad unlikely framework breaks down when base rates are low — a test that's 95% specific will still produce mostly false positives when testing for something rare. The p-hacking crisis, file drawer problem, and multiple comparisons issue all undermine the framework.

**3. Expected Value and Rational Decision-Making**
Expected value governs rational betting — but only when you can play many times. The Cash WinFall lottery exploit (MIT and Random Strategies groups) showed how sophisticated players could find positive-EV bets in poorly designed systems. Pascal's Wager extends expected value to infinite stakes. But expected value alone can mislead: utility (the subjective value of money) matters more than raw dollars, and variance determines whether a positive-EV bet is actually worth taking.

**4. Regression to the Mean**
Perhaps the most misunderstood statistical phenomenon. Galton discovered it studying hereditary height: tall parents have tall children, but not AS tall. This isn't a force — it's an arithmetic necessity whenever two variables are correlated but not perfectly so. Horace Secrist wasted ten years "proving" that businesses regress to mediocrity, not realizing it was mathematical inevitability. The same fallacy infects Scared Straight programs, medical studies on bran, and baseball's "Home Run Derby Curse."

**5. Correlation Is Not Causation (But It's Not Nothing)**
The smoking-lung cancer debate exemplifies the challenge. R.A. Fisher, the father of significance testing, argued that genetic confounders could explain the correlation — you couldn't rule out that the same genes that make you want to smoke also make you susceptible to cancer. It took decades of converging evidence (dose-response, cessation effects, biological mechanism) to establish causation. Berkson's fallacy shows that even spurious correlations can appear when you condition on a common effect.

**6. The Impossibility of Perfect Democracy**
Arrow's impossibility theorem proves that no voting system can simultaneously satisfy a small set of reasonable fairness criteria. The Condorcet paradox shows that majority preferences can be cyclic — A beats B, B beats C, C beats A — making "the will of the people" incoherent. This isn't a technical failure; it's a fundamental feature of collective decision-making. Burke, Florida 2000, and the Burlington VT mayoral race all illustrate the paradox in practice.

**7. The Creative Power of Mathematical Thinking**
Non-Euclidean geometry (Bolyai, Lobachevskii) showed that denying Euclid's parallel postulate doesn't produce contradictions but creates entirely new, consistent geometries. This transformed mathematics: Hilbert's formalist program, Russell's paradox, Gödel's incompleteness theorems, and the realization that mathematical truth is richer and stranger than anyone imagined. Mathematics is creative work — building new structures, not just discovering pre-existing ones.

### The Book's Central Argument
Mathematical thinking is not about computation but about reasoning correctly. It's "the extension of common sense by other means" — and when common sense fails (as it often does with probability, statistics, and complex systems), mathematical thinking is what keeps us from being wrong. The goal is not certainty but "principled uncertainty" — being rigorously unsure, knowing why you're unsure, and quantifying how unsure you are.
