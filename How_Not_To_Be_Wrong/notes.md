# How Not to Be Wrong — Jordan Ellenberg (2014)
# Raw Notes

## INTRODUCTION: When Am I Going to Use This?

- Opens with a student asking her math teacher "When am I going to use this?" about computing definite integrals. The standard teacher answer ("you might need it someday") is a lie both know.
- Better answer: Math is not just computation — it's like weight training for soccer. Integrals are drills that build strength, speed, insight. You may never use them directly but the mathematical thinking they develop is everywhere.
- "Mathematics is like wearing a pair of X-ray specs that reveal hidden structures underneath the messy and chaotic surface of the world."
- "Math is a science of not being wrong about things."

### Abraham Wald and the Missing Bullet Holes
- Central story: Abraham Wald, Jewish mathematician from Klausenburg (now Cluj, Romania), born 1902. Studied pure math at University of Vienna — set theory, metric spaces.
- 1930s Austria: couldn't get hired as professor (foreigner, Jewish). Rescued by Oskar Morgenstern (later co-inventor of game theory) who hired him at Austrian Institute for Economic Research.
- Got fellowship at Cowles Commission in Colorado Springs, then professorship at Columbia when Nazis took Austria.
- Joined the Statistical Research Group (SRG) — WWII classified program at 401 West 118th St, Manhattan. "The most extraordinary group of statisticians ever organized." Members included Frederick Mosteller (founded Harvard stats dept), Leonard Jimmie Savage (decision theory, Bayesian statistics), Norbert Wiener (cybernetics). Milton Friedman was often the fourth-smartest person in the room.
- Wald was the smartest. Functioned as "enemy alien" — secretaries pulled papers from his hands as soon as he finished writing.
- THE PROBLEM: Military wanted to armor planes but armor = weight = less maneuverable. Planes returning from combat had bullet holes concentrated in fuselage (1.73/sq ft), fuel system (1.55), but fewer in engines (1.11).
- Officers' instinct: armor where the bullet holes are (fuselage).
- WALD'S INSIGHT: Armor where the bullet holes AREN'T — on the engines. The planes hit in the engine didn't come back. Surviving planes with fuselage holes prove fuselage hits are survivable. This is survivorship bias.
- "Set some variables to zero" — if engine hit = guaranteed crash, you'd see bullet holes everywhere EXCEPT the engine on returning planes. That's exactly the data.
- Recommendations used through Korea and Vietnam.

### Survivorship Bias in Mutual Funds
- Morningstar's Large Blend funds: grew 178.4% average from 1995-2004 (10.8%/year). Sounds great.
- But Savant Capital study (2006): this only counts funds that survived 10 years. Dead funds (the failures) are excluded.
- Including dead funds: return drops to 134.5% (8.9%/year). Review of Finance study (2011): excess return rate of 2,641 survivors about 20% higher than if you include failed funds.
- Same survivorship bias as the bullet holes — you're only looking at what came back.

### Mathematics Is the Extension of Common Sense by Other Means
- Student asks: Wald's insight seems like common sense, not math. What's mathematical about it?
- Answer: Math IS common sense, extended by rigorous methods. Addition is commutative (a+b = b+a) — that's common sense formalized.
- Multiplication commutativity (a×b = b×a) is less obvious. Ellenberg's childhood memory: lying on floor age 6, looking at stereo speaker with 6×8 grid of holes. Realized he could see 6 rows of 8 OR 8 columns of 6 — same total either way. Commutativity of multiplication discovered, not told.
- Math takes common sense and extends it to places where common sense alone can't reach — that's "the extension of common sense by other means."

### Math as Common Sense (continued, pp. 21-27)
- Teaching math as "a long list of rules" is wrong. "This is not mathematics." Math is the study of things that come out a certain way because there is no other way they could possibly be.
- Not everything in math is as transparent as addition — you can't do calculus by common sense. But calculus is still *derived* from common sense. Newton took physical intuition about objects moving in straight lines, formalized it, built a universal mathematical description of motion on top.
- Our built-in mental systems for assessing probability are "pretty weak and unreliable, especially when it comes to events of extreme rarity." That's when we shore up intuition with theorems and make a mathematical theory of probability.
- "Math is like an atomic-powered prosthesis that you attach to your common sense, vastly multiplying its reach and strength."
- Iron Man analogy: the actual wall-breaking force comes from servomechanisms powered by a beta particle generator, but from Tony Stark's POV, he's just punching a wall — only much harder. Math extends what you can already do.
- To paraphrase Clausewitz: "Mathematics is the extension of common sense by other means."
- Without math's rigor, common sense can lead you astray (officers armoring the wrong parts of planes). But math *without* common sense is sterile rule-following — "math would actually be what the peevish calculus student believes it to be."
- John von Neumann's 1947 essay "The Mathematician" warned: when a mathematical discipline travels too far from its empirical source, it becomes "more and more purely l'art pour l'art" and risks degenerating into "a disorganized mass of details and complexities."

### What Kinds of Mathematics Will Appear in This Book? (pp. 23-27)
- School math gives false impression that math is "settled" — a sequence of facts and rules from higher authority. In reality, "even concerning the basic objects of study, like numbers and geometric figures, our ignorance is much greater than our knowledge."
- Four-quadrant model of mathematical ideas: Simple vs. Complicated on x-axis, Shallow vs. Profound on y-axis.
  - Simple/Shallow: 1+2=3, sin(2x) = 2sinxcosx — easy to verify, no conceptual heft
  - Complicated/Shallow: multiplying two 10-digit numbers, computing trace of Frobenius on modular form of conductor 2377 — hard work but doesn't enrich understanding of world
  - Complicated/Profound: Riemann Hypothesis, Fermat's Last Theorem, Poincare Conjecture, P vs. NP, Godel's Theorem — deep meaning but brutal technicality, each the protagonist of its own book
  - Simple/Profound: THIS is where the book lives ("YOU ARE HERE"). Ideas accessible with pre-algebra or a bit more, but they are *principles* whose applications extend far beyond what you'd think of as mathematical.
- Ellenberg's personal journey: grew up in pure math "convent," dedicated to number theory (Gauss called it "the queen of mathematics"). Worked on sums of fourth powers of whole numbers, then got enticed deeper — "residually modular Galois representations," "cohomology of moduli schemes," "dynamical systems on homogeneous spaces." Research stretched from Oxford to Princeton to Kyoto to Paris to Madison.
- But the more abstract his research got, the more he noticed simple, old, profound math was everywhere in the real world. Started writing for magazines/newspapers about math in everyday life — "even people who said they hated math were willing to read them."
- Von Neumann again: "It is pretty hard to *understand* mathematics without *doing* some mathematics." No royal road to geometry (Euclid to Ptolemy, or Menaechmus to Alexander the Great).
- Book will include: trigonometric functions describing variable relationships, calculus on linear vs. nonlinear phenomena, quadratic formula as cognitive model, set theory as metaphor for Supreme Court/baseball, analytic number theory, information theory and combinatorial designs (MIT undergrads winning Mass. state lottery).
- "There will be no homework, and there will be no test."

## CHAPTER 1: Less Like Sweden

### The Swedishness-Prosperity Debate (pp. 29-33)
- Opens with Daniel J. Mitchell of the Cato Institute blog post during ACA debate: "Why Is Obama Trying to Make America More Like Sweden when Swedes Are Trying to Be Less Like Sweden?"
- Cato worldview drawn as a chart: x-axis = "Swedishness," y-axis = prosperity. It's a STRAIGHT LINE sloping downward — more Swedish = worse off. Sweden is moving toward "Libertopia" (less government), so why is Obama going the other direction?
- Alternative picture (from liberals): same axes, but the curve has a HUMP in the middle. Peak prosperity is at a point more Swedish than America but less Swedish than Sweden. In this picture, it makes perfect sense for Obama to add more welfare state while Swedes trim theirs — both are converging on the optimum from different sides.
- KEY INSIGHT: The difference between these two pictures IS the difference between linearity and nonlinearity. The Cato curve is a line; the alternative is not. Lines have a special property: the maximum on a line segment must be at one end or the other. So if the relationship is linear, the answer is always "go to the extreme."
- "Nonlinear thinking means *which way you should go depends on where you already are*."
- This isn't new — Horace: "Est modus in rebus" ("There is a proper measure in things"). Aristotle in *Nicomachean Ethics*: eating too much or too little both harm the constitution; the optimum is in between. The relation between eating and health is curved, with bad outcomes on both ends.
- Mitchell's reasoning is an example of "false linearity" — assuming a line when the real curve is nonlinear.

### Something-Doo Economics: The Laffer Curve (pp. 33-40)
- The Laffer curve is actually that second picture (the humped curve). Economic conservatives used to understand nonlinearity better than anyone.
- Ben Stein in *Ferris Bueller's Day Off*: "Anyone know what this is? Class? Anyone?... The Laffer curve... Something-doo economics. 'Voodoo' economics."
- Legend: Arthur Laffer, economics professor at U of Chicago, dinner in 1974 with Dick Cheney, Donald Rumsfeld, and WSJ editor Jude Wanniski at upscale DC hotel restaurant. Arguing about Ford's tax plan, Laffer grabbed a napkin and drew the curve.
- The curve: x-axis = tax rate (0% to 100%), y-axis = government revenue. At 0% tax, revenue = 0 (obviously). At 100% tax, revenue also = 0 — because if government takes everything, why bother working? People either stop working or go underground. So the curve MUST go up from zero, reach a peak, and come back down to zero. The relationship between tax rate and revenue is necessarily nonlinear.
- This means: if you're on the LEFT side of the peak, raising taxes increases revenue (as you'd expect). But if you're on the RIGHT side, raising taxes *decreases* revenue. If you're right of the peak, there's a "politically peachy solution" — lower tax rates to increase revenue.
- "Which way you should go depends on where you are."
- In 1974, top income tax rate was 70% (applied to income above $200K). The idea of being on the right-hand downslope had appeal. Wanniski brought it to public consciousness in his 1978 book *The Way the World Works*. He was a "true believer" — when called a nut, he responded: "Thomas Edison was a nut, Leibniz was a nut, Galileo was a nut..."
- Ellenberg's aside: "People with out-of-the-mainstream ideas who compare themselves to Edison and Galileo are *never actually right*."
- Hal Varian: "You can explain it to a Congressman in six minutes and he can talk about it for six months." Wanniski became advisor to Jack Kemp, then Reagan.
- Reagan's own experience: as wealthy movie star in 1940s, wartime income surtax hit 90%. "You could only make four pictures and then you were in the top bracket... So we all quit working after about four pictures." (David Stockman recalling Reagan's reasoning.)
- But: Greg Mankiw (Harvard, Republican, chaired Council of Economic Advisors under Bush 43) writes that "subsequent history failed to confirm Laffer's conjecture." Reagan's tax cuts led to LESS revenue, not more. Personal income tax revenue per person fell 9% (inflation-adjusted) 1980-1984, even as average income grew 4%. America was probably on the LEFT side of the curve even in Reagan's day. Today top rate is 35%, making it even harder to argue we're past the peak.
- Milton Friedman didn't think we should aim for the top of the Laffer curve at all — money obtained by government "was more often spent badly than well." He just wanted lower taxes period.
- Interesting wrinkle from Mankiw: the very richest (paying 70% on top tranche) DID contribute more revenue after Reagan's cuts. This suggests the revenue-maximizing strategy might be: jack up taxes on the middle class (who have no choice but to keep working) while slashing rates on the rich (who can credibly threaten to withdraw economic activity). A vexing conclusion for liberals.
- Laffer himself was more modest than his followers. He drew no numbers on his curve. In congressional testimony about the optimal tax rate: "I cannot measure it frankly, but I can tell you what the characteristics of it are; yes, sir." The curve just says lower taxes *could* increase revenue under some circumstances — figuring out which circumstances "requires deep, difficult, empirical work, the kind of work that doesn't fit on a napkin."
- The false syllogism: "It *could* be the case that lowering taxes increases revenue; I *want* it to be the case; Therefore it *is* the case."
- The Laffer curve doesn't have to be a smooth hill — could be a trapezoid, dromedary's back (two humps), or wildly oscillating. But whatever its shape, if it slopes upward somewhere it must slope downward somewhere else. "There is such a thing as being too Swedish" — no economist would disagree.

## CHAPTER 2: Straight Locally, Curved Globally

### Linear Reasoning Is Everywhere (pp. 41-42)
- Linear reasoning is pervasive — every time you say "if something is good to have, having more of it is even better." Political shouters rely on it: "You support military action against Iran? I guess you'd like a *ground invasion* of every country that *looks at us funny!*" Or: "Engagement with Iran? You probably *also* think *Adolf Hitler* was just *misunderstood*."
- Why does anyone think all curves are straight lines when they're obviously not? Answer: in a sense, they ARE. That story starts with Archimedes.

### Exhaustion: Archimedes and the Area of a Circle (pp. 42-49)
- Problem: what's the area of a circle with radius 1? Today we know it's pi*r^2 = pi. But 2,000 years ago this was a vexing open question.
- Greeks didn't think of pi as a number — they only understood whole numbers (1, 2, 3...). The Pythagorean Theorem, their first great success, turned out to be the ruin of their number system.
- Pythagorean Theorem: hypotenuse squared = sum of squares of other two sides. For isosceles right triangle with legs = 1: hypotenuse^2 = 1 + 1 = 2. So hypotenuse = sqrt(2). Try to make it rational: legs = 5, hypotenuse ≈ 7 (but 5^2 + 5^2 = 50, 7^2 = 49 — close but not exact). Legs = 12, hypotenuse ≈ 17 (12^2 + 12^2 = 288, 17^2 = 289 — tantalizingly close).
- Around 5th century BCE, a Pythagorean discovered there was NO way to measure the isosceles right triangle so every side was a whole number. Sqrt(2) is irrational — not the ratio of any two whole numbers. To the Pythagoreans, this meant the hypotenuse was "not a number at all."
- The Pythagoreans were "extremely weird" — they believed odd numbers were good and even numbers evil; that a planet called Antichthon lay on the other side of the sun; that eating beans was wrong (repository of dead people's souls). Pythagoras "was said to have had the ability to talk to cattle."
- Story (probably apocryphal): Hippasus, who discovered the irrationality of sqrt(2), was tossed into the sea by his colleagues. "But you can't drown a theorem."
- Euclid and Archimedes rolled up sleeves. Method of exhaustion (original idea from Eudoxus of Cnidus, Euclid book 12, but Archimedes brought it to fruition).
- Inscribe a square inside the circle (radius 1): square breaks into 4 right triangles, each half of a 1x1 square ("tuna fish sandwich"). Area of inscribed square = 4 × (1/2) = 2. So circle area ≥ 2.
- Circumscribe a square around the circle: sides = 2, area = 4. So circle area ≤ 4.
- Now inscribe an octagon (8 sides): area = 2*sqrt(2) ≈ 2.83. Circumscribed octagon: area = 8(sqrt(2) - 1) ≈ 3.31. Circle area trapped between 2.83 and 3.31.
- Keep doubling sides: 16-gon gives area between 3.06 and 3.18. Then 32-gon, 64-gon... A 65,536-sided polygon looks indistinguishable from a circle.
- KEY INSIGHT: "It doesn't matter whether it's a circle or a polygon with very many very short sides." The area of the little fringe between circle and polygon has been "exhausted" by relentless iteration. The circle has a curve, but every tiny piece can be well approximated by a straight line — "just as the tiny little patch of earth's surface we stand on is well approximated by a perfectly flat plane."
- "The slogan to keep in mind: straight locally, curved globally."
- Zooming-in analogy: streak toward the circle from a great height — first you see the whole circle, then a segment of arc, then a smaller segment, until what you see is "pretty much indistinguishable from a line." An ant on the circle would think it's on a straight line.

### The Page Where I Teach You Calculus (pp. 52-55)
- Newton's insight: nothing special about circles — *every* smooth curve, when you zoom in enough, looks just like a line. Doesn't matter how winding or snarled, just that it doesn't have sharp corners.
- Missile trajectory: parabolic arc (gravity curves all motion toward earth). Zoom in on a small segment → looks like a straight line going upward at an angle. Zoom in more → even straighter. Closer and straighter, closer and straighter...
- Newton's conceptual leap: go all the way. Reduce your field of view to *infinitesimal* — smaller than any size you can name, but not zero. Studying the missile's arc not over a time interval but at a single moment. What was *almost* a line becomes *exactly* a line. The slope of this line is what Newton called the *fluxion*, now called the *derivative*.
- Archimedes wouldn't have gone this far — he understood polygons got closer to circles but wouldn't say a circle actually WAS a polygon with infinitely many infinitely short sides.
- George Berkeley, most famous objector, denounced Newton's infinitesimals: "And what are these fluxions? The velocities of evanescent increments. And what are these same evanescent increments? They are neither finite quantities, nor quantities infinitely small, nor yet nothing. May we not call them the ghosts of departed quantities?"
- Yet calculus WORKS. Swing a rock on a string and release it — it shoots off in exactly the direction calculus predicts (tangent line at point of release). Objects in motion proceed in straight lines unless a force intercedes — that's why linear thinking is natural; our intuition about time and motion is formed by physical phenomena.

### Evanescent Increments and Unnecessary Perplexities (pp. 55-60)
- Newton's critics had a point — his construction of the derivative wasn't rigorous by modern standards. The infinitely small was a sticking point for thousands of years.
- Zeno's paradox (5th century BCE, Eleatic school): to walk to the ice cream store, first go halfway, then half the remaining distance, then half again... You get closer and closer but never arrive. Diogenes the Cynic "refuted" this by simply standing up and walking across the room.
- Numerically: progress = 1/2 + 1/4 + 1/8 + 1/16 + 1/32 + ... Ten terms ≈ 0.999. Twenty terms ≈ 0.999999. Getting really close to 1 but never reaching it.
- This connects to: is 0.99999... equal to 1? "I have seen people come nearly to blows over this question." Debated on World of Warcraft fan pages and Ayn Rand forums.
- Quick arguments for 0.999... = 1: (1) 0.333... = 1/3, multiply by 3 → 0.999... = 1. (2) Multiply 0.999... by 10, get 9.999..., subtract original → 9 × (0.999...) = 9, so 0.999... = 1.
- But these arguments are "algebraic intimidation" — they don't address the deep unease.
- DANGER of applying same tricks to divergent series: 1 + 2 + 4 + 8 + 16 + ... Using the multiply-by-2 trick: 2S = S - 1, so S = -1. Adding bigger and bigger positive numbers somehow equals -1? That's absurd.
- Grandi's series: 1 - 1 + 1 - 1 + 1 - 1 + ... Group as (1-1) + (1-1) + ... = 0. Or group as 1 - (1-1) - (1-1) - ... = 1. Let T = the sum, then -T = T - 1, so T = 1/2. Italian mathematician/priest Guido Grandi (1703) argued the sum was 1/2 and that this represented creation of the universe from nothing. Leibniz and Euler were on board with the computation.
- A student named Brian, unhappy with classroom definitions, worked out his own theory of infinitesimals — "Brian numbers." He had a colleague at Princeton, Edward Nelson, expert in nonstandard analysis (developed by Abraham Robinson, mid-20th century, finally made sense of Berkeley's "evanescent increments"). When Nelson told Brian the infinitesimals wouldn't be called "Brian numbers," Brian lost all interest. "Moral lesson: people who go into mathematics for fame and glory don't stay in mathematics for long."
- The real answer: 0.999... is a sum .9 + .09 + .009 + ... But an infinite sum doesn't have a numerical value *until we give it one*. That was Augustin-Louis Cauchy's great innovation — introducing the notion of *limit* into calculus in the 1820s.
- G. H. Hardy in *Divergent Series* (1949): "It does not occur to a modern mathematician that a collection of mathematical symbols should have a 'meaning' until one has been assigned to it by definition." Before Cauchy, mathematicians asked "What *is* 1 - 1 + 1 - 1..." instead of "How shall we *define* 1 - 1 + 1 - 1..." — leading to "unnecessary perplexities and controversies which were often really verbal."
- This is not relativism — just because we CAN assign any meaning doesn't mean we should. Good definitions are ones that settle perplexities without creating new ones.
- Cauchy's solution: .9 + .09 + .009 + ... gets closer to 1 the more terms you add, and never gets farther away. No matter how tight a cordon around 1, the sum will eventually penetrate it and never leave. Under those circumstances, *define* the sum to be 1. He proved this didn't cause contradictions, creating the rigorous framework for Newton's calculus.

### Cauchy's Resolution and the Habit of Definition (pp. 61-62)
- The cost of Cauchy's approach: we can't have both "infinite series obey normal arithmetic" and "every number has a unique decimal expansion." We must discard one. Cauchy discards uniqueness of decimals — 1 and 0.999... are two strings of digits referring to the same number, like synonyms in English.
- Grandi's series (1 - 1 + 1 - 1 + ...) is outside Cauchy's framework — it's a *divergent series*, the subject of Hardy's book. Niels Henrik Abel (1828): "Divergent series are the invention of the devil." Hardy's modern view: more forgiving — some divergent series should get values, some shouldn't. If you assign a value to Grandi's series, all interesting theories agree it should be 1/2.
- Cauchy was "an unwavering conservative and a royalist, but in his mathematics he was proudly revolutionary." Once he figured out how to do calculus without dangerous infinitesimals, he rewrote his syllabus at the Ecole Polytechnique. This enraged everyone — mystified students (signed up for freshman calculus, not cutting-edge pure math), colleagues (engineering students didn't need this rigor), and administrators (whose official course outline he completely ignored). The Ecole imposed a new curriculum emphasizing the traditional approach and placed note takers in Cauchy's classroom. "Cauchy did not comply. Cauchy was not interested in the needs of engineers. Cauchy was interested in the truth."

## CHAPTER 3: Everyone Is Obese

### The Obesity Prediction (pp. 63-65)
- Opens with Eugene Mirman joke: "I read that 100% of Americans were Asian... But Eugene, *you're* not Asian... I read that I was!"
- Paper in the journal *Obesity* asked: "Will all Americans become overweight or obese?" Answer: "Yes — by 2048."
- Got huge press: ABC News "obesity apocalypse," *Long Beach Press-Telegram* "We're Getting Fatter." Tapped into American anxiety about national moral decline (1960s: long hair/Communists; 1980s: arcade games/Japanese competition; now: fast food/obesity).
- "I have some good news. We're not all going to be overweight in the year 2048. Why? Because not every curve is a line."

### Linear Regression Explained (pp. 65-68)
- Every curve is pretty close to a line (Newton's insight) — that's the idea behind *linear regression*, "the statistical technique that is to social science as the screwdriver is to home repair."
- Every newspaper claim about correlations (more cousins = happier, more Burger Kings = looser morals, extra $10K income = 3% more likely Republican) is the result of a linear regression.
- Example: tuition vs. SAT scores at 31 private North Carolina universities (2007 data). Elon University: SAT 1217, tuition $20,441. Guilford College: SAT 1131, tuition $23,420. Not a perfect relationship, but plot all 31 and a clear trend appears (Wake Forest and Davidson at top right, Cabarrus College of Health Sciences at bottom).
- Linear regression finds the line that comes closest to passing through all points. Slope ≈ 28: each extra SAT point = $28 more tuition. Raise SAT 50 points = charge $1,400 more. From parent's view: kid improves 100 SAT points = extra $2,800/year.
- "Linear regression is a marvelous tool, versatile, scalable, and as easy to execute as clicking a button on your spreadsheet." Works for 2 variables or a thousand.
- BUT: it's also dangerous. "I said linear regression was like a screwdriver... but in another sense, it's more like a table saw."

### The Missile That Never Comes Down (pp. 68-71)
- Missile example revisited: you're the TARGET, not the launcher. You track the missile's altitude at 5 time points — a line fits beautifully. Model says: every minute, missile rises 400 meters. After an hour, it's 24 km up. "When does it come down? It never comes down! An upward sloping line just keeps on sloping upward."
- "(Blood, gristle, screams.)" The missile's path is a parabola, not a line. Line is good for 5 seconds of extrapolation but catastrophically wrong for an hour.
- Mark Twain, *Life on the Mississippi*: The Mississippi between Cairo and New Orleans was 1,215 miles 176 years ago, 1,180 after a 1722 cutoff, 1,040 after the American Bend cutoff — losing 67 miles recently, averaging 1⅓ miles/year. "Therefore, any calm person... can see that in the Old Oolitic Silurian Period, just a million years ago next November, the Lower Mississippi River was upward of one million three hundred thousand miles long... And by the same token... seven hundred and forty-two years from now the Lower Mississippi will be only a mile and three-quarters long." Brilliant satire of linear extrapolation: "One gets such wholesale returns of conjecture out of such a trifling investment of fact."

### Aside: How to Get Partial Credit on My Calculus Exam (pp. 71-74)
- Calculus methods are mechanical — your calculator can carry them out, and it's dangerous to use them inattentively.
- A student gets -4 grams of water in a jug. If they write "I screwed up somewhere, but I can't find my mistake" — half credit. If they write "-4g" and circle it — zero credit. Working an integral or regression is something a computer can do; understanding whether the result makes sense requires human judgment.
- "A math course that fails to do so is essentially training the student to be a very slow, buggy version of Microsoft Excel."
- The "math wars" in teaching: traditionalists (memorization, fluency, algorithms, exact answers) vs. reformists (meaning, guided discovery, approximation). Fierce debate — "At a math dinner party it's okay to bring up politics or religion, but start an argument about math pedagogy and it's likely to end with somebody storming out."
- Ellenberg is neither camp. Can't throw out multiplication tables (you'll never achieve mental flow if you need a calculator for 6×8). But some classical algorithms can be safely discarded (extracting square roots by hand). Calculators are useful tools.
- Fields Medalist David Mumford has suggested replacing plane geometry with a first programming course — both require assembling simple components into sequences that accomplish meaningful tasks.
- The danger of over-emphasizing algorithms: creates students who "test very well but know no mathematics at all." The danger of over-emphasizing meaning: students who say "I get the concept, but I couldn't do the problems" — which is really shorthand for "I don't get the concept."
- William Carlos Williams: "*no ideas but in things*."
- Goal: teach "a mathematics that values precise answers but also intelligent approximation, that demands the ability to deploy existing algorithms fluently but also the horse sense to work things out on the fly, that mixes rigidity with a sense of play."

### Back to the Obesity Apocalypse (pp. 74-77)
- The *Obesity* paper used NHANES data (National Health and Nutrition Examination Study). Overweight = BMI ≥ 25. In early 1970s, just under half of Americans were overweight. Early 1990s: almost 60%. By 2008: almost 75%.
- Youfa Wang and coauthors plotted prevalence over time, drew a linear regression, and extended the line to 2048 where it crosses 100%.
- Problem: "current trends will not continue. They can't!" By 2060, the line gives 109% overweight — impossible. In reality, the curve must flatten as it approaches 100% (fewer skinny people left to convert). "The thin we have always with us!" Indeed, just 4 years after the paper, NHANES showed the upward march had already begun to slow.
- Even worse: Wang broke data by ethnic group/sex. Black men's obesity rate growing at half the overall rate — their line doesn't cross 100% until 2095. In 2048, only 80% of black men would be overweight. But if ALL Americans are supposed to be overweight by 2048, where are the 20% non-overweight black men? "Offshore?"
- "That basic contradiction goes unmentioned in the paper. It's the epidemiological equivalent of saying there are -4 grams of water left in the bucket. Zero credit."

## CHAPTER 4: How Much Is That in Dead Americans?

### The Rhetoric of Proportion (pp. 79-82)
- Opens with Daniel Byman (Georgetown counterterrorism) in *Foreign Affairs*: Israeli military reports 1,074 Israelis killed and 7,520 wounded by Palestinians from 2000-2005 — "the proportional equivalent of more than 50,000 dead and 300,000 wounded for the United States."
- This "how much is that in dead Americans" calculation is standard: U.S. House (Dec 2001) said 26 killed in Israel = 1,200 American deaths proportionally. Newt Gingrich (2006): 8 dead Israelis = almost 500 Americans. Ahmed Moor in *LA Times*: 1,400 Palestinians in Gaza = 300,000 Americans proportionally.
- Not limited to Israel: Gerald Caplan in *Toronto Star* (1988): 45,000 Nicaraguan casualties = 300,000 Canadians or 3 million Americans. Robert McNamara: 4 million Vietnamese deaths = 27 million Americans.
- This is "lineocentrism in its purest form." 1,074 out of 6-7 million Israelis = 0.015%. Apply 0.015% to 300 million Americans = 50,000. But push the logic: 1,074 = 7,700 Spaniards, 223,000 Chinese, 300 Slovenes, or "one or two Tuvaluans" (pop. 9,847).
- The reasoning breaks down quickly: "When there are two men left in the bar at closing time, and one of them coldcocks the other, it is not equivalent in context to 150 million Americans getting simultaneously punched in the face."
- Rwanda: 11% of population wiped out in 1994 — clearly among worst crimes of the century. But we don't say it was "nine times as bad as the Holocaust" proportionally. "And to do so would set teeth rightly on edge."

### Mathematical Hygiene and the Brain Cancer Paradox (pp. 82-85)
- Important rule: when field-testing a mathematical method, try computing the same thing several different ways. If you get different answers, something's wrong.
- Example: 2004 Atocha train bombings in Madrid killed ~200. What's the equivalent at Grand Central Station? U.S. has ~7x Spain's population → 1,300 Americans. But 200 is 0.006% of Madrid's population; scaling to NYC (2.5x larger) → 463. Or compare province of Madrid to state of New York → ~600. Multiple different answers = red flag.
- Proportions DO matter though. Stephen Pinker's *Better Angels of Our Nature*: world has grown less violent over history. The 20th century looks bad only because of large populations — the Thirty Years' War killed 1 in 100 people on Earth. Today that would be 70 million, more than both world wars combined.
- Brain cancer rates by state: South Dakota leads (5.7 per 100,000, vs. national average 3.4), followed by Nebraska, Alaska, Delaware, Maine. Places to avoid!
- But the LOWEST rates: Wyoming, Vermont, North Dakota, Hawaii, DC. Wait — these are also tiny states.
- The five states at top and five at bottom share the same characteristic: "hardly anyone lives there." Of the nine states/DC at the extremes, the biggest is Nebraska (37th most populous). Living in a small state either makes you much more or much less likely to get brain cancer — which makes no sense.

### The Coin-Flipping Analogy and the Law of Large Numbers (pp. 85-86)
- Game: "who's the best at flipping coins." Team Small flips 10 coins each, Team Big flips 100. Score by absolute heads → Team Big wins easily (typical Big player gets ~50, best Small player maybe 8-9 out of 10).

### Coin Flipping Simulations and the Law of Large Numbers (pp. 81-83)
- Score by proportion instead of raw number? Still unfair. With 100 Smalls, at least one likely gets 8/10 = 80% heads. None of the Bigs will get 80% — you'd need about two BILLION Big players before one gets that lopsided. "The more coins you throw, the more likely you are to be close to 50-50."
- Ellenberg's simulations: 10-coin flips: 4, 4, 5, 6, 5, 4, 3, 3, 4, 5, 5, 9, 3, 5, 7, 4, 5, 7, 7, 9... 100-coin: 46, 54, 48, 45, 45, 52, 49, 47, 58, 40, 57... 1000-coin: 486, 501, 489, 472, 537, 474, 508, 510...
- J. E. Kerrich, South African mathematician, made ill-advised visit to Europe in 1939 — "his semester abroad quickly turned into an unscheduled stint in an internment camp in Denmark." Flipped a coin 10,000 times tracking heads. The fraction of heads converges inexorably toward 50%, "as if squeezed by an invisible vise."
- Smalls range: 30-90% heads. With 100 coins: 40-60%. With 1,000: 46.2-53.7%. "Something is pushing those numbers closer and closer to 50%. That something is the cold, strong hand of the Law of Large Numbers."
- Principle first asserted informally by Girolamo Cardano (16th century), named "la loi des grands nombres" by Simeon-Denis Poisson in early 1800s.

### The Gendarme's Hat: De Moivre and the Normal Distribution (pp. 83-88)
- Jakob Bernoulli (early 18th century) gave the first precise statement and mathematical proof of the Law of Large Numbers.
- The Law tells you the Big-Small game is unfair. Scoring by raw number favors the Bigs; scoring by PERCENTAGE favors the Smalls. "The smaller the number of coins — what we'd call in statistics the *sample size* — the greater the variation in the proportion of heads."
- Same effect in political polls (less reliable with fewer voters), brain cancer rates (small states dominate both top and bottom), and NBA shooting percentages.
- NBA example: A month into 2011-12 season, five players tied for highest shooting percentage: Armon Johnson, DeAndre Liggins, Ryan Reid, Hasheem Thabeet, Ronny Turiaf. "*Who?*" They barely played — the five took 13 shots between them and hit them all. Armon Johnson appeared in one game for Portland, took one shot, made it. The real best shooter was Tyson Chandler (Knicks), 141/202. NBA restricts rankings to players above a playing-time threshold to avoid this.
- North Carolina school accountability: top 25 schools rated on test score improvement get a banner. C.C. Wright Elementary (North Wilkesboro) won with 91.5 score — 418 students. Kingswood Elementary (315 students) at 90.9, tiny Riverside in Appalachian Newland (161 students) at 90.4. Thomas Kane and Douglas Staiger study: 28% of smallest schools made top 25 at some point vs. only 7% of all schools. But small schools also dominated the BOTTOM (schools assigned state "assistance teams" for low scores were also predominantly smaller).
- "Riverside Elementary is no more one of the top elementary schools in North Carolina than Armon Johnson is the sharpest shooter in the league."
- Abraham de Moivre (early contributor to probability theory, 1756 book *The Doctrine of Chances*) wanted to know HOW MUCH closer to 50% you get. Key insight: the typical discrepancy from 50-50 is governed by the SQUARE ROOT of the number of coins. 100x as many coins → discrepancy grows by factor of 10 in absolute terms, but as a proportion it SHRINKS (because sqrt grows slower than the number itself). 1,000-coin flippers might miss by 38 heads, but that's only 3.8%.
- This is the same principle underlying standard error in political polls: to halve the error bar, survey 4x as many people.
- De Moivre also found that discrepancies from 50-50 form a perfect bell curve — the normal distribution. (Francis Ysidro Edgeworth proposed calling it "the gendarme's hat" — didn't catch on.)
- Bell curve: tall in middle, very flat at edges. If you flip N coins, chance of being within sqrt(N) of 50% heads ≈ 95.45%. For 1,000 coins (sqrt = ~31), 18 of 20 trials landed between 469 and 531 heads.

### The Law of Averages Is False (pp. 88-89)
- De Moivre saw regularities in coin flips as "the work of God's hand itself." Dangerous to think this way — it leads to the "so-called law of averages": five heads in a row means tails is surely next. Have three sons, a daughter is "due."
- But coins have no memory. After 10 heads in a row, the next flip is still 50-50. The coin doesn't know what happened before.
- How does the Law of Large Numbers work then? "Not by balancing out what's already happened, but by *diluting* what's already happened with new data, until the past is so proportionally negligible that it can safely be forgotten." If you flip 1,000 more times after 10 heads, you'll get ~500 heads out of 1,000, so 510 heads out of 1,010 total — close to 50%.

### Survivors: When Proportions Make Sense and When They Don't (pp. 90-92)
- If you rank bloodshed by proportion of population eliminated, worst offenses concentrate in smallest countries. Matthew White's *Great Big Book of Horrible Things*: top three 20th-century atrocities by proportion were massacre of Herero of Namibia by German colonists, Cambodian slaughter by Pol Pot, and King Leopold's war in Congo. Hitler, Stalin, Mao don't make the list (big populations they decimated).
- Rule of thumb: if the magnitude of a disaster makes it feel right to talk about "survivors," then it makes sense to measure by proportion. You can call any Tutsi living in Rwanda a "survivor" of the genocide (75% of Tutsi wiped out). But it's absurd to call someone in Seattle a "survivor" of the World Trade Center attack (0.001% of Americans died).
- Atrocities form a "partially ordered set" — some pairs can be meaningfully compared, others cannot. "The question of whether one war was worse than another is fundamentally unlike the question of whether one number is bigger than another. The latter question always has an answer. The former does not."
- Best approach to understanding 26 people killed by terrorism: "imagine twenty-six people killed by terrorist bombings — not halfway across the world, but in your own city. That computation is mathematically and morally unimpeachable, and no calculator is required."

## CHAPTER 5: More Pie Than Plate

### Percentages with Negative Numbers (pp. 93-96)
- Michael Spence and Sandile Hlatshwayo: between 1990 and 2008, U.S. gained 27.3 million net jobs. 26.7 million (98%) came from the "nontradable sector" (government, health care, retail, food service — can't be outsourced). Widely reported in *The Economist*, Bill Clinton's book.
- But 98% is misleading. The tradable sector grew by only 620,000 jobs — but it COULD HAVE DECLINED (and did between 2000-2008: lost 3 million while nontradable added 7 million — nontradable accounted for 175% of total gain!). Finance and computers alone added over 620,000 tradable jobs; manufacturing losses balanced them out.
- "Don't talk about percentages of numbers when the numbers might be negative."
- Historical context: even negative numbers were controversial. Sixteenth-century algebraists Cardano and Francois Viete argued about whether negative × negative = positive. Cardano called negative solutions *ficta* (fake).
- Coffee shop example: lost $500 on coffee, made $750 on pastry case, $750 on CD rack. Total profit: $1000. Pastry case = 75% of profits. CD rack ALSO = 75% of profits. If coffee losses were $1000 more, total = $0, and pastry = infinity percent of profits.

### Wisconsin Jobs and Romney's 92.3% (pp. 96-100)
- June 2011: Republican Party of Wisconsin touted that Wisconsin created 9,500 jobs when the nation only added 18,000 — "over 50% of U.S. job growth came from our state." But Minnesota (Democratic governor) added 13,000+, and Texas, California, Michigan, Massachusetts also outpaced Wisconsin. Job losses in other states roughly canceled out job gains, so EVERY growing state could claim a huge percentage of the (tiny) national total.
- Steven Rattner NYT op-ed using Piketty-Saez data: "93 percent of the additional income created in 2010 went to the top 1 percent." Infographic: 37% to top 0.01%, 56% to rest of top 1%, just 7% for bottom 99%.
- But the top 10%-but-not-1% (family doctors, non-elite lawyers, engineers) averaged $159K in 2009, rising to ~$161K in 2010 — accounting for 17% of total income gained. 93% + 17% = more than 100%. "More pie than plate." Works because the bottom 90% actually had LOWER income in 2011 than 2010. Negative numbers again.
- 1992: top 1% accrued 131% of national income gains. 1982-83: 91% went to top 10%-but-not-1%, AND top 1% got 63%. The bottom 90% was losing ground while the situation brightened for everybody else.
- Mitt Romney campaign (April 2012): "Women account for 92.3% of all jobs lost under Obama." Technically correct: Jan 2009 employment 133,561,000 → March 2012: 132,821,000 = 740,000 net loss. Women: 66,122,000 → 65,439,000 = 683,000 fewer. 683/740 = 92.3%.
- But these are NET job losses. If you start counting from February 2009 (one month later), the total loss was only 16,000, women lost 484,000 → women accounted for over 3,000% of all jobs lost. "That would have signaled to any but the thickest voters that this percentage was somehow not the right measure."
- Real story: Jan 2009-Feb 2010 (recession): men lost 2,971,000 jobs, women lost 1,546,000. Feb 2010-March 2012 (recovery): men gained 2,714,000, women gained 863,000. Men took it worse in the decline AND gained more in the recovery. Men's losses/gains nearly canceled out; women's didn't. The "92.3%" figure was "true but false" — *Washington Post*'s assessment.
- "Mitt Romney has never denied allegations that for years he's operated a bicontinental cocaine-trafficking ring in Colombia and Salt Lake City." Also 100% true! But designed to create a false impression.
- "Dividing one number by another is mere computation; figuring out *what* you should divide by *what* is mathematics."

## CHAPTER 6: The Baltimore Stockbroker and the Bible Code

### The Bible Code (pp. 101-110)
- Opens with math's territorial ambitions — even questions about God. Maimonides in *Mishneh Torah* describes Abraham reasoning his way to monotheism through observation of the celestial sphere. "God is a thing that *must be*, as surely as 8 times 6 must be the same as 6 times 8."
- The Torah as data: 304,805 characters, a "digital signal" transmitted faithfully. Researchers at Hebrew University (mid-1990s): Eliyahu Rips (group theorist), Yoav Rosenberg (CS grad student), Doron Witztum (physics). Used "equidistant letter sequences" (ELS) — plucking characters at regular intervals.
- ELS example: "DON YOUR BRACES ASKEW" → every 5th letter = DUCK.
- Rabbi Michael Dov Weissmandl pioneered ELS in Torah in 20th century — found "Mishneh" at 50-letter intervals, then 613 letters later, "Torah" at 50-letter intervals.
- Witztum-Rips-Rosenberg systematic study: chose 32 notable rabbis from Jewish history, searched Genesis for ELS proximity of names to birth/death dates. Shuffled dates a million times as control. Real matching scored 453rd out of 1 million — far better than chance.
- Control texts (War and Peace, Isaiah, scrambled Genesis): rabbinical dates stayed in the middle of the pack.
- Published 1994 in *Statistical Science* with unusual editor's preface from Robert Kass: "Our referees were baffled... offered to *Statistical Science* readers as a challenging puzzle."
- Michael Drosnin turned it into 1997 bestseller *The Bible Code* — claimed to predict Rabin assassination, Gulf War, Comet Shoemaker-Levy 9. Appeared on Oprah, met with Arafat, Peres, Podesta.
- Controversy split Orthodox Jewish mathematicians at Harvard — David Kazhdan (open to codes) vs. Shlomo Sternberg (called it "a hoax" in *Notices of the AMS*).
- Anecdote: distinguished rabbi at Purim dinner asks code adherent, "What if you found a code saying Sabbath was on Sunday?" Young colleague: "I'd have to think about it." Rabbi concluded codes must be rejected — using them to challenge fundamental law is "about as authentically Jewish as a bacon cheeseburger."

### The Baltimore Stockbroker (pp. 110-115)
- Parable: you receive 10 straight correct stock predictions by mail. On week 11, the broker asks for your money. Seems like a genius — odds of getting 10 right by chance are (1/2)^10 = 1/1024.
- THE TRICK: Broker sent 10,240 newsletters week 1 — half predicting up, half down. 5,120 got a correct prediction. He sends those 5,120 a new letter, again split. After 10 weeks, 10 people have seen 10 perfect picks. "No matter what the stock market does."
- Real-world version: Derren Brown's 2008 TV show mailed horse-racing picks to thousands of Britons, eventually convincing one person he had a foolproof system. Exposed the trick at the end.
- Mutual fund "incubation" — companies launch many funds in-house, only make the winners public. Runts are "mercy-killed, often without any public notice." Surviving incubated funds don't maintain their prenatal performance — roughly match the median fund.
- "Eat your vegetables and take the stairs" — put money in a big dull low-fee index fund. "You've been swayed by the impressive results, but you don't know *how many chances* the broker had to get those results."
- Scrabble analogy: Ellenberg's 8-year-old dumps and redraws tiles until he gets good letters. "He has no way of knowing what letters he's going to draw! But if you give yourself enough chances, you'll eventually come across that Z you're waiting for."
- R.A. Fisher: "the 'one chance in a million' will undoubtedly occur, with no less and no more than its appropriate frequency."

### Wiggle Room and the Names of the Rabbis (pp. 115-120)
- Brendan McKay (Australian CS) and Dror Bar-Natan (Hebrew U math) challenged Bible codes. Key insight: medieval rabbis had no passports — different authors use different appellations for the same rabbi.
- Example: Rabbi Avraham ben Dov Ber Friedman known as "Rabbi Avraham," "HaMalach," "Rabbi Avraham HaMalach" — which do you search for?
- McKay and Bar-Natan chose equally valid alternative appellations → Torah no longer detected rabbi dates. But *War and Peace* (in Hebrew) now nailed the dates perfectly.
- Not that Tolstoy encoded rabbis — rather, with enough wiggle room in name choices, ANY long text will yield impressive matches for SOME list.
- Simcha Emanuel (Talmud professor, Tel Aviv) drew up an independent appellation list not designed for either text. Torah did only slightly better than chance.
- "Given enough chances, finding codes is a cinch." Drosnin dared critics to find assassination predictions in *Moby Dick*. McKay promptly found ELS sequences for JFK, Indira Gandhi, Leon Trotsky, and Drosnin himself.
- Witztum et al. insist their name list was chosen in advance. "But even if it is, it casts the miraculous success of the Bible codes in a very different light."
- Critical question: if we reject Bible codes despite passing standard statistical tests, what does that say about the reliability of standard statistical tests? "It says you ought to be a little worried about them."

## CHAPTER 7: Dead Fish Don't Read Minds

### The Dead Salmon fMRI Study (pp. 121-125)
- Craig Bennett (UC Santa Barbara neuroscientist) presented at 2009 Human Brain Mapping conference: "Neural correlates of interspecies perspective taking in the post-mortem Atlantic Salmon."
- A dead fish was scanned in an fMRI, shown photos of humans, and "found to have a surprisingly strong ability to correctly assess the emotions." Dead fish reading minds = Nobel Prize material!
- It's a deadpan gag — Methods section: "One mature Atlantic Salmon (*Salmo salar*) participated... approximately 18 inches long, weighed 3.8 lbs, and was not alive at the time of scanning."
- Point: fMRI divides brain into tens of thousands of voxels. Random noise in each voxel. With so many voxels, some will spike at exactly the right moment by pure chance. Bennett found two voxel groups in the salmon "empathizing" with humans — one in medial brain cavity, one in upper spinal column.
- Most neuroimaging articles Bennett surveyed did NOT use "multiple comparisons correction" — statistical safeguards against exactly this kind of false positive.
- "Improbable things happen a lot." NC Cash 5 lottery drew same numbers (4, 21, 23, 34, 39) twice in 3 days — seems miraculous, but hundreds of lottery games run for years, so some coincidence is inevitable.
- Aristotle: "it is probable that improbable things will happen."

### Reverse Engineering, or Why Algebra Is Hard (pp. 125-130)
- Two moments kids fall off the math train: fractions (numbers as "what portion of") and algebra (computation backward).
- Leopold Kronecker: "God made the natural numbers, and all the rest is the work of man."
- Algebra is "reverse engineering" — you know the output and must find the input. x + 8 = 15 is easy (flip to x = 15 − 8 = 7). Quadratic equations like x² − x = 1 are harder.
- Missile example: height = 100 + 200x − 5x². When does it hit ground? Solve 100 + 200x − 5x² = 0. Trial and error gets close (x = 40.49 gives ~0.8m above ground). Quadratic formula gives exact answer: x = (1/10)(200 ± √(200² + 4·5·100)).
- The ± means TWO solutions — the missile is at ground level twice (once going up, once coming down). Graphically it's a parabola crossing the x-axis at two points.

### British Birth Control Pill Scare (pp. 131-133)
- October 18, 1995: UK Committee on Safety of Medicines sent "Dear Doctor" letter warning that third-generation oral contraceptives doubled thrombosis risk. AP headline: "Pills kill."
- 12% of pill users stopped immediately. Conception rate jumped, 26,000 more babies conceived in 1996 in England/Wales, 13,600 more abortions than 1995.
- But "doubled" means 1 in 7,000 → 2 in 7,000. "Twice a tiny number is a tiny number." Like doubling your Scrabble score on ZYMURGY vs. NOSE.
- CSM supporter estimated total embolism deaths prevented: "possibly one."
- Risk ratios applied to tiny probabilities mislead. CUNY study: infants in home day care had 7x fatality rate vs. day-care centers. But rate was 1.6 vs. 0.23 per 100,000. Only ~12 babies/year died in family day cares. Car accident risk from driving to the fancier, farther center might wipe out the safety advantage.
- "The null hypothesis, if we take it literally, is probably just about always false." With a powerful enough study, you can detect effects so minuscule they should be safely ignored. Wish we'd named it "statistically noticeable" instead of "statistically significant."

### The Myth of the Myth of the Hot Hand (pp. 133-142)
- B.F. Skinner: frustrated novelist turned behaviorist. English major at Hamilton College, got praise from Robert Frost at Bread Loaf conference. Failed summer writing in Scranton ("Literature must be demolished"). Found behaviorism via Bertrand Russell → John Watson.
- Skinner tried to debunk Shakespeare's alliteration using significance tests. Null hypothesis: Shakespeare picked initial sounds randomly. Analyzed 100 sonnets — Shakespeare "failed" the significance test. Skinner: "Shakespeare might as well have drawn his words out of a hat."
- But Skinner was wrong — his test was underpowered. Alliteration WAS a conscious, known device ("Full fathom five thy father lies"). But Shakespeare used it with restraint — fellow Elizabethan George Gascoigne mocked overuse: *"Crambe bis positum mors est"* ("Cabbage served twice is death").
- "An underpowered study is the equivalent of looking at the planets with binoculars."
- 1985: Gilovich, Vallone, Tversky (GVT) applied same logic to basketball "hot hand." Analyzed every shot from 1980-81 Philadelphia 76ers. Julius Erving: after 3 straight baskets, shot 48% (vs. 52% overall). Darryl "Chocolate Thunder" Dawkins: after a hit, 57%; after a miss, 73% (opposite of hot hand!). Possible explanation: after misses, Dawkins drove for backboard-shattering dunks ("In Your Face Disgrace," "Turbo Sexophonic Delight").
- GVT broke seasons into 4-shot sequences. For a 50% shooter, 5/16 (31.25%) should be "good" (3-4 hits), 5/16 "bad" (0-1 hits). Actual data matched chance perfectly. "No hot hand."
- Tversky: "I've been in a thousand arguments over this topic. I've won them all, and I've convinced no one."
- BUT: Korb and Stillwell (2003) simulated hot hands built into data (shooting jumps to 90% for two 10-shot intervals). GVT's test STILL failed to reject null hypothesis >75% of the time. The test was underpowered.
- Players do have "hot spells" of a predictable kind — shooting better vs. weak defenses (42% vs. Indiana Pacers, 47.6% vs. Cleveland Cavaliers). Maybe call it "the warm hand."
- Huizinga and Weil (2009): larger dataset, found players who just made a basket take harder shots next time. Yigal Attali (2013): a player who made a 3-pointer was much more likely to try another long shot than after a 3-point miss. Hot hand may "cancel itself out" — confidence → overconfidence → harder shots.
- "The nature of the analogous phenomenon in stock investment is left as an exercise for the reader."

## CHAPTER 8: Reductio Ad Unlikely

### Proof by Contradiction and Significance Testing (pp. 143-150)
- The philosophical problem with NHST: "Suppose the null hypothesis is true" — but we're trying to prove it ISN'T true. Seems circular.
- But this is reductio ad absurdum — a time-honored method since Aristotle. Assume the thing you want to disprove, show it leads to contradiction, conclude it's false.
- Example: "200 children killed by guns in DC in 2012." If true, total homicides ≥ 200. But DC had only 88 homicides. Hypothesis is false. No circularity.
- Pythagorean proof that √2 is irrational: assume √2 = m/n in lowest terms, derive that both m and n must be even → contradiction (can't be in lowest terms). "So awesomely paradigm-busting they had to kill its author."
- NHST is a "fuzzy" version: assume H, derive that observation O is very improbable (p < 0.05), but O happened → H is "very improbable" (not certainly false). "Reductio ad unlikely."

### The Pleiades and John Michell (pp. 150-155)
- John Michell (18th-century astronomer/clergyman): first statistical approach to astronomy. Are the Pleiades a real cluster or random alignment? Computed probability of 6 stars appearing so close by chance: ~1 in 500,000. Concluded they must be a real cluster. (They are — several hundred adolescent stars.)
- Fisher saw explicit analogy between Michell and the classical reductio.
- Diagram of random dots shows visible clumps are inevitable. A second diagram with NO clumps actually looks LESS random — it shows "a built-in disinclination to crowd." (*Numb3rs* pilot episode used this concept.)
- BUT: the reductio ad unlikely is NOT logically sound like the true reductio. Joseph Berkson (Mayo Clinic): 50 subjects hypothesized as human (H). Observe one albino (O). Albinism rate: 1 in 20,000. P-value of seeing an albino in 50 humans < 0.0025. By NHST logic, reject H → subjects aren't human. Absurd!
- "Impossible and improbable are not the same — not even close."
- Lottery example: NC Cash 5 drew 4, 21, 23, 34, 39 twice in 3 days. Any specific pair of lottery draws has ~1 in 300 billion chance. Should we call the lottery rigged every Thursday?

### Prime Clusters and Yitang Zhang (pp. 155-162)
- Random clusters appear in prime numbers too. Yitang "Tom" Zhang (2013, UNH lecturer) proved the "bounded gaps" conjecture — there are infinitely many pairs of primes within some fixed distance of each other.
- Zhang's backstory: star student at Beijing University, came to US for PhD, career stalled. Hadn't published since 2001. Worked at Subway. Former student tracked him down, got him a lectureship. Then proved what the biggest names had failed to prove.
- Primes are "the atoms of number theory." Euclid proved they're infinite. Prime Number Theorem (Hadamard and de la Vallée Poussin): among first N numbers, about N/log N are prime.

## CHAPTER 9: The International Journal of Haruspicy

### xkcd Jelly Beans and the File Drawer Problem (pp. 163-168)
- xkcd comic: scientists test 20 colors of jelly beans for acne link. 19 show no link (p > 0.05). Green jelly beans get p < 0.05. Newspaper headline: "GREEN JELLY BEANS LINKED TO ACNE! 95% Confidence."
- If 20 labs independently test the same jelly bean and 19 find nothing, only the 1 lucky lab publishes. "They got lucky — but they don't *know* they got lucky."
- This is the file drawer problem — survivorship bias in science. "They're running the con on themselves."
- P-hacking (Uri Simonsohn, Penn): scientists with p = 0.06 tweak — delete an outlier, control for age, control for weather — until they get p < 0.05. "Torturing the data until it confesses." Usually not malicious — believers find reasons why the tweaked analysis is the "right" one.
- P-value distribution analysis: if null hypothesis always true (haruspicy), p-values from 0 to 0.05 should be evenly distributed (flat line). If real effects exist, graph should slope downward (more very-low p-values). But in real journals (poli sci, econ, psych, sociology): suspicious UPWARD bump just below 0.05. "That slope is the shape of p-hacking."
- Scientists who can't hack below 0.05: resort to phrases like "almost statistically significant," "leaning toward significance," "hovers on the brink of significance."
- Living by the .05 threshold is like defining "recession" rigidly — you'd ignore unemployment, housing starts, etc. if the technical definition wasn't met. "The critics say that a great deal of scientific practice is nuts in just this way."

### The Detective, Not the Judge (pp. 168-172)
- Confidence intervals (alternative to pure p-values): test not just null hypothesis but a range of alternatives. Artisanal pinking shears A/B test: purchases up 10%, p = 0.03. But is the effect 5%? 25%? Confidence interval [+3%, +17%] says effect is positive but maybe not huge. Interval [+9%, +11%] says it's big. Interval [-0.5%, +0.5%] says intervention does nothing. Interval [-20%, +20%] says we have no idea.
- Confidence intervals credited to Jerzy Neyman (Polish mathematician). Neyman + Egon Pearson (inherited Karl Pearson's London position + feud with R.A. Fisher).
- Neyman-Pearson philosophy: "the purpose of statistics isn't to tell us what to believe, but to tell us what to *do*." Statistics = making decisions, not answering questions. A significance test is "a rule" telling people in charge whether to approve a drug, fund a reform.
- Criminal trial analogy: courts don't determine truth but follow rules. "Guilty" = convicted fair and square, not necessarily actually did it. Same trade-off: release criminals vs. imprison innocents.
- Fisher hated this — wanted scientists to form opinions, not follow rigid rules. 1951 letter to Hick: dismissed Neyman-Pearson approach entirely. Fisher (1926): "A scientific fact should be regarded as experimentally established only if a properly designed experiment rarely fails to give this level of significance."
- "The significance test is the detective, not the judge." A significant finding is the beginning of investigation, not the end.
- Replication is science's immune system — but journals prefer novel results over replications. Failed replications end up in the file drawer.
- Reform: *Psychological Science* (2013) started publishing Registered Replication Reports (studies accepted BEFORE results known). Many Labs project: 10 of 13 high-profile findings replicated successfully.
- Fisher (1956, near death): "no scientific worker has a fixed level of significance at which from year to year, and in all circumstances, he rejects hypotheses; he rather gives his mind to each particular case in the light of his evidence and his ideas."

## CHAPTER 10: Are You There, God? It's Me, Bayesian Inference

### Big Data and Its Limits (pp. 175-182)
- Target's algorithm correctly inferred a Minnesota teenager was pregnant (from purchase patterns of unscented lotion, mineral supplements, cotton balls) before her father knew. Spooky — but maybe we should worry more about crappy algorithms than eerily good ones.
- More data doesn't always help. Asteroid tracking: more data → better predictions. Weather: chaotic system (Edward Lorenz discovered chaos theory studying weather). "One flap of a sea gull's wing would be enough to alter the course of the weather forever." Hard limit ~2 weeks ahead, no matter how much data.
- Human behavior: harder than weather because we lack even a good mathematical model. "For human action we have no such model and may never have one."
- Netflix Prize ($1 million, 2006): predict how users would rate unseen movies from ~100 million ratings. Took 3 years to achieve 10% improvement. Winning team hybridized several almost-good-enough algorithms. Netflix never used the winner — had already shifted to streaming. "Recommendations remain pretty comically bad."
- Jim Bennett (Netflix VP for recommendations): said the prize should have been bigger — 10% improvement would recoup $1M "in less time than it takes to make another *Fast and Furious* movie."

### Does Facebook Know You're a Terrorist? (pp. 182-186)
- Thought experiment: Facebook develops terrorist-prediction algorithm. Scores users by probability of terrorist ties (like Target scores pregnancy probability). Terrorism is very rare, so even a "good" algorithm fails badly.
- The 2×2 box: 200 million Facebook users, ~10,000 actual future terrorists (1 in 20,000). Algorithm flags 100,000 users as "twice as likely" to be terrorists. Among flagged: 10 actual terrorists + 99,990 innocent people. Your neighbor is on the list — 99.99% chance he's innocent.
- P-value framing: null hypothesis = neighbor is innocent. Chance of innocent person landing on list = 99,990/199,990,000 ≈ 0.05%. That's below 0.05 threshold! By NHST logic, reject null hypothesis → neighbor is guilty. But we just showed he's almost certainly innocent.
- "The p-value is the answer to the question: what's the chance the test would give a result this extreme, IF the null hypothesis were true?" But what we WANT to know is: "What's the chance the null hypothesis is true, GIVEN the test result?" These are NOT the same question.

### Iranian Election Fraud and Randomness (pp. 187-189)
- People asked to "pick a random number" between 1-20 overwhelmingly choose 17. Between 0-9, they pick 7. Numbers ending in 0 and 5 feel "less random."
- 2009 Iranian election (Ahmadinejad): Bernd Beber and Alexandra Scacco (Columbia grad students) analyzed last digits of vote counts across 29 provinces × 4 candidates (116 numbers). Too many 7s, too few 0s and 5s — pattern of human-fabricated numbers, not random ones. Not proof of fraud, but evidence in that direction.

### Bayesian Inference via Roulette Wheels (pp. 189-196)
- Three theories about a roulette wheel: RED (biased 60% red), FAIR (50/50), BLACK (biased 60% black). Prior: 90% FAIR, 5% each RED and BLACK.
- Observe RRRRR (5 reds in a row). Compute conditional probabilities: P(RRRRR|FAIR) = (1/2)^5 = 3.125%. P(RRRRR|BLACK) = (0.4)^5 = 1.024%. P(RRRRR|RED) = (0.6)^5 = 7.76%.
- Expand to 6-box diagram (3 theories × 2 outcomes). After seeing RRRRR: BLACK drops to 1.5%, FAIR is 86.5%, RED rises to 12%. Belief in RED more than doubled.
- Alternative picture: 10,000 rooms with different wheels (500 RED, 500 BLACK, 9000 FAIR). 325 rooms get RRRRR. Of those, 281 are FAIR (86.5%), 39 are RED (12%), only 5 are BLACK (1.5%).
- "Prior" = what you believe before evidence. "Posterior" = beliefs after evidence. Passage from prior to posterior is Bayesian inference, governed by Bayes's Theorem.

### Priors Matter (pp. 196-198)
- A cynic (1/3 each) who sees 5 reds: posterior = 65% RED. A trusting soul (1% RED, 1% BLACK) seeing same 5 reds: only 2.5% chance of RED. "How much you believe something *after* you see the evidence depends not just on what the evidence shows, but on how much you believed it to begin with."
- This troubles people who want science to be objective. But no one actually ignores priors. A new cancer drug with p < 0.05? Probably real. Same results from putting patients in a plastic Stonehenge replica? You'd think they got lucky.
- Facebook terrorist example revisited: your neighbor on the list has some evidence against him, but your prior (most people aren't terrorists) keeps the posterior low.
- "Relying purely on null hypothesis significance testing is a deeply non-Bayesian thing to do."

### Fisher vs. Bayesianism and the Philosophy of Probability (pp. 198-200)
- Fisher panned Keynes's *Treatise on Probability* (probability as "degree of rational belief"): "If the views of the last section... were accepted as authoritative, mathematical students in this country... would be turned away, some in disgust, and most in ignorance."
- Is "5% chance RED is true" about the global distribution of roulette wheels, or about our mental state? If the latter, Bayes's Theorem becomes "numerically flavored advice" for updating beliefs, not just a mathematical fact.
- Hard-core Bayesians: ALL beliefs should be formed by Bayesian computation. Others: Bayes's rule is a "loose qualitative guideline."

### Why RRRRR Looks Less Random than RBRRB (pp. 200-201)
- Both sequences are equally improbable. But RRRRR activates a theory (RED) with non-negligible prior. RBRRB doesn't activate any plausible theory. Our priors are "not flat, but spiky" — we assign mental weight to only a few theories.
- Feynman's license plate joke: "I saw a car with the license plate ARW 357. What was the chance I would see that particular one tonight? Amazing!"
- Marijuana and flat priors: every stimulus seems "intensely meaningful." "A very interesting mental state to be in. But it's not conducive to making good inferences."

### Conspiracy Theories as Bayesian Armor (pp. 201-202)
- Boston Marathon bombing: friend says it was a government inside job (theory T). You assign 0.1 prior. Evidence rolls in (suspects identified, confession) — each should lower T. But friend adds theory U: media and government are in on it together. T + U is harder to believe initially but becomes immune to counter-evidence. "T + U remains untouched" by any amount of disconfirming data.
- Conspiracy theories are "the multi-drug-resistant E. coli of the information ecosystem."

### The Cleanest Man in School and the Argument by Design (pp. 202-206)
- College friend bought 200 Cat-in-the-Hat-drinking-beer T-shirts at $4 each, sold at $10. After selling 80 (recouped investment), stopped selling and started wearing them. Everyone thought he was the dirtiest man in school — but he was actually the cleanest, wearing a brand-new shirt every day.
- Sherlock Holmes: "When you have excluded the impossible, whatever remains, however improbable, must be the truth." But Holmes *should* have said: "...unless the truth is a hypothesis it didn't occur to you to consider."
- William Paley's *Natural Theology* (1802): the watchmaker argument. Find a watch on a heath → infer a maker. Complex life → infer God. Darwin admired the book. Modern intelligent design rests on this argument.
- Bayesian box: GOD vs. NO GOD, we exist vs. we don't. Using principle of indifference (50/50 prior), and assuming P(humans|NO GOD) is one-in-a-billion-billion while P(humans|GOD) is one-in-a-million: GOD box has a trillion times more probability. Seems to prove God.
- BUT: what about GODS (committee of gods — "I'm thinking pandas")? If we give GODS the same prior as GOD, Bayesian inference favors GODS over GOD. And SIMS (Nick Bostrom's simulation hypothesis): if a simulation would almost certainly include humans, SIMS gets prior probability of 1/4 → posterior is highest of all four!
- "If we took this approach seriously, we would tell our tenth graders" that the simulation hypothesis is the best-supported explanation for our existence. "Think the school board would go for this?"
- Ellenberg's honest conclusion: "On this matter, math is silent." Pascal in *Pensées*: "God is, or He is not. But to which side shall we incline? Reason can decide nothing here."

## CHAPTER 11: What to Expect When You're Expecting to Win the Lottery

### History and Expected Value of Lotteries (pp. 209-216)
- Lottery origins: 17th-century Genoa — evolved from betting on elections. Every 6 months, 2 *governatori* drawn by lot from 120 councilors. Gamblers bet on outcomes, then realized they didn't need actual elections. By 1700, Genoa ran a number-drawing lottery like modern Powerball.
- Spread to all of Europe and America. Harvard ran lotteries in 1794 and 1810 to fund dorms (still used for first-years).
- Adam Smith in *Wealth of Nations*: lotteries are "a tax on the stupid." But Smith's reasoning was flawed — he argued buying more tickets makes you MORE likely to lose. Actually, buying 2 tickets doubles your chance of winning.
- The missing concept: EXPECTED VALUE. Lottery with 10M combinations, $1 tickets, $6M jackpot. Buy all tickets: spend $10M, win $6M, lose $4M. Buy 6M tickets: 60% chance of winning, 40% chance of losing — but you're in a "much more dangerous position" than the 1-ticket buyer.
- Expected value calculation: (9,999,999/10,000,000 × $0) + (1/10,000,000 × $6,000,000) = $0.60. Ticket costs $1, so it's a bad deal.
- "Expected value is not the value you expect." Better name: "average value" — it's what you'd average over many plays (Law of Large Numbers).

### The Million Act and Edmond Halley (pp. 216-218)
- Expected value emerged mathematically in mid-1600s. Edmond Halley (comet guy) was one of the first to apply it: pricing life annuities for the "Million Act" of 1692 (Parliament raising war funds).
- Pre-Halley annuities were priced the same regardless of age — a grandfather paid the same as a child! Halley used birth/death statistics to compute probability-weighted expected payouts.
- "Eet ees obvious" anecdote: Harvard math Professor O, midway through a derivation, is asked why two operators commute. "Eet ees obvious." Student pushes back. Professor retreats to his office for 10 minutes. Returns: "Yes. Eet ees obvious."

### Don't Play Powerball (pp. 218-222)
- Powerball expected value with $100M jackpot: ~$0.94 for a $2 ticket. Bad bet.
- With $337M jackpot (Donald Lawson, Michigan, Aug 2012): EV = $2.29. Seems positive!
- But jackpot threshold for EV > $2 is ~$285M. Happened 3 times in 2012.
- HOWEVER: with 75 million players, 35% chance someone else also wins → split jackpot. Expected jackpot share: 65% × $337M + 35% × $168M = $278M. Below the threshold.
- Biggest Powerball: $588M (split by two). Biggest U.S. lottery: $688M Mega Millions (split three ways).
- Taxes, lump-sum discount, and state garnishment for child support/back taxes make it worse. Anecdote: man comes to lottery office with girlfriend to cash $10K ticket, finds out most goes to delinquent child support owed to ex-girlfriend. "This was the first the man's current girlfriend had heard of the man's child." Weekend did not go as planned.
- Three-point Powerball strategy: (1) Don't play. (2) If you play, only when jackpot is huge. (3) Pick unpopular numbers to avoid splits.

### The Cash WinFall Lottery Scam That Wasn't (pp. 222-232)
- July 12, 2005: MIT student buys 14,000 tickets ($28,000) at Cambridge Star Market. 12 more stores ask for waivers. Tens of thousands of tickets flowing to small groups in Quincy.
- Cash WinFall (launched fall 2004, replacing Mass Millions): when jackpot exceeded $2M without being won, the excess "rolled down" to enhance lower prizes. This was intentional — Massachusetts wanted a game where big wins were possible without hitting the jackpot.
- Normal day EV: ~$0.80 per $2 ticket (bad). Roll-down day (Feb 7, 2005): $600K rolled to match-5, $1.4M rolled to match-4 pools. Match-4 prize jumped from $150 to $2,385. EV of match-4 alone: $2,385/800 = $2.98. Total roll-down day EV: $5.53 per $2 ticket. "An investment where you make three and a half bucks of profit on a $2 investment is not one to pass up."
- James Harvey, MIT senior, discovered this while doing an independent study project comparing state lotteries. Formed "Random Strategies" (named after Random Hall dorm). Bought thousands of tickets, tripled their money on first roll-down day.
- Ying Zhang (medical researcher, Northeastern PhD) formed Doctor Zhang Lottery Club (DZLC). Bought $300K of tickets per roll-down. Zhang quit doctoring in 2006 to play full-time.
- Gerald Selbee, 70s retiree from Michigan with math degree. He and wife Marjorie drove to Deerfield, MA. First bet: 60,000 tickets → $50K profit. Selbee exploited 5% store commission — split halfsies with one store, netting thousands extra per roll-down.
- By 2007: 1M+ tickets per roll-down, mostly from three syndicates. Match-4 prize dropped from $2,300 to ~$800. But still profitable.
- State's perspective: on roll-down day with 1.5M tickets sold, state gets $3.8M ($1.2M to coffers + $2.6M from jackpot + receipts), pays out $3.8M. Break-even for players at 2.5M tickets ($5M revenue). Over WinFall's lifetime, always fewer than that → players always profited.

### Additivity of Expected Value (pp. 232-234)
- E(X+Y) = E(X) + E(Y) — the expected value of the sum equals the sum of expected values.
- With 1M players on roll-down day and $3.8M in total prizes: each ticket's EV = $3.8M/1.5M = $2.53. Expected profit per $2 ticket: $0.53 (25% return).
- Additivity seems obvious but ISN'T — substitute "most likely value" and it fails. Give fortune randomly to one of three children: most likely value for each = $0 (2/3 chance of nothing). But most likely value of the SUM is the whole estate.

### Buffon's Needle, Noodle, and Circle (pp. 235-238)
- Buffon's needle problem: drop a needle of length 1 on a floor with parallel lines spaced 1 apart. Probability it crosses a line = 2/π.
- Barbier's elegant proof via additivity of expected value: a "bent" needle (polygon) crossing a line is the sum of crossings by its component segments. A circle of diameter 1 (circumference π) always crosses exactly 2 lines, regardless of where it falls. So expected crossings = πp, and since it's always 2: p = 2/π.
- Generalization: ANY curve of length L has expected crossings = Lp = 2L/π. "Throw a mass of spaghetti on the tile floor and I can tell you exactly how many times to expect a strand to cross a line." This is Buffon's noodle problem.

### The Sea and the Stone (pp. 238-240)
- Pierre Deligne on his teacher Alexander Grothendieck: "Nothing seems to happen, and yet at the end a highly nontrivial theorem is there."
- Grothendieck's metaphor: math is like the sea rising around a stone — "the sea advances insensibly in silence, nothing seems to happen, nothing moves... yet it finally surrounds the resistant substance."
- "Mathematics as currently practiced is a delicate interplay between monastic contemplation and blowing stuff up with dynamite."
- Barbier published his proof at 21 (École Normale Supérieure). By 25, left town with a nervous condition. Found in a mental asylum 15 years later. Grothendieck also left academia in the 1980s, lives in Pyrenean seclusion.
- David Foster Wallace's "Math Melodrama" myth: math drives you crazy. Movies (*A Beautiful Mind*, *Proof*, *π*) perpetuate it. Ellenberg disagrees: "Mathematics tends to strengthen the mind rather than strain it to its breaking point... Math, like meditation, puts you in direct contact with the universe."

### Cash WinFall Endgame (pp. 240-244)
- Random Strategies made $3.5M over WinFall's seven-year life. Harvey bought a used 1999 Nissan Altima.
- August 2010: Random Strategies' masterstroke. Jackpot at $1.675M (below $2M roll-down threshold). Other cartels waited. Random Strategies quietly bought 700,000 tickets over the weekend, pushing jackpot to $2.1M. They held 90% of tickets. Made $700,000 profit (50% return on $1.4M investment).
- Lottery installed early-warning system. Random Strategies tried again Christmas Eve 2010; lottery announced roll-down on Christmas morning; other cartels canceled vacations and bought tickets.
- *Boston Globe* reporters Andrea Estes and Scott Allen broke the story July 31, 2011 — noticed Michigan people winning Massachusetts lottery. August 2011: lottery capped sales at $5,000 per retailer per day. Last WinFall drawing: January 23, 2012.

### Voltaire's Lottery (pp. 244-246)
- 18th-century France: government attached lottery to bond sales. Michel Le Peletier des Forts (deputy finance minister) botched the math — prizes exceeded ticket receipts. Positive expected value.
- Charles-Marie de La Condamine (mathematician/explorer) organized a ticket-buying cartel. Voltaire (François-Marie Arouet) joined, writing cheeky mottoes on tickets: "All men are equal!" and "Long live M. Peletier des Forts!" Then, as now, that's no way to get rich."

### Why Massachusetts Allowed It (pp. 246-248)
- Lottery kept 40 cents of every $2 ticket for commissions, operations, and city/town governments (~$900M in 2011). The $1.20 went to prize pool.
- Roll-down money was always earmarked as prize money. The state didn't lose on roll-down days — it was paying out accumulated jackpot funds. The more tickets sold, the more 40-cent cuts. "$120 million of revenue on Cash WinFall."
- The cartels "weren't beating the house. *They were the house.*" State was the Super-Casino above them.
- "If gambling is exciting, you're doing it wrong."

## CHAPTER 12: Miss More Planes!

### Utility and Expected Value (pp. 249-254)
- George Stigler (1982 Nobel): "If you never miss the plane, you're spending too much time in airports."
- Three options: arrive 2hrs early (miss 2%), 1.5hrs (miss 5%), 1hr (miss 15%). Expected value requires comparing apples to oranges — time wasted vs. flights missed. Enter UTILITY, measured in "utils."
- 1 hour at home = 1 util. Missed plane = −6 utils. Option 1: −2 + 2%(−6) = −2.12 utils. Option 2: −1.5 + 5%(−6) = −1.8 utils (best!). Option 3: −1 + 15%(−6) = −1.9 utils.
- If you HATE missing planes (−20 utils), Option 1 becomes best at −2.4 utils. But arriving 3 hours early = −3 utils (worse even than that). Graph of utility vs. arrival time is a Laffer curve — optimum in the middle, bad on both extremes.
- "If you've never totaled your car, you drive too slow." Government waste: SSA paid $31M to 1,546 dead people — but that's 0.004% of total disbursements. Eliminating the last bit of waste costs more than the waste itself. "What's the right amount of the taxpayer's money to be wasting?"

### Pascal's Wager (pp. 254-258)
- Pascal and Fermat's 1654 correspondence = birth of probability theory. Pascal's "night of fire" (Nov 23, 1654): mystical experience, documented on parchment sewn into his coat lining. Largely withdrew from math after. When Fermat proposed a meeting in 1660, Pascal replied: "I can find little difference between a man who is nothing else but a geometrician and a clever craftsman."
- *Pensées* ("Thoughts"): posthumous collection, aphoristic, despairing. "Let us imagine a number of men in chains, and all condemned to death..."
- Thought 233, "Infini-rien" (Infinity-nothing) = Pascal's Wager. God's existence can't be settled by logic. But belief is a gamble with the highest possible stakes.

### Pascal's Wager and the St. Petersburg Paradox (pp. 258-264)
- Pascal's "night of fire" (Nov 23, 1654): mystical revelation documented on parchment sewn into his coat. "FIRE. God of Abraham, God of Isaac, God of Jacob. Not of the philosophers and the scholars..." Largely withdrew from math after this. Told Fermat in 1660: geometry is "so useless" he "can scarcely remember that there is such a thing."
- *Pensées* ("Thoughts"): posthumous, aphoristic. Thought 233 "Infini-rien" = Pascal's Wager. God's existence can't be settled by logic, but belief is a gamble. Expected value of believing: if God exists, infinite reward (eternal life). Even with tiny probability of God existing, infinity × (tiny probability) = infinity. So the expected value of believing is infinite, while cost of believing is finite.
- St. Petersburg paradox (named by Daniel Bernoulli, 1738): flip a coin until tails. If tails on flip k, you win 2^k ducats. Expected value: (1/2)(2) + (1/4)(4) + (1/8)(8) + ... = 1 + 1 + 1 + ... = infinity. But nobody would pay a million ducats to play.
- Bernoulli's resolution: utility ≠ money. "A ducat in the hand of a rich man is not worth the same as a ducat in the hand of a peasant." Utility curves upward but bends — having 2,000 ducats isn't twice as good as having 1,000. Specifically, utility grows like the logarithm: 2^k ducats = k utils. Expected utility = (1/2)(1) + (1/4)(2) + (1/8)(3) + ... = 2 utils. Finite and small.

### Mankiw vs. Lebowitz Utility Curves (pp. 262-264)
- Different people have different utility curves. Greg Mankiw (Harvard economist): if taxes go up, he works less (diminishing marginal utility of money means his equilibrium shifts). Like a cowboy-movie star making fewer movies.
- Fran Lebowitz: drove a cab until she had enough for rent and food, then stopped and wrote. Money above her threshold had zero utility. If taxes go up, she works MORE to reach the same threshold.
- Two different utility curves produce opposite predictions about the same policy. There's no universal utility curve.

### Daniel Ellsberg and the Limits of Expected Utility (pp. 266-270)
- Ellsberg (before Pentagon Papers): brilliant RAND analyst studying nuclear strategy. Harvard PhD student. Had been picking at foundations of game theory since his senior thesis.
- Ellsberg's paradox: urn with 90 balls — 30 red, 60 black/yellow (unknown split). Four bets: RED ($100 if red), BLACK ($100 if black), NOT-RED ($100 if black or yellow), NOT-BLACK ($100 if red or yellow).
- People prefer RED over BLACK (known 1/3 probability) and NOT-RED over NOT-BLACK (known 2/3 probability). But RED + NOT-RED = $100 guaranteed, and so does BLACK + NOT-BLACK! How can one guaranteed $100 be preferable to another?
- Expected utility theory can't explain this: if each bet has a util value, RED > BLACK and NOT-RED > NOT-BLACK implies RED + NOT-RED > BLACK + NOT-BLACK, but they're identical.
- Ellsberg's answer: known unknowns (risk) vs. unknown unknowns (uncertainty). "Risky strategies can be analyzed numerically; uncertain strategies... were beyond the bounds of formal mathematical analysis." People averse to uncertainty, not just risk.
- "In the world of cold war Washington and RAND... that those tools might actually be limited in their application, especially in contexts for which there was no precedent and thus no means of estimating probabilities — like, say, *the instantaneous reduction of the human race to radioactive dust* — must have been at least a little troubling for Ellsberg."

## CHAPTER 13: Where the Train Tracks Meet

### Variance and the Cash WinFall Strategy (pp. 271-278)
- Selbee used Quic Pic (random numbers). Random Strategies picked numbers by hand. Same expected value — so why bother?
- Answer: VARIANCE. $50K sure thing vs. 50/50 bet on losing $100K or winning $200K have same EV ($50K), but the bet can ruin you. Corporation: loss = −100 utils, gain = 200 utils, EV = +50 utils. Poor person: loss = −1,000 utils, gain = 400 utils, EV = −300 utils. "The richer you are, the more risks you can afford to take."
- "Picking up pennies in front of a steamroller" — 99% chance of making $1M, 1% chance of losing $50M. Positive EV but lethal variance. Leverage: scale up to $100M per transaction. If the steamroller gets you: "if you're down a million bucks, it's your problem; but if you're down five billion bucks, it's the government's problem." (Long-Term Capital Management, 2008 crisis.)
- Bonds vs. stocks: bonds = low variance (sure money), stocks = higher variance (likely better return but might crash). Diversification = variance reduction.
- *If retirement planning is exciting...*

### The Transylvanian Lottery and Combinatorial Designs (pp. 278-282)
- Random Strategies picked numbers by hand to reduce variance. Mini example: 7-ball lottery, pick 3 for jackpot, also win for matching 2 ("deuces"). 35 possible combos.
- Selbee (Quic Pic, 7 random tickets): expected 2.4 deuces, but wide variance (5.3% chance of zero, 0.1% chance of all 7 being deuces).
- Harvey (hand-picked 7 tickets: 124, 135, 167, 257, 347, 236, 456): ALWAYS gets either jackpot or exactly 3 deuces. 20% jackpot, 80% three deuces. Same expected deuces (2.4) but much lower variance. Guarantees a minimum payoff.
- Finding optimal hand-picked tickets for 300,000 tickets from 10M options: combinatorial explosion. Traveling salesman problem has 30 vigintillion routes through 50 states.

### Projective Geometry and Perspective (pp. 282-286)
- Parallel train tracks appear to meet at a point. This is perspective — Florentine Renaissance painters (Filippo Brunelleschi) first understood it mathematically.
- Alhazen (Abu 'Ali al-Hasan ibn al-Haytham, 11th century): vision works by reflected light rays, not emanations from the eye. His *Kitab al-Manazir* translated to Latin, influenced European artists.
- A point on the canvas represents a LINE through your eye in 3D space. The two parallel rails define two non-parallel PLANES through your eye. These planes intersect in a horizontal line, which meets the canvas at the vanishing point V. Every set of parallel lines has its own vanishing point.
- Vanishing points = "points at infinity." Add them to Euclid's plane → the PROJECTIVE PLANE: every pair of lines meets exactly once (parallel lines meet at their point at infinity).

### The Fano Plane and Error-Correcting Codes (pp. 286-296)
- The projective plane over a 2-element field (F₂ = {0, 1}) has exactly 7 points and 7 lines, each line containing 3 points, each point on 3 lines. This is the Fano plane — the smallest projective plane.
- Connection to Cash WinFall: Harvey's 7 hand-picked tickets (124, 135, 167, 257, 347, 236, 456) ARE the 7 lines of the Fano plane! That's why his strategy guarantees either jackpot or exactly 3 deuces — every pair of points lies on exactly one line, so every pair of winning numbers appears on exactly one ticket.
- This connects to coding theory. Richard Hamming (Bell Labs, 1950): developed error-correcting codes. Problem: early computers stored data on punched cards, and card readers made errors. How to detect and correct single-bit errors?
- Hamming's insight: add redundancy. To transmit a 4-bit message, add 3 check bits, making a 7-bit codeword. The 16 valid codewords (out of 128 possible 7-bit strings) are chosen so that any two differ in at least 3 positions. If one bit gets flipped, the received word is closer to exactly one valid codeword — the correct message.
- The structure underlying Hamming's code IS the Fano plane. The 7 positions correspond to the 7 points, the check equations correspond to the 7 lines. "The geometry of Renaissance painting, the ## of lottery strategy, and the engineering of information transmission are all the same."
- Marcel Golay independently discovered similar codes. Golay worked at a Signal Corps lab, saw Hamming's paper, and within a single weekend worked out a more powerful code based on a larger combinatorial structure.
- These codes are everywhere: CDs, DVDs, hard drives, deep-space communications. The Voyager probes used Golay codes to transmit images of Jupiter and Saturn back to Earth.

### Information Theory and Claude Shannon (pp. 296-300)
- Claude Shannon (MIT/Bell Labs): founded information theory in 1948. Key insight: information can be measured in bits. A channel has a capacity — maximum rate of reliable transmission. Shannon proved that as long as you transmit below capacity, codes exist that make errors arbitrarily rare. Above capacity, errors are unavoidable.
- Shannon was also a legendary tinkerer — built a flame-throwing trumpet, a device that could solve Rubik's cube, and a machine whose sole purpose was to turn itself off when you turned it on (the "Ultimate Machine," anticipated by Marvin Minsky).
- Connection to expected value: the number of errors in a block of transmitted data is like a random variable. Coding theory uses the structure of projective geometry to control the variance of that random variable, ensuring reliable communication.

### Combinatorial Designs and the Real World (pp. 300-306)
- The Fano plane is the simplest example of a "combinatorial design" — a branch of math where geometry, combinatorics, and algebra meet.
- Fano plane's beauty: it satisfies the axioms of projective geometry just as well as the infinite Euclidean plane extended with points at infinity. "Geometry doesn't care how many points are in your space."
- Back to Cash WinFall: Harvey's team didn't literally use the Fano plane (that's for a 7-ball lottery). For the actual Cash WinFall (46 balls, pick 6), the optimal ticket-selection problem is much harder. But the same mathematical principles apply — finding arrangements of tickets that cover all possible partial matches as evenly as possible.
- The math behind low-variance lottery strategies, error-correcting codes, and Renaissance perspective is fundamentally the same: projective geometry and combinatorial designs. "Where the train tracks meet" = where these seemingly unrelated fields converge.

## CHAPTER 14: The Triumph of Mediocrity

### Francis Galton and Regression to the Mean (pp. 307-316)
- Francis Galton: Darwin's half-cousin, Victorian polymath. Obsessed with measuring everything, especially heredity. Coined "eugenics" (a stain on his legacy) and "regression to the mean."
- Galton's sweetpea experiment: selected seeds by weight, planted heavy and light ones. Offspring of heavy seeds were heavy but NOT as heavy as their parents. Offspring of light seeds were light but not as light. The distribution "regressed" toward the average.
- Same with human height: tall parents have tall children, but not AS tall. Short parents have taller-than-them children. Galton plotted this in the world's first scatterplot.
- KEY INSIGHT: This is NOT about genetics pulling things toward mediocrity. It's a mathematical phenomenon that occurs whenever two variables are correlated but not perfectly so. If the correlation between parent and child height is, say, 0.5, then a parent who is 4 inches above average will have a child who is on average only 2 inches above average.
- "Regression to the mean" is baked into the structure of correlation itself. It's not a causal force — it's an arithmetic necessity.
- Galton's scatterplot: x-axis = parent height, y-axis = child height. The points cluster around a line, but the line's slope is less than 1 (less than 45°). That flattened slope IS regression to the mean.
- Horace Secrist's 1933 book *The Triumph of Mediocrity in Business*: tracked top-performing businesses over time and found they became more average. He interpreted this as a law of nature ("competition drives firms toward mediocrity"). But it's just regression to the mean — businesses that had an unusually good year are likely to have a less unusual next year, statistically speaking.
- Harold Hotelling's devastating review in JASA: "The book... would be of interest mainly as an illustration of the fallacy of letting statistical methods run away with you."

### "On Pace" and Baseball Regression (pp. 316-322)
- False linearity in baseball: every April, ESPN reports which players are "on pace" for record-shattering seasons. Matt Kemp hit 9 home runs in 17 games — a linear equation H = G × (9/17) projects 86 home runs over 162 games. But not all curves are lines.
- Kemp won't hit 86 because of regression to the mean. The league leader in home runs at any point is a genuinely good home run hitter who has ALSO been lucky. The luck component won't persist, so you expect the pace to drop.
- Derek Jeter, asked about being on pace to break Pete Rose's hit record, told the NYT: "One of the worst phrases in sports is 'on pace for.'" Wise words.
- Ellenberg looked at first-half American League HR leaders from 1976-2000 (19 seasons). Only three (Jim Rice 1978, Ben Oglivie 1980, Mark McGwire 1997) hit as many as 85% of their first-half total after the break. Mickey Tettleton led the AL with 24 homers at the 1993 break and managed only 8 the rest of the way. On average, sluggers hit only 60% as many HRs in second half as first half.
- This isn't fatigue or August heat — if it were, you'd see a league-wide decline. It's simple regression to the mean.
- The "Home Run Derby Curse": batters complain the All-Star break derby throws off their timing. The WSJ ran breathless stories in 2009 and 2011. But there's no curse — derby participants are there because they had an unusually good first half. Regression demands their later production won't keep up.
- Kemp injured his hamstring in May, finished 2012 with 23 home runs, not 86.
- "There's something the mind resists about regression to the mean. We want to believe in a force that brings down the mighty."

### Secrist Meets His Match (pp. 322-326)
- Harold Hotelling's background: Minnesotan, son of a hay dealer, went to college for journalism but discovered math. PhD in algebraic topology at Princeton. Led the wartime Statistical Research Group in New York (same place Wald worked on armor placement). Young professor at Columbia by 1933. Memorized the Monopoly board and Chance/Community Chest card frequencies — played it as "a simple exercise in random number generation and mental bookkeeping."
- Hotelling's review of Secrist is polite but firm — "more in sorrow than in anger." He acknowledged the enormous labor of data collection ("must have been gigantic") before dropping the hammer.
- Key insight of Hotelling's critique: regression to mediocrity is "more or less automatic whenever we study a variable that's affected by both stable factors and the influence of chance." Secrist's hundreds of tables "prove nothing more than that the ratios in question have a tendency to wander about."
- Hotelling's decisive observation: if regression to mediocrity were caused by competitive forces (as Secrist claimed), those forces would have to work BACKWARD in time as well as forward. If you select stores with highest performance in 1922 and look BACKWARD to 1916, they would also be closer to mediocre — because in 1922 they were both lucky AND good, but their 1916 luck was different. This kills the competitive-forces explanation.
- Secrist didn't take the hint. He wrote a contentious rebuttal in the next issue of JASA, insisting regression to mediocrity was particular to "data affected by competitive pressure and managerial control." Hotelling stopped being nice: "The thesis of the book, when correctly interpreted, is essentially trivial... To 'prove' such a mathematical result by a costly and prolonged numerical study of many kinds of business profit and expense ratios is analogous to proving the multiplication table by arranging elephants in rows and columns, and then doing the same for numerous other kinds of animals."

### The Triumph of Mediocrity in Oral-Anal Transit Time (pp. 326-328)
- Even Galton himself took ~20 years to fully grasp regression to the mean. Biometrician W.F.R. Weldon (1905 lecture) noted biologists constantly misunderstood it as "a peculiar property of living things" rather than a mathematical necessity.
- Weldon's key point: regression works both directions. There's regression of fathers on children too — the fathers of abnormal children are on the whole less abnormal than their children. If you attribute regression to some vital property of heredity, you'd have to say children reduce the abnormality of their parents, which is absurd. So it must be mathematical, not biological.
- "Biologists are eager to think regression stems from biology, management theorists like Secrist want it to come from competition, literary critics ascribe it to creative exhaustion — but it is none of these. It is mathematics."
- 1976 British Medical Journal paper on bran and diverticular disease: recorded patients' oral-anal transit times before and after bran treatment. Found bran had a "remarkable regularizing effect" — fast transit times slowed toward 48 hours, slow ones sped toward 48 hours. But this is exactly what you'd expect if bran had NO effect — regression to the mean. Everyone has fast and slow days; an unusually quick Monday is likely followed by a more average Tuesday.
- Why good diet studies compare two candidate diets rather than just studying one — regression to the mean affects both groups equally, so the comparison is fair.
- Second albums by breakout bands, sophomore novels: artistic success is an amalgam of talent and fortune, so it's subject to regression. Running backs who sign big contracts after a great year tend to record fewer yards per carry the next season — they signed BECAUSE of an unusually good year.

### Scared Straight (pp. 328-330)
- Scared Straight: program where juvenile offenders toured prisons and inmates warned them about life inside. Original at New Jersey's Rahway State Prison, featured in Oscar-winning 1978 documentary. Spread across the US and to Norway.
- One New Orleans program reported participants were arrested less than half as often after the program. But this is Secrist's fallacy again: the juvenile offenders were selected for being the WORST behaved. Regression to the mean predicts their behavior will improve even without intervention. The very worst-behaved kids this year will likely still be problems next year, but not as much so.
- When Scared Straight was subjected to randomized trials (randomly selected subgroup goes through program vs. control group), researchers found the program actually INCREASED antisocial behavior. "Maybe it should have been called Scared Stupid."

## CHAPTER 15: Galton's Ellipse

### The Invention of the Scatterplot (pp. 331-336)
- Galton needed to visualize how strong heredity's influence was compared to chance. He plotted father-son height data on a grid: x-axis = father's height, y-axis = son's height. Each father-son pair becomes a point. This was the world's first scatterplot.
- The scatterplot descends from Descartes's analytic geometry — thinking of points in the plane as pairs of numbers (x-coordinate, y-coordinate), joining algebra and geometry.
- Scatterplots play to our cognitive strengths: brains are bad at columns of numbers but great at spotting patterns in 2D visual fields.
- Three extreme cases illustrated: (1) Perfect heredity, no chance: all points on the diagonal line x = y. (2) No heredity at all: a round blob, son's height independent of father's. The conditional expectation of son's height given father's height equals the unconditional expectation — full regression to the mean. (3) Real data: intermediate — a tilted elliptical cloud. Sons of 6'1" fathers are on average taller than average but not as tall as dad — you can SEE regression to the mean in the picture.
- Golden age of data visualization: Charles Minard's 1869 chart of Napoleon's army retreat from Russia ("greatest data graphic ever made"), descended from Florence Nightingale's coxcomb graph showing most British soldiers in Crimea died from infections, not Russians.

### Isopleths and the Ellipse (pp. 336-340)
- Galton drew curves on his scatterplot connecting points of equal density — isopleths. Analogous to isotherms (equal temperature), isobars (equal pressure), isonephs (equal cloud cover), isohypses (elevation contour lines) on maps.
- First published isoplethic map: 1701 by Edmond Halley — isogons showing where magnetic north and true north diverged, based on measurements from the Paramore with Halley at the helm.
- Galton's discovery: his isopleths were all ELLIPSES, nested within each other, same center (the point where both parent and child had exactly average height). The scatterplot was the contour map of an elliptical mountain — the 3D version of de Moivre's bell curve. In modern language: the bivariate normal distribution.
- The eccentricity (skinniness) of the ellipse measures how much heredity determines the son's height. Very skinny ellipse = high correlation, heredity dominant. Round ellipse = low correlation, chance dominant. A line is the most eccentric ellipse possible (perfect correlation). A circle means zero correlation.
- Galton called this measure CORRELATION — the term we still use. Using eccentricity — a geometric quantity dating to Apollonius of Perga in the 3rd century BCE — Galton had found a way to measure the association between two variables, solving a cutting-edge problem in biology.

### Correlation in Practice (pp. 340-342)
- What if the scatterplot doesn't look like an ellipse? Pragmatic answer: real-life data often DOES array itself in rough ellipses. Not always, but often enough.
- Examples shown: Kerry 2004 vs. Obama 2008 vote share by House district — very skinny ellipse above the diagonal (Obama did better than Kerry). Daily stock price changes for Google vs. GE — rounder ellipse, weaker correlation. SAT scores vs. tuition for North Carolina colleges — positive correlation. 50 US states by average income vs. Bush 2004 vote share — negative correlation (wealthy states vote less Republican).

### The Unreasonable Effectiveness of Classical Geometry (pp. 342-344)
- Why do ellipses keep appearing? For Apollonius and Greek geometers, ellipses were conic sections — surfaces from slicing a cone along a plane. Kepler showed planets travel in ellipses. Now ellipses enclose Galton's heredity data. Why?
- Fundamental property of mathematics: there are many complicated objects but only a few simple ones. If a problem has a solution with a simple mathematical description, there are only a few possibilities. The simplest entities get forced into multiple duty across all scientific problems.
- Simplest curves = lines (everywhere in nature: crystal edges, paths of moving bodies). Next simplest = quadratic equations: Ax² + Bxy + Cy² + Dx + Ey + F = 0. These curves are called conic sections (or quadrics). Despite infinitely many choices of constants, all quadrics fall into just three classes: ellipses, parabolas, and hyperbolas.
- These three curves appear as solutions to problem after problem: orbits of planets, designs of curved mirrors, arcs of projectiles, shapes of rainbows.
- Fun connection: Michael Harris (number theorist, Institut de Mathématiques de Jussieu) has a theory that three Pynchon novels are governed by the three conics: *Gravity's Rainbow* = parabolas, *Mason & Dixon* = ellipses, *Against the Day* = hyperbolas.

### Galton Confirms the Ellipse (pp. 344-345)
- Galton noticed his hand-drawn isopleths looked like ellipses but wasn't sure — was he letting his desire for elegance bias his perception? So he sought the advice of J.D. Hamilton Dickson, a Cambridge mathematician. He concealed the origin of the data, presenting it as a physics problem, to avoid prejudicing Dickson.
- Dickson confirmed: not only was the ellipse what the data suggested, it was what theory demanded.
- Galton's reaction: "I certainly never felt such a glow of loyalty and respect towards the sovereignty and wide sway of mathematical analysis as when his answer arrived, confirming, by purely mathematical reasoning, my various and laborious statistical conclusions with far more minuteness than I had dared to hope."

### Bertillonage (pp. 345-349)
- Galton understood correlation applied to ANY pair of related qualities, not just heredity. He had a massive database of anatomical measurements thanks to Alphonse Bertillon.
- Bertillon: French criminologist who devised a system of identifying criminals by numerical measurements — head length/breadth, finger length, foot length, etc. Each arrested suspect was measured, data filed on cards. If caught again, police could match measurements to the card. "You can replace your name by an alias, but there's no alias for the shape of your head."
- Bertillonage adopted by Paris Prefecture of Police in 1883, spread worldwide (Bucharest to Buenos Aires). Justice Anthony Kennedy invoked it in 2013's *Maryland vs. King*, comparing DNA databases to Bertillon cards.
- Galton's question: Was Bertillon's choice of measurements optimal? Could you do better with more? Problem: bodily measurements aren't independent. Men with big hands tend to have big feet. Adding correlated measurements provides diminishing returns. The best measurements to take are those UNCORRELATED with each other.
- Galton plotted height vs. cubit (elbow to middle fingertip) and found the same elliptical pattern — the two variables were correlated, not identical.
- Galton championed fingerprinting (dactyloscopy) over Bertillonage. Advantage: fingerprints could be found at crime scenes. Vincenzo Peruggia stole the *Mona Lisa* from the Louvre in 1911 — he had a Bertillon card on file but it was useless since he wasn't there to measure. Had the card contained fingerprints, the one he left on the discarded frame would have identified him immediately.

### Aside: Correlation, Information, Compression, Beethoven (pp. 349-352)
- Bertillon actually recorded measurements as categories (small/medium/large), not exact numbers. Five measurements × 3 categories = 3^5 = 243 groups. Add eye and hair color (7 options) = 3^5 × 7 = 1701 categories. Enough to narrow a search dramatically.
- Parsons code: a similar compression system for musical melodies. For each note, mark whether it goes up (u), down (d), or repeats (r) from the previous note. Beethoven's "Ode to Joy" opening = *ruurdddd. With just 17 notes, there are 3^16 = 43,046,721 possible codes — more than all melodies ever recorded. Exponential growth makes short codes incredibly powerful for discrimination.
- BUT: if measurements are perfectly correlated (e.g., finger and cubit always in the same category), you lose information. What looks like 9 choices (3×3) is really only 3 (small/small, medium/medium, large/large). The effective number of Bertillon categories drops from 7 × 3^5 = 1701 to 7 × 3^4 = 567.
- Galton's insight: even partial correlation reduces information. This is Shannon's information theory in embryonic form — correlation between variables reduces the informativeness of a measurement system.
- Modern application: digital compression. A 4-megapixel photo is 4 million numbers, but adjacent pixels are highly correlated (bright green next to bright green). The actual information is much less than 4 million numbers' worth. This is what makes compression (JPEG, MP3, etc.) possible. Developed via wavelets (Morlet, Mallat, Meyer, Daubechies) and compressed sensing (Candès, Romberg, Terry Tao).

### The Triumph of Mediocrity in Weather (pp. 352-355)
- Secrist's counterargument: when he tracked temperatures of US cities, the hottest ones in 1922 were still hottest in 1931. No regression! So regression must be specific to human enterprise, not universal.
- But Secrist was wrong — temperatures DO regress to the mean. It depends on how you choose your cities.
- 13 weather stations in southern Wisconsin (all within an hour's drive): the warmest in Jan 2011 (Charmany, Madison airport, Stoughton) regressed toward the middle by Jan 2012. The coldest (Cottage Grove, Lodi, Portage) also moved toward the middle. Classic regression.
- 10 California cities spread widely apart: no regression. The cold places (Truckee in the Sierra Nevada) stay cold, the hot places (San Diego, LA) stay hot. The scatterplot is a very skinny ellipse — nearly perfect correlation (Pearson r = 0.989).
- KEY INSIGHT: The difference is signal vs. noise. California cities have huge inherent temperature differences (signal) that swamp year-to-year fluctuations (noise) — skinny ellipse, little regression. Wisconsin stations are climatically similar (little signal), so year-to-year chance dominates (lots of noise) — rounder ellipse, more regression.
- What Secrist's weather data really shows: businesses are more like Wisconsin cities than California cities. Superior management plays a role, but plain luck plays roughly equal measure. That's the real lesson of his data.

### Eugenics, Original Sin, and This Book's Misleading Title (pp. 355-356)
- Galton coined "eugenics" and was its most prominent advocate. He believed in preventing "the free propagation of the stock of those who are seriously afflicted by lunacy, feeble-mindedness, habitual criminality, and pauperism."
- Ellenberg's reflection: "Mathematics is a way not to be wrong, but it isn't a way not to be wrong about everything. (Sorry, no refunds!) Wrongness is like original sin; we are born to it and it remains always with us, and constant vigilance is necessary if we mean to restrict its sphere of influence over our actions."
- Danger: mathematical skill can breed overconfidence that extends to areas where you're still wrong. "We become like those pious people who, over time, accumulate a sense of their own virtuousness so powerful as to make them believe the bad things they do are virtuous too."

### The Adventures of Karl Pearson Across the Tenth Dimension (pp. 356-362)
- Galton's invention of correlation was transformative — not just for statistics but for all of science. "Correlation does not imply causation" is the one thing everyone knows. But the deeper insight: you could talk about association between variables while remaining completely agnostic about any causal relationship. Like Darwin showing you could discuss progress without invoking purpose, Galton showed you could discuss association without invoking cause.
- Karl Pearson generalized Galton's correlation (which applied only to bell-curve-distributed variables) to any variables whatsoever.
- Pearson's formula has a beautiful geometric interpretation. A column of numbers is also a VECTOR — a point in high-dimensional space. The 10 California temperatures are a point in 10-dimensional space. The "dirty little secret of advanced geometry": we can do geometry in 10 or a million dimensions, but we picture it in 2 or 3 dimensions. This impoverished vision is usually enough.
- High-dimensional geometry matters because data is inherently high-dimensional. A 4-megapixel photo is a point in 4-million-dimensional space. A video is a curve in that space.
- Correlation between two variables, geometrically, is the ANGLE between two vectors. Specifically, it's the cosine of the angle. Cosine = 1 when angle = 0 (same direction, perfect positive correlation). Cosine = -1 when angle = 180° (opposite directions, perfect negative correlation). Cosine = 0 when angle = 90° (perpendicular/orthogonal, zero correlation).
- "Orthogonal" means perpendicular/unrelated. It's creeping from math into wider usage. Hilarious Supreme Court exchange where Justice Scalia encounters the word "orthogonal" for the first time during oral argument and is delighted by it.
- "I'm rooting for orthogonal to catch on. It's been a while since a mathy word really broke out into demotic English."
- Hipparchus wrote the first trigonometric tables in the 2nd century BCE to compute eclipses — his vectors were solidly 3D. But a tool that works for one purpose tends to make itself useful again and again.

### The Wealthy Liberal Elitist Paradox (pp. 356-359)
- David Brooks's "Bobos" (Bohemian Bourgeoisie): the idea that rich people now vote Democratic, breaking the old GOP=money/Dem=working man pattern. He compared affluent Montgomery County, Maryland (63-34 Democratic) with middle-class Franklin County, Pennsylvania (67-30 Republican).
- The scatterplot of states by income vs. Bush vote share confirms: rich STATES tend to vote Democratic. But Andrew Gelman found the story is more complicated. Within most states, rich PEOPLE still vote more Republican. In Texas and Wisconsin, richer counties vote more Republican. In Maryland, California, and New York, richer counties vote more Democratic — and those happen to be where pundits live.
- The apparent paradox: being rich is correlated with being from a rich state, and rich states vote Democratic. Doesn't that mean rich people vote Democratic? NO — because correlation is not transitive.
- Geometric proof: if vector 1 is at an acute angle to vector 2, and vector 2 is at an acute angle to vector 3, vector 1 does NOT have to be at an acute angle to vector 3. Three arrows can point in such a way that each pair shares a direction but the first and third oppose.
- Analogy: correlation is like "blood relation" — I'm related to my son, and my son is related to my wife, but my wife and I aren't blood relatives. Correlated variables "share part of their DNA."

### Non-Transitivity of Correlation: Niacin and HDL (pp. 359-361)
- Mutual fund example: Laura (50% Facebook, 50% Google), Tim (50% GM, 50% Honda), Sara (50% Honda, 50% Facebook). Laura correlates with Sara (shared Facebook), Sara correlates with Tim (shared Honda), but Laura and Tim have no reason to correlate. They're like parents contributing "genetic material" to Sara's hybrid fund.
- HDL cholesterol: high HDL is correlated with lower heart attack risk. Niacin increases HDL. So niacin should prevent heart attacks? No — correlation isn't transitive. A large National Heart, Lung, and Blood Institute trial (halted in 2011) found niacin raised HDL but didn't reduce heart attacks or strokes.
- The issue is HOW you boost HDL matters. If the drug works by affecting the same underlying factor that connects HDL and heart health, it helps. If it boosts HDL by some other mechanism, no benefit. This is the "surrogate endpoint problem" — using an easy-to-measure biomarker as a stand-in for the actual health outcome.
- Same story with hormone replacement therapy: estrogen correlated with lower heart disease risk, HRT raises estrogen, but the Women's Health Initiative found HRT actually INCREASED heart disease risk.
- "In the real world, it's next to impossible to predict what effect a drug will have on a disease, even if you know a lot about how it affects biomarkers." Drug development requires "a resilient psyche, not to mention a vast pool of capital."

### Uncorrelated Doesn't Mean Unrelated (pp. 361-363)
- Galton's correlation detects only LINEAR relations (increase in one variable → proportional increase/decrease in other). But not all relationships are linear.
- Political survey scatterplot (Public Policy Polling, Dec 2011): 1000 voters, x-axis = left-right political orientation, y-axis = "informedness." The plot is heart-shaped — two lobes (left and right) with a point at the bottom. More informed voters don't shift left or right, they get more POLARIZED. Less-informed voters cluster in the center.
- The two axes are uncorrelated (no linear relationship), but clearly related (the heart shape). Correlation can't see this any more than a camera can detect gamma rays.
- "Undecided voters, by and large, aren't undecided because they're carefully weighing the merits of each candidate, unprejudiced by political dogma. They're undecided because they're barely paying attention."
- KEY: when told two phenomena are "uncorrelated," it doesn't mean there's no relationship — only that there's no relationship of the sort correlation is designed to detect.

## CHAPTER 16: Does Lung Cancer Make You Smoke Cigarettes?

### Correlation and Causation (pp. 365-368)
- Binary variables (yes/no): correlation between two binary variables has a simple meaning. Saying marital status and smoking are negatively correlated = married people are less likely to smoke = smokers are less likely to be married. These are mathematically the same statement (multiply both sides of the inequality by the common denominator).
- Key subtlety: "If you're a smoker, you're less likely to be married" (indicative mood — states a correlation, a fact about the world) vs. "If you were a smoker, you'd be less likely to be married" (subjunctive mood — suggests causation, a counterfactual). The first is a statement about what IS, the second about what WOULD BE if we changed something.
- Everything is correlated with everything else (the null hypothesis of zero correlation is almost always false with enough data). So we only report correlations that are "strong enough" — i.e., statistically significant.
- The mathematical account of correlation has been fixed since Galton and Pearson. Putting causation on firm mathematical footing has been much more elusive.

### The Surrogate Endpoint Problem (pp. 368-369)
- HDL and heart attacks: maybe HDL directly scrubs lipidic cruft off arteries (in which case any HDL-raising drug would help), or maybe some unmeasured third factor raises both HDL and heart health (in which case an HDL drug might do nothing for the heart). Clinical researchers call this the "surrogate endpoint problem."

### Smoking and Lung Cancer: The Great Debate (pp. 369-374)
- At the turn of the 20th century, lung cancer was extremely rare. By 1947, it accounted for nearly a fifth of cancer deaths among British men — 15x increase in a few decades. Initially attributed to better diagnosis, but the increase was too large for that.
- Possible causes: factory smoke, car exhaust, unknown pollutants, or cigarettes (whose popularity had exploded during the same period).
- Doll and Hill (1950): landmark study of 649 male lung cancer patients in 20 London hospitals — only TWO were nonsmokers. In a control group of 649 men hospitalized for other complaints, 27 were nonsmokers. Heavier smokers had even higher risk: 168 of the cancer patients smoked 25+ cigarettes daily vs. only 84 of controls.
- But Doll and Hill's data showed correlation, not causation. As they wrote: "The association would occur if carcinoma of the lung caused people to smoke or if both attributes were end-effects of a common cause." Cancer causing smoking seems absurd (tumors can't go back in time), but the common cause problem is real.
- R.A. Fisher, founding hero of modern statistics, was a vigorous skeptic. He succeeded Pearson as Galton Chair of Eugenics at UCL (now called Galton Chair of Genetics). Fisher proposed the "constitutional hypothesis": maybe a genetic factor predisposed people both to lung cancer AND to smoking. The pre-cancerous condition involves chronic inflammation, which might drive people to seek the comfort of cigarettes. "To take the poor chap's cigarettes away from him would be rather like taking away his white stick from a blind man."
- Fisher was both a rigorous statistician demanding all possibilities be considered AND a lifelong smoker with affection for his habit. Some have noted his work as a consultant to the Tobacco Manufacturer's Standing Committee.
- Twin studies: Fisher proposed comparing identical vs. fraternal twins. If identical twins "match" on smoking more than fraternal twins (who share the same upbringing), it's evidence for heritable smoking behavior. Small-scale results supported this; more recent work confirms smoking has heritable components. BUT that doesn't mean those same genes cause lung cancer.
- Epidemiologist Jan Vandenbroucke on Fisher's tobacco papers: "To my surprise, I found extremely well-written and cogent papers that might have become textbook classics for their impeccable logic and clear exposition of data and argument if only the authors had been on the right side."
- By the 1950s, consensus slowly built. Surgeon General Leroy Burney (1959) declared smoking the "principal factor" in lung cancer. JAMA editor John Talbott fired back with a ghostwritten editorial (actually penned by rival PHS researchers). Historian Jon Harkness showed this public tussle was really an internal scientific in-fight projected onto a public screen.
- 1964: Surgeon General Luther Terry's blue-ribbon commission concluded smoking was not just correlated with but CAUSED lung cancer. The evidence: consistent association across many studies, dose-response relationship (heavier smokers = more cancer), cancer appearing at point of contact (cigarette → lung cancer, pipe → lip cancer), ex-smokers having less cancer than continuing smokers.

### It's Not Always Wrong to Be Wrong (pp. 374-376)
- Thought experiment: what if Fisher had been right? The surgeon general would have had to retract, tobacco companies would have lost money, smokers would have been unnecessarily deprived. All because a "strongly supported hypothesis" was declared a fact.
- But what's the alternative? Jerry Cornfield called the randomized experiment that would definitively prove causation "possible to conceive but impossible to conduct" (force random teenagers to smoke for 50 years). So we must act on correlational evidence.
- Policy makers don't have the luxury of scientific uncertainty. When the system works — as with tobacco — scientists and policy makers complement each other: scientists specify how uncertain to be, policy makers decide how to act given that uncertainty.
- Eggplant analogy: suppose studies show eggplant is correlated with heart failure (75% confidence in causation). Expected value of recommending against eggplant: 75% × 1000 lives saved + 25% × (-200 lives lost from worse diet) = 700 lives saved per year. Positive expected value, so make the recommendation despite uncertainty.
- "Refraining from making recommendations at all, on the grounds that they might be wrong, is a losing strategy. It's a lot like George Stigler's advice about missing planes. If you never give advice until you're sure it's right, you're not giving enough advice."

### Berkson's Fallacy, or: Why Are Handsome Men Such Jerks? (pp. 376-380)
- Correlations can arise from unseen common causes, but also from common EFFECTS — Berkson's fallacy, named after Joseph Berkson (the medical statistician from Chapter 8).
- Berkson was a vigorous skeptic of the tobacco-cancer link. An MD, he represented the old school of epidemiology: "Cancer is a biologic, not a statistical problem." He found it implausible that tobacco could be correlated with DOZENS of diseases simultaneously: "It is as though, in investigating a drug that previously had been indicated to relieve the common cold, the drug was found not only to ameliorate coryza, but to cure pneumonia, cancer, and many other diseases."
- Berkson's "constitutional hypothesis": the 85-95% of the population who smoked in midcentury were normal; the small minority of nonsmokers were "a hardy lot" who could resist social pressure and advertising, and such hardy types would naturally have lower death rates from everything.
- Berkson's key insight (1938): selecting patients in hospitals creates spurious associations. Example: suppose in a town of 1000 people, 30% have high blood pressure, 40% have diabetes, and the two conditions are completely independent (so 30% of diabetics = 120 people have both).
- In the general population, high blood pressure is no more common among diabetics (30% regardless). But the HOSPITAL population consists only of sick people: 180 with high BP only, 280 with diabetes only, 120 with both = 580 patients. Among the 400 hospital patients with diabetes, 120 have high BP = 30%. Among the 180 with high BP but no diabetes... wait — among hospital diabetics, the rate of high BP is 120/400 = 30%, same as general population. But among hospital NON-diabetics, it's 180/180 = 100%. The hospital sample creates a spurious negative correlation between the two conditions.

### Berkson's Fallacy continued: Handsome Men and Jerks (pp. 376-380)
- Berkson's hospital example in full: In a town of 1000, 30% have high blood pressure, 40% have diabetes, the two are independent. In the general population, high BP is equally common among diabetics and non-diabetics (30%). But in the HOSPITAL (where everyone is sick), the non-diabetics are there BECAUSE of their blood pressure, so 100% have high BP. The hospital creates a spurious negative correlation between high BP and diabetes — a statistical phantom caused by a common EFFECT (hospitalization).
- More realistic version: having two conditions makes you MORE likely to be hospitalized. So hospital population might be: 10 severed fingers, 18 high BP only, 28 diabetes only, 120 both. Among hospital diabetics, 120/148 = 81% have high BP. Among non-diabetics, 18/28 = 64%. Now it looks like high BP CAUSES diabetes — equally an illusion.
- Why handsome men seem like jerks: the "Great Square of Men" has niceness on one axis and handsomeness on the other. Assume these are independent (distributed equally across the square). But niceness and handsomeness have a common EFFECT — they put men in the "Acceptable Triangle" of men you notice. Mean ugly men are invisible. So within the Acceptable Triangle, the handsomest men run the full gamut of personality but the nicest men are only averagely handsome (the ugly nice guys barely squeezed in). The negative correlation between looks and personality is REAL within your dating pool but doesn't exist in the general population.
- Literary snobbery works the same way: popular novels seem terrible not because masses have bad taste, but because the only novels you hear about are either popular OR good. Force yourself to read random unpopular novels and you'll find they're just as bad as the popular ones.
- "The Great Square is too simple by far, of course. There are many dimensions, not just two... The Great Square is better described as a kind of Great Hypercube."

## CHAPTER 17: There Is No Such Thing as Public Opinion

### The Budget Paradox (pp. 383-386)
- CBS News poll (Jan 2011): 77% of Americans said cutting spending was the best way to handle the deficit, only 9% preferred raising taxes. Americans consistently say they want small government and spending cuts.
- But WHICH programs to cut? Pew Research poll (Feb 2011): asked about 13 categories of government spending, in 11 of them more people wanted to INCREASE spending than decrease it. Only foreign aid and unemployment insurance (under 5% of spending combined) got the ax. The average American is "eager to slash foreign aid, occasionally tolerant of cuts to welfare or defense, and pretty gung ho for increased spending on every single other program our taxes fund."
- Same at state level: overwhelmingly favor cutting programs AND raising taxes to balance budgets. But not a single specific cut (education, health care, transportation, pensions) or specific tax increase (sales, income, business) drew majority support.
- Bryan Caplan: "The most plausible reading of this data is that the public wants a free lunch." Krugman: "People want spending cut, but are opposed to cuts in anything except foreign aid." Harris poll: "Many people seem to want to cut down the forest but to keep the trees."

### Rational People, Irrational Countries (pp. 386-389)
- Word problem: 1/3 of voters want to raise taxes, 1/3 want to cut defense, 1/3 want to cut Medicare. "Should we cut spending?" — 2/3 say yes (67-33 margin). "Should we cut defense?" — 2/3 say no (tax raisers + Medicare cutters). "Should we cut Medicare?" — 2/3 say no (tax raisers + defense cutters). We want to cut but we don't want to cut anything!
- KEY INSIGHT: Each individual voter has a perfectly rational, coherent political stance. But in the aggregate, their position is nonsensical. The "infantile average American" who wants cuts but demands every program be preserved doesn't actually exist. The real average American thinks there are plenty of non-worthwhile programs to cut — the problem is there's no consensus on WHICH ones.
- ACA polling example: 52% opposed Obamacare in Oct 2010. But breakdown: 37% wanted outright repeal, 10% wanted it weakened, 15% wanted to keep it, 36% wanted to EXPAND it. Three options: leave it, kill it, make it stronger — each opposed by a majority! Fox News: "Majority opposes Obamacare!" MSNBC: "Majority wants to preserve or strengthen Obamacare!" Both true. Both incomplete.

### Villains Often Deserve Whipping (pp. 389-393)
- *Atkins v. Virginia* (2002): Daryl Atkins, IQ 59, convicted of capital murder. Virginia Supreme Court cited *Penry v. Lynaugh* (1989) ruling that executing the mentally retarded was constitutional. US Supreme Court reversed in a 6-3 decision.
- The Eighth Amendment bans "cruel and unusual punishment" — but what counts? "Evolving standards of decency" (*Trop v. Dulles*, 1958) means contemporary norms, not 1789 standards. Samuel Livermore of New Hampshire foresaw this problem in 1789: if the clause is taken broadly, "villains often deserve whipping, and perhaps having their ears cut off; but are we in future to be prevented from inflicting these punishments because they are cruel?"
- Justice O'Connor in *Penry*: opinion polls don't count as evidence of standards of decency; only legislation does. In 1989, only 2 states banned execution of the mentally retarded. By 2002, 18 states had acted.
- Scalia's dissent: 18 of 38 death penalty states = 47% — how is less than half a "national consensus"? The majority's math: count the 12 states that ban ALL capital punishment + 18 that ban it for the mentally retarded = 30/50 = substantial majority.
- The Amar brothers' mathematical argument: imagine 47 states have outlawed capital punishment but 2 of the remaining 3 allow execution of the mentally retarded. The right denominator is 48/50 (states that oppose the practice in some form), not 1/3.
- Scalia's real concern: "incremental abolition" — that the Eighth Amendment becomes a one-way ratchet, permanently freezing any temporary consensus on leniency. Ellenberg's response: "The immense ingenuity of the human species in devising ways to punish people rivals our abilities in art, philosophy, and science. Punishment is a renewable resource; there is no danger we'll run out."

### Florida 2000, the Slime Mold, and How to Choose a Wingman (pp. 393-397)
- Slime mold *Physarum polycephalum*: single-celled organism that coalesces into a collective plasmodium. Can navigate mazes. Makes surprisingly good decisions: choose between 3g oats in dark vs. 5g oats in UV light — picks each about 50% of the time (equal "utility").
- But add a THIRD option — 1g oats in dark (clearly inferior to 3g dark). The slime mold should never pick it, and doesn't. But its presence changes the ratio: now the slime mold picks 3-dark more than THREE TIMES as often as 5-light!
- The small dark pile is playing the role of H. Ross Perot — an "irrelevant alternative" that changes the outcome between the two real contenders.
- "Independence of irrelevant alternatives": the principle that a third option shouldn't affect the choice between two existing options. If you prefer Prius to Hummer, the availability of a Ford Pinto shouldn't change that.
- Florida 2000: Bush 48.85%, Gore 48.84%, Nader 1.6%. Virtually every Nader voter preferred Gore to Bush. A solid 51% majority preferred Gore — but the presence of Nader, the irrelevant alternative, gave the election to Bush.
- "Votes produce paradoxical outcomes, in which majorities don't always get their way and irrelevant alternatives control the outcome."

### The Borda Count (pp. 397-399)
- Jean-Charles de Borda (18th-century French mathematician, ballistician) thought about elections as machines: voter preferences go in, popular will comes out. "If you're grinding meat, use the whole cow!" — take into account voters' full ranked preferences, not just their first choice.
- Borda count: with 3 candidates, give 2 points for first place, 1 for second, 0 for third. In the Florida scenario (49% Bush-Gore-Nader, 25% Gore-Nader-Bush, 24% Gore-Bush-Nader, 2% Nader-Gore-Bush):
  - Bush: 2×0.49 + 1×0.24 = 1.22
  - Gore: 2×0.49 + 1×0.51 = 1.49
  - Nader: 2×0.02 + 1×0.25 = 0.29
- Gore wins under Borda, which matches the fact that 51% prefer Gore to Bush, 98% prefer Gore to Nader, and 73% prefer Bush to Nader. All three pairwise majorities get their way.

### Borda Count continued and the Slime Mold (pp. 396-399)
- Even when Bush has a majority of first-place votes (modified scenario: 51% Bush-Gore-Nader, 23% Gore-Nader-Bush, 24% Gore-Bush-Nader, 2% Nader-Gore-Bush), Gore STILL wins the Borda count 1.47 to 1.26. Why? Because Nader's presence on the ballot pushes Bush to third on many ballots, costing him points — the same "Irrelevant Alternative" problem from the actual 2000 election.
- Back to the slime mold: Latty and Beekman speculate it uses something like the Borda count. 50% of nuclei care about food quantity (rank: 5-light > 3-dark > 1-dark), 50% care about darkness (rank: 3-dark = 1-dark > 5-light). Borda scores work out to: 3-dark first (1.25), 5-light second (1.0), 1-dark last (0.75). Without 1-dark, the two options tie — exactly matching experimental results.
- "Asymmetric domination effect": introducing an inferior but similar option boosts the one it resembles. Not just slime molds — jays, honeybees, hummingbirds, and humans all exhibit this.
- Human experiment (Sedikides, Ariely, Olsen): undergraduates choosing hypothetical dates. Adam (81st percentile attractiveness, 51st dependability, 65th intelligence) vs. Bill (61st attractiveness, 51st dependability, 87th intelligence) — 50-50 split. Add Chris (81st attractiveness, 51st dependability, 54th intelligence — a slightly dumber Adam), and 2/3 of women chose Adam. "If you're a single guy looking for love... choose the [wingman] who's pretty much exactly like you — only slightly less desirable."

### Using the Whole Cow, in Australia and Vermont (pp. 399-402)
- Instant-runoff voting (IRV) / preferential voting: rank all candidates. Eliminate the one with fewest first-place votes, redistribute their ballots to the next preferred candidate. Repeat until someone has a majority.
- Florida 2000 under IRV: Nader eliminated first, his votes go to Gore (mostly), Gore wins 51-49. Used in Australia, Ireland, Papua New Guinea. John Stuart Mill called it "among the very greatest improvements yet made in the theory and practice of government."
- Burlington, Vermont 2009 mayoral race: Kurt Wright (R), Andy Montroll (D), Bob Kiss (Progressive). First-place votes: Wright 3297, Kiss 2982, Montroll 2554. Under traditional system, Wright (Republican) wins — absurd in ultra-liberal Burlington (just vote-splitting between two liberals).
- Under IRV: Montroll eliminated, his votes redistribute → Kiss 4314, Wright 4064. Kiss wins. BUT: head-to-head, Montroll beats Kiss (4067-3477) AND Montroll beats Wright (4597-3668). The centrist everyone liked best was eliminated first. IRV's weakness: centrists who are everyone's second choice but nobody's first get tossed early.
- Even worse: if 795 voters had switched TO Kiss (from Wright), Kiss gets eliminated instead, and Montroll wins. "We gave Kiss more votes — and instead of winning, he lost!"
- Summary: Traditional method → Wright. IRV → Kiss. Head-to-head → Montroll. Three methods, three winners.

### The Rabid Sheep Wrestles with Paradox (pp. 402-407)
- Simplified Burlington with just three ballot types: Montroll-Kiss-Wright (1332), Kiss-Wright-Montroll (371), Wright-Montroll-Kiss (1513). A vote triangle: Kiss beats Wright (majority in K and W slices), Wright beats Montroll (majority in M and K slices), but Montroll beats Kiss by a resounding 2845 to 371. Every candidate would lose a one-on-one race to one of the other two.
- These are Condorcet paradoxes, discovered by the Marquis de Condorcet (Marie-Jean-Antoine-Nicolas de Caritat) in the late 18th century. "Le mouton enragé" (the rabid sheep) — shy, quiet, quick-tempered. President of the Legislative Assembly despite being an unlikely politician.
- Condorcet believed in "social mathematics": that the social and moral world could be analyzed by equations. He agreed with Rousseau that the "general will" should govern, but unlike Rousseau, demanded mathematical justification for majority rule.
- Condorcet's "jury theorem" (1785 *Essay on the Application of Analysis to the Probability of Majority Decisions*): if each juror has >50% chance of being correct, a large enough jury is very likely to reach the right verdict — like the Phillies winning a 7-game series when they have a 51% edge per game. More games = more certainty. "I must act not by what I think reasonable, but by what all who, like me, have abstracted from their own opinion must regard as conforming to reason and truth."
- Condorcet was Thomas Jefferson's kindred spirit (shared interest in standardizing measurements). John Adams dismissed him as a "quack" and "mathematical charlatan." Condorcet's mathematically inspired Girondin Constitution was never adopted, but his logical consistency led him to insist — almost alone — that the Rights of Man belonged to women too.
- Condorcet and Voltaire: young Condorcet visited the aging Voltaire at Ferney (1770). Voltaire adopted him as protégé. Friction arose when Condorcet praised Pascal — Voltaire found Pascal's religious mathematics "not only wrong but dangerous." Condorcet split the difference: Pascal's mathematical lens on human behavior + Voltaire's faith in reason and secularism.
- Condorcet's axiom: "If the majority of voters prefer candidate A to B, then B cannot be the people's choice." He admired Borda but found the Borda count unsatisfactory because it violates this axiom (a third alternative can flip the result). Condorcet wanted to build a theory of voting from this axiom, as Euclid built geometry from five axioms.
- The Condorcet paradox shows the axiom can lead to circular outcomes — Kiss beats Wright, Wright beats Montroll, Montroll beats Kiss. NO candidate satisfies the axiom. "There is no such thing as the people's choice. It just doesn't exist."

## CHAPTER 18: "Out of Nothing I Have Created a Strange New Universe"

### Truth vs. Procedure (pp. 413-415)
- Condorcet believed questions like "Who is the best leader?" had right answers, and citizens were imperfect measuring instruments — subject to inaccuracy but on average quite accurate. Democracy and majority rule were ways not to be wrong, via math.
- Modern view: democracy's appeal is that it's FAIR (rights-based), not necessarily that it produces correct answers. This is a fundamental tension in all intellectual endeavor: are we trying to find what's TRUE, or what our rules and procedures CERTIFY as correct?
- Criminal law: defendants who committed crimes but can't be convicted (improperly obtained evidence) vs. innocents who are convicted. What's justice — punishing the guilty, or following procedure?
- In experimental science: Fisher (find what's true) vs. Neyman-Pearson (certify hypotheses by procedure).

### The Parallel Postulate and Non-Euclidean Geometry (pp. 415-420)
- Euclid's five axioms. The fifth (parallel postulate) — "there is exactly one line through P parallel to L" — always felt less elegant, more complicated than the others. "An inelegant axiom is like a stain in the corner of the floor." For 2000 years, mathematicians tried to prove it from the first four.
- Farkas Bolyai (Hungarian nobleman) wasted years on the problem and warned his son János: "You must not attempt this approach to parallels. I know this way to the very end. I have traversed this bottomless night, which extinguished all light and joy in my life."
- János ignored his father. By 1823 he had the answer: what if the parallel postulate were FALSE? No contradiction follows. There's ANOTHER geometry where the first four axioms hold but the fifth doesn't. This proves the fifth axiom can't be derived from the others. "Out of nothing I have created a strange new universe."
- Simultaneously: Nikolai Lobachevskii in Russia reached the same conclusions. Gauss had formulated similar ideas but hadn't published. When told of Bolyai's work, Gauss said: "To praise it would amount to praising myself." (Ouch.)
- Riemann observed a simpler non-Euclidean geometry: the sphere. Points = pairs of antipodal points. Lines = great circles. Line segments = arcs of great circles. Circles = any size allowed. The first four axioms all hold. But the fifth fails spectacularly: there are NO parallel lines (all great circles intersect). One-paragraph proof: if a great circle C' were parallel to C, it would be entirely on one side, enclosing less than half the sphere's area — impossible since all great circles enclose exactly half.
- In Bolyai's hyperbolic geometry, the opposite: there are INFINITELY many parallel lines through any external point.
- Spherical geometry = projective plane (Brunelleschi's perspective, where all lines meet). Not a coincidence — one can prove they're the same geometry.
- The proof: if the fifth axiom followed from the first four, then spheres (where it fails) would create a contradiction with the first four axioms. But spheres exist and the first four axioms hold on them. So the fifth axiom is independent. QED.

### Formalism (pp. 420-422)
- Once you see the first four axioms apply to many geometries, any theorem proved from just those axioms is true in ALL of them — a "mathematical force multiplier."
- Post-Einstein: non-Euclidean geometry IS the geometry of spacetime. Not just a game.
- Olivia Caramello's work on Grothendieck toposes: theories in different mathematical fields that are "classified by the same topos" let you carry theorems between areas that appear totally different.
- Formalism (G.H. Hardy's term): asking what things should be DEFINED as, rather than what they ARE. "Mathematics becomes a kind of game played with symbols and words." What's a Point? Anything that behaves the way the axioms demand. Could be a frog, a kumquat — doesn't matter.
- Hardy would have advised Condorcet to stop asking who the best candidate "really was" and instead ask which candidate the voting system should DEFINE as the people's choice. Our electoral system IS formalist: the mark on the ballot is what counts, not the voter's intent. Palm Beach County butterfly ballot (2000): thousands of Gore voters accidentally voted for Pat Buchanan. But Gore never argued for those votes. "We define the public will to be that mark that appears most frequently on the pieces of paper collected at the voting booth."

### Formalism in Law and Baseball (pp. 416-419)
- US Supreme Court in Bush v. Gore: Florida Supreme Court had allowed a recount in selected counties. The US Supreme Court stopped it — more counting might yield a more accurate result, but "the proper business of the state is not to count the votes as accurately as possible... but to obey the formal protocol that tells us, in Hardy's terms, who the winner should be defined to be."
- Justice Scalia is the fiercest advocate of legal formalism: "Long live formalism. It is what makes a government a government of laws and not of men." Stick to the words of the Constitution and statutes, treating them as axioms from which judgments follow by logical deduction. When judges try to interpret the law's "spirit," they're bamboozled by their own prejudices.
- Scalia's dissent in *In Re Troy Anthony Davis* (2009): a convicted murderer shouldn't get a new hearing even though 7 of 9 witnesses recanted. "This Court has never held that the Constitution forbids the execution of a convicted defendant who has had a full and fair trial but is later able to convince a habeas court that he is 'actually innocent.'" (Scare quotes around "actually" are Scalia's.)
- Chief Justice Roberts: "Judges are like umpires. Umpires don't make the rules; they apply them." Echoing Bill Klem, "the Old Arbitrator": "It ain't nothin' till I call it" — the bluntest ontological assertion ever made by a sports official.
- 1996 ALCS, Game 1: Derek Jeter's fly ball to right field — 12-year-old Jeffrey Maier reached over the fence and pulled it in. Not a home run. But umpire Rich Garcia called it one. "Jeter knew it wasn't a home run. Tarasco and Benitez knew it wasn't a home run. Fifty-six thousand Yankee fans knew it wasn't a home run." But baseball is formalist — it's a homer because the umpire says so.
- Since 2008, video replay has chipped away at baseball formalism. Many fans feel it's "somehow foreign to the spirit of the sport."
- Legal realist Richard Posner: Supreme Court cases are toss-ups by definition — if they could be decided by straightforward logic, they'd never reach the Court. Justices are in Pascal's position: reason doesn't determine the answer, so they must make pragmatic judgments. Posner says this is what happened in Bush v. Gore.

### The Specter of Contradiction (pp. 419-425)
- Even Scalia occasionally concedes absurd results require departing from strict textualism. No scientist really wants to treat dead salmon brain scans (p = .03) the same as promising clinical trials (p = .03).
- David Hilbert: formalism's greatest mathematical champion. His 23 problems (1900 International Congress) set the course for 20th-century math. Hilbert's reputation so vast that his taste for sandals with socks is allegedly why mathematicians wear them today.
- Problem 10 (solved in the 1960s-70s by Davis, Matijasevic, Putnam, Julia Robinson): no algorithm exists to solve all Diophantine equations. Number theorists breathed a sigh of relief — a formal algorithm would have put them out of work.
- Problem 2: prove the axioms of arithmetic are not contradictory. Hilbert rewrote Euclid's axioms removing all ambiguity (points and lines could be "frogs" and "kumquats"). Abraham Wald, as a student in Vienna, showed some of Hilbert's axioms derived from others.
- Hilbert's dream: extend formalism from geometry to ALL of mathematics, using Peano's axioms for arithmetic ("Zero is a number," "If x = y and y = z, then x = z," etc.). From these bare beginnings, Peano showed you could define rational numbers and prove their properties.
- The nightmare scenario: after rebuilding all of math from bedrock axioms, someone in Amsterdam proves a theorem while someone in Kyoto proves its negation. Reductio ad absurdum — but on WHAT? Were the axioms wrong? Or logical deduction itself?
- Gottlob Frege's set theory: seemed to provide a foundation for math. A set is a collection of objects. Among all sets, some contain themselves ("ouroboric" — the set of infinite sets is itself an infinite set). Let NO be the set of all non-ouroboric sets. Is NO ouroboric? If yes, it can't contain itself (it only contains non-ouroboric sets), so it's not. If no, it DOES contain itself, so it IS. Contradiction.
- Bertrand Russell wrote to Frege in June 1902 with this paradox. Frege's second volume of *Grundgesetze* was already in press. His postscript recording the paradox is "perhaps the saddest sentence ever written in a technical work of mathematics": "A scientist can hardly encounter anything more undesirable than, just as a work is completed, to have its foundation give way."
- Hilbert sought a FINITARY proof of consistency — one that didn't rely on the existence of infinite sets. But in 1931, Gödel's second incompleteness theorem proved no such proof could exist. "He had killed Hilbert's program with a single stroke."
- Ellenberg: "Should you be worried that all of mathematics might collapse tomorrow afternoon? For what it's worth, I'm not."
- Edward Nelson (Princeton, 2011) circulated a proof of arithmetic's INCONSISTENCY. Terry Tao found the mistake within days. Vladimir Voevodsky (Fields Medalist, IAS) also expressed doubt about arithmetic's consistency, proposing homotopy theory as a new foundation.

### Genius Is a Thing That Happens (pp. 430-434)
- Ramanujan: the paradigmatic lone genius. Prodigy from southern India, 4000+ theorems in notebooks, divine revelations from the goddess Namagiri, worked in isolation. But he's an outlier, famous precisely because he's so uncharacteristic.
- Hilbert was a very good but not exceptional student in Königsberg — that was Hermann Minkowski, two years younger.
- The cult of genius is damaging to math education. Students quit because someone in class is "ahead" of them. "I've never heard a student say, 'I like Hamlet, but I don't really belong in AP English — that kid who sits in the front row knows all the plays.'" Athletes don't quit because a teammate is better. But math students do.
- "We need more math majors who DON'T become mathematicians. More math major high school teachers, more math major CEOs, more math major senators."
- "Hardworking" isn't a veiled insult — the ability to work hard (psychologists call it "grit") is a genuine skill essential to math. Poincaré's breakthrough on Fuchsian functions came to him stepping onto a bus — but only after weeks of intense conscious and unconscious labor.
- There's ALWAYS someone ahead of you. Fields Medalists look to the dead greats. "And yet, in the last hundred years, the joined effort of all these dummies-compared-to-Gauss has produced the greatest flowering of mathematical knowledge the world has ever seen."
- Mark Twain: "It takes a thousand men to invent a telegraph... and the last man gets the credit and we forget the others." Mathematics is communal — like football, where a touchdown pass requires the whole offensive line.
- Terry Tao: "The popular image of the lone (and possibly slightly mad) genius... is a charming and romantic image, but also a wildly inaccurate one."
- "It's not wrong to say Hilbert was a genius. But it's more right to say that what Hilbert accomplished was genius. Genius is a thing that happens, not a kind of person."

### Political Logic (pp. 434-436)
- Gödel's 1948 US citizenship exam: he had found a constitutional contradiction that could allow a dictatorship. His friends Einstein and Oskar Morgenstern begged him not to mention it. Examiner: "What kind of government did you have in Austria?" Gödel: "It was a republic, but the constitution was such that it finally was changed into a dictatorship." Examiner: "This could not happen in this country." Gödel: "Oh, yes, I can prove it." The examiner hurriedly changed the subject.
- Hilbert refused to sign the 1914 Declaration to the Cultural World (defending Germany's war), saying he "was unable to verify to his exacting standards that the assertions in question were not true." When Göttingen faculty resisted hiring Emmy Noether because she was a woman: "I do not see how the sex of the candidate is an argument against her admission. We are a university, not a bathhouse."
- Hilbert in old age: his first PhD student Otto Blumenthal (Christian from a Jewish family, removed from his position at Aachen) visited in 1938. Hilbert didn't understand why Blumenthal no longer lectured. Blumenthal later died in Theresienstadt.

### Arrow's Impossibility Theorem and Condorcet's Death (pp. 436-438)
- Kenneth Arrow's 1951 PhD thesis: proved that even a much weaker set of axioms than Condorcet's leads to paradoxes in voting systems. Nobel Prize in economics, 1972. It would have disappointed Condorcet as much as Gödel disappointed Hilbert.
- Condorcet never succeeded in designing a paradox-free voting system. He wrote forlornly in 1785 that one must accept "equivocal" decisions and hope for enlightened voters or trustworthy candidates.
- The Revolution marginalized Condorcet. Forced into hiding, knowing he might not survive, he wrote his *Sketch for a Historical Picture of the Progress of the Human Mind* — astonishingly optimistic, predicting a world free of royalism, prejudice, hunger, and old age, all conquered by science. Captured in March 1794 (Germinal of Year 2), dead two days later — suicide or murder.
- His legacy: we no longer hope to find perfect voting systems, but we've committed to his core belief that quantitative "social mathematics" — social science — ought to guide government. "Condorcet's idea is so thoroughly intertwined with the modern way of doing political business that we hardly see it as a choice. But it is a choice. I think it's the right one."

## CONCLUSION: How to Be Right

### The TB Simulation (pp. 439-440)
- Ellenberg's summer job between sophomore and junior years: modeling tuberculosis prevalence through 2050 for a public health researcher. Built a simulation using empirical data on transmission rates, infection courses, medication compliance, broken down by age, race, sex, HIV status.
- The result: completely useless. Each empirical study had small uncertainties (transmission rate maybe 20%, maybe 13%, maybe 25%). These local uncertainties percolated through the simulation, feeding back into each other, until by 2050 "the noise had engulfed the signal." The simulation could produce anything from zero TB to universal infection. "I had no principled way to choose."

### The TB Simulation continued (pp. 436-437)
- The researcher didn't want to hear that the model was useless. He was paying for a number. "I know there's uncertainty, that's how medical research is, I get that, just give me your best guess." Ellenberg protested that any single guess would be worse than no guess at all, but eventually gave in. "I have no doubt he told many people, afterward, that X million people were going to have tuberculosis in the year 2050. And I'll bet if anyone asked him how he knew this, he would say, I hired a guy who did the math."

### The Critic Who Counts (pp. 437-439)
- Theodore Roosevelt's "Citizenship in a Republic" speech (Sorbonne, Paris, 1910): "It is not the critic who counts; not the man who points out how the strong man stumbles... The credit belongs to the man who is actually in the arena."
- Roosevelt's speech also touches on diminishing utility of money and the "Less like Sweden" fallacy ("It is just as foolish to refuse all progress because people demanding it desire at some points to go to absurd extremes, as it would be to go to these absurd extremes simply because some of the measures advocated by the extremists were wise").
- But Roosevelt's main theme is that the bold, commonsensical, virile man matters more than the "closet philosopher." He was speaking at the Sorbonne, where Hilbert had presented his 23 problems 10 years earlier.
- Ellenberg's riposte: Roosevelt dismisses the philosopher as "of no use in actual governmental work." But Condorcet, who spent his time in the library, contributed more to the French state than most practical men. And Abraham Wald "went his whole life without lifting a weapon in anger, but who nonetheless played a serious part in the American war effort, precisely by counseling the doers of deeds how to do them better. He was unsweaty, undusty, and unbloody, but he was right. He was a critic who counted."

### For This Is Action (pp. 439-441)
- John Ashbery's poem "Soonest Mended" as counterpoint to Roosevelt: "For this is action, this not being sure, this careless / Preparing, sowing the seeds crooked in the furrow, / Making ready to forget, and always coming back / To the mooring of starting out, that day so long ago."
- "For this is action, this not being sure!" — Ellenberg's mantra. Roosevelt would have called "not being sure" cowardly. Ashbery says it's the move of a strong person. "A kind of fence-sitting / Raised to the level of an esthetic ideal."
- "Mathematics is also a means by which we can reason about the uncertain, taming if not altogether domesticating it. It's been that way since the time of Pascal." Math gives us a way of being unsure in a principled way: "I'm not sure, this is why I'm not sure, and this is roughly how not-sure I am."

### A Man Who Swings from Poll to Poll: Nate Silver (pp. 441-444)
- Nate Silver as "the paladin of principled uncertainty" and "a kind of Kurt Cobain of probability." His 2012 NYT columns drew unprecedented public attention to probability theory. Both Silver and Cobain proved that if you carry a niche practice into the mainstream with an approachable style, you can make it massively popular.
- Silver was willing to treat uncertainty as a real, studyable thing, not a sign of weakness. Traditional pundits said "Obama is going to win" or "Romney is going to win." Silver said: "Either one might win, but Obama is substantially more likely to win." That probabilistic answer IS the right answer.
- Josh Jordan (National Review) criticized Silver for moving Obama from 85% to 67% without declaring the race transformed. Dylan Byers (Politico) said Silver "often gives the impression of hedging." Ellenberg: "What Silver offers isn't hedging; it's honesty."
- Silver's expected number of wrong state calls on Oct 26: for each of 18 competitive states, the expected wrong answers sum to 2.83. He expected to get about 3 states wrong. He got all fifty right.
- W.O.V. Quine: "To believe something is to believe that it is true; therefore a reasonable person believes each of his beliefs to be true; yet experience has taught him to expect that some of his beliefs, he knows not which, will turn out to be false." This parallels the budget paradox from Chapter 17 — Americans think each program is worth funding but not ALL programs.
- Silver presented probability distributions, not point predictions. "Silver was being uncertain, rigorously uncertain, in public, and the public ate it up. I wouldn't have thought it was possible. This is action, this not being sure!"

### Against Precision (pp. 444-446)
- Valid criticism of Silver: "Obama has a 73.1% chance of winning" implies false precision. The decimal suggests measurement accuracy that doesn't exist.
- "There's such a thing as being too precise." SAT scores could have more decimal places, but shouldn't. Students are anxious enough without worrying about a hundredth of a point.
- Florida 2000: a difference of a few hundred votes out of millions. "As a way of thinking about who Floridians wanted to be president, this is absurd; the imprecision caused by ballots spoiled, ballots lost, ballots miscounted, is much greater than the tiny difference in the final count. We don't know who got more votes in Florida."
- Charles Seife's *Proofiness*: the Franken-Coleman Minnesota Senate race (312-vote margin) was really determined by legal tussles over questions like whether a write-in for "Lizard People" counted. "The signal is lost in the noise."
- Seife's radical proposal: close elections should be decided by coin flip. Not crazy — close elections are ALREADY determined by chance (bad weather, broken machines, confusing ballots). A coin flip would at least be honest about it.
- Mathematicians aren't obsessed with precision for its own sake. "We want to compute everything to as many decimal places as necessary." Pi's 67,890 digits (memorized by Lu Chao) are an impressive memory feat but mathematically uninteresting — the digits are as good as random. "Pi itself is interesting, to be sure. But pi is not its digits."
- Benjamin Franklin on Thomas Godfrey: "He knew little out of his way, and was not a pleasing companion; as, like most great mathematicians I have met with, he expected universal precision in everything said."

### That Does Not Compute (pp. 447-450)
- *Ex falso quodlibet* ("the principle of explosion"): from a single contradiction in a formal system, you can prove literally anything is true. Example: if you believe "Paris is the capital of France" and "Paris is not the capital of France," you can logically prove the Portland Trail Blazers won the 1982 NBA championship. Captain Kirk used this trick to disable dictatorial AIs.
- But Kirk's trick doesn't work on humans. F. Scott Fitzgerald: "The test of a first-rate intelligence is the ability to hold two opposed ideas in the mind at the same time, and still retain the ability to function." Mathematicians do this constantly in reductio ad absurdum — "lucid dreaming of a very systematic kind."
- Common mathematical folk advice: prove by day, disprove by night. Topologist R.H. Bing allegedly split each month between two weeks proving the Poincare Conjecture and two weeks seeking a counterexample.
- The value of failure: each failed attempt to disprove a theorem hits a wall. Those walls accumulate into a structure — the structure of the proof itself. Bolyai tried to prove the parallel postulate from the other axioms. He failed, like everyone else. But unlike the others, he understood what was blocking him: the existence of a geometry without the parallel postulate. Each failure taught him more about the thing he didn't think existed.
- "Proving by day and disproving by night is not just for mathematics. I find it's a good habit to put pressure on all your beliefs, social, political, scientific, and philosophical. Believe whatever you believe by day; but at night, argue against the propositions you hold most dear."
- Fitzgerald's "The Crack-Up" (1936): his endorsement of contradiction was really about despair. Beckett's version is richer: contradiction pervades all of *Murphy*, even drawing on the Pythagorean proof of the irrationality of √2. Beckett's *Worstward Ho*: "Ever tried. Ever failed. No matter. Try again. Fail again. Fail better."
- David Foster Wallace put a tamed Russell's paradox at the center of *The Broom of the System*. He "wrestled with the paradoxes just the way mathematicians do."

### When Am I Going to Use This? (pp. 450-452)
- The mathematicians in this book weren't just critics — they BUILT things. Galton uncovered regression to the mean. Condorcet built social decision-making. Bolyai created non-Euclidean geometry. Shannon and Hamming built the geometry of digital signals. Wald put armor on the right part of the plane.
- "Every mathematician creates new things, some big, some small. All mathematical writing is creative writing."
- Mathematical understanding — "suddenly knowing what's going on, with total certainty, all the way to the bottom" — is a rare sensation. "You feel you've reached into the universe's guts and put your hand on the wire."
- "To do mathematics is to be, at once, touched by fire and bound by reason. This is no contradiction. Logic forms a narrow channel through which intuition flows with vastly augmented force."
- The lessons of mathematics: "There is structure in the world; we can hope to understand some of it; our intuition is stronger with a formal exoskeleton than without one. And that mathematical certainty is one thing, the softer convictions we find attached to us in everyday life another, and we should keep track of the difference if we can."
- "When are you going to use this? You've been using mathematics since you were born and you'll probably never stop. Use it well."
