Conclusion: How to Be Right

When Am I Going to Use This?

Somewhere in a classroom, a student is asking the question every math teacher dreads: When am I going to use this? The standard answer — you might need integrals in some future career — is a lie both teacher and student recognize. The number of adults who will ever use the integral of (1 − 3x + 4x²)² dx "can be counted on a few thousand hands."

The real answer is better and stranger: math is not the integrals themselves but the thinking they build. Like an athlete doing weight training who will never use a barbell on the playing field, the student is developing a capacity for reasoning that will prove indispensable in places she cannot yet imagine. Mathematics is not a body of formulas to memorize but a way of seeing hidden structure beneath the messy surface of the world — "like wearing a pair of X-ray specs."

The book's central exhibit is Abraham Wald, a Jewish mathematician from Klausenburg who escaped Nazi Austria and joined the wartime Statistical Research Group in Manhattan — "the most extraordinary group of statisticians ever organized." Members included Frederick Mosteller (who founded Harvard's statistics department), Leonard Jimmie Savage (decision theory), and Milton Friedman, who was often the fourth-smartest person in the room. Wald was the smartest, but he functioned as an "enemy alien" — secretaries pulled papers from his hands as soon as he finished writing.

The military wanted to armor returning bombers where the bullet holes were concentrated — the fuselage (1.73 per square foot) and fuel system (1.55), not the engines (1.11). Wald saw the problem everyone else missed: armor where the bullet holes aren't. The planes hit in the engine never came back. The holes on surviving planes marked the places that could absorb damage. This is survivorship bias, and the same logic explains why mutual fund performance looks better than it really is — dead funds are excluded from the averages, just as shot-down planes were excluded from the data.

Mathematics is the extension of common sense by other means. Jordan Ellenberg

Wald's insight looks like common sense in hindsight, and that is precisely the point. Math IS common sense, extended by rigorous methods to places common sense alone cannot reach. A six-year-old lying on the floor staring at a speaker grille notices that 6×8 holes can be read as 8 columns of 6 — discovering the commutativity of multiplication for himself. Calculus, probability, and statistical inference all grow from such intuitions, formalized and sharpened.

Without that rigor, the officers would have armored the wrong parts of the plane. Without the common sense, mathematics would be the sterile rule-following that the peevish calculus student believes it to be. John von Neumann warned in 1947 that when a mathematical discipline travels too far from its empirical source, it risks degenerating into "a disorganized mass of details and complexities." The book lives in the quadrant where ideas are simple and profound — accessible with basic math but limitless in application.

Ellenberg grew up in the pure-math "convent," dedicated to number theory, eventually working on "residually modular Galois representations" and "cohomology of moduli schemes." But the more abstract his research became, the more he noticed simple, old, profound math everywhere in the real world. He started writing about math in everyday life for magazines and newspapers, and discovered that "even people who said they hated math were willing to read them."

The book will include trigonometric functions describing variable relationships, calculus on linear vs. nonlinear phenomena, set theory as metaphor for Supreme Court decisions, and information theory explaining how MIT undergrads gamed the Massachusetts state lottery. "There will be no homework, and there will be no test."

Chapter 1: Less Like Sweden

During the Affordable Care Act debate, a Cato Institute blogger drew the relationship between government size and prosperity as a straight line sloping downward: more Swedish equals worse off. Sweden was trimming its welfare state, so why was Obama expanding America's? If the line is correct, the optimal policy is always at one extreme — shrink government to nothing.

But suppose the real curve has a hump in the middle, peaking at some moderate level of government. Then America, sitting left of the peak, would rightly add programs while Sweden, sitting right, would rightly trim. Both countries converging on the same optimum from opposite directions. The entire political disagreement reduces to a mathematical question: is the curve a line or not? "Nonlinear thinking means which way you should go depends on where you already are."

This is the Laffer curve, the most famous napkin doodle in economic history. At a 1974 dinner with Dick Cheney and Donald Rumsfeld, economist Arthur Laffer sketched the relationship between tax rates and government revenue. At 0% tax, revenue is zero. At 100% tax, revenue is also zero — nobody works if the government takes everything. Between those endpoints the curve must rise, peak, and fall.

Jude Wanniski championed the idea in his 1978 book The Way the World Works. When called a nut, he replied: "Thomas Edison was a nut, Leibniz was a nut, Galileo was a nut." Ellenberg's aside: "People with out-of-the-mainstream ideas who compare themselves to Edison and Galileo are never actually right." Wanniski became advisor to Jack Kemp, then Reagan.

Reagan had personal experience: as a wartime movie star facing a 90% income surtax, "you could only make four pictures and then you were in the top bracket, so we all quit working after about four pictures." But Greg Mankiw, a Republican economist who chaired Bush's Council of Economic Advisors, concedes that "subsequent history failed to confirm Laffer's conjecture." Reagan's cuts produced less revenue, not more. Personal income tax revenue per person fell 9% in real terms from 1980 to 1984.

An interesting wrinkle from Mankiw: the very richest DID contribute more revenue after Reagan's cuts. This suggests the revenue-maximizing strategy might be to jack up taxes on the middle class (who have no choice but to keep working) while slashing rates on the rich (who can credibly threaten to withdraw economic activity). A vexing conclusion for liberals.

Laffer himself was more modest than his followers. He drew no numbers on his napkin. In congressional testimony: "I cannot measure it frankly, but I can tell you what the characteristics of it are." The false syllogism: it could be the case that lowering taxes increases revenue; I want it to be the case; therefore it is the case. That leap from possibility to certainty through desire is the essence of false linearity.

Which way you should go depends on where you already are. Jordan Ellenberg

The Laffer curve doesn't have to be a smooth hill. It could be a trapezoid, a dromedary's back with two humps, or wildly oscillating. But whatever its shape, if it slopes upward somewhere it must slope downward somewhere else. Figuring out where you actually sit on it "requires deep, difficult, empirical work, the kind of work that doesn't fit on a napkin."

Chapter 2: Straight Locally, Curved Globally

If the world is curved, why does anyone assume it is straight? Because in a very real sense, every curve is straight — at least locally. Archimedes understood this through the method of exhaustion: inscribe a square inside a circle, then an octagon, then a 16-gon, doubling the sides until the polygon becomes indistinguishable from the circle. An ant walking on the circle would think it was on a straight line.

The method works by computing increasingly tight bounds. The inscribed square has area 2, the circumscribed square has area 4, so the circle's area is between 2 and 4. An inscribed octagon gives 2.83, a circumscribed octagon 3.31. A 16-gon narrows it to 3.06–3.18. Keep doubling: a 65,536-sided polygon looks indistinguishable from the circle. The fringe of difference has been "exhausted" by relentless iteration.

Newton generalized this: every smooth curve, zoomed in enough, looks like a line. Reduce your field of view to the infinitesimal — smaller than any size you can name but not zero — and what was almost a line becomes exactly a line. The slope of this line is the derivative. George Berkeley denounced Newton's infinitesimals as "the ghosts of departed quantities," and he had a point — the construction wasn't rigorous. But calculus works. Release a rock from a sling, and it flies off in exactly the direction the tangent line predicts.

Two centuries later, Augustin-Louis Cauchy replaced Newton's ghosts with the precise notion of a limit. The infinite sum .9 + .09 + .009 + ... gets closer and closer to 1 and never retreats. Cauchy defined the sum to be 1, proved this caused no contradictions, and created the rigorous framework underlying all of modern calculus. The cost: 1 and 0.99999... are two names for the same number, like synonyms.

Cauchy was "an unwavering conservative and a royalist, but in his mathematics he was proudly revolutionary." He rewrote his syllabus at the École Polytechnique, enraging students (who signed up for freshman calculus, not cutting-edge pure math), colleagues (engineering students didn't need this rigor), and administrators (whose official course outline he ignored). The school placed note takers in his classroom to monitor him. Cauchy did not comply. He was not interested in the needs of engineers. He was interested in the truth.

One gets such wholesale returns of conjecture out of such a trifling investment of fact. Mark Twain, Life on the Mississippi

Mark Twain satirized linear extrapolation brilliantly: the Mississippi had been shortening by 1⅓ miles per year, so "any calm person" could see that a million years ago it was over a million miles long, and 742 years from now it would be a mile and three-quarters. "One gets such wholesale returns of conjecture out of such a trifling investment of fact."

The danger is real. Linear regression — "the statistical technique that is to social science as the screwdriver is to home repair" — makes it dangerously easy to draw a line through five data points and project it to absurdity. A missile's trajectory is a parabola; a line fit to five seconds of data will be catastrophically wrong after an hour. "(Blood, gristle, screams.)"

The "math wars" in teaching pit traditionalists (memorization, algorithms, exact answers) against reformists (meaning, guided discovery, approximation). Ellenberg takes neither side. You need multiplication tables for mental flow, but extracting square roots by hand is safely obsolete. The goal is "a mathematics that values precise answers but also intelligent approximation, that demands fluency but also the horse sense to work things out on the fly."

Chapter 3: Everyone Is Obese

A paper in the journal Obesity announced that by 2048, every American would be overweight. ABC News covered the "obesity apocalypse." The methodology was simple: plot the upward trend in obesity rates, draw a regression line, extend it until it hits 100%. By 2060 the model gives 109% overweight — a physical impossibility.

The authors used NHANES data (National Health and Nutrition Examination Study). Overweight means BMI ≥ 25. In the early 1970s, just under half of Americans qualified. Early 1990s: almost 60%. By 2008: almost 75%. The trend looked linear, and the line pointed to 100% by 2048. But "current trends will not continue. They can't!" Just four years later, NHANES showed the upward march had already begun to flatten.

Even worse: the authors broke data by ethnic group and sex. Black men's obesity rate was growing at half the overall rate — their line wouldn't cross 100% until 2095. In 2048, only 80% of black men would be overweight. But if ALL Americans are supposed to be overweight by 2048, where are the 20% non-overweight black men? "Offshore?" The contradiction went unmentioned — "the epidemiological equivalent of saying there are -4 grams of water left in the bucket. Zero credit."

Meanwhile, why does South Dakota have the highest rate of brain cancer in America while North Dakota has one of the lowest? Both are tiny states. Of the nine states at the extremes (top five and bottom five), the largest is Nebraska (37th most populous). Small states dominate both ends not because of geography but because of the Law of Large Numbers.

With small sample sizes, random variation dominates. In a coin-flipping contest, someone flipping 10 coins will produce both the highest and lowest percentages of heads. Someone flipping 100 will cluster around 50%. The typical discrepancy from 50-50 grows with the square root of the sample size — 100 times as many coins yields discrepancies only 10 times as large in absolute terms.

J. E. Kerrich, a South African mathematician, made an ill-advised visit to Europe in 1939 — "his semester abroad quickly turned into an unscheduled stint in an internment camp in Denmark." He flipped a coin 10,000 times. The fraction of heads converges inexorably toward 50%, "as if squeezed by an invisible vise."

Abraham de Moivre found that discrepancies from 50-50 form a perfect bell curve — the normal distribution, which Francis Ysidro Edgeworth unsuccessfully proposed calling "the gendarme's hat." If you flip N coins, your chance of landing within √N of 50% is about 95%.

The so-called law of averages — five heads means tails is "due" — is flatly false. Coins have no memory. The Law of Large Numbers works not by balancing past results but by diluting them with new data until the past is so proportionally negligible that it can safely be forgotten.

Chapter 4: How Much Is That in Dead Americans?

When 1,074 Israelis were killed by Palestinians from 2000 to 2005, commentators scaled the figure to American population: "the proportional equivalent of more than 50,000 dead." This "how much is that in dead Americans" calculation is standard rhetoric, used by both sides of every conflict. The U.S. House called 26 killed Israelis the equivalent of 1,200 Americans. Newt Gingrich called 8 dead Israelis almost 500 Americans.

Push the logic and it breaks. The same 1,074 deaths equal 223,000 Chinese, 300 Slovenes, or "one or two Tuvaluans" (population 9,847). "When there are two men left in the bar at closing time, and one of them coldcocks the other, it is not equivalent in context to 150 million Americans getting simultaneously punched in the face."

Stephen Pinker's Better Angels of Our Nature makes the case that the world has grown less violent. The 20th century looks catastrophic only because populations are large — the Thirty Years' War killed 1 in 100 people on Earth, which today would be 70 million, dwarfing both world wars.

Whether such comparisons illuminate or distort depends on what question you are asking. If the magnitude of a disaster makes it natural to speak of "survivors," proportional thinking is appropriate — you can call any Tutsi in Rwanda a survivor (75% were killed). But calling someone in Seattle a "survivor" of the World Trade Center attack is absurd.

Atrocities form a partially ordered set — some pairs can be meaningfully compared, others cannot. Rwanda eliminated 11% of its population, clearly among the worst crimes of the century. But nobody says it was "nine times as bad as the Holocaust" proportionally. "And to do so would set teeth rightly on edge."

The best approach to understanding 26 people killed by terrorism is simply to "imagine twenty-six people killed by terrorist bombings — not halfway across the world, but in your own city. That computation is mathematically and morally unimpeachable, and no calculator is required."

Even testing mathematical methods requires hygiene: compute the same thing several ways and see if the answers agree. The 2004 Madrid train bombings killed about 200. Scale by national population: equivalent to 1,300 Americans. Scale by city population: 463. Scale by province vs. state: about 600. Three different answers from the same data — a red flag that the method itself is flawed.

"The question of whether one war was worse than another is fundamentally unlike the question of whether one number is bigger than another. The latter question always has an answer. The former does not."

Chapter 5: More Pie Than Plate

Between 1990 and 2008, the United States gained 27.3 million net jobs. Of these, 26.7 million (98%) came from the nontradable sector — government, health care, retail, food service. Widely reported as proof that globalization had gutted tradable employment. But the tradable sector grew by only 620,000 jobs net. Between 2000 and 2008 it actually lost 3 million while the nontradable sector added 7 million — making nontradable responsible for 175% of total growth. More pie than plate.

The problem is percentages with negative numbers. Even the concept of negative numbers was once controversial — sixteenth-century algebraists Cardano and François Viète argued about whether negative times negative equals positive. Cardano called negative solutions ficta (fake).

A coffee shop example makes it concrete: you lost $500 on coffee but made $750 on the pastry case and $750 on the CD rack. Total profit: $1,000. The pastry case is 75% of profits. The CD rack is ALSO 75%. If coffee losses had been $1,000 more, total profit is $0, and the pastry case is infinity percent of profits.

Steven Rattner reported in the New York Times that 93% of additional income in 2010 went to the top 1%. But the top 10%-but- not-1% captured 17%. That sums to over 100% — because the bottom 90% actually lost ground. In 1992, the top 1% accrued 131% of national income gains. Negative numbers again.

Dividing one number by another is mere computation; figuring out what you should divide by what is mathematics. Jordan Ellenberg

Mitt Romney's campaign claimed "women account for 92.3% of all jobs lost under Obama." Technically correct for the chosen start date (January 2009). Shift the start by one month and women account for over 3,000% of job losses. "That would have signaled to any but the thickest voters that this percentage was somehow not the right measure."

The real story: men took it worse in the recession AND gained more in the recovery. Men's losses and gains nearly canceled; women's didn't. The 92.3% figure was, as the Washington Post put it, "true but false" — designed to create an impression that the underlying data does not support.

"Mitt Romney has never denied allegations that for years he's operated a bicontinental cocaine- trafficking ring in Colombia and Salt Lake City." Also 100% true! Romney has never been accused of this, so of course he hasn't denied it. Technically correct, deliberately misleading — the same structure as the 92.3% claim.

The lesson: "Don't talk about percentages of numbers when the numbers might be negative." And more broadly: the choice of what to divide by what is itself a mathematical decision, not a computational one. Get it wrong and your perfectly calculated answer tells a perfectly misleading story.

Chapter 6: The Baltimore Stockbroker and the Bible Code

You receive ten consecutive correct stock predictions by mail. On week eleven, the broker asks for your money. The odds of ten correct picks by chance are 1 in 1,024 — surely this is genius. But the broker sent 10,240 letters on week one: half predicting up, half down. After each round, the wrong half was discarded. After ten weeks, ten people have seen ten perfect picks. "No matter what the stock market does."

Derren Brown's 2008 TV show replicated this on camera: he mailed horse-racing predictions to thousands of Britons, gradually winnowing the list until one person was utterly convinced she had found a foolproof system. He revealed the trick at the end. The same logic drives mutual fund "incubation" — companies launch many funds privately, publicize only the winners, and mercy-kill the rest. Surviving incubated funds don't maintain their prenatal performance.

In the mid-1990s, Eliyahu Rips and colleagues at Hebrew University searched Genesis for "equidistant letter sequences" (ELS) — characters plucked at regular intervals — and found the names and dates of 32 notable rabbis appearing in proximity far more often than chance would predict. Published in Statistical Science as "a challenging puzzle," it became Michael Drosnin's bestseller The Bible Code.

The controversy split Orthodox Jewish mathematicians at Harvard — David Kazhdan was open to the codes, while Shlomo Sternberg called it "a hoax." A distinguished rabbi at a Purim dinner asked a code adherent: what if you found a code saying Sabbath was on Sunday? The young man said he'd have to think about it. The rabbi concluded that codes capable of challenging fundamental law must be rejected — "about as authentically Jewish as a bacon cheeseburger."

Brendan McKay and Dror Bar-Natan cracked the code: the researchers had freedom in which name to search for each rabbi (medieval rabbis had no passports). With equally valid alternative names, the Torah lost its magic — but War and Peace in Hebrew suddenly nailed the dates. Drosnin dared critics to find assassination predictions in Moby Dick; McKay promptly found JFK, Indira Gandhi, and Drosnin himself.

Simcha Emanuel, a Talmud professor, drew up an independent appellation list not designed for either text. The Torah did only slightly better than chance. The lesson: with enough wiggle room in how you define your search terms, any long text produces impressive matches.

"You've been swayed by the impressive results, but you don't know how many chances the broker had to get those results." The Scrabble analogy: Ellenberg's eight-year-old dumps and redraws tiles until he gets good letters. "He has no way of knowing what letters he's going to draw! But if you give yourself enough chances, you'll eventually come across that Z you're waiting for."

If we reject Bible codes despite their passing standard significance tests, what does that say about the reliability of those tests? "It says you ought to be a little worried about them."

Chapter 7: Dead Fish Don't Read Minds

Craig Bennett presented at the 2009 Human Brain Mapping conference a study titled "Neural correlates of interspecies perspective taking in the post-mortem Atlantic Salmon." A dead fish was placed in an fMRI scanner and shown photographs of humans. Methods section: the salmon "was approximately 18 inches long, weighed 3.8 lbs, and was not alive at the time of scanning."

Two voxel clusters in the salmon appeared to "empathize" with humans — one in the medial brain cavity, one in the upper spinal column. The fMRI divides the brain into tens of thousands of tiny volume units. Random noise in each means some will spike at exactly the right moment by pure chance. Most neuroimaging articles Bennett surveyed did not use multiple-comparisons correction.

B.F. Skinner — frustrated novelist turned behaviorist, who had received encouraging words from Robert Frost at the Bread Loaf conference — tried to debunk Shakespeare's alliteration using significance tests. His null hypothesis: Shakespeare picked initial sounds randomly. He analyzed 100 sonnets, and Shakespeare "failed" the test. Skinner concluded: "Shakespeare might as well have drawn his words out of a hat."

But Skinner was wrong — his test was underpowered, "the equivalent of looking at the planets with binoculars." Shakespeare used alliteration with restraint. Fellow Elizabethan George Gascoigne had mocked overuse: Crambe bis positum mors est — cabbage served twice is death.

The same problem plagued the famous 1985 "hot hand" study. Gilovich, Vallone, and Tversky analyzed every shot from the 1980-81 Philadelphia 76ers. Julius Erving: after 3 straight baskets, shot 48% (vs. 52% overall). Darryl "Chocolate Thunder" Dawkins: after a hit, 57%; after a miss, 73% — the opposite of a hot hand. Dawkins drove for backboard-shattering dunks after misses ("In Your Face Disgrace," "Turbo Sexophonic Delight").

Tversky boasted: "I've been in a thousand arguments over this topic. I've won them all, and I've convinced no one." But Korb and Stillwell (2003) showed the test was underpowered — it would miss a genuine hot hand more than 75% of the time.

Attali (2013) found that players who make a three-pointer attempt harder shots next time, canceling out the streak. The hot hand may be real but invisible because confidence breeds overreach. "The nature of the analogous phenomenon in stock investment is left as an exercise for the reader."

"Improbable things happen a lot." North Carolina's Cash 5 lottery drew the same five numbers twice in three days — miraculous-seeming, but hundreds of lottery games run for years. Some coincidence is inevitable. Aristotle: "It is probable that improbable things will happen."

Chapter 8: Reductio Ad Unlikely

The null hypothesis significance test works by reductio ad absurdum — assume the thing you want to disprove, show it leads to something improbable, and reject it. If I claim 200 children were killed by guns in DC in 2012, but DC had only 88 total homicides, the claim is impossible. Clean reductio. Significance testing is the fuzzy version: assume the null, compute the probability of your observation (the p-value), and reject if p is small. "Reductio ad unlikely."

The Pythagoreans proved √2 is irrational this way: assume √2 = m/n in lowest terms. Derive that both m and n must be even — contradiction (can't be in lowest terms). "So awesomely paradigm- busting they had to kill its author." But significance testing replaces "impossible" with "very improbable," and the gap between those two words is vast.

Joseph Berkson of the Mayo Clinic skewered the logic: 50 subjects, hypothesized as human. One is an albino (1 in 20,000 chance among humans). The p-value is below 0.0025. By NHST logic, reject the null hypothesis. The subjects aren't human. Absurd! "Impossible and improbable are not the same — not even close."

John Michell, an 18th-century astronomer-clergyman, pioneered the statistical approach to astronomy. Are the Pleiades a real cluster or random alignment? He computed the probability of 6 stars appearing so close by chance: about 1 in 500,000. Concluded they must be a real cluster — and they are. Fisher saw explicit analogy between Michell's reasoning and classical reductio.

But a diagram of random dots shows visible clumps are inevitable. A second diagram with NO clumps actually looks LESS random — it reveals "a built-in disinclination to crowd." The appearance of pattern is not evidence of design; it's evidence of randomness. The Numb3rs pilot used this concept.

The lottery illustrates the distinction perfectly: North Carolina's Cash 5 drew the numbers 4, 21, 23, 34, 39 on two drawings just three days apart. Any specific pair of lottery draws has about a 1 in 300 billion chance. Should we call the lottery rigged every Thursday? Of course not — with hundreds of games running for years, some coincidence is guaranteed.

Yitang Zhang proved in 2013 that primes cluster too — there are infinitely many pairs of primes within some fixed distance of each other. Zhang's backstory is remarkable: star student at Beijing University, PhD in the US, career stalled, worked at Subway, hadn't published since 2001. A former student got him a lectureship at UNH. Then he proved what the biggest names had failed to prove.

It is probable that improbable things will happen. Aristotle

R.A. Fisher saw the significance test as "the detective, not the judge." A significant finding is the beginning of investigation, not the end. Near his death, he wrote: "No scientific worker has a fixed level of significance at which from year to year, and in all circumstances, he rejects hypotheses; he rather gives his mind to each particular case in the light of his evidence and his ideas."

Chapter 9: The International Journal of Haruspicy

An xkcd comic captures the problem: scientists test twenty colors of jelly beans for a link to acne. Nineteen show no effect. Green gets p < 0.05. Newspaper headline: "GREEN JELLY BEANS LINKED TO ACNE! 95% Confidence." If twenty labs independently test the same phenomenon, the one that gets lucky is the one that publishes. The nineteen failures go into the file drawer.

This is the file drawer problem — survivorship bias in science. "They got lucky — but they don't know they got lucky. They're running the con on themselves." The structure is identical to the Baltimore Stockbroker: enough chances produce impressive results from noise.

P-hacking, a term coined by Uri Simonsohn, describes scientists with p = 0.06 who tweak their analysis — delete an outlier, control for age, control for weather — until they cross 0.05. "Torturing the data until it confesses." Usually not malicious — believers find reasons why the tweaked analysis is the "right" one.

The telltale signature: graph p-values from published papers across disciplines (political science, economics, psychology, sociology). There should be a smooth distribution, but instead there is a suspicious upward bump just below 0.05. "That slope is the shape of p-hacking." Scientists who can't hack below the threshold resort to phrases like "almost statistically significant" or "hovers on the brink of significance."

Confidence intervals offer an alternative to raw p-values. Rather than testing only the null hypothesis, they test a range of alternatives. An A/B test shows purchases up 10%, p = 0.03. But is the effect 5% or 25%? A confidence interval of [+3%, +17%] says the effect is positive but maybe not huge. An interval of [-20%, +20%] says we have no idea.

Jerzy Neyman and Egon Pearson developed confidence intervals from a different philosophy than Fisher's. Neyman-Pearson: "the purpose of statistics isn't to tell us what to believe, but to tell us what to do." A significance test is a decision rule, like a criminal trial that follows procedure rather than seeking truth. Fisher hated this — he wanted scientists to form opinions, not follow rigid rules.

The reform movement has begun. Psychological Science started publishing Registered Replication Reports — studies accepted before results are known. The Many Labs project found that 10 of 13 high-profile findings survived replication.

But the deeper issue remains: living by the 0.05 threshold is like defining "recession" so rigidly that you'd ignore unemployment, housing starts, and consumer spending if the GDP definition weren't met. "The critics say that a great deal of scientific practice is nuts in just this way."

Chapter 10: Are You There, God? It's Me, Bayesian Inference

Target's algorithm correctly inferred that a Minnesota teenager was pregnant — from purchase patterns of unscented lotion, mineral supplements, and cotton balls — before her father knew. Impressive. But maybe we should worry more about crappy algorithms than eerily good ones.

More data doesn't always help. Asteroid tracking: more data means better predictions. Weather: Edward Lorenz discovered chaos theory studying weather — "one flap of a sea gull's wing would be enough to alter the course of the weather forever." Hard limit about two weeks ahead, no matter how much data. Human behavior is harder still: "we lack even a good mathematical model and may never have one."

The Netflix Prize ($1 million, 2006) asked teams to improve movie recommendations by 10%. It took three years. Netflix never used the winning algorithm — had already shifted to streaming. "Recommendations remain pretty comically bad."

Bayesian inference offers a different framework. Three theories about a roulette wheel: RED (60% biased red), FAIR (50/50), BLACK (60% biased black). Start with a prior: 90% FAIR, 5% each RED and BLACK. Observe five reds in a row. Compute likelihoods. Update: RED climbs from 5% to 12%, FAIR drops to 86.5%, BLACK falls to 1.5%. A cynic with equal priors would jump to 65% RED; a trusting soul gives RED only 2.5%.

"How much you believe something after you see the evidence depends not just on what the evidence shows, but on how much you believed it to begin with." This troubles people who want science to be objective. But nobody actually ignores priors: a cancer drug with p < 0.05 is probably real. The same results from putting patients in a plastic Stonehenge replica? You'd think they got lucky.

William Paley's watchmaker argument for God fails as pure Bayesian math. Using flat priors, the existence of complex life overwhelmingly supports a designer. But add GODS (a committee — "I'm thinking pandas") with the same prior, and GODS beats GOD. Add SIMS (Bostrom's simulation hypothesis) and SIMS wins decisively. "If we took this approach seriously, we would tell our tenth graders" that simulation is the best explanation. "Think the school board would go for this?"

RRRRR and RBRRB are equally improbable sequences. But RRRRR activates a theory (RED) with non-negligible prior. RBRRB doesn't activate any plausible theory. Our priors are "not flat, but spiky." Under marijuana, every stimulus seems "intensely meaningful" — flat priors. "A very interesting mental state. But it's not conducive to making good inferences."

Conspiracy theories exploit Bayesian mechanics. Evidence against theory T (government inside job)? Add theory U (media and government are in on it). T + U is harder to believe initially but becomes immune to disconfirming data. "Conspiracy theories are the multi-drug-resistant E. coli of the information ecosystem."

Chapter 11: What to Expect When You're Expecting to Win the Lottery

Expected value — the probability- weighted average of all possible outcomes — is not the value you expect. A $1 ticket in a 10-million- combination lottery with a $6 million jackpot has expected value of 60 cents. You will almost certainly win nothing; 60 cents is what you'd average over millions of plays. Adam Smith called lotteries "a tax on the stupid," though his reasoning was flawed — he argued buying more tickets makes you more likely to lose.

Edmond Halley (the comet guy) was among the first to apply expected value rigorously. Parliament was selling life annuities at the same price regardless of the buyer's age — a grandfather paid the same as a child. Halley used mortality statistics to show how absurd this was.

Even when a Powerball jackpot swells past $285 million and raw expected value exceeds the $2 ticket price, the bet stays bad. With 75 million players, there is a 35% chance of splitting the prize. After taxes, lump-sum discount, and garnishment (one winner discovered his current girlfriend didn't know about his ex's child — "the weekend did not go as planned"), expected return falls below breakeven.

The exception: Cash WinFall, a Massachusetts lottery where unjackpotted prize money "rolled down" to lower tiers. On roll-down days, a $2 ticket had expected value of $5.53. James Harvey, an MIT senior, discovered this during an independent study and formed Random Strategies. Medical researcher Ying Zhang quit doctoring to play full-time. Retiree Gerald Selbee, 70, drove from Michigan with his wife Marjorie, buying 60,000 tickets at a time.

If gambling is exciting, you're doing it wrong. Jordan Ellenberg

Random Strategies' masterstroke: August 2010, jackpot at $1.675 million (below the $2M threshold). Other cartels waited. Harvey quietly bought 700,000 tickets over the weekend, pushing the jackpot past $2.1M. His group held 90% of tickets. Profit: $700,000 — a 50% return on a $1.4 million weekend investment.

This was not new. In 18th-century France, La Condamine and Voltaire exploited a botched government lottery, writing cheeky mottoes on tickets: "All men are equal!" They got rich for life. "What — you thought Voltaire made a living writing perfectly realized essays? Then, as now, that's no way to get rich."

The state didn't mind. Massachusetts kept 40 cents of every $2 ticket. The roll-down money was always earmarked as prizes — the state wasn't losing, just paying out accumulated jackpot funds. The more tickets sold, the more 40-cent cuts. "The cartels weren't beating the house. They were the house." The state was the super-casino above them.

Additivity of expected value — E(X+Y) = E(X) + E(Y) — seems obvious but isn't. Substitute "most likely value" and it fails: give a fortune randomly to one of three children. The most likely value for each child is $0 (2/3 chance of nothing). But the most likely value of the SUM is the whole estate. Expected value doesn't work like most-likely value.

Chapter 12: Miss More Planes!

George Stigler, the 1982 Nobel laureate, declared: "If you never miss the plane, you're spending too much time in airports." The optimal strategy isn't zero missed flights — it's whatever miss rate minimizes total discomfort.

The math: arrive two hours early, miss 2% of flights but waste two hours every time. Arrive one hour early, miss 15% but save an hour of tedium. If a missed flight costs 6 utils and each airport hour costs 1, the sweet spot is 90 minutes early (expected cost: -1.8 utils). Even if you hate missing flights (-20 utils), arriving three hours early is worse (-3 utils) than the 90-minute option (-2.5).

The same logic applies to government waste. The SSA paid $31 million to 1,546 dead people — but that is 0.004% of total disbursements. Eliminating the last sliver of waste costs more than the waste itself. "What's the right amount of the taxpayer's money to be wasting?" Not zero.

Pascal's Wager pushes expected value to its limit. If God exists, the reward for belief is infinite. Even tiny probability times infinity is still infinity. You should believe. But the St. Petersburg paradox shows this logic breaks: flip a coin until tails; if tails on flip k, win 2k ducats. Expected value is infinite, yet no sane person would pay a million ducats to play.

Daniel Bernoulli's resolution: utility is not money. A ducat means more to a peasant than to a prince. Utility grows like the logarithm — twice as much money is not twice as good. Under logarithmic utility, the St. Petersburg game is worth exactly 2 utils.

Different people have different utility curves. Greg Mankiw: if taxes go up, he works less (diminishing marginal utility). Like Reagan making fewer movies. Fran Lebowitz: drove a cab until she had rent money, then stopped and wrote. If taxes go up, she works MORE to reach the same threshold. Same policy, opposite behavioral predictions.

Daniel Ellsberg — before the Pentagon Papers — spent years attacking the foundations of expected utility. His paradox: an urn with 30 red balls and 60 black/yellow in unknown ratio. People prefer red (known 1/3) over black (unknown), and NOT-RED (known 2/3) over NOT-BLACK (unknown). But RED + NOT-RED and BLACK + NOT-BLACK both guarantee $100. How can one guarantee be preferred to an identical one?

The answer: people are averse not just to risk (known unknowns) but to uncertainty (unknown unknowns). Expected utility theory cannot capture the difference. In the world of Cold War RAND, "that those tools might actually be limited... especially in contexts for which there was no precedent — like, say, the instantaneous reduction of the human race to radioactive dust — must have been at least a little troubling."

Chapter 13: Where the Train Tracks Meet

Selbee used Quic Pic (random numbers) for his Cash WinFall tickets. Harvey picked by hand. Same expected value — so why bother? The answer is variance. A $50,000 sure thing and a 50/50 bet on losing $100,000 or gaining $200,000 have the same EV, but the bet can ruin you.

"Picking up pennies in front of a steamroller" — 99% chance of making $1 million, 1% chance of losing $50 million. Positive EV but lethal variance. Scale up with leverage: "if you're down a million, it's your problem; if you're down five billion, it's the government's problem." Long-Term Capital Management. The 2008 crisis.

Harvey's hand-picked tickets exploited gorgeous mathematics. In a simplified 7-ball lottery (pick 3, win also for matching 2), 35 combos exist. Selbee's 7 random tickets: expected 2.4 deuces but wide variance (5.3% chance of zero). Harvey's 7 tickets — 124, 135, 167, 257, 347, 236, 456 — guarantee either jackpot or exactly 3 deuces. Same expected value, drastically lower variance.

The secret: those seven triples are the seven lines of the Fano plane, the smallest projective geometry. Every pair of points lies on exactly one line, so every pair of winning numbers appears on exactly one ticket. The same structure underlies Richard Hamming's error-correcting codes: a 4-bit message gets 3 check bits, making a 7-bit codeword where single errors can always be corrected.

Claude Shannon founded information theory in 1948, proving that below a channel's capacity, codes exist that make errors arbitrarily rare. Shannon was also a legendary tinkerer — he built a flame-throwing trumpet and a machine whose sole purpose was to turn itself off when you turned it on.

Buffon's needle problem: drop a needle of length 1 on a floor with parallel lines spaced 1 apart. Probability it crosses a line = 2/π. Barbier's elegant proof uses additivity of expected value: a circle of diameter 1 always crosses exactly 2 lines. So expected crossings = πp, and since it's always 2: p = 2/π. "Throw a mass of spaghetti on the tile floor and I can tell you exactly how many times to expect a strand to cross a line."

Alexander Grothendieck described mathematical progress as the sea rising around a stone: "Nothing seems to happen, nothing moves... yet it finally surrounds the resistant substance." The geometry of Renaissance painting (parallel tracks meeting at a vanishing point), lottery strategy, and deep-space communication are all the same mathematics — projective geometry and combinatorial designs.

Barbier published his brilliant proof at 21, left town with a nervous condition by 25, and was found in a mental asylum 15 years later. Grothendieck left academia in the 1980s for Pyrenean seclusion. David Foster Wallace called this "the Math Melodrama myth" — that math drives you crazy. Ellenberg disagrees: "Mathematics tends to strengthen the mind rather than strain it to its breaking point."

Chapter 14: The Triumph of Mediocrity

Francis Galton, Darwin's half-cousin and Victorian polymath, planted sweet peas sorted by weight and discovered something strange: offspring of heavy seeds were heavy but not as heavy. Offspring of light seeds were light but not as light. The distribution "regressed" toward the average. He found the same pattern in human height and invented the scatterplot to display it.

The crucial insight: this is not a force pulling things toward mediocrity. It is an arithmetic necessity whenever two variables are correlated but not perfectly so. If the correlation between parent and child height is 0.5, a parent 4 inches above average will have a child on average only 2 inches above average. The slope of the regression line is less than 1. That flattened slope IS regression to the mean.

Horace Secrist's 1933 book The Triumph of Mediocrity in Business tracked top firms over time and found they became more average. He interpreted this as competition driving firms toward mediocrity. Harold Hotelling had the devastating rebuttal: if you select the top stores of 1922 and look BACKWARD to 1916, they were also closer to mediocre — because in 1922 they were both lucky and good, but their 1916 luck was different. If regression were caused by competitive forces, those forces would have to work backward in time.

Secrist wrote a contentious rebuttal; Hotelling stopped being nice: "To 'prove' such a mathematical result by a costly and prolonged numerical study... is analogous to proving the multiplication table by arranging elephants in rows and columns, and then doing the same for numerous other kinds of animals."

Biologists are eager to think regression stems from biology, management theorists want it to come from competition, literary critics ascribe it to creative exhaustion — but it is none of these. It is mathematics. Jordan Ellenberg

Every April, ESPN reports who is "on pace" for record seasons. Matt Kemp hit 9 home runs in 17 games — projected to 86. Derek Jeter told the New York Times: "One of the worst phrases in sports is 'on pace for.'" Ellenberg checked first-half AL home run leaders from 1976-2000: sluggers hit only 60% as many homers in the second half. The "Home Run Derby Curse" is not a curse. It is arithmetic.

A 1976 British Medical Journal paper on bran and diverticular disease found bran had a "remarkable regularizing effect" — fast transit times slowed toward 48 hours, slow ones sped up. But this is exactly what you'd expect if bran had NO effect. Everyone has fast and slow days; an unusually quick Monday will likely be followed by a more average Tuesday.

The Scared Straight program selected juvenile offenders for being the WORST behaved, then reported they improved afterward. Regression guarantees improvement whether you intervene or not. When randomized trials were conducted, the program actually increased antisocial behavior. "Maybe it should have been called Scared Stupid."

Running backs who sign big contracts after a great season record fewer yards per carry the next year. Second albums by breakout bands disappoint. Sophomore novels underperform. Artistic success is an amalgam of talent and fortune — and fortune doesn't persist. "There's something the mind resists about regression to the mean. We want to believe in a force that brings down the mighty."

Chapter 15: Galton's Ellipse

Galton needed to visualize the interplay of heredity and chance. He plotted father-son height data on a grid — each pair a point, x-axis for father, y-axis for son — and drew curves of equal density: isopleths. To his astonishment, they were all ellipses, nested concentrically around the point of average height for both. The scatterplot was the contour map of a perfectly elliptical mountain — the bivariate normal distribution.

The eccentricity (skinniness) of the ellipse measures how strongly one variable determines the other. Very skinny: high correlation, heredity dominant. Round: low correlation, chance dominant. A line is the most eccentric ellipse possible. A circle means zero correlation. Galton called his measure correlation, using the eccentricity — a geometric quantity dating to Apollonius of Perga in the third century BCE — to quantify the association between variables.

Alphonse Bertillon had devised a system for identifying criminals by body measurements filed on cards. Galton asked whether adding more measurements always helped. Not necessarily: men with big hands tend to have big feet. Correlated measurements provide diminishing returns. The best measurements are those uncorrelated with each other. Galton championed fingerprinting instead, which proved its worth when Vincenzo Peruggia stole the Mona Lisa in 1911 — his Bertillon card was useless, but his fingerprint on the frame would have identified him immediately.

Karl Pearson generalized correlation to any variables, revealing that it is the cosine of the angle between two vectors. Perpendicular vectors have zero correlation — "orthogonal," a word creeping into wider English. Justice Scalia encountered it during oral argument and was delighted. Ellenberg roots for its adoption: "It's been a while since a mathy word really broke out into demotic English."

Correlation is not transitive. Rich states vote Democratic. Rich people tend to live in rich states. But rich people still vote more Republican within most states. Andrew Gelman showed the story is nuanced: in Texas and Wisconsin, richer counties vote more Republican. In Maryland, California, and New York, richer counties vote more Democratic — and those are where pundits live.

The same non-transitivity explains why niacin raises HDL cholesterol but doesn't prevent heart attacks. A large NIH trial (halted 2011) confirmed the failure: it boosts HDL through a mechanism unrelated to the one connecting HDL to heart health. This is the surrogate endpoint problem — using a biomarker as a stand-in for the actual outcome. Hormone replacement therapy had the same story: estrogen correlated with lower heart disease, HRT raises estrogen, but the Women's Health Initiative found HRT increased heart disease.

Wisconsin weather stations illustrate signal vs. noise. Thirteen stations within an hour's drive: the warmest in January 2011 regressed toward the middle by January 2012, and so did the coldest. Classic regression. But ten California cities spread widely apart showed almost no regression (correlation 0.989) — the inherent temperature differences (signal) swamp year-to-year fluctuations (noise).

Galton coined "eugenics" and was its most prominent advocate. Ellenberg's reflection: "Mathematics is a way not to be wrong, but it isn't a way not to be wrong about everything." Mathematical skill can breed overconfidence that extends to areas where you're still wrong. "We become like those pious people who accumulate a sense of their own virtuousness so powerful as to make them believe the bad things they do are virtuous too."

Chapter 16: Does Lung Cancer Make You Smoke Cigarettes?

At the turn of the 20th century, lung cancer was vanishingly rare. By 1947, it was nearly a fifth of cancer deaths among British men — a fifteenfold increase. Richard Doll and A. Bradford Hill studied 649 male lung cancer patients in 20 London hospitals: only TWO were nonsmokers. A control group had 27 nonsmokers.

The correlation was overwhelming. But Doll and Hill wrote that the association would also occur "if carcinoma of the lung caused people to smoke or if both attributes were end-effects of a common cause." Cancer causing smoking seems absurd — but the common cause problem is real.

R.A. Fisher, founding hero of statistics, proposed the "constitutional hypothesis": a genetic factor might predispose people both to lung cancer and to the urge to smoke. The pre-cancerous state involves chronic inflammation, which might drive people to seek the soothing comfort of cigarettes. "To take the poor chap's cigarettes away from him would be rather like taking away his white stick from a blind man." Fisher was both a rigorous statistician and a lifelong smoker.

Epidemiologist Jan Vandenbroucke: "I found extremely well-written and cogent papers that might have become textbook classics... if only the authors had been on the right side." By 1964, Surgeon General Luther Terry's commission concluded smoking caused lung cancer, based on converging evidence: consistent association, dose-response, cancer at point of contact (cigarettes → lung cancer, pipes → lip cancer), and ex-smokers having less cancer.

If you never give advice until you're sure it's right, you're not giving enough advice. Jordan Ellenberg

Policy makers cannot wait for certainty. Jerry Cornfield called the randomized experiment that would definitively prove causation "possible to conceive but impossible to conduct" — force random teenagers to smoke for 50 years. An eggplant analogy: if studies show 75% probability that eggplant causes heart failure, recommending against it saves an expected 700 lives per year even if wrong 25% of the time.

Berkson's fallacy shows how spurious correlations arise from conditioning on a common effect. In hospitals, non-diabetics are there because of their blood pressure, so 100% have high BP — creating a phantom correlation that doesn't exist in the general population.

The same logic explains why handsome men seem like jerks. Niceness and handsomeness are independent in the general population. But your dating pool — the "Acceptable Triangle" — screens for one or the other. Within that pool, the handsomest men run the full personality gamut, but the nicest men are only averagely handsome (ugly nice guys barely squeezed in). The negative correlation is real in your experience but a pure artifact of selection.

Literary snobbery works the same way: popular novels seem terrible not because masses have bad taste but because the only novels you hear about are either popular OR good. Force yourself to read random unpopular novels and you'll find they're just as bad as the popular ones.

Chapter 17: There Is No Such Thing as Public Opinion

In January 2011, 77% of Americans said cutting spending was the best way to handle the deficit. But when asked about 13 specific categories, in 11 of them more people wanted to increase spending. Only foreign aid and unemployment insurance — under 5% of the budget combined — drew the ax. Paul Krugman: "People want spending cut, but are opposed to cuts in anything except foreign aid."

The paradox is not that Americans are stupid. Suppose 1/3 want to raise taxes, 1/3 want to cut defense, 1/3 want to cut Medicare. "Should we cut spending?" — 2/3 say yes. "Cut defense?" — 2/3 say no (tax raisers + Medicare cutters). "Cut Medicare?" — 2/3 say no. The "infantile average American" who demands everything doesn't exist. What exists is a population with diverse, coherent preferences that aggregate into incoherence.

The ACA illustrates perfectly: 52% opposed Obamacare. But 37% wanted repeal, 10% weakening, 15% keeping, 36% expanding. Leave it, kill it, or strengthen it — each opposed by a majority. Fox News: "Majority opposes Obamacare!" MSNBC: "Majority wants to preserve or strengthen!" Both true. Both incomplete.

Florida 2000: Bush 48.85%, Gore 48.84%, Nader 1.6%. Virtually every Nader voter preferred Gore to Bush. A solid 51% majority preferred Gore, but Nader's presence as an "irrelevant alternative" gave the election to Bush. Even slime molds fall for this: Physarum polycephalum splits evenly between two food options, but adding a clearly inferior third changes the ratio three-to-one.

The Borda count (rank candidates, assign points) would have elected Gore. But it has its own problems. Instant-runoff voting eliminates the last-place candidate and redistributes ballots. Burlington, Vermont tried it in 2009: Kurt Wright (R) led first-round votes, Andy Montroll (D) was everyone's second choice, Bob Kiss (Progressive) won under IRV. But head-to-head, Montroll beat both. The centrist everyone liked was eliminated first. Traditional method → Wright. IRV → Kiss. Head-to-head → Montroll. Three methods, three winners.

These are Condorcet paradoxes, discovered by the Marquis de Condorcet — "le mouton enragé" (the rabid sheep). A quiet man who believed in "social mathematics": that the social world could be analyzed by equations. His jury theorem proved that large enough juries with better-than-random individual accuracy converge on the truth. He and Jefferson were kindred spirits. John Adams dismissed him as "a quack" and "mathematical charlatan."

Condorcet insisted — almost alone — that the Rights of Man belonged to women too. Forced into hiding during the Terror, he wrote his astonishingly optimistic Sketch for a Historical Picture of the Progress of the Human Mind, predicting a world free of prejudice and hunger. Captured in March 1794, dead within two days.

Kenneth Arrow's impossibility theorem (1951 PhD thesis, eventual Nobel) proved that no voting system satisfies even a modest set of fairness criteria. "Condorcet's idea is so thoroughly intertwined with the modern way of doing political business that we hardly see it as a choice. But it is a choice. I think it's the right one."

Chapter 18: "Out of Nothing I Have Created a Strange New Universe"

For two thousand years, mathematicians tried to prove Euclid's fifth axiom — the parallel postulate — from the first four. It felt inelegant, like a stain on an otherwise perfect floor. Farkas Bolyai wasted years and begged his son János not to follow: "I have traversed this bottomless night, which extinguished all light and joy in my life."

János ignored his father. By 1823 he had the answer: what if the postulate were false? No contradiction follows. Another geometry exists where the first four axioms hold but the fifth does not. "Out of nothing I have created a strange new universe." Simultaneously, Lobachevskii reached the same conclusion in Russia. Gauss had formulated similar ideas privately; told of Bolyai's work, he said: "To praise it would amount to praising myself."

Riemann observed a simpler non-Euclidean geometry: the sphere. Lines are great circles. The first four axioms hold, but the fifth fails spectacularly — there are NO parallel lines. In Bolyai's hyperbolic geometry, the opposite: through any external point pass infinitely many parallels. If the fifth axiom could be derived from the first four, spheres would harbor a contradiction. They don't. So the axiom is independent.

The practical consequence is formalism: ask not what mathematical objects "are" but what the axioms define them to be. A point can be a frog, a kumquat — anything satisfying the rules. Any theorem proved from the first four axioms is automatically true in every geometry. Scalia is the fiercest legal formalist: "Long live formalism. It is what makes a government a government of laws and not of men."

Bill Klem, "the Old Arbitrator" of baseball umpiring: "It ain't nothin' till I call it" — the bluntest ontological assertion ever made by a sports official. In 1996, twelve-year-old Jeffrey Maier reached over the wall and pulled in Derek Jeter's fly ball. Not a home run. But umpire Rich Garcia called it one. Baseball is formalist: it's a homer because the umpire says so.

A scientist can hardly encounter anything more undesirable than, just as a work is completed, to have its foundation give way. Gottlob Frege

David Hilbert dreamed of rebuilding all mathematics from bedrock axioms and proving the edifice consistent. Then Russell wrote to Frege with a paradox: the set of all sets that don't contain themselves either does or doesn't contain itself, and both answers produce contradiction. Frege's postscript recording the disaster may be "the saddest sentence ever written in a technical work." In 1931, Gödel's second incompleteness theorem proved no finitary proof of arithmetic's consistency could exist. Hilbert's program died.

The cult of genius that surrounds Ramanujan — prodigy from southern India, theorems from goddess Namagiri — is misleading. He is famous precisely because he is so unusual. The cult damages math education: students quit because someone in class is ahead of them. "I've never heard a student say, 'I like Hamlet, but that kid in the front row knows all the plays.'" What we need are "more math majors who DON'T become mathematicians."

"Hardworking" is not a veiled insult. Grit is a genuine skill. Poincaré's breakthrough on Fuchsian functions came stepping onto a bus — but only after weeks of intense labor. There is always someone ahead of you. Fields Medalists look to the dead greats. "And yet, in the last hundred years, the joined effort of all these dummies-compared-to-Gauss has produced the greatest flowering of mathematical knowledge the world has ever seen." Terry Tao: "The popular image of the lone genius... is a charming and romantic image, but also a wildly inaccurate one." Genius is not a kind of person. "Genius is a thing that happens."

Conclusion: How to Be Right

Between his sophomore and junior years, Ellenberg modeled tuberculosis prevalence through 2050 for a public health researcher. Each empirical input had small uncertainties — maybe transmission rate was 20%, maybe 13%, maybe 25%. These percolated through feedback loops until by 2050 the model could predict anything from zero TB to universal infection.

The researcher didn't want to hear the model was useless. "Just give me your best guess." Ellenberg gave in. "I have no doubt he told many people, afterward, that X million people were going to have tuberculosis in 2050. And I'll bet if anyone asked how he knew, he would say, I hired a guy who did the math."

Theodore Roosevelt's Sorbonne speech declares "it is not the critic who counts." But Abraham Wald "went his whole life without lifting a weapon in anger, but who nonetheless played a serious part in the American war effort, precisely by counseling the doers of deeds how to do them better. He was unsweaty, undusty, and unbloody, but he was right. He was a critic who counted."

Against Roosevelt's certainty, Ellenberg places John Ashbery's poem "Soonest Mended": "For this is action, this not being sure, this careless / Preparing, sowing the seeds crooked in the furrow." Not being sure is the move of a strong person. Roosevelt and the Housemartins — "the greatest Marxist pop band ever to pick up guitars" — would call it cowardly. Ashbery says it's "a kind of fence-sitting / Raised to the level of an esthetic ideal."

Mathematics gives us a way of being unsure in a principled way: "I'm not sure, this is why I'm not sure, and this is roughly how not-sure I am."

The paladin of principled uncertainty is Nate Silver, "a kind of Kurt Cobain of probability." Traditional pundits said "Obama will win" or "Romney will win." Silver said: "Either one might win, but Obama is substantially more likely." Politico's Dylan Byers complained Silver "often gives the impression of hedging." "What Silver offers isn't hedging; it's honesty." He expected to get about three states wrong. He got all fifty right.

W.O.V. Quine: "A reasonable person believes each of his beliefs to be true; yet experience has taught him to expect that some of his beliefs, he knows not which, will turn out to be false." This parallels the budget paradox — Americans think each program is worth funding but not all programs. Silver presented probability distributions, not point predictions. "Silver was being uncertain, rigorously uncertain, in public, and the public ate it up."

To do mathematics is to be, at once, touched by fire and bound by reason. This is no contradiction. Logic forms a narrow channel through which intuition flows with vastly augmented force. Jordan Ellenberg

Common mathematical folk advice: prove by day, disprove by night. Topologist R.H. Bing allegedly split each month between proving the Poincaré Conjecture and seeking a counterexample. Each failed attempt to disprove hits a wall; those walls accumulate into a structure — the structure of the proof. Bolyai tried to prove the parallel postulate. He failed. But he understood what was blocking him: the existence of a geometry without the parallel postulate.

The lessons of mathematics are simple and there are no numbers in them. There is structure in the world. We can hope to understand some of it. Our intuition is stronger with a formal exoskeleton than without. "When are you going to use this? You've been using mathematics since you were born and you'll probably never stop. Use it well."

Trading & Investing Lessons

Survivorship Bias in Fund Selection

Wald's bullet-hole insight applies directly to evaluating investment performance. Morningstar's Large Blend funds returned 178% average from 1995-2004 — but only counting survivors. Including dead funds, the return drops to 134%. Any time you evaluate a track record, ask how many chances the manager had to produce it.

Regression to the Mean in Performance Chasing

Last year's best fund is like the first-half home run leader — skill plus luck, where only skill persists. Running backs who sign big contracts after a great season record fewer yards the next year. The same arithmetic governs stock-picking contests and hedge fund of the year awards.

Variance Matters More Than Expected Value

A positive-EV trade that can wipe you out is worse than a lower-EV trade with manageable variance. Cash WinFall's cartels reduced variance through combinatorial ticket selection. Diversification in a portfolio serves exactly the same function.

Non-Transitivity of Correlation

A biomarker correlates with health. A drug correlates with the biomarker. But the drug may not correlate with health. The same applies to factor investing: momentum correlates with returns, a new ETF correlates with momentum, but the mechanism matters.

False Linearity in Projections

"Everyone will be overweight by 2048" is the epidemiological version of "this stock will grow at 15% forever." Every straight-line projection eventually curves. The question is when.

The Value of Principled Uncertainty

Nate Silver's approach — stating probabilities, not predictions — is exactly what good risk management requires. A trade thesis with 65% conviction is not hedging; it's honesty. Size positions to match your actual confidence.

Key Takeaways

Mathematics Is Extended Common Sense

Not arbitrary rules but common sense formalized and sharpened. Without rigor, the officers armor the wrong parts. Without common sense, math is sterile computation.

Not Every Curve Is a Line

Linear extrapolation is the default mode of human reasoning and the source of most analytical errors. Straight locally, curved globally.

Significance Is Not Truth

A p-value below 0.05 means the result would be unlikely if the null were true. With enough tests, wiggle room, or data, anything becomes significant. The dead salmon proves it.

Regression to the Mean Is Arithmetic

Not a force, not biology, not competition. Whenever two variables are correlated but imperfectly, extreme values in one correspond to less extreme values in the other.

Correlation Is Not Causation or Transitivity

A correlates with B, B with C, but A and C may be unrelated. Niacin raises HDL but doesn't prevent heart attacks. Rich states vote Democratic but rich people vote Republican.

There Is No Perfect Voting System

Arrow's theorem proves it. Condorcet's paradox demonstrates it. Recognizing this is mathematical maturity, not cynicism.

Be Rigorously Uncertain

"I'm not sure, this is why, and this is roughly how not-sure I am." Principled uncertainty is more honest, useful, and powerful than false certainty.

Contrarian Ideas

The 0.05 Threshold Is Arbitrary and Harmful

Fisher never intended p = 0.05 as a rigid cutoff. It rewards p-hacking, punishes honest uncertainty, and fills journals with results that are statistically significant but practically meaningless.

Close Elections Should Be Decided by Coin Flip

When the margin is smaller than the noise, the "winner" is determined by chance anyway. A coin flip would at least be honest about it.

More Data Can Make Predictions Worse

Each additional input introduces its own uncertainty. In chaotic systems, these compound through feedback loops until the model can predict anything.

Being Wrong Is Sometimes the Right Policy

If eggplant is 75% likely to cause heart failure, recommending against it saves 700 expected lives per year even when wrong 25% of the time.

Genius Is Not a Person but an Event

The greatest mathematical flowering came from the collective efforts of many, none of whom were Gauss. "It takes a thousand men to invent a telegraph, and the last man gets the credit."

Proposals & Frameworks

The Four Quadrants of Mathematical Ideas

Simple/Shallow (1+2=3), Complicated/Shallow (ten-digit multiplication), Complicated/Profound (Riemann Hypothesis), Simple/Profound — where this book lives.

Reductio Ad Unlikely

Assume the null, show the data is very improbable, reject the null. Sound but leaky — impossible and improbable are not the same.

Bayesian Updating

Prior → evidence → posterior. How much the evidence moves you depends on where you started.

Expected Value + Variance

EV tells you the average outcome. Variance tells you the scatter. A positive-EV strategy with catastrophic variance can ruin you. Both numbers together, never one alone.

Prove by Day, Disprove by Night

Work hard to prove your thesis, then spend the evening destroying it. The walls you hit become the structure of the proof.

What Didn't Fit

The Pythagoreans Were Extremely Weird

Beyond discovering irrational numbers, the cult believed odd numbers were good and even evil, that a hidden planet called Antichthon lay behind the sun, and eating beans was forbidden because they housed dead souls. Pythagoras himself was said to talk to cattle. Hippasus, who discovered √2 was irrational, was reportedly thrown into the sea. "But you can't drown a theorem." The proof survived in Euclid's Elements while the cult faded into eccentricity.

Yitang Zhang's Improbable Breakthrough

Star student at Beijing University, PhD in the US, career stalled, worked at Subway, hadn't published since 2001. A former student got him a lectureship at UNH. In 2013 he proved bounded gaps — infinitely many prime pairs within fixed distance. The biggest names had failed. A reminder that in mathematics, truth doesn't care about your CV.

Gödel's Citizenship Exam

Preparing for his 1948 US exam, Gödel found a constitutional contradiction allowing dictatorship. Einstein and Morgenstern begged him not to raise it. The examiner said: "This could not happen here." Gödel: "Oh, yes, I can prove it." The examiner hurriedly changed the subject. Nobody knows which flaw he found.

Hilbert's Humanity and Tragedy

Hilbert refused to sign the 1914 Declaration defending Germany's war, saying he "was unable to verify that the assertions were not true." When Göttingen resisted hiring Emmy Noether because she was a woman: "We are a university, not a bathhouse." In old age, his first PhD student Otto Blumenthal — removed from Aachen by the Nazis — visited. Hilbert couldn't understand why Blumenthal no longer lectured. Blumenthal later died in Theresienstadt.

Cauchy vs. the Engineers

When Cauchy figured out how to do calculus without Newton's "ghosts," he rewrote his syllabus at the École Polytechnique. The school imposed a new curriculum and placed monitors in his classroom. Cauchy ignored them. An unwavering conservative in politics, proudly revolutionary in his mathematics. "He was not interested in the needs of engineers. He was interested in the truth."

How Not to Be Wrong: The Power of Mathematical Thinking

Jordan Ellenberg · The Penguin Press, 2014 · 468 pages

A mathematician's guide to the simple, profound ideas that shape how we reason about everything — and how to avoid the errors that lead us astray.

About the Author

Jordan Ellenberg is the John D. MacArthur Professor of Mathematics at the University of Wisconsin-Madison. A child prodigy who competed in the International Mathematical Olympiad at sixteen, he earned his PhD from Harvard and has published research spanning number theory, algebraic geometry, and combinatorics.

Beyond academia, Ellenberg writes the "Do the Math" column for Slate and has contributed to the New York Times, Washington Post, and Wall Street Journal. How Not to Be Wrong was a New York Times bestseller, and his follow-up Shape (2021) continued the project of making geometry feel urgent and alive.

Why This Book Matters

Most popular math books are either accessible-but- shallow or profound-but-impenetrable. Ellenberg occupies the rare space between: genuinely deep ideas — survivorship bias, Bayesian reasoning, regression to the mean, the impossibility of perfect democracy — presented through stories anyone can follow.

In an era of data-driven decisions, the traps Ellenberg identifies — p-hacking, false linearity, confusing correlation with causation, ignoring survivorship bias — are more relevant than ever. The argument that mathematical thinking is essential to citizenship is as compelling as any case for liberal education ever made.
