Prologue: What Is an “Innocent Fraud”?

The term comes from John Kenneth Galbraith’s last book, The Economics of Innocent Fraud (2004). “Innocent” means the people perpetuating the fraud are not only wrong but too ignorant to understand what they are doing. They believe their own falsehoods. James K. Galbraith (J.K.’s son) writes the foreword, calling Mosler “a rare bird: a self-taught economist who is not a crank.”

Mosler frames the book against the backdrop of two dominant economic schools: Keynesian (fiscal policy—government spending and tax cuts to manage the economy) and Monetarist (Milton Friedman— balanced budgets and controlling the money supply). He notes that Volcker proved in the early 1980s that you can’t actually control the money supply, so monetary policy was quietly redefined as setting interest rates rather than controlling money quantity. And every time a recession hits, politicians inevitably reach for Keynesian fiscal policy (tax cuts and spending increases) regardless of their monetarist rhetoric. Nixon himself declared “We are all Keynesians now” in 1973.

The “Great Inflation” of the 1970s, which discredited Keynesianism in the public mind, was not caused by government overspending. It was cost-push inflation from the OPEC cartel raising oil prices from $2 to $40 per barrel. It was broken not by Volcker’s interest rate hikes but by natural gas deregulation in 1978 and the resulting supply response. The monetarist victory narrative is a myth.

Mosler ran for U.S. Senate from Connecticut in 2010 with three specific proposals that the book explains and defends: (1) a full payroll tax holiday, suspending all FICA deductions, giving the average working couple over $650/month in extra take-home pay; (2) $500 per capita in unrestricted revenue sharing to state governments; and (3) a federally-funded $8/hour transition job for anyone willing and able to work. The central question: “How are you going to pay for it?” That is what the seven frauds answer.

Fraud #1: The Government Must Tax or Borrow to Spend

This is the master fraud from which all the others flow. The widespread belief is that the federal government operates like a household—it must first collect revenue (through taxes or borrowing) before it can spend. Mosler demonstrates that this is operationally false. The U.S. government, as the sole issuer of the dollar, spends by changing numbers upward in bank accounts. When the Social Security Administration sends you a payment, your bank balance goes from $3,000 to $5,000. Where did the money come from? Nowhere. It was data entry on what Mosler calls “the U.S. dollar monetary system spreadsheet.”

Conversely, when you pay taxes, the government doesn’t “get” anything usable. If you pay by check, a number in your account goes down. If you pay in cash, the bills are literally shredded after collection—like a Super Bowl ticket torn up at the gate. The government has no more use for the cash it collects than the stadium has for the torn ticket stubs.

Mosler cites Fed Chairman Ben Bernanke on 60 Minutes confirming this: “It’s not tax money. The banks have accounts with the Fed… we simply use the computer to mark up the size of the account.” The chairman of the Federal Reserve, on national television, confirming that government spending is just data entry.

The critical insight is that the government must spend FIRST before it can tax. The dollars people use to pay taxes had to come from somewhere—and that somewhere is government spending (or government lending). It’s like parents who create coupons to pay their children for chores, then tax the children 10 coupons per week. The parents cannot collect coupons they haven’t first distributed.

So why does the government tax at all, if it doesn’t need the money? Two reasons. First, taxes create demand for the currency. People need dollars to pay their tax obligation, so they sell goods, services, and labor for dollars. This is how the government provisions itself—the same mechanism the British used with the “hut tax” in colonial Africa, where imposing a tax payable only in British coins instantly monetized the economy. Second, taxes regulate aggregate demand—removing spending power to prevent inflation. “Taxes function to regulate the economy, and not to get money for Congress to spend.”

The self-imposed constraints—debt ceiling, Treasury overdraft rules, Fed restrictions—are all created by Congress and are entirely counterproductive. They are political blockages, not operational necessities. Mosler recounts his talk in Australia where David Gruen of the Reserve Bank conceded after debate: solvency is not the issue; only inflation and currency value matter. But crucially, Mosler adds the caveat: “This does NOT mean the government can spend all it wants without consequence. Over-spending can drive up prices and fuel inflation.”

Fraud #2: Deficits Leave a Debt Burden to Our Children

The “right” level of taxes, Mosler argues, has nothing to do with matching spending. You first set government spending at whatever level is needed to provision public purpose (defense, education, infrastructure, etc.) based on real resource costs. Then you set taxes at whatever level leaves the private sector with just enough spending power to purchase the remaining output without causing inflation. If that means a deficit of 5% of GDP—as Mosler suggests may be the normal case—so be it. The deficit is a residual, not a target.

The conventional story is that every dollar of deficit today is a dollar our children must repay tomorrow. Mosler demolishes this with a simple question: “When our children build 15 million cars per year 20 years from now, will they have to send them back in time to 2008 to pay off their debt?” We don’t send goods back to 1945 to pay off World War II debt. Future generations consume whatever they can produce with the technology and resources available to them. The national debt number has zero bearing on their productive capacity.

What are Treasury securities, really? Just savings accounts at the Fed. When you buy a Treasury bond, you move dollars from your checking account (reserves) to your savings account (securities) at the Fed. When the bond matures, the Fed moves the dollars back. The $13 trillion national debt is simply the total holdings in savings accounts at the Federal Reserve. “Paying off the debt” means moving numbers from savings columns to checking columns. It is a non-event for the real economy.

What about “paying off China”? China has a reserve account at the Fed. When China sells us $1 billion in uniforms, the Fed credits China’s checking account. China buys Treasuries (moves money to savings). At maturity, the Fed moves it back to checking. No real resources are ever transferred. China can only spend its dollars by buying things from willing sellers at market prices. And what if China refuses to buy Treasuries? It doesn’t matter—they can leave the dollars in their checking account. There are no negative consequences for a government that understands its own monetary system.

The real burden on children is not the debt number—it’s lost output from unemployment TODAY, cutting education TODAY, failing to invest in productivity TODAY. Mosler points out a striking historical pattern: the United States has run budget surpluses only six times in over 200 years, and each of those six surplus periods was followed by one of the only six depressions in American history. Surpluses drain private savings, and the bust follows.

Fraud #3: Government Deficits Take Away Savings

The mainstream view—championed by everyone from Lawrence Summers to Robert Rubin—is that government deficits “crowd out” private savings and investment. Mosler shows this is not just wrong but exactly backwards. Every dollar of government deficit adds exactly one dollar to non-government sector savings. Not approximately—exactly, to the penny. This is not theory or ideology; it is basic accounting identity from national income accounting.

The step-by-step mechanics of a $100 billion deficit: the government sells $100 billion in Treasuries (the buyer moves money from checking to savings at the Fed, so their total savings are unchanged—just shifted from cash to securities). Then the government spends $100 billion, adding it to someone’s checking account. The non-government sector now has the original $100 billion in checking accounts PLUS $100 billion in new Treasury securities. Net result: $100 billion added to total private savings.

The anecdotes in this chapter are devastating. Mosler met with Senator Daschle and Assistant Treasury Secretary Summers. Summers insisted “the deficit takes away savings that could be used for investment.” Mosler explained the paradox of thrift. Summers replied: “Well, I really don’t understand reserve accounting, so I can’t discuss it at that level.” The next day Summers was on television with the Concord Coalition warning about deficits. The Assistant Treasury Secretary of the United States, admitting he doesn’t understand how the monetary system works, and then going on to make policy pronouncements about it anyway.

Robert Rubin fared no better. At a Citibank meeting around 2000, Mosler explained that when the government runs a surplus, we have to sell our securities to the Fed to get the money to pay taxes, and our savings go down. Rubin’s response: “No, I think you’re wrong.” The economy crashed shortly after. Al Gore, at a fundraiser, agreed with Mosler that the projected $5.6 trillion surplus would actually manifest as a $5.6 trillion deficit because the private sector’s savings desires averaged about 5% of GDP. Gore said the logic made sense but that he “couldn’t go there” politically.

The Wall Street Journal in July 1999 captured the absurdity perfectly: the left-hand headline praised the Clinton surplus while the right-hand headline lamented the declining savings rate. The accompanying graph showed the two as perfect mirror images. Surplus up, savings down. No one connected the dots. Professor Wynne Godley of Cambridge—the most successful British economy forecaster—used “sector analysis” to demonstrate this exact relationship: the government deficit equals the savings of the other sectors. Even his remarkable forecasting track record could not convince the mainstream. Abba Lerner’s “Functional Finance” framework from the 1940s made the same point: what matters is output and employment, not the deficit number.

Fraud #4: Social Security Is Broken

The entire Social Security “crisis” rests on the assumption that the government might not have the money to make payments. But as established in Fraud #1, government checks don’t bounce. The government spends by changing numbers in bank accounts. The Social Security “trust fund” is just record-keeping. Even if the trust fund number goes to zero or negative, the government can still make every payment—it just changes numbers on its spreadsheet.

What about privatization? Mosler walks through the macro-level accounting and shows it is a non-event. If workers stop buying Social Security bonds and buy stocks instead, the stock sellers receive cash and use it to buy the newly-issued government bonds. Net result: the same total stocks and bonds exist in public hands—they just changed ownership. No effect on the economy, savings, or investment. The only difference is who bears the risk: individual workers instead of the government. Mosler debated Steve Moore of the CATO Institute, walking him through each logical step. Moore conceded every point but concluded: “I believe in privatization.” Ideology trumping logic.

The REAL Social Security challenge is the dependency ratio—fewer workers supporting more retirees. But this is a productivity problem, not a money problem. The question is whether one worker in 50 years will be productive enough to support herself and her share of the retired population. Money doesn’t solve real resource constraints. What matters is investing in education and technology NOW. Mosler illustrates with the fallacy of composition: one person standing at a football game can see better, but if everyone stands, nobody does. Individual benefit from privatization does not generalize.

Fraud #5: The Trade Deficit Is Unsustainable

Conventional wisdom treats the trade deficit as a national weakness—we’re “losing” to our trading partners. Mosler flips this completely: “The real wealth of a nation is all it produces and keeps for itself, plus all it imports, minus what it must export.” Imports are real benefits (we get stuff). Exports are real costs (we give stuff away). A trade deficit means we’re getting more stuff than we’re giving up.

The Japan analogy is Mosler’s masterpiece of perspective-flipping. If General MacArthur had ordered Japan to send 2 million cars per year to the United States as war reparations, it would have been seen as exploitation of the vanquished. Yet Japan has voluntarily done exactly this for 60 years, and somehow they think they’re “winning” the trade war. China fills our stores with products and gets nothing in return but a bank statement from the Fed showing their dollar balance. And our leaders think we’re losing?

The notion that the US “borrows from abroad” is backwards. When you borrow from a US bank to buy a Chinese car, the bank creates the deposit that funds China’s dollar savings. It is domestic credit funding foreign savings, not the reverse. The US does not depend on foreign capital. Foreign savers have their dollars in our system because they want to play in our sandbox. Their options: do nothing, buy financial assets from willing sellers, or buy real goods from willing sellers. Both parties in every trade are happy—it is voluntary exchange. There is no “imbalance.”

Jobs lost from trade do not equal jobs lost overall. With the right fiscal policy—adequate spending power in the economy—full employment is always achievable regardless of trade deficits. Jobs shift between industries, but total employment depends on aggregate demand, which the government controls through taxation and spending.

Mosler also dismantles the notion of “dependence on foreign capital.” Foreign savers hold dollars in our system because they WANT to—it’s our spreadsheet and they have to play in our sandbox. Their options for those dollar deposits are: do nothing, buy other financial assets from willing sellers, or buy real goods and services from willing sellers. When they buy anything at market prices, both parties are happy. If they ever lose interest in holding dollars, they will either spend them here (buying our goods—which is good for us) or not sell to us anymore (which means a balanced trade position). Either way, it does not cause a financial crisis for the U.S. “The trade deficit is a boon to the US. There need not be a ‘jobs issue’ associated with it. Appropriate fiscal policy can always result in Americans having enough spending power to purchase both our own full employment output and anything the foreign sector may wish to sell us.”

Fraud #6: We Need Savings to Fund Investment

This fraud, Mosler argues, may be the most damaging of all—potentially draining over 20% annually from useful output and employment. The conventional belief is that we need to save more so there will be money available for investment. Mosler shows the causation runs the other direction: investment creates savings, not the reverse. “Savings is the accounting record of investment.”

The paradox of thrift: in the economy, spending must equal all income (including profits) for output to be sold. If anyone tries to save by spending less than their income, someone else must spend more— otherwise the output goes unsold, inventories pile up, production gets cut, workers get laid off, and the person trying to save ends up losing their job. Decisions to save by not spending income result in less income and no new net savings. Meanwhile, decisions to spend more (even by going into debt) cause incomes to rise and can drive real investment and savings.

Mosler gives a vivid example: suppose everyone ordered a new pluggable hybrid car. The auto industry can’t produce that many yet, so they borrow to build new factories, hiring workers to build plants and equipment—capital goods. Workers get paid but there aren’t new cars to buy yet, so they necessarily “save” their money. The decision to invest in car factories created an equal amount of savings. Just as loans create deposits in banking, investment creates savings in the economy.

Congress, misunderstanding this, creates tax-advantaged savings incentives—pension funds, IRAs, 401(k)s—that all function to remove aggregate demand (spending power) from the economy. This slows growth and forces the government to run larger deficits just to get back to even. Ironically, the same congressmen pushing savings programs are categorically opposed to the deficits those programs make necessary. And the massive pools of forced savings feed a bloated financial sector—“bloated whales” managed by thousands of fund managers whipping around trillions, with “sharks” (brokers, bankers, financial managers) feeding on them. The entire financial management industry owes its existence to this fraud.

Fraud #7: Higher Deficits Today Mean Higher Taxes Tomorrow

Mosler saves this one for last because, by this point, the reader has all the tools to see through it. “Your reward for getting this far is that you already know the truth about this most common criticism of government deficits. I saved this for last so you would have all the tools to make a decisive and informed response.” The statement “higher deficits today mean higher taxes tomorrow” is treated as a dire warning by the mainstream. Mosler’s response: “I agree—the innocent fraud is that it’s a bad thing, when in fact it’s a good thing!!!”

Why does the government tax? Not to get money to spend—that’s Fraud #1. It taxes to regulate spending power. If we have too much spending power and it’s causing inflation, we raise taxes to cool things down. Why are we running higher deficits today? Because the economy has a lot of unsold goods and services, unemployment is high, and output is below capacity. The government is buying what it wants, and we don’t have enough after-tax spending power to buy what’s left over. So we cut taxes and increase spending to help clear the shelves.

And why would we ever increase taxes tomorrow? Only when our spending power has become too high, unemployment has gotten very low, shelves have gone empty, and excess demand is causing inflation. In other words: the economy is booming and needs to be cooled down. Reframed honestly, the statement becomes: “Higher deficits today, when unemployment is high, will cause unemployment to go down to the point where we need to raise taxes to cool down a booming economy.” That is not a warning—it is the goal of good economic policy.

Part II: Early Life & Banking Career

Part II is Mosler’s autobiography, and it serves a specific purpose: showing how each job in his career taught him something about how money actually works, building toward the unified understanding that became the seven frauds. He was born in 1949 in Manchester, Connecticut—a typical middle-class family. Father was an accountant who tried managing a liquor store and selling life insurance. Mother was an RN working nights at the local hospital. Average student, tinkered with cars, graduated 30th out of 575.

He attended the University of Connecticut—a public university that cost only $300 per year for residents. He accepted a $1,000 scholarship and a slide rule from a local organization to study engineering, spending his first two years as an engineering student before switching to economics, and graduated with a B.A. in 1971—the same year Nixon took the US off the gold standard and imposed price and wage controls. He walked into a recession. After being greeted at a National Cash Register interview with “I don’t know why you and I are here. We’re laying people off,” he spent 18 months doing odd jobs including a stint as a pool attendant in Miami Beach, where he earned $20 a week (gross), 25-cent tips, and the privilege of sleeping in the storage room.

In 1973, after getting a haircut and re-applying, he was hired at the Savings Bank of Manchester— they had previously turned him down because his hair was too long. His first assignment: collecting delinquent loans, handed the collection books by department head Paul Coupe with a smile and the words “there’s gold in them there books.” This taught him a foundational truth: “Without enforcement, there is no extension of credit.” Years later, he would realize this applies to currency itself—the value of the dollar depends on the government’s ability to enforce tax collection. His mentor Paul Coupe taught him the “theory of lending”: the trick is not to avoid all losses (that means you’re making too few loans) but to make sure the interest covers expected losses with profit left over. “If you aren’t taking some losses, you’re costing the bank money.”

The collections experience taught him the nuances of what makes loans go bad. Even with the highest lending standards, job losses, illnesses, car accidents, and death could all cause defaults. The lender must try to quantify all these risks before extending a loan, set interest rates high enough to cover expected losses with profit, but not so high as to make too few loans. He still runs a “buy here pay here” used car lot today: 3 or 4 out of every 100 loans go bad, costing a few dollars each after repossession and resale. After 40 years, the same basic credit-worthiness principles still hold.

He managed a foreclosed motel (Piano’s, ~50 rooms), discovered all the light bulbs were missing (“Welcome to what happens when borrowers go bad!”), priced rooms at $49/week to cover fixed costs, filled the motel, sold it at a profit. This gave him confidence in his aptitude for the “logic of finance.” Promoted to investment officer managing the bank’s $10 million portfolio, he discovered GNMA (Ginnie Mae) pass-through securities and noticed price differentials between delivery dates. He booked the first fixed-income mortgage-backed securities arbitrage trade—buying for March delivery and simultaneously selling for June delivery at a locked-in profit.

Part II: Wall Street

George Weisse at Bache & Company recruited Mosler in 1975 as his assistant. He got his Series 7 license, made $5,000 in his first month of commissions, and when George left for Shearson, inherited the full book of accounts. In 1976, Jay Pomerenze offered him a position at Bankers Trust on Wall Street as Assistant Vice President of Sales and Trading for GNMA securities. The salary was $45,000—less than his $60,000 in commissions, but he took it for the opportunity.

New York City in the late 1970s: one bedroom on the 20th floor at 80th and York, $525/month. Federal taxes at 50%, state and local at 20%. Take-home pay about $350 a week—roughly what he made at the savings bank. But at Bankers Trust, he and Jay pioneered the use of newly-introduced derivative products: futures contracts, options, and forward delivery securities.

A revealing exchange about monetary operations: when the Fed raised the reserve ratio, trading manager Alan Rogers said he hoped the Fed “didn’t just give the banks the money.” Mosler corrected him—the Fed HAD to supply the reserves; it couldn’t just take them away since the banking system was a closed system of T accounts. “I don’t know how or why I came to know that type of thing back then, when no one else seemed to get it.” He also figured out that the money used to buy Treasury securities at auction came from the same deficit spending—a circular insight that eluded most people, including members of the Fed and Treasury.

Corporate bureaucracy revealed itself when Colonial Mortgage defaulted on an obligation to buy GNMA securities. When the time came to pay and take delivery, they didn’t have the money, and prices had dropped, so the trading desk sold the securities at a loss. The credit department had sent a senior team to visit Colonial and established their credit lines after extensive analysis—but when the default happened, every manager scrambled to avoid responsibility, and the blame landed on Mosler even though determining client credit-worthiness was explicitly not his job. As an Assistant Vice President of Sales and Trading, his responsibility was to make money using the credit lines that the credit department had established. “Enough said about this up-close look at institutional structure at work.”

His trading prowess showed in February when, covering for Jay on vacation, he exploited the month-end weekend to make $432,000 in one month from GNMA “carry”—the 2 extra days of interest from buying Friday before month-end and selling Monday after. Trading manager Alan Rogers dismissed it as “just carry”—not “real market risk.” And no bonus came, because Bankers Trust was a “Government Bond Shop” and his GNMA business didn’t count.

Part II: Illinois Income Investors

Buzz Newton at William Blair & Company in Chicago recruited Mosler to start a fixed income arbitrage department. The deal: no salary, 30% of net profits. He moved to Chicago (“like taking off tight shoes”) and took on two partners: Justin Adams from First Boston (who worked with Larry Fink, later founder of BlackRock) and Cliff Viner from Phoenix Mutual in Hartford.

The formative William Blair years included the legendary 10-year Treasury note futures trade. When the Chicago Board of Trade opened the new contract on May 3, 1982, the floor traders doing practice sessions didn’t realize that new 10-year notes auctioned in May would be deliverable into the June contract but not the March contract, making June inherently cheaper. Mosler sent broker Les—known for once entering an order backwards in the bond pit—to buy 5,000 March and sell 5,000 June contracts (a $500 million position) at a 1/8% spread. The floor traders, thinking Les was making his usual mistake, eagerly filled his order from their personal accounts. Les didn’t come back. Ten minutes later, they suspected the worst and scrambled to cover. Over the next few weeks, the spread converged to fair value, netting Blair almost $3 million.

Another pivotal episode during the Blair years: in early 1980, they bought 30-year Treasury bonds and sold overpriced 30-year bond futures. The position showed a $1 million mark-to-market loss when spreads widened dramatically. Mosler immediately sat down with Ned Janotta, explaining that the position was contracted to make $1 million profit at delivery in June, but the current mark-to-market required a $1 million margin call. What would happen if they waited? They would get both their originally contracted $1 million profit AND the $1 million margin money back. Ned backed the trade. Other Wall Street desks were not as fortunate—stories circulated of managers forcing immediate liquidation at substantial losses. When gold and silver collapsed shortly after, it emerged that the Hunt brothers had been buying the same Treasury futures with profits from their silver cornering scheme. Their forced liquidation of both silver and bond positions returned Mosler’s trade to profitability.

In 1982, Mosler, Adams, and Viner founded Illinois Income Investors (later shortened to III), with William Blair as a partner. III specialized in fixed income arbitrage using a market neutral/zero duration strategy—no interest rate exposure, profits from mispricings. They were paid 35% of profits with no fixed management fees. Over the next fifteen years they established a 20-year track record (1978–1997) with only one losing month—a tenth of one percent that reversed the next month—and no losing trades anyone could recall. III was ranked Number One in the world for highest risk-adjusted spreads by Managed Account Reports through 1997, peaking at $3.5 billion in capital and $35 billion in assets.

Part II: The JBU6 Trade

The trade that nearly became the book’s title. The September 1996 futures contract for 10-year Japanese Government Bonds (JBU6) was mispriced on the Tokyo exchange—too cheap relative to the interest rate swap market. Mosler’s team began buying JBU6 and selling swaps, slowly building the position to over 7,500 contracts (representing over $7 billion in bonds). Then they noticed something extraordinary: they had contracted to take delivery of more of the cheapest deliverable JGB than the government of Japan had issued. If they took delivery and the cheapest bonds weren’t available, the other side would be forced to deliver more expensive bonds—meaning even higher profits.

They kept adding, reaching over 14,000 contracts for III and their AVM clients, and the futures were STILL cheap. Craig Foster at Credit Suisse Tokyo had a similar position with 7,000 contracts. Together they arranged to take delivery of what was now some $20 billion in JGBs, with Craig securing a $20 billion credit line from Switzerland.

On notice day, the other side announced delivery of the cheapest bonds possible—except there weren’t that many. They had somehow gotten extra bonds that “didn’t previously exist” from the Bank of Japan. Then the phone calls started: dealers begging to BORROW the bonds back from Mosler so they could deliver on their obligations. “This was madness! They were committed to deliver them to us, and the penalty for failing to deliver was absolute dismissal from the yen bond markets.” They had been selling billions of Japan’s bonds short without having any to deliver.

Mosler let them out at about half the penalty they would have charged him. The Bank of Japan did “some serious wrist slapping.” Final profit: approximately $150 million. It went on record as the largest futures delivery of all time.

Part II: The Italian Epiphany

This is the intellectual climax of the book—the moment everything clicked. In the early 1990s, Italy’s debt-to-GDP ratio exceeded 110% and the lira interest rate exceeded the growth rate. Professor Rudi Dornbusch of MIT, an influential academic economist, insisted Italy was on the verge of default. Italian government securities denominated in lira yielded about 14%, while borrowing lira from banks cost about 12%—a free lunch of 2%, if you knew Italy wouldn’t default.

Mosler asked himself the question that changed everything: had any nation ever defaulted on debt denominated in its own nonconvertible currency with a floating exchange rate? The answer was no. The gold standard era—when government defaults did occur—ended in 1971 when Nixon closed the gold window. Since then, sovereign currencies had been pure fiat: nonconvertible and floating. Under those conditions, the government can always make payments in its own currency. There is no solvency constraint.

A conversation with research analyst Tom Shulke crystallized the mechanism: “If we buy securities from the Fed or Treasury, functionally there is no difference. We send the funds to the same place and we own the same thing—a Treasury security, which is nothing more than an account at the Fed that pays interest.” The Treasury sells securities to manage the interest rate, not to “fund” expenditures. It is all just a “glorified reserve drain.”

Mosler and colleague Maurice Samuels (Harvard Management) flew to Rome and met with Professor Luigi Spaventa, a senior official of the Italian Treasury Department, in a cavernous office with 20-foot ceilings. Mosler asked a pointed question: “Professor Spaventa, this is a rhetorical question, but why is Italy issuing lira securities? Is it to get lira to spend, or is it to prevent the interbank rate from falling to zero from your 12% target rate?” Spaventa paused, then replied: “The interbank rate would only fall to ½%, NOT 0%, as we pay ½% interest on reserves.” Here was a finance minister who actually understood monetary operations and reserve accounting.

Then Spaventa jumped out of his seat: “Yes! And the International Monetary Fund is making us act pro cyclical!” The IMF had been forcing Italy to tighten fiscal policy because of a default risk that did not exist. The meeting, planned for 20 minutes, stretched to over two hours, with associates being invited in and cappuccino flowing. “The dark cloud of default had been lifted. This was time for celebration!” A week later, the Italian Ministry of Finance announced: “No extraordinary measures will be taken. All payments will be made on time.” Over the next few years, Mosler’s funds made well over $100 million on Italian lira bonds.

Part II: Soft Currency Economics

As Mosler’s understanding deepened in the early 1990s, the US was in recession with deficits at 5% of GDP. The media was full of gloom—David Brinkley, Ross Perot, the Concord Coalition—all demanding fiscal responsibility and lower deficits. Mosler knew the larger deficits were actually fixing the broken economy, but watched helplessly as mainstream leaders prolonged the pain by demanding austerity.

He discussed his ideas with Ned Janotta at William Blair, who connected him to Donald Rumsfeld (Ned’s college roommate, close friend, and business associate, who personally knew many leading economists). Mosler met with “Rummy” for an hour in the steam room of the Chicago Racquet Club during his only opening that week. Rumsfeld sent him to Art Laffer, who assigned Mark McNary to co-author what became Soft Currency Economics, completed in 1993.

The paper describes how gold-standard thinking has been carried over to a world of nonconvertible currencies with floating exchange rates, and how this misunderstanding undermines national prosperity. It goes through the debits and credits of reserve accounting in detail, explaining how government, when the Fed and Treasury are considered together, is best thought of as spending first and then offering securities for sale. Government spending adds funds to member bank reserve accounts. If government securities are not offered for sale, it’s not that government checks would bounce, but that interest rates would remain at the rate paid on reserve balances. Turkey demonstrated this for a decade— quadrillions of liras of deficit spending, interest rate targets at 100%, continuous currency depreciation, no confidence—and yet government lira checks never bounced. Japan demonstrated it with over 200% debt-to-GDP, 7% annual deficits, a credit rating below Botswana—and 3-month government securities near 0%. Soft Currency Economics remains at the top of the “mandatory readings” list at moslereconomics.com and forms the academic foundation for this book.

Part III: Fiscal Proposals

Mosler opens Part III by defining the functions of government that serve “public purpose”: military, legal system, police, public health, education, strategic stockpiles, the payments system, and preventing races to the bottom between states on environmental and regulatory standards. The American economy’s strength, he argues, has always been that workers make sufficient take-home pay to purchase the goods and services they desire. The government’s core fiscal responsibility is keeping taxes low enough so that people have spending power.

His diagnosis of the 2010 economy is stark: the nation is grossly overtaxed for the current level of government spending, as evidenced by high unemployment and excess capacity. Working people are no longer taking home a large enough paycheck to cover their mortgage payments, car payments, and routine expenses, never mind any extras. “To address the current financial crisis and economic collapse, I recommend a number of proposals.”

His first proposal is a full payroll tax holiday—immediate suspension of all FICA, Medicare, and other federal payroll tax deductions for both employees and employers. For a couple making $100,000 per year, this means over $650 per month in additional take-home pay. People making mortgage payments is exactly what the banking system needs to recover—performing loans, not government bailouts.

Second: $500 per capita revenue sharing to states—an immediate $150 billion unrestricted transfer. States are in dire straits as the recession has collapsed their revenue sources. Distribution on a per capita basis is “fair to all” and doesn’t “reward bad behavior.” Third: a federally-funded $8/hour national service job for anyone willing and able to work, including child care, medical coverage, and standard federal benefits. This creates a “labor buffer stock”—countercyclical by design. In recession, more people enter the buffer (higher deficit); in expansion, private employers hire from the pool (deficit shrinks). The market automatically determines the right deficit level.

Fourth: universal health care via a $5,000 per person bank account—$1,000 for preventative care, $4,000 for everything else. Unspent funds are returned as a “cash rebate” at year’s end, incentivizing cost-consciousness. Above $5,000 is covered by a Medicare-like program. No restrictions on private insurance. The system maximizes choice, uses competitive market forces to minimize costs, and removes health care as a cost of production for businesses.

Part III: Monetary System Reforms

Mosler proposes six reforms to the monetary system. First, the Fed should lend unsecured to member banks—the current collateral requirement is redundant because banks already issue government-insured deposits without pledging collateral. With unsecured lending, bank liquidity would be immediately normalized. Second, remove the $250,000 FDIC cap on insured deposits and eliminate bank liquidity regulations. Lower the discount rate to the fed funds rate, removing the outdated concept of a “penalty rate.”

Third, eliminate the interbank market entirely. It serves no public purpose. Have the Fed offer direct loans to member banks for up to 6 months, with the FOMC setting the term structure at its regular meetings. Fourth, have the Treasury directly fund FHLB and FNMA to eliminate excess funding costs currently passed to home buyers. Fifth, have FNMA and FHLB “originate and hold” mortgages, eliminating the secondary mortgage market (which serves no public purpose when Treasury funding is available). Sixth, vigorously enforce mortgage fraud penalties.

On interest rates: the risk-free rate should be kept at a minimum, with the market setting credit spreads above it. Mosler would instruct the Treasury to stop issuing securities longer than 90 days—this lowers long-term rates and supports investment and housing. He notes that the Treasury issuing long-term securities and the Fed subsequently buying them back (quantitative easing) is functionally identical to not issuing them in the first place. He advocates a permanent 0% fed funds rate, noting that Japan had maintained near-zero rates for over a decade without inflation or currency collapse. Lower interest rates mean less income for savers (who are net holders of government securities), which actually means LESS spending power and is therefore LESS inflationary—the opposite of what most people assume.

Part III: Inflation

He also proposes that government securities not be issued for terms longer than 90 days. Since securities function to support interest rates rather than fund expenditures, there is no reason for the Treasury to issue long-term bonds. This would naturally lower long-term rates, supporting investment and housing. He notes that quantitative easing—where the Treasury issues long-term securities and the Fed buys them back—is functionally identical to not issuing them in the first place. Additionally, Mosler advocates expanding strategic stockpiles beyond oil to include food and other necessities, since families in cities can only save dollars, not actual supplies, and numbers in bank accounts will not help during real shortages of food and essentials.

For the housing crisis specifically, Mosler proposes a three-step plan: the government buys the house from the bank at the lower of fair market value or remaining mortgage balance during the foreclosure sale period; it rents the house back to the former owner at fair market rent; after two years, the house is offered for sale with the former owner having right of first refusal. This keeps people in their homes, preserves existing contract law, minimizes disruption for mortgage-backed securities holders, and minimizes moral hazard. “With this proposal, the foreclosure process is allowed to function according to law, so no contracts are violated.”

Mosler addresses the elephant in the room: if the government can always spend, what about inflation? He is clear that the risk of too-large deficits is NOT insolvency but excess aggregate demand driving up prices. However, he argues that in his 60-year lifetime, he has never actually seen demand-pull inflation in the United States. All problematic inflation has been cost-push, driven by energy prices— specifically OPEC in the 1970s.

The “pass through” mechanism works like this: energy prices rise, and the government pays the higher prices (through CPI-indexed wages, contracts, and direct purchases). Every time the government pays more for the same thing, it is effectively redefining its currency downward. The currency is a public monopoly, and the price level is a function of the prices the government pays when it spends and the collateral it demands when it lends.

He returns to the parent-children coupon analogy: if the parents pay one coupon for an hour of work, and all work is equally difficult, then one coupon is worth one hour of child labor. If the parents start paying two coupons for the same hour, each coupon is now worth only half an hour. The parents caused the coupons to lose value by overpaying—that is inflation. Today’s government does the same thing when it indexes wages to CPI and pays market prices that spiral upward.

Current policy uses unemployment as a buffer stock to control inflation: the theory is that higher unemployment means lower inflation. The tradeoff is unemployment vs. price stability. Mosler calls this “problematic” at best: “We sit with unemployment pushing 20% if you count people who can’t find full-time work, maybe 1/3 of our productive capacity going idle.” His alternative—the $8/hour employment buffer stock—achieves both full employment AND price stability by anchoring the base wage without leaving millions idle.

Without proper regulation, banks would exploit the system. With FDIC-insured deposits, bankers could borrow like crazy and pay themselves unlimited salaries at taxpayer expense—and that is essentially what happened in the S&L crisis of the 1980s, which drove the Reagan boom until it was discovered, just as the subprime boom drove the Bush expansion until it was discovered. Bank assets and capital ratios need to be regulated. But the constraint is regulation, not “running out of money.”

The mainstream “expectations theory” of inflation holds that inflation is caused by rising inflation expectations—people expecting price increases demand raises and buy ahead, making it self-fulfilling. A “falling output gap” (falling unemployment) causes inflation expectations to rise. The Fed’s job therefore becomes managing expectations, which is why every Fed speech includes a section about “working hard to control inflation.” Fed officials believe the direction of the economy depends on expectations, so they will always forecast “modest growth” or better—and they will never publicly forecast a collapse, because they believe that could cause one.

Mosler’s view: the biggest real inflation risk remains energy prices from monopolist producers (cost-push from the supply side), not demand-pull from government deficits. “Strictly speaking, it’s a relative value story and not an inflation story, at least initially, which then becomes an inflation story as the higher imported costs work their way through our price structure with government doing more than its share of paying those higher prices and thereby redefining its currency downward in the process.” The solution is not to accept 20% effective unemployment to prevent hypothetical demand-pull inflation, but to use the employment buffer stock to maintain full employment while anchoring the price level at the $8/hr wage floor.

Key Takeaways

The Government Is the Issuer, Not a User

The U.S. federal government creates dollars by spending. It cannot run out of dollars. Taxes and borrowing are not revenue-raising operations—they regulate demand and interest rates. This distinction between currency issuers and currency users is the foundation of everything else in the book.

Deficits = Private Savings (To the Penny)

Every dollar of government deficit adds exactly one dollar to non-government financial assets. Surpluses drain private savings. The six budget surpluses in U.S. history were each followed by depression. The Clinton surplus “achievement” was simultaneously a private savings collapse.

The Real Constraint Is Inflation, Not Solvency

Government checks cannot bounce. The only danger from too much spending is inflation (too much demand chasing too few goods). Taxes are the thermostat for regulating spending power, not a revenue source.

Taxes Drive Currency Demand

People need dollars to pay taxes. This is what gives the dollar its value and makes people willing to sell goods and labor for it. The British hut tax in Africa is the cleanest example of this principle.

Treasuries Are Savings Accounts

The national debt is just the total balance in savings accounts at the Fed. “Paying it off” means moving numbers from savings to checking columns. China “financing our deficit” is a myth—China holds savings accounts denominated in our currency, in our system.

Imports Are Benefits, Exports Are Costs

The real wealth of a nation is what it produces and keeps plus what it imports minus what it must export. Trade deficits mean we’re getting more stuff than we’re giving up. The trade “deficit” is actually a surplus of real goods.

Investment Creates Savings

Causation runs from investment to savings, not the reverse. Forced savings programs (IRAs, 401(k)s) are demand leakages that slow the economy and require larger deficits to compensate.

Buffer Stock Employment

Instead of using unemployment as the buffer stock to control inflation (current policy), use an employment buffer stock ($8/hr jobs). This achieves full employment and price stability simultaneously, and lets the market automatically determine the right deficit level.

Children as Investment, Not Expense

Our children are our fundamental real investment for the future. Without them, there won’t be much human life left in 100 years. Yet the tax code and institutional structure treat children as an expense. Deficiencies in education, child neglect and abuse, and other social ills could be addressed by modifying the financial incentives built into the system.

Real Costs vs. Financial Costs

The real cost of defense is not the dollars spent—it is the real resources (people, materials, technology) diverted from private sector production. The people serving in the military could have been auto workers, lawyers, doctors, and stock brokers producing goods and services for private consumption. The right size defense has nothing to do with budget numbers and everything to do with actual defense needs and the opportunity cost of real resources.

The Pentagon Anecdote

About 10 years ago (circa 2000), Mosler discussed the military with a Pentagon member who said the US needed to increase military size. Mosler replied that if we wanted to do that, we should have done it ten years earlier when we were in a recession with high unemployment and excess capacity—the real resource cost would have been minimal. The Pentagon official responded: “Yes, but back then we couldn’t afford it, the nation was running a budget deficit; while today with a budget surplus, we can afford it.” Mosler: “This is completely backwards! The government never has nor doesn’t have any dollars. The right amount of spending has nothing to do with whether the budget is in surplus or deficit.”

Contrarian Ideas

The Government Spends First, Taxes Second

The conventional sequence (tax → spend) is backwards. The government must distribute dollars before it can collect them. You can’t pay taxes with dollars that don’t exist yet.

Budget Surpluses Are Dangerous

Every surplus in U.S. history preceded a depression. Surpluses drain private savings and set up the conditions for economic collapse. The Clinton surplus was not an achievement but a time bomb.

Social Security Cannot Go Bankrupt

The trust fund is just record-keeping. Privatization is a macroeconomic non-event. The real challenge is productivity (dependency ratio), which money can’t solve.

China Is Not “Financing Our Deficit”

Domestic credit creation funds foreign savings, not the reverse. China has dollars in our system because they chose to net export real goods in exchange for numbers on a Fed computer.

Lower Interest Rates Are Deflationary

The non-government sector is a net saver (holds more government securities than it has borrowed). Lower rates mean less interest income and less spending power—the opposite of the conventional view that low rates are inflationary.

The 1970s Inflation Was Not Demand-Pull

It was cost-push from the OPEC oil cartel, broken by natural gas deregulation and supply response, not by Volcker’s interest rate hikes. The monetarist victory narrative is wrong.

Allegories & Cultural References

The Bowling Alley Scoreboard

Does the bowling alley worry about “running out of points”? Does it keep a “reserve of points” in a lock box? The scoreboard just marks up and marks down. That’s exactly what the government does with dollars.

The Football Stadium

Where do the 3 points “come from” when a field goal is scored? Nowhere. The scoreboard operator just changes the number. Same with government spending.

The Parent-Children Coupons

The book’s central analogy. Parents create coupons, pay children for chores, tax them 10 per week. Parents cannot collect coupons they haven’t distributed. If they pay 2 coupons for work that used to cost 1, the value drops by half (inflation). If they tax too many coupons, children can’t meet their obligations (unemployment).

The Super Bowl Ticket

When you hand your ticket to the usher, it gets torn and thrown away. The stadium doesn’t “use” the ticket. Same with the government and tax payments—cash collected is literally shredded.

The British Hut Tax

Colonial Africa: impose a tax payable only in British coins on people who have no coins. They must now sell labor and goods for coins to pay the tax. The British monetized the economy through the tax obligation. Modern tax systems work the same way.

MacArthur’s War Reparations

If MacArthur had ordered Japan to send 2 million cars per year as war reparations, it would be called exploitation. Japan does it voluntarily through trade and thinks it’s winning.

Standing at a Football Game

If one person stands, they see better. If everyone stands, nobody does. Applied to Social Security privatization: individual benefit doesn’t scale to collective benefit (fallacy of composition).

The Department Store

The economy is a department store with shelves of goods and services. The government takes what it wants. Taxes ensure there’s enough left on the shelves for the private sector without prices rising. The deficit is whatever it needs to be to keep the shelves clearing.

Buying Eggs from a Farmer

The bond/futures arbitrage at William Blair was “like buying eggs from a farmer and selling them to the store and making a large profit”—a simple, obvious mispricing that nobody else saw.

The $10 Bill / $20 Bill

Mosler explains mark-to-market losses with an analogy: you’re offered a free $10 bill if you come pick it up in 30 days. You take the deal. Next day, they’re offering free $20 bills in 30 days. Now your $10 contract shows a $10 “loss” because no one would take your $10 when $20 is available. But if you hold to delivery, you still get your $10 plus your original $10 back. Applied to bond futures arbitrage positions showing temporary paper losses.

Tight Shoes in New York

“Moving to Chicago from New York City was like taking off tight shoes. You don’t realize they’re tight until after you take them off.” NYC: 5 dry cleaners on every block, aggression for no purpose. Chicago: friendlier, with little or no edge.

Roman Spears in the Italian Treasury

When Mosler entered Professor Spaventa’s office in the Italian Treasury, the opening was 20 feet high. Spaventa laughed: “That’s so you could enter this room in Roman times carrying a spear.” The image of entering a modern monetary discussion in a room built for Roman soldiers.

Functional Finance (Abba Lerner)

Judge fiscal policy by its real-world results—output, employment, price stability—not by the budget numbers. The deficit is neither good nor bad in itself; what matters is whether the economy is at full employment without inflation.

Sector Balances (Wynne Godley)

The government sector balance + the private sector balance + the foreign sector balance = zero. If the government runs a surplus, the private sector must run a deficit (or the foreign sector must run a surplus). This is an accounting identity, not a theory.

Buffer Stock Employment

Replace the unemployment buffer stock (current policy: use joblessness to control inflation) with an employment buffer stock ($8/hr national service jobs). The government offers a job to anyone willing to work. In recession, the buffer expands (higher deficit). In expansion, private employers hire from the pool (buffer shrinks, deficit falls). The deficit automatically adjusts to the right level.

Currency as Public Monopoly

The price level is a function of prices paid by the government when it spends and collateral demanded when it lends. The government is the monopoly supplier of dollars. If it overpays for the same output (e.g., CPI-indexed wages), it redefines the currency downward (inflation). If it underpays, deflation.

Mosler’s Three-Point Recovery Plan

1) Full payroll tax holiday (~$650/month for average couple). 2) $500/capita revenue sharing to states (~$150B total). 3) $8/hr national service job for anyone willing to work. These three measures together restore spending power, support state services, and eliminate involuntary unemployment.

The 7 Deadly Innocent Frauds of Economic Policy · Warren Mosler · 2010 · Valance Co. · 114 pages

A hedge fund manager who made hundreds of millions in bond markets explains why almost everything politicians, economists, and the media say about government debt, deficits, taxes, Social Security, and trade is wrong—not because they’re lying, but because they genuinely don’t understand how modern money actually works. Part manifesto, part autobiography, part policy blueprint, the book presents Modern Monetary Theory in its most accessible form, grounded in Mosler’s real-world experience rather than academic theory. The Italian epiphany—realizing that a sovereign government issuing its own nonconvertible floating-rate currency can never involuntarily default—crystallized two decades of trading insight into a unified framework that challenges the most deeply held assumptions of mainstream economics.
