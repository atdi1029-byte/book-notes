## PART I: THE SEVEN DEADLY INNOCENT FRAUDS

### Fraud #1: Govt must tax/borrow to spend
**Fact:** Federal spending is in no case operationally constrained by revenues. No "solvency risk."

**How govt taxes:** When you pay taxes (check or cash), the govt doesn't "get" anything usable. Check = number in your account changes downward. Cash = literally shredded after collection. Like a Super Bowl ticket torn up at the gate.

**How govt spends:** Changes numbers upward in bank accounts. Social Security payment = $3,000 becomes $5,000 on your screen. Pure data entry on the "U.S. dollar monetary system" spreadsheet.

**Bernanke on 60 Minutes:** "It's not tax money. The banks have accounts with the Fed... we simply use the computer to mark up the size of the account." — The Fed chairman confirming it's just data entry.

**Key analogies:**
- **Bowling alley scoreboard** — does the alley worry about "running out of points"? Does it need a "reserve of points" in a lock box?
- **Football stadium** — where do the 3 points "come from" when a field goal is scored? Nowhere.
- **Parent coupons** — parents create coupons, pay children for chores, tax them 10/week. Parents don't need to "get" coupons from children first. They must SPEND first so children have coupons to pay the tax.

**Critical insight:** Govt must spend FIRST before it can tax. The funds to pay taxes come from govt spending (or lending). Like parents must distribute coupons before they can collect the coupon tax.

**Why govt taxes (since it doesn't need the money):**
- Creates demand for the currency — people need dollars to pay taxes, so they sell goods/services/labor for dollars
- Regulates aggregate demand — removes spending power to prevent inflation
- "Taxes function to regulate the economy, and not to get money for Congress to spend"

**British colonial "hut tax"** — imposed tax payable only in British coins on African colonies. Instantly monetized the economy. People had to earn British coins → offered labor and goods for sale → British could hire workers.

**The "right" level of taxes:** Set govt spending at desired level based on real costs (not financial constraints). Then set taxes to leave enough private spending power to buy remaining output without inflation. Deficit of ~5% GDP may be the norm.

**Self-imposed constraints:** Debt ceiling, Treasury-overdraft rules, Fed restrictions — all created by Congress, all counterproductive. Not operational necessities but political blockages.

**"Government checks don't bounce"** — Mosler's talk in Australia. David Gruen (Reserve Bank of Australia) conceded after debate: solvency is not the issue, only inflation and currency value matter.

**Caveat:** "This does NOT mean the government can spend all it wants without consequence. Over-spending can drive up prices and fuel inflation."

### Fraud #2: Deficits leave debt burden to children
**Fact:** Collectively, in real terms, there is no such burden possible. Children get to consume whatever they can produce.

**The time-travel absurdity:** "When our children build 15 million cars per year 20 years from now, will they have to send them back in time to 2008 to pay off their debt?" We don't send goods back to 1945 to pay off WWII debt.

**What Treasury securities actually are:** Just savings accounts at the Fed. Buying a Treasury = moving dollars from your checking account (reserves) to your savings account (securities) at the Fed. Maturity = Fed moves dollars back from savings to checking. That's it.

**National debt = $13 trillion** is simply total holdings of savings accounts at the Fed. "Paying off the debt" = moving numbers from savings accounts to checking accounts. A non-event for the real economy.

**"Paying off China":** China has reserve account at Fed. Bought $1B uniforms → Fed adds $1B to China's checking account → China buys Treasury (moves to savings account) → at maturity, Fed moves back to checking. No real resources transferred. China can only spend its dollars by buying things from willing sellers at market prices.

**What if China refuses to buy Treasuries?** Doesn't matter — they can leave dollars in checking account. No negative consequences for a govt that understands its monetary system. China doesn't "finance our deficits."

**Distribution matters, not the debt number:** Govt spending/taxing determines who gets what share of real output. The deficit size is an accounting artifact. What matters: output and employment.

**Real burden on children:** Lost output from unemployment TODAY. Cutting education TODAY. Not investing in productivity TODAY. The WWII-era US debt/GDP was far higher — no burden consequences.

**When economy is over-taxed:** Insufficient spending power → unemployment → recession spiral. "Count the bodies in the unemployment lines."

**Six surplus periods in 200+ years → followed by the only six depressions.** Budget surplus drains private savings → bust follows.

### Fraud #3: Govt deficits take away savings
**Fact:** Federal Government budget deficits ADD to savings.

**Accounting identity:** $1 govt deficit = exactly $1 net increase in non-government sector financial assets (savings). To the penny. Not theory — accounting fact. Basic national income accounting.

**Lawrence Summers anecdote:** Mosler met with Sen. Daschle + Asst. Treasury Sec. Summers. Summers: "The deficit takes away savings that could be used for investment." Mosler explained the paradox of thrift. Summers: "Well, I really don't understand reserve accounting, so I can't discuss it at that level." Next day Summers was with the Concord Coalition warning about deficits. "Sad but true."

**$100B deficit step-by-step:**
1. Govt sells $100B in Treasuries (buyer moves checking → savings at Fed)
2. Buyer's savings unchanged (just shifted from cash to securities)
3. Govt spends $100B → adds $100B to someone's checking account
4. Non-govt sector now has original $100B in checking PLUS $100B in new Treasuries
5. Net result: $100B added to total savings

**Budget surplus = drains savings:** A federal surplus means govt takes more from us in taxes than it spends. Our savings decrease by the surplus amount.

**WSJ July 1999:** Left headline praised Clinton surplus; right headline lamented low savings rate. Graph showed surplus up, savings down — mirror images. No one connected the dots.

**Al Gore anecdote:** Mosler sat next to Gore at fundraiser (2000). Told him the $5.6T surplus forecast would actually be a $5.6T deficit because private sector savings desires ~5% GDP. Gore agreed it made sense but said he "couldn't go there" politically. Economy collapsed shortly after.

**Robert Rubin anecdote:** Citibank meeting ~2000. Rubin (former Treasury Sec.): "No, the surplus buys Treasury securities, which adds to savings and investment." Mosler: "No, when we run a surplus we have to sell our securities to the Fed to get the money to pay taxes, and our savings go down." Rubin: "No, I think you're wrong." → Economy crashed soon after.

**Professor Wynne Godley:** Cambridge, most successful British economy forecaster. Used "sector analysis" — govt deficit = savings of other sectors. Even his success couldn't convince the mainstream.

**Functional Finance (Abba Lerner, 1940s):** What matters is output and employment, not the deficit number.

### Fraud #4: Social Security is broken
**Fact:** Federal Government Checks Don't Bounce.

**No operational constraint:** Govt spends by changing numbers in bank accounts. This includes Social Security. Trust fund is just record-keeping. Even if trust fund number goes negative, payments can still be made — govt just changes numbers.

**Privatization is a non-event at macro level:**
- Workers stop buying Social Security (bonds) → buy stocks instead
- Stock sellers get cash → buy the newly-issued govt bonds
- Net result: same total stocks + same total bonds in public hands. Just changed hands.
- No effect on economy, savings, or investment
- Only difference: who bears the risk (workers vs govt)

**Steve Moore debate (CATO Institute):** Mosler walked Moore through the logic step-by-step. Moore conceded each point but concluded "I believe in privatization" anyway. Ideology over logic.

**The REAL Social Security problem:** Dependency ratio (fewer workers, more retirees). But this is a PRODUCTIVITY problem, not a money problem. One worker in 50 years must be productive enough to support retirees. Money doesn't solve real resource constraints. Education and technology investment NOW is what matters.

**Fallacy of composition:** Individual can benefit from privatization → therefore everyone would? No. Like standing up at a football game — if one person stands, they see better; if everyone stands, nobody does.

### Fraud #5: Trade deficit is unsustainable, takes away jobs
**Fact:** Imports are real benefits. Exports are real costs. Trade deficits improve our standard of living.

**Core insight:** "The real wealth of a nation is all it produces and keeps for itself, plus all it imports, minus what it must export."

**Japan/WWII analogy:** If MacArthur had ordered Japan to send 2 million cars/year to the US for free, it would have been seen as war reparations and exploitation. Yet Japan has voluntarily done exactly this for 60 years, and they think they're "winning" the trade war.

**China:** They fill our stores with products and get nothing but a bank statement from the Fed showing their dollar balance. And our leaders think we're losing?

**"Borrowing from abroad" is backwards:** US doesn't depend on foreign capital. Domestic credit creation (bank loans) funds foreign savings. When you borrow from a US bank to buy a Chinese car, the bank created the deposit that funds China's dollar savings. It's domestic credit funding foreign savings, not the reverse.

**Jobs lost from trade ≠ jobs lost overall:** With right fiscal policy (adequate spending power), full employment is always achievable regardless of trade deficit. Jobs shift between industries but total employment depends on aggregate demand, which govt controls via tax/spending.

**No "imbalance":** Both parties in a trade are happy — voluntary exchange. China wants dollars more than goods; Americans want goods more than dollars. Everyone is "in balance."

### Fraud #6: We need savings to provide funds for investment
**Fact:** Investment adds to savings.

**The paradox of thrift:** In our economy, spending must equal all income, including profits, for the output to get sold. If anyone attempts to save by spending less than his income, at least one other person must spend more — or else unsold output → inventory pileup → production cuts → layoffs → the saver loses his job.

**"Savings is the accounting record of investment."** — Mosler's key phrase. Just as loans create deposits in banking, investment creates savings. The causation runs investment → savings, NOT savings → investment.

**Professor Basil Moore:** 1996 conference in New Hampshire. Moore asked to use this expression in a book he was writing. Published the book with that title.

**Demand leakages:** Congress creates tax-advantaged savings incentives (pension funds, IRAs, 401(k)s). All these do is REMOVE aggregate demand (spending power). They slow the economy and force larger deficits just to get back to even. Irony: the same Congressmen pushing savings incentives also oppose the deficits those incentives make necessary.

**Bloated whales and sharks:** The massive pools of forced savings (pension funds, etc.) feed a bloated financial sector — thousands of fund managers whipping around trillions. The brokerage, banking, and financial management industries owe their existence to this 6th fraud.

**Why huge deficits aren't as inflationary as expected:** The Congressionally-engineered "demand leakages" from savings incentives cut deeply into spending power, meaning the government has to run even larger deficits to maintain full employment.

### Fraud #7: Higher deficits today mean higher taxes tomorrow
**Fact:** "I agree — the innocent fraud is that it's a bad thing, when in fact it's a good thing!!!"

**Why govt taxes:** Not to get money — to regulate spending power. If too much spending power → inflation → raise taxes. If not enough → unemployment → cut taxes.

**Why higher deficits today:** Because the "department store" (the economy) has unsold goods, unemployment is high, output is below capacity. So we cut taxes and maybe increase spending to give people more spending power to clear the shelves.

**When would we raise taxes?** Only when spending power is TOO HIGH — unemployment very low, shelves going empty, inflation rising. Not to "pay for" past deficits.

**Reframed:** "Higher deficits today, when unemployment is high, will cause unemployment to go down to the point we need to raise taxes to cool down a booming economy." That's a GOOD thing.

## PART II: THE AGE OF DISCOVERY

### Early Life & Banking Career
- Born September 18, 1949, Manchester Memorial Hospital, CT
- Father: accountant, managed liquor store, sold life insurance, did tax prep. Mother: registered nurse
- Average student, UConn ($300/year), started engineering → switched to economics, BA 1971
- Graduated into Nixon's recession, price/wage controls, end of gold standard
- 18 months of odd jobs including pool attendant in Miami Beach ($20/week + tips + storage room to sleep in)
- 1973: hired at Savings Bank of Manchester. First job: collecting delinquent loans
- Key insight from collections: "Without enforcement, there is no extension of credit" — value of the dollar is only as good as the government's ability to enforce tax collection

### Theory of Lending (Paul Coupe)
- Paul Coupe mentored him. "If you aren't taking some losses, you're costing the bank money" — standards too tight = too few loans = unprofitable
- Balance between too tight (safe but unprofitable) and too lax (lots of loans but losses eat profits)
- Still runs a "buy here pay here" used car lot. 3-4 out of 100 loans go bad. After 40 years, same principles hold.

### Motel Turnaround
- Bank foreclosed on Piano's motel (~50 rooms) in downtown Manchester
- Mosler managed it. All light bulbs missing ("Welcome to what happens when borrowers go bad!")
- Strategy: $49/week rate (= $7/day vs $15/day market rate) → filled half the rooms → covered fixed costs → rest of rooms = pure profit
- Sold motel for a profit. This gave him confidence in the "logic of finance"

### Investment Officer
- Bank president retired, Bill Johnson took over. Promoted Mosler to portfolio manager ($5M stocks, $5M short-term)
- First CD purchase: called Merrill Lynch for $500K. Told minimum was $1M. "I thanked him and hung up the phone, now feeling very small!"
- Discovered GNMA pass-throughs (Ginnie Mae). Noticed price differentials between delivery dates → first fixed-income mortgage-backed securities arbitrage
- Buy March delivery, sell June delivery simultaneously = 5.5% annualized, borrow at 4.5% = locked-in 1% profit with zero risk
- Salomon Brothers called one Friday night asking him to explain the trade → his ideas were later used by Salomon's senior managers, three of whom went on to found Long Term Capital Management

### Wall Street
- **Bache (1975):** George Weisse offered him a job as his assistant. Series 7 license. First month made $5,000 in commissions. George left for Shearson after a year, leaving Mosler full commissions.
- **Bankers Trust (1976):** Jay Pomerenze offered AVP of Sales & Trading, GNMA securities. Salary $45K (he was making $60K). NYC apartment at 80th and York, 20th floor, $525/month. Federal taxes 50%, take-home $350/week.
- Pioneered derivative products (futures, options, forward delivery contracts)
- Reserve ratio insight: When Fed raised reserve ratio, trading manager Alan Rogers said "I hope the Fed didn't just give the banks the money." Mosler corrected him — the Fed HAD to supply the reserves, that's how the system works. "I don't know how or why I came to know that type of thing back then, when no one else seemed to get it."
- Colonial Mortgage default: blamed on Mosler despite credit department's failure to assess correctly. Lesson in corporate bureaucracy — everyone avoiding responsibility.
- February GNMA trading feat: $432,000 profit in one month from "carry" (month-end weekend interest). Trading manager dismissed it as "just carry" — not "real market risk."
- No bonus because Bankers Trust was a "Government Bond Shop" — GNMA business didn't count

### William Blair & Company (Chicago)
- Buzz Newton (Corporate Bond Dept head) recruited him to start fixed income arbitrage department
- No salary — 30% of net profits. Moved to Chicago.
- Partners: Justin Adams (First Boston, NYC — worked with Larry Fink who went on to start BlackRock) and Cliff Viner (Phoenix Mutual, Hartford)

Floor traders thought Les was making another mistake. Made ~$3M in a few weeks.

Ned Janotta (Senior Partner) backed the trade. When gold/silver collapsed, Hunt brothers forced to liquidate → Mosler's trade returned to profitability.

### Illinois Income Investors (III)
- Founded 1982 with Justin Adams and Cliff Viner, Wm Blair as partner
- Specialized in fixed income arbitrage, market neutral / zero duration
- 35% of profits, no fixed management fees
- 20-year track record (1978-1997): only ONE losing month (a tenth of one percent, reversed next month). No losing trades anyone can recall.

### The JBU6 Trade (1996)
- September 1996 futures contract on 10-year Japanese Government Bonds (JBU6)
- Contract was mispriced on Tokyo exchange — too cheap relative to interest rate swap market
- Built position to over 14,000 contracts (representing more of the cheapest deliverable JGB than Japan had issued)
- Other side had sold short bonds they didn't even have to deliver
- Dealers called begging to BORROW bonds from Mosler to deliver to him
- "This was madness! They were committed to deliver them to us"
- Let them out at about half the penalty they would have charged him. Made ~$150 million profit
- Went on record as largest futures delivery of all time

### Italian Epiphany (early 1990s)
- Italy's debt/GDP exceeded 110%. Professor Rudi Dornbusch (MIT) insisted Italy would default
- Italian lira bonds yielded 14%, borrowing cost 12% = 2% free lunch, IF no default
- Key question: Can a sovereign government that issues its own nonconvertible currency with floating exchange rate default? **No.** No nation had ever defaulted under those conditions.
- Gold standard ended 1971 when Nixon took US off gold convertibility
- Tom Shulke insight: "If we buy securities from the Fed or Treasury, functionally there is no difference. A Treasury security is nothing more than account at the Fed that pays interest." → Treasury sells securities to manage interest rates, not to "fund" expenditures. It's all just a glorified reserve drain.
- Met with Professor Luigi Spaventa (Italian Treasury Department) in Rome. Asked: "Is Italy issuing lira securities to get lira to spend, or to prevent the interbank rate from falling to zero?"
- Spaventa's answer: "The interbank rate would only fall to ½%, NOT 0%, as we pay ½% interest on reserves." — He actually understood monetary operations!
- Then Spaventa jumped up: "Yes! And the International Monetary Fund is making us act pro cyclical!" — The IMF was forcing Italy to tighten policy due to a default risk that didn't exist.
- Meeting went from planned 20 minutes to 2+ hours. "The dark cloud of default had been lifted. This was time for celebration!"
- Week later: Italian Ministry of Finance announced "No extraordinary measures will be taken. All payments will be made on time."
- Over next few years, made well over $100 million in profits on Italian lira bonds

### Soft Currency Economics
- Early 1990s US recession, deficit at 5% GDP, media calling for fiscal responsibility (lower deficits)
- Mosler knew larger deficits were FIXING the broken economy, but watched helplessly as leaders demanded austerity
- Discussed with Ned Janotta → Ned connected him to Donald Rumsfeld → Rumsfeld sent him to Art Laffer → Laffer assigned Mark McNary to co-author
- *Soft Currency Economics* completed 1993. Remains head of "mandatory readings" at moslereconomics.com
- Describes how gold standard rhetoric has been carried over to a nonconvertible currency with floating exchange rate, undermining national prosperity

## PART III: PUBLIC PURPOSE

### Core Philosophy
- Functions of government: military, legal system, police, public health, education, strategic stockpiles, payments system, preventing "races to the bottom" between states (environmental, enforcement, regulatory, judicial standards)
- American economy's strength: workers make sufficient take-home pay to purchase goods and services they desire. Government's job is to keep taxes low enough so people can spend.
- Diagnosis (2010): grossly overtaxed for the current level of govt spending → high unemployment, excess capacity

### Proposal 1: Payroll Tax Holiday
- Immediate suspension of all FICA, Medicare, and other federal payroll tax deductions for both employees and employers
- Couple making $100K/year → $650+/month extra take-home pay
- Restores ability to make mortgage payments, meet expenses, do some shopping
- Good for banking system: people making loan payments = loans performing = banks healthy

### Proposal 2: Revenue Sharing ($500/capita to states)
- Immediate $150 billion unrestricted transfer to state governments, ~$500 per capita
- States in dire straits from recession — revenue sources collapsed
- Per capita basis = "fair to all" and doesn't "reward bad behavior"
- Relief for essential services until economy recovers

### Proposal 3: National Service Jobs ($8/hour)
- Federally funded $8/hr job for anyone willing and able to work
- Includes child care, medical coverage, standard federal benefits
- Transition from unemployment to private sector employment
- Businesses hire from this pool as economy recovers (vs. hiring the long-term unemployed, which they resist)
- Forms a "labor buffer stock" — countercyclical (anti-inflationary in recovery, anti-deflationary in slowdown)
- Automatically sets deficit at near "neutral" level — lets the MARKET determine the right deficit size
- Side benefits: reduced crime, reduced incarceration, increased family coherence, teen/minority employment up

### Proposal 4: Universal Health Care
- $5,000 per person per year in a bank account for medical expenses
- $1,000 for preventative measures, $4,000 for everything else
- Unspent funds returned as "cash rebate" at year end → incentivizes cost-consciousness
- Above $5,000 covered by Medicare-like program
- No restrictions on private insurance
- Maximizes choice, competitive forces minimize costs, frees up physician time, reduces insurance company role

### Proposals for the Monetary System
1. **Fed should lend unsecured** to member banks (collateral requirement is redundant — banks already issue govt-insured deposits without collateral)
2. **Remove $250K FDIC cap** on insured deposits + remove bank liquidity regulations. Lower discount rate to fed funds rate (remove "penalty" concept)
3. **Eliminate the interbank market** — Fed offers direct loans to member banks up to 6 months, FOMC sets term structure at regular meetings
4. **Treasury should directly fund FHLB and FNMA** — eliminate excess funding costs passed to home buyers
5. **FNMA/FHLB should "originate and hold"** mortgages — eliminate that portion of secondary mortgage market (no public purpose)
6. **Enforce mortgage fraud penalties vigorously**

### Interest Rates and Monetary Policy
- Keep "risk free" interest rate at minimum (let market set credit spreads)
- **Treasury should stop issuing securities longer than 90 days** → lowers long-term rates, supports investment and housing
- Treasury issuing long-term securities + Fed buying them back (QE) is functionally identical to not issuing them in the first place
- **0% fed funds rate** — not inflationary, not currency-depreciating (Japan demonstrated this for 10+ years)
- Lower rates = less interest income for savers (net holders of govt securities) = LESS spending power = LESS inflationary
- High risk-free rates support rentiers (people living off interest income) → reduces labor force → reduces national output

### The Role of Government Securities
- Treasury securities don't "fund" expenditures — all spending is just crediting bank accounts at the Fed
- Selling securities doesn't remove wealth (cash → Treasury = same net financial assets)
- "A Treasury security is functionally nothing more than a time deposit at the Fed"
- Turkey: quadrillions of liras of deficit spending, 100% interest rates, no confidence — lira checks never bounced
- Japan: 200% debt/GDP, 7% deficits, downgraded below Botswana — yen checks never bounced, 3-month securities near 0%

### Strategic Stockpiles
- Families in cities can only save dollars, not actual food/supplies
- In a real crisis, numbers in bank accounts won't help
- Extend the strategic oil reserve concept to food and other necessities for emergency consumption

### Housing Proposal
1. Homeowner facing foreclosure notifies govt → govt buys house from bank at lower of fair market value or remaining mortgage balance
2. Govt rents house back to former owner at fair market rent
3. After 2 years, house is offered for sale — former owner has right of first refusal
- Keeps people in homes, doesn't interfere with contract law, minimizes moral hazard

### Industries with Strategic Purpose
- Defense contractors should use ONLY domestic steel (national security)
- Keeps domestic steel industry capable of meeting defense needs
- Better than general tariffs — targeted at strategic purpose

### Labor Buffer Stock → the "Optimum Deficit"
- $8/hr national service job with full benefits for anyone willing to work
- Workers naturally hired away by private sector as economy improves (countercyclical)
- Government deficit automatically adjusts: in recession more people in buffer stock (higher deficit), in boom fewer (lower deficit)
- "Let the market decide the optimum deficit" — the deficit becomes an automatic stabilizer

### Inflation
- The real risk of too-large deficits is NOT insolvency but INFLATION (excess aggregate demand)
- Mosler: "I've never seen demand-pull inflation in my 60-year lifetime"
- All problematic inflation was cost-push from energy prices (OPEC in 1970s)
- "Pass through" mechanism: energy prices rise → government pays higher prices (CPI-indexed wages/contracts) → currency redefined downward
- **Currency = public monopoly.** Price level is a function of prices paid by government when it spends and collateral demanded when it lends
- Without regulation, banks would borrow from FDIC-insured deposits to pay themselves unlimited salaries (exactly what happened in S&L crisis of 1980s)

### Buffer Stock vs Unemployment as Price Anchor
- Current policy: use UNEMPLOYMENT as buffer stock to control inflation (higher unemployment = lower inflation)
- Mosler's alternative: use EMPLOYMENT buffer stock ($8/hr jobs) — full employment + price stability
- With unemployment buffer stock: "We sit with unemployment pushing 20% if you count people who can't find full-time work, maybe 1/3 of our productive capacity going idle"

### "Expectations Theory" of Inflation (Mainstream)
- Mainstream: inflation is caused by rising inflation expectations. People expect prices to rise → demand raises → buy ahead → prices rise (self-fulfilling)
- "Falling output gap" (falling unemployment) causes inflation expectations to rise
- Fed's job becomes "managing inflation expectations" → every speech about "working hard to control inflation"
- Forecasters always predict "modest growth" (self-fulfilling). Never predict collapse (could cause one).
- Mosler's view: biggest inflation risk remains energy prices (cost-push from monopolist producers), NOT demand-pull from deficits
