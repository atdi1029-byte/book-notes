## Prologue: The Habit Cure

### Lisa Allen's Transformation

- **Lisa Allen**: 34-year-old subject who transformed her life
- Started smoking/drinking at 16, struggled with obesity, $10,000 debt
- After transformation: lean, fit, no debt, ran marathon, master's degree
- Turning point: husband announced affair during vacation planning
- Cairo morning: woke to mosque call, tried to light a pen instead of cigarette
- Decision: trek through Egyptian desert — required quitting smoking
- Quitting smoking led to jogging, which changed eating, sleeping, saving
- Eventually: marathon, grad school, house, engagement
- Brain scans: old habit patterns **overridden** by new patterns

### The Keystone Habit Concept

- **Keystone habit**: focusing on one pattern teaches how to reprogram others
- Companies transform through habits: **Procter & Gamble**, **Starbucks**, **Alcoa**, **Target**

### The Research Study

- **National Institutes of Health** funded three-year study
- Subjects: former smokers, overeaters, problem drinkers, obsessive shoppers
- Methods: vital signs, video cameras in homes, DNA sequencing, brain imaging

### Habit Definition and Impact

> "All our life, so far as it has definite form, is but a mass of habits."
> — **William James** (1892)

- **Duke University** (2006): over 40% of daily actions are habits, not decisions
- Only in past two decades have scientists begun understanding how habits work

### The Kufa Experiment

- Army major in **Kufa** (90 miles south of Baghdad) ran habit modification program
- Analyzed riot videotapes: crowd gathers → food vendors appear → violence
- Major's request to mayor: keep food vendors out of plazas
- Result: crowd at **Masjid al-Kufa** grew restless, hungry, no kebab sellers
- By 8 PM everyone gone — **no riot**

> "Understanding habits is the most important thing I've learned in
> the army. Once you see everything as a bunch of habits, it's like
> someone gave you a flashlight and a crowbar and you can get to work."

## Chapter 1: The Habit Loop — How Habits Work

### Eugene Pauly (E.P.)

- Fall 1993: elderly man walked into lab at San Diego
- Year earlier: preparing dinner, asked wife "Who's Michael?" about own son
- Illness: **viral encephalitis** — virus from cold sores invaded brain
- Temperature hit 105°F, became delirious, slipped into coma for ten days
- Virus destroyed oval of tissue near brain center: **medial temporal lobe**
- Recovery remarkable: within weeks talking normally, walking hallways
- But couldn't remember day of week, doctors' names, or recent decades
- Some mornings: cook bacon and eggs, go back to bed, then repeat 2-3 times

### Larry Squire's Research

- **Larry Squire**: UC San Diego professor, three decades studying memory
- Eugene's studies opened new world: even someone who can't remember age
  can develop **complex habits**

### H.M. (Henry Molaison)

- 1953 surgery removed **hippocampus** to stop seizures
- Seizures stopped but lost ability to form new memories
- From 1953 until death in 2008, every person and room was fresh each time
- Squire studied H.M. as PhD student at MIT thirty years earlier

### Eugene's Retained Habits

- Intelligence tests showed sharp mind despite no new memory formation
- Couldn't draw map of house or say where kitchen was
- Yet walked to bathroom without hesitation, flushed, washed hands, returned
- When asked what he'd do if hungry: stood up, walked to kitchen, took nuts
- **Key mystery**: How could someone who couldn't map his house navigate it?

### Eugene's Daily Walks

- Beverly (wife) took Eugene on same route every morning
- One morning he slipped out alone, collected pinecones, **found his way home**
- Soon walking independently, returning with objects he couldn't remember acquiring

### MIT's Basal Ganglia Research

- MIT labs studied habit formation with microscopic sensors in rat brains
- 1990s research focused on **basal ganglia** — oval of cells deep in brain
- Evolutionarily ancient, similar across fish/reptiles/mammals
- Controls automatic behaviors: breathing, swallowing, startle response

### The T-Shaped Maze Experiment

- Rats with brain sensors placed in T-shaped maze with chocolate at one end
- Initially: wandered aimlessly, **brain probes showed furious activity**
- After hundreds of runs: stopped sniffing, zipped through faster
- **Mental activity decreased** — route became automatic
- Within a week: brain structures for memory quieted
- **Basal ganglia took over** as rat ran faster and brain worked less

### Chunking: How Habits Form

- Brain converts action sequences into automatic routines = **"chunking"**
- Simple: putting toothpaste on brush
- Complex: backing car out of driveway (mirrors, brakes, traffic, distances)
- Now done with hardly any thought — **routine occurs by habit**

### Why the Brain Creates Habits

- Brain constantly looking for ways to **save effort**
- Efficient brain = less room = smaller head = easier childbirth
- Frees mental energy for inventing spears, irrigation, airplanes

### The Three-Step Loop

- **Cue**: trigger telling brain to go automatic and which habit to use
- **Routine**: physical, mental, or emotional action
- **Reward**: helps brain determine if loop worth remembering

> "Over time, this loop — cue, routine, reward — becomes more and more
> automatic. The cue and reward become intertwined until a powerful
> sense of anticipation and craving emerges."

### Brain Activity Spikes at Beginning and End

- Activity spikes when rat hears click (beginning) and finds chocolate (end)
- Brain spends effort at beginning looking for cue, shakes awake at end
  to confirm everything unfolded as expected

### Habits Never Disappear

> **Ann Graybiel** (MIT scientist): "Habits never really disappear.
> They're encoded into the structures of our brain. The problem is
> your brain can't tell the difference between bad and good habits."

- Can be ignored, changed, or replaced — but pattern remains
- Must create new neurological routines that overpower old behaviors
- Without habit loops, brains would shut down from minutiae
- People with **basal ganglia damage** often mentally paralyzed

### Eugene's Cardboard Test

- 16 objects on cardboard, divided into 8 pairs, one "correct" per pair
- Eugene couldn't remember any items no matter how many times tested
- But **performance improved**: 85% correct after 28 days, 95% at 36 days
- Asked researcher: "How am I doing this?"
- Pointed to head: "It's here somehow, and the hand goes out"
- **Habit loop**: pair = cue, choose and flip = routine, "correct" = reward
- When all 16 items placed out of context, Eugene **couldn't sort them**

### Habits Are Surprisingly Delicate

- If cues changed slightly, habits fell apart
- Street repairs or windstorm: Eugene got lost no matter how close to home
- If daughter stopped to chat 10 seconds before leaving, anger habit never emerged

### Revolutionary Impact

- Proved: **possible to learn and make unconscious choices without
  remembering anything** about the lesson
- Eugene showed habits, as much as memory and reason, are at root of behavior

### The Mice Experiment (National Institute on Alcohol Abuse)

- Trained mice to press levers until behavior became habit, rewarded with food
- Then **poisoned the food** or **electrified the floor**
- Mice knew food and cage were dangerous when offered directly
- But when saw old cues, **unthinkingly pressed lever and ate** even as
  they vomited or jumped from electricity
- **Habit so ingrained mice couldn't stop themselves**

### McDonald's Deliberate Habit Engineering

- Every McDonald's looks the same — **consistent cues to trigger eating**
- Fries designed to disintegrate on tongue, delivering salt/grease instantly
- Causes **pleasure centers to light up** and brain to lock in pattern

### Eugene's Final Years and Death

- By 2000: life achieved equilibrium — walked every morning, ate freely
- Negative habits emerged: sedentary, watching TV for hours
- Beverly used habit understanding to help: removed bacon, placed salad
- Spring day: minor heart attack, hospital stay
- Daughter's intervention: compliment his willingness to sit still
  ("You're doing something important for science") — **pride as reward**
- Fall 2008: broke hip after falling, remained at peace with uncertainty
- Died of massive heart attack — celebrated by researchers afterward

> **Beverly**: "He told me once he wanted to do something important
> with his life, something that mattered. And he did. He just never
> remembered any of it."

## Chapter 2: The Craving Brain — How to Create New Habits

### Claude Hopkins and Pepsodent

- **Claude C. Hopkins**: legendary early 1900s advertising executive
- Convinced Americans to buy Schlitz beer, Palmolive soap, Puffed Wheat
- Initially rejected Pepsodent — "financial suicide" (nobody brushed teeth)
- WWI recruits' poor dental hygiene was a national security risk

### Hopkins's Strategy

- Identified **tooth film** as the cue — natural mucin buildup everyone has
- Campaign: "Run your tongue across your teeth — you'll *feel a film*"
- Reward: beautiful teeth, prettier smile
- Within five years: one of best-known products globally
- Before Pepsodent: 7% of Americans had toothpaste; a decade later: 65%

### Hopkins's Two Rules

1. **Find a simple and obvious cue**
2. **Clearly define the rewards**

### The Missing Third Rule: Craving

- Hopkins relied on a third rule without knowing it
- **Pepsodent contained citric acid and mint oil** creating cool tingle
- Customers said they missed that sensation if they forgot to brush
- Hopkins wasn't selling beautiful teeth — he was selling a *sensation*

> "Consumers need some kind of signal that a product is working.
> The tingling doesn't make the toothpaste work any better." —
> **Tracy Sinclair**, Oral-B/Crest brand manager

- Today almost all toothpastes contain chemicals whose sole job is
  making your mouth tingle

### The Febreze Failure at P&G

- **Drake Stimson**: P&G rising star, led Febreze marketing (1996)
- **HPBCD**: chemist discovered odor-eliminating compound when wife
  noticed he didn't smell like smoke after work
- P&G spent millions perfecting — colorless, odorless, cheap, no stains
- NASA eventually used it to clean shuttle interiors

### The Park Ranger Discovery

- Phoenix park ranger: everything smelled like skunk — house, truck, clothes
- Love life crippled by smell (nice guy "took a break" after visiting)
- After Febreze samples: "This spray has changed my life"
- Stimson thought: *We're going to make a fortune*

### The Marketing Campaign Flops

- Designed ads following Hopkins's rules: obvious cue, clear reward
- Launched in test cities, gave away samples — sales got smaller
- Housewives had received samples but forgot about them

### The Fundamental Problem: No Cue

- Woman with **nine cats** — house overwhelming to visitors but she
  didn't notice the smell
- With constant exposure, scents fade — even the strongest
- **People with greatest need never smelled the odors** that should
  have triggered use
- The cue was hidden from those who needed Febreze most

### Wolfram Schultz and the Neuroscience of Craving

- **Wolfram Schultz**: Cambridge neuroscience professor
- Revolutionized understanding of cues, rewards, and habits over 20 years

### Julio the Monkey Experiment

- **Julio**: macaque with electrode in brain, learned to touch lever
  when colored shapes appeared for blackberry juice reward
- As habit formed: shapes = cue, touch lever = routine, juice = reward

### The Critical Discovery: Anticipation

- As habit strengthened, Julio's brain began **anticipating** the juice
- "I got a reward!" pattern fired *before* juice arrived — at cue alone
- When juice didn't arrive or was diluted: anger, mopey behavior
- New neurological pattern emerged: **craving**
- Once monkey anticipated reward, distractions held no allure
- Same way gamblers play slots after losing winnings

### Craving Makes the Habit Loop Work

- **Habits create neurological cravings** that emerge gradually
- Most cravings develop so slowly we're blind to their influence
- **Cinnabon** stores positioned away from food courts so cinnamon
  smell wafts uninterrupted — shoppers subconsciously start craving

> "There is nothing programmed into our brains that makes us see a
> box of doughnuts and automatically want a sugary treat. But once
> our brain learns that a doughnut box contains yummy sugar, it will
> start *anticipating* the sugar high." — Wolfram Schultz

- **Exercise cravings**: 92% in one study craved endorphins; 67% in
  another craved sense of accomplishment
- Cue and reward alone aren't enough — brain must start *expecting*
  the reward for habit to become automatic

### The Febreze Turnaround

- Stimson's team studied Wolfram Schultz's experiments
- Key discovery: woman in Scottsdale suburb used Febreze daily as
  "a nice way to make everything smell good as a final touch"
- Studied cleaning videotapes: women smiled *after* cleaning
- **Strategic pivot**: Repositioned Febreze as a **reward**, not
  problem solver
- Added more perfume creating distinct pleasant scent
- Changed ads: women spraying freshly made beds, laundered clothing
- "Spraying feels like a little mini-celebration when I'm done"
- **Results**: Summer 1998 relaunch, sales doubled in two months
- Within a year: $230 million in sales
- Now generates over **$1 billion annually**

### Applying the Craving Formula

- **Exercise**: choose cue (gym upon waking) and reward (smoothie)
  — anticipate the reward, craving drives the loop
- **Weight loss**: **National Weight Control Registry** study of 6,000+
  people who lost 30+ lbs — 78% ate breakfast every morning
- **Sunscreen**: less than 10% apply daily because there's no craving
- **Foaming**: shampoo, detergent, toothpaste don't need to foam
  — companies add foaming chemicals because people expect suds

## Chapter 3: The Golden Rule of Habit Change

### Tony Dungy and the Tampa Bay Buccaneers

- November 1996: **Tony Dungy** becomes head coach of Tampa Bay
  Buccaneers — one of NFL's worst teams (2-8 record)
- Seventeen years as assistant coach, four failed head coaching interviews
- Philosophy: change players' habits so they react automatically

> "Champions don't do extraordinary things. They do ordinary things,
> but they do them without thinking, too fast for the other team to
> react."

### The Golden Rule of Habit Change

- You can never truly extinguish bad habits — you must **replace** them
- **Keep the old cue, deliver the old reward, insert a new routine**
- Almost any behavior can be transformed if cue and reward stay the same

### Dungy's System in Action

- Opposite of complicated playbooks — only a handful of formations
- Taught players to focus on specific visual **"keys"** (cues)
- **Regan Upshaw** focused on: outside foot of lineman, shoulder rotation,
  gap width — practiced until reactions automatic
- **San Diego game**: Upshaw's automatic read forced quarterback
  **Stan Humphries** into hurried throw; safety **John Lynch** read
  cues, intercepted, ran 25 yards
- **Buccaneers won 25-17** — one of biggest upsets of the season

### Dungy's Teaching Method

- **Derrick Brooks** example: Dungy put visual keys in sequence,
  told him which choice to make for each — removed decision making
- Every player got similar instructions, practiced until automatic
- Took almost a year for habits to take hold
- Second season: won first 5 games, play-offs for first time in 15 years
- 1999: won division championship

### The Choking Problem

- 1999-2001: Bucs made play-offs but **fell apart in high-stress moments**
- 1999: blew conference championship against St. Louis Rams
- 2000: lost to Eagles 21-3 (one game from Super Bowl)
- 2001: lost to Eagles again 31-9

> "In a big game, players would say, 'I went back to what I knew.'
> What they were *really* saying was they trusted our system most of
> the time, but when everything was on the line, belief broke down."

- End of 2001: Dungy fired; Bucs won Super Bowl next year using his
  formations, players, and habits

### Bill Wilson and Alcoholics Anonymous

- 1934, New York City basement: one of largest habit change attempts born
- **Bill Wilson**: 39-year-old alcoholic, consuming three bottles daily
- Old drinking buddy called — sober two months: "Turn your life over to God"
- December 1934: **Charles B. Towns Hospital**, hallucinogenic drug
  **belladonna**, withdrawal agony
- White light experience: "I am ready to do anything. Anything!"
- **Never had another drink** — devoted next 36 years to founding AA

### AA's Scale and Methods

- Estimated **2.1 million** people seek help annually; up to **10 million**
  may have achieved sobriety through AA
- Twelve steps written in rush one night (twelve apostles)
- Seven of twelve steps mention God/spirituality
- No prescribed schedule, no professionals, few rules
- Almost no grounding in science or accepted therapeutic methods

### How AA Works: The Golden Rule Applied

- AA forces people to identify cues and rewards, then find new behaviors
- **Steps four and five**: "searching and fearless inventory" = mapping
  all triggers for alcoholic urges

> "When you make a self-inventory, you're figuring out all the things
> that make you drink." — **J. Scott Tonigan**, University of New Mexico

- Alcoholics don't necessarily crave feeling drunk — they crave escape,
  relaxation, companionship, blunting of anxieties
- AA provides meetings and sponsors offering same escape/catharsis

> "The triggers and payoffs stay the same, it's just the behavior
> that changes." — Tonigan

### The Magdeburg Brain Surgery Study

- 2007: **Ulf Mueller** implanted electrical devices in basal ganglia of
  five alcoholics (one had been through detox 60+ times)
- Devices overrode neurological cravings — men didn't touch a drop
- **But eradicating cravings wasn't enough**: four relapsed after
  stressful events — picked up bottle automatically
- Once they learned alternate routines (AA, therapy), drinking stopped
- Man who'd been to detox 60 times never had another drink

> "Some brains are so addicted that only surgery can stop it. But
> those people also need new ways for dealing with life." — Mueller

### Mandy and Habit Reversal Training

- **Mandy**: 24-year-old graduate student at **Mississippi State**,
  chronic nail biter (nails pulled away from skin, nerve damage)
- Treatment: **habit reversal training**
- **Awareness training**: identified tension in fingertips as the **cue**
- **Reward**: physical stimulation she craved
- **Competing response**: when feeling tension, put hands in pockets
  or grip pencil, then rub arm or rap knuckles (physical substitute)
- After week: bitten nails only 3 times, used competing response 7 times
- After month: habit completely gone, new routines automatic

> "It seems ridiculously simple, but once you're aware of how your
> habit works, you're halfway to changing it." — **Nathan Azrin**

- **Habit reversal therapy** now treats: tics, depression, smoking,
  gambling, anxiety, OCD, procrastination, and dozens of other behaviors

### The Missing Ingredient: Belief

- For most habits, identifying cues and changing routines works
- But for stubborn habits, **belief** is essential

### John's AA Story

- First got help when son **Sam** broke arm in drunk driving accident
- Clinic for 13 months, then stopped going to meetings
- Mom got cancer ~2 years sober — first thing: found a bar
- Drunk for 2 years, learned cocaine, flipped car in intersection
- Came back to AA; sponsor said: without higher power, nothing matters

> "I started working at believing in something bigger than me. I don't
> know if it's God, but there is a power that has helped me stay sober
> for seven years."

### Research on Belief

- **Alcohol Research Group** (California): habit replacement worked
  until **stresses of life got too high** — then alcoholics relapsed
- **2005 study** (UC Berkeley, Brown, NIH): alcoholics who believed
  in a higher power more likely to stay sober through stressful periods
- **Wasn't God that mattered — it was belief itself**
- Once people learned to believe in something, that skill spilled over
- **Belief = ingredient making reworked habit loop permanent**

> "You don't have to believe in God, but you do need the capacity to
> believe that things will get better." — **Tonigan**

### How AA Trains Belief

- AA puts alcoholics in meetings where belief is integral
- Lets people practice believing until things actually improve
- **Lee Ann Kaskutas** (Alcohol Research Group): "People look around
  the room and think, *if it worked for that guy, I guess it can work
  for me*. **A community creates belief.**"

### Dungy's Indianapolis Colts

- After firing, **Indianapolis Colts** owner left impassioned message
- Colts had **Peyton Manning** but just finished dreadful season
- Dungy implemented same system: first season 10-6, next 12-4
- Same troubling pattern: choked under play-off pressure

### Jamie's Death and the Team's Transformation

- 3 days before Christmas: son **Jamie** found hanging in apartment
- Dungy returned to sidelines — team lost first play-off game
- **Something changed**: "We had seen Coach through this terrible
  thing and all of us wanted to help him somehow"
- Players gave in to Dungy's vision in way they hadn't before
- **They started to believe**

### The 1994 Harvard Study

- Studied people who radically changed their lives
- Some after personal tragedy, some after watching friend's struggle
- Just as frequently: **no tragedy** preceded transformations
- Changed because embedded in **social groups that made change easier**

> "Change occurs among other people. It seems real when we can see
> it in other people's eyes." — **Todd Heatherton**, psychologist

### The 2006 Conference Championship

- Colts finished 12-4, won first play-off game, beat Baltimore
- January 21, 2007: conference championship vs. **New England Patriots**
- First half: Colts fell apart, **Manning** intercepted, Patriots led 21-3
- **No team in NFL history had overcome so big a deficit in
  conference championship**
- Halftime: Dungy told team **they had to believe**

> "This is *our* game. It's *our* time."

- Second half: Colts executed automatic plays, scored 76-yard drive
- Fourth quarter: teams traded points, Colts tied then fell behind 34-31
- Final drive: moved 70 yards in 19 seconds, crossed into end zone (38-34)
- **60 seconds left**: **Tom Brady** moved Patriots halfway down field
- 17 seconds remaining: **Marlin Jackson** read visual cues (gap width,
  running back depth), anticipated passing play
- Jackson plucked ball from air for interception — **game over**
- Two weeks later: **won Super Bowl**

> "We're proud to have won this championship for our leader, Coach
> Dungy." — **Peyton Manning**

### The Golden Rule Summary

- Habit cannot be eradicated — **must be replaced**
- Keep same cue and reward, insert new routine
- For permanent change, **people must believe change is possible**
- **Belief most often emerges with help of a group**
- Changing any habit requires determination and self-understanding
- Physical addiction to nicotine lasts only ~100 hours — many lingering
  urges are **behavioral habits** asserting themselves

## Chapter 4: Keystone Habits

### Paul O'Neill's Background

- Made lists his entire life; at Fresno State finished in 3 years
- 1960: one of 300 chosen from 3,000 for federal internship
- Veterans Administration → Pentagon → deputy director of OMB at age 38
- **Discovered institutional habits**: government spending driven by
  automatic routines, not logical decisions

### Organizational Habits

- Post-WWII hospital-building program still running 25 years later
  — bureaucrats automatically built hospitals regardless of need
- **Geoffrey Hodgson**: "Routines are the organizational analogue of habits"
- NASA applauded failed rocket launches to encourage risk-taking
- EPA's **William Ruckelshaus** engineered aggressive enforcement culture

### Move to Alcoa

- 1977: left government, joined International Paper, became president
- **Alcoa (1987)**: struggling company, 15,000 employees had gone on strike,
  burned dummies in parking lots
- O'Neill's chosen keystone habit: **worker safety**
- "Everyone deserves to leave work as safely as they arrive"
- **Audacious goal**: Zero injuries, no matter the cost

### The Safety Program's Design

- To prevent injuries → understand WHY they happen → study HOW
  manufacturing goes wrong → teach efficient processes
- **Core insight**: Correct work is also safer work
- **Habit loop**: Cue = any injury; Routine = unit president reports
  to O'Neill within 24 hours with prevention plan; Reward = promotion
- Required new communication from bottom to top, transforming hierarchy

### The Fatal Accident

- Six months in: Arizona plant worker crushed by swinging machine arm
- O'Neill ordered emergency meeting, re-created accident with diagrams

> "We killed this man. It's my failure of leadership. I caused his
> death." — O'Neill

- **Bill O'Rourke** (executive): "That meeting really shook everyone up.
  He was serious about this stuff, serious enough to stay up nights
  worrying about some employee he'd never met."
- Within a week: safety railings repainted yellow, new policies written
- **Noticeable decline in injury rate** — a small win

### Ripple Effects

- Unions embraced measuring worker productivity (identified safety risks)
- Managers welcomed workers shutting down production lines when overwhelmed
- Safety habits spilled into personal lives
- Workers started calling O'Neill's home — not about accidents but about
  **other great ideas**

### The Aluminum Siding Plant Transformation

- Struggled for years guessing popular colors, paid consultants millions
- Low-level employee suggested: group painting machines together, switch
  pigments faster, respond to demand shifts
- **Profits doubled in one year**
- Employee had been suggesting this for a decade but never told management
- Safety culture opened the communication channel

### Keystone Habits in Individual Lives

- **Exercise studies**: people who exercise start eating better, becoming
  more productive, smoking less, using credit cards less
- **James Prochaska** (University of Rhode Island): "Exercise spills over"
- **Family dinner**: children with better homework skills, higher grades,
  greater emotional control
- **Making your bed**: correlated with better productivity, stronger budgeting
- **Not causation but chain reaction**: initial shift helps other habits

### Characteristics of Keystone Habits

- Create **"small wins"** that help other habits flourish
- Establish cultures where change becomes contagious

### Michael Phelps and Small Wins

- 2008 Beijing: 200-meter butterfly race day
- 6,000+ calories over 16 hours, elaborate stretching regime
- Ankles so flexible they extended 90+ degrees (farther than ballerina)

### Bob Bowman's Keystone Habit Strategy

- Phelps started swimming at 7 to burn off energy
- Trouble calming down before races, parents divorcing
- **Bowman's insight**: perfect physique AND capacity for obsessiveness
- Provided habits to make him strongest **mental** swimmer

### The Videotape Visualization Habit

- End of each practice: "Watch the videotape before sleep and when you wake"
- Mental movie of perfect race — jumping off blocks, swimming flawlessly,
  every stroke, wall, finish, water dripping off lips
- Phelps would lie in bed watching entire competition until he knew
  each second by heart
- Eventually became automatic — Bowman whispered "Get the videotape ready"
  and Phelps would settle down and crush competition

### Small Wins Defined

- **Cornell professor (1984)**: "Once a small win has been accomplished,
  forces are set in motion that favor another small win"
- Small wins fuel transformative changes by leveraging tiny advantages

### Gay Rights Movement Example

- Late 1960s: initial efforts yielded only failures
- **Early 1970s**: American Library Association focused on ONE goal —
  reclassifying books from "Abnormal Sexual Relations" category
- **1972**: Library of Congress agreed, created new classification
- Ripple: fundraising, openly gay politicians, state anti-discrimination laws
- **1973**: American Psychiatric Association rewrote definition so
  homosexuality was no longer mental illness

### The Beijing Race with Broken Goggles

- Moisture leaked into goggles — by third turn, completely blind
- **Bowman had once made him swim in Michigan pool in the dark**
- Phelps had mentally rehearsed goggle failure
- Estimated 19-20 strokes for final push, started counting
- At 20 strokes: videotape said make huge stroke, glide, touch wall
- Timed it perfectly — ripped off goggles: **"WR" (world record)**

> "It felt like I imagined it would." — Michael Phelps

### Barton's Firing and Cultural Enforcement

- Valuable executive fired for violating safety culture
- **Keystone habits create cultures where new values become ingrained**
- Special vocabularies ("Core Programs," "Safety Philosophies") act as
  suitcases containing whole conversations about priorities

> "Not sharing an opportunity to learn is a cardinal sin." — O'Neill

### Grit at West Point

- All measured factors (grades, physical aptitude, self-discipline)
  mattered less than **"grit"** — maintaining effort despite failure
- Grit emerges from cultures cadets create: small groups forming
  keystone habits ("the musketeers")

### O'Neill's Legacy

- Retired 2000, became Treasury Secretary (2001-2002)
- Now teaches hospitals about worker safety and habits
- **IBM**, **McKinsey**, **Goldman Sachs** embraced keystone habits
- Alcoa 2010: 82% of locations had zero lost days due to injury

### Jeff Shockey's Parking Spot Story

- New plant manager painted over reserved parking spot titles
- Whoever arrives earliest gets best spot — "Every person matters"
- Extension of O'Neill's culture — "electrified the plant"

## Chapter 5: Starbucks and the Habit of Success

### Travis Leach's Story

- Parents were heroin addicts, father overdosed when Travis was 9
- Dropped out at 16, couldn't control anger at car wash, McDonald's,
  Hollywood Video
- Regular customer suggested Starbucks — became barista
- **At 25**: manages two Starbucks, 40 employees, $2M+ annual revenue
- Salary $44k, 401(k), no debt, never late

> "Starbucks is the most important thing that has ever happened to me."

### Starbucks as Educator

- **137,000+ employees, 1M+ alumni** — one of nation's largest educators
- 50+ hours in classrooms first year, workbooks earn college credits
- **Core focus: teaching willpower**

### Willpower Research

- **2005 UPenn study of 164 eighth-graders**: willpower predicted academic
  performance **more robustly than IQ**
- High willpower: higher grades, selective schools, fewer absences
- Best way to strengthen willpower: **make it into a habit**

> "Their willpower occurs without them having to think about it."
> — **Angela Duckworth**

### Starbucks' Willpower Curriculum

- Late 1990s: $4 coffee required cultivating joyful environment
- Spent **millions developing curricula** on self-discipline
- Growth: sleepy Seattle company → 17,000+ stores, $10B+ annual revenue

### Mark Muraven's Willpower Experiments

#### The Radish-and-Cookie Experiment (Case Western Reserve)

- Students faced cookies and radishes after skipping a meal
- Half told to eat cookies (ignore radishes); half the opposite
- Then given impossible puzzle
- **Cookie eaters** (unused willpower): worked ~19 minutes
- **Radish eaters** (depleted willpower): worked only ~8 minutes

> "Willpower isn't just a skill. It's a muscle, and it gets tired as
> it works harder." — Mark Muraven

### Australian Exercise Studies (Oaten & Cheng)

## Chapter 8: Saddleback Church and the Montgomery Bus Boycott

### Three-Part Process of Social Movements

1. **Starts** because of social habits of friendship and strong ties
2. **Grows** because of weak ties and community peer pressure
3. **Endures** because leaders give participants new habits creating
fresh sense of identity and ownership

### Weak Ties: Mark Granovetter's Research

- Harvard PhD student studied how 282 men found employment
- Expected friends helped most — but **weak-tie acquaintances were
often MORE important** for landing jobs
- Weak ties give access to social networks where we don't belong
- Without weak ties, momentum doesn't spread beyond the clique
- Peer pressure = sense of obligation communities place on themselves
- Ignoring social obligations = risk losing social standing

### Freedom Summer (1964)

- **Doug McAdam** (University of Arizona) analyzed 720 applications
- Motives didn't explain who went vs. stayed home; neither did
opportunity costs
- **Answer**: strong and weak ties working in tandem
- Students who participated were enmeshed in communities where both
close friends and casual acquaintances expected them to go
- Among applicants who mentioned religious orientation AND belonged to
religious organization: **every single one** went to Mississippi
- Secular organization members could withdraw with little decline in
social hierarchy

> "A miracle had taken place... Noisy youngsters could be heard singing

- Parks found guilty; 500+ blacks crowded courthouse

### The Neurology of Sleepwalking

- **Mark Mahowald** (University of Minnesota): "Sleepwalking is a
reminder that wake and sleep are not mutually exclusive"
- Most of the time during sleep, **brain stem paralyzes limbs** —
known as **"the switch"**
- Some brains experience switching errors: bodies active while dreaming
- Sleepwalkers can open eyes, drive cars, cook meals — all essentially
unconscious

### Sleep Terrors: The Critical Distinction

- During sleep terrors, brains shut down except most primitive
**"central pattern generators"** — same areas where habit loops reside
- No possibility of conscious intervention when sleep deactivates
prefrontal cortex
- People in sleep terrors have jumped off roofs, killed babies,
committed assaults
- **Jules Lowe**: acquitted of murdering 83-year-old father (90+
injuries) — sleep terror defense
- **Donna Sheppard-Saunders**: acquitted of attempted murder (pillow
suffocation) — sleepwalking defense
- **More than 150 murderers and rapists** escaped punishment using
automatism defense in past century

### Reza Habib's Neuroscience Study (2010)

- Twenty-two people in MRI watching slot machine: half pathological

- **Key finding**: pathological gamblers' brains showed more excitement
about winning; near misses looked like wins neurologically

- Brain regions involved: basal ganglia and brain stem — same areas
where habits reside and sleep terrors start
- **Slot machines reprogrammed**: more constant near wins

## Appendix: A Reader's Guide to Using These Ideas

### Step Four: Have a Plan

- Change requires **planning for the cue** and **choosing a behavior**
that delivers the reward
- **Implementation intentions**: plans specifying actions in response
to cues

#### Author's Cookie Habit Example

- **Cue**: roughly 3:30 PM
- **Routine**: go to cafeteria, buy cookie, chat with friends
- **Reward discovered**: not the cookie — **socialization**
- **Plan**: "At 3:30, every day, walk to a friend's desk and talk for
10 minutes"
- Set alarm; initial failures; after a few weeks became automatic
- Six months later: watch lost but at 3:30 he absentmindedly stands
up and finds someone to talk to
- Framework provides starting point; some habits harder than others;
change often requires repeated experiments and failures
