Chapter 1: The General Theory

Imagine you've been taught your entire life that the earth is flat. Every textbook says so. Every professor you've ever had agrees. Then one day, someone tells you: "Actually, the earth is round. And not just a little round — everything you learned that depends on it being flat is wrong too."

That's essentially what Keynes is doing in this opening chapter. He's telling every economist in the world: the theory you've been teaching for a hundred years? It only works in one very specific situation — when every single person who wants a job already has one. That's like having a theory of weather that only works on sunny days. It might be internally consistent, but it's useless for understanding storms.

The "classical" economists (Ricardo, Marshall, Pigou, and basically everyone who taught economics at a university) focused almost entirely on how an economy distributes resources among different uses. How much goes to wages, how much to profits, what gets produced where. But they took for granted the much bigger question: why are millions of people sitting at home with nothing to do? They assumed the answer was so obvious it barely needed discussion. Keynes says that assumption is where everything goes wrong.

This chapter is only three pages long, but it's a declaration of intellectual war. Keynes is saying: I'm going to build a theory that works in the real world — where unemployment is the norm, not the exception — and everything you think you know will have to change.

Chapter 2: What Classical Economics Gets Wrong

Here's what the old-school economists believed about jobs, boiled down to its essence: if there's unemployment, wages will fall. When wages fall, it becomes cheaper to hire people. When it's cheaper to hire, businesses hire more. Problem solved. The only people without jobs are those who refuse to work for the going rate.

Keynes says: look at what actually happens. During the Great Depression, millions of Americans were desperate for work at any wage. Factories sat idle. Crops rotted in fields. Was this because workers were demanding too much money? Obviously not. Something else was going on.

Here's the key insight. Workers don't bargain for "real wages" (how many loaves of bread their paycheck buys). They bargain for money wages (the number on the check). They'll fight hard against a pay cut from $20/hour to $18/hour. But if prices go up 10% while their pay stays at $20, they barely notice — even though the effect on their purchasing power is similar. Why? Because a pay cut feels like you specifically are being punished, while inflation hits everyone equally.

This seemingly small observation blows up the entire classical framework. If workers care about money wages rather than real wages, then the neat self-correcting mechanism doesn't work. You can't assume that unemployment will fix itself through wage adjustments.

Keynes uses a brilliant analogy. Classical economists, he says, are like geometry teachers who insist that parallel lines never meet. When you show them parallel lines meeting (mass unemployment that doesn't fix itself), they don't question their axioms. They blame the lines for not being straight enough. The real problem is that their axioms don't match the world they're living in.

The classical view assumes wage cuts increase hiring. Keynes shows that wage cuts also reduce spending, potentially making things worse.

Chapter 3: Effective Demand — The Big Idea

This is the chapter where Keynes drops the bomb. His entire revolution comes down to one concept: effective demand. Here's what it means in plain language.

Think of the economy as having two sides. On one side, businesses decide how many people to hire based on what they expect to sell. On the other side, those hired people (and everyone else) decide how much to spend. The number of jobs in the economy is set where these two sides meet — where what businesses expect to sell matches what people actually want to buy. That meeting point is effective demand.

Now here's the crucial twist. When people earn more money, they spend more — but not ALL of it. If you get a $1,000 raise, you might spend $800 and save $200. Keynes calls this the "fundamental psychological law." It seems harmless, but it has devastating implications.

As a society gets richer, the gap between what it produces and what it consumes keeps growing. The only thing that can fill that gap is investment — businesses building new factories, buying new equipment, developing new products. If investment doesn't fill the gap, you get unemployment. Not because anything is "broken," but because the math simply doesn't add up.

Consumer spending alone can never reach what businesses need. Investment must fill the gap. If it doesn't, jobs disappear.

This creates a painful paradox. A poor country where people spend almost everything they earn doesn't need much investment to achieve full employment. A rich country where people save a lot needs enormous amounts of investment just to keep everyone working. The richer you get, the harder it is to maintain full employment.

Keynes points out that this idea isn't actually new. Thomas Malthus argued something similar a century earlier. But David Ricardo demolished Malthus in debate, and the concept of effective demand was buried for a hundred years. Ricardo "conquered England as completely as the Holy Inquisition conquered Spain." Keynes is here to dig it back up.

Chapter 4: How to Measure an Economy

Before building his theory, Keynes has to deal with a measurement problem. Classical economists loved talking about "national output" and "the general price level," but Keynes thinks these concepts are meaninglessly vague. How do you add up the total output of an economy that produces everything from haircuts to battleships? You can't just count objects — three haircuts and one battleship don't add up to four of anything.

Keynes's solution is simple and clever: measure everything in terms of labor. Define a "labor-unit" as one hour of ordinary work and a "wage-unit" as what that hour gets paid. Now you have a common yardstick. Instead of asking "How much is the economy producing?" (an unanswerable question), ask "How many people are working?" (a question with a clear answer).

This isn't just a technical detail. By choosing employment as his fundamental unit, Keynes is making a philosophical statement: economics should be about whether people have jobs, not about abstract measures of output that nobody can actually calculate.

Chapter 5: Expectations Run Everything

Here Keynes makes a point that sounds obvious but has radical implications: businesses don't hire people based on what's happening right now. They hire based on what they expect to happen in the future. And there are two very different kinds of expectations.

Short-term expectations are about next month's sales. These are fairly predictable — businesses mostly assume current trends will continue, and they're usually roughly right. Not much drama here.

Long-term expectations are about whether it makes sense to build a new factory, develop a new product, or expand into a new market. These are essentially guesses about a fundamentally unknowable future. And they can change suddenly and dramatically, for reasons that may have nothing to do with objective reality.

The critical implication: mere changes in expectation can generate cyclical movements in the economy, even if nothing in the "real" world has changed. If business owners collectively become pessimistic about the future — maybe a political crisis, maybe a financial scare, maybe just a mood shift — they reduce investment. Reduced investment means fewer jobs, which means less income, which means less spending, which seems to confirm the original pessimism. The expectation becomes self-fulfilling.

The economy, at any given moment, is the result of overlapping decisions made under different expectations at different times. Some factories being built today were planned during an optimistic period. Some being abandoned were started during pessimism. It's like a palimpsest of past moods. This is why the economy always seems to have contradictory things happening at once — because it literally does. And it's why employment depends on expectations, not realized results. By the time last quarter's profits are tallied, the hiring decisions those profits should inform have already been made based on guesses.

Chapter 6: Saving Equals Investment (Always)

This chapter makes a point that confuses almost everyone at first: saving and investment are always exactly equal. Not "tend toward equality" or "are equal in equilibrium" — they are identical by definition, all the time, no matter what.

Here's why. Your income is everything you earn. Your consumption is everything you spend. Saving is the difference: income minus consumption. Investment is everything produced that wasn't consumed. Since everything produced was either consumed or not consumed, and everything earned was either spent or not spent, saving must equal investment. They're just two words for the same thing, viewed from different angles.

"But wait," you might say, "I can save money by putting it in the bank without anyone investing it." True for you individually. But your act of saving reduces someone else's income (because you didn't buy their product), which forces them to "save" less (or dissave). For the economy as a whole, it's a wash. Every buyer has a seller; every saver enables an investment somewhere in the chain.

This matters enormously because classical economics assumed the interest rate was the "price" that brings saving and investment into balance. If they're always equal anyway, the interest rate must be doing something else entirely.

Chapter 7: Cleaning Up Old Definitions

This is Keynes doing intellectual housework. In his earlier book, A Treatise on Money, he used different definitions of saving and investment that didn't require them to be equal. That caused massive confusion, and he spends this chapter correcting himself and addressing critics.

He also tackles the concept of "forced saving" — the idea that when banks create new money, people are "forced" to save because inflation erodes their purchasing power. Keynes thinks this concept is a muddle. Under full employment, it has a clear meaning (more money chasing the same goods means some consumption is effectively redirected to investment). But people keep using the term when the economy has lots of unemployed resources, where it doesn't really apply.

The honest self-correction is notable. Keynes doesn't pretend he was always right. He admits his earlier definitions caused confusion and abandons them "with much regret." It's unusual for an intellectual giant to publicly acknowledge his own past mistakes this clearly.

Chapter 8: Why People Spend What They Spend

Now we get to the first of Keynes's three big variables: the propensity to consume — fancy language for "how much of their income do people spend?"

The answer, Keynes says, follows a basic psychological law: when people earn more, they spend more — but not as much more as they earned. Get a $100 raise and you might spend $80 of it and save $20. This seems trivially obvious. But it means that as a society gets richer, a bigger and bigger chunk of income goes unspent. That unspent portion needs to be absorbed by investment, or jobs disappear.

Keynes uses the 1929 crash as an example. Through the 1920s, American industry expanded enormously. All that new equipment generated huge "sinking funds" — money set aside to eventually replace the equipment. This money wasn't being spent on consumption, and it wasn't creating new investment either. It was just sitting there, creating a growing hole in demand. By 1929, the hole was too big, and the economy fell in.

The chapter ends with a line that captures Keynes's entire worldview: "Consumption is the sole end and object of all economic activity." We don't produce things for the joy of producing them. We produce them so people can use them. Any theory that forgets this is building castles in the air.

Chapter 9: Why People Save What They Save

Keynes identifies eight reasons people save money, and they range from sensible to neurotic:

1. Precaution — having a rainy-day fund. 2. Foresight — saving for retirement or kids' education. 3. Calculation — earning interest. 4. Improvement — wanting to gradually live better over time. 5. Independence — the feeling of power that comes from having money. 6. Enterprise — building up a war chest for business ventures. 7. Pride — wanting to leave an inheritance. 8. Avarice — plain old miserliness.

But here's the kicker: classical economics said higher interest rates encourage more saving, which is good for the economy. Keynes flips this completely. Higher interest rates discourage investment (because borrowing costs more). Less investment means less income for everyone. Less income means less saving, not more. You can't make people save more by paying them higher interest if the higher interest is simultaneously destroying the income they'd save from.

"Virtue and vice play no part," Keynes writes. "It all depends on how far the rate of interest is favorable to investment." The old moral framework — saving is virtuous, spending is sinful — isn't just wrong. It's actively harmful when it leads to policies that choke off demand.

Chapter 10: The Multiplier — How $1 Becomes $5

This chapter introduces one of the most powerful ideas in economics: the multiplier effect. It works like this.

Say the government builds a $100 million bridge. The construction workers earn that $100 million and spend, say, 80% of it ($80 million) at local shops and restaurants. Those shopkeepers and waiters then spend 80% of their new income ($64 million). The next round spends $51.2 million. And so on. By the time the ripples die out, that original $100 million has generated $500 million in total economic activity. The multiplier is 5.

Each dollar of investment circulates through the economy multiple times, generating far more income than the original spending.

The formula is simple: the multiplier equals 1 divided by the fraction people save. If people save 20% (spend 80%), the multiplier is 1/0.20 = 5. If people save 10% (spend 90%), the multiplier is 10. A poor country where people spend almost everything has a huge multiplier but little investment to multiply. A rich country has more investment but a smaller multiplier because people save more.

This leads to Keynes's most famous thought experiment. If the government buried banknotes in old mines and let people dig them up, the resulting spending would eliminate unemployment. Obviously it would be better to build useful things. But if politics prevents useful spending, useless spending is still better than no spending at all. Ancient Egypt thrived on pyramid-building. Medieval Europe thrived on cathedral-building. "Two pyramids, two masses for the dead, are twice as good as one; but not so two railways from London to York."

Chapter 11: Expected Returns on New Investments

The second of Keynes's three big variables is the marginal efficiency of capital — which in plain English means: "What return does a business expect to get from building something new?"

Imagine you're deciding whether to build a new factory. You estimate it will cost $10 million. You project it will generate profits of $1.5 million per year for ten years. The "marginal efficiency" is the discount rate that makes those future profits equal to today's cost. If it works out to 8%, and the interest rate is 5%, you build the factory. If the interest rate is 10%, you don't.

The crucial word here is expected. You don't actually know what profits the factory will generate. You're guessing. And as more factories get built, the expected return on the next factory falls (because there's more competition and the best opportunities get taken first). Investment keeps going until the expected return on the next project falls to equal the interest rate. Then it stops.

There's also a nasty problem with risk. Both the borrower (who might not earn what they expect) and the lender (who might not get repaid) add risk premiums. This effectively doubles the hurdle rate for investment. During booms, both sides get reckless and the premiums shrink. During busts, both sides get terrified and the premiums balloon. This makes investment far more volatile than the underlying economic conditions warrant.

Chapter 12: Animal Spirits and the Beauty Contest

This is the most famous chapter in the book, and for good reason. It's where Keynes explains why financial markets are inherently unstable — not because of external shocks, but because of human psychology.

Start with a simple fact: nobody knows the future. When you invest in a ten-year project, your knowledge of what will happen "amounts to little and sometimes to nothing." So how do people make investment decisions? They follow a convention: they assume whatever is happening now will keep happening, unless they have a specific reason to think otherwise. Nobody actually believes this, but it provides a basis for action. The problem is that when the convention breaks — when people suddenly stop believing that current conditions will persist — everything can change overnight.

Now add organized stock markets. In the old days, when an entrepreneur built a factory, they were committed for the long haul. Today, you can buy shares in a company on Monday and sell them on Friday. This feels like "liquidity" — the freedom to change your mind. But here's Keynes's insight: the investments themselves haven't become liquid. Only the ownership has. The factory still exists. The community is still committed. The stock market just makes it possible for individual investors to pretend they're not committed.

This creates a perverse incentive. Instead of trying to figure out what a company is actually worth over the long term, investors focus on predicting what other investors will think. Keynes compares it to a newspaper beauty contest where you don't pick the prettiest face — you pick the face you think other people will pick as prettiest. And the really sophisticated players try to predict what other people think other people will think. It's turtles all the way down.

Keynes's beauty contest: investors don't value companies — they predict what other investors will predict about other investors' predictions.

The result: professional investment becomes a game of "Snap, Old Maid, Musical Chairs — a pastime in which he is victor who says 'Snap' neither too soon nor too late, who passes the Old Maid to his neighbor before the game is over, who secures a chair for himself when the music stops." Timing beats fundamentals.

Finally, there are animal spirits — the spontaneous optimism that drives people to start businesses, build things, and take risks. Most investment decisions, Keynes says, are made not from cold calculation but from a gut urge to act rather than sit still. If that urge fades — if people become frightened or pessimistic — no amount of low interest rates will get them investing again. The economy depends on the moods of entrepreneurs the way a garden depends on sunshine: essential, unpredictable, and impossible to manufacture on demand.

Chapter 13: What Interest Rates Really Are

Here Keynes redefines what interest rates actually mean, and this is one of his most important contributions.

The old explanation: interest is the reward for saving. You give up spending today, and the interest rate compensates you for your patience. Sounds reasonable, right?

Keynes says: think about it more carefully. If you save money and stuff it under your mattress, you're saving — but you earn zero interest. Saving and earning interest are two different things. What you earn interest for is not saving but giving up liquidity — letting go of cash, that infinitely flexible asset that you can use for anything at any time.

Interest is the price of persuading people to hold their wealth in something other than cash. It's determined by the supply of money (controlled by the central bank) meeting the demand for money (determined by how anxious people are to hold cash). This is Keynes's liquidity preference theory.

The interest rate (r*) is set where money supply meets people's desire to hold cash. More money → lower rates. More anxiety → higher rates.

This changes everything. If the interest rate is about liquidity rather than saving, then raising rates doesn't encourage saving — it just makes people more anxious to hold cash. And monetary policy works not by encouraging thrift but by changing how badly people want to hold onto money versus bonds and other assets.

Chapter 14: Why the Old Theory of Interest Is Wrong

The classical economists said: the interest rate is determined by the balance between saving (supply of funds) and investment (demand for funds). When people save more, the interest rate falls, which encourages more investment. It's a beautiful self-correcting mechanism.

Keynes says: there's a fatal flaw. This story assumes that income stays constant while saving and investment adjust. But that's impossible! When people try to save more, they spend less. When they spend less, businesses earn less. When businesses earn less, income falls. The whole framework collapses because it ignores the very thing it's trying to explain: changes in income.

Here's the practical difference. The old view says: if people save more, interest rates fall, investment rises, everyone benefits. Keynes says: if people save more, spending falls, businesses lay off workers, and income drops until people can no longer afford to save that much. Saving and investment end up equal again — but at a lower level of income and employment, not at a lower interest rate.

Keynes reserves special contempt for the Austrian school economists (Hayek, von Mises, Robbins) who argued that lowering interest rates artificially below their "natural" level distorts the economy. Their conclusions, Keynes says, are gotten "exactly the wrong way round." He quotes Ibsen: the theory "has dived down to the bottom — as deep as she can get — and bitten fast hold of the weed and tangle and all the rubbish that is down there."

Chapter 15: Why People Hoard Cash

Keynes identifies four reasons people hold cash instead of investing it:

1. The income motive — you need cash between paychecks. 2. The business motive — companies need cash between paying for inputs and receiving sales revenue. 3. The precautionary motive — you want a cushion for emergencies. 4. The speculative motive — you think bond prices might fall, so you'd rather hold cash and buy later at a better price.

The first three are fairly stable and predictable. The fourth — the speculative motive — is where all the action is. It's the channel through which monetary policy affects the economy. When the central bank pumps money into the system, it works by changing people's calculations about whether to hold cash or bonds.

But there's a trap. As interest rates get very low, the risk of holding bonds increases (because rates can only really go up from there, which means bond prices can only go down). At some point, everyone prefers cash because bonds are too risky. This is the practical floor below which monetary policy alone can't push rates — what later economists would call a "liquidity trap."

Keynes also makes a subtle but important point: the interest rate is more a convention than a fundamental economic variable. Whatever rate people expect to persist will persist, because expectations are self-fulfilling. This makes change hard: a rate "chronically too high for full employment" can persist for decades simply because everyone expects it to.

Chapter 16: Capital Isn't What You Think

Classical economics treated capital as "productive" — machines make stuff, so machines deserve a return. Keynes challenges this framing. A machine doesn't earn a return because it's productive. It earns a return because there aren't enough machines. If you built enough machines to satisfy all demand, the return on the next machine would be zero — even though it's just as physically productive as all the others. It's better, Keynes says, "to speak of capital as having a yield" rather than being inherently productive.

This distinction matters enormously. If capital is productive, then the interest rate is a natural reward for creating something useful. But if capital merely earns a yield because it's scarce, then the interest rate is just a toll extracted by scarcity — and scarcity is something that can be changed by policy.

Capital earns a return because it's scarce. And it's kept scarce by the interest rate on money, which sets a floor: nobody will invest in a factory that earns less than they could get from just holding cash. This means money — a purely human invention — is what prevents us from building enough productive capacity to make capital free. The "prospective yield" of any investment depends not on abstinence or savings but on expectations of future effective demand. Owners of wealth don't desire the capital-asset itself — they desire the income it will produce. And the alternative of simply holding money and debts is always available, setting the minimum standard that real investment must clear.

Keynes also dispenses with the classical notion that "roundabout" production processes are more efficient because they're lengthy. A longer process may or may not be more productive — there's no inherent advantage to taking more time. And ultimately, "labour is the sole factor of production" operating within a given environment of natural resources, technique, and equipment. Capital is accumulated labor, not an independent source of value.

Chapter 17: Why Money Beats Everything

This chapter contains one of Keynes's most original insights. Every asset — wheat, copper, houses, money — has its own "rate of return" composed of three elements: (1) its yield (what it produces or earns), (2) its carrying cost (what it costs to store or maintain), and (3) its liquidity premium (the convenience of being able to convert it instantly into anything else). Wheat has a yield (you can eat it or sell it) but also high carrying costs (it rots, it needs a warehouse). Houses have rent but depreciation. Money has virtually no yield at all and negligible carrying costs, but it has something nothing else has: perfect liquidity.

Because of this liquidity advantage, money's "own rate of interest" (yield minus cost plus liquidity premium) is typically higher than that of physical assets. And the economy gravitates toward whichever asset has the highest own-rate — this is the asset that "rules the roost," setting the standard that all other investments must beat. Money wins this competition because the forces that reduce returns on other assets do not operate for money. Build more houses and rents fall. Grow more wheat and prices drop. But you can't "build more money" in a way that erodes its liquidity premium.

This is why money acts as a bottleneck on the entire economic system. Investment in physical capital has to clear the hurdle set by money's return. If it can't, the investment doesn't happen — even if the factory would be genuinely useful, even if workers are idle and want jobs. The monetary system, which humanity invented to facilitate economic activity, ends up setting a floor under interest rates that keeps capital artificially scarce and employment permanently below its potential.

Every asset has a yield, a cost, and a liquidity premium. Money dominates because its liquidity premium vastly outweighs its zero yield — and unlike physical assets, building more of them doesn't reduce money's advantage.

Keynes makes this vivid with a thought experiment. Imagine a world where money spoiled like wheat — where holding cash cost you something, like Gesell's stamped money. Then money would lose its unfair advantage. Interest rates could fall to zero or near-zero, and investment would proceed until capital was abundant enough that its marginal return was negligible. The economy would be far wealthier. The only thing stopping this is the peculiar nature of money itself: it keeps too well, it costs nothing to hold, and everyone wants it.

Chapter 18: Putting It All Together

Halfway through the book, Keynes restates his complete system in one place. Here's the entire theory in a nutshell:

Three psychological variables → consumption + investment → effective demand → employment. That's the entire General Theory.

Three independent variables determine everything: (1) the propensity to consume (how much people spend), (2) the marginal efficiency of capital (expected returns on new investment), and (3) the interest rate (set by money supply meeting liquidity preference). These determine consumption and investment, which together make up effective demand, which determines how many people have jobs.

Keynes then describes why the economy oscillates around a point "appreciably below full employment" without either collapsing entirely or achieving lasting prosperity. Four stabilizing forces prevent extremes: the multiplier is big enough to matter but not so big that small changes cascade into catastrophe; moderate interest rate changes don't swing investment wildly; sticky wages prevent a deflationary death spiral; and capital equipment wears out over time, eventually creating new investment needs even after a crash.

The key message: this sub-optimal equilibrium "is not established by the laws of necessity." It's a fact of observation that can be changed through policy. The economy's resting point is a choice, not a fate.

Chapter 19: Cutting Wages Doesn't Fix Unemployment

This is one of the most important chapters for real-world policy. The standard prescription for unemployment was simple: cut wages. If labor is cheaper, businesses will hire more. The argument sounds so intuitive that it dominated economics for over a century. Keynes devotes an entire chapter to dismantling it piece by piece, and his demolition is devastating.

The logic sounds airtight for a single business. If one restaurant cuts its workers' pay, its costs go down, it can lower prices, attract more customers, and maybe hire more people. But what works for one business doesn't work for all businesses at once. When every business cuts wages simultaneously, every worker has less income. With less income, they buy fewer meals at restaurants. The restaurant that cut wages now has fewer customers, not more. This is the classic "fallacy of composition" — assuming what's true for one part must be true for the whole.

Keynes systematically works through seven channels by which wage cuts might affect employment. He finds that most are neutral or actively harmful:

The classical prescription: cut wages to create jobs. The Keynesian reality: wage cuts trigger a feedback loop that destroys demand, increases real debt burdens, and can spiral into depression.

Here's how the seven channels play out. Channel 1: Redistribution. Wage cuts transfer income from workers to employers, creditors, and rentiers. But workers spend almost all their income while the wealthy save a large share. So total spending drops. Channel 2: Expectations. If wages fall and people expect them to keep falling, they postpone purchases — why buy now when everything will be cheaper next month? This depresses demand further. Channel 3: Debt. Wages and prices fall but debts are fixed in money terms. A family owing $500 on a mortgage is worse off when wages drop from $100 to $80 per week — the debt hasn't shrunk, but their ability to repay it has. When debts become unbearable, bankruptcies cascade.

Channels 4 through 7 deal with effects on foreign trade, interest rates, and business confidence. The foreign trade effect is the only one that might help (cheaper exports), but it works only if your country alone cuts wages while others don't — and once everybody tries it, the advantage disappears. Interest rates might fall if prices fall (since the real money supply increases), but this is a roundabout and unreliable mechanism. And the devastating effect on business confidence from ongoing wage deflation tends to overwhelm any small gains elsewhere.

Keynes drives the point home with a real-world example. In Australia, the government tried to fix real wages by legislation. The result was wild oscillations in employment, because the economy was trying to absorb rigid wages through violent swings in output rather than smooth adjustments. The only escape was that the legislation wasn't very effective and Australia wasn't a closed economy.

He then takes on Professor Pigou, the leading Cambridge economist and defender of the classical view. Pigou had published a book titled The Theory of Unemployment that, Keynes argues, was "strangely titled" because it actually assumed full employment in its equations. Pigou had three unknowns but only two equations — he got around this gap by simply assuming what he was trying to prove. In the appendix, Keynes shows that Pigou's "elasticity of real demand for labor" is essentially a disguised version of Keynes's own multiplier, and that Pigou's system has "one equation short" of being a real theory.

His conclusion is forceful: "The maintenance of a stable general level of money-wages is, on a balance of considerations, the most advisable policy." If you need to adjust real economic conditions, use monetary policy (change the money supply) rather than wage cuts. Flexible wages are only practical in an authoritarian society where the government can decree universal, simultaneous changes. "One can imagine it in Italy, Germany, or Russia, but not in France, the United States, or Great Britain."

Keynes also reveals his preference for the long run: let wages rise slowly while keeping prices stable. Mildly rising wages are psychologically encouraging to workers, they gradually diminish the real burden of debts, and they make it easier to maintain full employment because employers expect future demand to grow. Deflation is always harder to manage than gentle inflation — an insight that central banks wouldn't formally embrace for another sixty years.

Chapter 20: The Math of Employment

This is the most technical chapter in the book, full of elasticities and equations that would make most readers' eyes glaze over. But the key ideas beneath the math are powerful and practical. Keynes is building the machinery for answering a crucial question: if you increase spending by a certain amount, exactly how many jobs does that create?

The answer depends on what he calls the employment function — the inverse of the supply function. For any level of spending, it tells you how many workers get hired. The twist is that this varies enormously by industry. Construction has high "employment elasticity" — most of the money goes to hiring workers. Oil refining has low employment elasticity — most of the money goes to capital equipment and profits.

This leads to the main insight: it matters where spending goes, not just how much there is. A million dollars spent on building houses creates far more jobs than a million dollars spent on automated manufacturing. The reason goes beyond the obvious. Industries with low employment elasticity give a larger share of revenue to profits rather than wages. Since owners save more than workers, money flowing to low-elasticity industries generates a weaker multiplier effect — less of it gets re-spent into the economy.

There's an additional complication. Products with longer "periods of production" — things that take a long time to make, like ships or factories — show lower initial employment elasticity. When demand first increases, it takes a while for these industries to ramp up hiring. Consumption goods, ironically, have the longest effective period because they represent the final stage of all productive processes (you need raw materials, then components, then assembly, then distribution, then retail). This means a sudden increase in consumer demand doesn't translate into a sudden increase in employment — it ripples backward through the supply chain over time.

Keynes also connects this to the classical theory. If the classical assumption held (that real wages always equal the marginal disutility of labor), then the concept of employment elasticity would have "no field of application." You couldn't increase employment by increasing spending, because wages would simply rise in proportion, leaving real conditions unchanged. But since the classical assumption doesn't hold, there's room for policy: increase spending until real wages fall to the point where full employment is reached.

The practical policy implication is direct: if you're trying to reduce unemployment, spend on labor-intensive sectors. A dollar spent on building schools creates more jobs than a dollar spent on financial engineering. And target industries where the money reaches workers' pockets quickly, because those workers will spend it, creating more demand, creating more jobs — the multiplier doing its work.

Chapter 21: When Do Prices Rise?

Classical economists had a simple theory about money and prices: increase the money supply and prices rise proportionally. Double the money, double the prices. This "quantity theory of money" treated the economy like a bathtub — pour in more water (money) and the level (prices) rises uniformly. Keynes says this is only true under a very specific condition — full employment — that rarely exists.

But first, Keynes makes a deeper point about economics itself. He argues that economics has been crippled by a false division between "value theory" (how prices of individual goods are determined) and "monetary theory" (how the overall price level behaves). In reality these are inseparable. Money isn't just a convenient unit of measurement — it's a link between the present and the future. People hold money not just to buy things today but because they're uncertain about tomorrow. You can't discuss economic expectations without discussing money.

In reality, what happens when you increase the money supply depends on how much slack is in the economy. Keynes identifies five complicating factors that make the relationship between money and prices far messier than the simple theory suggests:

First, effective demand doesn't change in exact proportion to the money supply — some of the new money gets absorbed into idle cash balances. Second, resources aren't identical — as you hire more workers, you're pulling in less productive ones, so output per worker falls (diminishing returns). Third, resources aren't interchangeable — some industries hit capacity while others still have plenty of slack. This creates "bottlenecks" where particular products become scarce and their prices spike even though the economy as a whole still has unemployment. Fourth, wages start rising before full employment is reached, because each group of workers gains bargaining power as the job market tightens. Fifth, the costs of using up equipment (what Keynes calls "user cost") may jump sharply as businesses run their machines harder.

Below full employment, more money creates more jobs. At full employment, it just raises prices. In between, you get a mix of both.

Keynes defines "true inflation" as the point where more spending produces zero additional output and 100% price increase. But in practice, this sharp boundary doesn't exist. Prices and employment both respond to spending changes, in varying proportions depending on how much slack remains. The result is a gradual transition: at high unemployment, more money creates almost entirely more jobs; near full employment, more money creates almost entirely higher prices; in between, you get an increasingly unfavorable mix.

The view that any increase in money supply is inflationary, Keynes says, "is bound up with the classical assumption that we are always at full employment." If you're not at full employment, more money mainly means more jobs. He's dismissive of mathematical economists who dress up the quantity theory in fancy notation: "Too much recent 'mathematical' economics are mere concoctions, as imprecise as the initial assumptions they rest on."

The chapter ends with a beautiful historical observation about the nineteenth-century balance. From 1820 to 1914, the world somehow maintained a rough equilibrium: interest rates around 3-5%, prices roughly stable (Sauerbeck's price index varied by only 50% across the entire century), employment below full but not catastrophically so. How? Interest rates were modest enough to encourage reasonable investment, employers were strong enough to keep wages from outpacing productivity, and the gold standard was flexible enough to allow tolerable monetary conditions. As Walter Bagehot observed, "John Bull can stand many things, but he cannot stand 2 per cent" — meaning that if interest rates dropped below 2%, the rentier class would revolt and destabilize the system.

But Keynes warns that this nineteenth-century balance cannot be recreated. The expected returns on investment (the marginal efficiency of capital) are now much lower than they were a century ago. The interest rate needed for full employment may be lower than wealth-owners are willing to accept. The "time-honoured methods" of gentle monetary adjustment may no longer be enough. Something more radical — active management of investment — may be required.

Chapter 22: Booms and Busts

Now Keynes applies his full theoretical apparatus to explain why economies go through boom-and-bust cycles — the "trade cycle" that had plagued capitalism since its inception. The answer is not interest rates or monetary policy. It's the collapse of expectations, specifically the marginal efficiency of capital — the expected return on new investment.

Booms build gradually as optimism compounds. Busts arrive suddenly when confidence collapses. Recovery is slow because confidence cannot be commanded — it must return on its own.

During a boom, businesses become increasingly optimistic. They build new factories, hire more workers, expand production. Their optimism feeds on itself: the more others invest, the better the economy performs, which justifies even more optimism. The later stages of the boom, Keynes writes, are "characterized by optimistic expectations as to the future yield of capital- goods sufficiently strong to offset their growing abundance and their rising costs of production." But the optimism is running ahead of reality. There are now more factories than the economy really needs, and each new one earns a little less than the last.

The key characteristic of the cycle is its asymmetry. The upswing is gradual — forces propelling the system upward "gather force cumulatively" and only gradually lose strength. But the downturn is sudden and violent. There is "no such sharp turning-point when an upward is substituted for a downward tendency." Booms die in a sudden cardiac arrest; recoveries are slow convalescences.

When enough people realize that the expected returns on all these new investments won't materialize, confidence collapses — not gradually but "suddenly and even catastrophically." The organized investment markets amplify the panic: most buyers are "largely ignorant of what they buy," and speculators are more concerned with predicting what other investors will think than with actually calculating returns. When disillusion falls on an "over-optimistic, over-bought market," the crash is inevitable.

What follows is the most important insight about recessions: they're intractable because the thing that needs to be fixed can't be fixed by policy. The problem is that business confidence has evaporated. You can lower interest rates, but that only helps if people want to borrow. You can increase the money supply, but cash piles up in bank vaults if nobody wants to invest. "It is this, indeed, which renders the slump so intractable." The return of confidence "is not susceptible to control in an economy of individualistic capitalism." This, Keynes acknowledges, is the aspect of slumps that bankers and businessmen are right to emphasize — economists had underestimated how impotent "purely monetary" remedies could be.

Recovery, when it finally comes, is governed by two factors: the lifespan of durable assets (how long it takes for the oversupply to wear out) and the carrying costs of surplus stocks (which force businesses to absorb excess inventory within 3-5 years). Keynes identifies four distinct phases of the slump: first, investment in new stocks offsets disinvestment in working capital; second, both decline together (the worst period); third, surplus stocks are gradually absorbed; fourth, when recovery is well advanced, both reinvestment and restocking move in the same positive direction.

Keynes illustrates his theory with the United States in 1928-29. Employment was "very satisfactory" by normal standards — there was no labor shortage except in a few specialized groups. But the previous five years had seen enormous investment. Correct foresight would have shown that expected returns were destined to fall to unprecedentedly low levels. Maintaining growth on a sound basis would have required very low long-term interest rates. But raising rates high enough to check the stock market speculation would have "checked every kind of reasonable new investment." It was "curing the disease by killing the patient."

He also notes the stock market's effect on consumption. In a "stock-minded" country like the United States, a rising market isn't just about investment returns — it makes people feel wealthier and more willing to spend. A severe market decline "naturally has a very depressing influence" on consumption, especially on leveraged investors who see their paper wealth evaporate. This consumption channel makes the stock market an amplifier of cycles, not just a reflection of them.

The New Deal gets a cameo. When Roosevelt began substantial loan expenditure, agricultural and other surplus stocks were at very high levels. The early New Deal was partly a "strenuous attempt to reduce these stocks," which actually offset the stimulative effects of government spending elsewhere. It took about two years for surplus stocks to be worked off. "Only when this had been completed was the way prepared for substantial recovery."

Keynes's policy prescription is counterintuitive: the remedy for a boom is not higher interest rates (which kill good investments along with bad ones) but lower rates that allow sustainable expansion to continue. Don't try to prevent booms. Try to prevent busts. "The right remedy for the trade cycle is not to be found in abolishing booms and thus keeping us permanently in a semi-slump; but in abolishing slumps and thus keeping us permanently in a quasi-boom." The view that booms need to be checked by high interest rates is "dangerously and unnecessarily defeatist." The wisest course is to advance on both fronts: manage investment to prevent wild swings while also raising consumption through redistribution, because even misdirected investment produces the "social advantage of increased consumption."

Chapter 23: The History Keynes Rescued

This is the most entertaining chapter in the book — a sweeping tour through centuries of economic thought that mainstream economics had dismissed as confused or superstitious. Keynes rehabilitates them all, arguing that generations of practical thinkers had grasped truths that academic economists spent two hundred years denying.

The mercantilists (1500s-1700s) believed that accumulating gold and maintaining a favorable trade balance were essential to prosperity. Economists since Adam Smith considered this hopelessly naive. Keynes says they were onto something real. In a world where money was gold, more gold meant lower interest rates, which meant more investment and more jobs. Their obsession with trade balances was a primitive but genuine understanding that monetary conditions affect employment. And they never made the mistake of thinking interest rates were "self-adjusting" — they were emphatic that "unduly high interest rate was the main obstacle to the growth of wealth."

Keynes traces the intellectual lineage through specific thinkers. Gerard Malynes wrote in 1622 that "plenty of money decreaseth usury in price or rate." Petty explained in 1676 how interest naturally falls when the quantity of money increases. John Locke saw that money had two distinct values: its "value in use" (given by the interest rate, like rent on land) and its "value in exchange" (its purchasing power, like a commodity). Locke stood "with one foot in the mercantilist world and one in the classical world" but never achieved a genuine synthesis. The mercantilists also despised hoarding — state accumulation of treasure was "shunned like the pest" because it robbed circulation. Keynes says he can't find "a single dissentient voice" on this point.

But Keynes is careful with the mercantilists. Their policies had real limitations: if your country's wages rose from too much gold flowing in, your trade advantage reversed (as happened to Spain in the 15th-16th centuries). If low domestic interest rates triggered excessive foreign lending, gold flowed right back out (Britain's problem in the 19th century). India was chronically impoverished despite enormous inflows of precious metals because of an insatiable "liquidity-preference" — the desire to hoard rather than invest. And immoderate mercantilist policies could lead to "senseless international competition for a favourable balance which injures all alike." The element of truth was real, but so were the dangers.

On international economics, Keynes is prescient. The gold standard, he argues, was "never in history a method devised of such efficacy for setting each country's advantage at variance with its neighbours'." A country that manages its own interest rates and investment for domestic full employment, by contrast, helps both itself and its trading partners. "It is the policy of an autonomous rate of interest, unimpeded by international preoccupations, and of a national investment programme directed to an optimum level of domestic employment which is twice blessed in the sense that it helps ourselves and our neighbours at the same time."

The usury laws of medieval Europe prohibited or restricted charging interest on loans. Economists dismissed this as religious superstition. Keynes says the medieval scholars who debated usury were making "an honest intellectual effort" to keep interest rates low enough that productive investment could flourish. They intuitively grasped that high interest rates choke economic growth. Even Adam Smith himself favored "a moderate application of usury laws." It was Bentham who objected that they were too harsh on "projectors" — entrepreneurs striking into new channels of invention. But Keynes notes that Bentham was writing in 1787, at the dawn of the greatest age of investment inducement in history. It was only the exuberance of the Industrial Revolution that could have made economists lose sight of the problem of insufficient investment.

Silvio Gesell (1862-1930) was a successful German merchant in Buenos Aires whose experience of the Argentine financial crisis of the late 1880s turned him to monetary theory. He proposed "stamped money" — currency notes that would lose value unless periodically stamped (like insurance cards), at a rate of about 5.4% per year. This would give money a "carrying cost" like any other commodity, eliminating the special advantage that cash has over real goods. Among professional economists, only Irving Fisher recognized his significance. Gesell even served briefly as Minister of Finance in Bavaria's short-lived Soviet government in April 1919. Keynes says "the future will learn more from the spirit of Gesell than from that of Marx." But Gesell's theory had a fatal gap: he never explained why the interest rate is positive in the first place, because "the notion of liquidity-preference had escaped him." And if you imposed carrying costs on currency, people would simply substitute bank deposits, foreign money, or jewellery as stores of value.

Mandeville's Fable of the Bees (1723) told the story of a prosperous beehive that decided to embrace thrift and virtue. The result: economic collapse. "The Building Trade is quite destroy'd, Artificers are not employ'd." The moral: "Bare Virtue can't make Nations live in Splendour." The book was convicted as a public nuisance by the grand jury of Middlesex. Only Dr. Johnson defended it, declaring it "opened his eyes into real life very much." Keynes sees Mandeville as essentially right, and notes how the Victorian era that followed buried his insight — Petty's vision of "entertainments, magnificent shews, triumphal arches" gave way to "the penny-wisdom of Gladstonian finance" that "could not afford" hospitals, open spaces, noble buildings, or the splendours of music and drama.

Malthus argued that too much saving could stifle economic growth — that "an attempt to accumulate very rapidly... by greatly impairing the usual motives to production must prematurely check the progress of wealth." Ricardo was "stone-deaf to what Malthus was saying," and Ricardo won the debate. For over a century, the problem was "dismissed from economics not by solving it but by not mentioning it."

Hobson and Mummery revived the argument in their 1889 book The Physiology of Industry and were blacklisted from academic economics for their trouble. Hobson's London Extension Board refused to let him teach political economy after an economic professor read the book and declared it "equivalent in rationality to an attempt to prove the flatness of the earth." Mummery — a businessman who also discovered a new route up the Matterhorn and died attempting Nanga Parbat in 1895 — was as daring in economics as in mountaineering. Their preface challenged the doctrine that "saving enriches and spending impoverishes the community along with the individual." Keynes calls them half-right: they identified the real problem (excessive saving suppressing demand) but laid too much emphasis on under-consumption and overlooked the role of interest rates and business confidence.

"The extraordinary achievement of classical theory," Keynes observes drily, "was to overcome the beliefs of 'natural man' and, at the same time, to be wrong." He remembers the Conservative politician Bonar Law's "mingled rage and perplexity" when economists denied what was obvious to anyone with eyes. One recurs, he says, to the analogy between the sway of classical economics and that of certain religions — "far greater exercise of the potency of an idea to exorcise the obvious than to introduce the recondite."

Chapter 24: What Should We Do?

The final chapter is where economics becomes politics. Keynes identifies two fundamental flaws in capitalism: it fails to provide full employment, and it distributes wealth too unequally. His General Theory has implications for both.

On inequality: The traditional defense of wealth concentration was that rich people's savings fund investment. Keynes has just spent an entire book arguing the opposite: in an economy with inadequate demand, the rich hoarding their money is the problem, not the solution. Income redistribution — through progressive taxes, death duties, public services — would raise consumption, increase demand, and actually help investment rather than hurt it.

Keynes doesn't want complete equality. Some inequality is useful: it channels "dangerous human proclivities" into relatively harmless competition over bank balances rather than over political power. "It is better that a man should tyrannize over his bank balance than over his fellow-citizens." But the game doesn't need to be played for such high stakes. Lower the prizes and people will still play.

The euthanasia of the rentier: This is Keynes's most radical vision. If the state maintains full employment long enough, the sheer abundance of capital will drive investment returns toward zero. The "rentier" — the person who earns money simply by owning capital, contributing nothing productive — would gradually become obsolete. Not through revolution, but through the quiet elimination of scarcity. "Interest today rewards no genuine sacrifice, any more than does the rent of land."

Socialization of investment does NOT mean the government takes over factories. It means the government takes responsibility for ensuring the total amount of investment is enough for full employment. Private businesses still decide what to build and how. The government just ensures there's enough building happening overall. "There is no more reason to socialize economic life than there was before."

International peace: When nations can achieve full employment at home, they have no need to fight over export markets. Trade ceases to be a weapon used to shift unemployment onto neighbors. "Let goods be homespun whenever reasonably possible, and above all, let finance be primarily national."

The book closes with its most quoted passage. "The ideas of economists and political philosophers, both when they are right and when they are wrong, are more powerful than is commonly understood. Indeed the world is ruled by little else." Practical men who think they're immune to intellectual influence are "usually the slaves of some defunct economist." The General Theory itself would prove this point better than any example Keynes could have imagined: within a decade of its publication, it had transformed how every major government in the world managed its economy.

The General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money (Plain English)
John Maynard Keynes · 1936 · 662 pages

A conversational guide to the book that invented macroeconomics. Keynes showed that economies can get stuck in unemployment not because anything is broken but because spending, investment, and confidence don't automatically align — and that governments have both the ability and the responsibility to fix it.
