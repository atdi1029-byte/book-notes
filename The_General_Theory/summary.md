# The General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money — Summary

**Author:** John Maynard Keynes
**Published:** 1936
**Pages:** ~400 (662 PDF pages including front/back matter)

## Overview

*The General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money* is the
foundational text of macroeconomics and arguably the most
influential economics book of the twentieth century. Written
during the Great Depression, Keynes set out to demolish the
orthodox "classical" economics — the tradition running from
Ricardo through Marshall and Pigou — which held that free
markets naturally tend toward full employment. Keynes argued
that this classical theory was not wrong in its internal logic
but fatally wrong in its premises, applicable only to a special
case (full employment) that rarely corresponds to reality.

In its place, Keynes constructed a "general theory" in which
the level of employment is determined by aggregate effective
demand, which itself depends on three independent variables:
the propensity to consume, the marginal efficiency of capital,
and the rate of interest. The book demonstrates that an economy
can settle into a stable equilibrium well below full employment
and remain there indefinitely — that involuntary unemployment
is not a temporary aberration but a chronic feature of
unregulated capitalism. This was a revolutionary claim that
reshaped government policy worldwide.

The book is addressed to fellow economists, not the general
public, and Keynes acknowledged it would be a "struggle of
escape from habitual modes of thought." Dense, discursive, and
often combative, it mixes rigorous theoretical argument with
historical examples, biting polemics against predecessors, and
sweeping social philosophy. Its final chapter envisions a
reformed capitalism where the state manages aggregate demand
to secure full employment while preserving individual liberty
and the advantages of decentralized markets.

## Core Thesis

The central argument is that the volume of employment in a
modern economy is not determined by wage bargains between
workers and employers (as classical theory assumed) but by the
level of aggregate effective demand — the total spending on
consumption and investment. Because individuals tend to save a
rising proportion of income as wealth grows, and because
investment depends on volatile expectations and the interest
rate, there is no automatic mechanism ensuring that total
spending will be sufficient for full employment. The state must
therefore take responsibility for managing effective demand,
primarily through influencing investment.

## Key Concepts

- **Effective Demand**: The point where aggregate demand
  intersects aggregate supply, determining the actual level of
  employment. This is the core of the entire theory.

- **The Propensity to Consume**: The functional relationship
  between income and consumption. Keynes's "fundamental
  psychological law" holds that people increase consumption as
  income rises, but not by as much as the increase in income.

- **The Multiplier**: Introduced by R.F. Kahn and adapted by
  Keynes. An increment of investment generates a multiple
  increase in income and employment, the size determined by
  the marginal propensity to consume.

- **The Marginal Efficiency of Capital**: The expected rate of
  return on a new investment, comparing prospective yields to
  the asset's supply price. Investment proceeds until this rate
  falls to equal the interest rate.

- **Liquidity Preference**: The desire to hold wealth as cash
  rather than interest-bearing assets. The interest rate is
  determined by the interaction of money supply with liquidity
  preference — it is the reward for parting with liquidity, not
  the reward for saving.

- **Animal Spirits**: The spontaneous optimism and urge to
  action that drives entrepreneurial investment decisions. Most
  long-term investment decisions are driven not by precise
  calculation but by confidence, temperament, and convention.

- **Say's Law ("Supply Creates Its Own Demand")**: The
  classical axiom Keynes rejected. He showed that decisions to
  save and decisions to invest are made by different people for
  different reasons, with no mechanism ensuring equality at
  full employment.

- **Involuntary Unemployment**: Unemployment that exists when
  workers would accept work at current wages but no jobs are
  available — a concept the classical framework could not
  accommodate.

- **The Paradox of Thrift**: In a wealthy community, attempts
  to save more (consume less) can reduce effective demand,
  lower income, and actually decrease both investment and
  total savings.

- **The Euthanasia of the Rentier**: Keynes's vision that
  sustained full employment and abundant capital would
  gradually reduce the interest rate toward zero, eliminating
  the "functionless investor" who earns returns purely from
  capital scarcity.

- **The Beauty Contest Metaphor**: Keynes's famous analogy for
  stock market behavior — investors try to anticipate what
  other investors will think, not what an asset is truly worth,
  leading to destabilizing speculation.

- **Socialization of Investment**: Keynes's policy conclusion
  that the state must take responsibility for the overall
  volume of investment, though not necessarily for its
  direction or for ownership of the means of production.

## Chapter Summary

**Chapter 1 (The General Theory):** Keynes declares his purpose:
to challenge classical economics, which applies only to the
special case of full employment, not the general case.

**Chapter 2 (Postulates of Classical Economics):** Identifies
and rejects the second classical postulate — that real wages
equal the marginal disutility of labor — thereby making
involuntary unemployment theoretically possible.

**Chapter 3 (The Principle of Effective Demand):** Introduces
effective demand as the intersection of aggregate supply and
demand, summarizing the theory in eight propositions and
explaining why Ricardo's victory over Malthus buried the
concept for a century.

**Chapter 4 (The Choice of Units):** Rejects the vagueness of
concepts like "national dividend" and proposes two fundamental
units: money-values and quantities of employment (labor-units).

**Chapter 5 (Expectation as Determining Output):** Distinguishes
short-term from long-term expectations; shows that employment
depends on expectations, not realized results, and that mere
changes in expectation can generate cyclical movements.

**Chapter 6 (Income, Saving, and Investment):** Defines income,
user cost, and supplementary cost with precision; proves that
saving and investment are necessarily and identically equal —
different aspects of the same bilateral transaction.

**Chapter 7 (Saving and Investment Further Considered):**
Corrects his own earlier definitions from the Treatise on Money,
engages critics (Hawtrey, Robertson), and addresses the confused
concept of "forced saving."

**Chapter 8 (Propensity to Consume: Objective Factors):** Lists
six objective factors affecting consumption and states the
fundamental psychological law; uses the U.S. 1929 crash and
British housing to illustrate how sinking funds suppress demand.

**Chapter 9 (Propensity to Consume: Subjective Factors):**
Catalogs eight motives for saving (precaution through avarice)
and argues that higher interest rates reduce income rather than
increase saving.

**Chapter 10 (The Multiplier):** Develops the investment
multiplier mathematically, estimates real-world values (2-5x),
and presents the famous buried-banknotes thought experiment and
the observation that pyramid-building and cathedral-construction
sustained ancient and medieval economies.

**Chapter 11 (Marginal Efficiency of Capital):** Defines
marginal efficiency as the discount rate equating an asset's
prospective yield to its supply price; shows investment depends
on expected, not current, returns; identifies the duplication of
borrower's and lender's risk.

**Chapter 12 (State of Long-Term Expectation):** The celebrated
chapter on speculation, animal spirits, and the beauty contest.
Argues that organized investment markets amplify instability and
that capital development conducted as a casino by-product will
be ill-done.

**Chapter 13 (General Theory of the Rate of Interest):**
Redefines interest as the reward for parting with liquidity
(not for saving); introduces the liquidity-preference function
M = L(r) and identifies four ways monetary expansion can fail
to stimulate the economy.

**Chapter 14 (Classical Theory of the Rate of Interest):**
Demolishes the classical view that interest equilibrates saving
and investment; shows the classical framework requires constant
income, which is internally contradictory; argues interest is
reward for not-hoarding, not not-spending.

**Chapter 15 (Incentives to Liquidity):** Elaborates the four
motives for holding cash (income, business, precautionary,
speculative); develops the two-part liquidity function M1 + M2;
explains why the interest rate is a conventional phenomenon
resistant to change.

**Chapter 16 (Nature of Capital):** Argues that saving does not
stimulate investment because prospective yield depends on
expected future demand, not abstinence; the money interest rate
sets a floor that keeps capital artificially scarce.

**Chapter 17 (Essential Properties of Interest and Money):**
Explains why money's interest rate dominates all others — money
has negligible carrying costs and high liquidity premium — and
argues capital should be described as "having a yield" rather
than being inherently "productive."

**Chapter 18 (General Theory Re-Stated):** Summarizes the full
system: given factors, independent variables (propensity to
consume, marginal efficiency, interest rate), dependent
variables (employment and income); explains why the economy
oscillates around a sub-full-employment norm without
catastrophic instability.

**Chapter 19 (Changes in Money-Wages):** Systematically
demolishes the case for wage flexibility as a cure for
unemployment through seven channels of analysis; concludes that
stable money-wages with flexible monetary policy is far
preferable to flexible wages.

**Chapter 20 (The Employment Function):** Develops the
mathematical framework for relating effective demand to
employment, introduces elasticities of employment and output,
and shows how demand composition affects aggregate employment.

**Chapter 21 (The Theory of Prices):** Bridges the false
division between value theory and monetary theory; identifies
five complicating factors (bottlenecks, wage pressure, non-
uniform costs) between the extremes of unemployment and true
inflation; critiques the quantity theory while salvaging a
generalized version.

**Chapter 22 (Notes on the Trade Cycle):** Explains business
cycles primarily through collapse in the marginal efficiency
of capital (not interest rate changes); argues slumps are
intractable because confidence cannot be commanded; proposes
that the remedy for booms is lower, not higher, interest rates.

**Chapter 23 (Mercantilism, Usury, Stamped Money, Under-
Consumption):** A sweeping intellectual history vindicating
mercantilists, medieval usury prohibitions, Gesell's stamped
money, and under-consumptionists (Malthus, Mandeville, Hobson)
against the classical orthodoxy that suppressed their insights.

**Chapter 24 (Concluding Notes on Social Philosophy):** Lays
out the policy implications: income redistribution to raise
consumption, socialization of investment (not production), the
euthanasia of the rentier, preservation of individual liberty,
international peace through domestic full employment, and the
supreme power of ideas over vested interests.

## Who Should Read This

This book is essential for anyone who wants to understand the
intellectual foundations of modern macroeconomics, fiscal policy,
and central banking. It is most rewarding for readers with some
background in economics who want to engage directly with
Keynes's arguments rather than relying on secondhand summaries.
Students of economic history, political economy, and the history
of ideas will find it indispensable — not just for what it
argues, but for how profoundly it reshaped the relationship
between governments and markets in the twentieth century.
