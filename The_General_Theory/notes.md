## Book 1: Introduction

## Chapter 1: The General Theory

- Title contrasts with **"classical" theory** dominant for ~100 years
- Classical postulates applicable to **special case only**, not the general case
- The special case is a **limiting point of possible equilibrium positions**
- Does not match the **actual economic society we live in**
- Classical teaching is **misleading and disastrous** when applied to experience

### Definition of "Classical Economists"
- Term invented by **Marx** for **Ricardo, James Mill**, and predecessors
- Keynes extends it to include followers of Ricardo: **J.S. Mill, Marshall, Edgeworth, Pigou**

### What Classical Theory Actually Addresses
- Most treatises focus on **distribution of a given volume** of employed resources
- Pure theory of **actual employment** of available resources seldom examined
- Fundamental theory deemed so simple/obvious it receives bare mention at most

## Chapter 2: The Postulates of the Classical Economics

### I. The Two Fundamental Postulates

**Postulate 1: The wage equals the marginal product of labor**
- Wage of employed person = value lost if employment reduced by one unit

**Postulate 2: Utility of wage equals marginal disutility of employment**
- Real wage is what employed persons judge sufficient to induce that volume of labor
- Compatible with **"frictional"** and **"voluntary" unemployment**
- Does **NOT admit "involuntary" unemployment**

### Four Possible Means of Increasing Employment (Classical View)
1. Improved organization/foresight (diminish frictional unemployment)
2. Decrease in marginal disutility of labor (lower real wage)
3. Increase in marginal physical productivity of labor in **wage-goods industries** (Pigou's term)
4. Increase in price of non-wage-goods vs. wage-goods

> "This, to the best of my understanding, is the stance of Professor Pigou's *Theory of Unemployment* — the only detailed account of the classical theory of employment which exists."

### II. Labor Behavior: Money-Wages vs. Real Wages

**Key observation**: Workers stipulate for **money-wages**, not real wages
- Resist money-wage cuts but don't withdraw labor when prices rise
- Fall in real wages due to rising prices does NOT cause labor withdrawal
- Classical school **tacitly assumes** this involves no theoretical change — but it does
- If labor supply is not a function of real wages alone, the argument breaks down

**Statistical fact**: When money-wages rising, real wages **almost always falling**; when money-wages falling, real wages rising — each occurs for independent reasons

**Great Depression evidence**: Not plausible that 1932 U.S. unemployment was due to
labor obstinately refusing wage reductions or demanding excessive real wages
- Wide variations in employment occur **without apparent change** in real demands or productivity

**Fundamental objection**: Second postulate assumes **real wages depend on wage bargains**
- But workers may have **no method** to bring money-wages into conformity with marginal disutility
- **Other forces determine the general real wage level** — this is a main theme

> "The traditional theory maintains, in short, *that the wage bargains between the entrepreneurs and the workers determine the real wage*"

### III. Why Workers Resist Money-Wage Cuts but Not Real Wage Reductions

- Money-wage struggles primarily affect **distribution of aggregate real wage** between labor groups
- Workers are **instinctively more reasonable economists** than the classical school
- Resist money-wage cuts to protect **relative real wages**
- Do NOT resist real wage reductions from **increased aggregate employment**

### IV. Defining "Involuntary" Unemployment

> "*Men are involuntarily unemployed if, in the event of a small rise in the price of wage-goods relatively to the money-wage, both the aggregate supply of labor willing to work for the current money-wage and the aggregate demand for it at that wage would be greater than the existing volume of employment.*"

- **Full employment**: absence of involuntary unemployment
- Classical theory best regarded as **theory of distribution in conditions of full employment**

### The Analogy to Non-Euclidean Geometry

- Classical theorists resemble **Euclidean geometers in a non-Euclidean world**
- Discovering parallel lines that meet, they rebuke the lines for not keeping straight
- Must **throw over the axiom of parallels** and work out non-Euclidean geometry
- Similarly must **throw over the second postulate** and work out a system where involuntary unemployment is possible

### V. The First Postulate Retained

- Real wages and employment volume are **inversely correlated** (diminishing returns)
- Employment increase requires decline in real wages — this Keynes retains
- But rejecting second postulate means the decline is **not caused by** labor demanding more

### VI. Say's Law: "Supply Creates Its Own Demand"

**J.S. Mill**: "All sellers are inevitably buyers... Could we suddenly double productive powers, we should double purchasing power."

**Marshall**: "A man purchases labor and commodities with that portion of his income which he saves just as much as he does with that he is said to spend."

- Doctrine never stated in crude form today but **still underlies the whole classical theory**
- Would collapse without it
- Based on **false analogy** from Robinson Crusoe economy
- Fallaciously supposes a **nexus** uniting decisions to abstain from present consumption with decisions to provide for future consumption
- The motives for the latter are **not linked in any simple way** with the former

**The "Axiom of Parallels"**: Assumption that demand price of output equals supply price
- Granted this, all follows: advantages of thrift, classical unemployment theory, quantity theory of money, *laissez-faire* in foreign trade

### VII. Three Assumptions of Classical Theory
1. Real wage = marginal disutility of existing employment
2. No involuntary unemployment exists
3. Supply creates its own demand (Say's Law)

- These three **stand and fall together** logically

## Chapter 3: The Principle of Effective Demand

### Basic Definitions
- **Factor cost**: amounts paid to factors of production
- **User cost**: amounts paid to other entrepreneurs + sacrifice from not leaving equipment idle
- **Income** of entrepreneur = output value minus (factor cost + user cost)
- **Aggregate Supply Function**: Z = phi(N)
- **Aggregate Demand Function**: D = f(N)

### The Point of Effective Demand
- Employment determined where D intersects Z
- At this point, entrepreneurs' profit expectations are maximized
- **"This is the substance of the General Theory of Employment"**

### Theory Summary: Eight Propositions

**(1)** Income depends on employment volume N

**(2)** Consumption expenditure D1 depends on **propensity to consume**

**(3)** Employment depends on D = D1 + D2 (consumption + investment)

**(5)** Employment depends on: aggregate supply function, propensity to consume, and volume of investment D2

**(8)** **Key psychological law**: when employment increases, D1 increases but **not by as much as D**
- Greater employment widens the gap between Z and D1
- Employment cannot increase unless D2 (investment) fills the gap

### The Paradox of Poverty Amid Plenty
- **Poor community**: consumes most of output — modest investment provides full employment
- **Wealthy community**: must find ample investment opportunities or face unemployment
- The richer the community, the wider the gap between actual and potential production

### Three Main Gaps in Knowledge
1. Analysis of **Propensity to Consume**
2. Definition of **Marginal Efficiency of Capital**
3. Theory of **Rate of Interest**

### Ricardo's Victory Over Malthus
- **Malthus** opposed Ricardo on effective demand but couldn't furnish an alternative
- **Ricardo conquered England** "as completely as the Holy Inquisition conquered Spain"
- Effective demand "ceased to be discussed" — could only live furtively in underworlds of **Karl Marx, Silvio Gesell, or Major Douglas**
- Not mentioned in works of **Marshall, Edgeworth, or Professor Pigou**

### Why Ricardo Won
- Different conclusions from ordinary person gave intellectual prestige
- Austere practice lent virtue; vast logical superstructure gave beauty
- Explained social injustice as inevitable incident in progress
- Afforded justification to free activities of individual capitalist
- Result: **signal failure for scientific prediction**, growing unwillingness to accord economists respect

### The Optimism Problem
- Celebrated **optimism** of traditional theory stems from neglecting insufficient effective demand
- Classical theory may represent how we'd *like* economy to behave

> "But to assume that it actually does so is to assume our difficulties away"

## Chapter 4: The Choice of Units

### The Three Perplexities
1. Choice of units for economic system as a whole
2. Part played by expectation in economic analysis
3. Definition of income

### Problems with Traditional Units
- **National Dividend** (Marshall/Pigou) = non-homogeneous complex, cannot be measured except in special cases
- Net output calculation requires comparing new and old equipment — an impossible task
- General price-level concept too vague for causal analysis

> "Our precision will be a mock precision if we try to use such partly vague and non-quantitative concepts as the basis of a quantitative analysis"

### Keynes's Two Fundamental Units
1. **Quantities of money-value** (strictly homogeneous)
2. **Quantities of employment** (can be made homogeneous)

- **Labor-unit**: hour of ordinary labor
- **Wage-unit**: money-wage of one labor-unit
- If E = wages bill, W = wage-unit, N = employment: **E = N.W**
- Special labor weighted proportional to remuneration

## Chapter 5: Expectation as Determining Output and Employment

### Two Types of Expectation
1. **Short-term**: price expected for "finished" output when committing to production
2. **Long-term**: future returns from purchasing/manufacturing capital equipment

- **All production ultimately satisfies a consumer** — but time elapses between costs and purchase
- Employment depends on **expectations**, not actually realized results
- Realized results only relevant insofar as they modify subsequent expectations
- A change in expectations produces full employment effect only **over considerable period**

### Long-Period Employment
- Every state of expectation has its **definite corresponding level of long-period employment**
- When new position has greater employment: rate of input most affected at beginning
- May rise to peak higher than new long-period level, then decline
- **Mere change in expectation** can produce oscillation of same shape as cyclical movement
- Economy always occupied with **overlapping activities** from various past expectation states

### Short-Term vs. Long-Term in Practice
- Short-term expectation revision is **gradual and continuous**, based on recent results
- Producers base expectations on most recently realized results continuing, except where definite reasons for change
- Can safely omit express reference to short-term expectation
- **Long-term expectations** for durable goods are **liable to sudden revision**
- Cannot be approximated or replaced by realized results

## Chapter 6: The Definition of Income, Saving, and Investment

### I. Income
- Entrepreneur sells output for A, purchases A1 from others, has equipment worth G
- **User cost (U)** = (G' - B') - (G - A1): sacrifice of equipment value from producing A
- **Prime cost** = Factor cost (F) + User cost (U)
- **Aggregate income = A - U** (completely unambiguous)

### Supplementary Cost and Net Income
- **Supplementary cost (V)**: expected depreciation beyond user cost (normal obsolescence)
- **Pigou**: depreciation "sufficiently regular to be foreseen, if not in detail, at least in the large"
- **Net income = A - U - V**
- **Windfall loss**: unforeseen capital value changes (war, earthquake, market shifts), charged to capital account
- Keynes's net income definition very close to **Marshall's income** (who took refuge in Income Tax Commissioners' practices)
- Also corresponds to **Pigou's** most recent definition of **National Dividend**

### Hayek's Alternative
- **Professor Hayek** suggests capital goods owner might aim at keeping income constant
- Keynes: "I doubt if such an individual exists"
- When Hayek infers saving/investment concepts are vague, only right if he means **net** saving/investment

### Income Proper vs. Net Income
- Mistake to put all emphasis on net income — only relevant to consumption decisions
- **Income proper** is the concept relevant to current production decisions, quite unambiguous
- In *Treatise on Money*, Keynes defined income in a special sense causing "considerable confusion"

### II. Saving and Investment
- **Saving** = Income - Consumption = A1 - U
- **Investment** = addition to capital equipment = A1 - U
- **Saving and investment are necessarily equal** — different aspects of the same thing
- Income = Consumption + Investment; Saving = Income - Consumption; therefore Saving = Investment

### The Bilateral Character of Transactions
- Equivalence of saving and investment emerges from **bilateral character of transactions**
- Producer on one hand, consumer or capital-equipment purchaser on the other
- Each entrepreneur's current investment = excess of equipment purchased over user cost

### The Banking System Illusion
- No one can save without acquiring an asset (cash, debt, or capital-goods)
- No one can acquire an asset without it being newly produced or someone else parting with it
- Bank credit creation: if bank grants credit allowing additional investment, **incomes necessarily increase** at rate exceeding increased investment rate
- Public exercises **"free choice"** dividing income increase between saving and spending
- Resulting savings are **just as genuine** as any other savings
- No one compelled to hold additional money unless deliberately preferring liquidity

> "The old-fashioned view that saving always involves investment, though incomplete and misleading, is formally sounder than the newfangled view that there can be saving without investment or investment without 'genuine' saving."

- Individual saving attempt by reducing consumption **affects others' incomes**, defeating itself
- Impossible for community to save less than current investment — attempt raises incomes until chosen savings = investment exactly
- **Closely analogous** to monetary theory: individual liberty to change money holdings reconciled with total money = cash created by banking system

> "Both these propositions follow merely from the fact that there cannot be a buyer without a seller or a seller without a buyer."

## Chapter 7: The Meaning of Saving and Investment Further Considered

### Treatise on Money Definitions
- In *Treatise*, income = "normal profit" (not realized profit)
- Excess of saving over investment meant entrepreneurs earning below normal profit
- **Self-criticism**: didn't clearly distinguish expected vs. realized results

> "I have decided to discard them — with much regret for the confusion which they have caused"

### Hawtrey's Critique: Liquid Capital
- **Mr. Hawtrey** emphasizes changes in **liquid capital** (undesigned increments in unsold goods)
- Keynes prefers emphasizing **total change in effective demand**, not just unsold stock changes
- Hawtrey's method cannot handle unused capacity factor equally well

### D.H. Robertson's Alternative
- Today's income = yesterday's consumption + investment
- Robertson's "excess of saving" = Keynes's "income is falling"
- Both attempt the same vital distinction: between **effective demand** and **income**
- If expectations always determined by yesterday's results, today's effective demand = yesterday's income

### Forced Saving
- Concept originates with **Bentham's "forced frugality"**: consequences of money increase under full employment
- Under full employment, real income cannot increase — additional investment involves forced frugality "at expense of national comfort and national justice"
- **Hayek** and **Robbins** use the term but with no definite relation to Keynes's distinction
- Extending to less than full employment **requires explanation or qualification** that modern writers overlook

## Book 3: The Propensity to Consume

## Chapter 8: The Propensity to Consume: I. The Objective Factors

### Defining the Propensity to Consume
- Functional relationship between income (in wage-units) and consumption: **Cw = chi(Yw)**
- **Subjective factors** (psychology, habits, institutions) change slowly — treat as given
- **Objective factors** drive short-period changes

### Principal Objective Factors
1. **Wage-unit changes**: consumption roughly proportional
2. **Income vs. net income differences**: practical importance doubtful
3. **Windfall capital-value changes**: much more important, wealth-owning class very susceptible
4. **Rate of time-discounting**: effect complex and uncertain; short-period influence secondary
5. **Fiscal policy**: income taxes, death-duties, sinking funds all affect propensity; more equal distribution increases propensity to consume
6. **Expectations of future income**: average out for community as whole

### The Fundamental Psychological Law

> "Men are disposed, as a rule and on the average, to increase their consumption as their income increases, but not by as much as the increase in their income."

- dCw/dYw is positive and less than unity
- Higher absolute income widens gap between income and consumption
- Employment can only increase **pari passu with investment** (unless propensity changes)

### Net Income vs. Income: The Asymmetry
- **Employment** = function of expected consumption + expected investment
- **Consumption** depends on **net income** (not gross income)
- When financial provision (sinking funds) exceeds actual current upkeep:
  - Excess neither increases current investment nor is available for consumption
  - More intense demand for new investment necessary for given employment level

### The US 1929 Example
- Rapid capital expansion of previous five years created enormous sinking funds and depreciation allowances
- Plant so huge that enormous new investment required merely to absorb financial provisions
- Almost hopeless to find still more investment to provide for savings of wealthy community at full employment
- **"This factor alone probably sufficient to cause slump"**
- "Financial prudence" continued through slump, creating serious obstacle to early recovery

### Great Britain 1935 Example
- Substantial house-building since war created sinking funds exceeding current repair needs
- Local authority sinking funds > 50% of amount spent on new developments
- **Ministry of Health** insisting on stiff sinking funds may be **aggravating unemployment**

### Kuznets Data (US 1925-1933)
- Net capital formation steady 1925-1929 (only 10% increase)
- Depreciation/depletion remained high even at slump bottom
- **Net capital formation suffered appalling collapse after 1929**: 1932 figure 95% below 1925-1929 average

> "Consumption — to repeat the obvious — is the sole end and object of all economic activity."

- Capital is not a self-subsistent entity existing apart from consumption
- Every weakening in propensity to consume weakens demand for **both** capital and consumption
- Each time we secure today's equilibrium by increased investment, we **aggravate the difficulty of securing tomorrow's**

> "We are reminded of 'The Fable of the Bees' — the gay of to-morrow are absolutely indispensable to provide a *raison d'être* for the grave of today."

## Chapter 9: The Propensity to Consume: II. The Subjective Factors

### Eight Motives for Individual Saving
1. **Precaution**: reserve against unforeseen contingencies
2. **Foresight**: provide for future needs (old age, family, education)
3. **Calculation**: enjoy interest and appreciation
4. **Improvement**: gradually increasing expenditure
5. **Independence**: sense of power to do things
6. **Enterprise**: *masse de maneuver* for speculative projects
7. **Pride**: bequeath a fortune
8. **Avarice**: pure miserliness

**Corresponding consumption motives**: Enjoyment, Short-sightedness, Generosity, Miscalculation, Ostentation, Extravagance

### Interest Rates and Saving

> "Aggregate saving is governed by aggregate investment; a rise in the rate of interest must have the effect of reducing incomes to a level at which saving is decreased in the same measure as investment."

- Higher interest might induce community to save more **if incomes unchanged**
- But higher interest retards investment, so incomes **must fall**
- **Obstinacy can bring only penalty, no reward**

> "Virtue and vice play no part. It all depends on how far the rate of interest is favorable to investment."

## Chapter 10: The Marginal Propensity to Consume and the Multiplier

### Defining the Multiplier
- **Marginal propensity to consume**: dCw/dYw
- DeltaYw = k * DeltaIw, where **k = investment multiplier**
- If community consumes 9/10 of income increment, multiplier = 10
- Concept introduced by **R.F. Kahn** (*Economic Journal*, June 1931)

### Factors Modifying the Multiplier
- **(i)** Financing: may raise interest rate, retarding other investment
- **(ii)** Confidence effects: government programs may increase liquidity-preference
- **(iii)** Foreign trade: part of multiplier benefits foreign employment
- Marginal propensity tends to **diminish as employment increases**

### Kahn's Quantitative Estimates
- Typical modern community probably consumes not much less than 80% of income increment
- Multiplier after offsets = not much less than 5 (closed system)
- Country with 20% foreign trade + large unemployment relief: multiplier may fall to 2-3x
- This explains how **small investment fluctuations generate much greater employment fluctuations**

### The Paradox of Thrift
- Greater marginal propensity = larger multiplier = greater employment disturbance
- **Poor community** (high marginal propensity): large multiplier but small absolute investment
- **Wealthy community**: small multiplier but large investment — effect on employment much greater
- Public works have **much larger aggregate effect** at severe unemployment than near full employment
- Public works of doubtful utility may pay for themselves at severe unemployment from diminished relief cost

### Wasteful Expenditure and the Famous Thought Experiment

> "If the Treasury were to fill old bottles with banknotes, bury them at suitable depths in disused coalmines... there need be no more unemployment and the real income of the community would probably become a good deal greater than it actually is."

- Would be more sensible to build houses, but if political difficulties exist, above is better than nothing
- **Ancient Egypt doubly fortunate**: pyramid-building + precious metals search — fruits couldn't stale with abundance

> "The Middle Ages built cathedrals and sang dirges. Two pyramids, two masses for the dead, are twice as good as one; but not so two railways from London to York."

## Book 4: The Inducement to Invest

## Chapter 11: The Marginal Efficiency of Capital

### Defining Marginal Efficiency
- **Prospective yield**: series of annuities Q1, Q2... Qn expected from an asset
- **Supply price**: replacement cost to produce one additional unit
- **Marginal efficiency of capital**: discount rate making present value of returns = supply price
- Investment proceeds until **marginal efficiency = current interest rate**

> "It depends on the rate of return expected to be obtainable on money if it were invested in a newly produced asset; not on the historical result."

### The Investment Demand-Schedule
- As investment increases, marginal efficiency diminishes (prospective yield falls + supply price rises)
- Aggregate schedules create the **investment demand-schedule**

### Irving Fisher's Contribution
- **"Rate of return over cost"** (*Theory of Interest*, 1930) = identical to Keynes's marginal efficiency
- Fisher uses the concept for the same purpose

### Expectation and Prospective Yield
- Critical confusion: marginal efficiency depends on **prospective**, not current yield
- **Expectation of falling money value** stimulates investment (raises marginal efficiency)
- **Expectation of rising money value** depresses it
- This dependence on expectations creates **violent fluctuations** explaining the Trade Cycle

### Two Types of Risk
- **Entrepreneur's (borrower's) risk**: doubts about earning prospective yield
- **Lender's risk**: moral hazard, security adequacy, monetary changes
- **Duplication problem**: both parties add risk premiums, effectively doubling the charge
- **During booms**: both risks estimated unusually and imprudently low

## Chapter 12: The State of Long-Term Expectation

### The Precarious Basis of Knowledge
- Knowledge for estimating 10-year yields "amounts to little and sometimes to nothing"
- Five-year estimates similarly precarious
- Without temptation to take chances, little investment from cold calculation alone

### Modern Organized Investment Markets
- **Separation of ownership and management** + daily revaluation = new instability
- Stock Exchange revalues investments daily, creating opportunity to revise commitments
- No sense building at cost exceeding purchase price of similar existing enterprise

### The State of Confidence
- Long-term expectation depends on most probable forecast AND **confidence** in that forecast
- State of confidence is not a separate factor — it is the **major factor determining marginal efficiency** (= investment demand-schedule)

> "The state of confidence, as they term it, is a matter to which practical men always pay the closest and most anxious attention. But economists have not analyzed it carefully."

### The Convention
- **Convention**: assume existing affairs continue indefinitely except for specific change reasons
- Doesn't mean we believe continuance — extensive experience shows this most unlikely
- Philosophically cannot be uniquely correct — insufficient basis for calculated expectation
- Stability persists **only as long as the convention itself persists**
- Organized markets allow individuals to feel "liquid" (though investments are **fixed for the community**)

### Old-Fashioned Enterprise vs. Modern Markets
- When enterprises owned by undertakers/friends, investment depended on "sanguine temperament"
- Average results probably disappointed hopes — **mixed game of skill and chance**
- Modern separation of ownership + organized markets = new instability factor
- Daily revaluations give individuals opportunity to revise commitments (not available to community)

### Factors Accentuating Precariousness
1. **Separation of ownership**: real knowledge in valuation has seriously declined
2. **Day-to-day fluctuations**: ephemeral changes have excessive, absurd influence (ice companies sell higher in summer)
3. **Mass psychology**: conventional valuation liable to **violent change** — waves of optimism and pessimism
4. **Professional focus on short-term**: energy occupied forecasting psychology, not long-term yield

> "They are concerned, not with what an investment is really worth to a man who buys it 'for keeps', but with what the market will value it at under the influence of mass psychology, three months or a year hence."

### The Beauty Contest Metaphor
- Like newspaper competitions picking prettiest faces from photographs
- Each competitor picks not faces **he** finds prettiest, but those **others** will pick
- We reach the **third degree**: anticipating what average opinion expects average opinion to be
- Some practice fourth, fifth, and higher degrees

> "Worldly wisdom teaches that it is better for reputation to fail conventionally than to succeed unconventionally."

### The Fetish of Liquidity
- Social object of skilled investment: defeat dark forces of time and ignorance
- Actual private object: **"to beat the gun"** — outwit the crowd, pass the bad half-crown to the other fellow
- **No such thing as liquidity of investment for the community as a whole**
- Investment becomes games of **Snap, Old Maid, Musical Chairs** — timing beats fundamentals

### Speculation vs. Enterprise
- **Speculation**: forecasting psychology of the market
- **Enterprise**: forecasting prospective yield over asset's whole life
- As investment markets improve, **risk of speculation predominating increases**
- Americans unduly interested in what average opinion believes average opinion to be
- Rare for Americans to invest "for income" like Englishmen — attach hopes to capital appreciation

> "When the capital development of a country becomes a by-product of the activities of a casino, the job is likely to be ill-done."

- London sins less than Wall Street — **Throgmorton Street inaccessible and expensive** vs. Wall Street
- **Government transfer tax** might be most serviceable reform to mitigate speculation in U.S.
- Making investment permanent/indissoluble (like marriage) would force focus on long-term prospects
- But creates dilemma: **liquidity facilitates but sometimes impedes new investment**

### The State of Credit
- Must consider confidence of **lending institutions**, not just speculator's confidence
- **Collapse requires weakening of either** speculative confidence or state of credit
- **Recovery requires revival of both** — strengthening of credit necessary but not sufficient

### Animal Spirits and Human Nature

- Large proportion of positive activities depend on **spontaneous optimism rather than mathematical expectation**
- Most decisions with long-term consequences taken as result of **animal spirits**
- Spontaneous urge to action rather than inaction
- **Not outcome of weighted average of quantitative benefits multiplied by probabilities**
- Enterprise pretends to be actuated by prospectus statements — only little more than expedition to South Pole based on exact calculation
- If animal spirits are dimmed, **enterprise will fade and die**
- Fears of loss may have no more reasonable basis than hopes of profit
- Economic prosperity **excessively dependent** on political/social atmosphere congenial to business
- Fear of Labour Government or New Deal depresses enterprise — **not result of reasonable calculation**
- Must have regard to **nerves, hysteria, digestions** of those upon whom spontaneous activity depends
- Should not conclude everything depends on irrational psychology — long-term expectation **often steady**
- **Innate urge to activity makes wheels go round** — rational selves choosing as best we can

### Factors Mitigating Ignorance
- **Buildings**: prospective yield dominated by near future; risk transferred via long-term leases
- **Public utilities**: substantial yield practically guaranteed by monopoly privileges
- **Public authority investments**: frankly influenced by presumption of social advantages

### Keynes's Skepticism of Monetary Policy
- Now **somewhat skeptical** of merely monetary policy directed at interest rate
- Expects State to take **ever greater responsibility for directly organizing investment**
- Fluctuations in marginal efficiency likely **too great to offset** by practicable interest rate changes

## Chapter 13: The General Theory of the Rate of Interest

### Interest Rate is NOT Return to Saving
- If a man hoards savings in cash, he **earns no interest** though saves just as much
- Interest rate is reward for **parting with liquidity** for a specified period

> "The rate of interest is not the 'price' which brings into equilibrium the demand for resources to invest with the readiness to abstain from present consumption. It is the 'price' which equilibrates the desire to hold wealth in the form of cash with the available quantity of cash."

### Liquidity-Preference
- **M = L(r)**: quantity of money = function of liquidity-preference at interest rate r
- This is where **money enters the economic scheme**

### Two Components of Time-Preference
- **Propensity to consume**: how much income consumed vs. reserved for future
- **Liquidity-preference**: in what form to hold command over future consumption

> "The mistake in the accepted theories of the rate of interest lies in their attempting to derive the rate of interest from the first of these two constituents of psychological time-preference to the neglect of the second."

### Why Liquidity-Preference Exists
- **Uncertainty about future interest rates** is the necessary condition
- If future rates certain, always more advantageous to hold debt than cash
- With uncertainty, risk of loss from holding long-term debt creates preference for cash
- Those differing from predominant market opinion have reason to **keep liquid resources to profit** when they turn out right
- Market price fixed at point where sales of "bears" and purchases of "bulls" are balanced

### Three Divisions of Liquidity-Preference
1. **Transactions-motive**: cash for current exchanges
2. **Precautionary-motive**: security as to future cash needs
3. **Speculative-motive**: profit from knowing better than market

### Multiple Transmission Failures
Money may fail to stimulate the economy if:
1. **Liquidity-preferences increase faster than money quantity**
2. **Marginal efficiency of capital falls faster than interest rate**
3. **Propensity to consume is falling**
4. **Prices rise before employment increases**

## Chapter 14: The Classical Theory of the Rate of Interest

### The Classical Position
- Interest rate = factor balancing investment demand with saving supply
- **Marshall**: "Interest tends towards equilibrium where aggregate demand for capital equals aggregate stock forthcoming at that rate"
- **Cassel** (*Nature and Necessity of Interest*): investment = demand for waiting; saving = supply of waiting
- **Carver** (*Distribution of Wealth*): interest equates marginal disutility of waiting with marginal productivity
- **Taussig**: "Rate of interest settles at a point where marginal productivity of capital suffices to bring out the marginal installment of saving"
- **Walras**: rate of interest is the variable bringing saving and investment to equality

### The Folk Theory
- Popular belief: when an individual saves, they automatically bring down the rate of interest
- This stimulates capital output; fall in interest just sufficient to stimulate output equal to increment of saving
- A **self-regulatory process** requiring no special intervention

### The Fatal Error
- Classical theory treats demand for capital and saving-out-of-income as independent curves determining interest rate
- **Assumes income is constant** — but this is inconsistent
- When either curve shifts, **income must change**
- Classical theory is **"nonsense"** because it doesn't allow for income changes

### The True Determinants
- **Saving and investment are the determinates, not the determinants**
- The true determinants: (1) propensity to consume, (2) marginal efficiency of capital, (3) rate of interest
- Traditional analysis knew saving depends on income but **overlooked that income depends on investment**

### Fundamental Practical Importance
- If what saving and investment determine is **not the rate of interest but employment volume**, outlook profoundly changed
- Decreased readiness to spend will be seen not as increasing investment but as **diminishing employment**

### Interest: Reward for Not-Hoarding, Not Not-Spending
- The mistake: regarding interest as reward for **waiting** as such, instead of reward for **not-hoarding**
- Only if money used solely for transactions (never as store of value) would different theory apply
- Classical school had quite different theory in Volume I (value) vs. Volume II (money), with no bridge between them

### Ricardo's Theory
- "The interest of money is not regulated by the rate at which the Bank will lend... but by the rate of profit which can be made by the employment of capital"
- Valid only under assumption of **always full employment** — only one rate compatible with full employment in long period
- Ricardo offers "supreme intellectual achievement of adopting hypothetical world remote from experience as though it were world of experience and then living in it consistently"

### Neo-Classical School and "Natural" Rate
- Two sources of supply: (1) savings proper, (2) sums from money quantity increase ("forced saving")
- Leads to idea of **"natural" or "neutral" rate** — equating investment to classical savings without forced savings
- If money quantity kept constant, complications would supposedly cease

> "The wild duck has dived down to the bottom — as deep as she can get — and bitten fast hold of the weed and tangle and all the rubbish that is down there."

### Austrian School (von Mises, Hayek, Robbins)
- Identify marginal efficiency of capital with rate of interest
- Lowering of marginal efficiency has **opposite effect** to what they assume
- Result: conclusions gotten **exactly the wrong way round**

## Chapter 15: Psychological and Business Incentives to Liquidity

### Hoarding vs. Liquidity-Preference
- Hoarding as actual cash-holding is an **incomplete and misleading idea**
- All the public's propensity toward hoarding can do is determine **the rate of interest** at which aggregate desire to hoard equals available cash

> "The habit of overlooking the relation of the rate of interest to hoarding may be a part of the explanation why interest has been usually regarded as the reward of not-spending, whereas in fact it is the reward of not-hoarding."

### Four Motives for Holding Cash
1. **Income-motive**: bridge interval between income receipt and disbursement
2. **Business-motive**: bridge between incurring costs and receiving sale-proceeds
3. **Precautionary-motive**: contingencies, unforeseen opportunities, fixed liabilities
4. **Speculative-motive**: needs detailed examination; key transmission mechanism for monetary policy

- By playing on **speculative-motive**, monetary management is brought to bear on the economic system
- Transactions/precautionary demand generally irresponsive except to actual income changes
- Speculative demand shows **continuous response** to gradual interest rate changes

### The Two Liquidity Functions

> **M = M1 + M2 = L1(Y) + L2(r)**

- M1 (transactions + precautionary) depends on income level Y
- M2 (speculative) depends on interest rate r
- Open-market operations work by changing r, which changes both M1 and M2
- Income-velocity of money V: **Y/V = M1** (V depends on banking organization, social habits, income distribution)

### The Obstacle to Very Low Interest Rates
- At 4% long-term rate, running yield offsets rise of only 0.16% per annum
- At 2% rate, running yield only offsets rise of 0.04% per annum
- A 2% rate leaves **more to fear than to hope** — chief obstacle to very low rates

### Interest Rate as Conventional Phenomenon

> "It might be more accurate to say that the rate of interest is a highly conventional, rather than a highly psychological, phenomenon."

- **Any level** of interest rate accepted with sufficient conviction as likely to be durable **will be durable**
- May fluctuate for decades about a level **chronically too high for full employment**
- Short-term rate easily controlled by monetary authority
- Long-term rate may be more recalcitrant once considered "unsafe" by representative opinion

### Monetary Policy and Public Opinion
- Monetary policy striking public as **experimental or easily liable to change** may fail to reduce long-term rate
- Same policy may succeed if appeals to public opinion as reasonable, practicable, promoted by **authority unlikely to be superseded**
- Stability of the system **dependent on existence of variety of opinion about what is uncertain**
- This method more **precarious in United States** where everyone tends to hold same opinion at same time

### Difficulties of Maintaining Effective Demand
- Ensue from association of **conventional and fairly stable long-term interest rate** with **fickle and highly unstable marginal efficiency of capital**

### Example: Britain After Gold Standard
- Fall in long-term interest rate effected by series of **discontinuous jumps**
- Public becoming accustomed to each successive reduction, then ready for the next
- Offers hope that convention, not deeply rooted, will not be unduly resistant to modest persistence by monetary authority

## Chapter 16: Sundry Observations on the Nature of Capital

### The Paradox of Saving
- Saving does not stimulate investment — it represents desire for "wealth" in general
- **Prospective yield depends on expectations of future effective demand**, not on saving
- Owners of wealth desire **prospective yield**, not the capital-asset itself
- Alternative to real capital always exists: **ownership of money and debts**
- Prospective yield cannot fall below the standard set by the **current rate of interest**

## Chapter 17: The Essential Properties of Interest and Money

### Why Money-Rate of Interest Dominates
- Every durable commodity has its own rate of interest (wheat-rate, copper-rate, house-rate)
- **The greatest own-rate of interest rules the roost** — sets the standard marginal efficiency must reach
- Money-rate is often greatest because forces reducing other rates **do not operate for money**

### Capital as Yield vs. Productive
- **Preferable to speak of capital as having a yield** rather than being "productive"
- An asset offers yield only because its services have **aggregate value greater than supply price**
- Capital becomes less scarce, excess yield diminishes — without becoming less productive physically
- **Labor is the sole factor of production** in a given environment
- Lengthy (roundabout) processes are not efficient **because they are long**
- Capital kept scarce because of **competition of rate of interest on money**

# Book IV: The Inducement to Invest (conclusion)

## Chapter 18: The General Theory of Employment Re-Stated

### Section I: Given Factors vs. Independent Variables

**What we take as given:**
- Existing skill and quantity of labor
- Existing quality and quantity of equipment
- Existing technique and degree of competition
- Tastes, habits of consumer, disutility of labor
- Social structure including forces determining income distribution

**Independent variables (first instance):**
- **Propensity to consume**
- **Schedule of marginal efficiency of capital**
- **Rate of interest** (though capable of further analysis)

**Dependent variables:**
- **Volume of employment** and **national income** (measured in wage-units)

**Ultimate independent variables** can sometimes be regarded as:
1. Three fundamental psychological factors (propensity to consume, attitude to liquidity, expectation of future yield)
2. Wage-unit as determined by bargains between employers and workers
3. Quantity of money as determined by central bank action

### Section II: Summary of the System

- Investment pushed to point where marginal efficiency of capital equals interest rate
- Physical supply conditions, confidence about yield, psychological attitude to liquidity, and money quantity determine rate of new investment
- Increase/decrease in investment carries increase/decrease in consumption (in same direction but smaller magnitude)
- Ratio given by **marginal propensity to consume**
- Employment increment derived by applying multiplier to investment change
- Assuming employment multiplier = investment multiplier (first approximation)

**Repercussions on liquidity-preference:** Three ways increased employment raises money demand:
1. Value of output rises even if wage-unit and prices unchanged
2. Wage-unit itself tends to rise as employment improves
3. Output increase accompanied by price rise (in wage-unit terms) due to short-period increasing cost

- None of above factors immune to substantial change without warning — hence extreme complexity of actual events
- But these are the convenient factors to isolate; practical intuition can account for more detailed facts than general principles

### Section III: Characteristics of the Economic System

**Outstanding characteristic:**
- Subject to severe fluctuations in output and employment
- Yet not violently unstable
- Capable of remaining in chronic sub-normal activity for considerable period
- Full employment rare and short-lived
- Fluctuations start briskly but wear out before reaching extremes
- Intermediate situation (neither desperate nor satisfactory) is the normal lot

**Four conditions for stability:**

**(i) Marginal propensity to consume:** Multiplier greater than unity but not very large. When real income increases, present needs pressure diminishes, margin over established standard increases. Without this law, small investment changes would set moving cumulative demand changes to extremes.

**(ii) Marginal efficiency schedule:** Moderate changes in prospective yield or interest rate not associated with very great investment changes. Due to increasing cost of producing enlarged output from existing equipment.

**(iii) Money-wage stickiness:** When employment changes, money-wages change in same direction but not in great disproportion. This is a condition of **price stability rather than employment stability**. The struggle for money-wages is essentially struggle to maintain high relative wage. Without wage rigidity, no stable equilibrium except at full employment — wage-unit might fall indefinitely until money abundance relative to wage-unit lowered interest enough to restore full employment.

**(iv) Capital asset depreciation:** Capital assets vary in age, wear out over time, not all long-lived. If investment falls below minimum, it's merely a question of time before marginal efficiency rises enough to bring recovery. Conversely, if investment rises too high, marginal efficiency will eventually fall and bring recession. These same forces then reverse again — recovery and recession cycles persist.

> "Thus our four conditions together are adequate to explain the outstanding features of our actual experience; — namely, that we oscillate, avoiding the gravest extremes of fluctuation in employment and in prices in both directions, round an intermediate position appreciably below full employment."

- This mean position is not established by laws of necessity; it is fact of observation, not a principle that cannot be changed

# Book V: Money-Wages and Prices

## Chapter 19: Changes in Money-Wages

### Section I: The Classical Theory's Blind Spot

- Classical Theory assumes economic system self-adjusts via fluid money-wages
- When rigidity exists, Classical Theory blames maladjustment on that rigidity
- **Classical argument**: money-wage reduction *cet. par.* stimulates demand by diminishing product price, increasing output and employment

**The fatal flaw:** Demand schedules for particular industries can only be constructed on fixed assumptions about aggregate effective demand. Invalid to transfer the argument to industry as a whole.

> "The precise question at issue is whether the reduction in money-wages will or will not be accompanied by the same aggregate effective demand as before measured in money."

### Section II: Seven Repercussions of Wage Reduction

**Two key questions:** (1) Does money-wage reduction have direct tendency to increase employment with propensity to consume, marginal efficiency schedule, and interest rate unchanged? (2) Does it affect employment through repercussions on these three factors?

**Answer to first question:** Employment volume uniquely correlated with effective demand measured in wage-units. Without changes in propensity to consume, marginal efficiency, or interest rate, wage reduction alone cannot change effective demand. Entrepreneurs increasing employment would see proceeds fall short of supply-price.

**The seven channels through which wage cuts might affect employment:**

**(1) Redistribution of real income** — Transfer from wage-earners to rentiers with fixed money income. Net result probably adverse to propensity to consume.

**(2) Balance of trade (open economy)** — If reduction is relative to wages abroad, favorable to investment via improved trade balance. Britain's greater belief in wage-cut efficacy attributable to being open system.

**(3) Terms of trade deterioration** — Though favorable balance may increase, likely worsens terms of trade.

**(4) Expectations and marginal efficiency** — If reduction believed to be final, favorable (increases marginal efficiency). If leads to expectation of further falls, opposite — diminishes marginal efficiency, postpones investment and consumption.

> "The effect of an expectation that wages are going to sag by, say, 2 per cent in the coming year will be roughly equivalent to the effect of a rise of 2 per cent in the amount of interest payable for the same period."

**(5) Liquidity-preference and interest rates** — Wage-bill and price reduction diminishes cash need, reduces liquidity-preference, lowers interest rate. But expectation effects and political discontent may offset.

**(6) Psychological effects on entrepreneurs** — May break vicious circle of pessimism. But labor troubles may offset this.

**(7) Debt burden effects** — Greater debt burden on entrepreneurs. If wages/prices fall far, insolvency may follow — severely adverse to investment.

### Section III: Three Outstanding Considerations Against Flexible Wages

> "There is, therefore, no ground for the belief that a flexible wage policy is capable of maintaining a state of continuous full employment."

**(i) Social justice and practicality:** Except in socialized community with decree-based wage policy, no means of securing uniform reductions for every labor class. Result only brought about by gradual, irregular changes, justifiable on no criterion of social justice or economic expediency. Probably completed only after wasteful and disastrous struggles, weakest bargaining position suffering relatively to rest. Money quantity change already within most governments' power by open-market policy. **"Only a foolish person"** would prefer flexible wage policy to flexible money policy.

**(ii) Price changes and fairness:** With inflexible money-wages, price changes mainly correspond to diminishing marginal productivity. Greatest practicable fairness maintained between labor and factors with contractually fixed money remuneration (particularly rentier class and persons with fixed salaries). If important classes have remuneration fixed in money terms anyway, social justice best served if all factors somewhat inflexible in money terms.

**(iii) Debt burden:** Increasing money quantity in wage-units by decreasing wage-unit increases debt burden proportionately. Increasing money quantity while leaving wage-unit unchanged has opposite (beneficial) effect. Having regard to excessive burden of many debt types, only inexperienced person would prefer wage reduction method.

**(iv) Sagging rates require sagging wages:** If sagging interest rate must be brought about by sagging wage-level, double drag on marginal efficiency of capital and double reason for postponing investment and recovery.

**Policy recommendation:**

> "The maintenance of a stable general level of money-wages is, on a balance of considerations, the most advisable policy for a closed system; whilst the same conclusion will hold good for an open system, provided that equilibrium with the rest of the world can be secured by means of fluctuating exchanges."

- Advantages in some flexibility in particular industries' wages to expedite transfers from declining to expanding industries
- In long period, Keynes prefers allowing wages to rise slowly while keeping prices stable — easier to maintain full employment with expectation of higher future wages; also social advantages of gradually diminishing debt burden and psychological encouragement

**The Australian example:** If attempt made to fix real wages by legislation, employment would oscillate violently between legal real wage level and no employment at all. Stability element would have to be found in factors controlling money quantity. In actual Australia, escape found partly in legislation inefficacy, partly in not being closed system.

**The case against flexible wages:**
> "If labor were to respond to conditions of gradually diminishing employment by offering its services at a gradually diminishing money-wage, this would not, as a rule, have the effect of reducing real wages and might even have the effect of increasing them, through its adverse influence on the volume of output."

- Chief result: great price instability, perhaps so violent as to make business calculations futile
- Flexible wage policy proper adjunct only of highly authoritarian society with sudden, substantial, all-round decreed changes
- Can imagine it in Italy, Germany, or Russia, but not France, United States, or Great Britain

### Appendix: Professor Pigou's Theory of Unemployment

- **Pigou** makes employment depend on: (1) real wage rates workers stipulate, (2) shape of Real Demand Function for Labor
- Pigou assumes **n = x + y** (labor supply equals employment), which amounts to assuming **no involuntary unemployment**
- His analysis has **three unknowns but only two equations** — gets around this by assuming full employment
- Pigou's book is "strangely titled" *Theory of Unemployment* when it actually analyzes employment levels given full employment conditions

**Pigou's equations:** Divides industries into wage-goods and non-wage-goods. With *x* men in wage-goods, *y* in other industries, output F(*x*), general rate of wages F'(*x*). This tantamount to assuming marginal wage-cost equals marginal prime cost. Pigou's "elasticity of real demand for labor" corresponds to function of Keynes's employment multiplier *k'*.

**The money-wage problem:** Admitting labor stipulates for money-wage (not real wage) introduces additional unknown without additional equation. System becomes indeterminate. "We are one equation short." The "pitfalls of a pseudo-mathematical method" — making everything a function of one variable, assuming partial differentials vanish, then admitting other variables without rewriting the analysis.

**Historical evidence:** Great Britain 1924-1934: money-wages stable within 6%, while real wages fluctuated over 20%. A scientific theory cannot require facts to conform to its assumptions.

**The primary vs. secondary employment fallacy:** Pigou's argument against "secondary" employment from public works is equally fatal to increased "primary" employment from same policy. Yet Pigou accepts the possibility of increased primary employment. The line between primary and secondary employment is where his common sense overrides his bad theory.

**Pigou's "Real Demand Function for Labor"** depends only on physical production conditions (F) and relationship between wage-goods and total employment (phi) — neither should change except gradually. Therefore **real demand for labor would remain virtually constant throughout a trade cycle**. Pigou has altogether omitted the unstable factor: fluctuations in scale of investment, which is **most often at bottom of fluctuations in employment**.

- Keynes criticized Pigou at length not because he is most open to criticism, but because his is **the only attempt to write down the classical theory of unemployment precisely**

## Chapter 20: The Employment Function

### Definition and Mathematical Framework

- **Employment function** is inverse of aggregate supply function
- For a firm/industry: **Nr = Fr(Dwr)**
- For industry as whole: **N = F(Dw)**
- Individual employment functions are additive: **F(Dw) = N = SUM(Nr) = SUM(Fr(Dw))**

**Elasticity of employment:** eer = (dNr/dDwr)(Dwr/Nr)
**Elasticity of output:** eor = (dOr/dDwr)(Dwr/Or)

- If eor = 0 (perfectly inelastic output): all increased effective demand accrues as profit
- If eor = 1 (unit elasticity): no part accrues as profit, all absorbed by marginal prime cost
- Condition eor = 1 means constant returns to increased employment

**Step toward Quantity Theory:** ep = 1 - eo(1 - ew)
- If eo = 0 or ew = 1: prices rise in same proportion as effective demand in money

### Classical Theory and Employment Elasticity

- Classical theory assumes real wages always equal marginal disutility of labor
- If true, impossible to increase expenditure in wage-units — money-wages would rise proportionately
- Concept of elasticity of employment would have no field of application
- When classical assumption doesn't hold, possible to increase employment by increasing expenditure in money until real wages fall to equality with marginal disutility of labor — at that point, full employment by definition

### Distribution of Demand Changes

- First approximation assumes employment depends solely on aggregate effective demand
- But direction of demand matters: increased demand for products with high employment elasticity produces greater aggregate employment increase
- Employment may fall off without any change in aggregate demand if direction of demand changes toward low-elasticity products
- Particularly important for short-period phenomena where supply cannot adjust quickly
- Products with longer "periods of production" show lower initial employment elasticity
- Consumption goods have longest period (final stage of all productive processes)
- Low-elasticity products give larger profit share to entrepreneurs vs. wage-earners when demand rises — may reduce favorable repercussions on expenditure since entrepreneurs save more

## Chapter 21: The Theory of Prices

### Section I: The False Division in Economics

- **False dichotomy**: Theory of Value vs Theory of Money
- **Correct dichotomy**: Theory of individual industry/firm vs Theory of output and employment as a whole
- **Money's importance** lies in being a **link between present and future**
- Cannot discuss changing expectations' effects except in monetary terms
- Any durable asset capable of possessing monetary attributes creates monetary economy problems

### Section III: Quantity Theory Under Simplifying Assumptions

With homogeneous resources, constant returns, and rigid wages while unemployment exists:
- Money quantity increase produces no effect on prices while unemployment persists
- Employment increases in exact proportion to effective demand increase
- Once full employment reached, prices change proportionally to money quantity

### Five Complicating Factors

1. **Effective demand will not change exactly proportionally to money quantity**
2. **Resources not homogeneous** — diminishing (not constant) returns as employment increases
3. **Resources not interchangeable** — some commodities reach inelastic supply while unemployed resources still available for others. Series of **"bottlenecks"** successively reached; supply of particular commodities ceases elastic, prices rise to divert demand
4. **Wage-unit tends to rise before full employment reached** — each worker group gains by own wage rise, employers more ready to agree when doing better business. Succession of "semi-critical points" where increasing effective demand tends to raise money-wages
5. **Factor remunerations entering marginal cost will not all change in same proportion** — marginal user cost may increase sharply as expected equipment replacement dates change

Result: prices rising gradually as employment increases — not constant prices during unemployment and proportional rises at full employment.

**Method of economic analysis:** Isolate complicating factors one by one for provisional conclusions, then allow for interactions. This is the nature of economic thinking — the other way leads to being lost in the wood.

> "Too much recent 'mathematical' economics = mere concoctions, imprecise as initial assumptions, lose sight of complexities and interdependencies in pretentious symbols."

### Section IV: Effect Through Interest Rate

Primary effect of money quantity change on effective demand = through interest rate influence. Derived from three elements:
- (a) Liquidity-preference schedule — how much interest rate must fall for new money absorption
- (b) Marginal efficiency schedule — how much given interest rate fall increases investment
- (c) Investment multiplier — how much given investment increase raises effective demand

**Income-velocity of money** is merely a name explaining nothing. No reason to expect constancy.

### Section VI: True Inflation

> "When a further increase in the quantity of effective demand produces no further increase in output and entirely spends itself on an increase in the cost-unit fully proportionate to the increase in effective demand, we have reached a condition which might be appropriately designated as one of true inflation."

- **Asymmetry**: workers resist money-wage reductions but no corresponding motive to resist increases
- The view that any increase in money quantity is inflationary is bound up with the classical assumption that we are always at full employment

### Section VII: Generalized Quantity Theory

**Four elasticities determine price effects:**
- **ed**: elasticity of effective demand in response to money
- **ew**: labor factors (money-wage response to employment)
- **ee and eo**: physical factors (decreasing returns)

General formula: e = ed(1 - ee*eo + ee*eo*ew)

> "I do not myself attach much value to manipulations of this kind... Perhaps the best purpose served by writing them down is to exhibit the extreme complexity of the relationship between prices and the quantity of money."

### Section VIII: Long-Run Relationships

- If tendency to long-run uniformity in liquidity-preference, may be rough relationship between national income and money quantity
- Tendencies work with less friction upward than downward
- If money very deficient for long time, escape found in changing monetary standard or system — rarely in forcing down wage-unit (increases debt burden)
- **Very long-run course of prices almost always upward**: when money abundant, wage-unit rises; when scarce, means found to increase effective quantity

### Section IX: The Nineteenth-Century Balance

- Long-run typical interest rate ~5%; gilt-edged 3-3.5% (1820-1914)
- These rates modest enough to encourage investment consistent with tolerable employment
- Balance of forces allowing fair measure of price stability; **Sauerbeck's index**: highest quinquennial average only 50% above lowest
- Individual employer groups strong enough to prevent wage-unit rising much faster than productivity
- Monetary systems sufficiently fluid yet conservative to provide average money supply allowing lowest acceptable interest rate to prevail
- Average employment substantially below full employment, but not so intolerably low as to provoke revolution
- **Bagehot's** 19th-century saying: "John Bull can stand many things, but he cannot stand 2 per cent"

**Today's problem:** Schedule of marginal efficiency of capital much lower than 19th century. Average interest rate allowing reasonable employment is **unacceptable to wealth-owners** — the most stable and least easily shifted element in the economy. After deductions from marginal efficiency for borrowing costs, taxes, risk allowance — if net yield infinitesimal under tolerable employment, time-honored methods unavailing.

# Book VI: Short Notes Suggested by the General Theory

## Chapter 22: Notes on the Trade Cycle

### Section I: Cyclical Movement Characteristics

- Forces propelling system upward gather force cumulatively, gradually lose strength, replaced by opposite forces
- **Crisis**: substitution of downward for upward tendency often sudden and violent; no such sharp turning-point when upward replaces downward

### Section II: Collapse in Marginal Efficiency — Essence of the Crisis

**Conventional crisis explanation criticized:** Rising interest rate under increased demand for money sometimes plays initiating/aggravating role. But more typical explanation is sudden collapse in **marginal efficiency of capital**.

> "The later stages of the boom are characterized by optimistic expectations as to the future yield of capital-goods sufficiently strong to offset their growing abundance and their rising costs of production and, probably, a rise in the rate of interest also."

**Nature of organized investment markets:**
- Purchasers largely ignorant of what they buy
- Speculators more concerned with forecasting next market shift than reasonable estimate of future yield
- When disillusion falls on over-optimistic, over-bought market, **collapse should be sudden and even catastrophic**

**Why collapse in marginal efficiency is essence of crisis:**
- Dismay and uncertainty precipitate sharp rise in liquidity-preference
- Rise in interest rate aggravates decline, but **essence is collapse in marginal efficiency of capital**
- Particularly for capital types contributing to previous heavy new-investment phase
- Liquidity-preference manifestations (except those from increasing trade/speculation) don't increase until AFTER collapse

### Section III: Intractability of the Slump

> "It is this, indeed, which renders the slump so intractable."

- Later decline in interest rate aids recovery, probably necessary condition
- But for the moment, collapse so complete no practicable interest rate reduction enough
- Not easy to revive marginal efficiency (determined by uncontrollable, disobedient business psychology)
- **Return of confidence** insusceptible to control in individualistic capitalism
- Aspect of slump bankers/businessmen right to emphasize; economists underestimated "purely monetary" remedy

### Section IV: Duration Factors

- Recovery interval governed by: (1) **length of life of durable assets** relative to normal growth rate, (2) **carrying-costs of surplus stocks** (seldom less than 10% per annum; force absorption in 3-5 years)

**Negative investment process:** Reduction in working capital attendant on output decline exerts strong cumulative influence downward.

**Typical slump phases:**
1. Investment in increasing stocks offsets disinvestment in working capital
2. Short period of disinvestment in both
3. After lowest point, disinvestment in stocks partially offsets reinvestment in working capital
4. When recovery well advanced, both factors simultaneously favorable

### Section V: Stock Market and Propensity to Consume

- Severe decline in Stock Exchange values naturally depressing, particularly for leveraged investors
- With "stock-minded" public (United States), rising stock-market may be essential condition of satisfactory propensity to consume

> "In conditions of laissez-faire the avoidance of wide fluctuations in employment may, therefore, prove impossible without a far-reaching change in the psychology of investment markets such as there is no reason to expect. I conclude that the duty of ordering the current volume of investment cannot safely be left in private hands."

### Section VI: Over-Investment vs. Misdirected Investment

- **"Over-investment" is ambiguous**: investments destined to disappoint expectations vs. every capital-good so abundant that no new investment earns more than replacement cost
- Keynes's typical boom characterization: NOT capital so abundant society has no use, but investments made in conditions that cannot endure, prompted by expectations destined to disappointment

> "Thus the remedy for the boom is not a higher rate of interest but a lower rate of interest! For that may enable the so-called boom to last. The right remedy for the trade cycle is not to be found in abolishing booms and thus keeping us permanently in a semi-slump; but in abolishing slumps and thus keeping us permanently in a quasi-boom."

**United States 1928-29:** Employment very satisfactory on normal standards, no labor shortage except few specialized groups. Previous five years: enormous new investment scale. Correct foresight would have brought down marginal efficiency to unprecedentedly low level. Very low long-term interest rate needed to continue on sound basis. But rate high enough to overcome speculative excitement would have checked every kind of reasonable new investment. Raising rates to check speculation = "curing disease by killing patient."

### Section VIII: Full Investment in the Strict Sense

- Very possible that prolonged approximately full employment in wealthy countries would eventually lead to a state where aggregate gross investment exceeds replacement cost
- But Keynes asserts a state of full investment in the strict sense **has never yet occurred, not even momentarily**
- Even if contemporary booms associated with momentary full investment, still absurd to regard higher interest rate as remedy
- Remedy would lie in measures to increase propensity to consume by consumption redistribution

### Section IX: Under-Consumption and Investment — Advance on Both Fronts

- Wisest course: advance on both fronts at once
- Aim at socially controlled investment with progressive decline in marginal efficiency
- Support all policies for increasing propensity to consume
- Unlikely full employment maintained with existing propensity to consume whatever done about investment

### The Argument Against High Interest Rates to Check Booms

- Banking policy nipping incipient booms early may create more stable expectations
- But this overlooks **social advantage of increased consumption** accompanying even misdirected investment
- This outlook is "dangerously and unnecessarily defeatist"
- The austere view that high rates restore equilibrium implies saving and investment can be unequal — has no meaning until defined in special senses
- Rising prices during booms wrongly attributed to increased investment rather than increased output
- **A theory assuming constant output is "obviously not very serviceable for explaining the trade cycle"**

### Section VII: Agricultural Fluctuations

- **Jevons** found explanation in agricultural fluctuations due to seasons
- Large harvests = addition to carry-over = added investment; poor harvests = disinvestment
- Now much less important: agriculture smaller proportion of output; world market averages effects
- Changes in stocks of raw materials still play important part in determining rate of current investment

**Roosevelt's New Deal:** When substantial loan expenditure began, stocks (especially agricultural) stood at very high levels. The "New Deal" partly consisted of strenuous attempt to reduce these stocks — this constituted substantial offset to loan expenditure in other directions, taking about two years. Only when completed was the way prepared for substantial recovery.

**Inventory fluctuations:** Manufacturers planning for expected consumption make minor miscalculations, creating minor oscillations within main trade cycle movement. Difference in pace between running ahead and dropping back proved sufficient in its effect on current investment rate.

## Chapter 23: Notes on Mercantilism, Usury Laws, Stamped Money, and Under-Consumption

### Section I: The Historical Debate

- For ~200 years, economic theorists and practical men believed in advantage of favorable balance of trade
- Past ~100 years: divergence — statesmen remained faithful; economists held it groundless
- Modern economists maintain mercantilist argument based on **intellectual confusion from start to finish**

### Section II: Element of Scientific Truth in Mercantilist Doctrine

**The core insight:** When a country grows in wealth rapidly, progress liable to be interrupted by insufficiency of inducements to new investment under laissez-faire. In conditions where money quantity linked to precious metals:
- **Rate of interest governed by quantity of precious metals** satisfying community's desire for liquidity
- Increases/decreases in metals largely depend on balance of trade
- Favorable balance stimulating; unfavorable may produce persistent depression

**General presumptions against trade restrictions:**
- Strong presumptions of general character against restrictions unless justified on special grounds
- Advantages of international division of labor are real and substantial (though classical school greatly overstressed them)
- Immoderate mercantilist policy may lead to senseless international competition for favorable balance injuring all alike
- A policy of trade restrictions is a treacherous instrument — private interest, administrative incompetence may divert it into producing opposite results

**Two limitations on success:** (1) If domestic costs rise from success, balance of trade reverses. (2) If low domestic rates stimulate excessive foreign lending, precious metals efflux.

**Historical examples:**
- **Spain (15th-16th centuries):** Foreign trade destroyed by effect on wage-unit of excessive precious metals abundance
- **Great Britain (19th century):** Excessive foreign lending stood in way of domestic interest rate reduction
- **India at all times:** Impoverished by strong liquidity-preference; even enormous chronic influx of metals insufficient

**The City of London's dangerous technique:** Bank rate coupled with rigid parity of foreign exchanges — meant domestic interest rate consistent with full employment was wholly ruled out.

> "Thus, the weight of my criticism is directed against the inadequacy of the theoretical foundations of the laissez-faire doctrine — against the notion that the rate of interest and the volume of investment are self-adjusting at the optimum level, so that preoccupation with the balance of trade is a waste of time."

### Section III: The Mercantilist Pioneers

**On interest rates and money:** Mercantilist thought never supposed self-adjusting tendency. They were emphatic that unduly high interest rate was main obstacle to growth of wealth.

Key thinkers:
- **Gerard Malynes** (1622): "Plenty of money decreaseth usury in price or rate"
- **Edward Misselden** (1622): "The remedy for Usury may be plenty of money"
- **Child** (1668): Most skilful advocate on money and interest
- **Petty** (1676): Explained "natural" fall in interest from 10% to 6% by increase in money amount
- **John Locke**: First to express in abstract terms relationship between interest rate and money quantity; parent of twin quantity theories (one for interest, one for value of money)

**Locke's twin quantity theories:** Locke explained money has two values: (1) value in use given by rate of interest — "in this it has the Nature of Land, the Income of one being called Rent, of the other, Use"; (2) value in exchange — "in this it has the Nature of a Commodity." Standing with one foot in mercantilist world and one in classical world, he was confused concerning the relation between these proportions and overlooked altogether the possibility of fluctuations in liquidity-preference. He never proceeds to genuine synthesis.

**On hoarding:** Mercantilists frankly opposed state accumulation of treasure. **Schrotter** drew lurid picture of how circulation would be robbed by increasing state treasury. **Davenant** explained poverty of many Eastern nations by fact that treasure "is suffered to stagnate in the Princes' Coffers." Private hoarding was "shunned like the pest" — Keynes doesn't think it possible to find single dissentient voice.

**The fallacy of cheapness:** Mercantilists knew that excessive competition could turn terms of trade against a country. **Malynes**: "the cheapness proceedeth of the small request and scarcity of money."

**Fear of goods as cause of unemployment:** Prohibition of imports to Florence (1426) to give work to tens of thousands. English legislation back to 1455. **Sir Edwin Sandys** (1621): looms standing idle "not for want of fruits of the earth, but for want of money."

**Propensity to save vs. inducement to invest:** Impossible to study mercantilist notions without perceiving chronic tendency throughout human history for propensity to save to be stronger than inducement to invest.

**Classical theory vs. common sense:**
- Mercantilists perceived existence of the problem without being able to solve it
- Classical school ignored the problem by introducing premises involving its non-existence
- **"The extraordinary achievement of classical theory was to overcome beliefs of 'natural man' and, at same time, to be wrong"**
- Keynes remembers **Bonar Law's** mingled rage and perplexity in face of economists denying what was obvious
- One recurs to analogy between sway of classical school and that of certain religions — far greater exercise of potency of an idea to exorcise the obvious than to introduce the recondite

**On war:** Mercantilists were under no illusions as to nationalistic character of their policies. Their realism "much preferable to the confused thinking of contemporary advocates of an international fixed gold standard."

- Never in history was there method devised of such efficacy for setting each country's advantage at variance with its neighbors' as the international gold (or silver) standard

> "It is the policy of an autonomous rate of interest, unimpeded by international preoccupations, and of a national investment program directed to an optimum level of domestic employment which is twice blessed in the sense that it helps ourselves and our neighbors at the same time."

### Section V: Usury Laws

- Provisions against usury are among the **most ancient economic practices** of which we have record
- In ancient/medieval worlds, liquidity-preference combined with risks diminishing marginal efficiency meant interest rate was almost inevitably too high
- The schoolmen's disquisitions sought to keep marginal efficiency of capital high while using rule, custom, and moral law to keep interest rate down
- Keynes was brought up to believe attitude of Medieval Church to interest was inherently absurd and attempts to distinguish return on money-loans from return to active investment were merely Jesuitical
- Now reads these discussions as honest intellectual effort to keep separate what classical theory inextricably confused: **the rate of interest and the marginal efficiency of capital**
- Even **Adam Smith** favored moderate application of usury laws — well aware individual savings may be absorbed by investment or by debts
- **Bentham** objected they were too severe on "projectors" — "all such persons as, in the pursuit of wealth, endeavor to strike into any channel of invention"
- It may be doubted whether we are hearing in Bentham (writing 1787 from "Crichoff in White Russia") the voice of the 19th century speaking to the 18th — for nothing short of the exuberance of the greatest age of inducement to investment could have made it possible to lose sight of its insufficiency

### Section VI: Silvio Gesell — The Unduly Neglected Prophet

- **Gesell** (1862-1930): successful German merchant in Buenos Aires, led to monetary study by Argentine crisis of late 1880s
- Standard work: ***Die naturliche Wirtschaftsordnung*** (English: *The Natural Economic Order*)
- April 1919: joined short-lived Soviet cabinet of Bavaria as Minister of Finance
- Became revered prophet of cult; **Professor Irving Fisher** alone among academics recognized significance

**Gesell's core insights:**
1. Distinguishes clearly between rate of interest and marginal efficiency of capital
2. Rate of interest is purely monetary phenomenon — money's negligible carrying costs are key
3. Comparative stability of interest rate throughout ages proves it doesn't depend on physical characters

**The famous prescription of "stamped" money:**
- Currency notes retain value only by being stamped monthly (like insurance cards)
- Gesell suggested 1 per mil per month (5.4% per annum)
- Creates carrying cost for money, reducing its excessive liquidity-premium

**The great defect:** Gesell altogether overlooks need for explanation why money-rate of interest is positive. **Notion of liquidity-preference had escaped him** — he constructed only half a theory of interest rate.

**Critical limitation:** If currency notes lost liquidity-premium through stamping, substitutes would emerge: bank-money, debts at call, foreign money, jewellery, precious metals.

> "I believe that the future will learn more from the spirit of Gesell than from that of Marx."

- Purpose of Gesell's book: **establishment of anti-Marxian socialism** — reaction against laissez-faire built on repudiation of classical hypotheses, based on unfettering of competition instead of its abolition

### Section VII: Theories of Under-Consumption

**Historical development:**
- Mercantilist "deep-rooted belief in the utility of luxury and the evil of thrift"
- **Laffemas (1598)**: denounced objectors to French luxury goods
- **Petty (1662)**: justified "entertainments, magnificent shews, triumphal arches"
- **Barbon (1690)**: "Prodigality is a vice that is prejudicial to the Man, but not to trade... Covetousness is a Vice, prejudicial both to Man and Trade"

**Mandeville's *Fable of the Bees* (1723):**
- Allegorical poem "The Grumbling Hive" depicting prosperous community that decides to abandon luxury and cut armaments in interests of Saving — result: economic collapse
- "The Building Trade is quite destroy'd, / Artificers are not employ'd"
- Moral: "Bare Virtue can't make Nations live / In Splendour"
- Convicted as nuisance by grand jury of Middlesex; only **Dr. Johnson** spoke a good word for it, declaring it "opened his eyes into real life very much"
- Mandeville's conclusion: "The great art to make a nation happy consists in giving everybody an opportunity of being employed; which to compass, let a Government's first care be to promote as great a variety of Manufactures, Arts and Handicrafts as human wit can invent"

**Victorian aftermath:** Wicked sentiments called down opprobrium of two centuries. Petty's "entertainments, magnificent shews, triumphal arches" gave place to penny-wisdom of Gladstonian finance — the State system that "could not afford" hospitals, open spaces, noble buildings, splendours of music and drama

**Malthus vs. Ricardo:**
- **Malthus** argued excessive saving could check progress of wealth: "an attempt to accumulate very rapidly... by greatly impairing the usual motives to production must prematurely check the progress of wealth"
- **Ricardo** was "stone-deaf to what Malthus was saying"
- **J.S. Mill's** successors rejected Wages-Fund Theory but overlooked that Mill's refutation of Malthus depended on it
- Problem was dismissed from economics not by solving it but by not mentioning it

**Hobson and Mummery's *Physiology of Industry* (1889):**
- First and most significant of many volumes by Hobson over fifty years — unflagging, unavailing ardor and courage against ranks of orthodoxy
- **Mummery**: businessman and mountaineer who discovered another way up the Matterhorn, killed attempting Nanga Parbat in 1895
- Hobson's London Extension Board refused to allow courses in Political Economy after an Economic Professor read the book and considered it "equivalent in rationality to attempt to prove flatness of earth"
- "Sound economists could not fail to view with horror argument which sought to check source of all industrial progress"
- Their preface challenged the doctrine that "saving enriches and spending impoverishes the community along with the individual" — "its ultimate acceptance was exclusively due to their inability to meet the now exploded wages-fund doctrine"

**Hobson's core insight (half-right):**
- **Primary evil**: propensity to save under full employment more than capital equivalent required, preventing full employment except during foresight mistakes
- But he laid too much emphasis on under-consumption, overlooking possible role of interest rate changes and business confidence

> "We are thus brought to the conclusion that the basis on which all economic teaching since Adam Smith has stood, viz. that the quantity annually produced is determined by the aggregates of Natural Agents, Capital, and Labor available, is erroneous."

- In normal state of modern industrial communities, **consumption limits production, not production consumption**

### Appendix: Mr. Hobson's Heresy and Major Douglas

- **Major Douglas** and his A+B theorem: includes "much mere mystification" but the destructive criticism has validity since orthodoxy had no valid reply
- If Douglas had limited B-items to financial provisions made by entrepreneurs to which no current expenditure corresponds, he would be nearer the truth

## Chapter 24: Concluding Notes on the Social Philosophy

### The Outstanding Faults of Economic Society

- **Two chief defects**: (1) failure to provide for full employment, (2) arbitrary and inequitable distribution of wealth and incomes

### On Inequality and Capital Accumulation

- Since late 19th century, progress toward reducing disparities through **direct taxation** (income tax, surtax, death duties)
- Two obstacles to further redistribution: fear of skillful evasion, and fear of diminishing risk-taking motive
- Most important obstacle: belief that **capital growth depends on savings from the rich's superfluity**
- Keynes's counter: up to the point of full employment, capital growth is **held back** by low propensity to consume, not helped by it
- Existing saving by institutions and through sinking funds is more than adequate
- Measures for income redistribution likely to **raise propensity to consume**, proving **positively favorable to capital growth**
- Common belief that death duties reduce capital wealth of the country is false except under full employment — increased habitual propensity to consume increases the inducement to invest, not decreases it

> "Our argument leads towards the conclusion that in contemporary conditions the growth of wealth, so far from being dependent on the abstinence of the rich, as is commonly supposed, is more likely to be impeded by it."

### Social and Psychological Justification for Some Inequality

- Keynes believes there is justification for significant inequalities but **not for such large disparities as exist today**
- Valuable human activities require profit motive and environment of private wealth-ownership
- Dangerous human proclivities can be canalized into comparatively harmless channels

> "It is better that a man should tyrannize over his bank balance than over his fellow-citizens."

- Not necessary that game be played for such high stakes as at present; much lower stakes will serve equally well once players are accustomed to them
- Task of transmuting human nature must not be confused with task of managing it
- Though ideal commonwealth may require men bred to take no interest in stakes, wise statesmanship may still allow game to be played subject to rules and limitations

### The Euthanasia of the Rentier

- Rate of interest best reduced to that point where there is full employment
- Demand for capital strictly limited — not difficult to increase stock until marginal efficiency falls to very low figure
- Within one generation, a community equipped with modern technical resources and stable population could bring down marginal efficiency approximately to zero
- This would mean the **euthanasia of the rentier** — the cumulative oppressive power of capitalist to exploit scarcity-value of capital
- Nothing sudden — merely gradual but prolonged continuance of what recently seen in Great Britain

> "Interest today rewards no genuine sacrifice, any more than does the rent of land. The owner of capital can obtain interest because capital is scarce, just as the owner of land can obtain rent because land is scarce. But whilst there may be intrinsic reasons for the scarcity of land, there are no intrinsic reasons for the scarcity of capital."

> "I see, therefore, the rentier aspect of capitalism as a transitional phase which will disappear when it has done its work."

- Entrepreneur who actively manages and organizes would remain essential and rewarded
- The functionless investor would gradually disappear — not by revolution but by obsolescence

### Socialization of Investment

> "I conceive, therefore, that a somewhat comprehensive socialization of investment will prove the only means of securing an approximation to full employment; though this need not exclude all manner of compromises and of devices by which public authority will co-operate with private initiative."

- **Not State Socialism**: not ownership of instruments of production but determination of aggregate resources devoted to augmenting capital and basic reward rate
- The necessary measures can be introduced **gradually and without a break in general traditions of society**
- Our criticism of classical theory has consisted not so much in finding logical flaws as in pointing out that its **tacit assumptions are seldom or never satisfied**
- If central controls succeed in establishing output corresponding to full employment, classical theory comes into its own from that point onwards — no objection to private self-interest determining what is produced, factor proportions, or distribution of final product
- Apart from necessity of central controls for adjustment between propensity to consume and inducement to invest, there is **no more reason to socialize economic life than there was before**

### The Volume, Not the Direction, of Employment

- When 9,000,000 out of 10,000,000 willing to work are employed, no evidence labor is misdirected
- Complaint is not that 9 million should be employed differently but that **tasks should be available for the remaining 1 million**
- **It is in determining the volume, not the direction, of actual employment that the existing system has broken down**

> "I agree with Gesell that the result of filling in the gaps in the classical theory is not to dispose of the 'Manchester System', but to indicate the nature of the environment which the free play of economic forces requires if it is to realise the full potentialities of production."

### Advantages of Individualism

- **Efficiency**: Decentralization of decisions and individual responsibility — greater advantages than 19th century supposed
- **Liberty**: Greatly widens field for exercise of personal choice
- **Variety**: Preserves traditions embodying most secure choices of former generations

> "Above all, individualism, if it can be purged of its defects and its abuses, is the best safeguard of personal liberty... It is also the best safeguard of the variety of life, which emerges precisely from this extended field of personal choice, and the loss of which is the greatest of all the losses of the homogeneous or totalitarian state."

### Effective Demand and Entrepreneurship

- If effective demand is deficient, the individual enterpriser operates with odds loaded against him
- Game of hazard furnished with many zeros — players as a whole will lose
- Hitherto increment of world's wealth has fallen short of aggregate of positive individual savings; difference made up by losses of the courageous who lacked exceptional skill or fortune
- **If effective demand is adequate, average skill and average good fortune will be enough**

### Authoritarian State Systems vs. Democratic Intervention

- Authoritarian systems solve unemployment **at the expense of efficiency and freedom**
- World will not much longer tolerate unemployment associated with present-day capitalistic individualism
- But it may be possible to **cure the disease whilst preserving efficiency and freedom**
- Keynes's enlargement of government functions defended as **only practicable means of avoiding destruction of existing economic forms in their entirety**

### International Implications and Peace

- New system may be **more favorable to peace** than the old
- War has several causes, but over and above natural bellicosity are **economic causes**: pressure of population and competitive struggle for markets
- Under domestic laissez-faire and international gold standard, there was **no means open to government** to mitigate economic distress except competitive struggle for markets — all measures helpful to under-employment were ruled out except improving trade balance
- If nations can achieve full employment domestically, no pressing motive to force exports on others or repulse imports
- International trade would cease being a desperate expedient to shift unemployment onto neighbors (beggar-thy-neighbor policy)

> "Ideas, knowledge, science, hospitality, travel — these are the things which should of their nature be international. But let goods be homespun whenever it is reasonably and conveniently possible; and, above all, let finance be primarily national."

### The Power of Ideas

> "The ideas of economists and political philosophers, both when they are right and when they are wrong, are more powerful than is commonly understood. Indeed the world is ruled by little else."

- **Practical men** who believe themselves exempt from intellectual influences are usually the slaves of some defunct economist
- **Madmen in authority** who hear voices in the air are distilling their frenzy from some academic scribbler of a few years back
- The power of vested interests is vastly exaggerated compared with the gradual encroachment of ideas

> "Soon or late, it is ideas, not vested interests, which are dangerous for good or evil."
