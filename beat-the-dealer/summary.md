# Beat the Dealer — Edward O. Thorp

## One-Line Summary
The first mathematically rigorous proof that a casino game could be beaten by a player using probability theory and card counting.

## Core Thesis
Blackjack is unique among casino games because it is not an independent trials process — cards dealt in previous rounds change the probabilities of future rounds. By tracking which cards have been played, a player can identify situations where they have a mathematical edge over the house and bet accordingly.

## Key Systems (Progressive Complexity)
1. **Basic Strategy** (~0.1% player edge): Memorized rules for every decision. No counting required. Reduces house edge to nearly zero.
2. **Five-Count System** (~3% edge): Track only the four Fives in the deck. When all gone, bet big.
3. **Simple Point Count** (high-low): Cards 2-6 = +1, 7-9 = 0, 10-A = −1. Bet proportional to count.
4. **Complete Point Count**: Divide running count by unseen cards for precise high-low index. Adjust strategy tables based on index.
5. **Ten-Count Method**: Track Tens vs. non-Tens. Ratio below 2.25 = player advantage. More precise but harder.

## The Nevada Test (Chapter 5)
- Backed by two millionaire gamblers with $10,000
- Played ~30 hours using the Ten-count method
- $10,000 grew to over $21,000
- Never dipped more than $1,300 into original capital
- Casinos responded with shuffling, refusing chips, switching dealers, and cheating

## The Salmon Story (Chapter 6)
- Inspired reader in Puerto Rico turned $200 into $50,000 using point count
- Played 40 hours/week for 40 weeks (1,600 hours, ~240,000 hands)
- Never bet more than $50/hand
- One of the greatest marathon gambling feats in history

## Casino Countermeasures
- Shuffling up (reshuffling when bets increase)
- Rule changes (April 1, 1964 — first time in history Las Vegas changed blackjack rules)
- Multiple decks (reduces but doesn't eliminate advantage)
- Barring known players
- Employing cheating dealers ("knockout dealers")
- All countermeasures reversed within weeks due to business losses

## Casino Cheating (Chapters 10-11)
- Thorp estimates average player faces cheating 5-10% of the time
- Methods: marked cards, dealing seconds, the step-up, anchor men, mechanics on call
- Organized crime connections documented extensively
- "Operation Moneypump" proposed to Treasury — never executed

## Beyond Blackjack (Chapter 12)
- Thorp successfully beat baccarat side bets ($100-$1,000/hour)
- Developed working roulette prediction system (44% profit on regulation wheel)
- Drew explicit parallels between casino gambling and stock markets
- Predicted computer technology would enable dramatic progress in predicting "chance" phenomena

## Why This Book Matters
Published in 1962, Beat the Dealer was the first popular book to prove mathematically that a casino game could be beaten. It launched the card counting industry, forced Las Vegas to change its rules for the first time in history, and pioneered the application of probability theory to real-world advantage play. Thorp went on to apply the same quantitative methods to Wall Street, founding Princeton Newport Partners — one of the most successful early quantitative hedge funds. The book's influence extends far beyond blackjack into modern finance, information theory, and the Kelly criterion.
