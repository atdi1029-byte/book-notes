Chapter 1: Introduction

Blackjack is one of the most widely played gambling games on earth. In the early 1960s you could find it in Nevada casinos, Caribbean resorts, London clubs, and backroom games from Hong Kong to Macao. The English called it "van-john," Australians said "pontoon," and the French knew it as vingt-et-un. Whatever the name, the game was everywhere — and everywhere, the house won.

What made blackjack different from every other casino game was a mathematical fact that no one had yet exploited: the game has memory. In roulette, the ball has no recollection of where it landed last. In craps, the dice start fresh every throw. But in blackjack, cards dealt in one round are gone from the deck until the next shuffle. This means the odds shift continuously as cards are consumed. Sometimes the remaining deck favors the player. Sometimes it favors the house. If you could tell the difference, you could bet accordingly — and win.

Edward O. Thorp, a mathematics professor at MIT and later UC Irvine, was the first person to prove this rigorously. Using an IBM 704 computer, he calculated the exact effect of removing each type of card from the deck and discovered that certain depletions gave the player a significant mathematical edge. He then designed a hierarchy of increasingly powerful systems that any dedicated player could learn.

The simplest system — the basic strategy — required no card counting at all. It was just a set of memorized rules for every possible decision: when to hit, stand, split, or double down. Played correctly, it gave the player roughly a 0.1% advantage over the house in a typical Las Vegas game. That alone was revolutionary. No other casino game offered the player an edge of any kind.

Beyond basic strategy lay the counting systems. The Five-count system tracked only the four Fives in the deck. When all four had been played, the player enjoyed a 3.6% advantage and bet big. It was startlingly simple — most people were amazed they only needed to track four cards, not all fifty-two. The point-count system assigned +1 to low cards and −1 to high cards, identifying favorable situations far more frequently. The Ten-count method tracked the ratio of Tens to non-Tens with even greater precision.

Thorp tested these systems with real money in real casinos. Backed by two millionaires with $10,000 in cash, he played thirty hours in Nevada and turned the stake into over $21,000 — never dipping more than $1,300 into the original capital. The theory worked exactly as predicted. Casinos responded with panic: shuffling after every hand, switching dealers, introducing cheating mechanics, and ultimately changing the rules of blackjack for the first time in the game's history.

"When a lamb goes to the slaughter, the lamb might kill the butcher. But we always bet on the butcher." — A casino operator on national television

Thorp's reply was quiet and devastating: "The day of the lamb had come." Beat the Dealer sold over a million copies, hit the New York Times bestseller list, and launched an entire industry of advantage play. Readers used its methods not just at blackjack tables but as launching pads for horse racing syndicates, stock option trading firms, and hedge funds. The bet-sizing methods Thorp developed for blackjack are now standard tools in quantitative finance.

Chapter 2: The Rules of Blackjack

Understanding blackjack's rules precisely is the foundation of everything that follows. Most players think they know the rules, but few grasp the exact effect of each variation — and those variations are where the mathematical edge lives. A casino that lets you double down on any two cards gives you a measurably different game than one restricting doubles to hard 11 only. The difference might be less than a percentage point, but over thousands of hands, fractions of a percent compound into serious money.

The basic structure is simple. One dealer faces one to seven players. Each receives two cards. The dealer shows one card face up. Players decide whether to hit (take another card), stand, split pairs into separate hands, or double down (double the bet for exactly one more card). The goal is to beat the dealer's total without exceeding 21. Go over, and you "bust" — losing immediately, regardless of what the dealer holds.

This bust rule is the casino's structural advantage. If both the player and the dealer bust on the same hand, the dealer wins. The player busts first because the player must act first. Even if both sides play identical strategies, this asymmetry gives the house roughly a 5.7% edge. Against that, the player has one powerful compensation: a natural blackjack (an Ace plus a ten-value card) pays 3-to-2 instead of even money. That bonus is worth about 2.3% to the player, cutting the house edge significantly but not eliminating it.

The critical distinction between hard and soft hands governs optimal play. A soft hand contains an Ace counted as 11 without the total exceeding 21. A hard hand is everything else. The best strategy for a soft 17 differs sharply from a hard 17 — drawing to a soft 17 has almost no downside, while drawing to a hard 17 risks busting. This distinction trips up even experienced players who play both types identically.

Splitting pairs lets you turn one bad hand into two potentially better ones. Always split Aces and Eights. Never split Fours, Fives, or Tens. Aces are the best pair to split because each gives you a strong chance at 21. Eights should be split because 16 is the worst possible total — you're replacing one terrible hand with two average ones. Tens should never be split because you're breaking up a 20, one of the strongest hands possible, for two hands that are merely above average.

Doubling down lets you double your bet in exchange for receiving exactly one more card. It's most profitable on totals of 10 or 11, where drawing a ten-value card gives you an excellent total. Nevada casinos generally allowed doubling on any two cards, while Puerto Rico restricted it to hard 11 only — a restriction worth nearly a full percentage point to the house. The insurance side bet, offered when the dealer shows an Ace, pays 2-to-1 if the dealer has a natural. On average it carries a 5.9% house edge, but as Thorp would later show, card counters can turn it into a weapon against the casino.

One rule variation deserves special contempt: games where the dealer wins all ties. This gives the house a crushing 9% edge and should be avoided absolutely. Similarly, games where the dealer draws on soft 17 (rather than standing) cost the player about 0.2%. These details seem minor but they accumulate. Thorp's Table 9.2 catalogues the precise effect of every common rule variation, allowing players to evaluate any casino in the world.

Beyond the printed rules lie the customs — unwritten practices that vary from casino to casino, shift to shift, even dealer to dealer. Shills are house employees who pretend to be players, betting house money to attract customers. They follow rigid rules: never double down, never split pairs, never insure. Understanding these customs matters because the winning strategies depend on the exact conditions of play. How often does the dealer shuffle? How many decks are used? How deep into the deck does play go before a reshuffle? Each factor changes the math.

Chapter 3: The Basic Strategy

The basic strategy was born during a Christmas vacation. Thorp and his wife visited Las Vegas — not as gamblers but as tourists, enjoying shows and swimming pools. A UCLA colleague, Professor Sorgenfrey, had told him about a mathematics journal article by Baldwin, Cantey, Maisel, and McDermott that computed a blackjack strategy limiting the house edge to just 0.62%. Intrigued, Thorp wrote the strategy on a card and took it to a $10 blackjack table.

The scene was almost comical. The dealer was scornful of this "system player" consulting a card. Bystanders pitied his bizarre plays: splitting Eights, doubling on (Ace,2) against a dealer Five, standing on hard 12 against a Four. But after twenty minutes of play, during which the dealer ran hot and other players hemorrhaged chips, Thorp's ten silver dollars held steady. Then he drew to a soft 18 and received an Ace for a seven-card 21 — a hand that occurs once in several thousand deals. The atmosphere shifted from amusement to respect. Thorp left up $8.50 and thinking: there might be a way to beat this game.

"There might be a way to beat it." — Thorp's realization after his first casino visit

Back home, Thorp began intensive study. He used the IBM 704 computer to improve upon Baldwin's strategy, producing what he called the "basic strategy" — the mathematically optimal way to play every possible blackjack hand when you have no information about the remaining deck beyond your own cards and the dealer's up card. His calculations showed that a player using the correct basic strategy had a 0.12% advantage in a typical single-deck Las Vegas game. In some casinos the edge reached 0.6%. Even under the most adverse rules, the disadvantage was less than 1%.

The strategy boils down to a set of standing numbers. Against a dealer showing 7 or higher, stand on hard 17 or more and draw on 16 or less. Against a dealer showing 6 or lower, stand on hard 12 or more — the dealer is likely to bust. For soft hands, the standing numbers are higher because drawing cannot make your hand worse when the Ace can flex between 1 and 11. You can draw to a soft 16 with zero risk of making your hand poorer.

Doubling down follows clear patterns. Always double hard 11. Double hard 10 against everything except an Ace or Ten. Hard 9 doubles less frequently. Soft doubling is most profitable against dealer up cards of 2 through 6. Thorp tells a story from the Silver Slipper in Las Vegas: he shoved out $30 on (Ace,2) against a dealer Five in a 6% advantage situation. The dealer's hole card was a Four, and he drew a Ten for 19. Thorp's third card was a Seven, giving him only 10. The dealer wore a strange expression — Thorp's enormous advance bet on such weak hole cards seemed either prescient or insane. The basic strategy didn't win that particular hand, but over thousands of hands, it makes you "luckier" than any other approach.

For pair splitting, the approximate rules are beautifully simple: always split Aces and Eights, never split Fours, Fives, or Tens, and split everything else against a dealer showing 2 through 7. This approximation introduces only five errors and adds just 0.13% to the house advantage. The logic is intuitive. Splitting Aces gives you a strong chance at 21 on each hand. Splitting Eights replaces the worst total in blackjack (16) with two average starting hands. Splitting Tens destroys a near-perfect 20 for two slightly above-average hands — a terrible trade.

How does the basic strategy compare to how people actually play? The numbers are stark. A basic strategy player has a +0.1% edge. A player following the advice of card game "experts" faces a −3.2% disadvantage. A player who mimics the dealer (drawing to 16, standing on 17) faces −5.7%. A "never bust" strategy — standing on any hand of hard 12 or higher — costs between 5% and 8%. The typical casino player loses between 2% and 15%. In other words, the gap between optimal and average play is enormous. Compared to other casino games, basic strategy blackjack is the only game where the player has any edge at all. Craps at best play costs −1.4%. European roulette costs −1.35%. American roulette costs up to −5.26%.

Professor John Blattner of San Fernando Valley State College provided a perfect cautionary tale. His barber insisted that "anyone can win by just refusing to go bust" — standing on hard 12 always. After an after-hours game where Blattner lost $1,500 to the barber (who was dealing), the barber agreed to settle the debt with free haircuts for a year. Did Blattner trim his barber? Absolutely — the never-bust strategy guarantees the house a crushing edge. The barber was simply dealing himself the house advantage and collecting.

Chapter 4: A Winning Strategy

Every casino game has a house edge — a built-in mathematical advantage that ensures the casino wins over time. The casino adjusts its rules to cover expenses and yield a profit on invested capital. Players, meanwhile, chase the dream of beating the house with betting systems. The most famous is the Martingale (doubling-up system): bet $1, and if you lose, bet $2, then $4, then $8, continuing to double until you win. The first win recoups all previous losses plus $1 in profit. It sounds foolproof. It isn't.

A string of nine consecutive losses requires a $512 bet. Casino table limits (typically $500) prevent you from continuing. Even without limits, mathematicians proved long ago that no betting system can overcome a house edge in an independent trials process — a game where each round is unaffected by previous rounds. The cards, as the saying goes, have no memory. This is true for roulette and craps. But blackjack is fundamentally different.

"The cards have no memory." — Popular gambling phrase (which happens to be wrong for blackjack)

In blackjack, what happens in one round directly influences the next. If all four Aces appear in the first round, there will be no naturals, no soft hands, and no Ace splits for the remainder of that deck — a situation that costs the player nearly 3% until the next shuffle. The deck literally remembers what has been dealt. This dependence is the crack in the casino's armor, and Thorp drove a mathematical truck through it.

One Nevada casino was caught exploiting this same principle dishonestly. They removed four Tens and a Nine from their deck, adding 2.5% to the house advantage. The Nevada Gaming Control Board caught them and brought them to trial. Ironically, the casino's operators knew the short deck helped them but had no idea how much. An expert witness testified the disadvantage was 25% — ten times the actual effect — and the casino had no counter-argument. Their license was revoked.

Thorp asked the IBM 704 a series of questions: what happens to the player's advantage when all four cards of a single denomination are removed from the deck? The answers revealed a striking pattern. Removing low cards (2 through 7) helped the player; removing high cards (9, 10, Ace) hurt the player. And one card stood out above all others: the Five. Removing all four Fives shifted the player's advantage by an enormous 3.58% — the largest single-card effect in the game.

This discovery led to the Five-count strategy. The method was astonishingly simple: play basic strategy while watching for Fives. When all four Fives had been dealt, the remaining deck gave the player a 3.6% advantage. At that moment, bet big. The rest of the time, bet small. The player would lose fractional pennies on the small bets and win dollars on the big ones. The math was overwhelmingly favorable. Playing head-on at 100 hands per hour with $1 small bets and $500 big bets, the expected profit was roughly $170 per hour.

An even more refined approach tracked the ratio of unseen cards to unseen Fives (the U/F ratio). A normal deck has a U/F ratio of 13 (52 cards divided by 4 Fives). When the ratio exceeded 13, the deck was Five-poor and the casino had the edge. When it dropped below 13, the deck was Five-rich and the player had the edge. This method found many more favorable situations than simply waiting for all Fives to disappear, and it worked against any number of decks without modification.

"You must NEVER, NEVER play with money that it will hurt you to lose." — Edward O. Thorp

Capital management was essential. Playing with "scared money" produced psychological disintegration, bad decisions, and a higher chance of ruin. With a stake of 150 units (small bets of 1 unit, big bets of 3 units), the chance of losing everything was less than 1 in 10. With 75 units, the chance of ruin rose to about 3 in 10. The Five-count was the simplest winning system, but far more powerful methods were waiting.

Chapter 5: My Ideas Are Tested in Nevada

Before going to Nevada, Thorp had to go public. He submitted a paper titled "Fortune's Formula: A Winning Strategy for Blackjack" to the American Mathematical Society's annual meeting in Washington, D.C. The abstract was published before the meeting, and a reporter from the Boston Globe called. The story ran on the front page. News services picked it up. Thousands of newspapers across America carried the story of a math professor who claimed he could beat the casinos.

Thorp's office at MIT was flooded with mail. Hundreds of letters requested copies of the paper. A remarkable number included offers to bankroll casino tests — over $100,000 in total backing was proffered by strangers. Thorp chose two New York multimillionaires he calls Mr. X and Mr. Y. Mr. Y had once lost $100,000 in a single casino session without serious financial distress. Mr. X was familiar with card counting and had made large sums at blackjack himself. More importantly, Mr. X had the practical knowledge to spot casino cheating.

They arrived in Reno at 2 A.M. during MIT's one-week spring recess, carrying $10,000 in cash — a hundred hundred-dollar bills. Thorp used the Ten-count method, tracking the ratio of non-Tens to Tens in the remaining deck. A full deck has a ratio of 36/16 = 2.25. When the ratio dropped below 2.25, the player had the edge. Thorp's betting was conservative: 2x minimum at 1% advantage, 4x at 2%, leveling off at 10x minimum at 5% or more.

The results validated the mathematics perfectly. The $10,000 grew to over $21,000 in about thirty hours of play. They never had to dig more than $1,300 into the original capital. But the casinos fought back with everything they had. Dealers shuffled whenever Thorp changed his bet size — or scratched his nose. Casino owners refused to sell large-denomination chips. Four new decks appeared in five minutes at one table. The dealer at one Reno casino accused Thorp of having "unusually acute vision" and memorizing every card — Thorp challenged him to demonstrate this skill for $5, then $50, then $500. The dealer refused.

"It is well known to students of mnemotechny that one can readily learn to memorize in proper order part or all of a deck of cards as it is dealt out." — Edward O. Thorp, responding to accusations of impossible memory

At the Primadonna casino, Thorp disposed of three silver dollars at a blackjack table and caught a great sequence of favorable situations — $3 became $35 in five minutes. But the most telling moment came when a balding man at the next club looked at them nervously and said, "No, thank you" — they had been recognized as system players and were being turned away. The system worked. The casinos knew it worked. The only question was how they would respond.

Thorp returned to MIT satisfied. The system had performed in practice exactly as theory predicted. He had proven something that no mathematician had ever proven before: a casino gambling game could be beaten consistently by a player using nothing but his brain and a sound understanding of probability.

Chapter 6: The Simple Point-Count System

The point-count system refined and simplified the Five-count into something far more powerful. The idea was elegant: assign a value of +1 to every low card (2 through 6) and −1 to every high card (10, Ace). Cards 7 through 9 count as zero. As you watch cards being played, keep a running total. When the total is positive, the remaining deck is rich in high cards and the player has the edge. When it's negative, the deck favors the house.

Betting was proportional to the count. If the count was zero or negative, bet the minimum. If positive, bet as many units as the count. A count of +4 meant the deck was as favorable as if all four Fives had been removed — but the point count found these situations far more frequently and far earlier in the deck. Players using this system could win several times faster than with the Five-count alone.

The system got its most spectacular test in Puerto Rico. After Beat the Dealer was published, Thorp appeared on the TV show I've Got a Secret, where comedian Henry Morgan was dispatched to test the system with a $200 bankroll. Thorp spent an afternoon teaching Morgan the basics, but Morgan's jokes proved more interesting than the training. Meanwhile, Thorp flew to Puerto Rico with two young entrepreneurs, Mr. M and Mr. N, for serious play.

Puerto Rico was a different world from Nevada. The government posted official rules on casino walls. Casinos couldn't suddenly change rules as in Nevada. A government official sat in the playing area at all times. No liquor was served. Two decks were dealt face up from a shoe, and — crucially — the dealing went all the way to the last card before reshuffling. This created perfect conditions for card counting. The betting limits were small ($1 to $50), but Thorp could play up to seven hands simultaneously at empty tables, multiplying his edge.

At the tables, Thorp encountered a legend. The casino called him the Salmon (Puerto Rican slang for "jerk"), and he cultivated that image brilliantly. The Salmon would stack chips in towering irregular columns, scatter them across the layout to block other players, and keep up a stream of amusing chatter with the dealers. He would sometimes hit a blackjack until it busted, or stand on a pair of deuces. The casino staff thought he was a madman. He was, in fact, using end play — a devastating technique made possible by the deal-to-the- last-card rule.

End play worked like this: near the end of the shoe, the Salmon would bet $1 on four or five hands, deliberately busting strong hands and standing on weak ones. This separated the high cards (Tens and Aces) from the low cards, routing the good cards into the discard pile. When the shoe ran out and the discards were reshuffled, the new shoe was rich in high cards. The Salmon then bet the maximum $50 on every hand. The advantage frequently reached 10% or more, sometimes approaching certainty.

The Salmon had started with $200 and a 99% chance of winning indefinitely. Over nine months of play — roughly 40 hours per week, 240,000 hands total — his $200 grew to $50,000. He never bet more than $50 per hand. In Las Vegas, with a $500 limit, the same results would have yielded $500,000. It was one of the great marathon feats in gambling history, and it proved the point-count system beyond any reasonable doubt.

Thorp also realized during this trip that a complete count of all cards — using a helper with a tally sheet — could reveal the dealer's exact hole card near the end of the shoe. Julian Braun of IBM had calculated that knowing the dealer's hole card gave the player a 9.9% advantage. At $50 per hand across six hands per hour, this translated to $900 per evening. Puerto Rican casinos changed their rules soon after, and the opportunity disappeared — but not before the Salmon's exploits had proven the mathematics spectacularly.

Chapter 7: The Complete Point-Count System

The complete point count — also known as the high-low system — was independently discovered by many readers of the first edition. The first person to announce it scientifically was Harvey Dubner of Simmons Precision Products, who presented it at a panel session of the 1963 Fall Joint Computer Conference in Las Vegas. The panel included Dubner, Julian Braun of IBM (whose computer analysis was the most accurate in existence), Allan Wilson (author of The Casino Gambler's Guide), and Robert Kalaba of the RAND Corporation. Thorp moderated.

The complete point count perfected the simple version by dividing the running point total by the number of unseen cards to produce the high-low index. This normalization made the count work accurately across any number of decks. With one deck, if four cards had fallen (5, 5, 3, 8), the point total was +3 and 48 cards remained unseen, giving an index of 3/48 ≈ 6%. With four decks, the same point total over a larger pool of unseen cards would produce a smaller index, reflecting the reduced advantage.

The betting scheme was straightforward: bet 1 unit for an index of 2 or less. At index 4, bet 2 units. The general rule was to bet half the index — index 6 meant 3 units, index 8 meant 4, and index 10 or more meant 5 units (the cap, because larger variations excited casinos too much). The system also adjusted playing strategy based on the count. When the deck was rich in high cards, you stood more often, doubled down more often, and split pairs more often. When it was poor, you drew more aggressively.

The strategy variations were codified in detailed tables. Table 7.1 governed hard standing decisions: for each combination of dealer up card and player total, a threshold index determined whether to stand or draw. If your index exceeded the threshold, you stood; otherwise you drew. The tables also covered soft hands, hard and soft doubling down, pair splitting, and insurance. The insurance threshold was simple: if the index exceeded 8, take insurance. Otherwise, don't.

Braun's computer analysis confirmed that the complete point count was comparable in power to the Ten-count method. In an era when casinos were learning to spot counters and tightening their rules, having two independent counting systems was a significant tactical advantage. A player caught using one could switch to the other. Thorp's recommendation: beginners should master either the point count or the Ten count (not both), while experts should learn both as alternate weapons.

The frequency data told the story of profitability. Based on 200,000 random shuffles, after five cards were dealt, the index fell between +5 and +15 about 9.5% of the time — situations where the player had a meaningful edge. The index fell between −5 and +5 about 81% of the time — essentially neutral territory. The beauty of the system was that the player gained more from positive-index situations than they lost from negative-index situations, because they could vary both their bets and their strategy.

Chapter 8: Counting Tens

The Ten-count strategy was the workhorse system of the first edition — the method Thorp used to win $21,000 in Nevada and the one that thousands of readers took into casinos around the world. It tracked two numbers: the count of non-Tens ("others") and the count of Tens remaining in the deck. A full deck starts at (36, 16). The ratio of others to Tens — 36/16 = 2.25 — is the key metric. When the ratio drops below 2.25, the remaining deck is Ten-rich and the player has the advantage. When it rises above 2.25, the house has the edge.

Learning to count required practice. Thorp recommended turning over a shuffled deck one card at a time while maintaining the running count. A speed of 25 to 50 seconds per deck was the target — 50 seconds was "more than adequate," 25 seconds was "excellent." Thorp himself had trouble breaking below 25 seconds until he realized it took nearly that long just to physically turn over the cards. The real test was maintaining the count while playing and making conversation — which required a different kind of practice, best done with a partner dealing hands.

The counting skill also served as a cheating detector. At one casino, Thorp watched a dealer spread a deck for examination after the joker vanished mid-deal. Players demanded to inspect the cards. The dealer spread and scooped the deck in about four seconds — far too fast for a normal examination. Thorp managed to count 38 cards in the fraction of time available, then quickly counted the remaining pile: 20 cards. The deck had 58 cards. The pit boss counted the deck privately, wore an odd expression, and walked away with it. The trusting players continued their game without a second thought.

The conservative betting scheme (Table 8.2) scaled wagers to the ratio: 1 unit above 2.00, 2 units between 2.00 and 1.75, 4 units between 1.75 and 1.65, and 5 units below 1.65. Bets capped at 5 units to avoid alarming the casino. The insurance rule was clean: insure when the ratio was below 2.00, don't insure when above. This turned the insurance bet — originally introduced by casinos as a trap for suckers — into a profitable weapon for the counter.

Two widespread misconceptions about insurance drove Thorp to distraction. The first was "always insure a natural." The reasoning sounded airtight: if the dealer also has a natural, you tie but win the insurance bet (1 unit). If the dealer doesn't, you win 1.5 units on your natural but lose 0.5 on insurance (net 1 unit). Either way, you lock in exactly 1 unit of profit. Why not take the sure thing? Because when the deck is depleted of Tens, the dealer can't possibly have a natural. In that case, not insuring nets you 1.5 units instead of 1. Insuring throws away half a unit.

The second misconception — "insure good hands, never poor ones" — was equally wrong. The decision to insure should depend entirely on the deck composition, not on your hand. Insurance is a side bet on whether the dealer has a Ten in the hole. Your cards are irrelevant to that question. When the ratio is below 2.00, insure everything. When it's above, insure nothing. It really was that simple.

Near the end of the deck, the Ten-count revealed extraordinary information. With a $250 hand, Thorp once knew from his count that only two Tens remained as the last cards. He held hard 18 against the dealer's Ten up. The dealer's hole card was certain to be the other Ten, giving the dealer 20. Drawing would expose the last card and force a shuffle. Thorp drew a 3 for 21 and won $250. In another hand, he avoided drawing on hard 19 against a dealer's certain 20, because the only remaining card was either an Ace or a deuce — neither of which could improve his hand past 20.

The end play — the technique of manipulating the deck composition near its end — produced the most dramatic gains. A legendary player Thorp calls "the little dark-haired guy" negotiated a high-stakes private game at a Reno casino under the condition that the deck would be dealt to the last card. He won $10,000 to $15,000 on the first night, lost and won similar amounts on successive nights (keeping the casino engaged), and then cleaned them out for somewhere between $40,000 and $86,000 on the final session. He sold the same proposition to other Nevada casinos over two years and won more than $250,000 before being barred statewide.

Chapter 9: Beating the Casino Countermeasures

Once Beat the Dealer hit the bestseller list, casinos had to respond. Their first instinct was the shuffle-up — reshuffling the deck whenever a player raised their bet. Thorp had initially feared this would neutralize card counting. He was wrong. Even with reshuffling after every hand, the player at a single-deck table still held the basic strategy's 0.13% edge. And a player taking six hands at once could gain roughly 1% just from seeing their own cards across the table before making decisions on later hands.

Some casinos taught their dealers to count cards. The purpose was twofold: spot counting players, and shuffle when the deck favored the player while continuing to deal when it favored the house. Thorp's counter-tactic was to sit in the last seat, where he could see the most cards before acting, and bet heavily on the first deal off a fresh shuffle (where the basic strategy edge applied). Against "fake" counters — dealers who pretended to count but actually had no idea — the solution was even sweeter. Start with a big bet when the deck was bad. The fake counter would reshuffle, destroying the unfavorable deck. Then bet normally against the fresh neutral deck. The fake counter was essentially shuffling away all the bad situations and leaving the player with nothing but average-to-good decks. It was money in the bank.

Multiple decks were another popular countermeasure. Some casinos moved to two or four decks, believing this would make counting impossibly difficult. It was slightly more work, but manageable for any practiced counter. The real drawback was that advantages in multi-deck games were smaller (about 0.4% less with two decks, 0.5% less with four) and favorable situations occurred less frequently. But multi-deck games had a compensating virtue: when a multi-deck shoe went good, it stayed good much longer. A player could sit down to a favorable situation and ride it for many hands without the composition shifting back.

The most dramatic countermeasure came on April 1, 1964. The Las Vegas Resort Hotel Association changed the rules of blackjack — the first time in history that a major casino game's rules had been significantly altered. Splitting Aces was forbidden. Doubling down was restricted to hard 11 only. Gabriel Vogliatti, the Association's spokesman, explained:

"In the last 15 years there hasn't been one plane that landed without at least one person in possession of a system. This guy [Thorp] is the first in Las Vegas history to have a system that works." — Gabriel Vogliatti, Las Vegas Resort Hotel Association

The system players went back the next day and continued winning. The rules changes cut their win rate but didn't eliminate it. What the changes did eliminate was tourist traffic. Overnight, blackjack play fell off. All gaming table play declined. Casino employees whose income depended on tips began screaming that the new rules were "the bane of the industry." As journalist Jude Wanniski reported in the National Observer, one casino quietly scuttled the new rules, then another. Within weeks, Las Vegas threw in the towel and restored the original rules. They would rather have system players winning than no players at all.

The paper-route technique provided the ultimate defense against all countermeasures. Set aside 20-25 units before each session. Play until you win or lose that amount, or until one hour has passed. Then move to the next casino. Stroll from casino to casino "collecting money, much as a newsboy for a paper route goes from door to door." No single dealer gets enough time to identify you. No single session creates enough drama to attract management attention. Return to dealers you win against. Avoid the ones you lose to (they might be cheating). The method was the practical application of all the theory that came before.

Thorp's own camouflage grew increasingly elaborate. After intense publicity and wide photo distribution made getting a normal game nearly impossible, he grew a beard, got contact lenses, and switched to sweep-around sunglasses. He won for two days in Las Vegas before casinos began treating all bearded players with suspicion. Later, after shaving the beard and changing his entire appearance, he returned to the same table where a blonde dealer had watched him play the night before. Zero recognition. He was, as he noted, "a springy, crew-cut young executive of twenty-five" instead of the "aging unkempt man of forty-five" he'd appeared the night before.

Chapter 10: How to Spot Cheating

Before becoming a blackjack researcher, Thorp believed what everyone believed: casinos could cheat but generally didn't. The reasoning seemed sound — why risk exposure and license revocation when the house already had a mathematical advantage? The answer, Thorp discovered through painful personal experience, was that organized crime didn't care about mathematical arguments. The Mafia and Cosa Nostra controlled many of the largest casinos. Men who bribed officials, financed drug operations, and committed murder to settle disputes were not going to stop short at a little cheating at cards.

Thorp estimated that the average player faced cheating about 5-10% of the time. The most common method was dealing seconds. The dealer would mark the deck (or use a pre-marked deck — millions of which were manufactured annually and available by mail order), peek at the top card, and deal the second card instead if the top card would help the player. A skilled "mechanic" could deal seconds so smoothly that even experienced observers couldn't see it. The telltale sign was a subtle wrist flick as the dealer released each card — an otherwise unnecessary motion that helped prevent the second card from hanging.

Mr. X learned this the hard way. Playing the Ten-count system at a large hotel, he won $13,000 in a few hours. The hotel brought in a "knockout" dealer — a cheat employed specifically to dispose of big winners. She peeked at the top card when drawing to her own hand. If she liked it, she dealt it to herself honestly. If she didn't, she dealt the second card. She worked in 40-minute shifts with 20-minute breaks (during which the game was honest). Mr. X's fatal error was continuing to bet at full scale against the knockout dealer instead of reducing to minimum bets. In ten hours he lost back $20,000 — wiping out his $13,000 win and putting him $7,000 in the hole.

Thorp witnessed cheating firsthand the next morning at the same casino. The dealer — a thinnish, grim-faced woman of about forty with black hair beginning to gray — bent the top card back slightly to peek at it. Thorp and Mr. Y watched her see the Queen of Hearts, which would have busted her. She dealt the second card instead (an Eight, giving her 21). When they angrily spelled out what she had done, she reddened, looked down, and said nothing. The pit boss showed no reaction. It was their word against hers.

The step-up was another elegant cheat. The dealer would stack discarded cards in a specific sequence and preserve that arrangement through apparently thorough shuffles. The result was predetermined winning hands for the house. A Newport, Kentucky dealer claimed to have invented the technique and wanted royalties from the casinos using it. At one casino, Thorp's team bet $1, and the dealer offered them a cut of an unshuffled deck. The cut revealed a step-up going to the top. The dealer received a 20 followed by a 21. When Thorp's team commented on the cheat, the dealer laughed happily, "proud of his handiwork."

"We commented on how we had been cheated by a step-up and the dealer laughed happily, proud of his handiwork and proud that someone finally appreciated it." — Edward O. Thorp

Anchor men were among the subtlest weapons. These were inconspicuous house plants who sat at the table and took signals from the dealer. When the dealer wanted to keep the top card for himself, the anchor man stood pat. When the dealer wanted to deny a player the top card, the anchor man drew. This made dealing seconds unnecessary — the anchor man simply controlled the flow of cards. Thorp's team learned to counter this by waiting until their turn to look at their own cards, denying the anchor man (and dealer) any information about their likely action.

One cheat dealer showed Thorp a novel card-marking method. He pressed his thumbnail against the top edges of Aces and Tens, creating a barely perceptible rounding. The cards looked identical to all others in the deck. But when held at a certain angle to the light, the marked edges gleamed just enough for a trained eye. Because of the reflection angles, when the dealer could see the gleam, no one else could. The Nevada Gaming Control Board had confiscated decks marked this way, projected them greatly enlarged on a wall, and still couldn't detect the markings. As this dealer confided to Thorp, he and his friends fell asleep at night "thinking up new ways to cheat 'em."

Chapter 11: Can the Cheating Be Stopped?

Life magazine broke the story. Reporter Paul O'Neil spent a week studying Thorp's methods in Las Cruces and four days with him in Las Vegas during the 1963 Fall Joint Computer Conference. O'Neil watched Thorp win $420 in 4.5 hours of spare-time play, betting $1 to $25. During 20 minutes of posing for photographs at the Tropicana (where Thorp was unrecognized), the tables rewarded him with $62.50. The twelve-page Life article was painstakingly accurate and thoroughly researched.

The response from Nevada was predictable. Edward A. Olsen, chairman of the Nevada Gaming Control Board, accused Life of libeling the state. He demanded evidence for the claim that Thorp had been "victimized by crooked dealers in almost all of the major casinos in Nevada" and suggested that Thorp's ability to sense cheating "in his nerve ends" was more worthy of investigation than his card counting system. Shortly after writing this letter, Olsen's own agency closed the Silver Slipper casino on cheating charges — crooked dice at the craps tables and a blackjack dealer caught cheating by undercover agents.

Thorp proposed a concrete solution to casino cheating: movies. A sub-miniature camera concealed on a player could capture the dealer's hands frame by frame, revealing seconds, step-ups, and other sleight-of-hand. The footage would need to show the cheating move, the dealer's face for identification, and the casino's emblems on the table layout. Nevada state law prohibited photography inside casinos without permission, but Thorp argued the legal difficulties were surmountable and the evidence would be devastating.

Even more provocatively, Thorp proposed "Operation Moneypump" to the U.S. Treasury Department. A federal agent had told him that certain Nevada casinos were taking enormous sums "off the top" — recording lower gross revenues than they actually received, thereby evading taxes. If declared casino gambling income was $220 million in 1961, and about one-third was being skimmed, the government was losing over $25 million in tax revenue annually.

The plan was elegant. A team of twelve agents (male and female) would learn the winning strategy in two to three days of training. They would gamble at selected tables, purchasing chips with hundred-dollar bills whose serial numbers had been pre-recorded. After the casino counted its drop boxes, investigators could check whether the marked bills appeared in the reported totals. If money was being taken off the top, the marked bills would disappear from the records at a statistically detectable rate. Since the agents were using a winning strategy, the same $30,000 bankroll could pump millions through the system.

Operation Moneypump was filed away in a dusty federal drawer. Certain casinos continued taking money off the top. The national tax collection continued falling short of expenditures. As Thorp ruefully observed, the plan died not because it was flawed but because it was too effective — its success would have embarrassed too many powerful people.

Chapter 12: Science Versus Chance

The history of system players began long before Thorp. Greasy John was the first known successful blackjack player — a large, obese man who acquired his name by bringing an enormous bag of fried chicken to the casino. He would play for twenty hours at a stretch, never leaving the table, his hands perpetually dripping with chicken fat. The grease transferred to the cards so aggressively that even frequent deck changes couldn't keep the table playable. Other players fled. Women were eventually banned from sitting at his table. Greasy John played for months, became wealthy enough to retire, and then suffered a fatal heart attack. Thorp believed he had discovered end play empirically.

Benjamin F. "System Smitty" Smith was, to Thorp's knowledge, the first person to employ a Ten-count system in casino play. Smitty spent years playing out 100,000 hands by hand to determine standing numbers for various Tens-to-others ratios. His system had moderate errors (no detailed strategy for doubling or splitting, which cost 2-3% of edge) and he likely overbet his bankroll — leading to spectacular swings. Mr. Z, a mutual acquaintance, watched Smitty win $108,000 at the blackjack tables in one night and lose it all back by breakfast, not even retaining the price of a meal.

Smitty's system spread to a small group: an old-time gambler called Mr. F, Mr. F's mistress, Mr. X and Mr. Z from Thorp's Nevada test, and a young player known as Junior (also "Sonny"). The group collectively pumped large sums out of Nevada blackjack tables — the grapevine credited Mr. F with $50,000, Mr. Z with $56,000, Mr. X with $100,000 to $150,000, and the "little dark-haired guy" with $250,000. After being universally barred, Junior went to a Hollywood movie studio makeup department and paid $500 to be disguised as a middle-aged Chinese man, complete with a fitted carapace over his torso. He tested the disguise at a casino with six employees who knew him. Five paid no attention. The sixth wandered over from the bar and exclaimed: "Hey, look everybody. There's Sonny all dressed up like a Chinese."

Beyond blackjack, Thorp applied the same mathematical approach to other games. He and trained players attacked baccarat side bets in Nevada, averaging $100 per hour for seven nights at one casino (which then removed the side bet) and $1,000 per hour at a second casino before being barred after two hours. Baccarat side bets disappeared from Nevada entirely after their success. He also developed a method for beating roulette — not by finding biased wheels, but through physical prediction. Testing on a regulation wheel in a famous scientist's basement laboratory, he averaged a 44% profit betting $25 per number, winning a fictional $8,000 in one hour.

Thorp's vision extended far beyond casinos. He drew explicit parallels between gambling and the stock market, calling Wall Street "the greatest gambling game on earth" with daily betting of $250 million. The similarities were striking: croupiers became customers' men, commissions became the house percentage, board rooms became casinos. He observed that stock prices showed mathematical patterns — that fractional endings (halves, quarters, eighths) were not uniformly distributed — and predicted that computer technology would enable "dramatic progress in predicting stock prices." Within a few years, he would prove himself right by founding Princeton Newport Partners, one of the first and most successful quantitative hedge funds.

"With the advance in computer technology and mathematical theory, we can expect dramatic progress in predicting stock prices." — Edward O. Thorp, 1966

The trajectory from Beat the Dealer to Wall Street was logical and inevitable. The same mathematical framework — identify situations where you have an edge, size your bets proportionally, manage your bankroll to survive the inevitable losing streaks — applied to securities markets exactly as it applied to blackjack. The Kelly criterion (bet a fraction of your capital equal to your percentage advantage) worked in both domains. Thorp had not just beaten a card game. He had demonstrated a universal principle: wherever probability could be calculated more accurately than the market consensus, there was money to be made.

Key Takeaways

Blackjack is not an independent trials process

Unlike roulette or craps, blackjack has memory. Cards dealt in previous rounds change the probabilities of future rounds. This dependence is the mathematical foundation that makes card counting possible. No betting system can beat an independent trials game, but blackjack's dependence creates exploitable shifts in player advantage.

The Five is the most important card in the deck

Removing all four Fives from a single deck shifts the player's advantage by 3.58% — the largest single-card effect. This is why tracking Fives was the simplest winning strategy and why the Five-count system worked despite tracking only four cards out of fifty-two.

Basic strategy alone nearly eliminates the house edge

A player using the correct basic strategy faces a house edge of roughly 0.1% in favorable conditions — essentially even. This compares to 2-15% for typical casino players. Basic strategy is the foundation upon which all counting systems are built.

The point count identifies favorable situations far more frequently than tracking single cards

The simple point count (+1 for low cards, −1 for high cards) identifies favorable situations earlier and more often than the Five-count. The complete point count, normalized by unseen cards, provides the most precise measure of player advantage and works across any number of decks.

Casino countermeasures consistently failed

Shuffle-ups, multiple decks, barring known players, and rule changes all reduced but never eliminated the counter's advantage. The April 1964 rule changes in Las Vegas were reversed within weeks because they drove away tourists faster than they stopped counters.

Bankroll management is as important as the counting system

Playing with scared money produces psychological collapse and bad decisions. Betting proportionally to your advantage (the Kelly criterion) maximizes long-term growth while managing ruin risk. A stake of 150 units with a 1:3 bet ratio gives less than a 10% chance of total ruin.

Casino cheating was widespread and organized

Thorp documented dealing seconds, marked cards, step-ups, anchor men, and knockout dealers at casinos across Nevada. An estimated 5-10% of all blackjack play involved some form of cheating, much of it connected to organized crime operations.

The same principles apply to financial markets

Thorp explicitly drew the connection between advantage play in blackjack and quantitative investing. Identify edge, size bets proportionally, manage risk. He proved this by going on to found one of the most successful quantitative hedge funds in history.

Contrarian Ideas

Consensus: No one can beat a casino game

For centuries, mathematicians proved that no betting system could overcome a house edge. Thorp showed this was true only for independent trials games. Blackjack's dependent structure meant the house edge fluctuated continuously, and a player who tracked these fluctuations could bet selectively and win.

Consensus: Always insure a natural

The logic seems watertight — lock in a guaranteed profit of 1 unit. But when the deck is depleted of Tens, the dealer cannot have a natural. Not insuring nets 1.5 units instead of 1. The decision to insure should depend entirely on deck composition, not on your hand.

Consensus: Conservative play (never bust) is the safest strategy

Standing on hard 12 to avoid busting seems prudent but costs 5-8% — worse than mimicking the dealer. Drawing to a hard 12 against a dealer's high card is mathematically superior, even though it sometimes causes you to bust. The "safe" play is actually the most expensive.

Consensus: Casino gambling is purely entertainment

Thorp proved that blackjack could be played as a profession. The Salmon turned $200 into $50,000 over nine months of disciplined play. System Smitty's group collectively extracted hundreds of thousands. Blackjack was not entertainment — it was a job with calculable hourly wages.

Consensus: Casinos are too heavily regulated to cheat

The Nevada Gaming Control Board closed more than twenty casinos for cheating in its first five years alone. Organized crime controlled many of the largest establishments. Mechanics were on 24-hour call. Regulation existed, but enforcement was sporadic and the incentives for cheating were enormous.

The Kelly Criterion for Bet Sizing

Bet a percentage of your bankroll equal to your percentage advantage. With a $200 bankroll and a 3% edge, bet $6. As your bankroll grows, bets grow proportionally. This maximizes the long-term growth rate of your capital while keeping the risk of ruin mathematically manageable.

The Paper-Route Technique

Divide your stake into equal portions (e.g., $200 into ten $20 parts). At each casino, buy in for one portion. Play until you double it or lose it, or until one hour passes. Move to the next casino. Return to dealers you win against. Never return to dealers you lose to. No single cheat can hurt you significantly, and no casino gets enough exposure to identify your system.

About the Author

Edward Oakley Thorp (born 1932) is an American mathematics professor, hedge fund manager, and author. He received his Ph.D. in mathematics from UCLA and held teaching positions at MIT, New Mexico State University, and UC Irvine, where he became a professor of mathematics and finance. His academic work spans probability theory, functional analysis, and mathematical finance.

Thorp is best known for two revolutionary applications of mathematics. First, Beat the Dealer (1962) demonstrated that blackjack could be beaten through card counting, making him the father of advantage play. The book sold over a million copies and hit the New York Times bestseller list. Second, he applied the same quantitative framework to financial markets, co-founding Princeton Newport Partners in 1969 — one of the first market-neutral hedge funds, which generated annualized returns of roughly 20% over nearly two decades with virtually no losing periods.

Along the way, Thorp co-invented the first wearable computer (with Claude Shannon) to predict roulette outcomes, developed early statistical arbitrage strategies, and helped expose the Bernie Madoff fraud years before regulators caught on. His later memoir, A Man for All Markets (2017), traces the thread from Las Vegas blackjack tables to Wall Street trading floors. At its core, Thorp's career demonstrates a single principle: wherever probability can be calculated more precisely than the market believes, there is an exploitable edge.

Why This Book Matters

Beat the Dealer is not just a gambling manual — it is one of the most influential applications of mathematics to the real world ever published. Before Thorp, the consensus among mathematicians was that no player could consistently beat a casino game. After Thorp, that consensus was shattered. The book proved that probability theory was not merely an abstract academic discipline but a practical tool for extracting profit from complex systems.

Its influence rippled far beyond the casino floor. The card counters who learned from this book went on to create horse racing syndicates, options trading firms, and hedge funds. The Kelly criterion for bet sizing, which Thorp popularized, became a standard tool in quantitative finance. The concept of identifying an "edge" and sizing your position accordingly — now fundamental to every systematic trading operation — traces directly to the blackjack table.

Perhaps most remarkably, Thorp's methods were validated by the casinos themselves. Las Vegas changed its rules for the first time in history to combat card counting. When that failed, they reversed the changes within weeks — an implicit admission that the math was right and the casino industry simply had to live with it. Over sixty years later, card counting remains legal, casinos remain profitable, and Beat the Dealer remains in print. It is the rare book that changed both an industry and a scientific discipline.
