## Part I: Verdict of History

## Chapter 1: The Case for Equity

### "Everybody Ought to Be Rich" — The Raskob Story

- Summer 1929: journalist **Samuel Crowther** interviewed **John J. Raskob** (senior financial executive at General Motors)
- *Ladies' Home Journal* article (August 1929): "Everybody Ought to Be Rich"
- Promised $15/month in stocks → $80,000 in 20 years (implied 24% annual return)
- September 3, 1929: Dow hit 381.17; seven weeks later stocks crashed
- July 8, 1932: Dow at 41.22 — market declined 89%, millions of savings wiped out
- **Senator Arthur Robinson** (Indiana) publicly blamed Raskob for crash
- Despite ridicule, Raskob's advice actually worked: $15/month from 1929 exceeded Treasury bills in less than four years
- By 1949: stock portfolio ~$9,000 (7.86% return); after 30 years: over $60,000 (12.72% return) — more than 8x bonds, 9x T-bills

### Asset Returns Since 1802

- **Annualized real returns 1802-2021**: Stocks 6.9%, Bonds 3.6%, Bills 2.5%, Gold 0.6%, Dollar -1.4%
- $1 in stocks (1802) → $54.2 million by 2021 (nominal 8.4%); bonds → $50,206 (5.0%); gold → $94 (2.1%)
- Stock returns hug trendline remarkably well over 220 years on logarithmic scale
- 6.9% real return = diversified stock portfolio nearly doubled purchasing power every 10 years
- Including 2022 bear market: long-term real return reduced to 6.7%

### Historical Perspectives on Stocks as Investments

- **19th century**: stocks deemed province of speculators and insiders
- **Edgar Lawrence Smith** (1925): *Common Stocks as Long-Term Investments* — first to show diversified stocks outperformed bonds in rising AND falling price periods
- *The Economist* (1925): "Every intelligent investor and stockbroker should study Mr. Smith's most interesting little book"

> "Unless we have had the extreme misfortune to invest at the very peak of a noteworthy rise, those periods in which the average market value of our holding remains less than the amount we paid for them are of comparatively short duration. Our hazard even in such extreme cases appears to be that of time alone." — Edgar Lawrence Smith

- **John Maynard Keynes** reviewed Smith's book: "the prejudice of investors in favor of bonds has led to relative over-valuation of bonds and under-valuation of common stocks"
- **Irving Fisher** (Yale economist, greatest US economist and father of capital theory) saw Smith as confirming bonds overrated
- Fisher's personal wealth summer 1929: exceeded $10 million ($150 million in 2021 dollars)
- October 14, 1929: declared stocks at "permanently high plateau" — two weeks before crash destroyed his reputation and fortune
- Similarity between 1920s stability and pre-2008 decade: both featured business cycle moderation and confidence Fed could mitigate cycles

> "The market overrates the safety of 'safe' securities and pays too much for them, overrates the risk of risky securities and pays too little for them." — Irving Fisher, 1925

- Post-crash: **Graham and Dodd** (1934, *Security Analysis*) attacked common stock theory
- **Lawrence Chamberlain**: "Common stocks, as such, are not superior to bonds as long-term investments, because in principal they are not investments at all. They are speculations."
- **Alfred Cowles III** (founder, Cowles Commission): constructed cap-weighted indexes back to 1871 for all NYSE stocks, confirming Smith's findings
- **Eiteman and Smith** (Michigan): dollar cost averaging in 92 industrials yielded 12.2% (later repeated: 14.2%)
- **Fisher and Lorie** (1964, University of Chicago): stocks returned 9.0% per year 1926-1960
- **Ibbotson and Sinquefield**: "Stocks, Bonds, Bills, and Inflation" — became industry return benchmarks

### The Great Bull Market of 1982-2000

- 1970s: surging inflation, sharply higher oil prices — negative real stock returns for 15 years (1966-summer 1982)
- August 1982 Dow low: 790 → January 2000: 11,723 (over 15% annually for 18 years)
- October 19, 1987: greatest one-day decline in US history — quickly erased by ongoing bull
- Market became national preoccupation: TVs in bars, airports, cruise ships tuned to CNBC; monitors in airline seat backs
- **David Shulman** (Salomon Brothers, September 1995): "Fear and Greed" — compared to 1929 and 1961 peaks
- **Alan Greenspan** "irrational exuberance" speech December 5, 1996, before American Enterprise Institute — little lasting impact
- **Glassman and Hassett** (1999): *Dow 36,000* — claimed Dow grossly undervalued; Siegel disavowed their conclusion
- March 29, 1999: Dow closed above 10,000 for first time
- November 1997-March 2000: Dow rose 40%; Nasdaq rose 185%; dot-com index soared nearly tenfold
- **March 10, 2000**: Nasdaq peaked at 5,048; then fell 78%; dot-com index fell >95%
- S&P 500 declined 49.15% — worst since Great Depression
- **Bill Gross** (PIMCO, September 2002): called for "Dow 5,000" — stocks never fell near that level
- Bar owner quote: "People are licking their wounds. It's back to sports, women, and who won the game"

### From Tech Bust to Financial Crisis

- Recovery: Dow low 7,286 (October 2002) → all-time high 14,165 (October 2007); S&P at modest 16x earnings
- Real estate prices nearly tripled previous decade, peaked summer 2006
- Great Financial Crisis: Dow plunged 53.5% to March 2009 low — largest since Great Depression
- Bears used **CAPE ratio** (Shiller) and **Q ratio** (Tobin) — both persistently bearish throughout subsequent bull
- *The Economist* (May 2011): "if you don't like what Shiller is telling you, it is because you're a 'bull' who thinks 'this time is different'"
- **Smithers** (Q ratio): predicted -3% annualized 10-year US equity returns early 2013
- **Jeremy Grantham** (GMO): predicted ~0% real returns end of 2010; actual returns: robust 12%
- Early 2018: Grantham told *Institutional Investor* market "feels like a full-fledged bubble," predicted 50% decline
- Covid-19 pandemic: shortest bear market and recession in US history; Dow crossed 36,000 by November 2021

### Psychology of Investing

- Publisher told Siegel pessimism outsells optimism 3-to-1
- **Joe Granville**: lauded for 1982 bear market call, remained staunchly bearish during tremendous bulls
- **Elaine Garzarelli**: warned of 1987 crash, missed virtually every move subsequently
- Late February 2020 dinner party: physician sold 100% before Covid crash — never re-entered market
- Money manager added to stocks during Covid sell-off (correct assessment); lead investor bailed at bottom, forced fund closure; fast recovery vindicated strategy — irreparable damage done
- Inherent psychology militates against buy-and-hold; "doing nothing" seems irresponsible during crises

## Chapter 2: Data Sources and Total Returns

### Stock Return Data: Three Periods

- **1802-1871**: Based on **Goetzmann-Ibbotson** research (decade-long study, monthly price and dividend data on 600+ securities); 6.9% annual real return
- **1871-1925**: Cowles Foundation capitalization-weighted NYSE index, reported by **Shiller (1989)**
- **1926-present**: CRSP data (all NYSE, Amex, Nasdaq stocks); all returns free from survivorship bias
- **McQuarrie challenge**: Including First/Second Banks of the US lowers 1802-1871 returns to 5.4%
  - These two banks dominated cap-weighted index (little diversification)
  - Reduces 220-year average from 6.9% to 6.4% — does not affect last 187 years

### Total Asset Returns (Nominal, 1802-2021)

- $1 invested in stocks (1802): $54.2 million (8.4% annualized)
- Bonds: $50,206 (5.0%); Bills: $5,677 (4.0%); Gold: $94 (2.1%); CPI: $23 (1.4%)
- Total return assumes all dividends/capital gains reinvested — grows far faster than total stock wealth or GDP

### Real Return Stability Across Periods

- 1802-1870: 6.7%; 1871-1925: 6.6%; 1926-2021: 7.1%
- Stability persisted despite: agricultural → industrial → service/technology economy; gold → paper money; weeks-long communication → instantaneous broadcast
- Stocks represent real assets — long-run returns not adversely affected by inflation
- Short-run: 1982-1999 gave 13.6% real (double historical); 1966-1981 fell behind inflation

### Fixed-Income Decline

- Treasury bill real returns collapsed: 5.1% (early 19th century) → 0.4% (since 1926)
- Long-term bond real returns: 4.8% → 3.7% → 2.6% across three subperiods
- **1946-1981**: real return on Treasury bonds was negative
- Never been even a 20-year period where real stock returns were negative
- Since 1981: declining inflation pushed bond prices up; bond returns virtually matched equities over four decades
- TIPS yields: 3.5% when first issued (1997) → peaked 4.40% (January 2000) → fell below -1% during pandemic

### Equity Risk Premium

- Historical ERP: 3.3% over long-term bonds, 4.3% over short-term bonds
- Forward-looking ERP at end of 2021: ~6% (nearly double historical average) given -1% TIPS yield
- **Equity premium puzzle**: economists wrestling with why ERP so high; hundreds of academic articles

### Worldwide Returns (Dimson, Marsh, Staunton)

- ***Triumph of the Optimists*** (2002): **Elroy Dimson, Paul Marsh, Mike Staunton** — 19 countries since 1900
- Every country exhibited positive real stock returns despite war, hyperinflation, depression
- World real annualized return: 5.3% (1.3 points below US); US was third-highest
- Equity premium substantial in every country; world market premium: 3.2%
- Countries with lower equity returns also had lower fixed-income returns
- Survivorship bias concerns "somewhat overstated"; US results have relevance to all investors

### Real Estate Returns

- Long-term data fraught with imprecise cost/rental estimates
- **Shiller**: owner-occupied housing 1.9% real price increase (far short of total return)
- **Chambers** (Oxford/Cambridge endowment): actual net real returns only 4.5% agricultural, 2.3% residential
- **REITs** (1971-2020): equity REITs returned 11.7% (slightly above S&P 500)
- REITs fell 74.5% in 2007-2009 crisis vs. S&P 500's 57.7% — volatility understated by reported sales data

> "The fact that you do not know how much its ready money quotation fluctuates does not, as is commonly supposed, make an investment a safe one." — John Maynard Keynes

- REITs represent just over 2% of S&P 500 but total housing equity approaches total stock value

### Gold, the Dollar, and Inflation

- US and UK consumer prices essentially same at beginning of WWII as 150 years earlier
- After WWII: price level rose almost continuously, sometimes at double-digit rates (1970s)
- 19th/early 20th centuries: gold standard restricted money supply and inflation
- Great Depression through WWII: world shifted to paper money standard — no legal constraint on money issuance
- Gold soared to $850/oz (January 1980) after 1970s inflation; by end of 2021: surpassed $1,800
- $1 of gold in 1802 worth $98 at end of 2021 — outpaced inflation but far behind stocks
- Paper money standard, properly administered, can prevent bank runs and severe depressions while maintaining low to moderate inflation

### Conclusion: Stocks for the Long Run

- Over 220 years: compound annual real return on diversified stock portfolio nearly 7%
- Value creation springs from: skillful management, stable political system respecting property rights, capacity to provide consumer value
- Superior stock performance explained by free-market economics dominance
- **Warning**: government bonds in paper money economies may fare far worse than stocks in political/economic upheaval
- Even in stable environments, risks in government bonds often outweigh those in stocks for long-term investors

## Chapter 3: Risk, Return, and Portfolio Allocation

> "The man or woman who invests in bonds is speculating in the general level of prices, or the purchasing power of money." — Irving Fisher, 1912

### Risk and Holding Period

- 1-year periods: stocks clearly riskier (best +66.6%, worst -38.6%)
- 5-year: worst stock performance (-11.9%/year) only slightly worse than worst bonds/bills
- 10-year periods: worst stock performance actually better than worst bond/bill performance
- 20-year periods: stocks never fallen below inflation; bonds/bills fell >3%/year below inflation (1961-1981)
  - Treasury bond portfolio fell nearly 50% in real value during that inflationary episode
- 30-year periods: worst stock return (2.6%/year, 1902-1932) not far below average fixed-income performance
- In 220+ years, stocks never delivered negative real return over periods as short as 17 years
- **One of greatest mistakes**: underestimating holding period — relevant period is how long you hold ANY stocks, not a particular stock

### Probability of Outperformance

- 1-2 years: stocks beat bonds/bills ~60% of time (nearly 2 out of 5 years, stocks trail T-bills)
- 10 years: ~75%; 20 years: ~85%; 30 years: 91.6% (99.3% since 1871)
- Short-run underperformance = primary reason difficult for many investors to stay in stocks
- Single 30-year period bonds beat stocks: January 1982-end 2011 (bonds 11.03% vs. stocks 10.98%)
  - Driven by 16%+ bond yields falling to historic lows; bonds earned 7.8% real = double average bond return
  - At current yields (~1.5% nominal, -1% TIPS), impossible to repeat

### Mean Reversion

- If stock prices follow random walk, standard deviation falls by square root of holding period
- **Actual data**: standard deviation falls nearly twice as fast for stocks as for fixed-income assets
- Over 30-year periods, standard deviation of equity returns falls to less than three-fourths that of bonds
- **Poterba and Summers** (1988), **Fama and French** (1988): confirmed mean reversion
- **Samuelson** (1992): "We have only one history of capitalism. Inferences based on a sample of one must never be accorded sure-thing interpretations."
- **Cochrane** (1999): "revolution in understanding — stock returns have a substantial predictable component at long horizons"
- Over 90% of financial economists now agree equity returns display long-term mean reversion
- Bonds show **mean aversion** — once returns deviate, increased chance of further deviation (especially hyperinflation)
- **Pástor and Stambaugh**: challenged this — parameter uncertainty may offset mean reversion; stocks may be riskier long-run than calculated

### Random Walk Theory

- **Paul Samuelson** (1965): "Proof That Properly Anticipated Prices Fluctuate Randomly"
- If stock prices follow random walks, long-horizon investor should NOT hold more stocks than short-horizon investor
- Samuelson argued: no "time diversification" — percentage of portfolio in stocks does not depend on holding period under random walk
- But data demonstrate random walk cannot be maintained for long term — mean reversion dominates
- **Tail risks and black swan events**: all assets subject; stock markets gone to zero (Russia, China mid-20th century); government bonds become worthless with even greater frequency
- Even gold may be shunned for digital currencies; equities (legal claims on productive capital) more likely to retain real value in fiat currency world

### Correlation Between Stock and Bond Returns

- 1926-1965: correlation only slightly positive (+0.05) — bonds were good diversifiers
- 1966-1997: correlation jumped to +0.36 — OPEC supply shocks caused inflation, stocks and bonds fell together
- 1998-2021: correlation switched to negative (-0.28) — government bonds became safe haven during crises
- Over longer periods, risk to bondholders is inflation — whatever short-term comfort from offsetting stock volatility is lost when inflation becomes significant

### Portfolio Allocation by Holding Period

- Minimum risk portfolio shifts dramatically with horizon:
  - 1-year: nearly 100% bonds; 5-year: 25% stocks; 10-year: >1/3 stocks; 20-year: >50% stocks; 30-year: **68% stocks**
- Historical data contradicts random walk — investment horizon must be considered

### The 60/40 Portfolio and Monte Carlo Simulations

- **William Bengen** (1994): "Determining Withdrawal Rates Using Historical Data" — recommended 50-75% equities
- Old rule: "age in bonds"; modern variant: "age minus 20" in bonds
- Monte Carlo simulations (30-year retirement, historical returns 7.12% stocks, 2.58% bonds):
  - 4% withdrawal: minimum shortfall probability 3.6% at 50/50; all-stock: 10% shortfall but legacy more than doubles
  - 5% withdrawal: minimum at 65/35 (13.6% shortfall)
- **Lower return scenario** (stocks 4.5%, bonds -0.5%):
  - 4% withdrawal: minimum shortfall at 80/20 stocks-bonds
  - 5% withdrawal: minimum at 95-100% stocks — optimal shifts strongly toward stocks
- **Javier Estrada** (19 countries, 110 years): 100% stock retirement portfolio "should be seriously considered"
- **Samuelson** (1967 *Newsweek*): "Is 100% in stocks wise? Perhaps yes... probably sleep better at 60-80%"

> "Historical data show that we can be more certain of the purchasing power of a diversified portfolio of common stocks 30 years hence than we can of the buying power of a 30-year US Treasury bond."

## Chapter 4: Global Investing

> "Today let's talk about a growth industry. Because investing worldwide is a growth industry." — John Templeton, 1984

### The Japanese Bubble

- Japan became first star of international investing — manufacturing juggernaut (autos, electronics, Sony Walkman)
- Nikkei: 2,000 (1970) → 39,000 (end of 1989); yen appreciated threefold; dollar investors experienced 6,000% return
- **NTT** had P/E above 300; its market value alone dwarfed most countries' aggregate stock values
- End of 1989: Japan's market value 40% of worldwide equities vs. US at 29% — first time since early 1900s US wasn't largest
- **Leo Melamed** (CME president) traveled to Japan 1987; asked how valuations could be warranted
  - Response: "You don't understand. We've moved to an entirely new way of valuing stocks here in Japan."
  - Melamed knew stocks were doomed: "It is when investors cast aside the lessons of history that the past comes back to haunt them."
- Nikkei fell to 7,000 in 2008 — less than 20% of peak; Japan's share fell to 7% by 2020
- At peak, Japanese stocks sold for well over 100x earnings — more than 3x US market's biggest bubble in 2000

### BRIC Phenomenon

- **James O'Neill** (Goldman Sachs, 2001): coined BRIC (Brazil, Russia, India, China)
- Goldman paper (2003): "Dreaming with BRICs" — predicted BRICs could surpass G6 in 40 years
- BRIC 50 stocks: up 600% (2003-2007), far outpacing S&P 500's 60% rise
- Then stalled — index at end of 2021 below the high reached 14 years earlier
- Mean reversion once again ruled

### World Equity Distribution (2021)

- Total: $70 trillion; US: 61.2%; Developed Europe: 16.1%; Japan: 5.5%
- Emerging markets: $7.8 trillion (11.2%) — China 32%, Taiwan 16%, South Korea and India each 12%
- Frontier markets: $103 billion (0.1%) — Vietnam 30%, Morocco 10%
- US $ returns 1970-2021: World 10.00%, EAFE 9.36%, USA 10.83%, Emerging 10.50% (since 1988)
- Correlations: EAFE-US 65%, Japan-US 39%, Emerging-US 60%

### Three Reasons to Diversify Internationally

1. **Arbitrary definition of "foreign"**: based on headquarters, not where firm produces/sells; S&P removed all non-US firms by 2002; industry factors now match or exceed country factors
2. **US imports $3.5 trillion**: if these goods in demand, companies producing them should be in your portfolio; S&P 500 gets 41% of revenues abroad but US still imports far more than exports
3. **Diversification**: markets not synchronous, often move opposite — this is the essence of diversification

### Currency Hedging

- **Foreign exchange risk** = domestic risk (local stock price) + exchange rate fluctuations
- Hedging has costs depending on interest rate differential between countries
- Long-term: may not matter (purchasing power parity equalizes); equities as real assets compensate for inflation-driven depreciation
- British pound fell from $4.80 to $1.60 over past century — unhedged investors earned higher returns than hedged
- Short-term: hedging reduces dollar risk; EAFE hedged return 5.4% vs. unhedged 5.0% (1988-2021), lower volatility
- Policy-driven depreciation: if central bank lowers currency to stimulate exports, hedged investors benefit

### Sector Allocation and Largest Firms

- Over 50% of US stocks in technology by end of 2021 (including biotech); no other region approaches this
- 2021 top firms: Apple, Microsoft, Alphabet, Amazon, Tesla, Meta, NVIDIA — all tech-related
- 2013: four of top seven were NOT tech (ExxonMobil, GE, J&J, Chevron)
- P/E of trillion-dollar US companies: ~50; Amazon at 69, Tesla at 90; Saudi Aramco: 18x earnings
- Likely many top tech companies will fall from exalted heights

### Conclusion

- Despite geopolitical tensions, globalization is not dead — inexorable trend toward integration
- No country will dominate every market; industry leaders emerge from anywhere
- US equities = only one-half of world market — sticking only to US = risky strategy
- Only fully diversified world portfolio will reap best returns with lowest risk

## Part II: Stock Returns — Measurement and Valuation

## Chapter 5: Stock Indexes

### Dow Jones Industrial Average

- Created by **Charles Dow** (founder of Dow Jones & Co., publisher of *Wall Street Journal*)
- February 16, 1885: daily average of 12 stocks (10 railroads, 2 industrials)
- May 26, 1896: DJIA created with original 12 stocks; expanded to 20 (1916), then 30 (1928)
- Price-weighted (high-priced stocks dominate regardless of company size) — increasingly flawed
  - February 2022: UnitedHealth Group 9% of index ($460) vs. Intel <1% (lowest-priced)
  - Tesla and Amazon excluded due to high share prices
- Past 50 years: Dow (11.26%) beat S&P 500 (11.14%) and Nasdaq (10.85%)
- Inflation-corrected Dow trend: 1.94%/year appreciation + ~4.3% dividends = 6.2%+ real total return
- Breaking above channel (1929, mid-1960s, 2000) → poor returns; below → superior returns

### S&P 500

- Born February 28, 1957; expanded from 90-stock composite (1926)
- Capitalization-weighted — best indication of overall market return; almost universally used for benchmarks
- Originated with **Standard Statistics Company** (1906); cap-weighting began 1918
- **Alfred Cowles** (1939): constructed indexes back to 1871 using Standard's methodology
- Base value = 10 (average 1941-1943); end of 2021: ~$42 trillion, ~90% of all US stock value
- ~20 additions per year (~5% of market value); highest: 1976 (60 additions, first time financials entered)

### Nasdaq

- February 8, 1971: 2,400 OTC stocks on automated system linking 500+ market makers
- Lower costs, less stringent requirements attracted technology firms; Intel and Microsoft chose to stay on Nasdaq even when qualified for NYSE
- Took 10 years to double to 200; another 10 to reach 500; first reached 1,000 in July 1995
- October 1999-March 2000: surged from 2,700 to peak 5,048; fell to 1,150 (October 2002)
- By 1998: Nasdaq capitalization exceeded Tokyo Stock Exchange
- By 1994: Nasdaq share volume exceeded NYSE; five years later: dollar volume surpassed NYSE
- Market peak March 2000: total Nasdaq value nearly $6 trillion — more than half NYSE, more than any other exchange
- Record 16,000+ by end of 2021

### Original Dow 12 Companies (1896)

- Early firms centered on commodities: cotton, sugar, tobacco, lead, leather, rubber
- Only U.S. Leather was liquidated (1952) — shareholders received $1.50 plus one share of Keta Oil & Gas; president **Lowell Birrell** fled to Brazil, looted assets — shares became worthless
- **American Cotton Oil** → Best Food → CPC International → Bestfoods → acquired by **Unilever** (2000) for $20.3B — most highly valued successor (~$100B in 2021)
- **General Electric**: in Dow for 122 years until removal June 2018; largest US market cap for many months in 1990s-2000s; declined ~70% under **Jeffrey Immelt** (2001-2017)
- **Distilling and Cattle Feeding** → multiple transformations → **LyondellBasell Industries** (still manufactures ethanol)
- **American Tobacco** → Fortune Brands → purchased by **Suntory** (2014, Japanese distillery)

### CRSP and Market Concentration

- **Professor James Lorie** (Chicago): created CRSP at request of Merrill Lynch (1959) — first machine-readable stock price file from 1926
- End of 2021: 4,317 stocks worth over $50 trillion; top 500 firms = 84.8% of market value
- Russell 1000 = 93.6%; Russell 2000 = 6.3%; remaining 1,317 stocks = 0.1%

### Why the Average Stock Is a Loser

- **Bessembinder** (CRSP data): only 50.8% have positive lifetime returns; median 90-year return just 9.5% vs. T-bills' 1,928%
- Only 27.5% beat T-bills; only 4% beat the value-weighted market
- JP Morgan confirmed: Russell 3000 median stock returned between zero and -10% (1980-2020)
- Mathematical reality: even with zero expected return, 75% of random stock picks lose due to compounding asymmetry
- Broad diversification is the only guaranteed way to approximate superior long-term returns

## Chapter 6: The S&P 500 Index

> "Nearly 94% of all domestic active stock fund managers had underperformed their respective S&P benchmarks in the past 20 years." — S&P Global, mid-2021

### Sector Rotation

- **Materials + energy**: nearly 50% (1957) → only 5.3% (2021)
- **Financial + health care + technology**: 6% (1957) → over 50% (2021)
- Rising/falling market shares don't necessarily correlate with returns — valuation more important than growth
- Energy shrank >85% but returns trailed S&P by <1%; financials and telecoms grew rapidly but actually lagged energy

### Oil Sector Surprise

- All 9 oil companies among 20 largest original S&P 500 firms finished in top 10 performers over 64 years
- Returns on all but one beat S&P 500 despite energy sector shrinking 85%+
- Gulf Oil (11.90%), Socony Mobil (11.66%), Standard Oil CA (11.52%), Royal Dutch (11.46%), Standard Oil NJ (11.24%)

### Original 500 vs. New Additions

- **Jeremy Schwartz** (2005) study: holding original 500 companies (1957) outperformed actual S&P 500 by ~1 percentage point per year through 2006
- New firms' earnings grew faster, but price paid was often too high
- Only consumer discretionary sector added firms that outperformed originals
- Mid-2010s shift: tech giants ended original companies' outperformance

### Top Performers

- **#1: Philip Morris/Altria** — 18.02% annualized (1957-2021); $1,000 → over $45 million (~600x the S&P 500)
- Introduced **Marlboro Man** (1955); acquired 10 other original S&P 500 firms (General Foods, Nabisco, etc.)
- **Melville Shoe** → bought Consumer Value Stores (1969) → became **CVS** — woeful shoe manufacturer became largest US retail pharmacy through serendipitous purchase

### "Top Dogs" (Highest Market Cap)

- AT&T: 456 months #1 (251 consecutive, 1929-1950); IBM: 236; GE: 117; Standard Oil: 116; Apple: 103
- Single-month top dogs: DuPont, Philip Morris, Wal-Mart, Amazon (January 2019)
- Average 1-year, 10-year, 20-year returns of top dogs beat S&P 500; outperformance fades at 30 years
- IBM: cautionary tale — hugely outperformed for one year after reaching top, then significantly underperformed
- Philip Morris: only one month as top dog but only true long-term winner
- "No tree grows to the sky and no bull market lasts forever"

## Chapter 7: Sources of Shareholder Value

> "Dividends not only dwarf inflation, growth, and changing valuation levels individually, but they also dwarf the combined importance of inflation, growth, and changing valuation levels." — Robert Arnott, 2003

### Discounted Cash Flows and Dividends

- Stock price = present value of future **dividends**, NOT future earnings
- **John Burr Williams** (*Theory of Investment Value*, 1938): "Earnings are only a means to an end, and the means should not be mistaken for the end."
- **Gordon Model**: P = d/(r-g); dividend policy does NOT impact price if firm earns same return on retained earnings
- **Berkshire Hathaway**: pays no dividends but maintains value because owners could sell assets or pay dividends

### Buyback Controversy

- Buybacks: tax-efficient alternative to dividends; don't change current price but increase per-share growth
- Tax advantage: price increases not taxed until sold; dividends taxed immediately
- Critics wrongly claim buybacks substitute for capital investment — evidence suggests market penalizes high investment
- Restricting buybacks would increase cash dividends, not capex
- Secondary driver: management stock options based on share price alone (not total return)

### Historical Trends

- Real return 1871-2021: 7.1% = 4.3% dividend yield + 2.5% capital gains + 0.3% P/E expansion
- Pre-WWII: 66.8% payout ratio, 5.31% dividend yield, 0.67% earnings growth
- Post-WWII: 49% payout, 3.28% yield, 3.43% earnings growth
- Before WWII: retained earnings too small → firms issued shares (diluting per-share growth)
- After WWII: firms retained more → funded growth without dilution → per-share growth accelerated

### Earnings Concepts

- **GAAP** (FASB rules): increasingly volatile from mark-to-market rules; write-down assets but cannot write back up
- **Operating earnings** (non-GAAP): exclude one-time events; always higher than GAAP
- **Buffett** criticized ASU 2016 rules: "wild and capricious swings" in Berkshire earnings — "For analytical purposes, Berkshire Hathaway's bottom line will be worthless"
- 2008-2009: GAAP earnings declined far more than Great Depression (when GDP fell 5x as much)
- Post-1990: GAAP profits fallen by more than twice NIPA drop (reversed pre-1990 pattern)
- ~65% of quarterly earnings reports beat consensus; recently 70-75% — firms tilt guidance pessimistic

## Chapter 8: Interest Rates and Stock Prices

> "It all comes down to interest rates. As an investor, all you're doing is putting up a lump-sum payment for a future cash flow." — Ray Dalio

### Decline in Real Interest Rates

- US, Europe, UK, Canada, Australia all dropped from 2-4.5% at turn of century to negative territory by 2021
- Popular explanation ("easy money") is largely mistaken — central banks set short-term rates, but long-term real rates driven by real nonmonetary forces

### Determinants of Real Interest Rates

- **Austrian-Swedish-American theory** (Böhm-Bawerk, Wicksell, Fisher): real rates driven by (1) economic growth, (2) time preference, (3) risk
- All three growth components trending down: population, labor force share, productivity
- World fertility decline surprised demographers; outside Africa, fertility at or below 2.1 replacement
- US 2020-2021 population increase: 393,000 (lowest in 100+ years)
- India: 2.0 overall, 1.6 in cities (2021 survey); China below replacement despite easing one-child policy
- Old-age dependency ratios: US 8→15→22% (1950/2020/2050); Japan 5→29→37%; South Korea 3→15→38% (highest forecast)
- US productivity: 2.2% (2000-2010) → 0.9% (2010-2020) despite tremendous tech gains
- Per capita GDP growth: US 2.21% (1970-2000) → 1.04% (2010-2020); Eurozone 2.32% → 0.33%

### Bonds as Hedge Assets

- **Most powerful yet most ignored factor** driving down real rates: increasingly negative stock-bond correlation
- 1960s-1970s: bonds had little hedge value (uncorrelated with stocks)
- 1980s-1990s: OPEC supply shocks caused inflation → stocks and bonds fell together (poor hedges)
- Post-2000: emerging market crises, GFC, Covid stoked deflation fears → bonds became excellent hedges
- 38% gain in 30-year Treasury during 2008 crisis while S&P fell 37%
- **John Campbell**: change in correlation could account for 3-percentage-point decline in long rates
- **Richard Clarida** (Fed vice chair): confirmed hedging characteristic as significant source of yield drop

### Central Banks and the "Natural Rate"

- **Wicksell** coined "natural rate": determined by productivity, time, risk — outside central bank control
- If central bank keeps rate below natural rate → inflation erupts; above → deflation
- Real long-term rates primarily determined by real economic forces, not monetary policy

### Interest Rates and Stock Prices: A Complicated Relationship

- If rate decline from lower growth → future cash flows reduced → ambiguous stock impact
- If from increased risk aversion → decline in risk-free rate must offset higher risk premium
- If from improved bond hedging → bond demand rises but may not increase stock demand
- Short-run Fed easing generally positive for stocks if not signaling excessive weakness

## Chapter 9: Inflation and Stock Prices

> "By a process of inflation, government can confiscate, secretly and unobserved, the wealth of their citizens." — John Maynard Keynes

- 1950: Truman predicted $12,000 family income by 2000; actual $41,349, but bought less than $6,000 in 1950 dollars — real incomes only doubled despite 12x nominal increase
- Since 1955: never a single year US CPI declined; money supply control shifted from gold to government
- **M2** has closest link to inflation (Milton Friedman); M2 increased 20%+ in 2020 (greatest in 150 years); inflation surged to 7%

### Stocks as Inflation Hedge

- Long run: stocks kept pace — post-WWII (most inflationary period) real returns exceeded prior 150 years
- Short run: stocks fail as inflation hedge because central bank raises **real** rates in response
- 30-year holding: stock returns stable (~6-7%) across all inflation ranges; bonds/bills decline as inflation rises
- **Gordon formula**: inflation raises both discount rate and dividend growth equally — effects cancel
- Real rates, not nominal, are proper discount rate for real assets

### Supply-Side (Nonneutral) Inflation

- 1970s OPEC: firms couldn't raise output prices as fast as soaring energy costs → earnings depressed
- Real stock prices (Dow) fell 65% from January 1966 high; half of Americans feared 1930s-style depression
- Chronic inflation in developing countries = government taking too large a role → lower growth, profits, prices

### Tax Distortions

- Capital gains taxed on nominal gains — no inflation adjustment; 3% inflation/5-year hold costs 60 basis points/year
- Corporate: depreciation understated (negative), inventory profits overstated (negative), interest expenses overstated (positive — reduces effective tax rate)
- Balance depends on leverage; overall, tax code not adequately indexed for inflation

## Chapter 10: Yardsticks to Value the Stock Market

### The Great Yield Reversal (1958)

- Until 1958, dividend yield always exceeded bond yields; reversal seen as "evil omen" (*BusinessWeek*)
- 1929, 1891, 1907 crashes all followed dividend yields approaching bond yields
- After 1958 reversal, stocks returned 30%+ in next 12 months; investor who exited waited 50 years to re-enter
- Over that half-century, real stock returns averaged over 6% per year
- Chronic inflation changed investor psychology: stocks viewed as real asset claims; bonds were not
- Dividend yield alone became poor indicator due to rise of buybacks (shareholder yield remarkably stable since 1960)

### P/E Ratio

- Median P/E (150 years): ~15; median earnings yield: 6.7% (close to long-run real stock return)
- GAAP-based P/E shows dramatic spikes in recessions due to FASB mark-to-market rules
- **Aggregation bias**: large losses by one firm can distort aggregate P/E dramatically (example: 95% of index at P/E 20, aggregate shows P/E 210)

### CAPE Ratio

- **Campbell and Shiller** (1998): 10-year average real earnings; predicted ~1/3 of future 10-year return variation
- Presented to Fed Board December 3, 1996 — Greenspan's "irrational exuberance" speech one week later
- Hit 43 at 2000 peak (correctly forecast poor returns); predicted poor returns after 1929 too
- **Since 2008**: almost always above average, forecasting below-average returns while actual returns were above average
- 1981-2021: all but four months, actual 10-year returns exceeded CAPE forecasts
- Reasons for bearish bias: declining dividend yield, lower transaction costs, FASB rule changes
- **Shiller's ECY modification** (2020): subtracts real interest rate from CAPE earnings yield — far less bearish
- **Shiller** told Zweig (February 2022): "We don't know the right CAPE ratio. We're constantly moving into a new era."

### Fed Model

- Three Fed researchers (1997): earnings yield vs. 30-year bond rate showed remarkable correspondence
- Most overvalued August 1987 (before crash); most undervalued early 1980s (before great bull)
- Major flaw: compares nominal variables; stocks are real assets — should compare to real rates
- Close relation only 1960-2000; very weak outside that period
- Useful at extremes: 2000 peak showed rare negative risk premium (earnings yield 3% vs. TIPS yield 4%+)

### Buffett Indicator (Market Cap/GDP) and Tobin's Q

- Rose to all-time high 2021; flawed: doesn't account for overseas revenues (~50% of profits) or shift from private to public
- **Tobin's Q**: market value / inflation-corrected book value; should equal 1.0 if properly valued
- **Smithers and Wright** (2000, *Valuing Wall Street*): Q best measure — suffered same failures as CAPE
- Book value fails for tech: Microsoft book $22 vs. market $300; Apple book $4.40 vs. $160

### Profit Margins and Transaction Costs

- Risen to 50-year highs; driven by low rates, lower corporate taxes, tech margins, foreign sales
- Excluding technology: margins reduced from 13.1% to 10.6%
- **Charles Jones** (Columbia): one-way trading costs fell from over 1% (pre-1975) to under 0.18% (2002)
- If required real return drops from 7% to 5%, equilibrium P/E rises from ~14 to 20
- **Equity premium puzzle** (Mehra and Prescott, 1985): standard models predict 1% premium vs. actual 3-4%
- **Bosland** (Brown, 1936): widespread knowledge of stock profitability causes competitive bidding, raising prices and diminishing future gains

> "I doubt that the devil himself knows what is the equilibrium P/E ratio on stocks." — Paul Samuelson

## Chapter 11: Individual Stocks vs. the Market

### IBM vs. Standard Oil Paradox

- $1,000 in 1950: IBM → $15M; Standard Oil → $33M — oil company won despite inferior growth
- IBM's average P/E ~2x Standard Oil's; oil company's dividend yield ~3 points higher
- Lower price + higher dividends = 11.87x share accumulation for Standard Oil vs. 3.63x for IBM

### GDP Growth Paradox

- **Negative correlation** between GDP growth and stock returns (21 countries, 1900-2020)
- China 1992-2020: 9.3% GDP growth but only 2.7% stock return (lowest among 18 emerging markets)
- **Denmark**: 13.5% annual return (highest developed) vs. second-lowest GDP growth
- Growth requires capital → share dilution; productivity gains accrue to workers, not equity holders
- Return to invested capital remained remarkably constant despite rising real wages since Industrial Revolution

### GOAT: Philip Morris

- 16.02% compound annual return 1926-2021; $1,000 → $1.33 billion (6 percentage points above market)
- Second: **Kansas City Southern** (railroad, 14.50%); then Eaton, Coca-Cola, PepsiCo, General Dynamics
- Lawsuits cost ~$100 billion, threatened bankruptcy → depressed price → reinvested dividends at low prices = "pile of gold"
- **Buffett**: "The best thing that happens to us is when a great company gets into temporary trouble."
- **Tobacco industry overall** (Dimson/Marsh/Staunton): 14.6% annually 1900-2014 — over 160x the market
- **Thatcher Glass** (milk bottles): business sank → acquired chain → eventually bought by Philip Morris → 100 shares → $40M
- **Kansas City Southern**: stayed exclusively in rail; great management but investors never put high valuations on rail → low prices kept returns near top

> "Don't fall in love with a company, fall in love with its price."

### Amazon.com

- 1999: roomful of finance experts predicted Amazon most likely to fail among dot-coms — "snickers of agreement"
- *Barron's* "Amazon.bomb" cover; stock fell from $59 to $5.51 (90%+); took nearly 10 more years to surpass 1999 high

## Chapter 12: Is Value Investing Dead?

### Origins

- **Benjamin Graham** (British-born New Yorker) + **David Dodd** (Columbia): *Security Analysis* (1934)
- Graham's *Intelligent Investor* (1949): sold millions, translated into dozens of languages
- **Mr. Market**: emotional, manic-depressive personality; buy below intrinsic value, sell above
- Best disciple: **Warren Buffett** (studied under Graham at Columbia); also **Walter Schloss, William Ruane, Tweedy Browne**
- Buffett's 1984 article: "The Superinvestors of Graham-and-Doddsville"
- Graham warned P/E above 16 (later 20) would likely lose money long-run
- **Basu** (late 1970s, building on **Nicholson** 1960): confirmed low P/E stocks have higher risk-adjusted returns

### Value's Historical Returns (1951-2021)

- Highest earnings yield quintile: 15.64%/year vs. lowest: 9.75% (market: 11.40%)
- 2007-2021: highest quintile 9.75%, lowest 11.68% — value underperformed
- Graham also emphasized dividend yield; **Ramaswamy and Litzenberger** (1978) confirmed correlation
- **O'Shaughnessy** (1951-1994): 50 highest-dividend-yield large caps beat market by 1.7 points/year

### Dividends and Value

- Graham believed firms frequently didn't use internal funds efficiently
- Some of his most profitable investments involved taking over firms and distributing unproductive surpluses to shareholders
- Value investors believe steady commitment to pay cash dividends incentivizes productive investment
- **O'Shaughnessy** (1951-1994): 50 highest-dividend-yield large caps beat market by 1.7 points/year

### Book Value Deterioration

- **Cate Elsten and Nick Hill**: 1975: 17% of S&P 500 value was intangible; 1985: 32%; 1995: two-thirds; 2015: 84%
- When researchers include intangible assets in book calculations, recent value performance improves
- Buybacks further reduce book value — firm buying stock at market price decreases book value
- Apple book value just dollars per share despite market value many times higher

### Historical Episodes of Value Underperformance

- **"Nifty Fifty"** (early 1970s): institutional buying pushed average P/E over 40 by December 1972 (2x S&P 500); included Philip Morris, Pfizer, Coca-Cola but also losers like Polaroid, Sears
- **Dot-com bubble**: technology firms with P/E ratios far higher than Nifty Fifty; Nasdaq fell nearly 80%, resurrecting value strategy
- Value continued outperforming market until peak in 2006

### Post-2006 Value Downturn

- Using Fama-French book value criteria: 55% drawdown (deepest on record); large-cap: 61%
- 13.5-year duration: unprecedented (previous record: 2.5 years after dot-com)
- **Not arbitraged away** — P/E gap between growth and value widened, not narrowed
- Perfect storm: tech proved undervalued after 2000 collapse; financials devastated 2008; oil collapsed (fracking + ESG); Covid boosted tech
- **ESG investing** (Pástor, Stambaugh, Taylor): avoidance of oil + preference for tech contributed to value underperformance
- Tech: from 19% to 40% of S&P 500; tech earnings grew 12.7%/year (5+ points above rest); P/E nearly doubled
- FAANG aggregate value: ~$1 trillion (2013) → ~$10 trillion (2021); over one-quarter of S&P 500
- Current tech valuations lower than dot-com bubble; not much higher than 1960s; and interest rates were higher in both periods

### Discount Rate Explanation

- Value stocks = shorter duration; growth stocks benefit more from falling interest rates
- Historical support weak — greatest growth burst (1999-2000) came during high real rates

## Chapter 13: Market Efficiency or Noisy Markets?

### Efficient Market Hypothesis

- **EMH** (coined **Harry Roberts** 1967, popularized **Eugene Fama**): all factors already incorporated into prices
- **CAPM** (Sharpe, Lintner; Markowitz): beta = only relevant risk; cap-weighted index = optimal for all investors
- **Beta**: correlation of stock's return with overall market; diversifiable risk doesn't warrant higher return
- Early studies (**Friend, Treynor, Jensen**, 1960s): mutual funds systematically failed to beat market

### Challenges to EMH

- **Fama and French** (1992): size and book-to-market more important than beta — evidence "compelling"
- Called CAPM "not a useful approximation"; suggested investigating "alternative asset pricing models" or even "irrational asset pricing stories"
- **Grossman and Stiglitz**: prices must deviate from value to motivate costly research — perfectly efficient markets impossible
- **Noise traders**: trade on imperfect or unrelated information, drive prices from fundamentals
- **Milton Friedman** (1950s): claimed deviations from fundamentals would not be large — speculators buying too high would suffer losses and become minor factor
- **Bradford de Long** and others (late 1980s): showed noise traders can cause significant divergence even when all future cash flows known; may even earn higher returns than fundamental investors
- Author coined **noisy market hypothesis** (2006): not easy to beat indexes, but opportunities exist
- **Fischer Black** (1986 AFA presidential address, surprising for strong EMH supporter): efficient = price within factor of 2 of value; "almost all markets are efficient almost all the time. 'Almost all' means at least 90%."

> "Markets can stay irrational longer than investors can remain solvent" — modern paraphrase of Keynes

- Dot-com shorts forced to cover at great losses; "meme" stocks (Reddit's WallStreetBets) forced hedge funds to reverse positions
- **Shleifer and Vishny**: career risks loom large for institutional managers with short-term performance pressure

### Short Selling Restrictions

- One CAPM assumption: as easy to short as go long — violated in practice
- Short sellers exposed to potentially unlimited losses if prices rise; long investors can lose at most 100%
- **Stambaugh, Yu, Yuan**: nearly 70% of long-short strategy returns from short leg
- Nearly 80% of profits occurred during high market sentiment periods (**Baker-Wurgler** index)
- When prices fall below intrinsic value, fundamental investors have easier time maintaining position
- But if stock falls 50% (still within Black's efficient range), investor who borrowed 50% on margin faces forced liquidation
- Well-known fund manager confided: even if strategy vindicated long-run, clients who abandon during tough times never come back

### The Market Portfolio Problem

- **Roll critique**: which assets constitute "the market"? Only US stocks, all global stocks, bonds, real estate, human capital?
- If stock returns correlate with labor income, those stocks should be underweighted
- Example: holding employer's stock in pension creates double risk if firm fails
- **Kogan, Papanikolaou, Stoffman**: investors buy growth stocks to hedge "creative destruction" that may hurt employment

### Intertemporal Risks

- **Merton's ICAPM** (1970s): single-period volatility may overstate true long-term risk
- **Campbell** ("Good Beta, Bad Beta"): separates market fluctuations into interest rate changes ("good beta") and business cycle beta ("bad beta")
- Business cycle beta explains returns better than static CAPM
- **Prospect theory** (Kahneman, Tversky): greater pain from losses than pleasure from gains — leads to disposing winners too early, holding losers too long

## Chapter 14: The "Factor Zoo"

> "The rate of factor production in academic research is out of control." — Campbell Harvey and Yan Liu, 2019

### Fama-French Models

- Three-factor (1993): market, size, valuation; later five-factor: added profitability and investment
- **Cochrane** (2011 AFA presidential address): called explosion of factors a "zoo"
- Most factors faded or disappeared 2006-2021

### Size Factor

- **Rolf Banz** (1981, CRSP data): smallest 20% exceeded largest 20% by ~4 points/year (1926-1980)
- **1975-1983**: small stocks averaged 35.3% (cumulative 1,400%) vs. 15.7% large; excluding this, returns virtually match
- Driven by ERISA (1974) and Nifty Fifty collapse
- January effect (nearly all excess small returns in January) has disappeared
- Size factor has added nothing for almost 40 years; debate continues whether true factor exists

### Size and Value Combined

- Small value stocks (1926-2021): 16.24%/year; small growth: only 2.83%
- Among largest 20%, growth edged out value by <1 point/year (2006-2021: growth won by 6 points)
- Small value outperformance persists internationally, strongest in emerging markets
- Why: fewer analysts follow small stocks → prices deviate longer; small growth attracts lottery-ticket seekers

### Momentum

- **De Bondt and Thaler** (1985): long-run reversals — 36 months after formation, losers earned 25% more than winners
- **Jegadeesh** (1990): short-run momentum — stocks outperforming prior 12 months continued next 12 months
- By far strongest factor over entire period; significant across global markets
- **Fama**: "Of all potential embarrassments to market efficiency, momentum is the primary one"
- **Graham**: "Never buy a stock because it has gone up"
- **Buffett**: "The dumbest reason in the world to buy a stock is because it's going up"
- Behavioral explanation: conservative bias (slow initial reaction) then extrapolation bias (overreaction)
- Self-reinforcing: correct prediction → reinforced conviction → other investors shift bullishly
- Runs in stock prices not inconsistent with efficient market — requires **martingale** (allows short-term correlation), but correlated movements followed by sharp unpredictable reversals
- "Up a staircase, down the elevator" — traders believe they can jump off before going over cliff
- Downsides: high turnover, sharp drawdowns near bear market bottoms, faded since 2006
- **Cliff Asness** (AQR): maintains downsides don't nullify significant long-term returns
- **Japan exception**: momentum returns actually negative; 1989-1993 bust (80% decline) may have dissuaded trend-following

### Investment and Share Issuance

- Firms expanding capital most → lower returns (Titman; Siegel's "Capital Pigs" chapter)
- **Buffett** (1985): refused textile investment — "each proposal looked like immediate winner" but competitors would neutralize gains
- **Jim Collins** (*Good to Great*): asked CEOs if they have "stop doing" list — regarded equally important as "to do" list
- Share issuance (Jay Ritter, early 1990s) followed by lower returns; buybacks → superior returns

### Profitability and Quality

- **Novy-Marx** (2013): profitability strongly predicts future growth; works especially well with value factor
- **Accruals** (Sloan): higher-than-normal accruals → lower forward returns
- **Stambaugh and Yuan**: combined 11 factors into single measure outperforming Fama-French five-factor
- No widely accepted EMH-consistent explanation for quality factors

### Low Volatility

- Theory: restrictions on short selling make it difficult to short overvalued low-vol stocks → they trade above intrinsic value → lower long-term returns
- High beta stocks have lower returns than CAPM predicts — linked to Fischer Black's early CAPM observations
- Risk-adjusted return fallen below zero (1963-2021) due to 2020-2021 downdraft

### Liquidity

- **Amihud and Mendelson** (1986): less liquid stocks (high bid-asked spreads) outperform
- **Ibbotson** confirmed using stock turnover ratio: lowest quartile 14.5%/year — double highest turnover (1972-2010)
- US Treasury "on-the-run" bonds command higher prices than identical maturity bonds — investors value flexibility
- Large mutual funds can't purchase large quantities of illiquid stocks without driving up prices
- **Pástor and Stambaugh**: marketwide liquidity risk — stocks easily bought/sold during crises (2008 Lehman, 1998 LTCM, Covid) preferred by investors, commanding premium
- Marketwide liquidity risk accounts for half momentum profits over 34 years
- Implications: investors who don't value crisis liquidity should tilt toward more sensitive stocks

### International Factor Investing

- Every region except Japan: average of five factors larger than US
- Momentum strongest in every region except Japan (where negative)
- Quality/profitability strong in every region
- Factor returns greater than diversification benefits from international investing

### Conclusion

> In a noisy market, a tilt toward stocks with lower valuations, especially based on earnings quality, valuation, and share buybacks, seems warranted. But patience must be exercised. Strategies can run dry for years, or even decades, before they reassert themselves, if they ever do.
