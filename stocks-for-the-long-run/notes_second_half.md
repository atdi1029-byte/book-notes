## Part IV: Styles, Trends, and the Calendar

## Chapter 15: ESG Investing

### The Friedman Doctrine Debate
- **Milton Friedman (September 1970)**: *NYT* essay arguing social responsibility of business is to increase profits — "Friedman doctrine" served as motif for generations of corporate leaders
- *NYT* claimed essay "changed the course of capitalism"
- **2019 Business Roundtable**: Fundamental commitment to all stakeholders, not just shareholders — compensate employees fairly, foster diversity, protect environment
- **Marc Benioff (Salesforce CEO)**: Friedman "brainwashed a generation of CEOs"

### ESG Investing Defined
- **ESG**: Environmental, Social, Governance — one of fastest growing investment styles
- US SIF Foundation: Professional investors considering ESG reached **$17 trillion** (2020), 42% increase since 2018; funds committed increased sevenfold 2012-2020
- Rating agencies: **MSCI**, **FTSE Russell**, **Sustainalytics** — evaluate firms on hundreds of criteria
- ESG ratings now rival traditional stock/bond ratings (S&P, Moody's)

### How ESG Can Enhance Value
- **Direct profit enhancement**: Better working conditions boost morale/productivity; diversity widens talent pool; good governance (independent board, compensation committee) raises earnings multiples
- **Customer premium**: Consumers pay more for "fair trade" products; electric vehicles demanded despite combustion engine cost advantages
- **Valuation enhancement** even if profits decrease — two mechanisms:
  - **(1) Psychic pleasure** from owning ESG stocks — investors willing to accept diminished monetary return for satisfaction
  - **(2) Risk hedging** against climate deterioration — green stocks as insurance policy
- **Bank of America**: S&P 500 firms with below-median carbon emissions sell at **5.1x book** vs. 4.2x for above-median
- Three largest carbon-emitting sectors (utilities, energy, industrials): firms with carbon-neutral targets sport **50% higher P/E ratios**
- **German government bonds**: Two identical bonds (one funding ESG projects); ESG bond sells at higher price (lower yield)

### Future Returns for ESG Stocks
- ESG premium creates **headwind** for investors: future return requires unexpected ESG demand/profitability changes to overcome higher valuations
- Current premium already includes expected future ESG growth — forecast of growing popularity insufficient for outperformance
- **Portfolio selection**: No psychic benefit → underweight ESG; average psychic benefit → hold at market weight; above-average preferences → overweight
- **Past performance misleading**: ESG improvers outperformed (21.9% annual vs. 16.5% for worst decliners), but detecting firms implementing ESG *before* market → likely outperform

### The "Sin Stock" Paradox
- Best long-term US stock = **Philip Morris** (wouldn't score high on ESG)
- **Dimson, Marsh, Staunton**: Tobacco outperformed all other industries 1900-present; UK: alcoholic beverages beat all sectors
- **Virtuous stocks short-run winners; sinner stocks historically took first place**

### ESG as Climate Risk Hedge
- Green stocks as insurance policy against bad climate outcomes — higher prices, lower returns than nonhedge assets
- **Overweight condition**: environmental quality more important to investor than average, or investor perceives climate threat greater than market perceives
- ESG advocates' comfort: higher ESG price reduces cost of capital, allows resource flow from weak-ESG to strong-ESG firms

> The Friedman doctrine, which emphasized the duty of management to their owners, may not, after all, be inconsistent with the pursuit of ESG goals. By doing "good," corporate executives might, indeed, do very "well" for their shareholders.

## Chapter 16: Technical Analysis and Investing with the Trend

### The Debate
- **Burton Malkiel** in *A Random Walk Down Wall Street* (1990): "The central proposition of charting is absolutely false"
- Academic opinion shifting — **200-day moving averages** and **short-term price momentum** can improve risk-return profiles
- **Charles Dow** created Dow Jones Industrial Average, founded *WSJ*; **Dow Theory**: stock prices move like ocean waves — primary wave (overall trend) with secondary waves superimposed
- **William Hamilton** extended Dow's approach in *Stock Market Barometer* (1922); **Robert Rhea** formalized in *Dow Theory* (1932)
- **Martin J. Pring**: Dow Theory followers 1897-1990 would have turned $100 into $116,000 vs. $6,000 buy-and-hold (excluding dividends)
- Problem: buy-sell signals are **purely subjective** — cannot be determined by precise numerical rules

### The Randomness of Stock Prices
- **Frederick MacCauley** (1925): striking similarity between stock market fluctuations and chance curve from throwing dice
- **Harry Roberts** (University of Chicago): simulated random price changes looked identical to real stock charts — patterns had no predictive content
- **Paul Samuelson** (1965): efficient market hypothesis — prices change only on new, unanticipated information; resulting movement is random
- **Figure 16.1**: Eight charts (four real, four random); top Wall Street brokers showed **no ability** to distinguish actual from computer-generated

### Trending Markets and Moving Averages
- Sayings: **"Make the trend your friend"** and **"Trust the thrust"**
- **Martin Zweig**: "I can't overemphasize the importance of staying with the trend"
- Channel bounds: lower = **support level**; upper = **resistance level**
- When market breaks channel bounds, large price changes often follow — self-fulfilling prophecy as traders reinforce channel importance
- **200-day moving average**: most popular technical tool; when prices penetrate, signals powerful underlying reversal
- **William Gordon** (1897-1967): buying when Dow broke above moving average produced **seven times the return** as buying when below
- **Robert Colby and Thomas Meyers**: best time period for moving average of weekly data is **45 weeks** (slightly longer than 200-day)

### Testing the 200-Day Moving Average (1885-2020)
- Buy when Dow closes **1%+ above** 200-day average; sell when **1%+ below**; proceeds to T-bills
- 1% band controls whipsawing — smaller band causes dramatically lower returns
- **1886-2020**: Timing strategy 9.66% return, 16.4% risk, 63.6% in market, 402 switches; buy-and-hold: 9.62%, 20.9% risk — barely edged out
- **Excluding 1929-1932 crash**: timing strategy 92 basis points **behind** buy-and-hold, but 4+ percentage points lower standard deviation
- **2001-2020**: buy-and-hold beats timing by **3 percentage points/year** due to whipsawing in trendless markets (20 switches in 2010-2012 alone clipped ~20 percentage points)

### Avoiding Bear Markets
- Major advantage: investor experiences **lower volatility**, in market less than two-thirds of time
- Greatest triumph: **1920s-1930s** — would have bought June 27, 1924 (Dow 95.33), ridden to 381.17 (Sept 3, 1929), exited **October 19, 1929** at 323.87 (ten days before Great Crash); reentered August 6, 1932 (Dow 66.56, just 25 points above absolute low)
- **October 1987**: sold out at close Friday October 16 — avoided Black Monday's 23% crash
- **2007-2009**: exited January 2, 2008 (Dow ~8% below peak); didn't reenter until July 15, 2009 (Dow ~40% lower)
- **Covid-19**: exited February 26, 2020 while Dow continued falling 30%+
- Average drawdown during bear markets using strategy: **13.25%** vs. **32.73%** for buy-and-hold
- **Downside**: modal annual return for timing is 0 to -5% (many small losses); worst year 2000 — 16 switches, loss exceeding 33%
- Distribution like buy-and-hold investor who purchased **index puts** to cushion declines — insurance cost drains returns when no decline

> "A moment's thought will show that there can be no such thing as a scientific prediction of economic events under human control." — Benjamin Graham

- Final word: requires **full-time attention** — October 16, 1987 Dow fell below 200-day at very end of Friday trading; miss that afternoon and you're swept into Black Monday's 22% nightmare

## Chapter 17: Calendar Anomalies

> "October. This is one of the peculiarly dangerous months to speculate in stocks. The others are July, January, September, April, November, May, March, June, December, August, and February." — Mark Twain

### The January Effect
- Discovered early 1980s by **Donald Keim** (University of Chicago graduate student); first significant finding flying in face of efficient market hypothesis
- **1925-2021**: Average January return — S&P 500: 1.29%, small stocks: **4.93%** (3.64 percentage-point gap)
- Holding small stocks in January + S&P 500 rest of year beat buy-and-hold S&P 500 by almost **4 percentage points/year**
- January effect explains virtually all of small stocks' superior total returns over 96-year period
- During **Great Depression**: small stocks posted January returns of +13%, +21%, +10% consecutively — investors could have increased wealth 50% during worst bear market
- Found globally — Japan's excess small stock January returns: **7.2%/year** (more than US)
- **Since 1995: January effect has disappeared** — returns from all strategies nearly the same

### Tax-Loss Selling Hypothesis
- Individual investors sell losing small stocks in December for tax benefits; stocks bounce back in January
- Evidence supports: stocks fallen throughout year fall even more in December, rise dramatically in January
- Problem: January effect existed in countries **without** capital gains tax (Japan before 1989, Canada pre-1972)
- Publicity about January effect may have motivated traders to exploit it, causing disappearance

### January as Predictor
- "As the market goes in January, so goes the rest of the year"
- When January positive, rest of year positive more than one-third of time
- **Weakening**: since 1995, 12 negative Januaries, only 3 had negative remainder months

### The September Effect
- By far worst month globally — only month with negative return including reinvested dividends
- $1 invested only in September since 1885 → **23 cents** today; $1 invested every month except September → **$6,167**
- 21 of 22 countries had negative September returns, 1970-2021
- September effect **still operative** unlike the disappeared January effect
- Possible explanation: approach of winter, shortening daylight — but also poor in Australia/New Zealand (spring there)
- Since 1994: **August** (not September) has become worst month as traders try to front-run

### Other Calendar Patterns
- **April** best month; virtually all market gains realized **October through April** — "Sell in May and go away" has empirical justification
- Returns between Christmas and New Year's: nearly **10 times** average daily return
- First half of month returns almost **three times** second half; first day of month especially positive (401k inflows)
- **Post-1995 reversals**: Friday went from best to worst day (traders hedge over weekend); Monday went from worst to second best; last trading day of year switched from positive to decisively negative (sell-on-close orders at year-end)
- **Diurnal pattern**: sinking in morning (especially Monday), firms midday, rising in **last half hour**

## Chapter 18: Money and Monetary Policy

### The Banking Crisis and Gold Standard
- Falling asset prices → bank runs → Fed's **institutional ineptitude** failed to provide reserves despite having explicit power
- **Great Britain** abandoned gold standard September 1931; **United States** suspended April 1933
- US stocks soared **9% day** government left gold standard, almost 6% next day — investors believed government could now provide needed liquidity
- **Bretton Woods** post-WWII: reinstated gold at $35/ounce for foreign central banks
- **President Johnson** (1965): "There can be no question of our capacity and determination to maintain the gold value of the dollar at $35.00 per ounce" — gold-backing requirement removed 1968
- US gold reserves: $30B (end WWII) → $11B (summer 1971)
- **August 15, 1971**: Nixon closed "gold window" — stock market jumped 4% on record volume; link of gold to money permanently broken

### The Volcker Revolution
- Fed largely ignored money targets in 1970s; surge of inflation in 1979 brought pressure
- **Paul Volcker** appointed April 1979; **Saturday, October 6, 1979**: Fed would control **supply of money** instead of just setting rates
- **"Saturday night massacre"**: Fed funds target boosted to record **20%**; stocks fell 8% in 2.5 days
- European central banks and Bank of Japan joined Fed calling inflation "public enemy number one"
- Restricting money growth proved to be only real answer to controlling inflation

### The Fed and Interest Rates
- Fed controls money supply via **open market operations** — buying/selling government bonds
- **Open market purchase**: Fed buys bond, credits reserve account of seller's bank — thereby creating money
- **Open market sale**: buyer's bank instructs Fed to debit reserve account — reserves disappear
- **Federal funds market**: active market for reserves; **federal funds rate** = interest rate at which reserves trade
- Fed funds rate forms anchor for all short-term rates (prime rate, short-term Treasuries); directly or indirectly basis for trillions of dollars of loans
- **Quantitative easing** (Great Financial Crisis): large purchases pushing reserves far in excess of requirements; funds rate fell to zero
- **Ample-reserve regime**: open market operations have very little effect; funds rate set by interest rate Fed **pays** on reserves (right granted by Congress 2009)
- **1975 congressional resolution**: obliged Fed to announce monetary growth targets; **Humphrey-Hawkins Act (1978)**: forced biannual congressional testimony
- Before 1990: little correlation between Fed funds rate and stocks; since then: stronger stocks associated with rising rates
- Switch explained by type of shocks: 1970s-80s **supply shocks** (oil) → higher inflation + lower stocks; post-1990 **demand shocks** → central bank lowers rates
- **Table 18.1**: S&P 500 returns below average following onset of rate hiking cycle; underperformance grows through first two years; most negative 3-month return: **-15.86%** (March 2022)
- Rate increases raise real interest rates (depress discounted value of future cash flows) and increase probability of recession

### Bitcoin: The New Money?
- **Cryptocurrency**: new monetary asset created outside government control; Bitcoin invented 2008; **decentralized digital currency**
- Created by solving mathematical codes (**mining**); maximum **21 million Bitcoins**; ~19 million created by end 2021
- Transferred without central bank; verified through **cryptography**; recorded in public ledger (**blockchain**)
- First traded at 5 cents; $1,000 investment in 2009 worth **~$1 billion** by December 2021 — no other publicly traded asset has appreciated this much
- Four money characteristics compared across currency, deposits, gold, Bitcoin:
  - **Unit of account**: virtually no prices quoted in Bitcoin — not yet a unit of account
  - **Medium of exchange**: several companies/agencies accept it, but rather large transaction costs; longer verification delays
  - **Store of value**: limited supply (like gold) but correlation with stocks substantially higher than gold; fell 50% during Covid while gold went up
  - **Anonymity**: early years excellent; now less so through organized exchanges; **IRS** requires crypto disclosure on tax forms
- **Silk Road** run by **Ross Ulbricht** — Amazon-like website for illegal goods, only Bitcoin; **Colonial Pipeline** paid $4.3M ransom to **Dark Side**, government recovered 90% — demonstrating anonymity penetrable
- Bitcoin advantages: lower costs for **international transactions**; trades 24/7/365
- **Macroeconomic concerns**: more crypto adoption → lower demand for central bank money → **inflationary**; reduced demand makes monetary control more difficult
- If goods priced in cryptocurrencies → monetary system resembles gold standard: no inflationary bias but inability to provide crisis liquidity
- Gold enthusiast's question: "If stranded on desert island, which would you prefer: debit card, Bitcoin, or gold coin?"
- Central banks should move aggressively to make currency attractive by keeping inflation low and improving transaction efficiency

## Chapter 19: Stocks and the Business Cycle

> The stock market has predicted nine out of the last five recessions. — **Paul Samuelson**, 1966

### The 1987 Crash: Right on Economy, Wrong on Market
- Well-respected economist predicted 4%+ GDP growth, no recession for 3 years, double-digit corporate profit growth — completely right on every economic prediction
- Advisors recommended clients increase stock allocation
- Weeks later, October 19, 1987: market crashed 23%; three months post-speech, stocks at half their value during his talk
- **If you can predict the business cycle, you can beat buy-and-hold** — but this skill eludes economists

### Who Calls the Business Cycle?
- **NBER** (founded 1920): developed business cycle chronology back to 1854
- From 1802-2021: **48 recessions** averaging 19 months; expansions averaged 34 months; economy in recession **one-third** of time
- **Post-WWII**: 12 recessions averaging only 10 months; expansions averaged 64 months; recession **less than one-ninth** of time
- **Longest expansion**: 10 years, 8 months (ended by Covid-19)
- NBER never rushes — March 1991 trough not called until 21 months later; 2007 peak not called until December 2008

### Stock Returns and Business Cycle Turning Points
- Out of 48 recessions: **44 (over 90%)** preceded by 8%+ stock declines
- Stock market peaked **0 to 13 months before** recession (average ~5 months lead)
- Stock gave **no advance warning** for January 1980, July 1990, and Covid-19 recessions
- **Average lead** between market bottom and economic trough: **4.3 months** (more consistent than peak timing)
- **By the time recession ends, market has already risen 25% on average from its low**
- **False alarms**: August-December 1987: 35.1% decline — largest in 220-year history without subsequent recession
- Perfect timing strategy gains **5 percentage points/year** over buy-and-hold; **two-thirds** from predicting recession end

### How Hard Is It to Predict?
- September 1974: almost every economist at **President Ford's** anti-inflation conference unaware US was in middle of most severe postwar recession; median forecaster **overestimated GNP growth by 6 percentage points**
- **July 1979**: strong majority of forecasters believed recession had already started — NBER declared it didn't begin until January 1980
- **July 1981** Blue Chip headline: "Economic Exuberance Envisioned for 1982" — 1982 was a disaster; nearly 70% thought recession would end by Q1 1982 (wrong — lasted until November)
- **April 1985**: forecasters predicted expansion would last another 20 months — actually ended **more than 3.5 years later**
- Post-1987 crash: forecasters cut 1988 GDP to 1.9%; actual growth: **nearly 4%**
- September 2001: only **13%** believed US in recession — NBER later showed it began six months earlier
- **December 11, 2007 FOMC**: Fed economist **Dave Stockton**: "We have a 'growth recession' in this forecast and nothing more than that"
- **Has the business cycle been conquered?** Late 1980s: growing conviction recession was a thing of the past
- **Ed Yardeni** (Prudential-Bache): wrote "New Wave Manifesto" (late 1988) — self-repairing economies would expand forever; on eve of one of worst worldwide recessions
- **Leonard Silk** (*NYT*, May 1990): "Most economists foresee no recession in 1990 or 1991"
- **Worst course for investors**: follow prevailing sentiment — buy at high prices when everyone optimistic; sell at the low when confirmed pessimistic

## Chapter 20: When World Events Impact Financial Markets

> "I can predict the motion of heavenly bodies, but not the madness of crowds." — Isaac Newton

### September 11, 2001
- 8:48 a.m.: plane hit North Tower; futures initially fell few points, buyers reappeared within minutes
- 9:03 a.m.: second plane — entire world changed; S&P futures plunged 30 points (~3%); **$300 billion** would have been wiped off US stocks
- Miraculously, buyers appeared despite enormity — futures gained back half the loss by 9:15 a.m.
- All US exchanges closed for rest of week — longest since FDR's 1933 bank holiday
- **German DAX** fell 9%+; British pound and euro rallied against dollar (normally dollar gains in crisis)
- Monday reopening: **Dow fell 685 points (7.13%)** — more than twice Pearl Harbor's 3.5% drop
- Week's total: down **14%** from September 10; nearly 30% from all-time high

### Big Moves and News Events
- Of all 157 days with 5%+ changes (1885-2021), only **42 (one in four)** linked to significant world events
- **Government policy changes** largest driver; top gain: **14.87%** (October 6, 1931) on Hoover's $500M bank pool proposal
- **October 19, 1987 crash (-22.6%)** has no identifiable news cause
- **Covid-19**: Eight trading days March 9-18, 2020 — seven had 5%+ changes; March 16 fell **12.96%**, exceeding October 28, 1929
- **Markets hate uncertainty** — events disrupting investors' analytical frameworks have devastating effects

### Presidential Deaths and Market Reactions
- **Eisenhower heart attack** (September 1955): -6.54%
- **Kennedy assassination**: Dow fell 2.9% Friday; by Tuesday (Johnson in charge), soared 4.5% — reversed once leadership uncertainty resolved
- **FDR's death**: stocks rallied 4%+ — investors "never forgave" FDR, viewing him as anti-Wall Street

### Democrats vs. Republicans
- Stocks historically did better under Democrats: 11.33% vs. 8.81% nominal (since 1888)
- **Real returns nearly identical** because inflation lower under Republicans
- **Since 1952**: Democrats significantly outperformed regardless of inflation
- **Third year of presidential term is the best** for stock returns (includes Hoover's disastrous 1931)
- Correlation vs. causation: Democrats inherit bad economies at market bottoms, rule during early/middle bull market

### Stocks and War
- Nominal returns equal in war and peace; **real returns during peacetime greatly outstrip wartime** (inflation averages 6% wartime vs. under 2% peacetime)
- Stock market **more volatile during peacetime** than wartime (greatest volatility: late 1920s-early 1930s)

### World War I
- **NYSE closed July 30, 1914** — never before or since closed so long (five months); illegal "curb" trading continued, prices fell 15-20%
- Ironic: closure when US not at war; traders realized US would benefit from European conflict
- **1915**: best single-year increase in Dow history (**82%**); stocks doubled from prewar levels by November 1916
- Fell 20% once US formally entered (April 1917) through Armistice (November 1918)

### World War II
- Those who bought at WWII onset expecting WWI replay were "sorely disappointed"
- Roosevelt determined corporations wouldn't profit like WWI; public criticism that war costs not shared equally
- **Excess profits tax** enacted by Congress removed expected wartime premium
- Day before Pearl Harbor: Dow down 25% from 1939 high, still under 1/3 of 1929 peak
- Stocks fell 3.5% day after Pearl Harbor; continued falling to low April 28, 1942
- When tide turned: market climbed; by Germany's surrender (May 7, 1945): Dow 20% above prewar level
- **Atomic bomb** (Hiroshima): stocks surged 1.7% as investors recognized war's end was near
- WWII far less profitable than WWI: Dow up only 30% during six years from German invasion of Poland to V-J Day

### Korea and Vietnam
- **Korean War** (June 25, 1950): North Korea invaded South Korea; Dow fell **4.65%** — greater than day after Pearl Harbor
- **Vietnam War**: longest, least popular US war; starting from **Gulf of Tonkin** (August 2, 1964)
- Year and a half after Tonkin: Dow reached all-time high of 995 (18% above pre-Tonkin)
- Fell 30% when Fed tightened credit; by Paris peace pact (January 1973), gains over eight years of war were small
- Market held back by rising inflation, interest rates, and problems unrelated to Vietnam

### Gulf War I (1991)
- **August 2, 1990**: Iraq invaded Kuwait; oil prices skyrocketed; US military buildup in Saudi Arabia
- Rising oil + slowing economy drove US deeper into recession; **October 11: Dow slumped 18%+**
- First major war fought in world where oil, gold, US bonds traded around the clock (Tokyo, Singapore, London, New York)
- **Markets judged victors in hours**: bonds surged on success reports; Brent Crude fell from $29 to $20/barrel
- Following day: Dow jumped 115 points (4.4%); large gains throughout Europe and Asia
- By time US deployed ground troops, market knew victory at hand; war ended February 28
- First week of March: Dow **18%+ higher** than when war started

### Gulf War II and Afghanistan
- March 2003: Dow hit intraday low 7,416 on March 12; anticipation of fast victory sent stocks 15% higher within eight days
- **Afghanistan** (2001-2021): America's longest war; impacted markets little — investors turned focus inward

### Conclusion on War and Markets
- **Sobering reality**: only 1 in 4 major market movements can be linked to news of major import
- Those who sold in panic at WWI outbreak missed 1915 (best year ever)
- Those who bought at WWII onset expecting WWI replay were disappointed (government capped profits)
- World events may shake markets in short run but unable to dent superior long-term equity returns

## Chapter 21: Stocks, Bonds, and the Flow of Economic Data

### The July 5, 1996 Employment Report
- Payroll up 239,000; unemployment at six-year low of 5.3%; wages up 9 cents/hour — **biggest increase in 30 years**
- Clinton hailed it; financial markets stunned — bond prices collapsed, rates rose 0.25 points
- S&P 500 futures fell ~2%; European markets sold off within seconds; world equity markets lost **$200 billion**
- **Key lesson**: What Main Street sees as good news is often bad news on Wall Street

### How Markets React to Economic Data
- Markets respond to **difference between expectations and reality**, not absolute good/bad news
- If market expects 400,000 jobs lost and only 200,000 lost → "stronger than expected" — same effect as gaining 400,000 when 200,000 expected
- Prices already incorporate all expected information; only **surprises** move markets
- **Consensus estimate**: gathered by polling economists, forecasters, traders; widely reported by financial press
- Strong growth has **two opposing effects**: increases future earnings (numerator) but raises interest rates (denominator/discount rate)
- **Business cycle position matters**: in recessions, earnings gains outweigh rate changes; near expansion peaks, rate effect dominates
- Stock market often **gyrates throughout the day** as investors debate earnings vs. rate implications

### Key Economic Reports
- **Employment report**: single most important monthly release, compiled by **Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS)**
- Two separate surveys: **payroll survey** (130,000 businesses, 670,000 worksites) measures jobs; **household survey** (60,000 households) measures unemployment
- Most important metric: **nonfarm payroll** change; unemployment rate gets media attention but based on different survey
- Tricky: payroll employment can rise while unemployment rate rises (workers with two jobs counted once in household, twice in payroll)
- **Labor force participation rate**: ~2/3 of adult population; rose in 1980s-90s as women entered workforce; fell during Covid
- **ADP National Employment Report**: released two days before BLS; based on ~500,000 businesses, 26M employees (1 in 6 private-sector workers)
- **PMI** (Institute for Supply Management): 50 = half rising/half falling; 60+ = strong; below 40 = almost always recession
- **Core inflation** (excluding food/energy): more important to central banks than overall rate
- **PCE deflator**: Fed's preferred measure; runs 0.25-0.50 points below CPI
- Labor costs average **nearly 2/3 of production costs**; hourly wage increases not matched by productivity may signal inflation

### Central Bank Impact on Stocks
- **Martin Zweig**: "Monetary conditions exert enormous influence on stock prices... the dominant factor in determining the stock market's major direction"
- Reducing rates and providing credit = **almost always extremely welcome** by stock investors (lowers discount rate + stimulates demand)
- Fed holds **8 scheduled FOMC meetings yearly**; last meeting each quarter (with Fed projections including fed funds rate) = particular importance
- **Semiannual Congressional testimony** (February and July) very significant; any Fed official speech can move markets
- **January 3, 2001**: Unexpected 50bp cut (6.5% to 6%) — S&P 500 up 5%, Nasdaq up all-time record **14.2%**
- **Bernanke June 2013** taper tantrum: stocks/bonds suffered largest loss in almost two years
- **Chairman Powell pivot November 2021** to hawkish policy: stocks fell
- Only case stocks react poorly to easing: if monetary authority eases **excessively** so market fears inflation
- Even then, **investor should prefer stocks to bonds** — fixed-income hurt more by unexpected inflation
- Financial market reactions to data **not random but not easily predicted**; trying to beat market by anticipating releases = tricky game for speculators
- Most investors better **watching from sidelines** — stick to sound long-run strategy

## Chapter 22: Market Volatility

### Does the Past Portend the Future?
- **Dow 1922-1932** compared to **1980-1990**: uncanny similarity; *WSJ* printed similar graph Monday morning October 19, 1987
- That day: greatest one-day drop in history; market continued tracking 1929 for remainder of year
- Many forecasters advised clients to sell based on 1929 parallel
- **Post-crash divergence**: by August 1989 stocks hit new highs; contrast: two years after 1929, stocks had lost >2/3 of value
- **What was different?** 1987 Fed had power to control money supply; **temporarily flooded economy with money**, pledged to stand by all bank deposits
- No bank runs, no money supply contraction, no deflation; economy expanded despite market collapse
- **Lesson**: sharp sell-off can be **opportunity for profit, not time to panic**

### The 1987 Crash
- **October 19, 1987**: 508-point (**22.6%**) decline; NYSE volume exceeded **600 million shares** (fateful week volume exceeded all of 1966); worldwide decline exceeded **$1 trillion**
- **Tokyo** fell record 15.6%; **New Zealand** fell nearly 40%; **Hong Kong** market closed
- Lead-up: $15.7B trade deficit reported Wednesday; Dow fell 95 points (4%, record) Wednesday, 166 more Thursday-Friday
- Late Friday: S&P 500 futures fell to **3% below spot** — money managers willing to sell at significant concession
- Overnight: Tokyo fell 2.5%; London fell 10% as managers sold US stocks before anticipated NY decline

### Black Monday and Terrible Tuesday
- **NYSE trading chaotic**: no Dow stocks traded near 9:30 opening; 11 Dow stocks still hadn't opened by 10:30
- **Portfolio insurers** heavily sold index futures; late afternoon: S&P futures at **25-point (12%) discount** to spot
- Dow collapsed **300 points in final hour** to record 508-point loss
- **Terrible Tuesday**: opened up 10% from Monday low, then plunged below Monday close by noon
- S&P 500 futures collapsed to 181 — **40 points/22% under** reported index value (Dow equivalent: 1,450)
- Near meltdown: trading halted in 200 stocks and S&P 500 futures
- Only **Major Market Index** futures (Chicago Board of Trade) remained open — brave buyers stepped in, futures shot up 120 Dow points in minutes, worst panic passed
- *WSJ* report: this futures market was **key to reversing catastrophic collapse**

### Causes
- No single precipitating event (war, assassination, bankruptcy)
- **Dollar crisis**: trade deficit + exchange rate policies; **Leo Melamed** (CME chairman): "What caused the crash was all that f— around with the currencies"
- World markets had boomed since 1982: British +164%, Swiss +209%, German +217%, Japanese +288%, Italian +421%
- Bond yields at **10.47%** morning of October 19 despite controlled inflation — record gap between stock/bond yields
- **Portfolio insurance**: sold index futures against portfolios to protect; when market gapped, no buyers existed, liquidity vanished
- Portfolio insurance was not insurance at all — replaced by **index options** (explicit, known premium cost)

### Circuit Breakers
- April 2013 SEC rules: 15-minute halt at **7%** decline, another at **13%**, trading halted all day at **20%**
- Rationale: gives time to reassess; criticism: discourages short-term buyers, can accelerate declines toward limits

### Flash Crash, May 6, 2010
- Dow collapsed 600+ points (5%) in minutes, recovered just as quickly
- Benchmark hit low at 2:47 p.m., nearly **10% below** previous close; **$800 billion** erased in five minutes
- Caused by $4 billion S&P 500 futures sale over three minutes; **high-frequency traders** initially absorbed then sold into thin market
- **Procter & Gamble** recorded trade at $39.37 (50%+ below $86 opening); **Accenture** fell from $38 to **one penny**
- 20,000 trades in 300 securities were 60%+ away from prior price — all canceled after close
- Extreme prices wouldn't have occurred if exchange **specialists** still controlled order flow — computerized systems programmed to withdraw when prices fall steeply
- **Stub quotes** (penny bids, $100,000 asks — placeholders not meant to be traded) got executed

### Historical Volatility
- Period of greatest volatility: **Great Depression**; 1932 annualized volatility **63.7%** vs. 1993's 3.36% (least volatile)
- Excluding 1929-1939, volatility averaged ~13% — remarkably stable over 190 years
- Average daily change over 125 years: **0.73%**
- Standard deviation **25%+ higher** in recessions than expansions (greater uncertainty + fixed costs magnify profit volatility)
- **VIX** peaked at **172** after 1987 crash; nearly **90** after Lehman; all-time low **8.56** (November 24, 2017)
- 36 of 157 large changes occurred in **October** — more than twice any other month; reputation fully justified

### The Economics of Market Volatility
- **Robert Shiller** (Yale, 1981): devised method of determining whether investors overreact to changes in dividends/interest rates
- Found stock prices far too variable to be explained merely by subsequent behavior of dividends and interest rates
- Investors price stocks in recession as if they expect dividends to go much lower — contrary to historical experience
- Since earnings/profits follow the business cycle (word *cycle* implies ups followed by downs), temporary drop in dividends should have very minor effect on price discounting cash flows into infinite future
- When stocks collapsing, worst-case scenarios loom large — **May 6, 1932**: after stocks plummeted 85%, **Dean Witter** memo:

> "There are only two premises which are tenable as to the future. Either we are going to have chaos or else recovery. The former theory is foolish. If chaos ensues nothing will maintain value; neither bonds nor stocks nor bank deposits nor gold will remain valuable."

### 1929-1932 Collapse
- Dow collapsed **89%**; 37 episodes with 5%+ moves — surprisingly **21 were increases** (short covering by speculators forced to buy back)
- "Up the staircase, down the elevator" in bull markets; reverse in bear markets
- Ordinary investors beware: not as easy to make money in trending markets as it looks

> "Should you not want to shop in a store where every so often you hear '10 percent to 20 percent off the price of all items for the next 30 minutes!'?"

## Chapter 23: The Great Financial Crisis of 2008-2009

### The Week That Rocked Markets
- **September 15, 2008**: Lehman Brothers filed for bankruptcy — largest in US history ($613B liabilities)
- Bear Stearns forced into J.P. Morgan at $2/share March 17 (99% below $172.61 high); "Bear Stearns was only the appetizer for this bear market"
- Monday: stocks surprised by opening higher; then risk premiums soared as lenders backed away from everything except Treasuries; Dow fell nearly 5%
- **September 16**: AIG plunged below $3 (from $60/year earlier); Fed loaned **$85 billion** — dramatic turnaround after rejecting $40B request just a week earlier
- **September 17**: Reserve Primary Fund broke the buck (97 cents) due to Lehman securities marked to zero
- Author noticed **three-month Treasury bill yields at 0.06%** — unprecedented flight to safety; last time near zero: Great Depression, 75 years earlier

> **Ben Bernanke**: "Regarding the Great Depression. You're right, we did it. We're very sorry. But thanks to you, we won't do it again."

> **Chuck Prince** (Citigroup CEO, July 2007): "When the music stops, in terms of liquidity, things will get complicated. But as long as the music is playing, you've got to get up and dance."

### Real Estate Bubble Origins
- Stocks selling for modest **16x earnings** at 2007 peak — no general overvaluation (unlike tech boom's 30x)
- Real estate prices nearly tripled in previous decade; peaked summer 2006
- **New Century Financial** (leading subprime lender) filed bankruptcy April 2007; **Bear Stearns** suspended fund redemptions June 2007
- Case-Shiller index: nominal home prices nearly tripled 1997-2006; real prices up **130%**
- Price-to-income ratio: tight range 2.5-3.1 from 1978-2002, then jumped to **4.1 in 2006** (nearly 50% above historical)
- **NAR** (January 2006): 43% of first-time buyers purchased with no-money-down loans; median down payment mere 2% of $150,000 home

### The Rating Agency Catastrophe
- Based on postwar data: probability nationwide housing prices would decline 20% was "virtually zero"
- **Fatal assumption**: if real estate always worth more than mortgage, creditworthiness of borrower doesn't matter → AAA ratings on subprime securities
- **Goldman Sachs**: April 2006 sold 12 mortgage bonds (10 investment grade, 3 AAA); by September 2007, 7 downgraded to junk, 4 wiped out
- Total subprime, alt-A, and jumbo mortgages reached **$2.8 trillion** by Q2 2007
- **Robert Shiller** and **Karl Case** first warned about housing bubble in 2003 *Brookings Papers*; **Dean Baker** wrote/lectured extensively 2005-2006

### Greenspan's Oversight
- Fed Chairman didn't warn public despite unprecedented housing price rise
- Fellow governor **Edward Gramlich** published book on subprime instruments June 2007
- Greenspan worried about *stock prices* a decade earlier ("irrational exuberance" speech, December 1996) — why not real estate?
- October 2008 congressional testimony: in **"shocked disbelief"** lending institutions didn't protect against meltdown
- Author's view: Greenspan not responsible for *creating* bubble — forces (long-term rate declines, subprime proliferation) operated worldwide

### Minsky's Financial Instability Hypothesis
- **Hyman Minsky** (Washington University): long stability periods draw in speculators and Ponzi schemes
- **Charles Kindleberger's** *Manias, Panics, and Crashes* attracted large following
- Great Moderation (1983-2005): GDP volatility fell by half; 1920s showed same pattern before 1929 crash

### Lehman's Fatal Bet
- Founded 1850s; 2007: fourth consecutive year of record profitability, $19.2B net revenues, 30,000 employees
- Financed **$17 billion** to Tishman Speyer for Archstone-Smith Trust ($22.2B); left with $5B in unsold real estate
- "Blackstone got the very last chair in the real estate closing room, but Lehman was left standing"
- Fed rejected $40B loan request; **Henry Paulson** (Treasury Secretary) adopted "No More Bailouts" after Bear/Fannie/Freddie criticism
- **Lawrence Ball** (Johns Hopkins): Fed had legal authority under Section 13(3) to lend in "unusual and exigent circumstances"

### Contrast with Dot-Com Bubble
- At dot-com peak, investment banks had sold off virtually all risky tech holdings before bubble burst
- At real estate peak, **Wall Street was up to its ears in housing-related debt**
- **AIG** offered to insure hundreds of billions via credit default swaps; when prices fell, couldn't produce reserves

### Fed's Response and Impact
- September 19: Treasury insured all money market funds using Exchange Stabilization Fund ($50B backing $2.5T+)
- **FDIC** increased deposit insurance to **$250,000**; created Temporary Liquidity Guarantee Program for senior debt
- **Bernanke** studied Friedman's *Monetary History* — determined to avoid repeating Fed's Depression-era failures
- Money supply (M2) rose during crisis vs. fell **29%** in Great Depression (1929-1933)

### Economic and Financial Impact
- US real GDP declined **4.0%** (worst since WWII); 18-month recession = longest since 43-month Depression
- Unemployment: **10.0%** (October 2009); remained above 8% for three years
- S&P 500 fell to **12-year low of 676** (March 9, 2009) — nearly **57% below** peak
- Worldwide: **$33 trillion** of stock market wealth lost — about half world's annual GDP
- **Financial sector declined 84%**; **Citibank** lost 98.3%; **AIG** lost **99.5%**; **Royal Bank of Scotland** fell 99%
- VIX reached nearly **90** after Lehman (exceeded only by 1987 crash); 15 days with 5%+ moves Sept-Dec 2008
- S&P 500 reported earnings fell **92%** (exceeded 83% Depression decline), driven by AIG's $61B Q4 loss
- LIBOR-Fed funds spread reached **364 basis points** (October 10, 2008)

## Chapter 24: Covid-19 Pandemic

### The Market Peak and the Crash
- February 12, 2020: stocks at all-time high; second longest bull market (nearly 12 years without 20% decline)
- Siegel polled ~100 advisors: "What will end the bull market?" — **only one person chose "pandemic"**
- Previous virus scares (SARS-1 2003, MERS 2012, Ebola) hadn't impacted US
- **March 29 bottom**: S&P 500 declined **34%**; over **$20 trillion** wiped off worldwide in less than seven weeks

### Rational Analysis vs. Panic
- Siegel's analysis: at 20x P/E, one year's lost earnings = only **5%** decline; even two profitless years = 10%
- **Fear has much stronger grip on investors than historical evidence**
- Spanish flu (deadlier for young/healthy) had minor economic/market impact; 1957 Asian flu, 1968 Hong Kong flu had little consequence
- But 24/7 news showing overtaxed hospitals + overflowing morgues amplified anxiety

### Economic Collapse and Response
- Weekly jobless claims: 200,000 (late February) → **6.1 million** (first week April) — exceeded previous high by factor of 9
- **CARES Act (March 2020)**: **$2.2 trillion** including PPP, enhanced unemployment, tax cuts; December added **$900 billion**
- Both measures almost unanimously passed Congress — urgency melted conservative opposition
- **Fed**: lowered rates to zero; announced open-ended bond purchases ("in amounts needed"); balance sheet grew to nearly **$9 trillion** by March 2022

### Money Supply Surge and Inflation
- M2 jumped over 3.5% in March-April 2020, annualized rate of **50%** — one of largest one-month increases in US history
- Key difference from 2008: Government credited money **directly** into bank accounts (not just bank reserves)
- 2020 money growth = highest single-year increase in US history, exceeding WWI and WWII
- Historical relationship: 1970-1986 (high inflation): M2 9.6%/year, CPI 7.0%; 1986-2020 (low inflation): M2 5.4%, CPI 2.6%

> "Rising inflation and interest rates will end the nearly forty-year bull market in bonds. There is no 'free lunch.' The 'War on Covid-19' will be paid for by those holding monetary assets whose value will be eroded by the upcoming inflation." — Jeremy Siegel, April 2020

### Fed's Inflation Miss
- December 2020 Fed forecast: PCE deflator would rise 1.8% in 2021; highest FOMC estimate: 2.3%
- Reality: PCE nearly 5%, CPI soared to **7.5%**
- Many overestimated inflationary consequence of 2008 QE, then reasoned: if massive reserves didn't cause inflation then, why should Covid stimulus? — key distinction: 2008 expanded reserves, not money supply
- Fed used **"transitory"** label; **Jerome Powell** "retired" the term November 2021
- BLS methodology extremely slow recording housing prices (1/3 of CPI weight); ignored Case-Shiller 20%+ increases

### Market Recovery
- Stocks increased **44%** to record highs February 2020-December 2021; from March bottom, S&P rose **over 100%**
- **TINA** ("There is no alternative") with rates so low
- Leveraged firms gain from inflation; fixed labor contracts benefit firms as unexpected inflation reduces real costs
- Oil fell to **-$40.32/barrel** (April 20, 2020) — sellers paid for removal; lumber quadrupled; Baltic Dry Index rose fivefold
- Residential real estate rose **more than 25%** March 2020-December 2021 — fastest rate since at least 1986; exceeded rate during 2005-2008 housing bubble
- Causes different from prior bubble: not subprime lending but decreased rates, desire for vacation homes, desire for larger homes (home office)
- **REIT index** fell 43% from February 20 to March 23, 2020; by December 2021 only 18% above prepandemic peak (far less than S&P 500's 42% increase)
- REIT components diverged: commercial office barely recovered; **data centers** up 50%; **self-storage** almost doubled

### Permanent Changes
- **MRNA vaccines**: breakthrough enhancing immune system, fighting cancer; longer life expectancy
- **Work-at-home economy** far more successful than expected; sharp decline in commercial office space demand
- Rise in **gig economy**; increase in productivity; acceleration of online shopping/entertainment
- **Over 90% of asset value** relies on earnings power more than one year into future — short-term disruptions don't justify sharp price declines
- Under fiat money economy, no natural limit to money creation — real assets (especially stocks) critical portfolio component

## Part VII: Building Wealth Through Stocks

## Chapter 25: How Psychology Can Thwart Investment Goals

> "The market is most dangerous when it looks best; it is most inviting when it looks worst." — Frank J. Williams, 1930

### Behavioral Finance Through "Dave and Jennifer"
- **October 1999**: Dave sells "old fogy" stocks (Philip Morris, P&G, Exxon) for internet stocks because friends making fortunes
- **March 2000**: Portfolio up 60%, Nasdaq crosses 5,000; constantly trading as "information hits faster"
- **July 2000**: Sold internet stocks, bought "backbone" (Cisco, EMC, Nortel, Oracle) — "picks and shovels" of internet gold rush
- **November 2000**: Down 20%; averaging down to "get even"
- **August 2001**: Lost three-quarters of retirement money; considers abandoning stocks for bonds entirely

### Key Behavioral Biases
- **Amos Tversky** and **Daniel Kahneman** developed **prospect theory** (1970s) — model of how people actually behave under uncertainty
- **Herding instinct**: **Solomon Asch** experiment — subjects gave incorrect answers when large group did, not from social pressure but disbelief that large group could be wrong
- **Information cascades**: choosing the busier restaurant; in markets, one bidder triggers others
- **Robert Shiller**: stock prices far too volatile to be explained by economic factors — excess volatility from fads and fashions

> "We find that whole communities suddenly fix their minds upon one subject, and go mad in its pursuit... Men think in herds... they go mad in herds, while they only recover their senses slowly and one by one." — Charles Mackay, 1852

### Excessive Trading and Overconfidence
- 2000 study "Trading Is Hazardous to Your Wealth": returns of heaviest traders **7.1% below** infrequent traders
- **Overconfidence**: most people believe they're better than average (statistically impossible)
- **Self-attribution bias**: taking credit for favorable outcomes when credit not due; early success feeds overconfidence
- **Representative bias**: seeing too many parallels between events that seem the same
  - Investment newsletters claim past events predict future moves — "but when I try to use that advice, it never works"
  - **Spectacularly wrong moves from representative bias**: WWI outbreak (1914) NYSE closed five months — wrong! 1915 became best year ever; WWII outbreak (1939) investors bought expecting WWI replay — wrong! FDR wouldn't let corporations prosper, six-year bear market
- **Data mining**: cheap computing finds spectacular but meaningless fits ("stocks rise every third Thursday when moon is full")
- **Cognitive dissonance**: discomfort when confronting contradictory evidence; experts even more subject to overconfidence — trained to find supporting evidence; tech analysts in 2000 downgraded stocks only *after* 80-90% falls
- Psychologically, humans not designed to accept randomness — deep need to know **why** something happens; reporters and "experts" fill holes with wrong explanations

### Loss Aversion and Holding Losers
- **Loss aversion**: individuals much more upset about losing than gaining same amount (Kahneman and Tversky)
- **Richard Thaler**: **mental accounting** or **narrow framing** — open mental account with purchase price as reference point
- Multiple losses: likely to aggregate (one big loss easier than many small); **avoiding realization of losses becomes primary goal**
- **Pride**: every investment involves emotional and financial commitment — hard to evaluate objectively
- **"Get-even-itis disease"** (Leroy Gross, 1982): probably caused more portfolio destruction than any other mistake
- Hard to admit bad investment, even harder to admit mistake to others; decisions must be **forward-looking** — past is sunk cost
- **Anchoring bias**: $40 for stock that was $80 seems cheap — "Cheap relative to past price or future prospects?"
- Prospect theory predicts: investors increase position and risk trying to get even
- Stocks above purchase price **50% more likely** to be sold than losers — bad strategy from both trading and tax standpoints
- Successful trader counseled: 80% of trades made money but down overall; after counseling: only one-third make money but "way ahead" — cuts losers quickly, holds winners

> "Cut your losers short and let your winners ride." — Wall Street adage

### Rules for Avoiding Behavioral Traps
- **Precommitment**: set asset allocation rule and stick to it — don't second-guess; basic return factors change far less than daily fluctuations
- If buying for short-term trade: **establish absolute selling point** to minimize losses; don't rationalize stock will come back
- **Don't tell friends** about trades — living up to expectations makes taking loss harder
- Set up **small separate trading account** if you enjoy trading — consider money completely lost, never exceed rigid limit

### Myopic Loss Aversion and the Equity Premium Puzzle
- **Benartzi and Thaler**: groups seeing yearly returns invested much smaller fraction in stocks than groups seeing 5/10/20-year intervals
- **Myopic loss aversion**: basing decisions on short-term fluctuations
- Key to solving **equity premium puzzle**: investors concentrate on too-short horizon, stocks seem risky, must be enticed with fat premium
- If evaluated every 10 years: premium needed only **2%**; every 20 years: **1.4%**; every 30 years: close to 1%
- Practical advice: look at stocks all you want — **don't alter long-term strategy** unless significant evidence of extreme overvaluation

### Contrarian Investing and Investor Sentiment
- **Contrarian**: dissents from prevailing opinion; **Humphrey B. Neill** (1951): "When everyone thinks alike, everyone is likely to be wrong"
- **Benjamin Graham** (~80 years ago): "The psychology of the speculator militates strongly against his success. For by relation of cause and effect, he is most optimistic when prices are high and most despondent when they are at bottom."
- **Investors Intelligence** (New Rochelle, NY): over 40 years, evaluated newsletter scores — when **bullish sentiment very high**, be wary; when **bearish sentiment spikes**, often good time to buy
- **VIX Index**: spikes upward when investor sentiment plunges; anxiety measured from put option premiums = almost perfect reflection of sentiment
- **De Bondt and Thaler**: examined five-year winner/loser portfolios — **winner portfolios lagged market by 10%**; **loser portfolios beat market by 30%**
- Related to **representativeness heuristic**: people extrapolate recent trends too far
- Strategy based on out-of-favor stocks: **Dogs of the Dow** (Dow 10 strategy)
- Investing success requires **deeper self-knowledge** than most jobs or personal relationships

> "The stock market is a very expensive place to find out who you are."

## Chapter 26: ETFs, Stock Index Futures, and Options

### Exchange-Traded Funds
- In 2021, highest dollar volume security traded on US exchanges wasn't Apple, Google, or Tesla — it was **spiders (SPY)**, the S&P 500 Depository Receipts ETF
- Over 18.6 billion shares worth **$7 trillion** traded in 2021
- First ETF launched 1993; other nicknames: **Cubes (QQQ)** for Nasdaq-100, **Diamonds (DIA)** for Dow Jones
- By 2012, ETF assets only 10% of mutual fund assets; by 2020 approached **30%** — growth much faster
- **Tax advantage**: swaps between ETFs and underlying shares are **exchanges in kind** (not taxable events); generate almost no capital gains
- Mutual funds generate capital gains from investor redemptions or index rebalancing — ETFs avoid this
- **Authorized participants** exchange underlying stocks for ETF units (creation unit: 50,000 shares), keeping prices extremely close to index via arbitrage
- Can be bought/sold anytime during day (mutual funds only at close); can be sold short; can be margined like stock (50%)
- **Hedging**: investor with gains in individual stocks can sell ETFs to cover portfolio value without triggering tax on individual stock gains

### Stock Index Futures
- S&P 500 futures launched **February 1982** by Kansas City Board of Trade
- By 1984, contract value surpassed NYSE total stock dollar volume; today exceeds **$300 billion/day**
- **E-mini**: 50 times the index; every point = $50 per contract
- Initial margin ~5% of value — command **$20** of stock per dollar of margin (vs. 50% margin for individual stocks)
- **Cash settlement**: no stock delivery; difference between contract and index at settlement
- Creating cash-settled futures required overcoming state gambling laws; federal CFTC regulation superseded

### Program Trading, Arbitrage, and HFTs
- **April 13, 1992**: Chicago River leak halted futures; NYSE appeared "brain-dead," volume dropped 25%+
- **Program traders**: exploit price differences between futures (Chicago) and component stocks (New York)
- In 1980s, most stock level changes originated on Wacker Drive (CME), not Wall Street
- **High-frequency traders**: **Michael Lewis** (*Flash Boys*) documented arms race for microsecond advantages
- Proposed reforms: **speed bumps** or **batch auctions** every few seconds
- HFTs virtually no consequence to long-term investors; bid-ask spreads much smaller than when specialists dominated

### Triple Witching and Fair Value
- **Triple witching**: four times yearly when index futures, index options, and stock options all expire third Friday
- Huge buy/sell imbalances cause volatile price swings; 1988 reform: settle futures at Friday opening instead of closing
- **Fair value** depends on **dividend yield** vs. **interest rate**; before financial crisis, rates exceeded yield so futures above spot; since crisis, reversed

### Leveraged ETFs
- $100,000 in triple bear SPXS (December 2015) worth **$2,000** six years later
- Constant rebalancing introduces **downward bias**; 3× bear lost almost 50% per year
- Triple bear rose 135% during pandemic crash but down 50% when market fully recovered

### Index Options
- **Calls** give right to buy stock at fixed **strike price**; **Puts** give right to sell
- Options on individual stocks existed for decades; organized trading began with **CBOE establishment in 1974**
- **Limited liability advantage**: if market moves against buyer, forfeit purchase price (**premium**) without margin calls
- Example: S&P 500 at 4,600; buy six-month call at 4,700 strike for 80 points ($8,000); if market rises to 4,880, profit = 100 points/$10,000 (over 100% return)
- Price depends on interest rates, dividend yields, and especially **expected volatility** (measured by VIX)
- **Fischer Black** and **Myron Scholes** developed first pricing formula (1970s); Scholes won Nobel Prize 1997
- **Black-Scholes formula**: instant success, programmed on handheld calculators worldwide
- Market professionals estimate **85% of individual options players lose money** — must be right about direction, timing, and strike
- **Protective puts**: insurance against decline (like buying insurance premium)
- **Buy and write**: call writers collect premium, keep it if stock doesn't rise above strike; popular among existing stock owners
- Options more flexible than futures/ETFs — can replicate any future or ETF with options, but not vice versa

## Chapter 27: Fund Performance, Indexing, and Investor Returns

> "I have little confidence even in the ability of analysts, let alone untrained investors, to select common stocks that will give better than average results." — Benjamin Graham

### The Average Investor Paradox
- Crucial difference between investing and other competitive endeavors: anyone can be as good as average with no effort
- Since sum of each investor's stocks must equal the market, for each dollar that outperforms, another must underperform
- Before 1975, matching market was virtually impossible; since then, **index mutual funds** and **ETFs** empowered small investors
- Index funds have **revolutionized investing**

### The Case Against Active Management
- **S&P Dow Jones Indices** biannual review: over **three-quarters** of domestic funds underperformed their index over 3 years; **over 90%** over 10 and 20 years
- **2008-2021**: not a single year where more than half of funds outperformed
- 132 funds surviving entire 50-year period (1972-2021): only **one** (AMF Large Cap Equity) beat S&P 500 by 2%+ per year; 83 of 132 (almost two-thirds) underperformed
- Theoretical distribution (random picks) would predict 21 funds outperforming by 3%+; reality: only one
- Underperformance not new: 1970 **Becker Securities** study showed median pension fund lagged S&P 500 by 1 point; confirmed by **William Sharpe**, **Michael Jensen**, **Irwin Friend**

### Star Managers and Reversion
- **Peter Lynch** (Fidelity Magellan, 1977-1990): over 29%/year, more than double S&P 500; probability of luck: **1 in 500,000**; after Lynch, Magellan's edge shrank dramatically
- **Warren Buffett** (Berkshire Hathaway): 17.88%/year 1972-2012, beat all other funds; but underperformed S&P 500 in following 10 years
- Top 15 funds beating S&P by ~3 points in first 40 years **underperformed by nearly same margin** in next 10 years
- **Bill Miller** (Legg Mason Value Trust): record 15 consecutive years beating S&P (1991-2005); turned cold 2006-2008
- Short-run persistence exists (managers follow styles in favor), but over longer periods, star performers often falter

### The Probability Problem
- Manager with 1% edge: only **62.7%** probability of beating market after 10 years; **71.2%** after 30 years
- Manager with 2% edge: **74.0%** after 10 years — still one-in-four chance of falling short
- To statistically confirm a bad manager: must underperform by **4%/year for ~15 years** — assets would be half indexed level by then
- Even hypothetical young Peter Lynch with 5% edge: **76.1%** chance of beating market over two years — nearly 1-in-4 chance of being fired as incapable

### Why Active Funds Underperform
- Performance lags largely because **funds impose fees and trading costs often as high as 2%+ per year**
- Three cost sources: **(1)** brokerage commissions and bid-ask spread, **(2)** management fees (and possibly load), **(3)** competition with equally or more skilled managers
- Mathematical impossibility for everyone to beat market — for every dollar that outperforms, another must underperform
- Investing $1,000 at 11%/year = **$23,000** over 30 years; **1% annual fee** reduces to **~$16,000** (almost a third less)
- **3% annual fee**: accumulation just over **$10,000** — less than half the market return
- Every extra percentage point of costs requires investors aged 25 to **retire two years later**
- **"A little learning is a dangerous thing"**: novice with some valuation knowledge often performs worse than indexed investor with no knowledge

> "Contrary to their oft articulated goal of outperforming the market averages, investment managers are not beating the market; the market is beating them." — Charles D. Ellis

### A Little Learning Is a Dangerous Thing
- Investor with some knowledge of equity valuations often performs **worse** than someone with no knowledge who indexes
- Novice sees stock with good earnings not rising as expected — buys thinking it's a bargain
- **Highly informed investors** know special circumstances caused the increase and won't repeat — happy to sell to novices
- Uninformed indexed investors who don't even know company earnings often do better
- Many seeming anomalies due to trading of informed investors with special information not easily processed by others
- When stock looks too cheap or too dear, easy explanation (emotional traders mispriced it) usually wrong

### The Rise of Index Funds
- **Vanguard 500** launched 1976 by **John Bogle**, raising only $11.4M initially — few believed concept would survive
- By end of 1995: $17 billion; March 2000 (market peak): over $100 billion (world's largest equity fund)
- End of 2021: **$800 billion** in Vanguard 500; Total Stock Market Fund over **$1.3 trillion**
- Charges only **4 basis points** (0.04%)/year — exactly equals underperformance vs. S&P 500 total return
- **S&P 500 inclusion creates overpricing**: anticipated index fund buying drives up stock before entry
  - **Yahoo!** (December 1999): surged **64%** in five trading days between announcement and inclusion
  - **Tesla** (December 2020): price jumped 56%+ until entry date; over 200 million shares traded on entry day
  - S&P 2000 study: shares rose average **8.49%** from announcement to admission; fell 3.23% in next 10 days
- Total stock market indexes avoid this overpricing problem but S&P 500 has still outperformed through 2021

### Fundamentally Weighted vs. Capitalization-Weighted Indexes
- **Capitalization-weighted** (standard): each firm weighted by market value; represent average dollar-weighted performance of all investors
- Offer **mean-variance efficiency** under efficient market assumptions — lowest risk for given return
- **Efficient market requirement** (very stringent): price must be unbiased estimate of true underlying value at all times; no easily obtainable information allows better estimates
- **Noisy market hypothesis**: stock prices change for reasons unrelated to fundamentals (liquidity, tax, speculation) — prices become noisy, biased estimates
- **Fundamentally weighted**: each stock weighted by **dividends, earnings, cash flows, book value** — not market cap
- If stock rises without fundamental support, shares sold (rebalanced); if falls without deterioration, shares bought at lower price
- Mitigates bubbles: would have sold tech stocks as prices rose in 1999-2000; still passive (no stock picking)
- Origins: 1980s Japan bubble → **MSCI** weighted countries by GDP; **Robert Jones** (Goldman Sachs, 1987) weighted by corporate profits
- **Robert Arnott, Jason Hsu, Philip Moore** (2005): "Fundamental Indexation" in *Financial Analysts Journal*
- **First fundamentally weighted ETF**: PowerShares FTSE RAFI US 1000 (December 2005); **WisdomTree** launched 20 dividend-weighted ETFs six months later
- Performance 2007-2022: PRF 9.15%, EPS-weighted 9.19%, SPY 9.66% — fundamentally weighted fell slightly short of S&P 500 but outperformed S&P 500 Value (7.00%) and Russell 1000 Value (6.89%)
- For investors wanting value tilt, fundamentally weighted indexes generally better diversified than most value funds

## Chapter 28: Structuring a Portfolio for Long-Term Growth

> "My favorite holding period is forever." — **Warren Buffett**, 1994

### Seven Principles for Successful Investing

**1. Expect 5% real returns going forward, not the historical 6-7%.**
- Lower transaction costs and easy diversification implies higher P/E ratios (~20 vs. historical 15)

**2. Stocks are real assets — excellent long-term inflation hedges.**
- Claims on real capital (plant, equipment, copyrights, intellectual capital)
- Virtually all inflation since WWII had no negative impact on real stock returns
- Short run: central bank tightening causes below-average returns

**3. Stock risk declines over time; bond risk increases.**
- Stocks display **mean reversion**: shocks offset by opposite shocks; bonds subject to inflation uncertainty
- Longer horizons → higher equity allocation

**4. Invest the largest percentage in low-cost index funds spanning a global portfolio.**
- Broad-based indexes outperformed vast majority of actively managed funds over past half-century

**5. Invest at least one-third in international stocks.**
- US has about half of world's equity capital but is highest-priced major market in 2022
- Lower valuation ratios apt to produce superior long-run returns

**6. Tilt toward value stocks using passive indexed or fundamentally weighted funds.**
- Growth stocks become overpriced every ~25 years: Nifty-Fifty, dot-com, 2020-2021 tech surge
- Value investing based on principle that noise traders deflect prices from fundamentals; stocks priced low relative to fundamentals likely offer better future returns

**7. Establish firm rules and stick to them.**
- Temptations to buy when bullish / sell when bearish are hard to resist
- Most investors who trade have poor returns

### Three Pitfalls
1. **Trading frequently** to beat the market — tales of quick wealth tempt us; transaction costs and bad timing sink returns
2. **Chasing hot mutual fund managers** — hot managers replace hot stocks as new strategy; same game, same poor results
3. **Timing market cycles** — surprisingly, often the best-informed investors fall into this trap; extraordinarily difficult to stay aloof from market opinion

> "Worldly wisdom teaches that it is better for reputation to fail conventionally than to succeed unconventionally." — **John Maynard Keynes**

### Implementing the Plan and the Role of an Investment Advisor
- **Knowing right strategy vs. implementing it are not the same** — as **Peter Bernstein** noted, many pitfalls prevent achieving intended goals
- Selective memory distorts: "I knew that stock was going up!" — we forget doubts we had at decision time
- Hindsight encourages playing hunches and outsmarting others who are playing the same game
- Failing by following experts' advice far easier than failing by rejecting consensus — **Keynes**: "better for reputation to fail conventionally"
- Proper investment strategy as much **psychological as intellectual** challenge
- If you seek help, select advisor who agrees with principles of diversification and long-term investing espoused in the book
- Within grasp of all to avoid investing pitfalls and reap generous rewards available in equities

### Conclusion
- Stock markets are driving forces behind allocation of world's capital and fundamental engines of economic growth
- Stocks represent best way to accumulate wealth in the long run — as true today as when first edition published in 1994
