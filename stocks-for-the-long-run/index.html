Part I: The Verdict of History

Chapter 1: The Case for Equity

The book opens with one of investing’s greatest ironies. In the summer of 1929, John J. Raskob, a senior financial executive at General Motors, told Ladies’ Home Journal that “everybody ought to be rich” by investing fifteen dollars a month in stocks. Two months later the market crashed, and Senator Arthur Robinson publicly blamed Raskob for the catastrophe. Raskob became a symbol of reckless optimism for generations. Yet the punch line is that his advice actually worked. An investor who put fifteen dollars a month into stocks starting at that catastrophic September peak would have beaten Treasury bills within four years. After thirty years the portfolio exceeded sixty thousand dollars, returning 12.72 percent annually—more than eight times what bonds delivered.

That anecdote sets up the central exhibit of the entire book: the 220-year record of American asset returns. One dollar invested in a diversified stock portfolio in 1802 would have grown to $54.2 million by 2021. The same dollar in bonds: $50,206. In gold: a mere $94. On a logarithmic chart, stock returns hug a remarkably steady trendline across two centuries of wars, panics, depressions, and technological revolutions. The compound annual real return barely wobbles from its 6.9 percent average regardless of the era.

The most important chart in the book: $1 invested in stocks in 1802 grew to $2.3 million in real terms by 2021. Note the logarithmic scale and the remarkably steady trendline for equities.

“Unless we have had the extreme misfortune to invest at the very peak of a noteworthy rise, those periods in which the average market value of our holding remains less than the amount we paid are of comparatively short duration. Our hazard even in such extreme cases appears to be that of time alone.” — Edgar Lawrence Smith, 1925

The intellectual history of equity investing is itself a rollercoaster. Edgar Lawrence Smith published the first rigorous case for stocks in 1925, and John Maynard Keynes praised it as proof that bonds were systematically overvalued. Then Irving Fisher, the greatest American economist of his generation and a man worth ten million dollars (over $150 million in 2021 money), declared in October 1929 that stocks had reached “a permanently high plateau.” Two weeks later the crash destroyed his fortune and his reputation. After the Depression, Graham and Dodd attacked the common stock theory entirely, and for decades equities were dismissed as speculation rather than investment.

The great bull market of 1982–2000 rehabilitated stocks in the public imagination. The Dow rose from 790 to 11,723, a compounding rate above fifteen percent annually for eighteen years. Stock tickers appeared in bars, airports, and airline seat backs. CNBC became appointment television. The mania peaked when Glassman and Hassett published Dow 36,000 in 1999, claiming equities were still grossly undervalued. Siegel publicly disavowed their conclusion. When the Nasdaq crashed 78 percent and the dot-com index fell over 95 percent, the bears emerged with calls for Dow 5,000. Neither extreme proved correct.

The psychology of investing, Siegel argues, is the real barrier to wealth. His publisher told him pessimism outsells optimism three to one. Forecasters like Joe Granville and Elaine Garzarelli became famous for single correct calls, then remained stubbornly wrong for years afterward. At a dinner party in February 2020, a physician sold his entire portfolio just before Covid struck—a brilliant call—but then never re-entered the market, missing the fastest recovery in history. No one has ever made money in the long run betting against stocks.

Chapter 2: Data Sources and Total Returns

Siegel’s data spans three distinct periods. From 1802 to 1871, the evidence comes from a decade-long study by Goetzmann and Ibbotson, who compiled monthly prices and dividends on over six hundred securities. From 1871 to 1925, the Cowles Foundation capitalization-weighted NYSE index provides the backbone. From 1926 onward, the CRSP database at the University of Chicago covers all NYSE, Amex, and Nasdaq stocks, free of survivorship bias.

Edward McQuarrie challenged the earliest period, arguing that including the First and Second Banks of the United States—which dominated the cap-weighted index and provided little diversification—lowers the 1802–1871 returns from 6.9 to 5.4 percent. This reduces the 220-year average to 6.4 percent but does not touch the last 187 years of data.

The stability of real returns across wildly different economic regimes is perhaps the most striking finding in all of finance. From 1802 to 1870 the real return was 6.7 percent; from 1871 to 1925 it was 6.6 percent; from 1926 to 2021 it was 7.1 percent. The American economy transformed from agricultural to industrial to service-based to technology-driven. Communication went from weeks-long journeys to instantaneous broadcast. The monetary system shifted from gold to paper. Through it all, stocks delivered essentially the same real return.

Fixed-income assets tell the opposite story. Treasury bill real returns collapsed from 5.1 percent in the early nineteenth century to just 0.4 percent since 1926. From 1946 to 1981, the real return on Treasury bonds was actually negative. The equity risk premium—the gap between stock and bond returns—has averaged 3.3 percent over long-term bonds and 4.3 percent over short-term. This premium is so persistent that economists have dubbed it the “equity premium puzzle,” generating hundreds of academic papers trying to explain why rational investors demand such a large reward for holding stocks.

International evidence confirms the pattern. Elroy Dimson, Paul Marsh, and Mike Staunton compiled returns for nineteen countries since 1900 in their landmark study Triumph of the Optimists. Every single country exhibited positive real stock returns despite wars, hyperinflation, and depression. The world average was 5.3 percent—lower than the American figure but still substantial. Concerns about survivorship bias are “somewhat overstated.”

Chapter 3: Risk, Return, and Portfolio Allocation

“The man or woman who invests in bonds is speculating in the general level of prices, or the purchasing power of money.” — Irving Fisher, 1912

Conventional wisdom holds that stocks are riskier than bonds. Over one-year periods, this is unambiguously true—stocks have swung from a best of +66.6 percent to a worst of -38.6 percent. But as the holding period lengthens, something remarkable happens. Over ten-year windows, the worst stock performance actually beats the worst bond performance. Over twenty years, stocks have never once delivered a negative real return, while bonds fell more than three percent per year below inflation from 1961 to 1981, destroying nearly half their real purchasing power.

Over 20+ year periods, stocks have never lost purchasing power. Bonds have. The worst 30-year stock return (2.6%) still matches average bond performance.

The probability of stocks outperforming bonds rises steadily with time: roughly sixty percent over one to two years, seventy-five percent over ten, eighty-five over twenty, and over ninety-one percent over thirty years. There has been only a single thirty-year period (1982–2011) where bonds beat stocks, and that was driven by the freakish circumstance of yields above sixteen percent falling to historic lows—a trajectory that cannot possibly be repeated from current yield levels.

The technical explanation is mean reversion. If stock prices followed a pure random walk, their standard deviation would decline only by the square root of the holding period. In reality, the standard deviation of equity returns falls nearly twice as fast as that. Over thirty-year periods, the standard deviation of stock returns actually drops below three-fourths that of bonds. Poterba and Summers and Fama and French confirmed this pattern in the late 1980s, and over ninety percent of financial economists now agree that equity returns display long-term mean reversion. Bonds, by contrast, exhibit mean aversion—once returns deviate, they tend to keep deviating, especially when inflation takes hold.

The practical implication is that the optimal allocation to stocks increases with the investment horizon. For a one-year holder, the minimum-risk portfolio is nearly all bonds. At five years, stocks should constitute about twenty-five percent. At twenty years, more than half. At thirty years, the optimal stock allocation reaches sixty-eight percent. William Bengen’s famous 1994 study recommended fifty to seventy-five percent equities for retirees. Under Monte Carlo simulations with lower expected returns (stocks at 4.5 percent, bonds at negative 0.5 percent), the optimal retirement portfolio shifts to ninety-five or even one hundred percent stocks.

The longer your investment horizon, the more stocks you should hold. For a 30-year investor, the historically optimal minimum-risk portfolio is 68% stocks — and many researchers argue for even more.

Paul Samuelson proved in 1965 that if stock prices follow a random walk, the long-horizon investor should hold no more stocks than the short-horizon investor. Under a random walk, there is no “time diversification.” But the data demolish the random walk assumption: mean reversion dominates over long periods. The correlation between stock and bond returns has shifted dramatically across eras. From 1926 to 1965, the correlation was barely positive (+0.05), making bonds excellent diversifiers. From 1966 to 1997, OPEC supply shocks pushed the correlation to +0.36 as stocks and bonds fell together. Since 1998, the correlation has turned negative (-0.28), with government bonds serving as safe havens during crises. Javier Estrada, studying nineteen countries over 110 years, concluded that a one-hundred-percent stock retirement portfolio “should be seriously considered.”

“Historical data show that we can be more certain of the purchasing power of a diversified portfolio of common stocks thirty years hence than we can of the buying power of a thirty-year US Treasury bond.” — Jeremy Siegel

Chapter 4: Global Investing

The case for international diversification begins with a cautionary tale. Japan was the star of international investing through the 1980s. The Nikkei surged from 2,000 to 39,000 over two decades, and NTT alone traded at a P/E above 300. By the end of 1989, Japan’s market represented forty percent of worldwide equity value, surpassing the United States for the first time since the early 1900s. Leo Melamed, the president of the Chicago Mercantile Exchange, traveled to Japan and asked how such valuations could be sustained. The response: “You don’t understand. We’ve moved to an entirely new way of valuing stocks.” Melamed knew they were doomed. The Nikkei fell to 7,000 by 2008—less than twenty percent of its peak.

The BRIC phenomenon followed a similar arc. James O’Neill at Goldman Sachs coined the acronym in 2001, and BRIC stocks soared six hundred percent from 2003 to 2007. Then they stalled. At the end of 2021 the index sat below its high from fourteen years earlier. Mean reversion, once again, ruled.

Siegel identifies three reasons to diversify internationally. First, the definition of “foreign” is increasingly arbitrary—a company’s headquarters says little about where it produces or sells. Second, the United States imports $3.5 trillion in goods; if those products are in demand, the companies making them belong in your portfolio. Third, and most fundamentally, markets do not move in lockstep, and that asynchrony is the essence of diversification. He recommends at least one-third of an equity portfolio in international stocks, noting that the US market is the most expensive major market by valuation ratios.

The numbers tell the story of concentration risk. By the end of 2021, total world equity value stood at roughly $70 trillion, with the United States commanding 61.2 percent, developed Europe 16.1 percent, and Japan just 5.5 percent. Emerging markets accounted for $7.8 trillion, led by China at thirty-two percent, Taiwan at sixteen, and South Korea and India at twelve each. From 1970 to 2021, US dollar returns for the world market averaged 10.0 percent, with EAFE (Europe, Australasia, Far East) at 9.36 and the US at 10.83. Correlations between EAFE and US markets ran at sixty-five percent—high, but far from perfect.

Currency hedging adds another dimension. The British pound fell from $4.80 to $1.60 over the past century, yet unhedged investors earned higher returns than hedged ones. Over long periods, purchasing power parity tends to equalize currency moves. In the short run, hedging reduces dollar volatility—EAFE hedged returned 5.4 percent versus 5.0 unhedged from 1988 to 2021, with lower risk. But the costs depend on interest rate differentials between countries, and for long-horizon investors they may not be worth paying.

Sector composition varies dramatically across regions. Over fifty percent of US stocks were in technology by the end of 2021; no other region comes close. The 2021 top seven firms—Apple, Microsoft, Alphabet, Amazon, Tesla, Meta, NVIDIA—were all tech-related. As recently as 2013, four of the top seven were not technology companies (ExxonMobil, GE, Johnson & Johnson, Chevron). It is likely, Siegel argues, that many of today’s tech champions will eventually fall from their exalted heights. Only a fully diversified world portfolio will reap the best returns with the lowest risk.

Part II: Stock Returns — Measurement and Valuation

Chapter 5: Stock Indexes

Charles Dow created the first stock average in 1885 with just twelve stocks, ten of them railroads. The Dow Jones Industrial Average as we know it launched on May 26, 1896. It is price-weighted, which means that a company’s influence depends on its share price rather than its size. By 2022 this produced absurdities: UnitedHealth Group represented nine percent of the index while Intel contributed less than one percent, and Tesla and Amazon were excluded entirely because their share prices were too high.

The S&P 500, born in 1957 and capitalization-weighted, became the universal benchmark. It originated with the Standard Statistics Company in 1906 and encompasses roughly ninety percent of all US stock value. The Nasdaq, launched in 1971 as an automated system for over-the-counter stocks, attracted technology firms with lower costs and less stringent listing requirements. Intel and Microsoft chose to stay on Nasdaq even when they qualified for the NYSE.

The most sobering statistic in the chapter concerns individual stocks. Hendrik Bessembinder studied the entire CRSP database and found that only 50.8 percent of individual stocks produced positive lifetime returns. The median stock’s ninety-year return was just 9.5 percent—less than one percent of what Treasury bills returned over the same period. Only 4 percent of stocks beat the value-weighted market. Broad diversification is the only guaranteed way to capture the market’s superior long-term returns.

Stock market gains are driven by a tiny fraction of companies. Nearly half of all individual stocks have negative lifetime returns, making broad diversification through index funds essential.

The Nasdaq’s story is particularly dramatic. It took ten years to double from its initial level to 200, another ten to reach 500, and first crossed 1,000 in July 1995. Then came the frenzy: from October 1999 to March 2000, the Nasdaq surged from 2,700 to a peak of 5,048. It crashed to 1,150 by October 2002. By 1998, Nasdaq capitalization exceeded the Tokyo Stock Exchange. By 1994, Nasdaq share volume had surpassed the NYSE, and five years later dollar volume followed. At the market peak in March 2000, total Nasdaq value approached six trillion dollars—more than half the NYSE and more than any other exchange in the world. By the end of 2021, it had recovered to a record above 16,000.

The original Dow 12 stocks of 1896 were centered on commodities: cotton, sugar, tobacco, lead, leather, rubber. Their corporate lineages trace fascinating paths through American business history. American Cotton Oil became Best Food, then CPC International, then Bestfoods, and was finally acquired by Unilever in 2000 for $20.3 billion—the most highly valued successor at roughly $100 billion in 2021. Distilling and Cattle Feeding underwent multiple transformations before becoming LyondellBasell Industries, which still manufactures ethanol. American Tobacco eventually became Fortune Brands, purchased by the Japanese distillery Suntory in 2014. The CRSP database, created by Professor James Lorie at the University of Chicago at Merrill Lynch’s request in 1959, became the first machine-readable stock price file and the foundation for modern financial research.

Chapter 6: The S&P 500

The sectoral composition of the S&P 500 has undergone a tectonic shift. In 1957, materials and energy accounted for nearly half the index. By 2021, they represented barely five percent. Financial, health care, and technology stocks—just six percent of the original index—grew to more than half. Yet sector growth does not predict returns. Energy shrank by more than eighty-five percent but its returns trailed the S&P by less than one percentage point. All nine oil companies among the twenty largest original members finished in the top ten performers over sixty-four years.

A complete reversal in 64 years. Sectors that dominated in 1957 (materials, energy) shrank to 5%, while sectors that barely existed (tech, finance, healthcare) now compose over half the index. Yet shrinking sectors sometimes delivered superior returns.

A 2005 study by Jeremy Schwartz revealed something startling: simply holding the original 500 companies from 1957 outperformed the actual S&P 500 by roughly one percentage point per year. New additions had faster earnings growth, but the price paid for that growth was typically too high. The all-time top performer was Philip Morris (Altria), which returned 18.02 percent annualized from 1957 to 2021—turning a thousand dollars into over forty-five million.

Among the original Dow 12 companies of 1896, only U.S. Leather was a total loss. Its president, Lowell Birrell, fled to Brazil after looting the company’s assets. American Cotton Oil, through a chain of acquisitions, eventually became part of Unilever, valued at roughly $100 billion by 2021. General Electric, a member of the Dow for 122 years, declined seventy percent under Jeffrey Immelt before being removed in 2018.

The “top dogs”—companies with the highest market capitalization at any given moment—tell their own story. AT&T held the top spot for 456 months, including 251 consecutive months from 1929 to 1950. IBM held it for 236 months, GE for 117, Standard Oil for 116, and Apple for 103. Average one-year and ten-year returns of top dogs beat the S&P 500, but the outperformance fades at thirty years. IBM is the cautionary tale: it dramatically outperformed for one year after reaching the top, then significantly underperformed. Philip Morris spent only a single month as the most valuable company, yet it was the only true long-term winner. “No tree grows to the sky,” Siegel reminds us, “and no bull market lasts forever.”

The Melville Shoe transformation is one of the book’s most delightful anecdotes. A struggling shoe manufacturer purchased Consumer Value Stores in 1969 on a whim. That serendipitous acquisition eventually transformed the company into CVS, now the largest retail pharmacy chain in the United States. A woeful business in a dying industry accidentally stumbled into one of the great American retail franchises.

Chapter 7: Sources of Shareholder Value

Stock prices are the present value of future dividends, not future earnings. John Burr Williams made this point in 1938: “Earnings are only a means to an end, and the means should not be mistaken for the end.” Share buybacks serve as a tax-efficient alternative to dividends because capital gains are not taxed until the shares are sold. Critics who claim buybacks substitute for capital investment are wrong—restricting buybacks would increase cash dividends, not capital expenditure.

“Dividends not only dwarf inflation, growth, and changing valuation levels individually, but they also dwarf the combined importance of inflation, growth, and changing valuation levels.” — Robert Arnott, 2003

The historical decomposition of real returns since 1871 is illuminating: 4.3 percent from dividend yield, 2.5 percent from capital gains, and 0.3 percent from P/E expansion. Before World War II, firms paid out two-thirds of their earnings and had to issue new shares to fund growth, diluting per-share returns. After the war, they retained more earnings, funded growth internally, and per-share growth accelerated dramatically.

Earnings measurement has become increasingly problematic. GAAP rules now require marking assets to market but forbid writing them back up, creating wild volatility. Warren Buffett called the resulting swings in Berkshire’s reported earnings “worthless” for analytical purposes. During 2008–2009, GAAP earnings declined ninety-two percent—far more than in the Great Depression, even though GDP fell only a fifth as much.

The Gordon dividend discount model (P = d/(r-g)) provides the theoretical backbone. It shows that dividend policy does not affect stock price if the firm earns the same return on retained earnings as shareholders require. Berkshire Hathaway pays no dividends yet maintains enormous value because its owners know the assets could be distributed at any time. The model also explains why buybacks are neutral to price: they do not change the current price but increase per-share growth by reducing the share count.

A secondary driver of the buyback boom is management compensation. Stock options are typically based on share price alone, not total return including dividends. This creates an incentive to repurchase shares (which boosts earnings per share and thus the stock price) rather than pay dividends (which benefit shareholders but do not directly increase the option’s value). Roughly sixty-five percent of quarterly earnings reports beat consensus estimates—recently seventy to seventy-five percent—because firms tilt their guidance pessimistically to manage expectations.

Chapter 8: Interest Rates and Stock Prices

Real interest rates across the developed world fell from two to four percent at the turn of the century to negative territory by 2021. The popular explanation—“easy money”—is largely wrong. Central banks set short-term rates, but long-term real rates are driven by real economic forces: slowing population growth, declining productivity, and aging populations.

World fertility rates have surprised demographers by plunging well below replacement almost everywhere. India reached 2.0 overall (1.6 in cities) by 2021. The old-age dependency ratio in South Korea is forecast to hit thirty-eight percent by 2050, the highest in the world. US productivity growth slowed from 2.2 percent in 2000–2010 to just 0.9 percent in 2010–2020 despite enormous technological advancement.

The most powerful yet most overlooked factor pushing rates down is the increasingly negative correlation between stocks and bonds. After 2000, government bonds became excellent hedges during crises—gaining thirty-eight percent during the 2008 crash while stocks fell thirty-seven percent. John Campbell estimated that the shift in correlation alone could account for a three-percentage-point decline in long-term interest rates. This hedge value drives enormous demand for bonds, pushing yields down independent of monetary policy.

The Austrian-Swedish-American theory of interest rates, developed by Böhm-Bawerk, Wicksell, and Fisher, identifies three components driving real rates: economic growth, time preference, and risk. All three are trending downward. US population growth in 2020–2021 was just 393,000—the lowest in over a hundred years. Per capita GDP growth slowed from 2.21 percent (1970–2000) to just 1.04 percent (2010–2020) in the US, and from 2.32 to a dismal 0.33 percent in the Eurozone.

Knut Wicksell coined the concept of the “natural rate” of interest, determined by real economic forces entirely outside central bank control. If a central bank holds its rate below the natural rate, inflation erupts; if above, deflation results. The relationship between interest rates and stock prices is complicated. If rates fall because of lower growth, future cash flows are also reduced, making the net effect on stocks ambiguous. Short-run Fed easing is generally positive for stocks, provided it does not signal excessive economic weakness.

Chapter 9: Inflation and Stock Prices

“By a process of inflation, government can confiscate, secretly and unobserved, the wealth of their citizens.” — John Maynard Keynes

In 1950, President Truman predicted family incomes would reach twelve thousand dollars by the year 2000. The actual figure was $41,349, but that bought less than six thousand 1950 dollars. Real incomes had merely doubled despite a twelvefold nominal increase. This is what inflation does: it turns impressive- sounding numbers into modest realities.

Over the long run, stocks are excellent inflation hedges. The post–World War II period—the most inflationary in American history—produced real stock returns that exceeded the prior 150 years. Over thirty-year holding periods, stock returns remain stable at roughly six to seven percent regardless of the inflation regime. The Gordon formula explains why: inflation raises both the discount rate and expected dividend growth by equal amounts, and the effects cancel.

The short-run story is entirely different. In the 1970s, OPEC supply shocks drove energy costs skyward while firms could not raise output prices fast enough. Real stock prices (the Dow, inflation-adjusted) fell sixty-five percent from the January 1966 peak. Half of Americans feared a repeat of the 1930s Depression. Supply-side inflation is genuinely destructive to equities because it compresses profit margins rather than simply inflating both revenues and costs.

The tax code makes inflation even more corrosive. Capital gains are taxed on nominal appreciation with no adjustment for inflation; at three percent inflation over a five-year holding period, the extra tax cost amounts to roughly sixty basis points per year. On the corporate side, depreciation is understated (since replacement costs exceed historical cost), and inventory profits are overstated. The overall balance depends on leverage, but the tax system is not adequately indexed for inflation.

Since 1955, not a single year has seen US consumer prices decline. The shift from the gold standard to paper money removed the natural constraint on money supply and inflation. M2, the monetary aggregate most closely linked to inflation according to Milton Friedman, increased by more than twenty percent in 2020— the greatest single-year increase in over 150 years of data. The resulting inflation surge to seven percent confirmed the historical relationship that Friedman had documented decades earlier.

Chapter 10: Valuation Yardsticks

Until 1958, dividend yields on stocks always exceeded bond yields. When stocks first yielded less than bonds, BusinessWeek called it an “evil omen.” An investor who sold on that signal would have waited fifty years to re-enter. Over that half-century, real stock returns averaged six percent annually. The reversal was not a warning but a permanent shift: chronic inflation made investors view stocks as real assets and bonds as not.

The CAPE ratio (cyclically adjusted P/E), developed by Robert Campbell and Robert Shiller, averages ten years of real earnings and correctly signaled overvaluation at the 2000 peak. But since 2008 it has been almost perpetually bearish, forecasting below-average returns while actual returns were well above average. From 1981 to 2021, in all but four months actual ten-year returns exceeded CAPE forecasts. Even Shiller acknowledged in 2022 that “we don’t know the right CAPE ratio. We’re constantly moving into a new era.”

The CAPE ratio peaked at 43 before the dot-com crash (2000) and at 33 before the 1929 crash. Since 2008 it has remained above its historical median of 15, persistently forecasting poor returns that never materialized.

“I doubt that the devil himself knows what is the equilibrium P/E ratio on stocks.” — Paul Samuelson

The Buffett Indicator (market cap to GDP) reached an all-time high in 2021, but the metric fails to account for overseas revenues (roughly half of S&P 500 profits) and the shift from private to public ownership. Tobin’s Q is equally flawed: Microsoft’s book value was $22 against a market price of $300, and Apple’s was $4.40 versus $160. In an economy dominated by intangible assets, book value has lost most of its meaning.

If the required real return on stocks has fallen from the historical seven percent to five percent—due to lower transaction costs, better diversification, and deeper markets—then the equilibrium P/E rises from about fourteen to about twenty. The equity premium puzzle, first identified by Mehra and Prescott in 1985, asks why investors demand a premium far larger than standard economic models predict.

The Fed Model, named after a 1997 paper by three Federal Reserve researchers, compares the earnings yield to the thirty-year bond rate. It showed remarkable correspondence from 1960 to 2000: stocks appeared most overvalued in August 1987 (just before the crash) and most undervalued in the early 1980s (just before the great bull market). Its major flaw is that it compares nominal variables when stocks are real assets. The relationship is weak outside its core period. But at extremes it can be useful: at the 2000 peak, it showed a rare negative risk premium, with the earnings yield at three percent versus TIPS yields above four.

Profit margins have risen to fifty-year highs, driven by low interest rates, lower corporate taxes, technology-sector margins, and foreign sales. Excluding technology, margins drop from 13.1 to 10.6 percent. Charles Jones of Columbia University documented that one-way trading costs fell from over one percent before 1975 to under 0.18 percent by 2002. These lower friction costs reduce the required return and support structurally higher P/E ratios. As Bosland warned as early as 1936, widespread knowledge of stock profitability causes competitive bidding that raises prices and diminishes future gains.

Part III: Market Analysis

Chapter 11: Individual Stocks vs. the Market

Consider the IBM versus Standard Oil paradox. A thousand dollars invested in IBM in 1950 grew to fifteen million; the same amount in Standard Oil of New Jersey reached thirty-three million. The oil company won despite inferior revenue growth because its valuation was consistently lower and its dividend yield three points higher. Reinvested dividends bought 11.87 times as many shares in Standard Oil compared to 3.63 times for IBM.

“Don’t fall in love with a company, fall in love with its price.” — Wall Street adage

Across twenty-one countries from 1900 to 2020, GDP growth and stock returns are negatively correlated. China posted 9.3 percent GDP growth from 1992 to 2020 but the lowest stock returns among eighteen emerging markets. Denmark delivered the highest developed-market returns alongside the second-lowest GDP growth. Growth requires capital, which means share dilution, and productivity gains tend to accrue to workers rather than shareholders.

The greatest stock of all time is Philip Morris: 16.02 percent compound annual return from 1926 to 2021, turning a thousand dollars into $1.33 billion. The lawsuits that cost roughly a hundred billion dollars actually helped long-term shareholders by depressing the stock price and allowing reinvested dividends to buy shares cheaply. The tobacco industry overall returned 14.6 percent annually from 1900 to 2014—over 160 times the market.

The story of Thatcher Glass illustrates how corporate evolution can defy expectations. A milk bottle manufacturer whose core business was sinking, the company acquired a chain that eventually led it into Philip Morris’s orbit. One hundred shares bought at the original price grew to $40 million. Kansas City Southern, the second-best performer since 1926, stayed exclusively in railroads. Great management kept the business running, but investors never placed high valuations on rail stocks, which kept prices low and allowed reinvested dividends to accumulate at bargain prices.

Amazon.com provides the contrarian case. In 1999, Siegel polled a roomful of finance experts on which dot-com was most likely to fail. Amazon drew “snickers of agreement.” Barron’s ran an “Amazon.bomb” cover story. The stock fell from $59 to $5.51—a decline of more than ninety percent—and took nearly ten more years to surpass its 1999 high. Yet for those who held through the carnage, it became one of the greatest wealth-creating machines in history.

Chapter 12: Is Value Investing Dead?

Benjamin Graham and David Dodd invented value investing with Security Analysis in 1934. Graham’s metaphor of Mr. Market—an emotional, manic-depressive partner who offers to buy or sell at wildly fluctuating prices—remains one of the most powerful mental models in finance. His best disciple, Warren Buffett, compiled a record that is statistically impossible to attribute to luck: a one-in-five-hundred-thousand probability.

From 1951 to 2021, the highest earnings-yield quintile returned 15.64 percent versus 9.75 percent for the lowest—a gap of nearly six percentage points per year. But from 2006 onward, value suffered an unprecedented drought. Using Fama-French book-value criteria, the drawdown reached fifty-five percent over 13.5 years, more than double any previous episode. The Nifty Fifty collapse of the early 1970s and the dot-com bust had each restored value investing within a few years. This time was different.

The reasons form a perfect storm: technology stocks proved genuinely undervalued after their 2000 crash; financials were devastated by the 2008 crisis; oil collapsed due to fracking and ESG divestment; and Covid massively boosted tech earnings. FAANG stocks went from roughly one trillion in aggregate value in 2013 to ten trillion by 2021—more than a quarter of the S&P 500. The P/E gap between growth and value widened rather than narrowed, suggesting the underperformance was not being arbitraged away.

Over 70 years, cheap stocks (highest earnings yield) outperformed expensive stocks by nearly 6 percentage points annually. But from 2006 to 2021, this pattern reversed for the longest stretch in history.

Book value itself has deteriorated as a metric. In 1975, intangible assets represented just seventeen percent of S&P 500 value. By 2015 the figure was eighty-four percent. Apple’s book value was a few dollars per share against a market price many times higher. Buybacks further reduce book value, making the metric nearly meaningless for modern technology firms.

The historical episodes of value underperformance follow a recurring pattern. In the early 1970s, institutional buying of the Nifty Fifty pushed their average P/E above 40 by December 1972—twice the S&P 500. The list included genuine winners like Philip Morris and Coca-Cola but also Polaroid and Sears, which never recovered. The dot-com bubble featured technology P/E ratios far higher than the Nifty Fifty, and when the Nasdaq fell nearly eighty percent, value investing was resurrected. But value continued outperforming only until its peak in 2006, after which the unprecedented drought began.

One explanation for value’s struggle is that growth stocks are longer-duration assets, meaning they benefit disproportionately from falling interest rates. But the historical evidence is weak: the greatest growth-stock burst (1999–2000) occurred during a period of high real rates. Another explanation centers on ESG investing—the avoidance of oil stocks and preference for technology contributed to value’s underperformance. Technology grew from nineteen to forty percent of the S&P 500, with tech earnings compounding at 12.7 percent annually, more than five points above the rest of the market.

Chapter 13: Market Efficiency or Noisy Markets?

The efficient market hypothesis, coined by Harry Roberts in 1967 and popularized by Eugene Fama, holds that all available information is already incorporated into stock prices. Under this theory and its companion, the capital asset pricing model, beta is the only relevant measure of risk and the capitalization-weighted market portfolio is optimal for every investor.

The hypothesis took a major blow in 1992 when Fama and French themselves showed that size and book-to-market value were more important than beta in explaining returns. Fama called the CAPM “not a useful approximation.” Grossman and Stiglitz demonstrated theoretically that perfectly efficient markets are impossible: if prices always reflected true value, no one would have an incentive to pay for the research that makes them efficient.

Siegel proposes his own noisy market hypothesis: prices deviate from fundamentals due to noise traders acting on imperfect information, liquidity needs, tax considerations, and speculation. It is not easy to beat index funds, but opportunities exist. Fischer Black, himself a strong supporter of the EMH, conceded in his 1986 presidential address that “efficient” means price within a factor of two of value—a remarkably wide band.

“Markets can stay irrational longer than investors can remain solvent.” — paraphrase of Keynes

Short-selling restrictions create a persistent asymmetry. Short sellers face potentially unlimited losses, while long investors can lose at most one hundred percent. Stambaugh, Yu, and Yuan found that nearly seventy percent of long-short strategy returns come from the short leg. When meme stocks driven by Reddit’s WallStreetBets forced hedge funds to cover at enormous losses, it demonstrated just how dangerous and unreliable the short side remains.

A well-known fund manager confided to Siegel that even if his strategy were ultimately vindicated in the long run, clients who abandon the fund during tough times never come back. Shleifer and Vishny formalized this insight: career risks loom large for institutional managers facing short-term performance pressure. An investor who holds employer stock in a pension fund faces double risk if the firm fails—losing both the job and the retirement savings. Kogan, Papanikolaou, and Stoffman argued that investors buy growth stocks specifically to hedge against “creative destruction” that might hurt their employment prospects.

Chapter 14: The Factor Zoo

John Cochrane used his 2011 presidential address to the American Finance Association to call the explosion of documented return factors a “zoo.” The original Fama-French three-factor model (market, size, valuation) expanded to five factors with profitability and investment, and the academic literature identified hundreds more. Most of these factors faded or disappeared entirely from 2006 to 2021.

The size effect, discovered by Rolf Banz in 1981, showed small stocks outperforming large ones by four percentage points per year. But strip out the extraordinary 1975–1983 period—when small stocks returned 35.3 percent annually while large caps earned 15.7—and the premium virtually vanishes. The January effect that once explained all of small stocks’ excess returns has also disappeared.

Momentum is the strongest factor over the full historical period: stocks that outperformed in the prior twelve months tend to continue outperforming over the next twelve. Fama himself called it “the primary embarrassment to market efficiency.” Yet momentum carries devastating downsides: high turnover, sharp drawdowns at bear market bottoms, and significant fading since 2006. In Japan, momentum returns are actually negative.

Liquidity may be the most intellectually robust factor. Amihud and Mendelson showed that less-liquid stocks outperform, and Ibbotson confirmed it: the least-traded quartile returned 14.5 percent annually, double the most-traded quartile. Investors who do not need crisis liquidity should tilt toward less-liquid securities.

The investment factor shows that firms expanding capital most aggressively tend to deliver lower returns. Warren Buffett explained in 1985 why he refused textile investment at Berkshire: “each proposal looked like an immediate winner” but competitors would neutralize any gains. Jim Collins, author of Good to Great, asked CEOs whether they maintained a “stop doing” list alongside their to-do list. Share issuance, documented by Jay Ritter in the early 1990s, also predicted lower subsequent returns, while buybacks predicted superior ones.

The profitability and quality factor, championed by Novy-Marx in 2013, shows that profitable firms strongly predict future growth and work especially well when combined with value. Higher-than-normal accruals predict lower forward returns. Stambaugh and Yuan combined eleven factors into a single measure that outperformed the Fama-French five-factor model. Internationally, every region except Japan shows average factor returns larger than those in the US, with momentum strongest everywhere except Japan (where it is negative) and quality/profitability strong across the board.

Part IV: Styles, Trends, and the Calendar

Chapter 15: ESG Investing

In September 1970, Milton Friedman published his famous New York Times essay arguing that the sole social responsibility of business is to increase profits. That doctrine shaped corporate governance for two generations. In 2019, the Business Roundtable reversed course, declaring a commitment to all stakeholders. Marc Benioff of Salesforce charged that Friedman had “brainwashed a generation of CEOs.”

ESG investing has become one of the fastest-growing styles, with seventeen trillion dollars of assets under management by 2020. Rating agencies like MSCI and Sustainalytics evaluate firms on hundreds of criteria. ESG can enhance value through better employee morale, consumer willingness to pay premiums for ethical products, and higher valuation multiples for environmentally responsible firms. At Bank of America, S&P 500 firms with below-median carbon emissions traded at 5.1 times book value versus 4.2 for above-median emitters.

But there is a paradox. If ESG stocks command higher valuations, their future returns will be lower. The best long-term American stock is Philip Morris, which would score poorly on any ESG screen. Dimson, Marsh, and Staunton found that tobacco outperformed all other industries from 1900 onward. Virtuous stocks may be short-run winners, but historically sinner stocks have taken first place.

Siegel lays out two mechanisms by which ESG premiums arise even when profits decrease. The first is psychic pleasure: investors derive personal satisfaction from owning virtuous companies and willingly accept diminished monetary returns. The second is risk hedging: green stocks function as insurance against climate deterioration. If environmental conditions worsen, green companies should benefit while carbon-heavy ones suffer. Both mechanisms push ESG stock prices higher than fundamentals alone would justify, creating a headwind for future returns.

The data on corporate behavior is revealing. The three largest carbon-emitting sectors— utilities, energy, and industrials—show that firms with carbon-neutral targets sport P/E ratios fifty percent higher than their sector peers. Germany issued two identical government bonds, one designated to fund ESG projects; the ESG bond sold at a higher price, accepting a lower yield. Past ESG performance is misleading: firms improving their ESG scores outperformed (21.9 percent annual versus 16.5 for the worst decliners), but detecting these improvers before the market recognizes them is the real challenge. The Friedman doctrine, Siegel suggests, may not actually conflict with ESG goals—by doing “good,” executives might indeed do very “well” for their shareholders.

Chapter 16: Technical Analysis and Investing with the Trend

Burton Malkiel declared in A Random Walk Down Wall Street that “the central proposition of charting is absolutely false.” Academic opinion has since softened. The 200-day moving average has demonstrated genuine ability to reduce portfolio volatility, even if it does not reliably improve total returns.

Siegel tested the strategy rigorously from 1886 to 2020: buy when the Dow closes more than one percent above its 200-day average, sell when it falls more than one percent below. The result: essentially the same return as buy-and-hold (9.66 versus 9.62 percent) but with standard deviation four percentage points lower and the investor in the market only sixty-four percent of the time. The strategy’s greatest triumph was the 1929 crash, where it would have exited on October 19 at 323 and re-entered in August 1932 near the absolute bottom.

The major drawback is whipsawing in trendless markets. From 2001 to 2020, the strategy lagged buy-and-hold by three percentage points per year. Twenty switches in 2010–2012 alone cost roughly twenty percentage points. The strategy functions like an insurance policy: it cushions bear markets but imposes a steady cost during normal times. It requires full-time attention—missing the close on Friday, October 16, 1987, meant being swept into Black Monday’s twenty-two percent nightmare.

The origins of technical analysis trace back to Charles Dow himself, who saw stock prices moving like ocean waves—a primary wave (the overall trend) with secondary waves superimposed. William Hamilton extended the approach in The Stock Market Barometer (1922), and Robert Rhea formalized it in Dow Theory (1932). Martin J. Pring calculated that Dow Theory followers from 1897 to 1990 would have turned $100 into $116,000 versus just $6,000 for buy-and-hold. The catch: buy-sell signals are “purely subjective” and cannot be determined by precise numerical rules.

The randomness of stock prices poses a fundamental challenge to all charting. Frederick MacCauley noted in 1925 the striking similarity between market fluctuations and curves from throwing dice. Harry Roberts at the University of Chicago generated simulated random price changes that looked identical to real stock charts. When the charts were shown to top Wall Street brokers, they demonstrated no ability to distinguish actual market data from computer-generated randomness.

“A moment’s thought will show that there can be no such thing as a scientific prediction of economic events under human control.” — Benjamin Graham

Chapter 17: Calendar Anomalies

The January effect was discovered in the early 1980s by Donald Keim, then a graduate student at the University of Chicago. From 1925 to 2021, small stocks averaged a 4.93 percent return in January alone versus 1.29 percent for the S&P 500. During the Great Depression, small stocks posted January returns of thirteen, twenty-one, and ten percent in consecutive years. Since 1995, however, the January effect has vanished entirely—likely because publicity about it motivated traders to exploit it in advance.

September is by far the worst month for stocks globally. One dollar invested exclusively in September since 1885 would be worth twenty-three cents today. The same dollar invested every month except September: $6,167. Twenty-one of twenty-two countries showed negative September returns from 1970 to 2021. Unlike the January effect, September remains operative. Virtually all market gains are realized between October and April, lending empirical support to the adage “sell in May and go away.”

The leading explanation for the January effect is tax-loss selling: individual investors dump losing small stocks in December to claim tax deductions, and the stocks bounce back in January when selling pressure evaporates. The evidence supports this—stocks that have fallen throughout the year tend to fall even further in December, then rise dramatically in January. But the theory has a problem: the January effect existed in countries without capital gains taxes, including Japan before 1989 and Canada before 1972.

Other calendar patterns abound, many of which have shifted over time. Returns between Christmas and New Year’s average nearly ten times the typical daily return. The first half of each month produces returns almost three times the second half, with the first trading day especially positive (driven by 401(k) inflows). Since 1995, several patterns have reversed entirely: Friday went from the best day to the worst as traders hedge over the weekend, and the last trading day of the year switched from positive to decisively negative due to sell-on-close orders. The diurnal pattern shows prices sinking in the morning (especially Monday), firming at midday, and rising in the last half hour of trading.

Perhaps the most telling observation is that April has been the best month for stocks, while since 1994, August (not September) has become the worst month as traders try to front-run the September effect. The anomaly hunters keep finding patterns, and the patterns keep shifting just enough to frustrate anyone trying to trade them systematically. As Mark Twain warned, every month is peculiarly dangerous for speculation.

Part V: Stocks, the Economy, and Policy

Chapter 18: Money and Monetary Policy

The history of American monetary policy is a story of abandoning gold in stages. Britain left the gold standard in September 1931. When the United States followed in April 1933, stocks soared nine percent in a single day. The post-war Bretton Woods system maintained a gold peg at $35 per ounce for foreign central banks. When President Johnson vowed in 1965 that there could be “no question” of maintaining gold at $35, the system had perhaps six years left. Nixon closed the gold window on August 15, 1971. Stocks jumped four percent on record volume.

Paul Volcker’s appointment in April 1979 marked a turning point. On Saturday, October 6, the Fed announced it would target the money supply rather than interest rates. The federal funds rate rocketed to a record twenty percent. Stocks fell eight percent in two and a half days. But restricting money growth proved to be the only real answer to inflation.

Siegel devotes careful attention to Bitcoin. A thousand-dollar investment in 2009 would have been worth roughly a billion dollars by December 2021—no other publicly traded asset has appreciated so much. But Bitcoin fails as a unit of account (virtually no prices are quoted in it), remains cumbersome as a medium of exchange, and correlates far more with stocks than with gold during crises. If goods ever were priced in cryptocurrency, the monetary system would resemble the gold standard: no inflationary bias but no ability to provide crisis liquidity either.

The Fed controls money supply through open market operations—buying and selling government bonds. When the Fed buys a bond, it credits the reserve account of the seller’s bank, creating money out of nothing. When it sells, reserves disappear. The federal funds rate—the interest rate at which reserves trade—forms the anchor for all short-term rates and is directly or indirectly the basis for trillions of dollars in loans. Since the Great Financial Crisis, the Fed has operated an ample-reserve regime where the funds rate is set by the interest the Fed pays on reserves rather than through traditional open market operations.

The relationship between rate changes and stock prices has shifted over time. Before 1990, there was little correlation between the federal funds rate and stocks. Since then, stronger stocks have been associated with rising rates. The explanation lies in the type of economic shocks: in the 1970s and 1980s, supply shocks (particularly oil) caused inflation and falling stocks simultaneously. After 1990, demand shocks dominated—when the economy weakened, the central bank lowered rates. Stock returns run below average following the onset of rate-hiking cycles, with the most negative three-month return being -15.86 percent in March 2022.

Chapter 19: Stocks and the Business Cycle

The stock market has predicted nine of the last five recessions, as Paul Samuelson quipped. Siegel illustrates with an economist who in 1987 correctly forecast four-percent GDP growth, no recession, and double-digit profit gains. Advisors loaded up on stocks. Weeks later the market crashed twenty-three percent. Accurate economic predictions do not guarantee stock market success.

Since 1802, there have been forty-eight recessions, and the stock market peaked before forty-four of them. But the timing is hopelessly unreliable: the lead ranges from zero to thirteen months. By the time a recession is officially confirmed (often more than a year after it begins), the stock market has already risen an average of twenty-five percent from its low. Economists have an abysmal record at forecasting turning points. In September 1974, virtually every economist at President Ford’s anti-inflation conference was unaware the country was in the middle of its worst postwar recession.

A perfect business-cycle timer would gain five percentage points annually over buy-and-hold, and two-thirds of that advantage comes from correctly predicting when recessions end, not when they begin. The worst course for investors is to follow prevailing sentiment—buying at high prices when everyone is optimistic and selling at the low when pessimism is confirmed.

The history of recession forecasting is a catalog of confident failures. In September 1974, virtually every economist at President Ford’s anti-inflation conference was unaware the country was in the worst postwar recession; the median forecaster overestimated GNP growth by six percentage points. In July 1981, Blue Chip ran the headline “Economic Exuberance Envisioned for 1982”—1982 was a disaster. In September 2001, only thirteen percent of forecasters believed the US was in recession, yet the NBER later showed it had begun six months earlier. On December 11, 2007, the Fed’s own economist Dave Stockton told the FOMC they had “a growth recession in this forecast and nothing more than that”— while the worst crisis since the Depression was already underway.

Periodically, economists convince themselves that the business cycle has been conquered. In the late 1980s, Ed Yardeni of Prudential-Bache wrote a “New Wave Manifesto” arguing that self-repairing economies would expand forever. He published it on the eve of one of the worst worldwide recessions. Leonard Silk of the New York Times wrote in May 1990 that “most economists foresee no recession in 1990 or 1991.” The recession began two months later.

Chapter 20: When World Events Impact Financial Markets

Of all 157 days in 136 years when stocks moved five percent or more, only forty-two—roughly one in four—can be linked to identifiable world events. The largest daily gain in history (14.87 percent on October 6, 1931) was driven by President Hoover’s proposal for a $500 million bank pool. The largest crash (22.6 percent on October 19, 1987) has no identifiable news cause at all.

Wars produce counterintuitive market reactions. When the NYSE closed for five months after World War I broke out in 1914, traders feared catastrophe. Instead, 1915 became the best single year in Dow history, with an eighty-two percent gain. Investors who bought stocks at the start of World War II expecting a replay of WWI’s wartime gains were “sorely disappointed”—Roosevelt capped corporate profits with an excess-profits tax.

Stocks have historically done better under Democratic presidents (11.33 versus 8.81 percent nominally since 1888), though real returns are closer because inflation has been lower under Republicans. The correlation is probably spurious: Democrats tend to inherit weak economies near market bottoms and preside during early bull-market years.

The September 11 attacks provide a case study in market psychology. At 8:48 a.m., when the first plane hit the North Tower, futures initially fell just a few points before buyers reappeared within minutes. At 9:03, the second plane struck and the world changed—S&P 500 futures plunged thirty points. Yet miraculously, even amid the enormity of the attack, buyers appeared and futures regained half the loss by 9:15. All exchanges closed for the rest of the week—the longest shutdown since FDR’s 1933 bank holiday. When markets reopened Monday, the Dow fell 685 points (7.13 percent), more than twice Pearl Harbor’s 3.5 percent drop.

Wars produce some of the most counterintuitive results. The Korean War triggered a 4.65 percent single-day decline—greater than Pearl Harbor. During the Gulf War of 1991, the first major conflict in an era of 24-hour global trading, markets judged victors within hours: bonds surged as success was reported, and Brent Crude fell from $29 to $20 per barrel. Within the first week of March, the Dow stood more than eighteen percent above where it was when the war started. Afghanistan, America’s longest war (2001–2021), had almost no impact on financial markets— investors had turned their attention inward.

Chapter 21: Stocks, Bonds, and the Flow of Economic Data

On July 5, 1996, the employment report showed payrolls up 239,000 with wages rising by the largest amount in thirty years. President Clinton celebrated. Financial markets panicked. Bond prices collapsed, interest rates spiked, and world equity markets lost $200 billion. What Main Street sees as good news is often terrible news on Wall Street.

Markets respond to the gap between expectations and reality, not to absolute outcomes. If traders expect 400,000 jobs lost and only 200,000 are lost, the reaction is the same as gaining 400,000 when only 200,000 were expected. Strong economic data has two opposing effects: it increases future earnings but also raises the discount rate. Near expansion peaks, the rate effect dominates, and “good” economic news hammers stock prices.

The single most important monthly release is the employment report, compiled by the Bureau of Labor Statistics from two separate surveys. The nonfarm payroll number is what truly moves markets. The PMI (Purchasing Managers’ Index) provides an early read: above 60 signals strong growth, below 40 almost always means recession. Martin Zweig argued that monetary conditions exert “enormous influence” on stock prices and are the “dominant factor” in determining the market’s major direction.

Surprise rate cuts have produced some of the most dramatic single-day moves. On January 3, 2001, the Fed unexpectedly slashed rates by fifty basis points (from 6.5 to 6.0 percent). The S&P 500 surged five percent and the Nasdaq jumped an all-time record 14.2 percent in a single session. Bernanke’s June 2013 “taper tantrum”—merely hinting at reduced bond purchases—caused the largest combined stock and bond loss in almost two years. The only scenario where stocks react poorly to easing is when the monetary authority eases excessively and the market fears inflation. Even then, Siegel argues, investors should prefer stocks to bonds, since fixed-income assets are hurt more by unexpected inflation.

The message for most investors is to watch economic data releases from the sidelines. Financial market reactions to data are not random but not easily predicted either. Trying to beat the market by anticipating employment reports or Fed decisions is a game for speculators, and most of them lose. Stick to a sound long-run strategy.

Part VI: Shocks and Crises

Chapter 22: Market Volatility

In early 1987, the Wall Street Journal printed a chart showing an uncanny resemblance between the 1922–1932 and 1980–1990 market patterns. On October 19, the parallel became reality: the Dow crashed 508 points, or 22.6 percent, in a single session. Trading volume exceeded 600 million shares—more than the entire year of 1966. Worldwide, more than a trillion dollars in market value vanished.

The aftermath diverged completely from 1929. The Fed, unlike its Depression-era predecessor, flooded the economy with money and pledged to back all bank deposits. There were no bank runs, no money supply contraction, no deflation. By August 1989, stocks had reached new highs. The lesson: a sharp sell-off can be an opportunity for profit, not a signal to panic.

The Flash Crash of May 6, 2010 showed how fragile modern markets had become. The Dow collapsed 600 points in minutes—nearly ten percent below the previous close, erasing $800 billion in five minutes. Procter & Gamble traded at $39 (half its opening price) and Accenture fell from $38 to one penny. The cause was a $4 billion futures sale over three minutes that overwhelmed high-frequency traders, who withdrew from the market when prices fell steeply. Stub quotes—penny bids never meant to be executed—were filled.

Excluding the Great Depression, market volatility has averaged roughly thirteen percent and remained remarkably stable over 190 years. October has earned its fearsome reputation: thirty-six of 157 large changes occurred in that single month, more than twice any other. The VIX peaked at 172 after the 1987 crash, nearly 90 after Lehman’s failure, and hit an all-time low of 8.56 on November 24, 2017.

The mechanics of the 1987 crash itself are harrowing. NYSE trading was chaotic that Monday morning—no Dow stocks traded near the 9:30 opening, and eleven still hadn’t opened by 10:30. Portfolio insurers sold index futures relentlessly; by late afternoon, S&P 500 futures traded at a twenty-five-point (twelve percent) discount to the spot index. The Dow collapsed three hundred points in the final hour alone. The next day, “Terrible Tuesday,” stocks opened up ten percent from Monday’s low, then plunged below the Monday close by noon. Near meltdown: trading halted in two hundred stocks and in S&P 500 futures. Only the Major Market Index futures on the Chicago Board of Trade remained open. Brave buyers stepped in, futures shot up 120 Dow-equivalent points in minutes, and the worst panic passed. The Wall Street Journal later reported that this single futures market was the “key to reversing catastrophic collapse.”

Robert Shiller of Yale devised a method for determining whether investors overreact to changes in dividends and interest rates. He found that stock prices were far too variable to be explained merely by subsequent behavior of dividends. Investors price stocks during recessions as if they expect dividends to go much lower—contrary to all historical experience. Since the word “cycle” implies ups followed by downs, a temporary drop in dividends should have only a minor effect on a price that discounts cash flows into an infinite future. Dean Witter captured this perfectly in a memo dated May 6, 1932, after stocks had plummeted eighty-five percent: “Either we are going to have chaos or else recovery. The former theory is foolish.”

Chapter 23: The Great Financial Crisis of 2008–2009

On September 15, 2008, Lehman Brothers filed for bankruptcy—the largest in American history, with $613 billion in liabilities. The crisis had been building for months. Bear Stearns was forced into J.P. Morgan at $2 a share in March, ninety-nine percent below its $172.61 high. But Lehman was “only the appetizer for this bear market.”

“When the music stops, in terms of liquidity, things will get complicated. But as long as the music is playing, you’ve got to get up and dance.” — Chuck Prince, Citigroup CEO, 2007

The real estate bubble that triggered the crisis was built on a catastrophic assumption: since nationwide housing prices had never declined twenty percent in the postwar period, the probability of such a decline was treated as “virtually zero.” This led rating agencies to stamp AAA on subprime mortgage securities—if the collateral was always worth more than the loan, borrower creditworthiness did not matter. Total subprime, alt-A, and jumbo mortgages reached $2.8 trillion by mid-2007.

Alan Greenspan had warned about stock prices a decade earlier with his “irrational exuberance” speech but said nothing about the unprecedented rise in housing prices. When he testified before Congress in October 2008, he expressed “shocked disbelief” that lending institutions had failed to protect themselves.

The economic damage was staggering. US real GDP declined four percent—the worst since World War II. Unemployment hit ten percent. The S&P 500 fell to 676, nearly fifty-seven percent below its peak, and worldwide stock losses totaled $33 trillion, roughly half the world’s annual GDP. The financial sector declined eighty-four percent. Citibank lost 98.3 percent. AIG lost 99.5 percent. The crucial difference from the Depression was that Ben Bernanke, a student of Milton Friedman’s A Monetary History, ensured the money supply rose during the crisis rather than contracting twenty-nine percent as it had from 1929 to 1933.

Hyman Minsky’s financial instability hypothesis offers a deeper explanation. Long periods of stability draw in speculators and Ponzi-like structures. The “Great Moderation” from 1983 to 2005 saw GDP volatility fall by half. The 1920s had shown exactly the same pattern before the 1929 crash. Lehman Brothers, founded in the 1850s, had posted four consecutive years of record profitability by 2007, with $19.2 billion in net revenues and 30,000 employees. It financed $17 billion to Tishman Speyer for the Archstone-Smith Trust, a $22.2 billion deal that left Lehman holding $5 billion in unsold real estate. “Blackstone got the very last chair in the real estate closing room,” Siegel writes, “but Lehman was left standing.” The contrast with the dot-com bubble was stark: when tech stocks crashed, investment banks had already sold off their risky holdings. At the real estate peak, Wall Street was “up to its ears in housing-related debt.”

“Regarding the Great Depression. You’re right, we did it. We’re very sorry. But thanks to you, we won’t do it again.” — Ben Bernanke to Milton Friedman

Chapter 24: The Covid-19 Pandemic

On February 12, 2020, stocks sat at an all-time high, closing the second-longest bull market in history. Siegel polled roughly a hundred financial advisors on what would end the bull run. Only one person chose “pandemic.” Within seven weeks the S&P 500 had fallen thirty-four percent, wiping more than twenty trillion dollars off global markets.

Siegel’s own analysis suggested the decline was wildly excessive. At a P/E of 20, losing one entire year of earnings should produce only a five percent price decline. Even two profitless years would justify just ten percent. But fear, he writes, “has a much stronger grip on investors than historical evidence.” The twenty-four-hour news cycle, with images of overtaxed hospitals and overflowing morgues, amplified anxiety far beyond what the economic data warranted.

The policy response was without precedent. Weekly jobless claims exploded from 200,000 in late February to 6.1 million in the first week of April—exceeding the previous record by a factor of nine. The CARES Act injected $2.2 trillion, followed by another $900 billion in December. The Fed lowered rates to zero and began open-ended bond purchases, eventually swelling its balance sheet to nearly nine trillion dollars.

The key monetary difference from 2008 was that the government credited money directly into bank accounts rather than merely expanding bank reserves. M2 surged at an annualized rate of fifty percent in March–April 2020. Siegel warned publicly in April 2020 that this would produce serious inflation, but the Fed forecast barely 1.8 percent for 2021. Reality: the CPI soared to 7.5 percent. Jerome Powell “retired” the word “transitory” in November 2021.

The recovery was astonishing. From the March 2020 bottom, the S&P 500 more than doubled. Oil went negative ($-40.32 per barrel on April 20, 2020), lumber quadrupled, and residential real estate rose more than twenty-five percent in less than two years. The pandemic left permanent changes: mRNA vaccines that could fight cancer, a work-from-home economy that exceeded all expectations, and a sharp decline in commercial office demand.

The REIT market revealed the pandemic’s uneven impact. The REIT index fell forty-three percent from February 20 to March 23, 2020, and by December 2021 sat only eighteen percent above prepandemic levels—far less than the S&P 500’s forty-two percent gain. But the components diverged wildly: commercial office REITs barely recovered, while data centers rose fifty percent and self-storage nearly doubled. Siegel emphasizes that over ninety percent of any asset’s value depends on earnings power more than one year into the future. Short-term disruptions—even a global pandemic—do not justify sharp, lasting price declines. Under a fiat money economy with no natural limit on money creation, real assets like stocks are essential portfolio components.

Part VII: Building Wealth Through Stocks

Chapter 25: How Psychology Can Thwart Investment Goals

Siegel introduces “Dave,” a composite investor who in October 1999 sold his “old fogy” stocks—Philip Morris, Procter & Gamble, Exxon—to chase internet names because his friends were making fortunes. By March 2000 he was up sixty percent and constantly trading. By August 2001 he had lost three-quarters of his retirement savings and was ready to abandon stocks forever. Dave is every investor who has ever confused a bull market for personal genius.

“We find that whole communities suddenly fix their minds upon one subject, and go mad in its pursuit... Men think in herds... they go mad in herds, while they only recover their senses slowly and one by one.” — Charles Mackay, 1852

Amos Tversky and Daniel Kahneman developed prospect theory in the 1970s, showing that people feel losses roughly twice as painfully as equivalent gains. This asymmetry drives a cascade of destructive behaviors. Loss aversion makes investors hold losing stocks far too long, hoping to “get even”—what Leroy Gross called the “get-even-itis disease” in 1982, which has “probably caused more portfolio destruction than any other mistake.” Stocks above the purchase price are fifty percent more likely to be sold than losers, which is bad strategy from both a trading and a tax standpoint.

Overconfidence is equally destructive. A 2000 study titled “Trading Is Hazardous to Your Wealth” found that the heaviest traders earned returns 7.1 percent below infrequent traders. People believe they are better than average (statistically impossible for a majority) and attribute favorable outcomes to skill while blaming unfavorable ones on bad luck.

The solution is precommitment: set an asset allocation rule and stick to it. If you must trade, establish a small separate account and treat the money as already lost. Never tell friends about individual trades—the social pressure to maintain a position makes cutting losses harder. And above all, resist myopic loss aversion: investors who see returns yearly invest far less in stocks than those who see five- or twenty-year intervals. If evaluated every thirty years, the equity premium needed to entice investors drops to barely one percent.

Contrarian investing turns behavioral finance on its head. Humphrey B. Neill argued in 1951 that “when everyone thinks alike, everyone is likely to be wrong.” Investors Intelligence in New Rochelle, New York, has evaluated newsletter sentiment scores for over forty years and found that when bullish sentiment is very high, caution is warranted, and when bearish sentiment spikes, it is often an excellent time to buy. De Bondt and Thaler examined five-year winner and loser portfolios and found that winners lagged the market by ten percent while losers beat it by thirty—a massive reversal driven by the representativeness heuristic, where investors extrapolate recent trends far into the future. The Dogs of the Dow strategy, which buys the ten highest-yielding Dow stocks, is built on this contrarian principle.

Spectacularly wrong moves from representative bias litter financial history. When World War I broke out in 1914, the NYSE closed for five months in fear. But 1915 became the best single year in Dow history. When World War II began in 1939, investors bought stocks expecting a repeat of WWI’s wartime gains—and were sorely disappointed when FDR capped corporate profits. The pattern repeats: investors read the past into the present, and the present refuses to cooperate.

“The stock market is a very expensive place to find out who you are.” — Wall Street saying

Chapter 26: ETFs, Stock Index Futures, and Options

In 2021, the highest-volume security on American exchanges was not Apple or Tesla but SPY, the S&P 500 ETF—over 18.6 billion shares worth seven trillion dollars. First launched in 1993, ETFs have grown from ten percent of mutual fund assets in 2012 to nearly thirty percent by 2020. Their tax advantage is significant: exchanges between ETFs and underlying shares are “exchanges in kind,” generating almost no capital gains distributions.

S&P 500 futures, launched in February 1982, command twenty dollars of stock exposure for every dollar of margin (versus two dollars for individual stocks). By 1984 their daily value surpassed total NYSE stock volume. The Chicago River leak of April 13, 1992, which halted futures trading, left the NYSE appearing “brain-dead”—volume dropped more than twenty-five percent, revealing how dependent stock markets had become on the futures pit in Chicago.

Leveraged ETFs carry a mathematical trap. A hundred thousand dollars in a triple-bear S&P 500 ETF (SPXS) in December 2015 was worth just two thousand dollars six years later. Constant rebalancing introduces a persistent downward bias; the triple-bear lost almost fifty percent per year. Options offer more flexibility but market professionals estimate that eighty-five percent of individual options players lose money.

ETFs carry a significant tax advantage over mutual funds. When an investor redeems a mutual fund, the fund must sell underlying stocks, potentially generating capital gains for all remaining shareholders. ETFs avoid this through authorized participants who exchange underlying stocks for ETF units in “exchanges in kind”—transactions that are not taxable events. This mechanism keeps ETF prices extremely close to their index through arbitrage while generating almost no capital gains distributions. ETFs can also be bought and sold at any time during the trading day (mutual funds settle only at the close), can be sold short, and can be margined like individual stocks at fifty percent.

Options deserve special attention for their flexibility and their dangers. Calls give the right to buy at a fixed strike price; puts give the right to sell. The Black-Scholes formula, developed by Fischer Black and Myron Scholes in the 1970s, was an instant success—programmed on handheld calculators worldwide. Scholes won the Nobel Prize in 1997. Options can replicate any futures or ETF position, but not vice versa, making them the most flexible derivative instrument. Protective puts function as portfolio insurance, while buy-and-write strategies (selling calls against existing stock positions) allow owners to collect premiums in exchange for capping their upside.

Triple witching days—when index futures, index options, and stock options all expire on the third Friday of the quarter— produce huge buy/sell imbalances and volatile price swings. A 1988 reform moved futures settlement to the Friday opening instead of the close to reduce disruption. The concept of fair value for futures depends on the relationship between dividend yields and interest rates. Before the financial crisis, interest rates exceeded dividend yields, so futures traded above the spot index. Since the crisis, that relationship has reversed.

Chapter 27: Fund Performance, Indexing, and Investor Returns

“I have little confidence even in the ability of analysts, let alone untrained investors, to select common stocks that will give better than average results.” — Benjamin Graham

The crucial difference between investing and other competitive endeavors is that anyone can match the average with zero effort. Since the sum of all investors’ holdings must equal the market, for every dollar that outperforms, another must underperform. The S&P Dow Jones Indices biannual review shows that over three-quarters of domestic funds underperform their benchmark over three years, and over ninety percent fail over ten and twenty years. From 2008 to 2021, there was not a single year when more than half of active funds beat their index.

Of 132 funds surviving the entire fifty-year period from 1972 to 2021, only one—AMF Large Cap Equity—beat the S&P 500 by more than two percentage points per year. Random probability theory would have predicted twenty-one such funds. Even Peter Lynch, whose Magellan Fund returned over twenty-nine percent annually from 1977 to 1990, saw his fund’s edge shrink dramatically after he left. Warren Buffett’s Berkshire Hathaway underperformed the S&P 500 in the ten years following its remarkable first forty years.

The math of costs is devastating. A one-percent annual fee reduces a thirty-year accumulation by nearly a third. Three percent in annual costs cuts it by more than half. Every extra percentage point of expenses forces a twenty-five-year-old to retire two years later. John Bogle launched the Vanguard 500 fund in 1976, raising just $11.4 million. By the end of 2021 it held $800 billion and charged just four basis points per year.

Even a manager with a genuine two-percent edge has only a seventy-four percent probability of beating the market over ten years—meaning one in four such managers will look incompetent through no fault of their own. A hypothetical young Peter Lynch with a five-percent edge has a nearly one-in-four chance of being fired as incapable after just two years. The statistical noise in returns is simply too large to identify skill reliably.

A “little learning is a dangerous thing” in investing. An investor with some knowledge of equity valuations often performs worse than someone with no knowledge who simply indexes. The novice sees a stock with good earnings that isn’t rising and buys it thinking it’s a bargain. But highly informed investors know the special circumstances that caused the earnings increase won’t repeat—and they are happy to sell to the novice. Many seeming anomalies are driven by the trading of informed investors with special information that cannot be easily processed by others. When a stock looks too cheap or too expensive, the easy explanation (emotional traders mispriced it) is usually wrong.

The rise of fundamentally weighted indexes offers an alternative to both active management and traditional cap-weighted indexing. Instead of weighting each stock by market capitalization, these indexes weight by dividends, earnings, cash flows, or book value. If a stock rises without fundamental support, shares are automatically sold during rebalancing; if it falls without deterioration, more shares are bought at lower prices. Robert Arnott, Jason Hsu, and Philip Moore published the landmark paper “Fundamental Indexation” in 2005. WisdomTree launched twenty dividend-weighted ETFs six months after the first fundamentally weighted ETF appeared. For investors wanting a value tilt, these indexes are generally better diversified than most value funds.

The S&P 500 inclusion effect creates its own distortion. Yahoo! surged sixty-four percent in five trading days between the announcement and its inclusion in December 1999. Tesla jumped fifty-six percent leading up to its entry in December 2020, with over 200 million shares trading on the inclusion day alone. An S&P study found that shares rose an average of 8.49 percent from announcement to admission, then fell 3.23 percent over the next ten days. Total stock market indexes avoid this overpricing problem, though the S&P 500 has still outperformed through 2021.

“Contrary to their oft articulated goal of outperforming the market averages, investment managers are not beating the market; the market is beating them.” — Charles D. Ellis

Chapter 28: Structuring a Portfolio for Long-Term Growth

The book concludes with seven principles distilled from 220 years of evidence. First, expect five percent real returns going forward rather than the historical six to seven, because lower transaction costs and easier diversification support higher equilibrium P/E ratios. Second, stocks are real assets and excellent long-term inflation hedges. Third, stock risk declines over time through mean reversion while bond risk increases through inflation uncertainty. Fourth, invest the largest percentage in low-cost index funds spanning a global portfolio. Fifth, put at least one-third in international stocks. Sixth, tilt toward value using passive or fundamentally weighted indexes. Seventh, establish firm rules and never deviate from them.

“My favorite holding period is forever.” — Warren Buffett, 1994

There are three pitfalls to avoid: trading frequently to beat the market, chasing hot mutual fund managers, and attempting to time market cycles. Siegel notes that often the best-informed investors fall hardest into the timing trap. Peter Bernstein observed that knowing the right strategy and implementing it are not the same thing. Selective memory, hindsight bias, and social pressure constantly conspire to push investors away from sound long-term strategies.

The three pitfalls deserve elaboration. On frequent trading: tales of quick wealth constantly tempt investors, but transaction costs and bad timing sink returns. On chasing hot managers: the top fifteen funds that beat the S&P by roughly three points in the first forty years of Siegel’s data underperformed by nearly the same margin in the next ten. Bill Miller of Legg Mason Value Trust set a record by beating the S&P for fifteen consecutive years (1991–2005), then turned ice cold from 2006 to 2008. On market timing: surprisingly, it is often the best-informed investors who fall hardest into this trap, because their knowledge gives them false confidence that they can anticipate turns. Selective memory distorts everything—“I knew that stock was going up!”—because we forget the doubts we had at the moment of decision.

“Worldly wisdom teaches that it is better for reputation to fail conventionally than to succeed unconventionally.” — John Maynard Keynes

Proper investing, Siegel concludes, is as much a psychological challenge as an intellectual one. Stock markets are the driving forces behind the allocation of the world’s capital and the fundamental engines of economic growth. Stocks represent the best way to accumulate wealth in the long run—as true today as when the first edition appeared in 1994.

The role of an investment advisor, Siegel emphasizes, is more psychological than analytical. Peter Bernstein noted that knowing the right strategy and implementing it are not the same thing. Hindsight encourages playing hunches and trying to outsmart others who are playing the same game. Failing by following expert advice is far easier to bear than failing by rejecting the consensus. If you seek help, select an advisor who agrees with the principles of diversification and long-term investing that the book espouses. For a quick fix during moments of panic, Siegel suggests pulling up the 220-year graph of real asset returns (Figure 1.1) that motivated the entire book. The relentless upward march of that line has calmed many an anxious investor.

Key Takeaways

The Equity Premium Is Structurally Necessary

Bonds are enforceable contracts; equities promise nothing and require faith in the future. If bonds outperformed stocks over the long run, risk would earn no reward—an unsustainable condition in any capitalist system. The 3.3 percent premium over bonds is not an accident but an economic necessity.

Time Transforms Risk into Safety

Over any twenty-year period in 220 years of data, stocks have never delivered a negative real return. Mean reversion causes stock volatility to decline faster than the square root of time, making equities less risky than bonds over thirty-year horizons.

The Average Investor Should Just Index

Over ninety percent of actively managed funds underperform their benchmark over ten or more years. Of 132 funds surviving fifty years, only one beat the S&P 500 by more than two points. Costs of one percent per year reduce a thirty-year accumulation by nearly a third.

Growth Does Not Equal Returns

GDP growth and stock returns are negatively correlated across countries. Fast-growing economies require capital that dilutes shareholders. The greatest stock of all time (Philip Morris) was in a shrinking industry.

Psychology Is the Biggest Enemy

Loss aversion, overconfidence, herding, and myopic loss aversion destroy more wealth than bad market conditions. Precommitment to a fixed allocation is the most reliable defense against behavioral traps.

Go Global

The US represents only about half of world equity value and is currently the most expensive major market. At least one-third of a portfolio should be in international stocks for diversification and to capture lower-valuation opportunities.

Contrarian Ideas

Bonds Are Riskier Than Stocks—for Long-Term Holders

Over thirty-year horizons, stock return volatility drops below that of bonds. Bonds are subject to inflation risk that compounds over time (mean aversion), while stocks self-correct through mean reversion.

Value Investing May Be Permanently Impaired

The 13.5-year value drought was unprecedented. Book value has become nearly meaningless in an economy where eighty-four percent of corporate value is intangible. The P/E gap between growth and value widened during the drought, suggesting it was not being arbitraged away.

The CAPE Ratio Has Been Systematically Wrong

Since 1981, CAPE has predicted below-average returns in all but four months while actual returns were above average. Even Shiller admits “we don’t know the right CAPE ratio.”

ESG Stocks May Underperform Precisely Because They Are Popular

Higher valuations for virtuous companies mean lower future returns. The best-performing stock in American history (Philip Morris) and the best-performing industry (tobacco) would fail any ESG screen.

Raskob Was Right

The man universally mocked as the symbol of 1929 speculation gave advice that actually worked. Dollar-cost averaging from the worst possible starting point still beat bonds within four years and delivered 12.72 percent annually over thirty.

Key Frameworks

Seven Principles for Successful Investing

  1. Expect 5% real returns, not 6–7%
  2. Stocks are real assets—excellent long-term inflation hedges
  3. Stock risk declines over time; bond risk increases
  4. Invest primarily in low-cost index funds
  5. At least one-third in international stocks
  6. Tilt toward value using passive indexes
  7. Establish firm rules and never deviate

Three Pitfalls to Avoid

  1. Trading frequently to beat the market
  2. Chasing hot mutual fund managers
  3. Timing market cycles

The Gordon Dividend Discount Model

P = d / (r − g), where d is the dividend, r is the required return, and g is the growth rate. Inflation raises both r and g equally, making stocks natural inflation hedges. Dividend policy does not affect price if the firm earns the same return on retained earnings.

The Noisy Market Hypothesis

Siegel’s alternative to the efficient market hypothesis. Prices deviate from fundamentals due to noise traders, but these deviations are difficult to exploit consistently. Markets are efficient enough that index funds beat most active managers, but noisy enough that value tilts and fundamentally weighted indexes have a structural edge.

Why This Book Matters

Stocks for the Long Run is the intellectual backbone of modern equity investing. The book’s influence extends beyond individual investors.
