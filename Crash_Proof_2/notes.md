## BATCH 5: Pages 81–100 (Ch 3: Fiat Money, History of Dollar, Gold)

### Chapter 3: For a Few Dollars More — Our Declining Currency

**Fiat Money:**
- Fiat money = money in name only. No intrinsic value. Value =
  purchasing power, which depends on economic strength and money
  supply management.
- Dollar was backed by gold until 1971. Abandoning gold standard freed
  the Fed from its only restraint on printing money.

**History of Money:**
- Barter → commodity money (cattle, beads, wampum, cigarettes)
- Gold and silver emerged as best money: doesn't tarnish/rust, malleable,
  divisible, portable, consistent quality.
- Medieval goldsmiths: warehouse receipts (first paper money), then
  bank notes (promises to pay) — began fractional reserve banking.

**Four functions of money:** unit of account, medium of exchange,
  store of value, unit of deferred payment. All assume money is sound.

**History of the dollar:**
- Constitution: Congress shall coin money in gold and silver. Art 1, Sec 10:
  states may not use anything but gold and silver as legal tender.
- Mint Act of 1792: dollar defined as 371.25 grains of fine silver.
- First US currency (1863): gold certificates. "This certifies there have
  been deposited... ten dollars in gold payable to the bearer on demand."
- 1934 Gold Reserve Act: made it illegal for Americans to own gold.
  Removed the word "gold" from Federal Reserve notes.
- Nov 2, 1963: redemption clause eliminated entirely. Dollar became
  "IOU nothing."
- Federal Reserve Act of 1913 (Owen-Glass Act): established Fed to
  regulate banking, manage money supply, act as clearinghouse.

**Bretton Woods (July 1944, Bretton Woods, NH):**
- Established dollar as world's reserve currency.
- Fixed foreign currencies to dollar; dollar fixed to gold at $35/oz.
- Also created World Bank and IMF.
- Reserve currency status = shield from free market forces.

**The Unraveling:**
- 1960s: Johnson/Nixon "guns and butter" (Vietnam + Great Society) →
  budget deficits → Fed monetized deficits → exported inflation abroad.
- Foreign countries returned surplus dollars demanding gold redemption →
  US gold reserves fell toward dangerous levels.
- 1971: Nixon closed the "gold window." "The national equivalent of
  declaring bankruptcy."
- Since 1971: floating exchange rates. Dollar = fiat, no gold backing.

**Dollar dynamics:**
- Dollar lost 24% vs other currencies 2002–2004. Bear market rally 2005.
  Resumed decline 2006.
- Dollar index 80 = key support. When broken, next leg down begins.
  Schiff predicted dollar would break 80 → it did in late 2007.
  Dollar then fell to ~70 in March 2008.
- Chinese yuan peg: US politicians publicly demand China float yuan.
  Schiff says this is reverse psychology or bluster. "Privately, I am sure,
  we are begging the Chinese not to float their currency."
- China is biggest buyer of US Treasuries and biggest supplier of cheap
  consumer goods. Float yuan = higher US consumer prices + higher
  interest rates = severe US recession.
- Gold bull: rose from below $300 to over $700 in six years.
  "A vote of no confidence in the dollar." 
- "Strong dollar policy" = mythical as Bigfoot or Loch Ness monster.
- End game: foreigners stop sending goods, spend accumulated dollars
  in US → "all that inflation we've created for 20 years that the Japanese
  and Chinese have kept at bay... will come back at us like a tsunami."
- Dollar Index bottom: originally predicted ~40, now predicts ~20 or lower
  due to additional stimulus policies.

**2009 Update:**
- Dollar DID break 80 in late 2007, fell to ~70 in March 2008.
- Then paradoxically RALLIED despite US economic problems. Why?
  (1) All world currencies falling vs. gold; dollar just falling slower —
  like being in slower-reversing car thinking you're going forward.
  (2) Trading partners providing vendor financing — buying dollars to
  prop up their best customer.
  (3) Global margin call: hedge funds sold liquid assets (gold, commodities)
  to meet redemptions → global demand for dollars.
  (4) Yen carry trade unwinding: borrowed yen at near 0% → US Treasuries
  at 3%+. As asset prices fell, yen loans repaid → more dollar demand.
- New dollar prediction: index to bottom at ~20, not ~40, due to stimulus.

### Chapter 4: Inflation Nation — The Federal Reserve Fallacy

**What Inflation Is and Isn't:**
- Inflation = expansion of money supply (not price increases — that's
  the EFFECT). Prices going up = symptom. Money supply expansion =
  disease.
- Say's Law (Jean-Baptiste Say): "production creates consumption."
  More money doesn't create more real goods; it just dilutes existing
  goods' values. More money chasing same goods = higher prices.

**Five reasons government CREATES inflation:**
1. Cheaper repayment of national debt
2. Politically hospitable to personally indebted voters
3. Finances social programs without raising taxes
4. Confuses inflationary spending with economic growth
5. Rises in nominal asset prices create illusion of wealth

**Five reasons government HIDES inflation:**
1. Keeps interest on national borrowings lower
2. Social Security/benefits indexed to understated CPI → cost less
3. Income tax brackets indexed using understated CPI
4. Lower inflation premiums keep rates low for consumer debt economy
5. TIPS payments tied to CPI (fox guarding henhouse)

**CPI manipulation:**
- "Cost-push inflation" and "demand-pull inflation" = scapegoats designed
  to blame businesses, workers, speculators — anyone but government.
  "Cost and price are two words that describe the same thing from different
  perspectives." So "cost-push inflation" = prices rise because prices rise.
- Inflation expectations blame: "analogous to blaming the rain on people
  having the foresight to carry umbrellas."
- PCE (Chain-Type Price Index): Fed's preferred inflation measure since
  2002. Makes constant substitutions — if filet mignon gets expensive,
  assume you substitute cat food. Same spending = no inflation.
  Schiff's analogy: man eating by candlelight wrapped in blankets eating
  cat food — PCE says no inflation because he spends same amount.
- Oil is "elephant in the living room." Rising oil prices are EFFECT of
  inflation, not cause. Schiff predicted oil above $70 in 2003.
- M3 money supply: Fed stopped releasing M3 figures in 2006 to make it
  harder to track money supply growth. M3 had increased nearly 20-fold
  since 1980.
- Real inflation = 8–9%, reported as 1–2%.

**Deflation ruse:**
- US 1780–1913: falling prices for 100+ years of unequalled prosperity.
  Only exception: Civil War (paper money created). After war, paper
  money removed and prices fell back.
- Flat-screen TVs: didn't sell at $10,000, sold at $2,000. Volume offsets
  lower margins. Corporate profits = margins, not absolute dollars.
- Japan: falling prices weren't Japan's problem; they were one bright
  spot. Japan's problems were government deficits and misallocated
  investment.
- Under guise of "price stability" (2–3% annual rises), government
  robs citizens of benefits of falling prices to buy votes.

**History of Fed policy:**
- Ford administration: WIN buttons ("Whip Inflation Now") — blamed
  public for inflation Greenspan helped create as Council of Economic
  Advisors chairman.
- Carter years: inflation out of control. Volcker under Reagan used high
  interest rates to break it.
- 1970s oil price rises: caused by 1960s Johnson/Nixon guns-and-butter
  spending monetized by William McChesney Martin Jr. Oil was effect,
  not cause. Same as today: oil prices = effect of Greenspan's policies.
- Continental dollar: Founders knew paper money danger firsthand —
  Continental ended up worth ~10 cents → expression "not worth a
  Continental." That's why Constitution specifically forbids government
  from issuing paper money (Art 1, Sec 10).
- "The framers didn't want [the government] to have the power to create
  inflation."

### Glossary (pp. 332–337) — key definitions captured
- Balance of trade: exports minus imports. US imports ~$800B/yr more
  than exports → $65B/month trade deficit.
- Budget deficit: govt spending minus tax revenues. Financed by
  securities issuance (adds to national debt) or monetization (inflation).
- Carry trade: borrow at X%, invest at Y% > X%. Positive carry = profit.
- Central bank: govt institution for monetary system. Fed = US.
- CPI: monthly index measuring basket of goods vs. 1982 baseline.
  Used for SS adjustments and tax brackets.
- Counterparty risk: risk either party to a contract fails to perform.
- Deflation: money/credit contraction relative to goods/services →
  falling price level.
- Derivative: contract whose value derives from underlying asset/index.
- Futures contract: agreement to buy/sell at a specified future price.
- GDP: total monetary value of final goods/services in US per year.
- Hyperinflation: rapid, out-of-control price rises with zero confidence
  in purchasing power.
- Inflation: money/credit expansion relative to goods/services →
  rising price level.
- Monetary policy: Fed decisions on money/credit supply via FOMC
  and federal funds rate target.
- Monetize: finance with printed money / expanded credit.
- National debt: accumulated past budget deficits (~$8.5T in 2006 =
  60%+ of GDP). Does NOT include unfunded liabilities (SS, Medicare,
  student loan guarantees, etc.).
- Reserve currency: dollar's Bretton Woods status — other govts
  settle accounts in dollars. Exempts US from trade balance pressure.
- Short selling: selling borrowed asset expecting price to fall, then
  buying back cheaper. Short covering = buying to close position.
  Short squeeze = forced covering drives prices up.
