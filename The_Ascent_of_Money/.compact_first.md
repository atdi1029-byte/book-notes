## Introduction: Money and History

### The Paradox of Prosperity and Inequality
- **Lloyd Blankfein** (Goldman Sachs CEO, 2007) earned $68.5M — roughly
  2,000 times median US household income
- Average American income grew only 5%, living costs rose 4.1%; median
  real income nearly flat since 1990 (up 7% in 18 years)
- Goldman Sachs assets exceeded $1T, revenue $46B (larger than many
  countries' GDP); meanwhile ~1B people survive on $1/day

### Why Finance Is Despised
- Three historical causes:
  1. **Debtors vs. creditors**: moral prejudice that lenders exploit borrowers
  2. **Crises and volatility**: collapses appear to cause poverty rather
     than create prosperity
  3. **Ethnic/religious minorities**: financial services disproportionately
     provided by excluded groups (Jews, Armenians, Chinese)
- Idea that financiers are parasitic on "real" productive activities

### Why Financial Innovation Matters
- Italian Renaissance: **Medici bankers** applied Oriental mathematics
  to money, enabling art and architecture boom
- **Dutch Republic** prevailed over Habsburg Empire through the world's
  first modern stock market — financial edge over silver mines
- **British Empire** strength inseparable from insurance, bonds, and
  corporate finance
- **Argentina** collapsed from financial folly despite being 6th-richest
  nation in 1880s

### Scale of Modern Finance (2007)
- Global GDP: ~$47T; Stock market cap: $51T; Bonds: $68T;
  Derivatives: $473T notional value (10x larger than bonds)
- Daily FX trading: $2T; Monthly stock trading: $7T
- Leveraged buyouts (2006): $753B; 7,000+ hedge funds, $1.9T AUM
- Financial sector: 2.3% of US GDP (1947) → 7.7% (2005); 1 of every
  $13 wages paid to finance workers
- Harvard 1970: 5% of male graduates entered finance; by 2007: >20%
  of men, 10% of women expected first job in banking

### The 2007 Crisis Warning
- Period 2001–2007: sustained expansion despite 9/11, wars, climate
- Subprime mortgages bundled as CDOs; poor families in Alabama–Wisconsin
  obtained mortgages repackaged into complex instruments sold globally
- German regional banks and Norwegian municipalities became de facto
  mortgage lenders
- CDOs sliced so finely that bottom tiers rated AAA (equivalent to
  US Treasury); when mortgage rates reset after teaser periods,
  borrowers defaulted; housing fell sharpest since 1930s
- Contagion: off-balance-sheet vehicles (conduits, SIVs) in distress;
  banks' capital ratios eroded; Fed, ECB, BoE intervened

### Financial Literacy Crisis
- 4 in 10 US credit-card holders don't pay full balance monthly
- Nearly 1/3 have no idea what their card's interest rate is
- 2/3 of Americans don't understand **compound interest**
- Only 14% of high school seniors understand stocks beat bonds over
  18 years; <23% know income tax applies to savings account interest
- >50% don't know difference between company pension, Social Security,
  and 401(k)

### Three Realizations
1. **Poverty from lack of finance**: efficient credit networks let
   industrious poor escape loan sharks (rates up to 1,100% annually
   in Glasgow). Problem is **absence** of financial infrastructure
2. **Inequality amplified by finance**: returns on capital dwarf
   returns on labor; financially literate prosper; China became US banker
3. **Complexity and unpredictability**: non-linear, chaotic relationships;
   financial history is jagged evolution — species of financing emerge,
   thrive, go extinct (**Anthony W. Ryan**, US Treasury, Sept 2007)

### Book Structure
- Credit (Ch. 1) → Bonds (Ch. 2) → Stocks (Ch. 3) → Insurance (Ch. 4)
  → Real Estate (Ch. 5) → International Finance (Ch. 6)

## Chapter 1: The Dream of Inca Gold

### The Human Cost of Potosí
- Miners died from mercury fumes (the **patio process**: ore ground
  into amalgam with mercury, then heated), rock falls, and 700-foot
  descents carrying ore sacks
- **Domingo de Santo Tomás** called Potosí "a mouth of hell, into
  which a great mass of people enter every year"
- **Rodrigo de Loaisa**: "if twenty healthy Indians enter on Monday,
  half may emerge crippled on Saturday"

> 'Every peso coin minted in Potosí has cost the life of ten Indians
> who have died in the depths of the mines.'
> — Fray Antonio de la Calancha, 1638

- As indigenous workforce depleted, African slaves imported as
  "human mules"
- Between 1556–1783, Cerro Rico yielded **45,000 tons of pure silver**;
  Potosí peaked at 160,000–200,000 people; *Valer un potosí* =
  Spanish for "to be worth a fortune"

### The Three Functions of Money
- **Medium of exchange**, **unit of account**, **store of value**
- Must be: available, affordable, durable, fungible, portable, reliable
- Earliest coins: ~600 BC, **Lydian** electrum coins at Temple of
  Artemis, Ephesus — lion's head; forerunners of Athenian **tetradrachm**
- Roman: **aureus** (gold), **denarius** (silver), **sestertius**
  (bronze) — ranked by metal scarcity
- China: standardized bronze coin 221 BC by **Qin Shihuangdi**
- Pattern: precious-metal coins associated with sovereigns who
  **monopolized minting as revenue**

### Medieval Silver Shortage
- Roman coinage outlived the empire — prices still in denarii under
  **Charlemagne** (768–814 AD)
- By 800, chronic silver shortage; metal drained eastward to the
  **Islamic Empire**
- 24 denarii bought a Carolingian cow; peppers and squirrel skins
  served as substitute currency
- Europe's solutions: (1) export labour/goods to Baghdad for silver;
  (2) plunder via Crusades
- **Contrarian claim**: Crusades were as much about overcoming
  Europe's monetary shortage as religious conversion

### The "Big Problem of Small Change"
- Medieval governments failed to stabilize coins of different metals
- Spanish New World silver appeared to break the constraint
- Convoys of up to 100 ships transported **170 tons of silver/year**;
  a fifth = **44% of total royal expenditure** at peak
- The **"piece of eight"** (from *thaler*, hence "dollar") became
  the **world's first truly global currency**

### The Price Revolution and the Resource Curse
- **"Price revolution"** 1540s–1640s: cost of food (flat for 300 years)
  rose markedly; English cost of living rose **sevenfold**
- Within Spain: silver as **resource curse** — like Arabian/Nigerian oil
  — removing productive incentives, strengthening rent-seeking autocrats
  (Charles V and Philip II) at the expense of the Cortes
- **"The value of precious metal is not absolute. Money is worth only
  what someone else is willing to give you for it."**

### Money as Trust, Not Metal
- No historical necessity for money = metal; this was contingent
- **Ancient Mesopotamia** (~5,000 years ago): clay tokens recorded
  transactions in barley, wool, silver; debts transferable ("pay the
  bearer" not a named creditor)
- **When humans first wrote things down, they did so not for history,
  poetry, or philosophy — but to do business**
- Clay tablet from Sippar, reign of **Ammi-ditana** (1683–1647 BC):
  bearer to receive barley at harvest
- Modern **banknotes** originated in 7th-century China — promissory
  notes like Babylonian tablets four millennia later
- US $10 bill: "In God We Trust" but really trusting the Treasury
  Secretary + Fed Chairman not to repeat Spain's error
- **Cash in hands of ordinary Americans = only 11% of M2**
- Ferguson's thesis: **"Money is not metal. It is trust inscribed."**
  Medium is irrelevant — silver, clay, paper, LCD

### Origins of Credit: Ancient Babylon
- Babylonian system sophisticated: debts transferable; clay receipts
  issued at temples/palaces; interest rates up to **20%**
- Mathematical exercises from **Hammurabi**'s reign (1792–1750 BC)
  show compound interest on long-term loans
- Laws of Hammurabi prescribed **debt forgiveness every 3 years**
- By 6th century BC, family firm **Egibi**: powerful lenders, commercial
  interests from Uruk to Persia; thrived **five generations**
- Root of "credit" is *credo* — Latin for "I believe"

## Chapter 1: Loan Sharks

### Fibonacci and the Mathematical Preconditions
- Early 13th-century Northern Italy: feuding city-states, Roman numerals
  ill-suited to commerce; Pisa had 7 forms of coinage
- **Leonardo of Pisa** (**Fibonacci**), son of a Pisan customs official
  in Algeria, immersed in "Indian method" of mathematics
- **Fibonacci sequence** (0, 1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, 21...); ratio
  approaches golden mean (~1.618)
- *Liber Abaci* (1202) introduced **Hindu-Arabic numerals**, decimal
  system, and the **concept of present value** — mathematics applied
  to making and lending money
- Venice became "Europe's great lending laboratory"

### Shylock and the Cultural Obstacles to Credit
- Shakespeare's *The Merchant of Venice* (from *Il Pecorone*, 1378,
  **Giovanni Fiorentino**) illustrates:
  1. Power of lenders to charge extortionate rates
  2. Importance of **law courts** in resolving financial disputes
  3. Vulnerability of minority creditors to backlash
- **Usury** condemned: excommunication by **Third Lateran Council (1179)**;
  arguing usury wasn't a sin = heresy at Council of Vienna (1311–12)
- **Dante's Inferno**, Canto XVII: seventh circle reserved for usurers,
  wearing money-purses around their necks

> 'Sorrow... gushed from their eyes...' — Dante, *Inferno*, Canto XVII

- Jews permitted loophole: Deuteronomy allows lending at interest to
  a stranger but not a brother

### Jewish Moneylenders in Venice
- Jews expelled from Spain 1492; *conversos* from Portugal 1497;
  many fled to Ottoman Empire then established Venice trading links
- **1516**: Venice designated old iron foundry as the **ghetto nuovo**
  (*getto* = casting); Jews confined nightly, required yellow O or hat
- Buildings grew to **seven storeys** to accommodate ~2,500 Jews (1590)
- **Daniel Rodriga** negotiated 1589 charter — Jews barred from guilds
  and retail, restricting them to financial services

### Modern Loan Sharks: Glasgow's Gerard Law
- 165,000 UK households use illegal moneylenders (2007); ~£40m/year
  borrowed, repaying **3x that amount**
- **Gerard Law**, Glasgow's East End: operated from the Argosy pub
  for 20 years; clients handed over benefit books as collateral
- Rate: **25% per week** = **over 11 million per cent compound annual**;
  "Beardy Al" borrowed £10, repaid £12.50 a week later
- Male life expectancy in Shettleston = 64 (same as Pakistan)
- Law sentenced to 10 months; his rationale: no mainstream institution
  would lend; high default risk necessitated high rates; but high rates
  made defaults more likely → **only intimidation kept people paying**

## Chapter 1: The Medici Bank

### Bills of Exchange and the Usury Workaround
- **Bills of exchange** (*cambium per literas*) were the core instrument
- Profits on exchange legal; charging interest was usury
- Depositors received ***discrezione*** (share of annual profits)

### The *Libro Segreto* and Business Model
- **Giovanni di Bicci de' Medici**'s "secret book": meticulous
  bookkeeping with liabilities (*vostro*) and assets (*nostro*)
- Key to success: **diversification** — multiple related partnerships,
  each separately negotiated; not a monolithic bank
- Branch managers were junior partners sharing profits —
  **decentralization** reduced systemic risk
- Capital ~20,000 florins (1402); profits 151,820 florins (1397–1420)
  = **32% annual return**; Rome branch alone over 30%
- Giovanni died 1429 worth ~91,000 florins

### From Bankers to Rulers
- By 1458, **Cosimo de' Medici** effectively *was* Florence; Pope Pius
  II: "Political questions are settled at his house... He is King in
  everything but name"
- **Francesco Guicciardini**: Cosimo "had a reputation such as probably
  no private citizen has ever enjoyed from the fall of Rome"
- Botticelli's *Adoration of the Magi* depicts Medici as wise men —
  "Having once been damned, bankers were now close to divinity"
- **Lorenzo the Magnificent** (took over 1469): "the erstwhile Sopranos
  had become the Corleones — and it was all based on banking"

### The Decline
- Lorenzo neglected business for politics; managers like **Francesco
  Sassetti** and **Tommaso Portinari** became less supervised
- Bad loans: Bruges branch to **Charles the Bold**; London branch to
  **King Edward IV** — both largely unrepaid
- Lorenzo raided the **Monte delle Dote** (dowry mutual fund)
- 1494: French invasion → family expelled, records burned by a mob
  answering **Savonarola**'s "Bonfire of the Vanities" (scorch marks
  still visible)

> 'If you would be happy, be so. / There is no certainty about
> tomorrow.' — Lorenzo de' Medici

- 1537: **Cosimo the Younger** recalled; Grand Duke of Tuscany 1569;
  ducal line lasted until 1743
- Legacy: "in finance small is seldom beautiful" — size + diversification
  spreads risk; Italian banking became the model for Dutch and English
  commercial success

## The Evolution of Banking

### Three 17th-Century Innovations

**1. Amsterdam Exchange Bank (*Wisselbank*), 1609**
- Resolved 14 different mints problem; pioneered **cheques and direct
  debits**; maintained near **100% reserve ratio** (19M florins deposits,
  16M metallic reserve) — immune to runs but incapable of credit creation

**2. Swedish Riksbank, 1656**
- Also a ***Lanebank*** — lent as well as facilitated payments
- Pioneered **fractional reserve banking**: lent in excess of metallic
  reserve, exploiting fact depositors unlikely to withdraw en masse

**3. Bank of England, 1694**
- Created for **war finance**; from 1709: only joint-stock bank allowed;
  from 1742: partial monopoly on **banknote** issue

### The Money Multiplier
- Central bank pays $100 → Bank 1 keeps $10 (10% reserve), lends $90 →
  Bank 2 keeps $9, lends $81 → and so on
- **M0** = $100; **M1** = $271 after three rounds — fractional reserve
  **creates credit and hence money**
- The reverse: a bank run collapses M1 as fast as it expanded

### Money Supply Definitions
- **M0**: monetary base; **M1**: cash + demand deposits; **M2**: adds
  savings, CDs; **M3**: broadest — eurodollar deposits, repos
- Money ceased to mean metal dug up and minted; became sum total of
  banks' liabilities. **Credit** = total of banks' assets (loans)

### Spain's Paradox
- Despite controlling Potosí, Spain **failed** to develop sophisticated
  banking; relied on Antwerp merchants; never grasped money is about
  **credit, not metal**
- Crown defaulted **14 times** between 1557 and 1696

> "In the modern world, power would go to the bankers, not the
> bankrupts." — Ferguson

### 19th-Century British Banking
- **Adam Smith** (1776): banking provides "a sort of waggon-way through
  the air"
- Post-*Wealth of Nations*: bill-discounting banks, merchant banks
  (**Barings**), country banks
- **Peel's Bank Charter Act (1844)**: split Bank into banking and issue
  depts; too rigid; suspended in crises of 1847, 1857, 1866
- **Walter Bagehot** (*The Economist*), after Overend Gurney collapse
  1866: **"lender of last resort"** — lend freely at penalty rate
- After 1858 joint-stock restrictions lifted → consolidation into
  **Lloyds** (1884), **Barclays** (1896)
- By eve of WWI: bank deposits = £1.2 billion vs. banknotes of only
  £45.5 million — "Money was now primarily inside banks, out of sight"
- Country banks declined from 755 (1809) to 17 (1913)

### Central Banking Goes Global
- **Banque de France** 1800, **German Reichsbank** 1875, **Bank of
  Japan** 1882, **Swiss National Bank** 1907

### US Banking: The Free Banking Experiment
- Political aversion → **Federal Reserve** not created until 1913
- Banks surged from <12,000 (1899) to >30,000 (peak 1922);
  under-capitalized → regular panics
- Great Depression: Fed **exacerbated** rather than mitigated the crisis
- **Deposit insurance (1933)** reduced vulnerability; interstate banking
  not legal until **Maine 1976**

### The Gold Standard's Fall
- **Keynes** (1924): "barbarous relic"
- Fatal flaw: impossible trinity — peg forces choice between free
  capital movements and independent monetary policy
- **15 August 1971**: **Nixon** closed the gold window — centuries-old
  link broken
- Dollar's purchasing power since then fallen **87%**; avg annual
  inflation >4%; $1,000 in gold (1970) = ~$26,596 by 2008

## Bankrupt Nation

### Memphis as Case Study in Credit Risk
- Ferguson visits **Memphis, Tennessee** (2007) — 1–2 million US
  bankruptcy cases/year, disproportionately in Tennessee
- Street-level ecosystem: car-title loans, cheque-cashing at 200%,
  pawnshops, Rent-A-Center, Plasma Center ($55/donation)
- "Modern Memphis gives a whole new meaning to 'bled dry'"

### American Bankruptcy as a Right
- No debtors' prisons in early 19th-century US
- Since 1898: **Chapter VII** (liquidation) or **Chapter XIII**
  (reorganization)
- Failed entrepreneurs like **Heinz**, **Barnum**, **Henry Ford**
  eventually succeeded after bankruptcy
- Reality: 98% of Memphis filings are **non-business** — especially
  **medical bills** from lack of health insurance
- 2007 US consumer debt: **$2.5 trillion** (record); consumer debt
  16% of disposable income (1959) → **24%**

### The Broader Implication
- Broad money/GDP: ~70% (pre-1971) → >100% (2005); European bank
  capital fell from ~25% of assets (early 20th c.) to <10%
- Total international banking assets (Dec 2006): ~$29 trillion ≈
  63% of world GDP
- Ferguson: poverty seldom caused by rapacious bankers — more often
  by the **absence** of banks

## Chapter 2: Mountains of Debt — Origins of the Bond Market

### War as the Father of Finance
- **Heraclitus**: "War is the father of all things" — equally true
  of the bond market
- The ability to **finance war through a market for government debt**
  was invented in the **Italian Renaissance**

### Florence and the *Monte Commune*
- **Sir John Hawkwood** (*Giovanni Acuto*): English mercenary from
  Sible Hedingham, Essex; most celebrated condottiere of the 1360s–70s;
  portrait still in Florence's Duomo
- Fought for Pisa (1364), then switched to Florence — "because
  Florence was where the money was"
- Florence's debt rose **100-fold** to **5 million florins by 1427**;
  borrowed money = ~**70% of city revenue**

### *Prestanze*: Forced Loans as Proto-Bonds
- Wealthy Florentines obliged to lend — ***prestanze***; received
  interest (not usury since compulsory: *damnum emergens*)
- **Hostiensis (c.1270)**: earliest use of *interesse* for return
  on a compulsory loan
- Certificates could be **sold on a secondary market**; two-thirds
  of households contributed
- System worked because the same oligarchical families who issued
  the bonds also bought them

### Venice's *Monte Vecchio* and *Monte Nuovo*
- Venice developed public debt even earlier, late 12th century
- ***Monte nuovo***: paid 5% annual interest from excise taxes
- Military reverses caused severe crashes: 1497 bonds at 80; 1500 at
  52; 1509 defeat at Agnadello: *monte vecchio* at 3, *monte nuovo*
  at 10
- **Buy distressed war bonds at 10 = 50% yield if the state survives**

### Northern European Annuities: The *Census* Workaround
- **Census contract**: buyer purchases annual payment stream — not
  technically a loan, so usury laws didn't apply
- ***Rentes héritables***: perpetual, bequeathable; ***rentes viagères***:
  life annuities
- By mid-16th century: ~**7% of revenues** in Holland

### Spain, France, and Absolute Monarchs
- Absolute rulers had no incentive to repay — **serial defaulting**
- **Spain defaulted**: 1557, 1560, 1575, 1596, 1607, 1627, 1647,
  1652, 1662

### The Dutch United Provinces: Republican Finance
- Amsterdam: life annuities, perpetual annuities, **lottery loans**
- By 1650: **65,000+ Dutch *rentiers*** invested in debt instruments
- Dutch debt: 50M guilders (1632) → 250M (1752); yields fell to
  **2.5% in 1747** — sign of confidence and capital abundance

### The Glorious Revolution
- **1688**: William of Orange; Dutch financial innovations migrated
  to London
- No more regular defaults (1672 "Stop of Exchequer" still remembered)
- **1749**: **Henry Pelham** created **consols** — arguably the most
  successful bond ever issued

### France: The Road Not Taken
- France: defaults, office sales, tax farming, budgets unintelligible,
  Estates General ceased meeting; relied on *rentes* and **tontines**
- London: thriving liquid consol market; Paris: no comparable market
- This **financial divergence would have profound political consequences**

### The British Consol
- Perpetual bonds, no fixed maturity; redeemable only if price ≥ par
- **Mrs Anna Hawes** purchased £100 5% consol in January 1796 for £101
  — ill-timed: Napoleon won at Montenotte that April
- Napoleonic Wars: debt tripled to **£745 million** (>2x annual output)
- Consol price fell from £96 (Feb 1792) to below £50 in 1797

## "The Bonaparte of Finance" — Nathan Rothschild

> "Master of unbounded wealth, he boasts that he is the arbiter of
> peace and war, and that the credit of nations depends upon his nod."
> — Radical MP **Thomas Dunscombe**, 1828

- **Nathan Mayer Rothschild**: for most of the 19th century, the
  biggest bank in the world
- Built 41 stately homes across Europe, including Waddesdon Manor
- Lord Rothschild: "short, fat, obsessive, extremely clever, wholly
  focused"
- Arrived in England 1799; decade in the North purchasing textiles;
  entered banking in London 1811
- Letters in ***Judendeutsch*** (German transliterated into Hebrew)

> "I do not read books, I do not play cards, I do not go to the
> theatre, my only pleasure is my business." — Nathan Rothschild

### Waterloo as Financial Contest
- Battle of Waterloo (18 June 1815): 67,000 British/Dutch/German
  troops vs. Napoleon's French
- More than military — contest between **rival financial systems**:
  France (plunder/taxation of conquered) vs. Britain (debt)

### Nathan's Gold Smuggling Mission
- Wellington needed **gold coins** — not banknotes — to pay troops
- Nathan's expertise: **gold smuggler** across Napoleon's Continental
  Blockade (French often tolerated, believing outflows weakened Britain)
- **January 1814**: Chancellor authorized secret collection of up to
  £600,000 in French/German/Dutch gold
- Nathan advanced **£1.2 million** by May 1814; total 1815 transactions:
  **£9.8 million**; commissions 2–6%

### The Rothschild Network
- Five brothers: **Nathan** (London), **Amschel** (Frankfurt),
  **James** (Paris), **Carl** (Amsterdam), **Salomon** (roving)
- Enabled **arbitrage**: transactions large enough to **move price
  differentials**

### The Waterloo Trade — Truth vs. Myth
- **Nazi propaganda** (1940 film *Die Rothschilds*, Goebbels-approved):
  Nathan bribed a general, triggered panic, bought at basement —
  pocketing £20–135M
- **Reality**: Rothschilds nearly **ruined** by Waterloo; Nathan sat
  on gold nobody needed (peace came suddenly)
- Nathan received news ~48 hours early — but it was **bad news** for him
- **Actual trade**: bought British bonds betting victory = reduced
  borrowing = rising prices; held **over a year** against brothers'
  entreaties; sold late 1817 with prices up **40%+**
- Profit equivalent to **~£600 million today**

> "He is in money and funds what Bonaparte was in war." — **Baring
> Brothers** partner

- Metternich's secretary: ***die Finanzbonaparten***
- **Heinrich Heine** (1841): "Money is the god of our time, and
  Rothschild is his prophet."

### Post-1815 Bond Dominance
- 1815–1859: **14 sovereign bonds**, ~**£43 million** — more than half
  total London issuance
- Key innovation: bonds **denominated in sterling**, payable at any
  Rothschild branch; 1818 Prussian 5% bond issued simultaneously in
  London, Frankfurt, Berlin, Hamburg, Amsterdam
- Only **investment-grade** securities; no bond in default by 1829
- Nathan died 1836: fortune = **0.62% of British national income**
- Combined capital: £1.8M (1818) → **£9.5M (1852)** → **£41M (1899)**
  — exceeded five biggest German joint-stock banks combined
- Family cohesion: of 21 marriages (1824–1877), at least 15 between
  direct descendants
- Mayer Amschel's maxim: **"If you can't make yourself loved, make
  yourself feared."**

### Anti-Semitic Reaction
- **Heine** placed Rothschild alongside **Richelieu and Robespierre**:
  Richelieu destroyed aristocratic power; Robespierre decapitated its
  remnant; Rothschild replaced it with mobile capital
- **Jules Michelet** (1842): "M. Rothschild knows Europe prince by
  prince, and the bourse courtier by courtier"
- Mississippi governor denounced "Baron Rothschild" for "the blood
  of Judas and Shylock"
- **Song Hongbing**, *Currency Wars* (China, 2007): claims Rothschilds
  still control global monetary system — debunked by Ferguson

## Chapter 2: The Civil War as Financial Contest

### Confederate Finance Problem
- Confederacy lost as much from **lack of hard cash** as from
  industrial or manpower deficits
- **April 29, 1862**: **Flag Officer Farragut** seized New Orleans —
  the real financial turning point
- Confederate Treasury raised $15M and $100M in domestic bonds, but
  liquid capital was scarce in the agrarian South
- Hoped the **Rothschilds** might finance them; hope proved fanciful

### Rothschild Neutrality
- NY agent **August Belmont** (Democratic chairman) had backed Douglas
  against Lincoln; **Salomon de Rothschild** expressed pro-Southern
  sympathies
- *Chicago Tribune* (1864): accused "Belmont, the Rothschilds, and
  the whole tribe of Jews" of buying Confederate bonds
- Reality: Rothschilds **opted out** — distaste for slavery + bad
  credit risk (**Jefferson Davis** had advocated repudiation)

### The Erlanger Cotton Bond
- 7% coupon, 20-year, convertible to cotton at pre-war **6d per pound**
- Cotton prices soared 6¼d → 27¼d; **"cotton famine"** threw half of
  Lancashire's 300,000 mill workers out of work; a quarter on poor
  relief
- Key buyers: future PM **Gladstone** and *Times* editor **Delane**
- Fatal flaw: when New Orleans fell, collateral became inaccessible —
  bondholders had to run the Union blockade **twice**
- By 1863 Lancashire found new cotton in China, Egypt, India

### Confederate Monetary Collapse
- Printed **$1.7 billion** in unbacked "greybacks"; worth **one cent**
  at war's end; rampant forgery of crude notes
- **Southern inflation: ~4,000%** vs. 60% in the North
- 14th Amendment: victorious Union pledged not to honor Confederate debts

> "It would not be the last time that an attempt to buck the bond
> market would end in ruinous inflation and military humiliation."

## Chapter 2: The Euthanasia of the Rentier

### Latin American Defaults and Bondholder Power
- **First great crisis: 1826–9** — Peru, Colombia, Chile, Mexico,
  Guatemala, Argentina all defaulted
- **"Painless to default when a substantial proportion of bondholders
  are foreign"**

### Gunboat Diplomacy
- **Gladstone ordered Egypt invasion in 1882** partly for bondholder
  interests (including his own)
- **Venezuela 1902**: joint British-German-Italian naval blockade
- US "especially energetic" in protecting bondholders in Central
  America and the Caribbean

### The Rentier Class
- British **rentiers**: fewer than 250,000 people (~2% of population);
  wealth exceeded entire national income; income ~7% of national income
- By 1822, interest on national debt = ~half of total public spending
- **William Cobbett**, *Rural Rides* (1830):
> "A national debt... has a natural tendency to draw wealth into great
> masses... for the gain of a few."

### The Five Steps to Wartime Inflation
**Milton Friedman**: "Inflation is always and everywhere a monetary
phenomenon"
1. War → shortages + 2. Government borrowing from central bank →
3. Debt → cash, expanding money supply → 4. Inflationary expectations
shift → 5. Prices rise

### Germany vs. Britain: Why Hyperinflation
- German banknote volume rose 1,040% (1913–18); Britain 708%
- Cost of living in Berlin 1918: 2.3× pre-war; London 2.1× — almost
  identical
- **Critical difference**: Germany couldn't access international bond
  market during war; ~a third of Reich debt was "floating" (unfunded)

### The Versailles Trap
- **132 billion gold marks** in reparations — >3x national income;

  to revise reparations — a miscalculation
- France **invaded the Ruhr (1923)**; Germany financed a general strike

### German Hyperinflation: Scale
- End of 1923: **4.97 × 10²⁰ marks** in circulation; annual rate:
  **182 billion per cent**; prices 1.26 trillion times higher than 1913

> "It is a witches' sabbath of devaluation where men and the units
> of their money have the strongest effects on each other..."
> — **Elias Canetti**

- Wiped out **all internal debt**; victims: rentiers, civil servants,
  professionals
- Ferguson's key distinction: *"Inflation is monetary; hyperinflation

> "By a continuing process of inflation, governments can confiscate,
> secretly and unobserved, an important part of the wealth of their

- Keynes attributed to Lenin: "no subtler means of overturning society

### Argentina: From Richest to Basket Case

- **Oligarchy of landowners** built on agricultural exports; failed

## Chapter 3: Blowing Bubbles

### The Joint-Stock Company
- **Joint-stock**: jointly owned; **Limited-liability**: loss capped
- Share price = collective estimate of future earnings; "stock markets
  hold hourly referendums on companies"
- Zoological imagery: bulls, bears, "electronic herd"

### The Five Stages of a Bubble (Hyman Minsky)
1. **Displacement** — new profitable opportunities
2. **Euphoria** (overtrading) — rising prices, rapid growth
3. **Mania** (bubble) — first-timers and swindlers enter
4. **Distress** — insiders sell
5. **Revulsion** (discredit) — outsiders stampede

### Three Structural Features
- **Asymmetric information**: insiders exploit outsiders fraudulently
- **Cross-border capital flows**: seasoned speculators exit better
- **Easy credit**: "without easy credit a true bubble cannot occur"

### *Business Week* as Contrary Indicator
- 13 August 1979: "The Death of Equities" — Dow at 875; then Dow
  rose nearly **12-fold** in 20 years
- 1999: *Dow 36,000* argued P/E of 100x was reasonable; dot-com
  crashed within 4 months

### Long-Run Stock Returns
- $10,000 in US stocks (1964) → **$700,000** (2007); bonds ~$23x;
  bills ~$12x; cash held as dollars: **down 85%**
- Best global: US stocks 4.73% real/year; worst: Argentina −5.36%
- 10 of 27 markets had **negative long-term real returns**
- Superior returns = **equity risk premium** — a risk often worth taking

### John Law — Origins of the Stock Bubble
- Convicted murderer, compulsive gambler, flawed financial genius
- May have caused the French Revolution by destroying the *ancien
  régime*'s best chance to reform
- Born Edinburgh 1671; fled to Amsterdam 1694 after killing a man

### Origins of the VOC
- **VOC** chartered **1602** — monopoly on Dutch trade east of Cape
  of Good Hope and west of Straits of Magellan
- Total raised: **6.45 million guilders** — dwarfed English East India
  Company (~820,000 guilders, 219 subscribers)
- Amsterdam: 1,143 subscribers; 445 invested <1,000 guilders
- 70 directors (*bewindhebbers*) → **Heeren XVII** (Seventeen Lords)
- Shares called *partijen* or *actien* ("a piece of the action")

### Birth of the Stock Market
- 1612: VOC declared it would not liquidate → shareholders wanting
  cash **had to sell** → secondary market born
- By 1607: one-third of VOC stock already transferred
- 1608: covered **Beurs** built on the Rokin
- Bulls (*liefhebbers*) vs. bears (*contremines*)

> "Hand-shakes are followed by shouting, insults, impudence, pushing
> and shoving." — Amsterdam Beurs

- **Isaac le Maire**: organized first short-selling attack on VOC

> "The account book must have been rubbed with bacon and fed to the
> dogs." — anonymous shareholder, 1622

### VOC's Commercial Empire
- **Jan Pieterszoon Coen**: seized Jakarta (**Batavia**) 1619;
  "We cannot make war without trade, nor trade without war"
- Wiped out indigenous Bandanese; executed British at Amboyna
- By 1650s: monopoly on cloves, mace, nutmeg; hub for intra-Asian
  trade (Japanese silver → Indian textiles → Chinese gold/silk)

### VOC Share Price — No Bubble
- 1602–1733: rose from 100 to peak of **786**; by 1650: dividends 8x
  original = **27% annual return**
- Unlike tulip bubble: gradual 100+ year ascent; descent took 60+ years
- Rise and fall tracked the Dutch Empire

### Law's Vision
- Concluded Dutch system incomplete: wrong to restrict share issuance;
  Exchange Bank too conservative

> "I have discovered the secret of the philosopher's stone, it is to
> make gold out of paper." — John Law

- Duke of Savoy demurred: "I am not rich enough to ruin myself."

## Chapter 3: The Mississippi Bubble

### Why France?
- Wars of **Louis XIV** left crown near third bankruptcy; *Visa*
  (debt review) already cancelled debts; economy in recession

### Building the System
- **Banque Générale** (May 1716) → **Banque Royale** (December 1718)
  — first French central bank
- Law's absolutist theory: "an absolute prince... can extend his
  credit further at lower interest"
- **Duke of Saint-Simon** warned:

> "Such a bank works only in a republic or monarchy like England...
> in an absolute state like France, stability must necessarily be
> wanting." — Duke of Saint-Simon

### Mississippi Company Expansion
- 1717: **Compagnie d'Occident** — 25-year Louisiana monopoly; shares
  purchasable with depreciated government debt (monetizing debt as equity)
- May 1719: absorbed East India/China companies → **Compagnie des Indes**
- July–Oct 1719: Law acquired mint profits, tax farm, lent 1.2B livres
  to pay off entire royal debt; took control of direct taxes

> Louis XIV said 'L'état, c'est moi.' John Law could say:
> 'L'économie, c'est moi.'

### The Bubble Mechanics
- "Mothers" → "daughters" (550 livres) → "granddaughters" (1,000) —
  each should have lowered price; instead it soared
- **Banque Royale** let shareholders borrow against shares — leverage loop
- Price: 2,750 (1 Aug) → 9,000 (autumn) → **10,025 (2 Dec 1719)**

> "Have you all gone crazy in Paris?" — **Voltaire**

- **Richard Cantillon** (Irish banker) sold up August 1719; made fortune
- **Daniel Defoe**: the French "run up a piece of refined air"
- The word ***millionaire*** coined during this frenzy
- **rue Quincampoix**: "princes and princesses... sell estates and
  pawn jewels to purchase Mississippi"
- December 1719: Law converted to Catholicism; appointed **Controller
  General** — controlled all indirect taxes, national debt, 26 mints,
  Louisiana, Mississippi Company, all overseas trade

### The Collapse
- By May 1720: money supply ~**4× larger** than all France's gold/silver
- Paris prices doubled in 11 months
- Law banned >500 livres of metal coin; gold price altered 28 times,
  silver 35 times

> Voltaire: "the most unjust edict ever rendered... the final limit
> of a tyrannical absurdity"

- 21 May 1720: decree to cut shares from 9,000 to 5,000 in steps AND
  halve banknotes — revoked 6 days later but confidence broken
- Shares: 9,005 (16 May) → 4,200 (31 May) → 2,000 (Sept) → 1,000 (Dec)
- Law fled December 1720 with almost nothing

> "I made mistakes because I am only human... but none proceeded from
> malice or dishonesty." — John Law

### Long-Term Consequences
- **Fatally set back France's financial development** — put Frenchmen
  off paper money and stock markets for generations
- French monarchy's fiscal crisis went unresolved → **French Revolution**

### Mississippi vs. South Sea
- South Sea Company **never gained control of the Bank of England**
- South Sea: £300 → £1,000 (June 1720); **Bubble Act** restricted
  new flotations; 190 companies had rushed to raise capital
- Price rise: South Sea 9.5× vs. Mississippi 19.6×; no lasting
  systemic damage in England

## The 1929 Crash and Great Depression

### Irving Fisher's Fatal Declaration
- 16 October 1929: "permanently high plateau"
- **Black Thursday**: Dow -2%; **Black Monday** (28 Oct): -13%;
  next day: -12%; over 3 years: **-89%**
- Didn't regain 1929 peak until **November 1954**
- Output collapsed by a third; unemployment hit 25%; world trade
  shrank **two-thirds**

### Structural Roots
- Global over-capacity from WWI; German reparation debts
- **RCA** rose 939% (1925–29); P/E at peak: 73; genuine innovations:
  **DuPont**, **P&G**, **IBM**; **Alfred Sloan** at GM
- Goldman Sachs Trading Corp announced 8 Aug 1929; margin buying rampant
- Insiders: **Charles Mitchell** (National City Bank), **William Durant**
  (GM); outsiders: **Groucho Marx**

### The Fed's Five Failures (1930–1933)
1. Nov–Dec 1930: 608 banks failed ($550M deposits) including **Bank of
   United States**; credit contraction not countered
2. Fed reduced credit (Dec 1930–Apr 1931); deposits fell $2.7B (9%)
3. Britain left gold (Sept 1931); Fed raised rates; 1,860 more banks
   failed; Fed gold reserves still exceeded legal requirements by >$1B
4. Large-scale open market ops only April 1932 — too late
5. Raised rates again during Roosevelt rumors; bank holiday 6 March 1933;
   **2,000 banks never returned**

- ~10,000 total failures; $15.6B deposit drop + $19.6B loan drop =
  **19% of 1929 GDP**

> "Inept monetary policy can turn a correction into a recession and
> a recession into a depression." — Ferguson

- **Friedman & Schwartz**: Fed should have aggressively injected liquidity
- The inter-war **gold standard itself** was the problem — transmitted
  crises internationally
- **Ben Bernanke** wrote his PhD on the Depression (noted by Ferguson)

### Fat Tails
- Under normal distribution: 10%+ annual drop once every 500 years;
  on the Dow: ~once every 5 years
- **Nine** crashes of 20%+ in the past century

### Black Monday 1987
- 19 October: Dow fell **23%** in a single session; ~$1 trillion lost
- Blamed on **portfolio insurance** and lack of circuit breakers
- **Greenspan** (2 months in): affirmed Fed as liquidity source;
  cut rates ~2% in sixteen days; no depression followed

### The Dot-Com Bubble and Enron
- 1990s displacement: PCs + Internet + accommodative monetary policy
- Fed rate cuts: 6% → 4.75% by late 1998; held until Dow crossed 10,000
- Greenspan's four reasons for not acting: (1) underestimated bubble;
  (2) believed Fed shouldn't target asset prices; (3) thought tech
  genuinely boosting productivity; (4) Russian default forced rate cuts

> The nineties: "a re-run of the Roaring Twenties" in trajectory —
> "more like a rerun of the 1720s" in other ways

### Enron: Mississippi Company of the 1990s
- *Fortune*'s "Most Innovative Company" six consecutive years
- **Kenneth Lay** parallels to Law: humble origins, government friends
- **Jeffrey Skilling** (McKinsey): create an 'Energy Bank'; **Phil
  Gramm** championed energy deregulation
- America's 4th-largest company; stock ~5x from <$20 to >$90
- Top 140 executives averaged $5.3M each; Skilling: "All that matters

- **Mark-to-market accounting** + **SPEs** concealed losses;

- California: **38 rolling blackouts** after deregulation; traders asked

- **Sherron Watkins** warned Lay August 2001
- Collapse: Oct 16 $618M loss; Nov 8 five-year profits restated ($567M);

- Lay guilty on 10 charges (died before sentencing); Skilling: 24 years

> "Law's philosopher's stone: 'gold out of paper.' Lay's: 'gold
> out of gas.'"

- Central thesis: companies are miraculous but **central bankers**

## Chapter 4: Taking Cover (Insurance)

### Origins of Insurance
- **Bottomry** (ship hull insurance): earliest form, 14th-century Italy
- **Francesco Datini** (c.1335–1410): risks "of God, of the sea, of
  men of war, of fire, of jettison"
- **Great Fire of 1666** → **Nicholas Barbon** established first fire
  insurance company
- **Lloyd's coffee house** → **Society of Lloyd's** (1774): 79 members,
  £15 subscription; underwriters had **unlimited liability**
- **Sun Insurance Office (1710)** brought limited liability

### Six Mathematical Breakthroughs (~1660 onward)
1. **Probability** — **Pascal** + **Fermat**
2. **Life expectancy** — **John Graunt** (1662) mortality statistics;
   **Edmund Halley** solved it with Breslau data: "100 to 1 that a Man
   of 20 dies not in a year, and but 38 to 1 for a Man of 50"
3. **Law of Large Numbers** — **Jacob Bernoulli** (1705)
4. **Normal distribution** — **Abraham de Moivre** (1733): bell curve
5. **Utility** — **Daniel Bernoulli** (1738): marginal utility declines
6. **Bayesian inference** — **Thomas Bayes** (1764): expected utility =
   probability × payoff

> "It was not merchants but mathematicians who were the true
> progenitors of modern insurance. Yet it took clergymen to turn
> theory into practice."

### The Scottish Ministers' Widows' Fund (1744)
- **Robert Wallace** and **Alexander Webster** with mathematician
  **Colin Maclaurin** created the first actuarial insurance fund
- Problem: widows received only half a year's stipend (Law of Ann, 1672)
- Innovation: premiums invested to grow a fund; widows paid from
  *returns* on investment, not just premiums
- Data: "930 ministers in life"; 27 die yearly, 18 leaving widows
- Projected £58,348 by 1765; **actual: £58,347** — off by one pound
- The **maximum principle**: capital accumulates until interest +
  contributions suffice for maximum annuities
- Within 20 years: copycat funds including **Presbyterian Ministers'
  Fund of Philadelphia (1761)**, **English Equitable Company (1762)**
- Fund became **Scottish Widows**; today manages >£100 billion

### Insurance as Institutional Investor
- Post-WWII: insurance companies owned ~one third of major UK companies
- Premiums as % of GDP: ~2% (pre-WWI) → ~10% today
