## Chapter 4: The Return of Risk — Safe as Houses

### Monopoly and the Property Dream
- **Monopoly** (Charles Darrow, 1935) — celebration of property ownership: "it's smart to own property, the more you own, the more you make"
- WWII: Monopoly boards used by British intelligence to smuggle escape kits (maps, currencies) to POWs in German camps via Red Cross packages
- **"Safe as houses"** — property uniquely safe collateral because unlike a borrower, the house can't run away (Germans call it *immobile* property)
- Since 1959, total US **mortgage debt** rose 75-fold; by end of 2006, owner-occupiers owed ~99% of US GDP vs. 38% fifty years before
- **Property-owning democracy**: 65-83% of households own homes across the Anglosphere — explicitly political, not just economic

> "Safe as houses" — the phrase tells you all you need to know about why people all over the world yearn to own their own homes.

### The Property-owning Aristocracy
- Pre-1832 English voting: only men owning **freehold property worth >=40 shillings/year** could vote — at most 435,000 people; 370 of 514 MPs selected by ~180 land-owning patrons
- Even today: ~40 million of 60 million UK acres owned by just **189,000 families**; the **Duke of Westminster** remains third-richest man in Britain (est. £7 billion)
- Aristocratic monopoly ended through **finance**: mid-19th century agricultural income collapse destroyed the economic basis of aristocratic power

### The Fall of the Duke of Buckingham
- **Richard Plantagenet Temple-Nugent-Brydges-Chandos-Grenville, 2nd Duke of Buckingham** — emblematic victim of the first modern property crash
- Owned ~67,000 acres; gross income £72,000/year — yet by 1845 spent £109,140/year; debts of **£1,027,282** (some at 15% interest)
- Fatal spending: refurbished Stowe House for Queen Victoria's 1845 visit — called out an entire Regiment of Yeomanry at personal expense; Queen remarked: *"I have no such splendour in either of my two palaces"*
- August 1848: entire contents of **Stowe House** auctioned — plate, wine, china, art, rare books — proceeds £75,000; *The Economist* sneered he was treated "as if the Duke were a bankrupt earthenware dealer"
- Died 1861 living at his son's expense in the Great Western Hotel at Paddington; his son was by then chairman of the London and North-western Railway Company
- **Key lesson**: in the modern world, a regular job mattered more than an inherited title

### Why Land Failed as Security
- Grain prices fell from $3/bushel (1847) to 50 cents (1894) after Corn Law repeal, falling transport costs, global supply expansion
- Returns on rural property: 3.65% (1845) → 2.51% (1885)

> *The Economist*: "No security was ever relied upon with more implicit faith, and few have lately been found more sadly wanting than English land."

- As late as 1938, fewer than one-third of UK housing was owner-occupied

### Home-owning Democracy
- Before the 1930s, fewer than two-fifths of American households were owner-occupiers; mortgages were short-term (3-5 years), **non-amortizing** with **balloon payments**
- Great Depression: 1932-33, over **500,000 foreclosures**; by mid-1933, 1,000+ mortgages foreclosed *per day*; house prices fell >20%

### Detroit 1932: The Revolutionary Moment
- **7 March 1932**: 5,000 unemployed Ford workers marched to River Rouge plant; police opened fire; **5 workers killed**; 60,000 sang 'The Internationale' at their funeral
- **Diego Rivera** commissioned by Edsel Ford to paint murals showing Detroit as cooperation — cost ~$25,000; murals unveiled 1933 caused outrage — Dr. George H. Derry: *"a Communist manifesto"*
- Rivera's next commission (Rockefeller Center for **John D. Rockefeller Jr.**): destroyed after he insisted on including a portrait of Lenin

### The New Deal Housing Revolution
- **FDR's** answer to revolutionary threat: **property-owning democracy** as antidote to communism
- Key institutions:
  - **Home Owners' Loan Corporation**: refinanced mortgages on longer terms (up to 15 years)
  - **Federal Housing Administration (FHA)**: federally backed insurance; encouraged large (80% LTV), long (20-year), fully **amortized**, low-interest loans
  - **1938**: **Fannie Mae** authorized to issue bonds and buy mortgages from S&Ls
  - S&Ls restricted by **Regulation Q** (deposit rate ceiling) and geography (could not lend >50 miles from office)
- Result: home ownership rate rose from 40% → 60% by 1960

> *It's a Wonderful Life* (Capra, 1946) as propaganda for the S&L model — George Bailey: "It's deep in the race for a man to want his own roof and walls and fireplace."

### Red-lining: The Hidden Exclusion
- 1941: a Detroit developer built a **six-foot wall** across 8 Mile Road to qualify for FHA loans (loans only on white side)
- Federal Home Loan Bank Board maps **color-coded** neighborhoods: D = red = predominantly black → origin of **"red-lining"**
- In the 1950s: 1 in 5 black mortgage borrowers paid >=8%; virtually no whites paid >7%
- **Detroit riot, 23 July 1967**: 43 killed, 467 injured, 7,200 arrested, 3,000 buildings burned — required regular army with tanks
- **1968**: Fannie Mae split into **Ginnie Mae** (poor borrowers, veterans) and rechartered Fannie Mae (privately owned GSE); **1970**: **Freddie Mac** created
- **Community Reinvestment Act (1977)**: statutory pressure to lend to poorer minority communities
- The **"3-6-3 rule"** — pay 3% on deposits, lend at 6%, be on the golf course by 3pm

### Britain's Path
- **Thatcher's revolution**: sold council houses at bargain prices to 1.5 million working-class families → owner-occupier share: 54% (1981) → 67% (1991)
- US mortgage interest deduction existed since **1913** (inception of federal income tax)

### The Inflationary Free Lunch
- 1960s-70s: inflation rates rose above interest rates → debtors got a **free lunch**
- Mid-1970s: homebuyers expected 12% inflation by 1980; lenders offered 30-year fixed loans at <=9% → lenders effectively paying people to borrow
- Property prices roughly **trebled** 1963-79 while consumer prices rose only 2.5x

### From S&L to Subprime

### The S&L Fraud Epidemic
- After deregulation: S&Ls could issue credit cards, pay any interest rate, raise money via "jumbo" $100,000 CDs — yet deposits still federally insured (max raised from $40k to $100k)
- Classic **moral hazard**: nothing to lose, everything to gamble with
- **William Crawford** (CA S&L Commissioner): "The best way to rob a bank is to own one"
- Dallas nexus of fraud: **Don Dixon** (Vernon S&L, "Vermin"), **Ed McBirney** (Sunbelt/"Gunbelt"), **Tyrell Barker** (State S&L/"You bring the dirt, I bring the money")

### Danny Faulkner and Empire Savings & Loan
- **Empire S&L** chairman Spencer Blain teamed with developer **Danny Faulkner**
- Faulkner's specialty: "the flip" — one parcel bought for $3 million, sold days later for $47 million
- Empire's assets grew from $12M to $257M in two years
- Dallas I-30 corridor lined with "see-through" office buildings (empty, still under construction)
- Faulkner flew investors over properties by helicopter — without landing
- **Edwin J. Gray** ordered Empire's closure March 14, 1984; cost to FSLIC: $300 million

### Scale of the S&L Catastrophe
- ~500 S&Ls collapsed; ~500 more merged under the **Resolution Trust Corporation**
- Near half of insolvent institutions had fraud by insiders
- By May 1991: 764 charged, 550 convicted, 326 jailed
- **Total cost 1986-1995: $153 billion (~3% of GDP); taxpayers bore $124B**

> "The most widespread, reckless and fraudulent era in this nation's banking history." — Edwin Gray

### The Birth of Securitization
- **Lewis Ranieri** (started in Salomon mailroom) bought desperate S&Ls' mortgages at rock-bottom prices
- Innovation: bundle thousands of mortgages as backing for **mortgage-backed securities (MBS)**; interest payments subdivided into "strips" with different maturities and credit risks
- First **collateralized mortgage obligation (CMO)**: June 1983
- GSE-backed MBS grew from $200M (1980) to $4 trillion (2007)
- In 1980: 10% of home mortgage market securitized; by 2007: 56%
- Contrast with *It's a Wonderful Life*: Jimmy Stewart knew his depositors AND debtors — in a securitized market the interest payer and ultimate recipient are strangers

### Property vs. Stocks — The "Safe as Houses" Myth
- $100,000 into US property Q1 1987 → ~$275K-$299K by Q1 2007. Same into S&P 500 with reinvested dividends → **$772,000**
- UK: £100K into property 1987 → quadrupled; into FTSE All-Share → **nearly seven times richer**
- Three disadvantages of housing: (1) depreciation, (2) illiquidity, (3) vulnerability to crashes
- **Case-Shiller composite 20**: peaked July 2006; down 15% by Feb 2008 — not seen since early 1930s

### Subprime: Detroit as Ground Zero
- "Subprime" mortgages targeted borrowers with poor credit histories — too risky for Fannie Mae
- Features: **adjustable-rate mortgages (ARMs)**, interest-only, 100% LTV, **"teaser" periods** (e.g., 9.75% for 2 years, then 9.125 points over LIBOR)
- 2006: subprime lenders injected **>$1 billion** into 22 Detroit ZIP codes
- Most subprime volume was **refinancing** — borrowers using homes as cash machines
- **Racial disparity**: 55% of Black and Latino borrowers had subprime mortgages (2005) vs. 13% of white borrowers

### Political Promotion
- **President George W. Bush**, Oct 2002: "We want everybody in America to own their own home" — signed **American Dream Downpayment Act** (2003)
- **HUD** pressured Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac to support subprime market
- **Beanie Self** (Memphis): "People don't realize you have a real estate industry, an appraisal industry, a mortgage industry now that can really push to put people into houses that a lot of times they really can't afford."

### The CDO Machine
- Banks bundled loans into **RMBS** → repackaged as **CDOs** → triple-A rated by **Moody's** / **S&P**
- Risk spread globally — **Rana, Hemnes, Hattjelldal, and Narvik** (Norway): invested $120M of taxpayers' money in CDOs backed by American subprime mortgages
- "Securitization was having the effect of allocating risk to those best able to bear it. Only later did it turn out that risk was being allocated to those least able to understand it."
- Brokers could make **NINJA loans** ("No Income No Job or Assets") and sell same day

### The Crisis Unfolds
- Fed raised short-term rates from 1% to 5.25%
- By March 2007: ~1 in 3 subprime mortgages in 48235 ZIP 60+ days in arrears
- By May 2008: 1.8M mortgages in default; ~9M households in negative equity
- Credit Suisse estimate: total foreclosures could reach **6.5 million**

### Contagion: From CDOs to Global Crisis
- **HSBC** (Feb 2007): admitted heavy losses — first major signal
- **Bear Stearns** hedge funds collapsed; ratings agencies downgraded scores of RMBS CDOs
- Banks had disguised exposure in off-balance-sheet **conduits** and **SIVs**
- **Counterparty risk** fears → credit markets seized
- **Northern Rock** nationalized; **Bear Stearns** sold to JPMorgan; bank write-downs >=**$318B**; anticipated total losses >$1 trillion
- "The subprime butterfly had flapped its wings and triggered a global hurricane."

### Fannie Mae's Fatal Irony
- Fannie/Freddie had only $84B capital against $1.7T in assets plus $2.8T in guaranteed RMBS — capital ratio ~5%

### Hernando de Soto's "Mystery of Capital"
- Total value of real estate occupied by world's poor = **$9.3 trillion** — roughly equal to total market cap of all listed companies in the world's top 20 economies; ~90x all foreign aid 1970-2000
- Core problem: squatters lack **secure legal title** → property can't be used as collateral → **"Dead capital"**
- Bureaucratic barriers: Peru: 6 years 11 months, 52 government offices; Philippines: 168-step, 13-25 year process involving 53 agencies; English-speaking world: 2 days to 3 weeks

> "Property law is not a silver bullet, but it is the missing link." — Hernando de Soto

- **Bill Clinton** called de Soto "probably the greatest living economist"; won **Cato Institute Milton Friedman Prize** (2004)
- **Shining Path** (Maoist) tried to assassinate him in 1992 (bomb killed three people)

### Does Title Actually Work?
- **Quilmes** (Buenos Aires): natural experiment — some squatters got title, others didn't
- Title-holders 34% more likely to say "money is important for happiness" — but only **4%** secured a mortgage
- Peru: only ~25% of title recipients obtained any loan by 2002
- Cambodia: granting title simply encouraged developers to buy out or evict poor residents
- Key insight: **"It's not owning property that gives you security; it just gives your creditors security. Real security comes from having a steady income."**

### Microfinance
- **Betty Flores**, El Alto, Bolivia: indigenous woman running coffee stall, client of **Pro Mujer** microfinance
- **Muhammad Yunus** (Nobel Prize): **Grameen Bank** ("Village Bank"), founded 1983 — ~7.5 million borrowers, nearly all women, no collateral required
- Borrowers organized in five-member groups (**koota**), meeting weekly
- Made >$3 billion in microloans; now self-sustaining and profitable
- Key revelation: **women are actually better credit risks than men**

### Limits of Microfinance
- ~2/5 of world population outside financial system entirely
- **Predatory microfinance**: some firms charge 80-125% interest annually

### Housing Bubble — The "Home Bias" Problem
- **Robert Shiller**: house prices rose far above rental income or construction cost justification — "classic speculative bubble"
- **Home bias**: housing = two-thirds of typical US household's portfolio
- Political encouragement of home ownership forces people to bet everything on a single leveraged, illiquid asset
- Financial security requires **diversified portfolio** with sustainable debt-to-income balance

## Chapter 6: From Empire to Chimerica

### The Great Divergence
- 300-400 years ago: per capita incomes in West and East roughly equal
- Ming China more sophisticated in many ways; Beijing was world's largest city; **Zheng He's** treasure ship dwarfed Columbus's *Santa Maria*
- By 1820: US income ~2x China; by 1870: ~5x; by 1913: ~10x; by 1950: ~22x
- **BRICs** projection: China to overtake US GDP revised to **2027**; China per capita GDP growth: compound annual rate of **8.4%** over 30 years

### Why China Fell Behind — A Financial Hypothesis
- China's problems were as much **financial as resource-based**
- Unitary empire precluded **fiscal competition** that drove financial innovation in Renaissance Europe
- Empire could finance deficits by printing money → discouraged development of capital markets
- Modern financial institutions arrived only in late 19th century as part of **Western imperialism**

### Globalization Is Not New
- Pre-1914 globalization was as extensive as today's in key dimensions
- International migration larger: >14% of US population foreign-born in 1910 vs. <12% in 2003
- Net foreign investment (rich → poor) was **much larger** in earlier period
- In 1913: 25% of world's foreign capital in countries with per capita income <=1/5 of US; by 1997: only **5%**

### The Opium War — Finance Backed by Force
- **William Jardine** and **James Matheson**: Scottish merchants importing government-produced opium from India
- March 1839: Imperial official **Lin Zexu** blockaded opium warehouses; ~20,000 chests (value £2 million) destroyed
- Jardine (known to Chinese as "**Iron-Headed Old Rat**") lobbied **Viscount Palmerston**

> "The Qing Empire was about to feel the full force of history's most successful narco-state: the British Empire."

- **Treaty of Nanking** (1842): Hong Kong ceded; five treaty ports opened; principle of **extraterritoriality**
- For China: drug addiction exploded; **Taiping Rebellion** killed 20-40 million
- Jardine Matheson: owned breweries, cotton mills, insurance company, ferry company, railways

### Victorian Globalization — Scale of British Overseas Investment
- By 1914: ~40 foreign stock exchanges; LSE listed bonds from 57 sovereign/colonial governments
- 1865-1914: **majority** of British capital raised went overseas; <1/3 invested in UK itself
- Gross foreign assets 1913: ~**150% of UK GDP**; current account surplus hit **9% of GDP**

> "It required scarcely any effort for a Londoner of moderate means to 'adventure his wealth in the natural resources and new enterprises of any quarter of the world.'" — **Keynes**

### Empire as Credit Guarantee
- 2/5 to 1/2 of all British overseas investment went to **British-controlled colonies**
- **Colonial Stock Act (1900)**: gave colonial bonds same **trustee status** as British consols
- British rule = implicit **"no default"** guarantee

### The Pre-1914 Golden Age — and Its Illusions
- By 1913 almost no sovereign bond traded more than **2 percentage points** above British consols
- **Ivan Bloch**, *The Future of War* (1898): argued modern war too destructive
- **Norman Angell**, *The Great Illusion* (1910): major war economically impossible
- **Henry Noel Brailsford**: "there will be no more wars among the six great powers"

### The 1914 Financial Crisis
- Financial markets initially **shrugged off** assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand (28 June 1914)
- When war likelihood grasped: liquidity sucked out "as if the bottom had dropped out of a bath"
- **Closure of world's major stock exchanges** for up to five months
  - London Stock Exchange didn't reopen until **4 January 1915** — first closure since founding in 1773
  - NYSE fully unrestricted trading not until **1 April 1915**
- "No act of state-sponsored terrorism has had greater financial consequences than Gavrilo Princip's in 1914"
- Emergency measures: bank holidays extended, moratoria on payments, emergency currency issued
- "Then, as now, the authorities reacted to a liquidity crisis by printing money"

### Cost of the Great War to Investors
- Investors who held **gilt-edged securities** through the war: inflation-adjusted losses of -46% by 1920
- Hyperinflation in Germany: by 1923 holders of all German securities had lost everything
- **Bolshevik regime** defaulted on Russian bonds February 1918
- Habsburg, Ottoman, and Romanov empires all collapsed — gold-denominated bonds became worthless

> "The first era of financial globalization took at least a generation to achieve. But it was blown apart in a matter of days."

### Collapse of the First Globalization
- Post-WWI: currency crises, defaults, Depression → exchange and capital controls + protectionist tariffs
- Key insight: financial markets were flashing green until the very eve of WWI — "abundant liquidity and the passage of time" blinded investors to geopolitical risk

### Bretton Woods and Controlled Globalization (1944-1971)
- **Bretton Woods**: trade liberalized but capital movements restricted
- **Keynes**: "control of capital movements" would be a "permanent feature of the post-war system"
- **The Impossible Trinity (trilemma)**: a country can choose only 2 of 3: free capital movements, fixed exchange rate, independent monetary policy
- Created **IMF** and **World Bank**; Marshall Plan revived Western Europe
- Third World aid programmes modeled on Marshall Plan largely failed

### Nixon Shock and Hot Money
- 1971: **Nixon** severed dollar-gold convertibility → death knell for Bretton Woods
- 1970s: Western banks recycled **petrodollar** surpluses; Latin American borrowings: $75 billion (1975) → $315 billion (1982)
- August 1982: **Mexico** declared inability to service its debt

### Washington Consensus and Conditionality
- IMF replaced gunboat as enforcer; **structural adjustment programmes**
- **Washington Consensus**: fiscal discipline, tax reform, market-set rates, trade/capital liberalization

### Stiglitz's Critique
- **Joseph Stiglitz**: IMF championed market supremacy with "ideological fervour"; premature capital liberalization "contributed to global instability"
- 1997 Asian crisis: IMF lent $95 billion but conditions worsened the crisis
- Ferguson's counter: capital liberalization driven first by **OECD**, then **European Commission** — arguably a "Paris Consensus" before Washington
- **Kenneth Rogoff** to Stiglitz: governments come to IMF when they can't find buyers for their debt — printing money causes inflation, not recovery

### George Soros and Reflexivity
- **George Soros**: Hungarian-born, named fund **Quantum Fund** after Heisenberg's Uncertainty Principle
- **Reflexivity**: financial markets cannot be perfectly efficient because prices reflect biases of millions of investors; those biases shape the events markets supposedly predict

> "Markets never reach the equilibrium postulated by economic theory. There is a two-way reflexive connection between perception and reality which can give rise to initially self-reinforcing but eventually self-defeating boom-bust processes, or bubbles." — Soros

### Black Wednesday: Soros Breaks the Bank of England
- Reasoned: German reunification costs → Bundesbank rates up → Britain's ERM peg untenable
- Borrowed **$10 billion** in sterling, invested in German currency
- **16 September 1992**: Britain withdrew from ERM; sterling slumped 20%; Soros profit: **>$1 billion**
- To partner **Stanley Druckenmiller**: "One billion dollars? You call that a position?"
- **Quantum Fund**: $100,000 invested 1969 → $130 million by 1994; average annual growth 35%

### LTCM: Black-Scholes and Mathematical Certainty
- **Black-Scholes model**: revolutionary formula for pricing options
- **Myron Scholes** + **Fisher Black** + **Robert Merton** (third collaborator)
- 1994: **Long-Term Capital Management (LTCM)** founded in Greenwich by **John Meriwether** (former Salomon bond arbitrage head)
- Dream team: Scholes + Merton (future Nobel laureates) + former Fed vice-chairman **David Mullins**
- First two years: returns of 43% and 41% after fees
- By September 1997: net capital $6.7 billion; leverage: **debt-financed assets $126.4 billion** — ratio 19:1
- Models said this was safe: ~100 uncorrelated strategies, 7,600 positions

### LTCM's Core Strategy and Fatal Bet
- Exploited **pricing anomalies** — any market where identical assets had fractionally different prices
- Biggest bet: selling long-dated options on stock markets — betting volatility would revert to 10-13%
- Earned nickname **"Central Bank of Volatility"**; $40 million riding on each percentage point change
- **VaR** models said losing all capital in a year had probability 1 in 10^24
- Models built on only *five years* of data — missing 1987 crash and 1917 Russian default

### The Nobel Prize and the End
- October 1997: **Merton and Scholes** awarded Nobel Prize
- December 1997: LTCM returned $2.7 billion to outside investors — peak arrogance
- **August 17, 1998**: Russia defaulted on rouble bonds; credit spreads exploded; volatility hit 45%
- **Friday August 21, 1998**: LTCM lost $550 million — 15% of capital — in a single day; leverage soared to 42:1
- All markets moved in sync, **correlations went to 1**, nullifying diversification

> "John, you were the correlation." — London hedge fund manager to Meriwether

### The Rescue
- Meriwether tried **Warren Buffett** (who had been shorted by LTCM — he declined)
- Sought **George Soros** — "the ultimate humiliation: quants from Planet Finance begging a bail-out from the earthling prophet of irrational, unquantifiable reflexivity"
- **Federal Reserve Bank of New York** brokered $3.625 billion bail-out by fourteen Wall Street banks
- Original investors saw holdings fall from $4.9 billion to $400 million

### Why LTCM Failed
- Black-Scholes assumed markets efficient, continuous, liquid; in crisis, participants *withdraw*, leaving illiquid portfolios
- Many firms copied LTCM's strategies — herd stampede for exits

> "If I had lived through the Depression, I would have been in a better position to understand events." — **John Meriwether**

> "The Nobel prize winners had known plenty of mathematics, but not enough history." — Ferguson

- **Long-Term Capital Management** ended up being **Short-Term Capital Mismanagement**

### The Hedge Fund Explosion
- Assets: 600 funds / $39bn (1990) → 7,601 / $1.9 trillion (Q1 2008)
- Returns falling: 18% (1990s) → 7.5% (2000-2006)
- 2007 earnings: **John Paulson** $3.7bn, **George Soros** $2.9bn, **James Simons** (Renaissance)
- **John Kay's point**: if Buffett had charged "2 and 20," he would have kept $57bn of the $62bn Berkshire made for shareholders over 42 years

### The "Bogus Hedge Fund" Template
- Park $100m in T-bills at 4%; sell covered options paying out if S&P falls >20%; earn **15.4% return**
- 60% chance this runs >5 years without triggering options = $15m profit for manager
- **Counterparty risk** and misaligned incentives created systemic threat

## "Chimerica"

### The China Paradox
- **Chongqing** (32m people): embodiment of China's transformation
- **Wu Yajun** ($9bn net worth), **Yin Mingsha** (Lifan motorcycles, 1.5m units/year) — 345,000+ dollar millionaires in China
- China avoided 1997-8 crises by: devaluing renminbi in 1994, retaining **capital controls**, requiring foreign capital as **direct investment**

### The Capital Flow Reversal
- Chinese households save unusually high proportion of income; Americans saved almost nothing
- 2007: US needed to borrow ~$800bn from rest of world ($4bn+ every working day)
- China ran $262bn current account surplus — "**The People's Republic of China has become banker to the United States**"
- China recycled export earnings into **US Treasury bonds** — some called this **"Bretton Woods II"**

### Chimerica: The Symbiosis

> "Welcome to the wonderful dual country of 'Chimerica' — China plus America — which accounts for just over a tenth of the world's land surface, a quarter of its population, a third of its economic output and more than half of global economic growth in the past eight years."

- **East Chimericans** saved; **West Chimericans** spent
- Chinese imports kept down US inflation; Chinese savings kept down US interest rates; Chinese labour kept down US wage costs
- **Ben Bernanke's "savings glut"** — Chimerica was the underlying cause of the surge in bank lending, hedge fund explosion, and mortgage market awash with cash

### The Fragility of Chimerica
- **Sovereign wealth funds** by end of 2007: ~$2.6 trillion; could reach $27 trillion within 15 years
- The Chimerica analogy to 1914: then it was **Britain-Germany** symbiosis that fractured into catastrophe

> "Maybe, as its name was always intended to hint, Chimerica is nothing more than a chimera — the mythical beast of ancient legend that was part lion, part goat, part dragon."

### Three Lessons of Financial History
1. Major wars can arise even when economic globalization is advanced
2. The longer without major conflict, the harder one becomes to imagine (and perhaps easier to start)
3. Crises cause much more disruption when they strike complacent investors — the really big crises come just seldom enough to be beyond living memory
- **Average Wall Street CEO career: just over 25 years** — memories don't extend back beyond 1983

## Afterword: The Descent of Money

### The Four-Millennium Summary
- Money → banks → bond markets (13th c.) → stock markets (17th c.) → insurance/pension funds (18th c.) → futures/options/derivatives (19th c.) → leveraged real estate democracy (20th c.)
- Economies combining all these institutions outperformed those that didn't
- **Frederic Mishkin**: "the financial system [is] the brain of the economy"

### Three Reasons for Inherent Instability

**1. Radical Uncertainty**
- **Frank Knight** (1921): distinction between *measurable* risk and *unmeasurable* uncertainty
- **Keynes** (1937): "uncertain knowledge" means not merely probable but unknowable — "we simply do not know"
- Three coping mechanisms: (1) assume present guides future; (2) assume existing prices are correct; (3) conform to majority behaviour

**2. Human Behavioural Biases**
- **Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky**: people are **risk-averse for gains, risk-seeking for losses**; a loss has ~2.5x the psychological impact of an equivalent gain
- Ten **heuristic biases**: availability bias, hindsight bias, problem of induction, fallacy of conjunction, confirmation bias, contamination effects, affect heuristic, scope neglect, overconfidence in calibration, bystander apathy

**3. Evolutionary/Darwinian Dynamics**
- **Andrew Lo** (MIT): re-conceptualizing markets as adaptive systems
- **Thorstein Veblen** first asked "Why is Economics Not an Evolutionary Science?"

### Creative Destruction in Finance
- **Joseph Schumpeter**: capitalism driven by internal mutation — new goods, methods, markets, organizational forms

> "This process of Creative Destruction is the essential fact about capitalism." — Schumpeter

- ~1 in 10 US companies disappears each year; of the world's 100 largest companies in 1912: 29 bankrupt by 1995, 48 disappeared, only 19 still in top 100
- **Banks don't fail as often only because governments protect them from collapse**

### Six Features of Finance as Evolutionary System
1. **Genes** — business practices as genetic material
2. **Mutation** — spontaneous innovation
3. **Competition** — firms compete for resources
4. **Natural selection** — market allocation; under-performance = death
5. **Speciation** — wholly new types of institution
6. **Extinction** — species die out

- Most financial mutation is **deliberate, conscious innovation** — more **Lamarckian than Darwinian**
- Financial history *begins* at a common trunk and **branches outward** — speciation, not consolidation

### Financial Crises as Mass Extinctions
- Crucial difference from biology: asteroids are exogenous; financial crises are **endogenous**
- 2007 credit crunch: total losses estimated >$1 trillion; **shadow banking system** makes contraction especially difficult
- European average bank capital now ~4% of assets (was ~25% in early 20th century)

> "As with past forest fires in the markets, we're likely to see incredible flora and fauna springing up in its wake." — **Andrew Lo**

### Regulation as "Intelligent Design"
- **"Too big to fail"** problem: implicit bailout guarantees create **moral hazard**
- From evolutionary perspective: TBTF institutions are *actively harmful* — without creative destruction, evolution is thwarted
- **Japan in the 1990s**: propping up insolvent banks created an "economic dead hand" for a decade

> "This economic system cannot do without the *ultima ratio* of the complete destruction of those existences which are irretrievably associated with the hopelessly unadapted." — Schumpeter

### Ferguson's Conclusion: The Mirror of Mankind
- Financial history has a **saw-tooth quality** — great reverses and crises — but no crash has set humanity permanently back
- Title nods to **Jacob Bronowski's** *Ascent of Man*

> "Financial markets are like the mirror of mankind, revealing every hour of every working day the way we value ourselves and the resources of the world around us. It is not the fault of the mirror if it reflects our blemishes as clearly as our beauty." — Ferguson

- **401(k) plans** introduced in 1980 as defined-contribution retirement vehicles
- **Fibonacci sequence** first appeared as *matrameru* in Sanskrit scholar **Pingala's** work
- **Illiquidity** = right assets but not marketable; **Insolvency** = liabilities exceed assets
- Mortgage debt rose from 54% to **140% of disposable personal income**
- **Highest recorded inflation**: Hungary, July 1946 — prices rose 4.19 quintillion per cent
- Long-run average P/E ratio (US, since 1871): **15.5x**; peak 1999: 32.6x
- Between 1997-2006, US consumers withdrew **$9 trillion** in cash from home equity
- In many US states, mortgages are **"no recourse"** loans — lender can only seize property
- House prices in England rose **15-fold** between 1975 and 2006
- **Enron** was originally going to be named **Enteron** — Greek for intestines
- **Charles Keating** (Lincoln S&L) received support from five Senators including **John McCain**
- Term "hedge fund" first used in 1966 for **Alfred Winslow Jones's** 1949 long-short fund

### Nathan Rothschild to Prussian Treasury Director Rother:
> "Dearest friend, I have now done my duty by God, your king and the Finance Minister von Rother... the King of Prussia should be well pleased and thank Rothschild, who is sending you so much money and raising Prussia's credit."

Ferguson: "That a Jew born in the Frankfurt ghetto could write in these terms to a Prussian official speaks volumes about the social revolution Nathan Rothschild and his brothers personified."

### Anti-Semitism & Finance
- **J. A. Hobson** (*Imperialism*, 1902): claimed banking and loan-floating form "the central ganglion of international capitalism," controlled "chiefly by men of a single and peculiar race"

### Insurance & Disasters
- Gulf Coast hurricane policies typically exclude "Water Damage" — small print "not readily intelligible to the policyholder"
- US property/casualty insurers: Katrina ultimately *enriched* the industry — $43bn (2005) and $64bn (2006) vs. avg <$24bn prior
- **Sir Walter Scott** ruined by the 1825 Latin American debt crisis — debts of £117,000-£130,000; vowed to write his way back to solvency; succeeded but died in 1832 of the effort

### Risk & Capital Regulation
- **Peter Bernstein**: "We pour in data from the past... but past data constitute a sequence of events rather than a set of independent observations... History provides us with only one sample"
- **Basel I** (1988): 8% capital against risk-weighted assets; **Basel II** (2004): liquidity risk classified as mere "residual risk" — ironic given 2007-8
- **Andrew Lo**: "Hedge funds are the Galapagos Islands of finance... The rate of innovation, evolution, competition, adaptation, births and deaths occurs at an extraordinarily rapid clip"
- JP Morgan EM bond spread: 3.3% (Oct 1997) → **17.05%** (10 Sep 1998) — scale of LTCM-era panic

### Sovereign Wealth Funds
- Longer history than assumed: **Kuwait Investment Authority** (1953), **Singapore Temasek** (1974)
- China Investment Corporation chairman **Lou Jiwei** on buying 9.9% of Morgan Stanley for $5bn: "If we see a big fat rabbit, we will shoot at it" — then added, "Some people may say we were shot by Morgan Stanley"
