## Chapter 1: Winter Comes Again

### America's Mood of Unraveling
- Despite peace and prosperity, America settled into pessimism — trust in institutions at new lows, highest incarceration rate, lowest voter participation of any major democracy
- Optimism attaches to self, not family or community; parents fear the American Dream won't exist for their children
- Civic problems feel like an insoluble Rubik's Cube — each problem requires fixing another first

> "We are unsettled to the very roots of our being." — **Walter Lippmann** during WWI

### The Four Turnings (Core Framework)
Anglo-American society enters a new era every two decades. Four turnings span one **saeculum** (~80-100 years, a long human life):

1. **First Turning (High)**: Strengthening institutions, weakening individualism, new civic order
2. **Second Turning (Awakening)**: Spiritual upheaval, civic order attacked by new values
3. **Third Turning (Unraveling)**: Strengthening individualism, weakening institutions
4. **Fourth Turning (Crisis)**: Secular upheaval, old civic order replaced by new one

**Current saeculum**: American High (post-WWII) → Consciousness Revolution (1960s-80s) → Culture Wars (1984-2005?)

### Three Prior Unravelings → Their Crises

**1920s Unraveling** (1918-1929): Post-WWI euphoria gave way to jazz-age nihilism — immigration into ghettos, KKK resurgence, union atrophy, government weakness. "Lost" generation's risky pleasures shocked decency crusaders. Parents protected scoutlike new generation (today's senior citizens).

**1850s Unraveling**: Mexican War victory short-lived. Cities mean and hateful, financial speculation boomed. Slavery debate erupted; Whigs and Democrats disintegrating. College enrollments plummeted as youth hustled west to gold towns.

**1760s Unraveling**: England's post-war taxation sparked discontent. **Benjamin Franklin** called it "white savagery" of youth. Colonists split into mutually loathing camps over the Crown.

**Common patterns**: frenetic individualism + social fragmentation anxiety; recent foreign victory followed by pessimism; aggressive moralism; immigration attacked; personal values secure but civic life corrupt; **res publica** on verge of disintegrating.

**Each Third Turning → Fourth Turning Crisis**: 1760s → Revolution; 1850s → Civil War; 1920s → Depression/WWII. Each came with scant warning (Boston Tea Party, John Brown's raid, Black Tuesday). Each required massive sacrifices and created a new social contract: 1790s created first democratic republic; 1860s forged genuine nation with liberty/equality; 1940s built Promethean superpower.

### Prediction: Next Fourth Turning
- Begins around **2005**, catalyzed by a sudden spark
- Before **2025**, America passes through a great gate in history
- **Risks**: could erupt into insurrection, civil violence, geographic crack-up, or authoritarian rule
- If war occurs, likely **total war** — each Fourth Turning has escalated destructive technology
- **Opportunities**: unique chance to achieve new greatness, renaissance of civic trust
- Third Turning's "Rubik's Cube" problems snap into Fourth Turning solutions
- Two possible outcomes: **Apocalypse** (nation ruined) or **Glory** (new golden age)

> "History is seasonal, and winter is coming."

### Theories of Time
Three conceptions: **Chaotic** (random, no meaning), **Cyclical** (natural rhythms, eternal return), **Linear** (progress toward endpoint). Linear time dominates America but cracks when progress stalls. Key thinkers: **St. Augustine** ("Only the wicked walk in circles"), **Mircea Eliade** on traditional cyclical cultures, **Mark Twain** on American newness obsession.

Failed linear forecasts: **Rostow**, **Kahn**, **Reich**, **Toffler**, **Fukuyama** — all predicted imminent final stage of history. Linearists' flaw: reduce future to straight-line extrapolation.

**The alternative**: restore cyclical perspective without losing hope of progress. **Aristotle**: history must reconnect to "timeless forms." Time's physical essence is cyclical (etymology: *time* from *tide*, *annual* from *annus* = circle).

## Chapter 2: Seasons of Time

### Generation as Historical Unit
- **Generation**: aggregate born over one phase of life (~20 years), sharing common collective persona
- Four phases: **childhood, young adulthood, midlife, elderhood**
- Each new constellation shifts national mood — roughly every 20 years

> "History creates generations, and generations create history."

### Why Forecasters Fail
- Late 1950s forecasters predicted orderly progress — missed Tet, Apollo 11, Watts, Watergate, Earth Day
- Late 1970s forecasts for 1990s equally wrong — predicted acceleration of 1960s trends
- **Error**: assumed linear extrapolation, ignored generational lifecycle dynamics

### Four Generational Archetypes
- **Prophet** (born during High): vision, values, moralism
- **Nomad** (born during Awakening): liberty, survival, pragmatism
- **Hero** (born during Unraveling): community, teamwork, civic duty
- **Artist** (born during Crisis): pluralism, expertise, process

Each archetype = expression of enduring temperaments. When history overlays archetypes atop turnings → four very different constellations, explaining why mood shifts every ~20 years.

> "The farther backward you look, the farther forward you are likely to see." — **Winston Churchill**

## The Etruscan Saeculum

### Origins and Roman Adoption
- **Etruscans** (pre-Roman Italy) invented ritual to measure the saeculum — marked by death of longest-lived person who remembered founding day
- First six Etruscan saecula averaged **107 years**
- Romans became obsessed: held **ludi saeculares** (three-day civic festivals) roughly once per century
- Romans distinguished **civil saeculum** (strict 100 years) from **natural saeculum** (stuff of life and destiny)
- Vulture augury: Rome fell to Alaric **A.D. 410** — roughly twelve saecula after legendary founding

### Ancient Cyclical Beliefs
- Nearly every ancient culture saw sacred time as rounded: Hindu *mandala*, Buddhist *chakra*, Chinese *tai chi*, Greek *kyklos*, Celtic Mag Ruith, Germanic rings, Mayan calendars, Native American sacred hoops
- Universal symbols: **looping serpent** (periodic renewal), **circle dance** (unbreakable continuity)
- Fourfold time dominated: seasons, directions, elements, humors, Hindu *yugas*, Greek metal ages, Daniel's "four ages of man"

### The Saeculum Emerges as Paramount
- As modernization occurs, one circle dominates: the natural human life span
- **Central irony**: life span dominates history's rhythm precisely when modern society has abandoned cyclical for linear time
- Modern generations exercise freedom to reshape environment based on life-cycle experiences

## The Saeculum Rediscovered

### Medieval Dormancy to Renaissance Revival
- After Rome fell, saeculum lay dormant ~1000 years; word lost specific meaning
- Renaissance: elites perceived themselves as rational actors capable of altering destiny
- **Erasmus** (1517): "Immortal God, what a century I see opening up before us!"
- **Fin-de-siècle** mood periodically reappeared — completion, exhaustion, escapism

### The Saeculum of War and Peace
- **Quincy Wright** (*Study of War*, 1942): war waging occurred "in approximately fifty-year oscillations"
- **Arnold Toynbee** (*A Study of History*): identified five repetitions of quarter-century "general wars" at roughly one-century intervals since Renaissance
- Average span between general wars: **ninety-five years** with "surprising coincidence" across millennia
- **George Modelski**: four-quarter-century phases — world power → delegitimizing → deconcentration → global war

### Anglo-American Crises: Mathematical Precision
- **85 years** between Pearl Harbor and Fort Sumter; same span to Declaration of Independence
- Pattern continues through colonial history: Glorious Revolution (1689), Armada (1588), Wars of Roses (1485)
- People recognized recurrences in real time: 1688 supporters cited centennial of Armada; Paine cited last Stuart king; Lincoln cited "fourscore and seven years"

### The Six Anglo-American Crises
1. **Wars of Roses** (1459-1487): medieval kingdom → modern nation-state
2. **Armada Crisis** (1569-1594): heretical nation → first-rank European power
3. **Glorious Revolution** (1675-1704): colonial backwater → stable provincial society
4. **American Revolution** (1773-1794): loyal colonies → republican democracy
5. **Civil War** (1860-1865): divided agrarian republic → industrializing dynamo
6. **Depression/WWII** (1929-1946): isolationist democracy → Promethean superpower

### Anglo-American Awakenings
- **Crisis** rearranges outer world (power/politics); **Awakening** rearranges inner world (spirit/culture)
- Crisis elevates group, reinvents public space; Awakening elevates individual, reinvents private space
- Five American Awakenings identified by **Robert Bellah** and **William McLoughlin**:
  1. **Puritan Awakening** (1621-1649): Winthrop led Great Migration to New England; culminated in Cromwell's Revolution
  2. **Great Awakening** (1727-1746): led by charismatic **Jonathan Edwards**; peaked during **George Whitefield's** American tour; "new light" vs. "old light" split colonial assemblies
  3. **Transcendental Awakening** (1822-1844): triggered by **Charles Finney's** preaching and **Nat Turner's** rebellion; spawned transcendentalist philosophy, utopian communes, dietary fads; ended when **Millerites'** predicted apocalypse failed
  4. **Third Great Awakening** (1886-1908): **Haymarket Riot**, muckrakers, settlement workers, "new woman" feminism; launched Bible Belt and Greenwich Village, NAACP and Wobblies
  5. **Consciousness Revolution** (1964-1984): urban riots to Summer of Love; peaked with Watergate; turned inward toward Human Potential Movement; ended as hippies reached yuppie chrysalis
- Each separated from prior by approximate length of saeculum, each halfway between neighboring Crises
- **Awakening = summer solstice** of saeculum; **Crisis = winter solstice**
- **Anthony Wallace**: "revitalization movement" — organized effort to construct more satisfying culture; hypothesized all today's religions are ossified remains of past movements
- **Robert Fogel** (Nobel economist): "to understand political trends, one must understand cycles of religiosity"

### Prediction of Next Crisis
- Saecular rhythm foretells another Crisis in **first quarter of twenty-first century**
- Climax between **2005** and **2025**
- **Thompson**: "average interwar interval period is about 80 years — year **2025** appears to represent reasonable projection"
- **Modelski** and **Ferrar**: targeted **2030** as next epicenter for "general" or "global" war
- **Joshua Goldstein** (USC): "highest danger of great power war sometime around decade of **2020s**"
- Crisis requires **ekpyrosis** — death of old order, rebirth of something new
- Saecular winter can produce destruction but also uncommon vision, heroism, sudden elevation

### The Saeculum in American Symbolism
- Inspect back of U.S. one-dollar bill: *annuit coeptis* (God smiled on the creation) borrowed from **Virgil's** praise of Augustan *saeculum aureum*; *novus ordo seclorum* (new order of the centuries)
- **When founders designed Great Seal, they put saeculum right on the money**

> "This society is no hypothesis. This society is America."

## Chapter 3: Seasons of Life

### The Universal Four-Phase Life Cycle
> "Life's racecourse is fixed. Nature has only single path and that path is run but once." — **Cicero**

- **Pythagoras**: first to interpret life as cycle of four ~20-year phases aligned with seasons
- **Romans**: *pueritia* → *iuventus* → *virilitas* → *senectus*
- Modern extension of life expectancy has **not lengthened** these phases — mostly reduced infant/child mortality
- Phase length determined by **social and biological dynamic of living**, which has slightly sped up over two centuries

**Four Phases:**
- **Childhood** (0-20): growth, receiving nurture, acquiring values
- **Young Adulthood** (21-41): vitality, testing values, launching careers
- **Midlife** (42-62): power, managing institutions, applying values
- **Elderhood** (63-83): leadership, transferring values

### Generations and Great Events
- **Karl Mannheim's** "crystallizing moment" transforms society differently according to phase-of-life response
- Children mirror dread, youths valor, midlifers competence, seniors wisdom
- WWII example: marked G.I.s as heroes, Silent as cautious, Boomers as entitled, Missionaries as visionaries

### Three Attributes for Identifying a Generation
1. **Common location in history** — Mannheim's "community of time and space"
2. **Common beliefs and behavior** — Dilthey's generational *Weltanschauung*
3. **Common perceived membership** — Marias: "to ask which generation we belong is to ask who we are"

### Identifying Generations
> "You belong to it, too. You came along at the same time. You can't get away from it." — **Thomas Wolfe** on his Lost Generation

- **Julius Peterson**: any generation includes "directive" (set tone), "directed" (follow cues), and "suppressed" (withdraw or battle against) members
- **Ortega**: each generation is "a species of biological missile hurled into space at a given instant, with a certain velocity and direction"
- **Martin Heidegger**: "the fateful act of living in and with one's generation completes the drama of human existence"
- Roughly once every twenty years, America discovers new generation — triggered by striking event where young behave differently than youth before

### The Generational Panorama
- 24 generations in Anglo-American lineage, averaging 24 years (shortened to 21 since Revolution)
- First birth year of each generation lies just before opening/closing of a Crisis or Awakening
- Pattern: Prophet → Nomad → Hero → Artist, always same order (one exception: Civil War skipped Hero)
- Corroborated by **Schlesinger Sr.**, **Morton Keller**, **Henri Peyre**, **Samuel Huntington** — every scholar who looked closely at American generational rhythms has seen similar pattern

### America as Laboratory for Generational Change
- America's existence as favored immigration destination played crucial role in emergence of generation as unit of history
- In early modern Europe, meaningful generational membership limited to elites; after Jamestown/Mayflower, New World offered this opportunity to anyone who could buy passage
- **By 1720**: 90% of free colonists had English/Scottish ancestors; as late as **1830s**, free population almost entirely Northern European Protestant
- African-Americans' and immigrants' challenges closely linked to cycle's rhythm: loudest anti-racism challenges coincided with Prophet coming-of-age; rise of new ethnicities with Nomad coming-of-age

## Chapter 4: Cycles of History

### The Four Temperaments and Archetypes
- **Hippocrates**: four humors/temperaments — sanguine (spring), choleric (summer), melancholic (autumn), phlegmatic (winter)
- **Carl Jung**: four archetypal functions from dynamic antagonism between opposites; "collective unconscious" hard-wires archetypes into mankind
- **Myers-Briggs** personality indicator descended from Jungian quaternities
- **Enantiodromia** (Heraclitus): natural running contrariwise — things convert to their opposites

### Archetypes Applied to Generations
- **Hero** enters midlife in saecular spring; **Artist** in summer; **Prophet** in autumn; **Nomad** in winter
- Two sets of opposing temperaments: Hero vs. Prophet, Nomad vs. Artist
- Each archetype's shadow revealed by the one two phases-of-life distant

> "Your generation isn't like the generation that shaped you, but it has much in common with the generation that shaped the generation that shaped you."

- **Stravinsky**: "We often feel closer to distant generations than to the generation immediately preceding us"
- **Gone with the Wind** example: Rhett Butler admired "pirate" grandfather, predicted children would be "soft, prissy creatures"

### Hero and Prophet Myths
- **Joseph Campbell's** two heroic deeds: physical (battle courage) and spiritual (supernatural journey returning with wisdom)
- Classic pairings of young hero-king + elder prophet: Joshua and Moses, King Arthur and Merlin, Frodo and Gandalf, Luke Skywalker and Obi-Wan Kenobi
- **Nomad myth** (Cinderella): hated child in hostile environment using competitive instincts to survive — *Aladdin*, *Hansel and Gretel*, *Pinocchio*; midlife Nomad = Han Solo, savvy loner doing dirty work
- **Artist myth**: dutiful youth in protective environment — Little Dutch Boy, *Bambi*, *The Little Engine That Could*; relations across generations harmonious

### Ancient Generational Sagas
- **Exodus**: Moses (Prophet), Golden Calf wanderers (Nomad), Joshua (Hero), Judges (Artist) — eighty-year saeculum beginning with Awakening, extending through Crisis
- **Homer's Iliad/Odyssey**: Nestor (Prophet), Agamemnon (Nomad), Odysseus (Hero), Telemachus (Artist) — saga ends after three-fourths of saeculum, no mention of Awakening
- **Polybius** (2nd century B.C.): linked political regime progression (kingship → aristocracy → democracy → anarchy) to generational succession — children of good rulers become weak and corrupt
- **Ibn Khaldun** (*Muqaddimah*): dynastic "prestige" endures "only four generations" — entire cycle ~100 years, abundant metaphors likening trajectory to human life cycle
- **Giuseppe Ferrari**: *Teoria del periodi politici* — revolutionary → reactionary → harmonizing → preparatory
- **Eduard Wechssler**: four "classical bases of perception" following each other in fixed sequence — physical-mechanical → rational-mathematical → cosmic-organic → ethical-personal
- **Samuel Huntington**: four-part IvI (Institutions vs. Ideals) cycle matching saeculum
- **George Modelski**: four-stage "generational mechanism" — constructive → adaptive → normative → competitive

### Cross-Cycle Shadow Relationships
- Each generation exerts dominant formative influence on people **two phases younger**
- G.I.s set tone for Boomers; Boomers for Millennials; Lost for 13ers
- **Oscillation between overprotection and underprotection** of children drives the cycle
- **Benjamin Spock**: "we need idealistic children" → raised Boomers accordingly
- **Judy Blume**: "I hate the idea that you should always protect children" → raised 13ers accordingly

## The Four Turnings: Detailed Typology

### First Turning (High)
- Renaissance of community life, commercial prosperity, institutional solidarity
- Big arguments over **means, not ends**; sense of shame reaches zenith
- Gender distinctions widest; child rearing most indulgent
- During High: obliging individuals serve purposeful society — even bad people harnessed to socially constructive tasks
- Eventually feels spirit-dead — "people can do everything but no longer feel anything"
- Historical examples: Tudor Renaissance, Merrie England, Augustan Age, Era of Good Feelings, Reconstruction/Gilded Age, American High (1946-1964)

### Second Turning (Awakening)
- Youth-fired attacks against established order; Utopian experiments seeking total fellowship with total autonomy
- Social progress no longer requires discipline; any collective effort meets withering controversy
- Gender distinctions narrow; child rearing reaches minimum protection
- People begin feeling guilt about what they earlier did to avoid shame
- Eventually enthusiasm cools, institutions delegitimized
- Historical examples: Protestant Reformation, Puritan Awakening, Great Awakening, Transcendental Awakening, Third Great Awakening, Consciousness Revolution

### Third Turning (Unraveling)
- Ethos of pragmatism, self-reliance, laissez-faire, national chauvinism
- Big arguments over **ends, not means**; sense of guilt reaches zenith
- During Unraveling: obliging society serves purposeful individuals — even good people find it hard to connect with community
- Personal satisfaction high, public trust ebbs; decisive public action very difficult
- "People can now feel, but collectively can no longer do"
- Historical examples: WWI/Prohibition era, 1850s, 1760s, current Culture Wars

### Fourth Turning (Crisis)
- Great perils boil off clutter: "The society must prevail"
- Government governs; community obstacles removed; laws that resisted change for decades swiftly shunted aside
- Personal sacrifice required; grim preoccupation with civic peril
- Gender distinctions widen; child rearing reaches smothering protection
- Wars fought with fury and for maximum result
- Eventually mood transforms into exhaustion, relief, optimism — buoyed by newborn faith in the group
- Future vision during last Crisis: "Somewhere over the Rainbow" → 1939 World's Fair Futurama

## Rhythms in History

### Peter Harris's Discovery (1969)
- **Peter Harris** (sociologist, later Temple history professor): found social indicators (birth rate, marriage age, wage growth, mobility, activism) turn abrupt corner every ~22 years
- Concluded long-term trends (urbanization, industrialization) may NOT be primary historical forces
- "Growth cycle interval" = essence of the turning — using his theory in 1689 to predict future, hypothetical crisis date would have been 1777 (actual: 1776)
- Campus rebellions show similar timing: memorable peaks in **1740s, 1830s, 1880s, 1960s**
- Utopian communes overwhelmingly clustered in Awakening years (1840, 1900, 1970)

### Social Attitudes by Turning
- In High: people want to **belong**; in Awakening: to **defy**; in Unraveling: to **separate**; in Crisis: to **gather**
- Post-Appomattox black leadership: **Booker T. Washington** (conformity) → **W.E.B. Du Bois** (defiance) → **Marcus Garvey** (separation)
- Post-V-J Day: CORE (conformity) → Black Panthers (defiance) → Nation of Islam (separation)

### Cycles Across Domains

**Politics**: **Schlesinger** liberal/conservative alternation aligns with Awakenings and Crises. **Burnham's** party realignment cycle: every ~40 years, always during Crisis or Awakening (1788, 1828, 1860, 1896, 1932, 1968-80). Steep slide in voter participation 1970-1990 resembles similar decline 1900-1920. **Ross Perot's** 1992 vote = largest third-party share since Bull Moose (1912). Authoritarian government isn't dead — it's hibernating, poised to return in Fourth Turning.

**Foreign Affairs**: **Frank Klingberg** discovered "introversion/extraversion" moods alternating ~47 years. Introversions overlap Awakenings/Crises; extraversions with Highs/Unravelings. Compare WWI (Congress waited two years, significant opposition) vs. WWII (declared war next day, one dissenting vote).

**Economy**: **Kondratieff** K-Cycles (40-55 years) — peaks near ends of Highs/Unravelings, troughs near ends of Awakenings/Crises. Greatest income inequality at ends of Unravelings (1990s, 1920s, 1850s, 1760s). Two most sustained poverty-rate declines (1946-1967, 1865-1890) coincide with last two Highs.

**Family/Gender**: **Betty Friedan** noticed feminism bursts on scene during Awakenings; gender gap narrowest in Unravelings; masculine power reidealized in Crises. Prophet generations include impassioned women (Anne Hutchinson to Susan B. Anthony to Hillary Clinton) deemed civic equals.

**Crime/Substance Abuse**: Rates rise during Awakenings, peak during Unravelings, fall sharply during Crises. Each crime peak produced mythic American images — from roaring '49er gold towns to gangland Chicago. Terms coined: *lynching* (1760s), *vigilante* (1850s), *G-Man* (1920s), *three strikes* (1990s). Per-capita alcohol consumption follows astonishingly regular cycle — peaked 1830 at four gallons/person/year, then fell by two-thirds before Civil War. **David Musto**: "a person growing up in 1890s and 1970s would have image of drug-tolerating society; in 1940s, one that firmly rejects narcotics."

**Culture**: Oscillates from inner focus (Awakening) to outer focus (Crisis). **Edmund Morgan**: "In 1740s intellectuals were clergymen thinking about theology; in 1790 statesmen thinking about politics." Musical styles track turnings: Awakenings = spirituals, ragtime, rock 'n' roll; Unravelings = jazz, rap; Crises = marches, swing; Highs = ballads, crooners.

**Population/Immigration**: Every Fourth Turning marked by fall in birthrates (Artists = baby busts); every High but one marked by rise (Prophets = baby booms). Immigration climbs in Awakenings, peaks in Unravelings, falls during Crises — reversed by nativist backlash (1850s, 1920s, 1990s).

### Technology Follows Mood, Not Vice Versa
- Autos/phones/radios seen as individualizing pre-WWI → symbols of civic purpose by WWII → conformist tools 1950s → fragmenting again 1990s
- Same technology serves opposite social purposes depending on the turning
- **Bill Gates** predicts unlimited personal options — but same circuits could empower central monitoring

### Accidents and the Saeculum
- Saeculum neither predicts nor precludes accidents — what matters is **society's response**
- Move Watergate to 1934: unthinkable. Move Depression to 1974: Boomers wouldn't don uniforms for CCC
- Hitler provoked total war during Fourth Turning — would he provoke same during an Unraveling? Maybe not
- During Fourth Turning, generational forces funnel exogenous events toward concerted national response: Prophet order-givers in charge, Hero order-takers on battlefield = maximum cooperation
- **Sam Adams, John Brown, and FDR** all plausibly accused of staging emergencies to galvanize younger people
- During Awakening (Vietnam): archetypal order-takers were old, order-givers young = maximum convulsion

### War Characteristics by Turning
- **High-era wars**: reconfirm prior settlements; standoffs with high patience, low enthusiasm (War of 1812, Korea)
- **Awakening-era wars**: enmeshed with youthful passions; controversial and badly remembered (Vietnam)
- **Unraveling-era wars**: swiftly victorious, momentarily popular, ultimately uncathartic (Kuwait)
- **Crisis-era wars**: large, deadly, decisive; outcome redefines nation/empire (Revolution, Civil War, WWII)

### Global Saecular Synchronization
- After WWII, saeculum timing became better synchronized globally than ever before
- Current global generations show striking parallels: G.I. peers (**Thatcher, Mitterrand, Deng Xiaoping**) associated with civic trust; Boomer peers pursuing glasnost and diplomacy; 13er peers (**Blair, Netanyahu, Walesa**) values-obsessed and drawn to angry partisans

## The Civil War Anomaly

- **Only conspicuous anomaly** in Anglo-American history — abbreviated Third and Fourth Turnings (22 years combined)
- Produced **no Hero generation** — only time in five centuries the cycle disrupted
- Three-archetype constellation (elder Compromisers, midlife Transcendentals, young-adult Gilded) = worst instincts prevailed
- **Failed efforts** of Clay, Webster, Calhoun to avert war
- Consequences: Union preserved, slaves emancipated — **at enormous cost**; Reconstruction collapsed into Jim Crow; all social agendas withered until next Awakening
- **Key lesson**: generational constellations can become dangerous; history is not predetermined
- Yet turnings of usual type followed — society restored equilibrating sequence

> "If learning from their example enables us to avert a catastrophe in the next Fourth Turning, our debt to the generations of Clay, Lincoln, and Grant will be very great indeed."

## Seven Saecula and Twenty-Four Generations

### The Anglo-American Saeculum (overview)
1. **Late Medieval** (1435-1487) — Wars of Roses
2. **Reformation** (1487-1594) — Armada Crisis
3. **New World** (1594-1704) — Glorious Revolution
4. **Revolutionary** (1704-1794) — American Revolution
5. **Civil War** (1794-1865) — Civil War
6. **Great Power** (1865-1946) — Depression/WWII
7. **Millennial** (1946-2026?) — Crisis pending

### Archetype Portraits in American History

**Prophets**: Best remembered for coming-of-age passion and principled elder stewardship. Increasingly indulged as children, increasingly protective as parents. Principal endowments: **vision, values, religion**. Leaders: **Winthrop, Samuel Adams, Franklin, Lincoln, FDR**. Early in life: none saw combat; late in life: revered more for inspiring words than deeds.

**Nomads**: Best remembered for hell-raising young adulthood and hands-on midlife leadership. Underprotected as children, overprotective parents. Principal endowments: **liberty, survival, honor**. Leaders: **Bacon, Washington, Adams, Grant, Truman, Eisenhower**. Cunning realists, tacticians and warriors. Only two presidents who had earlier hanged a man (Washington and Cleveland).

**Heroes**: Best remembered for collective coming-of-age triumphs and hubristic elder achievements. Increasingly protected as children, indulgent parents. Principal endowments: **community, affluence, technology**. Leaders: **"King" Carter, Jefferson, Madison, Kennedy, Reagan**. Vigorous institution builders, advocates of prosperity and public optimism.

**Artists**: Best remembered for quiet rising adulthood and flexible consensus-building midlife leadership. Overprotected as children, underprotective parents. Principal endowments: **pluralism, expertise, due process**. Leaders: **Shirley, J.Q. Adams, T. Roosevelt, Wilson, Powell**. Sensitive social technicians; with single exception of Jackson, most expert and credentialed American political leaders.

### Modern Era Generational Profiles

**Missionary Generation** (Prophet, 1860-1882): Indulged home-and-hearth children; labor anarchists and campus rioters coming of age; decency brigades imposing Prohibition at midlife; became "Wise Old Men" enacting New Deal, leading WWII. Examples: **FDR, Du Bois, Bryan, Jane Addams, MacArthur**. Foreign: **Churchill, Lenin**.

**Lost Generation** (Nomad, 1883-1900): Urban blight childhood, unregulated drugs, child sweatshops; flaming youth, doughboys, flappers; Roaring Twenties barnstormers and gangsters; Depression hit at midlife peak; provided war-winning generals. Examples: **Truman, Patton, Fitzgerald, Mae West, Louis Armstrong**. Foreign: **Hitler**.

**G.I. Generation** (Hero, 1901-1924): Good-kid reputation, Boy Scouts, child-labor restrictions; uniformed corps conquered depression and enemies; G.I. Bill suburbs, moon rockets; unprecedented presidential tenure. Examples: **Kennedy, Reagan, Disney, John Wayne, Cronkite**. Foreign: **Willy Brandt, Brezhnev**.

**Silent Generation** (Artist, 1925-1942): Suffocated children of war and depression; too late to be war heroes; early-marrying Lonely Crowd; sensitive rock 'n' rollers and civil rights advocates; midlife "passages" amid fragmenting families. Examples: **Powell, Mondale, Woody Allen, Martin Luther King Jr., Elvis Presley**. Foreign: **Anne Frank, Gorbachev**.

**Boom Generation** (Prophet, 1943-1960): Dr. Spock permissiveness, suburban conformity; Summer of Love to Days of Rage; self-absorbed yuppies; midlife Culture Wars. Examples: **Clinton, Gingrich, Spielberg, Bill Gates**. Foreign: **Tony Blair, Netanyahu**.

**13th Generation / Gen X** (Nomad, 1961-1981): Hurried divorce/latchkey childhood; denounced as wild/stupid; AIDS-era courtship; grunge to hip-hop; pragmatic nonaffiliation. Examples: **Tom Cruise, Jodie Foster, Quentin Tarantino**.

**Millennial Generation** (Hero?, 1982-?): Baby on Board signs; child abuse/safety hot topics; virtues and values best-sellers; school uniforms; Hollywood replacing child devils with angels.

## Chapter 5: Gray Champions

### Hawthorne's Legend
- **1689**: Aged man with "eye, face, attitude of command" stopped British governor Andros from crushing colonial self-rule
- **Hawthorne**: "Long, long may it be ere he comes again! His hour is one of darkness, and adversity, and peril"
- Pattern repeated: Sam Adams/Franklin at Bunker Hill (1775), Lincoln at Gettysburg (1863), FDR's Fireside Chats (1930s-40s)
- **Gray Champion = elder Prophet archetype** — arrival heralds new constellation

> "At each of these great gates of history, a similar generational drama unfolded. Four archetypes, aligned in the same order — elder Prophet, midlife Nomad, young adult Hero, child Artist — together produced the most enduring legends in our history."

### The Inevitability of Winter
- Less than 10% of Americans were soldier/riveter age on D-Day
- Less than 2% have adult memories of 1929's Black Tuesday
- Deep into Third Turning, goals of perpetual improvement feel like slipping away

> "We may not wish the Gray Champion to come again — but come he must, and come he will."

## The American High (1946-1964): First Turning

### Overview
- America strong where we today feel weak (public cooperation), weak where we today feel strong (personal fulfillment)
- Social movements stalled; middle class grew; churches buttressed government
- "End to ideology" — authorities presided over bland, modernist, spirit-dead culture

### Lost Entering Elderhood: Old Fogeys
- **Truman** showed guts firing MacArthur — even while Congress likened general to "words of God himself"
- Lifelong targets of criticism: *Atlantic Monthly* pre-WWI accused "rising generation" of "mental rickets and curvature of the soul"; FDR blasted "generation of self-seekers" for wrecking "temple of our civilization"; in old age castigated as reactionary
- **Henry Miller**: "The truth is, under the skin we are all cannibals, assassins, traitors"
- Rowdy childhood and careful old age as bookends, divided roughly in thirds by two world wars
- Grew up when children let loose to do anything — like seven-year-old **George Burns** singing for pennies in saloons
- **Fitzgerald**: "a new generation dedicated to the fear of poverty and the worship of success; grown up to find all gods dead, all wars fought, all faith in man shaken"
- WWII: shed isolationism, provided war-winning generals (**Patton**, **Bradley**, **Eisenhower**)
- First president dropped two atom bombs then arranged peace less vengeful than one he recalled from soldier days
- Scored far below G.I. seniors on self-esteem; distrustful of government; seldom lobbied on own behalf
- **Paul Tillich**: "Our generation has seen the horrors latent in man's being rise to the surface"
- Burdened themselves with unprecedented peacetime tax rates for infrastructure they wouldn't live to enjoy
- Nearly half of old Lost had never lived in dwelling with at least two bedrooms and a bathroom
- **Ethel Andrus** (AARP founder) refused to "bewail hardships of old age or stress the political strength of older folk"
- **1961 White House Conference on Aging**: "the individual will assume primary responsibility for self-reliance in old age"
- **Eisenhower**: cautious, opposed moon rocket, warned of "military-industrial complex," refused to fly in jet airplane until 1959
- Same generation that produced **Hammett, Chandler, Bogart** now led by **J. Edgar Hoover and the Dulles brothers** — files thick with plots and secrets

### G.I.s Entering Midlife: The Power Elite
- **William Manchester**: "We were a special generation, and we *were* America. You get used to that"
- Childhood: fussed over by protective parents — Boy Scouts, Miss Americas, vitamins, child-labor laws
- **Father Flanagan**: "There's no such thing as a bad boy" — distinguishing them from "bad" predecessors
- Young adulthood: CCC, unions, landslide FDR votes → government as trusted friend
- **Woody Guthrie**: "Your power is turning our darkness to dawn, so roll on Columbia, roll on"
- Midlife: peer-enforced, get-it-done attitude — G.I. Bill, suburbs, interstate highways, moon rockets
- 99 Nobel Prizes (mostly hard sciences); after **Gallup** defined "average man," **McNamara** designed average auto, **Wilson** opened Holiday Inns
- G.I. Bill = America's largest-ever intergenerational transfer — two of every five dollars of housing debt covered by taxpayers
- **C. Wright Mills**: "We do not engage in loose talk about 'ideals'" — the **Power Elite** wanted to "get right down to the problem"
- **Daniel Bell**: declared **End to Ideology**
- Kennedy's inaugural: "the torch has been passed to a new generation" — "bear any burden, pay any price"
- **James MacGregor Burns**: "He's Superman!" about Kennedy — **Neustadt**: "Presidential Power" could solve anything
- **Richard Rovere** coined "The Establishment" — at the time, purely positive
- **Walter Cronkite**: "That's the way it is"
- Since age-based polling began 1932, G.I.s have *never* collectively voted for losing candidate

### Silent Entering Young Adulthood: Gray Flannel Suits
- **Russell Baker**: "I hated the war ending... I wanted desperately to become a death-dealing hero"
- Denied wartime glory; became "content to tinker with techniques and technicalities"
- 1949 *Fortune*: chastised for "gray flannel mentality" — almost none wanted to start own businesses
- **Manchester**: "Never had American youth been so withdrawn, cautious, unimaginative — and silent"
- *Silent* was G.I. put-down, but silence had been golden during Crisis-era childhood
- **Norman Rockwell** depicted boys and girls as clean/obedient; major school discipline problems = gum chewing and line cutting
- Adolescent pathologies (suicide, crime, substance abuse) reached **lowest levels ever recorded**
- Median marriage age hit youngest ever (1956); steepest rise in real per-capita income of any generation
- College-educated women more fertile than non-college women (only time in U.S. history)
- **David Riesman**: dubbed them the **"Found Generation"** — as absorbed as Lost had been alienated
- Greatest two-generation advance in education: 13+ years vs. Lost's <9 years
- Many yearned to be **Rebel without a Cause** like **James Dean** — **Pauline Kael**: "the young boy as beautiful, disturbed animal, so full of love he's defenseless"
- **Elvis**: "All Shook Up, wondering what's wrong with me?"
- Beat culture: **Ginsberg's** *Howl!*, goateed bohemians — G.I.s found them amusing, not threatening
- **Buckley** launched *National Review* without expecting to change direction
- **Hugh Hefner** defined consummate playboy; **Andy Warhol** found art in G.I. soup can
- Decided to lend "humanity, sensitivity, and beauty" to rigid social order
- Biggest one-generation leap in credentialed professions — surged into medicine, ministry, law, teaching
- **Martin Luther King Jr.** led civil rights movement using nonviolence to appeal to G.I. conscience
- **Peter, Paul, and Mary**: "If I Had a Hammer" — G.I.s had all the hammers, building ICBMs and interstates

### Boomers Entering Childhood: Dennis the Menace
- **Benjamin Spock**: "We need idealistic children" — *Common Sense Book of Baby Care* sold >1 million copies annually
- G.I. parents feared recent crisis required new temperament: "We wanted our children to be inner-directed" (**Eda LeShan**)
- "Democratic discipline" — dealt with children "thoughtfully, reasonably, and kindly"
- "He'll-clean-up-his-room-when-he's-ready-to-have-a-clean-room" school of nurture
- Most secure family life in U.S. history — few preschoolers had working mothers; only 2% in institutional day care
- **Susan Littwin**: "being a parent was a career, and like any career, the harder you worked, the more you gained"
- Boomers least breast-fed generation (bottles preferred); most shots/operations (circumcisions, tonsillectomies)
- G.I. scientists conquered childhood diseases: diphtheria, polio; fluoridated water for teeth
- Schools at all-time institutional confidence; brightest G.I. women taught (most professions closed to them)
- **Television** as scientific nurturing device — competent parents, smart scientists, happy endings
- Children depicted as rambunctious **Dennis the Menaces**, mystery-solving masters of loyal **Lassie** animals
- **Art Linkletter**: "Kids say the darnedest things" — anything interesting to children keenly absorbed older people
- **Landon Jones**: **Great Expectations** — 1950s fads "almost without exception were creations of children" — Mickey Mouse ears, hula hoops, Silly Putty, Barbie
- **Time** magazine: "on fringe of golden era," soon to "write finis to poverty and war"
- **Kenneth Keniston**: Boomers later admitted "an unusually strong tie" to mothers; Vietnam poll: 32% mentioned mothers alone as "person who cares about me," only 8% said fathers
- **Daniel Yankelovich**: developed **"psychology of entitlement"** — "what other generations thought privileges, Boomers thought were rights"
- To middle-class youths, poverty/disease/crime invisible or temporary nuisances succumbing to affluence
- No other time in 20th century did mainstream culture impart such benign worldview to children
