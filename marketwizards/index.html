Preface & Schwager’s Story

The efficient market hypothesis holds that no one can consistently beat the market because all available information is already reflected in prices. It sounds reasonable in a classroom, but the real world demolishes it. On October 19, 1987, the S&P futures fell 29 percent in a single day. Under the assumptions of efficient markets, the probability of such a move is roughly 10 to the negative 160th power—the equivalent of randomly selecting the same atom twice from the entire observable universe. If the theory cannot explain the most dramatic market event of the twentieth century, something fundamental is wrong with its premises.

Schwager draws a useful analogy to chess. Every player knows the same rules and sees the same board, yet only a tiny fraction ever reach grandmaster level. The difference is skill, pattern recognition, and psychological composure under pressure. Markets are far more complex than chess, with more variables and more emotional interference. If identical information produced identical outcomes, there would be no Ed Seykota turning $5,000 into a 250,000 percent return over sixteen years, no Michael Marcus converting $30,000 into $80 million.

“There is certainly no need to resort to the assumption of market perfection to explain why winning in the markets is difficult.” — Jack Schwager

Schwager came to this project through painful personal experience. After graduate school he became a commodity research analyst, and his brother opened a $2,000 account for him to trade. His first serious analysis involved the cotton market—he reconstructed every season since World War II, identified only two that operated as true free markets since 1953, and forecast a peak at 32 to 33 cents. Cotton initially rose exactly as predicted, climbing from 28 to 31 cents in a single week. He went short near his projected top. The market kept going—32, 33, 34, 35 cents. His account was wiped out. Cotton eventually soared to 99 cents, more than double the highest price in the century’s history.

Two breakthroughs followed. The first came when he met Steve Chronowitz, a strictly technical analyst whose market calls kept proving correct. Schwager, a die-hard fundamentalist, grudgingly accepted that analysis alone was not enough—timing mattered, and charts provided it. The second breakthrough was simpler but more important: the realization that no single trade should ever be allowed to destroy an account. He began using good-till-cancelled stops just below recent lows. Combining technical timing, fundamental analysis, and strict risk control, he built a small stake to over $100,000.

Then he blew it again. An impulsive soybean trade that he was certain represented a bull market reaction led him to increase his position substantially, then add more right before a government crop report. The report came in bearish. He surrendered more than a quarter of his cumulative profits within days. He had rebuilt $8,000 to $100,000 twice but could never expand beyond that level. The frustration drove him to seek answers from traders who had solved the puzzle, and Market Wizards was born.

Part I: Futures & Currencies

Michael Marcus: Blighting Never Strikes Twice

Michael Marcus graduated from Johns Hopkins in 1969, Phi Beta Kappa, near the top of his class, with a Ph.D. fellowship in psychology at Clark University waiting for him. Instead he met a man named John through a mutual friend who claimed he could double money every two weeks. Marcus hired him at $30 a week plus free potato chips and soda. His first visit to Reynolds Securities in Baltimore felt reverent—mahogany panels, hushed tones, and an old-fashioned commodity board clicking prices so far away he needed binoculars to read them, like watching a horse race from the cheap seats.

The partnership with John was a catastrophe. Their first realization that something was wrong came when a loudspeaker recommended buying soybean meal and John looked uncertain, asking Marcus if they should do it. Their first eight trades were all losers. They measured success by how many days it took to lose money. John proposed a “can’t lose” pork belly spread that was fundamentally flawed—August pork bellies were not deliverable against the February contract. When the spread collapsed, John stood at the quote board shouting and pounding his fist. Marcus was wiped out. John announced he was going to Bermuda to wash dishes and raise a new trading stake. Marcus never heard from him again.

“You are making the greatest mistake of your life!” — John, when Marcus fired him

Marcus’s father had died when he was fifteen, leaving $3,000 in life insurance. He cashed it in against his mother’s objections and studied Chester Keltner’s books on wheat and soybeans. In the summer of 1970, he bought three contracts of December corn on Keltner’s recommendation. A corn blight devastated the crop, and Marcus accumulated $30,000—a princely sum for a middle-class kid. He was instinctively doing the right thing, taking profits on the way up and getting out when prices started falling.

The next spring, he theorized the blight had wintered over. He borrowed $20,000 from his mother and bet $50,000 total on corn and wheat—the maximum number of contracts his margin would carry. The Wall Street Journal headline told the story: “More Blight on the Floor of the Chicago Board of Trade Than in Midwest Cornfields.” The market went limit-down. Marcus was paralyzed, watching all day, hoping for a reversal that never came. He lost $30,000 of his own money and $12,000 of his mother’s.

The turning point came when he met Ed Seykota in October 1971. Seykota, fresh out of MIT, had developed one of the first computer programs for testing and trading technical systems. He taught Marcus the twin disciplines of cutting losses and riding winners. Marcus watched Seykota hold a short silver position while the market eked down half a penny a day and everyone around them was bullish. Seykota’s only explanation: the trend is down, and he would stay short until it changed. A second mentor, Amos Hostetter, reinforced the same principles. Getting the same lessons from two different sources made them impossible to ignore.

By August 1974, Marcus had joined Commodities Corporation—the first non-Ph.D. trader they ever hired. He started with $30,000 and over the next ten years turned it into $80 million, making at least 100 percent annually. His 1979 gold trade was legendary: he caught the move toward $800, buying in Australia and Hong Kong while his New York colleagues slept, riding $100-per-ounce chunks at a time. When he heard about the Afghanistan invasion on television, he called Hong Kong and bought 200,000 ounces before anybody else knew what was happening. Ten minutes later, when the news broke everywhere, he had an instant $10-per-ounce profit on 200,000 ounces.

Currency positions as large as 600 million Deutsche marks destroyed his marriage—he was waking every two hours to check markets in Australia, Hong Kong, Zurich, and London. But his skill at reading price action saved him repeatedly. In late 1978, the dollar was getting battered to new lows daily. Then it got mysteriously strong in a way that defied the bearish consensus. Marcus bailed out of his long currency positions. That weekend, the Carter administration announced a dollar support program. Currency futures locked limit-down for days. Marcus had gotten out right near the top, understanding that big players—including governments—always tip their hand through price action before announcements become public.

“If you become unsure about a position, and you don’t know what to do, just get out. You can always come back in.” — Michael Marcus

His core rules crystallized over two decades: never bet more than 5 percent on any one idea, always use stops, and maintain the mental flexibility to abandon a position the instant it feels wrong. The best trades have three things going for them simultaneously: fundamentals, technicals, and market tone. When all three align, press hard. When they diverge, step aside. By the late 1980s, markets had changed profoundly—the moment a chart broke a key level, every professional trader in the world saw it simultaneously, and there was nobody left to buy from. The era of easy trend-following was ending.

Bruce Kovner

Bruce Kovner may have been the largest trader in the interbank currency and futures markets when Schwager interviewed him. In 1987 alone, his profits exceeded $300 million for himself and his fund investors. Over ten years, he compounded at 87 percent annually—$2,000 invested in early 1978 would have grown to over $1 million. Despite this staggering record, he maintained a surprisingly low profile and had refused every previous interview request.

Before trading, Kovner taught political science at Harvard and the University of Pennsylvania, but he did not enjoy confronting a blank page each morning. He drifted into politics, then abandoned it for lack of financial resources. In the mid-1970s he fell in love with the yield curve, studying interest rate theory intensively. He found price distortions in early interest rate futures that the big banks had not yet bothered to exploit. His first trade, a textbook spread based on interest rate theory, worked perfectly and he was hooked. His second, a copper spread, was eventually right but too early. After two trades he was still ahead, $3,000 grown to about $4,000.

Kovner viewed technical analysis the way a doctor views a thermometer—ignoring it would be foolish. Unexplained breakouts from tight consolidations often produced the best risk-to-reward trades, precisely because nobody could identify a reason for the move. He applied Heisenberg’s uncertainty principle to markets: the more a pattern is watched by speculators, the more prone it becomes to false signals. The less observed the market, the better the trade—he found particular edge in currency cross rates that most participants ignored.

“I never think about that, because the point about a technical barrier is that the market shouldn’t go there if you are right.” — Bruce Kovner

Risk management was not a suggestion but a religion. Every trade required a predetermined stop, calculated before entry. He never risked more than 1 percent of his portfolio on any single trade, and his daily computer analysis tracked correlations across all positions—eight highly correlated positions equaled one position eight times as large. He preferred wider stops on fewer contracts over tight stops on many, placing his exits where they reasonably indicated the trade was wrong, not at some arbitrary dollar amount.

His mental process was scenario-based. He would imagine many alternative pictures of the world, waiting patiently for one to be confirmed by market action. Most scenarios were wrong—only a few elements would prove correct. But when nine out of ten elements clicked in a single picture, that scenario became his operating reality. The weekend after the October 1987 crash demonstrated this perfectly: he struggled through multiple visions of how the dollar would react, finally realizing that James Baker had no choice but to let it fall. He spent a tense weekend waiting for the Far East markets to open Sunday night, absolutely convinced of a conclusion that almost nobody else had reached.

“Undertrade, undertrade, undertrade. Whatever you think your position should be, cut it at least in half.” — Bruce Kovner

He operated around the clock, with monitors at his home and country house, call levels set for every currency. His assistant was allowed to wake him twice a year. He managed over $650 million by the late 1980s, which forced him to abandon smaller markets like coffee—a few million in profits simply did not justify the mental bandwidth when you were already trading hundreds of millions in currencies. His ultimate advice was devastatingly simple: whatever you think your position should be, cut it in half. Novice traders routinely take five to ten times too much risk, betting 5 to 10 percent on a trade that should risk 1 to 2 percent.

Richard Dennis: The Prince of the Pit

Richard Dennis got his first job on the exchange floor right after high school—a summer runner making $40 a week while losing $40 an hour trading. At seventeen, he was too young to stand in the ring, so his father held the exchange membership while Dennis signaled trades from the sidelines. When Dennis turned twenty-one, his father handed over the membership with visible relief, declaring he had no idea what he was doing.

Dennis started on the Mid America Exchange trading minicontracts, growing an account from $400 to $2,000 when the 1970 corn blight news hit on a Monday and markets opened limit-up. The lesson was not about corn blight but about going with the trend when it was strong. Shortly after, he enrolled at Tulane in New Orleans but lasted only a week, using laundromat quarters to phone trades into Chicago. When he ran out of quarters, he had nothing but dirty clothes and no choice but to come back.

“When you have a destabilizing loss, get out, go home, take a nap. When you are getting beat to death, get your head out of the mixer.” — Richard Dennis

One early disaster proved unexpectedly valuable. He made a bad trade, lost $300 (10 percent of his $3,000 capital), reversed the position, lost again, reversed back, and lost a third time. By the end of the day he had lost $1,000—a full third of everything he had. In retrospect he called it one of his best trades because it taught him never to try catching up or doubling down after a loss. A loss affects judgment, and the only remedy is putting time between the loss and the next trade.

The Turtle experiment became the most famous wager in trading history. Dennis had a philosophical argument with his partner and childhood friend: can trading be taught, or does it require some ineffable, mystical talent? Dennis believed the skills could be reduced to a set of rules. To settle the argument, he recruited from over 1,000 applicants, interviewed 40, and selected 23 across two groups. The name came from a visit to a turtle farm in Singapore where thousands of tiny turtles squirmed in huge vats. Training lasted only two weeks the first year and one week the second. The results silenced the skeptics: the twenty remaining Turtles averaged about 100 percent profit per year, each starting with $100,000 and averaging $2 million in their accounts.

“Almost anybody can make up a list of rules that are 80 percent as good as what we taught. What they couldn’t do is give them the confidence to stick to those rules even when things are going bad.” — Richard Dennis

Dennis believed 95 percent of profits come from 5 percent of trades, which meant the worst mistake a trader could make was missing a major opportunity. His public fund lost just under 49 percent before he stopped trading in April 1988, partly from the October 1987 crash when Eurodollar futures opened 240 points against him. He attributed the difficulty to a fundamental shift: the tremendous increase in computerized trend-following created more false breakouts, which in a perverse way represented the ultimate triumph of technical trading—a victory that devalued it. His solution: be far more short-term or as long-term as you can stand, and avoid the middle like the plague. He retired from trading to focus on politics, though Schwager speculated it would not be permanent.

Paul Tudor Jones: The Art of Aggressive Trading

Paul Tudor Jones returned 62 percent in October 1987—the month the market crashed. His Tudor Futures Fund delivered five consecutive years of triple-digit returns with extraordinarily low equity retracements, and $1,000 invested at inception in September 1984 grew to $17,482 by October 1988. He kept 85 percent of his net worth in his own funds, calling them the safest place in the world for his money.

His education came from Eli Tullis, a legendary cotton trader in New Orleans who routinely held positions of 3,000 contracts when the entire market’s open interest was only 30,000. Tullis carried on polite conversation while his positions were getting decimated, displaying a steel-hard emotional control that Jones internalized. The critical lesson: get out of a position when the market lets you, not when you want to. Do not wait for new highs or new lows to move large positions.

Jones’s worst disaster came in 1979 when, as a broker with speculative accounts, he went long 400 contracts of July cotton in the 82 to 86 cent range. He bought every dip to the low end of the congestion, then added 100 more on a macho bid at 82.90 right as Refco was preparing to deliver certificated stock against the contract. He recognized too late that the width of the congestion range predicted the magnitude of the coming decline, and that it was his money that would carry it there. He lost 60 to 70 percent of his clients’ equity in a single trade. He almost quit, but instead decided to become disciplined and businesslike. From that point forward, he thought constantly about losing money rather than making it.

“Don’t be a hero. Don’t have an ego. Always question yourself and your ability. Don’t ever feel that you are very good. The second you do, you are dead.” — Paul Tudor Jones

His risk management was obsessive: daily equity monitoring with the goal of finishing each day with more than he started, a monthly maximum loss of 10 percent, and ruthless position reduction during losing streaks. He traded smallest when performing worst. He never averaged losers and refused to risk significant money ahead of major reports, viewing that as gambling rather than trading.

The October 1987 crash was, for Jones, one of the most exciting periods of his life. He had expected a major collapse since mid-1986 and drew contingency plans for a financial meltdown. Peter Borish, his research director, had built an analog model superimposing the 1980s market over the 1920s—the correlation was remarkable and perfectly nailed the timing of the collapse. On the previous Friday, record volume on the downside replicated what had happened in 1929 two days before that crash. During the last half hour on crash day, Jones noticed bonds suddenly turning up after acting terrible all day—the Federal Reserve was adding massive liquidity. The instant bonds acted right, he went wild buying stocks, covering shorts and going long on the close of the worst day in market history.

Jones made his best money at market turns, not in the trending middle, which he estimated only comprised about 15 percent of market action. He would run a series of low-risk probes until finally catching the turn. His flexibility was extreme: in one meeting with Schwager he was heavily short the stock market; two weeks later he had reversed 180 degrees to bullish after the market failed to follow through on the downside. He also invoked Jesse Livermore’s haunting warning that in the long run, you can never win trading markets—a devastating admission that kept his defenses permanently high.

Gary Bielfeldt

Gary Bielfeldt started twenty-five years earlier with $1,000, trading a single corn contract from Peoria, Illinois. He refused to move to the Chicago trading floor, preferring to plow his trading wealth back into his community. His approach rejected conventional diversification—he believed in picking one area and becoming an expert.

The pivotal early trade came in 1965, when he put his entire $10,000 account into twenty soybean contracts at extremely high leverage. A 10-cent decline would have completely wiped him out. Prices initially fell, came perilously close to a margin call, then reversed. He more than doubled his equity, launching a full-time trading career. By the mid-1980s, a massive long bond position from May 1984 through April 1986—entered when five-year notes were yielding nearly 14 percent and no qualified borrower would take a three-year loan at 13 percent—catapulted him into the same league as major institutional players.

“My father taught me the concept of playing the percentage hands. You don’t just play every hand and stay through every card, forfeiting the ante. You should play the good hands, and drop out of the poor hands.” — Gary Bielfeldt

Bielfeldt drew his trading philosophy from poker: when the percentages are strongly in your favor, be aggressive and leverage the trade. When they are against you, fold and forfeit the ante. The five traits he considered essential were discipline, patience, courage, willingness to lose, and a strong desire to win. He measured his success not by trading performance but by what he did with the accumulated wealth, establishing a foundation in 1985 to share his success with his community.

Ed Seykota: Everybody Gets What They Want

Ed Seykota was completely unknown to the public when Schwager tracked him down, yet his track record was arguably the most extraordinary in the entire book. One account starting with $5,000 in 1972 was up over 250,000 percent by mid-1988. Adjusted for withdrawals, the theoretical return was several million percent. No other trader Schwager encountered had matched this over the same length of time.

Seykota worked from a house bordering Lake Tahoe, with no quote screens on his desk. His trading was confined to a few minutes each evening running a computer program that generated the next day’s signals. Schwager had planned a two-hour visit and ended up staying the entire day. The drive to the house—early morning, highway winding into the mountains, Mozart’s Clarinet Concerto on the radio—made Schwager admit jealousy as he compared it to the ugly building visible from his Wall Street office.

His first brush with markets came in the late 1960s when he decided silver had to rise after the U.S. Treasury stopped selling. A broker convinced him to short copper first—he was stopped out and lost money. Then he bought silver, and the price started falling. It was a stunning education in how markets discount news long before it becomes obvious. He became fascinated with how markets work, saw Richard Donchian’s newsletter implying a purely mechanical trend-following system could beat markets, and wrote computer programs on punch cards to test the theory. Amazingly, the theories tested true.

At his second brokerage, management interfered disastrously with his computerized system. The system generated a buy signal for sugar at 5 cents. Management thought it was overbought and changed the rule: buy on a 20-point pullback, then 30, then 50, then 100. They finally bought at 9 cents, near the peak. The most profitable trade of the year ended up losing money. Seykota quit and switched to a pure profit-incentive fee arrangement, escaping the commission-driven structure that had poisoned his results.

“Win or lose, everybody gets what they want out of the market.” — Ed Seykota

His ranking of priorities was stark: long-term trend first, current chart pattern second, picking a good spot to buy or sell third, and fundamentals a distant fourth. Fundamentals that you read about were already discounted—he called them “funny-mentals.” The rare cases where catching a fundamental insight early, before others believed it, he called “surprise-a-mentals.” His rules for trading were deceptively simple: cut losses, cut losses, cut losses. Then ride winners, keep bets small, follow rules without question, and know when to break the rules. He acknowledged the last two were contradictory but believed both.

Perhaps most fascinating was his psychological depth. He told the story of a trader who worked $10,000 to $250,000 in a couple of months, then changed personality and lost it all—a process that repeated like clockwork. Seykota believed the trader was getting exactly what he unconsciously wanted: excitement, martyrdom, sympathy, attention. He used hypnosis, breathing exercises, visualization, and gestalt therapy to work with traders, and found they either became much more successful or realized they had never really wanted to be traders at all. His advice for the average person was blunt: find a superior trader to manage your money, then pursue what you really love.

Larry Hite: Respecting Risk

Larry Hite arrived at trading through one of the most circuitous paths imaginable. He had an inauspicious academic record, worked a string of odd jobs, and pursued a dual career as an actor and screenwriter. One movie script was optioned so many times he began viewing it as a steady income source. Then one evening he heard H.L. Hunt on the radio describing cheap oil options that occasionally produced windfall profits with minimal risk. The same night, he met Brian Epstein, the Beatles’ manager. Two ideas fused in his mind: big money with minimal investment. He tried rock promotion, landed a few record contracts but no stars, and three shootings at clubs in a single weekend convinced him to change careers.

His first interview at an old-line Wall Street firm produced an epiphany. The interviewer proudly explained they only bought “blue chip” stocks for their clients. Hite looked up the derivation: blue was the color of the most expensive chip in Monte Carlo. The entire investment profession, he realized, was a gambling operation dressed in mahogany and wool. He threw away Graham and Dodd and bought Beat the Dealer by Edward Thorp. Successful investing was simply a matter of odds.

He founded Mint Investment Management with Peter Matthews (a statistics Ph.D.) and Michael Delman (a defense electronics systems designer). From April 1981 through mid-1988, Mint averaged over 30 percent compounded annual returns with remarkable consistency: worst year plus 13 percent, best year plus 60, and the largest loss in any twelve-month period was under 1 percent. They started with $2 million and grew to over $800 million under management.

“I don’t trade for excitement; I trade to win.” — Larry Hite

Mint operated on four rigid principles: always follow trends and never deviate from the system; risk a maximum of 1 percent of total equity per trade; diversify more broadly than any competitor, selecting systems for lack of correlation with each other; and track volatility to stop trading any market where return-to-risk deteriorated. The system ran on a three-light framework—green meant take all signals, yellow meant liquidate but take no new positions, red meant liquidate everything. When coffee went from $1.30 to $2.80 and back to $1.00 in 1986, Mint got out at $1.70 and stayed out. They may have sacrificed additional profit, but that discipline was precisely what produced their record.

Hite told the story of the world’s largest coffee trader, who operated from a palatial London home with a library containing every book on power. Five times the man asked how Hite could possibly know more about coffee than he did. Five times Hite told him he knew nothing about coffee—he did not even drink it—he just looked at the risk. Three months later, the coffee king had blown $100 million. He knew everything about coffee and nothing about risk. The speculator’s only true advantage, Hite believed, was the luxury of choosing not to play. The trade has product knowledge. The floor has speed. The speculator has neither—but he can wait until the odds are in his favor.

Part II: Mostly Stocks

Michael Steinhardt: The Concept of Variant Perception

Michael Steinhardt compounded at 30 percent annually over twenty-one years, with only two losing years (both under 2 percent). A $1,000 investment in 1967 grew to over $93,000 after fees by spring 1988, compared to $6,400 for an S&P 500 basket over the same period. His interest in markets began at thirteen when his father gave him 200 shares of stock. While his friends played stickball, Steinhardt watched the ticker tape. He graduated from Wharton at nineteen and founded Steinhardt, Fine and Berkowitz in 1967.

His core philosophy was variant perception—developing views he believed were at odds with the general market consensus, then playing those perceptions until they were no longer variant. He was willing to short stocks backed by institutional enthusiasm, tending to get in early and starting with losses. He made a critical distinction between traders, who go short as readily as long and focus on market direction, and investors, who stay 70 percent or more invested and focus on stock selection. He was unapologetically a trader.

The bond trade of 1981-82 was his masterwork. He went long debt markets in the spring of 1981, with treasuries not bottoming until September 30. Rates moved against him for the first six months—it was the worst experience of his business life, with thoughtful investors deeply unhappy with what he was doing. At one point he was leveraged three to four times his firm’s capital in five-year maturities. His reasoning was clear: with 14 percent available in long-term treasuries, stocks had to sell much lower to compete, and the unidirectional message of higher rates slowing the economy was unmistakable. The only question was timing the turn, and he never came close to liquidating.

On October 19, 1987, Steinhardt came in 80 to 90 percent net long and increased his exposure during the day—a contrarian bet that enormous single-day moves are usually best played from the opposite side. He finished October down over 20 percent but stood by his process. He had underestimated the forces diminishing market stability: portfolio insurance, program trading, and global asset allocation all exerted unidirectional selling pressure, while the natural stabilizers—individual investors and the specialist system—had been greatly reduced throughout the 1980s.

“The more things you bring to the table—shorting, hedging, participation in bond markets, futures market trading—the better off you are.” — Michael Steinhardt

His advice for laypeople was sobering: professionals devote their entire lives to trading and are usually on the opposite side of retail trades. If treasury bonds are yielding 9 or 10 percent, stock returns must justify the incremental risk. Do not underestimate the difficulty of the game.

William O’Neil

William O’Neil was born in Oklahoma during the Great Depression, raised in Texas, and started as a stockbroker at Hayden, Stone in 1958. Three back-to-back trades—shorting Korvette, going long Chrysler, then long Syntex—turned $5,000 into $200,000. By 1964 he had bought a New York Stock Exchange seat and formed his own firm serving over 500 institutional accounts. In 1983 he launched Investor’s Daily to compete with the Wall Street Journal, growing from 30,000 to 110,000 subscribers by 1988.

The CANSLIM formula emerged from O’Neil’s systematic study of every great stock winner from 1953 through 1985. The seed was planted in 1959 when he studied the Dreyfus fund managed by Jack Dreyfus, which had doubled its competitors’ results. O’Neil plotted every stock purchase on charts and discovered that every single one had been bought when it made a new high price. Each letter in CANSLIM represented a filter: Current quarterly earnings (the best stocks showed 70 percent average increases), Annual earnings growth (five-year average of 24 percent), something New about the company, Small shares outstanding (95 percent of winners had fewer than 25 million shares), Leaders not laggards (relative strength above 80), Institutional sponsorship (some but not too much), and Market direction (three out of four stocks follow the averages). At any given time, less than 2 percent of stocks fit all seven criteria.

“My philosophy is that all stocks are bad. There are no good stocks unless they go up in price.” — William O’Neil

His risk control was absolute: a maximum 7 percent loss rule applied to every position, automatically and without hesitation. He illustrated the danger of holding losers with Fred C. Kelly’s turkey trap story: a man rigs a trap and catches twelve turkeys, but one escapes. He waits for it to return. More escape while he waits. He keeps waiting for the count to go back up. He ends with nothing. The moral: stop counting turkeys and accept the loss.

O’Neil was devastating in his critique of conventional stock wisdom. Low price-to-earnings ratios had very little correlation with the best performers. Dividends had none. Diversification was “a hedge for ignorance”—he recommended owning just one or two stocks with $5,000 and six or seven with $100,000. The market was neither efficient nor random: not efficient because too many poorly conceived opinions drove prices, not random because strong investor emotions created persistent trends. Surprisingly, given his emphasis on individual stock picking, he endorsed mutual funds as an outstanding way to invest for the average person—with one caveat: sit and do not think. Hold for fifteen years or more and ride through every bear market.

David Ryan

David Ryan was O’Neil’s protege who adapted CANSLIM with his own modifications. Each week he screened 7,000 stocks, reviewed approximately 4,000 charts, applied fundamental filters, and narrowed his universe to about seven finalists. His requirements were strict: earnings rank above 80 (preferably 90), relative strength above 80, shares outstanding under 30 million, and the industry group in the top 50 of 200. He wanted institutional ownership between 1 and 20 percent—enough to power the stock but not so much that it was fully discovered.

Ryan’s win rate was only about 50 percent, which would seem mediocre until you understood his asymmetry. He cut losers within two weeks, usually faster, limiting losses to 7 percent or less. His profits came from the few stocks each year that doubled or tripled, held for six to twelve months. He preferred stocks that had already doubled, reasoning that if the situation was that good, the doubling might be just the beginning. He added a P/E filter that O’Neil himself did not use, targeting stocks with price-to-earnings ratios between one and two times the S&P 500’s ratio.

“It is like a giant treasure hunt. Somewhere in here there is going to be a big winner, and I am trying to find it.” — David Ryan

One lesson he learned the hard way involved a stock called Textone. He missed the trade trying to save a quarter-point on his entry. The stock went from $15 to $45 without him. Volume had to double on the breakout for a valid signal, and a stock should ideally show a profit on the very first day. Ryan kept a meticulous trader’s diary, annotating every purchase on charts and filing them in binders. The discipline cemented winning characteristics and prevented him from repeating mistakes—though it could not prevent all of them. In 1983-84, after a streak of success, he let it go to his head and broke his cardinal rule by buying overextended stocks.

Marty Schwartz: Champion Trader

Marty Schwartz spent a full decade losing money as a securities analyst. He was well paid but always broke from market losses. The transformation came when he switched from fundamental to technical analysis and, more importantly, when he separated his ego from his need to make money. For nine years, admitting he was wrong had been more painful than losing the money itself. He used to try to will things to happen. Once he stopped, he ran $5,000 up to $140,000 in two years.

He grew up in New Haven, modest means, shoveling snow for $10 at age seven. Amherst College was the first time he had to struggle intellectually. The U.S. Marine Corps tested him differently: as the only Jewish person in Officer Candidate School, a platoon sergeant drew a Star of David on his forehead with a magic marker. The hardest thing was scrubbing it off. He persevered. The Marines strengthened his body, and combined with Amherst, convinced him he could do almost anything.

On Wall Street, disasters piled up. A drunk analyst on a plane leaked Schwartz’s bearish hospital management report, causing the stocks to plummet. Schwartz was hauled before a New York Stock Exchange hearing for six hours, totally exonerated but professionally tainted. He cycled through jobs, found a “real lunatic” with commodity computer programs who cost him most of his remaining capital, and watched Edwards & Hanly go bankrupt in 1975 despite having a great trader named Bob Zoellner who had single-handedly kept the firm afloat by shorting in 1974.

Meeting his wife in 1976 was the real turning point. She made him realize his life was not a dress rehearsal. By 1979 he had a quote machine in his office at E.F. Hutton—the only analyst with one—and was synthesizing Terry Laundry’s Magic T Forecast with Bob Zoellner’s market analysis. He went on to dominate nine out of ten four-month trading championships, making more money than all other contestants combined, with an average return of 210 percent per contest. His single one-year championship return was 781 percent. He never lost more than 3 percent of equity on a month-end basis.

“Because they would rather lose money than admit they’re wrong. The ultimate rationalization of the trader who loses: ‘I’ll get out when I’m even.’” — Marty Schwartz, on why most traders lose

His most dramatic loss came on Election Day, November 1982, when Republicans did better than expected and the market surged 43 points. He was short, and the S&P locked at its limit against him. He lost $600,000 in a single day. His wife sat next to him the next morning repeating “get smaller, get smaller” every ten minutes. He shrank to a fifth or a tenth of normal size and focused only on printing black ink. He lost just $57,000 the rest of November after that $600,000 hit. On October 19, 1987, he liquidated his long position at 267-1/2 when the S&P’s Monday high was 269, losing $315,000 on 40 contracts but proud of executing the exit. By Thursday, with the Dow down 275 points, he called the S&P pit and bought twelve contracts. He covered them rapidly for a $250,000 profit. It was 1987’s most profitable year for him.

Part III: A Little Bit of Everything

Jim Rogers

Jim Rogers co-founded the legendary Quantum Fund with George Soros in 1973—one senior partner, one junior, one secretary. They traded stocks, bonds, currencies, and commodities, long and short, all over the world, leveraged to the hilt. If they disagreed on a trade, they usually did nothing. Consensus investing was a disaster in general, but between the two of them, they almost always came together. After Soros, Rogers was the analyst, not the trader. The fund had no losing year after September 1970.

Rogers’s own early disaster was instructive. In 1970, he bought puts, tripled his money by May, and thought he was the next Bernard Baruch. He went short to make money faster and was completely wiped out two months later. He shorted Memorex at 48—dead right on the fundamental analysis—but covered at 72 after getting destroyed on the way up. The stock eventually fell to 2. The lesson was searing: the market will go higher than you think it can and lower than you think it will, and being right about the direction means nothing if your timing is wrong.

By 1980 he retired, negotiating a deal with Columbia University to teach for free in exchange for lifetime access to the gym. His “Bulls and Bears” course required each student to find a major historical market move and explain what you could have known at the time. He studied hundreds, possibly thousands, of bull and bear markets, looking for anomalous years on long-term charts and asking why they happened.

“The biggest public fallacy is that the market is always right. The market is nearly always wrong.” — Jim Rogers

Rogers made only three to five decisions a year and stayed with them. He invested against central banks, never followed conventional wisdom, and had no patience for technical analysis—he claimed never to have met a rich technician. His description of the market hysteria cycle was clinical: prices start low and value buyers enter, then more buy because the fundamentals look sound, then people buy because it has been the thing to do, and finally the magical stage of hysterical buying pushes prices beyond any rational value. The same process operates in reverse. In 1968, major firms published research arguing there was a shortage of stocks. In 1987, he heard the same thesis recycled. His response: at the bottom of the next bear market, there would be a shortage of money.

His macro views in 1988 were characteristically bold. Japan’s stock market would have a major collapse—Australia had just sold a 1.5-acre plot in Tokyo for $450 million, bought twenty-five years earlier for $250,000, which was tulipmania by any definition. Oil would go “way down,” guaranteed under $12. Gold was not the great inflation hedge of the 1990s because supply had increased every year since 1980. And the U.S. budget deficit was a time bomb: like having termites in your foundation, you might not notice them until one day they gnaw away a big chunk and the house collapses.

Mark Weinstein: High-Percentage Trader

Mark Weinstein had no losing months since 1980, no losing weeks, and only seventeen losing trades out of thousands over two years—nine of which occurred because his quote machine went down. In an options contest among 47 CBOE floor traders with $100,000 accounts, he turned his $100,000 into $900,000 in three months with 100 percent winning trades and no pyramiding. His win ratio was near 99 percent.

He began as a real estate broker to whom charts looked like television test patterns. His first commodities trade in 1972 was a $8,400 get-rich-quick attempt in corn that lost $600 in three days. He spent years working seven days a week renting apartments, saving $24,000, and studying the gold market until charts and indicators finally made sense. His studio apartment had charts plastered on every wall. He slept with trading and dreamt about it. He was not motivated by money—he was hooked on the game.

His worst disaster involved a castle. He had nearly $1.5 million in his trading account when he saw a castle in the south of France for $350,000, complete with a moat. He came back to trading wanting to make exactly $350,000 for the castle—a target determined by a material desire rather than market analysis. He took a massive long soybean position, was up 25 percent on day one, and then watched the market collapse, locking limit-down five consecutive days. He lost over $600,000, about $125,000 per day at a time when the average American salary was $15,000 a year. On the fifth day, he sat in a park crying on a stranger’s lap, practically psychotic. Other traders overconsoled him and then laughed behind his back.

Years later, he got his revenge on soybeans. For years his eyes would edge toward the soybean chart before quickly flipping the page, like a Sicilian whose wife had been murdered ten years earlier, waiting for the perfect moment. He watched peripherally as soybeans hit $4.75, then bought at $6.18 before the 1988 drought, getting out at $7.25 and $9.92. The high was $10.46. He made his money back in spades. It was, he said, a total catharsis.

“The greatest traders are the ones who are most afraid of the markets.” — Mark Weinstein

How did he achieve a near-perfect win rate? Schwager’s analysis identified several factors: custom-designed computer systems monitoring momentum, proprietary values for technical indicators that he frequently adjusted, and a combination of cycles, Fibonacci retracements, and Elliott Wave analysis. But the real secret was patience. He only traded when nearly everything lined up and the timing felt virtually perfect. He used the cheetah analogy for position trading: although the fastest animal on earth, the cheetah hides in the bush for a week, waiting for a baby antelope—preferably sick or lame—and only attacks when there is no chance of losing its prey. That was the epitome of professional trading. For day trading, he invoked the sparrow: taking little pieces, flying away, coming back. You can never shoot a sparrow.

Part IV: The View from the Floor

Brian Gelber

Brian Gelber graduated in 1976 and started at a boiler room commodity broker in Salt Lake City. He recognized the fraud quickly and quit, joining Thomson McKinnon for $800 a month cold calling. By September 1977, at age twenty-five, he was a broker on the Treasury bond floor in Chicago. He visited eight firms in New York and seven opened accounts—right place, right time.

His early education was expensive. In 1979, big customers from CitiBank and Citicorp insisted rates could not go higher, and Gelber bought all the way down. The experience transformed him from an opinionated trader into a reactive one. Opinions, he concluded, were not worth much. He was trapped about twelve times when news broke and all customer orders went against his position, costing roughly $500,000 total. But the scars taught him what no textbook could: you cannot be a great broker and a great trader simultaneously.

His real edge was reading other traders. He talked to about twenty-five traders daily, and if twenty of the twenty-five were nervous about the market going higher—which meant they were implicitly short—he would trade the long side. He was not picky about how he made money. His most dramatic trade came in 1986 when the Japanese cornered the long bond market. He sold 1,100 contracts above 91.00. The market reversed and soared to 92.00. He was down $1 million in less than five minutes. Through patient management, he reduced the day’s loss to $400,000. The Japanese buying style baffled Western traders—their priority was market share, not yield. One Japanese trader bought every long bond on the screen, then fifteen minutes later asked why the basis was going out.

“I’m not afraid to lose. When you start being afraid, you’re finished.” — Brian Gelber

Gelber estimated that out of 100 people on the floor, maybe five would make at least $1 million within five years. Half would lose everything. Natural talent was essential—either you were born to be a good trader or you were not. He cycled through periods: four months hot, two months cold, six months breakeven. Trading was all-consuming: you never slept because you constantly thought about it.

Tom Baldwin

Tom Baldwin walked onto the Chicago Board of Trade floor in 1982 with no trading experience and only $25,000. His wife was pregnant. He turned a profit from the very beginning and was a millionaire before his first year was up—$30 million appears a conservative estimate of his subsequent wealth. On a typical day, he traded over 20,000 contracts, equivalent to $2 billion in face value Treasury bonds.

His method was deceptively simple. He stood in the pit six hours a day, every day, trading one lot at a time, building an intuitive sense for how the same scenarios developed repeatedly. He averaged four-tick profits on massive positions with holding times measured in minutes or seconds. His most he was ever down in a single trade was nineteen ticks. He used only bar charts covering the past six months—no intraday charts, no computers, no sophisticated analysis.

Less than 20 percent of floor traders were still around after five years, and only 1 percent ever reached the couple-million-dollar level. What separated that 1 percent, in Baldwin’s view, was hard work, perseverance, love of the business, and a total disregard for money. You had to trade for the challenge, not the money, and you could not translate losses into real terms. The winners had almost no fear—not recklessness, but a calm detachment that allowed clear thinking under pressure. The losers did not work hard enough, spent too much, assumed every trade was 50/50, and failed to watch the factors that actually drove the market.

“You don’t need any education at all to do it. The smarter you are, the dumber you are.” — Tom Baldwin

His approach to loss management was distinctive. He never abandoned a losing trade in panic, but he always maintained risk control. He waited for the optimum time to bail out, choosing his moment with the patience of a chess player. What looked like a five-yard loss could be turned into a two-yard loss by not rushing for the exit.

Tony Saliba

Tony Saliba started on the options floor in 1978, clerking for six months before a wealthy grain trader backed him with $50,000. His first two weeks produced a quick $25,000 profit through volatility spreads, but by spring 1979, volatility had collapsed and he was down to $15,000, feeling suicidal. He took a job at the Levy brothers’ restaurant while his backer gave an ultimatum: lose $5,000 more and the plug would be pulled.

Saliba switched from volatile Teledyne to tight-range Boeing and became a spread scalper targeting a quarter or an eighth of a point. He was called “one-lot” for what felt like an eternity. The introduction of puts in June 1980 was his liberation—the lead trader who dominated his section hated puts, so Saliba studied them intensely and carved out a niche. He eventually befriended the pit’s top trader, worked out strategies by hand without computers, and then broke away to trade independently.

His breakthrough came in 1984 when he built a position in out-of-the-money Teledyne October calls. Pacific Coast traders leaned on his longs every night, trying to push him out. Instead of backing down, he bid for fifty more. On May 9, trading was halted: Teledyne announced a stock repurchase at $200 when the stock was at $155 and Saliba owned the $180 calls. He made millions overnight. His goal of becoming a millionaire before thirty was achieved before twenty-five. He retired on his thirtieth birthday, lasted four months, and came back.

“A trader told me, ‘In stealing second, never take your hand off first until your other hand is on second.’ I always have insurance.” — Tony Saliba

His strategy revolved around what he called matrix trading: butterflies in the front month (limited risk, profiting from time decay if the market stayed calm) combined with explosion positions in the back month (limited risk with open-ended potential from large moves or volatility spikes). The butterflies and scalping paid for the time decay, while the explosions waited for chaos. During the October 1987 crash, he sold his exchange seat Monday morning for $452,000 and bought it back Monday afternoon for $275,000—the first time anyone had traded an exchange seat as a round-turn. He kept 95 percent of his position through Monday. On Tuesday, when half the world wanted puts and half wanted calls but everybody wanted volatility, the register started ringing.

Part V: The Psychology of Trading

Dr. Van K. Tharp

Dr. Van K. Tharp was a research psychologist who developed the Investment Psychology Inventory in 1982 after losing money trading options himself and concluding the losses had something to do with him personally, not with the markets or his system. His research identified three clusters that determined trading success: psychological factors, management and discipline, and decision-making processes.

The profile of the losing trader was depressingly consistent: highly stressed, negative outlook, blaming others for losses, no defined rules, following the crowd, and chronic impatience. The top traders, by contrast, shared five core beliefs that seemed almost paradoxical. First, money was not important to them. Second, it was okay to lose. Third, trading was a game. Fourth, mental rehearsal before the market opened was essential. Fifth, they had already won before they started. Most people approach trading specifically to make a lot of money, and that was the primary reason they lost.

Tharp identified five primary impediments to trading success. Poor risk attitudes topped the list: most people are risk-averse with profits (taking them too early) and risk-seeking with losses (holding them too long)—the exact opposite of what is required. Stress triggered the fight-or-flight response, which narrowed focus, reverted people to beginner behavior, and turned them into crowd followers. Internal conflict between different psychological “parts” with competing intentions could cap earnings at an arbitrary level—one floor trader with a successful alcoholic father could not earn more than $75,000 a year until a single negotiation exercise unlocked him and he made $650,000 in two months. A subconscious desire to lose affected fully half the traders Tharp worked with. And emotional control could be achieved through controlling posture, breathing, and muscle tension.

“The moment one definitely commits oneself, then Providence moves too.” — W.N. Murray, quoted by Dr. Van K. Tharp

Tharp distinguished between mechanical and intuitive super-traders. The mechanical type constructed visual models, refused to trust anything that had not been computerized, and let the computer do everything. The intuitive type traded from expectations converted into feelings and believed computerizing was a waste of time. Despite their opposite approaches, both had developed models, done extensive testing, and shared the same core beliefs about markets and themselves. He drew an analogy from the U.S. Army’s marksmanship program: modeling two champion sharpshooters reduced training time from four days to two and increased qualification rates from 80 to 100 percent. The best shooter mentally rehearsed the entire 1,000-round match the night before. Trading excellence, Tharp believed, could be modeled and transferred in exactly the same way.

Key Takeaways

There Is No Single Path to Success

The traders use contradictory approaches—pure technical versus pure fundamental, seconds-long scalping versus months-long positions, systematic computers versus pure intuition—yet all succeed. Marcus relies on gut feel, Seykota on mechanical systems, Steinhardt on variant perception, O’Neil on CANSLIM screening. The answer lies not in copying someone else’s method but in finding your own.

Risk Management Is Everything

Every wizard emphasizes cutting losses. Marcus caps at 5 percent per idea, Kovner at 1 percent per trade, Jones monitors daily equity with a 10 percent monthly maximum, Hite limits to 1 percent per trade across Mint’s entire portfolio. The traders who survived early catastrophic losses—Marcus, Jones, Weinstein—all credit strict risk control for their recoveries and subsequent fortunes.

Psychology Trumps Analysis

Tharp’s research reveals that top traders share five beliefs: money is not important, losing is acceptable, trading is a game, mental rehearsal matters, and they have won before they start. Most losing traders fail from ego, not from bad analysis. Schwartz lost money for a decade as a fundamentalist and became rich the day he separated his ego from his need to make money.

Patience Separates Winners from Losers

Weinstein’s cheetah hides in the bush for a week waiting for the sick antelope. Rogers makes only three to five decisions a year. Dennis says 95 percent of profits come from 5 percent of trades. The wizards spend most of their time doing nothing, waiting for the high-probability setup, and then pressing hard when it appears.

Markets Change but Human Nature Does Not

Trend-following became harder as more participants adopted it, creating more false breakouts and crowding out the easy money. But emotional patterns—fear, greed, hope, the refusal to accept a loss—remain constant across centuries. Hite traces the pattern from the 1637 tulip crash through 1929 through the 1980 silver collapse: people are the same, and that sameness is the edge.

Being Right Is Not Enough

Schwager’s own cotton trade, Rogers’s Memorex short, and the NASDAQ bubble all demonstrate the same principle: you can be perfectly correct about a market’s direction and still lose everything if your timing, position size, or implementation is wrong. Implementation matters as much as analysis—sometimes more.

Worst Drawdowns Follow Best Performance

Jones warns that the biggest hits always come after great winning periods. Complacency, failure to consider worst-case scenarios, and high exposure all converge at the moment a trader feels invincible. Schwartz’s rule: after a very successful period, take a day off. His biggest losses always followed his largest profits.

Contrarian Ideas

The Efficient Market Hypothesis Is Wrong

October 1987’s 29 percent single-day drop had a probability of 10 to the negative 160th power under EMH assumptions. Track records like Seykota’s 250,000 percent return and Marcus’s $30,000 to $80 million are statistical impossibilities if markets are truly efficient. Hite’s observation cuts deeper: everyone who told him markets are efficient was poor.

Diversification Is Overrated

Bielfeldt built his fortune concentrating in soybeans, then T-bonds. O’Neil calls diversification “a hedge for ignorance” and recommends as few as one or two stocks with small capital. Marcus bet 5 percent per idea, not per trade. The wizards concentrate in their best ideas rather than spreading across many mediocre ones.

Great Traders Are Both Made and Born

Dennis proved trading can be taught—his Turtles averaged 100 percent per year. Yet multiple traders insist innate talent is essential. Gelber says you are either born to be a good trader or you are not. Both statements appear true simultaneously: trading proficiency is achievable with work, but elite performance requires something innate, like the difference between running a marathon and running one in 2:15.

The Market Is Nearly Always Wrong

Rogers’s most provocative claim inverts the efficient market assumption entirely. The market does not efficiently aggregate information—it amplifies emotion. Prices overshoot in both directions, creating the very opportunities that the wizards exploit. The conventional wisdom is almost always wrong, which is precisely what makes it conventional and what makes contrarians rich.

Trading Frameworks

CANSLIM (William O’Neil)

Seven-factor stock screening: Current quarterly earnings growth, Annual earnings growth, New products or management, Small shares outstanding, Leader status (relative strength above 80), Institutional sponsorship, Market direction. At any given time, less than 2 percent of stocks qualify. Maximum 7 percent loss rule on every position, no exceptions.

Variant Perception (Michael Steinhardt)

Develop views at odds with the market consensus, play them until they are no longer variant. Requires the conviction to maintain large positions through extended adverse moves and the flexibility to reverse when the perception is confirmed or invalidated.

The Turtle System (Richard Dennis)

Mechanical trend-following rules taught in two weeks. The rules were 80 percent as effective as anything Dennis used—the remaining 20 percent was the confidence and discipline to follow them through drawdowns. Published openly, almost nobody would follow them.

Scenario Building (Bruce Kovner)

Imagine many alternative pictures of the world, waiting patiently for one to be confirmed. Most scenarios will be wrong. When nine out of ten elements click in a single picture, that scenario becomes the operating reality. Combine with mandatory predetermined stops and a 1 percent per-trade risk limit.

Matrix Trading (Tony Saliba)

Butterfly spreads in front-month options for income during calm periods, combined with explosion positions in back-month options for asymmetric payoffs during volatility events. Scalping pays for time decay while the explosions wait for chaos.

Three-Light System (Larry Hite / Mint)

Green: take all signals. Yellow: liquidate existing positions, no new trades. Red: liquidate everything. Combined with maximum 1 percent risk per trade, extensive diversification, and volatility tracking to exit markets where return-to-risk deteriorates.

Cheetah and Sparrow (Mark Weinstein)

Position trading as a cheetah: hide in the bush, wait for the sick antelope, attack only when there is no chance of failure. Day trading as a sparrow: take little pieces, fly away, come back. You can never shoot a sparrow. Both require extreme patience and the discipline to wait for virtually perfect conditions.

The Poker Model (Gary Bielfeldt)

Play only the good hands and fold the poor ones, forfeiting the ante. When the percentages are strongly in your favor, raise aggressively. Position sizing maps directly from poker to trading: bet big when the odds favor you, minimize exposure when they do not.

About the Author

Jack D. Schwager has spent over three decades as a trader, analyst, and author in the futures and hedge fund industries. He began as a commodity research analyst and learned through painful personal experience—multiple account wipeouts, impulsive trades, and the emotional roller coaster of leveraged speculation—the very lessons his interview subjects later articulated. His willingness to expose his own failures gives Market Wizards an authenticity that pure journalism could not achieve.

Schwager went on to write four additional Market Wizards volumes, including The New Market Wizards (1992), Stock Market Wizards (2001), Hedge Fund Market Wizards (2012), and Unknown Market Wizards (2020). He has also authored technical analysis texts and served as a portfolio manager and fund advisor. The interview format he pioneered became its own genre, spawning dozens of imitators across finance, sports, and business.

His background as a practitioner rather than a journalist is crucial. He knows which questions to ask because he has lived the mistakes himself. When Marcus describes the paralysis of watching a limit-down market or Jones recounts the 1979 cotton disaster, Schwager understands from the inside, and his follow-up questions reflect that understanding. This is not an outsider peering in but a fellow traveler mapping the territory.

Why This Book Matters

Market Wizards is the foundational text of the trading interview genre and arguably the most influential trading book ever published. Before 1989, the world’s greatest traders were anonymous figures known only by rumor and legend on exchange floors. Schwager gave them names, voices, and stories—and in doing so, demonstrated that consistent market-beating performance was not only possible but documented and verifiable. The book did more to demolish the efficient market hypothesis in practice than decades of academic debate.

Its influence extends far beyond finance. The core themes—that psychology matters more than technique, that risk management is the foundation of any high-stakes endeavor, that patience and discipline separate professionals from amateurs—apply to any field where decisions are made under uncertainty. The Turtle experiment alone has become one of the most cited examples of the nature-versus-nurture debate in popular culture, proving that excellence can be taught while simultaneously revealing how few people will actually follow through.

Nearly four decades after publication, the specific markets and instruments have changed but the human dynamics have not. Every generation of traders rediscovers the same emotional traps—the inability to cut losses, the compulsion to overtrade, the ego that demands being right over being profitable—that Schwager’s wizards describe with remarkable consistency. The book remains essential reading not because it teaches a system but because it teaches how to think about trading, risk, and the psychological warfare between discipline and impulse that every market participant must fight.

What Didn't Fit

Schwager's interviews are packed with specific trades and character-defining moments. Here are the best ones that did not make the chapter coverage.

Marcus: He Just Trades

When Amos Hostetter recommended Michael Marcus to Commodities Corporation, the company was steeped in academic pedigree — people like Paul Samuelson sat on the board. Their first question: “What articles has he written? In what journals has he been published?” The answer: “He just trades.” Marcus became the first non-PhD trader they hired. Starting with $30,000 in August 1974, he turned it into $80 million over the next decade. During the 1973 soybean limit-up streak, the stress became so unbearable that Marcus took thorazine, got on the subway, and started to collapse. He wandered home and fell through his doorway, knocked out. Looking up, he referenced the Fiddler on the Roof scene where the lead talks to God: “Am I really that stupid?” He seemed to hear a clear answer: “No, you are not stupid. You just have to keep at it.”

Kovner: The Soviets as Informed Traders

Bruce Kovner considered the Soviet Union one of the best-informed currency and grain traders in the world. Their intelligence capabilities gave them the ability to eavesdrop on commercial communications, and markets often moved before news reached typical traders. Large commodity trading firms sometimes used scramblers on their phone lines for sensitive calls — but the Soviets had better scramblers. Kovner also described a sterling/ mark cross-rate trade where the Bank of England defended the 3.00 level twenty times before finally giving in. Watching a central bank exhaust its reserves was, for Kovner, one of the most reliable setups in macro trading.

Jones: Credit Kills Civilizations

Paul Tudor Jones argued that credit eventually destroys every great society: the Romans, 16th-century Spain, 18th-century France, 19th-century Britain. America, he believed, was on the same trajectory. His proof of the culture's decay: Volvo offering 120-month car loans versus the 24-month standard of twenty years earlier. Jones also personally financed college education for 85 elementary students from Brooklyn's Bedford-Stuyvesant through the “I Have a Dream” program, meeting with them weekly. His Robin Hood Foundation targeted frontline workers with virtually no budget, not bureaucracies — “Markets have been so good to me that I feel I should give back something in return.”

Hite: The Minefield Boss

Larry Hite's most memorable cautionary tale: a company president got stuck with a losing options position. Hite told him to get out. The president held on, the market came back, and he liquidated at a small profit. Hite told a friend: “We work for a man who has just found himself in the middle of a mine field, and what he did was close his eyes and walk through it. He now thinks the proper technique is to close your eyes and go forward.” Hite also noted that when Iraq and Iran went to war, gold moved only $1 — “A Middle East war has just broken out and the best gold can do is go up $1; it has to be a great sale.”

Steinhardt: Half a Million in Twelve Seconds

Michael Steinhardt described the pre-1975 institutional markets as “taking candy from a baby.” His Penn Central trade captured the era: he bought 700,000 shares at 6-1/8 from a seller who had not checked the board (the stock was in Chapter 11, last trade at 7). He sold 700,000 at 6-7/8. Half a million dollars in twelve seconds. After 1975, when fixed commissions were abolished, the business became genuinely competitive and those easy profits disappeared forever.

Weinstein: Markets Go Down on Loss Taking

Mark Weinstein corrected one of the market's most common misconceptions. When reporters say a decline was caused by “profit taking,” Weinstein pointed out: “I think it would be wonderful if everybody was always taking profits.” The truth is most people lose money. Markets go down on loss taking, not profit taking. He also noted that after the JFK assassination, the market broke sharply then rebounded to new highs — “a market poised to move higher won't reverse because of news, even dramatic news.”
