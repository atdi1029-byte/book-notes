# Market Wizards — Compacted Notes (First Half)

## Preface & Prologue

### Efficient Market Hypothesis Challenged
- EMH assumes equal information = equal skill, but chess analogy proves otherwise: all players know same rules, yet few excel
- Markets are more complex than chess — a few highly skilled participants can exploit mistakes of weaker players and drive prices to incorrect levels
- Emotions cause bubbles and panics that rational models can't explain
- October 19, 1987: S&P futures fell 29% in one day. Under EMH, probability = 10^-160 (equivalent to randomly picking same atom twice in entire universe)
- NASDAQ bubble: shorting at 3,000 in 1999 was fundamentally correct (eventually traded 1,100-2,900), but market soared additional 68% to 5,048 by March 2000 first. Being right but early can destroy you.
- EMH fails to incorporate human emotions as key price driver. Emotions can temporarily outweigh informed traders (hedgers, governments trade for non-profit motives)

> "There is certainly no need to resort to the assumption of market perfection to explain why winning in the markets is difficult."

### Trading as Innate Talent
- Marathon analogy: most can run one with training, but only tiny minority can run 2:15 (men) or 2:30 (women) regardless of effort
- Trading proficiency achievable with work, but elite performance requires innate talent
- Market Wizards possess "some innate skill, or some inner radar" giving better-than-even probability of sensing market direction

### The Wizards' Stats
- Trader who turned $30,000 into $80 million after early wipeouts
- Fund manager with five consecutive years of triple-digit percentage returns
- Former securities analyst averaging 25% monthly return (over 1,400% annualized) for seven years
- MIT electrical engineering graduate: 250,000% return over sixteen years via computerized approach
- Common denominator: attitude, not methodology. Some use only fundamental, others only technical, time horizons vary from minutes to years

### Kilgore Trout Analogy (Kurt Vonnegut)
- Slaughterhouse Five reference: Earthlings kidnapped, put in zoo with fake stock quotes. Prayed during bad week in olive oil futures — olive oil went up.
- Random walk theorists say traders are like zoo inhabitants — success is luck, not skill
- After interviewing traders, hard to believe some can win with such consistency over so many trades and years

## Schwager's Own Story

- Commodity research analyst out of grad school. Brother opened $2,000 account; Schwager used basement pay phone to place orders (nerve-wracking delays)
- First major trade: detailed cotton analysis through post-WWII period. Only two seasons since 1953 were free markets. Forecast peak at 32-33 cents.
- Cotton initially rose as predicted (28 to 31 cents in one week). Went short near projected top.
- Market rebounded: 32, 33, 34, 35 cents. Account wiped out. Cotton eventually soared to 99 cents — more than double century's previous high.

> "That trade knocked me out of the box for a while."

- Two breakthroughs: (1) Meeting Steve Chronowitz — strictly technical analyst vs. Schwager's pure fundamental approach. Noticed Steve's calls were often right. Realized fundamental analysis alone insufficient — needed technical for timing. (2) Risk control epiphany — never lose everything on single trade, regardless of conviction.
- Turning point trade: Deutsche mark long with good-till-cancelled stop just below recent low. Stopped out at small loss. Previously this type of trade would have wiped him out.
- Japanese yen: bullish consolidation, could define risk at only 15 ticks per contract. Put on three-contract position. Market never looked back — tripled small account.
- Synthesis of technical + fundamental + risk control: built small stake to over $100,000
- Second wipeout: impulsive soybean trade, convinced decline was bull market reaction, substantially increased position. Compounded by adding in front of government crop report. Report bearish — surrendered over one-quarter of cumulative profits within days.
- Rebuilt $8,000 to $100,000 twice, but couldn't expand beyond that level. Frustration: "Why have I been able to multiply sub-$10,000 account more than tenfold twice, yet unable to expand equity beyond that level?" Wrote book seeking answers.

## Michael Marcus: Blighting Never Strikes Twice

### Origin Story
- 1969: Graduated Johns Hopkins, Phi Beta Kappa, near top of class. Ph.D. fellowship in psychology at Clark University.
- Met "John" through mutual friend who claimed he could double money every two weeks. Hired him at $30/week plus free potato chips and soda.
- Started with $1,000 saved. First visit to Reynolds Securities in Baltimore felt reverent — mahogany, hushed tones.
- Old-fashioned commodity board clicking prices. Had to use binoculars to see prices from gallery — "like watching a horse race."

### Early Disasters
- First realization John knew nothing: loudspeaker recommended buying soybean meal, John looked uncertain and asked "Do you think we should do it?"
- First eight trades: all losers. Neither knew contract sizes, only knew cost per tick.
- Measuring success by days it took to lose — took three days to lose money on winning wheat trade (bought wheat, held too long)
- John proposed "can't lose" pork belly spread — but August pork bellies weren't deliverable against February contract. The logic was fundamentally flawed.
- John went to quote board shouting "Doesn't anyone want to make a guaranteed profit?" — pounding his fist. Marcus wiped out entirely.

> "You are making the greatest mistake of your life!" — John, when Marcus fired him. Said he was going to Bermuda to wash dishes to make a trading stake. Marcus never heard from him again.

### The Corn Blight
- Father died when Marcus was 15, left $3,000 life insurance. Marcus cashed it in against mother's objections.
- Read Chester Keltner's books on wheat and soybeans. First real win: followed Keltner recommendation, made 4 cents/bushel ($200). Then bought again on own intuition — another profit. "Beginning to develop a sense for trading."
- Summer 1970: bought three contracts December corn on Keltner's recommendation. Corn blight devastated crop. Accumulated $30,000 — "princely sum" for middle-class background.
- "Instinctively doing the right thing even then" — took profits on way up and when markets started coming down.

### Graduate School & Second Blight Disaster
- Fall 1970: attended grad school in Worcester, Massachusetts. Couldn't focus on thesis — would sneak down to Paine Webber office to trade. "Handwriting was on the wall" — dropped out December 1970.
- Stayed at YMCA briefly, told people pompously "I was a speculator."
- Spring 1971: theory blight had wintered over. Borrowed $20,000 from mother, bet $50,000 total on corn/wheat — maximum contracts possible.
- Wall Street Journal headline: "More Blight on the Floor of the Chicago Board of Trade Than in Midwest Cornfields"
- Market went limit-down. Marcus paralyzed: "Hoping market would turn around. Watched and watched and then after it locked limit-down, I couldn't get out."
- Had all night to think but "really had no choice. Didn't have any more money."
- Lost $30,000 of own money + $12,000 of mother's loan = $42,000 total.

> "That was my lesson in betting my whole wad."

### Research Job & Secret Trading
- Found commodity research analyst slot at Reynolds Securities. "If you shoot for what you want, you stand a much better chance of getting it because you care much more."
- Analysts strictly forbidden to trade. Borrowed from mother, brother, girlfriend — opened account at another firm.
- Worked out intricate code system with broker — "sun was out" meant one thing, "weather is cloudy" meant something else.
- "In manic-depressive state throughout that time. Felt tortured because wanted to be free to trade."

### Meeting Ed Seykota — The Turning Point
- October 1971: Seykota recently graduated MIT, developed one of first computer programs for testing/trading technical systems.
- Seykota taught Marcus how to cut losses and importance of riding winners.
- Example: Seykota short silver, market eking down half penny/day, everyone bullish. "The trend is down, and I'm going to stay short until the trend changes." Marcus "learned patience from him in the way he followed the trend."

> "Am I really that stupid?" — Marcus looking up, referencing Fiddler on the Roof scene where lead talks to God. Seemed to hear clear answer: "No, you are not stupid. You just have to keep at it."

- Second mentor: Amos Hostetter, kind, knowledgeable semi-retired broker at Shearson. Reinforced principles Seykota taught. "Getting the same principles from two people."

### The Plywood Trade & Recovery
- Late 1971: totally out of money and lenders. Making $12,500/year, saved $700. Opened joint account with friend ($1,400 total). "Stubborn confidence that I could somehow get back on the right track."
- July 1972: under Nixon price controls, plywood frozen at $110 per 1,000 square feet.
- Marcus put out bearish newsletter: "Since prices couldn't go beyond $110, there was nothing to lose by going short at $110."
- Price hit $110, then $110.10, then $110.20 — futures trading over legal ceiling!
- Reasoning: "If they let it trade over $110 today, they might let it trade anywhere." Bought one contract. Plywood went to $200.
- Futures market functioned as supply of last resort — created "sort of legal black market."
- Then bet everything on lumber expecting same situation. Government announced crackdown on speculators. Nearly wiped out again.
- $12,000 shrunk to under $4,000. "Worst two weeks in my whole life. Went to office each day just about ready to give up." Hands shaking.
- "Never did it again. That was the last time I bet everything on one trade."
- After holding on, shortage proved real. Account: $700 → $12,000 → $4,000 → $24,000 by year-end 1972.

### 1973 Bull Market
- Government lifted price controls → numerous artificial shortages resolved → tremendous commodity run-up. "Just about everything went up. Prices doubled in many markets."
- Account grew $24,000 to $64,000. Marcus politically right-wing, inflation-alarmist — "the right theory for the right time."
- Soybeans went from $3.25 to nearly $12. Marcus impulsively took profits — "trying to be fancy instead of staying with the trend." Seykota stayed in.
- Soybeans went limit-up for twelve consecutive days while Marcus was out. "Dreaded going to work, because I knew soybeans would be bid limit again and I couldn't get in."
- So aggravating "felt I couldn't take it anymore." Took thorazine, got on subway, started to fall. Wandered home, fell through doorway, knocked out. "That was the low point in my trading career."

### The Cotton Exchange Floor
- Adrenalin rush hearing traders yelling — "seemed like the most exciting place in the world."
- Needed $100,000 net worth to qualify. Had virtually no assets outside commodity account.
- Good at picking trades, total bust at execution. Very shy, too timid to yell loud enough.
- Learned subconscious sense of market: intensity of voices tells you about price direction. When ring moderately loud then suddenly very loud — not signal of breakout, but market running into opposing orders.
- At key intraday chart points: take twenty-contract position instead of three-to-five with extremely close stop. Market either took off and ran, or was out. "Getting shot at making several hundred points and hardly risking anything."
- "Trading in those days was a little bit like being a surfer. Trying to hit crest of wave just at right moment."

### Joining Commodities Corporation
- Amos Hostetter recommended Marcus. At time, company theory: hire great econometricians as traders. People like Paul Samuelson on board.
- First question about Marcus: "What articles has he written; in what journals has he been published?" Punch line: "He just trades."
- First non-Ph.D. trader hired. Started with $30,000 in August 1974.
- After ten years: turned $30,000 into $80 million.
- Making at least 100% a year for years. Company taxed traders 30% annually for expenses.

### 1979 Gold Trade
- Caught gold going to over $800. In and out, catching $100/ounce chunks at a time.
- Buying gold in Australia, Hong Kong would push it up $10, another $10 in London — by time New York opened, selling at $30 profit.
- Advantage of being in California — up trading Hong Kong when New York colleagues asleep.
- Heard Afghanistan invasion on TV news. Called Hong Kong — nobody knew. Bought 200,000 ounces (2,000 contracts) before anybody knew what was happening.
- Immediate $10/oz profit on 200,000 ounces when news broke five to ten minutes later.
- Told on last visit to Hong Kong shouldn't visit gold floor — some people still remember.

> "It's hard to believe you could trade off the television news." — "First, last, and only time, but I did do it."

- Got out around $750 on way up. Gold went to nearly $900 then back to $400. Felt sick when saw it go up but much better when it came back.
- Rule: get out when volatility becomes insane. Third consecutive limit-up day = very cautious. Fourth = almost always out. Fifth = mandate to exit. "Just forced myself out of the market on that kind of volatility."

### Currency Trading
- Positions as large as 600 million Deutsche marks (~$300 million). One of bigger currency traders in world.
- "It killed my marriage" — waking every two hours to check markets. Tuned in every major center: Australia, Hong Kong, Zurich, London.
- Late 1978: dollar getting battered, falling to new lows daily. Period of collaborating with Bruce Kovner, talking hours daily.
- Noticed dollar got mysteriously strong — intense price movement couldn't be explained. Bailed out of long currency positions. That weekend, Carter announced dollar support program.
- Currency futures locked limit-down for several days after announcement. Marcus got out right near the top.
- Principle: big players, including governments, always tip their hand. European central banks notified about major changes as courtesy — price move shows up in Europe first.

### Traumatic Trades
- Deutsche mark: Bundesbank intervened to punish speculators. Down $2.5 million in five minutes. Got out rather than let it become $10M loss. Market recovered entire fall within half an hour.
- Poor investments: owned about ten houses, lost money on all of them (some sold before spending a single night). Plane charter service lost money. 30% to taxes, 30% to planes, 20% to real estate.

> "Out of a fairly large number of real estate transactions—many in California—I lost money on all but one of them. I am probably the only person alive that can claim that dubious distinction."

- Investment failures were emotional decisions without analysis — repeating early trading mistake of getting involved in things he knew nothing about.

### Key Trading Wisdom
- **Never bet more than 5% of money on any one idea** — 5% applies to ONE IDEA (two related grain markets = one idea)
- **Always use stops** — actually put them in, commits you to getting out
- **Mental flexibility**: if position doesn't feel right soon after entry, get out immediately. Don't worry about broker thinking you're an idiot.
- **Hold winners, cut losers** — both equally important. If you don't stay with winners, won't have profits to pay for losers.
- **Follow your own light** — don't trade on someone else's ideas. Incorporating someone else's approach often yields worst of both styles.
- Best trades: all three things going for you — fundamentals, technicals, market tone

> "If you become unsure about a position, and you don't know what to do, just get out. You can always come back in."

### Soybean Sentiment Example
- Extreme soybean shortage. Contact called with export figures: "Good news and bad news." Good news: fantastic export commitment. Bad news: "You don't have a limit position."
- Market expected to limit-up for three days. Marcus felt depressed not having larger position, bought more on opening.
- Market opened limit-up, then traded at limit, then eased off. "Soybeans were supposed to be limit-up for three days, and they can't even hold limit-up the first morning."
- Frantically sold everything. Accidentally ended up short — bought back 40-50 cents lower. Only time he made a lot of money on an error.
- Rule: when news is wonderful and market can't go up, be sure to be short.

### Teaching Bruce Kovner
- Marcus's best student and close friend. When Marcus was up 300%, Kovner was up 1,000%.
- What made Kovner special: objectivity — a good trader can't be rigid.
- Marcus conveyed principles from Seykota and Hostetter, plus acquired skills.
- Best trading occurred when collaborating — phenomenal results.

### Markets Have Changed
- In early days, Marcus bought at breakout, elevator operator got call from broker hour later, brokerage house recommended next day, dentists finally bought day after.
- Marcus was one of first professional traders. Would sell to dentists several days later.
- Now: moment market breaks key chart point, perceived by whole universe of traders simultaneously.
- Dentists don't count — trading one-lots meaningless when fund managers trading thousand at a clip.
- "Now you almost have to be contrary. You have to ask, 'Isn't it true that all my fellow professional traders are already in, so who is left to buy?'"
- Trend-following systems doomed to mediocrity unless major imbalance overrides everything.
- Markets more prone to false breakouts. Central banks preventing currency trends by taking other side.
- Secret: cutting down number of trades. Restrict to optimum trades only.

### Marcus's Personal Philosophy
- Gut feel very important. Don't know any great professional trader without it.
- Successful trading requires multiple types of courage: to try, to fail, to succeed, to keep going.
- All successful traders eventually got to balanced life with fun outside trading. Without other focus, wind up overtrading or excessively disturbed about temporary failures.
- Handling losing streaks: sometimes fought back by trading heavier (usually doesn't work), then cuts down or stops for three to four weeks. "Losing begets losing."
- What makes Marcus different: very open-minded. When market moves counter to expectations: "I had hoped to make a lot of money, but it isn't working, so I'm getting out."

## Bruce Kovner

### Background & Scale
- May be world's largest trader in interbank currency and futures markets
- 1987 alone: profits exceeding $300 million for himself and fund investors
- Ten years: 87% average annual compounded return
- $2,000 invested in early 1978 → over $1,000,000 ten years later
- Despite incredible track record, kept surprisingly low profile — refused all interview requests
- After Harvard, taught political science at Harvard and University of Pennsylvania
- Didn't enjoy confronting blank page each morning. Managed political campaigns, then abandoned politics — didn't have financial resources.

### Discovery of Trading
- Mid-1970s: believed economics and political science education provided right background
- Studied interest rate theory intensively, fell in love with yield curve
- Found price distortions in early interest rate futures: "The market hadn't become important enough for CitiBank or Solomon Brothers, but it was important enough for me"
- First trade: interest rate spread based on textbook theory. Worked perfectly. Hooked.
- Second trade: copper intra-market spread. Idea eventually right but too early. Lost money.
- End of two trades: still ahead, $3,000 grown to about $4,000

### Soviet Union as Informed Trader
- Soviets considered good traders in currencies and grains, operating through commercial banks
- Information advantage from intelligence capabilities — can eavesdrop on commercial communications
- Large commodity trading firms sometimes use scramblers for sensitive calls
- Markets often move before news reaches typical traders

### Technical Analysis as Thermometer
- Charts like a doctor's thermometer — ignoring them would be foolish
- New chart patterns signal unusual events worth investigating
- Will trade purely on unexplained technical breakouts — often best risk/reward trades
- Breakouts from tight consolidations with no clear explanation often most powerful
- Heisenberg principle applied: the more a pattern is watched by speculators, the more prone to false signals
- News-driven breakouts (e.g., Wall Street Journal story) less reliable than unexplained moves

> "I never think about that, because the point about a technical barrier is that the market shouldn't go there if you are right."

### Risk Management
- Predetermined stops mandatory: "That is the only way I can sleep"
- Position size determined by stop distance. Knows exit point before entering any trade.
- Tries never to risk more than 1% of portfolio on any single trade
- Daily computer analysis of position correlations
- Eight highly correlated positions = one position eight times as large
- Wider stops on fewer contracts rather than tight stops on many contracts
- Moral: place stops where they reasonably indicate trade is wrong, not at point determined by maximum dollar you're willing to lose per contract

### Scenario Building
- Mental scenario building: imagines many alternative pictures of world, waits for one to be confirmed
- Tries scenarios one at a time — most will be wrong (only a few elements correct)
- "All of a sudden, you will find that in one picture, nine out of ten elements click" — that scenario becomes world reality image

### Post-October 1987 Crash Analysis
- Friday after crash: unusual trouble sleeping. Struggled all week with how events would impact dollar.
- Tested different visions: total panic (dollar becomes safest haven → rises), confusion (dollar gives ground)
- Late Friday realization: combination of need for stimulus, reluctance of Bank of Japan/Bundesbank, wide U.S. trade deficits → only solution was for Baker to let dollar fall
- Absolutely convinced this was only thing Baker could do. Very tense weekend waiting for Far East markets to open Sunday night.

### 24-Hour Operation
- Monitors everywhere: home, country home. Literally on duty 24 hours/day.
- Call levels set for every currency. If currency breaks predetermined range, staff calls immediately.
- Assistant allowed to wake him twice a year. Create scenario for every currency at least once/week.

> "The screen almost reaches out and grabs you. The way the quotes are made changes: They get wider; they get wilder."

- "For me, market analysis is like a tremendous multidimensional chess board. The pleasure of it is purely intellectual."
- Example: figuring out New Zealand finance minister's problems — how he'll cope with Australia, U.S., labor unions. Job is to solve puzzle with him and figure out consequences he or market doesn't anticipate.

### Cross-Rate Trading
- Sterling/mark cross rate example: yearlong congestion between 2.96-3.00, broke out about a month ago.
- Day it broke out: challenged top of range ~20 times. Bank of England kept defending it.
- Bank of England finally gave in — cross rate pierced 3.01, then no trades until 3.0350. Moved virtually full 1% without trading. Very unusual — meant everybody watching 3.00 level.
- Violent quick breakouts much more reliable than typical breakouts. Worse the fills, better the trade.
- Far fewer people pay attention to cross rates. General rule: the less observed, the better the trade.

### Fundamentals vs. Technical Analysis
- Like asking doctor whether he'd prefer diagnostics or patient chart monitoring — need both.
- Fundamentals more important now vs. 1970s when easier to make money with technical alone.
- 1970s: far fewer false breakouts. Now everyone is a chartist, huge number of technical systems.
- Stock market has far more short-term countertrends than commodity markets.
- Commodity markets driven by supply/demand for physical goods — if true shortage, prices keep trending.
- Trend-following systems will eventually self-destruct under weight of size and similar approaches.
- Only thing that would save them: period of high inflation when simple trend-following works again.

### Managing Over $650 Million
- Fewer markets with sufficient liquidity for optimum trade size
- Copper: now "the elephant" — can comfortably move 500-800 contracts/day, uncomfortably beyond
- Daily copper volume: only 7,000-10,000 contracts (much is local trading/spreads)
- Coffee trade made few million last year — at $600M AUM, $2 million doesn't matter much and diverts focus
- 50-60% of profits from currency trading on average. Trades any highly liquid currency including Scandinavian, Asian, Mideast.
- Crosses most important vehicle outside IMM (e.g., buying pounds/selling marks). Can't trade crosses on IMM due to fixed contract sizes.
- Client money as a call option: unlimited profit potential on upside, risk limited to reputation on downside. "A much better position than a symmetrical win/lose position."
- Manipulation rarely works — leads to arrogance, loss of touch with market structure. British trading org tried to corner crude oil, succeeded initially, then lost control — crude fell $4, lost ~$40M.
- Expert systems unlikely to match human traders: rules of trading constantly change. Worked with developers who concluded trading poor candidate — too many types of knowledge, rules for interpreting information keep shifting.

### 1981 Loss Year
- Lost about 16%. Combination of errors and market nature: first major bear market in commodities.
- Bear markets different: sharp down movements followed by quick retracements.
- Poor money management: too many correlated trades.
- Afterward: designed risk management systems, pays strict attention to correlations, measures total portfolio risk daily.

### Key Advice
1. Risk management most important thing to understand
2. **Undertrade, undertrade, undertrade**
3. Whatever you think your position should be, cut it at least in half
4. Novice traders trade 3-5 times too big, taking 5-10% risk when should be 1-2%
5. Don't personalize the market — it doesn't care whether you make money. Saying "I wish" or "I hope" = destructive thinking.

> "Strong, independent, and contrary in the extreme" — traits of winning trader

- Worst mistake: "going bust trade" (spur of moment decision). Stick to game plan.

## Richard Dennis: The Prince of the Pit

### Early Career
- After high school, got summer job as runner on exchange floor. Making $40/week, losing $40/hour trading — "tuition was small for what I learned."
- 1968-1969: father had exchange membership but didn't know trading; Dennis (under 21) signaled trades from sidelines while father stood in ring.
- Father's reaction at 21: "I really hate this. I have no idea what I'm doing. It's yours!"
- Lost "a couple of thousand dollars during that period."

> "When you start, you ought to be as bad a trader as you are ever going to be" — cheaper to fail early

- Warning about early imprinting: traders who get first big profit on long side become perennial bulls — "that is very bad." Both sides must be "equally OK."

### First Big Break (1970)
- Started trading minicontracts on Mid America Exchange — "a few mistakes in me, and I made most, but not all of them"
- No real confidence, just "a need to try to succeed"
- Friday close: grain markets at yearly highs. Applied rule: go with the trend, stronger the better. Bought minicontracts.
- Monday: markets opened limit-up due to corn blight news. Grew account from $400 to $2,000.
- Key lesson: "I did something that should work in the long run — I went with the trend."
- Pattern: strong Friday close = don't be short if market closes at high, or long if closes at low.

### Graduate School
- Signed up before summer 1970. Made $3,000 in three months — big impression.
- Went to Tulane in New Orleans, lasted one week. Used laundromat quarters to phone trades into Chicago.
- "Once I used up all the quarters and had nothing but dirty clothes; I had no choice but to come back to Chicago."
- Full-time commodity trader since then.

### The $1,000 Loss Day
- Just quit grad school. Made bad trade, lost $300 (10% of $3,000 capital). Reversed position — lost again. Reversed back — lost third time. End of day: lost $1,000 (one-third of entire capital).

> "When you have a destabilizing loss, get out, go home, take a nap. When you are getting beat to death, get your head out of the mixer."

- In retrospect, one of his best trades: learned to avoid trying to catch up or double up. Loss affects judgment — must put time between loss and next trade. "When things aren't going right, don't push, don't press."

### 1973 Soybean Market
- Not just trend-following — "basically a pit trader, who traded in and out a lot." Markets excellent because of order flow.
- Many traders made terrible exits just to take profit, even when market locked limit-up and almost certain higher next day. "They couldn't stand the profits burning a hole in their pocket."
- Dennis would buy when they got out — high probability edge.
- Markets went limit-up ten days in a row. Buying at limit bid = odds play.

### Transition to Off-Floor Trading (1977-1978)
- 1978: worst year — compounding losses during transition from floor to desk
- "Had no idea how different they were." Must be longer-term as desk trader — lose the edge when putting in orders.
- Pit has subconscious indicators: "these three guys are never right at market turns" — light clicks on.

### The Turtle Experiment
- Philosophical argument with partner (friend since high school): Can trading be taught?
- Dennis: skills can be reduced to rules. Partner: something "ineffable, mystical, subjective, or intuitive."
- Dennis: "Here is a way we can definitely resolve this argument. Let's hire and train some people."
- 1,000 applications → 40 interviews → 10 selected (23 total over two groups)
- Won't disclose all criteria (mentioning chess players would cause resume flood). Intelligence was one trait but not essential.
- Training: shockingly short — two weeks first year, one week second year
- Results: 20 remaining averaged about 100% profit/year. Started with $100,000 each, now average $2 million.
- Name origin: just returned from Far East trip. "We are going to grow traders just like they grow turtles in Singapore." Visited farm with huge vat of thousands of squirming turtles.
- Told class: "We are going to teach you what we think works, but you are expected to add your own personal flair." Huge spread in results despite same training.

> "Almost anybody can make up a list of rules that are 80 percent as good as what we taught. What they couldn't do is give them the confidence to stick to those rules even when things are going bad."

- Could publish rules in newspaper — no one would follow them. Key is consistency and discipline.

### Discretionary vs. Systems
- Professional traders may do intelligent things but don't think systematically. Most don't ask "Why did it work? What did I do here I might do in another market?" Not a lot of reflection on the process.
- Academic types research before ever trading but lack "seat-of-the-pants knowledge."
- Real world: mechanical system might place stops where Dennis knows "there will tend to be a lot of stops." Not wise to have your stop where everyone else has theirs — above-average slippage.
- When feelings and systems absolutely opposed: do nothing until can resolve conflict.
- Advises traders: keep track of what doing right and wrong. Most tempting time to avoid thinking = when you should be thinking the most.

### Role of Luck
- Long run: "Absolutely zero. I don't think anybody winds up making money because they started out lucky."
- Individual trades: "almost all luck."
- 53% chance trade works → over long run, 100% chance system works. Takes couple years minimum to determine if one trader better than another.

### Key Trading Strategies
- "The market being in a trend is the main thing that eventually gets us in a trade."
- Method to enter less critical than ensuring if major trend, your approach gets you in it.
- "I could trade without knowing the name of the market" — if system doesn't work for both bonds and beans, don't care.
- Stocks vs. commodities: "Demonstrably, commodities are trending and, arguably, stocks are random." Not enough fundamental information per stock to create sufficient trends.
- Hypothetical to trainees: "Suppose everything indicates 'buy.' Then you call the floor and they tell you I'm selling. Do you: (a) buy, (b) go short, (c) do nothing?" If didn't understand (a) was correct, didn't fit the program.

### Sugar Trade & Expect the Unexpected
- Short sugar at 60 cents (November 1974 high of 66 cents → under 12 cents seven months later). Each 1-cent move = $1,120 per contract.
- "I've got ten stories like that. But in all honesty, I don't think the broad class of countertrend trades has been profitable."
- Lost more money going long sugar at 6 cents than made shorting at 60. "All market has to do is go down one more cent and you are out."

> "If there is any lesson I have learned in nearly twenty years, it is that the unexpected and the impossible happen every now and then."

- Beans at $4 went to $12.97 in four or five months. Correct: "this structure means up." Wrong: "this structure means up this much and no more."

### 95% of profits from 5% of trades — missing major opportunities is the worst mistake.

### Public Fund Shutdown & Market Changes
- Funds down just under 49% when stopped trading (April 1988). Rather than trigger automatic 50% termination, liquidated and resolicited.
- October 1987: short Eurodollar, market opened 240 points higher — blew 190 points in unavoidable skid.
- Only factor objectively identified: tendency toward more false breakouts, related to tremendous increase in computerized trend-following.
- "In a perverse sort of way, it represents the ultimate triumph of technical trading over fundamental trading. I say perverse because it is a victory that devalues technical trading."
- Solution: "Be far more short-term or as long-term as you can stand. Avoid the middle like the plague."
- Dennis retired from trading to focus on politics. Author speculates: "Will Dennis never trade again? Maybe, but don't bet on it."

### Emotional Control
> "Being a trader is like being a boxer: Every now and then, the market gives you a good wallop. After twenty years you get a bit punch-drunk."

> "Like playing golf: You can throw your clubs around after making a bad shot, but while you are making the next shot you should keep your head down and your eye on the ball."

- "There is more to life than trading." Avoids both emotional extremes. "There is no way to play just one side of that street."

### On Program Trading
> "The people that are complaining ought to be ashamed of themselves." — Scapegoat for declining market, "good excuse for doing a lousy job for yourself and your clients."

### Deficit as Time Bomb
> "It is like having termites in the foundation of your house. You may not notice them until one day they gnaw away a big chunk and the house collapses."

- "We expect continuity in our lives, but the economy, and certainly the markets, are more discontinuous than continuous."
- If were President: deficit first thing to change.

## Paul Tudor Jones: The Art of Aggressive Trading

### Performance
- October 1987 crash: Tudor Futures Fund returned 62% that month
- Five consecutive triple-digit return years with very low equity retracements (1986: 99.2%)
- Started as broker (second year: $1M+ in commissions)
- Floor trading: only one losing month in 3.5 years
- Tudor Futures Fund: $1.5M at inception (Sept 1984) → $330M by Oct 1988. $1,000 → $17,482.
- 85% of net worth in own funds: "Safest place in world for it"
- Public image: swaggering, egotistical. Reality: relaxed and unassuming privately.
- Chesapeake Bay mansion, 3,000-acre wildlife preserve.

### Philanthropy
- I Have a Dream Program: finances college education for 85 elementary students from Brooklyn's Bedford-Stuyvesant. Meets with students weekly — personally involved.
- Robin Hood Foundation: endowment grown to $5M. Targets frontline workers with virtually no budget, not bureaucracies.
- "Markets have been so good to me that I feel I should give back something in return."

### Mentor: Eli Tullis
- Legendary cotton trader, New Orleans. Position sizes of 3,000 contracts when entire market open interest was 30,000. Everyone knew his position.
- Steel-hard emotional control: carried on polite conversation while positions got decimated.
- "The hell with it, I'm going to take them head on."
- Key lessons: get out when market lets you, not when you want to. Don't wait for new highs/lows to move large positions. When markets look best at new highs, often best time to sell.

### The 1979 Cotton Disaster
- Broker with speculative accounts, long 400 contracts July cotton in 82-86 cent range. Bought every time market came to low end.
- Standing outside ring, told floor broker to bid 82.90 for 100 July (very big order). Refco broker screamed "Sold!" — they owned most certificated stock, intended to deliver.
- Congestion pattern insight: width of range would equal next move down (measured move concept).
- "It was my blood that was going to carry it there."
- Had entered long 400, added 100 day trade, final 100 on macho bid = 600 contracts total. Lost 60-70% of client equity in single trade.
- Almost quit, then decided to become disciplined and businesslike. Now thinks constantly about losing money, not making money. Always knows exit point before entry.

### Risk Management Rules
- Daily equity monitoring: goal to finish each day with more than started. Never thinks about overnight position carryover — mental reference = last night's close.
- Monthly max loss ~10%. Currently down 6.5% → has 3.5% stop for rest of month.
- Reduces position size during losing streaks — trading smallest when performing worst.
- Don't average losers. Never trade situations without control (e.g., risking significant money ahead of key reports = gambling).

> "Don't be a hero. Don't have an ego. Always question yourself and your ability. Don't ever feel that you are very good. The second you do, you are dead."

> "Risk control is the most important thing in trading."

> "Don't focus on making money; focus on protecting what you have."

- Jesse Livermore quote: "In the long run, you can't ever win trading markets" — devastating but frightening, guides Jones to play great defense.

### October 1987 Crash
- Expected major collapse since mid-1986, drew contingency plans for financial meltdown
- Week of crash = "one of most exciting periods of my life."
- Previous Friday: record volume day on downside — exact same thing happened in 1929, two days before crash
- Peter Borish's analog model: superimposed 1980s over 1920s market — remarkable correlation, perfectly nailed the collapse
- Baker's statement that U.S. wouldn't support dollar due to West Germany disagreements = kiss of death
- Covered shorts and went long on close of crash day itself. Also had extremely profitable bond position.
- Bond market acting terrible all day — very concerned about financial safety of clients' assets.
- During last half hour, bonds suddenly turned up → Fed adding massive liquidity. "As soon as I saw bond market act right for a moment, I went wild."

### Trading Style
- Best money made at market turns, not trend-following middle
- Markets only trend ~15% of time; rest they move sideways
- Does series of low-risk probes until finally hitting the turn. "Better story": catching exact turn vs. fifth try, but both work.
- Conventional wisdom: get killed picking tops/bottoms. Jones inverts this.
- Extreme flexibility: first meeting heavily short stock market, two weeks later shifted 180 degrees to bullish after market failed to follow through on downside. Willing to join opposite side when evidence proved him wrong.
- Uses time stop in addition to price stop: if thinks market should break and it doesn't, gets out even without losing money.
- More scared now than ever — biggest hits always come after great winning periods. Success breeds complacency.
- Managing large sums tremendously more difficult — could make substantially higher percentage returns trading smaller amounts.

### Trading System
- Found one system that works well after testing every system available. Trend-following system (secrecy important).
- Basic premise: markets move sharply when they move. Sudden range expansion = signal market getting ready to move in direction of that expansion.
- Human nature tries to fade price moves; range expansion = loud, clear signal.
- Good system may trade more markets effectively (unlimited computing power). Good trader usually outperforms good system.
- Good system helps diversify — catches 10x as much of price move during 15% trending periods.
- Hired ~35 traders via countless interviews. Mixed success — starting to believe it takes talent.
- Trading gives "incredibly intense feeling of what life is all about." Would take combination of winning and losing days over just winning.

### Economic Views
- Credit eventually kills all great societies: Romans, 16th-century Spain, 18th-century France, 19th-century Britain. "We are going to relearn what financial discipline is all about."
- Reagan: "biggest disaster that ever struck" economically — borrowed against future to create prosperity.
- Volvo offering 120-month car loans vs. 24 months twenty years ago (now 54 months average). Ease of credit delaying inevitable reckoning.

## Gary Bielfeldt: Yes, They Do Trade T-Bonds in Peoria

### Origin Story
- Started 25 years ago with $1,000, trading single corn contract from Peoria, Illinois.
- Doesn't believe in diversification — pick one area and become expert.
- Soybean complex focal point for most of career, then shifted to T-bonds.
- Refused to move to Chicago trading floor to maintain cherished lifestyle. Plows trading wealth back into community projects.

### The All-or-Nothing Soybean Trade (1965)
- Built $1,000 to $10,000 through painstaking effort.

- Consulted former agricultural economics professor Thomas Hieronymus who agreed soybeans would rise.
- A 10-cent decline would have completely wiped out account.

- Government position limits in soybeans became impediment as trading size grew.
- 1983 soybean loss forced shift to T-bonds (no position limit at time).
- May 1984: government auctioned five-year notes at 13.93% yield. No qualified borrowers for three-year loans at 13% since 1974. Unemployment approaching 20% in Peoria, farm crisis worsening.

- When percentages strongly in favor, be aggressive and leverage trade (like raising on good hands).
- Most important: method for staying with winners, getting rid of losers.
- Five traits: discipline, patience, courage, willingness to lose, strong desire to win.
- Most people judge success by performance. Bielfeldt: by what he does with accumulated money. Established foundation in 1985 to share success with community.

## Ed Seykota: Everybody Gets What They Want

### Record-Breaking Returns
- Completely unknown to public. One account starting with $5,000 in 1972 → over 250,000% by mid-1988.
- Normalized for withdrawals, theoretically up several million percent. No other trader matched this over same length of time.

> "You know, you really should interview Ed Seykota. He is not only a great trader; he is a mind." — Michael Marcus

### Interview Setting
- Works from house bordering Lake Tahoe. No quote screens on desk.
- Trading confined to few minutes running computer program for next day's signals.
- Interviewer planned two-hour visit, ended up spending entire day. Seykota said he knew from first phone conversation this would happen. "Extremely perceptive about people."
- Drive to house: early morning, highway winding into mountains, Mozart's Clarinet Concerto on radio — combination glorious. Interviewer's Wall Street office view: ugly building. Admitted jealousy.

> "How many minutes fast do you set your watch?" — Picked up on basic character trait after brief time together.

### First Involvement: Silver Disaster
- Late 1960s: decided silver had to rise when U.S. Treasury stopped selling. Broker convinced him to short copper first — stopped out, lost money. Then bought silver. Price started falling.

> "This was a very stunning education about the way markets discount news. I became more and more fascinated with how markets work."

### Building the First Computerized System
- Saw Richard Donchian's letter implying purely mechanical trend-following system could beat markets. Seemed impossible. Wrote computer programs on punch cards. "Amazingly, theories tested true."
- At second brokerage, used IBM 360 accounting computer on weekends — tested ~100 variations of four systems over ten years.
- Normal weekday job: load paper on Reuters machine, tear off news strips, deliver to brokers. Trick: tear between line-feeds for smooth edge.
- Started reading news and personally delivering to brokers — bonus: got to observe trading styles.

> "Because I knew I wanted to be in the business, and I didn't care what I did, or what I got paid."

### Management Interference Disaster
- Developed first large-scale commercial computerized trading system. Several hundred agents marketed program.
- System generated buy signal for sugar at 5 cents. Management thought overbought — changed rule: buy on 20-point pullback, then 30, 50, 100. Finally bought at 9 cents. Sugar peaked shortly after. Also ignored sell signal.
- Most profitable trade of year ended up losing money. Theoretical 60% return → actual losses for many accounts. Management wanted more trading for commissions. Seykota quit.
- Switched to pure profit incentive fee arrangement — getting away from counterproductive commission incentive.

### Track Record Details
- Does not publicize record other than "model account."
- Actual customer account: $5,000 in 1972 → over $15 million. Total return would have been many multiples larger without withdrawals.
- About half dozen accounts originally, four still with him. One made $15M and withdrew to manage himself. Another made $10M, bought house on beach and retired.
- Very rarely accepts new accounts — interviews clients for motivations and attitudes. People he associates with have "subtle yet very important effects" on performance.

### Trading Philosophy
- In order of importance: (1) long-term trend, (2) current chart pattern, (3) picking good spot to buy/sell. Fundamentals distant fourth — "quite likely, on balance, have cost money."
- Fundamentals you read about = "funny-mentals" (already discounted). If catch early before others believe = "surprise-a-mentals."
- "Being bullish and not being long is illogical." Turn bullish instant buy stop hit, stay bullish until sell stop hit.
- Elements of good trading: (1) cutting losses, (2) cutting losses, (3) cutting losses.
- Rules: cut losses, ride winners, keep bets small, follow rules without question, know when to break rules. Last two contradictory but believes both.
- Risk below 5% per trade. Set protective stops at same time as entry.
- "Losing position aggravating, whereas losing nerve devastating."

### Trend-Following Won't Die
- Life itself based on trends: birds south for winter, companies track trends, protozoa follow gradients.
- Profitability cycles: success → popularity → crowding → directionless markets → shakeout. Longevity is key.
- Over time, become more mechanical — more trusting of trend trading. Still goes through periods thinking can outperform system. "Such excursions often self-correcting through process of losing money."

### Silver Obsession & Dreams
- Lost repeatedly in silver. "Seemed to get into blood and hypnotize." Got killed so many times "started thinking was some kind of werewolf." Worked on self with hypnosis and positive imagery. Also avoided walks in full moon.
- Had dream of being in big, shiny, silver aircraft that stalled out and crashed. Eventually dumped position, went short. Dreams stopped.

### The Cyclical Loser
- One trader gets in near start of every bull move, works $10K to $250K in couple months, then personality changes and loses it all. Process repeats like clockwork.
- "On some level, I think he is really getting what he wants" — excitement, martyrdom, sympathy, attention.

> "Win or lose, everybody gets what they want out of the market."

### Why Traders Fail & Psychology
- "Many are called and few are chosen" — like baby turtles failing to reach maturity. Society works by attraction of many; as they're culled out, good ones left, others released to find their calling.
- Losing trader can do little to transform — "Not going to want to transform; that's what winning traders do."
- "Psychology is the driver and analysis is the road map."
- "A lot of people would rather understand the market than make money."
- Most flamboyant traders playing for more than profits — probably also excitement.
- Best way to increase profits: goal setting and visualizations to align conscious and subconscious.
- Uses combination of hypnosis, breathing, pacing, visualization, gestalt, massage to work with traders. They usually either get much more successful or realize they didn't really want to be traders.
- Quote machine on desk like slot machine — feed it all day. Gets price data after close.
- Great traders have special talent like musicians and athletes. "The talent has them" — they're absorbed by it.
- Luck plays enormous role. Some people lucky to be born smart; others smarter and born lucky.
- Advice for average traders: find a superior trader to manage money, then pursue what you really love. Most people better off not trading themselves.

> "Good traders trade. Good letter writers write letters."

> "Success has to do with finding and following one's calling regardless of financial gain."

### On Gut Feel and Intuition
- Gut feel important but can be deceptive — may reflect subconscious analysis or wishful thinking.
- Use meditation and reflection to distinguish true intuition from "into wishing."
- If gut feeling persists after reflection, may be valuable subconscious information.

### Worst Year: 1980
- Bull markets ended but kept buying dips. Never experienced major bear market before.
- Tried picking tops/bottoms in overbought/oversold markets — markets kept going.
- Eventually recognized futility and quit trading temporarily.

## Larry Hite: Respecting Risk

### Background
- Circuitous path: inauspicious academic performance, string of odd jobs, dual career as actor and screenwriter. One movie script optioned so often began viewing it as steady income source.
- "I may be the only person who ever worked as an actor and a scriptwriter to pay for my Wall Street career."
- Heard H.L. Hunt on radio describing cheap oil options — occasional windfall with minimal risk. Same night met Brian Epstein (Beatles manager). Two ideas fused: big money with minimal investment.
- Landed few record contracts as rock promoter but none reached stardom.
- Three shootings at clubs in one weekend convinced him to change careers.

### "Blue Chip" Revelation
- First interview at old-line Wall Street firm. Offices made you feel should speak in hushed tones.
- Interviewer: "We only buy blue chip for our clients."
- Looked up derivation: color of most expensive chip in Monte Carlo.

> "Aha, now I know what this game is all about — gambling."

- Threw away Graham and Dodd. Bought Beat the Dealer. Came away with idea: successful investment is matter of odds.

### Formation of Mint Investment Management
- Enlisted Peter Matthews (Ph.D. statistics) and Michael Delman (defense electronics systems designer).
- Performance: April 1981 through mid-1988, average annual compounded return over 30%.
- Consistency: worst year +13%, best +60%. Largest loss in any six-month period: 15%. Under 1% in any twelve-month period.
- Started with $2 million. Mid-1988: over $800 million under management.
- Believes can ultimately manage $2 billion — unprecedentedly large for futures fund.

### People Don't Change — That's the Edge
- 1637: tulips crashed 99%. 1929: Air Reduction fell 87%. 1961: Texas Instruments dropped 77%. 1980: silver fell 90%.
- "Because people are the same, if you use sufficiently rigorous methods, you can test a system and get a fairly good idea how it will perform in the future. That is our edge."
- Friend who is economist explained markets are efficient. Hite: noticed everyone who told him markets are efficient is poor.
- Peter's holding period analysis: 90% of six-month periods profitable, 97% of twelve-month, 100% of eighteen-month. After seven years actual trading: 90%, 99%, 100%.

### Insurance Business Analogy
- One sixty-year-old: no idea of odds he'll be alive in one year. 100,000 sixty-year-olds: excellent estimate.
- "We do the same thing; let the law of large numbers work for us. In a sense, we are trading actuaries."

### The Coffee Trader Story
- World's largest coffee trader, palatial London home, library with every book on power.
- "How can you know more about coffee than me? I know where the boats are; I know the ministers."
- Hite: "I don't know anything about coffee. I don't even drink it. I just look at the risk."
- Five times he asked, five times told him managed risk. Three months later: blown $100 million in coffee.
- "He does know more about coffee than I do. But he didn't look at the risk."

### The Bomb Disposal Expert
- Former colonel in British army. Specialty: dismantling bombs around world.
- "There are different styles of bombs; a bomb in Malaysia is different from Middle East. You go there and see what kind of bomb it is and take it apart."
- "What happens when you come across one you don't know?"
- "You record your first impression and hope it is not your last."

### Mint's Four Principles
1. Always follow trends, never deviate from system. Written agreement no one can countermand.
2. Maximum risk per trade: 1% of total equity. "Risk is no-fooling-around game."
3. Diversify extensively — more markets worldwide than any other money manager + multiple systems selected not only for individual performance but for lack of correlation with each other.
4. Track volatility — stop trading market when volatility adversely skews return/risk.

### Three-Light System
- Green: take all signals. Yellow: liquidate but no new positions. Red: liquidate everything, no new trades.
- 1986 coffee: $1.30 to $2.80 and back to $1.00. Got out at $1.70 and stayed out. May have lost additional profits, but "being out of markets like that is one of ways able to achieve rigid risk control."

### The Speculator's Advantage
- Trade has product knowledge. Floor has speed. Speculator has neither — but can choose not to play.
- "Speculator can choose to only bet when odds in his favor. That is an important positional advantage."

### Trading by System — No War Stories
- Friend: "Larry, how can you trade the way you do; isn't it boring?"

> "I don't trade for excitement; I trade to win."

- When get together with other traders exchanging war stories, has nothing to say. "To me, all our trades are the same."

### Don't Believe in Optimization
- "It is incredible how rich you can get by not being perfect." Not looking for optimum method; looking for hardiest.
- Overrated: overbought/oversold indicators. None prove out in testing.
- Two valuable indicators: (1) if market doesn't respond to important news in way it should, telling you something very important. Example: Iraq-Iran war news → gold only moved $1. "A Middle East war has just broken out and the best gold can do is go up $1; it has to be a great sale." (2) When market makes historic high, something has changed regardless of explanations.
- "We don't trade markets, we trade money."

### Cautionary Tales

#### The Prayer on the Stairway
- Noticed if bought pork bellies in September and sold before July, almost always made profit. Formed fund, doubled money, felt like genius.
- Talked into corn spread by friend. Government released surprising crop estimate. Long went limit-down, short went limit-up.
- Got down on knees in stairway: "Dear God, I don't care how much I lose, but please don't let the account go into a debit." Swiss banker walked past.

#### The Arbitrageur's Mansion
- Friend made fortune in option arbitrage. Developed trading system that made money.
- One day: "I am not taking the sell signal in gold; it doesn't look right to me. Besides, almost 50 percent of signals are wrong anyway."
- Not only didn't take sell signal, went long. Lost mansion and everything else. Lives in rented flat. Estate called "Beverly."
- "He had it and lost it all! And all because of one trade." If followed system, would have made fortune on that trade.

#### The Cousin's Options Strategy
- Cousin turned $5K to $100K. Strategy: stay in if it goes up, never take a loss.
- Hite warned him. Cousin: "It doesn't have to work in the long run, just till I make a million."
- Bought $90K in Merrill Lynch options, went to zero. Borrowed $10K to buy more at $1. Ended up $10K in debt.

#### Hunt Brothers
> "If you have a billion dollars and buy $20 billion worth of silver, you're in exactly the same risk position as the guy with $1,000 who owns $20,000 worth of silver."

#### The Minefield Boss
- Company president got stuck with losing options position. Hite: "Just get out." President held on, market came back, liquidated at small profit.
- Hite told friend: "We work for a man who has just found himself in the middle of a mine field, and what he did was close his eyes and walk through it. He now thinks the proper technique is to close your eyes and go forward."
- Less than one year later: same man had to liquidate huge delta neutral spread — went through all of firm's capital.

### Jack Boyd's Wisdom
- Six-foot-four analyst. Threw charts on floor, jumped up on desk. "Look at it, it will tell you!"

### Final Rules
> "If you don't bet, you can't win. If you lose all your chips, you can't bet."

## Michael Steinhardt: The Concept of Variant Perception

### Track Record
- 30% compounded annual growth over twenty-one years (under 25% after 20% profit incentive fee)
- $1,000 invested in 1967 → over $93,000 by Spring 1988 (after fees). S&P basket: $6,400.
- Only two losing years in twenty-one, both under 2%.
- Market interest began at thirteen: father gifted 200 shares, watched ticker tape while friends played stickball. Graduated Wharton at nineteen in 1960.
- Founded Steinhardt, Fine and Berkowitz in 1967 (later Steinhardt Partners).

### Variant Perception — Core Philosophy
> "I try to develop perceptions that I believe are at variance with the general market view. I will play those variant perceptions until I feel they are no longer so."

- Distinction: traders go short as readily as long, focus on market direction. Investors stay 70%+ invested, focus on picking best stocks. No value judgment — different thematic focus.
- Willing to be contrarian — shorts stocks backed by institutional enthusiasm.
- Tends to short early, often starting with losses. Willing to get "in the way of danger" to make money.
- Does not use charts — finds them "completely unhelpful." No stop-loss orders, no breakouts or breakdowns.

### Variant Perception Examples

**Genentech short** (held year and a half):
- Lost money for months maintaining position.
- Variant perception: TPA would be minor drug in 1-2 years, supplanted by more effective, cheaper alternatives. Entire company based on this one drug.
- Stock at $27 in June 1988, down from high of $65 (by late November fell to $15).

**Tobacco stocks short**:
- If plaintiffs won case, stocks down a lot; if lost, wouldn't rise much (tobacco companies never lost case before). Asymmetric payoff.
- Would cover immediately once news out (variant perception collapsed).

### Position Management
- As long as fundamentals (as perceived) unchanged, will hang tough regardless of how much position goes against him.
- Reviews portfolio six times daily. Acts as "negative monitor" — questions positions when they don't behave as expected. Talks to people only when things "lousy."
- If can't explain market action, covers anyway.

### 1981-1982 Treasury Bond Trade
- Went long debt markets in spring 1981. Treasuries bottomed September 30, 1981. Rates moved against him first half year — painful given position size.
- Summer 1981: worst experience of business life. Thoughtful investors very unhappy with what he was doing. Never came close to liquidating.
- Reasoning: higher inflation rates → higher interest rates → slowing economy. With 14% available in long-term treasuries, stocks had to sell much lower to compete. Clear unidirectional message — timing the turn was only issue.
- Leveraged: at one point 3-4 times firm's capital in five-year maturities. Treasuries allow 98% financing depending on maturity — no real constraint like stock margin.

### October 1987
- Spring 1987: wrote letter to investors reducing exposure. Identified unique combination: substantial equity reduction + more liberal debt attitude. Banks comfortable lending, junk bond market good, corporate managers repurchasing shares.
- Created unusual upward bias. Question: what would change? Answer: recession. Government lacked flexibility to fight one.
- October 19: came in 80-90% net long, increased exposure during the day. Contrarian trade: enormous moves usually right to take opposite view.
- "Buying on any 300-, 400-, 500-point down day is standard contrarian response."
- Down over 20% for October. Reduced position throughout next two months.
- Underestimated impact of forces diminishing market stability: portfolio insurance, program trading, global asset allocation all exerted unidirectional impact.
- Elements of stability (individual investor, specialist system) greatly reduced in 1980s.

### Hedge Fund Concept
- Original A.W. Jones concept: money managers cannot forecast stock market trends. Therefore, net exposure should be minimized. Be long stocks you think will outperform and short those that won't. Profit regardless of market direction.
- Steinhardt moved beyond original concept: developed ability to take directional bets (the 1981-82 bond trade).
