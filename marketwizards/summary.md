# Market Wizards

**Author:** Jack D. Schwager
**Published:** 1989 (updated 2012 edition with new preface/afterword)
**Pages:** ~393

## One-Line Summary
Interviews with seventeen of America's top traders reveal that consistent market-beating returns are possible, but success depends far more on psychology, discipline, and risk control than on any particular analytical method.

## Key Takeaways
1. **There is no single path to success:** The traders use contradictory approaches — pure technical vs. pure fundamental, seconds-long scalping vs. months-long positions — yet all succeed. The answer lies in finding your own method, not copying someone else's.
2. **Risk management is everything:** Every wizard emphasizes cutting losses. Marcus caps at 5% per idea, Kovner at 1% per trade, Jones monitors daily equity. The traders who survived early catastrophic losses all credit strict risk control for their recoveries.
3. **Psychology trumps analysis:** Van Tharp's research shows top traders share five beliefs: money isn't important, losing is OK, trading is a game, mental rehearsal matters, and they've won before they start. Most losers fail from ego, not bad analysis.
4. **Patience separates winners from losers:** Weinstein's cheetah analogy — wait for the sick antelope. Rogers makes only 3-5 decisions per year. Dennis says 95% of profits come from 5% of trades.
5. **Markets change but human nature doesn't:** Trend-following became harder as more participants adopted it, but emotional patterns — fear, greed, hope — remain constant.

## The Traders
### Part I: Futures & Currencies
- **Michael Marcus** — $30K to $80M. Learned from Seykota. "Never bet more than 5% on one idea."
- **Bruce Kovner** — 87% annual return over ten years. "Undertrade, undertrade, undertrade."
- **Richard Dennis** — Proved trading can be taught (Turtle experiment). "Get your head out of the mixer."
- **Paul Tudor Jones** — 62% return in October 1987 crash month. "Focus on protecting what you have."
- **Gary Bielfeldt** — $1K to T-bond giant. Poker analogy for position sizing.
- **Ed Seykota** — 250,000% over 16 years. "Everybody gets what they want out of the market."
- **Larry Hite** — 30%+ systematic returns. "I don't trade for excitement; I trade to win."

### Part II: Stocks
- **Michael Steinhardt** — 30% compounded over 21 years. "Variant perception" philosophy.
- **William O'Neil** — CANSLIM system. 7% maximum loss rule.
- **David Ryan** — O'Neil protege. 50% win rate, profits from the few that double/triple.
- **Marty Schwartz** — Lost for a decade as analyst, then 781% in one year as technician.

### Part III: Mixed
- **Jim Rogers** — Quantum Fund co-founder. "The market is nearly always wrong."
- **Mark Weinstein** — 99% win rate. Only trades when everything lines up.

### Part IV: Floor
- **Brian Gelber** — Edge from reading other traders' emotions.
- **Tom Baldwin** — $25K to $30M+ in year one. Scalps $2B in T-bonds daily.
- **Tony Saliba** — "One-lot" options trader. Butterfly + explosion strategy.

### Part V: Psychology
- **Dr. Van K. Tharp** — Five impediments to trading success.

## Contrarian Ideas
- **The efficient market hypothesis is wrong:** October 1987's 29% single-day drop had a probability of 10^-160 under EMH. Track records like Seykota's 250,000% return are statistical impossibilities if markets are efficient.
- **Being right about direction isn't enough:** You can be perfectly correct about a market's direction and still lose everything if your timing, position size, or implementation is wrong.
- **Diversification is overrated:** Bielfeldt, O'Neil, and Marcus all argue for concentration in your best ideas rather than spreading across many mediocre ones.
- **Great traders are made, not born — but also born, not made:** Dennis proved trading can be taught (Turtles averaged 100%/year), yet multiple traders insist innate talent is essential. Both appear true.

## Relevance to Trading/Investing
This is the foundational text of the trading interview genre and directly relevant to anyone using technical analysis, trend-following, or systematic trading. The risk management principles — position sizing, stop-loss discipline, correlation awareness — are universal and timeless. The book's psychological insights apply to any endeavor requiring disciplined decision-making under uncertainty.
