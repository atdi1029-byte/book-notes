# Market Wizards — Compacted Notes (Second Half)

## Michael Steinhardt

### Hedge Fund Pioneer
- Coined modern hedge fund approach: not true hedging but flexible
  long/short with performance-based compensation
- **Steinhardt Partners** averaged about **40% net long** over
  twenty-one years, ranging from 15-20% net short to over
  100% net long
- Flexibility to shift market exposure is "exceptionally
  meaningful -- sometimes perhaps too meaningful"
- "No formula for success in this racket. Anyone thinking they
  can formulate one is deluding themselves. Changes too quickly --
  formula's own success carries weight of inevitable failure."

### Market Evolution (1960s-1980s)
- Pre-1975: "Taking candy from a baby." Institutional traders
  were kids from Brooklyn with little education.
- **Penn Central trade**: Bought 700,000 shares at 6-1/8 (stock
  in Chapter 11, last trade at 7). Seller didn't check the
  board. Sold 700,000 at 6-7/8. Made half million dollars in
  twelve seconds.
- Post-1975: Much more competition, market became
  institutionalized
- World shifted from long-term investing to short-term trading.
  1950s-60s heroes were long-term investors. Today's heroes:
  "wise guys talking about capitalism but practicing short-term
  trading."
- 1967: typical to see McDonald's earnings estimated to year
  2000. Belief in America and steady growth. Today stocks
  don't lend themselves to same secular analysis.
- **Goldsmith example**: in Goodyear seven months, made eight
  billion dollars (greenmail), left management

### Trading Philosophy
- Balance between **conviction** and **flexibility** -- you will
  be wrong a considerable number of times
- "Respect for the person on the other side of the trade -- ask
  'Why does he want to sell? What does he know that I don't?'"
- **Intellectual honesty** -- all great traders are seekers of
  truth
- Being contrarian requires **timing**, not just sentiment
  surveys. Extreme readings also occur during extended trends.
- Taking on large positions when perceiving major opportunities
  separates great from good traders.

> "The more things you bring to the table -- shorting, hedging,
> participation in bond markets, futures market trading -- the
> better off you are."

### Conviction Under Pressure
- Treasury note position (1981-1982): maintained large position
  through six-month interest rate climax despite complaining
  investors
- No absolute formulae -- markets always changing

### Advice for Laymen
- Professionals devote their lives to trading and are often on
  the opposite side of retail trades
- If T-bonds yield 9-10%, stock returns must justify the
  incremental risk
- **Don't underestimate the difficulty of the game**

## William O'Neil

### Background & Success
- Born Oklahoma during Great Depression, raised in Texas
- Started as stockbroker at **Hayden, Stone** (1958)
- Pyramided three back-to-back trades: short Korvette, long
  Chrysler, long Syntex -- turned **$5,000 into $200,000**
- 1964: Bought NYSE seat, formed William O'Neil and Co.
  (500+ institutional accounts, 28,000 subscribers to Daily
  Graphs)
- 1983: Launched **Investor's Daily** competing with Wall Street
  Journal (30,000 vs. 2 million press run). By 1988: 110,000.
- Averaged over **40% annual profit** over past ten years
- Famous market calls: two full-page WSJ ads heralding bull
  markets (1978, February 1982)

### The CANSLIM Formula

**Origins (1959)**: Studied Dreyfus fund managed by Jack Dreyfus,
which doubled competitors' results. Plotted every stock purchase
on charts. Discovery: **every single stock had been bought when
it went to a new high price.**

**C = Current Earnings**: Best stocks showed 70% average quarterly
EPS increase before major advance. "Absolutely no reason for a
stock to go up if current earnings are poor."

**A = Annual Earnings**: Prior five-year average compounded growth:
24%. Strong current + high average growth creates superb stocks.

**N = New**: 95% of greatest winners had new product/service,
industry change, or new management. "What seems too high usually
goes higher and what seems too low usually goes lower."

**S = Shares Outstanding**: 95% of best performers had less than
25 million shares. Median only 4.6 million. Institutional
investors handicap themselves restricting to large-cap.

**L = Leader**: 500 best-performing stocks (1953-1985) had average
relative strength of 87 before major increase. Pick leaders
(RS above 80), avoid laggards.

**I = Institutional Sponsorship**: Leading stocks have backing, but
excessive sponsorship undesirable. "By the time almost all
institutions own a stock, it is probably too late to buy."

**M = Market Direction**: Three out of four stocks follow averages.

At any given time, **less than 2% of stocks fit CANSLIM** --
deliberately restrictive. "If you were recruiting a baseball
team, would you pick .200 hitters or try for .300 hitters?"

### Buying and Selling Discipline

**Buy at breakout, not during base**: At new high, stock usually
won't drop more than 7%. Within base, can fluctuate 10-15%.

**Don't buy extended stocks**: If stock already 10%+ beyond most
recent price base, vulnerable to sharp correction.

**Identifying market tops**: (1) Average makes new high on low
volume. (2) Volume surges for days with no upside progress.
Also: leading stocks break down; 2-3 Fed rate increases;
advance/decline line lags averages.

**Short selling**: Only made significant profits shorting in 2 of
last 9 bear markets. Never short just because price looks high.
Exit after 6-7% loss.

### Risk Control

> "My philosophy is that all stocks are bad. There are no good
> stocks unless they go up in price."

- **Maximum 7% loss rule** -- automatic, no hesitation
- "Letting losses run is the most serious mistake made by most
  investors."
- **Turkey trap** (Fred C. Kelly): Man rigs trap, catches 12
  turkeys. One escapes. Waits for it to return. More escape.
  Keeps waiting. Ends empty-handed. "Stop counting turkeys."
- Jesse Livermore: "It is never thinking that makes big money,
  it's the sitting."

### Conventional Wisdom Critique

**P/E Ratios**: Very low correlation with best performers. Bought
Northrop at 4x earnings, watched it decline to 2x. Meanwhile
someone complained Xerox overpriced at 50x -- it went to $1,300.

**Dividends**: No correlation with performance. 4% dividend with
25% stock decline = 21% net loss.

**Diversification**: "A hedge for ignorance." Recommended: $5K =
1-2 stocks; $100K+ = 6-7 stocks.

**Market efficiency**: "Neither efficient nor random. Not efficient
because too many poorly conceived opinions; not random because
strong investor emotions create trends."

**80% of brokerage research written on wrong companies** -- each
analyst must meet quota even on non-leading groups.

### Mutual Funds
- Surprisingly endorses as "absolutely outstanding way to invest"
- Key: **sit and don't think**. Hold 15+ years.
- Individual stocks: strict 7% stop-loss. Mutual funds: sit
  through bear markets. Most people do the opposite.

### O'Neil's 18 Common Mistakes (highlights)
- Buying on the way down / averaging down
- Buying cheap low-priced stocks (think in dollars, not shares)
- Buying on tips/rumors instead of own knowledge
- Afraid to buy at new high ground (98% afraid)
- Holding losses when small, cashing small profits
- Using limit orders instead of market orders
- Influenced by non-crucial factors (splits, dividends, news)

## David Ryan

### Stock Selection Process
- O'Neil protege who adapted CANSLIM with his own modifications
- Screens 7,000 stocks weekly -> reviews ~4,000 charts ->
  applies fundamental filters -> narrows to ~7 finalists
- Requirements: EPS rank 80+ (preferably 90+), relative strength
  80+, shares outstanding under 30M, industry in top 50 of 200
- Institutional ownership: 1-20% ideal (powers stock but not
  too much)
- "Something new" attracting people: Reebok shoes, Compaq
  portable computer, Microsoft software
- Prefers stocks that already doubled: "if situation is that
  good, a doubling may just be the beginning"

### Win Rate & Risk Management
- **50/50 win rate** -- cuts losers quickly (max 7%, usually
  faster)
- Makes money on "few stocks a year that double and triple"
- Big winners: 6-12 months; Losers: less than 2 weeks
- No price objective set in advance -- waits for stock to run
  up, build base, then break down

### Ryan's P/E Addition to CANSLIM
- O'Neil says P/E doesn't matter; Ryan disagrees
- Target: **P/E between 1x and 2x the S&P 500 P/E**
- Greatest winning recommendations started under 30x earnings

### Key Insights

> "If you really think the stock is going to make a big move,
> there is no reason to haggle over an eighth of a point."

- Missed Textone trying to save a quarter -- stock went from
  $15 to $45
- **Volume must double** on breakout for valid signal
- Stocks should be at a profit the first day you buy them
- "The same types of stocks work time after time. A 1960
  winner lines up with a 1980 winner -- exact same
  characteristics."

### Trader's Diary
- Annotates every purchase on charts, keeps in binder system
- Cements winning characteristics, avoids repeating mistakes
- 1983-1984 slump: let success go to his head, broke cardinal
  rule of never buying overextended stocks

### 1987 Crash Signals
- Advance/decline topped Q1 1987, well before August peak
- After Dow came off high at 2,746: feeble low-volume rally,
  then 90-point loss. Decided to exit.
- Late August discount rate increase "really stabbed the market"

> "It is like a giant treasure hunt. Somewhere in here there is
> going to be a big winner, and I am trying to find it."

## Marty Schwartz -- Champion Trader

### The Transformation
- **Spent a decade losing money** as securities analyst. Well
  paid, always broke from market losses.
- Changed from fundamental to technical analysis. "I used
  fundamentals for nine years and got rich as a technician."
- By mid-1979, ran **$5,000 up to $140,000** in two years

### The Psychological Shift

> "When I was able to separate my ego needs from making money.
> When I was able to accept being wrong. Before, admitting I was
> wrong was more upsetting than losing the money. I used to try
> to will things to happen."

### Trading Championships
- Nine of ten four-month championships: made more money than
  all other contestants combined
- **Average return: 210% (nonannualized)**
- Single one-year contest: **781% return**
- Never lost more than **3% of equity** on month-end basis

### Background: Amherst, Marines, Wall Street
- Grew up New Haven, modest means. Age seven: snow shoveling
  for $10.
- **Amherst College**: First time had to struggle. "When the
  light bulb clicked on, it was like looking at a magnificent
  painting."
- **U.S. Marine Corps**: Platoon sergeant drew Star of David on
  his forehead with magic marker. Only Jewish person in OCS.
  "The hardest thing was scrubbing the magic marker off."
  Persevered. "The Marines strengthened my body. Combined with
  Amherst, convinced me I could do almost anything."

### Wall Street Disasters
- **Leaked bearish report**: Wrote bearish hospital management
  report. Drunk analyst leaked it on a plane. Stocks plummeted.
  Schwartz hauled before NYSE hearing for six hours. Totally
  exonerated but "rotten, bitter experience."
- Lost job in bear market, out four months. "I was tainted.
  'Oh, aren't you the guy who wrote that report?'"
- Found "real lunatic" with commodity computer programs. Lost
  most of remaining capital.
- **Edwards & Hanly**: Met Bob Zoellner, "great, great trader"
  who single-handedly kept firm afloat in 1974 by shorting.
  Warning sign: "When the head of research, who never went out
  to lunch, began going out regularly, I started interviewing
  for another job." Firm went bankrupt fall 1975.

### Personal Turning Point (1976)
- Met wife-to-be: "She made me realize that my life was not a
  dress rehearsal; it was the real thing and I had been
  screwing it up."
- Despite good salaries, still almost broke from market losses.
- By mid-1978, securities analyst for eight years, intolerable.
  "I decided it was now time to be successful."

### Going Independent (1979)
- Got quote machine in office at E.F. Hutton (only analyst
  with one). Closed door to watch market.
- Bob Zoellner taught market analysis: "When market gets good
  news and goes down, it means market is very weak."
- Found **Terry Laundry** and his **Magic T Forecast** (MIT
  engineer): market spends same time going up as down, only
  amplitude differs. Used oscillator highs to measure timing.
- Synthesized multiple indicators for lower-risk entry points.
  "98% of moves stop at 2 standard deviations. I'll take that
  bet any day."

### October 19, 1987 Crash
- Treasury Secretary James Baker bludgeoning Germans about
  interest rates over the weekend
- Marty Zweig on "Wall Street Week" predicted another 500-point
  risk based on negative monetary indicators
- S&P high Monday: 269; liquidated long at 267-1/2. Lost
  $315,000 on 40 contracts. Proud of executing the exit.

> "One of the most suicidal things you can do in trading is to
> keep adding to a losing position."

- By 1:30 PM with Dow down 275: went to safe deposit box for
  gold, withdrew cash from banks, bought T-bills
- Marine training: "Never freeze when under attack" -- go
  forward or backward, don't sit still
- **Thursday**: Called S&P pit -- "Decembers at 230, offered at
  220, trading at 200." Made $250,000 on 12 contracts.
  "Cover!" One of most memorable trades.
- 1987 was his most profitable year

### Most Dramatic Loss: November 1982
- **$600,000 loss in one day**. Election Day, Republicans did
  better than expected, market up 43 points. Was short, S&P
  locked at limit against him.
- Wife next day: "Get smaller, get smaller" every ten minutes
- Recovery: shrank to 1/5 or 1/10 of normal. "Black ink, black
  ink." Not about how much -- about rhythm and confidence.
- Lost only $57,000 rest of November after $600,000 hit

### Trading Rules
1. Check charts and moving averages before any position
2. Look for divergences
3. Before position: "Do I really want to have this position?"
4. After successful period, take a day off -- biggest losses
   follow largest profits
5. Bottom fishing = expensive gambling
6. Know your "uncle point" before entering any trade
7. When T-bonds and T-bills diverge, have no position until
   one confirms direction
8. Work, work, and more work

### Key Principles
- "Money management, money management, money management"
- Over 90% of months profitable. Biggest career drawdown: 3%.
- "January 1, I'm poor" -- starts every year fresh
- "You can always put the trade back on, but if you go flat,
  you see things differently."
- Hired four people -- none lasted. "You can't teach them your
  stomach."

### Why Most Traders Lose

> "Because they would rather lose money than admit they're
> wrong. The ultimate rationalization: 'I'll get out when I'm
> even.'"

### Program Trading Critique
- "I hate it" -- killed natural ebb and flow
- Market closes near high/low of day 20% of time --
  "mathematically impossible by chance"
- Chinese wall problem: agency and principal traders on same
  floor

> "I am trying to earn infinity when I trade."

## Jim Rogers

### Early Disaster (1970)
- Bought puts, tripled money by May -- thought he was "the next
  Bernard Baruch"
- Went short to "make money faster" -- **completely wiped out**
  two months later
- Shorted Memorex at 48 (dead right), covered at 72 after
  getting destroyed. Stock eventually went to 2.
- "The market is going to go higher than I think it can and
  lower than I think it will. I had a tendency to think that if
  I knew something, everybody knew it."

### The Quantum Fund (1973-1980)
- Started by **George Soros** (senior) and Rogers (junior) --
  one senior, one junior, one secretary
- Traded stocks, bonds, currencies, commodities -- long and
  short -- all over the world
- Division of labor: Soros was the trader, Rogers the analyst
- "Leveraged to the hilt" -- when buying something new, sold
  whatever appeared least attractive
- If they disagreed: usually did nothing. "Consensus investing
  is a disaster, but we almost always came together."
- No losing year after September 1970

### Retirement and Teaching
- 1980: "Cashed in my chips and retired." Prefers "unemployed."
- Teaching at Columbia: deal with Dean -- teach for free in
  return for **lifetime access to gym**
- "Bulls and Bears" course: each student must find a major
  historical market move and tell what you could have known
- Studied hundreds, possibly thousands, of bull and bear
  markets. Finds anomalous years in long-term charts, asks
  "Why did that happen?"

### Macro Views (1988)
- Budget deficit causes trade deficit. Dollar will come under
  extreme pressure.
- Bond market collapses sooner or later -- could repeat British
  experience (bonds fell ~70%)
- Japan stock market will have major collapse. **Australia sold
  1.5-acre plot in Tokyo for $450 million** (bought for $250K
  twenty-five years ago) -- tulipmania parallel.
- Oil will go "way down" -- guarantees under $12 (was ~$16)
- Gold: not the great inflation hedge of 1990s. Supply up every
  year since 1980.

> "Generals always fight the last war. Portfolio managers always
> invest in the last bull market. The idea that gold has always
> been the great store of value is absurd."

- Sugar subsidies: U.S. spends $5 billion/year supporting sugar
  at 22 cents when world price is 8 cents. Better to give every
  grower $100K/year, a condo, and a Porsche to quit.
- American troops in Europe: costs $150 billion/year. 43 years
  as army of occupation. GAO says not enough bullets for a
  30-day war.

### August 1971: Dramatic Loss
- Long Japan, short America. Nixon took U.S. off gold standard
  while Rogers was on a motorcycle.
- Japanese market down 20%, U.S. up. Losing heavily on both.
- "You can't liquidate at a time like that."
- Stayed with positions. "The Nixon announcement was just
  another step in the dissolution of the Bretton Woods
  Agreement."

### Key Trading Principles
- "You always invest **against the central banks**."
- "The biggest public fallacy: that the market is always right.
  The market is nearly always wrong."
- "Never follow conventional wisdom in the market."
- Only makes 3-5 decisions a year and stays with them
- On technical analysis: "I haven't met a rich technician."
  Uses charts only for knowledge, not predictively. "Don't tell
  me about reversals. I don't want to know."
- "I don't play. I just don't play." Waits until all pieces
  fit. "If scenarios don't mesh, I do nothing."

### Market Hysteria Cycle
- Market very low -> some buy because undervalued -> more buy
  because fundamentally sound -> people buy because "it's been
  the thing to do" -> **magical stage: hysterical buying** --
  prices exceed rational value. Same process in reverse.
- 1968: Major firms published "shortage of stocks" thesis. 1987:
  heard it all over again. "At bottom of bear market there is
  going to be a shortage of money -- I assure you."

> "Good investing is really just common sense. But it is
> astonishing how few people have common sense."

### Rogers' Seven Trading Principles
1. **Buy value** -- if you buy value, won't lose much even wrong
2. **Wait for a catalyst** -- don't tie up capital in dead
   markets
3. **Sell hysteria** -- verify market is wrong, confirm you're
   right, hold tight (gold example: selling at $675, staying
   short through surge to $875, holding through collapse)
4. **Be very selective** -- never trade for trading's sake
5. **Be flexible** -- willing to buy or sell anything
6. **Never follow conventional wisdom**
7. **Know when to hold vs. liquidate** -- if analysis flawed,
   first loss is best loss; if original analysis valid, sit out
   the hysteria

## Mark Weinstein -- High-Percentage Trader

### Background
- Former real estate broker, charts looked like "TV test
  patterns" to him
- First trade (1972): $8,400 in commodities, down to $7,800
  in three days on corn. Get-rich-quick scheme.
- Recovery: worked seven days/week renting apartments, saved
  $24,000
- Studied gold market, learned charting, overbought/oversold
- Studio apartment with charts plastered on every wall. Slept
  with trading, dreamt about it. Trading 24 hours a day.
- Not motivated by money -- hooked on the game

### The Soybean Disaster
- Had nearly **$1.5 million** in trading account
- Saw castle in south of France for $350,000, with a moat
- **Critical mistake**: Came back wanting to make $350,000 for
  the castle immediately. Target determined by material desire,
  not market analysis.
- Massive long soybean position. Up 25% day one.
- Market collapsed, locked **limit-down five days in a row**
- Lost over **$600,000** -- about $125,000/day (average salary
  was $15,000/year)
- Fifth day: sitting in park crying on a stranger's lap,
  practically psychotic
- Other traders "overconsoled him then laughed behind his back"
- Ripped every chart off walls, tore up everything related to
  commodities
- "What was really un-American was not being able to get out of
  a position"

### The Options Mentor
- Friend told him to buy Teledyne calls right before expiration.
  Calls collapsed -- lost $40,000.
- Friend revealed he'd done exact opposite trade in another
  account -- Weinstein's money was safe.
- Not a joke: teaching him never to place blind trust in
  anyone. "**Self-reliance** in being a good trader."
- Friend eventually watched Weinstein trade, embraced him:
  "You finally made it." Said technical analysis was a crutch:
  "You are trading that way because of experience."
- They separated over style conflict: friend took big losses
  knowing big wins would compensate. Weinstein preferred
  small profits and eradicating losers.

### The Soybean Revenge
- For years, eyes would edge to soybean chart, then quickly
  flip page. Like a "**Sicilian whose wife had been murdered
  ten years earlier**" -- waiting for the perfect moment.
- When soybeans hit $4.75, watched peripherally for years.
  Bought at **$6.18** before 1988 drought. Got out at **$7.25**
  and **$9.92** (high was $10.46).
- "Made my money back in spades. It was a total catharsis."

### Phenomenal Win Rate
- **No losing months since 1980**, no losing weeks
- 17 losers out of thousands of trades over two years (9 from
  quote machine being down)
- Win ratio near **99%**
- Options contest: 47 CBOE floor traders, $100K accounts.
  Turned **$100,000 into $900,000** in three months, no
  pyramiding, 100% winning trades.
- Verification: Leigh Stevens confirmed -- of ~100 trades
  witnessed, could remember only one loser

### How He Does It (Schwager's Analysis)
- Custom-designed computer systems monitoring market momentum
- Uses own values for technical indicators, frequently adjusts
- Combines cycles, Fibonacci retracements, Elliott Wave
- Only trades when nearly everything lines up and timing feels
  virtually perfect
- **Virtually all trades profitable within 20 minutes of entry**
- Plays for quick profits -- covers within hours or minutes
- Trades markets in rotation, shifting to lowest-risk profit
  potential

### Trading Philosophy

> "The greatest traders are the ones who are most afraid of the
> markets."

**Cheetah analogy** (position trading): Although fastest animal,
hides in bush for a week. Waits for baby antelope -- preferably
sick or lame. Only attacks when no chance of losing prey. "That
is the epitome of professional trading."

**Sparrow analogy** (day trading): Takes little pieces, flies
away, comes back. "You will never be able to shoot a sparrow."
Trades mid-range where momentum is greatest.

**Stock vs. commodity markets**: Stocks rarely trend because
institutions scale in/out. Many good commodity traders lose
every time they enter the stock market. Weinstein wins in both
because he lets the market tell him where it's going.

**Market misconceptions**: JFK assassination -- market broke
sharply, then rebounded to new highs. "A market poised to move
higher won't reverse because of news -- even dramatic news."
Also: "profit taking" reports. "I think it would be wonderful if
everybody was always taking profits." Truth: most people lose
money. Markets go down on loss taking.

### Rules
1. Always do your homework
2. Don't be arrogant -- best traders are most humble
3. Understand your limitations
4. Think against the herd
5. Don't trade until opportunity presents itself
6. Strategy must be flexible when environment changes
7. Don't get complacent after profits

> "You have to learn how to lose; it is more important than
> learning how to win."

## Brian Gelber

### Career Path
- Graduated 1976, started at boiler room commodity broker in
  Salt Lake City. Quit after recognizing fraud.
- Joined **Thomson McKinnon** for $800/month cold calling
- September 1977: moved to Chicago, became broker on T-bond
  floor at age 25
- Visited eight firms in New York, seven opened accounts --
  "right place, right time"
- Started trading own account 1979

### Key Lessons
- 1979 mistake: listened to big customers (CitiBank, Citicorp)
  saying rates couldn't go higher -- buying all the way down
- "An opinion isn't worth that much. I became a reactive trader
  as opposed to an opinionated trader."
- "You can't be a great broker and a great trader. It probably
  took years off my life."
- Got trapped ~12 times when news broke and all customer orders
  went against his position -- cost ~$500,000 total

### Most Influential Books
- **Technical Analysis of Stock Trends** (Edwards & Magee)
- **Reminiscences of a Stock Operator** (Lefevre) -- "read at
  least a dozen times"

### Trading as Natural Talent
- "Either you are born to be a good trader or you are not."
- Cycle: four months hot, two months cold, six months
  break-even
- "You never sleep, because you constantly think about trading.
  It is an all-consuming thing."
- Out of 100 people on the floor, maybe 5 make at least
  $1 million within five years. Half lose everything.

### Reading Other Traders (Key Edge)
- Talks to ~25 traders daily. "The reason I have been so
  consistent is that I'm a great listener."
- If 20 of 25 nervous about market going higher (implicitly
  short), trade long side
- "I'm not picky about how I make my money. It doesn't matter
  if my opinion is right or wrong."
- Knows a cold trader. Last night wanted to be short. Gelber
  knew he'd be long. "As long as he is cold, he is cold."

### Most Dramatic Trade
- 1986: Japanese cornered long bond. Sold 1,100 contracts above
  91.00. Market reversed, soared to 92.00. **Down $1 million in
  less than five minutes.** "I had forgotten markets could run
  away like that."
- Ended losing only $400,000 for day after patient management
- Japanese buying style: "they buy them all" -- priority is
  market share, not yield. "When they want to buy, their
  priority is market share." One trader bought every long bond
  on screen, then 15 minutes later asked why basis was going
  out. "You just bought every bond available."

### Key Principles
- "When you don't care, you do well. When you try too hard,
  you don't."
- **"Never add to a loser."**
- "What the average trader does wrong: he overtrades and begs
  for tips."
- On losing streaks: instinctively trade smaller, take a break
- On liquidating good positions during bad streaks:
  "Absolutely. They are bound to turn against you too."
- "Successful trading is trying to avoid losses, but not being
  afraid of them."
- "I'm not afraid to lose. When you start being afraid, you're
  finished."

## Tom Baldwin

### Entry Story
- 1982: No trading experience. Left product manager job at
  meat-packing firm to lease seat on Chicago Board of Trade.
  Stake: **only $25,000**. Wife was pregnant.
- **Turned profit from the beginning. Millionaire before first
  year was up.** $30 million appears conservative estimate.
- Typical day: trades 20,000+ contracts (equivalent of $2
  billion face value T-bonds)

### Trading Style
- "One lot at a time. I always had an opinion." Stood there six
  hours/day. Same scenarios developed over and over.
- Most ever down: 19 ticks. "Pretty much started out profitably"
- "I worked hard. I stood there six hours a day, all day,
  every day."
- "You don't need any education at all to do it. The smarter
  you are, the dumber you are."
- Average 4-tick profits on big positions. Holding time: minutes
  or seconds -- "less risk. The object is always: minimize risk."
- Uses bar charts covering past six months. No intraday charts.
- Less than 20% of floor traders still around after five years.
  Only 1% reach couple-million level.

### What Separates the 1%
- Hard work, perseverance, love of the business
- **Total disregard for money** -- trade for the challenge
- Can't translate losses into real terms
- "Almost no fear -- winners have less fear than losers"
- Losers don't work hard enough, expenses too high, think every
  trade is 50/50, don't concentrate, don't watch the factors

### Loss Management
- "I have patience. If I know it is a losing trade, I wait for
  the optimum time to bail out."
- Never abandon losing trade in panic, but maintain risk control
- Choose your time -- wait for optimal exit, can turn five-yard
  loss into two-yard loss

### October 1987 Crash
- Built butterfly spread + explosion position (long OTM puts
  and calls in back months)
- Could feel it in wild gyrations by late September

- **Butterflies**: limited risk, profit from time decay if
  market doesn't move widely
- **Explosion positions**: limited risk, open-ended potential

- Front month: butterfly. Back month: explosion. Scalping pays
  for time decay.

## Dr. Van K. Tharp -- Psychology of Trading

### Background
- Research psychologist, Ph.D. Oklahoma (1975)
- Developed **Investment Psychology Inventory** (1982)
- Lost money trading options himself -- concluded losses had
  something to do with him personally

### Key Findings

**Three success clusters**: psychological, management/discipline,
decision-making

**Profile of losing trader**: Highly stressed, negative outlook,
blames others, no rules, crowd follower, impatient

**Top traders' five core beliefs**:
1. Money is NOT important
2. It is OK to lose in the markets
3. Trading is a game
4. Mental rehearsal is important for success
5. They've won the game before they start

"Most people approach trading to make a lot of money -- primary
reason they lose."

### Five Primary Impediments

**1. Poor Risk Attitudes**: Most people are risk-aversive with
profits and risk-seeking with losses -- the opposite of what's
required. They cut profits short and let losses run.

**2. Stress**: Fight/flight narrows focus, reverts to beginner
behavior, makes people crowd followers. Excess energy goes into
actively resisting closing losing position.

**3. Internal Conflict**: Different "parts" with competing
intentions. Floor trader with successful alcoholic father could
make $75,000/year but no more. After negotiation exercise: made
$650,000 in two months.

**4. Subconscious Desire to Lose**: Half of traders Tharp works
with have this problem.

**5. Emotional Control**: Control posture, breathing, muscle
tension to change emotional state.

### Mechanical vs. Intuitive Super-Traders
- Mechanical: constructs visual models, won't trust until
  computerized, computer does everything
- Intuitive: trades from expectations converted to feelings,
  believes computerizing is waste of time
- Both developed models, did extensive testing, share common
  beliefs

### On Commitment

> "The moment one definitely commits oneself, then Providence
> moves too." -- W.N. Murray

- If really committed: events seem to occur to help you --
  even big losses can be those events
- Justified confidence comes from constellation of beliefs +
  extensive testing + full commitment

### Modeling Trading Excellence
- Army modeled two best sharpshooters: reduced training from
  4 to 2 days, increased qualification from 80% to 100%
- Best shooter: rehearse entire 1,000-round match night before;
  important to hit center of bull's eye each shot (no extra
  points). Second best: only hit bull's eye.
- Recruits' beliefs: "Guns are evil," "Miss target = gun is
  misaligned." Just from beliefs alone, can understand the gap.

### Mental Strategies
- Same trading signal, two different thought sequences produce
  opposite actions
- Strategy 1: See signal -> think what might go wrong -> feel
  bad -> don't trade
- Strategy 2: See signal -> feel good about it -> trade
- "If trading a system, you need a simple strategy"

### Case Examples
- Trader unable to trade for over a year: ten-minute exercise
  to integrate conflicting parts. Two weeks later: trading.
- Reluctant follow-up client: simple exercise, totally changed.
  Two months later: $650,000 in trading profits.
- Man in late 40s living with mother: wouldn't commit to adult
  responsibilities. Untreatable without lifestyle change.

## Schwager's Final Reflections (2010 Postscript)

### Core Lessons

**Good vs. bad trades**: A losing trade adhering to profitable
strategy is still a good trade. A winning trade following a
process that loses money if repeated is still bad.

**Implementation matters as much as direction**: After March 2000
NASDAQ bubble, didn't short NASDAQ (40% rebound would stop him
out). Went long bonds instead -- "much easier way to trade the
same idea."

**You don't get paid for being right**: Many traders fail not
from bad trades but from trades they don't make when right.
Often requires doing trade that feels uncomfortable.

**Position size more important than entry point**: If size too
large, more likely to exit good trade on meaningless move.
"Trading size needs kept small enough so fear doesn't become
the prevailing instinct."

**Flexibility is critical**: "Really good traders can change
their mind in an instant. 'This market is going higher. No,
it's definitely going down.' If you can't do that, you will get
caught and wiped out."

**The market doesn't care where you entered**: Waiting to "get
out even" is ego protection. "The need to not be wrong is the
very reason most people lose money."

**Annual targets counterproductive**: Lead to trading too small
when opportunities favorable, too large when absent.

**No single path**: Approaches among Market Wizards not merely
different but sometimes mirror opposites. "The answer lies not
in copying someone else's method, but in finding your own."

> "Music is the space between the notes." -- Debussy.
> Analogously, times out of market can be critical.

**Worst drawdowns come after best performance**: Complacency,
failure to consider worst case, high exposure all converge.

### Common Denominators Among Market Wizards
1. Driving desire to succeed -- many overcame significant
   obstacles
2. Confidence they could continue winning long-term
3. Each found a methodology that worked and stayed true to it
   -- **discipline** most frequently mentioned word
4. Take trading very seriously -- most devote substantial
   waking hours to analysis
5. Rigid risk control
6. Patience to wait for right opportunity
7. Act independent of the crowd
8. All understand losing is part of the game
9. They all love what they are doing
