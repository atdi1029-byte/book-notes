How to Use This Guide

This is a companion to the actual textbook. The idea is simple: you read a chapter in the real book, then come here to make sure you actually understand what just happened.

The textbook is written for finance professionals. This guide is written for you — someone who's smart but doesn't live and breathe bond markets every day. Every concept gets explained in plain language with analogies and examples. No jargon without a clear "here's what that actually means."

Think of the textbook as the deep end of the pool. This guide is the friend standing on the side saying "okay here's what's actually going on down there."

Part I: The Basics

Chapter 1: What Is a Bond?

The Big Picture

A bond is basically an IOU. When a company or government needs money, they can either sell ownership (stocks) or borrow it (bonds). A bond says: "Give me $1,000 today, and I'll pay you interest every six months, then give you your $1,000 back on a specific date."

The global bond market is worth about $114 trillion — that's way bigger than the stock market. The U.S. alone is about $46 trillion of that. When people talk about "the markets," they usually mean stocks. But bonds are where the real money lives.

The Key Terms (In English)

Par value (also called face value): Usually $1,000. This is what you get back at the end. Think of it as the original loan amount.

Coupon: The interest payment. If a bond has a "5% coupon," you get $50 a year (5% of $1,000), usually split into two $25 payments every six months.

Maturity: When you get your money back. Short-term is under 5 years, intermediate is 5-12, long-term is over 12. Some bonds called "perpetuals" never mature — they just pay interest forever.

Coupon types: Not all bonds pay the same way. Fixed-rate pays the same amount every time. Floating-rate changes based on market rates (like an adjustable mortgage). Zero-coupon pays nothing until maturity but you buy it at a big discount — you might pay $600 for a bond that pays you $1,000 in 10 years. The difference IS your interest.

Embedded Options (Bonus Features)

Some bonds have built-in features that give either the buyer or the seller special rights:

Callable: The issuer can pay you back early. Think of it like a mortgage — the homeowner (issuer) can refinance and pay off the old loan whenever they want. This is bad for you as the bondholder because they'll only do this when rates drop, meaning you get your money back right when it's hardest to find a new investment that pays as well.

Putable: You can force the issuer to buy the bond back early. This is the opposite — it protects you. If rates go up and your bond is paying too little, you can hand it back and invest at higher rates.

Convertible: You can trade your bond for shares of the company's stock. It's like having a bond with a lottery ticket attached — if the stock takes off, you can convert and participate in the upside.

The Nine Risks

Every bond carries risks. Here are all nine, explained simply:

1. Interest-rate risk: The biggest one. When interest rates go up, your bond's price goes down. Why? Because new bonds are paying higher rates, so nobody wants your old lower-rate bond unless it's cheaper.

2. Call/prepayment risk: The issuer pays you back early, right when rates are low and you can't reinvest as well.

3. Yield curve risk: Different maturities can move by different amounts. Your 5-year bonds might be fine while your 30-year bonds get crushed.

4. Reinvestment risk: When you get your coupon payments, you need to reinvest them somewhere. If rates have dropped, you're reinvesting at lower rates than you expected.

5. Credit risk: The issuer might not pay you back. This breaks into three parts: default risk (they literally don't pay), spread risk (the market starts worrying they might not pay, so your bond's price drops even though nothing happened yet), and downgrade risk (a rating agency lowers their grade).

6. Liquidity risk: You can't sell your bond quickly without taking a big price hit. Many bonds rarely trade at all.

7. Exchange-rate risk: If you buy a bond in euros and the euro drops against the dollar, you lose money on the currency even if the bond itself is fine.

8. Volatility risk: If you own a bond with embedded options (callable, etc.), changes in expected market volatility affect how much those options are worth, which changes your bond's price.

9. "Risk risk": Not knowing what the risks actually are. "We didn't know this could happen" is what everyone says after every financial crisis.

Green Bonds

A newer development: bonds where the money raised must be used for environmental projects. Over $500 billion have been issued. There are also "carbon policy performance bonds" where the interest rate itself goes up or down based on whether the company meets its emissions targets — basically using the bond market to force companies to reduce pollution. The idea is that bond investors can have more impact on climate than stock investors because they directly control a company's borrowing costs.

Chapter 2: How Bond Prices Work

The One Rule That Governs Everything

A bond's price = the present value of all the money it will pay you in the future. That's it. Every single thing in bond pricing flows from this one idea.

Think about it: would you rather have $100 today or $100 a year from now? Today, obviously. Money now is worth more than money later. So when a bond promises to pay you $50 every six months for 10 years and then $1,000 at the end, you need to figure out what all those future payments are worth TODAY. You do that by "discounting" each payment back to the present.

The Price-Yield Seesaw

Here's the most important relationship in all of bond investing: when yields go up, prices go down. When yields go down, prices go up. They move in opposite directions, always. Think of a seesaw.

Why? Let's say you own a bond paying 4%. Then the market starts offering 5%. Nobody wants your 4% bond at full price anymore. You'd have to sell it at a discount to make it attractive. The discount is exactly enough to make the effective yield to the buyer equal to 5%.

The reverse works too: if rates drop to 3%, your 4% bond is now a hot commodity. People will pay MORE than $1,000 for it because it pays better than what's currently available.

Par, Premium, and Discount

Par: When the coupon rate equals the market yield, the bond trades at exactly $1,000 (face value). Everything's balanced.

Premium: When your coupon is higher than market rates, people pay MORE than $1,000. The bond's price is above par.

Discount: When your coupon is lower than market rates, the bond trades BELOW $1,000. But here's a cool thing: as the bond gets closer to maturity, it drifts toward $1,000 regardless. This is called "pull to par" — premium bonds slowly fall to par, discount bonds slowly rise to par.

The Convexity Bonus

The price-yield relationship isn't a straight line — it's curved. This curve is called convexity, and it's actually a gift to bondholders.

Here's why: if yields drop by 1%, your bond price goes up by, say, 10%. But if yields go up by the same 1%, your price only drops by, say, 8%. The gain from falling rates is BIGGER than the loss from rising rates. That's positive convexity, and it's like a built-in bonus that makes bonds less risky than they seem.

Clean Price vs. Dirty Price

When you see a bond price quoted, it's the "clean" price — it doesn't include the interest that has built up since the last coupon payment. But when you actually buy the bond, you pay the "dirty" price — clean price plus that accrued interest. Think of it like buying a house mid-month: you settle up the property taxes for the partial month.

Floating-Rate and Inverse Floaters

A floating-rate bond adjusts its coupon based on a reference rate (like the federal funds rate). So if rates go up, your coupon goes up too. This means the bond's price stays close to par — you don't get the seesaw effect because the coupon adjusts automatically.

An inverse floater does the opposite: its coupon goes DOWN when rates go UP. So if you think rates are going to fall, an inverse floater is a leveraged bet on that happening. These can have insanely high sensitivity to rate changes — the textbook gives an example where a bond with 30-year underlying mortgages has a "duration" (sensitivity measure) of 40, which should be impossible if duration meant time. It doesn't — duration measures price sensitivity, not time.

Chapter 3: Measuring What You Actually Earn

The Problem with Simple Yield Measures

There are several ways to measure how much money a bond makes you, and most of them are misleading. Here's the hierarchy, from worst to best:

Current yield: Annual coupon divided by price. A $50 coupon on a $900 bond = 5.56% current yield. Simple, but it completely ignores the fact that you paid $900 and will get $1,000 back at maturity. That $100 gain is part of your return!

Yield to maturity (YTM): This is the most commonly quoted number. It's the rate that makes the present value of all future payments equal to today's price. Sounds perfect, right? Except it assumes two things that are almost never true: (1) you hold the bond until maturity, and (2) you reinvest every coupon payment at the same YTM rate. In the real world, rates change constantly.

Yield to call, yield to worst: If a bond can be called (bought back early), you might want to know your yield assuming that happens. "Yield to worst" is the MINIMUM yield across all possible scenarios (all call dates, maturity) — it's the most conservative number.

The Hidden Monster: Reinvestment Risk

Here's something most people don't realize: for a typical long-term bond, almost HALF of your total return comes from reinvesting the coupon payments. If you own a 15-year 7% bond and you expected to reinvest coupons at 10%, but rates dropped to 5%, your actual return is way lower than the YTM suggested.

The only bonds with zero reinvestment risk are zero-coupon bonds — they pay nothing until maturity, so there's nothing to reinvest. That's why their YTM is the only one that's actually guaranteed (assuming no default).

Total Return: The Right Way

The textbook argues strongly that "total return" (also called horizon analysis) is the only honest measure. Here's how it works in five steps:

1. Calculate all the coupon payments you'll get over your holding period, PLUS the interest you'll earn by reinvesting those coupons at a realistic rate.
2. Estimate what your bond will be worth when you sell it (based on where you think rates will be).
3. Add those two numbers together = total future dollars.
4. Compare total future dollars to what you paid.
5. Convert to an annualized return.

Critics say this requires "too many assumptions." But YTM also makes assumptions — it just hides them. Total return makes the assumptions explicit and lets you run different scenarios. "What if rates go up 2%? What if they drop 1%?" You can model all of it.

Chapter 4: Duration & Convexity (How Sensitive Is Your Bond?)

Duration: The Most Important Number

Duration answers the question: "If interest rates move by 1%, how much will my bond's price change?" That's it. Despite the confusing name, it's NOT about time. It's about sensitivity.

If a bond has a duration of 7, that means: rates go up 1% → price drops about 7%. Rates go down 1% → price rises about 7%. Simple.

Why does everyone think it's about time? There IS a version called "Macaulay duration" that measures the weighted average time until you receive your cash flows. But for investing purposes, what matters is "modified duration" — the price sensitivity version. The textbook drives this home with a great example: a CMO tranche (a type of mortgage bond) can have a duration of 40 even though the underlying mortgages are only 30-year. That makes no sense as a time measure. But as a price sensitivity measure, it means "if rates move 1%, this thing moves 40%." Terrifying, but at least it makes sense.

What Affects Duration?

Three things drive duration:

Maturity: Longer bonds have higher duration. A 30-year bond is more sensitive to rate changes than a 2-year bond. Makes sense — you're locked in for longer.

Coupon: Lower coupon means higher duration. A zero-coupon bond (no coupon at all) has the highest duration for any given maturity because ALL of your money is returned at the end — nothing comes back early to cushion you.

Yield level: When yields are already high, duration is lower. When yields are low, duration is higher. This actually helps in practice — when rates are rising (bad), your duration drops (less sensitivity to further increases), slowing down your losses.

Convexity: The Curvature Correction

The price-yield curve bends in your favor. Duration is the tangent line; convexity is how much the curve bows above it.

Duration gives you a straight-line estimate. But the actual price-yield relationship is curved. Convexity measures that curvature.

Think of it like predicting where a thrown ball will land. Duration says "it's going in that direction at this speed" (the straight-line prediction). Convexity adds "but gravity is pulling it down" (the curve correction). For small throws, the straight-line is close enough. For big throws, you need the curve.

The formula looks scary but the idea is simple: price change ≈ -duration × rate change + ½ × convexity × (rate change)². The first part is the linear estimate; the second part is the curvature correction.

Positive convexity is a good thing. It means your gains from falling rates are bigger than your losses from rising rates. You WANT positive convexity. Callable bonds and mortgage-backed securities have negative convexity, which is the opposite — your upside is capped. More on that later.

Effective Duration: The One That Actually Works for Complex Bonds

Regular (modified) duration assumes the bond's cash flows don't change when rates move. That's fine for a simple government bond. But for a callable bond or a mortgage-backed security, cash flows absolutely DO change with rates — if rates drop, people refinance their mortgages and you get your money back early.

Effective duration fixes this by actually recalculating what happens to cash flows when rates change. The difference can be enormous: a bond might have a modified duration of 7 but an effective duration of 50 when the options kick in. If you used the wrong one, your risk management would be wildly off.

Key Rate Duration: Not All Rate Moves Are Equal

Duration assumes all rates move by the same amount at the same time (a "parallel shift"). But in reality, 2-year rates might go up while 30-year rates go down. Key rate duration breaks the problem into 11 pieces, one for each spot on the yield curve. This way you know: "I'm very exposed to 10-year rate changes but barely affected by 2-year moves."

Three portfolios can have identical overall duration but completely different key rate durations — meaning they'll behave totally differently depending on HOW rates move.

Chapter 5: Why Do Interest Rates Exist?

Three Big Ideas

Economists have debated this for centuries. The book covers three main theories:

1. Time preference (Böhm-Bawerk, Fisher): People prefer having things now rather than later. If I ask you to give me $100 and I'll pay you back in a year, you'll want something extra for waiting. That extra is interest. Böhm-Bawerk also had a cool concept called "roundaboutness" — building a pipe system to carry water is slower than walking to the spring, but way more productive in the long run. Interest compensates for choosing the slower, more productive method.

2. Loanable funds (Wicksell): Interest rates are set by supply and demand for borrowable money. Demand comes from businesses wanting to invest and people wanting to spend. Supply comes from savers and from the central bank creating money. The rate settles where supply meets demand.

3. Liquidity preference (Keynes): People want to hold cash because it's flexible — you can spend it anytime. To get people to give up their cash and buy bonds instead, you need to offer them interest. The more uncertain the world feels, the more interest you need to offer.

Real vs. Nominal Rates (Fisher's Equation)

Here's a crucial distinction: the interest rate you see quoted (say, 5%) is the NOMINAL rate. But if inflation is 3%, your REAL return is only about 2%. Fisher's equation: nominal rate ≈ real rate + expected inflation.

TIPS (Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities) let you see this in real time. If a regular Treasury pays 4.5% and a TIPS pays 2%, the market is saying expected inflation is about 2.5%.

Are Any Investments Truly "Risk-Free"?

Traditionally, U.S. Treasuries are called "risk-free." But the book challenges this. A former Treasury official called the idea of risk-free sovereign bonds "an oxymoron." Even Treasuries carry inflation risk, liquidity risk, and — over very long horizons — the theoretical possibility that even the U.S. government might not pay. There's actually a global shortage of "safe" assets, which has pushed interest rates down by about half a percentage point.

Negative Interest Rates

Yes, this actually happened. Several countries (Europe, Japan, Sweden) had negative rates, meaning you PAY the bank to hold your money. The idea: make it so painful to hold cash that people spend or invest it. Critics say it crushes bank profits, punishes savers, and creates asset bubbles. At one point, 25% of developed-world investment-grade bonds had negative yields.

The Volcker Shock

In 1979, inflation was out of control (13%+). Fed Chairman Paul Volcker raised rates to over 20%. This caused two recessions and 30-year Treasury yields above 14%. Brutal. But it worked — inflation dropped to 4% by 1982. The important takeaway: when people say rates should "return to normal highs," they're usually using the 1970s-2000s as a reference. But that period was historically extreme — more of an exception than the rule.

Chapter 6: The Yield Curve (Why Different Maturities Pay Different Rates)

What Is the Yield Curve?

The yield curve is just a chart showing what different maturities pay. The x-axis is time (3 months, 1 year, 5 years, 30 years), and the y-axis is yield. Usually it slopes upward — longer bonds pay more than shorter ones. But not always.

Normal (upward-sloping): The most common shape. You get paid more for locking up your money longer. Makes sense.

Inverted (downward-sloping): Short-term rates are HIGHER than long-term rates. This is rare and freaks people out because it has historically predicted recessions.

Flat: All maturities pay about the same. Usually a transition between normal and inverted.

Spot Rates and Bootstrapping

A "spot rate" is the yield on a zero-coupon bond for a specific maturity. You can't always observe these directly (there aren't zero-coupon bonds for every maturity), so you derive them from regular bond prices through a process called "bootstrapping."

Think of it like solving a puzzle: you know the 6-month rate. You use that to figure out the 1-year rate from a 1-year bond's price. Then you use both of those to figure out the 1.5-year rate. And so on, building up the entire curve one step at a time.

Forward Rates: What the Curve Implies

Here's a subtle but important concept. If the 1-year rate is 4% and the 2-year rate is 5%, the curve is implying something about what the 1-year rate will be ONE YEAR FROM NOW. That implied rate is called a "forward rate."

But — and this is crucial — forward rates are NOT predictions of future rates. Studies show they're terrible predictors. Instead, they represent the rate you can LOCK IN today through bond selection. They're a breakeven concept: "If I buy a 2-year bond instead of rolling two 1-year bonds, I'll break even only if the future 1-year rate equals this forward rate."

Why Does the Curve Slope Upward?

Three theories compete:

Pure expectations: The curve slopes up because the market expects rates to rise. Problem: this has been tested and it doesn't hold up. Forward rates consistently overestimate future rates.

Liquidity theory: Investors demand extra compensation (a "term premium") for locking up money for longer periods. Even if rates are expected to stay flat, the curve slopes up because of this premium.

Preferred habitat: Different investors want different maturities. Banks want short-term, pension funds want long-term. Supply and demand at each maturity point shapes the curve independently. If there's excess demand for 10-year bonds, that yield gets pushed down.

The evidence supports a mix of liquidity theory and preferred habitat. Pure expectations is largely rejected.

The LIBOR to SOFR Transition

LIBOR (the London Interbank Offered Rate) was the reference rate for over $375 trillion in financial products. But it was based on banks ESTIMATING what they'd charge each other, and it got manipulated in a major scandal. So the market is switching to SOFR (Secured Overnight Financing Rate), which is based on actual overnight lending transactions backed by Treasuries. The transition is messy because SOFR and LIBOR aren't equivalent — SOFR doesn't include a credit risk component, so you need a "spread adjustment" to convert old contracts.

Part II: Types of Bonds

Chapter 7: Government Bonds (Treasuries & Agencies)

U.S. Treasury Securities

Treasuries are bonds issued by the U.S. government. They're considered the safest bonds in the world because the U.S. can always tax people or (in theory) print money to pay you back. They come in several flavors:

Treasury bills (T-bills): Short-term (1 year or less). No coupon — you buy at a discount and get face value back. Buy a T-bill for $980, get $1,000 in 6 months. The $20 difference is your interest.

Treasury notes: 2 to 10 years. Pay a regular coupon every six months.

Treasury bonds: Over 10 years (usually 20 or 30). Same idea as notes, just longer.

TIPS (Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities): These automatically adjust for inflation. The principal goes up with CPI, so your coupon payments grow too. At maturity you get back the HIGHER of your inflation-adjusted principal or the original $1,000. It's a built-in inflation hedge. If regular Treasuries pay 4.5% and TIPS pay 2%, the market is telling you expected inflation is about 2.5%.

STRIPS: The government takes a regular bond and "strips" it into individual zero-coupon pieces. A 10-year note with 20 coupon payments becomes 21 separate securities. Each one is a standalone zero-coupon bond. Foreign investors love these because some countries tax interest but not capital gains, and a strip's entire return looks like a capital gain.

Floating-rate notes (FRNs): The newest Treasury product — pays a coupon that resets based on the 13-week T-bill rate. This means the price stays close to par because the coupon adjusts to market conditions automatically.

How Treasury Auctions Work

The government sells new Treasuries through auctions. There are two types of bidders: Competitive bidders (big dealers) specify the yield they want — if they're too greedy, they get shut out. Noncompetitive bidders (everyone else, up to $5M) just accept whatever yield is determined. Since 1998, everyone pays the same price ("Dutch auction"). About 24 "primary dealers" are required to participate meaningfully in every auction — they're the backbone of the Treasury market.

The most recently issued bond at each maturity is called "on-the-run" and is the most liquid — it's what everyone watches as the benchmark. Older issues ("off-the-run") trade at slightly higher yields because they're harder to buy and sell.

The Repo Market: The Plumbing of Finance

A repurchase agreement (repo) is basically a pawn shop for bonds. A dealer says: "I'll sell you this Treasury bond today, and buy it back tomorrow at a slightly higher price." That price difference is the interest. It's a collateralized short-term loan — the bond is the collateral.

The repo market is HUGE — over $4 trillion daily. It's how dealers fund their bond inventories, how portfolio managers get leverage, and how the Fed influences short-term rates. When the repo market broke in September 2019 (overnight rates spiked to 10%), it made headlines because it threatened the entire financial system's plumbing.

"Haircuts" are the safety margin — if you pledge $100 of bonds, you might only borrow $98. That 2% cushion protects the lender if prices drop before you buy them back. "Tri-party repos" use a custodian bank (usually BNY Mellon or JPMorgan) to manage the collateral, making it safer for both sides.

When dealers desperately need a specific bond to cover short positions, they'll lend at below-market repo rates — the bond is "special." This financing advantage is one reason on-the-run Treasuries are bid up: they earn a premium because they can be used to borrow cheaply in the repo market.

Government-Sponsored Enterprises (GSEs)

These are weird hybrid entities — technically private companies but with an implied government backstop:

Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac: They buy mortgages from banks and package them into mortgage-backed securities. Placed in government conservatorship in September 2008. Their debt doesn't have an EXPLICIT government guarantee, but everyone treats it like it does. Together they have hundreds of billions in outstanding debt.

Federal Home Loan Banks: 11 regional banks with about $1 trillion in debt, funding mortgage lending. TVA: The nation's largest public power company — bonds backed by power revenues. Farmer Mac: Secondary market for farm loans.

Agency bonds trade at a spread above Treasuries reflecting their credit risk — small in normal times, but that spread blew out during the 2008 crisis before the government stepped in.

Chapter 8: Corporate Bonds

The Pecking Order

When a company borrows through bonds, there's a strict hierarchy of who gets paid first if things go wrong. Think of it like lifeboats on the Titanic:

1. Secured debt: First off. Specific assets pledged as collateral. About 70% average recovery in bankruptcy.
2. Senior unsecured: No collateral, but highest unsecured priority. About 50% recovery.
3. Senior subordinated: Explicitly junior. About 35% recovery.
4. Subordinated: Even more junior. About 30% recovery.
5. Preferred stock: Looks like a bond but counts as equity.
6. Common stock: Last in line. Usually wiped out.

Chapter 7 vs. Chapter 11 Bankruptcy

Chapter 7 (Liquidation): Company is done. Sell everything, pay creditors in order.

Chapter 11 (Reorganization): Company gets a second chance — keeps operating while restructuring its debt under court supervision. The strict pecking order gets violated constantly in Chapter 11 because it's really a negotiation. Junior creditors may get paid something to avoid a long legal fight, even though seniors haven't been made whole.

DIP financing (debtor-in-possession): Fresh loans made to the bankrupt company. These get SUPER priority — they jump ahead of even existing secured debt. Why? Without new cash to keep the lights on, there's nothing left to reorganize. DIP lenders know this and demand premium interest rates.

Call Provisions and the Noncallable vs. Nonrefundable Trap

Callable bonds let the issuer buy them back early. The fancy version is a "make-whole call" — the issuer pays you the present value of all remaining payments discounted at Treasuries plus a small spread. This fully compensates you, so issuers only use it when they really have to.

Important trap the book warns about: "Noncallable" and "nonrefundable" are NOT the same thing. "Nonrefundable" only means the issuer can't use proceeds from new cheaper debt. They CAN still call using asset sales, equity, or cash on hand. "Noncallable" means they simply can't call. Period. This distinction matters enormously in high-yield covenants.

The Liquidity Crisis in Corporate Bonds

Dealer bond inventories dropped 80% since 2007 while issuance grew 50%. Post-crisis regulations made it expensive for banks to hold large inventories. Result: 38% of corporate bonds never trade. 23% trade only a few times. The average stock trades 3,800 times per day; the most liquid IG corporate bond trades only 85 times.

Electronic platforms are filling the gap. "All-to-all" trading lets any participant trade with any other — the buy-side now provides about 50% of liquidity. This is a massive shift from the old dealer-centric model.

The RJR Nabisco LBO: Event Risk

In 1988, RJR Nabisco — an investment-grade company — was hit with a $25 billion leveraged buyout. Overnight: Moody's slashed the rating from A1 to B3, spreads jumped from 100 to 350 basis points, and bondholders lost 15-20%. The lesson: even a strong company can be destroyed as a credit in a single announcement. This scared the market so badly that it drove demand for more predictable bond structures.

CLOs: The Bond Market's Layer Cake

A CLO (Collateralized Loan Obligation) pools leveraged loans and slices them into tranches:

The top layer (AAA, ~80% of deal) gets paid first — very safe, low yield. Each layer down takes progressively more risk. The bottom layer (equity, ~7%) absorbs first losses but targets 12-15% returns. "Coverage tests" are automatic alarms: if defaults get too high, cash gets redirected from lower layers to protect the seniors.

Leveraged loans themselves are floating-rate, have 5-8 year maturities, and historically recover about 70% in default (versus ~40% for bonds) because they're secured.

Default Rates and Recovery

Average corporate default rate: about 1.5% per year. For high-yield specifically: about 4.3%. But defaults spike during recessions — 11% in 1990-91, 13% in 2009. There's an "aging effect": defaults are most likely about 3 years after issuance, when the initial cash cushion from the bond offering has been spent.

Recovery rates also move with the cycle — averaging 50% overall but dropping to 42% in 2008 (when everyone is selling distressed assets at once). This creates a double hit: more defaults happen exactly when recovery rates are lowest.

Chapter 9: Municipal Bonds (Tax-Free Investing)

Why Munis Are Special

Municipal bonds are issued by state and local governments, and here's the magic: the interest is usually TAX-FREE at the federal level. The math is dramatic: a muni paying 3.4% is equivalent to a taxable bond paying 5.23% in the 35% bracket. At 45%? That's like getting 6.18%. The higher your taxes, the better munis look. And if you buy bonds from your own state, you often avoid state and local taxes too — "triple tax exemption."

Types of Municipal Bonds

General Obligation (GO) bonds: Backed by the government's full taxing power. "Unlimited tax" GO is the strongest — they can raise taxes as much as needed. "Limited tax" has a cap on the rate.

Revenue bonds: Backed by a specific project's income — toll roads, airports, hospitals, water systems. Detailed rules govern these: "rate covenants" require charges high enough to cover debt, "additional bonds tests" limit further borrowing. Revenue flows through a waterfall: operations get paid first, then debt service, then reserves, then maintenance. Bondholders are behind operations in priority — they need the project to keep running.

Moral obligation bonds: The state says "we morally feel obligated to replenish the reserve fund." But they're not LEGALLY required — the legislature has to vote each session. It's a promise on the honor system.

Pre-refunded bonds: The issuer has set aside Treasury securities in escrow that perfectly match all remaining payments. These are essentially risk-free — they carry near-AAA credit quality even if the original issuer is weaker.

The Default Myth

Official rating agency data shows 0.1% cumulative defaults. But broader studies found 2,521 defaults from 1970-2011 — roughly 50x the official stats. The agencies only count rated bonds and use narrow definitions.

The five biggest municipal bankruptcies: Detroit ($18.5B, largest by population — nearly half was unfunded pensions), Jefferson County AL ($3.2B), Orange County CA ($1.7B from leveraged inverse floater bets that blew up), San Bernardino CA ($1B), Stockton CA ($700M).

An interesting observation: when GM went bankrupt, nobody panicked about their Walmart bonds. But when a single municipality has trouble, everyone panics about ALL munis. The muni market is prone to irrational contagion.

The Monoline Insurance Collapse

Before 2007, about 50% of new munis came with insurance from "monoline" insurers. No insured muni had ever defaulted. Then these insurers got involved in subprime mortgage bonds, got destroyed, and lost their AAA ratings. The entire foundation of muni insurance collapsed overnight and permanently changed the market. Now investors have to actually analyze credit risk instead of relying on the insurer's stamp.

Tobacco Settlement Bonds

In 1998, the four largest tobacco companies settled with 46 states for $206 billion over 25 years. States then borrowed against those future payments by issuing "tobacco settlement bonds." The catch: if people stop smoking (or regulation crushes tobacco companies), those future payments might not come. These bonds are uniquely risky because their cash flows depend on an industry that everyone is actively trying to shrink.

Chapter 10: International Bonds

The Global Bond Market

Total global debt: $119.7 trillion. Bonds issued outside domestic markets fall into three categories with fun nicknames:

Foreign bonds: A foreign entity issues in YOUR country, in YOUR currency. Yankee bonds (U.S.), Samurai bonds (Japan), Bulldog bonds (U.K.), Kangaroo bonds (Australia), Maple bonds (Canada).

Eurobonds: Issued outside any single country, offered to investors globally. Not subject to any one country's securities laws. Despite the "Euro" name, they can be in any currency — a "Eurodollar bond" is just a dollar bond issued outside the U.S.

Global bonds: Trade simultaneously in multiple markets. Maximum liquidity because they're registered everywhere.

Currency Risk: Often Bigger Than You Think

Foreign bond returns have three parts: (1) interest income, (2) capital gain/loss on the bond, and (3) exchange rate gain/loss. Historically, the currency component is the BIGGEST source of volatility — often larger than the interest rate effect itself.

You can hedge currency risk by selling the foreign currency forward. But there's a catch called "covered interest rate parity": if one country's rates are higher, the forward exchange rate adjusts so that hedging costs eat up the yield advantage. When you fully hedge, you often eliminate the extra yield that attracted you to the foreign bond. Studies show hedged international bond portfolios have lower volatility and higher risk-adjusted returns than unhedged ones.

Covered Bonds: 250 Years, Zero Defaults

Covered bonds (called "Pfandbriefe" in Germany, since 1769) have dual recourse: if the issuer goes bankrupt, you still have a claim on a ring-fenced pool of high-quality assets kept separate from the bankruptcy estate. You get a claim on BOTH the issuer AND the pool. In over 250 years, no covered bond has ever defaulted. They dominate European bank funding.

Evaluating Countries as Borrowers

Three questions for sovereign debt:

Serviceability: Can they pay their bills right now? Look at foreign reserves, current account, short-term debt due.

Solvency: Is the debt path sustainable? Look at debt-to-GDP trends, fiscal deficits, whether debt is growing faster than the economy.

Structural fundamentals: Are institutions solid? Rule of law, governance, political stability, economic diversification.

The critical distinction: external debt (in foreign currency) is far more dangerous than domestic debt (in local currency). A country can always print its own currency (causing inflation but avoiding default). It CANNOT print dollars or euros. That's why most sovereign defaults are on external debt.

Part III: Mortgages & Structured Products

Chapter 11: Home Loans (Mortgages)

Mortgage Basics

A mortgage is a loan to buy a house, using the house as collateral. Most Americans have one. The bond market cares about mortgages because they get packaged into securities and sold to investors. Before we understand mortgage BONDS, we need to understand mortgages themselves.

Fixed-rate mortgage (FRM): The classic — same payment every month for 15 or 30 years. Each payment is part interest, part principal. Early payments are almost all interest; later payments are mostly principal. This is called "amortization." For investors who buy these as securities, the risk is that the borrower pays early (refinances) when rates drop, sending you cash back when you can't reinvest it well.

Adjustable-rate (ARM): The rate resets periodically based on a reference rate. Think of it as a floating-rate bond from the borrower's perspective. Has caps to prevent extreme jumps — a "2/6 cap" means no more than 2% per reset and no more than 6% total over the life. The most popular hybrid is a 5/1 ARM: fixed for 5 years, then adjusts annually.

Interest-only: No principal for an initial period — lower payments, but you're not building equity. Negative amortization: The payment is less than the interest owed, so your balance actually GROWS. These were a big contributor to the 2008 crisis — borrowers were accumulating debt while thinking they were paying off their house.

The Four Risks of Mortgages

1. Credit risk: The borrower stops paying. Measured by LTV (loan-to-value) — a 90% LTV means the borrower only has 10% equity. The less skin in the game, the more likely they walk away.
2. Liquidity risk: Individual mortgages are hard to sell.
3. Price risk: Interest rate changes affect the loan's value.
4. Prepayment risk: The borrower pays early — refinancing, selling the house, or just making extra payments.

Strategic Default

This became a huge issue in 2008-2012. When house prices crashed (Nevada dropped 60%), many borrowers who COULD afford their payments chose to walk away because they were "underwater" — they owed more than the house was worth. About 25% of foreclosures during this period involved borrowers who could have kept paying but rationally chose not to. The cost of walking away was less than the negative equity. This blew up the assumptions in every mortgage model.

Chapter 12: Mortgage-Backed Securities

What Is an MBS?

A mortgage-backed security (MBS) is created when a bank takes a bunch of mortgages, pools them together, and sells shares of that pool to investors. Each month, homeowners make their mortgage payments, and that cash flows through to the MBS holders.

Three agencies guarantee these: Ginnie Mae (backed by the full faith of the U.S. government — the safest), Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac (GSE guarantee, not explicit government backing). If borrowers default, the agency absorbs the loss and keeps paying investors.

Prepayment: The Central Problem

Here's what makes MBS different from every other bond: the borrowers can (and do) pay early whenever they want. This means you don't know when you'll get your money back. There are three reasons people prepay:

1. Housing turnover: People sell their houses (relocation, divorce, death, upsizing). This is relatively stable and predictable — maybe 5-7% per year for seasoned pools.

2. Cash-out refinancing: Homeowners refinance at a higher balance to extract equity — "my house went up $100K, let me tap that." Somewhat rate-insensitive.

3. Rate/term refinancing: THE big one. Rates drop, people refinance to a lower rate. This is the main driver of prepayment volatility and the source of the "negative convexity" problem.

The S-Curve and Burnout

Prepayment response to rate drops follows an "S-curve." When rates first start falling, prepayments are slow (people are cautious or haven't noticed). As rates drop further, prepayments accelerate rapidly (everyone's refinancing). Then it flattens out — once rates are low enough, nearly everyone who CAN refinance already HAS.

Burnout is a fascinating effect: after a pool has been through a period of low rates, the people most willing and able to refinance have already left. What's left are borrowers who CAN'T refinance (bad credit, not enough equity, too small a loan for the costs to make sense) or WON'T (inertia, can't be bothered). So the same rate decline produces less prepayment on a "burned out" pool than on a fresh one.

Negative Convexity: Why MBS Are Tricky

Remember positive convexity from Chapter 4? MBS have the OPPOSITE problem:

When rates fall: You'd normally expect your bond price to go UP a lot. But with MBS, falling rates cause people to refinance and prepay. You get your money back at par (face value) right when rates are low and you can't reinvest it well. Your upside is capped. This is called "contraction risk."

When rates rise: Nobody refinances, so your money is stuck in lower-rate mortgages for much longer than you expected. You wanted your money back to reinvest at higher rates, but you can't get it. This is "extension risk."

So you get the worst of both worlds: limited upside when rates fall, full downside when rates rise. This is why MBS pay a premium over Treasuries — investors demand extra return to deal with this headache.

The TBA Market

The TBA ("To-Be-Announced") market is the forward trading market for agency MBS. You agree to buy, say, $10 million of Fannie Mae 3.5% 30-year MBS, but you don't know which specific pools you'll get until two days before settlement. Only six things are agreed upon: agency, maturity, coupon, face value, price, and settlement date. This standardization makes it the second-most-liquid market in the world after Treasuries — earning the nickname "Eighth Wonder of the World" during the 2008 crisis when it kept functioning even as everything else froze.

Chapter 13: CMOs (Slicing Up Mortgage Risk)

The Basic Idea

A CMO (Collateralized Mortgage Obligation) takes a pool of mortgages and redistributes the cash flows into different slices called "tranches." Think of it as taking a single stream of water and splitting it into different pipes that deliver water at different speeds and in different amounts.

The critical insight: CMOs cannot eliminate prepayment risk. They can only REDISTRIBUTE it. If the pool gets prepaid faster, someone has to absorb that. CMOs decide WHO absorbs it and WHEN.

Sequential-Pay: First Come, First Served

Sequential-pay CMO: principal flows like a waterfall — top tranche gets paid first.

The simplest CMO structure. Imagine $400 million of mortgages split into four tranches: A, B, C, and D. Everyone gets interest, but ALL principal payments go to A first. Once A is fully paid off, principal goes to B. Then C. Then D.

The result: Tranche A has a short average life (about 3.5 years), while D has a long one (about 20 years). Insurance companies that want long-term bonds buy D. Money market funds that want short-term cash buy A. Everyone gets closer to what they want than if they all owned the raw mortgage pool.

Z-Bonds: The Deferred Gratification Tranche

A Z-bond (accrual tranche) receives ZERO current payments. Its interest accrues and gets added to its principal balance. Meanwhile, that interest is redirected to pay down the earlier tranches faster. Once all the senior tranches are retired, the Z-bond starts receiving everything.

Why would anyone want this? Because there's zero reinvestment risk. You don't receive any coupons that you need to reinvest at possibly lower rates. Your money just compounds until the Z-bond starts paying. It has the longest duration and highest sensitivity of any CMO tranche — in one example, the Z-bond had duration 11.24 while tranche A had 0.02. Same collateral, 500x difference in sensitivity.

PAC Bonds: The Protected Class

PAC (Planned Amortization Class) bonds have a prepayment "collar" — say, 90-300 PSA (don't worry about the units, just think of it as a speed range). As long as prepayments stay within that range, the PAC gets a perfectly predictable schedule. The volatility goes to the "support bonds" instead — they're the shock absorbers.

Think of it this way: the PAC is riding in a luxury car with suspension. The support bonds ARE the suspension — they absorb all the bumps. This is great until the support bonds get exhausted (paid off faster than expected). Once they're gone, the PAC has no protection and becomes a regular sequential-pay tranche. This is called a "busted PAC."

IO and PO Strips: Pure Plays

You can split a mortgage pool into two opposite bets:

PO (Principal-Only): You only get the principal payments. You buy at a deep discount. If prepayments speed up, you get your money FASTER — a home run. If rates fall, POs soar. If rates rise and prepayments slow, POs get crushed. Very volatile.

IO (Interest-Only): You only get the interest. If prepayments speed up, your interest stream SHRINKS because the pool balance is declining. IOs LOSE money when rates fall (opposite of normal bonds). They actually have "negative duration" — their price moves in the SAME direction as rates. This makes them useful as hedges.

Combined, IO + PO always equals the original pass-through. They're like matter and antimatter — opposite in every way, but together they're whole.

Floater/Inverse Floater

A fixed-rate tranche can be split into a floating-rate piece and an inverse floating-rate piece. The floater pays SOFR + a spread. The inverse floater pays whatever's left over — its coupon goes DOWN when rates go UP. The leverage factor determines how extreme the inverse floater is. With a leverage of 3, a 1% rate increase causes a 3% coupon drop on the inverse. These are the instruments that blew up Orange County, California in 1994 — $1.7 billion in losses from leveraged inverse floater bets that went wrong.

Chapter 14: Private-Label Mortgage Bonds

No Government Guarantee

Unlike agency MBS (Ginnie Mae, Fannie, Freddie), nonagency or "private-label" RMBS have NO government backing. If borrowers default, investors eat the losses. This means these deals need "credit enhancement" — structural protection for the senior bondholders.

The Senior-Subordinated Structure

Imagine a deal with $1 billion of mortgages. $900 million becomes "senior" bonds (AAA rated). $100 million becomes "subordinated" bonds (lower rated). If borrowers default and there are losses, the subordinated bonds absorb them first. The senior bonds don't lose a penny until the entire $100M subordination is wiped out. It's like the bottom floors of a building taking the flood while the upper floors stay dry.

The shifting interest mechanism adds another layer of protection: during the first 5 years, ALL prepayments are directed to senior bonds. This prevents the subordination from eroding too fast (if both seniors and subordinates get proportional prepayments, the protection ratio stays the same, but if ALL prepayments go to seniors, the ratio actually improves).

Other Credit Enhancements

Excess spread: The mortgages in the pool pay, say, 6.5%, but the bonds only cost 5.5%. That 1% difference ("excess spread") absorbs losses before any tranche is affected. It's like a running profit cushion.

Overcollateralization: The pool has $1.05 billion of mortgages backing $1 billion of bonds. That extra $50M is the first-loss cushion.

Third-party insurance: Rarely used anymore since the monoline insurers collapsed in 2007-2008.

Dodd-Frank and "Skin in the Game"

After the 2008 crisis, Dodd-Frank required that securitization sponsors keep at least 5% of the risk ("skin in the game"). The idea: if you have to eat your own cooking, you'll make better food. This was a direct response to pre-crisis practices where originators would make terrible loans and immediately sell them off to investors, keeping zero risk.

Chapter 15: Commercial Real Estate Bonds

How CMBS Differs from Residential

Commercial mortgage-backed securities (CMBS) are backed by loans on office buildings, malls, hotels, apartments, and warehouses. Two huge differences from residential MBS:

1. Nonrecourse: If a commercial borrower defaults, the lender can only take the property — they can't go after the borrower's other assets. So the analysis is entirely about the property, not the borrower's personal finances.

2. Strong call protection: Unlike residential mortgages (where people refinance freely), commercial mortgages have SERIOUS prepayment penalties. The four forms, from strongest to weakest: (a) Lockout : literally cannot prepay for a period, (b) Defeasance: borrower has to substitute Treasuries that perfectly replicate the remaining cash flows (expensive!), (c) Penalty points: a percentage fee, (d) Yield maintenance: a make-whole payment.

The Key Metrics

DSC ratio (Debt Service Coverage): Net operating income divided by the debt payment. If DSC is 1.5x, the property earns 50% more than it needs to pay the mortgage. Lenders typically want at least 1.25x.

LTV (Loan-to-Value): Lower is safer. A 65% LTV means there's 35% equity cushion before the lender loses money.

Balloon Risk

Commercial mortgages typically have 5-10 year terms but 25-30 year amortization schedules. This means a huge principal payment is due at maturity — the borrower has to refinance. If credit markets are tight when that balloon payment comes due, the borrower might not be able to refinance. This was a major concern during 2008-2012 when CMBS delinquency rates exceeded 9%.

Property Types

Multifamily (apartments): Most stable — people always need housing. Office: Long leases (5-10 years) provide stability but tenant concentration is risky. Retail (malls): Anchor tenant creditworthiness is everything. Hotel: Most volatile — it's like a daily-renewal lease. Industrial/warehouse: Growing fast with e-commerce.

Chapter 16: Asset-Backed Securities

Securitization: Turning Anything into a Bond

Asset-backed securities (ABS) are created when a company takes a pool of cash-flow-generating assets, puts them in a separate legal entity (an SPV — special purpose vehicle), and issues bonds backed by those cash flows.

The magic is the "true sale": the assets are legally separated from the original company. If the company goes bankrupt, the SPV and its assets are safe — creditors of the company can't touch them. This means ABS bonds can have HIGHER ratings than the company that created them. A BBB-rated company can issue AAA-rated ABS.

Auto Loan ABS

The most predictable type of ABS. People prepay auto loans when they sell/trade in the car or when the car is totaled, not because of interest rate changes (nobody refinances a car loan to save 0.5%). This makes prepayment modeling much simpler than mortgage-backed securities. Captive finance companies (Ford Motor Credit, etc.) are the main issuers.

Credit Card ABS

These have a unique "revolving" structure. During the initial period (often years), as cardholders pay down their balances, the money is reinvested in new receivables — the pool stays roughly the same size. Only after this revolving period ends do the bonds start paying down. There are "early amortization" triggers: if the portfolio performance deteriorates (charge-offs spike, payment rates collapse), the bonds start repaying immediately to protect investors.

The Weird Ones

The book mentions some creative securitizations: David Bowie bonds — securities backed by music royalty receivables from artists like Bowie, James Brown, and Ashford & Simpson. Also rate reduction bonds backed by utility surcharges — extremely stable cash flows because the charges are legislated as a statutory property right. The lesson: if something generates predictable cash flows, Wall Street will find a way to securitize it.

Chapter 17: Bond Funds & ETFs

Why Use a Fund Instead of Buying Bonds Directly?

Individual bonds are hard to buy in small amounts (typical minimum is $1,000-$10,000 per bond), you'd need dozens to diversify properly, and many bonds rarely trade. A fund solves all of this: you get instant diversification, professional management, and daily liquidity, all for a small annual fee.

Open-End Funds vs. Closed-End Funds

Open-end (mutual) funds: You buy and sell at the fund's net asset value (NAV), calculated once daily at market close. The fund issues new shares when people buy and redeems shares when people sell. The number of shares changes constantly.

Closed-end funds: Fixed number of shares that trade on an exchange like stocks. The price can be ABOVE the NAV (premium) or BELOW (discount). Sometimes you can buy $1 of bonds for $0.90 — but there's usually a reason for the discount (bad management, illiquid holdings, sentiment).

Bond ETFs: The Game Changer

ETFs combine the best of both: they trade throughout the day like stocks, have the diversification of a mutual fund, and typically have lower fees. The "creation/redemption" mechanism keeps prices near NAV — if the ETF price drifts above NAV, authorized participants create new shares (pushing the price down). If below, they redeem (pushing it up).

ETFs also have a tax advantage: the in-kind creation/redemption mechanism avoids triggering taxable gains, unlike mutual funds that must sell bonds (creating capital gains) when investors redeem.

But there's a catch that showed up in 2008: when the underlying bonds become extremely illiquid, the ETF price can decouple from NAV. High-yield bond ETFs saw tracking errors of 5-6% during the crisis because the arbitrage mechanism broke down — authorized participants stopped creating/redeeming because they couldn't price the underlying bonds. ETF liquidity is ultimately limited by the liquidity of what it holds.

Hedge Fund Bond Strategies

Convergence trading: Betting that two similar bonds will converge in price. Example: on-the-run and off-the-run Treasuries should trade at similar yields, but the liquidity premium creates a gap. Buy the cheap one, sell the expensive one, wait for convergence. This is how LTCM (Long-Term Capital Management) worked — until Russia defaulted in 1998 and spreads widened instead of converging, causing a spectacular $4.6 billion blowup.

Distressed investing: Buy bonds of companies near bankruptcy at deep discounts. Hedge funds bought Detroit pension bonds at 41 cents on the dollar. They bought 70% of a $3.5B Puerto Rico bond issue right after a junk downgrade. The bet is that forced selling creates prices below fundamental value — you're picking up dollars for 40-60 cents.

Part IV: Analysis & Valuation — Where the Math Gets Real

Chapter 18: How Bonds Actually Get Traded (And Why It's Harder Than You Think)

Here's something that surprises a lot of people: most bonds don't trade on an exchange the way stocks do. There's no "Bond Market" building on Wall Street with a big ticker tape showing prices. Instead, bonds mostly trade over the counter (OTC) — meaning buyers and sellers find each other through dealers, phone calls, and electronic platforms. It's more like buying a used car than buying shares of Apple.

Bond liquidity has five dimensions, and they're all intuitive once you hear them. Tightness means low transaction costs (the gap between what buyers pay and sellers receive). Immediacy means you can trade quickly. Depth means there are lots of participants. Breadth means there's enough volume to handle big trades without moving the price. And resiliency means the market can absorb a shock and bounce back. A truly liquid market has all five. Most bonds? They might have one or two on a good day.

Here's the dirty secret of bond markets: most bonds never trade on any given day. Think about it — a single company might have 20 or 30 different bond issues outstanding, each with different coupons, maturities, and terms. Most were bought by big institutions that plan to hold them forever. So when someone asks "what's this bond worth?" — the answer often comes from a pricing service that estimates the price based on similar bonds that did trade. It's called an "evaluated price" — basically an educated guess using math. Bloomberg, ICE, and Refinitiv all do this. And a 25 basis point pricing error on a $100 million position is a $250,000 mistake — so getting it right matters.

The way bonds are traded has been going through a revolution. The old model worked like this: big banks (broker-dealers) would buy bonds with their own money and hold them in inventory, then sell them to clients. They were basically used car dealers — buying wholesale, selling retail. But after 2008, regulations forced banks to hold more capital against their inventory, which made warehousing bonds much more expensive. Dealers cut their inventory by about 80%. That's a huge deal — it means there's way less grease in the machine.

Five types of electronic platforms have popped up to fill the gap: auction (competitive bidding), cross-matching (anonymous order matching), interdealer (dealer-to-dealer), multi-dealer (Tradeweb, MarketAxess — multiple dealers competing for your order), and single-dealer (a bank's own platform). The newest innovation is all-to-all platforms where literally anyone can trade with anyone — dealers, hedge funds, pension funds, everybody. The buy-side (investors) now provides about 50% of the liquidity. That's a massive shift from the old world where dealers provided nearly all of it.

TRACE (Trade Reporting and Compliance Engine) was a game-changer for transparency. Before TRACE, if you wanted to know what price a corporate bond last traded at, good luck — dealers had all the information and you were flying blind. Now every corporate bond trade must be reported, and anyone can see the actual transaction prices. It's like going from haggling at a bazaar to checking prices online.

Chapter 19: Bonds with Tricks Inside Them (Embedded Options)

Remember how some bonds are callable — the issuer can pay you back early if rates drop? And some are putable — you can force the issuer to buy it back? These are embedded options, and analyzing them is one of the most important (and hardest) things in bond investing. This chapter is about the tools for doing that analysis.

First, let's nail down static spread (also called Z-spread or zero-volatility spread). Remember how we talked about yield spread — the difference between a bond's yield and the Treasury yield? Well, that's a crude measure because it only compares to one point on the Treasury curve. The static spread is smarter: it asks "what constant spread do I need to add to every single point on the Treasury spot rate curve to make the present value of this bond's cash flows equal its market price?"

Here's a concrete example. Say you have a corporate bond priced at $87.08. If you used the traditional yield spread, you'd get 109 basis points. But if you properly discount each cash flow using the full Treasury curve plus a constant spread, you need 120 basis points to get to $87.08. That's an 11 basis point difference — and the static spread is more accurate because it respects the shape of the yield curve, not just one point on it. The steeper the yield curve, the bigger the difference.

Now, here's where it gets really interesting. You can break apart any bond with an embedded option into its pieces:

  • Callable bond = regular bond MINUS the value of the call option (the issuer "bought" a call from you, so your bond is worth less)
  • Putable bond = regular bond PLUS the value of the put option (you "bought" a put from the issuer, so your bond is worth more)
  • Convertible bond = regular bond PLUS a call option on the company's stock

This decomposition is powerful because it tells you something crucial: higher volatility hurts callable bonds but helps putable bonds . Why? Because when volatility goes up, all options become more valuable. If you sold an option (callable bond), the thing you sold is now worth more — bad for you. If you bought an option (putable bond), the thing you bought is now worth more — good for you.

Callable bonds also suffer from negative convexity — which sounds scary but is actually intuitive. Normal bonds have a nice property: when rates drop, the price goes up, and it goes up at an accelerating rate (positive convexity — the curve bends in your favor). But for callable bonds, as rates drop, the issuer gets closer and closer to calling the bond. So the price stops going up as fast. Eventually, the price just flattens out near the call price. You've got a ceiling on your upside but no floor on your downside. That's negative convexity, and it's why callable bonds have to offer higher yields — you're being compensated for this asymmetric payoff.

Here's a fun fact though: a bond can rationally trade above its call price. Say you have a bond callable at $104 in one year, with a 13% coupon and a 5% one-year yield. The coupon income during that year ($13) more than makes up for the difference between the market price ($111.52) and the call price ($104). You'd pay $111.52 even though you know it'll be called at $104, because the income stream justifies the premium.

To properly value bonds with embedded options, we need the binomial interest-rate tree . Think of it as a branching diagram of possible future interest rates. At each point in time, rates can go either up or down (with 50/50 probability). The tree is "calibrated" so that it correctly prices all the Treasury bonds we can observe in the market (that's what makes it "arbitrage-free" — no free lunch). Then we add the corporate bond's cash flows and at each node ask: "Would the issuer call this? Would the holder put it?" If yes, we replace the value with the call or put price. Working backward through the tree gives us the fair value.

The spread that makes the model's value equal the market price is the OAS — Option-Adjusted Spread. It's "option- adjusted" because the model already accounts for the option exercise decisions. The OAS tells you the spread you're earning for pure credit risk, stripped of the option effects. It's the most important metric for comparing bonds with different embedded options.

Effective duration uses the same tree: shift all rates up a little, recalculate the price with the OAS; shift all rates down, recalculate. The difference tells you how sensitive the bond is to rate changes including the option effects. This can be wildly different from regular duration. A callable bond might have a regular duration of 5 but an effective duration of 3 — because if rates drop, the call kicks in and shortens the bond's life. Some mortgage securities can have a regular duration of 7 but an effective duration of 50 — they're incredibly rate-sensitive once you account for prepayments.

One critical warning: all of this depends on the volatility assumption you put into the model. Higher assumed volatility means options are worth more, which lowers callable bond prices and lowers the OAS. If your volatility assumption is wrong, your entire output is wrong. This is called modeling risk, and it's why smart managers stress-test their portfolios across multiple volatility scenarios.

Chapter 20: Mortgage-Backed Securities — The Monte Carlo Chapter

Remember how we just used a binomial tree to value callable bonds? Well, we can't do that for mortgage-backed securities. Here's why: MBS cash flows are path-dependent.

What does that mean? For a regular callable bond, the issuer's decision to call only depends on where interest rates are right now. But for mortgages, prepayment behavior depends on the entire history of rates since the mortgage was originated. Here's the intuition: if rates dropped to 3% last year and a bunch of homeowners already refinanced, then even if rates drop to 3% again this year, fewer people will refinance — because the easy refinancers already left. This is called burnout. The current month's prepayment depends on what happened in every previous month. You can't capture that with a branching tree — you need to simulate entire paths.

Enter Monte Carlo simulation. Here's how it works:

  1. Generate 512 to 1,024 random interest-rate paths starting from today's yield curve
  2. For each path, calculate the refinancing rate at each month, and feed it into a prepayment model to get that month's cash flows
  3. Discount each path's cash flows back to today using the rates on that path
  4. Average across all paths — that's the bond's theoretical value

The OAS is the spread you add to all rates on all paths to make the average present value equal the market price. Same concept as before, just computed across hundreds of simulated scenarios instead of a tree.

Here's why the distribution matters, not just the average. Consider two CMO tranches, both with the same OAS. A PAC bond has an average value of $88 with a standard deviation of $1 — it's pretty stable across all scenarios. A support bond has an average value of $77 with a standard deviation of $10 — it could be worth anywhere from $57 to $97 depending on which rate path materializes. Same OAS, radically different risk. You must look at the distribution of outcomes, not just the average. This is one of the most important lessons in MBS analysis.

For CMO analysis, the asset manager's job is to figure out how the collateral's value gets distributed across the different tranches. Every deal has some tranches where value accumulates (low risk, high value — great combination) and others where risk accumulates (high risk, low value — the "toxic waste"). The challenge is accurately modeling how cash flows get allocated across the waterfall.

There are also market-based duration measures for MBS. The most creative is coupon curve duration (Douglas Breeden's idea): instead of shocking interest rates, you look at how the price changes when you roll up or down the coupon curve (comparing a 3.5% MBS to a 4.0% and 3.0% MBS). This captures real market pricing behavior. The limitation: it only works for generic pass-throughs, not for specific CMO tranches.

Chapter 21: Convertible Bonds — Bonds That Can Become Stocks

A convertible bond gives you the right to exchange your bond for a predetermined number of shares of the company's stock. Think of it as a bond with a lottery ticket attached. You get the safety of a bond (regular coupon payments, principal repayment at maturity) plus the opportunity to participate in stock price appreciation if the company does really well.

The key number is the conversion ratio — how many shares you get per bond. If a $1,000 bond converts into 50 shares, your conversion price is $1,000 / 50 = $20 per share. That means the stock needs to get above $20 for conversion to be interesting. The bond's minimum value is always the higher of two things: what you'd get if you converted right now (conversion value), or what the bond is worth as a plain bond with no conversion feature (straight value).

Let's say the stock is at $17 and the convertible is trading at $950. The market conversion price is $950 / 50 = $19 per share. You're paying $2 more per share than the stock price — that $2 is called the market conversion premium, and it's essentially what you're paying for the option to participate in the upside while having a bond floor underneath you.

How do you know if that premium is worth it? The premium payback period tells you how long it takes to earn back the premium through the convertible's higher income (the coupon) compared to stock dividends. If the bond pays $50/year and the stock pays $30/year in dividends (on 50 shares), you're earning $20/year more with the bond. The $100 total premium ($2 × 50 shares) takes 5 years to recover. Not terrible, not amazing.

The risk-return trade-off is straightforward. If the stock doubles from $17 to $34, a stock investor makes 100%, but the convertible investor makes about 79% (because you paid that $2 premium per share). If the stock crashes to $7, the stock investor loses 59%, but the convertible falls only to its straight bond value — maybe $788 — so you lose about 17%. You give up some upside for downside protection. That's the deal.

But here's the catch: the floor is a moving floor. If the stock is crashing because the company is in trouble, the straight bond value is also dropping (because credit risk is increasing). So the protection is weakest exactly when you need it most. It's like an umbrella that shrinks in heavy rain.

Convertibles come in different flavors. Equity-sensitive convertibles are deep in-the-money — the stock is way above the conversion price, so the convert basically moves with the stock (delta above 75%). Balanced convertibles are in the middle (delta 55-75%). Busted convertibles are deep out-of-the-money — the stock has fallen way below the conversion price, so they trade like regular bonds based on yield and credit quality. And distressed convertibles are busted PLUS the company might go bankrupt.

Hedge funds love convertibles for arbitrage strategies. The classic trade: buy the convertible (which includes the embedded stock option) and short the stock. If the option is cheap relative to the stock's actual volatility, you profit from the mispricing while being hedged against the stock's direction. You need to know the delta (how much the convert moves per dollar of stock movement) to get the right hedge ratio. If the delta is 0.60 and the conversion ratio is 50, you short 30 shares (50 × 0.60). But delta changes as the stock moves — so you need to keep adjusting, which generates transaction costs.

Chapter 22: Corporate Credit Analysis — How to Not Lose Your Money

This might be the most practically important chapter in the entire book. All the math and models in the world won't save you if the company you lent money to goes belly up. Credit analysis is about figuring out: will they pay me back?

Credit risk has three components: default risk (they stop paying), credit spread risk (the market decides they're riskier and the bond price drops), and downgrade risk (a rating agency lowers their grade, which usually triggers forced selling by institutional investors who can only hold investment-grade).

The foundation of credit analysis is understanding the business model. Before you touch a single financial ratio, you need to answer three basic questions: What does this company do? Where does revenue come from? Where does it incur costs? Sounds obvious, right? But the book's star case study shows how many "experienced" investors skip this step.

Porter's Five Forces gives you the industry framework. Think of it as a checklist of competitive threats: (1) How fierce is the competition? (2) Can new companies easily enter this industry? (3) Do suppliers have the power to squeeze margins? (4) Do customers have the power to negotiate lower prices? (5) Are there substitute products that could replace what this company sells? A company that's protected on all five fronts (like Coca-Cola) is a great credit. One exposed on all five (like a small commodity producer) is risky.

Corporate governance matters differently for bondholders than for stockholders — and this is a crucial insight most people miss. Stockholders want management to take risks because their upside is unlimited. But as a bondholder, your upside is capped (you get your coupons and your money back, and that's it). So "shareholder-friendly" actions like big stock buybacks funded by debt, special dividends, or aggressive acquisitions are actually bondholder-unfriendly because they increase leverage and risk without giving bondholders any extra return.

Covenants are your legal protections as a bondholder — think of them as a prenup. Affirmative covenants are promises the company makes (maintain insurance, file reports). Negative covenants are restrictions (limits on how much more debt they can take on, restrictions on selling assets, caps on dividends). Watch out for carve-outs — these are exceptions that weaken the covenants. If a covenant says "no additional debt above 4× leverage" but has a carve-out for $500 million in "permitted indebtedness," that protection has a huge hole in it.

For financial analysis, the key ratios are:

  • Interest coverage = EBIT / Interest Expense. Can they pay their interest? Below 1× means they literally can't cover interest from operations — they're borrowing to pay interest, which is a death spiral
  • Leverage = Total Debt / EBITDA. How many years of earnings would it take to pay off all debt? Under 3× is comfortable; 4-5× is stretched; above 6× is scary
  • Free Cash Flow: This is the real test. A company can have beautiful earnings but terrible cash flow if they're stuffing inventory, letting receivables pile up, or spending like crazy on capex. S&P has a useful hierarchy: operating cash flow → free operating cash flow (minus maintenance capex) → discretionary (minus dividends) → prefinancing (minus debt repayment)

The investment decision in credit comes down to this: don't trade safety for yield . Credit risk is nonlinear. Moving from BBB to BB might get you an extra 100 basis points of yield, but if things go wrong, you could lose 10 times that yield difference in capital losses. The risk-reward is asymmetric — you can only earn your coupon on the upside, but you can lose a big chunk of principal on the downside.

Case Study: Sino-Forest Corporation. This is a masterclass in what happens when you skip business model analysis. Sino-Forest claimed to be a leading Chinese timber company. It had 15 years of double-digit revenue growth and 69% EBITDA margins. Impressive, right? But if you actually looked at the business model, it was a middleman of middlemen — buying and selling trees through 4-5 intermediaries, never dealing directly with suppliers or customers. Despite those amazing margins, it never generated positive free cash flow. Three credit rating agencies had it at BB-level. Then it collapsed. Enterprise value went from C$7.2 billion to bankruptcy in under a year. Bondholders got 10-15 cents on the dollar. All three rating agencies rushed to downgrade after the stock had already crashed. If you'd done basic Five Forces analysis (commodity product, no barriers to entry, no bargaining power), you'd have stayed away.

Case Study: Sirius XM. This one's about covenants. Sirius had great fundamentals — monopoly in satellite radio, 26 million subscribers, 85% subscription revenue, $1 billion annual free cash flow. But majority owner John Malone wanted to lever it up to 4× to return cash to equity holders. The 2022 bonds were the only ones with proper high-yield covenants capping leverage at 3.5×. So the company did something clever: they granted the 2022 bonds a security interest, which triggered an investment-grade upgrade from all three rating agencies. That upgrade automatically suspended the high-yield covenants. Within a month, the company issued $1.5 billion in new debt. The 2022 bonds jumped $4 in price (they got security), but bondholders in every other tranche saw their protection evaporate. The lesson: read the covenant document, including the fine print about covenant suspension.

Chapter 23: Credit Risk Modeling — Teaching Computers to Predict Defaults

There are two main schools of thought on modeling whether a company will default, and they think about the problem in completely different ways.

Structural models (the Merton framework, 1974) start from a brilliant insight: owning stock is like owning a call option on the company's assets. Think about it. If a company has $100 million in assets and $80 million in debt, the stockholders own the residual. If the assets go up to $150 million, stockholders get the extra $70 million (after paying off $80M in debt). If the assets fall to $60 million, stockholders get zero (debt holders take everything) but can't lose more than their investment. That's exactly how a call option works — unlimited upside, limited downside, with the debt level as the "strike price."

Below the debt line: stockholders get nothing, bondholders take losses. Above it: bondholders are capped, stockholders keep all the upside.

From the bondholder's perspective: owning a corporate bond is like owning a risk-free bond but having sold a put option to the stockholders. If the company's asset value drops below the debt level, the stockholders "put" the company to you (they walk away and you're stuck with the underperforming assets). Default happens when asset value crosses below the debt threshold.

The original Merton model only allowed default at maturity (when the debt comes due). Later models improved on this. Black-Cox allows default whenever the asset value crosses a barrier at any time, not just at maturity. Shimko-Tejima adds the realistic feature that interest rates themselves are uncertain (stochastic). But all structural models share a big practical problem: you need to know the market value of the company's assets, which isn't directly observable. You have to infer it from the stock price using complicated iterative calculations.

Reduced-form models take a completely different approach. Instead of trying to model why a company defaults, they just model the probability of default as a random event with a certain intensity (likelihood per unit of time). Think of it like a light bulb — you don't model the exact physics of filament degradation; you just estimate the probability it burns out this month.

The main reduced-form models are Jarrow-Turnbull (calibrates default probability from market credit spreads) and Duffie-Singleton (more flexible — recovery can happen anytime, not just at maturity). Over 90% of practitioners prefer reduced-form models because they skip the impossible task of estimating asset value and instead work directly with observable market data (bond prices and CDS spreads).

The practical punchline: credit ratings from Moody's and S&P are actually not great at predicting defaults. Simple statistical models consistently outperform them. Kamakura Corporation analyzed Bank of America's bonds and found that out of 431 bonds they evaluated, BAC's 15 issues ranked in the bottom 17% on a return-risk basis — meaning the spread didn't adequately compensate for the default probability. Even heavily-traded, well-known names can be terrible relative value when you actually do the math.

There's also a hybrid approach from Giesecke and Goldberg: what if investors don't know where the default threshold is? This accounts for corporate fraud — Enron, WorldCom, Sino-Forest — where the true financial position was hidden. If you can't see the cliff, you can't tell how close you are to the edge. MSCI Barra uses this framework.

Part V: Portfolio Management — Putting It All Together

Chapter 24: Bond Portfolio Strategies — From Passive to Aggressive

Now that we understand individual bonds, how do you build a whole portfolio of them? It turns out there's a spectrum of strategies, from completely passive to extremely active, and where you sit on that spectrum depends on your beliefs about whether you (or your manager) can outsmart the market.

At one end: pure indexing. Just buy all the bonds in the benchmark index (like the Bloomberg U.S. Aggregate) in the right proportions and match it exactly. No thinking, no bets, minimal fees. Problem? It's nearly impossible. The Agg has over 6,500 bonds, many of which rarely trade. You can't practically buy all of them, especially the illiquid ones. So in practice, "passive" bond management still requires some art.

Next: enhanced indexing. You match most of the index's risk characteristics but make small tweaks to squeeze out a little extra return — maybe slightly overweighting sectors that look cheap, or holding bonds that are about to drop out of the index (more on that later). You're making small bets, but not big ones.

Then: active management. You're making deliberate bets that differ from the index. Maybe you think rates are going to drop, so you extend duration. Maybe you think corporate spreads will tighten, so you overweight corporates. You're trying to beat the benchmark, and you accept the risk of underperforming. Academic research shows that interest-rate forecasting is really hard — few managers can consistently get it right.

Absolute return strategies (hedge fund approach) don't care about a benchmark at all. They try to make money regardless of whether the bond market goes up or down. They typically use leverage (borrowing to amplify returns) and target 150-400 basis points over cash.

Finally: liability-driven strategies, where the benchmark isn't a market index but the investor's own obligations (pension payments, insurance claims). We'll cover this in depth in Chapter 27.

A popular middle ground is core/satellite: the core of your portfolio is passively indexed (capturing the market return cheaply) and the satellites are actively managed slices targeting specific opportunities (maybe high-yield, emerging markets, or distressed debt). You get most of the market return for cheap and add active bets only where you think you have an edge.

Here's a dirty secret about bond indexes that most people don't realize. Bond indexes have some serious structural problems:

  • The duration problem: The duration of the index is set by issuers (companies issuing debt at whatever maturity is cheapest for them), not by investors (who might want a different duration). So the index duration is basically a historical accident.
  • The "Bum's Problem": Companies with more debt get a higher weight in the index. Think about that — the most indebted companies have the biggest presence in your "benchmark." That's the opposite of what you want. In stocks, market cap reflects value creation. In bonds, bigger weight just means more borrowing.
  • Instability: Index composition changes monthly as new bonds are issued and old ones mature or get downgraded. Managers have to constantly trade to keep up, generating transaction costs.

Alternatives include GDP-weighted indexes (PIMCO's approach for sovereign debt), issuer caps (no company gets more than 3% of the index), and laddered indexes (equal weights across maturities). None is perfect, but they address the Bum's Problem.

For active strategies, the main risk factors fall into two buckets: systematic risks (market-wide, can't diversify away) and nonsystematic risks (specific to individual bonds, can diversify away). Systematic risk further breaks down into term structure risks (how the yield curve moves) and non-term-structure risks (sector risk, credit risk, optionality). A landmark study by Litterman and Scheinkman found that 91.6% of Treasury returns come from parallel shifts (everything moves up or down together), 5% from twists (one end moves more than the other), and only 3.4% from butterfly movements (the middle moves differently from the ends). Translation: getting duration right accounts for the vast majority of your returns. Everything else is a footnote — occasionally an important footnote, but a footnote.

Three classic yield curve positioning strategies:

  • Bullet: concentrate all your bonds at one point on the curve (say 5 years). Maximum yield for a given duration. Best when you expect small, parallel rate moves.
  • Barbell: split between the very short end (2 years) and the very long end (30 years). Higher convexity (the curve bends in your favor more) but you sacrifice about 25 basis points of yield. Best when you expect large moves or curve flattening.
  • Ladder: spread equally across all maturities. Most diversified, provides regular reinvestment, reduces timing risk. Best when you're unsure what rates will do.

Same total duration, very different risk profiles. Bullet = concentrated bet. Barbell = extremes. Ladder = diversified.

For spread strategies (betting on which sectors will outperform), your trades must be dollar-duration-neutral. This means that if you swap one bond for another, the portfolio's overall rate sensitivity stays the same. Otherwise, you think you're making a credit bet but you're actually making a hidden rate bet. It's like trying to taste-test two wines while also changing the temperature — you won't know what's actually causing the difference.

Chapter 25: Building the Portfolio — From Theory to Reality

So you've picked your strategy. Now how do you actually construct the portfolio? This is where the rubber meets the road, and it's harder than it sounds.

The classic approach is from Harry Markowitz (the same guy behind Modern Portfolio Theory for stocks): optimize the portfolio to maximize return for a given level of risk, considering how all the bonds move together (correlations). Problem: for 100 bonds, you need 4,950 correlation estimates. That's a LOT of estimation, and errors compound quickly. Even worse, bond returns aren't normally distributed (they have those asymmetric payoffs we discussed), so variance isn't a great risk measure for bonds.

What most bond managers actually care about is tracking error — how much the portfolio's return deviates from the benchmark's return. If you're measured against the Bloomberg Agg and you beat it by 0.50% one quarter and lag by 0.30% the next, your tracking error captures that volatility. A tracking error of 100 basis points means you have roughly a 67% chance of being within 1% of the benchmark in any given year. Most enhanced indexers target 50-75 bps of tracking error; active managers might target 150-300 bps.

There are two types: backward-looking (what actually happened — historical) and forward-looking (what we predict based on current positions). Forward- looking is much more useful because it tells you about the risk you're taking now, not last quarter. But a nice sanity check: the average forward-looking tracking error during a year should be reasonably close to the backward-looking number at year-end.

A simpler approach is the cell-based method. Divide the benchmark into boxes (cells) based on characteristics: duration buckets × maturity ranges × sectors × credit quality × call features. That might give you 96 cells (2 × 3 × 4 × 4). Then try to match the benchmark's weight in each cell. It's like a paint-by-numbers approach to portfolio construction. For passive management, match the cells exactly. For active, deliberately mismatch the cells where you have a view. The limitation: it ignores how cells are correlated with each other.

The state of the art is multi-factor risk models. These decompose total risk into six factors: yield curve, swap spread, volatility, government spread, corporate spread, and securitized spread. You can see exactly where your risk is coming from and whether you intended it.

Here's a real example that drives the point home. A 50-bond portfolio versus a 5,693-bond benchmark. The systematic tracking error was only 4.6 basis points per month (the portfolio matched the benchmark's factor exposures pretty well). But the idiosyncratic tracking error was 7.8 basis points — and it accounted for 87% of the total tracking error. The biggest contributor? A 19.9% weight in financial corporates versus only 9.7% in the benchmark. That's "name risk" — the risk that comes from holding specific issuers rather than broad market factors. Even if you nail every macro factor, concentrating in a few names creates enormous risk.

Leverage is a key tool for bond managers. You borrow money (typically through the repo market — repurchase agreements, which are essentially collateralized loans) and invest the borrowed money alongside your own. If you have $100M in equity and borrow $300M, you control a $400M portfolio. If the portfolio earns 5% and you're paying 4.5% to borrow, you earn that 0.5% spread on all $400M — but you only invested $100M of your own money. So your return on equity is much higher.

But leverage is a double-edged sword. That $400M portfolio with $100M equity has an effective duration of 12 (if the underlying duration is 3). A 1% rate move changes your equity by 12%, not 3%. And if things go really wrong, you can get margin calls that force you to sell at the worst possible time. This is exactly what killed LTCM in 1998 — 25:1 leverage with Nobel Prize-winning models that said their positions were safe. When Russia defaulted and global spreads blew out, their individually-sound positions became collectively catastrophic as forced selling triggered more forced selling.

A note on backtesting: when you test a strategy against historical data, beware of biases. Survivorship bias means only looking at bonds/companies that survived (ignoring the ones that defaulted). Look-ahead bias means using information that wasn't available at the time (like knowing which companies would be downgraded). Optimal period bias means cherry-picking the time period where your strategy looks best. Monte Carlo simulation (generating synthetic future paths) is the most robust backtesting method because it doesn't assume history will repeat exactly.

Chapter 26: Managing a Corporate Bond Portfolio

Corporate bonds have a unique characteristic that shapes everything about how you manage them: the upside is capped but the downside is large. If you buy a bond, the best case is you get all your coupons and your money back. The worst case is the company defaults and you get pennies on the dollar. This is fundamentally different from stocks, where the upside is theoretically unlimited.

This asymmetry has a profound practical implication. A study removed the top and bottom 10% performers from bond and stock indexes. For bond indexes: removing the losers improved returns dramatically, while removing the winners barely mattered. For stock indexes: the opposite — removing winners destroyed returns. The lesson: in credit investing, avoiding losers is far more important than picking winners . This shapes the entire philosophy of corporate bond management.

The best single measure of credit risk exposure is Duration Times Spread (DTS) = spread duration × credit spread. It's better than spread duration alone because it captures both how sensitive a bond is to spread changes AND how wide the spread is. An A-rated bond with 100 bps spread and duration 5 (DTS = 500) has less risk than a BB bond with 400 bps spread and duration 2 (DTS = 800), even though the BB bond has lower duration. DTS lines up beautifully with actual return volatility across all quality segments.

For high-yield bonds, there's a wrinkle: empirical duration. Here's the issue — high-yield bonds have two risk factors, not one. They respond to interest rates (like all bonds), but they also respond to equity markets (because if the stock goes up, the company is healthier, which is good for bondholders). These two factors partially offset each other: when rates fall (good for bond prices), spreads often widen (bad for bond prices, because lower rates usually coincide with economic weakness). The result is a duration multiplier that's less than 1.0. For A-rated bonds, the multiplier is about 0.85. For BBB consumer cyclicals, it's 0.63 — meaning a bond with analytical duration of 5 actually behaves like it has duration 3.15. For the lowest- rated junk bonds, the multiplier can be near zero — they don't respond to interest rates at all because equity risk completely dominates. They're bonds in name only.

The book outlines seven credit relative-value trades:

  1. Yield-spread pickup: swap into a higher-spread bond (making sure to stay dollar-duration-neutral)
  2. Credit-upside: buy bonds you think will be upgraded before the market prices it in
  3. Credit-defense: reduce exposure to sectors or issuers you think are deteriorating
  4. New-issue swap: trade into newly-issued bonds for better liquidity
  5. Sector rotation: over- weight sectors you think will outperform based on the economic cycle
  6. Curve adjustment: adjust your portfolio duration relative to the benchmark
  7. Structure trades: swap between callable/putable/bullet bonds to exploit option mispricing

One of the most reliable alpha sources in fixed income is fallen angels — bonds that get downgraded from investment- grade to high-yield. When a bond falls out of the investment-grade universe, indexed IG managers MUST sell it (their mandate requires it). Insurance companies and pension funds with IG-only policies MUST sell it. All this forced selling at the same time drives the price well below fundamental value. High-yield managers who buy the fallen angel capture the recovery as prices mean-revert. The data: fallen angels outperform their new high-yield peers by 6.63% over the two years after downgrade. This isn't about being smarter — it's about exploiting a structural inefficiency driven by regulatory and mandate constraints.

Similarly, constraint-tolerant investing — simply NOT selling bonds that violate index rules (downgraded, short maturity, small issue size) — consistently outperformed strict index replication from 1990 to 2009. All five tested tolerant strategies showed higher returns with equal or lower risk. The extra return isn't huge (6-7 basis points), but average active bond alpha before fees is only 44 bps — so every little bit matters.

Chapter 27: Liability-Driven Investing — Matching What You Owe

Everything we've discussed so far has been about beating a market benchmark. But many institutional investors — pension funds, insurance companies — don't care about the Bloomberg Agg. They care about one thing: can they pay their obligations? A pension fund needs to pay retired workers for 30+ years. An insurance company needs to pay out claims. Their real benchmark is their own liabilities, not a market index.

Immunization, invented by Frank Redington in 1952, is the foundational concept. Here's the key insight: when interest rates change, two things happen to a bond portfolio simultaneously. If rates rise, bond prices fall (bad) but future reinvestment income increases (good). If rates fall, bond prices rise (good) but reinvestment income decreases (bad). Redington figured out that if you match your portfolio's duration to the date when you need the money, these two effects exactly cancel out. You're "immunized" against interest rate changes.

The conditions for immunization are: (1) portfolio duration equals liability duration, (2) present value of assets is at least as large as present value of liabilities, and (3) you rebalance periodically as duration changes over time.

But there's a catch: immunization only works perfectly for small, parallel yield curve shifts. If the short end moves differently from the long end (a twist or butterfly), you're exposed. The M-squared measure from Fong and Vasicek quantifies this risk. M² measures how spread out your cash flows are around the liability date. A zero-coupon bond maturing exactly at the liability date has M² = 0 — perfect immunization, zero risk. A barbell (cash flows far from the liability date) has high M² — more exposed to non-parallel shifts. A bullet (cash flows clustered near the liability date) is in between. This is why Ronald Ryan advocates using only Treasury STRIPS — zero-coupon bonds matched to each liability date. No coupons means no reinvestment risk. It's the cleanest immunization possible, though it costs more.

Cash flow matching is the simplest approach: buy bonds whose cash flows (coupons + principal) exactly match what you owe, working backward from the last liability. It's bulletproof (no duration matching needed, no rebalancing, zero immunization risk unless bonds default), but it costs 3-7% more because you're constraining the optimization so tightly that you can't use higher-yielding bonds with imperfect timing.

A practical compromise: combination matching — cash-flow- match the near-term liabilities (where precision matters most) and immunize the long-term liabilities (where duration matching is good enough). You get high certainty for near-term payments without the full cost of matching everything perfectly.

Contingent immunization is clever. It says: as long as the portfolio value stays above the "immunization floor" (the amount needed to lock in the minimum required return at current rates), the manager can actively trade. If active bets go wrong and the portfolio value drops to the floor, everything gets immediately immunized — locking in the minimum acceptable outcome. The spread between current value and the floor is the manager's "cushion" for taking risk. Bigger cushion = more room for active management. Small cushion = stay close to immunized.

For pension plans specifically, there are several de-risking options when the funding status allows: freezing the plan (stop accruing new benefits), lump-sum buyouts (pay participants cash to leave the plan), annuity buy-ins/buy-outs (transfer the liability to an insurance company), and longevity swaps (hedge the risk that people live longer than expected). These tools let plan sponsors gradually reduce their pension risk exposure as funding levels improve.

Chapter 28: How Did We Actually Do? (Performance Measurement)

After all the strategy and portfolio construction, someone has to ask: did the manager actually add value? That's what performance measurement is about, and it's more subtle than you'd think.

There are three ways to measure returns, and they can give wildly different answers:

Arithmetic average: just add up the period returns and divide. This can be deeply misleading. If your portfolio goes up 100% one year and down 50% the next, the arithmetic average is 25%. But you actually ended up exactly where you started (100% gain on $1M = $2M; 50% loss on $2M = $1M). Your real return was 0%.

Time-weighted (geometric) return : this compounds the returns properly and strips out the effect of client cash flows. It's the standard for evaluating managers because it only measures investment decisions, not the timing of when money was added or withdrawn. If a manager has the same investment results but one client puts in a lot of money right before a bad quarter while another doesn't, the time-weighted return will be the same for both — as it should be, since the manager made the same decisions.

Dollar-weighted (IRR): this includes the impact of cash flows. Two identical managers can show dramatically different dollar-weighted returns (28.4% vs. 44.5%) solely because their clients added and withdrew money at different times. Useful for seeing how an actual fund grew, but unfair for comparing managers.

The general rule: arithmetic average is always greater than or equal to time-weighted return. They're only equal when every period has the same return (zero volatility). The more volatile the returns, the bigger the gap.

Performance attribution answers the more interesting question: why did we outperform (or underperform)? It decomposes the active return into four components: (1) interest-rate management (did our duration bet pay off?), (2) sector allocation (did we pick the right sectors?), (3) security selection (did we pick the right individual bonds?), and (4) trading effect (did we execute well?). This tells you whether a manager's outperformance was skill or luck, and which specific skills they have.

The information ratio (active return / tracking error) is the gold standard for measuring skill per unit of risk taken. An information ratio above 0.5 is considered excellent. Most active bond managers achieve ratios below 0.3. And the average active fixed-income alpha before fees is only about 44 basis points — which is barely enough to cover fees at most firms. That's why passive and enhanced indexing strategies have been gaining market share. It's really, really hard to consistently beat the bond market.

Part VI: Derivatives — The Power Tools

Chapter 29: Interest-Rate Futures — Betting on Rates Without Buying Bonds

Derivatives are financial instruments whose value is "derived" from something else. For bond markets, the big four are: futures, options, swaps, and credit default swaps. They're called "power tools" for a reason — they let you do things with incredible efficiency (change your portfolio's risk profile in seconds, hedge against losses, express very specific views) but they can also chop your arm off if you're not careful.

A futures contract is a standardized agreement to buy or sell something at a set price on a future date. The key difference from just buying the thing directly: a clearinghouse sits between every buyer and seller, guaranteeing both sides of the trade. This eliminates counterparty risk — you don't have to worry whether the other party will hold up their end of the deal. The clearinghouse takes care of it.

You don't pay the full price upfront. Instead, you post margin — a good-faith deposit, typically 3-5% of the contract value. Every day, your position is "marked to market" — if the futures price moves in your favor, money is added to your margin account; if it moves against you, money is deducted. If your account drops below the maintenance margin, you get a variation margin call — put up more cash within 24 hours or your position is closed. This daily settlement is what makes futures different from forward contracts (which settle only at the end).

The most important futures contracts for bond investors:

  • Treasury bond futures: the underlying is a hypothetical $100,000 par value, 20-year, 6% coupon bond. Prices are quoted in 32nds (so 97-16 means 97 and 16/32 percent = 97.50% = $97,500). One tick = 1/32 of a point = $31.25.
  • Treasury note futures: similar but for 2-year, 5-year, and 10-year maturities. These are the workhorses of rate hedging.
  • SOFR futures: based on the Secured Overnight Financing Rate (the replacement for LIBOR). One basis point = $25 for 3-month futures, $41.67 for 1-month. Among the fastest-growing products in CME history.

Here's a subtlety that matters: when a Treasury futures contract expires, the short (the seller) gets to choose which actual bond to deliver from a range of eligible bonds. They will obviously pick the cheapest one — the cheapest-to-deliver (CTD). This is the bond with the highest "implied repo rate" (the return from buying the bond now and delivering it at the futures expiration). The CTD changes as yields change: when yields are low, high-duration bonds tend to be cheapest; when yields are high, low-duration bonds win.

The theoretical futures price follows a simple logic: it should cost the same to (a) buy the bond now and hold it until delivery, or (b) buy the futures and take delivery later. The difference between these two strategies is the "carry" — the financing cost minus the coupon income you'd earn during the holding period. If the coupon income exceeds the financing cost (positive carry), futures trade at a discount to the cash price. If financing costs exceed coupon income (negative carry), futures trade at a premium.

The biggest practical use of futures is portfolio duration control. Say you have a $48 million portfolio with duration 2.97, but your benchmark has duration 3.68. You need more rate sensitivity. The math: target dollar duration ($48M × 3.68% = $1,766,400) minus current ($48M × 2.97% = $1,425,600) = $340,800 gap. If each five-year note futures contract has about $5,000 of dollar duration, you need 68 contracts. Buy 68 contracts and you've matched the benchmark duration — without buying or selling a single bond. That's the power of futures: you can reshape your portfolio's risk in minutes, at a fraction of the transaction cost of trading cash bonds.

Futures are also used for hedging . If you own $10 million in corporate bonds and worry about rates rising, you can sell Treasury futures to offset the rate risk. The hedge ratio depends on the PVBP (price value of a basis point) of your bonds versus the CTD, adjusted for a yield beta that accounts for the fact that corporate yields and Treasury yields don't always move in lockstep. This is cross-hedging, and it's imperfect — during credit crises, corporate spreads widen while Treasury yields fall, causing the hedge to break down exactly when you need it most. That residual uncertainty is called basis risk.

You can also create synthetic securities with futures. Holding a bond and selling futures against it turns your bond into a synthetic cash-like instrument. Holding cash and buying futures creates a synthetic bond exposure. This lets you shift between stocks and bonds using futures more efficiently than trading the underlying securities.

Chapter 30: Interest-Rate Options — Buying Insurance on Your Bond Portfolio

Options give you the right — but not the obligation — to buy or sell something at a specific price. A call option gives you the right to buy; a put option gives you the right to sell. You pay a premium for this right. If the market moves in your favor, you exercise the option and profit. If it doesn't, you let the option expire and your only loss is the premium you paid. It's insurance.

For interest-rate options, most trading happens on futures options (options on Treasury futures contracts). Three advantages: no accrued interest to worry about, plenty of supply for delivery, and futures prices are more transparent than cash bond prices. All Treasury futures options are American-style, meaning you can exercise anytime before expiration.

The profit/loss profiles are the key to understanding options. If you buy a call on a Treasury futures contract: your maximum loss is the premium you paid (if rates rise and bond prices fall, you just don't exercise), but your upside is unlimited (if rates plunge and bond prices soar, you capture all of it minus the premium). The leverage is enormous: $100 invested in call options can return 670% if rates drop 200 basis points, versus 23% from buying the bond outright. But if rates don't move or go up, you lose the entire $100. That's the options trade-off: massive leverage in both directions.

Why did Black-Scholes, the famous stock option formula, fail for bonds? Three reasons: (1) It allows unlimited bond prices — but bonds can't go above par at maturity (a zero-coupon bond converges to $100, so a call struck at $100.25 should be worthless, but Black-Scholes gives it a positive value). (2) It assumes interest rates are constant — but the whole point is that we're pricing options on interest rate changes. (3) It assumes constant variance — but bond price volatility decreases as maturity approaches (pull-to-par effect). The right approach is the arbitrage-free binomial model we discussed in Chapter 19 — it handles all three issues.

The option Greeks are the sensitivities that traders live by:

  • Delta: how much the option price moves per dollar move in the underlying. Zero for deep out-of-the-money options (they don't respond), about 0.5 for at-the-money, and near 1.0 for deep in-the-money (they move almost dollar for dollar). Delta also serves as the hedge ratio — if delta is 0.60, short 60 futures per 100 options to be delta-neutral.
  • Gamma: how fast delta changes. Highest for at-the-money options. High gamma means your hedge ratio changes rapidly, forcing frequent and costly rebalancing.
  • Theta: time decay. Options lose value every day as expiration approaches, and this accelerates in the final weeks. Think of it as the "cost of holding the insurance policy."
  • Vega (Kappa): sensitivity to volatility changes. If implied volatility goes up, option prices go up. This is crucial because volatility itself is tradeable — if you think implied volatility is below where it should be, options are cheap and you should buy them.

Two classic hedging strategies:

Protective put: You own bonds and buy put options. If rates rise and your bonds lose value, the put gains value — offsetting (or more than offsetting) the loss. If rates fall and your bonds gain value, the put expires worthless and you're out the premium. It's genuine insurance — you pay a known cost for protection against disaster.

Covered call: You own bonds and sell call options against them. You collect the premium (income), but you cap your upside — if bond prices rally past the strike price, you don't benefit beyond that point. It works well in stable or mildly-rising-rate environments where the calls expire worthless and you keep the premium income.

The collar combines both: buy a put (floor) and sell a call (cap). The call premium you receive helps pay for the put you bought. A "zero-cost collar" is when they exactly offset — you get downside protection for free, but you surrender all upside beyond the cap. Institutional investors love collars because they limit both downside loss and hedging cost.

Implied volatility deserves attention. It's the volatility level that makes the option pricing model match the market price. The volatility smile (ITM and OTM options have higher implied vol than ATM) shows that markets price in more extreme events than models predict. If implied vol is below historical vol, options are cheap — opportunity to buy. If implied vol is above historical vol, options are expensive — opportunity to sell. Trading volatility (rather than direction) is a whole sub-industry.

Chapter 31: Swaps, FRAs, Caps & Floors — The Swiss Army Knife of Fixed Income

An interest-rate swap is an agreement between two parties to exchange interest payments. One pays a fixed rate; the other pays a floating rate. No principal changes hands — only the net difference in interest payments. Think of it as two companies trading their interest-rate exposure.

Why would anyone do this? Consider a bank that made a bunch of fixed-rate loans (earning 7%) but funds itself with floating-rate deposits (paying LIBOR + 0.40%). If rates rise, the bank's funding cost goes up but its loan income stays flat — squeeze. The bank enters a swap as the fixed-rate payer (pays 5.45% fixed, receives LIBOR). Now: it earns 7% on loans, receives LIBOR on the swap, pays 5.45% on the swap, pays LIBOR + 0.40% on deposits. Net: 7% + LIBOR - 5.45% - LIBOR - 0.40% = 1.15% locked-in spread, regardless of where LIBOR goes. Rate risk eliminated.

Both sides eliminate rate risk. The dealer earns a small spread in the middle.

An insurance company has the opposite problem: it has floating-rate investments but guaranteed a fixed rate on its annuity products. It becomes the fixed-rate receiver and locks in a spread going the other direction. The swap market brings together natural counterparties — institutions that want to swap their rate exposures.

You can think of a swap two ways. First, as a package of forward rate agreements: each payment date is essentially a bet on where LIBOR/SOFR will be. Second, as a package of bonds : the fixed-rate payer has effectively sold a fixed-rate bond and bought a floating- rate note. This second view is especially useful because it tells you the swap's duration: it's approximately the maturity divided by 2 (since the floating side has near-zero duration and resets frequently). A 10-year swap has duration of about 5.

The swap rate is calculated using no-arbitrage: it's the fixed rate that makes the present value of fixed payments equal the present value of expected floating payments (using forward rates). The swap spread = swap rate minus the Treasury yield of the same maturity. It reflects credit risk in the banking system, supply/demand for fixed vs. floating exposure, and flight-to-quality effects. During crises, swap spreads can behave erratically — they briefly went negative in 2015, meaning the swap market implied that banks were safer than the U.S. government, which made no economic sense.

After a swap is initiated, its value changes as rates move. If you're the fixed-rate payer and rates rise, you're in the money — you locked in paying a low fixed rate and are receiving a higher floating rate. The swap's value is simply the PV of what you'll receive minus the PV of what you'll pay.

Structured notes combine swaps with bond issuance to create custom products. Example: a company wants fixed-rate funding at 6%. It could issue a regular fixed-rate bond at 6.10%. Instead, it issues an inverse floater (paying 13% - LIBOR) to investors who want leveraged rate exposure, then enters a swap (pays LIBOR, receives 7%). Net cost: 13% - LIBOR + LIBOR - 7% = 6.00%. That's 10 basis points cheaper than the straight bond. The investor gets the leveraged exposure they want; the issuer gets cheaper funding. Swaps make this possible.

Swap variants include amortizing swaps (notional decreases over time — great for matching a declining mortgage balance), basis swaps (both sides pay floating but on different reference rates), and forward-start swaps (begins at a future date).

Swaptions are options on swaps. A "3 into 10" payer swaption gives you the right to enter a 10-year swap as the fixed- rate payer in 3 years. If rates rise by then, you exercise and lock in paying today's lower fixed rate — profitable. If rates fall, you let it expire. Applications: mortgage lenders use payer swaptions to hedge their pipeline (the rate locks they've granted to borrowers); pension funds use receiver swaptions to protect against falling rates that would increase their liability present values.

Caps and floors are packages of options on interest rates, covering multiple reset periods. A cap pays you if the reference rate exceeds the strike rate at any reset date — it's insurance against rising rates. Each individual period's option is called a caplet. A floor pays you if the rate falls below the strike — insurance against falling rates. Each period's option is a floorlet. Buying both (long cap + short floor) creates a collar. Banks buy caps to limit their floating-rate funding costs. Insurance companies buy floors to protect their guaranteed rates on annuity products.

Example: a bank has floating-rate liabilities and buys a 5.2% cap on $10 million notional. If LIBOR/SOFR stays below 5.2%, the cap is worthless but the bank's funding costs are manageable. If rates spike to 7%, the cap pays the bank the difference (7% - 5.2% = 1.8% × $10M ÷ period) — offsetting the higher interest cost. The cap costs money upfront, so many banks simultaneously sell a floor (say at 3%) to generate premium income to offset the cap cost. That creates a collar: funding costs are bounded between 3% and 5.2%.

Chapter 32: Credit Default Swaps — Insuring Against Default

A credit default swap (CDS) is essentially an insurance policy on a company's debt. The protection buyer pays a regular premium (called the CDS spread) to the protection seller. In return, if the company has a "credit event" (defaults, goes bankrupt, restructures its debt), the protection seller compensates the buyer. Think of it like car insurance: you pay premiums, and if there's an accident, you get a payout.

The buyer pays steady premiums; the seller pays only if the reference entity defaults.

After the 2009 "Big Bang" reforms, CDS were standardized. Investment-grade CDS have a fixed 1% annual running spread. High-yield CDS have a fixed 5% spread. Since the fair spread might be different (say 2.5% for a particular company), the buyer or seller makes an upfront payment to compensate for the difference. Payments happen quarterly on standard dates (March 20, June 20, September 20, December 20).

What counts as a "credit event"? ISDA (the trade group that writes the rules) defines seven types: bankruptcy (formal legal proceedings), failure to pay (missed payments), restructuring (changing the terms of the debt — this is the most controversial because different contract versions define it differently), acceleration (debt becomes due early because of a covenant violation), cross default (defaulting on one debt triggers CDS on another), downgrade, and repudiation/moratorium (for sovereign governments that refuse to pay).

In 2019, a new category was added: narrowly tailored credit event. This was created because of a bizarre case where hedge fund Blackstone allegedly arranged a manufactured default on Hovnanian (a homebuilder) to trigger CDS payouts. Blackstone would provide favorable new financing; Hovnanian would technically default on an old obligation; Blackstone's CDS would pay out. It was like setting your own car on fire to collect insurance. The new rule was designed to close this loophole.

When a credit event occurs, settlement happens one of two ways. Physical settlement: the protection buyer delivers actual bonds to the seller and receives face value in cash. The buyer naturally delivers the cheapest bonds they can find. Cash settlement : an ISDA-administered auction determines the recovery price, and the seller pays par minus that price. Some real-world recoveries: Lehman Brothers bonds recovered just 8.625 cents on the dollar. JCPenney recovered 0.125 cents — nearly zero. Washington Mutual, surprisingly, recovered 57 cents.

Beyond single-name CDS, there are index CDS — standardized baskets like CDX.NA.IG (125 investment-grade names) and CDX.NA.HY (100 high-yield names). One index trade gives you diversified credit exposure without buying 125 individual bonds. New series are created every six months with constituent changes. Unlike single-name CDS (which terminate on a credit event), index CDS continue after a constituent defaults — just with reduced notional.

Tranche CDS slice the index into layers of risk, defined by how much loss each tranche absorbs:

  • Equity tranche (0-3%): absorbs the first losses. If the first default happens, equity tranche holders take the hit. With only 3% of the notional absorbing 100% of initial losses, this is about 33× leverage. Highest risk, highest return.
  • Mezzanine tranches (3-7%, 7-10%, 10-15%): middle layers, moderate risk.
  • Senior (15-30%) and super-senior (30-100%): only hit if losses are catastrophic. Almost no historical losses have reached these levels.

Why are CDS so popular? Four key advantages over buying or selling the actual bonds:

  1. Leverage: selling CDS protection gives you credit exposure without putting up the full bond price. It's an unfunded position — much less capital required.
  2. Easy shorting: want to bet against a company? Just buy CDS protection. In the cash bond market, you'd need to borrow the bond and sell it short — which is expensive and often impossible for corporate bonds.
  3. Fixed financing rate: the CDS premium is known upfront and fixed for the life of the contract. If you borrowed money to buy a bond via repo, the repo rate could change or the repo could be recalled entirely.
  4. Permanent financing: repo lending is short-term and can be pulled at any time (this is what killed Bear Stearns — their repo lenders stopped rolling). CDS has no such recall risk.

There's a quirky thing about CDS and municipal bonds. Municipal bond interest is tax-exempt, but CDS premium income is not. So a tax-exempt investor (like a muni fund) can't substitute selling CDS protection for owning the actual muni bond without a tax hit. This creates an asymmetry that affects pricing in the muni CDS market.

CDS played a starring role in the 2008 financial crisis. AIG had sold massive amounts of CDS protection on mortgage-backed securities without setting aside adequate reserves. When mortgage defaults surged, AIG owed billions to CDS protection buyers and couldn't pay. The U.S. government had to bail out AIG for $182 billion because its failure would have triggered a chain reaction across the financial system. Since then, central clearing through clearinghouses (like ICE Clear Credit) has been mandated for standardized CDS. The clearinghouse stands between counterparties, collects margin, and reduces the systemic risk of one party's failure cascading through the system. It doesn't eliminate the risk — but it centralizes and manages it much better than the pre-crisis bilateral web of counterparty exposures.

The CDS-bond basis (CDS spread minus cash bond spread) reveals relative value opportunities. When the basis is positive, CDS protection is expensive relative to the bond — attractive to sell protection. When negative, CDS protection is cheap — attractive to buy. The basis is driven by funding costs, the cheapest-to-deliver option embedded in CDS, counterparty risk, and supply/demand differences between the CDS and cash bond markets.
