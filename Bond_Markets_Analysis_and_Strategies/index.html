Part I: Background

Chapter 1: Introduction to Bonds

The global bond market totals $114 trillion outstanding across six sectors: sovereign, semi-government, corporate, ABS/MBS, covered bonds, and collateralized debt obligations. The U.S. bond market alone represents ~$46 trillion (40% of global). A bond is a debt instrument requiring the issuer to repay principal plus interest on specified dates.

Bond Features and Maturity

  • Maturity: short-term (<5 yrs), intermediate (5–12 yrs), long-term (>12 yrs); perpetual bonds have no maturity date
  • Par value: typically $1,000; also called redemption value, face value, or principal. May be repaid at maturity (bullet) or amortized over the bond’s life
  • Coupon types: fixed, floating (reference rate + quoted margin), zero-coupon, step-up, deferred, PIK (pay-in-kind where interest paid with additional bonds)
  • Embedded options: call (issuer can redeem early), put (investor can sell back), conversion (into equity), currency option
  • CUSIP: nine-character identifier (first six = issuer, next two = issue type, last = check character). Foreign securities use CINS (12 characters). Operated by S&P, owned by the American Bankers Association.
  • Bond citation convention: issuer, coupon rate, maturity date (e.g., PG 2.45s 11/3/2026 where “s” replaces “%”)

Nine Risk Types

  1. Interest-rate risk: most significant for investment-grade bonds; price moves inversely with yields
  2. Call/prepayment risk: issuer redeems early when rates fall, forcing reinvestment at lower rates
  3. Yield curve risk: non-parallel shifts (twists, butterflies) affect different maturities differently
  4. Reinvestment risk: coupon payments reinvested at lower rates than expected. Offsets interest-rate risk—forming the basis for immunization strategies
  5. Credit risk: decomposes into default risk (failure to pay), credit spread risk (spread widening without default), and downgrade risk (unanticipated rating deterioration)
  6. Liquidity risk: inability to sell at fair value; particularly acute for bonds that trade infrequently
  7. Exchange-rate risk: currency movements affect non-domestic bond returns
  8. Volatility risk: changes in expected volatility affect the value of embedded options
  9. “Risk risk”: not knowing what the risk of a security actually is. “We didn’t know this could happen” is the refrain of every financial calamity

Credit Risk Components

Credit risk decomposes into three sub-risks: default risk (failure to pay), credit spread risk (spread widening reduces price even without default), and downgrade risk (unanticipated rating deterioration). Counterparty risk is a form of credit risk particularly relevant for derivative transactions—exchange-traded instruments have minimal counterparty risk via the clearinghouse, but OTC derivatives face considerable exposure. Even investment-grade bonds face significant credit spread risk during risk-off periods—spreads can widen 100–200bps with no change in actual default probability.

Green & Climate Bonds

Green bonds finance environmental projects (>$500B cumulative issuance). The IEA estimates >$50 trillion investment needed by 2035 for 2°C target. The Green Bond Principles (ICMA) establish four pillars: use of proceeds (must finance eligible green projects), process for project evaluation, management of proceeds (tracked in sub-accounts), and reporting (annual impact disclosure).

Carbon policy performance bonds (CPPBs) represent a more aggressive mechanism: the coupon rate itself is linked to the issuer’s carbon emissions trajectory. If the issuer exceeds its emission target, the coupon steps up (penalty); if it meets or beats the target, the coupon steps down (reward). Christian Kopf proposed this structure as a way to use debt covenants—the bond market’s most powerful enforcement tool—to compel corporate decarbonization.

Mark Carney and Knut Kjaer argue that debt markets offer greater climate impact than equity markets: bondholders can directly penalize carbon-intensive companies through higher credit spreads, covenant restrictions, and refused refinancing—mechanisms unavailable to equity investors who can only vote or divest. Social bonds surged during COVID-19 as governments and development banks financed pandemic response through labeled bond issuance.

Chapter 2: Bond Pricing

Bond pricing rests on the time value of money: price equals the present value of all future cash flows. U.S. bonds use semiannual compounding: divide coupon and yield by 2, double periods.

  • PV formula: PV = FV / (1 + r)n; for annuity: PV = C × [(1 − (1+r)−n) / r]
  • Price-yield relationship: inverse and convex to the origin—meaning price appreciation for a given yield decrease exceeds price depreciation for an equal yield increase. This convex shape produces four key price-volatility properties: (1) all bonds move opposite to yield changes but by different percentages; (2) for small changes, % change is roughly symmetric; (3) for large changes, % change is asymmetric; (4) price increase > price decrease for equal yield changes (the long position benefit)
  • Par bond: coupon = yield; premium: coupon > yield; discount: coupon < yield. Pull-to-par effect: premium/discount bonds converge to par as maturity approaches
  • Price volatility determinants: for given maturity and initial yield, lower coupon → greater price volatility (zero-coupon has greatest). For given coupon and initial yield, longer maturity → greater price volatility. For given coupon and maturity, higher initial yield → lower price volatility. Example: 25-year 9% bond at 7% has 10.30% price decline for 100bp increase; at 14%, only 6.75% decline. Low-yield environments exhibit greater price sensitivity
  • Floating-rate notes: coupon = reference rate + quoted margin; price stays near par if margin matches current required market spread. Reset periods typically quarterly or semiannual
  • Inverse floater: coupon = K − L × reference rate; where L is the leverage factor. Duration = (1 + L) × fixed bond duration. If L = 4, inverse duration is 5× the collateral duration—amplifying interest-rate sensitivity dramatically. Even if collateral maturity is 30 years, inverse duration exceeds 30—proving duration is not a time measure
  • Clean price = quoted; dirty price = clean + accrued interest (actual settlement amount). Day-count: 30/360 for corporates, actual/actual for Treasuries, actual/360 for money market

Chapter 3: Measuring Yield

Yield Measures Hierarchy

  • Current yield = annual coupon / price; ignores capital gain/loss and reinvestment income. Simple but incomplete
  • Yield to maturity (YTM): the discount rate equating PV of all cash flows to price. Assumes reinvestment at YTM and holding to maturity—both unrealistic
  • Yield to call (YTC): uses call date and call price instead of maturity and par; relevant for premium bonds likely to be called
  • Yield to first call: YTC calculated to the first possible call date
  • Yield to first par call: YTC to the first date callable at par value
  • Yield to sinker: uses sinking fund schedule dates and prices
  • Yield to worst (YTW): the minimum yield across all call dates, sinking fund dates, and maturity—the most conservative conventional measure
  • Total return (horizon return): explicitly models reinvestment rate and horizon yield; the only measure that captures all three sources of bond return

Reinvestment Risk

Three sources of bond dollar return: (1) periodic coupon payments, (2) capital gain/loss, (3) interest-on-interest (reinvestment income). For a 15-year 7% bond at $769.40 (YTM 10%): total coupon = $1,050, interest-on-interest = $1,275.36, capital gain = $230.60. Interest-on-interest is nearly 50% of total dollar return. For long-term bonds, it can reach 80%—making YTM reveal little about actual realizable yield. Longer maturity → greater reinvestment risk; higher coupon → more reinvestment dependence. Zero-coupon bonds have zero reinvestment risk—the only bonds whose YTM equals promised yield exactly.

Total Return (Horizon Analysis)

The five-step procedure: (1) compute total coupon payments + reinvestment income over horizon using assumed reinvestment rate; (2) project sale price at horizon based on assumed yield; (3) sum = total future dollars; (4) semiannual return = (total / purchase price)1/periods − 1; (5) annualize: BEY = 2 × semiannual return. Example: 20-year 8% bond at $828.40, 3-year horizon, 6% reinvestment rate, 7% horizon yield → coupon + interest = $258.74, projected sale = $1,098.51, total return = 17.16% (vs. YTM that doesn’t capture horizon price changes or different reinvestment rates). Critics argue total return requires “too many assumptions”—but YTM also makes assumptions (reinvestment at YTM, hold to maturity), it just hides them. Total return makes assumptions explicit and enables scenario analysis across different rate environments.

Specialized Yield Measures

Discount margin for floaters: spread over reference rate equating PV of projected cash flows to market price (assumes reference rate unchanged over life—a limitation for volatile rate environments, and ignores caps/floors on the floating rate). Cash flow yield for MBS: monthly IRR annualized as BEY = 2[(1 + yM)6 − 1]; adds a third unrealistic assumption beyond YTM’s two: that prepayments occur as projected. Portfolio yield ≠ weighted average of individual bond YTMs; must find the single rate making PV of all portfolio cash flows equal to total market value. Treasury bill yields: discount yield quoted on 360-day basis; bond-equivalent yield converts to actual/365 for comparability. Yield changes measured two ways: absolute (basis points) and relative (percentage change, computed as natural logarithm of yield ratio).

Chapter 4: Duration and Convexity

Duration is the single most important risk measure for bonds. It approximates the percentage price change for a 100bp yield change.

“Duration is a price sensitivity measure, not a time measure. A CMO tranche can have duration of 40 while underlying mortgages are 30-year—making no sense as a time measure.”

Duration Types and Formulas

  • Macaulay: weighted average time to cash flows (weights = PV of each flow / price); for a zero-coupon bond, Macaulay duration = maturity
  • Modified = Macaulay / (1 + y/k): approximate % price change for 100bp; always less than maturity for coupon bonds
  • Dollar = modified × price × 0.01: absolute dollar price change per 100bp
  • PVBP (DV01) = dollar duration / 100: price change per 1 basis point
  • Effective: for bonds with embedded options; uses approximation (P₋ − P₊) / [2P₀Δy]. In commercial software, equation (4.23) is universally used rather than analytical formulas because it works for any bond—option-free or with embedded options
  • Spread duration: sensitivity to credit spread changes; for floating-rate bonds, near-zero interest-rate duration but potentially large spread duration. Approximation formula identical to regular duration but changes credit spread (holding Treasury yield constant) instead of total yield
  • Key rate duration: sensitivity at specific maturities (Ho’s 11 points: 3mo, 1yr, 2, 3, 5, 7, 10, 15, 20, 25, 30yr). Sum of key rate durations equals portfolio duration. Most popular measure for non-parallel yield curve risk

Key Properties

Longer maturity → higher duration. Lower coupon → higher duration. Lower yield level → higher duration. Higher yield environments have lower price volatility: a 25-year 9% bond at 7% yield has modified duration 11.21; at 14%, only 7.21. Duration moves in the “right direction” as yields change: when yields rise, duration declines (slowing the price decline); when yields fall, duration increases (accelerating the price appreciation).

Inverse Floater Duration Example

$100M collateral split into $80M floater + $20M inverse floater (L = 4). Collateral duration = 8. A 100bp rate increase causes collateral to decline 8% ($8M). Floater price unchanged. The entire $8M decline hits the inverse floater: $8M / $20M = 40% decline, so inverse duration = 40 = (1 + 4) × 8. Even with 30-year collateral maturity, duration exceeds 30—reinforcing that duration is not a time measure.

Convexity

Second-order correction improving on duration’s linear approximation:

% price change ≈ −duration × Δy + ½ × convexity × (Δy)²

Example for the 25-year 6% bond (modified duration 10.62, convexity 182.92):

  • 200bp yield increase: duration-only estimate = −21.24% (badly overestimates loss)
  • Duration + convexity estimate = −17.58% (much closer to actual −18.03%)
  • The convexity adjustment captures the curvature that duration’s straight-line approximation misses

Positive convexity is desirable: price appreciation exceeds price depreciation for equal yield change (the “long convexity” advantage). An investor holding two bonds with identical duration but different convexity will always prefer the higher-convexity bond in a volatile rate environment—it gains more when rates fall and loses less when rates rise.

Negative convexity (callable bonds, MBS) is undesirable: price appreciation is limited while depreciation is not. Callable bonds exhibit negative convexity when yields fall below coupon rate because the probability of call exercise increases—the bond’s price is effectively capped near the call price. MBS exhibit negative convexity because falling rates accelerate prepayments, returning principal at par just when the investor would most benefit from price appreciation.

Convexity Properties and Value

  • As yield increases, convexity decreases; as yield decreases, convexity increases
  • For given yield and maturity, lower coupon → greater convexity (zero-coupon has highest)
  • For given yield and modified duration, lower coupon → smaller convexity
  • The value of convexity depends on expected volatility: in low-vol environments, investors should sell convexity (callable bonds); in high-vol environments, buy convexity
  • Different vendors compute convexity with different scaling (some include the ½ factor, others don’t)—the final price change estimate is identical regardless

Portfolio Duration and SEDUR/LEDUR

Weighted average of component durations. Example four-bond portfolio: 10% at D=4, 40% at D=7, 30% at D=6, 20% at D=2 = portfolio duration 5.4. Contribution to duration = weight × duration for sector analysis. A duration-neutral portfolio (5.55 vs. benchmark 5.41) can still have very different sector exposures. SEDUR (short-end duration) and LEDUR (long-end duration) from Klaffky, Ma & Nozari measure sensitivity to yield curve slope changes at 2–10yr and 10–30yr segments respectively.

Chapter 5: Interest Rate Theory

Three classical theories explain why interest rates exist and what determines their level:

Time-Preference Theory (Rae, Böhm-Bawerk, Fisher)

Böhm-Bawerk (1889) gave three reasons for positive time preference: declining marginal utility of future income, undervaluation of future goods, and “roundaboutness”—indirect production that is more productive (building a pipe system vs. walking to a spring). Fisher (1930) called it the “impatience and opportunity” theory: interest rate = index of community’s preference for present over future income. Fisher dedicated his Theory of Interest to Böhm-Bawerk and John Rae, acknowledging their foundational contributions.

Loanable Funds and Liquidity Preference

Loanable funds (Robertson, Ohlin, extending Wicksell): equilibrium rate set by supply and demand for funds. Demand negatively related to rates (except government). Supply comes from savings, financial intermediaries, government, and changes in cash holdings. Liquidity preference (Keynes 1936): rate set by money supply/demand; people hold “money” (liquid) or “bonds” (interest-bearing). Three motives: transactions, precautionary, speculative. Three money supply effects: liquidity, income, and price expectations.

Real vs. Nominal Rates

Fisher’s Law: i ≈ r + p* (nominal = real + expected inflation). TIPS provide market-based real rate; expected inflation = Treasury yield − TIPS yield (e.g., 1.90% − 0.80% = 1.10%). The natural rate (Wicksell) is the real short-term rate consistent with stable output and inflation—estimated with downward trend across U.S., Canada, Eurozone, U.K.

Safe Asset Controversy

Safe assets serve four critical roles: satisfy regulatory requirements for financial entities, serve as pricing benchmarks, function as collateral in financial transactions, and provide the foundation for asset pricing theory and derivatives pricing. Gary Gorton defines a safe asset as one “valued at face value without expensive and prolonged analysis.”

“The idea of risk-free sovereign bonds is best thought of as an oxymoron or as an anomaly of recent history. It is not a useful, necessary or an enduring feature of the financial landscape.” —Peter Fisher, former U.S. Treasury undersecretary

The Ferreira-Shousha study (1970–2018) found that net supply of sovereign safe assets from 11 advanced economies is a key driver of natural interest rates: 20% of variance in natural rates explained by changes in net supply. Since the 1990s, global accumulation of sovereign safe assets for international reserves reduced available supply, pushing natural rates down by 50 basis points.

Negative Interest Rates

When deflationary pressure is sufficiently strong, central banks may implement negative rates. The rationale: depositors pay to keep money in bank (incentive to spend), borrowing costs decline further, currency weakens (reducing demand for currency to invest), and banks are encouraged to lend rather than hold reserves.

Arguments FOR negative rates:

  • Lower borrowing costs for businesses and consumers, stimulating physical investment
  • Weakens currency, boosting exports—the primary motivation for Bank of Japan in 2016
  • Encourages bank lending by penalizing excess reserve holdings
  • Creates incentive for spending over hoarding cash (negative carry on deposits)

Arguments AGAINST negative rates:

  • Penalizes savers—a wealth transfer from savers to debtors
  • Encourages speculative asset allocation, potentially inflating asset bubbles
  • Increases lending risk: banks extend credit to lower-quality borrowers without sufficient compensation
  • Devastating impact on depository institutions’ net interest margins
  • Hurts pensioners relying on interest income and undermines defined benefit pension plans and life insurance companies that must match long-duration liabilities

Historical implementation: ECB (2014, to prevent Eurozone deflationary spiral), Denmark, Sweden, Switzerland, Bank of Japan (January 2016, −0.1% on excess reserves to prevent yen strengthening). As of September 2019, ~25% of developed-market investment-grade bonds had negative yields. Critics point to evidence from Europe and Japan where negative rates have not achieved their intended objectives.

Historical U.S. Interest Rates

1870–1978: yields mostly 2%–7%. Volcker tightening (1979–87): Fed chairman Paul Volcker raised fed funds to 20%+ to crush stagflation. 30-year Treasuries exceeded 14%. Two recessions resulted (1980: 6 months; 1981–82: 16 months). Inflation fell from 13.3% (1979) to 3.9% (1982). Long-term corporate bonds returned −4.18% in 1979. The cure was painful but effective—followed by decades of declining rates and a stock market rally continuing into the late 2010s (interrupted by bear markets in 2000–02 and 2007–09).

Post-2008: 10-year Treasury near historical lows at 2.3%; by July 2020, 10-year at ~0.62%, 30-year at 1.3%. Quantitative easing: when near-zero rates proved insufficient, the Fed began buying long-term bonds to directly lower long-term rates. The Fed’s balance sheet quadrupled to $4.5 trillion under Ben Bernanke, unwinding began September 2017. The debate over when rates will return to “normal” reveals that the 1970–2000 period was historically exceptional, not a baseline for expectations.

Chapter 6: Term Structure of Interest Rates

The spot rate curve (zero-coupon yields) is derived from the par yield curve via bootstrapping: use known short-term spot rates to iteratively solve for longer-term rates from coupon bond prices. Forward rates are implied by the spot curve.

Bootstrapping Example

1.5-year spot: given 6-month spot 5.25% and 1-year spot 5.50%, solve for z₃ from 100 = 2.875/(1+z₁) + 2.875/(1+z₂)² + 102.875/(1+z₃)³. Result: 1.5-year spot = 5.76%. Process continues iteratively to 30 years. Government dealers profit from arbitrage between whole Treasury securities and their stripped components—this mechanism prevents spot rates from departing significantly from theoretical values.

Term Structure Theories

  • Pure expectations: forward rates = expected future spot rates (generally rejected empirically—forward rates consistently overpredict future rates)
  • Liquidity theory: forward rates include a term premium for longer maturities, explaining normally upward-sloping curves
  • Preferred habitat: supply/demand imbalances at specific maturities shape the curve independently; investors will move from preferred maturity only with sufficient yield incentive
  • Market segmentation: rates at each maturity determined solely by that maturity’s supply/demand (extreme version of preferred habitat, now largely rejected)

Seven Factors Affecting Benchmark Spread

Type of issuer, perceived creditworthiness, embedded options (call → larger spread; put → smaller), taxability of interest, expected liquidity, financeability (repo rate advantage for on-the-run issues), and term to maturity. On-the-run vs. off-the-run Treasury spread reflects both liquidity and financing advantage differences.

LIBOR → SOFR transition: Over $375 trillion in products must migrate. SOFR is overnight, backward-looking, and secured (Treasury repo). Spread adjustment required because SOFR lacks LIBOR’s credit risk component. The swap curve (SOFR/LIBOR curve) serves as an alternative benchmark to the Treasury curve, with swap spread = swap rate minus Treasury yield of same maturity.

Yield Curve Shapes

  • Normal (upward-sloping): long-term rates exceed short-term rates; the most common shape, consistent with liquidity/term premium
  • Inverted (downward-sloping): short-term rates exceed long-term rates; historically precedes recessions (strong predictive power for economic downturns)
  • Flat: little difference across maturities; often transitional between normal and inverted
  • Humped: rates peak at intermediate maturities, then decline for longer maturities; relatively rare

The shape of the yield curve shifts constantly in response to monetary policy, inflation expectations, supply/demand dynamics, and flight-to-quality flows. Yield curve risk (exposure to non-parallel shifts) is often the largest unmanaged risk in bond portfolios. Litterman & Scheinkman’s factor decomposition showed 91.6% of Treasury return variance comes from parallel shifts (level), 5.0% from slope changes (twists), and 3.4% from curvature changes (butterfly)—but the remaining 8.4% from non-parallel shifts can still produce substantial gains or losses for large portfolios.

Forward Rates as Hedgeable Rates

Forward rates derived from the spot curve represent the rates that can be locked in through appropriate bond selection—not predictions of future rates. Example: if the 1-year spot rate is 4% and the 2-year spot rate is 5%, the implied 1-year forward rate one year hence is approximately 6.01%. An investor choosing between (a) buying a 2-year zero or (b) rolling two 1-year zeros will break even only if the actual 1-year rate one year from now equals 6.01%. Empirical evidence consistently shows forward rates overestimate future spot rates—the systematic forecast error equals the term premium, confirming the liquidity theory of the term structure.

Part II: Government Securities & Corporates

Chapter 7: Treasury & Agency Securities

Treasury securities: bills (zero-coupon, ≤1 yr), notes (2–10 yr), bonds (>10 yr), TIPS (inflation-adjusted principal via CPI-U), FRNs (floating-rate notes), and STRIPS (Treasury-created zeros from stripping coupon bonds). Issued via single-price (Dutch) auction with competitive and noncompetitive bidding. On-the-run issues are most liquid benchmarks; off-the-run carry a small yield premium.

Treasury Auction Process

Competitive bids specify yield (converted to price); noncompetitive bids accept the determined yield (limited to $5M per bidder). All accepted competitive bids pay the same price (single-price/Dutch auction since 1998). The when-issued market allows trading before the auction settles, providing price discovery. Primary dealers (approximately 24 firms) are required to participate meaningfully in every Treasury auction and make markets in Treasury securities.

STRIPS and Coupon Stripping

STRIPS program launched February 1985: all Treasury notes and bonds eligible for stripping. A $500M 10-year note with 5% coupon creates 20 semiannual coupon strips ($12.5M each) + 1 corpus strip ($500M). Coupon strips (denoted ci) on the same payment date share a CUSIP and are interchangeable; principal strips (bp for bonds, np for notes) have unique CUSIPs. Foreign buyers often prefer principal strips because some countries treat the interest as capital gain (preferential tax rate). Reconstitution (buying strips to replicate a mispriced coupon bond) keeps spot rates aligned with theoretical values.

TIPS Mechanics

TIPS adjust principal by the CPI-U index ratio (current CPI / base CPI at issuance). Coupon rate is fixed, but applied to the inflation-adjusted principal, so dollar interest payments rise with inflation. At maturity, the investor receives the greater of the inflation-adjusted principal or the original par value (deflation floor). Expected inflation is estimated from the yield spread between nominal Treasuries and TIPS of the same maturity: e.g., 10-year Treasury at 1.90% minus 10-year TIPS at 0.80% = 1.10% expected annual inflation.

Repo Market and Financing

Where portfolio managers borrow using securities as collateral. A repurchase agreement is essentially a collateralized loan: dealer sells Treasury securities with commitment to repurchase at a higher price (the difference = dollar interest). Overnight repos roll daily; term repos have fixed durations. When dealers need a specific bond to cover shorts, they lend at below-market repo rates (“special” collateral), creating a financing advantage that bids up on-the-run issues.

  • Haircuts (1–3% of collateral value): protect the lender against collateral price declines; haircut size increases with collateral credit risk and maturity
  • Tri-party repos: a custodian bank (typically BNY Mellon or JPMorgan) manages collateral selection, settlement, and daily mark-to-market—reducing operational risk for both parties
  • Reverse repo: the mirror transaction—a cash lender purchases securities with commitment to resell. The Fed uses reverse repo operations (RRP) as a monetary policy tool to set a floor under short-term rates
  • Systemic importance: the U.S. repo market exceeds $4 trillion daily; disruptions (as in September 2019 when overnight repo rates spiked to 10%) can cascade through the entire financial system

The repo rate is generally below the fed funds rate because repos are secured, while fed funds are unsecured overnight lending.

Federal Agencies (GSEs)

FHLBanks (~$1T outstanding): 11 district banks issuing consolidated debt obligations (joint and several liability). Fannie Mae ($182B) and Freddie Mac ($283B): conservatorship since September 2008 under FHFA. Senior Preferred Stock Purchase Agreements provide financial support, but no explicit guarantee exists—the market prices them as if government-backed, creating a “too big to fail” dynamic. FFCBS ($294B): agricultural credit via Farm Credit Banks. TVA ($22B): largest public power system, self-funded via operating revenues and debt; issues PARRS (Putable Automatic Rate Reset Securities) for retail investors with estate put features. Farmer Mac ($19B): smallest agency, secondary market for agricultural and rural housing mortgages. Agency debentures trade at a spread over Treasuries that reflects their credit risk—this spread widened dramatically during the 2008 crisis before conservatorship.

Chapter 8: Corporate Debt Instruments

Corporate bonds follow a strict seniority hierarchy in bankruptcy:

  1. Secured debt: backed by specific collateral (property, equipment, receivables); first claim on pledged assets
  2. Senior unsecured (debentures): no collateral, but highest priority among unsecured claims
  3. Senior subordinated: explicitly subordinated to senior debt; common in high-yield capital structures
  4. Subordinated: junior to all senior claims; last debt class before equity
  5. Preferred stock: equity-like but with priority over common stock dividends
  6. Common equity: residual claim; last in line, first to absorb losses

The absolute priority rule holds in Chapter 7 liquidation but is frequently violated in Chapter 11 reorganization, where final distribution depends on bargaining power and negotiation.

Bankruptcy: Chapter 7 vs. Chapter 11

Chapter 7 (liquidation): all assets distributed to claim holders by absolute priority; no entity survives. Chapter 11 (reorganization): company files for protection, typically within two days gets an emergency hearing for DIP (debtor-in-possession) financing. DIP lenders get priority even over existing secured creditors, plus higher interest rates, because they are providing rescue capital. The company can cancel executory contracts (e.g., leases) and sell assets free of liens under court approval. A plan of reorganization must be approved by each class of creditors (two-thirds in amount, one-half in number); even if stockholders reject it, the court can approve via “cram down.”

“A secured creditor has little risk of realizing a loss if the issuer goes into bankruptcy”—this common belief is dangerously wrong. In Chapter 11, absolute priority is frequently violated through plan negotiations, and even secured claims can be modified.

Call Provisions and Make-Whole Calls

Traditional calls use a call schedule with declining premiums (e.g., first callable at 103, declining to par over 5 years). Make-whole calls: payment = PV of remaining cash flows discounted at Treasury yield + small spread. Example: 10-year 6% bond called after 6 years with 4-year Treasury at 3% and 50bp premium = call price $109.26. The bondholder is fully compensated for lost coupon income; the issuer only exercises when rates decline enough to justify the premium. Since 1986, some companies issue “bullet bonds” (noncallable-for-life). Sinking fund provisions require periodic retirement; satisfied by cash payment (bonds called by lottery at par) or open-market purchases. Accelerated sinking fund provisions give the issuer the option to retire more than the minimum—exercised when rates decline (call bonds at par below market value). Refunding protection differs from call protection: a bond can be noncallable or nonrefundable. Nonrefundable means the issuer cannot use proceeds of a lower-cost debt issue to retire the bond, but can call it using other funds (asset sales, equity issuance).

Market Structure Crisis

Dealer inventory collapsed 80% since 2007 (higher capital requirements from regulators via Basel III and Volcker Rule) while corporate issuance grew 50% (companies locked in historically low rates). TRACE data (2012): of 37,000 eligible issues, 38% never traded, 23% traded only a few times, 1% traded daily. Average stock trade: $7,000; average IG corporate trade: $500,000. The corporate bond market is fundamentally different from equities: enormous issue count with infrequent trading per issue. Electronic platforms (Tradeweb, MarketAxess) fill the voice-trading vacuum with all-to-all protocols where buy-side provides ~50% of liquidity—a dramatic shift from the dealer-centric model.

Medium-Term Notes & Structured Notes

MTNs are corporate debt offered continuously via shelf registration (Rule 415). Despite the name, maturities range from 9 months to 100+ years (Walt Disney 100-year MTN, 1993). Sold in small amounts with customizable features, often originated via reverse inquiry (investor requests specific terms, issuer accommodates). Structured notes combine MTN issuance with derivative transactions: inverse floaters, equity-linked, commodity-linked, credit-linked, currency-linked. Example: issuer creates an inverse floater for the investor by issuing a regular MTN and entering a pay-float/receive-fixed swap, achieving below-market fixed-rate funding while the investor gets leveraged rate exposure.

Commercial Paper

Short-term unsecured promissory notes, typically <30 days maturity, issued at a discount. Maximum 270 days to avoid SEC registration under the Securities Act of 1933. Federal Reserve discount window eligibility requires ≤90 days, so most issuers prefer short maturities.

Rollover risk is the primary concern: issuers continuously “roll over” maturing paper by selling new paper. If the market freezes—as during the Lehman Brothers collapse in September 2008 when the $1.8 trillion commercial paper market seized up—issuers lose access to their primary funding source. Mitigation: backup bank credit lines (committed but undrawn revolving credit facilities). Three issuer types: captive finance companies (GMAC, Ford Credit), bank-related finance companies, and independent finance companies. Tier-1 paper (rated “1” by ≥2 agencies) is required for money market fund investment under SEC Rule 2a-7. Asset-backed commercial paper (ABCP) is secured by financial assets in an SPV—a significant funding source for off-balance-sheet conduits before 2008.

CLO Structure

Collateralized loan obligations pool leveraged loans into tranches:

  • Class A (80% of deal, AAA, LIBOR+26): first claim on cash flows; protected by 20% subordination
  • Class B (8%, A, LIBOR+75): mezzanine; protected by 12% subordination
  • Class C (3%, BBB, LIBOR+180): junior mezzanine; cushion from D and equity only
  • Class D (2%, BB, LIBOR+475): near-equity risk; thin 7% protection
  • Equity (7%, residual): absorbs first losses, receives excess spread; targets 12–15% returns

Coverage tests (overcollateralization and interest coverage ratios) trigger cash flow diversion from junior to senior if breached—this is the dominant credit enhancement mechanism. The interest waterfall pays trustee fees first, then senior bond interest, then tests, then subordinate bond interest. The principal waterfall covers any interest shortfalls first, then reinvests during the reinvestment period, then repays principal in priority order. Leveraged loans have floating rates, 5–8 year maturity, no call protection, stronger covenants, and higher recovery rates (~70%) than high-yield bonds (~40%).

Default, Recovery, and High-Yield Structures

1981–2012 average default rate for all corporates: ~1.5%; high-yield: 4.26%; investment-grade: 0.11%. Default rates spike during recessions: 11% during 1990–91, 13% in 2009. The aging effect: defaults are most likely 3 years after issuance.

Recovery Rates by Seniority

  • Senior secured loans: ~70% average recovery (highest protection due to collateral)
  • Senior unsecured bonds: ~50% average recovery
  • Senior subordinated bonds: ~35% average recovery
  • Subordinated bonds: ~30% average recovery (lowest priority in liquidation)

Default loss rate = default rate × (1 − recovery rate)—this is the metric that actually matters for portfolio returns. Recovery rates are lower during recessions (42% in 2008) and higher during recoveries (70% in 2013), meaning default losses are doubly concentrated: more defaults occur during periods when recovery rates are also at their lowest.

Deferred-coupon structures (step-up, PIK) arose from 1980s LBO financing where heavy interest burdens required cash-free coupon periods. Extendable reset bonds reset coupon to market rate AND spread. Two categories of high-yield: original-issue HY bonds (rated non-investment-grade at issuance) and fallen angels (downgraded from investment grade due to LBO, recapitalization, or business deterioration). The 1988 RJR Nabisco LBO ($25B) demonstrated event risk: Moody’s dropped RJR from A1 to B3, credit spreads jumped from 100bps to 350bps, and the deal proved that any firm below ~$25B could be an LBO target—spreading credit risk concerns across the entire investment-grade market.

Rating Transition and Downgrade Risk

Rating transition matrices show how ratings change over 1-year periods. Key observation: downgrade probability consistently exceeds upgrade probability for investment-grade bonds. Example: 91.4% of Aa-rated bonds remain Aa after one year; 1.5% upgrade to Aaa, but 0.5% fall to Baa and 0.1% default.

Event risk encompasses sudden, dramatic credit deterioration from:

  • Leveraged buyouts (LBOs): the 1988 RJR Nabisco $25B deal proved any company below ~$25B could be targeted—spreads jumped from 100bps to 350bps overnight
  • Leveraged recapitalizations: company borrows heavily to fund special dividends or share buybacks, enriching equity at bondholders’ expense
  • Regulatory changes: new regulation can render business models unprofitable (e.g., utility deregulation stranding assets)
  • Headline risk: accidents, lawsuits, fraud revelations widen spreads without immediate rating changes—often the market reprices faster than agencies can react

Chapter 9: Municipal Securities

Municipal bond types by security pledge:

  • Unlimited tax GO: full faith and credit, strongest form—backed by income, sales, and property taxes with no cap on taxing authority
  • Limited tax GO: statutory cap on tax rates limits the revenue base available for debt service
  • Double-barreled bonds: combine general taxing power with identified fees or special charges—two revenue sources backing one bond
  • Revenue bonds: backed by specific project revenue with trust indenture, rate covenants, and additional bonds tests. Not backed by taxing power
  • Moral obligation bonds: state morally (not legally) obligated to replenish reserves—legislature must approve the appropriation each session
  • Dedicated tax bonds: backed by specific tax revenue source (e.g., sales tax, tobacco settlement payments)

Revenue Bond Fund Flow

All enterprise revenues flow to a revenue fund, then distribute in priority: (1) operation & maintenance fund (operations have priority over debt service), (2) sinking fund for bondholders per trust indenture, (3) debt service reserve fund (covers future shortfalls), (4) renewal & replacement fund, (5) reserve maintenance fund for extraordinary repairs, (6) surplus fund for discretionary use. The pledge to bondholders is a net revenue pledge—“net” meaning after operating expenses. Rate covenants dictate minimum charges on the service; additional bonds tests restrict further issuance without meeting coverage requirements.

Revenue Bond Types

Airport revenue bonds, higher education bonds, hospital revenue bonds, single-family mortgage revenue bonds, public power bonds, resource recovery bonds, student loan revenue bonds, toll road and gas tax bonds, water revenue bonds, pollution control bonds, and industrial development revenue bonds. Each has sector-specific risk factors: e.g., airport bonds depend on air traffic volume, toll roads on traffic counts, hospital bonds on occupancy rates and reimbursement policies.

Tax Treatment and Yield

Tax-equivalent yield = tax-exempt yield / (1 − marginal tax rate). At 35% marginal rate: 3.4% tax-exempt = 5.23% equivalent taxable; at 45%: 6.18%. Higher tax brackets benefit more. State/local taxes create spread differentials: high-tax states (New York, California) drive down in-state muni yields due to triple tax exemption (federal + state + local). Municipalities cannot tax interest from U.S. Treasury securities, so part of the Treasury-to-corporate spread reflects state/local tax exemption value. The benchmark for muni spreads is the generic AAA GO bond, not Treasuries.

Credit Enhancement and Refunding

Municipal bond insurance: insurer pays principal and interest if issuer defaults. Pre-2007, ~50% of new munis were insured by monoline insurers (no defaults or downgrades). After 2007, monoline involvement in subprime MBS led to insurer downgrades, reducing their role dramatically. Letter-of-credit (LOC) arrangements provide bank backing: direct-pay LOC (strongest—trustee draws without first requesting from issuer), standby LOC, and confirming LOC (correspondent bank wraps smaller bank’s LOC). Pre-refunded bonds are defeased with a portfolio of Treasury securities placed in escrow whose cash flows exactly match remaining debt service—they carry near-Treasury credit quality (AAA). Refunding motivations: (1) eliminate restrictive bond covenants, (2) alter maturity schedule, (3) exploit tax arbitrage when rates decline.

Default History and Municipal Bankruptcies

Default rates are severely underreported: Moody’s found only 113 defaults over nearly 50 years (0.1% cumulative)—but this excludes unrated issues. Broader studies find far more: Appleson, Parsons & Haughwout documented 2,521 defaults (1970–2011); Yang & Abbas found 2,563 defaults in general-purpose local government bonds (2009–2015 alone). Default causes: economic downturns, failed feasibility studies for revenue projects, fraud, mismanagement, and simple unwillingness to pay (debt repudiation).

Five largest municipal bankruptcies: Detroit MI (2013, $18.5B—largest by population), Jefferson County AL (2011, $3.2B—largest by county), Orange County CA (1994, $1.7B), San Bernardino CA (2012, $1B), Stockton CA (2012, $700M). Chapter 9 of the Bankruptcy Act applies specifically to municipal entities: no provision for liquidation (would violate the 10th Amendment), court’s role limited to eligibility determination, plan approval, and implementation oversight.

“When General Motors and American Airlines filed for bankruptcy, nobody invested in corporate bonds was worrying what that meant for their Procter & Gamble and Walmart bonds. But whenever there is an isolated problem with a municipal bond issuer, there’s an overreaction that this means the entire market is in trouble.” —James Evans, Eaton Vance

Pension obligations from unfunded defined benefit plans are an increasing driver of municipal financial stress—Detroit’s $18.2B in long-term debt was nearly half from unfunded retirement benefits. The same structural problem affects both private and public sector sponsors, but municipalities cannot reorganize through Chapter 7 liquidation. Build America Bonds (introduced 2009) and other taxable municipal structures expanded the investor base beyond tax-exempt buyers by providing a federal subsidy to issuers in lieu of tax exemption.

Tobacco Settlement Bonds

In 1998, four largest tobacco companies (Philip Morris, R.J. Reynolds, Brown & Williamson, Lorillard) settled with 46 state attorneys general for $206 billion over 25 years. States and municipalities securitized future payments as tobacco settlement bonds—NYC was first ($709M, November 1999). Credit risk depends entirely on tobacco companies’ ability to make payments, making these bonds sensitive to tobacco industry regulation and consumption trends.

Municipal Variable-Rate Securities

Three types: VRDOs (variable-rate demand obligations, reset daily or weekly, 20–30 year nominal maturity with 7-day put at par), FRNs (1–7 year maturity), and auction-rate securities (long-term maturity, rate set by auction process). Municipal floater/inverse floater pairs are created by placing a fixed-rate muni bond in trust; the floater resets via Dutch auction while the inverse floater receives residual interest. The split ratio determines leverage: a 50/50 split gives the inverse floater double the duration of the underlying fixed-rate bond.

Chapter 10: International Bonds

Global debt securities total $119.7 trillion ($94.5T domestic + $25.2T international). Currency breakdown: USD $50.6T, EUR $20.4T, JPY $12.8T, other $35.8T. The market is divided into three categories:

  • Foreign bonds: issued in a domestic market by a foreign entity, denominated in domestic currency. Named by market: Yankee (U.S.), Samurai (Japan), Bulldog (U.K.), Kangaroo (Australia), Maple (Canada), Matador (Spain)
  • Eurobonds: issued outside the jurisdiction of any single country, underwritten by international syndicates, offered simultaneously to investors in multiple countries. Not subject to a single country’s securities laws
  • Global bonds: trade simultaneously in multiple domestic and Euro markets with large issuance sizes for maximum liquidity

Covered Bonds and Sovereign Analysis

Covered bonds (Pfandbriefe in Germany, dating to 1769) provide dual recourse: claim on the issuer plus a segregated cover pool of high-quality assets (mortgages or public-sector loans). If the issuer fails, the cover pool is ring-fenced from bankruptcy and continues to pay bondholders. They dominate European bank funding and trade at tight spreads due to the dual protection—no covered bond has ever defaulted in over 250 years of history.

Emerging market credit analysis uses three pillars:

  • Serviceability: current payment ability, external debt ratios, foreign currency reserves, current account balance. Short-term focus—can the country pay its bills this year?
  • Solvency: long-term debt sustainability, fiscal trajectory, primary balance, debt-to-GDP trend. Medium-term focus—is the debt path sustainable?
  • Structural fundamentals: institutions, governance quality, rule of law, political stability, economic diversification. Long-term focus—does the country have the capacity to grow out of its debt?

Moody’s rates sovereigns on four factors: economic strength, institutional strength, government financial strength, and susceptibility to event risk. The distinction between external debt (denominated in foreign currency, subject to currency risk) and domestic debt (local currency, can be inflated away) is critical—most sovereign defaults involve external debt because the government cannot print foreign currency to service it.

Currency Risk and Eurobond Mechanics

Non-dollar bond returns have three components: income, capital gain/loss in local currency, and foreign currency gain/loss. Historically, the currency component is the major contributor to portfolio return volatility—often exceeding interest-rate effects.

Currency Hedging

  • Covered interest rate parity: links forward exchange rates to interest-rate differentials between currencies. The forward premium/discount should equal the interest-rate differential. When parity holds, hedging costs exactly eliminate yield advantages of higher-yielding foreign bonds
  • Forward hedging: sell foreign currency forward to lock in exchange rate; eliminates currency risk but also eliminates any potential currency gain
  • Options hedging: buy currency puts for downside protection while retaining upside participation; costs premium but preserves optionality
  • Unhedged: accept full currency exposure; appropriate only when the investor has a strong currency view or long time horizon

The decision to hedge currency risk is one of the most impactful portfolio choices for international bond investors. Empirically, hedged international bond portfolios have lower volatility and higher Sharpe ratios than unhedged portfolios, because currency movements add noise without reliable compensation.

Eurobond market covenants are relatively weak compared to U.S. corporates because geographically diverse investors across different legal jurisdictions find it difficult to agree on protective forms. Eurobonds pay coupons annually (not semiannually) due to higher international distribution costs. Bond-equivalent yield of a Eurodollar bond = 2[(1 + annual YTM)1/2 − 1]—always less than the stated annual YTM due to less frequent compounding.

Part III: Mortgage & Structured Products

Chapter 11: Residential Mortgages

Mortgages face four principal risks: credit (borrower default), liquidity, price (rate changes), and prepayment. LTV ratio is the primary credit risk indicator—higher LTV means less borrower equity cushion.

Mortgage Product Types

  • Fixed-rate level-payment (FRM): constant monthly payment for 15 or 30 years; transfers interest-rate risk to lenders who must fund long-duration assets with shorter-duration liabilities
  • Adjustable-rate (ARM): coupon resets periodically (typically 1-year) based on reference rate + margin; transfers rate risk to borrowers. Features periodic caps/floors (e.g., 2% per reset) and lifetime caps (e.g., 6% over initial rate)
  • Hybrid ARM: fixed for initial period (3, 5, 7, or 10 years), then adjusts annually. The 5/1 ARM is the most popular hybrid structure
  • Interest-only: no principal amortization for initial period; lower payments but slower equity building
  • Negative amortization: minimum payment may be less than interest due; unpaid interest added to principal balance. Payment-option ARMs (allowing borrowers to choose their monthly payment) contributed heavily to the 2008 crisis by enabling borrowers to accrue negative equity
  • Balloon: large lump-sum principal payment at maturity (typically 5 or 7 years); essentially a short-term loan with long-term amortization

The 30-year fixed-rate mortgage remains dominant in the U.S. due to GSE support and prepayment flexibility—a uniquely American product, as most countries rely on shorter-term or variable-rate structures. Conforming loans meet GSE guidelines (loan limits, documentation, LTV); nonconforming/jumbo exceed limits or fail to meet standards and cannot be securitized by agencies.

Strategic Default and Credit Classification

Strategic default became significant 2008–2012: underwater borrowers who could pay chose not to. Nevada saw 60% price declines; approximately 25% of foreclosed homeowners could have afforded their payments but rationally chose default when negative equity exceeded transaction costs of walking away. The credit quality classification distinguishes prime loans (strong credit, full documentation) from subprime loans (impaired credit, higher default probability)—the failure to properly price subprime risk was at the heart of the 2008 crisis.

Mortgage Servicing

The servicer collects monthly payments, manages escrow accounts for taxes and insurance, handles delinquencies, and initiates foreclosure. Servicing rights are a separate asset that can be bought and sold. The servicing fee (typically 25–50 bps of the outstanding balance) is deducted before passing cash flows to MBS investors. When servicers fail—as in the Conseco Finance bankruptcy (2002), where servicing fees increased from 50bps to 115bps by court order—subordinate tranche investors bear the cost through reduced excess spread and potential downgrades.

Chapter 12: Agency MBS Pass-Throughs

Agency pass-throughs distribute monthly principal and interest from mortgage pools to investors. The three guarantors: Ginnie Mae (full faith and credit of U.S. government—issues backed by FHA/VA/USDA mortgages), Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac (GSE guarantee, not explicit government backing—back conventional conforming mortgages). GNMA historically trades at a 4–8 tick premium to Fannie due to the explicit guarantee. Key pool characteristics: WAC (weighted average coupon of underlying mortgages), WAM (weighted average maturity), WALA (weighted average loan age), average loan balance, and FICO score.

Prepayment Modeling

Three prepayment components, each requiring separate modeling:

  • Housing turnover: life events (relocation, divorce, death, upsizing/downsizing)—insensitive to interest rates; relatively stable at 5–7% annually for seasoned pools
  • Cash-out refinancing: borrowers monetize home equity appreciation—somewhat rate-insensitive; driven by home price increases and consumer spending needs
  • Rate/term refinancing: borrowers lower interest cost by refinancing into a lower-rate loan—highly rate-sensitive; the dominant prepayment driver and the source of negative convexity in MBS

The PSA benchmark (Public Securities Association) assumes CPR ramps linearly from 0% to 6% over 30 months then stays flat—purely a market convention, not empirical evidence. It is used only as a reference point for communicating prepayment speeds (e.g., “200 PSA” = 2× the benchmark). SMM (single-monthly mortality rate) measures monthly prepayment speed; CPR = 1 − (1 − SMM)12.

The S-Curve and Burnout

Prepayment rate increases as the WAC/mortgage rate ratio rises, then levels off (the S-curve). At the steep portion, small rate changes produce large prepayment shifts; at the flat portion (ratios above ~1.3), additional rate declines have diminishing impact. Burnout: after prolonged low rates, willing/able refinancers exit the pool, leaving borrowers who cannot or won’t refinance (credit-impaired, insufficient equity, transaction costs). Each additional rate decrease produces less additional prepayment. Average life varies dramatically with PSA: 50 PSA = 15.11 years; 300 PSA = 5.92 years; 700 PSA = 2.78 years for a typical 30-year pass-through.

Negative Convexity and Institutional Impact

Pass-throughs exhibit negative convexity: when rates fall, accelerating prepayments limit price appreciation (contraction risk—average life shortens, investor receives principal back when reinvestment rates are low); when rates rise, extension risk amplifies price decline (average life lengthens, investor is locked into below-market coupon). This makes pass-throughs unattractive for: thrifts (duration mismatch with short-term deposits), insurance companies (guaranteed investment contracts requiring certainty), pension funds (want to lock in rates for liabilities). These institutional limitations drove the creation of CMO structures to redistribute prepayment risk among different investor classes with different tolerances.

TBA Market and Dollar Rolls

TBA (To-Be-Announced) market: forward trading where only six parameters are specified (agency, maturity, coupon, face value, settlement date, price)—specific pools are not identified until two days before settlement. This standardization creates the world’s second-most-liquid market after Treasuries, earning the nickname “Eighth Wonder of the World” during the 2008 financial crisis when, despite GSE conservatorship, TBA trading continued uninterrupted.

UMBS (Uniform MBS, June 2019) unified Fannie/Freddie pass-throughs for better TBA liquidity and fungibility—a major structural improvement. Previously, Fannie and Freddie pools were not interchangeable in TBA settlement, fragmenting liquidity.

Dollar rolls are the MBS equivalent of repo financing: sell a TBA for near-month settlement and simultaneously buy the same TBA for far-month settlement. The roll value = drop (price difference between months) minus foregone interest. When the roll is “special” (trading below implied financing rate), it creates an attractive financing alternative to repo—portfolio managers routinely use dollar rolls to reduce funding costs.

Chapter 13: Collateralized Mortgage Obligations (CMOs)

CMOs redistribute MBS cash flows into tranches with different risk profiles. First created in 1983. They cannot eliminate prepayment risk—only transfer it among bondholders. Issued as REMICs for tax efficiency.

Sequential-Pay Structure

Example FJF-01: $400M collateral, 7.5% coupon, four tranches (A/B/C/D). All tranches receive interest, but principal paid to A until retired (month 81 at 165 PSA), then B (month 100), then C (month 178), then D (month 357). Average lives at 165 PSA: A=3.48yr, B=7.49yr, C=11.19yr, D=20.27yr. Tranches have both shorter and longer lives than the 8.76-year collateral average life.

Z-Bonds (Accrual Tranches)

No current interest payments; interest accrues and adds to principal. Accrued interest diverted to accelerate paydown of earlier tranches. Result: tranche A retires by month 64 (vs. 81 without Z-bond), significantly reducing contraction risk for A. Appeals to investors concerned with reinvestment risk since there are no coupon payments to reinvest until the Z-bond begins paying down.

Floater/Inverse Floater

Created by splitting a fixed-rate tranche. Example: $96.5M tranche split into $72.375M floater (LIBOR+50bp) and $24.125M inverse floater (28.50% − 3×LIBOR). Leverage L=3 means 100bp LIBOR change causes 300bp inverse coupon change. Weighted-average coupon always equals the original 7.5%. Market categories: low-leverage (0.5–2.1), medium (2.1–4.5), high (>4.5). Synthetic coupon tranches (e.g., splitting $1B 6% pass-through into 75% interest / 50% principal A-1 at 9% and 25% interest / 50% principal A-2 at 3%) let investors express views on prepayment speeds.

PAC Bonds

Developed March 1987 after the RJR Nabisco LBO increased demand for predictable cash flows. PAC bonds have a principal repayment schedule satisfied within a prepayment collar (e.g., 90–300 PSA). Support bonds absorb prepayment variability—they are the “bodyguards.”

“The key to the prepayment protection offered by a PAC bond is the amount of support bonds outstanding. If the support bonds are paid off quickly because of faster-than-expected prepayments, there is no longer any protection for the PAC bonds.”

When support bonds are exhausted, the PAC structure effectively becomes sequential-pay. The effective collar changes monthly based on actual prepayment experience. Shorter PACs have wider effective collars (e.g., P-A: 90–450 PSA vs. initial 90–300).

IO and PO Strips

PO strips (principal-only):

  • Receive only principal payments; purchased at substantial discount to par
  • Benefit from faster prepayments (principal returned sooner at par value)
  • When rates decline: PO prices rise sharply (faster cash flows + lower discount rate)—double benefit
  • When rates rise: PO prices fall (slower cash flows + higher discount rate)—double hit
  • Extremely high effective duration and volatility

IO strips (interest-only):

  • Receive only interest payments; no par value—investor wants prepayments to be slow
  • Have negative duration—price tends to move in the same direction as rates
  • When rates rise: slower prepayments preserve the interest stream longer (IO value increases)
  • When rates fall: accelerating prepayments destroy the interest stream (IO value decreases)
  • Useful as hedging instruments against falling rates because of their negative duration

Both IO and PO strips exhibit substantially greater price volatility than the underlying pass-through. Their combined value must always equal the pass-through value (an arbitrage constraint).

Chapter 14: Nonagency RMBS

No government guarantee—requires credit enhancement. Unlike agency CMOs (which have only prepayment tranching), nonagency RMBS have credit tranching where subordinate classes absorb losses before senior classes.

Senior-Subordinated Structure

The dominant credit enhancement form. Subordinate tranches absorb losses first, protecting seniors. The shifting interest mechanism redirects prepayments disproportionately from subordinated to senior classes per a schedule:

  • Years 1–5: 100% of prepayments directed to senior classes (maximum protection during seasoning)
  • Years 6–9: percentage gradually decreases (e.g., 70%, 60%, 40%, 20%)
  • Year 9+: prepayments allocated pro rata to all classes

Step-down triggers (delinquency test and principal loss test) can override the schedule if collateral deteriorates—preventing the premature release of subordination. Post-crisis structures include tighter triggers and longer lockout periods.

Credit Enhancement Forms

  • Structural (internal): senior-subordinated with cash flow waterfall (losses absorbed from bottom up); this is the dominant form
  • Originator-provided: excess spread (collateral WAC minus bond coupon rates minus servicing fees), cash collateral accounts, overcollateralization (par value of assets exceeds par value of bonds)
  • Third-party (external): insurance, letters of credit, guarantees (rarely used post-2007 after monoline insurer downgrades destroyed the business model)

Credit Risk Analysis for ABS

Rating agencies evaluate three risk categories:

  • Asset risks: borrower credit quality, originator experience, concentration limits (too few borrowers eliminates diversification benefit), historical loss curves for the asset class
  • Structural risks: loss allocation, cash flow waterfall design, trigger events for early amortization, reserve fund adequacy
  • Third-party provider risks: servicer capability (collecting payments, managing delinquencies, recovering collateral), trustee oversight, and legal structure (true sale opinion, bankruptcy remoteness)

The Conseco Finance bankruptcy (2002) illustrated servicing risk: the court ordered an increase in servicing fees from 50bps to 115bps, reducing excess spread and causing subordinate tranche downgrades—demonstrating that even well-structured ABS can be undermined by servicer distress.

Default Measurement

CDR (conditional default rate): annualized value of newly defaulted unpaid principal as % of pool balance. First compute MDR (monthly), then CDR = 1 − (1 − MDR)12. PSA SDA benchmark (100 SDA): CDR ramps from 0.02% to 0.60% over 30 months, stays at 0.60% through month 60, declines to 0.03% by month 120, then constant. Post-crisis RMBS 2.0 deals (prime jumbo) and non-QM deals (self-employed, asset-rich borrowers) have simpler structures than legacy subprime deals. Dodd-Frank (2010) requires 5% risk retention (“skin in the game”) for securitization sponsors—aligning originator and investor interests—except for “qualified residential mortgages.”

Chapter 15: Commercial MBS (CMBS)

Key metrics: DSC ratio (net operating income / debt service)—lenders typically require DSC ≥ 1.25×; LTV typically ≤ 75%. Commercial mortgages differ fundamentally from residential: they are nonrecourse (borrower liability limited to property—lender’s only claim is the property itself), and borrowers are evaluated primarily on property cash flow rather than personal income.

Property Types and Risk Profiles

  • Multifamily: apartment buildings; most stable cash flows, lowest default rates (essential housing need); favored by GSEs (Fannie/Freddie have dedicated multifamily programs)
  • Office: long lease terms (5–10 years) provide cash flow stability; risk from tenant concentration and economic cycle sensitivity (vacancy rates spike in recessions)
  • Retail: regional malls, strip centers, single-tenant; anchor tenant creditworthiness is critical; co-tenancy clauses allow smaller tenants to renegotiate if anchors leave
  • Industrial/warehouse: lower construction costs, easier re-tenanting; growing demand from e-commerce logistics
  • Hotel/hospitality: highest volatility (daily “lease” renewal); most cyclically sensitive property type; revenue per available room (RevPAR) is the key metric
  • Healthcare: nursing homes, assisted living; regulatory risk (Medicare/Medicaid reimbursement changes) adds unique dimension

Call Protection

Four forms, from strongest to weakest:

  1. Lockout: no prepayment allowed for specified years (strongest protection)
  2. Defeasance: borrower substitutes a portfolio of Treasury securities generating identical cash flows—the loan continues but credit risk vanishes. Expensive for borrower but clean for bondholder
  3. Penalty points: prepayment premium as percentage of outstanding balance (e.g., 5-4-3-2-1 declining)
  4. Yield maintenance: make-whole premium based on Treasury spread—compensates bondholder for lost income

Balloon risk: commercial mortgages typically have 5–10 year terms with 25–30 year amortization schedules, creating large principal payments at maturity that require refinancing. If credit markets tighten at maturity, the borrower may be unable to refinance—balloon risk was a major concern during 2008–2012 when CMBS delinquency rates exceeded 9%.

Servicer Hierarchy

Three servicer types with distinct roles:

  • Master servicer: routine administration—collecting payments, managing tax/insurance escrows, reporting to trustees. Advances delinquent payments to bondholders if deemed recoverable
  • Primary servicer: day-to-day borrower interface, handling payment processing and communications
  • Special servicer: activated for workouts and distressed assets when loans default or face imminent default. Makes critical decisions on foreclosure vs. loan modification vs. maturity extension

The special servicer role creates potential conflicts of interest: special servicers often hold the “B-piece” (first-loss tranche), giving them economic incentives that may not align with senior tranche holders. Their decisions on foreclosure timing and loan workout terms directly affect bondholder recovery across all tranches. CMBS deals can be single-borrower/multiproperty or multi-borrower (conduit) structures.

Chapter 16: Asset-Backed Securities

Securitization creates a true sale to a special-purpose vehicle, legally separating assets from originator bankruptcy risk. This is the foundational legal requirement—the SPV cannot engage in other activities and exists solely to hold the assets and issue securities. The originator benefits from potentially lower funding costs (SPV credit quality can exceed the originator’s), while investors get direct claim on asset cash flows independent of originator health.

ABS Credit Enhancement Types

  • Internal (structural): senior-subordinated tranching with cash flow waterfall; losses absorbed from bottom up
  • Overcollateralization: par value of collateral exceeds par value of issued bonds (excess provides first-loss cushion)
  • Excess spread: collateral WAC minus bond coupon rates minus servicing fees; acts as ongoing credit enhancement
  • Cash reserve fund: upfront cash deposit to cover initial losses before excess spread accumulates
  • External (third-party): bond insurance, letters of credit, guarantees—rarely used post-2007 after monoline insurer failures

Dodd-Frank (2010) requires 5% risk retention (“skin in the game”) for securitization sponsors—aligning originator and investor interests. The retained risk cannot be hedged or transferred. Qualified residential mortgages are exempt from this requirement.

Auto Loan ABS

Most predictable prepayments of any ABS sector—driven by sales/trade-ins, repossessions, and vehicle loss rather than interest-rate refinancing. Prepayment measured by ABS (absolute prepayment speed): monthly prepayment as % of original collateral amount, related to SMM by ABS = SMM / [1 + SMM × (M − 1)] where M = months after origination. Captive finance companies (Ford Motor Credit, DaimlerChrysler Financial, Caterpillar Financial Services) are typical issuers. Auto ABS are widely used as the benchmark for ABS pricing. Deal structures include multiple sequential tranches (A-1 through A-4, B, C, D) with credit enhancement via subordination, overcollateralization, reserve funds, and excess spread.

Credit Card Receivable ABS

Issuers include banks (Visa, MasterCard), retailers (JCPenney, Sears), and travel companies (American Express). Revolving structure with lockout/revolving period (18 months to 10 years): during this period, principal payments are reinvested in new receivables to maintain pool size. Cash flow components: finance charge collections, principal collections, and fees. Pay-out events (early amortization) trigger if portfolio performance deteriorates—bond classes then retire sequentially. Key performance metrics: gross portfolio yield, charge-off rate, net portfolio yield (critical—bondholders receive interest from this), delinquency percentages (30/60/90 day), and monthly payment rate (extremely low MPR signals extension risk and potential early amortization).

Rate Reduction Bonds

Also called stranded cost bonds or competitive transition charge (CTC) bonds. Created during electric utility deregulation when utilities couldn’t recover costs of pre-deregulation assets. The CTC is designated by state legislature as a statutory property right—a legislated asset that can be sold to an SPV and securitized. A true-up mechanism periodically recomputes the CTC based on actual vs. projected collections, providing cash flow stability and credit enhancement. Result: extremely stable cash flows with near-Treasury credit quality, backed by regulatory authority guarantees. Notable niche: securities backed by music royalty receivables from artists like David Bowie, Ashford & Simpson, and James Brown.

Chapter 17: Collective Investment Vehicles

Benefits of collective vehicles vs. direct bond purchase:

  • Diversification: a single fund provides access to hundreds or thousands of bonds across sectors, maturities, and credit qualities—impossible for individual investors to replicate
  • Liquidity: fund shares trade daily (or intraday for ETFs) even when underlying bonds trade infrequently
  • Professional management: access to credit analysis, risk management, and trading infrastructure
  • Institutional benefits: access to sectors lacking in-house expertise, ability to create leverage and short positions, regulatory compliance infrastructure

Investment Company Types

Open-end funds (mutual funds): NAV determined once daily at close; shares issued and redeemed at NAV; number of shares varies. Closed-end funds: fixed number of shares trading on exchanges; price can differ from NAV (premium or discount). BlackRock analysis identifies factors driving closed-end fund discounts: current yield relative to peers, dividend changes, fund performance, sector outlook, investor sentiment, and availability of comparable products. Multi-class fee structures: Class A (front-end sales load, lower ongoing fees), Class B (no front-end load, contingent deferred sales load), Class C (12b-1 fees, lower loads). Expense ratio = management fee + 12b-1 fee + other annual expenses.

Bond Fund Categories

Taxable funds include government bond, investment-grade, high-yield (≥65% below BBB), inflation-protected (TIPS), bank loan (floating-rate), multi-sector, and convertible bond funds. Duration classifications: long-term (>6), intermediate (3.5–6), short-term (1–3.5), ultrashort (<1). Specialized structures: inverse bond funds (short the benchmark), leveraged bond funds (multiples of benchmark return), target-term trusts (closed-end funds with termination date). Tax-exempt municipal funds include national, state-specific (for triple tax exemption), and high-yield muni funds.

ETFs

ETFs dominate bond fund innovation with several advantages over traditional mutual funds:

  • Intraday trading: buy/sell throughout the trading day (vs. once daily at close for mutual funds)
  • Tax efficiency: in-kind creation/redemption mechanism avoids triggering taxable events for remaining shareholders (mutual funds must sell securities to meet redemptions, generating capital gains for all holders)
  • Price transparency: creation/redemption keeps prices near NAV—unlike closed-end funds with persistent discounts
  • Lower expense ratios: passively managed bond ETFs typically have expense ratios 10–30bps lower than comparable mutual funds

First-generation ETFs are indexed/beta products; second-generation ETFs are actively managed alpha products. However, tracking error increases with benchmark illiquidity. During the 2008 crisis, high-yield ETF tracking errors exploded to 5–6% as the creation/redemption mechanism strained under massive selling pressure and authorized participants withdrew from the market—revealing that bond ETF liquidity is ultimately constrained by the liquidity of the underlying bonds.

Hedge Fund Bond Strategies

Convergence trading: exploit historical yield relationships (e.g., on-the-run vs. off-the-run Treasury spread). Distressed investing: buy undervalued bonds near bankruptcy. Notable examples: Detroit pension bonds at 41 cents on the dollar; Puerto Rico 8% bonds at 93 (8.727% yield)—hedge funds bought 70% of the $3.5B issue immediately after junk downgrade, recognizing the forced-selling dynamic created value. Fund fees include management fees plus incentive allocations (performance-based compensation). For mutual funds, total annual operating expenses (the expense ratio) is the all-in cost measure investors should focus on.

Part IV: Analysis & Valuation

Chapter 18: Bond Liquidity & Trading

Liquidity has five dimensions: tightness (low transaction costs), immediacy (fast execution), depth (many participants), breadth (sufficient volume to execute large trades), and resiliency (absorb shocks without persistent price impact). Trading costs include explicit (bid-ask spread, commissions) and hidden (market impact, timing, opportunity cost). Accurate pricing data is essential both for trading decisions and for marking bond holdings to market—pricing services provide evaluated prices (model prices, not traded prices) for the vast majority of bonds that don’t trade daily.

Trade Process

Four parts: (1) sources of order flow and (2) trade execution occur in the “front office”; (3) clearance and (4) settlement occur in the “back office” (operations/post-trade functions). Straight Through Processing (STP) automates pre- and post-trade services, eliminating redundant order flow processes and reducing settlement risk.

Evaluated pricing is essential because most bonds don’t trade daily. Pricing services (Bloomberg, ICE, Refinitiv) use matrix pricing—deriving a bond’s price from observed trades in comparable securities adjusted for credit quality, maturity, coupon, and liquidity differences. For portfolio valuation, pricing accuracy is critical: a 25bp pricing error on a $100M position is a $250K mismark, distorting NAV calculations, performance reporting, and risk measurements.

Electronic Trading Revolution

The traditional intermediation model (broker-dealers as principals taking inventory risk) has been troubled since 2008 due to increased risk and decreased profitability from higher capital requirements. Five electronic platform types have emerged:

  1. Auction: competitive bidding for specific bonds (used for new issues and block trades)
  2. Cross-matching: matching buy and sell orders anonymously (minimizes market impact)
  3. Interdealer: dealer-to-dealer platforms for inventory management
  4. Multi-dealer (Tradeweb, MarketAxess): multiple dealers compete for investor order flow
  5. Single-dealer: proprietary platforms from individual banks

All-to-all platforms are the most recent innovation—connecting dealers, institutional investors, sell-side firms, and other participants on a centralized venue. The buy-side provides ~50% of liquidity; portfolio/basket trading enabled. Two trading models: RFQ (request for quote, dominant) and order-driven (order placement matched against posted quotes, growing).

“Major corporate bond dealers are not going anywhere and will continue to be at the center of risk transfer in this market. Their balance sheets, data, expertise, and role as new-issue underwriters ensure they are critical partners for asset managers and hedge funds. However, these same dealers have reduced their capital commitment to this business, creating a liquidity gap that still needs filling.” — Greenwich Associates study

TRACE (Trade Reporting and Compliance Engine) provides mandatory post-trade transparency for corporate bonds, enabling investors to see actual transaction prices. SEC Rule 22e-4 requires mutual funds to implement liquidity risk management programs with formal classification of holdings by liquidity tier. Despite transparency improvements, the structural liquidity gap remains: dealers reduced capital commitment 80%, yet they remain essential for risk transfer due to balance sheets, data, expertise, and new-issue underwriting roles. The Bloomberg Barclays Liquidity Cost Score (LCS) estimates round-trip transaction costs for each bond—a key tool for portfolio construction and liquidity budgeting.

Chapter 19: Embedded Options Analysis

Static spread (zero-volatility spread/Z-spread) is the constant spread over the entire Treasury spot rate curve that equates PV of cash flows to price. Unlike the traditional yield spread (a single point on the Treasury curve), the static spread compares the corporate bond to an entire portfolio of zero-coupon Treasuries matching its cash flow timing. Example: 8.8% 25-year corporate at $87.08; risk-free replication costs $96.61. Iterating: 100bp spread gives $88.55, 110bp gives $87.80, 120bp gives $87.08 (correct). The traditional spread was only 109bp—an 11bp understatement. The difference grows with yield curve steepness and is considerably greater for sinking fund bonds and MBS.

Bond Decomposition and Negative Convexity

  • Callable bond = noncallable bond − call option price (bondholder sold the call to the issuer)
  • Putable bond = nonputable bond + put option price (bondholder bought the put from the issuer)
  • Convertible bond = straight bond + call option on issuer’s stock (bondholder purchased the equity call)
  • Higher volatility → lower callable bond value (larger option subtracted) but higher putable bond value (larger option added)
  • The option cost in spread terms = Z-spread − OAS. For callable bonds this is positive (bondholder gave up spread to compensate for the sold call); for putable bonds this is negative (bondholder received less spread because the purchased put has value)

Callable bonds exhibit two disadvantages: reinvestment risk (issuer calls when yields fall below coupon, forcing reinvestment at lower rates) and price compression (limited price appreciation as rates decline because the market prices in expected call). Below a critical yield y*, callable bonds display negative convexity—price appreciation is less than price depreciation for equal-sized yield changes, the opposite of option-free bonds’ desirable positive convexity.

A bond can rationally trade above its call price. Example: 10-year 13% bond callable in 1 year at 104, with 1-year yield at 5%. Investors treat it as a 1-year bond: PV of two cash flows ($6.50 in 6 months + $110.50 in 1 year at 2.5% semiannual) = $111.52. It’s rational to pay $111.52 for a bond that will be called at $104 because the coupon income during the remaining year more than compensates for the call price difference.

Binomial Interest-Rate Tree

The arbitrage-free binomial model (Black, Derman & Toy) prices bonds with embedded options by modeling rate evolution as a lognormal random walk: rH = rL × e2σ. The tree is calibrated to on-the-run issue prices via iterative search for each year’s lower rate. At each node, the value is the average of discounted up-state and down-state values (50/50 probability). For callable bonds: if calculated value exceeds call price at any node, replace with call price. For putable bonds: if calculated value is below put price, replace with put price.

Three model types by security structure: (1) multiple exercise dates (e.g., 10-year corporate callable every anniversary starting year 3)—the standard binomial approach; (2) single exercise date (e.g., Fannie/Freddie callable on a specific date); (3) MBS/ABS—requires Monte Carlo simulation (Chapter 20) due to path dependency. The one-factor model assumes all rate movements driven solely by the short-term rate—a simplification, but sufficient for most embedded option analysis.

Calibration Example

Year 1: trial r1 = 4.5% gives tree value $99.567 vs. market $100 (too high rate). Correct r1 = 4.074% produces $100. With σ = 10%, r1,H = 4.074% × e0.20 = 4.976%. Year 2: r2 = 4.530% calibrates the 3-year bond. The resulting tree fairly prices all on-the-run issues used in construction (arbitrage-free). Example: 5.25% callable bond (callable at $100 in 1 year) values at $101.432 vs. noncallable $102.075, implying call option = $0.643. For a putable bond at the same terms: when node values fall below $100, replace with $100 (put exercise). Putable bond value: $102.523; put option value: $0.448 (bondholder purchased this option from the issuer).

Volatility Impact

For four 5% 10-year callable bonds with different call protection periods: option-free bond price remains unchanged regardless of assumed volatility (no embedded option affected). Longer deferred call → higher price (closer to option-free value). Callable bond price moves inversely to volatility: higher volatility → lower callable bond price. This is because the call option the bondholder sold becomes more valuable with higher volatility, reducing the callable bond’s value. Modeling risk is significant—if the volatility assumption is wrong, the output is wrong. Stress testing across multiple volatility scenarios is essential.

OAS and Effective Duration

  • OAS = spread added to every rate on the tree making model value equal market price; “option-adjusted” because cash flows already reflect option exercise decisions. Critical to know which on-the-run issues built the tree (Treasury → OAS includes credit spread; issuer curve → OAS reflects only richness/cheapness). Higher assumed volatility → lower OAS; lower volatility → higher OAS
  • Option value in spread terms = static spread − OAS (positive for callable bonds, negative for putable bonds)
  • Effective duration: shift all yields, rebuild the tree, add OAS, compute new prices; formula: (P₋ − P₊) / [2P₀Δy]. Differences from modified duration can be dramatic: callable bond modified duration 5, effective duration 3; highly leveraged MBS modified duration 7, effective duration 50. The five-step procedure: (1) calculate OAS, (2) shift on-the-run yield curve up by small basis points, (3) construct new binomial tree, (4) add OAS to each short rate, (5) determine P₊ from the adjusted tree. Repeat with downward shift for P₋
  • Effective convexity: same approach, using (P₊ + P₋ − 2P₀) / [P₀(Δy)²]. Standard convexity is inappropriate for embedded options. Example: a PO class has standard convexity near zero but effective convexity of 2,155—meaning a 100bp yield change produces a 21.6% price change from convexity alone
“Duration is a by-product of a pricing model. If the pricing model is poor, the resulting duration estimate is poor.”

Implementation Challenges

Finer node spacing is required for short-dated securities, but fine spacing becomes computationally inefficient for 30-year bonds. Time-dependent node spacing can distort the volatility term structure. Managing cash flows that fall between node lines requires interpolation. Approximation formulas work for any bond type: if cash flows don’t change with rates → modified duration and standard convexity; if cash flows change with rates (embedded options) → effective duration and effective convexity.

Chapter 20: RMBS Analysis (Monte Carlo)

MBS cash flows are path-dependent: this month’s prepayment depends on the entire history of rates, not just the current level (burnout effect). Unlike corporate bonds (where the refunding decision depends only on current rates), mortgage prepayments depend on prior refinancing opportunities since origination. CMO tranches have two sources of path dependency: (1) collateral prepayments are path-dependent, and (2) each tranche’s current-month cash flow depends on outstanding balances of other tranches (which require the full prepayment history to calculate). This rules out binomial trees and requires Monte Carlo simulation.

Static Cash Flow Yield Limitations

The simpler approach computes cash flow yield at a given PSA speed. Two fundamental shortcomings parallel YTM’s flaws: (1) assumes reinvestment at the cash flow yield rate, and (2) assumes the RMBS is held until final payout based on the prepayment assumption. Vector analysis partially overcomes the static PSA assumption by allowing prepayment speeds to change over time—e.g., 165 PSA for months 1–36, then different speeds for subsequent periods. Static spread (Z-spread) is also computable, but still assumes rates won’t change—an unrealistic assumption for securities whose cash flows are driven by rate movements.

Monte Carlo Methodology

  • Generate 512–1,024 interest-rate paths based on today’s term structure + volatility assumption; each month produces a discount rate and a refinancing rate
  • Refinancing rate feeds the prepayment model, generating path-specific cash flows. For nonagency deals, involuntary prepayments (defaults) must also be modeled with recovery rate and timing assumptions
  • Spot rates on each path derived from simulated monthly rates: zT(n) = {[1+f1(n)][1+f2(n)]…[1+fT(n)]}1/T − 1
  • Theoretical value = average PV across all paths. Scenarios are normalized so the average simulated zero-coupon Treasury bond price equals today’s actual price (arbitrage-free)
  • OAS = spread K added to all spot rates on all paths making average PV = market price
  • Option cost = static spread − OAS (tranche option cost is more stable than OAS in response to small market movements—a useful computational shortcut)
  • Distribution matters: PAC bond (value 88, std dev 1) vs. support bond (value 77, std dev 10)—same OAS but radically different risk profiles. The OAS alone is insufficient; one must examine the distribution of path values and the range/standard deviation of average lives across paths
  • Variance reduction techniques can cut required paths, allowing price estimates within a tick. Very sensitive CMO tranches may need >1,024 paths for one-tick precision

The Asset Manager’s Objective

Figure out how collateral value transmits to CMO tranches. Find where value goes and where risk goes. Every deal usually has tranches with low risk and high value (good combination), but the bad news: every deal also has tranches with low value and high risk. The key challenge is obtaining a good CMO structuring model to accurately project cash flow allocation across tranches.

FactSet Example (FNMA 3.5% 30-Year)

Fannie Mae 3.5% 30-year single-family MBS (2019 vintage): gross WAC 4.38%, current WAM 345 months, FICO 742, LTV 81%. Z-spread 84.53 bps; OAS 90.25 bps (lognormal model) / 92.98 bps (normal model); option cost 30.30 bps / 27.57 bps. Modified duration 2.564; effective duration 2.171 (lognormal) / 2.580 (normal); effective convexity −0.609 / −0.098. Spread duration: 3.195. For CMO FHLMC.4563 ($125M Freddie Mac deal, sequential-pay): bond class A has 0.02 effective duration, Z-bond has 11.24 effective duration and highest OAS (228.4 bps). The VB tranche is the only class with positive effective convexity (2.99).

Prepayment Sensitivity and Duration Types

Prepayment sensitivity = basis point change in RMBS price for a 1% increase in prepayments. Example: at 300 PSA, price = 106.10; at 303 PSA, price = 106.01; sensitivity = −9 (adversely affected by faster prepayments). Various duration measures exist for RMBS: model durations (modified, cash flow, effective) and market-based durations (empirical/regression-based, hedging/price-model, coupon curve/roll-up-roll-down). Coupon curve duration (Douglas Breeden): substitute prices from rolling up and down the coupon curve into the duration formula. Example: 4.0% coupon pass-through with 50bp yield decline produces current coupon duration of 4.14. Limitation: only works for generic RMBS and TBA pass-throughs, not for CMO tranches or derivatives. Total return analysis remains the correct ultimate performance measure.

Chapter 21: Convertible Bonds

A convertible grants the right to convert into a predetermined number of shares (conversion ratio). An exchangeable bond gives the right to exchange for stock of a different firm. Conversion price = par / conversion ratio ($1,000 / 50 = $20 per share). Minimum value = max(conversion value, straight bond value). If the convertible trades below straight value, arbitrage opportunity exists (buy bond, short stock, convert to cover). If it trades below conversion value, buy and immediately convert for profit.

Key Analytics

  • Market conversion price = market price of convertible / conversion ratio. Example: $950 / 50 = $19
  • Market conversion premium per share = market conversion price − current stock price ($19 − $17 = $2)—this is the price of the embedded call option
  • Premium payback period = market conversion premium / favorable income differential per share = years to recover premium through convertible’s higher income vs. stock dividends. Example: $2 / $1 = 2 years. Does not account for time value of money
  • Premium over straight value = (market price / straight value) − 1. Higher premium = less attractive. Key flaw: straight value moves with interest rates (rising rates lower the floor), so downside protection is not fixed

Risk-Return Trade-Off

If stock rises from $17 to $34: direct stock purchase returns 100%, convertible returns ~79% (investor paid $2 premium per share). If stock falls to $7: stock loses 59%, but convertible floors at straight value ($788), losing only 17%. The trade-off: limited upside participation (must pay premium) in exchange for downside protection (straight value floor). But the floor is a moving floor—a sharp stock decline may signal credit deterioration, lowering the straight value simultaneously.

Special Provisions

Net share settlement (cash-par settlement): issuer pays par in cash, difference above par in stock shares—$120B of $171B U.S. convertibles contained this provision before 2008 accounting changes. Contingent convertible (CoCo): conversion only when stock exceeds ~130% of conversion price for specified days—by 2003 accounted for 70%+ of new issuance. Contingent payment (CoPa): higher coupon if stock reaches specified threshold.

Option Greeks for Converts

  • Delta: sensitivity to stock price (0 = straight bond, 1 = mirrors stock); also the hedge ratio for market-neutral positioning. Example: delta 0.60 with conversion ratio 50 → short 30 shares for delta-neutral position. Delta changes over time—short position must be continuously adjusted
  • Gamma: change in delta as stock moves (analogous to convexity; highest for at-the-money converts). High gamma requires frequent rebalancing
  • Vega: sensitivity to implied volatility; if implied vol < historical, the embedded option is cheap—opportunity for volatility trading

Profiles and Arbitrage

Profiles: equity-sensitive (deep in-the-money, delta >75%, stock price movements dominate), balanced (conversion premium 15–40%, delta 55–75%, appeal to both convertible and equity funds), busted (deep out-of-the-money, high conversion premium, trades like straight bond, valued by yield/spread changes), distressed (busted + near bankruptcy, credit rating lowered or expected to be). Empirical duration (regression-based) is essential for converts because analytical duration captures only the bond component, not the equity sensitivity.

“Increased debt market volatility has driven home the point of duration risk inherent in any security with a fixed income component, including converts. The increased volatility of the spreads has heightened investor sensitivity to the reliability of the fixed income floor or bond value of the convert.” — Mihir Bhattacharya

Call risk: issuers force conversion when stock reaches conversion value—good for the issuer (retiring debt), bad for bondholders (lose the premium). Takeover risk: if issuer is acquired, stock may no longer trade, leaving the investor with a bond paying below-market coupon. Stefanini identified eight arbitrage strategies: (1) cash flow arbitrage (long convertible + short stock = market neutral, capture income differential), (2) volatility trading (nondirectional, profits from stock volatility regardless of direction), (3) gamma trading (take position based on expected delta change for additional income), (4) credit arbitrage, (5) skewed arbitrage, (6) carry trade, (7) refinancing play, (8) late-stage restructuring play. Desirable attributes: good bond liquidity, low conversion premium, high convexity, low implied volatility, high stock volatility, easily borrowable stock, low/no dividends.

Chapter 22: Corporate Credit Analysis

Three core areas: covenants (limits on management discretion), collateral (assets available in default), and ability to pay (business + governance + financial risk). Credit risk = default risk + credit spread risk + downgrade risk. Many “experienced” investors skip the foundational step of understanding the business model—the Sino-Forest case demonstrates why this is catastrophic.

Business Model Analysis

The three fundamental questions before any financial analysis: What does the company do? Where do revenues come from? Where does it incur costs?

Porter’s Five Forces provides the industry framework:

  1. Competitive rivalry: number and strength of existing competitors; price wars erode margins
  2. Threat of new entrants: barriers to entry (capital requirements, regulation, brand loyalty, economies of scale)
  3. Bargaining power of suppliers: concentrated suppliers can squeeze margins
  4. Bargaining power of buyers: concentrated customers can negotiate lower prices
  5. Threat of substitutes: alternative products that can replace the company’s offerings

Industry analysis must be relative—20% annual growth seems attractive in isolation, but if the industry is growing at 45%, the company appears competitively weak. Key dimensions: economic cyclicality, growth prospects, R&D dependence, market structure (monopoly/oligopoly/competitive), regulatory direction (focus on regulatory change, not presence/absence), and labor situation (unionization, strike history).

Corporate Governance Risk

Examines the principal-agent relationship between management and shareholders. Corporate scandals showed breakdown of internal controls destroyed shareholder wealth. Two mitigation categories:

  • Align interests: grant management equity interest, link compensation to stock performance, clawback provisions for accounting restatements
  • Internal control systems: majority of independent directors, committees with only independent directors, separation of CEO/chairman roles, independent audit committee

S&P Corporate Governance Score ranges from 10 (highest) to 1 (lowest). Moody’s evaluates six management quality factors: strategic direction, financial philosophy, conservatism, track record, succession planning, and control systems.

Bondholder-specific governance concerns differ from equity holders: shareholders want management to take risk (upside is unlimited), while bondholders want management to be conservative (upside is capped at par + coupon). This tension means strong “shareholder-friendly” governance can actually be bondholder-unfriendly—aggressive share buybacks funded by debt, special dividends, and leveraged acquisitions all increase equity value at bondholders’ expense.

Covenant Analysis

Affirmative covenants: promises to do things (maintain insurance, file reports). Negative covenants: restrictions on actions (debt incurrence limits, restricted payments, limitations on liens, asset sale restrictions). Two test types: maintenance tests (ratios must be met every reporting date, more stringent) and debt incurrence tests (ratios tested only when issuing new debt, less stringent). Subsidiary restrictions classify subsidiaries as restricted (consolidated for financial tests) or unrestricted (often foreign or special-purpose, excluded from parent covenants). Carve-outs weaken covenants by allowing exceptions—these loopholes often reveal management’s true intentions.

Financial Risk Ratios

  • Interest coverage: EBIT / total interest (use gross interest expense, not net of capitalized interest/interest income); EBITDA version always higher but masks capex needs. Fixed-charge coverage adds other fixed obligations (lease payments, contingent liabilities). If pretax interest ratio <1×, the company must borrow or use cash/assets to meet interest payments
  • Leverage: total debt / capitalization; total debt / EBITDA; total debt / EBIT. Supplement with market value of equity. Operating leases should be capitalized to show true leverage. Margin of safety = percentage by which operating income could decline and still cover fixed obligations
  • Cash flow hierarchy (S&P): operating → free operating (minus capex) → discretionary (minus dividends) → prefinancing (minus debt repayment). Critically distinguish maintenance vs. growth capex. S&P defines prefinancing cash flow as “extent to which company cash flow from all internal sources has been sufficient to cover all internal needs”
  • Working capital: current ratio, acid test (cash + securities + receivables / current liabilities); analyze days receivable and payable trends for deterioration signals. Companies may have receivable financing with recourse provisions—this distorts comparisons
  • Debt maturity structure: what percentage of debt is due in the next 5 years? Evaluate bank lines of credit: undrawn capacity, security interests, and the “material adverse change” clause (allows bank to withdraw the credit line)
  • Net assets to debt: consider liquidation value (differs dramatically from book value). Enterprise value = market value of equity + total debt − cash. Account for intangible assets, pension liabilities, plant condition

The Investment Decision

“Don’t trade safety for yield—credit risk is nonlinear. Junior debt may offer extra yield, but adverse changes can produce losses 10× the yield difference.”

Risk/reward is often asymmetric in high-yield investing. If price drops significantly below intrinsic value, the asymmetry can reverse: low price becomes the ultimate source of margin for error. The high-yield equity analysis approach (Stephen Esser): “as equity values go up, so does the equity cushion beneath the company’s debt. All else being equal, the bonds become better credits.” For high-yield bonds, dynamic equity-oriented analysis can either validate or contradict traditional credit analysis.

Case Study: Sino-Forest Corporation

Failed the business model test: claimed to be a “leading commercial forest plantation operator” but was really a middleman (of middlemen) buying and selling trees through 4–5 authorized intermediaries (wood dealers). Never dealt directly with upstream suppliers or downstream customers. Even for the ~10% of planted plantations, the company engaged third parties for all field operations. Despite double-digit revenue growth for 15 years and 69% EBITDA margins, never generated positive FCF. Enterprise value C$7.2B in March 2011; bankruptcy filed March 2012—bondholders expected 10–15% recovery.

Five Forces: homogeneous products (commodities), fierce competition, low barriers to entry, no bargaining power over limited suppliers or consumers. As Warren Buffett said: “If today you gave me $20 billion to create a rival against Coca-Cola, I couldn’t figure out a way to do it”—but for Sino-Forest, financial resources alone offered no competitive edge because the business model itself was questionable. Three CRAs (BB-category) unanimously failed to warn investors—they rushed to downgrade only after the stock had already plunged.

Case Study: Sirius XM Holdings

The 5.25% 2022 bonds were the only tranche with standard high-yield covenants. Company wanted to increase leverage from 2.5× to 4.0× to return cash to equity holders (John Malone/Liberty Media, owning >50%, is known for preferring 4–5× leverage). Constrained by the 2022 bonds’ restricted payments covenant (no unrestricted payments above 3.5× leverage). Creative solution: granted security to 2022 bonds, triggering investment-grade upgrade by all three CRAs and covenant suspension (high-yield covenants automatically suspended when rated investment-grade by two or more agencies). Result: $4 immediate price jump on 2022 bonds; company issued $1.5B new debt within a month. The 2022 bonds outperformed other unsecured bonds by 150–200bps purely because of covenant protection. The business model was attractive: monopoly satellite radio, 26M subscribers, subscription-based revenue (85%), EBITDA margins ~33%, maintenance capex <5% of revenue, ~$1B annual FCF—but management’s shareholder-friendly financial policy meant bondholders needed covenant protection against aggressive leverage.

Chapter 23: Credit Risk Modeling

Structural Models (Merton Framework)

Equity = call option on firm assets with strike = debt face value. Default occurs when asset value falls below debt at maturity. Bond value = assets minus call option sold to stockholders (equivalently, risk-free bond minus put option).

  • Merton (1974): foundational model; single debt maturity, default only at maturity. Limitation: no early default, requires observable asset value
  • Geske compound option: extends to multiple debt maturities as sequential contingent events—each maturity is an option on the option at the next maturity
  • Black-Cox first-passage time: default whenever asset value crosses a barrier, not just at maturity—modeled as a barrier option; more realistic for firms that can default between payment dates
  • Shimko-Tejima-van Deventer: adds stochastic interest rates to the structural framework, capturing the interaction between credit and rate risk

All structural models share a fundamental limitation: they require the market value of assets, which is unobservable (balance sheet assets ≠ economic value). Practitioners must infer asset value from equity prices using iterative calibration—computationally burdensome and sensitive to assumptions about asset volatility.

Reduced-Form Models

Jarrow-Turnbull, Duffie-Singleton: default as exogenous Poisson process with time-varying intensity λ (conditional probability of default per unit time). No assumption about cause of default—simplification that allows bonds with different maturities to be priced independently.

  • Jarrow-Turnbull (JT): default intensity calibrated from market credit spreads; recovery assumed to occur only at maturity as a fraction of par value
  • Jarrow-Lando-Turnbull (JLT): extends JT to multiple credit ratings with migration probabilities from rating transition matrices; default intensity varies by current rating
  • Duffie-Singleton (DS): addresses JT limitations—recovery can occur anytime (not just maturity) and is a fixed fraction of the pre-default bond price (fractional recovery model). More flexible and widely adopted in practice

Reduced-form models are favored by 90%+ of practitioners because they avoid the asset value estimation problem and quickly calibrate to market credit spreads. They enable construction of credit curves—term structures of default probability that allow relative value analysis across the maturity spectrum.

Incomplete-Information Models and Empirical Evidence

Giesecke-Goldberg: structural/reduced-form hybrid where investors don’t know the true default barrier (accounts for corporate fraud like Sino-Forest, Enron, WorldCom). Used by MSCI Barra. Hilscher-Wilson: credit ratings are inaccurate default probability measures—simple reduced-form models consistently outperform ratings for forecasting corporate failure. Kamakura Corporation BAC analysis (June 2014): 90%+ of sophisticated users chose reduced-form approach. Of 431 bonds analyzed, BAC’s 15 issues ranked in the bottom 17% on return-risk criterion (credit spread / default probability ratio), showing even heavily traded names can be poor relative value.

Practical Implications

The choice between structural and reduced-form models depends on the application. Structural models provide economic intuition about default drivers (leverage, asset volatility) and are useful for fundamental credit analysis. Reduced-form models excel at pricing and hedging because they calibrate directly to observable market spreads. For portfolio management, reduced-form models dominate because they enable rapid screening of thousands of credits for relative value. The key insight: a bond with a high credit spread relative to its model-implied default probability is potentially cheap (positive alpha), while one with a low spread relative to default probability may be rich.

Part V: Portfolio Management

Chapters 24–25: Portfolio Strategies & Construction

Strategy Spectrum

Five categories from least to most active: pure indexing (replicate benchmark exactly) → enhanced indexing (minor factor mismatches for incremental return) → active management (larger risk-factor bets based on views) → absolute return (positive return regardless of market conditions) → liability-driven (manage relative to liabilities, not an index). Core/satellite blends a low-cost indexed core (beta) with active satellites targeting specific sectors or themes (alpha). Research consistently shows asset allocation is the most important performance driver across time periods. The portfolio management team includes CIO, CCO (compliance monitoring is a key selling point for institutional mandates), sector analysts, traders, and economists.

Bond Benchmark Problems

  • Duration set by issuers (cost minimization—issuing at maturities where funding is cheapest), not investors (return maximization)—a fundamental misalignment. Index duration is a “historical accident”
  • Bum’s problem: more debt = higher index weight → overweights riskier/more-levered issuers (opposite of equities where market cap reflects value creation). Goltz and Campani study (1997–2010): cap-weighted IG indexes are “fairly unstable” in risk exposures—sector weights and durations shift as issuance patterns change
  • Alternatives: issuer caps (limit single-issuer weight), GDP-weighting (PIMCO), duration-weighting, laddered indexes, fundamental indexing (weight by economic footprint rather than debt outstanding)
  • Index composition changes: new issuance adds bonds monthly, downgrades/maturity shortening removes them—creating involuntary turnover, transaction costs, and tracking error for managers who must rebalance

Active Strategy Risk Factors

Primary risk factors decompose into systematic (market-wide, undiversifiable) and nonsystematic (security-specific, diversifiable). Systematic risks further divide into term structure risks (parallel shifts, twists, butterflies) and non-term-structure risks (sector risk, credit risk, optionality risk). Active strategies capitalize on expectations about how these factors will change over the investment horizon. The total return framework explicitly models reinvestment, horizon yield, and spread assumptions to assess how each risk-factor scenario affects portfolio performance.

Yield Curve Strategies

Three classical curve positioning strategies:

  • Bullet: concentrated at one maturity point; maximizes yield for a given duration target; lowest convexity. Best when expecting small parallel shifts
  • Barbell: positions at two extremes of the curve (e.g., 2-year and 30-year); higher convexity but sacrifices ~25 bps yield versus bullet. Best for large parallel shifts or curve flattening
  • Ladder: equal allocation across maturities; most diversified across the curve; provides regular reinvestment opportunities and reduces timing risk. Best for uncertain rate environments

Historical decomposition (Litterman & Scheinkman): 91.6% of Treasury returns from parallel shifts, 5.0% from twists, 3.4% from butterfly movements—meaning duration accounts for the vast majority of return variation. Dollar duration must be matched for spread trades to isolate credit views from rate exposure: if you swap one bond for another, the portfolio’s dollar duration must remain constant so that any gain/loss reflects the credit view, not an unintended rate bet.

“There is no way for a portfolio manager to earn substantial returns on a fixed-rate bond portfolio without bearing risk. Duration is the primary source of risk and return. All else is a footnote—occasionally an important footnote, but a footnote nonetheless.”

Portfolio Construction: Cell-Based Approach

Divide the benchmark into cells representing different characteristics: duration buckets, maturity ranges, market sectors (Treasury, agencies, corporate, MBS), credit quality (AAA/AA/A/BBB), call features, and sinking fund provisions. Select bonds to match each cell’s weight in the benchmark. Limitation: doesn’t capture correlations between cells or handle non-parallel yield curve movements well. More sophisticated than simple duration matching, but less powerful than full multi-factor optimization.

Multi-Factor Risk Models

Multi-factor models decompose risk into systematic (yield curve level/slope/curvature, swap spread, volatility, sector spreads) and idiosyncratic (issuer-specific, issue-specific). Tracking error = standard deviation of active returns (portfolio return minus benchmark return). Two types: backward-looking (historical, ex-post—reflects past decisions) and forward-looking (predicted, ex-ante—reflects current portfolio exposures). Forward-looking tracking error is far more useful because it shows the effect of current positions on future risk.

Example from Barclays: 50-position portfolio vs. 5,693 benchmark positions—idiosyncratic risk accounted for 87% of tracking error despite small standalone magnitude per bond. This demonstrates that even modest issuer concentration creates significant tracking error in bond portfolios, where the benchmark is vastly more diversified than any practical portfolio.

Leverage via Repo

Managers borrow to create market exposure exceeding cash available. Example: $50M equity + $150M borrowed = $200M position. If invested at 5% and borrowed at 4.5%, the 50bp spread is magnified by 4× leverage. Duration of leveraged portfolio: $100M equity invested in $400M of duration-3 bonds has levered duration of 12 (not 3)—a 100bp rate move changes the $100M equity by 12%, not 3%. Most common borrowing: repurchase agreements (collateralized loans via the repo market), with credit risk mitigated by haircuts (1–3%), daily mark-to-market, and tri-party custody arrangements.

“Leverage is a double-edged sword. The collapse of Long-Term Capital Management in 1998—with 25:1 leverage on a $4.7 billion equity base—demonstrated that even Nobel Prize–winning quantitative models cannot protect against illiquidity and correlated spread widening when leverage is excessive. LTCM’s positions were individually sound but collectively catastrophic when margin calls forced simultaneous liquidation.”

Leverage risk is asymmetric: a 4× leveraged portfolio earning 50bps spread captures 200bps annually in favorable conditions, but a 2% adverse move wipes out 8% of equity—potentially triggering margin calls that force liquidation at the worst possible time. Risk management for leveraged portfolios must account for funding liquidity (ability to roll repo), market liquidity (ability to sell positions without impact), and correlation risk (historically uncorrelated positions becoming correlated in crises).

Backtesting and Smart Beta

Backtesting methods: walk-forward (most common—assumes history repeats exactly), resampling (tests multiple resampled paths from historical data), Monte Carlo simulation (generates synthetic future paths, with four advantages per Marco López de Prado). Biases to guard against: survivorship bias (using only surviving bonds), look-ahead bias (using information not available at decision time), optimal period bias (cherry-picking time windows). Smart beta strategies apply equity-style factor investing to bonds—targeting risk premia beyond market-cap weighting. Felix Goltz (EDHEC): “There’s a long way to go before smart beta can become a reality for fixed-income portfolios”—minimum-volatility strategies that work for equities would simply concentrate bond portfolios in short-duration, high-quality issues, which may not be the desired outcome.

Chapter 26: Managing Corporate Bond Portfolios

Risk-Return Asymmetry

Bond upside is capped (coupons + par at maturity); downside is principal loss (partially offset by recovery). This creates a fundamental asymmetry: removing the worst 10% of performers improves bond index returns significantly; removing the best 10% barely changes it (opposite of equities where top performers drive index returns). Avoiding losers is far more important than picking winners in credit investing—this insight shapes the entire approach to corporate bond portfolio management.

Corporate Bond Style Factors

Applying equity-style factor investing to corporate bonds, research identifies several systematic risk premia:

  • Value: bonds with wide credit spreads relative to fundamentals tend to outperform; the spread captures compensation for risk that doesn’t fully materialize as losses
  • Momentum: bonds with recent positive excess returns continue to outperform in the near term; driven by slow information diffusion in illiquid markets
  • Low risk (quality): higher-quality bonds deliver better risk-adjusted returns than lower-quality bonds; the spread on low-quality bonds overcompensates for actual default losses but undercompensates on a volatility-adjusted basis
  • Size: smaller, less-followed issuers may offer a liquidity premium, though evidence is mixed due to the illiquidity of small corporate issues
  • Carry: bonds offering higher spread per unit of duration (excess carry) tend to deliver higher total returns

However, concerns remain about applying equity factor approaches to bonds: most corporate bonds have finite lives, are illiquid, and have asymmetric payoffs. Factor returns in bonds tend to be more cyclical than in equities, and transaction costs can erode much of the factor premium due to wider bid-ask spreads.

Duration Times Spread (DTS)

DTS = spread duration × credit spread. Superior to spread duration alone because excess return volatility increases linearly with DTS across different quality segments. Example: A-rated bond with 100bps spread and duration 5 (DTS=500) has less risk than BB bond with 400bps spread and duration 2 (DTS=800), despite higher duration. Contribution to portfolio DTS = DTS × portfolio weight. The finding holds for U.S. investment-grade, high-yield, and European corporate bonds—a robust cross-market result. DTS provides a single, intuitive measure of credit risk exposure that captures both the magnitude of the spread (compensation for risk) and the duration (sensitivity to spread changes).

Empirical Duration and Duration Multipliers

High-yield bonds have two risk factors: interest-rate and equity risk. Because spreads widen when rates fall (flight-to-quality), the two effects partially offset. The duration multiplier M = 1 + ρ(σs/σy), typically <1 since the correlation ρ between Treasury yields and credit spreads is negative. By rating: A-rated M≈0.85; BBB consumer cyclicals M=0.63; Ba and below can be near zero or negative (bond behaves more like equity than fixed income). This means a BBB cyclical bond with modified duration 5 behaves like it has effective duration 3.15 (5 × 0.63). Misestimating high-yield duration leads to dramatically different portfolio rebalancing: if you assume analytical duration 5.0 for HY but true empirical duration is 1.25, the non-HY portion needs duration 6.25 instead of 5.59 to hit the same target—a 66bp difference with real performance consequences.

Seven Credit Relative Value Trades

  1. Yield-spread pickup: swap for higher spread, must be dollar-duration-neutral
  2. Credit-upside: buy expected upgrades before the market prices in the improvement
  3. Credit-defense: reduce exposure to deteriorating sectors or issuers
  4. New-issue swap: seasoned to new issue for liquidity premium capture
  5. Sector-rotation: over/underweight sectors based on cycle positioning
  6. Curve-adjustment: modify duration relative to benchmark
  7. Structure trades: bullet/callable/putable swaps to exploit option mispricing

Fallen angels outperform peer HY bonds by 6.63% over two years after downgrade. The mechanism is systematic:

  • Index deletion forces indexed managers to sell immediately regardless of valuation
  • Mandate restrictions force IG-only managers to liquidate within their compliance window
  • The concentrated selling depresses prices well below fundamental value
  • High-yield buyers exploiting the forced-selling dynamic capture the recovery as prices mean-revert

This creates one of the most reliable alpha sources in fixed income—a structural inefficiency driven by regulatory and mandate constraints rather than fundamental mispricing.

Constraint-Tolerant Investing

Ng and Phelps (1990–2009): retaining bonds that violate index rules (downgrades, short maturity, low liquidity) consistently outperformed strict index replication. Five tolerant portfolio strategies tested:

  • Downgrade-tolerant: retain fallen angels rather than selling at depressed prices (+6bps annual spread premium)
  • Maturity-tolerant: hold bonds that fall below the index’s minimum maturity cutoff
  • Liquidity-tolerant: retain bonds that fall below minimum issue-size thresholds
  • Fully tolerant: combine all three tolerances (+7bps annual spread premium)

All five tolerant portfolios showed higher average annual credit spread premium than the index with equal or lower standard deviation. These aren’t trivial numbers when average active fixed-income alpha before fees is only 44bps. The lesson: mechanical index-following rules create predictable selling patterns that generate value for more flexible investors.

Chapter 27: Liability-Driven Investing

Immunization (Redington, 1952): match asset duration to liability duration so that rising rates offset bond price losses through higher reinvestment income, and vice versa. The two effects are equal and offsetting when duration matches the investment horizon.

Immunization Conditions

  • Portfolio duration = liability horizon (or target investment horizon)
  • Present value of assets ≥ present value of liabilities
  • Cash flow dispersion of assets brackets the liability (cash flows occur both before and after the liability date)
  • Portfolio must be rebalanced periodically as duration drifts with time and yield changes

M-Squared and Immunization Risk

M² = ∑[CFt(t−H)²/(1+y)t] measures risk from cash flow dispersion around liability date H. M² = 0 for a zero-coupon bond maturing at the liability date—this is the ideal immunizing instrument. Bullet portfolios have less immunization risk (lower M²) than barbells with the same duration because their cash flows cluster more tightly around the liability date. The portfolio with the lowest M² minimizes residual interest-rate risk from non-parallel yield curve shifts. Ronald Ryan’s approach uses only Treasury STRIPS (zero-coupon bonds) constructed to match projected liabilities exactly, eliminating reinvestment risk entirely since there are no intermediate coupon payments.

Cash Flow Matching vs. Multi-Period Immunization

Cash flow matching: select bonds whose cash flows match the liability stream (working backward from the last liability).

  • Advantages: no duration requirement, no periodic rebalancing needed, zero immunization risk barring defaults
  • Disadvantage: costs 3–7% more than multi-period immunization because constraining the optimization to exact payment matching excludes higher-yielding bonds with imperfect timing
  • Combination approach: cash flow match near-term liabilities (where certainty matters most) and immunize longer-term liabilities (where duration matching is sufficient)

Multi-period immunization has greater risk because it relies on reinvestment assumptions and is vulnerable to yield curve shape changes. The key trade-off: cash flow matching eliminates risk but at higher cost; immunization is cheaper but requires continuous monitoring and rebalancing.

Contingent Immunization

Combines active management with a safety net: as long as the portfolio value exceeds the immunization floor (the amount needed to immunize the remaining liability at current rates), the manager can actively trade to generate excess return. If the floor is reached (through poor active decisions or adverse market moves), the portfolio is fully immunized—locking in the minimum acceptable return. The spread between current portfolio value and the floor is the manager’s “cushion” for active management.

De-Risking Solutions for Pension Plans

Plan freezing: hard freeze (no new benefits accrual), partial freeze (existing benefits grow only by service), soft freeze (no new entrants). Plan termination: wind down completely. Risk transference mechanisms:

  • Lump-sum buyout: offer participants immediate cash settlement of their pension benefit
  • Annuity buy-in: plan purchases a group annuity from an insurer while retaining the liability on its books (insurer is an asset of the plan)
  • Annuity buy-out: insurer takes on the liability directly; participants become policyholders of the insurer (removes liability from plan sponsor entirely)
  • Longevity swaps: hedge the risk that pensioners live longer than actuarial assumptions—counterparty pays if actual mortality experience exceeds expected

Unfunded pension obligations from defined benefit plans are an increasing driver of both corporate credit risk and municipal financial distress—the same structural problem affects both private and public sector sponsors.

Chapter 28: Performance Measurement

Performance measurement (calculating returns) differs from performance evaluation (assessing value added). Three requirements: accuracy (recognize timing of cash flows), informativeness (explain how results were achieved), and simplicity (understandable by managers, clients, and stakeholders).

Return Calculation Methods

  • Arithmetic average: unweighted average of subperiod returns. Misleading: portfolio rising from $28M to $58M back to $28M shows 25% arithmetic average but 0% actual return
  • Time-weighted (geometric) return: compound growth rate of initial investment, assuming reinvestment. Preferred for manager evaluation because it eliminates distortion from client contributions/withdrawals. Formula: [(1+R1)(1+R2)…(1+RN)]1/N − 1
  • Dollar-weighted (IRR): interest rate equating PV of all cash flows + terminal value to initial value. Affected by contributions/withdrawals beyond manager control—two managers with identical investment decisions can show different dollar-weighted returns due to client cash flow timing. Useful for fund growth perspective but not for comparing managers

General rule: arithmetic average ≥ time-weighted average (equal only when all subperiod returns are identical). The divergence increases with the variability of subperiod returns. GIPS (Global Investment Performance Standards) from CFA Institute standardize how returns are calculated and presented, addressing historical abuse in performance reporting.

Performance Attribution

Performance attribution decomposes active return into components:

  1. Interest-rate management effect: return from duration and yield curve positioning decisions (parallel shifts, twists, butterflies)
  2. Sector allocation effect: over/underweighting sectors (Treasury, agency, corporate, MBS, ABS) relative to the benchmark
  3. Security selection effect: individual bond picks within sectors; includes spread changes on specific credits
  4. Trading effect: gains/losses from trading activity (buying cheap, selling rich) versus simply holding

Three model types: sector-based, factor-based, and hybrid. The goal is distinguishing skill from luck and identifying repeatable alpha sources. Factor-based attribution decomposes risk into yield curve factors (level, slope, curvature), spread factors (sector, quality, liquidity), and idiosyncratic factors (issuer-specific events).

Benchmark Selection

Benchmark selection fundamentally shapes evaluation. Common benchmarks include:

  • Broad market indexes: Bloomberg Barclays U.S. Aggregate Bond Index (the most widely used U.S. fixed-income benchmark)
  • Custom liability benchmarks: constructed from projected liability cash flows for pension funds and insurance companies
  • Normal portfolios: customized benchmarks containing securities the manager normally selects, weighted as the manager would weight them

The Sharpe ratio (excess return per unit of total risk) provides a single-index performance measure, but “slow rabbit” benchmarks (easy to beat) can inflate apparent skill. The information ratio (active return / tracking error) measures skill per unit of active risk taken—an information ratio above 0.5 is generally considered excellent, while most active bond managers achieve ratios below 0.3. Average active fixed-income alpha before fees is only ~44 basis points, underscoring how challenging it is to consistently outperform in the bond market.

Part VI: Derivatives

Chapter 29: Interest-Rate Futures

Futures are standardized contracts with clearinghouse guarantees, eliminating counterparty risk. The clearinghouse takes the opposite side of every trade, so investors can liquidate positions without involving the original counterparty. Margin is a good-faith deposit (not borrowed funds): initial margin to open a position, with daily mark-to-market via settlement prices. If equity falls below maintenance margin, a variation margin call (cash only) must be met within 24 hours or the position is closed.

Key Contracts

  • Treasury bond futures: underlying = $100,000 par value hypothetical 20-year 6% coupon bond. Quoted in 32nds (97-16 = 97.50% = $97,500). Minimum tick: 1/32 = $31.25
  • Treasury note futures: 2-year ($200K par), 5-year, 10-year ($100K par each)
  • Eurodollar futures: $1M 3-month LIBOR deposit; one tick = 1bp = $25. Quoted on index basis (100 minus rate). Among the most heavily traded futures globally. Being replaced by SOFR futures
  • SOFR futures: 1-month (SR1, $41.67/bp) and 3-month (SR3, $25/bp); use compounded daily SOFR since it’s an overnight rate. Among fastest-growing new products in CME history
  • EURIBOR futures: minimum tick 0.005 (half-basis-point), tick value €12.50
  • Ultra Treasury bond futures: deliverable bonds with ≥25 years to maturity, for managing the long end of the curve

Delivery Options and Cheapest-to-Deliver

The short has three timing/quality options: quality/swap option (choose which bond to deliver from eligible range—minimum 15 years to maturity), timing option (when in delivery month), and wild card option (notify after market close at the settlement price—benefiting if bond prices move after futures close at 3:15 PM). Cheapest-to-deliver (CTD) = bond with the highest implied repo rate (the return from buying an eligible bond and delivering it at the futures settlement date). Conversion factors adjust the invoice price for each deliverable bond to approximate fairness, but the adjustment is imperfect—creating the CTD dynamic. The CTD shifts as yields change: low yields favor high-duration bonds; high yields favor low-duration bonds.

Pricing and Arbitrage

Theoretical futures price = cash price + financing cost − coupon income earned during holding period (cash-and-carry). If futures price exceeds theoretical: buy bonds, sell futures, earn riskless profit (cash-and-carry arbitrage). If below: sell bonds, buy futures, invest proceeds (reverse cash-and-carry). The implied repo rate equals the theoretical rate at fair value—deviations create arbitrage opportunities that link futures to cash market prices.

Portfolio Duration Control

Number of contracts = (target dollar duration − current dollar duration) / futures dollar duration. Positive = buy futures to increase duration; negative = sell to decrease.

Duration Targeting Example

$48M portfolio with current duration 2.97 seeking to match benchmark duration 3.68:

  • Current dollar duration = $48M × 2.97% = $1,425,600
  • Target dollar duration = $48M × 3.68% = $1,766,400
  • Required increase = $340,800
  • Five-year note futures dollar duration per contract = ~$5,000
  • Contracts needed: $340,800 / $5,000 = 68 contracts (buy)

This achieves the same result as selling short-duration bonds and buying long-duration bonds, but without the transaction costs and market impact of cash market trades—one of the primary practical benefits of futures.

Hedging with Futures

Hedge ratio = PVBP of hedged bond / PVBP of CTD × conversion factor × yield beta. Yield beta adjusts for differential yield movements between the hedged bond and the deliverable (not all yields move in lockstep—credit spreads can widen while Treasury yields fall).

Sources of basis risk in hedging:

  • Cash-futures basis: the convergence between futures and cash prices is imperfect due to delivery options and CTD uncertainty
  • Cross-hedging risk: hedging a corporate bond with Treasury futures assumes a stable corporate-Treasury spread—this breaks down exactly when hedges are most needed (during credit stress, spreads widen as Treasury yields fall)
  • Yield curve risk: if the hedge instrument and hedged position are at different maturities, non-parallel curve shifts create gain/loss

Perfect hedges are impossible in practice—the goal is to reduce risk to an acceptable residual level. Hedge effectiveness is measured by the R² of the regression between the hedged position and the hedging instrument’s returns.

Chapter 30: Interest-Rate Options

Futures options are the most popular form for interest-rate options (all are American-style). Three reasons: no accrued interest complications (futures have no accrued interest), reduced delivery squeeze risk (deliverable supply adequate), and futures prices more readily available than cash bond prices. Exchange-traded options on physicals are less common; OTC options provide customization for institutional hedging.

Black-Scholes Limitations for Bonds

Three critical limitations make Black-Scholes inappropriate for bond options: (1) allows unlimited prices (impossible—bonds converge to par at maturity; a call on a zero-coupon bond struck at $100.25 should be worthless but Black-Scholes gives $2.79 because it doesn’t account for the pull-to-par effect), (2) assumes constant interest rates (the very thing being modeled—circular reasoning), and (3) assumes constant variance as maturity approaches (bond price variance actually decreases toward maturity as the bond converges to par). The proper approach is an arbitrage-free binomial model (Black-Derman-Toy) that incorporates the yield curve and accounts for the term structure of volatility.

Option Greeks

  • Delta: price sensitivity to underlying (0 for deep OTM, ~1 for deep ITM); also the hedge ratio for delta-neutral positioning. Delta changes as the underlying moves—requiring continuous rebalancing for hedged positions
  • Gamma: rate of change of delta; highest at-the-money; measures the option’s convexity. High gamma means delta changes rapidly—requiring frequent rebalancing and generating significant transaction costs
  • Theta: time decay per day; options lose value as expiration approaches (time value erodes). At-the-money options have highest theta. Time decay accelerates in the final weeks before expiration
  • Kappa/Vega: sensitivity to implied volatility changes; long options benefit from rising vol. “Trading and investment strategies in this market involve buying and selling volatility”—when implied vol is below historical vol, the embedded option is cheap, creating opportunity for volatility trading

Hedging Strategies

Protective put (buying a put): protect downside while preserving full upside—like insurance (pay premium, protected if rates rise). Maximum loss = put premium paid. Cost is the premium, which can be significant in high-volatility environments.

Covered call (selling a call against an existing position): generate income at cost of capping upside. Equivalent to a short put via put-call parity: long bond + short call = short put + risk-free investment. Works well in stable/mildly-rising-rate environments where the call expires worthless and the premium is retained.

Collar Strategy

Buy a put (floor) and sell a call (cap) simultaneously. The call premium received partially or fully offsets the put premium paid. A zero-cost collar results when put and call premiums exactly offset—the investor gets downside protection for free but surrenders upside above the cap. Collars are popular with institutional investors who must limit both downside loss and hedging cost.

Leverage in options: Long call provides enormous leverage. Example: $100 in calls on a 20-year bond can produce a 670% return vs. 23% from holding the bond outright if yields drop 200bps—but loses everything if rates stay flat or rise. This leverage works in both directions and is the source of both opportunity and catastrophic risk in options trading. No universally “best” strategy; each involves explicit trade-offs across different rate scenarios.

Implied Volatility

Solved by iterating the pricing model until the model price matches the observed market price. Volatility smile: ITM and OTM options have higher implied vol than ATM (market prices in tail-risk events more than models predict). Volatility skew: implied vol varies systematically with strike price. Yield volatility = price volatility / duration (using the CTD bond). Standard practice: use ATM implied volatility, but interpret with caution—the dual assumption that both the market and the model are correct rarely holds simultaneously.

Chapter 31: Swaps, FRAs, Caps & Floors

An interest-rate swap exchanges fixed for floating payments on a notional principal (no principal exchanged—only net interest differences change hands). Fixed-rate payer benefits from rate increases (pays fixed, receives rising float); fixed-rate receiver benefits from decreases. Swap spread = swap rate minus Treasury yield of same maturity, reflecting credit/liquidity premium in the interbank market. Swap rate is derived from no-arbitrage PV equality: the fixed rate that makes PV of fixed payments equal PV of floating payments (using forward rates).

Swap Decomposition

A swap can be viewed two ways:

  1. Package of FRAs: each semiannual exchange is a forward rate agreement on LIBOR/SOFR. The fixed-rate payer is long a series of FRAs (benefits when realized rates exceed forward rates)
  2. Package of cash instruments: the fixed-rate payer has effectively sold a fixed-rate bond and purchased a floating-rate note. The fixed-rate receiver has done the reverse

This decomposition reveals that a swap’s duration equals the difference between the durations of a fixed-rate and floating-rate bond of the same maturity: approximately the maturity of the swap divided by 2 (since the floating-rate bond has near-zero duration). A 10-year swap has an approximate duration of 5.

Swap Spread Determinants

The swap spread (swap rate minus Treasury yield of the same maturity) reflects several factors: (1) credit risk in the interbank market (the default risk premium embedded in LIBOR/SOFR), (2) supply and demand for fixed vs. floating cash flows, (3) Treasury supply effects (heavy issuance pushes Treasury yields up, narrowing swap spreads; supply reduction widens them), and (4) flight-to-quality effects (during crises, Treasury yields plunge faster than swap rates, causing spreads to widen dramatically—swap spreads briefly went negative in 2015, violating traditional arbitrage relationships).

Applications

  • Asset/liability management: banks with fixed-rate loans + floating-rate deposits enter pay-float/receive-fixed swaps to match; insurers with floating-rate assets and fixed-rate annuity obligations do the reverse
  • Structured notes: combine MTN issuance with swap. Example: issuer creates inverse floater for investor by issuing regular MTN + entering pay-float/receive-fixed swap, achieving below-market fixed-rate funding while giving the investor leveraged rate exposure
  • Duration modification: fixed-rate receiver increases portfolio duration; payer decreases it—without buying or selling cash bonds. The notional principal needed = (target dollar duration change) / (swap’s dollar duration per $1 notional)
  • Synthetic positions: pay-fixed swap converts floating-rate asset to synthetic fixed-rate; receive-fixed converts fixed to synthetic floating. Allows precise risk transformation without restructuring the underlying portfolio

Caps, Floors, and Collars

A cap is a package of call options on interest rates (caplets), each covering one reset period. At each reset, if the reference rate exceeds the cap rate (strike), the cap seller pays the difference. A floor is a package of puts (floorlets)—floor seller pays if the rate falls below the floor rate. A collar = long cap + short floor (or vice versa)—the floor premium partially offsets the cap cost, reducing net expense. Banks buy caps to ceiling funding costs on floating-rate liabilities. Insurance companies buy floors to protect guaranteed rates on annuity products. Captions are options on caps (compound options)—providing the right to buy a cap at a predetermined premium.

Swaptions

Options on swaps. Notation: “3 into 10” = right to enter a 10-year swap in 3 years.

  • Payer swaption = right to pay fixed (profit if rates rise—economically equivalent to a put on a fixed-rate bond)
  • Receiver swaption = right to receive fixed (profit if rates fall—equivalent to a call on a fixed-rate bond)

Key applications:

  • Mortgage pipeline hedging: originators who have granted rate locks to borrowers buy payer swaptions to hedge if rates rise before loans close—if rates rise, the swaption gain offsets losses on the locked-rate commitments
  • Callable bond replication: issuing a noncallable bond + purchasing a receiver swaption = synthetic callable bond, often more cost-efficient than embedding the call directly in the bond indenture
  • Liability management: pension funds buy receiver swaptions to protect against falling rates that increase the present value of liabilities

Chapter 32: Credit Default Swaps

Protection buyer pays periodic premium (the CDS spread); protection seller compensates if a credit event occurs on the reference entity. Post-2009 standardization (Big Bang): 1% running spread (investment-grade) or 5% (high-yield) with upfront payment for the difference between the standard coupon and the fair spread. Notional outstanding dwarfs underlying bonds—CDS allow exposure without owning the reference entity. Protection seller = leveraged unfunded long credit position; protection buyer = synthetic short credit position.

Credit Events

Seven types defined by ISDA:

  1. Bankruptcy: formal legal proceedings (Chapter 7 or 11 in the U.S.)
  2. Failure to pay: missed interest or principal payment beyond any grace period
  3. Restructuring: most controversial—different definitions in different contract versions (Old-R, Mod-R, Modified Mod-R, No-R); involves maturity extension, coupon reduction, or principal write-down
  4. Acceleration: debt becomes due before scheduled maturity due to covenant violation
  5. Cross default: default on another obligation triggers CDS on this reference entity
  6. Downgrade: rating falls below specified threshold
  7. Repudiation/moratorium: for sovereigns only—government refuses to honor debt obligations

Narrowly tailored credit event (2019) introduced to address manufactured defaults—the Hovnanian/Blackstone case where Blackstone allegedly arranged a technical default to trigger CDS payouts while simultaneously entering into a favorable new financing agreement. ABS CDS use special pay-as-you-go credit event definitions that include interest shortfalls and write-downs, not just default.

Settlement Mechanics

  • Physical settlement: buyer delivers eligible bonds, seller pays face value. The buyer chooses the cheapest-to-deliver obligation from all eligible deliverables—creating basis between CDS and specific bonds
  • Cash settlement: ISDA-administered auction determines recovery price; payout = par − auction price. Auctions involve dealer submissions and an open market process
  • Extreme recovery examples: Lehman Brothers 8.625% recovery (massive loss); JCPenney 0.125% recovery (near-total loss for bondholders); Washington Mutual 57% recovery (surprisingly high for a bank failure)

Index CDS and Tranches

CDX.NA.IG: 125 investment-grade names; CDX.NA.HY: 100 high-yield names. New series created every six months with constituent changes reflecting rating migrations. Unlike single-name CDS (contract terminates upon credit event), index CDS continues with reduced notional after a constituent defaults—the index is “self-healing” in a sense.

Tranche CDS defined by attachment/detachment points: equity tranche (0–3%, ~33× leverage—first losses hit here, highest risk/return), mezzanine tiers (3–7%, 7–10%, 10–15%), senior (15–30%), and super-senior (30–100%—almost no historical losses). The equity tranche is quoted upfront (like an option premium) while mezzanine and senior tranches quote running spreads. Tranche trading allows precise credit views: selling equity protection is bullish on credit; buying equity + selling mezzanine is a correlation trade. iTraxx SovX Western Europe Index (15 sovereigns) saw trading collapse after the EU banned naked short sovereign CDS positions in 2012—demonstrating regulatory risk in CDS markets.

CDS Advantages Over Cash Bonds

Four key advantages: (1) leverage—much less capital required than buying cash bonds (unfunded exposure); (2) ease of shorting—can easily express negative credit views (extremely difficult in cash bond market where borrowing bonds is expensive); (3) fixed financing rate—CDS premium is known upfront vs. variable and potentially withdrawn repo rates; (4) permanent financing—repo can be recalled by the lender; CDS financing lasts the full contract term. Single-name CDS provide pure credit exposure isolated from interest-rate risk (unlike cash bonds where price reflects both). Index CDS are particularly valuable for small portfolios that cannot hold all 125+ constituents of a broad-based bond index—one index trade replicates diversified credit exposure.

Valuation and Implied Default Probability

Single-name CDS value approximated by difference between par asset swap spread and spread over LIBOR in the repo market. More precisely, CDS valuation models extract implied default probabilities for each reference entity by equating the PV of premium payments (conditional on survival) to the PV of protection payouts (conditional on default). Term structures of default probability enable relative value analysis across the credit curve—comparing short-term vs. long-term CDS reveals the market’s expectations about default timing.

CDS Basis and Relative Value

The CDS-bond basis = CDS spread − cash bond spread. When positive (“positive basis”), CDS protection is more expensive than the cash bond implies—potentially attractive to sell CDS protection. When negative (“negative basis”), CDS protection is cheap relative to the bond—attractive to buy protection. The basis is driven by:

  • Funding costs: cash bonds require capital; CDS protection selling is unfunded (leverage advantage)
  • Cheapest-to-deliver option: CDS protection buyer can deliver the cheapest eligible obligation, creating option value in the CDS
  • Counterparty risk: CDS have counterparty exposure that cash bonds lack (offset by collateral posting under ISDA agreements)
  • Market segmentation: different investor bases for CDS vs. cash bonds create supply/demand imbalances

Kamakura Corporation’s analysis of 431 Bank of America bonds (June 2014) found BAC’s 15 issues ranked in the bottom 17% on return-risk criterion (credit spread / default probability ratio)—showing that even heavily traded names can be poor relative value when properly analyzed. Municipal CDS face a tax treatment asymmetry: municipal bond interest is tax-exempt but CDS spread payments are not, disadvantaging tax-exempt fund managers who want to use protection selling as a substitute for owning bonds.

Key Takeaways

  1. Duration is a price sensitivity measure, not a time measure. Despite common misinterpretation, duration measures approximate percentage price change for a 100bp yield change. A CMO tranche can have duration of 40 while underlying mortgages are 30-year—making no sense as a time measure. An option can have duration of 20 with only one year to expiration.
  2. No truly risk-free assets exist. Even U.S. Treasuries carry inflation, liquidity, and potential default risk over long horizons. Peter Fisher (former Treasury undersecretary): “The idea of risk-free sovereign bonds is best thought of as an oxymoron.” The global shortage of safe assets has driven down natural interest rates by ~50 basis points.
  3. Convexity has measurable value—and a price. In volatile markets, positive convexity becomes extremely valuable: price appreciation exceeds depreciation for equal yield changes. In stable markets, selling convexity via callable bonds can enhance yield. The key question: how much should investors pay? Answer depends entirely on expected rate volatility.
  4. Forward rates are hedgeable rates, not predictions. Academic studies consistently show forward rates are poor predictors of future spot rates, yet they remain central to bond analysis for the right reason: they represent rates investors can lock in through bond selection. Even if you expect rates to rise, you should choose the instrument whose expected rate exceeds the forward rate.
  5. LIBOR’s demise requires a massive market transition. Over $375 trillion in financial products tied to LIBOR must transition to SOFR. SOFR is overnight, backward-looking, and secured (Treasury repo), versus LIBOR’s forward-looking, unsecured bank lending rate. Spread adjustment required because SOFR lacks LIBOR’s credit risk component.
  6. Corporate bond liquidity has collapsed. Dealer inventory down 80% since 2007 while issuance up 50%. Some 38% of corporate bonds never trade; 23% trade only a few times. The average stock trades 3,800 times per day; the most liquid investment-grade corporates trade only 85 times. This creates systemic liquidity risk.
  7. Municipal default rates are severely underestimated. Official rating agency data shows 0.1% cumulative defaults, but broader studies reveal 2,521 defaults from 1970–2011—roughly 50x the official statistics. The narrow definitions used by agencies systematically exclude unrated issues.
  8. Mortgage prepayment modeling is enormously complex. Refinancing, housing turnover, and cash-out refinancing must be modeled separately. The S-curve shows how prepayment response increases then plateaus. Burnout effects mean pools with rate exposure see declining prepayments as willing refinancers exit. Monte Carlo simulation with 512–1,024 paths required for proper MBS valuation because cash flows are path-dependent.
  9. Total return analysis is superior to YTM. The five-step horizon analysis procedure explicitly models reinvestment rate assumptions and horizon yield changes. YTM assumes reinvestment at the same rate and holding to maturity—both unrealistic. Total return enables scenario analysis across multiple interest-rate environments.
  10. Key rate duration captures real yield curve risk. Traditional duration only measures parallel shifts, which explain 91.6% of Treasury returns. But twists explain 5% and butterfly shifts 3.4%. Key rate duration quantifies exposure at 11 specific maturity points. Three portfolios with identical duration of 4.26 can have completely different responses to 30-year rate changes.
  11. CMO structures redistribute risk, they don’t eliminate it. Sequential-pay, PAC, Z-bonds, and floater/inverse floater structures enable precise risk allocation across investor preferences. But support bonds absorb massive variability. When support bonds are depleted, PAC bonds become “busted” and lose all protection.
  12. Effective duration is required for embedded options. Modified duration fails for callable, putable, or prepayable bonds because it assumes fixed cash flows. Differences can be dramatic: a bond may have modified duration 7 but effective duration 50 when option effects dominate. Higher volatility lowers callable bond value but raises putable bond value.
  13. Avoiding losers matters more than picking winners in credit. Truncated index studies show removing the worst 10% of corporate bonds improves returns—the opposite of equities, where removing the best 10% destroys returns. Credit investing has capped upside (coupons + par) but deep downside (principal loss minus recovery). This asymmetry drives the entire credit strategy.
  14. Duration Times Spread (DTS) is superior to spread duration alone. DTS = spread duration × credit spread. Excess return volatility increases linearly with DTS across different quality segments, making it the proper measure of credit risk exposure. A BBB bond with spread of 200bps and duration 5 has DTS of 1,000—comparable risk to a BB bond with spread 400bps and duration 2.5.
  15. Green bonds and carbon policy enable climate finance. CPPBs tie coupons to emissions performance, creating market-based incentives. Knut Kjaer (founder, Norway sovereign wealth fund) argues debt markets give investors greater impact than equity markets: 100 fossil fuel-intensive companies borrow in debt markets, but only 40 are listed on exchanges.
  16. EBITDA is a dangerous metric used in isolation. It excludes capital expenditures that are absolutely required. Sino-Forest had 69% EBITDA margins and 10.4× interest coverage yet never generated positive free cash flow in 15 years. The superior measure is EBITDA minus maintenance capex.
  17. Fallen angels outperform peer high-yield bonds. Bonds downgraded from investment-grade suffer forced selling (index deletion, mandate restrictions) that pushes prices below fundamental value. They outperform peer HY bonds by 6.63% over two years after downgrade.
  18. OAS constancy is theoretically unjustifiable for MBS. IO + PO = pass-through must hold in dollar terms, but their OAS levels cannot all remain constant under rate changes. This is a fundamental limitation of standard MBS analytics that practitioners must acknowledge.
  19. The “bum’s problem” undermines bond benchmarks. Market-capitalization-weighted bond indexes overweight the most indebted issuers. Unlike equities where market cap reflects value, in bonds more debt means more weight—rewarding riskier behavior. Alternatives include issuer caps, GDP-weighting, and duration-weighting.
  20. Covenants are among the most important credit considerations. The Sirius XM case study demonstrates: 5.25% 2022 bonds with high-yield covenants outperformed other unsecured bonds by 150–200bps solely due to covenant protection. Loopholes in covenants reveal management intentions.
  21. Repo market is the plumbing of the bond market. Repurchase agreements are collateralized loans that fund dealer inventories, enable leverage for portfolio managers, and create the financing advantage that bids up on-the-run Treasury issues. When repo markets seize (as in 2008), the entire bond market freezes.
  22. Inverse floaters amplify duration beyond maturity. A $20M inverse floater created from $100M collateral with leverage L=4 has duration = (1+L) × collateral duration = 40, even if the underlying mortgages are only 30-year. This proves duration cannot be a time measure—it’s purely a price sensitivity metric.
  23. PAC bonds offer false security when support bonds deplete. PAC bondholders enjoy prepayment protection within a collar (e.g., 90–300 PSA) only as long as support bonds remain outstanding. Once support bonds are exhausted by faster-than-expected prepayments, the PAC structure “busts” and becomes sequential-pay with no protection.
  24. Z-bonds eliminate reinvestment risk by design. Accrual tranches receive no current interest—all interest accrues and compounds, then diverts to accelerate paydown of earlier tranches. Investors concerned about reinvesting coupon payments at unknown future rates find Z-bonds inherently appealing.
  25. CMO tranches on the same collateral have wildly different durations. In a FactSet example, a sequential-pay Freddie Mac deal shows class A with 0.02 effective duration and the Z-bond with 11.24 effective duration—over 500x difference—from the same underlying mortgage pool.
  26. Tax-equivalent yield magnifies muni advantages for high earners. A 3.4% tax-exempt municipal yield equals 5.23% taxable at 35% marginal rate, but 6.18% at 45%—making the same bond 18% more valuable to the higher-bracket investor. Triple tax exemption (federal + state + local) for in-state bonds amplifies further.
  27. Covered bonds have never defaulted in 250+ years. The German Pfandbrief model (dating to 1769) provides dual recourse—claim on the issuer plus a ring-fenced cover pool of high-quality assets. This structural protection has produced a zero-default track record unmatched by any other fixed-income instrument class.

Thematic Breakdowns

Bond Pricing & Valuation

  • Time value of money as foundation: present value of future cash flows using semiannual compounding (U.S. convention)
  • Inverse price-yield relationship with convex curvature; par/discount/premium dynamics and pull-to-par effect
  • Floating-rate and inverse floating-rate securities pricing; leverage factor L amplifies rate sensitivity: inverse floater duration = (1+L) × fixed bond duration
  • Day-count conventions: 30/360 (corporate/muni), actual/actual (Treasury), actual/360 (money market)
  • Accrued interest and clean vs. dirty pricing; settlement conventions; CUSIP identification system

Yield Measures & Their Limitations

  • Current yield (ignores capital gain/loss), YTM (assumes reinvestment at same rate), yield to call/put/worst
  • Total return (horizon analysis) as the correct framework: five-step procedure modeling reinvestment + horizon price
  • Cash flow yield for amortizing securities (monthly IRR × 12); discount margin for floaters (assumes reference rate unchanged)
  • Treasury bill quotations and bond-equivalent yield conversion; T-bill discount yield vs. investment yield

Duration, Convexity & Risk Measurement

  • Macaulay (weighted avg time to cash flows), modified (Macaulay/[1+y/k]), dollar (mod × price × 0.01), effective (allows cash flows to change), spread, and key rate duration
  • Convexity as second-order correction: % price change ≈ −duration × Δy + ½ × convexity × (Δy)²
  • PVBP/DV01: dollar price change per basis point; yield value of a price change (inverse measure)
  • Portfolio duration as weighted average; contribution to duration for sector analysis
  • Yield curve reshaping duration: SEDUR (short-end) and LEDUR (long-end) for non-parallel shifts
  • Analytical vs. empirical duration: formula-based vs. regression-based; empirical needed for high-yield where equity risk dominates

Interest Rate Theory & Term Structure

  • Three classical theories: time-preference (Böhm-Bawerk’s “roundaboutness,” Fisher’s impatience/opportunity), loanable funds (Wicksell/Robertson/Ohlin), liquidity preference (Keynes)
  • Fisher’s Law: nominal rate = real rate + expected inflation; natural rate of interest (Wicksell)
  • TIPS as market-based inflation expectation measure: 10-yr Treasury yield minus 10-yr TIPS yield = expected inflation
  • Bootstrapping to derive theoretical spot rate curve from par yield curve using iterative PV calculations
  • Forward rates as hedgeable rates; six forward rate interpretations across term structure theories
  • Pure expectations (rejected empirically), liquidity theory (term premium), preferred habitat (supply/demand at each maturity)
  • Swap curve (LIBOR/SOFR curve) as alternative benchmark; swap spread = swap rate minus Treasury yield

Fixed-Income Sectors

  • Treasury: bills (≤1yr, zero-coupon), notes (2–10yr), bonds (>10yr), TIPS, FRNs, STRIPS; single-price Dutch auction; on-the-run vs. off-the-run liquidity premium
  • Agencies: FHLBanks (~$1T outstanding), Fannie/Freddie (conservatorship since Sept 2008, implicit not explicit guarantee), FFCBS, TVA, Farmer Mac
  • Corporates: investment-grade vs. high-yield; strict seniority hierarchy (secured → senior unsecured → subordinated); CLO structures with AAA through equity tranches
  • Municipals: GO (backed by taxing power) vs. revenue (backed by project revenue); tax-equivalent yield = tax-exempt / (1 − tax rate); moral obligation bonds; pre-refunded/escrowed-to-maturity
  • International: Yankee (U.S.), Samurai (Japan), Bulldog (U.K.), Eurobonds; covered bonds (Pfandbriefe dual recourse); emerging market three-pillar analysis (serviceability, solvency, structural)

Structured Products & MBS

  • Residential mortgages: four principal risks (credit, liquidity, price, prepayment); LTV as primary credit indicator; conforming vs. nonconforming/jumbo
  • Agency pass-throughs: three prepayment components (housing turnover, cash-out refinancing, rate/term refinancing); PSA benchmark as convention not evidence; S-curve and burnout effect
  • CMOs: sequential-pay (priority waterfall), Z-bonds (accrual, no cash flow until prior tranches retired), PAC/support with collar (e.g. 90–300 PSA), floater/inverse floater, IO/PO strips
  • Nonagency RMBS: senior-subordinated with credit enhancement; shifting interest mechanism; CDR/SDA benchmarks; strategic default as factor (2008–2012)
  • CMBS: DSC/LTV metrics; four call protections (lockout, defeasance, penalty points, yield maintenance); balloon risk; three servicer types
  • ABS: true sale to SPV separating from originator bankruptcy; auto loans (predictable), credit cards (revolving with lockout), rate reduction bonds (stable); Dodd-Frank 5% risk retention

Credit Analysis & Modeling

  • Three pillars: covenants (limits on management), collateral (assets in default), ability to pay (business + governance + financial risk)
  • Financial ratios: interest coverage (EBIT/interest), leverage (debt/cap, debt/EBITDA), S&P cash flow hierarchy (operating → free → discretionary → prefinancing)
  • Rating agencies: Moody’s/S&P/Fitch; investment-grade (AAA–BBB) vs. high-yield (BB and below); rating migration matrices
  • Structural models (Merton: equity as call option on firm assets), reduced-form models (Jarrow-Turnbull: Poisson default process), incomplete-information hybrids (Giesecke-Goldberg: accounts for fraud)

Portfolio Management & Construction

  • Strategy spectrum: pure indexing → enhanced indexing → active management → absolute return → liability-driven
  • Core/satellite strategy: low-cost indexed core (beta) + active satellites (alpha). Research consistently shows asset allocation is the most important performance driver across time periods
  • Cell-based approach (divide benchmark into duration/maturity/sector/quality cells) vs. multi-factor risk models
  • Yield curve strategies: bullet (concentrated), barbell (two extremes, higher convexity, ~25bps sacrifice), ladder (equal distribution)
  • Immunization (Redington 1952), cash flow matching (no rebalancing needed, 3–7% more expensive), contingent immunization, M² measure
  • Bond style factors: size, value, momentum, low risk; performance attribution into sector, factor, security-selection components

Derivatives

  • Futures: Treasury bond/note (quoted in 32nds), Eurodollar (being replaced by SOFR), delivery options (quality/swap, timing, wild card); cheapest-to-deliver = highest implied repo rate
  • Options: protective puts, covered calls; Black-Scholes has three critical limitations for bonds (unlimited prices, constant rates, constant variance); binomial lattice approach preferred
  • Swaps: fixed-for-floating on notional principal; swap spread; duration modification; structured notes (MTN + swap); swaptions (“3 into 10” = right to enter 10-year swap in 3 years)
  • Caps (package of caplets), floors (package of floorlets), collars (long cap + short floor); banks buy caps to ceiling funding costs
  • CDS: single-name and index (CDX.NA.IG = 125 names, CDX.NA.HY = 100 names); tranche CDS with 33× leverage on equity tranche; standardized post-2009 (1% IG, 5% HY running spread)

Contrarian Ideas

  • Duration misunderstood as time: The widespread interpretation of duration as “weighted average life” is misleading. A CMO tranche can have duration 40 with 30-year underlying mortgages. A call option can have duration 20 with one year to expiration. The correct interpretation: approximate percentage price change for 100bp yield shift.
  • Safe assets don’t exist: The foundational assumption of a risk-free rate is increasingly questioned. A Ferreira-Shousha study (1970–2018) shows net supply of sovereign safe assets for 11 advanced economies is a key driver of natural interest rates, explaining 20% of their variance. Global safe asset accumulation has reduced rates by 50bps.
  • Forward rates don’t predict: Despite being central to bond analysis, forward rates consistently fail as predictors of future spot rates. But they are not meant to predict—they are hedgeable rates. Even if you expect rates to rise to 5.60%, you should still choose the 1-year instrument if your expected rate is below the 5.75% forward rate.
  • Municipal defaults severely underreported: Official 0.1% cumulative rates exclude unrated issues and use narrow definitions. Broader studies find 2,521 defaults from 1970–2011—roughly 50x official statistics. Detroit’s 2013 bankruptcy tested absolute priority doctrine.
  • Convexity overpriced in calm markets: Investors systematically overpay for convexity protection during low-volatility periods. With small yield changes, high-convexity and low-convexity bonds offer approximately the same price change. Sophisticated investors sell convexity (callable bonds) in calm markets to harvest yield premium.
  • Negative rates can be economically rational: Despite seeming to violate basic finance, negative rates serve multiple purposes: stimulate borrowing, weaken currency for exports (Bank of Japan 2016), penalize excess reserves to encourage lending. As of September 2019, ~25% of investment-grade bonds in developed markets had negative yields.
  • Debt markets more powerful than equity for climate: While ESG activism focuses on shareholders, bond investors can directly penalize carbon-intensive companies through higher credit spreads. 100 fossil fuel-intensive companies borrow in debt markets; only 40–41 are listed on exchanges. Actions in debt markets affect funding costs directly.
  • EBITDA is a dangerous metric: Sino-Forest had 69% EBITDA margins yet never generated positive free cash flow in 15 years. All three CRAs rated it BB category. EBITDA excludes capex that is absolutely required—the better measure is EBITDA minus maintenance capex.
  • Fallen angels outperform: Bonds downgraded from investment-grade to high-yield suffer forced selling (index deletion, mandate restrictions) that pushes prices below fundamental value. They outperform peer HY bonds by 6.63% over two years after downgrade.
  • OAS constancy is theoretically unjustifiable: IO + PO = pass-through must hold in dollar terms, but their OAS levels cannot all remain constant under rate changes. This means at least one of the three securities must be mispriced under the standard OAS framework.
  • Credit ratings are inaccurate default probability measures: Simple reduced-form models dominate credit ratings for forecasting corporate failure. The Sino-Forest case shows all three major CRAs unanimously failed to warn investors—they rushed to downgrade only after the stock had already plunged.
  • The 1970–2000 rate environment was the anomaly: Bond market observers debate when yields will return to “normal” highs of 5%+, using 1970–2000 as baseline. But this period (Volcker tightening, double-digit inflation, 20% fed funds rate) was historically exceptional, not representative.
  • Nonrefundable ≠ noncallable: A common investor error. Nonrefundable merely means the issuer cannot use lower-cost debt proceeds to retire the bond. The issuer can still call it using other funds (asset sales, equity issuance). Noncallable provides strictly greater protection than nonrefundable—a critical distinction in high-yield covenants.
  • DIP financing undermines secured creditors: Debtor-in-possession lenders in Chapter 11 bankruptcy receive priority even over existing secured creditors, plus higher interest rates, because they provide essential rescue capital. The “absolute priority rule” that theoretically protects seniority is frequently violated in reorganization through plan negotiations and bargaining dynamics.
  • Monoline insurers were the market’s hidden fragility: Pre-2007, ~50% of new municipal bonds carried monoline insurance, and no insured muni had ever defaulted or been downgraded. The monolines’ involvement in subprime MBS destroyed their credit ratings overnight, removing the cornerstone of municipal bond credit enhancement and permanently altering the market structure.
  • Option cost for MBS tranches is more stable than OAS: When market prices change slightly, a tranche’s option cost (static spread minus OAS) remains relatively constant while OAS itself fluctuates. This makes option cost a more reliable metric for monitoring relative value over time—a useful computational shortcut for portfolio managers.

Author’s Key Proposals

  1. Adopt total return framework over YTM. The five-step horizon analysis explicitly models reinvestment assumptions and yield changes rather than relying on yield-to-maturity’s unrealistic conditions (reinvestment at same rate, hold to maturity).
  2. Use key rate duration for real-world risk management. Traditional duration assumes parallel shifts; key rate duration quantifies exposure at 11 maturity points. Three portfolios with identical duration 4.26 had completely different risk profiles (barbell: 0.675, bullet: ~0, ladder: 0.594 for 30-year key rate).
  3. Implement effective duration for complex securities. Modified duration fails for embedded options. Use the approximation formula: (P₋ − P₊) / [2P₀Δy], which works for any bond or derivative including MBS, callable/putable bonds, and options.
  4. Transition from LIBOR to SOFR with spread adjustments. SOFR lacks LIBOR’s credit component; legacy contracts require robust fallback provisions. The transition affects $375 trillion in financial products.
  5. Develop comprehensive liquidity assessment. Incorporate trading frequency, dealer inventory trends, bid-ask spreads, and market impact costs. With 38% of corporates never trading, liquidity scoring is essential for portfolio construction.
  6. Apply CMO structural analysis to all ABS. Understanding sequential-pay waterfalls, PAC/support mechanics, coverage tests, and cash flow redirection applies universally across all structured credit products including CLOs, CMBS, and auto ABS.
  7. Integrate climate risk into credit analysis. Carbon emissions, policy exposure, and transition risk should be standard credit components. Carbon policy performance bonds (CPPBs) with coupon penalties for missing emission targets are a concrete mechanism.
  8. Use OAS methodology for fair value comparison. Option-adjusted spread enables apples-to-apples comparison by removing the spread component attributable to embedded options. Option value in spread terms = static spread − OAS.
  9. Match portfolio duration to investment horizon. Immunization requires duration matching and periodic rebalancing; M² quantifies residual risk from cash flow dispersion. Zero-coupon bond at the liability date has M² = 0 (perfect immunization).
  10. Distinguish spread duration from modified duration. Credit portfolios need spread duration to quantify sensitivity to spread changes independent of Treasury rate movements. Floating-rate bonds can have near-zero interest-rate duration but large spread durations.
  11. Always begin credit analysis with the business model. Before financial ratios, understand how the company makes money. The three fundamental questions: What does it do? Where do revenues come from? Where does it incur costs? Sino-Forest shows that strong financial metrics on a flawed business model guarantee eventual disaster.
  12. Read covenants within the context of corporate strategy. Covenants are not just legal provisions—they reveal management intentions. Loopholes, carve-outs, and covenant suspension clauses can dramatically shift bondholder risk. The Sirius XM case is the textbook example.
  13. Employ constraint-tolerant investing. Retaining fallen angels, maturity violators, and low-liquidity bonds rather than mechanically selling them outperforms strict index replication by 6–7bps annually (Ng & Phelps, 1990–2009). Predictable forced-selling patterns from index rules create systematic value for flexible investors.
  14. Use DTS (Duration Times Spread) for credit risk measurement. DTS = spread duration × credit spread. It captures both the magnitude of spread compensation and the sensitivity to spread changes in a single metric. Excess return volatility increases linearly with DTS across all quality segments, making it superior to spread duration alone.
  15. Apply vector analysis to MBS, not static PSA. Static PSA assumes constant prepayment speeds—unrealistic for securities whose cash flows are driven by rate movements. Vector analysis allows speeds to change over time (e.g., 165 PSA for months 1–36, then different speeds), while Monte Carlo simulation with 512–1,024 paths captures the full path dependency of MBS cash flows.

What Didn't Fit

Fabozzi's textbook is exhaustive, but a few specific examples and mechanisms from the notes deserve mention.

Interest Rates Are Mean-Reverting

Chapter 5 includes an important empirical finding that often gets buried in the math: interest rates tend to be mean-reverting over long periods. Extreme highs and lows eventually pull back toward historical averages. This is one of the few reliable patterns in rate forecasting and underpins many fixed-income strategies.

The Media Effect on Prepayments

Prepayment models account for rate incentives, burnout, and seasonality, but Chapter 12 notes a factor that models struggle to capture: the media effect. News coverage of low interest rates triggers prepayment spikes beyond what models predict, as homeowners who were not actively monitoring rates suddenly hear about refinancing opportunities on television or online.

The Five Largest Bond ETFs

As of 2020, the five largest bond ETFs were: AGG (iShares Core U.S. Aggregate, $78.7 billion), BND (Vanguard Total Bond Market, $59.4 billion), LQD (iShares Investment Grade Corporate, $55.9 billion), VCIT (Vanguard Intermediate-Term Corporate, $38.1 billion), and VCSH (Vanguard Short-Term Corporate, $30.7 billion). The dominance of passive vehicles in fixed income is a relatively recent development.

Dollar-Weighted Return Distortion

Chapter 28 includes a striking illustration of why dollar-weighted (IRR) returns are misleading for comparing managers. Two identical managers with the same investment decisions but different client withdrawal timing produce dollar-weighted returns of 28.4% versus 44.5%. The difference has nothing to do with skill — it is entirely driven by when clients happened to add or withdraw money.

The P&G Hedging Examples

Chapters 29 and 30 use Procter & Gamble bonds to illustrate hedging with futures and options. For futures: the PVBP ratio calculation produces a hedge ratio of 1.157, requiring 116 contracts, which a yield beta adjustment of 0.90 reduces to 105 contracts. For options: a protective put using 115 contracts at $192 each ($22,678 total) creates an effective floor of approximately $9.6 million for $10 million par. A covered call on the same bond generates $677,120 in premium income but caps the effective sale price at about 103.785. Neither strategy is universally better — each involves trade-offs across different rate scenarios.

Synthetic Securities

One of the most elegant concepts in Chapter 29: a long bond position combined with short futures creates a synthetic short-term riskless security. The formula RSP = CBP − FBP can be rearranged to create synthetic bonds or synthetic futures from the other components. This allows portfolio managers to shift allocations between stocks and bonds using futures far more efficiently than cash market trades — lower transaction costs and no disruption to underlying portfolio managers.

Implied Default Probability from CDS Spreads

Chapter 32 provides a useful approximation: implied default probability equals the CDS spread divided by 10,000, divided by one minus the recovery rate. A 500 basis point spread with 40% assumed recovery implies an 8.33% annual default probability. At 60% recovery, the same spread implies 12.5%. The formula is rough but gives a quick sanity check on credit risk pricing.

Cash Matching Variants

Chapter 27 describes two refinements to pure dedicated portfolios that the chapter coverage condensed. Symmetric cash matching allows short-term borrowing within the portfolio, reducing funding costs by 3–7% compared to strict cash matching. Combination matching cash-matches the first five years of liabilities (where precision matters most) and immunizes the remainder — a practical hybrid that balances certainty against cost.
