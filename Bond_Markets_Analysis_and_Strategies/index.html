Key Takeaways

  1. Duration is a price sensitivity measure, not a time measure. Despite common misinterpretation, duration measures approximate percentage price change for a 100bp yield change. A CMO tranche can have duration of 40 while underlying mortgages are 30-year—making no sense as a time measure.
  2. No truly risk-free assets exist. Even U.S. Treasuries carry inflation, liquidity, and potential default risk over long horizons. The global shortage of safe assets is a structural problem in modern finance.
  3. Convexity has measurable value. In volatile markets, positive convexity becomes extremely valuable. In stable markets, selling convexity via callable bonds can enhance yield—but the trade-off is asymmetric risk.
  4. Forward rates are hedgeable rates, not predictions. Academic studies consistently show forward rates are poor predictors of future spot rates, yet they remain central to bond analysis for the right reason: they represent rates investors can lock in.
  5. LIBOR’s demise requires a massive market transition. Over $200 trillion in financial products tied to LIBOR must transition to SOFR, with significant spread adjustment challenges due to SOFR’s backward-looking overnight nature versus LIBOR’s forward-looking term structure.
  6. Corporate bond liquidity has collapsed. Dealer inventory down 80% since 2007 while issuance up 50%. 38% of corporate bonds never trade, creating significant liquidity risk that traditional bond analysis ignores.
  7. Municipal default rates are severely underestimated. Official rating agency data shows 0.1% defaults, but broader studies reveal 2,521 defaults from 1970–2011—roughly 50x the official statistics.
  8. Mortgage prepayment modeling is enormously complex. Refinancing, turnover, and burnout effects must be modeled separately. PAC bonds lose protection when support bonds deplete. Monte Carlo simulation with 512–1,024 paths required for proper MBS valuation.
  9. Total return analysis is superior to YTM. Explicitly modeling reinvestment rate assumptions and horizon yield changes provides better evaluation than simplistic yield-to-maturity calculations that assume unrealistic conditions.
  10. Key rate duration captures real yield curve risk. Traditional duration only measures parallel shifts, which explain 91.6% of returns. Key rate duration quantifies exposure to specific maturity changes and butterfly shifts.
  11. CMO structures redistribute risk, they don’t eliminate it. Sequential-pay, PAC, Z-bonds, and floater/inverse floater structures enable precise risk allocation, but support bonds absorb massive variability.
  12. Effective duration is required for embedded options. Modified duration fails for callable, putable, or prepayable bonds. Differences can be dramatic: MBS modified duration 7 vs. effective duration 50.
  13. Avoiding losers matters more than picking winners in credit. Truncated index studies show removing the worst 10% of corporate bonds improves returns—the opposite of equities, where removing the best 10% destroys returns.
  14. Duration Times Spread (DTS) is superior to spread duration alone. Excess return volatility increases linearly with DTS, making it the proper measure of credit spread exposure across different-quality bonds.
  15. Green bonds and carbon policy enable climate finance. CPPBs tie coupons to emissions performance, creating market-based incentives. Bond investors can directly penalize carbon-intensive companies through higher spreads.

Contrarian Ideas

  • Duration misunderstood as time: The widespread interpretation of duration as “weighted average life” is misleading and can lead to poor investment decisions, especially for CMO tranches and leveraged structures.
  • Safe assets don’t exist: The foundational assumption of a risk-free rate is increasingly questioned. Treasuries carry inflation risk, liquidity events, and even sovereign default is possible over long horizons.
  • Forward rates don’t predict: Despite being central to bond analysis, forward rates are consistently poor predictors of future spot rates—they are hedgeable rates, not forecasts.
  • Municipal defaults severely underreported: Official 0.1% rates exclude unrated issues and use narrow definitions. Broader studies find 50x more defaults.
  • Convexity overpriced in calm markets: Investors systematically overpay for convexity protection during low-volatility periods, creating opportunities for those willing to sell convexity via callable bonds.
  • Negative rates can be economically rational: Despite seeming to violate basic finance, negative rates can make sense for central banks seeking currency depreciation and for investors paying for safety during crises.
  • Debt markets more powerful than equity for climate: While ESG activism focuses on shareholders, bond investors can directly penalize carbon-intensive companies through higher credit spreads and covenant restrictions.
  • EBITDA is a dangerous metric: It excludes capital expenditures that are absolutely required. Sino-Forest had 69% EBITDA margins yet never generated positive free cash flow in 15 years. EBITDA minus maintenance capex is the superior measure.
  • Fallen angels outperform: Bonds downgraded from investment-grade to high-yield suffer forced selling that pushes prices below fundamental value. They outperform peer HY bonds by 6.63% over two years after downgrade.
  • OAS constancy is theoretically unjustifiable: IO + PO = pass-through must hold, but their OAS levels cannot all remain constant under rate changes—a fundamental limitation of standard MBS analytics.

Author’s Key Proposals

  1. Adopt total return framework over YTM. Explicitly model reinvestment assumptions and horizon yields rather than relying on yield-to-maturity’s unrealistic conditions.
  2. Use key rate duration for real-world risk management. Traditional duration assumes parallel shifts; key rate duration provides accurate measurement for actual yield curve behavior.
  3. Implement effective duration for complex securities. Modified duration fails for embedded options; effective duration accounts for cash flow variability as rates change.
  4. Transition from LIBOR to SOFR with appropriate spread adjustments. Spread adjustment needed because SOFR lacks LIBOR’s credit component; legacy contracts require robust fallback provisions.
  5. Develop comprehensive liquidity assessment. Incorporate liquidity scores, trading frequency, and dealer inventory trends into bond selection and portfolio construction.
  6. Apply CMO structural analysis to all ABS. Understanding sequential-pay waterfalls, support mechanisms, and cash flow redirection applies universally across structured credit.
  7. Integrate climate risk into credit analysis. Carbon emissions, policy exposure, and transition risk should be standard components of fundamental credit evaluation.
  8. Use OAS methodology for fair value comparison. Option-adjusted spread enables apples-to-apples comparison across securities with different embedded option characteristics.
  9. Match portfolio duration to investment horizon. Immunization strategies require duration matching and periodic rebalancing; M-squared quantifies residual risk.
  10. Distinguish spread duration from modified duration. Credit portfolios need spread duration to quantify sensitivity to credit spread changes independent of Treasury rate movements.

Part I: Background

Chapter 1: Introduction to Bonds

The global bond market totals $114 trillion across six sectors: sovereign, semi-government, corporate, ABS/MBS, covered bonds, and collateralized debt obligations. A bond is a debt instrument requiring the issuer to repay principal plus interest on specified dates.

Bond Features

  • Maturity: short-term (<5 yrs), intermediate (5–12 yrs), long-term (>12 yrs); perpetual bonds have no maturity
  • Coupon types: fixed, floating (reference rate + margin), zero-coupon, step-up, deferred, PIK (pay-in-kind)
  • Embedded options: call (issuer can redeem early), put (investor can sell back), conversion (into equity)

Nine Risk Types

Interest-rate risk (most significant for investment-grade), call/prepayment risk, yield curve risk, reinvestment risk, credit risk (default + spread + downgrade), liquidity risk, exchange-rate risk, volatility risk, and inflation/purchasing-power risk.

Green & Climate Bonds

Green bonds finance environmental projects (>$500B cumulative issuance). Carbon policy performance bonds (CPPBs) tie the coupon to the issuer’s carbon emissions—penalizing those exceeding targets and rewarding those meeting them. Social bonds surged during COVID-19 to finance projects with positive social outcomes.

Chapter 2: Bond Pricing

Bond pricing rests on the time value of money: price equals the present value of all future cash flows. U.S. bonds use semiannual compounding (divide coupon and yield by 2, double periods).

  • Price-yield relationship: inverse and convex to the origin
  • Par bond: coupon = yield; premium: coupon > yield; discount: coupon < yield
  • Floating-rate notes: coupon = reference rate + quoted margin; price near par if margin matches market spread
  • Inverse floater: coupon = K − L × reference rate; duration = (1 + L) × fixed bond duration (leverage amplification)
  • Clean price = quoted; dirty price = clean + accrued interest (actual settlement amount)

Chapter 3: Measuring Yield

Yield to maturity is the discount rate equating PV of cash flows to price, but assumes reinvestment at YTM and holding to maturity—both unrealistic.

Total return (horizon analysis) is the superior approach: (1) compute coupon + reinvestment income, (2) project sale price at horizon, (3) calculate annualized return. This enables scenario analysis across different reinvestment and yield environments.

Discount margin for floaters: spread over reference rate equating PV of projected cash flows to price (assumes reference rate unchanged—a limitation).

Chapter 4: Duration and Convexity

Duration approximates the percentage price change for a 100bp yield change. It is the single most important risk measure for bonds.

“Duration is a price sensitivity measure, not a time measure. A CMO tranche can have duration of 40 while underlying mortgages are 30-year—making no sense as a time measure.”

Duration Types

  • Macaulay: weighted average time to cash flows
  • Modified = Macaulay / (1 + y/k): % price change approximation
  • Dollar = modified × price × 0.01: absolute price change per 100bp
  • Effective: allows cash flows to change with rates (required for embedded options)
  • Spread duration: sensitivity to credit spread changes
  • Key rate duration: sensitivity to yield changes at specific maturity points

Convexity

Second-order correction: % price change ≈ −duration × Δy + ½ × convexity × (Δy)². Positive convexity means price appreciation exceeds depreciation for equal yield changes—a desirable property. Callable bonds exhibit negative convexity when yields fall below the coupon rate.

Chapter 5: Interest Rate Theory

Three classical theories explain why interest rates exist and what determines their level:

  1. Time-preference (Böhm-Bawerk, Fisher): interest compensates for deferring consumption
  2. Loanable funds (Wicksell): rate set by supply/demand for funds
  3. Liquidity preference (Keynes): rate set by money supply/demand; speculative demand for money is key

Fisher’s Law: nominal rate = real rate + expected inflation. The natural rate (Wicksell) is the equilibrium rate consistent with price stability.

Negative interest rate policy: implemented by ECB, Denmark, Sweden, Switzerland, and Japan to stimulate borrowing and weaken currencies. Controversial due to potential harm to bank profitability and savings incentives.

Chapter 6: Term Structure of Interest Rates

The spot rate curve (zero-coupon yields) is derived from the par yield curve via bootstrapping. Forward rates are implied by spot rates and represent rates investors can lock in through bond selection.

Term Structure Theories

  • Pure expectations: forward rates = expected future spot rates (generally rejected empirically)
  • Liquidity theory: forward rates include a term premium for longer maturities
  • Preferred habitat: supply/demand at each maturity shapes the curve independently

LIBOR → SOFR transition: Over $200 trillion in products must migrate. SOFR is overnight, backward-looking, and secured (Treasury repo), versus LIBOR’s forward-looking, unsecured bank lending rate. Spread adjustment required because SOFR lacks LIBOR’s credit risk component.

Part II: Government Securities & Corporates

Chapter 7: Treasury & Agency Securities

Treasury securities: bills (zero-coupon, ≤1 yr), notes (2–10 yr), bonds (>10 yr), TIPS (inflation-adjusted principal), FRNs, and STRIPS (Treasury-created zeros). Issued via single-price (Dutch) auction. On-the-run issues are most liquid and serve as benchmark pricing references.

GSEs: Federal Home Loan Banks (~$1T outstanding), Fannie Mae/Freddie Mac (conservatorship since Sept 2008), FFCBS, TVA, Farmer Mac. GSE debt carries an implicit (not explicit) government guarantee; spreads to Treasuries reflect this ambiguity.

Chapter 8: Corporate Debt Instruments

Corporate bonds follow a strict seniority hierarchy in bankruptcy: secured → senior unsecured → subordinated → preferred → equity. The absolute priority rule holds in Chapter 7 liquidation but is frequently violated in Chapter 11 reorganization.

Market Structure Crisis

Dealer inventory collapsed 80% since 2007 while corporate issuance grew 50%. Some 38% of corporate bonds never trade. Medium-term notes (MTNs), structured notes, and commercial paper (<270 days, >$1T market) provide alternative funding channels.

CLO Structure

Collateralized loan obligations pool leveraged loans into tranches from AAA through equity. Coverage tests (OC and IC ratios) trigger cash flow diversion from junior to senior tranches if breached. Default rates average ~1.5% for all corporates, ~4.5% for high-yield, with recovery rates around 50%.

Chapter 9: Municipal Securities

General obligation bonds: backed by taxing power (unlimited or limited tax). Revenue bonds: backed by specific project revenue with trust indenture, rate covenants, and additional bonds tests. Moral obligation bonds: state is morally (not legally) obligated to replenish reserves.

Tax-equivalent yield = tax-exempt yield / (1 − marginal tax rate). Pre-refunded/escrowed-to-maturity bonds are defeased with Treasury securities and rated AAA. Default rates are severely underreported: 2,521 defaults from 1970–2011 versus official 0.1% cumulative rates.

Chapter 10: International Bonds

Global debt securities total $119.7 trillion. Foreign bonds include Yankee (U.S.), Samurai (Japan), and Bulldog (U.K.) bonds. Covered bonds (Pfandbriefe in Germany) provide dual recourse: claim on issuer plus segregated cover pool.

Emerging market credit analysis uses three pillars: serviceability (current payment ability), solvency (long-term debt sustainability), and structural fundamentals (institutions, governance, rule of law).

Part III: Mortgage & Structured Products

Chapter 11: Residential Mortgages

Mortgages face four principal risks: credit (borrower default), liquidity, price (rate changes), and prepayment. LTV ratio is the primary credit risk indicator. Nontraditional products include interest-only, negative amortization, and payment-option ARMs. Conforming loans meet GSE guidelines; nonconforming/jumbo exceed limits.

Chapter 12: Agency MBS Pass-Throughs

Agency pass-throughs distribute monthly principal and interest from mortgage pools. Prepayment modeling uses three components: housing turnover, cash-out refinancing, and rate/term refinancing.

Prepayment Dynamics

  • S-curve: refinancing response starts slow, accelerates, then flattens as rate incentive grows
  • Burnout effect: pools experiencing low rates see declining prepayments—willing/able refinancers already left
  • Negative convexity: when rates fall, accelerating prepayments limit price appreciation
  • TBA market: forward trading without specifying pools; UMBS (June 2019) unified Fannie/Freddie for better liquidity

Chapter 13: Collateralized Mortgage Obligations (CMOs)

CMOs redistribute MBS cash flows into tranches with different risk profiles:

  • Sequential-pay: principal paid to tranches in order (A, B, C, D)
  • Z-bonds: no cash flow until preceding tranches retired; accrued interest added to balance
  • Floater/inverse floater: created by splitting a tranche; inverse floater has amplified duration = (1+L) × underlying
  • PAC bonds: prepayment protection within a collar (e.g., 90–300 PSA); excess/shortfall absorbed by support bonds
  • IO strips: receive only interest (negative duration—price rises when rates rise); PO strips: receive only principal (benefits from faster prepayments)
Support bonds are the most volatile CMO tranche, absorbing all prepayment variability. When support bonds are depleted, the PAC becomes “broken” and loses its protection.

Chapter 14: Nonagency RMBS

Credit enhancement through senior-subordinated structure (subordinate tranches absorb losses first), excess spread, and overcollateralization. The shifting interest mechanism locks subordinate prepayment share to seniors during early years. Strategic default became a significant factor in 2008–2012 as borrowers who could pay chose not to on underwater mortgages.

Chapter 15: Commercial MBS (CMBS)

Key metrics: DSC ratio (net operating income / debt service) and LTV. Four call protection forms: lockout, defeasance (substitute Treasury portfolio), penalty points, and yield maintenance. Balloon risk = borrower must refinance large principal at maturity. Three servicer types handle routine administration through workouts.

Chapter 16: Asset-Backed Securities

Securitization creates a true sale to a special-purpose vehicle, separating assets from originator bankruptcy. Major types: auto loan ABS (most predictable prepayments), credit card receivables (revolving structure with lockout period), and rate reduction bonds (extremely stable). Dodd-Frank requires 5% risk retention (“skin in the game”).

Chapter 17: Collective Investment Vehicles

ETFs dominate bond fund innovation: trade intraday, tax-efficient via in-kind redemption, but tracking error increases with benchmark illiquidity. During the 2008 crisis, high-yield ETF tracking errors exploded to 5–6% as the creation/redemption mechanism strained.

Hedge fund bond strategies: convergence trading (exploit historical yield relationships) and distressed investing (buy undervalued bonds near bankruptcy). Detroit pension bonds at 41 cents; Puerto Rico 8% bonds at 93.

Part IV: Analysis & Valuation

Chapter 18: Bond Liquidity & Trading

Liquidity has five dimensions: tightness (low costs), immediacy (fast execution), depth (many participants), breadth (sufficient volume), and resiliency (absorb shocks). Trading costs include explicit (bid-ask spread) and hidden (market impact, timing, opportunity cost).

Electronic Trading Revolution

The traditional intermediation model (broker-dealers as principals) has declined since 2008. Electronic platforms fill the vacuum:

  • Multi-dealer: consolidate quotes (Tradeweb, MarketAxess)
  • All-to-all: any member trades with any other; buy-side provides ~50% of liquidity; enables portfolio/basket trades
  • RFQ model: request pricing from multiple dealers; order-driven model: submit orders matched against posted quotes

Chapter 19: Embedded Options Analysis

Static spread (zero-volatility spread) is the spread over the entire Treasury spot rate curve, found by trial-and-error. For bonds with embedded options, this isn’t enough—volatility affects option value.

Binomial Interest-Rate Tree

The arbitrage-free binomial model prices bonds with embedded options by modeling rate evolution as a lognormal random walk: rH = rL × e2σ. The tree is calibrated to on-the-run issue prices. For callable bonds, when calculated value exceeds call price at any node, replace with call price.

OAS and Effective Duration

  • OAS = spread over the binomial tree making model value equal market price; “option-adjusted” because cash flows reflect option exercise
  • Option value in spread terms = static spread − OAS
  • Effective duration: shift yields, rebuild tree, add OAS, compute new prices; duration = (P− − P+) / [2P0Δy]
  • Higher volatility → lower callable bond value (larger option subtracted); higher putable bond value (larger option added)

Chapter 20: RMBS Analysis (Monte Carlo)

MBS cash flows are path-dependent: this month’s prepayment depends on the entire history of rates, not just the current level. This rules out binomial trees and requires Monte Carlo simulation.

Methodology

  • Generate 512–1,024 interest-rate paths; each month produces discount rate + refinancing rate
  • Refinancing rate feeds prepayment model → generates cash flows along each path
  • Theoretical value = average PV across all paths
  • OAS = spread K added to all spot rates on all paths making average PV = market price
  • Distribution matters: PAC bond (value 88, std dev 1) vs. support bond (value 77, std dev 10)

FactSet Agency Pass-Through Example (FNMA 3.5% 30-Year)

Z-spread 84.53 bps; OAS 90.25 bps (lognormal); option cost 30.30 bps. Effective duration 2.171; effective convexity −0.609. For CMO FHLMC.4563: Z-bond has longest duration (11.24) and highest OAS (228.4 bps).

Total return analysis is the correct performance measure: projected cash flows + reinvestment income + horizon price. Neither cash flow yield nor Monte Carlo alone tells whether investment objectives are met.

Chapter 21: Convertible Bonds

A convertible bond grants the right to convert into a predetermined number of shares (conversion ratio). The minimum value = max(conversion value, straight value). Key analytics: market conversion premium, premium payback period, premium over straight value.

Option Measures

  • Delta: sensitivity to stock price (0–1); also the hedge ratio for market-neutral positioning
  • Gamma: change in delta (analogous to convexity)
  • Vega: sensitivity to volatility; if implied volatility < historical, the option is cheap

Profiles: balanced (delta 55–75%), equity sensitive (in-the-money), busted (deep out-of-the-money), distressed (busted + near bankruptcy). Eight arbitrage strategies identified by Stefanini including cash flow, volatility, and gamma trading.

Chapter 22: Corporate Credit Analysis

Three core areas: covenants (limits on management discretion), collateral (assets in default), and ability to pay (business + governance + financial risk).

Financial Risk Ratios

  • Interest coverage: EBIT / total interest (EBITDA version always higher but masks capex needs)
  • Leverage: total debt / capitalization; total debt / EBITDA
  • Cash flow hierarchy (S&P): operating → free operating → discretionary → prefinancing
  • Free cash flow = operating cash flow − capex; distinguish maintenance vs. growth capex

Case Study: Sino-Forest Corporation

Failed the business model test: claimed “forest plantation operator” but was a middleman with no control over suppliers or customers. Never generated positive FCF in 15 years despite double-digit revenue growth and 69% EBITDA margins. All three CRAs (BB-category) failed to warn investors. Lesson: if the fundamental business model is in question, good financial metrics are irrelevant.

Case Study: Sirius XM

Covenants were the single most important factor in the 5.25% 2022 bond outperformance. Company granted security to bypass restrictive covenants, triggered covenant suspension upon investment-grade upgrade, then immediately issued $1.5B new debt. The 5.25% bonds outperformed other unsecured bonds by 150–200 bps.

Chapter 23: Credit Risk Modeling

  • Structural models (Black-Scholes-Merton): bond value = assets − equity call option; default when asset value falls below debt. Extensions: Geske compound option, first-passage time (Black-Cox), stochastic rates
  • Reduced-form models (Jarrow-Turnbull, Duffie-Singleton): default as exogenous Poisson process; quickly calibrate to market; favored by 90%+ of practitioners
  • Incomplete-information models (Giesecke-Goldberg): structural/reduced-form hybrid; investors don’t know default barrier (accounts for fraud)

Credit ratings are inaccurate default probability measures; simple reduced-form models dominate ratings for forecasting corporate failure.

Part V: Portfolio Management

Chapters 24–25: Portfolio Strategies & Construction

Strategy Spectrum

Pure indexing → enhanced indexing → active management → absolute return → liability-driven. Core/satellite: low-cost indexed core (beta) + active satellites (alpha). Studies show asset allocation is the most important performance driver.

Bond Benchmark Problems

  • Duration set by issuers (cost minimization), not investors (return maximization)
  • Bum’s problem: more debt = higher weight → overweights riskier issuers
  • Alternatives: issuer caps, GDP-weighting (PIMCO), duration-weighting, laddered indexes

Yield Curve Strategies

Bullet (concentrated at one maturity), barbell (two extremes), ladder (equal across maturities). Barbell has higher convexity but lower yield (~25 bps sacrifice). Bullet outperforms for small parallel shifts; barbell for large shifts or flattening. Historical: 91.6% of Treasury returns from parallel shifts + twists; 3.4% from butterfly.

Portfolio Construction

Cell-based approach: divide benchmark into duration/maturity/sector/quality cells. Multi-factor models: decompose risk into systematic (yield curve, swap spread, volatility, sector spreads) and idiosyncratic. Example: 50-position portfolio vs. 5,693 benchmark positions—idiosyncratic risk accounted for 87% of tracking error despite small standalone magnitude.

Chapter 26: Managing Corporate Bond Portfolios

Risk-Return Asymmetry

Bond upside is capped (coupons + par); downside is principal loss (partially offset by recovery). Removing the worst 10% of performers improves bond index returns (opposite of equities). Avoiding losers is far more important than picking winners in credit portfolios.

Duration Times Spread (DTS)

DTS = spread duration × credit spread. Superior to spread duration alone—excess return volatility increases linearly with DTS across different quality segments.

Empirical Duration and Duration Multipliers

High-yield bonds have two risk factors: interest-rate and equity risk. Standard modified duration fails to capture the equity component. Duration multiplier M = 1 + ρ(σs/σy), typically <1 since correlation between Treasury yields and spreads is negative. A-rated: M~0.85; BBB consumer cyclicals: M=0.63.

Seven Credit Relative Value Trades

Yield-spread pickup, credit-upside, credit-defense, new-issue swap, sector-rotation, curve-adjustment, and structure trades. Fallen angels outperform peer HY bonds by 6.63% over two years after downgrade due to forced selling pressure.

Chapter 27: Liability-Driven Investing

Immunization (Redington, 1952): match asset duration to liability duration so rising rates offset through higher reinvestment income. Only works for parallel shifts and flat curves; requires rebalancing.

M-Squared and Immunization Risk

M² = ∑[CFt(t−H)²/(1+y)t]—quantifies risk from cash flow dispersion around liability date H. M² = 0 for a zero-coupon bond maturing at the liability date (ideal). Bullet portfolios have less immunization risk than barbells.

Cash Flow Matching

Select bonds whose cash flows match the liability stream (working backward). No duration requirement, no rebalancing needed, zero immunization risk (barring defaults)—but costs 3–7% more than multi-period immunization.

De-Risking Solutions

Plan freezing (hard, partial, soft), termination, and risk transference (lump-sum buyout, annuity buy-in/buy-out, longevity swaps).

Chapter 28: Performance Measurement

Time-weighted (geometric) return: preferred for manager evaluation—eliminates distortion from client cash flows. Dollar-weighted (IRR): affected by contributions/withdrawals beyond manager control; useful for fund growth perspective but not manager comparison. Performance attribution decomposes active return into sector, factor, and security-selection components.

Part VI: Derivatives

Chapter 29: Interest-Rate Futures

Futures are standardized contracts with clearinghouse guarantees. Key contracts: Treasury bond/note futures (quoted in 32nds), Eurodollar (being replaced by SOFR futures), EURIBOR.

Delivery Options and Cheapest-to-Deliver

The short has three options: quality/swap option (choose which bond), timing option (when in delivery month), wild card option (notify after market close). Cheapest-to-deliver = bond with highest implied repo rate.

Portfolio Duration Control

Number of contracts = (target dollar duration − current dollar duration) / futures dollar duration. Positive = buy; negative = sell. Example: $48M portfolio adjusting from duration 2.97 to benchmark 3.68 requires buying 68 five-year note futures.

Hedging

Hedge ratio = PVBP of hedged bond / PVBP of CTD × conversion factor × yield beta. Yield beta adjusts for differential yield movements between hedged bond and deliverable. Basis risk remains when cash-futures relationship changes unpredictably.

Chapter 30: Interest-Rate Options

Futures options are most popular for interest rates. Black-Scholes has three critical limitations for bonds: allows unlimited prices (impossible), assumes constant rates, and assumes constant variance as maturity approaches.

Option Greeks

  • Delta: price sensitivity to underlying (0 deep OTM → ~1 deep ITM)
  • Gamma: rate of change of delta (option price convexity)
  • Theta: time decay
  • Kappa/Vega: sensitivity to volatility changes

Hedge Strategies

Protective put: buy puts to protect downside while preserving upside. Covered call: sell calls to generate income at the cost of capping upside. No universally “best” strategy—each involves trade-offs across rate scenarios.

Chapter 31: Swaps, FRAs, Caps & Floors

An interest-rate swap exchanges fixed for floating payments on a notional principal. Fixed-rate payer benefits from rate increases; fixed-rate receiver benefits from decreases.

Swap Applications

  • Asset/liability management: banks convert fixed loans to floating; insurers convert floating to fixed
  • Structured notes: combine MTN issuance with swap (e.g., issue inverse floater + swap = synthetic fixed at below-market rate)
  • Duration modification: fixed-rate receiver increases portfolio duration; payer decreases it

Caps, Floors, and Collars

A cap is a package of call options on interest rates (caplets); a floor is a package of puts (floorlets). A collar = long cap + short floor. Banks buy caps to ceiling funding costs; insurance companies buy floors to protect guaranteed rates.

Swaptions

Options on swaps. “3 into 10” = right to enter a 10-year swap in 3 years. Payer swaption = right to pay fixed (profit if rates rise); receiver swaption = right to receive fixed (profit if rates fall).

Chapter 32: Credit Default Swaps

Protection buyer pays periodic premium; protection seller pays if credit event occurs. Post-2009 standardization: 1% running spread (investment-grade) or 5% (high-yield) with upfront payment for the difference.

Credit Events

Bankruptcy, failure to pay, restructuring (most controversial), acceleration, cross default, downgrade, repudiation/moratorium. Narrowly tailored credit event (2019) introduced to address manufactured defaults (Hovnanian/Blackstone case).

Settlement

  • Physical: buyer delivers bonds, seller pays face value (buyer picks cheapest-to-deliver)
  • Cash: auction determines recovery price; payout = par − auction price
  • Extreme examples: Lehman Brothers 8.625% recovery; JCPenney 0.125% recovery

Index CDS and Tranches

CDX.NA.IG: 125 investment-grade names; CDX.NA.HY: 100 high-yield. Tranche CDS defined by attachment/detachment points: equity (0–3%, 33x leverage), mezzanine tiers, and senior/super-senior.

CDS Advantages Over Cash Bonds

Leverage (much less capital than cash bonds), ease of shorting, fixed financing rate (vs. variable repo), and permanent financing (repo can be withdrawn; CDS financing lasts the full term). Protection seller = leveraged unfunded long; protection buyer = short position in reference entity.
