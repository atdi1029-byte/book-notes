Key Takeaways

  1. Risk is a choice, not a fate: The word "risk" comes from the Italian risicare — "to dare." The revolutionary idea separating modern times from the past is that humans can quantify uncertainty and make calculated decisions about it.
  2. The 1654 turning point: Pascal and Fermat's correspondence on dividing stakes in an unfinished game launched probability theory. Every major risk-management concept traces back to this moment.
  3. Utility, not expected value: Daniel Bernoulli (1738) showed that satisfaction from wealth increases is inversely proportionate to existing wealth. A poor person values $100 far more than a rich one — the foundation of risk aversion theory.
  4. Regression to the mean: Galton's discovery that extreme outcomes drift back toward the average is one of the most powerful — and most ignored — patterns in nature and markets.
  5. Risk vs. uncertainty: Frank Knight distinguished measurable risk (calculable odds) from true uncertainty (unknowable). Most real-world decisions involve uncertainty, not risk.
  6. Diversification is the closest thing to a free lunch: Markowitz proved (1952) that the variance of a portfolio matters more than the variance of individual holdings.
  7. Humans are systematically irrational: Kahneman and Tversky's Prospect Theory showed loss aversion, framing effects, mental accounting, and the endowment effect are hardwired biases.
  8. The wildness always lies in wait: Even with all our tools, "Nature has established patterns originating in the return of events, but only for the most part." That qualification is the whole story.

Part I: To 1200 — Beginnings

The Greeks and Romans

The Greeks had philosophy, logic, democracy, and geometry — but no theory of probability. Despite their love of gambling, they never crossed the boundary from games to systematic analysis. Why?

  • The Greeks preferred deductive reasoning (from theory to specific) over empirical observation
  • They believed the future was controlled by the gods, not calculable by mortals
  • Their numbering system (letters for numbers) made calculation nearly impossible
  • Aristotle distinguished between events "that cannot be other than they are" (certain) and events "that can be other" (uncertain) — but never quantified the distinction

The Hindu-Arabic Revolution

The breakthrough came from the East. Hindu mathematicians invented zero, place-value notation, and the numerals we use today. Arab scholars preserved and transmitted this knowledge.

  • Fibonacci (Leonardo Pisano, ~1170–1240): Published Liber Abaci (1202), introducing Hindu-Arabic numerals to Europe
  • This made practical calculation possible for the first time — without it, probability theory could never have developed
  • Fibonacci also discovered the famous sequence (1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13...) — his rabbit breeding problem

Part II: 1200–1700 — A Thousand Outstanding Facts

Luca Pacioli and the Problem of Points

In 1494, Pacioli posed the puzzle that would eventually birth probability theory: if two players are in an unfinished game, how should the stakes be divided fairly? He got it wrong — but the question lingered for 200 years.

Girolamo Cardano (1501–1576)

Physician, mathematician, gambler, astrologer, and "one of the most extraordinary characters in the history of mathematics." His Liber de Ludo Aleae was the first systematic analysis of probability.

  • Recognized that the probability of rolling a particular face on a die was 1/6
  • Understood the concept of combinations and sample spaces
  • But couldn't solve Pacioli's problem either

Pascal and Fermat (1654) — The Birth of Probability

The Chevalier de Méré, a wealthy gambler, challenged Blaise Pascal with the Problem of Points. Pascal wrote to Pierre de Fermat. Their correspondence — just a few letters — launched probability theory.

  • Pascal's approach: The Arithmetic Triangle (Pascal's Triangle) — a systematic way to calculate all possible combinations
  • Fermat's approach: Enumerated all possible outcomes and counted favorable ones
  • Both arrived at the same answer, validating each other's methods
  • Key insight: For the first time, the future could be analyzed mathematically, not just philosophically

The Port-Royal Logic (1662)

The monks of Port-Royal published La Logique, which introduced a crucial refinement: when making decisions under uncertainty, consider not just the probability but the consequences. "Fear of harm ought to be proportional not merely to the gravity of the harm, but also to the probability of the event."

John Graunt and the Birth of Statistics (1662)

A London haberdasher who analyzed the Bills of Mortality — weekly death records — and created the first statistical analysis of a population. Discovered patterns: more boys born than girls, certain diseases had regular frequencies, urban death rates exceeded rural.

The Bernoulli Family

Jacob Bernoulli — Law of Large Numbers

After 20 years of work, Jacob proved that with enough observations, the observed frequency of an event will converge on its true probability. "Even the most Stupid of Men" could figure this out — but the formal proof was monumental.

  • The law works only with infinite or near-infinite repetitions
  • Leibniz warned: "Nature has established patterns... but only for the most part" — the qualification that haunts all risk management

Daniel Bernoulli — Utility Theory (1738)

Published "Exposition of a New Theory on the Measurement of Risk." Central idea: "The utility resulting from any small increase in wealth will be inversely proportionate to the quantity of goods previously possessed."

  • The St. Petersburg Paradox: A game with infinite expected value that no rational person would pay much to play — because the utility of the later, enormous payoffs is negligible to someone who already has money
  • This explains risk aversion, insurance, and diversification — concepts that would take 200+ years to formalize

Part III: 1700–1900 — Measurement Unlimited

Abraham de Moivre and the Bell Curve

French Huguenot refugee in London, published The Doctrine of Chances (1733). Discovered the normal distribution — the bell curve — and the concept of standard deviation. Most observations cluster around the mean; deviations follow a predictable pattern.

Thomas Bayes and Inverse Probability

An English minister who solved the problem of working backwards from outcomes to causes. Bayesian analysis allows updating beliefs based on new evidence — essential for dealing with a world where we rarely know the underlying probabilities.

Francis Galton and Regression to the Mean

Darwin's cousin, obsessed with measurement. While studying hereditary traits, he discovered that extreme characteristics in parents tend to produce less extreme characteristics in children — and vice versa.

  • Sweet pea experiment: Large seeds produced large but smaller-than-parent offspring; small seeds produced small but larger-than-parent offspring
  • "The mean height of children who have a mid-parent's height of 72 inches turned out to be 69.5 inches"
  • This works in both directions — nature continuously pulls toward the average
  • Markets application: Hot stocks cool off; beaten-down stocks recover. Outperforming fund managers regress. Winning streaks end.

Quetelet and "L'Homme Moyen"

Belgian astronomer who applied the bell curve to human measurements — height, weight, chest measurements of soldiers. Proclaimed the "average man" as nature's ideal. His enthusiasm for the normal distribution led him to apply it where it didn't belong — a cautionary tale about over-trusting models.

Part IV: 1900–1960 — Clouds of Vagueness and the Demand for Precision

Frank Knight — Risk vs. Uncertainty

In Risk, Uncertainty, and Profit (1921), Knight made the crucial distinction:

  • Risk: Outcomes unknown, but probabilities are known or calculable (rolling dice, insurance tables)
  • Uncertainty: Neither outcomes nor probabilities are known (will there be a war? a revolution in technology?)
  • "Profit arises from uncertainty" — if outcomes were predictable, competition would eliminate profits

Keynes — "In the Long Run We Are All Dead"

Attacked the foundations of frequency-based probability. Many real-world events happen only once — you can't calculate the "probability" of the next war by counting past wars.

  • "Animal spirits" drive investment decisions more than rational calculation
  • Acknowledged the weight of evidence matters: past information enriches (or undermines) our confidence in a probability judgment

Von Neumann and Morgenstern — Game Theory (1944)

The Theory of Games and Economic Behavior transformed how we think about strategic decisions. Key insight: in games (and markets), your opponent is actively trying to counter your strategy.

  • Poker analogy: bluffing as optimal strategy when opponents are rational
  • "Real life consists of bluffing, of little tactics of deception" — unlike games of pure chance
  • Minimax strategy: minimize the maximum loss your opponent can inflict

Harry Markowitz — Portfolio Theory (1952)

"Diversification is both observed and sensible; a rule of behavior which does not imply the superiority of diversification must be rejected both as a hypothesis and as a maxim."

  • The variance of a portfolio depends on the covariance of its components, not just their individual variances
  • Combining assets that don't move in lockstep reduces risk without proportionally reducing return
  • The "efficient frontier" — the set of portfolios offering maximum return for each level of risk
  • Markowitz's dissertation committee almost rejected it: Milton Friedman said, "We cannot award the Ph.D. for a dissertation that is not economics, not mathematics, not business administration."

Kenneth Arrow — The Limits of Insurance

Born end of WWI, Arrow became convinced most people overestimate the information available to them.

  • Weather forecaster anecdote: "The Commanding General is well aware that the forecasts are no good. However, he needs them for planning purposes."
  • Arrow's "complete market": An imaginary institution insuring against any loss of any kind. The world would be better if we could insure against every future possibility — but moral hazard prevents it
  • S&L crisis of the 1980s: deposit insurance gave owners a chance to "win big if things went right and to lose little if things went wrong"

Part V: Degrees of Belief — Exploring Uncertainty

Kahneman and Tversky — Prospect Theory

Daniel Kahneman (psychologist) and Amos Tversky (mathematical psychologist) systematically demolished the assumption that humans are rational decision-makers.

Core Findings:

  • Loss aversion: Losses hurt roughly 2.5x more than equivalent gains feel good
  • Asymmetric risk-taking: When ahead, we play it safe (locking in gains); when behind, we gamble recklessly (trying to get even) — exactly backwards from rational theory
  • Failure of invariance: How a choice is framed changes our decision, even when the mathematics are identical. A surgery with "90% survival rate" sounds better than one with "10% mortality rate"
  • Mental accounting: Money is treated differently depending on which mental "pocket" it comes from. Lottery winnings are spent more freely than wages
  • Anchoring: We overweight the first number we hear, even if it's irrelevant
  • Availability bias: We judge frequency by how easily examples come to mind — plane crashes seem more common than they are

Thaler and Behavioral Finance

  • Endowment effect: Coffee mug experiment — owners wouldn't sell below $5.25; buyers wouldn't pay more than $2.25 for the identical mug
  • "Does the Stock Market Overreact?" (1985): Investors overweight new information, causing prices to systematically overshoot. Value stocks outperform over time because fear, regret, and myopia cause mispricing
  • Dividends and self-control: Investors insist on dividends even though selling shares produces identical cash — mental accounting prevents them from treating capital and income as interchangeable

Hypothesis Testing and Statistical Significance

  • We don't accept or reject hypotheses — we reject or not-reject. Certainty is impossible
  • 95% confidence convention: The modern equivalent of Bernoulli's "moral certainty." He required 1000/1001 confidence; we require only 1 in 20
  • 78% underperformance: Over the past decade, 78% of actively managed equity funds underperformed the S&P 500 index. But even this doesn't reach statistical significance for most individual managers

Chapter 18: The Fantastic System of Side Bets

Derivatives Explained

Financial instruments with no intrinsic value — they derive their value from some other asset. They exist to transfer risk from those who can't bear it to those willing to carry it.

  • Futures: Contracts for future delivery at specified prices (Chicago Board of Trade since 1865)
  • Options: Right (not obligation) to buy or sell at a prearranged price (Dutch tulip bubble, 1600s)
  • Confederate Cotton Loan (1863): A brilliant early derivative — principal in sterling/francs, with bondholder option to collect in cotton. Hedged currency risk, inflation risk, and Confederate survival risk simultaneously

Black-Scholes-Merton (1973)

The key insight: an investor who hedges an option by simultaneously trading the underlying stock creates a riskless position. Five inputs determine an option's value — but only volatility (the "whole trick in the formula") must be estimated.

  • Published the same year the Chicago Board Options Exchange opened
  • Fischer Black, Myron Scholes, and Robert C. Merton — "their theoretical work made the new exchange a success"

Portfolio Insurance and the 1987 Crash

Hayne Leland's brainstorm: insure portfolios against loss using dynamic hedging — sell stocks as prices fall, buy as they rise. By 1987, $60 billion in equity assets were covered.

  • Critical flaw: Unlike real insurance (with reserves) or real options (with bound counterparties), portfolio insurance relied on the market itself providing liquidity. "No reserves or collateral exist to guarantee that the liquidity will be there when called upon."
  • October 19, 1987: Dow fell 500+ points. Insured portfolios selling into the crash amplified the panic. The programs underestimated volatility and overestimated liquidity.

The 1994 Derivatives Disasters

Procter & Gamble, Gibson Greetings, and Metallgesellschaft suffered massive losses — but because they speculated instead of hedging.

  • P&G: Bet interest rates would keep falling after 4 years of decline. "Nobody in the executive offices had read Galton — regression to the mean was unknown to them." Result: 14.5% and 16.4% interest obligations
  • Gibson Greetings: Classic Prospect Theory — at $17.5M loss, chose to gamble rather than accept a certain loss. Lost $20.7M
  • The lesson: "The instrument is the messenger; the investor is the message." Derivatives don't cause losses — human decisions do

Chapter 19: Awaiting the Wildness

The Limits of All Models

"The real trouble with this world of ours is not that it is an unreasonable world, nor even that it is a reasonable one. The commonest kind of trouble is that it is nearly reasonable, but not quite.... its exactitude is obvious, but its inexactitude is hidden; its wildness lies in wait." — G.K. Chesterton
  • Chaos theory: Rejects the symmetry of the bell curve. Results are not proportionate to causes — "the balanced cone that topples over in response to a very slight tremor." But its accomplishments appear modest compared to its claims
  • The logician's trap: "Past data from real life constitute a sequence of events rather than a set of independent observations." History gives us only one sample
  • Risk management creates new risks: "Research reveals that seatbelts encourage drivers to drive more aggressively." Portfolio insurance encouraged higher equity exposure; diversification is used to justify risk in untested areas
  • The computer as oracle: "Nothing is more soothing or more persuasive than the computer screen... we tend to forget that the computer only answers questions; it does not ask them"

Bernstein's Conclusion

From Cardano the gambler to Pascal the geometer, from the Bernoullis through Gauss and Galton, from von Neumann to Arrow and Markowitz — all have transformed risk from chance of loss into opportunity for gain, "from FATE and ORIGINAL DESIGN to sophisticated, probability-based forecasts of the future, and from helplessness to choice."

But Leibniz's qualification endures: nature acts only "for the most part." Without that imprecision, there would be no risk. And without risk, there would be no reward.

Relevance to Trading

  1. Regression to the mean: Extreme winning or losing streaks will revert. Don't chase hot stocks or panic on drawdowns.
  2. Loss aversion trap: The urge to hold losers and cut winners is hardwired. Systematic rules (stop-losses, take-profits) override this bias.
  3. Prospect Theory in action: When losing, you'll want to gamble bigger to get even — exactly when you should be cutting risk.
  4. Mental accounting: Don't treat "house money" differently from "real money." Every dollar in your account has equal value.
  5. Overconfidence in models: Your backtests work on historical data, which is one sample of the universe. "The wildness lies in wait."
  6. Diversification is still the closest thing to a free lunch — Markowitz's insight hasn't been improved upon.
  7. The derivative lesson: Use risk tools to hedge, never to speculate. The P&G executives ignored Galton's regression to the mean and paid dearly.
