# Hedge Fund Market Wizards: How Winning Traders Win

**Author:** Jack D. Schwager
**Publisher:** Wiley
**Year:** 2012
**Pages:** 447

---

## Overview

The third installment in Jack Schwager's legendary Market Wizards series, *Hedge Fund Market Wizards* presents in-depth interviews with 15 of the world's most successful hedge fund managers. Unlike the first two books which focused on futures and commodity traders, this volume explores the contemporary hedge fund landscape spanning macro traders, multi-strategy players, and equity investors. Each interview reveals not just what these traders do, but how they think—offering timeless lessons on risk management, position sizing, market psychology, and the importance of finding a methodology that fits one's personality.

The book is structured in three parts: **Part One: Macro Men** (Colm O'Shea, Ray Dalio, Larry Benedict, Scott Ramsey, Jaffray Woodriff); **Part Two: Multistrategy Players** (Edward Thorp, Jamie Mai, Michael Platt); **Part Three: Equity Traders** (Steve Clark, Martin Taylor, Tom Claugus, Joe Vidich, Kevin Daly, Jimmy Balodimas, Joel Greenblatt). The conclusion distills **40 Market Wizard Lessons** applicable across all trading styles.

---

## Core Themes

### 1. There Is No Holy Grail
The diversity of approaches—from Ray Dalio's systematic fundamental macro to Jimmy Balodimas's intuitive short-biased trading—proves there are multiple paths to success. What works brilliantly for one trader may be disastrous for another.

### 2. Find What Fits Your Personality
Every successful trader developed a methodology aligned with their natural temperament, skills, and beliefs. O'Shea emphasizes: "If I try to teach you what I do, you will fail because you are not me."

### 3. Risk Management Is Everything
Despite wildly different approaches, all traders share obsessive focus on risk control. From Larry Benedict's 2% monthly loss limit to Michael Platt's 3% trader cutoff rules, protecting capital takes priority over maximizing returns.

### 4. Flexibility Trumps Conviction
The best traders hold strong views but remain willing to reverse positions instantly when evidence contradicts their thesis. O'Shea's April 2009 reversal from bearish to bullish transformed a potentially disastrous year into a winner.

### 5. Markets Reflect Human Nature
Since markets are driven by human emotion and behavior, the lessons remain timeless despite constantly changing market structures. As Ed Seykota notes in the Foreword, trading wisdom endures because "the constancies of human nature" persist.

## Part One: Macro Men

### Colm O'Shea — Knowing When It's Raining

**Edge:** Recognizing significant developments already happening but not yet noticed by markets. His 2008 success stemmed not from predicting the crisis, but from noticing money markets had frozen in August 2007—a signal most ignored.

**Philosophy:** "Fundamentals are not about forecasting the weather for tomorrow, but rather noticing that it is raining today." Rejects storytelling approach to macro; emphasizes implementation over narrative. Views trading ideas as testable hypotheses to be immediately abandoned if invalidated.

**Key Insight:** Participates in bubbles via long options (limited downside, unlimited upside), then waits to sell markets *after* they've turned—never tries to pick tops. "Taking Nasdaq as an example, you want to be selling Nasdaq at 4,000, but only after it has gone to 5,000."

**Track Record:** 11.3% annualized net return (2004-2011) with 8.1% volatility, worst monthly loss only 3.7%. Ran fund at half the risk level of typical macro managers; if scaled to match peers' volatility, would have returned ~23% annually.

---

### Ray Dalio — The Man Who Loves Mistakes

**Edge:** "Timeless and universal" approach testing strategies across all time periods and countries. To understand current U.S. deleveraging, studied 1930s depression and Japan's post-bubble era. Depression gauge developed 8 years *before* 2008 crisis.

**Philosophy:** Radical transparency culture at Bridgewater. Recording all meetings, encouraging thoughtful disagreement, and treating mistakes as learning opportunities. "The road to success is paved with mistakes."

**Diversification:** Calls it the "Holy Grail of investing"—can improve return/risk by factor of 5:1 if assets truly uncorrelated. Rejects traditional correlation metrics; instead focuses on underlying **drivers** that determine price behavior. Markets behave differently in deleveragings vs. recessions—correlations shift with regime changes.

**Key Trade:** Saw 2008 coming through systematic analysis of deleveraging indicators. Watched banks' 10Ks to mark balance sheets to market, monitoring appropriate factors based on historical deleveraging patterns.

**Long-Term View:** U.S. in Stage 5 decline of a 100-150 year cycle. Fifth phase (decline) typically lasts ~20 years. Doesn't have to be catastrophic if well-managed, but requires spreading problems over long period with nominal interest rates below nominal growth rates.

### Larry Benedict — Beyond Three Strikes

**Background:** Blew up three separate trading accounts before age 25. First account lost in 1987 crash (short straddles), second lost $16,000 in one month, third pulled by friend. Never gave up: "I just wanted to learn how to do it and win."

**Breakthrough:** Learning **correlation trading**—watching how markets move relative to each other. "I learned to pay attention to how markets moved relative to each other. I became a correlation trader—the same thing I still do today."

**Trading Style:** Pure tape reading with extremely short duration (seconds to two days). Primary market is S&P futures traded both standalone and versus other liquid futures. Makes hundreds of transactions daily, grinding out returns through superior execution.

**Risk Control:** If down more than 2% month-to-date any day, clears out everything and trades smaller until profitable again. Extreme risk aversion stems from early losing experiences: "I just don't want to lose again."

**Quote:** "I am the worst sore loser you ever met. I am brutal on myself."

### Scott Ramsey — Low-Risk Futures Trader

**Evolution:** Started pure technical but realized "need to embrace fundamentals alongside technicals" to make big money. Combines directional bias from fundamentals with technical confirmation for entries.

**Major Trade:** 1990s long bonds trade. General consensus: government borrowing bearish for bonds. Contrarian insight: "If everyone thinks this way, and it seems so logical, then if the market goes the other way, everyone is going to be wrong, and they will have to cover their shorts." Massive winning trade going opposite of consensus.

**Risk Management:** Typically risks ~10 basis points per trade from entry point, exits 90% of time on first day if position not working. "Might lose 10 times until 11th works—then big win makes up for small losses." Worst real-world month without large open profit: 2-3%.

**Market Correlation:** Since 2008, "Dog is stocks, tail is everything else." Post-crisis environment extremely correlated—must know equity direction and watch influence on other markets. Buys strongest, sells weakest markets in sector.

**Dedication:** Wakes multiple times every night to check positions. Continues trading and monitoring even on vacation. Not recommended as lifestyle, but observation of dedication required for success.

### Jaffray Woodriff — The Third Way

**Innovation:** Developed **third class of models** that are neither trend-following nor countertrend—pattern recognition predicting probable market direction over next 24 hours. One of most successful practitioners of systematic trading.

**Data Mining Done Right:** Uses "blind search through data" contrary to conventional wisdom requiring hypothesis-first approach. Avoids overfitting through rigorous protocols: training vs. validation data sets, random data baseline tests, proper cross-validation preventing cherry-picking.

**Key Discovery:** Systems working broadly across multiple markets are more robust than market-specific systems. Same models applied to ~150 markets using only daily open, high, low, close data. Over 1,000 models in system.

**Influence:** Bill James (baseball sabermetrics) inspired approach of deriving **secondary variables** from basic price data—quantifications with no direct relationship to price direction (e.g., volatility measures) that combine into predictive models.

**Performance:** Never had negative year. Averaged 20%+ returns with controlled volatility through position sizing based on exponentially weighted moving average of daily dollar range per contract.

**Volatility Management:** During 2008-2009 chaos, maintained portfolio volatility very near target of 12% annualized by dramatically reducing contract numbers as volatility increased.

## Part Two: Multistrategy Players

### Edward Thorp — The Innovator

**Track Record:** Princeton Newport Partners (1969-1988): 19.1% annualized gross return with 227 winning months out of 230 (98.7% win rate). Probability of achieving this by chance: approximately 1 in 10^76. Conclusive proof efficient market hypothesis is wrong.

**Firsts:** Co-developed first wearable computer (for roulette), first blackjack card-counting system (*Beat the Dealer*), first systematic warrant/convertible trading approach (*Beat the Market*), first option pricing model equivalent to Black-Scholes (used profitably years before their 1973 publication), founder of first market-neutral and first successful quant hedge fund.

**Philosophy:** Seeks to "remove the gambling from gambling." Casino games have negative edge; Thorp devised strategies with positive edge in games previously assumed unbeatable (roulette, blackjack, baccarat).

**Kelly Criterion:** Learned from Claude Shannon at MIT. Formula maximizes long-term compounded growth but assumes precisely known probabilities. In trading, **recommends betting half-Kelly** because: (1) overestimating correct size is severely penalized, (2) most people find full Kelly volatility too high for comfort.

**Convertible Arbitrage:** Buy convertible bond, short stock to neutralize long exposure (delta hedging). Built sophisticated model recognizing bond price floor assumption was wrong—if stock falls enough, bond grade also falls. Model analyzed entire family of related instruments to find optimal hedge combinations.

**Statistical Arbitrage:** Third iteration of strategy after continually adapting. Final version (1992-2002) achieved 21% annualized return with only 7% standard deviation by applying factor neutrality via principal components analysis, making strategy neutral to market and abstract risk factors.

**Madoff Investigation (1991):** Quickly determined manager was fraud after analyzing claimed strategy (buy stock, buy put, sell call). Returns not consistent with strategy given market conditions. Miraculous S&P futures positions appeared periodically to smooth everything out. Detailed forensic analysis years before Harry Markopolos.

### Jamie Mai — Seeking Asymmetry

**Strategy:** Selective option buyer seeking situations with greater-than-normal probability of large price move. Structures all trades to be **right-skewed**—maximum loss limited (option premium paid), upside open-ended.

**2008 Subprime Trade:** Bought credit default swap (CDS) protection on subprime mortgage bonds starting 2006. Recognized housing bubble driven by deteriorating lending standards. By 2007, paid ~$8 million in premiums; by end 2008, position valued at ~$80 million profit.

**Philosophy:** "The pursuit of asymmetry." Willing to lose small amounts repeatedly to capture occasional massive gains. Perfectly content to stay on sidelines and do absolutely nothing until trade opportunity meets guidelines.

**Best 2011 Trade:** Shorting dry bulk shippers—ironically originated with premise these represented buying opportunity. When research showed not only wrong but exactly backward, immediately reversed original trading plan. Exemplifies flexibility and lack of ego.

**Implementation:** Uses options for asymmetric payoff profiles, but also achieves asymmetry through trade structure. Example: Capital One options trade capitalized on binary outcome (acquisition or not) that options pricing models failed to reflect.

### Michael Platt — The Art and Science of Risk Control

**BlueCrest Performance:** Discretionary strategy managed over $10 billion with worst peak-to-trough drawdown under 5% over entire decade-plus track record. Achieved through rigorous exposure reduction rules.

**Risk Rules:** Discretionary traders restricted to 3% loss limit from starting allocation before exposure cut in half. Another 3% loss leads to removal of entire allocation. As traders register gains during year, can increase risk levels because original exposure reduction thresholds remain unchanged for year—upside open-ended, downside severely curtailed.

**Trading Philosophy:** Best trades stem from market's **failure to respond** to expected news. Example: Continuing stream of adverse news repeatedly made him expect losses, yet market didn't move against him. Read inability to respond as confirmation—quadrupled position into one of his biggest winners.

**Counter-to-Anticipated Response:** More meaningful than the news itself. Price action reveals what market truly thinks vs. what news suggests it should think.

**Quote:** "Markets are constantly in a state of uncertainty and flux, and money is made by discounting the obvious and betting on the unexpected."

## Part Three: Equity Traders

### Steve Clark — Do More of What Works and Less of What Doesn't

**Core Advice:** Most traders degrade performance by engaging in trades without clear edge. Figure out what you're best at and focus attention on those types of trades exclusively.

**When Losing:** Best way to handle losing streak is liquidate everything and take vacation. Physical break interrupts downward spiral and loss of confidence. When trading resumed, size should be kept small until confidence regained.

**Screen Watching:** Staring at screen all day can be expensive—leads to both overtrading and increased chance of liquidating good positions. Find more productive use of time.

**2008 Risk Management:** Cut exposure by factor of four in response to steep volatility increase. Maintained discipline to reduce positions during chaos protected capital.

---

### Martin Taylor — The Invisible Man

**Style:** Long-only UK equities focused on small/mid-cap stocks ignored by institutional investors. "Invisible stocks" too small for large funds but excellent opportunities for patient capital.

**Philosophy:** Deep fundamental research, long holding periods (years), concentrated portfolio. Willing to look foolish in short term if conviction about long-term value creation intact.

**1999 Underperformance:** Thought buying tech at inflated prices ridiculous—underperformed badly during bubble peak. Same decision key reason strongly outperformed when stocks slid in subsequent years during 2000-2002 bear market.

### Tom Claugus — A Change of Plans

**Background:** Chemical engineer on track to become Rohm & Haas CEO. Eventually earning more from stock account than executive position. Decided to abandon successful career—most difficult decision he ever made, led to clinical depression before finally committing.

**Track Record:** 17% average annual net return over 19 years. Natural contrarian: lags badly when others make easy money, soars when others panic. Achieved 18% annual compounded return through 1990s while being **net short throughout the period**.

**1987 Experience:** Started shorting early 1986, losing so much money by summer 1987 had to call mother for loan to meet margin call. Stayed short all the way through crash. First year made more money in market than job—every year after made more in market than job.

**Gates Policy:** Despite huge outflows in 2008, didn't put up gates because big difference between investors making own decision to stay vs. him telling them they can't leave. If loses their money after preventing redemption = different feeling than losing money for investors who reached own decision to stay.

### Joe Vidich — Harvesting Losses

**"Free Optionality" Strategy:** Looks for companies with future revenue generation potential not reflected in current market price. Market often fails to value revenue opportunities more than one year away.

**Examples:** Oil companies with leased acreage not yet producing, Apple priced only on existing products (not innovation potential), companies with new technology not yet commercialized. If revenue source is more than year away, market often assigns no significant value—get upside for free.

**United Airlines Trade:** One of largest long positions despite Warren Buffett's well-known airline avoidance. Contrarian reasoning: industry consolidated to three major players, cost structures finally competitive post-bankruptcy, United's superior route structure (50% US-Asia, 40% US-Europe). Trading at P/E of 3.3 with potential to double/triple.

**Sentiment-Based Exposure:** Varies net exposure between 70% net short and 110% net long based on price placement within long-term statistical band (regression line from 1932 with 95% confidence interval). Provides substantial performance improvement vs. narrow-range exposure.

**Good vs. Bad Trades:** Common error is judging decisions by outcome rather than process. Losing trade can reflect correct decision if process has positive edge; winning trade can be poor decision if process negative edge. Trading is matter of probabilities.

### Kevin Daly — Who Is Warren Buffett?

**Performance:** 20.8% annual gross return (1999-2011) with only 10.3% maximum drawdown despite two major bear markets. Cumulative 872% gross return vs. Russell 2000's 68% and S&P 500's -9%. Gain to Pain ratio: 3.2 (very high).

**Process:** Quantitative screening for cheap (low EV/EBITDA, P/FCF, P/E) + good business quality (high return on tangible capital). After screening ~10,000 stocks to ~200 candidates, applies qualitative assessment: easily understandable business, sustainable through difficult periods, potential acquisition targets.

**Timing:** Went ~90% cash at end of Q1 2000 (right at bear market start), stayed mostly cash until early 2003. During 2008, took exposure down to 60-70% cash. Superior stock selection alone isn't enough—being primarily in cash during negative environments is critical factor enabling large equity growth.

**Value Traps:** Won't invest in "melting ice cube" businesses (newspapers, yellow pages, video rentals) no matter how cheap. "Time is the enemy of the poor business and the friend of the great business." —Warren Buffett

**Horizon Lines Lesson:** Overleveraged container shipping company with regulatory moat. Became concerned about ability to generate meaningful free cash flow; feared debt load could sink company. Exited above average cost. "Learned valuable lesson about danger of investing in overleveraged companies. Stock today trades at 24 cents per share!"

**Advice:** "I'd rather miss an opportunity than lose money." Patience to stay on sidelines when environment adverse or opportunities lacking/suboptimal is essential—remained largely in cash for well over two years during 2000-2002 bear market.

### Jimmy Balodimas — The Pursuit of Catching Turning Points

**Style:** Short-biased equity trader (75% of time short) with intuitive sense for market turning points. Constantly trading in and out—makes hundreds of orders daily. "More about daily money-taking than being right on direction."

**Silver Example (May 2011):** Watched silver for six months, thought "It's time to sell this thing" after price nearly tripled. Went short the silver ETF as rally became vertical. Within three days down $4 million on trade. Market sold off to about his average price, covered entire position—broke even on balance. "My way of looking at the world is right"—silver fell from $49 to $32 in biggest selloff in over 30 years.

**Evolution:** Learned to give up vendettas after losing $7 million in one month on homebuilders. "Losing homebuilders money was biggest blessing—led to looking at life differently." Much more sensitive about feelings now; if position doesn't feel right, will make the shift immediately.

**Schwager's Challenge:** Balodimas skilled at trading around position, often making money even when longer-term trend against him. Schwager points out could do even better by combining position trading skill with original directional calls *after* market confirms the turn. "It seems like you are currently trading with two hands tied behind your back."

**Response:** Comfortable being uncomfortable when everyone else panicking, but trading with trend would require being uncomfortable differently. "Maybe just comfortable being comfortable." Very comfortable when panic has set in because "I got them. They're trapped."

### Joel Greenblatt — The Magic Formula

**Magic Formula:** Combined ranking of (1) **earnings yield** (EBIT/enterprise value) and (2) **return on tangible capital** (EBIT / [net working capital + net fixed assets]). Not looking for cheapest companies or best companies, but **best combination of cheap and good**.

**Backtest Results:** Top 30 names from 1,000 largest cap stocks approximately doubled S&P 500 return over 23 years: 19.7% vs. 9.5% annually. Perfect decile ordering from 1 to 10. During lost decade (2000-2009), averaged 13.5% annual return while S&P 500 down nearly 1% annually.

**Three Critical Lessons:**
1. Value investing works
2. Value investing doesn't work all the time
3. Item 2 is one of the reasons why Item 1 is true

**Time Arbitrage:** Value edge doesn't disappear because underperformance periods (few years) are long and severe enough to discourage investors. Shortening of investor time horizons creates opportunity for those with commitment to long-term holding periods—**time arbitrage** requires patience institutional investors no longer possess.

**Manager Selection Mistake:** Using past performance as guide for selecting managers. Empirical evidence shows no meaningful correlation between past and future manager performance rankings. Should select based on investment **process** rather than returns.

**Marriott Spinoff Trade:** Spun off hotels and debt into Host Marriott (toxic waste), kept good business as Marriott International. Recognized institutions would dump shares due to spinoff being 10-15% of original (too small), different business, and heavy leverage. Large insider ownership suggested deal wouldn't be structured to fail. Stock nearly tripled in four months.

**Wells Fargo LEAPS Trade:** Binary outcome (bank survives or fails). Bought long-term options with 2+ years to expiration instead of stock. Transformed 1:1 risk/reward into 1:5 risk/reward. If bank survived, stock should double (5x on options); if failed, only lose cost of options.

**Education Reform:** Started Harlem Success charter schools with Eva Moskowitz. All four tested schools beat Scarsdale, Great Neck, and all top districts in state—all located in Harlem with same kids, same parents, less money per student than public schools, but dramatically better results through lottery-based admission.

## Conclusion: 40 Market Wizard Lessons

*Selected highlights from Schwager's comprehensive conclusion:*

**1. There Is No Holy Grail** — Multitude of successful approaches, some polar opposites, proves no single solution.

**2. Find Methodology That Fits Your Personality** — Sound methodology for one trader can be poor fit and losing strategy for another.

**3. Trade Within Your Comfort Zone** — If position too large, fear dominates decision-making. Smaller net exposure may yield better returns even if market moves favorably.

**4. Flexibility Is Essential** — Best traders not only liquidate losing positions but will reverse them. O'Shea's April 2009 reversal turned potential disaster into winning year.

**10. Wait for High-Conviction Trades** — Patience greatly enhances return/risk. Mai perfectly content to do absolutely nothing until trade meets guidelines.

**12. The Importance of Doing Nothing** — Daly achieved 800%+ cumulative gross returns by having discipline to remain largely in cash during negative environments.

**13. Implementation Can Be More Important Than the Trade** — O'Shea's Nasdaq bubble trade: implemented via long bonds instead of short stocks, avoiding treacherous bear market rallies.

**14. Trading Around a Position** — Dynamic rather than static process. Reduce exposure on favorable moves, reinstate on corrections. Generates extra profits and increases chances of staying with good trades.

**18. Seek Asymmetric Return/Risk Profile** — Mai and O'Shea structure trades with maximum loss limited but upside open-ended through selective option buying and credit default swaps.

**22. Risk Control Is Critical** — Ramsey risks only 0.1% per trade; Benedict clears out at 2% monthly loss; Platt cuts traders in half at 3% loss, removes at 6%.

**24. Protective Stops Must Be Consistent with Trade Analysis** — O'Shea: traders set stops based on pain threshold rather than points disproving premise, leading to multiple losses cumulatively larger than single wider stop.

**27. Correlation Can Be Misleading** — Measures past relationships; only relevant if past correlation reasonable proxy for future. Dalio focuses on underlying **drivers**, not backward-looking correlation.

**30. Pay Attention to How Market Responds to News** — Counter-to-anticipated response more meaningful than news itself. Platt quadrupled position when market failed to respond to adverse news stream.

**33. Stock Can Be Well-Priced After Large Upmove** — What matters is price relative to *future* prospects, not past movement. Taylor's largest holding (Apple) had already seen large advance—prior gain irrelevant if still cheap on earnings projections.

**36. Value Investing Works** — But has periods of poor performance, which is actually *why* it maintains edge. Long-term efficacy requires similar long-term perspective.

**37. EMH Provides Inaccurate Model** — Prices sometimes much too high or too low. Greenblatt quotes Graham: market like highly erratic business partner sometimes willing to sell absurdly low, sometimes buy ridiculously high.

**40. Mistake to Select Managers Based on Past Performance** — Greenblatt cites studies showing past performance has no predictive value. Choose based on **process**, not returns.

## Epilogue Reflection

Schwager notes the interviews solidified principles important to trading success. Personal example: positioned short stock index futures expecting rally to fail. After extremely bearish employment report, market initially sold off sharply but recovered entire loss by end of week. Prepared to cover most position when market opened Sunday night. Market opened lower, and Schwager **immediately thought of Marty Schwartz's advice** from first *Market Wizards*: "If you're very nervous about a position overnight and you're able to get out at much better price than you thought when market trades, you're usually better off staying with position." Stayed with position—saved considerable money as market moved sharply lower in ensuing weeks.

Son **Zachary Schwager** reflects on how father's trading expo talk changed his life. Three insights resonated: (1) Trading not reserved for elite—traced to hard work and desire to avoid psychological impediments; (2) Trading is art and science—success built on ability to discover what others overlook; (3) No single right way—trying to replicate someone else's method almost always fails. Dropped out of film school, got finance degree, now works as junior trader at First New York Securities. Final tribute to father: "It would be far easier to be as successful as my dad in trading than to be as successful as he is on a human level."
