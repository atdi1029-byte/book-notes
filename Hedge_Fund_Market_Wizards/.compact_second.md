## Michael Platt (BlueCrest) - continued

### Correlation Between Forecasts and Positions

- If asking a hedge fund manager about 30-year bond direction yields interest rate increase forecasts, correlation with current position approaches 100% -- they're short bonds.
- "It's amazing how much information you can get about people's positions by simply asking them about their opinions."

### Crisis Management: ECB Rate Hike While on Flight to South Africa

- Massive long European interest rate futures while traveling for **ARK charity** (feeding program reaching 60,000 children).
- **European Central Bank unexpectedly hiked rates** mid-flight -- "probably the only time I got an ECB call wrong."
- Upon landing: position down $70-80 million.
- Immediate aggressive exit: "If they started hiking, they won't stop at 25 basis points. I can see this trade turning into a $250 million loss by week's end. Dump everything aggressively."

> "When I am wrong, the only instinct I have is to get out. If I was thinking one way, and now I can see that it was a real mistake, then I am probably not the only person in shock, so I better be the first one to sell. I don't care what the price is."

- Trading psychology principle: "In this game, you have an option to keep 20 percent of your P&L this year, but you also want to own the serial option of being able to do that every year. You can't be blowing up."

### Common Trader Mistakes

Two biggest mistakes:
1. Not doing enough homework
2. Being too casual about risk

### Risk Control Philosophy

- Risk control is "the most important thing."
- "I have lost money more than anything" -- loss aversion drives trading.
- Rarely uses outright directional positions -- prefers **long options or complex spread structures** with constrained theoretical risk.
- Strict maximum loss limits per position.
- If feels uncomfortable after entering a trade, gets right out.
- Most stopped trades never reach stop-loss points -- exits when trade doesn't work within reasonable time.
- Daily reevaluation: whether he would still place same trade today. If not, liquidates.

### BlueCrest Discretionary Strategy Risk Controls

Extremely tight capital preservation rules:
- **3% loss triggers 50% allocation reduction**
- **Two successive 3% losses (6% total) removes entire trader allocation**
- **Maximum 5% loss** on initial stake -- combination of two 3% losses equals less than 5% because second loss on reduced base
- Platt subject to same rules but has **never approached the 3% loss point**

### Asymmetric Risk Management Strategy

- 3%/3% rule applies to starting stake -- encourages caution at onset.
- As traders build profits, cushion widens -- 3% loss allowance grows with gains.
- Creates potential for large returns despite restrictive initial loss limitation.
- BlueCrest discretionary strategy: nearly **14% annual return net** with highly restrictive rules.
- Strategy assures capital preservation while keeping upside potential open-ended.

### Why Risk Control Matters Beyond the Obvious

Standard reasons: avoiding account-incapacitating losses, minimizing emotional pain, constraining compounding impact (large percentage losses require increasingly greater gains to recover).

Less obvious reason: losing trades mentally impede the trader and result in missed winning opportunities.

> "You feel like an idiot, and you're not in the mood to put anything else on. Then the elephant walks past you while your gun's not loaded."

- 80/20 rule: 80% of trader's profits come from 20% of trades.
- If psychological fallout from loss causes trader to miss a trade in the 20%, "it can be a big deal."

### Market Response to News as Trade Signal

- Platt pays attention to how markets respond to news.
- Example: held trade with continuing stream of adverse news items, yet market didn't move against him.
- Read market's inability to respond as confirmation of trade idea.
- **Quadrupled position**, turning it into one of his biggest winners ever.

### BlueCrest Systematic Trend-Following Performance

- Not in same return/risk league as discretionary strategy.
- Best-performing trend-following strategy: average annual compounded net return of **16%** with maximum drawdown under **13%**.

Two key factors for superior performance:
1. System liquidates positions when trends get overextended without waiting for trend reversal signals.
2. Continuous ongoing research and implementation of improvements.

> "Systems that don't change will eventually degrade."

- "System trading is a research war."

## Chapter 9: Steve Clark - Do More of What Works and Less of What Doesn't

### Background and Early Career

**Working-class origins:**
- Third generation in same council house (government housing), outskirts of London.
- Father absent, half-raised by grandparents.
- Left school at **age 17** after doing A levels a year early.

**Entry into finance:**
- Friend Grant called: "You should come and work in the city. There are 25-year-olds earning 50,000 pounds."
- Wrote many places -- always rejected: "no degree, no interview" or "no experience, no interview."
- Got temporary filing clerk job in 1986 (shortage of backoffice people), embroidered CV at each temporary position.
- Strategy: say he could do job, figure out how once hired.

**Two permanent job offers:**
- Merrill Lynch: run backoffice, 30,000 pounds
- Warburg Securities: blue button (trainee on trading floor), 7,000 pounds
- Chose 7,000-pound job because it got him on the trading floor.

**Interview moment:** Asked if he gambled. Clark: "I would never bet on anything where the odds were against me. Why would you do that?" -- instinctive answer that got him the job.

**Initial experience:**
- Started on South African gold shares -- "really had a miserable time. Couldn't understand what was going on, no one willing to teach."
- Transferred to European desk -- "completely different attitude. Very happy to talk to me and teach me."

### October 19, 1987 -- First Day Trading

First week running Scandinavian market-making book (trader on holiday). Literally October 19, 1987 was first day running book on his own -- the crash.

**Making prices during crash (Electrolux example):**
- Gets call asking for price. Quotes 43 bid/44 offered.
- Caller offers 10,000 shares at 43 -- buys 2,500 shares.
- Changes price to 42.5 bid/43.5 offered. Phone rings again -- buys 2,500 at 42.5.
- Sets price at 42/43, gets hit again, buys 2,500 at 42.
- Reasoning: "If I drop my price by a full $1, and I get hit on the offer, I'd be selling my shares at a loss."

**German trader lesson:**
- Shows trades to experienced German trader who looks at him "like I am a complete imbecile."
- German makes price 36/37 and still gets hit with offer at 36.
- Clark had been making totally wrong prices -- couldn't comprehend changing price by more than $1, let alone $6 (nearly 15%).
- **Key lesson**: "The price is where anyone is prepared to deal, and it can be anything."

**German trader's advice:** "Your position is growing. You cannot afford to let your next trade be a purchase. Whatever you have to do, you need to make sure your next trade is a sell."

**Outcome:** Clark lost several million pounds. German trader was short everything -- made several million pounds.

### Rise to Most Profitable Trader

- Eventually became most profitable trader in the group.
- "Very proactive in orientating the book. If I was in something that was wrong, I would cut it. I wouldn't defend it. I wouldn't average down. I would just cut it, cut it, cut it."
- Lack of fear from inexperience: "I didn't have the fear -- the fear that cripples people who have been in the business too long."
- Most traders can't maintain ability to take risk throughout career -- "too many bad things happen to them, too many fat tails."

### Developing an Edge

- Must be on right side of market to make money. Went back to facts.
- If you know a buyer for XYZ stock at some price below market, "you know one fact."
- Check what happened last time market got to this level -- use charts.
- Volume as screen for what stocks to look at: "There is lots of volume. Something is going on. Can I develop a view from that?"

**Chart philosophy:**
- "Charts are simply a record of how things have traded in the past. That's it."
- "You can never start with the chart. To say that you can predict the future from past data is patently untrue."
- "You can talk about percentage probabilities of what might happen next, but you can't go any further than that."
- Combined charts with other facts: where orders were, market volume.

### Lehman Brothers Transition

- Left Warburg after 2.5 years -- only paid 13,000/year despite being best trader.
- Lehman Brothers offered 50,000/year. Returned to Warburg with better offer.
- Warburg: "We can put you at 28,000... We have all these tiers, and you are a trainee."
- At Lehman, couldn't make a penny -- "terrible shock to my ego."
- "I had no idea how to make money outside of the Warburg environment, which was so rich in order flow information."

**Novo (Danish company) incident:**
- Playing basketball, got call: Novo up $2 from European close, 100,000 shares bid.
- Gut reaction: went short entire amount -- biggest position ever by a mile.
- Came in next morning, bought stock back for $100,000 profit.
- "From that moment, I couldn't stop making money."

**New edge without order flow:**
- Developed strong relationships with other brokers -- used their order flow information.
- Started screening for volume or story: "my trading was good enough so that if I could find volume or a story, I could trade it."

### Reading Market Wizards

- Seminal moment: "For the first time in my life I realized that I had a method."
- Before: "I thought I was playing a videogame, and I couldn't believe I was getting paid to do it."
- "The one thing I really took away was that once you understand you have a method, you can tweak that method."

> "Do more of what works and less of what doesn't." -- Clark's core advice

- "Dissect your P&L and see what works for you and what doesn't. It is a very interesting process to analyze where your profits come from, and traders often don't know."

### 1912DS Disaster

- Danish shipping company trading at **140,000 Danish kroner** per share. Traded in lots of one or five shares.
- Clark became bullish, bought a lot. Article in paper: company was a fraud.
- Bought at 140,000, opened next day at 100,000 offered, nothing bid.
- Wrote down price a little bit every day for next three months while trading hard to make money.
- No settlement price anywhere -- "Wild West. Traders marked their own book."

**Key lessons:**
- "Price is irrelevant; it is size that kills you."
- "If you are too big in an illiquid stock, there is no way out. I wanted to cut the position, but I couldn't."
- After this trade, became much more focused on arbitrage trades -- where he was making most of his money.

## Colm O'Shea - continued

### Transition to First New York Securities

- O'Shea was doing directional risk arbitrage at First New York Securities.
- Started own asset management business in UK in 2001 with event-driven fund.
- When fund grew to nearly **$1 billion**, told First New York he was too busy.

### O'Shea's Existential Struggles

- Still waiting to figure out what he wants to do when he grows up -- "quite depressing."
- "But what have I achieved? I haven't built anything."
- "You are only as good as your last trade, and that is it. You build nothing; you just trade... there is nothing left to show for it, except for money."
- Trading was fun when he could walk away. When you have a business, you can't -- becomes a prison.

### Position Sizing Psychology

> "It is the size of the position you put on rather than the price at which you put it on that determines your ability to keep the position."

- Traders often plan to scale down as losers (buy at 8, add at 7, 6, 5) -- but if you'll be "puking your guts out" at lower levels, you shouldn't be buying at all at 8.
- First lesson from first boss: price is irrelevant; it's all about controlling position size.
- Liquidity is critical -- when merger deal breaks, cut position immediately to get a pocket of liquidity, even at lower level. If you wait, liquidity dries up.

### Common Trader Mistakes

- Traders from information-rich environments (big investment banks) struggle in trading houses with no information flow.
- Trading in a vacuum -- incredibly strong traders in big size suddenly can't make money.
- Becomes downward spiral: trade smaller and smaller, every dollar lost becomes emotionally more important, lose all perspective.
- Advice: replace the information flow -- draw up list of 20 contacts and phone them every day. "They don't take the advice."
- Coming back to investment bank world: meetings suck up time but actually limit time staring at positions -- don't want 12 hours/day in front of screen.

### Patience as Common Denominator

- Successful traders exhibit patience, particularly regarding staying with good trades long enough.
- True regardless of time frame. The more time staring at screen, the less patience you'll have.

### When Trading Edge Disappears

> "I am never going to make a penny again. I am crap. I have always been crap. I have just been found out."

> "You had a knack for trading, but now it is gone. You lost it."

> "I lost it. I was never that good. It was just the market. Now the market is changed, and I don't know what to do. It is a horrible, horrible feeling."

- Even after making money for 10 years, can still get into that mind-set.
- Solution: don't do anything. Discipline yourself not to trade for two weeks.
- Take a holiday -- must get out of everything first, then go away. First few days will torture yourself. After a few days, can let it go.
- When ready to restart: trade a fraction of the size you'd normally trade.

### High Volume as Trading Signal

- At Lehman when short on information, would filter for European stocks with unusually large volume.
- Then go through charts to see if any making interesting bottoms.
- Call contacts and ask what they knew about those specific stocks.
- Volume just a way of identifying potentially interesting situations -- not a buy signal by itself.

### Example: FCC Approval Trade

- Merger deal had to be approved by FCC, nothing happening, spread widening.
- Kept calling FCC until got secretary of one of commissioners.
- Secretary: "Oh, that; it's on his desk ready to be signed, but he is away fishing this week."

### Example: Banker Funding Question

- On phone with banker regarding funding for deal. Kept asking question he couldn't answer in 10 different ways.
- After call, said "Sell it now" -- many things would have been reasonable for banker to say, but he didn't.
- All he said: "I can't answer that question." If you don't make the phone call, you will never know.

### Fiat 2003 Trade

- Made **20% in a month** on single transaction.
- Broad market rallying sharply; Fiat had big rights issue. Deutsche Bank disposing of industrial holdings, decided to sell their rights rather than exercise.
- Market going up, Fiat going down because of big seller. Seller not driven by fundamentals.
- Rights especially cheap because of Deutsche Bank selling.

Everything lined up:
1. Market very strong
2. Stock substantially underperforming for two technical reasons (rights issue + large seller) unrelated to fundamentals
3. Rights provided large position for small capital outlay
4. Rights trading below intrinsic value because Deutsche Bank was large seller and stock difficult to borrow

### Characteristics of Successful Traders

- All work hard. Nearly all are "one-trick ponies" -- do one thing very well.
- When they stray from single focus, often ends in disaster.
- Managers diversify away from expertise because they've made a lot of money and think they can do anything.
- Deep down they know one trick could end, but successful one-trick ponies learn another trick when needed.
- Capable of changing their mind in an instant: "This market is going higher. It's absolutely going up. No, it's definitely going down."

### Euro Disney Mind Change Example

- Already very long Euro Disney for US listing, put in buy orders to get more long on first day available.
- Market gapped higher, racing away. Weren't getting filled because so little for sale.
- **In an instant, completely switched orders and started selling whatever he could.**
- Recognized: if he had reached fever pitch trying to buy at any price, the moment market turned, it would just head straight down.
- Fell about 10% that day, kept going down in following days.

### Managing Through 2007-2008

- 2007: got rid of anything directional or long dated.
- Put almost all money in short duration risk arbitrage -- deals felt almost certain to close with wide spreads.
- To hedge systemic risk: bought OTM puts on S&P, sold OTM calls to pay for puts.
- 2008: volatility quadrupled. Decided should trade fraction of what used to trade -- most people didn't do that and got blown up.
- Had good month, then very good month, started next strong. Two days later down for month -- nothing had happened.
- **Took off 75% of exposure.** Not gradually -- "I don't believe in this idea, 'We will sell 25 percent and then think about it.'"

### O'Shea's Trading Rules

1. If you wake up thinking about a position, it's too big.
2. Never stop asking questions -- speak to as many people as you can.
3. When everything lines up, swing for it. But if position behaves unexpectedly, cut it -- market is telling you that you don't know.

> "Your job as a trader is to make the line go from bottom left to top right. That's it."

- Had managers in 2008 who lost 50%, some 80%. Why? Couldn't accept they were wrong. Kept doing the math, saying they were right.
- "Their job isn't to create billions of dollars of embedded value; their job is to make the line go from bottom left to top right."

## Chapter 10: Martin Taylor - The Tsar Has No Clothes

### Background and Performance

- Most hedge fund managers seek to grow assets -- Taylor chose the reverse course.
- Ten years after launching with **$20 million seed capital**, assets grew to over **$7 billion**.
- Notified investors he was closing fund in 12 months. Coordinated closing with opening of new fund that capped assets at less than one-quarter of previous fund's size.
- Named fund after **Alexander Nevsky** -- thirteenth-century Russian hero.
- 1995-2011: average annual compounded net return **over 27%**, more than doubling 12% EM index.
- 2000-2011 hedge fund: **over 22%** net annually, more than doubling HFRI EM index at 10%.
- Only long-only manager to avoid a loss in 1998.
- Only losing year was 2008: loss less than 1/3 the long-only index decline and 40% of hedge fund index loss.

### Russia 1998: Discovery of Balance of Payments Fraud

- Taylor became bearish ~1 year before August 1998 collapse. Took fund to **40% cash** -- extraordinary for long-only.
- No one else was discussing the balance of payments discrepancy.

**The fraud mechanism:**
- Russia showed current account surplus on paper, but reserves were declining (should have been increasing 10-11% of GDP yearly).
- Russian exports massively overstated in cash terms.
- Example: mining company exports $100M raw materials, reports sale as $50M. Missing $50M goes to private Swiss account. $40M of reported $50M also goes to Switzerland.
- Capital flight created massive black hole in capital account.
- Workers paid in company store tokens. Company owned housing, stores, everything. Workers had no choice, no money spent on infrastructure.

### October 1997: The Pivot

- Yeltsin health rumor crashed market **23% in one session**.
- Taylor started selling aggressively despite 30% bounce next day.

> "When markets are trending up strongly, and there is bad news, the bad news counts for nothing. But if there is a break that reminds people what it is like to lose money in equities, then suddenly the buying is not mindless anymore."

- Market down 50% by February 1998. Taylor resisted pressure to reinstate long positions.
- Wrote internal paper: **"The Tsar Has No Clothes"** -- "Russia's problems are unlikely to be tackled until a serious ruble crisis jolts them out of their complacency."

**Goldman Sachs Eurobond deal (early summer 1998):**
- Managed sale of $25B low-yielding, dollar-denominated Russian sovereign 10-year Eurobonds.
- Bonds immediately fell from day one.
- Foreign reserves should have doubled ($20B to $25B), but only increased $3B.
- "The emperor has no clothes" moment -- forced recognition of capital accounts data fraud.

### August 1998: Why Taylor Still Lost Despite Being Out of Russia

- 40% cash position, but invested portion held high-beta stocks (1.5+ beta).
- Good quality companies still declined 70-80% because widely owned.
- These companies continued 30% earnings growth -- prices shot back when market stabilized.
- Being 40% cash allowed buying in September after crash.
- Portfolio rebounded with "insane beta" -- finished 1998 slightly up despite index down 30%.

### Portfolio Philosophy

> "Buying low-beta stocks is a common mistake investors make. Why would you ever want to own boring stocks? If the market goes down 40 percent for macro reasons, they'll go down 20 percent. Wouldn't you just rather own cash?"

- Low-beta = "pigeon-and-elephant trade" -- eat like pigeon, shit like elephant.
- To get equity-like returns from boring stocks, must leverage up -- losses become massively asymmetric.
- **Always high-beta stocks balanced by cash or shorts.** "Boring companies never have the opportunity for earnings growth."

### Three Elements for Stock Purchase

1. **Favorable macro situation**
2. **Secular trend**
3. **Good company management**

Example: Russian mobile companies 1999-2005 (largest trade ever in Russia):
- Macro: 80% ruble devaluation (1998) + oil rebound from $12 = favorable balance of payments.
- Secular trend: mobile penetration increasing 25% annually.
- Company quality: good management, transparent accounting.

### Net Exposure Range and Contrarian Timing

**20% net long to 110% net long** -- never completely out.
- If completely out when market reverses, never get back in.
- Q1 2009: very bearish but 20% net long. When market reversed in March, mentally easy to double to 40%.

> "When the market is so bad that you think it is obvious that you should be net short, that's typically the time when it is all in the price and you should be buying."

- "Managing money is real life, not some bullshit strategist fantasy world."
- When market up: try to capture 70-80% of move. When market down: try to lose only 30-40%.
- Many hedge funds brilliant in 2008 (short all year) then blew up in 2009 by staying short.

### 2000-2002 and 2008

**Early success (2000-2002):** 27% annualized return vs. <3% for HFRI EM index.
- Underweighted tech stocks and high-beta bubbles (Turkey).
- Heavily invested in Russia -- cheap ruble + firm oil prices.

**2008:** EM down 54%, Taylor down 17% -- exactly in line with investor expectations.
- Entered 2008 at 85% net long high-beta stocks, 200% gross exposure.
- Troubled by widening credit spreads -- cut to 40% net long first week of January.
- Aggressively selling first week of January was critical -- up 4% at midyear.
- If hadn't sold early January, would have sold at "bloody lows" -- down 15% instead of 5%.
- Drastically cutting exposure early positioned fund to buy on SocGen collapse.
- After Lehman, reduced to 20% net long.
- Three 15-20% rallies in Q4 all followed by new lows. Might have lost more going flat due to whipsaws.

**Investor response:** No redemptions until October 2008. Nearly half of assets redeemed Oct 2008-March 2009, but all came back within 3 months.

### 2010 Fund Restructuring

- $20M grew to $7.5B ($3.5B hedge fund, $4B long-only) by February 2010.
- Shrunk to 20% of former client base. Gave clients one-year notice.
- New fund freedom: can do anything, no Asia if doesn't want it.
- "I'm also trying to get away from that tyranny in hedge funds: monthly performance."
- Trying to stop caring about what clients think -- freedom to maintain long-term focus.

### Apple Position (April 2011)

- **20% of portfolio** in Apple.
- U.S. analysts think limited growth -- fail to appreciate: 300M in U.S., 6.7B in rest of world.
- China (900M mobile subscribers): currently sold only 3M iPhones.
- Trading 16x forward (2011). Based on 2014 forecasted earnings: only **4.8x earnings**. $150B net cash by 2014 (nearly half market cap).
- RIMM PlayBook: "One of the most suicidal pieces of rubbish ever put out." Producing corporate tablet you can't use for email.
- April 30, 2011: Apple $350, RIMM $49. March 19, 2012: Apple $601, RIMM <$15.

### Short Selling Strategy

- Longs: cheap relative to sector, positive earnings surprises expected.
- Shorts: expensive relative to sector, negative surprises expected.
- Problem: bad companies = greatest takeover risk in EM. Only companies multinationals can take over are bad ones -- government/regulators more likely to approve.
- Solution: **half to three-quarters of shorts = stock index futures.** Only short individual companies that can't be taken over (government-owned, pension fund-owned).

### Risk Management

- Never uses quantitative risk control models.

> "All quantitative risk control models use historical volatility. It is like driving by looking in the rearview mirror."

- Never uses stops. Uses common sense risk management instead.
- Very risky companies: 1-5% of portfolio. Very high confidence: up to 20% (e.g., Apple).
- If 20% in Apple, can't have another significant position in same sector.

### Technical Analysis Usage

- Charts very important as supplemental tool.
- If stock very overbought, won't stop from buying but will start with small position (e.g., 1% instead of 10%).
- If stock massively underperformed, check who has been selling -- weakness might indicate management has lied.
- Charts as confirmation: stock breaks out on chart, go to full position. Breakout confirms market seeing same thing.
- RSI only useful in oversold direction. Extremely oversold usually acute phenomenon lasting only few weeks.

### Gut Feel

> "If something isn't acting right, it is an indication that you need to go back and recheck the fundamentals."

- Example: IBEX (Spanish index) -- bad statistics released, market went up. "So I scurried away." Didn't cover, but didn't add.

### Advice

> "You have to be an expert in what you invest in. You need to understand why you are invested. If you don't understand why you are in a trade, you won't understand when it is the right time to sell, which means you will only sell when the price action scares you."

### Taylor's 2009 Trading Mistake

- Up 32% for year, should have been 45-50%.
- Post-traumatic effects from Sep 2008-Feb 2009 altered behavior -- developed habit of taking quick 10-15% profits instead of letting winners run.
- Positions then continued up another 35-40%.
- Only stopped after giving 12-month notice (March 2010) that fund was closing. Relaxed and returned to former investment style.

### Stock Selection Insights

- Don't equate past price gains with overvaluation. Apple: despite large prior advance, very cheap based on earnings projections.
- Net exposure must match personal comfort level. For Taylor, comfort zone low end = 20% net long.
- Monthly returns obsession detrimental for long-term investors.
- Best opportunities: where you can identify potential trend market doesn't appreciate because it extrapolates history instead of looking forward.
- Don't equate manager performance in given year with skill. More skilled managers underperform during bubbles by refusing to participate.

## Chapter 11: Tom Claugus - A Change of Plans

### Background

- Chemical engineering degree + MBA from Harvard Business School.
- Worked at **Rohm & Haas**: within 15 years, manager of European operations, on track for CEO.
- Avid stock investor from childhood. Father invested in stocks; Claugus watched as child.
- Discipline: lived on 1/3 of income, invested remainder.
- Father: Depression product, scared of poverty. Somehow transferred insecurity to son.
- Freshman in college: created 30-year financial plan. Goal: saving 2/3 of income + earning 10% yearly = millionaire by age 53.

### Track Record

- **17% average annual compounded net return** over 19-year period.
- Personal account: 33% gross compounding for 7 years prior to fund launch.
- Combined 26-year track record: five losing years (two at 3% or less, 1991: -12%, 2008: -25%, 2011: -9%).
- After 2008, up **56% in 2009** (best year ever).
- Natural contrarian: lags when others make easy money, soars when others panic.
- **Net short throughout the 1990s** yet achieved 18% annual compounded return.

### First Stocks and Early Investing

- First interest age 9. First stocks bought with paper route money: Wheeling Pittsburgh Steel and McCrory (five and dime chain store). Both eventually bankrupt.
- Father's advice: "Tom, you are going to feel like you're going to jail. You can go out Friday night or Saturday night, but you can't go out both, or else you won't get ahead."

### Shorting and 1987 Crash

- Started shorting early 1986 -- almost 1.5 years before August 1987 peak.
- Before market cracks, lower-quality stocks often zoom. Summer 1987: losing so much, had to call mother for margin loan.
- Stayed short all the way through crash.
- October 19, 1987: as market crashing, started buying longs to reduce net short. Most memorable: buying Mead Paper at 32, filled at 27 -- market collapsing that quickly.
- Didn't cover many shorts during freefall -- most were low quality, didn't need to cover quickly. Started buying stocks he really liked instead.
- 1987: first year made more money in market than job. Every year after, same.

### Decision Crisis

- Net worth reached $1.6 million. Realized: "3 percent of $1.6 million is $48,000; I could live on that."
- Economic necessity to keep working went away. "Until that point, constantly asked: When are you going to do what you want to do? The answer would always be, Later."
- Started first fund 1990 with $3M (part his, rest friends/family).

**First time managing fund:**
- Went from regional director with everyone saying how smart he was to managing portfolio alone from home.
- After 9 months: "Claugus, you are a people person; you made a big mistake."
- Performance: up first 6 months, then started giving back. Still up a little when started questioning.
- Closed fund at end of first year.

**Rohm & Haas return offer:**
- Regional director of Asia based in Hong Kong.
- Couldn't bring himself to go. Walked out to put "for sale" sign in front of house, couldn't do it.
- At airport: got out of line, then back in, then back out. Finally got on plane.
- Impression of Hong Kong: very crowded. School said no room for trampoline.
- **Spent 5 months trying to get himself to go. Became depressed, lost 45 pounds.**
- Coach diagnosed clinical depression. Antidepressants helped within 2 weeks.
- Came out of depression very quickly after turning down Hong Kong job.

**Reason for depression:** giving up on dream. In heart, really wanted to be on own, hadn't taken it to completion.

**Lesson:** importance of having emotional side lined up with mental side for life-changing decisions.

### Second Fund Launch (January 1993)

- Waited 1.5 years after uncertainty first time.
- "If 100% dedicated and lose money, can look investor in eye and say 'I did my best.' If not 100% engaged, managing other people's money is very hard."
- Why no gates: had huge outflows in 2008, didn't put up gates. "Big difference between their making own decision vs. him telling them what's in their best interest."

### Warren Buffett's Airline Avoidance

- Buffett has full-time employee whose sole job is preventing him from buying airlines.
- Son's question: "How do you become a millionaire?" Answer: "Start with a billion and buy an airline."

### United Airlines - Contrarian Call

- Despite bearish airline thesis, United became one of largest long positions.
- Lack of profitability originated from regulation era -- deregulation forced legacy carriers (high costs) to compete with low-cost newcomers.
- Two transformative changes: (1) major carriers went through bankruptcy, restructuring costs; (2) industry consolidated to three major players.
- United's competitive advantage: controls ~50% of U.S.-to-Asia, ~40% of U.S.-to-Europe flights.
- At $20/share: 10% operating margin = $6 earnings = P/E of only 3.3. Even with 8-10x multiple, potential to double or triple.

### "Free Optionality" Stock Selection

- Core principle: look for companies with future revenue not reflected in current price.
- Market often fails to value revenue opportunities more than one year away.

**Oil company examples:**
- Petrominerales (Colombian oil/gas): produces 40K barrels/day, has 600K leased acres with active drilling. Market not pricing additional production.
- Canadian Natural Resources: new oil sands project with $1B/year cash flow potential. Market assigned zero value.
- Paramount Resources: acquires land with exploration potential, leases it. True value never appears in cash flow metrics.

> "The general principle is that we look for future revenue generation that can be reasonably anticipated but that is not reflected by the current market price. This idea is probably the single most important concept in our stock selection process."

### Good Trades vs. Bad Trades

- Common error: judging decisions by outcome rather than process.
- 2:1 odds fair coin toss: taking the bet and losing = still correct decision.
- Good trade: process profitable if repeated multiple times (can lose any individual trade).
- Bad trade: process that will lose money if repeated (may win any individual trade).
- Winning slot machine wager is still a bad bet.

### Investment Process

**Screening (~10,000 stocks):**
- For nonfinancials: EV/EBITDA, price/FCF, P/E, EV/EBIT (cap rate).

**Qualitative assessment (~200 candidates):**
- Easily understandable business (Buffett's "circle of competence").
- Avoids biotech, high technology.
- Good businesses: necessary product/service, sustainable balance sheet through difficult periods.
- Potential acquisition targets due to market share, IP, distribution, or real estate value.

- Stock eventually topped at **$570/share**.

> "I failed to appreciate that in a market where Internet stocks with little or even no earnings could magically levitate based on silly metrics like sales per engineer, there was no telling how high a 'real company' like Newport could go."

- Margins much lower than identical competitor Copart (CPRT). Couldn't determine why margins shouldn't increase.

- Stayed mostly cash probably till early 2003.

**2008:** Took exposure to 60-70% cash. Focus on valuation and good balance sheets.

**2010:** Economic indicators (including weekly rail car loadings) pointed to slowdown. Sold stocks. Then QE2 initiated. Still up 13.3% net.

> "I have no regrets, though, because I'd rather miss an opportunity than lose money."

- "Melting ice cube" businesses: newspapers, yellow pages, video rentals.

> "Time is the enemy of the poor business and the friend of the great business." -- Buffett

- Manages only $50M. Allows broad range across capitalization spectrum.

- Made virtually no effort to raise additional assets.

### Key Lessons

1. Superior stock selection alone isn't enough -- being in cash during negative environments equally critical.
2. Not being invested during two major bear markets enabled large equity growth.

## Conclusion: 40 Market Wizard Lessons

### 1. No Holy Grail
No single solution. Range of methods (some polar opposites) testament to diversity of possible approaches.

### 2. Find a Method That Fits Your Personality
> "If I try to teach you what I do, you will fail because you are not me. He became something else. He became him." -- O'Shea

### 3. Trade Within Your Comfort Zone
If position too large, fear dominates. Clark: "trade within your emotional capacity." Vidich: "Limit your size so fear does not become the prevailing instinct." Smaller net exposure may yield better returns even if market moves favorably. Taylor example: reducing early January 2008 positioned him to buy during plunge.

### 4. Flexibility Is Essential
O'Shea (April 2008): reversed pessimistic view based on market behavior -- turned potentially disastrous year into winning one. Mai's best 2011 trade: started as buying opportunity, reversed to short when research showed exactly backward. Clark: good traders can change minds in an instant.

### 5. The Need to Adapt
Markets change, strategies deteriorate. Thorp: maintained returns by continually adapting (third iteration, original system degraded). Platt: "research war" -- systematic approaches must be revised. Balodimas: adapted less aggressive posture as hedge fund participation created smoother trends.

### 6. Don't Confuse Winning/Losing Trades with Good/Bad Trades
Good trade can lose money; bad trade can make money. Even best processes lose certain percentage. Trade adhering to positive-edge process is good regardless of outcome.

### 7. Do More of What Works, Less of What Doesn't
Clark's core advice. Many traders degrade performance by engaging in trades without clear edge.

### 8. If Out of Sync, Trying Harder Won't Help
When trading going badly, trying harder often makes it worse. Clark: liquidate everything and take vacation.

### 9. Road to Success Paved with Mistakes
Dalio: learning from mistakes essential. Write down: (1) each mistake, (2) implied lesson, (3) intended change. Repeating same mistakes often the difference between success and failure.

### 10. Wait for High-Conviction Trades
Mai: perfectly content on sidelines until opportunity meets guidelines. Greenblatt: suboptimal positions may tie up capital needed for future opportunities.

### 11. Trade Because of Opportunity, Not Desire to Make Money
Benedict (end of 2010): marginal trades to reach profit target resulted in net losses.

### 12. The Importance of Doing Nothing
Daly: 800%+ cumulative gross during 12 years when broad markets flat -- achieved by discipline to stay in cash during negative environments.

### 13. Implementation Can Be More Important Than the Trade
O'Shea: implemented bearish equity view via long bonds rather than short Nasdaq. Nasdaq had 40% bear rally that would have stopped him out. Bond position had smooth uptrend. Trade succeeded because of implementation.

### 14. Trading Around a Position
As trade moves favorably, gradually reduce exposure. Reinstate on corrections. Generates extra profits, increases chances of staying with good trades. Balodimas: critical ingredient, allows profitability even on wrong-direction trades. Only negative: giving up portion of profits on trades that move smoothly.

### 15. Position Size More Important Than Entry Price
Trading too large causes good trades to be liquidated from fear. Trading larger for higher-probability trades can transform losing strategy into winning one. Thorp: varying bet size turned negative Blackjack edge into positive.

### 16. Determining Trade Size
Kelly criterion: mathematically optimal but assumes precisely known probabilities. Thorp: trade only half Kelly because penalty for overestimating is severe and most would find full Kelly volatility too high.

### 17. Vary Exposure Based on Opportunities
Claugus: varies net exposure from 110% long to 70% short based on whether stocks appear cheap or expensive.

### 18. Seek Asymmetric Return/Risk
Mai: right-skewed trades (limited loss, open-ended upside). O'Shea: options, CDS, TED spreads. Platt: achieves asymmetry at portfolio level through risk controls (tight loss limits, open-ended upside).

### 19. Beware of Euphoria-Borne Trades
Excessive euphoria should be cautionary flag of potential reversal.

### 20. If on Right Side of Euphoria/Panic, Lighten Up
Parabolic moves end abruptly and sharply. If petrified to be on other side, probably should be lightening.

### 21. Staring at Screen All Day Can Be Expensive
Clark: watching every tick leads to overtrading and increased chance of liquidating good positions.

### 22. Risk Control Is Critical

Risk control strategies mentioned by traders:
- **Ramsey**: risks only ~0.1% per trade.
- **BlueCrest**: 3% loss = 50% allocation cut; two 3% losses = full removal. Worst drawdown under 5% despite double-digit billions.
- **Benedict**: approaching 2.5% monthly loss triggers significant reduction.
- **Woodriff and Clark**: both cut exposure ~4x in response to 2008 volatility spike.
- **Thorp**: for well-defined risk, didn't reduce exposure. For directional trades with open-ended risk, exposure reduction on drawdowns part of methodology.

### 23. Don't Try to Be 100% Right
Vidich: instead of all-or-nothing, liquidate part of position. Much easier than liquidating entire position. Avoids riding whole position for large loss while preserving partial recovery potential.

### 24. Stops Must Match Trade Analysis
O'Shea: stops based on pain threshold rather than points disproving premise lead to repeated reentries with cumulative losses exceeding single wider stop. First decide where trade is wrong, then set stop accordingly. If implied loss too large, reduce position size.

### 25. Monthly Loss Constraints
Prudent for many traders, but detrimental for long-term investors. Taylor and Greenblatt: cutting exposure on interim weakness to limit monthly loss would conflict with strategy.

### 26. Power of Diversification
Dalio: "Holy Grail of investing." Truly uncorrelated assets could improve return/risk by factor of 5:1.

### 27. Correlation Can Be Misleading
Measures past relationships. Stocks and bonds sometimes move together, sometimes inversely. Using correlation during transition period can lead to exactly wrong conclusions.

### 28. Related Market Price Actions Provide Clues
Benedict and Ramsey: interaction of price movements in related markets is critical input. Divergences between related markets can be important signals.

### 29. Markets Behave Differently in Different Environments
Dalio: same conditions and government actions have different consequences in deleveraging vs. recession.

### 30. Pay Attention to How Markets Respond to News
Counter-to-anticipated response more meaningful than the news itself. Platt: adverse news without market response = confirmation, quadrupled position.

### 31. Major Events May Be Followed by Counterintuitive Moves
Dalio: gold standard abandonment (1971) and Mexican default (1982) both followed by rallies. Events may be overdiscounted, or government response overwhelms the catalyst.

### 32. Binary Outcomes = Excellent Option Buying Opportunities
Option models assume large moves unlikely. When fundamentals suggest binary outcome, options become mispriced. Greenblatt's Wells Fargo, Mai's Capital One.

### 33. Stock Can Be Well-Priced After Large Advance
What matters: price relative to future prospects, not past movement. Taylor's Apple.

### 34. Don't Make Decisions Based on Entry Price
Market doesn't care where you entered. Evaluate based on current outlook, not sunk cost.

### 35. Future Revenue May Not Be Reflected in Price
Claugus: companies with new revenue 1+ years out frequently not adequately discounted.

### 36. Value Investing Works
Periodic underperformance is the reason it maintains its edge. If worked all the time, enough followers would eliminate the edge.

### 37. EMH Is Inaccurate
Prices sometimes much too high, sometimes much too low. Greenblatt invokes Graham's Mr. Market: erratic business partner willing to sell at absurdly low or buy at ridiculously high prices.

### 38. Don't Alter Process for Investor Demands
Greenblatt: "You are setting yourself up for failure if you invest differently than you want to in order to please investors." Taylor: "I am trying to stop caring about what my clients think."

### 39. Volatility and Risk Are Not Synonymous
Low volatility doesn't mean low risk (selling OTM options: low vol until catastrophic loss). High volatility doesn't mean high risk (Mai's occasional large gains with curtailed downside). Conventional risk metrics misleading.

### 40. Don't Select Managers Solely on Past Performance
No meaningful correlation between past and future rankings. Choose based on process. Best performers during bubbles often most imprudent, not most skilled. Taylor underperformed 1999 (refused to buy inflated tech) -- same decision drove strong outperformance in subsequent years.

## Appendix A: The Gain to Pain Ratio

- Return only meaningful relative to risk required to get it. Could always get higher return by leverage.
- **GPR = sum of all monthly returns / absolute value of sum of all monthly losses**
- GPR of 1.0: average monthly losses equal to net gains. If 12% average return/year, monthly losses would also sum to 12%.
- GPR penalizes all losses in proportion to their size.
- Upside volatility beneficial (only impacts return). Sharpe ratio penalizes upside volatility.
- Rough guideline for liquid strategies: above 1.0 very good; above 1.5 excellent.
- Similar to "profit factor" (sum of all profitable trades / absolute value of all losing trades) but applied to monthly returns instead of trades.
- Algebraically: profit factor = GPR + 1 when applied to monthly returns.

## Appendix B: Options -- Understanding the Basics

- **Call option**: right (not obligation) to buy at strike price any time up to expiration.
- **Put option**: right (not obligation) to sell at strike price prior to expiration.
- Price of option = premium. Premium = intrinsic value + time value.
- **Buyer**: limited risk (premium paid), unlimited potential gain. **Seller**: unlimited risk, limited gain (premium received).
- Intrinsic value of call = amount market price exceeds strike. In-the-money: has intrinsic value. Out-of-the-money: no intrinsic value.
- Time value influenced by: (1) relationship between strike and market price, (2) time remaining until expiration, (3) volatility.
- **Implied volatility**: future volatility estimated from option premiums. May be higher or lower than historical.
- On average, implied volatility tends to exceed subsequent realized volatility -- options tend to be priced slightly high. Extra premium necessary to induce sellers to take open-ended risk (analogous to insurance premium margins).
