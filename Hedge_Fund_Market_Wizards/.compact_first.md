## Foreword by Ed Seykota

### The Parable of the Wheat Market
- King bans speculation during drought, making wheat crisis worse -- towns with excess can't share with towns in shortage
- Court jester proposes free market solution: post prices publicly, let traders arbitrage the spreads
- Markets resolve inefficiencies through price discovery and trading

> "Trading, it turns out, is the solution to most economic problems; free markets, sanctity of trading, and healthy economy are all ways to say the same thing."

### Schwager's Market Wizards Legacy
- Series inspired generations of traders since 1988
- **Ed Seykota** influenced by **Edwin Lefevre's** *Reminiscences of a Stock Operator* (still relevant 60 years after publication)
- Seykota keeps Schwager's books alongside *The Crowd* (Gustave LeBon) and *Extraordinary Popular Delusions and the Madness of Crowds* (Charles Mackay)
- Books become part of "consciousness and language of trading itself"

## Preface

### Selection Criteria and Diversity
**Three key criteria for interviews:**
1. Superior return/risk track records for 10+ years (often much longer)
2. Openness to provide valuable trading advice
3. Sufficient depth/color for readable chapters

- Half dozen interviews excluded for failing one or more criteria
- Diversity: traders range from $120B AUM (1,400 employees) to $50M solo operations
- Time horizons: some hold positions for years, others trade single-day windows
- Methods: fundamental only, technical only, or combined approaches

### Return/Risk as True Performance Measure
- **Return alone is misleading** -- high returns often reflect willingness to take more risk, not skill
- **Return/risk ratio** far more meaningful; fixation on return without risk = major investment mistake
- **Gain to Pain ratio** (Appendix A) is particularly useful metric

### Hedge Fund Performance Context
- Over 10-15 year periods, hedge funds consistently outperform equity indexes and mutual funds
- Typical pattern: modestly higher returns, far lower volatility and equity drawdowns
- **Ironic paradox**: hedge funds (viewed as speculative) are more conservative than mutual funds
- Best managers invariably found within hedge fund world (incentive fee structure draws top talent)

### Industry Growth and Market Changes
- 1988-1991: hedge funds minor player, $50-100B in assets
- Current: over **$2 trillion** (20x growth); enhanced role has influenced market behavior itself

> "Traders who are successful over the long run adapt. If they do use rules, and you meet them 10 years later, they will have broken those rules. Why? Because the world changed." -- **Colm O'Shea**

- Systematic trend-followers did enormously well in 1970s-80s (minority of futures activity)
- Performance declined as they became larger part of pool -- "too many big fish"
- **Ed Seykota**: "The markets are the same now as they were 5 to 10 years ago because they keep changing -- just like they did then"
- Goal: provide lessons meaningful 60 years from now, not "secret formula"

## Chapter 1: Colm O'Shea -- Knowing When It's Raining

### Profile and Philosophy
- **Global macro trader**: profits from directional trends in currency, interest rate, equity, commodity markets
- Views trading ideas as **hypotheses** -- market move counter to expected direction = proof hypothesis wrong
- No reluctance to liquidate immediately upon invalidation; sizes positions so invalidation loss = small %
- Hence no dramatic loss stories -- **wrong on 50%+ of trades** but never lets mistakes become "good stories"

### Performance Record (Dec 2004 - end 2011)
- Average annual compounded net return: **11.3%**
- Annualized volatility: **8.1%** | Worst monthly loss: **3.7%** | Max drawdown: **10.2%**
- All risk metrics ~half the average for global macro managers
- **Gain to Pain ratio: 1.76** | **Never had a losing year**
- If run at typical global macro exposure: ~**23%** annualized. As prop account: many times higher.
- Discrepancies disappear when measured in return/risk terms

### Career Path
- Interest sequence: politics -> economics -> markets
- Teen years coincided with **Thatcherism** and national debate over government's economic role
- Self-educated in economics before university

**Belgian Economy Forecast (age 19):**
- Consulting firm made him economist for Belgian economy
- General consensus: negative. After modeling, O'Shea concluded growth "pretty good"
- Wanted forecast 2%+ higher than any other economist
- Firm: "You can't do that. There is nothing to be gained by having a forecast outside the range. If you are wrong, we look ridiculous."
- O'Shea's forecast turned out right, but no one cared

> "As an economic consultant, how you package your work is more important than what you have actually done. There is massive herding in economic forecasting. By staying near the benchmark, you get all the upside of being right without the downside."

- One-year stint taught him he did NOT want to be an economic consultant

**Career progression:**
- **1992**: Graduated Cambridge, junior trader at **Citigroup** FX dept (profitable every year, steadily increasing trading line)
- **2003**: Portfolio manager for **Soros's Quantum Fund** (multibillion-dollar exposure)
- Two successful years at Soros, then global macro at **Balyasny** (began Dec 2004)
- **2007**: Formed own hedge fund, **COMAC** (launched June 2006)

### Origin: Markets Through Literature
- **Age 17 in Rome**: Discovered *Reminiscences of a Stock Operator* at open market -- only English book available
- "Everything in my life together" -- merged interest in politics/economics with market dynamics

> "I disagree. It's all there. The character Mr. Partridge tells him all that matters, 'It's a bull market.'"

> "Price movements only have meaning in the context of the fundamental landscape. To use a sailing analogy, the wind matters, but the tide matters, too."

### Economics Education Critique
- Studied economics at **Cambridge** from age 12 interest, not as pathway to markets
- Critical of modern economics education: mathematical rigor over philosophy

> "The thing about mathematical modeling is that in order to make problems tractable, you need to make assumptions. Assumptions then become axiomatic for the entire subject -- not because they are true, but because they are necessary to get a solution."

- Example: Efficient markets assumption needed for math, but markets aren't efficient
- Mathematical models can't include speculators' unpredictable impact
- Mathematization "has been a disaster" -- driven real economists into other fields

### Macro Philosophy: Keynes vs. Keynesianism
- **Favorite economist**: Keynes -- but Keynesianism distorted in US politics
- True Keynes: fiscal conservative -- deficits only in downturns (2008-2009), NOT during expansion
- **Key mistake**: Assuming there's always a good answer when there may not be one

### Soros ERM Trade (1992)
- O'Shea's first week: pound kicked out of ERM
- Soros read Bank of England had L10 billion reserves: "What a remarkable coincidence! That's exactly the size of the position I was thinking of taking."
- O'Shea argued UK wouldn't leave (political suicide) -- boss smiled, three hours later pound crashed

> "I had absolutely no comprehension of the power of markets versus politics. The policy makers didn't understand that either."

- UK remaining in ERM untenable: recession + overvalued currency while Germany needed high rates for reunification inflation
- Soros recognized the untenable -- like fighting gravity

### Trading Philosophy: Recognizing What Has Happened

> "Markets matter more than policy. You have to look at real fundamentals, not at what policy makers want to happen."

> "Fundamentals are not about forecasting the weather for tomorrow, but rather noticing that it is raining today."

- Edge NOT in forecasting but in recognizing what has happened
- Very difficult to pick major turning points -- losing strategy to try
- Wait until events confirm trading hypothesis
- Great trades don't require predictions: Soros's 1992 trade based on something already happened

> "Invest first; investigate later." -- George Soros

### The 2006-2007 Bubble: Pre-Crisis Positioning

**Bubble indicators:**
- Risk premiums too low everywhere
- Credit trading at ludicrous spreads with no concern for quality
- Companies posting suspiciously smooth earnings = manipulating numbers

> "The problem was most obvious in the credit markets. But you can't be short because you lose carry, and at the same time, the spreads get lower and lower."

- Being short credit in 2006-2007 was like being short Nasdaq in 1999 -- had to wait for right time

**Positioning strategy:**
- Participated in bubble with highly liquid positions and exits
- Traded through **options** -- bought calls in carry currencies (AUD at 5% vs US 1%)
- Long options: bubble continues = returns; collapse = only lose premium
- Option prices too cheap due to very low risk premiums

**Fed hiking cycle trades (2005-2006):**
- Futures market kept pricing imminent stops to hikes
- Great risk/reward betting they wouldn't stop -- kept repeating as months rolled on

### Why Macro Markets Are Inefficient

> "I try to avoid conceptualizing the market in anthropomorphic terms. Markets don't think. Just like mobs don't think."

- Very few market forces in macro vs. real money
- In FX or Treasuries, hedge funds tiny vs. PIMCO or Chinese government
- "I am a small fish swimming in a sea of real money. Fundamentals matter."

### LTCM Example
- Most famous hedge fund failure (distinguished from Madoff Ponzi scheme)
- First four years: steady profits, quadrupled assets
- May-Sep 1998: Lost all value, plunging 92%. Enormously leveraged, systemic risk.
- Fed orchestrated bailout. Impressive roster including two Nobel Prize winners.
- O'Shea didn't even know who LTCM was before crisis

### The 1998 Crisis: Trading Without Knowing Why
- T-bond futures going up limit every day -- didn't need to know why
- **Hypothesis testing approach**: something big happening, powerful enough to persist, trade in liquid positions
- If wrong, limited loss; if right, unknown upside

### August 2007: When It Really Started Raining

**O'Shea's view:** True start was **August 2007**
- Money markets stopped working -- banks didn't trust other banks
- **LIBOR rates spiked** -- clearest signal underlying assumptions broken
- Money market desks reported liquidity dried up completely
- Happened in most important market but largely ignored
- S&P made new highs in next two months

> "Declining housing prices were the impending storm clouds, but it started raining when money markets stopped working."

- Didn't need to forecast -- needed to recognize significance of what most ignored

**Critical insight**: As long as no one cares, there is no trend. Can't be short Nasdaq in 1999 just because overpriced. Wait until people care -- sell on way down, not up.

> "Taking Nasdaq as an example, you want to be selling Nasdaq at 4,000, but only after it has gone to 5,000."

### TED Spread Trade Rotation

**TED spread:** LIBOR minus T-bill rate. Normal ~25bp; Aug 2007 freeze: 200+ bp; post-Lehman record: 485bp.
- Wide TED = flight-to-quality; long TED = bearish position

**Q2 2008 rotation:**
- Corporate/bank credit spreads widened sharply in early 2008
- Forward market TED priced to narrow -- rotated from short credit into TED spreads
- When Q2 2008 optimism returned: TED near breakeven, short credit would have lost significantly
- Switched trade didn't have much downside during optimism

### Post-Lehman Collapse
- Most people knew it was going to happen but "failed to understand what it meant"
- People confused liquidity with solvency: "You can't fix a solvency problem by adding more liquidity"
- Banking system had solvency problem BECAUSE it had liquidity problem
- Simplified book, reduced leverage, restricted to liquid positions
- Confined counterparty risk to strongest counterparties
- Trades: long volatility, short credit, long TED spread, long dollar

### April 2009 Transition
- Stayed bearish until started losing money
- Two things changed: (1) economy stopped getting worse, (2) markets going up
- Underlying problems hadn't gone away, but not the market driver
- What matters: not whether growth good/bad, but whether getting better or worse

> "Never underestimate the ability of people to be optimistic and believe that everything is going to be okay."

- Formulated entirely new hypothesis: Asia-led recovery
- China turning around, metals higher, AUD moving up
- Went short long-term Treasuries, short dollar (reversal of flight-to-quality)
- Typically 10-15 trades in portfolio at any time

### Implementation Over Narrative

> "Implementation is the key in everything. Implementation is more important than the trade idea behind it."

- Macro most misunderstood as storytelling -- storytelling only 10%, other 90% = implementation and flexibility

**George Soros's key trait**: Least regret of anyone -- no attachment to ideas
- Made $250M on huge FX position in one day, quoted in press
- Market moved against, position disappeared -- didn't let structural views interfere
- "Will play up to public image as guru, but in trading has no attachment"

> "The danger in the narrative is that it may lead to the false impression that what you need to do to make lots of money is be really smart in economics. I don't really believe that is true."

### Empiricism and Flexibility
- Need method that suits personality; flexible enough to change mind
- **April 2009**: "Okay, I'm completely wrong. What is a different hypothesis?"
- Explanatory story comes AFTER hypothesis proven wrong
- Staying with original expectation would have been disastrous -- both equity and commodity markets embarked on multiyear rally

### Macro Process
1. **Empiricism first**: Notice what's actually happening
2. **Theme to fit observations**: Testable hypothesis, not grand theory
3. **Implementation**: Must be testable empirically in market

Difference from trend following: requires logical fundamental linkages. May end up in same trades but timing very different.

### Equity Avoidance
- Trades equity indexes but not individual stocks
- **Only one single-stock trade ever** -- bought Berkshire in 1999
- Price halved because Buffett refused dot-com: "stupidest reason ever"
- Held until started hedge fund -- "believed in himself more than Warren Buffett"

### Stop Loss Philosophy
- Generic rules designed for **gamblers** in emotional denial
- Sets stops **wide enough** based on hypothesis, not pain threshold
- "The market doesn't care about your pain"
- **First decide where you are wrong** -> stop level -> divide willing loss by per-contract loss -> position size
- Most common error: do it backwards (position size first, then pain = stop)
- Disciplined stops too close = death by 1,000 cuts
- If fundamental idea needs long time, short-term stop makes no sense
- **If entry/exit out of sync with reason you like trade**, money management plan will fail

### Biggest Errors: Opportunity Costs
- Most big errors are **opportunity errors**, not downside losses
- **2010 European debt crisis**: belief in severity kept him from participating in sentiment-driven bull
- Missed biggest macro theme of year (Sep onward: equities/commodities up)
- "No one else cared, and as long as no one cares, there is no crisis"

### Trading Bubbles
- **Trade long side, not short** -- uptrend smooth, downtrend erratic
- Initiate early; structure with limited worst-case (long calls in low-vol bubble)
- Be early; worst move: stubborn and late to convert
- Nasdaq: 1,500 (late 1998) to 5,000+ with hardly any corrections
- After breaking below 3,100 (Jun 2000), rallied to near 4,300 -- **40% rebound in dead market**
- "I don't think you will find many people that have made the majority of their money shorting bubbles"
- After Nasdaq top: played via long bonds (smoother path, same economic slowdown idea)

### Psychology vs. Fundamentals
- Bull markets can be **psychologically driven** rather than fundamentally driven
- Beliefs invulnerable to evidence are "quite durable" (political analogy)
- In next 10-20 years wants to **monetize other people's irrational euphoria** better
- Simple gauge for psychology: "if it trades like a bull market, it's a bull market"

### Gold Analysis

> "Gold is the only commodity where supply is literally about 100 times as much as physically used in any year."

- Not true of wheat or copper where true shortages develop
- No shortage of gold ever -- value entirely dependent on psychology
- Would **ignore production/consumption**; based price on inflation and dollar value
- "What do you think about gold?" at meetings = you should be long gold
- Ridiculous when analysts wrote lengthy reports on annual production and jewelry usage

### Silver Bubble Trade (May 2011)
- Traded via **options** due to gap risk; long side not short side
- **Tops are messy, reversals horrendous** -- very rare to find comfortable shorts in bear markets

### Differentiating Bubble Reversal from Bull Market Correction
- Difficult. Methods: (1) pretend you're a CTA -- systematic trend definition, or (2) look for exponential rise characteristic of late bubble

### Adaptability
- Uses **risk guidelines**, not rules
- "If use rules, 10 years later will have broken them. The world changed."
- **Can't trade for the money** -- won't cut it
- Successful traders continue well beyond financial need -- it's what they do

### Can Trading Be Taught?
- "Can't be taught, but can be learned"
- If try to teach you what I do, you'll fail: "you are not me"
- Friend sat next to O'Shea for years, now successful elsewhere
- "He is not the same as me. He became something else. He became him."

### Traits for Success
- **Perseverance** and **emotional resilience** to keep coming back
- If you don't love it, much better things to do with life

### VAR Discussion
- Tells how volatile portfolio was in past -- entirely backward looking
- Have to recognize future will be different
- 2008: managers said halved risk (leverage 4x to 2x)
- O'Shea: "Do you realize volatility has gone up five times?" -- risk actually increased

### Right-Skewed Trades (Common Theme)
- All trades: limited max loss, open-ended upside
- Long options, long CDS protection, long TED spread
- Yield curve spread: long short-term/short long-term = most profit with fraction of risk
- August 2007 example: expected rate cuts. Instead of just long short-term rates, also implemented as yield curve spread (curve relatively flat). If rates went up, flat curve meant long-term rates should go up at least as much. Much better return-to-risk.

## Chapter 2: Ray Dalio

### Correlation vs. Causation in Portfolio Construction
- **Rejects traditional correlation** as diversification tool -- merely average of historical behavior, not causality
- Correlation variable: stocks/bonds negatively correlated when growth expectations volatile, positively when inflation expectations volatile
- Chooses assets with different **drivers**; targets ~100 return streams
- At any time ~20 significant positions account for ~80% of risk

> "Correlation is just the word people use to take an average of how two prices have behaved together. When I am setting up my trading bets, I am looking at whether the drivers are different."

### Bridgewater Culture: Radical Transparency
- Seeks truth through independent thinking and innovation
- Record nearly all discussions to eliminate spin
- Encourages thoughtful disagreement; three CIOs must concur (Dalio, Bob Prince, Greg Jensen)
- Criticism flows both ways: subordinates criticize Dalio all the time
- After client meeting with European pension fund, salesperson criticized Dalio for being inarticulate
- Given grade of "F" by analyst one year out of school -- Dalio loved it

### Decision-Making: 99% Systematic, 1% Discretionary
- Focuses on **criteria** (principles), not individual positions
- Principles systematized for different circumstances
- Each strategy has deeply understood track record
- 36 years continuous development and refinement
- **No technical inputs** -- purely fundamental-driven systematic approach

### Origin of System (around 1980)
- Developed discipline to write down reasons for trades
- Compared actual outcome with original reasoning on liquidation
- Learning from experience inadequate -- takes too long for representative sample
- Backtesting gave perspective; defined decision rules requiring logical basis
- Individual rules sometimes revised (e.g., UK went from net oil importer to exporter -- changed oil price rule)

### Controlled Drawdowns
- Only two drawdowns worse than 12% in 20 years; worst was 20%
- Balance risk across multiple independent drivers
- Stress-test through multiple time frames and scenarios
- Managers with 5-year records think "this really works" but lack perspective

### Timeless and Universal Testing
- **Timeless**: test through all different times (1930s deleveraging for zero-rate environment)
- **Universal**: test across all countries (Japan postbubble for deleveraging)
- No reason effectiveness should change across time or geography
- Post-WWII U.S. data alone entirely inadequate -- doesn't contain deleveragings

### Risk Limits and Position Sizing
- Position size limits exist but not price stops
- Trade ~150 markets; don't reduce exposure during drawdowns

> "The only pertinent question is whether my being in a losing position is a statistically meaningful indicator of what the subsequent price movement will be. And it is not."

- Position sizing same whether at new high or down 15%
- If position works poorly, always causes reexamination of strategy

### 1994 Bond Lesson
- Long multiple bond markets, bonds sold off
- Realized: trading on **spread basis** (rather than absolute) produced much better return/risk
- Took advantage of universal truth: enhance return/risk by reducing correlation
- **Changed process from losing position insight**

### 2008: Depression Gauge
- Developed **eight years before 2008** (low rates + credit contraction + declining stocks + widening spreads)
- When triggered, system rules and risk constraints adjusted to fit deleveraging
- Studied inflationary deleveragings (Germany 1920s, Latin America 1980s)
- Studied deflationary deleveragings (Great Depression, Japan 1990s)
- Reviewed bank 10Ks, marked balance sheets to market
- Obviously investors heavily leveraged in carry trades that would unwind when credit bubble popped

### Pure Alpha Advantage
- Most hedge funds mix betas and alpha -- ~70% correlated with stocks
- Much "alpha" = beta at alpha prices; Pure Alpha avoids embedded betas
- Fall 2008: most traders selling to cut exposure; Bridgewater buying with liquidity (as long as no bearish fundamental shift)

### Managing Large Assets
- 2010: highest return ever despite largest assets ever
- Trades virtually every market, small amount per market relative to equity
- Changes positions slowly -- average 12-18 months for long/short reversal
- Transaction costs = function of amount moved in given time frame
- Considerably more capacity than managers trading fewer markets with higher turnover

### U.S. Economic Outlook
- Broad global deleveraging, negative for growth
- QE limited: bond sellers buy something similar, not houses/cars
- 2% growth won't meaningfully lower unemployment
- Best policy: spread problems over long period, nominal rates below nominal growth rates
- Infrastructure spending better than transfer payments -- put idle people to work on useful projects

### Inflation Risk from Money Printing
- Not near term; longer term: risk foreign investors pull back if fear currency weakness
- First sign: shortening duration of bonds bought by foreigners
- Central bank buys more bonds (monetization) -> currency weakens
- Early stages: deflation to low inflation (like Roosevelt going off gold standard 1933)
- Both gold and bonds can go up together in deleveraging (different from normal)

### Five-Phase Country Cycle
1. **Stage 1**: Poor, think poor
2. **Stage 2**: Getting rich, still think poor
3. **Stage 3**: Rich, think rich
4. **Stage 4**: Getting poorer, still think rich (leveraging, twin deficits, bubbles, infrastructure decay)
5. **Stage 5**: Deleveraging and decline (print money, currencies depreciate, reduced power)

- U.S. in Stage 5 since ~2008. Entire cycle ~100-150 years; decline ~20 years.
- Doesn't have to be terrible -- British Empire wasn't cataclysmic, Japan stayed about the same

### Key Lessons
- **Mistakes provide path to improvement** -- write down each, modify approach
- **Biggest investor mistake**: believing recent past persists; high returns imply expensive, not good
- **Holy Grail**: return/risk improved by 5:1 with truly independent assets
- Focus on **drivers** (forward-looking), not correlations (backward-looking)
- Heavy use of **spread positions** for different drivers
- Any fundamental model assuming static relationships will be flawed in different environments

## Chapter 3: Larry Benedict

### Profile
- **Pure tape reader** -- no charts during trading day, no fundamentals
- Has "the chart in my head" -- watches prices and markets vs other markets
- Trade duration: seconds to one-two days
- Primary: S&P futures, traded vs euro, yen, T-notes, gold, crude oil

### Career Origin: Repeated Failures
- Syracuse senior, no career plan; girlfriend's father (soybean trader at CBOE) took him to floor
- Immediate revelation: "This is what I got to do" -- loved aggressive, competitive environment
- Clerk at CBOE ($5K salary). Boss had $15M account.
- Fired first day (account off 20 lots), came back, fired again
- Six months with abusive boss

**Fossett partnership:** $10K own + $15K firm. 60% profits on firm's capital.
- "I didn't have a handle on what to do. I was just making bets."

**1987 Crash:**
- Short straddles: short 20 calls AND 20 puts
- Puts skyrocketed + volatility exploded -> lost money BOTH sides
- Account: ~$25K to deficit in hours. Covered puts at 20 (went to 200 by close).

> "Seeing that day taught me that anything can happen"

- "2008 paled by comparison because in 1987 virtually the entire move happened in one day"

**Three failures** before success:
1. Fossett: 1987 crash
2. Second $25K stake: lost $16K in one month
3. Friend's $50K prop: lost quickly

> "No. I never gave up."

- "I had no strategy and no discipline. I was, however, gaining experience."
- "All the mistakes were providing experience, which was critical."

### Turning Point: Spear Leeds
- **Larry Lovecchio** as mentor -- discipline by example
- Specialist + prop trader; order book provided inherent advantage
- Delta-neutral: sold calls, bought futures against them
- Started watching **intermarket price relationships**
- Example: S&P moving inverse to bonds, bonds down but S&P not responding -> sell S&P
- **Marty Schwartz**: "Don't average losing trades. Be smaller than you need to be. Take profits."
- Peter Kellogg warned about Schwartz: "No one can last very long." Schwartz shuddered at Benedict's name.

### Risk Management
- **Hard rule**: down >2% month-to-date = clear everything
- Mean reversion: three days up -> short; three days down -> long
- "Much tighter than most hedge fund managers"
- Source: "losing so many times early when I didn't have controls. I just don't want to lose again."

### Temper
- Staff stocks phones, keyboards -- "particularly vulnerable targets"
- Pre-electronic: "almost every day, sometimes multiple times"
- "I am the worst sore loser you ever met"
- Spear Leeds charged for broken phones

### Electronic Trading Benefits
- "Now I don't have to talk to anyone" -- anonymous
- Previously brokers stole from him; pit committee invalidated low-of-day fill

### Correlation Trading
- "I learned to pay attention to how markets moved relative to each other. I became a correlation trader."
- Cannot teach: "it is so innate." Constantly adapting to which correlations work.
- As soon as position on, looking for best offsetting hedge

**Real-time example (Oct 2011):**
- S&P at 1227, bonds at 20 bid (were 25 bid last time S&P there)
- Usually bonds down = S&P up. If S&P doesn't rally, bonds should.
- Could buy S&P and bonds simultaneously and make money
- "It is that simple. I do lots of these trades every day."

### Key Principles
- "I let the market dictate, not my macro views"
- "For the first five years, my opinion did nothing but lose me money"
- Bearish on S&P (Oct 2011) but net long: 85% of time market marks up on option expiration morning
- Failed traders: "gambler's mentality, always looking for one trade to make it all back"
- "This is a business where you have to work" -- hundreds of transactions daily; grinds out returns

### 9/11
- Sold futures immediately when first plane hit (market rallying on "small plane" story)
- When reopened: "Mr. Patriotic" -- bought stocks, lost all profit + more

> "Going long when the market reopened was a purely emotional trade."

### October 2008 Mistake
- Japan would outperform US. Every hedge fund long Japan/HK/China. Markets fell MORE than US (liquidation).

### Risk Management Summary (Mark Rossano, intern)
> "The biggest principle Larry pushes is that you are not a trader; you are a risk manager. Never stay in a losing trade because you think it will come back."

- **Biggest common mistake**: averaging losing trades
- Trade from opportunity, not desire to make money

## Chapter 4: Scott Ramsey -- Low-Risk Futures Trader

### Background
- College dropout (4 years, 3.9 GPA, didn't graduate)
- Phone clerk at CME -> commodity broker. First check: **$43**
- Early tech approach: **John Murphy's** book. Always used stops from day one.
- Key college lesson: T-bill long losing 3 ticks/day ($75/contract). 10-tick loss became 50-tick loss.

### Floor Trading = Disadvantage
- 1982: Leased IMM seat expecting Holy Grail
- Reality: "Almost no useful information" -- needed to see **relationships between markets**
- Standing in one pit = tunnel vision. Six months, then moved back upstairs.

### Transition to CTA
- 1993: First client ($100K). CTA charged $50/contract vs. his $12.
- 2000: Started fund for identical execution across all accounts.

### Methodology: Fundamentals + Technicals

**Long Bonds (1990s):**
- Consensus: government borrowing crowds out private (bearish bonds)
- Bond market NOT going down despite bearish consensus
- "If everyone thinks this way, and market goes the other way, they'll have to cover shorts."
- Began thinking from **perspective of other traders**: "What would I feel if I were short?"

**2000 Long Bond Trade:**
- After stock speculation, conditions couldn't get better
- **What happens if we slow at the margins?** First 3-4 years: 2/3 trades in fixed income.
- Uses fundamentals for directional bias, technicals to confirm

**Core:** Not paid to be right -- paid to make money.

### Market Response Analysis
- Calculate percentage response during crisis events
- **Rank strongest to weakest** -- strongest during crisis = leaders when pressure off
- Analogy: volleyball underwater -- push down (crisis), let go, ball pops out
- **"Always buy strongest, sell weakest"**
- **"Chaos creates opportunity"** -- learn about markets during crises

### High Correlation (Post-2008)
- "Dog is stocks, tail is everything else"
- If equities up but commodities stall next week -> suspect long commodity positions

### Discretionary By Personality
- Fits personality -- enjoys observation
- Pattern recognition becomes subconscious: "second nature like driving a car"

### Risk Control
- Typically risks **~10 basis points** per trade; 90% exits first day if behind
- "Chicken when it comes to risks" -- might lose 10 times until 11th big win
- **Most important: control downside** -- if mental energy on damage control, can't see opportunities
- **Worst month: October 2003 (-10%)** -- followed 200% over prior years in that theme
- Long fixed income, positive unemployment report -> giant reversal (theme ended)
- Really bad month without open profits: 2-3% loss

### Technical Tools
- **RSI** (divergences, not overbought/oversold), **200-day MA**, **Fibonacci retracements**
- Combined signals: price at 200-day + 50% retracement = significant

### Trade Examples

**QE2 Dollar Rally:**
- Market viewed QE2 as "money printing" -> alternatives
- Ramsey: QE2 = Fed swapping cash for bonds (not printing)
- Monitored 20+ currencies for first to weaken

## Chapter 6: Edward Thorp -- The Innovator

### Track Record Demolishes EMH
- **Princeton Newport Partners** (Nov 1969-Dec 1988): 19.1% gross (15.1% net)
- **227 winning months, 3 losing** (all <1%) -- 98.7% winning rate
- Probability by chance: ~1 in 10^76. With 1B traders: <1 in 10^66.

> "Track records such as Thorp's prove conclusively that it is possible to beat the market."

### List of Firsts
- First wearable computer (with **Claude Shannon**) for roulette
- First blackjack card-counting (*Beat the Dealer*)
- First systematic warrant/convertible trading (*Beat the Market* with **Kassouf**)
- First option-pricing model = **Black-Scholes** (used profitably years before publication)
- First market-neutral fund, first quant fund
- First convertible arbitrage, first statistical arbitrage
- Likely first to uncover **Madoff** fraud

### Background
- Father: WWI veteran (Purple Heart, Silver Star, Bronze Star), Depression security guard
- "Made me think carefully about planning and making sure I had the downside covered"
- Self-taught science from age 8-9; high school ranked 31/32 in LA
- Age 8: bought Kool-Aid for nickel, made six glasses, sold for penny apiece
- Shoveled snow: raised price from nickel to dime to 15 cents as demand allowed

### Roulette
- 1955: Others said impossible. "I don't think that is true."
- Analog experiment: ball down inclined track -- motion repeatable
- Shannon: reconditioned wheel ($1,500), Shannon's basement, slate pool table
- Strobe + clock: **44% edge** predicting most likely octant
- First wearable computer (now MIT Museum): cigarette pack size, toe switches, radio receiver, tones
- Shannon's idea: continuous calculation, last tone heard = octant to bet

**Vegas (August 1961):** Shannon timed/computed, Thorp bet. ~12-15 bets before wires broke.
- "For some reason I never was scared. I just don't scare. I am aware, and I avoid taking foolish risks."

### Blackjack Development
- December 1958: house edge 0.62% with basic strategy. Tracking cards = positive edge.
- IBM 704 at MIT (1959). Fives removed: 3.3% edge. Tens strategy: more opportunities.
- Visualized as **10-dimensional probability space**

**Meeting Claude Shannon:** Secretary: "five minutes." Found "elfish-looking man with twinkle in eye and ready intelligence." Shannon cross-examined 10-15 minutes: "really big ideas here." Five minutes became several hours when Thorp mentioned roulette -- Shannon = "ultimate gadgeteer."

**Publication:** AMS talk: expected 50, got 300 (sunglasses, pinky rings). 20,000 letters to MIT.
- **Tom Wolfe** (white suit and all) wrote AP story.
- **Kimmel**: midnight blue Cadillac, two blonde "nieces" in fur coats, $100K bankroll.

**Casino impact:** Three tiers: ~1,000 good players, ~10,000 basic strategy, ~1M who thought they could win.
- Blackjack became most popular game. Casinos had good thing, thought it was bad.
- 1964 meeting discussed "breaking knees" -- settled on changing rules.
- **Ken Uston**: "back room, cheek broken."

### Transition to Markets
- "The biggest game is Wall Street."
- Summer 1964: Edwards/McGee = "road not to go down." Warrants should be mathematizable.

## Chapter 7: Jamie Mai -- Seeking Asymmetry

### Option Pricing Inefficiencies
- **Best predictor of future vol spikes: historically low vol.** Complacency = mispricing.
- **Long-dated options underpriced**: longer duration = lower implied vol (makes no sense)
- **Sqrt(time)** breaks down for long periods: (1) vol reverts from lows, (2) trends produce larger moves

> "Option prices tend to be priced too low in smoothly trending markets."

### Brazilian Rate Trade (2010)
- Spot 8%, 6-month forward 12%. Implied vol = nothing happening is >4-sigma event.
- Strike ~10%: out-of-money vs forward, in-the-money vs spot. Profitable even if rates rose halfway.

### Worst-of Options (Euro Short)
- EUR/AUD puts + EUR/CHF puts. Cheap because -0.50 correlation assumed.
- Euro crash would swamp both, overwhelming inverse correlation.
- Vanilla: ~4.5%. Worst-of: **<1/10th**. Result: **6x capital**.
> "The market always assumes symmetry, and you look for potential asymmetry."

### XLP Outperformance
- XLP matched S&P despite 0.5 beta over entire period since inception. Beta = terrible long-term proxy.
- 25% revaluation potential. Outperformance option very cheap using 0.5 beta.

### Cheap Sigma
- Canadian dollar (2007): huge smooth move, very low vol. Move "nonsensically improbable" per vol.
- Deep out-of-the-money options severely mispriced in trending markets.

### Subprime CDO Trade
- BBB tranches -> AAA via CDOs (75-80% rated AAA from 100% BBB)
- CDO pool: **all clones** of lowest-tier subprime. If one fails, all fail.
> "The BBB tranches were like people contaminated by highly contagious flu."

**Three reasons false AAA:** (1) historical data from real mortgages ≠ no-doc; (2) low correlation assumptions; (3) rating agency conflict

- **Singer** (Elliot) showed prices just had to stop rising
- **Burry** (Scion) created synthetic securitizations for specific names
- CDS on AA tranches for LIBOR + 50bp: "complete and utter insanity"
- Lehman CDO at 70bp: wouldn't sell protection at 300. "How could you issue at 70 what you won't risk at 300?"
- **TABX** made charade impossible
- **August 2007**: $50K Friday -> **$4.5M Monday**

### SEC Meeting
- Rating agencies rated thousands same as Treasuries when "not worth paper printed on"
- Despite horrific record, market still jumps when agencies opine on sovereign credit

### Korea (2003-2005)
- Cheap because consistently cheap (circular). ~10 trips in 18 months.
- Companies at **negative enterprise values** (cap $300M, no debt, $550M cash)
- Catalyst: passage of time -- inexorable profit accumulation

### China Coal -> Reversal
- Coal imports as freight tailwind -> shipbuilding boom = 20%/year capacity increase
- Buy thesis became highest conviction **SHORT** in 2011. Puts on dry bulk operators.

### Five Pillars
1. Find mispricings from inappropriate standard assumptions
2. Skewed probabilities: gain x P(win) >= 2x loss x P(lose)
3. Asymmetric: limited down, open-ended up
4. Wait for high conviction (15-20 independent risks)
5. Cash to target risk (50-80%)

### Five Invalid Assumptions
1. Normal distribution (sometimes bimodal: Capital One 6x on calls)
2. Forward = future mean (sometimes spot/forward midpoint)
3. Vol scales sqrt(time) (breaks from low base)
4. Trend irrelevant to vol (assumes random direction)
5. Stable correlations (risk-on/risk-off breaks this)

> "We think about risk probabilistically. Comfortable losing 100% four times if 25x payout likely over 10."

- 50 dry wells per conviction trade. Volatility ≠ risk.

### Flexibility
- Dry bulk: started as buy, research proved wrong -> highest conviction short
- Crude oil arbitrage (2008): nearby at discount to forward, store on tanker, redeliver. Cornwall too small.
