Foreword & Preface

Ed Seykota opens with a parable: a king bans speculation during a wheat drought, making the crisis worse—towns with surplus can’t share with towns in shortage because no one can trade. The court jester proposes free markets as the solution: post prices, let traders arbitrage. Trading itself resolves economic imbalances. Seykota places Schwager’s books alongside LeBon’s The Crowd and Mackay’s Extraordinary Popular Delusions as essential trading literature. The lessons endure because human nature is constant.

Schwager’s preface establishes three selection criteria: superior return/risk over 10+ years, willingness to share genuine trading insight, and sufficient depth for readable chapters. The 15 traders range from Dalio’s $120B Bridgewater (1,400 employees) to solo operations running $50M. His central argument: return alone is meaningless. A trader who earns 20% with 30% volatility is less skilled than one earning 11% with 8% volatility. The Gain to Pain ratio (Appendix A) captures this properly. The hedge fund industry grew from $50–100B in 1988 to over $2 trillion by 2012—a 20x expansion that fundamentally changed market dynamics. Successful traders adapt to these shifts; the rules they followed a decade ago have been deliberately broken.

Part I: Macro Men

Colm O’Shea — Knowing When It’s Raining

O’Shea discovered markets at 17 when he found Reminiscences of a Stock Operator at a Roman book market—the only English book available. It fused his twin interests in politics and economics with the mechanism of markets. He graduated Cambridge in 1992, traded profitably every year at Citigroup with steadily increasing responsibility, managed money for Soros’s Quantum Fund, then launched his own fund COMAC in 2007. His combined public track record (2004–2011): 11.3% annualized return with 8.1% volatility and a worst monthly loss of only 3.7%. Run at half the risk of typical macro managers, if scaled to peers’ volatility his returns would be approximately 23%.

His first day at Citigroup coincided with the pound being kicked out of the ERM. He argued to his boss that the UK wouldn’t leave because it would be political suicide for the Conservatives. Three hours later, the pound crashed out. George Soros had read the Bank of England had £10 billion in reserves: “£10 billion. What a remarkable coincidence! That’s exactly the size of the position I was thinking of taking.” The lesson: “I had absolutely no comprehension of the power of markets versus politics. The policy makers didn’t understand that either.” Markets matter more than policy; fundamentals matter more than what policy makers want to happen.

His philosophy centers on treating each trade as a hypothesis. If the market moves counter to expectations, the hypothesis is disproven—exit immediately. Position sizes ensure that invalidation losses stay small, which is why he has no dramatic blowup stories: “Wrong on 50%+ of trades but never lets mistakes get close to ‘good story’ territory.” Before university, a consulting firm hired him to forecast the Belgian economy at age 19. His contrarian forecast (2%+ higher than consensus) proved correct, but the firm wouldn’t publish it—there’s nothing gained by being outside the range, and everything to lose if wrong. This taught him that economic forecasting rewards herding, not accuracy.

On the 2008 crisis: O’Shea noticed money markets had frozen in August 2007—a signal most ignored. LIBOR rates spiked, banks stopped trusting other banks, and the most important market in the world (money markets, the heart of capitalism) was breaking down while the S&P made new highs in the following months. His insight wasn’t prediction but observation: “Fundamentals are not about forecasting the weather for tomorrow, but rather noticing that it is raining today.” He participates in bubbles through long options (limited downside, unlimited upside), then waits to sell only after the market has turned. “You want to be selling Nasdaq at 4,000, but only after it has gone to 5,000.”

During Q2 2008, when Bear Stearns’ bailout convinced people the crisis was solved, O’Shea saw it differently: people confused liquidity with solvency. “You can’t fix a solvency problem by adding more liquidity.” He rotated from short credit into TED spreads—a better risk/reward expression of the same bearish thesis. When the TED spread narrowed during Q2 optimism, his position barely lost, unlike short credit which would have suffered. His April 2009 reversal from bearish to bullish—recognizing the government response was sufficient and growth was getting “less negative”—saved his year. “Never underestimate the ability of people to be optimistic and believe that everything is going to be okay.”

On implementation: “Implementation is the key in everything. Implementation is more important than the trade idea behind it.” Macro is most misunderstood as storytelling; storytelling is only 10% of importance, the other 90% is implementation and flexibility. Soros’s key trait was least regret of anyone: made $250M on a huge FX position in one day, then the position disappeared when the market moved against him—no emotional attachment. On stop losses: most traders set stops based on pain threshold rather than at points that disprove their thesis. First decide where you are wrong—that determines the stop level. Divide the amount you’re willing to lose by the per-contract loss to get position size. Most traders do it backwards: start with position size, then the pain threshold determines the stop. Result: death by a thousand cuts.

His trading rules evolved into three principles: (1) if you wake up thinking about a position, it’s too big; (2) never stop asking questions—speak to as many people as you can; (3) when everything lines up, swing for it, but if the position behaves unexpectedly, cut it immediately—the market is telling you that you don’t know. “Your job as a trader is to make the line go from bottom left to top right. That’s it.” He watched managers in 2008 lose 50%, some 80%. Why? They couldn’t accept they were wrong. They kept doing the math, insisting they were right. “Their job isn’t to create billions of dollars of embedded value; their job is to make the line go from bottom left to top right.”

Despite his success, O’Shea harbors existential doubts about trading as a career: “But what have I achieved? I haven’t built anything. You are only as good as your last trade, and that is it. You build nothing; you just trade… there is nothing left to show for it, except for money.” Trading was fun when he could walk away; when you have a business, you can’t—it becomes a prison. On losing streaks: “I am never going to make a penny again. I am crap. I have always been crap. I have just been found out.” Even after making money for ten years, traders can still fall into that mind-set. His solution: discipline yourself not to trade for two weeks. Take a holiday—but get out of everything first. First few days you’ll torture yourself, but after a few days, you can let it go. When ready to restart, trade a fraction of your normal size.

Ray Dalio — The Man Who Loves Mistakes

Dalio runs Bridgewater Associates, the world’s largest hedge fund with $120B in assets and 1,400 employees. His approach is “timeless and universal”—testing strategies across all time periods and all countries. To understand the 2008 crisis, he studied the 1930s Depression, Japan’s post-bubble decade, and Latin American deleveragings of the 1980s. He developed a “depression gauge” eight years before 2008, waiting patiently for conditions that would trigger it.

His most important insight is on diversification. He calls it the “Holy Grail of investing” but rejects the conventional approach of measuring backward-looking correlations between asset classes. “Correlation is just the word people use to take an average of how two prices have behaved together. When I am setting up my trading bets, I am not looking at correlation; I am looking at whether the drivers are different.” He focuses on the underlying drivers that determine price behavior—growth, inflation, risk premiums. During regime changes (deleveragings vs. normal recessions), historical correlations break down completely. Gold and bonds typically move inversely, but during early-phase deleveragings both can rise together as monetary easing reduces interest rates while enhancing long-term currency depreciation concerns. With 15 or more return streams uncorrelated by underlying driver, the return/risk ratio can improve by a factor of 5:1.

Bridgewater’s culture is built on radical transparency: every meeting is recorded, junior employees are encouraged to challenge senior ones, and mistakes are treated as learning opportunities. “The road to success is paved with mistakes.” After one client meeting with a large European pension fund, a salesperson criticized Dalio for being inarticulate and running on too long. A new analyst, just one year out of school, gave him a grade of “F.” Dalio loved it—he knew they were helping him improve and understood that honest feedback was their job. Decision-making requires three chief investment officers to concur, and each strategy develops a deeply understood track record: “You say that. Why do you say that? What is the evidence?”

His depression gauge, developed eight years before 2008, monitored conditions that historically coincided with depression-like environments: interest rates below a certain low level, contractions in private credit growth, declining stock markets, and widening credit spreads. When triggered, system rules and risk constraints adjusted to fit the deleveraging regime. In 2008, he watched banks’ 10-K filings to mark balance sheets to market, identifying that investors were heavily leveraged in carry trade positions that would unwind when the credit bubble popped. His Pure Alpha strategy avoided embedded betas—unlike most hedge funds, which are approximately 70% correlated with stocks and package beta as alpha. His biggest investor mistake insight: people assume what worked in the recent past will continue. “High past returns simply imply the asset has become more expensive and is a poorer, not better, investment.”

Dalio views the U.S. as in Stage 5 (decline) of a five-phase national cycle lasting 100–150 years. Stage 1: countries are poor and think they are poor. Stage 2: getting rich quickly but still think they are poor. Stage 3: rich and think of themselves as rich. Stage 4: becoming poorer but still acting rich—leveraging up, running twin deficits, spending on military to protect global interests, until bubbles form and burst. Stage 5: deleveraging and relative decline. The fifth phase typically lasts ~20 years. It doesn’t have to be catastrophic if managed well—spreading problems over time with nominal interest rates kept below nominal growth rates—but can be terrible if badly managed. The decline of the British Empire after WWII wasn’t cataclysmic because the adjustment was spread over decades.

Larry Benedict — Beyond Three Strikes

Benedict’s story is one of extraordinary persistence. He blew up three separate trading accounts before age 25: the first lost in the 1987 crash (he was short straddles and got caught on the downside), the second lost $16,000 in a single month, the third pulled by a friend who’d staked him. Each time he came back. “I just wanted to learn how to do it and win.”

His breakthrough came through learning correlation trading—watching how markets move relative to each other rather than in isolation. “I learned to pay attention to how markets moved relative to each other. I became a correlation trader—the same thing I still do today.” He trades S&P futures both standalone and against other liquid futures, exploiting temporary dislocations in cross-market relationships. Duration: seconds to two days. Hundreds of transactions daily. Pure tape reading with extremely short horizons.

His risk management is among the strictest of any trader interviewed: if down more than 2% month-to-date on any day, he clears out everything and trades smaller until profitable again. This extreme discipline stems directly from his early blowups. He never had a losing year after developing this framework. On the 1987 crash: he was on the floor and saw the panic firsthand. On 9/11: he was trading when the towers fell and had to navigate the chaos in real time. Both experiences reinforced his conviction that risk management is everything. “I am the worst sore loser you ever met. I am brutal on myself.”

Scott Ramsey — Low-Risk Futures Trader

Ramsey evolved from pure technical analysis to a hybrid approach combining fundamentals for direction with technicals for entry. His realization: “You need to embrace fundamentals alongside technicals to make big money.” He uses fundamental analysis to establish a directional bias, then waits for technical confirmation before entering. Within sectors, he buys the strongest and sells the weakest markets. His primary technical tools: RSI divergences (not as overbought/oversold), the 200-day moving average, and Fibonacci retracements—especially when multiple signals converge at the same level.

His defining trade illustrates the power of contrarian positioning. In the 1990s, the consensus was that government borrowing was bearish for bonds. Ramsey’s insight: “If everyone thinks this way, and it seems so logical, then if the market goes the other way, everyone is going to be wrong, and they will have to cover their shorts.” He went long bonds against the crowd and caught a massive move. He held for years but traded around the position—adding on weakness, lightening on strength.

His QE2 dollar trade demonstrates the full methodology. Many investors viewed QE2 as “money printing” and shifted dollars into alternatives. Ramsey’s contrarian view: QE2 was just the Fed swapping non-interest-bearing cash for interest-bearing bonds—when it ended, the shift out of dollars would stop and the dollar would recover. He monitored at least 20 currencies against the dollar, waiting for the first to weaken. The weak link: the Turkish lira, breaking out to a two-year low. He didn’t take the trade until the lira broke out of its trading range—“if no breakout, no trade.” His gold/platinum trade in August 2011 followed the same logic: when gold surged to $1,900 and platinum barely moved, he recognized platinum as the weak link. Novice speculators buy laggards as proxies for leaders, thinking they have “more upside.” Ramsey takes the other side: “The beauty of selling the weak link is that this market has already shown its reluctance to make new highs.”

Risk management: typically risks approximately 10 basis points per trade from entry point. Exits 90% of the time on the first day if the position isn’t working. “Might lose 10 times until the 11th works—then the big win makes up for all the small losses.” His worst real-world month without a large open profit cushion: 2–3%. Since 2008, he observes that “the dog is stocks, the tail is everything else”—post-crisis, markets are extremely correlated and you must know equity direction to trade anything else. On dedication: he wakes multiple times every night to check positions, continuing even on vacation. Some of his best trades come from looking at quotes on his iPhone at 3 a.m.—“This doesn’t look right”—and acting on the instinct. On hope: “Hope is the worst four-letter word for a trader.” A 2008 OTC fixed income trade taught him to never again trade a market where liquidity is at the whim of a dealer—it cost him 7% of what would have been a 26% year.

Jaffray Woodriff — The Third Way

Woodriff developed a third class of trading models that are neither trend-following nor countertrend—pattern recognition systems predicting probable market direction over the next 24 hours. His firm QIM runs over 1,000 models applied across approximately 150 markets using only daily open, high, low, and close data. He has never had a negative year, averaging 20%+ returns with controlled volatility.

His approach to data mining is deliberately contrarian. While academia demands hypothesis-first research, Woodriff uses “blind search through data.” His safeguard against overfitting is rigorous methodology: strict separation of training and validation data sets, random data baseline tests to establish what results chance alone would produce, and the requirement that models work broadly across many markets (not just the one they were developed on). A model that only works on soybeans but not corn is suspect; one that works across 150 markets is robust.

He credits Bill James (baseball sabermetrics pioneer) with the insight of deriving secondary variables from basic data—quantifications with no direct relationship to price direction (like volatility measures) that combine into predictive models. During the 2008–2009 volatility explosion, Woodriff maintained portfolio volatility very near his 12% annualized target by dramatically reducing contract numbers as market volatility increased—position sizing based on exponentially weighted moving average of daily dollar range per contract.

Woodriff grew up on a working farm near Charlottesville, Virginia. A summer job harvesting grapes with his sister shaped his business philosophy: paid by quantity, he and his sister earned 2–3x as much per hour as other workers who wasted time despite identical incentives. This led to QIM’s unusual fee structure: 0% management fee, 30% incentive fee (versus the typical 1–2% management plus 20–25% incentive). He wanted alignment, not guaranteed income. His early career was marked by failure: a first CTA partnership dissolved when the partner tried to be his boss, a second partnership (Blue Ridge Trading) ended in a lawsuit over informal terms, and Woodriff Trading launched in August 1994 with less than $50,000 from family, losing 16% in its first five months and another 12% in 1995. Then came a spectacular 180% gain in 1996, and he never had a negative year again.

Part II: Multistrategy Players

Edward Thorp — The Innovator

Thorp’s track record constitutes perhaps the strongest empirical refutation of the efficient market hypothesis ever produced. Princeton Newport Partners (1969–1988) returned 19.1% annualized gross with 227 winning months out of 230—a 98.7% monthly win rate. The probability of achieving this by chance: approximately 1 in 1076. His list of firsts is staggering: co-developer of the first wearable computer (for roulette, with Claude Shannon), first blackjack card-counting system (Beat the Dealer), first systematic warrant/convertible trading approach (Beat the Market), first option pricing model equivalent to Black-Scholes (used profitably years before their 1973 publication), and founder of the first market-neutral hedge fund.

His philosophy: “Remove the gambling from gambling.” He grew up during the Depression, watching his father—a WWI veteran with a Purple Heart, Silver Star, and Bronze Star—work as a security guard because it was the only job available. The experience made Thorp obsessively focused on covering the downside: “We had to be very careful with any possessions we had because it was all hard to come by.” At MIT, he met Claude Shannon—the father of information theory—by cold-calling to get his blackjack paper published. Shannon’s secretary warned he’d get five minutes at most. The meeting stretched to hours. Shannon asked what else Thorp was working on; Thorp mentioned roulette. Shannon, the “ultimate gadgeteer,” got excited. They ordered a reconditioned wheel from Vegas ($1,500), set it up on Shannon’s basement pool table, and built the first wearable computer (now in the MIT Museum). One person wore the computer with toe switches in their shoe to time the ball and rotor; the other wore a radio receiver and placed bets. They achieved a 44% edge predicting which octant the ball would land in.

When his blackjack paper was presented at the American Mathematical Society, Thorp expected 40–50 attendees for a sedate talk. Instead, the room overflowed with 300 people, many of whom “didn’t look like mathematicians—sunglasses, pinky rings.” Among the reporters was Tom Wolfe, “white suit and all.” The resulting AP wire story generated 20,000 letters to MIT’s math department. Casinos discussed “getting rid of” Thorp and “breaking knees” at a Nevada Resort Hotel Association meeting, but fortunately settled on changing the rules instead. Beat the Dealer became a bestseller that transformed blackjack into the most popular casino game—ironically benefiting casinos, since for every skilled counter there were thousands of poor players who merely thought they could win.

His transition from casinos to markets was driven by scale: “The biggest game in the world is Wall Street. Why don’t I look at and learn about that?” In convertible arbitrage, he bought convertible bonds and shorted stock to neutralize long exposure, building a model that recognized the standard assumption (bond price floor) was wrong—if stock falls enough, bond credit quality falls too. His AT&T Baby Bells trade put on 5 million shares ($330 million) against a $70 million capital base, the largest trade done on the NYSE at that time. His statistical arbitrage strategy (1992–2002) traded approximately 6 million shares per day—roughly half a percent of total NYSE volume—achieving 21% annualized return with only 7% standard deviation. The strategy required continual adaptation: the original “most up, most down” approach degraded, so he added sector neutrality, then factor neutrality via principal components analysis. Staying with the original system “because it worked so well at one time” would have fared poorly.

On the Kelly Criterion (which he learned directly from Shannon): he recommends betting half-Kelly because overestimating correct size is severely penalized, and in trading (unlike blackjack) the probabilities are always estimates. “If you bet half the Kelly amount, you get about three-quarters of the return with half the volatility.” For his trend-following system, he estimated optimal trade size was probably less than 1/10 of Kelly—so uncertain were the probabilities that he didn’t even use the criterion to size trades. His 1991 investigation of Madoff identified the fraud years before Harry Markopolos. He analyzed the claimed strategy (buy stock, buy put, sell call) and found the returns were inconsistent with market conditions. The smoking gun: half the claimed option positions appeared in issues with zero trading volume. “He is printing fake trades. He is making it all up.”

Jamie Mai — Seeking Asymmetry

Mai’s philosophy is “the pursuit of asymmetry.” Every trade at Cornwall Capital is structured to be right-skewed—maximum loss limited (option premium paid), upside open-ended. He is willing to lose small amounts repeatedly to capture occasional massive gains, and is “perfectly content to stay on the sidelines and do absolutely nothing” until a trade meets his criteria. His research process digs 50 dry wells for every trade he takes with conviction.

His defining trade: the 2006–2008 subprime bet. The opportunity arose from a cascading failure of assumptions: rating agencies used historical mortgage default data from an era of verified income and down payments to rate bonds backed by no-doc, no-down-payment loans. CDOs packaged the worst BBB tranches of these bonds and somehow received AAA ratings—“without a doubt, complete and utter insanity.” Mai bought CDS protection on AA tranches of CDOs consisting entirely of the worst-quality subprime mortgage bonds for only LIBOR + 50 basis points. By 2007, he’d paid approximately $8 million in premiums. By the end of 2008, the position was valued at approximately $80 million profit. When dealers were marking CDOs at cost despite the ABX index showing massive impairment, Mai went to the SEC to flag the systemic failure. They seemed interested but it’s unclear if they ever followed up.

His methodology identifies five situations where standard assumptions create mispricing: (1) options pricing models assume normal distributions but markets have fat tails, (2) rating agencies assume historical correlations persist but structural changes invalidate them, (3) binary outcomes are mispriced as continuous, (4) structural selling creates artificial cheapness, (5) volatility as a proxy for risk dramatically understates potential moves in trending markets. On the last point: if a smooth trend produces a large price change, implied volatility will be low despite the magnitude of the move—making options on further movement absurdly cheap. “The market always assumes symmetry, and you look for potential asymmetry.”

His best 2011 trade illustrates the flexibility Schwager emphasizes: Mai began researching dry bulk shippers as a potential long, but when the analysis showed the thesis was not just wrong but exactly backward, he immediately reversed and shorted them. The Capital One trade demonstrates his creativity: a binary outcome (acquisition happens or not) that options models failed to reflect, creating a mispriced opportunity. His Brazilian interest rate trade exploited forward rates pricing in a 400+ basis point increase that was almost certainly exaggerated, with options near the loss limit providing tremendous asymmetry.

Michael Platt — The Art and Science of Risk Control

Platt’s BlueCrest managed over $10 billion with a discretionary strategy whose worst peak-to-trough drawdown over a decade-plus track record stayed under 5%. This was achieved not through conservative positioning but through the most rigorous exposure management system in the book. His 3%/3% rule: each discretionary trader starts with a fixed allocation. Lose 3% and exposure is cut in half. Lose another 3% and the entire allocation is removed. But as traders register gains during the year, they can increase risk levels because the original reduction thresholds remain unchanged—upside is open-ended while downside is severely capped.

His most revealing insight concerns the counter-to-anticipated response. The best trades emerge when a market fails to react to news as expected. In one memorable case, a continuing stream of adverse news repeatedly made Platt expect losses, yet the market didn’t move against him. He read this inability to respond to bad news as profound confirmation of underlying strength and quadrupled his position—it became one of his biggest winners. “The reaction is more important than the news.”

Platt started trading on the floor during the 1987 crash at age 19, which permanently shaped his risk consciousness. He distinguishes sharply between volatility (price fluctuation) and risk (probability of permanent loss)—they are related but not the same. A position can be volatile but low-risk if the underlying thesis is sound. On trend-following: markets trend because of self-referential behavior (one trader’s buying triggers another’s), but the original Turtle Trader system degraded as more capital pursued the same signals. “Strategies can stop working when they become too popular.”

His hiring philosophy: he prefers former market makers over analysts because market makers understand risk viscerally. “There’s a big difference between shooting wine glasses at 20 yards and shooting a wine glass pointing a gun back at you.” He wants the type of person who gets up at 7 a.m. Sunday to pick off drunk poker players coming home from Saturday night—“someone who understands an edge.” Analysts have big egos and can never admit being wrong: “There are a lot of people that if you listen to their ideas, you can make more money off their thinking than they can.”

His three requirements for making money in any trade: (1) a decent fundamental story, (2) a good trend that looks like it will carry on, and (3) the market handling news the way you think it should. If a market stops responding to favorable news, the trade is over. He likes to know the consensus because most money is made when consensus shifts. Favorite question at dinner parties: “What is your opinion?”—people feel like you’re asking for advice and reveal their positioning. If a hedge fund manager forecasts interest rate increases, the correlation with their current position approaches 100%—“It’s amazing how much information you can get about people’s positions by simply asking them about their opinions.”

His most dramatic crisis came when the ECB unexpectedly hiked rates while he was on a flight to South Africa for the ARK charity (a feeding program reaching 60,000 children). He was massively long European interest rate futures. Upon landing: position down $70–80 million. His immediate instinct: “If they started hiking, they won’t stop at 25 basis points. I can see this trade turning into a $250 million loss by week’s end. Dump everything aggressively.” The trading psychology principle behind such instant action: “In this game, you have an option to keep 20% of your P&L this year, but you also want to own the serial option of being able to do that every year. You can’t be blowing up.” When wrong, his only instinct is to get out: “If I was thinking one way, and now I can see that it was a real mistake, then I am probably not the only person in shock, so I better be the first one to sell. I don’t care what the price is.”

Part III: Equity Traders

Steve Clark — Do More of What Works and Less of What Doesn’t

Clark grew up third generation in the same council house on the outskirts of London. Father absent, half-raised by grandparents. Left school at 17 after completing A levels a year early. A friend called: “You should come and work in the city. There are 25-year-olds earning 50,000 pounds.” He wrote to every firm—always rejected: “no degree, no interview” or “no experience, no interview.” He got temporary filing clerk jobs during a backoffice worker shortage, embroidering his CV at each position. Eventually he received two permanent offers: Merrill Lynch at £30,000 to run a backoffice, or Warburg Securities as a “blue button” (floor trainee) at £7,000. He chose the £7,000 job because it got him on the trading floor. When asked in the interview if he gambled: “I would never bet on anything where the odds were against me. Why would you do that?”—the instinctive answer that got him hired.

His first week running a market-making book happened to fall on October 19, 1987—Black Monday. Making prices on Scandinavian stocks during the crash, he quoted Electrolux at 43 bid/44 offered. A seller hit his bid for 10,000 shares—he bought 2,500. He moved his price down by half a krona, got hit again, moved down another half, got hit again. An experienced German trader looked at his trades “like I am a complete imbecile,” then quoted the stock at 36/37—a full $6 lower—and still got hit with selling. Clark had been making totally wrong prices: “The price is where anyone is prepared to deal, and it can be anything.” The German’s advice: “Your position is growing. Whatever you have to do, you need to make sure your next trade is a sell.” Clark lost several million pounds that day. The German trader was short everything and made several million.

Reading the original Market Wizards was the seminal moment: “For the first time in my life I realized that I had a method. Before, I thought I was playing a videogame, and I couldn’t believe I was getting paid to do it.” His central advice: “Do more of what works and less of what doesn’t.” Dissect your P&L and see where profits actually come from—traders often don’t know. His 1912DS disaster reinforced this lesson: a Danish shipping company trading at 140,000 kroner per share turned out to be a fraud. He’d built a large position at 140,000; it opened next day at 100,000 offered, nothing bid, and he couldn’t exit for three months. “Price is irrelevant; it is size that kills you. If you are too big in an illiquid stock, there is no way out.” After that trade, he focused almost exclusively on arbitrage—where he was making most of his money anyway.

At Lehman Brothers, after leaving Warburg (which paid him £13,000 despite being best trader), he initially couldn’t make a penny: “I had no idea how to make money outside of the Warburg environment, which was so rich in order flow information.” The breakthrough came with a gut-reaction trade on Novo (Danish company)—playing basketball, got a call that it was up $2 from European close with 100,000 shares bid. Shorted the entire amount, his biggest position ever. Bought back next morning for $100,000 profit. “From that moment, I couldn’t stop making money.” He’d found a new edge: strong relationships with other brokers replaced institutional order flow. On screen watching: “Find more productive use of your time.” During 2008, he cut exposure by a factor of four in response to surging volatility. On losing streaks: liquidate everything and take a physical vacation. The break interrupts the downward spiral. When trading resumes, keep size small until confidence returns naturally.

Martin Taylor — The Invisible Man

Taylor specializes in emerging market equities with a focus on stocks “invisible” to institutional investors—too small for large funds but excellent opportunities for patient capital. He launched with $20 million seed capital; ten years later assets had grown to over $7 billion. Then he did something almost unheard of: notified investors he was closing the fund in 12 months, coordinating its closure with the opening of a new fund that capped assets at less than one-quarter of the previous fund’s size. Named after Alexander Nevsky (thirteenth-century Russian hero), his fund delivered over 27% average annual net return from 1995–2011, more than doubling the EM index. He was the only long-only manager to avoid a loss in 1998.

His most dramatic episode was uncovering the 1998 Russian balance-of-payments fraud. Russia showed a current account surplus on paper, but reserves were declining when they should have been increasing 10–11% of GDP yearly. The mechanism: a mining company exports $100M in raw materials, reports the sale as $50M. The missing $50M goes to a private Swiss account. Of the reported $50M, $40M also goes to Switzerland. Capital flight created a massive black hole. Workers were paid in company store tokens—the company owned housing, stores, everything. Taylor took his fund to 40% cash a year before the August 1998 collapse. When Yeltsin health rumors crashed the market 23% in one session (October 1997), Taylor started selling aggressively despite a 30% bounce the next day: “When markets are trending up strongly, and there is bad news, the bad news counts for nothing. But if there is a break that reminds people what it is like to lose money, then suddenly the buying is not mindless anymore.” His internal paper was titled “The Tsar Has No Clothes.”

His portfolio philosophy is deliberately contrarian: “Buying low-beta stocks is a common mistake. If the market goes down 40% for macro reasons, they’ll go down 20%. Wouldn’t you just rather own cash?” He calls low-beta investing the “pigeon-and-elephant trade”—eat like a pigeon, shit like an elephant. To get equity-like returns from boring stocks, you must leverage up, making losses massively asymmetric. His approach: always high-beta stocks balanced by cash or shorts. Net exposure ranges from 20% long (very bearish) to 110% long, maintaining some long exposure even at maximum bearishness because getting flat means never getting back in when the market reverses. Three elements required for every stock purchase: (1) favorable macro situation, (2) secular trend, (3) good company management.

On Apple (20% of portfolio in April 2011): U.S. analysts focused on domestic market share saturation, but Taylor noted 300 million people in the U.S. versus 6.7 billion elsewhere, with China (900 million mobile subscribers) having Apple’s highest approval rating yet only 3 million iPhones sold. At the interview (Apple at $350), the stock was trading at only 4.8x his 2014 earnings forecast with $150B projected net cash (nearly half market cap). On RIMM’s PlayBook tablet: “One of the most suicidal pieces of rubbish ever put out.” At publication, Apple had risen to $601 while RIMM fell below $15.

His short-selling strategy reflects EM realities: bad companies are the greatest takeover targets because multinationals can only acquire bad ones (governments approve). Solution: half to three-quarters of shorts are stock index futures, with individual shorts limited to companies that can’t be taken over (government-owned, pension fund-owned). He rejects quantitative risk models entirely: “All quantitative risk control models use historical volatility. It is like driving by looking in the rearview mirror.” Instead, common-sense criteria: very risky positions get 1–5% of portfolio; high-confidence positions up to 20%. His 2009 trading mistake was revealing: up 32% for the year when it should have been 45–50%. Post-traumatic effects from September 2008–February 2009 altered his behavior—he developed a habit of taking quick 10–15% profits instead of letting winners run. Positions then continued up another 35–40%. He only broke the pattern after giving 12-month notice that his fund was closing, which freed him from caring about monthly performance.

Tom Claugus — A Change of Plans

Claugus was a Depression-era father’s son who internalized financial anxiety from childhood. As a college freshman, he created a 30-year financial plan: save two-thirds of income, earn 10% yearly, become a millionaire by 53. He bought his first stocks with paper route money—Wheeling Pittsburgh Steel and McCrory (a five-and-dime chain store). Both eventually went bankrupt. His father’s advice: “Tom, you are going to feel like you’re going to jail. You can go out Friday night or Saturday night, but you can’t go out both, or else you won’t get ahead.”

After Harvard Business School, he joined Rohm & Haas and rose to manager of European operations, on track for CEO. But he disciplined himself to live on one-third of his income and invest the remainder. When his net worth reached $1.6 million, he realized: “3% of $1.6 million is $48,000; I could live on that.” The economic necessity to keep working evaporated. He launched his first fund in 1990 with $3 million, but after nine months trading alone from home, isolated and uncertain, he closed it. Rohm & Haas offered him regional director of Asia, based in Hong Kong. He couldn’t bring himself to go. Walked out to put a “for sale” sign in front of his house, couldn’t do it. At the airport, got out of the boarding line, got back in, got back out. Spent five months trying to force himself to accept. Lost 45 pounds. Was diagnosed with clinical depression. The depression wasn’t from trading losses but from giving up on his dream—“in heart, really wanted to be on own, hadn’t taken it to completion.” Antidepressants helped within two weeks. He came out of the depression quickly after finally turning down Hong Kong.

His second fund launch (January 1993) produced a remarkable 17% average annual net return over 19 years. The track record is defined by its contrarian character: he lags badly when others make easy money and soars when others panic. He was net short through the entire 1990s bull market, yet still averaged 18% annual compounded returns. In 1987, he started shorting in early 1986, losing so much by summer 1987 that he had to call his mother for a margin loan. He stayed short through the crash. On October 19, as the market was crashing, he started buying longs to reduce his net short—buying Mead Paper at 32, filled at 27, the market collapsing that quickly. That was his first year making more in the market than at his job. Every year after, the same. After 2008 (down 25%), he was up 56% in 2009—his best year ever.

During the 2008 crisis, despite suffering huge outflows, Claugus refused to put up “gates” (restrictions on investor redemptions). His reasoning: “There’s a big difference between their making their own decision to stay versus him telling them what’s in their best interest.” If he loses their money after preventing redemptions, that feels qualitatively different from losing money for investors who chose to remain. This ethical framework around investor relations distinguishes him from many peers who gated during the crisis.

Joe Vidich — Harvesting Losses

Vidich’s edge is finding “free optionality”—companies with future revenue generation potential not reflected in current market price. Markets routinely fail to value revenue opportunities more than one year away. His oil company examples illustrate: Petrominerales produces 40,000 barrels/day from Colombia but has 600,000 leased acres with active drilling—the market ignores additional production. Canadian Natural Resources had a new oil sands project with $1 billion/year cash flow potential that the market assigned zero value. Paramount Resources acquires land with exploration potential and leases it—its true value never appears in cash flow metrics. “The general principle is that we look for future revenue generation that can be reasonably anticipated but that is not reflected by the current market price. This idea is probably the single most important concept in our stock selection process.”

His United Airlines position exemplified contrarian thinking: one of his largest longs despite Buffett’s famous aversion to airlines. (Buffett himself jokes about having a full-time employee whose sole job is preventing him from buying airlines. His son’s question: “How do you become a millionaire?” Answer: “Start with a billion and buy an airline.”) Vidich’s reasoning: the industry had consolidated to three major players, cost structures were finally competitive post-bankruptcy, and United’s route structure (50% U.S.–Asia, 40% U.S.–Europe) was superior. At $20/share with 10% operating margins: $6 earnings, P/E of only 3.3. Even with an 8–10x multiple, potential to double or triple. His exposure management uses a statistical band framework: net exposure varies between 70% short and 110% long based on price placement within a long-term regression line from 1932 with 95% confidence intervals.

Vidich emphasizes process over outcome: a losing trade can reflect a correct decision if the process has a positive edge, and a winning trade can be a poor decision if the process has a negative edge. Consider a 2:1 odds fair coin toss—taking the bet and losing is still the correct decision. A winning slot machine wager is still a bad bet. “Good vs. bad trades should be judged by the quality of the decision, not the result.” Instead of all-or-nothing, he advocates liquidating part of a position rather than the whole thing—much easier psychologically, avoids riding the entire position for a large loss while preserving partial recovery potential.

Kevin Daly — Who Is Warren Buffett?

The chapter title comes from Daly’s job interview at Hoefer & Arnett in 1983, when someone asked “Who is Warren Buffett?” and he couldn’t answer. He went home, read everything Buffett had written, and the question shaped his career-long value investing methodology. He launched Five Corners Partners in 1999 with $3 million (one-third his own, the rest from Chuck Royce and friends/family). Performance from 1999–2011: 20.8% annual gross return with only 10.3% maximum drawdown despite two devastating bear markets. Cumulative gross return: 872% versus the Russell 2000’s 68% and the S&P 500’s −9%. His Gain to Pain ratio of 3.2 is very high. He runs the fund solo from home and refuses to expand beyond a one-man operation, maintaining roughly one-third of invested assets himself.

The process: quantitative screening of ~10,000 stocks for cheapness (low EV/EBITDA, P/FCF, P/E) and business quality (high return on tangible capital), narrowing to ~200 candidates, then qualitative assessment for understandable business models, recession durability, and acquisition potential. His notable investments reveal the pattern. Newport Corporation (1999): known for semiconductor test equipment, with a hidden division producing fiber optic components. Bought at $14, trading at 11x. Took profits in the 30s thinking it had reached intrinsic value around $100. Stock eventually topped at $570—“I failed to appreciate that in a market where Internet stocks with little or even no earnings could magically levitate, there was no telling how high a ‘real company’ like Newport could go.” Measurement Specialties (late 2008): the CFO told him “The phone didn’t ring for about a month” when auto sales plummeted. “Unless I assumed no one was going to buy a car again, I knew this situation had to change.” Bought at $3.79, sold most in the $14s. Stock peaked at $38.98.

His timing was decisive. He went roughly 90% cash at the end of Q1 2000 (right at the bear market start), staying mostly in cash probably until early 2003. During 2008, he reduced exposure to 60–70% cash. “Superior stock selection alone isn’t enough—being primarily in cash during negative environments is the critical factor.” He won’t invest in “melting ice cube” businesses (newspapers, yellow pages, video rentals) no matter how cheap. His Horizon Lines investment taught the danger of overleveraged companies: Jones Act container shipping with significant barriers to entry, but debt load could sink the company. He exited above average cost. “The stock today trades at 24 cents per share!” His small fund size ($50M) is a deliberate advantage: best opportunities are in smaller-cap issues that would be too small if managing hundreds of millions.

“I’d rather miss an opportunity than lose money.” This patience extended to remaining largely in cash for well over two years during 2000–2002. Claude Debussy wrote that “music is the space between the notes”—analogously, the space between investments can be critical. The discipline to do nothing when the opportunity set is poor is what preserved capital for deployment when valuations became compelling.

Jimmy Balodimas — Catching Turning Points

Balodimas was influenced by the movie Wall Street—attracted to the action and energy, not just the money. His parents were immigrants who worked very hard. At Williams College, a one-month trading course taught by Simon from Bear Stearns sparked his career. Don Ehrenberg hired him at First New York Securities. His mother later told Ehrenberg: “I don’t know what you did to your son, but I never had anybody work so hard.” Year 1: $350K. Year 2: $800K. Year 3: $1M+. Never looked back. “What did you do to be successful from the start?”—“I was totally focused on trading. Nothing else mattered. Not my health; not my relationships.”

He is the book’s most intuitive trader—a short-biased equity specialist who spends 75% of his time short, with an uncanny sense for market turning points. He never uses fundamentals (had fundamental analysts for three years, found it a distraction from mastering discipline) and makes hundreds of orders daily. Volume is his key signal. On Goodyear: other auto parts stocks broke out while Goodyear lagged. Two weeks before entry, the day before earnings showed huge volume with price picking up—“I think this is our spot.” On a cotton ETF short: lost $200K in two days, then on the high day added 22,000 shares—“This is a squeeze. I don’t care what’s going on in Egypt. The stock has doubled. I should take my shot here.” Down 12% the next day. Net profit: $100K. On Chipotle: got caught short the whole way up from $140 to $270 in three months. “Really? I don’t care how good the tacos are.” Doubled up near the high, recovered about three-quarters of the loss on the retracement.

His silver trade in May 2011 captures his style. He watched silver for six months as it nearly tripled, growing convinced the rally was unsustainable. He shorted the silver ETF as the price went vertical. Within three days he was down $4 million. The market then sold off to approximately his average price and he covered the entire position—breaking even. Silver subsequently fell from $49 to $32 in its biggest selloff in over 30 years. He captured the thesis but not the profit, a pattern Schwager identifies as a significant weakness.

His most important evolution came from losing $7 million in one month shorting homebuilders. “Losing the homebuilders money was the biggest blessing—it led to looking at life differently.” Schwager challenges him directly: he’s skilled at trading around positions, often making money even when the longer-term trend is against him, but could do far better by combining his position-trading skill with his original directional calls after the market confirms the turn. “It seems like you are currently trading with two hands tied behind your back.” The $50 million opportunities in his career made only $3 million—a few weeks later would have been $10 million, a few months $25 million. The paradox: he’s “comfortable being uncomfortable when everyone else is panicking” but Schwager notes, “That’s ironic because what you consider comfortable—selling into a near vertical rally—is exactly what most people would find highly uncomfortable.” He never panics during flash crashes—first question is always “Is the data wrong?”—and “I’m not trying to prove I’m right. I’m not trying to prove the market is stupid. I just like making money.”

Joel Greenblatt — The Magic Formula

Greenblatt started investing after reading Ben Graham, buying net-nets before law school. His master’s thesis with Richard Pzena and Bruce Newberg tested Graham’s decades-old formula using the University of Pennsylvania’s DEC10 computer—it still worked extremely well. Published in the Journal of Portfolio Management (summer 1981). He started a fund with $250K from his father’s friends, returning 44% cumulatively over two to three years. At Halcyon Investments, he joined as the only analyst at $22K (half the MBA rate)—a startup risk arbitrage firm where a pedestrian year could return 60–80% in the “Wild West” era of merger inefficiencies.

He was never attracted to plain vanilla risk arbitrage: “Make $1–2 if merger succeeds, risk $10–20 if it breaks”—the exact opposite of Ben Graham’s asymmetric philosophy. Instead, he gravitated to special situations. The Marriott spinoff exemplifies his approach: Marriott spun off its hotels and debt into Host Marriott (the “toxic waste”), keeping the good management business as Marriott International. Host was heavily leveraged, out-of-favor, unwanted. But insiders retained 25% ownership—suggesting the deal wouldn’t be structured to fail. Expected to trade at $3–5 with $20–25 of debt: a 15% increase in asset value would nearly double the stock. It tripled in four months. His Wells Fargo LEAPS trade transformed a 1:1 risk/reward into 1:5 by buying long-term options instead of stock. If the bank survived (which he assessed at much better than 50/50), the stock should double—yielding 5x on options; if it failed, he’d only lose the premium. “If there was a 400-page document to read, I was attracted because I knew most other people wouldn’t read it.”

The Magic Formula emerged as he transitioned from concentrated to systematic value investing. Two factors, equally weighted: (1) earnings yield (EBIT/enterprise value)—superior to P/E because it accounts for debt and allows comparison across different capital structures; and (2) return on tangible capital (EBIT/[net working capital + net fixed assets]). Not the cheapest stocks. Not the best businesses. The best combination of cheap and good. His Jason’s Gum Shop analogy: $400K to set up, $200K profit = 50% return. Just Broccoli: $400K to set up, $10K profit = 2.5%. Same investment, radically different returns on capital. Backtest over 23 years: top 30 names from the 1,000 largest stocks approximately doubled the S&P 500 (19.7% vs. 9.5% annually), with perfect decile ordering from 1 to 10. During the “lost decade” (2000–2009), the formula averaged 13.5% while the S&P was down nearly 1% annually.

His three critical lessons: (1) value investing works, (2) value investing doesn’t work all the time, and (3) item 2 is one of the reasons item 1 is true. He calls this time arbitrage: institutional time horizons have shortened so dramatically that 97% of top-quartile managers spent at least 3 years in the bottom half, 79% spent at least 3 years in the bottom quartile, and nearly 50% spent at least 3 years in the bottom decile. Those were the managers that ended up with the best performance. The best-performing mutual fund of the decade returned 18% annually, but the average investor in that fund lost 8%—because of timing of flows. “People don’t fully appreciate the importance of not losing money. Negative compounding is very difficult to overcome. If you lose 50%, you have to make 100% to recover.”

His magicformulainvesting.com experiment revealed a behavioral paradox. Under 10% of investors chose to manage themselves; over 90% checked the box for automatic management. The self-managed investors underperformed by over 25% in the first two years—same principles, same stock list, but they reduced exposure when the market fell, sold underperformers, and missed the biggest winners. His worst personal mistake: Key3Media (Ziff-Davis spinoff), a trade show company running COMDEX that rented Vegas space at $2/sq ft and re-rented at $62. Bought at $3, it quadrupled, grew to ~40% of his portfolio. Then the company made a large acquisition two days before 9/11. After 9/11, people stopped traveling. Operating leverage reversed. He lost back all profits “and then some.” “Don’t fall in love with any position. Always keep a large margin of safety, even if you’re playing with house money.”

He also pioneered fundamentally weighted indexes: cap-weighted indexes are inherently inefficient because a higher price automatically means a larger index weight—you own too much of overpriced stocks. Equal weighting beat cap weighting by ~2%/year over 40 years. Greenblatt’s index, allocating more to cheaper stocks, beat the S&P 500 by 7%/year over 20 years with the same beta. Beyond investing, he co-founded Harlem Success charter schools with Eva Moskowitz. All four tested schools (all in Harlem) beat Scarsdale, Great Neck, and every top district in New York State—same kids, same parents, less money per student. Admission by lottery eliminated selection bias. K–12 education is a $600 billion business: “Philanthropy can show the way, capitalism must scale.” The Value Investors Club he founded (1999, limited to 250 members) was inspired by finding a Yahoo message board post with precisely the same analysis as one of their best ideas: “Well, apparently, there is intelligent life out there.”

40 Market Wizard Lessons

Schwager’s conclusion distills the interviews into 40 lessons—the most comprehensive set of trading principles in the Market Wizards series:

  1. There is no holy grail. The multitude of successful approaches, some polar opposites, proves no single solution exists.
  2. Find a methodology that fits your personality. A sound methodology for one trader can be a losing strategy for another who doesn’t have the right temperament for it. O’Shea: “If I try to teach you what I do, you will fail because you are not me.”
  3. Trade within your comfort zone. If the position is too large, fear dominates decision-making. Smaller net exposure may yield better returns even if the market moves favorably.
  4. Flexibility is essential. The best traders not only liquidate losing positions but will reverse them entirely. O’Shea’s April 2009 reversal turned a potential disaster into a winning year.
  5. The need to adapt. Markets change, strategies deteriorate. Thorp maintained returns through three iterations of statistical arbitrage; the original system degraded. Platt: “Systems that don’t change will eventually degrade—system trading is a research war.”
  6. Don’t confuse winning/losing trades with good/bad trades. A good trade can lose money; a bad trade can make money. Even the best processes lose a certain percentage. A trade adhering to a positive-edge process is good regardless of outcome.
  7. Do more of what works and less of what doesn’t. Clark’s core advice. Many traders degrade performance by engaging in trades outside their edge. Dissect your P&L to identify where profits actually come from.
  8. If out of sync, trying harder won’t help. When trading is going badly, trying harder often makes it worse. Clark: liquidate everything and take a vacation. The break interrupts the downward spiral.
  9. The road to success is paved with mistakes. Dalio: write down each mistake, the implied lesson, and the intended change. Repeating the same mistakes is often the difference between success and failure.
  10. Wait for high-conviction trades. Patience greatly enhances return/risk. Mai does absolutely nothing until a trade meets every criterion. Greenblatt: suboptimal positions may tie up capital needed for future opportunities.
  11. Trade because of opportunity, not desire to make money. Benedict’s marginal trades at the end of 2010 to reach a profit target resulted in net losses. Trading to hit a number is counterproductive.
  12. The importance of doing nothing. Daly achieved 800%+ cumulative returns partly by staying in cash during bear markets. Being out of the market can be nearly as important as the investments you make.
  13. Implementation can be more important than the trade idea. O’Shea traded the Nasdaq bubble not by shorting tech (treacherous 40% bear market rally) but by buying bonds (smooth uptrend as flight to safety).
  14. Trading around a position. Dynamic, not static. Reduce exposure on favorable moves, reinstate on corrections. Generates extra profit and increases the chance of staying with good trades longer. Balodimas: critical ingredient that allows profitability even on wrong-direction trades.
  15. Position size is more important than entry price. Trading too large causes good trades to be liquidated from fear. Trading larger for higher-probability trades can transform a losing strategy into a winning one. Thorp: varying bet size turned negative blackjack edge into positive.
  16. Determining trade size. Kelly criterion: mathematically optimal but assumes precisely known probabilities. Thorp: trade only half Kelly because the penalty for overestimating is severe and most would find full Kelly volatility too high.
  17. Vary exposure based on opportunities. Claugus: varies net exposure from 110% long to 70% short based on whether stocks appear cheap or expensive relative to his statistical framework.
  18. Seek asymmetric return/risk. Mai and O’Shea structure trades with limited loss but open-ended upside through options and CDS. Platt achieves asymmetry at portfolio level through tight loss limits with open-ended upside.
  19. Beware of euphoria-borne trades. Excessive euphoria should be a cautionary flag of potential reversal, not a reason to chase.
  20. If on the right side of euphoria/panic, lighten up. Parabolic moves end abruptly and sharply. If you are petrified to be on the other side, you should probably be lightening your position.
  21. Staring at the screen all day can be expensive. Clark: watching every tick leads to overtrading and increased chance of liquidating good positions prematurely. Find more productive use of your time.
  22. Risk control is critical. Ramsey: 0.1% per trade. Benedict: 2% monthly max. Platt: 3% before halving, 6% before removal. Woodriff and Clark: both cut exposure ~4x in response to 2008 volatility.
  23. Don’t try to be 100% right. Vidich: instead of all-or-nothing, liquidate part of a position. Much easier psychologically than liquidating entirely, avoids riding the whole position for a large loss while preserving partial recovery potential.
  24. Protective stops must be consistent with trade analysis. Stops set by pain threshold rather than points that disprove the thesis lead to repeated reentries with cumulative losses exceeding a single wider stop.
  25. Know your maximum tolerable loss before entering. If that loss is too large for comfort, reduce position size rather than tightening the stop to a meaningless level.
  26. The power of diversification. Dalio’s “Holy Grail of investing”: truly uncorrelated assets could improve return/risk by a factor of 5:1. But the key is diversifying by underlying driver, not by asset class name.
  27. Correlation can be misleading. It measures past relationships only. Stocks and bonds sometimes move together, sometimes inversely. Using correlation during a transition period can lead to exactly wrong conclusions.
  28. Watch for divergences between correlated markets. When one market fails to follow a normally correlated market, it reveals inherent strength or weakness. Divergences between related markets can be important signals.
  29. Markets behave differently in different environments. Dalio: the same conditions and government actions have different consequences in a deleveraging versus a recession. Historical analogies can be misleading if the regime is different.
  30. Pay attention to how markets respond to news. Counter-to-anticipated response is more meaningful than the news itself. Platt quadrupled his position after adverse news repeatedly failed to move the market against him.
  31. Major events may be followed by counterintuitive moves. Dalio: gold standard abandonment (1971) and Mexican default (1982) were both followed by rallies. Events may be overdiscounted, or the government response overwhelms the catalyst.
  32. Binary outcomes create excellent option buying opportunities. Options pricing models assume large moves are unlikely. When fundamentals suggest a binary outcome, options become mispriced. Greenblatt’s Wells Fargo LEAPS, Mai’s Capital One calls.
  33. A stock can be well-priced after a large up-move. What matters is price relative to future prospects, not past movement. Taylor’s Apple had already surged but was still cheap on his earnings projections.
  34. Don’t make decisions based on entry price. The market doesn’t care where you entered. Evaluate based on current outlook, not sunk cost.
  35. Future revenue may not be reflected in price. Claugus: companies with new revenue streams 1+ years away are frequently not adequately discounted. The market’s time horizon is shorter than most investors realize.
  36. Value investing works. But it has periods of poor performance, which is precisely why it maintains its edge. If it worked all the time, enough followers would eliminate the edge.
  37. EMH is an inaccurate model. Prices sometimes deviate enormously from fundamental value. Graham: the market is like an erratic business partner, sometimes willing to sell absurdly low, sometimes buy ridiculously high.
  38. Don’t alter your process for investor demands. Greenblatt: “You are setting yourself up for failure if you invest differently than you want to in order to please investors.” Taylor: “I’m trying to stop caring about what my clients think.”
  39. Volatility and risk are not synonymous. Low volatility doesn’t mean low risk (selling OTM options: low vol until catastrophic loss). High volatility doesn’t mean high risk (Mai’s occasional large gains with curtailed downside).
  40. Do not select managers based on past performance. No meaningful correlation between past and future rankings. Best performers during bubbles are often the most imprudent, not the most skilled. Choose based on investment process.

Epilogue

Schwager reflects on how the interviews solidified his own trading principles. He shares a personal example: positioned short stock index futures, expecting a rally to fail. After an extremely bearish employment report, the market initially sold off sharply but recovered the entire loss by end of week. He prepared to cover most of his position when the market opened Sunday night. It opened lower—and Schwager immediately thought of Marty Schwartz’s advice from the original Market Wizards: “If you’re very nervous about a position overnight and you’re able to get out at a much better price than you thought, you’re usually better off staying with the position.” He stayed short and saved considerable money as the market moved sharply lower in the ensuing weeks. Each trader must draw personal insights depending on their own style. The Balodimas interview influenced Schwager to trade more around positions (dynamic vs. static), and he found that some trades that went nowhere ended up generating net profits through dynamic management.

Son Zachary Schwager contributes a closing reflection. At age 8 on a “bring your child to work” day, his father tested him by cycling through charts and asking him to guess the next move. Five charts in, the elder Schwager concluded Zachary was “not one of those naturally gifted individuals.” During junior year of high school, Zachary visited John Bender (a retired Market Wizard) at his Costa Rica rain forest reserve—thousands of acres patrolled by armed guards against poaching. Bender talked for hours, ranging from quantum physics paradoxes to a particular disgust for one fund manager he insisted was a fraud. Zachary wouldn’t hear that name again for nine years: Bernie Madoff.

Zachary went to film school in New York. His father gave him a $500 Ameritrade account; two years later it was over $2,000. He liquidated and bought a film camera. Then he attended his father’s trading expo talk—pulling the only collared shirt from his closet while his mother said, “You’re such a handsome boy, but nobody would ever know by the way you are dressed.” He “didn’t know that moment would change the rest of my life.” Three insights resonated: (1) trading isn’t reserved for an elite—traders came from every background, with no correlation between success and school or occupation; (2) trading is both art and science, built on discovering what others overlook; (3) there is no single right way, and trying to replicate someone else’s method almost always fails. Zachary dropped out of film school, earned a finance degree, and became a junior trader at First New York Securities. His final tribute: “It would be far easier to be as successful as my dad in trading than to be as successful as he is on a human level.”

Key Takeaways

  1. There is no holy grail. Fifteen traders, fifteen radically different approaches—from Dalio’s systematic macro to Balodimas’s intuitive tape reading. Some are polar opposites yet all succeed. The diversity proves that methodology must fit the individual, not the other way around.
  2. Risk management is the one universal. Despite wildly different styles, every trader is obsessed with controlling losses. Benedict clears out at 2% monthly drawdown, Platt cuts traders at 3%, Ramsey risks 0.1% per trade. Capital preservation is the non-negotiable foundation of every career.
  3. Flexibility beats conviction. The best traders hold strong views loosely. O’Shea reversed from bearish to bullish in April 2009, saving his year. Mai researched dry bulk shippers as a long, discovered the thesis was backwards, and shorted them instead. Ego kills returns.
  4. Return alone is meaningless—return/risk is everything. O’Shea’s 11.3% annual return looks modest until you realize he ran at half the volatility of peers. Scaled to equal risk, his returns would be ~23%. Fixating on returns without considering risk is the most common investor mistake.
  5. Asymmetry is the edge. Mai structures every trade with limited downside and open-ended upside (options, CDS). O’Shea participates in bubbles via long options, then shorts after the turn. Greenblatt used Wells Fargo LEAPS to transform 1:1 risk/reward into 1:5.
  6. Doing nothing is a strategy. Daly sat in 90% cash for over two years during the 2000–2002 bear market, achieving 800%+ cumulative returns by avoiding drawdowns. Mai is “perfectly content to stay on the sidelines and do absolutely nothing” until a trade meets his criteria.
  7. Implementation matters as much as the idea. O’Shea traded the Nasdaq bubble not by shorting tech stocks (treacherous rallies) but by buying bonds (beneficiary of the eventual flight to safety). Same thesis, radically different implementation, radically different result.
  8. Counter-to-anticipated response reveals the truth. When a market fails to drop on terrible news, it’s telling you something the news can’t. Platt quadrupled his position after adverse news repeatedly failed to move the market against him. Price action trumps narrative.
  9. Correlation is not causation—focus on drivers. Dalio rejects backward-looking correlation metrics in favor of understanding the underlying drivers that determine price behavior. During regime changes (recessions vs. deleveragings), historical correlations break down completely.
  10. Persistence is prerequisite. Benedict blew up three accounts before 25. Thorp was rejected by every casino. Claugus called his mother for a margin loan. Greenblatt started with concentrated value picks that the market ignored for years. Every wizard has a story of surviving early failure.
  11. The efficient market hypothesis is demonstrably wrong. Thorp’s 227 winning months out of 230 (probability by chance: 1 in 1076) and Greenblatt’s Magic Formula doubling the S&P over 23 years provide empirical proof that markets can be beaten systematically.
  12. Value investing works because it doesn’t always work. Greenblatt’s three critical lessons: (1) value investing works, (2) value investing doesn’t work all the time, and (3) item 2 is one of the reasons item 1 is true. The pain of underperformance is what preserves the edge.
  13. Select managers by process, not past returns. Greenblatt cites studies showing no meaningful correlation between past and future manager performance rankings. Choose based on investment process, not the rearview mirror.
  14. Markets are driven by human nature, which never changes. Seykota’s foreword: trading wisdom endures because human nature is constant. Schwager began the series in 1988; the lessons are as relevant in 2012 as they were then, and will be in 2050.

Contrarian Ideas

  1. Stop losses set by pain threshold are worse than no stop losses. O’Shea argues that most traders set stops based on how much they can afford to lose, not at points that disprove their thesis. The result: multiple small losses that cumulatively exceed what a single properly-placed stop would have cost.
  2. Hedge funds are more conservative than mutual funds. Despite their “speculative” reputation, over 10–15 year periods hedge funds deliver modestly higher returns with far lower volatility and drawdowns than traditional equity mutual funds.
  3. Data mining can work—if done right. Woodriff uses “blind search through data” contrary to the academic requirement of hypothesis-first research. His safeguard: rigorous train/validate splits, random data baselines, and insistence that models work across 150+ markets (not just one).
  4. You should participate in bubbles. O’Shea buys options during bubbles (limited downside, unlimited upside), rides the mania up, then waits to sell only after the market has already turned. “You want to be selling Nasdaq at 4,000, but only after it has gone to 5,000.”
  5. Knowing your forecast is right doesn’t help if you’re early. Claugus shorted stocks throughout the 1990s bull market and was eventually right in 2000—but only after years of pain and a margin call funded by his mother. Being right and being early look identical in real time.
  6. More information doesn’t produce better decisions. O’Shea insists fundamentals aren’t about forecasting tomorrow’s weather but noticing it’s raining today. Most traders drown in analysis while missing what the market is already telling them through price action.
  7. Your biggest winner should scare you. Mai’s subprime CDS trade went from $8M premium to $80M profit. Balodimas was down $4M on silver before breaking even. The best trades require enduring periods of looking spectacularly wrong.
  8. Radical transparency actually works. Dalio records every meeting at Bridgewater and encourages junior employees to challenge senior ones. Most companies call this chaos; Bridgewater manages $120B and has one of the best track records in the industry.

Trading Frameworks

  1. Hypothesis-based trading (O’Shea). Treat every position as a scientific hypothesis. Define in advance what market action would disprove your thesis. When invalidated, exit immediately—no deliberation, no hoping. Position sizes are calibrated so invalidation losses stay small.
  2. Driver-based diversification (Dalio). Don’t diversify by asset class or historical correlation. Identify the underlying economic drivers (growth, inflation, risk premiums) and build portfolios balanced across those drivers. Can improve return/risk by 5:1 with 15+ uncorrelated return streams.
  3. The 3%/3% risk budget (Platt). Each discretionary trader at BlueCrest gets a fixed allocation. Lose 3% of starting allocation and exposure is cut in half. Lose another 3% and allocation is removed entirely. But gains extend the runway—upside is open-ended while downside is capped.
  4. The Magic Formula (Greenblatt). Rank all stocks by (1) earnings yield (EBIT/EV) and (2) return on tangible capital (EBIT/[NWC + net fixed assets]). Buy the top 30. Not the cheapest stocks. Not the best businesses. The best combination of cheap and good. Doubled the S&P over 23 years.
  5. Asymmetric structuring (Mai). Only take trades where maximum loss is defined and small relative to potential gain. Use options, CDS, or structural features to create right-skewed payoffs. Willing to lose premium repeatedly for the one trade that pays 10:1.
  6. The third class of models (Woodriff). Neither trend-following nor mean-reversion—pattern recognition predicting next-24-hour direction. Over 1,000 models applied across 150 markets using only daily OHLC data. Blind data search validated by train/test splits and random baselines.
  7. Correlation trading (Benedict). Watch how markets move relative to each other, not in isolation. Trade S&P futures against other liquid futures, exploiting temporary dislocations in cross-market relationships. Hundreds of transactions daily, duration seconds to two days.
  8. Sentiment-based exposure (Vidich). Vary net exposure between 70% short and 110% long based on price relative to a long-term statistical band (regression from 1932 with 95% confidence interval). Don’t fight the macro environment.

Appendices

Appendix A: The Gain to Pain Ratio. Return alone is meaningless—you can always get higher return by adding leverage. The GPR captures return relative to the pain required to achieve it: sum of all monthly returns divided by the absolute value of the sum of all monthly losses. A GPR of 1.0 means average monthly losses equal net gains. Above 1.0 is very good for liquid strategies; above 1.5 is excellent. Unlike the Sharpe ratio, the GPR does not penalize upside volatility (which is beneficial). It is similar to the “profit factor” (sum of profitable trades / sum of losing trades) but applied to monthly returns; algebraically, profit factor = GPR + 1.

Appendix B: Options Basics. A call option grants the right (not obligation) to buy at a strike price; a put grants the right to sell. The buyer’s risk is limited to the premium paid, while the seller’s risk is theoretically unlimited. Option price = intrinsic value + time value. Time value is influenced by the relationship between strike and market price, time remaining, and volatility. Implied volatility (estimated from option premiums) tends on average to exceed subsequent realized volatility—the extra premium is necessary to induce sellers to accept open-ended risk, analogous to insurance premium margins. This systematic overpricing of options is one reason option-selling strategies appear profitable—until they aren’t.
