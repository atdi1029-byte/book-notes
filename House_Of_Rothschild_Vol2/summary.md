# The House of Rothschild: The World's Banker, 1849-1999
## By Niall Ferguson

### Summary

Volume 2 of Ferguson's definitive Rothschild history traces the family's trajectory from the apex of financial power through relative decline and near-destruction, to eventual reinvention. Beginning where the 1848 revolutions left off, Ferguson argues that the Rothschilds' story after mid-century is not one of simple decline but of adaptation — from a monopolistic multinational banking partnership to a collection of nationally-rooted firms that survived world wars, confiscation, and the rise of joint-stock banking.

### Key Themes

**1. Relative Decline, Absolute Resilience**
The Rothschilds remained the largest private bank in the City of London through 1914, with combined capital peaking at £41.5 million (1899). But their share of the bond market shrank as joint-stock banks, deposit-taking institutions, and rivals like J.P. Morgan grew faster. Profits as a percentage of capital fell from 9.8% (1870s) to 3.9% (1900s). The family's deliberate conservatism — "never strive for excessive profits" — preserved capital but ceded market leadership.

**2. The Fracturing of the Multinational**
The five-house structure (London, Paris, Frankfurt, Vienna, Naples) that made the Rothschilds unique progressively disintegrated. Naples closed in 1863, Frankfurt wound up in 1901, and the multinational partnership was formally dissolved between 1905-1909. National rivalries, especially the Franco-Prussian War's aftermath, made cross-border cooperation increasingly fraught. By the early 1900s, London-Paris was essentially the only axis that mattered.

**3. Generational Transition — The Buddenbrooks Question**
Ferguson tests the Thomas Mann thesis of generational decline. The fourth generation (Natty, Alphonse, Albert) shifted from business obsession to country houses, art collecting, horse racing, and politics. Yet Ferguson argues the decline narrative is overstated — Natty, Alphonse, and Albert remained effective bankers even as their siblings pursued aestheticism. The real problem was structural, not personal.

**4. The Jewish Question**
The Rothschilds navigated rising anti-Semitism while serving as de facto leaders of European Jewry. Natty was president of the United Synagogue (1879-1915) and temporal chief of British Jews. The family responded with philanthropy (industrial dwellings, hospitals, schools) and political engagement, but rejected Herzl's Zionism as threatening their hard-won national identities. Edmond de Rothschild's Palestine settlements were colonization without statehood — he spent £5.6 million but explicitly opposed a Jewish state.

**5. Imperial Finance**
The London house advanced £4 million for Disraeli's Suez Canal purchase (1875) — the iconic transaction of Rothschild imperial finance. They dominated foreign government bond issuance (over 25% of all London foreign issues, 1865-1914) and built a mining empire spanning Rio Tinto copper, De Beers diamonds, Burma rubies, New Caledonian nickel, and Baku oil. Yet imperial issues were only 6% of their business — they preferred independent states whose bonds didn't depend on British political control.

**6. War and Catastrophe**
WWI shifted financial power to New York and J.P. Morgan. The interwar period saw the Creditanstalt crisis (1931) destroy the Vienna house, while Nazi confiscation wiped out the German and Austrian branches entirely. Louis de Rothschild was arrested in Vienna after the Anschluss; the Rosenberg task force looted 3,978 art items from Rothschild collections. Family members fought in both wars; some were killed in the Holocaust.

**7. Post-War Reinvention**
The London house shrank dramatically (capital from £8 million to £1.5 million) but survived through privatisation advisory work — Amersham International (1982), British Gas (1986), and eventually hundreds of privatisations worldwide. The French house was nationalised by Mitterrand in 1981 ("A Jew under Petain, a pariah under Mitterrand") but reestablished within three years. By the 1990s, the family had recreated a multinational structure through Rothschilds Continuation Holdings, with nineteen firms across twenty-plus countries.

### Why the Rothschilds Survived

Ferguson's central argument: the multinational family structure — the very thing that caused so much internal friction — was also what saved them. A crisis in one house could be contained with help from others. Low profitability reflected high capital ratios, which meant safety. The family firm's longer time horizon, considering future generations alongside present partners, gave them an advantage no joint-stock corporation could replicate. Their survival for over two centuries, through every major war and financial crisis, stands as one of the most remarkable stories in business history.
