Preface

The first volume ended with the revolutions of 1848 nearly destroying the Rothschild empire. Bond prices collapsed, the Vienna and Paris houses teetered on insolvency, and only bail-outs from London and Frankfurt preserved the multinational partnership. Yet the family survived what was the greatest financial crisis between 1815 and 1914. This second volume picks up the story at that moment of supreme vulnerability and traces it forward through 150 years of adaptation, relative decline, catastrophe, and reinvention.

Ferguson recaps the extraordinary rise from the Frankfurt Judengasse — the ghetto whose walls Napoleon literally demolished in 1796. Mayer Amschel Rothschild seized the chaos of revolutionary Europe to become fund manager for the exiled Elector of Hesse-Kassel, while his son Nathan moved from Manchester textiles to London finance. Nathan’s account with the British government totalled close to £10 million in 1815 alone. His supreme Meistergeschäft after Waterloo — buying British consols and riding the postwar stabilisation — realised more than £250,000 at a stroke.

“Keep your brothers together and you will become the richest people in Germany.” — Mayer Amschel on his 1812 deathbed

The multinational structure — five houses in London, Paris, Frankfurt, Vienna, and Naples — was the family’s greatest asset. It enabled arbitrage between markets, mutual bail-outs during crises, and by 1825 made them ten times the size of their nearest rival, Baring Brothers. The Rothschilds created the first truly international bond market, issuing Prussian bonds simultaneously in multiple cities with sterling denomination and sinking funds. They were, as Heinrich Heine put it, the prophets of money: “Money is the god of our time and Rothschild is his prophet.”

But the post-1848 world posed new threats. Bismarck, Cavour, and Palmerston elevated national self-interest above international balance. The Pereire brothers’ Crédit Mobilier broke the bond-market monopoly. The telegraph ended the Rothschilds’ courier advantage. Charlotte de Rothschild affirmed her belief “in a bright, European and Rothschildian future” — but nationalism and socialism would prove the greatest challenges, especially when combined.

The Rothschilds’ bond-market dominance rested on a simple insight: governments in distress will pay handsomely for capital. Mayer Amschel’s business rule — “It is better to deal with a government in difficulties than with one that has luck on its side” — guided every generation. They bankrolled the restoration of deposed monarchs after 1815, enabling Austria, Prussia, Russia, and the Bourbons to issue bonds at rates only Britain and Holland had previously enjoyed. Ludwig Borne complained that Rothschild was “the high priest of fear,” but the family saw themselves as enforcers of peace. Their leverage was simple: “no peace, no cash.”

Endogamy preserved both capital and cohesion. Of twenty-one marriages between 1824 and 1877, fifteen were between direct descendants of Mayer Amschel. This kept partnership shares within the family and created an almost royal sense of dynasty. Heine called them “the exceptional family” — other Jews regarded them as “the Kings of the Jews” and “the Jews of the Kings.” The social ascent was spectacular: “Plain Jacob Rothschild became Monsieur le Baron James de Rothschild,” filled his houses with Dutch paintings and French furniture, and privately viewed honours with cynicism — they were “part of the racket,” useful for access to power.

This volume covers 150 years of dramatic transformation: from the apex of bond-market dominance through the Franco-Prussian War, the Barings crisis, two world wars, Nazi confiscation, nationalisation, and eventual reinvention as a modern advisory bank. Ferguson draws on unprecedented access to the Rothschild archives — private letters, partnership agreements, profit-and-loss accounts — to tell a story that demolishes conspiracy theories while revealing something more interesting: a family that was genuinely powerful but also frequently wrong, occasionally desperate, and ultimately constrained by forces that no amount of money could control.

The central argument is that the multinational family structure — the very thing that caused so much internal friction and eventually broke apart — was also what saved the Rothschilds time and again. When one house faltered, the others could bail it out. When one market collapsed, diversification cushioned the blow. Low profitability reflected high capital ratios, which meant safety. The family firm’s longer time horizon — considering future generations alongside present partners — gave them an advantage no joint-stock corporation could replicate. Their survival for over two centuries, through every major financial crisis and war, stands as one of the most remarkable stories in the history of business.

Chapter 1: Charlotte’s Dream (1849–1858)

The four surviving sons of Mayer Amschel were old men by 1850: Amschel seventy-seven, Salomon seventy-six, Carl an ailing sixty-two. Only James remained indefatigable at fifty-six. Their mother Gutle Schnapper, born 1753, refused to leave the old house “zum grünen Schild” in the former Judengasse. Ludwig Borne praised her: “Look, there she lives, in that little house… and has no wish to leave her hereditary little castle.” She died May 7, 1849, in her ninety-sixth year. Bismarck dined with Amschel and found him “a pathetic old man… a poor man in his palace, a childless widower, cheated by his servants.”

The London partners wanted independence from continental commitments. Nat expressed “such a disgust to business” that he wanted “no more of any sort or description to transact.” James held the family together with sheer force of will. His passionate 1850 letter to Lionel warned that if unity broke, £3 million in capital would “fall into the hands of outsiders.” The 1852 partnership agreement renewed the system with modest decentralisation: partners informed each other of transactions over £830,000 and offered participations of up to 10%. Combined capital grew from £9.27 million in 1852 to £10.63 million by 1855, when Salomon, Carl, and Amschel all died within nine months of each other. The succession battles were fierce — Salomon tried to disinherit Anselm over harsh words exchanged during the revolutionary crisis, initially leaving £1.75 million to Betty’s children instead. In the end, Anselm inherited and chose which of his sons would follow him. James and Anselm each received a quarter of the combined capital, with Nathan’s and Carl’s sons dividing the remainder. No record of great grief survives for any of the three deaths.

Endogamy continued relentlessly. Between 1848 and 1877, nine marriages took place within the family. Charlotte assessed prospective sons-in-law with brutal candour: Gustave was “stupid, easily intimidated and unable to string ten words together”; Alphonse “combines the extraordinary energy and vitality of our uncle with Betty’s facility for languages.” The rationale was blunt: “For us Jews, and particularly for us Rothschilds, it is better not to come into contact with other families, as it always leads to unpleasantness and costs money.”

The eleven-year campaign for Jewish parliamentary emancipation dominated this period. Lionel was elected to the City of London constituency in 1847 but could not take his seat because of the oath “upon the true faith of a Christian.” On July 26, 1850, he appeared before the Commons and declared: “I desire to be sworn upon the Old Testament… that is the form most binding upon my conscience.” He was asked to withdraw. The Lords rejected emancipation bills repeatedly, with bishops leading the opposition.

“I went to sleep at 5 and woke against 6; I had dreamt that a huge vampire was greedily sucking my blood… Surely we do not deserve so much hatred.” — Charlotte de Rothschild after the Lords’ defeat, 1848

Disraeli and the Rothschilds formed a remarkable alliance. Disraeli dined frequently at 148 Piccadilly, and Lionel helped him speculate in French railways. The character of Sidonia in Coningsby is unmistakably Rothschildian: “The world is governed by very different personages from what is imagined by those who are not behind the scenes.” Disraeli’s financial dependence on Lionel may have precluded a U-turn on emancipation, even as he led a party hostile to Jewish rights.

Final triumph came on July 26, 1858. After the Earl of Lucan proposed allowing each House to set its own oath, Lionel was at last sworn in — eleven years after his first election. James called it “a real triumph for the family.” The Rothschilds also built magnificent country houses in this period: Mentmore by Joseph Paxton with twenty-six ground-floor rooms, Ferrières with eighty rooms and an underground railway connecting the kitchens, and Nat’s Château Mouton-Rothschild purchased in 1853.

Philanthropy deepened. James created a Jewish hospital on the rue Picpus (1852), whose opening was described as “one of the grandest ceremonies that Judaism has ever celebrated.” Charlotte ran the Jews’ Free School, trebling enrolment. Her dying words: “Remember the Poor.” Anselm in Vienna began each working day going through begging letters, personally determining sums. In Frankfurt, Wilhelm Carl processed twenty to thirty petitions daily from “all over the Jewish world.”

The grand houses of this period served as both private residences and centres of political power. Mentmore, built by Joseph Paxton for Mayer with twenty-six ground-floor rooms and a glass-roofed hall, was “essentially a hotel” for entertaining influential guests. James trumped it with Ferrières — eighty rooms, an underground railway to the kitchens, and Cordier’s caryatids symbolising Rothschild dominion over the four quarters of the globe. The Goncourts sneered at it as “a pudding of every style.” Nat purchased Château Brane-Mouton in 1853, renaming it Mouton-Rothschild. James secured Château Lafite in 1868 for £177,600 and immediately began bidding up the price of the new vintage. These properties were purchased shrewdly — in the wake of the agricultural crisis, when “Ireland completely ruined, now is the time for buying estates on the sly.”

The family also divided along religious lines that mirrored broader tensions within European Judaism. Wilhelm Carl was the only genuinely Orthodox member of the younger generation, supporting a new synagogue for Orthodox believers. The English brothers inclined toward Reform — Anthony’s wife Louisa wished they could “go to church and hear a good sermon.” Charlotte attended Catholic ceremonies yet remained critical of Jewish institutions’ lack of “heartiness.” James was the most pragmatic: he wrote business letters on the Sabbath but was willing to cancel a trip to read the Haggadah at Passover. Betty told Alphonse that synagogue attendance in New York was “a good thing, not only out of religious feeling, but out of patriotism, which in our high position is a stimulus to those who might forget it.”

Chapter 2: The Era of Mobility (1849–1858)

The 1850s are traditionally depicted as a difficult decade for the Rothschilds, but Ferguson argues this is misleading. Four challenges converged: Louis Napoleon Bonaparte overthrew the republic and proclaimed himself Emperor; James’s rival Achille Fould became Finance Minister, telling Napoleon “it is absolutely necessary that Your Majesty free yourself from the tutelage of Rothschild”; the Crédit Mobilier threatened their dominance; and the Crimean War broke the post-1815 peace. Yet the problems should not be viewed in isolation — while the Paris house navigated Napoleon’s erratic regime, the other Rothschild houses were prospering. James adapted with breathtaking pragmatism: after literally falling down the stairs on the night of the December 1851 coup, spraining his ankle as the Bonapartists struck, he quickly recovered his footing. Rentes leapt from 56 to 64 after the coup, and by its first anniversary stood at 83 — a capital gain of nearly 50%.

James championed the cause of Eugénie de Montijo when more snobbish Parisians assumed she would merely be Napoleon’s next mistress. At a Tuileries ball on January 12, 1853, Eugénie arrived on the arm of James himself; one of his sons escorted her mother. Three days later Napoleon proposed, and within a week they married. Railway investment increased fivefold between 1851 and 1856 under the new regime. The Rothschild-controlled Compagnie du Nord was the most intensively utilised and most profitable of the six big French lines — only 9% of total network length but carrying 14% of all freight and 12% of passenger traffic. Capital gains on continental railway shares were the principal reason the French house outgrew the English. James explained his strategy with customary bluntness: “I do not want a major operation to take place under a new government without our name being on it. If such an operation were to succeed without us, then people would say ‘We don’t need Rothschild any more.’”

The Pereire brothers posed the most serious competitive threat. Their Crédit Mobilier, founded November 20, 1852, mobilised smaller investors through joint-stock structures. Its initial capital of 20 million francs was dwarfed by de Rothschild Frères (88 million francs) and far smaller than the combined Rothschild houses (230 million francs), but shares opened at 1,100 on a 500-franc par value, touched 1,600 four days later, and peaked at 1,982 by March 1856. Dividends rose from 13% to 40%. James commissioned stockbroker Feydeau to buy a thousand Crédit Mobilier shares no fewer than five times, paying in full each time. When Feydeau expressed incredulity, James feigned surprise: “I haf the greatest possible confitence in the chenius of Messrs Pereire… I only regret that I cannot entrust all off my capital to such clefer men.” The rivalry was framed in cultural terms: Rothschild as “the aristocracy of money”; the Pereires as “financial democracy and an economic ‘1789.’”

The Crimean War (1853–56) emphatically reasserted Rothschild primacy in public finance. Gladstone as Chancellor had pledged to finance the war by taxation alone, but reality overwhelmed principle: the national debt stood at £782 million, and by the time Gladstone resigned his successor faced a shortfall four times larger. The government had no alternative but to turn to New Court. In 1855 the London house took a £16 million loan; in 1856 it submitted the only tender for a further £5 million, which was oversubscribed by a factor of nearly six. The Paris house handled 60 million francs of French war borrowing while the London house received subscriptions totalling 208.5 million. James gloated that Napoleon was “excessively displeased to see that the birth of a prince and the conclusion of peace are not having a better effect on public credit.” It helped that Barings — their old rival — was banker to the losing side.

“It is easier to break up a thing than to put it back together again. We have children enough to carry on the business for a hundred years.” — James de Rothschild, 1850

The Dover-Calais submarine cable of 1851 was the real technological watershed. Before it was laid, Julius Reuter had written to New Court offering exclusive transmission of Berlin and Vienna exchange rates. James complained bitterly: “the telegraph is ruining our business” — the courier network that had given Nathan his Waterloo edge was now obsolete. “Anyone can get the news,” he lamented. By the 1860s, Frankfurt families like the Speyers, Sterns, and Erlangers had established branches in London and Paris using the telegraph, and the Speyers reached New York. States began selling bonds by public subscription without Rothschild mediation. The Pereires’ expansion beyond France was genuinely threatening — the Darmstädter Bank, essentially the German Crédit Mobilier, was founded less than twenty miles south of Frankfurt in April 1853, with four of its nine directors from Frankfurt including the Rothschilds’ old rival Moritz Bethmann.

The failure to establish a house in New York was, Ferguson argues, “arguably the Rothschilds’ single greatest strategic mistake.” Betty pushed hard in 1849 for Alphonse to go: “wouldn’t it be a great help to the future of our family to set up a House in New York, a House which would bear our name… America’s future appears so grandiose.” Alphonse visited and spoke of putting affairs there “on a more convenient footing,” but could not bring himself to relinquish Parisian comforts. August Belmont, by then married to Commodore Perry’s daughter, had become too entrenched to replace. On the West Coast, Benjamin Davidson was sent to California with a £40,000 credit when gold was discovered, living in “a shanty built over his vault, like a pig in a sty” — his brother later reported profits of £37,762 in just two years. Brazil became an exceptionally monogamous financial relationship: between 1852 and 1914, the London house generated bond issues worth no less than £142 million for the Brazilian government.

James fought back against the Pereires on every front. Alphonse’s election as regent of the Banque de France in 1855 was a watershed — the Rothschilds were probably the Banque’s biggest shareholders, the Paris house holding over 1,000 shares at its peak. James launched the Réunion Financière in 1856, a confederation of private banks to challenge the Pereires. In Austria, the Creditanstalt (chartered November 1855) adopted Pereire methods but under Rothschild control, securing a dominant position in the Central European railway network. By end of 1858, the Pereires’ challenge had been quashed across the continent. The Rothschilds were an authentic multinational; the Pereires were fundamentally Parisian. The 1857 crash — triggered by Ohio Life and Trust — barely scratched the Rothschilds, while their London house profits remained positive. James’s personal letter to Napoleon warning that the Crédit Mobilier would be “excessively powerful and excessively vulnerable to crises” proved prophetic.

The Rothschilds also entered papal finance, hoping to secure concessions for Roman Jews. Cavour declared he would be “charmed to make a niche for this Jew who is strangling us” — the 1850 Piedmontese loan was “the most beautiful deal I’ve ever made,” James boasted, taking 20 million lire of 5% rentes at a price he could sell at a premium. Cavour fought back, telling his London ambassador to find a new banker “at all costs.” Hambro obliged with £3.6 million of Piedmontese bonds, but James orchestrated negative press and sold Piedmontese bonds with all his energy. His wordplay: “L’emprunt est ouvert, mais non couvert” — the loan has opened, but is not subscribed. Yet with astonishing prescience, by 1856 James had emerged as the main foreign shareholder in the new Piedmontese investment bank, already preparing to finance the next European war.

Chapter 3: Nationalism and the Multinational (1859–1863)

The Italian War of 1859 caught the Rothschilds in an impossible position. On January 14, 1858, the Austrian ambassador was dining at Alphonse’s when a clerk brought urgent news — Italian terrorists had attempted to assassinate Napoleon III and Empress Eugénie in the Orsini affair. Napoleon used the execution to communicate sympathy with Italian nationalism, then met Cavour secretly at Plombières in July 1858 to redraw Italy’s map: France would help create “Upper Italy from the Alps to the Adriatic” in return for Savoy. James was caught between financial involvement with France, Austria, and Piedmont simultaneously. His famous bon mot as war loomed: “The Emperor does not know France. Twenty years ago a war might have been proclaimed without causing any great perturbation. But today everybody has his railway coupons or his three per cents.” The conventional view that the wars of the 1860s cost the Rothschilds dearly is actually wrong — Ferguson’s data shows the 1860s and 1870s were two of the London house’s three most profitable decades before 1914, with annual profits reaching £221,278 in the 1860s and £468,308 in the 1870s.

The Naples house bore the brunt of Italian unification. Garibaldi’s invasion of Naples in September 1860 put the Rothschild house in an acutely difficult position. Adolph opted to flee with the Bourbon King, then withdrew from the partnership in 1863, pulling out £1,593,777 — the first crack in the multinational structure. James was furious, denouncing him as “riff-raff” and “a great lump.” For James, states were essentially businesses — where historians saw nation-building, he saw “mergers and demergers.” Italy became “our hobby horse” but a financial disappointment: between 1859 and 1865 the new government borrowed 1,875 million lire while tax revenues covered only half of expenditures. The lira’s convertibility was suspended in May 1866. Italian ministers were dismissed as “asses” and “imbeciles.”

The Austrian-Prussian yield gap told the real story of European power politics. Prussian 3.5% bonds never fell below 78; Austrian 5% metalliques touched 42, a level not seen since the Napoleonic period. The yield spread between the two averaged 5 percentage points throughout 1851–1868. Austrian debt had increased by a factor of 2.8 between 1847 and 1859, against Prussia’s 1.8. Austrian debt service consumed 26% of national income versus Prussia’s 15%. “Prussia stood above par in politics as on the bourse,” Ferguson writes. Austrian policy failed because it was financially unsustainable — unable to afford the military effort necessary for victory on both Italian and German fronts. James advocated that Austria should sell territory in one theatre to defend the other, but Franz Joseph scrawled in the margin of reports that James’s loan was conditional on recognising Italy as a kingdom: “There can be no talk of that.”

Bismarck lunched with Amschel in Frankfurt in June 1851, consciously echoing Metternich thirty years earlier, and the Rothschilds attended to his private finances on preferential terms while he provided political intelligence in return. But Bismarck was not rich — his expenditures of 27,000 thalers exceeded his salary of 15,000 plus estate income of roughly 4,000. The Rothschilds regarded him with “a mixture of antipathy and admiration.” James called him “a madcap”; Anselm described him as “a wild boar foaming with rage”; Charlotte called him “the great highway robber of the second half of the nineteenth century” yet could also “regard a Bismarck intelligence as a desideratum in a son-in-law.” Bleichröder gradually poached Bismarck’s account from the Frankfurt house, and by end of 1866 it was Bleichröder, not Rothschild, who managed the Iron Chancellor’s finances.

“Casca il mondo” (“The world is falling”) — The Papal nuncio after Königgrätz, 1866

Prussia’s decisive victory at Königgrätz on July 3, 1866 stunned the entire family. Rothschild properties came under Prussian control — at Schillersdorf, “a great part of the game” was plundered, and Ferdinand found Prussian cavalry officers who “cantered their horses all over the gravel paths in the park.” Frankfurt was occupied on July 17, and Mayer Carl, who had hoped Prussia would “get a famous licking,” instead found Prussian soldiers occupying “Willy’s house on the Zeil… every room, with the exception of Matty’s dormitory, and would drink nothing but Champagne at their meals!” James was “half crazy”: “I am wholly pro-Austrian this time, as the war is just too unjust.” Even his eight-year-old granddaughter Bettina was “very angry with Mr Bismarck.”

James had conceived a grand vision for 1865: a complex of interdependent transactions to liquidate Austria’s unsustainable empire without war. Austria would sell Venetia to Italy and Holstein to Prussia; both buyers would raise cash by privatising state railways; the Rothschilds would broker all three deals. The Prussian privatisation actually succeeded — the Cologne-Minden railway deal of July 1865 gave Bismarck’s government 13 million thalers. War Minister Roon exulted: “We have money, enough to give us a free hand in foreign policy, enough to mobilise the whole army.” James refused Bleichröder’s proposal to buy further shares for 20 million thalers: “I refused… on the grounds that we cannot give money to make war.” The Munich Punsch ran a cartoon: James clinging to his money-bags crying “I’m not giving anything! The only pleasure I get is neutrality.”

Napoleon’s speech at Auxerre in May 1866 denouncing the treaties of 1815 devastated the Paris bourse. At the Tuileries ball that night, “the faces of the ambassadors were so long, that one would have taken them for people condemned to death. But the longest of all was that of Rothschild. They say that he lost ten millions the evening before.” Alphonse’s brilliant analysis predicted that French victory would mean alignment with Austria, while defeat would bring Prussia’s domination of Germany — “an immense danger.” James drew back from Catholic Church finance despite the profits: “As a Jew, I don’t like to go against the clergy… I remain a financier and do not want to get involved with politics which will turn the clergy against us.”

Despite backing the losing side militarily, the Rothschilds profited handsomely from the financial aftermath. Austria needed emergency credits desperately, and the London house’s profits for 1866 were a healthy £170,000. The Polish crisis of 1863 had already set back plans for a long-term Russian relationship — Charlotte was “so glad that the Barings, and not the Rothschilds have taken the Russian loan… if our house had negotiated it, most assuredly there would have been a great outcry against those horrid jews for helping the cruel Russians to crush the poor Poles.” The entire family identified emotionally with the Austrian-German side, but financial reality demanded accommodation with the victors. This tension — between sentiment and survival, between loyalty and pragmatism — would intensify in every subsequent European conflict until the multinational structure finally broke apart in 1905.

Chapter 4: Blood and Silver (1863–1867)

James de Rothschild died on November 15, 1868, at age seventy-six. His estate was valued at over 1 billion francs — roughly £40 million — making him by far the richest man in France and probably the world. His funeral was attended by the Emperor’s representative, the entire diplomatic corps, and even Émile Pereire. In his last years, James’s bourse humour had become legendary: “At the bourse, there comes a time when, if you want to succeed, you have to speak Hebrew.” Asked about a turnstile at the bourse: “My fiew is that it vill cost me tventy sous a day.” The Goncourt brothers described a gathering of seventy-four Rothschilds at Gustave’s wedding as “pariah kings of the world… they covet everything and control everything, the newspapers, the arts, the writers and the thrones.” James in his office remained a force of nature: “He had the singular and precious ability to concentrate his thoughts… this Titan read all the letters, received all the despatches, and found time in the evening to perform his social duties despite devoting himself to business from five in the morning.”

The transition to Alphonse (born 1827) was remarkably smooth. James had been grooming him for years, and Alphonse inherited his father’s Judengasse mentality intact. Asked why he worked like a slave when he was so rich, he replied with chilling candour: “You don’t know the pleasure of feeling heaps of Christians under one’s boots.” James’s will divided the Paris house among his four sons — Alphonse as senior partner, Gustave, Salomon, and Edmond. In London, Lionel never addressed the House of Commons but was far from politically inactive, seeing senior politicians and journalists constantly at New Court and Piccadilly. Charlotte observed: “Politics interest your father to the exclusion of all other topics.” The Rothschild door remained open to all factions — “the two great rivals at dinner, the whig on Saturday, the tory on Sunday.”

Napoleon III’s clumsy diplomacy careered toward catastrophe. The Luxembourg crisis of 1867 was the first test of the new European order — Napoleon sought “compensation” for Prussian gains, first the Rhineland, then Belgium, finally Luxembourg. Bismarck leaked each demand to embarrass France. Alphonse warned that “this country is heading for catastrophe.” James had coined his famous epigram: “L’Empire, c’est la baisse” — punning on Napoleon’s own “l’Empire, c’est la paix” to mean “the Empire means a falling market.” His prescient summary of October 1850 proved prophetic: “In the end we shall have an Emperor, which will end with war, for if I wasn’t so afraid of war, then I would be an imperialist myself.”

“If I were Emperor, I would be ashamed of myself.” — James de Rothschild on Napoleon III

When the Hohenzollern candidacy for the Spanish throne triggered the crisis of July 1870, Alphonse and his brothers used every diplomatic channel to prevent war. The candidacy was withdrawn and peace seemed saved — but Napoleon’s insistence on a permanent guarantee, and Bismarck’s doctoring of the Ems telegram, made war unavoidable. France declared war on July 19. The Paris bourse collapsed; the Rothschilds lost heavily on their rente holdings. The French army was catastrophically unprepared — within six weeks, Napoleon III was captured at Sedan on September 2. The Second Empire fell; the Third Republic was proclaimed. Alphonse immediately began converting assets to gold and silver while Bleichröder proposed to Bismarck that the Rothschilds could serve as intermediaries for peace — an offer Bismarck initially rejected but later exploited.

The war was a personal catastrophe for the Paris Rothschilds. Ferrières — the palace James had built to outshine every house in France — was occupied by the Prussian army and became first Bismarck’s headquarters, then King William’s. Bismarck slept in Alphonse’s bed. The famous wine cellars were looted by officers who would drink nothing but Champagne at their meals. Alphonse remained in Paris through the four-month siege, communicating with the outside world via carrier pigeon and balloon post while his wife and children fled to London. The Commune (March–May 1871) added additional horror. Alphonse’s role was pivotal throughout: he negotiated directly with Bismarck at Ferrières — the supreme irony of conducting France’s financial surrender in his own palace — and with Thiers, the new French president. The armistice terms were harsh but the alternative — continued bombardment of Paris — was worse.

The Treaty of Frankfurt imposed an indemnity of 5 billion francs on France — the largest in history. Bismarck chose this figure partly because it matched what Napoleon I had extracted from Prussia after Jena, adjusted for inflation. Two enormous loans were organised: 2 billion francs in 1871 at 82.5, oversubscribed five times, and 3 billion in 1872 at 84.5, which attracted 43 billion francs in subscriptions — twelve times the amount offered. Money flooded in from across Europe, a vote of confidence in France’s recovery and in Rothschild competence. The commission was relatively modest, but the volume was immense. France paid off the indemnity by September 1873, nearly two years ahead of schedule, astonishing Bismarck. This was the greatest demonstration of Rothschild financial power since Waterloo.

The war fundamentally reshaped the Rothschild network. Frankfurt — now part of Prussia — declined as a financial centre. Berlin replaced it, and the Rothschilds had no house there. Bleichröder filled the vacuum. Neither Mayer Carl nor his brother Wilhelm Carl produced a male heir, sealing the Frankfurt house’s fate. Wilhelm Carl was ready to throw in his hand as early as 1890. Mayer Carl’s staff went to work for the Disconto-Gesellschaft. The office in the Fahrgasse — built by Amschel and Salomon in the first flower of their prosperity — became a museum for Jewish antiquities under Wilhelm Carl’s will. The fons et origo of the Rothschild fortunes was wound up in 1901. Only three houses remained: London, Paris, and Vienna.

The Rothschilds also failed to gain a lasting foothold in Russian finance. James had tried repeatedly to build a position in St Petersburg but found direct involvement in Russian railways “too risky.” Gorchakov urged Mayer Carl that “we ought to have a house at Petersburg — there is to be made in Russia c’est une mine d’or.” The Rothschilds refused, establishing a pattern of caution toward Russia that would have enormous diplomatic consequences. In April 1862 they secured a major Russian loan — £15 million in 5% bonds — but the Polish revolt of January 1863 drove down Russian and Italian bonds. Charlotte was relieved when Barings took the next Russian loan: “If our house had negotiated it, most assuredly there would have been a great outcry against those horrid jews for helping the cruel Russians to crush the poor Poles.”

Chapter 5: Bonds and Iron (1867–1870)

The Spanish question illustrated the dangers of mixing finance with diplomacy. French capital had penetrated deeply into Spain — mines, railways, government bonds — and French banks competed fiercely for influence after the 1868 revolution that overthrew Queen Isabella. The Rothschilds controlled the Almadén mercury mines, whose output they used as collateral for advances to the Spanish government. Between 1866 and 1882 Spain’s public debt exploded from 4.6 billion to 12.9 billion pesetas. The Zaragoza railway line had debts of 40 million francs and an annual deficit of 1.5 million by the mid-1860s. Anthony grumbled that “railroads are always at the bottom of the Baron’s business.” Spain was “the country of surprises, where one cannot even count on tomorrow coming” — military coups and revolutions made every calculation provisional. “A little security and stability in the political system,” one Rothschild partner sighed, “would be more efficacious than any subsidy.”

The Gründerzeit — the founders’ era of 1866–73 — saw a frenzy of joint-stock company formation across Germany and Austria. Mayer Carl reported from Frankfurt with alarm: “You have no idea what a competition there is now in business; it is more than a mania and a regular disease like Cholera.” He participated in loans to the cities of Danzig and Königsberg, to the Silesian, Magdeburg, and Cologne-Minden railways, and helped establish the Preussische Central-Boden-Credit Aktiengesellschaft — a Prussian mortgage bank modelled on the French Crédit Foncier. His philosophy was pragmatic: “Every little helps” and “half an egg is better than an empty shell.” But the Rothschilds grew increasingly alarmed at the speculative excess. The five German great banks — Darmstädter, Disconto-Gesellschaft, Deutsche Bank, Dresdner, and the Berliner Handels-Gesellschaft — were growing rapidly and taking deposits, something the Rothschilds had never done.

Relations with Bleichröder deteriorated sharply as Berlin replaced Frankfurt as Germany’s financial capital. Mayer Carl complained that Bleichröder was “an old fool who wants everybody to think & believe that he is our agent.” The Rothschilds preferred to work with Hansemann, whom they considered “first-rate and particularly honest.” But the underlying problem was structural: the Rothschilds had no direct presence in Berlin, and Bleichröder had already captured Bismarck’s personal account. Mayer Carl’s confidence in peace rested on economics — he was busy with Prussian finance, South German state loans, and railway construction across the new Confederation. “Why go to war for it?” he asked. Economic unification seemed to make military unification unnecessary. It was the reasoning of a banker, not a statesman, and Bismarck would prove him catastrophically wrong.

“Every little helps” and “1/2 an egg is better than an empty shell.” — Mayer Carl on small German state loans

The Austro-Hungarian Compromise of 1867 created new requirements for financial operations in both Vienna and Budapest, stretching the Vienna house’s resources. Anselm proved capable of managing the transition, though his son Ferdinand showed more interest in art collecting than banking. The Creditanstalt, effectively a Rothschild subsidiary, handled Austrian and Hungarian bond issues worth roughly 2.8 billion crowns (about £120 million). But the monopoly was crumbling — Austrian Finance Minister Böhm-Bawerk let non-Rothschild banks participate in conversion operations from 1903, and by 1908 Austria-Hungary adopted public subscription for new bond issues. Albert’s death in 1911 would leave the Austrian branch without strong leadership at the worst possible moment.

Napoleon III’s famous visit to Ferrières on December 16, 1862 symbolised the uneasy reconciliation between Rothschild and Bonaparte. A green velvet carpet with golden bees was rolled across the station platform. Imperial flags flew from all four towers. Rossini composed a “Chorus of Democratic Hunters.” Some 1,231 head of game were killed. Natty gave a sardonic account: “one of the most disagreeable rides I ever had… the cries of Vive l’Empereur were for the greater part uttered by paid agents… most of the shots had drunk 10 or 12 different kinds of wine they shot very badly.” James’s barbed farewell punned on mémoire (memory and bill): “Sire, mes enfants et moi, nous n’oublierons jamais cette journée. Le mémoire nous en sera cher” — suggesting Napoleon would pay for the visit.

The Crédit Mobilier’s death throes provided James detached Schadenfreude. Jules Mirès was arrested for fraud in 1861. James relished: “Rothschild is triumphant, and says that he is the sole baron of industry.” By 1864, “the old bank had become the new; the new bank had become the old.” Alphonse’s testimony at the 1865 monetary Enquête was an advance obituary for Pereire expansionism. Émile Pereire’s bitter defence laid bare the real scope of Rothschild operations: “it was not me who financed the Zaragoza and Alicante railways… it was not me who was responsible for the 1,500 million of Italian loans… and yet the signature which these operations all bear is among those which accuse us of having impoverished the national wealth!”

The American Civil War (1861–65) exposed the limits of Rothschild transatlantic influence. August Belmont, as the Democrats’ national chairman and supporter of Stephen Douglas, was reviled by both sides. Republicans called him a trimmer on slavery; Southern Democrats accused him of the same. The Chicago Tribune demanded: “Will we have a dishonourable peace, in order to enrich Belmont, the Rothschilds, and the whole tribe of Jews?” In fact the family was largely neutral. Mayer Carl told the American consul-general that the firm was “opposed to slavery and in favor of the Union.” The Confederate loan was issued by Erlanger, not Rothschild. Jay Cooke’s pitch for $5 million of Northern Pacific Railroad bonds got “short shrift” from Lionel. The Civil War caused a permanent decline in Rothschild transatlantic influence — the strategic cost of never having established a proper New York house was becoming painfully apparent.

Technology was eroding the Rothschilds’ traditional advantages at every turn. The telegraph had ended their courier network’s information edge — the very advantage that had made Nathan’s Waterloo trade possible. Joint-stock banks offered deposit-taking services the Rothschilds had never provided, building vast balance sheets that dwarfed even Rothschild capital. Public subscription of government bonds replaced private placement, eliminating the intermediary role that had been the foundation of their business. States no longer needed a Rothschild to sell their paper. By the early 1900s the five German great banks would have combined capital of 870 million marks, growing faster than the Rothschilds and capturing market share in every metric except raw capital. The world was changing faster than a family partnership, however wealthy, could adapt.

Chapter 6: Reich, Republic, Rentes (1870–1873)

The Franco-Prussian War created a fundamentally new European landscape that challenged every assumption of Rothschild banking. Germany was unified under Prussian leadership — a military power that had crushed France in six weeks and imposed the largest indemnity in history. France was a republic nursing a desire for revanche, its politics volatile and its finances dependent on Rothschild management. Austria-Hungary adjusted to permanent diminishment, squeezed between German power and Russian ambition. The five-house structure that Mayer Amschel had designed in 1810 had already shrunk to three — London, Paris, and Vienna — with the Naples house closed in 1863 and Frankfurt entering terminal decline. For the Rothschilds, the key question was existential: how to maintain a multinational banking network in a world where nationalism was making internationalism suspect and where each house was increasingly expected to serve its national government above all others.

The Vienna stock exchange crashed on May 9, 1873 — “Black Friday” — and the crisis spread across Europe and America in the first truly international financial crisis of the modern era. The years 1871–73 had seen a massive speculative boom across Germany and Austria, the Gründerzeit, fuelled by the influx of French indemnity money. The Rothschilds were relatively unscathed because of their habitual conservatism — Anselm had warned repeatedly about speculative excess but had been largely ignored. The Creditanstalt suffered significant losses but survived. The crash had profound political consequences: it discredited economic liberalism in Central Europe and fuelled anti-Semitic sentiment. The Gründerschwindel — “founding swindle” — was blamed on Jewish financiers, setting a pattern that would repeat with devastating consequences in the 1930s.

After the indemnity loans, Alphonse was the undisputed king of French finance. He maintained close relations with Thiers and the conservative republicans who governed France in the 1870s. The Paris house focused on French rentes, the Compagnie du Nord railway, and international loans. The balance of power within the Rothschild network shifted decisively from Vienna and Frankfurt to Paris and London. Alphonse’s political instincts were conservative-republican: he feared both Bonapartist restoration and radical republicanism. He supported the “moral order” governments while maintaining relations with all factions — the essential Rothschild survival strategy of backing every horse while betting most heavily on stability. Railway nationalisation was the great threat — “that old objective of 1848” which preyed on Alphonse’s mind, since the Compagnie du Nord was a major Rothschild asset.

“L’Empire, c’est la baisse.” — James de Rothschild (the Empire means falling prices)

A famous dinner during Gambetta’s premiership (1881–82) captured the Rothschild political method. Alphonse and Gambetta were spotted “chatting amiably in a window alcove, the two sovereigns — Gambetta, the actual master of France, and Rothschild.” Gambetta drank: “To a restored France!” Alphonse responded: “To the man who will restore her!” Gambetta paused and replied: “Ah! I would be willing. If only the electoral committee of Belleville had been there to see their Gambetta in the company of these princes and marquises.” Yet privately Alphonse told police in December 1881: “I want an all-out campaign; it is necessary to demolish Gambetta before he demolishes us.” Gambetta planned a massive 6 billion franc rente conversion and railway nationalisation. The Union Générale crash contributed to his fall.

The collapse of the Union Générale in January 1882 catalysed a massive outpouring of anti-Semitic propaganda. Paul Eugène Bontoux, a former Südbahn engineer, had built an anti-Rothschild Catholic investment trust with capital of 25 million francs. Its shares reached 3,005 francs by December 1881, then crashed to 1,300 in two weeks. Bontoux claimed a “Jewish plot” — no evidence supported the charge, and the Rothschilds actually contributed 10 million francs to the collective rescue of Paris banks. Zola’s L’Argent gave literary credibility to the idea that Rothschilds destroyed the bank; the character Gundermann was a thinly veiled James de Rothschild. The crash timed perfectly with Gambetta’s fall and France’s retreat from joint action in Egypt — clearing the way for unilateral British occupation.

British fiscal rectitude in this period meant the government scarcely needed Rothschild loans. Between 1858 and 1900 the national debt actually fell from £809 million to £569 million. Anselm’s bitter observation of March 1866 remained apt: “the political influence of England in continental affairs can be considered as nil.” A government that did not borrow was a government the Rothschilds could advise but not pressurise. Natty combined hero-worship of Gladstone, cynicism about Disraeli, and Cobdenite free trade enthusiasm. At the 1866 Reform Bill debates he noted “Mr. Gladstone’s oratory heavy and pompous, while Mr. Disraeli sparkled delightfully.” The Rothschild door remained open to all factions: “the two great rivals at dinner — the whig on Saturday, the tory on Sunday.”

Alphonse on socialism, in a remarkably candid 1897 interview with Jules Huret, revealed the worldview underlying Rothschild politics: “I am sure that, generally speaking, working people are very satisfied with their lot… Those who demand the eight hour day are the lazy, incapable ones… if they were all compelled to work only eight hours a day do you know what the majority of them would do? Well, they would drink!” On wealth inequality: “I have never understood what is meant by ‘haute banque.’ There are richer men and poorer men and that’s all there is to it!… Capital is labour! Apart from some unfortunate exceptions, each man has that share of the available capital that his intelligence, energy and industry merit.” This confidence in the justice of the existing order would face its most severe tests in the decades ahead.

Chapter 7: “The Caucasian Royal Family”

Ferguson tests Thomas Mann’s Buddenbrooks thesis of generational decline against the Rothschild evidence. The third generation died in rapid succession: Nat in 1870, after eighteen years fighting “what to others would have been an insupportable illness” — hours before death “he talked to Alphonse about American stocks and the Russian Loan”; Anselm in 1874, an ascetic who lived in just two rooms of his Vienna palace, travelled only by hansom cab, and was buried “with the greatest simplicity — as if it had been that of a poor Jew,” though he left over 50 million thalers, double the assets of the Jesuit order; Mayer in 1874; Anthony in 1876; Lionel in 1879. Lionel worked until the day he died — summoning his broker Edward Wagg to correct an addition error in his fortnightly account. He left £2.7 million. Disraeli called him “one of the ablest men I ever knew.”

The fourth generation seemed to confirm the decline narrative. Charlotte wrote of baby Natty: “a thin, ugly baby, but that did not signify.” By nine: “reserved and shy and not generous; he is the only one of my children fond of money for the sake of hoarding it… constitutionally indolent.” Natty struggled through Cambridge, devoted more time to riding with drag hounds and the Amateur Dramatic Club than studying, and left without taking finals. Politics was always his real interest — MP for Aylesbury at twenty-five, inheriting the baronetcy in 1876. When asked for his formula for financial success, he replied: “Yes, by selling too soon.” Clinton Dawkins of Morgan wrote in 1901: “The Rothschilds have nothing new but the experience and great prestige of old Nattie.”

Leo’s interests turned entirely to horse-racing. His horse Sir Bevys won the Derby in 1879; St Amant won again in 1904. He had St Frusquin modelled in silver by Fabergé. His routine at New Court: arrive at 2pm, lunch at 3:30, sleep on the sofa for the rest of the afternoon. Alfred lived as a fin de siècle aesthete — Max Beerbohm caricatured doctors consulting whether he might take a second praline before bedtime. He was the first Jew elected to the Court of Directors of the Bank of England (1868) but conspicuously failed to leverage the position. In the Austrian branch, Salomon James was banished by his father for losing a million francs speculating; three years later he was dead — “killed, so the Goncourts heard, by the stress of speculating at the bourse.”

“A gentleman is not to be found in the office before eleven and never stays beyond four.” — Alfred to Max Warburg

Competitors noticed the drift with undisguised contempt. Edward Baring declared the Rothschilds “so unreasonable and lazy that it is difficult to ensure a business being properly carried through under their direction.” Ernest Cassel was blunter: “absolutely useless & not remarkable for intelligence.” The real engine at New Court was Carl Meyer, a young Hamburg clerk who worked at frenetic pace while the partners arrived at eleven and left by four. Treatment of subordinates was de haut en bas — Natty told broker Fred Cripps that a Rio Tinto price was “wrong by a quarter of a point,” and visitors waited in an ante-room “as though it were Buckingham Palace.” When Meyer sought promotion to procurist in 1890, offering to sign for the firm, the brothers turned him down. He resigned in 1897 to work with Cassel — a catastrophic loss of talent that encapsulated the family firm’s inability to retain ambitious employees.

The multinational partnership fragmented irreversibly. £41.3 million was withdrawn between 1874 and 1905 as partners died and heirs cashed out. By the 1879 contract, James’s sons held 31.4% of total capital, Anselm’s sons 22.7%, Mayer Carl and Wilhelm Carl 22.3%, Lionel’s sons just 15.7%, and Nat’s sons 7.9%. By 1905 the French partners controlled 46.8%, the Austrians 25.9%, the English a mere 23.4%. The partnership was formally dissolved between 1905 and 1909. A French journalist had prophesied: “Divide up the house of Rothschild into a French house, an English, an Austrian… and its mediating influence disappears.” Lord Haldane later recalled rearranging “Rothschild partnerships which had got into a very vague relation, placing the whole family potentially at the mercy of one dishonest partner.”

Yet Ferguson argues the decline narrative is overstated. Natty, Alphonse, and Albert remained effective bankers even as their siblings pursued aestheticism. The houses fared reasonably well in profits and capital — combined profits for the most productive years (1879–82) averaged over £4 million annually, and the trend from 1888 to 1904 was upward, from £785,000 to £1.6 million. The problem was structural, not personal: joint-stock banks offered “careers open to talent” that the family firm could not match. Mayer Carl complained in 1873 that “Jews with their terrible ambition are the worst employees… every one of the clerks leaves the House, either to become the manager of a Bank or for the sake of establishing himself on his own account.” National rivalries made cross-border cooperation fraught.

The family’s country houses and art collections expanded spectacularly. Ferdinand’s Waddesdon — a Loire château in deepest Buckinghamshire with fifty greenhouses — was arguably the greatest Rothschild house, though not to every taste: Gladstone’s daughter Mary felt “oppressed with the extreme gorgeousness.” It cost Alice £7,500 a year just for the gardens. Alfred’s Halton featured a bowling alley, ice rink, indoor swimming pool, and Indian pavilion. Algernon West dismissed it as “an exaggerated nightmare of gorgeousness and senseless and ill-applied magnificence.” Ferdinand, neurotic and impulsive — “he did not eat but devoured, he did not walk but ran” — told Rosebery: “I am a lonely, suffering and occasionally a very miserable individual despite the gilded and marble rooms in which I live.”

Charlotte’s phrase captured the family’s self-image: “the Caucasian royal family.” When Alfred visited Leopold II in Brussels, the King said: “Votre famille m’a toujours gâté.” Alfred replied: “Pardon Sire, c’est Votre Majesté qui a toujours gâté notre famille.” Alfred’s mistress Marie Wombwell may have had an illegitimate child, Almina, to whom Alfred gave a £500,000 dowry when she married the Earl of Carnarvon, plus settled £150,000 in debts, and left a large portion of his £1.5 million estate. The Rothschilds managed the Prince of Wales’s debts — primarily a discreetly hushed-up £160,000 mortgage on Sandringham. They considered themselves equals of royalty — and with some justification.

Chapter 8: Jewish Questions

Endogamy gave way to marriages with other wealthy Jewish families after 1877. The Sassoon alliance was most significant: Leo married Marie Perugia in 1881 (the Prince of Wales attended), Aline married Sir Edward Sassoon in 1887, and Robert married Nelly Beer in 1907, also linked to the Sassoons. This signalled what Ferguson calls “the end of the marital exclusivism of the mid-nineteenth century and the integration of the Rothschilds — albeit as primus inter pares — into a wider ‘cousinhood’ of wealthy Jewish families.” Of thirty-one fourth-generation members who married, thirteen married another Rothschild. Edmond’s marriage to Adelheid proved the last pure Rothschild match — Margaretha had refused him.

Fourth-generation women married outside the faith: Annie to Eliot Yorke (1873), Constance to Cyril Flower (1877), Hannah to the 5th Earl of Rosebery (1878). The Jewish Chronicle lamented: “If the flame is seized on the cedars, how will fare the hyssop on the wall?” None actually converted — even Constance, who said “I wish I could be a Christian,” decided conversion would be “impossible” and “a falsehood.” Hannah’s wedding saw no male Rothschild in attendance; Disraeli gave the bride away. She inherited Mentmore, 107 Piccadilly, and £100,000 a year. Winston Churchill later called her “a remarkable woman on whom he [Rosebery] had leaned… a pacifying and composing element in his life which he was never able to find again.” Hannah died of typhoid in 1890, aged just thirty-nine.

Natty was elevated to the peerage on July 9, 1885 — sworn in with his hat on his head and his hand on a Hebrew Old Testament. Queen Victoria had resisted fiercely: “To make a Jew a peer.” Her objections were social, political, and religious — “She cannot think that one who owes his great wealth to contracts with Foreign Govts. for loans, or to successful speculations on the Stock Exchange can fairly claim a British peerage.” Gladstone argued forcefully until she yielded: “Her argument is null and void. If it be sound, she has been wrong in consenting to emancipate the Jews.” Natty became Baron Rothschild of Tring, soon joined by other Jewish peers: Lord Wandsworth (Stern), Lord Swaythling (Montagu), Lord Pirbright (de Worms). Nearly a fifth of City bankers acquired peerages after 1890.

Anti-Semitism transformed from incoherent prejudice into organised political movements. In Germany, Wilhelm Marr — who had worked as a young man for a Rothschild-linked family and been fired in 1841 — coined Antisemitismus. Werner Sombart’s The Jews and Economic Life (1911) argued that “the name Rothschild meant more than the firm which bears it; it meant all the Jews who are active at the bourse.” In Austria, Karl Lueger’s “Christian Social” campaign called for nationalising the Rothschild-owned Kaiser-Ferdinand-Nordbahn. When Lueger became Mayor of Vienna in 1897, he quickly discovered how hard it was to dispense with the Rothschilds. In France, the volume of anti-Semitic publication was greatest — Drumont’s Jewish France (1886) ran to 200 editions, claiming the Rothschilds controlled “nine-tenths of all Europe’s financial affairs.” Auguste Chirac’s Kings of the Republic cast Alphonse as “Moloch-Baal, that is to say the God Gold, marching towards the conquest of Europe.”

“The God Rothschild was the real master of France: Neither Emperor, nor Tsar, nor King… he disposes over all the governmental forces, all the resources of France for his private purposes.” — Drumont, The Jews against France (1899)

The Dreyfus Affair (1894) polarised French society. Alphonse was “irritated by the condemnation of Dreyfus and by the indifference of the French aristocracy.” Proust captured the schizophrenic attitudes in his Guermantes circle — Dreyfusard sympathies studiously concealed in a world where Jews and anti-Semites routinely socialised together. In England, anti-Semitism came more from the political left: the Labour Leader denounced the Rothschilds as a “blood-sucking crew,” and J.A. Hobson’s Imperialism (1902) blamed the Boer War on “a small group of international financiers, chiefly German in origin and Jewish in race.” Trollope’s Melmotte in The Way We Live Now was another thinly veiled portrait of Rothschild power.

Violence escalated as rhetoric inspired physical attacks. A crude letter bomb was sent to Alphonse at his home in the rue Florentin in August 1895; in his absence it was forwarded to the rue Laffitte office, where it exploded and seriously injured his head clerk. The Times commented drily that “an Anarchist outrage on one of the Rothschilds is not greatly to be wondered at… they stand out as the natural objects which Anarchists would seek to attack.” In London in 1912, William Tebbitt fired five shots at Leo as he drove from New Court, riddling his car with bullets and badly wounding the policeman on guard. Edouard and Robert de Rothschild duelled for honour against racial insults. In America, Populists mobilised Midwestern farmers against “the gold gamblers of Europe” and depicted the Rothschilds as an “English Octopus” with tentacles spanning the globe, feeding on nothing but gold.

The Rothschild response combined philanthropy, community leadership, and political engagement. Natty was president of the United Synagogue from 1879 to 1915, honorary president of the Federation of Synagogues, president of the Jews’ Free School, and vice-president of the Anglo-Jewish Association. He ran a “two-tier health service” on his Tring estate, established the Four Per Cent Industrial Dwellings Company to build housing in Spitalfields — the Charlotte de Rothschild Buildings housed 228 families in 477 rooms — and told the Hutchison House Club in 1905: “We want to instil into the boys ambition, the pride of being Jews and the pride in being Englishmen.” In Paris, the Rothschild Foundation for workers was endowed with 10 million francs. In Frankfurt, Hannah Mathilde founded a Jewish Children’s Home, Old People’s Home, and Sanatorium for Poor Jews.

Edmond de Rothschild funded Jewish settlements in Palestine from 1882, spending £5.6 million on colonies at Rishon le Zion (“First in Zion”), Zikhron Ya’aqov, and others. His approach was highly authoritarian: settlers signed agreements to “submit myself totally to the orders which the administration shall think necessary in the name of M. le Baron.” He directed viniculture, silk manufacture, perfume, and glass production — every detail supervised by his officials. By 1903, nineteen of twenty-eight Jewish settlements in Palestine were subsidised by him. But Edmond explicitly opposed a Jewish state. When Theodor Herzl sought Rothschild backing for his Judenstaat, offering to make the first Rothschild “prince,” Albert in Vienna received what “doubtless seemed to be yet another half-mad demand for money — a billion francs, no less.” Natty refused even to see Herzl: “The dream of Palestine is a myth and a will-of-the-wisp.” Ferguson’s verdict: “With the benefit of hindsight, we can see that Herzl was a prophet. Less than half a century after his death, the German, Austrian and French Rothschilds had all fallen victim to just the anti-Semitic onslaught he had foreseen.”

Chapter 9: “On the Side of Imperialism” (1874–1885)

The best-known example of Rothschild imperialism is the Suez Canal purchase of 1875. Disraeli asked Lionel to advance £4 million so Britain could buy the Khedive’s shares. The sum equalled 8.3% of the entire UK budget net of debt charges. Parliament was not sitting and the money was needed by the end of November. Ottoman Turkey had declared bankruptcy on October 7; the Khedive told Rothschilds he needed £3–4 million immediately. The French Foreign Minister blocked Crédit Foncier from buying the shares, leaving Britain as the only buyer. Derby and Northcote opposed; Disraeli prevailed in five Cabinet meetings between November 18 and 24. Montagu Corry rushed to New Court; Lionel “picked up a muscatel grape, ate it, threw out the skin, and said deliberately, ‘What is your security?’ ‘The British Government.’ ‘You shall have it.’”

By January 5, 1876, N.M. Rothschild had paid the full £3,976,582 2s 6d directly to Egyptian creditors. Parliament voted £4,080,000 on February 21 to repay, raised by creating 3.5% Exchequer Bonds. Commission was 2.5% plus 5% interest — Lionel defended it: the Khedive might have insisted on gold payment, money markets might have tightened. Disraeli wrote Queen Victoria: “It is just settled: you have it, Madam. The French Government has been outgeneraled.” The investment proved spectacularly profitable: shares rose 50% within two months, the government stake was worth £24 million by 1898, £40 million on the eve of World War I, and £93 million by 1935. But it also drew Britain irreversibly into Egyptian affairs — by 1876 debt charges consumed 55.5% of all Egyptian expenditure.

The Balkan Crisis of 1875–78 tested Rothschild loyalties severely. Between 1870 and 1875, the London and Paris houses had jointly issued Russian bonds worth £62 million. Russian 5% bonds rose from 85 in March 1870 to 106 in August 1875, then crashed to 74 in October 1876 and 68 in April 1877 when Russia declared war on Turkey. Natty and Alfred backed Turkey over Russia, aligning with Disraeli’s government at enormous financial cost — leaving the 1877 Russian loan to German bankers (Mendelssohn, Comptoir d’Escompte, Crédit Lyonnais). This excluded the Rothschilds from the vast Russian bond market for over twelve years, ceding ground to French competitors who built the financial foundation of the Franco-Russian Alliance. Disraeli “made up his mind to consult Messrs. Rothschild confidentially” on Russian undertakings; Derby complained that Shuvalov “finds the Rothschilds acquainted with everything that goes on.”

“I go to the House every morning and when I say ‘No’ to every scheme and enterprise submitted to me, I return home at night carefree and contented. But when I agree to any proposal, I am immediately filled with anxiety.” — Natty Rothschild

The Rothschilds dominated foreign government bond issuance in the City. Between 1865 and 1914, N.M. Rothschild & Sons was responsible for over a quarter of all foreign public issues (£1,085 million of £4,082 million total). Over 90% were public sector. Surprisingly, imperial issues accounted for only 6% of their business — they showed no preference for bonds guaranteed by British political control. Their geographic spread was wide: Europe 45.6%, North America 22.5%, Latin America 14.6%, Middle East 6.1%. Combined Rothschild capital rose from £34.4 million in 1874 to £38 million by 1887, peaking at £41.5 million in 1899. N.M. Rothschild remained far and away the largest private bank in the City of London, though profits as a percentage of capital declined from 9.8% in the 1870s to 3.9% by 1900–09.

The British occupation of Egypt (1882) was facilitated by Rothschild intelligence. Alphonse relayed to London that France “would not object to firm action by Britain” against Arabi Pasha — the key intelligence enabling unilateral British action. When British ships bombarded Alexandria in July 1882, Alphonse was “delighted.” After Wolseley’s victory at Tel-el-Kebir, four major Egyptian bond issues worth approximately £50 million followed. Under Evelyn Baring (Lord Cromer), Egyptian debt fell from a £106 million peak to £94 million by 1913. Gladstone himself held £45,000 of Ottoman Egyptian bonds bought at 38, which rose to 97 by 1891 — a capital gain of more than 130%. He called Turkish state bankruptcy “the greatest of all political crimes.”

British overseas investment rose from £370 million in 1860 to £3.9 billion by 1913, around a third of total British wealth. Britain accounted for 44% of total foreign investment on the eve of World War I. The Rothschilds rode this wave while remaining focused on government bonds rather than private enterprise. When Guinness sought to float on the stock market in 1886, the London house refused and Barings made £500,000 in profit. Natty explained his philosophy: “The whirring wheels may drag your whole body in after the finger.” The Rothschilds kept their distance from the London Underground, the Channel Tunnel, and the telephone — they still communicated London-to-Paris by handwritten letter.

Natty left £2.5 million at death in 1915 — the richest of his generation, but at least thirteen other British millionaires left as much. J.P. Morgan’s estate was roughly £24 million. Friedrich Gentz had described the Rothschild principle back in 1827: “never to strive for excessive profits in their undertakings, to give each of their operations clear limits… to insulate themselves from the play of happenstance.” The income earned on foreign investments more than matched the export of new capital, consistently exceeding the trade deficit throughout this period. Ferguson draws a parallel between the Rothschilds’ relative decline and Britain’s: both were accused of “entrepreneurial failure,” but both were adapting rationally to a world where capital export generated higher returns than domestic industry.

Chapter 10: Party Politics

The Rothschilds drifted from Liberal to Conservative driven by two issues: imperialism and Irish Home Rule. The shift was gradual. Mayer and Anthony remained firm Liberals — Anthony declared in 1866 that “the sooner we are rid of the colonies, the better for England.” Even Lionel’s sons began as Liberals: Leo first stood for the hustings in 1865 asking voters “whether you would rather be ruled by Palmerston, Russell and Gladstone or Derby, Disraeli and Malmesbury.” Gladstone maintained frequent contacts — dining at 148 Piccadilly in 1869 and 1870, corresponding with Emma (Natty’s wife) on theology and Goethe, and visiting Tring as late as 1893. But Natty’s definitive break came over Ireland. He told Hartington on November 29, 1885: “Gladstone’s name might well be changed to Ichabod” — Hebrew for “The glory is departing from Israel.” In April 1886 he openly declared himself at a Liberal Unionist meeting at the Westminster Palace Hotel, urging Churchill and Chamberlain to campaign in Scotland — “the old man’s dung hill.”

The relationship with Disraeli had always been the Rothschilds’ most important political connection. During the 1867 Reform debates, Disraeli and Lionel dined regularly after the House, exchanging political confidences. Disraeli was “remarkably candid” about his legislative programme, and Lionel actively leaked opposition intelligence to assist him. Disraeli’s finances were a mess — a Yorkshire landowner named Andrew Montagu bought up all his debts for a £57,000 mortgage at 3%. After Lionel’s death in 1879, his sons replied to Disraeli’s condolences saying their father “looked upon you as his ‘dearest friend.’” When Disraeli died on April 19, 1881, Natty carried out his last wishes — burial at Hughenden with his wife, declining the public funeral Gladstone proposed. Disraeli had valued Lionel precisely because he straddled party lines: “Baron Rothschild… is a Liberal, and… knows everything.”

Ferdinand’s remarkable 1885 letter to Dilke captured the Rothschild ambivalence about politics: “I am not as you think by nature a conservative. Conservatism has been the ruin of several foreign countries and liberal politics have been the making of England. To liberalism we — you — owe everything… But I would cheer the Union Jack planted on every island of the Polynees, on every crag of the Himalayas, on every minaret of the East.” The French Rothschilds navigated the Third Republic with characteristic pragmatism, maintaining relations with all factions from Orléanist aristocrats to moderate republicans. They funded moderate candidates while keeping channels open to the monarchist right — a two-track strategy that ensured survival regardless of who held power.

“Ferdinand is equally struck with the fact that the Liberal party has split into several sections, none of which has any definite programme… there is no alternative to Imperialism but anarchy.” — Ferdinand de Rothschild to Dilke

Lord Randolph Churchill became another key Rothschild political connection. His spectacular rise and fall — Chancellor of the Exchequer at thirty-seven, resigned and marginalised within a year — was intimately entangled with Rothschild finances. Between 1881 and 1887, Charlotte’s sons issued Indian railway shares worth £6.4 million. Churchill as Secretary for India wanted Rothschilds as bankers: “I shall fight a great battle against Currie to place it in the hands of the Rothschilds, whose financial knowledge is as great as that of the Bank of England is small.” The Burma Ruby Mines flotation of 1889 was “immensely successful” — the crowd was so large Natty “reputedly had to climb up a ladder to get into the bank,” and shares went to a 300% premium. But Churchill died owing “the astonishing sum of £66,902”; Natty’s bankrolling after 1886 was “primarily an act of friendship as syphilis inexorably took its toll.”

Rosebery’s brief premiership (1894–95) represented the high point of Rothschild political influence. As Hannah’s widower and a former habitué of Mentmore, he was the closest thing to a Rothschild Prime Minister. He initially refused a Cabinet post in 1884, citing “the extreme delicacy of my relation to that question, for although I am not a member of the House of Rothschild, I am allied to it as closely as possible by kinship and friendship.” As Foreign Secretary in January 1893, Rosebery used Reginald Brett to communicate to Natty and Alfred the government’s intention to reinforce the Egyptian garrison — they learned it “before reading it in the papers.” He was allotted £50,000 of the new Egyptian loan — paid “in accordance with Rosebery’s wishes” into Hannah’s account at the Bank.

Buckinghamshire politics were a Rothschild fiefdom unique in Victorian England. Their estates, tenants, and employment created a political machine that opponents found intolerable. Local Whig hostility was open: Lord Carrington called them “the Red Sea”; Acton Tindal resisted “the circumcision” of the Aylesbury party. Anti-Semitic campaigning intensified — Russell (Liberal candidate) “attacked Dizzy as a Jew, a Jingo & something else beginning with a J” (the word was “Juggler”). Opponents accused them of buying votes, not entirely without basis: Charlotte documented the sums “cashed up” for candidates, and Lord Grey “made no secret of his determination to carry his election by money.” The family contributed “£12,000 a year and large sums at Elections” according to Conservative chairman Steel-Maitland.

The Austrian Rothschilds faced different political dynamics. Albert was declared hoffähig in 1887 — the first time such a privilege had been conceded to persons of the Jewish religion in Austria. Nathaniel mixed socially with Viennese aristocracy, addressed with the familiar “du” by Count Wilczek, who regarded him as “an unusually charming man.” The relationship with the Prince of Wales (future Edward VII) remained the family’s most important social connection in England. Natty had met the Prince at Cambridge in 1861, and for the next four decades the Rothschilds managed his debts — primarily a discreetly hushed-up £160,000 mortgage on Sandringham — and entertained him lavishly. The Prince was a regular at Waddesdon, Mentmore, Tring, and Halton. When he fell down the stairs and broke his leg at Waddesdon in 1898, it made national newspapers. Alfred produced opera stars like Nellie Melba and Sarah Bernhardt at his dinners; Ferdinand knew exactly how to amuse the heir to the throne.

Chapter 11: The Risks and Returns of Empire (1885–1902)

The Barings Crisis of 1890 demonstrated both Rothschild conservatism and Rothschild power. Barings had overextended in Argentine loans, with profits averaging 13% of capital in the 1880s masking a fatally narrow base: just £2.9 million in capital against £21 million in liabilities. The fatal deal was a £2 million share issue for the Buenos Aires Water and Drainage Company — Barings failed to place more than £150,000 with the public. Alphonse had remarked two years earlier that Argentina “would have to grow rapidly very rich indeed” to service its debt. Natty initially argued for letting Barings go under — Russian deposits withdrawn from Barings had ended up at New Court, and supporting a rescue might have triggered a run on the Rothschilds themselves. He met Salisbury on November 12, warning that a catastrophe “would put an end to the commercial habit of transacting all business of the world by bills on London.” Lidderdale told him bluntly: “We can get on without you.” Natty was “shamed” into agreeing. By the end of a 24-hour window, £10 million was assembled (later £17 million, though only £7.5 million was actually needed). Natty arranged £3 million in gold from the Banque de France, boosting Bank of England reserves from their £11 million low to £16.6 million.

The Rothschilds survived similar exposure to emerging-market debt because of their higher capital ratios. London house capital was £5.9 million versus Barings’ £2.9 million, and the capital-to-liabilities ratio never fell below 19.5% compared to Barings’ 14%. Brazilian bonds were just 2.4% of assets in 1886. When Emperor Pedro was overthrown by republican revolution in 1889 — “it took the Rothschilds completely by surprise” — the London house lost “only” roughly £740,000 between 1890 and 1893. In 1898 they dictated terms of the Brazilian Funding Loan, consolidating obligations secured on customs receipts and demanding rigorous retrenchment. The lesson was visceral: Barings, once the Rothschilds’ greatest rival, was reduced to begging for rescue and reconstituted Barings only repaid advances in 1894.

Mining became the great new frontier — “the most important change in their mode of operation since their decision to become involved in railway finance in the 1830s.” The Exploration Company (founded 1886, relaunched 1889 with £300,000 capital) paid 265% total dividends on initial paid-up capital by 1895 and issued shares for 23 companies worth £20.7 million. The portfolio was global: Rio Tinto copper (over 10% of world production), De Beers diamonds (98% of South African output), Burma Ruby Mines, Le Nickel in New Caledonia, Penarroya (80% of Spanish silver, 60% of lead by 1913), and BNITO oil in Baku (roughly a third of Russian oil output at peak). In 1911 the French house exchanged its entire Russian oil operation for shares in Royal Dutch and Shell, becoming the largest shareholders in each.

“We can get on without you.” — Bank of England Governor Lidderdale to Natty during the Barings rescue

Cecil Rhodes drew the Rothschilds into South African gold and diamond mining. On August 4, 1887, Natty drove a hard bargain: Rothschilds advanced £750,000 for 50,000 new De Beers shares at £15 each, plus £200,000 in debentures, with a commission of £100,000 plus half the difference between £15 and the London market price. By March 1888, De Beers Consolidated controlled 98% of South African diamond output. Natty acquired 5,754 shares personally (versus Rhodes’s 4,000) and was the second biggest shareholder by 1899 with 31,666 shares. De Beers annual dividends reached 40%. Natty told Rhodes: “The history of the De Beers Company is simply a fairy tale.” But he firmly opposed using De Beers funds for the British South Africa Company: “if it became known that Debeers Company lent money to the Chartered Company, some Debeers shareholders might move for an injunction.” The Jameson Raid of December 1895 “appalled the Rothschilds, who had no inkling of the plans for a coup.”

The Boer War (1899–1902) was a watershed. Alfred telegraphed Kruger directly in June 1899 urging franchise reform, and at Hartington’s suggestion sent a telegram to Samuel Marks in Pretoria effectively reformulating British policy without Chamberlain or Salisbury’s authorisation. Salisbury repudiated this, fearing “subterranean entanglement.” When diplomacy failed and war came anyway, J.P. Morgan displaced the Rothschilds in organising British war finance. The first “Khaki loan” was an open market sale bypassing New Court entirely. When the government needed a massive £60 million consol issue, Morgan secured a commission twice the London banks’ rate. “The distinctly un-German and un-Jewish figure of Pierpont Morgan was the principal victor: for the first time in over a century, the British government had been forced to borrow a large sum from a foreign power to wage a war in its own empire.” New Year’s Eve 1900 at Mentmore: Rosebery proposed “prosperity to the House of Rothschild” in a touching speech that “elicited tears from Natty and Leo.”

The Rothschilds played a central role in maintaining the gold standard. They partnered first with Joseph Seligman, then with Junius Morgan’s group, issuing over £267 million in US bonds between 1873 and 1877 to enable gold standard adoption. The 1893 Cleveland gold crisis saw Rothschilds and Morgans jointly place $62.3 million of US 4% bonds — bought at 104.5, sold at 112.25 (price later rising to 119). “Tales of profits of $6 million being made in the space of twenty-two minutes were grist to the Populist mill.” The Rothschilds were not strict monometallists but “multimetallists” — Alfred surprised everyone at the 1892 Brussels Monetary Conference with a compromise plan. Harcourt observed: “The name of Rothschild will carry a weight which no other could command in the monetary world.”

The London Gold Fix was born at New Court on September 12, 1919. The Rothschilds advanced £3 17s 9d per standard ounce to South African mine companies (roughly half of world gold output), pooled any premium, and remitted to mines every six months. They chaired the twice-daily fixing until 2004, giving them a central role in the global monetary system that persisted long after their dominance in bond markets had faded. They liked gold for the same reason the rest of the City did: fearing that if the pound floated freely, London’s financial supremacy would pass irrevocably to New York.

By the turn of the century, the Rothschilds’ mining empire was vast but their core acceptance business was lagging badly behind competitors. Annual acceptances averaged just £2.7 million for 1890–1914, compared to £5.6 million for Barings, £7.2 million for Schröders, and £9 million for Kleinworts. In 1903, New Court had total assets of £25 million; ten years later it was unchanged while Schröders had grown to £19.1 million and Barings to £15.8 million. The family’s conservatism kept them solvent but cost them market share in every metric except raw capital. Edward Hamilton named Natty one of the “first counsellors” any new Chancellor should consult — but the days when Rothschild financial power could shape government policy were drawing to a close.

Chapter 12: Finances and Alliances (1885–1906)

Alfred worked persistently to build an Anglo-German alliance, using the “Schwabach-Rothschild channel” through Paul von Schwabach of Bleichröder in Berlin and Ambassador Hatzfeldt as unofficial diplomatic intermediaries. Alfred genuinely believed that an entente between Britain and Germany could stabilise Europe and protect Rothschild interests on both sides of the Channel. But Salisbury’s deep suspicion killed the project. His private secretary McDonnell dismissed Alfred as “suffering from megalomania” for accepting a German decoration (the Order of the Crown, First Class) and wrote a satirical memo mocking the German ambassador’s threats. The Times’ Berlin correspondent Saunders exploded: “I know the power of German influence… It is not business. It is dining, shooting, toasts, finance, honours, marriages, dynastic friendships.”

The Berlin-Baghdad Railway debacle exposed the limits of Rothschild diplomacy. Georg von Siemens of Deutsche Bank had won the concession in 1899, and Natty advised the British government to take shares, as with Suez. But right-wing press attacks on the 35% German investor share versus 25% British killed British participation in March 1903. The Kaiser’s “Kruger telegram” of 1896 — congratulating the Boers on repelling the Jameson Raid — had already poisoned Anglo-German relations beyond repair. Alfred warned that German press attacks amounted to a “‘pinprick’ policy — although a pin is not a very impressive instrument, repeated pricks may cause a wound.” Chamberlain’s rhetoric about a “New Triple Alliance between the Teutonic race and the two great branches of the Anglo-Saxon race” went nowhere.

The Entente Cordiale with France offered a better deal: final recognition of Britain’s position in Egypt in exchange for French control of Morocco. Central bank cooperation underpinned the entente. In November 1906, with the Bank rate at 6% and gold draining to Brazil and India, Natty and Edouard arranged £600,000 in gold sovereigns from the Banque de France, then another £400,000, topped up with £600,000 more — total: £1.4 million in gold swaps that may have averted a further Bank rate increase. The rate fell from 6% to 4% by April 1907. When the 1907 crisis hit (the worst pre-1914), Governor William Campbell sent for Natty personally, and Rothschilds provided £400,000 of a £3 million gold transfer. When J.P. Morgan directly approached the Banque de France for American market aid, he was rebuffed — vindication of the Rothschild cross-Channel link.

“I always dread the reopening of any Eastern Question.” — Natty Rothschild on Ottoman finance

Natty’s principled refusal to lend to Russia without concessions on Jewish rights was one of the most remarkable episodes of financial diplomacy. After the 1905 pogroms, Natty wrote to The Times denouncing “the unspeakable calamities which have befallen the Jews in Russia… mercilessly struck down and massacred.” When Raffalovich offered an “International Syndicate” willing to accommodate the Czar “with untold millions, anything between £60 to £120,000,000,” Natty refused: “we could & would not help Russia in her need until she had done for our co-religionists that which would make them happy & contented subjects.” Stolypin responded with contempt, blaming Jews for their own persecution: “if the Jews had equal rights they would soon hold all the land in Russia.” The massive £89 million Russian loan of 1906 was arranged by Revelstoke and Morgan — Rothschilds pointedly stayed out. Natty gloated when bond prices slumped.

The Russo-Japanese War (1904–05) vindicated those who had avoided Russian paper. Jacob Schiff of Kuhn, Loeb financed Japan’s victory; Natty was jealous of Schiff’s credit, complaining that Schiff was now a “welcome & much honoured guest at Tokio” who had “incense poured on his devoted head.” But Natty praised Japan: “their dense & intelligent population, their loyalty zeal & intelligence, will soon bring them to the front rank both in commerce & manufacture.” The London and Paris houses eventually participated in £25 million and £11.5 million Japanese loans in 1906 and 1907.

The Creditanstalt in Vienna faced growing challenges as the Habsburg Empire strained under competing nationalities and rising military budgets. Albert was described as “a peculiar mix of gentleman and brutal potentate” who exerted influence through interlocking directorships — “a sort of tyranny.” His death in 1911 left the Austrian branch without strong leadership at the worst possible moment. Son Louis modernised, but the Bodencreditanstalt broke ranks. The inter-house partnership had already been dissolved; the Vienna Rothschilds were increasingly on their own. The Creditanstalt would survive until 1931, when its collapse triggered the cascading financial crisis that turned depression into catastrophe.

Capital flows were driving diplomatic alliances more than alliances were driving capital flows. French savings flooded into Russian bonds because Russian yields exceeded French domestic returns — not because the Franco-Russian Alliance required it. By 1914, French investors held an estimated 25 billion francs in Russian securities — a colossal exposure wiped out by the Bolshevik repudiation of tsarist debts. Natty’s key insight on the gold standard captured the interconnection: “the whole trade of the world is carried on by bills on London” — £300–400 million in drafts always running on London, “probably more than half for foreign account.” The Rothschilds’ refusal to participate in Russian lending, driven by principled stance on Jewish rights, looked like a costly error in the short term but proved prescient when the entire Russian bond market went to zero after 1917.

Ferguson shows that the diplomatic alignments of 1904–07 — the Entente Cordiale, the Anglo-Russian Convention, the tightening of the Franco-Russian alliance — all had deep financial roots. Net creditor nations (Britain, France) gravitated together; net borrowers (Russia, Italy) gravitated toward their lenders. France was Russia’s main source of external finance, making the Franco-Russian entente economically logical despite having “less in common in terms of their internal politics than any other two-power combination.” The Rothschilds were at the centre of this web, connecting London and Paris through family ties that no joint-stock bank could replicate.

Chapter 13: The Military-Financial Complex (1906–1914)

The arms race had financial dimensions that Ferguson documents in extraordinary detail. Military expenditure by the Dual Entente (Britain, France, Russia) rose 40.8% between 1904 and 1913; the Dual Alliance (Germany, Austria-Hungary) increased spending by 158.5%. Defence as a share of net national product rose across the board: Britain from 2.0% (1873) to 3.2% (1913), France 3.1% to 4.8%, Russia to 5.1%, Germany 2.4% to 3.9%. The Rothschilds were involved through investments in Vickers and Maxim-Nordenfelt — in 1888 the London house issued £225,000 in shares for the Naval Construction and Armaments Company, then £1.9 million for the merger of Maxim Gun Company with Nordenfelt. Natty retained a substantial shareholding and exerted direct influence over management. The Maxim gun was used devastatingly from Sudan to Matabeleland. In Austria, the Witkowitz ironworks supplied iron and steel to the Austrian navy and later bullets to the army, though Albert had “not the slightest interest in the industrial installations that his house possesses… considers them as a disagreeable duty.”

Lloyd George’s People’s Budget of 1909 represented the greatest domestic threat to the Rothschilds in a generation. The budget proposed increasing “unearned” income tax to 1s 2d in the pound, a super-tax on incomes above £5,000, higher inheritance tax, and levies on land values. Natty organised a letter to Asquith signed by 21 City leaders and chaired a protest meeting at Cannon Street Hotel, passing a resolution that the budget would “weaken the security in all private property.” Lloyd George singled him out in devastating speeches at the Holborn Restaurant: “Really, in all of these things we are having too much Lord Rothschild.” At Walworth Hall, Lloyd George drew a more pointed barb: “There had been a very cruel king in the past who ordered Lord Rothschild’s ancestors to make bricks without straw. That was a much easier job than making Dreadnoughts without money.” The budget crisis led to the Parliament Act of 1911, permanently reducing the power of the Lords.

Natty found himself increasingly marginalised. Edward Hamilton had warned in 1902 that Natty “has become so strong a party man, he will now be ‘out of it’ whenever the other side comes in.” The 1906 Liberal landslide — 400 out of 670 seats versus the Conservatives’ 157 — was “unexpectedly bad.” The Conservatives took him for granted; the Liberals treated him as an enemy. The family contributed “£12,000 a year and large sums at Elections,” but Bonar Law replaced Balfour as leader in November 1911 and Natty barely knew him. His intelligence network still functioned, but knowledge without access was sterile. In a poignant 1912 essay, Natty wrote: “What have we not got in common with Germany? Nothing perhaps except their army and our navy. But a combination of the most powerful military nation with the most powerful naval nation ought to… ensure universal peace.”

“Too much Lord Rothschild.” — Lloyd George during the People’s Budget debates, 1909

The July Crisis of 1914 provides Ferguson’s most dramatic financial narrative. Of 25 letters between New Court and rue Laffitte from June 29 to July 23, only five mentioned Franz Ferdinand’s assassination. Natty dismissed it as “a sad example” of “Servian brutality.” On July 22 he was still confident Serbia would “eat humble pie.” By July 27: “No one thinks and talks about anything else but the European situation.” Austrian declaration of war on Serbia triggered market collapse — “all the foreign Banks and particularly the German ones took a very large amount of money out of the Stock Exchange.” Consols plunged from 74 to 69.5 on July 29. The Bank rate doubled to 8% on July 31, then rose again. The stock exchange closed — the worst crisis in a hundred years.

Natty’s last attempt at peace came on July 31, when he urged France to restrain Russia: “France is Russia’s greatest creditor… we hope you will do your best to bring any influence you may have, to bear upon your statesmen even at the last moment, to prevent this hideous struggle from taking place.” On August 1 he appealed personally to the Kaiser — who minuted “an old and much respected acquaintance of mine, some 75 to 80 years old.” It was futile: communications were cut before a reply was possible. The London house lost £1.5 million in the crisis — 23% of its capital — no other major City bank was so drastically affected. Lloyd George sought Natty’s advice; Natty interrupted: “Mr Lloyd George, this is no time to recall those things. What can I do to help?” He helped broker the compromise: gold convertibility maintained but Bank rate lowered. When Edouard telegraphed J.P. Morgan requesting $100 million, Morgan “had drawn a blank” — he had not forgotten being refused a share of the South African loan twelve years earlier. “United on the battlefields, we are also united in finance!” Edouard telegraphed New Court. But what united the Rothschilds after 1914 was decline.

Bond yields of the major powers diverged sharply in the final pre-war years, pricing in the rising probability of conflict that politicians refused to acknowledge. Russian and Austrian bonds sold off steadily while British and French paper held relatively firm. The spread between Austrian and German yields widened as investors distinguished between creditor and debtor nations within the alliance systems. The bond market, as so often in Rothschild history, was ahead of the diplomats. Investors who could read the yield curves in 1912 and 1913 knew that European peace was deteriorating even as politicians assured their publics that the alliance system guaranteed stability.

Ferguson shows that the Rothschilds’ inability to prevent World War I was the ultimate failure of their founding strategy. Mayer Amschel’s multinational structure had been designed to make war financially impossible. But by 1914, governments could finance wars through taxation, central bank credit, and public subscriptions — the Rothschild veto had been bypassed.

The national debt data tells the story of why the Rothschild peace mechanism failed. Britain’s debt was just 3.3 times revenue in 1913, with debt service consuming only 13.3% of expenditure. France’s numbers were slightly worse but still manageable. Germany spent 33.4% on debt service — high but not prohibitive. These were all manageable numbers compared to the Ottoman or Egyptian debts that had triggered financial crises in the 1870s. Governments had fiscal headroom to fight a major war, and when the moment came in August 1914, they used it without hesitation. The bond market could signal danger, but it could no longer veto war.

Chapter 14: Deluges (1915–1945)

The interwar period saw the Rothschilds navigate a succession of catastrophes. The London house issued bonds worth £28 million for the Brazilian federal government plus £17.5 million for Brazilian states and railways; Chile accounted for another £10 million. The American ambassador in Brazil reported that “the Rothschilds have so mortgaged Brazil’s financial future that… they will place every obstacle in the way of her entering into banking relations with any other house.” But Vargas’s coup in 1930 undercut negotiations, and in September 1931 he suspended foreign debt payments. Sterling bonds were not fully liquidated until 1962. In France, the 1934 Stavisky affair triggered an attempted right-wing coup, and Daladier launched a tirade against “two hundred families” allegedly “masters of the French economy.” The 1936 Popular Front government pledged “liberation of the State from the grips of financial feudalism” and nationalised the railways, though René Mayer’s hard bargaining meant the companies came out ahead.

The Creditanstalt crisis of May 1931 was the single most devastating blow. The great Viennese bank revealed losses of 140 million schillings — roughly £4 million — against paid-up capital of just 125 million. Actual losses were probably closer to 160 million schillings, most attributable to the merger with the Bodencreditanstalt which the government had insisted on. Louis had bailed out the Bodencreditanstalt just as his great-grandfather Salomon had bailed out Arnstein & Eskeles on the eve of the 1848 crisis. The Vienna house held roughly 16.7 million schillings of Creditanstalt’s capital; the Paris house lent a further 136 million francs. Yet the rescue failed to avert panic spreading from Vienna to Hungary, Germany, and all of Europe. Edmond’s chilling response: “What is happening in the Vienna bank does not concern us… not a penny more.” In September 1931 a run on the Bank of England ended the pound’s brief return to gold.

Walter Rothschild’s prescient 1931 analysis argued the gold standard’s breakdown “is owing to the greed of certain countries for gold” — the US and France were “sterilising” additions to reserves, refusing the central bank cooperation that had made the pre-1914 system work. Black Thursday (October 24, 1929) had wiped $30 billion off stocks worth $80 billion; the Dow fell from its peak of 381 to a final trough of 50 by May 1932. The London house survived involvement with Ivan Kreuger — issuing $10 million in shares that became worthless when the Swedish financier committed suicide in 1932. Goldschmidt-Rothschild & Co. in Frankfurt was handed over to the Reichs-Kredit-Gesellschaft — one of the lesser casualties of the German banking crisis.

“A Jew under Pétain, a pariah under Mitterrand — for me, that’s enough.” — Guy de Rothschild

The rise of Nazism brought existential danger. In December 1933, Frankfurt’s Rothschildallee was renamed Karolingerallee. After the Anschluss (March 1938), Louis de Rothschild attempted to leave Vienna but had his passport confiscated. He was arrested and taken to Gestapo headquarters at the Hotel Metropol on Morzin-Platz, where he found himself with former Chancellor Schuschnigg. When Himmler sent ornate French furniture to his prison, Louis complained it made his cell look like “a Cracow bordello.” He was released only after the family surrendered the Witkowitz ironworks to Göring — Eugène’s hopes of selling to the Czechoslovak state for £10 million were dashed by German partition. In Germany, Maximilian, aged ninety-five, was forced to sell his art collection for 2.3 million reichsmarks and donate 25% of remaining assets as an “atonement payment.”

Alfred Rosenberg’s Einsatzstab Reichsleiter Rosenberg seized 3,978 items from nine Rothschild locations in France alone, part of a staggering total haul of 21,903 works. The most prized: Vermeer’s Astronomer, Boucher’s Madame de Pompadour, plus thirty masterpieces by Hals and Rembrandt — all earmarked for Hitler. Göring visited the Jeu de Paume in November 1940 as Hitler’s “buyer,” grabbing Dutch and French works from Edouard’s collection and a Memling Madonna for his wife. The Vichy regime targeted them too: Pétain’s decrees restricted Jewish rights, and the aim was to pre-empt the Germans, not protect the Rothschilds.

Family members scattered across the world. Robert fled to Montreal; Edouard (now in his seventies) left France via Spain and Portugal for America. Guy narrowly avoided capture and fled via Morocco — his ship was torpedoed in the Atlantic but he survived. Robert’s sons Alain and Elie were taken prisoner; Elie ended up in Colditz. Guy earned the Croix de Guerre. Two family members died in the Holocaust: Aranka, who perished at Buchenwald, and Lili (comtesse de Chambure), who asked “Why should the Germans harm me? I am from an old French Catholic family” — arrested by Gestapo in July 1944, sent to Ravensbrück, brutally murdered. “The blackest of ironies that the only person named Rothschild killed by the Nazis was not a Jew.” Major Edmund de Rothschild led his battery into Mannheim through an archway bearing the inscription judenrein. People shouted: “Die Juden kommen!”

The London house shifted to domestic corporate finance — issues for London underground railway companies from 1928, a £2 million deal for Shell-Mex House in 1930, and a £9.36 million Woolworths share issue in 1931 that nearly collapsed when a minor City panic struck on the night before completion — staff worked all night behind locked doors. The grand country houses were sold or abandoned: Halton went to the RAF for £112,000, Aston Clinton became a hotel, Gunnersbury a public park. Walter sold nearly his entire Tring collection of stuffed birds to the American Museum of Natural History for $225,000 — less than $1 per specimen.

By 1945, the German, Austrian, and French Rothschild branches had been devastated beyond anything the family had experienced in two centuries. The Frankfurt house was gone — wound up in 1901, its remnants confiscated, the old Fahrgasse office and the Stammhaus in the Bornestrasse destroyed by Allied bombs. The Vienna house was gone, its partners arrested or exiled. The French house had been nationalised by Vichy and its assets scattered. On May 10, 1941, incendiary bombs hit New Court during a ferocious bombardment that literally surrounded it by fire. The Royal Mint refinery was converted to artillery-parts production; Exbury was taken over by the navy and temporarily renamed HMS Mastodon. London house capital had contracted from £8 million pre-war to £1.5 million. The question was whether a dynasty that had survived every previous crisis could reinvent itself when its multinational structure had been shattered.

Epilogue

Anthony dedicated himself to conserving what remained. He commuted daily from Leighton Buzzard to Euston to New Court. Harold Nicolson described lunch and then being “hurried out” at 2:30 as “the great wheels of the Maison Rothschild revolve.” But Anthony’s approach was fatalistic: “They know where we live. If they want to do business with us let them come and talk to us.” Partners arrived at the Room between 10 and 10:30 a.m. and spent the morning perusing mail “to see if there was anything likely to result in some business.” All documents required partner signatures; when Edmund questioned this practice, Anthony’s reply was invariably: “I’m afraid I don’t quite understand this… No. You wouldn’t.” London house capital had contracted from £8 million pre-war to £1.5 million, with a 1947 restructuring creating £1 million of voteless preference shares and £500,000 of ordinary voting shares. Anthony retained 60% of the ordinary shares, ensuring his dominance.

The post-war world offered little scope for traditional Rothschild banking. The Bretton Woods system of fixed exchange rates constrained international bond markets. British socialism under Attlee was actively hostile to the City; a Labour supporter declared in 1948: “I do not believe that people should be allowed to have a lot of money unless they have earned it; being the son of a rich man is not a good enough reason.” This hostility explains why Victor, the most intellectually gifted of the next generation, kept his distance from New Court, eventually leaving academia for Royal Dutch Shell in 1959. Victor had been influenced at Cambridge by Anthony Blunt and Guy Burgess, and years of speculation about whether he was the “fifth man” — a Soviet mole — haunted him. He found communism “rather dull” but the circumstantial evidence was uncomfortable: he had recommended Blunt to MI5 in August 1940, and Blunt and Burgess rented his Bentinck Street house during the war.

The Brinco project in Newfoundland — sixty thousand square miles leased for twenty years — was the most ambitious post-war venture. Lord Leathers said: “You did Suez, so why can’t you do Newfoundland?” Churchill himself bought ten thousand Brinco shares. But the project never escaped political tangles with Quebec and Canadian nationalism. In July 1960, David Colville became the first non-family partner, ending a century and a half of tradition. In September 1970, the partnership was finally incorporated as N.M. Rothschild & Sons Limited, ending unlimited liability. Issued share capital was just £10 million with £2 million in reserves — a Japanese newspaper reported construction of a new sixty-storey building, “typical of the outside world’s exaggerated impression.”

The rivalry between Evelyn and Jacob Rothschild defined the late twentieth century. Jacob’s 1965 declaration — “We must try to make ourselves as much a bank of brains as of money” — signalled his ambitions. His plan to merge N.M. Rothschild with S.G. Warburg (codenamed “War and Peace”) was blocked by Evelyn and Victor. Jacob departed New Court in 1980 and built an empire through Charterhouse J. Rothschild — market cap £400 million, more than double N.M. Rothschild. He acquired 9.6% of TV-am, 50% of New York merchant bank L.F. Rothschild, Unterberg, Towbin, and 29.9% of broker Kitcat & Aitken. But it unravelled: failed mergers, sold stakes, and L.F. Rothschild filed for bankruptcy in 1987. Jacob turned to public work — Chairman of the National Heritage Memorial Fund (1992–98).

“We must try to make ourselves as much a bank of brains as of money.” — Jacob Rothschild, 1965

Privatisation proved the London house’s renaissance. Victor’s role as head of Edward Heath’s Central Policy Review Staff (1970–73) brought Rothschilds back into direct contact with politicians. In February 1982, Amersham International — the first true privatisation — was oversubscribed twenty-three times at a fixed price of 142 pence. The British Gas “Tell Sid” campaign (1985–86) saw four million investors apply for £5.6 billion in shares, with underwriting commissions as low as 0.075%. By 1988 the firm was advising on eleven privatisations in eight countries. In 1996–97 they advised Brazil on Companhia Vale do Rio Doce, Zambia on copper, and Germany on Deutsche Telekom’s £6 billion flotation. Ferguson calls this “strongly reminiscent of its earlier role as the leading architect of the modern bond market.”

The French house was nationalised by Mitterrand in 1981 — thirty-nine banks with deposits above 1 billion francs taken into public ownership. Compensation was 450 million francs (£41 million), of which the family received a third. Guy de Rothschild wrote bitterly in Le Monde: “A Jew under Pétain, a pariah under Mitterrand, for me that’s enough.” But within three years, David and Edouard established a new Paris house via holding company Paris-Orléans Gestion. The government prohibited use of the family name, but share value trebled in the first two years. By 1986, under Chirac’s government, it became Rothschild & Cie Banque with capital of 150 million francs and roughly 15 billion francs under management — one of five leading French corporate finance firms.

Evelyn recreated the international structure through Rothschilds Continuation Holdings AG (Swiss-based), controlled by Rothschild Concordia AG. By 1997: nineteen principal firms across twenty-plus countries, with six family members holding thirty-seven board seats across fifteen component companies. In 1989, London and Paris Rothschilds opened Rothschild GmbH in Frankfurt — returning to the city where it all began. The Zurich crisis of 1991–92 — when rogue executive Jurg Heer authorised illegal loans costing 270 million Swiss francs, more than the firm’s entire capital — was contained by a 120.5 million franc injection. Unlike Barings, destroyed by Nick Leeson’s similar failures in 1995, the Rothschilds survived — one of only four elite City firms to retain independence.

Ferguson’s final assessment: shareholders’ equity of £460 million, total capital resources approximately £800 million. Far behind HSBC (£55 billion market cap) but comparable to Schroders. Capital grew from £12 million at incorporation (1970) to £460 million — nearly 400% adjusted for inflation. The secret of longevity was the multinational structure and the high ratio of capital to liabilities: “Part of the secret of long-run success in banking is, of course, not to go bust.” The family firm offered what modern corporations could not replicate: a longer time horizon, interests of future generations considered alongside present shareholders, trust and loyalty that endured across centuries. The final quote from the 1836 partnership agreement after Nathan’s death still resonated: “May our children and descendants in the future be guided by the same aim, so that with the constant maintenance of unity the House of Rothschild may blossom and grow into full ripeness.”

Trading & Investing Lessons

Capital Preservation Over Profit Maximisation

The Rothschild principle of low profitability and high capital ratios ensured survival when every aggressive competitor eventually blew up. Barings’ 13.6% return on capital looked impressive until 1890 revealed a capital base of just £2.9 million against £21 million in liabilities. The Rothschilds’ 3.9% return looked pedestrian until you measured it over two centuries of continuous operation.

The Recovery Trade

The Rothschilds consistently lost money during crises but made it back — and more — on the recovery. The French indemnity loans of 1871–72 were more profitable than the war losses were devastating. The pattern repeated with Egyptian bonds, the Barings rescue, and post-war privatisations. Surviving the crash is the prerequisite for profiting from the recovery.

Diversification as Insurance

The multinational structure created natural hedges. When Paris collapsed in 1848, London bailed it out. When Vienna failed in 1931, London survived. The geographical spread was the original portfolio diversification — more effective than any modern risk model.

Information Advantage Decays

The Rothschilds’ courier network gave them a decisive edge for decades. The telegraph destroyed it. They experimented with telephones in 1891 but continued writing letters by hand. The lesson: every information advantage is temporary. The firms that survive are those that find new edges, not those that cling to old ones.

Selling Too Soon Is a Strategy

Natty’s formula — “Yes, by selling too soon” — sounds like timidity but is actually a disciplined risk-management philosophy. Taking profits early and sleeping well is worth more over a multi-generational time horizon than maximising any single trade.

Political Risk Is the Dominant Risk

The Rothschilds lost more money to wars, revolutions, nationalisation, and confiscation than to any market crash. Their strategy of cultivating relationships across the political spectrum was not cynicism but rational risk management. The Nazis, not the bond market, ultimately posed the greatest threat to Rothschild wealth.

Key Takeaways

The Multinational Structure Was Both Strength and Weakness

The five-house system enabled arbitrage, mutual bail-outs, and unprecedented information flow. But it also created conflicts of interest as national rivalries intensified, ultimately proving unsustainable after the wars of 1859–71.

Decline Is Relative, Not Absolute

The Rothschilds remained the largest private bank in the City through 1914. Combined capital peaked at £41.5 million. They dominated government bond issuance with over 25% market share. The “decline” was only visible compared to the explosive growth of joint-stock banks and American rivals.

Family Firms Have Longer Time Horizons

The Rothschilds considered the interests of future generations alongside present shareholders. This longer time horizon explained their conservatism, which looked like weakness in boom times but proved decisive in every crisis.

Finance Follows Returns, Not Flags

The Rothschilds’ imperial investments were driven by yield, not patriotism. Only 6% of their issues were imperial. They showed no preference for British-controlled territories when independent states offered better returns.

Anti-Semitism Is the Constant Background

From Drumont to Hitler, the Rothschilds served as the personalisation of every conspiracy theory about Jewish financial power. Their wealth made them simultaneously the most prominent and most vulnerable Jews in Europe.

Adaptation Beats Optimisation

The family survived because they adapted — from courier networks to telegraphs, from sovereign bonds to mining, from private banking to privatisation advisory. Each generation found a new niche. The firms that died were those that optimised for the old world.

Contrarian Ideas

The Fourth Generation Was Not Incompetent

Conventional view: the Buddenbrooks pattern of generational decline explains Rothschild weakening. Ferguson’s counter: Natty, Alphonse, and Albert remained highly effective. The real problem was structural — joint-stock competition, national market segmentation, and the impossibility of retaining talented non-family employees.

British Economic “Decline” Was Also Overstated

The City of London was not draining capital from British industry. Capital exports generated returns that exceeded the trade deficit. The Rothschilds’ focus on foreign bonds was rational in a world where Britain accounted for 44% of total foreign investment.

The Rothschilds Were Not Imperialists

Only 6% of their London issues were imperial. They backed stable independent states (Brazil, Russia) as readily as colonies. Their political involvement in imperialism was driven by the need to protect bondholders, not by imperial ideology.

Zionism Was Rational, and the Rothschilds Were Wrong to Reject It

The Rothschilds opposed Herzl because a Jewish state would threaten their national identities as English, French, and Austrian citizens. Within fifty years, the German, Austrian, and French Rothschilds had all fallen victim to exactly the anti-Semitic onslaught Herzl predicted.

Proposals & Frameworks

The Rothschild Survival Framework

Ferguson identifies three mechanisms that explain the family’s extraordinary longevity: (1) multinational diversification enabling mutual bail-outs; (2) high capital ratios absorbing losses that destroyed competitors; (3) family governance with multi-generational time horizons that discouraged short-term risk-taking.

The Financial Diplomacy Model

The Rothschilds exercised political influence not through bribery or conspiracy but through their role as lender of last resort to governments. Access to Rothschild capital bought access to decision-makers. The model worked best when governments needed money and failed when they could borrow elsewhere.

The Bond Market as Peace Mechanism

The international bond market created financial interdependence between nations, raising the cost of war. This worked from 1815 to 1870 but failed when governments developed alternative funding sources (taxation, central bank credit, public subscription). The Rothschild veto on war finance was a powerful tool with an expiry date.

Relative Decline Is Not Absolute Decline

Ferguson’s analytical framework distinguishes between declining market share (which was real) and declining absolute capability (which was not). The Rothschilds remained the City’s largest private bank. They chose safety over growth — a rational strategy for a family with more to lose than to gain from aggressive risk-taking.

What Didn’t Fit

The Muscatel Grape

When Disraeli’s secretary Montagu Corry rushed to New Court to secure £4 million for the Suez Canal purchase, Lionel reportedly asked “What is your security?” Corry replied: “The British Government.” Lionel paused, ate a muscatel grape, and said: “You shall have it.” Whether apocryphal or not, the anecdote captures the Rothschild style perfectly: vast sums committed with theatrical nonchalance, backed by an instant assessment of political risk that no committee could replicate.

Gutle Schnapper’s Refusal to Move

The matriarch who founded the dynasty refused to leave the cramped house in the former Judengasse even as her sons became the richest family on earth. She lived to ninety-six, was visited by ambassadors in blue livery, and when asked about war rumours reportedly replied: “Nonsense — my boys won’t let them.” Her stubbornness epitomised the ghetto mentality that drove Rothschild ambition: never forget where you came from.

Ferdinand’s Evelina

Ferdinand married Lionel’s daughter Evelina in the supreme moment of Rothschild endogamy — 126 guests at 148 Piccadilly, Disraeli at the head table. Evelina died in childbirth a year later. Ferdinand never remarried: “Henceforth my life can merely be wrought with sorrow and anguish.” He built a children’s hospital and a mausoleum in her memory. The tragedy explains his lifelong melancholy and his compulsive collecting — filling Waddesdon with beauty to compensate for a loss nothing could fill.

Mary Anne Disraeli’s Will

Mary Anne burst into Charlotte’s drawing room, flung herself into her arms, and produced a will making six-year-old Evelina her sole beneficiary: “I love the Jews — I have attached myself to your children and she is my favourite.” This bizarre episode illuminates the intimate, slightly unhinged quality of the Disraeli-Rothschild friendship that shaped British politics for three decades.

The Zurich Rogue

In 1992, senior Rothschild Bank Zurich executive Jurg Heer was discovered to have authorised illegal loans totalling 270 million Swiss francs — more than the firm’s entire capital. The wider Rothschild structure injected 120.5 million francs to save the bank. Three years later, Barings was destroyed by a similar rogue-trading episode in Singapore. The Rothschilds survived because the multinational structure provided a backstop; Barings had no such safety net. It was the clearest modern proof of the founding principle: brothers who stick together cannot be broken.

About the Author

Niall Ferguson (born 1964 in Glasgow) is one of the most prominent and controversial historians of his generation. At the time of writing this book, he was Fellow and Tutor in Modern History at Jesus College, Oxford. He has since held professorships at Harvard, Stanford, and the London School of Economics, and is currently the Milbank Family Senior Fellow at the Hoover Institution.

Ferguson’s work spans financial history, imperial history, and the history of warfare. His previous books included The Pity of War and Virtual History: Alternatives and Counterfactuals. The two-volume House of Rothschild established his reputation as a financial historian of the first rank, drawing on unprecedented access to the Rothschild family archives. The New York Times named it a Notable Book of the Year.

Ferguson’s subsequent works — The Ascent of Money, Civilization, Kissinger, The Square and the Tower, and Doom — have continued to blend narrative history with quantitative analysis. His willingness to make controversial arguments (defending empire, questioning the costs of World War I) has made him a polarising figure, but his archival scholarship in the Rothschild volumes is universally respected.

Why This Book Matters

This is the most comprehensive history ever written of the world’s most famous banking dynasty during its most turbulent century and a half. Ferguson had unprecedented access to the Rothschild archives — letters, account books, partnership agreements — that no previous historian could consult. The result is a work that demolishes conspiracy theories while revealing a reality more interesting than any myth: a family that was extraordinarily wealthy and influential but also frequently wrong, occasionally desperate, and ultimately constrained by forces no amount of money could control.

For anyone interested in financial markets, the book demonstrates that the fundamental principles of banking — capital preservation, diversification, long time horizons, and the ability to survive crises — have not changed in two centuries. The Rothschild story is the longest continuous case study in the history of finance, and its lessons about the relationship between money, politics, and power remain as relevant today as they were when Mayer Amschel first lent money to the Elector of Hesse-Kassel.

The book is also essential reading on the history of anti-Semitism. Ferguson traces how the Rothschilds became the personalisation of every conspiracy theory about Jewish financial power — from Drumont’s Jewish France to the Populist “English Octopus” to Nazi confiscation. Understanding how these myths were constructed, and how they bore almost no relationship to the messy, human reality of Rothschild decision-making, is more important than ever in an age when conspiracy theories travel at the speed of the internet rather than the speed of a pamphlet.
