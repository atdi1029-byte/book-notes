Preface

The first volume ended with the revolutions of 1848 nearly destroying the Rothschild empire. Bond prices collapsed, the Vienna and Paris houses teetered on insolvency, and only bail-outs from London and Frankfurt preserved the multinational partnership. Yet the family survived what was the greatest financial crisis between 1815 and 1914. This second volume picks up the story at that moment of supreme vulnerability and traces it forward through 150 years of adaptation, relative decline, catastrophe, and reinvention.

Ferguson recaps the extraordinary rise from the Frankfurt Judengasse — the ghetto whose walls Napoleon literally demolished in 1796. Mayer Amschel Rothschild seized the chaos of revolutionary Europe to become fund manager for the exiled Elector of Hesse-Kassel, while his son Nathan moved from Manchester textiles to London finance. Nathan’s account with the British government totalled close to £10 million in 1815 alone. His supreme Meistergeschäft after Waterloo — buying British consols and riding the postwar stabilisation — realised more than £250,000 at a stroke.

“Keep your brothers together and you will become the richest people in Germany.” — Mayer Amschel on his 1812 deathbed

The multinational structure — five houses in London, Paris, Frankfurt, Vienna, and Naples — was the family’s greatest asset. It enabled arbitrage between markets, mutual bail-outs during crises, and by 1825 made them ten times the size of their nearest rival, Baring Brothers. The Rothschilds created the first truly international bond market, issuing Prussian bonds simultaneously in multiple cities with sterling denomination and sinking funds. They were, as Heinrich Heine put it, the prophets of money: “Money is the god of our time and Rothschild is his prophet.”

But the post-1848 world posed new threats. Bismarck, Cavour, and Palmerston elevated national self-interest above international balance. The Pereire brothers’ Crédit Mobilier broke the bond-market monopoly. The telegraph ended the Rothschilds’ courier advantage. Charlotte de Rothschild affirmed her belief “in a bright, European and Rothschildian future” — but nationalism and socialism would prove the greatest challenges, especially when combined.

Chapter 1: Charlotte’s Dream (1849–1858)

The four surviving sons of Mayer Amschel were old men by 1850: Amschel seventy-seven, Salomon seventy-six, Carl an ailing sixty-two. Only James remained indefatigable at fifty-six. Their mother Gutle Schnapper, born 1753, refused to leave the old house “zum grünen Schild” in the former Judengasse. Ludwig Borne praised her: “Look, there she lives, in that little house… and has no wish to leave her hereditary little castle.” She died May 7, 1849, in her ninety-sixth year. Bismarck dined with Amschel and found him “a pathetic old man… a poor man in his palace, a childless widower, cheated by his servants.”

The London partners wanted independence from continental commitments. Nat expressed “such a disgust to business” that he wanted “no more of any sort or description to transact.” James held the family together with sheer force of will. His passionate 1850 letter to Lionel warned that if unity broke, £3 million in capital would “fall into the hands of outsiders.” The 1852 partnership agreement renewed the system with modest decentralisation: partners informed each other of transactions over £830,000 and offered participations of up to 10%.

Endogamy continued relentlessly. Between 1848 and 1877, nine marriages took place within the family. Charlotte assessed prospective sons-in-law with brutal candour: Gustave was “stupid, easily intimidated and unable to string ten words together”; Alphonse “combines the extraordinary energy and vitality of our uncle with Betty’s facility for languages.” The rationale was blunt: “For us Jews, and particularly for us Rothschilds, it is better not to come into contact with other families, as it always leads to unpleasantness and costs money.”

The eleven-year campaign for Jewish parliamentary emancipation dominated this period. Lionel was elected to the City of London constituency in 1847 but could not take his seat because of the oath “upon the true faith of a Christian.” On July 26, 1850, he appeared before the Commons and declared: “I desire to be sworn upon the Old Testament… that is the form most binding upon my conscience.” He was asked to withdraw. The Lords rejected emancipation bills repeatedly, with bishops leading the opposition.

“I went to sleep at 5 and woke against 6; I had dreamt that a huge vampire was greedily sucking my blood… Surely we do not deserve so much hatred.” — Charlotte de Rothschild after the Lords’ defeat, 1848

Disraeli and the Rothschilds formed a remarkable alliance. Disraeli dined frequently at 148 Piccadilly, and Lionel helped him speculate in French railways. The character of Sidonia in Coningsby is unmistakably Rothschildian: “The world is governed by very different personages from what is imagined by those who are not behind the scenes.” Disraeli’s financial dependence on Lionel may have precluded a U-turn on emancipation, even as he led a party hostile to Jewish rights.

Final triumph came on July 26, 1858. After the Earl of Lucan proposed allowing each House to set its own oath, Lionel was at last sworn in — eleven years after his first election. James called it “a real triumph for the family.” The Rothschilds also built magnificent country houses in this period: Mentmore by Joseph Paxton with twenty-six ground-floor rooms, Ferrières with eighty rooms and an underground railway connecting the kitchens, and Nat’s Château Mouton-Rothschild purchased in 1853.

Philanthropy deepened. James created a Jewish hospital on the rue Picpus (1852), whose opening was described as “one of the grandest ceremonies that Judaism has ever celebrated.” Charlotte ran the Jews’ Free School, trebling enrolment. Her dying words: “Remember the Poor.” Anselm in Vienna began each working day going through begging letters, personally determining sums. In Frankfurt, Wilhelm Carl processed twenty to thirty petitions daily from “all over the Jewish world.”

Chapter 2: The Era of Mobility (1849–1858)

The Crimean War (1853–56) was the first major European conflict since 1815, and the Rothschilds found themselves financing both sides of the diplomatic equation. The London house floated a £16 million British war loan in 1855, while the Paris house managed French government borrowing. The war demonstrated that in the new world of national rivalries, the Rothschilds’ multinational structure could become a liability as much as an asset — each house was expected to serve its national government, even when those governments were at odds.

Railways transformed Rothschild business in this period. The Compagnie du Nord became the cornerstone of the Paris house’s portfolio, and capital gains on continental railway shares were the principal reason the French house outgrew the English. But railway finance was volatile and politically charged — hostility to wealth fused with hostility to Jews, and the Rothschilds became the perfect target for a new generation of anti-capitalist pamphleteers.

The Pereire brothers posed the most serious competitive threat. Their Crédit Mobilier mobilised smaller investors through joint-stock structures, breaking the Rothschilds’ near-monopoly on sovereign bond issuance. States began selling bonds by public subscription without Rothschild mediation. James fought back with characteristic aggression, eventually helping to engineer the Pereires’ downfall during the crisis of 1866–67.

The Naples house under Carl managed Italian bond issues and railway investments despite the political turmoil of unification. The discovery of rich mercury deposits at Almadén in Spain became a crucial revenue stream — the Rothschilds advanced money against the output of the mines, whose value was as dependable as Spanish paper was not. This continued to generate income until the 1920s.

“It is easier to break up a thing than to put it back together again. We have children enough to carry on the business for a hundred years.” — James de Rothschild, 1850

The personal stakes were immense. Combined Rothschild capital grew from £9.27 million in 1852 to £10.63 million by 1855. The partnership was renewed with new rules on information-sharing and profit distribution, but the fundamental tensions between centralisation and autonomy remained. Each house increasingly pursued its own national agenda while paying lip service to Mayer Amschel’s founding principle of unity.

Bond markets responded to every political tremor. The price of French 3 per cent rentes became the family’s emotional barometer — every revolution, assassination, or diplomatic crisis registered first in bond prices and only later in newspapers. The Rothschilds processed information faster than any government, using their private courier network to front-run markets on an almost industrial scale.

The social ascent continued. James had become “Monsieur le Baron” and filled Ferrières with Dutch paintings and French furniture. Yet privately the family viewed honours with cynicism — they were “part of the racket,” useful for access to power. Their sons’ real education was still in the “counting house.”

Chapter 3: Nationalism and the Multinational (1859–1863)

Italian unification threatened the Rothschild position fundamentally. The Naples house had been the family’s Italian base, but Garibaldi’s march and the fall of the Bourbon monarchy in 1860 destroyed its political foundations. Adolph withdrew from the partnership in 1863, pulling out £1,593,777 — the first crack in the multinational structure. James was furious, denouncing him as “riff-raff” and “a great lump.”

The wars of Italian and German unification proved that financial leverage could no longer prevent great-power conflict. Cavour, Bismarck, and Napoleon III all pursued national aggrandisement regardless of Rothschild pressure. The old formula “no peace, no cash” no longer held when governments could tap joint-stock banks and public subscriptions.

Yet the Rothschilds adapted. They financed both sides of the Italian equation — lending to Piedmont while maintaining ties with Austria. The Vienna house under Anselm managed Austrian war finance during the defeats of 1859 and 1866, while the Paris house profited from Napoleon III’s Italian adventures. The Creditanstalt, effectively a subsidiary of the Vienna house, became the dominant financial institution in the Habsburg Empire.

The Austrian-Prussian yield gap widened dramatically as investors priced in the growing probability of war. Austrian bonds fell while Prussian bonds rose, enriching those who had bet correctly on Bismarck’s ambitions. The Rothschilds, who remained emotionally attached to Austria, found themselves on the wrong side of this trade.

“Casca il mondo” (“The world is falling”) — The Papal nuncio after Königgrätz, 1866

Prussia’s decisive victory at Königgrätz (July 3, 1866) stunned the family. Rothschild properties were occupied by Prussian cavalry. Frankfurt itself was annexed, and Mayer Carl was forced to pay a crushing indemnity. James was “half crazy”: “I am wholly pro-Austrian this time, as the war is just too unjust.” Even his eight-year-old granddaughter Bettina was “very angry with Mr Bismarck.”

The period also saw the Rothschilds navigating the treacherous politics of Catholic Church finance. When the Italian government proposed selling ecclesiastical properties, a Rothschild syndicate was offered the business. But James drew back: “As a Jew, I don’t like to go against the clergy… I remain a financier and do not want to get involved with politics which will turn the clergy against us.” Alphonse agreed: “We are in a Catholic country, and one cannot go against the religious prejudices of the country where one lives.”

Despite backing the losing side militarily, the Rothschilds profited from the financial aftermath. Austria needed emergency credits, and the London house’s profits for 1866 were a healthy £170,000. The pattern would repeat throughout the century: the family lost on politics but won on the recovery trade.

Chapter 4: Blood and Silver (1863–1867)

James de Rothschild died on November 15, 1868, at age seventy-six. His estate was valued at over 1 billion francs — roughly £40 million — making him by far the richest man in France and probably the world. His funeral was attended by the Emperor’s representative, the entire diplomatic corps, and even Émile Pereire. The last of the founding generation’s sons was gone.

The transition to Alphonse (born 1827) as head of the Paris house was remarkably smooth. James had been grooming him for years, and Alphonse inherited his father’s Judengasse mentality. Asked why he worked like a slave when he was so rich, he replied: “You don’t know the pleasure of feeling heaps of Christians under one’s boots.”

Napoleon III’s increasingly erratic foreign policy terrified the financial markets. His speech at Auxerre denouncing the treaties of 1815 devastated the Paris bourse. James coined his famous epigram: “L’Empire, c’est la baisse” — the Empire means falling prices. Alphonse’s brilliant analysis of July 1866 predicted that French victory would mean alignment with Austria, while defeat would bring Prussia’s domination of Germany — “an immense danger.”

“If I were Emperor, I would be ashamed of myself.” — James de Rothschild on Napoleon III

The Rothschilds worked frantically to prevent the Franco-Prussian War. When the Hohenzollern candidacy for the Spanish throne triggered the crisis of July 1870, Alphonse used every diplomatic channel available. The candidacy was withdrawn, and peace seemed saved — but Napoleon’s insistence on a permanent guarantee, and Bismarck’s doctoring of the Ems telegram, made war unavoidable. France declared war on July 19.

The war was a personal catastrophe for the Paris Rothschilds. Ferrières was occupied by the Prussian army and became Bismarck’s headquarters. The famous wine cellars were looted. Alphonse remained in Paris through the siege, communicating with the outside world via carrier pigeon and balloon post. The Commune added additional horror, though Rothschild properties survived largely intact.

The Treaty of Frankfurt imposed an indemnity of 5 billion francs on France — the largest in history. The Rothschilds organised the payment through two enormous loans: 2 billion francs in 1871 and 3 billion in 1872. The second was oversubscribed twelve times — subscriptions totalled 43 billion francs. This was the greatest demonstration of Rothschild financial power since Waterloo. France paid off the indemnity by September 1873, nearly two years ahead of schedule.

The war reshaped the Rothschild network. Frankfurt — now part of Prussia — declined as a financial centre. Berlin replaced it, and the Rothschilds had no house there. Bleichröder filled the vacuum. Only three houses remained: London, Paris, and Vienna. The Paris house emerged stronger than ever, its prestige enhanced by the indemnity loans.

The Rothschilds also failed to establish a foothold in Russian finance during this period. James had tried repeatedly to build a position in St Petersburg but found the Russians uninterested. The Frankfurt house described Russia as “the Northern Barbarians” with whom “we have not had much luck.” It was the London house that eventually broke through with a £12 million Russian bond issue at 80 — “one of the most ambitious Rothschild undertakings of this period.”

Chapter 5: Bonds and Iron (1867–1870)

The Spanish question illustrated the dangers of mixing finance with diplomacy. French capital had penetrated deeply into Spain — mines, railways, government bonds — and French banks competed fiercely for influence after the 1868 revolution. The Rothschilds controlled the Almadén mercury mines, whose output they used as collateral for advances to the Spanish government. Between 1866 and 1882 Spain’s public debt exploded from 4.6 billion to 12.9 billion pesetas.

The Gründerzeit — the founders’ era of 1866–73 — saw a frenzy of joint-stock company formation across Germany and Austria. Mayer Carl reported: “You have no idea what a competition there is now in business; it is more than a mania and a regular disease like Cholera.” The Rothschilds participated cautiously in railway loans to German states but grew increasingly alarmed at the speculative excess.

Relations with Bleichröder deteriorated sharply. Mayer Carl complained that Bleichröder was “an old fool who wants everybody to think & believe that he is our agent.” The Rothschilds preferred to work with Hansemann, whom they considered “first-rate and particularly honest.” This hostility reflected the broader challenge: Berlin was replacing Frankfurt, and the Rothschilds had no direct presence in Germany’s new capital.

“Every little helps” and “1/2 an egg is better than an empty shell.” — Mayer Carl on small German state loans

The Russian Option presented itself repeatedly but the Rothschilds remained wary. James had tried to establish a Rothschild foothold in St Petersburg but found direct involvement in Russian railways “too risky.” Gorchakov urged Mayer Carl that “we ought to have a house at Petersburg — there is to be made in Russia c’est une mine d’or.” The Rothschilds refused, establishing a pattern of caution toward Russia that would have enormous diplomatic consequences.

Mayer Carl’s confidence in peace rested on economics. He was busy with Prussian finance, South German state loans, and railway construction across the new Confederation. The German capital market was integrating around Frankfurt, Berlin, and Hamburg. “Why go to war for it?” he asked. Economic unification seemed to make military unification unnecessary.

The period also saw the Rothschilds navigating the complexity of Austro-Hungarian dual monarchy finances. The Compromise of 1867 created new requirements for financial operations in both Vienna and Budapest, stretching the Vienna house’s resources. Anselm proved capable of managing the transition, though his son Ferdinand showed more interest in art collecting than banking.

The Franco-Prussian War, when it finally came, validated every fear. The financial damage was catastrophic: French rentes collapsed, the Paris bourse shut down, and the Rothschilds lost heavily on their bond holdings. Yet the recovery trade — organising the indemnity payments — proved even more profitable than the losses had been devastating.

Technology was eroding the Rothschilds’ traditional advantages. The telegraph ended their courier advantage; joint-stock banks offered deposit-taking services they had never provided; public subscription replaced private placement. The world was changing faster than the Rothschild business model could adapt.

Chapter 6: Reich, Republic, Rentes (1870–1873)

The Franco-Prussian War created a fundamentally new European landscape. Germany was unified under Prussian leadership, France was a republic nursing revanche, and Austria-Hungary adjusted to diminished status. For the Rothschilds, the key question was how to maintain their multinational network in an age of rising nationalism.

The Vienna stock exchange crashed on May 9, 1873 (“Black Friday”). The crisis spread across Europe and America — the first truly international financial crisis of the modern era. The Rothschilds were relatively unscathed because of their habitual conservatism. Anselm had warned repeatedly about speculative excess. The crash discredited economic liberalism in Central Europe and fuelled anti-Semitic sentiment: the Gründerschwindel (“founding swindle”) was blamed on Jewish financiers.

After the indemnity loans, Alphonse was the undisputed king of French finance. He maintained close relations with Thiers and the conservative republicans. The Paris house focused on French rentes, the Compagnie du Nord railway, and international loans. The balance of power within the Rothschild network shifted decisively from Vienna and Frankfurt to Paris and London.

“L’Empire, c’est la baisse.” — James de Rothschild (the Empire means falling prices)

The two enormous French government loans of 1871–72 represented the Rothschilds’ finest hour since Waterloo. The first (2 billion francs at 82.5) was oversubscribed five times. The second (3 billion francs at 84.5) attracted 43 billion francs in subscriptions — twelve times the amount offered. Money flooded in from across Europe, a vote of confidence in France’s recovery and in Rothschild competence.

The speed with which France paid off the indemnity astonished Bismarck. The last German troops left French soil in September 1873, nearly two years ahead of schedule. This was partly French fiscal discipline, partly the Rothschilds’ ability to mobilise international capital on an unprecedented scale.

The five German great banks — Darmstädter, Disconto-Gesellschaft, Deutsche Bank, Dresdner, and the Berliner Handels-Gesellschaft — had combined capital of 870 million marks by 1913, not much more than the Rothschilds a decade earlier. But they were growing faster and taking deposits, something the Rothschilds had never done.

The Frankfurt house went into terminal decline. Berlin was now Germany’s financial capital. Mayer Carl’s staff went to work for the Disconto-Gesellschaft. The office in the Fahrgasse — built by Amschel and Salomon in the first flower of their prosperity — became a museum for Jewish antiquities. The fons et origo of the Rothschild fortunes was wound up in 1901.

Alphonse’s political instincts were conservative-republican: he feared both Bonapartist restoration and radical republicanism. He supported the “moral order” governments while maintaining relations with all factions — the essential Rothschild survival strategy of backing every horse while betting most heavily on stability.

Chapter 7: “The Caucasian Royal Family”

Ferguson tests Thomas Mann’s Buddenbrooks thesis of generational decline against the Rothschild evidence. The third generation died in rapid succession: Nat in 1870, Anselm in 1874, Mayer in 1874, Anthony in 1876, Lionel in 1879. Lionel worked until the day he died — on his deathbed he summoned his broker to correct an addition error in his fortnightly account. He left £2.7 million. Disraeli called him “one of the ablest men I ever knew.”

The fourth generation seemed to confirm the decline narrative. Charlotte wrote of baby Natty: “reserved and shy and not generous; he is the only one of my children fond of money for the sake of hoarding it… constitutionally indolent.” Natty struggled through Cambridge, devoted more time to hunting than studying, and when asked for his formula for financial success, replied: “Yes, by selling too soon.”

Leo’s interests turned entirely to horse-racing. His horse Sir Bevys won the Derby in 1879; St Amant won again in 1904. Alfred lived as a fin de siècle aesthete — Max Beerbohm caricatured doctors consulting whether he might take a second praline before bedtime. He was the first Jew elected to the Court of Directors of the Bank of England (1868) but conspicuously failed to leverage the position.

“A gentleman is not to be found in the office before eleven and never stays beyond four.” — Alfred to Max Warburg

Competitors noticed. Edward Baring declared the Rothschilds “so unreasonable and lazy that it is difficult to ensure a business being properly carried through under their direction.” Ernest Cassel was blunter: “absolutely useless & not remarkable for intelligence.” The real engine at New Court was Carl Meyer, a young Hamburg clerk who worked at frenetic pace. When Meyer sought promotion to procurist in 1890, offering to sign for the firm, the brothers turned him down. He resigned in 1897 to work with Cassel.

The multinational partnership fragmented irreversibly. £41.3 million was withdrawn between 1874 and 1905 as partners died and heirs cashed out. By 1905 the French partners controlled 46.8% of total capital; the English just 23.4%. The partnership was formally dissolved between 1905 and 1909. A French journalist had prophesied: “Divide up the house of Rothschild into a French house, an English, an Austrian… and its mediating influence disappears.”

Yet Ferguson argues the decline narrative is overstated. Natty, Alphonse, and Albert remained effective bankers even as their siblings pursued aestheticism. The problem was structural, not personal: joint-stock banks offered “careers open to talent” that the family firm could not match, national rivalries made cross-border cooperation fraught, and the European capital market was segmenting along national lines.

The family’s country houses and art collections expanded spectacularly. Ferdinand’s Waddesdon — a Loire château in deepest Buckinghamshire with fifty greenhouses — was arguably the greatest Rothschild house. Alfred’s Halton featured a bowling alley, ice rink, and indoor swimming pool. Gladstone’s secretary dismissed it as “an exaggerated nightmare of gorgeousness and senseless and ill-applied magnificence.” Ferdinand told Rosebery: “I am a lonely, suffering and occasionally a very miserable individual despite the gilded and marble rooms in which I live.”

Charlotte’s phrase captured the family’s self-image: “the Caucasian royal family.” When Alfred visited Leopold II in Brussels, the King said: “Votre famille m’a toujours gâté.” Alfred replied: “Pardon Sire, c’est Votre Majesté qui a toujours gâté notre famille.” The Rothschilds considered themselves equals of royalty — and with some justification.

Chapter 8: Jewish Questions

Endogamy gave way to marriages with other wealthy Jewish families after 1877 — the Sassoons, Goldschmidts, and Halphens. The Sassoon alliance was most significant: Leo married Marie Perugia (1881, attended by the Prince of Wales), Aline married Sir Edward Sassoon (1887). This signalled the integration of the Rothschilds into a wider “cousinhood” of wealthy Jewish families.

Female Rothschilds married outside the faith: Annie to Eliot Yorke (1873), Constance to Cyril Flower (1877), Hannah to the 5th Earl of Rosebery (1878). None actually converted — even Constance, who said “I wish I could be a Christian,” decided conversion would be “impossible” and “a falsehood.” Hannah’s wedding saw no male Rothschild in attendance; Disraeli gave the bride away.

Natty was elevated to the peerage on July 9, 1885 — sworn in with his hat on his head and his hand on a Hebrew Old Testament. Queen Victoria had resisted fiercely: “To make a Jew a peer.” Gladstone argued relentlessly until she yielded. The Rothschild peerage was part of a broader boom in City peerages — nearly a fifth of bankers acquired titles after 1890.

Anti-Semitism transformed from incoherent prejudice into organised political movements. In Germany, Wilhelm Marr coined Antisemitismus; in Austria, Karl Lueger’s “Christian Social” campaign called for nationalising the Rothschild-owned Kaiser-Ferdinand-Nordbahn. In France, the volume of anti-Semitic publication was greatest — Drumont’s Jewish France (1886) ran to 200 editions, claiming the Rothschilds controlled “nine-tenths of all Europe’s financial affairs.”

“The God Rothschild was the real master of France: Neither Emperor, nor Tsar, nor King… he disposes over all the governmental forces, all the resources of France for his private purposes.” — Drumont, The Jews against France (1899)

The Dreyfus Affair (1894) polarised French society. Alphonse was “irritated by the condemnation of Dreyfus and by the indifference of the French aristocracy.” Proust captured the schizophrenic attitudes in his Guermantes circle — Dreyfusard sympathies studiously concealed in a world where Jews and anti-Semites routinely socialised together.

Violence escalated. A letter bomb was sent to Alphonse in 1895, injuring his head clerk. In 1912, William Tebbitt fired five shots at Leo as he drove from New Court, riddling his car with bullets. In America, Populists depicted the Rothschilds as an “English Octopus” feeding on nothing but gold.

The Rothschild response combined philanthropy, community leadership, and political engagement. Natty ran a “two-tier health service” on his Tring estate, established the Four Per Cent Industrial Dwellings Company to build housing in Spitalfields, and presided over the United Synagogue from 1879 to 1915. In Paris, the Rothschild Foundation for workers was endowed with 10 million francs.

Edmond de Rothschild funded Jewish settlements in Palestine from 1882, spending £5.6 million on colonies at Rishon le Zion, Zikhron Ya’aqov, and others. But he explicitly opposed a Jewish state, advising settlers to seek Ottoman citizenship. When Theodor Herzl sought Rothschild backing for his Judenstaat, offering to make the first Rothschild “prince,” Natty refused even to see him: “The dream of Palestine is a myth and a will-of-the-wisp.” Ferguson’s verdict: with hindsight, Herzl was a prophet.

Chapter 9: “On the Side of Imperialism” (1874–1885)

The best-known example of Rothschild imperialism is the Suez Canal purchase of 1875. Disraeli asked Lionel to advance £4 million so Britain could buy the Khedive’s shares. The sum equalled 8.3% of the entire UK budget. Parliament was not sitting and the money was needed by the end of the month. Disraeli told the Queen there was “scarcely breathing time.” Montagu Corry, Disraeli’s secretary, went to New Court; Lionel reportedly asked: “What is your security?” Corry replied: “The British Government.” Lionel ate a muscatel grape and said: “You shall have it.”

The commission was 2.5% plus 5% interest — modest for so vast a sum. The investment proved spectacularly profitable: by 1935 the shares were worth £93 million. But it also drew Britain into Egyptian affairs irreversibly. The Khedive’s debts continued to mount; by 1876 debt charges consumed 55.5% of all Egyptian expenditure.

The Balkan Crisis of 1875–78 tested Rothschild loyalties severely. The London house held £62 million in Russian bonds whose price crashed from 106 to 68 when war loomed. Natty and Alfred backed Turkey over Russia at enormous financial cost — the decision excluded the Rothschilds from Russian finance for over twelve years.

“I go to the House every morning and when I say ‘No’ to every scheme and enterprise submitted to me, I return home at night carefree and contented. But when I agree to any proposal, I am immediately filled with anxiety.” — Natty Rothschild

The Rothschilds dominated foreign government bond issuance in the City. Between 1865 and 1914, N.M. Rothschild & Sons was responsible for over a quarter of all foreign public issues (£1,085 million of £4,082 million total). Over 90% of their issues were public sector. Surprisingly, imperial issues accounted for only 6% of their business — they showed no preference for bonds guaranteed by British political control.

The British occupation of Egypt (1882) was facilitated by Rothschild intelligence. Alphonse provided crucial information about French intentions that enabled Britain to act unilaterally. Four major Egyptian bond issues worth approximately £50 million followed. Gladstone himself held Egyptian bonds and profited handsomely from the stabilisation he had authorised — a conflict of interest Ferguson notes with some irony.

British overseas investment rose from £370 million in 1860 to £3.9 billion by 1913 — around a third of total British wealth. Britain accounted for 44% of total foreign investment on the eve of World War I. The Rothschilds rode this wave while remaining focused on government bonds rather than private enterprise. When Guinness sought to float on the stock market in 1886, the London house refused and Barings made £500,000 in profit.

Ferguson draws a parallel between the Rothschilds’ relative decline and Britain’s. Both were accused of “entrepreneurial failure,” but both were adapting rationally to a world where capital export generated higher returns than domestic industry. The income earned on foreign investments more than matched the export of new capital, consistently exceeding the trade deficit throughout this period.

Natty thought of himself as “wearing different hats”: one for New Court and one for Westminster, one for the East End and one for the West End. The politics of imperialism sometimes took precedence over financial calculations, but the fundamental Rothschild instinct remained: back stability, avoid wars, and profit from the recovery when wars came anyway.

Chapter 10: Party Politics

The Rothschilds drifted from Liberal to Conservative in this period, driven by two issues: imperialism and Irish Home Rule. Natty’s final break with Gladstone came in April 1886 over the first Home Rule Bill. The family had been intimate with Gladstone — he dined regularly at 148 Piccadilly — but Home Rule was anathema to a family whose wealth depended on the stability of the British Empire.

The relationship with Disraeli remained the Rothschilds’ most important political connection. Disraeli was a genuine intimate: he shared intelligence, consulted on appointments, and used Rothschild channels for unofficial diplomacy. When Disraeli died in 1881, Natty lost not just a political ally but a personal friend.

In France, Alphonse navigated the treacherous politics of the Third Republic. He dined with Gambetta and maintained relations with all factions, but his politics were fundamentally conservative: he feared socialism more than any other force. The Rothschilds funded moderate republican candidates while keeping channels open to the monarchist right.

“Ferdinand is equally struck with the fact that the Liberal party has split into several sections, none of which has any definite programme… there is no alternative to Imperialism but anarchy.” — Ferdinand de Rothschild to Dilke

Lord Randolph Churchill became another key Rothschild political connection. His spectacular rise and fall — Chancellor of the Exchequer at thirty-seven, resigned and marginalised within a year — was intimately entangled with Rothschild finances. The family managed his investments and provided political intelligence. His son Winston would later maintain the connection through the Brinco project in Newfoundland.

Rosebery’s brief premiership (1894–95) represented the high point of Rothschild political influence. As Hannah’s widower and a former habitué of Mentmore, Rosebery was the closest thing to a Rothschild Prime Minister. But his government was weak and short-lived, and his political isolation after Hannah’s death suggested that her political energy had been more important than his own.

Buckinghamshire politics were particularly fraught. The Rothschilds essentially owned multiple constituencies in the Vale of Aylesbury, provoking anti-Semitic campaigning. Opponents accused them of buying elections — not entirely without basis, given the sums Charlotte documented as “cashed up” for candidates.

The Austrian Rothschilds faced different political dynamics. Albert was declared hoffähig in 1887 — the first time such a privilege had been conceded to persons of the Jewish religion in Austria. Nathaniel mixed socially with Viennese aristocracy, addressed with the familiar “du” by Count Wilczek, who regarded him as “an unusually charming man.”

The relationship with the Prince of Wales (future Edward VII) remained socially important. The Rothschilds managed his debts — primarily a £160,000 mortgage on Sandringham — and entertained him lavishly. When the Prince fell and broke his leg at Waddesdon in 1898, it made national headlines.

Chapter 11: The Risks and Returns of Empire (1885–1902)

The Barings Crisis of 1890 demonstrated both Rothschild conservatism and Rothschild power. Barings had overextended in Argentine loans — £2.9 million in capital against £21 million in liabilities. Bank of England Governor Lidderdale assembled a rescue fund. Natty was initially reluctant; Lidderdale told him bluntly: “We can get on without you.” The £10 million guarantee fund included £3 million in gold from the Banque de France — arranged through the Rothschild channel.

The Rothschilds survived similar exposure to emerging-market debt because of their higher capital ratios and broader diversification. They held major positions in Brazilian bonds (£28 million+) and Chilean paper (£10 million) but never concentrated risk the way Barings had. The lesson was clear: the Rothschild principle of low profitability and high capital was not stodginess but survival strategy.

Mining became the great new frontier. The Exploration Company (founded 1886) returned 265% in dividends. Rio Tinto copper, De Beers diamonds, Burma Ruby Mines, Le Nickel in New Caledonia, Penarroya lead and zinc, BNITO oil in Baku — the Rothschilds assembled a global mining and natural resources portfolio of extraordinary breadth.

“We can get on without you.” — Bank of England Governor Lidderdale to Natty during the Barings rescue

Cecil Rhodes drew the Rothschilds into South African gold and diamond mining, but they remained suspicious of his political ambitions. Natty opposed using De Beers as a vehicle for the British South Africa Company, correctly sensing that Rhodes’s imperial schemes carried risks no amount of diamonds could compensate.

The Boer War (1899–1902) was a watershed. Rothschild peace efforts failed, and J.P. Morgan displaced the Rothschilds in British war finance — marking a decisive shift of financial power toward New York. The war cost Britain £230 million and demonstrated that imperial expansion was no longer cheap.

The Rothschilds also played a central role in maintaining the gold standard. A $62.3 million US bond deal with Morgan in 1895 helped stabilise the dollar. But Natty’s preference was for “multimetallism” rather than strict monometallism — a more flexible approach to monetary management than most historians have recognised.

The London Gold Fix was born at New Court on September 12, 1919, with the Rothschilds chairing the twice-daily fixing until 2004. This gave them a central role in the global monetary system that persisted long after their dominance in bond markets had faded.

By the turn of the century, the Rothschilds’ mining empire was vast but their acceptance business was lagging. Annual acceptances averaged £2.7 million compared to £5.6 million for Barings and £9 million for Kleinworts. The family’s conservatism kept them safe but cost them market share in every metric except capital.

Chapter 12: Finances and Alliances (1885–1906)

Alfred worked persistently to build an Anglo-German alliance, using the “Schwabach-Rothschild channel” through Paul von Schwabach of Bleichröder in Berlin as unofficial diplomatic intermediary. But Salisbury’s deep suspicion killed the project. His private secretary dismissed Alfred as “suffering from megalomania” for accepting a German decoration.

The Berlin-Baghdad Railway debacle exposed the limits of Rothschild diplomacy. Natty advised the British government to take shares, as with Suez. But right-wing press attacks on the 35% German investor share killed British participation in March 1903. The Kaiser’s “Kruger telegram” of 1896, congratulating the Boers on repelling the Jameson Raid, had already poisoned Anglo-German relations beyond repair.

The Entente Cordiale with France offered a better deal: final recognition of Britain’s position in Egypt in exchange for French control of Morocco. The Rothschilds supported this alignment, which suited their financial interests in both countries. Central bank cooperation underpinned the entente — £1.4 million in gold swaps between London and Paris in 1906.

“I always dread the reopening of any Eastern Question.” — Natty Rothschild on Ottoman finance

Natty’s principled refusal to lend to Russia without concessions on Jewish rights was one of the most remarkable episodes of financial diplomacy. When Raffalovich offered £60–120 million in Russian bonds, Natty demanded improvements for Russian Jews. Stolypin responded with contempt. The Rothschilds were excluded from the vast Russian bond market that ultimately flowed through Paris and enriched their French competitors.

The Russo-Japanese War (1904–05) vindicated those who had avoided Russian paper — Russian bonds crashed while Jacob Schiff of Kuhn, Loeb financed Japan’s victory. The Rothschilds were slow to back Japan but ultimately benefited from the shift in Asian power dynamics.

The Creditanstalt in Vienna, effectively a Rothschild subsidiary, faced growing challenges. Albert’s death in 1911 left the Austrian branch without strong leadership. The inter-house partnership had already been dissolved; the Vienna Rothschilds were increasingly on their own.

Capital flows were driving diplomatic alliances more than diplomatic alliances were driving capital flows. French savings flooded into Russian bonds because Russian yields exceeded French domestic returns — not because the Franco-Russian Alliance required it. The Rothschilds understood this better than most: finance followed returns, and politics followed finance.

Ferguson shows that the diplomatic alignments of 1904–07 — the Entente Cordiale, the Anglo-Russian Convention, the tightening of the Franco-Russian alliance — all had deep financial roots. The Rothschilds were at the centre of this web, connecting London and Paris through family ties that no joint-stock bank could replicate.

Chapter 13: The Military-Financial Complex (1906–1914)

The arms race between the great powers had financial dimensions that Ferguson documents in extraordinary detail. Military expenditure by the Dual Entente (Britain, France, Russia) rose 40.8% between 1904 and 1913; the Dual Alliance (Germany, Austria-Hungary) increased spending by 158.5%. The Rothschilds were involved through investments in Vickers, Maxim-Nordenfelt, and the Witkowitz ironworks in Moravia.

Lloyd George’s People’s Budget of 1909 — with its land taxes and supertax — represented the greatest domestic threat to the Rothschilds in a generation. Lloyd George singled out Natty in devastating speeches: “too much Lord Rothschild.” The budget crisis led to the Parliament Act of 1911, permanently reducing the power of the Lords where the Rothschilds had their seats.

Natty found himself increasingly marginalised. Cut off from the ruling Liberals, he could no longer deliver the kind of behind-the-scenes influence that had characterised Rothschild politics for a century. The era of the private banker as unofficial diplomat was ending.

“Too much Lord Rothschild.” — Lloyd George during the People’s Budget debates, 1909

The July Crisis of 1914 provides Ferguson’s most dramatic financial narrative. The assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand triggered cascading market collapses across Europe. Stock exchanges closed one after another. The Bank Rate rose to 10%. Natty made futile peace appeals, drawing on the old Rothschild belief that financial interdependence could prevent war.

The London house lost £1.5 million in the crisis — 23% of its capital — a devastating blow. More importantly, J.P. Morgan emerged as Britain’s primary American financial agent, displacing the Rothschilds from their traditional role as the bridge between European and American capital markets. The shift of financial power from London to New York, which the war would accelerate dramatically, was already visible in the last weeks of peace.

Bond yields of the major powers diverged sharply in the final pre-war years, pricing in the rising probability of conflict that politicians refused to acknowledge. Russian and Austrian bonds sold off while British and French paper held firm. The bond market, as so often, was ahead of the diplomats.

Ferguson shows that the Rothschilds’ inability to prevent World War I was the ultimate failure of their founding strategy. Mayer Amschel’s multinational structure had been designed to make war financially impossible. But by 1914, governments could finance wars through taxation, central bank credit, and public subscriptions — the Rothschild veto had been bypassed.

The national debt data tells the story: Britain’s debt was just 3.3 times revenue in 1913, with debt service consuming only 13.3% of expenditure. Germany spent 33.4% on debt service. These were still manageable numbers — governments had fiscal headroom to fight a major war, and they used it.

Chapter 14: Deluges (1915–1945)

The interwar period saw the Rothschilds navigate a succession of catastrophes. In Latin America, the London house held £28 million in Brazilian bonds and £10 million in Chilean paper. Coffee price-support schemes and political instability in both countries produced chronic anxiety. The London Gold Fix, born at New Court on September 12, 1919, gave the Rothschilds a central position in the restored gold standard — but the standard itself was unstable.

Walter Rothschild’s prescient 1931 analysis warned that the gold standard was unsustainable. His prediction was vindicated when Britain abandoned gold in September 1931, triggering a global financial crisis. The Dow fell from 381 to 50 — the most devastating crash in market history.

The Creditanstalt crisis (May 1931) was the single most devastating blow. The great Viennese bank, effectively a Rothschild subsidiary for decades, collapsed under the weight of bad loans. When the Vienna Rothschilds appealed for help, Edmond in Paris responded with cold finality: “not a penny more.” The crisis spread to Germany and then to Britain, triggering the cascade of financial collapses that turned a recession into the Great Depression.

“A Jew under Pétain, a pariah under Mitterrand — for me, that’s enough.” — Guy de Rothschild

The rise of Nazism brought existential danger. After the Anschluss (March 1938), Louis de Rothschild was arrested at the Hotel Metropol in Vienna. He was held for over a year, released only after the family surrendered the Witkowitz ironworks to Göring. Louis described his prison conditions as resembling “a Cracow bordello.” In Germany, Maximilian was forced to sell family properties at a fraction of their value during Kristallnacht.

Alfred Rosenberg’s art-looting task force seized 3,978 items from nine Rothschild locations in France alone, part of a total haul of 21,903 works. The systematic plunder of Rothschild art collections represented one of the most comprehensive acts of cultural theft in history.

Family members fought on the Allied side. Some were imprisoned — one Rothschild ended up in Colditz. Others won the Croix de Guerre. But the Holocaust claimed Rothschild lives too: Aranka and Lili were killed. Edmund de Rothschild entered Mannheim at the end of the war through an archway bearing the inscription judenrein — “free of Jews.”

The London house shifted to domestic corporate finance during the interwar years — Shell-Mex, Woolworths — while the French house navigated franc devaluation politics. Edouard clashed with Bank of France Governor Moreau over monetary policy, while Maurice’s electoral scandals embarrassed the family.

By 1945, the German, Austrian, and French Rothschild branches had been devastated. The Frankfurt and Vienna houses were gone. Properties had been confiscated, collections looted, family members killed. The London house survived but was drastically diminished. The question was whether the dynasty could reinvent itself for a post-imperial, post-gold-standard world.

Epilogue

Anthony dedicated himself to conserving what remained. Harold Nicolson described being “hurried out” of lunch at 2:30 as “the great wheels of the Maison Rothschild revolve.” But Anthony’s approach was fatalistic: “They know where we live. If they want to do business with us let them come and talk to us.” London house capital shrank from £8 million pre-war to £1.5 million — accounting for the pound’s 40% purchasing-power loss, a dramatic contraction.

The post-war world offered little scope for traditional Rothschild banking. Bretton Woods constrained international bond markets. British socialism was hostile to the City. A Labour supporter declared in 1948: “Being the son of a rich man is not a good enough reason to have a lot of money.”

The Brinco project in Newfoundland — sixty thousand square miles leased for twenty years — was the most ambitious post-war venture. Lord Leathers said: “You did Suez, so why can’t you do Newfoundland?” Churchill himself bought ten thousand Brinco shares. But the project never escaped political tangles with Quebec.

The rivalry between Evelyn and Jacob Rothschild defined the late twentieth century. Jacob’s plan to merge N.M. Rothschild with S.G. Warburg (codenamed “War and Peace”) was blocked by Evelyn. Jacob departed New Court in 1980 and built an empire through Charterhouse J. Rothschild — market cap £400 million, more than double N.M. Rothschild. But it unravelled: failed mergers, sold stakes, and L.F. Rothschild in New York filed for bankruptcy in 1987.

“We must try to make ourselves as much a bank of brains as of money.” — Jacob Rothschild, 1965

Privatisation proved the London house’s renaissance. Amersham International (1982) was oversubscribed twenty-three times. The British Gas “Tell Sid” campaign (1985–86) saw four million investors apply for £5.6 billion in shares. By 1988 the firm was advising on eleven privatisations in eight countries. Ferguson calls this “strongly reminiscent of its earlier role as the leading architect of the modern bond market.”

The French house was nationalised by Mitterrand in 1981. Guy de Rothschild wrote bitterly in Le Monde: “A Jew under Pétain, a pariah under Mitterrand.” But within three years, David and Edouard established a new Paris house. By 1986, under Chirac’s government, it became Rothschild & Cie Banque — one of five leading French corporate finance firms.

Evelyn recreated the international structure through Rothschilds Continuation Holdings AG (Swiss-based). By 1997: nineteen firms across twenty-plus countries. In 1989, London and Paris Rothschilds opened Rothschild GmbH in Frankfurt — returning to the city where it all began. The Zurich crisis of 1991–92 — losses of 270 million Swiss francs from a rogue executive — was contained by a 120.5 million franc injection, unlike Barings, which was destroyed by similar failures in 1995.

Ferguson’s final assessment: shareholders’ equity of £460 million, total capital resources approximately £800 million. Far behind HSBC but comparable to Schroders. Capital grew from £12 million at incorporation (1970) to £460 million — nearly 400% adjusted for inflation. The survival of a family banking dynasty for over two centuries, through every major war and financial crisis, is “one of the most remarkable stories in business history.”

Trading & Investing Lessons

Capital Preservation Over Profit Maximisation

The Rothschild principle of low profitability and high capital ratios ensured survival when every aggressive competitor eventually blew up. Barings’ 13.6% return on capital looked impressive until 1890 revealed a capital base of just £2.9 million against £21 million in liabilities. The Rothschilds’ 3.9% return looked pedestrian until you measured it over two centuries of continuous operation.

The Recovery Trade

The Rothschilds consistently lost money during crises but made it back — and more — on the recovery. The French indemnity loans of 1871–72 were more profitable than the war losses were devastating. The pattern repeated with Egyptian bonds, the Barings rescue, and post-war privatisations. Surviving the crash is the prerequisite for profiting from the recovery.

Diversification as Insurance

The multinational structure created natural hedges. When Paris collapsed in 1848, London bailed it out. When Vienna failed in 1931, London survived. The geographical spread was the original portfolio diversification — more effective than any modern risk model.

Information Advantage Decays

The Rothschilds’ courier network gave them a decisive edge for decades. The telegraph destroyed it. They experimented with telephones in 1891 but continued writing letters by hand. The lesson: every information advantage is temporary. The firms that survive are those that find new edges, not those that cling to old ones.

Selling Too Soon Is a Strategy

Natty’s formula — “Yes, by selling too soon” — sounds like timidity but is actually a disciplined risk-management philosophy. Taking profits early and sleeping well is worth more over a multi-generational time horizon than maximising any single trade.

Political Risk Is the Dominant Risk

The Rothschilds lost more money to wars, revolutions, nationalisation, and confiscation than to any market crash. Their strategy of cultivating relationships across the political spectrum was not cynicism but rational risk management. The Nazis, not the bond market, ultimately posed the greatest threat to Rothschild wealth.

Key Takeaways

The Multinational Structure Was Both Strength and Weakness

The five-house system enabled arbitrage, mutual bail-outs, and unprecedented information flow. But it also created conflicts of interest as national rivalries intensified, ultimately proving unsustainable after the wars of 1859–71.

Decline Is Relative, Not Absolute

The Rothschilds remained the largest private bank in the City through 1914. Combined capital peaked at £41.5 million. They dominated government bond issuance with over 25% market share. The “decline” was only visible compared to the explosive growth of joint-stock banks and American rivals.

Family Firms Have Longer Time Horizons

The Rothschilds considered the interests of future generations alongside present shareholders. This longer time horizon explained their conservatism, which looked like weakness in boom times but proved decisive in every crisis.

Finance Follows Returns, Not Flags

The Rothschilds’ imperial investments were driven by yield, not patriotism. Only 6% of their issues were imperial. They showed no preference for British-controlled territories when independent states offered better returns.

Anti-Semitism Is the Constant Background

From Drumont to Hitler, the Rothschilds served as the personalisation of every conspiracy theory about Jewish financial power. Their wealth made them simultaneously the most prominent and most vulnerable Jews in Europe.

Adaptation Beats Optimisation

The family survived because they adapted — from courier networks to telegraphs, from sovereign bonds to mining, from private banking to privatisation advisory. Each generation found a new niche. The firms that died were those that optimised for the old world.

Contrarian Ideas

The Fourth Generation Was Not Incompetent

Conventional view: the Buddenbrooks pattern of generational decline explains Rothschild weakening. Ferguson’s counter: Natty, Alphonse, and Albert remained highly effective. The real problem was structural — joint-stock competition, national market segmentation, and the impossibility of retaining talented non-family employees.

British Economic “Decline” Was Also Overstated

The City of London was not draining capital from British industry. Capital exports generated returns that exceeded the trade deficit. The Rothschilds’ focus on foreign bonds was rational in a world where Britain accounted for 44% of total foreign investment.

The Rothschilds Were Not Imperialists

Only 6% of their London issues were imperial. They backed stable independent states (Brazil, Russia) as readily as colonies. Their political involvement in imperialism was driven by the need to protect bondholders, not by imperial ideology.

Zionism Was Rational, and the Rothschilds Were Wrong to Reject It

The Rothschilds opposed Herzl because a Jewish state would threaten their national identities as English, French, and Austrian citizens. Within fifty years, the German, Austrian, and French Rothschilds had all fallen victim to exactly the anti-Semitic onslaught Herzl predicted.

Proposals & Frameworks

The Rothschild Survival Framework

Ferguson identifies three mechanisms that explain the family’s extraordinary longevity: (1) multinational diversification enabling mutual bail-outs; (2) high capital ratios absorbing losses that destroyed competitors; (3) family governance with multi-generational time horizons that discouraged short-term risk-taking.

The Financial Diplomacy Model

The Rothschilds exercised political influence not through bribery or conspiracy but through their role as lender of last resort to governments. Access to Rothschild capital bought access to decision-makers. The model worked best when governments needed money and failed when they could borrow elsewhere.

The Bond Market as Peace Mechanism

The international bond market created financial interdependence between nations, raising the cost of war. This worked from 1815 to 1870 but failed when governments developed alternative funding sources (taxation, central bank credit, public subscription). The Rothschild veto on war finance was a powerful tool with an expiry date.

Relative Decline Is Not Absolute Decline

Ferguson’s analytical framework distinguishes between declining market share (which was real) and declining absolute capability (which was not). The Rothschilds remained the City’s largest private bank. They chose safety over growth — a rational strategy for a family with more to lose than to gain from aggressive risk-taking.

What Didn’t Fit

The Muscatel Grape

When Disraeli’s secretary Montagu Corry rushed to New Court to secure £4 million for the Suez Canal purchase, Lionel reportedly asked “What is your security?” Corry replied: “The British Government.” Lionel paused, ate a muscatel grape, and said: “You shall have it.” Whether apocryphal or not, the anecdote captures the Rothschild style perfectly: vast sums committed with theatrical nonchalance, backed by an instant assessment of political risk that no committee could replicate.

Gutle Schnapper’s Refusal to Move

The matriarch who founded the dynasty refused to leave the cramped house in the former Judengasse even as her sons became the richest family on earth. She lived to ninety-six, was visited by ambassadors in blue livery, and when asked about war rumours reportedly replied: “Nonsense — my boys won’t let them.” Her stubbornness epitomised the ghetto mentality that drove Rothschild ambition: never forget where you came from.

Ferdinand’s Evelina

Ferdinand married Lionel’s daughter Evelina in the supreme moment of Rothschild endogamy — 126 guests at 148 Piccadilly, Disraeli at the head table. Evelina died in childbirth a year later. Ferdinand never remarried: “Henceforth my life can merely be wrought with sorrow and anguish.” He built a children’s hospital and a mausoleum in her memory. The tragedy explains his lifelong melancholy and his compulsive collecting — filling Waddesdon with beauty to compensate for a loss nothing could fill.

Mary Anne Disraeli’s Will

Mary Anne burst into Charlotte’s drawing room, flung herself into her arms, and produced a will making six-year-old Evelina her sole beneficiary: “I love the Jews — I have attached myself to your children and she is my favourite.” This bizarre episode illuminates the intimate, slightly unhinged quality of the Disraeli-Rothschild friendship that shaped British politics for three decades.

The Zurich Rogue

In 1992, senior Rothschild Bank Zurich executive Jurg Heer was discovered to have authorised illegal loans totalling 270 million Swiss francs — more than the firm’s entire capital. The wider Rothschild structure injected 120.5 million francs to save the bank. Three years later, Barings was destroyed by a similar rogue-trading episode in Singapore. The Rothschilds survived because the multinational structure provided a backstop; Barings had no such safety net. It was the clearest modern proof of the founding principle: brothers who stick together cannot be broken.

The House of Rothschild: The World’s Banker, 1849–1999 by Niall Ferguson. Published 1999. 637 pages.

The second volume of Ferguson’s definitive history traces the Rothschild dynasty from the apex of financial power through world wars, confiscation, and reinvention — the most comprehensive account of how a family bank survived two centuries of European upheaval.

About the Author

Niall Ferguson (born 1964 in Glasgow) is one of the most prominent and controversial historians of his generation. At the time of writing this book, he was Fellow and Tutor in Modern History at Jesus College, Oxford. He has since held professorships at Harvard, Stanford, and the London School of Economics, and is currently the Milbank Family Senior Fellow at the Hoover Institution.

Ferguson’s work spans financial history, imperial history, and the history of warfare. His previous books included The Pity of War and Virtual History: Alternatives and Counterfactuals. The two-volume House of Rothschild established his reputation as a financial historian of the first rank, drawing on unprecedented access to the Rothschild family archives. The New York Times named it a Notable Book of the Year.

Ferguson’s subsequent works — The Ascent of Money, Civilization, Kissinger, The Square and the Tower, and Doom — have continued to blend narrative history with quantitative analysis. His willingness to make controversial arguments (defending empire, questioning the costs of World War I) has made him a polarising figure, but his archival scholarship in the Rothschild volumes is universally respected.

Why This Book Matters

This is the most comprehensive history ever written of the world’s most famous banking dynasty during its most turbulent century and a half. Ferguson had unprecedented access to the Rothschild archives — letters, account books, partnership agreements — that no previous historian could consult. The result is a work that demolishes conspiracy theories while revealing a reality more interesting than any myth: a family that was extraordinarily wealthy and influential but also frequently wrong, occasionally desperate, and ultimately constrained by forces no amount of money could control.

For anyone interested in financial markets, the book demonstrates that the fundamental principles of banking — capital preservation, diversification, long time horizons, and the ability to survive crises — have not changed in two centuries. The Rothschild story is the longest continuous case study in the history of finance, and its lessons about the relationship between money, politics, and power remain as relevant today as they were when Mayer Amschel first lent money to the Elector of Hesse-Kassel.

The book is also essential reading on the history of anti-Semitism. Ferguson traces how the Rothschilds became the personalisation of every conspiracy theory about Jewish financial power — from Drumont’s Jewish France to the Populist “English Octopus” to Nazi confiscation. Understanding how these myths were constructed, and how they bore almost no relationship to the messy, human reality of Rothschild decision-making, is more important than ever in an age when conspiracy theories travel at the speed of the internet rather than the speed of a pamphlet.
