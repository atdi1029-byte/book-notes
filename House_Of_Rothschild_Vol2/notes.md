## PREFACE

- The period 1789-1848 was the "age of revolution" and the Rothschilds were its supreme beneficiaries. The political upheavals of 1848-49 cost them dearly — revolutions caused bonds to plummet, and since the Rothschilds held immense wealth in bonds, they suffered heavy losses. The Vienna and Paris houses were brought to the brink of insolvency, requiring London, Frankfurt, and Naples to bail them out. Yet they survived even this, the greatest financial crisis between 1815 and 1914.
- The French Revolution of 1796 literally demolished the walls of the Frankfurt ghetto, enabling the Rothschilds' rise. Before 1789, Mayer Amschel's life was circumscribed by discriminatory legislation — Jews couldn't farm, deal in weapons/spices/wine/grain, live outside the ghetto, or go out on Sundays. They faced discriminatory taxation.
- Napoleon's invasion changed everything: the Judengasse opened, legal restrictions were largely removed. The disruption of established trade patterns created room for ambitious risk-takers.
- Napoleon's exile of the Elector of Hesse-Kassel allowed Mayer Amschel to become the Elector's principal fund manager, collecting interest on assets that eluded the French and reinvesting the money. The French police interrogated him but no prosecution resulted. The profits were proportional to the hazard.
- Nathan moved from Manchester textiles to London finance. His energy was prodigious: "I do not read books, I do not play cards, I do not go to the theatre, my only pleasure is my business." Napoleon's Continental Blockade (1806) inflated both risks and returns for those willing to beat it. Nathan moved to London in 1808.
- Nathan's "masterstroke" was using the Elector of Hesse-Kassel's English investments to bolster his own resources. Over four years he bought securities worth more than £600,000. The exiled Elector unwittingly became a sleeping partner in N. M. Rothschild.
- In 1813 Nathan offered services to the British government to finance Wellington's penultimate campaign against Napoleon. Carl later said the Elector — "the Old Man" — had "made our fortune."
- Mayer Amschel designed the partnership structure in 1810 that endured nearly a century: binding together the male line over four generations, rigorously excluding female Rothschilds and their spouses. His business rules: "It is better to deal with a government in difficulties than with one that has luck on its side"; "If you can't make yourself loved, make yourself feared."
- On his 1812 deathbed, Mayer Amschel told his eldest son: "Keep your brothers together and you will become the richest people in Germany." Thirty years later, they were the richest family in all history.
- The operations of 1814-15 raised immense quantities of bullion for Wellington and Britain's allies. Nathan's account with the British government totalled close to £10 million in 1815 alone. Lord Liverpool called Nathan "a very useful friend." Ludwig Borne called them "Finanzbonaparten"; Nathan was their "commanding general."
- Nathan's supreme *Meistergeschäft*: after Waterloo, a huge investment in British consols, riding the postwar financial stabilization, taking profits just before the market peaked — realizing more than £250,000 at a stroke.

**The Restoration Period (1820s):**
- The Rothschilds bankrolled the restoration of deposed monarchs. They enabled Austria, Prussia, Russia, and the restored Bourbons in France to issue bonds at rates only Britain and Holland had previously enjoyed. This facilitated Metternich's "police" Europe.
- Ludwig Borne complained: "Rothschild was someone who gives nobles the power to spite freedom and deprives peoples of the courage to resist violence... the high priest of fear."
- Yet the Rothschilds were ambivalent about the restoration — conservative elites sought to reimpose second-class Jewish citizenship. Nathan was not one to turn down good business on ideological grounds. Parliamentary monarchies proved better creditors than absolutist regimes.
- As Byron put it in *Don Juan*, the Rothschilds held sway over "royalist and liberal" alike. Heine called Rothschild a revolutionary on par with Robespierre — he "destroyed the predominance of land, by raising the system of state bonds to supreme power."
- Heine: "[M]oney is the god of our time and Rothschild is his prophet."

**The International Bond Market:**
- The Rothschilds' most important contribution to economic history was the creation of a truly international bond market. Never before had a country's bonds been simultaneously issued in multiple markets (as with Prussia in 1818) with sterling denomination, payment of interest at the place of issue, and a sinking fund.
- Bond issuance was not their sole business: they also discounted commercial bills, acted as bullion brokers, dealt in foreign exchange, commodity trade, insurance, and private banking for elite clients. Their role in gold and silver markets was important — they served as "lender of last resort to the lender of last resort," preventing a suspension of convertibility by the Bank of England in 1825.

**Multinational Structure:**
- Five houses in London, Frankfurt, Paris, Vienna, and Naples formed a unique partnership: acting jointly in big transactions, pooling profits, sharing costs. Regular correspondence overcame geographical separation.
- Advantages: (1) arbitrage between markets; (2) mutual bail-outs during crises — when Britain suffered in 1825, James could bail out Nathan; when Paris collapsed in 1830, Nathan reciprocated; (3) by 1825 they were ten times the size of their nearest rival, Baring Brothers.
- By rapidly accumulating capital and not distributing profits, the Rothschilds shifted from profit maximization to market dominance. Competitors like Jacques Laffitte who took bigger risks during upswings came unstuck when the cycle dipped.

**Social Ascent and Religion:**
- The Rothschilds personified new money: Jews, ill-educated, coarse — yet with paper wealth exceeding most aristocratic estates. They bought grand houses on Piccadilly and rue Laffitte, filled them with Dutch paintings and French furniture, sought titles and honors. "Plain Jacob Rothschild became Monsieur le Baron James de Rothschild."
- Yet privately they viewed honors with cynicism — they were "part of the racket," useful for access to power. The gentrification of the next generation was superficial: "their sons' real education was still in the 'counting house.'"
- The Rothschilds' most important reservation about assimilation was religious. Unlike many wealthy European Jews who converted to Christianity, the Rothschilds remained firmly attached to Judaism. James: "everything. Our good fortune and our blessings depend upon it." When Nathan's daughter Hannah Mayer converted to marry Henry Fitzroy in 1839, she was ostracized by nearly all her relatives, including her own mother.

**Endogamy:**
- Of twenty-one marriages involving descendants of Mayer Amschel between 1824 and 1877, no fewer than fifteen were between his direct descendants. This kept capital within the partnership. Heine called them "the exceptional family" — other Jews regarded them as "the Kings of the Jews" and "the Jews of the Kings."

**The 1830 Revolution:**
- Revealed two things: (1) the Rothschilds were not tied to the Holy Alliance — they were willing to serve liberal and revolutionary regimes too; (2) they had a strong interest in seeing great powers reach collective international arrangements, believing financial leverage could enforce peace.
- The Rothschilds' dominance of the bond market gave them a veto power: "no peace, no cash." Austrian diplomat Count Prokesch von Osten: "It is all a question of ways and means and what Rothschild says is decisive, and he won't give any money for war."

**After Nathan (1836):**
- James carried on as primus inter pares. He was masterful, indefatigable, addicted to bill broking and arbitrage. His longevity kept the Frankfurt ghetto ethos alive into the 1860s. Lionel proved as successful as his father Nathan, though sotto voce rather than explosive. Salomon's son Anselm also proved strong-willed.
- The shift from monarchy to oligarchy within the family was advantageous — it allowed more flexible responses to mid-century opportunities like railway finance in Austria, France, and Germany.

**Railway Finance:**
- In most European countries the state played a role in railways, so issuing railway shares/bonds was not unlike government bonds — except railway shares were far more volatile. The Rothschilds were drawn inevitably into closer involvement. Capital gains on continental railway shares were the principal reason the French house outgrew the English.
- Railway involvement exposed them to unprecedented public criticism. In the 1840s, hostility to wealth fused with hostility to Jews: anticapitalism and anti-Semitism complemented each other. The Rothschilds provided the perfect target.

**The Pereire Challenge:**
- The 1848 revolution weakened resistance to joint-stock company formation and limited liability. The Pereire brothers broke free in the 1850s, mobilizing smaller investors through the Crédit Mobilier.
- States began to sell bonds by public subscription without Rothschild mediation. The telegraph ended the Rothschilds' courier advantage. Their position became less monopolistic.

**Political Threats:**
- The triumph of Louis Napoleon Bonaparte introduced new uncertainty. Politicians like Palmerston, Cavour, and Bismarck elevated national self-interest above international "balance." The next thirty-three years (1848-1881) would be marked by wars the Rothschilds could not prevent.
- Charlotte de Rothschild affirmed in May 1848 her belief "in a bright, European and Rothschildian future." But nationalism and then socialism would prove the greatest challenges — especially when combined.

## PART I: UNCLES AND NEPHEWS

## Chapter 1: Charlotte's Dream (1849-1858)

**Epigraph:** Charlotte de Rothschild, May 1849: "I went to sleep at 5 and woke against 6; I had dreamt that a huge vampire was greedily sucking my blood... Apparently, when the result of the vote was declared, a loud, enthusiastic roar of approval resounded... throughout the House [of Lords]. Surely we do not deserve so much hatred."

- Though the Rothschilds weathered 1848 financially, it might still have proved a fatal turning point — for reasons unrelated to economics. The very structure of the family and firm was called into question. The four remaining sons of Mayer Amschel were old men by 1850: Amschel seventy-seven, Salomon seventy-six, Carl an ailing sixty-two. Only James was still indefatigable at fifty-six.
- Longevity was a family trait: their mother Gutle Schnapper, born 1753, lasted long enough to see the crown of a united Germany offered to a Prussian king. She became a by-word in the 1840s. The Times reported her remonstrating with her physician: "I do not ask you to make me younger. It is older I desire to become." Cartoons showed James with Gutle, telling well-wishers: "When she reaches par, gentlemen, I will donate to the state a little capital of 100,000 gulden."

**Gutle Schnapper's death and generational transition:**
- Gutle refused to quit the old house "zum grünen Schild" in the former Judengasse — Ludwig Borne praised her: "Look, there she lives, in that little house... and has no wish, despite the world-wide sovereignty exercised by her royal sons, to leave her hereditary little castle in the Jewish quarter." Charles Greville was amazed to see "the old mother of the Rothschilds" emerging from her "same dark and decayed mansion" in the Jews' street, with a smart calèche and footmen in blue livery outside.
- Gutle died May 7, 1849, in her ninety-sixth year. A spate of family deaths followed: Amschel's wife Eva (1848), Nathan's widow Hannah (1850), Carl's wife Adelheid (1853), Salomon's wife Caroline (1854).
- Bismarck, the new Prussian delegate to the restored German Confederation Diet, dined with Amschel and found him "a pathetic old man." He described Amschel as "short, thin, little man, and quite grey. The eldest of his line, but a poor man in his palace, a childless widower, cheated by his servants and despised by smart Frenchified and Anglicised nephews and nieces who will inherit his wealth without any love or gratitude."

**Succession and inheritance disputes:**
- The question of who should inherit consumed the old Rothschilds. Amschel's share of the firm was worth nearly £2 million. His share was ultimately divided so James got a quarter, Anselm a quarter, Nathan's four sons a quarter, and Carl's three sons the last quarter.
- Salomon tried to avoid making Anselm sole heir due to harsh words exchanged during the revolutionary crisis. He initially considered leaving £1.75 million to Betty's children (£425,000 each for boys, £50,000 for Charlotte). In 1853 he scrapped the clause leaving the choice of successor to Anselm and specified his eldest grandson Nathaniel — but ultimately all Salomon's schemes came to naught; Anselm inherited and decided which of his sons should succeed him.
- In 1855, Salomon, Carl, and Amschel all died within nine months. No record of great grief survives.

**Centrifugal forces after 1848:**
- The Vienna house's effective collapse was not easily forgotten. The London partners wanted to "liberate" themselves from continental commitments. Nat wrote in July 1848 that he wanted each house to "be in an independent position." Charlotte noted: "Uncle A. is weakened and depressed by the loss of his wife, Uncle Salomon by the loss of his money, Uncle James by the uncertain situation in France, my father [Carl] is nervous, my husband, though splendid, is stubborn when he is in the right."
- Nat expressed "such a disgust to business" that he wanted "no more of any sort or description to transact." He thought the uncles were "ridiculously fond of business for business' sake."
- Betty saw it clearly: "Our good Uncle [Amschel] can't tolerate a lessening of our fortune, and in his desire to restore it along previous lines, he wouldn't think twice about throwing us back into the disturbance of hazardous affairs."

**James holds the family together:**
- James played the vital role of bridging the generation gap and binding divergent branches together. Charlotte observed him in Frankfurt in 1849: "I have seldom seen such a practically shrewd man, so worldly and canny, so mentally and physically active and indefatigable... Every day he writes two or three letters and dictates at least six, reads all the French, German and English newspapers, bathes, has a one-hour morning nap, and plays whist for three or four hours."
- James's passionate 1850 letter to Lionel: "It is easier to break up a thing than to put it back together again. We have children enough to carry on the business for a hundred years and so we must not go against one another... the day when a [single] company no longer exists — when we lose that unity and co-operation in business which in the eyes of the world gives us our true strength — the day that ceases to exist and each of us goes his own way, then good old Amschel will say, 'I have £2 million in the business [but now] I am withdrawing it'... What will happen if we are not careful is that capital amounting to £3 million will fall into the hands of outsiders."

**The 1852 partnership agreement:**
- Partnership renewed in 1852 with limited alterations to the 1844 system. London partners got sweeteners: permitted to withdraw £260,250 from their share; interest on their share (20% of total) raised to 3.5% vs. 3% for James, 2.625% for Carl, 2.5% for Amschel and Salomon. Rules relaxed: no partner obliged to take business trips by majority vote, real estate investments no longer financed from collective funds.
- In return, London accepted "a new system of collaboration" — Clause 12: "to secure an open and brotherly co-operation." Partners would inform each other of transactions over 10 million gulden (c. £830,000) and offer participations of up to 10%.
- This was a compromise: the pre-1848 system resumed with only modest decentralization. After 1852, James showed more deference to nephews — "Do, dear nephews, what you wish."
- After 1855 deaths: new structure emerged. Anselm got all Salomon's collective capital. James and Anselm each received a quarter. Nathan's sons and Carl's sons divided the rest. Net effect: close to equal power for Anselm, James, and the English-born partners, while reducing Carl's sons' influence. Total combined capital grew from £9.27 million (1852) to £10.63 million (1855).

**Endogamy continues:**
- Between 1848 and 1877: nine marriages within the family. In 1849 Wilhelm Carl married Hannah Mathilde (Anselm's daughter); the next year Adolph married her sister Julie. In 1857 Alphonse married Leonora (Lionel's daughter) at Gunnersbury. With a single exception before 1873, those who didn't marry Rothschilds stayed within Jewish "cousinhood."
- Charlotte made no bones about the rationale: "For us Jews, and particularly for us Rothschilds, it is better not to come into contact with other families, as it always leads to unpleasantness and costs money."
- Charlotte coolly assessed prospective sons-in-law in her diary. Gustave: "an excellent young man... whether he is talented or not, I could not in all honesty say... he claims, stupid, easily intimidated and unable to string ten words together." Alphonse: "combines the extraordinary energy and vitality of our uncle [James] with Betty's facility for languages... He can converse on the topics of the day with an easy manner, without pedantry."
- She was only half-successful: nine years later it was Alphonse's profile she saw at the altar alongside Leonora. By then she had revised her opinion of Alphonse: "a man, who perhaps for ten of fifteen years has run the round of the world — is completely blase, can neither admire nor love — and yet demands the entire devotion of his bride, her slavish devotion."

**The Orthodox and the Reformed:**
- Endogamy was partly a function of Rothschild Judaism: sons and daughters could not marry outside the faith. Religious commitment in this period was arguably greater than in the 1820s-30s, and was an important source of familial unity.
- James was least strict in observance: he wrote to nephews on the Sabbath wishing them "a hearty good Sabbath" and saw nothing wrong with being at his desk that day. He and Carl were conspicuously erratic in synagogue attendance.
- Yet James remained convinced of the functional importance of Jewish identity. He nearly forgot Passover in 1850 but was willing to cancel a business trip to read the Haggadah.
- Betty (James's wife) was as secular-minded but had a strong sense that observance was a social imperative. When Alphonse attended synagogue in New York, she was "over the moon": "It's good thing, my good son, not only out of religious feeling, but out of patriotism, which in our high position is a stimulus to those who might forget it."
- Wilhelm Carl was the only Orthodox member of the younger generation. He supported creation of a new Israelite Religious Community for Orthodox believers, donating funds for a new synagogue. Yet he opposed the outright schism advocated by Rabbi Samson Raphael Hirsch.
- English brothers inclined toward Reform. Lionel spoke against "popery" when conservatives tried to exclude the Reform-inclined West London Synagogue from the Board of Deputies. Anthony's wife Louisa wanted to modernize services: "What a pity one cannot go to church and hear a good sermon."
- Charlotte (Lionel's wife), raised in Orthodox Frankfurt, nevertheless frequently attended non-Jewish services and institutions — she witnessed Catholic ceremonies and was attracted to Anglo-Catholicism. Yet she remained critical of Jewish communal institutions' lack of "heartiness" compared to Catholic ceremonies.

**Rothschild philanthropy:**
- Traditional male philanthropy was remarkably long-lived. In Vienna, Anselm began each working day at 9:30 a.m. going through begging letters, personally determining sums. In Frankfurt, Wilhelm Carl processed 20-30 petitions daily from "all over the Jewish world."
- After 1859, some charity work was delegated to the new Board of Guardians for the Relief of the Jewish Poor. In London, Asher Asher (a Scottish doctor) acted as Lionel's "private almoner" — "virtually the manager of the 'Charitable Department' of New Court." In Paris, "a special office" recorded and investigated requests for help.
- Rothschild women took an increasingly active philanthropic role. The Jews' Free School was a major focus — Charlotte and Louisa became personally involved in the 1850s-60s. Charlotte found the children "indescribably dingy and dirty" but couldn't help becoming "deeply interested in their progress." By the 1870s, her efforts (along with Anthony's) had trebled the number of pupils and raised the budget twenty-fold.
- Other institutions: Jews' College (1855), Sabbath schools, Borough Jewish schools. Louisa established a Jewish Convalescent Home; Charlotte a Home for Aged Incurables, reorganized the London Lying-in Charity, and was president of the Ladies' Benevolent Loan Society.
- Charlotte's dying words: "Remember the Poor." Her memorial sermon by Hermann Adler noted she distinguished between charitable "giving" and specifically religious donations.
- James's most marked change: after 1848 he became far more active in Jewish communal life. In 1850 he created a Jewish hospital at 76 rue Picpus. On December 20, 1852, the hospital — designed by Jean-Alexandre Thierry — was formally opened, described as "one of the grandest [ceremonies] that Judaism has ever celebrated within our midst." He also contributed to a new Romano-Byzantine synagogue in rue Notre-Dame-de-Nazareth and established two orphanages.
- The Rothschilds contributed extensively to a hospital in Jerusalem established in the 1850s by Albert Cohn — no fewer than eleven Rothschilds appear on the donor list. The family paid 122,850 piastres in "voluntary contributions."

**Lionel Stands — The Campaign for Jewish Emancipation in Parliament:**
- The Oath of Abjuration containing "upon the true faith of a Christian" prevented Jews from taking seats in the Commons. This was only one of several barriers — matriculation at Oxford/Cambridge, and social exclusion from clubs and country houses.
- The campaign was not purely instrumental (to maximize political leverage). Lionel, influenced by his mother Hannah, fought it as a matter of principle. James saw it as a symbolic battle on behalf of all Jews, in lineal succession to Mayer Amschel's battles in Frankfurt.
- It was not Rothschilds but others who won early victories: David Salomons became Sheriff of London (1835); Francis Henry Goldsmid was first Jew admitted to the Bar; Moses Montefiore was knighted then made baronet.
- Nathan had taken up the cause after the Catholic emancipation act of 1829-30. The Rothschilds became disillusioned with the Tories when they blocked Jewish emancipation. After Nathan's death, emancipation bills introduced by Robert Grant were repeatedly thrown out by the Lords.
- Wellington warned Peel: "The Rothschilds are not without their political objects... The old lady [Hannah] and Mr Lionel. They have long been anxious for support to the petitions of the Jews for concessions of political privileges." Nat's support for Peel was conditional: "I trust he will be liberally inclined... towards us poor Jews & if he is not he cannot expect our support."
- Paradox of 1848: while the Rothschilds were vilified on the continent as props of reaction, they were leading a liberal campaign for legal equality in Britain. Jewish emancipation had been one of the achievements of the Frankfurt parliament, though it was subsequently rescinded in 1852.
- There was rivalry with other Jewish elite members (David Salomons notably) — the Rothschilds were partly pursuing their own claim to be "the royal family" of Judaism.

**Lionel's reluctant entry into politics:**
- Lionel hesitated to follow Salomons into direct political activity. The practical reason: politics took time the senior partner of N. M. Rothschild & Sons could not spare. James shared the view (expressed as early as 1816) that "as soon as a merchant takes too much part in public affairs it is difficult for him to carry on with his bankers' business."
- Yet family pressure was considerable. James sought to encourage nephews to accumulate titles and decorations. Anselm hoped "in a year or two to be able to congratulate one of you on a seat in Parliament." When Isaac Lyon Goldsmid became the first Jewish baronet in 1841, Anthony wrote that he "should have liked Sir Lionel de R. much better."
- Lionel stubbornly refused Lord John Russell's offer of a baronetcy — he would accept nothing less than a peerage. Prince Albert reportedly said: "[Y]ou have nothing higher to offer me?" His mother Hannah was incensed at the refusal. The compromise: Anthony accepted the baronetcy instead, with "hesitation."

**Lionel's election (1847):**
- Adopted as Liberal London Registration Association candidate on June 29, 1847 — a watershed in Rothschild history. Multiple possible explanations: familial pressure proved irresistible; Lord John Russell may have talked him into it to secure Jewish votes; Lionel may not have expected to win — it was intended as a cause célèbre.
- The City of London constituency was large (nearly 50,000 votes), returning four MPs. Nine candidates stood. Lionel's platform: "liberty of religious conscience" plus free trade. He argued for lower duties on tobacco and tea and a property tax.
- Lionel had two key advantages: press support (John Thadeus Delane, the 29-year-old editor of The Times, helped draft his election address) and money. Lord Grey, the Whig Secretary for War, "made no secret of his determination to carry his election by money." Nat's letters confirm "large sums" were "cashed up."
- Lionel was elected third in the poll with 6,792 votes. Charlotte was in "a state of almost frenzied delight and gratitude." Nat called it "one of the greatest triumphs for the Family as well as of the greatest advantage to the poor Jews in Germany and all over the world." Congratulations came even from Metternich.
- But the euphoria overlooked that Lionel still had to swear the oath "upon the true faith of the Christian" to take his seat — unless Parliament changed the law.

**Disraeli and the Rothschilds:**
- Disraeli had known the Rothschilds for nearly a decade by 1847. By 1844-5 he and Mary Anne were dining with them frequently. In 1846, Lionel helped Disraeli speculate in French railways and later assisted with his debts (in excess of £5,000).
- The friendship was more than financial — it was Disraeli's most creative period as novelist. *Coningsby* (1844), *Sybil* (1845), and *Tancred* (1847) all drew heavily on the Rothschilds. The character of Sidonia in *Coningsby* is a cross between Lionel and Disraeli himself — Lionel's background, profession, religion, temperament, and perhaps even looks ("pale, with an impressive brow, and dark eyes of great intelligence"), but Disraeli's political and philosophical views.
- Sidonia's famous speech: "So you see my dear Coningsby, that the world is governed by very different personages from what is imagined by those who are not behind the scenes." This is unmistakably Rothschildian.
- In *Tancred*, the character Eva — a Syrian-Jewish princess — may have been modeled partly on Charlotte. Eva's views echo Rothschild attitudes: "The Hebrews have never blended with their conquerors... No; I will never become a Christian!" And the common origins of Judaism and Christianity: "Half of Christendom worships a Jewess, and the other half a Jew."
- There was an undertone of frustrated attraction in Charlotte's relationship with Disraeli, as well as jealous impatience with Mary Anne. Disraeli told Charlotte in 1867: "Amid the struggles of my life... the sympathy of those we love is balm, and there is no one I love more than you."
- Mary Anne's bizarre 1845 visit: she flung herself into Charlotte's arms, announced she was making six-year-old Evelina the sole beneficiary of her will, and produced the document from her pocket: "'In the event of my beloved Husband preceding me to the grave, I leave and bequeath to Evelina de Rothschild all my personal property.' ... 'I love the Jews — I have attached myself to your children and she is my favourite.'"

**Parliament and Peers — the battle for the oath:**
- Russell's bill to remove parliamentary disabilities attracted support from both sides — Gladstone and the Protectionist leaders Bentinck and Disraeli all spoke in favour (December 1847). But Bentinck's support split the Protectionist party. No fewer than 138 diehards voted against, led by Sir Robert Inglis. Bentinck resigned the leadership, leaving "the No Popery, No Jew Party" in the hands of Lord Stanley.
- Disraeli faced the formidable constraint of leading a party many of whose members were hostile to emancipation. He was widely regarded as "conscience-less" (Dickens's phrase). But he continued to vote and occasionally speak in favor. His financial dependence on Lionel may have precluded a U-turn — Charlotte suspected as much. Mary Anne revealed "that her husband was still deeply in debt and was being hounded terribly by the money-lenders and implored my husband for help and support." Lionel lent Disraeli a further £1,000.
- Gladstone found the decision to support emancipation "painful" but his logic was rigorous: having admitted Catholics, Quakers, Moravians, Separatists, and Unitarians, maintaining the ban on Jews was inconsistent. Peel himself spoke in favor in February 1848.
- The bill passed the Commons (first reading by 67, second by 73, third by 61) but was repeatedly rejected by the Lords. The bishops led the opposition — the Archbishop of Canterbury, the Bishop of Oxford (Wilberforce), and sixteen bishops voted against. Only four Whiggish bishops voted in favour. The measure was rejected by a majority of 35.

**Charlotte's dream — the epigraph explained:**
- Charlotte's diary after the Lords' defeat (May 1848): "the men came into the room, Lionel with a smiling face — he always has so much firmness and self-control — Anthony and Mayer crimson in the face... I went to sleep at 5 and woke again at 6; I had dreamt that a huge vampire was greedily sucking my blood... Surely we do not deserve so much hatred. I spent all day Friday weeping and sobbing out of over-excitement."
- The Duke of Cumberland (Queen's uncle, now King of Hanover) argued against emancipation, predicting "the whole of the riches of the country would by degrees come into the hands of the Jews, manufacturers and calico-makers."

**Lionel's bribery and Prince Albert:**
- Lionel proposed to buy votes in the House of Lords. Nat wrote (December 23, 1846): "I regret much to observe that you think it necessary to use certain means to secure some votes in the House of Lords which are not peculiarly commendable... we must not be too scrupulous and if it be necessary to ensure the success of the measure we must not mind a sacrifice."
- Lionel also sought Prince Albert's support (whose influence in the Lords was considerable). The Rothschilds had financial dealings with Albert: James invested 100,000 francs in Nord shares for Albert's adviser Baron Stockmar (1842). In 1847 they gave Albert's impecunious relative Prince Oettingen-Wallerstein a £3,000 loan which Albert personally guaranteed — and which the Prince defaulted on. Lionel contributed £50,000 to subsidize Albert's Great Exhibition of 1851. It appears Albert and Stockmar then helped induce Lord Aberdeen to drop his opposition to emancipation.
- Russell was blunt about the limits of bribery: "You have such an abominable habit of assigning to anything a money value that you seem to think even principles may be purchased."

**The dramatic oath scene (July 26, 1850):**
- Lionel appeared before the Commons, was asked whether he wished to subscribe the Protestant or Catholic oath, and replied: "I desire to be sworn upon the Old Testament." The Tory diehard Sir Robert Inglis protested. Lionel was asked why; he replied: "Because that is the form of swearing which I declare to be most binding upon my conscience." He was asked to withdraw.
- July 30: Lionel appeared again, took the oaths of allegiance and supremacy on the Old Testament, but when the clerk reached "upon the true faith of a Christian," he paused and said: "I omit these words, as not binding upon my conscience." He placed his hat on his head, kissed the Old Testament, and said "So help me, God." Loud cheers from the Liberal side. But Sir Frederick Thesiger rose to protest, and the Speaker required him to withdraw.
- This amounted to another defeat. The Lords rejected another bill in 1849 (by 95 to 25). The waiting game continued through the 1852 election (Lionel re-elected, Salomons defeated).

**Final triumph (1858):**
- After Lionel's 1857 re-election, Palmerston pushed another bill (majority of 123 on third reading). Key Tories now signaled a change of heart. But the Lords again blocked it. Disraeli, now Chancellor of the Exchequer in Derby's minority government, was at last able to repay his debts to the Rothschilds by persuading the reluctant Derby to compromise.
- The Earl of Lucan proposed the solution: each House could change its own oath of admission by resolution. On July 23, 1858, this became law. On July 26, Lionel appeared in the Commons for the last time, took the shortened oath, and was at last sworn in as a member — eleven years after his first election. The first piece of legislation he voted on: The Corrupt Practices Prevention Act Continuance Bill.
- James called it "a real triumph for the family." Lionel's brother Mayer joined him the following year; in 1865 his son Natty was elected. Disraeli was heard to remark after narrowly avoiding a government defeat over India: "What does the Baron say about it? He knows most things!"
- Lionel and Disraeli dined together in the decisive weeks. Lionel found Disraeli "in excellent spirits" and was told "our Bill would pass next Monday." Disraeli claimed credit: "it was by the greatest chance possible that we had this division for us instead of against us on the 2nd reading of our Bill — he worked all he could for us — so he said."

**Political significance:**
- Lionel had triumphed as a Liberal, forging political and social links with a group of Liberal MPs. Gladstone dined with the Rothschilds four times between 1856-1864. Regular Liberal visitors to 148 Piccadilly included Charles Villiers (President of the Poor Law Board) and Robert Lowe (Chancellor in Gladstone's first ministry). Lionel's first act after taking his seat was to shake hands with Disraeli — whose contribution in the final phase "may well have been decisive."

**Cambridge:**
- The Rothschilds did not need to go to Cambridge — their education was cosmopolitan, relying on private tutors and travel. Learning banking meant working in the bank. But they continued to attach importance to daughters' education too.
- Mayer had attended Trinity in the 1830s on a minimalistic basis, attending chapel passively. When Arthur Cohen (a cousin) sought to read mathematics at Cambridge in 1849, Mayer sought to bend the chapel rules through J. Abel Smith and the Master of Christ's, James Cartmell. Cartmell refused: "It would be a breach of good faith to conceal Cohen's religion."
- Mayer and Moses Montefiore turned to Prince Albert (Chancellor of the University), who pressured the Master of Magdalene to admit Cohen. Royal pressure succeeded where Rothschild pressure had failed. Cohen was admitted on a deal with the Dean: attend chapel on Wednesdays and Fridays only (10 minutes each), no attendance required on Sacrament Sundays.
- When Natty went up in 1859, the acts of 1854 and 1856 allowed Jews to take degrees (except in theology). But religious obligations persisted at the college level. Natty's tutor Joseph Lightfoot "promised to do all he can about Chapel." In 1862 Natty reported that "the Trinity Dons made themselves very unpopular by threatening to gate everyone who refuses to take the sacrament in Chapel."
- The "Little Go" exam required detailed knowledge of William Paley's *Evidences of Christianity*. Charlotte was furious when Leo apparently botched it.
- The Rothschild presence at Cambridge was a qualified victory — it was not until 1871 that the final religious tests were abolished. There was a "marked and not easily explicable contrast" between the willingness to attend chapel and study Paley, versus the refusal to swear the parliamentary oath.

**Great Exhibitions and Crystal Palaces — Country houses:**
- Between 1850 and 1860, the Rothschilds built no fewer than four immense country houses and rebuilt a fifth: Mentmore, Aston Clinton, Ferrières, Pregny, and Boulogne. These were more than imitations of eighteenth-century country houses — they were "advertisements for Rothschild power, five-star hotels for influential guests, private art galleries: in short, centres for corporate hospitality."
- They bought land in the wake of the great agricultural crisis of the mid-1840s — "going in at the bottom of the market." The Duke of Buckingham went bankrupt in 1848; Mayer received tips from Ireland: "Potatoes failing all directions and free trade ruining everybody... Ireland completely ruined, now is the time or at least it is fast approaching for buying estates on the sly."
- Land purchases were rational investments too. By December 1849 consol yields had fallen to 3.1% — it was "the most proper time to buy land when the funds lie so high."
- Wine estates: Nat purchased Château Brane-Mouton in 1853 (renamed Mouton-Rothschild). James fought a long battle to gain control of Château Lafite near Pauillac, finally securing it in 1868 for £177,600 — and almost immediately began bidding up the price of the new vintage.

**The grand houses:**
- Joseph Paxton, designer of the Crystal Palace, was chosen to build Mentmore for Mayer (begun August 1851). Elizabethan-inspired but with a huge glass-roofed hall, hot running water and central heating. Twenty-six rooms on the ground floor alone — "essentially a hotel" for entertaining. Trophy-like busts of European sovereigns by sculptor Raphael Monti. Three massive lanterns originally made for the Doge of Venice, Gobelin tapestries, antique furniture from sixteenth-century Italy and eighteenth-century France. Builder George Myers charged just £350,000 for all Rothschild work between 1853-1873.
- Aston Clinton (for Anthony) was botched by Paxton's son-in-law George Henry Stokes. Louisa thought it "at first sight the ugliest on earth."
- Ferrières (for James) was designed to trump Mentmore. James called in Paxton and Myers but then sought a second opinion from French architect Antoine-Julien Hénard. The result (completed 1860): an eclectic mixture of French, Italian, and English styles with over eighty rooms. James moved the kitchens a hundred yards away, building a small underground railway to connect them. The Goncourts loathed it: "an idiotic and ridiculous extravagance, a pudding of every style." Bismarck thought it "an overturned chest of drawers." Yet it was state-of-the-art, with Cordier's caryatids symbolizing Rothschild dominance over the four quarters of the globe.
- Pregny (for Adolph): Louis XVI-style overlooking Lake Geneva, primarily a showcase for art and objets d'art. Boulogne (by Berthelin, 1855): inspired by the Versailles of Louis XIV.
- Gardens: Paxton transformed Ferrières with ornamental bridges, greenhouses, and winter gardens. Charlotte called it "fairyland... the Palace of Aladdin." At Boulogne, landscape gardener Poyre built water gardens with "geese with curled feathers, white ducks, Egyptian donkeys and a talking parrot." Pregny had Patagonian hares, kangaroos, and antelopes. The animal collections foreshadowed later Rothschilds' passion for zoology.

**London houses:**
- Lionel acquired 148 Piccadilly from MP Fitzroy Kelly and commissioned Nelson & Innes to rebuild it. The basement was wine cellar and male servants' quarters; ground floor a spacious hall; marble staircase to huge reception rooms; kitchens under the terrace garden.
- Art collecting was never-ending. Charlotte's Parisian shopping: marble group (£2,000), small statues, crystal chandelier, busts of Roman Emperors, "two marvellous vases of rosso antico... exhibiting Neptune surrounded by tritons and sea-nymphs" (5,000 guineas). Nat reported from a Paris auction in 1869: "A trumpery little Raphael [for] 150,000 francs — the Cuyp 92,000 francs... One must have plenty of money now to buy pictures."
- New Court offices rebuilt in the early 1860s — Charlotte thought the new building "quite marvellous, and intended for magnificent business."

## Chapter 2: The Era of Mobility (1849-1858)

**Epigraph:** Cavour: "[I] would be charmed to make a niche for this Jew who is strangling us."

- The 1850s are traditionally seen as a difficult time for the Rothschilds — four challenges: (1) Louis Napoleon Bonaparte overthrew the republic and proclaimed himself Emperor; (2) James's financial rival Achille Fould became Finance Minister (Fould told Napoleon: "It is absolutely necessary that Your Majesty free yourself from the tutelage of Rothschild"); (3) the Crédit Mobilier threatened Rothschild dominance; (4) major wars between great powers became reality for the first time since 1815 (Crimea, Italy).
- But this account is misleading: historians have relied too heavily on biased sources like Count Hübner's diaries, overstating James's difficulties under Napoleon. The problems should not be seen in isolation when the other Rothschild houses were prospering.

**Two Emperors — James and Napoleon III:**
- Betty initially found General Changarnier — their key political contact — rather two-faced. But she accommodated herself to Napoleon as President: "Every day he gives certain proofs of [his belief in] the principle of order and legal authority."
- Betty developed an intense admiration for Changarnier, whom she saw as "protected by our worthy Changarnier" — a hero who would "hold the madmen in check." James was more cautiously amused, noting that Changarnier "has never got mixed up [in speculation] and he has never said to me that he wants to speculate."
- James feared Napoleon's erratic foreign policy might lead to war between France and Britain or Prussia. He called Napoleon "an ass... who would end up turning the whole world against him." He dread ending up "in the hands of the reds."
- As the coup d'état of December 1-2, 1851 approached, James began remitting gold to London: "I would rather have all my gold over there earning 3 per cent on deposit than put it in rentes or keep it in the cellar, when a man like that [Napoleon] might take my money away." On the night of the coup, James literally fell down the stairs and sprained his ankle — he was "quite literally prostrate when the Bonapartists struck."
- Betty was furious: "far from saving society, he will destroy it by applying his personal rule." Yet James was pragmatic: he accepted the new situation with "great resignation."
- The coup was actually good for business: rentes leapt from 56 to 64 (3%) and from 90.5 to 102.5 (5%) immediately after. By the first anniversary, 3% rentes stood at 83 — a capital gain of nearly 50%. Railway investment increased by a factor of five between 1851-1856.
- James's prescient summary (October 1850): "In the end we shall have an Emperor, which will end with war, for if I wasn't so afraid of war, then I would be an imperialist myself."
- After the coup, James quickly adapted: "My good nephews, how would you like a French constitution for two sous? They're being sold in the streets for that here." By October 1852 he was "on the best footing with the Emperor and everyone."
- James championed the cause of Eugénie de Montijo, who more snobbish Parisians assumed would merely be Napoleon's next mistress. At a Tuileries ball on January 12, 1853, Eugénie arrived on the arm of James himself — one of his sons (presumably Alphonse) escorted her mother. Three days later Napoleon proposed; a week later they married.

**The Crédit Mobilier:**
- Founded November 20, 1852 — two weeks before Napoleon proclaimed himself Emperor. Its significance has been misunderstood: it was not primarily a political challenge to the Rothschilds, and it was not a revolutionary new kind of bank.
- Joint-stock banks had been legal in Britain since 1826; by the time the Crédit Mobilier was founded there were already about a hundred joint-stock banks in England and Wales alone. Nor did British joint-stock banks eschew long-term lending — they extended overdrafts and discounted bills in ways that were effectively long-term.
- The Crédit Mobilier was essentially an investment trust — a Pereire-led group with capital of 20 million francs (later 60 million), attracting savings of smaller investors into railways. Its initial capital was smaller than de Rothschild Frères (88 million francs) and far smaller than the combined Rothschild houses (230 million francs). The Pereires themselves accounted for only 29% of its initial capital.
- The real difference was cultural: the Pereires used Saint-Simonian rhetoric about collective industrial benefit while speculating in rentes and railway shares. The Rothschilds made no secret of their profit motive and regarded charitable contributions as separate from business.
- Stockbroker Feydeau's vivid contrast: James was "simply a solid, intelligent and astute 'capital merchant'" for whom "the sole task of maximising the return on his colossal fortune constituted for him a round-the-clock occupation." At the Crédit Mobilier offices: "Acidulously polite men, ulcerated by hatred, always concentrating, hard and tense like bars of iron... Everyone wanted to get rich."
- James commissioned Feydeau to buy a thousand Crédit Mobilier shares no fewer than five times, paying in full each time. When Feydeau expressed incredulity, James feigned surprise: "I haf the greatest possible confitence in the chenius of Messrs Pereire... I only regret one thing, and that is that I cannot entrust all off my capital to such clefer men."
- The rivalry was characterized in cultural terms: Rothschild as "the aristocracy of money" and "financial feudalism"; the Pereires as "financial democracy and an economic '1789.'"

**Railway concessions under the Empire:**
- Napoleon's coup immediately revived railway construction: the very day after the seizure of power, the concession for the Lyon-Mediterranean line was granted, followed two days later by the Paris-Lyon consortium (in which both Rothschild houses participated). The Nord concession was renegotiated on terms singularly favorable to the company. Twenty-five concessions were awarded between 1852-1857, plus thirty more by 1870.
- The Rothschild-controlled Nord was the most intensively utilized and most profitable of the six big French lines: only 9% of total network length but carried 14% of freight and 12% of all passenger traffic. Traffic volume more than doubled between the 1850s and 1860s.
- James explained his strategy: "I do not want a major operation to take place under a new government without our name being on it. If such an operation were to succeed without us, then people would say 'We don't need Rothschild any more.'"
- The Pereires' break with the Rothschilds came gradually: first signs of dissension in 1849 when they sought to raise money for Paris-Lyon-Avignon without reference to the Rothschilds. James took 12% of the Paris-Lyon syndicate — an unambiguous rejection of the Pereires' rival scheme. The Crédit Mobilier was the Pereires' response to their exclusion from this new Talabot-Rothschild axis backed by the regime's man, Morny.
- Between them, the Pereires had eight seats on boards of various French railway companies; the Rothschilds had fourteen. Lines of battle were far less distinct than has often been claimed.
- Crédit Mobilier shares opened at 1,100 (500 franc par), touched 1,600 four days later, peaked at 1,982 in March 1856. Dividends rose from 13% in 1853 to 40% two years later (implying earnings of 4-10%). These were massive capital gains for original shareholders.

**James's objections to the Crédit Mobilier:**
- James wrote personally to Napoleon (November 15, 1852) warning that the bank would be "excessively powerful and excessively vulnerable to crises." He predicted that if it got into difficulties, the government would have to choose between "a general bankruptcy" or suspending gold and silver convertibility.
- He also raised the classic conservative objection: joint-stock directors would be "anonymous" and "irresponsible," concentrating "the greater part of the national wealth" in their hands. "The bank when it is fully active will be stronger than the government itself."
- Some of the Rothschilds' closest associates (Torlonia, Oppenheim, Heine) were among the Crédit Mobilier's shareholders — suggesting the anti-Rothschild thesis was overstated.
- In truth, the Crédit Mobilier was more of a challenge to the Banque de France than to the Rothschilds. It claimed to be introducing "a new fiduciary money" — essentially a quasi-monetary function that alarmed the central bank. This began the alliance between Rothschilds and the Banque de France, consummated when Alphonse became a regent of the Banque in 1855.
- The Crédit Mobilier risked overstretching: it was undercapitalized relative to its ambitions. As early as 1853 it sought to issue 120 million francs in bonds, but the government vetoed it. The bank increasingly relied on 60-100 million francs of conventional deposits from railway companies.

**The Crédit Mobilier goes pan-European:**
- What made the Crédit Mobilier genuinely threatening was its expansion beyond France. On April 2, 1853, the Darmstädter Bank fur Handel und Industrie was founded — essentially the German Crédit Mobilier. Capital set at 25 million gulden (~54 million francs), Pereire-style charter, located less than twenty miles south of Frankfurt. Among the nine directors were four from Frankfurt, including the Rothschilds' old rival Moritz Bethmann.
- The Pereires and Foulds were directly involved — 10,000 shares sold at par to Crédit Mobilier shareholders, with a majority shareholding held jointly by Oppenheim, Mevissen, Fould, and the Crédit Mobilier. By 1854, talk of Belgian, Austrian, and other satellites. In 1855-56, thirteen new joint-stock banks were established in German states alone, including Hansemann's Disconto-Gesellschaft, the Berliner Handelsgesellschaft, and the Vereinsbank.

**New competitors beyond the Pereires:**
- Traditional private and merchant banks posed a more enduring threat. In London: Schröders, Frühling & Goschen, C. J. Hambro & Son (1839), Overend Gurney & Co., Kleinwort & Cohen (1855). In Frankfurt: Erlanger & Söhne, Jacob S. H. Stern, Lazard Speyer-Ellissen, Moritz B. Goldschmidt (1851), Gebrüder Sulzbach (1856). In Paris: Lazard Frères (1854).

**The telegraph revolution:**
- The Dover-Calais submarine cable (1851) was the real watershed. Before it was laid, Julius Reuter wrote to New Court offering exclusive transmission of Berlin and Vienna exchange rates. James repeatedly complained: "the telegraph is ruining our business." The Rothschilds had stolen a march on rivals with couriers and carrier pigeons; now "anyone can get the news."
- James lamented: "One has too much to think about when bathing, which is not good." Yet the Rothschilds adapted, continuing to write letters in their accustomed fashion right up until World War I, while reluctantly using the telegraph.
- By the 1860s, Frankfurt families like the Speyers, Sterns, and Erlangers had established branches in London and Paris using the telegraph, and the Speyers in New York as well.

**The Gold Rush and Global Expansion:**
- In continents beyond the telegraph's reach (no link to North America until 1866, Latin America until 1869, Australia until 1873), the Rothschilds' traditional system of semi-autonomous agents remained unsurpassed. European agents: Weisweiller and Bauer (Madrid), Samuel Lambert (Brussels), Horaz Landau (Constantinople then Italy).
- The failure to establish a proper New York house was "arguably the Rothschilds' single greatest strategic mistake." Betty pushed hard in 1849 for Alphonse to go: "wouldn't it be a great help to the future of our family to set up a House in New York, a House which would bear our name... America's future appears so grandiose." Alphonse visited and spoke of "putting affairs over there on a more convenient footing." But he could not bring himself to relinquish Parisian comforts. Belmont, by then married to Commodore Perry's daughter and American ambassador to the Hague, had become too well-established to replace.
- On the West Coast, Benjamin Davidson was sent from Mexico to California with a £40,000 credit when gold was discovered. A clerk named May was sent from Frankfurt to join him. Davidson's brother later reported profits of £37,762 in just two years. The San Francisco agent lived in "a shanty built over his vault, like a pig in a sty."
- Mexico: Nathaniel Davidson sent to replace Benjamin. Important business: loans, mercury and coal mines, an iron foundry. Mexico became the object of French imperial ambitions in 1860-61.
- Brazil: became an exceptionally monogamous financial relationship. War with Argentina and Uruguay in 1851 forced Brazil to borrow. Between 1852 and 1914, the London house generated bond issues worth no less than £142 million for the Brazilian government.
- Asia: After the Opium Wars (1839-42), the London house established regular correspondence with a Shanghai merchant firm, Cramptons, Hanbury & Co., shipping silver from Mexico to China. By the later 1850s, correspondence with Calcutta firm Schoene, Kilburn & Co. It was "a pardonable exaggeration to say that 'the entire universe paid tribute [to Rothschild]; he had his offices in China, in India, in even the least civilised countries.'"
- Australia: gold discovered in New South Wales and Victoria in 1851. The Rothschilds were urged to establish a branch. Instead they relied on Jacob Montefiore as Melbourne correspondent — who went bankrupt in 1855 owing a substantial sum to the London house. Jeffrey Cullen was sent as agent, eagerly seeking consignments of mercury and alcohol.

**Gold refining:**
- James had operated a refinery in Paris since 1827 and partnered with Dierickx, the Master of the Paris Mint, in 1843. New gold discoveries made it "a revolution in the money market." Lionel resolved to enter the gold-refining business directly.
- In London, Lionel proposed to lease the Royal Mint's refinery. He lobbied political allies Russell and Abel Smith about reforming the Mint's system. Anthony acquired the lease in January 1852. By December, Lionel was asking the Bank of England's Governor Hankey "to be permitted to present directly to the Bank of England my gold and silver bars, refined and melted under my responsibility."
- In its first year the refinery processed over 300,000 ounces of Australian gold and 450,000 ounces of Californian. Gladstone visited in 1862. Control of refining and minting on both sides of the Channel enabled a unique "system" of arbitrage: London bought American/Australian gold on the French house's account, relaying it to Paris via bullion brokers. Paris bought silver for New Court, relaying via London or Southampton to the East. By the late 1850s this was becoming integral to the bimetallic international monetary system.

**Public Finance and the Crimean War:**
- For decades the Rothschilds had regarded a major European war as the greatest danger to their financial position — worse even than revolution. The Crimean War (March 1854) had origins in a dispute over the Holy Places in Jerusalem.
- Financial impact (Table 2a): British consols fell 15% (from 101.38 to 85.75); French 4.5% rentes fell 15%; Austrian 5% metalliques fell 24%; Prussian 3.5% bonds fell 11%.
- Public spending surged (Table 2b): Austria +42%, Britain +69%, France +53%, Russia +88% between 1852-1855.
- The Rothschilds looked worried. Their St. Petersburg correspondent had reassured them in June 1853 there would be no war. When Foreign Minister Clarendon saw Lionel on September 27 (shortly after ordering the fleet to the Straits), Lionel told him "he never remember[ed] such a day" in the City. Bismarck deliberately told news of the Russian ambassador's recall to Amschel: "He turned as white as chalk... His first remark was, 'If only I had known it this morning'; his second, 'Will you do a little business with me tomorrow?'"

**War finance — a bonanza for the Rothschilds:**
- Far from weakening their position, the Crimean War emphatically reasserted Rothschild primacy in public finance. Short wars created financial opportunities their multinational structure was especially well placed to exploit. It helped that Barings — their old rival — was banker to the losing side (Russia).
- Britain: Gladstone as Chancellor had pledged to finance the war by taxation, not loans ("wholesale systematic deception upon the people"). But the national debt stood at £782 million. Income tax was raised from 7d to 14d. By the time he resigned, there was a £6.2 million deficit. His successor Lewis faced a shortfall four times larger. The government had no alternative but to turn to New Court. In 1855, the London house took a £16 million loan. In February 1856 it submitted the only tender for £5 million more, and in May secured a final tranche of £5 million. The 1856 loans were heavily oversubscribed (by a factor of nearly six).
- France: Napoleon announced a large conversion operation in March 1852, cutting interest from 5% to 4.5%. A sudden slump in 5% rentes required a bank-led support operation — Hottinguer and de Rothschild Frères (not the Pereires) took the largest share. When France needed to borrow 250 million francs in 1854, the Crédit Mobilier competed. Mires later claimed credit for persuading the government to sell by public subscription. But by April 1855, with 750 million more needed, Finance Minister Magne told Napoleon the domestic market was saturated. The Rothschilds were once again in charge: the Paris house handled 60 million francs and the London house received subscriptions totalling 208.5 million.
- James gloated (April 1856): "The Emperor is excessively displeased to see that the birth of a prince and the conclusion of peace are not having a better effect on public credit, and that it might be said that he has been forced to make peace for want of money."
- Turkey: Horaz Landau had been sent as agent to Constantinople. In February 1855 he negotiated a £5 million loan guaranteed by France and Britain, drip-feeding short-term advances in classic Rothschild fashion. After the war, Alphonse was dispatched to discuss a new bank, but the 1857 crisis led to a retreat.
- Austria: Though not fighting, Austria's military preparations had a financial impact equivalent to outright war. Austrian bonds fell 24%. Defence spending and debt service consumed 60-80% of revenues. Between 1848-1865, funded public debt rose from 1.1 billion to 2.5 billion gulden. The gulden (decoupled from silver in 1848) depreciated 9-36% below par between 1853-54. In November 1851, Finance Minister Krauss begged James for advice: "requesting him to shed some light on the situation." Apponyi urged James "not just to shed some light, but to take a torch." Anselm tried to rebuild but was haunted by his father's downfall: "You mark my words, we are on the eve of a crisis; if something is not done to avert it, it will be upon us before the new year!"
- Still, successes: in 1852 the London and Frankfurt houses issued Austrian 5% bonds worth £3.5 million. In April 1854, during a currency run, Anselm organized a 34 million gulden credit (nearly half from Fould).

**Prussia and Bismarck:**
- Relations disrupted by a petty quarrel over the German Confederation's "fortress money" deposit with the Frankfurt house. Bismarck (as Prussian delegate to the Diet) made life as difficult as possible for Amschel, caught between Austrian and Prussian instructions. Thun threatened to move the account to another banker; Bismarck said he'd transfer the Prussian delegation's account to Bethmann.
- Bismarck's cynical flexibility: after goading Amschel, he quickly changed tone once the dispute was resolved. He began speaking up for the Rothschilds against Austrian-backed protests. He proposed that Mayer Carl be granted the Red Eagle of the Third Class — to woo the Rothschilds away from Austria. A bureaucratic debate erupted: should the cross be redesigned for a Jew? Manteuffel overruled objections: "the Prussians needed the Rothschilds."
- Mayer Carl hinted to Bismarck he'd be grateful for "a possibility of placing his money at 3 1/2 per cent." When Prussia nearly went to war in 1854, Manteuffel sent his adviser Niebuhr to negotiate a 15 million thaler loan (which fell through). In 1856 Mayer Carl placed 7 million thalers. Bismarck endorsed the idea of routing Prussian interest payments through Frankfurt.

**Papal finance:**
- The Rothschilds financed the Pope's restoration, hoping to secure concessions for Roman Jews. The Vatican refused to make the loan conditional on Jewish emancipation, though the Pope privately guaranteed the ghetto would be abolished. Final terms were exceptionally generous given Papal insolvency. In 1857 an ambitious attempt was made to consolidate the Papal debt: new 5% bonds worth 142.4 million francs floated in Paris (~40% of total Papal debt of 350 million francs).
- The paradox: substantial profits could be made as long as the Holy See did not reform its finances; but if it couldn't reform, it was unlikely to reform its treatment of Jews. The Rothschilds opted for profits over principles.

**Cavour and Piedmont-Sardinia:**
- In 1849 James secured a substantial loan to Piedmont, which dismayed the ambitious young politician Cavour. The 1850 loan was "the most beautiful deal I've ever made" — apart from 2.5% commission, James took 20 million lire of 5% rentes *à forfait* at a price he could sell at a premium.
- Cavour declared: "It would delight me to play a trick on that Jew who has us by the jugular." As Finance Minister from April 1851, he tried to break the Rothschild grip. Facing 25 million lire owed to James plus a 20 million budget deficit, he ordered his London ambassador to find a new banker: "We must at all costs extricate ourselves from the painful position in which we are placed with regard to the House of Rothschild."
- Cavour found Hambro willing to issue £3.6 million of Piedmontese bonds at 85. James fought back: he was behind a negative Times report on Piedmontese finances and was selling Piedmontese bonds with all his energy. His wordplay: "L'emprunt est ouvert, mais non couvert" (the loan has opened, but is not subscribed). Yet James could not buck the market he himself had created: "We can do what we like, but we cannot stop the Piedmontese from rising, as it was we who issued them at 85."
- Cavour's famous backhanded compliment (January 1855): "To do Rothschild justice, it must be said that he never asks for money. That is his better side." By February 1856, James emerged as the main foreign shareholder in the new Piedmontese investment bank, to the dismay of the Pereires. With astonishing prescience, James was already preparing to finance the next European war — between Austria and Piedmont.

**The Counter-Attack (rest of Ch2):**
- The 1857 crash (triggered by Ohio Life and Trust Co.) spread across the Atlantic. Glasgow, Liverpool, Hamburg hit hard. Peabody & Co. in London survived only via £800,000 Bank of England loan. Rothschilds were barely affected — London house profits for 1857 were down (to £8,000) but still positive; Naples house did better.
- French monetary policy was key to Rothschild counter-attack against the Pereires. Alphonse's election as regent of Banque de France in 1855 was a watershed — the Rothschilds were probably the Banque's biggest shareholders (Paris house held 1,000+ shares, peaking at 1,616 in 1864; individual family members held up to 200 more).
- The Banque's charter debate was crucial: Rothschilds argued against the Pereires' expansionist monetary schemes. Final outcome: Banque doubled capital, could raise discount rate above 6% — priority was exchange rate stability over Credit Mobilier-style expansion.
- James launched the Réunion Financière (1856) — a confederation of private banks to challenge the Pereires. His plan for a new joint-stock bank was blocked by Finance Minister Magne.
- Pan-European railway contest: Pereires came off second best after 1856-7. Railways became genuinely international, spilling across borders. In Belgium, James extended Nord influence over the Namur-Liège line. In Spain, James secured Madrid-Almansa concession (1855), created the Madrid, Zaragoza and Alicante Railway (opened May 1858). In Austria, the Creditanstalt (chartered November 1855) adopted Pereire methods — but under Rothschild control.
- Creditanstalt secured dominant position in Central European railway network. Lombard and Central Italian lines fell to the Rothschild side (1856). New Lombardo Venetian and Central Italian Railway Company launched with £6 million capital, half from London. The Rothschilds controlled 600+ miles of Italian railways.
- Russia was the one region the Rothschilds relinquished to rivals. James let Pereires take Russian railways, having received pessimistic profitability reports. Barings' Grande Société des Chemins de Fers Russes flopped.
- By end of 1858, the Pereires' challenge had been quashed across the continent. The Rothschilds were an authentic multinational; the Pereires were fundamentally Parisian.

## Chapter 3: Nationalism and the Multinational (1859-1863)

**The Italian War of 1859:**
- January 14, 1858: Austrian ambassador dining at Alphonse's when clerk brought urgent news — Italian terrorists had attempted to assassinate Napoleon III and Empress Eugénie (the Orsini affair).
- Napoleon used the Orsini execution to communicate sympathy with Italian nationalism. Met Cavour at Plombières (July 20, 1858) to redraw Italy's map: France would help create Upper Italy "from the Alps to the Adriatic" in return for Savoy.
- James was caught in an impossible position — financial involvement with France, Austria, AND Piedmont. He urged Napoleon to preserve peace while simultaneously calculating how to profit from both sides.
- James's famous bon mot as war loomed: "The Emperor does not know France. Twenty years ago a war might have been proclaimed without causing any great perturbation. But today everybody has his railway coupons or his three per cents. The Emperor meant peace, but what he does not know is that the Empire is done for if we have a war."
- "Entente fous," he concluded darkly, à la Nucingen — "bas de baix, bas d'embire."
- Lionel kept Disraeli closely informed via New Court intelligence. "The alarm in the City is very great: the whole of the Mediterranean trade is stopped. The reduced value of securities is not less than 60 millions sterling."

**The Finances of "Unification":**
- Between 1859-1871, wars confronted the Rothschilds with insoluble dilemmas — financial involvement with ALL sides. The "unifications" of Italy, Germany, and the US were really wars between multiple states with uncertain outcomes.
- Conventional view: the wars of the 1860s cost the Rothschilds dearly. Actually WRONG. Data shows the 1860s and 1870s were two of the London house's three most profitable decades before 1914 (Table 3a: annual profits £221,278 in 1860s, £468,308 in 1870s).
- Combined Rothschild houses' average annual profits rose to unprecedented levels 1852-1874 (£1,304,000 for 1852-62, £1,096,000 for 1862-74). The "unification years" were golden.
- Key insight: wars were fought by cash-strapped states — this INCREASED the role of bankers. Tax bases were narrow and shrinking (trade liberalization reduced tariffs). States had no alternative but to borrow.
- The Rothschilds looked worried publicly but privately calculated to ensure both sides paid for their financial services. James had "no hesitation" subscribing to the French 500 million franc "war loan" of 1858, while simultaneously the London house organized a £6 million loan to Austria.

**Italian Unification — Financial Aftermath:**
- Napoleon's hasty peace at Villafranca (July 12, 1859) left Cavour "high and dry" — Austria kept Venetia and the fortresses. Garibaldi's invasion of Naples (September 1860) put the Rothschild house there in an acutely difficult position. Adolph opted to flee with the Bourbon King. Naples house wound up in 1863.
- James's concept was "authentically supranational" — deaf to nationalist rhetoric. He thought of states as businesses. Where historians saw nation-building, James saw "mergers and demergers."
- For the Rothschilds, the most important consequence of Italian unification was that it transferred control of their Lombard and Central Italian railway from Austria to the new Kingdom of Italy. Railway concessions were confirmed by the Treaty of Zurich (November 1859).
- Italy became "our hobby horse" but a financial disappointment. Between 1859-1865 the new government borrowed 1,875 million lire; tax revenues covered only half of expenditures. The lira's convertibility was suspended in May 1866. Italian ministers were called "asses" and "imbeciles."

**Spain — A Parallel Nightmare:**
- Similar to Italy: railways + government debt + political instability. The Zaragoza line had debts of 40 million francs and an annual deficit of 1.5 million by the mid-1860s. Anthony: "railroads are always at the bottom of the Baron's business."
- Political chaos: military coups, revolutions (Queen Isabella overthrown in September 1868). "A little security and stability in the political system would be more efficacious than any subsidy."
- Spain was "the country of surprises, where one cannot even count on tomorrow coming."

**Napoleon at Ferrières:**
- The famous visit (December 16, 1862): Napoleon hunted at the Rothschild estate. Green velvet carpet with golden bees rolled across the station platform. Imperial flags on all four towers. Sèvres porcelain service, 1,231 head of game killed. Rossini composed a "Chorus of Democratic Hunters."
- Natty's sardonic account: "one of the most disagreeable rides I ever had... the cries of Vive l'Empereur were for the greater part uttered by paid agents... most of the shots had drunk 10 or 12 different kinds of wine they shot very badly. Altogether some 800 pheasants were murdered."
- James's barbed farewell: "Sire, mes enfants et moi, nous n'oublierons jamais cette journée. Le memoire nous en sera cher" — punning on "mémoire" (memory/bill), suggesting Napoleon would pay for the visit.
- The visit symbolized Rothschild-Bonaparte reconciliation but was essentially bogus. No real Anglo-French reconciliation followed.

**Credit Mobilier's Decline:**
- Jules Mirès arrested for fraud (1861). James relished: "Rothschild is triumphant, and says that he is the sole baron of industry."
- The Credit Mobilier's death throes provided "detached Schadenfreude." James even speculated in CM shares. By 1864, "the old bank had become the new; the new bank had become the old."
- Alphonse's testimony at the 1865 monetary Enquête was an advance obituary for Pereire expansionism. James to the commission: "You wish to give them the right to issue notes? Where will the confidence be then?"
- Emile Pereire's bitter defence (November 1865): "it was not me who financed the Zaragoza and Alicante railways... it was not me who was responsible for the 1,500 million of Italian loans... and yet the signature which these operations all bear is among those which accuse us of having impoverished the national wealth!"

**Fould-Rothschild Alliance:**
- Fould returned as Finance Minister (late 1861), welcomed by Rothschilds and the bourse. January 1862: conversion of 4.5% rentes to 3% — Paris house lent government 30 million francs and bought 85.9 million of debentures. "A success for the government; for his part, James was delighted to have reasserted traditional predominance in French public finance."

**James's Character in Old Age (rest of Ch3):**
- James's bourse humour became legendary: "At the bourse, there comes a time when, if you want to succeed, you have to speak Hebrew." "Do I know what causes the bourse to rise and to fall? If I knew that I would be a rich man!" Asked about a turnstile at the bourse: "My fiew is that it vill cost me tventy sous a day."
- "L'Empire, c'est la baisse" — punning on Napoleon's "l'Empire, c'est la paix." Literally: "the Empire means a falling market."
- The Goncourt brothers described a gathering of seventy-four Rothschilds at Gustave's wedding: "Pariah kings of the world, today they covet everything and control everything, the newspapers, the arts, the writers and the thrones."
- James in his office was a force of nature: "He had the singular and precious ability to concentrate his thoughts... this Titan read all the letters, received all the despatches, and found time in the evening to perform his social duties despite devoting himself to business from five in the morning."

**The Roots of British Neutrality:**
- Lionel never addressed the House of Commons but was far from politically inactive — he saw senior politicians and journalists constantly at New Court and Piccadilly. "Politics interest your father to the exclusion of all other topics."
- Lionel remained a Liberal but ties of friendship inclined him toward Disraeli. The Rothschild door was open to all: "the two great rivals at dinner — the whig [Gladstone] on Saturday, the tory [Disraeli] on Sunday."
- Natty combined hero-worship of Gladstone, cynicism about Disraeli, and Cobdenite free trade enthusiasm. At the 1866 Reform Bill debates: "Mr. Gladstone's oratory heavy and pompous, while Mr. Disraeli sparkled delightfully."
- British fiscal rectitude meant the government didn't need to borrow — between 1858 and 1900 the national debt FELL from £809 million to £569 million. "A government that did not borrow money was a government the Rothschilds could advise, but not pressurise."
- Anselm's bitter observation (March 1866): "the political influence of England in continental affairs can be considered as nil... the Reform Bill and the Schleswig and Holstein [are dearer] to the heart of John Bull than the duchies."

**The American Wars:**
- August Belmont (Democrats' national chairman) was Lincoln's opponent Stephen Douglas's supporter — incurring opprobrium from BOTH sides. Republicans reviled "the Douglas National Chairman" as a trimmer on slavery; Southern Democrats accused him of the same.
- Belmont struggled to bolster Union support while his "masters" in Europe were accused of backing the Confederacy. The Chicago Tribune (1864): "Will we have a dishonourable peace, in order to enrich Belmont, the Rothschilds, and the whole tribe of Jews?"
- The Rothschilds were in fact largely neutral. Mayer Carl told the American consul-general in Frankfurt that the firm was "opposed to slavery and in favor of the Union." The Confederate loan was issued by Erlanger, not Rothschild. The London house called it "of so speculative a nature that it was very likely to attract all wild speculators."
- James's third son Salomon, sent across the Atlantic in 1859, was inclined to sympathise with the South and argued Europe should recognise the Confederacy.
- Post-war: Rothschild involvement in US was increasingly confined to state and federal bonds. Pennsylvania tried to pay off creditors in depreciated dollars; when Belmont protested, the state Treasurer responded: "We are willing to give you the pound of flesh, but not one drop of Christian blood."
- Jay Cooke's pitch for $5 million of Northern Pacific Railroad bonds got "short shrift" from Lionel. The Civil War caused a permanent decline in Rothschild transatlantic influence.

**Mexico:**
- Napoleon III's invasion was one of the least successful ventures in imperialism. The Rothschilds had interests there (Davidson's $700,000 in loans, the San Rafael ironworks). Davidson welcomed European forces at Vera Cruz.
- James was sceptical but didn't want to lose the Mexican loan to rivals: "I don't quite understand how an Austrian Prince can go under the title of Emperor with French troops, and if they don't stay, who can guarantee that taxes will continue to be collected."
- Mexico distracted France from Central Europe — James put it bluntly: "sending money and troops to Mexico is not good for the Treasury, but it avoids war over Poland."
- When the French withdrew (1866), the Rothschilds were left with bills on Maximilian's doomed regime worth ~6 million francs. Alphonse's assessment after Maximilian's execution: "the tragic death of poor Maximilian is an event which could have very serious consequences."

## Chapter 4: Blood and Silver (1863-1867)

**Bismarck and the Rothschilds:**
- Bismarck lunched with Amschel in Frankfurt (June 1851), consciously echoing Metternich thirty years earlier. The Rothschilds attended to Bismarck's private finances on preferential terms; he provided political intelligence in return.
- Bismarck was not rich before 1866: his expenditures (27,000 thalers) exceeded his salary as Minister President (15,000) plus estate income (~4,000). The 400,000 thaler gift from the Prussian Landtag after victory over Austria cleared his debts.
- Bleichröder gradually poached Bismarck's account from the Frankfurt house. By end of 1866, it was Bleichröder, not Rothschild, who managed Bismarck's finances. Bismarck: "It is not necessary to let the Jews get the upper hand, or to come to depend on them financially to such an extent as is regrettably the case in several countries."
- The Rothschilds regarded Bismarck with "a mixture of antipathy and admiration." James called him "a madcap" (March 1866). Anselm: "a wild boar foaming with rage." Charlotte: "The terrible Bismarck is inexorable; he is the great highway robber of the second half of the nineteenth century." Yet Charlotte also called him "the white revolutionary" and could "regard a Bismarck intelligence as a desideratum in a son-in-law."
- After Bismarck's fall (1890), Alphonse paid tribute: "the European countries certainly cannot be said to dwell on solid fundamental principles."

**German Unification — Financial Background:**
- Prussia was in a fundamentally different fiscal position. Austrian debt increased by factor 2.8 between 1847-1859; Prussian by just 1.8. Prussia's debt service was ~15% of national income vs. France's 29-42% and Austria's 26%.
- Prussian 3.5% bonds never fell below 78; Austrian 5% metalliques touched 42 (not seen since the Napoleonic period).
- Yield spread between Prussian and Austrian bonds averaged 5 percentage points throughout 1851-1868. "Prussia stood above par in politics as on the bourse."
- Austrian policy failed because it was financially unsustainable — unable to afford the military effort necessary for victory on both Italian and German fronts. James advocated Austria should sell territory in one theatre to defend the other.

**Dress Rehearsal: Poland (1863):**
- In April 1862, the Rothschilds secured a major Russian loan — £15 million in 5% bonds, £5 million taken directly by Paris and London houses at 94. But the Polish revolt (January 1863) drove down Russian and Italian bonds.
- Disraeli's characteristically imaginative interpretation: "a war in the centre of Europe, on the pretext of restoring Poland, is a general war, and a long one... The Rothschilds, who have contracted two loans this year, one to Russia and the other to Italy, are naturally very nervous."
- The Polish crisis set back Rothschild plans for a long-term Russian relationship. Charlotte: "I am so glad that the Barings, and not the Rothschilds have taken the Russian loan... If our house had negotiated it, most assuredly there would have been a great outcry against those horrid jews for helping the cruel Russians to crush the poor Poles."

**Schleswig-Holstein:**
- After Italy in 1859, James never again regarded Austria as a great power in financial terms. "The main question was how to wind up Austria's presence in Italy... a bankrupt enterprise might be wound up: an insolvent empire needed to rationalise itself."
- Austrian loan relations deteriorated sharply 1859-1862. James wrote angrily: "We have got nothing from the Austrian sterling bonds, including a price at which we can sell them." He felt "ill" advancing money when Vienna provided no real security.
- Only when Anselm threatened to form a consortium with Erlanger did James engage with the 50 million gulden loan of 1862.

**Privatisation and Diplomacy (Ch4 continued):**
- James's grand vision for 1865: a complex of interdependent transactions to liquidate Austria's unsustainable empire WITHOUT war. Austria would sell Venetia to Italy and Holstein to Prussia; both buyers would raise cash by privatising state assets (railways); the Rothschilds would broker all three deals and secure tax breaks for their Lombard railway company in the process.
- The Prussian case: Bismarck's Landtag wouldn't approve loans. Bleichröder proposed two privatisation options: (1) Cologne-Minden railway share options for 14 million thalers; (2) selling Saar crown coal lands. The Cologne-Minden deal (July 18, 1865) gave the government 13 million thalers from its share options. War Minister Roon exulted: "We have money, enough to give us a free hand in foreign policy, enough to mobilise the whole army."
- The Austrian charge Chotek immediately reported that "such an important supply of money as one usually keeps in readiness in anticipation of war."
- Bismarck's objective: "to prevent by any means at his disposal the Austrian government from successfully floating a loan."

**A Meeting at Biarritz:**
- Bismarck visited Napoleon III at Biarritz in October 1865, then hunted at Ferrières with James. Mühlinen (Austrian diplomat) reported uneasily: "the old baron was very well disposed and drank to the success of all our wishes... while after the visit the negotiations took a turn for the worse."
- The Austrian loan negotiations collapsed. James wanted the Lombard tax exemption as a *sine qua non*. The Credit Foncier consortium outbid the Rothschilds, offering "much more than Rothschild and without asking anything in return." Austria got 150 million gulden bonds at an effective price of 61.25 — receiving just 90 million after commissions. James's proposed price had been 68.
- Franz Joseph scrawled in the margin of reports that James's loan was conditional on recognising Italy as a kingdom: "There can be no talk of that."

**The Road to Königgrätz:**
- By February 1866, the Rothschilds blamed Prussia with "unusual vehemence." Mayer Carl from Frankfurt: "the behaviour of Prussia assumes a character unheard of in the annals of history... everybody is of opinion that the Prussians deserve a good lesson."
- James refused Bleichröder's proposal (March 14) to buy 80,000 remaining Cologne-Minden shares for 20 million thalers: "I refused Bleichröder's associate, on the grounds that we cannot give money to make war."
- The Crown Prince: "Rothschild is moving heaven and earth [against] Bismarck." Munich Punsch ran a cartoon: "Rothschild's Readiness for War" — James clinging to his money-bags: "I'm not giving anything! I don't have any money! The only pleasure I get is neutrality."
- But Bismarck was in real financial difficulty. Bodelschwingh was down to his last 40 million thalers. On May 2, the Cabinet ruled out the Saar mines sale. As late as April 27, Bleichröder "still did not rule out the possibility that Prussia would give in and Bismarck would resign."
- Prussia mobilised nine army corps at 24 million thalers with a further 6 million per month. Emergency credit institutes set up; currency convertibility suspended.

**Italy and the Venetia Question:**
- The Rothschilds tried to broker a peaceful purchase of Venetia. Landau (Rothschild agent) was unexpectedly invited by the Italian government to "relay propositions for [the purchase of] Venetia and thus to avoid war."
- Esterházy flatly rejected the Landau proposal. Anselm urged that Landau "should not accept the Italian mission, in the belief that the proposed sale of Venetia would be rejected out of hand."
- The British government formally proposed the sale of Venetia for £40 million — too late. On April 8, Italy secretly signed an alliance with Prussia to go to war against Austria.
- James issued an unveiled threat to the Italians: "I who have been the patron of Italian funds in Paris, would repudiate completely all new dealings with Italy and would refuse henceforth to charge myself with the payment of the interest on the Italian debt."

**Napoleon's Double Game:**
- Napoleon tried to have it both ways — urging restraint while secretly encouraging Italy to join Prussia. His speech at Auxerre (May 6) denouncing the treaties of 1815 devastated the Paris bourse. At the Tuileries ball that night, "the faces of the ambassadors were so long, that one would have taken them for people condemned to death. But the longest of all was that of Rothschild. They say that he lost ten millions the evening before."
- James coined his famous epigram: "L'Empire, c'est la baisse."
- Alphonse's brilliant analysis (July 1): "If the Austrians win, our government will align itself with them, if they lose we will fall on them... France is playing for big stakes... The preponderance of the Prussians in Germany would be an immense danger."

**Silver Linings — After Königgrätz:**
- Austria's decisive defeat at Königgrätz (July 3, 1866) stunned the family. "Casca il mondo," said the Papal nuncio. Anselm's son Nathaniel: "I feel so deeply upset and depressed that I am hardly able to write."
- Rothschild properties came under Prussian control. At Schillersdorf, "a great part of the game" was plundered. Ferdinand found Prussian cavalry officers who "cantered their horses all over the gravel paths in the park."
- Frankfurt was occupied July 17 after Prussian victory. Mayer Carl had hoped Prussia would "get a famous licking." Instead, Prussian soldiers occupied "Willy's house on the Zeil... every room, with the exception of Matty's dormitory, and would drink nothing but Champagne at their meals!"
- The entire family identified with the Austrian-German side. James was "half crazy" after Königgrätz: "I am wholly pro-Austrian this time, as the war is just too unjust." Even his eight-year-old granddaughter Bettina was "very angry with Mr Bismarck."
- James's verdict on Napoleon: "If I were Emperor, I would be ashamed of myself." Peace between France and Prussia would only be "a bastard peace."

**Silver Linings — Financial Aftermath of 1866 (rest of Ch4):**
- Despite backing the losing side, the Rothschilds profited handsomely from the aftermath. Austria needed money desperately — Anselm organized emergency credits. The London house's profits for 1866 were a healthy £170,000.
- The Frankfurt house faced existential threat: Prussian annexation of Frankfurt (incorporated into Prussia in October 1866) meant the free city that had been the Rothschilds' cradle was gone. Mayer Carl was forced to pay a massive indemnity — the Prussians initially demanded 25 million gulden from Frankfurt, later reduced but still crushing.
- James died on November 15, 1868, at age seventy-six. His death marked the end of an era — the last of the founding generation's sons. His funeral was attended by the Emperor's representative, the entire diplomatic corps, and Émile Pereire. His estate was valued at over 1 billion francs (roughly £40 million), making him by far the richest man in France and probably the world.
- James's will divided the Paris house among his four sons: Alphonse (the eldest, now senior partner), Gustave, Salomon, and Edmond. The transition was smooth — James had been grooming Alphonse for years.

## Chapter 5: Bonds and Iron (1867-1870)

**The Franco-Prussian War and Its Origins:**
- The Luxembourg crisis of 1867 was the first test of the new European order. Napoleon sought "compensation" for Prussian gains — first the Rhineland, then Belgium, finally Luxembourg. Bismarck leaked each demand to embarrass France.
- The Rothschilds were appalled by Napoleon's clumsy diplomacy. When France demanded Luxembourg, Alphonse warned that "this country is heading for catastrophe." The crisis was defused by the Treaty of London (May 1867), which neutralized Luxembourg — but the underlying Franco-Prussian antagonism remained unresolved.
- James's last years were dominated by the conviction that Franco-Prussian war was inevitable. He died before it came, but his sons inherited both his pessimism and his determination to prevent it.

**The Spanish Succession Crisis:**
- The immediate trigger for the Franco-Prussian War was the Hohenzollern candidacy for the Spanish throne. When a Prussian prince was proposed as King of Spain (July 1870), France demanded not just withdrawal but a guarantee it would never be renewed.
- The Rothschilds worked frantically behind the scenes. Alphonse and his brothers used every diplomatic channel to prevent war. When the Hohenzollern candidacy was withdrawn, it seemed peace was saved — but Napoleon's insistence on the guarantee (the famous Ems telegram, doctored by Bismarck) made war unavoidable.
- France declared war on July 19, 1870. The Paris bourse collapsed; the Rothschilds lost heavily on their rente holdings.

**The War of 1870-71:**
- The French army was catastrophically unprepared. Within six weeks, Napoleon III was captured at Sedan (September 2). The Second Empire fell; the Third Republic was proclaimed.
- For the Paris Rothschilds, the war was a personal catastrophe. Ferrières was occupied by the Prussian army and became Bismarck's headquarters and then King William's. The famous wine cellars were looted. Alphonse remained in Paris through the siege, while his wife and children fled to London.
- The Frankfurt Rothschilds found themselves on the winning side but deeply uncomfortable. Mayer Carl had been among the most vocal critics of Prussian aggression just four years earlier.
- The London house played a crucial intermediary role, maintaining communication between the besieged Paris house and the outside world via carrier pigeon and balloon post.

**The Indemnity:**
- The Treaty of Frankfurt (May 1871) imposed an indemnity of 5 billion francs on France — the largest in history. Bismarck chose this figure partly because it was what Napoleon I had extracted from Prussia after Jena (adjusted for inflation).
- The Rothschilds were central to organizing the payment. Two huge French government loans were issued — in 1871 (2 billion francs) and 1872 (3 billion francs). Both were massively oversubscribed: the 1872 loan attracted subscriptions of over 40 billion francs, more than twelve times the amount offered. This was the greatest demonstration of Rothschild financial power since the Napoleonic Wars.
- Alphonse's role was pivotal: he negotiated directly with Bismarck (at Ferrières, ironically) and with Thiers, the new French president. The commission on the loans was relatively modest, but the volume was immense.
- The speed with which France paid off the indemnity astonished Europe. The last German troops left French soil in September 1873, nearly two years ahead of schedule. This was partly a tribute to French fiscal discipline, but also to the Rothschilds' ability to mobilize international capital.

**Impact on the Rothschild Houses:**
- The war fundamentally reshaped the Rothschild network. The Frankfurt house, already weakened by Prussian annexation, went into terminal decline. The Naples house had closed in 1863. Only three houses remained: London, Paris, and Vienna.
- The Paris house emerged stronger than ever, its prestige enhanced by the indemnity loans. Alphonse was now the dominant figure in French finance.
- The Vienna house under Anselm (who died in 1874) faced the challenge of adapting to the Austro-Hungarian Compromise of 1867 — the Dual Monarchy required financial operations in both Vienna and Budapest.

**Rest of Ch5 — The Death of James and the New Generation:**
- James's death (November 15, 1868) was mourned across Europe. His estate — over 1 billion francs — was the largest private fortune ever recorded. He was the last direct link to the founding generation.
- The transition to the next generation was remarkably smooth. Alphonse (born 1827) took charge in Paris; Lionel (born 1808) continued in London; Anselm (born 1803) in Vienna. But Anselm was ageing and his son Ferdinand was more interested in art collecting than banking.
- The new generation faced a fundamentally different world: the era of multinational private banking was giving way to joint-stock banks, national central banks, and increasingly competitive capital markets.

**The Franco-Prussian War — Detailed Financial Account:**
- When war broke out (July 19, 1870), Alphonse immediately began converting assets to gold and silver. The Paris house had substantial reserves but the siege of Paris (September 1870 to January 1871) cut off normal communications.
- Bleichröder proposed to Bismarck that the Rothschilds could serve as intermediaries for peace — an offer Bismarck initially rejected but later exploited. Bismarck used Ferrières as his headquarters, sleeping in Alphonse's bed.
- The armistice negotiations involved the Rothschilds at every stage. Alphonse met Bismarck at Versailles; the terms were harsh but the alternative — continued bombardment of Paris — was worse.
- The Commune (March-May 1871) was an additional horror. The Rothschild properties in Paris survived largely intact, but the political instability delayed the financial settlement.

**The Indemnity Loans — Details:**
- First loan (June 1871): 2 billion francs in 5% rentes at 82.5. Oversubscribed five times. The Rothschilds led the syndicate but shared it with other banks including Barings.
- Second loan (July 1872): 3 billion francs in 5% rentes at 84.5. Oversubscribed twelve times — subscriptions totalled 43 billion francs, a staggering figure. This was not just French money: subscriptions came from across Europe, a vote of confidence in France's recovery.
- The speed of payment stunned Bismarck. By September 1873, the last German soldiers left French soil. The financial operation was the Rothschilds' finest hour since Waterloo.

## Chapter 6: Reich, Republic, Rentes (1870-1873)

**The New European Order:**
- The Franco-Prussian War created a fundamentally new European landscape. Germany was now unified under Prussian leadership; France was a republic nursing a desire for revanche; Austria-Hungary was adjusting to its diminished role.
- For the Rothschilds, the key question was how to maintain their multinational network in an age of rising nationalism. The five-house structure (London, Paris, Frankfurt, Vienna, Naples) had already shrunk to three (London, Paris, Vienna).
- The Frankfurt house under Mayer Carl continued but was increasingly marginal. Berlin — not Frankfurt — was now Germany's financial capital, and the Rothschilds had no house there. Bleichröder filled the vacuum.

**The Gründerzeit and the Crash of 1873:**
- The years 1871-73 saw a massive speculative boom in Germany and Austria (the *Gründerzeit* — "founding era"). The influx of French indemnity money fueled a frenzy of company promotions, railway building, and stock market speculation.
- The Vienna stock exchange crashed on May 9, 1873 ("Black Friday"). The crisis spread to Germany, then to the rest of Europe and America. It was the first truly international financial crisis of the modern era.
- The Rothschilds were relatively unscathed because of their habitual conservatism. Anselm had warned repeatedly about the speculative excess but had been largely ignored. The Creditanstalt suffered significant losses but survived.
- The crash had profound political consequences: it discredited economic liberalism in Central Europe and fueled anti-Semitic sentiment (the "Gründerschwindel" — founding swindle — was blamed on Jewish financiers).

**Alphonse's Dominance in Paris:**
- After the indemnity loans, Alphonse was the undisputed king of French finance. He maintained close relations with Thiers and later with the conservative republicans who governed France in the 1870s.
- The Paris house was now primarily focused on French government bonds (rentes), railway investments (especially the Nord), and international loans. The balance of power within the Rothschild network had shifted decisively from Vienna and Frankfurt to Paris and London.
- Alphonse's political instincts were conservative-republican: he feared both Bonapartist restoration and radical republicanism. He supported the "moral order" governments of the 1870s while maintaining relations with all factions.

## PART II: COUSINS

## Chapter 7: "The Caucasian Royal Family"

**Epigraph:** Sir Charles Dilke, March 1879: "I could see how strangely like a Royal family the Rothschilds are in one respect—namely, that they all quarrel with one another, but are united as against the world."

**The Buddenbrooks Analogy — Generational Decline:**
- Thomas Mann's *Buddenbrooks* pattern: decadence detectable in third generation, fatal in fourth. Tempting to apply to Rothschilds after 1878 — as Mayer Amschel's grandsons died, leadership passed to a fourth generation that seemed to lack the entrepreneurial drive that made the family firm rise. New educational opportunities distracted them; social assimilation transported them from City to country.
- Deaths of the third generation: Nat (1870), Anselm (1874), Mayer (1874), Anthony (1876), Lionel (1879). Only Alphonse and their younger cousins remained.
- Nat — "for 18 years he has fought against what to others would have been an insupportable illness." Hours before death, "he talked to Alphonse about American stocks and the Russian Loan."
- Lionel — suffered crippling rheumatic gout, "wheeled from room to room of his house" for twenty years, yet "up to the last working-day before the day of his death he continued to be the mainspring of a business which had no parallel in magnitude." Disraeli called him "one of the ablest men I ever knew." Left £2.7 million plus houses at Piccadilly and Gunnersbury. Day before dying, summoned broker Edward Wagg: "I have been looking at my fortnightly account and you have made a mistake in the addition."
- Anselm — ascetic, keen reader, expert collector of *objets d'art*, lived austerely in just two rooms of his Vienna palace. Travelled only by hansom cab, "never had his own coach and four." Threw his pen across the room and spat when angry. Buried "with the greatest simplicity" — "The funeral was as unpretending as if it had been that of a poor Jew." Left over 50 million thalers — double the assets of the Jesuit order.

**The Fourth Generation — Epigoni:**
- Charlotte wrote of baby Natty: "a thin, ugly baby, but that did not signify." By nine: "He is reserved and shy and not generous; in fact he is the only one of my children fond of money for the sake of hoarding it... He is constitutionally indolent."
- Natty at Cambridge — struggled with the "Little Go" exam, devoted more time to riding with drag hounds and the Amateur Dramatic Club. Left without taking finals. Politics always his real interest — MP for Aylesbury (1865), inherited baronetcy (1876). Asked for formula for financial success, replied: "Yes, by selling too soon."
- Leo — even greater disappointment academically. Interests turned to horse-racing. Only "English" Rothschild to get a degree was Nat's son James Edouard. Leo's horse Sir Bevys won the Derby (1879); won again with St Amant (1904). Cricket match: Stock Exchange XI vs Leo's own XI (1880). First Rothschild with motor cars. Had racehorse St Frusquin modelled in silver by Fabergé.
- Alfred — lived the life of a *fin de siècle* aesthete. First Jew elected to Court of Directors of Bank of England (1868). Max Beerbohm cartoon: *Doctors consulting whether Mr Alfred may, or may not, take a second praline before bedtime.*
- Austrian branch: Nathaniel (b.1836) quarrelled bitterly with Anselm, considered financially incompetent. Ferdinand (b.1839) — "It is an odd thing, but whenever I sell any stock it is sure to rise and if I buy any it generally falls." Albert (b.1844) forced into business *faute de mieux*.
- Salomon James (b.1835) — banished by his father for losing a million francs speculating. Three years later "dead — killed, so the Goncourts heard, by the stress of speculating at the bourse."
- Edmond (b.1845) — dismissed as "a child" until his baccalaureate, then allowed to visit Egypt — beginning of lifelong Middle East interest.
- Alphonse (b.1827) — first-born of James, inherited Judengasse mentality. "Why, when he was so rich, he worked like a negro to become more so. 'Ah!' he replied, 'You don't know the pleasure of feeling heaps of Christians under one's boots.'"

**Country Houses and Art Collections:**
- Massive proliferation of country estates in 1870s-1880s. At least seven new town houses, eight new country houses in France alone. Edmond's S-shaped château d'Armainvilliers, Nathaniel's Reichenau and Enzesfeld in Austria. Original Rothschild house "zum grünen Schild" reconstructed 1884 as monument to ghetto origins.
- Art collecting reached unprecedented heights — Rothschilds became world's leading art buyers, driving up prices. Major sales (Blenheim, Leigh Court, Fountaine) saw big Rothschild purchases. Ferdinand and Alphonse paid over a quarter million pounds apiece for three supposed Rubens from Duke of Marlborough's collection.
- Ferdinand's Waddesdon — arguably the greatest Rothschild house. Loire château in deepest Buckinghamshire. 50 greenhouses, magnificent gardens. Not to every taste — Gladstone's daughter Mary felt "oppressed with the extreme gorgeousness." Cost Alice £7,500/year just for gardens.
- Alfred's Halton — novelties like bowling alley, ice-skating rink, indoor swimming pool, Indian pavilion. Algernon West dismissed it as "an exaggerated nightmare of gorgeousness and senseless and ill-applied magnificence."
- Ferdinand — "I am a lonely, suffering and occasionally a very miserable individual despite the gilded and marble rooms in which I live." Neurotic, impulsive, "He did not eat but devoured. He did not walk but ran."

**Partners — Perception of Decline:**
- Max Warburg told by Alfred: "A gentleman is not to be found in the office before eleven and never stays beyond four." Leo's routine: arrive 2pm, lunch 3:30-4pm, sleep on sofa rest of afternoon.
- Edward Baring: Rothschilds "so unreasonable and lazy that it is difficult to ensure a business being properly carried through under their direction." Ernest Cassel: "absolutely useless & not remarkable for intelligence."
- Carl Meyer — the real engine at New Court, worked at frenetic pace. Sought promotion to procurist in 1890 but turned down; resigned 1897 to work with Cassel.
- Treatment of subordinates was *de haut en bas*. Natty told broker Fred Cripps Rio Tinto price was "wrong by a quarter of a point." Visitors waited in an ante-room "as though it were Buckingham Palace."
- Yet the houses *did* fare reasonably well in profits and capital — the continuing effectiveness of Natty, Alphonse and Albert compensated for weaker members.

**Fragmentation of the Partnership:**
- Naples house closed 1863 — Adolph withdrew his £1,593,777 share, retired to *objets d'art* at Pregny. James was furious, denounced him as "riff-raff."
- Vienna house under Anselm increasingly autonomous — accused Paris of treating him as "a mere agent." Repaid all outstanding debts to Frankfurt ahead of schedule.
- Paris-Frankfurt friction intensified after 1870-71. Alphonse couldn't resist digs at Mayer Carl's "relatively poor financial performance."
- Partnership agreements: £41.3 million withdrawn between 1874 and 1905. Combined capital fell from £35.5m (1874) to L37m (1905) — if withdrawals hadn't occurred, capital would have been double.
- Share shifts: by 1879 contract, James's sons had 31.4%, Anselm's sons 22.7%, Mayer Carl and Wilhelm Carl 22.3%, Lionel's sons 15.7%, Nat's sons 7.9%.

**End of the Multinational Partnership:**
- By 1905, French partners had 46.8% of total capital; Austrians 25.9%; English just 23.4%. Paris house accounted for 57% of combined capital vs 22% Vienna and 20% London.
- Partnership discontinued between October 1905 and July 1909 — the "confederal" system dating to the 1820s finally ended. A French journalist had predicted: "Divide up the house of Rothschild into a French house, an English, an Austrian, a Neapolitan, and its mediating influence disappears."
- Structural factors mattered more than personal ones: segmentation of European capital market, joint-stock banks offering "careers open to talent," difficulty retaining able employees (Carl Meyer's departure). Mayer Carl 1873: "Jews with their terrible ambition are the worst employees... every one of the clerks leaves the House, either to become the manager of a Bank or for the sake of establishing himself on his own account."
- Frankfurt house wound up 1901 — the *fons et origo* of the Rothschild fortunes. Office in the Fahrgasse became a museum for Jewish antiquities. Staff went to work for Disconto-Gesellschaft.
- Lord Haldane recalled rearranging "Rothschild partnerships which had got into a very vague relation, placing the whole family potentially at the mercy of one dishonest partner."

**End of Endogamy — Mixed Marriages:**
- Of 31 fourth-generation members who married, 13 married another Rothschild. Last wave of endogamous marriages between 1849-1877. Edmond's marriage to Adelheid proved the last pure Rothschild match — Margaretha had refused him.
- Not science but social change ended endogamy — the family's changing attitude toward society's elite.
- Ferdinand married Lionel's daughter Evelina (supreme moment of Rothschild endogamy — 126-person dinner at 148 Piccadilly, Disraeli gave the bride away). Evelina died in childbirth December 1866; Ferdinand never remarried: "Henceforth my life can merely be wrought with sorrow and anguish."

**Peers and Peerages:**
- Fourth-generation women married outside the faith: Annie to Eliot Yorke (1873), Constance to Cyril Flower (1877), Hannah to Archibald Primrose, 5th Earl of Rosebery (1878). Also Margaretha to duc de Gramont, Bertha Clara to prince de Wagram, Hélène to van Zuylen de Nyevelt.
- *Jewish Chronicle* October 1877: "If the flame is seized on the cedars, how will fare the hyssop on the wall?"
- None actually converted to Christianity — even Constance, who said "I wish I could be a Christian," decided conversion would be "impossible" and "a falsehood."
- Hannah Rothschild and Rosebery — most famous mixed marriage. No male Rothschild attended the wedding; Disraeli gave bride away. She inherited Mentmore, 107 Piccadilly, and £100,000/year. Winston Churchill: "a remarkable woman on whom he [Rosebery] had leaned... a pacifying and composing element in his life which he was never able to find again." Hannah died of typhoid 1890, age 39.
- Rosebery's mother, Duchess of Cleveland: "No two persons of different religions can marry without making a very great sacrifice."
- Alfred's mistress Marie Wombwell — may have had illegitimate child Almina (name = "Al" + "Mina"). Alfred gave Almina £500,000 dowry when she married Earl of Carnarvon, plus settled £150,000 in debts, left large portion of £1.5 million estate.

**Elevation to the Peerage:**
- Queen Victoria strongly resisted making a Jew a peer: "To make *a Jew* a peer." Her objections were social, political, and religious — "She cannot think that one who owes his great wealth to contracts with Foreign Govts. for loans, or to successful speculations on the Stock Exchange can fairly claim a British peerage."
- Gladstone argued forcefully: "The merit of Rothschild is that his position is well defined and separated... Her argument is null and void. If it be sound, she has been wrong in consenting to emancipate the Jews."
- Victoria blocked the peerage throughout the 1870s. Lionel died a commoner. Natty finally elevated July 9, 1885 — "sworn in with his hat on his head and his hand on a Hebrew Old Testament." Became Baron Rothschild of Tring.
- Part of broader trend — nearly a fifth of City bankers acquired peerages after 1890. Natty soon joined by other Jewish peers: Lord Wandsworth (Stern), Lord Swaythling (Montagu), Lord Pirbright (de Worms).

**Royal Relationships:**
- Prince of Wales (future Edward VII) key social ally. Natty met him at Cambridge 1861. Regular guest at Rothschild houses. Prince fell and broke his leg at Waddesdon in 1898 — made national news.
- Charlotte considered Rothschilds equals of royalty: "the Caucasian royal family." "Un marriage d'ambition meant a marriage to a Rothschild or a Koh-i-Noor."
- Rothschilds managed Prince of Wales's debts — primarily keeping him solvent via a £160,000 mortgage on Sandringham.
- When Alfred visited Leopold II in Brussels (1892), the King said: "Votre famille m'a toujours gate." Alfred replied: "Pardon Sire, c'est Votre Majesté qui a toujours gate notre famille."

## Chapter 8: Jewish Questions

**Epigraph:** Theodor Herzl, address to the Rothschild Family Council, 1895: "Gentlemen, if you do not give us your support, we will probably have to proscribe you... If you do support us, however, we will make you greater than the modest founder of your house, or indeed his proudest grandson, could ever have dreamt."

**Marriage into the Jewish Elite:**
- After 1877, endogamy gave way to marriages with other wealthy Jewish families — Goldschmidts, Halphens, Sassoons. The Sassoon alliance was most significant: Leo married Marie Perugia (1881, attended by Prince of Wales), Aline married Sir Edward Sassoon (1887), Robert married Nelly Beer (1907, also linked to Sassoons).
- This signalled "the end of the marital exclusivism of the mid-nineteenth century and the integration of the Rothschilds — albeit as *primus inter pares* — into a wider 'cousinhood' of wealthy Jewish families."

**Lay Leaders of British Jewry:**
- Natty was President of the United Synagogue 1879-1915. Between 1868 and 1941, a Rothschild served without interruption as treasurer of the Board of Deputies.
- Natty was also honorary president of the Federation of Synagogues, president of the Jews' Free School, vice-president of the Anglo-Jewish Association.
- Challenge from Samuel Montagu's Federation of Synagogues (1887) — umbrella for Orthodox congregations. Natty forced to surrender office to Montagu but retained honorary presidency.
- Natty was the *de facto* temporal chief of British Jewry — "the scion of the richest of all Jewish families, a key figure in the City, an MP and then a peer."
- Secured appointment of Joseph Herman Hertz as Chief Rabbi (1912) — saw Hertz as likely to appeal to both Orthodox East End and assimilated West End.

**Rising Anti-Semitism:**
- Alphonse on Natty's peerage: "This news will have great repercussions in Austria and Germany, where anti-Semitism is still so virulent."
- Late nineteenth century saw transformation of incoherent prejudice into organized political anti-Semitism.

**Anti-Semitism — Germany and Austria:**
- Wilhelm Marr, who coined *Antisemitismus*, had worked as a young man for the Wertheimsteins (a Vienna Rothschild-linked family) and was fired in 1841 — "the 'goi' who had to bear the consequences of the economic crisis."
- Werner Sombart's *The Jews and Economic Life* (1911): "the name Rothschild meant more than the firm which bears it; it meant all the Jews who are active at the bourse." The modern bourse was "Rothschildian (and thus Jewish)."
- Max Bauer's *Bismarck and Rothschild* (1891): Bismarck as Teutonic peasant virtue vs Rothschild as cosmopolitan parasite — "He is never seen, just as the tapeworm remains invisible in the human body."
- Karl Lueger's "Christian Social" campaign in Vienna — called for nationalisation of Rothschild-owned Kaiser-Ferdinand-Nordbahn (1884). When Lueger became Mayor of Vienna (1897), he quickly discovered how hard it was to dispense with the Rothschilds.

**Anti-Semitism — France (Most Virulent):**
- Collapse of Union Générale bank (1882) catalysed massive outpouring — Bontoux blamed "la Banque Juive." Zola's *L'Argent* gave literary credibility to the idea that Rothschilds destroyed Union Générale. Character Gundermann = thinly veiled James de Rothschild.
- Edouard Drumont's *Jewish France* (1886) — 200 editions. Claimed Rothschilds controlled "nine-tenths at least of all Europe's financial affairs." *Testament of an Anti-Semite* (1894) calculated how much the Rothschild fortune would weigh in silver.
- Auguste Chirac's *Kings of the Republic* (1883) — Alphonse was "Moloch-Baal, that is to say the God *Gold*, marching towards the conquest of Europe."
- Dreyfus Affair (1894): Alphonse was "irritated by the condemnation of Dreyfus and by the indifference of the French aristocracy." Proust's Guermantes circle captures the schizophrenic attitudes — Dreyfusard sympathies studiously concealed.

**Anti-Semitism — England:**
- More from the political left: *Labour Leader* denounced Rothschilds as "blood-sucking crew." J.A. Hobson's *Imperialism* (1902) blamed the Boer War on "a small group of international financiers, chiefly German in origin and Jewish in race." Lloyd George singled out Natty during 1909 budget debates.
- Literary portrayals: Trollope's Melmotte in *The Way We Live Now*, Baron Glumthal in *Davenport Dunn*.

**Violence Against the Family:**
- 1895: letter bomb sent to Alphonse at rue Florentin, forwarded to rue Laffitte — blew up and injured head clerk.
- 1912: William Tebbitt fired at Leo five times as he drove from New Court, riddling his car with bullets.

**Responses to Anti-Semitism:**
- Edouard and Robert de Rothschild duelled for honour against racial insults.
- Mayer Carl told Bismarck (1875): "As for anti-semitic feeling the Jews themselves are to blame, and the present agitation must be ascribed to their arrogance, vanity and unspeakable insolence."
- The *nouveaux riches* and *Ostjuden* (2.5 million Eastern European Jews migrating westwards after 1881 pogroms) were sources of embarrassment. Natty on Bleichröder: "There is no doubt that Bleichröder himself is one of the causes of the Jewish persecution."

**Philanthropy as Response:**
- Rothschild charitable strategy consciously revived in 1880s-90s: hospitals (Evelina Children's Hospital, King Edward VII Fund), housing (Four Per Cent Industrial Dwellings Company — Charlotte de Rothschild Buildings in Spitalfields, 228 families in 477 rooms), schools (Brady Street Lads' Club, Hayes Industrial School).
- Paris: l'Oeuvre des loyers (1874) — 100,000 francs/year to help poor pay rent. Rothschild Foundation "for the Improvement of the Material Existence of Workers" with 10 million francs capital.
- Frankfurt: Hannah Mathilde founded Jewish Children's Home, Old People's Home, Jewish Home for Women, Sanatorium for Poor Jews.
- Natty's speech at Hutchison House Club (1905): "We want to instil into the boys ambition, the pride of being Jews and the pride in being Englishmen."
- Natty to United Synagogue (1891): "the paramount duty devolving upon the Jewish community was the task of Anglicising the numbers of their foreign brethren at present living in the East End of London."

**Immigration Debate:**
- Natty initially opposed immigration restrictions but was embarrassed when his own East End agents backed anti-immigration candidates in 1900 election.
- On Royal Commission: dissented from majority, argued against excluding "deserving and hard-working men." But also favoured "re-exporting" immigrants under certain circumstances.
- Aliens Act passed 1905 — Natty called it "a loathsome system of police interference and espionage."

**Edmond de Rothschild and Palestine:**
- Became interested in Jewish colonisation of Palestine in 1882 via Zadok Kahn and Alliance Israélite Universelle. Funded Samuel Mohilever's farmers from Belorussia and Josef Feinberg at Rishon le Zion ("First in Zion"), south of Jaffa. Gave 25,000 francs to drill for water.
- Aim: "to create models of future settlements, something like settlement nuclei, around which further groups of immigrants could subsequently settle."
- Highly authoritarian approach: settlers signed agreement to "submit myself totally to the orders which the administration shall think necessary in the name of M. le Baron." Directed viniculture, silk manufacture, perfume, glass production. Every detail supervised by the Baron's "officials."

**Edmond's Settlements — Results and Limits:**
- By 1889, investments totalling £1.6 million showed "numerous symptoms of economic failure" — a "dependency culture." Transferred administration to Jewish Colonisation Association in 1900 but continued as banker. By 1903, nineteen of twenty-eight Jewish settlements in Palestine were subsidised by him. Total spending: ~£5.6 million.
- Edmond's colonising should not be equated with Zionism. None of the Rothschilds favoured a Jewish *state*. Edmond explicitly advised settlers to seek Ottoman citizenship.

**Herzl and the Rothschilds:**
- Theodor Herzl's 66-page "Address to the Rothschild Family Council" (1895) was never sent. He offered to make the first Rothschild "prince" of the new state. Albert in Vienna received what "doubtless seemed to be yet another half-mad demand for money — a billion francs, no less — from a more than usually verbose *Schnorrer*."
- Natty refused even to see Herzl in 1901. "I view with horror the establishment of a Jewish Colony... the dream of Palestine is a myth and a will-of-the-wisp."
- Herzl's utopia had "markedly socialist characteristics (notably a nationalised banking system)" — hardly calculated to appeal to the Rothschilds. The deeper objection: a Jewish nation state would "probably encourage anti-Semites to question the existing national identities of assimilated Jews."
- Ferguson's verdict: "With the benefit of hindsight, we can see that Herzl was a prophet. Less than half a century after his death, the German, Austrian and French Rothschilds had all fallen victim to just the anti-Semitic onslaught he had foreseen."

## Chapter 9: "On the Side of Imperialism" (1874-1885)

**Epigraph:** J.A. Hobson, *Imperialism: A Study* (1902): "Does anyone seriously suppose that a great war could be undertaken by any European State, or a great State loan subscribed, if the house of Rothschild and its connexions set their face against it?... Finance is the governor of the imperial engine."

**Financial Performance — Relative Decline, Absolute Strength:**
- Combined Rothschild capital rose from £34.4m (1874) to £38m (1887), peaked at £41.5m (1899). N.M. Rothschild remained far and away the largest private bank in the City of London.
- But profits as percentage of capital declined: 9.8% in 1870s → 3.9% in 1900-09. Barings had 13.6% in 1870s. The Rothschilds played safe with inherited capital.
- Acceptances lagging: N.M.R. averaged £2.7m (1890-1914) vs £5.6m for Barings, £7.2m for Schröders, £9m for Kleinworts.
- Natty left £2.5 million at death (1915) — richest of his generation, but at least 13 other British millionaires left as much. J.P. Morgan's estate was ~$68.3m ($14m excl. art) = £24m. Clinton Dawkins (Morgan partner) in 1901: "The Rothschilds have nothing new but the experience and great prestige of old Nattie."

**Rothschild Principle — Low Profitability, High Longevity:**
- Friedrich Gentz (1827): "never to strive for excessive profits in their undertakings, to give each of their operations clear limits... to insulate themselves from the play of happenstance." Barings' 1890 crisis proved the wisdom of this: their high profit rate was partly a function of dangerously narrow capital base.
- Combined Rothschild profits: most profitable years were 1879-82 (avg >£4m/year). 1888-1904 trend upwards from £785,000/year to £1.6m.

**Role in British Imperialism:**
- Public issues of foreign securities in London 1865-1914: £4,082 million total. N.M. Rothschild responsible for over a quarter (£1,085m). No other bank matched this.
- Loans by geography: Europe 45.6%, North America 22.5%, Latin America 14.6%, Middle East 6.1%, Britain 8.7%. Over 90% were public sector (government bonds). Only 6% imperial issues — surprisingly low given the family's political involvement in imperialism.
- Natty on refusing Guinness flotation (1886, which Barings snapped up for £500,000 profit): "I go to the House every morning and when I say 'No' to every scheme and enterprise submitted to me, I return home at night carefree and contented. But when I agree to any proposal, I am immediately filled with anxiety... the whirring wheels may drag your whole body in after the finger."
- Rothschilds kept their distance from: London Underground, Channel Tunnel, telephone. Still communicated London-Paris by handwritten letter.

**The Financial Politics of Empire — Egypt:**
- Suez Canal purchase (1875): Disraeli asked Lionel to advance £4 million to buy the Khedive's shares. "Scarcely breathing time" — money needed by November 30. Parliament not sitting.
- £4 million = 8.3% of entire UK budget net of debt charges.
- Ottoman Turkey declared bankruptcy October 7, 1875 — deadlocked rival syndicates for Egyptian lending. Khedive Ismail told Rothschilds he needed £3-4 million by end of November.
- French Foreign Minister blocked Credit Foncier's purchase of canal shares. Khedive had to sell to Britain. November 23: offered shares for £4 million plus 5% premium.
- Derby and Northcote opposed; Disraeli prevailed in five Cabinet meetings November 18-24.

**The Suez Canal Purchase — Details & Aftermath:**
- Montagu Corry's famous account: waited outside Cabinet, Disraeli stuck his head out and said "Yes," Corry rushed to New Court. Lionel "picked up a muscatel grape, ate it, threw out the skin, and said deliberately, 'What is your security?' 'The British Government.' 'You shall have it.'" Ferguson notes this is partly fantasy — Disraeli had already privately discussed with Lionel.
- Terms: money advanced to British government (£1m on Dec 1, rest by January), commission of 2.5%, plus 5% per annum interest until Parliament repaid. By Jan 5, 1876, N.M. Rothschild paid full amount of £3,976,582 2s 6d directly to Egyptian creditors. Parliament voted £4,080,000 on Feb 21 to repay (raised by creating 3.5% Exchequer Bonds). Commission of £99,414 paid; interest due of £52,485 paid June 2.
- Disraeli to Queen Victoria: "It is just settled: you have it, Madam. The French Government has been outgeneraled." To Lady Bradford: "We have had all the gamblers, capitalists, financiers of the world organized and platooned in bands of plunderers, arrayed against us, and secret emissaries in every corner, and have baffled them all." "The Fairy [Queen Victoria] is in ecstasies."
- Disraeli told Prince of Wales the Rothschilds had "4 & 20 hours to make up their minds." He claimed Alphonse was "si francisé" that they couldn't use the Paris house without betraying the scheme.
- Liberal opposition fierce. Gladstone: even if done "in concert with the other Powers" it was "an act of folly, fraught with future embarrassment." Sir Robert Lowe argued Rothschild charges of £150,000 for a loan of £4m for three months amounted to 15% per annum — "more appropriate for an Egyptian government than a British one."
- Accusations of insider trading: Disraeli's solicitor Philip Rose believed Rothschilds had "benefited largely," having "bought to the extent of millions of Egyptian stock." Disraeli heard rumours of "at least 1/4 of a million" profit. But Montagu Corry heard from another source that "the Rothschilds had not made the slightest use of the intelligence."
- Rothschild profit and loss accounts refute the speculation charge: 1875 accounts show sale of just £12,682 of 1873 Egyptian bonds; even bought at 55 and sold at 76, total profit would have been only £3,505.
- Lionel to stockbroker Arthur Wagg: "Arthur Wagg, you're a young man and will learn better. I've made £100,000 out of the deal, I wish it had been £200,000." Lionel defended the commission: the Khedive might have insisted on gold payment; money markets might have tightened; another firm might have been called upon. "The entire absence of such disturbance" was the best vindication.
- The deal proved superb for British taxpayers: by January 1876 shares had risen from £22 10s 4d to £34 12s 6d (50% increase). Government stake worth £24m in 1898, £40m on eve of WWI, £93m by 1935 (~£528/share). Dividends of £200,000/year from Cairo between 1875-1895; proper dividends from 1895 rose from £690,000 to £880,000 by 1901.

**Another Eastern Question — The Balkan Crisis (1875-78):**
- Suez purchase was prelude to deeper British involvement in Egypt and the Eastern Question. Stephen Cave sent to Egypt to establish financial control.
- Rothschilds were reluctant imperialists — warned against "putting ourselves at the head of a large financial operation" in Egypt. Lionel and Alphonse felt Cave and Rivers Wilson underestimated the difficulty of stabilizing Egyptian finances under Khedive Ismail.
- Conflict between bondholders and short-term lenders paralyzed the new Caisse de la Dette Publique (May 1876). Without Anglo-French accord, Rothschilds refused to restructure Egyptian debt. Commission of inquiry fixed consolidated debt at £76m (excluding £15m private debts and £6m floating debt).
- 1878: "international" government under Nubar Pasha — Rivers Wilson as Finance Minister, Frenchman Blignières as Public Works. English and French Rothschilds floated an £8.5m loan secured on Khedive's domain lands.
- Eastern crisis intertwined: Russia pursuing "forward" policy toward Turkey since 1870. Revolt in Bosnia-Herzegovina (summer 1875). Disraeli accused Russia, Austria, Prussia of fomenting Ottoman disintegration. "Bulgarian atrocities" (up to 15,000 Christians killed by Ottoman irregulars) gave Gladstone his platform.
- Rothschild financial exposure enormous: between 1870-1875, London and Paris houses jointly issued Russian bonds worth £62m. Russian 5% bonds rose from 85 (March 1870) to 106 (August 1875), then crashed to 74 (October 1876) and 68 (April 1877 when Russia declared war on Turkey). The 1878 banking crisis — "the biggest ever known in the history of English banking" — followed.
- Critical dilemma: support Russia (their bondholder) or Turkey (Britain's ally). They chose Turkey, leaving the 1877 Russian loan to German bankers (Mendelssohn, Comptoir d'Escompte, Crédit Lyonnais). This excluded them from Russian finance for a decade and a half — a real sacrifice explained by Lionel's disgust at Gladstone's "Bulgarian Horrors" campaign (which smacked of anti-Jewish sentiment) and Rothschild concern for Jewish persecution in Eastern Europe.
- Article 44 of the Treaty of Berlin: guaranteed religious toleration in the Balkans — "manifestly counted for more in the Rothschilds' eyes than the convoluted compromise over Bulgaria."
- Rothschilds as intelligence channel: Disraeli "made up his mind to consult Messrs. Rothschild confidentially" on Russian undertakings to Austria. Derby in December 1877 complained: "Schou. [Shuvalov] tells Lady Derby that he finds the Rothschilds acquainted with everything that goes on."

**From Investment to Invasion — Egypt 1879-1885:**
- April 1879: Khedive dismissed "international" government. New Rothschild-issued bonds dropped sharply. Contrary to myth, Natty did not agitate for British military intervention — he accepted Rivers Wilson's argument for removing the Khedive but "object[ed] strongly" to withdrawing the 1877 loan, calling it "a very dishonourable proceeding."
- Union Générale crisis (1882) critical backdrop: Paul Eugène Bontoux, former Südbahn engineer, built an anti-Rothschild Catholic investment trust. Capital of 25m francs, assets peaked at little more than 38m francs. Shares reached 3,005 francs by December 1881; crashed to 1,300 in two weeks after January 4, 1882. Bontoux claimed "Jewish plot" — no evidence. Rothschilds actually contributed 10m francs to collective rescue of Paris banks. (Same pattern would repeat with Barings in 1890.)
- Union Générale crash timed perfectly: Gambetta fell from power. His successor Freycinet lost the vote on joint Anglo-French occupation of Egypt. This cleared the way for unilateral British action.
- Alphonse relayed to London that France "would not object to firm action by Britain" against Arabi Pasha — the key intelligence enabling British occupation. When British ships bombarded Alexandria (July 1882), Alphonse was "delighted": "England can now no longer withdraw until law and order are re-established all over the country."
- After Wolseley's victory at Tel-el-Kebir (September 1882): "it was clear that in Egypt 'it was England must secure the future predominance.'" The Rothschilds effectively encouraged Gladstone to override his own conscientious scruples.
- Sudan crisis: Gordon sought to fight the Mahdi rather than withdraw. French Rothschilds repeated the bourse story "that Gordon Pasha carried along £100,000 in Bank of England notes, the best English weapon to put an end to the revolt." Gordon's death (Feb 5, 1885) persuaded Rosebery to join government — Natty welcomed this warmly.
- After 1884, all Egyptian bond issues were effectively underwritten by Britain. Between 1885-1893, London, Paris, and Frankfurt houses jointly responsible for four major Egyptian bond issues worth nearly £50m. These were Rothschild operations, sometimes with Bleichröder.
- Under Evelyn Baring (Lord Cromer), Egyptian debt fell from £106m peak (1891) to £94m (1913); debt burden ratio fell from 10x to 5x current revenue. Substantial infrastructure built (railways, Aswan dam). British financial control so strict that Rothschilds complained their commissions were being squeezed.
- The ultimate irony: Gladstone himself was one of the principal beneficiaries. He had acquired £45,000 (nominal) of Ottoman Egyptian Tribute loan of 1871 at price of 38, plus £5,000 more by 1878 and £15,000 of 1854 Ottoman loan. By 1882 these were 37% of his entire portfolio. The 1871 bonds rose from 38 to 57 (summer 1882), reached 82 by December 1882, and touched 97 by 1891 — a capital gain of more than 130% on his 1875 investment. He called Turkish state bankruptcy "the greatest of all political crimes."

## Chapter 10: Party Politics

**Epigraph:** Lionel to Leo and Leonora, March 1876: "Dizzy was here... [O]ur friend [is] in very good spirits... What do you say to the visitor who is now with dear Ma whilst I am writing—this I have just heard, that the famous Mr Gladstone is with her drinking tea and eating bread and butter."

**From Gladstone to Disraeli:**
- Rothschild politics had a "revolving door" quality until 1905 — though increasingly identified with Conservatism, channels to Gladstonians were never closed.
- Mayer and Anthony remained firm Liberals. Anthony declared (September 1866): "The sooner we are rid of the colonies, the better for England." Anthony's daughters Constance and Annie stayed Liberal throughout; Mayer's daughter married the future Liberal PM (Rosebery).
- Even Lionel's sons began as Liberals. Leo first stood for hustings in 1865 asking voters "whether you would rather be ruled by Palmerston, Russell and Gladstone or Derby, Disraeli and Malmesbury." Natty stood as Liberal for Aylesbury in the same year.
- Gladstone maintained frequent contacts with Rothschilds even after becoming PM in 1868. Dined with Lionel and Charlotte at 148 Piccadilly in 1869 and 1870. Business meetings with Lionel. Gladstone and Emma (Natty's wife) corresponded on theology and Goethe. Gladstone visited Tring in 1893 despite political differences with Natty.
- Disraeli cultivated Rothschilds from his early years — romanticized them in novels, sought share tips. His finances were a mess; a Yorkshire landowner named Andrew Montagu bought up all his debts for a £57,000 mortgage at 3%. He inherited £30,000 from Mrs Brydges Williams and made ~£20,000 from novels.
- Intimate friendship: during 1867 Reform debates, Disraeli and Lionel dined regularly after the House, exchanging political confidences. Disraeli was "remarkably candid" about his legislative programme. Lionel was actively "leaking" opposition intelligence to assist Disraeli: "Yesterday the Diz's were our only visitors... [H]e did not tell me much and wanted to know all the reports."
- After Lionel's death (1879): sons replied to Disraeli's condolences saying their father "looked upon you as his 'dearest friend.'" When Disraeli died (April 19, 1881), Natty carried out his last wishes — buried at Hughenden with his wife, declining the public funeral Gladstone proposed.
- Disraeli: "Baron Rothschild... is a Liberal, and... knows everything." Lionel was valued for intelligence from the other side of political lines.

**Politics in "Bucks":**
- Rothschilds established themselves as a political force in Buckinghamshire from the 1850s. Local Whig hostility: Lord Carrington called them "the Red Sea"; Acton Tindal resisted "the circumcision" of the Aylesbury party.
- Lionel lost his City seat twice (1874, 1880) — first time likely because of association with a Jewish convert candidate; second time because of the rift with Gladstone over fiscal policy. At a major election meeting, Lionel argued against Gladstone's proposal to abolish income tax, saying new taxes on property were needed instead. "When his audience shouted 'No' and 'Economy,' he replied that economy had not got so far as to save four millions and a half a year."

**The Break with Gladstone over Foreign Policy and Ireland:**
- By the 1876 Buckinghamshire by-election, Lionel was "violently in favour of Dizzy & Derby." The anti-Semitic undertone of Liberal campaigning pushed them further right — Russell (Liberal candidate) "attacked Dizzy as a Jew, a Jingo & something else beginning with a J" (the word was "Juggler").
- Ferdinand's remarkable 1885 letter to Dilke captures the ambivalence: "I am not as you think by nature a conservative. Conservativism has been the ruin of several foreign countries and liberal politics have been the making of England. To liberalism we—you—owe everything... But I would cheer the Union Jack planted on every island of the Polynees, on every crag of the Himalayas, on every minaret of the East."
- Natty's definitive break with Gladstone came over Ireland and Home Rule. First sign: 1880 — Natty joined "Young Whigs" voting against Irish Land Bill. Told Disraeli the measure implied "nothing less than confiscation."
- Natty told Hartington (November 29, 1885): "Gladstone's name might well be changed to Ichabod" (Hebrew: "The glory is departing from Israel"). Five days later he briefed Randolph Churchill on the likely Liberal split.
- April 1886: Natty openly declared himself at a Liberal Unionist General Committee meeting at the Westminster Palace Hotel — "his final political break with Gladstone." Other Jewish MPs joined him (Ferdinand, Francis Goldsmid).
- July 1886 election results: "a 'smash' for Gladstone and Home Rule" — 316 Conservatives, 78 Liberal Unionists vs. 191 Gladstonians and 85 Irish Nationalists. Natty had urged Churchill and Chamberlain to campaign in Scotland — "the old man's dung hill."

**Churchill and Rosebery:**
- Natty's political activity in the 1880s was remarkably remote from his banking concerns — "for the first time, a Rothschild was engaged in politics as a vocation for its own sake."
- India connection: between 1881-1887, Charlotte's sons issued Indian railway shares worth £6.4m. Churchill as Secretary for India wanted Rothschilds as bankers: "If I am at the office next year... when the loan is brought out I shall fight a great battle against [Bertram] Currie to place it in the hands of the Rothschilds, whose financial knowledge is as great as that of the Bank of England is small."
- Burma Ruby Mines: 1889 flotation was "immensely successful" — crowd so large Natty "reputedly had to climb up a ladder to get into the bank," shares went to 300% premium. But the issue was only £300,000 and came out four years after Churchill left the India Office.
- Churchill's financial ruin: he died owing "the astonishing sum of £66,902" on his London house. By 1891 his overdraft at Rothschilds had ballooned to £11,000. Natty's bankrolling of Churchill after 1886 was "primarily an act of friendship as syphilis inexorably took its toll."
- Rosebery: married Hannah Rothschild (1878), then served as Foreign Secretary and became PM (1894). Rosebery initially refused a Cabinet post in 1884 citing "the extreme delicacy of my relation to that question [Egyptian finance], for although I am not a member of the House of Rothschild, I am allied to it as closely as possible by kinship and friendship."
- Rosebery was allotted £50,000 of the new Egyptian loan in August 1885 — "in accordance with [Rosebery's] wishes the Egyptian money [was] paid into the Bank to the credit of Hannah."
- Rare confirmed leak: as Foreign Secretary in January 1893, Rosebery used Reginald Brett to communicate to Natty and Alfred the government's intention to reinforce the Egyptian garrison — they learned it "before reading it in the papers."

**Conservatism in France:**
- French Rothschilds developed greater ideological neutrality/flexibility due to frequent regime changes. At heart Alphonse and brothers were Orléanists but worked with republican politicians.
- Key distinction: between moderate/conservative republicans and radical/"red" republicans. Welcomed Thiers replacing MacMahon (1873), lamented MacMahon's fall (1877). Reassured when their old friend Léon Say became Finance Minister.
- Railway nationalization was the great threat — "that old objective of 1848" which preyed on Alphonse's mind. Nord company was a major stake.
- Famous dinner during Gambetta's premiership (1881-82): Alphonse and Gambetta spotted "chatting amiably in a window alcove, the two sovereigns — Gambetta, the actual master of France, and Rothschild." Gambetta drank "To a restored France!" Alphonse responded: "To the man who will restore her!" Gambetta paused and replied: "Ah! I would be willing. If only the electoral committee of Belleville had been there to see their Gambetta in the company of these princes and marquises."
- Alphonse told police/journalists (December 1881): "I want an all-out campaign; it is necessary to demolish Gambetta before he demolishes us." Gambetta planned a massive 6 billion franc rente conversion and railway nationalization. Union Générale crash contributed to Gambetta's fall.
- Alphonse on socialism (1897 interview with Jules Huret): "I am sure that, generally speaking, working people are very satisfied with their lot... Those who demand the eight hour day are the lazy, incapable ones... if they were all compelled to work only eight hours a day do you know what the majority of them would do? Well, they would drink!"
- On wealth inequality: "I have never understood what is meant by 'haute banque.' There are richer men and poorer men and that's all there is to it!... Capital is labour! Apart from some unfortunate exceptions... each man has that share of the available capital that his intelligence, energy and industry merit."

## Chapter 11: The Risks and Returns of Empire (1885-1902)

**Epigraph:** Cecil Rhodes to Lord Rothschild, 1888: "[T]ake Constitution Jesuits if obtainable and insert English Empire for Roman Catholic Religion."

**Goschen's Conversion & Rothschild Dominance:**
- 1889: Chancellor Goschen converted £500m of 3% consols into 2.5% — nearly half the national debt. Testified to N.M. Rothschild's continuing dominance in the London bond market.
- Edward Hamilton recommended taking "Rothschilds... into his confidence" as well as Barings. Natty refused the Treasury's offer of 20-25m 2.5% consols at ~99, dismissing "the possible margin of profit" as "wholly out of proportion to the risk run" — insisting on a price no higher than 97.5. Only a year later, his prudence became vindicated with the Barings crisis.

**The Barings Crisis (1890):**
- Barings' Argentine involvement grew steadily from 1850s, profits averaging 13% of capital 1880-89. By late 1880s, fatal overconfidence. The Rothschilds anticipated the crisis at least two years early: Alphonse remarked (October 1888) Argentina "would have to grow rapidly very rich indeed" to service its debt. Gustave predicted "crash in Argentine funds."
- Fatal deal: £2m share issue for Buenos Aires Water and Drainage Company. Barings failed to place more than £150,000 with the public. Political crisis in Argentina (July 1890) precipitated default.
- Scale of disaster: Barings' capital was just £2.9m. Total liabilities close to £21m (including large Russian government deposits being withdrawn). Ratio of capital to liabilities: 14% (vs. N.M. Rothschild average of 39% for 1880-89). Argentine securities in portfolio larger than the firm's entire capital — "haphazard management, certain to bring any firm to grief."
- Natty initially argued for letting Barings go under. He "dismissed Lidderdale's suggestion that Rothschilds could somehow influence the Argentine government"; opposed Currie's idea of lending Barings "four millions to tide over their difficulties."
- Self-interest: Russian deposits withdrawn from Barings had ended up at New Court. "Had Natty supported B. Currie's original proposals, that order would have extended to the Rothschilds — it might have commenced a run upon them — a debacle."
- The rescue: Natty met Salisbury on November 12. Told PM the Barings were "finished" and would be left with "at most £10,000 a year apiece." Warned "a catastrophe [which] would put an end to the commercial habit of transacting all business of the world by bills on London." Salisbury and Smith agreed to bear half of any loss from Barings' bills.
- Guarantee fund assembled in the Governors' Room at the Bank of England. Lidderdale pledged £1m from the Bank on condition of £3m from City firms. Currie offered £500,000 conditional on Rothschilds matching. According to Tom Baring, Natty "hesitated and was only 'shamed' by Currie into agreeing." Lidderdale's pressure: "We can get on without you." By end of 24-hour window, £10m accumulated (later rose to £17m, though only £7.5m actually needed).
- Crucial gold transfers: Natty arranged £2m gold loan from Banque de France, plus further £1m — boosting Bank of England reserves from £11m low (Nov 7) to £16.6m a month later.
- Aftermath: Argentine securities fell to 40% of March 1889 value by July 1891. Barings guarantee had to be extended to November following year. Reconstituted Barings only repaid advances in 1894. Natty's standing in official circles hugely enhanced — Hamilton named him one of the "first counsellors" any new Chancellor should consult.

**Brazil — Why No Rothschild Crisis:**
- Rothschilds responsible for Brazilian government bond issues totaling £37m (1883-1889), plus £320,000 for Bahia-San Francisco railway.
- Emperor Pedro overthrown by republican revolution (1889) — "took the Rothschilds completely by surprise." Civil war by 1893; new revolt in 1896-97.
- Why they survived: (1) London house lost "only" ~£740,000 between 1890-93; (2) Brazilian bonds were just 2.4% of assets in 1886; (3) capital-to-liabilities ratio never fell below 19.5% (vs. Barings' 14%); (4) London house capital was £5.9m vs. Barings' £2.9m.
- 1898 Brazilian debt crisis: Rothschilds dictated terms of the Funding Loan — consolidating obligations secured on customs receipts, demanding rigorous retrenchment. Stern letter to President-Elect Campos-Salles published in The Times. Milreis appreciated from 7/4d to 16d by 1913, intensifying Brazilian coffee crisis.
- Between 1890-1914, London house issued £83m in Brazilian public sector bonds and £5.8m in private securities. Also £33m in Chilean bonds (1886-1914). These accumulations far exceeded economic growth capacity.

**"Staunch Monometallists" — Gold Standard:**
- Rothschilds played key role facilitating the gold standard globally. In the US: partnered with Joseph Seligman, then with Junius Morgan's group. Issued over £267m in US bonds between 1873-1877 to enable gold standard adoption. August Belmont denounced "free silver" as "open theft and blind and dishonest frenzy."
- 1893 Cleveland gold crisis: Rothschilds and Morgans jointly placed $62.3m of US 4% bonds — bought at 104.5, sold to investors at 112.25 (price later rose to 119). "Tales of profits of $6 million being made in the space of twenty-two minutes were grist to the Populist mill."
- Rothschilds were not strict monometallists but "multimetallists." English Rothschilds favored gold for Britain, French favored bimetallism for France — both logical given their arbitrage role between the two systems. They had private interests in mercury (main use: silver refining) and silver, so opposed complete demonetization of silver.
- Alfred sent as British delegate to 1892 Brussels Monetary Conference. Was supposed to be a "sane man" defending gold but surprised everyone with a compromise plan to reconcile bullionists and bimetallists. Harcourt: "The name of Rothschild will carry a weight which no other could command in the monetary world."

**An Empire Underground — Mining Investments:**
- Decision to build a mining empire was "the most important change in their mode of operation since their decision to become involved in railway finance in the 1830s." Mines offered higher returns than state bonds and were less liable to lose value through government default (though risks of punitive taxation/expropriation existed).
- The Exploration Company (founded 1886): crucial vehicle for Rothschild mining ambitions. Relaunched 1889 as joint-stock company with £300,000+ nominal capital. Charged 20% fee on nominal capital for flotations. Founding shareholders included Revelstoke, Everard Hambro, Henry Oppenheim, Arthur Wagg. By 1896: capital £1.25m, market value £2.24m. Between 1889-1903: issued shares for 23 companies worth £20.7m nominal. Paid 265% total dividends on initial £30,000 paid-up capital (1889-1895).
- Gold: held 5,000 then 13,000 shares in Consolidated Gold Fields of South Africa. When Wernher and Beit floated Rand Mines (Feb 1893), Rothschilds were allotted 27,000 of 100,000 shares; took £35,000 of bonds when Rand Mines raised further £1m in 1897. Gave them stake in the "Corner House" group controlling ~37% of Rand gold output (1902-1913). Rand Mines shares rose from £15 10s (low, 1897) to £45 (peak, 1899).
- Copper: acquired controlling interest in Rio Tinto mines (late 1880s) — over 10% of world copper production. "By the early 1900s the price of 'Tintos' was a benchmark cited almost as often in correspondence between London and Paris as the price of consols." London Rothschilds acted as Rio Tinto's banker, issuing £3.6m debentures. Paris house: 37.5% stake in Boleo Company (Mexican copper). Exploration Company financed Montana-based Anaconda Mining Company. Along with Lewisohn, Goldschmidt & Co., formed marketing syndicate that pushed copper back to £50/ton (from 1895).
- Diamonds: see De Beers section below.
- Rubies: floated Burma Ruby Mines (1889) after prolonged tussle for the mining concession. Proved highly profitable — ruby prices still rising strongly four years later.
- Nickel: Alphonse acquired 25% interest in Nickel Company (John Higginson), mining in New Caledonia. By 1884 acquired most European nickel refineries. But 1891 discovery of Canadian nickel mines shattered monopoly dream — "Le Nickel" forced to halve capital and share market with International Nickel Company.
- Lead: French house established Peñarroya Mining and Metallurgical Company. By 1913 produced over 80% of Spanish silver and 60% of lead. With 40% stake in Peñarroya and exclusive selling agency, the French house became one of the biggest single players in the international lead market.
- Oil: French house went into partnership with Deutsch de la Meurthe (1879) to manufacture kerosene. After Nobel Brothers rebuffed their partnership proposal, they bought the Batum Oil Refining and Trading Company (BNITO), building fleet of 2,000+ oil tank cars and refineries at Novorossiysk. At peak, ~1/3 of Russian oil output was Rothschild-controlled. Value estimated at 58 million francs (£2.3m). In 1911, exchanged entire Russian operation for shares in Royal Dutch and Shell — becoming the largest shareholders in each.

**Rhodes and the Rothschilds — De Beers:**
- Relationship traced to 1882 when Natty sent Albert Gansl to Kimberley to report on the Anglo-African Diamond Mining Company. Over 100 small companies were ruining each other by overproduction; Gansl urged amalgamation.
- Gardner Williams (American engineer recruited by Exploration Company) sent to South Africa in 1885 to report on mining prospects. Kimberley Central had market value of ~£2.45m; De Beers worth ~£2m.
- Rhodes realized the key to the takeover battle lay in London — "whoever secured the financial backing of a major City house would win." Williams became his entrée to New Court.
- August 4, 1887: Natty drove a hard bargain. Rothschilds advanced £750,000 for 50,000 new De Beers shares at £15 each, plus £200,000 in debentures. Commission of £100,000, plus half the difference between £15 and London market price. Total Rothschild syndicate paid ~£250,000 for advancing £750,000.
- Barnato secretly committed to Rhodes; but Baring-Gould (chairman of Kimberley Central) and Central shareholders resisted. Rhodes needed further £300,000 loan from Rothschilds to buy up Central shares. Natty acquired 5,754 De Beers shares himself (vs. Rhodes's 4,000). March 13, 1888: De Beers Consolidated formally registered — capital £3.1m plus £1.5m debenture stock. Barnato came round partly due to offer of "life governorship" (which Natty disliked). Final cost of merger: De Beers acquired 93% of rival Kimberley Central for less than a tenth of its £5.3m valuation by January 1889.
- De Beers Consolidated controlled 98% of South African diamond output. Diamond marketing syndicate agreed March 1890 with Wernher, Beit & Co. But Natty opposed any hoarding strategy — told Rhodes (July 1891): "no right to speculate in diamonds, but were bound to sell as best you could... the more I think of it the more I feel convinced that you cannot do better than follow the ordinary laws of supply and demand."
- By 1899: N.M. Rothschild was second biggest De Beers shareholder (31,666 shares) after Barnato's nephews the Joel brothers (33,576). Rhodes had only 13,537; Beit 11,858. De Beers annual dividends reached 40% (£1.6m) over the next five years after the initial sticky start. Natty to Rhodes in 1900: "The history of the De Beers Company is simply a fairy tale."
- Rhodes revised his will in 1888 to leave all his property except 2,000 De Beers shares to Natty — intended to fund "a society of the elect for the good of the Empire." Rhodes scribbled: "take Constitution Jesuits if obtainable, and insert English Empire for Roman Catholic Religion." Later revised will again making Natty a second trustee, complaining Natty was "honest but without sufficient brains."

**Rhodes's Imperial Ambitions vs. Rothschild Caution:**
- Natty firmly opposed using De Beers funds for the British South Africa Company ("the Charter"): "We have always held that the Debeers Company is simply and purely a diamond mining company... if it became known that Debeers Company lent money to the Chartered Company, some Debeers shareholders might move for an injunction."
- The Jameson Raid (December 1895) — an abortive invasion by Rhodes's private army in Bechuanaland — "appalled the Rothschilds, who had no inkling of the plans for a coup." Natty tried to patch relations between London and Pretoria, urging Kruger to come to London and assuring him: "you will obtain the independence of the Republic."

**The Boer War and Rothschild Decline:**
- Rothschilds tried to prevent war. Alfred telegraphed Kruger directly (June 1899) urging franchise reform. At Hartington's suggestion, telegram sent to Samuel Marks in Pretoria — effectively reformulating British policy without Chamberlain or Salisbury's authorization: "Government of Great Britain are anxious Peace. If agree to 5 Years Franchise without conditions Government of Transvaal have no reason to fear friendly discussion." Salisbury repudiated this, fearing "subterranean entanglement."
- Once war broke out (October 1899), Natty threw himself into the war effort. But privately deplored the "wretched guerrilla warfare." Advised Rhodes: "be careful in what you say regarding the conduct of the war and the military authorities. Feeling in this country [is] running high at present over everything connected with the war."
- The Boer War exposed Rothschild financial decline. First time since the Crimean War that Britain had to finance a major war by increasing national debt. Natty assumed "the Chancellor would send for me when he is ready." Instead, the first "Khaki loan" was an open market sale of Exchequer bonds at 98.5 — bypassing the Rothschilds. Hamilton discerned "the jealousy with which the Rothschilds regard Cassel."
- When the government needed a massive £60m consol issue, it called on J.P. Morgan. Half the total was taken by Morgan, N.M. Rothschild, and the Bank of England (£10m apiece) at 94.5. But Morgan secured a commission two times higher than the London banks. "The fact was that the distinctly un-German and un-Jewish figure of Pierpont Morgan was the principal victor: for the first time in over a century, the British government had been forced to borrow a large sum from a foreign power to wage a war in its own empire."
- Morgan muscled in again on a spring 1902 £32m loan and the 1903 Transvaal loan of £30m. On the latter, Natty's request for a 2.75% coupon was overruled by the Treasury — "a change of policy which Alfred angrily denounced as 'most un-English.'"
- New Year's Eve 1900 at Mentmore: "Rosebery after dinner proposed 'prosperity to the House of Rothschild' in a touching little speech, which elicited tears from Natty and Leo." The dawn of the new century had brought "the first unambiguous indication that the Rothschilds' dominance was coming to an end."

## CHAPTER 12: Finances and Alliances (1885-1906)

**The Failed Anglo-German Entente:**
- Rothschilds (especially Alfred in London) worked persistently to build an Anglo-German alliance. Alfred used the "Schwabach-Rothschild channel" — Paul von Schwabach of Bleichröder in Berlin and Ambassador Hatzfeldt — as unofficial diplomatic intermediaries.
- Ottoman Empire: Germans showed interest in Turkish finance from 1889. Berlin-Baghdad Railway (Georg von Siemens/Deutsche Bank, 1899) — Natty advised government to "take a part of the ordinary shares" as with Suez Canal. British pulled out of consortium in March 1903 after right-wing press attacked the 35% German investor share vs 25% British.
- Natty on the Ottoman Empire: "I always dread the reopening of any Eastern Question." Called it "the terrible Turk" and "the Turkish mess." After Young Turk revolution (1908), stopped short of lending — dismissed Ernest Cassel's efforts "to direct the policy of the Ottoman Empire by financial means."
- South Africa poisoned Anglo-German relations: Kaiser's "Kruger telegram" (1896) congratulating Boers on repelling Jameson Raid did lasting damage. Rothschilds relied on Warburgs to conciliate Kruger. Alfred proposed involving Germany in Uitlander negotiations (1897) — Chamberlain dismissed the idea.
- Alfred warned German press attacks on Britain amounted to a "'pinprick' policy — although a pin is not a very impressive instrument, repeated pricks may cause a wound."
- The Times Berlin correspondent Saunders exploded at the idea of Rothschild Germanophilia: "I know the power of German influence dynastic, racial &c., including the Rothschilds. It is not business. It is dining, shooting, toasts, finance, honours, marriages, dynastic friendships."
- Salisbury's deep skepticism killed the alliance. His private secretary McDonnell told Salisbury that Alfred was "suffering from megalomania" for accepting a German decoration (Order of the Crown, First Class). McDonnell wrote a satirical "stage directions" memo mocking Eckardstein's threats that the Kaiser would ally with Russia and France unless Britain cooperated.
- Chamberlain's "New Triple Alliance between the Teutonic race and the two great branches of the Anglo-Saxon race" rhetoric went nowhere — Bülow's Reichstag speech (Dec 11, 1899) simultaneously offered "full reciprocity" while the German press attacked Britain.
- Why Anglo-German entente failed: (1) Salisbury's fundamental suspicion; (2) France offered bigger concession — final acceptance of British Egypt in exchange for French Morocco; (3) Russo-Japanese War (1904-05) changed the Asian balance of power, making Anglo-Japanese alliance viable; (4) Germany didn't need British capital the way borrower nations like Russia did.

**The Rationale of the Entente with France:**
- France's offer: final recognition of British position in Egypt (20+ years of friction ended). France got right to "preserve order" in Morocco — de facto control.
- Cassel, not Rothschilds, was now the key financial figure in Egypt — raised money for Aswan dam after 1897, won Lord Cromer's confidence.
- Rothschilds added their voice: Natty's son Walter told Cromer "our cousins in Paris" would support Egyptian debt redemption only "with the agreement of the French Government."
- Anglo-Japanese alliance (Jan 1902) was the real watershed ending British isolationism. Japan, by contrast with Germany, had every reason to seek a European ally — Russia refused to compromise over Manchuria.

**Rothschilds and Japan:**
- N.M. Rothschild & Parr's had jointly underwritten Japan's first railway loan (Edo-Yokohama, 1872), but the connection lapsed. Barings took the lead in 1898.
- Japan sought £5.1 million loan. Natty told Lansdowne it was "a matter of political importance" but declined to lead — initiative passed to Barings and Hong Kong & Shanghai Bank.
- Curious that Natty missed this: 1903 was the year of the Kishinev pogrom (45 Jews killed), the 1905 pogroms intensified Rothschild Russophobia. Yet they were slow to back Russia's enemy Japan.
- Only after war broke out (1904) did Rothschilds participate in a £5 million Japanese loan (consortium with Kuhn, Loeb and M.M. Warburg). Coincided with "absolute" refusal of any Russian loan — Walter told Herbert Gladstone this.
- Paris Rothschilds also refused Russia: Alphonse told German ambassador (Aug 1904): "The Paris House of Rothschild is hostile to Russia... Russia had made fine promises regarding the future treatment of his co-religionists, if only the money were forthcoming, but his attitude was that these were empty promises."
- Japanese victories at Port Arthur, Mukden, and Tsushima (1905) vindicated the decision. London and Paris houses involved in a further £25 million Japanese loan (1906) and £11.5 million (1907).
- Natty on Japan: "their dense & intelligent population, their loyalty zeal & intelligence, will soon bring them to the front rank both in commerce & manufacture." But jealous of Jacob Schiff's credit: Schiff was now "welcome & much honoured guest at Tokio" who had "incense poured on his devoted head."

**Rothschilds Underpinning the Anglo-French Entente — Central Bank Cooperation:**
- After 1905, London and Paris Rothschilds acted as private helpmeets to the Bank of England and Banque de France, facilitating cross-Channel gold transfers essential to gold standard stability.
- Nov 1906: Bank rate at 6%, gold draining to Brazil/India — Natty and Edouard arranged £600,000 in gold sovereigns from Banque de France as "policy of courtesy & of aid." Then another £400,000, topped up with £600,000 more. Total: £1.4 million in gold swaps — may have averted a further Bank rate increase. Rate cut from 6% to 4% by April 1907.
- Rothschilds brokered Bank of England's secret purchase of Constantinople Quay company shares (1906-07) — joint Anglo-French bid to acquire controlling interest. Natty: diplomatic transactions "could not be left to the Foreign Office mandarins... they may be diplomatists but they are certainly not men of business."
- 1907 crisis (worst pre-1914): Natty forewarned, urged Bank rate up to 4.5% earlier. When American crisis hit in October, Bank rate raised again. Natty asked Paris for French gold — was "in no mood for cousinly prevarication." French Rothschilds blamed Roosevelt's "misguided attacks on Wall Street." Natty retorted: "It would be just as wise to lay down as a rule that in no case the fire brigade was to extinguish a fire if it originally arose out of the action of an incendiary."
- Nov 4, 1907: Bank rate to 6% — Governor William Campbell sent for Natty personally. Bank "unanimously decided to ask me to telegraph to you and ask you to use your best endeavours to renew with the Bank of France the operation which you did last year." Rothschilds provided £400,000 of a £3 million gold transfer. When J.P. Morgan directly approached Banque de France for American market aid, he was rebuffed — vindication of the Rothschild cross-Channel link.
- Natty's key insight on the gold standard: "the whole trade of the world is carried on by bills on London." £300-400 million in drafts always running on London, "probably more than half are for foreign account." Central bank cooperation was essential — without Banque de France acting generously, gold would drain to London creating "far worse" inconvenience. "As far as I can how intimately and of necessity all countries are bound together."

**The Anglo-Russian Antagonism & the Russian Loan Question:**
- Natty refused Russian loans on both moral (Jewish persecution) and financial grounds. Predicted Russian bonds would lose out due to "renewed discontent and rioting which may be sporadic in various places but which will become general." Compared election of first Duma to the Estates General of 1789.
- 1905 pogroms: Natty wrote to The Times denouncing "the unspeakable calamities which have befallen the Jews in Russia... mercilessly struck down and massacred. Fiendish savagery." Matched Jacob Schiff's £10,000 donation for victims.
- Russian envoys tried to trade loans for promises: Arthur Raffalovich proposed an "International Syndicate" willing to accommodate the Czar "with untold millions, anything between £60 to £120,000,000!" if reforms were promised. Natty refused: "we could & would not help Russia in her need until she had done for our co-religionists that which would make them happy & contented subjects."
- Dr. Brandt (Russian embassy) told Natty bluntly: "The Jew is an object of horror & detestation to everyone in Russia... you cannot emigrate 5 millions of Russian Jews." Natty's reply: "when the Russian Jew has liberty & equal rights, Russian finance would improve."
- The massive £89 million Russian loan (1906) was arranged by Revelstoke and Morgan with Asquith and Grey's blessing — Rothschilds pointedly stayed out. Natty gloated when bond prices slumped by July: "As we have been wise enough to eschew Russian finance for some considerable time... & lucky enough not to be inveigled by false promises."
- Stolypin's response to Rothschild pressure was contemptuous: blamed Jews for their own persecution, said "if the Jews had equal rights they would soon hold all the land in Russia."
- Russian 4 per cents: from low of 71.5 (Aug 1906) rose to peak of 96.25 (Dec 1910) — handsome profits for those who stayed in.

**Austria-Hungary — The Rothschild Group Disintegrates:**
- Vienna house links to other Rothschild houses essentially non-existent by 1900. Albert described as "a peculiar mix of gentleman and brutal potentate" who exerted influence through interlocking directorships — "a sort of tyranny."
- Rothschild consortium (Creditanstalt, Bodencreditanstalt, Ungarische Creditbank) handled Austrian/Hungarian bond issues worth ~2.8 billion crowns (c. £120 million). But monopoly crumbled: Austrian Finance Minister Böhm-Bawerk let non-Rothschild banks participate in conversion operations (1903). By 1908 Austria-Hungary adopted public subscription for new bond issues.
- Albert's death (1911) effectively dissolved the Rothschild group. Son Louis modernized but Bodencreditanstalt broke ranks.
- 1914: London Rothschilds (with Schröders) arranged £19.5 million in Austrian and Hungarian bonds — too little, too late to pull Austria from the Dual Alliance with Germany.
- Ferguson's key insight: capital flows shaped diplomatic alliances. Net creditor nations (Britain, France) gravitated together; net borrowers (Russia, Italy) gravitated toward their lenders. France was Russia's main source of external finance — this made the Franco-Russian entente economically logical despite having "less in common in terms of their internal politics than any other two-power combination." The Rothschilds played a central role in the Franco-Russian financial link despite their antipathy to Tsarist anti-Jewish policies.

## CHAPTER 13: The Military-Financial Complex (1906-1914)

**Could Bankers Prevent War?**
- Hobson (1902): "a great war could not be undertaken by any European state if the house of Rothschild and its connexions set their face against it." Ivan Bloch (1899) estimated war between continental powers at £4 million/day. Norman Angell's The Great Illusion (1912): war was irrational due to "the delicate interdependence of international finance."
- Henry Wickham Steed (foreign editor of The Times, July 1914) called Natty's efforts to avert war "a dirty German-Jewish international financial attempt to bully us into advocating neutrality."
- Ferguson rejects the Hobson/Angell thesis: the Rothschilds did succumb to "the militarisation of the bourgeoisie." By the fourth generation they were enthusiastic about the military — Natty held a commission in the Bucks Yeomanry (1863), promoted to captain (1884), his son reached major (1903).

**Rothschilds in the Arms Industry:**
- 1888: London house issued £225,000 in shares for the Naval Construction and Armaments Company, then £1.9 million for the merger of Maxim Gun Company with Nordenfelt Guns and Ammunition Company. This was one of the first deals with Ernest Cassel.
- Natty retained a substantial shareholding in Maxim-Nordenfelt and exerted direct influence over management. The Maxim gun — "the lethal automatic gun" — was used devastatingly from Sudan to Matabeleland. Hilaire Belloc's famous line about European hegemony.
- Cassel and Rothschilds financed Vickers brothers' takeover (with Naval Construction and Armaments Company) in 1897 — responding to increased British naval construction rooted in imperial policy.
- 1888: Natty tried to lure future First Sea Lord "Jacky" Fisher away from the navy to join Whitworths. Remained an enthusiast for naval construction even when costs meant higher taxes.
- Austrian Rothschilds: Witkowitz ironworks supplied iron/steel to Austrian navy and later bullets to the army. But Albert had "not the slightest interest in the industrial installations that his house possesses... considers them as a disagreeable duty." When asked for 400,000 gulden to invest in armour plating, Albert replied he'd rather buy country property.

**The Rising Cost of the Military-Financial Complex:**
- Table 13a: Total military expenditure of Britain, France, Russia (Dual Entente) rose from £123.4m (1894) to £173.7m (1913) — 40.8% increase. Germany and Austria (Dual Alliance): from £45.8m to £118.4m — 158.5% increase. Triple Alliance (adding Italy): from £59.8m to £158.0m — 164.2% increase.
- Table 13b: Defence as % of net national product rose across the board: Britain 2.0% (1873) to 3.2% (1913); France 3.1% to 4.8%; Russia to 5.1%; Germany 2.4% to 3.9%.
- Rising military spending precipitated both Randolph Churchill's resignation as Chancellor (1886) and Gladstone's as PM (1894) — first political casualties of the military-financial complex.

**Natty's Untenable Position — Wanting Naval Supremacy Without Paying For It:**
- March 1909: Natty told Institute of Directors that "the Fleet must be maintained in the highest state of efficiency" but simultaneously opposed paying for it through taxation. Supported the campaign for eight dreadnoughts (not four).
- This contradiction gave Lloyd George the perfect weapon. At the Holborn Restaurant, Lloyd George's famous speech: "Really, in all of these things we are having too much Lord Rothschild. We are not to have temperance reform... Why? Because Lord Rothschild has sent a circular to the Peers saying so. We must have more Dreadnoughts. Why? Because Lord Rothschild said so at a meeting in the City... Now, really, I should like to know, is Lord Rothschild the dictator of this country?"
- Lloyd George at Walworth Hall (Dec 18): "Who clamoured for additional Dreadnoughts? He remembered a great meeting in the City, presided over by Lord Rothschild, who demanded that eight Dreadnoughts should be instantly laid down... and Lord Rothschild would not pay (laughter). There had been a very cruel king in the past who ordered Lord Rothschild's ancestors to make bricks without straw (loud laughter). That was a much easier job than making Dreadnoughts without money."
- The anti-Semitic overtone (King John/bricks without straw) was noted but did not diminish the effectiveness of the attack.

**Lloyd George's People's Budget (1909) and the Constitutional Crisis:**
- Budget features: increase in "unearned" income tax to 1s 2d in the pound, super-tax on incomes above £5,000, increase in inheritance tax, levies on land values.
- Natty organized letter to Asquith signed by 21 City leaders (14 houses including Barings, Gibbs, Hambros, J.S. Morgan) warning that death duties would "eat into capital" and "discourage private enterprise and thrift."
- Chaired protest meeting at Cannon Street Hotel (June 23) passing resolution that budget would "weaken the security in all private property."
- Natty presented Budget Protest League petition (14,000 signatures) to the Lords (Nov 22). Called the budget "the end of all, the negation of faith, of family, of property, of Monarchy, of Empire — in short, revolution."
- But the markets refused to cooperate with Natty's doom narrative. Consol yield spread between Conservative and Liberal governments was less than 10 basis points. Consols fell from 90.4 (Feb 1906) to pre-war low of 71.8 (end 1913), but this was driven by inflation and global monetary conditions, not Liberal fiscal policy. Markets rose in first five months of 1909. A London County Council loan was heavily oversubscribed right after the budget.
- Ferguson's verdict: Liberal tax policy was "entirely orthodox" — aimed to reduce the deficit Lloyd George inherited. Natty's claim that surpluses would be "squandered on socialistic expenditure" was contradicted by his own letters describing "firm and brilliant markets" in the same breath.

**The Decline of Political Influence:**
- By 1906, Natty had become so overtly partisan Conservative that he was cut off from Liberal government intelligence. Edward Hamilton warned in 1902 that Natty had "become so strong a party man, he will now be 'out of it' whenever the other side comes in."
- Previously Rothschilds mixed with politicians of all parties. Now Natty was "himself a politician, making remarkably frequent public speeches and donating substantial sums to the Tory party machine."
- 1906 Liberal landslide: 400 out of 670 seats vs Conservatives' 157. Natty called it "unexpectedly bad" and a "disastrous result."
- Bonar Law replaced Balfour as Conservative leader (Nov 1911) — Natty barely knew him. Family contributed "£12,000 a year and large sums at Elections" according to Conservative chairman Steel-Maitland, but the relationship cooled.
- Natty in 1912 essay "England and Germany": "What have we not got in common with Germany? Nothing perhaps except their army and our navy. But a combination of the most powerful military nation with the most powerful naval nation ought to be such as to command the respect of the whole world, and ensure universal peace." — a poignant, almost pathetic hope by this point.

**"Hatred Let Loose" — The July Crisis and Outbreak of WWI:**
- Even in 1914, civil war in Ireland seemed more imminent than European war. June-July 1914 at New Court: dominated by Ulster and Brazilian loan negotiations. Max Warburg was in London three times to finalize his firm's role — a sign of "good financial relations."
- Of 25 letters between New Court and rue Laffitte (June 29 - July 23), only 5 mention Franz Ferdinand's assassination. Natty dismissed it as "a sad example" of "Servian brutality."
- July 22: Natty still confident Serbia would "eat humble pie" and Russia would "remain quiet." July 27: "No one thinks and talks about anything else but the European situation." Austrian ultimatum to Serbia would "give every satisfaction."
- July 27: Austrian declaration of war on Serbia — "All the foreign Banks and particularly the German ones took a very large amount of money out of the Stock Exchange to-day." Markets collapsed.
- July 28: Paris house sent coded telegram requesting sale of "a vast quantity of Consols" for French government/savings banks. Natty refused — "a deplorable effect if we were to send gold to a Continental Power for the purpose of strengthening itself at a moment when 'War' is in the mouths of everyone."
- July 29: Consols plunged from 74 to 69.5. Bank of England advanced £14 million to the discount market. Bank rate pushed from 3% to 4%.
- July 31: Bank rate doubled to 8%, then raised 2% more the next day. Stock exchange closed — worst crisis of preceding hundred years, unprecedented since 1847, 1857, 1866.
- Acceptance houses in "desperate straits" — around £350 million in bills of exchange outstanding, many unlikely to be honoured. Table 13g shows N.M. Rothschild's acceptances fell from £3.49m (1912) to £1.31m (1914).
- Natty's last attempt at peace (July 31): urged France to restrain Russia, appealing to Rothschild financial leverage: "France is Russia's greatest creditor, in fact the financial and economic conditions of the two countries are intimately connected & we hope you will do your best to bring any influence you may have, to bear upon your statesmen even at the last moment, to prevent this hideous struggle from taking place."
- Aug 1: Natty appealed personally to the Kaiser for peace — Kaiser minuted "an old and much respected acquaintance of mine. Some 75 to 80 years old." Futile — communications cut before reply possible.
- Aug 3: Grey addressed Commons — German invasion of Belgium was "an act which England could never tolerate." Natty told cousins he agreed.
- Natty accurately foresaw "the greatest military fight in the annals of the world, a horrid war, the duration of which no one could foresee."

**Financial Impact of the War:**
- N.M. Rothschild & Sons lost close to £1.5 million in 1914 — equivalent to 23% of its capital. No other major City bank was so drastically affected.
- Lloyd George sought Natty's advice on the financial crisis. Lloyd George recalled: "'Lord Rothschild, we have had some political unpleasantness.' He interrupted me: 'Mr Lloyd George, this is no time to recall those things. What can I do to help?' I told him. He undertook to do it at once. It was done."
- Natty helped broker the compromise: gold convertibility maintained but Bank rate lowered further; Bank would discount all bills accepted before Aug 4 at the new lower rate. Alfred and Leo congratulated Lloyd George on "the most successful manner with which you dealt with a difficulty quite unparalleled in the history of the finances of this Country."
- Cross-Channel war finance: initial loan of £1.7 million from Britain to France via Rothschilds, followed by advances against Treasury bills totalling £8 million (Oct 1914 - Oct 1917). But this was "small beer" — France borrowed £610 million from Britain during the war, dwarfed by £738 million from the United States.
- When Edouard telegraphed J.P. Morgan (Aug 1) requesting $100 million loan, Morgan "had drawn a blank." Morgan had not forgotten being refused a share of the South African loan twelve years earlier. The financial center of gravity shifted decisively across the Atlantic — confirming the strategic error of never establishing a Rothschild house in New York.
- "United on the battlefields, we are also united in finance!" Edouard telegraphed New Court. But what united the Rothschilds after 1914 was decline — a decline that continued for at least half a century.

## CHAPTER 14: "Deluges" (1915-1945)

**Interwar Latin American Business:**
- London house continued traditional relationship with Brazil and Chile. American ambassador in Brazil: "the Rothschilds have so mortgaged Brazil's financial future that... they will place every obstacle in the way of her entering into banking relations with any other house."
- London house issued bonds worth £28 million+ for Brazilian federal government in interwar years, plus £17.5 million for Brazilian states and railways. Chile figure around £10 million.
- Brazil's 1922 loan of £9 million (with Barings and Schroders) designed to finance coffee price-support scheme, placing coffee export control in hands of City banks.
- Brazil's £25 million loan in 1923: Lionel sent Edwin Montagu to impose "some palatable form of foreign financial control" on Banco de Brasil. Rejected — "most unpopular in Brazil for the national bank to be owned by foreigners."
- After spat with Britain over Germany's League of Nations admission, Brazil turned to Wall Street. Rothschild agent Henry Lynch ("Sir Lynch" locally) remained key figure in country's finances.

**Bodencreditanstalt and the Gold Standard:**
- Louis bailed out the Bodencreditanstalt — just as his great-grandfather Salomon had bailed out Arnstein & Eskeles on the eve of the 1848 crisis. This decision would bring the Vienna house to the brink of ruin.
- N.M. Rothschild took on role of intermediary between bullion market and Bank of England after wartime ban on gold exports was lifted. Advanced £3 17s 9d per standard ounce to South African mine companies (roughly half of world gold output), pooled any premium, remitted to mines every six months.
- Thus was born the "Fix" — the world gold price set every morning at 11 a.m. at New Court, beginning September 12, 1919.
- Rothschilds liked gold for the same reason the rest of the City did: feared that if the pound floated freely, London's financial capital would pass irrevocably to New York.
- Walter argued (rightly) in 1931 that the gold standard's breakdown was "nothing to do with the rights or wrongs of Capitalism or Socialism, but... is owing to the greed of [certain] countries for gold." Key difference from pre-1914: US and France "sterilised" additions to reserves, refusing central bank cooperation. Without it, the system could not survive.

**French Politics — Edouard and the Franc:**
- Edouard vehemently opposed as one of twelve regents of the Banque de France when franc was pegged at 20% of pre-war value in 1928. He wanted full return to pre-war parity.
- Appointment of Emile Moreau as Governor of the Banque in June 1926 diminished Rothschild influence; Moreau argued realistically for stabilisation at existing rate.
- Under Poincare, 3% rente rose from 48.25 to 67.60 francs; Rothschild influence declined.
- Maurice's political career: elected 1919 on National Bloc ticket using slogan "My name is my platform." Spent 1.6 million francs (£15,000) on one by-election. Election annulled — motion defeated only 180 to 178.

**The Crash and Great Depression:**
- Black Thursday (Oct 24, 1929) — $30 billion wiped off stocks worth $80 billion in a month. Dow from peak of 381 (Sept 1929) to final trough of 50 (May 1932).
- First Rothschild Depression crisis came in Brazil: Vargas coup (1930) undercut negotiations. September 1931 Vargas suspended foreign debt payments. 1934: complete restructuring with Rothschilds, Paribas, and Dillon Read. Government paid £6-8 million annually; sterling bonds not fully liquidated until 1962.
- Chile: COSACH (Compania de Salitre de Chile) set up 1931 on basis of £2 million loan issued jointly by N.M. Rothschild, Barings, Schroders, Morgan Grenfell. Doomed — exports declined, COSACH liquidated Jan 1933, moratorium on debt. Took twenty years to resolve.

**Creditanstalt Crisis (May 11, 1931):**
- Creditanstalt revealed losses of 140 million schillings (~£4 million) against paid-up capital of 125 million. Actual losses probably closer to 160 million schillings. Most losses attributable to merger with Bodencreditanstalt which government had insisted on.
- Vienna house held ~16.7 million schillings of Creditanstalt's capital. Paris house lent a further 136 million francs for six years.
- Yet rescue failed to avert panic spreading from Vienna to Hungary, Germany, and all of Europe. Bank of England's £4.3 million short-term loan insufficient. In September a run on the Bank of England ended the pound's brief return to gold standard.
- Creditanstalt crisis = final break between Vienna house and London house. Lionel declared putting more money into the "haemorrhaging bank" would be "inexpedient." Edmond warned it would be "dangerous" even to look at Vienna house's accounts.
- Edmond's chilling quote: "What is happening in the Vienna bank does not concern us. We advanced funds, it's a question of honour for Vienna to reimburse them... The Vienna house is not our business and in sum, as one of the heads of the Paris house, I do not wish to give them any money, not a penny more."
- September 1933: Louis wound up involvement in Creditanstalt, which became a state-controlled concern.
- Creditanstalt crisis = single most serious blow to Rothschilds' post-war position, biting deep into capital of all three houses.

**Involvement with Ivan Kreuger:**
- 1929: London house joined Lee, Higginson & Co. to issue shares for Swedish financier Kreuger totalling $10 million. Three years later Kreuger committed suicide, his empire collapsed, taking Lee, Higginson down. Rothschild houses survived.
- Goldschmidt-Rothschild & Co. (formerly A. Falkenberger) handed over to the Reichs-Kredit-Gesellschaft in 1932 — one of the lesser casualties of the German banking crisis.

**Shift to Domestic Corporate Finance:**
- Before 1914, N.M. Rothschild had been hesitant to involve itself in domestic economy. Changed from 1928: issues of debenture stock (with Barings and Schroders) for London underground railway companies.
- 1930: London National Property Co. raised £2 million through Rothschilds for Shell-Mex House purchase in the Strand, which it let to Shell Transport and Trading Co.
- 1931: Woolworths persuaded by Philip Hill to issue £9.36 million shares through New Court. Woolworths offer nearly wrecked by minor City panic — staff worked all night behind locked doors completing acceptance letters before subscribers could pull out.

**Fading Grandeur and the Next Generation:**
- Halton sold to Royal Air Force for £112,000. Aston Clinton became a hotel. Gunnersbury became public park. First Rothschild house in West End (107 Piccadilly) demolished 1929 for ballroom. Alfred's grand house at 1 Seamore Place demolished nine years later. Three of the French Rothschilds' houses given up.
- Walter sold nearly his entire Tring collection of stuffed birds to the American Museum of Natural History for $225,000 (less than $1 per specimen).
- *Jewish Chronicle* (1935): "The age of rationalisation and of multiple stores, of chemicals and oil has dawned... the sway of the former ruling families [is] no longer absolute."
- Cecil Roth's *The Magnificent Rothschilds* (1938) read as "an epitaph not only for the third and fourth generations... but also for the family's magnificence: 'All had passed away... It was a different world.'"

**Victor Rothschild and the Cambridge Spies:**
- Victor, the most intellectually gifted of the next generation, turned his back on banking — a vote of no confidence in the "moribund, boring, rather painful" 1930s City. Influenced at Cambridge by Anthony Blunt and Guy Burgess, whose sympathies were inimical to capitalism.
- Years of speculation that Victor was the "fifth man" — a last, hitherto unexposed Soviet mole. His relationship with Blunt and Burgess remained close enough to furnish circumstantial evidence: they rented his house in Bentinck Street during the war; Victor recommended Blunt to MI5 in August 1940; in Paris in 1944 he seconded Kim Philby's argument that Soviets should be given "Ultra" intercepts.
- Victor found communism "rather dull." When he finally discovered in 1962 that Philby was a communist, he had no hesitation reporting it to MI5.

**The Flood — Political Attacks from Left and Right:**
- Great irony: precisely when Rothschild myth of power reached its zenith, both radical left and radical right in France, Germany, and Austria directed unprecedented propaganda against the family. What was new: rhetoric now translated into political action.
- France 1934: Stavisky affair triggered attempted right-wing coup. Daladier launched tirade against "two hundred families" allegedly "masters of the French economy." Hint enlarged by Communist *L'Humanite* alleging links between Rothschilds and La Rocque.
- 1936 Popular Front government pledged "liberation of the State from the grips of financial feudalism." Railway nationalization: Compagnie du Nord received 270,000 shares in new Societe Nationale, guaranteed annual royalty, and a board seat. Rene Mayer's hard bargaining meant companies came out ahead — state took over 6 billion francs in debts while companies kept non-rail assets.

**Nazi Confiscation in Germany:**
- December 1933: Frankfurt Rothschildallee renamed Karolingerallee. Luisenplatz and Mathildenstrasse lost plaques identifying them with the family.
- April 1938: "Ordinance on the Registration of Jewish Assets" — Rothschild property came under direct attack. After Kristallnacht (November 1938): nearly all ~20 Rothschild charitable and educational foundations dissolved. Baron Wilhelm Carl von Rothschild Foundation "Aryanised" under pressure, all references to founder expunged.
- Maximilian (age 95) stayed on in the Bockenheimer Landstrasse house. Forced to sell property to Frankfurt for just 610,000 reichsmarks. After Kristallnacht: obliged to sell art collection for 2.3 million reichsmarks and donate 25% of remaining assets as "atonement payment." Died 1940; rest of property confiscated. Five years later, Allied bombers destroyed the house and the old Fahrgasse office building and the Stammhaus in the Bornestrasse.

**The Anschluss and Louis's Arrest (March 11, 1938):**
- Day after Anschluss, Louis attempted to leave Vienna. Passport confiscated, arrested, taken to Gestapo headquarters at Hotel Metropol on Morzin-Platz (where he found himself with former Chancellor Schuschnigg and socialist leader Kuntschak).
- SS men seen looting artworks from Louis's palace almost immediately. March 30: firm of S.M. von Rothschild placed under compulsory administration by Austrian Credit Institute under Walter Rafelsberger, charged with confiscating all Jewish assets in Austria.
- Louis had to hand over most Austrian assets to secure his release. When Himmler sent ornate French furniture to Louis's prison, Louis complained it made his cell look like a "Cracow bordello."
- Witkowitz ironworks: Goring wanted it for Reichswerke Hermann-Goring. Eugène's hopes of selling to Czechoslovak state for £10 million dashed by German partition of Czechoslovakia (March 1939). July 1939: agreed to sell plant for £2.9 million — but war gave Germans excuse not to pay. January 1941: Goring seized 43,300 Witkowitz shares from Paris house vaults. Communist Czechoslovak government only paid compensation (£1 million) in 1953.

**Nazi Art Theft:**
- Alfred Rosenberg — "Führer's Delegate for Total Spiritual and Philosophical Development of the NSDAP" — led plundering of Rothschild collections, arguing "the Rothschilds are an enemy Jewish family and all their machinations to save their possessions should leave us cold."
- Within a remarkably short time Rosenberg rounded up 203 private collections including most of those listed: total of 21,903 works from nine different locations. Rothschilds were the most important single source of plunder: 3,978 items taken from nine locations.
- Most prized: Vermeer's *Astronomer*, Boucher's *Madame de Pompadour*, plus thirty masterpieces by Hals and Rembrandt — all earmarked for Hitler.
- Goring visited Jeu de Paume in November 1940 as Hitler's "buyer" — grabbed Dutch and French works from Edouard's collection and a Memling Madonna for his wife.
- Vichy regime also targeted Rothschilds: Petain decrees (Oct 3, 1940 and June 2, 1941) restricted rights of French Jews. Maurice Janicot ran Public Property Office — Vichy aim was to pre-empt the Germans, not protect the Rothschilds.

**The Rothschild Family in Wartime:**
- Robert fled to Montreal before fall of Paris. Edouard (now in his seventies) left France via Spain and Portugal, reaching the US. Guy narrowly avoided capture, settled in La Bourboule (Auvergne) where de Rothschild Freres offices had moved; fled via Morocco in 1941. His decision to join de Gaulle nearly cost his life — ship torpedoed in the Atlantic. He survived.
- Robert's sons Alain and Elie taken prisoner; Elie ended up in Colditz. Guy earned the Croix de Guerre.
- Two family members died in the Holocaust: Victor's mother's eldest sister Aranka, who perished at Buchenwald; and Philippe's estranged wife Lili (comtesse de Chambure), who asked "Why should the Germans harm me? I am from an old French Catholic family." Arrested by Gestapo July 1944, sent to Ravensbruck, brutally murdered. "The blackest of ironies that the only person named Rothschild killed by the Nazis was not a Jew and had disowned the family name."
- Major Edmund de Rothschild led his battery into Mannheim in November 1944 "through an archway which still bore the repulsive legend *judenrein*." People shouted: "Die Juden kommen! Die Juden kommen!" At Hitler's Eagle's Nest: "Seeing a mass of broken Sèvres porcelain... I wondered if it had been stolen from one of my cousins' homes. It probably had."

**WWII Impact on the Bank:**
- WWII financed in ways that left no room for private bankers. New Keynesian age: governments managed economic life directly. New Court almost empty during war — Anthony presided. More than half clerical staff and records moved to Tring.
- May 10, 1941: incendiary bombs hit New Court during ferocious bombardment that destroyed Salters Hall and "literally surrounded New Court by fire."
- Royal Mint refinery converted to artillery-parts production. Exbury taken over by the navy (temporarily renamed HMS *Mastodon*). Charles's and Rozsika's house at Ashton Wold used by Red Cross and Ordnance Corps.
- Victor's sister Miriam at Bletchley: "The Holocaust; the war; my parents' deaths; the end of the garden. Nothing seemed to matter any more."
- Of the fifth generation, only Anthony lived to see Allied victory, serving as a private in the Home Guard. Charles and Walter died before the war; Lionel died January 1942.

## EPILOGUE: Post-War Recovery and the Modern Rothschild Group

**Anthony's Stewardship (1945-1955):**
- Anthony dedicated himself to conserving heritage. Commuted daily from Leighton Buzzard to Euston to New Court. Harold Nicolson described lunch then being "hurried out" at 2:30 p.m., "as then the work begins again and the great wheels of the Maison Rothschild revolve."
- But the war had reduced the firm's size. Anthony's approach was fatalistic: "They know where we live. If they want to do business with us let them come and talk to us."
- Partners arrived at the Room between 10 and 10:30 a.m., spent the morning perusing mail "to see if there was anything likely to result in some business."
- All documents required partner signatures. When Edmund questioned this practice, Anthony's reply was invariably: "I'm afraid I don't quite understand this... No. You wouldn't."

**Capital Contraction:**
- On eve of WWI, London house capital close to £8 million. By post-war period: reduced to £1.5 million — accounting for the pound's 40% purchasing power loss, a dramatic decline.
- When Lionel died: overdraft of £500,000, plus £200,000 in death duties for his children.
- 1947 restructuring: £1 million of voteless preference shares and £500,000 of ordinary voting shares created. Anthony retained 60% of ordinary shares, ensuring dominance. Edmund and Victor each got 20%.

**Post-War Constraints:**
- Bretton Woods system left little scope for traditional international bond issues. Zenith of British socialism: Attlee governments hostile to the City.
- Labour supporter (1948): "I do not believe that people should be allowed to have a lot of money unless they have earned it; being the son of a rich man is not a good enough reason."
- This hostility helps explain why the 3rd Lord Rothschild (Victor) kept his distance from New Court throughout the 1940s and 1950s. Left academia for Royal Dutch Shell in 1959.
- Gold market reopened — world gold price again set in New Court's "fixing room." But with international gold pool holding price at $35/ounce, this had lost much importance. Firm concentrated on documentary credit and acceptance business.

**Brinco — The Newfoundland Project:**
- Most ambitious post-war project: developing resource-rich Newfoundland. Joseph Smallwood's scheme strengthened by bank's continuing links with Churchill (whose private secretary was David Colville, brother of Rothschild partner Jock).
- Lord Leathers: "You did Suez, so why can't you do Newfoundland?" Yet Anthony was hesitant. Edmund's efforts kept N.M. Rothschild on board. March 1953: 60,000 square miles leased to Brinco consortium for twenty years.
- When consortium privately distributed two million Brinco shares, Churchill himself bought 10,000. But project never extricated itself from political tangles with Quebec government and Canadian nationalism.

**The New Generation — Evelyn and Jacob:**
- Anthony suffered stroke in 1955, died six years later. Evelyn joined bank in 1957; Jacob in 1963 (after Oxford, Cooper Brothers, Morgan Stanley, and Herman Robinow).
- July 1960: David Colville became first non-family partner — ending a century and a half of tradition. Michael Bucks elevated September 1961; Philip Shelbourne (tax lawyer) April 1962 to create new Finance Department.
- September 1970: partnership finally incorporated as N.M. Rothschild & Sons Limited, ending unlimited liability. New board: four non-executive directors, twenty executive directors.
- October 1962: old New Court offices demolished. Temporary move to City Gate House, Finsbury Square. New six-storey building constructed. Japanese newspaper reported construction of a new *sixty*-storey building — typical of outside world's exaggerated impression.
- At incorporation: issued share capital just £10 million (with £2 million reserves), balance sheet just £168 million. Jacob's declaration (1965): "We must try to make ourselves as much a bank of brains as of money."

**Rothschild Investment Trust (RIT):**
- July 1961: RIT set up with £3 million capital (two-thirds from outside investors). Under Jacob: initial pre-tax profits exceeded 20% of capital. By 1970 joined by four other publicly quoted Rothschild trusts. By end of 1970s: gross receipts nearly £7 million, net assets close to £100 million (vs. just £6 million in 1970).
- But RIT moved in a different direction from parent company. By 1975 N.M. Rothschild reduced stake to 9.4%. When Saul Steinberg's Reliance Group acquired quarter of RIT for £16 million in 1979, the link seemed likely to break altogether.

**Corporate Finance and Takeovers:**
- 1964: Manchester branch opened — first Rothschild office in the city since 1811. Leeds branch followed two years later.
- 1968: eighth in City takeover league, having organised five deals worth £370 million total. Two years later: fifth in issuing houses league, raising £20 million for client companies.
- 1969: first encounter with Robert Maxwell — advised Saul Steinberg's £25 million bid for Maxwell's Pergamon Press. Deal fell through when bidders uncovered irregularities prompting Board of Trade enquiry.

**The Jacob-Evelyn Split:**
- Jacob wanted to merge N.M. Rothschild with S.G. Warburg — plan codenamed "War and Peace." Opposed by Evelyn and Victor. Alternative "Pandora" plan (merge with RIT) also blocked.
- Jacob departed New Court in 1980. New business: J. Rothschild & Company (later known by acronym JRH). In 1981 merged RIT with Great Northern Investment to form RIT & Northern.
- In three years: acquired 9.6% of breakfast TV company TV-am, 50% of New York merchant bank L.F. Rothschild, Unterberg, Towbin, 29.9% of City broker Kitcat & Aitken, and merged with Charterhouse Group to form Charterhouse J. Rothschild — market cap £400 million, more than double N.M. Rothschild.
- But empire quickly unravelled: failed Hambro Life merger (April 1984), sold Charterhouse and Kitcat & Aitken stakes, L.F. Rothschild filed for bankruptcy in 1987. Jacob turned to public work — Chairman of National Heritage Memorial Fund 1992-1998.

**Privatisation — N.M. Rothschild's Renaissance:**
- Victor's role as head of Edward Heath's Central Policy Review Staff (1970-73) brought Rothschilds back into direct contact with politicians.
- 1971: Heath government entrusted N.M. Rothschild with sale of Industrial Reorganisation Corporation. 1972: selling bankrupt Rolls Royce Motors — bank took risk of offering shares at £38.4 million when no buyer offered above £35 million.
- February 1982: first true privatisation — Amersham International. Fixed price 142 pence, oversubscribed twenty-three times, shares rose to 193 pence.
- Biggest success: 1985-6 British Gas £6 billion sell-off ("Tell Sid" campaign). Four million investors applied for £5.6 billion of shares. Underwriting commissions exceptionally low: 0.25% on first £400 million down to 0.075% on £2.5 billion.
- October 19, 1987: stock market crash on eve of BP sale. N.M. Rothschild had won contract to sell government's remaining 31.5% BP stake (worth ~£5.7 billion). Bank argued for calling off sale. Chancellor Nigel Lawson insisted. Smith New Court lost more than £8.5 million.
- 1988 alone: eleven privatisations in eight different countries. 1996-7: advised Brazilian government on Companhia Vale do Rio Doce, Zambia on copper, Germany on Deutsche Telekom £6 billion flotation.
- Ferguson's verdict: "N.M. Rothschild's contribution to the privatisation revolution is strongly reminiscent of its earlier role as the leading architect of the modern bond market."

**French Rothschilds — Nationalisation and Rebirth:**
- Robert died end of 1946, Edouard three years later. June 1946: de Rothschild Freres' assets revalued at 250 million francs (~£1 million); did not include stakes in Compagnie du Nord and investments in Rio Tinto, Penarroya, Le Nickel.
- 1953: Societe d'Investissement du Nord created with total capital of 4 billion francs (~£4 million). By 1964: Compagnie du Nord had stakes in 116 different enterprises.
- Georges Pompidou recruited in 1954 to run ailing Transocean subsidiary. Rose to general manager of Banque Rothschild, then became de Gaulle's second Prime Minister (1962-68). His links with rue Laffitte sustained myth of Rothschild power.
- 1967: de Rothschild Freres became Banque Rothschild, a limited-liability company with capital of ~£3.5 million. Family held only 30% of shares; Compagnie du Nord (with ~20,000 shareholders) owned rest.
- When Banque Rothschild absorbed Compagnie du Nord completely in 1978: assets totalled 13 billion francs (~£1.3 billion).

**Mitterrand's Nationalisation (1981):**
- Socialists committed to nationalising "the totality of the bank and financial community." Thirty-nine banks with deposits above 1 billion francs taken into public ownership — including Banque Rothschild.
- Compensation: 450 million francs (£41 million), of which family received a third. Some saw it as blessing in disguise for an ailing firm.
- Guy's bitter response in *Le Monde*: "A Jew under Petain, a pariah under Mitterrand, for me that's enough."
- Within three years of nationalisation, new Paris house established via holding company Paris-Orleans Gestion (set up by David and Eric). David's half-brother Edouard also established small fund management company as subsidiary of Paris-Orleans. New venture launched July 1984 as "PO Banque" — government prohibited use of family name! Share value trebled in first two years; by 1986 managing £273 million.
- 1986: Chirac's "cohabitation" allowed revanche. New firm became Rothschild & Cie Banque (later Rothschild & Cie). Capital of 150 million francs (£19 million), ~15 billion francs (£1.9 billion) under management. One of five leading corporate finance banks in France.

**The Modern Rothschild Group:**
- Evelyn's strategy: recreate international partnership — "get back together as a family." Rothschilds Continuation Holdings AG (Swiss-based) became parent company. Controlled by Rothschild Concordia AG (52.4% stake).
- Structure at time of writing: nineteen principal firms across UK, Channel Islands, France, Switzerland, Netherlands, US, Australia, Hong Kong, Singapore. Offices in Argentina, Bermuda, Brazil, Canada, Chile, Colombia, Czech Republic, Indonesia, Isle of Man, Japan, Luxembourg, Malaysia, Malta, Mexico, New Zealand, Poland, Russia, South Africa, Zimbabwe.
- 1989: London and Paris Rothschilds opened Rothschild GmbH in Frankfurt — return to the city where it all began. Then Rothschild Italia SpA. By 1997: offices in 20+ countries.
- Six family members held thirty-seven board seats across fifteen principal component companies. Evelyn the key figure: chairman of Rothschild Concordia AG, Rothschilds Continuation Holdings AG, Rothschild Bank AG, N.M. Rothschild & Sons Ltd, and Rothschilds Continuation Ltd.
- Succession: David appointed deputy chairman of N.M. Rothschild & Sons January 1992. Evelyn told *Le Monde*: "If something happens to me, there is David. If something happens to him, there is Amschel. Working as a family has always been our trademark."
- Amschel's death in July 1996 — following a meeting to discuss merging the Rothschilds' international asset management operations — was a tragic blow.

**Rothschild Bank Zurich Crisis (1991-2):**
- Initially: 63.5 million Swiss francs of hidden reserves liquidated to cover bad loans of ~100 million Swiss francs (£40 million). Capital was 185 million francs (£74 million).
- September 1992: discovered senior executive Jurg Heer had authorised large illegal loans principally to two German-Canadian property financiers. Losses revised upward to 270 million Swiss francs — more than the firm's entire capital.
- Salvaged with injection of 120.5 million Swiss francs from the wider Rothschild structure. "The Zurich crisis was a reminder of the dangers of a multinational structure with a family firm at its core."
- Compared favorably with Barings in 1995 — bankrupted by rogue dealer Nick Leeson's illegal speculations in Singapore. Rothschilds one of only four elite City firms (of former Acceptance Houses Committee) to retain independence.

**Ferguson's Final Assessment:**
- Rothschilds Continuation Holdings: shareholders' equity (capital, reserves, accumulated profits) of £460 million, total capital resources ~£800 million. Paris-Orleans holding: ~£100 million additional. Far behind HSBC (market cap £55 billion) but comparable to Schroders.
- Capital increase from £12 million (at incorporation in 1970) to £460 million = growth of nearly 400% adjusted for inflation.
- Why the Rothschilds survived when so many rivals did not: the multinational structure. "Throughout the nineteenth century, the single most important reason why the Rothschilds were able to withstand the financial crises, revolutions and wars which swept so many of their competitors into oblivion was that a crisis in one house could be contained and resolved with the assistance of the others."
- The Rothschilds' strategy = defending independence in a world of ever-larger financial giants, not becoming one of those giants. "A bank of brains" rather than of sheer capital.
- Relatively low rate of return on capital in second half of nineteenth century, explained by high ratio of capital to liabilities. "Part of the secret of long-run success in banking is, of course, not to go bust; the Rothschilds' relative risk-aversion is one reason for their financial longevity."
- Family firm advantage: longer time horizon, interests of future generations considered alongside present shareholders. Trust and loyalty that modern corporations cannot replicate.
- Final quote from the 1836 partnership agreement after Nathan's death: "May our children and descendants in the future be guided by the same aim, so that with the constant maintenance of unity the House of Rothschild may blossom and grow into full ripeness... and may they remain as mindful as we of the hallowed precept of our noble ancestor and present to posterity the godly image of united love and work."
- "It is remarkable that, two centuries after Nathan first arrived in England, those words should still have a meaningful resonance."
