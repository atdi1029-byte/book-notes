# Thinking, Fast and Slow — Daniel Kahneman
## Notes

### Introduction (pp. 1-20)

**Origins of the Book**
- Kahneman's aim: enrich watercooler vocabulary for discussing judgments and choices of others
- "Intelligent gossip" as a motive for self-criticism — more powerful than New Year's resolutions
- Systematic errors = biases, recur predictably in particular circumstances
- Diagnostic labels (like "halo effect") make biases easier to anticipate, recognize, and understand
- Most impressions and thoughts arise in consciousness without knowing how they got there — mental work produces impressions, intuitions, and decisions in silence

**The Kahneman-Tversky Partnership**
- 1969: Kahneman invited Amos Tversky to speak at Hebrew University seminar on intuitive statistics
- Tversky was 32, Kahneman 35; Tversky considered the most intelligent person many had ever met — brilliant, voluble, charismatic, perfect memory for jokes
- Research question from U of Michigan: Are people good intuitive statisticians? (Like how children are intuitive grammarians)
- Tversky's answer: qualified yes. After debate, they concluded: qualified no.
- Met at Cafe Rimon in Jerusalem to plan study — concluded their own statistical intuitions were deficient
- Found expert statisticians (including textbook authors) at Society of Mathematical Psychology meeting also greatly exaggerated replicability of small-sample results
- "Even statisticians were not good intuitive statisticians"
- Working together was fun — Amos was very funny, made Kahneman funny too, spent hours in continuous amusement while doing solid work
- Both critical and argumentative but never rejected each other's ideas out of hand
- Amos: more logical thinker, unfailing sense of direction. Kahneman: more intuitive, rooted in psychology of perception
- Collaboration lasted 14 years, was the focus of their lives and their best work
- Their shared mind was superior to their individual minds

**Key Experiments & Heuristics**
- Steve the librarian vs. farmer: personality description ("shy, withdrawn, meek and tidy") triggers representativeness judgment — people ignore base rates (20+ male farmers per male librarian in US)
- Representativeness heuristic: judging probability by resemblance rather than statistical facts → predictable biases
- Availability heuristic: judging frequency/probability by ease of memory retrieval
  - Letter K: people think K more likely as first letter than third letter (easier to recall), but K actually appears more in third position
  - Divorce rate among professors: triggered memory search for divorced professors they knew
  - Politicians vs. physicians in adultery: availability of media coverage distorts impression
- Published "Judgment Under Uncertainty: Heuristics and Biases" in Science (1974) — 300+ scholarly citations by 2010, one of most cited works in social science
- Challenged two assumptions: (1) people are generally rational, (2) emotions explain departures from rationality
- They traced errors to the design of cognitive machinery, not corruption by emotion

**Where We Are Now — Expert Intuition & Heuristic Intuition**
- Now understand both marvels AND flaws of intuitive thought
- Expert intuitions (firefighter sensing danger, chess master seeing "White mates in three") arise from prolonged practice
- Herbert Simon on expert intuition: "The situation has provided a cue; this cue has given the expert access to information stored in memory, and the information provides the answer. Intuition is nothing more and nothing less than recognition."
- Valid intuitions = experts recognizing familiar elements in new situations
- BUT not all professional intuitions arise from true expertise
  - CIO of large firm invested tens of millions in Ford stock after attending an auto show — answered easy question (do I like Ford cars?) instead of hard question (is Ford stock underpriced?)
  - Affect heuristic: judgments guided directly by feelings of liking/disliking
- When faced with hard question, intuitive thought often substitutes an easier related question — usually without noticing
- "Fast thinking" = both expert intuition AND heuristic shortcuts + automatic perception/memory
- "Slow thinking" = deliberate, effortful, controlled thinking (activated when intuitive solution fails or isn't available)

**The System 1 / System 2 Framework**
- Two agents metaphor: System 1 (fast, automatic, intuitive) and System 2 (slow, effortful, deliberate)
- System 1 is more influential than experience tells you — secret author of many choices and judgments
- Book is about workings of System 1 and mutual influences between System 1 and System 2

**Book Structure (5 Parts)**
- Part I: Two Systems — basic elements of two-systems approach, how associative memory (System 1's core) constructs coherent interpretation of the world
- Part II: Heuristics and Biases — why statistical thinking is so difficult; we think associatively, metaphorically, causally — statistics requires thinking about many things at once (not System 1's design)
- Part III: Overconfidence — excessive confidence in what we believe, inability to acknowledge ignorance and uncertainty; overestimate understanding of the world, underestimate role of chance; fed by illusory certainty of hindsight; influenced by Nassim Taleb's Black Swan
- Part IV: Choices — conversation with economics on rationality assumption; prospect theory; framing effects; features of System 1 challenge rationality assumption
- Part V: Two Selves — experiencing self vs. remembering self; they don't have same interests; worse experience that's longer can leave better memory (if it ends well); implications for well-being measurement and policy
- Concluding chapter: implications of three distinctions (experiencing/remembering selves, classical/behavioral economics agents, System 1/System 2)
- Two appendices: original Judgment Under Uncertainty article (Science, 1974) and Prospect Theory article (1984)

### Chapter 1: The Characters of the Story (pp. 21-35)

**Fast vs. Slow Thinking Demonstrated**
- Angry woman's face: instantly know she's angry, sense she's about to say unkind words — automatic, effortless, System 1
- 17 x 24: know it's multiplication, know 12,609 and 123 are implausible, but precise answer doesn't come automatically — requires deliberate effort, System 2
- Computing 17 x 24: muscles tense, blood pressure rises, heart rate increases, pupils dilate — body responds to mental effort
- Answer is 408

**System 1 and System 2 Defined**
- Terms from psychologists Keith Stanovich and Richard West
- System 1: operates automatically and quickly, little or no effort, no sense of voluntary control
- System 2: allocates attention to effortful mental activities including complex computations; associated with subjective experience of agency, choice, and concentration
- System 2 believes itself to be the hero, but System 1 is the true protagonist — effortlessly originating impressions and feelings that are the main sources of System 2's beliefs and choices

**System 1 Activities** (automatic, in rough order of complexity):
- Detect distance, orient to sounds, complete "bread and..." 
- Make disgust face, detect hostility in a voice, answer 2+2
- Read words on billboards, drive on empty road
- Find strong chess move (if expert), understand simple sentences
- Recognize occupational stereotypes
- Includes innate skills (shared with animals) AND learned skills through prolonged practice
- Some are completely involuntary (you CANNOT prevent reading a word in a language you know)

**System 2 Activities** (require attention, disrupted when drawn away):
- Brace for starter gun, focus on specific person in crowd
- Search memory, maintain faster walking speed than natural
- Count occurrences of a letter, tell someone your phone number
- Park in narrow space, compare washing machines, fill out tax form
- Check validity of complex logical argument

**The Invisible Gorilla & Attention Blindness**
- Christopher Chabris and Daniel Simons: basketball passing video, half of viewers miss woman in gorilla suit crossing court
- Shows two facts: we can be blind to the obvious, AND we are blind to our blindness
- Intense focus on one task makes people effectively blind to normally attention-grabbing stimuli

**Plot Synopsis — How the Two Systems Interact**
- Both active whenever we're awake; System 1 runs automatically, System 2 in comfortable low-effort mode
- System 1 continuously generates suggestions for System 2: impressions, intuitions, intentions, feelings
- If endorsed by System 2, impressions become beliefs, impulses become voluntary actions
- Usually System 2 adopts System 1's suggestions with little or no modification
- When System 1 runs into difficulty, it calls on System 2 for detailed processing
- System 2 mobilized when surprised (event violates System 1's model of the world)
- Division of labor is highly efficient — System 1 is generally very good at what it does
- System 1 has biases: sometimes answers easier questions than asked, little understanding of logic and statistics, cannot be turned off

**Conflict Between Systems — Stroop Effect**
- Figure 2 exercise: identifying upper/lowercase vs. left/right position of words like "LEFT" "RIGHT" — second column creates conflict between what you read and what you must report
- System 2 is in charge of self-control — overcoming impulses of System 1

**Illusions — Visual and Cognitive**
- Muller-Lyer illusion: lines with fins look different lengths but are identical
- After learning the illusion, you KNOW lines are equal but still SEE them as different — cannot prevent System 1 from doing its thing
- Cognitive illusions: psychotherapy teacher's story about charming psychopath patient — sympathy you feel for such a patient is a cognitive illusion, like the fins on the parallel lines
- "Can cognitive illusions be overcome?" — not encouraging. System 1 cannot be turned off at will
- Best we can do: learn to recognize situations where mistakes are likely, try harder to avoid them when stakes are high
- "It is easier to recognize other people's mistakes than our own"

**Useful Fictions**
- System 1 and System 2 are fictitious characters — not systems in standard sense, no one brain part for either
- Justified because sentences about agents (System 2 calculates) are understood more easily than descriptions of properties
- The mind has a special aptitude for constructing stories about active agents with personalities
- Why "System 1/2" not "automatic/effortful system"? Shorter labels take less working memory, leaving more room for thinking

### Chapter 2: Attention and Effort (pp. 36-46)

**The Add-1 and Add-3 Tasks**
- Add-1: hear 4 digits, report each incremented by 1 (5294 → 6305), keeping rhythm with metronome
- Few people can cope with more than 4 digits; Add-3 is much harder
- The pupil of the eye is a faithful record of how hard you worked

**Kahneman's Pupillometry Research**
- At University of Michigan, found article by psychologist Eckhard Hess in Scientific American about pupil as window to soul
- Hess: pupils dilate for attractive pictures, constrict for others; belladonna was used as cosmetic pupil-dilator; bazaar shoppers wear dark glasses to hide interest level
- Hess found pupils are sensitive indicators of mental effort — dilate more for harder multiplication problems
- Kahneman and graduate student Jackson Beatty developed experimental setup: chin-and-forehead rest, camera, infrared flash every second to photograph pupils
- Pupil size varies second by second, reflecting task demands — inverted-V pattern: builds with each digit, peaks during transformation, relaxes as you unload short-term memory
- Add-1 with 4 digits: larger dilation than holding 7 digits for recall
- Add-3: pupil dilates ~50% of original area, heart rate increases ~7 beats/min — near human capacity ceiling
- When exposed to more digits than could handle: pupils STOPPED dilating or actually SHRANK — the mental equivalent of giving up

**"Window to Your Soul"**
- Projected pupil image (~1 foot diameter) on corridor screen — could tell from the corridor when a participant gave up
- Would ask "Why did you stop working just now?" — astonishing visitors

**Key Insight: Mental Life as Walking Pace**
- Mundane conversation: pupil barely dilates — no more effort than retaining 2-3 digits
- Eureka moment: the tasks they'd chosen for study were exceptionally effortful
- "Mental life is normally conducted at the pace of a comfortable walk, sometimes interrupted by episodes of jogging and on rare occasions by a frantic sprint"
- Add-1 and Add-3 are sprints; casual chatting is a stroll

**Attention Blindness During Mental Effort**
- During Add-1 task, subjects also asked to detect letter K flashed on screen
- At beginning/end of task (low effort): almost never missed K
- At peak effort: missed target almost half the time, despite staring at it with wide-open eyes
- Failures of detection followed same inverted-V pattern as pupil dilation

**The Electricity Meter Analogy**
- Pupils = electricity meter for mental energy
- Like electricity: usage depends on what you choose to do; each appliance draws what it needs, no more
- Unlike circuit breaker (all-or-nothing shutdown), mental overload response is selective — System 2 protects most important activity, allocates spare capacity second by second

**Law of Least Effort**
- As you become skilled in a task, demand for energy diminishes — brain studies show fewer regions involved
- Highly intelligent individuals need less effort for same problems (smaller pupils, less brain activity)
- "Law of least effort" applies to cognitive and physical exertion alike
- If several ways exist to achieve same goal, people gravitate to least demanding course
- In economy of action, effort is a cost; laziness is built deep into our nature

**What Requires System 2 Effort?**
- Maintaining simultaneously in memory several ideas requiring separate actions combined by a rule
- System 2 is the ONLY one that can follow rules, compare objects on multiple attributes, make deliberate choices
- System 1 detects simple relations but can't deal with multiple topics at once or use purely statistical information
- Crucial System 2 capability: "task sets" — programming memory to obey instruction that overrides habitual responses
- Task switching is effortful, especially under time pressure
- Working memory tests (switching between two tasks) correlate with general intelligence and predict real-world performance (air traffic controllers, Israeli Air Force pilots)
- Time pressure compounds effort — memory decays, forcing you to refresh/rehearse before information is lost ("like a juggler with several balls in the air")

### Chapter 3: The Lazy Controller (pp. 47-60)

**Walking and Thinking**
- Kahneman's Berkeley hill walks: 17 min/mile = stroll, can think and work easily
- At stroll pace: mild physical arousal may spill over into greater mental alertness
- System 2 also has a natural speed — monitoring environment, random thoughts, little strain
- Walking + thinking work fine together UNTIL the extremes compete for limited System 2 resources
- Ask someone computing 23 x 78 while walking: they will stop in their tracks
- Accelerating past strolling speed → sharp deterioration in ability to think coherently
- Self-control and deliberate thought apparently draw on the same limited budget of effort

**Flow (Csikszentmihalyi)**
- Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi studied "flow" — effortless concentration so deep you lose sense of time and self
- Flow separates two forms of effort: concentration on task vs. deliberate control of attention
- In flow, maintaining focused attention requires no exertion of self-control, freeing resources for the task
- Many activities induce flow: painting, racing motorcycles, writing, competitive chess

**The Busy and Depleted System 2**
- Self-control and cognitive work are both forms of mental work
- Cognitive load + temptation: if asked to hold 7 digits and offered chocolate cake vs. fruit salad, more likely to choose cake — System 1 has more influence when System 2 is busy
- Cognitively busy people: more selfish choices, sexist language, superficial social judgments
- Self-control impaired at night for morning people, and vice versa

**Ego Depletion (Roy Baumeister)**
- All variants of voluntary effort (cognitive, emotional, physical) draw on a shared pool of mental energy
- Ego depletion: after forcing yourself through one task, you're less willing/able to exert self-control on the next
- Examples: stifling emotional reaction to film → poor physical stamina; resisting cookies/radishes → giving up earlier on difficult cognitive task
- Situations that deplete self-control: avoiding thought of white bears, inhibiting emotional response, making conflicted choices, trying to impress others, responding kindly to bad behavior
- Signs of depletion: deviating from diet, overspending, reacting aggressively, performing poorly on cognitive tasks
- Unlike cognitive load (which can be overcome with strong incentive), ego depletion is partly a loss of motivation

**Glucose and Mental Energy**
- Baumeister's surprising finding: mental energy is more than metaphor
- Nervous system consumes more glucose than most body parts; effortful mental activity is especially expensive
- Blood glucose drops during difficult cognitive reasoning or self-control tasks
- Effects of ego depletion can be undone by ingesting glucose
- Experiment: ego-depleted volunteers who drank glucose-sweetened lemonade were NOT depleted on next task; those who drank Splenda-sweetened version showed expected depletion

**Israeli Parole Judges Study**
- Published in Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences
- 8 parole judges in Israel, cases in random order, ~6 min per case, default = denial (only 35% approved)
- After each meal break: approval rate spikes to ~65%, then drops steadily to near zero before next meal
- Tired and hungry judges fall back on easier default of denying parole
- Both fatigue and hunger play a role

**The Lazy System 2**
- One of System 2's main functions: monitor and control thoughts/actions suggested by System 1
- The bat-and-ball problem: bat + ball cost $1.10, bat costs $1 more than ball, what does ball cost?
  - Intuitive answer: 10 cents (wrong — that would make total $1.20)
  - Correct answer: 5 cents
  - More than 50% of Harvard, MIT, Princeton students gave wrong answer
  - At less selective universities: over 80% failed
- Failure to check is remarkable because cost of checking is so low (few seconds of mental work)
- "People who say 10 cents appear to be ardent followers of the law of least effort"
- Flowers syllogism: "All roses are flowers / Some flowers fade quickly / Therefore some roses fade quickly" — sounds right but is logically invalid. Most people don't check.
- Michigan murder rate: knowing Detroit is in Michigan is not the same as retrieving that fact when it's needed — knowledge is not all-or-none
- When people believe a conclusion is true, they believe arguments that support it even when those arguments are unsound

**Intelligence, Control, Rationality**
- Walter Mischel's marshmallow experiment: 4-year-olds choosing 1 cookie now vs. 2 cookies in 15 minutes
  - Half managed to wait, mainly by keeping attention away from reward
  - 10-15 years later: resisters had higher executive control, higher intelligence, less likely to take drugs
- University of Oregon research: training attention control in children (ages 4-6) with computer games improved nonverbal intelligence scores, lasting several months
- Shane Frederick's Cognitive Reflection Test (CRT): bat-and-ball + 2 other questions designed to invite compelling but wrong intuitive answers
  - Low scorers: impulsive, impatient, keen on immediate gratification (63% prefer $3,400 now vs $3,800 next month)
  - High scorers: only 37% prefer immediate smaller amount
  - Characters of the "psychodrama": System 1 is impulsive and intuitive; System 2 is capable of reasoning but lazy
- Keith Stanovich and Richard West: originally introduced System 1/System 2 terms (now prefer Type 1/Type 2)
  - Spent decades studying individual differences in susceptibility to biases
  - Key insight: rationality should be distinguished from intelligence
  - Superficial or "lazy" thinking is a flaw in the reflective mind — a failure of rationality, not intelligence
  - Bat-and-ball question is a better predictor of cognitive errors than IQ tests

### Chapter 4: The Associative Machine (pp. 61-70)

**Bananas Vomit — Associative Activation**
- Reading "Bananas Vomit" triggers cascade: unpleasant images, disgust face, slight recoil, heart rate increase, sweat glands activated, temporary aversion to bananas
- Your body reacted in attenuated version of actual vomiting — cognition is embodied; you think with your body, not only your brain
- The response pattern is called "associatively coherent" — self-reinforcing pattern of cognitive, emotional, and physical responses
- System 1 made as much sense as possible: linked two words in a causal story, evaluated possible threat, prepared for future developments, evaluated how surprising the event was

**The Association of Ideas — From Hume to Modern Networks**
- David Hume (1748, An Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding): three principles of association — resemblance, contiguity in time/place, causality
- Modern view: ideas as nodes in vast associative memory network, linked by causes→effects, things→properties, things→categories
- Not sequential conscious ideas but simultaneous activation — a great deal happens at once
- Most associative thinking is silent, hidden from conscious selves
- "You know far less about yourself than you feel you do"

**Priming Effects**
- Priming effect: exposure to a word causes immediate changes in ease of evoking related words
- EAT primes SOUP (SO_P completed as SOUP not SOAP); WASH primes SOAP
- Priming spreads like ripples on a pond through network of associated ideas
- Not limited to concepts/words — actions and emotions can be primed too

**The Florida Effect (John Bargh)**
- NYU students assembled sentences from word sets containing elderly-associated words (Florida, forgetful, bald, gray, wrinkle)
- After task, walking speed down hallway was significantly slower — primed by elderly theme
- None of the students noticed the common theme or believed they were influenced
- The ideomotor effect: ideas prime actions associated with them
- Works in reverse too: German study had students walk at 30 steps/min (one-third normal pace) for 5 minutes → much quicker to recognize words like "forgetful," "old," "lonely"
- Reciprocal priming: primed to think of old age → tend to act old; acting old → reinforces thoughts of old age

**Embodied Cognition**
- Pencil-in-mouth experiment: holding pencil crosswise (forces smile) → found Far Side cartoons funnier than those holding pencil lip-to-lip (forces frown)
- Squeezing eyebrows together (frown) → enhanced emotional response to upsetting pictures
- Head nodding → more likely to accept message heard; head shaking → more likely to reject it
- "Act calm and kind regardless of how you feel" — you are likely to actually feel calm and kind

**Money Priming (Kathleen Vohs)**
- Subtle money reminders (Monopoly money on table, screensaver of floating dollar bills) change behavior
- Money-primed people: more independent, persevered almost twice as long on difficult problem before asking for help
- Also more selfish: less willing to help confused student, picked up fewer dropped pencils, sat farther apart from others (118 vs 80 cm), preferred being alone
- Theme: money primes individualism — reluctance to be involved with/depend on/accept demands from others
- "Living in a culture that surrounds us with reminders of money may shape our behavior and attitudes in ways we do not know about and of which we may not be proud"
- Death primes increase appeal of authoritarian ideas; shame primes increase desire for cleaning products ("Lady Macbeth effect")

**Honesty Box Experiment**
- British university office kitchen: "honesty box" for tea/coffee payments
- Banner poster alternated weekly: eyes staring at observer vs. flowers
- No one noticed or commented on the posters
- "Eye weeks" contributions were almost THREE TIMES higher than "flower weeks"
- Purely symbolic reminder of being watched improved behavior without any awareness

### Chapter 5: Cognitive Ease (pp. 71-82)

**The Cognitive Ease Dial**
- System 1 runs continuous assessments: Is there a threat? Anything new? More effort needed?
- One dial measures cognitive ease, range from "Easy" to "Strained"
- Easy = things going well, no threats, no need to redirect attention or mobilize effort
- Strained = a problem exists, requiring increased mobilization of System 2
- Inputs to ease: repeated experience, clear display, primed idea, good mood
- Outputs of ease: feels familiar, feels true, feels good, feels effortless
- The various causes are interchangeable — you may not know what specifically makes something feel easy or strained

**Cognitive Ease → Casual, Superficial Thinking**
- In a state of cognitive ease: more likely to believe what you see/hear, trust intuitions, feel situation is familiar
- Also: more casual and superficial in thinking
- When strained: more vigilant, suspicious, invest more effort, make fewer errors, but also less intuitive and less creative

**Illusions of Remembering**
- Larry Jacoby's experiment: "Becoming Famous Overnight" — previously seen non-famous names later judged as famous
- Familiarity is an illusion based on cognitive ease: you see the name more clearly because you've seen it before → this ease gives the impression of familiarity → you attribute familiarity to fame
- The various ways of inducing cognitive ease are interchangeable

**Illusions of Truth**
- Frequent repetition → cognitive ease → feeling of truth
- "A chicken has four legs" is harder to reject than "A chicken has three legs" because many associations with "four legs" are activated
- You don't have to repeat the entire statement — partial familiarity suffices ("body temperature of a chicken" repeatedly → more likely to accept "body temperature of a chicken is 144 degrees")
- Authoritarian institutions and marketers have always known this; familiarity is not easily distinguished from truth

**How to Write a Persuasive Message**
- Maximize legibility: bold font > light font ("Adolf Hitler was born in 1892" in bold believed more than same claim in light font — both wrong)
- High-quality paper, maximize contrast between characters and background
- Bright blue or red text > green, yellow, pale blue
- Use simple language — pretentious vocabulary signals poor intelligence and low credibility (Danny Oppenheimer's paper: "Consequences of Erudite Vernacular Utilized Irrespective of Necessity")
- Make it memorable — rhyming aphorisms judged more insightful than non-rhyming equivalents ("Woes unite foes" > "Woes unite enemies")
- Choose sources with easy-to-pronounce names — Turkish company reports from "Artan" given more weight than from "Taahhut"

**Strain and Effort**
- Cognitive strain, from any source, mobilizes System 2 → more engaged and analytic mode
- CRT in washed-out gray font: only 35% made at least one mistake vs. 90% in normal font
- Counterintuitive: performance was BETTER with the bad font because it mobilized System 2

**The Pleasure of Cognitive Ease**
- "Mind at Ease Puts a Smile on the Face" — briefly shown pictures that were subtly made easier to recognize → slight smile measured in facial muscles
- Companies with pronounceable names outperform those with clunky names in first week after stock issuance
- Stocks with pronounceable tickers (KAR, LUNMOO) outperform tongue-twisting ones (PXG, RDO) and retain advantage over time
- Swiss study: investors believe stocks with fluent names (Emmi, Swissfirst) will earn higher returns

**Mere Exposure Effect (Robert Zajonc)**
- Repetition of an arbitrary stimulus → mild affection for it
- Turkish-sounding words placed in student newspapers at varying frequencies → more frequently presented words rated much more favorably
- Effect confirmed with Chinese ideographs, faces, randomly shaped polygons
- Does not depend on conscious experience — works even when stimuli shown too quickly for conscious awareness; effect is actually STRONGER for stimuli never consciously seen
- Zajonc's evolutionary argument: in a dangerous world, cautious reaction to novelty is adaptive; repeated exposure without bad outcome = safety signal = good
- Chicken eggs exposed to different tones; after hatching, chicks emitted fewer distress calls to tone heard in shell

**Ease, Mood, and Intuition**
- Sarnoff Mednick's Remote Association Test (RAT): find word linking three words (cottage/Swiss/cake → cheese; dive/light/rocket → sky)
- People's guesses are much more accurate than chance — faint signal from associative machine "knows" coherence before solution is retrieved
- Good mood → doubled intuition accuracy; unhappy subjects → no better than random
- Good mood, intuition, creativity, gullibility, reliance on System 1 cluster together
- Bad mood, vigilance, suspicion, analytic approach, increased effort cluster together
- Happy mood = signal that environment is safe → let guard down; bad mood = signal of threat → vigilance required
- Coherent word triads produce slight smile; feeling of cognitive ease that comes with coherence is mildly pleasurable in itself
- Proof of causation: when participants told music in their earphones "influences emotional reactions," the effect of coherence on intuition was eliminated — the good feeling was attributed to music instead

### Chapter 6: Norms, Surprises, and Causes (pp. 83-92)

**Assessing Normality**
- System 1 maintains and updates model of your personal world — what is normal in it
- Constructed by associations linking circumstances, events, actions, outcomes that co-occur regularly
- Two varieties of surprise: active (expecting specific event that doesn't happen) and passive (not expecting something, but not surprised when it happens — it's normal)
- Single incident can make recurrence less surprising: meeting psychologist Jon at Great Barrier Reef resort → 2 weeks later, Jon sits next to Kahneman at London theater → more remarkable coincidence but LESS surprising because first meeting changed what's "normal" about Jon
- Burning car on road NY→Princeton → next week another burning car at same spot → less surprised, spot became "the place where cars catch fire"

**Norm Theory (Kahneman & Dale Miller)**
- Restaurant scenario: first guest winces at soup → alters normality of many events (same guest startled by waiter = unsurprising; another guest winces at soup = unsurprising)
- Second event retrieves first from memory; they're interpreted together, creating coherent explanation
- The Moses illusion: "How many animals of each kind did Moses take into the ark?" — most people miss it (Moses didn't; Noah did)
  - "Moses" is not abnormal in a biblical context; has same vowel sound and syllable count as "Noah"
  - Replace Moses with George W. Bush → everyone catches it (no cognitive ease with Bush + ark)

**Violations of Normality — Brain Response**
- Brain detects violations with astonishing speed: "Earth revolves around the trouble every year" — distinctive brain pattern within 0.2 seconds of odd word
- Same brain response to male voice saying "I am pregnant" or upper-class voice saying "I have a large tattoo on my back"
- System 1 has access to norms of categories — typical/average size, range of variability
- "The large mouse climbed over the trunk of the very small elephant" → you didn't picture a mouse larger than an elephant, but each separately adjusted (shoe-sized mouse, sofa-sized elephant)

**Seeing Causes and Intentions**
- "Fred's parents arrived late. The caterers were expected soon. Fred was angry." → You instantly know Fred is angry because his parents were late, not because of the caterers
- System 1 constructs causal stories automatically; System 2 is offered the interpretation and accepts it
- Nassim Taleb's Black Swan example: bond prices rose on Saddam Hussein's capture → "HUSSEIN CAPTURE MAY NOT CURB TERRORISM" → hour later, bonds fell → "HUSSEIN CAPTURE BOOSTS ALLURE OF RISKY ASSETS" — contradictory outcomes both "explained" by same event
- Jane's missing wallet in New York's crowded streets → "pickpocket" more strongly associated than "sights" — System 1 constructs causal story automatically

**Physical vs. Intentional Causality**
- Albert Michotte (1945): we SEE causality, not infer it — moving square contacts another, second begins to move → powerful "illusion of causality" even when observers know there's no real contact
- 6-month-old infants see cause-effect sequences; surprise when sequence is altered → impressions of causality are innate, products of System 1
- Fritz Heider and Mary-Ann Simmel (1944): film of triangles and circle moving around house-shape → viewers see bullying, fear, joining forces, explosive finale; only people with autism don't perceive intentions
- Infants under 1 year identify bullies and victims, expect pursuers to follow direct paths
- Physical causality vs. intentional causality (freely willed action) are quite separate experiences
- Paul Bloom (The Atlantic, 2005): our inborn readiness to separate physical and intentional causality explains near-universality of religious beliefs — perceiving "soulless bodies and bodiless souls"

**Causal vs. Statistical Thinking**
- Prominence of causal intuitions is a recurrent theme — people prone to apply causal thinking inappropriately to situations requiring statistical reasoning
- Statistical thinking derives conclusions from properties of categories and ensembles; System 1 cannot do this
- System 2 can learn statistical thinking, but few people receive the training

### Chapter 7: A Machine for Jumping to Conclusions (pp. 93-104)

**Jumping to Conclusions is Efficient**
- Danny Kaye: "Her favorite position is beside herself, and her favorite sport is jumping to conclusions"
- Efficient if conclusions likely correct, costs of occasional mistake acceptable, saves time and effort
- Risky when situation is unfamiliar, stakes are high, no time to collect more information
- Preventable by deliberate intervention of System 2

**Neglect of Ambiguity and Suppression of Doubt**
- Figure 6: A B C / ANN APPROACHED THE BANK / 12 13 14 — same middle shape read as "B" in letter context and "13" in number context
- "Ann approached the bank" — you imagined a woman walking toward a building with tellers; if preceded by "They were floating gently down the river," you'd imagine a riverbank
- System 1 generates likely context on its own, bets on interpretation using recent events and current context
- System 1 does not keep track of alternatives it rejects; only one interpretation comes to mind
- "Conscious doubt is not in the repertoire of System 1" — maintaining incompatible interpretations requires mental effort = System 2

**A Bias to Believe and Confirm**
- Daniel Gilbert (Stumbling to Happiness): belief/unbelief theory traced to Spinoza
- Understanding a statement begins with attempt to believe it — you first know what it means if true; only then can you decide to unbelieve
- Initial belief is automatic System 1 operation; unbelieving requires System 2
- Gilbert's experiment: nonsensical assertions ("a dinca is a flame") followed by "true"/"false" label
  - When System 2 was disrupted (holding digits in memory), people couldn't "unbelieve" false sentences
  - In later memory test, depleted participants thought many false sentences were true
- "When System 2 is otherwise engaged, we will believe almost anything"
- System 1 is gullible and biased to believe; System 2 is in charge of doubting and unbelieving, but is sometimes busy and often lazy
- People more susceptible to empty persuasive messages (commercials) when tired and depleted

**Confirmation Bias**
- "Is Sam friendly?" → different instances come to mind than "Is Sam unfriendly?"
- Positive test strategy: people (and scientists) seek data compatible with beliefs they currently hold, not disconfirming data
- Asking about probability of tsunami hitting California → tsunami images flood mind → overestimate probability

**Exaggerated Emotional Coherence (Halo Effect)**
- Tendency to like (or dislike) everything about a person, including things you haven't observed
- You meet Joan, find her personable → later asked about her generosity → you'll assume she's generous (no evidence)
- Solomon Asch: Alan (intelligent—industrious—impulsive—critical—stubborn—envious) viewed much more favorably than Ben (same traits reversed)
- Initial traits change the meaning of later traits: stubbornness in an intelligent person = justified determination; intelligence in an envious person = dangerous
- The halo effect suppresses ambiguity, like the word "bank"

**Kahneman's Essay Grading Discovery**
- Grading student booklets sequentially: first essay colored evaluation of all subsequent essays
- If first essay was strong → gave benefit of doubt on later ambiguous answers
- Solution: grade all students' answers to question 1, then question 2, etc.
- After switching: less confident in grades, discovered uncomfortable inconsistency, grades varied more within same student
- The consistency he'd enjoyed earlier was spurious — System 2 was lazily accepting System 1's coherence

**Decorrelate Errors**
- Principle: to derive most useful information from multiple sources, make sources independent of each other
- Pennies in jar estimate: individuals poor, but average of many independent estimates remarkably accurate (Wisdom of Crowds, James Surowiecki)
- Error reduction works only when observations are independent and errors uncorrelated
- If observers share a bias or influence each other, aggregation won't help
- Practical application: before discussing an issue in a meeting, all members should write brief independent summaries of their position — prevents early speakers from anchoring the group

**WYSIATI — What You See Is All There Is**
- Essential design feature of associative machine: it represents only activated ideas; unactivated information might as well not exist
- System 1 excels at constructing best possible story from currently activated ideas
- "The measure of success for System 1 is the coherence of the story it manages to create. The amount and quality of the data on which the story is based are largely irrelevant"
- "Will Mindik be a good leader? She is intelligent and strong..." → You quickly answered yes, but what if the next adjectives were corrupt and cruel?
- You didn't start with "What would I need to know?" — System 1 jumped to a conclusion
- Amos's legal case study: participants given one-sided evidence were fully aware of the setup and could have generated the other side's argument; nevertheless, one-sided presentation had very pronounced effect; those who saw one side were MORE confident than those who saw both sides
- "It is the consistency of the information that matters for a good story, not its completeness"
- WYSIATI explains: overconfidence, framing effects ("90% survival" > "10% mortality"), base-rate neglect (Steve the librarian)

### Chapter 8: How Judgments Happen (pp. 105-112)

**Basic Assessments**
- System 1 shaped by evolution for continuous assessment of survival problems: threat level, opportunities, normality, approach/avoid
- Good mood and cognitive ease = assessments of safety and familiarity
- Discriminating friend from foe at a glance: specialized capability, evolved for survival

**Alex Todorov's Face Study**
- Princeton colleague explored biological roots of rapid judgments about strangers
- We evaluate in a single glance: how dominant (threatening) and how trustworthy (friendly or hostile)
- Dominance cues: "strong" square chin; intention cues: facial expression (smile/frown)
- Todorov showed campaign portraits of politicians for as little as 1/10 of a second
- Students rated faces on competence — in about 70% of races (senator, congressman, governor), the winner was the one rated higher on competence
- Result confirmed in Finland, England, Australia, Germany, Mexico
- Competence ratings = combination of strength + trustworthiness (strong chin + slight confident smile)
- Competence ratings were far more predictive of voting outcomes than likability ratings
- No evidence facial features predict actual performance — but we're biologically predisposed to reject candidates who lack attributes we value
- Effect of facial competence on voting is ~3x larger for information-poor/TV-prone voters vs. better-informed ones

**Sets and Prototypes**
- System 1 can instantly estimate the average length of an array of lines but NOT the total length — total requires System 2 (count x average)
- System 1 represents categories by prototypes or typical exemplars; deals well with averages but poorly with sums
- The number of instances tends to be ignored in judgments of "sum-like variables"
- Exxon Valdez oil spill: willingness to pay for nets to save 2,000 vs 20,000 vs 200,000 birds → $80, $78, $88 — almost no difference. People reacted to the prototype (helpless bird drowning in oil), not the quantity

**Intensity Matching**
- System 1 can match intensities across diverse dimensions: if crimes were colors, murder = deep red, theft = pale pink
- "Julie read fluently when she was four" → "How tall is a man who is as tall as Julie was precocious?" → most people answer ~6'1" (fairly remarkable but not extraordinary)
- Can also match to income level, crime severity, GPA — all done effortlessly
- This mode of prediction by matching is statistically wrong but perfectly natural for System 1

**The Mental Shotgun**
- System 1 carries out many computations at any one time — some routine (3D visual representation, threat assessment), some triggered by intention
- "Mental shotgun": impossible to aim at a single point — System 1 computes more than intended
- VOTE-NOTE rhyme detected slower than VOTE-GOAT because spelling mismatch interfered even though only sound comparison was requested
- "Some roads are snakes" — literally false but takes longer to reject because it's metaphorically true; the metaphorical computation was triggered automatically

### Chapter 9: Answering an Easier Question (pp. 113-125)

**Substitution**
- You are rarely stumped — you have intuitive feelings and opinions about almost everything
- If a satisfactory answer to a hard question (target question) isn't found quickly, System 1 will find a related easier question (heuristic question) and answer that instead
- Technical definition: a heuristic is a simple procedure that helps find adequate, though often imperfect, answers to difficult questions (from same root as "eureka")
- George Polya's How to Solve It: "If you can't solve a problem, find an easier problem you can solve" — but Polya's heuristics are deliberate (System 2); the heuristics in this chapter are automatic consequences of the mental shotgun

**Table of Substitutions (Target → Heuristic)**
- "How much would you contribute to save endangered species?" → "How much emotion do I feel thinking of dying dolphins?"
- "How happy are you with your life?" → "What is my mood right now?"
- "How popular is the president right now?" → "How popular will the president be six months from now?"
- "How should financial advisers who prey on elderly be punished?" → "How much anger when I think of financial predators?"
- "How far will this woman go in politics?" → "Does she look like a political winner?"

**The 3-D Heuristic**
- Three men in a corridor drawn in perspective: figure on right appears larger because 3-D interpretation automatically substitutes for 2-D judgment
- Figures are actually the same size on the page — but you can't help seeing the "distant" one as larger
- Illustrates: a judgment based on substitution will inevitably be biased in predictable ways

**The Mood Heuristic for Happiness**
- German students asked: "How happy are you?" then "How many dates did you have last month?"
- In this order: correlation between dates and happiness was about zero — dating wasn't what came to mind
- Reverse order (dates question first, then happiness): correlation was extremely high
- Being asked about dates created an emotional reaction; when happiness question came up next, that emotion was still active → substituted romantic satisfaction for life satisfaction
- Same pattern found with questions about finances, parental relations — any emotionally significant question that immediately precedes the happiness question dominates the answer
- Present state of mind looms very large when people evaluate their happiness: WYSIATI

**The Affect Heuristic (Paul Slovic)**
- People let likes and dislikes determine their beliefs about the world
- If you like the current health policy → believe its benefits are substantial and costs manageable
- If you dislike nuclear power → believe its risks are high and benefits negligible
- Your emotional attitude drives your beliefs about benefits AND risks, even though they're logically independent
- Learning that risk is lower than you thought → your view of benefits also improves (even though nothing was said about benefits)
- System 2 is "more of an apologist for the emotions of System 1 than a critic" — an endorser rather than an enforcer
- Its search for information is mostly constrained to information consistent with existing beliefs

**Comprehensive List of System 1 Characteristics**
- Generates impressions, feelings, inclinations (endorsed by System 2 → beliefs, attitudes, intentions)
- Operates automatically with little or no effort
- Can be programmed by System 2 to mobilize attention when particular patterns are detected
- Executes skilled responses and generates skilled intuitions after adequate training
- Creates coherent pattern of activated ideas in associative memory
- Links cognitive ease to illusions of truth, pleasant feelings, reduced vigilance
- Distinguishes surprising from normal; infers and invents causes and intentions
- Neglects ambiguity and suppresses doubt; is biased to believe and confirm
- Exaggerates emotional consistency (halo effect); focuses on existing evidence (WYSIATI)
- Represents sets by norms/prototypes, does not integrate; matches intensities across scales
- Computes more than intended (mental shotgun); substitutes easier questions (heuristics)
- Is more sensitive to changes than to states (prospect theory)*
- Overweights low probabilities*; shows diminishing sensitivity to quantity (psychophysics)*
- Responds more strongly to losses than to gains (loss aversion)*
- Frames decision problems narrowly, in isolation*
(*These characteristics are covered in Parts III-V)

## PART II: HEURISTICS AND BIASES

### Chapter 10: The Law of Small Numbers (pp. 129-140)

**Kidney Cancer in Rural Counties**
- Counties with lowest kidney cancer incidence: rural, sparsely populated, traditionally Republican, Midwest/South/West
- Natural causal story: clean rural living, no pollution, fresh food
- Counties with HIGHEST kidney cancer incidence: also rural, sparsely populated, traditionally Republican, Midwest/South/West
- Natural causal story: poverty of rural life, no medical care, high-fat diet, alcohol, tobacco
- Both stories are wrong — the key factor is small population. Small samples yield extreme results (both high and low) more often than large samples
- "The incidence of cancer is not truly lower or higher than normal in a county with a small population — it just appears to be so because of an accident of sampling"

**The Urn Model and Sampling**
- Urn with half red, half white marbles. Jack draws 4, Jill draws 7. If they record homogeneous samples (all one color):
  - Jack will observe extreme outcomes by a factor of 8 more often than Jill (12.5% vs 1.56%)
- Small samples = more extreme results. This is a mathematical fact, not a causal story — no hammer, no causation
- US population as giant urn, marbles marked KC for kidney cancer → rural counties are smaller samples → more likely to show extreme rates

**The Law of Small Numbers**
- Kahneman-Tversky collaboration began 1970s with discussion of "intuitive statistics"
- Michigan researchers optimistic that people are good intuitive statisticians; K&T concluded they are not
- Kahneman had recently discovered he himself was not a good intuitive statistician — chose samples too small, got odd results that were actually artifacts of method
- Psychologists routinely chose samples so small they exposed themselves to 50% risk of failing to confirm true hypotheses
- "Belief in the Law of Small Numbers" (first joint article): intuitions about random sampling satisfy the law of small numbers — we believe small samples closely resemble the population
- Recommendation: "regard statistical intuitions with proper suspicion and replace impression formation by computation whenever possible"

**A Bias of Confidence Over Doubt**
- "In a telephone poll of 300 seniors, 60% support the president" — your 3-word summary would be "elderly support president," ignoring the sample size
- Summary would be the same if sample were 150 or 3,000 — "people are not adequately sensitive to sample size"
- System 1 is not prone to doubt; it suppresses ambiguity, constructs stories as coherent as possible
- Unless immediately rejected as a lie, a message activates associations as if it were true: WYSIATI
- "Sustaining doubt is harder work than sliding into certainty"
- The law of small numbers is a manifestation of a general bias that favors certainty over doubt

**Cause and Chance**
- Associative machinery seeks causes; statistical regularities call for a different approach — what could have happened instead
- Birth sequence BBBGGG vs. GGGGGG vs. BGBBGB — all equally likely, but only third appears random
- We are pattern seekers, believers in a coherent world where regularities appear not by accident but by causation
- We do not expect to see regularity produced by a random process; when we detect it, we reject the idea that the process is truly random

**London Bombing and Hot Hand**
- William Feller: during WWII, Londoners believed rocket bombing had deliberate patterns (conspicuous gaps = German spies) — statistical analysis showed distribution was typical of randomness
- "To the untrained eye, randomness appears as regularity or tendency to cluster"
- Kahneman during 1973 Yom Kippur War: two Israeli Air Force squadrons from same base, one lost 4 planes while other lost none → inquiry launched to find what was wrong → Kahneman advised it was blind luck (different outcomes were due to random variation, not performance differences)
- Hot hand in basketball (Gilovich, Vallone, Tversky): analysis of thousands of shot sequences → no such thing as a hot hand; the sequence of successes and misses satisfies all tests of randomness
- Red Auerbach (Celtics coach): "Who is this guy? So he makes a study. I couldn't care less."
- "The tendency to see patterns in randomness is overwhelming — certainly more impressive than a guy making a study"
- Extends beyond basketball: How many good years before concluding an investment adviser is skilled? How many successful acquisitions before CEO has extraordinary flair? "If you follow your intuition, you will more often than not err by misclassifying a random event as systematic"

**Gates Foundation & Small Schools**
- Research found most successful schools are small (6 of top 50 in 1,662 Pennsylvania schools = 4x overrepresentation)
- Gates Foundation invested $1.7 billion+ in creating small schools; joined by Annenberg, Pew, U.S. Department of Education
- But worst schools are also small — small schools are not better on average, they are simply more variable
- Large schools tend to produce better results, especially in higher grades where variety of curricular options matters
