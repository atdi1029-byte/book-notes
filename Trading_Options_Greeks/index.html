Glossary

Foundational Concepts

Option: A contract giving the owner the right (not obligation) to buy or sell a fixed quantity of an underlying security at a specific price within a set time.
Call: The right to BUY the underlying at the strike price. Valuable when the stock rises above the strike.
Put: The right to SELL the underlying at the strike price. Valuable when the stock falls below the strike.
Strike price: The fixed price at which the holder may buy (call) or sell (put) the underlying upon exercise.
Expiration: The date after which the option ceases to exist. For equity options, the Saturday after the third Friday of the stated month; last trading day is that Friday.
Premium: The price paid for an option contract. Equals intrinsic value plus time value.
Intrinsic value: How far an option is in-the-money. A call with stock at $55 and strike at $50 has $5 intrinsic value. OTM options have zero intrinsic value.
Time value (extrinsic value): Premium minus intrinsic value. Represents the possibility of further favorable movement before expiration. Decays to zero at expiration.
In-the-money (ITM): A call whose strike is below the stock price; a put whose strike is above it.
At-the-money (ATM): Strike price approximately equal to the current stock price.
Out-of-the-money (OTM): A call whose strike is above the stock price; a put whose strike is below it.
Parity: An option trading with only intrinsic value and no time value left. Deep ITM options often trade at parity.
American-style: Can be exercised any time before expiration. All equity options listed on US exchanges.
European-style: Can only be exercised at expiration. Common for index options like SPX.

The Five Greeks

Delta (Δ): Rate of change in option price per $1 move in the underlying. Calls have positive delta (0 to 1.00); puts have negative delta (0 to −1.00). ATM options have delta near ±0.50.
Gamma (Γ): Rate of change in delta per $1 move in the underlying. The "delta of delta." ATM options have the highest gamma; ITM/OTM have low gamma. Long options = positive gamma; short options = negative gamma.
Theta (Θ): Daily rate of time decay. How much the option loses in value each day assuming all else equal. Long options have negative theta (you pay decay). Short options have positive theta (you collect decay). Theta accelerates in the final 30 days, especially for ATM options.
Vega (V): Change in option price per 1% change in implied volatility. ATM options have the highest vega. Long options have positive vega (rising IV helps); short options have negative vega (rising IV hurts).
Rho (ρ): Change in option price per 1% change in the risk-free interest rate. Calls have positive rho (higher rates favor calls); puts have negative rho. Most significant for LEAPS and deep ITM options.

Volatility Terms

Historical volatility (HV): Annualized standard deviation of daily returns over a past period. Also called realized volatility or statistical volatility. Measures how much the stock actually moved.
Implied volatility (IV): The volatility figure implied by the current market price of an option, backed out from a pricing model. Reflects the market's expectation of future volatility, not historical behavior.
VIX: CBOE Volatility Index; measures 30-day implied volatility of S&P 500 options. Often called the "fear gauge." Typically inversely correlated with the S&P 500.
Volatility skew: The difference in IV across strikes (vertical skew) or expirations (term structure skew). OTM puts typically have higher IV than OTM calls — the "smirk."
IV rush: A rapid rise in implied volatility before a known event (earnings, FDA decision). Option prices inflate even if the underlying doesn't move.
IV crush: Sharp collapse in implied volatility after the event resolves. Option prices drop regardless of the direction of the underlying's move.
Realized volatility: How much the stock actually moved during a period. Compared against implied volatility to evaluate whether options were fairly priced.

Strategies and Structures

Covered call: Long stock plus short call on the same underlying. Caps upside in exchange for premium income.
Protective put: Long stock plus long put. Insurance that limits downside while preserving upside.
Vertical spread: Buy one option, sell another at a different strike, same expiration, same underlying. Bull call spread (buy lower, sell higher) or bear put spread (buy higher put, sell lower put).
Iron condor: Short OTM call spread plus short OTM put spread. Four legs. Profits when the underlying stays in a range.
Butterfly: Buy one option, sell two at a middle strike, buy one more at an outer strike. Maximum profit when underlying pins at middle strike at expiration.
Calendar spread: Buy a longer-dated option and sell a shorter-dated option at the same strike. Also called a time spread. Trades volatility term structure.
Straddle: Buy (or sell) a call and a put at the same strike and expiration. Profits from large moves in either direction (long) or from range-bound action (short).
Strangle: Like a straddle but with different strikes — OTM call and OTM put. Cheaper premium, wider break-even points.
Ratio spread: Buy fewer options than you sell (e.g., buy 1, sell 2). Creates a position with extra short gamma beyond the hedge.
Backspread: Sell fewer options than you buy. Opposite of ratio spread. Net long gamma, benefits from very large moves.
Delta-neutral: A position with total delta close to zero. Not direction-indifferent — gamma will make it directional as the stock moves. Isolates the vega/gamma trade.
Gamma scalping: Dynamically delta-hedging a long-gamma position by buying stock when the price falls and selling when it rises. Monetizes realized volatility.
Conversion/reversal: Synthetic arbitrage position combining stock, call, and put to lock in risk-free profit when put-call parity is violated.
Pin risk: The risk that an underlying settles exactly at the strike at expiration, leaving the short option writer uncertain whether they will be assigned.

Part I — The Basics of Option Greeks

Chapter 1: The Basics

An option is a contract giving its owner the right to buy or sell a fixed quantity of an underlying security at a specific price within a certain time. There are two types: calls give the right to buy; puts give the right to sell. The buyer — called the holder or long — owns the right and pays a premium for it. The seller — called the writer or short — receives that premium and incurs an obligation. Unlike stock ownership, option holders receive no dividends and have no voting rights. The corporation has no knowledge of option contract parties.

When a trader who is long an option exercises, a trader with a short position gets assigned — required to fulfill the obligation to buy or sell at the strike. Shorting an option requires no borrowing, unlike shorting stock; the contract is simply created between two parties. For every open long contract there is an equal and offsetting short contract — they are exactly as common as each other.

All orders are either opening or closing transactions. A new long position is established with a buy-to-open order; an existing long is exited with a sell-to-close. A new short is established with a sell-to-open order; it is covered with a buy-to-close. Volume measures contracts traded in a session and resets to zero each day; open interest is a running total of all outstanding contracts. When Trader A opens long from Trader B who opens short: volume = 1, OI = 1. When Trader B buys back from Trader C (both transacting but neither pair is net new): volume = 2, OI stays at 1.

The Options Clearing Corporation (OCC) is the ultimate guarantor of every options trade — in 2010, nearly 3.9 billion contracts. It works through clearing members: both sides of a trade submit to their respective clearing firms, which both report to the OCC, which matches the trade. If the original seller covers his position, the OCC reassigns an obligation to a different short holder — the OCC is the ultimate counterparty to both exercise and assignment. Exchange-listed options are standardized: fixed contract size (typically 100 shares), listed strike increments, and standard expiration schedules.

An option's premium has two components. Intrinsic value is the amount by which an option is in-the-money: an IBM 170 call with IBM at $171.30 has $1.30 intrinsic value. Time value is what remains — the possibility of further favorable movement. OTM options are entirely time value; deep ITM options trading with virtually no time value are said to be trading at parity.

The four basic positions map to distinct risk profiles. Buying a call: Intel June 22.50 call at $0.85 with INTC at $22.25 — limited risk ($85 per contract), unlimited upside, break-even of $23.35. At $27 expiration the return is 429%; leverage cuts both ways. Selling a naked call: TGT October 50 call at $1.45 with TGT at $49.42 — the $1.45 is the maximum profit; risk is unlimited above the $51.45 break-even. This position requires margin and is "not bullish" — it merely requires TGT not to rise above $50.

Selling a covered call — owning the stock and writing a call against it — caps upside in exchange for income. Isabel buys TGT at $49.42 and sells the October 50 call at $1.45: maximum profit is $2.03 (the $0.58 stock gain to $50 plus the $1.45 premium) and the break-even drops to $47.97. Selling a put is the bullish-neutral income trade: Sam sells a BA January 65 put at $1.20 with BA at $69.77, willing to own the stock at an effective cost of $63.80. If the put is never assigned, Sam keeps the $1.20 regardless. The long put mirrors the short call: buying the SPY May 139 put at $2.30 delivers a break-even of $136.70 and limited risk equal to the premium. A protective put — stock plus long put — is pure insurance: Kathleen's maximum loss on the SPY position is $3.65 per share regardless of how far the market falls.

Chapter 2: Greek Philosophy

Options have a price in the market and a value determined by an options-pricing model. The model (typically Black-Scholes) takes inputs — current stock price, strike, time to expiration, risk-free rate, and volatility — and outputs a theoretical value. When market price diverges from theoretical value, a tradeable opportunity may exist. The greeks are the derivatives of the pricing model: they measure how sensitive the option's value is to each input changing by a small amount. They do not predict the future — they describe the current risk landscape.

Delta is the rate of change in option value per $1 move in the underlying stock. A call with delta 0.57 gains approximately $0.57 in value for every $1 rise in the stock. Delta ranges from 0 (deep OTM call) to 1.00 (deep ITM call). ATM options typically have delta near 0.50. Passarelli offers four definitions: (1) the option's rate of price change per dollar move in the stock; (2) the hedge ratio — the number of options needed to hedge 100 shares of stock; (3) the percentage probability of expiring ITM; and (4) the equivalent stock position — a 0.50 delta call behaves like owning 50 shares.

"Option greeks are metrics used to measure an option's sensitivity to influences on its price. Comprehensive knowledge of the greeks can help traders to avoid common pitfalls and increase profit potential." — Dan Passarelli
Delta is not fixed — it changes as the stock moves, as time passes, and as volatility shifts. This rate of change is gamma. An option with delta 0.50 and gamma 0.05 will have delta 0.55 if the stock rises $1, and 0.45 if it falls $1. ATM options have the highest gamma; far ITM and far OTM options have near-zero gamma. Long options have positive gamma — the delta is always working "with" you, increasing when the stock moves in your favor and decreasing when it moves against you. Short options have negative gamma — the delta always works against you, creating the constant risk of an accelerating loss.

Theta is the daily cost of owning time value. A long ATM option losing $0.013 per day (theta = −0.013) is slowly being consumed by the passage of time. Theta is not linear: it accelerates sharply in the final 30 days before expiration, especially for ATM options. The exhibit of ATM theta over the final 10 days shows the decay curve becoming increasingly steep. Short options earn positive theta — the option seller's edge is this steady collection of time value. The theta-gamma trade-off is the central tension of all options strategies: long gamma means negative theta, short gamma means positive theta, and neither side gets both.

Vega measures sensitivity to implied volatility. An AT&T call with vega of 0.10 gains $0.10 for every one-point rise in IV. The relationship is symmetrical: the same option loses $0.10 for every one-point fall. Vega is highest for ATM options with the most time remaining — longer-dated options have more of their premium composed of uncertainty, which responds more to volatility changes. Short-dated options and deep ITM/OTM options have low vega. This is why the rule holds:
"Buy options when expecting volatility to increase; sell options when expecting volatility to decrease." — Dan Passarelli, recurring principle throughout Trading Option Greeks

Rho measures the sensitivity to interest rates. It enters the pricing model through put-call parity: calls benefit from rising rates (the cost of carrying stock) while puts are hurt. For most retail traders, rho is the least important greek in the short run. Its influence grows substantially with time — a LEAPS option can have rho ten times that of a near-term option at the same strike. Passarelli notes that online brokers sometimes quote greeks using slightly different model assumptions, and traders should understand what they are seeing.

Two forces constantly reshape delta: the passage of time and changes in implied volatility. As expiration approaches, the delta of an ITM option migrates toward 1.00 (certainty of being assigned) and the delta of an OTM option migrates toward 0.00 (certainty of expiring worthless) — this polarization is why gamma peaks near ATM options close to expiration. Rising volatility has the opposite effect: it widens the probability distribution, so ITM deltas fall below 1.00 and OTM deltas rise above 0 — the option market is expressing greater uncertainty about where the stock will finish. Passarelli's delta matrices (Exhibit 2.3 and 2.4) show this directly: a call option that has delta 1.00 at 10% volatility has delta only 0.79 at 45% volatility, because high implied vol means even a deep-in-the-money option carries meaningful probability of expiring OTM before expiration. Understanding these two forces — time and volatility pulling on delta simultaneously — is the foundation of advanced options position management.

Chapter 3: Understanding Volatility

Historical volatility (HV) — also called realized volatility or statistical volatility — is the annualized standard deviation of daily returns over a past period. It tells you how much the stock actually moved. Standard deviation, represented by the Greek letter sigma (σ), measures dispersion from a mean. In a normal distribution, roughly 68% of observations fall within one standard deviation of the mean, 95% within two, and 99.7% within three — the "68-95-99.7 rule." Applied to stocks: if a stock has 20% HV and is trading at $50, there is a 68% probability it will be between $40 and $60 in one year.

Implied volatility (IV) is the volatility figure implied by the option's current market price. Where HV asks "how much did the stock move?" IV asks "how much does the market expect it to move?" IV is derived by plugging the current option price back into the pricing model and solving for the volatility input. When fear rises — before earnings, during crises — option buyers pay up and IV inflates. When calm returns, IV collapses. The VIX index measures 30-day IV on S&P 500 options and has a persistent inverse relationship with the index itself: market drops drive VIX up, rallies bring it down.

To convert annual IV into a single-day expected move, divide by the square root of 252 (trading days in a year). A stock with 25% IV has a daily expected move of 25% ÷ √252 ≈ 1.57%. For a $40 stock: the one-standard-deviation daily move is $40 × 1.57% ≈ $0.63. This calculation lets traders compare the magnitude of implied moves against their directional forecasts or strategy requirements.

Extending to a multi-day expected move uses the same formula scaled by the square root of time. For a 30-day move at 25% IV: 25% × √(30/252) ≈ 8.63%. For the same $40 stock, this translates to a one-standard-deviation expected move of $3.45 over 30 days. Straddle traders use this calculation to evaluate whether the premium they are paying for a straddle is justified by the expected move: if a 30-day straddle costs $3.00 and the standard deviation expected move is $3.45, the options are priced fairly. If the straddle costs $5.00, options are expensive relative to historical movement patterns — a sell signal. This is how professional traders convert abstract "implied volatility" numbers into concrete dollar move expectations.

Volatility exhibits mean reversion: extremely high and extremely low IV levels tend to revert toward their historical averages. The SPX has had HV ranging from roughly 7% to 80% over the decades. When IV is unusually elevated relative to HV, it often signals an opportunity to sell volatility; when IV is depressed relative to its historical norms, buying volatility becomes more attractive. But mean reversion is probabilistic — IV can stay elevated longer than a trader can stay solvent on short volatility positions.

Two types of skew matter to options traders. Term structure skew: near-term IV often differs from longer-term IV. Before an expected event (earnings, Fed meeting), front-month IV spikes while back-month IV may remain stable, flattening or inverting the term structure curve. Vertical skew: OTM puts typically have higher IV than OTM calls on the same expiration. This "volatility smirk" reflects persistent demand for downside protection — put buyers willing to overpay for insurance, market makers pricing in tail risk. The Citigroup exhibit shows a clear downward slope: the 30-strike put carries meaningfully higher IV than the 35-strike call.

The practical challenge of volatility forecasting is distinguishing temporary IV spikes from genuine regime changes. Passarelli recommends studying at least six months of IV history for the specific underlying — some stocks chronically trade at elevated IV (biotech, small-caps), others at persistently low IV (utilities, large-cap index ETFs), and neither is abnormal for that name. The GM term structure exhibit illustrates this vividly: near-month IV at 32%, back-month IV at 34.6%, with the gap between them reflecting only normal term structure premium — not a meaningful signal. The trader who does not know whether that gap is wide or narrow by historical standards has no reliable basis for a vol trade. Building a personal vol database — even informally, tracking the six-month IV range for every underlying you trade — is Passarelli's prescription for developing the intuition that experienced floor traders acquired simply by watching markets every day for years.

Chapter 4: Option-Specific Risk and Opportunity

Each position in an option has a unique greek profile that changes as the underlying moves, as time passes, and as volatility shifts. Understanding these dynamics is not academic — it determines whether a trade is appropriate for a given forecast. Passarelli structures this chapter around two traders, Kim and Mick, who each analyze Disney (DIS) options using the full suite of greeks before and after opening positions.

Kim examines long ATM calls on Disney at $35. The 37.50 strike call (OTM) costs $0.55 and has a delta of 0.35, gamma of 0.166, theta of −0.013, and vega of 0.048. The 35 strike call (ATM) costs $1.30 and has delta 0.57, gamma 0.166, theta −0.013, and vega 0.048. The ATM call has higher delta but the same gamma and vega as the OTM call — yet costs more than twice as much. This highlights a key insight: gamma and vega are similar across nearby strikes at the same expiration, but delta and premium differ substantially.

A long ATM call on Disney with stock at exactly $35 — break-even is $36.30 ($35 strike + $1.30 premium). The time-price matrix (Exhibit 4.2) shows how the value of this call changes across a range of stock prices and days remaining. At $37 with 44 days left the call is worth $3.30; at $37 with 20 days left it is worth $2.90; the decay cost of time is immediately visible. OTM calls are really about gamma, time horizon, and magnitude: an OTM call only profits with a large, fast move. The theta and time demands on an OTM call make it a bet on both direction and speed — a higher hurdle than most traders realize.

Traders who watch only delta and forget theta are surprised by this: the stock can move in your favor and the option can still lose value if time passes faster than delta gains.

Passarelli's core observation: all options trades are, at their heart, volatility trades. Buying options is a bullish volatility position: you want IV to rise (making your option worth more) or realized vol to exceed what you paid for. Selling options is a bearish volatility position: you want IV to fall and the stock to move less than the option premium implied. This framework cuts across direction: even a directional long call is also a bet that realized vol will justify the premium paid. The taxonomy of buying strategies (long calls, long puts, long straddles) vs. selling strategies (covered calls, short puts, iron condors) maps directly onto long-vol vs. short-vol exposures.

"There should be no statistical advantage to systematically buying or selling options. The options market will always equalize imbalances." — Dan Passarelli, Chapter 4
The teenie sellers of the CBOE floor — traders who habitually sold deep OTM options for "teinies" ($0.0625, the minimum tick in pre-decimal days) — appeared to have a reliable edge collecting small premiums month after month. Eventually, a single catastrophic event wiped out years of gains in a day. The Babe Ruth analogy captures the other side: Ruth had a low batting average but a very high expected value per at-bat because his home runs far exceeded his strikeouts in dollar terms. Long-option traders accept many small losses for occasional large winners. The Fair Game argument: in an efficient market, there should be no systematic statistical edge to buying or selling options — the market prices IV at its fair expected value, just as a fair die game offers no edge at the correct entrance fee. Individual mispricings exist at moments; a systematic advantage for one side does not.

Chapter 5: Introduction to Volatility-Selling Strategies

Along with death and taxes, Passarelli writes, one fact of options life is certain: the time value of all options ultimately goes to zero. Volatility-selling strategies exploit this: they are income-generating positions that are short gamma, short vega, and long theta. The seller is "effectively paid to assume the risk of movement" — collecting premium in exchange for bearing the risk that the underlying moves farther than the market priced in.

"Along with death and taxes, there is one other fact of life we can all count on: the time value of all options ultimately going to zero." — Dan Passarelli, Chapter 5
The gamma-theta trade-off is the engine of all volatility-selling positions. Short options have negative gamma (delta always working against you as the stock moves) but positive theta (daily income from time decay). The seller is a slow, steady earner — collecting a small amount each day — who faces the occasional outsized loss if the underlying moves sharply. The buyer has the mirror profile: daily bleed from negative theta, but gains sharply when a big move arrives. Passarelli argues that the greeks matter for volatility sellers even if the seller intends to hold to expiration: they determine when to exit early, how to size stop-losses, and how to manage the position if the environment changes.

Brendan's naked JNJ call: Johnson & Johnson has been trading in a $60–$65 channel for months; the stock approaches the $65 resistance level. Brendan sells 10 JNJ November 65 calls at $0.66 with the stock near $64 — maximum profit $660, 28 days to expiration. He checks: no earnings, no takeover news, ADX/DMI confirms no trend. The greeks on entry: delta −0.34, gamma −0.15, theta +0.02, vega −0.07. To plan his stop-loss, Brendan uses delta and gamma: to trigger a stop at the $1.10 repurchase price, the stock needs to rise only $1.01 — not the $1.24 suggested by delta alone — because gamma accelerates the option's price sensitivity as the stock rises.

The naked put — Stacie's JNJ 65 put — appears safer than the naked call because stocks don't typically fall as fast as they rise, but it carries a subtle extra risk: when a stock falls sharply, IV almost always spikes simultaneously. A naked put holder faces a "double whammy": the delta loss from the stock falling plus the vega loss from rising IV, both hitting simultaneously.

The covered call — Bill's Harley-Davidson HOG March 70 call — replaces the unlimited risk of a naked call with the stock as collateral. But this is not "free money": the stock can still fall sharply, and the $1.45 premium provides only $1.45 of downside cushion. Passarelli notes the IV term structure opportunity: if near-month IV is elevated relative to back-months, the covered call seller is collecting inflated premium that is likely to revert. The covered put — Libby's HOG short put paired with short stock — is the mirror strategy, appropriate when the trader is bearish on the stock with a neutral short-term IV view.

The "Would I Do It Now?" rule appears here for the first time: periodically examine every open short position and ask whether, starting fresh with no position, you would establish this short right now at the current price. If the answer is no — if market conditions have changed enough that you'd pass on this trade — then close it. This rule combats the inertia that keeps traders holding losers through cognitive dissonance rather than clear-eyed decision-making.

The covered call vs. naked put equivalence is one of the most important insights from put-call parity: a covered call (long stock + short call) and a cash-secured short put at the same strike and expiration produce identical P&L profiles at expiration. Bill's covered call on HOG at the 70 strike has the same at-expiration diagram as Libby's naked put at the 70 strike. Market makers arbitrage any pricing divergence between the two instantly through conversions and reversals. This equivalence helps traders choose the most capital-efficient version of the same trade.

Passarelli frames the buyer-vs-seller debate through the Babe Ruth analogy and the fair game argument. The option seller is like an insurance company: collecting small premiums on many policies, accepting the occasional large payout. The option buyer is Babe Ruth: he struck out more than almost any batter in history but his home-run rate was so high that his expected value per at-bat was extraordinary. Neither approach is superior in an efficient market — option prices embed the market's collective forecast, and the pricing model ensures that if buyers consistently won, sellers would leave the market and bid up premiums until the advantage vanished. What separates professionals from amateurs is not a systematic buy or sell bias — it is discipline in position sizing, stop-loss management, and the ruthless application of the "Would I Do It Now?" test to every open position at every point in its life.

Chapter 6: Put-Call Parity and Synthetics

Put-call parity is the most fundamental no-arbitrage relationship in options. For European options: call price − put price = stock price − present value of strike. Without dividends: C − P = S − Xe^(−rT). With dividends: C − P = S − PV(dividends) − Xe^(−rT). This means that a call and a put on the same underlying, same strike, same expiration, combined with the appropriate stock or bond position, must produce an equivalent payoff — or arbitrage exists.

Passarelli walks through the arithmetic concretely. If a stock is at $50, the 50 call is $3.00, and the 50 put is $2.50, and the risk-free rate is 5% with 60 days to expiration: the call-put parity formula predicts the difference should be $50 − $50 × e^(−0.05 × 60/365) ≈ $0.41. The actual difference is $3.00 − $2.50 = $0.50. The call is overpriced by $0.09 — or the put is underpriced — and a conversion or reversal could capture this. In practice, the bid-ask spread, commissions, and borrowing costs for the stock position consume most of any small arbitrage profit, which is why only institutions with near-zero transaction costs run conversion/reversal books profitably.

The interest advantage embedded in calls explains why they always trade at a premium to puts of the same strike: the call buyer avoids tying up capital in the stock, earning interest on the difference. A married put (long stock + long put) and a long call are synthetic equivalents — same payoff profile at expiration — but the call is cheaper because the investor doesn't pay for the stock upfront.

The four basic synthetic relationships: long stock = long call + short put (same strike, same expiration); short stock = short call + long put; long call = long stock + long put; long put = short stock + long call. These are not just theoretical curiosities — they are actively used by market makers to hedge exposures and by traders to construct positions more capital-efficiently.

Pin risk is the silent danger for short-option holders near expiration: if the stock settles exactly at the strike, the trader doesn't know whether they'll be assigned. A market maker short 100 calls at the 50 strike with stock at exactly $50.00 at expiration may be assigned on some, all, or none of those contracts — and won't find out until after the weekend. Passarelli extends put-call parity to two advanced structures: boxes and jelly rolls. A box — buying a call spread at one set of strikes and selling a put spread at the same strikes — locks in a fixed payoff at expiration equal to the width between the strikes. A 50–60 box with 90 days to expiration should trade at approximately $9.85 (the present value of $10 discounted at the risk-free rate); paying $9.85 for a $10 box is equivalent to lending money at 6%. A jelly roll combines a synthetic long position in one expiration with a synthetic short in another — it is an interest-rate bet on the difference in financing costs between two expiration months. Both structures strip out directional, volatility, and time risk, leaving rho as the primary exposure — which is why they are used almost exclusively by institutional market makers to manage short-term cash and financing costs at near-zero bid-ask spreads. For retail traders, the lesson is conceptual: understanding boxes and jelly rolls explains why conversions and reversals are priced as they are, and why deviations from put-call parity vanish so quickly in liquid markets.

Chapter 7: Rho

Rho measures the change in option price per 1% change in the risk-free interest rate. Calls have positive rho (rising rates benefit call holders by making the "borrow and buy" alternative less attractive relative to the leveraged call position); puts have negative rho (rising rates reduce the present value of the fixed-rate put payoff).

The magnitude of rho scales with three factors: the depth of the option in-the-money, the level of the interest rate, and most importantly, time to expiration. A near-term option's rho is negligible — a 30-day ATM option might have rho of 0.02, barely visible. A LEAPS option with 639 days remaining can have rho of 0.638 — making a 1% rate move equivalent to a $0.64 change in the option's price per contract (or $64 for a standard contract).

The asymmetry between call and put rho traces directly back to put-call parity and the interest embedded in every option contract. When you buy a call instead of buying the stock, you keep the stock purchase price in an interest-bearing account — effectively lending that capital to someone else. Rising interest rates make that lending more profitable, so the call gains value. Conversely, when you buy a put instead of selling the stock short, you miss the interest you would have earned on short-sale proceeds; rising rates therefore reduce the put's attractiveness, lowering its value. This is not a quirk — it is the structural reason why calls always trade at a premium to equivalent puts when interest rates are positive, and it is why Passarelli calls rho "the interest rate embedded in every options position."

Hard-to-borrow stocks create rho complications that can surprise traders using standard model assumptions. When a stock is difficult to borrow — heavily shorted biotechs, companies near bankruptcy, recent hot IPOs — the short-stock rebate rate (the interest earned by holders of short positions) drops sharply, sometimes turning negative. In that environment, the call-put parity relationship breaks down for anyone who cannot easily borrow the stock. The put may trade at a substantial premium over what standard rho calculations suggest because the synthetic short stock (via options) has become more valuable than the actual short. Passarelli's point: the "correct" interest rate to plug into any options model is personal — it depends on your actual borrowing cost or short-sale rebate, not the published risk-free rate. A retail trader and a hedge fund with a prime brokerage relationship can see a 1–2 IV point difference in fair value for the same option if their financing rates differ significantly.

Rho's sign tells traders the direction of interest rate exposure: positive rho (long calls, short puts) benefits when rates rise; negative rho (long puts, short calls) benefits when rates fall. Calendar spreads and LEAPS strategies can accumulate substantial rho by combining positions with very different time-to-expiration. A long LEAPS call hedged with a short near-term call creates a diagonal that is net long rho — it benefits from rising rates because the back-month call's rho dwarfs the front-month's rho. Passarelli notes that many retail LEAPS strategies run inadvertent positive rho exposure without the trader being aware, and the 2022–2023 Federal Reserve hiking cycle — the most aggressive in four decades — turned that invisible exposure into meaningful losses for holders of deep ITM LEAPS who hadn't accounted for it in their entry pricing.

Calculating rho exposure for a multi-leg portfolio requires summing each position's rho with the correct sign. A position that is long 10 LEAPS calls (rho +0.638 each) and short 10 near-term calls (rho +0.018 each) carries net rho of +(10 × 0.638) − (10 × 0.018) = +6.20, meaning a 1% rise in the risk-free rate adds $620 of value to the portfolio. For traders primarily interested in delta and volatility, this rho exposure is easy to overlook — but it compounds over months-long LEAPS holding periods. The practical takeaway: before initiating any LEAPS position, calculate the rho explicitly using a model, note the sign, and decide whether you want that interest rate exposure or want to hedge it by layering a short put (negative rho) to offset. This is especially important in macro-volatile environments where central bank policy is changing rapidly.

Chapter 8: Dividends and Option Pricing

Dividends affect option pricing in ways that surprise traders who haven't studied them carefully. Every dividend has four key dates: declaration date (board announces the dividend), ex-dividend date (first day trading without right to dividend), record date (shareholders of record who receive payment), and payment date (cash arrives). Traders who buy stock on or after the ex-date don't receive the current dividend; options pricing reflects this adjustment.

When a stock goes ex-dividend, the stock price drops by approximately the dividend amount on the opening. This automatic price adjustment shifts option values: calls become worth less (the stock will fall), puts become worth more. A call priced without accounting for a $1 dividend will be overpriced by roughly $1 × delta; a put will be underpriced by the same amount. Input the correct dividend data into pricing models or the greeks will be wrong — the most common dividend error is using the wrong ex-date, which can create a 2-point IV misread.

Early exercise of American calls is normally irrational — you give up time value by exercising early. The exception is dividends: if the dividend you would receive from exercising exceeds the time value you would sacrifice, early exercise is rational. The decision rule: early exercise makes sense when Dividend > Interest + Put Bid, where the put bid is the time value of the equivalent put and interest is the cost of carrying the stock. Deep ITM calls with little time value and large dividends are the primary candidates for early assignment before the ex-date.

A "dividend play" involves buying stock just before the ex-date specifically to capture the dividend, while short calls on that stock face assignment risk. Before the ex-date, volume in deep ITM calls spikes dramatically as dividend-play traders buy calls, exercise them to get long stock, and collect the dividend. GE's dividend — a 30-year streak of quarterly payments — was cut during the 2008–2009 financial crisis, surprising traders who had priced in dividend continuity and suddenly found their pricing models producing strange results. Near the ex-date, the sum of call delta and put delta can exceed 1.00 in absolute terms — an anomaly caused by the discontinuous dividend adjustment, not a model error.

The MSFT special dividend of 2004 — $3 per share — created enormous disruption in Microsoft options. Traders who had not explicitly modeled the one-time $3 payment found their options pricing models suddenly wrong by a significant amount. Short call holders faced unexpected early assignment as smart money exercised to capture the dividend; short put holders benefited as the stock dropped predictably by $3 on the ex-date. Passarelli notes the more typical version: a company like XYZ that normally pays $0.10/quarter raises its dividend to $0.50. A model using $0.10 will misprice options significantly — an error that compounds if the position is held through multiple ex-dates.

Special and irregular dividends pose the most serious pricing risk for options holders. The Microsoft $3.00 special dividend of 2004 is Passarelli's cautionary example: traders who had not explicitly modeled the one-time payment found their pricing models producing wrong theoretical values, their greeks shifted significantly, and short calls faced waves of early exercise as smart money exercised to capture the $3 payment. The standard practice is to subtract expected dividends from the current stock price when inputting into a pricing model, but only declared dividends with confirmed ex-dates should be included — anticipated-but-unconfirmed dividends (like an expected raise or a speculative special payment) must be handled as a probability- weighted input, not a certainty, or the model will systematically misprice the options by a measurable amount.

Dividends interact with put-call parity in a way that explains the persistent put premium on dividend-paying stocks. Because the stock will fall by the dividend on the ex-date, put holders benefit mechanically while call holders are disadvantaged. The parity formula adjusts: C − P = S − PV(X) − PV(dividends). Every dollar of expected dividend shifts value from calls to puts by roughly the dividend amount multiplied by the probability of being ITM at expiration (the delta). For a 0.50-delta call on a stock paying a $2 dividend, the call loses approximately $1 of value relative to what it would be worth on a non-dividend-paying stock with identical characteristics. Traders building LEAPS positions on high-dividend stocks must account for cumulative dividend drag across multiple quarters — failing to do so produces a systematic underperformance bias in long-call strategies on REITs, utilities, and other high-yield sectors.

Part II — Spreads

Chapter 9: Vertical Spreads

A vertical spread is an options position combining a long option and a short option at different strikes, same expiration, same underlying. Buying a lower-strike call and selling a higher-strike call is a bull call spread — it costs less than the outright call but caps the upside at the short strike. The premium paid ($4.40 for an AAPL 395–405 bull call spread when AAPL is at $391) is the maximum loss; the difference between strikes minus the premium paid ($10 − $4.40 = $5.60) is the maximum gain.

The greek profile of a vertical spread shifts as the stock moves through the strikes. When AAPL is at $395 (near the long strike): the long call's greeks dominate — negative theta, positive gamma, positive vega. The trader needs upward movement to overcome time decay. When AAPL is at $400 (the midpoint): both calls influence greeks roughly equally, and the position becomes nearly a pure delta play with minimal theta or vega. When AAPL reaches $405 (near the short strike): the short call dominates — gamma turns negative, theta turns positive. Time decay now works for the trader who bought the spread, helping consolidate the gain.

The comparison between a spread and an outright call depends on timing: at expiration, the bull call spread wins decisively if the stock reaches but doesn't exceed the short strike. Vertical spreads interact with volatility. A bull call spread on a takeover candidate is interesting: if no deal happens, the stock stays range-bound and the spread's limited premium decays; if a deal happens, the spread pays its maximum regardless of the acquisition premium. The trader is wrong on the spread being OTM but still wins — a "win-win-win" scenario. Put-call parity means that every credit spread has an equivalent debit spread: a bear call spread (sell lower, buy higher) produces the same economic result as a bull put spread (buy lower put, sell higher put) at the same strikes. A box — combining both a bull call spread and a bull put spread at the same strikes — locks in a known profit (or loss) regardless of underlying movement, reducing to a pure present-value calculation.

The Sam and Isabel box — using JNJ options — illustrates this concretely. Sam establishes a bull call spread (buy 65 call, sell 70 call). Isabel simultaneously establishes a bull put spread on the same expiration (buy 65 put, sell 70 put). Together, the box's value at any stock price at expiration is exactly $5.00 — the width of the strikes. The current combined price of the box in the market is the present value of $5.00 discounted at the risk-free rate. If the market misprices this, arbitrageurs buy the box at the discount and hold to expiration, collecting the difference. In practice, boxes trade at very tight margins because institutional desks monitor these relationships continuously. For retail traders, the box is most useful as a lens: if you own a bull call spread and the underlying falls, selling the corresponding bear put spread (converting to a box) can lock in any remaining profit without closing the position.

Credit spreads — selling the closer-to-money option and buying the further-out option — collect premium at the cost of capped maximum risk. Passarelli's bear call spread example: sell the lower-strike call and buy a higher-strike call, collecting a net credit. The bear call spread profits if the stock stays flat or falls below the short strike at expiration; maximum profit is the credit received, maximum loss is the spread width minus the credit. The equivalent position from put-call parity is the bull put spread — sell a higher-strike put, buy a lower-strike put — which produces identical P&L at expiration. Which structure to use depends on whether the trader prefers a call spread or put spread mechanic — in normal markets with a typical skew, puts carry higher IV so credit spreads are often wider using puts, making bull put spreads slightly more attractive in terms of premium collected per dollar of risk.

The bear put spread — Passarelli's XOM example — buys a higher-strike put and sells a lower-strike put to express a bearish view at reduced cost. Exxon Mobil is trading at $80; the trader buys the 80 put and sells the 75 put, paying a net debit. The spread profits at expiration if XOM falls below the long put's break-even; maximum gain occurs if XOM falls below $75 (both puts fully in-the-money). The key decision with debit spreads is whether the stock move expected is proportional to the premium paid — a $2.50 debit on a $5 wide spread requires XOM to fall at least $2.50 before the spread breaks even at expiration. Compared to a naked long put, the spread's cost is cut in half but so is the maximum gain; the choice between them depends on the trader's conviction in the magnitude of the expected decline.

Managing vertical spreads through time requires understanding how the greek profile shifts as the stock moves through the strike range. A bull call spread that has moved into the money — stock above the short strike — is now a short-gamma, positive-theta position: time decay works in the holder's favor, and additional movement hurts the spread's P&L by limiting any further gain. At this point, the "Would I Do It Now?" test becomes essential: would a neutral trader put on this short-gamma position today at current prices? If not, closing the spread early and locking in most of the maximum profit can be superior to holding for the full decay, especially if significant time remains before expiration and volatility is elevated. Conversely, a spread that has gone deeply out of the money has become a long-gamma, negative-theta position — still directionally unfavorable, but with the character of a cheap lottery ticket. The decision to exit early versus hold and hope depends on theta, the remaining time value in the spread, and the trader's updated directional conviction.

Chapter 10: Wing Spreads

Wing spreads — condors and butterflies — are multi-leg strategies that profit from the underlying staying within a range or pinning at a specific price. A butterfly buys one option at a lower strike, sells two at a middle strike, and buys one at an upper strike — all same expiration. Maximum profit occurs when the stock pins exactly at the middle strike at expiration; the position loses money outside the wings.

UPS butterfly example: UPS at $70; buy 1 UPS 67.50 call, sell 2 UPS 70 calls, buy 1 UPS 72.50 call. The at-expiration break-evens are $67 and $73 — stock must stay within that range. Maximum profit is the width of one wing minus the debit paid. An iron butterfly uses puts on one side and calls on the other, typically for a net credit: sell ATM call and put, buy OTM call and OTM put as wings. The iron butterfly is equivalent to the standard butterfly but creates a credit rather than a debit; the same break-even mechanics apply.

Strike selection for iron condors is critical. Too narrow and the maximum profit zone is small and easily violated; too wide and the premium collected is negligible relative to the risk. A directional butterfly skews the wings: instead of centering on the ATM strike, center the body on where you think the stock will be at expiration. Walgreen's directional butterfly, bought for just $0.10 debit, illustrates how a low-cost asymmetric bet can be constructed — the implied delta of the position is far greater than the tiny premium suggests. For management: if the underlying approaches a wing strike, the iron condor can be converted to a long strangle by buying back the short side — paying to defend rather than taking maximum loss.

The iron condor's greek profile over time is the central management challenge. At entry, the position looks safe: short strikes are far from the stock, premium collected is defined, and all four legs have small individual greeks. But as time passes and expiration approaches, the inner short strikes develop sharply increasing gamma if the stock drifts toward them — the same gamma acceleration that makes ATM options dangerous near expiration. A condor sold 45 days out with the stock 10% away from each short strike can suddenly have extreme negative gamma if the stock moves 8% in one direction with three weeks left. Passarelli calls this the "turkey" scenario: the position looks calm and profitable until an unexpected move turns the mild theta earner into a gamma bomb. Active management — closing condors when 50–80% of the maximum profit has been collected, rather than holding to expiration — avoids the final-week gamma explosion.

Strike selection and wing width are the two structural decisions that determine an iron condor's quality. Setting strikes using the options market's own implied volatility — choosing short strikes at one standard deviation away from the current price — ensures the probability of expiring inside the tent is roughly 68%. Wing width determines the capital at risk: narrow wings collect more credit relative to the spread width, but offer less cushion if the stock drifts toward the short strike. The DJX condor Passarelli describes had its break-evens set so far from the market (based on the low-volatility conditions of 2007) that the position seemed immune to ordinary market movement — until the 2008 crisis, which was not ordinary, broke through both wings simultaneously. The lesson: IV used to select strikes reflects only recent volatility history; it does not account for tail risks that are possible but have not occurred in the lookback window.

The butterfly's unique advantage over the iron condor is its concentration of theta at a single price target. A butterfly centered on $70 for a stock currently at $68 is a low-cost directional bet that the stock will end near $70 at expiration; the maximum profit is earned only within a narrow band around the body strike. As the stock moves toward the body, the butterfly's theta increases dramatically because the short ATM options are now decaying at maximum speed. The butterfly is also self-liquidating: the risk profile changes fastest in the final two weeks, so holding to expiration captures the most time value but also exposes the trader to pin risk and last-day gamma instability. Many professional traders prefer to close butterflies with 7–10 days remaining, capturing most of the theoretical profit without riding the binary outcome of the final trading session.

Chapter 11: Calendar and Diagonal Spreads

A calendar spread — also called a time spread or horizontal spread — buys a longer-dated option and sells a shorter-dated option at the same strike. The position profits when the front-month option decays faster than the back-month option, and when implied volatility rises. It is fundamentally a short-gamma, long-vega position — the opposite of a covered call.

BBBY calendar spread: Bed Bath & Beyond trading at $35; sell the near-month 35 call, buy the two-month 35 call. The net debit is $0.80. At expiration of the front month, if BBBY is still at $35, the front-month call expires worthless and the back-month call retains significant value — the position profits from the differential decay rates. If BBBY moves sharply in either direction, both options move ITM or OTM, the differential narrows, and the spread loses money. This illustrates the paradox: a long calendar has negative gamma (dislikes the stock moving) yet positive vega (benefits from rising IV) — two characteristics that usually travel together are here separated.

Passarelli warns: short calendars are less intuitive and require very specific volatility views to justify the position. A double calendar sells both a call calendar and a put calendar simultaneously, earning income from two strikes; the MMM double calendar example shows this creates a wider profitability zone than a single calendar.

A diagonal spread is a calendar spread with different strikes — buy a further-dated option at one strike, sell a shorter-dated option at a different strike. The Apple diagonal (buy back-month call at one strike, sell front-month call at a higher strike) adds a directional bias to the time trade. A double diagonal — JPMorgan example — combines a call diagonal with a put diagonal to create a wide income zone that profits from both moderate directional movement and time decay.

Part III — Volatility

Chapter 12: Delta-Neutral Trading — Implied Volatility

Direction-neutral means having no directional view; direction-indifferent means having a view on volatility regardless of direction. A delta-neutral position — achieved by combining long options with short stock (or long stock) in the exact ratio of the option's delta — is direction-indifferent at the moment of entry. But gamma will make the position directional as the stock moves: a long-call, short-stock delta-neutral position becomes net long delta as the stock rises (gamma helps the call) and net short delta as the stock falls.

Portfolio margining — available to qualifying traders — calculates margin on the net risk of a portfolio rather than on each position individually. It dramatically reduced the capital required to run delta-neutral books, opening professional-style volatility trading to larger retail accounts. The core trade in this chapter: buying options when IV is low (or expected to rise) and selling stock against them to remain delta-neutral, profiting from the IV expansion without taking directional risk.

The IV rush and crush cycle follows a predictable arc around scheduled events. Four to six weeks before earnings, IV begins rising as traders buy options to position for the expected move. The week before earnings, IV accelerates sharply — sometimes doubling from the prior-month baseline. On the announcement date, with the event resolved, IV collapses to its normal range regardless of what the stock does. A stock that beats earnings and gaps up 8% can still produce a loss for a long straddle holder if the IV collapse is large enough to more than offset the favorable move.

Passarelli quantifies this for Susie Seller's earnings straddle: she sold the straddle at 38% IV collecting $1,150 in theoretical value. After the stock moved modestly and IV collapsed to 22%, the straddle was worth $350 — a $800 gain. Had the stock moved more aggressively, the gamma loss would have offset more of the vega gain; had it moved less, the full $1,150 would have been captured. Bobby Buyer on the other side — long the straddle — profited $400 from the IV rush in the days before the announcement, before the stock even moved. The patience lesson: Bobby bought his options four weeks early when IV was 2 points cheaper, saving $200 in premium; traders who rush in two months early pay a much steeper cost for weeks of IV buildup with minimal reward.

Establishing a delta-neutral position requires calculating the hedge ratio and executing both legs simultaneously. If a trader buys 10 calls with 0.60 delta, the position carries +600 deltas — the equivalent of being long 600 shares. To neutralize, the trader shorts 600 shares against the calls. The resulting position has near-zero delta at the moment of entry, but gamma will immediately begin shifting the delta as the stock moves. Passarelli's rule of thumb: recalibrate the hedge after every $1–$2 move in the underlying for near-term ATM options, or after every 0.05 change in position delta. Smaller hedging intervals capture more realized volatility profit but generate more transaction costs — the optimal interval depends on the bid-ask spread of the underlying and the gamma of the position. For illiquid underlyings with wide spreads, over-hedging destroys the profitability of an otherwise sound vol trade.

The timing of IV-rush trades relative to earnings cycles is as important as strike selection. Entering a delta-neutral long options position six weeks before earnings, when IV is still near its baseline, captures the full buildup — but pays six weeks of theta before the event occurs. Entering two weeks before earnings pays a smaller theta bill but buys into an already-inflated IV, reducing the potential vega gain. Passarelli's optimal window: three to four weeks before earnings, when IV has begun rising from its baseline but has not yet accelerated into the final-week spike. The ideal entry is confirmed by a volatility chart: look for IV starting to inflect upward from a low relative to the preceding three-month range, with the earnings date visible on the calendar. This timing discipline is what separates profitable earnings vol traders from traders who buy options in the final days before the announcement and wonder why they lose money even when the stock moves.

Chapter 13: Delta-Neutral Trading — Realized Volatility

Where Chapter 12 traded implied volatility, this chapter trades realized volatility — how much the stock actually moves, regardless of IV. Gamma scalping is the mechanism: maintain a delta-neutral position in a long-gamma option, and every time the stock moves a meaningful amount, re-hedge. When the stock rises, the long call's delta has increased (gamma); sell stock to reduce back to delta-neutral, locking in a stock profit. When the stock falls, the call's delta has decreased; buy stock to re-hedge, again locking in a stock profit.

Harry's 7-day gamma scalping journal: Harry buys one ATM call with delta 0.50 and shorts 50 shares against it — delta-neutral on Day 1. Day 2: stock rises $2; call delta is now 0.55 — Harry sells 5 shares at the higher price to re-hedge. Day 3: stock falls $3; delta falls back — Harry buys shares at a lower price. By Day 7, even if the stock ends near where it started, Harry has locked in a series of small stock-to-option cross-hedge profits. The cumulative gain exceeds the theta cost of the long call — Harry profits because realized vol exceeded implied vol. Mary's mirror journal shows the short-gamma perspective: identical moves produce identical losses on the short side, since every re-hedge forces buying high and selling low.

The fundamental equation of gamma scalping: if realized volatility exceeds implied volatility over the life of the position, the long-gamma trader profits. If implied exceeds realized, the short-gamma seller wins. The 25% IV used to price Harry's options encodes a specific gamma/theta ratio: 2.80 gamma units per 0.50 theta per day — a 5.6× ratio. At 50% IV, the ratio inverts: 1.40 gamma per 0.90 theta. Higher IV makes gamma more expensive in theta terms. This benchmark lets traders compare IV levels to assess whether long-gamma or short-gamma has the better risk-adjusted edge.

Chapter 14: Studying Volatility Charts

Passarelli identifies nine recurring volatility chart patterns that signal tradeable conditions. Diagnosing these patterns before entering a position determines whether the trade setup has edge or is merely a coin flip.

Exhibit 14.1-14.9[view] Exhibit 14.5[view] Exhibit 14.8[view]

The nine chart patterns span the full range of IV/HV relationships: each one suggests a different options strategy and a different expected outcome. Passarelli uses 30-day realized vol and current IV as the two lines on the chart — when they converge, diverge, spike, or plateau, each configuration tells a different story.

The nine patterns are: (1) IV above HV — options are expensive relative to how the stock has actually moved; sell volatility. (2) IV below HV — options are cheap; buy volatility. (3) IV at HV — fairly priced; neither side has an obvious vol edge. (4) Rising IV with rising stock — bullish and fearful simultaneously, unusual; IV usually falls as stocks rise. (5) IV mesa — prolonged elevated IV plateau; implies a sustained period of option overpricing if mean reversion holds. (6) IV spike followed by crush — the earnings pattern; sell the straddle before the spike peaks. (7) HV spike above IV — the stock is moving more than the options predicted; long gamma has edge. (8) Convergence — IV and HV coming together; directional trades become cleaner as vol premium compresses. (9) Divergence — IV and HV separating; a widening gap suggests an impending vol mean reversion.

The most actionable pattern in the book is the IV spike followed by crush — the earnings pattern. A stock with normally 20% IV will see IV spike to 40–50% in the weeks before an earnings report, then collapse back to 20% the day after. Selling a straddle or iron condor at the IV peak — a few days before the announcement — captures that collapse. The catch: the stock may also make a large move, and the direction-indifferent premium seller must size the position such that the IV crush profit exceeds the directional loss on any reasonable stock move. This is why Passarelli's Susie Seller collects $1,150 of theoretical premium but only books $800 — the stock moved moderately, limiting the crushing effect on the straddle's value even as IV fell from 38% to 22%.

The IV mesa — a prolonged plateau of elevated IV — is the rarest pattern and the most valuable to identify. When a stock maintains abnormally high IV for weeks without a corresponding level of realized movement, it signals persistent option overpricing: the market is pricing in a risk that isn't materializing. Short volatility traders who identify a mesa and sell options repeatedly into it — collecting elevated theta with controlled gamma exposure — can generate outsized returns if the mesa eventually resolves back to normal IV. The risk: the mesa exists because something specific is holding IV elevated (merger uncertainty, regulatory decision, patent litigation), and if that event triggers, the gap between IV and HV closes violently in the wrong direction.

Reading a vol chart requires knowing which time frame to use for the HV calculation. Passarelli's standard is 30-day realized vol (the annualized standard deviation of the past 30 trading days' daily returns) as the primary HV line. A 10-day HV line can be added as a faster-responding signal: when it spikes above the 30-day HV and both exceed IV, the stock has been moving far more than the options market expected — a classic long-gamma setup. When 10-day HV falls sharply below IV, the stock has gone quiet even as options remain expensive — the short vol setup. The three-line chart (IV, 30-day HV, 10-day HV) provides more nuanced information than a simple IV-vs-HV view, revealing whether a vol signal is persistent or just a momentary blip in realized movement. Passarelli emphasizes that vol chart analysis is a skill that improves only through observation — looking at hundreds of charts for the same name across different market conditions is the only way to calibrate what "high" and "low" truly mean for a specific underlying.

Translating a vol chart pattern into a trade requires matching the specific pattern to the appropriate strategy. When IV is above HV (pattern 1 — sell vol): sell a straddle or iron condor — short gamma, positive theta, short vega. When IV is below HV (pattern 2 — buy vol): buy a straddle or back spread — long gamma, negative theta, long vega. When the term structure is inverted (near-month IV above back-month): sell calendar spreads — short vega in the front, long in the back. When the term structure is steep (near-month IV well below back-month): buy calendar spreads — long vega in the back month, short in front. Each pattern maps cleanly to a defined position structure — the vol chart is not just an indicator but a trade blueprint. The remaining judgment calls are position size (based on the magnitude of the IV divergence), timing (how long has the divergence persisted, and is it widening or narrowing?), and stop-loss (at what IV level does the thesis break down?).

Passarelli's nine patterns can be further sorted into three actionable categories: sell vol (IV above HV, IV mesa, IV spike without stock movement), buy vol (IV below HV, HV spike above IV, pre-event IV underpricing), and wait (IV equal to HV, IV and HV converging, divergence too small to trade net of transaction costs). The waiting category is as important as the other two — most experienced vol traders are only actively positioned for two or three of the nine patterns in their primary underlyings at any given time, and they stay entirely flat on the others. Forcing a trade into a "convergence" or "fair pricing" environment adds cost without edge — the equivalent of paying for insurance you don't need. Passarelli's repeated advice: vol trading is patient trading.

The position is long gamma, short theta, and long vega: it wants the stock to move, time to pass slowly, and IV to rise.

The short straddle — John's XYZ position — collects a large premium in exchange for unlimited risk in both directions and the ever-present threat of pin risk. A synthetic straddle replaces one leg with a stock position. A long call plus short stock at the correct delta ratio recreates the P&L of a long straddle at lower initial premium — but it requires more active management as the delta shifts. A strangle purchases (or sells) OTM options on both sides: buy an OTM call and an OTM put. The strangle is cheaper than a straddle — OTM options carry less premium — but requires a larger move to profit. The break-even points are further from the current price, making the short strangle more forgiving of small moves but still exposed to large ones.

Managing the short straddle from a greek perspective requires close attention to delta drift. At entry, John's XYZ short straddle has delta near zero — the short call and short put deltas roughly cancel. But as the stock moves, gamma accumulates delta in the direction of the move: if XYZ rises $5, the short call's delta grows while the short put's delta shrinks, giving the position significant negative delta (net bearish exposure). Left unmanaged, this directional drift can turn a theta trade into an unintended directional bet. Professional short straddle managers typically re-delta-hedge periodically — buying or selling small amounts of the underlying to return the position to near-zero delta — treating the straddle as two separate risk problems (directional risk and vol risk) rather than one monolithic position.

The long straddle's worst enemy is not a market that doesn't move — it is a market that moves just a little, repeatedly, in both directions. Each small move adds some gamma-scalping profit, but the cumulative theta loss from holding the position overwhelms those small gains if the moves are insufficient. Passarelli's standard: a long straddle needs to experience at least one move larger than the break-even (combined premium paid) before it can be profitable at expiration. Options traders commonly underestimate the magnitude of move required — a $3 straddle on a $50 stock needs a 6% single- direction move just to break even, which occurs less often than intuition suggests. The exit discipline: if the expected catalyst has passed and the stock has not moved sufficiently, sell the straddle immediately rather than holding through additional theta decay in hope of a second chance.

A strangle versus straddle decision comes down to one question: how large is the expected move? If the trader expects a modest move barely beyond the straddle's break-even range, the straddle is better — it breaks even sooner and gains faster per dollar of move because both legs are ATM. If the trader expects a very large move — 15–20% or more — the strangle can be superior because the OTM options have higher leverage once the stock blows through the short strike of an equivalent straddle. The strangle also carries lower theta per day (OTM options decay more slowly) and lower vega, making it less sensitive to IV swings. For earnings trades where the expected move is already priced in to ATM options, the strangle can be cheaper on a risk-adjusted basis when the trader believes the actual move will substantially exceed the implied move — accepting the wider break-even in exchange for a lower premium cost.

Chapter 16: Ratio Spreads and Complex Spreads

A ratio spread sells more options than it buys: buy 1, sell 2 (or buy 10, sell 20) — a 1×2 ratio. The net delta and vega profile is intermediate: the position benefits from moderate moves toward the short strikes but carries extra short gamma beyond the hedge. A backspread is the opposite: sell fewer options than you buy (e.g., sell 1, buy 2). Backspreads have long gamma and benefit from large moves in either direction; they lose in the middle zone.

The ratio spread exploits the shape of the gamma curve in a specific directional range. The backspread inverts this: sell 1 option, buy 2 further-out options, typically for a small debit or credit. A call backspread profits from a very large move up or from the stock staying flat (both legs decay, but the short leg decays faster if ATM), while suffering maximum loss if the stock parks near the long strike at expiration. Backspreads are best entered when IV is low — cheap options make the long legs cost less relative to the premium collected on the short — and closed if IV rises before the expected big move.

The striking insight: a position that appears delta-neutral at the aggregate level may have strongly positive gamma in the front month and negative gamma in back months. This is not a contradiction — it is a term-structure gamma bet, a view that short-term realized vol will differ from long-term IV. Passarelli's discipline was to monitor the aggregate table daily, not individual legs, and to manage the greek exposure of the book as a whole rather than trying to manage each trade independently. For retail traders, the lesson scales down perfectly: keeping a simple Greek summary table for all open positions — even a handwritten one — prevents the common error of "forgetting" the cumulative exposure that individual trades have built up.

Multiple-class risk — correlations across different underlying names — is the hidden danger in sophisticated volatility portfolios. A trader who is long delta-neutral straddles in two correlated stocks (say, two large banks) assumes the vol exposures will be independent. But correlated underlyings can both spike in vol simultaneously (as they typically do in market stress), doubling the vega exposure; they can also diverge in realized movement, creating a situation where one straddle profits from gamma scalping while the other bleeds theta. Passarelli's point is structural: the only way to manage multiple-class risk is to calculate a position-level greek table across all names, applying appropriate correlation assumptions, before committing capital. Treating each position as isolated — the natural tendency of traders who think "this is just my IBM position and this is just my GS position" — can create a portfolio-level gamma and vega concentration that is invisible until it bites.

Option B: roll the short call from $50 to $55 — extend the range but accept more time and more risk. Option C: buy an additional call above $50 to cap the loss on the upside — converts the call spread into a butterfly. Option D: buy stock or a single call to delta-hedge — makes the overall position directionally positive to offset the call spread's negative delta. Each adjustment reflects a different updated forecast for Halliburton's movement and a different tolerance for the resulting risk.

The three-step learning process — study, paper trade, real money — is Passarelli's organizing principle for the entire book's application. Paper trading is not a compromise for beginners; it is a mandatory verification step even for experienced traders entering a new strategy. The greeks behave in ways that consistently surprise traders until they have personally experienced the scenarios: the vega crush on a long straddle even when the stock moved as expected; the pin risk anxiety at expiration Friday close; the gamma explosion that wipes out five weeks of theta income in a single session. Reading about these outcomes intellectually is not the same as watching them happen in a paper account — the emotional experience of seeing a position go wrong creates the gut-level understanding that prevents the same mistake with real money. Passarelli's specific recommendation: paper trade the same strategy for at least one full earnings cycle, including the full IV rush and crush sequence, before committing real capital to vol-based earnings trades. The pattern cannot be understood from a single observation — it must be internalized across multiple repetitions until the greek signals become automatic.

The Open Interest Signal

Open interest spikes in deep ITM calls before an ex-dividend date are one of the most reliable signals in listed options: a sudden surge in volume for calls that are trading at parity signals dividend-play traders are exercising to capture tomorrow's dividend. The mirror signal: if you are short those same calls, you will almost certainly be assigned. Understanding this — and building it into a checklist before selling ITM calls in dividend-paying stocks — prevents the surprise of a Monday morning assignment notice for a trade that appeared safe on Friday.

Trading & Investing Lessons

The vol-selling decision framework: five checkpoints before entering any short-volatility position. Each step filters for trades with genuine statistical edge.

Delta as Portfolio Exposure

Every position — stocks, options, spreads — can be expressed in delta terms. A portfolio with net delta of +3,000 is equivalent to being long 3,000 shares of the underlying. Institutional traders manage total portfolio delta daily; retail traders who track only individual positions miss the aggregate directional risk they've accumulated. Greeks unify the language of portfolio risk across all strategies.

Volatility Has a Price — Know If You're Overpaying

Comparing IV to HV before every options trade tells you whether the market is pricing fear or complacency. Buying options when IV is at 52-week lows and selling when IV is at 52-week highs is not market timing — it is simply purchasing insurance when it is cheap and selling it when it is expensive. The VIX chart is the simplest version of this for S&P 500 traders.

Gamma-Theta is the Risk-Reward Unit

Every long options position is a bet that the stock will move more than the theta cost. Every short options position bets the stock will move less. Before entering a long straddle on earnings, calculate the breakeven daily move needed to offset theta; if the stock rarely moves that much, selling the straddle has edge. This gamma-theta framing turns vague intuition about volatility into a quantitative decision process.

The Vol Surface as a Market Map

Every stock has a volatility surface: IV plotted across all available strikes and expirations simultaneously. Reading the surface reveals where the market expects risk to be concentrated — high IV OTM puts signal crash fear; elevated near-term IV versus flat back-month IV signals a near-term event. Professional traders monitor the shape of the surface as closely as they monitor the stock's price. When the surface changes shape — near-term IV spikes while back-month stays flat, or OTM calls suddenly carry higher IV than OTM puts — it signals a shift in the market's collective fear or greed that may not yet be reflected in the stock price itself.

Spreads as Position Management

Selling a spread against an existing long option is not just a new trade — it is a position management decision. Converting a long call into a bull call spread by selling a higher strike eliminates vega exposure, reduces theta cost, and partially monetizes a gain before expiration. Every spread decision is implicitly a decision about which greeks to keep and which to hedge away.

IV Crush is a Tax on Directional Options Buyers

Buying calls before earnings expecting a stock to gap up, only to watch the option lose value despite the rise, is one of the most common beginner losses. The reason is IV crush: the surge in IV before the event inflated the option's premium; once the event passes, IV collapses and erases the time value. The lesson: account for the post-event IV level, not just the directional move, when estimating option P&L after earnings.

Calendar Spreads as a Volatility Lens on Time

Every calendar spread is a bet that the current volatility term structure will change in your favor. When front-month IV is unusually high relative to back-month IV, short-calendar positions (sell back, buy front) benefit from term structure normalization. Term structure analysis applies to all assets: futures curves, bond yield curves, and options term structures all reveal the market's current assumptions about uncertainty over time.

Key Takeaways

Greeks are the Language of Options Risk

Delta, gamma, theta, vega, and rho are not optional theory — they are the only way to precisely describe how an options position will behave across a range of outcomes. A trader who cannot explain every position in greek terms does not fully understand the risk they are carrying.

Gamma and Theta Always Trade Off

There is no position that is simultaneously long gamma and long theta — these are opposite sides of the same trade. Income-seeking strategies (short gamma) pay for premium collection with exposure to large moves; protection-seeking strategies (long gamma) pay a daily theta cost for the right to benefit from those large moves. The ratio between gamma and theta is set by implied volatility: higher IV means you pay more theta per unit of gamma because the market is pricing in greater expected movement. This is why high-IV environments favor selling options and low-IV environments favor buying options — not as a market-timing rule, but as a statement about which side is paying a fair price for the risk they are accepting.

Moneyness Determines the Greek Profile — Choose Deliberately

Choosing between an ITM, ATM, or OTM option for the same directional view is not a minor detail — it is a fundamentally different trade. The ATM option has the highest gamma, highest theta, highest vega, and a delta near 0.50. The deep ITM option has a delta near 1.00 but minimal gamma, theta, and vega — it behaves like stock ownership with a capped downside. The OTM option has the lowest premium and delta but the same relative gamma and vega per dollar as ATM — it is the highest- leverage, highest-risk choice. Passarelli's greek matrices showing Disney and QQQ options across strikes make this comparison concrete: side-by-side numbers replace vague intuitions about "aggressive" versus "conservative" with precise risk measurement.

All Options Trades Are Volatility Trades

Even a simple directional call purchase is a bet on volatility: the realized vol of the stock must justify the IV embedded in the premium. A directional trader who ignores IV is systematically overpaying when fear is elevated and underpaying when complacency rules.

ATM Options Have the Most Time Value Per Dollar

ATM options have the highest theta, highest gamma, and highest vega in absolute terms. They are the most sensitive to the passage of time and to volatility changes. Moving OTM reduces the premium paid but also reduces all the greeks — the leverage profile changes dramatically.

The "Would I Do It Now?" Rule Prevents Cognitive Traps

Holding a losing short option position because "I already have the premium collected" is a sunk-cost trap. Re-underwrite every open position as if you had no position; if you would not enter today, close the trade.

Volatility Mean-Reverts — But Not on a Schedule

IV at extremes tends to revert toward its historical average, but can stay elevated or depressed longer than most traders expect. Mean-reversion trades require defined risk; open-ended positions on volatility can survive being right on direction but wrong on timing. The most dangerous IV trade is a naked short position entered when IV is elevated: if IV continues rising before it eventually falls, the short option holder faces both mark-to-market losses and an accelerating delta risk that can become unmanageable. The professional solution is always defined-risk structures — iron condors, credit spreads, butterflies — that cap the worst-case loss regardless of how long the mean-reversion takes.

Spreads Define Risk and Change Greek Exposure

Every spread decision is a decision about which greeks to keep and which to hedge away. Buying a vertical spread versus buying an outright call is not just a premium decision — it changes gamma, theta, and vega dramatically and alters the range of profitable outcomes.

Paper Trading is Mandatory Before Real Money

Options positions behave in unexpected ways until you've lived through earnings IV crush, early assignment on covered calls, and the acceleration of theta in the final week. Paper trading with real market data builds intuition before real capital is at risk. The goal of paper trading is not to prove that a strategy "works" — it is to internalize the greek dynamics so that when you see your first real position in IV crush, or your first short iron condor being tested at the wing, you have a reference point for how it should behave and what the correct response is. Passarelli's three-step framework — Study, Paper Trade, Showtime — is the minimum viable path to informed options trading. Skipping the paper-trade step is the single most common cause of avoidable losses for new options participants.

Contrarian Ideas

Delta-Neutral Hides Gamma Risk

A position with zero net delta at the moment of entry can become violently directional within hours. Gamma is the force that converts a "neutral" position into a directional one. A short straddle with zero delta at entry will be long 50 deltas if the stock rallies $1 (negative gamma makes calls more valuable, increasing the net delta exposure). Retail traders who see "delta neutral" on their option chain display and conclude they have no directional risk are reading one snapshot of a dynamic system. Managing a delta-neutral position means continuously monitoring and re-hedging as gamma moves the delta — not a one-time setup.

Neither Buying Nor Selling Options Has Systematic Edge

The conventional retail narrative is that "selling options is better" because premium sellers consistently win. Passarelli's Fair Game argument challenges this: in an efficient market, option prices reflect the correct expected value of future volatility. Neither side has a statistical edge over time; the edge belongs to whoever manages positions more skillfully.

A Directional View Is Not Enough to Trade Options Profitably

Being right about the direction of a stock's move does not guarantee profit from a long option position. IV crush, excessive premium relative to the actual move, and poor timing can all turn a correct directional call into a losing trade. Options require a view on both direction and volatility.

The Calendar Spread's Hidden Paradox

Long calendar spreads are short gamma but long vega — two greek signs that normally travel together in an outright long option but are here decoupled. A trader who uses a calendar spread as a cheap substitute for an outright long option will be surprised when a large stock move (which would profit the outright option holder) damages the calendar spread. Understanding this paradox prevents misapplication of the strategy: calendar spreads are for traders with a specific view on volatility term structure, not for traders who simply want a cheaper version of a directional bet.

Deep ITM Calls Sometimes Make More Sense Than Shares

Traders assume that buying stock is the "conservative" alternative to buying calls. Deep ITM calls with delta near 0.90 offer nearly identical directional exposure to stock while capping the maximum loss at the premium paid — effectively buying stock with a guaranteed stop-loss built in. For the same capital, you can own more "stock equivalent" exposure with defined risk.

Calendar Spreads Are Primarily Volatility Trades

Most retail traders use calendar spreads as "cheap income" plays. Passarelli reframes them as volatility term structure bets: the key decision is whether front-month IV is overpriced relative to back-month IV, not whether the stock will stay flat. This reframe changes how to select strikes, expirations, and entry timing.

Delta-Neutral Is Not Market-Risk-Free

Achieving delta-neutrality does not eliminate directional risk — it only eliminates it at the current moment. Gamma will re-introduce directional risk as the stock moves, and a "delta-neutral" multi-stock book carries correlation risk that is invisible in the individual position's greek readout.

The Three-Step Mastery Process

Study → Paper Trade → Real Money ("Showtime"). Study means understanding the greek behavior of every strategy before market hours. Paper trading means running real trades with real market prices but no real money — the only way to experience IV crush, pin risk, and assignment before they cost you real capital. The Vol-Selling Decision Framework

Before selling volatility: (1) Compare current IV to HV — is IV elevated? (2) Check IV percentile relative to its own 52-week range. (3) Assess the catalyst calendar — is there a known event that will drive IV higher before expiration? (4) Map the break-evens to standard deviation ranges. (5) Apply the "Would I Do It Now?" rule at each management point.

Calendar Spread Entry Filter

Before entering a calendar spread, analyze the term structure: (1) Is front-month IV higher than back-month IV? (The spread profits from this differential compressing.) (2) Is there an event in the front month that is inflating its IV? (3) What is the expected magnitude of the stock's move relative to the calendar's profit range? A calendar spread entered when the term structure is flat or inverted (back month IV higher than front month) starts at a structural disadvantage.

What Didn't Fit

The Visteon Spinoff and Three Classes of Ford Options

In the summer of 2000, Ford spun off its Visteon auto-parts subsidiary in a complex transaction that created three distinct classes of Ford options simultaneously. The standard "F" class represented 100 shares of new Ford stock. The "XFO" class represented 100 shares of Ford plus $2,000 cash plus additional consideration. The "FOD" class represented 100 shares of new Ford plus 13 shares of Visteon plus $2,001.24. This real-world example illustrates that exchange-listed options are not always the clean 100-share instruments assumed in textbooks — corporate actions create non-standard contracts that persist until expiration. Traders must always verify contract specifications before assuming standard terms.

The GM Volatility Term Structure Exhibit

General Motors options provided Passarelli with a striking term-structure exhibit showing near-month IV significantly elevated above longer-dated IV — a common pattern before earnings or restructuring announcements. The exhibit makes visible what the numbers alone cannot convey: the front-month IV spike is so sharp relative to the back month that the term structure resembles a cliff rather than a slope. Calendar spread traders who identified this pattern before GM's earnings had a structural advantage: the front-month short was collecting 8–10 points of IV above the back-month long, creating an immediate edge even if the underlying moved modestly. This pattern recurs across earnings season every quarter.

The Theta Acceleration Final-Week Math

Passarelli presents a specific exhibit showing ATM option theta in the final 10 trading days: the decay is not linear but exponential. With 10 days remaining, an ATM option might lose $0.015 per day in time value. With 5 days remaining, the same option loses $0.022 per day. With 2 days remaining, it loses $0.030 per day. By the final day, theta can be three to four times the rate of day 10. This acceleration is the reason short-option sellers prefer near-expiration options: the theta income is most efficient in the final 7–10 days. It is also why long-option holders who are slightly OTM with a week left face a brutal uphill battle — the theta clock is running fastest precisely when the option needs the stock to move most urgently. The practical rule: avoid holding long options into the final week unless you have a specific event catalyst that justifies the accelerating decay cost.

The Citigroup Vertical Skew Exhibit

The Citigroup volatility skew exhibit — OTM puts with IV of 35% versus OTM calls at 22% — captures the asymmetric fear premium baked into financial stock options after the 2008 crisis. Put buyers had learned an expensive lesson: tail risk in large banks was not theoretical, and they were willing to pay significantly elevated premiums for downside protection. The slope of this skew tells a story about the market's collective memory of the financial crisis and its ongoing expectation of non-normal distribution in financial stocks. For traders, the key implication: selling OTM puts in financial stocks carries more tail risk than the standard delta alone suggests, because the high skew reflects genuine fear of discontinuous downside moves.

Passarelli's Ford Motor Floor Position

The most vivid illustration of multi-greek portfolio management in the book is Passarelli's own Ford Motor options book from his market-maker days. The position spanned multiple expirations (near-month, next-month, and LEAPS), with both calls and puts, long and short. The exhibit of the full greek summary by expiration showed that the book was net delta-neutral overall — but the breakdown by expiration revealed something important: positive gamma and negative theta in the near month (long-gamma bias), but negative gamma and positive theta in the back months (short-gamma bias). This meant the book behaved like a long straddle on a day-by-day basis for the near expiration but like a short straddle for the longer-dated positions. A large one-day move in Ford stock would simultaneously help the near-month Greeks and hurt the back-month Greeks — partially self-hedging, but only partially. Managing this kind of book required calculating the net effect of each scenario on the entire portfolio, not just looking at any single position in isolation. This is why institutions run "scenario analysis" rather than individual trade review — the interaction between positions is where the real risk lives.

The Standard Deviation Formula for Iron Condor Strikes

Passarelli provides a practical formula for setting iron condor break-even targets using the options market's own implied volatility: multiply the stock price by IV, divide by the square root of 252 (for a one-day move) or scale by the square root of days to expiration for multi-day targets. This gives a one-standard-deviation range — roughly 68% probability the stock stays within it. Setting condor wings at one standard deviation produces a high probability of profit (about 68%) but a narrow maximum gain zone; two standard deviations produces lower probability but more room to be wrong. The SPX iron condor example shows how this math translates to specific strike selection.

Conversions, Reversals, and the Jelly Roll

The jelly roll — combining a conversion in one expiration with a reversal in another — is a pure interest-rate instrument used by institutions to roll positions forward in time while locking in financing rates. Retail traders rarely execute jelly rolls, but understanding them illuminates why put-call parity violations persist across expirations: the jelly roll arbitrage keeps term structure relationships aligned. Passarelli's conversion/reversal exhibits show rho as the dominant greek in these positions — the entire profit (or loss) of a conversion is driven by interest rates, not by any movement in the underlying stock or its volatility. This reveals rho's importance in institutional options books, where LEAPS and long-dated positions make interest rate sensitivity a first-order risk.
