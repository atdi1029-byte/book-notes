# The Taylor Trading Technique — Summary
## By George Douglass Taylor (originally published ~1950, Traders Press edition with Raschke/Angell commentaries)

### What This Book Is
A short (76 pages of original text) manual for short-term swing trading using a hand-kept ledger ("the Book") that tracks daily OHLC prices and classifies each day as a Buy Day, Sell Day, or Short Sale Day in a repeating 3-day cycle. Taylor developed this method from decades of observing grain futures and stocks in the 1930s-1950s.

### The Core Idea: The 3-Day Cycle
Markets move in a repeating rhythm of approximately 3 days up followed by 1-2 days of correction. This rhythm is driven by the accumulation-distribution activities of large operators/manipulators who buy on the way up and sell/short on the way down in a predictable pattern. Taylor's method tracks this cycle mechanically through a tabular "book" that records prices and classifies trading days.

### The Three Day Types
1. **Buy Day (Day 1)**: Look for support at or near the previous day's low. Buy when the low is made FIRST (early in the session). Expect a rally.
2. **Sell Day (Day 2)**: Expect the rally to continue and penetrate the Buying Day's high. Sell your long position at or through this penetration.
3. **Short Sale Day (Day 3)**: The rally's final push. Expect price to penetrate the Selling Day's high. Short sell at or through this penetration, then expect a decline back to a new Buy Day.

### Key Statistical Findings (from Yearly Plates)
- The 3-day cycle produces approximately 100 trading opportunities per year in each direction (long and short)
- Gain day swings: ~88-89% of the time
- Loss day swings: ~11-12% of the time
- Selling Objectives (penetrations) achieved ~55-66% of the time
- Buying Day Low Violations occur ~35% of the time
- Total trading points captured are 10-35x greater than buy-and-hold returns over the same period

### The Signal Marks (X and ✓)
- **X** = the trading objective (high or low) was made FIRST in the session — favorable, trade is quick
- **✓** = the objective was made LAST — less favorable, requires watching the entire session
- These marks are the most important trend indicators in the system

### Key Trading Rules
1. **Never average a loss** — sell out immediately if wrong, buy back when right
2. **Take small, quick profits** — don't hold for what you think the market "should" do
3. **Trade what the market does, not what you think it should do**
4. **Never make a trade unless it favors your play** — better to sit out an entire session
5. **The daily trader must be in complete command of his cash at all times**
6. **Cover short sales on the same day when the spread is wide**
7. **Do not carry losing positions overnight** (daily method)

### The Book's Columns
- **D (Decline)**: Spread from Short Sale Day high to Buy Day low — measures short sale opportunities
- **R (Rally)**: Spread from Buy Day low to Sell Day high — measures buying opportunities
- **BH (Buy Day High)**: Penetrations of Short Sale Day high on Buy Days
- **BU (Buy Under)**: How far price sold under previous day's low on Buy Days
- **BV (Buy Violation)**: Violation of the Buying Day Low — a "false move" that is usually recovered
- **T-L (Three Day)**: Spread from Buy Day low to Short Sale Day high — total 3-day swing

### Two Trading Methods
1. **Daily Trading**: Trade each day according to its specific Objective (Buy, Sell, or Short Sale). Faster, requires tape reading skills, smaller position sizes.
2. **Three Day Method**: Buy on Buy Day and hold until Short Sale Day. Simpler, more mechanical, requires more capital to ride through Violations, but captures larger swings.

### Linda Bradford Raschke's Key Insights
- Read Chapter XV first, skip Chapters 2-4
- Think in CONCEPTS not specifics — Taylor's labels are just a systematic way of classifying days
- The core concept is: how many days up/down is the market from the previous swing low/high
- Most successful mechanical systems work on "in one day, out the next" principle
- Never average down — most destructive trading habit
- Taylor's rules are fundamentally defensive — exit when the market doesn't act "correctly"
- Adapted Taylor's techniques for S&P trading

### Why This Book Matters
Taylor's observation that markets move in predictable short-term cycles driven by large-operator accumulation/distribution is foundational to modern swing trading. His emphasis on mechanical rules, position management, and never averaging losers anticipates the discipline-focused trading psychology that became mainstream decades later. The specific 3-day rhythm he identified remains observable in modern markets, though compressed by algorithmic trading.
