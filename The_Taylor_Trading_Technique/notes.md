# The Taylor Trading Technique — Notes
## By George Douglass Taylor

## Preface (p.4)
- Taylor acknowledges difficulty writing theory about something so practical as speculation
- The method is anticipatory and forecast-based — describing action around one trading objective requires also describing the nearby future "play"
- Repetition is intentional: describing a Buying Point requires explaining what precedes it, leads up to it, and what follows
- Theory and practice must combine — theory alone leaves trader "at sea" in practice, but experience makes the method useful
- Seasoned traders relying on luck/intuition can benefit from acquiring some theory
- NOT hard and fast rules — based on long observation of what GENERALLY happens
- Successful speculation requires flexibility: "a trader must not be so rigid as to stick to a stubborn theory"
- Key insight: knowing how to act when the expected happens puts you in better position when the unexpected happens

## Chapter I: How the Market Trend is Made (pp.7-13)

### The Book Method Defined
- The "Taylor Trading Technique" is a book (ledger) kept in tabular form to anticipate and follow price trends on the tape
- Points out important stopping places for buying and selling
- Wholly concerned with the TECHNICAL side of the market for trading grains, stocks, and commodities
- Particularly adapted to daily trading in grains — more activity, doesn't require great investment
- Aimed at daily traders profiting from rise and fall of daily movements
- Trading size: 1,000-20,000 bushels (grains) or price equivalent in stocks — average trading orders, not limits

### What the Book Method is NOT
- NOT a charting system
- Does NOT depend on daily telegrams, weather reports, open interest, or market commentary
- Does NOT concern itself with commodity economics or broader speculation
- Short-term trader doesn't need to look far ahead

### Core Concept: Objective Points
- Concerned entirely with Objective Points for Buying, Selling, and Short Selling
- These are the Minor Movements of the market
- Trains trader to depend on own judgment — "from his book he will get his own 'tips' on the movement of prices"
- Trader moves with greater assurance than from daily "dope" he reads
- Trader's decision is made BEFORE market opening — no reaching for gossip file
- Must NOT permit outside influence to interfere with Objective Points as they appear on the tape
- Prices on tape will appear CONTRARY to most news — they discount everything

### How Manipulation Creates the Trend
- A grain/stock trader/operator IS essentially a manipulator
- Good manipulator ≠ good trader, but both must understand how buying and selling moves prices
- Before organized buying/selling (pools) was prohibited, a manipulator would trade thousands of shares to move price for his own account
- Even with prohibitions, price action is the same — prices move up and down at regular intervals

### The Accumulation-Distribution Cycle
- Accumulation of grains/commodities is mostly for purposes OTHER than speculation — only small percent is speculative
- The BOOK TRADER is not concerned with WHY accumulation/distribution happens — only interested in when the movement STARTS and which way it goes
- Once a trend starts (Bull or Bear), it continues for an indefinite period with many Trading Areas, rallies, and declines

### The Minor Move Pattern (KEY CONCEPT)
- When conditions are favorable and a move starts from some source:
  - Stock/grain becomes strong and active, price rising
  - Observe this action for a few days — price only goes up so far, stops, reacts a little
  - Seems to arrest the downward movement, starts up again
  - Goes up a few cents or points more, then goes down again
- CRITICAL OBSERVATION: From bottom of minor move to top = about THREE DAYS on average
- The corrective decline = completed in ONE or possibly TWO days
- This creates the 3-day cycle

### How the Manipulator Creates the Pattern
- Manipulator starts by buying stock/grain ABOVE the market by a small distance (4-5 cents/points)
- This clears resting orders of traders who are "hung up" and want to get out
- Relieves market of selling pressure, establishes upward trend
- Rising prices attract public buying (professionals, outside buyers)
- Outside demand fills the stock — operator is compelled to buy MORE than he planned
- When this happens, he SELLS IT SHORT — experienced tape reader would notice the slackening of rise
- Market deprived of buying power starts the decline
- Experienced traders sell out and sell short, further pushing price down
- Operator then buys back, covers shorts, supports the stock
- This process repeats: alternately buying and selling on the way up, always working higher
- Can be weekly or monthly — continues to top of the rise
- This buying and selling keeps the market in check and stabilizes it

### The 3-Day Rhythm (CORE FINDING)
- The book records the rhythm of the market for Buying, Selling, and Short Selling in a very dependable manner
- "It is a fact and the records show it, for many years back that the market has a definite 1-2-3 rhythm"
- Variations: extra beat of 1-2-3-1 at times (4th and 5th days = 1st and 2nd of new cycle)
- Market goes up 1-2-3 days and reacts
- The 4th and 5th figure is the variation — runs an extra day or two, both up and down
- This occurs in BOTH Bull and Bear trends
- These variations occur with surprising regularity
- 3 days = one trading cycle. The 4th and 5th days are the 1st (and 2nd) days of a new cycle
- Day 1 = Buying Day, Day 2 = Selling Day, Day 3 = Short Sale Day

### Trading Day Objectives
- On BUYING DAY: expect support, watch tape to confirm support and rally to start
- On SELLING DAY: expect selling, watch tape for confirmation of selling and reaction to follow
- On SHORT SALE DAY: expect hesitant action of the rise, watch if selling is stronger than buying; if buying is overcome → expect decline

### Tape Reading Skills Required
- Tape reading is difficult, requires long experience
- Real heart of "reading the tape" = detecting concentrated buying/selling and determining trend of prices
- Requires observation, memory, and mental arithmetic of percentages — calculations made INSTANTLY, in your head, not with pad and pencil
- On Buying Day: observe how many points stock is down from the high (Short Sale Day), whether stock opened up/down, whether making high FIRST or low FIRST
- Making high FIRST on Buying Day → look for selling at that point and reaction
- Making low FIRST on Buying Day → look for support buying and rally

### The Three Day Method Alternative
- Trader can either trade each day with its specific Objective OR use the Three Day Method: buying and then selling every third day
- Both approaches work — explained in later chapters

### Classifying Stocks in the Book
- All stocks and grain futures show the same pattern with little variation — reasonable to suppose future will follow past
- Enough similarity to classify them for "repeats" on rallies and declines
- Some swing wider on rallies/declines, make highs/lows at different times
- Trader keeping book on half dozen stocks/grains can "spot" the active wide-swinging ones
- Each trading day is separated and designated for its own expected action
- Eliminate all other actions and influences — concentrate on the specific Objective Point for that day (Buy, Sale, or Short Sale Day)

### On Short Sale Days
- From a low on a Buying Day, thousands of transactions occur before reaching Short Sale Day Objective (three sessions into the future, assuming rally from Buying Day low to Short Sale Day high)
- Strong close on Buying Day, high made FIRST with penetration of Short Sale Day Objective → about 50% of the time in Uptrend Trading Area/Seasonal Upswing
- This is the longest unbroken rally the minor trend can make
- Would NOT put out short sale when high is being made LAST on Short Sale Day — wait for high made FIRST on the Buying Day (starting another cycle)

## Chapter II: How to Make Up a Trading Book (pp.15-18)

### Book Setup
- Head-line with yearly date above first column
- Name of stock/commodity
- First column: month, starting day and date
- Next four columns: Opening, High, Low, Close (daily entries)
- Two grain options or stocks can be kept in each book

### The Cycling Process
- Enter daily prices for 10 days or three (3) swings
- Go back and ring (circle) the lowest price in the third column
- First cycle's low point = Buying Day
- High of day before = Short Sale Day (ring and mark)
- High going back one more day = Selling Day (ring and mark)
- Continue circling in same order back to starting day
- Carry forward in same order: Buy Day, Sell Day, Short Sell Day, Buy Day, Sell Day, Short Sell Day, etc.
- Not concerned with how the book started — whether it was a Buy, Sell, or Short Sale
- Book is ALWAYS kept in this order, never change the continuity
- No lines left open for Sundays or Holidays — market = continuous sessions without a break

### The Book's Physical Form
- Single page plate shows actual size of the "Book" trader carries
- Contains all information and records needed — fits in trader's hand
- Available at stationary stores, horizontally ruled

### Column Definitions
- **D column (Decline)**: spread from Short Sale Day high to Buying Day low — number of points price sold down. Zero if no loss in points.
- **R column (Rally)**: used only where Selling Day High exceeded the High of the day before (the Buying Day High). Also records Short Sale Exceeded points (Short Sale high exceeded Selling Day high). Alternate color inks for summation.
- **BH column (Buy Day High)**: highest point at which it sold on a Buying Day, and number of points sold through Short Sale Day High Point. Zero if it did not sell above this point.
- **BU column (Buy Under)**: number of points on a Buying Day that the price sold under the low of the previous day (Short Sale Day). Zero if it did not sell under.
- **BV (Buy Violation)**: also entered in the BU column — shows spread of decline, amount it sold under the Buying Day Low point. Check mark under it when using same color ink.
- **T-L column**: used for Three Day Method — spread/number of points from Buying Day Low to Short Sale Day High, in points gained. "T" if it exceeded or sold through the Selling Day High.
- **Weekly totals**: wide column separated on last day of week — square to left = total of D column declines, square to right = total of R column rallies
- **Vertical figures**: loss or gain points from Saturday to Saturday closing prices
- No column for daily gains/losses — all interest is in the spreads from highs to lows and lows to highs during each trading day

### Signal Marks (X and ✓) — MOST IMPORTANT
- **X mark**: the Objective for Buying, Selling, or Short Selling was made FIRST
- **✓ (check) mark**: the high or low was made LAST
- These marks are placed inside the Circles on Buying, Selling, and Short Sale Days at close of session
- Show whether high or low of the day was made FIRST or LAST
- Intermediate swing highs and lows tracked with arrows alongside date column — Trading Areas are between these highs and lows
- Mark up line with Blue ink, down line with Red ink

## Chapter III: Uses for the Columns and Marks (pp.19-23)

### The D Column (Decline Column)
- Shows least and greatest declines from Short Sale Objective to Buying Day Objective in points
- Used to judge decline timing and magnitude by averaging past declines
- Provides number of Short Sale opportunities and total Short Sale points
- Narrow-range markets = narrow spreads; high-priced/wide-range markets = wider spreads
- Near expiration dates and with stocks after long upward moves, technical position causes wide spreads
- Declines are not always from true highs — we use the spread from Short Sale Day High to Buying Day low regardless
- Points out buying opportunities in any one week and during the life of a grain option/stock
- Average of declines = better check on buying point than just the trading rule
- Trading rule summary: "Cover Short Sales and Buy, at or below the low of the previous day on a Buying Day"

### The Decline Zero
- Rally from Short Sale Day low, with closing price up or on the high for the day, plus a wide up opening on Buying Day
- Does not happen very often but can be anticipated by watching the low of Buying Day
- When low of Buying Day and high of Short Sale Day holds at same or higher price → visualize Zero in D column
- Decline Zero generally means nearby higher prices for a day or many times longer
- See WK and CK Plates — note the trend after a Zero appears

### Short Sale Coverage Rule
- Always cover Short Sale on the same day when spread is wide and/or within the limits of what a decline normally should be
- Range: Short Sale Day High to Buying Day Low
- If price doesn't go lower, this low = our point at which to buy next day on Buying Day
- Don't wait for Buying Day to cover Short Sale when action is "panicky" and sell off is within range or more
- Fast declines from Short Sale Day High to Buying Day Low are many times followed by fast rallies
- Cover Short Sale and WAIT — do NOT buy Long stock on this low
- Only buy Long stock on a Buying Day or on a Buying Day Low Violation made FIRST
- From this low, stock can close near the low without a rally OR rally and recover all losses of the day
- "We don't care" — we sold short and covered with a profit

### The R Column (Rally Column)
- Shows least and greatest rally from Buying Day Low to Selling Day High in points
- Shows how much of the decline has been recovered — all, part, or none
- Forecasts trend as up or down for the next day or so and sometimes longer
- After large D column unit, notice R column unit and the action for a day or so after
- Number of selling opportunities and total rally points on the Long side
- Narrow markets: rallies just about reach and penetrate Selling Objectives
- Higher markets: rallies run through for much deeper penetrations either way
- 56% penetration rate on Selling Objective in recent past grain futures — almost sure profits
- Probable profit = spread from Buying Day low to high at close, plus amount of Selling Day penetration of Buying Day High

### General Trading Rule for Selling Objectives
- "Sell your Long stock at or through the high of the previous day, this high of previous day is the high reached on the BUYING DAY"

### The Rally Zero
- Lack of rally from Buying Day Low with flat closing on/near the low, plus a wide down opening on Selling Day
- Doesn't happen often, can be anticipated by watching low on Buying Day
- When price holds at or under Buy Day low and high on Selling Day → visualize Rally Zero
- Rally Zero generally means nearby lower prices
- Rally Zero IS a Buy Day Violation — low usually made FIRST
- If opening is not too severe, rally starts from this low
- Rally comes up to and through Buying Day Low point many times, then declines again
- When buying on a Buying Day Low Violation: use Buying Day Low as Selling Objective instead of Buying Day High
- Market usually rallies from a Violation decline made FIRST and comes up to and through the Buying Day Low point

### The Diagonal Mark (✓)
- Placed above rally figures showing the number of Selling Day penetrations
- Can tally them for percentage of times they penetrate or sell through, or percentage that fail to penetrate
- Studying them gives an average of what to expect at Selling Objectives
- Penetrations at about the same fractions or points with occasional very deep penetrations (in trader's favor)

### The BH (Buy Day High) Column
- Shows number of times and extent of penetrations of Short Sale Day High
- When no penetrations → Zero
- BH and BU Zeros don't have same forecasting significance as Decline and Rally Zeros
- BUT the BH Zero shows the break and termination of the 3-day rally swing and establishes the high on Short Sale Day
- Place for a quick Short Sale when price goes through FIRST
- For this to happen: rally must have started on Short Sale Day and closed strong, continuing to rally up to this point (2+ days of rally = enough to exhaust the minor swing to some extent)

### The BU (Buy Under) Column
- Shows the unit that the stock sold under the Short Sale Day Low on a Buying Day
- If no declines → Zero
- BU Zero points out Higher Buying Day Bottoms — all higher support levels on declines from Short Sale Day rallies
- Spread between these two points = concession to buy at
- Watch this column for an average — runs from fractions to points
- Usually in Uptrend Trading Area: heaviest selling on Short Sale Day, decline "levels off" at a little above/below the Short Sale Day low
- Around this point: expect support, watch tape to confirm it, buying back of Long stock sold on last rally plus short covering creates the support
- Outside traders buying at top of rallies on balance, inside operators putting out short sales

### The BV (Buying Day Low Violation)
- Also entered in BU column — shows spread of the decline, how much it sold under Buying Day Low
- This is a "false move" — when made FIRST it is usually recovered
- Though market may sell down again after this rally
- While part of a larger decline and beginning of a Down Trend Trading Area, it is PROFITABLE to trade on this action most of the time
- Takes place from a decline on a Buying Day where there was no rally and close was heavy on the low
- Indicates lower prices and a down opening on Selling Day (unless in market of high prices from which Downward Limit Days could take place)
- Also could be a "leveling off" of a 2-3 day decline
- Watch for support at any place under Buying Day Low — check back 2-3 weeks for recent range on Violation declines (fractions to a point more or less)
- When decline low is made FIRST and early in session: buy as near this low point as possible
- From a Violation decline made FIRST: market usually rallies up to and through Buying Day Low point
- Buying Day Low becomes our Selling Objective instead of Buying Day High
- After recovering the "false move" or Violation points and wiping out 1/3 to 2/3 of the Real decline: "cinch" profits and anticipate next play = Short Sale on the Short Sale Day High, probably made FIRST

## Chapter IV: The Symbols (X) and (✓) as Trend Indicators (pp.25-28)

### The Two Signal Marks
- X = Objective for Buying, Selling, or Short Selling was made FIRST
- ✓ = high or low was made LAST
- Placed inside the Circles on Buying, Selling, and Short Sale Days at close of session

### Objective Made FIRST (X) — Favorable
- Example: Stock reacted from Short Sale Day High, made a low on Buying Day early in the season, rallied and traded the rest of session between high and low with closing price nearer the high
- Put (X) in Buying Day Circle — low being made FIRST
- Trader likes to see this — opening on Selling Day would generally be UP with penetration of Buying Day High
- Selling Objective would be made FIRST and an (X) placed in the circle
- Trader would sell at or above this penetration of Buying Day High — out of market without much delay
- Stock having sold through Selling Day FIRST → generally a reaction from this high
- Even after selling, trader watches for indication: if stock rallies and closes high enough → Up opening and another penetration of Selling Day High made FIRST on Short Sale Day
- Short Sale Day Circle would be marked (X) — "strong spot" for short sale

### Objective Made LAST (✓) — Less Favorable
- When objective is made LAST → trader must watch the entire session to make a trade, consumes more time
- Example of LAST: stock sold down from Short Sale Day High, made low, rallied, closed UP with opening higher, making high FIRST on Buying Day then began to "sell off" all through session, made a low but little or no spread from this low to closing price
- This = making Buying Day Objective LAST → put ✓ in circle, trend is DOWN
- Even though it may be end of decline, carries implications of a Buying Day Low Violation

### Mixed Signals
- When stock completes its cycle from Buy Day Low to Short Sale Day High with ALL Objectives made FIRST → ideal ("kind of movement we like to see")
- During other cycles: Objectives are mixed FIRST and LAST
- Caused by real trend being interrupted by smaller rallies/declines, currents and cross-currents throughout trading session
- These DO NOT change the ultimate completion of trading Objectives
- Must at all times ferret out the Real trend regardless of inter-day "jiggles"
- The Book does this almost automatically by pointing out the kind of trading day and signal marks

### False Moves
- Inside operators/insiders push market toward one objective; "false moves" are the smaller trends that confuse traders
- Must use greatest care in getting signal marks correct — the difficult but real trend follows these marks
- Traders who buy and sell on rallies/declines during the day entirely ignorant of trend kind → occasionally profit by "hitting" the real trend, but next trade = guess with ultimate total loss

### When High is Made FIRST on Buying Day
- High made FIRST on Selling Day → reaction continues up to next day → low price generally made on opening or after → rally probably starts carrying price up making high LAST on Short Sale Day
- Do NOT put out short sales on a high made LAST (unless it favors your play)
- Check back in book for a Violation of previous Buy Day low → expect stock to continue up, make high FIRST on Buying Day → watch for Short Sale at Short Sale Day High

### The Ideal Cycle
- Buy Day Low to Short Sale Day High with all Objectives made FIRST
- See Plate on RZ 1943 — profits visible as movement unfolds

### RZ 1943 Plate Example (p.27)
- Shows October 1943 December Rye trading
- One week of data: Mon Oct 11 through Mon Oct 18
- All Objectives made FIRST in this period — ideal cycle
- Shows one Buying Day Low at the low of Short Sale Day on Oct 13, and one Buy Day low under on Sat Oct 16
- Bottoms just equaled and exceeded, tops penetrated at Selling and Short Sale Highs
- Prices in narrow range of 4-5 points for about 9 weeks
- D and R Columns total 8 1/8 points while closing price gain was only 2 1/4 points — trading opportunities were 4x greater than buy-and-hold
- When markets are narrow: moves are small, profits are small
- Recurrence of Buy/Sell/Short Sale signals in series = not exceptions — market is filled with them and trading is most mechanical at these times
- Shows two sets in series: Short Sale → Buy → Sale → Short Sale → Buy → Sale — All Made FIRST
- When all signals made FIRST: no delay, trader buys and sells just as Objectives are reached or penetrated
- When mixed: trader must watch entire session to complete trade; sometimes wiser to stand aside when movement is too narrow

## Chapter V: A Buying Day (pp.29-31)

### Core Buying Day Philosophy
- Buy when stock is on "bottom dead center" and trend is about to turn up
- Sell when on "top dead center" and trend is about to turn down
- On a Buying Day when rally from the low is sufficiently large → sell out on the SAME DAY
- Rally may put price up to and through the high of the day before (Short Sale Day)
- Close may be strong — but we sell out just before the close anyway
- Don't follow the rally too far, don't take chances with this kind of move
- Secure profits before close of market
- Reason for same-day exit: stock may open down and spend whole Selling Day without reaching Selling Day Objective (Buying Day High)

### Where to Buy
- Buy at a little above or below the low of previous day (Short Sale Day low)
- Watch the range or spread on Short Sale Day, the "sell off" from high to low
- The price we have to pay next day (Buying Day) for our long stock = provided decline ends at or near this low
- Get indication from how stock closes on Short Sale Day

### Reading the Close on Short Sale Day
- Close is strong (price up from low, nearer the high) → expect higher opening on Buying Day, wait for price to decline from high made FIRST
- Short Sale Day Low = our point to watch and buy at or below
- In a strong uptrend: decline may "fall short" of reaching this low → Higher Buying Day Bottom → generally profitable
- Price may open up and rally farther, making high FIRST, then give way "grudgingly" on decline — stock seems resilient, "sort of springs back" from each low
- Decline Column Unit is usually small in this case

### When High is Made FIRST on Buying Day
- High made FIRST → buying point to be made LAST
- Stock may trade down with no indication to rally, close may be near low of day
- In this case we would NOT buy at all, even if lower than Short Sale Day low
- Anticipate a Buying Day Low Violation and wait for it

### When Low is Made LAST on Buying Day
- Low is higher than Short Sale Day Low (Higher Bottom) → buy as near this low as we can
- Generally a rally starts from this higher bottom
- Should any gain at close be lost on opening of Selling Day → sell on any rally after the decline, use Buy Day Low as Selling Objective
- Rally usually carries up to and through this low, even though it again declines
- Strong rallies at times go not only up to and through this low but continue up through High of Buying Day, completing Primary Selling Objective with a penetration

### Key Rule: The Low Made on Buying Day IS the Low
- Has nothing to do with whether it may be violated
- Next day you buy at what you consider to be the low — many times you actually get it
- After buying, it may go a little lower, but your line of thought changes: NOT watching the price you paid, but expecting the stock to rally and show a gain above this low at the close
- Even if profit is a little less because price sold down after buying → the point is: after buying at the correct place, reverse your thinking to look forward to where you can sell out with a profit

### Critical Money Management Rule
- "NEVER AVERAGE A LOSS, SELL OUT IF YOU THINK YOU ARE WRONG AND THEN BUY BACK AGAIN WHEN YOU BELIEVE YOU ARE RIGHT"
- Temptation after buying near the low: stock goes lower causing a Violation → low made FIRST = strong "spot" to average → looks like it would have been a "cinch" after rally sets in → BUT DON'T DO IT
- Trader takes losses when they are small — does not hold on and hope for a rally
- Sells out before the close, takes small loss, begins to anticipate a Buy Low Violation

### Loss vs. Gain Statistics (from Yearly Plates WK and CK)
- Loss day swings run about 12% of total
- Gain days about 88%
- Occasionally a loss day swing can be very severe — daily trader should not take chances where loss could be greater than greatest daily gain
- "The daily trader can trade on a 'shoe string' but the 3 day trader is prepared to finance himself over a 'bump'"
- Daily trader must be in complete command of his cash at all times
- Takes small losses by selling out the instant he sees he is wrong

### Uptrend vs. Downtrend Buying Days
- In Uptrend Trading Areas: low on Buying Day generally made FIRST with closing price up from this low
- "Flat" or weak closings are usually made in Downtrend Areas
- Regardless of kind of Trading Area: buying low points made FIRST on a Buying Day are mostly profitable (except in precipitous decline after steep downtrend)

## Chapter VI: Buying on a Buying Day Low Violation (pp.33-36)

### Violation Prerequisites
- Buying Day Low Violation MUST be made FIRST
- Result of Buying Day Low made LAST with a "flat" closing
- "Flat" closing favors the probabilities that opening prices will be down
- Down opening is often the start of the Violation
- Decline continues until it meets support

### The Support Mechanism
- Support is mostly inside short covering for profit + stabilization of the market
- Also includes some of the Long stock sold to outside traders at higher prices
- Decline can stop anywhere under Buying Day Low
- From the book's column: we know the range of past Violations and their fractional limits
- Declines are occasionally very severe but knowing the range + watching the activity → generally get indication of a coming rally

### How to Trade the Violation
- Watch market closely for decline to slow down and stop
- At turning point: stock makes a low, then rebounds in fractions or points depending on decline severity
- If severe: wide range churning up and down, then activity slows, market goes quiet
- Market goes dead but prices tick up a little from extreme low
- Buy on the above described action and expect rally to start from this point

### The Rally from Violation
- Rally from Buying Day Low Violation made FIRST = caused by short covering from the inside
- Large short interest seems to be that part of the covering movement that carries price up to and through the LOW of Buying Day
- Extended and over-extended short interest + traders who sold short too near the bottom → forced to cover in haste → rally is fast, active, wide spread between transactions
- Price reaches up to and through HIGH of Buying Day — extent depends on size and urgency of outside short interest

### "Take Off" Pattern
- Starts with a "run in" of the shorts but usually we watch this full rally
- We watch the spread from Buying Day Low to the Low of Violation
- If spread is wide enough to make profit (bought on extreme low of Violation and sold at or through the Low of Buying Day) → stock should rally back this high
- When decline is severe: rally may not carry back as high as Buying Day Low → rally is "dull" → lack of buying going dead on top → decline may start again = indication of a downtrend
- When spread is narrowed (fractionally or a point): price is holding with covering and support buying → expect more from any rally from this point

### Recovery Percentages
- Each eighth above the Violation recovery recovers a certain percent of the Real Decline
- More rally = more of the Real Decline recovered
- Rally may "wipe out" all the D column unit loss and more
- Many times when D column unit is small, the R column unit is large

### Key Statistics
- About 50% of the time a trader can make initial purchase on a Buying Day Low where NO Violation occurs
- Violations occur about 35% of the time on average, during the total trading swings of a grain future or stock during its ordinary up and down move in the general market

### Selling After a Violation
- ALWAYS sell on a recovery of the Violation — get off the "hook" without a loss or as little as possible
- After buying near low on Buying Day and getting caught in Violation on down opening of Selling Day → loss is at least temporary
- Usually after 2-3 days the decline Violation will be recovered and a certain percent of the D Column Unit
- Don't hold on too long — try to get some part of the rally above the Buying Day Low
- Don't hope for more rally — at or through the 226 1/2 (Buy Day Low) the stock may start to decline again
- The rally stopping at this point is an "exit" from a bad situation

### March Soybeans (SH) Example (p.35-36)
- Purchase at 227 on Buying Day — half cent above the low
- Decline is large and above average for D column decline
- Buying Day Objective is 2 points under the previous low (Short Sale Day Low) — within average for buying
- Stock closed up on Buying Day — expected higher opening on Selling Day but did NOT get it
- Opened down and declined quickly to 225 1/4 or (1 1/4) points Violation — 1 3/4 points loss on paper
- Buy Day Low Violation was made FIRST
- Violation was the third day of a decline → expected stock to rally and it did
- Rally quickly recovered the Violation decline
- Immediately alert for place to sell Long stock — somewhere at or through or above the Buy Day Low of 226 1/2
- Recovery above the Buy Day Low: each recovery tick is figured as a certain percent of the D column decline
- At 229 the recovery of the 9-point decline was approximately 27.7% — slightly below the one-third minimum
- Rally stopped at 229 and declined to 227 3/4 at close
- Work Sheet figures at left = "ticks" in order on the tape until top reached at 229

## Chapter VII: A Selling Day (pp.37-39)

### When to Sell
- When low made FIRST on Buying Day and closing price is up from the low, nearer the high → expect up-opening and penetration of Buying Day High on Selling Day
- These penetrations occur more often in Up-trend Trading Area
- Should you sell out too soon after a penetration → lose some probable profits, but in Uptrend expect "deeper" penetrations and progressively higher tops
- Activity of market after penetrations is a good sign to watch, also after small D column decline units

### Same-Day Exit on Selling Day
- From a strong closing on Buying Day when opening of Selling Day is up and wide → generally a penetration of Buying Day High
- Sell out "at the market" without waiting for the next transaction after the opening price
- Price may go higher (and many times does) — but what we sell on may be the high
- Opening price may be the high and a reaction may start from the next transaction
- We sell and are not concerned with how high price may go after we sell out

### When Opening is Down on Selling Day
- Purchase on Buying Day shows good gain above the low at close → but gain partly lost on opening of Selling Day
- If opening is at or near Buying Day closing price → sell out, don't wait
- If opening is down and declining further from opening price → sell on any rally from the low of this decline
- This point would be at, through, and above the Low of Buying Day — now your Primary Selling Objective instead of Buying Day High

### Stalled Opening on Selling Day
- Where opening and previous close are the same → trader sells "at the market" immediately
- Should following prices be up → order executed on a rising trend, gets more of the rally
- Should following prices be down → loss will be much smaller, his order is in time to "duck" the real sell off
- Many traders at this point do nothing — wait to see what will happen → "most likely the expected doesn't happen, either way, and the net result is a greater loss"

### When Rally Falls Short on Selling Day
- Reaction from so short a rally = indicates weak up-trend
- Generally "fall short" of a penetration of Buying Day High (Selling Objective)
- Even though decline does not violate Buying Day Low → sell out, just the same
- Opening should have been up and continued the rally — it is the better "play" to take a small loss than to hold on for a much greater reaction
- Stock that should rally from a decline on a Selling Day (from a low of Buying Day) and does not → better to sell on any rally, take the loss

### Selling Objective Changes Based on Violations
- Normal: Sell at or through High of Buying Day on Selling Day
- When decline occurs with or without a Buying Day Low Violation instead of continuation of rally → change Selling Objective to at or through the Low of Buying Day
- If decline stops within a fraction or point and rally starts with activity picking up through the Low of Buying Day with no hesitancy → rally may carry further, even penetrate the High of Buying Day
- In that case: switch back to Primary Selling Objective (Buying Day High)

### Penetration Statistics
- Yearly Tables show approximately 66 penetrations on CK May Corn and 55 penetrations on WK May Wheat out of a total of 100 Selling Objectives
- See SH Plate for failures to penetrate and selling through Buying Day Low points, also selling stock bought on Buying Day Low Violations

### Signal Marks Importance
- "These signal marks 'X' FIRST and '✓' LAST make the difference between being right or wrong on the trend"

## Chapter VIII: A Short Sale Day (pp.41-43)

### Setup for Short Sale
- Assume close on Selling Day was strong → indicating up-opening and continuation of uptrend
- Stock or future opens up and penetrates the Short Sale Objective (Selling Day High) on Short Sale Day
- After penetration: watch for slowing down of the move, detect if price is meeting resistance, if selling is overcoming the buying

### Inside Selling Dynamics
- Selling we look for at this point is INSIDE selling — it gradually overcomes unwise long buying by traders at top of rally
- Either stops rally abruptly and decline starts right from opening prices, OR the activity dries up and it comes down gradually
- Multiple futures/stocks at top of Short Sale Day: one or two will be made strong to create an air of strength, test buying power, put out short sales in all others under cover of strength in the ones they are "putting" higher
- Same action on Buying Day in reverse: one or two "put down" for new low while all others hold

### Executing the Short Sale
- Make short sale at and through and above the High of Selling Day on Short Sale Day
- Most favorable: high made FIRST on penetrations of Selling Day Highs
- Would NOT put out a short sale if stock opened down and declined without a rally → implies rally may start later in session, may cause high to be made LAST
- Where stock opened at same price as previous close and declined early then rallied higher than opening → put out short sale just as rally began to exhaust itself after the penetration
- Not as favorable as the first scenario

### After the Short Sale
- When reaction takes place after penetration of Selling Day High and movement is in "no hurry," stock just trades down → stay short anticipating our covering point next day on Buying Day
- Can cover short sale when we buy our "long" stock or perhaps a little before
- This gives more time to concentrate on Buying Objective — covering short + purchase will be at a little above or below the low of previous day (Short Sale Day)
- If "sell off" is severe with activity during Short Sale Day session → cover short sale on dullness near the low of this reaction, or if and when we have a profit
- Then out of the market, wait for next play (Buying Day Objective)

### Short Sale at Higher Bottom
- Stock or future rallies fast from severe decline, closes strong → indicates high made FIRST on next day (Buying Day) = another chance for short sale
- Taking profits on this fast decline strengthens position: if decline from this rally doesn't go as low on next Buying Day → may have to buy at Higher Bottom
- Short sale profits can be used to reduce cost of Buying a Higher Bottom — "averaging" carried out in two separate trades

### Premature Short Sales
- Should you sell short too soon after penetration and it happens to be one of those more than average penetration moves → price goes through, temporarily against you
- However, a rally so active and strong would no doubt meet with profit taking and higher short selling → would cause stock to react or decline low enough for you to "cover" with small loss, profit, or even break before the close
- More-than-average penetrations are usually fast rallies back following a severe decline → they widen trading swings both up and down for a few sessions

### Trend Reversals
- Reversals between sessions are seldom important enough to cause a reversal of trend after it has been established — running with price up or down for some time
- Down opening from a strong close OR up-opening from a weak close MAY be the beginning of change of trend either way
- Up-closing on Selling Day in Up Trend Area → indicates up-opening on Short Sale Day → may cause penetration of Selling Day High FIRST
- This is the rally we use for our short sale — "put out" the short at and through this penetration
- Generally stock reacts from this high made FIRST
- BUT in case where Violation of Buying Day occurs → stock rallies from this decline, closes up, making high LAST, indicating further up-trend and high made FIRST on Buying Day

### When Stock Opens Down on Short Sale Day
- Opens down and declines further → let it alone, it does not favor your play
- Plenty of Selling "spots" made on penetrations and FIRST that are more favorable
- In "putting out" a short sale on a penetration made FIRST → generally after a rally of 2 or 3 days

### D Column vs R Column Comparison
- Compare D column with R column to see trend continuing up or down, reversing itself, or forming a trade range
- Check back over D column units of recent past several weeks
- Note if spreads have been wide enough to make profit from short selling — if not, wait until movement begins to widen
- Square at Left of Weekly Column gives total of fluctuating declines

## Chapter IX: A Short Sale at High of Buying Day Made First (pp.45-46)

### The Setup
- Short Sale "put out" at high of Buying Day made FIRST on the penetration of Short Sale Day High
- Should be covered on the reaction whenever it sells down enough for a profit or on first indications of dullness around the reaction low
- Short selling on Buying Day High made FIRST is generally a WEAK short sale
- Prices are relatively low — especially in Uptrend Trading Area
- The decline is of short duration when trend is just starting up from the low point of the last Downtrend Area

### Coverage Rules
- Cover short sale quick on a Buying Day when stock acts "tight" and resists an "easy sell off" — look ahead for a point on this day to go "long"
- NEVER sell a stock or future short on a Buying Day when it makes the low FIRST (by opening down) — even if it is the 3rd or 4th day of the rally — not enough spread, can't make a profit with reasonable certainty
- Don't let it bother you because you covered your short and price went lower — closing on the low means you have a profit, that's what you're trading for: quick small profits

### In Strong Uptrend
- Stock will make the High FIRST on a Buying Day about 35% of the time on average
- Then declines, then rallies and holds the gains for a strong closing
- Trader sees up-opening or failure to penetrate Short Sale Day High, notes activity at and through this point as it slackens → sends order to sell
- Watches reaction: if stock shows no indication to rally → waits before going "long"
- From this decline low the stock often rallies (in Uptrend) → should it make a new top (going higher than opening price or the high of the first rally) → buys on any set back from this high before the close → covers short sale earlier as insurance

### Why Short Selling on Buying Day High Made FIRST
- Rally from last Buying Day Low has been in progress 4 days up → in process of exhausting the upswing
- In many cases the stock makes the high on Buying Day — when preceded by a Buying Day Low Violation, it is being made after 3 days of rally

### High Made FIRST by Opening Down
- High made FIRST may be established by opening DOWN on a Buying Day, but this would be of no interest for short selling
- The probabilities would favor a rally from this low before the close → high would be made LAST
- Stock would be selling off from a previous high price — Buying Day Objective is based on a decline of 1-2 days from higher prices
- Any rally from this low making a new top → would reverse the high made FIRST → high becomes LAST → Buying Day Low made FIRST → strong confirming action for "long" side

### Wide Spread from Low to Close
- Generally where there's a wide spread from low to close, after stock has sold down from higher prices → getting your profit in the same session
- Take it and don't enter the market again (in same stock/future) until the Short Sale Day, no matter what the stock does in the interval

## Chapter X: Failures to Penetrate (pp.47-49)

### At the Top of Uptrend Trading Area
- At or near top of Uptrend Trading Area (after uptrend of 1-2+ weeks, 5+ cents/points gain from previous Downtrend low)
- Extreme high is generally made on a relatively fast, active, wide move with volume (in stocks; no volume on commodity tape)
- Watch comparative activity of the market
- After extreme high is established: price probably breaks back under this high, trading and closing price slightly under this top
- Inside selling by "those" who think price is high enough (at least temporarily)
- Enough selling to stop the rise but not enough to break price wide open

### On Run-ups
- Run up attracts "outside long" buying — demand filled during trading that takes place under this low and at the opening next morning
- Many times supply > demand at higher price → stock opens down and declines further
- Rally follows from this low but accomplished on quieter market with decreased activity/volume
- Rally fails to reach extreme high → decline starts again from this failure
- This is the TOP — Uptrend Trading Area is now changing to Downtrend

### At the Bottom of Downtrend Trading Areas
- Same action in reverse: extreme low on heavy volume and activity
- Stock rallies with light or heavy volume but decline takes place on greatly decreased volume
- Heavy buying at this low for support and other purposes is held → pressure is not great enough for stock to go as low
- May penetrate the first established low point fractionally

### Failure to Penetrate Statistics
- Failures to penetrate Buying or Selling Objectives happen approximately 40% of the time on average
- This movement is a very definite part of the method as a whole

### Failures to Penetrate at Top Side
- Usually found in Downtrend Areas and when trend is changing at the top
- Forecast by weak or "flat" closings on Buying, Selling, and Short Sale Days
- At bottoms: rallies before the close that hold → forecast up-openings and failures to go lower

### Failures on the Sell Side
- Stock makes high FIRST on Selling Day with penetration of Buying Day High → then reacts, selling nearer the low at the close → indications for lower opening on Short Sale Day
- Lower opening: after decline, stock will attempt to rally → in most cases rally will attempt to penetrate the High of Selling Day
- If immediate trend is higher → should rally fail to reach this Objective and activity dies out at top with narrow transactions → "put out" a short sale on this declining trend (J-U-S-T as it starts)
- When prices are comparatively high for near the bottom of an Uptrend Area → may not be enough room in the spread to make a profit

### Failure on the Buy Side
- Higher Buying Day Low point — failure to penetrate on the down side (Short Sale Day Low)
- Stock shows support and can go lower after a rally
- Many times close will be up from the low on a Buying Day but on Selling Day opening price will be same as closing price → failure from here on any rally to penetrate the Selling Day Objective (Buying Day High)
- Decline starts from this failure → stock makes Buying Day Low Violation LAST

### Forecasting Rules from Closings
- Weak closings forecast failures to penetrate "top side" on the rallies after declines
- Strong closings forecast failures to penetrate "down side" on the declines after rallies

## Chapter XI: The Trend Line and Trading Areas — Market Trends (pp.51-52, partial)

### The Trend Line
- Line running alongside Date Column = trend line of the market
- Not used for primary term possibilities but used for its implications for Bull and Bear intermediate swings
- Always keep the Seasonal Trends in mind
- Longer term line broken into shorter swings = Trading Areas
- Trading Areas are moves of 5+ cents/points or more
- Can be any number of these areas in the longer term or Seasonal Trends

### Trading Areas Characteristics
- Watch highs and lows of these areas, number of points moved up or down, and time consumed in each
- Particularly after a move of 2-3 weeks in either direction
- In Seasonal Trend Upward Swing: expect penetrations at tops of Trading Areas, watch for support, progressive lifting of bottoms after declines
- Note the kind of Objective Day we are trading on — if at top of previous area, expect wider moves on day of "break thru" or penetrations
- Same at bottom of areas in Seasonal Downward swing

### One of Several Objectives Must End a Move
- At the tops: a Sale, Short Sale, or a high made FIRST on a Buying Day
- At the bottoms: a Buying Day Objective or a Violation of it
- By concentrating on these smaller areas → eliminate confusion, only need to watch highs and lows of previous Trading Area

### Penetrations at Area Boundaries
- When High of Area in Up Seasonal Trend approaches high of previous Area → look for penetration of this top
- When comparatively low → look for supply at tops, support at bottoms with volume and activity at either end
- Watch the kind of Trading Objective Day when penetrations are made → generally wide moves, can hold on for deeper penetrations

### Up Trend vs Down Trend Trading Areas
- Up and Down Trading Areas: buy, sell, and short in both
- In Up Trends: expect Selling Objectives to be reached and penetrated
- In Down Trends: look for Buying Day Under moves (under Short Sale Day Low) and Buying Day Low Violations = low under the Buying Day
- In Down Trend: expect not only two decline sessions (low of Short Sale Day and the Buying Day Low Under) to complete a move, but also Buying Day Low Violations before rallies start
- In Up Trend: expect corrective decline to be completed in one and perhaps same session — this is the low made on Short Sale Day, from a fast decline with an active fast rally from this low up to the close

### Fast Upward vs. Downward Movement (p.52)
- In fast Upward Movement: Higher Buy Day Bottoms, penetrated Selling Objectives
- Reason we "cover" short sale when decline is severe in same session and equals average of an ordinary decline from High on Short Sale Day to Low on Buying Day
- On fast Upward Movement: compare D and R Column Units — note penetration mark over R Column Unit, absence of loss unit in 3-day column
- We DO get loss swings in Uptrend but generally the loss unit is fractional; when D Column Unit is small, R Column Unit is generally large
- On fast Downward Movement: D and R Columns reverse themselves, loss swing begins to show up in 3-Day Column
- Very narrow "line" Trading Area — see Chart on "X" for 1944 — without enough movement to trade for profit
- Industrial Average fluctuated in about 16-point range during entire year of 1944
- These "patches" are parts of several kinds of trends — taken from both Bull and Bear Primary Markets, but highly representative of the whole trend

### Stocks vs. Commodities
- Stocks do not have same velocity of movement as grains and other commodities
- Stocks trade around highs and lows, spend more time in reaching them — possible to buy/sell within an eighth or quarter of a point
- Trader must not become accustomed to wider commodity movements and expect same in stocks
- U.S. Steel as an example — all stocks exhibit same pattern of movement, but many others are better trading vehicles with wider movement and broader daily trading range
- Trader must select his own stocks — make up a book on them and study it
- Many stocks have "set" habits and quirks that a trader will learn to recognize
- Surprising how often a stock will move as expected

### Seasonal Trends (Commodity Charts, pp.60-62)
- **Wheat**: Traditional pattern — easy prices prevail at harvest time, but once harvest pressure is over, price works higher until again time for new crop. Downturn usually occurs about one month prior to harvest. Based on average farm prices 1922-41: low around July, rises through winter, peaks around March-April.
- **Cotton**: Based on average farm prices 1922-41: peak in August when remaining supplies of old crop are scarce. After August, prices ease in anticipation of new crop supplies. Low around November-December.
- **Corn**: Distinct seasonal pattern. Low in November, prices move higher each month until peak about August when remaining supplies are scarce. After August, eases in anticipation of new crop.
- **Feed Grains (Corn, Oats, Barley)**: All follow similar seasonal pattern — low Oct-Nov, rising through spring, peak summer.
- **Soybeans**: Based on average farm prices 1937-41. By May-June prices decline again, most crushers' needs satisfied. Price index begins to decline before harvest, then harvest forces prices lower. Crushers purchase about 50% of crop before December 31. Price moves steadily and sharply upward from October lows. Peak around March-April.
- The seasonal longer up-trend (e.g., November low to August high) is broken into numerous trading rallies and declines = short-term Trading Areas

### Market Tone Terminology (p.53)
- "MARKET STRONGER" = actual and general price advances
- "MARKET STRONG" = upward trend with bullish sentiment anticipating further advances
- "MARKET FIRM" = increasing confidence, prices holding at level of day before or a shade higher
- "MARKET STEADY" = no appreciable price changes or trends in either direction
- "MARKET DULL" = relative market inactivity, no definite tendency
- "MARKET UNSETTLED" = market uncertainty, waiting attitude for factors (storm, labor, weather, legislation)
- "MARKET WEAKER" = actual and general price declines
- "MARKET WEAK" = downward trend with market sentiment anticipating lower prices
- "MARKET DEMORALIZED" = unusual cases, oversupplied market, sales only at very low prices
- Trader who commits these to memory will have better understanding of changing market sentiment

### The Dilettante Speculator (Part-time Trader, p.52)
- Those who can't attend market sessions daily can also trade at their convenience
- Just so long as he keeps his book → always has a check on the market
- Even if absent for a week or month, he doesn't have to fill in daily price entries — just keep circles in continuity
- Upon return: refer to newspaper for prices from the day before → knows at once whether to buy, sell, or sell short

## Chapter XII: Limit Day Moves (pp.63-64)

### Downward Limit Moves
- Beginning must start from comparatively high range of prices
- Three (3) Selling Days: the Selling Day Objective, the Short Sale Objective, and the high made FIRST on a Buying Day — but only two could be used: Short Sale Day and Buying Day High made FIRST in a downward movement
- Beginning of movement must start from one of these three tops
- If decline starts from a Selling Day Objective → we would be out of market by virtue of having sold our long stock → we would miss the move → but being out conserves resources and gives anticipation of increased buying power at much lower prices
- "This in itself is one of the fundamentals of speculation — to be able to protect your capital and be in a position to act on a more favorable opportunity when it comes along"

### Downward Limit Warning Signs
- On a Buying Day when stock shows no tendency to rally and looks like it will close "flat" (low and close at same price) → it usually goes lower
- In a move of this kind: closing prices are generally right on the bottom eighth
- Do NOT buy long stock, for when Low is made LAST → Buying Day Circle would have a check (✓) indicating a lower trend

### Upward Limit Moves
- Starting of a series must start from a comparatively low range — perhaps restricted prices or the culminating move after a long slow rising market
- Would generally start from a Buying Day Objective or from a Violation of a Buying Day Low made FIRST or from a low on a Short Sale Day, from an active severe "sell off" from the Short Sale Day High
- When this happens: decline is panicky with each price change going lower until it reaches a bottom, then the turn is very active and fast with gains usually held
- Sometimes followed by a decline from this rally and while the first low may not be reached → the next day (Buying Day) → real start of upward move from the Higher Bottom on Buying Day
- Rally would start when market had the appearance of going lower and with a general bearish atmosphere
- The manipulator would only start this move after a strong technical position had been built up → too many unwise short positions → way to get an extended short position → make it look weak and have traders sell it short at or near the bottom

### Two Chances to Get Aboard
- Upward move: Buying Day Low and the Violation of Buying Day Low made FIRST → should the turn come on either of these days
- Downward move: Short Sale Day and the Buying Day High made FIRST

## Chapter XIII: The Three Day Trading Method (pp.65-67)

### Applicability
- Applicable to both Grains, Commodities, and Stocks on the "Big Board"
- For stocks: select only those appearing on tape every day with large volume relative to daily transactions of whole market — public trading favorites with narrow bid-ask spread
- Low-priced stocks won't do — not enough fluctuations
- Many Dow-Jones stocks are good — they move over a 2-3+ point range during each swing
- Also many others in the $40-$75 price range applicable to this method

### Setup Process
- Select a few stocks and make up a book on them using back-number newspapers from public libraries
- Start book from the low point of any Secondary Reaction or at least go back a few months
- Study past action, observe rally/decline patterns, spreads from highs to lows, regularity and continuity
- All stocks have certain characteristics — some rally ahead of market, others with it, others fluctuate "Up and Down and Up and Down" just "plugging" their way along
- The "pluggers" that trade with wide enough spread are good for this method
- After finding a good trading stock: stay with it and forget about the others

### Method Description
- Purely mechanical in its strictest sense
- Develop confidence in its movements through knowing how it recorded movement in the past → reasonably certain future will preserve same movement
- You need NOT invoke the finer points of trading (as with daily method)
- Based on greater percentage of gains over losses — you will have loss swings, but:
  - Loss swings in points are generally small (with few exceptions)
  - Profit swings in points are generally large (with few exceptions)
  - Profit swings must of necessity outnumber loss points
- Method: consistently buying and selling as advantageously as possible at Buying and Selling Objectives → profitable in the long run, profits made on balance

### Long Side + Short Side
- The long side of the market is well handled by this method
- Want short side too — that's half the profits
- Selling short stocks on the "Big Board" is a little different than commodities due to Stock Exchange ruling restricting short sales to some extent
- Both with Three Day Method and Daily Trading: we many times hit the top price when selling, even to the top eighth → but with stocks may not be able to "get off" a short sale as easily as with commodities

### Key Rules
- **DO NOT HOLD ON OR CARRY YOUR STOCK DOWN** — at your Selling Objective you must sell out, either way, with a profit or loss on the penetration or as near to it on the failure to penetrate
- Next day (Buying Day): probably get opportunity to buy stock at lower price with chances of making up any loss
- Violation of Buying Day Low does NOT change your Selling Objective (as it does in daily method) — you do your buying on a Buying Day and hold on, ride through this reaction, wait for your Selling Objective (penetration or failure to penetrate the Selling Day High on a Short Sale Day)

### Statistics for Three Day Method
- Trader using this for grain futures will find approximately ONE HUNDRED Opportunities or trading swings on the long side, plus an equal amount on the short side, from start to expiration dates
- Past records show over 50% of the Buying and Selling Objectives were accomplished at penetrations at these points
- Trends in markets vary year to year — some Bull and Bear Markets will be achieved by wide trading moves in both Primary swings and Secondaries; other years will be narrow, persistent upward and downward movements

### Reaction Trend Forecasting
- Trader has a fairly reliable guide on kind of reaction trend to expect by noting the type of upward movement that preceded it
- Fast and almost vertical climb → expect reaction to be of the same nature, perhaps precipitous decline
- Three Day Method trader would forego buying on Buying Day Objective and wait for Short Sale Day → then sell short, covering on Buying Day, and again selling on Short Sale Day → until the urgency of the decline was over and the stock began to receive support, after which trading rallies began

### Loss Day Swings
- Can be very severe and look more formidable while happening
- Takes more courage than most traders have to "ride out" these moves
- Greater part of loss may be on paper and only temporary
- Requires considerably more money to finance them

## Chapter XIV: The Investor and Swing Trader (pp.68-72)

### Applying the Book Method to Investing
- Investor and Swing Trader have a means of accumulation and distribution in the use of the Three Day Method
- When buying for accumulation: don't try to buy a full line all at once (same for selling)
- Market may not be technically strong enough to take the selling — may get a price far less than would suit him

### Buying for Accumulation
- Apply Buying Day Objective to accumulation, Selling Day Objectives to distribution → save many points and dollars
- Example: Line of 500 shares → use Buying Day, place order for 100 shares → after getting them, wait until next Buying Day for another lot → continue until full line bought
- Higher average cost but each lot would be more apt to show a profit and trader has assurance he is following a rising trend
- Had he started buying during comparatively low range and in a rising market → not likely to carry a heavy load at a loss in case of sudden decline

### Selling for Distribution
- Same procedure: sell only on Selling Objective (Short Sale Day and Buying Day High made FIRST) → liquidating stock on a rising trend
- In either case: trader has assurance he is getting or trying to get the best prices at the time of his buying or selling

### Stock Selection Criteria
- Only good judgment to get a report on the company, past and future probabilities
- Unless market action confirms the report → watch but don't buy
- Time for buying should be based solely on technical indications and present market position relative to the low and high for the year
- Could be so near to a top (temporarily) that by waiting a short time it can be bought at a wide concession
- Most Bull markets show at least one or more important declines during the year that are buying opportunities
- Most important: starting at exactly the right time

### Two Types of Tops
- **Real and Actual Top**: Real Top is where first heavy liquidation takes place. After a decline of a week or more, a rally or continuation of the advance many times will exceed the first high, making the Actual Top on volume as large or more. Trader should be on alert to begin selling the instant he detects this inside selling.
- Experienced traders take profits at this point and do NOT try to anticipate an Actual Top
- The high made on this kind of action may be one and the same — the Real and Actual
- **Gradual Tops**: Other stocks make one top (Real and Actual) on comparatively light volume with no signs indicating a top has been reached. Long decline starts with a series of small reactions and rallies but with a definite downward tendency. These are difficult to recognize. Trader should understand characteristics of the stocks and have some idea of the percentage appreciation from past swings.

### Bottom Formation
- Bottoms of long declines: established by a particular violent downward thrust → immediate sharp rally → subsequent slower decline on greatly reduced volume and activity → stopping short of the last low
- Trader does NOT try to buy long stock on this fast decline — waits for the "quiet spot" on the reaction after the rally — when market becomes "dull"
- He will buy part of his line at this point (with intent to sell out should stock make a new low; then add to it where stock begins to make new highs in series)
- An action of this kind cleans up all the "stops" below the line of bottom prices and "shakes off" a following before the real move gets under way
- Having acquired his stock he holds on for the move — perhaps 3-6 months or longer, or until he recognizes the described action around tops

### Stock Characteristics and Selection
- Most stocks that make up the backbone of the market are in the conservative class — trading profits should be figured on a PERCENTAGE basis not on points
- Get the highs and lows of one or more sustained swings that a stock makes each year, figure percentage gains and losses from lows to highs and highs to lows
- Market Averages may show great gains/losses in points, but per cent gain/loss of an individual stock may be disappointingly small

### Buying at Bottoms around Bear Bottoms or Secondary Reactions
- Use the 3-day rule for accumulation
- Carefully check percentage rise of stock as price moves up from low point
- Most important: a stock that is going up will do so with small and normal setbacks until it reaches the high
- In many cases the top will be made quite unexpectedly but not without warning
- Watch build-up of volume a few days prior to top, then greatly reduced volume the next and for a few days after (or volume might remain large for a few days before and after the high is made)
- Decrease in volume caused by a let-up in selling pressure → stock may not be able to withstand further heavy selling without breaking the price too much
- Many times a trading range is formed between the high and the low of the breakback and the stock trades in this range indefinitely

### Market Distribution Prior to Bull Market Endings
- Individual stocks, one after another, refuse to participate in any further rise while general market continues to advance
- Same action in a lesser degree before severe intermediate declines
- Low-priced late movers that move up fast and start to decline, seldom going back to their highs a second time — must hustle to catch up with the general market which is plainly showing a declining tendency
- Sudden reaching of a top on greatly increased volume, then a slight break of several percent from the high while volume continues large = heavy selling mixed with public buying and inside short selling

### Swing Trading = Same as 3-Day Method on Enlarged Scale
- Market action for a swing trading is identically the same as for the 3-day method, only on an enlarged scale
- Minor movement receives same support on declines and same pressure on rallies

### Rally vs. Change in Trend
- Experienced traders and tape readers can generally distinguish a rally from a change in trend
- A normal rally is from one-half to two-thirds of the decline — if decline is not over, the rally will fall short
- Large transactions on the downside and smaller ones on the upside → volume light → activity "fades away" with rally "dying out" at the top
- Where trend is to continue: steady rise on increasing volume and demand for the stock, urgent demand for "short covering" → pace accelerated, large transactions on the upside at higher prices

## Chapter XV: Pertinent Points (pp.73-76)

### Paper Trading Practice
- Do paper trading (try buying and selling) for about 10 days until you get the "feel" of the market
- Not daydreaming or reading prices after close with recriminations — take pad and pencil to market, buy and sell using Objectives
- Check yourself after a couple of weeks of this practice
- This trading practice will teach self-control, curb impetuous buying and selling, control patience

### Key Trading Maxims
- "Never try to anticipate the market farther ahead than your signals"
- "Cinch" your profits as the market gives them to you — don't hold on past a signal because a trend looks strong
- "Your 'play' is to take what the market offers you and at the exact time of this offering"
- Don't think about any other Objective, only the immediate one
- "Never make a trade unless it favors your 'play'" — better to pass up entire session than buy/sell on a guess
- Wait until you are reasonably sure and the market makes it very plain when to buy or sell

### Multiple Books
- Take only one or at most two books to market — one for Short Sale Objective, one for Buying Objective
- This eliminates confusion — as one option moves, others will usually be in line
- After making up several books on grains and stocks → some will differ (one at Buying Objective while another at Sale or Short Sale) — this is normal continuity, follow them that way
- Nothing inconsistent — trader can have a book on two Wheat Futures, Corn or Soybeans, or stocks — one a buy and one a short sale on the same day

### Decline Zero and Rally Zero (Summary Rules)
- When low on Buying Day is higher than high of previous day (Short Sale Day) → Decline Zero → forecast of a rally, usually profitable
- Amount of rally from Short Sale Day Low plus higher opening on Buying Day causes the Decline Zero
- Lack of rally from Buying Day Low plus down opening on Selling Day causes the Rally Zero
- Rally Zero always violates the Buying Day Low

### Two Swings Per Week
- About two swings a week: one Upward and one Downward
- In Bull movement: one downward will be longer and is the swing that causes failures to penetrate Selling Objectives and causes Buying Day Low Violations
- When trading on Three Day Method — buy on Buying Day and sell out on Short Sale Day — when previous Buying Day Low is NOT violated: expect greater gains (when violated: gains are usually small, may even show a loss for the swing)

### Accumulation Using Three Day Method
- Buy part of your line only on a Buying Day, then another part on the next Buying Day, etc.
- Where a Buying Day Low may be Violated → don't buy at all, but having bought on the low and stock rallied but began to sell off near the close → stock may close on the low for the day → sell out your last purchase before the close
- Let the market alone until next Buying Day

### Whipsaw Markets
- When aimless whip-saw rallies and declines don't favor your play → stay out of the market
- Don't try to trade these — they are aimless movement

### Price at Time of Buying/Selling
- "The price you pay at the time of buying and selling has nothing to do with it — the point is to determine the correct place for buying or selling"
- In Uptrend: continue to buy and sell on a rising range of prices
- In Downtrend: do the same on a declining range of prices

### Commodity Seasonal Considerations
- Since all trends in commodities are seasonal → watch position of your future, up or down and in what month
- Months when they normally sell at highest and lowest points
- At times during last few days before option expires → technical position may cause several wide rallies and declines in same session → take your profit at the first opportunity

### Charts and This Method
- Traders who use charts in conjunction with this method → "well and good"
- Those who go in for astrology and statistics → might ask themselves if the "boy" on the floor who makes the market understands these theories
- "Perhaps not, and cares less, however, he does understand how the markets of the past were made and how well they worked and is not losing much sleep trying to alter or devise a new way"
- Intent has been to keep the method as simple as possible and rely on the fact that at these trading Objectives a trader can buy and sell with a profit
- "Use it in its most mechanical form and stay away from production and marketing statistics, if possible, for in short term trading, the trader is concerned only with the day to day price changes"

### Soybeans as Trading Vehicle
- "Soybeans have a very promising future as a trading vehicle — they are a useful and minority crop"
- Would be an astonishing revelation to make up a book and record fluctuations for 1948-49-50

### Universal Applicability
- Tape action, rise and fall of prices in the market, is the same for all
- A book is made up and traded in exactly the same way for cotton, fats and oils, or any other commodity that has continuous daily price change on the "tape"

## Addendum: Trading in Stocks (pp.87-97)

### Stock Trading Book Setup
- Next four columns are for Volume, High, Low, and Closing Prices (Volume replaces Open for stocks)
- Same 10-day cycling process as commodities
- Book order: Buying Day, 1st Selling Day, 2nd Selling Day (instead of Buy, Sell, Short Sale)
- "Never change the continuity from the way it was started"

### Abbreviations Used in Trading (p.88)
- CD = closed down on day or previous close
- CU = closed up on day or previous close
- NH = new high since last trend high
- NL = new low since last trend low
- HB = higher (support) bottom, or last trend low
- MU = mark-up, big support buying (comparative)
- MD = mark-down, big selling (comparative)
- RH = real high, on greater or less volume
- AH = actual high, on greater or less volume
- RL = real low, on greater or less volume
- AL = actual low, on greater or less volume
- TR = trend reversal (supplementary rule)
- X = up-trading area
- XX = down-trading area
- V = volume; represents all factors combined: movement, price changes, news, etc., modified as large VL or small VS (used comparatively to previous daily volume)
- VLX = increasing on rally
- VSX = decreasing on rally
- VLXX = increasing on decline
- VSXX = decreasing on decline

### The D Column for Stocks
- Shows unit of decline from high of previous day (2nd Selling Day) to low of Buying Day
- When no decline occurs: put in a Zero
- Zero shows higher support above low of previous day → generally means nearby higher prices for a day and at times longer
- When decline unit is large: rally unit is generally small
- When decline unit is small: rally unit is generally large

### The R Column for Stocks
- Shows extent of rally from low of Buying Day to high of 1st Selling Day, and amount of decline in D column recovered
- When no rally occurs: put in a Zero
- Zero shows lower support under low of previous day → generally means nearby lower prices
- D and R columns are used by tape-reading traders, using low of Buying Day and high of 1st Selling Day as their buying and selling objectives

### The 3 D R (Three Day Rally) Column
- Shows extent of rally from low of Buying Day to high of 2nd Selling Day
- When swing shows a loss: put in loss unit with "L" beside it
- When gain: put in gain unit
- When gain exceeds high of 1st Selling Day: put "T" alongside (meaning "sold through" the high or previous day)
- When comparatively large: shows big interest and volume at or prior to start or reversal of an uptrend

### The X and ✓ as Trend Indicators (for Stocks)
- X = trading objective high or low was made FIRST
- ✓ = trading objective high or low was made LAST
- These marks point the probable trend between trading sessions
- Placed in circles of a Buying Day and both Selling Days at close of market
- Most important part of active trading: buying or selling, or buying stocks for trend trading

### The Real Trend
- The Real Trend is the trend between the lows of buying days and the highs of selling days
- Distinguished from the many currents, smaller rallies and declines
- For instance: low of day may be 50 and the established high 53, with price trading between the two points as 51 3/4
- After these deviations the price will swing back to the real trend, through either 50 or 53
- These fractional or point trends interfere and confuse a trader but do not change the ultimate direction of price toward the highs or lows of the trading objectives

### Uninformed Trader Problem
- "The uninformed trader buys into the market on a reaction or low point and by luck he hits the real trend just as the price starts to rally; then he sells with a few points profit, but on his next trade he buys or sells one of these cross-currents and the trade promptly shows a loss"
- After a few trades of this kind: has a losing average

### Buying Day Example
- At opening: notice whether price is up and continuing higher (making high FIRST) or opened down and declining further (making low FIRST)
- Making high FIRST → expect selling from this point and a reaction to follow on an up-opening, wait. Don't buy on "bulges"
- Know a rally is limited and perhaps in the 3rd or 4th day up from the last Buying Day low or under
- On a down-opening: observe spread from high of previous day to low of present decline → if price is under the low of previous day → check back over D column units → compare to determine if this is a normal trading decline or a severe reaction perhaps following a change of trend
- Normal trading decline stops at a little above or below the low of previous day (low of 2nd Selling Day) — "buying spot" is around this low point when activity quiets down and trading becomes listless and dull
- Activity of the market is a good sign to watch — whether increasing or decreasing on the rise, after penetration, also after small D column decline units

### Selling Day Example
- When low is made FIRST on Buying Day with closing price nearer the high → expect up-opening or higher prices with penetration of high of previous day
- Sell at-the-market, at and above this high
- Don't sell too soon after a penetration → expect not only slight but deeper penetrations of the highs because the tops must be broken and progressively higher in uptrend trading areas
- From high close on Buying Day when opening next day is up with wide gain → sell at-the-market without waiting for the next transaction after opening price
- Price may go higher (and many times does) but the opening price may be the high and a decline may start from the next transaction that could be beginning of a down trend

### Loss Swing Statistics (Stocks)
- Out of each 100 successive (decline rally) 3-day swings examined: loss swings occurred 12 times on average
- Of the total swing points: loss points were about 3% of the gains
- Zero and "L" swings generally appear in downtrends
- When they occur in uptrends: loss is generally less than one point

### Salient Features of Important Tops and Bottoms (Stocks)
- Big market participation, interest in the stock
- Fast mark-ups or down
- Big intersession price changes (see units in 2-day column)
- Volume — are lacking
- These factors are noticeably absent after minor trend changes
- At the low: volume may or may not increase before the trend again changes
- After trend changes: volume shows a progressive increase as the rise is resumed → major movement is to be continued
- During corrective periods: trend is contained between the last important high and low
- Price of necessity fails to make new highs with the lows going lower → lows may approach or equal the low made at the last trend decline → but so long as this low holds: intervening minor trend is down
- A trader should use as his guide: the points, percentage move from last low, and length of time the trend has been running since the last important high or low → be on alert for trend change signals

### Northern Pacific Railway (NP) Stock Example (pp.93-97)
- Used throughout the Addendum as representative of all features required for active, intermediate, and primary trend trading with price appreciation
- Illustrations show the approach to highs and lows of swings (a few days prior to and after they have been made)
- Places of major and minor trend changes can be identified
- Trading characteristics remain the same regardless of movement size — big or little

### Trend Trader Accumulation Summary
- Not wise to buy a full line at once — 100 or 500 shares placed in odd lots (25 or 50 shares at a time)
- Wait for each Buying Day to place another lot → continue until full line
- Higher average cost but each lot more apt to show a profit
- If started buying at comparatively low range and was in a rising market → not likely to carry heavy load at a loss in sudden decline
- Selling: alert for danger signals at tops, use 1st and 2nd Selling Days
- "Trading technique is simply the ability through study, observation and experience, to detect the manipulation that takes place in the markets at all times, and to recognize the signals in each of the several phases of the market movement"
- Most important consideration: technical position of the stock in relation to the market as a whole — leading or trailing, position within its own group (leading or trading), and the group attention in the news

## Comments on Usage by Linda Bradford Raschke (pp.99-100)

### Raschke's Relationship to Taylor
- "I have organized much of my trading methodology and philosophy around short-term techniques, as defined by George Douglass Taylor"
- Has recommended the book as "one well worth studying"
- "Taylor was definitely a better trader than author!"

### Raschke's Reading Recommendations
- READ Chapter XV "Pertinent Points" two or three times FIRST — much better introduction to the subject matter
- SKIP Chapters 2, 3, and 4 completely
- Does not make up a "book" nor does she recommend doing so
- DOES write down the open, high, low and close and underline the 3 to 4 day swing lows and swing highs
- Keeping a hand log keeps trading "systematic"
- Where Taylor discusses Buy Day or Sell Day concepts → useful to diagram examples in the margin using candlesticks
- Read with yellow highlighter — "I have gone through several copies because they keep getting so marked up"

### Raschke's Key Interpretive Framework
- "Think in terms of CONCEPTS instead of specifics"
- Concepts include: trading within a 2-3 day time frame; price action around previous day's high/low; length of upswing relative to downswing; ignoring ALL news and fundamentals
- Concentrating strictly on what tape or price action is telling you — "That is the only 'truth' in short-term trading"
- Taylor's labels (Buy Day, Sell Day, Short Sale Day) were just his way of staying systematic, simple and consistent
- "If you read between the lines, you'll see his specific rules for shorting on 'buy' days and buying on 'sell short' days"
- It really has to do with how many days up/down the market is from the previous swing low/high

### Raschke's Bigger Picture Insight
- Though trading on a 1-2 day basis, BE CONSCIOUS OF THE BIGGER PICTURE
- Even Taylor said to skip the first "buy" trade when breaking down out of a congestion area
- Wait five to six days before looking to buy, instead of two or three
- Taylor emphasized importance of overall long-term and seasonal trends
- "Understanding markets includes recognizing when price is discounted and when marked up and watching for signs of support by interests larger than ourselves"

### Raschke's Market Models
- Found the most successful mechanical systems/price patterns work because they use the concept of "in one day, out the next"
- Examples: Larry Williams' patterns, Bob Buran's methodology, Toby Crabel's studies
- Key concept: "Enter on a trigger or pivot, exit on the next day's opening or close"
- Tests patterns on a close-to-close basis — enter on close, exit on close one or two days later
- Systems have no more than two parameters — crude models that highlight market tendency
- Example: "selling a higher opening after two higher closes" illustrates a market's tendency to pull back at that point
- Models indicate whether odds favor being a buyer or seller for the day → then use Taylor's "rules" to trade as if it is a "buy day" or "sell short" day

### Raschke's Trading Rules
- Hold the trade overnight and exit according to Taylor's guidelines
- "Don't carry losing positions overnight" — better to exit with intention of re-entering at a better trade location the next trading day
- Adapted Taylor's techniques for S&P trading
- Taylor emphatically teaches: they must NEVER average down
- "I have yet to come across a consistent, winning trader who practices this most destructive of habits"
- Many of Taylor's rules are defensive in nature — e.g., exiting on the first reaction when a market does not act "correctly"
- "Following Taylor's guidelines diligently should develop within you the best possible trading 'habits'. Ultimately, it is one's trading habits which govern one's success"

### How Raschke Discovered Taylor
- First came across Taylor's name in George Angell's book "Winning in the Futures Market," specifically the chapter referring to the "LSS Method"
