Key Takeaways

  1. The lucky fool is everywhere. A person who benefits from a disproportionate share of luck but attributes success to skill. This confusion pervades politics, business, science, and especially financial markets.
  2. Survivorship bias blinds us. We see only the winners and reverse-engineer explanations for their success. A million traders start; a few become rich purely by chance. We study the survivors and assume skill, never accounting for the silent graveyard of identical strategies that failed.
  3. Asymmetry is what matters, not frequency. Don’t ask how often you are right — ask how much you make when right vs. how much you lose when wrong. A strategy that loses 999 out of 1,000 times can be wildly profitable if the single win is large enough.
  4. Rare events dominate outcomes. The world is driven by rare, large-impact events that are systematically underestimated. Calm periods breed complacency. The longer you go without a blowup, the worse it will be when it arrives.
  5. Monte Carlo methods reveal the role of luck. Running thousands of simulated alternative histories shows that many real-world “track records” are statistically indistinguishable from pure chance.
  6. Popper’s falsificationism is the antidote. You can never prove a theory right, only prove it wrong. Use data to disprove, never to prove. There are two types of theories: those proven wrong, and those not yet proven wrong.
  7. Noise increases faster than signal. At shorter timeframes, randomness overwhelms information. Checking your portfolio minutely is 99.5% noise; yearly it is closer to 50/50.
  8. We are wired for pattern detection, not randomness. Cognitive biases are biological, not cultural. Skinner’s pigeons developed elaborate rain dances from random food delivery. We are the pigeons.
  9. Path dependence traps beliefs. Once committed to a position, we become married to it. The best traders (like Soros) reverse their opinion without embarrassment — completely free from path dependence.
  10. Emotions override knowledge. Even a probability expert cannot will his heart to match his brain. The solution is not self-discipline but environmental design — tricks like Odysseus tying himself to the mast.
  11. Dignity is the only rational response to randomness. Since outcomes are substantially random, we can only control our behavior. The stoic tradition prescribes elegance in the face of fortune. “The only article Lady Fortuna has no control over is your behavior.”

Contrarian Ideas

  1. Track records are nearly meaningless over short periods. Even 10 years of outperformance can be explained by chance given enough practitioners. Fund selection based on track record is a sucker’s game.
  2. “Risk managers” who measure risk create risk. Markowitz, Merton, and Scholes thought they could scientifically measure risk; the result was LTCM’s catastrophic blowup. The illusion of measurability encourages overleveraging.
  3. Journalism is toxic to rational thinking. It systematically overweights recent noise, constructs causal narratives from randomness, and triggers emotional responses. Turn off CNBC — or at least mute it.
  4. Evolution can be fooled by randomness too. “Survival of the fittest” operates on averages over very long time horizons. Any individual animal could have survived through a lucky sample path.
  5. Science evolves from funeral to funeral. Individual scientists are biased, path-dependent, and defensive. But science as a system self-corrects — when the old guard dies, new knowledge gets integrated.
  6. Self-contradiction is a virtue. Carneades argued brilliantly for and against justice on consecutive days. Path independence of beliefs — changing your mind without shame — is the mark of a real thinker and trader.

Author’s Key Proposals

  1. Think in alternative histories. Don’t evaluate outcomes — evaluate the quality of the decision given all possible paths. Russian roulette winners are not role models.
  2. Be long optionality. Structure your career and portfolio to benefit from rare events rather than being destroyed by them. Buy options, don’t sell them. Prefer asymmetric payoffs.
  3. Minimize exposure to noise. Check results less often. Read fewer newspapers. The less frequently you observe, the higher the signal-to-noise ratio.
  4. Use Popper’s method. Seek to disprove your hypotheses, not confirm them. Any strategy “validated” by backtesting is suspect until it has survived genuine out-of-sample stress.
  5. Distinguish Mediocristan from Extremistan. Some domains are governed by mild randomness (height); others by extreme randomness (wealth, markets). Methods that work in one fail catastrophically in the other.
  6. Practice stoic detachment from results. You can only control your process, not outcomes. Exhibit personal elegance regardless of Fortune’s verdict.
  7. Stay humble about what you know. “Start every meeting by convincing everyone that we are a bunch of idiots who know nothing and are mistake-prone, but happen to be endowed with the rare privilege of knowing it.”

Relevance to Trading & Investing

  • Asymmetric trading: Taleb makes money infrequently from rare events, losing small amounts regularly — the opposite of most traders who make small amounts regularly until a blowup wipes them out.
  • The 70/30 bull/bear paradox: A market can have 70% probability of going up but still be better to short, if the expected down move is 10% vs. the expected up move of 1%.
  • Frequency vs. expected value: Don’t confuse being right often with being profitable. Frequency of wins is irrelevant; expected value (probability × magnitude) is everything.
  • Blowup risk: Strategies that “never lose” are the most dangerous — collecting pennies in front of a steamroller.
  • Noise filtering: Apply elementary methods to separate signal from noise. A $100K profit may have only 2% probability of being skill; a $1M gain may be 99%. Act accordingly.
  • The LTCM lesson: No mathematical model can capture the full range of market behavior. Rare events are by definition outside the model. Build strategies that survive model failure.
  • The Soros method: Be completely free from past positions. Every day is a clean slate. Change your mind instantly when evidence warrants it.
  • The dentist’s pain: A strategy with 93% annual probability of gain causes emotional torture if checked minutely (only 50.2% positive minutes). Optimal monitoring frequency: as infrequent as bearable.

Prologue: Mosques in the Clouds

Core thesis: “This book is about luck disguised and perceived as non-luck (that is, skills) and, more generally, randomness disguised and perceived as non-randomness (that is, determinism).”

Manifests as the lucky fool: a person who benefited from a disproportionate share of luck but attributes his success to some other, generally very precise, reason. The confusion is endemic in politics (“a country’s president discoursing on the jobs that ‘he’ created”), business (biographies presenting “their specific explanation on how they made it big”), and even science.

The Table of Confusion

The central distinctions of the book — left is mistaken for right:

DomainMistaken for……Actually
GeneralLuck, Randomness, ProbabilitySkill, Determinism, Certainty
MarketsLucky idiot, Survivorship biasSkilled investor, Outperformance
FinanceVolatility, Stochastic variableReturn, Deterministic variable
PhysicsNoiseSignal

Humans are “intentionally prone to the confusion between noise and meaning.” We give meaning to all manner of shapes, detect human figures in inkblots. Rimbaud: “I saw mosques in the clouds.”

The Cost Asymmetry

Mistaking randomness for skill is more costly than the reverse. “Bad information is worse than no information at all.” Probability theory is a young arrival in mathematics; probability applied to practice is “almost nonexistent as a discipline.”

Part I: Solon’s Warning — Skewness, Asymmetry, Induction

Chapter 1: If You’re So Rich Why Aren’t You So Smart?

Nero Tulip — Character Portrait

Semi-autobiographical character. Became obsessed with trading after witnessing a scene at the Chicago Mercantile Exchange — a red Porsche screeches up, a flushed trader runs in chased by the sound of pigs being slaughtered, then a bored young clerk in a yellow jacket descends the steps unfazed. The contrast struck Nero as a coup de foudre.

Background: undergraduate in ancient literature and mathematics from Cambridge, Ph.D. at University of Chicago (switched from statistics to philosophy — “a moment of temporary sanity”). Career arc: academy → floor trader → desk trader → quantitative financial products → brief Wall Street stardom → proprietary trader.

Trading philosophy: extremely risk-averse. “I am shooting for longevity.” Never sells naked options. Rapidly exits after a predetermined loss. Never puts himself in a position to lose more than $1M regardless of probability. “I love taking small losses. I just need my winners to be large.”

Lives modestly despite seven-figure income — apartment full of 4,000 books, after-tax savings about $500K/year into Treasury bonds. Did not invest in the stock market during the bull market.

The Solon-Croesus Warning

Croesus, King of Lydia, was the richest man of his time. Solon, the Greek legislator, showed zero admiration for his wealth. Pushed to name a happier man, Solon cited men who lived noble lives and died in battle: “The observation of the numerous misfortunes that attend all conditions forbids us to grow insolent upon our present enjoyments.”

Solon’s key insight: what comes with luck can be taken away by luck — things that come with little help from luck are more resistant to randomness. Croesus later lost to Persia and was about to be burned alive, shouting “Solon, you were right.”

John, the High-Yield Trader

Nero’s neighbor — aggressive trader in high-yield bonds with “the intellectual depth and sharpness of mind of an aerobics instructor.” Making at least 10x Nero’s income. Two top-of-the-line German cars, collectible Ferrari, massive house that keeps getting bigger.

His strategy involves invisible risk: the high-yield market “resembles a nap on a railway track” — make money month after month, then lose a multiple of cumulative performance in a few hours. Nero’s seemingly modest returns are risk-adjusted superior. He bets that over any sufficiently long period, John-type traders will blow up.

The Pecking Order Problem

Kahneman and Tversky showed people prefer to make $70K when others make $60K than to make $80K when others make $90K — it’s all relative. Despite intellectual contempt, Nero couldn’t prevent himself from judging his condition relative to John. The house across the street kept getting bigger.

In September 1998 (Russian crisis / LTCM), Nero was vindicated. John was fired, lost almost everything. “He would no longer question his trading style when others were getting rich because they misunderstood the structure of randomness.”

Serotonin and Randomness

Lucky fools don’t know they’re lucky. Success releases serotonin, creating a positive feedback loop — dominant posture, confident style, increased “leadership ability.” Monkeys injected with serotonin rise in the hierarchy. But when the cycle breaks, it reverses viciously.

One cab driver near the Chicago Board of Trade could tell if traders were doing well: “They get all puffed up.”

Your Dentist Is Rich, Very Rich

A dentist is “considerably richer than the rock musician driven in a pink Rolls Royce” — in expectation. Relive the dentist’s life a million times: narrow range of outcomes. Relive the lottery winner’s life a million times: almost all paths show janitorial activities and fruitless tickets. You cannot consider a profession without taking into account the average of all who enter it, not just the survivors.

Chapter 2: A Bizarre Accounting Method

Alternative Histories and Russian Roulette

Any visible outcome is just one realization from many possible paths. Russian roulette may produce a $10 million payoff 5 times out of 6 — but judging the player only by the visible outcome is insane. “One cannot judge a performance in any given field by the results, but by the costs of the alternative.”

Reality is “far more vicious” than simple Russian roulette: it is “like a revolver that would have hundreds, even thousands of chambers instead of six.” After a few dozen tries, one forgets the bullet exists — a “numbing false sense of security.” Unlike the game where risks are visible, in markets “one does not observe the barrel of reality.”

The Cemetery of Invisible Failures

Behind every spectacular success stand the corpses of identically positioned people who tried the same thing and failed. Heroes are often heroes by accident. Journalists and commentators overfit narratives to outcomes: “Everything in past history seems to be perfectly explainable — after the fact.”

Kenny vs. Jean-Patrice: Two Kinds of Bosses

Taleb’s two bosses illustrate contrasting approaches. Kenny: suburban family man, epitome of trustworthiness, rose rapidly despite lacking technical competence — “too much a no-nonsense person to make out my logic.” Jean-Patrice: moody Frenchman, hyper-aggressive, rare person who “had the guts to care only about the generator, entirely oblivious of the results.” Called Taleb at 3am from nightclubs to discuss risk.

Kenny climbed the ladder but all his traders “have all exited the business.” Jean-Patrice didn’t have a happy career institutionally but taught Taleb to “beware of mature financial institutions.”

George Will vs. Robert Shiller

Shiller had been pronouncing the stock market overpriced. George Will pointed out that those who listened to Shiller lost money as the market doubled. But Shiller was a scientist, not a prophet: his views on market irrationality are not invalidated by being wrong in the past. “Mixing forecast and prophecy is symptomatic of randomness foolishness.”

Furthermore: “What sounds intelligent in a conversation or a meeting, or, particularly in the media, is suspicious.” Einstein’s remark: “Common sense is nothing but a collection of misconceptions acquired by age 18.”

Risk Managers

Someone supposed to monitor the institution and verify it is not playing Russian roulette. Their dilemma: “the generator of reality is not observable.” They can’t stop profitable traders from taking risks (ex post they’d be accused of costing the firm money), but a blowup is their responsibility. “My greatest risk is to become successful, as it would mean that my business is about to disappear.”

Chapter 3: A Mathematical Meditation on History

Monte Carlo Simulation

Taleb introduces Monte Carlo methods — generating thousands of artificial histories by random sampling. “Mathematics is principally a tool to meditate, rather than to compute.” The stereotype of the mathematician is “an anemic man with a shaggy beard,” but Monte Carlo conjures “a suntanned urbane man of the Europlayboy variety entering a casino.”

Pioneered in martial physics at Los Alamos during A-bomb preparation. Computer programs simulate virtually anything. Key experiment: start with 10,000 fictional traders, each with 50/50 chance of making or losing $10K per year. After 5 years, roughly 313 traders will have made money every single year. “The population of these final winners would have a very interesting set of beliefs about their investment strategy.”

Denigration of History

“Learning from history does not come naturally to us humans.” Children learn only from their own mistakes (the stove). Adults too — Kahneman and Tversky confirmed we are “extremely lax in the area of detection and prevention” of risks from others’ experience.

All colleagues who blew up spectacularly — 1987, Japan 1990, bonds 1994, Russia 1998, Nasdaq 2000 — showed “remarkable similarities in their approaches” and all claimed “these times are different.” They were “unable to accept that the experience of others was out there, in the open, freely available to all, with books detailing crashes in every bookstore.”

The Dentist’s Paradox

A dentist who is a competent investor (15% return, 10% volatility) will see a positive result:

  • 93% of any given year — pleasing
  • 67% of any given month — still okay
  • 54% of any given day — starting to hurt
  • 51.3% of any given hour — painful
  • 50.02% of any given second — pure torture

Noise-to-signal ratios: Over 1 year: 0.7 noise per 1 signal. Over 1 month: 2.32. Over 1 hour: 30. Over 1 second: 1,796 noise per signal.

Given prospect theory (losses hurt roughly 2.5x as much as gains feel good), looking at the screen every second delivers net emotional pain despite being a winning strategy. The dentist checking daily: 241 pleasurable minutes vs. 239 unpleasurable ones in an 8-hour day. Checking yearly: 19 pleasant surprises for every unpleasant one. The solution: check less often.

Distilled Thinking vs. Cheap Thinking

“Only the distilled form of thought should be relied on for our decisions.” Monte Carlo simulations of heterogeneous trader populations showed “significant advantage in selecting aged traders, using cumulative years of experience rather than their absolute success.” Under regime switching, the oldest are most resistant — having been exposed to rare events longer.

Life insurers in Renaissance Italy charged the same insurance rate for a man in his 20s as one in his 50s — “once a man crossed the 40-year mark, he had shown that very few ailments could harm him.”

Chapter 4: Randomness, Nonsense, and the Scientific Intellectual

The Vienna Circle and Logical Positivism

The Vienna Circle (1930s) wanted to strip thinking from rhetoric. Introduced rigor: statements are either deductive (“2 + 2 = 4”) or inductive (“it rains in Spain”). Anything else was “plain unadulterated hogwash.”

Connection to finance: most market commentary is neither verifiable nor tautological — it is “empty of meaning, like the ‘It will rain tomorrow’ uttered by the sage who covered himself by adding ‘unless it doesn’t.’”

The Reverse Turing Test

Turing test: a computer is intelligent if it can fool a human. The converse: “a human can be said to be unintelligent if we can replicate his speech by a computer.” The Dada Engine (Andrew C. Bulha, Monash University) generates postmodernist papers randomly — producing sentences indistinguishable from real Derrida. Taleb applied the same logic to business speeches: randomly select five phrases and connect them with grammatical minimum → produces a CEO speech indistinguishable from the real thing.

References Alan Sokal’s Fashionable Nonsense — a hoax paper published in a prominent journal, generating Monte Carlo-like nonsense that fooled postmodernist editors.

The Father of All Pseudothinkers

Hegel: “the father of all pseudothinkers” — writes jargon “meaningless outside of a chic Left-Bank Parisian café.” Even a Monte Carlo engine couldn’t sound as random as Hegel. Hegelian thinking linked to Marxist regimes — “these ‘thinkers’ should be given an undergraduate-level class on statistical sampling theory prior to their release in the open world.”

Monte Carlo Poetry and Exquisite Cadavers

There are instances where Taleb likes to be fooled by randomness — art and poetry. “If I am going to be fooled by randomness, it better be of the beautiful (and harmless) kind.”

Surrealist poets (André Breton, Paul Eluard) in post-WWI cafés created poems by randomly writing parts of sentences on folded paper. First publicized result: “The exquisite cadavers shall drink the new wine” (Les cadavres exquis boiront le vin nouveau). Some random combinations are ravishingly beautiful.

Key insight: “We do not need to be rational and scientific when it comes to the details of our daily life — only in those that can harm us and threaten our survival.” Be rational about risk; be irrational (aesthetic) about art. Colleagues who mock his love of Baudelaire “get sucked into listening to the ‘analyses’ of a television ‘guru.’”

Chapter 5: Survival of the Least Fit

Carlos the Emerging Markets Wizard

Patrician Latin-American, impeccably dressed, traded emerging market Brady bonds. The community: cosmopolitan patricians — “Mexicans based in London trade Russian securities, Iranians specialize in Brazilian bonds.” They are “generally urbane, dress well, collect art, but are non-intellectual.” Hold Metropolitan Opera season tickets.

Carlos bought dips — accumulated more when prices fell, surviving the 1995 Mexican devaluation and the 1997 dip. His string of successes made him feel invincible. “He could do no wrong.”

Summer 1998: the market turned. He averaged down aggressively. Russian bonds dropped from ~$52 to the $20s. He put $5 million of personal money ($14M net worth) in Russia Principal Bonds at $48: “I will retire on these profits.” His “lines in the sand” kept being crossed. By August, Russian bonds were at $10. Carlos was dismissed.

The firehouse effect (veteran trader Marty O’Connell): “firemen who talk to each other for too long come to agree on many things that an outside, impartial observer would find ludicrous.”

John the High-Yield Trader — Full Story

Pace Business School grad, rose to head 10 traders. By age 32, net worth $1M; by 35, it exceeded $16M ($14M invested in his own trades). Business model: buy “cheap” high-yield bonds at 10% yield, borrow at 5.5%, pocket the 4.5% differential, leveraged massively. “$3 billion in face value” across continents.

Henry the quant: Operations Research grad student whose risk calculations gave the event that destroyed John a probability of “1 in 1,000,000,000,000,000,000,000,000 years” — a “ten sigma” event.

John’s reaction to losses: “One would go crazy if one were to listen to the mood swings of the market” — he called it “noise” that would “mean revert.” But the “noise” kept adding in the same direction. “In a biblical cycle, it took seven years to make John a hero and just seven days to make him an idiot.” He lost $600 million for his last employer in barely a few days.

Traits of Market Fools

Both Carlos and John were dip buyers — the most desirable style between 1992–1998. They replaced traders who were perhaps better but didn’t fit the latest cycle. Shared traits:

  • Overestimation of accuracy: Never considered that past success might be coincidental
  • Married to positions: “Traders divorce their spouse sooner than abandon their positions”
  • Changed the story: Became “long haul” investors after losses to postpone denial
  • No precise game plan: Simply not aware that they could lose
  • No stop losses: Did not consider their method might be wrong
  • Denial: When losses occurred, “the price on the screen lost its reality in favor of some abstract ‘value’”

Naive Evolutionary Theories

Bad traders have a short- and medium-term survival advantage over good traders. Darwinian fitness applies over very long time, not short term — “time aggregation eliminates much of the effects of randomness.”

Natura non facit saltus (nature doesn’t make jumps) “turned out to be entirely wrong, as it was denied by quantum mechanics.” Particles jump discretely between states. When randomness changes shape (regime switches), all attributes become unrecognizable.

Can evolution be fooled? Yes. On average animals will be fit, but any individual could have survived because its sample path was lucky. “The longer these animals can go without encountering the rare event, the more vulnerable they will be to it.” By ergodicity, extend time to infinity and the rare event will happen with certainty — “the species will be wiped out!”

Chapter 6: Skewness and Asymmetry

The Median Is Not the Message

Stephen Jay Gould, diagnosed with deadly cancer, was told median survival was eight months. But those who die do so early; those who survive go on living “generally going about life just like a regular person.” Average survival was considerably higher than the median — prompting Gould’s famous essay “The Median is Not the Message.”

People confuse probability with expectation (probability times the payoff). Most schooling uses symmetric examples (coin tosses) where this distinction doesn’t matter. “In fact the so-called ‘Bell Curve’ that seems to have found universal use in society is entirely symmetric.”

The frequency of the loss is irrelevant; the magnitude is everything. “It is not how likely an event is to happen that matters, it is how much is made when it happens.”

Bull and Bear Zoology

“Bullish” and “bearish” are hollow in an asymmetric world. At a discussion meeting, Taleb stated the market would go up with about 70% probability — yet he was massively short. Explanation: 70% chance of going up 1% but 30% chance of going down 10%. Expected value: −2.3%.

“I did not change my mind! I have a lot of faith in my bet! As a matter of fact I now feel like selling even more!”

An Arrogant 29-Year-Old Son

A friend’s wealthy father wanted Taleb to predict European bond markets by Christmas. Taleb refused — he doesn’t make predictions, he studies the structure of randomness. The father complained: “No specialist ever gives me disrespect. Your insolent and conceited 29-year-old son is playing prima donna and refuses to answer me about the direction of the market!”

The Rare Event Fallacy

The peso problem: Mexican economic variables during the 1980s switched erratically between stability and turbulence. Currencies with the greatest historical stability are most prone to crashes (Asian crisis 1997: pegged currencies of Malaysia, Indonesia, Thailand). “Beware of calm waters.”

Stationarity problem: If the distribution itself changes over time — someone mischievously adding and removing balls from the urn — we may never know the urn’s composition. “Much of our knowledge derived through statistics is quite shaky.”

The Lucas critique: Robert Lucas argued that if people are rational, they’d figure out predictable patterns and arbitrage them away — making past data unreliable for predicting the future.

Chapter 7: The Problem of Induction

From Bacon to Hume

Francis Bacon shifted science from scholasticism to empirical observation. But Hume warned that without proper method, empirical observations can lead astray.

Hume’s black swan: “No amount of observations of white swans can allow the inference that all swans are white, but the observation of a single black swan is sufficient to refute that conclusion.”

The absurdity of naive induction:

“I have just completed a thorough statistical examination of the life of President Bush. For 55 years, close to 16,000 observations, he did not die once. I can hence pronounce him as immortal, with a high degree of statistical significance.”

Niederhoffer vs. Taleb

Victor Niederhoffer: finance’s Francis Bacon — stood against the “cobweb of learning” and the efficient market religion of Chicago. Looked at data in search of anomalies, built a successful career. His trainees who added rigor and methodology to their statistical inference fared better than the master.

Key difference: “I can use data to disprove a proposition, never to prove one. I can use history to refute a conjecture, never to affirm it.”

Both Taleb and Niederhoffer lead Victorian gentleman-scholar lives scattered with books. But they trade in diametrically opposite ways: Niederhoffer sells out-of-the-money options (betting rare events won’t happen), Taleb buys them (betting they may).

Discovering Popper Through Soros

Taleb discovered Karl Popper through George Soros’s writings. Soros “presented himself as promoter of Popper’s ideas, not a mere trader” — the only wealthy person Taleb looked at as remotely a role model. Soros knew how to handle randomness by keeping a critical open mind, changing opinions with minimal shame.

Taleb found Popper at a Barnes & Noble on 21st Street and Fifth Avenue in 1987. Read 50 pages of The Logic of Scientific Discovery — “the thoughts that rushed through my head like a revelation.” Memory is tied to geography: Paul Claudel remembered the exact column in Notre-Dame where he converted to Catholicism.

Popper’s Answer: Falsificationism

Two types of theories:

  1. Theories known to be wrong — tested and adequately rejected (he calls them falsified).
  2. Theories not yet known to be wrong — not falsified yet, but exposed to being proved wrong.

A theory cannot be verified. Newtonian physics is scientific because it allowed us to falsify it (Einstein’s relativity proved it wrong). Astrology is not scientific because it does not offer conditions under which we could disprove it.

“These are men with bold ideas, but highly critical of their own ideas; they try to find whether their ideas are right by trying first to find whether they are not perhaps wrong.”

Open Society and Falsificationism

An open society allows no permanent truth to exist; counterideas can emerge. Popper believed any idea of Utopia is necessarily closed — “a simple notion of a good model for society that cannot be left open for falsification is totalitarian.” Shared ideas with Von Hayek, who endorsed capitalism as a state where prices disseminate information that bureaucratic socialism would choke.

Popper himself was rather un-Popperian in person: “brilliant, but self-focused, both insecure and arrogant, irascible and self-righteous. He was a terrible listener and bent on winning arguments at all costs.” The divorce between idea carriers and practitioners is common.

Pascal’s Wager

Taleb’s variation: “I will use statistics and inductive methods to make aggressive bets, but I will not use them to manage my risks and exposure.” Take the benefits of statistics but not the tail risks. Beneficial asymmetry.

All surviving traders Taleb knows trade on ideas based on observation (including past history), but like Popperian scientists, make sure costs of being wrong are limited. Unlike Carlos and John, they know before getting involved which events would prove their conjecture wrong — the stop loss, a predetermined exit point, protection from the black swan.

Part II: Monkeys on Typewriters — Survivorship and Other Biases

“If one puts an infinite number of monkeys in front of (strongly built) typewriters, and lets them clap away, there is a certainty that one of them would come out with an exact version of the Iliad.” But would you bet your savings the monkey would write the Odyssey next?

Chapter 8: Too Many Millionaires Next Door

Marc and Janet — The Survivorship Trap

Marc: Park Avenue lawyer, $500K/year, Harvard College and Yale Law, rotund, overworked. His wife Janet feels he is a failure by comparison — because their co-op is inhabited by corporate executives, Wall Street traders, and trophy wives. Compared to the general U.S. population, Marc has done better than 99.5% of his compatriots. Compared to Harvard classmates, better than 90%. Compared to Yale Law, better than 60%. Compared to co-op neighbors: at the bottom.

Why? Because he chose to live among people who have been successful — “in an area that excludes failure. Those who have failed do not show up in the sample at all.” Taleb’s advice: move to a low-key blue-collar neighborhood where the deformation works in reverse.

Double Survivorship Bias

The Millionaire Next Door by Stanley and Danko claims frugal, boring “accumulators” become wealthy. But the study suffers from double survivorship bias:

  1. Visibility winners: Only the currently rich were studied. No attempt to count the “accumulators” who accumulated the wrong things — managed currencies about to be devalued, stocks of companies that went bust.
  2. Bull market bias: Virtually all subjects became rich from asset price inflation starting in 1982. “An investor who engaged in the same strategy during less august days for the market would certainly have different story to tell.” Imagine the book written in 1935, or consider those who bought Lebanese lira Treasury bills, junk bonds from Michael Milken, or Russian Imperial bonds bearing the signature of Czar Nicholas II.

Taleb also notes he sees “no special heroism in accumulating money, particularly if the person is foolish enough to not even try to derive tangible benefit from wealth, aside from the pleasure of regularly counting beans.”

Population Size Matters

With 10,000 fund managers, some will beat the market 10 years running by pure chance. If five monkeys produced the Iliad, one might suspect reincarnation of Homer. If a billion, you wouldn’t be impressed. “I have rarely seen anyone count the monkeys.” The more practitioners in a field, the more “track records” are created by luck.

Chapter 9: It Is Easier To Buy and Sell Than Fry An Egg

Placebo Investors — Monte Carlo Proof

Create 10,000 fictional managers, each with 50/50 chance of making or losing $10K per year (fire anyone with a losing year). After year 1: 5,000 survive. Year 2: 2,500. Year 3: 1,250. Year 4: 625. Year 5: 313 — in a fair game, 313 managers with perfect five-year track records, out of pure luck.

Push further: make the odds 45/55 against each manager (incompetent by design). After 5 years: still 184 managers with unblemished records. Less than 2% of the original cohort — “but they will get attention. Nobody will mention the other 98%.”

The Earnings Season

Wall Street analysts are trained to find accounting tricks but not to deal with randomness. When a company shows increased earnings once, no attention. Twice, it shows up on screens. Three times, it merits a “strong buy.” Start with 10,000 companies barely returning the risk-free rate — after three years, 1,250 “stars” emerge by pure chance.

Apply the same reasoning to investment categories: standing in 1900, you might rationally have bought stocks of Argentina, Imperial Russia, and the United States. The rest of the story is well known — “the investor in Imperial Russia would have no better than medium-quality wallpaper in his hands.”

Cancer Cures and Data Mining

TV vendors present testimonials of cancer patients cured by vitamins. The mechanism: spontaneous remission — a very small minority of patients whose immune systems eradicate cancer cells. They would have been equally cured “by drinking a glass of Vermont spring water.” Linus Pauling, Nobel laureate in chemistry, contributed to the vitamin C myth from his bully pulpit.

Carl Sagan examined cure rates from visits to Lourdes and found the cure rate was, if anything, lower than the statistical rate for spontaneous remission.

Professor Pearson Goes to Monte Carlo

Karl Pearson examined millions of roulette runs at Monte Carlo in July 1902 and discovered with high statistical significance that the runs were not purely random. But the data had been fabricated by journalists. Real randomness does not look random — “a single random run is bound to exhibit some pattern if one looks hard enough.”

Cancer clusters: 16 random darts on a 16-square grid will rarely land one per square — clusters are expected. Transpose to a map, and newspapers declare radiation in the clustered area. Sullivan, Timmerman, and White (1999) showed that technical trading rules that appeared profitable were entirely explainable by data snooping bias.

“I Have No Conclusion”

“When is it truly not luck?” — Taleb prefers to remain skeptic. “Never said that every rich man is idiotic and every unsuccessful person unlucky. Only that in absence of much additional information, I prefer to reserve judgment. It is safer.”

Chapter 10: Loser Takes All — On the Nonlinearities of Life

The Sandpile Effect

Nonlinearity: small advantages translate into highly disproportionate payoffs. One extra grain of sand causes the entire pile to collapse. “Pascal said that if Cleopatra’s nose had been slightly shorter, the world’s fate would have changed.”

Survivorship bias in acting: the actor who lands the lead role may have had some skill but also a specific charm or physical trait — otherwise he would be “serving caffe latte at Starbucks.” Fame has its own dynamics: becomes known because he is known, a rotating helix selection process.

QWERTY and Path Dependence

The QWERTY keyboard was arranged non-optimally to slow typing and avoid jamming ribbons. Despite better word processors, multiple attempts to rationalize the keyboard failed. People trained on QWERTY; habits are hard to change — a path dependent outcome that thwarts mathematical modeling.

Polya Process and the Matthew Effect

An urn with red and black balls. Every time you draw a red ball, you add a red ball. Over time, small initial advantages compound — creating lock-in effects. Winning probability increases after past wins, decreases after past losses, producing huge variance: astonishing successes and large failures.

Compare roulette (independent draws, no path dependence) vs. the Polya process (independence violated). The Matthew Effect in academic careers: early success breeds further success through citation advantages and preferential attachment.

Complexity Theorists and the Santa Fe Institute

Economists in a rush introduced mathematical modeling techniques (culprits: Walras, Debreu, Samuelson). The math they dealt with “didn’t work in the real world.” Complexity theorists at the Santa Fe Institute came to the rescue: Monte Carlo simulations “get results where math fails us, free from traps of inferior math.”

Buridan’s Donkey

A donkey equidistant between food and water starves because it cannot choose. Inject randomness: a tiny random nudge breaks the symmetry. Named after 14th-century philosopher Jean Buridan (who “had an interesting death — thrown in the Seine tied in a bag and drowned”). Randomness can be beneficial when systems are stuck.

When It Rains, It Pours

The world’s bi-polarity: either succeeds wildly attracting all cash, or fails to draw a single penny. Books: either everyone wants to publish, or nobody interested in returning calls. With a profound Mediterranean sense of metrion (measure): “too much success is punishment, too much failure is demoralizing. I would like the option of having neither.”

Chapter 11: Randomness and Our Brain: We Are Probability Blind

Paris vs. the Bahamas

Two vacation options: 50% Paris (Musée d’Orsay, Pissarro), 50% Caribbean (towel on the beach with Tom Clancy and Amianus Marcellinus). The brain can handle one and only one state at a time — a linear combination of two states. We imagine the expected value rather than computing it mathematically. Left to our own devices, one of the states dominates the picture.

Nero’s Cancer Diagnosis

Nero’s secret: at 35, in a doctor’s office overlooking a bland Upper East Side backyard, the news was delivered with a grave voice: “I have some... I got the pathology report... It’s... It is not as bad as it sounds... It’s cancer.” The declaration caused an electric discharge running through his knees. What scared him was not the news but the sight of the doctor — too much fear in the doctor’s eyes.

At the medical library that night, drenched from hours of rain, he read: “72% 5-year actuarially adjusted survival rate.” In his mind, 28% chance of death = image of himself dead, cumbersome funeral details. 72% survival = cheerful planning, skiing in the Alps. At no point did Nero think of himself as 72% alive and 28% dead.

The O.J. Simpson Error

One Harvard lawyer used the specious argument that only 10% of men who brutalize wives go on to murder them. But the correct calculation: given that a battered wife is dead, what percentage were killed by their husband? Answer: about 50%. People routinely confuse conditional probabilities: P(A|B) is not P(B|A).

“The probability of being a terrorist given that you are Muslim” vs. “the probability of being Muslim given that you are a terrorist” are radically different numbers. “How can we expect an untrained person to understand randomness when a Harvard professor who deals and teaches probabilistic evidence can make such an incorrect statement?”

Medical Test False Positives

A test with 5% false positives for a disease striking 1/1,000. A positive result means only ~2% probability of actually having the disease (1 true positive out of ~51 total positives). Most doctors answered 95%. Less than one in five got it right.

Option Blindness

People undervalue options because they cannot correctly evaluate instruments with uncertain payoffs. A stock at $100, an option to buy at $110: 90% chance it expires worthless, but 10% chance it’s worth an average of $10. Selling for $1 is not “free money” — the seller confuses expected value with the most likely scenario.

Two categories of option traders: premium sellers (steady income until catastrophic blowup) and premium buyers (steady losses until big payoff). “Option sellers eat like chickens and go to the bathroom like elephants.” Most are premium sellers — “when they blow up, it is generally other people’s money.”

Kahneman-Tversky’s Prospect Theory

The most influential economists of the century — not Keynes, not Samuelson, not Friedman, but Kahneman and Tversky. Their research: we do not correctly understand contingencies, and even when we do, we don’t consider them in behavior.

Losses hurt approximately twice as much as equivalent gains feel good. The conjunction fallacy: people assigned higher probability to “feminist bank teller” than “bank teller.” Normative economics studies how people should behave (efficient markets); positive economics studies how they actually behave. Physics is inherently positive; economics is predominantly normative.

Bloomberg and the Life Expectancy Fallacy

Bloomberg terminals explain pure noise: “Dow is up 1.03 on lower interest rates.” A move of 0.01% warrants no explanation. A headline of Dow moving by 1.3 points “has less than one millionth of the significance of a serious 7% drop.”

TV financial gurus commit the life expectancy fallacy: “Average American is expected to live 73 years, so if you are 68 you have five more years.” What if you are 80? Life expectancy −7 years? Conditional life expectancy increases as you age — “other people, by dying, have taken your spot in the statistics.”

Damasio: The Emotional Brain

Antonio Damasio’s Descartes’ Error: patients with damage to the emotional brain cannot make decisions at all. Emotions are not the enemy of rationality — they are necessary for it. We think with emotions and there is no way around it. But they evolved for a world without probability computation, newspapers, and millisecond portfolio updates.

Engineers use filtering methods to clean up noise from signal — small amplitude changes are likely noise; the likelihood of being signal increases exponentially with magnitude. Our brain cannot perform such filtering by itself.

Part III: Wax in My Ears — Living with Randomitis

Odysseus, forewarned by Circe, contrived a ruse against the sirens: filled his men’s ears with wax, had himself tied to the mast. As they approached, the music was so ravishing he struggled to get loose — but the men tied him further.

“The first lesson I took from the story is not to even attempt to be Odysseus. He is a mythological character and I am not. He can be tied to the mast; I can merely reach the rank of a sailor who needs to have his ears filled with wax.”

Chapter 12: Gamblers’ Ticks and Pigeons in a Box

Taxi-Cab English and Causality

Early in his career at Credit Suisse First Boston (midtown, not actual Wall Street), Taleb lived in a book-choked apartment on the Upper East Side — bareness “not ideological; simply because never managed to enter a furniture store, as would eventually stop at a bookstore along the way.” Kitchen devoid of any food, save a defective espresso machine.

One day, his cab driver stubbornly took the wrong route, forcing him to use the 52nd Street entrance. That day, his portfolio surged. The next day, he caught himself instructing a new cab to take the same route. He wore the same tie with yesterday’s coffee stains. “There was someone in me who visibly believed in a strong causal link between my use of the entrance, my choice of tie, and the previous day’s market behavior.”

A little brooding revealed his life had been governed by mild superstitions — Eastern Mediterranean roots: never grab the salt shaker from another’s hand, knock on wood upon receiving a compliment.

The Skinner Pigeon Experiment

B.F. Skinner (1948) placed pigeons in a box, delivering food at random intervals. Result: the pigeons developed elaborate ritualistic behaviors — one swung its head rhythmically, another spun counterclockwise — each convinced its ritual caused the food delivery.

We are the pigeons. Our bias is to immediately establish a causal link between two observed events. Type I and Type II errors: it is emotionally harder to reject a hypothesis than to accept it.

Philostratus Redux: Tricks, Not Discipline

The Greek philosopher Pyrrho advocated equanimity. But much of what we learn about probability does not register deeply enough to impact actual conduct — like the doctor who treats the 2% probability patient as if it were 95%.

Since the heart does not agree with the brain, the solution is tricks, not discipline: deny yourself access to your performance report unless it hits a predetermined threshold. No chocolate boxes under the trading desk. “Wax in my ears” — bind yourself to the mast.

The Odyssean Mute Command

In the trading room, CNBC stays on all day — with the volume turned off. When the television is silent, the babbling person looks ridiculous. Pundits’ facial contortions, stripped of sound, reveal how little content they convey. “Suddenly pundits become clowns.”

Stripping language from the orator — listening to a speech in a language you don’t understand — reveals how much authority is merely performance. Henri Bergson observed this about silent films.

Chapter 13: Carneades Comes to Rome: On Probability and Skepticism

Carneades and the Doctrine of Uncertainty

Around 155 B.C., Carneades of Cyrene came to Rome as one of three Athenian ambassadors. First day: brilliant oration praising justice. Second day: demolished his own arguments with equal force, establishing the doctrine of uncertainty of knowledge.

Cato the elder was so enraged that he persuaded the Senate to expel the ambassadors, fearing their argumentative spirit would weaken Roman military culture. Carneades was not merely a skeptic but a dialectician — never committed to any premises or conclusions he argued.

Historical Development of Probabilistic Thinking

Probability in a legal framework from Greek Sicily (6th century B.C.) — the first rhetorician Korax of Syracuse taught arguing from probability. His student Gorgias took the method to Athens. Skeptics’ main teaching: nothing could be accepted with certainty, only conclusions of various degrees of probability.

Until Mediterranean monotheism, skepticism flourished. Romans had no religion per se — too tolerant to accept a given truth. Cicero preferred to be guided by probability rather than allege with certainty. Medieval Arab philosopher Al Gazali, Hume, and Kant continued the tradition — but only Popper elevated skepticism to all-encompassing scientific methodology.

Monsieur de Norpois and Path Dependence of Beliefs

Proust’s In Search of Time Lost features the diplomat Marquis de Norpois who contradicts himself without shame. Proust reviles him, but Taleb argues this is the mark of a great trader.

“Monsieur de Norpois was not lying. He had just forgotten. One forgets rather quickly what one has not thought about with depth, what has been dictated to you by imitation.”

The best traders (Soros, Nigel Babbage) are completely free of path dependence. Anecdote from Jean-Manuel Rozan’s biography: Soros (thinly disguised as “Georgi Saulos”) expressed large bearishness and was obviously short. Days later, the market rallied violently. “We made a killing,” Saulos said. “I changed my mind. We covered and went very long.”

The Painting Test

A painting bought for $20K, now worth $40K. Would you buy it at $40K today? If not, you are married to your position. People who switch parties become “traitors, renegades, or worst of all, apostates.” Some medical researchers find purely rational behavior a sign of amygdala defect — “could Soros have a genetic flaw that makes him rational as a decision maker?”

1968 Paris student movement: “We demand the right to contradict ourselves!”

Computing Instead of Thinking: Markowitz and LTCM

Harry Markowitz received the “Nobel” for creating a method of computing future risk if one knows future uncertainty — which is clearly unknowable. The “risk measurers” emerged, claiming to assess risks in social sciences where the rules are not defined.

The LTCM collapse (1998): Merton and Scholes made absolutely no allowance for the possibility of not understanding markets and their methods being wrong. Their blowup caused Taleb’s firm Empirica to be born — “capital started flowing to people who did the exact opposite of what they did for a living.”

“My lesson from Soros is to start every meeting at my trading boutique by convincing everyone that we are a bunch of idiots who know nothing and are mistake-prone, but happen to be endowed with the rare privilege of knowing it.”

From Funeral to Funeral

People confuse science and scientists. Science is great, but individual scientists are dangerous — hard-headed, biased, forced to act like “cheap defense lawyers” defending their theses. A doctoral thesis is “defended” by the applicant; it would be rare to see a student change his mind upon being supplied with a convincing argument.

“It was said that science evolves from funeral to funeral.”

Chapter 14: Bacchus Abandons Antony

Montherlant and the Stoic’s End

French classicist Henry de Montherlant, told he was losing his eyesight to a degenerative disease, took his own life. “Such is the end that becomes a classicist.” The stoic prescribes electing what one can do to control destiny in front of a random outcome.

Being a hero does not mean extreme acts — having control over randomness can be expressed in manner one acts in the small and the large. Heroes judged by their actions, not by the results. We are left with only dignity — “a protocol of behavior that does not depend on the immediate circumstance. Grace under pressure, for example.”

Cavafy’s Poem: The God Abandons Antony

At Jackie Kennedy Onassis’s funeral, Maurice Tempelsman read C.P. Cavafy’s Apoleipein o Theos Antonion. Cavafy — Alexandrian Greek civil servant — wrote lean poetry with acute aestheticism stripped of sentimentality, worth learning formal Greek just to savor.

The poem addresses Marc Antony after defeat by Octavius, forsaken by Bacchus. It tells him not to mourn his luck, not to enter denial, not to “degrade yourself with empty hopes”:

Just listen while shaken by emotion but not with the coward’s imploration and complaints.

Nothing wrong with being shaken by emotion. What is wrong is not following the heroic, the dignified path. That is what stoicism truly means: the attempt by man to get even with probability. Cavafy himself, dying of throat cancer decades later, “did not quite follow his own prescription — used to randomly enter undignified spells of crying and cling to visitors.”

A Brief History of Stoicism

Started by Zeno of Kitium, Phoenician Cypriot. Roman development: life based on virtù (virtue as its own reward). Victorian England adopted the social model. The stoic combines wisdom, upright dealing, and courage — immune from life’s gyrations. The stern Cato found it beneath him to have human feelings.

A more human version: Seneca’s Letters From a Stoic — a soothing and surprisingly readable book Taleb distributes to trader friends. (Seneca also took his own life when cornered by destiny.)

Randomness and Personal Elegance

Ideas do not truly sink in when emotions come into play. Self-help books are largely ineffectual — good advice does not register when it goes against our wiring. But stoicism plays on dignity and personal aesthetics, which are part of our genes.

Exhibit sapere vivere (“know how to live”) in all circumstances. “Dress at your best on your execution day; try to leave a good impression on the death squad by standing erect and proud.” Be courteous to your assistant when you lose money. Never exhibit self-pity. Do not blame others for your fate.

“The only article Lady Fortuna has no control over is your behavior. Good luck.”

Epilogue: Solon Told You So

Years later, Nero’s skepticism paid off. He beat the 28% odds of survival and made a fortune while Wall Street hot shots got poor — “could buy goods they owned at very large discounts.” But he engaged in occasional excess.

Friday afternoon traffic in London can be dreadful. Nero, obsessed with traffic jams, spent five hours moving west from his office in the City towards a cottage in the Cotswolds. The frustration prompted him to get a helicopter flying license in Cambridgeshire. The other result: dangerous commuting by bicycle between Kensington and the City.

Nero’s excessive probability consciousness in his profession somehow did not register fully into his treatment of physical risk.

His helicopter crashed near Battersea Park on a windy day. He was alone in it.

“In the end the black swan got its man.”
