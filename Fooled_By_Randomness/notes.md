## Prologue: Mosques in the Clouds (pp. 1-5)

### Core Thesis
- "This book is about luck disguised and perceived as non-luck (that is, skills) and, more generally, randomness disguised and perceived as non-randomness (that is, determinism)."
- Manifests as the **lucky fool**: "a person who benefited from a disproportionate share of luck but attributes his success to some other, generally very precise, reason."
- Confusion is endemic in politics ("a country's president discoursing on the jobs that 'he' created"), business (biographies of successful people presenting "their specific explanation on how they made it big"), and even science.

### Table P.1: Table of Confusion
Central distinctions of the book — left column is mistaken for right:
- **General**: Luck/Skills, Randomness/Determinism, Probability/Certainty, Belief/Knowledge, Theory/Reality, Anecdote/Causality, Forecast/Prophecy
- **Market Performance**: Lucky idiot/Skilled investor, Survivorship bias/Market outperformance
- **Finance**: Volatility/Return, Stochastic variable/Deterministic variable
- **Physics**: Noise/Signal
- **Literary Criticism**: None (literary critics don't seem to have a name for things they don't understand)/Symbol
- **Philosophy of Science**: Epistemic probability/Physical probability, Induction/Deduction, Synthetic proposition/Analytic proposition

### Symbolism and Pattern-Seeking
- Humans are "intentionally prone to the confusion between *noise* and *meaning*" — we give meaning to all manner of shapes, detect human figures in inkblots. References Rimbaud: "*I saw mosques in the clouds*"
- "Science has only recently been able to handle randomness (the growth in available information has been exceeded by the expansion of noise). Probability theory is a young arrival in mathematics; probability applied to practice is almost nonexistent as a discipline."

### The Cost Asymmetry
- Mistaking the right column for the left (thinking randomness is skill) is more costly than the opposite. "Bad information is worse than no information at all."
- However, the opposite error (missing real patterns) is "worse than no information at all" too — but Taleb's professional focus is where luck is most conspicuously mistaken for skill: **trading**.

### Three-Part Structure
1. Part I: Solon's warning — introspection on rare events, visible and invisible histories
2. Part II: Collection of probability biases encountered in trading career
3. Part III: Conclusion — "perhaps ridding ourselves of our humanity is not in the works; we need tricks, not some grandiose moralizing help"

## Part I: Solon's Warning — Skewness, Asymmetry, Induction

### The Solon-Croesus Story (pp. 7-10)
- Croesus, King of Lydia, was "considered the richest man of his time." Visited by Solon, the Greek legislator known for "dignity, reserve, upright morals, humility, frugality, wisdom, intelligence, and courage."
- Solon showed zero admiration for Croesus's wealth. Croesus, irked, asked if Solon knew a happier man. Solon cited men who lived noble lives and died in battle. Pushed further, Solon answered: "The observation of the numerous misfortunes that attend all conditions forbids us to grow insolent upon our present enjoyments, or to admire a man's happiness that may yet, in course of time, suffer change."
- Modern equivalent: Yogi Berra — "it ain't over until it's over" (or "it ain't over until the fat lady sings").
- Part I is about how situations may change over time due to luck (Fortuna, Jupiter's firstborn daughter). Solon's key insight: **what comes with luck can be taken away by luck** — and the flipside: things that come with little help from luck are more resistant to randomness.
- Solon also intuited the **problem of induction** (which Taleb calls "the black swan or the rare event") and the **skewness** issue: "it does not matter how frequently something succeeds if failure is too costly to bear."

### The Croesus Postscript
- Croesus later lost a battle to Persian king Cyrus, was about to be burned alive, shouted "Solon, you were right." Cyrus, impressed by the story, spared his life and reflected on his own vulnerability. "People were thoughtful at that time."

## Chapter 1: If You're So Rich Why Aren't You So Smart? (pp. 11-24)

### Nero Tulip — Character Portrait
- **Origin story**: Became obsessed with trading after witnessing a scene at the Chicago Mercantile Exchange — a red Porsche screeches up, a flushed trader runs in chased by the sound of pigs being slaughtered, then a bored young clerk in a yellow jacket descends the steps unfazed. The contrast between the two struck Nero as a *coup de foudre*.
- **Background**: Undergraduate in ancient literature and mathematics from Cambridge. Enrolled in Ph.D. statistics at University of Chicago, completed prerequisites, then switched to philosophy department — called it "a moment of temporary sanity." His thesis was on methodology of statistical inference applied to social sciences — "indistinguishable from a thesis in mathematical statistics, just a bit more thoughtful (and twice as long)."
- **Career arc**: Academy → trainee at Chicago Mercantile Exchange → self-employed floor trader → desk trader in New York → quantitative financial products → brief Wall Street stardom → back to anonymity → proprietary trader for past decade.
- **Trading philosophy**: Extremely risk-averse. "I am shooting for longevity." Never sells naked options. Rapidly exits trades after a predetermined loss. Never puts himself in a position to lose more than $1,000,000 regardless of probability. His goal is not to maximize profits but to avoid having trading taken away from him ("blowing up").
- **Personal style**: Swings between "deportment and speech manners of a church historian and the verbally abusive intensity of a Chicago pit trader." Reads voraciously, hates business vocabulary ("game plan," "bottom line," "our mission"). Social life includes almost no business people. Allergic to businessmen "on plain aesthetic grounds."
- **Income**: Averaging between $300K and $2.5M, after-tax savings about $500K/year into Treasury bonds. Several million in medium maturity Treasuries. Did not invest in the stock market during the bull market — viewed it as potentially a scam due to dependence on randomness. His net worth is not a function of savings investment but of cash earnings.

### Nero's Temperament
- "I love taking small losses. I just need my winners to be large." Doesn't mind losing small money. Wants to benefit from rare events (panics, crashes) rather than be destroyed by them. The opposite of most traders.
- Risk aversion is extreme because blowing up = returning to academia, the tedium of the university, quiet hallways, papers kept awake by bad coffee.

### John the High-Yield Trader — Foil Character
- Lives across the street from Nero. High-yield bond trader. "Not a trader in the style of Nero" — brief conversation reveals "the intellectual depth and sharpness of mind of an aerobics instructor."
- Making at least 10x Nero's income. Two top-of-the-line German cars, collectible Ferrari, two convertibles. Massive house that keeps getting bigger.
- His business "depends on some knowledge of the 'odds'" but Taleb questions "What do such fools know about odds?" Uses "quantitative tools" that Taleb disagrees with. The high-yield market "resembles a nap on a railway track" — make money month after month, then lose a multiple of cumulative performance in a few hours.

### The Pecking Order Problem
- Kahneman and Tversky showed people prefer to make $70K when others make $60K than to make $80K when others make $90K. It's all relative.
- Nero couldn't prevent himself from judging his condition relative to John. Despite intellectual contempt, the social pressure weighed on him. The house across the street kept getting bigger.
- Nero tried to comfort himself with Proust's Swann — the refined art dealer in *In Search of Time Lost* who was at ease with aristocrats but acted middle-class around the bourgeois Verdurins.

### The Red-Hot Summer (1998)
- In September 1998, Nero was vindicated. John was fired — lost almost everything he had. Russian crisis / LTCM blowup period.
- Nero felt *schadenfreude* but also pride: his strategy and track record were vindicated. "He would no longer question his trading style when others were getting rich because they misunderstood the structure of randomness and market cycles."

### Serotonin and Randomness (pp. 22-24)
- Can we judge success by personal wealth? Sometimes, but not always. "At any point in time, a large section of businessmen with outstanding track records will be no better than randomly thrown darts."
- **Serotonin feedback loop**: Lucky fools don't know they're lucky. Their successes inject them with serotonin, creating a positive feedback loop — dominant style, confident posture, increased "leadership ability." But when the cycle breaks, it reverses viciously: drops in pecking order → behavioral changes → further performance drops.
- One cab driver near the Chicago Board of Trade could tell if traders were doing well: "They get all puffed up." Evolutionary psychology: physical manifestations of dominance signal performance, used for mate selection.
- "Never ask a man if he is from Sparta: if he is from Sparta, he would have let you know such an important fact — and if he were not, you could hurt his feelings. Likewise, never ask a trader if he is profitable."

### Your Dentist Is Rich, Very Rich (pp. 24-25)
- Nero is "prosperous but not 'very rich'" by his day's standards — but according to a "strange accounting method" (alternative histories), he is extremely rich. On the average of lives he could have led, he takes so little risk that very few sample paths would be marred by bad luck, and very few would be affected by extreme good luck. His life in stability "would be similar to that of an ecclesiastic clock repairman."
- **The dentist thought experiment**: A dentist is "considerably richer than the rock musician who is driven in a pink Rolls Royce" — *in expectation*. Cannot consider a profession without taking into account the average of all who enter it, not just the survivors. John Doe A (lottery winner, moved to wealthy neighborhood) vs. John Doe B (35-year dentist). Relive Doe B's life a million times — narrow range of outcomes. Relive Doe A's life a million times — almost all show janitorial activities and fruitless lottery tickets.
- "The idea of taking into account both the observed and unobserved possible outcomes sounds like lunacy. For most people, probability is about what may happen in the future, not events in the observed past; an event that has already taken place has 100% probability."

## Chapter 2: A Bizarre Accounting Method (pp. 26-39)

### Alternative History
- "One cannot judge a performance in any given field by the results, but by the costs of the alternative" — alternative histories. The quality of a decision cannot be solely judged based on its outcome.
- Politicians on their way out tell "those members of the press who still listen to them that they followed the best course" — eliciting the "yes, we know" commiseration.

### Russian Roulette (pp. 27-28)
- The $10 million Russian roulette thought experiment: eccentric tycoon offers you $10M to put a revolver with one bullet in six chambers to your head and pull the trigger.
- Five of six histories lead to enrichment; one leads to "an obituary with an embarrassing (but certainly original) cause of death."
- "Only one of the histories is observed in reality; and the winner of $10 million would elicit the admiration and praise of some fatuous journalist (the very same ones who unconditionally admire the Forbes 500 billionaires)."
- The role of the Wall Street executive: "being no more than a judge of results delivered in a random manner." The public observes wealth without ever glimpsing the generator.
- If the roulette-betting fool keeps playing, the bad histories will catch up. With thousands of 25-year-old players, we'd see a handful of extremely rich survivors and a very large cemetery.
- Personal note: Taleb lost a comrade to Russian roulette during the Lebanese war. Graham Greene also flirted with the game as a youth.

### An Even More Vicious Roulette (pp. 28-29)
- Reality is "far more vicious" — the fatal bullet comes infrequently, "like a revolver that would have hundreds, even thousands of chambers instead of six." After a few dozen tries, one forgets the bullet exists — a "numbing false sense of security."
- This is the **black swan problem** (Chapter 7): linked to the problem of induction and the "denigration of history."
- Unlike well-defined Russian roulette where risks are visible, in markets "one does not observe the barrel of reality" — you're playing Russian roulette and calling it "low risk."

### Smooth Peer Relations (pp. 29-30)
- Resistance to randomness is "an abstract idea, part of its logic counterintuitive, and its realizations non-observable."
- Two polar categories of people: those who never accept randomness, and those who are tortured by it.
- 1980s Wall Street: trading rooms "populated with people with a 'business orientation', that is, generally devoid of any introspection, flat as a pancake, and likely to be fooled by randomness." Their failure rate was extremely high, especially in complex instruments like exotic options — "counterintuitive payoffs that were too difficult for someone of such a culture to handle. They dropped like flies."
- Taleb had a strong bias in favor of Russian scientists on Wall Street: "many can be put to active use as chess coaches." When MBAs applied for trading positions boasting "advanced chess skills," Taleb used to verify by "pulling a chess set out of a drawer and telling the student, 'Yuri will have a word with you.'"
- Scientists' failure rate was "better, but only slightly so than that of MBAs" — linked to being "on average devoid of the smallest bit of practical intelligence." Capable of complex calculations but "totally incapable of solving a problem with the smallest connection to reality."

### Salvation Via Aeroflot (pp. 30-32)
- 1990s brought "people of richer and more interesting backgrounds" to trading rooms. Many scientists from former Soviet Union arrived to make money. "It was said that every plane from Moscow had at least its back row full of Russian mathematical physicists *en route* to Wall Street."
- Hiring Russian scientists was cheap labor — "going to JFK airport is (mandatory), randomly interviewing those that fitted the stereotype."
- Taleb's two bosses illustrate contrasting types:
  - **Kenny**: suburban family man, epitome of trustworthiness, rose rapidly in institution despite lacking technical competence. "Too much a no-nonsense person to make out my logic." Once blamed Taleb for not being impressed by European bond traders' success in 1993 — which Taleb considered random survivorship.
  - **Jean-Patrice**: moody Frenchman, explosive temper, hyper-aggressive personality. Expert at making subordinates uncomfortable. Rare person who "had the guts to care only about the generator, entirely oblivious of the results." Presented Solon's wisdom but lived colorfully — flashy dresser, frequented *Regine's* nightclub, called Taleb at 3am from nightclubs to discuss risk. Steady bathing habits of a New York Frenchman.
- Kenny climbed the institutional ladder but all his traders "have all exited the business." Jean-Patrice didn't have a happy career institutionally but taught Taleb to "beware of mature financial institutions."
- "Beware the spendthrift 'businesswise' person; the cemetery of markets is disproportionately well stocked with the self-styled 'bottom line' people."

### George Will Is No Solon: On Counterintuitive Truths (pp. 34-37)
- "Realism can be punishing. Probabilistic skepticism is worse."
- **Perceptional illusion**: Hard to think of Alexander or Julius Caesar as men who won only in the visible history. They took considerable risks alongside thousands of others; they were courageous and noble — "but so did thousands of others who live in the musty footnotes of history."
- Homer's *Iliad*: "the epic poet did not judge his heroes by the result: heroes won and lost battles in a manner that was totally independent of their own valor; their fate depended upon totally external forces." Patrocles is heroic not for his accomplishments but because "he preferred to die than see Achilles sulking into inaction."
- **George Will vs. Robert Shiller episode**: Shiller had been pronouncing the stock market overpriced for a long time. George Will pointed out that those who listened to Shiller lost money as the market doubled. Shiller, as a scientist, "did not claim being a prophet" — his views on market irrationality are not invalidated by being wrong in the past.
- "Mixing forecast and prophecy is symptomatic of randomness foolishness (prophecy belongs to the right column, forecast is its mere left-column equivalent)."

### Humiliated in Debates (pp. 37-38)
- Alternative history "does not make intuitive sense" — we are "not wired in a way to understand probability."
- **Correctness vs. intelligibility**: "Conventional wisdom favors things that can be explained rather instantly and 'in a nutshell'." French school maxim: *Ce qui se conçoit bien s'énonce clairement / Et les mots pour le dire viennent aisément* (What is easy to conceive is clear to express/Words to say it would come effortlessly). Taleb's disappointment: "most poetic sounding adages are plain wrong. Borrowed wisdom can be vicious."
- Einstein's remark: "common sense is nothing but a collection of misconceptions acquired by age 18."
- Furthermore: "*what sounds intelligent in a conversation or a meeting, or, particularly in the media, is suspicious.*"

### Risk Managers (pp. 38-39)
- The position of risk manager: "someone who is supposed to monitor the institution and verify that it is not too deeply involved in the business of playing Russian roulette."
- Risk managers' dilemma: "the generator of reality is not observable." They can't stop profitable traders from taking risks (*ex post* they'd be accused of costing the firm money), but a blowup is their responsibility.
- Risk managers often play politics, issuing vague internal memoranda that warn without condemning.
- Like a doctor torn between two types of error: false positive (telling patient has cancer when he doesn't) and false negative (telling patient is healthy when he has cancer).
- "My greatest risk is to become successful, as it would mean that my business is about to disappear; strange business, ours."

## Chapter 3: A Mathematical Meditation on History (pp. 40-59)

### Europlayboy Mathematics (pp. 40-41)
- Stereotype of mathematician: "anemic man with a shaggy beard and grimy and uncut fingernails." But Monte Carlo conjures "a suntanned urbane man of the Europlayboy variety entering a casino under a whiff of Mediterranean breeze."
- Monte Carlo mathematics combines "the Monte Carlo man's realism without the shallowness combined with the mathematician's intuitions without the excessive abstraction." It is of "immense practical use" and "shaped my thinking in most matters related to randomness."
- "Mathematics is principally a tool to meditate, rather than to compute."

### The Tools: Monte Carlo Methods (pp. 41-43)
- **Sample paths**: invisible histories subjected to technical refinement. Not just where a bird ends up tomorrow, but "all the various places it can possibly visit during the time interval." The word *sample* stresses one realization among a collection of possible ones.
- **Stochastic processes**: the study of the evolution of successive random events — "the mathematics of history." Key feature: it has time in it.
- **Monte Carlo generator**: a perfect roulette wheel in your attic. Computer programs simulate virtually anything. "They are even better (and cheaper) than the roulette wheel built by your carpenter."
- Monte Carlo simulations are "closer to a toy" — generate thousands/millions of random sample paths and examine prevalent characteristics. Set conditions resembling a virtual casino and launch simulations around possible events.
- Pioneered in martial physics at Los Alamos during A-bomb preparation. Became popular in financial mathematics in the 1980s with random walk theories.

### Monte Carlo Mathematics (pp. 43-45)
- "True" mathematicians dislike Monte Carlo methods — they call it "brute force." But "we can replace a large portion of mathematical knowledge with a Monte Carlo simulator."
- Example: computing Pi by drawing a circle inside a square and shooting random bullets — ratio of hits inside/outside converges to Pi with infinite precision.
- Taleb is not a "native" mathematician: "I am someone who does not speak mathematics as a native language, but someone who speaks it with a trace of a foreign accent." Interested in application, not properties *per se*.
- **Math as grammar analogy**: "We are called 'quants' — like physicists, we have more interest in the employment of the mathematical tool than in the tool itself."
- Probability is an introspective field: "It is impossible to assess the quality of the knowledge we are gathering without allowing a share of randomness." In science, "probability and information are treated in exactly the same manner."
- Two great thinkers who started their intellectual journeys with probability: **Einstein** (1905 paper on Brownian motion, backbone of random walk theories used in finance) and **Keynes** (author of *Treatise on Probability* before venturing into political economy; was a probabilist who "blew up trading his account after experiencing excessive opulence").

### Fun In My Attic (pp. 45-47)
- Became addicted to Monte Carlo engines in the early 1990s — "thrilled to feel that I was generating history, a *Demiurgus*." The dispersion between results indicates degree of resistance to randomness.
- Simulated populations of "idiotic bull," "impetuous bear," and "cautious" traders under different regimes. Under such structure, "idiotic bull" traders get rich from the rally, buy more assets, drive prices higher — "until their ultimate shellacking." Bearish traders rarely made it from boom to bust. "My models showed almost nobody to really ultimately make money."
- Exception: option buyers with "remarkable staying power" — "they could buy the insurance against blowup; they could get anxiety-free sleep at night."
- Evolutionary thinking came from Monte Carlo simulators, not natural sciences.
- "Summing under histories" — borrowing from Feynman who applied such methods to particle dynamics.
- Monte Carlo simulations reminded him of experimental novels (Alain Robbe-Grillet): "the same chapter would be written and revised, the writer each time changing the plot like a new sample path."

### Denigration of History (pp. 47-49)
- "Learning from history does not come naturally to us humans" — our genetic endowment as *homo erectus* does not favor transfers of experience.
- Children learn only from their own mistakes (the stove). Kahneman and Tversky confirmed: adults too are "extremely lax in the area of detection and prevention" of risks from others' experience.
- **Historical determinism**: experts believe "that we would know when history is made." But people who witnessed the 1929 crash didn't know it was historic at the time. "It is hard to imagine that people who witnessed history did not know at the time how important the moment was."
- All colleagues who blew up spectacularly — 1987, Japan 1990, bonds 1994, Russia 1998, Nasdaq 2000 — showed "remarkable similarities in their approaches" and all claimed "these times are different." They were "unable to accept that the experience of others was out there, in the open, freely available to all, with books detailing crashes in every bookstore."

### The Stove Is Hot (pp. 48-50)
- Every blown-up trader believes himself "quite different" — amplifying the "why me?" shock.
- **Historical determinism**: we believe people who witnessed 1929 knew it was a crash. But "life for us is made to resemble an adventure movie, as we know ahead of time that something big is about to happen."
- Two ways of learning from history: reading the past (the elders), and Monte Carlo simulations of the future.

### My Solon (pp. 50-51)
- Personal connection to Solon's warning: Taleb's family from "the very same strip of land in the Eastern Mediterranean." Ancestors experienced "extreme opulence and embarrassing penury over the course of a single generation." Grandfather was a former deputy prime minister, living in a nondescript apartment in Athens.
- The fall of Constantinople (1453) "had been wired into our soul." Financial impoverishment is more emotionally devastating than physical danger: "Croesus worried more about the loss of his Kingdom than the perils to his life."
- **Ergodicity**: Mathematicians give a fancy name to the observation that "bad trades catch up with you" — very long sample paths end up resembling each other. "The lucky fool might have benefited from some luck in life; over the longer run he would slowly converge to the state of a less-lucky idiot."

### Distilled Thinking on Your PalmPilot (pp. 51-59)

#### Breaking News
- The journalist is his *bête noire*. Difference between noise and information has analog: "between journalism and history."
- A competent journalist should view matters like a historian: "today the market went up, but this information is not too relevant as it emanates mostly from noise."
- Age is beauty for ideas: "Mathematically, progress means that some new information is better than past information, not that the average of new information will supplant past information." Optimal strategy: "when in doubt, to systematically reject the new idea, information, or method."
- Only reads news in rare circumstances — prefers poetry. "If an event is important enough, it will find its way to my ears."

#### Shiller Redux (pp. 53-54)
- Robert Shiller's 1981 paper on market volatility: if a stock price is the estimated value of discounted cash flows, prices are "way too volatile in relation to tangible manifestations" — they overshoot both up and down. This meant "rational expectation" didn't work; efficient markets theory was challenged.
- Robert C. Merton criticized Shiller on methodological grounds while defending efficient markets. The same Merton later "introduced himself as the 'founding partner' of a hedge fund that aimed at taking advantage of market inefficiencies" — LTCM, which "blew up rather spectacularly from the black swan problem." "It is as if the Pope converted to Islam."

#### Gerontocracy (pp. 55-56)
- Preference for distilled thinking favors old investors/traders — counter to Wall Street's preference for the young and recently profitable.
- Monte Carlo simulations of heterogeneous trader populations showed "significant advantage in selecting aged traders, using, as a selection criterion their cumulative years of experience rather than their absolute success."
- Under regime switching, "those who will survive are not necessarily those who appear to be the fittest" — older people, having been exposed to rare events longer, are more resistant.
- Life insurers in Renaissance Italy reached same conclusion: same insurance rate for a man in his 20s as one in his 50s — "once a man crossed the 40-year mark, he had shown that very few ailments could harm him."

#### Philostratus: On the Difference Between Noise and Information (pp. 56-59)
- Cavafy's poem (after Philostratus): "For the gods perceive things in the future, ordinary people things in the present, but the wise perceive things about to happen."
- **Table 3.1 — The dentist thought experiment**: A happily retired dentist, excellent investor, 15% return with 10% volatility.
  - At 1-year scale: 93% probability of making money
  - At 1-month: 67%
  - At 1-day: 54%
  - At 1-hour: 51.3%
  - At 1-minute: 50.17%
  - At 1-second: 50.02%
- "Over a short time increment, one observes the variability of the portfolio, not the returns." What you see is "at best a combination of variance and returns, not just returns."
- The dentist checking daily: 241 pleasurable minutes vs. 239 unpleasurable ones per day (8 hours). Checking yearly: 19 pleasant surprises for every unpleasant one.
- **Noise-to-signal ratios**: Over 1 year: 0.7 noise per 1 signal. Over 1 month: 2.32 noise per signal. Over 1 hour: 30 noise per signal. Over 1 second: 1,796 noise per signal.
- **Emotional asymmetry**: "A negative pang is not offset by a positive one (some behavioral economists estimate the negative effect to be up to 2.5 the magnitude of a positive one)." So checking frequently = net emotional deficit.
- Three conclusions: (1) Over short time increments, you observe variability, not returns. (2) Our emotions are not designed for this — the dentist would do better with monthly or yearly statements. (3) When Taleb sees an investor monitoring live prices on a phone, "I smile and smile."
- "My problem is that I am not rational and I am extremely prone to drown in randomness and to incur emotional torture." His solution: avoid information except in rare circumstances. Read poetry instead. *The Economist* on Saturdays, not the *Wall Street Journal* daily.
- "People who look too closely at randomness burn out, their emotions drained by the series of pangs they experience."

## Chapter 4: Randomness, Nonsense, and the Scientific Intellectual (pp. 60-63+)

### Randomness and the Verb (pp. 60-62)
- Monte Carlo engine takes us into "more literary territory." The distinction between the scientific intellectual and the literary intellectual — the "science wars."
- **Vienna Circle** (1930s): physicists decided science was making gains significant enough to challenge humanities. Wanted to strip thinking from rhetoric. Introduced rigor: statements are either *deductive* ("2 + 2 = 4") or *inductive* ("it rains in Spain," "New Yorkers are generally rude"). Anything else was "plain unadulterated hogwash."
- One way to discriminate: "a scientific intellectual can usually recognize the writing of another but that the literary intellectual would not be able to tell the difference between lines jotted down by a scientist and those by a glib non-scientist."
- References Alan Sokal's *Fashionable Nonsense* — hoax paper published in a prominent journal, generating Monte Carlo-like nonsense that fooled postmodernist editors.

### Reverse Turing Test (pp. 62-63)
- Turing test: a computer is intelligent if it can fool a human. The converse: "A human can be said to be unintelligent if we can replicate his speech by a computer."
- **The Dada Engine** (Andrew C. Bulha, Monash University): generates postmodernist papers randomly. Taleb tried it — produced sentences indistinguishable from real Derrida/Paglia.
- Applied same logic to business speeches: randomly select five phrases and connect them with grammatical minimum → produces a CEO speech indistinguishable from the real thing. If the result "bears too close a resemblance to the speech you just heard from the boss of your company, then I suggest looking for a new job."
### The Father of All Pseudothinkers (p. 64)
- Hegel: "the father of all pseudothinkers" — writes jargon "meaningless outside of a chic Left-Bank Parisian café." Even a Monte Carlo engine couldn't sound as random as Hegel (it would take too many sample runs to get such a mixture of *heat* and *sound*). Hegelian thinking linked to Marxist regimes — "these 'thinkers' should be given an undergraduate-level class on statistical sampling theory prior to their release in the open world."

### Monte Carlo Poetry (pp. 64-67)
- There are instances where Taleb *likes* to be fooled by randomness — art and poetry. "If I am going to be fooled by randomness, it better be of the beautiful (and harmless) kind."
- **Exquisite Cadavers**: Surrealist poets (André Breton, Paul Eluard) in post-WWI cafés created poems by randomly writing parts of sentences on folded paper. First publicized result: "The exquisite cadavers shall drink the new wine" (*Les cadavres exquis boiront le vin nouveau*). Some random combinations are ravishingly beautiful.
- The existence of surviving holy languages (Hebrew, Arabic, Church Greek) argues that keeping language away from daily corruption preserves its aesthetic power. The Catholic Church's shift to vernacular liturgies may have caused a drop in religious belief.
- Yiddish saying: "If I am going to be forced to eat pork, it better be of the best kind."
- Key insight: "We do not need to be rational and scientific when it comes to the details of our daily life — only in those that can harm us and threaten our survival." Be rational about risk; be irrational (aesthetic) about art. "I have encountered colleagues, 'rational' no-nonsense people, who do not understand why I cherish the poetry of Baudelaire... Yet they get sucked into listening to the 'analyses' of a television 'guru', or into buying the stock of a company they know absolutely nothing about."

## Chapter 5: Survival of the Least Fit — Can Evolution Be Fooled by Randomness? (pp. 68-83)

### Carlos the Emerging Markets Wizard (pp. 68-74)
- Carlos: patrician Latin-American, impeccably dressed, shy with ladies, traded emerging market bonds (Brady bonds from Russia, Mexico, Brazil, Argentina, Turkey). "Emerging market" is the "politically correct euphemism to define a country that is not very developed."
- Background: Harvard economics (couldn't find a decent thesis topic), settled for Master's, joined Wall Street. Started in 1992 when emerging market desk was small and in undesirable parts of the trading floor — but it rapidly became a large part of the bank's revenues.
- **The community**: cosmopolitan patricians from across the emerging market world. "Mexicans based in London trade Russian securities, Iranians and Greeks specialize in Brazilian bonds." They are "generally urbane, dress well, collect art, but are non-intellectual." Between 30-40, hold Metropolitan Opera season tickets. "True traders, I believe, dress sloppily, are often ugly and exhibit the intellectual curiosity of someone who would be more interested in the information-revealing contents of the garbage can than the Cézanne painting on the wall."

### The Good Years (pp. 71-72)
- Carlos bought dips — accumulated more when prices fell, as he believed in the economic intuition behind the bonds. His string of successes (surviving the 1995 Mexican devaluation, the 1997 dip after the false crash) made him feel invincible. "He could do no wrong."
- Looking back at his track record from 1992-1997: upward sloping line with occasional blips — all of which turned out to be "excellent buying opportunities."

### Averaging Down & Lines in the Sand (pp. 72-73)
- Summer of 1998: market started falling. Carlos averaged down aggressively. Russian bonds dropped from ~$52 to $20s. He kept buying: "Read my lips: It's a li-qui-da-tion!" He put $5M of personal money ($14M net worth) in Russia Principal Bonds at $48. "I will retire on these profits."
- Carlos calculated that even if Russia defaulted, bonds would "never, ever trade below $48." His "lines in the sand" kept being crossed. By August, Russian bonds were at $10. Carlos was dismissed. So was his boss, demoted to "a newly created position."
- The **firehouse effect** (veteran trader Marty O'Connell): "firemen who talk to each other for too long come to agree on many things that an outside, impartial observer would find ludicrous." Emerging market economists around the globe all agreed the sell-off was overdone.

### John the High-Yield Trader — Full Story (pp. 74-78)
- John (from Chapter 1): Pace Business School grad, rose to head a team of 10 high-yield bond traders. 20% profit-sharing contract. By age 32 net worth $1M, by 35 it exceeded $16M ($14M invested in his own trades).
- Business model: buying "cheap" high-yield bonds at 10% yield, borrowing at 5.5%, pocketing 4.5% interest rate differential, leveraged massively. "$3 billion in face value" across continents. Hedged interest rate exposure with government bond futures — felt "cocooned" against nasty fluctuations.
- **Henry the quant**: Operations Research grad student, "Computational Finance" specialty ("running computer programs overnight"). Income went from $50K to $600K in three years. His risk calculations gave the event that destroyed John a probability of "1 in 1,000,000,000,000,000,000,000,000 years" — a "ten sigma" event. Doubling the odds didn't help much.
- John's reaction to losses: "One would go crazy if one were to listen to the mood swings of the market" — he called it "noise" that would "mean revert." But the "noise" kept adding up in the same direction. "In a biblical cycle, it took seven years to make John a hero and just seven days to make him an idiot."
- John lost $600M for his last employer in barely a few days. Of his $16M personal wealth, he saved $1M. But the difference between $16M reached from above and $1M reached from below is vast: "full of shame; he still worries about running into old friends on the street."

### The Traits They Shared / Review of Market Fools (pp. 78-80)
- Both Carlos and John were **dip buyers** — their style was the most desirable between 1992-1998. They replaced traders who were perhaps better but didn't fit the latest cycle. The **cross-sectional problem**: at any given time, the most profitable traders are likely to be those best fit to the latest cycle.
- Shared traits of market fools of randomness:
  - **Overestimation of accuracy of beliefs**: Never considered their past success might be coincidental
  - **Tendency to get married to positions**: "Traders divorce their spouse sooner than abandon their positions"
  - **Tendency to change their story**: Become "long haul" investors after losses to postpone denial
  - **No precise game plan**: Simply not aware that they could lose; bought more after declines
  - **Absence of critical thinking / "stop losses"**: Did not consider that perhaps their method was wrong
  - **Denial**: When losses occurred, "the price on the screen lost its reality in favor of some abstract 'value'"

### Naive Evolutionary Theories (pp. 81-83)
- Bad traders have a short- and medium-term survival advantage over good traders. Simplistic "Darwinism" drawn to markets is misapplied.
- **Darwinian fitness applies over very long time, not short term** — "time aggregation eliminates much of the effects of randomness." In the short term, we don't live in a world that "converges continuously towards betterment."
- **Life is not continuous**: *Natura no facit saltus* (nature doesn't make jumps) — attributed to Linnaeus, used by Leibniz — "turned out to be entirely wrong, as it was denied by quantum mechanics." Particles jump discretely between states.
- **Regime switches**: When randomness changes shape, all attributes of a system change to the point of becoming unrecognizable. What seems like evolution "may be merely a diversion, and possibly regression."
- **Can evolution be fooled by randomness?** Yes. On *average* animals will be fit, but any individual could have survived because its sample path was lucky. "The longer these animals can go without encountering the rare event, the more vulnerable they will be to it." By ergodicity, extend time to infinity and the event will happen with certainty — "the species will be wiped out!"
- The profitable person like John, "a pure loser in the long run," presents "a high degree of eligibility in the short run and has the propensity to multiply his genes." His pseudo-success signals superior genetic makeup to potential mates.

## Chapter 6: Skewness and Asymmetry (pp. 84-98)

### The Median Is Not the Message (pp. 84-86)
- Stephen Jay Gould diagnosed with deadly cancer, told *median* survival was eight months. But expected (average) survival was considerably longer. "Median means roughly that 50% of the people die before eight months and 50% survive longer than eight months. But those who survive would live considerably longer."
- **Asymmetry**: those who die do so early; those who survive go on living "generally going about life just like a regular person and fulfilling the average 73.4 or so years predicted by insurance mortality tables." Average survival has nothing to do with *median* survival — prompted Gould's famous essay "The Median is Not the Message."
- **Table 6.1**: A strategy with 999/1000 chance of making $1 and 1/1000 chance of losing $10,000. Expectation = -$9.001. "Odds are far more likely than B. But it is not a good idea to bet on event A."
- People confuse *probability* with *expectation* (probability times the payoff). Most schooling uses symmetric examples (coin tosses) where this distinction doesn't matter. "In fact the so-called 'Bell Curve' that seems to have found universal use in society is entirely symmetric."

### Bull and Bear Zoology (pp. 86-88)
- "Bullish" and "bearish" are "hollow words with no application in a world of randomness — particularly if such a world, like ours, presents asymmetric outcomes."
- At a weekly "discussion meeting" at a large investment house, Taleb was asked if he was bullish or bearish. He stated the market would go up slightly with about 70% probability. "But, Nassim, you just boasted about being short a very large quantity of SP500 futures." He explained: 70% chance of going up 1% but 30% chance of going down 10%. Expected value: -2.3. "I did not change my mind! I have a lot of faith in my bet! As a matter of fact I now feel like selling even more!"
- **Table 6.2**: Market up 70%/+1% = 0.7 expected; Market down 30%/-10% = -3.00 expected. Total = -2.3. Being "bullish" (high probability of up) is compatible with being short (negative expected value).
- "It is not how likely an event is to happen that matters, it is how much is made when it happens that should be the consideration."

### An Arrogant 29-Year-Old Son (pp. 88-89)
- A friend's wealthy father visited New York, wanted Taleb to predict European bond markets by Christmas. Taleb refused — he doesn't make predictions, he studies the structure of randomness. Father complained: "When I ask a lawyer a legal question, he answers me with courtesy and precision. When I ask a doctor a medical question, he gives me his opinion. No specialist ever gives me disrespect. Your insolent and conceited 29-year-old son is playing *prima donna* and refuses to answer me about the direction of the market!"

### Rare Events (pp. 89-90)
- "The best description of my lifelong business in the market is 'skewed bets', that is, I try to benefit from rare events, events that do not tend to repeat themselves frequently, but, accordingly, present a large payoff when they occur."
- Why rare events are undervalued: psychological bias — people focus on frequency rather than magnitude. Jim Rogers: "I don't buy options. Buying options is another way to go to the poorhouse. Someone did a study for the SEC and discovered that 90 percent of all options expire as losses." Taleb's response: if you make 50x your bet on average when in the money, buying options is the way to the palazzo.
- 1987 crash made Taleb a trader and scholar. "Nero of the smaller house in Chapter 1 aims to get out of harm's way by avoiding exposure to rare events... I am far more aggressive than Nero and go one step further; I have organized my career and business in such a way as to be able to benefit from them."

### Symmetry and Science / The Rare Event Fallacy (pp. 90-98)
- Techniques imported from other fields (education, medicine) into finance don't account for asymmetry. Professors remove outliers from students' grades — but in finance, outliers are everything.
- **The peso problem**: Mexican economic variables during the 1980s switched erratically between stability and turbulence. "Beware of calm waters" — currencies with the greatest historical stability are most prone to crashes (Asian crisis 1997: pegged currencies of Malaysia, Indonesia, Thailand).
- **Rare events are always unexpected** — caused by panics, liquidations, investors rushing to the door. If expected, they wouldn't happen.
- **Pushing randomness under the rug**: Investors are drawn to strategies that experience rare but *large* variations — because they experience mostly small positive returns (dentist effect from Chapter 3). The same with scientific research: mouse scientist sees no gain for years, then one day produces a result. "Someone observing the time series of his occupation would see absolutely no gain, while every day would bring him closer *in probability* to the end result."
- **Why don't statisticians detect rare events?** Confidence levels increase by the square root of *n*. Asymmetric distributions mean knowledge of the *absence* of rare events increases very slowly, while knowledge of their *presence* increases dramatically when one is found. This is the core of Hume and Popper's philosophical problem.
- **The mischievous child replaces the black balls**: If the incidence of red balls in the urn is itself randomly distributed — the **problem of stationarity** — we may never know the urn's composition. Someone is adding/removing balls without our knowledge. "Much of our knowledge derived through statistics quite shaky."
- **The Lucas critique**: Robert Lucas (Nobel) argued that if people are rational, they'd figure out predictable patterns and arbitrage them away. The practice of "financial engineering" came with "massive doses of pseudoscience."
- The mere possibility of distributions not being stationary "makes the entire concept seem like a costly (perhaps *very costly*) mistake." This leads to the problem of induction.

## Chapter 7: The Problem of Induction (pp. 99-103+)

### From Bacon to Hume (pp. 99-101)
- The problem of induction: "a problem in inference well known as the problem of induction... it has been haunting science for a long time, but science has not been as harmed by it as the financial markets. Why? Because the randomness content compounds its effects."
- **Francis Bacon**: shifted science from scholasticism to empirical observation. But Hume warned that without proper method, empirical observations can lead astray.
- **Hume** (*Treatise on Human Nature*): "No amount of observations of white swans can allow the inference that all swans are white, but the observation of a single black swan is sufficient to refute that conclusion." Hume was the first modern epistemologist — obsessive skeptic who never believed a causal link could be truly established.

### Niederhoffer, Victorian Gentleman (pp. 100-101)

- Niederhoffer's empiricism missed "just a modicum of methodology" — his trainees who added rigor to their statistical inference fared better.
- Key difference between Taleb and Niederhoffer: "I can use data to disprove a proposition, never to prove one. I can use history to refute a conjecture, never to affirm it."
- Asymmetry of knowledge: **Statement A** ("No swan is black because I looked at 4,000 swans and found none") vs. **Statement B** ("Not all swans are white"). Can never logically make statement A, but finding one *cygnus atratus* (black swan, discovered in Australia) refutes "all swans are white."

### Popper's Answer: Falsificationism

**Two types of theories**:
1. Theories known to be wrong (tested and adequately rejected) - called *falsified*
2. Theories not yet been known to be wrong, not *falsified* yet, but exposed to be proved wrong

**Why is a theory never right?**
- Will never know if all swans are white
- Borrowed Kantian idea of mechanisms of perception
- Testing mechanism may be faulty
- A theory cannot be *verified*
- Baseball coach **Yogi Berra**: "past data has a lot of good in it, but it is not all we got"
- Can only be provisionally accepted
- Outside these two categories is not a theory

**Charlatanism vs. science**:
- Astrologer can always find reason to fit past event ("Mars was probably in line but not too much so")
- Trader without point that would make him change mind is not a trader
- **Newtonian physics** is scientific because it allowed us to falsify it (Einstein's relativity proved it wrong)
- **Astrology** not scientific because it does not offer conditions under which we could disprove it

> "These are men with bold ideas, but highly critical of their own ideas; they try to find whether their ideas are right by trying first to find whether they are not perhaps wrong. They work with bold conjectures and severe attempts at refuting their own conjectures."

- Quote applies to scientists, but could be anything
- Context: Popper rebelling against growth of science

- Scientification of things (literally everything under the sun)

### Verification is Dangerous

## Part II: Monkeys on Typewriters - Survivorship and Other Biases

### The Monkeys Problem

**Infinite monkeys on typewriters** thought experiment:
- If infinite number of monkeys clap away at strongly built typewriters, certainty that one would produce exact version of the *Iliad*
- Upon examination, may be less interesting than appears at first
- Such probability is very low
- Carry reasoning one step beyond: now that we found hero among monkeys, would any reader invest life savings on bet that monkey would write *Odyssey* next?

**Second step is interesting**:
- How much can past performance (typing the *Iliad*) be relevant in forecasting future performance?
- Same applies to any decision based on past performance
- Monkey showing up at your door with impressive past performance - quickly sign him up for sequel?

**Major problem with inference**:
- Those whose profession is to derive conclusions from data often fall into trap faster and more confidently than others
- More data we have, more likely to drown in it
- Common wisdom among people with budding knowledge of probability laws: base decision-making on following principle: *unlikely for someone to perform considerably well in consistent fashion without doing something right*
- Track records become preeminent
- Call on rule of likelihood of successful run and tell themselves that if someone performed better than rest in past, great chance of him performing better than crowd in future
- As usual, beware the middlebrow: small knowledge of probability can lead to worse results than no knowledge at all

### It Depends on the Number of Monkeys

**Presumption might be weak** to point of being useless in decision making:
- Depends on two factors: randomness content of profession and number of monkeys in operation
- Initial sample size matters greatly

**If five monkeys in game**:
- Would be rather impressed with *Iliad* writer, to point of suspecting reincarnation of ancient poet
- If billion monkeys, would not be impressed
- Surprised if one did not get some well known (unspecified) piece of work by luck (perhaps Casanova's *Memoirs of My Life*)
- **One monkey** would even be expected to provide former **Vice President Al Gore**'s *Earth in the Balance*, perhaps stripped of platitudes

**Business world enters more viciously than other walks of life**:
- High dependence on randomness (already contrasted randomness-dependent business with dentistry)
- Greater number of businessmen, greater likelihood of one performing in stellar manner just by luck
- Rarely seen anyone count monkeys
- Few count investors in market in order to calculate conditional probability of successful runs given number of investors in operation over given market history

### Vicious Real Life

**Other aspects to monkeys problem**:
- In real life, other monkeys not countable, let alone visible
- Hidden away, as one sees only winners
- Natural for those who failed to vanish completely
- One sees survivors, and only survivors, which imparts mistaken perception of odds
- Do not respond to probability, but to *society's assessment of it*
- As saw with **Nero Tulip**, even people with training in probability respond unintelligently to social pressure

- Also set out own ideas, those not explored in literature

## Chapter 13: Carneades Comes to Rome: On Probability and Skepticism

### Carneades and the Doctrine of Uncertainty

- Around 155 B.C., **Carneades of Cyrene**, Greek post-classical philosopher, came to Rome as one of three Athenian ambassadors seeking political favor
- Represented the **Academy** (New Academy) — argumentative institution known as hotbed of skepticism in ancient world
- First day: delivered brilliant harangue in praise of justice and its importance as top human motive
- Second day: contradicted and refuted his own arguments with equal persuasiveness, establishing **doctrine of uncertainty of knowledge**
- Convinced same audience in same spot that justice should be low on list of human motivations
- **Cato the elder** (the "censor") was enraged and persuaded Senate to send ambassadors packing to prevent their argumentative spirit from weakening Roman military culture
- Carneades was not merely a skeptic but a **dialectician** — never committed to premises or conclusions he argued
- Stood against arrogant dogma and belief in one sole truth

> "Ask your local mathematician to define probability; he would most probably show you how to compute it. As we saw in Chapter 3 on probabilistic introspection, probability is not about the odds, but about the belief in the existence of an alternative outcome, cause, or motive."

## Chapter 14: Bacchus Abandons Antony (pp. 191-196)

### Montherlant and the Stoic's End

- When the classicist aristocratic French writer **Henry de Montherlant** was told he was about to lose his eyesight to a degenerative disease, he found it most appropriate to take his own life
- "Such is the end that becomes a classicist" — the stoic's prescription is to elect what one can do to control one's destiny in front of a random outcome
- At the end, one is allowed to choose between no life at all and what is given by destiny — we always have an **option against uncertainty**
- Not limited to stoics; both stoicism and **Epicurianism** recommended such control (difference between the two resides in minor technicalities)
- Being a hero does not necessarily mean extreme acts like getting killed in battle — having control over randomness can be expressed in manner one acts in the small and the large
- Heroes judged by their **actions, not by the results** — no matter how good we are at dominating the odds, randomness will have the last word
- We are left with only **dignity** — defined as execution of a protocol of behavior that does not depend on the immediate circumstance
- "It may not be the optimal one, but it certainly is the one that makes us feel best. *Grace under pressure*, for example."
- Examples: not toadying up to someone for a reward, fighting a duel to save face, signaling to a prospective mate "I am obsessed with you, but I will not do a thing to compromise my dignity"

### Notes on Jackie O.'s Funeral: Cavafy's Poem

- **C.P. Cavafy** — Alexandrian Greek civil servant with Turkish or Arabic last name, wrote classical and modern Greek lean poetry that eluded last fifteen centuries of Western literature
- Most of his poems take place in Syria, Asia Minor, and Alexandria — worth learning formal semi-classical Greek just to savor his poems
- Acute aestheticism stripped of sentimentality provides relief from centuries of mawkishness in poetry and drama
- **Jackie Kennedy Onassis**'s last consort **Maurice Tempelsman** read Cavafy's valedictory *Apoleipein o Theos Antonion* (The God Abandons Antony) at her funeral
- The poem addresses **Marc Antony**, who has just lost the battle against Octavius and was forsaken by Bacchus the god who had protected him
- One of the most elevating poems Taleb has ever read — epitome of dignified aestheticism
- Gentle but edifying: advises a man who has just received a crushing reversal of fortune
- Tells Antony not to mourn his luck, not to enter denial, not to believe his ears and eyes are deceiving him — "do not degrade yourself with empty hopes"

> *Just listen while shaken by emotion but not with the coward's imploration and complaints.*

- While shaken with emotion — no stiff upper lip. Nothing wrong with having emotions. What is wrong is not following the heroic, the dignified path
- That is what **stoicism** truly means: the attempt by man to get even with probability
- Cavafy himself, while dying of throat cancer decades later, did not quite follow his own prescription — used to randomly enter undignified spells of crying and cling to visitors

### History of Stoicism

- Stoicism has little to do with stiff upper lip notion
- Started as intellectual movement by **Zeno of Kitium**, a Phoenician Cypriot
- Developed in Roman time into life based on system of virtues — *virtù* meant virtue is its own reward
- Developed social model for stoic person, like the gentlemen in Victorian England
- Stoic tenets: person who combines qualities of **wisdom, upright dealing, and courage**
- Stoic will be immune from life's gyrations — but can be carried to the extreme; the stern **Cato** found it beneath him to have human feelings
- A more human version in **Seneca's *Letters From a Stoic*** — soothing and surprisingly readable book Taleb distributes to trader friends (Seneca also took his own life when cornered by destiny)

### Randomness and Personal Elegance

- Ideas do not truly sink in when emotions come into play — we do not use our rational brain outside classrooms
- Self-help books are largely ineffectual; good advice does not register for more than a few moments when it goes against our wiring
- Stoicism plays on **dignity and personal aesthetics**, which are part of our genes — this makes it work
- Exhibit *sapere vivere* ("know how to live") in all circumstances
- "Dress at your best on your execution day; try to leave a good impression on the death squad by standing erect and proud"
- Be extremely courteous to your assistant when you lose money; do not blame others for your fate; never exhibit self pity
- "The only article Lady Fortuna has no control over is your behavior. Good luck."
