## Chapter 1: Financial Crisis: A Hardy Perennial (pp 1-23)

### The Landscape of Crisis
- Post-1970s: unprecedented volatility in commodities, currencies, real estate, stocks — and unprecedented frequency of financial crises
- Three waves of bank failures: early 1980s, early 1990s, late 1990s — all systematically related to asset bubbles and rapid economic changes
- Japan 1980s: massive bubble in real estate + stocks, banks grew deposits/loans faster than any Western banks, 7-8 of world's 10 largest banks were Japanese → bubble imploded 1990, banks became wards of government. "A crash without a panic" because government would socialize losses
- Nordic countries (Norway, Sweden, Finland) replicated Japan's pattern simultaneously
- Mexico 1994: success story turned crisis — NAFTA optimism, foreign capital inflow, real peso appreciation, 7% GDP trade deficit, political incidents → peso collapse, banks failed
- Asian crisis 1997: Thailand's domestic loan losses → baht collapse → contagion to Indonesia (rupiah lost 80%), Malaysia, South Korea → spread to Russia (ruble debacle, summer 1998) → LTCM collapse
- Cross-border capital flows = "hot potato" — money from securities sales gets reinvested at ever-higher prices

### The Big Ten Financial Bubbles
1. Dutch Tulip Bulb Bubble 1636
2. South Sea Bubble 1720
3. Mississippi Bubble 1720
4. Late 1920s stock price bubble 1927-1929
5. Bank loans to Mexico and developing countries in 1970s
6. Japan real estate and stocks 1985-1989
7. Finland, Norway, Sweden real estate/stocks 1985-1989
8. Thailand, Malaysia, Indonesia, Asian countries 1992-1997
9. Foreign investment surge in Mexico 1990-1993
10. US over-the-counter stocks (NASDAQ) 1995-2000

### The Thesis
- The cycle of manias and panics results from **pro-cyclical changes in credit supply** — credit expands rapidly in good times, contracts sharply when growth slackens
- A mania = prices in present/near-future inconsistent with prices in distant future
- Rational exuberance morphs into irrational exuberance → "time to get on the train before it leaves the station"
- The bicycle analogy: after years of bubble behavior, the rider needs forward momentum or the bike becomes unstable — there is no plateau, no middle ground
- "Greater fool" theory: buying not for investment returns but anticipating selling to someone else at higher price

### Bubbles vs Ponzi vs Chain Letters
- Ponzi schemes: promise 30-50% monthly returns, pay early investors with new investor money, collapse by month 4-5
- Chain letters: pyramid redistribution from late-comers to early entrants
- Key distinction: Ponzi/chain letters = isolated segments; asset bubbles = macroeconomic consequences because associated with economic euphoria and spending increases

### Policy Implications
- Central question: should governments moderate asset surges to reduce crisis likelihood, or ease hardship when prices decline?
- Lender of last resort dilemma: if investors know government will bail them out, they become less cautious (moral hazard) — but during crisis, priority is stopping the panic
- Thomas Joplin (1825): "There are times when rules and precedents cannot be broken; others, when they cannot be adhered to with safety"
- The "neat trick": always come to the rescue to prevent needless deflation, but always leave it uncertain whether rescue will arrive — instills caution
- Voltaire's Candide reference: "a general was cut off 'to encourage the others'"

### Kindleberger's Framework for the Book
- Central model: Hyman Minsky's financial instability hypothesis (Ch 2)
- Speculation and whether it's destabilizing (Ch 3)
- Credit expansion fuels the flames (Ch 4)
- Crisis stage — can warnings halt manias? (Ch 5): Greenspan's "irrational exuberance" speech (Dec 1996, Dow at 6,600) → Dow peaked at 11,700, NASDAQ went from 1,300 to 5,000+
- Euphoria spills across markets (Ch 6): "A housing boom in Houston is an oil boom in drag"
- International contagion (Ch 7): trade, capital flows, hot money, psychological contagion
- Three linked bubbles: Tokyo → Bangkok → New York (Ch 8)
- Swindles emerge during mania, revealed during panic (Ch 9)
- Policy responses: from laissez-faire to lender of last resort (Ch 10-11)
- International lender of last resort (Ch 12): 1930s depression was wide/deep because no international LOLR existed
- Money is a public good that can be exploited by private parties; banking is difficult to regulate because the public creates close substitutes for money (like lawyers finding tax loopholes)

## Chapter 2: Anatomy of a Typical Crisis (pp 24-27+)

### History vs Economics
- Historians: each event is unique. Economists: there are patterns and regularities
- Crisis cycle models differ from standard business cycle models (Kitchin 39-month inventory, Juglar 7-8 year investment, Kuznets 20-year housing)
- In first two-thirds of 19th century, crises occurred regularly at 10-year intervals: 1816, 1826, 1837, 1847, 1857, 1866 — then less regularly: 1873, 1907, 1921, 1929

### The Minsky Model
- Hyman Minsky's financial instability hypothesis: the financial system is inherently unstable, fragile, and prone to crisis
- Pro-cyclical credit: credit supply increases when economy booms, decreases during slowdowns
- During expansion: investors become optimistic → revise profitability estimates upward → borrow more; lenders' risk assessments decline → they make riskier loans
- When economy slows: investors become cautious, lenders' loan losses increase → both sides retreat
- Tradition of classical economists: John Stuart Mill, Alfred Marshall, Knut Wicksell, Irving Fisher — all focused on credit instability
- Minsky followed Fisher: heavily indebted borrowers who increased debt to finance asset purchases for capital gains — when asset appreciation slows below borrowing costs → distress selling
- Crisis starts with a **"displacement"** — an exogenous shock that changes economic outlook and profit opportunities in at least one important sector

### Displacement Examples
- 1920s US: automobiles, highways, electrification, telephones
- 1980s Japan: financial liberalization + yen surge
- 1980s Nordic: financial liberalization
- 1990s Asia: implosion of Japan bubble → money flows to Thailand, Malaysia, Indonesia + financial liberalization
- 1990s US: information technology revolution, computers, wireless, e-mail
- Also: wars, bumper harvests, crop failures, canals, railroads, unanticipated monetary policy changes

### Displacement → Credit Boom
- If shock is large enough, profit share of GDP increases (US corporate profits: 3% of GDP early 1980s → 10% late 1990s)
- Boom fueled by credit expansion: prebanking era used personal credit; banks expanded via bank notes then deposits; new banks form to grab market share (1970s: European banks poached US banks' Latin America lending)
- Banks "swing in the wind" — euphoric lending then extreme caution
- Even if bank credit is controlled, personal credit can finance the boom
- Positive feedback: more investment → higher national income → more investment

### Minsky's Three-Part Taxonomy
- **Hedge finance**: operating income covers both interest and scheduled principal repayments
- **Speculative finance**: operating income covers interest but must refinance maturing principal with new loans
- **Ponzi finance**: operating income can't even cover interest — must increase debt or sell assets
- Minsky's hypothesis: when economy slows, hedge firms get shunted to speculative, speculative to Ponzi
- Named after Carlos Ponzi (Boston, early 1920s): promised 30% monthly returns, paid old depositors with new deposits, collapsed by month 4

### Overtrading → Revulsion → Panic
- Adam Smith called it "overtrading": speculation, overestimate of returns, excessive leverage
- "There is nothing as disturbing to one's well-being and judgment as to see a friend get rich" — the follow-the-leader effect
- Wall Street analysts in late 1990s projected 15% annual profit growth for 5 years (would have made profit share of GDP 40% higher than ever)
- Even as leverage rises, debt-to-equity ratios may APPEAR to decline because asset prices are rising
- Bubble definition used here: upward price movement over 15-40 months that then implodes — someone with "perfect foresight" would have known it was unsustainable
- Insiders sell to outsiders at the top: "if newcomers are buyers, then insiders must be sellers" — at every moment, purchases by outsiders are balanced by sales by insiders
- NYSE 1928: market value increasing 36%/year → first 8 months of 1929: 53%/year; NASDAQ 1998: 41%/year → next 15 months: 101%/year
- Financial distress → awareness it's time to become liquid → race out of real assets into money
- Trigger: bank failure, swindle revealed, sharp price fall
- 19th century vocabulary: "overtrading" → "revulsion" → "discredit"
- German term: *Torschlusspanik* — "door-shut-panic" — investors crowd through the door before it slams

### International Propagation
- Four channels: (1) arbitrage (commodity/security prices), (2) income/trade links, (3) capital flows, (4) psychological contagion
- October 19, 1987: stock declines instantaneous across all financial centers (except Tokyo) — faster than arbitrage/trade can explain → pure psychological contagion
- "Hawaii is to Tokyo as Miami is to New York" — Japan's boom created a real estate bubble in Hawaii (Japanese bought second homes and golf courses)
- In textbook gold standard: gold flows should be self-correcting. In reality, credit expansion in one country may NOT cause contraction in another — speculative interest overwhelms

### Three Criticisms of the Minsky Model
1. **Each crisis is unique** → response: "the more something changes, the more it remains the same" — details proliferate, structure abides
2. **Model is obsolete** due to institutional changes → response: Japan 1980s, Mexico/Brazil 1980s, Asian crisis 1990s all fit the Minsky pattern perfectly
3. **Bubbles can't exist** because "all information is in the price" (efficient markets) → response: this book provides the counter-evidence

### The Model's Relevance Today
- Applies to forex: currency overshooting/undershooting, speculation-driven crises
- Developing country debt: $125B (1972) → $800B (1982), loans growing 30%/year, interest ~8% — borrowers paid interest with new loan proceeds, lenders never asked "where will they get cash if we stop lending?"
- Japan 1980s: real estate prices up 10x, stocks up 6-7x, annual returns ~30% — business firms abandoned manufacturing for real estate speculation using borrowed money. When Bank of Japan limited new RE loans (early 1990), negative-carry firms couldn't refinance → sold → bubble imploded
- Kindleberger's warning: current US international financial position parallels Mexico/Brazil/Argentina in the 1970s — unsustainable current account deficits funded by foreign capital
- "This book is a study in financial history, not economic forecasting. Investors seem not to have learned from experience."

## Chapter 3: Speculative Manias (pp 38-47+)

### Rationality of Markets
- "Mania" implies loss of touch with rationality — mass hysteria. But economic theory assumes rationality. These must be reconciled.
- Rational expectations: "all the information is in the price" — investors react as if always fully aware of long-term implications (Superman-like kryptonic vision)
- Adaptive expectations: "the trend is your friend" — future = extension of recent past
- Key distinction: rational expectations = future determines present (backward-looking from future); adaptive expectations = past determines future
- Rationality is an *a priori* assumption about how the world *should* work, not a description of how it *has* worked — but it's a useful "pregnant hypothesis" (Karl Popper)

### Friedman vs Nurkse on Destabilizing Speculation
- Ragnar Nurkse (1920s French franc/German mark): speculation in forex WAS destabilizing
- Milton Friedman's counter: destabilizing speculation is impossible because those who buy high and sell low would go bankrupt (Darwinian argument)
- Response: from time to time some investors DO follow loss-making strategies — and they can cause damage before they fail

### The Language of Irrationality
- Historical phrases: "manias... blind passion... financial orgies... frenzies... feverish speculation... epidemic desire to become rich quick... intoxicated investors... turning a blind eye... people without ears to hear or eyes to see... overconfidence... a raging appetite... a mad rush to expand"
- Overend, Gurney (London, Black Friday 1866): called "sapient nincompoops" — losses "so reckless and so foolish that one would think a child who had lent money in the City of London would have lent it better"
- Adam Smith on the South Sea Bubble: "They had an immense capital dividend among an immense number of proprietors. It was naturally to be expected, therefore, that folly, negligence, and profusion should prevail"
- Alfred Marshall: bank panic spreads "as a fire spreads from one wooden house to another until even fireproof buildings succumb to the blaze of a great conflagration"

### Individual Rationality vs Market Irrationality
- Mob psychology / group thinking: all participants change views simultaneously
- Alternative: individuals change views at different stages — most start rational, gradually lose contact with reality
- Fallacy of composition: individual rationality ≠ collective rationality
- Keeping up with the Joneses (Duesenberry effect): both Smiths and Joneses increase spending when incomes rise, reluctant to cut when they decline
- Charles MacKay (South Sea Bubble): banker bought £500 of stock saying "When the rest of the world are mad, we must imitate them in some measure"
- Gustave LeBon: *The Crowd* — mob psychology as occasional deviation from rational behavior

### Two Stages of Speculation
- Stage 1 (sober): rational response to a genuine displacement — firms/investors respond to real profit opportunities
- Stage 2 (mania): anticipation of capital gains dominates — "the first taste is for high interest, but that taste soon becomes secondary. There is a second appetite for large gains to be made by selling the principal"
- 1830s US: initially bought land for cotton cultivation → then bought land purely to resell at higher prices
- British railways 1830s: first phase (pre-1835) were legitimate — shares sold to merchants, Quaker capitalists, industrialists; second phase (post-1835) were bubbles — promoters attracted ladies and clergymen for quick profits
- Vienna building sites 1870s: initially bought for construction → later traded like "speculative poker chips"
- Junk bonds: initially rational market → supply surged → creditworthiness declined sharply
- Key pattern: **reversal between objective and process** — lenders become so enthusiastic about the process (making loans) that they forget the objective (getting repaid)

### Gold Bubble of the 1970s
- Gold: $40/oz (Jan 1970) → $970 (Dec 1979) — had been linked to $35 parity since 1934
- After 1971 Nixon shock, gold became "just another commodity" like pork bellies
- Gold is a "good inflation hedge" over 400 years — real price roughly constant long-term
- But in 1970s: gold price increase far outpaced inflation → buying because price was rising, price rising because of buying
- By end of 1990s: back to ~$300/oz → inflation hedge cliché restored (15x increase since 1900, matching goods prices)

### Insiders vs Outsiders
- Professional insiders destabilize by exaggerating upswings and downswings — they follow "the trend is my friend" (tape watchers / momentum investors)
- Outsider amateurs buy high and sell low — victims of euphoria that affects them late in the day; after they lose, they go back to their regular jobs and save for 5-10 years before the next splurge
- Gold panic of 1869: Jay Gould (insider) drove up gold price, tried to persuade President Grant to depreciate the dollar; outsider speculators bought gold expecting government would restore pre-Civil War parity. On Sep 16, outsiders abandoned their expectation and joined Gould's view. On Sep 22, Gould learned via Grant's brother-in-law that the plan would fail → Gould sold. Black Friday of September 23, 1869 — prices collapsed
- **Bucket shops**: took orders from public to buy/sell securities but never executed them — they assumed the outsider's bet would be wrong. If outsiders proved right, bucket-shop operators fled (to Latvia, Brazil, Costa Rica, Cuba)
- Boiler shops evolved from bucket shops: hustled untutored investors with promises of quick gains using telemarketing — sold to "dentists and undertakers in small towns of America," managed to increase stock prices until most shares had been sold to gullible investors who found, when they tried to sell, that there were no buyers

### Isaac Newton and the South Sea Bubble
- Newton sold his South Sea Company shares in spring 1720 at 100% profit (£7,000). Then the mania gripped him — he bought back in near the top and lost £20,000
- "I can calculate the motions of the heavenly bodies, but not the madness of people"
- He put it out of his mind and never for the rest of his life could he bear to hear the name South Sea
- Classic example: even the most brilliant individual can be an outsider destabilizing speculator

### Fallacy of Composition and Rational Irrationality
- "Devil take the hindmost," *sauve qui pent*, *die Letzen beissen die Runde* ("dogs bite the laggards") — recipes for panic
- Fire in a crowded theater analogy: it is rational for an individual to participate early in the chain and believe all others will think they are rational too
- The **cobweb demonstration**: demand and supply linked with a lag — displacements change expectations, but individual investors fail to account for similar responses by others
- Example: shortage of physicists → young people study physics → by graduation, excess supply → jobs scarce. Same pattern with coffee, sugar, cotton commodities — price increases sharply on initial demand surge, then declines rapidly when new supply arrives
- 1808: Brazil opened to British goods → more goods shipped in weeks than consumed in 20 years, including ice skates and warming pans
- 1820s Latin American independence: boom in lending, mining shares, exports → "The demand is sudden, and as suddenly stops. But too many have acted as if it were likely to continue"
- 1830s cobweb: two-year periodicity, merchants ignorant of how much others would bring to market
- 1850s California gold discovery: extraordinary overshipment from London and Boston — 6-8 cargos/month would suffice, eastern shippers sent 12-15 fully laden first-class ships monthly

### Three Borderline Cases of Rationality
1. **Target workers / Duesenberry effect**: people who get used to a certain income find it difficult to adjust spending downward — "John Bull can stand many things but he cannot stand 2 percent"
- John Stuart Mill: low rates of profit and interest make capitalists dissatisfied with safe mercantile gains → they seek risky speculation
- In France (Restoration to July Monarchy, 1826-1832): landowners earned 2.25-3.75%, industrialists tried for 7-9%, merchants/speculators sought 20-25% — each group chasing higher returns in sequence
- Third World bank loans 1970s: Fed's expansive policy → low interest rates → banks flush with cash → lent to Latin American governments at above-trend rates → commodity prices crashed early 1980s → borrowers couldn't repay
- "When interest goes down, the English commercial world... deserts its usual business in favour of the more profitable... speculation leads to disaster and ultimately must be borne by the central bank"

2. **Hanging on / errors of omission**: Jay Cooke & Co. failed September 18, 1873 because of loans to railroads that couldn't sell bonds (Berlin/Vienna had stopped lending to US). Similarly, when US lending to Germany stopped in 1928, NY banks continued making short-term loans to German borrowers — "when riding a tiger, it seems rational to hang on — at least for a while"
- Hamburg banks lent to Swedish banks during Crimean War for smuggling into Russia → when peace came, Swedes used the money to speculate in shipbuilding, factories, mining → helped embroil Hamburg in the 1857 crisis

3. **Right model, wrong application (Maginot Line psychology)**: having the right mental framework but applying it past its expiration date
- 1760s Hamburg merchants not hurt by commodity price falls until the end of the Seven Years' War; 1799 merchants continuing Napoleonic War expectations into peace
- French copper ring 1888: bankers extrapolated from OPEC's oil success → assumed copper cartels would also work → French syndicate owned 60,000 tons + contracts to buy more → copper price collapsed from £80 to £38/ton → Comptoir d'Escompte saved by 140M franc advance from Bank of France

### Financial Deregulation as Displacement
- Ronald McKinnon (early 1970s) attacked "financial repression" — segmented markets favoring government borrowers and large firms in developing countries
- Latin American countries deregulated → rapid growth of new banks → rapid credit growth → inflation → bank collapses
- McKinnon himself concluded deregulation must be staged carefully
- Same pattern in Poland vs Soviet Union: Poland's transition succeeded partly because people remembered pre-socialist capitalism; Russia's longer socialist period had erased market memory
- **Albania's Ponzi schemes**: transition from command economy → unregulated "banks" promising 30%/month → Albanians sold homes and deposited proceeds → diaspora sent money from NY, Chicago, Frankfurt → some stopped working because interest income exceeded wages → "Alas it was too good to be true"

### Cognitive Dissonance and Repressed Evidence
- Vienna 1873: Austrian enterprises invested heavily in anticipation of the World Exhibition (May 1) → liabilities far exceeded liquid assets → banks stretched credit to the limit → exhibition opened, sales disappointed → May 5-6 market collapsed
- Germany late 1920s: J.W. Beyen noted that the dangers of excessive short-term borrowing from abroad were not faced, even by Finance Minister Schacht — "consciousness was being repressed"

### Displacements — A Catalog
- **Definition**: an outside event or shock that changes horizons, expectations, anticipated profit opportunities — "some sudden advice many times unexpected"
- Must be sufficiently large to have impact on economic outlook; most devaluations and daily events don't qualify
- **War**: crises at beginning of wars (August 1914), end of wars (1713, 1763, 1783, 1816, 1857, 1864, 1873, 1920), and 7-10 years after wars when delayed expectations are falsified (1720, 1772, 1792, 1825, 1873, 1929)
- **Political revolution**: Glorious Revolution 1688 → boom in joint stock companies (140 by 1695, £4.5M capital); Bubble Act of July 1720 forbade new joint-stock companies (ostensibly to protect South Sea Company from rival cash-attracting companies); French Revolution → specie movements 1792-1797; Restoration (1815), July Monarchy (1830), February 1848 revolution, Second Empire (1852); Sepoy Mutiny 1857
- **Monetary displacements**: *Kipper- und Wipperzeit* 1619-1623 (money-changers debased coins, rising debasement spread state to state until coins became worthless); Frederick II of Prussia 1763 (bought silver to replace debased coinage, withdrew old money before new issued → deflationary crisis); Franco-Prussian indemnity post-1871 (coin circulation tripled in 3 years → inflation); Sherman Silver Act 1890 (threat to gold convertibility → crisis of 1893); British debt conversions (1822, 1824, 1888, 1932)
- **Deregulation of financial institutions**: major factor in Japan 1980s bubble — each bank wanted to "grow its loans" faster to climb the hit parade → reckless lending
- **Technological revolution**: 1920s (automobiles, electrification, telephones, radio, movies); 1990s (computers, internet, wireless) — VC firms eagerly financed engineers → mezzanine financing → IPOs arranged by Merrill Lynch, Morgan Stanley → IPO "pop" (shares often 3-4x IPO price on first day) → encouraged more and more IPOs

### "Dow 36,000" Box
- Three books published 1999: *Dow 36,000* (Glassman & Hassett), *Dow 40,000* (Elias), *Dow 100,000* (Kadlec) — nearly identical themes
- Logic: if interest rates stay low and corporate earnings keep growing, Dow must rise much higher because stocks are "no more risky than bonds" → P/E should be much higher
- Reality check: profit share of US GDP has been remarkably constant at ~8%, P/E ratio averaged ~18, bond yields ~5% (earnings yield ~20) — the books' projections required extraordinary departures from long-term norms

### Objects of Speculation
- In the 20th century: primarily real estate or stocks. Earlier periods: more diverse (commodities, land, canals, railroads, foreign bonds, mining shares)
- Only a small proportion of shocks lead to speculative manias
- **Multiple objects = bigger crisis**: in most significant crises, at least two objects of speculation were involved in at least two markets
- 1720: South Sea and Mississippi bubbles related — monetary expansion in both England and France supported speculation; speculation spread from South Sea stock → banks, insurance, real estate, land
- 1763: government war expenditure + chains of discount bills → DeNeufville Brothers' failure triggered sell-off of commodities, ships, securities
- 1772: East India Company stock + collapse of Ayr Bank; heavy investment in housing, turnpikes, canals strained credit
- 1793: country banks, canals, Reign of Terror → fund flows to Britain
- 1825: canals, South American bonds/mines, British exports, cotton, land
- 1847: railway mania + potato disease + wheat crop fluctuation + European revolution
- Japan & Asian countries: real estate and stocks bubbled together — symbiotic relationship where RE companies' stock values track property prices, construction booms boost GDP, bank loan losses stay low while RE rises (and vice versa on the decline)

### National Differences in Speculative Temperament
- Brabanters (Antwerp migrants to Dutch Republic after 1576/1585 sieges) brought a strong gambling temperament; gambling instinct coexisted with Calvinist/Lutheran frugality
- Banking institutions may matter more: Juglar argued French crises in 18th century were less abrupt because (after John Law) credit was less used and less abused in France
- Alternative French view: severe bankruptcy law created high business honesty — "sons toiling for years to pay the debts of their fathers"
- United States: "the classic home of commercial and financial panics," presumably because of wildcat banking
- Michel Chevalier (1830s): contrasted French moderation with American speculation (but noted American speculation was productive — canals, railroads, factories)
- "All the world speculates and it speculates on everything. From Maine to the Red River, the United States has become an immense Rue Quincampoix [the Wall Street of the Mississippi Bubble]"
- Contradictory national characterizations abound: "The French nation is prudent and economical, the English nation is enterprising and speculative" vs "France has not shown proofs of prudence equal to those of Scotland; its nerves are extremely susceptible, impressionable in matters of credit"
- Conclusion: speculative temperament may differ among countries AND within a country over time, depending on moods of national elation or depression

## Chapter 4: Fueling the Flames — The Expansion of Credit (pp 64-83)

### The Two Axioms
- **Axiom 1**: Inflation depends on the growth of money
- **Axiom 2**: Asset price bubbles depend on the growth of credit
- Most expansions of money and credit do NOT lead to manias — there are many more economic expansions than manias — but every mania has been associated with credit expansion

### Historical Credit Instruments
- Each era found its own credit mechanisms to fuel speculation:
  - Tulip mania: credits from bulb sellers ("vendor financing")
  - Mississippi Bubble: John Law's Banque Générale/Royale
  - South Sea: Sword Blade Bank
  - Holland 1763: *Wisselruiti* (chains of accommodation bills merchant to merchant)
  - Canal mania 1793: new country bank loans
  - US early 19th century: bills of exchange replaced silver in triangular US-China-Britain trade (lower transaction costs → silver stayed in US → expanded money supply)
  - 1850s global boom: new gold discoveries + new banks (France, Germany, US) + clearing-houses (NY, Philadelphia, London) — clearing-house certificates became a new form of money
  - France 1882: fortnightly stock exchange clearing (*reportage*) gave buyers up to 14 days interest-free credit
  - US 1920s: call-money market expansion financed stock market boom
  - US 1893: addition of silver coins to money supply
  - 1907: trust company loan expansion
  - Pre/post-WWI: gold-exchange standard expanded international credit base

### Credit Innovation is Systematic, Not Accidental
- After WWII: negotiable certificates of deposit (CDs) contributed to credit expansion — Austrian banks had developed similar *Cassenscheine* in the 1870s
- 1950s-60s US banks: shifted from **asset management** (loan growth follows deposit growth) to **liability management** (deposit growth follows desired loan growth) — banks became far more aggressive
- **Kuwait Souk al-Manakh (1977-1982)**: shares and real estate bought with post-dated checks; value of circulating checks reached ~$100 billion — far exceeding bank deposits. In July 1982, sellers tried to collect → checks bounced
- Key inference: credit expansion is not accidental but systematic — participants continuously seek to reduce transaction costs and economize on holding money

### The Perpetuum Mobile of Money
- Central bankers' efforts to control money growth have been continuously offset by development of new near-money substitutes
- History: copper displaced by silver, silver by gold — each was an effort to gain control over money supply
- Central banks monopolized currency note issuance (restricted private/country/joint-stock banks)
- Reserve requirements imposed: Bank Act of 1844 → Federal Reserve Act of 1913 → requirements against CDs → against offshore borrowings
- The process is Sisyphean: "fix any M_i and in economic booms the market will create new forms of money and near-money substitutes to get around the limit"
- **Eurocurrency deposits** (1960s): US banks' offshore branches in London/Zurich/Luxembourg escaped interest rate ceilings, reserve requirements, FDIC premiums — an end-run around regulation
- **Money market funds** (1970s): stock brokerages paid interest on deposits not guaranteed by any government agency
- **Radcliffe Commission** (1959, UK): "an indefinitely wide range of financial institutions" create close money substitutes — "there is no limit to the velocity of circulation"
- **Home-equity credit lines**: recent innovation letting households engage in liability management (like banks did 30-40 years earlier) — economize on money holdings, increase spending with same money supply

### What IS Money? — The Definitional Problem
- Bagehot: "Men of business in England... are perplexed to define accurately what money is: *how* to count they know, but *what* to count they do not know"
- John Stuart Mill: purchasing power = money in pocket + money at banker's + all credit one can command
- Debate over M₁ vs M₂ vs M₃ has lasted two centuries
- "Beautiful credit! The foundation of modern society... 'I wasn't worth a cent two years ago, and now I owe two million dollars'"

### Quality of Debt (Minsky Taxonomy Applied)
- Credit-rating agencies rank debt quality of firms, governments, households
- Minsky's three-part taxonomy applied to corporate debt:
  - **Hedge finance**: cash from operations > debt service (interest + principal)
  - **Speculative finance**: cash covers interest but must refinance principal
  - **Ponzi finance**: cash can't even cover interest — must borrow more or sell assets; "unless there is a miracle" the firm cannot make payments
- Parallel to public finance: a government with a "primary fiscal deficit" (receipts < payments excluding interest) must borrow more than needed to pay scheduled interest
- During booms: firms shift toward speculative/Ponzi as lenders become willing to make riskier loans
- During slowdowns: firms that were hedge get shunted to speculative; speculative firms get shunted to Ponzi — "an edifice of debt contracted to finance risky ventures is inherently unstable"

### Drexel Burnham Lambert and Junk Bonds
- Junk bonds = bonds of firms not rated by major credit-rating agencies, paying 3-4% higher interest than investment-grade
- Many were "fallen angels" — once investment-grade, subsequently downgraded
- Innovation in 1970s-80s: Drexel Burnham Lambert (mastermind: Michael Milken) began issuing new junk bonds — firms issued them to finance leveraged buyouts
- **The "free lunch" sales pitch**: diversified junk bond portfolio would earn enough extra interest to cover occasional defaults. Reality: by early 1990s, junk bond owners lost one-third of their money; the extra 3-4% interest was insufficient to compensate for defaults
- Many buyers were S&Ls and thrift institutions that attracted deposits with government guarantees, then used the cash to buy Milken's junk bonds → about half the firms went bankrupt → thrifts failed → taxpayer losses of tens of billions
- **Consistent with Minsky**: many junk bonds were speculative-grade when issued during good times; recession pushed issuers into Ponzi territory → default was inevitable without "a miracle." "A very expensive free lunch."

### Bills of Exchange
- Claims for future payment from seller of goods — a form of vendor financing developed because coin supply was inelastic
- Buyer repaid in 90 or 120 days; bills discounted with banks for cash
- Bills circulated like money — passed from hand to hand with endorsements, might have 5-10 endorsers; "The bill was now money"
- Some bills for as little as £10 circulated with 50-60 names on them
- In Lancashire, bank notes were disliked; bills of exchange were the primary payment instrument — Bank of England note circulation actually declined £9M from 1852-1857 while bank deposits rose from £17.7M to £40M and bills of exchange rose from £66M to £200M
- **Accommodation paper**: bills gradually separated from actual commercial transactions — drawn when firms needed money, not tied to specific sales; became "a post-dated check or a promissory note"
- Bills in chains: A draws on B, B on C, C on D — each link increases credit supply. If one link fails, the chain collapses and brings down everyone including well-capitalized firms
- Sir Francis Baring: clerks not worth £100 were allowed discounts of £5,000 to £10,000
- 1857 London: firms with capital under £10,000 had obligations of £900,000
- Hamburg 1857: man with capital of £100 had £400,000 of acceptances outstanding
- **LTCM parallel**: equity capital of $5B, borrowings of $125B — leverage ratio of 25:1 (vs typical hedge fund <10:1). In 18th century, many firms speculated at 10-20x their capital
- Fictitious names introduced into chains to improve appearance of creditworthiness; bills written for odd amounts to suggest underlying commercial transaction

### Call Money — The Crash of 1882 (France) and 1929 (US)
- **Union Générale crash (France, 1882)**: Eugène Bontoux's bank, capitalized at 25M francs, grew rapidly via stock issuance. Stock rose from 1,250 francs (March 1881) to 3,040 (Dec 14, 1881) → then crashed: 2,950 (Jan 10), 2,800 (Jan 16), 1,300 (Jan 19). Brokers were 18M francs short on Jan 19 and 33M short at month-end liquidation Jan 31
- Trading was 10% margin — borrow 90% via call money (*reports*). Rising prices needed new money to pay withdrawing winners; falling prices triggered margin calls
- "Silk merchants, cloth manufacturers, industrialists, tradesmen, dry-goods merchants, grocers, butchers, people with fixed incomes, janitors, shoemakers" — all diverted from regular business to stock market
- **1929 US crash**: call loans from "all others" (non-NY banks + individuals) rose from <$2B (end 1926) to ~$4B (end 1928) to >$6.6B (Oct 4, 1929). Brokers' loans of NY banks actually declined from $1.6B to $1.1B over same period
- After crashes: in 1929, NY banks maintained/increased brokers' loans; in 1882, Paris banks advanced 18M francs to Union Générale and 80M to brokers — central money market banks eased the adjustment but didn't prevent the collapse
- **Post-1929 regulation**: Fed imposed 50% margin requirement on stocks. But S&P 500 futures on Chicago Mercantile Exchange only required 10% margin — arbitrageurs linked the two markets, effectively allowing 10% margin nationwide. After October 1987, debate over unifying regulation

### The Gold-Exchange Standard
- Central banks held liquid securities denominated in pounds/dollars/marks alongside gold reserves
- Developed before WWI (not just post-Genoa 1922 as commonly believed); Montagu Norman of Bank of England promoted it to support British balance of payments
- Key mechanism for credit expansion: if a country sells bonds in London and holds the proceeds as reserves, and Britain doesn't contract its own credit in response → double-counting → net credit expansion
- Same unstable dynamic as earlier gold standard: US borrowed in gold from Britain during 19th century upswings — gold loans expanded credit without contracting it in the lending country

### Instability of Credit and the Great Depression
- **Monetarist view** (Friedman & Schwartz): Depression caused by Federal Reserve's failure to expand money supply — money supply declined Aug 1929 to March 1933; stock market crash was incidental
- **Keynesian challenge** (Peter Temin): Did spending decline cause money supply to fall, or vice versa? Temin used econometrics — found spending decline preceded money supply decline. Real money balances actually *increased* 1-18% between 1929-1931 depending on measure. "No evidence that changes in the money supply between the stock market crash and the British departure from gold in September 1931 caused the depression"
- **Credit instability view** (Minsky, Henry Simons): Neither monetarists nor Keynesians account for the instability of credit and fragility of the banking system
- Industrial production declined 4-5 months BEFORE the stock market crash (index: 127 June → 122 Sept → 117 Oct → 106 Nov → 99 Dec; auto production: 660,000 March → 440,000 Aug → 319,000 Oct → 92,500 Dec)
- As stock market approached its apex, funds were channeled from consumption/production to the call-money market: call money rose from $6.4B (end Dec 1928) to $8.5B (early Oct 1929)
- When the market crashed, credit froze — loans to finance imports declined sharply
- **Henry Simons**: recommended 100% currency reserve against bank deposits + restrictions on open-book credit and installment loans + limiting government debt to non-interest-bearing money and very long-term perpetual obligations — goal was to stamp out the variability of credit

### Simons vs Friedman vs Neo-Austrians
- **Henry Simons**: all financial wealth should be held in equity form, no fixed money contracts, 100% reserve banking — wanted to eliminate the variability of credit entirely. Utopian but "his diagnosis of the tendency toward unstable short-term borrowing and repayment is on target"
- **Milton Friedman**: at sharp variance with Simons — let market forces determine demand/supply for financial assets; controlling money supply growth alone would prevent major crises. "Confident that control of the growth of the money supply would prevent major business cycles and that instability of the credit mechanism is not to be feared"
- **Neo-Austrian school** (Hayek, Timberlake, Vaubel, Yeager, White, Selgin): abolish active monetary policy entirely — let any bank, company, or person issue "money." Market competition ensures good money drives out bad. White defends this using Scottish free banking 1772-1845 as evidence
- Problem: historical evidence doesn't support "good money drives out bad" — English country banks (1745-1835), Michigan wildcat banking (1830s), Latin American/East Asian deregulation all produced bad money alongside good

### Can Central Banks Restrain Credit?
- Global 1970s inflation: US expansive monetary policy + other countries' reserve accumulation → credit bubbles
- Central question: can a central bank restrain credit instability? If they fix money supply, raise rates at first sign of euphoria — would that prevent manias?
- Historical evidence: weight of history suggests different monetary policy might have *moderated* booms but would not have *eliminated* them completely
- **Bank of England failures**: attacked for blindness to approaching 1839 crisis; 1850s gold glut → interest rate declines → bills of exchange kept increasing even as discount rate rose → crisis of 1857 not forestalled
- 1863-64: Bank of England raised rate to 9% twice — delayed but didn't prevent 1866 crash
- 1873: Bank of England changed discount rate 24 times, avoided Austrian/German/US crises — but unclear whether this was fine-tuning or just increasing capital flow sensitivity
- **1907 panic**: preparatory expansion involved out-of-town banks lending in NY + heavy London accommodation paper borrowing. No US central bank existed to act. London raised bank rate in Oct 1906 → slowed boom → but didn't prevent "rich man's panic" (March 1907) or full panic (October 1907)
- **1929**: Fed raised discount rate from 5% to 6% on August 9, 1929 — "with the same timing and the same absence of result"
- "There are no positive limitations to the expansion of individual credit"
- **Division of labor**: by 1825, implicit agreement — private bankers in London/provinces would "finance the boom" while Bank of England would "finance the crisis." In the US after 1837 (no central bank), major NY banks were caught between profit-making (contributing to credit instability) and guardian role (holding deposits of banks elsewhere)

## Chapter 5: The Critical Stage (pp 90-116)

### Changing Expectations
- Lord Overstone's pattern (quoted by Bagehot): "quiescence, improvement, confidence, prosperity, excitement, overtrading, CONVULSION, pressure, stagnation, ending again in quiescence" — five stages of euphoria before the convulsion
- Rational expectations theory assumes instantaneous adjustment to each shock; financial history shows expectations change slowly at some times, rapidly at others — "sometimes at different moments and at other times more or less simultaneously"
- The shift from confidence to pessimism = the source of credit instability — borrowers realize their indebtedness is too large relative to income, reduce spending to pay down debt; lenders recognize risky loans, refuse to renew them, raise credit standards

### Financial Distress — Duration and Character
- Distress may last weeks, months, even years: US post-1929 downturn lasted 4 years; Japan's post-1990 malaise lasted 10+ years; Korea recovered from 1998 crisis by 1999; Hong Kong deflation lasted 5 years after 1997
- Two meanings: **commercial distress** (prices/profits declining, firms going bankrupt) vs **financial distress** (profitability declined so sharply that firm likely can't service debt)
- Vocabulary of distress: *uneasiness, apprehension, tension, stringency, pressure, uncertainty, ominous conditions, fragility*
- Meteorological metaphors: "the oppressive atmosphere that precedes a storm"
- Geological metaphors: "a slight premonitory movement of the ground under our feet" (Lord Overstone, 1845 — two years before the 1847 panic)
- "A general collapse of credit, however short the time it lasts, is more fearful than the most terrible earthquake" (Michel Chevalier)
- **Distress indicators**: gold reserve ratios, debt-to-capital ratios, bank losses relative to capital, external debt service / export earnings, P/E ratios for stocks, rental ratios for real estate
- **The terror of limits**: approaching any ceiling (Bank of England note ceiling, US Treasury $100M gold minimum, Reichsbank gold reserve ratio) psychologically amplifies the panic — "when you impose a limit, the existence of that limit must increase the alarm"
- **Symptoms of distress**: sharply rising interest rates, widening subprime-prime spread, currency depreciation, rising bankruptcies, end of price increases in commodities/securities/real estate

### Government Warnings — Do They Work?
- Historical record: "little support for the view that statements from government officials have much impact in dampening euphoria"
- When asset prices are rising 20-30%/year, warnings are likely to be ignored
- **1825**: Prime Minister Canning, Chancellor of Exchequer, Sir Francis Baring, McCulloch all warned of speculation excesses → crisis happened anyway; Lord Liverpool then refused to rescue speculators because he'd warned them 9 months earlier
- **1837**: Belgian and French authorities tried to repress speculation by forbidding quotation of certain notes and shares → futile; "women and foreigners" were becoming involved
- **1839**: Lamartine warned about railroad securities in French Chamber of Deputies
- **1857**: French minister of interior acted against swindlers, Napoleon III published letter limiting support to catastrophes "beyond human anticipation" → may have briefly helped
- **1869/1873**: Austrian National Bank restrictions in 1869 produced a mini-crash but not the big one (1873). Warnings about Prussian government-railroad corruption had no impact
- **1888**: *The Economist* warned about Argentine *cedulas* (land bonds) → warnings proved ineffective
- **1929**: Paul Warburg (Kuhn, Loeb & Co., Fed co-founder) warned in February 1929 that stock prices were too high → investors paused briefly, then kept buying
- **Greenspan "irrational exuberance" (Dec 1996)**: Dow at 6,600 → Dow peaked at 11,700; NASDAQ went from 1,300 to 5,000+. August 1999: raised discount rate 0.25% → stock market barely noticed
- **The timing dilemma**: warnings must come early enough to forestall excesses but late enough to be credible. Roger Babson sold clients' stocks in 1928 → appeared foolish for over a year
- **Punch bowl metaphor**: authorities reluctant to "take the punch bowl away from the party just as the party is getting going"
- **Inflation targeting**: modern central banks focus on consumer price stability but the policy question is whether they should also target real estate/stock prices when these diverge far from equilibrium

### The LTCM Case Study
- LTCM: $5B equity, $125B debt (25:1 leverage vs <10:1 for typical hedge funds), plus tens of billions in derivatives positions
- Strategy: exploit small price differentials between similar securities (e.g., buy illiquid 29-year Treasury, short liquid 30-year Treasury — tiny yield difference × massive position = big profit)
- Major banks that lent to LTCM also mimicked its portfolio positions → crowded trades
- Spring 1998: long emerging market bonds, hedged by shorting US Treasuries → Russian crisis → EM bonds collapsed, Treasury prices rose → LTCM lost on BOTH legs of its hedge
- "Rock and a hard place": selling individual positions would depress prices further → accelerating losses
- Fed used its regulatory muscle to induce banks to invest in LTCM → banks acquired 90% ownership → orderly wind-down instead of firesale

### Onset of a Crisis — *Causa Remota* vs *Causa Proxima*
- **Causa remota**: the deep structural cause — expansion of credit and speculation
- **Causa proxima**: the immediate trigger — a bankruptcy, suicide, flight, fraud revelation, refusal of credit, or change of view by a major market participant
- The *causa proxima* may be trivial but it reverses expectations → leveraged investors face margin calls → forced selling → prices fall further → bank failures → race for liquidity
- Conspiracy theories always emerge about who sold first: Joseph Kennedy, Bernard Baruch (1929); Protestant-Jewish cartel (1882); Thomas Guy who sold £54,000 of South Sea stock over 6 weeks in 1720 (and used the fortune to endow Guy's Hospital — "the best memorial of the Bubble")

### Specific Crisis Triggers
- **1847**: French sold surplus wheat to Britain at below-market prices (46→93 shillings Aug 1846 to May 1847 → 56 shillings by July); wheat imports rose from 2.3M to 4.4M quarters; Ireland's potato disease + France's smallest wheat crop in 100 years, followed by France's largest crop in 100 years
- **Baring crisis 1890**: German investors stopped buying Argentine bonds (1888) — whether from general uneasiness, FX instability, or domestic boom diverting capital. British absorbed more of the £200M Argentine bond total. Buenos Aires Drainage & Waterworks offering failed (Nov 1888) → Baring stuck with acceptance credits → Argentine commodity prices fell → government couldn't repay → Bank of England warned Baring to limit acceptances (summer 1890, £30M outstanding) → NY crisis (October) → £4M of Baring acceptances matured in November → Baring couldn't sell or borrow → crisis
- **Paris-Lyon-Marseilles railroad**: cost 300M francs instead of projected 200M → precipitated selling
- **Argentina 2001**: 1:1 peso/dollar peg + currency board (strict Currency School doctrine). Government ran fiscal deficits funded by privatization and borrowing. As dollar appreciated in late 1990s → Argentine exports uncompetitive → tax receipts fell → interest rates rose → Brazil devalued (Jan 1998) intensifying Argentine recession → eventually devalued + defaulted
- **Greenspan's Y2K paradox**: In 1999 Fed flooded the system with liquidity to prevent Y2K computer failures → money had to go somewhere → fed stock market speculation
- **The shotgun vs rifle dilemma**: when asset prices and commodity prices move in different directions, central banks can't target one without hitting the other — "policymakers cannot kill two birds with one stone." Japan 1980s: housing prices required tighter credit, but economic expansion required accommodative policy

### The Pinprick — What Ends the Boom?
- The specific signal that shifts sentiment is unpredictable — a bankruptcy, a fraud exposure, a refusal to renew credit, sometimes an apparently minor event
- **Japan 1990**: Ministry of Finance raised margin requirements on stock purchases → Nikkei fell from 39,000 (Dec 1989) to 20,000 by October 1990; real estate continued rising until 1991 then began long decline
- **NASDAQ 2000**: peaked March 10, 2000 at 5,048 → fell 40% in 12 months; trigger debated — may have been Microsoft antitrust ruling, or simply exhaustion of greater fools
- **Asian crisis 1997**: Thai baht defense failed July 2, 1997 → contagion to Indonesia, Malaysia, Korea within weeks; trigger was depletion of foreign exchange reserves defending the peg
- "Like an earthquake, the loss of confidence becomes self-fulfilling — the fear of a crash helps cause the crash"

### Policy Lags and Foxwell's Dilemma
- **Foxwell's dilemma**: if central bank restricts credit too early, boom is stunted and potential growth lost; too late, bubble inflates further and crash is worse
- "No man of spirit will consent to remain poor if he believes he is getting rich fast" → euphoria suppresses rational calculation
- Policy actions take 6-18 months to affect economic conditions → by the time a bubble is recognized, tightening arrives too late to prevent the crisis — it arrives just in time to worsen the downturn
- Paul Volcker (1979): raised rates dramatically to kill inflation → created the deepest recession since 1930s, but succeeded in breaking inflationary psychology. Tradeoff: timing was right for inflation but devastating for employment

### Bank Runs and Financial Contagion
- When depositors fear bank failure, they queue to withdraw → fear becomes self-fulfilling because even sound banks can't liquidate all assets immediately
- **Ohio and Maryland S&L crises (1985)**: state-insured (not FDIC) savings institutions → one institution failed → depositors at other state-insured S&Ls panicked → runs spread across the state system
- **Franklin National Bank (1974)**: $5B bank, largest US bank failure at the time. Connected to Michele Sindona (later convicted of fraud). Bank tried to hide foreign exchange trading losses → when revealed, interbank lenders withdrew → FDIC rescue
- **Continental Illinois (1984)**: $40B bank, 7th largest in US. Bought $1B in loan participations from Penn Square Bank (Oklahoma) → Penn Square failed (oil prices dropped) → Continental's loans turned bad → electronic bank run (institutional depositors withdrew via wire transfer) → FDIC + Fed rescue, "too big to fail" doctrine born
- **Fidelity 1987**: when Fidelity Magellan fund announced losses after October 1987 crash → phone lines jammed with redemption requests → forced to sell holdings → depressed prices further
- **Indonesia 1997-98**: IMF required closure of 16 banks → depositors at remaining banks panicked ("if 16 can close, why not mine?") → runs spread → IMF intervention worsened the crisis

### Crashes, Panics, and the Debt-Deflation Spiral
- A crash = sudden, severe price decline in assets. A panic = sudden fear leading to a rush for liquidity
- Not all crashes produce panics and not all panics follow crashes — but the combination is devastating
- **Positive feedback loops**: price decline → margin calls → forced selling → further price decline → more margin calls → fire-sale prices → insolvencies → bank failures
- **Irving Fisher's debt-deflation theory (1933)**: falling prices increase the real burden of debt → debtors sell assets to service debt → selling depresses prices further → more debtors default → banks fail → credit contracts → spending falls → prices fall more. "The more the debtors pay, the more they owe"
- Fisher's 9-link chain: debt liquidation → distress selling → contraction of deposit currency → fall in price level → fall in net worth → fall in profits → reduction in output/trade/employment → pessimism → hoarding/slowing velocity

### Panic of 1825 — A Case Study
- One of the most severe in British financial history — 73 of England's ~770 banks failed
- Background: Latin American independence boom (bonds, mines, exports) + cheap credit + speculative company promotions (including one company to "drain the Red Sea and recover gold from Pharaoh's army crossing")
- Trigger: collapse of commodity prices + failure of several London banks
- Consequences: near-barter conditions in parts of England — people couldn't get money at any price; some firms offered 30-60% interest for short-term loans
- Bank of England almost failed to meet its own note obligations; rescued at the last minute by finding a box of £1 notes in a vault

### The Panic of 1857 — Data
- Railway securities collapsed: NY Central stock 93 → 61; Erie Railroad 64 → 18
- Commodity prices crashed: pork $24/bbl → $13; flour from $7.70 → $3.50
- 1,415 banks suspended payments; interest rates reached 60-100% on call money
- "One of the most disastrous years in the memory of any man of business" — *London Economist*

### 2001 — A Mild Recession Leading to a Housing Bubble
- After NASDAQ crash, Fed cut rates aggressively: from 6.5% (Jan 2001) to 1.75% (Dec 2001) to 1% (June 2003)
- Low interest rates triggered mortgage refinancing boom → home equity extraction → consumer spending supported despite stock market losses
- Housing prices rose 50-100% in major cities (2001-2005) as mortgage rates hit 40-year lows
- "The stock market bubble was replaced by a housing market bubble" — the cure for one crisis planted the seeds of the next
- Greenspan dismissed the idea of a housing bubble: "at most a froth" — because housing markets are local, not national
- Warning signs ignored: mortgage underwriting standards collapsed, subprime lending exploded, adjustable-rate mortgages with teaser rates became common, mortgage-backed securities became complex and opaque

## Chapter 6: Euphoria and Economic Booms (pp 113-122)

### The Skyscraper Indicator
- Mark Thornton: correlation between construction of world's tallest building and onset of economic crisis
- Singer Building (1908) → Panic of 1907; Woolworth Building (1913) → 1914 crisis; Chrysler Building + Empire State Building (1929-31) → Great Depression; Sears Tower + World Trade Center (1973-74) → stagflation; Petronas Towers (1997) → Asian crisis; Taipei 101 (2004) → Burj Khalifa planned during euphoria
- Mechanism: skyscrapers are speculative real estate + cheap credit + corporate prestige → they get built at the peak of euphoria, completed just as the downturn begins

### Feedback Loops — Wealth Effect and Cost of Capital
- **Wealth effect**: as stock/RE prices rise → households feel wealthier → increase consumption spending → GDP rises → corporate profits rise → stock prices rise more (positive feedback loop)
- **Cost of capital channel**: rising stock prices → cost of equity capital falls → firms invest more → GDP rises → profits rise → stock prices rise
- Both channels reinforce each other during booms and reverse during busts
- The feedback loops explain why crashes can be so sudden — once the loop reverses, each falling domino accelerates the next

### Tulipmania — The Full Story
- Tulips arrived in Europe from Turkey (Constantinople) in the 1550s
- Speculation concentrated in autumn 1636 — bulbs traded while still in the ground (futures contracts)
- Down payments made in kind: houses, livestock, paintings, land → implying sellers expected tulip prices to keep rising forever
- Single Semper Augustus bulb: 6,000 guilders (a skilled craftsman earned 200-300 guilders/year)
- Context: tulipmania was part of a broader Dutch boom — overseas trade expansion, East India Company profits, Amsterdam warehouse and canal construction, brewery investments, Baltic grain trade → general euphoria
- Crash: February 3, 1637 — prices collapsed overnight when a routine bulb auction found no buyers. Within weeks, prices fell to 1-5% of peak values
- Aftermath: government tried to enforce contracts at 10% of agreed price → most contracts simply voided → relatively little economic damage because the real economy was sound

### Stock Market and Real Estate Connections
- Real estate and stock markets often bubble simultaneously because: (1) RE companies' stock valuations reflect property values; (2) construction booms boost GDP → corporate profits → stock prices; (3) both depend on credit availability
- **Homer Hoyt's five Chicago real estate cycles** (1830-1933): each cycle lasted 15-20 years; each boom involved increasingly speculative platting of lots far beyond current demand
- Berlin 1870s: called "Chicago on the Spree" because of frenzied real estate development after German unification (1871)
- **The greater fool theory**: "it was a great place to make money... in its real estate because theichiban baka, or No. 1 fool, could always be relied upon to pay the No. 2 fool more than No. 2 had paid"

### Japan's Bubble — The Full Story
- Real estate prices: index 100 (1955) → 5,800 (1980) → roughly 9x more by 1991 peak
- Nikkei stock index: from ~100 (1950s) → 39,000 (December 1989)
- Industrial firms abandoned manufacturing to speculate in real estate → Toyota, Matsushita, Sony had RE divisions
- Deregulation in 1980s: each bank raced to "grow its loans" → banks lent against inflated RE collateral → RE prices rose more → banks lent more (positive feedback)
- Crash began January 1990: Nikkei lost 48% in 9 months
- RE peaked in 1991, then declined for 15 years — commercial RE eventually fell 80-90% from peak
- "Debt, deflation, default, demography, and deregulation" — five Ds explaining Japan's lost decade(s)
- By 2002: Nikkei at 8,000 (down 80% from peak), bank bad loans estimated at 30-40% of GDP, consumer prices falling, workforce aging

### Should Central Banks Target Asset Prices?
- Traditional view: central banks should target only consumer price inflation (CPI), not asset prices
- Challenge: Japan's consumer prices were stable during the 1980s bubble, but asset prices rose 400-900% → CPI targeting gave a false "all clear" signal
- Counter-argument: difficult to identify bubbles in real time; if central bank raises rates to cool asset prices, it may crash the economy unnecessarily
- Middle ground (some economists): central banks should "lean against the wind" — not explicitly target asset prices but factor them into monetary policy decisions as indicators of financial stability risk
- Kindleberger's implicit view: the failure to address asset price bubbles was the major policy error of the late 20th century (Japan, NASDAQ, housing)

## Chapter 7: International Contagion (pp 123-?)

### Allocating Blame for Crises
- Andrew Jackson (1837): "The bank is trying to kill me, but I will kill it" — blamed the Second Bank of US for the crisis
- After each crisis, blame is apportioned to foreign influences, domestic speculators, central banks, or governments depending on national prejudice
- Academic debate: were crises transmitted internationally or were they independent events that happened to coincide?

### Purely National vs International Crises
- Some crises are overwhelmingly domestic: US S&L crisis (1980s), Japan's bubble (1990s), Albania Ponzi schemes (1997)
- Others are clearly international: 1825, 1857, 1873, 1890, 1907, 1929, 1997-98
- Key question: what are the transmission mechanisms?

### Transmission Mechanisms — Arbitrage and Market Links
- **Commodity market links**: price changes in one country's commodity markets transmit through trade to other countries (wheat, cotton, metals trade globally)
- **Securities market links**: foreign securities listed on multiple exchanges → price changes transmit instantly
- **Banking links**: international interbank lending → when one country's banks fail, foreign creditor banks face losses
- **Psychology/contagion**: news of a crash in one market causes fear in others, even without direct financial links → "pure contagion"
- Stock price correlations across countries have increased over time as markets became more integrated — 1987 crash transmitted globally within hours

### Capital Flows and Currency Effects
- US inflation in the late 1960s-70s → capital flows from US to Germany, Japan → their monetary bases expanded → inflation spread internationally
- Capital movements respond to: wars, revolutions, technical innovations, new markets/resources, changes in growth rates, monetary/fiscal policy changes, privatization of state firms, perception of currency mispricing
- **Currency appreciation → goods deflation**: appreciation of the yen put downward pressure on internationally-traded goods in Japan; depreciation of Argentine, Uruguayan, Australian, New Zealand currencies in early 1930s → decline in US wheat prices → farm bankruptcies in Missouri, Indiana, Illinois, Iowa, Arkansas, North Carolina
- **Boom-drain dynamic**: a boom in one country attracts foreign money AND may reduce money flow to others — in 1872 Berlin/Vienna stopped lending to NY; in 1928 US stock market boom curtailed loans/bond purchases from Germany, Australia, Latin America → tipped their economies downward BEFORE the October 1929 crash
- Similarly in 1982: collapse of syndicated bank loans to Mexico, Brazil, Argentina → sharp currency depreciations

### Scandinavian and Hawaiian Spillovers
- Stock and RE prices in Scandinavian countries rose 3x (Norway) to 5x (Sweden, Finland) in late 1980s — connected to Japanese bank deregulation allowing offshore lending
- Tokyo's RE surge transmitted to Hawaii ("Tokyo as Miami is to New York") — Japanese tourists, second homes, golf course developments; one hotel property would have needed $800/night to cover costs
- When Tokyo's boom ended → Hawaii entered a lost decade of economic stagnation

### The Kipper- und Wipperzeit (1619-1623)
- A financial crisis with only metallic money, no bank credit
- Princes, abbots, bishops, the Holy Roman Emperor debased subsidiary coinage (not gold/silver) to fund the Thirty Years' War (1618)
- Debasement was initially local → entrepreneurial spirit found it more profitable to take bad coins across borders to exchange for good coins → the victim territory then debased its own coin in defense → cascading competitive debasement
- Spread from small scale (~1600) through Germany, Austria, Hungary, Czechoslovakia, Poland, and via Lvov to Russia
- "Debasement accelerated in hyperfashion until the subsidiary coins became practically worthless, and children played with them in the street" — as recounted in Tolstoy's "Ivan the Fool"

### South Sea and Mississippi Bubbles (1720) — International Links
- Åkerman called 1720 "the first international crisis" — affected France, Britain, Netherlands, northern Italy, Hamburg
- Connected: as early as 1717, British investors traded shares of John Law's companies on Rue de Quincampoix in Paris. By May 1719, the British ambassador in Paris received letters from Scotland begging him to buy Compagnie des Indes stock
- 30,000 foreigners including British nobility traveled to Paris to subscribe
- Ambassador Stair urged the British government to compete with Law and slow money flow to Paris
- Cross-flows: as Law's system peaked in Dec 1719, some (including Duke of Chandos) sold South Sea stock to buy Mississippi stock; simultaneously continentals were buying South Sea stock in London
- Banker Martin subscribed £500 to South Sea with the remark: "When the rest of the world are mad, we must imitate them in some measure"
- Canton of Berne speculated with £200,000 of public funds, sold for £2M profit
- Amsterdam profited from its position between Paris and London — Dutch sold Mississippi at the right psychological moment, lost little. Dutch banker Crellius: "Exchange Alley resembled nothing so much as if all the Lunatics had escaped out of the Madhouse at once"
- By autumn 1720, London and the Continent shared the financial disaster — Dutch banks "shortened sail, recalling advances, refusing further credit, selling stocks held as collateral"

### 1763 to 1819 — A Succession of International Crises
- **1763**: Holland, Hamburg, Prussia, Scandinavia, with help from London. Amsterdam was the entrepôt for payment of money to British allies; Dutch expanded credit via *Wisselruiti* (chains of accommodation bills) → DeNeufville Brothers' failure triggered collapse. Frederick II's *coup de grâce*: recalled debased wartime coins, withdrew old before issuing new → deflationary crunch. London rescued Amsterdam and took over Dutch trade/finance with Scandinavia and Russia
- **1772**: spread from Scotland/London to Amsterdam, Stockholm, St. Petersburg
- **1792-93**: French Revolution outflows of specie; canal and country bank mania in Britain
- **1810**: British exporters overdid sales to Brazil, then cut off from Baltic outlets by Napoleon's blockade
- **1816/1819**: end of Napoleonic Wars → British exports flooded the Continent like the South Sea/Mississippi bubbles → prices collapsed → goods diverted to North America → tariff of 1816; 1818-19 panics on both sides of the Atlantic. Second Bank of US precipitated panic by calling on state banks to redeem notes — the bank itself was corrupt ("greedy directors, promissory notes, stock registered in different names")

### 1825 to 1896
- **1825**: Anglo-South American crisis; spillover to Paris reached January 1828. London panic → continental sales halted → banks in Paris, Lyons, Leipzig, Vienna impacted. Alsatian textile firms circulated 9-16M francs in promissory notes as money substitute → edifice collapsed when Parisian banks refused to renew Alsatian paper
- **1836-39**: more complex than 1825. Wildcat banking + silver imports in US; new joint-stock banks in Britain. British speculation in cotton/textiles/railroads; American in cotton/land. Started in Britain (1836-37), spread to US, then erupted in Belgium/France/Germany (May 1838), bounced back to Britain and US (1839). Transatlantic financial web: Lyons felt the loss of US silk market immediately; American commission houses in Paris financed through London; Hottinguer bank helped Nicholas Biddle underwrite a cotton corner that strangled spinners in Manchester, Rouen, Alsace
- **1847**: Anglo-French crisis with echoes in British-Indian trade, Amsterdam, Germany, NY. Table 7.1 shows monthly failures across 20+ cities — the shock wave is visible spreading from London (Aug-Nov 1847) to Paris (Jan-Mar 1848) to other centers. A. Schaaffhausen Bank of Cologne failed March 29, 1848 → Prussian government saved it by converting to joint-stock company → precedent for German banking expansion in the 1850s
- **1857**: "the first worldwide crisis" — gold discoveries in California (1849) and Australia (1851) expanded credit bases globally. Railroad + banking booms in both Europe and US. Dominoes started in Ohio → fell in NY, Pennsylvania, Maryland, Rhode Island, Virginia → then Liverpool, London, Paris, Hamburg, Oslo, Stockholm. Clapham: crisis appeared at almost the same moment in US, Britain, Central Europe, South America, South Africa, Far East. Chamber of Commerce of Elberfeld: "The world is a unit; industry and trade have made it so"
- **1866**: tail end of crisis begun in 1864. Black Friday May 11, 1866 tied to Prussian-Austrian war → stock market collapses → Italian *corso forzoso* (suspended convertibility). Overend, Gurney collapse triggered by internal run on notes against gold → capital withdrawals toward Paris. Alfred André: "an exhausting week" — concluded finance companies were ruined and business paralyzed in Italy, Prussia, Austria, Russia
- **1869**: US gold crisis (Jay Gould/Jim Fisk) and Austrian crisis of same month — both countries had floating currencies post-war. Possible link through wheat prices: Gould trying to raise gold premium, which affected wheat competitiveness
- **1873**: Franco-Prussian indemnity (one-tenth paid in gold) → German speculation → Austrian building boom → Jay Cooke overextended in Northern Pacific railroad (couldn't compete with German/Austrian booms for European capital). Crisis erupted May 1873 in Austria/Germany → September in US → Italy, Holland, Belgium → Britain, France, Russia. Rothschild: "the whole world has become a city"
- **1878-1893**: less intimate connections. City of Glasgow (1878), Union Générale (1882), NY stock market (1884), European war scare (1887), copper corner (1888), Baring crisis (1890), Panama scandal (1892), NY panic (1893). The 1890 Baring crisis produced financial stringency in NY as British investors sold US stocks to cover Latin American losses

### 1907 — "The Panic as a World Phenomenon"
- Italy participated in early upswing: speculation fed by credit, fictitious ventures, a steel trust speculating in its own securities, dividends paid from borrowings
- Società Bancaria Italiana: capital rose from 4M lire (1898) → 50M (1906); head office in Milan didn't know risks taken by Genoa branch
- When Paris and London cut off new credits to Italy and the US (spring 1907), the upstart bank was doomed
- Connections were indirect: Italian centers → Paris → London ← NY. Paris cut-off of loans to Italy moderated by emigrant remittances (largely NY to Naples)
- Frank Vanderlip (NY banker): "The Panic as a World Phenomenon" — blamed Boer War, Russo-Japanese War, San Francisco earthquake, overtrading by trust companies

### The International Ramifications of 1929
- Hoover: the real cause was wartime expansion of production outside Europe at 1925 prices → proved excessive when European production recovered
- Additional factors: reparations, war debts, overvalued pound, undervalued franc, Dawes Plan recycling (Americans bought German bonds → Germany paid reparations → Britain/France paid war debts to US)
- NY stock market boom (March 1928) → US purchases of foreign bonds halted → Germany deflated to make reparation payments; Argentina, Australia, Uruguay, Brazil found their balances of payments sharply adverse → currencies depreciated after October 1929 crash → commodity prices (wheat, coffee, rubber, sugar, silk, cotton) fell sharply
- NY Fed open-market program (early 1929) temporarily alleviated credit squeeze; international lending volume in April-June 1930 was the largest of any quarter in the 1920s-30s
- But confidence collapsed: German National Socialist gains (September 1930) → Central European banks tried to improve positions by bidding up their own stocks → two French banks (Banque Adam, Banque Oustric) failed → Credit Anstalt failure (Vienna, May 1931) → Danatbank failure (Germany, July) → German standstill → withdrawals from London (August) → Britain abandoned gold (September 1931)
- Gold bloc (France, Belgium, Netherlands, Switzerland) bought gold with US dollars → drained US bank reserves → Japan went off gold December 1931
- Glass-Steagall Act (February 1932) enabled open-market reflation — too late. Debt-deflation spiral of bank failures continued until the general Bank Holiday (March 1933) and dollar depreciation
- Conclusion: "This history does not lead to the conclusion that the 1930s depression originated in the United States"

### Contagion and the East Asian Crisis (1997-98)
- Proximate origin: Thailand declared inability to service foreign debt, July 2, 1997
- Blame divided between: the countries themselves (Thailand, Indonesia, Malaysia, South Korea) and the foreign lenders/experts who urged deregulation then halted lending abruptly
- East Asian Tigers had grown rapidly in 1980s-90s; herd behavior: borrowed in foreign currencies, speculated in RE, pegged currencies to USD → overvaluation as domestic prices rose
- Thailand lost $24B of reserves defending the baht, then allowed depreciation. Indonesia floated the rupiah quickly. Malaysia's Mahathir blamed George Soros and foreign speculators, imposed limited moratorium on foreign debt repayment
- Hong Kong: lowered construction loan limits from 90% to 60% — so interest rates could rise without imposing large losses on bank mortgages
- Spread to Russia (August 11, 1998 stock market collapse, ruble floated six days later) and Brazil (deregulation, fiscal deficit, lost Asian export markets → real depreciated early 1999) was largely psychological — markets recognized similar vulnerabilities

## Chapter 8: Bubble Contagion — Tokyo to Bangkok to New York (pp 142-?)

### Four Linked Bubbles in Fifteen Years
- **Bubble 1**: Tokyo real estate + stocks (second half of 1980s)
- **Bubble 2**: Nordic countries (Finland, Norway, Sweden) — real estate + stocks (same period)
- **Bubble 3**: Asian Tigers (Bangkok, Kuala Lumpur, Jakarta, Hong Kong) — mid-1990s
- **Bubble 4**: US stocks, especially OTC/NASDAQ (second half of 1990s)
- Asset price bubbles in major industrial countries are rare — previous US bubble was 1920s; Japan had never had one before; six to eight countries simultaneously was "nothing like it had ever happened before"

### The Causal Chain — "Monies Sloshed"
- These four bubbles were systematically related, not coincidental
- **Japan → Nordics**: Japanese banking deregulation relaxed restrictions on foreign activities of Japanese banks AT THE SAME TIME Nordic countries relaxed restrictions on borrowing abroad → surge of loans from Japanese bank offices in London/Zurich to Nordic borrowers → RE + stock price increases
- **Japan → Asian Tigers**: after Tokyo bubble imploded → yen appreciated → Japanese firms outsourced to Southeast Asia → capital outflow from Tokyo to Thailand and neighbors → RE + stock bubbles in Asian countries
- **Asian Tigers → US**: when Asian bubbles burst (1997) → currencies depreciated → trade positions reversed → dramatic increase in fund flows from Asia to US → contributed to US stock price increases. Fed provided extra liquidity in 1998 (LTCM) and 1999 (Y2K) → further fuel
- The money was "like the proverbial 'hot potato,' passed from investor to investor at ever-increasing prices"

### The Chicken-and-Egg of Capital Flows
- Capital inflows → currency appreciation + asset price increases → more capital attracted (positive feedback)
- Higher stock prices → lower cost of capital → more investment → higher GDP → higher profits → higher stock prices
- Rising household wealth → higher consumption → GDP growth → confirms the boom
- Overshooting: currency appreciates beyond what inflation differentials justify → destabilizing

### Momentum Investing and Extrapolation
- One key similarity across all four bubbles: market participants began forecasting prices by extrapolating from recent increases rather than from fundamental earning power
- "Tape watchers" / "momentum investors" / "day traders" — extrapolate recent price changes as forecasts; "it's as if the increases in the prices from Monday to Tuesday are used on Wednesday to forecast the levels of the prices on Friday"
- In Japan and Asia: RE + stocks bubbled together (RE increase "pulled up" stock prices); in the US the bubble was primarily stocks, though RE surged in Silicon Valley and financial centers

### Asset Price Bubbles in Tokyo and Osaka
- By end of 1980s: market value of land under the Imperial Palace > market value of all real estate in California

- California's land area is billions of times larger than Imperial Palace grounds — but per-hectare prices at Ginza rates implied the comparison
- Market value of Japanese stocks = 2x US stocks (though Japan's GDP < half of US GDP)
- Japanese RE value = 2x US RE value (though Japan's land area is 5% of US and 80% mountainous)
- Land value per capita in Japan > 4x the US, despite per capita income being only 60-70% of US
- 7 of 10 largest banks worldwide were Japanese (by assets, not by profits); Nomura's capital exceeded the 5 largest US banks combined

### Japan's Zaibatsu System and Postwar Transformation
- Industrial economy developed around feudal families: Mitsui, Mitsubishi, Sumitomo, Yamoto — each formed a *Zaibatsu* (bank + trading company + steamship company + steel mill under a holding company)
- MacArthur outlawed holding companies → replaced by cross-shareholding (*Keiretsu*): each company in the group owned shares in every other
- Post-WWII: Japan was an economic pariah, exports derided as rip-offs. By 1950s-60s: 10%+ annual growth. Herman Kahn: *Japan: the First Superstate* (1971)
- By 1980s: second industrial power — Toyota, Nissan, Honda led autos; Sony, Matsushita, Sharp led electronics; Nikon, Canon dominated photo-optics
- "Japan Inc." narrative: Ministry of Finance + MITI picked winners/losers, gave them low-cost loans, tariff protection, government procurement

### Japan's Financial Repression and Liberalization
- Ministry of Finance maintained low interest rate ceilings on deposits AND lending rates → real rates of return on deposits were negative → households had to save a high proportion of income just to preserve wealth
- Demand for loans far exceeded supply at below-market rates → government used "window guidance" to direct banks toward favored firms
- Result: negative real returns on deposits/bonds, positive real returns on RE and stocks → rational to chase RE/stocks
- **1980s liberalization** (under US pressure to "open up"): interest rate ceilings raised, window guidance relaxed, restrictions on foreign investment of Japanese firms lifted → Japanese banks opened branches in NY, Chicago, LA, London, Zurich, Frankfurt
- Financial liberalization → banks could increase loans to RE borrowers → RE prices rose 30%/year → "perpetual motion machine": RE prices ↑ → stock prices ↑ → bank capital ↑ → more lending → more RE purchases → RE prices ↑

### The Perpetual Motion Machine in Detail
- Cross-shareholding magnified the impact: industrial firms began borrowing to buy RE and shares in other firms because returns far exceeded manufacturing returns
- Banks established *jusen* (housing loan intermediaries) — seven large banks set them up; then Ministry of Finance created public-sector housing lenders; then banks decided to make housing loans directly → triple competition for mortgage borrowers
- Convertible bonds: firms issued USD-denominated bonds convertible to yen shares at ~2% interest (because investors were so bullish on Japanese stocks the bond was effectively a call option) → firms deposited cash at banks at 4-6% → free money
- Loans collateralized by RE (70% of appraised value); some *jusen* loans went to *yakuza* (organized crime) who secured inflated appraisals. With prices rising 30%/year, "a modest error by an appraiser would soon be corrected by the market"
- Rental yields fell far below interest rates → investors had negative cash flow → but could refinance against rising property values (classic Ponzi finance)
- Foreign investors + global index funds bought Japanese stocks → benefited from both stock appreciation + yen appreciation

### Why and When the Tokyo Bubble Imploded
- End of 1989: RE prices so high that Yogi Berra's quip seemed applicable — "It's so expensive that no one can afford to live there"
- Banks developed 100-year, three-generation mortgages
- New Bank of Japan governor concerned about social harmony → regulation: RE loan growth rate must not exceed total loan growth rate
- Once RE loan growth slowed → recent buyers developed cash binds (rental income < mortgage payments, can no longer refinance) → distress selling → RE prices declined → the cliché "the price of land always rises" was tested and found false
- Stocks declined 30% in 1990, another 30% in 1991; by 2003, stocks at same level as 20 years earlier (real economy much larger)
- The "perpetual motion machine" ran in reverse: RE decline → stock decline → bank capital decline → loan contraction → more RE/stock decline
- Consumer prices began declining 1-2%/year by 1999 → concern about classic debt-deflation
- Global index funds sold Japanese stocks, bought US stocks (value was declining in Tokyo, rising in NY)
- Despite massive bank losses, depositors did NOT withdraw (no formal deposit insurance but implicit guarantee — banks "too big to fail")
- Banks became super-cautious: "For the first time the banks began to ask, 'If we make this loan, what is the likelihood that we will be repaid?'"
- "Japan premium": foreign banks charged Japanese banks higher interest rates → Japanese firms and individuals shifted funds to non-Japanese banks → capital outflow from Japan increased

### The East Asian Economic Miracle and Crisis
- World Bank published *The East Asian Miracle* (1992) — Thailand to South Korea growth comparable to Japan's 1950s-60s
- China opened under Deng Xiaoping (1978): 10%+ annual growth for 20+ years; Hong Kong became entrepôt for Chinese goods
- Thailand, Malaysia, Indonesia: stock prices rose 300-500% in early 1990s; RE prices soared; trade deficits grew
- Bubbles were pervasive despite different economic structures, exchange rate arrangements, creditor/debtor status → strong presumption of common external origin (Japanese capital outflows)
- Japanese firms outsourced manufacturing to Southeast Asia for lower labor costs; direct investment pulled supplier firms and banks from Japan
- Export-led growth: based on undervalued currencies → but as money poured in, currencies appreciated → trade deficits
- **Winter 1996**: Thai consumer finance companies (created by large banks to circumvent lending regulations) experienced large loan losses → foreign lenders became concerned → capital flows declined → Bank of Thailand exhausted reserves defending the baht → early July 1997: baht depreciated sharply
- Contagion: within six months, currencies across the Asian arc (except Chinese yuan and HK dollar) lost 30%+ of value; stock prices fell 30-60%; RE prices collapsed; most banks failed (except Singapore and Hong Kong)
- Indonesia: bank closings triggered racial strife and an immense currency run (70%+ loss of value)
- "The chatter about the East Asian miracle disappeared and new buzzwords arose — crony capitalism, spontaneous privatization, and destabilizing speculation"

### Rational Exuberance and Irrational Exuberance (US 1990s)
- 1982-1999: US stock prices increased 13x — most remarkable run in 200 years of American history
- Market value of US stocks went from 60% of GDP (1982) to 300% of GDP (1999)
- Economy boomed: inflation fell from 6% to <2%; unemployment 8% → <4%; growth 2.5% → 3.5%; productivity surged; fiscal balance swung from -$300B deficit to +$200B surplus
- Trade deficit hit $500B/year; household saving rate hit new lows
- **The "new economy"**: IT, dot.coms, e-commerce, fiber optics — sharp declines in cost of transmitting/storing information. E-bay, Amazon, Peapod, Priceline, Charles Schwab. Day traders quit jobs to trade stocks from home
- **VC → IPO pipeline**: VCs funded ideas → 3-5 year incubation → IPO road-show → investment banks set price and quantity → shares popped 99.46% of the time on first day of trading
- IPO "pop" created its own perpetual motion machine: bigger pop → more investor demand for IPOs → more VCs funding entrepreneurs → more IPOs → investment bankers engaged in "spinning" (allocating IPO shares to heads of major firms to win their future business)
- **Greenspan "irrational exuberance" (Dec 1996)**: Dow 6,300, NASDAQ 1,300. By Dec 1999: Dow 11,700, NASDAQ 5,400. NASDAQ stocks = 80% of market value on NYSE
- **Dating the bubble**: possibly spring 1995 (34% annual stock price increase), or summer 1998 (post-Asian crisis + LTCM). Mexico peso crisis (1994) → trade balance shifted → capital inflow to US → US stock prices rose. After Asian crisis: US trade deficit increased $150B → more capital inflows → Fed eased (LTCM, Y2K) → more fuel
- June 1998 to June 1999: NYSE market value +40% ($9T → $12.7T); NASDAQ +90% ($1.8T → $3.2T)
- 95-97% of foreign capital inflows used to buy other US securities (hot potato), only 3-5% leaked to consumption → but that leakage reduced household saving rate
- Y2K: Fed expanded bank liquidity in 1999 → banks increased loans → more fuel for stock prices
- **January 2000**: Fed withdrew Y2K liquidity → stock prices began to decline. Aggregate decline: 40% (NYSE), 80% (NASDAQ)

### Sloshing Money and Asset Bubbles — Summary
- The sequence Japan → Asia → US was a unique monetary event: three decades of Japanese RE appreciation + financial liberalization + Bank of Japan FX intervention → Tokyo bubble → money sloshed to Thailand/Asia → Asian bubble → money sloshed to US → US stock bubble
- Pattern of Ponzi cash flows in ALL THREE: Japanese RE investors paid interest from new loans; Thai/Asian debtors paid interest from new foreign borrowing; US stock buyers bet on finding greater fools
- "There were many greater fools but not so many that the investors that otherwise would be thought of as being rational and conservative could avoid large losses when stock prices tumbled"

## Chapter 9: Frauds, Swindles, and the Credit Cycle (pp 165-?)

### Fraud Follows the Bubble
- The implosion of an asset price bubble always leads to the discovery of fraud — Enron tumbled into bankruptcy within months of the US stock market peak; MCI-WorldCom overstated investments and understated expenses by $10B (largest bankruptcy ever at the time)
- Junk bond market collapsed after interest rates rose in late 1980s and stocks crashed in October 1987
- "The supply of corruption increases in a procyclical way much like the supply of credit" — when credit contracts, "the fraud sprouts from the woodwork like mushrooms in a soggy forest"

### The Legal Borderline
- Some fraudulent behavior is clearly illegal; other behavior hovers on the borderline
- Should stock options to management be expensed as a cost (like wages) or buried in footnotes so profits appear higher? The answer determines stock price trajectories
- Should Henry Blodgett, Mary Meeker, Jack Grubman (telecom analyst gurus of the 1990s) have been required to explain the rationale for price targets, not just announce them?

### The Poster Children of Financial Excess
- Enron, MCIWorldCom, Adelphia, Tyco, HealthSouth, Global Crossing — all 1990s bubble frauds
- Fraudulent behavior occurred DURING the mania but was obscured by rising prices; high-risk borrowers could refinance because lenders were eager
- Investment banks operated on *caveat emptor*: "customers old enough to vote presumably were capable of looking out for their own financial interests"
- CPA firms (Arthur Andersen, Big Five) were supposed to verify corporate accounts — but some were "captured" by the firms they audited, colluding in deception

### Inventory Frauds — A Long Tradition
- McKesson Robbins (1930s): forged warehouse receipts as loan collateral
- Billie Sol Estes (1960s): falsified fertilizer tank counts, borrowed against fictitious tanks
- Tino De Angelis (1960s): American Express — used tanks of "salad oil" as collateral; knew oil was less dense than water, floated a 6-inch layer of salad oil on top of 20 feet of water

### Wall Street Analysts as "Shills"
- "They're like the shills in front of the side-shows at carnivals whose role is to persuade the public to buy tickets to see the sword swallowers and the hermaphrodites"
- Stocks increase twice as often as they fall → market strategists will be "right twice as often as they are wrong" without any special skill
- Analysts rarely suggest individual stock prices will decline — the firm's CEO would "threaten never to bring any underwriting business to the investment bank ever again"

### Corruption Is Procyclical
- Corrupt behavior increases when asset prices rise 30-40%/year: "Some individuals wish to keep up with the Joneses, and they may blur the truth and cut a few corners"
- Reward/risk calculation shifts: the potential gain from fraud seems large relative to the risk of being caught; "the prisons for white-collar crime are like modest country clubs with drab clothing"
- Crash and panic induce cheating to forestall bankruptcy — "a little cheating today may avert catastrophe tomorrow"; when losses mount, there's a tendency to "make a big bet in the hope that a successful outcome will enable escape"

### Nick Leeson and Rogue Traders
- Leeson: one of five or six employees in Barings' Singapore office, traded Nikkei options. Made an error → sold put options to cover the loss → Kobe earthquake crashed Tokyo stocks → loss on options far exceeded premium income → doubled down → lost again → repeated until losses equaled all of Barings' capital
- Probability of 4-5 consecutive losing bets: ~1 in 32 (coin flip). "If he had won just one of the bets, his misadventure would not have made the front pages"
- **Other rogue traders**: John Rusnak (Allied Irish Bank, Baltimore) — $750M FX losses; Mr. Hamanaka (Sumitomo Bank, NY) — billions in copper market; five traders at National Australian Bank — hundreds of millions in FX
- Common pattern: extended series of losing bets, each doubling down; discovered only when they can't pay

### BCCI — One of the Largest-Ever Ponzi Schemes
- Bank of Credit and Commerce International: chartered in Pakistan, expanded through Middle East, London, Europe, Africa
- Prospered from 1970s oil price surges; early depositors were Persian Gulf sheiks; paid high deposit rates
- Made large political loans to rulers (easing regulatory problems); refinanced nonperforming loans to delay recognizing losses; bribed auditors; valued "in the money" options at market and "out of the money" at zero
- "Continued to expand as long as it could continue to sell its deposits to its 'captive clientele'"

### Japan's "Evergreen Finance" and Crony Capitalism
- Japanese tradition: loans would be refinanced rather than repaid — borrowers used cash from new loans to pay interest on outstanding loans; loan amounts increased at the interest rate
- "Evergreen finance" was safe as long as RE prices rose 3-4x faster than interest rates
- When RE prices declined: fraud surfaced — a woman with a small Osaka restaurant had borrowed billions from Sumitomo Bank via a "friendly relationship" with the local banker; *yakuza* had secured inflated RE appraisals; Ministry of Finance officials entertained at "pantyless shabu-shabu restaurant" in exchange for advance regulatory information
- Thailand/Malaysia/Indonesia: "crony capitalism suddenly appeared — favored treatments for certain borrowers." Indonesia was a "Suharto family business" for 30+ years; banks happily lent to the president's children's enterprises

### The US Savings & Loan Debacle
- Thrifts: funded short-term deposits → invested in long-term fixed-rate mortgages → "transformation risk"
- For 50 years this worked because government-regulated deposit rate ceilings prevented competition
- Late 1970s: market interest rates surged → depositors withdrew to buy Treasuries/money market funds → thrifts caught between rock (raise deposit rates → negative spread → slow death) and hard place (sell mortgages at a loss → immediate capital hit)
- "Most thrifts decided to pay the higher interest rates on deposits; slow death was preferable to sudden death"
- Hundreds then thousands failed; FSLIC and FDIC reserves exhausted
- **"Forbearance" policy**: regulators lacked funds to close failed thrifts → allowed bankrupt institutions to continue operating → losses compounded
- 1982: Congress deregulated thrifts — allowed them to buy almost any security; raised insured deposit ceiling from $40,000 to $100,000
- Failed thrifts became "natural markets" for junk bonds; Milken's friends took control of thrifts and insurance companies to create captive buyers for Drexel's junk bonds
- Merrill Lynch brokered deposits nationwide to thrifts offering highest rates — government-guaranteed deposits were "all that the buyers wanted to know"
- Corporate raiders financed by Milken "used Uncle Sam's money — money obtained from the sale of deposits guaranteed by the U.S. government — to acquire more than fifty firms"; overpaid, "paying with Sam's money rather than their own"
- Firms couldn't earn enough to pay junk bond interest → issued new securities to pay interest on old ones → "a money machine that worked as long as the junk bond market remained vibrant"
- Late 1980s: regulators changed rules, thrifts couldn't buy junk bonds → market collapsed → Drexel bankrupt (1992)
- **Total cost**: ~$150 billion to the federal government; had thrifts been closed in early 1980s when capital was depleted, cost would have been $20-30B
- "Milken and his family became billionaires and he probably remained one even after paying a fine or penalty of $550 million and spending thirty months in a federal country club"

### Enron — The Full Story
- Born from merger of two regulated natural gas pipelines; "Enron Heavy" (India power plant, Mexico water), "Enron Light" (electricity/gas/broadband trading)
- Peak market cap $250B, stock $100/share, bonds $40B
- Compensation tied to stock options → powerful incentive to inflate profits → "smoothing" earnings to match Wall Street analyst quarterly predictions to the penny
- **Sale-and-leaseback schemes**: Merrill Lynch and JP Morgan paid above-market prices for Nigerian barges so Enron could book a profit; banks charged correspondingly higher lease rates (borrowing from next year's profits to inflate this year's)
- Esoteric futures contracts: valued at high prices with no ready market reference — "a sort of 1990s counterpart to Billie Sol Estes"
- **Off-balance sheet financing (SFVs)**: partnerships named from *Star Wars* (JEDI, Chewco) — 97% owned by Enron, 3% by "unrelated individuals" (actually senior employees and bankers). SFVs borrowed from banks, invested in Enron, supported stock price — "more or less a Nick Leeson go-for-broke strategy"
- Arthur Andersen: $2M/year auditing fees + $25M/year consulting → "Andersen's desire to retain the consulting income clouded its judgment"; convicted of obstruction of justice (document shredding) June 2002, folded
- 30+ Enron officials charged; Ken Lay, Jeff Skilling (35 violations), Andrew Fastow (pled guilty, 10 years minimum)

### WorldCom / MCIWorldCom
- Bernie Ebbers (former high school history teacher): 60+ acquisitions; EPS grew because WorldCom's P/E was higher than acquired firms' P/E → buying other companies' earnings, not growing organically
- "Riding the proverbial tiger" — had to keep acquiring larger firms to maintain earnings growth rate; supply of attractive targets was declining
- Last acquisition: MCI (broke AT&T's long-distance monopoly via microwave towers) → attempted Sprint merger blocked by regulators → filed bankruptcy (largest in US history)
- Fudged $4.8B of expenses as "investments" → eventually $10B total; Ebbers got $400M+ in off-the-books loans from MCI, used WorldCom stock as collateral + bought more WorldCom stock with the loan

### Tyco, Adelphia, HealthSouth, ImClone, Others
- **Tyco** ($200B conglomerate): Dennis Kozlowski — self-awarded options, used corporate treasury for personal expenses ($6,000 shower curtain, $2M birthday party with Michelangelo's *David* ice sculpture dispensing vodka); acquired hundreds of firms, massaged earnings
- **Adelphia** (6th largest US cable): Rigas family collected $3B+ in off-balance sheet loans; inflated capital expenses; used corporate borrowings to support stock price
- **HealthSouth**: $3B accounting fraud; Richard Scrushy claimed subordinates committed the fraud
- **ImClone**: Sam Waksal (founder) tipped family to sell before adverse FDA announcement → Martha Stewart's obstruction of justice conviction was related
- **Richard Grasso** (NYSE chairman): $150M retirement package → suspicion of soft regulation in exchange for generous compensation set by the directors he was supposed to regulate
- **Global Crossing**: swapped network capacity with other carriers to inflate revenues; shredded documents; Gary Winnick received $800M from stock sales before bankruptcy
- **Shell Oil**: $150M fine for overstating oil reserves
- **Parmalat** (Italian dairy): fabricated $4B in certificates of deposit by superimposing documents on a copying machine — deception lasted 10+ years

### Mutual Fund Scandals (2003)
- Half of the 20 largest US mutual fund families gave hedge funds preferential trading privileges
- Hedge funds traded on "stale prices" of foreign securities (markets closed in different time zones) — "like shooting fish in the proverbial barrel"
- Mutual funds informed hedge funds about stocks being bought/sold during quarters; some fund managers front-ran their own funds; some traded their own fund shares on an in-and-out basis
- **Quid pro quo**: hedge funds bought shares in underperforming mutual funds in exchange for trading privileges
- Canary Capital: $30M restitution; Massachusetts Financial Services: $200M settlement; Alliance Capital: $250M penalty + $350M fee cuts; Morgan Stanley: instructed brokers to push proprietary funds (payola)

### The Celebrity Analysts: Blodgett, Meeker, Grubman
- Earned $10-20M/year in late 1990s as "apostles of the dot.coms"
- Blodgett (Merrill Lynch): set price targets for dot.com stocks — in internal emails derided the very stocks he promoted publicly
- Grubman (Salomon Smith Barney/Citigroup): changed AT&T recommendation after boss Sandy Weill asked; quid pro quo involved Citigroup donating $1M to the 92nd Street Y to get Grubman's twins into kindergarten. Paid $20M penalty, left the business

### Ponzi Finance and Its Theorists
- Many swindles involve "Ponzi finance" (Minsky's term): interest payments exceed cash flows from operations → must borrow more or sell assets → unsustainable
- "Most of the entrepreneurs who establish a Ponzi finance operation appear to know and understand what they are doing; they live high for a few months or years before the authorities catch up with them"

### Boiler Shops and Swindle Mechanics
- "Boiler shops" (named for dingy basement offices near boiler rooms): high-pressure phone sales operations pushing worthless securities
- Fly-by-night firms would sell subscriptions to nonexistent companies, pocket the money, dissolve, and reform under new names
- Scale ranged from petty fraud to massive operations; the swindlers understood that "the easiest time to attract investors is when prices of real securities have been rising rapidly"

### Swindles vs Bribery
- Daniel Defoe: "There are Two Classes of People, those who are cheated and those who do the Cheating"
- Bribery of officials is a separate category — political corruption that enables or facilitates financial fraud
- The relationship between financial bubbles and political corruption is procyclical: rising asset prices generate the excess cash that funds bribes and campaign contributions

### Fraud and Euphoria — Overend Gurney
- Overend Gurney & Co. collapsed in 1866 — the "Quaker bank" had been one of the most respected discount houses in the City of London
- Moved from safe bill discounting into speculative finance, RE, railways — accumulated massive unrealizable assets
- When directors realized the extent of losses, they converted from partnership to limited liability company (selling shares to the public to recapitalize) — "the public was being asked to buy shares in a bankrupt firm"
- Collapse triggered the worst panic in 19th century London; Bagehot wrote *Lombard Street* partly in response

### Literary Parallels
- Dickens (Nicholas Nickleby, Little Dorrit, Martin Chuzzlewit): company promoters as villains; Mr. Merdle in *Little Dorrit* is a financier whose collapse destroys investors — "half-credit, half-loss" world of speculation
- Balzac (*Melmoth réconcilié*): a bank cashier sells his soul to the devil in exchange for the power to make money on the Bourse; the Faustian bargain as metaphor for speculative excess
- Zola (*L'Argent*): roman à clef based on the Union Générale crash of 1882; Aristide Saccard character — a plunger who builds a bank to rival the Rothschilds, rises spectacularly, crashes

### Bubbles and Swindles — Mississippi vs South Sea
- John Law's Mississippi Company (1719-20): NOT a swindle in Kindleberger's view — Law genuinely believed his monetary theories would work; the scheme was a sincere (if misguided) experiment in managed currency and trade
- South Sea Company (1720): WAS a swindle — directors knew the scheme was fraudulent, sold their own shares near the top, manipulated the share price through staged installment purchases and fictitious transactions
- Both happened simultaneously (1719-20) and were financially interconnected through capital flows between London and Paris

### Charles Ponzi and the Original Scheme
- Ponzi's scheme (1920): promised 40% return in 45 days (later 50% in 90 days) — claimed profits from arbitraging International Postal Reply Coupons across countries with different exchange rates
- At peak: taking in $1M/day in 1920 dollars from thousands of Boston-area investors
- When arrested: had taken in $7.9M total but held only $61 in postal reply stamps — the supposed "investment vehicle"
- Classic pyramid: early investors paid from late investors' money; worked because "word of mouth is the most powerful form of marketing"

### Spitzeder — The Munich Ponzi
- Adele Spitzeder: Munich actress in the 1860s who opened a "bank" paying 20% monthly interest to Bavarian farmers
- Thrived on word-of-mouth from small-town depositors; became enormously popular in rural Bavaria
- Collapsed when withdrawals exceeded new deposits; Spitzeder jailed
- Kindleberger uses this as evidence that Ponzi-type frauds recur independently across centuries and cultures

### 1720 Gossamer Bubble Schemes
- During the South Sea mania, dozens of schemes were floated for absurd purposes: "For an Undertaking which shall in due Time be reveal'd" — collected £2,000 in subscriptions in a single morning; the promoter "was philosopher enough to be off to the Continent that evening"
- Other schemes: extracting silver from lead, perpetual motion machines, trading in hair, insuring marriages against divorce

### Noble Gamblers and Aristocratic Speculation
- Zola's Aristide Saccard: modeled on Paul Eugène Bontoux (Union Générale, 1878-82) who organized Catholic/monarchist investors against the Rothschilds/Republicans
- The social dimension: speculation attracted aristocrats who needed income, military officers on half-pay, clergy with modest stipends — people seeking to restore pre-revolutionary fortunes
- "The noble gambler believed his status entitled him to special returns"

### Venal Journalism
- Daniel Defoe: the first great financial journalist — also a speculator and pamphleteer
- Jacques Laffitte (French banker): financed newspapers to promote his own financial interests
- Gerson Bleichröder (Bismarck's banker): paid journalist Paul Lindau to write favorably about Mexican railroad bonds that Bleichröder was underwriting
- Theodore Dreiser (novelist): *The Financier*, *The Titan*, *The Stoic* — based on Charles Tyson Yerkes, the Chicago streetcar tycoon; detailed the interplay between finance, politics, and media

### Dubious Practices — George Hudson and Robert Schuyler
- **George Hudson** ("The Railway King"): greatest figure in British railroad history; practiced every variety of fraud simultaneously — paid dividends from capital, issued shares without authorization, manipulated accounts, bribed Parliament members for railroad concessions. When exposed: "practiced every type of fraud that 19th century finance offered — simultaneously"
- **Robert Schuyler** ("The Genteel Swindler"): president of the New York & New Haven Railroad; issued $2M in fraudulent shares; when discovered, absconded to Europe where he lived comfortably. "The swindler of breeding and education"

### 1920s Swindlers
- Harold Russell Snyder: pyramid scheme using US Senate connections
- Arthur Montgomery: sold nonexistent oil leases in Oklahoma
- Charles V. Bob: forged Texas bank documents
- **Ivar Kreuger** ("The Match King"): built a global match monopoly; lent $125M to France in exchange for monopoly; forged Italian government bonds to use as collateral. When the scheme unraveled: committed suicide in Paris (1932). "The most creative financial mind of the 1920s"
- **Samuel Insull**: Chicago utility empire; holding companies controlling holding companies → 5 layers of leverage; collapse wiped out 600,000 shareholders. Fled to Greece, extradited, acquitted at trial

### 1920s vs 1990s Corruption Comparison
- Kindleberger argues MORE corruption in the 1980s-90s than the 1920s, for four reasons:
  1. **Decline in moral norms**: "It is hard to prove, but the American business community, and especially Wall Street, became less ethical"
  2. **Skewed incentives**: stock options → executives enriched when stock price rose regardless of reason; incentive to manipulate earnings, inflate forecasts, repress bad news
  3. **Democratization of finance**: millions more participants → more potential victims; mutual fund expansion brought naïve investors into contact with sophisticated manipulators
  4. **CPA capture**: accounting firms became consulting firms (Arthur Andersen earned more from consulting than auditing) → auditors reluctant to challenge clients who paid consulting fees

### The Temptation of Banks
- Gibbons (1859): "There is perhaps no record of a bank fraud extant of which the perpetrator was not honest yesterday"
- Hedge fund term for a grand scam is "a Madison Avenue home run"
- **Chinese wall erosion**: Glass-Steagall repeal (1999) reunited commercial and investment banking → investment banks' research departments pressured by underwriting departments
- Merrill Lynch "scorecard": investment banking department rated research analysts on how many underwriting deals their recommendations generated
- Citigroup "scorecard": similar; Sandy Weill personally pressured Jack Grubman's AT&T rating

### Wages of Sin
- **Suicides**: Charles Blunt (South Sea director, 1720 — slit his throat); Charles Bouchard (Geneva, 1977 — shot himself after losses); various Parisian financiers in the Denfert-Rocherau crashes (1888); Ivar Kreuger (Paris, 1932); one Enron official
- **Flights**: Robert Knight (South Sea cashier — fled to Paris); Robert Vesco (IOS scandal — fled to Costa Rica, then Cuba, where Castro imprisoned him); Arend Joseph (1763 — fled Amsterdam to Kruilenburg castle with 600,000 guilders)
- **Jail time comparison**: 1920s penalties were light; 1980s slightly heavier; 1990s → Michael Milken: fined $550M, served ~1,000 days. "He was paid $100,000 for each day in the jail"
- **Molesworth's proposal** (quoting ancient Rome): swindlers should be "sewn into sacks with a monkey and a snake and thrown into the river" — Kindleberger notes this with dark humor

## Chapter 10: Policy Responses: Letting It Burn Out, and Other Devices (pp 203-?)

### The Core Dilemma
- After a crash or panic, policymakers face a choice: intervene (provide liquidity, bail out institutions) or let the fire burn itself out
- This chapter examines the "let it burn" philosophy and its alternatives

### Benign Neglect — The Case Against Intervention
- Herbert Spencer: the classical liberal view — interference with natural market processes is harmful; "the fire can be left to burn itself out"
- Moral hazard argument: if participants know they'll be rescued, they take greater risks → "intervention skews the risk and reward trade-off toward more risk-taking"
- Each bailout creates expectations of future bailouts → progressively larger bubbles → progressively larger crashes
- "The discipline of the market economy will be weakened if there is a widespread belief that a lender of last resort will bail out those who get into trouble"

### Mellon's Liquidationism
- Andrew Mellon (Hoover's Treasury Secretary): "Liquidate labor, liquidate stocks, liquidate the farmers, liquidate real estate... It will purge the rottenness out of the system. High costs of living and high living will come down. People will work harder, live a more moral life. Values will be adjusted, and enterprising people will pick up the wrecks from less competent people"
- Hoover himself thought this was too harsh — but Mellon represented a genuine intellectual tradition that saw depressions as necessary purgatives
- The opposing view: a deflationary spiral, once started, can "spread and wipe out sound investments by the nonspeculators" — innocent bystanders destroyed alongside the guilty

### Liquidity Crises and Interest Rate Irrelevance
- In a true liquidity crisis, interest rates are misleading — quoted as percentages per annum but actually applying to one-, two-, or three-day loans; the real question is whether money is available at ANY price
- Evidence from multiple crises that borrowing becomes simply impossible:
  - **1763**: "Panic even on securities and on goods, no money was to be had"
  - **1825**: "A panic seized upon the public, such as had never been witnessed before: everybody begging for money—money—but money was hardly on any condition to be had"
  - **1847**: Thomas Tooke testified to Parliament that for several days the Bank of England was the ONLY institution discounting paper; the Governor said he could not sell £1M of government bonds without a massive price decline
  - **1857**: "Impossible to negotiate paper at all, the charge under the most favorable circumstances being 12 and 15 percent." Liverpool bills "of the first Quality" were "absolutely inconvertible into Cash"
  - **Hamburg 1857**: "Commercial confidence is entirely at an end. Bills of only three or four of the first houses are negotiable at the highest rate of interest." A government bond advance of 15 million marks banco payable by banks failed to help — "The panic was so great that government bonds could not be discounted, and on no security whatsoever would capitalists part with their money." But when it became known on Dec 12 that assistance would help all, "the panic ceased"
  - **1866**: Bank rate raised to 9%, loans on government securities available at 10% — before that, "impossible to sell either Consols or Exchequer bills"
  - **1873**: National Trust Company of New York had $800,000 in government securities "but not a dollar could be borrowed on them; and it suspended"
  - **1883**: "Money famine. Time loans were unobtainable, call loans were 72 percent in June, 72 percent on July 28th, 51 percent on August 4"
- Counter-evidence from 1884: high interest rates (4% for 24 hours) brought in foreign capital → turned the foreign exchanges → helped resolve the crisis. But this was BEFORE the acute liquidity shortage developed

### The IMF and the Asian Crisis (1997)
- IMF acted as lender of last resort — but belatedly, after currencies had already depreciated sharply
- Insisted on budget balance + interest rate increases → economists objected these would be deflationary and hurt the poor, while the problems were caused by "comfortable officials and well-to-do bankers"
- Japan's experience supported orthodoxy in one way: expansive monetary policy + yen depreciation → "liquidity trap" — banks reluctant to lend (loan losses eroded capital), firms reluctant to borrow (sluggish demand)
- Japanese near-zero interest rates → **carry trade**: US hedge funds borrowed yen cheaply, bought US dollar assets at 3-4% → capital flow from Tokyo to New York → yen depreciation → larger Japanese trade surplus → useful supplement to Japan's fiscal/monetary expansion

### Moral Suasion and Other Exhortatory Devices
- The dominant argument against "let it burn": panics are almost never left alone in practice — "In panic after panic, crash after crash, crisis after crisis, the authorities or some 'responsible citizens' try to halt the panic by one device or another"
- The Chicago School assumes market participants are always smarter than authorities — but both authorities and leading market figures exert themselves to halt falling prices, so the hypothesis is untestable
- "If there is a learning process at work — and the assumption of rationality requires one — the lesson has been that a lender of last resort is more desirable and less costly than relying exclusively on the competitive forces of the market"
- Historical pattern: authorities initially resolve NOT to intervene, then reluctantly do:
  - Lord Liverpool threatened to resign as Chancellor of the Exchequer (1825) if Exchequer bills were issued to rescue markets
  - William Lidderdale (Bank of England Governor, Baring crisis) refused a "letter of indemnity" to exceed the Bank's lending limit
  - Both occasions: "The initial strong moral stand not to intervene was reversed on many other occasions as the panic escalated"

### Stalling and Bank Holidays
- **Slow-pay tactics**: banks stall depositors during runs, hoping "like Micawber, for something to turn up"
- **Bank of England, 1720**: during South Sea run, organized friends at front of line, paid them slowly in sixpence coins; friends brought cash back through another door, redeposited, paid out again — staved off the run until Michaelmas holidays (Sept 29); when holidays ended, "so was the run, and the Bank remained open"
- **Sword Blade Bank, 1720**: similar tactic — brought up wagonloads of silver, paid out "slowly in small change"; one depositor received £8,000 in shillings and sixpences
- **Young Pretender crisis, 1745**: Bonnie Prince Charlie invaded England; panic on Black Friday (Dec 5, 1745); Bank of England paid in sixpence coins + collected 1,140 signatures of London merchants pledging loyalty and readiness to accept Bank notes → gained time for government to organize the army that defeated the Young Pretender at Culloden (April 1746)

### Complete Shutdown and Bank Holidays
- NYSE closed in 1873 (panic) and 1914 (outbreak of war); NYSE closed for a week after 9/11 (2001) due to inoperative communications
- Legal holidays declared during 1907 panic (Oklahoma, Nevada, Washington, Oregon, California) — forerunners of the bank holidays that started locally in fall 1932 and went national on March 3, 1933 (FDR's inauguration)
- **Risk**: shutdowns may drive trading underground and intensify panic; fear of future shutdowns accelerates withdrawals — in October 1929, fear that trading might be halted hastened withdrawal of call money by out-of-town banks
- **Suspending bank statements** (1873): "What you don't know won't hurt you" — hide large reserve losses to preserve confidence
- **Circuit breakers**: daily price limits; NYSE proposal after Black Monday (Oct 19, 1987) to halt trading for 20 minutes when order imbalance too large
- **Moratoriums**: on debts, bills of exchange; regulatory forbearance (ignoring bad loans in bank portfolios); IMF/World Bank allowed African countries' debt to compound rather than declare defaults; REIT lenders, mothballed Boeing 747 owners let interest compound to delay recognizing losses

### Clearinghouse Certificates
- Pre-Federal Reserve US device: banks issued clearinghouse certificates (near-money backed by a group of large banks) to pay departing depositors instead of coin
- NY clearinghouse established 1853; Philadelphia 1858
- 1857: NY banks failed to cooperate; Mercantile Agency argued that if 4-5 strong banks had assisted Ohio Life and Trust, "the business and credit of the country would have been preserved"
- 1873: NY banks accepted clearinghouse certificates for settled checks → reduced incentive for competitive deposit-poaching; reserves pooled
- 1907: 60 of 160 US clearinghouses adopted certificates
- **Drawback**: only acceptable locally (NY, Philadelphia, Baltimore) — maintained local payments (payrolls, retail) but "broken down domestic exchange and paralyzed to a large extent the business of the country" (Jacob Schiff, December 1907)

### Stock Market Consortiums and Bank Collaboration
- **October 24, 1907**: bankers' pool headed by J.P. Morgan loaned $25M at 10% in call money to stem stock market collapse
- **Black Thursday, 1929**: Richard Whitney walked the NYSE floor placing bids on behalf of Morgan syndicate — exactly 22 years later
- **Rescue committees**: Vienna (May 1873), Paris banks for Alsace (1828), Hamburg (1857), Baring Brothers guarantee (1890)

### The Alsatian Crisis of 1828 — A Case Study
- Three textile firms failed at Mulhouse (December 1827); Paris banks refused all Alsatian paper; Bank of France set limit of 6M francs — "scarcely the fortune of two Alsatian houses"
- Textile merchants Koechlin and Dollfuss traveled to Paris to raise cash; sold inventories at 30-40% discounts; nine houses failed January 26 to February 15
- Last-minute rescue: syndicate of 26 Paris banks (led by J.-C. Davillier) extended 5M franc credit to Koechlin and Dollfuss → they distributed 1M francs to colleagues who offered guarantees, kept 4M for themselves → confidence restored

### The Hamburg Crisis of 1857 — Day by Day
- Hamburg: the "all-English" city, close trade relations with US (sugar, tobacco, coffee, cotton); Crimean War had expanded credit
- Ohio Life failure (August 24) arrived in Hamburg three months later with 30% price declines and the suspension of Winterhoff and Piper
- Daily dispatches from British consulate:
  - **Nov 21**: leading houses and two banks plan relief
  - **Nov 23**: two major London-trade houses fail; Discount Guarantee Association grows cautious; exhausted in three days
  - **Nov 24**: Garantie-Diskontverein formed with 10M marks banco capital (later raised to 13M)
  - **Nov 28**: chamber of commerce and merchants push senate to issue government bonds to lend 50-66⅔% against hypothecated goods
  - **Dec 1**: Ullberg and Cremer suspended; ten to twelve Swedish trade houses gone; Guarantee Association at standstill
  - **Dec 2**: proposal to change bankruptcy laws for creditor attachment
  - **Dec 7**: proposal for 30M marks banco state bank (rejected by parliament; senate insists on silver standard)
- Resolution: State Loan Institute of 15M marks (5M in Hamburg government bonds + 10M silver borrowed abroad) — the Silberzug (silver train, Chapter 12)
- Total rescue funds: 35M marks banco vs 100M marks in protested bills — sufficient because merchants speculated with capital equal to only ⅙ of goods value; a 17% price decline would wipe them out
- Senate response to criticism it was "300 years behind the times": "The merchants have been 300 years ahead of the times in issuing debt. State help merely means assistance for speculation and perpetuation of higher prices at the cost of the consumer"

### Guarantees of Liabilities — The Baring Crisis (1890)
- **Esdailes, Grenfell, Thomas and Company (1836)**: London agents for 72 country banks; "too big to fail" — paper included all the best names in the City; Bank of England led guarantee list with £150,000; Esdailes survived only two more years
- **Baring Brothers (1890)**: William Lidderdale (Bank of England Governor) — refused the usual remedies (letter of indemnity to exceed lending limit)
  - August 1890: warned Barings to moderate Argentine acceptances
  - November 8 (Saturday): Barings revealed acute distress
  - November 10 (Monday): Bank of England met Exchequer, turned down letter of indemnity, formed committee headed by Lord Rothschild for Argentine securities overhang
  - Lidderdale learned Barings needed £8-9M; obtained government agreement to share losses on Barings paper discounted Friday-Saturday
  - Met 11 private banks → guarantee fund of £3.25M (including £1M from Bank of England, £500K each from Glyn, Mills, Currie, Rothschilds)
  - Got State Bank of Russia to agree not to withdraw its £2.4M deposit at Barings
  - Used private bank commitments to obtain £3.25M more from five London joint-stock banks
  - **November 15**: *The Times* announced Barings would fail but there would be no loss; guarantee fund continued growing — from £7.5M Saturday morning to £10M by 4pm; eventually reached £17M
  - Martin's Bank (in distress over Barings + Murrietta loans) joined guarantee fund for £100,000 on November 18 — "too late to afford much help to Barings, but early enough to demonstrate to the world its own strength"
  - Powell: "The Bank is not a single combatant who must fight or retire, but the leader of the most colossal agglomeration of financial power which the world has so far witnessed"
  - November 25: new Baring Brothers and Co., Ltd formed with £1M capital
  - Guarantee fund text: each guarantor liable ratably, maximum period three years

### LTCM Rescue (1998) — Contrast to Barings
- William McDonough (NY Fed president) induced 14 large banks + investment banks (Merrill Lynch, Morgan Stanley Dean Witter, J.P. Morgan, Chase Manhattan, UBS) to provide $3.6B capital, acquiring 90% of LTCM equity
- Key difference: the rescuing banks were LTCM's major creditors — "bailout involved a change in the legal nature of their claim"; they rescued themselves as much as LTCM
- Fed feared LTCM's massive derivatives positions would paralyze markets if unwound chaotically

### Deposit Insurance
- FDIC (since 1934): originally $10,000 ceiling, gradually raised to $100,000
- Ceiling increase was a political compromise ($50K House proposal + $60K Senate → $100K)
- Continental Illinois (1984) and First Republic of Dallas (1988): FDIC removed all deposit limits → established "too big to fail" doctrine; foreign branch deposits implicitly insured even though only domestic deposits paid premiums
- **Deposit brokers**: arranged $100K parcels across multiple accounts/banks to maximize insurance → wealthy individuals fully insured despite the "little man" ceiling
- **Moral hazard**: insured deposits removed the major check on banking irresponsibility — banks could take risks knowing depositors wouldn't run; if risky loans profited, owners benefited; if they defaulted, FDIC absorbed losses
- Japan 1990s: no formal deposit insurance but implicit guarantee — no bank runs despite massive bank losses; depositors confident government would make them whole
- **Pre-1933 resistance**: in the wildcat banking era, deposit guarantees were rejected as "a license to speculate, if not embezzle" — even on March 2, 1933 (eve of bank holiday), Fed Board of Governors refused to recommend guarantees
- **FDIC track record**: excellent through 1970s (premiums far exceeded payouts; 1934-1970 only one bank >$50M deposits failed)
- **Late 1970s-1990s**: FDIC + FSLIC overwhelmed; rescued Continental Illinois, First Republic of Dallas at billions in cost; usual procedure: close failed bank → carve "good bank" → sell bad assets through Resolution Trust Corporation (RTC)
- Total S&L cost: estimated $150B but RTC recovered more than expected from asset sales → actual cost ~$100B

### Exchequer Bills — An Ancient Device
- Government issues marketable securities to distressed firms against collateral — the British Exchequer bill
- **1793**: after 100 of 300 country banks failed + commodity price collapse, City leaders met PM William Pitt; parliament authorized £5M in Exchequer bills against goods deposited in customs houses; also lowered minimum note from £10 to £5 to economize gold/silver
- 338 firms applied for only £3M; £2.2M granted to 228 firms; only two subsequently went bankrupt; applications for £1.2M+ were withdrawn after panic abated — "the device worked like a charm"
- **1799**: Liverpool echo of Hamburg panic — Parliament provided £500K in Exchequer bills against £2M of warehoused goods
- **1811**: Select Committee on Commercial Credit recommended £6M in Exchequer bills for distressed West Indies/South America exporters; opposition argued relief would "aggravate the evil" by funding further speculation — Huskisson: "persons of notoriously small capital had now eclipsed those of the greatest consequence; so that speculations now took place even in the lowest articles of commerce"
- In the end only £2M advanced; few applications made because "the radical evil was that there was no market for [the goods]"
- **1825**: Exchequer bills "sternly rejected" — the political mood had shifted against bailouts

### Bank Regulation and Supervision
- Can stricter regulation prevent crises? Kindleberger is skeptical:
  - Rules for sound banking already exist in regulations and tradition — but "many of the rules are ignored by banks and regulators alike"
  - Mark-to-market requirements routinely suspended; reserves against problem loans delayed
  - Citicorp (1987): wrote down Third World debt while FSLIC allowed 500 insolvent banks to remain open hoping they'd rebuild capital
  - Capital requirements as % of assets → banks shifted to "off-balance sheet" operations (swaps, futures, options, repos, note-issuing facilities) — generating fees without showing on the balance sheet
- **Divided responsibility**: Comptroller of the Currency, Federal Reserve Banks, state banking commissioners — "Competition not in deregulation but in reregulation"; "Divided responsibility is no responsibility" (German banker-politician)
- The astute personnel needed in emergencies are unwilling to endure the boredom of long calm periods; "mismanagement of banks is hard to detect before a crisis"
- "In boom, entropy in regulation and supervision builds up danger spots that burst into view when the boom subsides"

### Luck: A Tailpiece
- Brothers Kauffmann in Hamburg were failing during the 1799 crisis; one brother's bride received a Hamburg city lottery ticket (first prize: 100K marks banco); she bought the same number in a Duchy of Mecklenburg lottery (prize: estate worth 50K Prussian thalers = 100K marks banco); she won BOTH → Kauffmanns fully rehabilitated
- "The odds at Las Vegas may be higher for the solution to a global crisis"

## Chapter 11: The Domestic Lender of Last Resort (pp 225-?)

### The Concept
- The hallmark of "the Art of Central Banking" over two centuries: the evolution of the lender of last resort
- From French *dernier ressort* (last legal jurisdiction for appeal) — in central banking, emphasis shifts to the LENDER's responsibilities, not the borrower's rights
- The lender of last resort "stands ready to halt a run out of real assets and illiquid financial assets into money by supplying as much money as may be necessary to forestall the run" — an "elastic supply of money" that expands to meet panic demand
- Key questions: How much money? To whom? On what terms? When?

### The Moral Hazard Dilemma
- If investors believe they'll be rescued, they take greater risks → weaker lending discipline during booms
- But if a panic rush to liquidate cannot be halted, the "fallacy of composition" takes over: each individual selling to minimize losses → collective selling drives prices down → solvent firms become insolvent
- "The case for the opposition to a lender of last resort has been made continuously": Napoleon's treasury minister François Nicholas Mollien wrote against interventionism

### The Case Against Intervention — Key Voices
- Napoleon's treasury minister François Nicholas Mollien: opposed saving failing manufacturers damaged by the Continental System blockade — "a start in this direction would only get the Treasury in deeper and deeper"
- Louis Antoine Garnier-Pagès (French finance minister, 1848): "It was useful to precipitate a crisis to render it less durable: 'Do nothing to save the *rente*, clean out stocks; sell merchandise'" — credited this policy with France's brilliant recovery 1850-52
- Murray Rothbard: "any propping up of shaky positions postpones liquidation and aggravates unsound conditions"
- Herbert Spencer: "The ultimate result of shielding man from the effects of folly is to people the world with fools" — Kindleberger notes this is "understandable in a Darwinian age"

### Origin of the Concept
- The lender of last resort evolved from PRACTICE, not theory — Ashton: Bank of England was already acting as lender of last resort in the 18th century, "long before the rules for the treatment of crises were laid down by economists"
- E.V. Morgan: Bank of England's realization of its responsibility was delayed by government's Exchequer bills (1793, 1799, 1811); the Bank assumed the role only gradually "in spite of the opposition of theorists"
- Bank of France: 1833 Conseil Général concluded its major function was to defend the franc; when a crisis occurred, "the Bank should provide abundant and cheap discounts to moderate the intensity of the crisis and shorten its duration"
- **Bagehot's *Lombard Street* (1873)**: made the doctrine respectable among theorists
  - Traced the idea to David Ricardo, then Baring and Thornton (*Paper Credit*)
  - Ricardo (1875 Parliamentary testimony): "The orthodox doctrine... is that there is a period in a panic at which restrictions upon the issue of legal tender must be removed"
  - Bagehot (1848, his first published article): "paper money can be supplied in unlimited quantities... This power of issuing notes is one excessively liable to abuse... It should only be used in rare and exceptional cases"

### The Prisoner's Dilemma of Last-Resort Lending
- "Central banks should lend freely to halt the panic, but leave the market to its own devices to reduce the likelihood of future panics" — actuality vs. principle; "today wins over tomorrow"
- **Bank Act of 1844**: Currency School (fixed money supply) over Banking School (elastic money supply); both opposed last-resort lending as temporary expedient
- Suspended in 1847 and 1857 when crises required more money; parliament investigated both times; concluded it was undesirable to build in suspension provisions — to limit precedent, bill brokers after 1857 were told "not to expect the like again"
- By the 1866 suspension, the principle was "so widely accepted that there was no demand for a new investigation"
- Lord Overstone (Currency School champion): reluctantly admitted panics may require "that power which all governments must necessarily possess, of exercising special interference in cases of unforeseen emergency and great state necessity"; produced a ringing metaphor: "There is an old Eastern proverb which says, you may stop with a bodkin a fountain, which if suffered to flow, will sweep away whole cities in its course"

### Who Is the Lender of Last Resort?
- In Britain: no clear agreement between Treasury (Exchequer bills) and Bank of England (discount freely at penalty rate)
- 1825: task given "resolutely to the Bank of England whose reluctant acceptance was 'the sulky answer of driven men'"
- 1890: guarantees used rather than Bank or Exchequer
- Gradually responsibility devolved on the Bank — Alfred Marshall: "its directorate came to be regarded at home and abroad as a committee of safety of English business generally"
- **Bank of France**: agreed by 1830s it had crisis responsibilities, but used them strategically — let regional banks fail in 1848 to convert them into subsidiaries (*comptoirs*), gaining monopoly of note issue
  - Banque du Havre trip to Paris (Feb 1848): "The return was not glorious. The Bank of France had been impitiable" — refused mortgages, refused to accept a comptoir
  - Bank of France discounted freely in 1810, 1818, 1826 but lacked courage in 1831-32 crisis
  - After gaining monopoly and converting regional banks to branches: established 60 *comptoirs d'escompte* + *sous-comptoirs* (organized by trade branches to hold goods and issue paper)
  - Three-name paper requirement: Louis Raphael Bischoffsheim mocked it — "The number is not important. With bad signatures one can collect 10 instead of three. I prefer one good to 20 bad"
  - Crédit Mobilier (Pereire brothers) not saved in 1868 — Bank of France refused to discount its paper; interpreted as Rothschild revenge against the Pereires (outsiders)
  - Union Générale not rescued in 1882 but Comptoir d'Escompte rescued in 1889 — "a second large bank failure in seven years might have completely destroyed the French banking system"
  - Union Générale: Paris banks withdrew from speculative activity as it peaked (August 1881) and advanced 18.1M francs after the crash (January 1882) for orderly liquidation

### The United States — A Revolving Door of Last-Resort Identity
- First and Second Banks of the United States: ambiguous as lenders of last resort
- U.S. Treasury repeatedly ad hoc: accepted customs receipts in 30-day notes (1792), made special deposits in troubled banks (1801, 1818, 1819), relaxed specie requirements (1801)
- After 1833 (Second Bank charter not renewed): Treasury even busier — paid interest/principal early, deposited in banks despite 1845 law requiring Treasury to keep funds out, accepted non-government-bond collateral for deposits, bought/sold gold and silver
- Fall 1872: Secretary George S. Boutwell served as lender of last resort by reissuing retired greenbacks — "which may have been illegal"
- 1907: Treasury issued $50M Panama Canal bonds + $100M certificates of indebtedness — crisis averted by >$100M capital inflow from Great Britain
- Analysis of 1857: "the Federal government was incapable of intervening effectively and the public, including the banks, was left without guidance to stem the crisis"; 1857 Treasury intervention "proved to be too much and too early"
- **Sprague** (Aldrich Commission historian): believed the BANKS should have taken responsibility for maintaining reserves, not the Treasury; specifically the 15 of 50 NY banks holding 70-80% of bankers' deposits were "directly responsible for the satisfactory working of the credit machinery of the country"
- Sprague's extended argument: NY banks should maintain reserves for seasonal needs, out-of-town withdrawals, and international balance of payments — but they didn't because "the outside banks feel no responsibility for the course of the market"
- Leading NY bankers drew a different conclusion: the problem was "lack of elasticity of the money supply" → led to the Federal Reserve Act (1913) and the real-bills doctrine (money supply backed by trade bills → elastic and non-inflationary)

### Constructive Ambiguity
- Some ambiguity about WHO is the lender of last resort may be HELPFUL — keeps bankers self-reliant, provided the uncertainty doesn't disorient markets
- "In London it was vaguely understood that there should be no formal provision for a lender of last resort but that there should be in time of crisis"
- The "intuitive politicians in the British government and the merchant bankers who ran the Bank of England thought it best to give power to grant relief neither wholly to the Bank nor wholly to the government, but to leave it uncertain"
- If relief were formally within either's power, "pressure from the public would be difficult to resist"
- "No one would bear the responsibility in too large a group. If only one entity were responsible, pressure for action might become irresistible. The optimum may be a small number of actors, closely attuned to one another in an oligarchic relation"
- **Italy 1907**: Società Bancaria Italiana failing; Bank of Italy involved "early and deeply, and almost became too heavily committed"; Treasury finally rescued at insistence of Bank of Italy governor Stringher; Bonelli: indecision "might develop when the economy works for more than ten years with no one in charge"
- Sir Robert Peel (Bank Bill, June 4, 1844): "My Confidence is unshaken that we have taken all the Precautions which legislation can prudently take... It may occur in spite of our Precautions; and if it be necessary to assume a grave Responsibility, I dare say Men will be found willing to assume such a Responsibility"

### George Harrison and the 1929 Crash
- Harrison (NY Fed president): opened discount window wide at crash; exceeded Board instructions by buying $160M in government bonds in October + $210M in November
- Board of Governors in Washington resented NY's high-handedness (legacy of Benjamin Strong, who died 1928); had "little compunction in reining in Harrison"
- Division between Washington and New York "handicapped more effective action in coping with the collapse of stock prices in 1929"
- **Alan Greenspan (October 19, 1987)**: "impeccable" timing — poured in high-powered money "right and left" (Bagehot's expression); similarly effective for Asian crisis 1997, Russia/LTCM 1998, Y2K 1999, stock decline 2000-01

### To Whom on What? (Bagehot's Rules)
- Bagehot: lend to all comers on sound collateral "as largely as the public asks for them" — at a PENALTY RATE
- But Bagehot resisted turning last-resort lending over to government commissioners — they'd be subject to political pressure; Bank of England is "a body withdrawn from the political world and not subject to political pressures"
- **Collateral dilemma**: the longer a panic continues, the more collateral declines in value → "the less sound the collateral"; eventually must look at the CHARACTER of the borrower — "something that J.P. Morgan was reported to consider exclusively"
- The wry banker's comment: "bankers lend money only to those that do not need it"
- Central bank rules often rigid (Federal Reserve Act 1913: only gold and negotiable bills of exchange as reserves → frequently trouble); the "beauty" of the Chancellor's letter of indemnity was that it "preserved the rule while violating it and did not create a precedent"
- **Accusations of venality**: Protestant/Jewish Bank of France directors allegedly punished Catholic Union Générale supporters (1882) while saving insider Comptoir d'Escompte (1888); Bank of England (1772) changed discounting rules "deliberately to break up a group of Dutch speculators"; Bank of the United States allowed to fail (December 1930) amid accusations of "being punished for its pushy ways"
- Discounting evolved slowly: at first only "respectable London names on paper with no more than two months to run"; by 1816, Bank of England broke its own rule against mortgages — "a Transaction quite out of the ordinary course of Business to relieve the distress of poor people in the Black Country"; even lent on a West Indies plantation mortgage and on unimproved land belonging to a duke
- Railroad collateral from 1842; Bank of France lent to railroad syndicate from 1852 (accused of "supporting, if not starting, the feverish speculation in railroads")
- "The rule is that there is no rule": lend to insolvent banks to avoid mischief if the Lord Mayor of London goes bankrupt (1793), or to maintain a payroll in Newcastle (a town "used to banking disasters")

### When and How Much?
- "'Too little, and too late' is one of the saddest phrases in the lexicon not only of central banking but of all activity"
- Bagehot: lend freely at a penalty rate to solvent borrowers with good collateral — "subject to the inevitable exceptions"
- In 1772, Bank of England tried to brake overtrading by selective limitation of discounts; in 1797 began to prorate discounts; by May 1783 had discounted so heavily it refused advances on new government bond subscriptions — "behaving like a private bank worried about its own safety rather than a public institution"
- **Open-market operations vs. discount window**: with discounts, Bagehot specified the right amount — "all the market will take" through solvent houses at penalty rate; with open-market operations the decision is harder, but "given a seizure of credit in the system, more is safer than less since the excess can be mopped up later"
- **1929**: NY Fed bought $160M October + requested authority for $200M more; Board reluctantly approved November 27; $155M purchased by January 1; by then discounts were running off, interest rates had fallen, "the need for a lender of last resort — to accommodate the liquidation of call loans in the market — was over"
- Friedman & Schwartz: ambivalent on 1929 — praised Harrison's October action as "timely and effective" but opposed discounting generally; believed stock market crash "was not a major factor in producing or deepening the depression"; ultra-monetarist view: open-market operations constituted "a renewal of the credit inflation of the 1920s"
- In retrospect: open-market operations "woefully inadequate" mid-October to end of November — enabled NY banking system to take over out-of-town call loans but "at the cost of reducing the amount of credit available for purchases of stocks, commodities, and real estate, which led to declines in their prices and unleashed the depression"
- **Timing problem**: during boom, slow it without precipitating panic; after crash, "wait long enough for the insolvent firms to fail, but not so long as to let the crisis spread to the solvent firms" — "delaying the death of the strong swimmers" (Clapham)
- Disraeli (1857 debate): quoted an unnamed Lombard Street leader who said that "had it not been for some private information... to the effect that in case of extreme pressure there would be an interference on the part of the Government, he should at that moment have given up the idea of struggling any further"
- 1857: Treasury came to the rescue too early and "helped it inflate still further"; 1873: response too slow; 1907: Sprague called it "the unfortunate delay of the Clearing House"
- "Timing is an art. That says nothing — and everything."

## Chapter 12: The International Lender of Last Resort (pp 243-?)

### Why an International Lender of Last Resort?
- Deflationary pressure transmits across borders through exchange rate changes — Finnish markka devaluation (1992) → deflationary pressure on Sweden; Thai baht/Malaysian ringgit/South Korean won depreciation (1997) → massive deflationary impulse to the US; Japanese trade surplus in 1990s → deflationary impact on US
- Exchange rate overshooting/undershooting: costly adjustments; sharp depreciations → widespread bank/firm failures; sudden reversals from overshooting to undershooting caused by sudden reversal of cross-border capital flows
- **Domestic lender of last resort**: reduce likelihood that liquidity shortage cascades into solvency crisis → save healthy firms from being dragged down by distress selling
- **International lender of last resort**: provide liquidity to ameliorate exchange rate overshooting and obviate changes "not required by the economic fundamentals"

### The IMF and Its Origins
- International credits have existed for 400+ years — governments borrowing abroad; private bankers relying on foreign counterparts
- 1920s: first concerted worry about international liquidity shortage (declining gold production + new central banks in former Austro-Hungarian territories → increased demand for monetary gold); League of Nations conferences urged central banks to hold more reserves in foreign securities (pounds, dollars)
- **IMF established in 1940s**: formal arrangement for credit among governments during foreign exchange crises; motivated by interwar competitive devaluations, trade wars, and the belief that destructive competition contributed to WWII
- Member countries agreed to: (1) limit exchange rate fluctuations around parity, (2) seek IMF approval before changing parity significantly
- IMF endowed with pool of gold and currencies from member subscriptions; members could borrow to finance payments deficits
- **Devaluation dynamics**: should improve trade balance but adjustment is time-dependent (J-curve); while adjusting, momentum traders may push currency even further down → overshooting

### Overshooting, Undershooting, and the Need for International Intervention
- Mexico (1994-95): needed peso decline because current account deficit was unsustainable (3-4% of GDP); when the shock occurred, capital outflow was so sharp that current account balance swung to 10-12% of GDP briefly — massive overshooting
- Similar overshooting: Argentina (2001), South Korea (1997, lost ⅓ of won value despite 1% GDP current account deficit), Indonesian rupiah during Asian crisis
- Without an international lender of last resort, depressions following financial crises can be "long and drawn out, as in 1873, 1890, and the early 1930s"
- **Key problem**: no world currency → international lender must lend the currency of one or several major financial centers; must distinguish between necessary exchange rate changes and unnecessary overshooting

### The Debt-Deflation Cycle
- 1930s: business failures → inventory liquidation → price level decline → reduced net worth of other firms → more bank loan losses → banks refuse to renew maturing loans → more failures (Irving Fisher's mechanism)
- Japan (late 1990s): prices declined 1%/year → "exceptionally high level of business failures"
- Hong Kong: six-year debt-deflation after Asian crisis + sovereignty handover; currencies of other Asian countries depreciated → Hong Kong firms' competitiveness declined

### The Analogy Between Domestic and International — And Where It Breaks
- Domestic: lend to illiquid but solvent institutions; don't lend to insolvent ones (FDIC/deposit insurance handles those)
- International: many crises involve currencies that are "seriously overvalued" — must judge whether a change is necessary (fundamental disequilibrium) or temporary
- Argentina (1999-2000): borrowed from IMF to avoid devaluation — then devalued anyway and "snubbed its creditors, wanting them to settle for twenty-five or thirty cents on the dollar"
- Parade of currency crises in 35 years: Mexico/Brazil/Argentina (1982), British pound/Italian lira (1992), Mexican peso (1994), Thai baht/Asian currencies (1997), Russian ruble (1998), Brazilian real (1999), Argentine peso (2001)

### A Historical View of International Crises
- Central bank history distinguishes internal drains (bank runs) from external drains (gold outflows)
- External drain: raise interest rate → "draw gold from the moon" (Bank of England quip); but sometimes rate increases are seen as weakness → accelerate capital flight
- **Rothschild rescue (1846-48)**: bond prices fell 75% across Europe; Nathan Rothschild in London saved with help from Auguste Belmont (NY representative) shipping silver; Warburg family performed similar role for Hamburg (1930s, $9M credit)

### Central Bank Cross-Border Lending — A Chronology
- **1694**: Bank of England borrowed 2M guilders from States General in Holland (Nine Years' War)
- **1695-97**: Amsterdam took over Bank of England's protested bills at 10% (penalty rate, but business not public good)
- **1763**: Bank of England + London bankers rescued Dutch correspondents with enlarged credits + five gold shipments + delayed bill presentations; Wilson: "none of this was pure altruism" — British prosperity was linked to Dutch prosperity
- **1772-73**: Amsterdam paralyzed; Bank of England opened on a Sunday (Jan 10), allowed specie to be drawn against notes/government stocks; "loads of bullion sent on the first packet boat"; simultaneously refused to discount doubtful paper → broke many Jewish-owned banks in Amsterdam. Catherine the Great of Russia helped British merchants — "the first of a number of occasions when czarist Russia assisted Western Europe in crisis"
- **1825**: Rothschild banks shipped £400,000 (mostly in sovereigns) from Paris → arrived Monday Dec 19 — Bank of England coffers were "virtually empty after the peak of the run on the previous Saturday"
- **1836-39**: Bank of England drew bills on Paris (£400K first time); in 1838 arranged credit line → drew £2M in 1839 via Baring Brothers + ten Paris banks. Also sent 320K + 360K sovereigns to the US; Clapham: "without precedent" and "damaging insofar as it encouraged American bankers to issue more securities in British markets"
- **1846-47**: Bank of France borrowed 25M francs from British capitalists; Emperor of Russia offered to buy 50M francs of French *rente* to help finance wheat imports
- **1857 Silberzug (Silver Train)**: Hamburg senate voted 15M mark banco fund (5M in Hamburg bonds + 10M silver to be borrowed abroad); appeals to Rothschilds/Baring (London), Fould (Paris), Amsterdam, Brussels, Berlin, Dresden, Hanover — ALL turned down. Berlin: "Bruck and the Kaiser are not financially ambitious." By Dec 8 every Hamburg bank except Heine's was threatened. Word came from Vienna that they'd take the whole loan → silver train arrived → panic ended Dec 12 when it became known there was enough silver. Some firms (like Donner & Co., allotted 700K marks) turned out not to need any once confidence restored
- **1860-61**: Civil War panic in NY drew specie from Paris/London; French exchanged £2M silver for £2M gold with Bank of England; French then bought gold in London at above export point
- **1873**: NO international cooperation; Bank of England denied rumors of seeking Bank of France loan; Bank of Prussia (pre-Reichsbank) offered gold loan to Bank of England — "politely but curtly declined: 'The Bank is not, nor has it been in want of such aid'" (Clapham: "this suggestion from the nouveau riche would have seemed impertinent")
- **1890 (Baring)**: Lidderdale secured £3M from Bank of France + £1.5M from State Bank of Russia (both in gold); told Bank of France governor that "ordinary operations of bank rate would have brought the gold in time" — using unusual measures only to meet an "unusually sudden storm." Clapham: "Suppose for some political-financial reason they had been unwilling to oblige?"
- **1906-07**: Bank of France forwarded 80M francs in US gold eagles to London; much debate about whether Bank of England ASKED for help (British sources: no announcement, no request; French sources: allegedly to relieve Bank of England from needing to raise its discount rate). Hartley Withers (*Times* financial editor): Bank of England's determination "finally compelled the Bank of France to take some share in the international burden"

### London vs Paris as World Financial Center
- Anglo-Saxon view: London was world financial center from early 19th century to 1914
- Counter-view: "France was the political nerve center of Europe... during 1850-1857, it was the center from which fluctuations in the economic cycle radiated"
- Paris between 1820-1870: assisted London's foreign payments in the Baltic, Russia, China, Latin America, US; became "first place in Europe for foreign exchange"
- **Franco-Prussian War changed the situation** — Bagehot: "Since the Franco-German war, we may be said to keep the European reserves... Formerly there were two such stores in Europe; one was the Bank of France and the other the Bank of England. But since the suspension of specie payments by the Bank of France, its use as a reservoir of specie is at an end... Accordingly London has become the sole great settling-house in Europe"
- Bonelli: Paris was the center that "regulated world liquidity" (Italy's links to 1907 crisis)
- Morgenstern reconciliation: Paris had the "abundance of funds," London had the "machinery" for moving them; each had its clients (Italy/Russia for Paris; US/British colonies for London)

### The Lender of Last Resort After World War I
- No international lender in 1920-21 crisis; currencies depreciated → hyperinflation in Eastern/Central Europe (1923) in newly independent states from Austro-Hungarian Empire
- Paul Erdman's thriller *The Crash of '79*: financial crisis handled by printing massive currency and letting the dollar float → demand surge for goods
- 1920s: stabilization loans arranged for Austria, Hungary, Eastern European central banks under League of Nations/London/Paris/NY auspices; Dawes and Young loans recycled German reparations
- **French franc under attack (1924)**: franc fell from 98/pound (Feb 17) to 107/pound (March 4); J.P. Morgan offered $100M six-month revolving credit (Bank representative Thomas Lamont "judged too small"); Bank of France resisted pledging gold as collateral → yielded after face-saving formula; Bank regents (Rothschild, de Wendel) extracted conservative fiscal program from government. Within three days of March 9, speculators routed — franc appreciated from 123 to 78 by March 24
- 1926: another attack → franc depreciated to 240/pound; Poincaré reforms + interest rate surge → appreciated to 125 francs

### The Crisis of 1931 — The Catastrophic Failure
- **Credit Anstalt (Vienna, May 1931)**: leading Austrian bank; May 11 statement revealed 140M schilling losses (~¾ of capital); Austrian government sought League of Nations help → BIS arranged 100M schilling loan from 11 countries; exhausted by June 5; second loan by June 14 (150M schillings, 2-3 years) — but French demanded Austria renounce customs union with Germany → Austria refused → Bank of England offered 50M schillings ($7M) for one week → Austria stopped pegging to gold → schilling depreciated
- **Run shifted to Germany**: weakened by speculation, loan write-offs, fraud, banks buying own shares; Jacob Goldschmidt (Danatbank outsider) was resented by other bankers; Nordwolle (woolen firm) failure June 17 brought down Danatbank; other banks refused help "because of their dislike of Goldschmidt"
- June 25: $100M international loan ($25M each from Bank of England, Bank of France, NY Fed, BIS) — for three weeks only. Hans Luther (Reichsbank president) wanted larger loan with amount concealed; Reichsbank reserve cover at 40.4% (just above 40% minimum) → "Devil take the hindmost"
- Germans wanted $1 billion; French willing to offer $500M but with political conditions; US worried about own $1.6B budget deficit; British foreign secretary Henderson attracted to idea but Montagu Norman (Bank of England) held that the Bank had "already lent quite as much as is entirely convenient"
- By July 20, loan idea "brushed aside as impractical" — Germany imposed Standstill Agreement (froze foreign debt)
- **Run shifted to Britain** (mid-July 1931): losses on Continental holdings + May/Macmillan reports revealing large budget deficits + unexpectedly high foreign fund withdrawals; Bank of France had been converting pounds to gold since 1927 (via roundabout: buy pounds spot, buy forwards, demand gold on maturity)
- Fed + Bank of France each loaned $125M to Bank of England → exhausted; British sought $400M one-year loan from NY/Paris; Bank of England reported foreign bankers wouldn't lend while budget deficit was so large; trade unions opposed unemployment benefit cuts → Labour government fell August 24
- August 28: new "national government" borrowed $200M from Morgan (NY) + $200M from French syndicate → not enough; Britain went off gold September 21
- Bank of France then joined gold bloc in converting $750M of US dollar deposits to gold → deflationary pressure on US → weakened US banking system
- NY Fed code of central bankers: "stiff upper lip, reminiscent of Walter Mitty refusing the blindfold before the firing squad"; Harrison (1929) asked Norman if Fed's British pounds were convertible to gold — curt reply: "Of course, the sterling is repayable in gold. This is the gold standard"
- **Five lessons of 1931**: (1) Britain unable to act as lender of last resort; (2) US unwilling beyond inadequate loan; (3) France tried to gain political objectives (Austria customs union, German political conditions); (4) German paranoia about inflation after 1923; (5) irresponsibility of smaller countries
- **Hawtrey's analysis**: "The crisis of 1931 differed from earlier crises in its international character... foreign creditors of Germany and Eastern European debtors feared that the foreign exchange market would break down *even if the debtors remained solvent*... The need arises for an *international lender of last resort*"
- Hawtrey on loan size: "If credits are to be granted to a central bank in difficulties at all, they should be granted up to the full amount needed. There should be no limit. If the amount is inadequate and the exchange gives way after all, the sums lent are completely wasted"

### Bretton Woods and the International Monetary Arrangements
- Early 1940s debate between British (Keynes's plan: institution with own money/unit of account) and American (White plan: gold + national currencies deposited, country quotas based on trade + gold holdings)
- US view prevailed ("the Americans had all the money"); British got them to double initial capital
- Articles of Agreement: countries state parity (gold or USD), maintain narrow band around parity, seek IMF approval for changes >10%
- **But**: IMF structure meant it could NOT act as true lender of last resort — didn't have its own money; lent member currencies that had to be repaid; narrow quotas divided into four tranches (only first tranche near-automatic); access was "a matter of grace... rather than a matter of right as Keynes's plan had proposed"
- IMF/World Bank sidelined during postwar recovery (Marshall Plan did the heavy lifting); Bretton Woods system didn't truly operate until 1958 (franc devaluation + pound convertibility)
- Capital controls proved porous — "leads and lags" in trade finance allowed large capital transfers
- 1960: General Arrangements to Borrow (GAB) — Group of Ten pledged additional $6B on top of $14.4B in IMF quotas; still inadequate; IMF found "not to work in timely fashion" — three weeks to frame a proposal + obtain instructions from directors representing multiple countries
- Cross-border capital flows exploded: maximum daily speculation on pound was under $100M in 1947 vs. $1.5B on deutsche mark (May 1969) and $1B into Germany in one hour (May 1971); by late 1990s, daily FX turnover reached $1 trillion

### The Swap Network and Basel Agreement
- 1960s: Fed organized bilateral swap lines with European central banks — short-term reciprocal credit arrangements to defend currencies
- Basel Agreement (March 1961): eight central banks agreed to cooperate in foreign exchange markets — "a gentleman's club arrangement"
- Swap network grew from $2B (1962) to $30B; meant to supplement IMF, not replace it
- Swaps were temporary (3-6 months) and supposed to be repaid — different from Bagehot's "lend freely" doctrine

### Bretton Woods Breakdown
- Dollar as world reserve currency created Triffin's dilemma: US needed to run deficits to supply world with dollars, but persistent deficits undermined confidence in the dollar
- August 15, 1971: Nixon closed gold window → end of Bretton Woods system → floating exchange rates
- No orderly transition — competitive depreciations and oil shock followed
- "The world moved to floating rates not by design but by default"

### Herstatt and Franklin National — Twin Bank Failures of 1974
- **Herstatt** (Cologne): speculated in foreign exchange; closed by German regulators on June 26, 1974 at 3:30 PM local time — after European markets closed but BEFORE NY markets settled → counterparties had already paid deutsche marks but hadn't received dollars; created "Herstatt risk" (settlement risk across time zones)
- **Franklin National** (NY): 20th largest US bank; massive foreign exchange losses disclosed May 1974; Fed provided $1.75B in discount window credit; FDIC arranged takeover by European-American Bank (October)
- Both failures showed that banking crises no longer respected national borders

### Oil Shocks and Recycling
- 1973-74 and 1979-80 oil price increases: OPEC surpluses → petrodollar recycling through commercial banks → loans to developing countries (especially Latin America)
- Banks acted as intermediaries: took OPEC deposits, lent to Mexico/Brazil/Argentina at floating rates
- When Volcker raised US interest rates (1979-82): debt service costs soared for borrowing countries → debt crisis

### The Mexican Crises
- **1982**: Mexico announced it could not service its $80B foreign debt (August 12); Fed/Treasury organized $3.5B emergency bridge loan; Baker Plan (1985): more lending + structural reform; Brady Plan (1989): debt reduction through bond swaps
- **1994-95**: peso crisis after devaluation (December 1994); massive capital flight; Clinton administration organized $51.6B rescue package ($20B US Exchange Stabilization Fund + $17.8B IMF + $10B BIS + $3B commercial banks); Mexico repaid early and in full
- The 1994-95 rescue was controversial: "bail out the bankers" criticism; moral hazard of rescuing investors who'd bought high-yielding *tesobonos*

### East Asian Crisis — Lending Table
- **Thailand (1997)**: $20.1B total package ($4B IMF + $7.6B bilateral + $8.5B other)
- **Indonesia (1997)**: $30B ($10B IMF + $18B bilateral + $2B other); ultimately collapsed → Suharto resigned
- **South Korea (1997)**: $57B ($21B IMF + $10B World Bank + $4B ADB + $22B bilateral); most successful — repaid rapidly
- **Mexico (1994-95)**: $51.6B for comparison
- Pattern: crisis country receives large package; market tests whether package is large enough; if inadequate → second wave of selling

### The IMF Is Not a Central Bank
- IMF cannot create money — it lends member currencies and SDRs
- Lending is conditional (structural adjustment programs) and takes time to negotiate
- Conditionality often pro-cyclical: countries in recession forced to tighten fiscal policy and raise interest rates → deeper recession
- "The IMF works in Washington time — decisions take weeks, not the hours or minutes a financial crisis demands"
- IMF staff culture: mostly PhD economists, few with market experience; "they understand theory but not the plumbing of financial markets"

### The United States and the Dollar
- US transformed from world's largest creditor (1914-80s) to largest debtor nation
- Trade deficits persistent since early 1980s; financed by foreign purchases of US assets
- Wynne Godley's "Seven Unsustainable Processes" (1999): warned that US current account deficit + private sector financial deficit + fiscal surplus were incompatible
- **Plaza Agreement (1985)**: G-5 agreed to depreciate dollar (then overvalued by ~40%)
- **Louvre Accord (1987)**: tried to stabilize dollar after it had fallen enough; largely failed — dollar continued declining
- US as hegemon: declining willingness to lead rescue packages; "leading from behind"
- US benefited from dollar's reserve currency status (seigniorage, borrowing in own currency) but also bore the cost of providing global liquidity

### Limitations of International Lending
- Too many parties with conflicting interests: creditor countries want conditions; debtor countries want unconditional lending; private banks want to be rescued but not constrained
- Size problem: crises keep getting larger — $3.5B for Mexico (1982), $51.6B for Mexico (1994-95), $57B for South Korea (1997) — packages must be "large enough to impress speculators"
- Speed problem: financial markets move instantly; IMF/government negotiations take weeks
- Political problem: in democracies, taxpayer-funded bailouts of foreign countries (or foreign investors) are unpopular
- "The international lender of last resort remains an improvisation — a series of ad hoc arrangements rather than a systematic institution"

## Chapter 13: The Lessons of History and the Most Tumultuous Decades Ever (pp ~280-?)

### Four Centuries of Financial Crises
- Financial crises are not random events but follow recognizable patterns recurring across centuries
- From tulipmania (1636) through South Sea/Mississippi (1720) to 1920s stock bubble → same sequence of credit expansion, speculative excess, panic, and crash
- The HALLMARK of the 1930s was not just severity but SEQUENCE: Austrian banking crisis → German banking crisis → British abandonment of gold → American banking collapse → gold bloc deflation — "a chain reaction across national borders"
- No previous crisis had cascaded through the international system with such devastating interconnection

### The Most Tumultuous Period
- Period since mid-1960s: MORE banking system collapses than in any comparable period — including the 1930s
- Loan losses reached 20-50% of bank assets in some countries
- Taxpayer costs of bank bailouts: 15-20% of GDP in worst cases (Japan, Scandinavia)
- **Three waves of bank failures**:
  1. Currency depreciations wiping out unhedged foreign borrowings
  2. Real estate price collapses after speculative booms
  3. Stock market declines destroying bank capital and collateral values

### Causes of Financial Tumult — Seven Major Shocks
- **1960s US inflation increase**: Vietnam War spending + Great Society programs → loss of price stability → end of the long postwar expansion
- **1970s global inflation**: commodity boom + wage-price spirals → worldwide; Nixon's price controls created distortions
- **1973 Yom Kippur/oil shock**: oil price quadrupled → stagflation (simultaneous inflation + recession); recycling of petrodollars created new vulnerabilities
- **1979 Volcker shock**: Fed funds rate to 20% → killed inflation but triggered deep recession; devastated Latin American borrowers with floating-rate debt
- **1980s Japanese deregulation**: removed constraints on Japanese banks → massive expansion of lending → real estate + stock market bubble → Bank of Japan tightened 1990 → "lost decade" (actually two decades)
- **1985 dollar depreciation**: Plaza Agreement → dollar fell → Bank of Japan lowered interest rates to support Japanese exporters → fueled domestic bubble further; "the dollar's decline was the proximate cause of the Japanese bubble"
- **Brady Bonds → NAFTA → Mexico (1989-94)**: debt-reduction bonds → capital inflow to Latin America → NAFTA expectations → surge in Mexican asset prices → peso crisis when capital flow reversed

### "Predictable Shocks" — An Oxymoron?
- Reliance on continued foreign capital inflow to pay interest on existing debt was ALWAYS unsustainable — yet markets behaved as if it would continue indefinitely
- Each country: "We're different" — Mexico (oil), Thailand (export growth), Indonesia (natural resources), Argentina (currency board) — all claimed their situation was unique
- "Bubbles always implode" — but the timing is genuinely unpredictable; market participants who bet against bubbles early get crushed by the momentum
- Kindleberger's key insight: the shock itself may be predictable in retrospect but NOT in timing → "the tragedy is that the pattern is clear only after the damage is done"

### The Impacts of Monetary Shocks and Credit Market Shocks
- The striking feature since early 1970s: variability of cross-border money flows as measured by changes in trade balance / GDP ratios — "much larger than in any previous period"
- **Mexico example**: booming economy → trade deficit 7% of GDP; financial crisis → trade surplus 4% of GDP — an 11-percentage-point swing with massive impacts on peso, asset prices, inflation, and solvency
- These sharp changes resulted from sharp changes in volume and direction of cross-border capital flows
- **Minsky's domestic framework extended internationally**: increase in fund flow to a country → currency appreciation + asset price increases + domestic credit expansion → economic boom; then a shock reverses cross-border flows → crash with "sharp declines in prices of currencies, securities, and other assets"

### Monetary Shocks vs Credit Market Shocks — Different Transmission
- **Monetary shocks** (inflation expectations): investors concerned about US inflation → sold dollar securities → bought DM/Swiss franc/pound → dollar depreciated even faster than inflation differentials warranted (overshooting). Early 1980: investors became convinced US inflation would DECLINE → sold foreign securities → bought dollars → dollar appreciated rapidly
- **Credit market shocks**: Japanese stock/real estate bubble affected demand for yen securities from both sides (Japanese buying foreign + foreigners buying Japanese); credit shocks in Mexico, Thailand, US dollar "had no direct impacts on anticipated inflation rates" but massive impacts on exchange rates because they changed the amounts of securities investors wished to hold
- **Key distinction**: monetary shocks impact anticipated exchange rates through inflation differentials; credit shocks impact actual exchange rates through changes in asset demand

### Why Greater Variability Under Floating Rates
- When currencies were pegged, central banks absorbed capital flows into reserves → smaller impact on asset prices
- When currencies float, capital inflow → currency appreciation → cheaper imports → consumption boom → wealth effect → savings decline → trade deficit widens — "the invisible hand" creates a feedback loop
- "The economic boom was prolonged and pervasive; many of the participants may have failed to recognize that the cross-border pattern of flows of cash could not be sustained"
- Pattern in the data: flow of savings to a country → economic boom → rates of return seem high → attract MORE funds → feedback loop → bubble → eventual reversal

### Could an International Lender of Last Resort Have Made a Significant Difference?
- Financial tumultuousness since mid-1960s is a result of the large variability of cross-border flows of funds
- Asset price bubbles in Finland, Norway, Sweden (1980s) resulted from cross-border fund inflows; similarly Thailand, Malaysia, and other Asian countries (early 1990s) followed from increased flow of savings from Japan
- **One role** would be to WARN — suggest that the volume of international capital flows to a country was too large to be sustainable, and that the adjustment would be costly
- **Another role**: provide credits when depreciation occurs to reduce undershooting and limit contagion
- US Treasury acted as lender of last resort to Mexico (end of 1994) — if initiative had been taken "one or several weeks earlier, the undershooting of the peso would have been smaller, and the adverse impacts... would have been less severe"
- **IMF's track record**: established in the 1940s precisely to act as international lender of last resort; fund staff visit member countries twice a year but "rarely sounded the alarm" that borrowing patterns were unsustainable; "nor has the IMF been able to provide the credits at the time of the crash to avoid extensive and debilitating undershooting"
- Final verdict: "Whether the shortfall in the performance of the IMF relative to the ambitions that led to its establishment has resulted from the failures of analysis or policy or member country truculence is a topic for another book"
