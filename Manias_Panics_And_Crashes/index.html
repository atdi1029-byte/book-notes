Foreword (Robert M. Solow)

Nobel laureate Robert Solow introduces Kindleberger as a scholar who approached financial crises the way Darwin approached the Galapagos — collecting specimens, classifying patterns, searching for the underlying logic beneath bewildering variety. Kindleberger was a natural skeptic who mistrusted both free-market fundamentalists and social engineers.

He believed that clinging to rigid beliefs in the face of disconcerting evidence was a particularly dangerous form of irrationality — one that afflicted economists as much as anyone. Solow notes that the 5th edition, updated by Robert Aliber after Kindleberger’s death in 2003, imposes somewhat more order on what was occasionally a wayward path through Kindleberger’s specimen cabinets.

The core warning remains: when large quantities of liquid capital are sloshing around the world, the possibility that they will overflow the container should be taken seriously. The book is a monument to the idea that history matters — that you cannot understand today’s financial system without understanding the crises that shaped it.

Chapter 1: Financial Crisis — A Hardy Perennial

The book opens with a sweeping survey of the financial wreckage of the post-1970s era: unprecedented volatility in commodities, currencies, real estate, and stocks, and an unprecedented frequency of financial crises. Three successive waves of bank failures struck the global system — early 1980s, early 1990s, late 1990s — each systematically connected to asset bubbles and rapid cross-border capital flows.

Japan’s 1980s bubble saw real estate and stock prices reach absurd levels while Japanese banks grew faster than any Western institutions, with seven or eight of the world’s ten largest banks. When the bubble imploded in 1990, it became “a crash without a panic” because the government socialized the losses. Nordic countries replicated Japan’s pattern simultaneously. Mexico’s 1994 crisis turned a NAFTA success story into catastrophe within months. The Asian crisis of 1997–98 cascaded from Thailand through Indonesia, Malaysia, and South Korea to Russia and LTCM.

Kindleberger identifies ten great financial bubbles spanning four centuries, from the Dutch Tulip Bubble of 1636 through the NASDAQ bubble of 1995–2000. His central thesis: the cycle of manias and panics results from pro-cyclical changes in the supply of credit. Credit expands rapidly during good times and contracts sharply when growth slackens.

A mania occurs when prices in the present become inconsistent with prices in the distant future. Rational exuberance morphs into irrational exuberance — the time comes to “get on the train before it leaves the station.” The bicycle analogy captures the dynamic: after years of bubble behavior, the rider needs forward momentum or the bike becomes unstable. There is no plateau, no middle ground — only continued ascent or a fall.

The chapter distinguishes bubbles from Ponzi schemes and chain letters. Ponzi schemes promise 30–50% monthly returns, paying early investors with new money until collapse. Chain letters redistribute from latecomers to early entrants. But asset bubbles differ fundamentally because they affect the macroeconomy — they are associated with economic euphoria, increased spending, and massive credit expansion that touches every sector.

The central policy question emerges: should governments moderate asset surges to reduce crisis likelihood, or ease hardship when prices decline? This is the lender-of-last-resort dilemma that runs throughout the entire book and has no clean resolution.

Chapter 2: Anatomy of a Typical Crisis

The analytical backbone of the book is Hyman Minsky’s financial instability hypothesis, which Kindleberger enriches with centuries of historical evidence. The Minsky model identifies a sequence: displacement (an exogenous shock changes economic outlook), credit expansion (banks and new institutions flood the system with lending), euphoria (rising prices create feedback loops), critical stage (insiders begin selling), and revulsion (panic selling, credit contraction, contagion).

The displacement can be any significant shock — the end of a war, a new technology, a major policy change, a financial deregulation. What matters is that it creates profit opportunities in at least one sector. Investment and production pick up, funded by an expansion of bank credit. But the expansion goes further than the underlying opportunity justifies: speculation shifts from legitimate enterprise to pure capital gains.

The objects of speculation vary wildly across history: tulips in 1636, South Sea and Mississippi Company shares in 1720, canals and cotton in the early 19th century, railroads mid-century, real estate in every era, foreign sovereign bonds at irregular intervals, stocks from the late 19th century onward, tech companies in the 1990s. The objects change; the underlying pattern of credit-fueled excess does not.

Kindleberger presents Minsky’s three-stage classification of financial positions. Hedge finance: cash flow covers both interest and principal payments. Speculative finance: cash flow covers interest but principal must be refinanced at maturity. Ponzi finance: cash flow covers neither interest nor principal — the borrower depends entirely on continued asset appreciation to avoid default.

As a boom progresses, the mix shifts from predominantly hedge positions to increasingly speculative and Ponzi positions. This makes the entire system fragile — dependent on continued price increases. When the music stops, the Ponzi positions collapse first, dragging speculative positions with them, and threatening even legitimate hedge positions through contagion and fire-sale pricing.

Chapter 3: Speculative Manias

Are markets rational? The efficient markets hypothesis, championed by the Chicago School, holds that prices always reflect fundamental values and that destabilizing speculation is self-eliminating because speculators who buy high and sell low lose money and are driven from the market. Kindleberger marshals four centuries of historical evidence against this reassuring view.

Rational behavior by individuals — each one logically buying into a rising market to capture gains — can produce collectively irrational outcomes. The fallacy of composition means that what is rational for each individual investor becomes disastrous when everyone does it simultaneously. The chapter draws on crowd psychology — Gustave Le Bon, Charles Mackay’s Extraordinary Popular Delusions and the Madness of Crowds — to argue that speculative manias involve a genuine shift in mass behavior.

People who would normally be cautious become aggressive; outsiders who have never speculated before enter the market, drawn by the sight of neighbors making easy money. The “amateur speculators” are the surest sign of a late-stage mania. Adam Smith himself recognized this pattern: “overtrading” follows easy credit as naturally as night follows day.

Milton Friedman argued that destabilizing speculation is theoretically possible but empirically rare. Kindleberger’s counter: this argument assumes a closed system where foolish speculators are eliminated. In reality, new fools continuously enter the market, drawn by the spectacle of others making money. The supply of “greater fools” is constantly replenished — until suddenly it isn’t, and the crash comes with devastating speed.

The debate between rational markets and mob psychology is not merely academic. It determines whether regulators should intervene during bubbles or stand aside and let markets self-correct. Kindleberger sides firmly with intervention — the historical record shows too many instances of catastrophic damage for the “markets are always right” view to be credible.

Chapter 4: Fueling the Flames — The Expansion of Credit

The fuel for every mania is credit expansion. Kindleberger argues against the monetarist view that the money supply is an exogenous variable controlled by central banks. In practice, the supply of credit is endogenous — it expands from within the financial system through innovation, new institutions, and the creation of near-moneys that bypass whatever controls central banks impose.

Every generation invents new ways to create credit: bills of exchange replaced coin, bank notes replaced bills, deposit creation replaced notes, and in the modern era, commercial paper, repos, derivatives, and securitization have created vast new reservoirs of credit outside the banking system proper. The Bank of England was founded in 1694 partly because England needed credit innovation to finance wars against France.

Country banks proliferated after 1750, often issuing far more notes than their reserves could support. In the 19th century, joint-stock banks and discount houses created layers of intermediation that multiplied the effective money supply. Each innovation bypassed whatever safeguards the previous generation had erected. Central banks found themselves perpetually fighting the last war — regulating the forms of credit that caused the previous crisis while new forms grew unchecked in their blind spots.

The international dimension magnifies the problem enormously. Cross-border capital flows add fuel to domestic credit booms. When funds flow into a country, they typically end up in the banking system, which uses them as a base for further domestic credit expansion. The original inflow gets multiplied through fractional reserve banking.

When the flow reverses — often suddenly and without warning — the credit contraction is similarly amplified. This is why international financial crises tend to be more severe than purely domestic ones: the leverage is greater, the contagion paths more numerous, and the coordination problems more intractable.

Chapter 5: The Critical Stage

Every bubble reaches a critical stage where the smart money begins to exit. Insiders who understand that prices have departed from fundamentals sell their holdings. The difficulty is identifying this moment in real time. The period between the first sales by insiders and the general recognition that the party is over can be brief (the South Sea Bubble collapsed within months) or extended (Japan’s real estate bubble deflated over a full decade).

Kindleberger identifies several signals of the critical stage: the entry of amateurs who have never speculated before, the shift from long-term investment to short-term trading, increased use of leverage, the proliferation of new financial products designed to facilitate speculation, and growing confidence that “this time is different.”

But these signals are clear only in retrospect. During the bubble, each can be rationalized: amateurs are entering because the opportunity is genuine; leverage is safe because prices only go up; new products represent financial innovation, not excess. The genius of the bubble is that it makes its own destruction look like progress.

When the critical stage tips into panic, the dynamics reverse with terrifying speed. What was confidence becomes fear; what was a scramble to buy becomes a scramble to sell. The fallacy of composition takes over: each individual’s attempt to minimize losses by selling drives prices lower, making everyone else’s position worse.

The contagion effect means that perfectly solvent institutions can be destroyed because the assets they hold are being liquidated at fire-sale prices by distressed sellers elsewhere in the system. A liquidity crisis becomes a solvency crisis not because the underlying businesses are worthless but because the market for their assets has temporarily ceased to function.

Chapter 6: Euphoria and Economic Booms

Euphoria is not a momentary emotion but an extended economic condition. During the euphoric phase, asset price increases create wealth effects that boost consumer spending, which in turn strengthens the economy, which validates the asset price increases — a self-reinforcing loop that can persist for years before exhausting itself.

In Japan during the late 1980s, land prices reached levels where the Imperial Palace grounds were theoretically worth more than all the real estate in California. The Nikkei index tripled between 1985 and 1989. Japanese corporations issued bonds at below-market rates because the equity warrants attached to them were so valuable — the bonds were effectively free money as long as stock prices kept rising.

The Nordic countries replicated this pattern almost exactly. Finland, Norway, and Sweden all deregulated their financial systems in the mid-1980s, triggering domestic credit booms fueled by cross-border capital inflows. Real estate and stock prices soared. When the bubble burst in 1990–92, all three countries experienced banking crises with loan losses reaching 10–15% of GDP.

The Mexican experience of 1990–94 added a developing-country dimension. NAFTA negotiations created optimism about Mexico’s economic future, attracting massive foreign capital inflows. The peso appreciated in real terms, making Mexican exports less competitive while imports surged. The trade deficit reached 7% of GDP — financed entirely by foreign capital that could reverse at any moment.

When a series of political shocks rattled investor confidence (the Chiapas uprising, the Colosio assassination), the capital flow reversed and the peso collapsed. The same pattern was about to play out on a vastly larger scale across Asia, with even more devastating consequences for countries that had believed their rapid growth made them immune to the historical pattern.

Chapter 7: International Contagion

Financial crises spread across borders through multiple channels: trade linkages, capital flows, shared creditors, and pure psychological contagion. Kindleberger traces the evolution of international contagion from the earliest crises through the modern era, showing that globalization has amplified rather than dampened the transmission mechanisms.

In the 18th century, the connection between Amsterdam, London, and Hamburg meant that a crisis in one financial center almost inevitably affected the others. The failure of great Hamburg trading houses in 1763 was triggered by the end of the Seven Years’ War removing the speculative opportunities that had sustained them; the crisis spread to Amsterdam within weeks through interconnected bills of exchange.

The 19th century saw contagion grow with the expansion of international trade and capital flows. The 1825 crisis began in London with speculative investments in newly independent Latin American republics and spread to every major financial center in Europe. The 1857 crisis was genuinely global — starting in the American Midwest with railroad speculation, it transmitted to New York, then London, then Hamburg, then Scandinavia.

The Hamburg crisis of 1857 reads like a medical case study. Day by day, the city’s banks came under pressure; the senate voted emergency funds; appeals went out to every financial center in Europe; all were rejected. Finally, word came from Vienna that they would take the entire loan, and the famous Silberzug (silver train) arrived, ending the panic when word spread that sufficient silver existed.

The modern era has seen contagion accelerate dramatically. The Asian crisis of 1997 began with Thailand’s baht devaluation in July and within months had spread to Indonesia, Malaysia, South Korea, Russia, and Brazil. The mechanism was partly rational (countries with similar vulnerabilities) and partly psychological (investors lumped all “emerging markets” together and fled indiscriminately). The LTCM crisis of 1998 showed that contagion could threaten even the most sophisticated financial institutions in the most developed markets.

Chapter 8: Bubble Implosion — Domestic and International

The crash, when it comes, is characterized by a sharp and sudden revision of expectations. Prices that seemed to rise inevitably now fall with equal inevitability. The psychological shift is as dramatic as the economic one — what felt like a new era of prosperity is suddenly revealed as a house of cards.

The chapter examines how bubbles deflate: sometimes through a sharp crash (October 1929, October 1987), sometimes through a prolonged decline (Japan after 1990), and sometimes through a combination of both. The Asian crisis featured sharp currency devaluations followed by years of economic stagnation; the NASDAQ bubble saw a sharp initial decline followed by years of grinding losses.

The domestic dynamics of implosion follow a recognizable pattern. Asset prices fall, destroying the collateral that supports bank loans. Banks call in loans or refuse to roll over maturing credit, forcing borrowers to sell assets into a declining market. Forced selling drives prices lower, creating more bad loans, in a vicious cycle that Irving Fisher called “debt-deflation.”

The international dimension adds currency depreciation: as capital flees a country, the currency falls, increasing the domestic-currency value of foreign debts. Firms that borrowed in dollars or yen find their debt burden multiplying even as their revenue in domestic currency shrinks. In Indonesia during 1997–98, the rupiah lost 80% of its value, turning manageable foreign debts into crushing burdens that bankrupted entire sectors of the economy.

Kindleberger emphasizes the role of specific triggering events — a bank failure, a fraud revelation, a political shock — in tipping a fragile system into crisis. But these triggers are merely the proximate cause. The underlying cause is always the same: excessive credit expansion has created a structure so leveraged and so dependent on continued price appreciation that any significant shock will bring it down. The trigger matters less than the vulnerability it exploits.

Chapter 9: Fraud, Swindles, and the Cycle

Fraud is not an aberration that occasionally accompanies financial bubbles — it is an integral and predictable feature of them. Kindleberger provides a magnificent survey of financial fraud across four centuries:

“The propensity to swindle grows as the propensity to speculate grows.”

During booms, oversight is lax, returns are easy, and the rising tide conceals the fraudsters.

The varieties of fraud are extensive: outright embezzlement, accounting manipulation, insider trading, Ponzi schemes, bucket shops (where customers’ orders were never actually executed), stock watering, phantom companies, and venal journalism (reporters paid to tout stocks). All flourish during bubbles because the euphoria suppresses the skepticism that would normally detect them.

The specific cases are extraordinary. Enron created off-balance-sheet partnerships named after Star Wars characters (JEDI, Chewco) — 97% owned by Enron, 3% by senior employees — to hide debt and inflate profits. Merrill Lynch and JP Morgan paid above-market prices for Nigerian barges so Enron could book gains, charging correspondingly higher lease rates. Arthur Andersen earned $2 million per year in auditing fees but $25 million in consulting — the desire to retain consulting income “clouded its judgment.” Bernie Ebbers of WorldCom, a former high school history teacher, made 60+ acquisitions to maintain earnings growth, fudged $10 billion of expenses as “investments,” and took $400 million in off-the-books loans from his own company.

The S&L debacle of the 1980s was fraud on an industrial scale. Michael Milken’s associates took control of failed thrifts and insurance companies to create captive buyers for Drexel’s junk bonds. Corporate raiders “used Uncle Sam’s money — money obtained from the sale of deposits guaranteed by the U.S. government — to acquire more than fifty firms.” The total cost: $150 billion to taxpayers. Had regulators closed insolvent thrifts in the early 1980s, the cost would have been $20–30 billion.

Nick Leeson at Barings Singapore made an error, sold put options to cover it, then the Kobe earthquake crashed Tokyo stocks. He doubled down, lost again, doubled down again — repeating until losses equaled all of Barings’ capital. Ivar Kreuger, “The Match King,” built a global monopoly, lent $125 million to France for exclusive rights, then forged Italian government bonds as collateral. Adele Spitzeder, a Munich actress in the 1860s, opened a “bank” paying 20% monthly interest to Bavarian farmers — a Ponzi scheme that thrived on rural word-of-mouth until withdrawals exceeded deposits.

Kindleberger draws brilliantly on literature as well as economics. Dickens’s Mr. Merdle in Little Dorrit, Zola’s Saccard in L’Argent, and Trollope’s Augustus Melmotte in The Way We Live Now are all portraits of financial swindlers drawn from real-life models. The parallels between fiction and reality are remarkably consistent: the charismatic promoter, the compliant press, the credulous investors, the inevitable exposure.

The chapter distinguishes between outright swindles and sharp practices. Overend, Gurney & Company, the great London discount house that failed spectacularly in 1866, was not technically fraudulent but engaged in practices so reckless as to be indistinguishable from fraud. When directors realized the extent of losses, they converted from partnership to limited liability — selling shares to the public to recapitalize what was already a bankrupt firm. The South Sea Company’s directors were corrupt insiders who sold their own shares near the top. The Mississippi Company under John Law was different — a genuine visionary whose scheme became a bubble because success attracted imitators and speculators who perverted the original design.

During the 1720 South Sea mania, dozens of absurd schemes were floated:

“For an Undertaking which shall in due Time be reveal’d” — collected £2,000 in subscriptions in a single morning; the promoter “was philosopher enough to be off to the Continent that evening.”

Other schemes proposed extracting silver from lead, perpetual motion machines, and insuring marriages against divorce.

The relationship between fraud and the business cycle has policy implications. If fraud proliferates during bubbles and is exposed during crashes, then regulation alone cannot prevent it — the regulators themselves are caught up in the euphoria. In Japan, a woman with a small Osaka restaurant borrowed billions from Sumitomo Bank via a “friendly relationship” with the local banker; yakuza secured inflated real estate appraisals; Ministry of Finance officials were entertained at a “pantyless shabu-shabu restaurant” in exchange for advance regulatory information. The astute personnel needed to detect fraud during booms are unwilling to endure the boredom of calm periods.

Chapter 10: Policy Responses

When a crisis hits, policymakers face a fundamental choice: do nothing and let the market sort itself out, or intervene to prevent cascading failures. Kindleberger examines the full spectrum of historical responses, from complete inaction to massive intervention, and the arguments for each approach.

The case for benign neglect rests on three arguments: the market will self-correct, intervention creates moral hazard, and the cleansing effect of crises removes inefficient firms and restores discipline. Andrew Mellon’s infamous advice to President Hoover during the Great Depression captures this view at its most extreme:

“Liquidate labor, liquidate stocks, liquidate farmers, liquidate real estate... it will purge the rottenness out of the system.” —Andrew Mellon

Kindleberger is skeptical. The medicine may be worse than the disease, and the “rottenness” being purged often includes perfectly sound businesses destroyed by a liquidity crisis they did nothing to cause. He examines whether raising interest rates can burst a bubble without causing broader damage. The answer is usually no — during genuine panics, the demand for liquidity becomes so intense that interest rates lose their normal signaling function.

The chapter surveys a rich array of intervention tools. Moral suasion (jawboning markets). Stalling and bank holidays (buying time for passions to cool). Clearinghouse certificates (emergency currency issued by banks collectively). Stock market consortiums (pools of banks buying to support prices). Deposit insurance. Direct central bank lending.

Hamburg’s response to the 1857 crisis is described day by day: the city senate voting emergency funds, appeals to foreign banks (all rejected), and finally the arrival of the famous Silberzug (silver train) from Vienna that ended the panic when word spread there was enough silver. The Baring crisis of 1890 was managed through a guarantee fund organized by the Bank of England with French and Russian gold.

The 1998 rescue of Long-Term Capital Management illustrates the modern version: the New York Federal Reserve convened a consortium of fourteen banks and securities firms who collectively invested $3.65 billion to prevent LTCM’s disorderly failure from destabilizing global markets. No public money was used, but the Fed’s role as convener was essential. Kindleberger approves: the alternative — letting LTCM fail and watching the resulting fire sale destroy counterparty balance sheets worldwide — would have been far more costly.

The chapter also covers Exchequer bills (used by the British government in 1793 and 1799 to inject liquidity), circuit breakers and trading halts, moratoriums on debt repayment, and the role of luck — illustrated by the tale of the Kauffmann brothers’ bride who won two separate lotteries during the 1799 crisis, fully rehabilitating the failing firm.

Chapter 11: The Domestic Lender of Last Resort

The lender of last resort is the central institution of financial crisis management, and Kindleberger traces its evolution from practice to theory over two centuries. The concept derives from French legal terminology (dernier ressort, the court of last appeal), but in central banking the emphasis shifts to the lender’s responsibilities. The function is to “halt a run out of real assets and illiquid financial assets into money by supplying as much money as may be necessary to forestall the run.”

The moral hazard dilemma is inescapable. If investors believe they will be rescued, they take greater risks, producing weaker lending discipline during booms. But if a panic rush to liquidate cannot be halted, the fallacy of composition takes over and solvent firms are destroyed alongside insolvent ones.

The case against intervention has distinguished advocates. Napoleon’s treasury minister Mollien warned that “a start in this direction would only get the Treasury in deeper and deeper.” The French finance minister Garnier-Pagès in 1848 argued for precipitating the crisis to make it shorter: “Do nothing to save the rente, clean out stocks; sell merchandise.” Murray Rothbard insisted that “any propping up of shaky positions postpones liquidation and aggravates unsound conditions.” Herbert Spencer offered the Darwinian argument:

“The ultimate result of shielding man from the effects of folly is to people the world with fools.” —Herbert Spencer

Bagehot’s Lombard Street (1873) codified the doctrine: lend freely at a penalty rate on good collateral to all comers. But each element creates difficulties in practice. “Good collateral” deteriorates during crises as asset values fall. The penalty rate is meant to discourage frivolous borrowing, but during genuine panics it may discourage necessary borrowing. “All comers” raises the question of whether insiders who caused the crisis should be saved alongside innocents.

J.P. Morgan reputedly looked only at the CHARACTER of borrowers, not their collateral. The wry banker’s observation: “bankers lend money only to those that do not need it.” The beauty of the Chancellor’s letter of indemnity (suspending the Bank Act during crises) was that it “preserved the rule while violating it and did not create a precedent.”

The question of WHO acts as lender of last resort was never cleanly resolved. In Britain, responsibility oscillated between Treasury (Exchequer bills) and Bank of England (discounting). In 1825, the task was given “resolutely to the Bank of England whose reluctant acceptance was ‘the sulky answer of driven men.’”

In France, the Bank of France played the crisis strategically. In 1848, it deliberately let regional banks fail in order to convert them into subsidiaries (comptoirs), gaining monopoly of note issue. When the Banque du Havre sent representatives to Paris begging for help, “the return was not glorious. The Bank of France had been impitiable.” The Crédit Mobilier (Pereire brothers) was not saved in 1868 — interpreted as Rothschild revenge against outsiders. But the Comptoir d’Escompte was rescued in 1889 because “a second large bank failure in seven years might have completely destroyed the French banking system.”

In the United States, identity shifted among the First and Second Banks, the Treasury, private bankers like J.P. Morgan, clearinghouses, and finally the Federal Reserve. In fall 1872, Treasury Secretary Boutwell served as lender of last resort by reissuing retired greenbacks — “which may have been illegal.” Discounting rules evolved slowly: by 1816 the Bank of England broke its own rule against mortgages to “relieve the distress of poor people in the Black Country”; it even lent on a West Indies plantation mortgage and unimproved land belonging to a duke.

Kindleberger argues that some ambiguity about WHO will rescue the system is actually helpful — “constructive ambiguity” keeps bankers self-reliant while preserving the option of intervention. If relief were formally within any one entity’s power, political pressure for action would become irresistible. The optimal arrangement is “a small number of actors, closely attuned to one another in an oligarchic relation.”

George Harrison, president of the New York Fed, opened the discount window wide during the 1929 crash and bought $160 million in government bonds in October alone — exceeding instructions from the Board of Governors. Washington resented New York’s high-handedness. Alan Greenspan, by contrast, managed the 1987 crash “impeccably” by pouring in high-powered money “right and left.”

Kindleberger’s concluding judgment on timing:

“‘Too little, and too late’ is one of the saddest phrases in the lexicon not only of central banking but of all activity.”
“Timing is an art. That says nothing — and everything.”

Chapter 12: The International Lender of Last Resort

If the domestic lender of last resort is a complex and contested institution, the international lender of last resort barely exists at all. Yet the need for one has grown enormously as cross-border capital flows have exploded. Kindleberger traces a 400-year history of international crisis lending, from the Bank of England borrowing two million guilders from the Netherlands in 1694 to the IMF’s massive packages for Asian countries in 1997–98.

The historical record is fascinating. In 1763, the Bank of England and London bankers rescued Dutch correspondents with enlarged credits, five gold shipments, and delayed bill presentations — not from altruism but because British prosperity was linked to Dutch prosperity. In 1772, the Bank of England opened on a Sunday to ship bullion to Amsterdam but simultaneously refused to discount doubtful paper, deliberately breaking Dutch-Jewish banking houses.

In 1825, Rothschild banks shipped £400,000 in sovereigns from Paris, arriving on Monday morning when the Bank of England’s coffers were virtually empty after Saturday’s bank run. In 1890, Lidderdale at the Bank of England secured £3 million from the Bank of France and £1.5 million from Russia in gold to manage the Baring crisis. Catherine the Great of Russia had earlier helped British merchants in the 1770s — “the first of a number of occasions when czarist Russia assisted Western Europe in crisis.”

The French franc crisis of 1924 shows international lending at its best. The franc fell from 98 to 107 per pound in two weeks. J.P. Morgan offered a $100 million six-month revolving credit. Within three days of March 9, speculators were routed — the franc appreciated from 123 to 78 per pound by March 24. Decisive action, adequate size, swift execution.

The crisis of 1931 shows the opposite — catastrophic failure. The Credit Anstalt in Vienna revealed 140 million schilling in losses in May; the BIS arranged a loan from 11 countries, exhausted by June 5. The run shifted to Germany, where Jacob Goldschmidt’s Danatbank was failing — other banks refused help “because of their dislike of Goldschmidt.” A $100 million international loan (four-way split among Bank of England, Bank of France, NY Fed, and BIS) lasted only three weeks. The Germans wanted a billion dollars; the French were willing to offer $500 million but with political conditions; the Americans worried about their own budget deficit; the British said the Bank had “already lent quite as much as is entirely convenient.”

The run then shifted to Britain itself. The Bank of France had been quietly converting pounds to gold since 1927. Fed and Bank of France each loaned $125 million to the Bank of England — not enough. Britain went off gold September 21. The Bank of France then converted $750 million of US dollar deposits to gold, transmitting deflationary pressure to America and weakening the US banking system.

Hawtrey’s devastating analysis:

“If credits are to be granted to a central bank in difficulties at all, they should be granted up to the full amount needed. There should be no limit. If the amount is inadequate and the exchange gives way after all, the sums lent are completely wasted.” —R.G. Hawtrey

The IMF, established at Bretton Woods in the 1940s, was designed to prevent a repeat. But its structure made it unable to act as a true lender of last resort: it could not create money, lending was conditional and time-consuming, quotas were inadequate, and member countries’ political agendas interfered with timely action. The gap between the IMF’s ambition and its performance remained, in Kindleberger’s judgment, “a topic for another book.”

The debate between London and Paris as world financial center adds a colorful dimension. The Anglo-Saxon view held London supreme from the early 19th century. But Paris between 1820 and 1870 assisted London’s foreign payments in the Baltic, Russia, China, Latin America, and the United States. Bagehot himself admitted that the Franco-Prussian War of 1870 changed the equation by suspending French specie payments, making London “the sole great settling-house in Europe.”

Chapter 13: The Lessons of History

The final chapter pulls together four centuries of evidence. Financial crises are not random events but follow a recognizable pattern that has recurred since the tulipmania. The objects of speculation change but the underlying dynamic of credit expansion, euphoria, and crash is constant. The 1930s hallmark was not merely severity but SEQUENCE: Austrian banking crisis cascading to Germany to Britain to America to the gold bloc, each country’s failure worsening the next.

The period since the mid-1960s has been the most tumultuous in financial history — more banking system collapses than any comparable period, including the 1930s. Loan losses reached 20–50% of bank assets in some countries. Taxpayer costs of bailouts reached 15–20% of GDP. Seven major shocks in rapid succession created cascading vulnerabilities: US inflation, global inflation, oil shocks, Volcker’s interest rate revolution, Japanese deregulation, dollar depreciation, and the Brady Bonds/NAFTA/Mexico sequence.

The most striking feature of the post-1970s era is the variability of cross-border capital flows. Capital flowing into a country strengthens its currency, raises asset prices, and creates a wealth effect that boosts consumption — which attracts more capital in a self-reinforcing feedback loop. When the flow reverses, the mechanism works in devastating reverse.

Each country believed it was different: Mexico had oil, Thailand had export growth, Indonesia had natural resources, Argentina had a currency board, the US had technology. None was different.

“Bubbles always implode.”

But the timing is genuinely unpredictable, and market participants who bet against bubbles early get crushed by momentum.

Could an international lender of last resort have made a difference? Kindleberger’s qualified yes: one role would be to warn when capital inflows are unsustainable; another would be to provide credits during crashes to reduce overshooting. The US Treasury’s initiative in acting as lender of last resort to Mexico in late 1994 limited the damage. But the IMF has largely failed — sounding few alarms during booms and providing too little, too late during crashes.

The book ends not with a solution but with a challenge: the international financial system remains “a series of ad hoc arrangements rather than a systematic institution.” Four centuries of evidence suggest that financial crises will continue until the institutional architecture catches up with the scale and speed of modern capital flows.

Key Takeaways

Financial crises follow a predictable pattern

From tulipmania to the NASDAQ bubble, every crisis follows the same sequence: displacement, credit expansion, euphoria, critical stage, and revulsion. The objects of speculation change but the underlying dynamic does not. Recognizing this pattern is the first step toward managing it.

Credit is pro-cyclical and endogenous

The money supply is not controlled by central banks. Credit expands from within the financial system through innovation — new instruments, new institutions, near-moneys — that bypass whatever controls exist. Every generation invents new ways to create credit.

Rational individuals create irrational markets

The efficient markets hypothesis cannot explain bubbles and crashes. Individual rationality — buying into a rising market to capture gains — produces collective irrationality when everyone does it simultaneously. The fallacy of composition is the fundamental flaw in the rational markets argument.

International contagion is primary, not secondary

Financial crises propagate across borders through trade, capital flows, shared creditors, and psychology. The international dimension often determines whether a crisis remains contained or becomes systemic.

Fraud is intrinsic to bubbles

The propensity to swindle grows as the propensity to speculate grows. During booms, rapid price increases create both the opportunity and the cover for fraud. Regulation alone cannot prevent it because regulators are caught up in the euphoria.

The lender of last resort is necessary but imperfect

Bagehot’s rule — lend freely at a penalty rate on good collateral — is elegant in theory but messy in practice. Moral hazard is real but the alternative is worse. Timing is “an art.”

No functioning international lender of last resort exists

The IMF was designed to fill this role but has largely failed. It cannot create money, imposes pro-cyclical conditions, moves too slowly, and is subject to political manipulation. International crises remain ad hoc improvisations.

Hegemonies stabilize; vacuums destabilize

When one country dominates the financial system, someone takes responsibility for stability. When hegemony fades, no one leads and crises cascade unchecked through the international system.

“This time is different” is always wrong

Every bubble claims unique characteristics that make historical patterns irrelevant. Every bubble is wrong about this. The pattern is clear only in retrospect — the tragedy is that it repeats because each generation must learn the lesson for itself.

Contrarian Ideas

Markets are not self-correcting

Against the dominant economics of his era, Kindleberger insists that financial markets systematically overshoot and undershoot fundamental values. The efficient markets hypothesis is not just theoretically questionable but empirically disproven by four centuries of evidence.

Central banks cannot control the money supply

The monetarist prescription of controlling the money supply to prevent crises founders on the reality that credit is endogenous. Financial innovation constantly creates new forms of money-like instruments beyond central bank control.

Free trade in capital is dangerous

While supporting free trade in goods, Kindleberger is skeptical of completely free capital flows. The massive variability of cross-border capital movements since the 1970s has been the primary driver of financial instability.

Moral hazard is overrated as a concern

While acknowledging that bailouts encourage risk-taking, Kindleberger argues that the alternative — allowing preventable cascading failures — inflicts far greater damage on innocent parties. Constructive ambiguity is the practical answer.

Regulation cannot prevent crises

Rules for sound banking already exist but are systematically ignored during booms by bankers and regulators alike. Crisis prevention requires judgment, not just rules — and judgment is precisely what evaporates during euphoria.

The US is becoming a destabilizing force

As the US transitioned from world’s largest creditor to largest debtor, its willingness to act as international stabilizer declined. The world moved to floating rates “not by design but by default.” The declining American hegemony creates a vacuum that no institution has filled.

Allegories & Cultural References

Darwin on the Beagle

Solow compares Kindleberger to Darwin: a naturalist collecting specimens from around the world, classifying them, and discovering underlying patterns. Financial crises are his finches — endlessly varied on the surface but revealing the same evolutionary logic.

The Bicycle Analogy

A bubble economy is like a bicycle: it needs forward momentum or it falls over. There is no stable plateau. Once the growth that sustains confidence slows, the whole structure wobbles. The rider must either pedal harder or crash.

Literary Swindlers

Kindleberger draws on 19th-century literature to illuminate financial fraud: Dickens’s Mr. Merdle (Little Dorrit), Zola’s Saccard (L’Argent), and Trollope’s Melmotte (The Way We Live Now) — all portraits of financial charlatans drawn from real-life models.

The Bodkin and the Fountain

Lord Overstone’s Eastern proverb: you can stop a fountain with a bodkin (needle), but if you let it flow, it will sweep away cities. Early and decisive intervention is cheap; delayed intervention costs everything.

Walter Mitty Before the Firing Squad

Central bankers maintaining composure during crises are compared to Walter Mitty refusing the blindfold. Montagu Norman’s crisp insistence that sterling was “of course” convertible to gold, weeks before Britain abandoned gold, epitomizes the pose.

The Hamburg Lottery Bride

The Kauffmann brothers, failing during the 1799 crisis, whose bride won BOTH a Hamburg city lottery and a Duchy of Mecklenburg lottery — fully rehabilitating the firm. Kindleberger’s dry comment: “The odds at Las Vegas may be higher for the solution to a global crisis.”

The Silberzug (Silver Train)

During the 1857 Hamburg crisis, after every European financial center refused to help, Vienna agreed to take the entire loan. A train loaded with silver arrived, and the panic ended overnight. Some firms allotted emergency funds turned out not to need them once confidence was restored. Salvation arriving by rail has a fairy-tale quality.

Musical Chairs

The critical stage of a bubble is like musical chairs: everyone knows the music will stop but no one wants to sit down first. Being early is as costly as being late — you miss the gains. The rational strategy is to keep dancing until the last possible moment.

Frameworks & Models

The Minsky-Kindleberger Model

The five-stage sequence: (1) Displacement — exogenous shock changes expectations; (2) Boom — credit expands, investment rises; (3) Euphoria — speculation dominates, leverage increases; (4) Critical stage — insiders sell, smart money exits; (5) Revulsion — panic, fire sales, contagion, crash.

Minsky’s Three Financial Positions

Hedge (cash flow covers both interest and principal), speculative (covers interest but not principal), and Ponzi (covers neither — depends on asset appreciation). As bubbles progress, the mix shifts toward speculative and Ponzi, making the system fragile.

Bagehot’s Rules

Lend freely, at a penalty rate, on good collateral, to all comers — “subject to inevitable exceptions.” The beauty of the Chancellor’s letter of indemnity was that it “preserved the rule while violating it and did not create a precedent.”

Fisher’s Debt-Deflation Spiral

Business failures → forced liquidation → price decline → reduced net worth → bank losses → credit contraction → more failures. A self-reinforcing cycle requiring external intervention to break.

Triffin’s Dilemma

The reserve currency country must run deficits to supply world liquidity, but persistent deficits undermine confidence in the currency. This inherent contradiction doomed Bretton Woods and continues to create tensions with the dollar.

The Appendix Table

A comprehensive matrix of 38 crises from 1618 to 1998, tracking countries affected, triggering events, objects of speculation, sources of credit expansion, peak/crash dates, and lender of last resort responses. The empirical backbone of the book’s argument that crises follow recurring patterns.

Referenced Works

Walter Bagehot — Lombard Street (1873). The foundational text on the lender of last resort; Kindleberger both builds on and qualifies Bagehot’s rules throughout.

Hyman P. Minsky — John Maynard Keynes (1975) and the Financial Instability Hypothesis. The theoretical backbone; Minsky’s model structures every chapter.

Charles Mackay — Extraordinary Popular Delusions and the Madness of Crowds (1841). The classic account of tulipmania and the South Sea Bubble; Kindleberger both draws on and corrects Mackay.

Milton Friedman & Anna Schwartz — A Monetary History of the United States (1963). The monetarist counter-narrative; Kindleberger engages with their interpretation of the Great Depression throughout.

Joseph Schumpeter — Business Cycles (1939). The theory of creative destruction and the role of innovation in driving economic cycles; provides the concept of displacement.

Adam Smith — The Wealth of Nations (1776). Referenced for his analysis of overtrading and the observation that speculation follows easy credit.

Irving Fisher — The Debt-Deflation Theory (1933). The mechanism by which falling prices increase real debt burdens, creating a self-reinforcing downward spiral.

John Clapham — The Bank of England: A History (1945). The definitive institutional history; Kindleberger draws on it extensively for British crisis episodes.

O.M.W. Sprague — History of Crises Under the National Banking System (1910). Detailed accounts of American financial crises; argued banks should maintain their own reserves.

R.G. Hawtrey — The Art of Central Banking (1932). Analysis of 1931; the argument that international credits must be unlimited or they are wasted.

Fernand Braudel — Civilization and Capitalism (1981). The longue durée approach to economic history; influenced Kindleberger’s method of studying centuries-long patterns.

H.M. Hyndman — Commercial Crises of the Nineteenth Century (1892). Historical detail on 19th-century financial crises from a socialist perspective.

Manias, Panics, and Crashes: A History of Financial Crises by Charles P. Kindleberger & Robert Z. Aliber. 5th Edition, Wiley, 2005. ~340 pages.

A four-century survey of financial crises arguing that bubbles are not random accidents but the predictable result of pro-cyclical credit expansion, speculative euphoria, and the absence of adequate institutional safeguards — domestically and internationally.

About the Author

Charles Poor Kindleberger (1910–2003) was an American economic historian and one of the twentieth century’s foremost authorities on financial crises. He spent the bulk of his academic career at MIT, where he taught international economics for over three decades. Before academia, he served as a senior official in the Office of Strategic Services during World War II and later helped design the Marshall Plan at the State Department—an experience that shaped his lifelong conviction that international economic cooperation was both essential and fragile.

Kindleberger wrote over thirty books across a remarkably broad range of economic history, but Manias, Panics, and Crashes (first published 1978) became his defining work. His method was distinctively historical rather than mathematical—he believed you could learn more about financial crises from reading four centuries of dispatches, memoirs, and banking records than from any econometric model. Nobel laureate Robert Solow compared him to Darwin: a naturalist collecting specimens and searching for the underlying logic beneath bewildering variety.

Robert Z. Aliber, professor emeritus of international economics and finance at the University of Chicago’s Booth School of Business, took over as co-author after Kindleberger’s death and updated the book through its later editions. Aliber brought particular expertise on currency crises and cross-border capital flows, expanding the analysis to cover the Asian crisis, LTCM, and the post-2000 bubble economy.

Why This Book Matters

Manias, Panics, and Crashes is the most widely cited academic treatment of financial crises ever written. Now in its eighth edition, it has been continuously in print since 1978—a lifespan that itself testifies to the recurring nature of the phenomena it describes. Every major financial crisis since its publication has sent readers back to Kindleberger’s pages, finding the same patterns playing out with different instruments and different actors.

The book’s core contribution is the Minsky-Kindleberger model: the five-stage sequence of displacement, boom, euphoria, critical stage, and revulsion that has become the standard framework for understanding how bubbles form and collapse. By grounding Minsky’s theoretical model in four centuries of historical evidence—from Dutch tulips to dot-com stocks—Kindleberger made the case that financial instability is not an anomaly but a structural feature of capitalist economies.

Perhaps most importantly, the book challenged the dominant economic orthodoxy of its era. At a time when the efficient markets hypothesis reigned supreme and many economists believed that bubbles were impossible by definition, Kindleberger marshalled overwhelming historical evidence that markets systematically overshoot and undershoot, that credit expansion is endogenous and pro-cyclical, and that the lender of last resort is a necessary—if imperfect—institution. The 2008 financial crisis vindicated nearly every argument in the book, making it required reading for a generation of regulators, policymakers, and investors who had been told that such crises were relics of the past.
