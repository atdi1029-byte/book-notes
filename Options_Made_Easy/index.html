Glossary

Core Options Terms

Call option: The right, not the obligation, to buy an underlying asset at a fixed strike price before expiration. Memory aid: you “call” the asset away from the writer, the way you call a friend — you reach up to get something.

Put option: The right, not the obligation, to sell an underlying asset at a fixed strike price before expiration. Memory aid: you “put” the asset to the writer, the way you put your pen down on a table — you let go of it.

Strike price (exercise price): The fixed price at which an option can be exercised. A 40 strike call gives you the right to buy 100 shares at $40 regardless of where the stock is trading.

Expiration date: The last date an option can be exercised. For U.S. equity options, expiration falls on the Saturday after the third Friday of the expiration month. Trading actually ceases on Friday; Saturday is the exercise deadline.

Premium: The price paid by the buyer to the seller for an option contract. Quoted per share; multiply by 100 for the actual dollar cost of one U.S. equity option contract.

Intrinsic value: The in-the-money (ITM) portion of an option’s premium. For a call: stock price minus strike price (minimum zero). For a put: strike price minus stock price (minimum zero).

Time value (extrinsic value): Premium above intrinsic value. Represents the market’s expectation of future price movement. OTM options have zero intrinsic value and are 100% time value.

Moneyness

In-the-money (ITM): A call where stock price > strike, or a put where stock price < strike. Has intrinsic value; more expensive but lower leverage.

At-the-money (ATM): Strike price roughly equal to current stock price. Delta approximately 0.5; highest time value as a percentage of premium.

Out-of-the-money (OTM): A call where stock price < strike, or a put where stock price > strike. Zero intrinsic value; 100% time value; cheapest but highest percentage loss if wrong.

The Greeks

Delta (Δ): Rate of change of option price vs rate of change of underlying price. ATM options have delta ≈ 0.5. Also interpreted as the probability of expiring in-the-money.

Gamma (Γ): Rate of change of delta vs rate of change of underlying price. Highest near-the-money; lowest for deep ITM or OTM options. Identical for calls and puts at the same strike and expiration.

Theta (θ): Rate at which option premium decays as time passes. Negative for option buyers (time hurts you); positive for sellers (time helps you). Non-linear — accelerates sharply in the final 30 days.

Vega (κ): Sensitivity of option price to changes in implied volatility. Positive for long options (rising volatility increases premium). Highest for ATM options; decreases as expiration approaches.

Rho (ρ): Sensitivity of option price to changes in risk-free interest rate. Positive for calls, negative for puts. Least significant Greek for most practical trading.

Option Styles and Orders

American-style option: Exercisable any time before expiration. All U.S. equity options are American-style.

European-style option: Exercisable only at expiration. Common for futures and some index options. Slightly less valuable due to reduced flexibility.

Open interest: Total number of outstanding option contracts that have not been settled. High open interest = liquid market; low open interest = illiquid, wide bid-ask spreads.

Delta neutral: A position where the sum of all deltas equals zero. Not risk-free — gamma exposure means the position’s delta will change as the underlying moves.

Implied volatility (IV): The expected volatility derived from the actual market price of an option. Reverse-engineered from an options pricing model (Black-Scholes for stocks). Rising IV increases option premiums; falling IV decreases them.

Historical (statistical) volatility: Volatility calculated from actual past price movements. Annualized standard deviation of daily closing prices. Benchmark against which implied volatility is compared.

Spread Strategies

Bull call spread: Buy a lower-strike call, sell a higher-strike call (same expiration). Net debit; long-term bullish income strategy. Capped upside, reduced cost and risk versus a straight long call.

Bull put spread: Sell a higher-strike put, buy a lower-strike put (same expiration). Net credit; short-term monthly income strategy. Profits if stock stays above the short put strike at expiration.

Straddle: Buy an ATM call and an ATM put at the same strike and expiration. Net debit; directionally neutral; profits from large moves in either direction.

Strangle: Buy an OTM call and an OTM put at different strikes, same expiration. Net debit; cheaper than straddle but requires larger price move to profit; wider breakeven points.

Butterfly: Buy one ITM option, sell two ATM options, buy one OTM option (all same type, same expiration). Net debit; profits if stock stays near the middle strike; maximum loss is the premium paid.

Condor: Four-legged strategy with a “flat top” profit zone between two middle strikes. More expensive than the nearest butterfly equivalent; profits across a range rather than at a single point.

Collar: Buy stock, sell a call, buy a put (same or similar expiration). Goal is zero or near-zero risk. Maximum reward = call strike minus put strike minus net debit/credit.

Synthetic call: Buy the underlying stock and buy a put option. Creates the same risk profile shape as a long call but with lower risk as a percentage of capital at risk.

Covered call: Buy (or already own) the stock and sell call options against it to generate monthly income. Creates same profile shape as a short (naked) put.

Chapter 1: Introduction to Options

Guy Cohen opens not with a definition but with a confession: he once made thousands of percentage gains in a single year, thought he was invincible, and promptly gave some of it back. The book you’re reading is the distillation of everything he learned between that humbling experience and the moment he created the OVI indicator used by professionals at E*Trade, optionsXpress, and FlagTrader. The opening lesson is not about options mechanics — it is about what kind of person can actually profit from them.

Six mental prerequisites stand between the new trader and consistent profitability. Patience is first: if you can make one percent per week, you will compound to over 67 percent per year — a return that would make any fund manager envious. Most beginners rush past this arithmetic without absorbing it, chasing 50 percent weeks instead. Perseverance comes next: even the most unlikely students become great traders if they stay long enough. Cohen notes that the extreme teachers — the funniest, the scariest — are the ones you remember. The same is true of trades.

Knowledge is the third prerequisite, but Cohen is careful to distinguish academic knowledge from experiential knowledge. He passed his first options exam with 80 percent marks — fifth in a large class — yet academic preparation did not prevent him from later placing a spread trade only to discover, moments after the order went in, that his maximum reward was zero. He cancelled it before it filled. That moment of embarrassment taught him more than any chapter in a textbook.

Honesty is the fourth prerequisite and the hardest. Your trading results are entirely your own responsibility. Blaming a broker, a stock, or a news event prevents you from identifying and correcting the mistakes you actually made. The fifth prerequisite is pre-planning: every trade requires five numbers before entry — maximum risk, maximum reward, breakeven point(s), entry and exit levels, and stop loss. Stops should be based on the underlying stock price (more liquid, more meaningful) rather than the option price.

Discipline is the sixth and final prerequisite, and Cohen is blunt about his own failure here early in his career. The day he finally implemented a hard stop rule was his worst trading day: he gave back over 50 percent of accumulated gains in a single session. But he never violated the stop rule again. Discipline without a money management system is meaningless theater; money management without discipline is a plan that exists only on paper.

The formal definition of an option has four parts: the right (not the obligation) to buy or sell an asset at a fixed price before a predetermined date. Each component matters. The buyer of an option has a right, which means they can choose not to exercise it — maximum loss is always the premium paid, nothing more. The seller of an option has an obligation, which means they must perform if the buyer exercises. This asymmetry defines everything that follows.

The four components of an option: right not obligation, to buy or sell, at a fixed price, before a predetermined date. [view]

American-style options can be exercised at any time before expiration; European-style can only be exercised at expiration. U.S. equity options are American-style; many futures options are European-style. American options are worth slightly more due to added flexibility. In practice, most equity options are not exercised early because selling the option is nearly always more profitable than exercising it early (retaining time value).

The value of any option has two components. Intrinsic value is the in-the-money portion — what the option would be worth if exercised right now. A 40-strike call when the stock is at 45 has intrinsic value of 5. Time value is everything else — the market’s estimate of what additional intrinsic value the option might accumulate before it expires. Out-of-the-money options consist entirely of time value and will lose 100 percent of that value if the stock never reaches the strike.

Intrinsic value + time value = total option premium. OTM options are 100% time value; deep ITM options are dominated by intrinsic value. [view]

Seven factors determine how much you pay for an option: the type (call or put), the price of the underlying, the strike price, the time to expiration, implied volatility, the risk-free interest rate, and dividends. Of these, volatility is the most dynamic and the least intuitive. Higher volatility means wider, faster price swings, which increases the probability of the option landing in-the-money — and therefore makes options more expensive.

All options strategies, however complex, are combinations of four basic positions. Buying a call gives an upward-sloping risk profile: limited loss on the left side, unlimited gain to the right. Selling a call (naked) inverts this: the writer keeps the premium if the stock stays flat or falls, but faces unlimited loss if the stock rises. Buying a put gives a downward-sloping profile: limited to the premium paid on the upside, capped gain at the put strike minus premium on the downside. Selling a put creates the mirror image of a bought put.

Long call risk profile: maximum loss = premium paid (left of breakeven); unlimited theoretical gain above breakeven. [view]
Short (naked) call risk profile: maximum gain = premium received; unlimited theoretical loss as the stock rises. [view]
Long put risk profile: maximum loss = premium paid; maximum gain = strike price minus premium (if stock falls to zero). [view]
Short (naked) put risk profile: mirror image of the long put. Maximum gain = premium received; maximum loss = large if stock falls sharply. [view]
All four basic risk profiles together: long call, short call, long put, short put. Every options strategy combines or modifies these shapes. [view]

Cohen introduces a memory trick for constructing these profiles: multiplying signs algebraically. A “long” position is positive (+); a “short” position is negative (−). A call has an upward slope (+); a put has a downward slope (−). Long call: (+) × (+) = (+), an upward diagonal. Short call: (−) × (+) = (−), a downward diagonal capped at the received premium. This algebra works for all four, and once you know long call you can construct the others by symmetry.

Sign algebra for risk profiles: long (+) × call (+) = upward; short (−) × put (−) = upward from below. Knowing one profile lets you construct all four. [view]
Long call vs short call side by side: the buyer’s limited risk is the seller’s limited reward, and vice versa. [view]
Long put vs short put: the downward payoff of the long put becomes an upward (then capped) shape for the short put seller. [view]

Chapter 2: Into the Marketplace

Options are not abstract instruments — they trade on exchanges with real bid-ask spreads, expiration calendars, and contract sizes. One U.S. equity option contract represents 100 shares. A premium quoted at 1.45 therefore costs $145 in real money. In the UK, one contract typically represents 1,000 shares. Options symbols since 2010 encode all the information you need: AAPL130119C00550000 means Apple, expiring January 19, 2013, a call, strike $550.00.

A typical option chain: underlying, strike, bid, ask, volume, and open interest for each contract. Volume measures activity today; open interest measures total outstanding contracts. [view]
The standardized options symbol format post-2010: ticker + expiration date + call/put indicator + eight-digit strike price. [view]

Strike prices in U.S. markets are structured in $2.50 increments up to $25, $5 increments from $25 to $200, and $10 increments above $200. Large-cap stocks often have tighter increments and weekly options in addition to the standard monthly cycle. Expiration falls on the Saturday following the third Friday of each month, with actual trading ceasing on Friday. Weekly options have grown in popularity but remain less liquid than the standard monthlies.

Strike price increments by stock price range. The standardized structure ensures predictable spacing between options at different moneyness levels. [view]
Expiration cycle: the Saturday following the third Friday of the expiration month. Trading ceases Friday; exercise notification due Saturday. [view]

Margin rules create an important asymmetry between buying and selling options. Buyers must pay the full premium in cash — no margin allowed, because options already contain significant embedded leverage. Sellers (writers) of naked options must maintain a margin account and meet collateral requirements calculated as the greater of two formulas based on the stock price and the out-of-the-money amount. The proceeds from the option sale can be applied against the initial margin requirement.

Naked call margin: the greater of (a) 20% of stock value minus OTM amount plus premium, or (b) 10% of stock value plus premium. Option sale proceeds apply against initial margin. [view]
Naked put margin uses the same two-formula greater-of approach, substituting the put strike price in formula (b). [view]

Order types matter enormously for options traders. Market orders execute at the best available price but can fill at wide spreads in illiquid contracts. Limit orders are strongly recommended for options and especially for spreads, where you are simultaneously entering multiple legs. Stop orders are used for exit management: a sell stop triggers if the stock falls below a specified level, removing discretion from a painful decision. Fill-or-kill and Good-till-cancelled orders have different priority treatment by floor traders; day-only orders incentivize faster execution.

Order types: market, limit, stop, buy stop with limit, GTC, day-only, fill-or-kill, all-or-none. Limit orders are recommended for all options trades. [view]

Leverage is the central mathematical fact about options. Suppose stock ABCD trades at $20 and you buy the 25-strike call for $1.00 per share ($100 per contract). If the stock rises to $30, the call’s intrinsic value is $5 — a 400 percent gain on a 50 percent move in the stock. But leverage cuts both ways: if the stock falls to $15, your option expires worthless and you lose 100 percent, whereas the stock buyer has lost only 25 percent. The key is choosing the right strategy for the right scenario.

Leverage comparison: same dollar move in the stock produces a much larger percentage change in the option. Risk is symmetric — leverage amplifies losses as readily as gains. [view]
Delta as the speed of the option: ATM options move at half the speed of the underlying (δ ≈ 0.5). As an option moves deeper ITM, delta approaches 1.0. [view]
Delta neutral position: buying 100 shares (+100 deltas) hedged with two ATM puts (−50 deltas each) equals a net delta of zero. Delta neutral ≠ risk-free. [view]

Open interest and volume serve different diagnostic functions. Volume measures how many contracts traded today. Open interest measures how many contracts are outstanding and have not yet been closed, exercised, or expired. A rising open interest alongside rising volume and price confirms that new money is entering a trend, not just old positions being shuffled. Falling open interest with a price drop means longs are closing out rather than new shorts being initiated — a subtly different signal about the nature of the selling pressure. For options specifically, open interest below a few hundred contracts typically means wide bid-ask spreads and difficult exits; Cohen recommends minimum open interest of 500-1,000 contracts on the strike you plan to trade.

The bid-ask spread is the hidden cost that most options beginners underestimate. A stock option quoted at 3.00 bid, 3.20 ask costs you 3.20 to buy and earns you only 3.00 to sell immediately — a 6.7 percent round-trip friction before any stock movement occurs. For spread trades where you are simultaneously buying and selling options, this friction compounds across both legs. Cohen’s insistence on limit orders for all options trades is a direct response to this reality: a market order in an illiquid option can easily fill at the ask on entry and the bid on exit, costing several percent of the total premium before the trade has any chance to work.

Exercising an option is almost always inferior to selling it. When you own a call that has moved in-the-money, the option still contains time value on top of its intrinsic value. Exercising it converts 100 percent of that value into stock ownership but destroys the time value entirely. Selling the option instead captures both components. The only scenario where early exercise of a call makes mathematical sense is if the underlying stock is about to pay a dividend large enough to exceed the option’s remaining time value. Understanding this clarifies why the vast majority of equity options are never exercised — they are bought and sold as instruments in their own right.

A full option chain showing calls and puts at multiple strikes with bid, ask, volume, and open interest. Wider bid-ask spreads signal lower liquidity. [view]
Stop loss placement: base stops on the underlying stock price at a meaningful technical level, not on the option premium (which moves faster and can stop you out prematurely). [view]
The five numbers every trade requires: maximum risk, maximum reward, breakeven point(s), maximum acceptable loss, and profit target. Know these before you enter. [view]

Chapter 3: The Basics of Fundamental Analysis

Fundamental analysis is the study of a company through its revenues, profits, assets, and borrowings. Most financial ratios are manipulations of these four items. But Cohen’s key insight is that stock prices are driven not by the numbers themselves but by expectations about what those numbers will be. Expectations are formed by sentiment, and sentiment is shaped by news and past history. A company can report record profits and watch its stock fall because the market had expected even better.

The chain of influence: news and past history → sentiment → expectations → stock price. Each layer filters through the one above it. [view]

The broader economy provides the essential backdrop. Benjamin Graham’s “Mr. Market” — the manic-depressive business partner who offers you prices each morning based on his mood rather than intrinsic value — is Cohen’s framework for market sentiment. Statistics confirm that 50 percent of any individual stock’s movement is attributable to overall market direction, and a further 30 percent to its sector. If you can correctly identify the direction of the market and the sector, you have already explained 80 percent of the stock’s likely move before analyzing the company at all.

50% of a stock’s movement = overall market direction; 30% = sector direction. Correct top-down assessment dramatically improves trading odds. [view]
Sector influence: stocks within a rising sector benefit from collective momentum, just as individual stocks suffer when their sector is under pressure. [view]

Key U.S. economic indicators release on predictable schedules: the CPI on roughly the 13th of each month, the Employment Report on the first Friday, GDP in the third or fourth week after quarter-end, Housing Starts around the 16th. Bonds and stocks have an inverse relationship through interest rates: strong bond prices (low yields) support equity valuations; weak bonds (rising yields) compress multiples. Cohen’s rule of thumb: yields above 6.75 percent strain equities; yields below 3.5 percent support them.

Major U.S. economic indicators with typical release schedules. Trading around these releases requires awareness of the expected vs actual dynamic. [view]

Corporate fundamentals require careful reading. Cohen walks through a vivid example of share dilution: Takeoverco acquires Targetco by issuing 2.5 million new shares. Total profits rise from $25 million to $30 million — impressive on the surface. But earnings per share fall from $2.50 to $2.40 because the denominator (share count) grew faster than the numerator (earnings). The acquiring company’s shareholders are actually worse off. The lesson: always track EPS growth, not just total earnings growth.

Dilution example: company profits rise but EPS falls after a rights issue. Total earnings growth is a misleading headline without per-share context. [view]

The PE ratio (price divided by earnings per share) represents the number of years of current earnings required to justify the stock price. A higher PE means investors rate the company more highly and expect future earnings growth to justify today’s price. Growth companies can sustain extreme PEs until the growth fails to materialize, at which point PE compression is swift and painful. Revenue growth should precede earnings growth in any durable business; a company growing earnings by cutting costs is on a different trajectory than one growing earnings by selling more products.

PE ratio: years of current earnings implied in the stock price. High PE = high investor expectations; low PE = skepticism or declining growth. [view]

William O’Neil’s CAN SLIM formula provides one of the finest stock-screening frameworks Cohen knows: Current earnings growth, Annual earnings growth rate, New products or management, Supply and demand in volume, Leader or laggard, Institutional sponsorship, Market direction. This seven-factor filter, applied rigorously, dramatically narrows the universe of stocks to genuinely high-quality candidates. Cohen also endorses the Peter Lynch approach of noticing products becoming ubiquitous in everyday life and researching who makes them.

CAN SLIM: seven factors for identifying high-quality stocks. Created by William O’Neil of How to Make Money in Stocks fame. [view]
Simple fundamental filters: consistent EPS growth, rising revenues, manageable debt, uptrending sector. Apply before selecting options strategy direction. [view]

The problem with sell-side analyst research is structural, not individual. Brokerage analysts have historically maintained a bullish bias because their firms derive revenue from investment banking relationships with the companies they cover. A “sell” rating on a corporate client’s stock damages that relationship; a “buy” costs nothing. Cohen advises treating analyst upgrades and downgrades as sentiment data — what is the consensus believing? — rather than as independent research. The contrarian opportunity frequently appears when a company with deteriorating fundamentals is still widely rated a “buy” by the Street, or when a recovering company is still widely rated a “hold.”

Cohen’s ultimate message about fundamental analysis is pragmatic: use it to filter for quality and direction, but never forecast. Warren Buffett’s warning hangs over the entire chapter: “Forecasting tells you much about the forecaster and nothing about the future.” The job of the fundamental analyst is to identify companies with strong underlying dynamics; the job of the technician is to identify when the market is ready to recognize that value. Both disciplines are incomplete without the other.

Chapter 4: The Basics of Technical Analysis

Technical analysis has three components in Cohen’s framework: recognizing price patterns, interpreting them for timing, and implementing a trading plan around the interpretation. It comes in two flavors — price patterns (visual structures in raw price movement) and technical indicators (mathematical algorithms applied to price and volume). The chapter is the longest in the book because charting skill is the foundation of everything that follows; without it, you cannot find the setups the strategies require.

Three ways to view price: line chart (close only), bar chart (OHLC), and Japanese candlestick (body + wicks). Each reveals a different level of detail about intraday price behavior. [view]

Japanese candlesticks are Cohen’s preferred chart type. Each candle’s body spans from open to close; wicks extend to the session’s extremes. An up-candle (hollow or white body) shows the close above the open; a down-candle (filled or dark body) shows the close below. Two specific patterns are invaluable for reversal signals. The doji forms when open and close are within 20 percent of the bar’s total range, indicating indecision between buyers and sellers. The hammer has a small body at one extreme of a long bar, showing that one side tried hard but failed. Both become dramatically more significant when they appear at price extremes — 20-day highs or lows.

Up candlestick: hollow or white body from open to close. Wicks show intraday extremes above and below the body. [view]
Down candlestick: filled or dark body where close is below the open. Long lower wick suggests buying pressure reversed early selling. [view]
Doji candlestick: open and close within 20% of bar range. Signals indecision; most powerful at price extremes on high relative volume. [view]
Hammer bar: small body at one extreme of a long candlestick. At a 20-day low, indicates that sellers tried to push lower but buyers reclaimed most of the ground. [view]

Support and resistance are the bedrock of practical technical analysis. Support is a price level where buying pressure has historically been strong enough to stop a decline; resistance is where selling pressure has historically capped a rally. These levels persist because the same traders who bought at support buy again when price returns, and those who were burned by resistance sell again when they get a second chance. A critically important corollary: when a support level is decisively broken, it frequently becomes resistance on subsequent rallies. And when resistance is broken, it frequently becomes support on the subsequent pullback.

Support and resistance levels: price bounces upward from support and downward from resistance. Broken support often becomes new resistance (and vice versa). [view]
False breakout: price briefly breaches a support or resistance level on above-average volume, then snaps back. Often caused by specialists triggering stop orders clustered just outside key levels. [view]

Double tops form when a rising stock makes a second peak that fails to exceed the first. The failure to make a new high is itself the weakness signal. The entry rule: wait for (a) a clear double top, (b) the rising trendline to break, and (c) the prior bottom between the two peaks to be breached. Cohen uses a real PCLN (Priceline) example from April-May 2012: the double top was textbook, and the stock fell over $70 in a single month once the formation completed. Triple tops follow the same logic with three peaks.

Double top: second peak fails to exceed the first. Confirmation signal is the break below the trough between the two peaks. [view]
PCLN double top April-May 2012: the pattern resolved into a $70+ decline in one month. Real examples anchor the theory in observable market behavior. [view]
Double bottom: second low fails to breach the first. Strength signal; confirm with a trendline break and breach of the prior peak between the two lows. [view]
Head and shoulders: central peak (head) flanked by two lower peaks (shoulders). Minimum expected decline equals the distance from neckline to the head. [view]
Goldman Sachs head and shoulders Feb-May 2012. The pattern played out precisely: neckline break confirmed the reversal. [view]
Reverse head and shoulders: middle trough (reverse head) flanked by two higher troughs (reverse shoulders). Minimum expected gain = neckline to reverse head distance. [view]
PCLN reverse head and shoulders Nov 2011-Jan 2012: the pattern suggested a $60 minimum move. The actual breakout delivered over $250. Minimum targets are floors, not ceilings. [view]
Pennant: lower highs and higher lows converging symmetrically. Decreasing volatility makes pennants excellent setups for straddles; either direction is possible. [view]
Ascending triangle (flat top, rising bottom) = bullish continuation. Descending triangle (flat bottom, falling top) = bearish continuation. [view]
Flag pattern: a brief, tight consolidation interrupting a strong trending move. Two parallel trendlines form the flag; the dominant trend resumes on breakout. [view]
AAPL bull flags Dec 2011-Feb 2012: multiple flags formed as the stock stair-stepped higher. Each flag breakout offered a new entry with a well-defined stop. [view]
Cup and handle: bowl-shaped base followed by a brief bull flag (the handle). The left lip of the cup becomes the resistance level to break through for confirmation. [view]
Reverse cup and handle: inverted bowl with a brief bear flag. Bearish continuation pattern; mirror image of cup and handle. [view]
Channel lines: parallel trendlines connecting price bar lows (lower channel) and highs (upper channel). Cohen used a one-year ALTR channel to execute a bull put spread yielding 74% in under one month. [view]
Fibonacci retracement levels: 38.2%, 50%, 61.8%. Key support/resistance levels derived from the golden ratio. Cohen advises using sparingly and only as one of several confirming factors. [view]
Four Fibonacci scenarios: upward retracement in downtrend, downward retracement in uptrend, retracement followed by reversal. The direction of the primary trend determines interpretation. [view]
Elliott Wave: five-wave impulse (1,2,3,4,5) followed by three-wave correction (A,B,C). Cohen’s assessment: no two experts can agree on wave count until after the pattern is over. [view]
Gann levels (G1-G4): percentage-based support and resistance. G1 is most significant. Cohen’s experience: hit-and-miss in practice. [view]
Four gap types: breakaway (new trend begins), continuation (measured gap mid-trend), exhaustion (end of trend), and island reversal (price gaps away then gaps back). [view]
PMC Sierra Inc showing multiple gap types on one chart. Gaps often fill because outstanding orders remain at those price levels, but not all gaps fill. [view]
Volume analysis: rising price with increasing volume confirms a sustainable move. Rising price with declining volume suggests the move may not be durable. [view]
Volume spikes: bars exceeding the 21-day average by 50%+. Combined with a doji bar, a volume spike is one of the most powerful reversal signals available. [view]

Moving averages are the most widely known indicator, but Cohen uses them sparingly. In strongly trending markets they work beautifully: when a short moving average crosses above a long moving average, the trend is confirmed bullish, and vice versa. He cites AMZN from 1998 to 2000, where the 10/40-week crossover perfectly captured the entire move. But in sideways markets, moving averages generate endless false signals — FNM from 1999 is his counter-example. Longer periods (200, 50 days) filter more noise but react slower.

AMZN 1998-2000: the 10/40-week moving average crossover captured the entire bull run with minimal false signals on the weekly chart. [view]
FNM in a sideways market: moving average crossovers produce constant whipsaw signals. Moving averages are trend-following tools, not range-trading tools. [view]
MACD (Moving Average Convergence Divergence): measures the relationship between two moving averages. Three signals: crossovers, overbought/oversold, and divergence from price. [view]
Stochastics: oscillator measuring closing price vs high-low range. Overbought above 75, oversold below 25. Problem: in strong trends, readings stay extreme for extended periods, causing premature exit signals. [view]
RSI (Relative Strength Index): oscillates 0-100. Midline 50: above = bullish, below = bearish. RSI divergence (price new high but RSI fails) is one of the strongest reversal signals in technical analysis. [view]
The OVI indicator: Cohen’s proprietary tool measuring options transaction activity plotted under a stock chart. Oscillates between −1 and +1; no lag; almost completely objective. Full treatment in Chapter 12. [view]

The deeper logic of flags is worth dwelling on. A flag forms because a strong trending move cannot sustain its velocity indefinitely. Buyers who entered the trend need to breathe; some take partial profits; volume contracts. The brief pause forms the flag — parallel trendlines at a shallow angle against the dominant trend. What makes a flag powerful for options is that the price range is compressing, which directly reduces historical volatility for that stock. Lower historical volatility tends to push down implied volatility, which makes buying options cheaper precisely when a breakout is approaching. The flag is simultaneously a technical entry signal and a volatility-based bargain indicator.

False breakouts deserve more attention than most technical analysis texts give them. They occur when price briefly crosses a support or resistance level on higher volume, triggering stop orders clustered just beyond the level, and then snaps back. This is not random noise — it is often the result of market makers and specialists triggering those stops deliberately, buying shares being sold by panicked stopouts, and then letting the stock recover. The tell is timing: false breakouts typically reverse within 30 minutes of the break. Cohen’s practical solution is to place stop-limit orders just beyond the breakout level rather than market orders, and to use conditional entry orders that require the price to hold above/below the level for a brief period before triggering the full position.

Cohen’s most important conclusion about technical analysis is about focus: master a small number of patterns that work reliably, and ignore everything else. His core toolkit is just three setups — consolidations and flags, support and resistance breakouts, and reversal doji bars at price extremes. Most of the other patterns and indicators are worth knowing conceptually, but spending equal time on all of them dilutes the trader who tries to use all of them simultaneously.

Chapter 5: Synthetic Calls, Covered Calls & Collars

Cohen’s first two strategy chapters cover approaches that most stock investors have probably heard of without fully understanding their structure. The synthetic call — sometimes called a protective put — combines a long stock position with a long put, creating a risk profile that looks like a bought call: unlimited upside, capped downside. Why “synthetic”? Because the shape of the payoff diagram is identical to an outright long call, even though the components are entirely different.

The key insight is about risk as a percentage of capital at risk, not nominal dollar risk. A straight call on a $25 stock might cost $5.25 per share. A synthetic call — buying the stock at $25 and protecting it with a 25-strike put at $4.50 — costs a net debit of $29.50. In dollar terms the synthetic call is far more expensive. But the maximum loss on the synthetic call is just $4.50 (the put strike equals the stock price, so there is no unprotected downside gap), whereas the straight call’s maximum loss is $5.25. Lower maximum loss, same unlimited upside: the synthetic call wins on risk-adjusted terms.

Synthetic call (stock + put) risk profile: same upward-sloping shape as a long call. Maximum loss is limited to the put premium paid (plus any gap between stock price and put strike). [view]
Comparison: straight call vs synthetic call on the same stock. The synthetic call has a higher absolute cost but lower maximum loss as a percentage of capital deployed. [view]

The synthetic call has a counterintuitive advantage in volatile conditions. When a stock gaps down sharply at the open after bad news, the straight call buyer loses the entire premium in an instant. But the synthetic call holder owns the stock — which gaps down to the same loss — yet is simultaneously protected by the put, which explodes in value as the stock drops through the strike. The net loss on the synthetic call in a gap-down scenario is capped at the put premium plus any distance between the stock price and the put strike. This gap protection is not available to the buyer of a standard call option, whose loss is already fully capped at the premium paid. The synthetic call outperforms on the downside in catastrophic scenarios precisely when the investor needs the protection most.

The choice of put strike determines the tradeoff. A higher put strike (at-the-money) provides maximum insurance and lowest risk, but raises the breakeven significantly because you pay more for the put. A lower put strike reduces the insurance cost and lowers the breakeven, but leaves a gap between the stock purchase price and the strike where losses accumulate uncovered. The right choice depends on where strong technical support lies: the put strike should ideally align with a meaningful support level below the current price.

The covered call is the most popular options strategy among stock owners who want to generate monthly income. You buy (or already own) the stock and sell call options against it. If the stock stays flat or falls modestly, you keep the entire premium. If the stock rises above the sold strike, you deliver the shares at that price — capturing the gain up to the strike plus the premium, but no more. The maximum reward is capped; the maximum risk is still the full stock price minus the premium received.

Covered call and naked put have identical risk profile shapes. Key difference: covered call requires full stock purchase capital; naked put requires only margin. Same risk, different capital requirements. [view]

The covered call writer needs to decide how aggressively to sell. Rolling the sold call forward each month — buying back the expiring short call and selling the next month ’s — is the core operational rhythm. If the stock rallies hard and approaches the strike with two weeks remaining, the call can be “rolled up and out”: close the current month position at a loss on the short call, and sell the next month at a higher strike. This sacrifices some premium income but allows participation in the continued rally. If the stock falls significantly, the short call expires worthless (full premium kept) but the stock loss may exceed that income. The covered call does not protect against a major decline; it only reduces the breakeven by the premium received. For genuine downside protection, the covered call must be combined with a bought put — which is how the collar is constructed.

A concrete example: BORT stock at $28.10, selling 8 contracts of the 30-strike April call at $0.85 each. Buy 800 shares ($22,480) and collect $680 in premiums, for a net outlay of $21,800. If the stock rises to $30 or above, the calls get exercised and you deliver shares at $30 — an effective yield of about 9.8 percent. If the stock stays at $28.10, you keep the full $680 premium and can sell calls again next month. Doing this every month on a stable, high-quality stock is the closest thing to a salary that the options market offers.

The practical challenge of collar construction is finding the right balance between the premium collected on the short call and the premium paid for the long put. In normal market conditions, put implied volatility trades at a premium to call implied volatility (the volatility skew), meaning puts are more expensive relative to their delta than equivalent calls. This makes zero-risk collars difficult to construct except on high-volatility stocks with long-dated (LEAP) options where the premium differentials are large enough to offset the skew. For most stocks in most market conditions, the collar achieves near-zero rather than true zero risk. Cohen’s examples show a “low risk” collar (1.5% max risk) and a zero-risk collar as the ideal. The investor must decide how much residual risk is acceptable and select strikes accordingly.

The collar adds a bought put to the covered call, protecting the downside while capping the upside. The goal is to create a position where maximum risk is zero or near zero. Cohen’s formula: risk = (put premium paid minus call premium received) plus (stock price minus put strike). If this sum is zero or negative, the trade is riskless (assuming the options are properly priced). In practice, zero-risk collars typically require high-volatility stocks with LEAP options (one year or more out) and entry at a strong support level.

Chapter 6: An Introduction to the Greeks

The Greeks are the five sensitivities that govern how an option’s price changes in response to changes in the underlying price, time, volatility, and interest rates. Each is named after a Greek letter, and each answers a specific practical question: how fast will my option move with the stock, how quickly will that speed change, how much am I losing to time each day, how does a volatility shift affect my position, and does the interest rate environment matter to my trade? Understanding the Greeks transforms options from black boxes into predictable instruments.

Delta is the most important Greek for most traders. It measures how much the option price changes for each one-point move in the underlying. An at-the-money option has a delta of approximately 0.5, meaning it moves about 50 cents for every dollar the stock moves. A deep in-the-money call approaches delta 1.0 and behaves almost like owning the stock outright. An out-of-the-money call approaches delta zero and barely responds to stock price changes. Delta is also a proxy for the probability that the option will expire in-the-money: an ATM option with delta 0.5 has roughly a 50 percent chance of finishing ITM.

Long call delta profile: delta starts near zero for deep OTM options, rises to ~0.5 at ATM, and approaches 1.0 for deep ITM options. The S-shaped curve shows delta is highest near-the-money. [view]
Long put delta profile: mirror image of the call delta. ATM put delta ≈ −0.5; deep ITM puts approach delta −1.0; deep OTM puts approach zero. [view]

The leverage example makes delta’s practical importance concrete. Buy 100 shares of a $50 stock for $5,000. Alternatively, buy one call contract at $7 for $700. If the stock rises to $55, both positions gain $500. But the stock position gains 10 percent on capital deployed; the options position gains 71 percent. If the stock falls to $45, both lose $500 in dollar terms, but the stock position loses 10 percent while the options position loses 71 percent. Same dollar swing, radically different percentage exposure. Delta translates that leverage into a precise number.

Leverage example: $500 gain on a $5,000 stock position = 10%. Same $500 gain on a $700 options position = 71%. The percentage exposure is what makes options powerful — and dangerous when misused. [view]
Delta neutral: sum of all deltas = zero. Buy 100 shares (+100 deltas); buy 2 ATM puts (−50 each) = net zero. The position neither gains nor loses on a small stock move, but is exposed to larger moves and to gamma. [view]
Bull call spread delta profile: the delta of the spread is smaller than a straight long call. Slowed daily fluctuations give you time to exit before serious damage if the stock moves against you. [view]

Gamma is the “speed of speed” — the second derivative of the option price relative to the underlying. It measures how quickly delta changes as the stock moves. An option with high gamma will see its delta change rapidly; an option with low gamma will see its delta change slowly. Gamma is highest when an option is near-the-money and close to expiration. Deep ITM or OTM options have very low gamma. Critically: gamma is identical for calls and puts at the same strike and expiration. A long put and a long call at the same strike have exactly the same gamma profile.

Long call gamma profile: bell-shaped curve, highest near the current stock price. As expiration approaches, the peak sharpens dramatically — gamma explodes near ATM in the final days. [view]
Long put gamma profile: identical to the long call gamma. Gamma is the same for puts and calls at the same strike and expiration — it measures delta sensitivity, not option direction. [view]

Theta is the Greek most options buyers need to fear. It measures the daily rate at which an option’s time value erodes, all else being equal. For bought options, theta is negative — every day that passes without a favorable stock move costs you money. For sold options, theta is positive — time is your ally. The decay is non-linear. An option that loses about $0.10 per day with 90 days left will lose far more per day in its final two weeks. The curve steepens dramatically in the last 30 days, which is why Cohen’s hard rule is clear: never hold a bought OTM option with less than one month to expiration unless it is part of a spread.

Theta time decay curve: relatively flat in the early months, then steepening rapidly in the final 30 days. This acceleration is why bought OTM options become extremely difficult to profit from as expiration nears. [view]
Long call theta profile: negative throughout. The absolute value of theta is highest when the option is near-the-money — ATM options lose the most time value per day. [view]
Long put theta profile: also negative. Option buyers pay time decay continuously; sold options accrue the same amount as credit. [view]
Deep ITM options: dominated by intrinsic value with minimal time value to decay. ATM options: dominated by time value, most exposed to theta. This is why deep ITM options are preferred when buying short-term options. [view]

The relationship between theta and strike moneyness has an important practical consequence. Deep ITM options have very little time value — their premium is almost entirely intrinsic. This means theta barely touches them. A deep ITM call on a $100 stock with a $70 strike has intrinsic value of $30; perhaps $0.50 of time value. That $0.50 decays to zero over months, but it is not the dominant concern. An ATM call on the same stock with a $100 strike might have $5 of time value, all of which is at the mercy of theta. Cohen uses this asymmetry explicitly: when he wants short-term directional exposure, he buys deep ITM options — paying mostly for intrinsic value that will not erode — rather than ATM or OTM options where time decay is the primary enemy.

Vega measures sensitivity to changes in implied volatility. When volatility rises, option premiums rise; when volatility falls, they fall. Long options (bought calls or puts) have positive vega: you benefit from rising volatility because your positions become more valuable. Sold options have negative vega: rising volatility hurts you because the options you sold become more expensive. Cohen explains the key concept: implied volatility is derived by reverse-engineering the options pricing model with the actual market price of the option. Unlike historical volatility (which you can calculate from past prices), IV is what the market currently expects volatility to be going forward.

Black-Scholes model: six known inputs (stock price, strike, type, expiration, interest rate, dividends) plus one unknown (expected volatility). When you solve for volatility using the actual market option price, you get implied volatility. [view]
The rubber band effect: implied volatility tends to revert toward its own historical average over time. When IV stretches far below its average, options may be cheap; when it stretches far above, options may be expensive. [view]
Long call vega profile: positive and highest near the money. Rising volatility benefits the long call holder; falling volatility hurts. [view]
Long put vega profile: identical to long call vega. Vega is the same for calls and puts at the same strike and expiration, just as gamma is. [view]
Rho profile for long call: slightly positive (higher rates modestly benefit call holders). Rho is the least significant Greek for most practical options trading. [view]
Rho profile for long put: negative (higher interest rates modestly reduce put premiums). In low-rate environments, rho has negligible practical impact. [view]
Greeks summary: delta (speed), gamma (acceleration of speed), theta (time decay), vega (volatility sensitivity), rho (interest rate sensitivity). Together, they describe how an option position will behave under all market conditions. [view]

The Greeks do not operate independently. A position’s overall behavior is the sum of its delta, gamma, theta, and vega exposures interacting simultaneously. A straddle is delta-neutral, positive gamma, negative theta, and positive vega: it sits still in a quiet market (losing money to theta each day) but gains rapidly when the stock moves big (positive gamma quickly builds delta) or when implied volatility spikes (positive vega directly increases premium). A bull call spread is positive delta, reduced positive gamma, negative theta, and positive but smaller vega than a straight long call — because the sold upper call offsets each Greek partially. Understanding the full Greek profile of a position tells you precisely which market conditions will reward you and which will hurt you, before you enter the trade.

Chapter 7: Bull Call Spreads and Bull Put Spreads

Spread strategies transform the risk profile of a single option by adding a second leg in the opposite direction. For bullish trades, there are two fundamental constructions. The bull call spread is a net-debit strategy for long-term investors: buy a lower-strike call, sell a higher-strike call at the same expiration. The bull put spread is a net-credit strategy for short-term income: sell a higher-strike put, buy a lower-strike put at the same expiration. Cohen’s memory rule: bull spreads always involve buying the low strike and selling the high strike, regardless of whether calls or puts are used.

The distinction between bull call and bull put spreads is not just about calls vs puts — it is about which side of the time-value equation you want to be on. A net-debit strategy (bull call spread) pays premium to be long; time decay costs you money every day that passes without the stock moving. A net-credit strategy (bull put spread) collects premium to be short the expensive option; time decay earns you money every day. This fundamental difference in theta sign means the two strategies should never be used interchangeably. The question “should I use a bull call spread or a bull put spread?” is not about which is better but about which time horizon you are operating on. Long term (6-12 months): bull call spread. Short term (monthly income): bull put spread.

The bull call spread has three effects compared to a straight long call. First, it reduces the cost of entry (the sold call offsets part of the bought call’s premium), which reduces both risk and breakeven. Second, it caps maximum profit at the sold strike. Third, it hedges delta, slowing down the daily fluctuation of the position. That third point is more valuable than it sounds: if you buy a straight ATM call and the stock moves against you, the position can be decimated in a single day. With a spread, the slowed delta response gives you time to exit before suffering serious damage.

A bear call spread sells the lower call and buys the higher call (net credit, short-term income, profits if stock stays below the short strike). Chapter 8: Straddles and Strangles

The straddle and strangle belong to a broader category of positive gamma strategies: positions that gain delta as the stock moves in either direction. This is the opposite of being a premium seller (negative gamma), where you are effectively short volatility and gain from stillness rather than movement. Cohen’s framework for choosing between buying and selling volatility starts with the IV comparison. If current IV is below its own 3-12 month average, options are relatively cheap — buy volatility (straddle/strangle). If current IV is well above its average, options are relatively expensive — sell volatility (covered call, bull put spread, or if you are very experienced, iron condor). This IV-relative-to-its-history framework prevents the most common volatility-trading mistake: buying expensive options or selling cheap ones because you only looked at absolute levels rather than context.

Straddles and strangles are the two core volatility strategies in Cohen’s toolkit: both profit from large price moves in either direction without requiring you to predict which way the stock will go. A straddle buys an ATM call and an ATM put at the same strike and expiration. A strangle buys an OTM call and an OTM put at different strikes. Both are net-debit trades; both profit only if the stock moves far enough to cover the cost of the options. The difference is cost vs breakeven: the strangle is cheaper but requires a larger move to turn profitable.

For a straddle to work, four criteria must align. First, implied volatility should be below its own historical average — you want to buy when options are cheap, not after they have already risen in anticipation of news. Second, the stock should be showing a clear consolidation pattern on the chart: narrowing price bars, converging trendlines, or an obvious flag. Consolidation depresses historical volatility, and depressed historical volatility tends to compress implied volatility, lowering the cost of the straddle. Third, the stock should be above $10 to give the put room to travel. Fourth, timing should put you one to two weeks before a known catalyst such as earnings, with exactly three months of expiration remaining.

The timing of entry relative to news events is more nuanced than it first appears. Cohen’s rule is to enter before the news, not after. The reason is implied volatility dynamics: in the week or two before a major catalyst such as earnings, IV typically rises as market participants buy options to hedge or speculate. This IV expansion directly inflates straddle premiums. If you enter one to two weeks early, you capture the cheaper options before this expansion. If you wait until the day before earnings, you are paying peak premium — and then when earnings are announced and the uncertainty resolves, IV collapses (the IV crush), often destroying the straddle’s value even if the stock makes a reasonable move. Timing the entry to exploit the pre-announcement IV rise rather than suffer the post-announcement IV collapse is the difference between a good and poor straddle entry.

The 60-day rule is Cohen’s quantitative filter for three-month straddles. The straddle’s total cost must be less than [(60-day high minus 60-day low) divided by 2]. This tests whether the recent price range is wide enough to cover the straddle premium if repeated. For a two-month straddle use a 40-day rule; for a four-month straddle use an 80-day rule. If the straddle costs more than the filtered range permits, pass on the trade — the options are too expensive relative to the stock’s actual movement history.

Four exit scenarios govern straddle management. If no big move materializes after the news event, exit within days to recover most of the time value before decay accelerates. If a big move occurs, you can close the profitable leg and hold the unprofitable leg in case of a retracement. Never hold an ATM option with less than one month to expiration — this rule overrides everything else. Set a profit target (Cohen typically uses 50 percent) and exit mechanically when it is hit, regardless of what the stock appears to be doing.

The HOV (Hovnanian Enterprises) story from May 2004 is the companion lesson to EXPE. HOV was perfectly set up: earnings upcoming, Federal Reserve rate announcement approaching, stock forming a tight consolidation. Cohen entered the straddle. Earnings came in way ahead of expectations; the stock rallied $4 then retraced. The Fed announcement was in line with expectations; the stock tried to break out repeatedly and failed each time. Cohen had learned from EXPE — he knew to be patient and not exit early just because the position was not immediately profitable. But HOV simply would not move. He held through weeks of agonizing stasis, watching theta decay chip away at the premium. Finally, in early July, HOV plunged, and the position turned a modest profit. The two stories together map out the full psychological terrain: EXPE punished impatience; HOV tested patience to breaking point. Both test the trader’s commitment to the plan.

Cohen’s most painful lesson about straddles came from EXPE (Expedia) in March 2003. The Gulf War was imminent, earnings were approaching, and the stock had formed a textbook consolidation. He bought the July 35 straddle at 8.20 and exited two days later at a +10 percent profit when the stock fell to support. He then had to travel. The stock subsequently rose over $20 following an acquisition announcement plus earnings beat. The straddle would have been worth approximately 24 — a 300 percent gain. “Even though I made a profitable trade,” Cohen writes, “the consequences of disobeying my trading plan made it a bitter disappointment.” The lesson: set your exit criteria before you enter, and do not change them because of travel plans or distractions.

Chapter 9: Butterflies and Condors

The stock selection criteria for sideways strategies are the mirror image of straddle criteria. For straddles, you want low IV (cheap options) and an imminent catalyst (expected big move). For butterflies and condors, you want high IV (expensive options that will decay in your favor) and the absence of any catalyst (no expected big move). Stocks forming tight multi-week ranges with clearly defined support and resistance, high IV relative to their own history, and no earnings or major news events scheduled within the trade’s lifespan are the candidates. This requirement is precise and demanding; it means sideways strategies are not applicable to most stocks at most times. When all five criteria align — range, defined support and resistance, high IV, no news, one-to-two month expiration — the probability of success rises meaningfully.

If straddles and strangles profit from big moves, butterflies and condors profit from the opposite: a stock that stays exactly where it is. These are the two basic sideways strategies, and they are among the most complex multi-leg structures in the options world. Both are net-debit trades. Both generate maximum profit at a specific price (butterfly) or across a range (condor) at expiration. Both have maximum loss equal to 100 percent of the premium paid.

A key timing insight governs both strategies. The optimal time to expiration for sideways strategies is one to two months. With more than two months remaining, the stock has too much time to breach the wings — the narrow profit zone of a butterfly or condor can easily be violated by a trending move. With less than one month remaining, the deep ITM leg of the butterfly becomes very expensive (its time value has largely decayed but the intrinsic value makes it costly to purchase), inflating the net debit to the point where the maximum return becomes insufficient. The one-to-two month window is the sweet spot where the deep ITM leg is affordable, the short-term theta decay is working forcefully in your favor, and the wings are far enough out to capture realistic price ranges.

A butterfly using calls requires three strikes and three legs: buy one ITM call, sell two ATM calls, buy one OTM call. The sold ATM calls generate premium that offsets much of the cost of the two bought calls. The risk profile has a distinctive mountain peak: maximum profit occurs only if the stock is exactly at the middle (ATM) strike at expiration. As the stock moves away from that peak in either direction, profit declines until the position reaches maximum loss at the outer wings. Put butterflies use the same structure inverted.

The iron condor — the net-credit cousin of the standard condor — is not covered in depth by Cohen but deserves mention as the most popular professional implementation of the sideways strategy concept. Instead of buying the expensive deep ITM options at the wings (which makes the condor a net-debit trade), the iron condor sells an OTM call spread and an OTM put spread simultaneously, collecting premium on both sides. Maximum profit is the total credit received; maximum loss is the spread width minus the credit. The iron condor generates positive theta (time works for you) and negative vega (low volatility environments favor it), making it the ideal strategy when IV is high and the stock is expected to stay within a defined range. It is structurally the professional’s choice for income generation in sideways markets because the net credit eliminates the “pay now, profit later” psychology of the debit condor.

Chapter 10: Trading and Investing Psychology

“80 percent of trading success is psychology; 20 percent is technical ability.” Cohen does not know who first calculated that ratio, but he agrees with the principle completely. He has met brilliant quantitative analysts who cannot trade because they cannot manage the emotional experience of watching money flow in and out. He has also met modestly educated people with no formal background who became consistently profitable traders because they learned to master their internal states. Technique without psychology is a plan that exists only on paper.

Trading myths vs realities: “you can get rich quickly” vs consistent slow accumulation; “options are dangerous” vs dangerous only when misused; “you must predict the market” vs recognize, not predict. [view]

Among Cohen’s 22 behavioral guidelines, several address specific failure modes that repeat across traders. The warning against message boards is pointed: online forums are heavily populated by promoters and short-sellers talking their positions, with the legitimate participants drowned out by noise. The warning against black-box systems is equally direct: any static algorithm optimized on historical data becomes progressively less effective as market conditions change. The market adapts; a rule set that worked from 2000-2005 may fail from 2006-2010. The warning against simulated results in advertising is perhaps the most practically important: curve-fitting a system to historical data creates the appearance of extraordinary past performance that cannot be replicated going forward. The only valid test is real-time paper trading at actual bid/ask prices with realistic commission costs.

Cohen’s most repeated observation about aspiring traders: “I would have made a profit, but I keep breaking my rules.” He hears this constantly. The follow-up question is always the same: are your rules actually worth keeping? Rules should embrace entry conditions, position sizing, trade management, and exit conditions. They should not be aspirational guidelines that evaporate under market pressure. The only rules worth keeping are the ones you have tested, understand the reasoning behind, and can execute without flinching.

Cohen’s 22 trading tips: from “get into a relaxed state before analyzing” to “beware advertorials with simulated results.” Psychological rules outnumber technical rules by roughly three to one. [view]

The money management rules embedded in this chapter deserve quantitative framing. Cohen’s guideline: never risk more than 5 percent of capital on a single trade (10 percent for accounts under $20,000). At 5 percent per trade, you would need 20 consecutive maximum losses to wipe out a portfolio — statistically near-impossible if each trade is independently selected. This means the trading account survives almost indefinitely as long as position sizing is maintained. The corollary: “success is defined as still being in the game after two years.” Most new traders fail not because their stock picks are consistently wrong but because a single over-sized loss destroys the capital base before their skills can develop. Survival is the precondition for profitability, and position sizing is the mechanism that ensures survival.

The optimal trading mindset has three components: relaxed, confident, and in control. Relaxation means freedom from physical tension: your muscles should be completely unclenched, your breathing slow and deep. Confidence comes from having a tested, credible trading plan — not from arrogance, but from legitimacy. You have paper-traded this system; you understand its edge; you know what to do in each of the five possible outcomes. Control is the product of relaxation and confidence: a virtuous cycle where being relaxed and confident makes you more capable of controlling your decisions, which in turn reinforces both.

The 22 tips contain one observation that deserves special emphasis: “loss of opportunity is not loss of capital.” This reframe is powerful because it addresses the emotional cost of watching a stock you analyzed rise without you. Missing a trade feels like a loss. But no cash left your account; no position moved against you. The fear of missing out (FOMO) is the single most common reason traders enter setups that do not fully meet their criteria, use market orders instead of limit orders to ensure they “get in,” or size up to compensate for a previously missed opportunity. Cohen’s counter-instruction is to ask: if I spotted a great opportunity and did not act, why not? What criteria were not met? Answering that question honestly is more valuable than chasing the stock.

Cohen teaches an NLP-derived technique for building this state reproducibly. First, breathe: long, slow, deep breaths filling the stomach, exhaled as slowly as possible. Second, recall a moment of genuine confidence — not necessarily in trading, but in anything (cooking a complex meal, finishing a difficult paper). Replay the memory with full sensory detail: the colors, the brightness, the sounds. Amplify it. Add a soundtrack. Step into the image until you are inside the memory, not watching it from outside. Third, at the peak of that state, trigger a physical anchor: click your fingers, or pinch the back of your hand. If your spine tingles and your breathing slows when you trigger it later, the anchor is set. Use it before every trading session.

Chapter 11: Putting It All Together

The choice of trading instrument for Cohen’s setups is worth examining in detail. He uses three things: stock (with margin), deep ITM options, and straddles/strangles. Why deep ITM options instead of ATM options for directional plays? Because deep ITM options have delta near 1.0 — they move almost dollar-for-dollar with the stock — while having minimal time value to decay. A deep ITM call at a $70 strike when the stock is at $100 has $30 of intrinsic value and perhaps $0.50 of time value. You are paying almost entirely for intrinsic value: real, permanent value that cannot erode. Compare this to an ATM call at the $100 strike: perhaps $6 of time value, all of which decays to zero at expiration. If the stock goes nowhere for two weeks, the deep ITM holder has lost $0.25 in time value; the ATM holder has lost $2 or more. For short-term directional trades, this difference in theta exposure is decisive.

Cohen now assembles everything into a complete trading framework. His day-to-day reality: 90 percent of his trading uses just three instruments — long and short stock (with margin), deep ITM calls and puts, and straddles or strangles. He occasionally uses spreads but rarely. He uses three chart patterns exclusively for entry: flags and consolidations, support and resistance breakouts, and reversal doji bars at 20-day price extremes. That’s it. Master these three and ignore everything else.

For flags, consolidations, and channel breakouts, the OVI indicator is mandatory (covered fully in Chapter 12). The OVI must be persistently positive for a bullish setup; persistently negative for a bearish setup. Entry is placed just above the resistance level for bulls, just below support for bears — using stop-limit or conditional orders so you don’t chase a gap open. At the same moment as entry, the initial stop is set: typically at a meaningful technical level that invalidates the setup if reached.

Cohen’s strategy selection in practice reflects a preference for simplicity and instrumentational versatility. When he identifies a bull flag with positive OVI, the question is not “which strategy?” but “which instrument?” For slower-moving large-cap stocks where he wants to stay in for weeks to months, deep ITM calls provide the same directional exposure as stock but with defined maximum risk. For faster-moving setups where he wants rapid payback in days, the stock itself (with a hard stop) can be simpler and more responsive. Straddles are reserved for a different animal entirely: the rangebound stock with a known catalyst approaching. Each instrument is a lens for the same underlying setup; the choice depends on the expected magnitude of move, the time horizon, and the current volatility environment. This instrument flexibility is what distinguishes a complete options education from a strategy-specific manual.

At the first profit target (P1), Cohen closes exactly half the position. P1 is calculated as 38.2 percent of the flag pole length extended from the breakout point. Why half? Because “the fear of letting a winner slide into a loser is the most common trading failure.” By banking half at P1, you reward yourself for a good call. Even if the stock immediately reverses after P1 is hit, you have locked in a net positive outcome on the trade. For the remaining half, the initial stop is adjusted to near the entry level, making the position effectively risk-free.

The P1 / P2 two-stage exit system solves one of the most persistent psychological problems in trend trading: the fear of giving back a winner. Without P1, a trader who enters a bull flag breakout holds the entire position as the stock moves to a new high, then watches it retrace. Human psychology typically interprets this retrace as “I was right but now I’m losing” — which triggers premature exit at a price below the peak, often near the entry. With P1, half is closed at the first target and the stop is moved to near entry. Now the remaining half is riding for free: a 50 percent retracement that takes the stock back to the entry price still produces a net-positive trade overall. This asymmetry — partial exit + zero-risk second half — is what allows traders to run winners without the psychological pressure that normally causes early exits.

Chapter 12: Trading with the OVI

The book’s final chapter culminates in the revelation and detailed application of Cohen’s proprietary OVI indicator. OVI stands for Options Volume Indicator, though Cohen does not spell this out explicitly — the focus is on its behavior rather than its label. The OVI measures options transaction data for individual stocks: volume, open interest, and implied volatility, combined into a single oscillating line that sits between −1 and +1. It has no lag and requires no parameter adjustment. Either it works on a given stock, or it doesn’t.

The Bear Stearns case study is the book’s most dramatic illustration. From January through late February 2008, Bear Stearns traded between $68 and $93. The stock was not collapsing yet; most commentators were still bullish. On March 3, 2008, Bear Stearns closed at $77.32. On that same date, Cohen’s OVI had dropped to its lowest possible reading. For the two weeks that followed, the OVI remained at or near its most negative reading almost every single day. On March 17, Bear Stearns was trading at $2.84 in free fall, ultimately rescued by JP Morgan at $10. The entire collapse — from $77 to $2.84 in two weeks — was not anticipated by a single public commentator. But the options market had been screaming it for two weeks.

Bear Stearns March 3, 2008: stock at $77.32; OVI at its lowest possible reading. Someone in the options market knew something. Two weeks later the stock was at $2.84. [view]

The OVI works because well-informed institutional participants who have material non-public information about a company need two things: discretion and leverage. They cannot buy millions of stock shares without moving the price and alerting the market. But they can accumulate large option positions quietly, using out-of-the-money puts (for bearish bets) or calls (for bullish bets) that do not require massive capital. The OVI captures this accumulation of smart-money options flow and simplifies it into a single line. When that line is persistently negative, someone somewhere is betting heavily on decline. When it is persistently positive, someone is betting heavily on advance.

Not every stock is suitable for the OVI. The options must be liquid enough that the OVI “wiggles” noticeably almost every day. A stock with a flat, horizontal OVI that barely moves has illiquid options and is unreadable. A stock where the OVI swings between extremes without any consistent direction is also problematic. The best OVI candidates are large-cap, actively traded stocks with substantial institutional participation. XOM (ExxonMobil) is Cohen’s example of a qualifying stock; CBEY is his example of a non-qualifier.

The OVI indicator’s signal is stronger when it has been persistent for a longer period. A one-day spike in the OVI above zero is not a trade signal. What Cohen is looking for is persistence across multiple sessions: seven, ten, or even twenty consecutive days of positive readings as the chart pattern forms. The longer the persistence, the more confident he is that the smart money has been accumulating a directional position — not just hedging or speculating on one specific event. Trading & Investing Lessons

Defined Risk as Portfolio Discipline

Every strategy Cohen teaches has a defined maximum loss. Buying an option: maximum loss = premium paid. Bull call spread: maximum loss = net debit. Bull put spread: maximum loss = spread width minus credit received. Straddle: maximum loss = total premium. In portfolio terms, this is position sizing done automatically by the instrument itself. Stock investors who want to enforce defined maximum risk need stop orders; options traders have it built in structurally. The practical implication: you can allocate a fixed percentage of capital to each trade and know precisely the worst case before entry — an enormous advantage for risk-adjusted portfolio construction.

Leverage as a Double-Edged Amplifier

Cohen’s leverage example — $500 gain on a $5,000 stock position is 10 percent; the same $500 gain on a $700 options position is 71 percent — maps directly onto portfolio management. Options allow a portfolio manager to take a view with a fraction of the capital, freeing the rest for diversification or cash management. But the reverse is equally true: position sizing mistakes in options are amplified by the same leverage ratio. The lesson for investors is not to use options recklessly but to understand the true capital efficiency of each instrument and size accordingly.

The Covered Call as a Return-Enhancement Engine

Systematic covered call writing is an institutional strategy that has been packaged into ETFs (notably the CBOE BuyWrite Index). Cohen’s presentation makes the mechanics accessible: selling one month of time premium monthly, compounding those credits across a large stock portfolio, dramatically raises portfolio yield without changing the underlying holdings. The key constraint is willingness to see the stock called away at the sold strike — which requires either owning shares you are comfortable selling or selecting strikes far enough OTM that the probability of assignment remains low.

Implied Volatility as a Market Valuation Signal

Cohen’s rubber-band concept — implied volatility reverts toward its own historical average — is a variant of mean-reversion that applies beyond options. When the VIX (the market-wide implied volatility index) reaches extreme highs, it signals maximum fear in the market and often coincides with tradeable lows in equities. When the VIX reaches historic lows, complacency is at its peak and the risk of a volatility expansion is high. Monitoring IV relative to its own history provides a quantitative measure of crowd sentiment that complements traditional valuation metrics.

The OVI and Information Asymmetry

The Bear Stearns case is not unique. Insider trading prosecution cases routinely find unusual options activity preceding corporate announcements. The OVI operationalizes the observation that well-informed participants systematically prefer the options market for its combination of leverage and discretion. For the independent investor, this provides a crucial signal: when large sophisticated players are loading up on options in one direction, the odds of being on the wrong side of the trade increase dramatically. Tracking OVI is a form of “following smart money” that is legal, observable, and derived from public data.

Flags and Breakouts as Momentum Entries

Cohen’s primary setup — the bull flag breakout with OVI confirmation — is a version of momentum investing that academic research has consistently validated. Stocks that have been trending tend to continue trending; the flag is the pause that allows institutional players to reload before the next leg higher. The 38.2 percent Fibonacci P1 target is conservative by design: it captures the first extension of the prior move without requiring the stock to immediately rally by the full flag-pole length. The trailing trendline to P2 then captures whatever the trend delivers beyond that minimum.

Key Takeaways

Options Are Defined-Risk Instruments When Used Correctly

Buying options limits your maximum loss to the premium paid, period. The popular perception of options as dangerous refers to selling naked options, which does carry unlimited risk. As a buyer, you cannot lose more than you paid. This structural protection makes options superior to leveraged stock positions for traders who want controlled exposure.

Know Five Numbers Before Every Trade

Maximum risk, maximum reward, breakeven point(s), maximum loss you will accept, and profit target. These five numbers define the trade completely before entry. If you cannot calculate all five, do not enter. This discipline prevents the most common trading failure: letting losses run without a predetermined exit level.

Time Decay Accelerates in the Final 30 Days

Never buy OTM options with less than one month to expiration unless the trade is part of a spread. Time decay is nonlinear; the steepest portion of the theta curve is the final 30 days. An OTM option bought with two weeks to expiration needs a very large, very fast move to overcome the daily decay rate.

Straddles Require Three Months, Not One

One-month straddles are too expensive on a per-day basis and too sensitive to rapid theta decay. Three months is the optimal expiration for a straddle because it allows you to be right about the direction while exiting with one month remaining (before the acceleration zone). The 60-day rule quantifies whether the straddle cost is justified by the stock’s actual range history.

Bull Call Spreads Are Long-Term; Bull Put Spreads Are Monthly

The directionality of theta determines the appropriate time horizon for each spread type. Bull call spreads (net debit) are harmed by theta — use minimum six months, ideally one year. Bull put spreads (net credit) are helped by theta — use one month, repeat monthly for compounding income. Confusing the two time horizons inverts the theta advantage.

Deep ITM Options as Stock Substitutes

Deep ITM calls or puts have delta near 1.0 and minimal time value. They behave almost identically to owning or shorting the stock at a fraction of the capital cost. They are Cohen’s preferred instrument when he wants directional exposure: lower absolute outlay, same dollar movement, defined maximum loss.

The OVI Works Only with Continuation Patterns

A persistently positive OVI on a stock with no chart pattern is not a trade signal. The OVI provides confirmation, not discovery. The pattern must come first — flag, consolidation, channel — and the OVI must align with the expected breakout direction. Without the pattern, there is no trigger and no logical stop level.

Always Exit with One Month Remaining

This rule applies to straddles, strangles, and any single-leg long option position. The final 30 days of an option’s life are dominated by accelerating theta decay. Holding through this period hoping for a last-minute move gives the decay far more time to compound against you than any reasonable move can offset.

Contrarian Ideas

Options Can Be Lower Risk Than Stock Ownership

The synthetic call example is Cohen’s sharpest challenge to conventional wisdom. The synthetic call — stock plus a protective put — has a higher absolute dollar cost than buying a call outright, yet it has a lower maximum loss as a percentage of capital at risk. The claim that “options are risky” conflates selling naked options with buying options. These are entirely different risk profiles. Buying options is structurally less risky than buying stock on margin.

Fundamental Analysis Is Less Important Than Sentiment

Stock prices are driven by expectations, not fundamentals directly. A company can report record profits and see its stock fall; another can miss estimates and see its stock rise. What matters is whether the outcome beats or misses consensus expectations. Cohen builds on Benjamin Graham’s Mr. Market framework to argue that the market is a voting machine in the short run and a weighing machine only in the long run. Traders who understand this do not fight the market when sentiment overrides fundamentals.

Moving Averages Are Useless in Sideways Markets

The AMZN 1998-2000 and FNM 1999 comparison is Cohen’s proof that moving averages are conditionally useful, not universally applicable. In trending markets they are excellent. In sideways markets they generate endless false crossovers and whipsaws that destroy capital. Most traders use moving averages without conditioning their use on the presence of a trend. Cohen explicitly discourages this uncritical application.

Fibonacci, Elliott Wave, and Gann Are of Limited Value

Cohen’s iconoclasm extends to the most mystical elements of technical analysis. He applies Fibonacci retracements sparingly and as only one of several confirming factors. For Elliott Wave, he is dismissive: no two experts can agree on wave count until after the trade opportunity has expired. For Gann levels, his track record is “hit-and-miss.” His warning: “The more you believe in some divine order for the markets, the more your trading plans become compromised.”

Condors Are Rarely Worth the Trouble

Despite the appeal of a “flat-top” profit zone, Cohen finds condors difficult to structure with sufficient return. His FRE condor examples produce maximum yields of 19 percent with breakevens that fail to fully capture the $60-$70 range he was targeting. The stock at $69 at expiration still loses money. Condors require precise prediction of a price range, yet they cost enough in premium that the margin for error is small. Cohen does not actively trade them.

Psychological Capital Is More Important Than Technical Knowledge

Cohen estimates 80 percent of trading success is psychology. Most education focuses on the 20 percent that is technique. This inversion means that technically sophisticated traders who have not done the inner work will underperform less sophisticated traders who have learned to manage their emotional responses. The NLP anchoring technique, trade journals, and the public accountability of the bank loan story are all interventions in the psychological domain — more important, in his view, than learning one more options strategy.

Proposals & Frameworks

The Five Prerequisites for Trading Success

Cohen’s six-factor model for trading readiness: patience (1% per week compounds to 67% per year), perseverance (keep going through all market conditions), knowledge (experiential, not just academic), honesty (results are your responsibility), pre-planning (five numbers before every trade), and discipline (execute the plan without deviation). These are sequential: without patience and perseverance, knowledge cannot compound; without honesty, pre-planning is meaningless; without discipline, pre-planning is theater.

The Bull vs Bear Spread Time Horizon Rule

Bull call spreads (net debit) = long-term strategy, minimum six months, ideal twelve. Bull put spreads (net credit) = short-term income strategy, one month, repeated monthly. The directional relationship of theta determines everything. Net debit = theta works against you = use maximum time. Net credit = theta works for you = use minimum time. This framework applies symmetrically to bear spreads.

The 60-Day Straddle Filter

For a three-month straddle to be worthwhile, its total cost must be less than [(60-day high minus 60-day low) divided by 2]. For two-month straddles, use the 40-day version. For four-month straddles, use the 80-day version. This quantitative test filters out straddles where the options premium exceeds the stock’s actual documented price movement, ensuring you are not overpaying for volatility.

The OVI Trade Execution Framework

Step 1: Identify chart pattern (flag, consolidation, channel). Step 2: Confirm persistent OVI direction (same as expected breakout). Step 3: Verify no imminent earnings announcement. Step 4: Place conditional/stop-limit entry just beyond breakout level. Step 5: Set initial stop simultaneously at meaningful technical level. Step 6: At P1 (38.2% extension), close half, adjust stop to entry. Step 7: Manage trailing diagonal trendline until stopped out at P2. This seven-step framework applies identically to bulls and bears and to stock, deep ITM options, and spread instruments.

The Optimal Trading State (NLP Model)

Relaxed (zero physical tension) → Confident (tested plan with legitimate edge) → In Control (product of relaxation and confidence) → Better decisions → Better results → More confidence → More relaxation. The virtuous cycle can be initiated artificially through deep breathing and the anchoring technique. The cycle can be broken by a single large unexpected loss if rules have not been pre-committed to; pre-commitment is what makes the rules survive market pressure.

The First Spread Trade (Maximum Reward = Zero)

This story appears briefly in Chapter 1 but deserves fuller treatment. After scoring 80 percent on his options exam and feeling confident, Cohen placed his first spread trade using a broker. He then sat down and built a spreadsheet to calculate the position’s risk and reward. As the numbers came together, he realized with growing alarm that his maximum reward was exactly zero. Goldman Sachs Trade: P1 Hit on Entry Day

In the GS channel breakout example, an unusual outcome occurred: P1 was hit on the same day as the breakout entry. The channel had been building for two months, OVI was persistently positive, and when GS finally broke above $107.34, the move was so decisive that the 38.2% Fibonacci target of $109.29 was reached within hours. This scenario — P1 same-day — is actually the best-case scenario because it maximizes the speed of capital rotation. Half the position is booked immediately; the stop is adjusted to near entry; and the remaining half has months of time to compound. Cohen uses this example to illustrate why the P1 system works regardless of pace: whether P1 takes hours or weeks, the mechanics are the same.

OVI False Signals and Limits

Cohen is candid that the OVI is not infallible. It cannot be used for reversal setups — only for continuation patterns. Stocks with illiquid options produce flat, horizontal OVI readings that carry no signal. Stocks with chaotic OVI swings (extreme positive one day, extreme negative the next) are also unreadable. The indicator is most reliable on large-cap, institutional-grade stocks where sophisticated participants are consistently active in the options market. For small-caps, mid-caps, and thinly-traded names, the OVI signal is frequently meaningless noise. This is an important limitation that the book acknowledges but the main text sometimes glosses over in the excitement of the Bear Stearns and AAPL examples.
