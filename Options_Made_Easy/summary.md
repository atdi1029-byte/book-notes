## What the Book Is

A ground-up options trading education written in plain English with payoff
diagrams for every strategy. Cohen assumes you're starting from scratch, but
builds methodically through definitions, Greeks, spreads, volatility plays,
and his own proprietary OVI indicator. The 3rd edition adds real trade
examples from 2011-2012 and more detail on the OVI's power to detect
informed money flows. Endorsed by professionals at E*Trade, BigTrends, and
optionsXpress, but written for the self-directed individual trader.

## Core Thesis

Options are not inherently dangerous — they are dangerous only when used
without understanding. Properly applied, they offer leverage, portfolio
protection, income generation, and the ability to profit without knowing
market direction. Cohen's framework: six mental prerequisites, thorough
pre-trade planning, defined risk profiles, and (for continuation patterns)
the OVI indicator to confirm direction before entering.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Six prerequisites for success**: patience, perseverance, knowledge,
   honesty, pre-planning, discipline. Every trade needs max risk, max reward,
   breakeven point(s), and stop levels defined in advance.

2. **Risk profiles are the foundation**: All options strategies reduce to four
   basic shapes — long call, short call, long put, short put. Everything else
   is a combination. Understanding the shape tells you everything about how
   the position behaves.

3. **Calls vs puts**: calls = right to buy (upside); puts = right to sell
   (downside). Buying an option limits risk to premium paid. Selling (writing)
   an option creates potentially unlimited risk and requires advanced approval.

4. **The Greeks**: delta (speed vs stock price), gamma (acceleration of delta),
   theta (time decay — enemy of buyers, friend of sellers), vega (volatility
   impact — positive for long options), rho (interest rate impact, least
   important). Knowing these lets you predict how your position will change.

5. **Spread strategies**: Bull call spread = long-term bullish income (net
   debit). Bull put spread = short-term monthly income (net credit). Spreads
   reduce cost and risk but cap the upside. Preferred for most traders over
   naked options.

6. **Straddles and strangles**: directionally neutral; profit from big moves.
   Three-month expiration optimal. Exit with one month remaining. Always check
   that IV is below its own historical average before entering.

7. **Time decay (theta)**: Not linear — accelerates dramatically in final 30
   days. Never buy OTM options with less than one month to expiration unless
   part of a spread. Deep ITM options minimize time value and decay risk.

8. **The OVI indicator**: Cohen's proprietary tool measuring options volume,
   open interest, and implied volatility for a single stock. Plots as a -1 to
   +1 line. Use ONLY with continuation patterns (flags, consolidations,
   channel breakouts). Predicted Bear Stearns collapse in March 2008 two weeks
   before it happened.

9. **The full trading plan**: Enter just above resistance (bull) or below
   support (bear) using conditional/stop-limit orders. Set initial stop
   simultaneously. Close HALF at P1 (first target = 38.2% Fibonacci extension
   of flag). Adjust stop to near entry. Manage trailing trendline to P2.

10. **Psychology = 80% of trading**: The most common trader statement Cohen
    hears: "I would have made a profit, but I keep breaking my rules." Relaxed
    + confident + in control = the optimal trading state. Use NLP anchoring
    to build this state consistently before analyzing or trading.

## Most Memorable Examples

- **EXPE straddle (2003)**: Cohen bought a straddle before earnings + Gulf War,
  exited at +10% profit on day 2. Stock then rose $20+, making the straddle
  worth +300%. "The consequences of disobeying my trading plan made it a
  bitter disappointment."

- **Bear Stearns (2008)**: OVI hit its lowest reading on March 3 when BSC was
  at $77. It stayed there for two weeks. On March 17, BSC was at $2.84.
  No commentator saw it coming. The options market did.

- **Bank loan story**: Cohen borrowed money with an explicit verbal commitment:
  buy at $20, stop at $19. Treated the trade with extreme care because it
  wasn't "his" money. Stock doubled in six months. His lesson: treat every
  trade as if you borrowed the capital from someone you promised to protect.

- **AAPL flag (2011)**: OVI positive for over one month while AAPL formed a
  textbook bull flag. Breakout led to a $200+ move over three months — a 50%
  gain on the underlying.
