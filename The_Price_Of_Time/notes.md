## Introduction: The Anarchist and the Capitalist

### The 1849 Debate on Interest

- **Pierre-Joseph Proudhon** vs. **Frédéric Bastiat** debated interest legitimacy in *La Voix du peuple* (1849)
- Proudhon (anarchist): interest is usury and plunder, "reward for idleness," causes inequality
- Famous slogan: **"I call interest THEFT"**
- Compounding creates exponential debt → "larger than an orb of gold the size of the Earth"
- Interest slows money circulation → stagnation, unemployment, bankruptcy
- Workers can't afford products they make

### Proudhon's Revolutionary Proposals

- Nationalize **Banque de France**, expand money supply, reduce interest to near zero
- **People's Bank** charging 0.5% to cover costs
- Replace gold with paper money, tax on capital
- Promises: no debt, fewer bankruptcies, increased consumption, guaranteed employment

### Bastiat's Defense of Interest

- Interest is fair reward for mutual exchange, not theft
- Lender provides use of capital over time — **time has value**
- **Benjamin Franklin**: **"Time is money — Time is the stuff of which life is made"**
- Interest is "natural, just and legitimate" — makes labour more productive
- Benefits "particularly the long-suffering classes"

### Bastiat's Critique of Proudhon

- If lending not rewarded, would be penalized — **abolishing capital**
- National bank requiring collateral would exclude workers
- **"Abolition of interest benefits only the wealthy"** — rich borrow free, poor cannot borrow at any price
- Free credit = **"calamity for the working classes"** — fewer businesses, falling wages, capital flight
- Debate's end: Proudhon's final letter: **"Monsieur Bastiat, you are a dead man"** — Bastiat died one year later

### Bastiat's "Broken Window" Parable

> "In the sphere of economics, a habit or law engenders not just one effect but a series. Only the first is immediate; it is *seen*. The others occur successively; *they are not seen*."

- **Bad economist** relies on visible effect
- **Good economist** accounts for both seen and foreseen effects
- **Henry Hazlitt** (*Economics in One Lesson*, 1946): elaborated Bastiat's parable
- Attacked "full employment fetish" and price controls
- **Schumpeter's "creative destruction"** must operate unhindered

### The Fear of "Excessive" Interest Rates

- **Hazlitt on easy money**:

> "creates economic distortions … encourages highly speculative ventures that cannot continue except under artificial conditions … artificial reduction of interest rates discourages normal thrift, saving, and investment. It slows down that increase in productivity."

- **William Graham Sumner's "Forgotten Man"**: A and B plan to help X, but ignore impact on C — **C is the victim of the reformer**

### Proudhon's Dream Is Realized

- After **Lehman Brothers bankruptcy (2008)**, central banks implemented Proudhon's scheme
- Interest rates pushed to **lowest level in five millennia**
- Europe and Japan: rates turned **negative**
- Results diverged from Proudhon's expectations — closer to Bastiat's warnings

### William White's 2012 Warning

- **William White** (Canadian economist): *"Ultra Easy Monetary Policy and the Law of Unintended Consequences"*
- Sharp decline in rates encouraged households to **spend more and save less**, but people must save more for retirement (harder at low rates)
- Firms actually investing *less* despite cheap credit
- Ultra-easy money responsible for **misallocation of capital**
- **Creative destruction thwarted**
- Easy money served only to **postpone day of reckoning**
- **"At best, it buys time to rebalance economies. In reality, this opportunity is wasted"**

### Bastiat Vindicated: The Inequality Effect

- Post-crisis decade: incomes barely grew, low-paying jobs proliferated
- **Less well-off forced to borrow at high rates and received negative real returns on deposits**
- **Wealthy speculators and corporations borrowed cheaply and made out handsomely**

### The Book's Thesis

- Ultra-low interest rates contributing to current woes:
  - Collapse of productivity growth
  - Unaffordable housing
  - Rising inequality
  - Loss of market competition
  - Financial fragility
- Ultra-low rates played role in **resurgence of populism**

### The Complexity of Interest

- **Eugen von Böhm-Bawerk** catalogued two dozen different theories in *Capital and Interest*
- **Irving Fisher**: theories that appear antagonistic are "harmonious and complementary"
- Various perspectives: wages of abstinence, cost of leverage, capitalization of assets
- Most encompassing: **"the price of time"**

### The Book's Central Question

- **Can capitalist economy function properly without market-determined interest?**
- Book's argument: Interest required to direct allocation of capital
- As "reward for abstinence" interest incentivizes saving
- Interest is cost of leverage and price of risk
- As **Bastiat** understood: very low rate may benefit the rich more than the poor

## Chapter 1: Babylonian Birth

### Etymology of Interest — Natural Origins

- **Interest predates coined money** (originated 8th century BC)
- Ancient etymologies derive from offspring of livestock:
  - Sumerian *mas*: kid goat
  - Greek *tokos*: calf
  - Latin *foenus*: connotes fertility
  - English *capital* from *caput*: head of cattle

> "loans of seeds and of animals. These were loans for productive purposes. The seeds yielded an increase. At harvest time the seed could conveniently be returned with interest."
> — Sydney Homer and Richard Sylla

- **Irving Fisher**: "Nature's productivity has a strong tendency to keep up the rate of interest"

### Babylonian Mash — The Credit System

- **Mesopotamian clay tablets** record loans (3rd-2nd millennia BC)
- Names of creditor/debtor, amount, date, interest, collateral
- Interest generally paid in same commodity as loan (silver or barley)
- Standard rates: **33.33% for barley, 20% for silver**
- Each tablet sealed and witnessed; tablets destroyed when loans repaid

### Purposes of Mesopotamian Credit

- Many commodities sold on credit: barley, reeds, livestock
- Loans for: real estate, slaves, business ventures, consumption
- **Foreign trade often financed with credit**
- **Bottomry loans** (loans on ship's keel) common — higher interest, but principal not repaid if business failed

### Interest as Rent — Anticretic Interest

- Lender could receive interest as labor services — **anticretic interest**
- When paid by taking possession of collateral (land), equivalent to rent
- Babylonians used same word *mas* for interest and rent
- *Rentier*: person who lives off interest

### Female Creditors & Priestesses as Bankers

- **Priestesses of sun god Samas** conducted extensive credit operations
- Known as *naditu*, drove male merchants from lending business
- Acquired great fortunes, fine houses, gardens
- **"Finance was born in the shades of sanctity"**
- **Neo-Babylonian Egibi family** provided array of merchant-banking services — "The Rothschilds of the Ancient Near East"

### The Eighth Wonder — Compound Interest

- Mesopotamians invented **compound interest**
- **Clay cone** (before 2400 BC) records dispute: debt accrued compound interest at 33.33%, totalled **4.5 trillion litres of barley** — unpayable
- Sumerian phrase for compound interest: ***mash mash*** (livestock replicating itself)
- **Richard Price** (18th century): "A penny... put out to 5% compound interest at our Saviour's birth, would... have increased to more money than would be contained in 150 millions of globes, each equal to the earth in magnitude, and all solid gold"

### Debt Crises & Jubilees

- **Enmetena of Lagash**: first ruler to proclaim **debt cancellation**
- Mesopotamian debt write-offs announced at start of new reign
- **594 BC**: Athenian **Solon** announced "shaking off of burdens" — smashed mortgage stones, released citizens from debt bondage

### Hammurabi's Code — First Financial Regulation

- **Hammurabi's Code** (c. 1750 BC): earliest known laws, engraved on basalt stele
- **Maximum rates**: 20% for silver, 33.33% for barley
- **Penalties** for lenders who charged more, failed to subtract repayments, used fraudulent weights, demanded compound interest
- After crop damage: interest must be forgiven

### Regulatory Arbitrage in Ancient Babylon

- **"Regulatory arbitrage"** as old as law itself
- Lenders could apply maximum rate for term as short as month — extremely high APR
- One loan demanded late payment penalty at annualized **260%**
- Attempts to disguise interest-bearing loans as merchandise advances

### The Level of Interest Rates — Stability and Decline

- Babylonian rates came in **two standard forms**: 20% silver, 33.33% barley
- Rates unchanged for centuries (2100-1750 BC)
- Over centuries, rates in ancient world declined substantially
- Influenced by **changes to standard of measurement** rather than economic factors
- **Keynes**: interest rates determined by **custom** rather than market forces
- **Marx**: "customs, juristic tradition, etc., have as much to do with determining the average rate of interest as competition itself"

### Monetary Regimes vs. Economic Factors

- **Bank for International Settlements research**: interest rates over past hundred years more influenced by nature of monetary regimes than by economic factors
- Yet also clear: **ancient rates not solely determined by custom**
- Market forces at play — variety of rates and risk premiums
- **Interest rates were not correlated with economic growth**, either in ancient world or afterwards

### Real vs. Monetary Factors

- **Suetonius**: when Emperor Augustus brought Egyptian treasure to Rome, money became plentiful, interest rates fell from 6 to 4%
- After Augustus' death: Emperor Tiberius hoarded money, rates rose, banking crisis (AD 33)
- Tiberius lent out imperial treasure free of interest → immediate decline in rates
- **World's first experience of quantitative easing**

### Böhm-Bawerk's Cultural Mirror Thesis

- **Böhm-Bawerk**: cultural level of nation mirrored by its rate of interest
- **Babylon, Greece, Rome**: interest rates followed U-shaped curve — declining as civilizations prospered, rising sharply during decline
- **Very low interest rates appear to have been calm before storm**
- **Not a comforting thought** given extraordinarily low rates of early 21st century

### The Necessity of Interest

- **Adam Smith**: humans have "propensity to truck, barter, and exchange"
- Seems likely **credit antedated money** and earliest forms bore interest
- Interest arose from **need and greed**
- Existed because **capital was in short supply**
- Lenders able to charge interest because **wealth was unevenly distributed**

### William Goetzmann on Interest as Innovation

> "The emergence of interest to incentivize lending is the most significant of all innovations in the history of finance"

- **Finance allows people to transact across time**
- Industrial processes require time in production
- When capital tied up, interest bears connection to time used

### Interest Required for Capital Allocation

- Payment of interest required to induce people to lend their resources
- Without interest, would inevitably hoard capital
- Rate of interest and productivity of capital should bear relation

### Interest Required to Value Long-Lasting Assets

- Interest required to place value on long-lasting assets (housing)
- When Emperor Augustus flooded Rome with treasure, interest rates fell and house prices rose
- **Two millennia later: property markets remain sensitive to changes in money supply and interest rates**

### Modern Policymakers vs. Historical Understanding

- To 21st-century policymakers: interest simply lever to control inflation
- Yet interest has always been with us because **resources have always been scarce**
- As **Böhm-Bawerk** says: **"interest is the soul of credit"**
- Interest exists because: loans are productive, those with capital need to be induced to lend, lending is risky, production takes time, humans are naturally impatient

### Earlier Economists on Importance of Interest

- **Böhm-Bawerk**: interest was "an organic necessity"
- **Irving Fisher**: "too omnipresent a phenomenon to be eradicated"
- **Joseph Schumpeter**: interest "permeates, as it were, the whole economic system"
- **Marx**: "usury lives in the pores of production, as it were, just as the gods of Epicurus lived in the space between worlds"

## Chapter 2: Selling Time

### Historical Condemnation of Usury

- **Moneylenders universally despised**: Aristotle, Plato, Augustine, Aquinas, Dante, Luther, Shakespeare, Marx, Hitler — all condemned interest
- **Old Testament bans**: Deuteronomy 23:19, Exodus 22:25 forbid charging interest to fellow Israelites
- **Hebrew word *neschek***: literally "to bite"

### Aristotle's Attack on Usury

- **The Politics**: money intended for exchange, not to increase at interest
- **Etymology argument**: usury (*tokos*) means "birth of money from money" — most unnatural
- Plato depicted usury as setting rich against poor

### Aristotle's Flaw: Money as Store of Value

- **Aristotle/Aquinas claimed**: money exists solely for exchange
- **Fatal oversight**: money is also a *store of value*
- Lender retains stored value (in debt form) while surrendering exchange value
- **Aristotle ignored the temporal dimension**: lending takes place across time

### Thomas of Cobham: Selling Time

- **14th-century theologian**: would have become Archbishop of Canterbury
- **Critical insight**: usurer is **selling time** — "time belongs properly to God"
- Usurer becomes "thief of time"

### Time as Valuable: Ancient and Renaissance Views

- **Antiphon** (480-411 BC): "the most costly outlay is time"
- **Seneca the Younger**: time is precious because man is mortal
- **Seneca's hypocrisy**: at one moment suggests fair to pay more interest than agreed; at another, compares usury to murder

> "I see there letters of credit, promissory notes, and bonds, empty phantoms of property, ghosts of avarice… what are your documents, *your sale of time*, your blood-sucking twelve per cent. interest?"

- **Super-Rich Seneca** was covetous usurer; his "insatiable usury" allegedly provoked Boudica's rebellion (AD 60)

### Renaissance: Time Becomes Human Possession

- **Leon Battista Alberti** (1404-72): proper time management crucial
- **Fernand Braudel**: Alberti signals shift from Scholastic view (time belongs to God) to secular view (time is human possession)
- **Braudel's "merchant's time"**: Renaissance brought secularization of time
- By Alberti's birth, all large Italian cities had public clocks
- **Paolo da Certaldo**: "If you have money, do not keep it lying idle at home, for it is better to work in vain than to be idle for nothing"

### Francesco Datini: The Merchant of Prato

- **Francesco Datini** (1335-1410): precursor to modern capitalist
- Ledgers opened "In the name of God and of Profits"
- **Double-entry bookkeeping** spreading across northern Italy
- For international merchants, **credit indispensable**, time (measured in interest) precious
- **Benjamin Franklin's observation**: "time is money… credit is money"

### Datini's Wool Operation: Time and Profit

- **3.5 years from first order to sale** in Valencia
- **Profit less than 9%** — no wonder Italian merchants viewed time as cost element

### Decline in Interest Rates (1200-1570)

- Medieval Italy: commercial interest rates fell from ~20% (1200) to ~5% (1550)
- Established Italian merchants accessed cheap credit, ousted Flemish merchants
- By 15th century, Venetian merchants paid as little as 5%

### Evading Interest Regulations

- Medieval bankers found countless evasion methods:
  - Overstating loan principal
  - Clipped coin loans repaid in unclipped coin
  - Impossibly short loan periods with interest concealed in penalties
  - False sales
- **Dry Exchange (*Cambi Secchi*)**: most common — overseas bill with interest incorporated in FX transaction

### England's "Economy of Obligation"

- By early 16th century, England transformed into "economy of obligation"
- Lending at interest extended throughout town and country
- All occurred before 1571 legislation made taking interest legal

### Interest Governs the World

- Once time had value and was individual's possession, clerical injunctions against usury lost force
- Word derives from Latin *interesse* — compensation paid by defaulting debtor
- Interest viewed as **opportunity cost** suffered by creditor
- **Medieval concept**: *lucrum cessans* (foregone profit)
- **Bernardino of Siena** (Franciscan): "money has not simply character of money, but productive character, which we commonly call capital"

### Distinction Between Consumer and Commercial Lending

- Scholastic denunciation suited agrarian society (loans for consumption)
- Shattered by extension of trade/industry through Middle Ages
- Interest on consumption loans condemned; charging for productive capital increasingly acceptable
- Medieval Church acknowledged interest for loans involving risk: *periculum sortis* and *damnum emergens*

### Thomas Wilson: Time as Valuable But Usury Still Evil

- **Thomas Wilson** (Elizabethan diplomat), *Discourse Upon Usury* (1572): likens usurer to "hel unsaciable, the sea raging, a cur dog"
- Yet Wilson aware contemporaries value time: "They saye tyme is precious"
- **Wilson's definition**: "Usurye is also saide to be the price of tyme, or of the delaying or forbearing of moneye"
- **Interest is the price of time — no better definition**

### Legalization and Moral Shift

- **Queen Elizabeth's law (1571)**: legalized lending at interest, maximum 10%, distinguished between interest and usury
- Contemporary memorandum: "usury contayneth inequalitie and unnatirall dealing and trewe interest observeth equitie"

### Interest in Broader Sense: Stake and Self-Interest

- Modern era: avarice of usurer supplanted by interest as share or stake
- **Lord Shaftesbury**: "Interest governs the World"
- **Bernard Mandeville**, *Fable of the Bees* (1714): exposed paradox that private vices brought public benefits
- **Adam Smith**: "By pursuing his own interest he frequently promotes that of the society more effectually than when he really intends to promote it"

### Credit Networks and Capitalism

- Capitalism transmitted across credit networks connecting lenders/borrowers through bonds of mutual self-interest
- **Turgot**: "the capitalist lender of money… ought to be considered as a dealer in a commodity which is absolutely necessary for the production of wealth"
- Without interest, no capital. Without capital, no capitalism.

### Franklin and Le Goff: Merchants of the Future

- **Jacques Le Goff**: "instigators of capitalism were usurers: merchants of the future, sellers of time"

### A Bird in the Hand: Time Preference Explains Interest

- **Turgot's answer**: "A bird in the hand is worth two in the bush"
- Earliest reference to **time preference**: propensity to place higher value on immediate pleasures
- Interest is difference in monetary values across time, rate at which present consumption exchanged for future consumption
- Interest represents **time value of money**

### The Marshmallow Test: Behavioral Evidence

- **Walter Mischel** (Stanford, late 1960s): famous study of deferred gratification
- Children offered choice: consume single treat immediately or wait 15 minutes for double reward
- Of 600 children tested, 1/3 deferred gratification long enough to receive extra marshmallow
- Humans exhibit positive time preference
- Bonus marshmallow = kind of interest

### Explanations for Time Preference

- Various arguments: life is nasty/brutish/short, humans congenitally short-sighted, when economy expanding people can expect to become richer
- **Time preference infinitely varied**: may reflect nation's moral/intellectual qualities (thrift, industry)
- Age influences time preference: youth naturally impatient; older people lower time preference
- Countries with ageing populations tend to have falling interest rates (Japan: rates fell to zero after working population contracted mid-1990s)

### Universal or Abolishable?

- **Irving Fisher**: maintained impatience (positive time preference) "fundamental attribute of human nature"
- **Ludwig von Mises**: "time preference is a category inherent in every human action"
- **If true, interest rate must always be positive and negative rate unnatural**

### Time Preference and Investment

- Act of investment requires investors forgo current consumption
- In theory, investment should only be made if rate of return at least equal to investors' time preference
- **Jevons** and **Böhm-Bawerk**: businesses borrow to invest up to point where marginal return equals rate of interest

### What If Interest Rates Don't Reflect Time Preference?

- **Problem exercised Böhm-Bawerk's pupils**: Hayek, Mises, Schumpeter
- When interest rates decline, businesses invest in projects with more distant pay-offs
- **If rates kept below natural level, misguided investments occur**: too much time used in production
- **"Malinvestment"** (Austrian term): expensive white-elephant projects
- Consumer behavior affected when rates pushed below society's time preference: cheap credit encourages too much debt

## Chapter 3: Child's Play

### The Culpepers' Campaign for Lower Interest

- **Sir Thomas Culpeper the Younger** continued father's advocacy for low rates
- Claimed abatement of interest would "revive our dying Manufacture"
- Lower interest would raise land prices, ease debt burden of landowners

> "To the King, increase of his Customs. To the Kingdom, increase of Land. To the Nobility and Gentry, deliverance from Bondage and Debt."

### Sir Josiah Child's Arguments

- **Sir Josiah Child** (merchant, East India Company leader) advocated reduction to 4%
- Lower interest increases industry, multiplies traders, increases merchant gains
- Real wealth might double in 20 years with lower rates

### Historical Context: European Rate Declines

- Late Middle Ages: European rates fell as capital accumulated
- By 1600: Genoese loan yields below 3%
- End of 17th century: Amsterdam borrowing cost reached **1¾%** — lowest ever in Northern Europe

### Child's Self-Interest Exposed

- **Child extraordinarily wealthy** — diarist called him "most sordidly avaricious"
- Control of East India Company maintained through debt
- Cynical pamphleteer: demands for lower interest designed for "ingrossing all trade, into hands of a few rich Merchants"

## Chapter 3 (continued): Locke's Considerations

### Locke's Pamphlet (1691)

- **John Locke** (liberal philosopher): *Some Considerations of the Consequences of the Lowering of Interest*
- Saw many disadvantages from forced borrowing cost reduction

> "It will be a loss to Widows, Orphans, and all those who have their Estates in Money… a very hard case upon a great and innocent part of the People."

### Locke's Critique of Lower Interest

- Winners (bankers) not deserving
- Indebted businessmen like Child would profit
- Lower rates would:
  - Increase loan demand — "every man would be forward to borrow"
  - Multiply borrowers excessively
  - "Discourage lending at such a disproportion of profit, to risque"
  - Money would be hoarded
  - Withdrawn money would increase in value (deflation)

### Dutch Wealth: Causation Reversed

- Child claimed low interest caused Dutch wealth
- **Locke's rebuttal**: lowness of interest in Holland "not an effect of Law … but as the Consequence of great Plenty of ready Money"
- Dutch prosperity from "industry and frugality"
- England's trade thrived under Queen Elizabeth when interest was 10%:

> "I think I may reasonably infer… that lowering of *Interest* is not a sure way to improve either our Trade or Wealth."

### Political Outcome

- **Locke won the debate practically**
- Child failed to secure rate reduction in 1660s or early 1690s
- 1713: max legal rate fixed at 5%
- Only 1854: England's usury law finally removed

### The Natural Rate of Interest

- Anti-usury polemicists called interest inherently unnatural
- Most 17th-century English writers accepted interest as natural phenomenon
- Argument: appropriate *level* of interest
- Child's critics: state attempts to control interest inherently futile
- **Sir William Petty** dismissed "vanity and fruitlessness of making Civil Positive Laws against the Laws of Nature"
- Interest like any other price, determined by buyers/sellers in market

### Determining the Natural Rate

- **Locke's money theory**: precious metals with intrinsic value, limited supply
- Interest level fixed by supply/demand for money, like any commodity
- Locke believed England's natural rate above 4%, probably closer to 6%

> "what the stated Rate of Interest should be in the constant change of Affairs, and flux of Money, is hard to determine."

### Later Theories on Natural Rates

- **John Massie**: "natural rate of interest is governed by the Profits of Trade"
- **David Hume** ("Of Interest"): "Money having chiefly a fictitious value, the greater or less plenty of it is of no consequence"
- Natural rate prevails regardless of money supply changes

### Divergence from Natural Rate: Problems Arise

- **Henry Thornton** (turn of 19th century): usury laws prevented Bank charging >5% — "unnatural and extraordinarily low"
- Borrowers obtained loans cheaply: "That which they obtain too cheap they demand in too great quantity"
- Providing paper credit below natural rate created conditions for unstable financial boom

### Wicksell's Contribution

- **Knut Wicksell** (Swedish economist): discussed market rates diverging from natural rate
- Discrepancy revealed by price level changes: too low → inflation; too high → deflation
- Conventional wisdom among monetary policymakers in 21st century

### The Natural Rate as Abstraction

- Natural rate unobservable, pure abstraction
- Modern world: money created when banks make loans
- Early 1930s: **Keynes** and **Piero Sraffa** denied natural rate could exist for entire economy
- Nevertheless, concept remains indispensable for economists

### Signs of Departure from Natural Rate

**Cost of borrowing too high:**
- Businesses won't borrow to invest
- Capital values depressed, workers idle, economy stagnates
- Bond yields far exceed national income growth → debt burdensome
- Britain suffered these ills after Gold Standard return (1920s)

**Excessively low rates signaled by:**
- Asset price bubbles proliferate, credit booms
- Savings collapse, capital misallocated
- Market rates not aligned with natural rate

### Locke's Prescience Vindicated

- Locke first to consider damage from taking rates below natural level
- Position in modern language:
  - Financiers benefit at expense of "widows and orphans"
  - Wealth redistributed from savers to borrowers
  - Creditors inadequately compensated for risk
  - Bankers hoard money rather than lend
  - Deflation
  - Too much borrowing
  - Asset price inflation makes rich richer
  - Rate reduction wouldn't revive moribund economy

### Post-2008 Outcomes Mirror Locke's Warnings

- After decade+ of unconventional monetary policies, Locke prescient
- Ultra-low rates hurt savers, benefited financiers/executives
- Paper wealth multiplied; genuine wealth stagnated
- Collapse of rates didn't reduce debt but induced excess borrowing
- **Locke spot on**

### Reevaluating Child's Claims

- Recent experience: Child's claims (lower rates reduce inequality, discourage luxury) cruelly misguided
- Still, 17th-century easy money proponents wanted higher land prices
- Post-2008: ultra-low rates inflated house price bubbles worldwide
- Child's claim: rate decline brings further decline ("as love begets love") vindicated
- We see vicious cycle: falling rates induce stagnation
- **Child right**: low rates beget lower rates

## Chapter 4: Money Printer Extraordinaire

### John Law's Background

- **John Law** (1671-1729): Scottish gambler, convicted murderer, financial theorist
- Dueled in London (1694), fled to Continent
- *Money and Trade Considered* (1705): advocated paper money and national bank
- Convinced **Philippe, Duke of Orléans** (Regent of France) to implement scheme

### France's Financial Crisis (1715)

- **Louis XIV** left France bankrupt
- National debt: 2 billion livres (equivalent to 18 years' tax revenue)
- Interest payments consumed 60% of budget
- Coinage debased multiple times, tax collection farmed out

### Law's Monetary Theory

- Paper money backed by land would increase money supply, lower interest rates
- More money → more commerce → more employment → prosperity
- Believed "Money is not the Value for which Goods are exchanged, but the Value by which they are exchanged"
- **Credit, not gold, was the true basis of money**

### The System Unfolds

- 1716: Law founded **General Bank** (private bank issuing notes convertible to metal)
- 1717: acquired **Mississippi Company** (monopoly on Louisiana trade)
- Merged bank and company; expanded monopolies to include all foreign trade, tobacco, mint, tax collection
- By 1719: Mississippi Company = France's economy

### Mississippi Stock Mania

- Shares initially 500 livres, reached peak of 10,000 livres (September 1719)
- Frenzied speculation in Rue Quincampoix (Paris)
- New term: **"millionaire"** (fortunes made overnight)
- **General Bank** became **Royal Bank** (1718), notes backed by king's word rather than metal

### The Monetary Engine

- Massive paper money inflation: note circulation increased by ~1 billion livres over 1719
- Royal Bank ran eight printers around the clock
- By May 1720: total notes exceeded 2 billion livres — **50x earlier issuance**, roughly twice all gold/silver coins

### Interest Rates Collapsed as Law Predicted

- August 1719: Mississippi Company took over 1.2 billion livres of national debt, receiving 3% payment
- Government creditors could exchange bonds for shares, but refusal meant interest dropped from 5% to **2%**
- **Bank money and Mississippi stock became interchangeable**. Loans to speculators at 2%.
- Mississippi Company bought its own stock with zero-interest bank loans

### Asset Inflation Across the Board

- At market peak: government debt yield = 2%, margin loans = 2%, Mississippi shares traded at ~50x earnings
- **Law explicitly justified high share price by reference to low interest rates**
- Falling rates "carried value of lands to 80 and 100 years purchase, raised up stately edifices"

### Brief Prosperity

- Law later recalled: "Prince was head of rich people, revenues increased and burdens reduced. No longer uncultivated lands or workers without work."
- September 1719: Ambassador praised Law as "first minister" who would "raise France to greater height than ever"

### Why the System Collapsed

- **Powerful enemies**: Scottish interloper created enemies among tax farmers and financiers
- **Law's dictatorial measures**: altered monetary value of gold/silver, forced banknotes upon people, banned possession of precious metals
- **Character flaws**: vanity and ambition turned Law's head. Paid little attention to management.
- End of 1719: massive banknote issue produced "wild inflation"
- As confidence in paper currency evaporated, money flowed out of country

### Desperate Measures and Final Collapse

- May 21: Law announced share price pegged at 5,000 livres (half peak), banknotes to lose half value
- Riots broke out, Royal Bank stormed
- Regent quickly annulled decree and sacked Law
- Early December: Law tendered resignation
- Following month, slipped out of France, leaving dreams, fortune, wife and daughter behind

### Richard Cantillon's Monetary Analysis

- **Richard Cantillon** (Parisian banker): provided "most penetrating contemporary analysis"
- *Essai Sur La Nature du Commerce* (1730s)

**Short-term benefits of money printing:**
- National bank could drive down rates by purchasing government debt with newly printed money
- Risk: Operations only work as long as extra money balances remain trapped in financial system
- "The excess banknotes… do not upset the circulation, because being used for buying and selling of stock they do not serve for household expenses"

**Long-term risks:**
- Once money escapes into wider economy, consumer prices bound to rise
- Danger that speculators would lose confidence

### Earl J. Hamilton's Modern Verdict

- Two centuries later, **Hamilton** advanced Cantillon's monetary critique
- Interest rates "too low during bubble years, but Law was 'irreversibly committed' to easy money"
- Had Law reversed course and hiked rates, Mississippi stock price would have crashed

### Modern Economists Defend Law

- Verdict by modern economists "surprisingly favourable"
- **Peter Garber** (Brown): Law's credit theory "centerpiece of most money and macroeconomics textbooks"
- **William Goetzmann** (Yale): credits Law's belief that too little money constrains activity as "essential principle underlying decisions by U.S. Federal Reserve today"

### Post-2008 Parallels

- Collapse of Lehman (2008) produced conditions similar to France after Louis XIV's death
- Monetary policymakers took leaf from Law's copybook: pushing down rates, acquiring government debt with newly printed money
- After 2008, Federal Reserve embarked on deliberate policy of boosting asset prices
- As **Cantillon** pointed out: newly created money initially trapped in financial system, inflating financial assets rather than consumer prices

## Chapter 5: John Bull Cannot Stand Two Per Cent

### The Trade Cycle

- English commerce exhibited pattern of regular fluctuations from early 18th century
- When credit abundant, interest rates low: merchants became daring, bankers accommodated recklessness
- Periods of low interest rates provided fillip to capital-intensive industries

### Low Rates Fueled Capital-Intensive Industries (1750s-1760s)

- 1750s: British government debts consolidated into single perpetual bond (Consols)
- "Output of bricks moved inversely with yield on Consols"
- Great building boom (mid-1760s) when mortgage rates fell to abnormally low 3.5%
- **Horace Walpole**: "rage of building every where"

### Lord Overstone and Walter Bagehot

**Overstone's Credit Cycle Phases (1837):**

> "state of trade is subject to various conditions which are periodically returning; it revolves in an established cycle: quiescence, – improvement, – growing confidence, – prosperity, – excitement, – overtrading, – convulsion, – pressure, – stagnation, – distress, – ending again in quiescence."

**Bagehot's Background:**
- **Walter Bagehot**: renowned editor of *The Economist*, born above/next to **Stuckey's Bank**
- Managed Bristol branch, later vice-chairman

**Bagehot's Analysis:**
- Cycle arose from "different amounts of loanable capital available at different times"
- **Psychological element critical**: "Credit, the disposition of one man to trust another, is singularly varying"
- **Interest is the barometer of trust**

**Peak of Credit Cycle:**

> "The good times of high price almost always engender much fraud. All people are most credulous when they are most happy."

- As cycle entered final stages, Bank of England forced to raise rates
- "The best method to contract the entire credit currency is to raise the rate of interest"
- After prolonged high rates: "business is checked, prices fall, the exchanges are righted"

### Railway Mania and Easy Money (1840s)

- Britain's railway investment reached £560 million by mid-1840s (exceeding national income)
- **Railway mania** coincided with easy money period
- Discount rates fell below 3% by mid-1842, touched 2% in 1843

> "the ill effects of the Bank of England lowering the rate of discount to 4 per cent are now universally felt and acknowledged. Money is so very abundant that dealers in it have literally no employment for it." — *The Times*, April 1842

- **The Economist** blamed mania on easy money
- **Walter Bagehot**: "money was abundant… every place was to have a railway… There was no toleration for secure gain… *people could not stand two per cent*"

### Overend Gurney Collapse (1866)

**Black Friday, May 11, 1866:**
- **Overend Gurney** discount house closed May 10, 1866
- "Reign of terror" gripped London — likened to earthquake
- *The Times* called it "national calamity"

**The Firm's Fatal Mistakes:**
- Founded 1802, main business: discounting commercial bills
- Young partner **D. W. Chapman** tied up capital in bad investments: Greek & Oriental Steam Navigation, railways, iron works
- Shifted from discount brokering to "financing" — locking assets in illiquid securities promising high interest
- Funded with daily calls on money market using three-month commercial paper

### The Finance Mania of the 1860s

- Hundreds of speculative companies registered in 1863
- **David Morier Evans**: "amount of transparent jobbery… exceeded that known in great bubble period of 1824–25, or railway mania of 1845–46"
- Finance mania took off when rates at nadir
- Summer 1862: Bank of England rate at **John Bull's** dreaded 2%
- Within years, rates soared: 1864 Bank rate hit record **9%**

### The Bagehot Rule

**Lombard Street (1873):**
- Collapse inspired Bagehot to write *Lombard Street*
- Bank of England had duty to lend during panics
- "A panic is a species of neuralgia, and according to the rules of science you must not starve it"
- Recommended: **lend copiously against good securities at high rate of interest**

**Thomson Hankey's Objection:**

> "the most mischievous doctrine ever broached… that it is the proper function of the Bank to keep money available at all times to supply the demands of bankers who have rendered their own assets unavailable."

**Bagehot Rule's Design:**
- Original rule sought to mitigate moral hazard
- Recommended: Bank lend for short period, against high-quality collateral, at high rate

**Modern Departures from Bagehot:**
- After 2008: **Tim Geithner** called *Lombard Street* "bible of central banking"
- But modern lenders of last resort deviated:
  - Do NOT lend at high interest but at lowest rates
  - Emergency loans not short period but years on end
  - Do NOT lend against high-quality collateral
  - Show far less concern for moral hazard

**Bagehot and Deflation:**
- Nowhere does Bagehot consider deflation a financial risk
- Victorian era marked by long mild deflation punctuated by occasional severe deflation during panics
- **Deflation appearing at such moments = symptom of credit sickness**, not the cause

### Foreign Investments

**Post-Overend Recovery:**
- After Black Friday: Consol yields declined to 3½%, short-term loans under 2%
- **George Goschen** (Bank of England director) penned essay "Two Per Cent." (1868)
- Credit broken: "The cheapness of money has not borne its accustomed fruit"

> "Speculation remains cold and refuses to be comforted. Two per cent. woos in vain!"

**Bagehot on Foreign Lending Risks:**
- Problem: in many parts of world "the idea of interest is developed very imperfectly"

**"On the Dangers of Lending to Semi-Civilised Countries" (1867):**
- Borrower: **Egypt**
- Required for repayment: "a continuous polity; a fixed political morality; and a constant possession of money"
- Egypt lacked these qualities

> "We lend to countries whose condition we do not know, and whose want of civilisation we do not consider, and, therefore, we lose our money."

**The Foreign Lending Mania:**
- Egypt promising 10%
- Full-blown mania in foreign bonds
- Parliamentary Committee (July 1875): revelations concerning market-rigging, loan-issues for fat commissions

**Wave of Defaults:**
- Many foreign loans defaulted by 1875: Costa Rica, Dominican Republic, Paraguay (1872), Spain (June 1873), Bolivia, Guatemala, Liberia, Uruguay
- Egypt nearly stopped (1873) but managed to roll over debt at extortionate rates

**Bagehot's Reflection (February 1876):**

> "the human mind likes 15 per cent; it likes things which promise much… A long and painful experience is necessary to teach men that '15 per cent' is dangerous; that new and showy schemes are to be distrusted."

### The Barings Crisis (1890)

**Long-Term Rate Decline:**
- After foreign lending debate, English long-term rates continued falling
- Some ascribed decline to lack of capital demand after railway network completion
- Others blamed absence of significant gold discoveries

**Goschen's Conversion (1888):**
- **George Goschen** (now Chancellor of Exchequer) converted 3% Consols into bonds with lower yield
- Greatest conversion of public debt in Britain's history
- **Sir John Clapham**: "By stinting the hungrier capitalists of interest, it had a place in the boom and crisis history of the decade"

**The Speculative Boom:**
- Animal spirits surfaced in London stock market
- Flotation of **Guinness** produced "a great froth"
- "South African fever" raged for mining stocks
- **Investment trusts** attracted "investors who felt they could no longer obtain an adequate return on government securities"

**Bank of England Warning (October 1889):**

> "present tendency of finance… is distinctly in direction of danger… we have most of the elements of a Crisis."

**Argentina as Investment Destination:**
- Vast natural resources, largely European population
- Viewed as prospective United States
- Foreign investors overlooked: republic defaulted on first foreign loan (1820s)
- **Buenos Aires** took advantage of low rates: "new loans often being made to pay interest on former ones"

**Banking Panic and Barings' Fall:**
- Banking panic broke out Buenos Aires (April 1890)
- November: **Baring Brothers** (once "fifth power in Europe") broke
- News caused stampede on Buenos Aires stock exchange

**Bagehot's Warnings Ignored:**
- Bagehot died pneumonia (March 1877) at age fifty-one
- Best remembered for lender of last resort doctrine
- Modern monetary mandarins never consider Bagehot's warnings about adverse consequences of easy money
- How rates at 2% or less fuel speculative manias, drive savers to risky investments, encourage bad lending

## Chapter 6: Un Petit Coup de Whisky

### The Long Island Meeting (July 1927)

- **"The World's Most Exclusive Club"**: four central bankers hosted by **Benjamin Strong** (NY Fed President)
- Guests: **Montagu Norman** (Bank of England Governor), **Hjalmar Schacht** (Reichsbank President), **Charles Rist** (Bank of France Deputy Governor)
- **Schacht**: concerned too much foreign capital flowing into Germany
- **Norman**: opposite problem — Britain's economy trapped in depression, Bank reserves dangerously low
- Norman wanted Federal Reserve to lower US rates to take pressure off sterling
- **Strong** agreed at Long Island meeting to cut benchmark rate
- Another consideration: Strong told **Rist** rate cut would provide "petit coup de whisky" to NY stock market
- Turned out to be pure moonshine

### An Elastic Currency

- Federal Reserve Act of 1914: new central bank would "furnish an elastic currency"
- **Elastic currency** meant: interest rates no longer determined by requirement to convert notes into gold
- Implied possibility of fine-tuning monetary policy

### Post-WWI Inflation and Deflation

- New central bank's first test came after end of Great War
- Fed's response brutal: Early 1920, NY Fed's discount rate hiked from ~4.5 to 7%
- Commodity and consumer prices collapsed, industrial production contracted by quarter, 500+ banks failed
- Sharp deflation warmly welcomed by Governor **William Harding**: "the shrinkage which has taken place is somewhat analogous to that which occurs when a balloon is punctured"
- US economy bounced out of depression (1922), profits soared, largest improvement in US productivity ever recorded
- Welfare toll high: unemployment risen to 12%, country experienced "suicide wave"
- Nearly sixty years would pass before Federal Reserve attempted again to purge inflation with high rates

### More Elasticity

**The Gold Exchange Standard:**
- 1922: finance ministers met in Genoa to discuss reconstructing international monetary order
- Proposed modified version: **Gold Exchange Standard**
- Main idea: economize on gold — government securities held by central bank counted as reserves alongside gold
- Intended to be more "elastic" than predecessor, but elasticity brought problems
- Credit imbalances between countries could run far longer without being corrected
- For first time: possible for central bankers to conduct "active" monetary policy

**British Political Tensions:**
- Summer 1920: Bank of England lending rate climbed to 7%
- "Interest rates," Norman said, "are now a political as well as a financial question"
- Social tensions produced by Britain's high rates eventually erupted in **General Strike of 1926**
- Britain's ill-fated return to gold turned **Keynes** into vehement critic of Gold Standard and high rates in general

### Price Stability

**The New Monetary Orthodoxy:**
- New monetary order designed to prevent consumer prices from falling
- **Ben Strong** at NY Fed converted: interest-rate policy should be guided by goal of price stability

**Wicksell's Influence:**
- Notion that central banks should avoid both inflation and deflation gained ground since **Wicksell's** *Interest and Prices* (1898)
- **Wicksell** described world without money where "natural rate" of interest reflected return on capital
- In actual world, Wicksell identified natural rate by absence of either inflation or deflation
- Recommended interest rates set to maintain stable price level, envisaged co-operation between central banks

**International Collaboration:**
- Gold Exchange Standard went halfway towards realizing Wicksell's vision
- Many world's top economists advocated price stabilization: **Keynes**, **Ralph Hawtrey**, **Irving Fisher**
- **Strong**: "our whole policy would be directed toward the stability of prices so far as it was possible"

### Stable Prices Achieved (1922-1929)

- Between end of 1922 and 1929: index of US consumer prices scarcely budged
- In absence of inflation, Fed able to adopt less restrictive policy
- From summer 1924: Fed engaged in large-scale open-market operations, purchasing ~$500 million government securities
- Interest rates fell sharply: Fed's discount rate at 3% (September)
- On campaign trail, **Calvin Coolidge** boasted "policy of this Administration to reduce discount rates"

### Benjamin Anderson's Critique

- **Benjamin Anderson** (chief economist, Chase National Bank) believed monetary policy too loose
- Fed created to provide assistance during crisis, "but from early 1924 to spring of 1928, it was used… to finance a stock market boom"
- During those years, bank credit in US more than doubled
- Much new lending went to finance stock loans, real estate mortgages, foreign securities purchase

### The "Petit Coup de Whisky"

- After Long Island meeting: NY Fed reduced discount rate by 50 bps (to 3.5%), Fed banks boosted government securities purchases
- Margin loans soared, stock prices accelerated higher
- Over 1927: S&P 500 climbed by 37.5%
- Strong feared raising rates to curtail margin loans would produce collateral damage:

> "any policy directed solely to forcing liquidation in the stock loan account will be found to have a widespread and somewhat similar effect in other directions, mostly to the detriment of the healthy prosperity of this country."

- Swedish economist **Gustav Cassel** endorsed Strong's view that central bankers should maintain price stability and not concern themselves with stock market

### The Natural Rate and Productivity Growth

- Viewed from different perspective, Fed's interest-rate stance lax
- **Wicksell** claimed natural rate derived from economy-wide return on capital
- 1920s were period of remarkable productivity growth, brought about by electrification and "Fordism"
- Between 1923-1928: US economy grew at annual rate of nearly 8%
- Yet NY Fed's discount rate averaged less than half this number
- If consumer prices gave no sign rates too low: because supply-side improvements generating productivity gains also kept inflation at bay

### Credit Growth and Speculation

- Easy money fostered credit growth, credit growth fostered speculative excesses
- Property bubbles popped up from Florida (where **Carlo Ponzi** active) to Chicago
- More skyscrapers in 1920s than any other decade
- Loans on securities accounted for almost third of lending by Federal Reserve member banks
- By early 1929: total outstanding broker loans reached ~$7 billion
- **Benjamin Anderson**: "'Cheap money' characterized both 'new eras,' and cheap money is the most dangerous intoxicant known to economic life"

### The Discounting Error

- By placing too low discount on future earnings, investors ended up paying too much
- Early 1928: **Moody's** declared stock prices "over-discounted anticipated progress"
- After crash, **Benjamin Graham** and **David Dodd** in *Security Analysis*: late 1920s witnessed "transfer of emphasis from current income to future income and hence to capitalized value of future income and prospects"

### Foreign Lending and the Bubble

- Low US rates drove massive capital outflows to higher-yielding foreign bonds
- **German call money rates** averaged 8½% in 1925 (3× US Treasury bills)
- Foreign securities issuance peaked at $1.5 billion in 1927 (6× the 1923 level)
- **National City Bank** sold Brazilian bonds despite internal warnings — nearly two-thirds of 1928 foreign bonds later defaulted

### The Coup de Whisky Sours

**July 1927 Long Island Meeting:**
- **Hjalmar Schacht** reportedly said: "Don't give me a low rate, give me a true rate, and then I shall know how to keep my house in order"
- **Adolph Miller** (Fed Board member) bitterly opposed, later called it "one of the most costly errors committed by it or any other banking system in the last 75 years"
- President **Coolidge** (easy money supporter) declined to intervene

**The 1927 Rate Cut's Effects:**
- NY Fed stepped up government security purchases and lowered lending rate
- New credit "went rapidly into security loans and bank investments in securities"
- **S&P Industrials** rose nearly 20% between July and December
- **Strong** later regretted giving stock market its "coup de whisky"

**Tightening Fails to Stop Speculation:**
- Fed sold securities to drain liquidity, NY Fed discount rate raised from 3.5% to 5%
- Credit growth continued outpacing economic growth
- Stock market climbed nearly 30% (Feb 1928 – Aug 1929)
- Monetary tightening had unintended consequence: higher US rates reversed international capital flows
- **Lord Rothermere**: "Wall Street has become a colossal suction-pump, which is draining the world of capital"

**International Fallout:**
- Germany and Britain suffered gold losses, forced to hike rates
- **Ben Strong** succumbed to tuberculosis (October 1928)
- **Montagu Norman** sank into depression

**The Reversal and Crash:**
- Capital flow reversal out of Europe set time bomb
- October 1929 Wall Street crash followed 18 months later by **Creditanstalt** collapse
- Europeans cut off from US credit, forced to reduce purchases of US manufactures
- US industrial production began contracting by 1928

**Warning Signs Ignored:**
- **Russell Leffingwell** (JP Morgan partner) warned March 1929: "I think we are going to have a world credit crisis"
- **Paul Warburg** (Kuhn Loeb head, Fed founding father) feared call loan market made financial system dependent on stock prices

**Price Stability Advocates Remained Confident:**
- **Irving Fisher** bullish on eve of crash, lost fortune in market rout
- **Keynes** poorly positioned, believed stable consumer prices meant tight money unnecessary

### Keynes Meets Somary (April 1928)

- **Felix Somary** (Swiss banker, studied with **Böhm-Bawerk**) met Keynes at Berlin
- **Somary's** warning: danger came from "wide gap between yields on highest-priced shares and ordinary interest rates"

> "Keynes asked me what I was advising clients. 'To insulate themselves as much as possible from the coming crisis, and to avoid the markets,' I replied. Keynes took the opposite view. 'We will not have any more crashes in our time,' he insisted."

- Between 1927 and end of 1929, Keynes's personal fortune declined by more than three-quarters

### Austrian School Economics

- **Austrian school axiom**: interest necessary so investment and consumption decisions co-ordinated over time
- **Böhm-Bawerk**: rate of interest reflects society's time preference
- When free market sets interest, time preference and return on capital should equalize
- Danger: authorities push rates too low → credit takes off, bad investments abound

### Austrian Critique of Price Stability

- Austrians disagreed with **Wicksell** that natural rate could be divined by observing consumer prices
- **Hayek** (1927): appointed first director of **Austrian Institute for Business Cycle Research**
- Policy of price stabilization (advocated by Fisher, implemented by Strong) was misguided
- In capitalist economy, productivity advances mean consumer prices have natural tendency to decline
- Pursuit of stable prices worked against this natural tendency
- Monetary policy directed at stabilizing prices "administers excessive stimulus to expansion as costs fall, and thus regularly makes a later fall in prices with simultaneous contraction of output unavoidable"

### Hayek's *Monetary Theory and the Trade Cycle* (1929)

- Criticized Federal Reserve for setting interest below natural rate
- Fed's error didn't show in overt consumer price inflation
- Instead, **Hayek** alluded to hidden or "relative inflation" accompanied by destabilizing credit growth and malinvestment
- Advocates of stable prices failed to understand function of capital and interest

### Post-1929 Critique Grows

- **Chester Phillips, T. F. McManus, R. W. Nelson** (*Banking and The Business Cycle*, 1937): "the futility of price level stabilization as a goal is evidenced by the fact that end-result of probably the greatest price-stabilization experiment in history proved to be, simply, the greatest and worst depression"

### Keynes's Second Thoughts

- In *Treatise on Money* (1930), expressed something close to *mea culpa*:

> "Anyone who looked only at index of prices during 1920s would see no reason to suspect any material degree of inflation; whilst anyone who looked only at total volume of bank credit and prices of common stocks would have been convinced of presence of an inflation actual or impending."

### Central Bankers Reconsider

- German economist **Wilhelm Röpke**: "Better no credit control at all… than one based on this treacherous and dangerous criterion of price stability"
- 1937: Federal Reserve's *Objectives of Monetary Policy* affirmed "inadequacy of price stability as a guide to policy"

### Orthodox Narrative vs. Reality

- Most financial histories fail to mention contemporary criticisms of Fed's price stabilization policy
- Orthodox narrative: nothing wrong with 1920s economy, everything fine until Strong retired
- **Ben Bernanke**: "The true story is that monetary policy tried overzealously to stop the rise in stock prices"

### The Fed's Response to the Crash

- Fed slashed interest rates after October Crash: 6% (Oct 1929) to 1.5% (summer 1931)
- Critics say Fed should have done more to prevent money supply collapse
- Fed allowed debt deflation to take hold, deepening crisis
- Real interest rates climbed into double digits
- "Barbarous relic" (not price stabilization policy) held responsible for Great Depression

## Part Two: How Low Rates Begot Lower Rates

### Fisher's Debt-Deflation Theory

- **Irving Fisher's** 'Debt-Deflation Theory of Great Depressions' (1933)
- Debt-deflation cycle: borrowers pay down debts → depressing household spending → money supply contracts → prices fall further → borrowers owe more in real terms
- Debt deflation continues to point of "universal bankruptcy"
- Fisher's prescription: "reflation and stabilization of the price level"

### Ben Bernanke's Interpretation

- **Ben Bernanke** (head of Princeton economics, Fed governor from 2002): described himself as 'Great Depression buff'
- "Overwhelming evidence" main factor depressing demand was worldwide contraction in money supplies
- Future Fed Chairman concluded Great Depression was consequence of "hapless policymakers trying to make sense of events for which their experience had not prepared them"

### Hayek's Good Deflation Argument

- New technologies and efficiency improvements of 1920s brought "good" deflation
- When policymakers ease to ward off benign price decline, people incentivized to borrow more
- **Irving Fisher** observed: debt deflation starts from position of over-indebtedness
- **Bernanke** conspicuously overlooks this
- **Hayek** concluded: attempts to avoid good deflation only make 'bad' deflation more likely
- Bad deflation appearing during crisis should be viewed as symptom, not cause

### Evidence on Deflation and Growth

- **Keynes** thought attitude of Hayek and fellow 'liquidationists' sadistic
- Historical evidence suggests only tenuous link between deflation and economic weakness
- *The Forgotten Depression* by **James Grant**: during 1921 US deflation (price level -15% annualized, real rates ~20%), economy soon recovered and productivity soared

### Bank for International Settlements Study

- 2015 BIS paper examined data from 39 countries over 1.5 centuries (1870–2013)
- Several prolonged deflation periods accompanied by strong economic growth
- "On balance, relationship between changes in consumer price index and output growth is episodic and weak"
- Great Depression of 1930s is exception to the rule

### History Written by Victors

- Late 1920s: **Hayek** claimed monetary policy had taken wrong course, predicted deflationary bust
- **Irving Fisher** saw nothing wrong, opined summer 1929 US stocks reached "permanently high plateau"
- Yet Austrian's interpretation has been air-brushed from history books, while Fisher's monetarist view became received wisdom

### Bernanke's "Holy Grail of Macroeconomics"

- According to Bernanke's reading: Fed's key error was allowing consumer prices to fall after 1929
- At **Milton Friedman's** 90th birthday (Nov 2002), Governor **Bernanke** apologized on behalf of Fed for 1930s catastrophe
- Vowed it would not happen again
- Six years later: US facing most severe financial crisis in decades
- As newly installed Fed Chairman, **Bernanke** found himself in position to make good on promise
- He wasn't going to make the same mistakes again

## Chapter 7: Greenspan's Put

### The Greenspan Era

- **Greenspan** studied clarinet at Juilliard before economics; known for opaque communication
- October 1987: confronted with worst stock market collapse since 1929
  - Cut Fed funds rate, flooded Wall Street with liquidity
  - Market bounced back
  - Fed switched from bank borrowing control to direct interest rate targeting
  - **Key shift**: monetary policy focused on near-term inflation, ignoring financial imbalances

### The 1990s: Riding the Yield Curve

- After S&L crisis, Fed funds rate cut to below 3%, roughly half nominal GDP growth
- Greenspan wanted to help Wall Street by providing cheap short-term credit for "riding the yield curve"
- Mid-1990s: US productivity pickup suggested natural rate rising, yet short-term rates held below GDP growth rate
- **"New Paradigm"/"Goldilocks Economy"**: not too hot, not too cold
- December 1996: Greenspan's "irrational exuberance" question was rhetorical, elicited no action

### The Greenspan Put

- Low inflation gave Greenspan freedom to apply monetary balm during financial distress
- September 1998: Fed cut rates 25 bps after **Long Term Capital Management** near-failure
- **"Greenspan put"**: unwritten contract committing Fed to intervene to halt market declines
- Between October 1997 and peak (2.5 years later), Nasdaq gained nearly threefold
- **Greatest bubble in US history** (in valuation terms)

### Ben Bernanke Joins the Fed (2002)

- Provided intellectual ballast for ignoring asset price bubbles
- Argued bubbles impossible to identify in real time; monetary policy shouldn't act pre-emptively but deal with aftermath
- Strong advocate for acting pre-emptively against **deflation**
- November 2002: suggested Fed could drop "helicopter money" if necessary
- Spring 2003: Fed funds rate cut to 1%, remained there over a year
- Following five years: key policy rate remained well below economic growth rate
- **Era of easy money** had begun

### The Great Moderation

- Inflation remained under control; central bankers meeting targets
- Economists called low inflation/downturns of early 2000s the **"Great Moderation"**
- Bank of England coined **NICE** (non-inflationary consistent expansion)
- **Gordon Brown** (UK Chancellor) hailed end to boom and bust
- 2004: Greenspan boasted strategy of addressing bubble consequences had been "successful"

### Ignoring Real Estate Bubbles

- Replay of 1920s Fed under **Ben Strong**: scant attention to rapid credit growth or declining credit quality
- No attempt to restrain real estate bubbles
- Early 2006: Greenspan belatedly acknowledged some regional housing markets appeared "frothy"
- Bernanke told Congress house price increases "largely reflect strong economic fundamentals"
- In decade to 2008: Americans extracted **$9 trillion in home equity**

### FOMC Admission: Deliberate Asset Price Distortion

- 14 March 2004: **Donald Kohn** admitted:

> "policy accommodation is distorting asset prices. Most of this distortion is *deliberate* and a *desirable* effect of the stance of policy. *We have attempted to lower interest rates below long-term equilibrium rates and to boost asset prices in order to stimulate demand.*"

- None of Kohn's colleagues (including Greenspan and Bernanke) dissented

### William White's Dissent: "Is Price Stability Enough?"

- **William White** (chief economist, BIS) — exception to prevailing groupthink
- Like Hayek, White distinguished between:
  - **Good deflation**: from productivity improvements
  - **Bad deflation**: follows credit bust
- Credit growth, asset price inflation, large-scale capital inflows — more reliable crisis harbingers than consumer price inflation
- Questioned Bernanke's policy of dealing with aftermath rather than forestalling bubbles
- Over-indebted economy might enter **liquidity trap**
- If capital misallocated during boom, low rates after bust might contribute to economic sclerosis (as Japan experienced)
- "If low rates are maintained for extended period, they clearly have negative long-term effects with respect to aggregate supply"

### Post-Crisis Explanations

- Countless books written about global financial crisis causes
- Mainstream economists (oblivious beforehand) suddenly full of explanations
- **Bernanke** inclined to blame poor financial regulation (ironic: Fed chaired by Bernanke was chief US regulator)
- Among policymakers, regulatory interpretation won the day

### Monetary Policy's Role Downplayed

- Fed's role in crisis run-up downplayed:
  - Decision to take policy rate to post-war low
  - Keeping Fed funds rate below growth rate for five consecutive years
  - Intentional stoking of housing market
  - Opening monetary spigots
- Bernanke denied US monetary policy too loose from 2002: Fed's forecasts suggested heightened deflation risk

### The Reality: Low Rates Fueled Housing Bubble

- Bernanke's analysis ignores: riskiest subprime loans priced off short-term rates, including adjustable-rate mortgages with negative amortization
- Only after Fed's easy money policy launched:
  - Credit growth picked up
  - Financial leverage soared
  - Housing markets bubbled
  - Underwriting standards declined
- Low rates fed demand for credit; financial innovation increased supply

### Some Fed Officials Admit Errors

- **William Poole** (St Louis Fed President): acknowledged low rates fuelled housing boom
- Told Fed's committee (2007): "We had a boom driven by period of very low interest rates. Then you had a lot of financial innovations and subprime mortgages"
- **Thomas Hoenig** (Kansas City Fed): found "strong supporting evidence" that negative real rates between 2002-2004 contributed to housing and credit boom

### Leading Economists Concur

- **Anna Schwartz**: pinned responsibility on Fed; underlying propagator was "too-easy monetary policy and too-low interest rates"
- **John Taylor** (Stanford, "Taylor Rule" author): suggested "monetary excesses were the main cause of the boom and the resulting bust"

## Chapter 9: The Raven of Basel

### Larry Summers' Reversal on Secular Stagnation

- Summer 2019 tweet blamed central bankers for ultra-low nominal yields reducing aggregate demand, breeding financial instability, "zombification" and reduced economic dynamism
- Radical claim: ultra-low rates might **cause** secular stagnation rather than result from it

### Hume's Monetary Theory and Its Legacy

- **David Hume** viewed money as mere representation; interest determined by frugality (savings) and industry (return on capital)
- Hume's ideas anticipated **Milton Friedman's "helicopter money"** and **"neutrality of money"**
- **Hume was wrong** about British finance: dismissed bills of exchange, derided "paper credit", doubted banks' value
- Hume's framework embedded in canonical central bank models: interest derives from demographics, savings, profitability (real factors only)
- Central bankers denied responsibility for long-term rate collapse after 2008, blaming it on secular stagnation

### William White and Claudio Borio at the BIS

- **William White** (BIS chief economist) rare exception who anticipated financial crisis
- **Claudio Borio** (Oxford-educated) succeeded White as head of BIS Monetary and Economics Department in 2013
- 2003: White and Borio warned at **Jackson Hole** that financial storm clouds gathering

### Borio's Heterodox View

- Borio rejected secular stagnation narrative, casting aside "money veil"
- Financial crisis caused by **credit glut** ("banking glut"), not savings glut
- Banks conjure deposits, spending power, and savings when making loans

> "Think monetary! Modelling the financial cycle correctly… requires recognising the fundamental monetary nature of our economies."

> "Economics without finance is like Hamlet without the prince."

- We inhabit a **looking-glass world** where finance acts upon reality, not mirrors it

### Interest Rates Are a Policy Variable

- Borio's team found **no stable relationship** between interest rates and savings, investment, or demographics
- Interest rates influenced by **monetary regimes**: higher under Gold Standard than Bretton Woods, lowest after 1971
- **Raghuram Rajan**: if short-term rates expected to remain low for ten years, long-term rates will be low
- Fed funds rate had unusually strong influence on Treasury yields from turn of century onwards
- US long-term rates dropped decisively below post-war average only after Fed's influence became semi-permanent

### The Natural Rate Fallacy

- BIS questioned conventional "natural rate" concept (r-star)
- Borio argued: stable price level doesn't indicate natural rate — **both 1929 and 2008 crises occurred during low, stable inflation**
- Borio developed **financial cycle** based on credit growth and asset prices (better indicator than output gap)

### The Debt Trap and "Debt Supercycle"

- Debt was missing link in secular stagnation narrative
- BIS defined interest as **"price of leverage"**
- **Debt-service ratio** tends to remain constant over time
- Ever lower rates needed to sustain rising debt stock — **vicious cycle**: more debt requires lower rates, lower rates result in more debt

> "After all, if the origin of the problem was too much debt, how can a policy that encourages the private and public sectors to accumulate more debt be part of the solution?"

- Post-crisis problem: too much debt **and** poor quality debt
- Ultra-low rates after 2008 enabled banks to delay dealing with bad debts
- **Zombie companies** became commonplace — cumulative effect reduced potential output growth

### Fed's Asymmetric Policy and Moral Hazard

- Fed's reflexive easing after every bust but slow tightening during booms created **downward bias to rates** and **upward bias to debt**
- **Philip Lowe** and Borio (2002): "lowering rates when problems materialise but not raising rates as imbalances build up, can be rather insidious in longer run"
- Promotes moral hazard, sows seeds of instability

### Cross-Border Capital Flows

- BIS focused on **cross-border capital flows**
- **Gross capital flows** provide better indicator of financial fragility than current account balances
- Low US rates after 2002 spurred massive international capital flows
- Embers of Lehman still warm when global carry trade rekindled by Fed's zero rates
- Emerging markets accumulated trillions in foreign debts, sensitive to Fed tightening
- Summer 2013: **"taper tantrum"** when Bernanke signaled intention to reduce securities purchases

### Borio's Warnings About the Endgame

- Borio feared longer US rates stayed near zero, greater the build-up of global financial imbalances
- Envisaged **"epoch-defining seismic rupture"** heralding end of globalization
- Few months after Lehman, Borio warned about dangers of keeping extreme policies too long

> "The highly abnormal is becoming uncomfortably normal." (October 2014)

### Path Dependency and Loss of Efficacy

- Monetary policy is **path dependent** — once wrong turning taken, financial system and economy driven off course
- Economic growth falters, financial imbalances accumulate, capital misallocated, bubbles form, too much debt
- After crisis, central bankers tried QE, paying banks interest on reserves, zero/negative rates

> "If I were you, I would not start from here."

- Feared monetary policy losing efficacy; central bankers running out of ammunition

### Academic Establishment Dismisses BIS

- Establishment economists dismissed BIS warnings
- Critics compared Borio to **Hyman Minsky** (maverick whose insights belatedly recognized after subprime crisis)
- Both unorthodox, eclectic thinkers who observed **real world** vs academic models
- Both asserted primacy of finance in modern capitalism
- From Borio's perspective, secular stagnation was **monetary disease**
- Hume was mistaken: money is not veil; monetary policy not neutral; interest rates not determined solely by savings and industry

## Chapter 10: Unnatural Selection

### Interest and Creative Destruction

- **Arthur Hadley** (late 19th-century economist, Yale President): interest helps natural selection of most competent employers and best processes
- Interest eliminates "industrially unfit"
- **Joseph Schumpeter** (*Capitalism, Socialism and Democracy*): capitalism is process of industrial mutation — **Creative Destruction** is essential fact

> "The true function of interest is, so to speak, the brake, or governor on economic activity."

- Interest turns time into cost of production — entrepreneurs who save time emerge as winners
- Interest rations capital; from this perspective, **not a deadweight but spur to efficiency**

### Interest Rate as Shot Clock and Metronome

- **Matthew Klecker** (Chicago investor): compares interest rate to **shot clock** in basketball
- **James Grant**: zero-percent rates "institutionalize delay in every business and investment transaction"
- Grant compares rate to **metronome** whose beat helps musicians play in time
- At zero rates, economy progresses at **solemn pace of funeral march**

### Creative Destruction During Downturns

- Forces of creative destruction most unremitting during sharp economic downturns
- Cost of borrowing soars; sales dry up; bankers call in loans
- Firms cut costs fast, finding efficiency improvements
- Weakest players fail; survivors emerge leaner, fitter, better adapted

### Andrew Mellon vs. Keynes

- **Andrew Mellon's** advice to President Hoover: "liquidate labor, liquidate stocks, liquidate the farmers, liquidate real estate"
- **John Maynard Keynes** rejected laissez-faire approach to depressions
- October 1931: Keynes lectured "I do not understand how universal bankruptcy can do any good or bring us nearer to prosperity"
- Keynes's views became orthodox; Mellon's "liquidationist" ideas ridiculed

### The Great Depression and Productivity Growth

- Recent studies suggest otherwise
- **Car manufacturing**: sales fell ~two-thirds during Depression, but most efficient operators continued investing in new plants
- Inefficient car makers failed; survivors prospered after recovery

> "The Depression thus was, at the level of industries, an evolutionary event."

- Great Depression propelled productivity improvements across many industries
- **Alexander Field**: 1930s were most technologically progressive decade in American history
- Railroads: third of nation's trackage went into receivership, radically changed operations, became more efficient

### Post-Depression Economic Recovery

- Between 1929 and 1941: US economy expanded at **2.8% annual rate** (in line with long-term average)
- Americans growing up after war suffered no permanent welfare loss
- Even more remarkable productivity gains after **"forgotten depression" of 1921**: following year, manufacturing productivity jumped **massive 20%**

### Clément Juglar and Recessions as Creative Events

> "Paradoxical as it may seem, the riches of nations can be measured by the violence of the crises which they experience."

- Some economists take "pit-stop" view of recessions
- Business failures during downturns essential to economy's evolution
- **Frank Borman** (former astronaut, airline boss): "capitalism without bankruptcy is like Christianity without hell"

### Europe's Sovereign Debt Crisis

- Eurozone's sovereign debt crisis (erupted 2010) viewed through competitiveness lens
- From interest rate perspective: **one size didn't fit all**
- From mid-1990s onwards, rates among future Eurozone members converged toward German benchmark
- Ireland: rates fell, housing boom gathered pace
- Early 2000s: ECB conducted monetary policy for lethargic Germany (rates too low for booming Ireland and Greece)

### PIIGS Crisis and Draghi's Intervention

- **PIIGS** (Portugal, Italy, Ireland, Greece, Spain) found themselves deep in mire
- **Gita Gopinath**: shift toward construction depressed productivity

> "By lowering interest rates and encouraging inflow of capital, adoption of euro may have been partly responsible for misallocation of capital and low productivity observed in South of Europe."

- 2010: financial markets viewed Europe's periphery as over-indebted and uncompetitive
- Bound by euro-fetters, couldn't regain competitiveness by devaluing
- **Mario Draghi** became ECB President
- July 2012 promise: "do whatever it takes" to hold Eurozone together
- Draghi successfully extinguished Europe's debt crisis, saved Single Currency

### The Living Dead: Zombie Companies

- Term **"zombie"** first applied to floundering US Savings & Loan associations (late 1980s)
- **Gresham's Law**: bad money drives out good money
- Under certain conditions applies to business: "bad zombie thrifts tend to drive out healthy competition"
- After Japan's Bubble Economy collapse, graveyard full of corporate zombies
- First half 1990s: Japanese banks decided to "evergreen" non-performing loans rather than recognize losses
- **"Unnatural selection"** determined capital allocation
- Zombification of Japan's economy associated with excess capacity across many industries
- Corporate profits and returns on capital declined, productivity growth suffered

### Japan's Zero Interest Rate Policy

- Between 1991 and 1995: **Bank of Japan** lowered policy rate from 6% to 0.5% (on way to zero)
- Extremely low rates spread loan forbearance into deepest reaches of economy
- IMF economists found zero rate policy helped banks disguise problem loans, relaxed pressure on policymakers to institute structural reforms
- As pace of economic change slowed, Japan embarked on lost decade

### A New Plague of Zombies in Europe

- Europe post-2012 proved ideal breeding ground for new generation of corporate zombies
- Zero rates kept loss-making companies on life support
- By 2016: **OECD** found **10% of firms unable to cover interest payments from profits** (definition of zombie)
- Europe turning Japanese

### Italy's Zombie Infestation

- Lowest insolvency rates reported by Greece, Spain, Italy
- Italy had worst zombie infestation
- Industrial overcapacity rife; country home to two dozen tire producers
- As in Japan, Europe's zombies gnawed at vitals of banking system
- By 2015: estimated European banks had **more than €1 trillion of bad debts**

### ECB's Monetary Balm Removes Reform Impetus

- Another feature in common with Japan: ECB's monetary balm removed impetus for structural reforms
- Prior to Draghi's arrival, around half of OECD's recommended growth initiatives adopted
- After sovereign debt crisis alleviated, politicians sat back
- IMF warned: "without drastic changes, they are likely to follow Japan's path to long economic stagnation"

### Italy's Lost Fifteen Years

- Fifteen years since start of euro project: Italy enjoyed no increase in income per capita
- Labor costs climbed relative to Germany's
- Italy's public debt trailed only Japan's and Greece's
- Italian banks loaded down with hundreds of billions of bad debts
- Many largest businesses certified zombies
- IMF warned: "in absence of deeper structural reforms, medium-term growth is projected to remain low"
- As in Japan, easy money bought time but **time was wasted**

### A Herd of Unicorns

- Zombies aren't only malinvestments induced by ultra-low rates
- Easy money encourages investment in projects whose returns lie in distant future
- After US real estate bubble imploded (2006), investors searched for other long-dated investments
- Silicon Valley was only too happy to supply their needs

### Unicorns Defined

- 2013: venture capitalist **Aileen Lee** coined term **"unicorn"** for start-ups valued at more than billion dollars
- By 2015: nearly 150 existed with combined market valuation around half trillion dollars

> "A little-known fact about unicorns is that they feed on interest rates. They like low, little rates – the tinier, the better." — **James Grant**

- Low rates made it attractive to trade current earnings for potential future revenues
- **Elizabeth Holmes** and **Theranos**: claimed blood test technology, achieved $9 billion valuation — but the technology didn't work. Bled investors of $1+ billion before bankruptcy
- **Uber**: founded 2010, raised $20 billion, lost $14+ billion in four years before 2019 IPO. **Hubert Horan**: "What Uber has disrupted is the idea that competitive markets will maximize welfare by rewarding companies with superior efficiency."
- **WeWork**: revenue doubled to $1.5 billion in H1 2019, but operating losses grew faster. *Reuters*: "WeWork is a poster child of longest U.S. economic recovery on record, and an era where money is virtually free."

### The Productivity Puzzle

- Post-2008 productivity collapsed across developed economies
- US annual productivity growth: 0.5% (quarter of its level two decades earlier)
- UK: output per hour hardly budged — worst performance since Industrial Revolution
- Central bankers didn't foresee it — called it "conundrum," "puzzle," "drag"

**Conventional explanations (all flawed):**
- Demographics, regulation, lack of innovation — didn't apply equally to every afflicted economy
- Secular stagnationists couldn't explain why productivity collapse only became marked *after* Lehman's failure

### Business Formation Collapsed

- New business formation in US fell sharply after 2008
- 2016: business deaths outnumbered births for first time since 1978
- Regulatory red tape discouraged entrepreneurs, but **unconventional monetary policies were probably more influential**
- Bank of England officials privately admitted UK's mild recession "had not 'generate[d] sufficient destruction to enable creation of more productive companies'"

### Job Market Stagnation

- **Job churn rate** tracked falling interest rates
- After 2008: fewer bankruptcies, fewer start-ups = less job creation and destruction
- Fewer jobs created or destroyed by technology than at any time since Industrial Revolution
- **Steven Davis** and **John Haltiwanger**: "Stifling [job] reallocation stifles growth as well"

### Investment Famine

- Ultra-low rates supposed to induce borrowing and investment — but investment collapsed throughout developed world
- Economies on both sides of Atlantic saddled with **excess capacity**
- 2017 New York Fed study: adjusted for faster depreciation rates, US had enjoyed little net investment
- Even Federal Reserve agreed falling investment contributed to slowdown in productivity growth (but couldn't acknowledge monetary policy's role)

### Zombies Explain the Puzzle

- Growing research suggests **zombies** lay behind productivity debacle
- Zombies discouraged other firms from investing
- Zombies slowed adoption of new technologies and business practices

> "When too many resources are stuck in low productivity areas and in zombie businesses… then the potential for the wider positive impact of particular innovative business investments will be frustrated."

### Central Banks' Response: More Easing

- Central bankers saw productivity decline as reason to *extend* unconventional monetary policies
- **Ben Bernanke**: described interest-rate implications of sinking US productivity as "dovish"
- **Claudio Borio** (BIS) turned conventional thinking upside down: referred to "interest rate–productivity nexus," with causality running from low interest rates to low investment and falling productivity
- BIS found close relationship between share of zombie companies and decline in policy rates
- Low rates begot zombies, and zombies begot lower rates
- Rate of interest both reflects *and* determines return on capital

### Economic Stagnation

- Ultra-low rates reduced economic volatility
- But economic volatility, as **Clément Juglar** observed, is a source of vitality
- **Schumpeter**: "Capitalism, then, is by nature a form of economic change and not only never is but never can be stationary."
- Lowest interest rates in history put brake on Schumpeter's evolutionary process
- **Creative destruction replaced by unnatural selection and capital destruction**

## Chapter 11: The Promoter's Profit

> "When a nation is running to decay and ruin, the merchant, and monied man, do what you can, will be sure to starve last." — **John Locke**, 1691

### The Gilded Age: Wall Street's First Golden Era

- 1892: **General Electric** formed from merger, managed by **Charles Coffin**
- GE was a creature of Wall Street from outset
- **Thomas Lawson**: GE's "high-priest was J. Pierpont Morgan; its home, Wall Street"
- **"The System"**: means by which savings of ordinary people were "always at absolute service and mercy of votaries of frenzied finance"

### Interest Rates and the Bond Bull Market

- Following financial crisis of 1873, interest rates in US declined for more than a century
- In decade when GE founded, US Treasury bond yields fell below 2%
- Federal surpluses: US national debt fell by half between 1880 and 1893
- Strong demand from National Banks sent yields on some Treasuries below zero
- **First and only time long-term interest rates turned negative prior to 21st century**

### Easy Money and Trust Formation

- Easy money enabled Wall Street to consolidate swathes of American industry
- **Arthur Hadley**: defined interest as "the price paid for the control of industry"
- Industries that came under trust control: coal, iron, cement, tobacco, paper, sugar, petroleum, rubber, flour

### The Robber Barons

- Trusts gave birth to **"robber barons"**
- **John D. Rockefeller**: forced competitors to sell to his **Standard Oil**
- **Edward H. Harriman**: became America's most powerful railway magnate
- Same year: Morgan amalgamated **Carnegie Steel** with other concerns
- Result: **United States Steel Corporation** — world's largest steel producer
- When went public, US Steel capitalized at $1.4 billion, more than double book value
- **"Over-capitalization"**: anticipated profits that would accrue to its monopoly

### The Promoter's Profit

- Most profits from forming trusts fell to Wall Street
- Trick: acquire company on leverage, "water" the stock, merge operations, float amalgamated business at higher price
- **Thorstein Veblen** (*Theory of Business Enterprise*, 1904):

> "the certainty of gain… seems rather more assured in large-scale manipulation of vendible capital than in business management… Indeed, so secure and lucrative is this class of business, that it is chiefly out of gains accruing from such traffic in vendible capital that the great modern fortunes are being accumulated."

### Rudolf Hilferding and the Promoter's Profit

- **Rudolf Hilferding**, *Finance Capital* (1910): coined concept of **"promoter's profit"**
- Observed share prices rise/fall in inverse relationship to rate of interest
- **Hilferding's original observation**: when returns on industry were above cost of borrowing, financiers poised to reap windfall gains
- **The lower the interest rate, the higher the promoter's profit**

### Wall Street's Focus on Financial Engineering

- Wall Street more interested in financial chicanery than directing capital toward economic development
- Only small part of Union Pacific Railroad securities spent on fixed assets — rest going "mainly to supply funds for engaging in illegal combinations or stock speculation"
- **Pierpont Morgan** himself had little interest in new technologies

### Other People's Money

- Trust movement seen to be supported by "vested interests"
- **Brandeis**, *Other People's Money* (1914): inveighed against "financial recklessness," "mad pursuit of monopoly," "curse of bigness"
- Year Brandeis' polemic appeared: several directors of New Haven Railroad indicted for conspiracy to monopolize
- By this date, great Pierpont Morgan was dead

### Fast-forward a Hundred Years

- Striking similarities between financial conditions of early 20th century and post-Lehman era
- Once again, "price paid for control of industry" at all-time low, creating conditions for wave of anti-competitive mergers
- Once again, Wall Street's efforts directed towards manipulation of share prices
- Once again, workers felt pinch and talk of economic stagnation filled the air

### 'The Gold-Diggers' Song'

- Early May 2018: **Mike Coupe** (CEO of Sainsbury's) inadvertently captured on camera singing: "We're in the money / Come on my honey / Let's lend it, spend it, send it rolling along"
- Merger "tsunami" prompted no response in Washington
- US Justice Department failed to file single anti-trust case in 2014
- 2015: global M&A topped $5 trillion for first time

### Collapse in Corporate Borrowing Costs

- More important than Washington's passivity: collapse in corporate borrowing costs
- Large companies could borrow at lower rate than they earned on their capital, creating opportunity to extract "promoter's profit"

### Kraft Heinz: A Case Study

- March 2015 acquisition of **Kraft** by ketchup-maker **H. J. Heinz** (itself LBO controlled by **3G Capital** and **Warren Buffett's Berkshire**)
- Aim: boost returns by adding debt and cutting costs
- Over following couple years: net debt increased by more than half, per-share earnings nearly doubled

### Platform Companies and Monopoly Formation

- **Kraft Heinz**: one of many "platform companies" (similar to trusts of robber baron era), which used debt to finance anti-competitive mergers
- No platform company soared higher than **Valeant Pharmaceuticals**
- **Valeant** acquired 20+ medical and drug businesses, boosted profits by slashing costs and pushing through large price hikes
- By 2015: Valeant's long-term liabilities approached $30 billion
- Despite debt load, borrowing costs remained below 6%

### Rising Corporate Monopolies

- Merger wave brought about rise in corporate monopolies not seen since Gilded Age
- Number of listed US companies halved in two decades prior to 2016
- Academic study: three-quarters of American industries had become significantly more concentrated
- **University of Michigan economists**: found price-fixing cartels more influenced by level of interest rates than any other factor

### Impact of Industry Concentration

- Firms in industries with greatest increase in concentration enjoyed higher profits
- But as **Adam Smith** observed, monopolies don't serve public good
- Rising industry concentration associated with: higher pay for senior executives, decline in workers' bargaining power, falling investment and R&D
- **NBER economists**: "low interest rates have traditionally been viewed as positive for growth… extremely low interest rates may lead to *slower* growth by increasing market concentration"

### The Virtual Wealth Bubble

#### Extraordinary Wealth Surge
- 2009-2018: US household wealth nearly doubled to **$100 trillion+** (5x GDP)
- Post-war decades: household wealth averaged 3.5x GDP
- Never before had Americans been so rich, never before done so little to amass such wealth

#### John Stuart Mill vs. John Ruskin on Wealth
- **John Stuart Mill**: wealth consists of anything "which enables a person to claim from others a part of their stock of things useful or pleasant"
- By Mill's definition: high-flying but useless cryptocurrency = wealth
- **John Ruskin**: wealth derived from "possession of useful articles which we can use"

> "That which seems to be wealth may in verity be only the gilded index of far-reaching ruin; a wrecker's handful of coin gleaned from beach to which he has beguiled an argosy" — Ruskin

#### Frederick Soddy on Energy-Based Wealth
- On Ruskin's gravestone: "there is no wealth but life"
- **Frederick Soddy** (Nobel-prize winning chemist, self-taught economist): life = struggle for energy, capital = stored energy
- "All wealth is the product of work, in the physical sense of the expenditure of available energy"
- "Wealth" of millionaires "turns out to be virtual … and to consist mostly of claims to wealth"
- **Adam Smith**: "Real wealth derives from annual produce of land and labour of society"
- Much millennial wealth was mere claims whose market value multiplied as discount rate declined

#### Professor John C. Edmunds: "New World Wealth Machine" (1996)

> "Many societies, and indeed the entire world, have learned how to create wealth directly. The new approach requires that a state find ways to increase the market value of its stock of productive assets … *An economic policy that aims to achieve growth by wealth creation therefore does not attempt to increase the production of goods and services, except as a secondary objective.*"

#### Federal Reserve's Wealth Machine
- Simplest way to inflate asset values: lower interest rate (path chosen by Fed post-2008)
- **Chairman Bernanke** (November 2010 *Washington Post*): "Lower corporate bond rates will encourage investment. Higher stock prices will boost consumer wealth and help increase confidence"
- Fed using monetary policy to power "New World Wealth Machine"

#### Japan's Lesson Ignored
- Late 1980s: Japanese brought down rates to boost domestic consumption
- Unnamed BoJ official: "We intended first to boost both stock and property markets [with low rates] … This wealth-effect would in turn touch off personal consumption"
- After Nikkei turned down (1990), senior official: "it's time for bubble to burst … real, productive economy won't really be hurt. Land and people won't disappear, but phony wealth will"
- **Delusion**: Japanese companies engaged in *zaitech* (financial engineering), faced large losses after property collapse
- Too much "real" economic activity dependent on "phony wealth"

#### Post-Crisis Financialization Accelerates
- By 2008: 1 in 10 US workers employed in industry
- Pre-crisis: most new jobs in construction or services benefiting from real estate/credit boom
- FIRE sectors (finance, insurance, real estate) rose to 50%+ larger than manufacturing
- Global financial crisis didn't kill financialization but accelerated it
- Post-Lehman: finance contributed even larger share of US economic growth
- Finance sector's share of economic pie grew to roughly 3x historic average
- "We've substituted finance for industry as locomotive of economic growth. In GDP terms, it looks terrific. But it is neither enduring nor real"

### A Profits Bubble

#### Post-2008 Profit Boom
- After 2008: even as factories idle and unemployment high, profits rebounded
- 2010-2014: total profits (as share of GDP) ~40% above post-war average
- Most important contributor to rising profitability: steep decline in corporate borrowing costs
- **Jeremy Grantham**: "single largest input to higher [profit] margins … is likely to be existence of much lower real interest rates since 1997"
- Had interest rates remained at normal levels, profits would have been average
- Another study: policy of "maintaining overall production (GDP) while lowering both labor and capital costs has inflated a *profits bubble*"
- Post-2010: financial services accounted for 20%+ of total US profits (~2x post-war average)

### The Bubble Economy

## Chapter 13: Your Mother Needs to Die

> "Wipe out rentiers with cheap money." — Martin Wolf, 2014

### Interest as "The Wage of Abstinence"
- Interest affects valuation/allocation of capital plus how much saving takes place
- Saving requires consuming less today — exercise of thrift not easy
- **Nassau Senior** (Oxford, 1836): "To abstain from the enjoyment which is in our power, or to seek distant rather than immediate results, are among the most painful exertions of the human will"

> "Interest is *the wage of abstinence*" — Nassau Senior

### Socialist Mockery
- **Ferdinand Lassalle** (German socialist leader) mocked: "Interest is the 'wage of renunciation!' Your European millionaires are ascetics. Like Indian penitents they stand, like stylites on one foot atop a pillar"
- **John Stuart Mill** pointed out: while rich had enormous resources, ordinary people also saved part of incomes
- Interest reflected the *marginal* pain of their abstinence

### Interest as Time Preference and Intertemporal Bridge
- **Frédéric Bastiat**: "To save is deliberately to put an interval between the moment when the services are made for the society, and that when the equivalent is received from it"
- The reward for this *ajournement* is interest

> "Interest is, as it were, human impatience crystallized into a market rate." — Irving Fisher

- Economists talk about **time preference** — how people value present vs. future goods differently
- **Finance is the intertemporal bridge** joining present and future
- By borrowing/lending and saving/investing, we shift expenditures across time
- Interest = toll on borrowers for bringing forward consumption, fee to savers for moving consumption into future

### Market Rate vs. Society's "Crystallized Impatience"
- Balance upset when market rate of interest falls below society's "crystallized impatience"
- When market rate below public's time preference: people borrow to consume
- Abnormally low rate boosts current spending, but benefits don't last

> "Becoming rich is a choice between today's consumption and tomorrow's. If I cut my consumption sharply and save a large proportion of my wage income, then in a few years I will be richer." — **William Easterly**

### Gustav Cassel on Compound Interest
- **Benjamin Franklin**: "Money is of a prolific generating Nature. Money can beget Money, and its Offspring can beget more"
- **Gustav Cassel** (*The Nature and Necessity of Interest*, 1903): published table showing years required for savings to double at different interest rates:

| Rate | 6% | 3% | 2% | 1.5% | 1% |
|------|----|----|----|----- |----|
| Years to double | 12 | 24 | 35 | 47 | 70 |

- At 6%, savings double every 12 years; at 1%, takes 70 years
- Cassel believed if rate fell below 1%, saving would no longer be viable
- Faced with such low returns, retirees could only maintain spending by consuming capital

> "If such a practice became general, it would have quite a disastrous effect." — Gustav Cassel

## Piketty's Error (Sidebar)

### Capital in the Twenty-First Century
- **Thomas Piketty's** book became overnight sensation (spring 2014)
- Proposed fundamental law: inequality rises whenever return on capital (profits, interest, dividends, rents) exceeds growth rate of economy
- Formula: **r > g** (r = return on capital, g = rate of economic growth)

### Scrutiny of Piketty's Theory
- IMF tested fundamental law in nineteen advanced economies: found in most countries, inequality declined when gap between returns on capital and growth expanded
- Historical data from US early 19th century doesn't support hypothesis
- During this period: rate of return on capital and interest rates declined and asset valuations rose

> "The wealthy pulled ahead of the rest of society especially in the cities of the Northeast, on the strength of their savings, access to credit, and capital gains, not from any rise in the rate of return. And the wealthy pulled ahead for another reason: the development of the domestic financial sector." — **Lindert and Williamson**

### Piketty's Flawed Assumptions
- Piketty assumed wealth compounds with time, whereas rich divide fortunes and most savings spent in retirement
- 'Buddenbrooks effect' — rags to riches and back to rags in three generations
- Piketty made fundamental error: employed terms 'capital' and 'wealth' interchangeably, making no distinction between *capital* (productive assets) and *wealth* (financial claims with no link to production)
- Most of 'comeback of private wealth' was in form of financial claims (*virtual wealth*) at time when capital investment weakening

### Alternative "Iron Law of Inequality"
- On closer inspection: Piketty's 'capital in twenty-first century' turns out to be wealth bubble
- Since wealth unevenly distributed and top incomes correlated with stock prices → wealth bubbles responsible for much increase in inequality
- Central argument of book: wealth bubbles occur when interest rate held below its natural level
- Thus: inequality liable to increase when rate of interest held *below* economy's growth rate
- **r < g** (r = rate of interest, g = economy's trend growth)
- Formula (inverse of Piketty's) can explain both changes in distribution during 1920s and rise in inequality since 1980s

## Chapter 15: The Price of Anxiety

### Galiani's Innovation
- **Ferdinando Galiani** (Neapolitan abbé): "most profound, sharp-sighted and perhaps the most salacious man of his century" (Nietzsche)
- **Schumpeter**: 'one of ablest minds ever became active in our field'
- First to explain why useful things like water have lower market value than diamonds

### On Money (1751)
- Galiani rejected traditional view that charging for loans inherently unjust
- Correctly described interest as representing difference 'between present money and money that is distant in time'
- But justified existence of interest on grounds that each loan carries some risk of loss, which induces anxiety in lender

> "Keeping someone in anxiety is pain, hence it must be paid for"

> "What is called the product of money, when it is legitimate, is simply *the price of anxiety*"

### Interest as Price of Anxiety/Insurance
- Interest 'bears the same ratio to the capital as the probability of loss bears to the probability of repayment'
- Since each loan carries different risk, interest

> "is as varied as are the almost infinite degrees of probability of loss, which is very great in some cases (as in maritime usury), sometimes falls to zero (as in the banks and companies of republics), and sometimes even below zero (as happened in France in the time of Law's System)"

- Interest can be seen as 'price of insurance'

### Risk and Interest Connection
- Galiani's description of interest as insurance premium strikingly modern
- Finance in twenty-first century underpinned by role interest plays in pricing risk
- Enormous variety of risks incorporated into rate of interest: credit risk, legal risk, liquidity risk, inflation risk, duration risk
- Bank spread between loans and deposits akin to insurance premium

### Moral Hazard in Credit Markets
- Moral hazard occurs when interest rate (Galiani's *price of insurance*) set too low
- Credit booms occur at times of easy money, when financial risks mispriced
- On eve of financial crisis: insurance giant **Marsh** ran campaign with slogan "I risk therefore I am"
- Capitalism is economic system which both rewards and punishes people for taking risks
- When price of risk set too low: too much risk assumed, new risks multiply, financial system becomes unstable

### Investor Behavior at Low Rates
- **Walter Bagehot** understood investors prone to behave rashly when interest rates fall below certain level
- Recent study finds: when government bond yields fall below average, investors take on more risk
- "Interest rates play an important role in affecting risk-taking behavior" — **Ma and Zijlstra**
- Investors "have a deeply ingrained notion that saving is the preservation of wealth and wealth should grow at a 'decent' rate"
- Propensity to assume risk moves inversely with interest rate
- Risk-taking becomes extreme when bond yields fall below 3%

## Carry Trades and Financial Instability

### Carry Trade Mechanics
- When short-term rates low: investors have incentive to take out loans to buy higher-yielding assets
- Difference between cost of borrowing and return on risky loans = 'carry'
- **Carry trades** involve numerous forms: hedge funds using leverage to buy mortgage-backed securities, landlords taking mortgages on rental properties
- Returns are **asymmetric**: steady small gains with exposure to sudden large losses
- "Pick up nickels in front of steamrollers" or "eat like a bird, shit like an elephant"
- Losses can't be accurately predicted since returns are **fat-tailed**

### 2007 Carry Trade Collapse
- When liquidity dried up and credit spreads widened early 2007, leveraged players forced to unwind
- **Shadow banks** collapsed: mortgage REITs, structured investment vehicles, bank conduits
- **AIG** discovered it had sold credit default swap insurance too cheaply

### Déjà Vu All Over Again
- After Fed cut rates to zero in 2008, investors desperate for yield
- Post-2008 yield-chasing took myriad forms: peer-to-peer lending, yieldcos, BDCs, preference share funds, high-yield municipal bonds, REITs
- Money market funds "broke the buck" after Lehman, forcing them to take more credit risk
- **Catastrophe bonds** became popular

> "The fixed-income market was 'acting like one giant insurance company'" — *WSJ* investor quote

### The Fed as Carry Trader
- QE explicitly designed to encourage Wall Street to take more risk via **portfolio balance channel**
- Fed sold Treasury bonds/mortgage securities to investors, who were supposed to buy higher-yielding securities
- "The Fed became possibly the biggest carry trader of all"
- In 2015, Fed earned nearly **$100 billion profit** on its securities portfolio, financed by printing dollars

## Junk Bonds and Leveraged Loans

### Collapse in Underwriting Standards
- After Lehman, US junk bond yields soared 20+ percentage points above Treasuries
- Fed's actions prevented expected corporate bankruptcy tsunami
- **Henry Kravis** (KKR): Del Monte Foods acquisition (March 2011) completed with "most attractive financing that we've ever done"
- **Edward Altman** (NYU): attributed incipient credit bubble to "insatiable appetite for higher yields in this low-interest rate environment"

### Leveraged Loans Explosion
- Issued by companies with poor credit ratings, often for buyout deals
- **Covenant-lite** issues dominated: no traditional creditor protections
- By 2018: **$1 trillion** leveraged loans outstanding (equal to high-yield bond market)

### Private Equity Feeding Frenzy
- Buyout firms used leveraged loans/junk bonds to finance deals, extract dividends, flip companies
- Easy financing let PE firms raise more money, acquire more companies, pile on more leverage
- Yield-chasing "nothing if not myopic"

### Investment-Grade Debt Binge
- Post-2008: American companies issued record quantities of investment-grade bonds at unprecedented low yields
- **Gresham's Law** operated: debt on lowest rung (BBB) proliferated, while AAA bonds became rarity
- Most money raised **not for productive investment** but for **share buybacks** in record quantities
- "The most extensive carry trade of all"

### Subprime Auto Loans
- This time subprime loans went to **car buyers** with poor credit instead of homebuyers
- Outstanding US car loans passed **$1 trillion** in 2015 (one-fifth owed by subprime borrowers)
- Glut of vehicles coming off leases flooded secondhand market
- Car owners left "upside down" — just like subprime housing crisis

## Duration Risk

### Duration as Profit Strategy
- When yield curve slopes upward, longer-maturity bonds provide more income
- When rates decline, owning long-dated bonds generates capital gains
- **Japan 40-year bond** (late 2015): 1.4% coupon; after BoJ announced negative rates, price climbed **more than one-third**

### Yield-Starved Duration Extension
- Post-2008: investors embraced duration "as never before"
- American banks extended duration in securities portfolios
- 2016 survey showed several central bank asset managers lengthened portfolio duration

### Negative-Yield Duration Play
- By summer 2016: **$12 trillion** of bonds had negative yields
- Tokyo insurance executive: "Yields don't matter" as redemption yield on 30-year Japanese bond turned negative
- **Capital gains offset income loss** as rates declined
- Investors should buy negative-yielding bonds for capital gains, equities for income (inverted logic)

### Government Century Bonds
- Governments capitalized on duration frenzy to lengthen loan maturities
- 2015: **Ireland and Belgium** issued bonds with **100-year maturities**
- **Petrobras** (Brazil): bonds promising redemption into twenty-second century found strong demand
- **Italy**: fifty-year bond yielding less than 3% heavily oversubscribed

### Duration Risk Exposure
- Duration profitable in bull market but also a **risk factor**
- When rates rise, long-dated bondholders suffer most
- **Goldman Sachs**: aggregate duration of corporate bonds worldwide **doubled between 2006 and 2016**
- **Bloomberg**: mere half percentage point rate increase would generate **$1.6 trillion of losses** in fixed-income markets

### Bond Market Bubble Characteristics
- Abnormal valuations
- "This-time-is-different" mindset
- Moral hazard from continuous central bank interventions
- Massive bond issuance
- Widespread myopia blinding investors to loss prospects
- "No capitalist was ever more supine than the millennial fixed-income investor"

### Warnings Ignored
- **James Grant** (summer 2016): negative-yielding bonds in fiat currency belong in next edition of *Extraordinary Popular Delusions*
- **Paul Singer** (month earlier): "biggest bond bubble in world history"
- **Alan Greenspan**: "By any measure, real long-term interest rates are much too low and therefore unsustainable"
- Retired Fed chief told *Bloomberg* (July 2017): "We are experiencing a bubble, not in stock prices but in bond prices"
- **Greenspan never identified a single bubble during his Fed tenure**

## Liquidity Risk

### Keynes on Liquidity Premium
- **Keynes**: interest exists because savers must be bribed to surrender cash safety
- "Rate of interest" is "the reward for parting with liquidity for a specified period"

### Easy Money Destroys Liquidity
- When rates decline to very low levels, investors "blithely surrender liquidity for a little extra income"
- During Greenspan's easy money: money poured into asset-backed securities and illiquid shadow banking products
- Subprime crisis produced **"dash for cash"** as leveraged players unwound positions
- Wall Street's carry traders "well and truly steamrollered"

### Post-Crisis Shadow Banking 2.0
- Ultra-low rates induced amnesia about painful losses
- **Collateralized loan obligations (CLOs)** — nearly identical to toxic CDOs — found ready market
- **Ultra-short bond ETFs** provided income-enhancing alternative to money market funds
- Decade after financial crisis: **$1 trillion+** invested in credit ETFs

### ETF Liquidity Illusion
- Investors "sailing into very shallow waters"
- Trading in ETFs/bond funds exceeded turnover of underlying assets
- **Liquidity risk exacerbated** by new banking regulations stopping Wall Street speculation
- Broker-dealers retreated from traditional market-making role
- Daily turnover in bond markets declined

> "[T]he extent of the breakage is masked by the current environment of low interest rates and low volatility, coupled with the positive impact of QE on credit markets … A less-friendly market environment will expose the underlying structure as broken, with the potential for even lower liquidity and sharp, discontinuous price deterioration." — **BlackRock**, 2014

### Liquidity Crises
- **Keynes**: "there is no such thing as liquidity of investment for the community as a whole"
- **Late August 2015**: after China currency devaluation, hundreds of ETFs traded below net asset value
- **December 2015**: Third Avenue suspended redemptions in illiquid corporate debt fund
- **Summer 2018**: Global Asset Management bond fund manager sacked after investing in opaque/illiquid assets
- *FT*: case of "illiquid love" signaled "potential pitfalls of the frenetic search for higher returns in an era of record-low interest rates"

## The Price of Volatility

### Volatility Basics
- **Liquidity**: ability to trade without moving prices
- **Volatility**: price movement between trades
- Inversely related: volatility declines when liquidity abundant; volatility spikes when liquidity dries up
- **VIX index**: calculates implied volatility of US stock market from options prices on S&P 500

### Post-Crisis Volatility Collapse
- After Lehman: VIX climbed to record high (just short of 90)
- Post-crisis: market volatility collapsed, VIX remained lower for longer than ever before
- Each Fed QE round occurred at successively lower stock market volatility levels
- **Kevin Warsh** (former FOMC member): QE "works because we take volatility out of financial markets"
- **Dan Galai** (VIX creator, September 2017): link between ultra-low rates and ultra-low volatility

### Volatility Selling as Carry Trade
- Once volatility declined, traders bought stock market dips, confident Fed had their backs
- Traders sold **VIX futures** ("shorting vol"), earning profits as long as markets stayed becalmed
- "Carry trade of carry trades proved the most profitable of all"
- **Credit Suisse XIV fund**: climbed **elevenfold** between late 2010 and early 2018

### Volatility Strategies and Leverage
- IMF estimated (2017): **$500 billion** managed in volatility-targeting strategies
- IMF warned: low volatility regime encouraging investors to take on more leverage
- Hedge funds implemented **"risk parity"** strategy allocating investments based on historic volatility, leveraging less volatile assets
- Complex strategies had much in common with **portfolio insurance** (triggered October 1987 crash)

### February 5, 2018 "Volmageddon"
- Slight uptick in VIX forced volatility sellers to cover positions
- Scramble to buy volatility protection drove VIX higher, creating doom loop
- Over course of day: **VIX doubled** (largest increase on record)
- Credit Suisse's note lost **97% of value**
- US stocks: sharpest decline in years, shaving around **$1 trillion** off market cap
- Yet nothing happened on Main Street
- **"Volmageddon"** turned out to be Matrix glitch — wealth that evaporated was virtual

## Beer Goggles

### Central Banker Deniability
- Central bankers' attitude to yield-chasing, bond bubble, credit/duration/liquidity/volatility risk: **plausible deniability**
- Theoretical models stated monetary policy shouldn't change investors' risk perceptions
- In practice: Fed doing everything to get investors to assume more risk
- **Chairman Bernanke**: got around quandary by joking QE worked in practice but not in theory

### Secular Stagnation Narrative
- Central bankers denied responsibility for long-term rate collapse
- Financial world suffering from "abundance of caution" after 2008
- Cursory observation suggested investors "only too tolerant of risk"
- Stock of Treasury debt **doubled** in post-crisis decade — if Treasuries in short supply, largely because **Fed snapped up so many**

### "Beer Goggles" Critique
- **Larry Summers** (2014): post-crisis interest rates consistent with full employment not consistent with financial stability
- **PIMCO's Bill Gross**: "Never have investors reached so high in price for so low a return"
- **James Grant**: "Federal Reserve policy of zero per cent interest rates and monetary expansion has, by design, forced investors further out on the risk–return spectrum"
- Grant compared low rates to **"beer goggles"** blinding investors to financial risk
- **Christopher Cole** (Artemis Capital): monetary policymakers brought investment returns from future into present, pushing financial risk from present into future

### Jeremy Stein's Warnings (February 2013)
- **Jeremy Stein** (Harvard economist, single term on Fed board): landmark speech suggesting changes in monetary environment altered risk-taking behavior

> "a prolonged period of low interest rates, of the sort we are experiencing today, can create incentives for agents to take on greater duration or credit risks, or to employ additional financial leverage, in an effort to 'reach for yield'."

- Low rates spurred development of new financial products to satisfy demand for investment income
- Regulation "all very well" but only monetary policy "gets into all the cracks"
- Central bank's predictability encouraged financial players to take on more leverage
- More Fed fretted about Wall Street, more cautious it became, lower interest rates fell

### Shadow Banking Warnings
- **Dallas Fed** (2016): warned about re-emergence of unregulated shadow banking
- **Boston Fed President**: US commercial real estate market "driven in part by the scramble for returns in the low-yield world"
- As tenth anniversary of Lehman approached, **IMF sounded alarm** on leveraged loans, deteriorating credit standards
- No substantive action taken
- Conventional wisdom: interest-rate policy directed at inflation target, financial system protection left to regulators

### Regulatory Explosion
- Post-2008: great deal more regulation
- **Dodd-Frank Act** (2010): tens of thousands of pages
- **Andrew Haldane**: "Dodd–Frank makes Glass–Steagall look like throat-clearing"
- Basel I (1988) 30 pages → Basel III (2011) **616 pages**
- **James Grant**: "Central banks are printing rules almost as fast as they're printing money"

### Regulatory Proliferation
- Late 1970s: Old Lady of Threadneedle Street employed fewer than 80 regulators (one for every 11,000 City workers)
- After 2008: UK financial regulators to finance employees ratio fell to **1:300**

### Regulatory Arbitrage
- Post-crisis regulations unlikely to achieve intended aim
- **Clever financiers can always find loopholes**
- **Regulatory arbitrage** existed since Babylonian times
- "Central problem with central planning"
- Ultra-low rates made regulators' task even more intractable
- **Claudio Borio**: monetary policy and "macroprudential" regulation pulled in opposite directions

### Ultra-Low Rates Consequences Summary
- Induced increase in leverage
- Debt-fuelled merger boom
- Countless carry trades (most involved "volatility-selling")
- Collapse in underwriting standards
- Return of less-regulated shadow banks
- Regulatory arbitrage abounded

### Andrew Haldane's Risk Conservation Theory
- Financial regulation didn't reduce systemic risk, merely influenced where risk appeared
- "Risk, like energy, tends to be conserved not dissipated, to change its composition but not its quantum"
- "So it is possible the financial system may exhibit a new strain of systemic risk … now originating on the balance sheets of mutual funds"

### The Problem with Ultra-Low Rates
- Bank of England's top economist **understated the problem**
- Quantum of risk doesn't remain same under all conditions
- **Ultra-low rates encourage investors to take more risk**
- As **Hyman Minsky** pointed out: **financial stability is destabilizing**
- **Moral hazard**: when insurance premiums set too low, people build houses on flood plains
- Activist central banks (setting Galiani's "price of insurance" at nothing) had similarly deleterious influence on Wall Street behavior

### The Trap
- Solution to regulatory conundrum: **higher interest rates and less accommodating monetary policy**
- Unfortunately, **Jeremy Stein's** clever comment about monetary policy "getting into all the cracks" fell on deaf ears
- By February 2013: ravenous hunger for yield already unleashed million carry trades
- **No turning back** — monetary policymakers fearful removing extraordinary measures would induce financial turmoil
- After Fed slowly started hiking rates from late 2015, **Volmageddon** showed how unwinding obscure carry trade could wreak havoc
- **Low rates begot more risk, and ever-present financial fragility required that rates remain low**

### A True Rate
- **Dennis Robertson** (Keynes's Cambridge contemporary): enthralled by Lewis Carroll
- Robertson thought Keynes's interest theory as insubstantial as Cheshire Cat's grin
- Unlike Keynes, believed in necessity of interest:

> "Something is needed to ensure the equality between the amount of tea which sellers are willing to put on to the market and the amount which buyers are willing to take off it; and that something is a certain price of tea. Similarly, something is needed to ensure equality between the amount of money which lenders are willing to put on the market for loans and the amount of money which borrowers are willing to take off it; and that something is a certain rate of interest."

### The Natural Rate vs. Policy Rates
- **Reichsbank President Hjalmar Schacht**: called this "true rate" of interest
- Reflects supply and demand for actual savings
- True/natural rate not necessarily revealed by presence of inflation/deflation
- Central bankers who fix rates by reference to consumer price index changes set themselves up to fail
- Problem: capital is limited, but medium by which savings transferred (money) can be created without limit
  - By central banks (fiat money)
  - By commercial banks (fountain-pen money)
- Result: interest rate on money loans becomes detached from savings

### Hayek's Vision: Competitive Private Money

- Advent of cryptocurrencies has potential to realize Hayek's vision

- Still possible one day cryptocurrency may emerge:
  - Safe to store

### Central Bank Digital Currencies (CBDCs)
- Alternative: central banks developing digital currencies to compete with cryptos

- **Digital Gold Standard**: some advantages, fewer disadvantages than classical Gold Standard
- One advantage: central bankers no longer able to pursue active monetary policy
- Cost of borrowing would more accurately reflect supply/demand for actual savings
- Guided by market's invisible hand: rate of interest would find its natural level
