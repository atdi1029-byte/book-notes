# The Price of Time: The Real Story of Interest
## Edward Chancellor (2022)

### One-Line Summary
Interest is the price of time itself, and when central banks suppress it to near zero, they unleash a cascade of economic distortions — from zombie companies to asset bubbles to rising inequality — that vindicate centuries of warnings about the dangers of artificially cheap money.

### Core Thesis
Chancellor argues that interest rates are not merely a policy lever for central banks to tweak inflation and output — they are a fundamental organizing principle of capitalism, rooted in human time preference and stretching back to ancient Babylon. When rates are artificially suppressed, the consequences are devastating: misallocation of capital, financial fragility, wealth inequality, declining productivity, and the erosion of creative destruction. The book traces the 5,000-year history of interest to show that the post-2008 experiment with ultra-low and negative rates was not just unprecedented but profoundly damaging.

### Key Arguments

1. **Interest is ancient and necessary** — originating in Mesopotamia around 3000 BC, interest exists because resources are scarce, production takes time, and humans naturally prefer present over future consumption. It cannot be abolished without destroying capital allocation.

2. **Every era of artificially low rates has produced a bubble** — from John Law's Mississippi scheme (1720) to the Railway Mania (1840s) to the Roaring Twenties to the 2000s housing bubble. The pattern is consistent across five millennia.

3. **The post-2008 experiment was the most extreme in history** — rates pushed to the lowest level in 5,000 years, with $17 trillion in negative-yielding bonds at the peak. Rather than healing the economy, ultra-low rates created zombie companies, inflated asset bubbles, widened inequality, and killed productivity growth.

4. **Low rates hurt the poor more than the rich** — Bastiat predicted this in 1849: free credit benefits the wealthy (who can borrow cheaply to speculate) while punishing savers, pensioners, and workers through inflation, unaffordable housing, and stagnant wages.

5. **Financial repression is the endgame** — governments use negative real rates to erode their debt burdens at the expense of savers, a pattern Chancellor traces from post-WWII Western economies to modern China's entire development model.

### Structure
- **Part One (Ch 1-6)**: Historical origins of interest from Babylon through the 1920s crash
- **Part Two (Ch 7-16)**: How post-2008 ultra-low rates distorted the modern economy
- **Part Three (Ch 17-18)**: Global consequences — emerging markets, China's debt bomb
- **Conclusion + Postscript**: The road to serfdom through monetary policy; COVID-era inflation

### Who Should Read This
Anyone interested in monetary policy, financial history, central banking, or understanding why the post-2008 economy felt broken despite "recovery." Essential reading for investors trying to understand the distortions created by a decade of zero rates.
